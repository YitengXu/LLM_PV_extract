    , Accepted 29th July 2020First published on 29th July 2020AbstractConjugated polymers consisting of electron-rich and electron-deficient units as alternative structures have played important roles in the field of organic solar cells (OSCs). A thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (TPD) unit as an electron-deficient unit has been used to construct conjugated polymers for application in fullerene and non-fullerene based OSCs. TPD-based monomers can be simply prepared and TPD-polymers can be synthesized via environmentally friendly direct (hetero)arylation polymerization, providing a possibility for large quantity preparation. TPD-polymers usually have deep frontier energy levels, wide band gaps with absorption onset around 700 nm and good charge transport properties, showing the advantages of high open-circuit voltage, high fill-factor and excellent spectral matching with a small band gap non-fullerene acceptor. From the material design and synthesis and their optoelectrical properties, TPD-polymers have great potential applications in OSCs toward large-area devices. In this review, we provide an overview of TPD-polymers for OSCs in the last ten years, including the design and synthesis of TPD-polymers, and their application in fullerene and non-fullerene OSCs. We will also provide some perspective about the research of TPD-polymers that meet the requirement of OSCs. We hope that our universal summary can stimulate the study of TPD-polymers in the future, especially toward high performance, low cost and stable OSCs.
1. Introduction
Owing to the advantages such as light weight, large-area printing fabrication and flexibility, organic solar cells (OSCs) have attracted wide research interest over the past two decades.1,2 Based on the development of materials and device fabricating technologies, the application of OSCs in wearable equipment3 and indoor light environments, such as powering the Internet of things (IoTs),4 can be realized in the near future. One milestone in the development of OSCs is the bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) structures for the active layers, creating large contact interface of donor/acceptor (D/A) to facilitate efficient exciton separation.5 For the acceptor materials in the BHJ layer, typical electron acceptors have evolved from fullerene derivatives, such as phenyl-C61(71)-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM and PC71BM) at the early stage, to high-performance non-fullerene acceptors nowadays.6 With respect to the donor materials, the most successful electron donors belong to the π-conjugated copolymers with electron “push–pull” structures.7 When incorporating electron donating and accepting (D–A) units into the conjugated backbones, the D–A copolymers can not only display tunable frontier energy levels to match with electron acceptors, but also show narrow band gaps to optimize the solar light-harvesting ability. The commonly used electron-donating units include oligo-thiophenes (oligo-Ts), benzodithiophene (BDT), carbazole, fluorene and other electron-rich π-conjugated units.8 Typical electron-deficient moieties include benzothiadiazole, thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (TPD), isoindigo, diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP), naphthalene diimide (NDI) and other groups.9–15 Combination of these conjugated units can yield numerous conjugated polymers, and therefore provide the opportunity to tune their optical and electrochemical properties toward high performance OSCs.
Among the above-mentioned electron-deficient units, the TPD unit has the advantages of easy synthesis, high coplanarity and strong π–π stacking properties. The TPD unit was first synthesized by Sicé in 1954 by treating thiophene-3,4-dicarbocylic acid with ammonia.16 TPD-based conjugated copolymers were firstly reported by Tour and co-workers in 1997,17 and then were applied in BHJ-type OSCs in 2010.18–21 After that, the development of TPD-polymer based OSCs has gained much attention and made impressive progress among the past ten years. In particular, we have devoted efforts to the development of TPD-polymers for non-fullerene OSCs (NFOSCs) with low energy losses, high open-circuit voltage (Voc) and high performance by using proper non-fullerene acceptors and a cross-linking strategy.22–24 Meanwhile, TPD-based double-cable conjugated polymers for single-component organic solar cells (SCOSCs) were also explored.25,26 In this Feature Article, we will provide an overview of the development of TPD-polymers that act as a donor and acceptor in OSCs. The synthesis of TPD-polymers is also highlighted since they can be easily prepared via C–H activation polymerization. Finally, a perspective on the application of TPD-polymers in OSCs is provided.
2. Synthesis of TPD-polymers
The N-substituted TPD and dibromo-TPD molecules are the most used TPD monomers (Scheme 1). The dibromo-TPD monomer can be used to prepare TPD-only polymers via Cu-catalysed homo-coupling reation.27,28 TPD units are excellent candidates for Stille polymerization with various electron-rich units ended by bis-stannyl substitutions.17 Actually, most TPD-polymers have been obtained through Stille polymerization to date. Apart from the traditional reaction condition by using heat, it was also reported that microwave synthesis can afford high-quality TPD-polymers for BHJ OSCs.29 Efforts were also devoted to the continuous flow synthesis of TPD-polymers, showing their potential in large-scale production.30
 Scheme 1  Structures of the TPD unit and the D–A polymers. Two main routes of synthesizing the TPD based D–A polymers, Stille polymerization and the DHAP method, are also shown. 
Despite the efficient preparation of TPD-polymers via Stille polymerization, the severe conditions for the synthesis and use of organic tin compounds represent a big obstacle in material development. Therefore, the direct hetero-arylation polymerization (DHAP) method, avoiding the use of organic tin or boron compounds, has attracted much attention in recent years (Scheme 1).31–36 Leclerc's group firstly synthesized TPD-polymers by using the DHAP method,37 and then they extended the copolymerized monomers from dibromo bithiophene37 to BDT,38 DPP,39 and isoindigo40 in the following reports. Ozawa and co-workers tried to reduce the amount of Pd catalyst to improve the polymer quality since the residue of transition metals was detrimental to the device performance.41–43 Kanbara et al. developed a new catalyst system, making this DHAP process simplified and amenable to the air.44 Very recently, Wang and co-workers extended the monomer to thiazole-linked TPD. The resulting copolymers based on TPD and NDI units bridged with non-substituted thiazole showed high electron mobilities.45 Although a Cu catalyst has been used in the DHAP process to afford TPD-based small molecules,46 Pd compounds are the most widely reported and efficient catalysts for this reaction. Another report on electrochemical polymerization was also used in simple TPD-thiophene copolymers, but this method has attracted less attention.47 Since the DHAP method exhibits the advantages of easy synthesis and being environmental-friendly, and meanwhile, TPD-polymers prepared from this method have shown similar or even better performance in BHJ OSCs as compared with the polymers from Stille reaction,48 this synthetic protocol will unambiguously attract more attention in the future.
3. TPD-polymers for fullerene-based OSCs
Fullerene acceptors have played vital roles in the development of BHJ OSCs. The power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of fullerene-based BHJ-type OSCs have increased above 11%.49,50 TPD-polymers have also been used in fullerene-based OSCs as electron donors, affording PCEs close to 10%.51–53
3.1 Structural engineering of TPD-polymers

3.1.1 TPD-polymers containing a BDT unit. 
Benzodithiophene (BDT) is widely used as an electron-donating unit in D–A conjugated polymers.8,54 The central benzene ring of BDT can be decorated with different side chains, such as alkoxyl and thienyl groups with F, Cl and S atoms. As a result, BDT-based polymers have tunable energy levels and excellent charge carrier mobilities. The application of TPD-polymers into OSCs started with a series of PBDTTPDs with alkoxyl groups on BDT (PD1 to PD5) in 2010 reported by several groups (Fig. 1).18–21 These polymers exhibited similar absorption spectra (1.7–1.8 eV) and frontier energy levels due to them having the same conjugated backbones (Fig. 2). When blended with fullerene acceptors including PC61BM, PC71BM and ICBA (indene-C60 bisadduct), the BHJ solar cells gave PCEs from 3.4% to 6.8% (Table 1). By optimizing the active layers with solvent additives or adjusting the molecular weight, the PD1:PC61BM based devices presented improved PCEs to over 7%.55,56 As the TPD-BDT polymers usually have deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels, the solar cell devices can be expected to show high Vocs (>0.9 V).21,55–57 When the alkyl chain attached to TPD was replaced by a corresponding ketone group, the HOMO level of PD6 could be further decreased, resulting in a higher Voc, even to over 1 V.58 When the donor polymer PBDTTT-C-T, with similar conjugated backbones, was incorporated into PD1, the ternary solar cells exhibited an outstanding PCE of 9.3% with a high Jsc up to 17.2 mA cm−2.53

When the side chains of a BDT unit were replaced by thienyl groups (Fig. 1), the solar cell devices based on PD7 and PD8 could deliver Vocs exceeding 1 V with high PCEs over 6%.59,60 Replacing BDT with the O analogues such as benzodifuran (BDF) or thienylbenzofuran (TBF) could change the energy levels and thus lead to different Vocs. Specifically, the BDF based device gave a moderate Voc of 0.9 V for PD9, while the TBF based device gave a high Voc close to 1.1 V (Table 1, PD10).61,62 When bare thiophene (T) was introduced as a spacer between BDT and TPD, the performances based on PD11 and PD16 were significantly reduced due to the low crystallinity.63 But this could be well resolved by appropriate substitution of the T spacer, such as decorating with a hexyl unit or F atom as shown in PD13 to PD15.64,65 Besides the single T spacers, a thienylthiophene (TT) spacer was also investigated in PD17.51,66,67 Notably, the linking mode of the TT spacer in the resulting polymers played significant roles in determining the device performance. When TT adopted a similar conformation to TPD in the polymers, the PD18 and PD19 based solar cells delivered PCEs of 7.50% and 9.10% with excellent Jsc of 18.15 mA cm−2 and FF of 0.75, respectively.51,67 Polymers with conjugated side chains attached to BDT units (PD12, PD20 and PD21) also gave comparable PCEs (Table 1).68,69 The optical band gaps of TPD-polymers containing BDT units and their photovoltaic performance in fullerene-based solar cells are summarized in Table 1.
Random terpolymers are an effective strategy to develop new conjugated polymers with tunable optoelectrical properties. BDT and TPD units have been used to construct “D1–A1–D1–A2” and “D1–A1–D2–A1” type random copolymers, such as PD22 to PD28 (Fig. 1). The reported electron-deficient A2 units included DPP,70 bis(thiophen-2-yl)pyrrolopyrroledione (TDPPDT),71 thiadiazoloisoindoledione (TID),72 benzothiadiazole (BT)73 and benzotriazole (BTz),73 and the D2 units were usually alkyl-substituted terthiophenes.74 Interestingly, Zhou et al. adopted the single T spacer with thienylenevinylene side chains as A2 in a random polymer TTV1.75 Most of these random terpolymer based fullerene solar cells could present considerable PCEs.


3.1.2 TPD-polymers containing oligothiophene. 
Polythiophenes, especially the well-known poly(3-hethylthiophene) (P3HT), are of paramount significance in the development of BHJ-type OSCs due to their strong crystallinity. Thiophene and its derivatives are good choices as spacers or directly as the electron-donating parts in D–A conjugated polymers. Therefore, bithiophenes (T–T),76,77 terthiophenes (T–T–T)78 and even tetrathiophene (T–T–T–T),79–81 and also thiophenes bridged with thienylthiophene (T–TT–T,82 TT–T–TT,52 T–T–TT–TT52) have been incorporated into the TPD-polymer backbones (Fig. 3, PD29 to PD41). These polymers as an electron donor exhibited PCEs up to 9.2% for PD38 and fill factors (FFs) close to 80% for PD35 in fullerene-based solar cells (Table 2). It is worth mentioning that, the polymers PD40 synthesized by traditional Stille coupling and DHAP methods gave comparable PCEs when blended with PC71BM. This result demonstrated that the DHAP method had great potential in high-performance TPD-polymers.83 Some studies also used vinylene84,85 (PD42 to PD44) and phenyl86–88 (PD45 to PD47) spacers between thiophenes to construct TPD-polymers. In particular, the PD42:PC71BM device exhibited high PCE with thick active layers (Fig. 3, PCE of 6% with 450 nm thick film).84 They were also used to construct random terpolymers (PD48 to PD52) with DPP,89,90 benzothiadiazole (BT or BDT)91,92 and isoindigo (iI),93 providing PCEs close to 7%.92

3.1.3 TPD-polymers containing fused conjugated units. 
In this part, we summarize the TPD-polymers containing fused conjugated units (Fig. 4 and Table 2). The cyclopentadithiophene (CPDT) is a strong electron-donating unit that can be used to lower the band gaps of conjugated polymers. Therefore, the band gaps of conjugated polymers based on CPDT and TPD units (PD53 to PD57, PD68) are usually around or lower than 1.8 eV, which is favourable for improving light-harvesting ability and obtaining high short-circuit current densities (Jscs).94–98 The PD54:PC71BM based device gave a PCE of 6.41% with the superior Jsc of 14.1 mA cm−2 (Table 2).95 Impressively, the polymer with the same backbone but synthesized via DHAP could deliver even higher PCEs up to 6.8% due to a higher open-circuit voltage (Voc).99 Polymers with increased dielectric constants (εr) are favourable for better device performance as a consequence of the suppressed recombination loss. This was well demonstrated by adjusting the side chains on CPDT from alkyl groups to oligo(ethylene glycol) with the εr increased from 3.1 to 4.9. The PC71BM based device displayed improved PCEs from 3.30% to 4.42% with higher Jsc and FF but reduced Voc.100 When the central sp3 C was replaced by Si or Ge as dithienosilole (DTS, PD59 to PD62, PD85) or dithienogermole (DTG, PD58) fused units, little impact on the band gaps of the polymers was observed (Fig. 4).101–108 However, the average FF of the corresponding devices is much higher than that of those with CPDT units. This could be attributed to the improved intermolecular packing of the polymers and hence better phase separation in the photoactive layers.101 The strong electron-donating unit dithienopyrrole (DTP) was also used to design TPD-polymers (PD63, PD64), which always resulted in lower PCEs, although the polymers displayed broad absorption spectra up to 750 nm.109,110
 Fig. 4  Typical structures of TPD-polymers containing fused conjugated units. 
In comparison with the dithiophene based fused units, TPD-polymers containing carbazole111–114 (PD65 to PD67) and fluorene115–117 (PD69, PD70) units exhibited large band gaps almost up to 2.3 eV (Table 2). Although high Vocs could be obtained, the PCEs were much lower than those of other TPD-polymers. In contrast, the benzene-extended BDT units, such as naphthodithiophene (NDT, Fig. 4, PD71 to PD73) with a fused tetracyclic structure, not only endowed deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels118–120 but also showed good phase separation, resulting in a high PCE of 7.5%.119 It is worth mentioning that conjugated polymers based on pyrene and TPD units obtained by a DHAP method could provide considerable PCEs up to 2.06% (PD74) (Table 2).121 Fused pentacyclic units conjugated with TPD have also been developed (PD75, PD76).122,123 Specifically, as shown in Fig. 4, the core X7 that was used to construct the famous non-fullerene acceptor ITIC128 was also accommodated into PD76, affording a PCE of 5.23% (Table 2).123


3.1.4 Conjugated polymers by using bis-TPD units. 
To further decrease the band gaps and deepen the HOMO energy levels, an effective strategy is to increase the content of electron-withdrawing units in the polymers. Therefore, double TPD-containing monomers have been developed to synthesize bis-TPD based copolymers (Fig. 5). The polymer PD77 containing bis-TPD and BDT units bridged with thiophene delivered a high PCE of 7.59% with a Voc of 1 V (Table 2).124 When incorporating a conformational lock into the polymer backbone, the PCE based on PD78 was further increased to 8.18%.125 Compared to the thiophene-bridged polymer (PD79), the selenophene bridged polymer PD80 showed an improved PCE of 3.85% due to its high hole mobility.126 When copolymerized with thienylthiophene (PD81, PD82), the TPD-polymer:PC71BM blend displayed a high PCE of 9.08% (Table 2),127 which is among the highest PCEs of TPD-polymer:fullerene based OSCs to date. The outstanding performance of bis-TPD based copolymers as donors is also confirmed in NFOSCs, which will be discussed in the following section.

3.2 Study of the morphology in BHJ films based on TPD-polymers
The microphase separation between donor and acceptor in the photoactive layers plays a crucial role in the photovoltaic performance of BHJ-type OSCs.129 The polymer structures, such as conjugated backbones, side units, heteroatoms and molecular weights, as well as the solution processing conditions, (such as solvents, high boiling point additives and the temperatures for film formation), could adjust the crystallization/aggregation process of conjugated polymers, resulting in a distinct morphology in blended thin films. The relationships between chemical structure, morphology and device performance have been well-studied in OSCs based on TPD-polymers as donors.
The copolymer backbone has a pronounced impact on the phase segregation of the BHJ active layer. This is demonstrated clearly by the one atom variation in the three polymers PD55, PD60 and PD58, in which C, Si and Ge were used in the fused conjugated backbones.130PD55 gave a high PCE (over 7%) compared to those of PD60 and PD58 (∼5%). Investigation of the morphology of BHJ blends revealed that PD55 mainly exhibited “edge-on” orientation, while PD60 and PD58 showed “face-on” orientation that the conjugated backbones aligned parallel to the surface. These differences may be responsible for the distinct FFs and PCEs of OSCs.
Side chains of TPD-polymers have also been intensively studied to tune the morphology and photovoltaic performance in solar cells. For example, the linear side chains on the TPD unit could provide improved PCEs for TPD-polymers compared to branched side units, such as a 2-ethylhexyl unit (PD1vs.PD2 and PD55vs.PD56, respectively).98,131 Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) revealed that the nanoparticles in PC71BM blended thin films of PD55 were significantly increased when the length of the side chains was elongated in PD53 or switched to branched side units in PD56 (Fig. 6). In another work, the minimal methyl substituents on the TPD moiety were explored in order to enhance the intermolecular interactions between the polymer donor and the fullerene acceptor. Efficient charge dissociation was realized, providing a high PCE of 7.5% with PC71BM.132 Similarly, rigid side chains on TPD were also favourable for efficient charge separation, in which a low content of fullerene acceptor could be realized to control the cost.133 In addition, the optimization of the side chains on both the BDT and TPD units was also studied in detail.134–136 Preferential orientation of the polymer in the films and proper phase segregation size could be obtained. As a result, the PCEs could be enhanced from 2.15 to 8.14%.135 The impact of the side chain length (from C6 to C8 to C10) attached to the thienothiophene spacers was also considered to tune the molecular packing and the hole mobility, which led to a high PCE of up to 8.7% for PD83 (Fig. 1).137

Another approach to tune the microphase separation is the solvent additives, which are mainly volatile organic small molecules with high boiling points, such as 1,6-diiodohexane (DIH), 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO), 1-chloronaphthalene (CN), diphenylether (DPE), etc. Diiodoalkanes have been widely used as additives in TPD-polymer based OSCs.20,76,138–143 In the early studies, DIO was always used as an additive in TPD-polymer solar cells, but it did not always show positive effects on the photovoltaic performance.20 In another report, diiodoalkanes with different alkyl length, diiodobutane (DIB), DIH and DIO, were systemically used to optimize the PD29/PC71BM blend films.76 Although all three additives were able to increase the PCEs, the DIH processed OSC showed the most remarkable increment to 7.3%, compared with DIB (5.9%) and DIO (6.6%), while the as-cast device showed a PCE of 5.0%. The effect of solvent additive was extensively studied by the synchrotron grazing-incidence small-/wide-angle X-ray scattering (GISAXS/GIWAXS) and TEM methods. The better performance by using these additives could be attributed to both the improved polymer packing and removal of the grain boundary of the aggregated fractal-like PC71BM clusters, leading to superior pathways for carrier transport.76
Apart from the intrinsic crystallinity and ordered polymer packing, the surface properties can also be well tuned by changing the solvent additives. It was revealed by atomic force microscopy (AFM) that the large lateral phase separation of PD84 (Fig. 1) and PC71BM was significantly reduced, resulting in fewer surface pinholes (Fig. 7), which was responsible for reduced current leakage and lower Voc losses. As a consequence, the performance of the device could be enhanced from 3.08% to 7.71%.141
 Fig. 7  Tapping mode AFM height images of PC71BM blended with (a) PD84 without DIO and (b) PD84 with 3% DIO. Reproduced from ref. 141 with permission from the American Chemical Society. 
In the optimization of TPD-polymer based OSCs, 1-chloronaphthalene (CN) was widely used in controlling the phase separation.144–147 The interplay between the 1-CN as an additive and TPD-polymers with different side chain combinations,144,146 polymer molecular weights145 and also polarity of the side chains,147 was demonstrated. In particular, aggregation domains of the polymers with various molecular weights were revealed by the ultra-small angle X-ray scattering and small-angle X-ray scattering. Firstly, for polymers with higher molecular weight, heavier aggregation in solution was able to reduce PCBM-rich domains, and therefore could form fibrillar phase separation. Secondly, for the polymer with low molecular weight, this could only be realized by the addition of 1-CN since PCBM showed much higher solubility in 1-CN than the D–A copolymer.145 Based on this fact, the PCE could be enhanced from 3% to exceed 8%. Moreover, solvent additives also played important roles in the optimization of random TPD-polymer based OSCs. For the random polymer of TPD conjugated with both BDT and dithiophene phenyl units, the additive DPE gave a better performance in OSCs with PCEs up to 8.25%, compared to DIO (7.15%) and 1-CN (6.75%).148
3.3 Study of stability and application of OSCs based on TPD-polymer donors
In view of the commercialization of OSC technology, one of the obstacles to overcome is the long-term stability under operation. The degradation pathways of the devices have attracted wide research interest. It was recently demonstrated that not only the dimerization of fullerene acceptors but also the polymerization of the donor parts leads to the phase degradation of the active layer.149 On the other hand, the photo cross-linkable sites, including alkyl bromine150 and terminated vinylene,151,152 on the polymer can endow improved stability of TPD-polymer based OSCs. In another report, through excluding low molecular weight TPD polymer, which is prior to decomposition into TPD monomers, the device stability was enhanced.153
Another important aspect concerning device development is the optimization of the interfacial layers. In this part, the n-type transition metal oxide electron transport layers (ETLs) have been extensively explored. For example, sol–gel derived ZnO,158 ZnO-PVP (polyvinyl pyrrolidone),159 pre-synthesized ZnO colloidal particles,160 sputtered ZnO161 and also TiOx162,163 were used in the fabrication of OSC devices. Based upon these well-developed interfacial layers, TPD-polymers have been used to fabricate tandem154,156 and flexible155 organic photovoltaics, as shown in Fig. 8a–c. Interestingly, the triple homo tandem device could efficiently promote water splitting with an overall efficiency of 6%.156 Moreover, the TPD-polymers have been applied to indoor photovoltaic (Fig. 8d)157 and quantum dot based hybrid solar cells.164,165 In particular, TPD-polymers have wide optical band gaps that can be well-matched with the spectra of indoor light (such as an LED lamp), so TPD-polymers as a donor with wide bandgap electron acceptors such as perylene bisimide derivatives can be promising candidates for indoor photovoltaics.

4. TPD-polymers as an electron donor for NFOSCs
Recently, the non-fullerene acceptors have unambiguously been the major focus of research for the BHJ OSCs.12,166,167 In particular, since the fused ring electron acceptors (FREAs) were developed by Zhan et al. in 2015,128 OPVs have made rapid progress with fast enhancement in PCEs, leading to a new era. The significant characters of the FREAs include small band gap, deep frontier energy levels and excellent electron transport due to the special interpenetrated network formed by these molecules.168,169 TPD-polymers usually show wide band gaps and deep energy levels, which can form complementary absorption, high Vocs and low energy losses when combined with FREAs. Therefore, TPD-polymers are a good candidate to work with FREAs.
Accordingly, three BHJ blends PD86:IDIC,170PD13:ITIC171 and PD87:ITIC172 afforded PCEs over 10% (Fig. 9, Table 3). Among them, we intentionally selected three TPD-polymers with distinct frontier energy levels, which could afford different Vocs and energy losses (Eloss, defined as the difference between the optical band gap and Voc). We found that the PTPDBDT(PD7):ITIC blends displayed low Eloss of 0.54 eV and thus high Voc of 1.05 V (Table 3),24 but the quantum efficiency was dramatically decreased, resulting in low photocurrent. This was reflected by the low photoluminescence quenching efficiency of this blend (Fig. 10). Since in many NFOSCs, high PCEs could be simultaneously obtained with low Eloss, the unusual behaviour in PD7:ITIC is puzzling. It may be due to the large microphase separation in the blends and hence low quenching efficiency, but more profound studies will be required in the future.

We then tried to enhance the photovoltaic performance of NFOSCs based on PD7. We introduced halogen atoms into the FREA ITIC, as F-ITIC, Cl-ITIC, Br-ITIC and I-ITIC, to tune the crystallinity and frontier energy levels of the acceptors.23 These halogenated ITICs provided down-shifted HOMO and LUMO levels, which is beneficial for sufficient driving force for charge separation. As a consequence, NFOSCs based on PD7 as an electron donor and these halogenated ITICs as an acceptor showed significantly enhanced Jscs, and hence high PCEs compared to non-halogenated ITIC. It is worth mentioning that this is the first example to introduce halogen atoms into FREAs. Optimization of the TPD-polymer backbone173 (PD88) and introducing a small amount of PCBM as a second acceptor174 (PD89) could further enhance the PCEs of halogenated FREAs based OSCs to over 13% (Table 3).
Another attempt to enhance the performance of PD7 based NFOSCs is to use a cross-linking strategy in our recent work.175 We introduced different contents of a brominated-TPD cross-linker into the cross-linked TPD-polymers (PD90–95, Fig. 9). The synthetic routes of these cross-linked polymers are present in Fig. 11. The new polymers had similar absorption spectra and energy levels, but they exhibited distinct microstructures in blended thin films. As revealed by resonant soft X-ray scattering (R-SoXS) and AFM, the domain size and roughness were reduced in the blends when the content of cross-linker was increased in the TPD-polymers, leading to good phase separation (Fig. 11). Therefore, the PCEs were enhanced from 7.56% with PD7 without a cross-linker to 12.18% based on PD90–3. This was a typical example to show how to tune the microstructures from the minor modification of the chemical structures.22 In another case, irregular polymer PD91 exhibited higher PCE compared to the regular counterpart owing to better morphology derived from the lower crystallinity of the irregular structure.176

Since the new FREA Y6 was reported by Zou and co-workers, the PCE of single-junction BHJ OSCs has been enhanced to over 18%.169,177,178 For TPD-polymers, the Y6-based BHJ devices have realized PCEs up to 14% (Table 3). Specifically, the TPD-polymer based on bis-TPD units showed outstanding performance in Y6-based BHJ OSCs. In comparison with the PCE of 5.9% for mono-TPD based copolymer PD16, the bis-TPD copolymer PD92 could give much higher PCE of 14.2% due to the enhanced Voc, Jsc and FF.179 The whole alkyl side chains were also explored, indicating that along with the number of carbon atoms reducing from 56 to 48, the polymer PD93 provided a higher PCE of 14.1%.180 These PCE values are the highest for TPD-polymers both as a donor and an acceptor. It should be noted that higher PCEs can be expected since more Y-series non-fullerene acceptors are now being developed.181
In addition to the well-known FREAs, another type of non-fullerene acceptor is the so-called rylene diimide-based materials, for example, NDI and perylene diimide (PDI) NFAs. Very recently, Kim et al. blended the TPD-polymers with different alkyl side chains with Y6 or an NDI-based polymer to evaluate their application in BHJ-type OSCs. When TPD-polymers were blended with an NDI-polymer acceptor, the blends showed clear aggregation behaviour, while this aggregation tendency was significantly reduced when using the Y6 acceptor. When blending with the small non-fullerene acceptor Y6, the polymer PD95 exhibited the strongest aggregation behaviour and gave a PCE of 12.02%, while the PCE was only 8.85% for polymer PD87 (Table 3).182 In contrast, the NDI-polymer based device showed decreased PCE from 7.47% for PD87 to 3.14% for PD95. Besides, other kinds of TPD-polymers, with different conjugated backbones,184 varied side chains183 (PD96) and even random polymers185–187 (PD97 to PD99) could also deliver moderate PCEs with NDI polymer acceptors. Notably, the NDI-polymer based device could display high Voc up to 1.1 V, while retaining a good PCE of 8% (Table 3).183
5. TPD-polymers as electron acceptors or single-component active layers for OSCs
TPD-polymers usually have deep frontier energy levels, indicating that they can be potentially used as electron acceptors for NFOSCs by rationally selecting the electron donors.193,194 To date, there have been limited reports on this kind of polymer acceptor (Fig. 12). In general, electro-negative hyperfluorinated groups, such as penta-fluorine phenyl (PA1)188 and difluorine thiophene (PA2),189,190 were copolymerized into these materials. The PCE10:PA2 blends showed a high Voc of 1.1 V with a PCE of 4.4% (Table 3).189 When bis-TPD was used, the band gap of the polymer PA3 was reduced to 1.7 eV, exhibiting better light-harvesting ability. Therefore, NFOSCs based on PA3 exhibited a high Jsc and PCE close to 5%.190 Another example is the combination of TPD and NDI units into one polymer PA4. This polymer electron acceptor provided a PCE of 6.62% when blending with another TPD-polymer donor.191 Remarkably, the random copolymer PA5, consisting of TPD and a bithiophene imide derivative (BTI), gave the highest PCE of 8.28% among the TPD-polymer acceptors to date.192
 Fig. 12  Typical structures of TPD-polymers used as electron acceptors and typical TPD-based double-cable polymers. 
It is also worth mentioning that TPD-polymers could also be designed as a double-cable polymer when using PDI units as the side chains (Fig. 12). When the content of PDI side units increased, the energy levels of the double-cable polymers were close to those of pure PDI. These PBDTTPD(PDI)x (PA6–PA8) polymers could not only deliver PCEs of ca. 2% as the single-component active material, but also blend with low content PTB7-Th donor and gave PCEs of 3%.26 Replacing the PDI units by derivatized benzo[ghi]perylenetriimide (BPTI), the double-cable polymer PA9 delivered a higher PCE of 4.34% than single-component OSCs based on PA6.25 We envisioned that the large π-conjugation and the parallel configuration of BPTI units could build channels for efficient electron transport, so that high performance could be obtained.
6. TPD-based small molecules for OSCs
In this section, we provide a short summary about TPD-based small molecules (SMs) for application in OSCs. Few TPD-based SMs have been developed for OSCs as compared with the huge amount of TPD-polymers. These molecules contain TPD as a core, spacer or end group, and they can also be used as an electron donor or acceptor in BHJ OSCs.195–200 In the year 2012, Lin and coworkers used TPD as a core and triphenylamine as end groups to construct the molecule SD1 (Fig. 13), and then applied them into fullerene-based OSCs with a PCE of 3.31%.201 Zhang et al. developed a donor molecular SD2 with TPD as a core and rhodamine as an end group, providing an attractive PCE of 7.47% with PC71BM as an acceptor.202 Recently, TPD-based SMs as an electron acceptor have also been reported. Li et al. reported a molecule SA1 based on indacenodithiophene as a core, dicyanomethylenerhodanine as end groups and TPD as bridges, and then used this molecule as an electron acceptor in OSCs to provide a PCE of 5.53% when combining with PTB7-Th as a donor.194 Geng and coworkers used TPD as a core, cyclopentadithiophene units as bridges and dihydroinden-1-ylidenemalononitrile as end groups to construct a molecule SA2. When using the polymer PBDB-T as a donor, SA2 based OSCs provided an impressive PCE of 10.12%. Apparently, TPD-based SMs are far from fully explored, when considering the merits of a TPD unit, such as deep frontier energy levels and easy preparation.

7. Conclusions and outlook
To conclude, the applications of TPD-polymers in BHJ OSCs have been systemically reviewed as an electron donor and acceptor in BHJ-type OSCs and single-component OSCs. From the above investigation, it is obvious that NFOSCs based on TPD-polymers as a donor have received less attention. We think that some critical aspects are required to be focused on in the study of TPD-polymers in OSCs in the future.
Firstly, for the synthesis of TPD copolymers, the DHAP method holds great promise in avoiding specific organo-reagents and harsh process conditions while affording equivalently efficient or even better photoelectric materials. This is also important for the industrial application of OSCs. However, the DHAP method in the synthesis of TPD-polymers is somehow ignored. A possible reason is that side reactions such as homo-coupling are always exhibited in the DHAP process, resulting in uncontrollable reactions and imperfect polymer structures. We speculate that the side reactions in TPD-polymers could be effectively reduced by limiting the reaction sites of TPD units. Besides, TPD-polymers synthesized by the DHAP method have also received less attention for application in OSCs, especially in non-fullerene OSCs. Therefore, the development of the DHAP method in the synthesis of TPD-polymers is an important research topic for TPD-polymers. Another critical issue is the upscaled synthesis of TPD-polymers toward large-area fabrication of OSCs. Continuous flow synthesis has been reported to make conjugated polymers toward killogram scales,203–205 but the reactivity, reaction time and miscibility should be considered. We think that TPD-polymers with the DHAP method would be a candidate for the study of continuous flow synthesis.
Secondly, it is necessary to pay more attention to the application of TPD-polymers in NFOSCs: from material design, and morphology control, to the energy loss related charge separation. Another family of wide band gap polymers based on PBDB-T has received much attention with PCEs toward 18% in NFOSCs. Researchers found that PBDB-T derivatives could form fibrillar structures and interpenetration networks for efficient charge generation.206 Although the TPD-polymers can provide comparable PCEs of 14% in NFOSCs, the relative molecular packing and film morphology are seldom studied. The difference of the PBDB-T series and the TPD series in structure, charge carrier mobilities and aggregated/crystalline properties should be universally studied. These studies will be helpful to understand the TPD-polymers so as to obtain comparable or even higher performance.
Thirdly, the applications of TPD-polymer based OSCs, such as large-area and flexible devices, tandem devices and indoor photovoltaics, are critically important. For example, TPD-polymer based OSCs could provide high Vocs, which are important as a front cell in tandem devices. The wide band gap properties of TPD-polymers are also suitable for indoor photovoltaics due to the spectral matching with absorption between 400 and 700 nm of LED light. In addition, the operational stability and ambient processing conditions of TPD-polymer based OSCs are also required to be evaluated for practical applications.
In conclusion, TPD-polymers have received sufficient attention in fullerene-based OSCs, but their potential in NFOSCs is far from explored. Their merits, including facile synthesis, ambient stability, deep frontier energy levels, good crystallinity and charge transport properties, enable TPD-polymers to have great potential toward high-performance and stable OSCs.
