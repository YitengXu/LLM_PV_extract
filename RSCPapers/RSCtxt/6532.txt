    , Accepted 24th September 2010First published on 29th October 2010AbstractDespite the increasing interest of the scientific community in carbon nanotubes and graphene, fullerene C60 still plays an important role in the family of nanocarbons. A quarter of a century of research on fullerenes has been dedicated to understanding the way to produce stable, well-characterized and highly soluble fullerene derivatives, which retain the original properties of C60. The applications in a wide number of fields, especially on new materials design, have enriched the literature with fascinating examples where fullerenes are directly involved. We report here a brief summary of recent achievements in some exciting applications of fullerene derivatives.
1 Introduction
Since the first experimental observation of C60 in 1985,1 the scientific community has contributed to the discovery of an increasingly high number of members of the fullerene family that have been recently expanded with the isolation of C90.2 However, during this period, interest in the study of C60 has not decreased. C60 is the most abundant representative of the fullerene family and can be produced in a ton-scale by the arc discharge of graphite electrodes.3

        C60 is highly symmetrical (icosahedral Ih with all 60 carbon atoms chemically equivalent) and is relatively reactive owing to its enhanced curvature (vide infra). For all these characteristics, C60 retains a privileged status in the carbon family. From the beginning, the typical structural, physicochemical and electronic properties of fullerenes have stimulated the development of new fullerene-based materials with enormous potential. However, C60 is only sparingly soluble in some organic solvents and not at all soluble in water, where it exhibits a high degree of aggregation, a problem that can be solved by applying chemical modifications.
This feature article is an update of a previous contribution, which appeared in 1997 in this journal.4 The aim here, is to briefly summarize the fundamental properties and reactivity of C60, including old and new functionalization methods and to give a general overview of a few representative examples of fullerene-based materials.
2 Fundamentals of the reactivity of C60
The structure of C60 consists of 20 hexagons and 12 pentagons fused together into a hollow carbon sphere. Initially C60 was considered as a “super aromatic molecule”, but X-ray diffraction revealed rather a polyenic structure.5 In fact, C60 presents two types of bonds: (i) those joining two hexagons (6,6 junctions, 1.38 Å); and (ii) those at the junction of a pentagon and a hexagon (5,6 junctions, 1.45 Å). The deviation from planarity of the double bonds, due to their geometrical constraints, results in an enhancement of the reactivity.6 The main driving force for the reactivity of fullerenes is therefore the relief of strain associated with a change in hybridization at the reacting carbons on the spherical surface from sp2 to sp3 (Fig. 1).
 Fig. 1  After reaction has occurred on the surface of C60, a change in the hybridization from sp2 to sp3 occurs at the involved carbon atoms. 
In many instances, fullerene functionalization is considered the best approach to solve several problems associated with fullerenes since a better solubility is obtained and the main fullerene properties are either retained or enhanced in the functionalized derivatives. Covalent functionalization typically takes place at 6,6 junctions through cycloadditions or nucleophilic additions.
The reactivity of C60 was initially and extensively studied in Santa Barbara, in the group of Professor Fred Wudl.7 His group has contributed to the development of countless areas of fullerene materials science, including high Tc ferromagnetic materials, photovoltaics, medicinal chemistry, etc. In terms of chemical reactivity, the Wudl group was the first to show that C60 is mainly an electrophilic reagent.8 The demonstration that C60 reacts readily with diazo compounds opened the access to many functional derivatives, which were familiarly called “fulleroids” (Scheme 1).9 It was later discovered that “fulleroids” convert to methanofullerenes under many reaction conditions.

          Cycloadditions
        
[1 + 2] cycloadditions take place with divalent species such as carbene10 (Scheme 2, route a), nitrenes11 and silylenes12 to obtain a huge variety of methanofullerene or heteroanalogues. In the addition of nitrenes, “azafulleroids” and azamethanofullerenes are obtained,13 which are precursors to azafullerenes.

Alkynes and alkenes (Scheme 2, route b) can undergo [2 + 2] cycloaddition reactions either photochemically or thermally.14 Moreover, C60 oligomers have been prepared: by solid state synthesis in the presence of KCN to give dimers,15 or under high pressure to obtain polymers.16

          C60 can react with dipoles to yield cycloadducts at the 6,6 junctions. The 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition of azomethine ylides is a powerful and widely used methodology (Scheme 2, route c), because of the versatility of the reaction and the stability of the resulting fulleropyrrolidines.17 The ylide is generated in situ by thermal ring opening of aziridines or by decarboxylation of the immonium salts derived from condensation of α-amino acids and aldehydes. It has been possible to image fulleropyrrolidines when located in the interior or in the vicinity of SWCNT by using high resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM).18 The retrocycloaddition of fulleropyrrolidines has been recently described either by using high temperatures while trapping the ylide intermediate with a metal and a dipolarophile,19 or by combining microwave (MW) irradiation and ionic liquids.20 The harsh conditions required illustrate the high stability of the fulleropyrrolidines, and offer a better comprehension and control of the 1,3-cycloaddition reaction. Very recently, the first enantioselective 1,3 dipolar-cycloaddition to C60 has been described, by using Cu(II) and Ag(I) salts as catalyst, a chiral ligand and an aminoester, obtaining enantiomeric excesses higher than 90%.21
Also [4 + 2] cycloadditions take place at 6,6 junctions. Diels–Alder-type reactions with 1,3 dienes have been described (Scheme 2, route d).22 The main drawback is the stability of the cycloadduct versus the retro-Diels–Alder reaction that strongly depends on the electronic nature of the diene substituents.
Nucleophilic additions
The nucleophilic addition of bromomalonates to C60, the so-called Bingel or Bingel–Hirsch reaction,23 has been widely used in the design of new fullerene derivatives. The original conditions involved the treatment of diethyl bromomalonate with NaH in the presence of C60 but many other modifications of the Bingel reaction have been developed (Scheme 2, route e).24 Although methanofullerenes formed in the Bingel reaction are normally stable, the retro-Bingel reaction25 has been described both electrochemically26 and under mild chemical conditions.27

Organometallic compounds, such as organocopper,28cyanide anions,29 organolithium or Grignard reagents30 can give nucleophilic reactions to C60 with interesting results but with strong drawbacks, such as (i) low monoaddition selectivity (ii) low functional group compatibility and (iii) high load requirement of organometallic reagents. Variations of the well-known organolithium addition have permitted the synthesis of complex multifullerene derivatives.31 New arylation and alkenylation reactions to C60 have been performed by using Rh as catalyst and organoboron compounds (Scheme 2, route f).32
Other approaches have also been developed, involving zwitterions derived from dimethyl acetylenedicarboxylate (DMAD) and 4-dimethylaminopyridine (DMAP) at room temperature (Scheme 3).33 The mechanism was proposed to go through (i) a first addition of the zwitterion intermediate to the electrophilic fullerene, (ii) attack of the fullerene anion on the carbonyl carbon of the ester group and (iii) replacement of the DMAP group with a methoxy group.34

Radical functionalization
Radical functionalization has been less explored than the other functionalization methods previously described as a consequence of the high reactivity of radicals towards C60. As a matter of fact, a complex mixture of polyadducts is obtained, while the monoaddition has only been observed by mass spectroscopy and electron spin resonance (ESR).35
Nonetheless, in the past few years, exciting results have been obtained. The well-known acyl radical reactions in organic chemistry have been successfully applied to the fullerene functionalization. Thus, a single addition to the fullerene has been obtained, using a variety of aldehydes as acyl radical precursors and tetrabutylammonium decatungstate ((n-Bu4N)4W10O32, TBADT, Scheme 4).36 In a similar fashion, the photochemically-assisted, direct addition of ethers to fullerene has been recently achieved (Scheme 4).37
 Scheme 4  Radical functionalization of fullerene. Top: the addition of acyl radicals photochemically assisted to C60 Bottom: the preparation of 1,3 dioxolanes using Fe3+ salts. 
Another example is a straightforward and general procedure to prepare 1,3-dioxolanes on a 6,6 junction from aldehydes and ketenes has been described. The reaction proceeds via radical addition to C60 promoted by Fe3+ salts (Scheme 4).38
Multiple additions
The addition of an organic residue to a 6,6 junction of C60 leads to monoadducts, but also to a complex mixture of multiadducts. There are 8, 46 and 262 possible regioisomers for the bis, tris and tetrakis-additions of a symmetrical addend, respectively. The second addition to a fullerene monoadduct is controlled by the frontier molecular orbitals,39 while the control over the regiochemistry of multiple additions is only achieved in a few cases,40 where the steric hindrance plays a role.41 In general, the hard and time-consuming purification is the main factor that has minimized the interest in multiadducts. Nonetheless, major efforts have been undertaken to understand the behavior of the multiadditions in the last years. Thus, by controlling the stoichiometry of the reagents and the reaction conditions mono-, bis-, tris, etc., are formed. This methodology was followed in order to achieve the isolation and full analytical characterization of the whole series of the pyrrolidine bisadducts 1 (Scheme 6) (trans-1, trans-2, trans-3, trans-4, equatorial, cis-3, cis-2 and cis-1).42
Earlier work made possible the partial characterization of some of the patterns,43 while cis-3, cis-2 and cis-1 fulleropyrrolidine isomers were isolated for the first time. Moreover, the same strategy was applied in order to synthesize, isolate and characterize nine different trisadducts 2 (Scheme 5).44 One of the most effective geometrical construction approaches to control multiaddition is the tether-directed remote multifunctionalization that combines a cyclopropanation with a Diels–Alder reaction.45 By controlling the length and geometry of the tether, equatorial,46cis-2,47 or even trans-248 and trans-149 can be almost exclusively obtained. For example, in Scheme 3, the use of the doubly bridged porphyrin 3 induces the preferential formation of the trans-2 bisadduct 4.50 Variations of this methodology and incorporation of macrocycles helps the formation of regioselective trisadducts and bisadducts.51 Recently, new bicycloadducts with three fused pentagons on the same pentagon have been synthesized by using an intramolecular Pauson–Khand reaction.52
 Scheme 5  Top: fullerene bisadduct and trisadduct derivatives containing triethylenglycol chain Bottom: the reaction of a bis-malonate porphyrin allowing the trans-2 regioselective biscyclopropanation. 
In a recent approach, a highly regioselective method to prepare multiple Bingel adducts has been described, which is based on the use of macrocyclic malonates. Through this methodology a variety of bis-, tris-, tetra, and hexaaducts have been synthesised including trans-4,trans-4,trans-4 adducts.53

          C60 can form organometallic σ- and π-type complexes that can be used to functionalize fullerenes with metals and/or metal clusters, and simultaneously with several organic residues. Fullerene functionalized with clusters of Os, Ru and Ir have been reported and characterised by X-ray.54 Also one of the five membered rings of the fullerene core have been used to prepare ferrocene,55ruthenocene,56rhenium,57 and cobalt58 complexes (Scheme 6).
 Scheme 6  Different fullerene metal complexes of (a) Fe, Ru (b) Co (c) Re 
Polyhydroxylated fullerenes can be obtained by using acidic59 and basic60hydroxylation methods yielding water-soluble fullerenols. Nitronium chemistry has been used as well to synthesize fullerenols consisting of 14–20 hydroxy moieties.61 Fullerenols with less than 12 hydroxyl groups present poor water solubility, while the presence of more hydroxyl groups (16–24) enhanced the water affinity.62 Very recently, a series of fullerene derivatives containing 2–8 hydroxy groups have been synthesized by means of a direct reduction of peroxo groups to hydroxy groups and a following thermolysis of the peroxo groups to epoxy groups. A final hydrolysis permitted the conversion to the desired hydroxy groups.63 A great solubility in water of 58.9 mg mL−1 with a high dispersion in the molecular level was observed with the synthesis of a fullerenol in which more than 30 hydroxyl groups were included by using hydrogen peroxide instead of sodium hydroxide.64
Several examples of fullerene bromides,65 chlorides66 and fluorides67 have enlarged the family of polyhalogenated fullerenes. One of the most impressive examples was the synthesis of C60F15[CBr(CO2Et)2]3 (generally called emerald green C60).68 The treatment of C60F18 with diethylbromomalonate produced an intermediate anion that attacks the fullerene sphere but instead of the bromide ion the fluoride ion is lost from a δ position, constituting the first proof of a SN2′ substitution in a fullerene.
By using a hexaanionic C60 intermediate (C60−6), emerald green fullerenes have been also synthesized.69 Polyanionic C60 can be easily prepared by treatment of C60 with excessive lithium metal or potassium with naphthalene.70 These polyanions, can be trapped with different electrophiles to prepare fullerene derivatives. Reaction of C60−n intermediates with methyl iodides afforded the polymethylated C60 derivatives.71 The reaction with 1,4-butane-sultone, instead, leads to the formation of hexa(sulfo-n-butyl)-C60 regioisomers, demonstrating C60−6 as the major product in the mixture. Penta and hexakis(ethylmalonate) adducts of methanol-C60 as major products were obtained with diethyl 2-bromomalonate.72
Non-covalent functionalization
Supramolecular chemistry allows the preparation of functional architectures by means of non-covalent interactions, including hydrogen bonding, π–π, and metal coordination. For example, C60 derivatized with pyridyl groups can coordinate spontaneously to zinc and ruthenium tetraphenyl porphyrins.73,74 Other examples include the covalent attachment of hydrogen bond motifs to the surface of fullerenes that can be used to functionalize supramolecularly fullerenes in a second stage.75 On the other hand, fullerene can participate directly in the self-assembly process through interactions with its curved π-system. C60 can form inclusion complexes with bisporphyrin hosts76 and with extended-tetrathiafulvalene tweezers (exTTF).77
Atoms, molecules, or ions can be trapped inside the cavity of a fullerene cage to form endohedral fullerenes. H2, N2, and a wide variety of noble gases and transition metal atoms have been successfully encapsulated.78 Physical methods for the preparation of endohedral fullerenes are tedious and with a clear lack of efficiency, reaching isolated yields of only 1%.79 Recently, a new creative concept has been developed, called “molecular surgery” to introduce guests inside the fullerene sphere (Scheme 7). The procedure starts with a ring opening or a ring expansion generally induced by cycloadditions or radical-oxidating reactions in order to open a “hole” in the sphere and put in the desired species. The reconstitution of the cage after the encapsulation has been achieved at high temperatures.80
 Scheme 7  “The molecular surgery” approach: hole generation by provoking σ and π bond scission, insertion of the guest molecule, and reconstruction of the fullerene structure. 
Functionalized and endohedral fullerenes can enter SWCNTs forming “peapod” structures.81 These structures are stabilized by Van der Waals interactions between the fullerene and SWCNT and have been observed in detail by using a new aberration-corrected high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (AC-HRTEM).
3 Applications in material science
Donor–acceptor systems
The research on donor–acceptor systems involving fullerenes has attracted a lot of attention in an attempt to simulate the photosynthetic system and transform light into chemical energy. In the natural photosynthetic reaction center, several photoactive units are coupled together, so that several photoinduced electron transfer events take place after irradiation, giving a long distance and long-lived charge separated state in which the energy is stored. The easiest approach to an artificial model consists of two different units (dyad), an electron-donor and an electron-acceptor linked together by a covalent or non-covalent spacer. C60 have been widely used as an electron-acceptor because of its high electron affinity and small reorganization energy.82 Since the overall lifetime of the radical pairs depends on the distance and orientation between the donor and the acceptor, a series of C60 dyads with different donor–acceptor geometries have been prepared and studied with a series of electron donors, including porphyrins,83,62aphthalocyanines and their metalated analogs84tetrathiafulvalene (TTF),85ruthenocene and ferrocene.86 A large number of examples of dyads have been reported in the past few years and the field has been extensively reviewed.87 In this section, only a selection of a few representative examples is discussed.
Several dyads have been reported to give charge separated states with lifetimes over the μs. For instance, the ferrocene-fullerene dyad 5 radical pairs with lifetimes of 2.5 μs,88 while for exTTF-C60 dyad 6, 174 ns was measured.89 The phthalocyanine-C60 dyad 7 containing a polar triethylenglycol (TEG) chain self-aggregates into nanostructured nanotubules enhancing the charge separation lifetimes up to 1 ms.90 The longest radical-ion pair lifetimes for linked donor–acceptor systems has been reported for a Zn chlorin-C60 dyad 8 (Scheme 8) (120 s at −150 °C).91
 Scheme 8  Representative photoactive covalent dyads: ferrocene-C60 dyad (5), exTTF-C60 dyad (6), phthalocyanine- C60 dyad (7), Zn chlorin-C60 dyad (8). 
Dyads can also be assembled through non-covalent interactions (Scheme 9). Dyad 9 has been assembled by axial coordination between a fullerene functionalised with a pyridyl group and Zn-porphyrin, in which a charge separated state with a lifetime of 8.6 μs in polar solvents.73b An example of long-lived radical pairs has been reported for porphyrin-C60 dyad 10 assembled supramolecularly by H-bonds (10 μs).92
 Scheme 9  Non-covalent fullerene dyads. 
A ferrocene-C60 dyad has been assembled on a fullerene-driven bistable rotaxane (11) (Scheme 9). In non-polar solvents, such as chloroform, the macrocycle with the ferrocene electron donors forms hydrogen bonds with the two amide groups present on the thread (11A), while in polar solvents, such as DMSO or DMF, the macrocycle is free to move to the opposite end, where it forms π–π stacking interactions with the fullerene sphere (11B). This large positional change of the macrocycle can be used to modulate the kinetics of intra-ensemble charge separation and charge recombination leading to completely different lifetimes.93
Multistep electron transfer events can take place if more than two photo/electroactive units are linked to C60. In this context, triads composed of a π-extended TTF,57,94 an oligophenylvinylene (OPV) molecular wire, and a C60 electron acceptor (compound 12, Scheme 10) can give a 40 Å distant charge separated state upon illumination. Analogous systems have been prepared with porphyrins connected to C60 through oligothiophene,95 oligo(phenylene ethynylene) (OPE)96 and combination of oligothiophene and OPV.97 Also, triads assembled simply by supramolecular interactions have been described.98 Triad 13 (Scheme 10) is composed of three photo/electroactive units that are organized along a supramolecular redox gradient by a combination of hydrogen bonding and metal coordination, giving two consecutive charge transfer reactions.62 In addition, more complex derivatives can be assembled by combining 4 electroactive units, such as tetrad 14 (Scheme 10), composed of ferrocene, two porphyrins and C60, which gives a radical-pair with a lifetime of 380 μs after 3 consecutive charge transfer reactions.99

Photovoltaics
The excellent electron-accepting properties of fullerenes have been exploited in the preparation of organic solar cells, which are considered a possible alternative to silicon solar cells. [6,6]-Phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) (Scheme 11, compound 15) was first synthesized in the laboratory of Professor Wudl13a,100 and is the most widely used C60 derivative in plastic solar cells, together with the C70 analog PC71BM.101
 Scheme 11  
Fullerene derivatives used for the preparation of solar cells: PCBM, Indene-C60 bisadduct and a cyanine-C60 dyad. 
Bulk-heterojunction photovoltaic cells are prepared from blends of soluble fullerene acceptors with polymers as electron donating materials, typically poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) or copolymers with thiophene units. These types of cells display a high power conversion efficiency, which range from 5 to 7.4%.102,103 A wide range of fullerene derivatives have been used in combination with different polymers, mainly methanofullerenes, [2 + 4] cycloadducts and fulleropyrrolidines. Regarding methanofullerenes, PCBM has been widely used because of its excellent electronic properties, solubility and miscibility with conjugated polymers. A large number of PCBM analogs have been synthesized and studied, which have given raise to a large number of fullerene-based solar cells with continuously improving efficiencies.104 In a very recent work, a number of blends containing P3HT and various PCBM derivatives were tested as solar cells, obtaining power conversion efficiencies in a range from 0.02% to 4.1% as a consequence of the different solubilities of the PCBM derivatives. This fact strongly affects the morphology of the composite with P3HT and consequently the resulting efficiency.105 An electron tomography technique was used in order to study the 3D morphology of blends of P3HT and PCBM and the influence on the power efficiency of the final system.106
Concerning [2 + 4] cycloadducts, an impressive 6.5% efficiency has been recently achieved with a bulk-heterojunction polymer solar cell consisting on a blend of P3HT as an electron donor and a C60 bisadduct (Scheme 11, compound 16). In addition, the results were found to be highly reproducible and independent of the active layer thickness in the range 100–300 nm.107
Fulleropyrrolidines have been used as well as the electron acceptor for the construction of solar cells, presenting lower power conversion efficiencies, but promising results.108 For instance, a novel cyanine-fullerene dyad has been used to build up a double-layer device obtaining an energetic conversion efficiency of 0.1% (Scheme 11, compound 17)109 while a 0.02% was found for phthalocyanine–fulleropyrrolidine dyad thin films.110 In a similar fashion, π-conjugated oligomer–tetrafullerene and P3HT was used to build a photovoltaic device exhibiting a quantum efficiency of 15%.111 Other functionalized fullerenes can be useful in order to design efficient solar cells. Endohedral fullerenes as acceptor materials have been used in combination with P3HT showing 4% power conversion efficiency.112 Nonetheless, trannulenes53 have been recently used as photoactive materials for solar cells in combination with polyfluorene polymers.113
Photoactive polymers based on fullerene have also been synthesized for photovoltaics applications.114 The double cable approach, in which fullerenes are grafted to the polymer backbone have been introduced as a means to control the morphology of the polymer films.115 One of the first examples of fullerene functionalized with macromolecules for photovoltaic cells is the polythiophene derivative 18 (Scheme 12).116 Other polymers have been synthesized to improve the electro and photoactive properties of the final product, such as 19 that presents phenyl-vinylene, vinylcarbazole and C60 units.117
 Scheme 12  “Double-cable approach”: fullerene based polymers for the construction of photovoltaic devices. 
The creation of well-ordered monolayers or thin films containing fullerenes on a surface is necessary to transfer fullerene properties to a solid surface. This topic has been recently reviewed.73 The formation of self-assembled monolayers (SAM) containing unmodified fullerenes is usually made difficult by the tendency of fullerenes to aggregate and form multilayers. Several approaches have been developed to overcome this problem. C60 can be introduced as a host in the cavity of amphiphilic molecules like calixarenes and azacrowns, or chemical functionalization on the fullerene can be achieved in order to obtain an adduct with amphiphilic character.118 Solar cells displaying quantum yields up to 25% can be prepared on a gold electrode by the formation of a SAM with alkylthiols functionalized with a ferrocene–porphyrin–C60 triad.119
In a similar fashion, porphyrins with thiol functionality have been used to prepare gold nanoparticles. The porphyrin-functionalized nanoparticles can complex C60 molecules by π-stacking interactions, leading to materials that can be used in the construction of novel organic solar cells.120
Organic field-effect transistors (OFETs)
Single C60 molecules121 and C60 films122 display n-type semiconducting behavior. Charge mobilities of up to 6 cm2 V s−1 have been observed in C60 FETs. This field has also been recently reviewed.85a,123
OFETs based on C60 thin films have been prepared by growing C60 on top of organic dielectric divinyltetramethyldisiloxane-bis(benzocyclobutene) using a hot wall epitaxy (HWE) technique. The growth parameters during the process of film growth directly influence the device performance.124 In a similar work, C60 OFETs have been fabricated by using two different polymer dielectric materials: poly(methylmethacrylate) (PMMA) and cross-linkable poly(4-vinylphenol) with a high electron mobility of 0.66 cm2 V s−1 for the PMMA/C60 one.
The major challenge for the preparation of low-cost OFETs, relies on solution-processable fullerene derivatives.125 Among all the soluble fullerene derivatives, [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) is also one of the most studied fullerene derivative for OFET applications. In a recent work, two isomeric stable imino-PCBMs ([5,6]-open APCBM and [6,6]-closed APCBM) have been synthesized. An n-channel OFET containing [5,6]-open APCBM exhibited an electron mobility of μ = 4.1 × 10−2 cm2 V s in a solution-processed device exceeding the highest performance of PCBM (μ = 2.8 × 10−2 cm2 V s−1).126
Another approach is the construction of ambipolar OFETs, which work as both p- and n-type organic semiconductors.127 A recent work showed the construction of a solution-processed ambipolar OFET by using a C60 attached oligothiophene working successfully as an active-layer of the ambipolar OFET.128
Nonlinear optics (NLO) applications
Optical limiting is a nonlinear phenomenon in which, after passing a threshold, the absorption of a laser light in a material increases when the incident radiation intensity increases. Studies on fullerene derivatives have shown the great potential of this class of materials for optical limiting applications.129Fullerene derivatives exhibit a sizeable NLO response, both in solution and in the solid state.130

Fullerene derivatives in solution are efficient optical limiters, but for practical applications it is desirable to use solid state devices. One solution explored was to incorporate the fullerene into glassy matrices via sol–gel processing.131 For this purpose, compounds 20, 21, 22 (Scheme 13) have been synthesized incorporating siloxane groups, finding a similar efficiency as optical limiters both in the solid state and in solution.132
 Scheme 13  
Fullerene derivatives studied for NLO applications. 
Reduced fullerene derivatives can also be used as optical limiters.109b The neutral fulleropyrrolidine13323 and its methylated counterpart13424, as well as their reduced species 25 and 26, have been studied and they show a high, non-linear response.
New approaches are the synthesis of photonic fullerene-based materials with NLO properties that can be modulated.135 Molecular machines like rotaxane–fullerene dyad 11 (Scheme 9) can be used to manipulate the microscopic third order NLO response since the NLO behavior of 11A and 11B is different, offering new opportunities for the design of light-driven/electrochemically-driven molecular shuttles.136

Fullerene aggregates

Fullerene tends to aggregate in polar solvents, mostly because of its low solubility and high hydrophobicity. Notably, when organic appendages are attached to C60, the solubility is increased. The attachment of hydrophilic groups to the fullerene spheroid results in the preparation of amphiphilic fullerenes that can become partially or totally soluble in water, giving rise to aggregates of different shapes.137 When some amphiphilic compounds are deposited on the surface of water, it is possible to observe the formation of monolayers at the air–water interface. These films, usually called Langmuir films, can be transferred onto solid substrates by Langmuir–Blodgett (LB) or Langmuir–Schäfer (LS) techniques. Dendrimers,138 polyadducts,139 supramolecular140 and donor–acceptor assemblies141 have been tested in the production of organized mono- or multilayer films. Some examples are sketched in Scheme 14. The series of derivatives 27a–e are able to self-organize in solution, at the air–water interface and onto surfaces.81 The LS method was used to prepare a mixed composite thin film of the fulleropyrrolidine 27a and a water-soluble anionic porphyrin.142 On the other hand, it is possible to fabricate high-quality and robust photoactive ITO electrodes using derivatives 27b and 27c by the layer-by-layer (LBL) technique.143
 Scheme 14  Amphiphilic fullerenes derivatives. 
Amphiphilic fullerene derivatives with a terminal ammonium salt can be easily synthesized by azomethine ylide cycloaddition (compounds 27, Scheme 14).144 Moreover, these systems can be methylated at the pyrrolidine nitrogen, thus producing the doubly charged species 28 (Scheme 14). These amphiphilic compounds are able to self-organize when dispersed in water by sonication.145Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) studies have shown that 27a tends to aggregate in long uniform rods. Langmuir thin films have been obtained from 27c. Brewster angle microscopy (BAM) analysis of the floating film revealed the existence of aggregates on the water surface. The second positive charge in 28 confers a better solubility in water, presenting the same rod-like linear structures while compound 27b forms nanotubules with a strongly improved morphology and uniformity.146 The same results were found when the aggregation properties of the analogous compound 7 were studied.147 In a recent work, a phthalocyanine has been covalently attached to the fullerene spheroid giving wire-like superstructures on a graphite surface, which present remarkably high conductivities.148
As alternatives to the introduction of polar groups, such as amines and carboxylic groups, other groups have given interesting results. The addition of polarizable phenyl groups results in a stabilization of its anion (Scheme 15, compound 29).149 Therefore the hydrophobic fullerene is linked to a hydrophilic ion provoking a surfactant functionality to the penta-substituted fullerene. Long alkyl chains can be grafted on C60 in order to build interesting supramolecular assemblies based on Van der Waals interactions. Thus, microparticles or nanowhiskers are observed with a high control of their dimension, permitting the design of new soft materials.150 Compound 30 (Scheme 16), exhibited various supramolecular shapes by using different solvents. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and HRTEM were used in order to observe a variety of structures, such as spherical vesicles, fibrous structures, single bilayer nanodiscs and conical objects.151
Liquid cristals

Fullerenes containing mesomorphic promoters, such as dendrimers or cholesterol units, have been studied by several groups. The most direct approaches to incorporate liquid crystalline addends have been the Bingel or the 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition reactions, which have produced mesomorphic methanofullerenes or fulleropyrrolidines, respectively, with smectic,152,153 columnar,154 nematic155 and chiral nematic156 phases. Some examples of fullerene derivatives used to prepare liquid crystals are shown in Scheme 16. In the case of compound 28, a ferrocene unit was incorporated with a cyanobiphenyl dendrimer, obtaining an enantiotropic smectic phase, organized into a partial bilayer structure with lifetimes for the photoinduced charge-separated state of 490 ns in benzonitrile.111 Chiral fullerenes can also be used for the design of optically active liquid crystals. In a recent work, the four diastereoisomeric fullerodendrimers 29 have been synthesized, which display a chiral nematic phase.157

Conclusions
A short and incomplete summary on the current state of fullerene materials science has been presented in this feature article. The electronic characteristics of C60 have been exploited to achieve derivatives with innovative properties. In this context, efficient electronic devices with high mobilities have been prepared, as well as photovoltaic devices in which the efficiency has improved to reach the level of other high-performance organic-based solar cells. Moreover, the combination of a wide range of organic molecules with C60 has permitted the preparation and study of a wide range of fullerene-based materials including polymers, liquid crystals and NLO devices among others.
Recent interest has been dedicated to the supramolecular control of molecular constructs by assembling different binding motifs. The obtention of ordered nanostructures and the development of intelligent supermolecules able to perform specific tasks, reveal a bright future in the development of new generation materials.
Further research is still needed, but it is clear that C60 has not yet exhausted its role as a leading innovative material, at least in basic studies.
