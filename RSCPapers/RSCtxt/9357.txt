    , Accepted 8th June 2015First published on 10th June 2015AbstractSilver molybdate (Ag2MoO4) nanoclusters were first prepared by a dynamic template route under 20 °C. The heat released from the polymerization of the acrylamide (AM) assisted templates induced high active sites to be generated on the surface of the crystal where more attracted nuclei were adsorbed on. The continuously formed nuclei assembled together and the nanoclusters were formed. The paper provided an explanation for the formation of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters through the research on reaction conditions; on the other hand, the comparison of photocatalytic performance and antibacterial properties between the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters and the Ag2MoO4 crystals of other morphologies showed that the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters had better photocatalytic and antibacterial activities, which was a preliminary perspective for the application of the nanocluster materials.
1. Introduction
Nanoclusters, consisting of a large amount of nanoparticles, are a kind of special material, which possess unique photoluminescence properties including strong luminescence,1 large Stokes shifts,2 excellent photostability3 and two-photo absorption cross sections.4 Therefore, as a zero dimensional quantum system, nanoclusters have been developed as a special branch of experimental and theoretical research. This has attracted great attention.4–19 H. Dong et al.4 who prepared the RuO2 nanoclusters doped on the surface of TiO2 found that electrons transferring from ruthenium oxide clusters to the surface upon light irradiation increased the possibility of spatially separating photoinduced electrons and holes, which was beneficial to the enhancement of photocatalytic activity. Q. F. Zhuang et al.5 prepared a mitochondria-targeted fluorescent probe based on a covalent link between triphenylphosphonium (TPP) cations and chitosan-coated Au nanoclusters. The result showed that this novel probe possessed superior photostability, low cytotoxicity and high sensitivity. S. L. Li et al.6 who synthesized near-infrared fluorescent silver nanoclusters stabilized by the MUC1 aptamer proved the bright near-infrared luminescence, high stability and excellent biocompatibility of the MUC1-Ag nanoclusters. Y. Xu et al.7 developed a novel probe prepared by the fluorescent lysine-functionalized gold nanoclusters for the detection of Cu2+ ions, verifying the simplicity, selectivity, sensitivity and low cost of the probes. All these research studies showed the advantages of nanoclusters in each kind of application. Meanwhile, most research studies on the preparation of the nanoclusters applied template assemblies such as DNA,8 polypeptides,9,10 polymers,11 dendrimers,12 proteins,13–15 thiols16 and so forth. For example, Y. Y. Wang et al.17 prepared the primary amine derivative of the magic-size CdSe nanoclusters in the amine-bilayer templates. H. Miyamura et al.18 employed polystyrene-derived polymers with epoxide and alcohol moieties as supports to prepare heterogeneous gold nanocluster catalysts. And L. Gao et al.19 synthesized novel ZrO2 nanorod and nanocluster superstructures through a simple and mild solution-growth method using micromolecule templates.
A metal molybdate material is an important family of inorganic materials which has been investigated in the field of phytoremediation,20,21 heavy metal disposal,22,23 illumination,24,25 humidity sensors,26 magnetic properties,27,28 photoluminescence,29–32 lasers,33,34 and catalysis.35–40 Silver molybdate (Ag2MoO4), a kind of metal molybdate, was prepared through a hydrothermal route to form the products in most research studies.41–43 G. Nagaraju et al.41 used the organic free hydrothermal process to prepare the silver molybdate nanowires, nanorods and multipods. X. J. Cui et al.42 and D. P. Singh et al.43 used a hydrothermal route to synthesize the single-crystal silver molybdate nanowires and broom-like heterostructure of silver molybdate through pH-controlled self-assembly. Meanwhile, the application of Ag2MoO4 materials mainly focused on the field of biological sterilization44 because a silver ion has a bacteriostatic (growth inhibition)45 or bactericidal (antibacterial) impact.46 However, the research studies on the preparation of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters and the study on the photocatalysis of this structural material have not been shown in public.
Therefore, in this paper, different from the hydrothermal route, the dynamic template route47 was applied to prepare the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. Through the analysis of various reaction conditions, the growth mechanism shows that the thermal perturbation by the polymerization of the AM free radicals during the crystal growth was the main factor to form the nanoclusters.
2. Experimental sections
2.1 Preparation of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters by a dynamic template method
The eggshell membrane as a basic template was made from a fresh eggshell after removing the outer shell and being washed with deionized water. Thereafter it was fastened in a reactor to separate it into two horizontal compartments, where 25 mL of 0.1 mol L−1 AgNO3 solution and 25 mL of 0.05 mol L−1 Na2MoO4 solution were added, respectively. 0.05 g (i.e., 2 g L−1) of AM was added to both sides as the assisted templates. The system was kept at 20 °C for 3, 6, 12 and 24 h, respectively. The obtained products were separated by centrifugation at 1500 rpm, followed by washing with alcohol and deionized water for several times, until no Ag+ ions and extra AM and PAM were detected. The clear products were collected and dried in a desiccator. The reason for calling the preparation method a “dynamic template method” was that AM as the assisted template would dynamically react during the growth of the nanoclusters.
2.2 Characterizations
The structures of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters were characterized by X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) using a Shimadzu XD-3A diffractometer. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (Shimadzu ESCA-3400, Mg Kα radiation) measurements were carried out so as to evaluate the surface electronic state and to analyze the surface atom of the sample. The microstructures and morphologies were analyzed by Philips S-4800 scan electron microscopy (SEM). The optical properties of the products were studied by Fourier conversion infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR). UV-vis absorption spectrum was investigated at room temperature (25 °C) by using a UV-2450 (Shimadzu) spectrometer.
2.3 Photocatalytic degradation experiment
0.2 g of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters were added into 50 mL of 2 × 10−5 g L−1 Rhodamine B (Rh. B) solution and then exposed under a mercury lamp (500 W) and sunlight (the photodegradation reaction time was limited between 9:00 am and 3:00 pm, and the temperature was about 25 °C). The clear liquid of the Rh. B solution was extracted to be analyzed by using a UV-2450 (Shimadzu) spectrometer after the sonication and centrifugation procedures of the solution were carried out to eliminate the influence of the adsorption of dye molecules on the structure of the Ag2MoO4 products. The UV-vis absorption spectra of the samples taken as the time changed were tested at room temperature (25 °C).
2.4 Antibacterial experiment
To assess the antibacterial activity of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters, DH 5α bacteria were cultured in Luria–Bertani (LB) liquid medium at 37 °C with shaking until the medium optical density (O.D.600) reached 0.3 to 0.6. Then the bacteria were collected by centrifugation at 5000 rpm for 10 min. The pellet was then resuspended gently with the fresh LB medium (where O.D.600 = 0.5). 3 mL of the resuspended bacteria were pipetted in a 15 mL tube, after adding Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. The bacteria were cultured at 37 °C with shaking, and O.D.600 was detected at different culturing times (1 h, 2 h, 3 h, 4 h, 6 h, 8 h and 10 h) with a spectrophotometer.
3. Results and discussions
3.1 Morphology and structure

Fig. 1 shows the SEM images of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters prepared by a dynamic template method. A large amount of nanoclusters could be observed under low magnification as shown in Fig. 1a. Flower-like clusters of which the size was about 1.2 μm are shown in Fig. 1b. The units forming the clusters were the nanoparticles with a size of about 50 nm on average (Fig. 1c). Compared with general Ag2MoO4 micron-sized materials, Ag2MoO4 clusters possessed a larger specific surface area, which enabled the material to be potentially applied in the field related to surface activity. Meanwhile, the micron-sized materials could be separated from the solution more easily than the nanomaterials, which made the recovery process more convenient in the industrialized application.
 Fig. 1  SEM images of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters under different microscopic dimensions. (a) Low magnification; (b) high magnification; (c) detailed depiction. 
The XRD pattern of the obtained Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters is presented in Fig. 2. The nanocrystalline phase analysis was carried out by X-ray diffraction studies. The diffraction peaks matched well with the standard XRD data of Ag2MoO4 with the spinel structure (JCPDS, no: 08-0473).48 The highest intensity of the diffraction peak at a 2θ value of 31.84° corresponded to the (311) nanocrystal face. The well observed planes for the Ag2MoO4 structure (viz. 111, 220, 311, 222, 400, 331, 422, 511, 440, 533, 622, and 731) agreed with the main standard nanocrystal faces. The space group was Fdm and each cubic nanocrystal cell contained 8 units with cell parameters of 9.3 Å. Sharp peak intensity and well resolved peaks in XRD patterns proved complete nanocrystallization of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. No other small satellite peaks verified the pure phase of the sample.
 Fig. 2  The XRD pattern of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. 
3.2 Composition analysis
The chemical compositions of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters were analyzed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) to further understand the forms of each element. The survey indicated the presence of the elements Mo, Ag and O. High resolution scanning XPS spectra of the Ag 3d, O 1s and Mo 3d lines are shown in Fig. 3a–c. In Fig. 3a, the electron binding energies of Ag 3d5/2 and Ag 3d3/2 were 365.43 eV and 371.38 eV, respectively, which were similar to that measured for AgI in silver oxides (Ag2O).49 A gap of 5.95 eV indicated that the Ag ion valence was high. In Fig. 3b, the oxygen on the surface of the sample existed in two forms with binding energies of 527.8 eV and 528.7 eV. The shoulder peak at 528.7 eV was attributed to the presence of the carboxylic group.50 In Fig. 3c, the electron binding energies of Mo 3d5/2 and Mo 3d3/2 were 229.28 eV and 232.43 eV, which were similar to that of pure α-MoO3.51 The XPS characterization clearly showed the elementary composition of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters, which further ensured the pure phase of the products.
 Fig. 3  The XPS spectra of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters: (a) Ag 3d5/2 and Ag 3d3/2; (b) O 1s; (c) Mo 3d5/2 and Mo 3d3/2. 
3.3 Optical properties
The FT-IR spectrum of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters is presented in Fig. 4a. The characteristic peak at 3380 cm−1 was assigned to the O–H stretching vibration of intermolecular hydrogen bonding. The peak at 1650 cm−1 was assigned to the H–O–H group. The stretching vibration of O–Mo–O was shown at the peak of 830 cm−1.50 As can be seen from the UV-vis absorption spectrum (Fig. 4b), Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters had excellent absorption covering the ultraviolet region (300–400 nm) and the full visible region in the solar spectrum region (400–700 nm). The maximum absorption peaks were at 348 nm and 420 nm, where the intensity reached 1.2 and 0.8, respectively. The FT-IR spectrum clearly showed the favourable optical properties of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters.45
 Fig. 4  The FT-IR spectrum (a) and UV-vis spectrum (b) of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. 
3.4 Formation mechanism

3.4.1 The effect of eggshell membrane. 
The Ag2MoO4 samples prepared by the template method (with the presence of an eggshell membrane template) were compared with the Ag2MoO4 samples prepared by the direct precipitation method (without the presence of an eggshell membrane template). Both experiments added no AM to eliminate the influence of the assisted templates. Aging time was fixed at 24 h. The products were obtained after being washed and centrifuged. Fig. 5a shows the SEM image of the Ag2MoO4 crystals prepared by the template method. The products were regular dendritic nanocrystals in a large dimension. The morphology of Ag2MoO4 prepared by the direct precipitation method was the micro-sized particles as shown in Fig. 5b. The different morphologies of the two samples obviously showed that the eggshell membrane played an important role during the growth process.
 Fig. 5  The SEM images of Ag2MoO4 nanocrystals prepared by (a) the template method and (b) the direct precipitation method. 
The eggshell membrane was a microporous network with pore diameters of 1.5–10 μm composed of protein fibers with an average diameter of about 2 μm (Fig. 6). It mainly contained collagen, glycoprotein, and proteoglycan. The latter two macromolecules contained ionic hydrophilic and hydrophobic domains.47 As the template, the eggshell membrane was a natural microfluidic reactor in which molecular diffusion effect played the main role in chemical reaction. Reynolds number (Re) could be used to characterize the flow characteristic.52 Re = ρVdc/μ(1)where ρ is the fluid density, V is the mean velocity, dc is the channel diameter, and μ is the fluid viscosity. Under the gravity drive and the control of the micron-sized channel, the value of Re was very small, which resulted in the molecular diffusion being the main transport of reaction ions. Therefore, the eggshell membrane offered a physical site in the channels where two kinds of reaction ions met, reacted and aggregated to form Ag2MoO4 crystal nuclei and then grew into Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. As can be seen in Fig. 7a–b, the Ag2MoO4 crystal nuclei were formed on the surface of the eggshell membrane. Secondly, the eggshell membrane could control the transport of ions due to its semi-permeable structure and the macromolecules on the surface of the curving hole channels of the eggshell membrane would have an effect on the morphology of the crystal.53 The reason for the formation of a dendritic Ag2MoO4 nanocrystal was as follows: without the influence of the assisted organic templates, the atom deposition was limited on strongly bound facets and allowed on weakly bound ones.54,55 The structure of the eggshell membrane would adjust the reaction ions to adsorb on particular crystal faces. Meanwhile, netted protein fibres would limit the growth of defined crystal planes.56,57 Anisotropic growth of nanocrystals was induced. The facets with a slower growth rate would be preserved and exposed in the final products. Finally, the dendritic Ag2MoO4 nanocrystals were formed. Moreover, the polar groups on the surface of the eggshell membrane could interact with additional reagents through hydrogen bonding, consequently controlling the growth of products.58 Therefore, the eggshell membrane as the basic template played an important role in the formation of the Ag2MoO4 samples.
 Fig. 6  The SEM image of the eggshell membrane. 
 Fig. 7  The SEM images of Ag2MoO4 crystal nuclei on the surface of the eggshell membrane. a) Low magnification; (b) high magnification. 


3.4.2 The effect of different assisted templates. 
The eggshell membrane was applied as the basic template. 0.05 g (2 g L−1) of PAM and 0.05 g (2 g L−1) of AM as the assisted templates were added into the reaction system to analyze the effect of different assisted templates on the morphology of the Ag2MoO4 crystal. Different from the dendritic crystals which were prepared without the presence of assisted templates, particle aggregates and clusters were synthesized under the existence of PAM and AM templates, respectively (Fig. 8a–b). The reason for the agglomeration of products in Fig. 8a was that PAM as the template acted as the bridge to lead the granular nanocrystals to gather together; hence the Ag2MoO4 aggregates were formed. However, the reason for the formation of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters in Fig. 8b was not the structure-directing effect of AM templates. At first, reaction ions would interact with each other and formed nuclei. Nuclei gradually grew into grains. Under the irradiation of sunlight, Ag2MoO4 grains could generate light-absorbing particles and electron transition occurred. These photoproduction electrons on the conduction band (CB) of the Ag2MoO4 grains possessed high activity and had a reaction with the AM adsorbed on the surface of the Ag2MoO4 grains to form highly reactive radicals. PAM was then generated due to the polymerization of AM free radicals. The process of polymerization of PAM has been confirmed according to the previous work, in which the production rate of PAM under different polymerization times was determined by gas chromatography.58 During the process of polymerization, heat energy would be released inducing thermal perturbation.59,60 The energy variation on the surface of the Ag2MoO4 grains enabled active sites to be produced which became the new growth centre to form the new nuclei.61 These nuclei grew into grains and circularly formed the new nuclei on the surface following the same process. The assembly process proceeded persistently until the thermal perturbation vanished. Meanwhile, formed PAM provided the structure-directing effect and the nanoclusters were formed.
 Fig. 8  SEM images of the Ag2MoO4 nanocrystal synthesized by the template method (a) with the presence of 2 g L−1 PAM and (b) with the presence of 2 g L−1 AM. 


3.4.3 The effect of different temperatures. 
With the change in temperature, the formed morphology of the nanocrystal was different. Therefore, the effect of different temperatures on the morphology of the Ag2MoO4 crystal was investigated. Fig. 9 shows the SEM images of the Ag2MoO4 crystal under 5 °C and 50 °C. Products tended to agglomerate with each other and big crystals were formed at low temperature (5 °C), as shown in Fig. 9a. With the increase in temperature, the morphology of Ag2MoO4 crystals changed from nanoclusters to dispersed nanoparticles (Fig. 9b). It was because the elevation of the temperature could intensify the molecular mobility and increased the chance of effective collision according to the collision theory.62 Meanwhile, the influence of thermodynamics could be explained by the Einstein model, in which vibrational energy was the main factor to influence the stability of the products.63 According to Formulas (2) and (3), Ev is the vibrational energy, k is the Boltzmann constant, N is the molecule, θE is the Einstein temperature, and T is the temperature. Ev = 3NkT[θE/2T + θE/T/(eθE/T − 1)] = 1.5NkθE + 3NkθE/(eθ/TE − 1)(2) θE = hv/k(3)
 Fig. 9  The SEM images of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters under different temperatures: (a) 5 °C; (b) 50 °C. 
With the temperature rising, vibrational energy would increase correspondingly. High vibrational energy would cause the assembled clusters to be unstable. The products tended to form the small particles. On the other hand, the increase in temperature would decrease the value of Ksp of Ag2MoO4 crystals according to Formulas (4) and (5). More reaction ions on the crystal surface tended to dissolve instead of the sediment. It would further result in the instability of the micron-sized nanocrystal. Therefore, with the increase in temperature, the Ag2MoO4 crystal tended to form the individual particles instead of the assembled clusters as shown in Fig. 9c. d ln Ksp/dT = dH/RT2(4) ln Ksp(T2)/ln Ksp(T1) = − ΔH(1/T2 − 1/T1)/R(5)where Ksp is the solubility product, T1 and T2 are two different temperatures, H is the enthalpy of solution, and R is the molar gas constant. (The supporting images of Ag2MoO4 crystals prepared at 30 °C and 40 °C are presented in Fig. 1 in the ESI.†)


3.4.4 The effect of the concentration of AM. 
0.02 g, 0.05 g, and 0.1 g of AM assisted templates (i.e., 0.8 g L−1, 2 g L−1, and 4 g L−1, respectively) were added into 25 mL of solution under a temperature of 20 °C to investigate the effect of different concentrations of the AM assisted templates on the morphology of Ag2MoO4 crystals. Fig. 10 shows the SEM images of the obtained Ag2MoO4 nanocrystals. In Fig. 10a, nanospheres with a size of about 100 nm were obtained at the low concentration (0.8 g L−1) of AM assisted templates. When the concentration increased to 4 g L−1 in the system, the obtained products transformed from nanoclusters to dispersed nanoparticles, as shown in Fig. 10b–c. An insufficient amount of acrylamide would result in low heat generated from the polymerization of AM radicals. The number of the active sites generated from the released heat on the surface of the crystal would decrease. It limited the growth of the crystals. The structure-directing effect of formed PAM was of little effect during the process. Meanwhile, the AM covering the surface of the nuclei tended to make the crystals to form spheres (Fig. 10a). When excessive AM assisted templates were added into the system, the coating effect of the templates would intensify and prevent the attachment of nuclei on the surface of the crystals. Meanwhile, polymerization and nucleation of more Ag2MoO4 nanocrystals would be influenced in the reaction liquid,55 which affected the formation and growth of the nanocrystal nuclei, further leading to the dispersed nanocrystal particles (Fig. 10c).
 Fig. 10  The SEM images of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters prepared by the dynamic template method under different concentrations of AM assisted templates: (a) 0.8 g L−1; (b) 2 g L−1; (c) 4 g L−1. 


3.4.5 Growth stages. 
To explain the growth process of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters, the morphologies of Ag2MoO4 products under different aging times were researched. During the initial stage, reaction ions interacted with each other and amorphous crystal nuclei were gradually formed. With the increase in time, nuclei began to grow into grains and agglomerate with each other. When the aging time extended to 12 h, the prototype of the distinct flower-like products was formed. More nuclei grew on the surface of Ag2MoO4 grains and an assembled structure was generated. At the final stage, micron-sized clusters were formed consisting of nanoparticles with a size of 50 nm on average after being aged for 24 h. (The patterns of growth morphology of Ag2MoO4 at different aging times are presented in Fig. 2 in the ESI.†)


3.4.6 The growth mechanism of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. 
The growth mechanism could be given combined with the synergistic influence of the eggshell membrane, AM assisted templates and temperature. During the initiation of the reaction, silver ions and molybdate ions interacted with each other on the eggshell membrane. The formed nuclei tended to aggregate with each other due to the low energy and the adjustment of the eggshell membrane.58 With the increase in time, amorphous nuclei began to grow into grains. During the reaction process, excessive AM assisted templates would cover exceedingly on the surface of the Ag2MoO4 crystals, which restricted the formation of the clusters. Also, low temperature and high temperature in the reaction system would influence the vibrational energy which was the main factor to determine the stability of the nanoclusters. Therefore, 0.05 g of AM assisted templates and a temperature of 20 °C were the optimum reaction conditions. Under the irradiation of sunlight, Ag2MoO4 grains could generate light-absorbing particles and electron transition occurred. These photoproduction electrons on the conduction band (CB) of the Ag2MoO4 grains possessed high activity and had a reaction with the AM adsorbed on the surface of the Ag2MoO4 grains to form highly reactive radicals. PAM was then generated due to the polymerization of AM free radicals. During the process of polymerization, heat energy would be released inducing thermal perturbation. The energy variation on the surface of the Ag2MoO4 grains enabled active sites to be produced which became the new growth centre to form the new nuclei. Following the same process, these nuclei grew into grains and circularly formed the new nuclei on the surface. The assembly process proceeded persistently until the thermal perturbation vanished. Meanwhile, formed PAM provided the structure-directing effect and finally the nanoclusters were formed. The mechanism image is shown in Fig. 11.

3.5 Degradation activity
Considering the particular geometrical morphology, quantum size effects,55 and excellent light response of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters, photocatalysis experiment was performed to show the catalytic activity of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters to the organic dyestuff Rh. B solution. A 500 W mercury lamp (300–500 nm) and sunlight (300–800 nm) were used as the light source. As shown in Fig. 12a–b, Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters could degrade 2 × 10−5 mol L−1 Rh. B solution within 3 h under irradiation of a 500 W mercury lamp while the degradation time extended to 6 h under sunlight (9:00 am–3:00 pm). The temperature in both experiments was fixed at 25 °C. The Ag2MoO4 catalysts were recycled three times and the degradation time was still kept for 3 h and 6 h under the mercury lamp and sunlight, which indicated that Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters possessed favourable reusability (Fig. 12c–d). The reason for the lower degradation efficiency under sunlight was due to the lower absorption of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters in the visible region. But the good reusability enabled the material with the structure of nanoclusters to possess application potential in the field of industrialization.
 Fig. 12  The curves of photocatalytic degradation and recyclable efficiency by Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters: (a, c) 500 W mercury lamp; (b, d) sunlight. 

Fig. 13a–d show the patterns of photocatalytic degradation of Ag2MoO4 samples with different morphologies under the mercury lamp (the power was 500 W). It could be seen that the difference of the morphology influenced the degradation efficiency. Dendritic crystals and granular crystals in Fig. 13a–b were on a micrometer scale; the specific surface area was relatively smaller than the Ag2MoO4 samples in Fig. 13c–d which consisted of nanoparticles. The nanoclusters in Fig. 13d as the particular structure possessed smaller assembled particles than the aggregate as shown in Fig. 13c. It enabled the nanoclusters to have a bigger specific surface area, which added the chance of interaction between the Ag2MoO4 catalysts and the Rh. B molecules. Therefore, the material possessing excellent light response performance and a particular morphology had a possibility of application in the photocatalysis field.
 Fig. 13  The photocatalytic degradation patterns of Rh. B solutions by Ag2MoO4 samples of different morphologies under 500 W light: (a) eggshell membrane as the template; (b) direct precipitation method; (c) eggshell membrane and PAM as the cooperation template; (d) dynamic template method. 
3.6 Sterilization of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters
In order to investigate the bactericidal effect of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters, it was necessary to evaluate the bacterial proliferation rate of DH 5α under the increasing concentration of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. Fig. 14 shows the bacteriostasis curve of the Ag2MoO4 products with different morphologies. The same characteristic each experiment group possessed was that although the DH 5α survived to reproduce during the initial stage of the reaction, growth was inhibited as time went on. Fig. 14d was the bacteriostasis curve of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters. The bacterial proliferation rate gradually decreased with the concentration of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters increasing from 0 to 100 μg mL−1. It indicated that the bacteriostatic activity was observably enhanced. When the concentration reached 100 μg mL−1, the products realized the best bacteriostatic activity, while the same experiments with the same concentration of other Ag2MoO4 products in Fig. 14a–c showed worse performance. Due to the fact that excess production hinders the absorption,58 the bactericidal effect of higher concentration (300 μg mL−1) of Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters was poor. Therefore, appropriate concentration of the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters could have good antibacterial properties.
 Fig. 14  Bacteriostasis curves of Ag2MoO4 products: (a) eggshell membrane as the template; (b) direct precipitation method; (c) eggshell membrane and PAM as the cooperation template; (d) dynamic template method. 
4 Conclusions
In summary, the Ag2MoO4 nanoclusters with high photocatalytic and bacteriostatic activities were successfully prepared by a dynamic template method through thermal perturbation nucleation. The photoproduced electrons in the conduction band of Ag2MoO4 grains could react with AM molecules to form AM radicals with high activity which promoted the formation of PAM through the polymerization process. The released heat perturbed the energy variation on the surface of the grains and further promoted new nuclei to form and grow. The circular process proceeded and formed nuclei assembled together, which induced the nanoclusters to be formed in the end.
