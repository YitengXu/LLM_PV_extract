    , Accepted 10th July 2018First published on 10th July 2018AbstractOrganic photovoltaics (OPVs) represent one of the potential candidates of next-generation solar cells for converting green and sustainable solar energy into electrical power. An OPV cell utilizes a blend of electron donor (D) and electron acceptor (A) materials as photo-active layers, where photogenerated excitons are separated into mobile electrons and holes. Relative to fullerene acceptors, nonfullerene small-molecule acceptors (NF-SMAs) have several advantages such as synthesis-facile chemical modifications and straightforward tunability in absorptivity, spectral coverage, optical band gap, and Frontier molecular orbitals. In the recent 3 years, progress in design and synthesis of fused-ring NF-SMAs with perpendicular side-chains on an electron-rich core, and again, on design and synthesis of wide/medium/low band gap polymer donors have led to realizations of over 13% power conversion efficiencies (PCEs). These rapid advances require timely review articles. 1. Introduction
Studies on organic photovoltaics (OPV) are in their 4th decade since the first invention in 1986 by Tang.1 In an OPV device, electron donor (D) and electron acceptor (A) materials are blended to create a bulk-heterojunction to fulfil efficient exciton separation and transport. Both D and A materials are of equal importance. If we focus on the A materials, then OPV history can be divided into two parts – the fullerene age and the nonfullerene age after 2013. These include a series of interesting wide band gap (WBG) nonfullerene small-molecule acceptors (NF-SMAs); for example, perylene dimide2–8 and other types of electron-deficiency macrocycles.9–12 Reported 3–4% power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) were achieved from these types of nonfullerene acceptors when pairing with low band gap (LBG) polymer donors.13–21 Particular successes came from the invention of the fused-ring NF-SMAs with a ladder-type electron-rich core, such as ITIC – 3,9-bis(2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)indanone))-5,5,11,11-tetrakis(4-n-hexylphenyl)-dithieno[2,3d:2′,3′d′]-s-indaceno[1,2b:5,6b′]dithiophene,22 IEIC – 2,2′-((2Z,2′Z)-((5,5′-(4,4,9,9-tetrakis(p-hexylphenyl)-4,9-dihydro-s-indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b′]dithiophene-2,7-diyl)bis(3-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophene-5,2-diyl))bis(methanylylidene))-bis(3-oxo-2,3-dihydro-1H-indene-2,1-diylidene))dimalononitrile),23 and rhodanine flanked IDTBR.24 When pairing with wide/medium/low band gap polymer donors, over 13% PCEs25–27 have been reported very recently, which makes OPVs to be a candidate competitive with other types of next-generation solar cells. It has been speculated that if the non-radiation recombination Voc (open-circuit voltage) loss can be reduced to about 0.21 V or the energy loss can be reduced to about 0.5 eV, then PCE with over 18% has a very high possibility within reach for OPVs.28
Although a large step has been achieved, successes with OPVs still largely lagged behind their perovskite counterparts from which over 20% PCEs have been straightforwardly realized. To further improve OPV performance, design and synthesis of new acceptor and donor materials are of particular importance. Relative to recent advances on NF-SMA, several critical reviews have been published including energy loss,29 physics,30,31 molecular design,28,32–39 and full reviews. Additional reviews have been published on charge generation pathways31 and all types of nonfullerene acceptors.40–43 Very rapid advances are being achieved in this field and this requires a timely review article. In this review article, we focus on successes achieved from the design and synthesis of fused-ring type NF-SMAs which involve an electron-rich core with side-chains perpendicular to its backbone plane and, in particular, achievements published in the past 3 years. In this article, we will shed light on the advances on their molecular structures and the relationship between structure and OPV parameters of Voc, Jsc (short-circuit current-density), and FF (fill-factor). Certainly, the pairing D materials are also included.
2. D:A energy levels alignments and spectral coverage complement
The active layer of an OPV cell is made of a blend of D and A materials. Both D and A materials absorb solar photons and the photogenerated D and A excitons are separated via an electron transfer (path A, Fig. 1a) and hole transfer (path B) under driving forces provided by the D–A lowest unoccupied molecular orbit (LUMO) energy offset, ΔELUMO,D–A, and the D–A highest occupied molecular orbit (HOMO) energy offset, ΔEHOMO,D–A, respectively. To fulfil efficient charge separations, the D–A energy levels must be aligned. Because organic semiconductors intrinsically show a narrowed absorption band, complementary spectral coverage between D and nonfullerene acceptor (NF-A) is generally required (Fig. 1b) to more fully cover the solar emission spectrum. Accordingly, LBG D vs. WBG NF-A (nonfullerene acceptor) or WBG D vs. LBG NF-A combinations are generally selected though there are cases reported with the D and A materials having close values of optical band gap (Eoptg).
 Fig. 1  Diagrams on the energy level alignment (a) and complementary spectral coverage requirements (b) between the electron donor (D) and nonfullerene acceptor (NF-A) materials. 
A > 0.3 eV of D:A LUMO energy offset is generally required to efficiently drive the electron transfer, although it has been observed that a hole transfer can be still efficient even if the D:NF-A HOMO energy offset is smaller than 0.3 eV when the WBG donor is blended with a fused-ring NF-A.44–49

Fig. 2 illustrates hybridization of the LUMO–LUMO and HOMO–HOMO of the electron-donating (D) and electron-accepting (A) units in a D–A conjugated molecule which generates new Frontier molecular orbitals of an acceptor molecule.
 Fig. 2  Diagrams of the hybridizations of LUMO and HOMO between the electron-donating and -accepting units and formation of the Frontier molecular orbitals of an acceptor molecule. 
As shown in Fig. 3, density functional theory (DFT) calculations indicated that the central IDTT (dithieno[2,3d:2′,3′d′]-s-indaceno[1,2b:5,6b′]dithiophene)50 unit contributes to both ITIC LUMO and HOMO. Again, the IC methylene, oxygen atom, and even the dicyanomethylene-nitrogen and carbon also contribute, albeit not much, to the ITIC LUMO and HOMO. However, the IC fused-benzene rings contribute only to the ITIC LUMO and large distribution on the ITIC LUMO which is seen at the dicyanomethylene-nitrogen and carbon. DFT calculations suggest that chemical modifications on both D and A units can effectively tune the acceptor LUMO and HOMO energies and its energy gap. Otherwise, the fused benzene-ring can affect the ITIC HOMO through a “push–pull” effect and, therefore, substitutions on the fused-benzene or replacements of the benzene-ring with other hetero-rings can effectively tune the acceptor molecule HOMO energy level and optical property of that molecule, as shown in the later part of this review. Since the side chains can modulate π–π-interactions, side chain engineering is another way to tune optical property and Frontier molecular orbital energy levels. The fused-ring NF-SMAs are generally constructed in an A–D–A or A–π–D–π–A type with the ladder-type D and A covalently linked via single carbon–carbon bonds or through π-bridges. Therefore, a π-bridge is another place which can be modified to tune the optical property and Frontier molecular orbital energy levels for A–π–D–π–A type acceptors.

3. Crystal structure
DFT calculations clearly indicate that the p-hexylphenyls at the 4,4,9,9-positions of IDTT are positioned perpendicular to the ITIC backbone plane, therefore, preventing the ITIC molecules from forming H-type aggregates, while preferring to push the acceptor molecules to form J-aggregates (Fig. 4).
 Fig. 4  (a) Illuminations of the 3-types of IC–IC π–π stacks with different backbone–backbone distances. (b) Diagrams of typical cluster-like and chain-like molecular aggregates. With permissions from ref. 51 Royal Society of Chemistry. Copyright© 2017, Royal Society of Chemistry. 
Yi et al. reported the ITIC crystal structure, from which it is clearly seen that the ITIC molecules are packed and driven by the IC–IC π–π-stacks by which the ITIC molecules form cluster-like and chain-like aggregates, favourable for electron transport with the ITIC phase.51
Jen et al. reported the crystal structure 6TIC-4F. The crystal structure indicated that the 6TIC-4F backbone is wave-like shaped and the confirmation that the molecule is locked through IC–O⋯S-thienyl interactions. The 6TIC-4F molecules packed and were crosswise arranged via intermolecular π–π-interactions between IC-4F and the outmost TT units.52
4. A–D–A type NF-SMAs with a fused-ring ladder-type core
4.1. ITIC and studies on new donor materials
Since its invention by Zhan et al., ITIC has been used as NF-SMA to study photovoltaic properties of different polymers and small-molecule donor materials (Table 1). Initially, a PCE of 6.80% was obtained with PTB7-Th as the pairing donor. That low PCE is likely related to the spectral overlap of the PTB7-Th (poly[[2,6′-4,8-di(5-ethylhexylthienyl)benzo[1,2-b;3,3-b]dithiophene][3-fluoro-2[(2-ethylhexyl)carbonyl]thieno[3,4-b]thiophenediyl]])53 and ITIC absorption.22 Hou et al. selected PBDB-T, a WBG polymer donor, to pair with ITIC, and obtained a recorded PCE of 11.3% in 2016.54,55 The Voc from the PBDB-T:ITIC system was 0.9 V. Here, PBDB-T was poly[(2,6-(4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene)-co-(1,3-di(5-thiophene-2-yl)-5,7-bis(2-ethylhexyl)benzo[1,2-c:4,5-c′]dithiophene-4,8-dione).56 Recently, Zhan et al. reported a PCE of 10.3% by using a binary blended small-molecule electron-transporting layer (ETL) because a binary blended ETL is able to tune the Al electrode work function more effectively than a single small molecule so that better ohmic contact between the active layer and Al cathode is achieved.57 Li et al. reported a Voc of 0.94 V and a PCE of 11.41% with J71 as the pairing polymer donor material due to the lower-lying HOMO energy level of J71 than PBDB-T.45 Hou et al. achieved a Voc of 0.95 V and a PCE of 11.34% by reducing the ΔEHOMO,D–A.58 Peng et al. reported a Voc/PCE of 0.92 V/11.47%, 1.01 V/11.12%, and 1.10 V/12.80% by using a quinoxalino[6,5-f]quinoxaline,59 4-fluoro-5-alkylthiothienyl-substituted benzodithiophene-difluorobenzothiazole,60 and 1,3,4-thiadiazole (TDZ)-benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (BDT)61 based copolymer as the donor material, respectively. Typically, through tuning the donor material's HOMO energy level via modifying the polymer's backbone or the side chains, a Voc of 0.80–1.10 and a PCE of 7–13% have been reported (Table 1). For the design of polymer donor materials and their related optical, electrochemical, and photovoltaic properties, readers can read recent review articles for getting more information.62 Ma et al. fabricated PBDB-T:ITIC solar cells with a blade-coating technique under ambient conditions and reported PCEs of 10.03 ± 0.17%.63

The LUMO and HOMO energy levels of ITIC are −4.02 and −5.61 eV as measured with an acetonitrile solution64 and −3.97 and −5.66 eV measured with a chloroform solution.65 The small differences in the Frontier molecular orbitals energies are related to polarity difference of the solvent used for cyclic voltammetry (CV). To tune the Frontier molecular orbits energy levels to get larger Voc, Jsc, and/or FF, chemical modifications have been made on terminal A, central D, and side chains (Fig. 5). Obtaining insightful knowledge and target-directed designing by precisely modifying the A, D, and side chains are one way to obtain targeted optical, electrochemical, and photovoltaic properties.
 Fig. 5  Chemical structures of some A–D–A type fused-ring NF-SMAs with modifications on the electron-donating and -accepting units as well as on the side chains. 
4.2. Modifications on the terminal A unit
Modifications on the terminal A units were first made on the IC (2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)indanone)) unit. Hou et al. found that introduction of one and two methyl units at 5 and 6 positions upshifted the LUMO/HOMO energies from −4.02/−5.61 eV (ITIC) to −3.98/−5.58 eV (IT-M) and −3.93/−5.56 eV (IT-DM), which led to a larger Voc: 0.94 and 0.97 V for IT-M and IT-DM, respectively, when pairing with PBDB-T. Among the three SMAs, the best film-morphology was obtained from the PBDB-T:IT-M system, which gave rise to the largest Jsc and FF, and hence, the highest PCE of 12.2%.64,66 Huang et al. introduced cyclobutane on the 5,6-positions of IC and synthesized the acceptor IDT-HN which gave a Voc of 0.93 V and an FF of 0.764 with a PCE of 10.22%.67 When a stronger electron-donating unit such as methoxyl was introduced at the 5 and 6 positions, the LUMO/HOMO energy levels of the resulting SMAs then were tuned to −3.80/−5.52 eV (IT-OM3, at the 5 position) and −3.86/−5.49 eV (IT-OM2, at the 6 position), respectively. A Voc of 0.93 and 0.97 V was obtained with PBDB-T as the donor. The IT-OM2 exhibited smaller π–π-stacking distance, larger coherence crystalline length, and larger electron mobility, which was attributed to the larger Jsc and FF.68
Oppositely, introduction of an electron-withdrawing unit, for example, fluorine atoms at the 5,6-positions of the fused-benzene of the IC unit, led to a reduction of the acceptor Eoptg, by which the SMA more fully covered the near infrared solar emission spectrum, which was helpful to obtain a larger Jsc. However, this approach also shifted down the LUMO level and a reduction of Voc was generally observed. Zhan et al. reported that the introduction of fluorine at the IC's 4, 5, or 6, and both 5 and 6 positions in sequence down shifted the SMA's LUMO/HOMO energies, going from −3.88/−5.45 eV (without any F substitutions) to −3.97/−5.54 eV (one F at 4 position), −3.98/−5.52 eV (IC-F), and −4.02/−5.52 eV (IC-2F), and meanwhile, the Eoptg was reduced. With FTAZ69 as a polymer donor, the device Voc reduced from 0.957 to 0.929, 0.903, and 0.857 V, while the Jsc increased from 13.51 to 16.63, 17.56, and 19.44 mA cm−2, and finally a PCE of 11% was obtained from the IC-2F-based acceptor system.70 Hou et al. reported the acceptor IT-4F which was synthesized by covalently linking IDTT and IC-2F. PCEs of 13% (ref. 25) and 13.3% (ref. 26) were reported from IT-4F with PBDB-T-SF and PBDB-TF (a conjugated polymer of 4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)-4-fluorothiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]-dithiophene and 1,3-bis(thiophen-2-yl)-5,7-bis(2-ethylhexyl)benzo-[1,2-c:4,5-c′]dithiophene-4,8-dione)71 as the polymer donor, respectively. Additionally, Li et al. used a PBDB-T analogue, PM7, with 4-chloro-5-(2-ethylhexylthienyl) as the benzodithiophene (BDT) side chains, and reported a PCE of 12.6% with a Voc of 0.88 V and a Jsc of 20.1 mA cm−2.72 When pairing with a deeper-HOMO polymer donor, PDTB-EF-T (a 2,4,6,8-tetra(thiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (DTBDT) based polymer) (EHOMO = −5.50 eV, Eoptg = 1.93 eV), a Voc of 0.898 V, a Jsc of 20.77 mA cm−2, a FF of 0.75, and a PCE of 14.0% was reported by Hou et al.73 Substituents at the IC-5,6-positions with one or two chlorine atoms yielded IC-Cl or IC-2Cl. Conjugation of IC-Cl and IC-2Cl with IDTT afforded the acceptors of IT-2Cl and IT-4Cl, respectively. When using PBDB-T-2F as the polymer donor, the fullerene-free PSCs with IT-2Cl and IT-4Cl as the blended acceptor, respectively, exhibited a Voc of 0.922 and 0.790 V, a Jsc of 19.08 and 22.67 mA cm−2, and a PCE of 13.16% and 13.45%.74
Replacement of the fused-benzene ring on IC is another way to tune the optoelectronic properties. The naphthalene-fused SMA exhibited a much smaller Eoptg than the benzene fused acceptor, due to its relatively lower-lying LUMO and higher-lying HOMO. Its solar cell devices showed much larger Jsc and FF because of its stronger π–π-interactions and more ordered face-on orientation75,76 and gave rise to a PCE of 12% (ref. 76) when using PBDB-TF as the donor. Interesting results were found from the thiophene-fused SMAs. Hou et al. synthesized the cyclopenta[b]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CT[b]) and the related SMA conjugated with IDTT unit, ITCC.77 Almost at the same time, Yang's group, Zou's group, and Zhan's group reported the synthesis of cyclopenta[c]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CT[c]) and the related SMA also conjugated with a IDTT unit, which was named as ITCT, or ITCPTC, or ITTC.65,78,79 ITCC showed a higher-lying LUMO and HOMO as well as a larger Eoptg than ITIC and the device with PBDB-T as the donor exhibited a higher Voc, while a smaller Jsc. Oppositely, ITCT showed enhanced quinoidal character, which down shifted the LUMO energy level and reduced the optical band gap, related to ITIC, and the device also with PBDB-T as the donor had a larger Jsc and FF, but a little smaller Voc.65 Nevertheless, both ITCC and ITCT gave rise to a PCE of >11%. Sun et al. again paired ITCT with PBT1-C with a HOMO energy of −5.43 eV and reported a PCE of 12.5% with a Voc of 0.94 V and an FF of 0.76.80,81
Hou et al. introduced a methyl on the 5-position of the fused-thiophene of CT[b] and synthesized ITCC-M which was paired with PBDB-T as the front sub-cell to fabricate a tandem cell with PBDTTT-EF-T:IEICO as the back sub-cell, showing a PCE of 13.9%.82 Yang et al. reported that introduction of a methyl at the 2-position of the CT[c] unit slightly upshifted the LUMO and HOMO of the resulting SMA, and a higher Voc and Jsc with a PCE of 12.1% was enabled from the cell with J71 as the donor.83 Cheng et al. reported the cyclopenta[b]thieno[3,2-b]thiophen-4-one-5-methylene-6-(1,1-dicyanomethylene) (CTT[b]) and synthesized the acceptor BDCPDT-TTC by conjugating CTT[b] with benzodi(cyclopentadithiophene) (BDCPDT). This acceptor had a LUMO energy of −3.78 eV and showed a Voc of 0.94 V with PBDB-T as the donor.84
If the IC terminals were replaced with weaker A units such as N,N′-diethyl thiobarbituric acid, then the Eoptg was shifted to 2.0 eV due to the much higher-lying LUMO and the solar cell with PTB7-Th as the donor had a Voc of 1.01 V.85
4.3. Modifications on the central D unit
Indaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b′]dithiophene (IDT)86 is another ladder-type D unit reported in 2008. A conjugated SMA with IDT and IC was reported by Zhan et al. which showed a Voc of 0.89 V and a PCE of 8.71% pairing with PBDB-T1. Its LUMO and HOMO levels were estimated to be −3.91 and −5.69 eV when measured with the acetonitrile solution, which was a little higher than the ITIC LUMO and a little lower than the ITIC HOMO energy, due to the relatively weaker electron-donating ability of IDT than IDTT.87,88 IDIC is another NF-SMA used for paring with newly synthesized polymer or small-molecule donors. Use of PTFBDT-BZS as the WBG polymer donor, a higher Voc than the PBDB-T1:IDIC system with a PCE of 11% was reported by Zhan et al.89 Recently, Li et al. reported that the pairing of IDIC with a newly synthesized WBG polymer donor, PTQ10, whose LUMO/HOMO energy levels were −2.98 and −5.54 eV, exhibited a PCE of 12.3% and a Voc of 0.97 V.90 Tang et al. reported a PCE of 13% from a FTAZ:IDIC system by using a nanostructured Al2O3 doped ZnO composite film as the cathode interlayer of the inverted cell.91 Yang et al. reported that a thin film of IDIC spin-coated atop the cathode interlayer before the deposition of the fullerene-free binary system enabled improving the device's performance.92 Other studies with IDIC as the blended acceptor material can be found in Table 1.
IDICs also have been utilized to fabricate some all small-molecule solar cells (all SMSCs).93,94 The Voc achieved from the “All SMSCs” devices were observed to increase with the lower-lying of the HOMO energies of the small molecule donor materials, in sequences of 0.84 V for DRBDT-TVT (−5.14 eV)95 < 0.90 V for a BDT-based small-molecule (SM1) (−5.24 eV)96 < 0.92 V for NDTSR (−5.25 eV)97 < 0.96 V for a difluoro-benzotriazole (2F-BT) based small-molecule (H11) (−5.31 eV)98 < 0.99 V for DRTB-T (−5.51 eV).99 The Jsc observed from these studies was about 15 mA cm−2 and the FF was about 0.65.
Yang et al. conjugated IDT with CT[c] and synthesized IDTPC whose fullerene-free device pairing with PTQ10 exhibited a PCE of 12.2% with a Voc of 0.93 V, a Jsc of 17.5 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.746.100
BDT101 is widely used for designing high-efficiency polymer and small-molecule donors.102 The conjugation of BDT with thiophene via the cyclopentane ring made a new electron-rich core structure BDCPDT. Its SMA conjugated with IC showed a higher-lying LUMO (about −3.83 eV) and also HOMO (about −5.41 eV) and a smaller Eg of 1.54 eV than ITIC and about 10% PCEs were obtained with PBDB-T as the donor.49,103 Forrest et al. reported that this acceptor gave rise to a smaller Voc but a larger Jsc than ITIC when pairing with J71.104 Thiophene is less aromatic so the incorporation of thiophene is helpful to enhance the quinoidal character of the molecule. Electron donating capability increases in sequences – benzene < thiophen (T) < thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (TT) < dithieno[3,2-b:2′,3′-d]thiophene (DTT). Stronger D units are available by fusing between T, TT, and DTT, for example, 4T fused between T and TT and 6T fused between TT and TT (Fig. 2). The Eoptg of the SMAs (IHIC/6TIC) conjugated between 4T and IC/IC-F had an Eoptg of 1.38/1.35 eV.105–108 Zhan et al. reported that the 6T and IC-F conjugated SMA (FOIC) exhibited a much higher-lying HOMO than the IDTT and IC-F conjugated one (−5.36 vs. −5.54 eV) and a much smaller Eoptg (1.32 vs. 1.55 eV), by which a much larger Jsc was achieved (23.5 vs. 17.0 mA cm−2) when blended with PTB7-Th.109 When the 6T was conjugated with IC-2F, an even smaller Eoptg of 1.27 eV was obtained for the resulting SMA (F8IC), due to the even lower-lying LUMO, and an even larger Jsc of 25 mA cm−2 was reported. However, the Voc was reduced to 0.636 V and the PCE was only 10.5%.110 Jen et al. blended this acceptor with PBDB-T and reported a PCE of 10.63%.52 Fusion of two thiophene rings with IDTT through cyclopentane afforded a new core structure, IU, and then conjugation of IU with IC-2F afforded an acceptor with an Eoptg of 1.42 eV, which exhibited a Voc of 0.79 V, a higher value than F8IC, and a Jsc of 21.5 mA cm−2 with PTB7-Th as the donor.111
Yang et al. synthesized pyran-bridged indacenodithiophene (PDT) with n-hexylphenyl as the side chains and reported its SMA (PTIC) with IC as the terminal A units. The LUMO and HOMO energies of PTIC were of −3.85 and −5.67 eV with an Eoptg of 1.55 eV. The device with PBDB-T as the donor exhibited a Voc of 0.84 V and a Jsc of 14.2 mA cm−2.112 Zhu et al. reported another acceptor, NBDTP, by conjugating this electron-rich core (with 2-ethylhexyl as the side chains) with CT[b], which gave rise to a Voc of 0.937 V and a Jsc of 15.72 mA cm−2.113 In another work, Wang et al. synthesized an octyl substituted core and conjugated it with IC-M. The resulting acceptor, DTDP-MIC, exhibited a Voc of 0.80 V and a Jsc of 16.77 mA cm−2 also using PBDB-T as the donor.114 Ding et al. used a C–O bond to replace the IDT's methylene and synthesized a new D unit, COi8. The 2H-pyran based D unit exhibited much stronger electron-donating capability than the IDT. Its SMA conjugated with IC-2F and gave rise to a much smaller Eg (1.26 eV) and its solar cell pairing with PTB7-Th gave a Jsc of 26 mA cm−2.115 Tang and Zhang et al. reported indacenobis(dithieno[3,2-b:2′,3′-d]pyrrol) (INP) and its SMA conjugated with IC-2F (INPIC-4F), whose Eoptg was 1.39 eV and its LUMO/HOMO energies were −3.94/−5.42 eV. A solar cell of PBDB-T:INPIC-4F had a PCE of 13% with a Voc of 0.85 V and a Jsc of 21.6 mA cm−2.27 Zhu et al. used benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b0]bis(dithienosilole) (FDTSB)116 as the core and IC-2F as the terminal A units and reported an acceptor that showed an Eoptg of 1.40 eV and a Voc of 0.744 V, a Jsc of 20.1 mA cm−2.117
Oppositely, incorporation of benzene helps to raise the LUMO and get a higher Voc. Zhu et al. based on an indacenodibenzothiophene (IDBT) core synthesized a WBG A–D–A type NF-SMA, NIDBT, which exhibited an Eoptg of 1.96 eV. This device showed a Voc of 0.83 V and a PCE of 4.3% with PTB7-Th as the donor.118 Chen et al. reported the synthesis of cyclopenta[2′′,1′′:5,6;3′′,4′′:5′,6′]diindeno[1,2-b:1′,2′-b′]dithiophene (CDID) and its SMA with IC as the terminal A units (FDICTF). The LUMO energy of FDICTF was −3.71 eV and its Eoptg was 1.72 eV. The device showed a Voc of 0.94 V with PBDB-T as the donor.119 On the basis of this electron-rich core, they further observed that SMAs with bromo-substituted IC (F-Br) as the A units had a larger Jsc than the chloro- and fluoro-IC.120 Liao et al. reported that with a indacenodithiophenoindacenodithiophene (IDTIDT) core and its SMA conjugated with IC ends, compared to IDIC, resulted in both LUMO and HOMO energies being raised, while its Eoptg was reduced, and, both larger Voc and Jsc were obtained when pairing with PTB7-Th.121 They again synthesized indacenodithiopheno-indacenodiselenophene (IDTIDSe) and its acceptor, IDTIDSe-IC, which showed a Voc of 0.91 V with J51 as the donor.122 Hsu et al. reported a core structure of dithienocyclopentacarbazole (DTCC) and its acceptor with IC as the terminal A units, whose LUMO energy was of −3.65 eV. When pairing with PBDB-T, a Voc of 0.97 V was obtained.123 Zhu et al. reported the electron-rich core of dithienocyclopentaindenoindene (FRII) and its acceptor of ZITI with IC-F as the A units, showing a LUMO energy of −3.74 eV. When paired with J71, a Voc of 0.93 V, a Jsc of 20 mA cm−2, and a PCE of 13% were obtained.124
Naphthalene is another unit used for constructing electron-rich central D units. A thiophene can be fused through thiophen-2-yl and naphthalene-1,5-di-yl or naphthalene-2,6-di-yl positions, affording ladder-type dithienonaphthalene (DTN) or iso-DTN. Zheng et al. reported the synthesis of DTN and its SMA with IC as the A units (DTNIC8). The PBDB-T:DTNIC8 system showed a Voc of 0.96 V.125 Chen et al. reported the synthesis of iso-DTN and its acceptors NTIC126 which showed a PCE of 8.63% and O-NTIC127 which had a PCE of 11%, both pairing with PBDB-T. Another isomer with thiophene fused on the 2,3- and 6,7-positions (p-DTN) has been also reported.128 Zhan et al. synthesized a naphthodithiophene based core structure of naphtho[1,2-b:5,6-b′]di-4H-cyclopenta[2,1-b:3,4-b′]dithiophene (NTT) and its SMA IOIC2 by conjugating with IC-2F. Compared to a DTN based acceptor, IOIC2 exhibited higher lying LUMO and HOMO as well as a smaller Eoptg and this fullerene free device showed a Voc of 0.898 V, a Jsc of 19.4 mA cm−2 with FTAZ as the donor.129 Zou et al. synthesized dithieno[3,2-b]pyrrolobenzotriazole (BZTP) based on benzotriazole. Its SMA conjugated with IC (BZIC) showed a LUMO energy of −3.88 eV and the solar cell with HFQx-T exhibited a PCE of 6.3%.130 Jen et al. reported a perylene based electron-rich core, dithienopicenocarbazole (DTPC). The LUMO/HOMO energies of the resulting SMA with IC-2F as the electron-withdrawing units were estimated to be −4.10/−5.31 eV with a Eoptg of 1.21 eV and the solar cell showed a PCE of 10.21% and a Jsc of 21.9 mA cm−2. The Voc was about 0.76 V.131
Sun et al. reported an asymmetric core structure, 4,9-dihydro-s-indaceno[1,2-b]thiophene-alt-[5,6-d]thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (IDT-alt-TT), that was synthesized by fusing benzene with thiophene and thieno[3,2-b]thiophene, respectively.186 The noted feature of this asymmetric core is that the covalent linking of the ending A units leads to a bent shape which helps to give rise to a molecular dipole moment.187 Sun et al. selected IC as the A units and the resulting acceptor showed comparable performance to ITIC when pairing with PBT1-C.186 Yang et al. selected CT[c] and IC-M as the A units, respectively, and observed a larger molecular dipole moment by using IC-M than CT[c]. The IC-M based acceptor (IDT6CN-M) showed better device performance than the CT[c] based molecule.187
4.4. Side chains engineering
In ITIC, the side chains attached at the 4,4,9,9-positions are para-hexylphenyl units. Li et al. reported m-ITIC in which the side chains are meta-hexylphenyl groups. Their studies indicated that m-ITIC showed a higher film absorption coefficient, a larger crystalline coherence, and higher electron mobility than ITIC and solar cells with J61 (2D-conjugated benzodithiophene-alt-fluorobenzotriazole copolymer with alkylthio side-chain) as the donor exhibited a PCE of 11.5% with a Jsc of 18.3 mA cm−2.138 Further with the use of J91 (a copolymer with bi(alkyldifluorothienyl)-benzodithiophene as the D unit) as the donor, the device showed a Voc of 0.984 V due to the lower-lying HOMO (−5.50 eV) of the polymer.47 Again, Yang et al. reported 11% efficiency with a random donor polymer based on thieno[2′,3′:5′,6′]pyrido[3,4-g]thieno[3,2-c]isoquinoline-5,11(4H,10H)-dione (TPTI) containing two simple thiophene (T) and bithiophene (2T) electron-rich moieties (PTTI-T70).188
With conjugation of meta-hexylphenyl-IDTT with IC-F/IC-2F Li et al. reported m-ITIC-2F/m-ITIC-4F both of which showed a PCE of 12.03% pairing with PTQ10.190 Yang et al. synthesized a meta-hexylphenyl based SMA with CT[c]-M as the terminal A units, whose device with J71 as the donor showed a PCE of 12%.191 Gao et al. synthesized the acceptors by inserting oxy between the phenyl and the para-n-hexyl at either ITIC or m-ITIC and reported comparable performance when pairing with PTB7-Th.192 Li et al. also observed that the MOITM with meta-hexyloxylphenyl side chains exhibited larger Jsc than the para-hexyloxylphenyl substituted counterpart.193 Zou et al. replaced the meta-hexylphenyl at m-ITIC with n-octyloxyphenyl and reported an acceptor m-ITIC-OR which showed a Voc of 0.90 V with HFQx-T as the donor.194 Tang et al. reported that the introduction of n-hexyl at the IDTT 3,9-positions helped to increase the device Voc, from 0.91 V to 0.97 V, and FF, from 0.69 to 0.73, when pairing with PBDB-T.195
Zhan et al. reported ITIC-Th in which the side chains at the 4,4,9,9-positions are 5-hexylthiophene. The solar cells with PBDB-T1 as the donor showed a PCE of 9.6% and a Voc of 0.88 V.196 Yan et al. reported a Voc of 0.972 V with a deeper HOMO polymer, PffBT4T-B, as the donor.48 Peng et al. used CuI to coordinate 1,3,4-oxadiazole based WBG polymer donor through the CuI⋯N coordination and reported a PCE of 12.34% with a Voc of 1.06 V.198 Zhan et al. synthesized another ITIC-Th analogue, ITIC-Th1, in which IC-F, a stronger A unit than IC, was used. The ITIC-Th1's LUMO was down shifted and its Eoptg was reduced compared to ITIC-Th. When pairing with FTAZ, a PCE of 12% and a larger Jsc of 19.2 mA cm−2 were reported.200 Yang et al. replaced the IC with CT[c] and synthesized ITCPTC-Th, which exhibited a Voc of 0.850 V and a Jsc of 17 mA cm−2 with PBDB-T as the donor.202
In ITIC, the side chains are n-hexyl. Heeney et al. reported that with a change of para-hexylphenyl in ITIC to n-octylphenyl, the resulting SMA, C8-ITIC, exhibited a reduction in the optical band gap, higher absorptivity, and an increased propensity to crystallize and its solar cell device with PFBDB-T, a PBDB-T analog with 3-fluoro at the thienyl units as the donor, showed a Voc of 0.93 V, a Jsc of 19.3 mA cm−2, and a PCE of 12.4%.203 Marks and Melkonyan et al. reported a n-nonyl substituted ITIC (ITIC-C9) which showed a Voc of 0.99 V with PBDB-TF as the polymer donor.204 Bo et al. reported a 2-ethylhexyl-thio substituted ITIC which had a Voc of 0.92 V pairing with PBDB-ST.205
Modifications at the 4,8-positions of BDCPDT have been reported. Chen et al. reported a modification by introducing two octyl chains at these positions, which was then conjugated with IC-F, and the resulting acceptor (NCBDT) showed a downshifted LUMO and an upshifted HOMO as well as a smaller Eoptg than the mother molecule. The solar cell showed a Jsc of 20.3 mA cm−2 with PBDB-T as the donor.206 Liao et al. modified the BDCPDT core by using two 2-ethylhexyloxyl chains and the resulting SMA conjugated with IC (BTIC) had a higher-lying HOMO (−5.32 eV) and a smaller Eoptg than the unmodified molecule. A Voc of 0.90 V and Jsc of 17.7 mA cm−2 were reported with J71 as the donor.207 Zhan et al. reported modifications with 5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophene and its acceptor with IC as the A units, which exhibited an Eoptg of 1.53 eV. This fullerene-free device showed a Voc of 0.922 V and Jsc of 18.6 mA cm−2 with FTAZ as the donor.208 Yang et al. modified this core by using 5-(2-ethylhexyl)thienyl as the side chains and conjugated the modified electron-rich core with IC-M, affording BDTThIT-M, which gave a Voc of 0.942 V, Jsc of 18.0 mA cm−2, and a PCE of 11.9%.209 Ding et al. investigated the effects of the BDCPTT side chains length on device performance and found better performance was obtained with the hexyl chains on both BDT-4,8-positions and cyclopentane phenyl-4-positions.210
Modifications on the NTT units were also tried. Zhan et al. synthesized IOIC3 by replacing the hexyl chains on the naphthalene of IOIC2 with the hexyloxyl groups, which gave a smaller Voc due to a lower-lying LUMO and larger Jsc because of a smaller optical band gap and a higher FF as well when pairing with PTB7-Th.211 Yang et al. synthesized BTTIC by using 5-(2-ethylhexyl)-thienyl to replace the hexyl on IOIC2 and using CT[c]-M as the ending A units. This SMA gave a PCE of 13.2% with a Voc of 0.904 V with PBDB-T as the electron donor material.212
Bo et al. reported asymmetrical modifications on the IDT's 4,4,9,9-positions by introducing two different side chains: –octyl at one set of 4,9-positions and para-hexylphenyl at another set of 4,9-positions (Fig. 5). The FF and PCE of the solar cell device with the unsymmetrically modified molecule as the acceptor were observed to be less dependent on the active layer film thickness than the device parameters using the symmetrically modified acceptors.213 They based on a CDID core and synthesized an acceptor with asymmetric side chains by introducing n-hexyl at the methylene-carbon positions between the fused benzene and n-octyl at the methylene-carbon positions between the fused thiophene and benzene, which gave rise of a Voc of 0.93 V and a Jsc of 18.55 mA cm−2 with FTAZ as the donor.214
4.5. The A–D–A type with perylene diimide ends
Perylene diimide (PDI) and naphthalene diimide (NDI) chromophores are typical WBG n-type organic semiconducting moieties. Zhan et al. conjugated IDT with 7-butoxylPDI through its 1-position and reported the acceptor IDT-2PDI.218 Chen et al. synthesized a PDI based acceptor by fused the PDI with the IDTT core, FITP (Fig. 6), which showed a Voc of 0.99 V and a PCE of 7.33% (Table 1) with PTB7-Th as the donor material.216 Tajima et al. reported an acceptor, IDT-NDI-2EH, with thiophene-fused naphthalene diimide as the ending A units which were conjugated with IDT core, from which a Voc of 0.92 V and a PCE of 9.07% were reported with PBDB-T as the donor.217

5. The A–π–D–π–A type NF-SMAs with a fused-ring ladder-type core
This type of LBG SMAs can be classified into two classes according to the electron-donating or accepting nature of the π-bridge: either A–πD–D–πD–A or A–πA–D–πA–A (Fig. 7).

5.1. The A–πD–D–πD–A type
IEIC is made of IDT, 3-(2-ethylhexyl-thiophene), and IC (Fig. 3); a PCE of 6.3% and a Voc of 0.97 V were reported with PTB7-Th as the donor, but the device FF is very low, only 0.48%.23 Yan et al. reported a higher Voc (1.0 V) and FF (0.59) with Pff2T-FTAZ-2DT as the donor.219 A tandem cell using IEIC was reported by Chen et al.220 Hou et al. reported an IEIC analogue, e.g., IEICO, by replacing the 2-ethylhexyl side chains on the IEIC π-thiophene with 2-ethylhexyl-oxy, a stronger electron-donating group than 2-ethylhexyl, and a larger reduction of Eoptg (1.34 vs. 1.50 eV), as originating from the upshifting of the HOMO by 0.15 eV and downshifting of the LUMO by 0.05 eV, more fully covered the solar emission spectrum by using PBDTTT-E-T as a polymer donor, which afforded a larger Jsc (17.7 vs. 11.7 mA cm−2).221 As the IC units were replaced with IC-2F, the Eoptg of the resulting SMA, IEICO-4F, was further reduced to 1.24 eV due to reduction of the LUMO downward to 4.19 eV and an even larger Jsc of 22 mA cm−2 was reported with PBDTTT-E-T or J52 as the donor.222,223 The shortcoming of this approach is a large reduction of Voc, from 0.90 V to 0.82 V and then 0.74 V by pairing with PBDTTT-EFT, due to a decrease of the acceptor LUMO. Otherwise, the device FF is below 0.6. It is interesting, as Yang et al. reported, that the LUMO of SMA with CT[c] as the terminal A units was upshifted when the 2-ethylhexyl on the thiophene bridge was replaced with 2-ethylhexyl-oxy, and a higher Voc and comparable Jsc were obtained with PBDB-T as the pairing polymer donor.224 Geng et al. reported a SMA, IDT2Se-4F, by replacing the 2-ethylhexyl-oxythiophen on IEICO-4F with selenophene as the bridge, which showed a Voc of 0.79 V and a Jsc of 21 mA cm−2 with PBDB-T as the donor.225 A report by Bo et al. indicated that enhancement of the D–A co-planarity by using para-dimethoxylphenyl as the π-bridge on IDT-BOC6 led to a large increase in both the Jsc and FF.226 Li et al. reported the vinyl-bridged SMAs, whose Eoptg was reduced to 1.35 eV with the use of IC-2F as the terminal A units, and a Jsc of 20 mA cm−2 with J71 as the polymer donor.227 With CN (1-chloronaphthalene) as a solvent additive to optimize film-morphologies, a PCE of 12.1% was reported with PTB7-Th as the polymer donor.228 Wu et al. conjugated IC-4F through the thienyl-4-position and reported i-IEICO-4F which exhibited a twisted conformation between the IDT-T and the IC-2F planes, while exhibiting a higher Voc (0.849 vs. 0.704 V), a larger Jsc (22.86 vs. 20.48 mA cm−2), and again a higher FF (0.679 vs. 0.569), and finally a PCE of 13.18% was obtained.229 Bo et al. reported IEOIC-2F on which the bridged theinyl-3,4 positions were functionalized with hexyloxyl side chains. The fullerene-free solar cell with PBDB-T as the donor exhibited a PCE of 12.17% with a Voc of 0.896 V, a Jsc of 21 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.645.230
Liao et al. reported another IEIC analogue, IDSe-T-IC, by replacing the fused-thiophene at IDT with selenophene, and the fullerene-free PSCs pairing with J51 exhibited a Voc of 0.91 V and an FF of 0.62.232 Huang et al. reported that variations of the terminal A units largely affected the device's performance due to changes of the optical and electrochemical properties as well as the film-morphologies.233 Zhu et al. synthesized a SMA, AA1, with thieno[3,4-b]thiophene as the bridges and 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)rhodanine as the terminal A units, which showed a Voc of 0.87 V, a Jsc of 16.5 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.70 when pairing with PTB7-Th.235 As IC was used as the terminal A units, the Eoptg of the resulting SMA, e.g., AA2, was reduced to 1.32 eV from the AA1′s 1.54 eV and a Jsc of 20.7 mA cm−2 was obtained. However, the Voc and FF were reduced to 0.73 V and 0.63, respectively.236 They again reported another SMA, NITI, with carbon-bridged oligo(p-phenylenevinylene) (COPV)267 as the core and IC-2F as the terminal A units, which gave a higher Voc of 0.86 V and a larger FF of 0.71 than AA2 with comparable Jsc when pairing with PBDB-T.238 An NITI analogue, NSTI, with a fluoro-substituted bis-silicon-bridged stilbene as the central D unit was again reported by the authors, which showed a smaller Voc and Jsc than NITI.239 Hou et al. reported that steric hindrance from the non-conjugated side-chains of the central D unit played an important role in affecting the π–π-stacking mode of the small-molecule backbones, which determined the role of that small molecule acting as an electron donor or electron acceptor material in the organic photovoltaic cells.240
5.2. The A–πA–D–πA–A type
McCulloch et al. reported O-IDTBR with octyl-substituted IDT as the core (Fig. 6). The O-IDTBR:P3HT devices showed a Voc of 0.72 V, a Jsc of 13.9 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.60. Remarkably, the Voc was much higher than the P3HT based fullerene cells, which could be due to its higher-lying LUMO than PCBM ([6,6]-phenyl C60-/C70-butyric acid methyl ester).24,243 When pairing with the LBG polymer of PffBT4T-2DT, a Voc of 1.07 V, a Jsc of 15.0 mA cm−2, an FF of 0.62, and a PCE of 9.95% were obtained.244 Yan et al. reported a PCE of 11.4% with a polymer donor of PvBDTTAZ, a copolymer based BDT and difluoro[2,1,3]benzotriazole.44 O-IDTBR has been used as a nonfullerene SMA to pair with different electron donor materials, either polymer or small molecule, and a Voc of 0.98–1.1 V has been reported, but unfortunately, the FF is generally low, typically, below 0.64. A PCE of 12.0% with a Voc of 1.03 V was reported with the fluorinated polymer PBDTTT-EFT as the donor which indicated that a suitable selection of polymer donor, for example, with a deep HOMO energy and appropriate side chains, might be helpful for achieving high efficiency.250 Zhan et al. reported an n-hexylphenyl substituted analogue, IDT-2BR, which exhibited a Voc of 0.84 V and an FF of 0.68.251 They then replaced the side chains into n-hexyl and synthesized IDT-2BR1 which gave rise to a smaller Voc but a larger Jsc when pairing with PTB7-Th.252
Bazan et al. observed that the orientation of the fluoro atom at the BT, relative to the IDT core, impacts the device performance.254 Zhou et al. used benzo[d][1,2,3]triazole (BTA) as an electron-accepting π-bridge to replace BT on O-IDTBR and the resulting SMA (BTA1) also showed a Voc of 1.02 V and a much better FF of 0.70.255 As the CS on rhodanine was changed into CO, the SMA's LUMO was increased and a Voc of 1.22 V was obtained also pairing with P3HT.256 By changing the S atom on rhodanine into 1,1-dicyanomethylene, they synthesized BTA3 and reported a 1.21 V Voc by pairing with PIDT-DTffBTA, a WBG polymer which was conjugated from IDT and BTA, although the LUMO was much lower-lying on BTA3 than BTA2 (−3.73 eV vs. −3.43 eV), which was speculated as originating from both the SMA and polymer containing the same D and A units.257 When pairing with J61, BTA3 gave rise to a Voc of 1.15 V.258 Huang et al. reported the device performance of this acceptor with PBDB-T, which gave rise to a Voc of 0.93 V.260 In another work, Zhou et al. selected quinoxaline (Qx) as the π-bridge and achieved a 0.95 V Voc when pairing with P3HT.261
Zheng et al. used DTN to replace the IDT on O-IDTBR and reported DTNR, which had a Voc of 1.08 V, a Jsc of 15.72 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.56 when pairing with PTB7-Th.262 Zhan et al. used IDTT as the core, BT as the bridge, and 1,1-dicyanomethylene as the terminal A units and synthesized IDTT-2BM, which gave rise to a Voc of 0.845 V, a Jsc of 9.7 mA cm−2 and an FF of 0.568 when pairing with PBDTTT-C-T.263 Laquai et al. reported a Voc of 0.83 V, a Jsc of 15.5 mA cm−2, and an FF of 0.63 and indicated that the core structure impacted geminate recombination.264 Chen et al. inserted a thiophene bridge between BT and 1,1-dicyanomethylene substituent rhodanine and synthesized IDT-FBTR which had a LUMO energy of −3.69 eV and an Eoptg of 1.67 eV. With PTB7-Th as the donor the device exhibited a Voc of 1.02 V and a PCE of 8.92%.266
6. The A–π–D–π–A type NF-SMAs with fluorene or its analogue core
Fluorene is a tricyclic fused-ring with two benzene rings fused by a cyclopentane and the side chains at its 9,9-positions orienting perpendicular to the fluorene plane. It is a common moiety widely-used to design NF-SMAs. McCulloch et al. reported a NF-SMA, FBR (Fig. 8), in 2015, in which 9,9-dioctylfluorene was linked with 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BT) and then flanked with rhodanine terminals. A Voc of 0.82 V and a PCE of 4.1% were reported with P3HT as the donor.268 Chen et al. selected thiobarbituric acid as the terminal A units and thiophene as the bridge to conjugate with central fluorene and reported DTBTF.269 A number of WBG NF-SMAs have been reported with fluorene as a ladder-type core. Singh et al. used selected electron-donating hexadiathiafulvalene (HDT) to replace the rhodanine ends and synthesized BAF-2HDT, with which a Voc of 0.77 V and a PCE of 7.13% were reported.270 They reported another FBR analogue acceptor by inserting two furans between the rhodanine and BT and synthesized FRd2, from which a Voc of 0.833 V and an FF of 0.723 was obtained with PTB7-Th as the donor material.271 They then synthesized BAF-4CN, which was blended with PffBT4T-2OD to fabricate a fullerene-free solar cell with a Voc of 0.769 V and a PCE of 8.40%.272 They replaced the rhodanine-S in FRd2 with 1,1-dicyanomethylene and reported FRdCN2, from which a Voc of 0.934 V and a PCE of 10.70% were obtained with PTB7-Th as the donor.273 Beaujuge et al. used 1,1-dimalononitrile to replace the FBR rhodanine and synthesized FBM by which they reported a PCE of 5% and a Voc of 0.88 V with PCE10 as the donor. They also observed that comparable device performance was obtained with use of carbazole rather than fluorene as the core.274

7. Conclusions and outlook
The types of NF-SMAs reviewed in this article possess very high possibilities to tune their optical, electrochemical, and photovoltaic properties via modifications of their chemical structures. Although hundreds of acceptors have been reported via modifications on either their electron-rich (D) core, electron-withdrawing (A) group, side chains, or π-bridge and combinations via covalently linking different types of D, A, side chains, and π-bridge, a much large area is yet awaiting for you to design and synthesize new acceptors with device-performance directed molecular designing. The big issues remaining to be resolved include: (1) reduction of Voc loss via molecular design, (2) achievement of large electron mobility via molecular design, (3) achievements in large absorptivity and wide absorption band via backbone structure design, and (4) design of new structures, for example, in D, A, the π-bridge, and the conjugation, which are available for high-efficiency OPVs.
As the OPV active layer includes both an NF-SMA and a donor material, the design of new polymer/small-molecule donors with strong capability to absorb solar light, large hole mobility, and suitable Frontier molecular orbitals are highly required. Another interesting direction in materials designing is the possibility of these types of SMAs as building blocks to design high-performance polymer acceptors. Li et al. reported a novel polymer acceptor of PZ1 by conjugation of IDIC-C16 with thiophene through the IC-5/6-positions of IDIC-C16 and the thiophene-2,5-positions. This polymer acceptor had a higher absorption coefficient than N2200 and a smaller Eoptg of 1.55 eV and upshifted LUMO and HOMO levels than the parent IDIC-C16. When blended with PBDB-T, a PCE of 9.04% with Voc = 0.829 V, Jsc = 15.78 mA cm−2, and FF = 69.12% were obtained.287
Experimental values of the device parameters Voc, Jsc, FF, and PCE for each solar cell with each kind of photovoltaic materials as the active layer are below the Shockley and Queisser (S–Q) limit. Fig. 9 shows the device parameters from record-efficiency solar cells from each of the photovoltaic materials compared to the calculated S–Q limit.288 For the OPVs, the experimental Jsc is approaching the S–Q limit JSQsc, while, the Voc and FF are both largely below the S–Q limit values, VSQoc and FFSQ. The FF values achieved in state-of-the-art fullerene-free OPVs are typically between 0.65 and 0.75 (for example, see Tables 1–3), which are much farther from the S–Q limit, expectedly as 0.85–0.95.

For OPVs, the total Voc loss (ΔV) between the band gap (Egap) and the experimental Voc value comes from three parts: the Voc loss from radiative recombination from absorption below the bandgap, ΔVrad.below gap, the Voc loss from nonradiative recombination, ΔVnonrad, and the energy difference between the Egap and qVSQoc according to the following equation:289 qΔV = (Egap − qVSQoc) + qΔVrad.below gap + qΔVnonrad = ΔE1 + ΔE2 +ΔE3(1)where q is the elementary charge, ΔE1 is from the radiation recombination above the band gap, which is present for any kinds of solar cells with a value of 0.25–0.30 V, and ΔE2 is due to the presence of the donor–acceptor charge-transfer state,40,290 which provides the driving force for the charge separation of the strong binding Frenkel exciton. For OPVs, ΔE3, as originating from a non-radiation recombination, is much larger and it was suggested to be a major factor for the Voc loss in an OPV device.28 The total Voc loss is normally above 0.6 eV with some examples having a Voc loss value between 0.5 and 0.6 eV.
The large Voc loss and the large difference between the experimental FF and FFSQ mean that there is still a very large area to improve OPV efficiencies. Designing donor and acceptor materials with decreasing non-radiation recombination is required to reduce Voc loss. Improving the fill-factor of an OPV device can be achieved by: (1) designing organic donor and acceptor materials, for example, optimizing their side chains to improve hole and electron mobilities, (2) optimizing film-morphologies291,292 to improve the active layer electronic properties, and (3) engineering the active layer–electrode interfaces293–297 to improve charge collections.
Normally, OPV device performance is very dependent on active layer thickness and the active layer thickness is normally ∼100 nm for optimal OPV devices. However, processing towards large-scale and large-area production of OPVs requires that device performance be independent of the active layer thickness. Therefore, designing new materials with device performance dependence on reduced active layer thickness is required.25,100,213,298,299 This can be possibly carried out by designing high hole mobility polymer donor materials and high electron mobility nonfullerene acceptors because the hole mobilities reported so far from fullerene-free solar cells are still low, typically on the order of magnitude of 10−5–10−4 cm V−1 s−1, while electron mobilities are normally on the order of magnitude of 10−4 cm V−1 s−1.
Large-scale and large-area production of OPVs available for future commercial applications also required environmentally friendly processing organic solvents. Therefore, seeking non-halide organic solvents available for achieving high-performance OPVs is another topic.300–303
Stability is another issue with OPVs. For example, a study by Hou et al. indicated that a PBDB-T:ITIC solar cell showed much higher thermal stability than its PBDB-T:PCBM counterpart – the PCE of the former cell decreased from 11% to 10% when the active layer was heated at 200 °C for 30 min, while the efficiency of the latter decreased from 7.4% down to 3.4%.55 Otherwise, the results by Krebs and Zhan et al. indicated a fullerene-free solar cell showed higher light stability than the corresponding fullerene device.304 Studies by McCulloch et al. indicated that nonfullerene SMA-based devices had longer storage lifetime and better photo-stability24 as well as oxidative stability243 than fullerene-based devices. Studies by Ma et al. indicated that PBDB-T:ITIC solar cells fabricated by the blade-coating method had excellent stability when stored in an ambient condition without encapsulation and when using a small amount of 1,8-diiodooctane as a solvent additive.63 Research by Choi et al. showed that the 3MT-Th:ITIC solar cell had a long-term stability – the device performance was nearly maintained for 1100 h storage under ambient conditions.160
