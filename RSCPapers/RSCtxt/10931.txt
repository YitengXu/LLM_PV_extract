First published on 20th October 2004AbstractThis review complements previous reports1 and is a collection of the highlights revealed by database searches for the year 2003. During the year significant advances have been made towards carbon nanotube functionalisation and solubilisation, though characterization of the reaction products remains a major challenge.
1 Synthesis, separation and properties of fullerenes
[60]Fullerene has been produced, albeit in low yield, in the liquid phase for the first time using an aerosol of a precursor soot produced from pyrolysis of acteylene.2 Addition of 3% of CCl4 to the helium atmosphere results in an increase in the yield of [60]fullerene under otherwise standard arc discharge conditions.3 A catalogue of growth transformations involving carbon insertion or extrusion from the fullerene surface has been developed.4 Graph-theoretical considerations have been used to develop a single configurational descriptor for any chiral fullerene.5 Semi-conducting fullerene nanowires, which may be potentially important components of chemical sensors and nanoscale electronic devices based on fullerenes, comprising of polycrystalline [60]fullerene and a rhombohedral polymeric phase have been grown using the pores in anodic aluminium oxide templates.6 A ferromagnetically ordered state with a saturation magnetisation Tc of ≈820 K, four times larger than the previous highest value reported, has been observed in a material produced by the high pressure treatment of [60]fullerene.7 HPLC separation of the product from laser irradiation of [60]fullerene in the presence of BN powder followed by mass spectrometry shows that boron and nitrogen atoms have been substituted into the fullerene cage structure producing the heterofullerene BNC58.8 The stability and aromaticity of fullerenes,9,10 heterofullerenes of the form C48X1211 and BiNi rings,12 where i
				= 2–9 have been published. A theoretical study of small cage fullerenes, C20 to C36, using ab initio methods to calculate the ground-state energy has been performed and the energy gain from Jahn–Teller distortion found to decrease as the size of the fullerene cage increases.13 The semiempirical PM3 method has been used to predict that isolable or extractable fullerenes have relatively large heats of atomisation and relatively large minimum anion localisation energies, which for C84 isomers is highly correlated with the HOMO–LUMO energy separation.14 The Stone–Wales transformation of [60]fullerene to a non-classical C2v fullerene containing two adjacent five-membered rings has been studied theoretically and two possible transition states identified; one has C2 molecular point group symmetry and the other has a carbene-like structure in which a single atom is significantly moved away from the fullerene cage surface.15 Extrapolation of vapour–pressure curves of fullerene systems has been postulated to be applicable up to 1800 K and has been used to predict the boiling points of a range of fullerenes and endohedral fullerenes.16 A primary ion beam system delivering at least 1 nA of C60+ current and having a sub-3 µm spatial resolution with a 0.1 nA beam current has been used in a time of flight secondary ion mass spectrometer with overall efficiencies 102–104 higher than that of instruments employing atomic primary ions.17
2 Endohedral fullerenes
The degree of endohedral incorporation of, for example, a noble gas is increased by adding potassium cyanide to the mixture during the entrapment procedure.18 Molecular hydrogen has been introduced quantitatively into the fullerene cavity of an open-cage fullerene derivative which has a 13-atom ring orifice (see Fig. 1a for structure).19 N@C60 is less reactive towards disilarane under photochemical conditions than [60]fullerene which is attributed to the shorter lifetime of the triplet excited state of the endohedral species.20 Oxidation using, for example, silver salts has been used to improve the solubility, and hence the separation, of several endohedral fullerenes such as Tm@C60+.21 Water soluble endohedral Gd@C60[C(COOH)2]1022 and lanthanoid@C82(OH)n23 have been used as effective MRI contrast agents. The 3He NMR chemical shifts of 3He-encapsulated fullerenes and derivatives have been calculated.24

The structure of a covalently-bonded scandium dimer has been confirmed in an isolated pentagon rule (IPR) violated [66]fullerene.25 Similarly the X-ray structure of Sc3N@C68 shows that the fullerene cage does not obey the IPR and that the structure is stabilised by coordination of the scandium atoms over a folded pentalene ligand, which is part of the fullerene cage.26 An endohedral metallofullerene, La2@C72, of one of the ‘missing’ cage fullerenes has been isolated and 13C NMR spectroscopy used to show that this molecule also has an IPR violated fullerene cage structure.27 A thin film of the dimetallofullerene La2@C80 has been used in an n-type field effect transistor.28 Monolayer and multilayer islands of La2@C80 and La@C82 on hydrogen terminated Si(100)-2 × 1 surfaces have been investigated by scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and significant electron transfer from the La atoms to the fullerene cage observed.29 Langmuir–Blodgett films of Dy@C82 mixed with metallophthalocyanines have been prepared and photocurrent quantum yields of up to 7–8% observed.30 Ce@C82 has been shown to be a π–f composite nanomagnet in which an anisotropic f-electron spin couples with the rotational motion of the fullerene cage that has π-electron spin.31 ENDOR and NMR studies have shown that the electron spin density partially localises beyond the fullerene cage in La@C82.32
3 Chemistry of the fullerenes
Organic chemistry
Once again the past year has seen a large number of papers dedicated to fullerene organic chemistry. The carbocation of the azafullerene, C59N+, which is isoelectronic with [60]fullerene, has been prepared by reaction of the dimer (C59N)2 with the stable radical cation of hexabromo(phenyl)carbazole and the counter carborane anion derived from the silver(I) bis-carborane complex, Ag(CB11H6Cl6)2.33 The foregoing azafullerene carbocation has been neutralised by reaction with electron rich aromatic species such as anisole.34 The azafullerene dimer has also been derivatised by radical reactions with 9-substituted fluorenes.35
The first non-classical fullerene derivatives of C62, which have a four-membered ring as part of the fullerene cage, have been prepared by the [4+2] cycloaddition of tetrazines with [60]fullerene which results in the formation of diimine intermediates, irradiation with visible light then causes a rearrangement to the new C62 fullerene cages.36 Fullerene derivatives with extended openings containing up to 13 atoms, which have been used to form endohedral fullerenes,19 have been synthesised by rearrangement of the product derived from reaction of [60]fullerene with a substituted triazine followed by insertion of sulfur (Fig. 1a).37 Solvent free reactions of N-alkylglycines with, and without, aldehydes with [60]fullerene have been performed and found to produce the expected fulleropyrrolidines.38 Ionic liquid solvents and ultrasonic irradiation have been used to promote the reaction of carbenes with [60]fullerene with methanofullerenes obtained in good yield.39
The retrocyclopropanation of methanofullerenes has been studied electrochemically and the reaction found to occur after the addition of about 2 electrons per starting fullerene molecule.40 A stable intermediate formed during the foregoing electrochemically induced rearrangement is thought to be a singly bonded dimeric [60]fullerene derivative.41 Furthermore a systematic study using digital simulation to determine the kinetic parameters required for the electrochemically induced retrocyclopropanation of methanofullerenes has been performed.42 Methanofullerenes bearing an α-ylidic ester have been found to undergo an acid catalysed rearrangement to cyclopentanofullerenes as a result of a vinyl cyclopropane-type ring expansion.43 A theoretical study of the rearrangement of cyclohexadiene derivatives of [60]fullerene to two bis(fulleroids) and two bis(methano)fullerenes has been performed and the mechanism found to proceed via a single intermediate.44
Several new methanofullerenes, each bearing a macrocyclic malonate functionalised crown ether, have been synthesised using standard techniques.45 A water soluble poly(amidoamine) fullerodendrimer results from the Diels–Alder reaction of [60]fullerene with an anthracene-based dendron.46 Neutral methanofullerene-based [2]catenanes and [2]rotaxanes bearing electron deficient aromatic diimides have been prepared in good yield.47 Photolysis of poly- and di-silanes with [60]fullerene results in silylated fullerenes with lower oxidation potentials than the parent fullerene.48 Phosphonate-containing [60]fulleropyrrolidines have been incorparated into a scaffold based upon the γ-phase of zirconium phosphonates.49 A methanofullerene bearing an acetyl chloride moiety is a useful precursor for ester formation.50 Standard reaction conditions for the cycloaddition of azides with fullerenes have been used in the formation of sulfonyl-azafulleroids and sulfonylaziridinofullerene.51 Crystal structures of mono- and bis-acene derivatives of [60]fullerene have been reported52 and a tris[60]fullerene adduct of tetraphenylheptacene isolated.53
					[60]Fullerene and mono-adducts of [60]fullerene react with 4-methyl-1,2,4-triazoline-3,5-dione; the latter resulting in highly regioselective addition to a double bond adjacent (a so called cis-1 bond) to the original site of addition.54 Chiral HPLC has been used to separate a series of chiral bis-adducts of [60]fullerene.55 The absorption spectra of several mono- and bis -pyrrolidine adducts of [60]fullerene have been recorded and the visible region of the spectra shown to be significantly modified by the addition pattern observed in the fullerene derivative.56 Similarly the reduction potentials of bis-pyrrolidine adducts of [60]fullerene have been systematically investigated.57 Several tetrakis-silylated isomers of [60]fullerenes have been separated by HPLC and shown to have low oxidation potentials.58 The crystal structure of the 7,8 (dichloromethano)homo[70]fullerene confirms the site of addition of the carbene.5913C NMR spectra of fullerene derivatives with three- four- and five-membered annulated rings have been completely assigned using 2D INADEQUATE spectroscopy and examined for general trends which are found to support the hypothesis that ring currents are critically important in the determination of the chemical shift values of signals in 13C NMR spectra of fullerene derivatives.60
A fluoroxyfluorofullerene, C60F17(OF), and an oxahomofluorofullerenol, C60O(F17)(OH), have been separated from complex mixtures produced by MnF3 and K2NiF6 induced fluorination of [60]fullerene.61 Electrophilic substitution of C60F18 into phenols results in the formation of benzofuranohexadecofluoro[60]fullerene, C60F18(C6H4O), via a 1,3-fluorine shift or more likely a β-fluorine elimination.62 Further oxides of fluorofullerene derivatives have been separated from a solution of C60F36 in solvent containing a trace of water.63 A trannulene-based donor–acceptor dyad with a long-lived charge separated state has been produced by reaction of C60F18 with suitably functionalised malonate derivatives.64 Reaction of C60F18 with tetrathiafulvalene (TTF) resulted in the formation of an unusual thiiranofullerene after elimination of a thioketene moiety from the TFF moiety and a second adduct formed by cycloaddition of [60]fullerene with a dithiolactone, formed in situ by loss of carbon disulfide from TTF, has also been isolated.65 Trannulene derivatives are magnetically aromatic as they have a diamagnetic ring current that has the same orbital symmetry explanation as an ordinary aromatic annulene.66 The application of Knudsen-cell mass spectrometry towards the characterisation of fullerene derivatives from, for example, fluorination reactions has been reviewed.67 Trifluoromethylation of [60]- and [70]fullerenes using CF3CO2Ag results in complex mixtures of products some of which, including adducts in which addition has unusually taken place across 5 ∶ 6 bonds, have been separated by HPLC.68 X-ray crystal structures of Cs symmetrical C70Br1069
					(Fig. 2) and of two isomers of C78Br1870 have been reported. A mixture of hydrogenated fullerenes, C60H38 to C60H44, has been prepared under Benkeser reduction conditions and the products shown by NMR spectroscopy to be complex mixtures of isomers.71C1

Advanced materials
The application of fullerenes in the development of nanostructured artificial photosynthetic systems has been reviewed.77 Evidence for the Marcus inverted region in both electron-transfer reduction and oxidation of fullerenes78 and fullerene-based dyads79 has been provided by, for example, pulse-radiolytic studies. Axial coordination of zinc naphthalocyanine with a fulleropyrrolidine bearing an imidazole ligand self-assembles into a 1 ∶ 1 supramolecular dyad and evidence for the formation of a radical ion pair has been obtained using picosecond transient-absorption spectral studies.80 The use of [60]fullerene as a photoactive core in dendrimers has also been reviewed.81 The presence of hydrophobic chains on one hemisphere of a fullerene-based dendrimer and hydrophilic chains on the other enables the production of stable Langmuir films and moreover the presence of mesogenic groups such as cyanobiphenyl units results in the formation of a well-defined liquid crystalline smectic A phase.82 An increase in fullerene triplet lifetimes in fullerene dendrimers is observed as the size of the dendrimer branches increases leading to a decrease of oxygen diffusion rates as in more polar media the fullerene core is encapsulated by the dendrimer units.83 Photoinduced electron transfer between the positively charged Zn analogue of Cytochrome c and a negatively charged polyanionic dendrofullerene results in fluorescence quenching of the porphyrin emission.84 Zinc porphyrin terminated dendritic arrays harvest visible light effectively and produce electron transfer to the [60]fullerene terminus.85 A series of TTF and p-quinoid extended TTFs bearing fullerene derivatives has been synthesised86a–c and incorporated into fullerene dyads and the slow recombination kinetics observed shown to be marginally effected by the type of fullerene cage functionalisation employed.86c Fullerene-based molecular triads with long lifetimes of up to 111 µs for the distant radical pair have been observed.87 High-performance solar cells have been prepared using a new, low molecular weight alternating polyfluorene copolymer as an electron donor and a methanofullerene, [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butryric acid methyl ester (PCBM), as an electron acceptor.88 The X-ray crystal structures from chlorobenzene and dichlorobenzene of PCBM used in the preceding study show that different types of packing leads to the suggestion that chlorobenzene is the solvent of choice for production of solar cell materials as, for example, the molecular arrangement obtained from this solvent allows electrons to hop from molecule to molecule easily in several directions whilst optimal blending morphology of the fullerene and polymer is also achieved.89 Use of the equivalent methano[70]fullerene, being less symmetric and therefore having an increased absorption coefficient in the visible region, has been used in photovoltaic cells which have a 50% increased current density compared with the PCBM based system.90 A thiophene-based polymer has also been used as the donor system in fullerene-based solar cells.91 A detailed analysis of the charge transfer absorption and emission spectra, time-dependant spectroscopic measurements and molecular dynamic simulations have been used to show that the same two moieties in porphyrin–[60]fullerene dyads can produce widely different electron-transfer regimes depending upon their interchromophore separation, their angular relationship and the spatial overlap of the electron clouds.92 High (17%) incident photon-to-photocurrent efficiency in electrophoretic deposited composite clusters of [60]fullerene and porphyrin has been observed.93 The quantum yield of photocurrent generation of self-assembled monolayers of porphyrin–fullerene dyads is 250 times larger on indium tin oxide (ITO) electrodes than that from the corresponding monolayers on gold electrodes.94 New [60]fulleropyrrolidine ligands with appended pyridine moieties capable of forming axially symmetric complexes with zinc tetraphenyl porphyrin have been synthesised.95 Long-lived charge separation in the solid state of a phthalocyanine–fullerene dyad has been measured and the dyad used in the construction of a solar cell with moderate (0.02%) power conversion efficiency.96
Fullerene derivatives containing ferrocenyl moieties have been synthesised by either the cyclopropanation route97 or by 1,2-carbanion addition.98a,b The non-linear optical (NLO) properties of molecules from the latter study have been investigated and the fullerene derivatives shown to have large hyperpolarizabilities.98a In addition, charge separated states have been observed for the first time in ferrocene–fullerene dyads.98b Derivatives of fullerene arylethynes bearing carborane moieties have been synthesised using carbanion chemistry and the carborane derivatives found to have exceptionally high NLO β values which are attributed to the synergistic effect of the prolonged conjugation present in the molecule and the electron-withdrawing effect of p-carborane.99 Similarly large enhancement of the non-linear optical response of a series of fulleropyrrolidine derivatives has also been reported.100 Acetylenic [60]fullerene derivatives bearing terthiophene moieties have been used to prepare electroactive polymers by electrolytic oxidation of the terthiophene moiety.101 A dendrimer with a hexakis–methano[60]fullerene core bearing 12 symmetrically attached pyrene units has fluorescence emission kinetics that are simpler than those found in the mono-adduct due to the higher symmetry of the more highly functionalized fullerene.102 A methanofullerene bearing an ammonium group forms a supramolecular complex with a crown ether–porphyrin conjugate.103 After photoinduced electron transfer zinc imidazoporphyrin–[60]fullerenopyrrolidine dyad exists in a charge separated state which has a lifetime of 310 µs.104 A new zinc porphyrin [60]fullerene rotaxane has been prepared and through-space forward and backward electron transfer processes observed.105 The photodynamics of exciplex formation from the singlet excited state in porphyrin–[60]fullerene dyads in which the fullerene and porphyrin units are close together has been shown to be extremely fast due to the close proximity of the two chromophores.106 The formation of a giant dipole with a moment in excess of 150 Da has been observed using the transient dc photocurrent method in carotene–porphyrin–fullerene triads.107 Supramolecular triads of N,N-dimethylamino-phenyl-fullerene-pyridine coordinated to zinc porphyrin undergo efficient charge separation upon excitation yielding radical ion pairs having relatively long-lived (30–40 ns) charge-separated states.108 Similarly an oligoaniline–oligo(p-phenylene vinylene)–fullerene triad has been prepared and the charge separated state formed at a higher rate and in higher quantum yield in thin films than in solution.109 Alkylpyrrole-containing fullerene triads undergo intramolecular photoinduced electron transfer producing charge separated states.110 A copper–porphyrin-containing fullerene dyad has been prepared and the paramagnetic copper(II) core shown to influence the charge-transfer processes taking place on excitation.111 The fullerene to porphyrin energy transfer observed in a conjugate with two fullerene moieties covalently attached to a triply fused porphyrin are completely different to the porphyrin to fullerene electron transfer observed in a biaryl-type bisporphyrin conjugate.112
Biologically active fullerenes
The chemistry, biology and nanoscience of water soluble fullerene derivatives have been reviewed.113 Self-assembly using hydrogen bonding has been investigated in several fullerene-containing molecules such as barbituric acid derivatives,114 2-ureido-4[1H]-pyrimidone derivatives115 and 2,6-diaminopridine-containing fullerenes.116 A hydrogen bond assembled [2]rotaxane containing a [60]fullerene moiety has been prepared and the fullerene group shown to act as a photoactive unit and also a stopper in this molecular shuttle.117 Self-assembling methanofullerenes bearing lipopeptide conjugates of alamethicin form voltage-dependant, stable ion channels.118 A fulleropyrrolidine having a glutamic acid sidechain has been prepared and shown by molecular modeling to have a binding capacity for the major histocompatability complex.119 Fullerene glycoconjugates bearing five carbohydrate groups have been prepared by reaction of a fullerene pentaarylthiol derivative with an appropriate base and 2-bromoethyl glycoside.120 Hydrated fullerene colloids stabilize protein hydration and decrease the specific surface energy in the water–protein matrix in salt solutions.121
					[60]Fullerene is able to penetrate a membrane core when a poly(vinyl alcohol) film immobilized with cyclodextrin, known to have a high affinity for [60]fullerene, is used.122 A significant pro-oxidant effect of fullerenes towards DNA under the influence of UV and X-ray irradiation has been observed.123 Azafulleroids bearing pendant sugar groups are photocytotoxic.124 Water soluble C60(ONO2)7 has been found to attenuate ischemia-reperfusion lung weight gain and fullerene derivatives of this type may therefore find application in preventing oxidant induced pulmonary diseases.125 Studies under physiological conditions implicate oxygen radicals such as the superoxide anion radical in DNA cleavage studies rather than singlet oxygen.126 A bis-pyrrolidine fullerene derivative has been shown to produce hydrogen peroxide from dioxygen without photoirradiation.127 A simple dimethoxymethanofullerene strongly inhibits amyloid peptide aggregation as it binds to the central hydrophobic core of the peptide.128 A water soluble carboxylated methanofullerene has been shown to be an effective anti-oxidant, which may find application in treatment of, for example, Parkinson's disease.129 Dihydropyridine containing fulleropyrrolidines have been synthesised and molecular modeling used to show that the presence of the fullerene group does not significantly alter the conformation required in the 1,4-dihydropyridine moieties for biological activity to be present.130 Bis-adducts of [60]fullerene with high solubility in aqueous solvent systems have been prepared.131 Cationic pyrrolidinium iodide fullerene derivatives have been shown to be effective antibacterial and antitumour agents.132 Similar bis-pyrroldinium derivatives, in particular the trans-2 isomers, have activity against HIV-1 type in the low micromolar range.133 A molecular dynamic study has shown that the flexible flaps of the HIV-1 protease become tightly shut when an effective binding molecule, such as a fullerene derivative, is present.134 A method for the purification of a dendritic methanofullerene octadecaacid, which has interesting biological activity, using octadecylsilica bonded phases has been developed.135
Polymers
Fullerene-coated polymer beads have been prepared by reaction of the amino groups on poly(styrene-co-divinyl)benzene with the fullerene cage and the resultant beads used as heterogeneous catalysts.136 A chiral, nematic tetrapedal liquid crystalline methanofullerene self-organises into helices.137 A water soluble polymer containing regularly spaced, pendant fullerene molecules has been synthesised and shown to have enhanced photoconductivity.138
					[60]Fullerene-containing polyurethane films have large ultrafast non-resonant third-order non-linearity at 1550 nm.139 By contrast double [60]fullerene end-capped poly(ethylene oxide) has reduced optical limiting properties compared with that of the parent fullerene.140 Redox active films with, to date, unknown structures can be electrochemically generated by the reduction of ferrocene-containing [60]fullerenepyrrolidines in the presence of palladium triacetate.141
					[60]Fullerene derivatised poly(thiophene)s which have enhanced conjugation length, faster switching time and improved stability under redox cycling have been electrochemically prepared.142 Thermal treatment of hexanitro[60]fullerene results in the formation of poly(fullerene)oxide films that are stable up to 800 K.143 Atom transfer radical polymerization of N-vinyl carbazole with polychlorinated [60]fullerene has been used to produce fullerene functionalized poly(vinylcarbazole).144 Contact angle measurements show that as the [60]fullerene content of polymethacrylates increases the surface of the polymer becomes increasingly hydrophobic.145 Two [60]fullerene end-capped polysiloxanes have been prepared by the reaction of the fullerene with the terminal amino groups of the polymer.146
					[60]Fullerene end-capped poly(ethyleneoxide)s form smaller aggregates than singly end-capped poly(ethyleneoxide)s.147 A high molecular weight, soluble [60]fullerene end-capped polyrotaxane, comprising of a central polyazomethine chain encapsulated by cyclodextrin units along the chain has been prepared.148
Inclusion and van der Waals complexes
A systematic search of the interactions of CH groups with the convex surface of fullerenes in the crystal structures deposited at the Cambridge structural database has been performed.149A new 1 ∶ 1 complex of calix[5]arene and [60]fullerene, the formation of which is facilitated by the presence of [70]fullerene, has been identified.150 Crystal structures of a 1 ∶ 1 complex of [60]fullerene with bromoform151 in which the fullerene molecules are interspersed with disordered bromoform molecules arranged in zigzag chains and an unstable 1 ∶ 12 [60]fullerene with bromotrichloromethane152 complex have been characterized using single crystal X-ray diffraction. [60]Fullerene induces the formation of an organogel in which one fullerene molecule is complexed with two hydrogen-bonded amide-appended porphyrin molecules.153 A brief review of fullerene complexes with conjugated belts and ring structures with radially oriented p orbitals has been published.154 The synthesis of the carbon skeleton of the belt region of D2
					[84]fullerene has been achieved.155 The synthesis of a [10]cyclophane hoop which forms part of the [60]fullerene structure has been reported after further reaction of C60Me5CN with a copper-coupled Grignard reagent at the opposite hemisphere of the fullerene cage.156 Stable complexes of [60]fullerene,157 a carboxylated methano[60]fullerene157 and [70]fullerene158 with carbon paraphenyleneacetylene nanorings have been isolated and characterized using single crystal X-ray diffraction.
[70]Fullerene forms a complex with calix[5]arene when p-xylene is used as solvent.159
					[70]Fullerene, rather than [60]fullerene, is preferentially complexed by p-halohomooxacalix[3]arenes.160 An NMR study of crown ether complexes with [60]- and [70]fullerenes has been performed.161 Self-organised ordered adlayers of [60]fullerene and calix[8]arene have been observed using high-resolution scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) with the fullerene molecule sitting in the central cavity of the calixarene on a Au (111) surface (Fig. 3).162 Similarly a hydrogen-bonded open honeycomb network of perylene tetracarboxylic diimide and melamine on a Ag-terminated silicon surface has been used to complex [60]fullerene molecules with six fullerenes trapped in each pore in the honeycomb network.163 The orientational ordering of a 2D array of [60]fullerene has been shown by calculation and STM studies to be very different to that found in solid [60]fullerene.164 High level calculations have been performed in order to better understand the intimate details of the interactions between fullerenes and porphyrins and metalloporphyrins.165 The crystal structure of a platinum(II) octaethylporphyrin complex with [60]fullerene has been reported in which each ordered fullerene molecule is situated close to the platinum porphyrin.166 Supramolecular ‘peapods’ comprised of hydrogen-bonded dendrimeric zinc porphyrin nanotubes and [60]fullerene molecules in which the fullerene molecules are the ‘peas’ have been characterized using transmission electron microscopy (TEM).167 The neutral complex of the covalently linked μ-oxo dimer Fe(III) tetraphenylporphyrin with [60]fullerene has [60]fullerene molecules situated in pockets which are formed by interacting porphyrin units.168 Decamethylferrocene produces a neutral salt with [60]fullerene as the major reaction product, there is however a second product formed in low yield which initial results indicate may be a result of electron transfer between the two molecules.169

Organometallic chemistry
A review of [60]fullerene–metal cluster complexes has been published.170 Selective insertion of dioxygen into an iridium-to-fullerene bond has been observed in an η2–[60]fullerene derivative resulting in the formation of a peroxoiridacycle derivative with a σ-coordinated fullerene ligand.171 The reaction of the cis-dihydride osmium complex [OsH2(CO)(PPh3)3] in the presence of tert-butylisocyanide results in the formation of η2–[60]fullerene complexes.172 Bimetallic fullerene complexes, (η2–C60)M(dpaf) or (η2–C60)M(dppf) where M = platinum or palladium, have been produced by initial in situ formation of the η2–[60]fullerene complex using standard techniques followed by reaction with 1,1′-bis(diphenylphosphino)ferrocene (dppf) or 1,1′-bis(diphenylarsino)-ferrocene (dpaf).173 Several optically active η2–[60]fullerene organotransition metal complexes that contain chiral (+) or (−) 2,3-O,O′-isopropylidene-2,3dihydroxy-1,4-bis(diphenyl-phosphanyl)butane ligands have been synthesised.174 Organocobalt complexes containing substituted cyclopentadienyl ligands, (η5-RC5H4)Co(PPh3)2 where R = EtO2C or MeCO, react by ligand exchange with [60]fullerene producing η2–[60]fullerene complexes.175 A [60]fullerene tetrairidium metal cluster complex has been synthesised in which two metal centres bridge two [60]fullerene cages as a result of unusual μ4–η1–η1–η2–η2–[60]fullerene bonding.176 A [60]fullerene dimer connected by a germanium bridge results from the solid state high speed vibration milling reaction of [60]fullerene with dichlorodiphenylgermane and lithium powder.177 The reactivity of [60]fullerene-based ferrocene analogue, Fe–C60H5(C5H5), towards electrophiles has been exploited to synthesise a range of several new functionalized [60]fullerene ferrocene hybrids.178 The ruthenium chlorodicarbonyl complex of pentamethylated [60]fullerene, Ru(η5–C60Me5)Cl(CO)2, has been produced in high yield and further Fullerides
A microscopic mechanism of superconductivity in fullerides based on the topological superconducting model of Wiegmann has been proposed.183 The Jahn–Teller phonons in superconducting fullerides create a local pairing which is reasonably resistant to Coulomb repulsion and as a result the requirement for coherence throughout the solid results in a very strong doping dependence to Tc.184 Thin films of fulleride ions have been created on Li-covered Ni(110) surfaces.185 Potassium related dipole active extramolecular phonons in addition to the expected intramolecular IR active modes of T1u symmetry are observed on the surfaces of K-doped fulleride thin films using high resolution electron energy loss spectroscopy in reflection geometry.186 A series of semi-conducting mesoporous tantalum oxide–sodium fulleride intercalates have been prepared and the structures shown to consist of anionic [60]fullerene columns enclosed in a double wrapping of Na cations and the walls of the tantalum oxide mesopores.187 Two different body-centred cubic-related packings of the fulleride anions in ammoniated Na2C60 samples have been identified.188 Electron repulsion has been identified as the interaction which is suppressed on the transition to metallic and superconducting behaviour in the cubic C603− system which has degenerate orbitals that adopt the Mott–Hubbard insulating localized electron ground state.189 Ammoniated Rb3C60 is transformed to an ordered antiferromagnetic state at temperatures below ∼51 K.190 Photoemission studies have been employed to show that metallic Rb3C60, Carbon nanotubes

Preparation. 
A review of carbon nanotube preparation, properties and uses is available.213 It has been shown that it is not necessary to include molybdenum in the catalyst in order to achieve SWNT production.214 Catalytic decomposition of acetylene over a Fe–Mo/Al2O3 catalyst produced high quality SWNTs free of defects.215 A narrow distribution of nanotube types are produced when using a silica-216 or alumina-217 supported Co–Mo catalyst. A mesoporous silica template impregnated with an Fe catalyst has been used to produce nanotubes with controlled orientation and diameter.218 Double-walled nanotubes (DWNTs)
						(Fig. 5) have been produced by several methods including floating chemical vapour deposition with sulfur as a catalyst,219 gram-scale production using a MgCoO solid solution containing Mo oxide,220 an iron catalyst supported on alumina,221 catalytic decomposition of alcohol over an alumina supported Fe–Mo catalyst,222 reaction of ethanol with Mg in a stainless steel autoclave,223 decomposition of acetylene using zeolite supports224 and use of a high-temperature pulsed arc discharge method with Y/Ni catalysts.225 Rings composed of DWNTs have been observed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) amongst the carbon material produced by acetylene decomposition over a solid solution of Mg0.9Co0.1O.226 High density multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWNTs) are produced when ferrocene is used as a catalyst under a continuous growth process regime.227 A large pore mesostructured silica has been used to produce carbon nanotubes.228 Similarly, carbon nanofibres result from the use of a mesoporous SBA-15 silica.229 Chemical vapour deposition of acetylene over nanoparticles of Co produces good quality aligned nanotubes.230 MWNTs doped with N concentrations of up to 20 atom% result from an aerosol production technique using acetonitrile as the N source.231 A 40% yield of high quality MWNTs have been produced by simply lowering a hot graphite rod into a beaker of water without the need of a catalyst.232 Millimeter long SWNTs have been grown on a flat substrate suitably patterned with a Fe–Co catalyst.233 A detailed study, and optimization, of the conditions necessary for the production of nanotubes in molten NaCl has been performed.234 A method for the growth of nanotubes suitable for use as microscope tips based upon standard nanotube production techniques has been published.235 Large fullerenes are present in the product from HiPco production of SWNTs.236 Co-pyrolysis of Fe(CO)5 and pyridine or toluene results in high yields of helically coiled MWNTs.237 Carbon nanopipettes, tapered hollow carbon needles, are formed under microwave plasma chemical vapour deposition of methane.238 Lithography has been used to control the diameter and predefined position of carbon nanotubes produced by catalyst mediated chemical vapour deposition.239 A mechanism explaining the formation of nanotubes on SiO2 which identifies the importance of the formation of active γ-iron particles in nanotube growth has been postulated.240 Aligned carbon nanotubes have been grown on thin quartz flakes in high yield and purity using ferrocene in toluene.241 A study of a number of metal substrates for the efficient growth of nanotubes has been performed and the interaction between the catalyst, iron and the other metals used thought to be the main factor in determining whether there is nanotube growth or not.242

Nanotube functionalisation
The functionalisation of nanotubes, which has been a significant area of development during the last year, has been reviewed.243 Theoretical models to predict the addition to both the internal and external surfaces of nanotubes have been published244a,b and suggest that very reactive reagents are required.244a Density functional calculations show that CO2 may lead to selective etching and formation of armchair nanotubes only.245 Intercalative doping of nanotubes with acids, such as sulfuric-, nitric- and hydrochloric-acids has been identified by X-ray induced photoelectron spectroscopy.246 Preadsorption of dioxygen plays a key role in the reversible and selective protonation of surfactant-dispersed metallic SWNTs.247 SWNTs have been exfoliated using a process which requires the use of concentrated H2SO4, P2O5, KMnO4 and H2SO4 again resulting in individual oxidised tubes containing OH, CO and CO2H groups.248 Exposure of thin films of SWNTs to nitric acid results in modification of the nanotubes which can be reversed by thermal or visible laser irradiation annealing.249 Nafion has been used to solubilise nanotubes and the resulting materials identified as useful biosensors.250
A wide range of reactions have been used to covalently functionalise carbon nanotubes including fluorination251a–c with a ratio of CF0.43 reported to result after thermal reaction at 250 °C.251a Reaction with, for example, benzoyl peroxide results in the addition of phenyl radicals to the nanotube.252 Hydroboration of SWNTs is not feasible according to quantum chemical calculations.253 Cycloadditions of SWNTs with carbenes,254a silylene,254a germylene254b and nitrenes,254a,b have been supported by evidence provided by NMR spectroscopy. Bromomalonate derivatives appended with sulfur-containing groups react with SWNTs which form easily visualised, using for example atomic force microscopy (AFM), complexes with Au colloids.255 Reaction of fluorinated nanotubes with alkyl lithium reagents results in reversible alkylation.256 Azomethine ylide addition of peptide derivatives to SWNTs has been successful and the new materials shown to be potentially useful for biomedicinal diagnostic purposes.257 Carboxylic acid nanotube end groups have been used to attach fluorescent chromophores to SWNTs.258 Hydroxyl groups have been introduced into SWNTs, which endows them with water solubility, by a mechanomechanical technique using KOH.259 Solvent free reactions using diazonium salts also results in nanotube functionalisation with aryl groups.260 Polystyrene chains have been grafted onto SWNTs using reduced nanotubes.261 Fluorinated nanotubes react reversibly with diamines with up to 1 amino functional group per 8 to 12 nanotube carbon atoms.262 Osmium tetroxide also reacts reversibly with metallic SWNTs leading to an increase in electrical resistance of several orders of magnitude in the functionalised nanotubes.263 A diamine terminated poly(ethylene glycol) has been used to preferentially solubilise small diameter SWNTs.264 Polymer modified SWNTs have been found to have large optical limiting effects better than, for example, [60]fullerene alone.265 Amine functionalised CdSe quantum dots have been used to functionalise the ends of MWNTs.266 By contrast, CdTe quantum dots have also been added to the body of oxidised MWNTs.267
SWNTs coated with crystalline tin oxide have been prepared using SnCl4 as a precursor.268 Similarly anatase269 and rutile270 TiO2 nanoparticles have been formed on the external surface of MWNTs using precursors such as titanium bis-ammonium lactato dihydroxide.269 Silica coated SWNTs have also been produced by addition of a silica/H2SiF6 solution to the nanotubes and spontaneous interconnection between isolated SWNTs observed.271 Supercritical carbon dioxide is a useful solvent for the hydrogen reduction of a Pd(II)-β-diketone in the presence of MWNTs and results in the formation of Pd nanoparticles supported on the MWNTs.272
Non-covalent nanotube functionalisation has been achieved using the attraction between porphyrins and the side walls of the SWNTs.273 Anthracene derivatives also adsorb strongly onto the sidewalls of SWNTs but can be displaced by pyrene.274 Pyrene-containing methyl methacrylate polymers have been used to solubilise MWNTs by non-covalent nanotube side-wall modification.275 Thiol terminated pyrene has been used as a linker to construct self-assembled systems comprised of gold nanoparticles, the pyrene and carbon nanotubes.276 Similar self-assembled gold systems have also been constructed using covalently modified nanotubes bearing thiol groups277 and hydrolysed-poly(styrene-alt-maleic anhydride).278 Methathesis polymerisation using SWNTs non-covlently modified by a pyrene-linked ring opening methathesis polymerisation initiator results in a composite material in which nanotubes are coated with a poly(norbornene) sheath.279
In addition to the methods already described for covalent and non-covalent functionalisation of nanotubes, which generally produce solvent soluble nanotube containing fractions, solubilisation of nanotubes has also been achieved using, for example, amine modification,280 polyaniline,281
					η-cyclodextrin,282 cationic,283a anionic283a,b and non-ioinic surfactant,283a,b and helical amylose complexation.284 Liquid alkanes at elevated temperatures285 and ultrasound286 have also been used to disperse nanotubes in organic solvents and SWNTs have also been encapsulated in cross-linked surfactant micelles.287 The interaction of oxidised nanotubes with liquid crystalline epoxides has been investigated and the composites shown to have increased conductivity.288 A binary solvent mixture of ethanol and cyclohexane has been employed to determine the hydrophilic and hydrophobic properties of MWNTs.289 Steady state and time resolved emission experiments show that chemical functionalisation of SWNTs changes the emission behaviour.290 Ionic liquids have been used to form SWNT-containing gels at room temperature.291 The particle-coagulation spinning process produces SWNTs fibres with a well-developed ‘skin-core’ structure.292
Ion irradiation has been used to encapsulate Cs within the core of SWNTs.293 Whilst Ni encapsulated nanotubes can be produced by pyrolysis of acetylene in an alumina membrane support.294 Similarly CoFe2O4 nanowires have been produced by loading nanotubes with an aqueous nitrate precursor followed by heating.295 MWNTs containing a complex pentlandite-type alloy of general composition (Fe,Ni,Co)9S8 are formed as a by-product from an arc-evaporation synthesis of DWNTs.296 Supercritical water has been used to transport Ag nanoparticles into opened MWNTs by capillary action (Fig. 6).297 SWNTs templates enable the production of metal nanowires at the apex of scanning probe microscopy tips.298 Copper nanowires have been formed around the outside of MWNTs by chemical deposition processes and these composites used to reinforce brittle YBaCuO superconductors.299

Properties and applications
The electrocatalytic properties and sensor applications of nanotubes have been reviewed.325 A number of nanotube-containing electrodes have been prepared and used to, for example, detect purines, nucleic acids and DNA hybridisation,326 to investigate the electrochemistry of microperoxidase MP-11,327 to examine neurotransmitters such as dopamine,328 to biosense glucose and ethanol328 and to determine the presence of hormones such as indole-3-acetic acid.329 Nanoelectrode arrays have been constructed by plasma enhanced chemical vapour deposition330 and a similar electrode used for detecting DNA.331 Carbon nanotubes loaded with CdS particle tracers have been used to amplify electrical detection of DNA hybridisation.332 Amidoferrocenyl-functionalised SWNTs are efficient receptors for the redox recognition of anions such as H2PO4−.333 MWNTs have been used as sold phase extractants for molecules such as bis-phenol A.334 Carbon nanotubes have been used as a matrix to trap analytes such as peptides for use in laser desorption/ionisation time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOFMS).335 The flow of a liquid, which induces a voltage, over SWNTs has been used to develop nano-flow sensors.336
The use of nanotubes in electronic systems has been reviewed.337 A field-effect transistor, which operates at room temperature, has been fabricated using DNA templation.338 Nanotube network transistors have been transferred to flexible polymer supports which enables the transistors to be bent without significantly changing their propeties.339 Ambipolar transistors, which have excellent capacitive coupling between the gate and nanotube and have been constructed from single carbon nanotubes and used as logic circuits.340 The hysteresis often observed in nanotube field-effect transistors is now thought to be due to the presence of water which causes charge trapping.341 An electrochemical actuator with a MWNT as the key motion-enabling element has been constructed.342 Nanotube field effect transistors with ballistic electron transport have been constructed using palladium to contact the nanotubes.343 Photoconductivity has been observed from a single carbon nanotube in a field-effect transistor.344 SWNTs prepared by pulsed laser vaporisation are weakly photoluminescent345 and fluorescence spectroscopy has been used to determine the electronic properties of single molecules.346 Bulk defective nanotubes have been found to be more electrically conducting than graphitic nanotubes in polymer composites.347 Large resistance drops and strong non-linearities in the IV characteristics provide evidence for the existence of superconductivity in ropes of SWNTs.348 Carbon nanotubes are effective anti-oxidants for a number of polymer systems.349 Fibres woven from SWNTs are extremely strong and may be used in electronic textile devices.350 Thermally driven mechanical vibrations have been observed in scanning electron microscope images of suspended doubly clamped SWNTs.351
