First published on 2nd May 2007AbstractThis review summarises the literature reported in 2006 on the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear and supramolecular assemblies. The popularity of this field means that the review has been constrained to only cover literature from the major high-impact journals.
Highlights
Highlights for the year include a giant porphyrin wheel containing 24 Zn(II)-porphyrins that displays efficient excitation energy transfer around the wheel, and the direct observation of a 1MLCT excited-state in [Mo2(O2C-9-anthracene)4] by transient absorption spectroscopy. Application of metal complexes as dyes for solar cells, phosphorescent emitters in OLED devices and as fluorescent sensors and bioprobes continue to be areas that attract strong interest.
Review articles
A number of reviews have appeared on the topic of solar energy conversion, including; the design of metal complexes and their optimisation for use as dyes in dye-sensitized solar cells,1,2 and construction of artificial light harvesting systems using porphyrin, phthalocyanine, perylenebisimide and polypyridyl based metal complexes.3 Synthetic strategies towards lanthanide ion complexes for application as luminescent chemosensors4 and the photophysical properties of Cm(III) in crystals and solutions5 have been reviewed. The design and photophysical properties of a variety of Os(II) and Ru(II) complexes for utilisation in OLED fabrication has been reviewed6 along with light-emitting molecular devices that incorporate Ni(II), Cu(II) and Zn(II) metal complexes.7 The photophysical properties of metal-mediated assemblies of porphyrins have been summarised.8 Applications of bis-cyclometallated Ir(III) complexes and methods for the synthetic tailoring of their excited state properties have been reviewed.9 The photophysics and applications of rigid-rod di-, oligo- and polymetallaynes has also been discussed.10
An entire issue of Coordination Chemistry Reviews (2006, 250, issue 13–14) was dedicated to papers based on oral presentations at the 16th International Symposium on the Photochemistry and Photophysics of Coordination Compounds (which took place in California during July 2005).
Main group compounds
The properties of [Alq3] (q = quinolin-8-olate) derivatives that are blue emitting have been tuned for potential application in OLED devices in a number of studies. Al(III) complexes incorporating quinolin-8-olate ligands fluorinated at the C5 position exhibit a red-shift (λem = 407 nm) and decrease in emission intensity in CHCl3 solutions by comparison to [Alq3] (λem = 387 nm). However, fluorination at the C6 position of q causes a blue-shift (λem = 375 nm) and significant increase in emission intensity.11 [Alq2(OPh)] complexes containing a variety of electron poor aryl substituents in the C5 position of q display strong blue emission between 455 and 504 nm.12 The use of substituted phenolates in [Alq2(OPh)] compounds does not result in a significant change of the emission energy, but can affect the shape and intensity of the emission band.13 Attachment of oligofluorene Pt(II) porphyrin acceptors to the [Alq3] core (see I) results in donor–bridge–acceptor triads. OLED devices incorporating these species showed pure red emission from the Pt porphyrin, with improved output for the longer donor–acceptor distance (n = 4) because of a better alignment of the donor–bridge–acceptor triplet energy levels.14
  
Aluminium complexes containing a series of chelating anilido-imine ligands, [ortho-C6H4(NAr′)(CHNAr″)AlCl2] (Ar′, Ar″ = 2,6-iPr2C6H3, 2,6-Me2C6H3 or p-MeC6H4), display bright fluorescence in both solution and the solid state, with the emission colour in solution (λem = 510–554 nm) tuned by the ortho substituents on the rotatable aryl rings of the ligand.15 Complexes of Ga(III) and In(III) with 5-mesityldipyrrin (mesdpm) emit green light upon excitation with UV light at room temperature with quantum yields of 2.4%, [Ga(mesdpm)3], and 7.4%, [In(mesdpm)3].16
Biarsenical dyes with fluoro-substituted versions of fluorescein show increased photostability, lower pH sensitivity, strong absorbance and high quantum yields, which improves their use as tetracysteine probes.17 The closely related lead coordination polymers {[Pb4(OH)2(SIP)2(H2O)](H2O)5}n and [Pb3(SIP)2(H2O)5]n (SIP = 5-sulfoisophthalate) display fluorescent and phosphorescent solid-state emission, respectively, due to differences in their solid-state structures.18 A fluorescent sensor for detecting lead in living cells, combining a fluorescein-type scaffold and dicarboxylate pseudocrown receptor, exhibits a selective turn-on fluorescence emission at 514 nm in response to Pb(II) ions in aqueous solutions.19
The organic-inorganic hybrid material (HL)6[Ge6(OH)6(hedp)6]·2(L)·nH2O (Hhedp = 1-hydroxyethylidenediphosphonic acid) displays emission from the lowest energy triplet-state at 320 nm from 14 K to room temperature when L = phen. In contrast, when L = Hq, triplet emission (530 nm) is only detected at low temperature because of thermally activated non-radiative mechanisms.20
Copper, silver and gold complexes
The coordination of Cu(II) to anthrancenyl and dansyl based fluorophore ligands significantly quenches the fluorescence of the ligands. When exposed to NO(g) in pH 7.0 or 9.0 buffered aqueous solutions, a fluorescence increase is observed from NO-triggered Cu(II) reduction.21 The use of Cu(II) complexes as fluorescent turn-on sensors for the detection of nitric oxide has been extended to π-conjugated polymers containing bipyridyl or terpyridyl substituents in the π-conjugated backbone, or at side chain positions. Coordination of Cu(II) to the polymers results in fluorescence quenching of the polymer emission, but upon addition of NO there is up to a 3-fold enhancement of the metallopolymer emission due to reduction of Cu(II).22,23 A derivative of the dual fluorescent charge transfer fluoroionophore 2-methoxybenzaldehyde acylhydrazone acts as a sensor for the selective detection of Cu2+ with up to 170-fold fluorescence enhancement in the presence of Cu(II) ions.24 A dipyrromethene boron difluoride fluorescent reporter, attached to a thioether receptor, also acts as a turn-on fluorescent sensor for Cu2+ in living cells, with a 10-fold increase in fluorescent response and good selectivity over other biologically relevant metal ions.25
Mononuclear Cu(I) complexes, containing 2-(2′-pyridylbenzimidazolyl)benzene and a variety of phosphine based ancillary ligands, display MLCT emissions with long decay lifetimes (>200 μs) at 77 K when doped into PMMA polymer films.26 Sterically encumbered phenanthroline ligands containing 3,5-di-tert-butyl-4-methoxyphenyl and 2,4,6-trimethylphenyl substituents in the 2,9-positions were used to generate a [Cu(phen)2]+ complex. This complex exhibits unusually strong emission at 77 K due to the sterically protected coordination environment that prevents the formation of non-emissive pentacoordinated excited complexes.27 In a similar fashion, the 3MLCT lifetime and emission intensity of [Cu(2,9-Me-phen)(PP)]+ (PP = diphosphine ligand) complexes increases as the bulkiness of the diphosphine ligand is increased.28 DFT calculations on the luminescent tetranuclear Cu(I) complexes [Cu4(μ-dppm)4(μ4-E)]2+ (E = PPh and S) reveal that the emissive state in both complexes is derived from an admixture of 3LMCT (E → Cu) and 3MCC (3d → 4p) (MCC = metal-cluster-centred) characters.29 Self-assembly of nanometer scale [Cu24I10L12]14+ (L = triazole based N-heterocyclic ligand) cages with Keggin anions, [PMoV2MoVI10O40]5−, results in the formation of a 3D framework that exhibits photoluminescence at ambient temperature. The two prominent emission peaks at 436 and 462 nm are attributed to a mixture of I → Cu charge transfer and d–s transitions due to Cu–Cu interactions.30
The halogeno(cyano)argentite [Ag5(CN)Br6]2+ displays strong blue fluorescent emission at 450 nm (τ = 3.7 ps) upon photo-excitation at 397 nm, but [Ag2(CN)2Cl]+ exhibits a relatively weak band at 390 nm (τ = 7.0 ps) with photo-excitation at 307 nm.31 Strong emission is observed in the solid-state from silver cyano coordination polymers [Ag5(CN)5(bpy)2]n and [Ag5(SCN)4(CN)(bpy)2]n originating from an MLCT transition (Ag → bpy), and in [(Me4N)Ag3(CN)4]n from a coupled MLCT (Ag → CN−) and metal centred transition.32 The Schiff-base ligands L1 and L2 display very weak luminescence in solution but strong luminescence when coordinated to Ag(I) centres; attributed to the conformational rigidity of the ligands in the metal complex that reduces nonradiative decay of the intraligand 1(π–π*) excited state.33
  
Luminescence studies were performed to determine the intracellular distribution of a dinuclear Au(I) complex, containing a bidentate N-heterocyclic carbene ligand that supports Au⋯Au interactions.34 Cyclic Au(I) triazolate trimers, [Au(Tz)]3 (Tz = 3,5-diisopropyl-1,2,4-triazolate), form fully overlapping aurophilically bonded dimer-of-trimers that give multiple phosphorescence bands in both the solid state and solution at 280 K.35 A series of Au(I) thiolates, [(P)Au{SC(OMe)NC6H4-4-NO2}] (Pphosphine ligand) and [(PP){AuSC(OMe)NC6H4-4-NO2}2] (PP = bridging diphosphine ligand), were structurally characterised; the presence or absence of Au⋯Au interactions do not impose significant differences on the emission energy and excitation maxima.36 Thiolate (2-SC6H4NH2) complexes of Au(I) with mono- or di-phosphine ligands display intense photo-luminescence when cooled to 77 K. Reaction of the thiolate complexes with Au(C6F5)3 resulted in the formation of non-emissive Au(I)–Au(III) compounds.37
The coinage metal clusters [Au2M2(C6F5)4(NCCH3)2]n (M = Ag, Cu) display intense luminescence in the solid state at room temperature, but in donor solvents the emission is quenched for M = Cu due to solvent coordination.38
Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexes
Zn(II) is an abundant element in natural biological systems and a number of fluorescent dyes have been developed for use as turn-on fluorescence sensors in biological systems. These sensors incorporate derivatives of tris(2-pyridylmethyl)amine,39 tetra-azacyclododecane,40,41 bisimidazole,42 pyridyl,43,44 2,2′-dipicolylamine45 and quinoline46 as Zn(II) ion receptors. A phenyl-bridged bis(pyrrol-2-yl)methyleneamine derivative (BPBA) is a sensor for Zn(II) ions based on chelation-enhanced fluorescence. In turn, the BPBA-Zn(II) complex acts as a Hg(II) ion sensor due to replacement of central metal ion induced chelation-enhanced fluorescence quenching.47 Dinuclear Zn(II) complexes with a terpyridine bridge and phenanthroline based ancillary ligands can display fluorescence quenching in the presence of Hg(II) ions, but fluorescent enhancement in the presence of Pb(II) ions.48
The slight differences in the rates of electron energy transfer (EET) between two isomeric forms of a Zn(II) tetraaza macrocycle appended by a naphthalene donor and anthracene acceptor was determined [kEET = (0.92 ± 0.02) × 109 and (1.31 ± 0.01) × 109 s−1], and are in accord with the expectations of the Förster equation.49
The cadmium cluster [Cd10S4(SR)12] [R = an oligo(ethyleneglycol) unit] acts as a photoluminescence sensor with a turn-on response to Cu(II) ions in solution.50 The emission energy of a 3D porous metal-organic framework based on [Cd3L12] [L = 2,6-di(4-triazolyl)pyridine] clusters can be tuned between the UV and visible wavelengths by controlling the number of guest molecules in the framework.51 A series of 4-substituted triazole Cd(II) materials with zero-, one-, two- and three-dimensional frameworks exhibit strong blue fluorescence emission bands in the solid state at room temperature, that blue-shift in aqueous solutions.52
Signal ratio amplification via modulation of resonance energy transfer was demonstrated using a dithiazacrown-substituted benzaldehyde cation receptor appended to a boradiazaindacene dyad that displayed a 35-fold change in emission ratios upon addition of Hg(II) ions.53 Pentafluorophenylmercury chloride interacts with phenanthrene in the solid state and displays only fluorescent phenanthrene based emission at room temperature, but both fluorescent and phosphorescent emission is exhibited at 77 K.54 Fluorescein based fluorescent sensors appended with pyridyl-amine-thiol groups that selectively bind Hg(II) ions can detect mid-nanomolar to low-micromolar concentrations of Hg(II) in aqueous solution.55
Miscellaneous transition metal complexes
Subphthalocyanine chromophores act as electron-accepting units in subphthalocyanine-ferrocene dyads that, upon charge separation from singlet excited states, form long-lived (τ = 231 μs) charge-separated states.56 Efficient PET is observed in dendrimers of C60 with two and four ferrocene units on their periphery. Lifetimes of the [(C60)˙−-(dendron)˙+] charge-separated state vary between tens of nanoseconds in nonpolar solvents, to hundreds of nanoseconds in polar solvents, with higher values observed for the dendron containing four ferrocene units.57 Fluorescence quenching of a C60 acceptor having covalently linked ferrocene and N,N-dimethylaniline donors indicates that charge-separation takes place via the singlet excited state of the C60 moiety in both polar and non-polar solvents.58
The 1MLCT excited-state of [Mo2(O2C-9-anthracene)4] has an unusually long lifetime (∼10 ps), allowing the 1MLCT absorption spectrum to be recorded using ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy. The 3MLCT excited-state also has a long lifetime (τ = 76 μs), and the longer excited-state lifetimes of MM quadruply bonded complexes containing aryl ligands, relative to mononuclear complexes, is thought to be a general phenomenon.59 Spectroscopic studies on a series of tetra-substituted thiol oxotitanium phthalocyanines revealed low fluorescence yields (0.05–0.14), relatively long triplet lifetimes (150–210 μs) and triplet yields of 0.31–0.82.60 The absorption spectra of the complexes trans-(N2)2W(R2PCH2CH2PR2)2 (R = Ph, Et, 4-CF3-Ph, 4-CH3O-Ph) display peaks assigned to 3MLCT and 3(d → d) transitions that become allowed due to spin–orbit coupling. Total luminescence from the R = 4-CF3-Ph complex at 5 K is thought to be composed of simultaneous emission from near-degenerate 3MLCT and 3(d → d) terms.61
A DNA based building block, pyridobenzimidazole nucleoside, acts as a fluorescent sensor for a wide variety of metal ions with different emission quenching, enhancement and energy responses observed for the metal ions.62 A fluorescein based thioester (SR) was employed as a light harvesting antenna that increases the rate of photogenerated NO production from the Roussin red salt ester [Fe2(NO)4(μ-SR)2].63
Rhenium complexes
The photophysical properties of a series of Re(CO)3(L) complexes, where L is a tridentate ligand derived from quinoline, benzimidazole, or tryptophan, were studied and show emission from MLCT or LC excited states.64 A series of tricarbonyl Re(I) phenanthroline estradiol compounds exhibit long-lived 3MLCT emission that is enhanced upon binding of estrogen receptor α.65 Luminescent rhenium(I) tricarbonyl amidodipyridoquinoxaline biotin complexes display a profound enhancement of the 3MLCT emission intensity upon binding to the avidin protein.66 The 3MLCT excited states of rectangular Re(I) compounds, [{Re(CO)3(μ-4,4′-bpy)Br}{Re(CO)3(μ-L)Br}] (L = a series 4,4′-dipyridylalkynes) are quenched by electron transfer reactions from aromatic amines more quickly than monomeric Re(I) complexes.67 Re(I) compounds of formula [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)]+, [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)Re(CO)3(bpy)]2+ and [(bpy)(CO)3Re(PCA)Ru(NH3)5]3+ (PCA = 4-pyridinecarboxaldehydeazine), do not emit at room temperature in acetonitrile but, in aqueous solutions, a decrease in pH induces luminescence in all of them from MLCT excited states.68 DFT has been used to investigate the role of MLCT excited states in the trans–cis isomerisation of fac-[Re(CO)3(bpy)(stpy)]+ (stpy = trans-4-styrylpyridine). It is concluded that photoisomerisation of the stpy ligand under visible light operates by a triplet intraligand mechanism via the 3(πstpy
				→
				π*stpy) state, that is populated by intramolecular energy transfer from the Re(I) 3MLCT state.69 The Re(III) metallocarborane [7,8,9-ReC2B7], containing a {Re(CO)(PPh3)2} fragment, displays turquoise blue dual fluorescence accredited to metal-perturbed 1LC states.70
The fluorescent complexes [Re(X)(diimine)(CO)3] (X = OH2, SCN− or CN−) have sufficiently long-lived excited state lifetimes to act as catalysts for the photocatalytic reduction of CO2.71 Ultrafast time-resolved emission and nanosecond transient absorption spectroscopies were used to monitor the kinetics involved in the photogeneration of fluorotyrosyl radicals from a Re(I) tricarbonyl polypyridyl complex.72 A multicomponent complex containing a Re(I) dppz emissive chromophore, an electron transporting 1,3,4-oxadiazole, and a hole transporting thiophene, was evaluated for application as a dopant in OLED devices.73 A series of mixed ligand bpy and phen Re(I) dicarbonyl complexes, [Re(CO)2(P–P)(N–N)]+ (P–P = chelating diphosphene, N–N = chelating polypyridine ligand), were synthesised and display a broad range of exited state lifetimes (25–1147 ns) and quantum yields (0.002–0.11).74 A methanofullerene, bearing a pyridine or bpy ligand separated from the fullerene through an oxyethylene macrocyclic spacer, was complexed with {Re(bpy)(CO)3}+ and {Ru(bpy)2}2+ fragments. Steady-state and time-resolved spectroscopy revealed luminescence quenching of the MLCT state, and triplet–triplet energy transfer from the metal centres to the fullerene.75
Ruthenium and osmium complexes
Mononuclear ruthenium complexes
Broadband femtosecond fluorescence spectroscopy was used to capture the early relaxation processes leading to the steady-state emission of the 3MLCT state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+. The dissipation of energy within the manifold electronic and vibrational levels of the 3MLCT state was proposed to occur via ultrafast intramolecular vibrational-energy redistributions.76 Slight modification of L to L′ in the complex [Ru(bpy)2(L)]2+ (L = diacetyl dihydrazone, L′ = the 1:2 condensate of L and acetone) induces a change from Ru(II) → L charge transfer to Ru(II) → bpy charge transfer as the lowest excited state.77 Perdeuteration of the bpy ligand in [Ru(NH3)6−2n(bpy)n]2+ or [Ru(en)3−n(bpy)n]2+ results in much larger blue shifts of the emission maxima when n = 1 than when n = 3, but perdeuteration of the ligands in [Ru(pyridine)4(bpy)]2+ does not result in any measurable shifts in the emission spectra.78 DFT calculations were used to probe the origin of the very long-lived (30 μs) excited state for [Ru(bpy)2(L3)]2+. The calculations provided evidence of excited states lower in energy than the MLCT states, originating from intraligand charge transfer from the phenolic rings to the phenanthroline part of L3.79 The complex [Ru(bqp)2]2+ [bqp = 2,6-bis(8′-quinolinyl)pyridine] has a remarkably long-lived room temperature 3MLCT excited-state lifetime (τ = 3.0 μs), rationalised by the near octahedral geometry of the Ru(II) core that raises the energy of the short-lived triplet metal-centred state. The MLCT excited states of [Ru(bpy)2(deeb)]2+ (deeb = 4,4′-(CO2CH2CH3)2-bpy) in dichloromethane are efficiently quenched by iodine via an electron transfer mechanism that results in the transient formation of I2˙− in high yield (Φ = 0.25).80 The synthetic application of photochemical upconversion was demonstrated using selective excitation of [Ru(dmb)3]2+ at a variety of visible wavelengths to efficiently drive bimolecular [4 + 4] cycloaddition between two anthracene molecules.81 A series of complexes of the type [Ru(NN)(CN)4] (NN = bpy, phen, terpy, dmb, bpym, L4, L5) showed the presence of 3MLCT-based emission with lifetimes of between 0.25 and 471 ns that were determined from luminescence studies or, for compounds with very weak luminescence, transient IR spectroscopy.82 Ruthenium bpy-based complexes, containing a dppz ligand with alkyl and aryl groups substituted at the 11 and 12 position, exhibit prolonged excited state lifetimes for the aryl substituted dppz ligands.83 A series of compounds of formula [Ru(bpy)2(L6)]2+ display electrochemiluminescence (ECL), with the most basic benzimidazole (X = NCH3) displaying the highest quantum yield for luminescence, and highest ECL efficiency.84 Variations in the nonchromophoric ligands of [Ru(L4)(bpy)] (L4 = four mono-, two bi- or one tetra-dentate amine ligands) result in large changes in emission band shapes, even when the emission energies are similar, due to different vibronic contributions and excited state distortions.85 Time-resolved luminescence and transient absorption studies of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ assembled in various alkali-metal exchanged zeolites reveals fluorescence quenching of the 3MLCT from PET to the alkali metal cations.86
  
The emission intensity of the complex rac-[Ru(dmp)3]2+ (dmp = 5,6-dimethyl-phen) is dramatically enhanced on binding with DNA, to a higher degree than the unsubstituted phen analogue.87 The MLCT luminescence intensity of a heteronuclear Ru(II)Pt(II) dimer, containing a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ derivative with pendant N-(3-ammoniopropyl)carbamoyl arms attached to a cis-PtCl2 unit, is enhanced by complexation with calf thymus DNA. The Ru(II)Pt(II) dimer also exhibits a relatively high efficiency towards the visible-light-induced strand scission of DNA, thought to proceed via an electron transfer process between the photogenerated Ru(III) centre and a guanine base bound to a Pt(II) centre.88
Covalent attachment of a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ photosensitiser to a diiron electron acceptor, which is a model of the iron hydrogenase active site, does not result in electron transfer. Instead the light-induced MLCT excited state is reductively quenched by the diiron site.89
Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes containing bpy and terpy subunits with pyrromethene-BF2
					‘bodipy’ chromophores have long-lived (μs) non-emissive excited states at room temperature. However, at 77 K the complexes exhibit bodipy based fluorescence or phosphorescence.90 A [Ru(bpy3)]2+ compound with a covalently attached methylviologen (MV2+) electron acceptor forms a stable 1:1 inclusion complex with cucurbit[8]uril (CB[8]). When this system is irradiated with visible light a PET process occurs, and a long-lived (τ = 2 μs) charge-separated state (Ru3+-MV+˙-CB[8]) is observed in aqueous solution.91 The heteroleptic complex [Ru(terpy-COOH)(terpy-NH2)], to which a ferrocene unit is attached at the N-terminal group, is significantly more luminescent than the analogous complexes with C-terminal attached ferrocene or both N- and C-terminal attached ferrocene. This is attributed to quenching of the ruthenium based 3MLCT state by greater electron transfer from the more efficiently coupled C-terminal ferrocene group.92 Efficient energy transfer from ruthenium to iridium occurs in heteroleptic bimetallic systems having one metal centre (Ru or Ir) coordinated to two terpy ligands, a second metal centre coordinated to three bpy ligands (Ir or Ru), and an aryl linker.93 Emission from CdSe nanocrystal quantum dots (NQDs) is quenched upon binding to the surface of [Ru(bpy)3−n(mcb)n]2+ (mcb = 4-carboxy-4′-methyl-bpy, n = 0, 1, 2) complexes. The NQD functions as a light sensitiser that, upon photoexcitation, oxidises the attached Ru(II) complexes via efficient hole transfer.94
Steady-state fluorescence and time-resolved flash photolysis measurements on a Ru(II) fullerene complex, [Ru(bpy)2(bpy-C60)]+, showed a rapid intramolecular quenching of the Ru(II) 3MLCT excited state by transduction of the triplet excited state energy to the fullerene.95 Photoinduced charge separation in ruthenocene-C60 dyads is more efficient with a 2-pyrazoline donor–acceptor bridge compared to a pyrrolidine linker, due to the higher electron donating ability of the pyraziline ring.96 Intramolecular triplet energy transfer between a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ based donor and C60 acceptor bridged by a short ethylene spacer is quantitative at all temperatures. The energy-transfer mechanism is dominated by super-exchange interactions at low temperature, and a through-space mechanism at higher temperatures as a result of a linker distortion.97 Photophysical studies on [M(η5-C60R5)(η5-C5H5)] (M = Fe and Ru, R = Me and Ph) results in a rapid charge separation for ‘bucky ferrocenes’, with radical ion pair lifetimes of between 27 and 39 ps. No charge separation was found in the corresponding ‘bucky ruthenocene’, due to faster excited state-deactivation from the heavier ruthenium centre.98
A dual signal responding fluoride sensor comprising of a Ru(II) bpy fluorophore and a 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone chromophore displayed a new intense absorption band at 580 nm and a significant enhancement of the emission intensity upon titration with F− ions.99 Luminescent [Ru(bpy)2(L7)]2+ complexes act as fluorescence sensors for Cu(II) ions, with complete quenching of the luminescence observed upon binding of Cu(II) to the bis(pyridinamide) coordination site in the μM concentration range.100 Addition of Ba2+ ions to [Ru(phen)3]2+ complexes, with azacrown ethers attached to the 4,7 positions of the phenanthrolines, results in shifts of up to 87 nm in the emission spectra, and shifts of up to 370 mV in the redox potentials.101
Efficient energy transfer to 3,6-diphenyl-1,2,4,5-tetrazine from the excited state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ produces the triplet excited state of tetrazine that has a much longer lifetime (τ = 28.4 μs) and higher oxidising ability (Ered* = 1.09 V vs. SCE) than the 3MLCT state of [Ru(bpy)3]2+.102 The photo-reduction of the Dawson polyoxometalate anions [S2M18O62]4− in the visible region was sensitised by [Ru(bpy)3]2+ cations.103 A single-component photo-hydrogen-evolving device comprising of a [Ru(bpy)3]2+ derivative as a photosensitisor, covalently attached to a Pt(II) H2-evolving catalyst, is successful in promoting visible-light-induced EDTA-reduction of water into molecular hydrogen.104
Visible excitation of the heteroleptic rotaxane [Ru(terpy)(L8)]2+ complex causes efficient electron transfer to the 4,4′-bipyridinium unit; a process that is slowed by a factor of two to three when a crown ether surrounds the cationic axle and it is totally blocked in a rigid matrix at 77 K.105 The complex [Ru(bpy)2(L9)]2+ displays room-temperature emission in the far-red region of the visible spectrum, from an excited state based on the imine ligand. However, the complex [Ru(terpy)(L9)Cl]1+ does not emit at room temperature, indicating that the energy of the 3MLCT excited state responsible for deactivation of emission in Ru(II) terpy complexes is unaltered.106
  
The complex [Ru(dcb)(L10)(NCS)2] has a superhigh light-harvesting capacity and is an effective photosensitisor for DSSCs, with a power-conversion efficiency 10% greater than that of [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] under the same conditions.107 Heteroleptic [Ru(dcb)(L11)(NCS)2] compounds have high molar extinction coefficients and, correspondingly, are highly effective sensitisers for DSSCs, with an overall conversion efficiency of 9% for the para-methoxystyryl derivative.108 The related complex [Ru(dcb)(L12)(NCS)2] also performs well as a TiO2-bound sensitisor in DSSCs, with a high overall conversion efficiency (9.4%). Calculations reveal that, importantly, excitation directs charge onto the dcb ligand bound to the TiO2 surface.109 The efficiency of a DSSC incorporating the sensitisor [Ru(dcb)(dppz)(NCS)2] was lower compared to that of [Ru(dcb)(phen)(NCS)2], attributed by DFT calculations to a smaller contribution from the dcb ligand to the character of the MLCT transitions.110 A series of DSSCs using [Ru(L13)2(NCS)2] sensitisors showed greater photon-to-current efficiencies than [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] in the region beyond 625 nm.111 The electron injection quantum yield of [Ru(bpy)2(11-PO{OEt}2-dppz)]2+ complexes anchored to anatase TiO2 thin films in 0.1 M LiClO4/MeCN solutions was reduced from 80% to 30% upon addition of large concentrations of water.112 Efficient-excited state dynamic quenching by I3− of [Ru(bpy)2(deeb)] [deeb = 4,4′-(CO2CH2CH3)2-bpy] anchored to mesoporous nanocrystalline TiO2 thin films was observed in CH2Cl2 and MeCN solutions. This suggests that for weak sensitisor–semiconductor electronic coupling, sensitised quenching by I3− may compete with electron injection, reducing the efficiency of DSSCs.113 Long-lived charge separated states that store energy in excess of the equilibrated excited state were formed in mesoporous TiO2 thin films functionalised with [M(bpy)2(deebq)]2+, [Ru(bq)2(deeb)]2+, [Ru(deebq)2(bpy)]2+ and [Ru(bpy)(deebq)(NCS)2] (M = Ru, Os; bq = 2,2′-biquinoline; deeb and deebq = 4,4′-diethylester derivatives of bpy and bq).114
  
The complex [Ru(dpphen)3]2+ (dpphen = 4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) shows promising electroluminescent behaviour in a solid-state light-emitting electrochemical cell (LEC), reaching a high power efficiency (1.9 Lum/W) at a luminous brightness of 390 cm/m2. Importantly, it is a lot more stable than [Ru(bpy)3]2+-containing LEC devices due to the increased hydrophobic nature preventing the formation of quenching H2O containing complexes.115 A chemiluminescent (CL) sensor based on a Ru(II) bpy derivative covalently immobilised on a silica surface has a number of advantages over sensors in which the CL reagent is immobilised by adsorption or trapping; these include no leaching, and a greater sensor lifetime and reproducibility.116

Rhodium and iridium complexes
Ir(III) complexes have an increased ligand-field stabilisation energy in comparison to Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes and, as a consequence, a less thermally accessible non-emissive triplet metal-centred state. This results in long-lived and intense emission for Ir(III) complexes containing polypyridyl (N,N-donor) or cyclometallating (N,C-donor) ligands, that can be tuned over the whole visible region by varying ligand substituents. Metalloporphyrin complexes
Meso-to-meso ethyne-bridged (porphinato)Zn(II) oligomers are low band gap materials with intense, tunable excited state absorptivity and large NIR fluorescence quantum yields that have potential application in a range of photonic applications.179 The photophysical properties of the fluorescent S2 and S1 excited states in a range of Zn(II) porphyrin complexes were investigated using steady-state absorption and fluorescence spectrometry, with time-dependent DFT calculations employed to help interpret the results.180 Fluorescence from Zn-imidazolyl-porphyrin dimers, functionalised in the meso position with ferrocene, is quenched according to the degree of electronic coupling between the ferrocene and porphyrin.181 A bis(Zn-porphyrin) tweezer, with anthracene components as the apex and side-arms, acts as a host for a series of dipyridylporphyrin guests. The more densely packed dendrimers enhanced the efficiency of PEnT processes to give effective light-harvesting systems.183 Singlet–singlet and triplet–triplet PEnT was observed in a nonameric porphyrin assembly from eight peripheral zinc porphyrins to a central free base that is connected by nucleosidic linkers.184 Macrocyclic arrays of m-bis(ethylene)phenylene-linked Zn(II) imidazolylporphyrins display shorter excitation energy hopping times for the hexagonal array (12.8 ps) compared to the pentagonal array (21 ps) due to a better orientation of transition dipoles.185 Butterfly-type molecular construct VII displays a PEnT process from the central free base to the Zn-porphyrin. Metallation of the central terpy ligand (VIII) results in a change of the molecular conformation from “W” to “U”, and strong quenching of the Zn wing luminescence due to heavy atom effects and fast electron-transfer to the ground state of the “wing”.186 The photophysical properties of ethyne-bridged (porphyrin)Zn(II)–(porphyrin)Fe(III) complexes have been studied in detail, and unusually long excited-state lifetimes were observed for Fe-porphyrin pentacoordinate complexes.187
  
PEnT in oligothiophene linked Zn(II) porphyrins changes from a Förster mechanism mediating the singlet–singlet energy transfer to a Dexter mechanism, if the bridge has sufficiently low lying energy levels.188 Experimental and theoretical methods showed that singlet energy transfer in Zn(II) porphyrin (donor), p-phenyleneethynylene (bridge), free porphyrin (acceptor) systems occurred by both Förster and Dexter mechanisms.189 PET and PEnT processes in directly linked Zn(II) porphyrin–Zn(II) phthalocyanine heterodyads were studied. Charge-separated states that were formed with a 93% efficiency have high free energy and long lifetimes, that make them good candidates for use in photovoltaic cell applications.190 A giant 24-mer porphyrin wheel comprising of six meso–meso-linked tetra-Zn(II)-porphyrins, with a diameter of approximately 7 nm, exhibits an efficient excitation energy hopping rate of (35.1 ps)−1 around the wheel.191
A molecular triad comprising of two free-base porphyrins linked by a meso-phenylene [Ru(terpy)2]2+ bridge displays PEnT from the Ru-3MLCT state to the lowest energy π–π* triplet state localised on the porphyrins. Selective illumination also results in PEnT from the lowest energy singlet excited state on the porphyrin to the Ru-3MLCT state.192 The 3MLCT excited state of [Ru(terpy)(bpy)]+ fragments attached to tetra(pyridyl)porphyrin is effectively quenched by electron transfer to the porphyrin at room temperature, but electron transfer is not efficient at low temperature and emission from the Ru-3MLCT is observed.193

A quadruply azulene fused Ni(II) porphyrin complex has a very effective π-electron conjugation pathway throughout the porphyrin moiety, with an intense near-IR absorption and a large two-photon absorption cross-section.198 Electropolymerised thin-films of Fe(III) and Mn(III) porphyrin derivatives have second hyperpolarisability and two-photon absorption cross-sections that are enhanced by at least an order of magnitude in comparison to those of the free base porphyrin.199
The spectroscopic characteristics of the radical form of a tetraarylporphycene, and its Pd(II) complex, follow the π-radical pattern found in alkyl substituted porphycene; both the radical anions and the radical cations absorb at longer wavelengths than the triplet states.200 Ultrafast charge separation in Mg and Zn porphyrins, covalently linked via an amide bond to [Re(bpy)(CO)3] units, occurs upon excitation of the porphyrin, with charge-separated state IR bands appearing on a time scale of less than 1 ps (Mg) and ∼5 ps (Zn).201
