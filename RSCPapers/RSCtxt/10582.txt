E-mail: n.patmore@sheffield.ac.ukFirst published on 7th May 2008AbstractThis review summarises developments in the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear assemblies reported in the literature for 2007.HighlightsHighlights include the study of excited state intervalence transfer in a photo-chemically prepared diruthenium mixed-valence species stabilised in a polymer film1 and determination of the ultrafast relaxation dynamics of iron(II)-trisbipyridine upon excitation into the singlet MLCT charge transfer band.2 Luminescent metal complexes are continuing to be developed for use as phosphorescent emitters in light emitting diode devices, as biological probes for in vivo studies, and as sensitisers in dye-sensitised solar cells. Examples include the development of a highly phosphorescent perfect green emitting Ir(III) complex for application in organic light emitting diodes3 and the use of a Zn(II) phthalocyanine/organic dye mixture to achieve co-sensitisation for efficient panchromatic dye-sensitised solar cells.4Review articlesA number of review articles on emissive complexes and materials with potential application in optoelectronic devices based on: pyridyl azolate Ru(II) and Os(II) complexes;5 polymers and complexes incorporating Ln(III) ions;6 gold-heterometal complexes;7 and organometallic poly(aryleneethynylene)s have appeared.8 Multicomponent systems that employ light to induce energy and electron transfer or molecular motions,9 excitation energy transfer processes in covalently linked cyclic metalloporphyrin arrays that show potential as artificial photosynthetic antenna,10 and electron transfer capabilities of donor–acceptor systems having conjugated bridges of various lengths have been described.11 Luminescent Pt(II) terpyridyl complexes that exhibit pH and metal cation sensing abilities,12 as well as metal-based sensors that act as fluorescent probes for detecting nitric oxide13 and chemical warfare agents,14 have been summarised. The properties and applications of ruthenium phthalocyanine and naphthalocyanine complexes15 and the use of metalloporphyrin arrays containing poly(pyridyl) ligands as artificial photosynthetic reaction centre models have been reviewed.16 The effect of substituent changes or aggregation on the photochemical and photophysical properties of main group metal phthalocyanines was discussed.17 A cross-section of the current literature on luminescent polynuclear arrays designed using metal ions has been presented,18 and recent work on transition-metal sensitised near-IR luminescence from lanthanides in d–f heteronuclear arrays summarised.19 A special issue of Coordination Chemistry Reviews (2007, 251, Issues 3–4) was devoted to “highlighting the many aspects of the electronic spectroscopy of inorganic compounds”.20Main group compoundsThe electron-to-photon efficiency of OLED devices incorporating an [Alq3] (q = quin-8-olate) donor core covalently attached to three [Pt(TPP)] acceptors using a variety of oligofluorene bridges varied from 0.001 to 0.208, indicating that careful alignment of the component triplet energy levels is required for good device efficiency.21 The hemicage metal chelates [M(L1)] (M = Al3+, Ga3+, In3+) display improved photoluminescence quantum efficiencies that are 1.48 (Al3+), 1.79 (Ga3+) and 1.26 (In3+) times greater than their uncaged [Mq3] analogues. This is likely to be due to the rigidity of the hemicage structure that decreases nonradiative decay of the excited states.22 Ligands based on 8-hydroxyquinoline and having extended conjugated fluorophores were optimised for use in turn-on and ratiometric fluorescence sensor arrays that can distinguish between a variety of cationic metal ions (including Al3+, Mg2+ and Ca2+).23Water soluble In(III) phthalocyanines, containing quarternized 3-pyridyloxy substituents, displayed lower fluorescence quantum yields and shorter triplet state life times but higher triplet state quantum yields when compared to metal phthalocyanines in general. The complexes gave good singlet oxygen quantum yields in water (∼0.56) and show potential as Type II photosensitisers for photodynamic therapy of cancer.24 The photochemistry and photophysics of related phenoxy and tert-butylphenoxy In(III) phthalocyanines display similar trends, with the large triplet state and singlet oxygen quantum yields attributed to the heavy atom effect of the In central metal.25 The heavy atom effect is also used to account for the thousand-fold reduction in fluorescent quantum yield when Tl(III) ions are added to an aqueous solution of 5,10,15,20-tetrakis(4-sulfanatophenyl)porphyrin.26Copper, silver and gold complexesDetailed theoretical investigations employing time-dependent DFT revealed that emission from the cyclic trinuclear complex {[3,5-(CF3)2-pyrazolate]Cu}3 originates from the lowest triplet excited state that has 3MCCT (metal-centred charge transfer) and 3LMCT mixed character.27 The dinuclear Cu(I) complexes [Cu2(μ-X)2(μ-1,8-naphthyridine)(PPh3)2] (X = I, Br) exhibit red phosphorescence at room temperature from a naphthyridine based 3MLCT state.28 Unusually long-lived (up to 150 μs) and efficient (0.16 < Φ < 0.70) emission for monocopper systems was observed in amidophosphine complexes of copper of formula [(L2)Cu(L)2] (L = PPh3, PMe3; (L)2 = dppe).29 Structural rearrangements in the MLCT excited state of [Cu(NN)2]+ (NN = 2,9-dimethyl- or 2,9-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) were investigated using transient absorption and fluorescence up-conversion spectroscopies, that revealed slow (10–15 ps) ISC from the 1MLCT state to the 3MLCT states and ultrafast (∼80 fs) structural rearrangements.30 Transient absorption spectroscopy revealed that the presence of an anthracene chromophore in [Cu(NN)2]+ [NN = 2,9-bis(dimethylene-anthracene)-1,10-phenanthroline)] results in equilibration between the close-lying 1MLCT, 3MLCT and 3(π–π*) excited states via ISC and triplet–triplet energy transfer.31 Addition of fluoride ions to the triarylboron centre in the Cu(I) complex [Cu(L3)(PPh3)2][PF6] results in the formation of a 1:1 complex that displays a distinct visual red-shift (∼100 nm) of the MLCT absorption.32 The related complex [Cu(L4)(PPh3)2][PF6] displayed highly efficient (Φ = 0.88) bright yellow–green phosphorescence in the solid state at ambient temperature that is quenched upon fluoride ion addition, this time due to ligand dissociation from the metal.33 The amino amidate Cu(II) complex, [Cu(L5)2], acts as a turn-on fluorescent indicator for citrate, based on a decomplexation-induced fluorescent enhancement process, with micromolar sensitivity.34  A series of luminescent cyclometalated alkynylgold(III) complexes, I, display an absorption band around 400 nm that is assigned to originate predominantly from an LC transition (π → π*) of the cyclometalated ligand with some mixing from a [π(CCR) → π*(C∧N^C)] LLCT. The emission in both the solid state and solution is from a 3LC state of the C^N^C ligand in almost every instance.35 The crystalline solids [Au(CO)Cl] and [Au(RNC)Cl] (R = alkyl group) both display similar emission spectra consisting of orange–red unstructured phosphorescence with extremely large Stokes shifts. Experimental and theoretical studies indicate that Au–Au covalent bonds form in the distorted Au-centred phosphorescent excited state.36 Photolysis of [Au(L)Cl] [L = CO, (p-tosyl)CH2NC, (alkyl)NC] results in the formation of Au(III) and Au(0) photoproducts; the better π-accepting ability of L the better the photoproduct quantum yield.37 The d10 complexes [M(PPh3)(L6)] (M = Cu, Ag, Au) all display an intense absorption at ∼600 nm and solution luminescence, which could potentially be suitable for applications in vivo.38 Pentanuclear Au(I)–Cu(I) and Au(I)–Ag(I) mixed metal alkynyl complexes [nBu4N][Au3M2(CC-C6H4-p-R)6] (M = Cu, Ag; R = alkoxy group) display emission from [(CC-C6H4-p-R) → Au3M2] or [(AuCC-C6H4-p-R) → Au3M2] charge-transfer triplet excited states.39 The emission energy of the highly luminescent cluster compounds [E(AuL)3M]2+ [M = Cu, Ag; E = O, S, Se; L = PPh2-py, PPh2(CH2)2-py] is influenced by the chalcogenide, and varies from blue for O to green for S to orange for Se.40  In the solid state, the dinuclear complex {[Tl(η6-toluene)][Au(C6Cl5)2]} displays intense blue phosphorescence (3MMCT) and rigidochromic behaviour.41 The macrocyclic octanuclear cluster cyclo-{[Pt(C6Cl5)2(μ-OH)(μ-Ag)]4} surprisingly displays bright long-lived green–yellow emission (τ = 5–6 μs, λem = 521 nm) from a 3(dσ*pσ) excited state related to the Pt–Ag bonding interactions.42Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexesA variety of zinc based complexes have been developed for the fluorescent sensing of biologically relevant compounds. The position of these 3MLCT states markedly affects photo-decarboxylation from this complex, initiated in 3LLCT (RCO2 → NN) states.81 The luminescence spectra of [Re(CO)4(phen)]+ and [Re(CO)3CN(phen)] are assigned to emission from 3MLCT states (τ = 1–3 μs), whereas for [Re(CO)4(N^C)] (N^C = benzo[h]quinoline) emission is from the 3LC state (τ = 35 μs).
