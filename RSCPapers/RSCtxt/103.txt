Introduction
As portable electric devices become more and more popular, wearable devices (solar cells,1 supercapacitors,2 and generators3) have been rapidly attracting attention. Fiber-shaped solar cells can be woven into wearable energy cloths (such as coats, hats or bags) to charge portable electric devices in a very handy way.4 Currently, fiber-shaped dye-sensitized solar cells (FDSSCs), in sharp contrast to the flat counterpart, represent typical fiber-configuration electronics for solar energy harvesting.5–13 A typical FDSSC consists of a fiber-shaped photoanode (an n-type semiconductor film sensitized with a dye), a redox electrolyte (an organic liquid solvent dissolving with I3—/I— redox species), and a fiber-shaped counter electrode (CE).
Optimization of the I3— reduction reaction conducted with a CE is of utmost significance for enhancement of the photovoltaic performance of a FDSSC. Pt fibers have proven to be well stable and highly efficient electrocatalysts in liquid state FDSSCs for the I3—/I— redox couple. Motivated by practical and economical considerations, great effort has been made to explore low-cost, Pt-free CEs, such as specific transition metal carbides,14 nitrides14,15 and chalcogens.16–24 The Pt-free CE used for the FDSSC generally grows on a tread-like conductive substrate that collects electrons from the external load and transfers electrons into the electrocatalyst film.13–24 The good performance of the Pt-free CE could be achieved through mediating the features of the CE, including improving the surface area, enhancing the electrical conductivity, or exposing high-activity crystal facets.
In this paper, we directly grew single-crystalline metal (Ni, Co) selenium nanosheets on metal fibers (briefly called M–M0.85Se) toward low-cost, high-performance FDDSCs. The growing M0.85Se nanosheet layer acts as electrocatalytic centers for the reduction of I3—, and the underlying metal fiber as a conductive collector for collecting electrons. Photovoltaic measurements demonstrate that the M–M0.85Se fibers thus obtained show good electrocatalytic behavior as CEs for high-efficiency FDSSC (Ni–Ni0.85Se for 7.07% and Co–Co0.85Se for 6.55%), comparable or even superior to Pt fibers (6.54%). The good performance of the present Pt-free CE-based solar cell was believed to originate from: (1) the intrinsic electrocatalytic properties of the single-crystalline M–M0.85Se; (2) the enough void space among M0.85Se nanosheets that allows easier redox ion diffusion; (3) the two-dimensional morphology that provides a large contact area between the CE catalytic material and electrolyte; (4) in situ direct growth of the M0.85Se on metal fibers that renders good electrical contact between the active material and the electron collector.
Results and discussion
X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns show that the growing nickel selenium and cobalt selenium on the metal fiber could be well indexed to hexagonal Ni0.85Se (JCPDS no. 18-0888) and hexagonal Co0.85Se (JCPDS no. 52-1008), respectively (Fig. 1). The field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM) images clearly show that the Ni0.85Se nanosheets were uniformly in situ grown on and fully covered the underlying Ni fiber (Fig. 2b and c). The high-resolution FE-SEM image further shows that the formed Ni0.85Se nanosheets are about tens of nm in thickness and 1 μm in width, and nearly vertically stand on the fiber substrate (Fig. 2d and e). Particularly, the nanosheets were observed to cross-link together to form a porous architecture with the pore size ranging from 200 to 500 nm, as marked in Fig. 2d. Similarly, the generated Co0.85Se also exhibits a nanosheet-like morphology with random arrangement, assembling into a foam-like structure with the pore size of hundreds of nm (Fig. 2g–i). The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images further reveal the two-dimensional morphology of Ni0.85Se and Co0.85Se (Fig. 3a and d). The order dot patterns of selected area electron diffraction (SAED) demonstrate that both the Ni0.85Se and Co0.85Se nanosheets are single crystals (Fig. 3c and f), implying the potential good electrocatalytic properties due to less defects in single-crystal materials. In addition, the TEM image also shows the ultrathin feature of the Co0.85Se nanosheets with the thickness smaller than 10 nm (Fig. 3d).

The FDSSC devices were fabricated with the various CEs of similar cell configuration (the preparation process details are shown in the ESI†), schematically illustrated in Fig. 4a. The M–M0.85Se or Pt fiber was twisted with the TiO2 nanotube array-coated Ti fiber (Fig. S1†) as photoanodes, which was then inserted into a transparent capillary filled with the electrolyte to be assembled into a FDSSC. The FDSSCs with the M–M0.85Se fiber CEs achieved the power conversion efficiency of 7.07% (Ni–Ni0.85Se) and 6.55% (Co–Co0.85Se), respectively, comparable or even superior to that of Pt fiber CE (6.54%) (Fig. 4b and Table 1). A slightly higher power conversion efficiency of the Ni–Ni0.85Se arises from a higher photocurrent density (JSC).

To further reveal the high performance of the M0.85Se nanosheets, the catalytic activity of the M–M0.85Se fibers was analysed using cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements in a three-electrode system. The details of the tests are described in the ESI.† Two pairs of oxidation and reduction peaks in CV cures were observed, which are consistent with the as-referred Pt fiber electrode (Fig. 5). In the CV curves, the pair of redox peaks at low potential correspond to the I3−/I− redox reactions, while the pair of redox peaks at high potential can be attributed to I3−/I2 redox reactions.14b,23 The electrocatalytic activity of the CE can be evaluated from the two parameters of the CV curve, i.e. the peak-to-peak separation (ΔEp) and the peak current density, and the characteristics of low potential pair peak separation are generally considered extremely important in analyzing the catalytic activity of CEs in DSSCs. Both ΔEp values of the Ni–Ni0.85Se and Co–Co0.85Se are lower than that of the Pt fiber (Fig. 5), implying that the charge transfer rate (ks) of both M–M0.85Se fiber electrodes is higher than that of the Pt fiber electrode as the ΔEp varies inversely with ks.14b The Ni–Ni0.85Se electrode exhibits a higher redox peak current density than Pt and Co–Co0.85Se fibers, which may be contributed to the higher power conversion efficiency.
 Fig. 5  CV curves for Ni–Ni0.85Se, Co–Co0.85Se and Pt fibers. 
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) experiments were also carried out through using symmetric cells with two identical electrodes (CE/electrolyte/CE) under dark conditions. Fig. 6 shows the Nyquist plots of M–M0.85Se fiber and Pt fiber electrodes, respectively. The first arc arises from the charge-transfer resistance (Rct) at the CE/electrolyte interface, and the second arc relates to the Nernstian diffusion element (ZN).14b The series resistance (Rs) corresponds to the intercept of the first arc on the real axis. Rs values of Ni–Ni0.85Se, Co–Co0.85Se, and Pt fiber electrodes were measured to be 2.08 Ω cm2, 2.06 Ω cm2, and 1.96 Ω cm2, respectively. Rct of the three electrodes were in the sequence of Ni–Ni0.85Se (0.38 Ω cm2) < Co–CoxSe (0.65 Ω cm2) < Pt (1.04 Ω cm2). The smaller Rct of the Ni–Ni0.85Se fiber proves that the Ni0.85Se nanosheets exhibit better catalytic activity in the reduction of I3— ions than Pt fibers and Co0.85Se, which is consistent with the conclusion obtained from the analysis of CV. The larger ZN of M–M0.85Se fibers originates from the M0.85Se layers with several μm thickness, which increases the resistance of I3−/I− ion diffusion.
 Fig. 6  Nyquist plots of the symmetric cells with two identical counter electrodes of Ni–Ni0.85Se, Co–Co0.85Se and Pt fibers. 
Conclusion
The unique Ni0.85Se and Co0.85Se nanosheets were successfully in situ grown on the metal (Ni or Co) fiber through one-step solvothermal reaction. Both Ni–Ni0.85Se fibers and Co–Co0.85Se fibers exhibit excellent electrochemical catalytic activity for the reduction of I3− to I− in FDSSCs. The intrinsic electrocatalytic properties, porous architectures, two-dimensional morphology, and single crystal feature of M0.85Se were believed to account for the excellent photovoltaic performance of the corresponding FDSSCs. This work has tremendous implications that substitution of low-cost, easily handled M–M0.85Se fiber CEs with good performance for the precious metal Pt will foster the development of FDSSCs and reduce the cost for energy conversion.
