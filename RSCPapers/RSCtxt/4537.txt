    , Accepted 1st September 2016First published on 7th September 2016AbstractTwo regioregular narrow bandgap conjugated polymers (PM1 and PM2) containing the repeat unit BDT-PT-CPDT-PT (BDT = benzodithiophene, PT = pyridyl[2,1,3]thiadiazole, CPDT = cyclopentadithiophene) and different solubilizing alkyl side chains were prepared with the goal of understanding how chemical structure impacts the performance of low Voc loss bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells containing PC61BM as the acceptor semiconductor. Both polymers show nearly identical orbital energy levels, a face-on orientation relative to the surface normal, and can be processed to yield continuous fiber-like networks in the active layer. Due to the choice of repeat units within the backbone structure, PM1 and PM2 exhibit shorter π–π stacking distances, relative to the previously reported low Voc loss regioregular polymer PIPCP. Finally, PM1 achieves an average PCE of 6.2 ± 0.2% and PM2 achieves an average PCE of 7.2 ± 0.1%. Devices exhibit low Voc loss and high short circuit current Jsc, but, most significantly, display improved fill factors compared to previously reported PIPCP. A discussion is provided that seeks to identify structural features in conjugated polymers that lead to devices with low Voc loss and high external quantum efficiencies.
Introduction
Organic semiconductors remain under study within the framework of fabricating light-weight, flexible polymer solar cells (PSCs)1via solution processing methods that rely on low toxicity materials.2 Power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) in excess of 10% have been reported with polymer/fullerene bulk heterojunction (BHJ) active layer structures, which have highlighted the development of donor polymers and the importance of thin film processing.3 A relevant opportunity for further improvement concerns controlling and optimizing the open circuit voltage (Voc). Of relevance is that the Voc loss (here defined as Eloss = Eg − eVoc, where Eg is the optical bandgap) in PSCs is usually higher than that observed in inorganic or hybrid solar cells.4 While significant progress has been made in other device parameters, including the short current density (Jsc)5 and fill factor (FF),6 guidelines for Voc improvements have proven more difficult to attain. New donor materials based on narrow bandgap conjugated polymers that target low Voc loss, while maintaining high Jsc and high FF, are an important topic for design and mechanistic studies.
The polymer PIPCP was recently reported and shown to exhibit a Voc loss of ∼0.52 eV in PSCs fabricated under various film processing conditions.7 The general structural design can be described as a regioregular “Weak Donor–Strong Acceptor–Strong Donor–Strong Acceptor” strategy.8 Such backbone structures can successfully tune the optical bandgap and yield well-ordered polymer crystallite phases in spin-coated thin films. Most importantly, among narrow bandgap PSCs (Eg < 1.5 eV), the Voc of PIPCP device is surprisingly high, around 0.85–0.90 V. However, solar cells containing PIPCP are limited by low FF values despite a range of compositions and processing conditions, which counteract the benefit of high Voc. One possible way to improve FF is to increase the crystallinity and decrease the π–π stacking distance between the polymer chains.3a,3c,6 PIPCP films exhibit a relatively large π–π stacking distance at about 4.3 Å, which may be decreased through molecular design and processing conditions.
Based on the comments above, we modified the molecular structure of the backbone with the general goal of improving inter-chain order in the solid state. As shown in Fig. 1, the design maintains the theme of using the strong donor unit cyclopentadithiophene (CPDT) and the strong acceptor unit pyridyl[2,1,3]thiadiazole (PT) to favour a narrow bandgap via intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) from CPDT to PT.8 The benzodithiophene (BDT)9 unit is incorporated as a substitute for the indacenodithiophene (IDT) in the PIPCP backbone. Our rationale is that IDT-based polymers can show relatively large π–π stacking distances, such as 4.1 Å reported for IDTBT polymer films.10 This is most reasonably due to the four bulky, out-of-plane side chains close to the polymer backbone, which prevent adjacent polymer chains from packing tightly.11 As for IDT, the BDT unit also contains a ladder-type conjugated framework with appropriate electron-donating ability, but it provides two in-plane conjugated positions that can be substituted to tune both the electronic structure of the backbone and overall solubility of the material.12 As a result, π-face-on orientation with short π–π stacking distances have been observed in high performance BDT-based PSCs, factors that favor good charge transport, especially relevant in thick active layer devices.13 Therefore, introducing the BDT substructure into a PIPCP-like framework provides the opportunity to understand the role of side chain engineering on the organization of regioregular narrow bandgap conjugated polymers.14

Results and discussion
Design, synthesis and characterization of PM1 and PM2
In this contribution, we provide the synthesis of two new polymers, namely PM1 and PM2, which can be described in the regioregular fashion of “BDT-PT-CPDT-PT”. As shown in Scheme 1, the dibromo monomer “PT-CPDT-PT” was synthesized via literature precedent.7 The corresponding BDT bis-stannylated starting materials were prepared through a multi-step process adapted from the literature.15 In the case of PM1, 3-decylthiophene was brominated using NBS and a second decyl side chain was added using the appropriate Grignard reagent and dichloro[1,3-bis(diphenylphosphino)propane]-nickel as the catalyst, ultimately providing 2,3-didecylthiophene. Subsequently, 2,3-didecylthiophene was deprotonated, reacted with benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene-4,8-dione, and dehydrated using stannous chloride in aqueous hydrochloric solution. The resulting BDT derivative was stannylated by using a standard lithiation/stannylation sequence. For PM2, 2-octyldodecyl alcohol underwent a substitution reaction using elemental bromine, the resulting product was converted to a Grignard reagent and was reacted immediately with 2-bromothiophene to produce 2-octyldodecylthiophene. Then, the stannylated monomer was synthesized using the same process as PM1. Finally, the dibrominated PT-CPDT-PT core and the corresponding bis-stannyl BDT precursors were reacted under microwave assisted Stille polymerization using Pd(PPh3)4 as the catalyst and xylene as the solvent. The BDT structural unit in PM1 contains two linear decyl chains on the thiophene side group, whereas PM2 has a BDT unit with a branched 2-octyldodecyl on each thiophene. Further details on the preparation and characterization of PM1 and PM2 can be found in the ESI (page 3–6†). PM1 can be obtained with a number average molecular weight (Mn) of 83 kDa with a dispersity (Đ) of 2.2, while for PM2 one finds Mn = 83 kDa with Đ = 2.0. Variations in polymer properties in subsequent characterization studies can therefore be reasonably attributed to the BDT side-chain variations rather than differences in molecular weight.

Absorption spectra of solutions and thin films of PM1 and PM2 in the 300–1000 nm region are shown in Fig. 2. One immediately observes that their absorption profiles are red-shifted relative to that of PIPCP both in solution and when prepared as thin films. Substituting the IDT unit in PIPCP with a BDT unit thus lowers the bandgap of the polymers. As determined by the absorption onset, the Eg of neat PM1 and PM2 is ∼1.42 eV, while the Eg of neat PIPCP is 1.47 eV. PM1 and PM2 exhibit absorption bands in the 350–450 nm region, which are typically attributed to π–π* transitions. In a chlorobenzene solution, PM1 shows an ICT transition with a vibronic peak at 722 nm and 790 nm respectively. The corresponding ICT and vibronic peaks for PM2 occur at 725 nm and 783 nm respectively. Temperature dependent absorption measurements in solution show that the lowest energy transition peak intensities decrease gradually with increasing temperature. These changes are reversible (ESI, page 7†), and indicate that there is substantial aggregation of polymer chains in solution. It is also noted that the absorption spectra of thin films in Fig. 2 are similar to those in solution at room temperature. These results suggest a good degree of structural order, both within the polymer backbone and in the multi-chain aggregates present in solution.
 Fig. 2  UV-vis absorption: (a) in solution; (b) in thin films. 
Thin film cyclic voltammetry (CV) was used to estimate HOMO energy levels by determining the onset of oxidation (ESI, page 9†).16 LUMO energy levels were subsequently determined by addition of Eg to the HOMO energies. The HOMO levels of both PM1 and PM2 are at approximately −5.3 ± 0.1 eV, consistent with their similarity in molecular structures. LUMO levels were calculated to be around −3.9 ± 0.1 eV. It is worth pointing out that there is considerable error associated with the use of CV for establishing orbital energy levels, including the determination of the redox onsets, structural heterogeneity at the electrode surface, film quality, and diffusion of charge compensating ions. For example, the LUMO level of PC61BM has been reported in the −3.7 to −4.3 eV range.17 We examined the reduction onset of neat polymer films and polymer/PC61BM (1/1, w/w) blend films. The reduction onset of the neat film is ∼0.3 eV lower than the blend (ESI, page 10†). This difference provides a reasonable estimate of the LUMO–LUMO offset between the PIPCP, PM1 or PM2 and PC61BM.
Solar cell fabrication and characterization
Single-junction solar cells were fabricated using an inverted device structure comprising: ITO/ZnO/(PM1 or PM2):PC61BM/MoO3/Ag. Specifically, approximately 40 nm of zinc oxide was deposited as a thin film atop indium tin oxide (ITO) coated glass substrates. Polymer:PC61BM blends were spin-coated, followed by the evaporation of 4 nm of molybdenum oxide and 40 nm of silver. A preliminary screening of BHJ compositions and deposition conditions was carried out. The following polymer:PC61BM weight ratios were tested: 1:1, 1:2 and 1:3. The 1:1 combination performed best among these conditions. Solutions were prepared in chlorobenzene and 2% by volume 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO). The total concentration was 21 mg mL−1, and the spin-cast speed was set to 1000 rpm, films were annealed at 100 °C for 10 minutes, which provided an active layer thickness of approximately 110 nm.
As shown in Table 1, average PCEs were 6.2 ± 0.2% for PM1 and 7.2 ± 0.1% for PM2 based on 16 devices. Fig. 3a provides the current density vs. voltage characteristics observed for the highest performing cells. PM1:PC61BM devices achieve a Jsc of 13.6 mA cm−2, a Voc of 0.80 V, a FF of 60%, and finally a PCE of 6.5%. The Jsc calculated from the external quantum efficiency (EQE) plot in Fig. 3b is 14.3 mA cm−2. PM2:PC61BM devices provide a Jsc of 14.3 mA cm−2, a Voc of 0.79 V, a FF of 65%, and finally a PCE of 7.4%. The Jsc calculated from the EQE is 14.5 mA cm−2. Both polymers show Voc ∼ 0.80 V, which indicates minimal impact by the alkyl chain variations on relevant energy levels. The Jsc values are also very close to each other, but PM2:PC61BM devices exhibit higher FFs (average 0.65 ± 0.01) relative to PM1:PC61BM devices (average 0.58 ± 0.02). Of particular note, is that these FF values are higher than those observed for PIPCP:PC61BM devices, see Table 1.

PC71BM was also tested as the acceptor semiconductor under similar processing conditions to those described above. Relevant solar cell parameters for PM1:PC71BM and PM2:PC71BM devices are summarized in Table 2. Comparison of Tables 1 and 2 shows that PC61BM and PC71BM provide remarkably similar performances. Fig. 4 allows one to compare the EQE plots for the four different polymer/acceptor combinations. Focusing on the 300–600 nm region one observes a higher maximum EQE value for both PM1 and PM2 when blended with PC71BM. For example, in Fig. 4b, the PM2:PC71BM device has an EQE peak of 48% at 480 nm, while the PM2:PC61BM device has an EQE peak of 42% at 390 nm. The higher EQE value in this region is typically attributed to the contribution by PC71BM absorption. However, in the region of 600–900 nm, the peak EQE value of PM2:PC61BM device is 66% at 780 nm, while that of PM2:PC71BM device is 58% at 790 nm. These differences may be related to morphological variations in the bulk heterojunction blend when blending PC61BM vs. PC71BM.19 Note that similar trends are observed with PM1 shown in Fig. 4a. For both systems, the EQE values in the two absorption regimes counterbalance, leading to similar Jsc values for the two acceptors.

Morphological characterization of thin films
Grazing incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) experiments were used to examine the organization of polymer chains.20 Results for the neat polymers and their blend films are shown in Fig. 5. Peaks at qxy = 0.27 Å−1 are observed in the in-plane direction for both neat polymer films, which are assigned to side-chain stacking and correspond to a lamella distance of ∼2.3 nm. The alkyl-chain stacking is likely dominated by the longer chains on the BDT unit, in comparison with 2-ethylhexyl on the CPDT unit (ESI, page 12†). In addition, the PM1 side-chain diffraction peak extends to the out-of-plane direction while the PM2 side-chain diffraction peak is primarily localized in the in-plane direction. Both PM1 and PM2 display strong peaks in the out-of-plane direction at qz = 1.62 Å−1 for PM1 and qz = 1.67 Å−1 for PM2. These peaks are attributed to the π–π stacking and their orientation suggests a face-on arrangement of the polymer backbones relative to the surface normal. Using these qz values one can determine π–π distances of 3.9 Å in PM1 and 3.8 Å in PM2, which are almost identical to each other and are both shorter than that determined from PIPCP films (∼4.3 Å). PM2 contains branched solubilizing groups on the BDT thiophene fragments, whereas PM1 has two linear C10 alkyl groups. How the different steric requirements impact the overall organization in the solid state is not obvious at this time. However, the well-ordered structure and π-face-on orientation suggest favourable hole transport in the polymer phases of their PSCs.

Blend morphologies were also probed by bright-field transmission electron microscopy (BF-TEM), and the results are provided in Fig. 6a–d. The bright regions in the BF-TEM images are assigned to the polymer phase, while the dark regions are assigned to the fullerene phase. For both PM1:PC61BM (Fig. 6avs.c) and PM2:PC61BM (Fig. 6bvs.d), there is a significant reduction in the size of the donor and acceptor phases when the films are cast from the solvent system used to attain optimal device performance, relative to the as-cast films. Indeed, one finds that the addition of DIO leads to morphologies that are nearly indistinguishable from each other and that contain a continuous interpenetrating network of polymer fibres with widths of 20–40 nm. The high boiling point additive DIO therefore serves the role of controlling overgrowth of the two phases.21

Discussion of energy losses
Using Eg values determined from the absorption onsets of the neat polymer films, we determine that the Voc losses (Eloss = Eg − eVoc = 1.4–0.8 eV) for PM1 and PM2 devices are on the order of 0.6 eV, slightly higher to what was reported for PIPCP:PC61BM.7b Recent studies also indicate that Voc is influenced by the charge transfer state (CTS) energy gap (Ect).22 Koen et al. developed the following empirical relationship23 upon examination of several organic photovoltaics systems:eVoc = Ect − 0.6 ± 0.1
Using the above relationship and that eVoc = Eg − 0.6 ± 0.1 in PM1:PC61BM and/or PM2:PC61BM, suggests that Ect would have to be close in energy relative to Eg. For comparison, devices fabricated using PBDTTPD:PC61BM blends (PBDTTPD = poly(di(2-ethylhexyloxy)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene-co-octylthieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione)) show Eg − Ect of ∼0.38 eV.24
It is also worth noting that there is a relatively small LUMO offset (ΔELL) compared to the fullerene (∼0.3 eV) in PIPCP/PM1/PM2 materials as discussed in the CV measurements section, a necessary condition previously established for achieving small Eloss.1,8,25Table 3 provides a selected sampling from the literature of systems for which Eloss ∼ 0.6 eV and ΔELL ≤ 0.3 eV. For example, Janssen et al. reported OPV devices for which Eloss < 0.6 eV and ΔELL ∼ 0.2 eV.26 Other donor materials in Table 3 include PDPP2Tz2T, PDPP2TzDTP and DPPEZnP-TEH, which also display small ΔELL.27 And recently the polymer PNOz4T could even achieve a PCE of ∼9% with ΔELL ∼0.1 eV.27f As mentioned above, the most obvious molecular design strategy available to obtain low Eloss in polymer/fullerene devices remains achieving a small ΔELL through chemical structure modifications that target lowering the LUMO energy of the polymer close to the LUMO energy of the acceptor.1,8,25 Less clear is how these modifications impact Ect, although theoretical efforts are highlighting the importance of the orientation and close proximity between the polymer and fullerene in this context.28 Despite these new insights, the overall intermolecular arrangements are driven by relatively weak forces that are difficult to preprogram at the chemical structure level and are dependent on the processing conditions.29 A well understood drawback of minimizing ΔELL is that one may reach a situation where the driving force for photo-induced electron transfer is lost. Indeed, one finds suggestions in the literature that ΔELL needs to be greater than 0.2–0.3 eV.30 These considerations are relevant when examining the properties of several materials26,31,32 whose LUMO levels are close to that of PCBM, and show low Eloss, at the expense of Jsc or the FF, relative to the examples provided in Table 3.

In light of the preceding discussion it is worth examining the impact of structural variations on four of the donor materials in Table 3, namely PDPP2Tz2T, PDPP2TzDTP, X6, PNOz4T and PSiNO, see Fig. 7. Fig. 7 also compares the EQE and Eloss of these materials relative to close structural derivatives with weaker acceptor fragments within the conjugated donor framework. In the case of DTPDPP4T (Eg = 1.43 eV, ELUMO = −3.64 eV, Voc = 0.64 V)34 and PDPP2Tz2T (Eg = 1.47 eV, ELUMO = −3.95 eV, Voc = 0.92 V),26 replacement of thiophene for thiazole leads to a lowering of the LUMO level, with a concomitant decrease in Eloss, from 0.79 eV to 0.55 eV. However, one also observes a decrease in the EQE from 60% to 40%. Thiophene for thiazole substitution leads to the same effect when one compares PDPPTDTPT (Eg = 1.23 eV, ELUMO = −3.68 eV, Voc = 0.43 V, ELUMO = 0.8 eV, EQE = 71%)33vs. PDPP2TzDTP (Eg = 1.28 eV, ELUMO = −3.94 eV, Voc = 0.59 V, Eloss = 0.59 eV, EQE = 52%).26 For the molecular systems X2 (Eg = 1.41 eV, ELUMO = −3.71 eV, Voc = 0.66 V) and X6 (Eg = 1.36 eV, ELUMO = −3.79 eV, Voc = 0.75 V), the increased electron affinity of PT relative to BT leads to the same counterbalance of EQE and Eloss.27b For polymer PNTz4T (Eg = 1.56 eV, ELUMO = −3.46 eV, Voc = 0.74 V), replacing naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c′]bis[1,2,5]thiadi-azole (NTz) to the more electron poor unit naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c′]bis[1,2,5]oxadiazole (NOz) reduces the Eloss in the PNTz4T devices (Eg = 1.52 eV, ELUMO = −3.65 eV, Voc = 0.97 V), but the EQE decreases from 75% to 66%.27f However, there are examples that show that this trend is not universal, as illustrated by examination of PSiBO (Eg = 1.70 eV, ELUMO = −3.55 eV, Voc = 0.84 V) and PSiNO (Eg = 1.56 eV, ELUMO = −3.73 eV, Voc = 0.90 V) where 2,1,3-benzoxadiazole (BO) is replaced for NOz. In this case, Eloss decreases from 0.84 eV to 0.66 eV, however EQE increases from 47% to 62%.27c For these situations, one must examine factors extrinsic from purely electronic considerations, for instance general morphological characteristics that impact charge carrier extraction which is also pointed out in the study of high performance PNOz4T device.

Since the set of materials7,26,27b,27d–f that show PCE over 5%, together with Eloss ≤ 0.6 eV is limited, we also examined classes of polymers that lead to devices with Eloss ≤ 0.7 eV with the intent of identifying possible structural commonalities (ESI, page 15†). Interestingly, two structural units frequently encountered include diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP)35 and benzothiadiazole (BT) derivatives,36 which are often used as electron deficient subunits in the design of narrow bandgap absorbers. Identifying these substructures suggests that their incorporation in materials design has a good probability to achieve low Eloss with Jsc > 10 mA cm−2. One possible consideration may be that these chemical structures lead to appropriate interactions with fullerenes so that Ect is not much lower than Eg, when the appropriate side-chain modifications are included and processing conditions are managed.28,29
Conclusions
To summarize, we disclose the synthesis of two novel narrow bandgap polymers with regioregular structural features similar to PIPCP with the goal of understanding how the chemical structure impacts the OPV performance. Both PM1 and PM2 can be used to prepare active layers for which Eloss ∼ 0.6 eV, which is close to previous observations with PIPCP. Unlike PIPCP, for which FF ∼ 50%, one can achieve FF values of >60% through the proper use of the solvent additive DIO and thermal management of the resulting film. The role of DIO, as determined by TEM analysis, is to minimize overgrowth of individual donor and acceptor phases and promote the formation of fibrous interconnected polymer domains within the BHJ morphology. We noticed that PM1/PM2 exhibited higher crystallinity and shorter π–π distance than PIPCP, and also form suitable fibre networks37 in the OPV blends. A relevant observation is that despite the small ΔELL (∼0.3 eV), PM1/PM2:PC61BM devices exhibit EQEs of over 60%. It appears that the PT-CPDT-PT structural fragment is useful as a basis to generate polymer donors that, when in the presence of fullerene acceptors, give rise to devices with morphological characteristics for effective charge generation despite small ΔELL, and local intermolecular arrangements that lead to CTS energies similar to Eg.
