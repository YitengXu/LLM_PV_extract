    , Accepted 26th July 2017First published on 27th July 2017AbstractPoly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) is a popular and promising hole transport material for making efficient inverted-planar perovskite solar cells (IP-PSCs). However, the mismatch between the work function of conventional PEDOT:PSS and the valence band maximum of perovskite materials is still a challenge for efficient hole extraction. Here, we report systematic studies on the work-function modification and thermodynamic morphological evolution of PEDOT:PSS films by tuning the PSS/PEDOT ratio, along with its effects on the photovoltaic responses of IP-PSCs. We found that the open-circuit voltage (VOC) of an IP-PSC could be enhanced by controlling the work function of PEDOT:PSS. Furthermore, the optical transmittance of the PEDOT:PSS film could be maximized by controlling the morphological evolution, which will further increase the short-circuit current density (JSC) of the IP-PSC. The VOC and JSC of the IP-PSC with the optimized PEDOT:PSS composition increased from 0.88 to 0.93 V and from 17.11 to 20.77 mA cm−2, respectively, compared with an IP-PSC containing commercial PEDOT:PSS, which results in a power conversion energy that is greatly improved from 12.39 to 15.24%.
Introduction
Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) containing ABX3-type organic lead halides have attracted attention around the world due to their high power conversion efficiency (PCE), low cost, tunable bandgap and superior electronic properties.1–3 Over the past few years, the PCE of PSCs has rapidly increased from 3.8% to certified PCEs of up to 22.1%4, as a result of pioneering works5–7 and subsequent studies.8–10 Conventional PSCs with an n–i–p structure including mesoporous metal oxides can exhibit superior photovoltaic responses, but require a long time for device fabrication, due to a high-temperature thermal annealing process. In this regard, many researchers have studied planar PSCs employing low-temperature processable metal oxide electron transport layers11–14 or inverted-planar perovskite solar cells (IP-PSCs) with a p–i–n structure from low-temperature solution processability.15–17 To date, various conductive polymers have been employed as hole transport layers (HTLs), such as poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS), poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA), poly(p-phenylene), poly(4,4-bis(N-carbazolyl)-1,1-biphenyl) (PPN), poly(N,N′-bis-4-butylphenyl-N,N′-bisphenyl)benzidine (poly-TPD) and poly(3-carboxydithiophene) (P3CT).18–23 Among them, PEDOT:PSS,24–27 which has been popularly used as a HTL for various organic optoelectronic devices, is a promising HTL material for high-efficiency IP-PSCs because of its high conductivity, optical transparency in the visible region and simple low-temperature solution processability (T < 120 °C).17,28
Even though PEDOT:PSS has already been commercialized due to the aforementioned advantages, its hygroscopic and strongly acidic characteristics have been clearly noted as serious drawbacks, as these factors present potential risk to the stable and long-term operation of optoelectronic devices.25,26 More importantly, mismatch between the work function (WF) of PEDOT:PSS (∼4.8–5.1 eV) and the valence band maximum (VBM) of perovskite materials (e.g., VBMMAPbI3 = −5.43 eV) has recently surfaced. Due to the non-ideal WF of PEDOT:PSS, IP-PSCs containing this material produced a low open-circuit voltage (VOC), resulting in low PCEs of approximately 12–13%.29–32 To increase the VOC of PSCs containing PEDOT:PSS, studies have focused on developing methods for modifying the PEDOT:PSS HTL with various post-treatment techniques33,34 and additives.35–42 Previous studies increased the WF of PEDOT:PSS by employing various modification methods, which led to an enhanced VOC, thereby improving the PCE of the IP-PSC device. Therefore, tuning the composition of the PEDOT and PSS moieties to control the WF of PEDOT:PSS may be a simple and effective way to obtain a better photovoltaic response in IP-PSC devices.43–45 Unfortunately, this method for optimizing IP-PSCs has not yet been thoroughly studied, as only a limited number of commercial PEDOT:PSS materials with unknown additives are available.
In this study, we synthesized various PEDOT:PSS solutions by controlling the ratio of PEDOT to PSS from 1:0.5 (PEDOT1:PSS0.5) to 1:12 (PEDOT1:PSS12) and then used the materials as HTLs in IP-PSC devices, as schematically shown in Fig. 1a. The surface properties of the PEDOT:PSS films with different amounts of PSS, including the surface chemical composition, film morphology, surface energy and the WF of PEDOT:PSS, were investigated for their application in IP-PSCs. As the PSS/PEDOT ratio increased, the WF of the PEDOT:PSS film gradually increased from −4.81 to −5.36 eV (see Fig. 1b), and the film showed an enhanced PCE due to the VOC variation. Furthermore, we found that not only the electronic energy-level state but also the optical transmittance of the PEDOT:PSS film can increase as the PSS/PEDOT ratio increases due to the compositional ratio variation, which will further influence the short-circuit current density (JSC) of the IP-PSC. The VOC and JSC of an IP-PSC with the optimized composition of PEDOT:PSS increased from 0.88 to 0.93 V and from 17.11 to 20.77 mA cm−2, respectively, compared with an IP-PSC containing commercial PEDOT:PSS, which results in a PCE that is greatly improved from 12.39 to 15.24%. However, when the PSS/PEDOT ratio exceeded its optimum point at approximately 1:5.0 (PEDOT1:PSS5.0), the photovoltaic response of the PSC decreased slightly. We also analyzed the influence of the modified PEDOT:PSS film on the photovoltaic response of IP-PSCs using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS).

Results and discussion
Surface characterization of PEDOT:PSS films with controlled PSS/PEDOT ratios
The surface composition of the PEDOT and PSS components in PEDOT:PSS films prepared from various PEDOT:PSS solutions with different amounts of PSS was investigated by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (Fig. 2a). Fig. 2a shows the S 2p spectra of the PEDOT:PSS films. Because the sulfur atoms in PEDOT and PSS are in different molecular environments, the S 2p electrons have different binding energies; the higher binding energy peak at approximately 169 eV corresponds to the sulfur atoms in the sulfonic acids of PSS, and the lower binding energy peak at 164 eV corresponds to the sulfur atoms in PEDOT. Therefore, the component ratio of PSS to PEDOT at the surface can be estimated using the intensity ratio of the peaks assigned to PSS and PEDOT.25,43–46As the PSS content in the PEDOT:PSS solutions increased, the intensities of the S 2p peaks representing the PSS/PEDOT ratio at the film surface increased. To further explore the PSS/PEDOT ratio of the synthesized polymer films, the water contact angle was measured, as shown in Fig. 2b. As the PSS content increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS12, the water contact angle of the PEDOT:PSS film decreased gradually from 35° to 12°. Thus, as we increase the PSS content in the PEDOT:PSS solution, the surface of the spin-coated, solid PEDOT:PSS film becomes more hydrophilic. These findings confirm that a PSS-enriched surface was present on top of the polymer films due to thermodynamic vertical phase separation.46
 Fig. 2  (a) XPS, (b) contact angle (inset: a picture of the measurement) and (c) AFM images of various PEDOT:PSS films with controlled PSS/PEDOT ratios (top: height, bottom: phase). All AFM images are displayed on a 1 μm × 1 μm scale. 
To examine the morphology of the films with different PSS contents, atomic force microscopy (AFM) images were obtained, as shown in Fig. 2c. The AFM phase image of the PEDOT:PSS film shows phase separation of the PEDOT and PSS components, in which the bright (positive) and dark (negative) phase shifts correspond to PEDOT-rich grains and PSS-rich grains, respectively. We observed that the roughness (Rq) of the films decreased as the PSS content increased, from 7.002 nm for PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to 1.259 nm for PEDOT1:PSS12. The phase images showed that the PEDOT grain size decreased as the PSS content increased in the PEDOT:PSS films. Colloidal theory47 tells us that the decrease in PEDOT grain size with increasing PSS content is due to the PSS acting as a primary dopant as well as a surfactant in the PEDOT:PSS solution. Fig. 3a shows a schematic illustration of the PEDOT:PSS films with different PSS/PEDOT ratios. As mentioned earlier, due to vertical phase separation, all PEDOT:PSS films have a PSS-enriched top surface layer. Presumably, though the PEDOT:PSS films had the same overall thickness, the thickness of the PSS-rich top layer changed as the PSS/PEDOT ratio increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS12.
 Fig. 3  (a) Schematic of the compositional morphology, (b) UPS spectra and (c) optical transmittance spectra of various PEDOT:PSS films on ITO glass depending on the PSS/PEDOT ratio. 
From the results of the morphological analysis, we can assume that the electronic energy states and optical properties of the PEDOT:PSS layers can be tuned by the PSS/PEDOT ratio. Fig. 3b presents the He I (21.22 eV) spectra of the PEDOT:PSS films, obtained by UV photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS), with their corresponding WF values as a function of the PEDOT:PSS ratio shown in the inset table of Fig. 3b. It should be noted that PEDOT:PSS films with higher PSS content showed higher WFs, as shown in this table. The presence of the insulating PSS phase at the surface strongly attenuates the signal from the bulk PEDOT. Therefore, the PSS-enriched top surface can have a higher WF than the bulk film. On increasing the PSS content, the WF of the surface could be enhanced from −4.81 (PEDOT1:PSS0.5) to −5.36 eV (PEDOT1:PSS5.0) and saturated around −5.35 eV (PEDOT1:PSS12). The saturated WF of the PEDOT:PSS layer with high PSS content may result from the thick PSS-rich top layer, as the penetration depth of UPS is less than 10 nm. The variation in WF of the PEDOT:PSS layer depending on the PSS/PEDOT ratio will presumably influence the VOC of IP-PSCs containing this material.29–32
To analyze the optical characteristics of the PEDOT:PSS layers with respect to the PSS/PEDOT ratio, we measured the UV/Vis transmittance spectra of the various PEDOT:PSS films on ITO glass, as shown in Fig. 3c. When the PSS/PEDOT ratio increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS12, the optical transmittance of the PEDOT:PSS films increased over the entire wavelength range, with a remarkably increase in the wavelength region from 350 nm to 450 nm and from 600 nm to 800 nm. This trend can be explained by the schematic illustration in Fig. 3a. Because the PEDOT:PSS layers have the same thickness of approximately 40 nm, the PEDOT:PSS film may develop a thinner PEDOT-rich bottom layer as the PSS ratio increases. Previous studies48–51 have shown that PEDOT exhibits a relatively sharp absorption at 300–450 nm in the UV region due to n–π* transitions in the PEDOT polymer backbone, as well as a small absorption band at 600 nm resulting from π–π* transitions and a strong absorption commencing at 700 nm known as the “free carrier tail”. Therefore, as the PSS content increases, the PEDOT:PSS films show improved transmittance in the wavelength range from 350 to 450 nm and from 600 to 800 nm, which will in turn increase light absorption by the perovskite layer, resulting in a higher JSC in the IP-PSCs.
Device characteristics of IP-PSCs based on various PEDOT:PSS HTLs
To investigate the effect of the PSS/PEDOT ratio on the solar cell performance, IP-PSC devices with various PEDOT:PSS HTLs were prepared in the configuration glass/ITO/PEDOT:PSS/CH3NH3PbI3 (MAPbI3)/PC60BM/Ag. The current–voltage (J–V) characteristics of the IP-PSCs are shown in Fig. 4a, and the solar cell parameters of the IP-PSC devices are summarized in Table 1. During the measurements, we did not observe noticeable hysteresis in the devices (Fig. S1†). We measured the steady-state current density and PCE, as shown in Fig. S2,† and we found that all IP-PSC devices containing the various PEDOT:PSS films were stable and showed a good reliability of the J–V scans. When the PSS/PEDOT ratio increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS12, the VOC of the IP-PSC increased gradually from 0.86 V (PEDOT1:PSS0.5) to 0.93 V (PEDOT1:PSS5.0) and decreased to 0.88 V for PEDOT1:PSS12.0. Similarly, we found that the JSC and fill factor (FF) of the devices increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 (JSC = 16.83 mA cm−2, FF = 42.50%) to PEDOT1:PSS5.0 (JSC = 20.77 mA cm−2, FF = 79.12%) but decreased for PEDOT1:PSS12.0 (JSC = 19.34 mA cm−2, FF = 75.33%). The PCE of the IP-PSC with the PEDOT1:PSS5.0 HTL is the best due to its highest VOC and JSC. Compared to conventional PEDOT:PSS (P VP AI 4083), the VOC and JSC of the IP-PSC with the optimized PEDOT:PSS composition increased from 0.88 V to 17.11 mA cm−2 and from 0.93 V to 20.77 mA cm−2, respectively, which resulted in an increase in the PCE from 12.39 to 15.24%, as shown in Fig. 4b.

Generally, the alignment of the HTL WF with the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level of the photoactive material is important to minimize energy loss at the interface.29–31 As mentioned previously (Fig. 3), increasing the PSS content in the PEDOT:PSS film can enhance the WF of PEDOT:PSS due to the increased PSS-rich top layer. As the WF of the PEDOT:PSS film increases from −4.81 (PEDOT1:PSS0.5) to −5.36 eV (PEDOT1:PSS5.0), the energy offset between the PEDOT:PSS WF and the HOMO of MAPbI3 decreased, and the VOC of the devices increased from 0.86 to 0.93 V. Although there is a large WF difference between ITO and PEDOT1:PSS5.0 top surface layers, efficient hole migration from the PEDOT1:PSS5.0 layer to ITO can be expected due to vertical phase separation of the PEDOT1:PSS5.0 layer. In the PEDOT1:PSS5.0 layer, the PEDOT-rich region, which is related to a low work function, is present on the ITO electrode surface, while PSS-enrich region, which is related to a high work function, exists on the top surface. Thus, PEDOT1:PSS5.0 provides a work function gradient and allows holes to efficiently transport from the perovskite layer to the ITO electrode29,52 (as shown in Fig. S3†). The VOC of the IP-PSCs did not increase further when employing a HTL with an even higher content of PSS (PEDOT1:PSS12.0) due to the saturated WF of the PEDOT:PSS layer (−5.35 eV).
Previous studies have reported that a large grain size and reduced grain boundary in the perovskite film could minimize trap-assisted recombination, resulting in an improved VOC in the PSC devices.19,53–57 In general, HTLs with different surface energies could affect the nucleation of the perovskite layer, thereby changing the grain boundary and grain size.19 In Fig. 2b, we confirmed that the wettability of the PEDOT:PSS surface by water depended on the PSS/PEDOT ratio. However, as shown in Fig. S4 and S5,† we could not find notable differences regarding the grain size, grain boundary or film thickness upon varying the PSS/PEDOT ratio, as confirmed by the surface and cross-sectional morphology of the perovskite layers on the various PEDOT:PSS HTLs, obtained by scanning electron microscopy (SEM). To investigate the crystallinity of perovskite films on various PEDOT:PSS films, we performed X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectroscopy, as shown in Fig. S6.† All XRD spectra show typical diffraction peaks for MAPbI3 at 14.08°, 28.42°, and 31.88°, corresponding to (110), (220) and (310) of a tetragonal MAPbI3 crystal structure (I4/mcm, no. 140). In these data, all XRD spectra are very similar, indicating similar perovskite crystallinity without impurity.

Fig. 4c shows the external quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra and integrated JSC data of the IP-PSCs with various PEDOT:PSS HTLs. As the PSS/PEDOT ratio increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS12, the EQE of the IP-PSCs increased over the entire wavelength range. From the normalized EQEs of the IP-PSCs, shown in Fig. S7,† the PSCs with higher PSS content showed noticeably increased EQEs in the wavelength ranges of 350 nm to 450 nm and 650 nm to 770 nm. This trend coincides with the UV/Vis transmittance spectra of the various PEDOT:PSS films (Fig. 3c). Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that the enhanced JSC of the IP-PSC device with high PSS content originated from the increased transparency in the visible region.
However, when the PSS content exceeded that of the optimum conditions, the JSC and EQE of the IP-PSC device decreased despite the higher transmittance. More seriously, the FF of the IP-PSC containing the PEDOT1:PSS12 HTL also decreased, similar to the trends of JSC and EQE, due to the thick insulating nature of the PSS top layer, which interferes with efficient hole extraction from the photoactive perovskite layer. To confirm this, we measured the EIS spectra (Fig. 4d). The Nyquist plots of all the IP-PSC devices show a semi-circle with a high frequency (>2.5 kHz). When the applied DC voltage is close to the VOC, the charge transport phenomenon is dominated by diffusion due to the decreased internal electric field in the device. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the high-frequency impedance is related to the total series resistance of the IP-PSC devices, which can be directly interpreted as the charge transport resistance (Rtr).58–61 As shown in Fig. 4d, as the PSS content increased from PEDOT1:PSS0.5 to PEDOT1:PSS5.0, the Rtr of the IP-PSC devices gradually decreased due to the improved hole extraction resulting from the WF manipulation at the PEDOT:PSS/MAPbI3 interface. Due to this, although there was a slight transmittance difference between PEDOT1:PSS5.0 films and PEDOT1:PSS2.5 films, the IP-PSC device with PEDOT1:PSS5.0 exhibited higher JSC than the one with PEDOT1:PSS2.5. However, the Rtr of the IP-PSC device containing the PEDOT1:PSS12.0 HTL increased dramatically. This indicates that a PEDOT:PSS HTL that exceeds the critical thickness of the insulating PSS top layer cannot function well in hole extraction, resulting in a decreased JSC and FF.
Conclusion
We demonstrated that efficient IP-PSCs can be obtained through WF manipulation by tuning the thickness of the PSS-rich top layer of the PEDOT:PSS HTL. The WF was systematically controlled by formulating PEDOT:PSS solutions with different amounts of PSS, which then underwent thermodynamic phase separation during spin-coating of the PEDOT:PSS films. In addition, the PSS content in the PEDOT:PSS solution also affected the optical and electronic properties of the resultant HTL. As a result, various photovoltaic parameters of the IP-PSCs, including JSC, VOC, and FF, were strongly influenced by the ratio of PEDOT to PSS. We can conclude that by increasing the PSS content in PEDOT:PSS to a ratio of 1:5.0, the WF of the PEDOT:PSS film increases and can be better matched with the VBM of perovskite materials, leading to an increase in the VOC. Furthermore, we observed an enhancement in the JSC of the devices due to optical gains from the decreased amount and grain size of the PEDOT polymer. However, excess PSS affects the hole extraction resistance and decreases the photovoltaic response of the IP-PSCs. Therefore, it is noted that counterbalancing between the positive and negative effects of the optical, electronic and electrical properties of the PEDOT:PSS films, depending on the PEDOT and PSS ratio, is critical to further optimize IP-PSC devices. We believe that this study will introduce new routes for high-efficiency IP-PSC devices based on PEDOT:PSS HTLs.
Experimental
Preparation of the PEDOT:PSS dispersion
The PEDOT:PSS dispersion was obtained following the Baytron P synthetic procedure.62 Poly(4-styrenesulfonate) (PSS, MW = 4.0 × 105 g mol−1) was used as both the dispersant and the dopant for PEDOT. A PSS and distilled water solution was bubbled using nitrogen gas (99.999%) for 60 min at a rate of 3 L min−1 to prevent oxidation from the dissolved oxygen in the water. To this solution, EDOT monomer was added, and the solution was stirred using a mechanical stirrer for 30 min. The direct synthesis of the PEDOT:PSS dispersion was carried out by a Fe3+-catalyzed oxidative polymerization process. The oxidizing reagents iron(III) sulfate and sodium persulfate were dissolved in distilled water by sonication and added to the reaction solution. The polymerization was performed for 24 h at 10 °C with bubbling nitrogen gas. After the polymerization of PEDOT:PSS, the product was mixed with a mixture of cation and anion ion exchange resins for 1 h and filtered with a 30 μm mesh filter. Then, the PEDOT:PSS dispersion was homogenized with a high-pressure homogenizer at 1000 bar to prepare a well-dispersed solution. Finally, the dispersion was passed through a 5 μm filter. To control the ratio of PEDOT to PSS, the PEDOT:PSS dispersion was synthesized by changing the mass ratio of the EDOT monomer to PSS. All the obtained PEDOT:PSS dispersions had a solid content of 1.1%.
Characterization of the PEDOT:PSS films
The thickness of the films was measured using a surface profiler (Alpha-Step 500, KLA-Tencor). The PSS/PEDOT ratio and chemical composition on the film surface were determined by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo K-Alpha XPS, Thermo Fisher Scientific, West Palm Beach, FL, USA). The surface images of the polymer films were obtained using atomic force microscopy (AFM, Park Systems XE-100) in non-contact mode. Using UV photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS), the WF was determined from the secondary cutoff region (Ecutoff) and the Fermi energy (EFermi), which were clearly defined and calculated by using φ = hv = 21.2 eV − Ecutoff + EFermi, where hv = 21.2 eV is the incoming photon energy from the He I source and a −5 V bias was applied to produce a clear boundary in the Ecutoff region. The transmittance spectra of the various PEDOT:PSS layers were obtained using a UV/Vis/NIR spectrophotometer (Varian Cary 5000 UV/Vis/NIR spectrophotometer).
Preparation of the perovskite solution
To prepare the perovskite solution, methylammonium iodide (CH3NH3I, MAI) was synthesized according to a reported method.5–7 First, methylamine (27.86 mL, 40% in methanol) was reacted with hydroiodic acid (30 mL, 57 wt% in water, Aldrich) in a 250 mL round-bottomed flask at 0 °C for 2 h with stirring. The precipitate was recovered via evaporation at 50 °C for 1 h. MAI was dissolved in ethanol, recrystallized from diethyl ether, and dried at 60 °C in a vacuum oven for 24 h. Lead iodide (PbI2, 99.999%), N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 99.8%) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, 99.5%) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.
Fabrication of the IP-PSC devices
Indium tin oxide (ITO) glass substrates were cleaned using acetone and 2-propanol in a bath sonicator, followed by UV/ozone (Altech LTS) for 30 min. After cleaning, various PEDOT:PSS solutions with controlled weight ratios (1:0.5, 1:2.5, 1:5.0, and 1:12) and a conventional PEDOT:PSS solution (Clevios P VP AI 4083) were spin-coated on the ITO glass substrates at 4000 rpm for 30 s and were then heated at 150 °C for 10 min on a hot plate. We used the solvent engineering method to form the perovskite layer.9,10 The prepared MAI powder and PbI2 in a 1 M MAPbI3 solution were stirred in a mixture of DMF and DMSO (7:3 v/v) at 70 °C for 1 h. The completely dissolved solution was spin-coated on various PEDOT:PSS layers at 5000 rpm for 40 s. During spin-coating, toluene (1 mL) was dropped on the rotating substrate. After spin-coating, the film was heated at 100 °C for 10 min. After forming the perovskite layer on the PEDOT:PSS layer, a 2 wt% PC60BM in chlorobenzene solution was then spin-coated on the perovskite layer at 1000 rpm for 60 s. Finally, an Ag electrode with a thickness of 150 nm was deposited on the PC60BM layer under 2 × 10−6 Torr vacuum in a thermal evaporator.
IP-PSC device measurements
To improve the accuracy of the device area, the active layer region of the PSCs was examined with a video microscope (Sometech, SV-35) using an aperture placed on the bottom of the cell; the aperture area was 11.430 mm2. The current–voltage (J–V) characteristics of the PSCs were measured with a Keithley Series 2400 Source Measure Unit under AM 1.5 white light (100 mW cm−2) illumination. A solar simulator (Oriel Sol 3A, class AAA) with a filtered 450 W xenon lamp was employed as the light source, and proper adjustments were made with a Si reference cell (VLSI standards, Oriel P/N 91150 V) in order to produce a 1 sunlight intensity of 100 mW cm−2. The external quantum efficiency (EQE) was measured using a Solar Cell QE/IPCE Measurement System (Zolix Solar Cell Scan 100). Impedance spectroscopy was conducted using an impedance analyzer (IVIUM Tech.). A small AC voltage of 20 mV was applied at 0 V and VOC under 1 sun illumination (AM 1.5G, 100 mW cm−2). The AC voltage was applied with a frequency ranging from 1 MHz to 10 Hz.
Characterization of the perovskite films
The cross-sectional and surface morphologies of the perovskite films were measured by field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM, JEOL, JSM-7001F). The crystal structural characteristics were obtained by XRD (SmartLab, Rigaku) with Cu Kα radiation (1.5418 Å).
