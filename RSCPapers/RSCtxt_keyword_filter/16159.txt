    , Accepted 17th March 2020First published on 20th March 2020AbstractAlthough the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells (PSCs) is comparable to those of commercial solar cells, a challenging problem of instability hampers their further commercialization. In recent years, in comparison with organic–inorganic hybrid PSCs, cesium-based all-inorganic perovskites show better light, moisture and especially thermal stability, and therefore they have exhibited great potential and received widespread attention. However, an unavoidable issue is that the PCE of all-inorganic PSCs still lags behind that of hybrid perovskite devices. To solve this problem, some organic or inorganic interlayer materials are introduced into all-inorganic PSCs as additive, passivation agent and charge transport materials to improve device performance. Compared to inorganic materials, organic materials present some advantages, such as energy level controllability, molecular structure diversity, and surface wettability modifications. Thus, the PCE of all-inorganic PSCs has been significantly improved through the use of organic materials. In this review, we summarized the recent strategies for improving the performance of all-inorganic PSCs through organic interlayer materials, including crystallization control, defect passivation, interface engineering, and expanding the light harvesting capability. Finally, a perspective on challenges and opportunities is proposed in the field.
Introduction
In recent years, lead halide perovskite materials have attracted great attention owing to their excellent optical and electrical properties, such as a high light absorption coefficient, low exciton binding energy, ultra-long charge carrier diffusion length, tunable optical band gap, and super high defect tolerance with low trap state density.1 Generally, perovskite materials have a formula defined as ABX3, and the most common cations at the A site are monovalent cations such as methylammonium (CH3NH3+, MA+), formamidinium (HC(CH2)2+, FA+), and Cs+. The B site is Pb or Sn, and the X sites are halide anions such as Cl−, Br−, and I−. Fig. 1a shows the crystal structure of ABX3. PSCs with organic cations at position A are known as organic–inorganic hybrid PSCs. The past decade has witnessed the unprecedented efficiency progress of lead halide hybrid PSCs soaring from unstable 3.8% to certified 25.2%.2 The PCEs of organic–inorganic hybrid PSCs are comparable to those of commercial solar cells. Although a great deal of effort has been invested on improving the stability of hybrid PSCs and some stable PSCs have exhibited a long-term stability for thousands of hours,3 long-term stability is still facing a big challenge of further developing hybrid PSCs because of their potential degradation under thermal pressure and chemicals.3 As an alternative, cesium-based all-inorganic perovskite (CsPb(Sn)I3−xBrx) materials not only show better light, moisture and especially thermal stability, providing a possible path to stable PSCs,4 but also show a tunable bandgap from 1.73 to 2.3 eV when x changes from 0 to 3. This makes it possible to fabricate tandem solar cells by combining with silicon solar cells or narrow bandgap organic solar cells.5 In recent years, all-inorganic PSCs have a faster increase in PCE and the best record of 19.03% is encouraging.6 However, compared with the PCE of organic–inorganic hybrid PSCs, that of all-inorganic PSCs still lags behind and therefore further improvement of their PCE is urgent and highly desired.

These phases are metastable and transform to the thermodynamic δ-CsPbI3 upon standing.8 
It is well known that perovskite crystal structures have a critical impact on device performance, so developing perovskite crystals with high quality is critical to achieve high PCE. CsPb(Sn)I3−xBrx has four types of crystal polymorphs: the cubic structure (α), the tetragonal structure (β), and two orthorhombic phases (a black γ-phase and a non-perovskite yellow δ-phase). The stability of each phase is temperature dependent, having symmetry structures stable at higher temperatures.7 For example, for CsPbI3, as the temperature decreases from that where the α-phase is stable, the crystal structure will transform from the α to the lower-symmetry β and then γ and δ-phase forms (shown in Fig. 1b and c).
The phase stability of ABX3 (the generic perovskite composition) is largely determined by the volumetric ratio between the BX6 octahedra and the A cation. The formation of the perovskite structure can be predicted from the Goldschmidt tolerance factor, t, where t is given by the equation:8 t = (rA + rX)/(21/2[rB + rX])(1)where rA, rX, and rB are ionic radii for A, X, and B sites, respectively. Therefore the parameter of t is a determinant of structural stability.9 A cubic perovskite structure can, as a general rule, be obtained if t is between 0.9 and 1. However, a perovskite can form when 0.8 ≤ t ≤ 1, although the perovskite structure will be distorted as the tolerance factor decreases below 0.9 because of the BX6 octahedra tilting. The A-site cation is too large to form a perovskite configuration when t > 1, whereas when t < 0.8, the A-site cation is too small to form the perovskite.9 For CsPb(Sn)I3−xBrx, the undesired phase change is easy to occur due to the smaller size of the Cs+ cation compared to MA+ and FA+ cations in the hybrid perovskite. In addition, because of the large bandgap of the all-inorganic perovskite and defects in the perovskite film, interfacial non-radiative recombination and Eloss will generally appear in these all-inorganic PSCs. Through massive scientific studies, it has been demonstrated that the interfacial non-radiative recombination of PSCs originates from the cathode and anode interfacial loss,10 and subsequently the large Eloss observed in all-inorganic PSCs has become one of the dominant hindrances to impair the ultimate efficiency.
In previous studies, a great deal of effective methods was developed for obtaining high performance all-inorganic PSCs. These methods toward improving the PCE and stability of all-inorganic PSCs mainly include:
(1) Controlling crystallization: adding an additive to the perovskite precursor solution to crosslink or permeate the perovskite crystal to increase the phase transition barrier of the perovskite crystal, thereby increasing the phase stability of the perovskite crystal.
(2) Defect passivation: defects in the perovskite film lead to non-radiative recombination of charges. By adopting reasonable organic/inorganic materials to passivate defects on the surface of the perovskite film and grain boundaries, non-radiative recombination could be reduced, and the PCE of the device is enhanced.
(3) Interfacial engineering: charge transport layers are adopted to improve charge extraction and transmission performance, thereby reducing the Eloss (Eloss = Eg − eVoc) and increasing the open-circuit voltage (Voc) of the device.
Most of these interlayer engineering approaches focus on organic or inorganic materials to improve the performance of PSCs. The most popular inorganic interlayer materials are copper based materials (CuSCN, CuI, Cu2O, CuO, CuS, CuGaO3, CuAlO2, etc.), carbon based materials (carbon black, activated carbon, graphite powder, graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), etc.), nickel oxide (NiOx), molybdenum trioxide (MoO3), etc. Inorganic interlayer materials possess small molecular weight, high solubility in the precursor solution, and show a positive regulation effect on the crystallization kinetics of the perovskite. However, there are few types of inorganic interlayer materials so that the application range of them is narrow and it is hard to alter the basic physical and chemical properties of inorganic interlayer materials. More importantly, these inorganic interlayer materials are not ideal for improving the performance of all-inorganic PSCs compared to organic interlayer materials (Fig. 2). Organic interlayer materials with facilely tailored chemical structure show distinguished advances in easy and precise modulations of the electronic, optical, and morphological characteristics of the interlayer materials, demonstrating great potential in enhancing photovoltaic performances. Firstly, the energy levels of organic interlayer materials can be finely tuned to match well with those of perovskite materials, facilitating efficient charge extraction and transport. Secondly, various functional groups in organic interlayer material molecules can supply lone-pair electrons for defect passivation. Thirdly, organic interlayer materials have excellent molecular structure controllability. Fourthly, the most frequently used all-inorganic perovskite materials exhibit a light absorption range below 750 nm. Some organic interlayer materials can be used as light-harvesting materials due to their strong absorption in the near-infrared region, which can be extended to the photo response of PSCs to the near-infrared region. Finally, the origins of organic interlayer materials show a broader range than their inorganic counterparts. Therefore, the performance of organic interlayer material based all-inorganic PSCs is generally more excellent than that of inorganic interlayer material-based PSCs. This paper mainly reviews the research progress of organic materials adopted in all-inorganic PSCs in terms of their different roles including additives, interface-modifying materials, charge-transport materials, and light-harvesting materials used in PSCs. The effects of organic materials on device efficiency and stability are discussed. Finally, the remaining key challenges and promising future research directions of organic material-based inorganic PSCs are also proposed.

Additives
All-inorganic PSCs based on CsPbI3 (or CsPbI2Br) are easily converted from their black phase to yellow phase due to the sensitivity to moisture, which is the main reason for their instability. The ionic covalent nature of the perovskite framework makes it easy to form chemical interactions with other functional groups. By adding functional organic additives to the perovskite precursor solution to cross-link or chelate the perovskite crystal framework, the simple steric effect, the lattice distortion could be effectively prevented and the crystallization process could be easily controlled to obtain a large grain size and improved stability (Fig. 3).
 Fig. 3  Chemical structures of organic interlayer materials applied as additives in PSCs. 
In 2017, Fu and others first used oleylammonium (OA) and phenylethylammonium (PEA) in the CsPbI3 precursor solution, showing that the incorporation of OA into the solution process stabilizes the CsPbI3 perovskite in the cubic phase (α-CsPbI3) whereas the use of PEA additive stabilizes the CsPbI3 perovskite in the much less common orthorhombic (β-CsPbI3) structure (as shown in Fig. 4). And both perovskite phases were stable over at least 4 months at room temperature (RT). More importantly, a planar heterojunction solar cell based on the stabilized CsPbI3 perovskite (β-CsPbI3) thin film was demonstrated to show a PCE of 6.5%.11
 Fig. 4  Chemical structures of (a) OA; (b) PEA. (c) Schematic illustration showing the formation of α- and β-CsPbI3 using OA and PEA ligand additives.11 
Using the same idea, Wang et al. prepared a deformed black CsPbI3 film by the synergistic effect of hydriodic acid (HI) and phenethylamine ammonium iodide (PEAI) additives (Fig. 5). It is important that the optical bandgap of the HI-induced distortion intermediate is 1.69 eV, and the distorted black phase hinders its phase change by the space steric effect. After optimization, the solar cell efficiency reached 15.07%, and the device maintained 92% of its initial cell efficiency after 2 months of storage under ambient conditions.12
 Fig. 5  (a) Schematic of common CsPbI3 phases; (b) HI-induced and PEAI-stabilized distorted black CsPbI3 orthorhombic phase.12 
Almost at the same time, Wang et al. used phenylethylammonium chlorine (PEACl) treatment to significantly improve the moisture-resistance of CsPbI2Br perovskite films.13 However, this ligand-induced phase stabilization usually causes a smaller grain size in the CsPbI3 (CsPbI2Br) perovskite films, which still limits the performance of all-inorganic PSCs.
To solve this problem, Han et al. rationally designed an organic ligand by studying the phase stability and photovoltaic performance of CsPbI3 perovskite in 2019.14 The problem of reducing the grain size caused by increasing the phase stability is solved. Fig. 6a illustrates the phase stabilizing mechanism of CsPbI3 perovskite with organic terminal group (OTGs) molecules as the surface capping layer. OTG molecules can systematically control the steric hindrance of surface organic terminal groups. This steric hindrance effectively prevents lattice distortion, thereby increasing the energy barrier of the phase transition. Moreover, the carboxylate group on OTG molecules passivated the surface defects and further reduced the non-radiative recombination of charge carriers. Therefore, stable PSCs with a surface organic capping layer reached 13.2% for the inverted planar structure. More importantly, optimized equipment retains 85% of its initial PCE after 30 days of aging under ambient conditions. At the same time, top-view scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of the corresponding perovskite films (Fig. 6b–e) revealed the evolution difference in grain size before and after treatment with OTG molecules.
 Fig. 6  (a) The phase stabilizing mechanism of CsPbI3 perovskite with OTG molecules as the surface capping layer. Top-view SEM images of (b) pristine CsPbI3, (c) CsPbI3–OTG1, (d) CsPbI3–OTG2 and (e) CsPbI3–OTG3 films.14 
Similarly, Zhang et al. demonstrated the successful growth of stable and highly crystalline α-phase CsPbI2Br via crystal cross-linking (Fig. 7).15 4-Aminobenzoic acid (ABA) was used as a nonvolatile additive to bridge the adjacent CsPbI2Br to form a grain-interconnected film. Apparently, the pristine film is composed of densely packed small grains due to the fast crystallization process, which is in good accordance with previous reports. However, adding ABA drastically changes the morphology of the perovskite, resulting in the appearance of compact large grains (Fig. 7b–f).
 Fig. 7  (a) Schematic illustration of two neighboring grain structures cross-linked by ABA.15 Top view SEM images of CsPbI2Br films (b) without and with (c) 1 mg mL−1, (d) 2.5 mg mL−1, (e) 5 mg mL−1 and (f) 10 mg mL−1 ABA additive. 
Optimized PSCs based on ABA-incorporated CsPbI2Br using a TiO2/Al2O3/perovskite/NiO/C architecture exhibited an efficiency of 8.44%. Moreover, the additive enables significant improvement of phase stability against moisture.15 In addition, divalent S2− exhibits highly electronegative nature compared with that of I− or Br−, which is expected to have strong chemical bonds with the perovskite structure and increases electrostatic interaction to stabilize the perovskite phase.16 However, their stability still fails to meet the requirements of commercial applications. Wang et al. applied xanthate in an all-inorganic perovskite to realize sulfur-doped CsPbIBr2 for superior phase stability. The additive cesium xanthate (CsXth) could be decomposed into cesium sulfides during the annealing process. The divalent S2− lies in the interstices of the perovskite lattice and strongly coordinates with CsPbIBr2 (Fig. 8) to stabilize the α-phase. Moreover, under humid air, they obtained a champion efficiency of 9.78% with a high Voc of 1.30 V based on 5% CsXth–CsPbIBr2, and the performance of the sulfur-doped perovskite device without encapsulation shows almost no decay after 10 h of storage under high humidity (65%).16 However, it can be seen from Fig. 8b–e that the crystal size after modification is also reduced compared to the pure CsPbIBr2 crystal size, and the perovskite solar cell based on pure CsPbIBr2 also has superior phase stability. The durability and efficiency of the above all-inorganic perovskite solar cells are summarized in Table 1. We can see that there is still no device with high stability and high efficiency. Therefore, there is still a need to explore an additive that can solve the phase stability problem of all-inorganic PSCs and can be applied on a large scale.

Defect passivating agents
The trap states of perovskite films can not only cause ion migration, current density–voltage (J–V) hysteresis and device degradation in the ambient environment, but also generate shunt-leakage paths due to direct contact between the electrode and the charge transport layers (CTLs), resulting in large charge recombination and reduced Voc and fill factor (FF) of PSCs. It is necessary to remove or passivate the trap states of perovskite films in order to obtain efficient and stable all-inorganic PSCs.17 Defect passivating agents have significant effects on the efficiency and stability of PSCs, since they can improve the quality of electron transport layers and perovskite films, passivating the defects caused by unsaturated Pb and I ions, which promotes perovskite crystallization as well as suppressing ion diffusion and resisting moisture erosion (Fig. 9).
 Fig. 9  Chemical structures of organic interlayer materials applied as defect passivating agents in PSCs. 
Passivated perovskite films
The performance of PSCs is directly affected by the electronic properties and surface of the corresponding perovskite films. On the one hand, non-radiative recombination of charge carriers occurred in defects at the surface of perovskite films. And defects in the grain boundary of perovskite films would lead to a shorter carrier lifetime and photoluminescence (PL) intensity thereby affecting the Voc and PCE of PSCs. On the other hand, defects may allow water and oxygen to infiltrate into the perovskite film and thus accelerate its decomposition, ultimately affecting the stability of PSCs.18 Passivation of electronic defects at the surface and grain boundaries of perovskite materials has become one of the most important strategies to suppress charge recombination in PSCs.
To reduce the defect states of organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite films, a variety of defect passivating agents have been developed so far, such as poly-[3-hexylthiophene-2,5-diyl] (P3HT), poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA), polyethylene glycol (PEG), poly-vinylpyrrolidone (PVP)-induced, glycol fullerene, quaternary ammonium halides, etc. These materials can also be applied in all-inorganic PSCs. Therefore, Zeng et al. proposed a facile strategy in which they showed that P3HT, deposited on the top of CsPbI2Br, could significantly reduce electron–hole recombination within the perovskite, which was attributed to the electronic passivation of surface defect states. Modifying perovskite films by annealing with polythiophene results in an increasing Voc of up to 1.32 V and a decreased Eloss of 0.5 eV.19 Liu and co-workers introduced PEG into the CsPbIBr2 precursor solution to enhance the PCE of CsPbIBr2 based PSCs. From the PL curve (Fig. 10), it can be observed that devices with PEG showed an increase in PL intensity, indicating the passivation of PEG onto the perovskite film. The best-performance PSC based on PEG-passivated CsPbIBr2 exhibited a Voc of 1.28 V, a Jsc of 8.80 mA cm−2, a FF of 0.649, and a PCE of 7.31%.
 Fig. 10  Normalized steady-state PL spectra of devices with PEG and without PEG.18 
Furthermore, the PEG-passivated PSC showed improved frame stability in comparison with the reference device without PEG-passivation.15 Zhang et al. reported an efficiency of 10.95% by modifying the CsPbI2Br/C interface using PMMA. Moreover, the unencapsulated CsPbI2Br-based device modified with PMMA demonstrates improved stability and retained about 95% of its initial efficiency after being exposed to air (relative humidity of 25–35%) for 20 days.21 And Zhang et al. reported that a polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)-induced surface passivation strategy stabilizes inorganic perovskite CsPbI3 with a cubic crystal structure via a repeatable solution-chemical reaction process (Fig. 11). It is revealed that the acylamino groups of PVP induce an electron cloud density on the surface of CsPbI3, thus lowering surface energy, which is conducive to the stabilization of cubic CsPbI3 even at the micrometer scale. The cubic-CsPbI3 based PSCs exhibited an extra-long carrier diffusion length (over 1.5 μm), highest PCE of 10.74% and excellent thermal/moisture stability.20 These studies provided a facile strategy to fabricate all-inorganic PSCs with enhanced Voc, enlarged PCE, and improved stability by defect passivation. However, the effect of these methods on the passivation of perovskite films did not achieve the desired result.
 Fig. 11  Mechanism of PVP-induced cubic phase stability.20 
To further improve the performance of all-inorganic PSCs, Zhao et al. achieved superior performance by using dual-functionalized molecules. Zhao et al. discovered that a simple phenyltrimethylammonium bromide (PTABr) post-treatment could achieve bifunctional stabilization including both gradient Br doping (or alloying) and surface passivation as schematically illustrated in Fig. 12.22 The highly stable PTABr treated CsPbI3 based PSCs exhibited a reproducible photovoltaic performance with a champion efficiency of up to 17.06% and stable output of 16.3%. The CsPbIBr2 has excellent stability but a large band gap of 2.05 eV, resulting in poor light absorption ability and low performance. Yang et al. reported a method using a bifunctional dye molecule [5,15-bis(2,6-dioctoxyphenyl)-10-(bis(4-hexylphenyl)-amino-20-4-carboxyphenylethynyl)porphyrinato]zinc(II) (YD2-o-C8) for the passivation of the CsPbIBr2 film. Results indicated that the Voc was increased from 1.18 V to record-high 1.37 V, achieving a high PCE of 10.13%. In addition, the device with the dye layer possessed better stability under ambient conditions without encapsulation, mainly because of the protective effect from the dye molecules.23 However, most of these passivation materials are insulators and limited by their thickness in the device to avoid high series resistance and the lack of hydrophobic groups to protect the device from moisture. A conjugated polymer, poly(N-alkyldiketopyrrolopyrrole dithienylthieno[3,2-b]thiophene) (DPP-DTT), with high mobility was introduced as a novel interface passivation material for CsPbI2Br based PSCs, which can significantly reduce non-radiative recombination in the perovskite, leading to significant enhancement in both efficiency and stability of CsPbI2Br-based PSCs. Through DPP-DTT passivation, a champion efficiency of 15.14% was obtained in CsPbI2Br-based PSCs. Moreover, the Lewis base DPP-DTT could serve as an ultrahydrophobic agent to hold the photovoltaic performance of CsPbI2Br-based PSCs under an ambient environment with humidity.24
 Fig. 12  Schematic illustration of gradient Br doping and PTA organic cation surface passivation on the CsPbI3 perovskite thin film.22 
Passivated electron transport layers
Passivation of electronic surface trap states has proved to be necessary to reduce the charge recombination rate and enhance the efficiency of PSCs. Among the ETLs, ZnO has excellent electron transport properties and a suitable conduction band energy level, being a promising ETL material for inorganic PSCs. However, there exist hydroxyl groups and oxygen deficiency on the ZnO surface, which could accelerate perovskite decomposition and limit the PCE. To solve this problem, Ding et al. developed an effective method to modify the ZnO ETL by using 3,3-diphenylpropylamine (DPPA) as a passivation material (Fig. 13). The defects on the ZnO surface were passivated due to Zn–N bond formation. The CsPbI2.25Br0.75 film on modified ZnO exhibited an enhancement of crystallinity, larger grain size and a decrease of trap density. The conduction band of modified ZnO matched that of the perovskite well, and CsPbI2.25Br0.75 solar cells with modified ZnO ETL gave enhanced Voc (1.27 V) and PCE (15.98%).25
 Fig. 13  The preparation process of ZnO/DPPA films.25 
Similarly, SnO2 is also an electron transport layer that is often used. Tian et al. applied an amino-functionalized polymer (PN4N) as the passivation material to passivate the SnO2 electron transport layer defects, and the interface dipole formed at the cathode interface reduced the work function of SnO2, which significantly increased the Voc of PSCs. The PN4N layer could also adjust the surface wetting properties to promote the growth of high-quality all-inorganic perovskite films with a larger grain size and higher crystallinity, and reduce the composite efficiency of the optimized CsPbI2Br-based PSC interface by 16%.26 The enhanced performance is mainly attributed to the reduced trap state density and suppressed charge recombination. These could be confirmed by normalizing the transient PL decay curve (Fig. 14).
 Fig. 14  Normalized transient PL decay profiles of CsPbI2Br, SnO2/CsPbI2Br, and SnO2/PN4N/CsPbI2Br.26 
Although many passivating molecules have been reported, the passivation mechanism for various functional groups is still unclear. The structure of passivated molecular functional groups is systematically designed to study their passivation ability for perovskite films or ETLs. It is important to provide guidance for the design of new passivating molecules in the future.
Charge transport layers (CTLs)
Functions of CTLs are to extract electrons (holes) from perovskite layers and transport charges to the cathode (anode) and block holes (electrons) to reduce carrier recombination. Controlling Eloss by adjusting the energy level between interfaces using CTLs (hole or electron transport layers) is an essential method to achieve high performance all-inorganic PSCs (Fig. 15).
 Fig. 15  Chemical structures of organic interlayer materials applied as charge-transport materials in PSCs. 
Hole transport layers (HTLs)
The most widely used inorganic perovskite light absorbers CsPbI2Br, CsPbI3 and CsPbIBr2 have relatively deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy levels compared to commonly used HTLs such as P3HT, 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis-(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenylamine)-9,90-spiro-bifluorene (spiro-OMeTAD), poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS), (PTAA) (3.0–5.2 eV), etc. The difference in energy levels between the perovskite layers and electrodes causes Eloss of the devices, which leads to a reduction in all photovoltaic parameters (such as Voc, Jsc and FF). To date, spiro-OMeTAD has been the preferred HTM for high-performance PSCs. In order to obtain high hole mobility and conductivity, pristine spiro-OMeTAD requires additional dopants, such as bis(triuoromethane) sulfonimide lithium (LiTFSI), which can adversely accelerate device degradation. When tBP is used as an additive, it has an adverse effect on the long-term preservation of the structure and electrical properties of spiro-OMeTAD.69 The hygroscopicity of Li-TFSI may cause moisture-sensitive phase degradation.74 Therefore, it is also required to develop a hole transporting material which is excellent in performance and has good stability. Furthermore, spiro-OMeTAD is very expensive, resulting in an economic burden for commercial PSCs.
Yang et al. reported an efficient and reproducible method for altering the interface between the CsPbI2Br absorber and the polythiophene hole acceptor to minimize Eloss.19 It is demonstrated that P3HT was deposited on the top of CsPbI2Br, which could significantly reduce electron–hole recombination within the perovskite, due to the electronic passivation of surface defect states.19 However, the lower hole mobility of P3HT compared with that of spiro-OMeTAD leads to a reduced photoelectric conversion performance of PSCs. Some high mobility organic materials have been introduced into P3HT to form composite HTMs with high conductivity. Liu et al. used P3HT/zinc phthalocyanine (ZnPc) composite materials as HTMs to reduce energy level differences between the perovskite layers and electrodes. Besides, the addition of ZnPc passivates perovskite film surface defects and improves the hole transporting ability of P3HT.6 Similarly, Wang et al. used multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) to increase the hole transporting ability of HTLs.117 Moreover, Im et al. fabricated n–i–p type hysteresis-less mesoscopic CsPbI2Br-based PSCs composed of FTO/c-TiO2/m-TiO2/CsPbI2Br/HTL/Au as basic model devices and enhanced the Voc of the mesoscopic CsPbI2Br-based PSCs by adapting HTMs with different HOMO values. The used model HTMs are P3HT, poly-triarylamine (PTAA), poly-fluoren-8-triarylamine (PF8-TAA), and poly-indenofluoren-8-triarylamine (PIF8-TAA), and their HOMO energy level positions were −4.98, −5.09, −5.45, and −5.52 eV, respectively. As a result, the PIF8-TAA HTM-based mesoscopic PSC exhibited a high Voc of 1.31 V and a PCE of 14.86% for the reverse scan condition under 1 sun illumination (100 mW cm−2 AM 1.5G). Its performance was superior to those of devices with P3HT and PTAA as HTLs. In addition, unencapsulated mesoscopic CsPbI2Br-based PSCs exhibited 10–14% of PCE degradation compared to their initial efficiency in maximum power point tracking under continuous 1 sun light soaking at 85 °C for 1000 h.107 However, these hole transport materials have poorer performance than spiro-OMeTAD.
Zhang et al. synthesized three novel polytriphenylamine-based polymer derivatives (H-Z1, H-Z2 and H-Z3), and the energy levels of these polymers could be tuned feasibly through introducing the electron-withdrawing group of trifluoromethyl into the triphenylamine (TPA) unit. Due to the gradually deepening HOMO energy levels from H-Z1, H-Z2 to H-Z3, the Voc values were elevated from 1.23, 1.28 to 1.30 V, respectively, where the Eloss is decreased from 0.69, 0.64, to 0.62 eV for H-Z1, H-Z2 and H-Z3, respectively. More importantly, both of the H-Z1 and H-Z2 based devices showed the highest PCE over 14% in all-inorganic PSCs, which were effectively comparable to the results of the reference device by using spiro-OMeTAD as the HTL.10 Yang et al. reported the design of fluorinated X59 and its application as a new sensitive-dopant-free HTL for CsPbI2Br PSCs. Introduction of electron withdrawing fluorine atoms into the X59 molecule is an effective way to downshift its HOMO level, which facilitates charge transfer by controlling the energetic offset. As a result, a PCE of 13.42% was obtained in CsPbI2Br PSCs with 2mF-X59 (doped with F4TCNQ) as the HTL, which was comparable to that of the device with the conventional spiro-OMeTAD (doped with Li-TFSI and tBP). The use of 2mF-X59 or 2mF-X59 + F4-TCNQ avoids the sensitive doping and oxidation process required by conventional spiro-OMeTAD. It was found that CsPbI2Br-based PSCs based on 2mF-X59 + F4-TCNQ achieved more efficient charge extraction at perovskite/HTL interfaces than the device based on conventional spiro-OMeTAD.118 Moreover, Hua et al. developed an efficient cyclopenta[hi]aceanthrylene-based dopant-free donor (D)–acceptor (A)–donor (D) type HTM YN3 for application in all-inorganic PSCs with high performance. CsPbI2Br-based PSCs devices based on YN3 as a dopant-free HTM achieved a very impressive PCE of 12.05%, which was much higher than that of the doped spiro-OMeTAD-based reference device (11.51%) under the same working conditions,119 meanwhile the YN3-based PSCs without encapsulation displayed good long-term stability. Yuan et al. avoided the need for complex doping and oxidation processes required for spiro-OMeTAD by using an undoped conjugated polymer PTB7 as the HTM.119 And the PCE was close to 13%, which was much higher than that of the doped spiro-OMeTAD-based reference device (8.20%).

Considering that common processing solvents are toxic for PCBM, such as chloroform and chlorobenzene, Miao and co-workers discovered that an alcohol soluble non-fullerene small molecule perylene diimide derivative (PDIN), which was a traditional cathode interface material, could be used as a promising electron transport material for efficient p–i–n perovskite solar cells. A PCE of 15.28% was achieved from the device with PDIN as the electron transporting layer, which was significantly higher than that of the PCBM only device.122

Light-harvesting materials
The most frequently used all-inorganic perovskite materials exhibit a light absorption range below 750 nm, but a portion of the visible light and all near-infrared light cannot be fully utilized, which hampers the further improvement of PCEs for all-inorganic PSCs. Tan and co-workers reported CsPbI2Br/PBDTTT-E-T:IEICO all-inorganic perovskite/organic integrated solar cells, in which the CsPbI2Br perovskite served as the visible light harvesting layer and the PBDTTT-E-T:IEICO organic blend as the NIR light harvesting layer. The absorption range of the ISCs extended to 900 nm, resulting in an enhanced Jsc of 15.98 mA cm−2 compared to the normal CsPbI2Br-based PSCs (14.03 mA cm−2).123
Besides, Yang et al. used a bifunctional dye molecule YD2-o-C8 as the light-harvesting material in PSCs for broadening the absorption spectra. Results indicated that the Jsc was remarkably improved from 9.37 to 12.05 mA cm−2 (Fig. 17).23

Summary and perspectives
In this review paper, we have explored the use of various organic materials in all-inorganic PSCs, including the addition of small organic molecules as additives to the perovskite precursor solution to improve the crystallization process of perovskites. On the other hand, organic polymers and organic small molecules (such as spiro-OMeTAD, PCBM, PDIN, etc.) can act as hole transporting materials to enhance the hole extraction ability and hole transporting ability, or as passivation materials to passivate defects of the perovskite film and the ETL, finally achieving improved PCEs of the device. In addition, organic light-harvesting materials exhibit very strong absorption in the NIR region, which can be used as light-harvesting materials to complement the absorption of the perovskite and extend the photoresponse of CsPbI3, CsPbI2Br, CsPbIBr2 and CsPbBr3 based PSCs to the NIR region. So, the development of organic materials with excellent performance is essential for the improvement of all-inorganic PSCs.
Meanwhile, it should be noted that, although organic layer materials outperformed their inorganic layer counterparts, there is still great potential for developing more efficient organic interlayer materials for all-inorganic PSCs. For organic additives and defect passivants, they have greatly improved device performance but are still not ideal. For the doped HTM, the PSC device stability is still facing a great challenge. Meanwhile, S-shaped J–V curves indicate severe charge recombination at the interface between the perovskite and organic layers, which is responsible for low Voc and FF.
Therefore, it is necessary to deeply understand the mechanism of passivation, and then to prepare organic passivation materials with excellent performance. Further, we can develop organic materials with multiple functions at the same time, which can not only passivate the defects of the perovskite, but also improve the stability of the devices and the hole extraction and transport performance of the interface. In addition, due to the limited light absorption range of perovskite materials, the use of narrow band-gap organic materials to prepare integrated devices can improve the device's Jsc, and the preparation process of integrated devices is relatively simple and it is very likely to achieve an avenue towards highly efficient, stable and low-cost all-inorganic perovskite solar cells in the future.
