    , Accepted 25th March 2020First published on 25th March 2020AbstractInorganic ferroelectric perovskite oxides are more stable than hybrid perovskites. However, their solar energy harvesting efficiency is not so good. Here, by constructing a series of BiFeO3-based devices (solar cells), we investigated three factors that influence the photovoltaic performance, namely, spontaneous polarization, terminated ion species in the interface between BiFeO3 and the electrode, and polarized light irradiation. This work was carried out under the framework of the density functional theory combined with the non-equilibrium Green's function theory under a built-in electric field or finite bias. The results showed that (1) the photocurrent is larger only under a suitable electronic band gap rather than larger spontaneous polarization; (2) the photocurrent reaches the largest value in the Bi3+ ion-terminated interface than in the case of Fe3+ or O2− with the SrTiO3 electrode; (3) the photocurrent can be largely enhanced if the polarized direction of the monochromatic light is perpendicular to the spontaneous polarization direction. These results would deepen the understanding of some experimental results of BiFeO3-based solar cells.
1. Introduction
Since energy shortage is now a worldwide problem,1–4 researchers have begun to investigate renewable energy materials, especially solar energy harvesting materials, such as solar cells. Researchers are gradually losing interest in improving the energy conversion efficiency of silicon p–n junction solar cells. Their cost is relatively high and their efficiency cannot exceed the Shockley–Queisser limit easily5,6 because the open circuit voltage cannot exceed the value of the band gap.
However, the recently widely studied perovskite materials do not encounter the open circuit voltage problem theoretically. The open circuit voltage can exceed the band gap of the material and sometimes reach several dozens of volts;7 thus, the energy conversion efficiency might exceed the Shockley–Queisser limit.8,9 Theoretically, the larger the spontaneous polarization (Ps), the larger the open circuit voltage in a ferroelectric-based solar cell. This is because the electric field (E) is proportional to the spontaneous polarization value Ps (Ps = χε0E), and the open voltage (Uo) is proportional to E (Uo = cE, where c is the thickness of the device). We can draw the relationship Uo ∝ E ∝ Ps, which has been verified in experiments. For example, Bhatnagar et al.10 found that the open circuit voltage of BiFeO3 (Ps = 80.2 μC cm−2) could reach as high as 15 V, while Spanier's work11 indicated that the open circuit voltage of BaTiO3 (Ps = 26 μC cm−2) was about 4.8 V. The ratio of the open circuit voltage is approximately equal to their ratio of spontaneous polarization, i.e., Uo ∝ Ps.
According to the depolarization theory,12–15 the above-mentioned electric field mainly originates from the extra positive and negative charges in the two interfaces with the electrodes of the cell. This built-in electric field is also called the depolarization field. The photovoltaic phenomenon in ferroelectric perovskites is usually called the bulk photovoltaic phenomenon (BPV).16 Excitons in ferroelectric perovskites are separated under this built-in electric field. In contrast, in p–n junction solar cells, the photoexcited excitons are separated by the built-in electric field in the depletion layer. The depolarization field is inversely correlated with the thickness of the cell in experiments. This is because the thicker the cell in the experiment, the more the domains and defects, and these defects and domains would cancel out some intrinsic electric fields in the perovskites.17,18
Some of these perovskites include the widely studied organic–inorganic hybrid halides perovskites. The energy conversion efficiency of these has reached 22%. However, they have encountered a stability problem and may pollute the environment.19–21 Inorganic ferroelectric perovskites rarely encounter stability problems. One of the candidate inorganic materials of BPV effect perovskites is bismuth ferrite1,7,10,22–33 (BiFeO3, or BFO). The extensively studied BFO is a multiferroic material that has multiple properties of ferroelectric, anti-ferromagnetic, and ferroelastic.34 The spontaneous polarization (Ps) in the hexagonal or rhombohedral BFO is about 80 μC cm−2, which is nearly the largest room temperature value among all the known perovskites.35
However, the actual energy conversion efficiency of BFO is still too low (<2%).3 Subsequently, some experimental works have reported attempts to enhance the photovoltaic performance of BFO-based solar cells.8,10,15,28,29 For example, Choi et al.24,30 studied the relationship between the electronic transport characteristics and the direction of spontaneous polarization by experimental methods. In addition, they also studied the relationship between the photocurrent densities and polarized light. The interface effect is also a key factor that influences the photovoltaic performance.22,36 However, systematic theoretical works to uncover these mechanisms of the photovoltaic properties of BFO, especially the electron-transport properties based on the above three factors under sun illumination, are still rare.
In this work, we carried out systematic first-principles studies of BFO-based solar cell device to investigate the effect of Ps, terminated ions with the electrode in the device, and the directions of polarized light on the photovoltaic performance. With the help of density functional theory (DFT) combined with non-equilibrium Green's function (NEGF), the electron density under bias or a built-in electric field could be obtained. Subsequently, we investigated how the photovoltaic performance changed by adjusting one main factor while keeping other factors fixed as much as possible. The results may be helpful for the experiments to enhance the power conversion efficiency.
2. Computational details
We first optimized the unit cell of BFO using the Vienna Ab-initio Simulation Package (VASP).37–40 The PAW method37 was adopted to generate wave functions, and the interaction between ion cores and valence electrons was treated with the ultrasoft pseudo potential.38 The exchange and correlation effects of valence electrons were treated by the generalized gradient approximation (GGA/PBE),40 which is on the second rung of Jacob's ladder.41 The room temperature rhombohedral BFO is a G-type antiferromagnetic spin structure with a Néel temperature of 643 K.42 Although the spin direction is a little tilted in the adjacent direction, we only considered a collinear G-type antiferromagnetic model as in other first-principles works to reduce the computational cost.42,43 So, we considered spin polarized calculation by SGGA. The strong correlation effect of Fe 3d electrons was considered by the Coulomb repulsive energy Ueff = 5 eV. Thus, we adopted the SGGA + U44,45 method. This is a relatively computing resources inexpensive and suitable method, and it has been widely adopted to deal with BFO rather than hybrid functional or GW cases.
Second, we constructed the devices based on the optimized structures by VASP. As shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†), the device was composed of a left electrode, central region (including left electrode extension region, central scattering region, and right electrode extension region), and right electrode. Here, we assumed that the electronic transport direction was along the z axis. The device was two-dimensional periodic on the xy plane and non-periodic in the z-axis direction. The device calculations were performed with the Atomistix ToolKit (ATK) package.46 We adopted the linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO)47 method to generate wave functions, and the interactions between ion cores and valence electrons were treated with the OMX pseudo potential,48,49 which is suitable to treat large systems with high accuracy. Here, we also adopted the SGGA + U method to treat the exchange and correlation effect, spin polarization and strong correlation effect.
The easy polarization direction was along [1 1 1] for the room temperature BFO.35 The value of Ps was obtained with the theoretical framework for the Berry phase,50 which is the modern theory of polarization. In this method, the charge centre of dipoles in ferroelectrics are obtained by calculating the real space localized Wannier functions.
Under the built-in or applied electric field, the electron density in the device could not be obtained merely by DFT. However, the electron density could be obtained by DFT combined with the NEGF method (DFT + NEGF).46 In this method, the non-equilibrium electron density is obtained by the NEGF method, and then the Hamiltonian is obtained by the DFT method. The newly constructed Hamiltonian will then be put into the next NEGF calculations until the input and output charge densities are self-consistent.
The Hamiltonian of the device was divided into the left part HL, central part HCR, and right part HR,46 which respectively correspond to the left electrode, central region, and right electrode in Fig. S1 (ESI†). Here, HL and HR were calculated under periodic boundary conditions. The electron density of these two electrodes could be obtained by DFT. As for the central region, HCR was evaluated with open boundary conditions in the z-axis direction and periodic boundary conditions in the x-axis and y-axis directions. 3. Results and discussions
The room temperature BFO is not a standard cubic structure. It tilts from a structure with the Fmm cubic space group, and leads to a R3c hexagonal (R3cH) or rhombohedral (R3cR) crystal structure with the Glazer notation62a−a−a− as shown in Fig. 1a. The driving force for the structure distortion originates from the hybridization of the O 2p orbitals and Fe 3d orbitals under the repulsive force of the stereochemically active Bi3+ 6s lone pairs.63 We adopted the room temperature BFO as a prototype to construct pure BFO solar cell devices, with the Mn and Cr elements modulating the solar cell devices (see Fig. 2a and b) and the SrTiO3 (or STO) lattice matched solar cell devices (see Fig. 3a–c).

Due to the large computing resource consumption, the central scattering region of the device here was relatively smaller than the one that would be in a real device. We performed a convergence test for the effective potential of the BF(Cr,Mn)O-based and BF(Mn,Cr)O-based devices (see Fig. S2, ESI†). Here, we found that even if the central scattering region was smaller than the one in Fig. 2a and b, it would not change the relative potential barrier height.
To check the rationality of our theoretical methods adopted in this work, we computed the band gap and Ps of bulk BFO. The gap of BFO was 2.41 eV, while the Ps along the [1 1 1] direction was 80.19 μC cm−2. The results are consistent with previous theoretical work.43 The calculated electrostatic potential difference in the z direction of the perfect R3cH BFO cell was 0.976 V (This could be the open circuit voltage of the device, and was consistent with the experimental results64 at 0 K.) Here, the lattice length in the z direction was 1.37 × 10−9 m. According to eqn (8), the calculated depolarization field was about 7.06 × 108 V m−1, i.e., larger than the experimental value of 5 × 107 V m−1,65 as shown in Fig. 1d and in the lower panel of Fig. 1e. This was possibly because the device model that we adopted was perfect without considering the complex domain structures in the experiments.66–71 The different directions of the depolarization field in these domains could cancel out some of the components in the z direction overall. The depolarization field was the main origin of the internal built-in electric field in this nano device.14
3.1 Polarization effects on the photocurrent
To investigate the effects of Ps on the photovoltaic performance, let us consider such cells that keep the structures nearly unchanged, and have a different Ps in comparison with pure BFO. Fig. 1f and g present two such cells: Cr- and Mn-doped BFO cells (BF(Cr,Mn)O and BF(Mn,Cr)O). As shown in the upper panel of Fig. 1f, BF(Cr,Mn)O was constructed by using Cr and Mn to replace two adjacent Fe atoms in one BFO cell (R3cH) in the z-axis direction, while BF(Mn,Cr)O in the upper panel of Fig. 1g was obtained by exchanging the Mn and Cr positions of BF(Cr,Mn)O. The concentrations of Cr or Mn in the above two structures were all 3.3%. After optimization, the two cell structures were nearly unchanged. Fig. S3 and Table S1 (ESI†) show the bond length and bond angle of Cr(Mn) with the nearest O in the two cells. The valence states of Cr and Mn were all +3.
Here, Cr and Mn were chosen based on three factors as follows. First, Cr and Mn have previously formed perovskite compounds with Bi, Fe and O in experiments, such as Bi2FeCrO672 and BiMnO3.73 Second, Cr and Mn have similar ion radii with Fe. The cell structures were thus nearly unchanged after substituting Fe with Cr and Mn in BFO. Third, Cr and Mn have different electro-negativities compared to Fe. In the substituted BFO by Cr and Mn, there existed an additional electric field induced by Cr and Mn ion pairs, leading to an increase or decrease in the intrinsic electric field in pure BFO. As seen from the lower panel of Fig. 1f, the electric field of (Cr,Mn) pairs in BF(Cr,Mn)O is opposite to the intrinsic electric field in BFO, leading to a decrease in the intrinsic electric field in BFO; while the electric field of the (Mn,Cr) pairs in BF(Mn,Cr)O was in the same direction with that of BFO, which enhanced the intrinsic electric field in BFO (see the lower panel of Fig. 1g). The electric field in BF(Cr,Mn)O should thus be smaller than the one in BF(Mn,Cr)O. The different electric field corresponds to a different Ps. The Ps in BF(Cr,Mn)O is smaller than the one in BF(Mn,Cr)O.
Subsequently, two devices were constructed based on the above substituted hexagonal BFO structures. The corresponding devices of the substituted cells of BFO are shown in the upper panels of Fig. 2a and b separately. The calculated Ps was 78.47 μC cm−2 for BF(Cr,Mn)O, and 87.12 μC cm−2 for BF(Mn,Cr)O, as shown in Table 1, verifying the above analysis for Ps or the electric field in the BF(Cr,Mn)O and BF(Mn,Cr)O cells.

A larger depolarization electric field usually leads to a larger electric current. Except for the depolarization electric field, the current is also related to other factors, such as the electrostatic potential barrier and carrier mobilities. As shown in Fig. 1d, the difference in electrostatic potential in BF(Cr,Mn)O is smaller than the one in BF(Mn,Cr)O. This means that the electrons in BF(Mn,Cr)O would be harder to move than in BF(Cr,Mn)O. In addition, the calculated band gap (1.37 eV) for BF(Cr,Mn)O was smaller than the one (1.41 eV) for BF(Mn,Cr)O, as shown in Table 1. A smaller band gap generally gives a larger electronic dielectric constant, leading to a smaller exciton bonding energy because, as we can see in the Mott–Wannier model,74,75 the exciton bonding energy is directly related to the dielectric constant: Eb = 13.6 μ/ε2. This indicated that the excitons in BF(Cr,Mn)O were easier to be separated by the depolarization electric field than in BF(Mn,Cr)O. That is, that the carrier density in BF(Cr,Mn)O would be larger than the one in BF(Mn,Cr)O. So, the photovoltaic performance of BF(Cr,Mn)O-based solar cells should be better than that of BF(Mn,Cr)O-based solar cells.
In order to illustrate this opinion, we calculated the photocurrent under AM1.5 (defined as the ratio between the length of sunlight propagation in the atmosphere d and the atmosphere thickness D; here the ratio was 1.5) solar spectrum as shown in Fig. 2d. The photocurrent for the BF(Cr,Mn)O-based device presented by the solid black line is larger than that for the BF(Mn,Cr)O-based one. The photocurrent density in the BF(Cr,Mn)O-based device under no bias voltage was 1.06 × 102 μA cm−2, larger than the 6.73 × 101 μA cm−2 in the BF(Mn,Cr)O-based device, as shown in Table 1. Although the device was under AM1.5 solar spectrum, only photons with energy larger than the band gap could be absorbed. This is because the electron hopping from the valence band maximum (VBM) to the conduction band minimum (CBM) needs to absorb photon energy larger than the band gap. The onset of the photocurrent was about 2.5 eV, larger than the corresponding band gap. Here, the band gap was calculated by the DFT method, while the photocurrent was calculated mainly by NEGF. Both DFT and NEGF are useful methods for solving the many-body Schrödinger equation. NEGF could give more precise results than DFT.76 The conventional exchange and correlation functionals, such as GGA/PBE and LDA, usually underestimate the band gap of some semiconductors due to the self-interaction error in the approximate exchange functional. So, the starting value of the photocurrent is larger than the band gap, as shown in Fig. 2d. Here, we only care for the relative band gap, rather than the absolute one.
To uncover the reason why the Ps value is not a key factor for the solar cell performance, we could also explain it in terms of the transmission eigenstates under finite bias (−1 to 1 volt), as shown in Fig. 2a and b in the lower panels. Obviously, the transmission eigenstates in the BF(Cr,Mn)O-based device spread nearly over the whole scattering region, see the lower panel in Fig. 2b, while they only gather in the extension region of the left electrode in the BF(Mn,Cr)O-based device, see the lower panel in Fig. 2a. From the perspective of the effective potential, there exists a valley in the potential curve for the BF(Cr,Mn)O-based device, whereas there exists a peak for the one in the BF(Mn,Cr)O-based device, as shown in Fig. 2c. The electrons could more easily be transported in the former one than the latter. So, the conductance in the former device is better than that of the last one, because the excitons are easier to be formed and separated in the lower band gap materials. So, our result is similar to the case of organic–inorganic hybrid perovskites.77 Our calculated photocurrent density is larger than that in the experiments by Choi et al.30 This is because their experiments were adopted with only green light irradiation, while the photon energy ranged from 0 to 3.6 eV in our whole theoretical work.
3.2 Interface atomic layer effect on the photocurrent density
This part mainly focuses on the impact of special terminated ions (Bi, Fe and O) on the transport or photovoltaic performance of the device. So, we need to ignore some factors that do not influence the main conclusions, such as the large lattice mismatch with the metal, and Coulomb screening at the interface of BFO and the metal. We constructed a series of devices with different terminated ions for the right part of the device, where the interfaces were formed by the (1 1 1) surface of BFO and the (1 1 1) surface of STO, as shown in Fig. 3a–c. At the same time, we kept the left part of the device unchanged. Here, we chose STO instead of a metal as the electrode after considering the following factors. First, STO is a perovskite oxide. The lattice mismatch between STO and BFO is smaller than for other materials,78,79 such as metals. If we use a metal as the electrode, many other factors that influence the photovoltaic performance would occur. Second, the band gap of STO is 3.25 eV,80 smaller than the widely known wide band gap semiconductor GaN (3.4 eV).81 The conductivity of STO should be higher than GaN. Third, we chose STO as an electrode material based on some experimental works.82,83
We built the BFO/STO interface of the heterogeneous structure using the Virtual NanoLab (VNL) tool as used by Stokbro et al.84 The interface was constructed with an average stress εav less than 0.65%. Here εav is defined as the average stress of the three directions of the heterogeneous structure.85 The stress in each direction is related to the rotation angle of the BFO and STO interface. Finally, we chose the structure with a relatively lower average stress and strain energy. Thus, three devices with Bi-, Fe- and O-terminated ions were obtained. In addition, we kept the terminated ions of STO on the right part of the electrode unchanged in the above three cases.
In the Bi-terminated device (Device 1), the photocurrent density under no bias is larger than the one in the O-terminated device (Device 3), which was also larger than the Fe-terminated one (Device 2), as shown in Fig. 3e and Table 2. In order to find the reason for this, we first calculated the transmission eigenstates of the three devices, as shown in Fig. 3a–c. The transmission eigenstates in Device 1 spread almost over the whole device (see the lower panel in Fig. 3a), while the states in Devices 2 and 3 mainly focused on the edge of the BFO scattering region (see the lower panels in Fig. 3b and c). The results suggested that the conductance in the latter two devices was not so good compared to the first device. To uncover the origin of this phenomenon, we calculated the effective potential of electrons at finite bias, ranging from −1 to 1 V, as shown in Fig. 3d. Obviously, the potential barrier in Device 1 (blue dotted line) was lower than the one in Device 2, so the electrons could easily pass through it. For Device 2 (red dashed line) and Device 3 (green solid line), the potential barriers were all higher than the one for Device 1. Obviously, the electrons could easier pass through Device 1 than Device 2 and Device 3. Due to the dramatic potential energy change near the right electrode in Device 2 (about 38 Å in the z direction), the resistant force on the electrons (F = −∂Veff/∂z) was larger than the one in Device 3. So, the conductance was not so good compared with Device 3, because the electrons were blocked on the left interface (see the lower panel in Fig. 3b). The large potential energy difference near the right electrode in Device 2 mainly originated from the big electronegativity difference between Fe3+ in BFO and O2− in STO. At the same time, the photocurrent showed a similar tendency to the conductance of the devices. The different feature of these three devices was the different terminated ion species with the same STO electrode on the right part of these devices. Thus, the terminated ions of BFO have a great impact on the photovoltaic performance. We also deduced that the possible reason for the photovoltaic difference was the local dipole difference in the interfaces of the three devices. With the current level of experimental control, detailed studies into the effects of the ions at the interfaces are not achievable.

3.3 Polarized light effect on the solar cell performance
For the anisotropic BFO ferroelectric materials, the polarized light should have an important impact on the excitons formation.29,30 In order to explain this phenomenon, two kinds of polarized light, namely x direction polarized and z direction polarized, were imposed on Device 2, and then the corresponding transmission eigenstates and photocurrent were calculated, as shown in Fig. 3b and e separately. The absolute value of the photocurrent density was calculated and the values are shown in Table 2. Obviously, the photocurrent density under no bias in the x-direction polarized light device was almost 10 times larger than the one in the z-direction polarized light device.
In order to explain this phenomenon, we calculated the projected band structure of bulk BFO along the high symmetry points in the Brillouin zone, as shown in Fig. 1b and c separately. We found that the O 2p orbitals dominate the VBM, while the Fe 3d orbitals dominate the CBM, and the effective mass of electrons (related to the curvature radius of the E–k curve) in the valence band along the z direction (A → Γ, M → L) is larger than the one in the x direction (Γ → K, H → A). The electrons could easily hop from O 2p orbitals to Fe 3d orbitals under the illumination of x-direction polarized light, because the electronic component in the electromagnetic wave is the main driving force to excite the electrons hopping from the valence band to the conduction band. Consequently, the photocurrent was larger in the device under x-direction polarized light. Our theoretical work deepens the understanding of the experimental results reported by Choi et al.30
4. Conclusions
In this article, we mainly investigated three key factors that influence the photovoltaic performance of solar cells by constructing a series of devices based on BFO. First, although the photocurrent originates from the built-in electric field formed by electric dipoles, the larger Ps is not the main factor that induces larger photocurrent, because the band gap was another key factor for the carriers’ formation. A smaller band gap gives generally a larger electronic dielectric constant, leading to a smaller exciton bonding energy, and thus to a larger photocurrent under the same solar illumination. Second, the interface ions of BFO have an important influence on the photocurrent when BFO is made into a heterogeneous solar cell, because the local electric field formed by the interface ions could impact the electronic transport properties. This discovery inspired us to believe that the photovoltaic performance could be enhanced not only by controlling the terminated ion species but also by contacting with an electrode with a different chemical potential. Third, the energy conversion efficiency could be enhanced if the incident monochromatic light was polarized along a special direction. This phenomenon may originate from the anisotropy of the carrier effective mass in the lattice of BFO. These results will deepen the understanding of some experimental results of BFO-based solar cells.
