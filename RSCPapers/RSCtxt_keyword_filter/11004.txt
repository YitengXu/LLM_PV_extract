    , Accepted 17th September 2013First published on 17th September 2013AbstractNovel n-type star-shaped molecule TriPBI containing a HAT core and three fused PBI “arms” was synthesized and characterized. The n-type charge carrier mobility as high as 3.34 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1 of TriPBI is evaluated by SCLC measurements, which is considerably higher than 8.71 × 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 of the PBI monomer.
Star-shaped molecules of π-conjugated chromophores containing a polycyclic aromatic core, because of their properties of isotropic absorption,1 strong propensity for stacking into 1D columns as the preferred charge transporting pathway,2 have generated significant interest for use in optoelectronic devices.3 While a number of p-type star-shaped molecules with mobilities comparable to amorphous silicon have been developed over the past decades, including triphenylenes,4 phthalocyanines,5 porphyrins,6 hexabenzocoronene,3,7 and truxenes.8 In contrast, n-type disk-shaped molecular systems are still rather underdeveloped.9 However, both p-type and n-type semiconductors are necessary in OPV, p–n junction diodes, bipolar transistors, and complementary integrated circuits. Therefore, studies on n-type electronic properties in the context of thin film devices have recently attracted increasing interest.
Herein, as shown in Fig. 1, we design a novel n-type star-shaped molecule TriPBI that contain a hexaazatriphenylene (HAT) core fused with three perylene-3,4,9,10-tetracarboxylic acid bisimides (PBI) “arms” at the bay sits, which is composed of 25 fused six-membered aromatic rings. As a result of extended conjugation, the fusion supposed to show more extended π-conjugation and a higher tendency to aggregate, which are essential for material applications in electronic devices. As a core of star-shaped ladder conjugated oligomer, HAT and its derivatives have been investigated as a new discogen with the potential to act as electron carriers. They have three-deficient pyrazine nuclei and tend to self-organize into columnar structures in solution and bulk state in the solid state, when suitably substituted, to self-assemble into columnar structures with large bandwidths.10–12 In a recent report by Marder et al., tris(ester) derivatives of HAT, have proved to be excellent electron transport materials.13PBI as a well-known n-type semiconductor, also have been widely used in solar cells,14 field-effect transistors,15 and fluorescent dyes.16 Fusing this electron deficient unit into star-shaped core not only increased the electron affinity but also poses as an appealing approach toward achieving good electronic enhancing intermolecular interactions, and improving their kinetic stability towards H2O and O2.17
 Fig. 1  Structures of HAT, PBI and TriPBI. 

Scheme 1 shows the synthesis of TriPBI. PBI fused o-phenylenediamine building block 1 as a regioselectivity photocyclization intermediate, which synthesized by Suzuki coupling, photocyclization and catalytic hydrogenations, has been reported by our group.18 This multifunctional block can be used to construct ladder-type conjugated star-shaped molecule. Swallow-tail chains have to be attached onto the peripheries, because of the extremely poor solubility of common PBI in organic solvents which often hampers further structural modifications and characterizations. TriPBI gives very broad NMR spectra across a wide range of temperatures in CDCl3 and 1,2-dichlorobenzene-d4. Nevertheless, their identities are fully supported by high-resolution mass spectrometry. The decomposition temperature (Td, corresponding to 5% weight loss) of under nitrogen is 266.5 °C (Fig. S2, shown in ESI†). No thermal transitions could be observed in differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) in a wide temperature range from 20 to 270 °C at various scanning rates (10 and 2 °C min−1).

The absorption and photoluminescence (PL) spectra of TriPBI and its PBI monomer in chloroform (1 × 10−5 mol L−1) and in a thin film are shown in Fig. 2. TriPBI possessing three PBI segments shows a strong absorption bands across a broad range of 300–550 nm. There are five absorption peaks at 341 nm, 445 nm, 467 nm, 489 nm and 516 nm (=80800, 127400, 117900, 91800, 46700 M−1 cm−1 respectively). In compared with its PBI monomer, TriPBI's absorption magnitudes at different wavelengths in the visible range are far from the three folds of PBI's, which indicates that three PBI branches in TriPBI are no longer the single and independent PBI chromophores, but have been integrated into a larger system. In addition, TriPBI displays considerably broadened absorption and emission bands compared with those of PBI. However the fine absorption bands in the TriPBI spectrum show a hypsochromic shift when compared to the PBI monomer, together with an additional broad shoulder at longer wavelength. Several groups have observed similar phenomena based on the enlarged perylene bisimides both at the bay-sits and peri-sits.19 The UV-vis spectra of spuncast thin film of TriPBI shifts less than that of PBI monomer, which might be ascribed to the small torsion angle between the HAT core and PBI “arms” to weaken the π–π intermolecular interactions (Fig. S3, shown in ESI†). The emission spectrum of TriPBI is similar with PBI monomer's emission, but shows a hypsochromic shift, which is also reflected by the widened optical energy bandgap. The fluorescence quantum yield of TriPBI in chloroform is 0.40. Although this value is remarkably lower than that (0.98) of PBI,20 it is sufficiently high for practical applications as fluorescent material. Broad absorption and high extinction coefficients make TriPBI potential material in solar cell and photodetector.

The electrochemical property of TriPBI was reflected by cyclovoltammetry and differential pulse voltammetry (DPV) (Fig. S1, ESI†) in DCM solution. As shown in Fig. 2d, TriPBI shows three reduction peaks at 1.27 V, 1.46 V and 1.94 V respectively, implying their ability to accept at least three electrons. Both the first and the second reduction waves of TriPBI are at 0.13 and 0.11 V lower potentials than those of PBI, indicating a bit higher LUMO energy of TriPBI relative to PBI. No obvious redox waves are observed upon oxidation up to 1.5 V. The LUMO energy of TriPBI is −3.81 eV, which indicates strong electron-accepting ability of TriPBI. The optical energy bandgap (Eoptg) is 2.19 eV derived from the low-energy absorption edge in thin films, and then the HOMO energy level can be estimated by HOMO = LUMO − Eoptg, which is −6.00 eV. These data indicate that TriPBI is a potential n-type material.

TriPBI and PBI monomer's SCLC device structure and J–V characteristics are shown in Fig. 3. By using this method, the value of μ0 = 4.48 × 10−6 cm2 V−1 s−1 and γ = 0.012 cm0.5 V−0.5, which lead the electron mobility μ = 3.34 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1 for TriPBI in the case of the 171 nm thickness. We also measured SCLC data of the PBI monomer: μ0 = 2.25 × 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 and γ = 2.46 × 10−3 cm0.5 V−0.5, so the value of μ = 8.71 × 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 was calculated by eqn (2) in the thickness of 193 nm. The value of electron mobility for TriPBI is more than one order of magnitude higher than that of the PBI monomer. It is evident that γ is the major cause for the increase in mobility. As γ is related to the degree of disorder in the film25 and we applied the same condition in device fabrication, we can concluded that, due to its large conjugated planar and large molecule weight, the film of TriPBI is more orderly than its PBI monomer. This preliminary result of TriPBI is comparable to the electron mobility data of some well-known electron acceptors. For example, SCLC electron mobilities as good as 2 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1 and 1.2 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1 have been reported for PCBM.24,26 In a recent report, organic solar cells with an electron mobility of 1.2 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 contributed by a PBI derivative showed good power conversion efficiency of up to 3.17%.27 We therefore demonstrate here that TriPBI is suitable for applications in organic semiconductor devices.

In summary, novel n-type star-shaped molecule TriPBI has been constructed a HAT core and three fused PBI arms. As a result of the fusion, TriPBI has broad and intensive absorption from 300–550 nm (mole extinction coefficients up to 127400 M−1 cm−1). SCLC measurements indicate the remarkable charge transport in TriPBI, which is up to 3.34 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1, considerably higher than 8.71 × 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1 of the corresponding PBI monomer. Further work on the devices is ongoing.
