Abstract

Lithium metal has attracted considerable attention due to its ultrahigh theoretical capacity. Nevertheless, issues such as dendritic Li formation and instability of the Li metal/electrolyte interface still restrain its practical applications. In this work, we design a Li composite anode with fluorinated carbon nanotubes (FCNT) fabricated by a simple meltingâ€“soaking method. It was found that trace amounts of added FCNT (only 1.6 wt%) lead to a significant chemical/electrochemical stability of metallic Li. The obtained Li/FCNT composite electrode (LFCNT) exhibits much better stability in open air and electrolyte than bare Li. The LFCNT enables uniform plating/stripping of metallic Li, preventing the dendrite formation during repeated cycling. In situ optical microscopy observations confirm dendrite-free Li deposition with the mechanism clarified by density functional theory calculations. Compared with bare Li, the LFCNT shows a considerable improvement in rate capability, voltage hysteresis and cycle performance, sustaining stable cycling at a high current density of 3 mA cmâˆ’2 or a capacity up to 5 mA h cmâˆ’2. Liâ€“O2 cells with a LFCNT anode exhibit a long life of 135 cycles at a capacity of 1000 mA h gâˆ’1, which is six-fold than that with the bare Li anode.

Introduction

It is generally recognized that the massive use of fossil fuels not only leads to serious pollution, but also causes global warming with alarming consequences. Though solar, wind and wave energies are promising clean energies, they are variable with time and require energy storage devices.1 Over the past few decades, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have achieved great success and enabled the popularity of portable electronics such as mobile phones, laptops and drones. Nevertheless, issues such as energy density, cost, safety and service life still restrain the employment of LIBs for the huge market of electric vehicles, which could effectively reduce the consumption of fossil fuels.2,3 The energy density of traditional LIBs using graphite anodes has almost reached their theoretical value which cannot meet the demand of long-range electric vehicles. Therefore, advanced electrode materials are urgently needed for next-generation high-energy-density batteries.4,5

Among various anode materials, Li metal with an ultrahigh theoretical capacity (3860 mA h gâˆ’1) and the lowest electrochemical potential (âˆ’3.040 V versus standard hydrogen electrode) has attracted much attention.6 However, severe challenges have hindered its practical applications. The instability of the Li metal/electrolyte interface can cause irreversible reactions and continuous consumption of Li and the electrolyte. The generated thick solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer increases the interface resistance and electrochemical polarization, thus jeopardizing the battery performance.7 During the Li plating/stripping process, the uncontrolled formation and growth of Li dendrites lead to short circuit and safety issues. Besides, large volume changes of Li metal during cycling can easily break the SEI layer, and the fresh Li will be exposed to the electrolyte that generates a large-surface-area SEI layer, resulting in a rapid capacity decay and low coulombic efficiency. Li dendrites are also prone to detach from Li metal to form dead Li which is electrochemically inert due to the easy electron reception and early dissolution of dendrite roots.8,9 In recent years, Liâ€“air (O2) and Liâ€“S batteries have triggered a wave of research due to the much higher energy densities compared to conventional LIBs.10â€“12 Though the fabrication of cathode materials consisting of catalysts for O2 reduction/evolution or sulfur has made great progress, the utilization of stable Li metal is critical for the practical applications of both batteries.

To inhibit dendrite growth and obtain a stable Li anode, numerous strategies have been proposed during the past decades. One option is the use of solid-state electrolytes with a higher modulus than that of Li, which could block the penetration of Li dendrites and improve the safety performance. However, the ionic conductivity of the present solid electrolytes at room temperature is still far below that of the nonaqueous liquid electrolyte (10âˆ’3 S cmâˆ’1).13,14 Another method to obtain a dendrite-free Li anode is the rational structure design of the anode. Considering the fact that large thickness change and uneven plating/stripping of Li during cycling would induce Li dendrite formation, the design of novel current collectors based on nanomaterials and nanotechnology has been proposed. Recent reports on designs of some current collectors, such as nickel,15 copper,16 carbonaceous framework,17â€“20 and transition metal compound arrays,21â€“23 show a significant improvement in coulombic efficiency and dendrite-free Li deposition. Nevertheless, the interface reactions between Li metal and electrolyte still remain a critical challenge. Therefore, an artificial SEI layer with robust mechanical stability and high Li-ion conductivity is necessary to stabilize the interface and to suppress Li dendrites. Electrochemical pretreatment,24â€“26 chemical pretreatment27â€“29 and physical pretreatment30â€“32 are three usual methods to fabricate an artificial SEI layer. Although electrochemical or chemical pretreatments are promising to modify Li metal, they usually involve a complicated procedure. For example, the electrochemical pretreatment includes steps such as pretreatment in cells, dismantling cells to obtain the modified anode, and assembly of the new cells. In comparison, physical pretreatment such as coating is more facile and cost-effective for mass production. Each of the methods mentioned above has its own merits and their integration might realize a safe Li metal anode.

Herein, we propose a facile method to synthesize a composite Li anode modified by trace amounts (only 1.6 wt%) of fluorinated carbon nanotubes (FCNT) using a physical pretreatment method. The FCNT-modified Li electrode (LFCNT) realizes in situ formation of a LiF-rich artificial SEI layer via an electrochemical process. Experiments and density functional theory (DFT) calculations demonstrate that FCNT could react with Li during Li plating to form carbon nanotubes (CNT) and LiF. The presence of a CNT-reinforced, LiF-rich layer enables uniform plating/stripping of metallic Li, inhibiting the growth of Li-dendrites effectively. The even distribution of CNT with a high mechanical strength and LiF-rich SEI layer preserves the structural integrity of the Li anode and maintains the interfacial stability with the electrolyte upon long-time cycling. As a result, the LFCNT electrode can sustain stable cycling at high current density up to 3 mA cmâˆ’2 and large capacity up to 5 mA h cmâˆ’2. The Liâ€“O2 cell with the LFCNT anode shows a six-fold cycle life compared to that with the bare Li anode, indicating excellent performance of the LFCNT anode.

Experimental section

First, FCNT powder (â‰¥99.5%, XFNANO Co., Ltd) was dried in a vacuum oven at 80 Â°C for 10 h. Results and discussion

A versatile and simple approach was employed to fabricate a LFCNT composite electrode. Li metal was heated at 350 Â°C under argon, and the molten Li was mixed with FCNT powder to obtain homogeneous LFCNT. SEM characterization studies were performed to investigate the morphology of the composite electrode. Fig. S1 (ESIâ€ ) shows the initial morphology of FCNT, which indicates that the nanotubes have a diameter of 20â€“30 nm and a length of several microns. As shown in Fig. 1aâ€“d, bare Li exhibits a relatively flat surface, while the LFCNT shows a rough surface. It can be seen in the enlarged image that FCNTs are uniformly dispersed on the surface of the electrode, and the energy dispersive X-ray spectrometry (EDS) mapping of the F element also confirms the uniform distribution of FCNTs on the electrode surface. Fig. S2 (ESIâ€ ) shows the cross-sectional image of bare Li and LFCNT. Compared to the neat surface of Li, the presence of FCNTs is obvious for LFCNT, indicating that FCNTs were introduced into Li during the melting/mixing process. XPS in Fig. 1e shows that an additional Li 1s peak appears in the spectrum of LFCNT compared with that of FCNT, indicating the successful formation of the LFCNT composite. The appearance of the O 1s peak is due to air exposure before the XPS characterization. The appearance of the LiF peak in Fig. 1f reveals that part of FCNT has reacted with Li during the meltingâ€“soaking process, where the large Gibbs free energy change is responsible for the reaction and formation of LiF.38 As a result, the addition of 1.6 wt% FCNT changes the surface morphology of Li. It is expected that the FCNT in the composite electrode will regulate the stripping/plating behaviors of metallic Li during cycling.

To evaluate the protection effect of FCNT, bare Li and LFCNT electrodes were placed in open air (RH = 60%) for 75 h. As shown in Fig. 2a, the surface of bare Li changes from silver to grey in 1 min, and the surface color becomes white after 75 h. It can be seen from the side view that the center of bare Li becomes sunken and its edge becomes warped. These transformations are due to the reactions between Li and N2, O2, CO2, H2O in air, and the final white surface indicates that most of the Li has transformed to LiOH after 75 h. In contrast, the color of the LFCNT surface has no obvious change and the electrode maintains a flat surface without warping. Fig. S3 (ESIâ€ ) shows the composition evolution of LFCNT and bare Li in open air. In the initial state, both Li and LFCNT show a sharp lithium peak and the LFCNT shows the Li3N peak due to the exposure to N2 in air during the XRD test. After being exposed to air for 75 h, the main degradation product of bare Li is LiOH while it is Li3N for LFCNT (Fig. S3c, ESIâ€ ). It can be concluded that the LFCNT is very stable towards H2O in air. H2O is one harmful component in the electrolyte and is detrimental to the Li anode and cycle life of cells while N2 content is usually low in the electrolyte.


Fig. 2 (a) Variation of bare Li and LFCNT electrodes with time in open air, impedance evolution of the symmetric cells with (b) LFCNT and (c) bare Li electrodes in open circuit voltage and after cycling (5 cycles at a capacity of 1 mA h cmâˆ’2 and a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2), (d) rate capability of the symmetric cells at a capacity of 1 mA h cmâˆ’2, and (e) polarized potentials of bare Li and LFCNT at a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2.

EIS measurements were conducted to study the resistance variation with time of the symmetric cells using LFCNT and bare Li electrodes. Fig. 2b and c exhibit the impedance change of LFCNT and bare Li from 0 h to 200 h. The Nyquist plots begin with ohmic resistance (Re) at high frequency followed by semicircles at lower frequencies that correspond to the surface film resistance of SEI (Rf) and charge transfer resistance (Rct) of the electrode, respectively.7,39 Here we define the sum of Rf and Rct as the electrode interface impedance (Rinterface). Table S1 (ESIâ€ ) summarizes the fitting results of impedance using the equivalent circuit in Fig. S4 (ESIâ€ ). Under open circuit voltage conditions, the Rinterface of LFCNT and bare Li converges to the maximal values after 200 h, indicating that the SEI thickness and composition tend to be constant. The results reveal that Re is far lower than Rinterface, which thus suggests that the overpotential and Li plating/stripping behavior depends mainly on Rinterface. As time increases, the Rinterface of the symmetric cell with LFCNT changes from 147 Î© to 422 Î©, and the major change comes from the increase in Rct, while for the symmetric cell with bare Li, the increase in Rinterface is 456 Î© (from 300 Î© to 756 Î©), where both Rf and Rct have a huge increase. The increase in Rf and Rct is due to the thickness growth of SEI and inevitable reaction between Li and electrolyte.40 After cycling (5 cycles), the Rinterface value of the symmetric cell with LFCNT is 41.2 Î©, which is lower than that with bare Li (81.1 Î©), corresponding to the lower voltage hysteresis of the symmetric cell with LFCNT in the following tests. The Rinterface values of symmetric cells with both LFCNT and bare Li are much lower than those measured just after assembly (0 h). It can be due to a large increase in the surface area of the lithium anode after cycling. The lower impedance and less variation of LFCNT can be attributed to the CNT/LiF layer in situ formed on the surface. As previously stated, the reaction between FCNT and Li produces LiF, which is a strong electron insulator.41 The SEI layer in the thickness direction is not homogeneous. The layer close to the Li metal surface contains Li2CO3, LiOH, Li3N, LiF and Li2O, labeled as the inorganic layer, while the outer part of the surface film is labeled as the organic layer containing ROCO2Li and RCOO2Li (R is an organic group related to the solvent).8 Li2CO3 has a relatively high ionic conductivity (âˆ¼10âˆ’8 S cmâˆ’1) but it is not a good electron insulator, while LiF is a good electron insulator but has relatively low ionic conductivity. Neither of them alone meets the desired properties of an ideal SEI. Hence, LiF can form a heterogeneous structure with Li2CO3 to improve ionic conduction and block electron transfer.42 Ozhabes reported that the surface diffusion barrier for ion transfer at the LiF/Li surface is about 0.1 eV, much lower than that of Li2CO3 (0.24 eV).43 The low diffusion barrier facilitates Li-ion conductivity and reduces the Rct. The CNT/LiF layer can also suppress electrolyte decomposition and Li consumption corresponding to the tiny change of Rf. Fig. 2d presents the rate capability of bare Li and LFCNT electrodes tested by cycling the symmetric cells at different current densities with a fixed areal capacity of 1 mA h cmâˆ’2. The LFCNT demonstrates a stable voltage polarization of 35, 50, 100 and 150 mV at a current density of 0.5, 1, 3 and 5 mA cmâˆ’2, respectively. Nevertheless, bare Li exhibits a much larger voltage polarization, especially at a high current density of 5 mA cmâˆ’2, where the voltage hysteresis exceeds 300 mV. Such phenomenon is attributed to the uneven Li plating/stripping process and large kinetic obstacle at high current density.7,44 The improved rate performance of LFCNT indicates that the modified surface layer facilitates the transport of Li-ions and reduces the difference of local current densities to promote the even deposition of Li. Fig. 2e shows the polarization curve of bare Li and LFCNT at a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2. The LFCNT electrode delivers a capacity of 96 mA h without voltage fluctuation which corresponds to a utilization ratio of 96.4% of Li in LFCNT, demonstrating the stable SEI layer and dendrite-free behavior during Li deposition. In contrast, bare Li shows drastic fluctuation and large polarization potential, implying the uneven deposition and formation of Li dendrites. Based on the above results, we can conclude that the LFCNT composite could effectively alleviate side reactions between Li and electrolyte and reduce the interface impedance to favor the uniform plating/stripping of Li.

Fig. 3 shows the voltage hysteresis of the symmetric cells with LFCNT and bare Li electrodes at different current densities and capacities. Voltage hysteresis is considered to be the sum of overpotential for Li plating/stripping.45 As shown in Fig. 3a, the LFCNT cell exhibits stable voltage profiles with a low voltage hysteresis and maintains a long cycling time over 1300 h without voltage fluctuation. In contrast, the bare Li cell shows a much higher voltage hysteresis in the first few cycles, corresponding to the formation of the SEI layer, and displays a gradual augment in hysteresis voltage which is always larger than that of the LFCNT cell. There are some fluctuations in the voltage profiles of bare Li cells during cycling and a sudden voltage drop is detected after 1300 h, which is ascribed to the fracture of the SEI on bare Li and formation of Li dendrites.8 Voltage profiles of the LFCNT cell at different cycles further demonstrate the lower overpotential and relatively flat voltage plateau compared to that of the bare Li cell (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ). When the current density is increased to 3 mA cmâˆ’2, the LFCNT cell still presents much better cycling stability without voltage fluctuation, while the bare Li cell exhibits an obviously fluctuated voltage profile and worse cell performance. Fig. S6 (ESIâ€ ) and Fig. 3c present the voltage hysteresis of the LFCNT cell and bare Li cell with a large capacity of 3 mA h cmâˆ’2 and 5 mA h cmâˆ’2, respectively. The voltage profiles of bare Li exhibit conspicuous fluctuations in the first several cycles, indicating a big obstacle for Li plating/stripping with a high capacity, in good agreement with Fig. 2e. In contrast, the LFCNT cell shows a much lower overpotential and more stable cycling, which is attributed to the superior mechanical stability of the CNT/LiF layer and its ability to regulate the uniform Li plating/stripping.

To further understand the reasons for performance improvement of the LFCNT electrode, the morphologies of bare Li and LFCNT electrodes were observed after 100 cycles at 1 mA cmâˆ’2 with a capacity of 1 mA h cmâˆ’2. As shown in Fig. 4a and b, the bare Li electrode exhibits a rough surface composed of large Li agglomerates, indicating the uneven deposition of Li and formation of Li dendrites. Such a huge morphology variation of the bare Li accounts for the large voltage hysteresis and poor cycling stability of the symmetric cells. In contrast, the surface of LFCNT remains flat without detectable Li agglomerates and dendrites (Fig. 4d). The enlarged image in Fig. 4e shows that the SEI layer of LFCNT possesses a flat surface with little undulation. Compared to the XPS spectrum in Fig. 1f, it can be speculated that the FCNT on the surface of LFCNT has reacted with Li during cycling and it was converted to CNTs and LiF. The LiF-rich SEI layer allows for the formation of a robust structure inhibiting the puncture of Li dendrites and a higher ionic conductivity, facilitating rapid Li-ion transport. DFT calculations were conducted to gain insight into the mechanism for the performance improvement of the LFCNT electrode. The FCNT we used is multi-walled fluorinated carbon nanotubes with a diameter of 20â€“30 nm. Here we adopt bilayer fluorinated graphene including 16 C and 16 F to simulate the partial configuration of FCNT (Fig. S7a, ESIâ€ ). Eight Li atoms are intercalated into the fluorinated graphene layers to simulate the Li plating process. As shown in Fig. 4i, F atoms of the fluorinated graphene layers between the Li atoms are detached after structural optimization, generating a flat graphene layer, while the other fluorinated graphene layers have no obvious structural changes. The side view of the optimized structure in Fig. S7b (ESIâ€ ) further verifies the formation of a flat graphene layer and LiF. The strong interaction between Li and F atoms results in the fracture of the Câ€“F bond, causing the hybridization of carbon to change from sp3 to sp2. The calculation results indicate that the above change is spontaneous and an energy of 4.103 eV is released with each insertion of the Li atom. With the progress of Li plating, the FCNT is converted to carbon nanotubes with more LiF formed in the SEI layer. The SEI layer containing carbon nanotubes and LiF can effectively promote the uniform Li stripping/plating and suppress the growth of Li dendrites, while the SEI of bare Li has a poor mechanical stability and is easy to be fractured.

In situ optical microscopy images were recorded to investigate the Li deposition behavior on LFCNT and bare Li directly. From Fig. 5a, it is clear that Li dendrites grow rapidly from the surface of bare Li at a current density of 1 mA cmâˆ’2, which would cause safety issues such as cell short circuit and capacity loss due to the formation of dead Li. Once Li dendrites form, the roots of dendrites tend to dissolve earlier during stripping, resulting in the shedding of Li dendrites and fracturing of the SEI layer. In contrast, no obvious surface variation is observed on the surface of LFCNT, suggesting the uniform deposition of Li and the good structural stability of the SEI layer. It can thus be concluded that the introduction of FCNT significantly improves the deposition behavior of Li and thereby the electrochemical performance. Videos were recorded to see the dynamic changes of Li deposition behavior on LFCNT and bare Li electrodes (Videos 1 and 2â€ ). To display the changes more obviously, the videos are played at a speed 100 times that of the original one. The results indicate that a small amount of FCNT (1.6 wt%) can effectively inhibit the growth of dendrites.

Based on the comprehensive analysis of experiments and calculation results, diagrams of the Li plating/stripping behavior on bare Li and LFCNT electrodes are presented in Fig. 5b. For the bare Li electrode, the inhomogeneous plating of Li results in the growth of Li dendrites, and the large contact area of Li with the electrolyte causes huge side reactions and consumption of Li and electrolyte. During the stripping process, the uneven current density distribution leads to the undulating surface and rupture of the SEI layer. The faster dissolution of dendrite roots causes the detachment of the dendrite tip (dead Li) which damages the electrode and reduces capacity gradually. As for LFCNT, the robust protection layer containing CNT/LiF exhibits a good mechanical stability and high conductivity for Li-ion transport. During the Li plating/stripping process, the surface of LFCNT remains flat with Li depositing or stripping beneath the SEI layer homogeneously. Consequently, the LFCNT electrode shows lower voltage hysteresis, better rate capability and longer cycle life.

To highlight the critical role of FCNT introduction for Li dendrite elimination and the accompanied performance improvement, a separator was attached to one side of the symmetric cells. As shown in Fig. 6a, for the bare Li electrode, Li dendrites can easily penetrate the separator during the Li deposition process. Videos were also recorded to vividly see the Li deposition behavior on the LFCNT and bare Li electrodes (Videos 3 and 4â€ ). It can be seen from Video 4â€  that the separator moves in the opposite direction of Li-ion transport at the beginning of Li deposition. As the Li deposition proceeds, some Li dendrites can penetrate the separator and even reach the other side of the symmetric cell. In an actual cell, this will inevitably cause a short circuit of the cell. Besides the formation of dendrites, the newly deposited Li layer is loosely stacked, in sharp contrast to the dense packing of the pristine electrode. Therefore, after repeated Li plating/stripping processes, dead Li would form which would lead to the degradation and final death of the Li electrode. In contrast, for the LFCNT electrode, no dendrites can be observed and the newly deposited Li layer is also densely stacked as shown in Fig. 6b and Video 3.â€

The superior performance can be attributed to the stable structure of LFCNT with little degradation during cycling. The LiF-rich SEI layer can facilitate the Li-ion transport and promote uniform Li plating/stripping with inhibited Li dendrites. As a result, the cell with LFCNT shows a lower overpotential, a larger discharge capacity and longer cycle life. Conclusion

In summary, a LFCNT composite anode has been fabricated to address the dendrite issues of the Li metal anode. Through a simple synthesis method, the Li electrode protected by the FCNT layer with a small amount (1.6 wt%) shows much better stability in open air and slower degradation in the electrolyte than bare Li. During initial plating processes, a CNT-reinforced, LiF-rich SEI layer with robust mechanical stability and flat surface formed on the surface of LFCNT. Owing to the high Li-ion conductivity of the LiF-rich layer and effective electron transfer with carbon nanotubes, uniform plating/stripping of metallic Li is achieved, promoting the dendrite-free Li deposition and flat morphology of the LFCNT electrode. As a result, symmetric half cells with LFCNT electrodes show a significant improvement in rate capability, voltage hysteresis and cycle life, with stable cycling even at a high current density of 3 mA cmâˆ’2 and a large capacity of 5 mA h cmâˆ’2. Liâ€“O2 full cells with the LFCNT anode deliver a high capacity of 4916 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100 mA gâˆ’1 and a long cycle life of 135 cycles at a limited capacity of 1000 mA h gâˆ’1 under a current density of 400 mA gâˆ’1, greatly improved compared to those with the bare Li anode. This work provides a facile, cost-effective strategy to prepare dendrite-free Li metal electrodes, which show promising applications in Li metal batteries.
