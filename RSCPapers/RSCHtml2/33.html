Abstract

Here, we report zinc sulfide quantum dots, ZnS(QDs), moored on N-doped functionalized multiwall carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) wrapped with reduced graphene oxide (rGO). The MWCNTs have a tangled network, a particular surface area, and a distinctive hollow structure that may be suitable for use as a counter electrode (CE) material. A ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO composite as the CE on a fluorine-doped tin oxide substrate in a dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) was fabricated using a doctor blade technique. The electrochemical performance showed that at the electrolyte/CE interface, the ZnS(QDs) and N-doped functionalized MWCNTs wrapped with rGO (ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO) electrode has a lower transfer charge resistance (Rct) and a greater catalytic capacity than naked ZnS(QDs). A power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 9.4% was attained for this DSSC gadget, which is higher than that of a DSSC gadget utilizing ZnS(QDs), ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs, ZnS@rGO and Pt. Also, the DSSC device using ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO had a fill factor (FF) that was better than the other counter electrodes. The cyclic voltammetry and electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) electron transfer measurements showed that ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO films can provide fast electron transfer from the electrolyte to the CE and great electrocatalytic activity to reduce triiodide to a CE based on ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO in the DSSC.

1. Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are promising next generation solar cells owing to their simple formation and the ability to achieve high photovoltaic (PV) productivity.1–3 Phenomenal progress has been made over the previous century in upgrading the performance and lowering the cost of creating DSSCs.2–5 A classic dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) relies primarily on a platinum Pt-based electrocatalyst as a counter electrode (CE) for reducing triiodide to iodide. The use of expensive and corroded Pt counter electrodes (CEs) restricts the future enhancement of easy DSSCs.3,6 In this respect, the other materials used to replace the Pt electrocatalyst in DSSCs (CEs) are extremely attractive. DSSCs are one of the most encouraging photovoltaic gadgets that have been used to tackle cost-effective fabrication and moderate gadget implementation in clean electricity tests.7–10 The counter electrode of the DSSCs assumes two key parts in a DSSC gadget: the reduction of I3− to I− in the transport reaction utilizing electrocatalytic reduction, and the gathering of electrons from the outer circuit. In this case, great CE materials must show a high synergistic action towards the CE reduction response and great conductivity. Presently, the noble metal Pt is the most helpful counter electrode in DSSCs, but its use is constrained due to it being an exceptional but very expensive material. In this way, it is exceptionally attractive to investigate easy and plentiful materials to supplant Pt as the counter electrodes of DSSCs for future large scale manufacture. Up until this point, various new materials have been reported as being valuable and proficient CEs of DSSCs such as metals,11 metal oxides,12–14 chalcogenides,15–18 and carbon-based materials.5,19–24 Carbon-based materials are particularly appealing because of their natural richness, take minimal effort to manufacture, and their great electrocatalytic activity.

Over the last few decades, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have been considered in various fields because of their physical and chemical properties.25–27 The doping or covering and functionalizing of CNTs is considered to improve the properties of CNTs for different applications.28,29 Alteration of CNTs by filling or covering with different metal-collides, oxides, or sulfide have been widely reported, yet methods for controlling them are indispensable to researchers.30–36 Chemically altered CNTs might be enhanced to give outstanding characteristics and extraordinary potential for implementation.37–39 We have recently shown that nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes (N-CNTs) encapsulating FeNi nanoparticles could be perfect for use as CEs in DSSCs, demonstrating a great synergistic action in the redox response of the (I−/I3−) transport and a superior gadget productivity in correlation with that of the Pt CE, although Fe nanoparticles restricted to CNTs did not display adequate movement in the CE reactions of the DSSCs. Different metals can also have an exceptional impact on the electronic structure of the CNTs on the surface which would have a considerable impact on the carbon surface catalytic activity.40–42 Subsequently, it is essential to investigate the effect on the motion of the CE reactions of multiple metals decorated with CNTs and the related execution of the DSSC that gives a standardized technique for guiding the CE strategy of the DSSCs. In this way, it is important to examine the impact of various metal decorated CNTs on the movement of CE reactions and the related DSSC execution, which could give a rational method to direct the structure of the CEs of the DSSCs. Diverse nano-materials such as SnO2, ZnO, and Pt with a controlled size have been utilized for the covering of multiwall carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs).43–46 One atom thick graphene and the two-dimensional layer of the sp2-bound crystal structure is a creative example of the carbon crystal family owing to its excellent properties such as thermal conductivity, high carrier mobility, transparency, and electric conductivity.45,47 In any case, neither zinc sulfide quantum dots (ZnS(QDs)), MWCNTs, nor graphene oxide (GO) can achieve remarkable conversion proficiency in DSSCs as a CE. To accomplish great proficiency for DSSCs as a CE, ZnS(QDs) attached to N-doped functionalized multiwall carbon nanotubes (N.f-MWCNTs) and wrapped with rGO could be an astounding solution. In this research, we have prepared ZnS(QDs) attached to N.f-MWCNTs and wrapped them with rGO as a CE in DSSCs for the first time. The size of the ZnS(QDs) in the filling and covering procedure of the N.f-MWCNTs surface can be constrained simply by adjusting the reaction time and temperature. The ZnS(QDs), ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs, ZnS@rGO and ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO composites have been prepared by means of the controllable and low-cost hydrothermal method.

In this research work, we have formed N-doped f-MWCNT confining ZnS(QDs), which are correspondingly assigned as N.f-MWCNT–ZnS and further surveyed their suitability as a CE for DSSCs. The results demonstrated that the composites showed a high synergistic activity in the CE reduction reaction (I3− to I−) and excellent electrochemical stability. Furthermore, the DSSC gadgets yielded a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 8.5% for the ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs framework and 9.4% for the ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO, which are practically identical to or superior to the control gadget made with Pt (as the CE), indicating their potential for application in the advancement of low-cost and high productivity DSSCs.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials

All of the reagents used in this study were purchased from commercial sources, they were of chemical grade and were used without further purification. Shanghai Mackin Biochemical Co., Ltd provided the pure MWCNTs, according to the producer pattern; the inner diameter of the MWCNTs was approximately 5–12 nm, the external diameter 25–50 nm, and length 10–20 μm with a purity ≥95%. Zinc acetate dihydrate (C4H6O4Zn·2H2O) (AR, 98%), sodium sulfide nonahydrate (Na2S·9H2O) (AR ≥ 98%), ammonia solution (NH4OH) (AR, 25–28%), sulfuric acid (H2SO4) (AR, 95–98%), phosphoric acid (H3PO4) (AR ≥ 85%), nitric acid (HNO3) (AR ≤ 65–69%), potassium permanganate (KMnO4) (AR, 99.5%), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) (AR, 30% aqueous solution), and polyethylene glycol (C2H6O2) (AR, 99%), were purchased from Guoyao Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. Deionized (DI) water was obtained from an electrothermal distiller in our lab and used for all experiments.

The working electrode (WEs), TiO2, had an active surface area of 0.16 cm2 (0.4 cm × 0.4 cm) and a thickness of 10 μm, the Pt coated counter electrode (CE), fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) layered glass substrates “NSG, 13 Ω sq−1, 1 × 2 cm, “Dye “N719” and the electrolyte were supplied by Yingkou OPV Tech. New Energy Co., Ltd. Liaoning (Yingkou) China. According to the manufacturer's specification, these electrolytes consist of 0.6 M 1-propyl-2,3-dimethyl-imidazolium iodide, 0.5 M LiI, 0.05 M I2 and 0.5 M 4-tert-buylpyridine in acetonitrile solution.

2.2. Preparation of the ZnS(QDs) and ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs CE catalyst

The ZnS(QDs) and ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs catalysts were prepared using the hydrothermal technique.48 In brief, the N.f-MWCNTs (0.1 g, further details can be found in the ESI†) were scattered in 10 mL of ethanol and sonicated until all of the ethanol was removed from the mixture to form a homogenous solution. Subsequently, a C4H6O4Zn·2H2O (0.43902 g, 2 mmoL) and DI water (60 mL) mixed solution was added to the above solution and this was ultrasonicated for another hour to form a homogeneous solution. After this, NH4OH was added dropwise into the solution under magnetic stirring until the pH value reached 9. Subsequently, Na2S·9H2O (0.96072 g, 4 mmoL) was dissolved in DI water (10 mL) in another beaker and was added into the above mentioned solution under magnetic stirring for 1 h. After the solution was well mixed, the composite solution was poured into a Teflon-lined steel autoclave with a capacity of 100 mL and heated to 140 °C for 10 h. After the autoclave had naturally cooled down to normal temperature, the obtained precipitates were washed several times with DI water and ethanol. Lastly, the residue was gained by lyophilization at −75 °C for 24 h for subsequent use. Pure ZnS(QDs) were also prepared under the same reaction conditions but without the addition of N-doped f-MWCNTs for comparison.

2.3. Acidification and functionalization of the MWCNTs

The MWCNTs were gained from commercial suppliers, and the purity of the MWCNTs was improved by removing the carbonaceous material. Corrosive handling is a simple and helpful approach to expel the impurities and increments in the convergence of the (–COOH and –OH) functional groups by using a refluxing strategy.49–51 Corrosive handling and functionalization of the pure MWCNTs are essential for the formation of ZnS(QDs) on the surface walls of the MWCNTs. 2.5. Preparation of the ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO CE catalyst

The ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO catalyst was prepared using the hydrothermal technique. The ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs and GO were referred to as a base material in the aqueous solution. In brief, GO (0.03 g) was added into 30 mL of ethanol solution in a beaker with a 50 mL capacity under the presence of ultra-sonication and stirred for 1 h to produce a homogeneous solution. Subsequently, the ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs nanocomposite (0.09 g) was added slowly into the above described GO solution while stirring. After that, the mixture was poured into a Teflon-lined steel autoclave with a capacity of 100 mL and placed at 160 °C for 12 h. Subsequently, it was cooled at room temperature and the precipitates were rinsed with DI water and ethanol. Finally, the residue was gained by lyophilization at −75 °C for a period of 24 h for subsequent use, and the ZnS@rGO was also prepared under the same reaction conditions without the addition of N-doped f-MWCNTs for comparison. During the hydrothermal reduction process, GO was reduced to reduced graphene oxide (rGO), as the functional groups (–COOH and –OH) attached to the edges of the GO were removed.

3.2. Photoelectric properties of the as-synthesized CE catalyst

In this research study, we selectively built DSSCs to examine the photoelectrical characteristics using the samples as counter electrodes. Therefore, for instruments fitted with distinct CEs, we carried out electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS), Tafel polarization, cyclic voltammetry (CV), and photocurrent density–voltage (J–V) experiments.
3.2.1. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy investigations. The electrocatalytic activity can be assessed using EIS measurements. To examine the interfacial charge transfer method between the CEs and the electrolyte solution, and the electrocatalytic activity of the cell in the DSSC, an EIS examination was performed and is shown in Fig. 4a. In this, the ion reduction of I3− in the CEs and the equivalent circuit have been extensively used.62 This is a useful way to illustrate the electrocatalyst of all of the different pairs of electrodes, which can clearly explain the internal interface charge transport system. In EIS analysis, three semicircles can be observed in the Nyquist curves, which show the different impedance of the DSSC. It is remarkable that in Fig. 4a there are two semicircles observed. The first semi-circle on the left indicates the high-frequency area in the Nyquist curve that is attributed to the charge transfer resistance (Rct) of the CE/electrolyte interface and represents the catalytic activity of the CE for I3− reduction. 4. Conclusions

In summary, we have examined the synergistic impact of carbon-based counter electrodes on DSSCs. ZnS(QDs) have been successfully fabricated for the first time using a hydrothermal method, with N-doped MWCNTs encapsulated with rGO. The synergistic effects, from EIS, Tafel polarization, CV, and J–V studies, have been evaluated. In addition, the N.f-MWCNTs in rGO significantly strengthened the electrocatalytic activity of ZnS(QDs), which can offer a rapid transport media for electrons and decrease the Rct of the CEs in DSSCs. The composite-based CE (ZnS@N.f-MWCNTs@rGO) has a PCE of 9.4%, greater than conventional Pt-based CEs and offers more stable catalytic activities in CV tests. Therefore, further optimization and reliable photovoltaic cell manufacturing of the hybrid counter electrode based on MWCNTs will help to increase the PCE levels. In particular, the approach presented provides a sensible method for the design of versatile, cost-effective electrocatalysts with high catalytic activity.
