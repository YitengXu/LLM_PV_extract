An A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecule donor for high-performance organic solar cells†

Qian Zhang‡ *a, Yanna Sun‡ b, Xianjie Chen a, Zhijing Lin a, Xin Ke b, Xiaoyuan Wang a, Tian He a, Shouchun Yin a, Yongsheng Chen b and Huayu Qiu *a
aCollege of Material, Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Hangzhou Normal University, Hangzhou, 311121, P. R. China. E-mail: qzhang@hznu.edu.cn; hyqiu@hznu.edu.cn
bThe Centre of Nanoscale Science and Technology and Key Laboratory of Functional Polymer Materials, State Key Laboratory and Institute of Elemento Organic Chemistry, Collaborative Innovation Center of Chemical Science and Engineering (Tianjin), College of Chemistry, Nankai University, Tianjin, 300071, China

Abstract

A new A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecule denoted as BDD–IN was synthesized employing a strong electron-withdrawing unit, 3-bis(4-(2-ethylhexyl)-thiophen-2-yl)-5,7-bis(2-ethylhexyl)benzo[1,2:4,5-c′]-dithiophene-4,8-dione (BDD) as the central acceptor unit (A1), thiophene-alkoxy benzene-thiophene as the π-bridge, and indenedione (IN) as the end-capped unit (A2). An optimal power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 8.70% with a high open-circuit voltage (Voc) of 0.965 V and a high fill factor (FF) of 72.3% was achieved.

Organic solar cells (OSCs) have emerged as a promising renewable energy source to address the energy crisis and environment-related issues.1 Through great efforts devoted to photoactive material innovation and device optimization, a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of over 14% has been achieved for single-junction polymer non-fullerene solar cells.2 Compared with polymers, small molecules have many unique advantages, such as well-defined molecular structure and high reproducibility, simple synthesis and so on.3 And small molecules show great potential for high performance solar cells.4 Among various types of small molecules containing a donor–acceptor (D–A) structure, A–π–D–π–A-type small molecules, which incorporate an electron-donating unit as the central core, two conjugated π bridges, and two electron-accepting terminal units, have presented outstanding photovoltaic performance whether used as electron donors or electron acceptors.5 For example, Chen's group reported a small molecule DR3TSBDT with dialkylthiol substituted BDT as the core and 3-ethylrhodanine as the end-capped unit which exhibited a high PCE of 9.95%.6 Wei's group reported a series of small molecules which incorporated thiophene-substituted benzodithiophene as the core and fluorinated 1H-indene-1,3(2H)-dione as the end-capped units, among which a high PCE of 11.08% was achieved.7 In addition, since Zhan's group reported ITIC based on a bulky fused-ring core (indacenodithieno[3,2-b]thiophene) end-capped with a 2-(3-oxo-2,3-dihydroinden-1-ylidene)malononitrile unit, a large number of chemists have focused on the A–D–A or A–π–D–π–A-type non-fullerene acceptors, and excellent photovoltaic performances have been obtained.8

So, how about substituting the central electron-donating unit with an electron-accepting unit, namely A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecules?9 It has been demonstrated that the open-circuit voltage (Voc) mainly depends on the energy level difference between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level of the donor materials and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) level of the acceptor materials.3a,b Previous research studies have indicated that the HOMO energy level and the LUMO energy level are generally dependent on the electron-donating ability of the donor units and the electron-withdrawing ability of the acceptor units, respectively.5,10 Therefore, it is expected that a larger Voc could be obtained by increasing the electron-accepting ability of the whole molecule when the small molecule is used as the donor material. A 3-bis(4-(2-ethylhexyl)-thiophen-2-yl)-5,7-bis(2ethylhexyl)benzo[1,2:4,5-c′]-dithiophene-4,8-dione (BDD) unit is a strong electron-withdrawing and planar group, which is widely used for constructing D–A alternate copolymer donors.11 Notably, the polymer PBDB-T based on the BDD unit developed by Hou's group has exhibited excellent performance as a wide bandgap donor in non-fullerene polymer solar cells.12

Incorporating noncovalent intramolecular interactions into the molecular backbone is an effective way to enhance chain coplanarity, which could lead to enhanced molecular order beneficial for exciton diffusion and charge carrier transport.13 In particular, noncovalent intramolecular O⋯S interactions between alkoxy substituents and thiophene rings have been demonstrated to be effective for minimizing torsional angles within polymer backbones.14 Herein, we designed and synthesized a new A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecule BDD–IN which incorporated BDD as the central building block (A1), thiophene-alkoxy benzene-thiophene as the π-bridge and indenedione (IN) as the terminal units (A2). Its chemical structure is depicted in Fig. 1a. Bulk-heterojunction OSCs using BDD–IN as the donor and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM) as the acceptor were prepared and the photovoltaic properties were optimized. A high PCE of 8.70% was achieved along with a high Voc of 0.965 V, a short-circuit current density (Jsc) of 12.44 mA cm−2 and a high fill factor (FF) of 72.3%.

The target molecule could be obtained via Stille cross-coupling reaction and Knoevenagel condensation within a few steps, and the detailed synthetic procedures and characterization data are provided in the ESI.† The chemical structure of BDD–IN was confirmed by NMR spectroscopy and mass spectrometry (Fig. S1–S12, ESI†). The compound showed good solubility in common organic solvents, such as chloroform, and chlorobenzene. BDD–IN shows good thermal stability with the decomposition temperature (Td, 5% weight loss) of 351 °C under a N2 atmosphere (Fig. S13a, ESI†). A clear melting temperature (Tm) of 216 °C upon heating and the recrystallization point (Tcr) at 197 °C upon cooling for the compound could be observed on the differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) curves, indicative of its crystallinity (Fig. S13b, ESI†).

The UV-vis absorption spectra of BDD–IN in chloroform solution and a thin film are shown in Fig. 1b. The absorption of BDD–IN in diluted CHCl3 solution exhibits a peak at 532 nm. The maximum extinction coefficient is 1.12 × 105 M−1 cm−1. From the solution to the solid state, the absorption spectrum was broadened and red-shifted with maxima at 569 nm. Besides, there exists a pronounced shoulder peak at 616 nm, suggesting effective π–π stacking between molecular backbones. The optical band gap of BDD–IN is 1.83 eV calculated from the absorption edge. Theoretical calculation using density functional theory (DFT) at the B3LYP/6-31G(d) level was also performed to evaluate the distribution of the frontier molecular orbitals and the molecular geometries. As shown in Fig. S14 (ESI†), the electron densities of the HOMO levels are mainly delocalized along the entire molecular backbone except for the end-capped groups, while the electron densities of the LUMO levels are delocalized along the entire molecular backbone. The extended LUMO wave function results in enhanced overlap with the HOMO wave function, which could contribute to its higher extinction coefficients.15 From Fig. S15 (ESI†), BDD–IN showed a dihedral angle of 21.62° between the BDD unit and its adjacent thiophene unit. The indicated dihedral angle might break the great aggregation tendency of fully planar molecules, which would contribute to the nanoscale bi-continuous interpenetrating network morphology.16

The electrochemical properties of the compound were studied by cyclic voltammetry (Fig. 1c). The potentials were internally calibrated using the ferrocene/ferrocenium (Fc/Fc+) redox couple (4.8 eV below the vacuum level). The HOMO and LUMO levels calculated from the onset oxidation and reduction potentials are −5.40 and −3.51 eV, respectively. The electrochemical band gap of BDD–IN is estimated to be 1.89 eV, which is consistent with its optical band gap. The lower HOMO level indicates that a larger Voc could be expected by using the compound as a donor material.

To evaluate the photovoltaic performance of BDD–IN, conventional devices using BDD–IN as the donor material with the configuration of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/BDD–IN:PC71BM/PrC60MA/Al were fabricated, where PrC60MA represented a fulleropyrrolidinium iodide derivative developed by Alex K-Y. and its structure is shown in Fig. S16 (ESI†).17 Typical current density–voltage (J–V) curves of the as-cast and the optimized devices are shown in Fig. 2a. After a series of optimizations, it was found that solvent vapour annealing (SVA) treatment could deliver the optimal performance (Tables S1–S4, ESI†). Without any treatment, the devices delivered a very low PCE of 1.70%, with a Voc of 1.042 V, a Jsc of 5.75 mA cm−2 and an FF of 28.3%. The high Voc is attributed to the low HOMO energy level of BDD–IN. After the SVA treatment, the PCE was sharply enhanced to 8.70%, with a high Voc of 0.965 V, a Jsc of 12.44 mA cm−2 and a high FF of 72.3%. The reduction in the Voc after the SVA treatment may be due to increasing intermolecular interactions between the donor and acceptor.18 The strength of intermolecular interactions could be represented by Jso. As shown in Fig. S18 and Table S5 (ESI†), in comparison with the as-cast devices, the devices with the SVA treatment exhibit a higher Jso, which is in accordance with the decreased Voc. Notably, the FF is up to 76.3% when the donor concentration is 8 mg mL−1 (Table S1, ESI†). The FF values are higher than those of previously reported similar molecules.9a,b,15 The external quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra of the as-cast and optimized devices are shown in Fig. 2b. From Fig. 2b, the as-cast devices exhibit a relatively low EQE response with the maximum value of 37.5%. After the SVA treatment, uniform enhancement of the spectral response across the 300–700 nm wavelength region is clearly observed and the maximum value is 76.1%. The calculated Jsc values estimated from the integration of the EQE curves are 5.51 and 11.90 mA cm−2 for the as-cast and the optimized devices, respectively, which are in agreement with the values obtained from the J–V curves within an acceptable deviation of ∼5%.


Fig. 2 (a) Typical J–V curves and (b) EQE curves of the as-cast and SVA-treated devices based on BDD–IN.

To investigate the charge generation, collection and recombination behaviours, the photocurrent density (Jph) as a function of effective voltage (Veff) is plotted in double logarithmic coordinates shown in Fig. 3. The Jph/Jsat ratio can be employed to evaluate the overall exciton separation and charge collection efficiency. Under the short-circuit conditions, the Jph/Jsat ratios of the as-cast and optimal devices are 48% and 97%, respectively. Under the maximal power output conditions, the Jph/Jsat ratios of the as-cast and optimal devices are 25% and 83%, respectively. These results suggest higher exciton dissociation and charge collection efficiency, and less bimolecular recombination for devices with SVA treatment, and thus an enhanced Jsc and FF.


Fig. 3 Variation of Jph with Veff for the as-cast and SVA-treated BDD–IN:PC71BM-based devices.

To understand the effect of SVA treatment on the device performance, atomic force microscopy (AFM) was used to characterize the morphologies of the active layers. From Fig. 4a and b, the surface root-mean-square (RMS) roughness values for the as-cast and SVA-treated BDD–IN:PC71BM blend films are 0.67 and 0.79 nm, respectively. The blend films under different conditions all showed nanoscale phase separation, which may benefit from the twisted backbones of the small molecule donor compared with previously reported molecules with a nearly planar backbone.9b,15 Nevertheless, for the as-cast film, there are many aggregates with a size of about 50 nm, which are larger than the exciton diffusion length and adverse to exciton dissociation, and thus lead to a poor Jsc. The discontinuous phase aggregation could cause severe bimolecular recombination, which leads to a low FF. After the SVA treatment, the largely segregated donor and acceptor phases become adequately intermixed to form a good interpenetrating network, which could be beneficial for exciton dissociation and charge transport, and therefore the Jsc and FF were greatly enhanced. Besides, from the X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the neat BDD–IN film (Fig. S19a, ESI†), BDD–IN exhibited a very weak diffraction peak (100) at 2θ = 5.57°. After the SVA treatment, a distinct diffraction peak (100) at 2θ = 5.73° was observed. Similarly, for the as-cast BDD–IN:PC71BM blend film, no obvious diffraction peaks appeared. However, a diffraction peak at 2θ = 5.60° was present after SVA treatment (Fig. S19b, ESI†). These results indicate that SVA treatment enhanced the crystallinity of BDD–IN, which is good for exciton diffusion and charge transport.


Fig. 4 AFM height images of BDD–IN:PC71BM blend films (a) as-cast and (b) with SVA treatment. The scale bar is 5.0 μm.

The hole mobility (μh) and electron mobility (μe) of the blend films were tested by the SCLC method (Fig. S20, ESI†).19 For the as-cast devices, the hole/electron mobilities are 2.48 × 10−4 and 7.49 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively. For the SVA-treated devices, the hole/electron mobilities are 1.83 × 10−3 and 5.33 × 10−3 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively. After SVA treatment, higher mobilities are good for efficient charge transport and thus excellent Jsc and FF.

In conclusion, a new A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecule BDD–IN using BDD with strong electron-accepting ability as the central unit was designed and synthesized. This compound exhibited strong absorption in the wavelength range of 300–700 nm and deeper HOMO energy levels. After SVA treatment, the BDD–IN:PC71BM based devices delivered a high PCE of 8.70%, with a high Voc of 0.965 V, a Jsc of 12.44 mA cm−2 and a high FF of 72.3%. The results suggest that the A2–π–A1–π–A2-type small molecules are very promising to construct more efficient photovoltaic devices.
