Abstract

Owing to their capability of bypassing conventional high-priced and inflexible silicon based electronics to manufacture a variety of devices on flexible substrates by using large-scale and high-volume printing techniques, printed electronics (PE) have attracted increasing attention in the field of manufacturing industry for electronic devices. This simple and cost-effective approach could enhance current methods of constructing a patterned surface for nanomaterials and offer opportunities for developing fully-printed functional devices, especially offering the possibility of ubiquitous low-cost and flexible devices. This review presents a summary of work to date on the inorganic nanomaterials involved in PE applications, focused on the utilization of inorganic nanomaterials-based inks in the successful preparation of printed conductive patterns, electrodes, sensors, thin film transistors (TFTs) and other micro-/nanoscale devices. The printing techniques, sintering methods and printability of functional inks with their associated challenges are discussed, and we look forward so you can glimpse the future of PE applications.

1. Introduction

PE is a set of electronic devices manufactured by traditional printing techniques. The most important benefit of printing methods is their low-cost high-volume fabrication. Compared with conventional electronics, PE technology will bring about the possibility of preparing stacks of micro-/nanoscale devices in a much simpler, faster and cost-effective way. For example, traditional complex production processes and modern printing techniques are used in the manufacture of Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) antennas. The traditional method is etching the metal film to form the antenna, which will also produce a lot of acidbase waste in the production process. As shown in Fig. 1, it is clear that printing methods can save a lot of energy and reduce the amount of labor. Recently, various studies have illustrated that the PE technique is becoming an effective exploratory method to fabricate large-area and flexible electronic devices via the patterning of functional materials-based inks.1 Upcoming flexible/wearable electronics and the Internet of Things (IoT) are receiving widespread attention as a type of innovative technology that will drastically change our daily lives. Various PE techniques will make it possible to speed up their realization.
Fig. 1 The traditional manufactural procedures and printing technique for producing RFID antennas.

Therefore, the surging field of PE offers a promising methodology to manufacture novel functional electronic devices. The main focus of the present study is the development of printable functional inks and thin or flexible electronic devices. As shown in Fig. 2, PE technology is employed and used in various applications, including conductive lines or electrodes, energy conversion and storage devices (solar cells, capacitors, etc.), flexible displays, sensors, printed field-effect transistors (FETs), and intelligent and interactive packaging. Now, PE involves a series of technique applications from micro-/nanoscale electronic devices to large-area electronics across a large number of products. Innovation in printing technologies has made the high-volume manufacture of electronic devices possible at low cost on soft plastic, paper or textile substrates. This flexibility will enable the integration of printed and flexible electronics into everyday items and places we had only dreamed of before.


Fig. 2 The various application fields of the PE technique.

In this review, the advances and recent progress on inorganic nanomaterials-based PE technologies for various printed devices are described and updated, including printing techniques, printability requirements, sintering treatments and device fabrication. Various literatures have reported that a great number of inorganic nanomaterials have been synthesized and used in a variety of PE applications, which reveals that the performance of PE devices depends strongly on the combinations of printable materials, printing methods, printability, structures, sintering parameters and other process conditions during fabrication. These circumstances make analysis and speculation complicated. Indeed, one intriguing question that arises is whether all-printed electronic devices are the optimal method for fabricating high-volume customized electronics. Furthermore, the durability of printed devices is also a key factor from the practical application perspective. Consequently, parallel activities of basic research (finding new printable inorganic nanomaterials and/or working mechanisms) and practical applications (developing low-cost, thin and flexible devices) are needed to promote the development of PE techniques. We hope that these unique features of printable materials research, strongly tied to device architectures and manufactural processes, will be fully understood.

2. Printing technology and printable nanomaterials: requirements and challenges

Generally, printing is a reproductive process in which inks are deposited onto the printing substrates for transfer of information (such as images, graphics, text), and the forms reappear in seemingly different substrates by utilizing an image-carrying medium (such as the printing plate), and color reproduction becomes very important in traditional printing.2 However, PE is being explored for the fabrication of large-area and flexible devices by the patterned application of functional inks during the printing process. Therefore, PE becomes a cutting-edge technique that promises to enable a large number of creative applications in electronic fields through integrating functional nanomaterials with conventional printing technologies. Various printable functional materials have been developed and optimized for traditional printing approaches, including screen, inkjet, gravure, flexographic, and offset printing.

2.1 Screen printing

Screen printing is a wonderful technology which is especially beneficial for printing inks onto various rigid or flexible substrates, and the entire procedure is very simple, versatile and inexpensive.3 Indeed, screen printing is a push-through process using a porous material as the stencil, which means that during the printing process the inks pass through the stencil and are deposited onto the substrate. The fine mesh is often made from porous nylon textile, and the whole screen stretches over an aluminous or wooden frame, in which the range of fineness of the fabrics is 150 to 400 fiber per inch. However, a metal screen is often used in PE fabrication, and the fineness of the fabrics can reach 850 fiber per inch. The printable inks can penetrate the designed pattern zones because these areas are open, and the other zones are blocked off by hardened photosensitive materials, which form a screen plate. As shown in Fig. 3, the screen plate is placed on top and the inks put on it, until all the mesh openings are filled by ink. The operator then uses a squeegee to move the mesh down to the substrate and pushes the squeegee onto the back of the screen. In the mesh openings, the inks are pumped by capillary effect or squeezed onto the substrate in a manageable amount. When the squeegee moves forward, the tension on the back of screen will decrease, making a mesh out of the substrate, leaving the inks upon the substrate surface. Then we can obtain the designed patterns on the substrate, which can be used in the next stage. The inks go through four stages in the printing process: filling, contact, adhesion and release.
Fig. 3 Schematic demonstration of the manufacturing process of screen printing: (a) the synthesis of printable inks; (b) the inks will be printed as patterns on a rigid/flexible substrate by a designed screen plate; (c) the designed patterns can be obtained after the inks have dried.

With screen printing, the patterning of both inorganic and organic layers is possible. For example, Sekitani et al. developed a large-area wireless power-transmission sheet by using a printed organic transistor and a plastic MEMS switch. The position-sensing loop arrays were printed by a screen printing technique. The inner and outer sizes of the copper loops are 10 mm and 25 mm, respectively. Both the width and the spacing of the printed lines are 0.1 mm. The inductance and resistance are 20 H and 17 , respectively.4 Kuo et al. compared the conversion efficiencies of dye-sensitized solar cells produced by utilizing different printed working electrodes (PWE) via a stainless steel cloth screen (SSCS) or a stencil screen (SS), respectively. The results show that a tri-layer PWE fabricated by using an SSCS gives a cell with a conversion efficiency of 5.46%; in contrast, a mono-layer PWE fabricated by using an SS gives a cell with a conversion efficiency of 5.30%.5 Although several groups have reported the fabrication of various functional devices using the screen printing method, the achievable resolution is still too low for printing drain and source contacts.6 In the traditional screen printing process, the printed line width is in the range of 30100 m, and could reach ca. 10 m when a high-resolution metal screen is used.

2.2 Inkjet printing

As shown in Fig. 4, a drop-on-demand (DOD) printer only ejects drops of inks as required when the digital signal asks for it. There are two different methods in which ink is jetted from the nozzle. In a bubble-jet printing model, a heating unit inside the print head prepares the bubble of ink and forces a drop of ink out the nozzle. In a piezoelectric printing model, a ceramic piezoelectric tile in the nozzle flexes into the ink reservoir in the print head, forcing the drop from the nozzle.
Fig. 4 Schematic illustration of inkjet printing.

As an inexpensive research tool, DOD inkjet printing brings an initial set of skills and methods of exploration to facilitate the development of PE in the numerous laboratories around the world. The fascination of this technique lies in it being a noncontact patterning method: the whole process does not need to use any mask. A variety of patterned thin films can be directly deposited onto the substrate by inkjet printing, and the designed patterns could be easily changed by the computer. Other appeals of this technique are: reduced wastage of nanomaterials, cheapness, and scalability to large-area fabrication.7

Direct inkjet printing of functional nanomaterials may offer an alternative method for the low-cost manufacture of integrated circuits and micro-/nanoscale devices. For example, Finn et al. deposited Ag NWs networks with different well-defined lines and complex patterns by an inkjet printing method. The inks were made up of Ag NWs and optimized isopropyl alcoholdiethylene glycol dispersions. The printed lines exhibited excellent electrical performances with a low sheet resistance of 8 sq1 and corresponding high conductivity of 105 S m1.11 Sirringhaus and co-workers demonstrated the manufacture of whole transistor circuits by direct DOD inkjet printing. The circuits consisted of interconnections via holes based on solution-processed polymer conductors, insulators, and self-organizing semiconductors. The practical channel length was 5 m and showed a very high precision, because they used substrate surface-energy patterning to direct the flow of water-based inkjet droplets. This device exhibited a high mobility of 0.02 cm2 V1 s1 and an onoff current switching ratio of 105.12 Recently, Kim et al. fabricated ambipolar FETs by an inkjet-printing method, including one layer of SWCNTs and another amorphous zinc tin oxide (ZTO) layer. This double-layer heterostructure will enable electron transfer to occur in the ZTO layer and hole transfer to occur in the SWCNT layer. The FETs showed good mobility (>2 cm2 V1 s1) at low operating voltage (<5 V) in air.13 Recently, some state-of-the-art high-precision printers have been available for printing extremely precise thin-film structural elements, and the line width can reach the sub-micrometer scale, which gives a great impetus to the development of PE techniques.

Besides the conventional inkjet printing technique, aerosol jet printing (AJP),1416 electrohydrodynamic (EHD) jet printing1719 and dispensing printing techniques2022 are also used in PE fabrication. AJP is a new printing technology developed in recent years, which involves the formation of an ink aerosol followed by spraying the aerosol onto the surface of the substrates. The viscosity requirement for the inks is very low, and the resolution is very good due to the printed size generally being in the range of 1 to 5 m, which facilitates the printing of fine PE patterns. However, aerosol printing has a more significant drawback: that is, it cannot use printing ink with a low boiling point solvent system. Additionally, the AJP technique requires control over a number of process parameters, including the flow rate of the carrier gas that transports the aerosol mist to the substrate, the flow rate of the sheath gas that collimates the aerosol into a narrow beam, and the speed of the stage that transports the substrate beneath the beam.23 In EHD jet printing technology, the electric field will induce and produce the necessary fluid flow, and the functional nanomaterial droplets will be ejected from the micro-nozzle to the substrate, thus obtaining high-resolution (<10 m) patterns and achieving the manufacture of micro-nano-scale PE devices. The EHD jet printing technique can use 300 nm or even smaller diameter nozzles, in order to achieve as low as 240 nm ultra-high resolution printing patterns.24 Recently, Kim et al. demonstrated the materials and operating conditions that allow for high-resolution EHD jet printing of layers of quantum dots (QDs) for the fabrication of QD LEDs. The homogeneous arrays of patterns of QDs serve as the basis for corresponding arrays of QD LEDs that exhibit excellent performance.25 Clearly, AJP and EHD have been developed to elevate the resolution of printed patterns to the microscale or submicroscale to supplement these shortcomings in traditional inkjet printing. The dispensing printing technique applies a pasty material onto a substrate via capillaries. The applied material is dried or, depending on the material, sintered. Typical materials are metals, semi-conductors, polymers, ceramics and composites. For example, Shin et al. developed electrostatic-force-assisted dispensing printing to construct electrodes with a high-aspect-ratio of 0.79 on a textured surface with improved adhesion and contact resistivity.20 The main advantages of the dispensing printing technique are there are no material losses and it has higher energy efficiency.

2.3 Roll-to-roll (R2R) printing

R2R printing is a classic printing technology with a printing master. The desired lines or patterns are designed and formed onto a printing plate, which is often loaded onto a roller. And hence printed electronic patterns are generated on the surface of the substrates by the transfer of as-used printable functional inks from the printing plate by R2R printing. Generally, there are four conventional R2R printing technologies: gravure, flexographic, offset and rotary screen printing. As shown in Fig. 6, printable functional inks are transferred in the contact zone. Sufficient process-dependent contact pressure (controlled by the impression roller) is required, and this pressure can come from the printing plate roller contacting the substrate directly by controlling the impression roller, or by using an intermediate roller, as in offset printing. Then the functional inks are transferred to the substrate when they contact each other during printing. R2R patterning technologies are very suitable for high-volume and large-scale manufacture of PE devices. Therefore, R2R is an important class of substrate-based manufacturing process in which additive and subtractive processes can be used to build complex structures and patterns in a continuous manner. High throughput and low cost are the characteristics that differentiate R2R manufacturing from conventional manufacturing, which is slower and of higher cost due to the multiple steps involved, for instance, in batch processing.
Fig. 6 Schematic illustration of conventional R2R patterning technologies: (a) gravure, (b) flexographic, (c) offset and (d) rotary screen printing.

Among the R2R printing methods, the unique character of the gravure printing technique is that the desired printing patterns are engraved onto the surface of the gravure roller, which is immersed in and flooded with ink before printing (Fig. 6a). The inks are reserved in the image cells and the excess inks are removed by the squeegee. The depth of the cell is 1030 m (maximum 50 m) and the width of cell wall is 35 m. Under the action of the impression roller, the inks are transferred from the cell and printed onto the substrate because of the driving of printing pressure and internal adhesive forces, and finally form printed patterns after drying. The minimum printed spot size is about 30 m by the gravure printing method. Gravure printing technology allows good image reproduction, variable-depth and variable-area printing and can be used to print on various rigid or flexible and absorbent or non-absorbent printing substrates. The very high costs of gravure rollers dictate that gravure printing can only be used in high-volume production for PE devices. The gravure printing technique is often employed to fabricate TFTs in the laboratory. For example, Koo et al. used three different nanomaterial-based inks (Ag NPs, BaTiO3 NPs and single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWNTs)) to demonstrate that gravure printing is a suitable advanced manufacturing method for flexible TFTs. These inks were selected and optimized to enable the realization of fully-printed SWNT-based TFTs on 150 m-long rolls of 0.25 m-wide PET.26

In flexographic printing, the necessary printed patterns in the plate are raised above the non-printed zones. As shown in Fig. 6b, image areas on the soft/flexile printing plate are coated with a layer of functional inks by using an anilox roller. The depth of the cell in the anilox roller is constant, resulting in a constant thickness of inks being transferred to the soft plate roller. Then the inks are transferred to substrates by printing pressures (coming from the impression roller), finally forming printed patterns. The minimum printed spot size is about 20 m by the flexographic printing method. In PE fabrication, the patterned conductive networks within a micrometer scale on transparent conductive substrates could be prepared by flexographic printing, and their film thickness could be controlled by tailoring the anilox specifications in a large-scale industrial experiment.27

In contrast, in an offset printing technique, inks are firstly transferred onto a flexible intermediate carrier (blanket roller) from the printing master, and then transferred onto the print substrate (Fig. 6c). The distinctive features of offset printing are that the patterns are printed according to the surface and interface physics between the inks and the printing plates. However, reports on the manufacture of PE devices by offset printing are very scarce so far. As shown in Fig. 6d, rotary screen printing is different from conventional flat-to-flat screen printing, because the screen plates used are cylindrical, and the printing roller, substrate and impression roller move synchronously. The inks come from an inner ink fountain through the cylindrical printing plate and are then transferred onto the surface of the substrates.

R2R printing is considered to be a leading candidate for the manufacture of flexible and low-cost PE devices in the near future. For instance, Krebs et al. fabricated a polymer solar cell by utilizing fully R2R processes. ZnO, P3HT:[60/70]PCBM, PEDOT:PSS and Ag were deposited as 5 mm wide lines onto ITO-PET substrates by flexographic printing, slot-die coating and rotary screen printing techniques. The technical yield was 89% and the power conversion efficiency was 1.96 0.34% in the case of modules based on P3HT:[60]PCBM. The whole process was fully automated and illustrated the capacity of polymer solar cells in the setting of soft PE devices.28 To enable the optimal utilization of R2R printing in PE fabrication, the limits of overlay printing registration accuracy and maximum printing areas with respect to the other physical parameters of the different R2R systems, including as-used substrate and ink, should be characterized and discussed before manufacture.29

2.4 Requirements of printable nanomaterials

Fig. 7 compares the capacity for fine pitch resolution among various printing techniques. The selection of printing approaches becomes a major issue before launching research projects or before building up production lines. On the lab scale, new superfine ink-jet printers are able to form features down to 1 m, but these have not yet been scaled up to production. Recently, aerosol jet printing and electrohydrodynamic inkjet printing have also received a lot of attention because their printed minimum size could be smaller than 1 m. Different printing methods need to use different rheological properties of the ink, so the viscosity ranges of the inks and the obtained thickness of the printed layer should be considering before printing.
Fig. 7 Resolution and throughput for various printing technologies.

The desired PE inks should possess excellent printability and satisfy the following requirements. Firstly, the preparation process of the inks should be both uncomplicated and high-yield. Secondly, the viscosity and surface tension of printable inks should be located within a suitable range to make them compatible with a variety of printing technologies. Thirdly, the ink should remain stable at room temperature (RT) for months without particle precipitation.30 For conductive inks, the printed patterns should possess high conductivity at RT and obtain bulk conductivity upon sintering at lower temperatures (<150 C). Insulator inks should have the best insulation properties, which is also often used to prevent metal ink oxidation in PE fabrication.

Though tremendous progress has been made in producing inorganic conductive inks by using different approaches, it is still a very critical goal in modern materials chemistry and PE research fields, and has attracted substantial interest in the last few years. To date, various materials, such as conductive polymers,3133 carbon,34,35 and metallic NPs,3640 have been synthesized and their use as functional inks has been demonstrated. These functional inks have recently been widely used in the design and manufacture of plastic, textile, and paper substrates-based PE devices, which have become a hotspot in the research into printed electronics.

3. Inorganic nanomaterials-based inks: synthesis and properties

The development of low-cost functional inks with desired printability is crucial to realizing the manufacture of PE devices and exploring various PE applications. The most crucial part of printing techniques, affecting both printing conditions and pattern resolution, is the ink and its physical properties like viscosity and surface tension. Therefore, how to develop and integrate innovative PE materials has become a core activity since a variety of functional nanomaterials are required for PE fabrication, such as high conductivity metals for electrode and interconnections, high capacitance/low leakage gate dielectrics for supercapacitors and TFTs, and high-mobility n-type and p-type semiconductors for complementary, low-power logic circuits and solar cells. Among the aforementioned inorganic materials, the predominant printable inorganic nanomaterials include metallic NPs, carbon nanomaterials, semiconductor nanomaterials and reactive inks.

In addition, printing ink is a complex mixture of ingredients that are combined in a specific formulation to meet desired characteristics of the printing application of the ink. Generally, the function of inorganic nanomaterials is to provide the electronic properties for PE devices. Resins, oils or carriers, additives and solvents are also added to formulate the inks, in which the resin is often used as a binder to affix the nanomaterials to the print substrates, the oil or carrier is the medium for transferring the functional nanomaterials and resin through the press, and the additives and solvents are used to control the rheological properties of the ink. In terms of ink formulation, the choice of solvents is extremely important and there is currently a push towards the use of water as it is a non-toxic, cheap and an environmentally friendly solvent.41 However, the development of suitable water-based inks for printing on plastic substrates has met certain challenges, because the surface energy of typical plastic substrates is much lower than that of water, resulting in poor wetting of these materials by water-based inks. Much effort has been addressed to the optimization of various ink parameters and has facilitated the use of a large number of functional inorganic nanomaterials in printed electronics.

3.1 Metallic nanoparticles

Various metallic NPs-based inks have been developed and used in printed electronics, including Au,4244 Ag,45,46 Pd,47 Cu,36,48,49 Sn,38 Ni50 NPs and their alloys or composite NPs.51,52 An important feature in the determination of which metallic NPs to utilize is the ease of achieving a printable functional ink, because metallic NPs show a reduced melting temperature compared to the bulk material. For fabricating metallic NPs-based functional inks, the first task is to prepare the metal NPs at a large scale, which is also required to suit the demands for concrete performances of the PE devices.53

Top-down (TD) and bottom up (BU) routes are employed in the preparation of metal NPs. As shown in Fig. 8, in TD routes, the bulk metals are broken down to the nanoscale and dispersed into a proper medium, such as ball milling and laser ablation. It is hard to obtain high-quality NPs with a uniform size, and the product prices are very high because of their high energy consumption, as well as the need to use a lot of expensive equipment. In the BU approach, metallic ion-precursors are often reacted with reducing agents or decomposed directly in a wet-chemical medium and finally generated as NPs. Typically, the products are dispersed in the solution and form colloids or precipitates, and their various particle features such as size distributions, morphologies and particle stability should be noted. The control of these properties can be achieved by varying the experimental parameters.54 Indeed, the electronic performance of metallic NPs inks are highly dependent on the particle characteristics. For example, we investigated the effect of different size distributions of Ag NPs in conductive inks on the conductivity properties of flexible screen-printed patterns, and an optimal electrical resistivity of 3.83 cm was realized by sintering at 160 C for 75 min, and the electrical resistivity of as-prepared Ag inks is very close to that of bulk silver.55 Yang et al. studied the effect of different shapes of Ag NPs in conductive inks on the conductivity performances and microstructures of printed tracks. The results show that conductive inks filled with a mixture of nanorods and NPs were more favorable for generating random 3D interconnected conduction networks and exhibit intriguing electrical features.56

In the case of metallic NPs-based inks, stabilizing these particles to avoid agglomeration becomes a key factor because organic long-chain surfactants or polymers (insulating materials) are formed or coated onto the surface of the particles. An additional sintering treatment process is necessary to decompose the organic compounds in the ink to increase the conductivity. Indeed, the organic compounds are often strongly adsorbed onto the surface of metallic NPs, and are difficult to remove at room temperature or low temperature. In addition, many pores are formed by the decomposition pathway of organic compounds, suppressing the densification and electrical conductivity of metallic NPs. We need to continue to pay attention to the above issues and resolve them.

3.2 Transparent conducting oxide (TCO) nanoinks

Conventional TCO inks include indiumtin oxide (ITO), ZnO doped with aluminum (AZO), gallium (GZO), and indium (IZO), SnO2 doped with fluorine (FTO), CdO, Ga2O3, and In2O3 inks. Among these, ITO is mainly used to make conductive inks (ca. 104 1 cm1, typical sheet resistance is 10 sq1) and transparent (>80% in the visible range) coatings for electronic display, and heat-reflecting coatings for architectural, automotive, and light bulb glass, which is the primary direction for the application of transparent electrode materials today. An ITO transparent electrode is often fabricated by depositing polycrystalline ITO thin films via sputtering techniques. Nowadays people look to technology as an alternative method to reduce the cost, improve material utilization and elevate the compatibility with various flexible substrates. Solution-based ITO deposition methods, especially printing techniques have become a strong candidate and hold all expectations, and display the potential to enable the practical fabrication of low-cost PE devices.66,67 Besides ITO, the other TCO materials greatly reduce the usage of indium because indium is a rare metal and hence is highly expensive and will become exhausted. Therefore, it is expected that these TCO nanomaterial-based inks will be the next-generation electrode materials to replace both those prepared by sputtering technologies and those consisting of expensive rare metals for PE fabrication.

A large number of methods are used to synthesize TCO NPs, but the most convenient way is to decompose the metal complexes in a wet-chemical reaction. For example, as shown in Fig. 9, Zeng et al. developed a universal one-pot strategy to synthesize many kinds of TCO nanocrystal inks by simply heating a mixture of metalorganic precursors. Ester elimination and condensation reactions contributed to the formation of TCO nanocrystals in the reaction process. The results demonstrated that this route could be used to synthesize various TCO nanocrystal inks with yields as high as 10 g. Additionally, a sheet resistance of 110 sq1 could be realized with a transmittance of 88%.68 Kashiwagi et al. synthesized ITO NPs with an average size of 25 nm by the controlled thermal decomposition of a mixture of indium precursors and tin precursors. Then, the ITO NPs-based inks were printed on the GaN:Eu LED substrates by a screen printing technique, after annealing at 850 C for 10 min in air. The resistivity of the as-obtained ITO film was 5.2 m cm.69

Currently, the printing of TCO NPs to achieve transparent and flexible electrodes has been illustrated but is of limited practical application because of its suboptimal performances. For instance, the sheet resistances are much higher than those of traditional TCO films obtained by sputtering at a similar transparency.70 To address these issues, further improvements in the synthesis, dispersion, storage and printing procedures for TCO NPs are still required.

3.3 Carbon nanomaterials

Carbon nanomaterials exhibit considerably greater stability in various chemical and physical environments, such as in acid or base media, as well as at high temperatures and pressures. For twenty years, carbon inks have been particularly attractive for printing electrochemical electrodes for sensing applications because their price is relatively low and their electrochemical performance is good (low background currents and broad potential windows).71 Such inks are often composed of graphite particles, a polymer binder and other additives (for the dispersion, printing and adhesion tasks). Subsequently, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have been employed in the fabrication of printed devices because of their unique electrical properties and their potential for a variety of applications.7274 Recently, promising commercial developments of CNT electronics have included low-cost printing of TFTs,75 as well as RFID tags.76 Recent commercial development effort has resulted in SWCNT films with 90% transparency and a sheet resistivity of 100 sq1.77 Inkjet printing and screen printing are often used to print the graphite and CNTs. For example, Kords et al. reported that an aqueous dispersion of the functionalized CNTs was inkjet printed onto the surface of paper and plastic substrates, and electrically conductive patterns could be fabricated.78 Baechler et al. manufactured MWCNT electrodes to fabricate dielectric elastomer actuators by an inkjet printing method. After optimization of the printing parameters, as-obtained thin film electrodes exhibited conductivities larger than 30 S cm1 without the need for any sintering process.79

Graphene, a new type of 2D carbon material, with a honeycomb structure one-atom-thick, has attracted increasing attention in both fundamental and experimental scientific research because of its remarkable physical and chemical properties. Graphene could be stacked into 3D graphite, rolled into 1D nanotubes, or wrapped into 0D fullerenes.80 Various studies demonstrated that flexible and transparent electronic devices with extremely high mobilities could be manufactured from a high-quality graphene sheet.81 Yang and Wang reviewed the progress of graphene and graphene hybrid inks, with a particular focus on their formulation and properties.82 Secor et al. reported inkjet-printed graphene inks and achieved conductive features with a low resistivity of 4 m cm after sintering at 250 C for 30 min. The samples show uniform morphology, compatibility with flexible substrates, and excellent tolerance to bending stresses.83 As shown in Fig. 10, Huang et al. printed patterns on different flexible substrates (including paper, PET and polyimide (PI)) by using single-layered graphene oxide (GO) and few-layered GO aqueous solutions as inks. The printed patterns displayed a higher image quality, and were obtained by a facile and inexpensive inkjet printing method. Among the three substrates, the printed patterns on PI substrates exhibited a high electrical conductivity (874 S m1) after thermal reduction.34

Recently, more and more literature has been focused on printed graphene electronics.8486 For example, Torrisi et al. prepared graphene-based ink by liquid phase exfoliation of graphite in N-methylpyrrolidone, and used it to inkjet print TFTs, with mobilities up to 95 cm2 V1 s1, as well as transparent and conductive patterns, with 80% transmittance and 30 k sq1 sheet resistance.10 The features of graphene or its derivatives play key roles for inks because they determine the electrical and mechanical performances for printed patterns. Although high-quality graphene could be synthesized by liquid phase exfoliation without oxidation, pristine graphene still shows poor solubility in conventional solvents, which is the major obstacle to manufacturing graphene-based PE devices.

However, the conductivity of the as-obtained patterns printed from pure carbon nanomaterials is still very low and constrains its application. In order to realize high-conductivity applications, carbon/metal hybrid inks may be a better solution. For example, we prepared carboncore/Agshell (C@Ag) NPs-based conductive inks and when screen printed as conductive tracks, the conductivity is similar to the conductivity of commercial silver paste.87 Zhang et al. prepared an Ag/RGO composite as an effective conductive inkjet printing ink and used it for the fabrication of flexible PE devices. The size and morphology of Ag NPs anchored on RGO could be easily tailored, and the conductivity was improved from 800 to 2000 S m1 by the attached Ag NPs.63 However, the conductive mechanism of carbon/metal hybrid inks should be studied and further understood in the future.

3.4 Semiconductor nanomaterials

For fabricating semiconductor thin films, most preparation techniques use a sputtering process. Solution-processing methods have also been employed to manufacture semiconductor devices, but have suffered from poor device performance or the requirement to use high sintering temperatures.88 However, the required temperatures are often too high to be compatible with flexible plastic substrates. Recently, interest in inorganic semiconducting nanomaterials has grown constantly because of their huge potential applications in large-area and inexpensive electronic devices. Because of the growing demand for highly integrated functional structures in electronic applications, PE technology has drawn attention in nanomaterial sciences.

Many semiconducting inorganic oxide nanomaterials are employed to fabricate PE devices, including ZnO, TiO2, MoS2, perovskite, and kesterite.8991 For example, Schneider et al. investigated a series of zinc complexes with oximate ligands for their suitability as precursors for inkjet printing ZnO, and showed that ZnO microstructures could be used in PE device fabrication.92 As shown in Fig. 11, we prepared ZnO NPs-based inks and printed them as patterns by a screen printing method, and then a patterned ZnO nanowire array (NWA) could be fabricated by a facile hydrothermal method. The as-obtained NWA showed uniform morphology and exhibited hydrophilic and lipophilic properties, and the patterns of NWA could be easily controlled by the screen printing plate.93 Recently, Mei et al. fabricated a hole-conductor-free, all-printed solar cell with high stability by printing a double layer of mesoporous TiO2 and ZrO2 as a scaffold infiltrated with perovskite. The cell achieved a certified power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 12.8%, exhibited excellent stability and could remain stable for >1000 h in ambient air under full sunlight.94

Several semiconducting 2D nanomaterials show a transition from an indirect band-gap in the bulk to a direct gap in the monolayer. For example, in MoS2 the bulk indirect bandgap of 1.3 eV increases to a direct bandgap of 1.8 eV in the single layer, promising interesting new FET and optoelectronic devices.95 Recently, many 2D semiconductors, especially the transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) have also been used and printed onto different substrates for the fabrication of PE devices, such as MoS,96 MoSe2,97 and Bi2Te3.98 Indeed, this liquid exfoliation of layered materials is often used to produce inks for inkjet printing and to fabricate PE devices.99101 For instance, Li et al. demonstrated a reliable and efficient method for inkjet printing of multilayer MoS2 nanosheets (>6 layers, 57 nm thick). Conformal and uniform patterns could be printed with a resolution of about 80 m. This method facilitates the integration of printed MoS2 patterns with other components, and hence allows the fabrication of various functional PE devices.102 The printing and patterning of high-quality 2D TMDs nanocrystals with unique surface structures and various fascinating electronic properties offers great potential for batch fabrication and integration of electronic and optoelectronic devices. However, it remains challenging, requiring accurate control of the thickness, position, orientation and layout.

3.5 Reactive inks (nanomaterials-free inks)

Recently, reactive inks or reactive printing methods have become a strong candidate for manufacturing PE devices, especially in the fabrication of conductive metal tracks or electrodes, where reactive inks have also become a powerful alternative to NPs-based inks. The primary concept of reactive inkjet printing (RIP) is using an inkjet printer to combine one droplet of reactant with a second reactant. As shown in Fig. 12, the RIP method contains two printing processes: firstly the metal precursors will be printed, and then the reducing reactants will be deposited at the same place (or vice versa) to obtain the elemental metal directly on the surface of the substrates.103 For example, Li et al. reported that conductive Cu tracks were directly written on paper through an RIP approach, using copper as the precursor with aqueous solutions of copper citrate and NaBH4, respectively, as reducing reactants. The copper salt was printed first and the reducing chemical was subsequently printed from a multi-color print head. The estimated conductivity obtained on paper (1.8 106 S m1) is about 1/30 that of bulk metal Cu (59.6 106 S m1 at room temperature). The same approach has also been applied to the printing of Ni where oxidation is less marked.104 Walker and Lewis prepared reactive Ag inks for printing highly conductive patterns (>104 S cm1) at room temperature. These inks are highly transparent, do not contain any nanoparticles, are very stable and can be applied to a variety of patterning techniques. It is noteworthy that these reactive Ag inks are also suitable for ultra-fine nozzles with a nozzle diameter of 100 nm. Upon sintering at 90 C, the printed electrodes exhibited an electrical conductivity equivalent to that of bulk Ag.30
Fig. 12 A schematic illustration of the in situ synthesis of self-assembled Au NPs through the RIP method (reproduced with permission.103 Copyright 2014, WILEY-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim).

The advantage of using RIP is that the products can also be patterned into the desired device geometries using the same process that was used to deposit the reactants, which makes the method very effective.105 These inks are also called metalorganic-decomposition (MOD) inks, and are very suitable for inkjet printing.106110 In general, MOD inks are aqueous solutions, which reduce nozzle clogging, and do not require colloidal stabilizers. In contrast, NPs-based inks usually have a higher content of particle loading, are more widely available commercially and have been researched widely because of their prominent feature of higher conductivity.111 However, the printed tracks of MOD inks are electrically insulated until sintered above 150 C to decompose metal slats into conductive elemental metal. The high sintering temperatures limit the selection of substrates because most flexible polymer substrates are thermoplastic materials with a low softening point (Tg), and excessive temperatures will cause the substrate to deform or become damaged.112 The Tg of commonly used polymeric substrates, like PET or polycarbonate (PC), is below 150 C. Therefore, many researchers are looking for new methods to reduce the sintering temperature for MOD inks. As an example, Shin et al. reported the combination of two different approaches. Combining the advantages of both Ag NPs inks and MOD inks, they suggested that the next generation of conductive inks for a R2R printed transparent conductive film should contain a silver salt as both a metallic additive and a dispersant. Its sheet resistance after being sintered at 150 C is as low as 1 sq1 with a line width of 20 m and optical transmittance of 85% and above.113

4. Post-printing treatment

Recently, the manufacturing of electrodes or conductive patterns on heat-sensitive flexible substrates such as paper and polymers has attracted wide attention as a pathway to realize flexible and low-cost PE devices. In general, the metal NPs are dispersed in an aqueous solution or an organic solvent to prepare the conductive inks, and surfactants or polymer resins are added to tailor the printability of the ink, and finally conductive patterns are manufactured by a printing technique.114116 The printed and dried resulting patterns are composed of conducting metallic NPs capped with organic stabilizers that act as an insulator. The presence of insulating organic components between the particles results in a drastic reduction in the number of percolation paths in the printed patterns. But this also results in a very high resistivity of printed tracks that cannot be put into practical application. As shown in Fig. 13, this obstacle is conventionally overcome by a typical sintering process, which is achieved by heating the printed patterns to high temperatures. The sintering treatment induces conductive neck formation between adjacent NPs in printed tracks. Organic inks typically show high resistivity, whereas metallic NPs-based inks often require a high sintering temperature (>150 C) to remove the stabilizing agent and other polymeric additives that inhibit electrical conductivity. Therefore, understanding the correlation between sintering temperatures, sintering process times, electrical properties of printed patterns/devices and the morphology of nanomaterials is very important. The main sintering process methods include conventional thermal sintering, photonic sintering, laser sintering, plasma sintering, microwave sintering and electrical sintering techniques.

4.1 Traditional thermal treatment

Thermal treatment is the conventional method for metallic NPs sintering. Thermal sintering depends greatly on the diameter of metallic NPs, the volatilization or decomposition temperatures of the organic additives to the ink, dispersant, binder and the boiling of the solvent itself. Extending the thermal sintering time is beneficial to improving the conductivity of the printed tacks. For instance, as shown in Fig. 14, the electrical resistivity of printed Ag tracks gradually reduced with the extension of sintering time (the oven sintering temperature is 160 C), and many necks between the adjacent Ag NPs could be found and gradually formed a conductive network.117
Fig. 14 Electrical resistivity of printed Ag patterns after sintering at 160 C with different times (a); representative SEM images of Ag tracks by sintering in an oven in 30 (b), 60 (c), and 90 min (d) (reproduced with permission.117 Copyright 2015, Royal Society of Chemistry).

Sintering in an oven is typically a long process. It is essential to avoid damaging the underlying substrates when thermal-sensitive flexible materials are printed, and hence the development of low-temperature thermal sintering technology is particularly important in the PE field. In recent years, researchers have demonstrated various approaches to reduce the sintering temperatures or times for metallic particle inks by tailoring the organic content in the ink. For instance, Magdassi et al. reported a novel method to realize the coalescence and sintering of metallic NPs at room temperature. It was found that the Ag NPs undergo a spontaneous coalescence process even under unheated conditions, which is similar to the case where electrolytes with opposite charges were in contact with each other. The resulting high conductivity, having a built-in sintering mechanism, which was triggered during sintering of the printed patterns, enabled the manufacturing of various PE devices, as illustrated by the inkjet printing of a flexible electroluminescent device.118,119 Choa et al. systematically investigated and compared the microstructures, electrical properties and bending strength of conductive printed patterns obtained from two different nanomaterials: traditional NPs and nanosized platelets. The stack of nanoplatelets was very neat on the substrate after noncontact direct printing, which avoids the formation of voids during the sintering process, resulting in excellent low-resistivity while interfering with external repetitive bending stresses. Meanwhile, its conductive performance was better than that of devices fabricated with traditional NPs.120

4.2 Photonic sintering

Photonic sintering is a kind of low-temperature, selective and non-contact sintering technology, which can sinter nanomaterial inks on the various substrates, realizing the function and obtaining the physical properties of PE devices. Photonic sintering exhibits internal damping properties because the densification process will gradually reduce the subsequent photonic heat until it is completely sintered.121 Photonic sintering technology includes laser sintering, infrared (IR) sintering and pulsed/flashing light sintering.

Generally, selective laser sintering utilizes a laser as the power source to sinter the metallic NPs-based inks by aiming the scanning laser beam automatically at points in space (controlled by a computer system), and finally binding the nanomaterials together to form solid structures with full density. As shown in Fig. 15a, the energy of the focused laser beam is absorbed by the ink and generates instantaneous heat around it, and the organic components in the inks will decompose or evaporate, resulting in rapid sintering. This sintering technique can be applied to sinter fine PE patterns or a device by moving the laser beam to the target area via scanning systems, achieving the desired electrical conductivity and functionality.122 Local thermal control of the laser sintering process could minimize the heat-affected zones and thermal damage to the underlying substrates, which is beneficial for maintaining the resolution of printed patterns. For example, Hong et al. reported a facile method to prepare metallic grid transparent conductors (MGTC) on a plastic substrate by using selective laser sintering of metallic NPs inks. The MGTC exhibited high transmittance (>85%) and low sheet resistance (30 sq1), which could be used to fabricate on rigid glass and flexible polymer substrates at a large scale under ambient conditions.123 In the laser sintering process, if the organics decomposition and evaporation rate is too fast, this will result in sintered metal line geometry and the microstructure will deteriorate. Delamination of the layer is observed, which results from excessive heating and evaporation of the organics primarily from the outermost ink layer, forming a compact metal barrier when the sintering rate is beyond the optimal rate.124 In fact, various metallic nanoparticle inks are sintered by lasers of different wavelengths, such as Au NPs by an 514.5 nm Ar ion laser,125 Cu NPs by an 808 nm diode laser,126 and Ag inks by a 532 nm laser.127


Fig. 15 (a) Scheme of laser sintering principle: a focused beam locally heats the printed NPs layer; (b) schematic view of all-photonic drying and sintering process of Cu NPs ink and the morphology change in Cu NPs in ink during the photonic drying and sintering process (reproduced with permission.131 Copyright 2016, Nature Publishing Group); (c) scheme of the experimental setup of R2R IR drying and sintering of inkjet-printed Ag layers on flexible polymer substrates (reproduced with permission.134 Copyright 2015, Royal Society of Chemistry).

In contrast, the wavelength span of an intense pulsed light (IPL) from 200 to 2000 nm can achieve large-area, high-speed sintering under ambient conditions, without any limitation in terms of the type of substrates used. Very brief irradiation on a sample patterned with ink can produce instantaneous sintering under room temperature conditions.128 A pulsed/flashing light sintering method is often used to cure Cu nanoparticle inks, because this sintering technique can instantly remove the oxide shells of Cu NPs and generate a pure conductive Cu film under ambient conditions in a few milliseconds without damaging the underlying substrates.129,130 Additionally, the flashing of a xenon lamp could be utilized to sinter a large area of Cu ink-based printed patterns. As shown in Fig. 15b, Hwang et al. demonstrated an ultra-high-speed photonic sintering approach involving flashing white light combined with near infrared (NIR) and deep UV light irradiation to generate highly conductive Cu tracks, with a resistivity of 7.62 cm, which was 4.5 times higher than that of bulk Cu film (1.68 cm).131

In laser and IPL sintering processes, the layer of printed nanomaterials is dried by absorbing the emitted photonic energy. The areas covered by the two sintering techniques are not the same: the laser technology can only sinter a square millimeter of the region, depending on coherent light emission with high energy density at a fixed wavelength, while IPL technology can achieve a square centimeter range of sintering, more suitable for fast sintering of a large-area pattern, because a wide wavelength spectrum is emitted from the scalable xenon flash lamp used in IPL technology.132 For sintering large patterns, the laser technology is realized by scanning the small laser spot over the printed area to be treated.

Additionally, IR sintering is a conventional and low-cost method to obtain highly conductive printed patterns.133 It was recognized that most of the sintering processes limit the feasibility of R2R manufacturing because of the long process times required to achieve conductivity, with damage to the underlying substrates. These low-cost R2R compatible sintering techniques are especially suitable on flexible paper, because these printing substrates possess highly diffuse reflectance, relatively high thermal stability and low thermal conductivity features. As shown in Fig. 15c, Sowade et al. reported an inkjet-printed Ag NPs ink dried by R2R IR sintering within 1 second.134 However, the drawback of the IR sintering technique is that the underlying plastic substrates are easily damaged because their polymers also have high absorbance in the IR region of the electromagnetic spectrum. Recent studies have illustrated that the NIR radiation technique could drastically reduce sintering times, achieving conduction of 0.03 sq1 in under 2 s compared to conventional hot-air oven (60 min) and IR sintering techniques (84 s).135 Moreover, NIR has been used to enable the sintering of TiO2 films on FTO glass in 12.5 s.136

4.3 Plasma sintering, microwave sintering and electrical sintering

Besides photonic sintering, plasmas exhibited good performance in the densification of particulate nanomaterials, which also show better performance in the etching and sintering of thermosensitive materials. The interaction of high-energy plasma species with ceramics was also reported to enhance grain boundary diffusion, making it suitable for the sintering of metallic NPs-based inks.137 Joule heating was found to play a major role in the densification of nanoparticle compacts, which leads to the achievement of near theoretical density at lower sintering temperatures compared to common sintering methods.138 Argon plasma sintering (APS) is one selective sintering method that uses plasma inherent excited species to remove the stabilizing agent from printed NPs. For low-pressure APS, the printed structures are forwarded to a vacuum plasma chamber that is purged with Ar. Recently, a rapid low-pressure APS treatment of inkjet-printed Ag NPs was reported, resulting in a conductivity of 11.4% of bulk Ag within 1 min of plasma exposure and a final conductivity up to 40% of bulk Ag for longer sintering times.139 Wnscher et al. developed atmospheric pressure APS of Ag NPs inks to improve the plasma sintering process in terms of sintering times, substrate friendliness and technical complexity.140,141 Kim et al. compared the features of printed Cu patterns sintered by atmospheric-pressure plasma (APP) to those sintered by conventional radiationconductionconvection heating. And the results illustrated that Cu nanopaste and APP sintering are highly adoptable for mass production of flexible PE devices at low cost (Fig. 16a). The printed Cu patterns sintered at 250 W for 40 min exhibited the best conductivity of 21.06 cm, which is around 12.61 times that of bulk Cu.142

(b) Schematic demonstration of CNT-mediated microwave sintering, which enabled the formation of high-conductivity Ag patterns within a short sintering time by rapid and selective microwave heating of CNTs (reproduced with permission.146 Copyright 2016, Royal Society of Chemistry). The utilization of microwaves to treat absorbing materials was more intensively studied in the 1970s and 1980s,143 and has now been applied to sintering printed patterns. In the microwave sintering process, functional inks are printed with microwave coupling and the absorption of electromagnetic energy, which will be converted into heat, removing the stabilizing agent. This is different from conventional sintering techniques where heat is transferred between objects by the mechanisms of conduction, radiation and convection.144 This technology shortens the necessary sintering times dramatically and is independent of the substrate used. Compared to thermal annealing, microwave-plasma irradiation greatly facilitates sintering and the interconnecting of particles to form dense and uniform films.145 As shown in Fig. 16b, Jung et al. developed a metal-on-CNT method to achieve effective sintering for printed Ag patterns. Ag inks could sinter rapidly (<1 s) via heat transfer from CNTs by selective microwave heating of CNTs, and this rapid curing process cannot damage the underlying heat-sensitive flexible substrates.146 Paul et al. studied the influence of microwave sintering on the dielectric properties of screen-printed Ba0.6Sr0.4TiO3 thick films, and the results demonstrated microwave sintering preferentially heats up parts of the film where an increased defect density exists and therefore causes a selective heating process.147 Until now, microwave sintering of metallic NP inks has only generally been carried out in the laboratory. In a smaller working window, the microwave curing technology can achieve rapid curing (seconds) of printed conductive inks, but the biggest problem is the difficulty in ensuring the uniformity of sintering, which is extremely difficult to control.148

Electrical sintering is another fast sintering technique in which a voltage is applied across the printed region. This causes current flows through the printed NPs layers, resulting in local heating by dissipation, as shown in Fig. 16c.149 Therefore, electrical sintering is a Joule heating sintering method.150 A heat-equation solution is also derived for a generic geometry of a printed conductor.151 Hummelgrd et al. found the onset of Joule heating induced sintering and the coalescence of NPs at power levels of 0.110 mW m3 by in situ TEM probing.152 Jang et al. found that when the initial resistance (Ri) is too high, electrical sintering will not be achieved. All the printed tracks were burned and blown out when the input voltage was >40 V. When the Ri is lower than 100 k and the input voltage is in the range of 20 V to 40 V, electrical sintering exhibited good performance and could not damage the underlying paper substrate.153 Electrical sintering is a rapid sintering method for the fabrication of electronic devices: for example, all-printed low-voltage programmable resistive write-onceread-many (WORM) memories on a plastic substrate were prepared by electrical sintering of Ag NPs.154

Indeed, each sintering method has its own intrinsic shortcomings and advantages. Recently, a combination of a variety of sintering methods has become an attractive new direction for post-printing treatment in PE manufacturing. For example, Perelaer et al. combined the photonic and microwave flash exposure techniques to sinter Ag tracks, resulting in demonstrably improved conductivity, while the initial sintering time was quartered. The inkjet-printed Ag NPs were sintered from their nonconductive status to 28% of the bulk Ag conductivity in <15 s without damaging the underlying plastic substrate. This method leads to conductive tracks on flexible substrates in short times that are compatible with R2R production.155 Subsequently, a combination of plasma and microwave flash sintering was employed to sinter an inkjet-printed and tailored Ag NPs formulation. After sintering by two sintering technologies sequentially, a final conductivity of 60% of bulk Ag could be obtained without damaging the underlying polymer substrate.156 Park et al. presented fast sintering for Ag NPs and flake layers printed using R2R gravure printing. An IR sintering module and hot-air dryer zone was applied to an R2R system to shorten the sintering duration of a gravure-printed Ag layer. The fastest sintering of 0.34 sq1 was achieved with a 50 mm distance, 1000 W IR lamp power, and 1.08 s sintering time.157 Now that sintering techniques are often focused on the fast sintering of various functional PE inks with the aim of enabling R2R processing, both low sintering temperatures and short sintering times are required to achieve R2R production. Since every sintering method follows a different mechanism, it is difficult to appoint one technique which is suitable for any inks, substrates and applications, so we can take full advantage of hybrid sintering methods to realize the optimal post-printing treatment and obtain the best performance in PE application.

5. Various applications

Currently, non-patterning coating techniques such as spin-coating technology are often used in the laboratory to manufacture electronic devices. Since the development of complex electronic systems requires the integration of different electronic devices together, it becomes mandatory to have accurate and scalable control of their active layers by utilizing patterning technology. This paradigm change is non-trivial because of the close and subtle relations between deposition protocols, active layer microstructures and final device performances. High-throughput printing technology enables cost-effective manufacturing of different devices into large-area electronic systems and that has been employed in the past to fabricate various electronic devices, including conductive tracks/electrodes and flexible circuits,158 high-performance FETs,159162 memory devices,163 advanced sensors,164166 and lightweight energy devices such as supercapacitors, batteries, solar cells and nanogenerators.167169

5.1 Conductive tracks and patterns

Printed tracks/patterns with low conductivity are one of the challenges for PE application because they exist in almost every electrical device. To improve the conductivities of printed tracks/patterns, material scientists and engineers have dedicated their efforts to improving nanostructures of conductive material, ink formulations, and printing procedures.170

To date, the conductivity of printed Ag tracks is close to that of bulk Ag under well-controlled conditions. Compared with Ag tracks, most recent studies have focused on and attempted to achieve high electrical conductivity and reliability of conductive Cu tracks at a low sintering temperature. For example, Kwon et al. reported plasma-sintered Cu patterns with a fully densified microstructure with a resistivity of 3.23 W cm, and a Cu conductive track with dense microstructure which retains its electrical resistivity after 1 month.171 Recently, Yim et al. developed a simple method to prepare hybrid Cu/Ag conductive tracks under flashing light sintering. When exposed to an ambient atmosphere at a temperature of 220 C, the resistance changed to 106 sq1 for copper-only ink and 50 sq1 for copper and Ag hybrid inks.172 These metallic NPs are often printed as conductive tracks and patterns directly on the substrates.

Besides the direct printing, the chemical reaction process is also employed to fabricate conductive tracks. For instance, Chen et al. demonstrated Ag reactive inks for fabricating conductive tracks on flexible substrates by inkjet printing. The as-obtained reactive inks could be sintered at low temperatures, because diethanolamine in the inks would decompose at temperatures >50 C and generate formaldehyde, which reacts spontaneously with silver ammonia ions to form elemental Ag. The electrical conductivity of the printed tracks could reach 26% of that of the bulk Ag after sintering at 75 C for 20 min. Additionally, the Ag inks could be tightly adhered to the underlying flexible plastic substrates.173 Liu et al. demonstrated the fabrication of metal Cu fine circuits by a galvanic replacement deposition method. A Zn NPs filled epoxy resin paste was first printed onto the substrates as the seed layer. Subsequently, an electrically conductive Cu layer could be deposited by electroplating because of the galvanic replacement reaction between Zn and Cu2+.174

Flexible circuits are used in a wide range of applications from computers to vehicles, but they perform the same fundamental function: the transportation of electrical current in three-dimensional paths. Current fabrication techniques have gone a long way to meeting the price-based driver of the market's existing commercial success, but a new and more scalable fabrication process is still needed that could both cut costs and improve the performances and functionalities for end users through its integration into their devices. This is an important research direction for accelerating the applications of PE. Zheng et al. developed a highly cost-effective and entirely automatic printing method for making personal electronics, through the introduction of a tapping-mode composite fluid delivery system for printing liquid metal ink. As shown in Fig. 17, a series of representative electrical conductive patterns spanning simple tracks to desired complex configurations could be printed.175 This easily controllable and personally affordable system opens the way for large-scale applications of PE devices and offers a powerful tool for electronics manufacture in coming daily life.

5.2 Printed TFTs

TFTs are special structure of FETs made by depositing thin films of active semiconductor layers as well as the dielectric layers and metallic contacts over a supporting (but non-conducting) substrate. As shown in Fig. 18, this layer structure of TFT is beneficial for fabrication by printing methods. Metal oxide TFTs are intrinsically superior to organic ones in terms of mobility and stability, and relevant studies based on printing methods have grown continuously.
Fig. 18 Schematic illustration of the printing process for TFT by a four-colour press.

One approach to printing TFTs uses nanosized inorganic components as inks and prints the partial structure of TFTs. For example, top-gate TFTs were made of sorted semiconducting SWCNTs inks as channel materials and printed Ag tracks as top electrodes and interconnections. The hole mobility, Ion/off ratio and subthreshold swing of the as-obtained TFTs were 46.2 cm2 V1 s1, 105 and 109 mV per decade, respectively.176 In general, metal NPs-based inks are often used to fabricate the contact electrodes in organic TFTs (OTFTs) by an inkjet printing method. For instance, Fukuda et al. studied the mechanical durability of OTFTs with printed Ag contacts, and the results demonstrated that as-prepared Ag NPs-based inks could be tightly adhered to the underlying organic layers, and the prepared OTFTs displayed good reproducibility in bending cycle tests.177

Another approach to fabricating TFTs uses a full printing process, and all-printed TFTs are a key component of ubiquitous flexible electronic devices.178181 For example, extreme bendability is observed in fully-printed TFTs by an inverse gravure printing technique, because the TFTs exhibited good electrical performances without measurable change when the curvature is down to a small radius (1 mm).182 Homenick et al. fabricated fully-printed TFTs based on poly(9,9-di-n-dodecylfluorene) (PFDD) wrapped semiconducting SWCNT channels by a practical route that combines R2R gravure and inkjet printing, with R2R printed gate electrodes and dielectric and inkjet-printed channels and source/drain electrodes. Optimum conditions were obtained on Si/SiO2 in which an ink consisting of 6:1 ratio of PFDD:SWCNT with 50 mg L1 SWCNT concentration printed at a drop spacing of 20 m resulted in TFTs with mobilities of 25 cm2 V1 s1 and on-/off-current ratios >105.183 Obviously, high-throughput printing techniques could serve as a promising manufacturing solution for a wide range of large-area TFTs electronic applications.

5.3 Printed solar cells

A solar cell, or photovoltaic cell, is an electrical device that converts the energy of light directly into electricity by the photovoltaic effect, which is a physical and chemical phenomenon. A common flexible substrate (such as paper or PET) costs approximately a thousandth that of glass, and solar cells using printing processes can be much cheaper and lighter than conventional solar panels. Therefore, flexible printed solar cells make for a variety of possibilities in terms of how they can be set up: throw it on a tent, hang it from a tree, from a backpack or just roll it out on the ground, and which also could offer an affordable solution to satisfy the requirements of increasing energy demands around the world.

Many types of printed solar cells are being developed and fabricated by inkjet printing. For example, Hashmi et al. successfully inkjet-printed an ionic liquid electrolyte in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) as a part of a new cell manufacturing sequence that eliminates drilling holes in the DSSCs substrates. The as-printed DSSCs showed 6% enhancement in overall PCE compared to the reference cells.188 Lin et al. demonstrated an 11.3% efficiency boost for a printed Cu(In,Ga)(S,Se)2 thin film solar cell by a vacuum-free, and scalable route via DOD inkjet printing.189 Jung demonstrated all-inkjet-printed organic solar cells with an average PCE of 2%, and all the fabrication and measurement processes were carried out under ambient conditions. The results revealed that highly controlled conducting Ag, semiconducting ZnO films and organic layers using engineered inks for inkjet printing enable good efficiencies for printed cells.190 A large number of reports indicated that inkjet printing is an attractive digital printing technology for cost-effective, environmentally friendly integration of photovoltaic cells onto plastic substrates.

The past 3 years have brought large advances in the area of mixed organicinorganic halide perovskite materials for solar cells. It is noteworthy that organicinorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells have emerged as the most promising of the third-generation solar cells, with an increased record of efficiency that has risen to 19.3%.191 The challenge in this field is to translate the lab-scale process to a large-area production process, which will preferably include a cost-competitive printing process. A scaling effort on perovskite solar cells has been presented where the device manufacture has progressed onto flexible substrates using scalable techniques such as printing under ambient conditions.192 Recently, a fully-printed perovskite solar cell was fabricated by a large-scale R2R printing technique combined with a sequential slot-die coating process, where all layers except the electrode were printed and the cells demonstrated up to 11.96% of PCE.193 Mass production at low cost is what the solar industry sorely needs. Printed solar cells won't become widespread until they are cheaper and safer to make.194 Researchers and businesses must work together to improve the efficiency, environmental impact and stability of these printed solar cells, scale up their manufacture and plan their market penetration.

5.4 Printed supercapacitors

Supercapacitors (electrochemical double-layer capacitors) are advanced and practical because of their good power density, long cycle and shelf life, and they also exhibit rapid charging/discharging behaviors and good performance over a wide temperature range. With the boost of wearable and bendable consumer electronics that have an aesthetic appeal and unique functionality, it is required that energy storage devices show not only excellent electrochemical performances, but also flexibilities and shape versatilities.195 Under these circumstances, printed supercapacitors (PSCs) have become strong candidates and were often fabricated to be applied as a part of a hybrid power source in PE applications.

Numerous printing techniques using metal NPs inks had attracted increasing attention for manufacturing the current collectors on PSCs.196200 For instance, flexible all-solid-state PSCs with a flexible Ag NPs-based current collector were prepared by an R2R gravure printing process combined with a fast, low-temperature laser sintering treatment. The PSCs exhibited excellent capacitive properties in a number of electrochemical studies and retained their performances even under physical disturbance, such as bending over 1000 operating cycles, without any severe loss of capacitance.201 As an alternative, conductive carbon nanomaterials are also used to print current collectors or electrode active materials. For example, a low concentration of GO aqueous dispersion was utilized as an inkjet printing ink and printed onto the surface of current collectors as electrode active materials, and a repeated bending test of the PSCs for hundreds of cycles resulted in a less than 5% decrease in capacity.202 Additionally, perhaps the electrolyte and separator will be printed in PSCs. As an example, Yang et al. fabricated the separators of supercapacitors on cellulose film and paper by using an inkjet printing method. Several different inks, including CNTs and metal oxides, were studied and the influence of the base material used as a separator was also investigated.203

As an alternative, all-printed supercapacitors have also been developed and fully-printed PSCs have become an attractive research field.204 As shown in Fig. 21, all-printed, solid-state flexible PSCs on paper are fabricated by using a commercial desktop inkjet printer, which is composed of activated carbon/CNTs and an ionic liquid/ultraviolet-cured triacrylate polymer-based solid-state electrolyte. CNT-assisted photonic sintered Ag NWs are introduced onto the electrodes to further elevate the conductivity of the electrodes. The PSCs showed reliable electrochemical performance over 2000 cycles as well as excellent mechanical flexibility.205 A facile, low-cost, and textile-compatible method involving screen printing and transfer printing has been developed to construct all-solid-state supercapacitors on a single silk fabric. The PSCs exhibited a high specific capacitance of 19.23 mF cm2 at a current density of 1 mA cm2 and good cycling stability with capacitance retention of 84% after 2000 charging/discharging cycles.206

5.5 Printed batteries

Batteries are having an increasing technological impact on our society and have become an essential part of our daily lives. In the classic production processes for conventional batteries, pick-and-place techniques are typically employed, which require significant tooling costs for product change-over. Employing printing techniques for the manufacturing of lightweight batteries promises cheap mass production processes, whilst keeping the highest flexibility for product designers. In printed batteries, the most often used battery types are lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) and zinc based batteries. When printing processes are well established, they can be used to deposit battery components by designing printable inks for the active layers, current collectors, and electrolytes.

LIBs power most of our portable electronics by virtue of their high energy and power density.207 Screen printing methods are often used to fabricate the active anode/cathode in printed LIBs, and various Li ion materials (such as LiMn2O4, LiCoO2, LiTi5O12, LiFePO4, and Li3BO3) and graphite are formulated as printable inks.208210 For example, Gren et al. prepared new inks based on C-LiFePO4 and graphite to fabricate cathodes and anodes in screen-printed LIBs, respectively, and the green solvent N,N-dimethylpropyleneurea (DMPU) and poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) are used as binders. The electrodes prepared with DMPU and processed by screen-printing show a capacity of 52 mA h g1 at 2C for the cathode and 349 mA h g1 at C/5 for the anode, after 45 chargedischarge cycles.211 Previous demonstrations of flexible LIBs show a tradeoff between low areal capacity, poor mechanical flexibility and/or high thickness of inactive components.212 However, a reinforced electrode design and novel architecture could help to increase the areal capacity of LIBs and improve the tensile strength and mechanical flexibility of the electrodes.213215

In addition, zinc based batteries are able to offer high performance, employ aqueous electrolytes and do not have any of the flammability problems associated with LIBs. Various types of printed Zn based batteries have been developed, such as a Znair battery, a ZnMnO2 alkaline battery, a ZnAg battery, and a Znsilver oxide battery.216218 Printable electroactive zinc formulations have been commercialized, based on a combination of Zn particles and a polymer binder such as polyhydroxy ether, polyurethane and/or a copolymer of acrylonitrile/vinylidene chloride dissolved in a suitable solvent system, with the optional addition of carbon as a conducting bridging agent.219 Ink formulation is very important for obtaining high-performance printed zinc based batteries. The screen printing technique is a good method to create an efficient three-phase reaction zone in a Znair battery, which enables the printed battery to be capable of high rates of discharge by promoting the oxygen reduction reaction which has a decisive impact on its performance.220 Recently, ZnAg battery systems have been particularly attractive for printing because of the high energy, areal density and air stability that this system offers, with a specific energy of 200 Wh kg1 and an energy density of 750 Wh dm3.221 As shown in Fig. 22, Kumar et al. screen-printed a highly stretchable, ZnAg2O battery by incorporating polystyrene-block-polyisoprene-block-polystyrene (SIS) as a hyperelastic binder for custom-made printable inks. The remarkable mechanical properties of the SIS binder led to an all-printed, stretchable ZnAg2O rechargeable battery with a 2.5 mA h cm2 reversible capacity density even after multiple iterations of 100% stretching.222 Due to the dimensions and material set currently required for reliable low-temperature print processing of electronic devices, a battery potential greater than that sourced by single cells is typically needed. For example, a screen-printed flexible high-potential battery with an open circuit potential of 14 V has been achieved by interconnecting ten low-resistance ZnMnO2 batteries.223

Moreover, innovations in battery design and packaging can enable the possibility of powering electronics embedded within jewelry, tattoos, and clothing.224 For example, a typical AgZn tattoo cell reveals a capacity density in the range 1.32.1 mA h cm2 and stability up to 13 cycles.225 Furthermore, in order to avoid deterioration in the performance of flexible printed batteries, it is necessary to introduce suitable materials that are mechanically flexible and stretchable to the same extent as the individual components. To address these issues, a systematic study of the mechanical and electrochemical properties of fully-printed batteries should be one of the most important topics in future research.

5.6 Printed nanogenerators

Harvesting mechanical or vibrational energy from our surroundings to acquire a steady and high self-powered output has attracted intensive interest because of the fast development of wireless devices and portable electronics. Nanogenerators (NGs) were first developed for self-powered systems based on the piezoelectric effect and the triboelectrification effect for converting tiny amounts of mechanical energy into electricity, which have applications in IoT, environmental/infrastructural monitoring, medical science and security.226228 Traditional fabrication techniques used to realize NGs involve plasma etching, soft lithography and nanoparticle deposition for higher performance. But the lack of truly scalable fabrication processes still remains a critical challenge and bottleneck in the path of bringing NGs to commercial production. Various literatures reveal that printing techniques have potential applications for manufacturing self-powered and flexible NGs.229231

Harvesting mechanical energy by piezoelectric NGs (PENGs) has received a great amount of attention because of the diverse flexibility of its sophisticated design to directly convert mechanical energy into electricity because of the piezoelectric effect of nanomaterials, such as ZnO nanowires and zirconate titanate (PZT) nanoribbons. Chung et al. first demonstrated the fabrication of all-solution-processed flexible thin film PENGs using simple aqueous ZnO ink as a thin film. Screen printing of Ag paste was applied on the top of the NGs for a top-electrode formation.232 Indeed, the top electrodes are often fabricated by a printing method in printed PENGs because of the inherent characteristics of PENGs.233 Recently, Ali et al. reported a fully-printed piezoelectric energy harvesting device which was fabricated by using a flexographic printing process. A printed Ag layer with an average thickness of 5 m and a printed PZT layer with an average thickness of 96 m could be achieved by flexographic printing methods.234

In contrast, triboelectric NGs (TENGs) use triboelectrification and electrostatic induction to convert ambient mechanical energy into electrical energy. TENGs consist of a multi-layer structure and possess an inherent flexibility. When two contact surfaces of TENGs with opposite triboelectric charges are contacted and detached repeatedly, the potential difference drives free electrons to flow between two electrodes connected with a lead wire. And hence the friction of two adjacent layers plays a key role in the final performance of TENGs. A roll printing process could improve the energy harvesting performance of TENGs.235 For example, Hong et al. used microscopically roll-printed wrinkled electrodes to realize the enhanced performance of TENGs (137% of that of flat electrodes), which were created using a simple and rapid roll process involving Ag NPs ink and a polydimethylsiloxane roller. The fabricated wrinkled patterns were periodic with an interval of about 7 m and a height of about 80 nm.236

Recently, flexible printed triboelectricpiezoelectric hybrid NGs were also proposed and fabricated.237 It could be expected that high-volume printing techniques will have a drastic impact on flexible NGs, and we believe that the proposed flexible, lightweight, low-cost NGs will supply effective power energy sustainably for wearable devices in practical applications.

5.7 Printed sensors and actuators

A sensor is an object whose purpose is to detect events or changes in its environment and send the information to a computer which then tells an actuator to provide the corresponding output. And hence an actuator is a component of a machine that is responsible for moving or controlling a mechanism or system. Flexible sensors/actuators are an increasing field of research and development because of the growing demand for artificial skin, chemical sensors, force and deformation sensors, etc. In comparison with traditional fabrication approaches, PE techniques have attracted increasing attention for the implementation of flexible sensors and actuators because of the aforementioned intrinsic advantages of printing techniques.

Recently, innovative technologies and functional materials have been explored to develop applications of printed sensors and actuators, including gas sensors, strain sensors and temperature sensors.238243 For instance, numerous attempts have recently been made to realize a novel printed strain sensor showing high stretch ability and good sensitivity by engineering architectures of conductive nanomaterials on flexible substrates.244,245 Correia et al. demonstrated strain sensors with different structures (including a single sensor, sensor arrays and a sensor matrix) that could be fabricated by an inkjet printing technique.246 A fully-printed strain sensor array is a recent example of a low-cost, flexible and lightweight system that provides a reliable method for monitoring the structural health of aircraft.245

As shown in Fig. 23, Ali et al. proposed a differential temperature sensor (DTS) to compensate for the bending effect of printed resistance temperature detectors (RTDs), which was composed of two serially connected similar meander patterns fabricated back-to-back on a PET substrate by inkjet printing Ag NPs. The designed DTS had a total length of 75 mm and device dimensions of 7 7 mm2. The total variation in resistance was observed to be 15.5 against a temperature variation from 0 to 100 C, and the temperature coefficient of resistance was 1.076 103 C1. When the environment temperature remained unchanged, a change in resistance of the designed DTS was not obvious under mechanical deformation.247 Harada et al. fabricated all-printed multifunctional artificial electronic whisker arrays showing good sensitivity by integrating the strain and temperature sensors.248 They also demonstrated that all-printed fingerprint-like three-axis tactile force and temperature sensors could be used as artificial skin.249


Fig. 23 The structure of a printed DTS and the bending analysis of the DTS that drastically reduces the mechanical deformation effect (reproduced with permission.247 Copyright 2016, American Chemical Society).

A gas detector is a device that detects the presence of gases in an area, often as part of a safety system. Researchers are trying to develop soft, thin and low-cost gas sensors for various applications.250 As an example, an inkjet-printed, fully passive sensor capable of either humidity or gas sensing was developed, which was composed of an interdigitated electrode, a customized printable gas-sensitive ink and a specialized dipole antenna for wireless sensing. A fast response time of 3 min was achieved at room temperature for a H2S concentration of 10 ppm at a relative humidity (RH) of 45%.251 More recently, Alshammari et al. prepared all-printed, high-performance and low-cost polymer-functionalized CNT gas sensors by an inkjet printing method. Performance analysis showed a significant enhancement in the sensitivity of the sensor with polymer-wrapped nanotubes in comparison with oxidized nanotubes, with enhancement factors greater than 2.5.252

These excellent performance capabilities make printed sensors applicable for precise and quantitative functional sensing in various wearable electronic applications, such as human motion detection, personal health monitoring, virtual reality, and smart clothing systems. Mattana and Briand reviewed recent progress in printed sensors on flexible substrates, demonstrating a large set of printed sensor techniques on soft substrates that are being studied and achieved.253 However, the overall level and maturity of printed sensors or actuators are strikingly different: few of them are already commercial products, some have established production, and many others are more or less advanced lab prototypes, in the early stages of their development, or at the proof-of-concept level.

5.8 Fully-printed devices

Clearly, the fully-printed or all-printed process is attractive for manufacturing low-cost, flexible, and large-area electronics in a high-throughput manner with minimal material waste. So why not print the whole device? Nonetheless, developing scalable and low-cost methods for fabricating high-performance fully-printed devices remains a major challenge.

Various printing techniques have been employed to fabricate different fully-printed devices, such as screen-printed diodes,254 screen-printed active-matrix electrochromic displays,255 screen-printed thermoelectric generators,256 inkjet-printed capacitors,257 gravure-printed transistors,258 and electrohydrodynamic jet printing for LEDs.259 As shown in Fig. 24, Cao et al. fabricated top-gated CNT TFTs on rigid and flexible substrates by a fully screen-printed method, which presented decent performance, with mobility up to 7.67 cm2 V1 s1, an on/off ratio of 104105, minimal hysteresis, and a low operation voltage (<10 V).260 Another group reported SWCNT TFTs fabricated by utilizing the metallic and semiconducting SWCNT as an ink via an inkjet printing process. The whole manufacture process could operate under ambient conditions and no costly cleanroom facility is necessary for this fabrication. The as-obtained non-optimized SWCNT TFTs showed an Ion/off ratio of 92, mobility of 2.27 cm2 V1 s1 and transmissivity of 82%.261 Recently, a R2R gravure press with 6 printing units was successfully utilized to print a rectenna as a wireless power transmission device at 13.56 MHz. The whole fabrication process showed a low-cost feature because the printing speed could reach 8 m min1 and the device yields were larger than 90%. In order to avoid any intermixing of printed inks at the interface between two layers, NPs-based inks (including Ag, ZnO and BaTiO3) were well formulated to meet the wetting on PET and printed layers using solvents orthogonal to each other.262

In addition, two or more different printing techniques are often integrated to fabricate devices. For example, Rehman et al. illustrated the preparation of a memory device with the layer structure PET/Ag/MoS2-PVA/Ag by using a hybrid fully-printed method. The bottom Ag electrodes, active layer of hybrid MoS2-PVA nanocomposite and top Ag electrode were fabricated by reverse offset printing, electrohydrodynamic (EHD) atomization and EHD patterning, respectively.263 Lien et al. demonstrated the fabrication of a resistive random access memory on paper by utilizing a sequence of inkjet and screen-printing technologies. The memory was a simple metalinsulatormetal device structure and it has shown a potential to reach gigabyte capacities on a piece of A4 paper. The printed-paper-based memories exhibited reproducible switching endurance, reliable retention, a tunable memory window, and the capability to operate under extreme bending conditions.264 To the best of our knowledge, all-printed or fully-printed electronic devices, especially transistors and diodes, achieved on various flexible substrates, have so far not been demonstrated. Printing consistency with defect-free deposition and line resolution improvements should continue to be investigated in order to achieve reliable low-cost all-printed wide-area structural devices for practical applications.

5.9 PE for biomedical applications

The thin, flexible and lightweight features of PE devices suggest that the development and utilization of PE technology in biomedical applications has enabled this possibility, yet sometimes their actualization is not straightforward. Research in this field is growing very quickly, attributed to the success and combined efforts of the multidisciplinary biotechnology and electronics communities.

As an example, the sustainable development of disposable screen-printed electrodes (SPEs, including microelectrodes and modified electrodes) that respond to the growing need to perform rapid in situ analyses and which have become a strong candidate to replace traditional electrodes. In order to become common low-cost electrochemical biosensors, SPEs have undergone significant improvements over the past few decades with respect to both their format and their printing materials.265 And finally current SPEs possess a lot of advantages, such as low price, low sample volume requirement, high sensitivity, high and rapid volume production, portability and easy handling.266 SPEs-based disposable biosensors have led to new possibilities in the detection and quantification of various biological specimens, including biomolecules, pesticides, antigens, DNA, microorganisms and enzymes.267 As shown in Fig. 25, a stretchable and screen-printed electrochemical sensor for glucose determination in human perspiration was developed. Glucose was quantified by measuring H2O2 reduction by chronoamperometry at 0.35 V (vs. pseudo-Ag/AgCl) using glucose oxidase immobilized on Pt-decorated graphite. The sensor performed well for the quantification of glucose in the obtained working range and sensitivities were 33 M900 M and 105 A cm2 mM1, respectively, which exhibited high sensitivity and selectivity, and a low limit of detection.268 It is expected that in the near future, these SPEs will be widely commercialized, and we can use them to find solutions to health monitoring and disease control issues, especially in remote areas.

Progress in device designs, mechanical analysis, and microscale or nanoscale manufacturing technologies have accelerated the development of flexible PEs and printed optoelectronics applications in the biomedical field.269 Besides SPEs, other materials and printing methods have also been employed to fabricate PE devices for biomedical applications. In particular, when the sensing element is bonded, a patterned deposition of the adhesive cured under ambient conditions is required. For example, Hamad et al. demonstrated a preparation approach for all-printed microfluidic systems with sensing elements by using inkjet and stereolithographic 3D-printing of UV-curable adhesive and dielectric inks.272

Advances in nanomaterials, microscale mechanics, and printing techniques enable the possibility of constructing high-quality PE and optoelectronic devices, which can be easily integrated with the soft, curvilinear, and time-dynamic surface of the human body.273 For example, human skin possesses an important sensory role. It is the attachment site for touch, pain and thermoreceptors. To achieve the functions of human skin, a smart electronic skin (e-skin) is proposed and has become a focus of research in the PE field, in which a variety of functional sensor devices with distinct flexible, stretchable, and robust features are integrated and finally form large-area PE networks. Various multifunctional e-skins have been developed and have become an important testament to the progress of PE in bioapplications.274,275 However, the current fabrication method of most e-skin devices is transfer printing, not the aforementioned printing techniques.276278 As an example, a resistive graphene sensor has been printed on silk substrates, and this sensor could be transfer printed on the surface of teeth (Fig. 26). The results reveal that the Helicobacter pylori in human saliva could be detected by installing a graphene sensor on a tooth.279 With the integration of printing and biomedical disciplines, printing precision continues to improve, and the fast growth in this field is promising for the development of real, all-printed integrated e-skins in the near future.

6. Outlooks and perspectives

Although PE techniques promise to radically change our means of manufacturing electrical devices towards the direction of low-cost, high-throughput, and sustainable productions on large-area flexible or elastic substrates, there are several remaining challenges and disadvantages for the effective integration of these materials in PE devices, such as how to elevate their poor resolution and control the film thickness in the printing process, and how to improve the slow circuit speeds and high operating voltages in most PE devices.

Inks play a key role in PE fabrication. Manufacturing functional ink produced from salt precursors with tuned viscosity and surface tension at a molecular level to sit within the printability range is a target. A large number of functional nanomaterials with varying sizes, dimensions, material properties, and functions have been employed to prepare printable inks. After printing and deposition of the inks, post-treatment becomes very important because drying, curing, or sintering steps are required to obtain a functional layer or functional devices. And thus the lower temperature becomes a real key because most functional inks need to be printed on flexible substrates like paper and plastic, which would obviously deform, melt or burn at higher temperature. We now know that is possible, and how to do it. Various low-temperature sintered inks and advanced sintering techniques have been developed, and the sintering process should be both fast and cheap. Until now, however, we haven't really understood the underlying physics of what was going on in the sintering process and the corresponding sintering mechanism.

PE techniques are also desirable as they provide the promise of mechanically flexible, readily customizable, rugged, thin and lightweight devices. To fully realize these advantages, flexible circuits, stretchable sensors, soft power sources and other flexible functional devices must be developed. While some may consist of a very simple structure with only a few electrodes, others are much more complex and require the printing deposition of multiple functional layers. Most thin film solar cells, capacitors, and other energy storage devices often depend on sandwich layer structures, where two electrodes face each other and the charge flows between them. The electrodes can be easily destroyed, resulting in a short-circuit when the devices are bent or deformed under an external force. Therefore, innovative design is very important for PE devices. As an example, Li et al. had come up with a novel design for energy storage devices that is both flexible and semitransparent, in which electrodes with comb-teeth structures were prepared on the same two-dimensional plane.280 Indeed, the current collector material in printed energy devices should be highly flexible and have high conductivity and high crease resistance to ensure no ohmic loses during flexing. Furthermore, the prospect of developing materials with high power density and a long cycle life for printed energy devices is an exciting direction that has yet to be realized.281 Proper evaluation of new printable materials and their charge storage mechanisms will facilitate progress in this important field of printed electrical energy storage devices.

Additionally, printing techniques would be instrumental in high-throughput manufacturing of these novel circuits and electronic devices. However, achieving all-printed devices on flexible substrates in large areas remains an enormous challenge. PE devices will increasingly be used in wearable electronics, health monitoring and IoT. Currently, existing worldwide research activities are focused on the printing of individual devices. There is a pressing need to realize the printing of integrated electronics systems (PIES), which may include active electronic devices such as transistors, LEDs, sensors and detectors, and power sources, along with passive components such as inductors, capacitors, optical waveguides and interconnections, which have a bright future for the development of printed electronics.
