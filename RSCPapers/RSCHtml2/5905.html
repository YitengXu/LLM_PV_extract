Abstract

Over billions of years, evolutionary pressure has facilitated the development of sophisticated and diverse light-harvesting systems in photosynthetic organisms, enabling them to adapt to a variety of habitats and light conditions. Today, driven by the need for cheap and efficient solar power, we turn to photosynthetic organisms and their light-active supramolecular assemblies for bio-inspiration. By studying natural systems, we can learn more about the basic principles behind the fine-tuned functionalities of light capture and energy transfer on the molecular level. In this review, we use ideas from evolutionary ecology and quantum mechanics to elucidate the parameters that underpin the efficient and robust light-harvesting machinery of natural light-harvesting systems. The family of antenna proteins of cryptophyte algae serves as an example to illustrate the evolutionary diversification process on a structural and consequently a photophysical level. Two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy experiments reveal the existence of coherence among vibronic levels in the initial response of light-harvesting proteins to femtosecond optical excitation. We discuss what implications quantum transport processes might have for light harvesting.


Broader context

We suggest that expanding the scope of bio-inspiration has the potential to yield paradigm shifts for the design of energy-conversion systems. To explore this idea we speculate on the limits for controlling excitation energy flow in supramolecular assemblies and we ask whether examination of biological ecosystemsrather than solely isolated proteins like reaction centerssuggests strategies for beating the thermodynamic limit of solar power conversion. In a sense, photosynthetic ecosystems function like dynamic multijunction solar cells. Zooming in to the length scales on which molecules interact with each other during the transfer of excitation energy, we examine the limits for rapid energy transfer and long exciton diffusion lengths. We discuss recent experiments that reveal a potential role played by coherence, possibly quantum mechanical in nature, in light harvesting. While coherence effects may not profoundly change energy migration efficiencies in large light harvesting antenna systems, they likely influence the underlying distribution of energy trapping times. Understanding and controlling these distributions may provide opportunities for quantum engineering in chemistry and biology.

Introduction

Plants turn sunlight into energy using abundant ingredients like water and carbon dioxide.13 This living solar cell is the energy hub for life on Earth. Despite such global impact, these are not highly efficient solar cells4,5but photosynthetic systems exhibit such diversity and agility in adapting to ambient conditions that they serve as inspiration.6 Identifying and examining some of these inspirations in the context of solar energy conversion using organic materials are the goals of this article. This paper has two main points. First, we suggest that expanding the scope of bio-inspiration has the potential to yield paradigm shifts for the design of energy-conversion systems. To explore this idea we speculate on the limits for controlling excitation energy flow in supramolecular assemblies and we ask whether examination of biological ecosystemsrather than solely isolated proteins like reaction centerssuggests strategies for beating the thermodynamic limit of solar power conversion. In parallel, we investigate the impact of evolutionary processes on the diversification of light-harvesting machinery, where changes to the composition and architecture of photosynthetic proteins can dramatically alter the mechanism of light capture. Second, we discuss recent experiments that reveal a potential role played by coherence,7 possibly quantum mechanical in nature,8 in light harvesting. We discuss how to think about quantum mechanical effects in light harvesting and how they might help light harvesting and capture of excitation energy at a trap.

The architectures of photosynthetic systems have motivated the design of light-active supramolecular assemblies.911 Using the principles of natural light harvesting may help inspire ways of increasing the capture of solar energy, for example, by materials used for solar fuel production.9,10,1217 It is worth emphasizing that bio-inspiration is much more interesting than searching for an optimized machine. Studies of how the photosynthetic apparatus has evolved over the course of the past 3 billion years in a variety of organisms has yielded a compelling picture of how the machinery has been not only refined, but also diversified.1821 Despite possible optimization by evolution, a challenge for elucidating key design principles by studying natural systems is that details can be obscured by accumulated complexity.22 Dutton and co-workers argue that parallel bottom-up design is needed to avoid such excess complexity.23 They argue that atomic level mimics of natural systems are unnecessary, but the challenge is to identify the key features of minimal functional units.

Bio-inspiration is evidently a complicated aim. Comparisons have been made between biological functions and engineering systems.24,25 Studies like these clearly indicate an element missing from current organic photovoltaic designsour solar cells are docile. In contrast, photosynthetic systems use a range of active mechanisms for regulating excitation transport to reaction centers and ensuring the system is photoprotected.2628 Imagine a solar panel that rewired itself in response to changing conditions of sun and cloud during the day using feedback and control loops.29 The engineering principles to achieve that are well known, but the requisite design elements at the molecular level have not yet been developed. They have, however, been discovered by nature and are utilized in many photosynthetic light-harvesting complexes.

The present paper focuses on light harvesting. Light harvesting is the process whereby excitation energy is captured by an assembly of molecules, the antenna, and subsequently transferred by Frster-type energy transfer to an acceptor (schematically illustrated in Fig. 1a). The acceptor is usually a chromophore with lower excitation energy than the donor molecules in the antenna. The result is a greatly enhanced spectral and spatial absorption cross-section for the acceptor. A synthetic and a naturally occurring light-harvesting assembly are presented in Fig. 1bd respectively. Fig. 1b shows a dendrimer macromolecule designed so that the peripheral chromophores are the primary light absorbers. Excitation energy is efficiently transferred to the core chromophore via a number of possible pathways. Thus the effective absorption cross-section for the core chromophore is increased substantially. The reaction center at the core of this complex is photosensitized by the surrounding 100 or more chromophores.30

The efficiency or distance range (diffusion length) of energy transfer and trapping via a random walk of excitation jumps depends on the excited state lifetime compared to the average time scale for a jump. For example, Pearlstein31 and Montroll32 have found that for N molecules arranged in a square lattice, where one of those molecules is a trap, it takes on average (1/)NlogN jumps to reach the trap. Considering the relatively short excited state lifetime of a typical molecule (say 2 ns) and an antenna comprising 400 molecules (meaning it is about 20 nm in length) an energy transfer jump time of 300 fs is required to attain a 90% quantum efficiency of trapping at a trap site. Energy transfer on this kind of time scale often involves mechanisms beyond those accommodated by Frster theory.33,34 Thinking beyond Frster theory opens up interesting opportunities for the control of energy transfer using molecular arrangements of molecules or perturbation of electronic interactions.35 For example, a remarkable recent finding has been the detection of coherence involving the excited chromophores in biological light-harvesting complexes, althoughas we will discuss in a later section of this articleit remains to be seen what new opportunities the coherence provides.

Available light utilization in nature

Light-harvesting complexes are not optimized to a standard set of metrics in isolation, like an absorption cross-section that perfectly complements the AM1.5 solar spectrum at one sun irradiance, for example. Like other living systems, local habitat, prevailing weather, and competing organisms in the habitat all need to be accounted for in the optimization of an antenna. Consequently, there is vast compositional and architectural variation in light-harvesting antennae of photosynthetic complexes, schematically depicted in Fig. 2a. The modification of the chemical nature of pigment molecules associated with light-harvesting complexes, as well as their number and mutual arrangement within the antenna structure allowed photosynthetic organisms to evolve to optimize their light-harvesting capabilities to particular regions of the visible and near-infrared spectrum. The challenge in bio-inspiration from photosynthetic organisms therefore is to find the rules guiding optimization. There may also be something to be gained by thinking about bio-inspiration differently. Perhaps there are alternatives to the goal of designing a single perfect device optimized for the perfect operating conditions. Naturally, the parameters defining an optimal solar cell are well defined, but are discoveries of new paradigms unduly constrained by the benchmarks characterizing a certified solar cell?

Photosynthesis has been optimized for plant survival, not light harvesting. Many factors, collectively, lead to the success of an organism, and the challenge is to determine the crucial parameters that have been selected for in evolution. For example, it is impractical to draw conclusions about relative optimization of photosynthetic light harvesting in gymnosperms versus angiosperms starting from the observation that gymnosperms (primitive seed-producing plants, like conifers, Ginkgo, etc.) are outnumbered 800 to 250000 by angiosperms (flowering plants). That marked difference in species diversity is due to the mode of reproduction and growth form, allowing flowering plants to master an incredible diversity of environments.37,38 Their photosynthetic apparatus is about the same. Similarly, it is clear that the bottom line on photosynthetic solar energy conversion is not the ratio of chemical energy produced to incident solar energy, but is more closely tied to survival fitness. For example, in one documented case, researchers discovered purple bacteria that deliberatively short-circuit light harvesting complexes to reduce photosynthetic efficiency.39 These bacteria inhabit the South Andros Black Hole, Bahamas. The carotenoids in the light harvesting complexes of these bacteria are modified so that most of the light absorbed by the carotenoids is transduced into heat. That allows these bacteria to control the temperature of their surroundings, making it inhospitable for competing organisms. In this example, light harvesting is used to fend off the opposition.

We believe it is important to keep an ecological perspective when trying to elucidate design principles by studying the properties and performance of isolated photosynthetic light harvesting complexes. For instance, the nature of the light environment is crucial for phototrophic microorganisms and has significant impact on their light-dependent metabolic activities. The presence of these pigment rich phototrophs themselves can greatly alter the light quality in a certain environment,40 and therefore, in order to cope with rapidly changing light quality and spectrum, photosynthetic species are often stratified in the aquatic environment. We think of the levels of perspective ranging from the physical functionality of isolated light-harvesting complexes (biophysics) to the function of those complexes in the organism (biology) to the interplay of various organisms cohabitating a community (ecology). The overall picture can be compared to a solar farm, where different parts of the sun's spectrum are harvested by different devicesa similar idea to tandem cells.

Microbial mats are examples of stratified light harvesting. They are model communities that have been studied to gain insight into the interplay of photosynthetic organisms.41 Pierson and co-workers have reported a striking example of complementary light harvesting in microbial mats found in the intertidal zone of Great Sippewissett Salt Marsh, Massachusetts.42,43 In Fig. 2b, a schematic depiction of the depth profile in a microbial mat is shown. Five distinct layers of phototrophic organisms were identified.

The incident solar irradiance spectrum and its attenuation through these layers was characterized.43 The incident solar irradiance spectrum is shown in the plot in Fig. 2c. Dramatic attenuation of the irradiance is observed in the subsequent spectrum, measured beneath the first two layers, the gold layer (diatom rich) and the green layer of cyanobacteria. The notable troughs in the transmitted spectrum can be attributed to chlorophyll-a (670680 nm), phycocyanin (620630 nm) and phycoerythrin (580590 nm). The strong absorption of carotenoids, chlorophylls (Soret peaks) and other pigments contribute to the dramatic decrease of irradiance below 500 nm. Below the pink layer (predominantly purple bacteria), the characteristics of the solar spectrum become washed out (Fig. 2c third spectrum), and the transmitted light is mainly in the near-infrared region because these purple bacteria harvest light in the 800850 nm range. The next layer of purple bacteria utilize bacteriochlorophyll-b in place of bacteriochlorophyll-a in their antenna complexes, and thus they can harvest the transmitted near infrared light (9001000 nm). The green sulfur bacteria at the bottom of the mat photosynthesize using the trickle of photons in the 750 nm region. They are living under conditions equivalent to a moonlit night, where each bacterium receives only about 300 photons per second!

Cryptophyte antenna complexes and light-harvesting innovation

Cryptophyte algae

Algae are plants lacking roots, stems, and leaves (thallophytes) and are found mainly in water.45 In most habitats, they are the primary producers in the food chain and fix about the same quantity of CO2 annually as higher plants.20 Our recent work has focused on cryptophytes, a family of motile, unicellular algae that occur in a variety of aquatic environments, both marine and freshwater. The cryptophyceae have a couple of notable features that make them interesting models for learning about light-harvesting design. First, they come in several colours, Fig. 3, as we will examine below. The marked colour variation stems from a remarkable range of chemical types of chromophores (bilins) that are found in the antenna complexes of cryptophytes. Second, cryptophytes derived from a secondary endosymbiosis, whereby the genetic information encoding photosynthesis was obtained by a non-photosynthetic protist from an enslaved red alga.21,4651 A striking observation here is that many of the genes for the phycobilisome antenna system of red algae (and cyanobacteria) were lost. Glazer and Wedermeyer highlighted why that is surprising: why would a sophisticated multi-subunit antenna that incorporates a highly effective spatial gradient of chromophore types and the ability to modify itself to adapt to local light quality reduce to an apparently much more primitive light-harvesting complex in cryptophytes? That question remains unanswered, but the cryptophyte antenna complexes are now known to function very efficiently.52

Chromophore diversity in cryptophyte light harvesting complexes

There are approximately 200 known species of cryptophyte algae.45 Each species contains only a single phycobiliprotein antenna complex; multiple species can produce the same or very similar phycobiliproteins. In Fig. 5 we elaborate on the diversity of the bilin chromophores in the phycobiliproteins. All cryptophyte phycobiliproteins are structurally similar in that they are based on an 2 tetramer complex, where each subunit carries one bilin chromophore, each subunit carries three bilins, and the attachment positions are completely conserved: -Cys 18 (or 19), -Cys 50,61(di-linked bilin), -Cys 82, and -Cys 158. The two classes of phycobiliproteins encountered in cryptophytes are phycoerythrins (red coloured) and phycocyanins (blue coloured), with subclassification being primarily based on their absorption spectrum maximum. Three types of cryptophyte phycoerythrins (Cr-PE) and five types of cryptophyte phycocyanins (Cr-PC) have been reported: PE 545, PE 555, two types of PE 566 (see discussion below), PC 569, PC 577, PC 612, PC 630 and PC 645.

The absorption of phycobiliproteins is ultimately linked to the bilin composition and content, where bilins have evolved to absorb light efficiently in the green, yellow, orange, or red portion of the visible spectrumthe spectral window where chlorophyll a absorption is very weak. Those chromophores are not all isomers; variation in the oxidation state is also observed. From the list of bilins in Fig. 5, variations in the number of double bonds (e.g. eight for phycocyanobilin (PCB) and phycoerythrobilin (PEB), nine in bilin 584 and 10 in bilin 618) and degree of conjugation are evident in the bilin library. Only PCBs and PEBs are present on cyanobacterial and rhodophytan biliproteins, whereas the others have only been detected in cryptomonads.

Determination of cryptophycean phycobiliprotein classification solely on absorption maximum is not efficient, as demonstrated by the case of PE566. Wedemeyer et al.54 have shown that two strains of PE566, the Bernani strain and the CBD strain, both exhibit an absorption maximum at 566 nm. However these phycobiliproteins differ in their bilin composition, specifically the chromophores attached to the subunit, where the Bernani strain has bilin 584 and the CBD strain binds bilin 618 instead. Bilin 584 has been noted to have an additional double bond between C-12 and C-12 compared to PEB.55 The difference in the bilin composition has led to further photophysical differences between these PE566 strains, where the distinction is made based on the absorbance ratio A600nm:Apeak (>0.23 for CBD, and <0.18 for Bernani) and the location of the fluorescence emission maximum (616 3 nm for CBD, and 601 1 nm for Bernani).54

The absorption spectra of photosynthetic pigments are affected by their local environment in the protein scaffold. Attachment of phycobilins to the apoprotein leads to several changes compared to free phycobilins in solution: attachment may alter the wavelength of their absorption maximum, narrow their absorption line width, and increase their fluorescence amplitude. The differences in absorption spectra among different phycobiliproteins arise owing to the nature of the chromophores that are bound to these proteins, their chemical environment, and the number and ratio of the pigments bound to the apoprotein. Phycoerythrins (PE545 and PE555) typically have only one maximum in their absorption spectrum and an absorption bandwidth of approximately 0.240.29 eV (FWHM of spectrum), as PEBs dominate the composition. The case of PE545 and PE555 is also a good example illustrating why the absorption spectrum of a phycobiliprotein is not equivalent to the sum of all the spectra of constituent chromophores. The two proteins both contain three PEBs and one dihydrobiliverdin (DBV), but the variation in the local environment of the bilins, arising as a result of folding of protein subunits and the variation of amino acid residues in the vicinity of phycobilins, significantly modifies the overall absorption spectrum.

From an evolutionary aspect, phylogenic analyses have shown that the origin of the subunits of cryptophyte biliproteins (both phycoerythrins and phycocyanins) can be traced back to red algal phycoerythrins (with respect to the apoprotein).56 It is believed that PEB is the ancestral character state of the cryptophyte biliprotein.47,57 As such, it is often suggested that the basalmost lineage often contains PE545, whose subunit presumably has retained some of the ancestral character state.57 To date there are no clues on the evolutionary origin of the subunit in cryptomonad phycobiliproteins, as this subunit does not appear to be compatible with the assembly of the trimeric structure exhibited by phycobilisomes in red algae and cyanobacteria. Cryptomonads synthesize a greater number of bilins than are known to occur in cyanobacterial and red algal species, but at the same time only produce one phycobiliprotein polypeptide, which in all cases is closely homologous to B-phycoerythrin subunit. The spectral variation in cryptomonad phycobiliproteins ultimately has resulted from their bilin diversity.

Some clues to the evolution of different bilins might lie in the biosynthetic pathways of these pigments as was proposed by Beale and Cornejo58 for the atypical acidophilic red alga Cyanidium caldarium:

heme biliverdin IX 15,16 dihydrobiliverdin IX (DBV) 3(Z)-phycoerythrobilin (PEB) 3(Z)-phycocyanobilin (PCB) 3(E)-phycocyanobilin (PCB)

From this scheme, it is evident that three of the cryptomonad phycobiliproteins (15,16-DBV, PCB and PEB) occur as intermediates in this pathway, whereas mesobiliverdin (MBV) can potentially be attained through a single step from the intermediates.59 The acryloyl bilins, bilin-618 and bilin-584, which occur exclusively in cryptomonads, might be generated through an alternative pathway based on acrylo-protoporphyrin. The evolution of PE545 can then be envisioned to have occurred as one PEB on the subunit was exchange for a DBV bilin, a precursor in the biosynthetic pathway of PEB and PCB as shown above. Further substitutions on different sites have lead to the formation of the bilin diversity in phycobiliproteins encountered in cryptophyte species. What factors drive changes in bilin type and how an organism iterates to a new antenna type are interesting questions. Another question that arises is whether small differencessuch as the ones observed for the two types of PE566tend to be strain specific or environmentally induced.

Antenna diversity and innovation

How species are selected for or against during evolution is a complex and fundamental biological question.6065 Driving forces for speciation can be adaptation or migration to new environments owing to the development of new traits. The diversity of cryptophyte light-harvesting spectra could be an example of speciation that enables habitation of aqueous strata or niches of varying light quality, or it may enable complementary habitation of an environment. It is unknown how the selection of species drives design of organisms,61 so it is difficult to draw conclusions about optimal light-harvesting antenna systems from surveying the extant species. Nevertheless, by examining the emergence of species richness, using the evolutionary tree for cryptophytes reported by Hoef-Emden,66 it is evident that antenna design represents an innovation. By innovation we mean an attribute that significantly contributes to diversification and serves as a branching point in evolution. For example, in Fig. 6 we plot the cumulative number of cryptophyte species with various antenna types. Notice that each new antenna type has spurred species diversification, suggesting that the antenna absorption spectrum is an innovation. It is also worth emphasizing that such innovations are non-trivial; it is not the result of a few random base mutations, but involves significant gene evolution.67

The analysis reported in Fig. 6 suggests that antenna typedetermined mainly by chromophore varietyis an innovation. Precisely what advantage is conferred by each of these differently coloured antenna types in different habitats is unknown. It could be that there is no distinct advantage, since the diversity of light-harvesting complexes in photosynthesis suggests there are many good solutions for light harvesting. Alternatively, the advantage could be that other organisms in the ecosystem are somehow disadvantaged, like the case of the purple bacteria in the South Andros Black Hole, mentioned earlier in this article. Plants only absorb a relatively narrow spectral range, and it is likely that differently coloured antennacomplementing chlorophyll absorptionallow other photosynthetic species to competitively occupy the same environment as higher plants and green algae. In any case, the range of cryptophyte light-harvesting complexes is decided mainly by the selection of bilin chromophores incorporated in the complex. A secondary control knob is the degree of excitonic coupling between the central pair of bilins. The coupling ranges from strong in the case of PC645 to weak amongst the phycobiliproteins of the Hemiselmis clade. Electronic interactions between the two bilins positioned at the center of light-harvesting complex lead to quite large splitting of the absorption band, thus modifying the absorption spectrum quite significantly.68,69 In principle, the exciton splitting can help bridge large energy gaps caused by chromophore type mismatches.

There is a vast range of length scales and time scales for photosynthesis in cryptophyte algae. The architectural and compositional elements of light-harvesting antenna seem to be optimized not only for a particular colour of light but also for adaptability, meaning that the complex interconnectedness of the light-harvesting elements allows organisms to adapt to different light intensities.

Light scavenging

Oftentimes photosynthesis is not light-limited. For example, throughout most of the day, plants dissipate absorbed excitation energy using ingenious photoprotection circuits (see ref. 6 for a brief review and relevant references). Indeed, it is likely that an innovation that makes the major light-harvesting complex of higher plants and green algae, LHCII, so ubiquitous is its ability to undo light harvesting by dissipating excess excitation energy effectively, as mentioned in the introduction to this article and described in detail elsewhere.6,27,28,70,71 However, where there is light, there is photosynthesiseven if the light is dim!

It is common to find that the main difference between a photosynthetic organism adapted for low-light conditions versus one grown in high-light conditions is the antenna size. More precisely, the ratio of antenna-bound molecules to reaction centers increases as light becomes scarcer. For example, in Fig. 7 we show micrographs and absorption spectra recorded for cryptophyte cells cultivated under three different light conditions. We studied the cryptophyte Proteomonas sulcata (CCMP705), which utilizes the light-harvesting complex PE545 depicted in pink in Fig. 2a, 4e and f. Chlorophylls a and c are found in membrane-bound proteins associated with the reaction centers, photosystems I and II, which give rise to the absorption band at about 680 nm. We grew the algae under three different light conditions: high light (20 Em2 s1), medium light (5 Em2 s1) and low light (1 Em2 s1). To put these irradiances in perspective, higher plants on land normally grow under 1001000 Em2 s1 irradiance sunlight. It has been found that optimum photosynthetic production for cryptophytes is at an irradiance of 40 Em2 s1, though this likely depends on the species. The micrographs presented in Fig. 7 suggest the highest loading of pigment is in algae grown under light-limited growth conditions, while those organisms grown at higher light intensities appear much lighter in colour because they carry a smaller load of pigment/organism. The absorption spectra of the three samples, normalized at 545 nm (the absorption maximum of PE545), illustrate the highest chlorophyll-to-PE545 ratio for algae grown at high light, while cryptophytes grown at lower light intensities utilize the phycobiliprotein antenna complex for light absorption to a much larger degree.

Quantum transport and interference

When Frster formulated his famous theory for energy transfer he made several assumptions that are generally useful. One specification he discussed at length was his assumption of very weak electronic coupling between the donor and acceptor.102 Taken together with the fact that spectral lines are homogeneously broadened, the very weak coupling approximation means that we can specify that one chromophore absorbs the incident radiation, leading to localized electronic excitation. The very weak coupling of that state to the acceptor means that transfer of excitation can be modeled as a decay of the initially excited state into a continuum of final states. In other words, the Fermi Golden Rule allows a rate to be defined and calculated. Researchers have often wondered what would happen if this approximation were to fail, in which case interference effects would modify the rate expression for multi-chromophoric systems in rather interesting ways.103105 It is only recently that experiments have been developed to detect evidence that such effects, referred to generally as electronic coherence, might be sufficiently long lived that they can affect energy transfer. At present we do not know quantitatively how significantly coherence modifies energy migration in disordered molecular assemblies. Future work is needed to ascertain how coherence could be exploited for man-made energy conversion systems.

Energy migration and trapping at reaction centers is a transport problem. Modeling such a process therefore calls for a treatment in the framework of transport theory.106,107 Transport theory can be formulated in various ways, including in (classical) probabilistic terms, as a (quantum) dynamical theory which describes and characterizes the propagation of wave packets, or from a scattering perspective. Probabilistic and dynamical treatments follow the specific paths excitation takes from the donor to the acceptor, in classical phase space or in quantum mechanical state space, while the scattering picture provides a mapping from all possible initial to all possible final states, and defines the associated transition amplitudes. The big question is whether quantum probability laws matter. The crucial quantum ingredient is that classically mutually exclusive alternativesfor example, distinct paths to get from the donor to the acceptor, along a sequence of molecular sites or, alternatively, a ladder of energy levelscan be simultaneously realized in the quantum world, and therefore need to be superimposed coherently, by proper account of the amplitudes and phases accumulated along the associated paths. This modulates classical transport coefficients by quantum interference, and can suppress or enhance classical transport, depending on the distribution of amplitudes and relative phases.

The paradigmatic double-slit experiment illustrates quantum interference in a minimalistic setting,108,109 with only one relative phase and equal amplitudes. The situation of course becomes considerably more complicated when transport occurs via many paths, in which case the quantum mechanical corrections to the dynamics are very sensitive to disorder and noise. That is much like the case of an interference grating or a high-quality FabryPerot interferometer, where the cost associated with the sharper interference structures, arising from interference effects, is that the patterns are more sensitive to small perturbations. Multi-path interference effects can give rise to phenomena with no classical analog. For example it can lead to resonant tunneling in extended potentials,110 as well as to localization107 in the presence of disorderwhich is generic in nature. While constructive quantum interference enhances transport in the former case, destructive quantum interference may totally halt transport in the latter. We thus witness quantum transport characteristics that are fundamentally different from the corresponding classical picture, where transport is either fully suppressed by potential barriers, or unbounded and diffusive even in the presence of disorder.

In the context of transport in light-harvesting antenna complexes, interference phenomena may be more subtle to detect. Let's say there are two pathways for transferring excitation from molecule A to B in a light harvesting complex. Classically, the probability of the energy transfer is simply the sum of the probability of taking each path. In quantum mechanics, however, that probability law is modified, resulting in the common explanation that both paths are taken simultaneously. What happens is that the probability of energy transfer from A to B is calculated differently: we assign a probability amplitude to each path, sum those amplitudes, then convert the sum to probability by taking the modulus squared. For example, quantum coherence as corrections to the classical transfer rates from donor to acceptor due to multi-path interference has been considered.111,112 When it is weak, we can treat the electronic coupling perturbatively, thereby adding corrections to the Fermi Golden Rule expression for energy transfer. Multi-path interference is very sensitive to perturbations in the scattering potential. For instance, if we sample over different, possibly continuously parameterized realizations of the scattering potential that defines the relative path amplitudes and phases, we will find strong fluctuations in the transport coefficients, as well as in analogously defined cross-sections. In a transport scenario defined by a disordered energy landscape, the fluctuations can be interpreted as due to the accidental shifting of the system's site energies into and out of resonance under different realizations of disorder. Different realizations may be considered as an expression of randomness if no correlations or residual symmetries can be detected; the phrase may also be interpreted as a reflection of the deterministic dynamics of some background degrees of freedom.

Two-dimensional spectroscopy reveals coherence in antenna complexes

Typical studies of ultrafast dynamics in the condensed phase focus on measuring rates of decay of states photo-excited by a short laser pulse, and concomitant growth of other excited-state populations. As such, these kinds of studies are restricted to the realm of classical probability laws and are not well suited for providing strong tests of mechanisms promoting the dynamics. Indeed, it is even difficult to discriminate different kinetics models from the analysis of population transients.114117 It is moreover very difficult to resolve questions concerning the significance of coherence and deviations from the classical probability laws owing to interference effects because resolution of such questions really requires access to information about probability amplitudes.118 Multidimensional electronic spectroscopy and related methods have played an important role addressing these issues.7,119129 Such experiments, typically two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy (2D ES), record signal amplitudes that correlate the energies of states that are photo-excited with those involved in radiating the signal after a waiting time (the population time T or t2) that is the same delay time variable in pumpprobe experiments.130137 While 2D ES does not reveal directly the properties of coherent dynamics, it can show the existence of superpositions of vibronic states after femtosecond excitation. This inspires deeper theoretical studies to predict the role coherence plays in energy transfer.

2D ES is similar to one-dimensional four-wave-mixing measurements like transient absorption (often called pumpprobe spectroscopy). In a formal description of transient-absorption measurements based in the framework of nonlinear optics, the pump beam electric field interacts twice with the sample to photo-excite a fraction of the chromophores into electronic excited states. These two interactions simply represent the product of the transition dipole () connecting the ground state (0) to an excited state () with its complex conjugate to give the probability of pump absorption, proportional to |0|2. A probe pulse arrives after a variable delay t2 to stimulate a signal field that is detected as a change in transmission of the probe. 2D ES can be understood in a similar way, except that both the spectrum of states photo-excited as well as the spectrum of states probed after t2 are recorded and correlated. This can be accomplished138,139 by first separating the two pump electric-field interactions into distinct laser beams and then scanning the delay time between them. The pump pulse sequence now has a time period, t1 (often known as the coherence time ), which is scanned for each value of t2. Time period t1 is scanned over negative as well as positive time delays (a negative time delay means that the ordering of pulses 1 and 2 is reversed). At every t1 value, the spectrum of the signal radiated during the final time period, t3, is measured, Fig. 9a. This provides the signal as a function of frequency, 3, for each t1 value for each specified waiting time. Fourier transformation with respect to t1 of the t1versus 3 spectral interferogram, Fig. 9b, at each waiting time t2 produces a 2D spectrum that correlates signal as a function of 1 with signal as a function of 3, Fig. 9c and d.

Electronic and vibrational coherence

The 2D spectra also display information relevant for investigating quantum-mechanical phenomena in energy-transfer dynamics. In particular, cross peaks can show the relationships among electronic states, often called coupling. These kinds of features are apparent in the 2D spectrum shown in Fig. 9d. In this experiment the two lowest-energy exciton bands of zinc-blende structure CdSe nanocrystals (solution sample, 298 K) were coherently excited by 11 fs laser pulses.145 Bleach features of the pair of exciton bands are seen along the diagonal. The cross peaks in the upper and lower antidiagonal positions show that these exciton bands involve common orbitals. Indeed, while electrons are excited from different valence bands in each case, they are promoted to a common conduction band. Thus photo-excitation of the lower-energy exciton produces not only a bleach on the diagonal, but also a bleach at an energy corresponding to the upper exciton transition (because of Pauli blocking), and vice versa. In spectroscopic terminology, the two electronic transitions share a common ground state. In the plot shown the diagonal peaks do not lie exactly on the diagonal, which is partly related to the position of underlying excited state absorption bands.

One of the highly interesting applications of 2D ES has been to monitor the time evolution of electronic coherences produced by photo-exciting a superposition of two vibronic transitions. When a femtosecond laser pulse excites a coherent superposition comprising two excited states, | and |, the 2D spectrum contains a signal contribution originating from the off-diagonal density ||. The result is a cross peak positioned at (v,v) in the 2D spectrum that oscillates as a function of waiting time t2. It is apparent from our most recent 2D ES studies that electronic and vibrational coherences can appear hand-in-hand.148 This is also suggested in an independent study of this same light harvesting complex by Davis and co-workers.126 Recent 2D ES studies from the Engel group on the FMO complex from green sulfur bacteria also reveal multi-frequency beating patterns associated with electronic coherence,125,149 and the Moran group has previously identified strong vibrational effects that possibly accompany electronic coherences in components of phycobilisome antenna systems from cyanobacteria.150154 Some years ago Cina and Fleming examined the possibility that vibrational coherences were transferred from donor to acceptor in energy transfer in order to explain unusual quantum beats on measurements of LH1 from purple bacteria.155 Recently, Cina and Biggs have examined in some detail how vibrational wavepackets can be used to control energy transfer.156,157 Very new, unpublished work has focused on the role of high-frequency molecular vibrations in coherent energy transfer.158,159 These studies reveal that energy gaps arising from chemically different chromophores in an assembly together with vibrations that bridge these gaps enable directional control of ultrafast energy transfer when the dynamics involve coherence. This is precisely the power of the Frster spectral overlap principle,79,80,160 but in the coherent limit. All of this work points to the importance of vibronic effects, and future work must resolve these issues with more realistic exciton models, such as those evidently required for understanding high resolution spectra in the frequency domain.161,162

Substantial evidence suggests that electronic coherence is involved in the excited state evolution of photosynthetic light-harvesting complexes after photoexcitation with femtosecond pulses. There are some important questions that have yet to be resolved: (a) Do coherent dynamics play a role in energy transfer initiated by incoherent light (e.g. sunlight)? (b) How should we think about what coherence brings to excitation transfer and transport in the weak-intermediate electronic coupling regime; how could we detect its role? (c) What role does coherence play that matters for biological function?

Quantum engineering in chemistry and biology?

Perspective on the physics of complex quantum systems

The coupling of electronic transitions to vibrational modes as well as other background degrees of freedom is an important issue for theories of quantum-coherent dynamics because it is the timescales and strength of this coupling which may cause dephasing in the electronic degrees of freedom. Furthermore, the microscopic and heterogeneous nature of the systembath interactions in proteins are little understood.163166 Changes that are slow relative to the timescale for energy transfer are referred to as static disorder and translate into a statistical distribution of site energies and electronic couplings. This disorder, usually assumed to be random, manifests itself as inhomogeneous broadening in spectral line shapes and energy transfer rates.167 However, vibrational dynamics of the macromolecular backbone may be deterministic, at least to some extent.168,169

The perspective from quantum chaos theory has not been deeply explored for this problem. It is well known from the theory of quantum chaos170,171 that the non-perturbative coupling of very few degrees of freedom (at least two in autonomous systems, one in the presence of periodic driving) can induce large-scale fluctuations of matrix elements. To quote a well-understood example, the photoionization cross-section of atomic Rydberg states in strong, static magnetic fields exhibits quasi-erratic, large-scale fluctuations under small changes of the field, while the underlying, effectively 2D dynamics are perfectly deterministic.172 In some sense, the quasi-random fluctuations of the photoionization cross-section express the sensible dependence of the Rydberg wave functions' localization properties in the decay channel on the precise value of the magnetic field (which strongly distorts the Rydberg wave function).173,174

Under rather general conditions, fluctuations such as those underpinning the examples mentioned above can be described by ensembles of random matrices, that is, they look random on certain scales, though actually they are a unique and unambiguous fingerprint of the underlying, non-integrable (in the sense of nonlinear dynamics) deterministic Hamiltonian.170,171 In other words, the distinctive properties of such complex quantum systems are encoded in these fluctuations, rather than in their smooth, average behavior which is determined by coarse grained properties like the accessible phase space volume. These connections have been elucidated for the exemplary case of Rydberg states in static magnetic fields, which, in some sense, as effectively two degrees of freedom system, define a minimal scenario.175,176 In our present context, one could imagine the electronic degree of freedomfor example modeled by a one dimensional double-well potentialstrongly coupled to a vibrational degree of freedom. The only ingredient that is required to realize the quantum-chaos scenario is that the coupling of both degrees of freedom be non-integrable.177

If we were to transfer and validate the above scenarios to light-harvesting molecular networks that mediate excitation transfer, we first wish to identify the different pathways along which transport can occur.178,179 Identifying and measuring the associated phases and amplitudes, however, is a formidable exercise in systems where the number of actively involved degrees of freedom is not precisely known, and one isby constructionbound to work with effective degrees of freedom on a level that cannot be absolutely gauged. In multichromophoric systems, generally only electronic degrees of freedom and one or more strong localized vibrational modes can be probed. Furthermore, the separation between the electronic and vibrational degrees of freedom is not always clear or possible.180 A loss of interference contrast in certain experimental observables may therefore be due to an implicit average over unresolved degrees of freedom,109,181 while the actual dynamics are coherent, or protected from actual (dynamical) environmental noise. Again, probing the statistics of the molecular response to a well-defined probe (in terms of the preparation of the initial condition) under variations of the specific realization might allow one to determine unambiguously the quantum versus classical character of the underlying dynamics, since quantum interference can induce fluctuations which cannot be reproduced by classical stochastic processes,106 and ambient noise needs finite time to make this interference fade away.182 Alternatively, an efficiency that depends on the phase relations embedded in the initial electronic state could also provide a clear signature of quantum transport even in the presence of static disorder.183,184

The picture outlined above is largely inspired by the phenomenology of coherent quantum dynamics on a finite number of strongly coupled degrees of freedom, and may suffice to elucidate, at least qualitatively, a large part of the collected experimental evidence on quantum coherence in light-harvesting complexes. However, the current debate in the literature also suggests an important role of ambient noise in the excitonic transport process.93,111,185188 Whether quantum coherence can play a functional role on the level of macromolecular dynamics depends on the comparison of the functionally relevant time scales with the time scales on which the noise can intervene and effectively destroy quantum coherence.182,189 Asymptotically, noise will always win, and the role of quantum coherence, if any, must therefore be associated with transient time scales (i.e. ultrafast nonequilibrium energy transfer).

Challenges for quantum engineering in chemistry and biology

One of the fascinating and largely open questions is to understand the design principles which were developed by nature to achieve the required balance of time scales for electronic resonance versus dephasing. In a quantum-engineering language, how did nature enter the strong coupling (between the transporting degrees of freedom, as opposed to those of the detrimental part of the environment) regime190,191in a really complex system? That is, at effectively high spectral densitiesin contrast to the quantum engineering scenario, where low spectral densities (ideally two-level systems) are machined. In stark contrast to the quantum engineer, nature has ample resources and typically builds multipurpose machines. Under competition, these machines need not be optimal, but just better than the competition and they must be robust. Therefore, when it comes to controlling the function of complex systems, statistical192,193rather than deterministicoptimization in the sense briefly sketched above may be the route to success. This observation may suggest a route for robust quantum technologies in complex environments.

On longer length and time scales compared to the workings of a single light-harvesting complex, biological functionality very often requires the response to external stimuli which need to be gauged against physiologically defined thresholds, and that these activation processes are intrinsically nonlinear.194,195 Consider a paradigmatic example of threshold activation, the non-perturbative coupling of a simple double-well potential to a second degree of freedom. This model can display large-scale fluctuations needed for a small control signal to be amplified to a response, if quantum coherence prevails. This could be a way to implement a highly sensitive quantum switch.177,196,197

Given the stunning degree of molecular organization on various scales as observed in light-harvesting complexes, one may speculate that exciton dynamics as carried by electronic and/or vibrational degrees of freedom could be orchestrated with the molecular backbone's dynamics, allowing some control in response to the environmental boundary conditions. However, one may also argue that such fine-tuning on the level of single light-harvesting complexes is unlikely given the disorder in site energies.198 Nevertheless, since biological functionality is frequently stabilized by some level of redundancy, it may suffice that a certain molecular configuration, associated with a certain transfer efficiency, have a sufficient statistical weight within the different realizations which are assembled in a single organism. In such a perspective, (multi-)functionality could be hardwired in statistical distributions rather than in unique molecular architectures and would automatically obtain the advantage of robustness.113 However, it hitherto remains unclear how the relevant parts of such distributions are activated under specific environmental stimuli to inhibit or activate the desired function. An interesting experiment would be a vast set of single-molecule experiments to search for subsets that have consistent site energies and energy-transfer dynamics.198200

Conclusions for solar energy technologies

The need for cheap and efficient solar power motivates us to look at natural light-harvesting systems for inspiration, keeping in mind that our photovoltaic goals and nature's photosynthesis goals are parallel but not identical. Despite more than a century of research, there is still much to learn about how plants and other photosynthetic organisms gather energy from the sun. In this work we discussed two contemporary areas in photosynthesis research that may represent excellent avenues for photovoltaic research. We have endeavored to discuss improvements organisms have made to photosynthetic light-harvesting machinery over time and provide some perspectives on how to think about the driving forces behind those improvements and diversifications.

The first event in solar energy conversion is absorption of light. The conversion of the energy carried by the incident photons to stored energy is first limited by how effectively the incident spectrum is captured and the energy lost from that spectrum during relaxation of excited electronic states and conversion to charge separated states. In photosynthesis the incident spectrum is harvested by a community of organisms, often with complementary absorption spectra. The key to the complementary absorption is variation in the apparatus specializing in light harvestingthe primary light harvesting complexes. Often organisms are stratified, for example in the water column, and therefore function in the spirit of a tandem solar cell. Those organisms found deeper in the column must function under lower average irradiance, and hence light harvesting optimizations are important.

Light harvesting complexes tend to direct excitation energy to reaction centers via a rather disordered energy landscapethat is, the excitation energies of chromophores in the antenna are not uniform. Sometimes the process is facilitated by an energy gradient, or funnel, most notably in purple bacteria and cyanobacteria. This allows excitation energy to be directed, and therefore has a significant influence on maximizing the exciton diffusion length while maintaining high efficiency of trapping. Experiments have recently provided evidence that coherent effects can modify energy transfer mechanisms in natural light harvesting complexes, but it is not clear whether such phenomena profoundly range the mechanism of long-range exciton diffusion. Nevertheless, they can bring energy transfer into a regime where energy jumps are as rapid as possible (cf.Fig. 8a).

The energetic and structural disorder that characterizes natural light harvesting complexes in combination with quantum mechanical delocalization of excitation and coherence challenges traditional ways of thinking about energy transfer. It is likely that key opportunities lie not in designing systems based on their average performance (exciton diffusion time or efficiency). Instead, we should work out how to utilize the tails of distributions, where quantum effects are predominant. It is not clear at present what opportunities may be presented, but it will be fruitful to pursue this idea in two ways. First, it is imperative to develop experiments capable of detecting the edge of distributions rather than focusing on the mean. That might be accomplished by filtering an ensemble to register and characterize the fastest energy transfer events. Note that single molecule/complex experiments are likely not the solution, at least not directly, because dynamic disorder renders the systems ergodic. Second, theories can explore how to utilize quantum effects in complex, noisy environments. The crucial breakthrough will be to discover how subtle quantum mechanical effects at the level of few interacting molecules can lead to new kinds of emergent phenomena in a highly complex system.

Note that phase and state space define the most general scenery for transport. For the efficient description of a specific transport problem, in particular when of a certain degree of complexity, it is crucial to choose the appropriate coordinate system or representation think, e.g., of transport problems in real space, formulated in the site or in the energy basis. While, on the one hand, the proper choice of a coordinate system is a practically very important ingredient of the art of high performance computing, it almost always relies, on the other hand, on a good degree of a priori intuition (ideally aided by some systematic use of group theory) of which are the dominant transporting modes.
