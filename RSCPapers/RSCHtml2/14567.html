Abstract

In this work we have synthesized Mn doped GdFeO3 nano-particles using a green and facile sol gel method and studied their photocatalytic, optical, vibrational and electrical properties. The Rietveld refinement of the XRD profiles suggests that all the materials have an orthorhombic Pbnm crystal structure. The transmission electron microscope (TEM) images show the decrease of the average particle size from 140 to 80 nm with the Mn concentration. The high crystallinity of the synthesized particles is confirmed from the HR-TEM images. Raman spectrum is employed to investigate the phonon modes of the materials. The optical band gap of the materials is obtained from the UV-vis reflectance spectroscopy (DRS) using Tauc relation which indicates the reduction of the band gap from 2.18 to 1.72 eV with Mn-doping. The photocatalytic activity of the materials is studied by the photocatalytic degradation of rhodamine B (Rh-B) in aqueous solution under visible light illumination. The substitution of Mn at the Fe site introduces an extra electronic state between the conduction band and the valence band which reduces the electronic band gap and enhances the Rh-B degradation efficiency. A 30% Mn doping at the Fe site (GFMO3) provides an optimum space charge width which assists to attain the maximum rate of degradation of the Rh-B dye. The doping of Mn3+ reduces the photogenerated electron and hole recombination rate and hence more charge carriers take part in the redox reaction which facilitates the photo-catalytic efficiency in GFMO3. The degradation rate enhances by a factor of 2.5 for GFMO3 as compared to pure GdFeO3. The highest photocurrent density of 1.31 μA cm−2 of GFMO3 with respect to other materials promotes the separation and transfer of the photo generated charge carriers. The possible photocatalytic mechanism of the Mn doped GdFeO3 is also critically discussed. Alternating current impedance spectroscopy is used to study the electrical properties of the synthesized materials. The increase in the conductivity with the Mn concentration is explained on the basis of the band gap reduction and this is consistent with the Smit and Wijn theory. Magnetic measurement is performed to measure the magnetization strength which is useful to separate the photocatalyst by simply using a magnet. The temperature dependent magnetization measurement suggests the anti-ferromagnetic (AFM) behaviour of the studied materials with the decrease of Néel temperature (TN) with Mn concentration. The XPS study reveals the presence of multiple oxidation states of Fe(2+/3+) and Mn(4+/3+) in these materials which facilitates the conductivity as well as the oxidation/reduction efficiency at the surface of the catalyst. The band gap reduction and its effect on the enhancement of the photocatalytic degradation efficiency with Mn doping are also discussed from the density of states calculations. Thus, this study describes a promising approach for the organic pollutant degradation by designing an efficient and stable perovskite photocatalyst.

I. Introduction

Environmental remediation has attracted our attention because of the expansion of the utilization of dyes in various industries like paints, leather, rubber, cosmetics, plastics, etc. The dyes discharged from these industries create environmental issues such as contamination of water, release of poisonous gases with unpleasant smell, etc. In this regard, solar light driven photocatalysis has been considered as an efficient, sustainable and eco-friendly way to degrade organic pollutants.1,2 Recently, the photocatalysis process has been extensively used to degrade organic dyes (such as methyl red, methyl orange, rhodamine B (Rh-B), thymol blue, and ethylene blue) in wastewater in an environmentally friendly green way.3,4

In the photocatalytic process, the photogenerated electrons and holes are formed by absorbing the high energy photons, where electrons are transferred to the conduction band (CB). Furthermore, the excited electrons and the holes are migrated to the surface of the catalyst for the reduction/oxidation reactions to occur. The excited electrons react with the adsorbed oxygen molecules on the surface to produce superoxide radicals (O2˙−), and the hydroxyl radicals (˙OH) are formed by the holes via reaction with the water molecules, which further reduce the organic compounds.5–10 Therefore, the efficiency of a photocatalyst depends on two factors: the efficient light absorption and the electron–hole separation.

Since the discovery of water photolysis in 1972 using TiO2 photocatalyst as the photoanode,11 oxide semiconductors have attracted numerous research interests in this field. Although TiO2 has been considered as a benchmark catalyst due to its high efficiency, nontoxicity and abundancy, its application is limited within the UV region because of the large bandgap (∼3.2 eV). Thus, research focus has been devoted to developing a low bandgap semiconductor catalyst which can absorb visible light consisting of 43% of the total solar spectrum.12 In this contest, the ABO3 type oxide semiconductors having a perovskite structure with corner sharing eight BO6 octahedra and 12 oxygen coordinated with A cations have received significant attention in photocatalysis due to their high crystal stability, tunability, suitable bandgap and band edge potentials.13,14 Depending on the ionic radii and the electro-negativity of the A and B site cations, the tilting of the octahedra takes place, which further influences the electronic and optical properties (via band structure), the electron and hole transport, the photoluminescence and the dielectric behaviour.15 An efficient catalyst can be designed with a suitable combination of A and B site cations. Therefore, perovskite oxides are widely used in various applications, like memory devices, sensors, supercapacitors, photovoltaics, and energy harvesting and thermoelectric applications.16–22 For example, the perovskite BiFeO3 is a well-known room temperature multiferroic material in which the ferroelectric and the anti-ferromagnetic properties exist simultaneously, which makes it attractive for the energy harvesting applications.23 On the other hand, SrTiO3 has shown an excellent photocatalytic activity in organic pollutant degradation because of its low band gap and high amount of oxygen vacancies.24,25 Recently, rare earth based orthoferrite perovskites like LaFeO3, SmFeO3, and GdFeO3 are gaining attention as photocatalysts due to their excellent photodegradation rate and photo-Fenton-like reaction.26–30 Hydroxyl radicals are produced on the surface of these photocatalysts by the Fenton oxidation reaction of Fe(II)/Fe(III) and hydrogen peroxide. The synergistic effect between the photocatalytic activity and the Fenton-like reaction may further accelerate the degradation rate of the pollutants.31 In this regard, gadolinium iron oxide, GdFeO3 (GFO), a prototype orthoferrite perovskite oxide belonging to the Pbnm space group family with a G-type magnet, has become an interesting material for the photocatalytic organic pollutant degradation.32–34 Moreover, GFO possesses a strong uniaxial anisotropy, and high coercivity and Faraday rotation, and is hugely used in terahertz sensors and magneto-optical data storage devices because of its interesting electrical and magnetic properties.35,36

It is well known that the fast recombination of the photogenerated electrons and holes for these perovskite semiconductors reduces the catalytic activity.37–42 Up to now, various strategies, such as surface engineering, crystal facet engineering, doping, morphology tuning, coupling with other materials, heterostructure formation etc. have been used to overcome the issues of large bandgap as well as fast recombination.43–47 Among these, doping is one of the effective ways to tune the band gap of a semiconductor by introducing an extra electronic state.48,49 For example, Bhat and his co-workers48 have developed Rh-doped BaTiO3 by one pot hydrothermal method and demonstrated that 0.5 mol% Rh-doping at the Ba-site leads to a decrease in the band gap due to the appearance of donor Rh3+ states and consequently enhances the degradation of methylene blue (96%) within 120 mins under visible light irradiation.48 Ma et al.50 have reported that the doping of transition metals (Ni, Cu, Fe, Mn, and Rh) in the SrTiO3 lattice effectively reduces the band gap and enhances the visible light absorption ability with improved photocatalytic H2 evolution from water. Bantawala et al.51 have shown the role of atomic ratio variation of Sr and Ti in the case of SrTiO3 for the organic pollutant methylene blue degradation. Very recently, they have prepared SrTiO3 nanocubes and modified them by partial V-doping at the Ti-site to introduce a dopant state near the CB, which reduces the fast recombination rate by acting as an electron trap centre.52 Lu et al.53 have modified the perovskites ATiO3 (A = Ca, Sr, Br) by co-doping La at the A-site and Cr at the Ti-site to make them active for H2 generation through the water reduction under visible light illumination. Furthermore, structure dependent photocatalytic activity has been shown by Lv et al.54 for the Ni-doped double perovskite A2InTaO6 (A = Sr or Ba). They have demonstrated that Ni-doping has generated an additional spin-polarized band of In(Ni)–O–Ta inside the intrinsic band gap of the perovskite semiconductors which effectively reduces the band-gap and improves the metal–oxygen–metal interaction. Abdi et al.55 have also reported effective longer wavelength light absorption and 19-times greater photocatalytic activity of La–Fe co-doped SrTiO3 perovskite for organic pollutant degradation.

It is to be mentioned that many studies have been reported on the photocatalytic degradation of different pollutants (like rhodamine-B, methyl orange etc.) by GdFeO3 and its composites with other materials.56–62 Inspired by the crystal engineering technique, we have tried to tune the band gap of GFO by adding Mn at the Fe-site to enhance its visible light photocatalytic performance. In this work, the effect of partial substitution of Fe by Mn in GFO has been studied towards the optical, electrical, magnetic, electrochemical and photocatalytic performance; a 30% doped material, GdFe0.7Mn0.3O3, has shown maximum photocatalytic degradation efficiency for rhodamine-B (Rh-B) under visible light irradiation. A complete degradation of Rh-B is achieved by GdFe0.7Mn0.3O3 in only 25 min. Thus with respect to the previous studies, GdFe0.7Mn0.3O3 is more promising to be used as a photocatalytic material for the degradation of organic pollutant under visible light irradiation in a very short time. Finally an effort has been made to establish a correlation between the photocatalytic and the electrical properties. The X-ray photoemission spectroscopic (XPS) study has also been performed to find out the oxidation state of the cations in the materials. The band gap reduction with Mn doping is also discussed from the density of states calculations.

II. Experimental section

A. Synthesis of Mn doped GdFeO3

The GdFeO3 (GFO), GdFe0.9Mn0.1O3 (GFMO1), and GdFe0.7Mn0.3O3 (GFMO3) nano-crystals were synthesized by a low cost sol–gel method.63 The Gd(NO3)3·6H2O (Alfa Aesar), Fe(NO3)3·9H2O (Loba Chemie, 98%) and Mn(NO3)2·4H2O (Merck, 98.5%) were taken according to the stoichiometry ratio and dissolved in the deionized water separately. After this, the solutions were mixed together and stirred vigorously using a magnetic stirrer for 30 min to form a stable and transparent solution. Citric acid was taken according to the 1:1 ratio with the metal nitrates and dissolved in deionized water. The citric acid solution was added to the previously mixed solution of metal nitrates. Then, ethylene glycol was added drop-wise to the above mixture in a ratio of 1:4 with metal nitrates. In this process, citric acid was used as a chelating agent and ethylene glycol was added to the chelated solution to initiate a poly-esterification reaction. The mixture was stirred continuously for 6 hours at a constant temperature of 353 K to form a uniform solution. Then, the solution was dried to form a gelled mass. The gelled mass was heated until auto combustion takes place. The formed dried gel was ground using an agate mortar pestle. After this the ground gel was calcined at a temperature 1073 K for 8 h. The calcined powder was compressed to form a pellet with a radius of 8 mm and thickness of 1.5 mm. The pellets were sintered at the temperature of 1173 K for 6 h in air and cooled down to the room temperature at a cooling rate of 100 K h−1.

B. Characterization

The room temperature X-ray diffraction (XRD) data of the calcined materials were obtained using an X-ray powder diffractometer (Rigaku Miniflex II) in the 2θ range of 10° to 80° with a step scanning at 0.02° per step to identify the phase and to obtain the lattice parameters of the as prepared GFMO samples. The Rietveld refinement of the XRD patterns is performed using the Full-prof code.64 The particle size, the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern and the high resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images of the samples were obtained using a transmission electron microscope (FEI Tecnai G2, 200 kV). The room temperature Raman spectra were taken using the back-scattering geometry using a Lab RAM HR Raman spectroscope (Jobin Yvon) to ensure about the phase formation of the materials. An Ar-ion laser of effective power of 200 mW was used as the exciting line (488 nm). The complex impedance, the conductivity and the phase angle of the sintered pellet of the samples were measured at an AC voltage of 1.0 V in the frequency range from 42 Hz to 5 MHz and in the temperature range from 313 K to 723 K using a HIOKI LCR meter. The measuring temperature was controlled using an inbuilt cooling-heating system and each measured temperature was kept constant with an accuracy of ±1 K. The optical band gap of the materials was obtained from the diffuse reflectance (DR) spectrum in the wavelength range of 200–800 nm using a Shimadzu 2401 spectrophotometer. The photoluminescence spectra of the samples were collected at room temperature using a JASCO FP-8500 spectrofluorometer. The magnetic measurement was performed using a vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM; Lakeshore). The temperature vs. magnetization measurement was carried out at an applied magnetic field of 2 kOe with an accuracy of ±5 K. The field dependent magnetization was measured at room temperature.

C. Photocatalytic activity

The photocatalytic experiment was performed through the degradation of organic pollutant Rh-B under the illumination of a 250 watt MPMVL UV-vis lamp, used as a visible light source. The suspension of the Rh-B solution (5 mg L−1) and the photocatalyst was stirred using a magnetic stirrer for one hour to attain the adsorption–desorption equilibrium. To enhance the photocatalytic performance of the materials, 1 mL of 4% H2O2 was added to initiate the reaction. The reaction temperature was kept constant by a cool water flow in the system to prevent any thermal catalytic effect and the thermal evaporation of the solution. A small amount of the suspension was collected from the reactor at regular time intervals. The photocatalyst powder was separated from the suspension using a Millex syringe driven filter of 0.22 micro-metre. The clear solution was then taken to the UV-Vis spectrophotometer to measure the absorbance and thereby evaluate the degradation percentage of Rh-B.

D. Photo-electrochemical measurements

The photo-electrochemical (PEC) properties of the synthesized materials were investigated using a galvanostat-potentiostat (PGSTAT302 N, Autolab, The Netherlands) with a standard three-electrode cell. In this three electrode system, Pt wire was used as the counter electrode, the reference electrode was saturated Ag/AgCl and the thin film of the as prepared material on the FTO was used as the working electrode. To prepare the thin film, 1 mg of the synthesized powder was mixed with 2.5 mL of ethylene glycol (EG) in a centrifuge tube under sonication and the resulting paste was coated on the FTO glass. The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) measurement was carried out within the potential range of 0.6 V to 1.0 V vs. Ag/AgCl at a scan rate of 10 mV s−1 using a medium pressure vapour lamp of 100 W with an incident beam intensity of 100 mW cm−2. 0.1 M Na2SO4 phosphate buffer (pH 7) was used as the electrolyte. The I–t amperometry study was performed at 0.5 V vs. Ag/AgCl potential to study the stability of the synthesized materials. The photo-response of the as prepared electrode was measured under the periodic chopped irradiation.

E. X-Ray photoemission spectroscopic study

The XPS images of the samples were taken using a commercial VG ESCALAB 220I-XL imaging system. The spectra were collected using an Al Kα radiation source at 1486.6 eV. The energy spectra of the electrons were analysed using a hemispherical mirror analyser with an energy resolution of 0.5 eV. The measured binding energies were compensated in a conventional manner with a C 1s binding energy (BE) of 285 eV. The core level XPS spectra of the ions were de-convoluted using the XPSPEAK 4.165 software. The Shirley-type background subtraction was done to remove the unwanted spectrum and the spectra were fitted with the sum of the Lorentzian–Gaussian peaks. The accuracy of the determination of peak position is ±0.03 eV.

F. Density of states calculations

The total density of states (DOS) and the site projected partial density of states (PDOS) of the doped GFMO3 and the un-doped materials, GdFeO3 and GdMnO3, were calculated using the WIEN2k electronic structure code.66 In our calculations we have used 2 × 2 × 2 supercells for the un-doped systems with 20 atoms per unit cell. The 3 × 3 × 3 supercell calculations were carried out to model the doped system where the host Fe ion is replaced by Mn ion. The experimental structure of the orthorhombic GFMO was used as the initial structure. All the geometries were fully optimized by the relaxation of both the ionic positions and the cell parameters using the DFT formalism with the modified Beckee Johnson (mBJ)67 potential as the exchange correlation term. In the calculations, an energy cut-off of 680 eV was employed for the plane wave basis. The self-consistent iteration process was continued till the total energy and the charge density convergence reaches less than 0.01 mRy per unit and 0.001 e a.u−3, respectively. The onsite Coulomb potential U values of 7.1, 6.8 and 5.8 eV were applied for the f-state of Gd, d-state of Mn and d-state of Fe respectively in the calculations.

III. Results and disscussions

A. Structural analysis

The Rietveld refinement of the room temperature XRD patterns of pure GdFeO3 and Mn doped GdFeO3 is shown in Fig. 1(a)–(c). The refinement shows that the materials are synthesized in the orthorhombic phase with a Pbnm space group. A schematic representation of the GFMO unit cell is shown in Fig. 1(d), with the following distribution of ions in the crystallographic positions: Gd3+ ions at 4c, Fe3+/Mn3+ ions at 4b and O2− ions at 4c and 8d. In this crystal structure the Fe3+/Mn3+ ions are surrounded by six O2− ions which basically constitute the BO6 octahedra. The orientation of the corner-sharing BO6 octahedra in GFMO can be depicted by the Glazer system a−a−c+ which demonstrates that the octahedra turn along the Cartesian axes x and y in the consecutive layers in the inverse directions, and along the z-axis in the same direction.68 The refined unit cell parameters, the ionic positions and the isothermal parameters of the constituent ions of GFMO are listed in Table 1. The obtained lattice parameters a, b and c are given as a function of Mn content. The lattice constants a and c decrease with the increase in Mn content while the lattice constant b increases with the increase in Mn. As a result, there is a small decrease in the unit cell volume with the increase in Mn. It is observed from Table 1 that the decrease of the lattice parameter a, is lesser than the lattice parameter c and up to 30% doping of Mn at the Fe site; all the lattice parameters follow the condition a < c/√2 < b which confirms that the compound GdFe1−xMnxO3 exhibits O-type orthorhombic structure.69,70 The reliability factors, the bond lengths and the angles of Fe/Mn–(OI,OII)–Fe/Mn calculated from the refined structural parameters of GFMO are also given in Table 1. From the refinement data we have seen that with increase in the Mn concentration at the B-site of the material, the bond angle is increased which means that the distortion is getting less with the Mn concentration.

B. Raman spectra

The role of the lattice distortions in the interplay of the various physical properties of the perovskite oxides is widely recognized. Raman spectroscopy can provide a significant additional information on the distortions and their variations with both the temperature and the cations at the A and B sites. The ideal perovskite with a cubic (Pm3m) unit cell has no Raman-active modes. But due to the loss of translational symmetries or for a less symmetric crystal structure the Raman lines are observed. The symmetry reduction in these materials can be considered as a combination of four basic distortions: an out of phase tilting of the neighbouring BO6 octahedra around the cubic [101] axis D[101] an in-phase tilting around the cubic [010] axis D[010], a Jahn–Teller distortion of the octahedral DJT and a shift of the R3+ cations DR.71–75 With respect to the parent cubic perovskite phase Pm3m, the Pnma structure in orthoferrites can be derived using the octahedral rotations. In contrast to the Pnma coordinate axis orientation, the Pbnm orientation is also used in some cases with the axis inter-conversion code from Pnma to Pbnm (a → b, b → c and c → a) and the notations of the phonon normal modes of the B symmetry are B1g ↔ B2g. The a- and c-axes of the orthorhombic Pbnm unit cell are obtained from the prototype Pnma cell by a 45° rotation of the cubic [100] and [001] axes around the [010] cubic axis and multiplication by a factor , while the orthorhombic b axis is obtained by multiplying the cubic [010] axis by a factor of 2.76,77 The Raman spectra of GFMO are measured in the frequency range of 50–800 cm−1 and are shown in Fig. S1(a)–(c) (ESI†). The identification of the microphases in GFMO requires a knowledge of the spectra of the two parent compounds, GdFeO3 and GdMnO3.78,79 The observed experimental Raman spectra of GFMO are de-convoluted using a number of Lorentzian profiles. As observed from the XRD analysis that GFMO crystallizes in the Pbnm (D162h) symmetry, the Fe/Mn ion should occupy the 4b Wyckoff-site of the Ci symmetry, the Gd and O(1) ions occupy the 4c sites of the CXZS symmetry and O(2) occupies the 8d sites of the general C1 symmetry. The irreducible representation of the D162h point group obtained from the site group symmetry is given by:80–83

Thus, from the group theoretical analysis, the Pbnm symmetry allows 24 Raman active phonon modes. These phonon modes correspond to the vibrations of the Gd3+ cations (2Ag ⊕ Au ⊕ B2g ⊕ 2B2u ⊕ 2B1g ⊕ B1u ⊕ B3g ⊕ 2B3u), the oxygen anions (5Ag ⊕ 4Au ⊕ 4B2g ⊕ 5B2u ⊕ 5B1g ⊕ 4B1u ⊕ 4B3g ⊕ 5B3u) and the Fe3+/Mn3+ ions (3Au ⊕ 3B1u ⊕ 3B2u ⊕ 3B3u) in GFMO.

Schematically, the phonon modes below 200 cm−1 are mainly characterized by the displacement of the heavy rare-earth ion Gd. Above 300 cm−1 the motions of the light oxygen ions dominate and in the inter mediate frequencies the phonon modes are characterized by the vibrations of both the cations. In the present study, the Fe3+/Mn3+ ions occupy centres of inversion in the Pbnm structure, and therefore the characteristic phonon vibrations involving Fe3+/Mn3+ remain Raman inactive. The Raman spectra of GFMO varying with x are identified and indexed. The remainder of the predicted modes are either masked by the band overlap or their intensities remain low due to the polycrystalline nature of the materials.

In the case of pure GdFeO3, multiple Raman active bands are visible which correspond to the 〈Fe–O(1)–Fe〉 bond angle, and Fe–O1, Fe–O2 and 〈Gd–O〉 bond lengths. As the Mn-doping increases the Raman modes undergo shift in the frequencies. Also the peak broadening results from the lattice disorder due to the incorporation of Mn at the Fe-site. The band between 600 cm−1 and 645 cm−1 presents some peculiar characteristics. The frequency seems to be independent of the doping concentration of Mn in GdFeO3, besides showing strong asymmetry. As can be seen from Table 2, the calculations predict two bands of B1g and B3g symmetry in this region. Since, all the seven Ag modes are conclusively identified and indexed within 500 cm−1 no Raman active Ag mode is present between 600 cm−1 and 645 cm−1. We therefore conclude that this is not a first order Raman mode. A precise interpretation of this band is beyond the scope of this work. But we note that similar features have been shown to exist for other rare earth orthoferrites78 and also for other perovskite oxides83 with unclear assignments and conflicting reports.

C. Electrical conduction mechanism

The temperature and the frequency dependent ac conductivity of GFMO samples is represented in Fig. 4(a–c). In general the ac conductivity is analysed to understand the nature of the conduction mechanism followed by the materials. The frequency dependent ac conductivity is originated due to the free as well as the bound charge carriers. When the ac conductivity is decreasing with the increase in temperature, the free charge carriers are responsible for the conduction mechanism. In our case the conductivity is increasing with the increase of temperature which is due to the bound charge carriers. As the conductivity depends on temperature, the conduction process is thermally dependent. Due to the increase in temperature, the bound charge carriers are released from the electronic trap centres in the materials which causes the conductivity to increase. For a particular temperature the frequency dependent conductivity spectrum is divided into two regions: the first region which lies in the lower frequency side is frequency independent. This plateau region when extrapolated towards the zero frequency gives the dc conductivity value. The second frequency dependent region in the high frequency side is called the dispersion region. In this region the conductivity isotherms tend to get superimposed with each other. All the experimental conductivity data are fitted with the Jonscher's power law defined as:84
 
σ = σdc[1 + (ω/ωH)n](1)
where σdc represents the value of dc conductivity, ωH is the hopping frequency which divides the plateau and the dispersion regions of the conductivity spectrum and n represents the degree of interaction between the mobile ions and the lattices.

Since the material is an orthoferrite material, the conduction mechanism in this type of material occurs due to the hopping of the delocalized electrons between the available localized sites. In this material, the 3d electrons are split into less stable eg electrons and more stable t2g electrons due to the electrostatic interaction. The electron density in the eg level is represented by the equation ne = n0exp(eV/kT), where ne is the electron density at any temperature T (K), n0 is the electron density at zero temperature, e is the electron charge and V is the potential applied. With the increase in temperature the hopping probability of the electrons is increased between the localized sites and hence the conductivity increases.85,86 It is also observed from Fig. 4(a–c) that with the increase in Mn concentration, the conductivity increases. The Mn doping at the B site of GFO introduces some extra conduction paths between the grains through which the electrons are conducted.87 Moreover the increase in the Mn concentration reduces the deviation of the bond angle (Fe/Mn–O–Fe/Mn) from the ideal 180° and therefore reduces the octahedral distortion. This results in the increase of the overlap of the d-orbitals of Fe/Mn with the p-orbital of O which also assists to increase the conductivity.88

The dc conductivity values are plotted in Fig. 4(d) against the inverse of temperature according to the Arrhenius equation σdc = σ0exp(Ea/kT) [where, σ0 is the pre exponential factor, k is the Boltzmann constant and Ea is the activation energy for conduction]. The dc conductivity data are fitted linearly to derive the activation energy (presented in Fig. 4(d)). The obtained values of the activation energy are found to be 0.43 ± 0.01, 0.45 ± 0.01, and 0.42 ± 0.01 eV for GFO, GFMO1, and GFMO3, respectively. It is known that if the value of activation energy lies in between 0 and 0.2 eV, the n-type polaronic hopping takes place whereas a value between 0.2 and 0.9 eV indicates the p-type polaronic hopping.89 So, the obtained values of the activation energy for the GFMO materials indicate that the conductivity mechanism is due to the p-type polaronic hopping between the available localized sites.

J. Density of states calculations

The spin-polarized DOS of GFMO3 obtained from GGA calculation is shown in Fig. S7 (ESI†). No energy gap is observed in the up spin channel at a Fermi level set at 0 eV, whereas an energy gap of 2.82 eV is present in the down spin channel between the O-p states in the VB maxima and the Fe-d states in the CB minima. In GFMO3, Mn is in the 3+ oxidation states with the electronic configuration [Ar] 3d4, thus the partially field d-states of Mn contribute at the Fermi level in the up spin channel. To open up an energy gap at the Fermi level in the up spin channel we have used an orbital potential term U with mBJ calculations. The total DOS and the PDOS obtained from the mBJ+U calculations for GFMO3 are shown in Fig. 13. It is observed that an energy gap of 1.15 eV is opened between the O-p states at the VB and Mn-d states at the CB in the up spin channel. As well as in the down spin channel 4.2 eV energy gap is present between the O-p states at the VB and the Mn-d states at the CB. The analysis of the PDOS spectra indicates that in the up spin channel the VB extended from 0 eV to −10 eV is mainly consists of the Gd-f, Fe-d, Mn-d and O-p states. On the other hand in the down spin channel the major contribution of the d-states of Fe and Mn and the f-states of Gd appears in the CB extended from 1.15 eV to 8 eV. The outer electrons from Gd, Fe and Mn atoms are transferred to complete the 2p shell of the oxygen atoms, resulting in the nominal chemical formula of Gd3+Fe0.73+Mn0.33+O32−. The over-lapping of the O-p states and the d-states of Fe and Mn in the VB suggests the strong hybridization of the O-p states with the d-states of Fe and Mn. In GFMO3 the electronic configuration of Gd3+ is [Xe]4f7 thus the half-filled f-states of Gd reside in both the VB and the CB. In the up spin channel the bonding state of Gd-4f7 is completely filled and lies in deep VB around 7.5 eV whereas the anti-bonding state lies in the CB. Both the bonding and the anti-bonding states of Gd-4f7 lie in the CB at the down spin channel. The splitting of the 3d states of Fe and Mn is due to the combined effects of the crystal-field, the exchange, and the Jahn–Teller (JT) distortion. The JT distortion of the O octahedron in this material can be viewed as the t2g3eg2 and t2g3eg1 electronic configuration of Fe and Mn, respectively. The Jahn–Teller effect is usually important for an odd occupancy level of eg and it is very week in Fe3+ (t32ge2g). Moreover due to the orthorhombic crystal structure of GFMO3 all the t2g (dxy dyz dzx) and eg (dx2−y2 dz2) states are non-degenerated and hence the d-states of Fe and Mn are extended in a wide energy range in the VB and the CB. It is observed from the PDOS spectra that the Mn-d states are present in between the O-p states in the VB and the Fe-d states in the CB and hence the doping of Mn in GFO reduces the band gap which is in good agreement with the experimental results. These Mn-d states can act as the trapping centre for the charge carrier which facilitates the migration of the photogenerated electrons; as a result, the photocatalytic efficiency increases with the increase of Mn content.

In general the opposite alignment of the spins of the individual magnetic species gives rise to anti-ferromagnetism in the materials. With the increase in Mn concentration, the number of Fe ions decreases in GFMO which may oppose the cancellation of all the Fe spins due to the unpaired Fe ions. Thus the unpaired Fe or Mn ions in GFMO result in the increase of the magnetization with Mn doping. The magnetic separation of the materials has also been examined by using a magnet placed at the bottom of the glass bottle. The black samples are strongly attracted by the magnet which indicates that the samples are magnetically separable from the solution. This experiment denotes that this kind of magnetically separable material having higher photocatalytic efficiency is highly desirable for environmental remediation.

IV. Conclusions

All the materials under investigation are synthesized by the sol gel method. The Rietveld refinement of the XRD profiles suggests that all the materials have an orthorhombic Pbnm crystal structure as that of the parent compounds GdFeO3 and GdMnO3. The Mn doping at the Fe site of GdFeO3 shows particle size reduction with the dopant concentration which effectively enhances the photocatalytic efficiency of the material. The UV-vis spectra reveal the band gap reduction with the increase in Mn concentration which helps to absorb a wide range of visible light and hence increases the photocatalytic efficiency. The band gap reduction with Mn doping is also verified from the DFT calculations. The Mn doping at the Fe site reduces the band gap by forming a midgap between the Fermi level and the conduction band. It is also seen that Mn doping reduces the recombination rate of the carriers and hence more separated electrons and holes are created. These electrons and holes produced more hydroxyl radicals and super oxide anions which degrade the pollutant. The XPS study reveals that with the increase in the Mn content, the Mn3+/Mn4+ ratio increases and Fe3+/Fe2+ ratio decreases. This observation supports the proposed charge transfer theory. Since the increase of Mn doping produces more trivalent and tetravalent states, the Fe3+ ions may transfer to Fe2+ and Mn3+ goes to the Mn4+ state to maintain the charge neutrality. This charge transfer produces more hopping of the charge carriers between Fe3+ ↔ Fe2+ which reduces the resistance and hence the dielectric constant of the material increases. GFMO3 shows the highest photocatalytic efficiency and degrades the Rh-B pollutant in just 25 min under the visible light illumination which is much lower than the degradation time of pure GFO. Furthermore, due to the nearly same ionic radius of Mn3+ and Fe3+, GFMO materials have good structural stability and provide reliable recycling photocatalytic performance. The reusability experiment shows that GFMO3 can be reused for five successive cycles which is also crucial for the further applications of this photocatalyst in the industry.
