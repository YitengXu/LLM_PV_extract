Abstract

A general methodology has been proposed for the straightforward access to 4,8-functionalized benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophenes (BDTs) via Pd mediated coupling reactions including Suzukiâ€“Sonogashira coupling and carbonâ€“sulfur bond formation reactions. This versatile platform can be used to construct a library of BDT core centred conjugated systems, featuring large fused-ring structure and good charge mobility, where a hole mobility of 0.061 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 is demonstrated. With the energy level fine-tuned with functionalization, the charge transporting BDTs show great potential for donorâ€“acceptor polymers.

To date, benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene (BDT) has been proved to be one of the most important fused-ring structured building blocks in the design of conjugated polymer donors for bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells due to its high mobility and lower highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level.1â€“4 As a donating moiety, BDT has been chosen for the following merits: (a) the fused BDT ring structure allows the incorporation of substituents on the central benzene core, while also maintaining the planarity of the two thiophene units; (b) its structural symmetry and the rigid fused aromatic system could enhance the electron delocalization and interchain interactions to improve the charge mobility and eliminate the need to control the regioregularity during the polymerization process;5 (c) it would maintain a low HOMO energy level of the resulting polymers as demonstrated by donor-strong and acceptor-weak polymers.6 Kobmehl et al. first synthesized 4,8-dimethoxybenzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene with benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene-4,8-dione as the precursor using a two-step reaction protocol (Scheme 1).7 Several extended methods were then explored for the synthesis of alkyl, alkenyl and aromatic ring substituted BDT derivatives.8,9

The BDT derived polymers were first developed as donors for BHJ solar cells by Hou et al. in 2008.9 In the meantime, many research groups showed strong interest in these fascinating building blocks, and a large spectrum of BDT derived donors have been developed since then by introducing various substituents including alkyl, alkylthienyl, and alkyl phenylethynyl to the benzene core of BDT.3b,10 Particularly, the ethynylene and thienyl-substituted BDT derived linear or two-dimensional (2D) small molecules and conjugated polymers exhibited promising performance in BHJ solar cells. For example, small molecules and polymer based ethynylene-containing benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene derivatives displayed low HOMO levels and an excellent hole mobility up to 1.1â€“1.2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 (on/off current ratio âˆ¼ 107) in field effect transistors (FETs).2b,d The blend of the 4,8-triisopropylsilylethynyl functionalized BDT polymer and PC71BM in 1:2 weight ratio delivered a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 6% in BHJ cells.11 A PCE of 8.13% was even achieved from 2D small molecules with a bithienyl-substituted BDT donor, which performed much better than its thiophene-substituted BDT analogues.12 The open circuit voltage (Voc) is directly proportional to the HOMO level of the electron donor.13 The lower the HOMO level of the donor, the higher the Voc. Thus, the high mobility and low HOMO level are prerequisites to achieve high-performance solar cells.6a,13,14

To achieve air-stable and high charge-transporting donor materials, the design and preparation of 2D BDTs by attaching conjugated substituents onto the central benzene core to fine-tune the energy levels and facilitate regular Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking of conjugated molecules and polymer backbones is considered to be a critical issue in the development of high-performance solar cells.12,15 However, the previous methods have certain drawbacks: (i) the synthesis of BDT derivatives with the n-BuLi/SnCl2 method has a limited scope for the substituents that may be introduced;9 (ii) the use of the Grignard method16 for the preparation of acetylene-substituted BDTs instead of lithiation only work for limited phenyl substituents with low yields; (iii) larger fused aromatic substituents like thio[3,4-bâ€²]thiophene could not be accessed with any methods in the literature, probably due to steric hindrance.

In conjunction with the development of new donors for solar cells, we are keen to search for more general synthesis approaches to expand novel Ï€-extended BDT-based donor materials. Herein we report a versatile method (Scheme 2) for the synthesis of a library of amazing compounds using 4,8-triflated BDT as the precursor for the first time. Aryl triflates have frequently been used as electrophiles in coupling reactions.17 By introducing trifluoromethanesulfonyloxy groups on 4,8-positions of BDT as leaving groups, a library of 4,8-functionalized BDT derivatives have been successfully prepared via Pd mediated coupling reactions including Suzuki and Sonogashira coupling. Importantly, the straightforward access to dialkylthio BDTs can be easily achieved with high yields (â‰¥90%) through one-step palladium-catalyzed coupling reaction. Dialkylthio substituted BDTs were reported to be synthesized with a two-step protocol, where a Newmanâ€“Kwart rearrangement under 245 Â°C heating is the key step.18 Excitingly, the Suzuki coupling can be successfully extended for the synthesis of conjugated polymers. This effective strategy provides a versatile platform to construct 2D conjugated systems centred on the BDT core, featuring large fused-ring structure and good planarity. The new BDT derivatives developed with this approach, we believe, will find great potential in the design of new molecular/polymeric semiconductors for organic electronics.

To the best of our knowledge, the larger the conjugated ring directly linked to the core in the cross-coupling, the more difficult it is to achieve it. Hence, the coupling with fused thio[3,4-bâ€²]thiophene (TT)19 and bithiophene gave 8 and 9 (entries 5â€“6) in relatively low yields (55% and 60%). It should be noted that 8 was first prepared with BDT triflate, since the incorporation of a TT ring using n-BuLi was found to be inaccessible. The results indicated the applicability of this protocol for the incorporation of relatively large substituents to 4,8-positions of BDTs. We extended this approach to other aromatic rings. For instance, the coupling with 2-furanboronic acid afforded 10 (entry 7) in 81% yield. And the synthesis of BDTs with phenyl or its analogues with fluorine and trifluoromethyl substituents (11â€“13) was successfully achieved with a yield of 90, 81 and 82% (entries 8â€“10), respectively. It should be noted that aryl boronic acids were more reactive than the corresponding Bpin esters in Suzuki cross-coupling reactions and the electron-withdrawing strength of substituents had a positive effect on the yield of couplings with phenyl analogues.

The BDT triflate was also explored for Sonogashira cross-coupling reactions with acetylene substituents (Scheme 3). The reaction proceeded smoothly with 10 mol% [PdCl2(PPh3)2] and 20 mol% CuI as catalysts in the DMFâ€“Et3N (2:1, v/v) solvent mixture under heating at 100 Â°C for 16 h. The coupling between 3 and 2.5 equiv. of 1-octyne, or 4-methyl-phenyl-acetylene gave the corresponding products 15 and 16 (entries 12 and 13) with a yield of 80 and 88%, respectively. We were pleased to note that our protocol can also be used for the preparation of BDT thiols from the alkyl/aryl thiol substrates with BDT triflate (entries 14â€“16). The BDT sulfides 17â€“19 (Scheme 3) were successfully prepared via transition-metal-catalyzed cross-coupling transformation using Pd2(dba)3 and the Xantphos phosphine ligand without formation of their disulfide compounds.20 Octylmercaptan, dodecylmercaptan and benzylmercaptan were S-arylated in excellent yields of 90%, 91% and 90%, respectively.

We are also interested in the developing protocols that allow the preparation of conjugated polymers from BDT triflate 3 and aryl boronic ester. Fluorene is the most frequently used moiety for organic electronics.14,21 Hence, a Suzuki cross-coupling between BDT triflate 3 and fluorene diboronic ester was explored in a biphasic mixture of aqueous K2CO3 and toluene with Pd(PPh3)4 as the catalyst and tetrabutylammonium bromide as the phase transfer catalyst (Scheme 3). The polymer P1 was obtained with good solubility in organic solvents (ca. CHCl3, THF, and dichlorobenzene) and a high molecular weight (Mw = 21.4 kDa, Mw/Mn = 1.81), a value comparable to those of polyfluorenes prepared with arylbromide.19b

In order to evaluate the applicability of the as-prepared BDT monomers as donating units in the development of donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) alternating narrow bandgap polymers for solar cells, the optical and electrochemical properties of the as-prepared BDT monomers (4â€“19) were further studied. The UV-vis absorption spectra of monomers 4â€“19 in both CHCl3 solutions and thin films are shown in Fig. S39.â€  All spectra feature two distinct absorption peaks, with the one at 300â€“450 nm assigned to the delocalized Ï€â€“Ï€* transition between the BDT core and the attached side chains and the other beyond 300 nm corresponding to the incorporated side chains. For electron-rich thiophene analogues (4â€“9), a relatively large red-shift absorption was observed for the films compared to the solutions. Interestingly, in the case of 4 and 9, by changing the single thiophene unit to the bithiophene unit vertical to the core of BDT, the low-energy absorbance increased in intensity and a larger red-shift was observed. The reason was that the resonance structure in BDT increased the double-bond character of the single bonds in 9 and this consequent reduction of the bond-length alternation effectively modified the absorption behaviours of BDT.22 When comparing the UV-vis spectra of 8 with that of 4, the larger planar structure of the thio[3,4-bâ€²]thiophene unit was the so-called â€œprequinoidâ€ monomer and the quinoid resonance form is lower in energy than the single thiophene form. The stabilized quinoid form effectively reduces the bandgap of related conjugated systems.3a,21,23 As expected, the thio[3,4-bâ€²]thiophene substituted BDT monomer 8 exhibited red shifted absorption than 4 in both solution (15 nm) and the film (nearly 20 nm). By attaching trimethylsilyl or octyl thiophene, the resulting BDT monomers 5 and 6 exhibited similar absorption spectra with 4, indicating that alkyl chains had a negligible effect on the electronic structures of BDT.24

For 10, the film with an onset absorption (Î»edge) of 490 nm displayed 55 nm bathochromic shift than its solution, indicating an increased degree of BDT aggregation in the solid state. Compared with monomer 11, it was found that the anchoring of fluorine or trifluoromethyl groups at 4,8-positions of the BDT core for 12 and 13 had no distinct effect on the electronic structures. The phenylacetylenated 16 exhibited the widest absorption band, with a Î»edge extended to 500 nm. And a remarkable red-shifted absorption (âˆ¼80 nm) was observed in the film when comparing with its dilute solution, suggesting J-aggregation as the dominant packing mode in the solid state.25 Monomers 17, 18 and 19 exhibited about 5 nm red-shifted absorption than the reference 4,8-didodeoxyl BDT (BDT-O-C12H25). This indicates that stronger Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking between BDT and the diarylthio or dialkylthio substituent occurs than that in dialkoxy substituted BDT analogues.

The HOMO energy level of BDT monomers is found to be dependent upon the electron-donating substituents on the BDT core. For monomers with relatively electron-deficient phenyl analogues, the HOMO energy levels were estimated to be âˆ’5.55, âˆ’5.56 and âˆ’5.58 eV, respectively, for 11â€“13. According to the electrochemical studies discussed above, the electron-deficiency of the screened substituents can be arranged by the order: thiophene analogues < ethynylene analogues < phenyl analogues. The poorer the aryl substituent, the lower the HOMO level. Amazingly, most of BDT monomers exhibit a HOMO level close to the HOMO of â€œidealâ€ polymers for high-efficiency solar cells (ca. âˆ’5.4 eV).13 Though there are arguments on the inheritance of the HOMO level of Dâ€“A polymers from the electron-rich monomers,28 the closeness of the HOMO level of BDT monomers to that of the â€œidealâ€ polymer donors in a necessarily desirable situation will help in finding matchable electron-deficient monomers to obtain new Dâ€“A polymers for high-performance solar cells.

In order to demonstrate the electronic structures as well as molecular energy levels of the BDT monomers, quantum chemistry calculations were performed by using the density functional theory (DFT) on the B3LYP/6-31G level.26 As shown in Fig. 2, these four units show planar structures and the Ï€-electrons are well delocalized along their backbones, however, the HOMO and LUMO levels are different. The bandgaps of 5, 8 and 9 are 3.75 eV, 3.58 eV and 3.37 eV, respectively, indicating that the bandgap becomes smaller with increase of the conjugation length of substituents. The HOMO level of 17 is âˆ’5.61 eV, implying that the introduction of sulfur as the heteroatom affects the molecular energy levels of fused aromatic systems distinctly.27 The results from DFT calculations clearly show the similarity/difference of the energy levels of the frontier orbitals as well as the molecular electronic structures among 5, 8, 9 and 17.

It was amazing that negligible variation in structures of monomers led to prodigious differences in the XRD, AFM and FET devices. The correlation study of structureâ€“property is still under progress and the optimized FET data will be reported in due course. The as-prepared BDTs exhibited low HOMO levels close to that of an â€œidealâ€ polymer as well as high hole mobilities. According to the strategy of weak donorâ€“strong acceptor to design ideal polymers for organic solar cells,28a if suitable strong acceptors, such as diketopyrrolopyrrole,29 benzooxadiazole30 and naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c]bisthiadiazole,31,32 would be selected to construct alternating low bandgap copolymers via internal charge transfer with as-developed BDT monomers, we believe, new Dâ€“A polymers with suitable energy level alignment and high hole mobilities would be developed to achieve high-performance solar cells with optimized device engineering.

In summary, we have presented here a versatile method for the 4,8-functionalization of benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene via palladium-catalyzed Suzuki and Sonogashira couplings as well as carbonâ€“sulfur bond formation reaction. Using this methodology, a library of 4,8-functionalized benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene monomers have been developed with aryl, ethynyl, arylethynyl, alkylthio and arylthio substituents. The absorption and energy levels of BDT monomers can be fine-tuned by adjusting the donating strength of the incorporated substituents. The as-prepared BDT monomers exhibit a bandgap ranging from 2.48 to 3.34 eV and a relatively low HOMO energy level. The high crystalline nature and hole mobility of BDT monomers demonstrated for 5 and 17 shows that manipulating the functionalization of BDT core can result in prodigious differences in charge transport properties. High FET mobilities of 0.061 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and 0.025 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 were readily achieved for 5 and 17. The as-developed BDT monomers may be promising donating units for constructing Dâ€“A narrow bandgap polymers for high-performance solar cells and p-type FETs if high mobility co-monomers are used and the resulted polymers have highly ordered structures.
