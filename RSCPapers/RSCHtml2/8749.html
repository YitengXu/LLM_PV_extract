Abstract

In this work, binder-free electrodes of CuS@Ni(OH)2 were fabricated by hydrothermal and electrodeposition methods. X-ray diffraction (XRD) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) results confirmed the crystallinity of the electrodes. Nanosheet-like morphology and surface modification of CuS were confirmed by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images. The actual composition of the coreâ€“shell-like hybrids was identified by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis. The thickness of the electrodes was measured by atomic force microscopy (AFM) imaging. Cyclic voltammetry results revealed the redox nature and improved electrochemical activity with shifted redox peaks. The ionic conductivity of the coreâ€“shell hybrids was much higher than that of the pristine CuS. CuS@Ni(OH)2 â€“ 5 minutes delivered an ultra-high specific capacitance of 3585 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1. The retained capacitance of CuS@Ni(OH)2 â€“ 7 minutes was 465 F gâˆ’1 (59%) in the 4000th cycle. The above results endorse that the coreâ€“shell-like hybrids of CuS@Ni(OH)2 satisfy the demands of supercapacitor applications.

1. Introduction

Very recently, investigators have aimed to design high-efficiency, clean energy storage and conversion devices. It is obligatory to satisfy the needs of the rapidly growing portable electronic device field.1 Usually, the performance of the electronic devices depends on the function-cum-abilities of their key components. Among the key components, the electrode material plays a vital role in deciding the capabilities of the electronic devices.2 Nowadays, the supercapacitor is the most intensely investigated energy storage and conversion device because it works in between batteries and fuel cells and has the merits of both. Supercapacitors provide long cycle life, better safety, high power density, and higher energy density than capacitors.3 Supercapacitors can be classified into three types: (1) electrical double-layer capacitors, which function based on charge separation at the electrode surface, for example graphene and carbon-based materials;4 (2) pseudocapacitors, which work based on the intercalation/deintercalation of electrolyte ions with partial redox process, for example conductive polymers and metal oxides;5,6 (3) battery-type supercapacitors, with strong redox peaks (faradaic process), for example metal sulfides, metal oxides etc.7 Usually, supercapacitors have very high power density, fast cycling with stability, etc., but suffer from poor energy density. The drawbacks of supercapacitors are overcome by battery-like supercapacitor materials because they have faster surface redox reactions.

In recent years, transition metal sulfides have emerged as potential materials in a variety of applications such as solar cells, catalysis, sensors, batteries, and supercapacitors.8 They are suitable candidates for battery-like supercapacitor applications because they have lower electronegativity and are more highly electrochemically active than conventional metal oxides.9 Various naturally occurring layered and non-layered metal sulfides have been reported as having supercapacitor applications. Among them, CuS is a widely attractive material in a variety of applications. It has a narrow band gap energy of 2.0 eV, and good electronic conductivity, and is environmentally benign, easy to prepare, cheaper and naturally available in the Earth's crust.10 So far, different morphologies of CuS such as nanoneedles,11 hierarchical structures,12 nanoplatelets,13 nanowires,14 microspheres,15 3D hierarchical microspheres,16 nanosheets,17 nanowalls,18 nanoribbons,19 flower-like,20 nanostructures,21 nanoparticles, nanofibers,22 nanoflower,10,23 nanospheres,24 nano hollow spheres,25 and double-shell nanocages3 have been carefully developed for supercapacitor applications. As well as the various morphologies of CuS, composites of CuS with carbon nanotubes (CNTs),11 reduced graphene oxide (rGO),20,26â€“28 polypyrrole,29 bacterial cellulose,30 carbon quantum dots,31 Cu(OH)232 and Ni3S233 have been reported for improvement of supercapacitor applications. Still, some of the problems are unsolved. Further attention is needed to improve the capacitance of CuS.

Among the various metal hydroxides, Ni(OH)2 is a good candidate for electrochemical applications owing to its merits of high theoretical capacitance, environment friendliness, low cost, different morphologies, etc. Moreover, the layered structure of Ni(OH)2 is helpful in providing a more active site for the dissemination of electrolyte ions. Ni(OH)2 thus develops the electrochemical performance of these materials. However, the poor electronic conductivity of Ni(OH)2 effectively hinders the improvement of capacitance in practical uses.

Nowadays, metal sulfide/metal hydroxide materials with coreâ€“shell-like hybrid structure are long-established supercapacitor materials.34 They have provided a wealth of electrochemically active materials directly grown on the surface of the substrate, which offer the benefits of faster cycling, improved capacitance, and better rate capability compared with the pure metal sulfides. To our knowledge, there have been few reports about nickel hydroxide-modified metal sulfides such as Co9S8@Ni(OH)2,35 NiCo2S4@Ni(OH)236 and Ni3S2@Ni(OH)237 as electrode materials for supercapacitors. Herein, we present results for electrodeposited Ni(OH)2@CuS as an electrode material for supercapacitor applications.

In this present study, we have designed and fabricated a CuS (core) and Ni(OH)2 shell-like hybrid for use in binder-free electrodes for ultra-high-performance supercapacitors. Coreâ€“shell-like hybrids of CuS@Ni(OH)2 â€“ 5 minutes delivered a capacitance of 3585 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1 with improved cycle stability in the 4000th cycle.

2. Experimental

2.1 Synthesis of CuS nanosheets

In a typical synthesis, 0.1486 g of CuSO4Â·6H2O and 1.0852 g of urea were dissolved in 70 ml of Millipore water, and the solution was transferred into a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave. A piece of precleaned Ni-Foam (1 Ã— 1 cm2) was added to this solution inside the autoclave. The reaction was heated at 150 Â°C for 12 h in a hot air oven. After cooling to room temperature, the CuO-covered Ni-Foam was washed several times with water and ethanol and dried at 80 Â°C for 12 h. The CuO-covered Ni-Foam was placed in a solution of 10 mmol of sodium sulfide in 50 ml Millipore water for an hour. During this sulfidation process, the CuO covering the Ni-Foam was completely converted into CuS. After that, the Cuâ€“S-covered Ni-Foam was washed in water and dried at 80 Â°C for 12 h. The mass loading of CuS was 2.5 mg for each electrode.

2.2 Electrodeposition of Ni(OH)2

Electrodeposition of Ni(OH)2 was carried out using a three-electrode system, which consisted of Ag/AgCl, Pt, and CuS@Ni-Foam as reference, counter, and working electrodes respectively, in an Autolab instrument. In this process, 10 mmol of NiCl2Â·6H2O and 0.1 mol of KNO3 in 50 ml of Millipore water were used as the electrolyte. Chronoamperometry was performed at âˆ’0.9 V for different time intervals of 3, 5, and 7 minutes. The prepared electrodes were named CS, CS-3, CS-5, and CS-7 for 0, 3, 5, and 7 minutes of Ni(OH)2 electrodeposition on the electrodes. The mass loading of the electrodes was 2.6, 2.8, and 3.0 mg for CS-3, CS-5, and CS-7, respectively. During this process, nitrate ions reacted with water molecules to produce hydroxyl ions, which combined with nickel cations to deposit at the surface of the working electrode as Ni(OH)2. 3. Results and discussion

3.1 Fabrication of CuS@Ni(OH)2

Fig. 1 displays a schematic representation for the preparation of CuS@Ni(OH)2 electrodes. In this first step, CuO-covered Ni-Foam was prepared by a hydrothermal method; this was followed by sulfidation of the CuO. During the sulfidation process, anionic exchange reactions took place and replaced the oxygen in CuO by the sulfide anion in the sodium sulfide solution. The yellow color of the sodium sulfide solution disappeared completely, which ensured the completion of the sulfidation process, which produced CuS. The final step indicates the surface modification of CuS by Ni(OH)2 using the chronoamperometric method (electrodeposition). During this electrodeposition process, nickel hydroxide was uniformly coated onto the surface of the CuS.

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) analysis was used to study the kinetics of the electrochemical reactions occurring at the electrode surface. The frequency range of 100 kHz to 0.1 Hz and an applied amplitude of 5 mV sâˆ’1 were used. EIS plots were fitted with an equivalent circuit (inset in Fig. 8). In the fitted equivalent circuit, the components Rs, Rct, CPE, Cdl, and Cf represent the internal resistance, charge transfer resistance, constant phase element, double-layer capacitance, and faradaic capacitance, respectively. The absence of a semicircle in the Nyquist plots indicates that improved charge transfer kinetics (lower Rct value) were attained by direct growth of CuS on the surface of Ni-Foam. In the high-frequency region, the slope at the intercept with the X-axis corresponds to the internal resistance of the system. The values of the internal resistance (Rs) of the electrodes were 0.55 Î© for Ni(OH)2, 0.83 Î© for CS, 0.72 Î© for CS-3, 0.64 Î© for CS-5 and 0.53 Î© for CS-7, which indicated that the longer the time used to electrodeposit Ni(OH)2 on the electrodes, the higher the penetration of electrolyte ions inside electrode surface.31 This points to the improved capacitance of the materials. The more vertical line in the low-frequency regions revealed the poor ionic resistance of the electrodes. These EIS results are good evidence for the improved current capability of Ni(OH)2-modified CuS electrodes.

Cycle stability of the electrodes was evaluated for practical applications up to 4000 continuous cycles at a current density of 20 A gâˆ’1, as depicted in Fig. 10. The cycle stability of CS, CS-3, CS-5, CS-7, and Ni(OH)2 electrodes was 58.3, 57.3, 56, 59 and 56.6% capacitance retention in the 4000th cycle, with capacitance values of 280, 315, 420, 465 and 340 F gâˆ’1, respectively. The capacitance value of the CS-7 in the 4000th cycle was much higher than the values for the other electrode materials. These results strongly proved that a higher amount of Ni(OH)2 loaded onto the electrodes gave better capacitance retention during the cycling process at a high current density of 20 A gâˆ’1. Overall, the electrochemical results clearly indicate the following: (1) the presence of highly conductive CuS core on the surface of Ni-Foam substrate reduces the charge transfer resistance, which is helpful for a faster charge/discharge process. (2) The surface modification of CuS by layered nickel hydroxide improves the wettability by electrolyte ions of the surface of the coreâ€“shell-like hybrids, and drastically improves the penetration by electrolyte ions.47 The nanosheet morphology of CuS@Ni(OH)2 produces a strong synergistic effect of the coreâ€“shell-like hybrids leading to high performances for supercapacitor applications.

4. Conclusions

In brief, we successfully fabricated coreâ€“shell-like hybrids of CuS@Ni(OH)2 on Ni-Foam for supercapacitor applications. Crystallinity and composition were confirmed by XRD, SAED, and XPS analysis. The nanometer thickness of the electrode materials was revealed by AFM images. Surface morphology of the electrodes was studied by SEM and TEM imaging. CV results revealed the redox behavior with improved current response. EIS results agree with the improved ionic conductivity of the coreâ€“shell-like hybrids. The obtained specific capacitance value of coreâ€“shell-like hybrid CS-5 was 3585 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1, with improved capacitance retention at 20 A gâˆ’1 (750 F gâˆ’1). The cycle stability of the CS-7 electrode was 59%, much higher than that of other electrodes, in the 4000th cycle. These results proved that the hybrids of CuS@Ni(OH)2 meet the demands of supercapacitor applications.
