Abstract

The search for highly effective and environmentally safe photocatalysts for water splitting and photovoltaic solar cells is essential for renewable solar energy conversion and storage. Based on first-principle calculations, we show that novel 2D Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) monolayer possesses excellent stability and great potential in solar energy conversion applications. Comprehensive studies show that the Î²-PdS2 monolayer exhibits semiconductor characteristics with an indirect gap, suitable band alignment, efficient carrier separation, and high solar to hydrogen (STH) efficiency, supporting its good photoelectronic performance. The surface catalytic and adsorption/intercalation energy calculation reveals that the photogenerated electrons have adequate driving forces to render hydrogen reduction half-reactions to proceed spontaneously and the ability to cover and incorporate water molecules on the Î²-PdS2 monolayer. Besides, the Î²-PdTe2 monolayer is a promising donor material for excitonic solar cells with high photovoltaic performance. More importantly, due to suitable donor band gap and small conduction band offset in the proposed type-II heterostructure, the power conversion efficiencies (PCE) were calculated up to â¼23% (Î²-PdTe2/WTe2), â¼21% (Î²-PdTe2/MoTe2) and â¼18% (Î²-PdTe2/Î²-PdS2), making it a promising candidate for solar energy conversion applications.

1. Introduction

Solar energy conversion technologies, such as photocatalytic water splitting and photovoltaic cells are demanding to produce clean energy and to reduce significant environmental and energy concerns. Since the realization of semiconductor photocatalyst TiO2 for solar water splitting,1 researchers have devoted significant efforts to explore effective photocatalysts capable of producing hydrogen energy.2â7 To achieve cost-effective hydrogen production, photocatalysts' solar to hydrogen (STH) efficiency should be at least 10% for large-scale production.8 The favorable hydrogen and oxygen evolution reaction (HER and OER), efficient separation with fast carrier migration and robust light-harvesting, leads to high STH efficiency. Also, the band gap must be equal or greater to 1.23 eV for a viable photocatalyst, and VBM and CBM appropriately straddling the redox potentials of the water. If these criteria are satisfied, water can be split without the need for any sacrificial agents.9 However, due to low quantum efficiency induced by charge recombination both on the surface and in the bulk of the catalysts, only a few show good photocatalytic activity for water splitting.10

In the last few decades, 2D materials have been suggested as promising solar energy harvesters due to high optical absorption, high carrier mobility and sufficient reactive sites.11â19 Unfortunately, many of them have poor long-term endurance and are effectively inactive for photocatalysis water splitting.9,20 Also, the significant energy loss produced by the high overpotential in HER and OER needs to be reduced. Therefore, novel 2D photocatalysts, particularly those with low overpotential, are of great interest for realizing the water-splitting reaction without the use of sacrificial reagents or co-catalysts.

Among 2D materials, transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) research has sparked a lot of interest in the creation of few-layer or monolayer nanosheets.21,22 These advancements bring new life to photocatalytic water splitting due to the fact that TMDs possess a high optical absorption efficiency, high carrier mobility, high charge transfer ability and a large number of catalytic sites.23â25 Despite the potential advantages of 2D chalcogenides for photocatalysis, the hydrogen yield from water splitting remains low due to the rapid recombination of photogenerated holes and electrons.26 TMDs with abundant active sites can enhance the efficiency to trigger the overall water splitting reactions.

Besides photocatalyst, the solar cell is also an essential renewable energy conversion technology that converts solar radiation directly into electricity. Solar cell materials with enhanced power conversion efficiency (PCE) have been investigated continually since the invention of the first operational solar cell in 1954.27 For van der Waals (vdW) heterostructure solar cells with a high PCE should meet two main criteria.28 First, acceptor and donor materials should have type II band alignments in the heterostructure. Second, the donor material's band gap should be between 0.90â1.70 eV to ensure effective light-harvesting performance. The light-harvesting performance, the carrier mobility and the separation efficiency of excitons also play essential roles in determining the PCE.29

Thus, 2D materials also have been regarded as promising candidates for excitonic solar cells (XSCs) due to their adjustable bandgap and good light-harvesting performance with high carrier mobility.30â32 The electronic characteristics of individual monolayers can be tuned by linking their band edges using a van der Waals (vdW) heterostructure generated by stacking distinct monolayer materials.33,34 Therefore, the fabrication of efficient type-II vdW heterostructures is considered promising for next-generation solar cell applications.

In this work, density functional theory (DFT) computations were performed to describe the photocatalytic water splitting and photovoltaic activity of Î²-PdS2 and Î²-PdTe2 monolayers, respectively. We performed molecular dynamics calculations to investigate these monolayers' thermodynamic and kinetic stabilities. Remarkably, the Î²-PdS2 monolayer has an indirect band gap (2.10 eV) with suitable band edge positions, and the potential to meet the requirement for redox potentials of photocatalysis. The adsorption/intercalation energies are obtained to check whether the coverage and incorporation of water molecules are energetically feasible or not. Furthermore, the Î²-PdTe2 monolayer exhibits an indirect band gap of â¼1.29 eV with suitable type-II vdW heterostructure alignments, suggesting that it could serve as suitable donor material for constructing 2D heterostructure as an excitonic solar cell. Significantly, the estimated PCE of Î²-PdTe2/Î²-PdS2, Î²-PdTe2/MoTe2 and Î²-PdTe2/WTe2 heterojunctions, can be as high as 17.79%, 20.59% and 23.14%, respectively.

2. Computational method

The calculated results were obtained by employing density functional theory (DFT) as the Quantum-ESPRESSO package.35,36 We employed the PerdewâBurkeâErnzerhof (PBE) approximation of the exchangeâcorrelation (XC) functional. The convergence criterion for the forces was set to 0.01 eV Ãâ1 and a norm-conserving pseudopotential with a 90 Ry energy cutoff. The first Brillouin zone (BZ) integration was performed with 24 Ã 24 Ã 1 k-point grid for MonkhorstâPack mesh. To break unphysical interactions between periodic system images, we inserted a large vacuum of 20 Ã perpendicular to the 2D structure. Moreover, the sophisticated HSE06 hybrid functional was also used for calculating the electronic structures.37,38 To include van der Waals interactions, we used Grimme's DFT-D2 approach for all heterostructure calculations.39 The AIMD simulations were performed using NosÃ© thermostat algorithm at different temperatures of 300 K, 500 K and 1000 K for a total of 5 ps with a time step of 3 fs using the SIESTA package.40

We used the G0W0 + BSE method as incorporated in the YAMBO code41 coupled with the Quantum-ESPRESSO package for optical absorbance. To achieve convergence, 200 bands were undertaken in the sum over the state for the correlation part of self-energy. To ensure the absorption spectrum converges, three valence and two conduction bands are used to solve the BetheâSalpeter equation and dielectric function. Moreover, to account for solvent effects in simulations, we used the implicit solvent model within the Environ code42 of Quantum ESPRESSO using the 78.3 dielectric constants for liquid water. The bulk Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) exhibits trigonal symmetry with P3121 space group.43 As shown in Fig. 1(a), the layers of Î²-PdX2 are stacked together along the z-axis through interlayer vdW bonding. As depicted in Fig. 1(c), the Ecl are 0.47 and 0.88 J mâ2 for Î²-PdS2 and Î²-PdTe2 monolayers, respectively. Remarkably, the calculated Ecl of Î²-PdS2 is quite comparable with 2D MoS2 (0.42 J mâ2),57 PdSeO3 (0.42 J mâ2)12 crystal that has been realized experimentally via exfoliation techniques. Furthermore, to examine the energetic stability of the Î²-PdX2 monolayer, we calculated the cohesive energy (ECoh), which is defined as ECoh = (EPd + EX â EPdX2)/3, where EPd, EX and EPdX2 are the total energy of a single Pd, X (X = S, Te) atom and total energy of PdX2 unit cell, respectively. The obtained cohesive energy of 4.0 eV per atom for Î²-PdS2 is higher than that of other 2D materials, such as silicene (3.98 eV per atom), whereas the calculated ECoh of Î²-PdTe2 (3.3 eV per atom) is comparable to that of black phosphorene (BP) (3.4 eV per atom).61 Our investigation demonstrates the high feasibility of exfoliating Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) monolayers from their bulk counterpart as free-standing monolayers.

3.3. Photocatalytic properties

To assess whether the potential of utilizing the Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) monolayer for photocatalytic water splitting, we further aligned the band edge positions with respect to vacuum level relative to that redox potential for hydrogen reduction (EH+/H2) and water oxidation (EO2/H2O) at pH = 0. The potential of the CBM (VBM) should be higher (lower) than the reduction level of hydrogen (oxidation level of water) for an overall water splitting photocatalyst. As shown in Fig. 4(a), the VBM and CBM of Î²-PdS2 monolayer surpass the standard oxidation (O2/H2O) and reduction (H+/H2) potential level, indicating sufficient activity of the Î²-PdS2 monolayer in both OER and HER processes. On the other hand, VBM of the Î²-PdTe2 monolayer is larger than the oxidation level of oxygen (â5.67 at pH = 0) (Fig. 4(b)), indicating that oxidation half-reaction is not possible in the Î²-PdTe2 monolayer.

To further assess the practical applications, the stability of photocatalysts in aqueous solution with illumination was also checked. 3.4. Î²-PdS2 as photocatalyst for water splitting

The presence of a suitable bandgap, band edge position and optical absorbance are not sufficient to imply that the Î²-PdS2 monolayer will act as a promising photocatalyst for overall water splitting. 4. Conclusions

In summary, using first-principles calculations, we propose the 2D Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) monolayers for efficient solar energy conversion applications. We systematically explored the stability, optoelectronic, photocatalytic and photovoltaic properties of the newly emerging Î²-PdX2 (X = S, Te) monolayers. The thermal and kinetic stability evaluated based on AIMD simulations and phonon dispersion, and the energetics based on cleavage and cohesive energy suggests their feasibility for experimental synthesis. The moderate carrier mobility and pronounced light absorption ability of the Î²-PdX2 monolayer make them a suitable candidate for energy conversion devices. The Î²-PdS2 monolayer shows a considerable band gap of 2.10 eV, and redox potential is within band edge position of VBM and CBM. Especially, the photogenerated electrons have adequate driving forces to render the hydrogen reduction half-reactions proceed spontaneously on the Î²-PdS2 monolayer at pH = 0 to 4. Besides, the additional external potential of 1.97 V for OER and 0.13 V for HER would be needed to trigger the whole sequence for Î²-PdS2 monolayer at pH = 7. Moreover, the adsorption/intercalation energies of Î²-PdS2 revealed the coverage and incorporation of water molecules are energetically feasible with high STH efficiency. In addition, the 2D Î²-PdTe2, as characterized by a moderate bandgap of 1.29 eV, is a suitable donor material for constructing the heterostructure as an excitonic solar cell. The proposed Î²-PdTe2/Î²-PdS2, Î²-PdTe2/MoTe2 and Î²-PdTe2/WTe2 heterojunctions can achieve a power conversion efficiency (PCE) up to 17.79%, 20.59% and 23.14%, respectively. It is expected that these new 2D Î²-PdS2 and Î²-PdTe2 monolayers can be applied in photocatalysis and photovoltaic in the near future.
