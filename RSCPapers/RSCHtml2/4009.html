Abstract

V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites with different morphological variations have been synthesized by a one-step mechanical alloying method for the first time. The surface morphologies of all the synthesized composites are revealed from their respective FESEM images. Detailed microstructure characterization of these composites has been carried out by analyzing XRD patterns employing the Rietveld refinement method, HRTEM images and FTIR spectra. The existence of a less-abundant brookite TiO2 phase in the V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites has been confirmed for the first time by microstructure characterization, HRTEM and FTIR spectrum analysis. The optical properties of all the nanocomposites have been investigated by analyzing their respective UV-Vis absorption spectra. The photocatalytic degradation of Rhodamine B under visible light in presence of 15 h milled V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposite has been enhanced significantly to âˆ¼89% with the addition of 1 mL of H2O2 to the aqueous solution, in comparison with both nanocrystalline TiO2 and V2O5. This is presumed to be due to both the synergistic effects of TiO2 and V2O5 and the reduction in the recombination of charge carriers in presence of H2O2.

1. Introduction

Water is one of the prime necessities of every industry and most of the used water is returned containing substantial amounts of contaminants, which significantly affects the product line of the industry as well as extensively polluting the environment.1â€“4 Various sources such as industries, agricultural runoff, chemical spills are positively connected with the disposal of a range of toxic pollutants that are harmful to the environment as well as to the human health.2â€“4 These toxic pollutants are quite stable and very difficult to degrade them by natural means.1 Traditional processes are not sufficient to purify the huge amount of highly contaminated wastewater for its reuse. Various methods have been developed for the degradation of these non-biodegradable, stable toxic pollutants from wastewater, drinking and ground surface water.5â€“9 Some of these methods are reverse osmosis, active sludge, flocculation, combined coagulation, electrochemical oxidation etc.10â€“12 Recently, semiconductor-based photocatalysts have been considered as a promising â€˜greenâ€™ technique for solar energy conversion with respect to the decomposition of toxic and hazardous organic pollutants.13â€“15 Visible-light-driven photocatalysts are now in focus as the content of visible and ultraviolet (UV) light in sunlight is about 50% and 4%, respectively. Semiconducting metal oxides can degrade a wide variety of dyes under UV-Vis light irradiation, but the high recombination rate of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs reduces the photocatalytic efficiency of the material.16 After the discovery of photo-induced splitting of water on TiO2 electrodes,17 titanium dioxide is widely used as an environmental purifier, photoconductor, solar cell and antibacterial material due to its low cost, high chemical stability and non-toxic nature.14,18â€“24 V2O5 is also a bright n-type semiconducting material used as a photocatalyst to degrade organic pollutants under visible light due to its qualitative nature like non-toxicity, chemical stability, high visible light absorption and photostability.25 The use of V2O5 in the form of a nanostructure is also of great interest due to its size and morphological changes. Nanostructured V2O5 with its wide morphologies such as nanobelts, nanotubes, nanorods, nanosheets, nanowires etc. are used in many fields for modern applications.26â€“28 However, due to the wide band gap of TiO2, the light absorption edge of pure titanium is <387 nm and solar light cannot be used extensively in photocatalysis, as >95% of sunlight is within the visible region (400â€“800 nm). The low quantum yield is another main drawback of TiO2 for use as a photocatalyst, which depends on the ratio of the surface charge carrier transfer rate and the recombination rate of the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pair.29 Several attempts had been made to minimize the low quantum yield and enhance the photocatalytic performance of TiO2 by modifying its surface structure, surface properties and composition.30â€“33 The surface can be modified by doping or coating with transition metals or noble metals onto the surface or into the crystal lattice of TiO2.34 Heterogeneous semiconductor-based photocatalysts have emerged as potential photocatalytic materials for water purification in which solar light can be used.16,35â€“39 These materials are considered as an effective tool for solar water splitting and degrading organic pollutants in wastewater with non-metallic doping elements such as boron, carbon, nitrogen, fluorine and sulphur.40â€“43 It is reported that the spreading of V2O5 layers over the TiO2 nanoparticles results in the enhancement of the photocatalytic activity of the nanocomposite.44â€“46 To enhance the photocatalytic activity of TiO2, in the present study a V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposite has been synthesized as a heterogeneous photocatalyst of brookite TiO2 and orthorhombic V2O5 phase by surface modification through spreading the V2O5 layers over the brookite TiO2 nanoparticles.

In previous reports, V2O5â€“TiO2 catalysts were typically synthesized by solâ€“gel, wet impregnation, micelle-template, electro-spinning, magnetron sputtering and direct current (DC) arc plasma processes etc. with low photocatalytic activities.47â€“53 However, Wang et al. reported the 90% degradation of RhB in 180 min in the presence of a 1D TiO2/V2O5 heterostructure synthesized by the RF reactive magnetron sputtering method.52 So far, there is no report on RhB degradation in the presence of a TiO2â€“V2O5 nanocomposite catalyst synthesized by a mechanical alloying process. The method of mechanical alloying (MA) is also an attractive procedure to synthesize heterogeneous catalysts.39,54 The kinetic energy generated during MA is utilized primarily to (i) produce heat energy through the ball-powder-ball, ball-powder-vial wall collisions, (ii) reduction of the particle size followed by an increase in surface area, (iii) formation of lattice imperfections of different kinds within the crystal lattice, (iv) increase in lattice strain, (v) local melting and (vi) formation of a new phases with alternative polymorphs.55

It is to be noted that heterostructured nanocomposites synthesized by MA with controlled morphologies have not yet been explored in the removal of organic pollutants from wastewater through photodegradation using visible light.

The primary objectives of the present study are to (i) fabricate V2O5â€“TiO2 heterostructured nanocomposites by mechanical alloying, (ii) structure and microstructure characterizations of nanocomposites employing X-ray diffraction and electron microscopes and (iii) study their photocatalytic performances for the degradation of an organic dye, Rhodamine B (RhB) in the presence of visible light. The Rietveld structure and micro/nanostructure refinement method56â€“59 has been employed to analyze the XRD patterns of all of the nanocomposites with highly overlapped reflections to determine several structural/microstructural parameters, the quantitative contents of the crystalline phases and the preferential growth direction of the nanocomposites, considering all reflections of all pertinent phases present in the respective XRD patterns.

In the present work, V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites with controlled morphologies have been synthesized for the first time by mechanical alloying and their growth mechanism is revealed by the Rietveld refinement and time-resolved FESEM and HRTEM images. The photocatalytic properties of the nanocomposites have been investigated through the degradation of an organic dye, Rhodamine B in presence of visible light. The V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposite milled for 15 h (VT-15 h) exhibits excellent photocatalytic activity under visible light irradiation in comparison to pure TiO2 and pure V2O5 nanocrystals, both milled for 15 h with the same milling conditions, due to the synergistic effect. H2O2 plays a dual role in the photocatalytic degradation process â€“ it prevents the recombination of photogenerated electrons and holes by accepting electrons in the conduction band, as well as generating highly reactive hydroxyl radicals in presence of a photocatalyst, which results in an enhancement of the photocatalytic activity.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis of V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites by mechanical alloying

Titanium dioxide (purity 99.5%, Loba chem.) and vanadium pentoxide (purity 99.5%, Sigma Aldrich) powders were taken in an equal weight ratio for mechanical alloying in a planetary ball mill with a ball-to-powder mass ratio (BPMR) 35:1. The powder mixture was kept inside an 80 ml chrome-steel vial filled with 30 chrome-steel balls of 10 mm diameter. The mechanical synthesis of the mixture was carried out at room temperature in a planetary ball mill (P6, M/S Fritsch, GmbH, Germany) at 300 rpm for different time durations with intermittent pauses in milling to avoid possible contamination from the milling media. Pure TiO2 and V2O5 powders were milled for 15 h, V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites milled for 5 h, 10 h, 15 h and 25 h were designated as Ti-15 h, V2O5-15 h, VT-5 h, VT-10 h, VT-15 h and VT-25 h respectively.

2.2. Material characterization

The relative phase abundances, structure and microstructure parameters of all the nanocomposites were obtained by analyzing their respective X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns, recorded with Ni-filtered CuKÎ± radiation from a Bruker D8 Advanced diffractometer within 20Â°â€“80Â° 2Î¸ range in step scan mode with a step size of 0.02Â° 2Î¸ and scan time 2 s per step. XRD patterns of all the nanocomposites were analyzed using the Rietveld whole-profile fitting method based on structure and microstructure refinement. The leaf-like morphology of the nanocomposites was revealed initially from FESEM images (ZEISS-SIGMA). Structure and microstructure characterizations of these nanocomposites were also revealed by analyzing HRTEM (JEOL-JEM, 2010) images. The Fourier transform infrared spectrum (FTIR) was recorded at room temperature in the range 400â€“4000 cmâˆ’1 with a Perkin Elmer FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets. Optical band gaps of the nanocomposites were obtained by analyzing UV-Vis absorption spectra recorded with an aqueous solution of the powdered samples using a UV-Vis spectrophotometer (SHIMADZU UV-1800, JAPAN) in the frequency range 200â€“800 nm.

2.3. Photocatalytic test

Photocatalytic activities of the pure TiO2, pure V2O5 milled for 15 h, and V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites milled for 5 h, 10 h, 15 h and 25 h were performed through the photocatalytic degradation of a model organic pollutant Rhodamine B (RhB) dissolved in deionised water. 20 mg Lâˆ’1 of each powered sample was added into 5 mg Lâˆ’1 of RhB aqueous solution in a 150 mL beaker and continuously stirred for 60 min at room temperature in darkness to ensure the adsorption/de-adsorption equilibrium between RhB and the photocatalytic samples. Then the solution was exposed to visible light irradiation from a halogen lamp of 500 W power.60 The distance between the lamp and the reactor was kept at 15 cm to avoid heating from the light source on the aqueous solution and a water bath was used to maintain a constant temperature of the solution. The photo-reacted samples were collected after some interval of time to monitor the residual concentration of the RhB in the respective UV-Vis absorption spectrums.

3. Method of analysis

3.1. Rietveld refinement analysis of XRD patterns

The Rietveld's powder structure refinement method56â€“59 using traditional XRD data is the most efficient technique for characterizing the structure, microstructure and quantitative phase estimation of individual phases present in a multiphase compound. In the present study, to determine the structure and microstructure parameters, as well as the quantitative estimations of all the pertinent phases in these nanocomposites, the Rietveld refinement method has been adopted. As the XRD patterns of the unmilled and all ball milled powders are composed of several overlapping reflections of different phases, structure and microstructure parameters of an individual phase can only be obtained by considering all reflections of all contributing phases in these nanocomposites. The Rietveld software MAUD 2.2661 is specially designed to analyze simultaneously both the structure and microstructure through the Marquardt least-squares method. To fit the experimental profile of the samples, all kinds of instrumental corrections have been taken into consideration. Peak broadening of all phases may arise due to overlapping reflections of different phases and also from the small anisotropic crystallite size and microstrain broadening. To consider anisotropic peak broadening, the Popa rule of peak broadening has been adopted in the MAUD software.62 The progress of the refinement of the fitting is monitored by the goodness of fit (GoF), which is defined as the ratio of the weighted residual error (Rwp) and expected residual error (Rexp) expressed as,

5. Conclusions

A number of V2O5â€“TiO2 nanocomposites have been synthesized by mechanical alloying for the accelerated removal of organic pollutant from wastewater. Microstructure characterization and the relative phase abundances of these milled nanocomposites have been carried out employing the Rietveld refinement, FTIR and analyzing HRTEM images. Elemental compositions/mappings of these nanocomposites have been accomplished from HRTEM EDX spectra. Sequential changes in the morphology with increasing milling time from the flake/leaf-like to granular type have been clearly revealed from the FESEM images. It is observed that after 15 h of milling, the anatase (pure) TiO2 transformed to rutile and high-pressure polymorphic phases, whereas the nanocomposites become a composite of the polymorphic brookite TiO2 and the V2O5 phases with a trace amount of rutile TiO2. The leaf-like morphology formed after 15 h of milling of the nanocomposite transforms to granular-like within 25 h of milling. The difference of particle size and the band gap energy between the heterojunction of the brookite TiO2 and the V2O5 nanocrystals is found to be at a minimum in the 15 h milled nanocomposite among all the samples. Photodegradation studies of a model organic pollutant RhB in aqueous solution with the nanocomposites and nanocrystalline pure TiO2 and V2O5 in the presence of visible light reveal that the 15 h milled nanocomposite has superior photocatalytic activity. An addition of 1 mL of H2O2 in 1a 50 mL aqueous solution results in an enhanced rate of degradation to âˆ¼89% within 270 min. The probable reasons for such enhanced photocatalytic property may be due to the leaf-like morphology of the nanocomposite, heterojunction generated between the brookite TiO2 and the V2O5 interfaces with less band gap energy, the spreading of the V2O5 over TiO2, and also the optimum amount of phases present in the VT-15 h nanocomposite. The notable photocatalytic activity of this nanocomposite is reported for the first time for the case of a mechanosynthesized material. This facile approach obviously opens up pathways to synthesize novel photocatalysts with potential applications for the accelerated removal of a huge amount of organic pollutants from wastewater that is released every day from the textile, chemical and other industries.
