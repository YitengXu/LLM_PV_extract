Abstract

Solar-driven water splitting offers a leading-edge approach to storing abundant and intermittent solar energy and producing hydrogen as a clean and sustainable energy carrier. More importantly, constructing well-designed photocatalysts is a promising approach to develop clean hydrogen energy. In this paper, flower spherical UiO-66-(SCH3)2/ZnIn2S4 (UiOSC/ZIS) photocatalysts are successfully synthesized by a simple two-step hydrothermal method, and they exhibit high hydrogen production activity in light-driven water splitting. The optimized 30-UiOSC/ZIS (the content of UiOSC was 30 mg) composite exhibits optimal hydrogen production activity with a hydrogen production of 3433 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, which is 5 and 235 times higher than that of pure ZIS and UiOSC, respectively. In addition, a long-cycling stability test has shown that the UiOSC/ZIS composite has good stability and recyclability. Experimental and characterization results show the formation of a type-II heterojunction between UiOSC and ZIS. This effectively suppresses the recombination of electronsâ€“holes and promotes the carrier transfer, thus significantly improving the hydrogen production performance. This research further promotes the application of UiO-66-(SCH3)2 in the field of photocatalytic hydrogen production and provides a reference for the rational design of UiO-66-based composite photocatalysts.

1. Introduction

Fossil fuels are non-renewable energy sources and have limited availability on Earth. With the increasing consumption of fossil fuels, there is already a shortage of fossil energy.1,2 The survival of human beings is inseparable from energy, which stimulates an urgent need to find a new energy source with abundant reserves that does not depend on fossil fuels.3 Hydrogen has attracted much attention as a renewable and clean energy source.4,5 The Earth contains abundant water resources, and solar energy is an inexhaustible source of energy, so photocatalytic technology can be used to drive water splitting into H2 under sunlight.6,7 Thus, it will realize the conversion of solar energy to hydrogen energy.8,9 In order to obtain high photocatalytic efficiency of hydrogen production and realize the practical application of this technology, the development of sustainable, efficient and stable photocatalysts has become the focus of research.10,11

Efficient photocatalysts need to have a suitable forbidden bandwidth, energy band position, high photocatalytic efficiency, good stability, and low cost. Among the many catalysts, metal sulfides (e.g., CdS, Cd1âˆ’xZnxS, and ZnIn2S4)12â€“15 exhibit more attractive activity compared to metal oxides (e.g., TiO2, ZnO, SrTiO3).16â€“19 This may be due to their remarkable visible light absorption ability and suitable energy band position.20 Among these sulfides, ZnIn2S4, as a sheet-like ternary metal-sulfide compound, shows outstanding chemical stability, visible light absorption, and environmental friendliness, and may be a highly potential and promising photocatalytic material.21â€“23 In particular, the nano-sheet-like structure of ZnIn2S4 not only completely ensures the contact area with other semiconductors, but also shortens the charge transfer distance.24,25 Nevertheless, the severe recombination of photo-generated electronâ€“hole pairs greatly limits its application in photocatalytic H2 production.26,27 In order to solve this problem, different strategies have been proposed to accelerate its carrier transport, suppress carrier recombination, and improve the electronâ€“hole separation efficiency, such as structural engineering, noble metal loading, ion doping, and construction of heterojunctions.28â€“30 Yin et al. reported that with the introduction of MoS2, CdS corrosion was obviously prohibited. The CdS@MoS2 core@shell heterostructure showed efficient charge separation, excellent hydrogen production performance, and high stability.31 Lu et al. researched the effect of MoSe2 distribution on CdS nanorods on the catalytic performance of composites.32 Decorating sulfides with a cocatalyst and construction of a heterojunction are promising strategies.

UiO-66 is a representative metalâ€“organic framework (MOF) material in the field of photocatalysis, which is constituted by zirconium ions and 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate.33 As a common MOF, UiO-66 not only has a highly porous structure, large surface area, and more active sites, but also has high stability and a flexible structure.34,35 One of the most fascinating characteristics is the ability to precisely fine-tune their chemical and physical properties due to the rich coordination chemistry of metal cations and the diversity of organic linkers.36â€“38 For example, changing organic linkers and introducing functional groups can provide a wide variety of compounds with different properties.39â€“42 Liu et al. reported that UiO-66-X variants (X = â€“H, â€“NH2, â€“NO2, â€“OH, â€“Br, â€“Br2) are widely used for photocatalytic oxidation. The UiO-66-NH2 group improves the NO removal efficiency most under irradiation with visible light.43 Li et al. studied the effect of different electron-donating groups (X = â€“OH, â€“NH2, â€“COOH, â€“NO2 and â€“H) in MOFs on the efficiency of photocatalytic degradation organic pollutants.44 However, the introduction of the functional group methylthio (â€“SCH3) is still relatively little studied. The â€“SCH3 group, as an electron-donating group, can better regulate the energy band position of methylthio-functionalized UiO-66.45 It is anticipated that it will contribute S 3p electrons to the aromatic connecting unit, acting as an auxochromic and bathochromic substituent on the aromatic ring to expand the absorption band to the visible-light area.46 However, the activity of using MOFs as the main catalyst is still not very high and requires precious metals for modification. In order to reduce the cost and apply it in practice, UiO-66 can be modified with functional groups, adjusted to a suitable band gap, and then a heterojunction can be constructed with ZnIn2S4 to enhance its photocatalytic activity of hydrogen production under visible light irradiation.

Considering the above advantages of â€“SCH3, herein, we have pre-synthesized UiO-66-(SCH3)2 (UiOSC). Then UiOSC was coupled with ZnIn2S4 to form an efficient UiOSC/ZIS composite via a solvothermal method for visible-light driven H2 release. Due to the appropriate conduction band and valence band positions of UiOSC and ZIS, a type II heterojunction was constructed. The strong light absorption properties of ZIS and the efficient electron extraction effect of UiOSC are utilized. The two materials were tightly combined, which promoted the separation and transport of electronâ€“holes and effectively generated H2 from water splitting. Among these UiOSC/ZIS composites, 30-UiOSC/ZIS shows the best performance with the maximum amount of hydrogen evolution reaching about 3433 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, which is 5 times that of the ZIS monomer (673 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1). In addition, the composite has high stability. After 16 h long-cycle test, the performance remains almost the same as the original, and the structure does not change significantly.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Preparation of UiOSC

The preparation of UiO-66-(SCH3)2 (UiOSC) was carried out through a solvothermal method.45 Typically, 64.5 mg of H2BDC-(SCH3)2 and 58.3 mg of ZrCl4 were dissolved in a mixed solution of 15 mL of DMF and 1.8 mL of acetic acid with continuous stirring for 30 min. The mixed solution was quickly transferred to a 25 mL high temperature Teflon-lined autoclave. Then, it was heated to 120 Â°C, and maintained for 24 h. After the stainless steel autoclave was gradually cooled to room temperature, the autoclave was opened and the final products were poured into a 50 mL centrifuge tube for centrifugal separation and precipitation. The product was purified with DMF and dichloromethane thoroughly and vacuum dried overnight at 60 Â°C to obtain the UiOSC sample.

2.2. Preparation of UiOSC/ZIS

The UiOSC/ZIS composite was rationally designed and prepared by a simple one-step solvothermal method. First, 34.0 mg of ZnCl2 and 110.1 mg of InCl3 were dissolved in 30 mL of a solvent mixture containing 20 mL of deionized water and 10 mL of anhydrous ethanol. Then, 30 mg of the as-prepared UiOSC was added. After sonication and stirring of the mixture for a few minutes, 75.1 mg of thioacetamide was dispersed in the solution mixture under continuous stirring. Subsequently, the above solution was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon liner, covered and loaded into a stainless steel reactor. The autoclave was placed into an oven and heated at 160 Â°C for 6 h. After being cooled to room temperature naturally, the resultant yellow precipitate was isolated by centrifugation. Subsequently the yellow precipitate was repeatedly washed twice with deionized water and ethanol, respectively. Finally, the obtained powder was dried at 60 Â°C using a vacuum oven overnight. The mass of UiOSC was controlled at 10 mg, 20 mg, 30 mg, 40 mg and 50 mg. Accordingly the synthesized samples were labelled as 10-UiOSC/ZIS, 20-UiOSC/ZIS, 30-UiOSC/ZIS, 40-UiOSC/ZIS, and 50-UiOSC/ZIS. Pure ZnIn2S4 was synthesized through a similar process without addition of UiOSC, and was also marked as ZIS. The synthetic process of UiOSC/ZIS is described in Scheme 1.

2.3. Photocatalytic H2 evolution measurements

The photocatalytic water-splitting reaction was carried out in a 200 mL Pyrex reaction cell connected with a closed glass gas circulation system (Labsolar-6A, BeijingPerfectlight). At the top of the Pyrex reaction cell, there is a quartz window for vertical illumination. Typically, 30 mg of the photocatalyst was dispersed in 80 mL of aqueous solution containing 0.75 M Na2S and 1.05 M Na2SO3 as sacrificial reagents. Before the reaction began, the mixed solution needed to undergo several complete evacuations to completely remove dissolved air and 20 mL of argon gas was added as the carrier gas. The reaction solution was kept at 25 Â°C in a thermostatic reaction bath connected to the reactor and vigorously stirred by using a magnetic stirrer at all times of the reaction.

A 300 W Xe arc lamp (PE300BFA, BeijingPerfectlight) with a UV cutoff filter (Î» â‰¥ 420 nm) was used as the visible light source. The generated hydrogen was transferred to an on-line gas chromatograph (Nanjing Renhua, GC-9890A) through a gas circulation system. Gas chromatography with a thermal conductivity detector (TCD, 5 Ã… molecular sieve column) was used to detect the volumetric amount of H2 produced. The entire reaction and detection unit was hermetically sealed. The apparent quantum efficiency (AQE) was estimated by using the formula given in the ESI.â€ 47

The photocatalytic hydrogen production performance of these materials was tested under simulated visible light (Î» â‰¥ 420 nm) irradiation. In the presence of the same sacrificial agent, 0.75 M Na2S and 1.05 M Na2SO3 mixed aqueous solution, the hydrogen production properties of the UiOSC, ZIS, and various ratios of UiOSC/ZIS composites were investigated as shown in Fig. 4a and b. The photocatalytic hydrogen production performance of pure UiOSC was almost zero. In contrast, the pure ZIS catalyst showed some photocatalytic hydrogen production performance with a hydrogen production rate of about 673 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1. After UiOSC was compounded with ZIS, various ratios of UiOSC/ZIS composites all exhibited photocatalytic hydrogen production activity, and the hydrogen production performance of the UiOSC/ZIS composites showed a parabolic shape. When the loading of UiOSC increased, the hydrogen production performance increased to a maximum and then decreased. When the content of UiOSC was high, the excessive UiOSC particles covering the surface of ZIS may block the active site or restrict the light absorption thus reducing the photocatalytic activity. To gain insights into the photoelectrochemical properties of the samples, their transient photocurrent and electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) were analyzed. The transient photocurrent of UiOSC/ZIS, UiOSC, and ZIS was detected by using an electrochemical workstation under Xe lamp irradiation. The low photocurrent intensity of UiOSC and ZIS, as illustrated in Fig. 6a, could be ascribed to the rapid recombination of photoexcited electronâ€“hole pairs. However, compared with two monomers, UiOSC/ZIS showed the most superior photocurrent value, demonstrating that UiOSC/ZIS possessed the best charge separation and transfer efficiency. This may be the reason that the charge was transported between ZIS and UiOSC, slowing down the recombination of photogenerated electrons and holes. Furthermore, EIS was carried out to evaluate the interfacial charge separation ability of the photocatalysts. A much smaller semicircular arc radius denotes the more rapid interfacial charge transfer efficiency of photogenerated carriers. From the Nyquist curve (Fig. 6b), UiOSC/ZIS had the smallest radius of the arc in the impedance spectra, indicating the lowest charge transfer resistance (Rct). The value of Rct could be fitted by using a simple equivalent model in the low-frequency region. The Rct value of UiOSC/ZIS was 62.51 Î© which was lower than those of ZIS (104.21 Î©) and UiOSC (78.01 Î©). The results confirmed that UiOSC/ZIS achieved a low charge transfer resistance and excellent catalytic activity.

Photoluminescence (PL) and time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) measurements were performed to reveal the recombination of photogenerated electrons and holes of the material, which further illustrated the good photocatalytic activity of the catalyst. Fig. 7a shows the photoluminescence (PL) spectrum of these catalysts. All samples were monitored at an excitation wavelength of 375 nm. The PL peaks are rather broad, which may indicate varied recombination processes occurred. There are mainly two processes that the electronâ€“hole pairs recombined by means of radiation or non-radiation.57 From the PL spectra (Fig. 7a), the peak centered at approximately 470 was due to radiative recombination, and was an extra emission band with lower energy than the band edge emission. The emission peak at 500 nm could be classified as band edge emission, which corresponded to the starting point of the absorption edge as shown in Fig. 5a.58 The low-energy tail band may be due to a recombination of donorâ€“acceptor pairs.31,32 Pure ZIS showed strong PL intensity at 470 nm, indicating that ZIS shows severe carrier recombination. In comparison, the PL intensity of the UiOSC/ZIS composite was significantly weakened, demonstrating that the recombination of photogenerated electrons and holes was significantly inhibited. The time-resolved PL spectra of the ZIS monomer and UiOSC/ZIS composite are shown in Fig. 7b. The average lifetimes of pure ZIS and UiOSC/ZIS were 5.38 and 5.21 ns, respectively (Table S2â€  lists the lifetime decay fitting parameters). The shorter lifetime of the UiOSC/ZIS composite revealed that the charge transfer rate between ZIS and UiOSC was increased, which suggested that this material effectively suppressed the recombination of charge carriers. Based on the above results, it was reasonable to infer that the heterojunction between UiOSC and ZIS allowed effective carrier separation and transfer, resulting in enhanced photocatalytic H2 production performance.

In order to investigate the photocatalytic mechanism in this research, the energy band positions of ZIS and UiOSC were analyzed. Firstly, the Mottâ€“Schottky measurements were performed for ZIS and UiOSC. As could be clearly seen in Fig. S8,â€  the slopes of the Mottâ€“Schottky curves for both materials were positive, indicating that they were both typical n-type semiconductors. The flat-band potentials of ZIS and UiOSC at pH = 7 were about âˆ’1.50 V and âˆ’1.15 V vs. Ag/AgCl, as shown by the Mottâ€“Schottky plots. For n-type semiconductors, the conduction band potential is negative 0.1 eV compared to the flat band potential. Thus the conduction band potentials of ZIS and UiOSC are âˆ’1.60 and âˆ’1.25 eV vs. Ag/AgCl, respectively. When converted to a standard hydrogen electrode, the conduction band potentials of ZIS and UiOSC are âˆ’1.40 and âˆ’1.05 eV vs. the NHE (EAg/AgCl = 0.197 eV), respectively. The band gap energy of ZIS and UiOSC was calculated to be 2.55 and 2.70 eV, respectively. Based on ECB = EVB âˆ’ Eg,59 the valence band (VB) potentials of ZIS and UiOSC could be estimated to be +1.15 and +1.65 eV, respectively. The VB-XPS test (Fig. S9â€ ) also showed that the VB potential of ZIS and UiOSC was +1.12 and +1.65 eV, respectively. This further demonstrates the accuracy of the band gap test results.

Based on the analysis of material characterization, energy band position analysis and experimental test results of photocatalytic water-splitting of hydrogen in this research, the possible charge transfer process of the UiOSC/ZIS heterostructure in the H2 production reaction and its photocatalytic mechanism were speculated. Depending on the energy band position, the conduction band (CB) potential of ZIS is âˆ’1.40 and that of UiOSC is âˆ’1.05 eV. In comparison, the CB of ZIS is more negative than that of UiOSC, while the VB of UiOSC is more positive than that of ZIS (Fig. 8a). When the co-catalyst UiOSC bound to the ZIS nanoflower-spheres, a type-II heterostructure was formed due to the matching of their CB and VB potentials. Under visible light irradiation, the ZIS nanoflowers were excited to produce electron (eâˆ’) and hole (h+) pairs due to their inherent optical absorption properties. Since the CB of UiOSC was more positive than that of ZIS, the photogenerated electrons of CB could quickly migrate from ZIS to UiOSC. The electrons then reacted with H+ in the water to form H2. The simultaneously generated holes were transferred from UiOSC to the surface of ZIS, which then reacted with the sacrificial agent (Fig. 8b). This promoted the efficient separation of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs in the UiOSC/ZIS heterostructure, and their recombination of electronâ€“holes was significantly suppressed compared to the single component. Therefore, the II-type heterojunction structure was constructed between the ZIS nanoflowers and the UiOSC nanoparticles. The synergistic effect between the two materials promoted the separation and transport of photogenerated electrons and holes, thus exhibiting a better photocatalytic hydrogen production activity.

4. Conclusions

In a nutshell, we have successfully designed a simple and easy method to synthesize a composite catalyst as a visible-light photocatalyst for H2 evolution. Here, by exploring the band gap structure of UiOSC and ZIS materials, a type-II heterojunction catalyst was rationally designed. This UiOSC/ZIS heterojunction catalyst could promote the separation and transfer of photo-generated electrons and holes, so it had high photocatalytic activity. The 30-UiOSC/ZIS composite showed high hydrogen production activity, which could reach 3433 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1. In addition, the construction of the heterojunction enhanced the stability of the material. The UiOSC/ZIS composite could achieve cycling stability lasting for at least 16 h. Our results will pave the way for the construction of UiO-66-based heterojunction catalysts for solar-powered clean fuel production.
