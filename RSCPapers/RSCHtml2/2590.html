Characterization techniques for dye-sensitized solar cells

Abstract

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs) have been widely studied in the last two decades and start to be commercialized in the photovoltaic market. Comprehensive characterization is needed to fully understand and optimize the device performance and stability. In this review, we summarize different characterization methods for dye-sensitized solar cells with liquid redox electrolytes or solid state hole transporting materials, most of which can also be used for similar devices such as perovskite based thin film solar cells. Limitations and advantages of relevant methods for studying the energy levels and time scales involved in charge transfer processes as well as charge transport related characteristic lengths are discussed. A summary of recent developments in DSCs and the importance of measured parameters for the device optimization procedure are mentioned at the end.

Broader context

Solar energy is a potential candidate to supply the future energy demands with low environmental impact. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs) are a class of solar cells which are highly efficient in low light and under indoor conditions, with the possibility for low cost fabrication, utilization of flexible substrates and for colorful and transparent devices. Due to the different working principles in comparison to other solar cell technologies, many techniques have been used to characterize the function of DSCs and to understand their properties. However, the understanding of the working mechanism of DSCs is not complete yet, and further improvement of device efficiencies requires a deeper understanding of the materials and processes in DSCs. Therefore, new methods are continuously being developed for the characterization of DSCs to understand how further improvements can be achieved. In this review paper, we summarize the characterization techniques for DSCs in a pedagogical way and also include newly developed techniques that are important for the future development of DSCs.

1. Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells have been widely studied in the last two decades and start to be commercialized in the photovoltaic market. Stability, performance, production cost and colour are the main factors that are considered for the commercialization of dye sensitized solar cells (DSCs).1 The number of registered patents in the DSC field grew exponentially year by year reaching the number of 400 in year 2010.2 So far the topic of new materials has received the most attention in DSC related patents; materials of interest have been tested in order to improve the stability and efficiency and reduce the production costs.3–7

A full understanding of the processes occurring in DSCs is necessary for further optimization and stabilization of the device while the current picture is far from complete and still there are un-answered questions especially in the dye/electrolyte/semiconductor interface6,8–10

Implementation of standard characterization methods is inevitable to elucidate and understand the impacts of each device component on the photovoltaic performance, and also elaborate the processes occurring in the stability tests such as ongoing chemical reactions under long term heating or light illumination conditions. Considering the DSC as a molecular device, energy levels and time scales of electron transfer processes are crucial parameters for estimation of device operation and performance. Besides, optimization of device components towards the best stabilities and efficiencies, also understanding of how overall device efficiency is related to each component and how each component can affect the others can be only accomplished through proper characterization of the device.

Characterization methods for DSCs have been developed from the first years of their invention by Michael Grätzel and Brian O'Regan,11 however limitations and reliability and the degree of non-destructivity of each method have not been comprehensively explored yet. A review of the DSC characterization methods is the topic of the present paper.

There are few reviews about the characterization of DSCs in the literature in which much attention is devoted to a special technique in detail.12–18 Snaith and Yang et al. have reported separately about reliable solar cell efficiency measurements.12,13 Takagi et al. have reported about the IPCE and current–voltage characteristics.14 Barnes et al. have published a review about the photo voltage/current transient and charge extraction methods15 and a review about electrochemical impedance spectroscopy in dye-sensitized solar cells has been published by Fabregat et al.16 Moreover, Johansson et al. have reviewed the application of X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) in DSC characterization17 and Katoh et al. wrote a review about electron injection efficiency measurements in DSCs.18 In this review we will give an overview and comparison of all the most relevant characterization techniques and how to apply them to obtain relevant information for device optimization. The emphasis of the presented content is on the device characterization, reliability of each method and optimization of the related energy levels/charge transfer time scales. The outline of the paper is as follows: in Section 2 we have a short summary of liquid DSC working conditions, components, energy levels and timescales and introduce the important processes which should be characterized. Almost the same arguments can be used for ssDSCs considering in mind that hole conduction occurs within the hole conductor rather than the liquid electrolyte. Section 3 describes the general techniques for material characterization of DSC components such as electron microscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, scanning tunnelling microscopy, UV-Vis, Raman and FTIR spectroscopies, conductivity measurements and electrochemical methods. Section 4 represents the most important characterization methods for solar cells: voltage–current and IPCE. Special points for proper evaluation of the efficiency are mentioned subsequently. Photo-induced spectroscopy and voltage/current modulation techniques are investigated in Sections 5 and 6. Section 7 is devoted to final remarks about the DSC (liquid, quasi solid state and solid state) development trends and a summary and conclusion is mentioned at the end in which some stability measurements are also discussed.

2. Dye-sensitized solar cells

2.1 Device components

The conventional liquid electrolyte based DSC is a two electrode electrochemical cell in which the counter electrode is fluorine doped tin oxide conductive glass (FTO) coated with a thin layer of the platinum catalyst. The working electrode is a mesoporous layer of titanium dioxide nanoparticles coated on FTO onto which a monolayer of dye molecules is adsorbed. A blocking layer of dense TiO2 may be pre-coated on the FTO to prevent recombination at the FTO substrate/electrolyte interface. The two electrodes are sealed together by polymer sealants and a liquid electrolyte, consisting of the redox mediator, additives and the solvent fills the space between the electrodes.11 Incoming visible light is transmitted by the transparent FTO and wide band gap TiO2 films, but absorbed by the dye molecules. The excited dye molecules inject electrons into the TiO2 conduction band (CB). The oxidized dye molecules are subsequently regenerated by electron transfer from the redox mediator to dyes. Electrons in the mesoporous TiO2 and positive charges (‘holes’) in the electrolyte diffuse to the FTO back contact and counter electrode, respectively. The difference between the Fermi levels of the working and the counter electrode (the latter is defined by the redox potential of the redox mediator at the counter electrode interface and the former is defined by the TiO2 Fermi level at the FTO interface) under illumination results in the electric potential difference between the electrodes by which one can operate electronic devices.6Fig. 1 shows the schematic of a liquid based device based on the cobalt redox mediator. In solid state dye-sensitized solar cells (ssDSCs), a solid hole conductor replaces the liquid electrolyte, regenerates the dye molecules and conducts the holes to the back contact of the solar cell which is a metallic layer of silver or gold.19,20 The Fermi level difference between the working electrode and back contact is equivalent to the potential difference of two electrodes in ssDSCs. In quasi solid state dye solar cells (qssDSCs),21–23 a polymer or a gel electrolyte is implemented instead of a liquid electrolyte in order to improve the long term stability of the device. The same concepts, the energy levels, and charge transfer phenomena are valid for qssDSCs as well.

2.2 Device operation and timescales of the processes

Different operational processes in DSCs are illustrated in Fig. 2. Favourable processes such as dye photon absorption resulting in the excitation of an electron from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) (1), electron injection from the LUMO to the TiO2 CB (2), dye regeneration by the redox mediators (or hole conductor) (3) and electron/hole transport in the TiO2/electrolyte (or hole conductor layer) (4) should therefore be optimized in terms of energy levels and timescales. There are unfavourable processes (shown by red arrows) consisting of electron recombination to the oxidized species of the dye (5) and the electrolyte (or the oxidized hole conductor) (6) and de-excitation of the dye excited state (7). Minimization of the unfavourable processes is also vital for optimization of device performance. All these processes are coupled together and therefore replacement of one component can have simultaneous impacts on different favourable or unfavourable processes. Description of these processes and their relation to the device component energy levels have been investigated previously and are summarized in several comprehensive reviews.6,24,25

Under operating conditions there are competitions between the favourable and unfavourable processes:

(1) Electron injection competes with de-excitation of the dye. Different dyes have different time constants for injection/luminescence phenomena. Shorter injection times and longer luminescence life times are favourable for better device performance. The position and the nature of dye LUMO levels and TiO2 CB states, length and nature of the dye acceptor ligand, the adsorption mode of the dye on the TiO2 surface, the presence of adsorbed species at the interface i.e. Li+ ions and the dye/TiO2 surrounding medium can affect the injection rates from the dye to the TiO2 CB according to the Marcus theory.26–29 The nature and position of dye HOMO/LUMO levels and the surrounding medium of the dye can affect the transition probabilities for de-excitation of the dye.30,31 In typical DSCs, electron injection occurs on the time scale of femto to pico seconds, while luminescence life times of typical ruthenium18 and organic32 sensitizers are in the range of nanoseconds and sub nanoseconds, respectively. (2) Electron collection versus electron recombination

Injected electrons inside TiO2 diffuse to the working electrode contacts; it can be modelled by the random walk model.33 Typical diffusion coefficients in the mesoporous films depend on the Fermi level position and are reported to be in the order of 10−3 to 10−6 cm2 per sin DSCs.34 Crystallite size, trap density and its energy level position/physical location, porosity and degree of disorder in the working electrode affect the effective electron diffusion coefficient (and the corresponding transport time).32–37 On the other hand recombination of electrons to the oxidized dye or the oxidized redox mediator in the electrolyte results in lowering the Fermi level and decreasing the current and potential of the device. The time scale of the electron collection inside the mesoporous TiO2 film is in the order of milliseconds, while the recombination to the electrolyte under open circuit conditions is in the order of a few ms.6 Faster collection and slower recombination are favourable for better device performances.

2.3 Energy levels and the multi trapping model

A DSC is a molecular device in which the PCE depends a lot on the energy levels of its components. The efficiency of the above mentioned electron transfer processes depends on the energy levels of donor and acceptor compounds. In the presence of a liquid electrolyte the energy levels of the dye and excited state dye molecules as well as redox mediators have Gaussian shape distributions (shown in Fig. 2) which is described by the Gerischer model52 (see Section 3.9). The electron diffusion coefficient and transport inside the TiO2 mesoporous film depend strongly on the density, location and position of the semiconductor energy levels.53–55Fig. 2 shows the typical energy levels in the liquid based DSCs. Dye HOMO and LUMO energy levels have key roles in the light absorption and efficiency of the device. Considering the Shockley–Queisser limit for the photovoltaic absorbers, the efficiency of the device strongly depends on the band gap of the absorber.56 Here, optical E0–0 transition of the dye corresponds to the Eg fundamental gap of the semiconductor. A smaller E0–0 distance leads to more light absorption by the dye sensitizer. However, there are limitations for the dye energy levels: in order to have an efficient and rapid electron injection into the TiO2 CB and also rapid dye regeneration by redox mediators, an over-potential of several hundreds mV between the acceptor–donor species levels is needed. Therefore HOMO–LUMO levels should be kept apart sufficiently.6,57 Marcus theory can describe the electron transfer rates in the injection or regeneration processes on which exponential dependence of the electron transfer rate on the free energy difference of acceptor/donor levels is expected.26 Description of Marcus theory and its relation to DSCs (for example electron injection,28 recombination41 and dye regeneration58) is beyond the scope of this article and further relevant information can be found somewhere else.26,59,60

The TiO2 CB can be shifted and in fact tuned by interface dipoles of surface treated films,61 the adsorbed dye molecule dipoles62 and surface adsorbed charges or molecules from the electrolyte.63 By shifting the TiO2 CB, the dye LUMO level should be tuned respectively in order to have an efficient electron injection. Moreover, electrolyte redox potential (E0) depends strongly on the nature of redox mediators and the surrounding ligands in the case of metal organic complexes. For having a high open circuit potential, the dye HOMO level should be tuned according to redox potential or vice versa in order to satisfy the criterion of a high dye regeneration rate as well.57 Reported by Feldt et al.,64 typical energy level values in standard cobalt electrolyte based DSCs are +0.56, +1.04 and −1.14 V vs. NHE for the redox potential of the cobalt bpy mediator, E0(D+/D) and E0(D+/D*) energy levels of the D35 organic dye respectively. The CB edge of TiO2 is considered as −0.5 V vs. NHE.65

Electrons will experience a random walk in between the traps and diffuse to the working electrode contacts. After trapping in trap states, electrons wait for a certain waiting time and are de-trapped to the CB until they can be trapped again.33

Density and depth of the traps depend on the morphology and preparation procedure of semiconductors66,67 and are the determining factors for the electron transport inside the film as well as for the electron lifetime and DSC open circuit voltage.40,68–70 Lower trap densities cause less trapping–detrapping processes and faster transport inside the film and thus allowing the electrons to reach the electrode contacts efficiently before they experience recombination processes.

Regarding the device efficiency, there are few studies about estimation of maximum achievable efficiency for DSCs.25,71 The 33.7% upper limit of efficiency for thin film solar cells was proposed initially by Shockley and Queisser (SQ) in 1961 by considering the absorption of light and excitation of electrons between the semiconductor energy levels, electron hole recombination and the thermalization of hot charge carriers.56 Snaith has estimated the highest obtainable efficiency for DSCs to be 21%,71 lower than the SQ-limit because of the necessity of over-potentials for efficient dye injection and regeneration. Strategies of further improvements of the PCE from the current record value of 14.7%72 can be pursued and are discussed in Section 7.

3. Materials characterization

3.1 Electron microscopy

The mesoporous morphology of the working electrode is very important for the device performance.5 Dye adsorption and electron recombination are proportional to the working electrode surface area. Electron transport and the effective diffusion coefficient depend on the underlying structure of the working electrode. One dimensional structures and branched structures may have faster electron transport compared to nanoparticulate films73–75 however the interface grain boundaries may limit the conductivity.76

Scattering of high energy electron beams from the surface of nanomaterials is the concept of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) to study the surface morphology of nanostructures.77 SEM is the conventional and standard method for microscopy studies of nanostructure film morphologies such as DSC working and counter electrodes.78,79Fig. 3 shows a typical SEM image of a DSC mesoporous TiO2 film. Porosities of 50–65%, pore diameters of 15–20 nm and particle sizes of 20–30 nm are typical and convenient values for DSC working electrodes.80

Thicknesses of the layers in a DSC can be measured by cross sectional SEM which is the standard method to estimate the thickness of solid state device layers.19 In solid state devices the thickness of every layer such as the TiO2 porous film, the blocking layer, the metal back contact and the hole conductor capping layer directly affects the photovoltaic performance. The dependence of open-circuit voltage (Voc) and hole conductivity of ssDSCs on the thickness of TiO2 films was investigated by Snaith et al.81,82 Both Voc and hole conductivity are reduced by increasing the TiO2 film thickness while increasing the dye loadings. Therefore high extinction coefficient dyes are employed in the record efficiency ssDSCs.83 A typical cross sectional SEM of a ssDSC is shown in Fig. 4. Furthermore, pixel contrast histograms in cross sectional SEM images have also been used for studying the pore filling of spiro OMeTAD (spiro) in ssDSCs; sharper particle edges and pixels with more contrast show uncovered TiO2 particles.84 Cross sectional SEM is still the most common tool for estimation of pore filling and the thickness of hole conductor capping layers; it can also be considered as a method for evaluation of other relevant parameters for pore filling studies in ssDSCs.82,85–87


Fig. 4 SEM cross section image of a typical ssDSC device showing the layers of FTO/blocking layer (BL)/TiO2–dye–hole conductor/hole conductor capping layer (CL)/silver back contact (SC). The scale bar is 1 micrometer.

Compared to the working electrode, the porosity of the counter electrode is less important but in turn affects the efficiency of the device. For example, using different electrochemical deposition routes for a PEDOT (poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)) counter electrode, Ellis et al. have obtained different porosities (investigated by SEM) and investigated possible changes in electro-activity of the PEDOT counter electrode in the device.88

Elemental analysis of the films can be done by energy dispersive X-ray analysis (EDAX) along the whole thickness of the films. Doping of the semiconductor, composition of composites, co-sensitization of the different dyes along the film thickness, pore filling of the hole conductor in ssDSCs and coverage of perovskite absorber thin films can be studied by EDAX analysis of the working electrode.89–91 Typical resolution of EDAX is in the order of several hundred nanometres. In transmission electron microscopy (TEM), the energy of the electron beam is higher than in SEM and the electron beam is transmitted through the sample which can be used to determine detailed information about a material.92 Features as small as a few nanometers can be observed using TEM. High resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) can be used to observe even finer details (single crystalline domains and the lattice plane distances) of film crystalline planes as low as a few Angstroms. Other methods such as atomic force microscopy (AFM) are able to investigate the surface morphology of a material but are not as common as SEM or TEM for the DSC research field.

If the wavelength of the electron wave function is short enough (in the case of a high energy electron beam) and in the order of the sample lattice spacing, a diffraction pattern can be formed by which one can study the reciprocal lattice of the sample (called selected area electron diffraction, SAED). If the sample is single crystalline, a series of dots will appear showing the reciprocal lattice of the sample. If it is poly-crystalline, a series of rings will appear. Information about the sample crystal structure is therefore obtained from this technique.92Fig. 6 shows the TEM picture of mesoporous microbeads as well as the SAED pattern.93 Crystallite sizes and preferential crystalline planes have crucial importance and impacts on electron transport and adsorption of dyes on the particles.37,94 The effective catalytic activity of the counter electrode materials depends strongly on the surface area and the details of crystalline planes and defects on their surface have been investigated using HRTEM and TEM.78,95

The TEM sample preparation method can have destructing effects on the sample. In order to be investigated by TEM, the nanostructured films should be scratched from the substrate and therefore possible mechanical damage can be imposed on the sample or new surface defects can be formed.

3.2 Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method

The BET method is the standard technique for estimation of the mesoporous film porosity, pore size distribution and its surface area per gram.96 BET theory is based on the physical adsorption/desorption of the gas molecules (N2 inert gas as a standard for this method) and determines the sample surface area and pore volume. Studied by BET, it has been proposed that more organised pore sizes enhance the efficiency of DSCs.97 BET is the conventional method for evaluation of the surface area of new particles/pastes for DSCs as well as new working electrode preparation conditions.93,98,99 Dehong Chen et al. have synthesized mesoporous microbeads with a high surface area and porosity.93 The surface area per gram of the material can be controlled by using different molar ratios of precursors in the sol–gel step or by using different concentrations of ammonia in the solvothermal step. BET has been used to characterize the specific surface area per gram of the as synthesized materials. By engineering the microbead pore size, Dehong Chen et al. overcame the diffusion limited current in bulky cobalt electrolyte based DSCs.100 Furthermore, Zhang et al. have studied the effect of pore size distribution on the performance of polymer based solid state DSCs in which the pore size has a direct effect on the formation of in situ polymerized hole conductor layers in the device.101 This method requires a large area of the material for example some hundred milligrams of powder to be characterized by BET.

3.3 Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM)

In STM,102,103 the sample is voltage-biased with respect to the instrument tip which stands above the sample at a certain distance. Scanning the sample by the tip and measuring the tunneling current between the tip and the sample give information about the surface of the sample. Quantum mechanical tunneling current is sensitive to the distance between the tip and the sample surface on the sub nanometer scale. STM can be used for observing the surface or adsorbate molecules (the tunneling current will be different if the electron comes from adsorbed molecules or the metal/semiconductor substrates) as well as estimation of surface density of states.104

There are a few investigations of the dye adsorption on the TiO2 surface reported using STM.105–110 Boschloo et al. have investigated the surface coverage and adsorption of the N719 dye on the TiO2 surface. The dye covered the TiO2 surface completely but the aggregation of the N719 dye was apparent as spherical clusters of 2–4 nm size.107 Ikeda et al. investigated the black dye adsorption on the TiO2(110) surface. Comparing the adsorbed dye heights measured by STM with single dye heights estimated by density functional theory (DFT) calculations, they conclude two possible adsorption modes of the dye on the surface.111

There are reviews about studying the adsorption modes of the dye on different crystalline surfaces of TiO2 including the theoretical investigations by DFT.110,112–114 The adsorption mode of the dye is important not only from the stability issues but also from the device performance point of view. Dyes with bidentate adsorption mode are more difficult to be desorbed from the TiO2 surface, if compared to monodentate mode with the same anchoring group.115 Different binding modes result in different bond lengths from the dye binding group to the surface which affects the electron injection rate. Orientation of the dye is also important for the cobalt electrolyte based DSCs in terms of the electron lifetime within the device.116

Sasahara et al. studied the N3 dye adsorption on the TiO2(110) crystal. Adsorption configurations with two carboxylate groups bound to the surface are in agreement with the measured STM image heights (Fig. 7).108

Recently a new technique called scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) has been used to study the dye regeneration in liquid DSCs. SECM has a STM tip which is connected to a potentiostat and the current of the STM tip can be coupled to electrochemical measurements.49 Photo-assisted spatially resolved STM has also been used for investigation of nanoporous traps and their relevant effects on the current of DSC devices.118

3.4 Raman and FTIR spectroscopy

FTIR and Raman spectroscopy techniques are other methods for studying the dye adsorption mode in which each dye binding group has its own finger prints in the FTIR or the Raman spectrum.119–121 Raman and FTIR methods can be utilized for characterization of surface modifications or probing the presence of any absorbed molecules on the surface of TiO2 electrodes122,123 or the characterization of the counter electrode materials.124,125 They have been previously implemented to monitor the evolution of surface adsorbate species during the stability tests.126,127

By a relevant method called ultra high vacuum tip-enhanced Raman spectroscopy, Raman spectra of a few single molecules and even a single molecule adsorbed on a surface can be acquired.128

3.5 X-Ray diffraction (XRD)

XRD is the conventional method for exploration of the crystalline structure of nanomaterials. The crystalline phase and details of the working electrode semiconductor film lattice structure are determined by XRD. The XRD spectrum presents intensity versus 2-theta (the angle of X-ray to the sample). In X-ray diffraction, an incident beam of X-ray is scattered from the surface and a diffraction pattern is formed as a consequence of the scattering of short wavelength X-ray photons by an electronic cloud of highly ordered positive ion centers within the material. Regularity and ordering of the ions forming a lattice in the sample can be detected as well.129 The Bragg formula dsinθ = nλ is used for determination of lattice spacing in which d is the distance between special crystal planes, θ is the angle of the X-ray beam to the sample, n is an integer number and λ is the wavelength of the X-ray beam (1.54 eV for the standard copper-α beam). Peak broadening can be related to a small crystal size, which was analysed using the Scherrer formula, τ = Kλ/βcosθ, in which θ is considered for a specific XRD peak, K is a dimensionless shape factor close to 1, β is full width at half maximum of the peak and τ is the mean crystallite size for the specific crystal plane of the peak. For example, the effect of doping on the crystallite size of the as grown TiO2 nanoparticles has been investigated.130,131

The TiO2 crystallite size can affect the diffusion coefficient of the electrons inside the mesoporous film.37 Besides, the interface crystalline surfaces affect the dye adsorption to a large extent94 and adsorption of different dyes on the different crystalline planes of anatase TiO2 has been studied.110

Anatase TiO2 is the most commonly used material for DSC photoanodes, it has an indirect band gap of 3.2 eV, which is a little higher than that of the rutile phase of TiO2; allowing more photons to be absorbed by the dye.132 Dye adsorption and electronic transport of rutile and anatase nanocrystals are different.133,134 The CB position and single crystalline conductivity are higher in the anatase compared to the rutile phase.6 In terms of device efficiency, the energy levels of anatase are more appropriate for the superior device efficiency. Rutile is the most stable TiO2 phase; at higher temperatures (more than 600 °C) anatase converts to rutile.132,135 Moreover, the crystalline phase of new counter electrode materials is typically evaluated by XRD.78,95,136

3.6 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS)

By illumination of the surface with high energy photons (X-rays), electrons can be ejected from the material according to the photoelectric effect. The kinetic energy of the ejected electrons (EK) is measured using photoelectron spectroscopy (PES or XPS) and is equal to the difference in the photon energy (hν) and the energy of the electrons in the material, i.e. the binding energy (EB) and the work function (Φ) of the material. This is described in eqn (5).
 
EB = hν − EK − ϕ(5)
Binding energies of energy levels from atom-like orbitals (core levels) are characteristic of each element and therefore one can obtain information about the elemental composition of surface atoms by XPS. XPS is also sensitive to chemical shifts, i.e. to the electronic surroundings of specific atoms and therefore can give information about the chemical state of the material.

The intensity of the measured electrons depends on the cross section for photon absorption by the specific orbital, on the surface density of the elements and on the mean free path of electrons in the material. The mean free path depends on the kinetic energy of the escaping electron and is usually quite short making XPS usually a highly surface sensitive technique. Furthermore, this makes it a requirement for measurements to be carried out under vacuum conditions so that electrons can reach the detector without collisions with molecules in the gas phase. Although XPS is generally a very surface sensitive technique, XPS with very high photon energies (Hard X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy, HAXPES) can be used to study the atomic composition deeper inside a material, even for hundred nanometer film thicknesses.17 Another way to analyse deeper into a material is to use sputtering and subsequent XPS analysis.

Different DSC components (i.e. thin films, dye molecules adsorbed on the electrode surfaces or molecules solvated in the electrolyte) can be studied by means of XPS. This information for example can be used for studying the elemental composition of device components, and binding of dye molecules to the electrode. The valence energy levels can also be measured using XPS, which gives further understanding of the energy levels involved in the electron transfer processes in solar cells. It is also possible to study the orbital composition of the valence levels using resonant photoelectron spectroscopy, which is also important for the fundamental understanding of the light to electricity conversion process.17 XPS can also be used for atomic composition analysis of any new implemented DSC components, for example, Shi et al. have used XPS to investigate a new counter electrode material.136

With new technologies it is also possible to use the XPS technique to study liquid systems, in liquid jet XPS, liquid jets are passing the vacuum with very high speeds and therefore it is possible to study the binding and composition of atoms in the liquid electrolyte.137,138

Ericsson et al. have for example studied the formation of new bonds between the tri-iodide and water in water based electrolytes. For the case of water based iodide electrolytes, the XPS measurements show an asymmetry in the I3− solvated ions, hydrogen bond interactions are responsible for the symmetry breaking.137 In DSCs, activation of the I3− ion in asymmetric environments is important for its function through interactions with surfaces and dye molecules.

Another new possibility is to use time resolved photoelectron spectroscopy using pulsed X-rays from free electron lasers or higher harmonic generation light sources for studying the conversion process at an atomic scale with ultrafast time-resolution.17

3.6.1 Core level analysis. Different atom-like orbitals and core levels can be specifically useful for studying the elemental composition,86,122,139–141 dye binding configuration and surface coverage116,139,140,142–146 and possible chemical bindings of atoms in electrolytes or on the semiconductor surface.122,137

The dye configuration at the surface may for example be studied for dye molecules containing chemically different atoms of the same element. In that case the surface sensitivity can be used to determine which of the atoms are located closest to the surface and thereby the orientation of the molecule at the surface. This approach has for example been used to study the orientation of organic triarylamine based dye molecules containing nitrogen atoms with different chemical surroundings, which gives different separated N 1s peaks in the measured spectra. By comparing the intensity of the different nitrogen atoms it is then possible to determine the orientation of the molecule on the TiO2 surface.143,144

Since PES is a very surface sensitive technique, the estimation of the dye coverage is true for the outermost surface of the TiO2 film. The orientation and coverage of the dye is very important on the electron lifetime and device performance of cobalt electrolyte based DSCs. The blocking effect of the dye and its effect on electron lifetime have been studied by several groups.147–150

The chemical bond between the dye and the semiconductor surface may also be studied using XPS. The binding group of the dye to the TiO2 surface is usually a carboxylic group and by studying the O 1s core level, it is possible to investigate the dye binding. However, since dye-sensitization in the solar cell is usually not made in a perfectly clean environment (not ultra-high vacuum), contaminants that contain oxygen are also present on the surface, which makes the analysis more difficult. The binding of the carboxylic group to the TiO2 surface has therefore been studied in a UHV for a model-system only containing the ligand with the carboxylic group and a very clean TiO2 surface. The results suggest that the molecule binds with both oxygen atoms in the carboxylic group on the TiO2 surface in a 2-bidentate mode.145,146

The amount of any dopant/surface adsorbate, the percentage of the Ti4+ species and formation of new species in stability tests on the working electrode can be investigated by XPS86,122,139–141,151

3.6.2 Valence electron and molecular orbital analysis. The valence electronic structure is involved in the electron transfer reactions occurring in the solar cell, and is therefore of particular interest for the function of the device. For example the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of a dye molecule and the valence band of TiO2 can be measured by photoelectron spectroscopy. The relation between the energy levels of the dye and the semiconductor surface can therefore be determined by this method.17,140,152 The redox potentials of dye molecules are usually determined using electrochemistry and it is usually difficult to determine the energy levels of the semiconductor (TiO2) when the dye is attached to the surface. This may be a problem since the dye may affect the position of the energy levels of the semiconductor and the combined system may therefore show different energy levels compared to the separate materials. By photoelectron spectroscopy it is possible to determine the energy levels of both the dye and the semiconductor simultaneously, which may be advantageous to understand the possible electron transfer reactions.16

Using photoelectron spectroscopy it is also possible to study if there are any filled states within the band gap of the semiconductor, which can be important for electron transport and recombination of the photogenerated charges.17,153 Recently we have reported on the application of mesoporous microbead films in liquid and solid state based DSCs which show higher open circuit voltage and a faster transport time compared to conventional DSCs.154,155 Photoelectron spectroscopy measurement of the films shows that microbead films have less intra band gap states which in part can explain the behaviour of the device in comparison to standard Dye sol paste films (Fig. 8). Intra band gap states are very important for the electron transport, lifetime and device performance of the solar cell. More numerous and deeper traps can slow down the electron transport and increase the back reaction of electrons and act as recombination centres. This will therefore decrease Jsc and Voc of the device. The nature of these traps is not completely understood9,156–158 but the preparation method of semiconductor films affects the density of traps66,156 and consequently chemical capacitance and recombination resistance of the films.67

It is also possible to measure the elemental composition of the molecular orbitals in the valence electronic structure using resonant PES or measurements with different X-ray energies. 3.8 Conductivity measurements

Conductivity of each DSC component is important and the influencing parameter for the optimization procedure of the device PCE is related for sure to the fundamental understanding of the device behaviour.170–186 The thickness of each device component is typically optimized in terms of contribution to the total device series resistance. For example increasing the thickness of the PEDOT counter electrode on glass enhances the conductivity of the film, and in turn limits the transparency of the electrode.88,170 The same argument is true for other device components, i.e. conductivity of metal back contact versus its light scattering properties in ssDSCs that should be optimized regarding the metal contact thickness. In ssDSCs, the hole conductivity is a limiting factor for device PCE and higher conductivities are desired. Conductivity measurements for DSCs include investigation of the hole conductor, the mesoporous semiconductor and redox mediators in the electrolyte as well as the counter electrode.

Applying a voltage on the two (or four) contacts of the material and recording the current is a conventional way for measuring the conductivity. A simple resistor model can be used to estimate the conductivity from current–voltage data in the dark or in different light intensities (i.e. measuring the photo doping effects). Special care should be taken about the length of the top metal contacts and how the total current will distribute to the semiconductor (it defines the effective thickness and area of the semiconductor engaged in electronic conduction). Depending on the metal work function and the position of the Fermi level in the semiconductor, we have contact resistances which can be of the ohmic or Shottky type. Metal contacts should be chosen appropriately to make ohmic contact with the semiconductor and their corresponding contact resistances should be taken into account in the measurement. In the two point probe measurements, this can be estimated by measuring through different channel lengths. However by using the four point probe technique, one applies the current from two contacts and measures the voltage from another two contacts. In this way, the contact resistance is removed from the measurement.

Due to its crucial importance to the device efficiency, hole conductor conductivity has been widely investigated for ssDSCs.172–174,180 Typically two gold electrodes are evaporated on the film (Fig. 9a) and the voltage–current characteristics are recorded. A simple resistor model can be used to determine the conductivity (σ): σ = L/Rwd. L is the channel length, R is resistance obtained from the I–V curve, d is the thickness of the film and w is the channel width.172 As is shown in Fig. 9, the absence of FTO is necessary here to measure the contribution of only the hole conductor and moreover the presence of a mesoporous scaffold is necessary to take into account the film porosity in the real device.

3.9.2 Spectroelectrochemistry. In spectroelectrochemistry the optical changes of the device components (typically dye covered TiO2 electrodes) under different electrochemical conditions are recorded. The setup consists of a three electrode electrochemical cell in which UV-Vis absorption of the electrode is simultaneously monitored during the electrochemical reactions. Using different applied bias voltages on the dyed electrode in the neat electrolyte, one can alter the populations of the dye/oxidized dyes or hole conductor oxidized molecules according to the Nernst equation (eqn (17)) and monitor the changes in the absorption spectra of the electrode.218,219

By applying a step potential (positive enough to oxidize dye) to the dyed electrode and simultaneously monitoring the oxidation current and absorption changes, coloration efficiency (CE(λ)) and delta extinction coefficient (Δε) can be estimated.85

These data will be of essential importance for transient kinetic absorption spectroscopy in which the fingerprints of the oxidized dye and its extinction coefficient are necessary to quantify the charge recombination and dye regeneration kinetics after the excitation of the dye (see Section 5.1).220

The TiO2 working electrode should be thin enough to minimize the optical reflections and potential drops in the electrode. If the applied potential became too positive, it is possible that each dye is oxidized with more than one electron or even desorbed from the surface that should be taken into account. The extinction coefficient of the reduced dye (which is different from the excited state dye) can be measured in the same way as described above by using a negative reduction potential.

DFT calculations can be combined with spectroelectrochemistry to find out the origin of different absorption changes of the dye in terms of orbital energies and physical locations of donor/acceptors in the dye structure.197,221

Hole hopping is a lateral diffusion of holes in between the dyes in DSC working electrodes. Charge collection efficiency of solid state solar cells can be improved by the hole hopping,222 also the Stark effect is strongly dependent on that (Section 5.3). It can be measured by laser spectroscopy163 and cyclic voltammetry or spectroelectrochemistry.223 Using no under layer on the FTO and measuring the cyclic voltammogram of the dyed electrode, current peak in positive potentials corresponds to the hole hopping current (in positive potentials the TiO2 film is an insulator and the measured current is just from hole hopping between the dyes). The hole hopping rate and its corresponding activation energy, reorganization energy and diffusion coefficient can also be measured by CV.223

In the case of nanostructured semiconducting electrodes with a distribution of intra band gap states below the CB edge, scanning the applied bias from positive to negative potentials, the trap states of un-dyed films will be filled by the electrons. Injected charge to the electrode (from integration of current versus time) in each potential is proportional to density of trap states of un-dyed films in that particular potential.209 Monitoring the absorption spectra changes gives information about the energy position and the extinction coefficient of electrons in those states studied by spectroelectrochemistry.224 For light scattering samples one can use electrolyte electroreflectance (EER) spectroscopy instead.225 Spectroelectrochemistry has also been used to rationalize the Stark effect and oxidized dye contribution in photoinduced spectroscopy of DSCs (see also Section 5.3).226

3.10 Isoelectric point and the zeta potential measurements

Measurements of surface charge and interface potential drops for semiconductor nanoparticles have been implemented to further understand the device performance of liquid electrolyte DSCs. The surface charge of nanoparticles and the so called Helmholtz layer surrounding the particle in contact with the electrolyte cause a certain potential difference between the particle surface and the electrolyte environment. The interaction with the electrolyte results in a potential of the nanoparticle called zeta potential. The zeta potential cannot be directly measured however can be estimated from electrophoretic mobility measurement.227 The isoelectric point is defined as the pH at which the particle statistically has no net surface charge. Zeta potential measurements have been used to evaluate the anion uptake of magnesia (MgO) nanoparticles and their effect on the electron recombination kinetics in the liquid electrolyte DSCs.228 The zeta potential for nanoparticles of a certain metal oxide depends on the electrolyte environment, surface defects, oxygen or metal richness of the surface as well as the preferential crystal planes on the nanoparticle surface. It can directly affect the Helmholtz capacitance and the photoinduced Stark effects (Section 5.3).

A higher isoelectric point of the particle means a more basic surface and the ability of higher dye uptake229,230 (for dyes with carboxylic or phosphonic anchoring groups). The cation and anion uptake for alumina, magnesia and silica nanoparticles and their dependency on the particle isoelectric point in the quasi solid state DSCs have been reported by Mohanty et al.231

4. Device characterization

4.1 Current–voltage measurements

Solar cells are evaluated by power conversion efficiency (PCE) which is calculated from the current–voltage spectrum of the device. PCE is proportional to the amount of delivered electricity and important commercial and also economical parameters of the photovoltaic devices are declared by PCE i.e. efficiency per mass, area or cost. By illuminating the solar cell, scanning the voltage and measuring the current using a potentiostat/sourcemeter, we can plot the current–voltage curve of the cell. The photovoltage at zero current and photocurrent at zero voltage are called open circuit voltage (Voc) and short circuit current (Jsc). PCE of the solar cell is the ratio of maximum solar cell power to the intensity of incoming light. The maximum power point is defined in the point that has a maximum value for the product of current and voltage (Fig. 11). Under open circuit conditions there is no current, and electrons have filled the trap states of TiO2 up to the Fermi level and trapped electrons are in equilibrium with conduction band electrons.40 Equilibrium density of electrons depends on the recombination rate krec;232 in Voc, electron generation rate G is equal to the recombination rate. Considering first order kinetic recombination, krec depends on the density of electrons n, and the electron lifetime τ:

The higher the lifetime is, the higher the density of electrons in the film will be. Open circuit voltage of the device is the difference between the Fermi levels of counter and working electrodes, in order to have higher Voc we can reduce the recombination rate or lower the Fermi level of the counter electrode (which is almost equal to the electrolyte Redox potential). For example the redox potential of electrolytes with cobalt complex redox couples is usually more positive than the redox potential of the standard iodide based electrolyte. The electrolytes with cobalt complexes therefore usually result in higher voltages of the solar cell devices. In low surface area working electrodes (nanostructures with dimensions larger than 30 nm) it is possible to have band bending on semiconductor nanostructures in which resulting electric fields can reduce the recombination rate and increase Voc.74,233,234 Equivalently, keeping the recombination rate fixed and increasing the electron generation rate (G) Voc will be increased.

Under short circuit conditions the Fermi level at the contacts is basically the same. Theoretical calculations and experimental measurements show that in short circuit there is a gradient of the Fermi level position within the TiO2 film thickness.24,235 The Fermi level difference is a driving force for the electron collection considering that the recombination rate in the short circuit is an order of magnitude lower than open circuit conditions. Here the generated electrons will diffuse to the working electrode and holes to the counter electrode. Diffusion is the rate limiting process in the electronic transport and depends on the light intensity.33 Stronger light harvestings and higher light intensities result in more generated electrons and more filled traps, which means faster transport in DSC working electrodes. The effective diffusion coefficient in the film (Dn) depends on the diffusion coefficient of free electrons in TiO2 (D0) and the trapping rate between trapped and free electrons.40 Lower trap densities and one dimensional structures promote faster transport and higher Fermi levels at the same charge density.75 The most important factor for achieving high current is high light harvesting efficiency; and in efficient nanoparticulate based liquid DSCs the collection efficiency is near 100%.10

The FF depends on the resistance of the device which is mainly composed of series resistance, diffusion resistance and shunt (recombination) resistance. High diffusion and series resistances and lower recombination resistances cause the FF to go down. Near the Jsc point the recombination rate is much lower than that at Voc and the series and diffusion resistances are dominant. Near the Voc point the current is low (low series potential loss) and the dominant resistance is the recombination resistance which can be basically calculated from slope of the tangential line from the current–voltage curve.

4.1.1 Importance of the mask, light source and scan rate. For proper evaluation of the efficiency, the size of the mask should be the same as the active area.12,13 The three dimensional hetero-structure of the DSCs can absorb light equally from different incident directions and DSCs are not sensitive to the direction of incoming light. If the incoming light is not 100% perpendicular to the surface, bigger masks allow penetration of excess light (including ambient diffusive light) into the DSC and overestimation of efficiency. A smaller mask can also underestimate the efficiency. In this case, after the dye excitation followed by injection of electrons, the Fermi level in the whole device area is not the same; covered parts can suck some percentage of electrons and lower the efficiency.

Another important factor for the measurement is the light source; the spectrum of the light source should be exactly the same as the solar spectrum. Usually a xenon lamp together with optical filters can simulate the solar spectrum. A stable reference silicon diode with a known spectral response can be used for the calibration of the intensity of the sun simulator. A correction factor is needed to account for the photocurrent differences between reference and test cells.12 There are some edge effects on the edge of the mask and a slightly smaller mask than the active area is the best choice for the IV measurements.12

In the current measurement, voltage is increased step by step with a certain scan rate. After applying a step to increase the voltage, there is a waiting time for the device to reach the current equilibrium. Delay of the scanning rate is very important to be long enough in order to reach equilibrium in the current (due to increasing the voltage). Dependence of cell parameters on the scan rate is discussed in ref. 12. If the scan rate is too fast, there is no time for the photocurrent to be stabilized after the voltage increase and leads to some uncertainties.12 The scan rate of IV measurement is of crucial importance for correct evaluation of perovskite based solar cells which show anomalous hysteresis.236

5. Photo-induced spectroscopy

In this section, we will summarize the techniques which measure optical responses of the system following a perturbation by light. We have roughly sub-divided this section into techniques which use short laser pulses for excitation (pulsed laser spectroscopy) such as transient absorption spectroscopy (TAS) and time-resolved fluorescence spectroscopy and techniques, which used an on/off modulated source (photo-induced absorption spectroscopy). We also include the discussion of the Stark effect and electroabsorption spectroscopy in this section as understanding this effect is important for the interpretation of transient and photo-induced absorption spectroscopies.

5.1 Pulsed laser spectroscopy

Pulsed laser techniques are typically used to measure the kinetics of the electron transfer reactions (see Section 2.2) within the solar cell. Electron injection, recombination and dye regeneration times, also dynamics of other photo-induced processes such as the Stark effect can be monitored by these types of methods.

The underlying concept of laser spectroscopy is to use a laser pulse to excite the sample (the pump pulse) and then to follow the induced changes in the sample over time. In time-resolved fluorescence spectroscopy, the emission from a sample is monitored over time (see Section 5.1.3). In transient absorption spectroscopy, the absorption of a second light beam (the probe) by the sample is monitored. The change in absorbance (ΔA(λ)) is then calculated from the intensity of the transmitted probe beam in the presence of the pump (I(λ)pumped) and the intensity of the transmitted probe beam in the absence of the pump (I(λ)unpumped) according to:

 
ΔA(λ) = −log(I(λ)pumped/I(λ)unpumped)(35)
For dye-sensitized solar cells, ΔA may contain contributions from various transient species and processes, such as ground-state bleaching of the dye, absorption of the excited or the oxidised state of the dye, absorption of electrons in TiO2 and holes in the electrolyte/hole conductor, stimulated emission from the excited state of the dye and finally shifts of the ground state absorption spectrum of the dye due to the Stark effect263,264 (Section 5.3). Which species and processes are observed depend on the time scale of the measurement, and TAS can be subdivided into ultrafast TAS and nano-microsecond TAS.

In ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy, both pump and probe pulses are generated from the same (femtosecond) laser and time resolution is achieved through the variation of the relative arrival times of the pulses at the sample (Section 5.1.2). A continuous probe beam can be used for measurements on longer time scales and is typically combined with a nanosecond pulsed laser as the pump (nano-millisecond TAS, Section 5.1.3).

5.1.1 Time resolved fluorescence spectroscopy. In time-resolved fluorescence spectroscopy, a short light pulse is used to excite the sample and the resulting emission of the sample is measured as a function of time. For dye-sensitized solar cells, it is typically of interest to measure the excited state lifetimes of the sensitizer either in solution or attached to films by monitoring its emission. Depending on the desired time-resolution, there are different methods for detecting the fluorescence. For a time resolution of up to nanoseconds, fast-detection electronics can be used.

Time-Correlated Single Photon Counting (TCSPC) is a technique for measuring fluorescence lifetimes with a picosecond time resolution.265 In this technique, only one emitted photon can be analysed per laser pulse and histograms of the various arrival times of the emitted photons make up the fluorescence decay curve. The count rate of the measurement has to be kept low, in order to ensure that the decay does not become biased by early arriving photons.

Fluorescence up-conversion spectroscopy can be used to measure fluorescence with sub-picosecond time resolution266 and has been used to study electron injection rates in DSCs.267 In this technique, the sample is excited with a femtosecond laser pulse. The fluorescence of the sample is then frequency mixed in a nonlinear crystal with a probe pulse from the same laser to generate frequency mixed radiation, which is then detected. The frequency mixing only occurs when the probe is present in the crystal and therefore time resolution of the measurement is within the laser pulse width. By varying the arrival time of the probe pulse at the crystal in relation to the excitation, the kinetics of emission can be measured.

Emission from the sensitizers’ excited state is together with non-radiative decay back to the ground state: one of the loss mechanisms competing with electron injection in dye-sensitized solar cells. Emission intensities can therefore be taken as a measure of the excited state concentration at a particular time and emission rate constants (kobs) can be related to injection rates (kinj) by:

 
kobs = kinj + k0(36)
where k0 is the rate of excited state relaxation (of both radiative and non-radiative relaxation combined).

5.1.2 Ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy. Ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy (ufTAS) can be used to monitor processes occurring in a dye-sensitized solar cell following from femtoseconds to a few nanoseconds after excitation. Typically, the main process studied in this time regime is electron injection from the dye to the metal oxide18,30,269–271 but also fast regeneration by electrolytes272 or solid state hole conductors220,273,274 has been measured.

A schematic diagram of the main components of a typical ufTAS set-up is shown in Fig. 15. The pulses from a femtosecond Ti-sapphire laser are split into two separate beams to create the pump and the probe for measurement. Typically, an optical parametric amplifier (OPA) is used to change the wavelength of the fundamental laser to a wavelength desired for sample excitation. The pump pulse can then be sent through a chopper before it hits the sample in order to reduce its repetition rate by half, so that there is only a pump pulse at the sample for every second probe pulse. Either the pump or the probe (shown as the probe in Fig. 15) will have a delay state in its path so that the relative arrival times of the two pulses at the sample can be varied. Before reaching the sample, the probe pulse is focussed on a crystal (for example made of sapphire or calcium fluoride) where a white light continuum is generated. What wavelength range is generated depends on the set-up but is most commonly in visible or near infrared wavelengths and could also be infrared for infrared transient absorption spectroscopy or the terahertz frequency regime for terahertz transient absorption spectroscopy. Finally, the transmitted probe light is detected with a suitable detector after the sample and the difference in absorption is calculated from the probe pulses where the pump was presented on the sample and where it was not. By measuring transient absorption spectra at different pump–probe delay times, ΔA can be determined as a function of both wavelength and time. The time resolution of the experiment is determined by the cross-correlation of the pump and probe pulses and can therefore be very high.


Fig. 15 Schematic diagram of the main components in an ultrafast transient absorption set-up.

There are different contributions, which can be observed in the ufTAS spectra of dye-sensitized solar cells:

(1) Bleaching of the dye’s ground state due to the depopulation of the ground-state

(2) Absorption of the dye’s excited state due to the population of the excited state by the pump pulse

(3) Stimulated emission from the dye’s excited state back to the ground state. Photons from the probe pulse can induce this process and therefore the emitted photon will travel in the same direction as the probe pulse and be detected. This signal shows up as negative ΔA in the ufTAS spectra with approximately the same shape of the dye’s emission spectrum and gives a measure of the concentration of the excited state.

(4) Absorption of the products formed in electron transfer reactions. These are the oxidized dyes and electrons in TiO2 for electron injection into TiO2. If electron injection is followed by ultrafast regeneration, also the oxidised species of the redox electrolyte or the hole conductor could be observed.

(5) A shift in the ground-state absorption spectrum due to the Stark effect226,263,264 (ΔA) has the shape of the first derivative of the dye’s absorption spectrum (see Section 5.3).

When analysing electron injection kinetics using ufTAS data, care has to be taken when selecting a wavelength for showing the kinetics of the oxidized dye.275 For many dyes, there may be significant overlap between the absorption spectrum of the excited and the oxidized dye. Selecting a wavelength where both species absorb could lead to misinterpretation of injection kinetics. A further issue of transient absorption spectroscopy can be that measurements are carried out under different conditions compared to the working conditions of dye-sensitized solar cells. Many of the studies showing ultrafast electron injection into TiO2 were carried out on dyed films in the absence of an electrolyte. Recent studies have shown that injection times observed for such films do not correspond to electron injection times observed in the presence of an optimized DSC electrolyte.272,275,276 A further drawback of ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy can be that the peak powers used are orders of magnitude higher than 1 Sun illumination. These high laser powers can influence recombination kinetics and thus lead to a skewing of results.

Instead of generating visible or NIR light to be used as probe light, terahertz or mid-IR pulses have been generated and used as probe pulses in transient absorption experiments. Terahertz radiation is absorbed by free charges and terahertz spectroscopy can therefore be used to study the time-resolved conductivity of a sample (Section 3.8). It has been used to study the electron injection of dyes into TiO2 and other semiconductors.277,278

Mid-IR probes can be used to study molecular vibrations279 as well as the concentration of injected electrons at wavelengths where molecular vibrations are absent due to the broad absorption of conduction band electrons in the mid-IR region.272,276,279

5.1.3 Nano- to millisecond transient absorption spectroscopy. For transient absorption measurements where a time-resolution of up to nanoseconds is sufficient, kinetics can be measured electronically using fast detectors. Therefore, pump and probe pulses can be generated using separate light sources and delay lines are not required to achieve time resolution. In nano- to millisecond TAS, pump pulses are typically generated by nanosecond lasers such as Nd:YAG lasers or nitrogen-laser pumped dye lasers. The probe light is often provided by a continuous white light source employing monochromators before and/or after the sample. Typical detectors for measurements of time traces are silicon photodiodes or photomultiplier tubes (PMTs).

When measuring the kinetics of dye-sensitized samples on a nanosecond or a longer timescale, electron injection is usually completed and therefore one of the main signals observed in nano- to millisecond TAS are those arising from the oxidized dye.

5.2 Photo-induced absorption spectroscopy

Photo-induced absorption spectroscopy is a relatively simple method, which can be used to qualitatively assess injection and regeneration efficiencies of dye-sensitized solar cells.284 Rather than using a short laser pulse as the pump in the experiment, a monochromatic light source with an on–off modulation is used to excite the sample. The change in absorption of a probe light by the sample is measured following this on–off modulation, resulting in the determination of the quasi-steady state spectra of the transient species under illumination. The PIA spectra of complete devices can show what transient species are present during solar cell operation, if pump intensities providing a similar amount of absorbed photons as 1 Sun illumination are used. 6. Voltage/current modulation techniques

6.1 Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is one of the most frequently used characterization techniques for DSCs. In this method a small sinusoidal voltage modulation (dV, typically ∼ 10 mV) is superimposed on the potential that is applied to the DSC by a potentiostat. The resulting current modulation (di) is monitored with respect to both amplitude and phase shift. The complex impedance is defined as z = dV/di; z = z′ + iz′′, where z′ and z′′ represent the real and imaginary parts of the impedance. The real part reflects the resistance, while the imaginary part is affected mainly by capacitance (negative z′′) and inductance (positive z′′). A wide range of frequencies, typically in the range of 105 to 10−2 Hz, is measured to create an impedance spectrum. The impedance spectrum is plotted as a Nyquist plot, (−z′′ vs. z) or as a Bode plot wherein the parameters (z′, z′′, |z|, phase) are plotted versus logarithm of frequency. Equivalent circuits, corresponding to a typical DSC, are used to analyze the impedance spectrum and extract the related information i.e. charge transport or recombination resistances. They are composed of standard electrical components, such as resistors (R) and capacitors (C), as well special components, such as the Warburg impedance that describes diffusion processes.

EIS measurements on DSCs are performed under dark or illumination conditions. Parameters such as the series resistance, chemical capacitance, transport resistance, recombination resistance, counter electrode charge transfer resistance and capacitance, as well as Warburg impedance can be obtained from the EIS spectra.67,298–300Fig. 21 shows a typical EIS spectrum of a liquid electrolyte DSC. If the characteristic time constants of the different processes are well separated, resistances and time constant can be obtained graphically from the EIS spectrum. Usually, fitting with an equivalent circuit is performed for further analysis of DSC parameters. Suitable equivalent circuits have been derived for liquid electrolyte and solid-state DSCs, as well as for perovskite solar cells, which can be used to characterize and simulate device behavior according to its virtual electrical components.301–304 Any virtual electrical component is related to a particular parameter (i.e. a reaction rate or density of states) in the device. The standard model used for fitting of the data is the transmission line model for DSCs, see Fig. 22.305–308 This model can be more simplified when using certain conditions, such as open circuit conditions under illumination. Fabregat et al. have written a comprehensive review about EIS of DSCs, explaining the interpretation of data for different materials in DSCs.16

The origins of high open circuit voltage in polymer electrolyte DSCs have been investigated recently by Kim et al. using EIS.327 They conclude that the capture of Li+ ions by the electrolyte polymers and the formation of a polymeric passivation layer on the TiO2 nano particle surface cause a CB downward shift and retarded electron recombination kinetics resulting in a high open circuit voltage.

6.2 Small light perturbation photovoltage/current response

Small light perturbation techniques, where a small square-wave, sine wave or pulse of light is added to a much larger constant bias light intensity, and the resulting photovoltage or photocurrent is monitored, offer rapid insight into some of the most important electron transfer reactions in DSCs. If the perturbation is small (<10% of the bias light), electron concentration in the mesoporous TiO2 can be treated as being constant, and electron transfer reactions appear to have simple first order kinetics. 6.3 Large light perturbation photovoltage/current techniques

Additional information on DSCs is obtained from the large light perturbation method, where the light is switched on or off.
6.3.1 Charge extraction. Duffy et al. introduced the charge extraction method for DSCs: the cell is illuminated (keeping under certain open (short) circuit conditions) and then the light is switched off, while the cell is switched to short circuit conditions and its current is integrated versus time to give the extracted charge under open (short) circuit conditions (QOC or QSC).361 The amount of stored charge in the working electrode is indicative of the density of states in the mesoporous semiconductor. Comparative studies can indicate band shifts that are important factors for the evaluation of the cell performance.123,357,362,363 Under UV irradiation, photo-induced positive states are formed at TiO2, which can shift the TiO2 CB band as well.286

For charge extraction measurements, recombination of electrons to electrolyte species is a source of error, which can be significant. Because of the high recombination rates for solid-state DSCs, this method is not reliable. A capacity method as developed by Snaith et al. can be used instead: the capacity versus voltage is measured under working conditions and by integration of capacitance versus voltage the charge is calculated.37

6.3.2 Measurement of the Fermi level in mesoporous electrodes. Under short circuit conditions there is a non-uniform distribution of electrons in the film thickness, which results in a Fermi level gradient. This Fermi level is around 200 mV lower than open circuit conditions.357 By using a switching method, the approximate average Fermi level can be determined: the cell is first kept under short circuit conditions under light, then, simultaneously, the light is witched off and the cell is switched to open circuit conditions. This gives a time dependence of the potential that was built up from the remaining electrons in mesoporous TiO2, from which VSC, and average of the Fermi level in the mesoporous electrode for DSCs, kept under short circuit conditions, can be determined.342,347,357 Higher VSC, at the same current density, can be explained by a higher specific surface area of the particles, a lower electronic diffusion coefficient or because of band bending in bigger particles.

An alternative method for measuring VSC requires the deposition of an additional titanium electrode on top of the mesoporous TiO2 films. The surface of the metal is passivated by thermal oxidation and does not lead to an additional recombination pathway for electrons in TiO2. The potential of the additional electrode can be measured under operating conditions and directly gives the quasi-Fermi level at the outside of the mesoporous TiO2 films.365 Both methods give similar results.

Simulations of small/large modulation of light intensity experiments with electron diffusion equations/random walk models have been performed by several groups to understand and rationalize the device performance and its changes under different conditions.8,15,69,235,360,366–368 Most of the described techniques in the present Section 6 are coupled to simulations and models in order to further elucidate information about the DSC parameters (such as reaction orders and activation energies).

7. Remarks on DSC characterization

7.1 Remarks on solid state DSCs

Introduced by Grätzel and Bach, solid state DSCs have an important architecture, which eliminates the need of a liquid electrolyte in the device. In fact, recently developed highly efficient perovskite solar cells are derived from the ssDSC architecture. There are several review reports on the optimization of device components such as the hole conductor, back contact and the dye.19,369–372 For ssDSCs, the same presented characterization methods can be employed for evaluation of LHE, injection and regeneration efficiencies as well as charge collection efficiency in the device. Due to a higher recombination rate in ssDSCs, special care is needed for measuring the transport time in these devices – see Section 6.2.2. ssDSCs are more sensitive to the thickness and conductivity of each component compared to liquid electrolyte based DSCs. Pore filling of the hole conductor, interface recombination and conductivity of the hole conductor are still the main issues to be considered towards fabrication of highly efficient devices.77,370

In ssDSCs, FF losses are because of series resistance (conductivity) of components or recombination losses, while potential losses are mainly due to the overpotential needed for electron injection/dye regeneration; moreover the photocurrent losses stem from limited LHE/photon absorption by the device.369 Since the recombination rate is higher in solid state DSCs, the role of blocking layers is more prominent than in liquid DSCS. Furthermore as a result of a higher recombination rate and poor pore filling of a hole transporter within the mesoporous film, the limited device thicknesses necessitate the use of very high extinction coefficient dyes.83 Thus LHE is an issue which is limited by the device thickness.

Under short circuit conditions, device efficiency is limited by the diffusion length of electrons therefore new TiO2 structures with higher conductivities and larger crystallite sizes together with higher surface areas37,373 are under investigation. On the other hand in the higher charge densities the conductivity of spiro can be a limiting factor and doping of spiro with chemical dopants or employing the photodoping ability of Li is implemented by several groups to achieve high efficiencies.171,174,181,374–376

New dyes with a higher extinction coefficient and broader spectra83 and new hole transport materials are on the way to increase the LHE and lower the overpotential voltage losses in the device.346,350,377 Recently polymer based hole transporters are reaching 6.8% efficiency by using PEDOT polymers.378 Utilizing thicker TiO2 films became possible by means of in situ light driven polymerization of EDOT on the dye sensitized films.379

The best reported ssDSC efficiency so far is 7.7% in which spiro is the hole conductor doped with an additive dopant.380 Studying and optimizing the device continue while the ssDSC concepts are considered as insightful to help obtain higher efficiencies for perovskite solar cells.

7.2 Remarks on liquid–electrolyte DSCs

The only class of DSCs which are really commercialized in the market are liquid–electrolyte DSCs; they have a range of colours and transparencies and benefit from long term stability/high efficiencies in the low light intensities.1,6

The efficiency improvement rate during the last 10 years was so slow although the expected theoretical efficiency for liquid DSCs is about 20% with supposed 920 mV Voc and 30 mA cm−2Jsc.71 Engineering the dyes and electrolytes to reduce the potential losses and employing new redox mediators with more positive redox potentials are the main strategies to further increase the voltage. Voc values of more than 1 volt have been reported and are easily attainable with new metal complex redox mediators,71 although the corresponding potential loss is still too much, i.e. for the record cell dye in the cobalt electrolyte based device it is about 900 mV. At the moment the highest obtained efficiency is 14.7%.72

Improving the TiO2 Fermi level using novel nanostructures with lower trap densities, decreasing the dye regeneration overpotential by either using new redox mediators or dye molecules, reduction of recombination losses by modification of dye linker units, decreasing the thickness of TiO2 films using the higher extinction coefficient dyes are among the recent efforts and strategies to improve the open circuit voltage of the device.

On the other hand, employing new dyes with higher light absorption and higher injection efficiency is the main strategy to improve the photocurrent. Hence, tuning the optical band gap of the dye to harvest enough light and keep Voc high enough is vital. Photocurrent densities of more than 20 mA cm−2 are reported using Ruthenium dyes but for the record efficiency porphyrin dye, Jsc is still below 20 mA cm−2.71 Decreasing the oxidized dye recombination, modification of the dye with electron deficient moieties, harvesting of more photons by changing the E0–0 values of the dye and having the colourful dyes for transparent market applications are some of the hot topics for dye engineering in the DSC field.

Due to the recent success of cobalt complexes as redox mediators in DSCs, the research on other metal complexes381 with less optical absorption, a smaller molecular size and more positive redox potential becomes more prominent recently. The larger size of these complexes compared to iodide necessitates the optimization of working electrode porosity to avoid mass transport limitation in the device.

Nevertheless there is still some ongoing research on the novel metal oxide architectures and advanced photonic managements of the scattering layer, so far the initial mesoporous titanium dioxide films have the best performance and have been used in all reported record efficiencies. IPCEs of close to 90% and charge collection efficiencies of close to 100% are achieved by mesoporous TiO2 nanoparticles, leaving the main bottle neck of efficiency more towards the dyes and redox mediator side.3,5,6,382–390

7.3 Remarks on quasi solid state DSCs

The device structure and the concepts of quasi solid state DSCs (qssDSCs) are similar to liquid DSCs; and in principle similar characterization techniques have been used to investigate and improve the device performance of qssDSCs.391 In qssDSCs, a higher thermal stability is obtained due to the negligible electrolyte vapour pressure and better device stability is achieved through the addition of an inert polymer or a polymer matrix such as poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA)392 or poly(vinylidene fluoride-co-hexafluoropropylene) (PVDF–HFP),393 and/or addition of metal oxide nanostructures such as silica,394 or alumina395 in the liquid electrolyte or gelation of ionic liquid electrolytes.396

The same bottle necks for the device PCE of liquid DSCs are valid for qssDSCs as well. However the maximum reported efficiency of 9.61%397 and maximum achievable current density for qssDSCs are lower than the reported values for liquid DSCs. The presence of metal oxide nano particles or the organic polymers in the electrolyte affects the pore filling and viscosity of the electrolyte and the diffusion of redox mediators which lowers the efficiency. Thus the thinner sealants and smaller redox couples are more preferable. The mass transport is a more prominent issue377,398,399 and the corresponding ionic transport in the electrolyte is evaluated by impedance spectroscopy (Section 6.1), light intensity dependence of current density (Section 4.1.2) or CV (3.9.1).195,196,327,377,391,392,398 Furthermore, the presence of the polymer/nanoparticles in the electrolyte results in new interactions with the TiO2/dye interface and/or ionic species in the electrolyte and thus affects the device efficiency.228,327 Absorption, rheological and thermal (TG) analyses are particularly employed to further characterize the gel electrolytes. The main efforts have been devoted to achieving better ionic conductivities, less mass transport limitations and also reaching better pore filling of the polymer electrolyte in the pores of the photoanode. We refer the reader to a recently published review about the characterization techniques for quasi solid state polymer electrolyte DSCs for further information.196

8. Concluding remarks

As DSC research continues going forward, the need of more fundamental research becomes more clear and essential for developing a low cost, large area, highly stable and efficient solar cell. For stability tests, comprehensive characterization of different light/temperature induced chemical changes and the corresponding impacts on the device efficiency are vital. Under thermal stress DSCs can change from the situation of Rrec ≫ Rtr to Rrec ≪ Rtr and alter the impedance behaviour of the cell changes respectively.320 Relative positions of the redox Fermi level and the CB position, the diffusion coefficient of the redox mediator and the electron lifetime of the TiO2 electrons are dependent on the temperature of the device40,342,400 and can be further altered by temperature induced changes in the long term stability tests. A 20 year lifetime for DSCs is predicted401 and studying high viscous electrolytes such as MPN is currently pursued specially for stability tests.169,402 Besides, in a very recent study, Jiang et al. have reported the 2000 hour stability of cobalt electrolyte based DSCs with acetonitrile.403 Mass spectroscopy can be an interesting method for studying the chemical reaction in stability tests.404
