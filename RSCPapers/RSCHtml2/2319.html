Research progress on two-dimensional (2D) halide organic–inorganic hybrid perovskites

Abstract

Since its discovery, perovskite has experienced tremendous development. Furthermore, due to the outstanding stability of two-dimensional (2D) perovskites, they have been attracting increasing attention. A reduction in the dimensions of perovskites and the introduction of long organic chains yield new structures and properties. As an emerging semiconductor material with high stability, 2D perovskites have many applications in the field of optoelectronics. This review mainly describes the development and application of 2D perovskites in recent years. Firstly, we introduce the basic structure and classification of 2D perovskites. Also, we discuss the change in their characteristics and performances with a variation in the number of inorganic layers. Next, 2D perovskites are classified according to their applications in different fields. Finally, a summary and outlook based on the current development trends of 2D perovskites are presented.

1. Introduction

Currently, organic–inorganic halide perovskites (OIHPs) have experienced tremendous development, and their history can be traced back to the discovery of CaTiO3 in 1839. Wells first synthesized the perovskite structure CsPbX3 in 1893, and Weber prepared OIHPs for the first time in 1978.1 Further, up to 90s, Mitzi studied the structure and optical properties of perovskites, enriching relevant knowledge in this field.2 Modern perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have been developed based on dye-sensitized solar cells. Since halide perovskites were first applied in solar cells as a sensitizer in 2009, they have achieved a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 25.5% after long-term research.3–5 Three-dimensional (3D) perovskite materials have many excellent characteristics such as adjustable band gap, high carrier mobility, high external quantum efficiency (EQE) and large light absorption coefficient, together with physical properties such as magnetism and ferroelectricity. These characteristics have gradually deepened the research on perovskite. However, although great progress has been achieved, the application of 3D perovskites has been limited by their instability.6–9 Accordingly, researchers have developed various methods to increase the stability of perovskites, such as wrapping them with a protective layer,10 adding a certain amount of additives to the perovskite precursor solution,11,12 and reducing their dimensionality.13,14 Reducing the dimensionality of perovskites can yield two-dimensional (2D) perovskites. The introduction of organic spacer cations (L) in 2D perovskites will hinder the intrusion of oxygen and water. Also, an alternating arrangement of an organic spacer cation layer and perovskite octahedral inorganic layer will form a multiple quantum well (MQW) structure in energy. It is difficult for excitons to dissociate into electron–hole pairs in the inorganic layer to transfer energy through the organic layer. Therefore, excitons are directionally transported in the inorganic layer between the organic layers. The stacking of crystal grains will also affect the transmission of excitons. A disordered grain arrangement will adversely hinder the effective transfer of energy.15,16 The preparation method can be changed by adding Cl−,17,18 Cs+,19 SCN−,16,20,21 and different solvents to make perovskite grains more regular. Another problem is the environmental pollution.22,23 Most PSC devices are based on Pb, which is a toxic heavy metal. However, Sn-based, Ge-based perovskite and double-perovskite (DP) devices currently lag behind lead-based perovskite devices, and there is still a long way to go for the development of 2D perovskites.

The arrangement of the 2D perovskite octahedral unit cell can be obtained from the specific crystal plane of 3D perovskite, and the resulting layered perovskite is separated by L. The number of octahedrons in the inorganic layer between adjacent organic spacer layers is defined as n. The value of n can be determined by X-ray diffraction (XRD), grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS), photoluminescence spectroscopy (PL), ultraviolet visible spectroscopy (UV-vis) and other characterization methods.24 Changing the value of n will change the layer spacing and band gap. As n increases, the layer spacing increases, and the band gap will decrease accordingly. There is a large difference in the dielectric constant between the organic layers and inorganic layers. As the dimensionality decreases, the binding energy between the electron–hole pairs gradually increases. Excitons cannot undergo long-distance transport, but they have a high photoluminescence efficiency. A reduction in dimensionality will also cause second-harmonic generation (SHG),25,26 two-photon absorption (2PA),27,28 third-harmonic generation (THG),29–31 and other nonlinear optical effects. The first solar cell based on the 2D halide perovskite (PEA)2MA2Pb3I10 (PEA = phenylethylammonium) was manufactured in 2014, which achieved a PCE of 4.73% and showed excellent stability.32 Later, researchers improved the preparation process to further increase the PCE of 2D perovskite GA2MA4Pb5I16 (GA = guanidinium) solar cells to 19.3%.33 2D perovskites can form heterojunctions to improve the performance of optoelectronic devices, such as the mixing of different B-site ions,34 2D/3D mixed perovskite heterojunctions35,36 and formation of heterojunctions with other types of 2D materials.37,38 Compared with 3D perovskites, 2D perovskites have larger band gap tunability and environmental stability, and are comparable to a variety of semiconductors in optoelectronic applications, such as in solar cells,26,39,40 light-emitting diodes (LEDs),41–44 detectors,45–47 field effect transistors (FETs),48,49 lasers,15,50,51 resistive switching memories (RSMs)52,53 and other applications.

In this review, we summarize the classification and structure of 2D perovskites. Their basic optical properties and the influence of a change in n are explained, and the application of (100)-2D perovskites in the field of optoelectronics is illustrated in detail. Finally, the development and progress of 2D perovskite are prospected.

2. Characteristics of 2D perovskites

2.1 The structure of 2D perovskites

3D perovskites have an ABX3 structure,54,55 where the A-site ions are generally methylammonium (MA+), formamidinium (FA+) and cesium (Cs+); the B-site ions are metal elements such as Pb, Sn and Ge; and the X-site ions are Cl, Br, and I halide elements. Their crystal structure stability is restricted by the Goldschmidt tolerance factor (t) and the octahedral factor (μ), t = (RA + RX)/√2(RB + RX) and μ = RB/RX, where RA, RB and RX are the radius of the A, B, and X ions, respectively. t and μ of a stable 3D perovskite structure are in the range of 0.8 ≤ t ≤ 1.0 and 0.44 ≤ μ ≤ 0.90, respectively.56 If they are not in this range, the 3D structured network will collapse.57,58 Perovskites possess an octahedral structure with shared corners, where the halogen elements are distributed at the apex of the octahedron. The perovskite octahedral units are stacked to form a 3D perovskite (Fig. 1a and b). For 2D perovskites, the interlayer cations separate the perovskite inorganic layers, and thus t is greater than 1.59 The organic layer and the inorganic layer alternately exist to form an MQW electronic structure, showing a relatively large dielectric constant and exciton binding energy,60 which can generate stable excitons at room temperature (Fig. 1c).

Cutting along the three planes, i.e., (100), (110) and (111), of 3D perovskites and the addition of an aliphatic or aromatic alkylammonium cation L can result in three types of 2D perovskites. Herein, n, m, and q are defined as the number of inorganic layers between the organic spacer cation, L (Fig. 1d–f), with the general structural formula of (L)2An−1BnX3n+1, (L)2AmBmX3m+2 and (L)2Aq−1BqX3q +3, respectively.

2.1.1 (100)-2D perovskite. (100)-oriented perovskites are currently the most common 2D perovskites (Fig. 1d). Three types of perovskites including Ruddlesden–Popper (RP) perovskites,61,62 Dion–Jacobson (DJ) perovskites63,64 and alternating cations in the interlayer space (ACI) perovskites65,66 can be obtained by adjusting the type of L, with the general structural formulas of (L)2An−1BnX3n+1, (L)An−1BnX3n+1, (L)AnBnX3n+1, respectively.

L of the RP structure is a monoamine organic cation, which is connected to other L through van der Waals force. There is a (1/2, 1/2) displacement between the layers of the perovskite inorganic layer. The displacement is related to type and size of L (Fig. 2a).32,67 2D perovskites based on the DJ structure show higher stability than the RP structure, mainly due to the diamino organic cation L. The strong hydrogen bond interaction between the organic layer and the inorganic layer will not cause any relative displacement in the adjacent inorganic layers (Fig. 2b).68,69 The composition of the ACI-type perovskite is close to that of 3D perovskites. The L- and A-site ions are alternately arranged in the spacer layer, and there is a (1/2, 0) displacement between adjacent inorganic layers (Fig. 2c).65,70

2.1.2 (110)-2D perovskites. The general formula for (110)-oriented 2D perovskites is (L)2AmBmX3m+2 (m > 1),87 which have a corrugated layer (Fig. 1e). The L that can stabilize this type of perovskite generally has a high symmetry. (110)-oriented 2D perovskites have broadband white light emission in the visible light range, which is mainly attributed to self-trapped excitons (STEs),88 followed by material defects89,90 and dopants.91 These characteristics give (110)-oriented 2D perovskites great application prospects in white LEDs.92,93 A corrugated layer is composed of an “n × n” structure (n = 2, 3, and 4). 2D perovskites with a corrugated structure of “n × n” include (EDBE)PbBr4 (EDBE = 2,2′(ethylenedioxy)bis(ethylammonium)) (Fig. 2e),89 (N-MEDA)PbBr4 (N-MEDA = N1-methylethane-1,2-diammonium) (“2 × 2”),92 and α-(DMEN)PbBr4 (DMEN = N1,N1-dimethylethane-1,2-diammonium) (“3 × 3”)94 (Fig. 2f). When the composition of the 2D perovskite is (EDBE)PbBr4, there is a hydrogen bond interaction between the ammonium group of the organic cation and ether group of the adjacent organic cation, forming a stable structure.89
2.1.3 (111)-2D perovskites. When the body of the perovskite is divided diagonal to get the (111) plane orientation, the general formula of the resulting 2D perovskite is (L)2Aq−1BqX3q+3 (Fig. 2f). When q = 2, the structure of the perovskite is transformed into A3B2X9 (A = MA, Cs; B = Sb, Bi; X = Cl, Br, I).95–97 However, the structural stability of (111) 2D perovskites is poor, resulting in low PCEs. For example, (MA)3Sb2I9 is one of substitutes for lead-free perovskites, but its PCE still needs to be improved.98 When q = 3, Cs4B2+B23+Cl12 (B2+ = Cd, Cu, Mn; B3+ = Bi, Sb) can be obtained.99 In this type of layered bimetallic perovskite Cs4(CuSb2)Cl12, Cu2+ (or Mn2+, Cd2+) is inserted between Sb3+(Bi3+) to form a layered structure (Fig. 2h).100

2.2 2D perovskite crystal arrangement

The arrangement of 2D perovskites on the substrate has an important effect on perovskite devices. A disorderly distribution of perovskite crystals will hinder the transmission of electrons and holes. In uniform 2D perovskites, electron–hole pair transmission channels can be formed between the organic layers to improve the charge transfer efficiency. 2D perovskites have three types of growth patterns on the substrate, parallel, vertical and non-oriented (Fig. 3a).101 In the parallel pattern, the extension plane of the 2D perovskite inorganic layer is parallel to the substrate, whereas in the vertical pattern, the inorganic plane is perpendicular to the substrate. The parallel and perpendicular crystal arrangements are dependent on the value of n, where when n = 1, most 2D perovskites grow parallel to the substrate and when n > 1, the growth orientation appears out-of-plane.45,67

The perovskite arrangement is also related to the type of L. For 2D DPs, when n = 1, perovskites with 1,3-diaminocyclohexane have reflection peaks such as (020) and (040) in their powder X-ray diffraction pattern (PXRD). These reflection peaks indicate that the 2D perovskite grew perpendicular to the substrate. Cyclohexylamine-based 2D perovskites have (00-2) and (00-4) reflection peaks, which grow parallel to the substrate. 2D perovskites with L as 1-methylpiperidin4-amine are arranged in a non-oriented pattern and show disordered reflection peaks in their PXRD patterns (Fig. 3b).85 Moreover, the length of L has a certain influence on the growth and arrangement of the perovskite crystals. As the organic chain becomes longer, the van der Waals force between the inorganic layers decreases, and the arrangement of the crystal grains tends to be disordered.102

It is not easy to prepare a perovskite film with a uniform n-value distribution. In fact, 2D perovskite films are composed of different n-value components. During the spin coating process, a perovskite with a small n value will be preferentially formed on the substrate, and the phase with a large n value will appear on the top of the film. These n-value phase gradient changes can be characterized by the strength of the different n-value peaks in the PL and UV-vis spectra.103–105 An MQW with a smaller value of n is preferentially stacked parallel to the substrate. When the thickness of the MQW increases (2 ≤ n ≤ 4), a vertical stacking orientation appears. When n is greater than 5, the vertical distribution of the perovskite is dominant (Fig. 3c).106 The order and disorder state of perovskites can be judged by the degree of dispersion of the GIWAXS diffraction spots.107,108 If the perovskite phase is disorder, the GIWAXS spectrum shows a type of ring-shaped spot; on the contrary, clear and concentrated diffraction spots prove that a neatly arranged perovskite structure is formed. Actually, the distribution ratio of the n-phase can be affected by changing the heating-spinning temperature (Fig. 3d). By changing the arrangement and combination of the perovskite crystals, the carrier transmission efficiency inside the crystals can be improved.105

The selection and adjustment of organic solvents are worthy of careful consideration for 2D perovskites given that they affect the growth and arrangement of the perovskite crystals. When dimethyl formamide (DMF) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) are used as organic solvents (DMF/DMSO = 5:5), the formation of a mesophase will be suppressed. 2D perovskites are less affected by the intermediates, and the layers are stacked to form a vertical and orderly crystal arrangement (Fig. 3e).109 The regular and orderly growth arrangement of perovskite crystals can also be promoted by adding suitable additives.34,110

By selecting and improving conductive substrates, the contact, morphology, and crystal arrangement of the 2D perovskite film can be changed to varying degrees. Graphene has a greater preferred orientation in the vertical direction than FTO, and the resulting film surface is smoother.111 The crystallization of the 2D perovskite film can be controlled by vacuum poling treatment, and the n-phase arrangement after treatment is shown in Fig. 3f. The perovskite phases with different n values are evenly arranged together, instead of gradually increasing the n value from the bottom to the top.112 In addition, the hot casting method can change the arrangement of the crystals and improve the stacking of perovskites that are originally disordered or parallel to the substrate.113

The value of n has a significant influence on the characteristics of 2D perovskites. With an increase in n, the layer spacing increases. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) clearly showed the step-like morphology of the 2D perovskite (PEA)2PbBr4, where the height of the step is nearly the thickness of an inorganic layer and an organic layer unit (Fig. 4a).114 For monolayer 2D perovskites prepared via the chemical vapor deposition (CVD) method, increasing n can improve the height profile of MAPbI3.115

2D perovskites with n = 1 have a (00l) reflection peak in their XRD spectrum. When n ≥ 2, (0k0) will gradually replace the reflection peak of (00l), and the crystal arrangement will also change from parallel to vertical. As n increases, the interplanar spacing increases, which is reflected in the XRD pattern, such as a decrease in the 2θ value corresponding to the (080) reflection peak of (PEA)2(MA)n–1PbnI3n+1 (Fig. 4b).116 The distribution of the GIWAXS diffraction spots can also reflect the influence of n. For 2D perovskites with different n, the Qz value of n = 2 is larger than that of n = 1 on the same (020) crystal plane in the GIWAXS spectrum (Fig. 4c).15

The band gap of 2D perovskites is larger than that of 3D perovskites, and the color of the optical images of 2D perovskites is lighter than that of 3D perovskites. The band gap can be adjusted by adjusting n and the ratio of halogen elements. With a decrease in the value of n, the band gap of the (BA)2MAn−1PbnI3n+1 2D perovskite increases and its conductance band minimum (CBM) and valence band maximum (VBM) also increase. CBMs with different n values are higher than that of the TiO2 electron collecting layer, which is beneficial to promote the separation of electrons and holes (Fig. 4d).67 In the UV-vis and PL spectra of 2D perovskites, when n increases, the curve shifts to a high energy band gap (Fig. 4e and f).45 Even if a 2D perovskite with a specific n value is prepared strictly according to the stoichiometric ratio, there will be perovskite phases with different n. Therefore, in the absorption and fluorescence spectra of polycrystalline films, there are different peaks corresponding to the perovskite phase with different n on a curve.17,103,104

As shown in Fig. 4g, the phase composition of the bottom and top of 2D perovskite thin films is different. The closer to the bottom, the greater the proportion of low-n-value phases. The 2D perovskite phase distribution can be characterized by the number and position of the PL peaks.105 The messy distribution of 2D perovskites with different n values will hinder carrier transport and combination. In fact, by using suitable processing methods to form an ordered state from low to high n, the performance of related devices can be improved. Carriers absorb energy in the low n region, quickly pass through the increasing n value region, and finally recombine in the high n phase (Fig. 4h).117 In recent years, this type of 2D perovskite n-value graded distribution has been widely used in optical devices. For example, as shown in Fig. 4i and j, for the (BAB)FAn−1PbnX3n+1 (X = Br, I) 2D perovskite with L as 1,4-bis(aminomethyl)benzene (BAB), when the ratio of BAB to FA was adjusted from 33% (BAB33) to 25% (BAB25), the single-layer perovskite (n = 1) became a 2D perovskite with a multiphase n-value distribution. The gradual existence of a multi-phase n will greatly extend the carrier lifetime and improve the energy transmission efficiency.118

3. Application of 2D perovskites

3.1 Solar cells

One limitation of 3D perovskite materials is that they are susceptible to moisture and oxygen, which reduce their stability.8,9,119,120 Thus, they can be transformed into a yellow non-perovskite phase at room temperature. Sn-based and Ge-based perovskites are easily oxidized to a positive tetravalent chemical state. Accordingly, the decomposition of halide perovskites can be suppressed in various ways to improve their thermochemical stability. One strategy is to reduce their dimensionality. The introduced L can resist the erosion due to oxygen and moisture similar to a protective cover, and it also breaks through the limit of the tolerance factor. As a type of perovskite material, 2D perovskite materials inherit many advantages of 3D perovskite materials with stronger stability, endowing them better application prospects.121–124 2D perovskites also have compositions that can be significantly adjusted, which enriches the means for their modification. At present, the research surrounding 2D perovskites is mainly focused on their preparation, organic amines and halogen ions.
3.1.1 Pb-based 2D perovskite solar cells. 3D perovskites are unstable at room temperature and easily decompose. In contrast, 2D perovskites contain L, which can isolate water and oxygen. In 2014, Smith et al. used the RP 2D perovskite (PEA)2MA2Pb3I10 as a light absorbing layer and used the spin-coating method to fabricate a 2D perovskite solar cell that does not require high temperature annealing (Fig. 5a).32 Most previous methods for the preparation of 2D perovskites involved a spin-coating step. In 2019, Zuo et al. prepared a perovskite film using a simple drop-casting method without spin-coating. The (BA)2MA3Pb4I13 2D perovskite could self-assemble in air to form a uniform and highly oriented film (Fig. 5b). The PCE of this device reached 14.9%.125 Cs+ can effectively promote the formation of uniform and stable crystals of 3D perovskites.126 It plays an important role in the improvement of the surface quality, density of trap states and carrier mobility. Zhang et al. incorporated Cs+ into a 2D perovskite and prepared a PSC based on (BA)2(MA0.95Cs0.05)3Pb4I13. The PCE of the device increased from 12.3% to 13.7%. In the case of no encapsulation and 30% RH, after 1400 h, compared with the initial efficiency, the doping with 5% Cs+ resulted in a reduction of the PCE to only 11% (Fig. 5c).19

3.1.2 Sn-based 2D perovskite solar cells. Most B-site ions of the 2D perovskites used in solar cells are Pb. However, although Pb-based 2D perovskites have achieved high efficiency through various methods, their shortcomings must be considered. Pb, which is not easily recycled in the natural environment, is not easily discharged after being absorbed by the human body and will pollute water sources. Thus, if Pb-based 2D perovskites are used in industry on a large scale, it is necessary to develop special recyclable devices. Accordingly, environmentally friendly lead-free perovskites have gradually become a research focus. Currently, there is no Ge-based 2D perovskite battery, and Cu-based 2D perovskites do not have a particularly high efficiency due to their poor optical properties. The element that can replace Pb in the battery field is mainly Sn. Sn2+ has an ionic radius similar to that of Pb2+, and the fabricated solar cells also have similar photovoltaic characteristics. Its susceptibility to oxidation can be alleviated by various effective methods. However, although Sn-based PSCs have been fabricated, their record PCEs are still at a low level.152,153 One of the problems with Sn perovskites is that Sn2+ is easily oxidized to Sn4+, which leads to the formation of Sn vacancies and high levels of self-p doping.152 The second issue is the generation of defects. Defects play a key role in the degradation process. There are mainly two processes involved in the degradation of perovskites, namely moisture-6,8 and oxygen and light-induced degradation.7 Thus, to slow down these processes, phenylhydrazine hydrochloride (PHCl),154 triethylphosphine (TEP),101 SnF2,14,101,102,155 Sn powder156 and PEABr157 have been used to improve the stability of Sn-based perovskites.

Although 2D perovskites exhibit higher stability than 3D perovskites, their PCE has not yet reached the expected level. Cao et al. prepared the Sn-based the RP-type quasi-2D perovskite (BA)2MAn−1SnnI3n+1via the spin-coating method, and the absorption curve shifted to a high energy band gap with an increase in n (Fig. 6a). For the first time, TEP was employed as an antioxidant to promote the crystal growth of the crystal grains and obtain a uniform film (Fig. 6b). The PCE of the device based on (BA)2MA3Sn4I13 was 2.53%.101

3.2 Light-emitting diodes

3D perovskite films have defects and stability problems during the preparation process. In contrast, the QW containing multiple n-phases improves the directional transport efficiency of carriers in 2D perovskites. Under light excitation, energy quickly transfers from the low-n region to the high-n region. The increase in carrier density greatly passivates defects and increases the radiation recombination efficiency, thereby obtaining a high photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY). The adjustable composition of 2D perovskites is very convenient for the application of perovskite LEDs (PeLEDs). 3D perovskites can realize a change in LED color and improvement in purity by changing the type and proportion of the X-position ions, and 2D perovskite can also similarly be implemented using this method. However, compared with 3D perovskites, a major advantage of 2D perovskites is that the control of their n value will also change their band gap.174 Since the EQE of the first PeLED reached about 0.1% in 2014,175 the EQE of PeLEDs has been improved significantly. LEDs based on 2D perovskite include near-infrared, red, green, and blue LEDs. PeLEDs have the advantages of high efficiency, high color purity, and continuous adjustment in the visible light region. Thus, they have important application potential in lighting, imaging and other fields.
3.2.1 Near infrared light-emitting Diodes. To enhance the carrier injection efficiency and radiation recombination rate, Yuan et al. prepared the 2D perovskite (PEA)2MAn−1PbnI3n+1 containing different n values in 2016. Energy transfers among different dimensionalities and converges in phases with small band gaps, which weaken the non-radiative recombination. The EQE of the device manufactured using (PEA)2MAn−1PbnI3n+1 (n = 5) was 8.8% (Fig. 8a).176 In addition, the performance of LEDs can be improved by changing the proportion of halogen elements, with the EQE reaching 11.7%.41

3.2.2 Red light-emitting Diodes. For 2D perovskites, adjusting the proportion of halogen elements can change their luminous color. Lanzetta et al. prepared (PEA)2SnIxBr4−x 2D PeLEDs. With an increase in the proportion of Br ions, the band gap increased, and the absorption and emission curves exhibited a blue shift. The PeLED based on (PEA)2SnI4 achieved a brightness of 0.15 cd m−2 at 4.7 mA cm−2.177

The Sn2+ of Sn-based PeLEDs is easily oxidized to Sn4+,178 which limits the performance of perovskite devices. Thus, to realize the coordinated adjustment of crystallization control, passivation defects and anti-oxidation simultaneously, Gao et al. introduced 2,3-dihydroxynaphthalene-6-sulfonic acid sodium salt (DSAS) in a perovskite, and the EQE of the 2D (PEA)2SnI4 PeLED increased from 0.124% to 0.72% (Fig. 8c).179

By controlling the ratio of L in the precursor solution, He et al. prepared a quasi-2D (PBA)2Csn−1PbnI3n+1 high-efficiency red PeLED (Fig. 8d). The choice of L has an important influence on the performance of the device. Compared with the EQE based on the BA PeLED, that of the PeLED based on PBA was higher at 13.3%.180

Accordingly, Wang et al. adjusted the proportion of chlorine in a quasi-2D CsPbBrxCl3−x perovskite film to achieve luminescence from green to blue.187

Insulating bulky organic cations can hinder the transfer of charges. When preparing quasi-2D perovskites, Wang et al. used a dual-ligand strategy to control the ratio of iBA and PEA. The introduction of iBA improved the uniformity of the perovskite film and enhanced the photoelectric performance. The maximum luminescence of the PeLED reached 1130 cd m−2 and the EQE reached 7.84% (Fig. 9e).44 To reduce the generation of the n = 1 phase and facilitate the effective energy transfer of excitons, Pang et al. incorporated sodium ions into a quasi-2D perovskite. 3.3 Detectors

The unstable characteristics of 3D perovskites are also reflected in the repeatability of the detector signal. After a certain period of use, the electrical signal intensity of the detector will gradually become weaker. In contrast, the flexible chain L improves the stability of perovskites in detectors, and it also makes 2D perovskites show the advantages and unique features compared to 3D perovskite. For example, van der Waals force is present between the 2D perovskite inorganic layers, which is conducive to the release of lattice pressure without causing large-scale crystal deformation. This is very beneficial for the construction of flexible detectors. Similar to most 2D materials, a reduction in dimensionality will also change the optical properties of 2D perovskites. For example, nonlinear optical phenomena such as SHG and THG signals will be observed. Compared with passing through the organic layer, carrier transport in the inorganic layer is more efficient. In this case, the anisotropy of 2D perovskites facilitates the detection of polarized light.

3.3.2 Ultraviolet (UV) detectors. Besides the visible light range, 2D perovskites can also be used for UV detection. Currently, mostly NS and 2D halide perovskites with n = 1 are used in UV detectors. UV detectors prepared using NSs have the advantages of low driving voltage, high responsivity and fast response while maintaining high stability.213

Compared with polycrystalline films, 2D perovskite single crystals with n = 1 have the advantages of no grain boundaries and longer carrier diffusion length. Zhang et al. used a (PEA)2PbBr4 single crystal to fabricate planar-type UV-photodetectors (Fig. 11a). It was found that the UV photodetector had a low dark current (∼10−13 A), high detectivity (∼1013 A), and extremely fast photocurrent response (trise = 0.41 ms and tfall = 0.37 ms) (Fig. 11b and c). These excellent characteristics lay the foundation for the application of 2D perovskites in ultrafast optical calculation and communication.214 In 2021, Wang et al. used the gas–liquid interface crystalline method to prepare a wafer-sized 2D perovskite (BA)2PbBr4 single-crystal thin film (SCTF) with an area of 38 cm2 (Fig. 11d). The surface of the single crystal was smooth, without obvious defects, exhibiting an excellent UV detection performance. The detector had a very low dark current (∼10−14 A) and detectivity of 1.08 × 1012 (Fig. 11e and f, respectively).215

2D perovskites have important applications in many fields, but mainly concentrated in solar cells, LEDs and detectors. It can be concluded from Tables 2–5 that to improve the performance of devices, the main consideration is the modification of 2D perovskites and the contact between the perovskite and electron/hole transport layers. Simultaneously, attention should also be paid to improving the electron/hole transport layer, the use of additives, and the preparation methods. Changing the doping ratio of A-site ions with different radii can adjust the growth orientation of the 2D perovskite grains and the tightness of their arrangement, thereby regulating the surface morphology. Moreover, the organic layer can be composed of two different types of L, and the B-site ions can also be doped with Pb/Sn mixture. The device performance can also be improved by changing the proportion and species of the X-site ions. The smaller the number of inorganic layers, n, the larger the band gap, and the smaller the light absorption range. Thus, choosing an appropriate value of n is vital for the promotion of the device. The change in band gap combined with the adjustment of the X-site ion allows the diverse modification of PeLEDs. For the transport layers, adding appropriate additives or adding a thin spacer layer between the transport layer and the 2D perovskite layer can improve the transport characteristics of the carriers. The structure of 2D perovskite solar cells and LEDs is relatively complex. In addition to the characteristics of 2D perovskites, the energy band arrangement and surface contact of other transmission layers must also be considered. The components of detectors are relatively simple, where their performance mainly depends on the 2D perovskite layer. Because thin-film detectors often have pinholes and defects during the preparation process, 2D single crystal perovskites have great application potential in the visible light, ultraviolet light and X-ray detection fields due to their low defect density and excellent carrier transmission.

3.4 Field-effect transistors

FETs can amplify or switch electronic signals by changing the electric field, which have a wide range of applications in the field of electronics. However, in the case of halide perovskites, two major problems limit their development as channel materials for FETs. The first problem is their sensitivity to polar solvents238 and the second is ion migration.239 These factors have adverse effects on perovskites such as interface defects, low carrier injection efficiency and excessive defect density. The above-mentioned shortcomings will reduce the hole mobility (μh), which is one of the indicators of the device performance of FETs. Accordingly, 2D perovskites have become another promising material for FET applications due to their advantages of good stability, long exciton diffusion distance and low defect density.

3.5 Lasers

The 3D perovskites used in lasers have high quality factors and low lasing thresholds, but are accompanied by random orientation and low stability in solution. In contrast, 2D perovskites have high photo/chemical stability, while maintaining the above-mentioned advantages. The flexibility provided by the organic chain L is significant for the preparation of nanowire (NW) laser arrays. The controllability of the growth direction and the diversity of components of 2D perovskites indicate a strategy to improve the energy utilization efficiency. Since 2014 when the first amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) was observed in an MAPbI3 film,245 optical pump lasers based on microcavity structures such as distributed feedback (DFB) gratings,246 Fabry-Pérot cavities247 and whispering gallery cavities248 have been created. Li et al. fabricated 2D perovskite MAPbI3 NSs by combining the solution process and vapor phase conversion methods to construct the ring cavity of a mode-locked fiber laser (Fig. 13a). Ultrafast picosecond pulses could be generated at 1064 nm (Fig. 13b). Compared with the bulk perovskite film, the 2D perovskite NSs have a larger modulation depth and a low saturation intensity.50

3.6 Resistive switching memories

OIHPs have advantages such as low operating voltage, high on/off ratio and flexibility.249–251 Thus, these characteristics make them a substitute for oxychalcogenides in RSM devices. However, the switching stability of 3D perovskites is poor. The instability of perovskites will greatly reduce the on/off ratio and repeatability of the device. Generally, 3D perovskites cannot meet the requirements of commercial RSM. However, by introducing L to reduce their dimensionality, it was found that the on/off ratio of RSM devices based on 2D perovskites increased by several magnitudes. This can be attributed to the increase in the band gap, which increases the Schottky barrier, thereby reducing the high resistance state (HRS) current, and finally increasing the on/off ratio. The high stability of 2D perovskites coupled with their high switching ratio give them a greater application advantage over other perovskites. In 2017, Seo et al. changed the crystal size by controlling the phase composition of the (BA)2MAn−1PbnI3n+1 perovskite. When the dimensionality was reduced from 3D MAPbI3 to 2D (BA)2PbI4, the on/off ratio increased from 102 to 107, showing higher switching stability (Fig. 14a). The change in dimensionality did not affect the appearance of bipolar resistive switching behavior, and the SET voltage decreased as n decreased (Fig. 14b).52

4. Conclusion and outlook

The efficiency of 3D perovskite devices has achieved significant process, but the stability of 3D perovskites as a light-absorbing material is still poor. Nowadays, the stability of 3D perovskite can be improved by adjusting their preparation process and changing their composition. By reducing the dimensionality of 3D perovskites to prepare 2D perovskites, their stability is improved, while changing their optical properties.

The adjustment of the composition of 2D perovskites is closely related to their performance. Changing their composition can adjust the n value, that is, the layer spacing, and thus their optical properties will change to a certain extent. The choice of L and the regulation of the types and proportions of the A, B and X ions, which play an important role in the structure of perovskites, have been further explored. These characteristics are closely related to the transmission efficiency of excitons.

In addition to common solar cells and LEDs, 2D perovskites have also experienced relatively significant developments in applications such as detectors, FETs, lasers, and RSMs. However, although many achievements have been made, there are urgent problems in the preparation process and production conditions of 2D perovskites as follows.

(1) Precise control of the distribution of 2D perovskite phases. Even if the perovskite precursor solution is configured in proportion, the perovskite light absorbing layer formed by annealing is not a perovskite phase with a specific n value, but an aggregate containing perovskite phases with different n values. Also, more effective characterization methods should be employed to further determine the distribution of different n-value phases in perovskites.

(2) Stability issues. Although 2D perovskites have high stability, they also need to be insulated from oxygen and water in a glove box. If the stability problem cannot be solved, this will affect the prospects for the industrialization of 2D perovskites.

(3) Contact of the device interface. The low hydrophilicity of the transmission layer will lead to poor contact between various layers of the device. Excitons are dissociated into electrons and holes in the perovskite layer and cannot be transported out well, which ultimately affects the efficiency of the device.

(4) Compatibility with the environment. At present, most of the applications of perovskites are based on lead-based perovskites. However, lead and other heavy metal elements have a bad influence on the human body and environment. The efficiency of Sn and Ge based is still not up to expectations due to problems such as oxidation.

(5) Cost issues. Related perovskite research is still in its infancy in the laboratory, and it is not yet possible to prepare simple and efficient 2D perovskite devices using cheap raw materials.

Many studies on perovskite-related aspects have been carried out. However, due to various limitations such as experimental conditions, 2D perovskites are still far from commercial application, but have a bright future as an emerging material.
