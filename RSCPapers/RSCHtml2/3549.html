Abstract

The shift towards renewable energy is one of the main challenges of this generation. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), based on donorâ€“acceptor architectures, can help in this transition as they present excellent photovoltaic efficiencies yet cheap and simple manufacturing. For molecular heterojunction DSSCs, donorâ€“acceptor pairs are linked in a covalent manner, which facilitates their tailoring and rational design. Nevertheless, reliable computational characterization of charge transfer rate constants (kCT) is needed to speed this development process up. In this context, the performance of time-dependent density functional theory for the calculation of kCT values in donorâ€“acceptor fullerene-based dyads has not been benchmarked yet. Herein, we present a detailed analysis on the performance of seven well-known density functional approximations (DFAs) for this type of system, focusing on several parameters such as the reorganization energies (Î»), electronic couplings (VDA), and Gibbs energies (Î”G0CT), as well as the final rate constants. The amount of exact exchange at short range (SR) and long range (LR) electronâ€“electron distances (and the transition from the SR to LR) turned out to be key for the success of the prediction. The tuning of these parameters improves significantly the performance of current DFAs.

Introduction

Renewable energy boosting is the 7th target of the United Nation member statesâ€™ 2030 Sustainable Agenda Development1 whose premise is to accelerate the transition to a competitive low-carbon economy. The production of non-contaminating renewable energy is undoubtedly one of the biggest challenges facing humanity nowadays. The Earth receives just a tiny fraction of the Sun's colossal power output. But even that tiny fraction represents 120000 trillion Watts and, consequently, we would need to harvest less than 0.02% to fulfill the energy consumed on the planet.2 Energy from the Sun can be collected with solar cells, which are devices that convert absorbed incident sunlight into electricity. Most current solar cells are silicon-based inorganic cells. These cells have a high cost, heavy weight, and their production requires high energy consumption and leads to environmental pollution.3 Thanks to their excellent photovoltaic efficiency and simple and low cost production process, dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSC) are a promising member of the so-called 3rd generation of photovoltaic devices. Fullerenes and their derivatives have emerged as promising materials for the design of efficient DSSCs.4â€“8 Two different types of DSSCs are usually distinguished: bulk heterojunctions9 (BHJs) where the donor and acceptor (D/A) moieties are not covalently connected, and molecular heterojunctions10 (mHJs) with covalently linked donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) dyads. The main advantage of mHJs is that charge transfer (CT) processes can be rationally designed since a better structural control and charge mobility tuning can be achieved within this approach.

Tuning the electronic and structural parameters of solar cells for optimum performance is a difficult task because several trade-offs should be considered. The experimental approach to optimize DSSCs is heuristic, mainly based on trial and error. In this sense, computational modelling of molecular interactions and charge transport in macromolecular photovoltaic materials is the most rational approach for the design of high-performance DSSCs. Computational modelling of the power conversion efficiency of DSSCs requires the knowledge of the charge transfer rate constants (kCT).11 In some cases, kCT values are known experimentally, but in most cases, the most efficient way for designing or tuning DSSCs is to estimate the kCT from calculations, and select the most promising Dâ€“A dyads to be used as building blocks for mHJ DSSCs, which should also contain anchoring groups to interact with TiO2 electron acceptors.12â€“15

There are a number of studies that have benchmarked different density functional approximations (DFAs) for the calculation of excited states based on time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT).16â€“23 It is widely accepted that standard functionals underestimate the excitation energies of CT states and only range-separated DFAs provide good estimates of these states.24â€“29 To our knowledge, despite its relevance, no benchmarks of DFAs for the calculation of kCT in Dâ€“A (fullerene) dyads are available yet. The present work aims to fill this void. In particular, we compare six well-known range-separated DFAs (CAM-B3LYP, Ï‰B97xD, LC-Ï‰hPBE, LC-TPSS, LC-BLYP, and M11) and one meta-hybrid-generalized gradient approximation (MHGGA) functional, MN15. For these seven DFAs, we calculate kCT of four Dâ€“A dyads for which experimental charge transfer rate constants are available.

Computational methods

The ground state geometry for each structure was optimized at the B3LYP-D3(BJ)/6-311G(d,p) level of theory,30â€“32 including dispersion interactions through the D3 empirical model of Grimme, with the Beckeâ€“Johnson damping.33,34 Previous studies have demonstrated that CT in DSSCs are mostly purely electronic processes and, for this reason, it is usually not necessary to account for the structural changes of the Dâ€“A dyads induced by the charge-transfer processes,35â€“37 except for the calculation of the reorganization energy. Nevertheless, in order to confirm whether this assumption holds for the systems studied herein, we have computed the root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) of the atomic positions of D* with respect to D+ and A* with respect to Aâˆ’, i.e. the two molecular fragments involved in the CT reaction. In addition, Dâ€“A dyads considered in this work are relatively rigid, and, therefore, we do not expect large conformational effects.

The donor and acceptor geometries needed to estimate the internal reorganization energy (vide infra) and the geometrical changes upon CT were obtained at the same level of theory as the ground state equilibrium geometries.

Î”G0CT calculations

We have seen already that Î» does not have a critical impact on the evaluation of the kCT for the four systems studied since the variation among them is minor. Î”G0CT is present in the exponential part of the Marcus equation as well, thus it should be analysed carefully since the errors present in Î”G0CT have an exponential translation into the rate constant. CT reactions should present negative or slightly positive Î”G0CT values to take place. Computationally, small positive values can also be accepted at the DFT level, since other electronic states are usually very close in energy to the ones of interest and therefore, for an accurate (and far more expensive) calculation of these energies one should use multireference methods. Fortunately, for C60-Az, C59N-Pc, and C60-TPA experimental Î”G0CT values are available and the DFT accuracy is benchmarked against them in Table 3 (and Fig. S9 and S10 in the ESIâ€ ). For a light-harvesting material, the Gibbs energy for the charge separation (Î”G0CS) is usually found in the normal Marcus region, therefore Î”G0CT values are expected to be negative in order for the charge transfer to occur at a given rate (and that is what happens herein). This is a great difference with respect to the charge recombination processes, which present their Î”G0 (Î”G0CR) in the Marcus inverted region. In this manner, the lifetime of the CTS is usually longer. If this is the case, their theoretical evaluation becomes even more challenging. For Î”G0CT values, not as that in previous variables, it can be seen that the choice of DFA is crucial for its correct evaluation. LC-Ï‰hPBE, LC-TPSS, and LC-BLYP always give positive Î”G0CT values when they should be negative. Similar to theoretical predictions of the UV-visible absorption spectra of fullerenic and non-fullerenic DA molecules,65 the amount of exact exchange added to the DFAs through the range-separated scheme appears to be a key parameter to control the performance of DFA for charge-transfer reactions. One could think that having less HFX at the short range region will be somehow compensated by a larger value of the attenuation parameter (to switch to the long-range region at shorter distances, i.e. LC-Ï‰hPBE, LC-TPSS or LC-BLYP). However, this compensation is not observed and some amount of HFX must be added at the short-range to get accurate rates, as in cases with CAM-B3LYP (19%), MN15 (44%), and Ï‰B97xD (22%). On the other hand, the optimal amount of HFX at long range needs to be <100%, as the best DFAs have 0.65 (CAM-B3LYP) and 0.44 (MN15) of total HFX at long-range. The same requirement has been reported to be beneficial, in terms of the accuracy of the results, for other long-range properties such as electronic excitation energies.66,67 The usage of (Î± + Î²) < 1 can be rationalized as a requirement to reproduce the effective dielectric constant of the material (Î± + Î² = Îµeff) that tailors the decay of the XC potential as (Îµeffr)âˆ’1.66,67 For M11 functional Î± + Î² is equal to 1.0. However, it has two other desirable features: (1) the attenuation parameter is relatively low (clearly lower than in LC-BLYP, LC-TPSS or even LC-Ï‰hPBE), and (2) the HFx term at short electronâ€“electron distances is not zero.

Tuning: a look into the future?

Charge transfer rate constants demand properly taking into account the long-range interactions to be accurately computed, a well-balanced self-interaction error and a proper decay of the exchangeâ€“correlation potential. In this and some other fields, range-separated functionals can be tuned to meet such requirements.68 Herein we have tested, as a proof of concept, two tuning schemes for LC-BLYP, which is the worst method overall, trying to improve their results for C60-TPA. Our goal was to perform a preliminary study to explore if the tuned-DFAs have the potential to be good candidates to be used for the evaluation of log(kCT).

First, we forced LC-BLYP to fulfill Janak's theorem (OT1) by screening the Ï‰ parameter,69 as proposed by Kronik and coworkers.70,71 The working Ï‰ for OT1 was 0.236. Similarly, we also tested a novel tuning strategy recently developed by some of us (OT2).72 In OT2 the empirically tuned attenuation parameter is given by a function of a molecular descriptor defined from the linear polarizability and the number of electrons in the molecule. The resulting Ï‰ was determined to be 0.303.

Then, charge-transfer rates for OT1 and OT2 were computed (Table 5). In that line, the ideal candidate to be tuned in further work using the OT2 procedure is CAM-B3LYP. There are other alternatives to tuned DFAs, such as the new revM11 functional, which is tailored to tackle ground- and excited-state properties.74

Conclusions

We have analysed several factors that can determine the accuracy of the computed charge transfer rate for four fullerene-based systems, namely, C60-Az, C59N-Pc, C60-ZnTPP, and C60-TPA, at the DFT level of theory with six well-known range-separated GGA DFAs (CAM-B3LYP, Ï‰B97xD, MN11, LC-Ï‰hPBE, LC-TPSS, and LC-BLYP) and one meta-hybrid-GGA functional, MN15. We have found that the estimation of the reorganization energy and the electronic coupling given by all benchmarked DFAs have a small impact on the final accuracy of charge transfer rate. In contrast, Î”G0CT has been proven to be a critical parameter for the CT rate computation. Only the MN15, Ï‰B97xD, and especially CAM-B3LYP functionals are able to describe Î”G0CT accurately enough, correctly reproducing their negative sign. These three DFAs give also best results for the kCT estimated using the Marcus theory. Overall, we recommend CAM-B3LYP as the best choice for modelling fullerene-based materials. Tuning the amount of full range and long range HFX seems to be a promising strategy that needs to be explored in order to develop more accurate functionals for predicting the photophysical properties.
