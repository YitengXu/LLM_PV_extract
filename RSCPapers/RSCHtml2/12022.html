Abstract

The skilful synthesis of nanohybrids composed of noble metals (Au, Ag, Pt and Pd, as well as AuAg alloy) and metal oxides (ZnO, TiO2, Cu2O, MnO2, Fe2O3, WO3 and CeO2) has received considerable attention for applications in photocatalysis, solar cells, drug delivery, surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy and many other important areas. The overall architecture of nanocomposites is one of the most important factors dictating the physical properties of nanohybrids. Noble metals can be coupled to metal oxides and metal chalcogenides to yield diverse nanostructures, including noble metal decorated-metal oxide nanoparticles (NPs), nanoarrays, noble metal/metal oxide core/shell, noble metal/metal oxide yolk/shell and Janus noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanostructures. In this review, we focus on the significant application based advances in neat and tailored nanostructures of noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanohybrids and touched upon chalcogenides also. The improvement in performance in representative energy conversion, electrochemical water splitting, photocatalytic hydrogen generation, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, photocatalytic degradation of organic pollutants and dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSCs) applications is discussed. Finally, we conclude with a perspective on the future direction and prospects of these controllable nanohybrid materials.

Hybrid nanomaterials possessing combined optical, electronic and magnetic properties have attracted much interest in recent years due to their wide-range of applications in environmental remediation and solar energy conversion.23â€“26 In general, the successful application of such nanohybrids is determined by their structure, composition, particle size and other parameters.27,28 One of the major challenges in this area is the integration of two dissimilar materials with different structures to yield unique hybrid systems with diverse functionality for real time application (Fig. 2b).29â€“32

With the development of synthetic techniques in nanoscience, small noble metal NPs, especially atomically precise metal oxide hybrid nanostructures, have been successfully fabricated very recently, and these materials exhibit remarkably enhanced catalytic activity and selectivity.56,57 However, this simple loading form cannot meet the growing demand for stability, because noble metal catalysts contain numerous exposed surfaces and are always used in harsh environments, such as those with high temperatures and concentrated acidic and alkaline solutions. The lack of surface protection causes noble metal NPs to easily aggregate to minimize the surface energy, which can cause a serious loss of the catalytic active sites, a reduction of the catalytic activity and even complete inactivation. 2. Synthesis of noble metal and metal oxide based nanocomposites (NMâ€“MOs)

Among the variety of nanocatalysts developed, controllable integration of different materials such as noble metals (e.g., Au, Ag, Pt, and Pd) and metal oxides (e.g., TiO2, CeO2, and ZrO2) into single nanostructures has recently become one of the hottest research topics, because they not only combine the function of individual nanoparticles (NPs), but also bring unique collective and synergetic catalytic properties compared with single-component materials. Traditionally, these composites are easily prepared by various methods such as impregnation,58,59 coprecipitation,60 depositionâ€“precipitation,61 and so on. To design high performance catalysts, many strategies have been adopted and can be generally classified into four categories: (1) confinement of noble metal NPs into mesoporous supports;62,63 (2) modification of the supports;64 (3) replacement of mono-component noble metal NPs with alloy NPs;65,66 and (4) encapsulation of noble metal NPs into supports with a coreâ€“shell structure.67â€“71 The various synthetic protocols for the fabrication of NMâ€“MOs are briefly discussed in this section.

2.1. Chemical methods

This is the simplest methodology, comprising the adsorption of metal precursors on the surface of a metal oxide semiconductor followed by chemical reduction in a solution medium. It is worth mentioning that much care has to be taken for chemical reduction of metal precursors. The chosen reductant should be employed judiciously so that desorption of the reduced metal does not take place from the adsorbent surface.72 In the case of Au NPs, there are different reliable methods to prepare colloidal suspensions in suitable solvents. Among them, the citrate reduction method (Turkevich method),73 Brust method74 and reduction by NaBH4 are the most convenient and well-recognized protocols to obtain Au NPs on the surface of semiconductors or these pre-synthesized Au NPs can be adsorbed on the surface of the support by simply mixing an Au colloidal solution with the semiconductor dispersion.75â€“77 One of the commonly observed disadvantages of this method is the aggregation of NPs during the adsorption stage, thus reducing the efficiency of the desired application. Anchoring of Au NPs onto the chemically modified surface of semiconductors using linker molecules, thereby reducing the aggregation or coalescence of Au NPs, was reported by a few groups.78â€“80 The polyol process is considered as the most convincing method to prepare Ag NPs, where ethylene glycol (EG) is used as both the solvent and the reducing agent and polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) is used as the stabilizer. In a typical procedure, EG is added into a round-bottom flask and heated to 160 Â°C in an oil bath. Then, PVP solution in EG is injected into the flask under stirring followed by the addition of AgNO3 in EG drop by drop under stirring. The colloidal solution thus-obtained was centrifuged and used for the process.81â€“84 The sonochemical method is another method to obtain Ag NPs deposited on semiconductor surfaces. A ZnO oxide precursor or combined ZnO and metal precursors are dispersed in an appropriate solvent. After sonicating the dispersion for an appropriate amount of time, NaOH is added. The product is recovered by centrifugation.85,86 Anandan et al.87 prepared Auâ€“TiO2 photocatalysts using a sonochemical pathway. Murdoch et al.88 fabricated Au NPs by a depositionâ€“precipitation method over TiO2 anatase and rutile particles prepared by the solâ€“gel process. Li et al.89 prepared ZnO/Ag sea urchin-like hybrids using a photochemical method. Use of ion exchange resins90 and polymer supports91 is also a neat platform to syntheses.

2.2. Thermal methods

Hydrothermal and solvothermal processes are the two common wet chemical methods that fall into this category. In the hydrothermal process metal oxide or metal oxide precursors are mixed with a metal precursor and a base in water. The thermal treatment of the reaction mixture in an autoclave above the boiling temperature of water has to be performed. During this process metal precursors are reduced and adsorbed simultaneously on the surface of semiconductors.92â€“94 The same protocol is applied for the solvothermal process except that the aqueous medium is replaced with appropriate solvents. Although these methods are simple and cost effective methods, they often suffer from the problem of aggregation of metal NPs. Thermal evaporation is one of the physical methods to deposit metal NPs on semiconductor surfaces, where a highly pure metal is evaporated at very high temperatures and the metal vapors are allowed to deposit on the surface of a semiconductor.95

Different oxoâ€“hydroxo species96 with improved thermal stability,97 at times pure oxides with hierarchical structures21 and noble metal incorporated composite materials, were successfully obtained from a simple modified hydrothermal (MHT) reaction (Fig. 2c) inside a close wooden box fitted with an electric bulb.

2.3. Depositionâ€“precipitation method

It is reported that this method renders very small metal NPs that are catalytically very active. For instance, pre-grown MOs are dispersed into a Au precursor solution, such as HAuCl4. The precipitates obtained after a period of stirring are then calcined to decompose the Au precursor to the metallic state. However, the pH range used in the depositionâ€“precipitation method is a key factor determining the structures and properties of the resulting NMâ€“MOs.98,99 Damato et al.100 prepared Au NPs decorated on a TiO2 nanocomposite by means of reducing an Au source using ascorbic acid (Fig. 3a). Moreau et al.101 found that Au/TiO2 catalysts prepared by DP at pH = 9 produced the optimum catalytic activity. Since the TiO2 support possesses negative surface charges at this pH, it is unfavorable for the deposition of chlorine ligands which are detrimental to the catalytic properties.102 It has also been demonstrated by the work of Tran et al. that the final pH of the preparation solution strongly influences the Au particle size, Au deposition yield, chlorine removal efficiency and consequently the overall catalyst activity.103 In addition to pH, the choice of precipitation agent is also important. The most common choice is NaOH in which the size of Au NPs can be controlled by varying the solution pH and calcination temperature. However, one problem with the use of NaOH is that not all the Au in the growth solution deposits on the MO support. Some precipitates in free solution. By using urea as the precipitation agent, all Au from the solution was deposited on the MOs without precipitation in the free solution to achieve a higher Au loading.104,105

2.4. Photodeposition method

In this methodology, electrons and holes are produced first upon irradiation of TiO2 or ZnO with light. These electrons are then used for the reduction of a metal precursor to metal, which is simultaneously deposited on the surface of a semiconductor. The presence of a hole scavenger such as alcohol is important to avoid the accumulation of positive charge during the photodeposition. Usually the metal oxide nanostructures are dispersed in the metal precursor for the required time to allow the complete adsorption of metal ions on the surface of the support. The product is then exposed to light with appropriate intensity, resulting in the deposition of metal NPs on the semiconductor. The important parameter in this method is the intensity of the light since the average particle size decreases as the intensity of light increases.107,108 As represented in Fig. 3b, Walker et al. designed a synthetic pathway for Au NP deposited TiO2, where the photoreduction technique has been employed to prepare Au NPs over the TiO2 surface.109

2.5. Sputtering method

Here, the metal atoms are ejected from the solid target material due to the bombardment of the target by energetic particles. Sputtered atoms ejected to the gas phase are not in a thermodynamic equilibrium state, so they are easily deposited on the substrate. This whole process is usually performed using argon plasma in a vacuum chamber. A thin film with a uniform thickness of metal layer can be deposited. This method is particularly useful when a metalâ€“semiconductor hybrid system has to be prepared on substrates (Fig. 3c).110â€“114

2.6. Pulsed electrodeposition

Since most metal oxide nanoarrays are formed on conductive substrates such as silicon wafers, fluorine-tin-oxide (FTO) glass or metal substrates, pulsed electrodeposition (PED) can also be used to construct highly dispersed NMs on nanoarrays.115â€“118 The density and size of NMs can be tuned by changing the electrochemical parameters such as peak potential, deposition time and pulse cycles. The average size of Ag NPs loaded on the surface of TiO2 nanotube arrays can be precisely controlled from 1.3 nm to 21.0 nm by carefully controlling PED parameters.115 This method can effectively suppress the agglomeration of NMs due to the high nucleation rate under the pulse current at the nanotube entrances to prevent the pores from becoming clogged.

2.7. Physical vapor deposition (PVD)

This method can also be used to deposit NMs on the surface of metal oxide nanoarrays (Fig. 3d). Libuda et al. have grown Pt NPs at the tube openings of TiO2 nanotubular arrays prepared by electrochemical methods using PVD in an ultrahigh vacuum.119 The relationship between the nanostructure of TiO2/Pt and their adsorption kinetics was demonstrated using in situ infrared reflection absorption spectroscopy and molecular beam methods. The results indicated that the distribution of NPs in the nanotubes led to significant differences in adsorption and saturation behavior due to the differences in surface and gas phase transport resulting from the different nanostructures. Thus, it may be possible to tailor the transport processes in catalysts by controlling their structures.

3.2. Yolkâ€“shell nanostructures

NM/MO yolk/shell nanostructures possess a hollow metal oxide shell with a solid NM core that can freely move within the shell. It is easy to encapsulate metal cores within SiO2 shells to form yolk/shell nanohybrids by selectively etching part of the noble metal core of preformed core/shell NPs.130â€“132 SiO2 hollow shell-based yolkâ€“shell nanostructure becomes popular especially after the pioneering work by the Xia group.133 However, since the SiO2 hollow shell is insulating, it can only be used as a stabilizer. The use of functional MOs, such as ZnO, Fe3O4 and NiO as the shell, adds other properties and functionalities to the overall architecture.134,135 Compared to SiO2, it is more challenging to produce yolk/shell nanostructures with other MO shells because of the corrosion of most MOs during the etching process. As a result, SiO2 is widely used to synthesize NM/MO yolk/shell nanostructures by serving as a sacrificial hard template. Au/ZrO2 yolk/shell catalysts with high-temperature stability have also been synthesized using SiO2 as a sacrificial template.136 Au/SnO2 yolk/shell nanospheres with improved sensing properties have been successfully synthesized by also using Au/SiO2 nanospheres as sacrificial templates.137 Similar to SiO2, carbon can also be used as a hard template to fabricate noble metal/TiO2 yolk/shell nanostructures.138

It is more advantageous to use carbon templates compared to SiO2 since the removal of carbon and crystallization of MOs can be accomplished simultaneously by thermal treatment. Some MOs, such as ZnO and Cu2O, are sensitive to both acidic and alkaline conditions. This makes it impossible to use SiO2 as a template to fabricate yolk/shell nanostructures. Thus carbon is an attractive alternative template for the synthesis of this kind of nanostructure. Li et al. successfully prepared Au/ZnO yolk/shell structures with an average diameter of about 280 nm and an average ZnO shell thickness of 40 nm by coating ZnO on the surface of Au/carbon core/shell nanostructures followed by removal of the sacrificial carbon shell though calcination at high temperatures in air.139

In the area of chemical sensors for example, Au/NiO yolk/shell composite NPs showing a high response to H2S have been fabricated via a precipitation method using carbon encapsulated Au core/shell NPs as a sacrificial template.140 The maximum response of Au/NiO yolk/shell NPs was approximately 4 times higher than that of bare NiO hollow nanospheres due to the hollow spaces that allowed the accessibility of Au NPs to gas molecules. Wang et al.141 prepared yolk@shell nanostructures of Au@r-GO/TiO2 by using a solâ€“gel coating technique on Au/SiO2 core/shell spheres, followed by calcinations and template removal (Fig. 6a). In this case SiO2 acts not only as a template to produce void space but also promotes coating of r-GO and TiO2 layers. In addition to hard templates, soft templates such as poly-(2-vinyl pyridine)-block-poly(ethylene oxide) (PVP-b-PEO) have also been used to fabricate Au/TiO2 yolk/shell nanostructures (Fig. 6b).142 This method is similar to using block copolymers to fabricate core/shell nanostructures. The difference is that the reduction of the Au core precursor is facilitated by a deep UV treatment, and the outer PEO blocks serve as the anchoring sites for the growth of the TiO2 shell thus leading to the formation of Au/TiO2 core/shell nanostructures. The internal cross-linked PVP-b-PEO copolymers are then removed by another deep UV treatment to form Au/TiO2 yolk/shell nanostructures. This method is easy to control and is an alternative way to prepare other kinds of yolk/shell materials sensitive to pH.

4. Catalytic applications of noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanocomposites

The unique properties of nanomaterials such as surface- and strain-driven lattice distortion, variation in electronic state density and oxidation-induced charge redistribution could also benefit the catalytic performance.176,177 Nowadays, NMâ€“MOs gain tremendous attention because of their mutual and synergetic catalytic properties compared to their single counterparts. With the rapid advances in synthesis chemistry, nanomaterials with well-defined sizes, shapes, crystal facets, structures and compositions have been made available,178,179 which have provided many opportunities for developing catalysts with excellent activity for energetically challenging reactions, high selectivity to valuable products, and long-term stability under tough reaction environments.

4.1. Mechanisms of high performance applications

It is useful to understand the physical mechanisms for the enhancement of various properties when NMs and MOs are combined. The two main phenomena involved in the improved photoreaction are localized surface plasmonic resonance (LSPR) and Schottky barrier formation. Both of these are discussed in the following section.
(a) Localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR). LSPR can be described as the collective oscillation of free electrons induced by incident light inside NMs. When the frequency of incident light matches the natural frequency of free electrons oscillating against the restoring force of positive nuclei, the oscillation amplitude reaches a maximum. The resonant photon wavelength is called the LSPR wavelength, which is different for different NMs, and is also tuned by varying the size and shape of NMs.180 As to NMâ€“MOs, a direct benefit is that the light-absorption spectral ranges of TiO2 and ZnO can be easily extended to visible and even near-infrared regions by the LSPR effect of NMs. Liu et al. reported wide absorption spectra of TiO2 by integrating Au nanorods as antennas.181 The plasmon resonance absorptions of nanostructures can be tuned from 630 nm to 810 nm by manipulating the aspect ratio of the Au nanorods.
(b) Schottky barrier. The formation of the Schottky barrier at the NMâ€“MO interface is highly dependent on the work function of the NM (Ï•M) and the electron affinity of the MO (XSM). Normally, the value of Ï•M is larger than that of XSM, where XSM is the energy difference between the minimum conduction band (CB) and the vacuum (Vac) energy. Also the Fermi level (EF) of NMs is typically lower than that of MOs as shown in Fig. 9.182 When the NMs and MOs come into contact, the electrons in the MOs are transferred to the NMs until they reach equilibrium. This results in the bending of the CB (i.e. Schottky barrier Ï•B). 4.2. Photo-degradation of organic pollutants

Photocatalysis using semiconductor metal oxide nanoparticles has attracted increasing interest in solving global pollution problems. One of the major applications of this technology is the degradation of organic pollutants in water.187 The process of photo-degradation of organic pollutants can be described as follows: under illumination of incident light with photon energy higher than the band gap of MOs, the electrons in the valence band (VB) are injected into the conduction band (CB) leaving behind the same number of holes in the VB. The electrons trapped by O2 dissolved in solution form Ë™O2âˆ’ superoxide radicals which subsequently transform into Ë™OH radicals. Meanwhile, photo-generated holes react with H2O adsorbed on the surface of MOs to produce Ë™OH. Both Ë™O2âˆ’ and Ë™OH are highly reactive and can completely degrade most organic waste materials into low toxicity inorganic small molecules. However, the use of bare TiO2 or ZnO is limited due to their wide bandgap and the high recombination rate of photo-generated charges. Coupling with NMs is an emerging strategy to improve the degradation rate of organic pollutants under visible light conditions. NMâ€“MO nanocomposites provide an effective way to tailor the properties of semiconductor metal oxide photocatalysts through promoting interfacial charge-transfer processes and enhancing charge separation. TiO2 is known for its high-quality photocatalytic activity towards water pollutant removal. This photocatalytic process involves excitation of a semiconductor with photons of equal or higher energy than the band gap energy producing electronâ€“hole pairs. Moreover, in order to remove pollutant molecules, the presence of a metal is needed to trap the photo-excited electrons from the conduction band and transfer them to the pollutants. 4.3. Photocatalytic H2 generation

Photovoltaic cells are the most direct way to capture solar energy, but the electricity converted from solar energy must be used immediately or stored in some form.201 Hydrogen is one kind of alternative energy resources that is both renewable and environment friendly. Since the pioneering work of Honda and Fujishima on photocatalytic H2 generation on TiO2 electrodes in 1972,20 it has been recognized as a promising strategy for storing solar energy in chemical bonds without the production of harmful by-products.202 The mechanism for photocatalytic H2 generation is similar to that for photocatalytic degradation including the absorption of solar light, generation of electrons and holes and catalytic reactions for H2 or O2 evolution. Reichert et al. studied photoelectrochemical water oxidation on planar nanocrystalline Au/TiO2 catalyst film electrodes.266 The catalysts were prepared by a photodeposition method and deposited on conductive glass substrates. The Au nanoparticles on TiO2 affect the photoelectrochemical process in different ways, by (i) catalyzing there combination of photogenerated holes and electrons as well as parasitic side reactions (O2 reduction) in the photoelectrochemical water oxidation reaction, by (ii) affecting the selectivity in that reaction, and by (iii) improving the separation of photogenerated electrons and holes by catalyzing the reaction with sacrificial agents such as methanol, thus enhancing the photocatalytic H2 evolution reaction.

4.8. Solar cells

In addition to photo-catalysis, another emerging application for MOs is in photovoltaic solar cells, which can directly convert sunlight into electricity. Dye Sensitized Solar Cells (DSSCs) are one kind of third generation solar cell possessing high efficiency and low cost. The efficiency of DSSCs is mainly decided by the collection of incident light and charge separation efficiency in the cell. The anode is comprised of a metal oxide (such as TiO2, ZnO etc.), which is sensitized by a dye. The electrons are injected from the excited dye into the metal oxide during the absorption of light. The holes in the dye are then neutralized by a redox-based mediator such as Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’.270â€“272 Although there are intense efforts to maximize the performance, the highest efficiency reported so far is only 11% and there is no marked improvement in the efficiency which was achieved in the 1990s. Even though there is enough scope for improving the photocurrent and fill factor as well, the parameter which consumes the major part of the research activity is the photovoltage.10 The enhancement in photovoltage can be achieved by decreasing the cell dark current, which can be obtained by the incorporation of dyes with a larger absorption cross section or by coupling dyes with plasmonic metal NPs, since they are considered as two complimentary approaches to achieve high photovoltage.273â€“275 Therefore, plasmon enhanced dye absorption became one of the recent topics that researchers have been working on to enhance the light absorption and photocurrent generation in DSSCs. The introduction of NMs into DSSCs to boost performance has attracted increasing attention. Noble metal NPs enhance the efficiency of DSSCs by the following mechanisms: (1) light scattering by incorporated NPs: effective scattering of light is a very important factor in increasing the power conversion efficiency of DSSCs, since it can increase the path length of photons by multiple scattering and can concentrate the incoming light by effective trapping. (2) Near field enhancement by plasmonic antenna: the near field enhancement by the excitation of LSPR of plasmonic NPs can increase the charge carrier generation, resulting in enhancement of the photocurrent. (3) Surface plasmon polariton (SPP) mode at the interface of metal/semiconductor. The propagated electromagnetic waves along the metal surface can intensify the field of light and thus light penetration depth can be increased.272 Generally, two different approaches have been adopted to combine NMs with DSSCs. (1) Noble metal NPs with different sizes are first prepared and then mixed with commercial or homemade TiO2 NPs with subsequent thin film processing on a conductive substrate.273,276,277 (2) Prior to fabrication into films on FTO glass, various NMâ€“MO composites (e.g., Au NP decorated anatase TiO2 NPs, Au NP decorated-ZnO nanotube arrays and Ag/TiO2 core/shell NPs) are fabricated as photo-anode materials.278â€“281 Chen et al.282 prepared ZnO nanorods coated with Au NPs and found that the efficiency of the DSSC increased from 0.7% to 1.2% after the incorporation of Au NPs. The presence of Au NPs enhanced the optical absorption in the visible range due to the SPR effect. The excited electrons in Au can be transferred to the CB of ZnO and drop to the bottom of the ITO substrate. The reverse flow of electrons from ZnO to Au was restricted by the Schottky barrier between the ZnO and Au interface. The positive charge in Au was compensated by the redox species in the electrolyte. Reineck et al.283 proposed a plasmon-induced charge separation process in a solid-state plasmonic solar cell, which has a plasmonic photoanode consisting of Au or Ag NPs self-assembled on a TiO2 surface via electrostatic interactions. The hot electrons generated by excitation of the SPR can be transferred to the TiO2 CB, while the hole can be injected into the hole transport material. Consequently, a plasmonic photocurrent is generated and the spectral photocurrent behavior corresponds closely to the characteristic LSPR band of metal NPs. Recent investigation showed promise towards utilization of metal doped semiconductor particles for possible fabrication of solar cell devices which would exclude dye molecules.284,285 Recently, Zhao et al. constructed a highly efficient quantum dot sensitized solar cell (QDSC) based on Ag NP decorated TiO2/ZnO nanorod arrays (NAs) (Fig. 15). The QDSCs employing TiO2/ZnO NAs modified with Ag NPs have achieved much higher power conversion efficiency (PCE) than the QDSCs without Ag NPs. The incorporation of Ag NPs into TiO2/ZnO NA photoelectrodes can offer several advantages: (1) enhancing the light absorption, (2) increasing the electron injection rate, (3) suppressing the charge recombination, and (4) prolonging the electron lifetime.286

(a) Oxidation of CO. Among the oxidation processes, oxidation of carbon monoxide to carbon dioxide has been extensively studied because of the high toxicity of carbon monoxide and its importance in fuel cell technology, where the preferential oxidation of carbon monoxide in excess of hydrogen is a key process for the production of clean fuel.287 Imamura et al. synthesized a Pd/Mn2O3 catalyst with high CO oxidation activity.288 The role of Mn2O3 would be to permit the incorporation of O2. The enhanced catalytic performance for CO oxidation could be attributed to the synergistic effect of both metal and metal oxide (Fig. 16a).289

Wang et al.294 synthesized PAMAM dendrimer-stabilized Pt NPs immobilized on ordered mesoporous CeO2. This Pt-based composite material combined the merits of the hyperbranched structure of dendrimers and the unique physicochemical properties of ordered mesoporous CeO2. (d) Surface plasmon (SP) induced catalysis. In catalytic reactions surface plasmons play two types of roles: (1) direct induction of chemical reactions on the surface of metal nanostructures,297 or (2) effective assistance of chemical reactions performed on the surface of semiconductor nanocatalysts.88 Then in situ Surface Enhanced Raman Scattering (SERS) spectroscopy can be employed to carefully monitor SP induced catalytic reactions, which mainly focuses on photodimerization of amino or nitro groups into azo groups.298 The oxidation of p-aminothiophenol (PATP) to p,p-dimercaptoazobenzene (DMAB) has been widely used as a model reaction to probe the SPR mediated catalytic activities of Au and Ag NPs.299 In this process, O2 molecules in air are activated by adsorbing the hot electrons from the SPR-excited metal NPs, as a result the generated 3O2 species subsequently drive the PATP oxidation.300 Wang et al.301 have shown selective SPR-mediated conversion of PATP to DMAB on the surface of Auâ€“TiO2 NPs. 5. Conclusion

Rapid population growth and industrialization have raised concerns regarding energy availability and more so environmental pollution.306 Addressing these problems has been the focus of research the world over. Solar energy, a clean and effectively infinite energy source, can be efficiently harnessed by photocatalytic and photovoltaic processes to aid environmental remediation and green energy generation. In recent years, extensive research involving noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanocomposites has demonstrated their key role in the conversion of solar energy into electrical and chemical energy because of their prompted and enhanced light harvesting ability and high degree of charge carrier separation. The structure of these composites is critical in determining their photocatalytic and photovoltaic performance. In this review, we mainly centred our focus on the different synthetic strategies and implementation of improved properties of noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanocomposites with several different nanostructures including noble metal-decorated metal oxide nanoarrays, noble metal/metal oxide core/shell and yolk/shell nanostructures, and Janus noble metalâ€“metal oxide nanostructures in various catalytic applications. The applications of these materials related to solar energy conversion in photocatalytic degradation of environmental contaminants, water splitting into H2, photocatalytic reduction of CO2, dye-sensitized solar cells, conversion of chemical energy to electric energy by fuel cells, electrochemical or photoelectrochemical H2 generation and different catalytic reactions are presented. Finally, the challenges and future directions of these materials are highlighted. We hope that this review will serve as a useful guide in the design of noble metalâ€“metal oxide hybrid nanostructures for various catalytic applications for both experts and novices alike.
