Abstract

Semiconductors based on tin(II) show promising hole-transport characteristics due to the 5s electrons that form the valence band. In this paper, we report the synthesis and comprehensive characterization of tin(II) thiocyanate [Sn(NCS)2] and identify it as a novel transparent coordination polymer semiconductor. The single crystal X-ray analysis reveals covalently-bonded 1D polymeric chains that form a 2D structure through Snâ€“S tetrel bonds. Density functional theory calculations also confirm the importance of the van der Waals interactions between the 2D sheets. Furthermore, we show that the s character of Sn(II) is maintained at the top of the valence band, resulting in dispersed states with a small hole effective mass. The coordination with NCS ligands also leads to a conduction band which is high in energy, giving rise to a wide band gap and excellent transparency in the visible spectrum. This is the first report on the electronic properties of Sn(NCS)2 which highlights the potential of developing new transparent semiconductors based on thiocyanate coordination polymers.

1. Introduction

Transparent electron-transporting (or n-type) oxides, such as ZnO, In2O3, and Inâ€“Gaâ€“Znâ€“O (IGZO), consistently display excellent electrical performance due to their highly dispersive conduction bands (CBs), consisting primarily of the metal ns orbitals which are strongly coupled and delocalized even in the amorphous state.1,2 On the other hand, hole transport in metal oxides is generally hampered by strong localization of the O 2p states at the top of the valence bands (VBs), resulting in highly stable states which are low in energy and dispersionless, i.e., having deep energy levels and small hole effective masses.3 This leads to difficulties in generating carriers (holes) either by current injection or chemical doping as well as limited hole mobility. One strategy to tackle this issue is to find cations with suitable valence states that can hybridize effectively with O 2p to push the VB edge higher in energy (i.e., lower ionization energy, IE) as well as to disperse the states at the top of the VB. Copper in the +1 oxidation state [Cu(I)] with 3d10 valence electrons is one such cation, and various Cu(I)-based compounds have been explored for p-type conductivity, such as cuprous oxide Cu2O, delafossites CuMO2 (M = Group 13), and oxychalcogenides LaCuOX (X = S, Se, or Te).3â€“6 Nevertheless, their practical applications in electronic devices have been limited.7

Expanding beyond oxides, copper(I) thiocyanate (CuSCN) is another promising transparent p-type inorganic semiconductor. Recently, its opto/electronic applications have been extensively demonstrated from active semiconducting channel layers in thin-film transistors (TFTs)8,9 to transparent hole-transport layers in organic photovoltaics (OPVs),10 organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs),11 and perovskite solar cells (PSCs).12,13 These demonstrations are highly remarkable given that CuSCN is a coordination polymer, a family of materials that have been largely unexplored for electronic applications. The unique properties of CuSCN are enabled by the electronic structure based on the interactions of Cu(I) and the molecular SCN ligand. In particular, the top of its VB retains the Cu 3d dominant character similar to other p-type Cu(I)-based compounds with some hybridization with the S 3p states that allows for attractive hole-transporting characteristics with the field-effect hole mobility in the range of 0.01 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and higher.14 On the other hand, the CB of CuSCN, consisting largely of the antibonding molecular orbital (MO) of the anionic SCN ligand,15 is high in energy, giving rise to excellent transparency in the visible spectrum.

However, material systems based on metal ns2 valence states also present strong potential for hole transport, in particular tin in the +2 oxidation state [Sn(II)]. SnO has been intensively explored as an active channel layer in p-type oxide TFTs recently, and field-effect mobilities in excess of 1 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 have been reported in several publications.7 Similar to electron-transporting oxides, the s electrons are more easily delocalized due to their spherical symmetry, hence more dispersion in the VB and larger hole mobility is expected.16 Nevertheless, two serious disadvantages of SnO remain: phase instability and a small fundamental band gap. SnO is metastable under ambient conditions with SnO2 being the more thermodynamically favorable phase; SnO films typically contain mixed phases, which are detrimental to charge carrier transport.17â€“19 Furthermore, the fundamental band gap of SnO is small with a value of 0.7 eV,20,21 which limits its use as a transparent hole-transporting layer in optoelectronic devices or as a pairing with transparent n-type oxides to realize transparent electronics.

As exemplified by p-type Cu(I) compounds, i.e., comparing Cu2O with CuSCN, the thiocyanate ligand preserves the metal-hybridized character of the VB while significantly widening the band gap from 2.1 eV in Cu2O (ref. 4) to more than 3.5 eV in CuSCN.8 The thiocyanate can also stabilize the reduced oxidation state as it is a much softer Lewis base than oxygen.22 We were therefore motivated to investigate the thiocyanate compound of Sn(II), Sn(NCS)2, with the aim of studying its electronic properties and potential as a transparent hole-transporting inorganic semiconductor. In this paper, we report the synthesis, structural analysis, and properties of Sn(NCS)2 based on a comprehensive array of experimental techniques and density functional theory (DFT). We also show for the first time the electronic application of Sn(NCS)2 as a hole transport layer (HTL) in organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells.

2. Structural analysis and properties of Sn(NCS)2

2.1. 1D and 2D structures of Sn(NCS)2

As-synthesized, Sn(NCS)2 appears as white needles (Note S1 and Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ). Single crystal X-ray diffraction studies show that Sn(NCS)2 crystallizes in the triclinic centrosymmetric P space group with an asymmetric unit consisting of a mononuclear Sn(II) center bonded to two NCS ligands with their nitrogen atoms, and hence the NCS designation of the thiocyanate in this case (Fig. 1a; crystallographic data are provided in the footnotes;â€¡ bond lengths and angles are listed in Tables S1 and S2 in the ESI,â€  respectively). This crystal structure agrees with the previous work by Filby et al.23 In addition to the reported structure, our analysis here reveals the low-dimensional structures of Sn(NCS)2 which are built up from the basic structural unit of a 1D chain that results from the bridging nature of the NCS ligand. These covalently-bonded chains pack together to form 2D sheets which are held together by non-covalent interactions.

2.3. Chemical states and optical properties

In addition to the analyses of the crystal and micro structures, the bulk physical properties of the Sn(NCS)2 powder were also studied via X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), attenuated total reflectance Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (ATR-FTIR), and ultraviolet-visible-near infrared spectroscopy (UV-Vis-NIR). The XPS narrow scan of the Sn 3d states (Fig. 3a) shows that Sn exists only in one chemical state which can be attributed to Sn(II) bonded to NCS groups. The binding energies of Sn 3d3/2 and Sn 3d5/2 are 495.4 eV and 486.9 eV, respectively. This emphasizes the ability of the NCS ligand to stabilize Sn(II), which would be advantageous for practical applications. The narrow scans of other elements also corroborate that the sample is Sn(NCS)2 as only a single bonding state is found for each element (except the adventitious carbon C 1s at 284.8 eV). Fig. S5aâ€“c (ESIâ€ ) show binding energies of N 1s at 399.2 eV, C 1s at 286.2 eV, S 2p1/2 at 163.6 eV, and S 2p3/2 at 162.4 eV all of which are attributed to bonding states in Sn(NCS)2. For CuSCN as a reference of another polymeric metal thiocyanate compound, N 1s is found at 398.5 eV, C 1s at 286.0 eV, and S 2p3/2 at 163.4 eV.12 In order to affirm the chemical stability of Sn(NCS)2, the Sn 3d XPS spectrum of an aged sample (stored under air at room temperature and a relative humidity of 30% for 8 months) was re-measured and compared with that of a freshly prepared sample. The results, plotted in Fig. S5d (ESIâ€ ), show no differences, corroborating the stability of the compound.

3. Electronic structure of Sn(NCS)2

3.1. Electronic band structure

Structure relaxation calculations were performed via DFT calculations using the Vienna Ab Initio Simulation Package (VASP).40 A large deviation of 15% from the experimental lattice constant in the a-direction occurs when using an exchangeâ€“correlation functional that does not account for dispersion interactions.41 However, when using the rev-vdW-DF2 exchangeâ€“correlation functional, which takes into account the dispersion interactions,42â€“44 these deviations shrink to 2%, resulting in a superior agreement with the experimental structure. These calculations therefore show that vdW interactions are crucial for describing the structure of Sn(NCS)2 and further supports our analysis of the 2D structure which packs in the a-direction via vdW forces (Section 2.1).

The calculated electronic band structure of Sn(NCS)2 is displayed in Fig. S7 (ESIâ€ ) with the top of the valence band (VB) or the highest occupied state set as E = 0 eV. The valence band maximum (VBM) is found along the Î“â€“R path whereas the conduction band minimum (CBM) is at the Y point. The fundamental band gap is therefore found to be an indirect type with a value of 2.35 eV. However, there exists another peak in the VB at the Y point which differs in energy from the true VBM by only 0.03 eV, suggesting that a direct transition is also possible in Sn(NCS)2 with a direct band gap of 2.38 eV. The calculated band gaps are smaller than the experimental values, however this is expected due to the well-known fact that the DFT underestimates the band gap of bulk solids.21,45,46 The problem arises from the fact that the exchangeâ€“correlation contribution to the energy is unknown and must be approximated in DFT. A better prediction of the band gap might be obtained by using hybrid functionals, which incorporate Hartreeâ€“Fock exchange into the energy calculation;21,47 however, such calculations also demand more computational resources. At the VBM and CBM, the hole and electron effective masses (mh* and me*) are calculated to be mh* â‰ˆ 0.4m0 and me* â‰ˆ 0.3m0, respectively, where m0 is the electron rest mass. It should be noted that these values are similar to those recently reported for CuSCN,48i.e., mh* â‰ˆ 0.5m0 for light holes and me* â‰ˆ 0.4m0. We could then expect Sn(NCS)2 to be another transparent pseudohalide semiconductor with good carrier transport in addition to CuSCN and CuSeCN.32,47

3.2. Density of states analysis

The density of states (DOS) and partial density of states (PDOS) are plotted in Fig. 4a. From the DOS, there are two highly localized and stable states centered at âˆ’18 eV and âˆ’14 eV which result from the bonding within the NCS ligand. From the PDOS, we can see that the first one is mainly due to the Ïƒ-bond between the N 2s and C 2s states, and the latter is also the Ïƒ-bond but between the S 3s and C 2p electrons. These assignments are also in agreement with the results of the electronic structure calculations of the free thiocyanate ion.36,49 At higher energies of the VB and the bottom part of the CB, the DOS has a broader distribution due to overlapping states: a result of the Sn 5s and 5p states interacting with the molecular orbitals of NCS.50

The PDOS of the 5s and 5p states of Sn and the valence p states of S, C, and N in the energy range of âˆ’10 eV to 7 eV are shown in Fig. 4b. We can divide the VB in this range into five sub-regions (I, IIa, IIb, IIIa, and IIIb) based on different orbital characteristics. Region I is related to the bonding states between Sn and NCS ligands (Sn 5s states with strong mixing from the N 2p states). Region II is largely due to the internal bonding within the NCS ligands (p states of N, C, and S). Finally, region III has a large contribution from the valence electrons of the NCS ligands that also mix with the Sn 5s states. The â€˜aâ€™ and â€˜bâ€™ designations are due to different Ïƒ and Ï€ MO characters of NCS ligands which will be described more in Section 3.3. Interestingly, the presence of the Sn 5s character near the top of the VB, associated with the Sn lone pair, results in band dispersion that could contribute to appreciable hole transport.

The bottom of the CB, which covers the energy range from 2 eV to 6 eV, is dominated by the Sn 5p states especially in the lower energy part with strong mixing from the C 2p and N 2p states. In addition, the Sn 5d states give only a minor contribution to the DOS, mostly mirroring the Sn 5p states in the VB and contributing to the high energy portion of the CB (Fig. 4a). The overall feature of the anion dominating the top of the VB (S 3p and N 2p) and the cation contributing largely to the bottom of the CB (Sn 5p) is a sign of the ionicity in the bonding. On the other hand, the mixing of Sn states in the VB as well as the strong directionality of the bonds are characteristics of covalent bonds. As expected, Sn(NCS)2 has a mixed bonding character.

Furthermore, the partial electron density plots help elucidate the bonding nature of the five VB sub-regions. Region I (Fig. 5e and f) represents the main bonding interactions between Sn and N. The two Snâ€“N bonds are likely Ïƒ-bonds between Sn 5s and the Ïƒ MO of the NCS units. Region IIa (Fig. 5g and h) and region IIb (Fig. 5i and j) appear to be related to the internal Ïƒ and Ï€ MOs, respectively, of the NCS ligands with minimal interactions with Sn. Most importantly, region IIIa (Fig. 5k and l) and region IIIb (Fig. 5m and n) correspond to the antibonding states between Sn and NCS that give rise to the lone pair on Sn. Region IIIa appears to represent the Ïƒ MO of the NCS, and the antibonding orbital on Sn is also of the Ïƒ type (with the Sn 5s states dominant). On the other hand, region IIIb appears to be associated with the Ï€ MO of NCS, and hence the Sn states are a hybridization of Sn 5s and Sn 5p.

4. Discussion on the electronic structure, properties, and application of Sn(NCS)2

The three Snâ‹¯S non-covalent bonds that add to the monocapped octahedral geometry are best described as tetrel bonds, a type of Ïƒ-hole interaction.51,52 The charge re-distribution that results from the formation of covalent bonds, N1â€“Sn, N2â€“Sn, and S1iâ€“Sn, leads to electrostatically positive regions around Sn that can interact with the Ï€ electrons on the S atoms of the NCS ligands forming tetrel bonds.26â€“29 The fact that the contribution of the Sn 5d orbitals is almost negligible in the VB (Fig. 4a) also supports such analysis because if the Snâ‹¯S contacts are of classical covalent type, we would expect the d states to participate in the bonding.

The overall DOS structure of Sn(NCS)2 shares many similarities with those of Sn(II) chalcogenides (SnX, X = O, S, Se, or Te)21,53â€“55 as well as that of Sn(II) iodide (SnI2).56 In particular, the highest VB of these Sn(II) compounds can be divided into three distinct regions. Starting from the lower energy, region I has strong contributions from both the Sn 5s states and the valence p states of the anion, yielding bonding interactions. The mid energy region, region II, is dominated by the anion valence p states with a smaller contribution from the Sn 5p states, also of bonding character. The high energy part, region III, is the antibonding states between the Sn 5s and the anion valence p states with some hybridization with the Sn 5p electrons. The CB is then composed of antibonding interactions between the Sn 5p and the anion valence p electrons. As seen from the PDOS (Fig. 4b) and the partial electron density plots (Fig. 5câ€“n), the VB structure of Sn(NCS)2 is broadly similar: region I with bonding character between Sn and NCS; region IIa and IIb with a dominant NCS character; and region IIIa and IIIb with antibonding interactions between the Sn 5s states and NCS (also with some hybridization with Sn 5p). The key difference is the fact that NCS has multiple MOs (as opposed to only valence p states of the chalcogens or halogens) that can interact with Sn, hence regions II and III are divided further into sub-region â€˜aâ€™ of NCS Ïƒ-states and sub-region â€˜bâ€™ of NCS Ï€-states.

Considering the formation of the lone pair in Sn(II) compounds, Walsh and Watson have theorized that the origin of the stereochemically active lone pair in compounds of post-transition metals with an ns2 electron configuration is based on the coupling of metal np states with the antibonding states of metal nsâ€“anion np electrons.55,57,58 In this case, the classical model of hybridization between the metal ns and metal np states to form the lone pair has been shown to be invalid; first of all, the p states of the anion are required to interact with the metal ns electrons to form the low-energy bonding states (region I) and simultaneously the intermediate high-energy antibonding states with a significant metal ns character. The latter would further interact with the metal np states forming the mid-energy bonding VB (region II) and the top antibonding VB states of the lone pair (region III). The metal ns2 electrons are therefore active in bonding (not an inert pair), and the stereochemically active nature also relies on the site distortion that forces sâ€“p hybridization. In the case of Sn(NCS)2, the Sn 5s electrons are indeed active in bonding as evident from the PDOS (Fig. 4b) and partial electron density map of region I (Fig. 5e and f). The highly distorted structure as a result of the bulky and cylindrical shape of the NCS ligands also promotes sâ€“p hybridization. The antibonding interactions of the Sn 5s and the MOs of NCS therefore give rise to the stereochemically active lone pair in Sn(NCS)2.

The band structure of Sn(NCS)2 shows that there are specific directions (around R and Y points) where dispersion is high (Fig. S7, ESIâ€ ), from which we can expect good hole transport. To demonstrate the electronic application of Sn(NCS)2, we fabricated organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells using Sn(NCS)2 as a hole transport layer (HTL) (see the detailed description of the device fabrication and characterization in Note S3, ESIâ€ ). The unoptimized cells show a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 4.06 Â± 0.29% (averaged from five cells) with the best-performing cell reaching 4.55% (see Fig. S8 and Table S3 in the ESIâ€ ). The results are highly encouraging as the processing and the properties of the Sn(NCS)2 film have not been optimized. The current deposition method yielded a rough and discontinuous Sn(NCS)2 layer which is highly detrimental to the device performance, reflected in the low fill factor (FF) of the cells. Nevertheless, this is the first successful demonstration of the electronic application of Sn(NCS)2. Further optimization of the film processing to obtain smooth and uniform films is expected to significantly improve the cell performance as well as allow the testing of other device applications, such as thin-film transistors (TFTs).

Also, from the perspective of material design, one way to further enhance the hole transport properties is to increase the interactions between the lone pairs on the Sn centers. Various studies have shown the significance of lone pairâ€“lone pair interactions toward hole transport in SnO. The 2D sheets of SnO have Sn lone pairs pointing outward, and the bulk structure is built from the vdW stacking of the sheets in the c-direction.21 Expanding the lattice along the c-axis of SnO leads to less dispersion at the top of the VB,54,59 and isolating the 2D Snâ€“O sheets into a double-layer or a single-layer results in highly localized states at the VBM.59 Both cases signify the role that lone pair interactions play on the dispersion of the VBM.

Consequently, recent design principles of p-type oxides based on Sn(II) with predicted low hole effective masses focus on structural modifications that achieve greater lone pair interactions: Ha et al.60 studied the effects of modifying Sn(II)-based oxides with K whereas Li et al.61 focused on Group 2 metals. Both employed DFT calculations and found that the alkali or alkaline earth ions effectively alter the structure of SnO but do not contribute to the electronic states of the top of the VB. Their results showed that small hole effective masses are found in structures with stronger Snâ€“Sn (i.e., lone pairâ€“lone pair) interactions as well as more overlap between Sn and O. Similar to SnO, our analyses have shown that Sn(NCS)2 also features 2D sheets with lone pairs pointing towards the interlayer space (Fig. 5a). Therefore, such structural modifications should be possible with Sn(NCS)2 and are the subject of our current investigations. In addition to metal ions, Sn(NCS)2 could also be modified by various organic ligands, as in the case of CuSCN.39

It is noteworthy that using NCS ligands to form compounds with Sn(II) retains the VB characteristics that are similar to those of Sn(II) chalcogenides, particularly the mixing of the Sn 5s and anion p states at the top of the VB that contribute to dispersed states. On the other hand, the CB of Sn(NCS)2 that results from the interactions of Sn 5p with NCS MOs (mostly the NC portion, Fig. 4a and b) is higher in energy, widening the band gap and rendering Sn(NCS)2 transparent to the visible spectrum. This work, therefore, further provides an example of realizing transparent p-type semiconductors of coordination polymers based on NCS ligands.

5. Conclusions

Sn(NCS)2 can be synthesized from the simple crystallization of SnSO4 and NaSCN. The detailed analysis of X-ray single crystal data reveals that the bulk structure is built up from covalently-bonded 1D chains (along the b-axis) which are packed into a 2D structure in the bc-plane via tetrel bonds (non-covalent interactions). The lone pair on Sn points outward from the 2D sheets to the interlayer space, and the bulk packing along the a-direction takes place through vdW interactions. XPS and UV-Vis-NIR results confirm that the thiocyanate ligand can stabilize Sn(II) and widen the band gap to 3.37 eV. DFT calculations support the lone pair orientation and the large band gap and show that the Sn 5s electrons are active in bonding as well as forming dispersed states at the VBM that could contribute to hole transport. The electronic application of Sn(NCS)2 has been demonstrated by employing it as the HTL in OPVs, yielding PCEs up to 4.55%, with the performance expected to be enhanced further by optimizing the processing methods.

We have shown that the thiocyanate ligand can be used to engineer transparent p-type semiconductors; its hybridization with the cations preserves the character of the VB while opening up the band gap. The flexibility of coordination chemistry will also allow for further modifications, e.g., by adding electronically inactive cations or other organic ligands, to tune the VB dispersion and consequently hole-transporting characteristics. We are also actively investigating in detail the solution-based thin-film deposition of Sn(NCS)2 and its applications in opto/electronic devices, and the results will be reported in a separate publication.

6. Experimental section

6.1. Materials and synthesis

Materials. Tin(II) sulfate (SnSO4, 95%, Aldrich) was used as a tin(II) precursor and sodium thiocyanate (NaSCN, 98%, Acros) as a thiocyanate source. Sulfuric acid (H2SO4, 96% AR grade, Carlo Erba) was diluted with deionized (DI) water to a concentration of 1 M before use. All chemicals were used as-received without further purification.
Synthesis. Sn(NCS)2 was synthesized using a crystallization method.62 NaSCN (12 g) was dissolved in DI water (50 mL) while SnSO4 (8 g) was separately dissolved in DI water (42 mL) and 1 M H2SO4 (8 mL), yielding a slightly cloudy solution. The final product was collected by filtration, washed three times with DI water, and dried in a vacuum oven (10 mbar) at 80 Â°C for 8 h. The average weight of Sn(NCS)2 from five repeated syntheses was 3.79 g, a yield of 43.3% (based on Sn).
