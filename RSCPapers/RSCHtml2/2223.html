Abstract

Incorporation of lanthanide ions (Ln) into TiO2 nanomaterials has recently been proved to be an efficient strategy to explore photoelectric active materials. Herein, three lanthanideâ€“titanium oxo-clusters (LnTOCs), formulated as [Ln2Ti6O2(C2O4)4(NO3)2(OiPr)20]Â·2HOiPr (Ln = La (1), Ce (2) and Eu (3)), are reported. Their structures feature two Ti3O13 subunits and a central La2O14 subunit bridged by four oxalate ions. The cluster core is surrounded by 20 OiPr groups, which easily undergo dealkoxy reactions. TOCs have now been used as photocatalytic materials under ambient conditions, and in most cases they should already not be the original clusters due to their sensitivity to moisture. However, less work on the degradation clusters has been discussed. In this work, the degradation products were carefully characterized and polymeric Lnâ€“TiOâ€“C2O4 materials were obtained. Using such materials, photocurrent response and photocatalytic dye degradation were examined. The improvements in the photocurrent intensity and photocatalytic activity of dye-degradation are attributed to the cooperative effects of lanthanide and oxalate doping in TiO catalysis.

1. Introduction

Incorporation of lanthanide ions (Ln) into TiO2 nanomaterials has been proved to be an efficient strategy to explore new functional materials. The photocatalytic activity of the Lnâ€“TiO2 materials can be improved by increasing the optical absorption, stabilizing the anatase phase and decreasing the electronâ€“hole recombination.1 In addition to enhancing the photocatalytic activity, recent studies have demonstrated that lanthanide-doped TiO2 can also be used as smart photoactive materials to enhance the efficiency of dye-sensitized solar cells2 and to explore fluorescent labeling materials for effective multiplex assay.3 On the other hand, lanthanideâ€“titanium oxo-clusters (LnTOCs) or lanthanideâ€“polyoxotitanates (LnPOTs) have recently been receiving great attention, because they can act as model compounds helping to understand the varied bonding structures of Ln ions in TiO2 materials and the energy transition between lanthanide ions and the TiO matrix.4 They can also act as a single molecule source for the fabrication of nanomaterials and film materials of Ln-doped titanium oxide.5 Similar to the lanthanide-doped TiO2 materials, LnTOCs can also be used as effective catalysts for water splitting6 and as luminescent materials.7

In the past few years, TOCs have been largely developed for syntheses and applications.8 Nevertheless, only a few LnTOCs have been reported. Except syntheses and crystal structure characterization,9 studies on their potential applications have been scarce up to now.4â€“7 Herein, we report the syntheses and crystal structures of three LnTOCs formulated as [Ln2Ti6O2(C2O4)4(NO3)2(OiPr)20]Â·2HOiPr (Ln = La (1), Ce (2) and Eu (3)). In these three compounds not only Ln ion but also oxalate were introduced into the LnTOCs. Oxalate, the simplest and cheapest binary carboxylic acid, has been used as an activating component for the photoredox catalysis and degradation of organic pollutants,10 and has also been utilized for the assembly of lanthanide metalâ€“organic frameworks (LnMOFs)11 and lanthanide coordination polymers12 due to its high affinity to Ln. However, based on our knowledge, no LnTOC with oxalate has been documented.

Recently, TOCs have been used as photocatalytic materials under ambient conditions and even in water systems. In most cases, they were already not the original clusters due to their sensitivity to moisture. However, less work on the degradation clusters was discussed.13 In this paper, the degradation products were carefully characterized. Using the degradation Lnâ€“TiOâ€“C2O4 materials, the photocurrent response and photocatalytic dye degradation were examined. The improvements in the photocurrent intensity of the Lnâ€“TiOâ€“C2O4 electrodes and the photocatalytic activity for dye-degradation are attributed to the cooperative effect of lanthanide and oxalate doping in TiO catalysis. We also find that the CeTOC-doped electrode exhibited the most excellent photocurrent response and effective photodegradation ability for amaranth dye.

2.4 Photocurrent and photocatalytic experiments

The photocurrent experiments were performed on a CHI650 electrochemistry workstation in a three-electrode system, the microcrystal samples were mixed with Nafion-D521 (Alfa) and coated directly on a cleaned ITO electrode (50 Î© per square cm), which was used as the working electrode. A Pt plate and a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) were used as the auxiliary electrode and the reference electrode, respectively. The working electrodes were positioned 20 cm from the light source with an effective irradiation area of 0.5 cm2. An aqueous solution of Na2SO4 (0.1 mmol Lâˆ’1, 100 mL) in a quartz cell was used as the supporting electrolyte. A commercial amaranth dye was used in the photocatalytic experiment and the initial concentration of the dye solution was 10 mg Lâˆ’1. A high pressure mercury lamp (250 W) served as the light source and was positioned 20 cm over the reaction cell. To 100 mL of the dye solution, 20 mg of powdered samples were dispersed in and the suspension was magnetically stirred under dark conditions for 30 min to reach the adsorptionâ€“desorption equilibrium. A water jacket was applied to maintain room temperature. During the photoreaction, 3 mL aliquots of the solution were collected at intervals of 10 min for analysis. A control experiment was performed without catalyst. The concentration of amaranth was determined using a UV-vis spectrophotometer at 520 nm.

3.1 Crystal structures

Compounds 1â€“3 were prepared directly by one-step in situ low-heat synthesis in anhydrous isopropanol. They are isomorphous in the formula [Ln2Ti6O2(C2O4)4(NO3)2(OiPr)20]Â·2HOiPr, Ln = La (1), Ce (2) and Eu (3). Single-crystal X-ray analysis shows that they have a [Ln2Ti6O2(C2O4)4(NO3)2] cluster core. Only the structure of 1 is discussed here to illustrate the structural features of the three compounds (Fig. 1a and Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ). The cluster is constructed of two Ti3O13 subunits and a centered La2O14 subunit and they are further assembled from four C2O42âˆ’ ions to form a sandwiched structure. Two Ti(IV) atoms in the Ti3O13 subunit take the distorted octahedral geometry (green in Fig. 1b) while the other Ti(IV) adopted a trigonal bipyramid geometry (cyan in Fig. 1b). The three TiO polyhedra are edge-shared with a Î¼3-oxo unit to form the Ti3O13 subunit. The La atom takes a nine-coordinated geometry with a chelated nitrate anion and the two LnO9 units are edge-shared together to form the La2O14 subunit. The oxalate ligands acting as bridges adopted two distinct modes when bridging the La(III) and Ti(IV) ions in complexes: one is the normal Î¼2-Îº1:Îº1:Îº1:Îº1 type (type I) and the other is the Î¼2-Îº1:Îº1:Îº1:Îº2 type (type II)12b (Fig. 1c). The C2O42âˆ’ planes of different types are almost perpendicular to each other with a dihedral angle of 82.9Â°. The main body of the [La2Ti6O2(C2O4)4(NO3)2] core is surrounded by 20 OiPr groups with bridged or non-bridged coordination modes, which easily undergo dealkoxy reactions, leading to the instability of the compounds. The stability of TOCs varied tremendously from stable to very unstable. In this case the crystals are very unstable in comparison with most of the reported TOCs.8

3.2 Studies of dealkoxy reactions

Numerous TOCs have been reported and now they have been used as materials or precursors for catalysis and photoelectroactive materials even in water systems. However, some of them are unstable under ambient conditions and only a few of them are stable as they are stabilized by other ligands, such as catechols.17 It is unbelievable that less research on the degradation and the degradation materials has been discussed,13 which might be due to the complication of the degradation reactions. 3.3 Photocatalytic dye degradation

It is known that most organic azo-dyes, such as amaranth, are hazardous to the environment and human beings. The prolonged intake of amaranth can cause tumors, allergies, respiratory problems, and birth defects in the human body. Photocatalytic dye degradation using TiO2 has emerged as one of the most promising routes for the removal of amaranth from water bodies.22 The photocatalytic ability of the aged samples of 1â€²â€“3â€² and Ti7 was evaluated by the photocatalytic degradation of amaranth under UV light illumination (see the Experimental section). As shown in Fig. 5a, after 40 min illumination, the residual concentrations in percentages were 25.5, 31.0 and 15.0% for 1â€²â€“3â€², which exhibited much higher UV light photocatalytic activity compared with that of Ti7, which should be ascribed to the doping of Ln3+ and oxalate. Among these photocatalysts, 2â€² exhibited the most excellent photocatalytic ability, which is in accordance with their photocurrent behaviors discussed above. The excellent redox property of Ce(III) is the key factor for the higher photocurrent and photocatalytic activities of 2â€².23 In order to further study the effects of the doped Ln ion and oxalate, the photocatalysis of the deoxalate sample of 2â€² (calcined at 500 Â°C) and the oxalate were also evaluated under the same catalytic conditions. The corresponding curves are plotted in Fig. 5b and about 35% and 55% of the dye were photodegraded, respectively. Comparing with the percentage of Ti7, both the doped Ln ion and oxalate can promote the photocatalytic activity of the TiO material, but the oxalate was more effective in the catalytic reaction. During photocatalysis, the absorption of photons by the TiO photocatalyst causes the excitation of electrons from the valence band to the conduction band, thus generating electronâ€“hole pairs (eâˆ’/h+). The presence of lanthanide ions with partially filled d or f-orbital can effectively trap the electron in the conduction band and significantly reduce the recombination with holes in the valence band,1a,24 which are advantageous and useful in the photodegradation of the dyes. On the other hand, oxalate can be oxidized by the reaction with a hole giving rise to a radical oxalate and to yield carbon dioxide and a CO2Ë™âˆ’ radical. The CO2Ë™âˆ’ and oxalate radicals react with molecular oxygen in water yielding superoxide and hydroperoxyl radicals (O2Ë™âˆ’/HO2Ë™âˆ’). The O2Ë™âˆ’/HO2Ë™âˆ’ radicals are key intermediates because of their strong oxidizing power, which degrades the organic dyes.10b Based on these photocatalytic mechanisms and our experiments, a proposed cooperative effect of Ln(III) and oxalate promoted the photocatalytic degradation of the amaranth dye and is presented in Fig. 5d, where the oxalate as a sacrificial component plays a role in improving the photocatalytic performance of titanium dioxide.

4. Conclusion

In summary, three isostructured LnTOCs (Ln2Ti6, here Ln = La, Ce and Eu) were synthesized and characterized crystallographically. The cluster features two Ti3O13 subunits and a central La2O14 subunit bridged by four oxalate ions. The main body of the cluster is coordinated around by 20 OiPr groups, which easily undergo dealkylation. The degradation clusters were carefully characterized and were found to be the fused microparticle material with the most probable composition [Ln2Ti6O11(C2O4)4(NO3)2]. The photoelectrochemical properties of the materials were examined and the results showed the enhancement in photocurrent density because of the lanthanide metal doping of the TiO electrodes and oxalate aiding in the electron transfer process. Photocatalytic dye degradation using the degradation material was also evaluated. A cooperative effect of lanthanide and oxalate doping for the degradation of amaranth dye was first reported. These materials are a kind of unique catalyst with Lnâ€“TiOâ€“C2O4 multifunctional components and the oxalate anion plays an important role in the dye degradation.
