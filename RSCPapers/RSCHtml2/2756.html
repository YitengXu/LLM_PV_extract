Abstract

Modification of the Ïƒ-framework of [60]fullerene has been shown to be a new avenue toward n-type materials for bulk-heterojunction solar cells. This approach allowed distinct control of the LUMO level of the C60 Ï€-system and resulted in a clear improvement of an open-circuit voltage.

Ï€-Conjugated semiconductor materials have occupied a dominant position in organic thin-film photovoltaic devices, so called bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells.1 The core of the device is a single active layer which typically consists of a blend of an electron-donating polymer, such as poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT), and an electron-accepting molecule, such as (6,6)-phenyl C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM).2 In the light of easy fabrication through a solution process, flexibility, and large area application, the BHJ solar cell will be a potential alternative to the conventional silicon-based solar cell, if the low power conversion efficiency (PCE) is overcome.1 During the last few years the PCEs have been rapidly improved largely owing to development of a wide variety of donor copolymers made of electron-rich and electron-poor building blocks with enhanced absorptions in the visible area.3

It is well known that electronic properties of the donor polymers should be controlled to match those of the acceptors. For instance, the energy level of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the donor is required to be higher than that of the acceptor by 0.3â€“0.5 eV to overwhelm the binding energy of excitons.1 Under the circumstances of the rapidly extended repertoire of the low band gap polymers,1,3 establishment of new methodology capable of tuning the electronic properties of the [60]fullerene is of increasing significance for creating a rational acceptor/donor pair. However, most studies on the acceptors have so far been focused on the structural modification of the benchmark acceptor, PCBM, toward raising its LUMO energy level by (i) installation of electron-donating group(s) on its phenyl ring,4 (ii) saturation of one more CC double bond on the fullerene cage to give an almost inseparable mixture of regioisomers,5 and (iii) encapsulation of a trimetallic nitride cluster inside the C80 cage to give the endohedral PC81BM analogs.6 As another approach, 1,4-di(organo)C60 derivatives7a and indene-C60 bisadduct7b were recently reported.

During our studies on organic synthesis of endohedral fullerenes encapsulating small gaseous entities,8,9 so called molecular surgery,10 we have established techniques to modify the Ïƒ-framework of fullerenes to create a wide opening. The unique features of the skeletally-modified fullerenes (SMFs) lie in the retention of the 60Ï€- or 70Ï€-conjugated systems as well as in the appearance of rich chemical reactivity at the opening.11 Actually, as we reported recently, the opening of SMF derivative 1 could be effectively transformed to that of 2 and 3.12 These SMF derivatives have excellent solution processability due to two n-octyl chains.

Thus, the present approach provides a useful way to control the LUMO energy level of C60 without causing significant loss of the native Ï€-conjugation. Since an open-circuit voltage (Voc) has an association with the energy difference between acceptor's LUMO and donor's HOMO,14 the higher LUMO energy level of 3 than that of PCBM should contribute to improve the Voc of the device when 3 is combined with a donor polymer with an adequately high LUMO level. In order to observe the effects of controlled LUMO levels of SMFs on the open-circuit voltage, BHJ solar cells were fabricated using 1â€“3 as the acceptor with a combination of the representative donor polymer, P3HT, by basically following the procedure established for a PCBM:P3HT-based device.15 The devices with a layered structure of glass/ITO/PEDOT:PSS/SMF:P3HT/TiOx/Al were prepared as follows. The chlorobenzene solutions containing an SMF derivative and P3HT at a weight ratio of 1:1.6 were spin-coated onto an indium tin oxide (ITO)/glass substrate covered with a PEDOT:PSS layer under argon, and the resulting films were annealed at 150 Â°C for 6 min to induce phase separation. Then, a TiOx layer was coated on the active layers in air and subsequently an aluminium electrode was thermally deposited under vacuum. The optimum thickness of the BHJ films was approximately 80 nm. The surface morphologies of the active layers of 1:P3HT, 2:P3HT, 3:P3HT, and PCBM:P3HT as a standard reference were observed by the use of atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Fig. 2). All films using 1â€“3 showed fine features similar to that using PCBM. The root mean square (rms) roughness was 1.0 nm for 1:P3HT, 0.9 nm for 2:P3HT, 1.1 nm for 3:P3HT, and 1.0 nm for PCBM:P3HT, respectively, indicating that the domain size of all blend films was almost identical regardless of the skeleton-modifications of C60 and the introduction of the n-octyl chains.

Actually, the shape of the LUMO was relatively localized at the opening (see the ESIâ€ ).19 The almost completely delocalized LUMOs of 2 and 3 as well as PCBM are considered to contribute to their improved Jsc and FF.

In summary, we have demonstrated that the structural manipulation of the C60 skeleton is a promising approach for the creation of acceptor materials for BHJ solar cells. This approach can be used to effectively control the electronic properties of the C60 moiety with the original 60Ï€-conjugation being largely retained to rationally match the LUMO level to that of the donor materials. Actually, by combining the SMF derivatives with a typical donor polymer, P3HT, we obtained the higher Voc value of 0.74 V than that of the PCBM:P3HT-based device by Î”Voc = 140 mV. In order to improve the performance of the BHJ solar cells, it is also indispensable to enhance the carrier mobility. We are currently studying the structural optimization of the substituents toward improvement of the morphology in the BHJ films.
