Although solar cells are recognized as promising candidates to compensate for the electrical energy demand because they can directly convert the clean, abundant, and sustainable solar light energy into the most convenient and safe electrical energy, the power generation cost of the conventional crystalline Si solar cells is still more expensive than that of fossil fuel.

To date, solution processable organic photovoltaics (OPVs),1 thin-film solar cells (T-FSCs),2 dye-sensitized solar cells (D-SSCs),3 and quantum dot-solar cells (QD-SCs)4 have been intensively studied to develop more cost effective solar cells than the crystalline Si solar cells. Unfortunately, the cost effective solution processable solar cells with high efficiency among the abovementioned solar cells have not been reported because their power conversion efficiency is <13% under conditions of 1 sun, which is much lower than the efficiency of crystalline Si solar cells.

Recently, cost effective perovskite hybrid solar cells with high power conversion efficiency were reported, which can be fabricated by simple solution processes such as spin-coating, spray coating, slot die coating, and 3D printing, and their certified record efficiency reached over 20% at conditions of 1 sun.6k Since Kojima et al.5 reported the liquid junction perovskite-sensitized solar cells, to date efficient solid state mesoscopic,6 planar,7 and meso-superstructured8 perovskite hybrid solar cells have been developed. The high efficiencies of perovskite hybrid solar cells seem to be attributed to the unique material properties such as high absorption coefficient by direct bandgap, high open circuit voltage by small exciton binding energy, long diffusion length of charge carriers through the formation of shallow traps, and low temperature solution processability.

In general, the perovskite materials have a AMX3 crystal structure (A: monovalent metal cation or organoalkyl ammonium, M: divalent metal such as Pb, Sn, Ge, Cu, Mn, and Co, and X: halogen such as Cl, Br, and I). For solar cells, pure AMX3 (A: methyl ammonium or formamidinium, M: Pb, X: Br or I) perovskite or mixed halide AMX3âˆ’xYx (A: methyl ammonium and formamidinium, M: Pb, X: I, and Y: Br or Cl) perovskite materials are usually used. Pure CH3NH3PbI3 (MAPbI3) and CH3NH3PbI3âˆ’xClx (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) mixed halide perovskite hybrid solar cells are known to have charge carrier diffusion lengths of âˆ¼100 nm and âˆ¼1000 nm, respectively.9 In terms of charge collection efficiency and reproducibility of device efficiency, according to the thickness deviation of the perovskite layer formed by the solution process, the mixed halide perovskite solar cells have greater benefit than of the pure perovskite solar cells. However, the CH3NH3PbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite material cannot be directly formed by a simple solution process but is obtained by the long thermal annealing processing (âˆ¼40 min) of a 3:1 molar mixture of MAI:PbCl2 and the subsequent elimination/sublimation of unreacted MAI and MACl byproduct,8a,10 whereas pure MAPbI3 perovskite materials can be directly formed by a simple drying process.7j,k Therefore, it is quite difficult to form uniform pinhole-free dense MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite thin films over large area via a single step solution process; however, the pure MAPbI3 perovskite can form a uniform dense thin film via a single step solution process. Therefore, it is quite challenging to form uniform dense MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite thin film over large area via single step solution process. Herein, we report the fabrication of uniform dense MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite thin films via a single step spin-coating process and highly reproducible planar MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite hybrid solar cells.

To eliminate the problematic long thermal annealing process for the formation of a MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite, we tried to synthesize it via simple solution chemistry. For a typical procedure, we manually mixed a 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2 powder (3 g: 1.76 g) in a vial and poured 15 mL iso-propanol into the vial. The solution mixture was then magnetically stirred for 30 min at 60 Â°C for the chemical reaction of MAI and PbCl2. Note that the reaction can proceed at room temperature but to ensure/promote the reaction, we intentionally heated the solution to 60 Â°C. Initially, the solution mixture was turbid and gradually changed color to black. To separate the black precipitated powder from the solution, we centrifuged the solution for 10 min at 3000 rpm. The black powder was then dried in a vacuum oven for 5 h at room temperature, as shown in Fig. 1. Herein, it should be noted that iso-propanol (IPA) can selectively dissolve unreacted MAI and MACl byproducts formed by the long heat-treatment process, but it does not dissolve MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite. The IPA solvent therefore has dual function as (i) the reaction medium for the formation of MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite by the reaction of 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2, and (ii) a selective remover of unreacted MAI and MACl byproducts. These byproducts are not uniformly removed/sublimated by the conventional, relatively long heat-treatment processing of the 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2 film at 100 Â°C for âˆ¼40 min, and this causes pin-holes or a rough surface due to the removed sections in the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide film. Accordingly, the overall reaction for the new process (1) and the conventional process (2) can be written as follows:

To examine the composition of the materials produced by the solution reaction of a 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2 in IPA solution at 60 Â°C for 30 min and the subsequent repeated washing process, we analysed the XRD (X-ray diffraction) patterns of the dried powders of samples (aâ€“e), as shown in Fig. 2(g). Conclusions

In summary, we successfully synthesized a pure MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite powder via simple solution chemistry and demonstrated highly reproducible, efficient planar type MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells. To synthesize MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite powder, we reacted a 3:1 molar ratio of MAI:PbCl2 powder mixture in IPA solution for 30 min at 60 Â°C. We then repeated the centrifugation and washing process in 1 to 5 cycles. The 1 and 2 round repeated centrifugation and washing processes yielded the formation of MACl and PbCl2 due to incomplete reaction, and 3 rounds of repeated centrifugation and washing produced a pure MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite powder. Over 4 rounds of repeated centrifugation and washing formed MAI and PbI2 impurities due to the partial decomposition of MAPbI3âˆ’xClx. Through the simple one step spin-coating of pure MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite powder in DMF solution with HI additive, we deposited a pinhole-free, dense MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite film on a TiO2/FTO substrate without a further long heat-treatment process. The deposited MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite film revealed uniform composition throughout the entire area, and the ratio of Cl to I + Cl and I + Cl to Pb was constant at âˆ¼0.03 and âˆ¼1/3, respectively. On the other hand, the conventional MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite film prepared by a long heat-treatment process had non-uniform composition because the ratio of Cl to I + Cl fluctuated greatly from 0 to 7.2, due to non-uniform solidâ€“solid phase reaction and incomplete elimination/sublimation of unreacted MAI and MACl byproducts. The average efficiency of planar type MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells was 18.65% Â± 0.30%, and the champion cell had 1.11 V Voc, 22.1 mA cmâˆ’2Jsc, 77% F.F. and 18.9% Î· for forward scan conditions, and 1.11 V Voc, 22.1 mA cmâˆ’2Jsc, 78% F.F. and 19.1% Î· for reverse scan conditions. Therefore, the pure MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide planar type perovskite solar cells did not show significant Jâ€“V hysteresis with respect to the scan direction. Even though the thickness of the MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite layer varied from âˆ¼500 nm to âˆ¼900 nm, the efficiency varied only slightly from âˆ¼18.3% to âˆ¼19.0%; therefore, we could reproducibly fabricate highly efficient planar MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite solar cells with narrow efficiency deviation. We believe that this pure MAPbI3âˆ’xClx mixed halide perovskite material will benefit the fabrication of a large area submodule with high reproducibility via a simple solution coating process such as spray coating and bar coating.
