Abstract

Given the critical demands on energy conversion, storage, and transportation, tremendous interest has been devoted to the field of material development related to energy harvesting, recently. As the only route towards energy utilization, the carriers with the characteristics of low carbon are regarded as the future choice, e.g., hydrogen and ammonia. To this end, electrocatalysis provides a green way to access these substances. However, the unfulfilled conversion efficiency is the bottleneck for practical application. In this review, the promising characteristics of amorphous materials and the amorphous-induced electrocatalytic enhancement (AIEE) were emphasized. In the beginning, the characteristics of amorphous materials are briefly summarized. The basic mechanism of heterogeneous electrocatalytic reactions is illustrated, including the hydrogen/oxygen evolution and oxygen/nitrogen reduction. In the third part, the electrocatalytic performance of amorphous materials is discussed in detail, and the mechanism of AIEE is highlighted. In the last section of this review, the challenges and outlook for the development of amorphous enhanced electrocatalysis are presented.

1. Introduction

Energy has already become one of the cornerstones of modern society, especially since the world economy dramatically depends on the cost of energy production. However, the continuous exploitation and consumption of fossil fuels during the past century has raised a series of environmental concerns, including the greenhouse effect, ocean pollution, and haze.1 Additionally, as a non-renewable energy resource, fossil fuels are suffering from their limited stock. Therefore, the identification of new eco-friendly energy sources with advantages of high energy density, chemical stability, and low cost like fossil fuel becomes an urgent task at this moment.1

On the other hand, people never stop their steps towards the exploration and utilization of new energy resources such as wind, solar, tide, and geothermal. This kind of energy cannot be stored as such.2 One of the most promising ways to utilize this intermittent energy is to store them in different carriers like hydrogen (H2), ammonia (NH3), and hydrocarbons, in which the discontinuous energy is stocked in chemical bonding and can be feasibly extracted later.3 In industry, H2 is mainly obtained by methane steam reforming and coal gasification, but both of these methods produce large amounts of carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide byproducts, which in turn lead to environmental problems and are not conducive to sustainable development.4 Therefore, the production of high purity H2 by water electrolysis using electricity generated from renewable sources is one of the most important solutions to fuel depletion and environmental problems. NH3 is used in all aspects of human life, but the predominant method of producing ammonia today is HaberBosch ammonia synthesis, which uses high purity nitrogen (N2) and H2 at high temperatures and pressure.5 This has also led to an energy crisis and environmental pollution. Zincair batteries (ZABs) are known as the most promising energy storage devices because of their high specific energy density, which involves oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR).6 Therefore, a summary of the elementary-gas-involved electrocatalysis will be of great help for our future research, and the electrocatalytic method has been regarded as a promising candidate in recent years due to its moderate reaction conditions and high efficiency. However, with the thermodynamic barrier of the reaction process, usually, the high-active noble-metal-based catalysts are applied. Recent researches have demonstrated that the efficiency of the electrocatalytic process highly depends on the binding strength between the surface atoms from the electrocatalyst and the intermediate products. With an example of Pt, its suitable surface d-electronic structure offers a moderate binding strength, and thus a dynamic balance between adsorption and desorption of intermediate products is achieved. The moderate adsorption/desorption of intermediate products is ascribed to be the dominant reason for the outstanding electrocatalytic activity.

Beyond the chemical composition, enhanced electrocatalytic performance is also observed on atoms under nonequilibrium/unsaturated coordinated environments (e.g., point defect, grain boundary, and interface), or known as non-crystalline region. In order to interpret this phenomenon, various concepts have been introduced, for instance, defect engineering, grain boundary effects, or interfacial engineering.7 However, the intrinsic relation between these early researches is usually neglected, that is, the disordered atomic arrangement and the resulting nonequilibrium ligand field affect the electronic structure of involved atoms, thereby regulating the adsorption/desorption of electrocatalytic intermediate products. In consideration of the above-mentioned mechanism, herein, the concept of amorphous-induced electrocatalytic enhancement (AIEE) was proposed. There are many research studies on the electrocatalytic properties of crystalline materials.812 Thus, we believe that a comprehensive review of amorphous electrochemical catalysts is urgent for an intensive understanding of the recent advances in this field. In this review, the definition of crystalline and amorphous electrocatalysts is illustrated in the first section. In the following sections, recent developments of AIEE on hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR), and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) are briefly summarized. The underlying mechanism of AIEE is also highlighted. Finally, the challenges and prospects of AIEE are proposed based on its current developments.

2. Crystalline and amorphous materials

In the studies of electrocatalysts, principles derived from solid-state chemistry are widely used towards understanding the interaction mechanisms at the atomic level. Particularly, the crystalline solids consisting of atoms, ions, or molecules were believed to be the electrocatalytic active species and have been taken as model structures for mechanism investigation in the past century. In crystalline solids, the basic constituents are arranged repetitively in three dimensions, forming a well-defined and long-range ordered microscopic structure.13

However, due to thermal motion, the atoms, ions, or molecules in a crystal lattice sometimes deviate from the ideal position, resulting in the disorder at the atomic level, which is the so-called defect. As shown in Fig. 1a, given the dimensions, there are mainly four kinds of defects in the catalyst: point defects (e.g., doping and vacancy); line defects (e.g., dislocation and steps); planar defects (e.g., boundary, twining, and stacking faults); and bulk defects (e.g., pores, cracks, and inclusions, or even a spatial lattice disorder).14,15 Typically, the stepped structure is a one-dimensional (1D) dislocation formed in the process of crystal growth. The coordination of the atoms at step sites is incomplete. The atoms at the step edge possess more unsaturated dangling chemical bonds than the atoms on the flat surface or in the bulk, which results in distinct differences in physical and chemical properties. Therefore, the stepped structure is often desired in the preparation of various advanced catalysts.1618 Moreover, defects play an important role in electrocatalysis since they have a non-equilibrium bonding environment as well as a distinctive local electron density of state and are often the active sites during the catalytic process. Additionally, defects can also affect the local electronic structure around them and generate more catalytic sites on the surface.19,20 Thus, defect engineering represents the most effective technique to precisely control the electrocatalytic performance.21

Notably, in an extreme situation, the entire lattice constituents deviate from their position, which means the long-range ordered microscopic structure is totally disrupted. Due to the random and disorderly arrangement of constituents, the amorphous solids usually present some unique physicochemical properties when compared with the crystalline counterpart (Fig. 1b). Generally, it is believed that higher catalytic activity can be released by disordered atomic arrangements in amorphous catalysts than the ordered crystalline lattices.22 Thereby, amorphous materials have shown satisfactory performance as advanced electrocatalysts. For instance, the composition of amorphous materials can be adjusted more casually due to the unfixed atomic ratio and bonding, greatly expanding the range of designing catalysts with the best content.23 In addition, amorphous materials produce plenty of coordination unsaturated surface sites and act as catalytically active centers, and we can combine experimental results with calculations to investigate the mechanism in more depth.24,25 Besides, the manufacturing of amorphous materials usually requires low-cost precursors under mild conditions, which will greatly reduce the manufacturing cost.26

3. Basics of typical heterogeneous electrocatalytic reactions

3.1 Hydrogen evolution reaction

As an anodic half-cell reaction of the electrochemical water-splitting process,27 the HER involves two-electron transportation, in which the reaction pathways of hydrogen intermediate (H*) can be divided into VolmerHeyrovsky process (electrochemical desorption mechanism) or VolmerTafel process (recombinant desorption mechanism). As summarized below and shown in Fig. 2, the generation of hydrogen intermediate (H*) will strongly depend on the pH of the electrolyte:

In acidic media:

H+ + e H*(1, Volmer step)

H+ + e + H* H2(2, Heyrovsky step)

H* + H* H2(3, Tafel step)

In alkaline media:

H2O + e H* + OH(4, Volmer step)

H2O + e + H* H2 + OH(5, Heyrovsky step)

H* + H* H2(6, Tafel step)


Fig. 2 Schematic illustration of HER reaction pathways.

In fact, the energy barrier of each step affects the overall HER process. Particularly, the HER process in an alkaline medium involves the cleavage of the HO bond from the water molecule, which requires much more energy than the reaction in acid. For example, for the precious metal Pt, the HER activity in the alkaline medium is dramatically decreased to less than one percent of that in acid. However, although the electrocatalytic process of HER has been studied for nearly one century, the underlying mechanisms are still under debate.

In the past decades, various theories have been proposed to quantitatively explain the underlying relationship between elementary composition, phase, and HER activity. Sabatier first qualitatively proposed the volcanic diagram in heterogeneous catalysis and pointed out that there must be appropriate chemical interactions between the catalyst and reactant. With either a too weak or too strong interaction, the HER kinetics will be slowed down.28 Concerning electrocatalytic HER, Parson proposed a volcanic type diagram linking the exchange current density J0 to the thermodynamically derived GH*.29 At the top of the volcano, GH* = 0, corresponding to the maximum J0. Only metals showing moderate hydrogen adsorption have high J0 values, such as Pt, Pd, and Rh. More simply, according to Sabatier's principle, from a physicochemical point of view, the change of GH* on the catalyst surface can be evaluated by HER free energy diagram to intuitively judge the difficulty of H* adsorption and H2 desorption.30 Therefore, in order to achieve the maximum HER rate, GH* should be zero. Besides, the d-band theory is another earliest and most important electronic descriptor, where a higher (lower) d-band center position would yield a stronger (weaker) affinity toward adsorbates.31 Therefore, the regulation of intrinsic HER activity should be based on the optimization of intermediate adsorption energy. The unique coordination environment on the surface of amorphous materials meets the diversified control requirements.

3.2 Oxygen evolution reaction

For the cathodic half-cell reaction of electrochemical water-splitting, the OER, consisting of four-electron transfer steps, shows much more sluggish kinetics than the HER counterpart. Therefore, the OER process usually plays a critical role in the rate of the overall water-splitting process, which is known as the rate-determining reaction. Similar to HER, the OER also presents different reaction mechanisms under acidic or alkaline conditions. Fig. 3 illustrates the possible reaction pathways for OER under acidic and alkaline conditions, which can be summarized in eqn (7)(15):

In acidic media:

H2O OH* + H+ + e(7)
OH* O* + H+ + e(8)
O* + H2O OOH* + H+ + e(9)
OOH* O2 + H+ + e(10)

In alkaline media:

OH OH* + e(11)
OH* + OH O* + H2O + e(12)
O* + OH OOH* + e(13)
OOH* + OH O2 + H2O + e(14)

Under both conditions:

O* + O* O2(15)


Fig. 3 Schematic illustration of OER reaction pathways.

As presented, it can be found that O2 can also be generated via the recombinant desorption mechanism (eqn (15)). On the other hand, the OER process in either acidic or alkaline media involves the transformation of oxygen-containing intermediates (OH*, O*, and OOH*). Unfortunately, the reactivity of these oxygen-containing intermediates and the possible side reactions make the exploration of the exact OER reaction mechanism rather difficult. In recent years, various in situ techniques, including Mssbauer spectrum, X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS), and Raman spectroscopy, have been applied to the characterization of the detailed OER mechanism.3235 Particularly, it is nearly a basic consensus that the OOH* intermediate can strongly bind with the surface metal atoms of catalysts, which contribute to a thin layer of metal oxyhydroxide (M-OOH) cover on the catalysts.3639 Interestingly, due to the continuity of the OER process, the generated M-OOH layer is in dynamic equilibrium, which is hard to crystallize. Great progress has been made in the study of the catalytic mechanism of amorphous catalysts for OER. Lian and his colleagues found that the performance of amorphous Ni-rich NiCo alloy is better than that of the crystalline alloy, which is the first report in history showing that amorphous materials are more dominant than crystalline materials in the process of electrocatalytic decomposition of water.40 They later revealed that the mechanism of OER for amorphous NiCo alloy in alkaline solution changed from low overpotential to high overpotential.41 In addition, a suitable amount of Fe and Ni-doped Co-based catalyst can produce higher OER activity and lower overpotential, which is related to the decrease in catalyst surface adsorption energy; under OER conditions, Ni substitution can promote the surface reconstruction to active CoOOH.42,43 Yan et al. proposed that the doping of Ni improved the cooperative work under OER, promoted the surface reconstruction, and evolved into active hydroxyl sites.44 Another study on the catalytic mechanism utilized the operando Mssbauer spectrum to acquire direct evidence towards the formation of Fe4+ during the steady-state catalytic turnover of NiFe oxide catalyst.45 The existence of Fe4+ in NiFe catalyst is mainly due to the stabilization of the NiOOH lattice. It is considered that OER occurs in the active Fe4+ species produced at the edges, corners, or surface defect sites of the NiOOH matrix. Consequently, amorphous materials tend to have more defects than crystalline materials, which are often catalytically active sites and play an important role in the OER process.

3.3 Nitrogen reduction reaction

Except for the electrocatalytic water-splitting process, in recent years, the NRR has received increasing attention as well. As a competitive reaction with HER, the NRR also occurs under electroreduction conditions. However, six electrons are involved during the NRR process, which makes the NRR much unfavorable on the traditional water-splitting electrocatalysts. More importantly, the cleavage of the NN triple bond from the N2 molecule will occur during the NRR process, which requires much higher energy than the HER. From the thermodynamic point of view, for example, the first hydrogenation step of NRR in acidic conditions requires an equilibrium potential lower than 3 V vs. reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE), which is much more negative than the reduction potential of H+ (0 V vs. RHE). Therefore, under the reduction potential, most of the protons or water molecules are consumed for the generation of H2 rather than NH3, resulting in a serious selectivity issue. As shown in Fig. 4, depending on the hydrogenation mechanism, the NRR process in either acidic or alkaline media can be divided into three reaction routes: the distal route, the alternating path, or the dissociative route. For the distal and alternating pathways, the N2 molecule was adsorbed on the surface of the catalyst with a side-on configuration, and the NH3 products were released in turn. Therefore, these two mechanisms were also called associative paths. On the contrary, the NN triple bond was broken before adsorption, and the hydrogenation process was started on each single adsorbed N atom on the surface of the catalyst, which is named the dissociative path.
Fig. 4 Schematic illustration of NRR reaction pathways.

Luckily, it was found that the energy barriers of succeeding hydrogenation steps are much lower than the first hydrogenation step. Therefore, the major bottleneck on the improvement of NRR selectivity might depend on the first hydrogenation step. For example, Yu et al. prepared amorphous precious metal-free Bi4V2O11/CeO2 hybrid material (BVC-A) by single spinneret electrospinning combined with calcination as the cathode of electrocatalytic NRR.46 CeO2 not only induces the transition of Bi4V2O11 to an amorphous state, but also participates in the energy band arrangement of the catalyst, realizes the rapid transfer of interfacial charge, and produces rich defects as active centers. BVC-A showed a higher average NH3 yield (23.21 g h1 mg1cat). Herein, a typical work highlights the excellent selectivity of amorphous catalysts. Yan et al. served PdCu amorphous nanoclusters on reduced graphene oxide (RGO) as an NRR catalyst, and the optimized Pd0.2Cu0.8/rGO owns the best performance.47 Generally, Pd is considered to be more capable of binding to hydrogen atoms than nitrogen atoms, so Pd is often covered by hydrogen molecules (H2).48 The introduction of Cu accelerates the hydrogen desorption on the surface of Cu metal. Facile hydrogen dissociation on Pd atoms and weak binding with Cu makes the hydrogenation reaction highly selective. Compared with pure metal Pd or Cu, on the one hand, the electron conversion between bimetals produces new catalytic sites, which helps to activate gas molecules. On the other hand, the dangling bonds on amorphous materials provide active sites with higher activity than crystalline catalysts. These factors increase the possibility of NRR. In addition, in the amorphous sulfur multilayered modified gold NWs (S/Au-NWS) prepared by a two-step method, the chemically adsorbed sulfur multilayers are conducive to the selective hydrogenation of nitrogen to ammonia and provide sufficient exposed active sites for NRR.49

3.4 Oxygen reduction reaction

As another classical electrocatalytic reaction that has been studied for a long time, the ORR is also essential, especially in fuel cells. In acidic media, the mechanism of ORR can be summarized as in Fig. 5, which shows similar pathways to the NRR process: the dissociative path and the associative path, including the distal route and the alternating route. In examples of the ORR process on Pt surface, the dissociative path can be described as the direct four-electron pathway, which starts from adsorption of an O2 molecule, followed by OO bond breaking and forming two adsorbed reactive oxygen species (O*). The O* further gains two electrons and two protons to directly form the final product.

Compared with the clear ORR mechanism under acidic conditions, the reaction process under alkaline conditions is more complex, involving a variety of intermediates, which is also the focus of current research.5052 For amorphous catalysts, Cho et al. synthesized a composite electrode composed of amorphous manganese oxide (MnOx) NWs supported by carbon black (KB) by polyol method. The large surface area with high defect density on the amorphous MnOx NWs facilitates oxygen adsorption, which significantly enhances the ORR activity when assembled in a zincair battery (peak power density 190 mW cm2).53 Besides, Lu et al. successfully prepared Pd modified amorphous NiB/C catalyst (Pd@NiB/C), which showed a significant increase in ORR activity.54 Partial discharge modification significantly increased the half-wave potential and limited the current. Pd@NiB/C catalyst also showed impressive methanol tolerance. It has the advantages of high performance, strong methanol resistance, and ultra-low palladium load.

4. Amorphous materials and the electrocatalytic enhancements

4.1 Amorphous carbon-based electrocatalysts

Given the energy and environmental crisis, the mass production and utilization of novel clean and renewable power resources has become more important in modern society. In this regard, hydrogen as a promising energy carrier has raised increasing attention. Meanwhile, the electrocatalytic HER technique, which provides an efficient and clean pathway for hydrogen, has motivated the exploration of cheap and highly active HER electrocatalysts. In addition to HER catalysts, amorphous carbon-based materials have also been used to study OER, ORR, and NRR. In particular, carbon-based electrocatalysts (carbon cloths (CC),5557 carbon nanotube (CNT),58 porous carbons,59,60 carbon nanofiber (CNF),6163 graphene,64,65 graphdiyne,66,67etc.) commonly show outstanding HER performance, which can be ascribed to their distinctive huge surface area, chemical stability, and enhanced conductivity. However, carbon materials are mostly used as carriers, and there are relatively few metal-free carbon-based catalysts.

A large number of amorphous carbon-based catalysts compounded with metal materials are used to enhance HER and OER performance and stability. Amorphous carbon materials have more defects and dangling bonds than crystalline carbon, increasing the active sites and intrinsic activity for electrochemical reactions.68 Fan et al. prepared amorphous carbon-coated Co/Co9S8MoS2 and demonstrated excellent HER and OER performance and stability. The preparation process is shown in Fig. 6a. The carbon-coated Co/Co9S8MoS2 shows a low overpotential of 128 mV for HER and 325 mV for OER in 1 M KOH solution (Fig. 6b and c). The amorphous carbon layer protects Co from corrosion and also exhibits excellent stability.69 Amorphous metalorganic framework (aMOFs) is an OER catalyst material with large application potential. Amorphous metalorganic framework-dominated nanocomposites (aMOF-NC) were prepared using a simple method by Yu et al. The aMOF-NC possesses a coreshell nanorod@nanosheet architecture, including a Fe rich FeCo-aMOF core and a Co-rich FeCo-aMOF shell in the coreshell structured nanorod, and amorphous Co(OH)2 nanosheets as the outer layer (Fig. 6d). As shown in Fig. 6e and f, aMOF-NC possesses enhanced OER activity with an overpotential of 249 mV at 10 mA cm2. Amorphous framework, three-dimensional hierarchical structure, and abundant Fe and Co bimetallic centers contribute to the excellent OER activity of aMOF-NC.70 Amorphous metal-free carbon materials are also widely studied because of their effective cost. Peng et al. synthesized scalable N-doped giant carbon nanotubes (N-GCTs) with homogeneous thickness and length (Fig. 6g). In Fig. 6hj, N-GCTs has lower overpotential, faster kinetics, and smaller impedance than GCTs and MWCNTs.

4.2 Amorphous metal and alloy electrocatalysts

Benefitting from the favorable vacant or partial-occupied d-orbitals, metals and alloys exhibit high HER activity. Amorphous metals and alloys have been recently reported as a promising family for the HER electrocatalyst. Investigation of the amorphous metal and alloy on electrocatalysis has made many considerable achievements in recent years. For example, dozens of different amorphous metals and alloy nanosheets (NSs) with thickness less than 10 nm were facilely prepared by Li et al. (Fig. 8a).74 Meanwhile, the OER performance of these catalysts can be optimized by tuning the atomic composition. Approximately 7.2 nm thick amorphous Ir NSs with uniform distribution of each element are shown in Fig. 8b and c. Amorphous RhFe bimetallic NSs were also successfully obtained with a 7.6 nm thick nanosheet (Fig. 8d and e). Many other bimetallic and even trimetallic nanosheets with a homogeneous distribution of elements were also prepared, such as amorphous IrFe (IrNi, IrCo, IrRu) NSs, RhNi (RhCo, RhRu) NSs, RuNi (RuCo) NSs, and IrRhRu NSs. This general synthesis of amorphous bimetallic and even trimetallic nanosheets offers the possibility of excellent catalysts to produce hydrogen. Due to a large number of samples, the researchers tested the OER performance of amorphous Ir NSs, demonstrating excellent activity and fast reaction kinetics (Fig. 8f and g).

Apart from amorphous metal materials, many amorphous alloy materials are used to improve their electrocatalytic performance. A NiRu@NC nanohybrid with low Ru-content was fabricated by Wang's group. The Ru-doping and pyrolysis of a Ni-based MOF resulted in carbon-wrapped alloy particles (Fig. 9a).75 Benefiting from the doped structure and surface effect, the NiRu@NC presents a high HER catalytic activity in a wide pH range. Meanwhile, the optimized S-2 sample delivered a comparable HER activity with commercialized Pt/C catalysts under both acidic and alkaline conditions. The optimal overpotentials to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm2 were only 50 mV and 32 mV with Tafel slopes of 36 and 64 mV dec1 in 0.5 M H2SO4 and 1.0 M KOH, respectively (Fig. 9bi).

In addition, L. Mihailov and colleagues studied the electrocatalytic HER activity of nanocrystalline ZrNi amorphous alloys. They found that nanocrystalline alloys manifest high catalytic performance over amorphous and crystalline counterparts due to the large density of defects and infirm hydrogen adsorption.76 Later, Mller et al. studied Fe-based amorphous melt-spun alloys (Fe82B18, Fe80Si10B10, and Fe60Co20Si10B10) for cathode materials via changing the metal-metalloid configuration, in which the Co contain sample demonstrated high HER activity.77 Besides, in recent years, increasing attention has been drawn on S-contained amorphous alloys for HER catalyst. Xu and co-workers analyzed the sulfur-containing amorphous PdCuS materials, and the catalyst demonstrates a small overpotential of 58 mV with a Tafel slope of 35 mV dec1. The efficiency improvement was ascribed to the presence of S22 and apical S2 species in the amorphous phase.78 Afterward, an effort has been put into studying the effectiveness between amorphous-crystalline interface alloys for HER catalyst. In this sector, the synergistic effect of crystalline Ni(Cu) alloy and amorphous NiFeP-derivatives were analyzed for the superior HER catalyst. The HER enhancement of Ni(Cu)@NiFeP/NM could be explained by the synergistic effect expressed by 54 times increment of ECSA and a large amount of exposed active sites.79 To reduce the utilization of precious metals, transition metals and nonmetallic elements are used as alloys. In this scenario, a nanoporous amorphous Pd10Fe70P5C17 catalyst was tested for HER and exhibited high activity and excellent stability. In amorphous Pd10Fe70P5C17, more coordination defects around Pd atoms result in a higher concentration of active sites, which produces improved HER. Besides, the amorphous structure is still reserved after the long-term operation.80

Moreover, Shi et al. demonstrated the preparation of PdCu amorphous nanocluster on reduced graphene oxide (Pd0.2Cu0.8/rGO) for highly efficient NRR electrolysis (Fig. 10).81 In Fig. 10bd, the Pd0.2Cu0.8/rGO has a high ammonia yield, high Faraday efficiency, and long-lasting stability at various potentials. Very lately, it has been found that an organic ligand (1,3,4-thiadiazole-2,5-dithiol, namely bismuthiol I) can induce the face-centered cubic (FCC) Pd NPs phase to amorphous phase without altering the integrity.82 In oleylamine solutions, bismuthiol I induced an amorphization of the outer atoms of FCC Pd NPs at an early stage, leading to a lattice expansion of the inner atoms and the transformation of Pd NPs into an amorphous phase. For HER, the Pd NPs-bis-24 h sample demonstrated lower overpotential (below 60 mV) and smaller Tafel slope (30 mV dec1). Based on a series of characterization analyses, it is revealed that the increased bond length in the amorphous structure of Pd weakens the interaction between Pd and hydrogen, thus improving the HER activity and stability. This work provides a facile and effective method for preparing amorphous Pd nanomaterials, which might also be applicable in the preparation of amorphized alloy catalysts.

4.3 Amorphous oxide and hydroxide electrocatalysts

Oxides and hydroxides are the most widely researched compounds in electrocatalytic OER, and a small part of them are used to study HER, ORR, and NRR. Amorphous oxides and hydroxides have been contributed considerably in this research area, in which the bifunctional oxygen catalysts for zincair batteries (ZABs) are recently under the spotlight.

Amorphous oxide as an oxygen electrode in ZABs has a lower overpotential and can be assembled to obtain a battery with a higher energy density. As shown in Fig. 11ac, the freestanding NiCo2O4@NiCoFe-OH@carbon cloth (CC) electrode containing an amorphous trimetallic hydroxide component (NiCoFe-OH) exhibited excellent bifunctional activities for both OER and ORR processes. The potential difference between these two reactions is only 695 mV, thereby giving high energy efficiency and volumetric energy density (38.1 mW h cm3) in flexible ZABs.83 For Co/Co3O4@NHCS used only as an ORR electrocatalyst, amorphous Co3O4 as a shell could effectively prevent the electrochemical oxidation/dissolution of metal Co core in strongly alkaline solution (Fig. 11d and e). It could also create an abundant interface that provides sufficient electron supply and reaction sites and thus make it promising for high efficiency of electron transfer. As shown in Fig. 11f, Co3O4@NHCS exhibits a half-wave potential of 0.82 V, and there was merely a negative shift (3 mV) of E1/2 for the Co/Co3O4@NHCS catalyst after 3500 potential cycles.84 In addition, iridium-based oxides are considered to be the most promising OER electrocatalysts. The amorphous IrOx nanosheets prepared by Yamachi et al. exhibited high catalytic activity and stability under both acidic and basic conditions and were closer to commercial OER electrocatalysts.85

Large quantities of amorphous oxides and hydroxides have been studied for designing and fabricating highly efficient and stable OER electrocatalysts. Diversified OER catalysts have been intensively investigated, including precious metals (e.g., Ir and Ru) and oxides,99,100 transition metal (e.g., Fe, Co, Ni, Mo, and V) oxides, layer hydroxides,101104etc. Most OER catalysts for amorphous oxide hydroxides use strategies such as metal doping, anion doping, heterogeneous interface formation with other materials, or preparation of polymetallic oxide hydroxides to improve their activity and stability. The metal doping strategy can change the charge distribution of the material itself, introduce new active sites, and change the performance of the catalyst. Nam et al. prepared Zn-doped NiFeOxHy with excellent activity and stability by a two-step method of chemical bath deposition followed by electrochemical activation. As shown in Fig. 12eg, the improvement with Zn-NiFeOxHy is due to the Zn2+ dopant, which is a strong Lewis acid and can modulate the electronic structure of the NiFeOxHy matrix for more moderate intermediate binding, thereby resulting in the stabilization of the OER-active phase.38 Metal doping can improve catalyst performance. By interfacial engineering of composite materials and metals, doping works together to not only improve the charge transfer of materials, but also increase the active site of the interface, creating a path for a more efficient OER catalyst. The ultra-fine amorphous FeOOH film prepared by Liu et al. was electrodeposited on a V-doped NiS NWs with nickel foam as substrate (eFe/NiVS/NF), exposing more active sites and showing strong OER activity.105 A variety of catalyst enhancement strategies applied to synthetic catalysts can produce more excellent electrocatalysts for various electrocatalyst reactions.

4.4 Amorphous sulfide electrocatalysts

Amorphous metal sulfides have been used to study OER, HER, NRR, and other electrocatalytic reactions due to their high catalytic activity, abundant defects, and strong activity. First, amorphous sulfides exhibited robust HER catalytic activity and are competent with precious benchmark Pt catalysts. In this sulfide composition, MoSx (here x = 2, 3) has been studied widely as an HER catalyst that showed superior activity than other non-noble metals and is also comparable with Pt under certain conditions.

Benck et al. investigated the HER catalytic properties by wet chemical synthesis of amorphous MoS3 (Fig. 13a and b). It is interesting to note that the amorphous domain of MoS3 has reduced into a MoS2 amorphous structure during the catalytic process.106 There has been a strong debate about the real active sites in amorphous MoSx. In situ and ex situ sophisticated instrumental studies proved that sulfur atoms were the active site for the HER process, and also the concentration of S22 ligands directly correlate with HER performance.108 In addition to MoSx, RuSx, NiSx, CoSx,109etc., the amorphous sulfides obtained by composite or doping were also used to show excellent HER activity. For example, in the sulfide-based compound, another type of material such as CoNiSx had its HER behavior studied. As shown in Fig. 13c, CoNiSx/NF-25 was synthesized by the sulfurization of NF-supported bimetallic CoNi carbonate hydroxide hydrate precursor (CoNiCHH/NF). In Fig. 13df, CoNiSx/NF-25 shows an overpotential of 123 mV along with a Tafel slope of 89 mV dec1, and also demonstrates a higherdouble layer capacitance (Cdl) of 132 mF cm2.107 The MOF-derived materials have shown high catalytic activity for HER. For example, MOF (ZIF-67) converted amorphous CoSx was synthesized by the facile electrochemical method.109

4.5 Amorphous boride electrocatalysts

Amorphization engineering is deemed as an operational methodology to modify the structure of catalysts to achieve propitious electrochemical properties. Because the amorphous structure occupies plentiful unsaturated coordination sites and surface-exposed defects, it is able to act as active sites to advocate the electrocatalytic reaction energetics and kinetics.113 Lately, transition-metal borides (TMBs) are deemed to serve as excellent electrocatalysts, which can indeed replace noble metal catalysts.114,115 The boride family mostly includes mono-metal borides, poly-metal borides (bimetallic borides, trimetallic borides), and metal boride composites.116 To verify the supremacy of the boride family in electrocatalysis, a range of amorphous CoMB ternary metal borides (M = Fe, Ni, Cu, Mo, Mn, W, or Cr) were studied for HER by Fernandes et al.117 They reported that, apart from CoCrB, all other variations displayed better HER activity than bare CoB. In Fig. 15ah, Co-xMB samples show diffused rings, confirming their amorphous nature, whereas for Co3MoB, nano-sized crystalline domains are visible, and the lattice fringes (0.21 nm) correspond to the Co2B phase. Fig. 15il illustrates the electrocatalytic performances of the samples. Among all, the CoMoB catalyst presented the lowest overpotential of 95 mV in a neutral electrolyte and 67 mV in an electrolyte with a pH of 14 to reach 10 mA cm2. The explanations for the facilitated electrocatalytic rate, caused by the presence of second metal in CoB, were examined by reflecting on factors like surface area, turn-over frequency (TOF), surface composition and chemical states, and charge-transfer resistance.117 The work presented harmonized experimental results and computational calculations to recognize the appropriate sites for H-adsorption and decide their free energies. Indeed, the crucial industrial possibility of amorphous boride-based catalysts can be confirmed through the examples and results.

Among all the metal-based borides, Co- and Ni-based borides particularly hold an unusual dominance and have been widely studied. The B-included electrocatalysts were reported to function as a stabilizer to prevent the crystalline metallic core from being grown and prevent oxidation.118 For instance, CoNiB ternary boride deposited on NF through electroless plating method displayed good stability in alkaline solutions, and an excellent electrocatalytic activity was delivered on the subject of both OER and HER processes.118 Doping small elements of iron or nickel to dicobalt boride is yet another usually established strategy. For instance, S. Klemenz et al. tried the incorporation of iron into Co2B.119 Interestingly, such extra addition of elements causes a rearrangement in the electronic contribution, which leads to enhanced OER activity. In contrast to conventional high-temperature synthetic routes for boride materials, they introduced a low-temperature solution, which could yield highly reactive powders. Following this work, Schuch et al. tried the integration of nickel into nanoscale Co2B to achieve an enhanced activity and stability in alkaline electrolytes.120 The addition of such a small amount of Ni can increase the electrochemical activity, which is independent of the different surface areas. Combined with the discussed work, an electrocatalyst of bimetallic FeNi boride (FeNiB NSs) on NF was also reported, which exhibited a higher OER activity with a low overpotential of 237 mV as well as fast kinetics (Tafel slope of 38 mV dec1).121 The electrode operating stability was explored and ascribed to the covalent character of metal boride. Similarly, a sequence of amorphous NiFeB NPs with controllable Fe/Ni atomic ratios was obtained through a facile chemical-reduction route. Interestingly, the formation of a borate-enriched surface layer on NiFeOOH NPs played a critical role in OER performance. The presence of borate in electrolyte facilitated the proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) process and moreover gifted catalysts with the capability to self-assemble, encouraging the OER activity.122

Other than metallic elements, non-metallic elements were also reported in tailoring the chemical composition of metal borides. For example, metal borides doped with hetero P atoms were used as a facile protocol to design OER/HER bifunctional catalysts to result in the synergic effects.123 Similarly, amorphous N-doped cobalt borophosphate catalyst synthesized via calcination of zeolite-type cobalt borophosphate precursor with NH3 molecule was reported, in which the N-dopant accelerated the electron transfer by modifying the electronic structure of this borophosphate. Additionally, the amorphous phase along with zeolite-like porous structures increased the active site density and advanced the electron and mass transfer.124 Amorphous metal borides are materials that can spontaneously oxidize while exposed to air and water, which indeed lead to a surface intermediate layer of oxides/hydroxides.125 Noble metal incorporation stands next in this line. Pd-Decoration can modify the surface of amorphous NiB/C particles via a simple chemical replacement method (Pd@NiB/C) that results in improved oxygen reduction reaction performance. An improved electrocatalytic performance was observed in 1.5 wt% Pd@NiB/C, which was as good as that of commercially available 40 wt% Pd/C or Pt/C catalysts. 4.6 Amorphous phosphide electrocatalysts

Unlike the oxides and hydroxides, which usually exhibit semiconducting or insulating behavior, amorphous metal phosphides are another alternative electrocatalyst due to their high conductivity.139,140 The theoretical DFT studies also support the strength of phosphate-based catalysts in electrocatalysis.141 For example, Hu et al. have reported an amorphous metallic NiFeP (a-NiFeP) OER catalyst, which presented high catalytic performance either in acidic or alkaline media. Specifically, a-NiFeP represents a perfect combination of high macroscopic conductivity and surface activity: a-NiFeP delivered a low overpotential of 219 mV under alkaline conditions, accompanied by a low Tafel slope of 32 mV dec1. Meanwhile, an overpotential of 540 mV is attained in an acidic electrolyte.142 Besides, Huang et al. have prepared amorphous bimetallic phosphides (a-CoMoPx/CF) with effective catalytic ability for water-splitting. Porous Co-MOF/CF and defective CoMoO4/CF were used as structure-directing templates to form a-CoMoPx/CF with rich defects and large voids. Thus, it can provide a superior mass transfer capacity and fully exposed atoms, increasing the electrochemically active surface area. The a-CoMoPx/CF shows high HER activity, displaying an ultra-low overpotential of 59 mV under alkaline conditions. When it was used as an overall water splitting catalyst, a low voltage of 1.581 V was operated to achieve 10 mA cm2, and a stable operation at 100 mA cm2 was done for more than 100 h.143

An interesting transformation of NiFe double hydroxide into metallic NiFeP was reported through PH3 plasma treatment.103 Therein, as soon as the conversion happened into NiFeP from NiFe double hydroxide, the material exhibited distinguishable OER functioning mainly because of porous and amorphous structures. Another such experimental proof where it validates the amorphous nature of the material outperforming the crystalline counterpart was done by Beltrn-Suito et al.144 From both the electronic properties and surface characteristics, the amorphous CoP here presented superior performance compared to the crystalline CoP with NF or fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) as substrates. Throughout OER, rigorous oxidation of CoP to Co-oxy(hydroxides)/oxides with losing P atoms was testified as fundamental to upsurge the concentration of CoOx active sites. 5. Summary and outlook

Electrocatalysis is the green process to convert renewable energy such as wind, solar, etc. As an important family of eletrocatalysts, tremendous and continuous efforts have been devoted to studying the electrocatalytic properties ofamorphous materials. In this review, we summarized the basic aspects of the amorphous phase in different types of electrocatalysis. We also have highlighted the amorphous and amorphous-crystalline interface electrocatalysis enhancing their performance in various electrochemical reactions, including HER, OER, total water-splitting, NRR, and ORR. Table 1 compares the performances of various amorphous electrocatalysts in different reactions. In recent years, many breakthrough research studies have been made from amorphous electrocatalysts. However, the existing research is still in the early stages, especially ORR and NRR. There are more opportunities available in this field, but challenges are also associated with it. The primary problem of amorphous catalysis is studying the intrinsic properties because in the amorphous phase, we cannot use existing techniques like diffractometer, electron microscopy, and Raman spectroscopy, which analyze the properties of crystalline rather than amorphous materials. Without such instruments, we could not understand the reaction mechanisms and efficiency improvement. The second issue is the lack of preparation techniques for amorphous materials. Innovative synthesis techniques are highly recommended to prepare a series of amorphous materials. Presently available methods are used for the preparation of crystalline materials frequently. Last but not the least problem is electrocatalytic performance analysis. Few reports have shown the use of operando measurements to investigate the catalyst performance. Most of the reports used conventional instruments to measure and analyze catalyst efficiency. We believe that the upcoming researchers can overcome the problem with suitable, scalable, and novel strategies to design state-of-the-art electrocatalysts (Fig. 21).
