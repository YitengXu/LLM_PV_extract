Abstract

We present a systematic comparative density functional theoryâ€“density functional tight binding study of multiple derivatives of C60 and C70 with different addends, in molecular as well as solid state. In particular, effects due to fullerene size, type and number of addends, and of crystallinity on band structure, charge transport, and optical properties are investigated. These are important, in particular, for rational selection of fullerene derivatives as acceptor and electron transport layers in organic as well as planar inverted perovskite solar cells. We find that by the choice of type and number of addends, one can modulate the LUMO within 0.4 eV. Changes in the HOMO can reach 0.7 eV. Substituting C70 for C60 results in destabilization of the HOMO by about 0.1 eV for indene and quinodimethane addends and by a less significant amount for PCBM addends. The effect of C70â€“C60 substitution on the LUMO is of similar magnitude. A more significant change in HOMOâ€“LUMO energy is seen for the aryl addends. On the other hand, all C70 based molecules have strong visible absorption. For most addends, the crystal packing leads to a stabilization of both the LUMO and HOMO by about âˆ¼0.2 and âˆ¼0.1 eV, respectively, vs. single molecules. When using bis-addends, it is also possible to enhance the visible absorption. Electron and hole transport rates are computed to vary vastly depending on the addends chosen; specifically, we compute that indene and quimodimethane addends can enhance charge transport rates while the aryl addend is predicted to result in substantially smaller mobilities of electrons and holes, vs. PC60BM. Furthermore, the â€“CH2 and bisaddend addition can significantly enhance the charge transfer rates for the PCBM addend.

Introduction

Advanced types of solar cells, including all-organic solar cells and perovskite-based solar cells, hold the promise of efficient and economic solar-to-electrical energy conversion and are likely to take a significant part in the future solar energy mix. Organic solar cells are a potentially cost-effective, scalable, and lightweight alternative to solid semiconductor-based solar cells.1â€“3 Organic (usually of BHJ, bulk heterojunction type) solar cells (OSCs) utilize polymeric or molecular organic chromophores and usually fullerene-based acceptors.4â€“7 Energy conversion efficiencies (Î·) exceeding 10% have been realized8,9 with these solar cells. New materials are needed to improve the efficiency further, as the efficiency is still lower than that of silicon cells or dye-sensitized cells.10 In these types of OSCs, optimal energy level matching and charge separation characteristics are the key requirements for the donorâ€“acceptor pair. The acceptor material plays a critical role of inducing charge separation and efficient conduction of electrons from the donorâ€“acceptor interface to the negative electrode.11 The electron transfer rate is strongly dependent on the difference Î”G between the energy of the excited state of the donor and the LUMO (lowest unoccupied molecular orbital) of the acceptor and would typically increase with lower acceptor LUMO, as follows from the Marcus equation,12

On the other hand, the open circuit voltage (VOC) decreases with the decrease of the acceptor LUMO level. Therefore, it is important to control the energy level of the acceptor material to simultaneously achieve a high charge separation rate and high VOC. The acceptor LUMO level depends not only on the molecular structure of the acceptor molecule but also on crystal packing. The charge mobility in the acceptor also strongly depends on the crystal packing (via J) as well as on the molecular structure (via Î”G and Î»). PCBM ([6,6]-phenyl C61/71-butyric acid methyl ester, hereafter referred to also as PC61BM), and other fullerene derivatives are the most widely used acceptor compounds due to their high electron affinity and effective charge transport properties.14 We will also use notation PC60/70BM to highlight the fullerene core used.

The perovskite solar cells, mostly based on methylammonium lead iodide (CH3NH3PbI3) as the chromophore and donor, have achieved efficiencies exceeding 20% in the sensitized configuration (i.e. with TiO2 as an acceptor).15,16 The potential widespread use of the perovskite solar cells depends on advances in many directions including solvent engineering and interface engineering.17 In particular, planar inverted perovskite cells (the so-called OPV-type perovskite solar cells) are advantageous, as their manufacturing does not require high-temperature processes and they are suited for applications where flexibility is required.18,19 Cells made using solution-based hot-casting techniques have now achieved efficiencies of about 18% with the most commonly used fullerene-based acceptor PCBM.20 Huang's group demonstrated that the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of planar heterojunction photovoltaic cells can be increased up to 19.4% by reducing the energy disorder in the fullerene layer.21 More efficient perovskite solar cells are expected in the near future as the PCE has already reached up to 22% after a few years of academic research, and planar perovskite cells could have efficiencies comparable to the sensitized configuration cells. This type of solar cell benefits from the use of an organic electron transport layer (ETL).

Different fullerene derivatives are also successfully used as acceptors/electron transport layers in planar perovskite solar cells. There are by now multiple reports that other fullerene derivatives can achieve higher performance than PCBM under the same conditions and in a way dependent on the specific perovskite.22,23 For example, a new fullerene derivative, bis-4-propylpentyl-[6,6]-methanofullerene (NCBA), using 4-nonanone as a building block has been synthesized.24 Wen et al. have demonstrated an indene-C60-bis adduct based fullerene/perovskite planar heterojunction hydride solar cell.25 Ela and coworkers have recently demonstrated a new fullerene derivative (benzoic acid fullerene bis adducts, BAFB) based perovskite solar cell.26 Imahori and coworkers have used soluble C60-9-methylanthracene mono-adduct (C60(9MA)) as a thermal precursor to the C60 ESL, which resulted in 15.0% PCE in a planar perovskite solar cell.27 Liu et al. have investigated a fluoroalkyl-substituted fullerene/perovskite solar cell with a decent PCE of 15.4%.28 Also, a 19.5% PCE in a perovskite cell has been achieved by using a cross-linkable silane-functionalized molecule with the doped fullerene as an electron transport layer.29 In particular, more environmentally friendly (with less or no Pb content) perovskites using different organic cations or halides can profit from the use of different fullerene derivatives as the ETL, in particular, because their different CBM (conduction band minimum) can achieve an optimal balance between VOC and Ï‰.30â€“37

The use of non-PCBM fullerene derivatives has also been shown to lead to higher efficiencies of BHJ cells, depending on the donor.24 Given the virtually inexhaustible number of possible donors, with different LUMO energies and reorganization energies, it is clear that one needs to be able to select rationally the most appropriate fullerene material. The use of different fullerene derivatives can also enhance visible light absorption of the acceptor which leads to additional photocurrent.38â€“41 Clearly, engineering an appropriate fullerene-based material for a given application (either a given donor of a BHJ cell or a given perovskite) is important to maximize the efficiency and fully utilize the advantages of a given donor material and chromophore.

Unlike other aspects of the BHJ and the OPV-type perovskite solar cells that have been widely investigated, the different fullerene-based acceptor compounds have not been. It is therefore clear that a systematic study of fullerene derivatives will be beneficial for the fullerene based solar cell technologies. Therefore, to enable rational design of optimal fullerene derivatives for applications in all-organic BHJ solar cells as well as in OPV-type/inverted perovskite cells, a clear understanding of the trends in the properties of various fullerene derivatives is needed. This calls for a comparative study of multiple derivatives that differ by core size (e.g. PC61BM vs. PC71BM) and the functional groups (i.e. replacing â€œP-BMâ€ with aryl (Ar), indene (IND), quinodimethane (QM) etc.). As mentioned above, some progress on the experimental side has been achieved.42â€“45 However, to disambiguate from circumstantial effects due to impurities in the materials and experimental conditions and to rationalize experimental trends (in Î·, VOC, JSC, FF etc.) with different fullerene derivatives, a comparative ab initio computational study is required. A limited number of computational studies have been reported on different fullerene based acceptor materials; they are also dominated by studies of [6,6]-phenyl C61/71-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) and its derivatives.46â€“49 While separate studies computing the properties of specific other fullerene derivatives exist, these are often limited to isolated molecules and did not consider effects due to the crystal structure or even the effects of Î”G50 as was often done when modelling other organic materials as well.51â€“56 These are expected to have important effects on energy level matching and charge transport. In particular, electron and hole transport rates critically depend on mutual orientations of molecules in the crystal as well as on Î”G. Therefore, it is important to compare different fullerene materials to estimate the effect on fullerene acceptors due to the change in addends, size, and crystallinity. Such a comparison has to be done at the same level of theory and with the same computational parameters as practical ab initio approaches (i.e. DFT) are only semi-quantitative.

Therefore, in this manuscript, we present a comparative ab initio computational study of multiple fullerene derivatives (shown in Fig. 1) that considers the molecular structure, frontier orbital energy levels, redox potentials and bandgap, optical properties, reorganization energies and coupling elements that determine charge mobility. Charge transfer rates for different pathways in realistic crystal structures are computed. Trends in properties with the size of the fullerene and with organic addends are established. We compute single-molecule properties by the DFT method and the effects due to crystallinity by the Density Functional Tight Binding (DFTB) method. Therefore, we also present a comparison between DFT and DFTB.

Methods

The DFT calculations were performed using Gaussian0957 for the optimization, estimation of the frontier orbital energies, and absorption spectra of individual molecules and for dimers (for charge transport calculations). The B3LYP functional58 with the 6-31+g(d,p) basis set was used for these calculations. The redox potentials were computed with the Ï‰B97XD functional,59 as it was shown that a large fraction of exact exchange is needed to accurately compute them and Ï‰B97XD was suggested to be accurate for such calculations.60â€“62 The partial density of states (PDOS) was extracted by the GaussSum63 program, from the output of the Gaussian calculations. The reorganization energies were also computed for all the molecules with the same level of theory. For comparison with DFTB calculations, the PBE functional64 was also used. All molecular calculations were done in a vacuum. The absorptivity Îµ in L molâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1 was calculated from oscillator strengths f and excitation energies of transitions Eexc as with the HWHM (half width half maximum) broadening Ïƒ = 0.25 eV.57

The oxidation (reduction) potentials were computed as Eox(red) = E+(âˆ’) âˆ’ E0, where E+, Eâˆ’, and E0 are the energies of the cation, anion and neutral molecules.62 The change in Î”G, which is omitted in some studies52â€“56 needed for charge transfer rate calculation by the Marcus equation, was estimated from the difference in the redox potentials of the neutral and charged systems: Î”G = E+(âˆ’)ox(red) âˆ’ E0ox(red), where, E+(âˆ’)ox(red) and E0ox(red) are the oxidation (reduction) potential of ionic and neutral molecules. We used the redox potentials for the calculation of Î”G because they are more reliable than HOMO (highest occupied molecular orbitals) and LUMO values; specifically, they do not suffer from the changes in reference energy between neutral and charged species by which HOMO and LUMO levels are affected.

We also investigated both the molecular and the crystalline structures of all the fullerene derivatives by the self-consistent charge density functional tight binding scheme (SCC-DFTB) using the DFTB+ code.65,66 We used the parameter set (Slaterâ€“Koster files) 3ob-3-167 and the DFTB368 approximation, which was shown to be accurate for predicting the interactions in organic molecules. For silicon derivative of fullerenes, the matsci-0-369 parameter set was utilized (as Si parameters are missing from 3ob-3-1). The relative SCC tolerance was set to 1 Ã— 10âˆ’5 for both the molecular and crystalline structure optimization. Dispersion interactions were accounted for by using the UFF force field.70

The reorganization energy was calculated following the equation , where is ion energy at the geometry of the neutral molecule, E+(âˆ’) is ion energy at its optimized geometry, E* is the energy of the neutral molecule at ion's geometry, and E is the energy of the neutral molecule at its optimized geometry. The reorganization energy computation by the Gaussian (B3LYP) and the DFTB code followed the same route.

We took the experimentally available crystal structures of PC60BM71 and C60CH2IND72 as the reference or initial guess and made the crystal structures for other molecules (both C60 and C70 derivatives) by placing optimized single molecules into a similar crystal structure as PCBM and CH2IND. Molecular dynamics (MD) was performed in DFTB+ to help identify stable structures and to confirm the stability of the crystals. This was possible due to low energy barriers for orientational motion; indeed, molecules were found to easily reorient themselves during MD. After attaining an equilibrium crystal structure, we optimize the structure and compare the final energy with its initial (before MD run) energy to identify the lowest energy structure. Therefore, all the calculations were performed with the lowest energy structures (all the crystal structure along with the lattice constants are reported in Section 8 of the ESIâ€ ). The Noseâ€“Hoover thermostat73â€“75 and the Berendsen barostat76 are used with a coupling strength of 3000 cmâˆ’1 and a timescale of 100 femtoseconds, respectively. The time step for the integration of the equations of motion was 1 fs and the length of the run was at least 10 ps. The Brillouin zone was sampled with at least one k point per 30 Ã…âˆ’1. Thus, different values of k-points were assigned for different cell vectors.

Finally, the charge transfer rate was calculated using the Marcus equation (1). We computed the transfer integral by the DFT/DIPRO approach included in the VOTCA-CTP module.77,78 The dimers forming the donorâ€“acceptor pair in the charge transfer (hopping) process were cut out from the optimized crystal geometry. As the crystals were computed with DFTB (parameterized to PBE DFT), the PBE functional was used to compute the orbitals of monomers and dimers, to minimize strain caused by using one method on a (slightly different) structure computed with another. These orbital energies and the expansion coefficients were used to estimate the transfer integral values obtained by the VOTCA-CTP code.

Absorption spectra were computed using TD-DFT79,80 (in Gaussian 09) considering 36 lowest singletâ€“singlet transitions.

Results

Molecules studied

We have investigated fullerene derivatives with both C60 and C70 cores. Different types of addends are studied, such as indene (IND), quinodimethane (QM), phenyl-butyric acid methyl ester (P-BM), and aryl (Ar). On top of that, the effect on the molecular properties of addition of an extra â€“CH2 group and bis-addends was also considered. It was shown72,81 that the dihydromethano group improves the performance of bulk heterojunction organic solar cells. All the addends with a fullerene C60 core are represented in Fig. 1. The same addends with a C70 core are also considered. The first addend in Fig. 1 is indene (IND), synthesized by Li and co-workers.82 They reported both the mono and the bis addends and showed that the bis addend can significantly improve the PCE compared to PCBM as an acceptor in a polymer solar cell (PSC).82 Furthermore, they optimized the PSC system by the donor acceptor weight ratio, solvent annealing and pre-thermal annealing to improve the efficiency.45 Later, Wen et al. demonstrated an improved PCE with the indene-C60-bisaddend (ICBA) for perovskite-fullerene planar heterojunction hydride solar cells. Other indene derivatives have also been reported as efficient acceptor materials for the perovskite based solar cell.23,83 The second type of addend is quinodimethane (QM). Ding's group synthesized both QM and thieno-o-quinodimethane based fullerene derivatives, including those with bis addends, and achieved an increased solar cell efficiency compared to PC60BM with P3HT as a donor.84 They further showed an enhanced efficiency by introducing a â€œâ€“CH2â€ group along with the QM addend.85 They also reported the efficiency of C70QM and C70-bis-OQM in a fullerene/P3HT solar cell.86 The next addend is the widely used fullerene derivative i.e. the phenyl-butyric acid methyl ester addend with both the C60 (PC60BM) and C70 (PC70BM) fullerenes. Many research groups have used PCBM as the electron transfer layer for organic and/or perovskite based solar cells.87â€“90 Several other applications are also reported in the literature for PCBM based systems.91â€“93 You et al. reported high efficiency for a fluorinated polymer blended with PC60BM in a solar cell.94 Cheyns et al. also reported an increased efficiency due to the use of PC60BM with crosslinked 1,6-diazidohexane (DAZH).95 GrÃ¤tzel et al. demonstrated that along with the performance, the stability of the device can be further improved by using the bis-PCBM adduct.96 Also, the PC70BM was used as an efficient acceptor material. For example, Li et al. demonstrated triple-junction polymerâ€“PC70BM fullerene solar cells that achieved PCEs up to 9.6%.97 Kim et al. showed that the PCEs can be improved by using the PC70BMâ€“polymer composite.98 Liang and co-workers prepared a solar cell from the polymerâ€“PC70BM blend that also exhibited a high solar energy conversion efficiency of 5.6%.99 Other fullerene derivatives with aryl and silicon substituted aryl addends were also reported. Nakamura et al. synthesized and developed the aryl-fullerene derivatives as an efficient alternative to PCBM.100â€“102 We used a single isomer of each of the molecules as shown in Fig. 1. We restricted this work to one isomer because (i) it would be impractical in this work to consider multiple isomers, as we analyse a couple dozen molecules with several methods, (ii) fullerene derivatives can be synthesized regioselectively,103 and the isomers we consider here are those reported in experimental literature cited above. Our results therefore provide useful information with which to compare effects of addends in different fullerene molecules. The effects of isomerization, however, will require further studies.

Charge transfer rate

We calculate the charge transfer rate using the Marcus equation (eqn (1)). In order to compute the value of Ï‰, one has to calculate the key parameters in that equation, which are the driving force (Î”G), the reorganization energy (Î») and the transfer integral (J). The Î» and the Î”G are strongly related to the molecular properties that are computed with isolated molecules. In contrast, the transfer integral (J) is related to the crystallinity of the material, i.e. J depends on mutual orientation of the monomers within the crystal. DFTB is used to compute all the crystal structures. We first perform benchmarking of DFTB vs. DFT for all isolated molecules.

There is a limited number of rate calculations in the literature, for specific fullerene derivatives. For example, Arntsen et al.116 estimated an electron transfer rate (via LUMO) of 5.88 Ã— 1012 sâˆ’1 for the closest dimers of PC60BM using the Marcus equation, which is very close to our value of 5.87 Ã— 1012 sâˆ’1. This is why it is important to have a truly comparative set of values for different molecules computed with the same approximations and computational parameters, as is done here.

Conclusions

Rational selection of fullerene derivatives as acceptor and electron transport layers in organic and planar perovskite solar cells is very important to maximize power conversion efficiency. Therefore, it is essential to have a truly comparative picture of properties that can alter the PCE for all the major fullerene based solar cells. This Density Functional Theoryâ€“Density Functional Tight Binding based systematic and comparative study considered several C60 and C70 derivatives with different sets of addends in the molecular as well as solid state.

Our study indicates that the chemical nature and the number of addends as well as fullerene core size can significantly affect the molecular properties key for solar cell functionality. Along with the molecular properties such as HOMO and LUMO levels and optical properties, the effect of crystallinity on the band structure and charge transport are also investigated.

We have shown that the choice of addends and of the core can change the HOMO, LUMO and gap values by fractions of an eV and can have a significant impact on band alignment and light absorption properties. This is in spite of the fact that addend-centered electronic states are significantly above the LUMO and below the HOMO energies, while both frontier orbitals remain centered on the fullerene in all cases.

Visible absorption is computed to be weak with C60 derivatives and strong with C70 derivatives, as is well known. Here, we have shown that the extinction coefficient can be modulated by the choice of the addends and that with some addends, addend states significantly contribute to the absorption spectrum.

We have shown that effects of crystallinity on band alignment are important and can be effectively modeled with the DFTB method; their inclusion improves substantially theoretical estimates of the LUMO and thereby band alignment. Modeling of the crystal structure is also necessary to compute charge transfer rates, which was done here. The rate calculations showed that both electron and hole transfer rates can change by orders of magnitude depending on the choice of the addends.

The data on band alignment and charge transport rates presented here are expected to be directly useful for rationalizing the performance of devices using different fullerene derivatives and for rational selection of such materials.
