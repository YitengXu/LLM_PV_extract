Abstract

A facile and low-cost method for preparing a nickelâ€“cobalt phosphide/multilayer graphene (MLG) (NiCoP/MLG) composite was developed in this study using a new strategy. NiCoP/MLG was prepared through calcination of NiCo-benzoate layered double hydroxide (NiCo-B-LDH) to obtain NiCoO2/MLG first followed by phosphorization to obtain the composite of NiCoP/MLG. Here, NiCo-B-LDH served as a self-sacrificing template. The obtained porous flower-like NiCoP/MLG was tested as a material for supercapacitor electrodes, which exhibited excellent electrochemical performance in terms of specific capacitance (1419.6 F gâˆ’1 at 1 A gâˆ’1), durability at high current density and long-term operation with 91.7% capacitance retention after more than 3000 cycles at a current density of 5 A gâˆ’1. Moreover, the symmetrical supercapacitor based on NiCoP/MLG delivered a high energy density of 32.19 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 741.65 W kgâˆ’1. Therefore, the as-prepared NiCoP/MLG could serve as a high-performance supercapacitor electrode material.

1. Introduction

With the gradual increase of population and the rapid industrialization of developing countries, the non-renewable energy sources have been unable to meet the increasing global energy demand.1 Therefore, development of new energy sources and new technologies associated with energy conversion and storage is urgently needed.2 Supercapacitors are energy storage devices with a high power density and long life,3,4 which can be divided into electric double layer capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudo capacitors. The former are based on the adsorption of ions on active materials, and the latter work through rapid and reversible surface redox reactions.5â€“8 It is well known that electrodes made from pseudo capacitance materials such as transition metal oxides and conducting polymers usually show higher specific capacitance than those made of carbon materials with electrochemical double layer capacitance.

Transition metal phosphides (TMPs) have attracted particular attention due to their higher theoretical pseudo capacitance. Compared with the corresponding oxides, TMP has a non-metallic structure and good electrical conductivity, which give it good application prospects in the field of capacitors. Phosphide nanoparticles,9,10 nanowires11 and nanosheets12 have shown good performance as pseudo capacitance electrodes. However, due to their poor rate performance, low cyclic stability and insufficient electrochemical activity, the development of TMPs has been limited. Many research groups are currently making efforts to improve TMP performance. Dong13et al. reported that the electrochemical performance of a P-doped reduced graphene oxide/Niâ€“Co phosphide composite was better than that of TMP.

It is currently understood that the capacitive properties depend to a large extent on the morphology, porosity and crystallinity of the electrode material, and that a porous structure is especially beneficial for effective ion and electron transport. Another way to improve the electrochemical properties of materials is to introduce conductive phases, such as carbon, into active electrode materials. In the field of energy storage, during the last decade, carbon materials and their composites such as carbon nanotubes, carbon gels and graphene have become popular candidates. Recently, researchers have paid more attention to graphene because of its outstanding physical/chemical properties, which include high room temperature electron mobility, high theoretical specific surface area and good chemical stability.14 In addition, graphene has excellent electrochemical properties, such as a wide electrochemical potential window,15,16 low charge-transfer resistance and excellent electrochemical activity. Based on the physical and chemical properties of graphene and its huge potential, it has been widely used in many fields such as supercapacitors,17 transistors, sensors,18 conductive films,19 lithium ion batteries,20 catalysts,21 composites22,23 and solar cells24 in recent years. The scientific and commercial value of graphene is not limited to the original monolayer or single layer graphene, but also includes related two-dimensional materials that involve few-layer graphene (FLG) and multilayer graphene (MLG).25 The single-layer graphene is still the best suited for significant applications, but the synthesis process of monolayer graphene often involves some strong oxidizing reagents, and the cost of production remains expensive. Therefore, it is not conducive to the sustainable development of materials industry by saving both energy and the environment. So, the much lower cost MLG has attracted a huge amount of interest for electrochemical applications and, thus, MLG has become a promising material that may enhance the electrochemical performance of TMPs. Hence, our objective in this research was to improve the electrochemical performance of TMP by compositing it with MLG. Here, we designed and synthesized TMP/MLG composites by using layered double hydroxide (LDH) as a template for MLG.

Hydrotalcite-like compounds are a kind of anionic clay based on the layered structure of hydrotalcite, also known as LDH,26,27 whose ideal composition can be expressed as [M(II)1âˆ’xM(III)x(OH)2]x+(Ax/nnâˆ’)Â·mH2O,28 where M(II) is a divalent cation such as Mg, Fe, Ni, Co, etc., M(III) is a trivalent cation such as Co(III), Al, Fe(III), etc., and A is the anion, either inorganic or organic, which balances the positive charge of the layers through intercalation in the interlayers. Thus, LDH is composed of positively charged brucite-like layers similar to the structure of mineral brucite (Mg(OH)2),29 and negatively charged anions between layers to balance the positive charge. Due to the small interlayer space of LDH, the intercalated organic anions are confined and restricted between the layers by electrostatic attraction, which can effectively avoid a large amount of organic accumulation. After calcination at a certain temperature, we hypothesize that the organic anions between the layers can be decomposed, carbonized and graphitized to form MLG.

Our group's previous research studies have shown that heat preservation and structural/lattice orientation effects of the confinement space in LDHs can reduce the conventional temperature of graphitization.30,31 Herein, we further developed the above concept and designed a facile method to synthesize nickelâ€“cobalt phosphide/multilayer graphene composites (NiCoP/MLG) by intercalating benzoate anions into NiCo-LDH, followed by a calcination process. During the calcination process, the benzoate anions confined in the 2D galleries of LDH were converted into amorphous carbon while the layered structure of NiCo-LDH collapsed and this subsequently led to the in situ formation of NiCoO2 with MLG. Then, the composite material was phosphorylated at an appropriate temperature to obtain the final NiCoP/MLG composite product. The as-obtained 3D petal-like structure of the NiCoP/MLG composite is shown here to have an excellent specific capacitance and this material was successfully assembled into a symmetrical supercapacitor.

Raman scattering measurement was carried out on the residuum (MLG) remaining after acid etching and its Raman spectrum is shown in Fig. 2, which shows D and G peaks located at 1335 cmâˆ’1 and 1392 cmâˆ’1, respectively. The D band is associated with defects resulting from the vibration of sp3 carbon atoms, while the G band is ascribed to vibration of all sp2 carbon atoms within a two-dimensional graphitic hexagonal lattice.34 Specifically, the relative intensity ratio of the D band and G band (ID/IG) is a key parameter for evaluating the degree of graphitization of a carbon material.35,36 According to the relevant literature, the ID/IG ratio follows a two-stage evolution:37 (1) corresponding to low-defect graphene, (2) corresponding to high-defect graphene. Normally, the transition of ID/IG between stage 1 and stage 2 is about 3.38 For our materials, the ID/IG ratio of MLG is 0.85, which suggests that the defect degree of MLG is small. Due to lattice orientation/confinement effects, the intercalation of benzoate anions into layered double hydroxides (LDHs) can improve the graphitization of benzoate anions at relatively low temperature.30

The microstructure and morphology of the composites were characterized by SEM and TEM. Fig. 3a to d reveal the SEM images of the normal NiCo-LDH, NiCo-B-LDH, NiCoO2/MLG and NiCoP/MLG. 3.3. Electrochemical performance

The electrochemical properties of different samples in a three-electrode system were studied by cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD), cyclic stability and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements. It is well known that the value of the specific capacitance is proportional to the area enclosed by the CV curves. It can be seen from Fig. 6a that the integral area of NiCoP/MLG is larger than that of pure NiCoP, which demonstrates that the composite material has a higher electrochemical activity and specific capacitance, indicating that the synergistic effect of graphene and NiCoP can effectively improve the electrochemical performance of the materials. In addition, NiCoP nanoparticles without graphene are easily agglomerated leading to a reduced specific surface area and so they could not fully participate in the redox reaction.

In order to further study the electrochemical properties of the different electrode materials, GCD measurements were carried out under the current density of 1 A gâˆ’1 within the voltage range of 0â€“0.5 V (Fig. 6b). Each chargeâ€“discharge curve is nonlinear and has a platform transition region with an obvious slow change, which further confirms the pseudo capacitance behavior of NiCoP. The transition zone of the platform can be attributed to the charge accumulation at the electrode/electrolyte interface and the reversible redox reaction between the electrode surface and the interior. The discharge time of NiCoP/MLG is significantly longer than that of NiCoP at the same current density, indicating that NiCoP/MLG has larger specific capacitance values. By calculation, the specific capacitance values of NiCoP and NiCoP/MLG are 1105.4 F gâˆ’1 and 1419.6 F gâˆ’1 at 1 A gâˆ’1, respectively. These results are similar to the CV results discussed above. This is due to the synergistic effect of graphene and NiCoP. The existence of graphene can not only effectively prevent the agglomeration of NiCoP nanoparticles but also improve the electrical conductivity of the composites.

Cyclic voltammograms of NiCoP/MLG at different scan rates over a potential window of 0 V to 0.5 V are presented in Fig. 7a. A pair of redox peaks can be clearly observed in all CV curves at different scanning rates, which shows the pseudo capacitance of NiCoP. At a low scanning rate, the redox peaks are located near 0.22 V and 0.41 V. With increased scanning rate, the redox peaks were slightly shifted, but the shapes of the closed curves did not change much, indicating that the internal resistance and polarizability of the NiCoP/MLG composite electrode material are small and it has better rate performance at a higher scanning rate.

The rate capabilities of NiCoP/MLG and NiCoP were studied as a function of current density and specific capacitance. As shown in Fig. 7c, when the current density increases, the specific capacitance of the sample tends to decrease. In order to further understand the electrochemical properties, the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) spectra of the sample were recorded in the frequency range of 10âˆ’2â€“105 Hz. All Nyquist curves (Fig. 7d) exhibited two typical characteristics: a semicircle in the high and medium frequency range and an oblique line in the low frequency range. The semicircle represents the charge transfer resistance between the active material and the liquid electrolyte, which is related to the redox reaction while the semicircular radius is proportional to the charge transfer resistance. In addition, the intercept between the semicircle and the X-axis represents the internal resistance, including the contact resistance between the active material and the collector and the resistance of the active material itself. The equivalent series resistance value of NiCoP/MLG is about 0.56 Î©, which is smaller than that of pure NiCoP (0.64 Î©). The above result indicated that multilayer graphene promoted charge transport and improved the conductivity of the NiCoP/MLG composite, which provided an easier charge transfer process at the electrode/electrolyte interface. Cyclic stability is an important parameter of electrode materials, and it is one of the factors that determine whether electrode materials can be applied in practice. Fig. 7e reveals the results of 3000 GCD tests for NiCoP/MLG and NiCoP samples at the current density of 5 A gâˆ’1. It can be observed that NiCoP/MLG has a good cycling stability in which the retention rate of capacitance after 3000 cycles is 91.7%, while the normal NiCoP shows very poor decay to 74.6% after 3000 cycles. In addition, the Coulombic efficiency (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ) of NiCoP/MLG is approximately 100% during all cycles. The above stability can be attributed to its special petal porous structure and the synergistic effect between NiCoP and graphene, in which graphene not only increases the conductivity of the composite material, but also hinders the aggregation of NiCoP particles, and maintains the overall stability of the structure during charging and discharging by acting as a buffer layer.

With the increase of voltage window, the curve shape remains the same, indicating the good capacitive performance of the capacitor. Fig. 8b demonstrates the GCD plots for the fabricated supercapacitor at different current densities ranging from 1.0 A gâˆ’1 to 5.0 A gâˆ’1. When the current density was 1 A gâˆ’1, the specific capacitance of the device could reach 103 F gâˆ’1, which can be competitive with those of NiCoP-based systems reported in recent years (Table S2, ESIâ€ ). As can be seen from Fig. 8c, with increasing current density, the specific capacitance of the device decreased, which is due to the insufficient penetration and diffusion of electrolyte at high current density, as has been suggested previously by others.42 It is well-known that the energy density and power density are two key factors to measure the overall performance of supercapacitors and their relationship is shown in Fig. 8d. It can be seen from Fig. 8d that the energy density decreased with increasing power density, which could be due to blocking of the movement of electrolyte ions by diffusion at high current densities leading to incomplete reaction of the active material of the electrode. 4. Conclusions

In summary, we have demonstrated a facile and eco-friendly method for the preparation of a 3D NiCoP/MLG composite under mild conditions. The composite of nickelâ€“cobalt phosphide and multilayer graphene (NiCoP/MLG) was obtained by confinement of benzoate anions in the 2D galleries of NiCo-LDH, followed by a calcination process first at 500 Â°C and subsequent phosphorization at 350 Â°C. The benzoate intercalated LDH retained its porous petal-like structure after it was transformed to NiCoP/MLG by calcination and phosphorization, which was found to be beneficial for the transmission of electrolyte ions. The NiCoP/MLG composite as an active electrode material exhibited excellent electrochemical performance with a high specific capacitance of 1419.6 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1, and it also showed good rate capability and cycling stability. In addition, NiCoP/MLG was successfully assembled into a symmetrical supercapacitor with a high energy density of 32.19 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 741.65 W kgâˆ’1. The currently developed strategy of structural confinement of organic anions to prepare MLG using LDH as a template was found to be effective to prepare inorganic NiCoP/MLG composites and such a strategy is expected to be broadly useful in the field of energy storage.
