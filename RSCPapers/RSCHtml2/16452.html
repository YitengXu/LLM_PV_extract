Abstract

As a promising idea to design high-efficiency organic photovoltaics, singlet fission (SF) mechanism, i.e., generating two triplet excitons out of a single photon absorption, has recently come into the spotlight. Even though much effort has been devoted to this arena, accurately accounting for the SF process from the theoretical perspective has proven to be challenging. Herein, the SF energetics have thoroughly been investigated with the help of optimally tuned range-separated hybrid functionals (OT-RSHs) in both gas and solvent phases. Taking a series of experimentally known SF chromophores as working models, we have proposed and validated several variants of OT-RSH approximations for the reliable prediction of the energy levels which match the crucial criteria for the SF process, namely, the negative singlettriplet and triplettriplet energy gaps. We scrutinize the role of the OT-RSH ingredients, i.e., the underlying density functional approximations, short- and long-range exact-like exchange, as well as the range-separation parameter, for our purpose. The newly designed OT-RSHs outperform the standard RSHs and other related schemes such as screened-exchange approximations as well as other density functionals from different rungs for describing the SF energetics. More importantly, it is unveiled that although the OT-RSH coupled with the polarizable continuum model, OT-RSH-PCM, as well as the screened versions, OT-SRSHs, which account for the screening effect by the electron correlation through the scalar dielectric constant have some advantages over gas-phase computations using OT-RSHs, the energetics criteria of the SF process may not necessarily be satisfied. This in turn corroborates the idea of performing both the optimal tuning procedure and subsequent computations of the SF relevant energetics using OT-RSHs as a more reliable and affordable framework, at least for the present purpose. The applicability of the proposed models is also put into broader perspective, where they are used for the computational design of several chromophores as promising candidates prone to utilization in the SF-based materials. Hopefully, our recommended OT-RSHs can function as efficient models for both the theoretical modeling of SF chromophores and confirming the experimental observations in the field.

1. Introduction

The incidence of a photon on the chromophore layer of photovoltaic devices leads to the creation of one singlet exciton. This exciton may also diffuse to the chromophore interface, where due to the charge separation, a pair of free charge carriers can be formed. Because of the possibility of radiative and radiationless decay paths to the ground state, singlet excitons are usually short-lived and can lower the photoelectric conversion efficiency of photovoltaic devices. Nonetheless, with certain relations among the energies of the excited states of the chromophores, the singlet exciton may fission into two triplet excitons through a photophysical process called singlet fission (SF),16 resulting in twice the number of excitons with a longer life. Multiple exciton generation within the SF process provides a potential route to exceed the ShockleyQueisser efficiency limit in conventional photovoltaic cells (30%),7 where the photoelectric transformation efficiency of a single-junction photovoltaic device can increase up to 46%.8 Besides, the external quantum efficiency above 100% has also been predicted in the SF-based organic solar cells, suggesting promising applications of SF-based materials for achieving extraordinarily high efficiency.911

The first mention of SF dates back to more than half a century ago, when this process was used to explain fluorescence quenching in crystalline anthracene.1214 Since then, the SF process has been further characterized in other crystals and polymers.1527 However, inspired by the work of Hanna and Nozik,8 where SF was proposed as a possible mechanism to enhance the efficiency of photoelectric conversion, the interests toward the SF idea as one of the most promising multiexciton generation mechanisms for solar energy conversion have been strongly revitalized in the recent years.2850Scheme 1 shows the key pathways and the corresponding electronic rearrangements in the SF process.

In general, the SF process requires two or more chromophores to reside in close proximity and to satisfy certain energetic constraints. One molecule in the ground state (S0) is excited to its first singlet excited state by the absorption of high-energy photons (h). The resulting molecule in the singlet excited state (S1) interacts with a nearby ground state molecule, leading to an excimer where one molecule is in its excited state and another is in its ground state. Herein, three pathways can come into play. As one possible route, direct internal conversion from the singlet exciton to a bound triplettriplet biexciton, 1(TT), as a two-electron process without including charge transfer states, can be noted. The latter biexciton is a pair of triplet states that are coupled to form an overall singlet and its generation can be occur within a time scale as short as a subpicosecond. In turn, this process outcompetes the S1 radiative and radiationless decays, which usually occurs on the nanosecond or longer timescales; therefore, molecules with ultrafast S1 to S0 decay cannot be considered as appropriate SF chromophores.

There are still two other indirect ways towards 1(TT). One is through the charge transfer-mediated path, where the charge transfer states as the virtual states assist the formation of 1(TT) in a superexchange mechanism. Another route is via the excimer-mediated pathway, namely, sequential two one-electron transfer steps, local and charge transfer, with the charge transfer state as a real intermediate effectively populated prior to the generation of 1(TT). Finally, the SF process is completed when the triplettriplet biexciton dissociates further into a pair of free triplets (T1) with a time scale of about a microsecond. The resulting triplets may also relax down to the corresponding ground state by emitting phosphorescence. Note, however, that due to the spin-conservation selection rule of the decay channels, these triplet excitons do not decay to the ground state rapidly.

Accordingly, an efficient SF requires certain relations among the excited state energies of the chromophores. Two important energy level matching criteria are as follows.16

E(S1) 2E(T1)(1)
E(T2) 2E(T1)(2)
where E(S) and E(T) denote the energy of the excited states in the singlet and triplet spin manifolds, respectively, and the subscripts 1 and 2 indicate the first and second excited states. The inequality in eqn (1) ensures that the fission of singlet excitons into triplet excitons is exoergic and thermodynamically favorable. On the other hand, the inequality in eqn (2) guarantees that the fusion of T1 excitons to T2 exciton is endoergic and thermodynamically unfavorable. Imposing the latter condition is, however, important not only to avoid non-fission decay into the triplet manifold but also for suppressing the triplettriplet annihilation process, where a singlet high-energy exciton can be created by two low-energy triplet excitations. Notice, however, that based on earlier experiences in the field, it would be relatively easier to satisfy eqn (2) than eqn (1); consequently, the latter equation can be considered as a primary criterion for SF chromophores. We mention in passing that although, in terms of spin angular momentum addition treatments, two triplet excitons may also fuse to give one quintet exciton, the energy of the latter exciton is usually greater than that of the former; therefore, the channel towards the quintet manifold is hindered.

Owing to its importance in energy science, green chemistry, photovoltaics, etc., SF is a rapidly advancing research field. As can be witnessed from the literature, there is a rich collection of references in this regard, providing a much deeper knowledge regarding this peculiar photophysical phenomenon. For some review articles dedicated to the field of SF, we refer the interested reader to the references.16 Among a multitude of related research reports, it is difficult to disregard the importance of theoretical and computational chemistry. As a matter of fact, quantum chemical computations have greatly improved our understanding in this context. The role of such studies was found to be essential for different purposes, among which we can note some representative examples such as appreciating the most relevant factors for the efficiency of the process, properties of the corresponding electronic states, the potential fission mechanisms, and the key parameters controlling the rate and dynamics of the SF process. Notwithstanding earlier efforts, theoretically predicting the SF relevant energetic criteria, which in turn allows us to propose potential candidates for practical purposes, is still an unaccomplished task. In this context, choosing an appropriate level of theory that is able to provide a reliable description of the SF process is an important issue. The computational cost of wave function-based methods can, however, become prohibitive for medium and large systems. As a workaround for this bottleneck, time-dependent (TD) density functional theory (DFT),5157 as an alternative with affordable computational cost and reasonable accuracy, is of interest. As the cornerstone of the present contribution, we present a detailed investigation on the TD-DFT modeling of the SF process. To do so, we considered a different approach to address the issue, where our work is not limited to assessing the available DFT approximations through a benchmarking study against the experimental data. Instead, we propose the optimally tuned range-separated hybrid functionals (OT-RSHs),5861 without invoking fitting procedures and only based on the satisfaction of the inherent constraints of density functionals, for describing the excited states energetics involved in the SF process. Perusing the ingredients of the proposed models, we plan to unveil whether there exists an OT-RSH with superior performance compared to the earlier approximations for the computational modeling of the SF process. To the best knowledge of the present authors, there has been no detailed and systematic analysis in this regard as yet. Lastly, the best proposed models will also be employed for the computational design of several chromophores as promising candidates that can be utilized in SF-based materials.

B. Optimal (non-empirical) tuning procedure

The optimal or non-empirical tuning procedure for obtaining the range-separation parameter uses no reference data from the experiment or high-level theoretical approaches but relies on the agreement between the ionization and orbital energies (ionization energy theorem).5861 In practice, for each value of , the ionization potential (IP) and electron affinity (EA) are calculated via delta self-consistent field (SCF) procedure as IP(N) = E(N 1) E(N) and EA(N) = E(N) E(N + 1), respectively, where the differences are between the energies of the ground state for the N and N 1 electron systems. Considering these latter relations, one has EA(N) = IP(N + 1). On the other hand, for the exact XC functional, IP(N) = HOMO(N), where HOMO is the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy. However, there is no similar relation that can equate EA with LUMO(N), where LUMO denotes the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital. In fact, the derivative discontinuity of the exchangecorrelation potentials leads to a difference between the two quantities. Nonetheless, this discontinuity can be removed by considering the IP of the anion instead of the EA of the neutral system, i.e., IP(N + 1) = HOMO(N + 1).7880

Because of the approximate nature of the density functional approximations (DFAs), there are still differences between the IPs and orbital energies, where these differences can be considered as the two minimizing conditions through the relations j(N;) = HOMO(N) + IP(N) and j(N + 1;) = HOMO(N + 1) + IP(N + 1). However, a reliable minimization should include both the neutral and ionic systems; therefore, the latter functions should be minimized simultaneously, leading to a more general minimization procedure using a function defined as J = j(N;) + j(N + 1;).6977 Finally, depending on the dependencies of the HOMO and total energies on the range-separation parameter, the values of are close to the minima of the j(N;) and j(N + 1;) functions; thus, the minimization of the following target function has been suggested.5861

Before computing the SF relevant energetics for these chromophores using the proposed OT-RSHs, we first performed the tuning procedure to obtain the optimal values of the range-separation parameter. The value of the range-separation parameter, with an approximate range from 0.05 to 0.30 bohr1 in increments of 0.05, is varied until its optimal value is obtained by minimizing the target function in eqn (8). Moreover, the range of changes for the SR HF exchange should also be determined. In many earlier studies, the minimization process and even property calculations were often performed with = 0.0 and consequently = 1.0. However, in our calculations, to examine the effect of SR HF exchange on the computed SF energetics, we have considered different percentages for this term. The SR HF exchange values can be determined either from the first principles arguments9396 or by empirical fittings against standard databases. It has been repeatedly shown that 0.2 leads to satisfactory results for various properties.97100 However, since this choice may not necessarily work well for all purposes, we have included a broader range for SR HF exchange, ranging from 0% to 20% with intervals of 5%.

Note, however, that for very large values of SR HF exchange, the corresponding range-separation parameter satisfying Koopmans theorem to a meaningful accuracy may not be found. This is because there is also a limit to the extent of SR HF exchange that can be employed, while still maintaining compatibility with the correlation expression.101 Finally, although we have considered different SR HF exchange contributions, in all the cases, the full exchange at the asymptotic distance, namely, the constraint of + = 1, has been imposed.

The DFAs in the energy expression of the OT-RSHs need also to be specified. As our earlier studies have demonstrated,71,72,74,75 irrespective of the DFAs, generalized gradient approximations (GGAs) or meta-GGAs, used during the optimal tuning of the range-separation parameter, almost similar optimally tuned values for are obtained. We have relied on the widely used GGA, namely, BLYP,102,103 for the exchange and correlation terms during all the optimal tuning processes. However, in addition to the BLYP approximation, other DFAs, namely, PBE104 and TPSS,105 have also been considered for SF energetics calculations using the developed OT-RSHs.

Following the previous thorough analyses and suggestions in the field, we used the widely adopted B3LYP functional and 6-31G(d) basis set for geometry optimization and frequency calculations.35,101,106,107 On the other hand, it has been found earlier that the optimally tuned values of the range-separation parameter obtained based on different geometries are qualitatively consistent, where there is no substantial dependence on the choice of basis sets and their extension.108,109 It has also been shown that for TD-DFT calculations, a double-zeta basis set gives results close to convergence and it is probably not necessary to use larger basis sets when modeling low-lying excited states, as in our case.110 Accordingly, following the recent efforts, we have used the 6-31+G(d) basis set in this work. This choice, in turn, gives us an opportunity to make a consistent comparison between the results presented herein and the previously related published data. For computations in solution, the solvent effects have been modeled through the polarizable continuum model (PCM)111113 in the integral equation formalism (IEF)114116 with the same solvents as the ones used in the corresponding experiments, namely, cyclohexane, acetonitrile, dichloromethane, and toluene, for I, II, III, and IVVIII, respectively. Since it has been previously shown that the optimal tuning procedure in the solvent may lead to unrealistic values for the range-separation parameter, we have alternatively performed the optimal tuning of range-separation for isolated chromophores in vacuum and then added the solvation model for subsequent calculations.69,117,118

Standard continuum solvation models, however, work by adding an external potential due to the surface charges induced by the charge distribution of the molecule to the gas-phase Hamiltonian.113 The resulting solvation from such a framework, missing out on the correlation-induced screening, has small effects on the orbital energies.69 In order to account for the screening effects, we have also relied on a different approach, where the polarization can directly be introduced into the framework of RSHs without resorting to solvation models.69,99 The screening effect by electron correlation can simply be modeled by modifying + = 1 to + = 1/, where the asymptotic potential is appropriately screened by the scalar dielectric constant as 1/r as compared to 1/r in the gas phase. This approach has, in turn, some advantages, where it can not only account for dielectric screening while using the same range-separation parameter as the one tuned for gas phase calculations but parameter can also be determined as = (1/) using the preset values of for systems in the gas phase.119 The OT-RSHs constructed upon this screening condition are called as OT-SRSHs in our discussion.

From the methodological side, TammDancoff approximation120 has been used in our calculations, mainly due to its lower computational cost and avoiding the triplet instability problem.121 Vertical excitation energies from the ground state to the first singlet excited state as well as to the first and second triplet excited states have been computed as E(S1) = [E(S1)/E(S0)] E(S0), E(T1) = [E(T1)/E(S0)] E(S0), and E(T2) = [E(T2)/E(S0)] E(S0), respectively, where E(S1)/E(S0), E(T1)/E(S0), and E(T2)/E(S0) denote the energies of the respective excited states at the optimized geometry of the ground state. Accordingly, the SF energy gap of the chromophores as the driving force for singlet exciton fission and the triplettriplet (TT) energy gap can be expressed as ESF = 2E(T1) E(S1) and ETT = 2E(T1) E(T2), respectively.

Most of the runs during this work were implemented in the GAUSSIAN09 suite of codes.122 Moreover, for some other analyses and TD-DFT computations using specific functionals, we also used the ORCA quantum chemistry package.123 As the metrics for comparing the performances of the functionals, we have used mean signed deviation (MSD), , mean absolute deviation (MAD), , maximum absolute deviation (MaxAD), MaxAD = max|xi|, minimum absolute deviation (MinAD), MinAD = min|xi|, and root mean square deviation (RMSD), , where xi is the deviation of the computed results from the reference data and the summations are run over the set of the considered chromophores, n.

An accurate description of the singlet and triplet components should, however, be accounted for. Scrutiny in this line not only enables us to disclose the components that are better reproduced but it is also unveiled that what excitation energies govern the good performance of the OT-RSHs for the prediction of the SF energetics. All the numerical raw data as well as the corresponding statistical analyses have also been presented in the same tables. Comparing this data with those obtained earlier, we find that there are some slight differences among the numerical values within the vertical and adiabatic treatments, where for some SF chromophores, smaller deviations with respect to the reference data are obtained from the adiabatic considerations, while in some other cases, the vertical analysis is the winner. Furthermore, although the results of the statistical analysis of both the vertical and adiabatic treatments are consistent, the statistical descriptors of the latter scheme are, however, comparable to or larger than that obtained from the former, which in turn can lead to larger deviations from the derived SF energy gaps.108 However, from the obtained data regarding the adiabatic analysis, it was found out that the performance of the proposed model based on TPSS DFA is slightly outperformed by the PBE-based approximation. On the other hand, regarding the singlet excitation energies, the related analyses unambiguously demonstrate that as compared to the standard LC approximations, our OT-RSHs provide more accurate data, which is consistent with earlier reports.142 Summing up, it seems that the accountability of the proposed OT-RSHs can be disclosed not only for the SF energetics criteria but also for the corresponding individual transition energies.

We have also compared our results on the singlet and triplet excitation energies as well as some of the corresponding energy gaps with those obtained with other high-level wave function-based approaches, where besides the employed methods, the observed discrepancies may, however, be related to other factors such as optimized geometries and basis sets. We have further examined the capability of the predicted chromophores to be employed in the SF material. To this end, the relationships of the SF energy gap and its ingredients, singlet and triplet excited state energies, and the HOMOLUMO (HL) energy gap for these molecules have been investigated. We can see from the correlations given in Fig. 7 that there exist linear correlations among the quantities with regression coefficients larger than 0.95. From these regression analyses, we also find that when the HL energy gaps are smaller than 2.5 eV, E(S1) is larger than 2E(T1), leading to negative ESF values, consequently to the thermodynamically favorability. This is in agreement with the earlier reports regarding other systems with diradical character, where it has been found that small HL gaps are related to small SF energy gaps.106,158,159 However, when the HL energy gap increases, such as for XIII and XIV chromophores, a reverse trend between the excited state energies is in place, where the values of ESF become positive; therefore, the corresponding molecules are thermodynamically unfavorable. These findings, in turn, unveil the role of the HL energy gap for proposing chromophores that can be used in SF-based materials. Putting all these findings together, the developed models in this work should hopefully be applicable for the modification of existing systems for optimizing the SF process as well as for searching new and optimal candidates prior to their challenging synthesis.

5. Concluding remarks

With the objective to design new theoretical approaches for the investigation of singlet fission process, in this work, we proposed to use optimally tuned range-separated density functional theory for the purpose. We have presented several variants of the optimally tuned range-separated approximations for predicting the singlet fission relevant energetic criteria for a series of experimentally known and theoretical designed chromophores. Based on the results of our study, it has been shown that the newly developed optimally tuned range-separated functionals lead to a reliable theoretical description of the singlet fission process. Moreover, the imperative roles of range-separation parameter tuning as well as short- and long-range exact-like exchange were highlighted. Taking all the comparisons together, we found that although the optimally tuned range-separated functionals coupled with the solvation models as well as the screened-exchange versions show some improvements from the statistical analysis viewpoint, satisfying the energy levels matching the criteria of the SF process should also be taken into account. Accordingly, performing both the optimal tuning procedure and subsequent computations on the energetics of the SF process in the gas phase can be considered as a promising protocol with affordable accuracy-to-computational cost ratio for the present purpose. Comparisons against recent literature unveiled that the newly proposed models outperform the conventional counterparts as well as other hybrids with both fixed and interelectronic distance-dependent exact-like exchange portions. The accountability of the presented models was further validated as a useful tool for the theoretical design of the chromophores that can be utilized in the singlet exciton fission materials.
