Abstract

A conjugated boron-dipyrromethene (BODIPY)â€“platinum polymer possessing a low bandgap of 1.7 eV has been synthesized and characterized. Bulk heterojunction solar cells employing this polymer gave high open circuit voltages up to 0.92 V and power conversion efficiencies close to 1%.

1. Introduction

Solution processable conjugated polymers possessing low bandgaps have been the subject of intensive research in the field of organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices.1 Incorporation of transition metals into conjugated polymer structures has been an intriguing strategy in order to obtain organic materials with novel properties characteristic of metals.2 Among various transition metal complexes, Pt(II) complexes are unique for intrinsically adopting square planar geometries and can serve as building blocks for linear long-chain structures.3 These complexes typically show strong spinâ€“orbital coupling effects that induce formation of long-lived triplet excitons, which have been considered beneficial for OPV operations.4 Thus, Pt containing conjugated polymers have recently attracted significant attention in OPV research.5 A variety of conjugated polymers containing Pt(II)-diacetylenide building blocks have been prepared and studied in detail.6 Promising efficiencies over 4% have been observed in some devices applying such polymers possessing low bandgaps.7

Pt-containing polymers applied in OPVs to date exclusively have the basic structures as shown in Scheme 1. The trans-bis(trialkylphosphine)platinum diacetylenide building blocks serve as structural scaffoldings that connect various organic and organometallic chromophores to form linear conjugated polymer chains. Electronic and photophysical properties of the resulting polymers, including bandgaps, frontier energy levels and triplet yields, are mainly determined by the nature of these chromophores. Typical low bandgap Pt polymers contain organic chromophores featuring several electron-rich and -poor aromatic moieties connected in series.7 Low bandgaps are resulted from intra-molecular charge transfer excitation. In such donorâ€“acceptor design, chromophores typically contain several aromatic rings side-by-side and are relatively long in length. The increased chromophore sizes can potentially dilute Pt induced spinâ€“orbital coupling effects and lower triplet generation yields.4c


Scheme 1 Schematic representation of platinum containing conjugated polymers applied in organic photovoltaics.

Boron-dipyrromethene (BODIPY) dyes are a unique class of organic chromophores, possessing compact sizes, high stability, large fluorescence quantum yields and tunable chemical/physical properties through well-established reactions.8 BODIPY dyes and conjugated polymers containing BODIPY building blocks have recently been applied in bulk heterojunction (BHJ) OPVs and promising performances have been identified.9 Interestingly, Zhao et al. have recently reported bis-platinated BODIPY small molecule derivatives that displayed long-lived room temperature phosphorescence and were applied in photosensitized up-conversion through tripletâ€“triplet annihilation.10 Thus, combining BODIPY and Pt-acetylenide building blocks into polymer structures, which has not been reported previously, provides new opportunities in OPV research.

We report herein the synthesis and photovoltaic properties of a novel low bandgap, solution processable conjugated polymer based on Pt-diacetylenide scaffolds and BODIPY chromophores.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Synthesis and characterization

The synthesis of monomers and the BODIPYâ€“Pt alternating polymer P1 is summarized in Scheme 2. The trans-dichlorobis(tri-n-butylphosphine)platinum(II) (Pt1) was prepared according to reported procedures.11 All synthetic intermediates were fully characterized by NMR spectroscopy and the chemical structure of M1 was further confirmed by high resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS). The reaction of an equal molar amount of M1 and Pt1 in Et3Nâ€“CH2Cl2 mixed solvents (1/2, v/v) at room temperature in the presence of a catalytic amount of CuI led smoothly to P1 within 12 h. P1 was isolated as a purple powder after precipitation from the reaction mixture to methanol and purified by sequential Soxhlet extraction steps with methanol, acetone and chloroform. The molecular weight of P1 was estimated by size exclusion chromatography (SEC, Fig. S1â€ ) against polystyrene standards to be 27300 (Mn) with a polydispersity index (PDI) of 2.1, corresponding to on average ca. 25 BODIPY or Pt units along the polymer main-chain.

The chemical structure of P1 was fully characterized by multi-nuclear NMR spectroscopy as shown in Fig. 1. The 1H NMR spectrum of P1 appears approximately as a superposition of spectra of M1 and Pt1, except that signals of the polymer are broader, less resolved and slightly up-field shifted. Complete disappearance of the acetylenic signals at 3.32 ppm, which are observed in the spectrum of M1, indicates successful polymerization. Integration of the P11H NMR spectrum leads to a 1/1 ratio between BODIPY and (nBu3P)2Pt units along the polymer main-chain, suggesting the absence of alkyne homo-coupling reactions during polymer formation. 11B NMR measurement of M1 gave a triplet at 0.2 ppm with a 1JBâ€“F constant of ca. 32 Hz (Fig. 1A). Upon polymerization, the 11B signal for P1 becomes broad and slightly shifted to 0.4 ppm. Both chemical shifts are typical for tetra-coordinated boron species,12 suggesting a lack of unwanted side-reactions at these boron centers during polymerization. Similar trends are observed in the 19F NMR spectra (Fig. 1B). A quartet having a 1JFâ€“B constant of ca. 31 Hz is observed at âˆ’144.57 ppm for M1, while the signal for P1 up-field shifts to âˆ’145.34 ppm and becomes unresolved. Furthermore, the 31P NMR spectrum of Pt1 has a pseudo triplet at 5.05 ppm with a large 1JPâ€“Pt constant of ca. 2380 Hz, while the 31P signal for P1 is observed at 3.76 ppm with a similar 1JPâ€“Pt constant of ca. 2366 Hz (Fig. 1C). These chemical shifts and coupling constants are consistent with reported values in the literature.11c,13

3.2 Electronic properties

Absorption and emission spectra of M1 and P1 both in solutions and as thin films are shown in Fig. 2. M1 in dilute CHCl3 solution has an absorption maximum (Î»max) at 545 nm and emission maximum (Î»em) at 560 nm when excited at Î»max. A quantum efficiency of ca. 23% was estimated for M1. Sharp absorption and emission profiles, small Stoke's shift and high quantum efficiency of M1 are typical of BODIPY dyes.8 Upon polymerization, Î»max and Î»em of P1 in dilute CHCl3 solution became broader and significantly red-shifted to 677 nm and 693 nm, respectively, due to elongated conjugation and delocalization along the polymer main-chain. Î»max of P1 is also larger than those of similar bis-platinated BODIPY compounds bearing nBu3P ligands as reported by Zhao et al. (Î»max = 643 nm and Î»em = 671 nm),10b indicating certain electron delocalization through the Pt-diacetylenide centers in P1. Resemblance of both absorption and emission spectra of P1 with those of M1, as well as the relatively small Stoke's shift (Î”Î» = 16 nm), suggests that electronic transitions in P1 are intra-ligand (IL) in nature. Fluorescence quantum yield of P1 is greatly suppressed to ca. 0.3%, possibly caused by enhanced inter-system crossing due to the presence of Pt centers. However, no room temperature phosphorescence could be observed in deaerated solutions, which can be explained by the low bandgap of P1 and the energy gap law,14 and is consistent with observations in the other Pt-containing low bandgap polymers reported in the literature.7a,b Only a slight red-shift in both absorption and emission spectra is observed in thin films of P1 (Î»max = 685 nm and Î»em = 706 nm) with similar line-shapes to those of solution profiles, which is consistent with the amorphous nature of the polymer as suggested by DSC measurements. From the absorption edge, a bandgap of ca. 1.7 eV is estimated for P1. Cyclic voltammetry measurement on thin films of P1 revealed a non-reversible multi-step oxidation event and a very small non-reversible reduction peak (Fig. S3â€ ), from the onset of which a HOMO energy level of ca. âˆ’5.3 eV and a LUMO energy level of ca. âˆ’3.5 eV are estimated. This leads to an electrochemical bandgap of ca. 1.8 eV for P1, slightly larger but consistent with the observed optical bandgap.

3.3 Solar cell performance

BHJ solar cells were initially fabricated employing P1 and phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) using a conventional device geometry of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/active layer/Al. Different weight ratios between P1 and PCBM were tested in order to optimize the device performance and the results are summarized in Table 1. The best device power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 0.21% was achieved using a P1/PCBM ratio of 1/3. A relatively low open circuit voltage (VOC) of 0.57 V and a fill factor (FF) of 32% were recorded. We suspected that the anode interfacial layer, PEDOT:PSS, having a work function of ca. âˆ’5.0 eV (0.3 eV higher than the HOMO of P1)15 might not be optimal in terms of energy level alignment and Ohmic contact formation. We thus turned into thermally evaporated MoO3, which has been extensively studied as an anode interfacial layer in OPVs and work functions matching or below the HOMO of P1 have been reported.16 Indeed, by using 10 nm MoO3 as the anode interfacial layer, VOCs of all devices significantly increased. A very high VOC of 0.92 V is observed in the P1/PCBM (1/2) devices but the overall PCEs are limited by low short circuit current (JSC) and FF. The best performances were observed in devices employing P1/PCBM at 1/3 weight ratio, giving a high VOC of 0.86 V and an average PCE of 0.91% (Fig. S4â€ ). The VOC enhancement of ca. 0.3 V correlates well with the difference between the work function of PEDOT:PSS and HOMO of P1, suggesting an Ohmic contact formation using MoO3. PCEs are still limited by the relatively small JSC (2.2 mA cmâˆ’2) and FF (48%). Some possible reasons are an unfavourable morphology and poor charge transport due to the polymer's amorphous nature. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of active layers from the best performing devices using PEDOT:PSS (Fig. S5â€ ) show no discernible phase separation. Furthermore, thermal annealing seemed to have no significant, sometimes negative, effects on device performance, presumably due to high polymer fullerene miscibility and low polymer crystallinity. The large, cone-shaped nBu3P ligands can effectively prevent polymer chain close-packing and are thus most likely responsible for the polymer's amorphous nature. By using planar ligands that are still capable of forming stable complexes with Pt-diacetylenides, more crystalline polymers and better performing devices are expected. Another possibility to account for the low PCEs is non-optimal energy level alignment between donor triplet states and acceptor molecules and unfavourable charge transfer dynamics. Detailed photophysical studies on P1 at ambient and cryogenic conditions, as well as investigations on charge generation/separation phenomena at the organic/organic interfaces, are currently underway.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully prepared a novel BODIPY and platinum containing conjugated polymer, P1, possessing low bandgaps. OPV devices employing P1 and PCBM were fabricated and evaluated. Commonly applied anode interfacial layer PEDOT:PSS was found to be non-optimal due to the deep lying HOMO level of P1 at âˆ’5.3 eV, while thermally evaporated MoO3 formed ohmic contact and high VOCs were obtained. Detailed studies on photophysical properties of the polymer and charge generation mechanisms in polymer/fullerene blends are currently being carried out. Replacing nBu3P ligands on Pt centers with less sterically demanding ones is expected to increase crystallinity of these platinum-containing polymers in order to improve device performances.
