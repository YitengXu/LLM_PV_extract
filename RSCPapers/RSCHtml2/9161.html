Abstract

1. Introduction

Over the past few decades, in spite of diligent efforts, harnessing charge recombination reactions (CRRs) has remained an unsolved challenge in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). It is expected that the CRR of photogenerated charge carriers could play a characteristic role in the non-fulfillment of high power conversion efficiency (PCE) in DSSCs, while accusing fingers are pointing at the non-optimized molecular design of dyes. CRRs could be categorized into â€˜innerâ€™ and â€˜outerâ€™ types that impact the structural and physicochemical properties of dyes, which is important for overcoming their rate.1 It is well known that excited-electron injection via interfacial electron transfer (ET) occurs in pico- or femto-second order of time, whereas the injected electrons could recombine with oxidized organic dye (D*+) with several times slower kinetics in micro-second time scale.2,3 In order to rationalize this mismatched and stochastic interfacial ET kinetics, three main approaches have been developed so far. (i) According to the first approach, there are some differences between molecular orbitals (MOs) of organic dyes, which are involved in interfacial ET. Photoexcited electron injection in organic dyes commonly goes forward via ET from Ï€* orbitals of anchors (e.g. cyanoacrylic acid and carboxylic acid), whereas CRR must happen to Ï€ orbitals of donor (D) part(s) of dyes, which not only is/are in the farther distance, but also experience(s) weaker coupling to TiO2. (ii) According to the second approach, a difference in thermodynamics driving force of CRR is responsible for such mismatched interfacial ET. For electron injection of excited states, the driving force is inherently activationless that happens in the continuum of states, while alteration in the inverted region of Marcus kinetics makes it possible for the CRR to go forward with a large free energy.4â€“6 (iii) The third approach states the blueprint of local electric fields at interfaces that facilitate the electron-D*+ separation as fast as possible for electron injection. In order to achieve to clear understanding that each of the above-mentioned contributors how remarkable impact on interfacial rectification is a great puzzle that has remained under speculation.7â€“11

Among the harnessing strategies of CRRs, special attention has been paid to the molecular structures of organic dyes. The donorâ€“Ï€â€“acceptor (Dâ€“Ï€â€“A) framework of organic dyes which have a rod-like molecular configuration might undergo unwanted aggregations, and consequently, foment more self-quenching and CRRs owing to D* â†’ D energy transfer via stacking interactions Ï€â€“Ï€.12 Such interactions impose an inefficient electron injection process on the DSSCs. The inclusion of long alkyl chains/bulky aryl substitutions that introduced the starburst dyes was another synthesis paradigm for suppressing CRRs.13 These types of dyes are beneficial to anti-aggregation multidonor architectures that have been satisfactory options but not fully qualified candidates against CRRs. In the opposite direction, the dianchoring dyes cannot afford a better suppressing effect against CRRs. They are known to be successful candidates for achieving greater binding strength onto the semiconductor surface, and their Ï€-conjugated building blocks could provide a reinforced photocurrent and more appropriate molar absorption coefficient. However, the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A designs of dianchoring dyes are suffering from a weaker open-circuit voltage (VOC), and the high rate of CRRs is responsible for reduced VOC and lower PCE. In this respect, Hagfeldt and co-workers14â€“16 proposed a dianchoring type of double-branched triphenylamine-based dyes, but they mentioned that these dyes are faced with unrestrained CRRs and still there are ongoing efforts to introduce new structurally optimized candidates. Thiophene units as a cross-bridge of double Dâ€“Ï€â€“A dyes with greater Ï€ â†’ Ï€* transitions and weaker Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions represent a blocking effect against I3âˆ’ spices of electrolyte that increasing in charge density is another strategy that was reported by Wang et al. They stated that the advantages of these dyes could have a downward shifting of the conduction band (CB), which causes a retarded CRR and enhances the VOC of DSSCs.17

In 2012, three comprehensive benchmarks were drawn for empowering the PCE of organic solar cells (OSCs) up to 20% by J. C. Hummelen and co-workers.18,19 (1) The first benchmark referred to a radiation limit that provokes weak ET absorption, which needs to be appropriately circumvented. (2) According to the second benchmark, a â€˜drift-diffusionâ€™ model20 of charge carriers could be utilized for considering the impact of space charges on bimolecular recombination, local electric fields, and electron injection. (3) Third and the most important one, they stated that the crucial role of the dielectric constant of dyes is felt missing, while it could be applied for diminishing the CRR in DSSCs. Based on the fact that the singletâ€“singlet exciton recombination21 and charge transfer exciton recombination are responsible for more than 12 and 32% of PCE loss in DSSCs, respectively, their main reasons are weak dielectric constant of DSSCsâ€™ components. In this respect, by focusing on enhancing the dielectric constant of dyes as the main component of DSSCs, synthetic paradigms were majorly concentrated on adjusting the Ï€-conjugated building blocks of dyes by including polarizable substitutions.

Up to now, the influence of the Ï€-bridge structure of dyes and their successful alternatives in tuning the dielectric constant to greater values have remained unknown, and as the main target of the present study, we designed two triphenylamine Dâ€“Ï€â€“A types of dyes with naphthalene and anthracene units (TpAzo 1 and TpAzo 2) as Ï€-bridges and triphenylamine and carboxyl FGs as D and A parts, respectively. We used these dyes as dielectric layers of a capacitor to be able to characterize the dielectric constant properties.

3. Capacitor device fabrication

3.1. Electrode preparation

The sputtering methods of the electrode fabrication procedure have been reported previously.25â€“27 The rf-sputtering instrument provides some advantages such as uniform thickness and dense coatings with a high deposition rate, which have been previously reported for thin layer deposition of ZrO2, TaN and In2O3, for developing electrodes of capacitors.25â€“27 In order to fabricate the electrodes of the capacitor, an Al thin film was deposited onto glass substrates (1.0 Ã— 1.0 Ã— 0.2 cm) using an rf-sputtering instrument (Desk Sputter Coater DST3, Nano-Structured Coatings Co. Iran). As the first step, the treatment process of glass samples was started by washing substrates with acetone and ethanol under ultrasonic conditions at room temperature (RT) for 20 minutes. In the second step, the Al target (Sindlhauser Materials GmbH; purity: 99.99%) was pinned at the gun of the rf-sputtering instrument chamber, and a vacuum turbopump (Pfeiffer Vacuum GmbH) was set to obtain high-vacuum conditions (inside pressure â‰ˆ1.96 Ã— 10âˆ’5 torr) and the flow rate and working pressure of Ar gas were kept in 33 sccm using a mass flow controller (MFC, Line Tech Co.) and 2.86 Ã— 10âˆ’2 torr, respectively. A pre-sputtering step was executed for 10 minutes and the power supplier was set to 100 W. The thickness of the sputtered layer was detected by a quartz crystal-based monitoring system in the sputtering instrument.

3.2. Dye deposition

In order to deposit a thin film of dye, the sol inks were formulated by dispersing 0.2 g of TpAzo 1 and TpAzo 2 dyes in 2.0 mL of ethanol for five minutes under the ultrasonic condition at RT. Then, the sol inks were deposited onto Al-based substrates by a spin-coating method at 2000 rpm for three minutes. The spin-coating method has been previously applied for developing organic semiconductor thin layers for transistors and can be utilized for the dye deposition process.28 The samples were dried at RT for 50 minutes and prepared for the deposition of back-contact. The Al back contact electrode of the capacitor was deposited by a rf-sputtering method for 900 s.

As depicted in Fig. 2, Îµâ€² and tanÎ´ belonging to dye layers were gathered in the range of f that the values of Îµâ€² and tanÎ´ undergo a sharp decrease.
