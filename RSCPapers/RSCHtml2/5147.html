Fusion or non-fusion of quasi-two-dimensional fused perylene diimide acceptors: the importance of molecular geometry for fullerene-free organic solar cells

Abstract

In this work, two small molecular electron acceptors (BTFPDI and fBTFPDI) comprising a central bithiophene (BT) bridge and two fused perylene diimide (FPDI) units were designed and synthesized for fullerene-free organic solar cells (OSCs). The molecular geometry of these electron acceptors can be adjusted through a locked conformation via a photocyclization reaction. As expected, BTFPDI has a highly twisted geometry with the assistance of the quasi-two-dimensional (quasi-2D) configuration of the FPDI unit and modest dihedral angles between FPDI and BT units, while fBTFPDI shows a relatively planar geometry with symmetrically aligned FPDI wings. When paired with polymer donor PTB7-Th, the intrinsic configuration characteristic of BTFPDI guaranteed an impressive power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 8.07% with an open circuit voltage (Voc) of 0.81 V, and a record high short-circuit current density (Jsc) of 17.35 mA cm2, which is almost the highest Jsc value reported for PDI-based fullerene-free OSCs. However, the optimal device employing fBTFPDI as an electron acceptor delivered a PCE of only 5.89% with a significantly reduced Jsc of 12.30 mA cm2. The interesting results show that, unlike traditional PDI molecules, the ring-fusion molecular design did not produce the desired positive effect in FPDI derivatives, and also provide a new insight into the regulation of the molecular geometry based on FPDI acceptors with an intrinsic quasi-2D structure.

Introduction

Recently, fullerene-free electron acceptors as alternatives of traditional fullerene electron acceptors for organic solar cells (OSCs) have undergone unprecedented progress because of their inherent advantages such as strong optical absorptivity, flexible tunability of optical band gaps and energy levels, long-term device stability, and so on.14 The latest power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of single-junction fullerene-free OSCs have reached over 16%, showing great research prospects in breaking traditional efficiency barriers in fullerene-based OSCs.57 Among the numerous reported fullerene-free electron acceptors, perylene diimide (PDI) is one of the most studied and desirable electron-deficient units for constructing n-type electron acceptors because of the good absorption, high electron mobility, low-lying energy level and good chemical and environmental tolerance.812 However, the current state-of-the-art efficiencies of PDI-based OSCs still largely lag behind those of other fullerene-free acceptors.1315 The main obstacle is the existence of strong intermolecular self-aggregation tendency, which results in unfavorable micrometer-sized phase separation in bulk-heterojunction films, thus leading to undesirable photovoltaic performance in OSCs since the exciton diffusion is typically within 1020 nm.1618 To address this problem, one strategy is to construct a twisted three-dimensional (3D) molecular geometry, which can partially suppress the strong intermolecular aggregation and reduce undesirable domain sizes in PDI-based fullerene-free OSCs.1921 However, the excessively twisted 3D PDI molecules will reduce the intermolecular interactions, and cause low crystallinity and charge transport.22 Another recent, successful and feasible design strategy is to construct a quasi-two-dimensional (quasi-2D) fused perylene diimide (FPDI) unit, which can efficiently prohibit the excessive self-aggregation due to a small built-in twisted molecular structure in FPDI unit which provides more favorable intermolecular interactions.2325 As a result, better morphological compatibility between FPDI and the donor in the blend was observed, and significantly improved photovoltaic performances in either small or polymeric FPDI electron acceptor based OSCs were obtained in comparison with their PDI counterparts.2629

It has been well-known that control of the molecular geometries is important to achieve promising photovoltaic performance of PDI-based OSCs.3032 For example, a series of twisted PDI tetramers (TPC-PDI4, TPE-PDI4 and TPPz-PDI4, Fig. S1) were synthesized by choosing central cores with various degrees of intramolecular twisting, and the result illustrated that reducing the intramolecular twisting can significantly improve the photovoltaic performance of the resulting molecules.3335 Another example of geometrically controlled acceptor is clearly demonstrated through two bay linked PDI derivatives (Sdi-PBI-S and Sdi-PBI, Fig. S1) with or without heteroannulation in bay regions, and the results showed that increasing the intramolecular twisting of Sdi-PBI-S (80 vs. 67) can lead to more favorable phase separation and balanced charge mobility in the blend film.36 On the other hand, recently aromatic ring-fusion of PDI has also been shown to be effective in improving device performance in fullerene-free OSCs.11,37 It is always concluded that ring-fused PDI acceptors, such as PDI dimers, trimers or tetramers with phenylene, indacenodithieno[3,2-b]thiophene, thiophene, furan and thienothiophene fusion bridges, normally possess better device performance in OSCs than their non-ring-fused counterparts.32,38,39 A classic example can be observed in two propeller-shaped PDI acceptors (TPB6 and TPB6-C, Fig. S1), which proved that the aromatic ring-fusion strategy can help to enhance the charge transport ability, promote a stronger face-on orientation, and achieve higher PCEs.40 Similarly, Yan et al. reported two PDI-based tetramers (FTTB-PDI4 and TTB-PDI4, Fig. S1) with a fused and non-fused configuration, and found that structural differences underlie the significantly superior electron transport ability and device performance of fused PDI tetramers than those of the non-fused counterpart.30 These results actually raised an argument on the balance between the formed intramolecular twisted geometries, which plays a significant role in restraining the strong self-aggregation of the PDI unit, and the rigidity of PDI through ring-fusion, which provides a better charge carrier generation efficiency. This therefore illustrates that although the formation of intramolecular twisted geometries seems to be a successful design strategy in obtaining better morphological domains for better device performance, the more or less detrimental effects on their electron transport capability in the blend from the increased steric hindrance also need to be considered, in which case increasing the rigidity of PDI derivative can handle this trade-off. This thus requires that the rigidity and molecular geometry of PDI acceptors should be carefully designed and controlled in order to pursue highly efficient PDI acceptors for OSC application. More importantly, to the best of our knowledge, such a design strategy has been rarely explored and investigated in promising FPDI-based electron acceptors with an intrinsic quasi-2D twisted configuration, which is realistically meaningful for further improving the efficiency of fullerene-free OSCs.

Herein, inspired and motivated by the previous FPDI studies by us and other groups,2325,28 we designed and synthesized two FPDI-based electron acceptors (BTFPDI and fBTFPDI), with unlocked or locked molecular geometries through non-fusing and fusing the bithiophene (BT) bridge at the inner bay position of two FPDI units (Scheme 1b). As expected, the unlocked and non-fusion BTFPDI shows a more semi-flexible geometry with a large dihedral angle between FPDI and BT units, whereas fBTFPDI possesses a completely different molecular geometry with restricted rotational freedom between FPDI and BT units after ring-fusion. The photovoltaic properties of devices were carefully optimized, and the fullerene-free OSCs based on the PTB7-Th:BTFPDI blend shows a superior PCE of 8.07% with an impressive Jsc of 17.35 mA cm2, whereas the ring-fused fBTFPDI device gave an inferior PCE of 5.89%, and this result is a counterexample compared to previous reports. The origin of the different PCEs was then systematically investigated by comparing the opticalelectronic properties, exciton dissociation efficiency, charge recombination dynamics, film morphology and molecular stacking behavior. These results indicate that even though ring-fusion in PDI derivatives is a generally recognized design strategy for PDI acceptors, the molecular geometry indeed plays a more important role in determining the photovoltaic performance, and this counterexample may open a new window to design novel PDI-based electron acceptors for achieving the state-of-the-art performance in fullerene-free OSCs.

Results and discussion

The chemical structures of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI and the synthetic route are presented in Scheme 1. Briefly, BTFPDI was synthesized by a classical MigitaKosugiStille cross-coupling of the monobrominated-FPDI (FPDI-Br) and bithiophene distannyl compound with 82% yield. The ring-fused and locked analogues, fBTFPDI, were then obtained through a photocyclization reaction with 80% yield. These fullerene-free acceptors are fully characterized by 1H and 13C NMR to confirm their structures. BTFPDI and fBTFPDI are readily soluble in common solvents, such as dichloromethane, chloroform and chlorobenzene, at room temperature.

The UV-vis absorption spectra of BTFPDI, fBTFPDI and the corresponding blend films are depicted in Fig. 1a. BTFPDI shows two broad absorptions centered at 399 nm and 545 nm, corresponding to localized * transitions and intramolecular charge transfer (ICT), respectively, while fBTFPDI gave a significant bathochromic shift due to the enlarged conjugation length with the increased planarity. It can be found that the ICT peak of fBTFPDI is pronounced and red-shifted with two comparable maximum absorptions at 540 (01 transition peaks) and 590 nm (00 transition peaks), which is also the result of the stronger interaction between FPDI and BT units due to the more planar molecular geometry after ring-fusion. To elucidate the intermolecular interactions, temperature-dependent absorption spectra of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI in chlorobenzene solution were collected and are shown in Fig. S3. It can be found that both BTFPDI and fBTFPDI show pronounced ICT absorption peaks between 30 C and 100 C, which suggests that temperature does not affect the intrinsic electronic interactions. As shown in Fig. S3a, the absorption spectra of BTFPDI showed a very weak blue-shift while increasing the temperature from 30 C to 100 C, and there are little changes in the high temperature range, which indicates that there are strong intermolecular interactions between the molecular geometries of BTFPDI. In the case of fBTFPDI, however, a large blue-shifted absorption can be found with the temperature increasing from 30 C to 100 C, indicating weaker intermolecular interactions than that of BTFPDI. Upon cooling, the absorption spectra of both BTFPDI and fBTFPDI can return to their original status (Fig. S3b and d). These results demonstrated that BTFPDI has a stronger aggregation effect even in the solution state than fBTFPDI, and a possible reason for the different temperature-dependent aggregation behaviors of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI is the synergistic contribution of polarization-induced inter-chain and torsion-induced intra-chain disorders.41,42 The optical band gaps of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI calculated from the onset of film absorption are 1.9 eV and 2.0 eV, respectively. Moreover, BTFPDI and fBTFPDI-based blend films all displayed strong and broad absorption response ranging from 300 to 750 nm, which is more desirable for capturing more photons and resulting in high current density in OSCs. It is interesting that the BTFPDI-based blend exhibited a higher maximum molar extinction coefficient (6.7 104 M1 cm1) than the fBTFPDI film (2.7 104 M1 cm1) (Fig. S1a), which is much helpful for better light harvesting, and an increased short-circuit current density is expected. The possible reason for this enhanced absorption ability of BTFPDI is that the ring fusion may alter the electron distribution of the frontier orbitals, which gives a large transition probability.43 The optical properties of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI demonstrated that the ring fusion had a large effect on both their absorption intensity and profile.

The electrochemical properties of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI are investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements and the energy level diagram is provided in Fig. 1b. The CV curves show that both the acceptors exhibit reversible reduction peaks and no obvious oxidation peaks can be identified (Fig. S2b), which is similar to that of the reported FPDI-based acceptors.2528 According to the reduction onsets and calibration of the ferrocene/ferrocenium (Fc/Fc+) redox couple, the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMOs) of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI are estimated to be 4.09 and 3.96 eV, respectively, and then the HOMO energy levels also can be calculated as 5.99 and 5.96 eV (Fig. 1b). Similar to previous ring-fusion cases,30,32,39 the fused molecule (fBTFPDI) showed an upshifted LUMO level relative to the non-fused one (BTFPDI) due to the delocalization of frontier molecular orbitals, which was expected to result in higher Voc in the related fullerene-free OSCs. Moreover, large HOMO and LUMO offsets between the polymer donor and electron acceptors also can be observed, thus creating enough driving force for the efficient exciton dissociation in devices.

To gain deeper insight into the effect of the different molecular geometries in BTFPDI and fBTFPDI molecules, density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using Gaussian 09 at the B3LYP/6-31G level, and the calculated results are shown in Fig. 1c and the ESI. It can be clearly seen that the LUMO and HOMO energy levels of unlocked BTFPDI are exclusively delocalized on FPDI and BT moieties, respectively, whereas ring fusion alters the electron distribution of frontier orbitals and slight amounts of extended frontier molecular orbitals can be observed as seen from the pink circle in Fig. S4. The DFT simulation LUMO/HOMO levels of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI acceptors were calculated to be 3.68/5.73 and 3.62/5.97 eV, respectively, which are well consistent with the CV measurements (Fig. 1b). As mentioned previously, the ring-fusion design route plays a critical role in optimizing the molecular geometry and achieving superior performance in traditional PDI molecules, but leaves a large gap to be filled in understanding its effect on FPDI acceptors with quasi-2D features. As expected, BTFPDI shows a relatively twisted molecular configuration with a high degree of rotational freedom, and the dihedral angles are calculated to be 24.5, 119.7, 12.9, 58.1 and 23.9, respectively. After the photocyclization reaction, the rotation between FPDI and BT is completely locked with resulting torsional angles of 1 = 23.7, 2 = 0.5, 3 = 14.9, 4 = 0.3 and 5 = 23.7, owing to the less intramolecular twisting of the locked fBTFPDI (Fig. 1c). Therefore, the FPDI molecules, BTFPDI and fBTFPDI, exhibit distinctly different molecular geometries, thus providing a suitable platform for a deep understanding of the structureproperty relationship of FPDI-based fullerene-free electron acceptors.

To evaluate the photovoltaic properties of these fullerene-free electron acceptors, fullerene-free OSC devices with a conventional configuration of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/photoactive layer/PFN-Br/Ag were fabricated. The details and optimization of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI devices can be found in the ESI (Fig. S5 and S6, Tables S1 and S2). The corresponding current densityvoltage (JV) characteristics of these devices based on BTFPDI and fBTFPDI blends are shown in Fig. 2a, and their optimized device parameters are summarized in Table 1. In the case of PTB7-Th:BTFPDI, we can see that thermal annealing (TA) treatment dramatically increased Jsc and FF, thus leading to a remarkable PCE of 7.8% with a Voc of 0.81 V, Jsc of 16.96 mA cm2 and FF of 56.9% (Fig. S5 and Table S1). Then, a slightly improved PCE of 8.07% with a record high Jsc of 17.35 mA cm2 can be achieved after adding a small amount of additive (0.5% DIO) in the solution of PBT7-Th:BTFPDI (Fig. 2a). To the best of our knowledge, the observed Jsc of 17.35 mA cm2 in PTB7-Th:BTFPDI is the highest value reported to date for PDI-based OSCs. In contrast, when the fused derivative fBTFPDI was used as the electron acceptor, TA treatment delivered a lower PCE of 5.47% with a slight increased Voc of 0.82 V, which also matched well with the upshifted LUMO level of fBTFPDI. In addition to TA, a small volume fraction of DIO (0.25%) is subsequently applied, leading to an enhanced PCE of 5.89% with a Voc of 0.83 V, and an inferior Jsc of 12.30 mA cm2 (Table 1). As discussed above, it is known that the ring-fusion strategy has shown significant efficiency improvement in previous PDI systems,30,32,39 but it is a counterexample in the case of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI-based OSCs. For the PTB7-Th:fBTFPDI device, its unexpected efficiency loss is mainly due to the remarkably lower current density. The corresponding external quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI devices are shown in Fig. 2b. The fullerene-free PTB7-Th:BTFPDI device showed a broad photo response from 300 to 780 nm with a maximum EQE value of 79.2%, while the fBTFPDI device exhibited a similar but weaker photo response profile with a maximum EQE of 64.1%, which can partly explain the significant current density difference in the photovoltaic devices. In addition, the integrated Jsc values of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI blends based on the EQE spectra are 16.73 mA cm2 and 12.25 mA cm2, respectively, which are well consistent with the Jsc values obtained from JV measurement (Table 1).

To compare the exciton dissociation, charge recombination and collection properties of the two fullerene-free OSCs with BTFPDI and fBTFPDI as electron acceptors, we investigated the exciton dissociation probabilities (Pdiss), the maximum exciton generation rate (Gmax), and the dependence of Jsc on light intensity according to the reported method,44 and the corresponding results are shown in Fig. 3a and b. The saturation currents (Jsat) of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI devices are calculated to be 21.13 mA cm2 and 15.16 mA cm2, respectively. Accordingly, a charge dissociation probability (Pdiss) of 82.1% was achieved for the PTB7-Th:BTFPDI device, which was comparable with that of the fBTFPDI device (82.7%) under short-circuit and maximum power conditions. In addition, the fullerene-free OSCs based on the PTB7-Th:BTFPDI blend delivered a high Gmax value of 1.08 1028 m3 s1, whereas the fBTFPDI device gave a Gmax value of 8.13 1027 m3 s1, which are in good agreement with the higher molar extinction coefficient (6.7 104 M1 cm1) of the BTFPDI blend and the record high current density (17.35 mA cm2) of the BTFPDI device. On the other hand, the incident light intensity dependence of Jsc was also investigated to have a better understanding of the carrier recombination mechanism in the two fullerene-free OSCs. As shown in Fig. 3b, the PTB7-Th:BTFPDI device showed a higher slope of 0.987 than the fBTFPDI-based device (S = 0.973), indicating that relatively weak bimolecular recombination occurred in the BTFPDI device. To gain deeper insight into the charge transport properties of these fullerene-free OSCs, we further measured the hole and electron mobilities of BTFPDI and fBTFPDI devices by using the space-charge-limited current (SCLC) method (Fig. 3c and d). For the PTB7-Th:BTFPDI blend, the hole mobility (h) and electron mobility (e) are estimated to be 6.4 104 cm2 V1 s1 and 4.3 104 cm2 V1 s1, respectively, with a balanced charge transport (h/e of 1.49), while the PTB7-Th:fBTFPDI blend shows a similar and more balanced charge carrier transport (h/e of 1.20). Consequently, BTFPDI and fBTFPDI showed similar exciton dissociation probabilities and balanced charge transport, but the higher exciton generation rate and weaker bimolecular recombination for BTFPDI devices synergistically contributed to the higher current density and better device performance.

To gain more insight into the molecular packing of both the pristine and blend films, grazing incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) was carried out, and the 2D GIWAXS patterns and the corresponding line-cut scattering profiles in the in-plane (IP) and out-of-plane (OOP) directions are shown in Fig. 4. The neat BTFPDI film has an obvious (010) diffraction peak at 1.45 1 with a d-spacing of 4.33 in the OOP direction and the corresponding (100) peak is observed at 0.28 1 with a d-spacing of 22.44 , indicating a preferential face-on orientation in the neat BTFPDI film. However, the pristine fBTFPDI film shows a random molecular orientation and the weaker (010) and lamellar (100) diffraction peaks are found at 1.45 1 (d-spacing of 4.33 ) and 0.36 1 (d-spacing of 17.45 ), respectively. Similarly, the non-fused BTFPDI showed strong packing behavior compared to the more planar fBTFPDI as observed from the results of GIWAXS, which is consistent with the temperature-dependent absorption results, but opposite to the DFT calculations. In order to further compare the molecular packing orientations in BTFPDI and fBTFPDI, analysis of scattering intensity distribution as a function of the azimuth angle was performed (Fig. 4c). It can be seen that both the BTFPDI pristine film and PTB7-Th:BTFPDI blend film exhibited strong scattering intensities in comparison with the fBTFPDI pristine film and PTB7-Th:fBTFPDI blend film, which means that there exists stronger molecular packing in BTFPDI pristine and its blend films. On account of this we can find that the BTFPDI and fBTFPDI neat films show entirely different molecular packing orientations (preferential face-on vs. amorphous properties), implying that the molecular orientation and crystallization behavior of the resulting molecules can be manipulated significantly via the ring-fusion strategy. As for the neat PTB7-Th film, a strong stacking diffraction peak at 1.57 1 with a d-spacing of 4.0 is only observed in the OOP direction (Fig. S7).


Fig. 4 Two-dimensional (2D) GIWAXS patterns (a), scattering profiles (b) and azimuth intensity profiles (c) for the pristine films of BTFPDI and fBT-FPDI, and blend films of PTB7-Th:BTFPDI and PTB7-Th:fBTFPDI.

From Fig. 4, it can find that the PTB7-Th/BTFPDI blend film did not show any obvious change in the crystallization behavior of BTFPDI, but a significantly pronounced (010) stacking peak at 1.57 101 is observed in the OOP direction, which is from PTB7-Th and expected to be beneficial for the vertical charge transport in the blend film. However, the addition of PTB7-Th led to a significant change in the molecular orientation of fBTFPDI, and the change of the stacking orientation from an isotropic crystallite orientation in the neat film to a predominant face-on orientation in the blend film can be observed from the 2D patterns and the azimuth intensity profiles. In addition, the scattering intensity of (010) peaks of the BTFPDI blend film is much stronger than that of the fBTFPDI blend, indicating that there is a highly ordered packing and a preference for a face-on oriented stacking in PTB7-Th:BTFPDI, which can partly explain the significantly enhanced current density obtained from the BTFPDI-based fullerene-free OSC. On the other hand, the values of the stacking coherence length of the two blend films were calculated from the full width at half maximum of the OOP (010) peaks using the Scherrer equation.45 The PTB7-Th/BTFPDI blend exhibits a slightly larger (010) coherence length than the PTB7-Th and fBTFPDI blend (8.05 nm vs. 7.43 nm), which is beneficial to enhance the molecular packing and charge transport in fullerene-free OSCs.

In order to understand the effect of the surface morphology of the photoactive layer, which is crucial to the device performance, atomic force microscopy (AFM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were performed, and the corresponding images are shown in Fig. 5. It can be found that the photoactive layers based on BTFPDI (Fig. 5a and b) and fBTFPDI (Fig. 5d and e) acceptors were highly uniform and smooth, and no large fibrillary aggregates were observed in the AFM images, implying good miscibility and morphology compatibility between PTB7-Th and FPDI acceptors. Additionally, the root-mean-square surface roughness (Rq) value for the BTFPDI blend film was calculated to be 0.877 nm, while a slightly increased Rq value of 0.931 nm was observed in the fBTFPDI film because of its more rigid molecular configuration. A similar phenomenon can also be observed in the TEM images (Fig. 5c and f); more defined nanoscale phase separation features and appropriate domain sizes are seen in the two films, which may contribute to the promotion of the exciton dissociation efficiency and charge transport properties, therefore resulting in impressive photovoltaic performance of fullerene-free OSC devices. These GIWAXS, AFM and TEM results helped explain the significant differences in device performance, especially Jsc and FF, resulting from controlling the molecular geometry of FPDI fullerene-free acceptors with a built-in quasi-2D configuration.

In conclusion, we have designed and synthesized two novel electron acceptors (BTFPDI and fBTFPDI) with different steric configurations for fullerene-free OSCs. The density functional theory calculations show that BTFPDI preserved the suitable twisted connection between BT and FPDI units, preventing large undesirable phase aggregation and crystallinity in the blend film. After ring fusion, the molecular geometry of fBTFPDI could be significantly manipulated and formed a relatively rigid planar configuration, enhancing the intermolecular interaction, molecular ordered packing and also symmetrically aligned the FPDI moieties. The optimal fullerene-free conventional OSCs based on PTB7-Th:BTFPDI delivered an impressive PCE of 8.07% with a champion Jsc of 17.35 mA cm2, which is among the highest current density reported to date for PDI-based photovoltaic devices. On the other hand, when using the fused molecule (fBTFPDI) as the electron acceptor, the best devices with 0.25% DIO treatment showed an inferior PCE of 5.89% with a significantly lower Jsc of 12.30 mA cm2, which is attributed to the lower molar extinction coefficient, stronger bimolecular recombination and unfavorable packing properties of the fBTFPDI-based blend film. Our preliminary investigations illustrated that geometrically controlled FPDI molecules have great potential and could be promising electron acceptor candidates for high performance fullerene-free OSCs.

Experimental section

Materials

Monomer FPDI-Br and 5,5-bis(trimethylstannyl)-2,2-bithiophene were synthesized using known procedures.4648 PTB7-Th (Mw: 47000, polydispersity index: 2.0) was purchased from Derthon Optoelectronic Materials Science Technology Co Ltd. All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification unless otherwise specified.

Synthesis of BTFPDI

A solution of FPDI-Br (400 mg, 0.27 mmol) and 5,5-bis(trimethylstannyl)-2,2-bithiophene (53.6 mg, 0.11 mmol) monomers in toluene (40 mL) was degassed under N2 three times. Then, tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0) (10.1 mg, 0.01 mmol) and tri(o-tolyl)phosphine (20.2 mg, 0.07 mmol) were quickly added, and the solution mixture was further evacuated and refilled with N2 three times and then was heated at 110 C for 12 h under nitrogen. After cooling to room temperature, the resultant solution was removed under reduced pressure and the product was purified by column chromatography on silica gel (petroleum ether/dichloromethane, 2:1 to 1:2, v/v) to yield dark brown solid BTFPDI (270 mg, 0.09 mmol, 82%). 1H NMR (600 MHz, CDCl3) 10.79 (d, 8H), 10.44 (s, 1H), 9.41 (d, 8H), 7.54 (d, J = 8.6 Hz, 1H), 7.36 (d, J = 2.4 Hz, 2H), 7.28 (d, J = 10.0 Hz, 4H), 7.11 (ddd, J = 8.5, 2.1 Hz, 2H), 5.48 (d, 8H), 2.48 (d, 16H), 2.03 (d, J = 7.0 Hz, 16H), 1.31 (dd, 99H), 0.960.81 (m, 46H). 13C NMR (151 MHz, CDCl3, ): 149.21, 149.18, 147.69, 147.64, 147.04, 146.62, 145.47, 138.96, 138.94, 138.77, 138.70, 138.54, 138.48, 136.91, 136.21, 135.32, 134.88, 134.11, 134.08, 134.05, 134.00, 127.47, 127.37, 127.35, 126.95, 126.86, 126.62, 125.84, 124.48, 124.39, 124.01, 123.50, 122.73, 119.13, 119.02, 118.90, 35.00, 34.89, 34.55, 34.46, 32.49, 31.95, 31.89, 31.53, 31.46, 30.22, 30.16, 29.72, 29.68, 29.38, 29.36, 26.81, 22.71, 22.64, 14.14.

Synthesis of fBTFPDI

In a photochemical reactor covered with a thick layer of aluminium foil, BTFPDI (150 mg, 0.05 mmol) and iodine (60 mg) were dissolved in 360 mL of toluene. Then, the solution mixture was photoirradiated using a 450 W mercury lamp for 36 h with cooling. The reaction mixture was terminated until the starting material could not be detected by thin layer chromatography (TLC).
