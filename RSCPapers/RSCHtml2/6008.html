Stabilization of CsPbBr3 quantum dots for photocatalysis, imaging and optical sensing in water and biological medium: a review

Abstract

Of all the quantum dots, CsPbBr3 perovskite quantum dots (CPQDs) have been extensively studied for sensing and photocatalysis because of their desired photophysical properties such as optical absorption, high quantum yield and narrow emission bands. However their photo, thermal and aqueous instability has limited their use in real time applications. The review provides a fundamental understanding of this material's intrinsic behavior towards these degradation factors and discusses elaborately about the various strategies being implemented namely surface passivation through (a) ligand exchange/doping, (b) coating silica shells and (c) developing polymer encapsulations to tweak its behaviour for water related applications. A detailed track record against our focussed set of qualities for developing water stable CPQDs will help us look ahead in the right direction. It provides an assessment of the figures of merit of these tweaked or stabilized CPQDs, to check the reliability of this material for photocatalysis and biosensing in water. The insights offered here however open scope, not only for developing various other water stable, lead free and double perovskites which are environmentally more benign and useful, but would also give perspectives to validate their developed schemes towards the use of these materials for water related applications.

1. Introduction

Perovskite QDs (PQDs) resemble chalcogenide QDs (CQDs) very closely but have much improved optical properties. A higher quantum yield with narrow FWHM (full width of half maximum) allows an intense fluorescence emission and the confined size range in these perovskite quantum dots results in stronger quantum confinement leading to narrow emission bands.1 These factors and the faster photoluminescence (PL) decay of CQDs that occurs at higher emission energies, all together make PQDs attractive over CQDs. The highly volatile nature of organic (A) cations in these PQDs of the ABX3 structure is known to result in faster degradation than that of the all-inorganic PQDs.2 An element such as cesium in the ‘A’ site instead of an organic cation, that forms CsPbBr3 PQDs, has shown enhanced overall lifetimes.3 In addition cesium perovskites have higher stability and improved fluorescence emission with a tunable band gap energy4 that makes these all-inorganic PQDs impressive towards photon energy conversion devices5,6 like solar cells, light emitting diodes,7 optical sensors and photodetectors.8 Subsequently the tunable cationic and anionic composition of these inorganic PQDs gives the advantage of high optoelectronic and photophysical properties.9 ‘A’ site cations are electronically inactive.10 Electronically active ‘B’ site cations form a lower conduction band from divalent metal ions, and can be replaced accordingly with cations of similar atomic radii.11 ‘X’ site anions can be oxygen or halides such as chloride, bromide, and iodide that form an upper valence band and they are also responsible for the quantum confinement.12,13 Different compositions of perovskites having different cations and halides show different electronic,14 redox15 and polarization16 properties. Through various studies, it is suggested that among the all inorganic perovskite compositions, CsPbBr3 quantum dots (CPQDs) have relatively greater luminescence, and are stable and less volatile.2 The wide bandgap of the CPQDs of around 2.3 eV, with a high absorption coefficient and high charge carrier mobility enhances the efficiency of CPQDs towards various photo applications.17 Also, the high quantum yield and sensitivity17 of these CPQDs give way for their utilization as fluorescent probes to detect various analytes. Recently environmental applications like photocatalysis18 which involves water splitting19 and bioimaging have been explored for these CPQDs.

Accordingly, enormous efforts were made to develop CPQDs with different shapes, sizes and structures by various precursors and synthesis methods such as hydrothermal methods,20 hot injection,21 chemical precipitation, spin coating22 and chemical vapor deposition.23 However, obtaining water stable and compatible CPQDs is quite challenging although one can find well established reports on the successful attempts of making chalcogenide QDs (CdS, CdTe, CdSe, and PbS) which are water stable and biocompatible. Perovskites are basically salts and can react with air, light, moisture and heat effortlessly, leading to degradation if they are not packaged well.24 Stability is the first complex challenge in the domain of these PQDs and if achieved can revolutionize their applications.

Similar strategies performed for CQDs to achieve stability and compatibility have been extended to PQDs but with little success.25 Some of the strategies are very complicated and challenging.26Fig. 1 shows the so far attempted various strategies used for different applications put into a triangle, with techniques at the edges namely coating with polymers/micelles,27–30 surface modification with ligand exchange31,32 and encapsulation within silica and other shells to make CPQDs applicable to various applications depicted at the edges of another triangle.33–35

With all the plethora of knowledge, lead halide perovskites are yet to achieve a satisfying outcome in terms of aqueous stability and photostability which leaves huge room for improvements at the material level. CPQDs possess exceptional fluorescence properties, a longer decay time, and a higher molar extinction coefficient, which warrant their higher sensitivity in photo applications, particularly photovoltaics, photocatalysis and sensing applications that include bioimaging. The review articles published so far present diverse perspectives.36,37 Many of them focus on material synthesis and the properties of CsPbBr3.38,39 Few reviews have been covered in the field of photovoltaics of CPQDs,17 but here we wish to focus narrowly on the water related applications for which the stability of CPQDs is the qualifying parameter. Just a handful of them have dedicated only a small portion of their review on the stability on CPQDs40 or on applications namely photocatalysis41 or sensing in organic medium.42 Therefore, a review on CPQDs for applications that use water or biological medium is important. So, we believe important developments that have come about on the stabilization of CPQDs by various strategies and their utilization in the above said water related applications need elaborate assessment. Thus, we discuss the changes observed in specific intrinsic properties of these CPQDs (when these stabilizing strategies are applied) in terms of (a) figures of merit required to be qualified as a good optoelectronic material for photocatalysis and biosensing applications (b) to arrive at conclusions on the reliability of these modified CPQDs for further research and development. The insights offered here open scope for developing various other water stable lead free and double perovskites which are environmentally more benign and useful.

2. Unstable CPQDs: factors and mechanisms

2.1. Brief overview of the effect of air and water on the degradation of CPQDs

Moisture is the presence of water in diffused form and when it comes in contact with water it causes structural degradation of CPQDs.43 Water molecules penetrate into the inner inorganic framework of CsPbX3 to trigger off the intake of two hydrogen and one oxygen molecules forming lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2).44 The formation of such lead hydroxide (Pb(OH)2) induces structural (inner phase) degradation of CPQDs.45 (Pb (OH)2) further decomposes to form lead oxide PbO and H2O molecules resulting in further hydration thereby allowing the entire crystal structure to collapse and degrade completely.

CPQDs when exposed to environmental oxygen results in the physisorption of oxygen (O2) molecules on the surface, leading to oxidation and formation of the same PbO. The adsorption of molecular oxygen on the surface of the electropositive element occurs with a weak electrostatic bond. This triggers degradation as molecular oxygen directly adsorbed on the metal ions eventually separates as metal oxide or hydroxide. The extreme electronegativity of oxygen, then bromide and chloride ions, causes the shift of higher affinity of the central metal (Pb) cations to form Pb–O chemical bonds. Oxygen (O2) interacts with the conduction band of lead and this encourages the breakage of PbBr3 bonds to form PbO.46 Due to lead oxide formation the electron from the conduction band of CPQDs is transferred to oxygen molecules, leading to the formation of superoxide anions on the surface that alters the band gap of CPQDs. This can also lead to the formation of trap states as shown in Fig. 2a leading to charge recombination and exciton decay thus causing photodegradation of CPQDs.47

2.2. Influence of thermal energy and light energy on the degradation of CPQDs

Thermal degradation was studied by subjecting CPQDs to different temperatures and complete degradation of CPQDs was observed at 125 °C.49 High thermal energy gained by CPQDs leads to initial degradation due to the dissolution of ligands and results in an uncovered core and this slowly causes disruption of bonds between cations and anions. Consequently, structural degradation as well as fluorescence degradation occurs, thereby resulting in loss of the entire property of the material.49,50 The change in the PL intensity of CPQDs due to applied temperature is shown in Fig. 3.

Photodecomposition of CPQDs initiated by photons on continuously illuminating CPQDs causes fast anionic movement of bromide ions. Light can also cause continuous and rapid transfer of electrons from the conduction band of Pb2+ ions to the valence band of Br–ions and affect the quantum yield (QY) of the fluorescent material along with the simultaneous degradation of its structure.51 Furthermore, the superoxide formation due to oxidation will trigger the degradation by the defected band structure that instigates the faster transfer of electrons even under ambient light conditions. This simultaneous action of oxide formation and illumination under ambient light results in the reduction of the overall lifetime of CPQDs and causes the complete photo-degradation.52 In addition, other substantial changes that occur during illumination includes ion migration and halide segregation due to a photon/thermal effect leading to compositional degradation. As mentioned earlier defects formed as a consequence of these processes can also lead to severe instability in CPQDs.53

A different mechanism occurs under UV irradiation. The high photon energy and momentum transferred to CPQDs facilitate ‘Ostwald ripening’ which means that small sized dots are consumed by larger dots resulting in structural and morphological changes, thus altering the optical properties.54 The photons hit the surface of CPQDs and change the arrangement of unstable surface atoms leading to structural deformation and size. However, Huang et al. have reported positive changes in the nature of CPQDs upon UV light illumination. They developed CPQDs for LED lights and the group studied facts behind the degradation of CPQDs under UV light conditions. Under UV light irradiation a positive phenomenon called photo annealing occurs and this allows reconstruction of the structure by removing the dangling bonds and unsaturated atoms on the surface. The diffraction peaks became sharp and strong signifying better crystallinity. A further increase in radiation power triggers aggregation of CPQDs and this is indicated by structure deformation to an oval shape (from the sharp cubic structures) as shown in Fig. 4a. Furthermore, the surface trap states also increase and cause PL loss as shown in Fig. 4b48 and the structural deformation from cubic to deformed CPQDs due to UV light irradiation was clearly seen in the HRTEM images shown in Fig. 4c. It is hence clear that factors and pathways that initiate and cause degradation of CPQDs shown in Fig. 4d are many and inevitable to avoid. Most of these unwanted reactions occur on the surface and surface engineering to protect this surface has tremendous potential as this opens a plethora of applications where these stabilized CPQDs can be utilized due to the obvious optoelectronic properties they possess.

3. Strategies to protect CPQDs

Considering the factors responsible for the degradation of CPQDs, various methods have been designed and developed to protect CPQDs from these factors. Surface engineering techniques essentially performed to induce chemical modifications on the surface can be categorized mainly into silica coatings, polymer encapsulation and surface passivation. These modifications may on the other hand result in undesirable crystal defects and structural changes in CPQDs. Accordingly, this section is focused on these methods and challenges addressed thereafter to upkeep their properties.

3.1. Surface passivation

Passivation of ligands or modifying ligands to protect CPQDs from degradation is well studied and is usually done by ligand exchange (Scheme 1). The other method is the direct passivation of the core, which in turn has also reflected in additional benefits like higher crystallization, and better PLQY and stability. Apart from ligand exchange techniques, oxide shell formation is also an effective strategy to develop environmentally stable CPQDs.

The effect of factors like oxygen, water, light, and temperature on CPQDs is observed to significantly reduce when ligands are appropriately engineered to resist these factors. Ligands used in the synthesis of perovskite QDs have a strong influence on the stability and dispersibility of CPQDs in various solvent media.55 Long chain ligands like oleic acid (OA) and oleyl amine (OLA) are established capping ligands that are used mostly to prepare CPQDs.56 Cubic and orthorhombic CPQDs have a rare crystallographic arrangement of cations and anions, which are interconnected and stabilized using these hydrophobic ligands. This long chain hydrophobic ligand present on CPQDs is extremely sensitive to exposed air, water, light and temperature.44,57,58 These ligands are thus easily degraded by the above-mentioned environmental factors.

3.1.1. Ligand exchange. Ligand engineering to achieve compatible QDs via “ligand exchange” is an established strategy among chalcogenide quantum dots.59,60 Researchers have extended this strategy to perovskite quantum dots too61,62 and have used various kinds of ligands with different functional groups to make them compatible/stable in different systems.63 Surface passivation through ligand exchange (better passivating ligands) is mainly beneficial in optoelectronic devices such as solar cells,64 LEDs65 and optical sensors.66

Ligands could be short chain-monofunctional, bidentate, bifunctional, multidentate and so on, based on the different properties and applications required. High molecular length ligands would result in larger inter particle spacing leading to lower charge/energy transfer67 and hence inducing short chain alkyl ligands68 are in demand for photo applications. Ligand exchange with short chain ligands like decanoic acid (DA),31 cinnamic acid,69 2-hexyldecanoic acid, and phosphoric acid62 allow better charge transfer, lower recombinations and better carrier lifetimes. However, they appear to enhance the stability only by a couple of hours and are not compatible in various solvent systems.

Bifunctional ligands that possess highly reactive sulfonic,70 and carboxylic acid groups donate electrons, necessary to bind with uncoordinated metal ions, in addition to providing functionalization of the core on the other hand such that it can be allowed to tag with any molecule namely analytes in sensors, and electron transport layers in solar cells, thus giving it necessary compatibility. Ligands that contain bi-carboxylic acid groups namely succinic acid (SA)71 have been found to exhibit enhanced stability and optical properties.72 However, ligand exchange with folic acid, glutamic acid, and EDTA used to substitute OLA/OA have resulted in futile efforts, since its binding energy was weaker than OA binding to CPQDs.

Bidentate ligands have resulted in both core stability and ligand stability. For example zwitterionic ligands like alpha amino butyric acid, iminodibenzoic acid,72,73 and lysine allow both functional groups in them to bind to Pb2+ ions enhancing fluorescence and stability.71 The Zhang group in 2020 reported air stable CPQDs by incorporating an L-lysine ligand via a spin coating method.74 Initially L-lysine PbBr3 was coated on the substrate and dried, and it was found to have a good porous structure with enlarged grain size and lower grain boundaries (as observed in TEM images). CsBr solution when coated on this was taken through the pores to form a good CPQD perovskite film. The XRD patterns reveal high crystallinity of L-lysine CPQDs by showing a positive shift in diffraction peaks. Also, the binding energy of L-lysine to CPQDs was much stronger than OA binding to CPQDs. L-lysine possessed two amino groups and two carboxyl groups that encouraged hydrogen bonding. This efficiently passivates the defects that arise in bonding of Pb2+ (Cs+) with Br−. This is also reflected in reduced recombinations and enhanced carrier lifetimes. The developed surface passivated L-lysine CPQD material showed good environmental stability for more than 45 days (in exposed air with high humidity) and also 500 h under illumination of daylight.

Perfluoro decanoic acid (PFDA) and decanoic acid (DA) ligands were used to increase the thermal stability and to enhance the resistivity of CPQDs towards polar solvents.31 The strong electronegativity of F− played a significant role in this. Compared to the conventional oleic acid ligand (OA) and the proposed ligand DA, PFDA ligand exchanged CPQDs showed remarkable stability due to the decrease of bromide vacancies. Vacancies on the surface were more occupied by the PFDA ligand than by OA and DA. The fluorocarbon component easily coordinates with metal ions causing reduction of surface defects. Their thermal stability study also showed good enhancement. PFDA CPQDs were subjected to 100 °C for 4 h. The PL intensity was constant at 92.1% for PFDA-CPQDs, while it showed a drastic reduction for pristine CPQDs and DA-CPQDs. Resistivity in polar solvents (ethanol) followed the same trend with PL maintained at 36.5% for PFDA-CPQDs while it reduced to 9.53% and 13.6% for the other two. Thus, surface passivation using a PFDA ligand conferred good photo and thermal stability together with solvent resistivity, helping in the construction of stable electroluminescent devices. Cao et al. in 2020 showed self-enhanced electrochemiluminescence (ECL) in tri-n-octyl phosphine (TOP) capped CPQDs.32 The TOP ligand replacing the OA ligand, which is much similar to CQDs, is expected to bind better with Pb2+ ions and this gave a beneficial morphology. The binding energy of TOP with metal ions was found to be higher than that of pristine CPQDs. Pristine CPQDs showed a PLQY of 42% and after surface passivation using TOP the PLQY was increased to 55.2%. The stability of the synthesized material was studied in air and water, and complete degradation of pristine CPQDs was observed after 8 h but the TOP capped CPQDs show 77% of PL emission for twenty days. To maintain the PL emission of TOP CPQDs, they were stored at 4 °C and storing at a much lower temperature helps in preventing aggregation. However, the acute toxicity of phosphine ligands has hindered their research and application further.

Multidentate ligand capping was implemented on the surface of SA CPQDs using N-hydroxy succinimide in water (NHS-water) to form an NHS-SA CPQD which enhances the fluorescence emission in water and the PL stability of NHS-SA CPQDs in water remained undisturbed for 7 days with a gradual reduction of fluorescence emission and complete degradation after 14 days.75 Thus, the desired functionalization on the ligand of the core on the other hand strongly helps in enhancing its stability in the corresponding solvent systems.

So, surface passivation by a ligand exchange process is a simple mechanism that helps in inducing passivation and hence enhances surface compatibility, protecting CPQDs from degradation in air, water, light and heat. It is important to note that the binding energy of the new ligand with the core must be much stronger than the existing system. Short chain ligands containing specific functional groups like COOH, NH2 and PO4 are preferred, but bifunctional, and multidentate ligands can (a) stabilize the ligand in different environments/solvent systems and also (b) stabilize the core (Pb2+ and Br−) thus reducing the density of trap states which helps in preventing non-radiative recombinations and exciton decay in the material.76 However still the possibility of such ligand capped CPQDs being exposed to light radiation thus affecting the surface atoms and loosening the surface binding ligands cannot be avoided. So, this kind of disadvantage has been overcome by complete encapsulation of CPQDs within a shell using silica precursors, polymeric beads and polymer coatings. 3.1.2. Core–shell heterostructure. Developing oxide shells around quantum dots (core) is another easy strategy to enhance their stability and prevent their degradation due to environmental air and moisture.76 Strong shell growth around CPQDs by semiconductor materials like SnO2, TiO2, ZnO, and Al2O3 acts as a protective barrier and also induces new/changes in band gap properties that enhance the optoelectronics of CPQDs.77,78 The thickness of the shell around PQDs can be altered by manipulating the reaction temperature and precursors used.76 TiO2 coated CPQDs were synthesized by a wet chemical approach using titanium butoxide (TBOT)77 and the core–shell composite showed good photostability in water for 80 days while the pristine CPQDs degraded within 2 days.

Likewise, the zinc oxide shell developed around CPQDs by a non-stoichiometric solution phase method using zinc acetate and lithium hydroxide79 rendered aqueous stability for a few days in water. The shells helped in the passivation of uncoordinated sites on the CPQDs, also confining the electrons to the core and enhancing the optical emission and absorption of CPQDs and they can be referred to as type-I heterostructures. Reports on chalcogenide heterostructures indicate type-II wherein CPQDs/CdS80 and CsPbBr3/ZnS81 have staggered band alignment unlike in type-I (straddling band alignment) where the shell acts as an absorber and a passivating layer that effectively reduces free charge carriers from reaching the trap states located on the particle surface. In such a case it is very important to have a shell material with a wider bandgap than that of core PQDs to render a stable core–shell structure. This is also an alternate method to obtain a broad range of absorption with a stronger redox ability.

3.1.3. Doped CPQDs. Doping CPQDs with appropriate metal and non-metal ions has added to their stability. Metal ions like Ag+, Bi3+, Ce3+, Zn2+, Mn2+ and Cd2+82–86 and anions Cl2 and I287,88 have helped in the passivation of uncoordinated Pb2+ or Br− ions, thus strongly confining the atoms on the surface and electrons within the core. This not only enhances PL but also stability. Doping with anions has led to the formation of mixed halides, which undergo undesirable phase segregation with light, leading to instability of the phases they created.89 Doping of trivalent ions at the B site, e.g. in CsPbI3 done by Pradhan et al.,90,91 increases the interaction of ions with the crystal frame which may add to the stability of the perovskite structure avoiding the need for functionalization, and does not necessarily compromise the optoelectronic performance. However even cation doped CPQD modifications have only helped with little more environmental resistance in general and still possess all the undesirable properties that do not allow them to be dispersed in water or biological medium.

3.2. Silica coatings

This section discusses the use of inorganic silica (SiO2) as a coating agent to protect the inner materials of CPQDs from degradation. Silica coatings using hydrophilic silane precursors such as tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS),92 (3-aminopropyl)trimethoxysilane (APTMS),93 tetramethoxysilane (TMOS),94 tetramethyl orthosilicate (TEOS)94 and (3-aminopropyl) triethoxysilane (APTES)95 efficiently serve as obstructing layers to moisture and water molecules. They form shells around CPQDs via their functional groups and protect them from degradation.96 They also act as protective bags by adsorbing/absorbing CPQDs through the successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR).97 The biggest advantage of silica precursors is that they are compatible with both toluene98 and water99 systems.

These silica coatings can be implemented through amine linkages (with cations) or by growing perovskite nanocrystals in mesoporous silica templates.100 The as-prepared composites appeared somewhat stable. These are powdery materials with more silica and are achieved by complex synthesis involving high costs. CPQDs for cell culture applications were stabilized using SiO2 wrap to form a CPQD/SiO2 composite by the Ding research group. Silica spheres were developed using the TEOS precursor and the prepared silica spheres were mixed with CPQD solution in the presence of ammonium hydroxide to prevent aggregation. The PLQY stability of the encapsulated CPQDs remains the same even after 30 days due to the rigid composite of CPQD/SiO2, but the PLQY of pristine CPQDs was reduced to 0% after 30 days.101 Sun et al. worked on developing stable CPQD LED lights using APTES in a one pot synthesis. Silane precursor APTES effectively binds with PbX3, via the amine group which helps in passivating the surface and maintaining the original PLQY even under adverse conditions. The silyl ether groups of silane precursors hydrolyzed to form a cross linked matrix to cover CPQDs in order protect them from thermal decomposition and oxidation. The silica precursors underwent hydrolysis to wrap CPQDs into the shell. Encapsulation of CPQDs enhanced the device lifetime sufficiently under atmospheric conditions.102 Since the hydrolysis reaction is water-assisted, PQDs will be decomposed by water before SiO2 generation.103 Despite the challenges, many research groups made appreciable contributions. One more amine functionalized silica precursor APTMS was used to achieve a stable emission wavelength of 512 nm for light emitting diode applications. Silica coated CPQDs were highly stable for 3 months and after 3 months the PLQY was reduced by 5% but the PLQY of pristine CPQDs was degraded and their fluorescence reduced at the rate of 10% within 6 h when exposed to high humidity and temperature.104 CPQDs passivated using TMOS encapsulation by in situ synthesis cause the enlargement of CPQD cubic crystals, which was confirmed by TEM and XRD patterns. The resulting down conversion showed red shift emission by 4.5 nm. The stability studies of the encapsulated CPQDs in water show stable fluorescence emission even after 3 h. But complete thermal degradation of these silica coated on CPQDs was observed at 120 °C. The developed material was however very stable towards a bright emission LED light for 13 h and is efficient for display devices.105 The same TMOS precursor used in toluene medium gave CPQD/SiO2 core–shells and these were well sonicated in water to show their stability. The orthorhombic crystal structure with a clear silica layer around the nanoparticle was confirmed by TEM and EDAX elemental mapping. However only 40 minutes of stability on vigorous ultra-sonication was observed for silica capped CPQDs over 16 minutes for uncapped ones indicating the role of silica as a protective layer (to some extent). The fluorescence stability range of CPQD/SiO2 and CPQD/SiO2 in water is shown in Fig. 5a.25 Similarly, CPQDs were embedded into TMOS silica shells to form a CPQD@SiO2 core shell through a wet chemical method. The overall reaction time was from 24 h and this enhanced PL emission of CPQD@SiO2 in water was due to the reduction of surface defects upon encapsulation. But the CPQDs without the SiO2 shell degraded within a few minutes in water.106 The (3-mercaptopropyl) trimethoxy silane (MPTMS) precursor was used to form a shell around CPQDs through Pb–S bonding107 and this shows a good PLQY of ≈78% with excellent structural integrity. 50% of fluorescence reduction occurs over a span of 20 days unlike pristine CPQDs which drops to 0% in water within minutes. MPTMS molecules tightly adsorb onto PQDs through Pb–S bonding. These bound molecules decrease the interfacial energy between the PQDs and silica by providing additional steric stabilization, thereby avoiding phase separation between the PQDs and silica. Different from all the above, the silane molecules first physically adsorb on the surface of PQDs, then hydrolyze and condense to form silica. The LED developed from this nano silica composite shows good operative stability in water for up to 15 days.

The combination of two silica precursors through steric hindrance forms a dual-shell that helps in initiating the process of nucleation of CPQDs inside the silica shells, via the possible N–Pb linkages108 as shown in Fig. 5b. Thus, the dual linkages with silica and Pb2+ of CPQDs are one method to develop a surface that does not affect CPQDs upon exposure to moisture. Recently, in 2020, Qui et al. also developed stable CPQDs by a SILAR method, implementing silica coatings by combining two silica precursors APTES and TEOS thus forming a dual layered silica shell. The mesoporous structure of TEOS adsorbs CPQDs within pores and forms a complex amine linkage between the inner core of PbBr3 crystals and the shell of TEOS, which is again surrounded by an outer layer of APTES.97 PbBr3 crystals are then converted to CPQDs by a SILAR technique. Encapsulated CPQDs show 89% PL intensity and photo-stability studies under UV light show retention of the PL intensity up to 72 h, while the PL intensity of pristine CPQDs is reduced to 0% within 30 h. Even though there is an advantage in increasing the stability after encapsulation, the presence of excess amine after encapsulation causes structural defects which cause changes in the crystallinity of the material which could be detected in XRD patterns.

Introducing a polymer cover over the silica shell (formed by TMOS) using a pegylated phospholipid (PEG-DSPE) has enhanced the photostability.109 CPQD@SiO2 formed via the LARP method using TMOS was embedded in PEG-DSPE micelles through the hydrolysis process to form a CPQD/SiO2/PEG-DSPE composite. The composite was also dispersed in different biological buffers with different pH values from 3 to 8 and it was noticed that the RCPQD composite was stable in the pH range from 5 to 8, which covers the intercellular and extracellular pH of normal cells and cancer cells.

3.3. Polymer encapsulation

Polymers are more like gel matrices which can be hydrophobic, hydrophilic and also amphiphilic.110,111 Polymers naturally possess functional groups and can also incorporate desired functionalization112 into CPQDs through appropriate reactions with organic molecules. The introduction of a polymer ligand forms a metal complex with QDs by replacing the original ligand and the new polymer ligand covers the surface to prevent QDs from degradation.113 These polymer encapsulations are made in situ using polymer beads114 and polymer gels,115 which can engulf CPQDs in them, and act as a protective barrier towards all environmental conditions, including heat. Some selective polymers such as poly(styrene-butadiene-styrene) (SBS), poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA), polystyrene (PS), and poly(isobutylene-alt-maleic anhydride)116–118 can withstand very high temperatures.

Transparent and conductive polymers with good charge transport properties were tried as coatings on CPQDs and are applied in optoelectronic and photocatalysis applications. For example, a polypyrrole (PPy) conducting polymer increases the stability of CPQDs, and also simultaneously enhances the charge transport. Although there was a slight decrease in its optical fluorescence due to encapsulation, the stability remained undisturbed for 30 days in water. The photoelectric device developed with this was stable and along with benzoquinone (as an electron acceptor) the device showed large inhibition towards electron–hole recombination.119

Jiang et al. in 2019 incorporated CPQDs into polymeric nanofibers through electrospinning to develop highly stable luminescent LEDs. This group also synthesized tunable green and red CsPbX3 (X = Cl, Br, and I) LEDs by altering different halides and embedded them in different transparent polymers poly(styrene-butadiene-styrene) (SBS), poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) and polystyrene (PS), by an electrospinning method. The illustration of the biaxial electrospinning technique for fabricating core [CsPb(Br0.4I0.6)3@PS QD]/shell (CPQD@PS QD) light-emitting nanofibers is shown in Fig. 6a. The photostability of these core shells enhanced significantly after polymer coating compared to pristine CPQD QDs. The PL intensity remained undisturbed even after 3 months of storing in water96 and this opens new scope for application of these core shells in other optoelectronic applications as well.

Poly zwitterionic ligands were used for enhanced stability of CPQDs and also for their effective transfer into different solvents without losing their structure and properties. Zwitterions (such as sulfobetaine) possess both positive and negative charges and have the ability to coordinate with the metal and halide of CPQDs. Polymeric complexes are polymerized using different polymers namely octylamine (OCA), poly(isobutylene-alt-maleic anhydride) (PIMA), and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) to extend their chain lengths.13 Using the above polymers, ZW-PIMA-OCA and ZW-PIMA-PEG polymer compositions were synthesized and used for ligand exchange in OA capped CPQDs (in THF solvent). Although the phase appeared unperturbed in an open environment initially, complete structural changes and loss of fluorescence were observed after a week in both the ligand exchanged ZW-PIMA-OCA CPQDs and ZW-PIMA-PEG CPQDs. These CPQDs when transferred to acetone, ethanol, 1-propanol and 1-butanol solvents showed enhanced fluorescence and stability. This was reflected as a change in the hydrodynamic radius attributed to solvent interaction with ligand exchanged CPQDs. ZW-PIMA-OCA CPQDs in 1-butanol showed the highest PL emission and stability (for three months) under normal environmental conditions. A stability study of polymeric ligand exchanged CPQDs was performed by preserving them under atmospheric conditions in open air and at room temperature and it was observed that the photo stability of CPQDs remains unchanged for 1.5 years, the highest so far. Thus appropriately engineered zwitterionic ligand capped CPQDs showed enhanced performance in display and energy conversion devices.120

Polyethylene glycol is widely used for rendering hydrophilic surfaces. But PEG coatings are susceptible to high temperatures and cause swelling in hydrophilic solvents within seconds.121 Encapsulating CPQDs with Santa Barbara Amorphous-15 (SBA) a mesoporous silicate and its embedment in PMMA polymeric gel gave water and thermally stable CPQD nanocomposites and look promising. Rigid polymer composites polystyrene-block-poly(ethylene-ran-butylene)-block-polystyrene (PS-PEB-PS) and poly(ethylene glycol)-block-poly-(propylene glycol)-block-poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG-PPG-PEG)] were mixed with hexane containing CPQDs to form polymer encapsulated CPQDs having hydroxyl groups on the surface. The quantum yield of the CPQDs after polymer encapsulation was enhanced from 83% to 88% due to the reduction of surface defects upon encapsulation. Macroscale polymer matrices with different shapes of CPQDs were developed using poly(styrene-ethylene-butylene-styrene) (SEBS) poly(lauryl methacrylate) to enhance the stability and polarization. But without polymer encapsulation the synthesized nano cubes and nanowires completely degraded within 60 min.125

While all of these approaches have provided more resistance against environmental factors, such as air, water, and temperature, most of these methods encapsulate the PQDs into bulk or thin film polymer matrices,126,127 which are not ideal for biological applications because of their restricted fluorescence stability in entailed biological medium. Notably, the synthesis of confined nanoscale polymer–perovskite composite particles has been demonstrated but is cumbersome, involves several steps128 and typically results in micron sized particles129 due to the entanglement of high molecular weight polymer chains. For example, the Ligand Assisted Reprecipitation (LARP) method, which was conducted in the presence of polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), along with the precipitation process of polystyrene (PS), resulted in the production of water-resistant micrometer scale PQD@PS microspheres, but their photoluminescence quantum yield was limited to ≈27%.130 Similarly CPQDs loaded in poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) microspheres prepared by the LARP method131 show only a PLQY of 33%. When using the highly hydrophobic PMMA with branched poly(ethylenimine) (b-PEI), a double shell-ligand formation occurs on the PQDs.132 This renders a high PLQY of 75% (and retained by 80% in water for up to 40 days) due to the strong binding of amine rich compounds (b-PEI) with CPQDs.

Apart from the LARP process, CPQDs can be packed into crosslinked polystyrene (PS) beads via a simple swelling/shrinking strategy,133 wherein the process is carried out in toluene and hexane without any polar solvent. The PL emission of the composite remains unchanged in water (with 64.7% PLQY) for nine months. The ion exchange reaction of the perovskite is successfully averted here. So, this method essentially protects PQDs in a monodisperse nano/micron scale without fluorescence quenching. The CPQD/PANI composite [formed by using a donor (aniline)–acceptor (benzoquinone) strategy, followed by polymerization] showed excellent photo stability (with 93% PL) in water for four weeks without any structural degradation. The photoelectrochemical measurement also showed an enhanced photocurrent (due to increased conductivity) suggesting the improved charge transfer efficiency. Also, the rigid composition of polymers around CPQDs helps to maintain the structural and fluorescence stability of CPQDs compared to other methods. The broad choice offered by polymeric ligands is also promising for introducing the desired functional groups into CPQDs for bioconjugation and hence biosensing applications. 4. Property enhancements in CPQDs from an application perspective

Appropriately engineered-silica encapsulated, ligand exchanged or polymer coated CPQD perovskite nanocrystals showed sufficiently enhanced water/air stability, photostability and thermal stability, because of which they were successfully applied for a range of applications such as photovoltaics, lasing, bio-imaging, water splitting and other photocatalytic applications. But our concern was more on applications that necessitated a water/biological/natural environment as the medium.

4.1. Photocatalysis

Photocatalysis is a photochemical reaction in which light energy can accelerate the chemical reaction with the help of light sensitive semiconductor catalysts. With the required characteristics for photocatalysis,138 CPQDs qualify themselves as a good photocatalytic material with an apt electronic structure and a band gap of 2.3 eV.139

CPQDs exhibit several interesting electrical and optical properties that are best suited for the utilization of solar energy like long charge carrier diffusion lengths,140 a high absorption coefficient141 and shallow energy states from intrinsic defects. The relative positions of the CPQD conduction band (CB) and valence band (VB) are depicted with the redox potentials in Fig. 7.142 This emphasizes their remarkable reduction ability, the relative positions of their CB that are negative enough for H2 generation, CO2 reduction, and superoxide radical generation. On photoexcitation (as photocatalysts) they generate an electron hole pair that induces redox reactions on the substrate.143 The excited electron engages itself in the reduction process while the holes undergo an oxidation process, thus facilitating many environmentally important processes like deodorization, antifouling, self-cleaning, conservation, sterilization, antifogging, water treatment, CO2 and air purification, etc.

4.1.1. CO2 reduction. The photo-illumination of CPQDs generates electron–hole pairs that can be used to reduce CO2 to CO, CH4, H2, and O2. The OA ligand when replaced with tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (PF6) σ in CPQDs allows the surface to possess a negative charge that is essential to get immobilized on Ni(tpy) to form CPQD-Ni(tpy) catalysts144 and this metal complex provides sufficient catalytic sites. CPQD-Ni(tpy) catalysts result in an increase in the molar extinction coefficient and cause multi-electron transfer to significantly enhance CO2 reduction and produce CO and CH4 as presented in Fig. 8a. The resulting photocatalyst exhibits a high yield achieving 1724 μmol g−1 of CO/CH4, which is approximately 26-fold higher than that of pristine. The same group decorated CPQDs on platinum (Pt) to enhance CO2 reduction further. The Pt catalyst reduces the recombination of electron–hole pairs thus enhancing the CO2 reduction rate. CPQDs were synthesized by an anti-solvent method and platinized for 1 h under a Xe lamp using a 320 nm cut off filter.145 The ionic nature of perovskites is known to be sensitive to degradation by polar molecules; hence, Kuang et al.146 and Sun et al.147 have used low-polarity solvent (ethyl acetate) for CO2 reduction wherein the fairly weak dipole moment of the solvent does not easily allow the coordination with the CPQD crystals, causing dissociation into complexes of the ionic species. Ethyl acetate is mixed with trace amounts of water as a hole scavenger. CO2 reduction is carried out in different solvents that include toluene, ethyl acetate (EAC), isopropanol (IPA), acetonitrile (ACN), dimethyl formamide (DMF) and methanol (MeOH) proving the stability of CPQDs towards all these solvents. The orthorhombic phase of CPQDs remains undisturbed in all the above solvents. However, CO2 reduction in ethyl acetate was more effective than other solvents with an electron yield of 2.7 μmol g−1 h−1 and it gave a selectivity of 95.2%.

Ethyl acetate shows high solubility of CO2 and also the low polarity facilitating CPQD stability comparatively and this together enhances CO2 reduction. Pt as a co-catalyst doubles up the electron yield to 5.6 μmol g−1 h−1. Nevertheless, retaining a polar environment, even with the presence of small numbers of polar molecules, is a positive route towards the stability and equilibrium of the process.

Reports on the formation of favourable heterojunctions between the “zero-dimensional” (0D) CPQDs nanocrystals and nanosheets through the formation of metalσhalide chemical bonds between the surface Br− ions of CPQDs and cations in nanosheets148 for CO2 appear very promising. Reduction of CO2 to CO in ethyl acetate/water solution with one order-of-magnitude higher than that of the pristine CPQDs alone was achieved, when 2D nanosheets of Bi2WO6 were used with CPQDs. Stability enhancement was considered to arise from a reduced photo corrosion of CPQDs due to the rapid extraction and subsequent recombination of photogenerated holes across the heterojunction with electrons from Bi2WO6. This can also be referred to as Z scheme photocatalysis.

Copper loaded reduced graphite oxide CPQD (Cu-RGO CPQD) photocatalyst nanocomposites prepared via an ex situ synthesis method were experimented for CO2 reduction in water under sunlight.149 Though the stability of sintered CPQDs nanosheets remained the same for two days, they completely decomposed after a week. So, CPQDs were developed into nanocomposites of copper loaded reduced graphite oxide CPQDs. The nanocomposite formation enhances the charge carrier separation and suppresses the electron–hole recombination. Cu-RGO CPQDs nanocomposites show 90% of photo catalytic reduction of CO2 in water to form CO and CH4. A schematic diagram of the photocatalytic mechanism using Cu-RGO CPQDs nanocomposites is shown in Fig. 8b. CPQD Cu-RGO possesses more hydrophobic surfaces thus suppressing water adsorption and formation of a hydrated complex, enhancing chemical stability. Hydrophobicity has been known to also boost CO2 reduction.150 A strategy such as encapsulating MAPbI3 quantum dots in an Fe metal porphyrin-based organic framework (PCN-221- 48 Fex)) by Lu et al.151 for CH4 production can be extended to CPQDs, wherein the encapsulation might render the necessary protection and environment to conduct the reaction in water.

Oxidation of styrene and many other organic transformations including C–C coupling,152 and α-alkylation of aldehydes153 have been fruitfully carried out with CPQDs. Not only C–C bond formation but also a series of organic transformations, C–O and C–N bond formation,154 can be found. But most of these impressive transformations are done in organic medium like ethyl acetate. Surface functionalization of CPQD nanosheets with Zr species and Cl doping facilitated visible light-oxidation of styrene.155 The doping causes alteration in the band structure which widens the bandgap that helps in enhancing the transport of photogenerated carriers for the photocatalytic process. The extent of photocatalytic oxidation of styrene by doped CPQDs (1098 μmol g−1 h−1) was far higher by pristine CPQDs (372 μmol g−1 h−1). The addition of ZrCl4, beyond a particular limit, expands the bandgap and reduces the surface photovoltage. However, since the medium of the reaction was organic, the CPQDs were well protected within the medium, and we have limited our discussion only to reactions carried out in water medium.

Also the adverse photocatalytic effects of solvents and sacrificial hole scavengers have been assessed and we concluded that the detected carbonaceous product and oxygen evolved significantly different from the expected quantity, and this is an outcome of solvent photolysis.156 Furthermore, this can lead to over estimation of the results achieved. These experiments when conducted using CPQDs as photocatalysts (in an acetonitrile + extremely low quantity of water mixture) for CO2 reduction and oxygen evolution too showed an over estimation of yield due to solvent photolysis which cannot be neglected during such studies. This procedure may also lack an adequate proton supply due to insufficient water in the medium. Photolysis of CH3CN, ethyl acetate, TEA, and TEOA under UV-visible light can produce CO and H2 even without catalysts156 and this further substantiates the overestimation of yield due to solvent photolysis.

4.1.2. Water splitting. CPQDs are semiconducting in nature and can induce photo catalysis causing water splitting157 to produce the most promising alternate energy hydrogen. Semiconductor quantum dots act as photoanodes and on light harvesting cause redox reactions with water molecules to produce oxygen and hydrogen.158,159 Graphite sheet (GS) protected CPQDs act as photoanodes to evolve oxygen from water under solar light illumination.160

Because of CPQDs’ inherent ionic nature and water instability, reports on applying the precipitation–solubility equilibrium between the soluble ionic species and perovskite phase seem successful. Saturated hydrogen iodide (HI) acid aqueous solution was used with MAPbI3 perovskite polycrystalline powder to generate H2161 by splitting HI. On visible light irradiation, roughly around 26 μmol of H2 (in 9 h) is obtained, with the photogenerated holes driving the oxidation of I− to I3−. H3PO2 was added to reduce this I3− to I− and maintain the I−concentration and equilibrium. Because of the dynamic equilibrium between the perovskite and ions in the saturated solution, MAPbI3 remained stable for 160 h under continuous irradiation, without any compromise in the activity. The same concept could however be extended to CPQDs. The stabilization strategy of these CPQDs in an aqueous solution should be based on the dynamic equilibrium between the precipitated ionic crystal and its saturated aqueous solution.

As discussed, earlier 2D sheets offered good stability to CPQDs. CPQDs were wrapped within graphene sheets (GS) to protect them from aqueous media. Due to the sealing of CPQDs with GS the potential onset of the composite for oxygen evolution was increased because of accelerated charge transfer upon formation of the composite. The IPCE efficiency of the composite was 70% (measured at 500 nm). The developed photo anode shows high oxygen evolution in water with a Faraday's efficiency measure of 70%.

A NiO/CPQD-GS/ZnO composite was synthesized by a one-step solution method, followed by deposition on an FTO glass substrate as the photoelectrode for water reduction.162 The group had adopted NiO and ZnO with CPQDs by a spin coating method as the hole and electron transport layer respectively and this architecture led to an effective charge separation to reduce electron hole recombination and realize the utilization of photogenerated electrons. The XRD pattern of CPQDs remains unchanged and the lambda max was characteristic of CPQDs at 517 nm. Under AM 1.5 G simulated sunlight conditions the onset potential of the photocathode was observed to be 1.16 VRHE with a current density of 1.2 mA cm−2 at 0 VRHE. The photocurrent remains consistent even after continuous illumination for 1 h with a faradaic efficiency of 90% thus showing good photo electrochemical stability.

Reports on the formation of seamless heterojunctions between the “zero-dimensional” (0D) CPQD nanocrystals and nanosheets through the formation of metal–halide chemical bonds between surface Br− ions of CsPbBr3 and cations in nanosheets148 appear very promising. Reduction of CO2 to CO in ethyl acetate/water solution with one order-of-magnitude higher than that of the pristine CPQDs alone was achieved, when the 2D nanosheets of Bi2WO6 were used with CPQDs. The stability enhancement was considered to arise from a reduced photo corrosion of CPQDs due to the rapid extraction and subsequent recombination of photogenerated holes across the heterojunction with electrons from Bi2WO6. This is usually referred to as the Z scheme in photocatalysis.

Wrapping of CPQDs in graphite sheets and metal oxides (as ETL/HTL) resulted in water stable photoelectrodes. However, the information on the maximum number of hours that these photoelectrodes can be used and their recyclability would have been more useful to obtain insights into the long-term stability of the material, which is to a large extent doubtful.

Thus, it can be summarised that to surpass CPQD instabilities, (a) designing systems for CO2 reduction in low-polarity solvents, (b) use of saturated halo acid solutions for H2 generation, and (c) capping and encapsulation seem to be promising solutions for CO2 reduction and photoelectrochemical water splitting using CPQDs.

4.2. Bio-sensing and bio-imaging

Bio-imaging is the process of visualizing or imaging biomolecules in biological solvents using fluorescent quantum dots for biomedical purposes.163,164 High quantum yields (up to 90%)165 of all inorganic CPQD nanocrystals have enabled them to be explored as optical probes and achieve high sensitivity166 and rapid sensing.167 However, building a compatible surface on CPQDs for applications like sensing analytes in aqueous medium is highly challenging. CPQDs are hydrophobic and are environmentally unstable in biological solvents like water and buffers of different pH. As discussed earlier various strategies namely silica shells, polymer coatings and ligand exchange have helped CPQDs become hydrophilic and biocompatible, but very limited research can be found. Antibody anti-CD63 specific for binding with CD63 exosome tumor cells was coated on a CPQD polymer composition via hydrogen bond formation, followed by injection into tumor solution. Multidentate ligand (succinic acid-NHS) capped CPQDs provided the necessary hydrophilicity to bind to biomolecules and this concept was used to detect BSA and achieved an LOD up to the micromolar range.75 These NHS-SA CPQDs were stable for 14 days in water. Only a limited number of studies involve CPQDs for imaging, which may be due to the extreme complexities involved in synthesizing compatible QDs for bioconjugation. It is important to say that the focus should be more on retention of optical properties on functionalization and to achieve compatibility in aqueous/biological medium such as bioimaging and biosensing applications.

5. Summary and outlook

The intrinsic properties of “degradation of PQDs” have been tweaked by strategies namely surface passivation, silica coatings and polymer encapsulation, and the extent of enhancement in its resistance to environmental conditions has been assessed. An assessment of the figures of merit for the stabilized or modified CPQDs is presented to check the reliability of this material for photocatalysis and biosensing applications. The analysis was mainly based on its stability in aqueous and biological medium.

The ligand exchange process is a simple mechanism that helps in inducing passivation and hence enhances surface compatibility, protecting CPQDs from degradation in air, water, light and heat. It is important to note that the binding energy of a selected ligand with the core must be much stronger than the existing system. The short chain ligands containing specific functional groups like COOH, NH2 and PO4 are preferred, but bifunctional and multidentate ligands that (a) stabilize the ligand in different environments/solvent systems and also (b) stabilize the core (Pb2+, Br−) thus reducing the density of trap states are to be given priority for photocatalysis and fluorescence sensing.

Silica capped CPQDs have proven to protect CPQDs from water and open air and are compatible with both toluene and water systems and hence applicable to the said applications. Since the silyl ether groups of silane precursors hydrolyse to form this cross linked matrix cover over CPQDs and if the hydrolysis reaction is water-assisted, it needs to done with care. The thermal instability of such silica coated PQDs is however compromised and can be overcome by polymer coating. MOF encapsulated CPQDs are water stable and appear very promising. This opens new avenues for the design of other such water stable MOF PQDs for a wide range of optoelectronic properties.

Wrapping of CPQDs in graphite sheets induces hydrophobicity, facilitating suppression of water adsorption in water splitting reactions thus providing stability. However, research on the maximum number of hours that these photoelectrodes can be used and their recyclability is required to be done to gain insights of their long-term stability.

Bioimaging has only been attempted with polymer/silica capped CPQDs, while hydrophilic ligand capped CPQDs have successfully facilitated bioconjugation. Very few reports have limited or proper assessment with regard to biosensing. Based on our assessment, the answer to the question of whether these modified CPQDs are reliable as a photocatalyst and fluorescent probe in water/biological medium is positive. The insights offered here however provide scope for developing various other water stable lead free and double perovskites which are environmentally more benign and useful.
