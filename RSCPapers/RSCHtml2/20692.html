Low-temperature solution-processed efficient electron-transporting layers based on BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2 nanorods for high-performance planar perovskite solar cells

Abstract

To fabricate cost-effective solar cells, some prerequisites such as low-energy consumption, simplified technological processes, and high efficiency must be achieved. Low-temperature solution-processed planar perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are desirable for achieving these aims. Although TiO2 is the most widely used material in preparing electron-transporting layers (ETLs) of PSCs, it usually requires a high-temperature sintering process to enhance electron mobility, which not only consumes considerable energy but is also unsuitable for polymer-based flexible substrates. Here, we propose a novel strategy involving the use of a sophisticated solvothermal method with the aid of oleic acid (OA) ligands to synthesize high aspect ratio and well crystallized TiO2 nanorods. Then, the ligand-exchange treatment is applied to substitute the insulating OA ligands with BF4âˆ’ ions. With this protocol, low-temperature solution-processed high-quality TiO2 ETLs can be produced with high electrical conductivity and fast electron extracting and transporting rates. Finally, the PSCs with BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2 nanorod ETLs achieves a high-power conversion efficiency of 18.76% and good reproducibility. This work offers a feasible method to achieve high-efficiency low-temperature solution-processed planar PSCs.

Introduction

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have attracted much interest in recent years and have become primary candidates for next-generation cost-effective high-performance photovoltaics.1â€“3 Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite materials were initially used as sensitizers in liquid-junction dye-sensitized solar cells.4,5 Because of their poor stability, the liquid electrolyte was substituted by the well-known organic hole-transporting material of 2,2â€²,7,7â€²-tetrakis(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenylamine)-9,9â€²-spirobifluorene (spiro-OMeTAD), which led to a high-power conversion efficiency (PCE) exceeding 9% with good stability.6 Soon afterwards, the photovoltaic performance of mesoscopic PSCs, which evolved from solid-state dye-sensitized solar cells, showed great progress and immediately overtook the record efficiencies of dye-sensitized solar cells, organic solar cells, quantum dot solar cells, and even other types of solar cells.7,8 Although the mesoscopic structure makes for efficient electron extraction and transport in PSCs, it also brings about some apparent defects, including complex structure, inadequate pore filling of perovskite materials, and the high-temperature sintering process.9â€“13 Fortunately, due to the ambipolar nature of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite materials, the planar heterojunction PSCs also work efficiently,14,15 and therefore, it is possible to eliminate mesoscopic layers in the PSCs to simplify the device structure. Without mesoscopic layers in the planar PSCs, the high-temperature sintering process can be substituted with a low-temperature process by selecting suitable charge-transporting materials, which is beneficial for application of flexible polymer conductive substrates, fabrication of tandem devices, and utilization of roll-to-roll mass production in the future.

In planar PSCs, the preparation of high-quality electron-transporting layers (ETLs) is a prerequisite for achieving high photovoltaic performance because the ETLs play key roles in electron extraction and transport, hole blocking, and interface contacts in the devices.16â€“18 Due to the proper conduction band energy, good chemical stability, and high transparency, TiO2 has been widely used for ETLs.19,20 However, a high-temperature sintering process is usually needed to enhance the crystallinity and conductivity of the TiO2 ETLs prepared with the solâ€“gel method or spray pyrolysis.21â€“23 With the aid of expensive equipment for atomic layer deposition, electron beam evaporation, and magnetron sputtering, high-quality TiO2 ETLs can be prepared at low temperature.24â€“26 Nonetheless, these methodologies are complicated, time-consuming, and expensive, which is why it is necessary to develop low-temperature solution-processed methodologies. Yang's group fabricated high performance planar PSCs with the highest PCE of 19.3% by a low-temperature solution-processed methodology.27 They used yttrium-doped TiO2 as the ETLs to enhance carrier concentration and modified the indium tin oxide conductive glasses with a polymer-containing simple aliphatic amine group to reduce the work function, which resulted in improved electron extraction and transport and efficient photovoltaic performance. Sargent's group further verified that efficient and stable low-temperature solution-processed planar PSCs could be achieved via a contact passivation strategy using chlorine-capped TiO2 colloidal nanocrystal film.15 This ligand-engineering strategy is widely used in fabricating high performance quantum dot solar cells and achieves great success.28â€“30

The development of advanced colloidal synthesis methods enabled us to synthesize high-quality monodisperse nanocrystals, including metallic, semiconductor, magnetic, and so on, with exactly controlled compositions, shapes, sizes, and crystal structures.31â€“33 Ligands, especially long hydrocarbon ligands such as oleic acid (OA) and oleylamine (OAm), are often employed for controlling nanocrystal growth, stabilizing nanocrystals in nonpolar solvents, and triggering ordered self-assemblies of nanocrystals.34 However, these long hydrocarbon ligand-capped nanocrystals cannot be directly used in fabricating functional devices such as solar cells, light-emitting diodes, and photodetectors because the long hydrocarbon ligands create insulating barriers around the nanocrystals to restrict electronic coupling.35,36 The ligand-exchange strategy solves these problems through replacing the original insulating ligands with specifically designed species to achieve functional applications, and the quantum dot solar cell community has demonstrated the usefulness and versatility of this design.37â€“39 Therefore, it is reasonable to expect that high-quality low-temperature solution-processed TiO2 ETLs can be achieved with the same strategy.

Here, we report the application of TiO2 nanorods (TiO2-NRs) to construct efficient ETLs. The high aspect ratio of TiO2-NRs is a benefit for constructing well connected electron-transporting networks, which not only form satisfactory interfacial contacts with perovskite layers but also improve electron extracting and transporting rates.40 In order to ensure the synthesis of high-quality TiO2-NRs, we utilized the sophisticated solvothermal synthesis method with the aid of OA ligands to control the growth of TiO2-NRs. Subsequently, these insulating OA ligands were substituted with BF4âˆ’ ions through ligand-exchange treatment. Comparatively, the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs are advantageous for preparing effective ETLs because of their higher electrical conductivity, faster electron extraction, and transport properties. As expected, devices with BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs show superior photovoltaic performance as compared to devices with OA-capped TiO2-NR ETLs.

Experimental

Materials

All of the chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich without further purification, unless specifically mentioned. Methylammonium bromide (MABr) and formamidinium iodide (FAI) salts were obtained from Xi'an Polymer Light Technology Corp., China. Spiro-OMeTAD was supplied by Luminescence Technology Corp., Taiwan, China. Fluorine-doped tin oxide-coated conductive glass substrates (FTOs) with sheet resistance of 15 Î© â–¡âˆ’1 (Nippon Glass Corp, Japan) were used as conductive substrates. TiO2-NRs were synthesized according to a previously reported method.41

Preparation of BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2 nanorods

It has been reported that NOBF4 can effectively peel off native OA and OAm ligands on colloidal nanocrystals and exchange these ligands with BF4âˆ’ ions while preserving the size and shape of the nanocrystals.37 Here, we used the same method to prepare BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs. In a typical process, 10 mL hexane solution of TiO2-NRs (10 mg mLâˆ’1) was mixed with 10 mL dichloromethane solution of NOBF4 (0.02 M) under vigorous stirring for 5 min. After removing the supernatant by centrifugation, the precipitated TiO2-NRs were dispersed in N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF) with a concentration of 20 mg mLâˆ’1.

Device fabrication

Firstly, the laser-patterned FTOs with size of 1.5 Ã— 1.5 cm2 were consecutively washed with isopropanol, acetone, distilled water, and ethanol; then they were finally treated with UV-ozone for 30 min.

Secondly, the TiO2 ETLs were prepared by spin-coating the toluene solution of TiO2-NRs on the patterned and cleared FTOs at 4000 rpm for 30 s, and then heating at 150 Â°C for 30 min. In order to compare the performance differences of OA-capped and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR-based ETLs, the same concentration was used of both toluene solution of OA-capped TiO2-NRs (20 mg mLâˆ’1) and DMF solution of BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs.

Thirdly, cesium-containing triple cation perovskite layers were deposited on the TiO2 ETLs using a previously reported method.42 Namely, a precursor solution was prepared containing FAI (1 mol Lâˆ’1), PbI2 (1.1 mol Lâˆ’1), MABr (0.2 mol Lâˆ’1), and PbBr2 (0.2 mol Lâˆ’1) in anhydrous DMF and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) mixed solvents with a volume ratio of 4:1. Then, CsI (0.175 mol Lâˆ’1), predissolved as a 1.5 mol Lâˆ’1 stock solution in DMSO, was added to the mixed perovskite precursor solution to achieve the desired triple cation composition. The precursor solution was spin-coated at 1000 rpm for 10 s and then at 6500 rpm for 20 s. After 5 s of the spin-coating procedure at 6500 rpm had elapsed, 130 Î¼L of anti-solvent consisting of chlorobenzene was poured on the spinning substrates to rinse residual DMSO and DMF from the precursor films. The substrates were then heated at 100 Â°C for 30 min to form crystalline triple cation perovskite layers.

Fourthly, the spiro-OMeTAD layers were constructed on the as-prepared triple cation perovskite layers by spin-coating 20 Î¼L chlorobenzene solution of spiro-OMeTAD composed of 72.3 mg spiro-OMeTAD, 28.8 Î¼L 4-tert-butyl pyridine, and 17.5 Î¼L acetonitrile solution of lithium bis(trifluoromethane sulfonyl)imide (Li-TFSI) (520 mg Li-TSFI dissolved in 1 mL acetonitrile) at 4000 rpm for 30 s.

Finally, 100 nm Au electrodes were thermally evaporated on the spiro-OMeTAD layers under high vacuum through a shadow mask. The entire preparation process, from ligand-exchange to fabrication of planar PSCs, is illustrated in Scheme 1.

Results and discussion

TiO2 nanorod properties

The typical TEM, high resolution TEM (HRTEM), and selected-area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns were used to characterize the TiO2-NRs. The TEM image shown in Fig. 1a reveals that the as-synthesized products contain mostly TiO2-NRs with few TiO2 nanoparticles. The products are well dispersed without any agglomerations. The length and diameter distribution histograms shown in Fig. 1d and e reveal that the average length and diameter of the TiO2-NRs are 35.78 Â± 6.06 nm and 2.80 Â± 0.83 nm, respectively, resulting in a high aspect ratio of 12.78. The HRTEM image and SAED patterns shown in Fig. 1b and c present clearly visible atomic lattice fringes and diffraction rings, suggesting the formation of highly crystalline TiO2-NRs.
Fig. 1 TEM image (a), HRTEM image (b), and SAED patterns (c) of TiO2-NRs; length (d) and diameter (e) distribution histograms of TiO2-NRs taken from (a).

Fig. 2a shows the XRD patterns of OA-capped and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs. The as-synthesized TiO2-NRs without sintering treatment present high crystallinity, which is consistent with the results of the HRTEM image and SAED patterns shown in Fig. 1b and c, respectively. The diffraction peaks and relevant indexed crystallographic planes marked in the XRD patterns are consistent with the anatase crystalline phase (JCPDS#21-1272). The ligand-exchange treatment did not change the crystallinity of the TiO2-NRs, because the two samples have almost similar XRD patterns.

Fig. 2b shows the FTIR spectra of OA-capped and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs. Comparing the spectra of the two samples, it was found that the characteristic Câ€“H stretching vibrations at 2800â€“3000 cmâˆ’1 ascribed to OA ligands have noticeably disappeared after ligand-exchange treatment.37,38 Furthermore, the stretching vibrations of CO and CC at 1738 cmâˆ’1 and 1640 cmâˆ’1, respectively, and the symmetric and asymmetric â€“COOâ€“ stretching modes at 1521 cmâˆ’1 and 1431 cmâˆ’1, respectively, also disappeared.45,46 The rest of the infrared absorption peak at 1651 cmâˆ’1 can be ascribed to the CO stretching vibration of the DMF solvent, and the peak at 1067 cmâˆ’1 is associated with BF4âˆ’ ions.37,38 Therefore, we can conclude that the OA ligands were successfully substituted by BF4âˆ’ ions after ligand-exchange treatment.

TiO2 nanorod ETL morphology, transmittance, and charge transfer

Fig. 3aâ€“c show top and cross-sectional SEM images of FTO and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs on FTO. Due to the presence of different sizes of F-doped SnO2 grains, from tens to hundreds of nanometers, the bare FTO exhibits a highly rough surface (Fig. 3a). After spin-coating BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs on the FTO, the originally clear outlines of F-doped SnO2 grains become indistinct and a compact and pin-hole-free TiO2-NR ETL appears (Fig. 3b). The cross-sectional view presented in Fig. 3c reveals that the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs can sufficiently cover the surface of F-doped SnO2 grains to avoid the formation of charge shunt pathways by the direct contact of perovskite layers with FTO. Although the TiO2-NRs are too small to be individually distinguished by SEM, the extremely small size of the TiO2-NRs is beneficial for producing continuous and compact ETLs. From the cross-sectional view of the device as shown in Fig. 3d, we can see that the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETL can easily support the perovskite layer with a good contact interface.

Fig. 4a shows the transmittance spectra of the bare FTO, OA-capped, and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs on FTOs. The two TiO2-NR ETLs have almost the same transmittance because the ligands are usually adsorbed on the monolayer surface of the nanocrystals, and the thickness is extremely thin, having little influence on the transmittance of the films.47 Compared with the bare FTO, the TiO2-NR ETLs on FTOs show decreased transmittance, whereas the variations of transmittance are very marginal (within 3%). The high transparency of TiO2-NR ETLs is propitious to limit the loss of light energy and achieve high photocurrent with the devices.48Fig. 4b shows the Tafel curves of OA-capped and BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs assembled in the architecture of FTO/TiO2-NR ETL/Au. Comparing the differences in current density between the two samples, one can evaluate the altered electrical conductive properties by ligand-exchange treatment.49,50 The substitution of OA ligands with BF4âˆ’ ions can result in clearly enhanced current density in the Tafel curve, with a higher conductivity of the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETL than that of the OA-capped TiO2-NR ETL. The superior electrical conductivity of the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETL is advantageous for the transmission of photogenerated electrons and reduction of dark current.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have demonstrated a novel strategy to prepare high-quality TiO2-NR ETLs with low-temperature solution-processed methodology for efficient planar PSCs. The utilization of the OA-assisted solvothermal method can synthesize high aspect ratio and well crystallized TiO2-NRs, which were verified by TEM and HRTEM images and SAED patterns. Subsequently, the application of ligand-exchange treatment with NOBF4 reagent can absolutely substitute OA ligands with BF4âˆ’ ions and maintain the same crystal structure, as confirmed by FTIR spectra and XRD patterns. The as-prepared BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NRs can fully cover the F-doped SnO2 grains in the FTO substrate to form compact ETLs with high transmittance, as confirmed by the SEM images and transmittance spectra. Furthermore, compared with the OA-capped TiO2-NR ETLs, the BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs show faster electron extracting and transporting rates, as verified by the steady-state PL spectra and transient TRPL curves. Therefore, as expected, the device with BF4âˆ’-capped TiO2-NR ETLs achieves better photovoltaic performance than that of the device with OA-capped TiO2-NR ETLs. The findings suggest a desirable method with characteristics of low-energy consumption, simplified technological processes, and high efficiency for fabricating cost-effective solar cells.
