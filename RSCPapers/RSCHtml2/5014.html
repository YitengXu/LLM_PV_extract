Merging plasmonics and metamaterials by two-dimensional subwavelength structures

Abstract

As one of the major fields of photonics in the last few decades, plasmonics takes advantage of the coupling of light and free electrons in noble metals, and allows the localization of light beyond the diffraction limit. Recently, plasmonics has been introduced into the newly emerging metamaterials, which are artificially structured effective materials with unique physical properties that do not exist in natural materials. Based on the strong light–matter interaction below the diffraction limit, the plasmonic metamaterials have found important applications in various photonic devices, such as color filters, flat lenses and perfect absorbers. In this review article, we focus on the planar plasmonic metamaterials that rely on the patterned arrangement of metallic and dielectric inclusions in the two-dimensional (2D) space. The interesting optical effects, underlying physical principles and the practical applications in functional devices are outlined, with a brief discussion of their future development trends.

1. Introduction

Plasmonics and metamaterials are two emerging branches of modern optics and nanophotonics.1,2 Although they have been developed in parallel during the last one hundred years, the discoveries of extraordinary optical transmission and perfect imaging properties of structured metal films by Thomas Ebbesen and John Pendry respectively have linked them together.3,4 Since then, the exotic properties of surface plasmon polaritons (SPPs) were taken into account to design various metamaterials with unnatural optical parameters such as the negative refractive index,4,5 biological and chemical sensors6–9 and perfectly matched absorbers.10–15

One of the most important and interesting aspects of the combination of plasmonics and metamaterials is the naissance of 2D metamaterials, or the so-called metasurfaces.16–23 Different from the definite permittivity and permeability in metamaterials, these bulk properties are less important or even meaningless in many metasurfaces.11,24 As an alternative, sheet impedance is often exploited in the characterization of light–matter interactions. The plasmonic circuit models can also be used as useful tools for impedance analysis.25 Compared with previous metamaterials, metasurfaces have much smaller thickness and thus are far easier to fabricate. As a result, it was expected that the first generation of practical metamaterial devices would utilize metasurface implementations.18

The core of the problem in designing plasmonic metasurfaces is to arrange various chemical materials with various types of topological structures. In essence, almost all the associated electromagnetic phenomena can be understood by using Maxwell's equations. Nevertheless, the numerical solutions of Maxwell's equations ignore the physical meanings and are troublesome in many cases, and thus an analytic or quasi-analytic approach would help the further understanding of these light–matter interactions. In the past few years, many analytical methods such as the transfer matrix method (TMM) and rigorous coupled wave analysis (RCWA) have been provided. By utilizing the boundary conditions and the superposition principle, many problems, such as the grating diffraction, multilayer filter, etc., can be simplified but other complicated issues are still difficult to solve.26

As an alternative, here we use a phenomenological approach to describe these electromagnetic behaviors. Without the loss of any generality, all EM waves can be classified into two categories: the freely propagating waves and bound waves. In free space and common homogeneous materials, all waves are propagating without any limitations and physical boundaries. These waves can be easily decomposed into a series of plane waves through the superposition theorem and Fourier theory.27 According to the Fourier principle, the free waves have an upper limit of spatial frequency, which is the product of the material refractive index and the free space wavenumber k0. This limited wavenumber is also the key of the diffraction limit,28 which means that the resolution of optical microscopy and lithography technology cannot exceed one half of the working wavelength.29

Different from the free waves, the waves bound at the boundaries between different materials may have very large spatial frequency. For instance, the wavenumber of the SPP excited at the metal–dielectric interface has an effective wavelength much smaller than that in vacuum.30,31 Such a short-wavelength property has made the SPP a promising candidate to construct sub-diffraction imaging/lithography systems and integrated optical circuits.19,32,33 In the infrared and microwave frequencies, the use of similar phenomena such as spoof surface plasmon in complex metallic structures can also boost the obtainable high spatial frequency.34,35 Furthermore, the wavenumber of waves bound in a thin film of high refractive index can also be very large, and thus could also produce many exotic optical phenomena such as structural colors and ultra-broadband absorption.36–38

This review paper describes the optical phenomena and practical applications regarding the plasmonic light–matter interaction in the 2D space. The role of high-k bound surface waves as well as the exchange between free and bound waves in various functional devices, such as color filters, flat lenses and perfect absorbers, is discussed with detail. At the end of this paper, the potential applications of new nano-materials such as doped semiconductors, graphene and phase-change materials are also envisioned.

Once the freely propagating wave is coupled to an SPP, the SPP would be localized and/or propagate along the surface. As can be seen in Fig. 2, the localization of the SPP and similar bound waves has found applications in areas such as sub-diffraction waveguiding, imaging, lithography, and recording systems, etc. Besides the direct utilization of the localized waves, the exchange of free and bound waves is also very useful in many practical applications, enabling new methods to manipulate the freely propagating waves with deep-subwavelength spatial resolution. Depending on the characteristics of propagating waves, the SPP-enabled functionalities can be divided into the following main categories: the amplitude modulation, phase/polarization modulation and the frequency modulation. On the one hand, the amplitude, phase and polarization at each single frequency should be tuned following rules that depend on the exact applications.22 On the other hand, the dispersion properties should be elaborately controlled to realize multi-frequency or broadband functionalities.62–65 In addition, the interaction of multi-frequency light waves in subwavelength structures is essential for nonlinear nanophotonic devices.66–68

Similarly, using the enhanced transmission effect of bull's eye structures, a simple multicolor detection scheme can be realized. 2.2.2 Wavefront and polarization engineering. The recent years have witnessed the rapid development of flat optical devices. A major part of these works is associated with engineering of the wavefronts and beam shapes.16,33 One initial motivation to design flat optical devices at the nanoscale is to circumvent the diffraction effect, which makes the nanoscale light beam diffract rapidly when leaving the radiating structures.

Many novel surface structures are found to be able to create collimated light. The first type is the so-called bull's eyes. As shown in Fig. 4a and b, when a metallic screen is milled with a central hole and periodically grooves at both the input and output surfaces, the incoming light can be transmitted and excite SPPs at the output surfaces,73 which are then collectively scattered into the farfield.81 Under appropriate resonant conditions, the transmitted light could be collimated with a very small divergence angle. Owing to the collective response of the SPPs, this scheme is not only valid for normal laser beams,82 but also useful for fluorescence emissions83 (Fig. 4c) and light beams carrying orbital angular momentum.84

More recently, the extraordinary transmission and beaming effect based on groove structures have been expanded to other frequency bands. Pendry et al. demonstrated that even a perfect electric conductor can support surface-plasmon-like electromagnetic waves.34 In order to distinguish it from traditional SPPs, this surface plasmon supported by groove structures at low frequency is termed the spoof surface plasmon (SSP). Besides the plasmonic leaky-wave-based beam collimator, flat plasmonic lenses can also be designed by utilizing the width-dependent propagation constant of MIM waveguides. Based on the gradient phase realized by propagation delay, the metasurface-assisted law of refraction and reflection (MLRR) can be constructed to change the light propagation defined by traditional Snell's law.19,86,87 When the gradient phase follows Φ(r) = k0(r2 + f2)1/2, where r is the radius and f is the focal length, traditional bulky aplanatic lenses can be shrunk into the 2D plane.

2.3 From Mie resonance to dielectric metamaterials

Besides plasmonic interactions in metal–dielectric composites, recent years also witnessed a rapid development of dielectric metamaterials. In particular, the dielectric materials with high refractive indices such as silicon, germanium and TiO2 have attracted much attention owing to their strong Mie-type resonances and low optical loss.122–125 The Mie theory for the scattering and absorption of spherical particles was developed by Mie in 1908.1 In general, the Mie theory can deal with both dielectric and metallic problems. The localized plasmon resonance of spherical metal particles can be well understood by using this approach.

One important problem of the Mie-type dielectric metamaterial is the validity of the effective medium theory, which assumes that deep-subwavelength features cannot be distinguished by the electromagnetic waves, thus structured materials behave the same like suppositional homogeneous materials. Although effective materials approach is valid in the deep-subwavelength scale, the accuracy would deteriorate as the size of unit cell increases. In particular, when the period is larger than λ/2, the high order diffraction may take place at oblique incidence. For Mie-type metamaterials, the period is usually not much smaller than the operating wavelength, and thus transition from photonic crystals to dielectric metamaterials typically occur owing to the interplay between Bragg and Mie resonances.126 In order to handle this problem, it is necessary to apply different theories in different frequency regimes, as demonstrated in the broadband terahertz absorber based on doped silicon.127,128

Although dielectric metamaterials and metasurfaces provide a new route to realize low-loss functional devices, the thicknesses are a bit larger than their plasmonic counterparts,129,130 while the energy efficiencies are dependent on the wavelength and angle of incidence. Furthermore, low-loss high refractive materials are hard to find in some frequency bands, thus limiting their wide-spread utilization.

3. Critical coupling to bound surface waves and perfect absorption

In many plasmonic applications, the ohmic loss in noble metals is not desired and much effort has been devoted to the research of low-loss plasmonic materials and loss-compensation techniques.131–133 Nevertheless, it is still interesting that the plasmonic loss can be utilized to enhance the absorption of electromagnetic energy. The intuitive idea to create a plasmonic absorber can be found in the old concept of the blackbody. As shown in Fig. 8a, when light is transmitted into a lossy cavity through a small hole, almost all energy would be converted into heat by multiple reflection and absorption. A plasmonic blackbody absorber composed of a metallic cavity is shown in Fig. 8b. When the metallic loss is relatively weak such as that in gold, the blackbody can still work well in a narrowband.15 However, when the metallic loss is increased by reducing the conductivity, broadband blackbody absorber can be constructed.134 Owing to the hexagonal lattice, such an absorber could absorb light coming from all directions with all polarizations. The metallic cavity absorber can also be transformed into a simpler configuration.11,135 As depicted in Fig. 8c, such a structure is similar to the MIM waveguide and has many variations in geometric shapes.135–138

The plasmonic perfect absorber can be interpreted using the free-bound wave exchange principle19 and the concept of critical coupling. In general, the reflected wave from a structured surface can be treated as a superimposition of the directly reflected and re-emitted waves of the bound oscillations. When these two components interfere destructively, all the incident waves can be converted to bound waves, and finally be converted to heat or other kinds of energy. We note that the coherent perfect absorption effect36,37,139,140 can also be understood using the two-wave exchange and the critical coupling mechanism.

Plasmonic cavity absorbers have three superiorities over traditional absorbers: first, the absorption is almost independent on the incident angle and thus a true omnidirectional absorber can be achieved;15,134 secondly, the thickness of the absorber can be greatly reduced using the magnetic resonance that occurred in the cavity;136 thirdly, the absorption bandwidth can be tuned on demand by utilizing the dispersion of these resonances,141,142 which will be discussed in the following section.

The angle-independent absorption phenomenon can be explained using the effective medium theory. Considering the resonant characteristics, the effective refractive index is very large at the resonant frequency, which leads to the fact that the critical coupling conditions are independent of the incident angle. As depicted in Fig. 8d, when the refractive index of the dielectric spacer in a Salisbury absorber is very large, the absorption bandwidth as well as the angle-sensitivity would decrease. Using the Snell's law, it is easy to see that waves in the high-index material propagate approximately perpendicular to the surface, regardless of the incident angle. More precisely, the phase delay between adjacent reflection waves can be written as 2nkd(1 − (sinθ/n)2)1/2, which is almost independent of the angle θ when n ≫ 1. By engineering the resonances, one can further achieve dual-band (Fig. 8e) or wide-band absorption within a wide range of incident angles.11,143

One important application of the plasmonic absorber is the photovoltaic solar cells,144 which convert light into electricity or other kinds of useful energy. Although the solar energy is transformed into heat for common noble-metal-based absorbers, it has been shown that such an absorption mechanism can also be extended to photovoltaic cells by exchanging the ohmic loss and with useful optical absorption in semiconductor layers.24 Following the competition mechanism of the dielectric and metallic absorption, one can decrease the ohmic absorption by increasing the metal thickness to be much larger than the skin depth (Fig. 9a). By gradually increasing the absorption strength of the semiconductor layer, the photons that reflected originally due to impedance mismatch can be shifted to and absorbed in the semiconductors (Fig. 9b).

Different from the direct conversion from photons to photo-current, the ohmic loss induced heat can also be converted into narrowband thermal emission and reused in thermo-photovoltaic systems.146,147 Wang et al. demonstrated a solar thermo-photovoltaic device that reaches experimental efficiencies of 3.2%.146 By using vertically aligned multiwalled carbon nanotubes as absorbers, a near-complete absorption over a broad solar spectrum was achieved. One-dimensional photonic crystals were optimized to provide a cut-off in the thermal emission right at the bandgap of the semiconductor, thus strongly suppressing sub-bandgap thermal radiation.

4. Dispersion and bandwidth engineering

As mentioned in the above discussions, the dispersion of electromagnetic modes plays an important role in almost all plasmonic devices. On the one hand, the short wavelength and directive propagating properties of bound surface waves can be directly observed in the dispersion diagram of single metal–dielectric interfaces and metal–dielectric multilayers. On the other hand, the frequency dispersion of these modes makes multi-wavelength, broadband and ultra-narrowband applications become possible.138,141,148–150

When designing broadband plasmonic devices, it is useful to retrieve the ideal frequency dispersion of a perfect device using theoretical approaches. For perfect absorbers and polarization converters with only one metasurface layer, the corresponding dispersion curves can be given using the transfer matrix method.114,141 Feng et al. demonstrated that the absorption bandwidth can be enlarged dramatically through utilizing the metasurface to mimic the dispersion of the ideal absorbing sheet.141 By introducing a specific dispersion relation in the form of the Lorentz model,151 a nearly perfect absorber was obtained with absorption greater than 97% over a one-octave bandwidth (Fig. 10a and b). It was noted that the Lorentz form is just one type of dispersion that mimics that of an ideal absorbing sheet, and thus further engineering of the dispersion may lead to an even larger bandwidth.

An achromatic circular polarization converter with a 5:1 bandwidth (two times of previous broadband converters) was numerically and experimentally demonstrated (see Fig. 10c and d).

The dispersion engineering for reflective devices can also be expanded to transmissive applications. Grady et al. used a metal grating that transmits the cross-polarized waves while still acting as a ground plane for co-polarized waves.152 To retain the backward propagating cross-polarized waves without blocking the incident ones, they added an orthogonal metal grating in front of the cut-wires (Fig. 10e). This ultrathin device serves as a high-performance linear polarization converter in transmission with reduced co- and cross-polarized reflections.152

Although the line width of plasmonic resonance can be reduced by the above methods, the inherent absorption limits the obtainable quality factor. As an alternative, it has been shown that the all-dielectric approach can greatly enhance the Q-factor, when the dielectric constant is large enough to support Mie-type resonances.158 As shown in Fig. 11e and f, an ultrahigh Q factor (larger than 10000) can be achieved with an array of dielectric cylinders,158 based on the mode interference between the broadband Fabry–Perot (FP) resonance and the narrowband guided mode stemming from coupled quadrupoles (here the effective medium theory is not a good choice to explain this effect). Since a wide gap region for field enhancement is formed between adjacent rods, such a structure may be an ideal platform for related applications such as biological sensing and nonlinear devices.

5. The promise of new plasmonic materials

The optical and electric properties of constitutive materials are critical for the performance of all plasmonic devices. Unfortunately, noble metals used in most plasmonic applications have many drawbacks such as relatively high ohmic loss and limited operating wavelength ranges. In recent years, a lot of new plasmonic materials have been proposed to reduce the loss, extend the frequency bands and realize special functionalities.131,159–161 According to the origins of free electrons, plasmonic materials can be classified into metals (Au, Ag, Al, etc.) and doped semiconductors (including graphene). Based on the different chemical compositions, these materials bear a variety of physical and chemical properties, making them suitable for respective applications. An exhaustive survey of alternative plasmonic materials and a detailed discussion of the chemical compositions and preparation processes can be found in previous literature reports.131,162,163 Nevertheless, it should be noted that these new plasmonic materials still possess their disadvantages compared to noble metals, which means that a compromise in the design must be made in general cases.

5.1 Doped semiconductors

Traditional plasmonic materials based on noble metals are limited by the natural plasmonic resonance frequencies. Over the past few years, several approaches have been proposed to tune the plasmonic resonances. Besides the metallic wire arrays proposed by Pendry in 1996,164 heavily doped oxide semiconductors such as doped silicon,36 indium tin oxide (ITO) and aluminum-doped zinc oxide (AZO) can be used as alternative plasmonic materials, which offer both functional and fabrication advantages in the near-infrared wavelength range.165,166 The plasmonic performance can be well designed by the carrier density, damping rate and effective mass in doped semiconductors.127 By properly tuning the chemical composites, the optical and electric properties may be further improved for plasmonic applications.131

5.2 Graphene

As a one-atom-thick carbon material described by the two-dimensional (2D) Dirac-like equation, graphene has attracted much attention in recent years because of its extraordinary electronic and optical properties.167 Graphene has a rather high quantum efficiency for light–matter interactions and supports plasmons with extremely small effective wavelength compared to that in vacuum.168 Furthermore, the performance of graphene can be modified by gating, by doping, by chemical means and through conventional plasmonics based on noble metals.169 Based on the electric tunable electromagnetic properties of graphene, various functional devices have been proposed in the last decade. Among these demonstrations, the tunable absorbers are of particular interest, as a result of the fact that a single sheet of homogeneous graphene has a limited optical absorption of A ≈ πα ≈ 2.3%, where α ≈ 1/137 is the fine structure constant.170 When a single layer of graphene is placed above a metal plate and separated by a dielectric layer, a tunable Salisbury type absorber is obtained.171,172 As shown in Fig. 12a and b, by applying a voltage bias, one can tune the Fermi energy of graphene up to 1 eV and achieve a critical coupling condition at 2.8 THz with an absorption of 99%.172

In the Salisbury-type graphene absorber, the absorption peak positions do not change with the bias voltage. Yao et al. showed that a widely tunable metasurface composed of optical antennas on graphene can be incorporated into a subwavelength-thick optical cavity to tune the absorption peak positions.173 By switching the absorber in and out of the critical coupling conditions via the gate voltage applied on graphene, a modulation depth of up to 100% can be achieved. In particular, ultrathin high speed (up to 20 GHz) optical modulators over a broad wavelength range (5–7 μm) were demonstrated. Another method to use graphene as an optical modulator is to cover the graphene above the waveguide. By electrically tuning the Fermi level of the graphene sheet, Liu et al. modulated the guided light at frequencies over 1 GHz with a broad operation spectrum that ranges from 1.35 to 1.6 μm.174

The single layer graphene can also be used as an ultra-broadband absorber,175 by taking advantage of the concept of coherent perfect absorption or time-reversed current radiation.36 As illustrated in Fig. 12c and d, the absorption in the frequency range of 7 to 13 GHz can be larger than 90%. Considering that the thickness of graphene is only about 0.34 nm, such an absorber has unparalleled performance regarding the thickness and bandwidth.

5.3 Refractory materials

Refractory materials bear high melting point and strong chemical stability at high temperatures, and thus have many applications in areas including industrial furnaces and space shuttle shields.176 However, common refractory materials are usually nonmetallic and the optical properties of refractory metals were not good enough. Since refractory materials that exhibit reasonably good plasmonic behavior would enable new devices and boost existing applications such as heat-assisted magnetic recording (HAMR) and solar/thermophotovoltaics (S/TPV), there have been many research studies on these problems during the recent years.160,176,177

Among the various refractory materials, Titanium nitride (TiN) has a melting point as high as 2930 °C and exhibits a plasmonic resonance in the visible-NIR range. Li et al. have designed, fabricated and characterized a broadband TiN-based absorber with an average absorption of 95% over the range of 400–800 nm (Fig. 13a).176 In contrast with noble metal optical absorbers, the TiN absorber enables one to combine both the intrinsic loss of the material and the plasmonic resonances. As a result, the reflection is greatly suppressed and efficient, broadband absorption can be achieved. Due to its high melting point and the combined absorption mechanisms, the TiN absorber can work at high temperatures and endure strong light illumination, which has been verified by experimental testing (see Fig. 13b and c). Besides TiN, tungsten (W) and molybdenum (Mo) also possess high melting temperatures and have been used in various solar absorbers.153,177

5.4 Phase-change materials

The performance of plasmonic and photonic devices such as planar lenses and absorbers has been optimized through many sophisticated designs; however, most of these devices so far lack the tunability once being fabricated. Recently, phase-change materials have emerged as promising candidates for dynamic tuning, due to their extreme phase switching speeds and the drastic changes in their optical properties upon phase change.178

Among the various phase-change materials, the chalcogenide compound Ge2Sb2Te5 (GST) is widely exploited in rewritable optical disk storage technology and non-volatile electronic memories due to its good thermal stability and high switching speed.179 As illustrated in Fig. 14, two basic approaches have been applied in the GST-based active optical devices. First, as in the optical disk, a designed pattern can be written with high accuracy in the chalcogenide glass phase-change film by trains of femtosecond pulses (Fig. 14a). Chen et al. proposed an approach to engineer the phase front at 1.55 μm in the conventional telecom band.180 By introducing GST into a metallic slit array as shown by the schematic drawing in Fig. 14b, they were able to achieve different focusing patterns from a geometrically fixed planar lens. By constructing twenty one such slits into a planar lens, the phase front of a plane wave can be further shaped with various curvatures.

Although phase-change materials are promising in tunable meta-devices, several challenges still exist. First, it is difficult to change the local phase state when the size of arrays becomes very large; secondly, the optical and electric properties are not ideal for practical applications. For example, the electrical conductivity of VO2 is 140 S m−1 and 5 × 105 S m−1 before and after the phase transition,182 which are not small and large enough to behave as a pure dielectric and conductor.

6. Fabrication of plasmonic structures

The high-precision fabrication of nanostructures (characterized by optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy and transmission electron microscopy, etc.) is essential for the optical performance of plasmonic metamaterials and metasurfaces. Chemical synthesis was the primary means of growing different shaped metal nanoparticles by controlling the reaction conditions such as temperature, surfactants, and precursors. However, the uniformity of these nanostructures is very bad and it is a challenge to fabricate nanostructure arrays with well-defined orders.183

In order to fabricate artificially designed periodic and quasi-periodic metamaterials and metasurfaces, mechanic and optical approaches (including lithography and laser direct writing) are needed. As the working frequency range goes into the near-infrared and visible spectra, the characteristic dimensions typically decrease to sub-100 nanometers, which could not be realized by traditional mechanical or optical methods. Instead, electron beam lithography (EBL) and focused ion beam milling (FIB) are frequently adopted for experimental demonstrations.184 Recently, some novel techniques such as plasmonic lithography and nanosphere lithography (NSL) have been proposed for the fabrication of plasmonic structures.44,49,185,186 While plasmonic lithography and other near-field-based optical lithography are promising alternatives to traditional expensive lithography techniques,48,187 the NSL is particularly suited for the creation of large-area, single-layer metasurfaces.

Besides the fabrication technologies aforementioned, some other methods are also introduced in some special situations. The capacities of nanoimprint, multiple photon polymerization and microsphere lithography have been demonstrated for making various subwavelength structures.80,188,189

7. Conclusions

In summary, we have reviewed the basic principles and recent progress of 2D photonic devices based on plasmonic effects in complex metal–dielectric structures. In general, there are two main research directions: how to break the diffraction limit and how to control light at the deep subwavelength scale. On the one hand, the short wavelength effect of SPPs and their application in light localization and super-resolution imaging/nanolithography are highlighted. On the other hand, the exchange of the freely propagating and bound waves assisted by the subwavelength inclusions is found to be of particular importance. By locally tuning the bound waves’ complex propagation constants and scattering behaviors, their optical properties including the phase, amplitude and polarization of the re-emitted waves could be arbitrarily controlled, and therefore many traditional rules such as Snell’ law and Fresnel's equations could be revised or reversed.19,23 In particular, when the directly reflected and re-emitted waves interfere destructively, all incident waves would be completely converted into bound waves and finally absorbed by the lossy materials. Under such critical coupling conditions, the corresponding devices could be termed perfect absorbers. At the end of this review, some promising techniques and materials such as dispersion engineering, doped-semiconductor-based plasmonic devices, refractory materials, and phase-change materials are introduced, which we think will open a new avenue for both fundamental and practical applications across the areas of nanophotonic and plasmonic metamaterials.
