Abstract

Phosphor converted white light-emitting diodes (pc-WLEDs) are an alternative choice for general lighting due to their superior features such as high efficiency, durability and reliability. However, most pc-WLEDs in the market suffer from problems resulting from the lack of red emission, which can be resolved by adding red-emitting phosphors. The currently dominant red-emitting phosphors are Eu2+-doped nitrides, but the requirement of elevated temperature during synthesis makes them costly, and moreover the over broad emission may result in loss of lumen efficiency. Recently, transition metal Mn4+ doped materials showing very narrow red emission have attracted tremendous interest for compositions based on abundant resources and mild production processes, resulting in a highly efficient way to obtain phosphors with favorable luminescence properties. In this work, we describe the recent progress on transition metal Mn4+-doped inorganic luminescent materials, including oxides (subdivision into alkaline-earth germanates, arsenates, aluminates, titanates, pyrosilicates, phosphates, zirconates, gallates, simple oxides and others), fluorides and nitrides. More specifically, the review focuses on Mn4+ activated red-emitting phosphors that can be effectively excited by NUV and blue LED chips. The excitation and emission spectra as well as the preparation process of some representative phosphors are given and discussed. Meanwhile, the merits and drawbacks of several kinds of matrix materials in applications for white LEDs, as well as some problems, development trends and application prospects in this field of Mn4+ doped phosphors are summarized.

1. Introduction

In the past few decades, rapid progress has been made in the fabrication and utilization of light emitting diodes (LEDs), especially white LEDs (WLEDs), which show superior features such as high efficiency, compactness, long operational lifetime, good material stability and environmental friendliness. The major WLEDs presently on the market are phosphor-converted LEDs (pc-LEDs) made of a blue-emitting chip (usually InGaN, the most practical chip with the lowest price) covered with a yellow-emitting phosphor [i.e. (Y,Gd)3(Al,Ga)5O12:Ce3+, abbreviated as YAG:Ce3+].1–4 However, the luminescence of the present WLEDs suffers from problems like cool white light and low colour rendering index (CRI), which result from the lack of red emission. In order to get warm white-light emission together with a high CRI, a red-emitting phosphor needs to be added into the system, where it plays a key role in solid-state lighting for artificial lighting applications. Generally, the phosphors applied in an LED device must fulfill the following main requirements:4 (1) be highly excitable in the UV-blue spectral range; (2) have high quantum efficiency and strong red emission intensity; (3) have high chemical and thermal stability (retaining their luminescence characteristics over long periods at 150 °C). Because of these ambitious prerequisites, designing a red-emitting phosphor according to the appropriate utilization in LEDs remains a major challenge. The main red-emitting phosphors dominating the current market are Eu2+-doped nitrides (Ca,Sr)AlSiN3:Eu2+, which are mostly synthesized by applying temperatures above 1800 °C and a high nitrogen gas pressure. Thus, the demand for a large amount of input energy combined with a scarcity of the nitride phosphor starting material makes them somewhat costly.2,3 In addition, a better compromise between high lumen efficiency and high colour rendering index can be obtained by very narrow red-emitting phosphors.5 It is therefore urgent to develop new red-emitting phosphors for the future WLEDs market.

Transition metal elements, in particular manganese, are widely used in the fabrication of green (divalent) and red (tetravalent and divalent) emitting phosphors. Manganese, an element with a rich oxidation state variation, where valence, local coordination and distribution in the host lattices determine its luminescence properties, is considered as an alternative to rare earth elements for the design of suitable phosphors. The crystal field dependences of the energy levels of transition metal ions are usually described using a Tanabe–Sugano diagram. Fig. 1 shows the Tanabe–Sugano diagram for Mn2+, Mn3+ and Mn4+ ions in octahedral symmetry. The Mn2+ ion, acting as an activator, has an emission consisting of a broad band ranging from green to deep red, the position of which strongly depends on the host lattice. The Mn2+ ion located in tetrahedral coordination with a weak crystal field, usually gives a green emission; while the Mn2+ ion situated in an octahedral symmetry environment with a strong crystal field, usually gives an orange to red emission. According to the Tanabe–Sugano diagram for Mn2+ with a d5 electron configuration (see Fig. 1(a)), the emission can be assigned to the 4T1 → 6A1 transition. Examples of the Mn2+ ion acting as an activator are BaAl12O19:Mn2+,6 ZnS:Mn2+,7 LiZnPO4:Mn2+,8 KAlSi2O6:Mn2+,9 Zn2SiO4:Mn2+.10–14 Zn2SiO4:Mn2+, which emits green light, is associated with the 4T1 → 6A1 transition of Mn2+.10–14 In this phosphor material, the doped Mn2+ is supposed to predominantly substitute at the Zn2+ site with a tetrahedral [MnO4] coordination.

Mn3+ ion seems to be an interesting candidate as an active laser center within this spectral region, on account of its emission covering the entire wavelength region between 550 and 1300 nm.15,16 The crystal field splitting of the Mn3+ energy levels is described by the Tanabe–Sugano diagram according to the d4 electron configuration (see Fig. 1(b)).17 Although a large number of energy levels exist in a d4 electron system, two of them are quintet states, named the 5E ground state and the 5T2 excited state. Many excited-state absorption transitions from the 5T2 into higher lying levels are expected, but all of these transitions are spin-forbidden, i.e., the excited-state absorption cross section is much smaller than the emission cross section of the 5T2 → 5E transition.

All Mn4+-doped materials exhibit both broadband excitation and sharp emission lines due to the distinct electronic structure of Mn4+. The Mn4+ ion belongs to the d3 electron configuration by no accident, the crystal field splitting of the Mn4+ ions energy levels is described by the Tanabe–Sugano diagram in an octahedral symmetry environment (see Fig. 1(c)).17 In an octahedral environment, the Mn-3d states are split into three- and two-fold degenerate t2g and eg states, respectively. The three Mn-3d electrons of Mn4+ exactly fill the majority-spin t2g states. The crystal field splitting creates a large gap between the t2g and eg states, stabilizing the 4+ oxidation state.18 Therefore, Mn4+ ions are usually found on the octahedral sites of solids. In addition, the optical properties of Mn4+ doped materials are strongly influenced by local crystal field environments. For example, the excitation and emission peaks of Mn4+ in oxide hosts usually appear at 300 and 660 nm, respectively. However, when Mn4+ ions are situated in fluoride hosts with octahedral coordination, they exhibit the most intense broadband excitation at 460 nm with a bandwidth of 50 nm and very sharp emission lines peaking at 630 nm as a result of the weaker crystal field for Mn4+ ions. These unique optical properties meet the spectral requirements for an ideal red-emitting phosphor, in addition to rather simple synthetic processes and abundant starting materials, enable Mn4+-doped compounds to be promising candidates as red-emitting phosphors for blue-chip excited warm white LEDs with high luminous efficiency of radiation. The available literature about different types of Mn4+-doped materials includes: (1) oxides, subdivided into (1.1) alkaline-earth germanates19–39 and arsenates;40–42 (1.2) aluminates;43–90 (1.3) titanates;91–105 (1.4) pyrosilicates;106–112 (1.5) phosphates;113–115 (1.6) zirconates;116,117 (1.7) gallate;118–129 (1.8) simple oxides130–141 and (1.9) other oxides;18,142–149 (2) fluorides150–219 and (3) nitrides,220–224 all of which are summarized in Fig. 3(a). The early research on Mn4+-doped materials was basically focused on germanate systems, until the end of the 20th century. The research emphasis gradually transferred to aluminate systems from 2000. Mn4+ activated fluoride phosphors have attracted considerable interest and became a research hotspot in 2008 due to their potential application as red-emitting phosphors in LED devices. However, the utilization of fluoride phosphors in high-power LEDs is largely impeded by their low stability. Additionally, the Mn4+ emission wavelengths of more chemically stable oxide phosphors are longer than those of fluorides. This is an important trend and can explain the higher 2Eg → 4A2g emission energies of Mn4+ in fluorides than in oxides by recognizing the weaker Mn–ligand hybridization in fluorides.

The first synthesis of Mn4+-doped luminescent materials was reported in 1947;19 since then the research on this topic has never been interrupted. Especially over the last 10 years, the number of publications in this field has massively increased (see Fig. 3(b)), reflecting their promising capability for optical applications. In order to obtain a better understanding, this review, for the first time, offers a complete overview of more than 200 references published in this field, with highlights of different Mn4+-doped inorganic compounds and their luminescence characteristics.

2. Mn4+-doped phosphors and their luminescence properties

With the same electronic structure (3d3 electron configuration) of the incompletely filled shell as Cr3+,17 the electrons of the Mn4+ ion are located in an outer orbital, due to which its optical properties can be heavily affected by the matrix. The Mn4+ ion with three d electrons has a ground level of 4A2. Thus, the optical absorption will, in good approximation, occur only to the spin-quartet level (2S + 1 = 4) in view of the spin selection rule.225 In other words, the red-emitting wavelengths consisting of several associated vibronic sidebands are highly dependent on the covalency and coordination symmetry of the “Mn4+–ligand” bonding, which is due to the spin-forbidden transition of Mn4+: 2Eg → 4A2g. As a result, the host lattice plays a crucial role in the optical properties of Mn4+-doped compounds.

2.1 Mn4+-doped alkaline-earth germanates and arsenate systems

The first report on Mn4+ activated germanate luminescent materials was dated in 1947.19 This work investigated magnesium oxide in detail with respect to the luminescence efficiency. The phosphor prepared according to the formula 4MgO·GeO2:0.01Mn (Mg4GeO6:Mn4+) has five times higher efficiency than 2MgO·GeO2:0.01Mn (Mg2GeO4:Mn4+), while 3MgO·GeO2:0.01Mn and 5MgO·GeO2:0.01Mn have about 75% of the efficiency of the optimum composition. Subsequently, a typical example of Mn4+ doped germanate phosphors is 3.5MgO·0.5MgF2·GeO2:Mn4+ (Mg28Ge10O48:Mn4+), which was derived by replacing part of MgO in 4MgO·GeO2 with a fluxing agent MgF2. The part substitution of O by F can give significant rise to the sample's luminescence intensity.21 The deep red emission associated with the 2E2 → 4A2 transition in octahedra is attributed to Mn4+. In the Mg28Ge10O48 host lattice, Mg2+ ions occupy octahedral sites, while two-thirds of the Ge4+ ions are tetrahedrally coordinated. Mn4+ ions may enter the Ge4+ or the Mg2+ ion sites. Shamshurin et al.27 suggested that Mn4+ enters the Ge4+ lattice with a similar radius and the same charge, but Blasse226 speculated that Mn4+ ions probably occupy octahedral Mg2+ sites, though Mn4+ has the same ionic radius as Ge4+. The commercialization of 3.5MgO·0.5MgF2·GeO2:Mn4+ red emitting materials was realized in the 1960s. These phosphors have been applied as colour correction phosphors in high pressure Hg lamps, plant growth fluorescent lamps, red light phototherapy equipment, and many energy-saving fluorescent lights.25

Furthermore, Okamoto S.29 found that the emission intensity of 3.5MgO·0.5MgF2·GeO2:Mn4+ red-emitting phosphors can be enhanced by adding a certain amount of alkaline earth fluoride instead of MgF2 [(3.5 − x)MgO·0.5MgF2·xAF2·GeO2:Mn4+ (A = Ca, Sr, Mg, Ba)]. The optimal alkaline earth fluoride is SrF2. In this case, the Mg2+ ions occupy the center of the octahedral coordination sites by six oxygen atoms with two different Mg2+–O2− bond distances. And Mn4+ ions were suggested to occupy Mg2+ sites in the center octahedral symmetry. Yuan, et al.38 investigated the effect of adding Al2O3, and observed a blue-shift with the increase of Al2O3 addition. The reason can be attributed to the crystal field strength Dq of the Mg2+ octahedral lattice sites, which could be changed by an Al3+ additive due to the variation of the Mn4+–O2− bond length. According to the results, the 6MgO·As2O5:Mn4+ arsenate has the strongest luminescent efficiency, with a band-like red emission consisting of five narrow peaks at 620 nm, 630 nm, 640 nm, 650 nm and 658 nm, respectively. 2.2 Mn4+-doped aluminate system

Aluminates are the most intensively investigated host lattices for Mn4+ ions, among which, CaAl12O19:Mn4+,43,48 is the most reported phosphor. The main reason probably is that this phosphor could exhibit strong red emission under UV and blue excitation, which is suitable for LED applications. Murata T. et al.48 found that CaAl12O19:Mn4+ phosphor can be excited by 400 nm wavelength near UV light excitation, and CaF2 and MgF2 can improve the relative luminescence intensity of CaAl12O19:Mn4+, which should be a consequence of the synergetic effect caused by the flux and charge compensation of CaF2 and MgF2, respectively. CaF2 would accelerate the crystal growth of CaAl12O19:Mn4+ and Mg2+ ions would compensate the local charge balance surrounding Mn4+ ions instead of Mn2+. Moreover, as recently reported, a remarkable enhancement of the luminescence intensity of the CaAl12O19:Mn4+ phosphor has been achieved by composition modification through mixing with MgO,57,58 GeO2,61 and Li+, Na+ and K+ ions.68 However, its counterpart, BaAl12O19:Mn4+,52 does not efficiently emit under UV excitation. The luminescence properties of Ba-containing compounds are related to different co-ordinations of the activator ions, which makes them deviate from other aluminate compounds (i.e. CaAl12O19:Mn4+).

Besides CaAl12O19:Mn4+, other aluminates, e.g., Sr2MgAl22O36:Mn4+,59 CaAl4O7:Mn4+,60,70 SrAl4O7:Mn4+,88 SrAl2O4:Mn4+,78 CaAl2O4:Mn4+,78 Sr4Al14O25:Mn4+,71,75,79,80,89 CaMg2Al16O27:Mn4+,85 CaYAlO4:Mn4+,86 and SrMgAl10O17:Mn4+,52,77,82 have been also investigated and reported. In addition to the above mentioned aluminates, a novel Ca14Al10Zn6O35:Mn4+81,83,84 phosphor showed emissions in the wavelength range from 650 to 750 nm at room temperature with the maximum value located at 715 nm when excited at 460 nm. In the crystal lattice of Ca14Al10Zn6O35, with a cubic structure with the space group F23, four of the five independent positions that are occupied by Al3+ and Zn2+ are in tetrahedral coordination, and the fifth independent position is in octahedral coordination. 2.3 Mn4+-doped titanate system

Titanate compounds are well known for their excellent stability and, thus, are considered as a kind of suitable host material for luminescent phosphors such as Ca0.8Zn0.2TiO3:Pr3+,227 Ca2Zn4Ti16O38:Pr3+,228 CaTiO3:Eu3+,229 and Mg2TiO4:Eu3+.230 One remarkable example of an Mn4+-doped titanate is Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ synthesized by a sol–gel route, which efficiently emits red light with a wavelength centered at 655 nm.102 Mg2TiO4 has an inverse spinel structure, in which the Ti ions occupy octahedral sites and the Mg ions occupy both tetrahedral and octahedral sites. The ionic radius of Mn4+ for six-coordination is 0.53 Å, close to that of six-coordination Ti4+ (0.605 Å).231 In addition, the same oxidation state indicates that charge compensation is not necessary for the substitution of Mn4+ for Ti4+. Therefore, the substitution of Mn4+ for Ti4+ in Mg2TiO4 is highly feasible. The results of X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS) and electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy investigations confirmed the octahedral coordination and the tetravalent state of the doped Mn ions in the Mg2TiO4 material. Moreover, a decrease in lattice parameter was observed with the increase of Mn4+ content in Mg2TiO4, further confirming the replacement of Ti4+ ions by smaller Mn4+ ions in the host lattice. Fig. 8(a) shows the excitation spectrum of Mg2TiO4:Mn4+.102 The strong excitation band at 475 nm is assigned to the 4A2 → 4T2 transition of the Mn4+ ion, whereas an even stronger broad excitation band at 325 nm arises from the 4A2 → 4T1 transition. Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ shows emissions in the wavelength range between 600 and 775 nm at room temperature with the maximum value located at 655 nm in the visible light region. Fig. 8(b) shows the emission spectra of Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ phosphors prepared through sol–gel and solid-state reaction methods under the same excitation at 475 nm. The main peaks from 640 to 680 nm correspond to the 2E2(t23) → 4A2(t23) transition of the Mn4+ ion. Obviously, the emission intensity of the Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ phosphors prepared through the sol–gel route is approximately 1.5 times higher than that of the solid-state reaction methods. Moreover, the photoluminescence performance of the sol–gel Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ sample was also compared with that of the commercial red-emitting phosphor CaAlSiN3:Eu2+ purchased from Intematix. For both Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ and CaAlSiN3:Eu2+, the emission bands peak at approximately 650 nm, and the integrated emission intensity of Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ is comparable to that of CaAlSiN3:Eu2+ under the same 460 nm light excitation. However, the measured external quantum efficiency of the sample Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ is around 38.3% and lower than that of the commercial CaAlSiN3:Eu2+ red-emitting phosphor. It is believed that the observed low external quantum efficiency of Mg2TiO4:Mn4+ may be further improved by process optimization.

2.5 Mn4+-doped phosphate, zirconate and gallate systems

Phosphates, zirconates and gallates have also been investigated as suitable host lattices for Mn4+ doped phosphors. Ming, C. et al.114 investigated the behavior of the photoluminescence of Mn4+ doped phosphate glasses (80P2O5–20Li2O). Under 408 nm wavelength light excitation, the luminescence wavelengths of the samples are located in the 600–700 nm range. With increasing Mn4+ ion concentration, the emission peak wavelength red shifts gradually, the emission intensity increases gradually and the luminescence lifetime changes from a few microseconds to one millisecond. According to the fluorescence lifetime analysis, the following conclusions can be obtained: (a) at low Mn4+ ion concentration, the fluorescence comes from the 2T1 → 4A2 transition of Mn4+ ion, whose fluorescence lifetime is several microseconds; (b) at high Mn4+ ion concentration, the emission originates from the 2E → 4A2 transition of Mn4+ ion, whose lifetime is about one millisecond. The sample doped with Mn4+ with a concentration of 2.4 mol% shows the strongest emission intensity. Another Mn4+-doped phosphate α-Zn2P2O7:Mn4+ was also investigated by Gupta, S. K. et al.115 In this work, both Mn2+ and Mn4+ were presented in the α-Zn2P2O7 host. EPR spectroscopy study confirmed the presence of Mn2+ and Mn4+ in 6-coordinated and distorted 5-coordinated Zn2+ sites, respectively. In the emission spectra, two bands were observed: one is green emission with maximum intensity centered at 500 nm, which should be attributed to the 4T1(4G)–6A1(6S) transition of Mn2+; the other is red emission centered at 686 nm, which results from the electronic transition between 2E and 4A2 of Mn4+ accompanied with vibronic transitions. The photoluminescence properties of Mn4+-doped zirconate CaZrO3:Mn4+ were discussed by G. Blasse, et al.116 in 1981. The CaZrO3:Mn4+ shows an efficient red luminescence ranging from 650 to 750 nm with the maximum emission peak located at 690 nm. However, only luminescence spectra at liquid nitrogen temperature were measured.

For gallate-based compounds doped with Mn4+ ions, typical examples are Gd3Ga5O12:Mn4+,119,122–124 ZnGa2O4:Mn4+ and LiGa5O8:Mn4+.120,121,125,127 The luminescence emission spectrum of Gd3Ga5O12:Mn4+ measured at room temperature exhibits characteristic emission bands in the range of 650–700 nm with the maximum value at 682 nm. Earlier studies have indicated that the divalent manganese (Mn2+) ions in the tetrahedral (A) sites of several AB2O4 spinel compounds including ZnGa2O4, MgGa2O4, ZnAl2O4, MgGa2O4 and MgAl2O4, behave as an activator for green fluorescence. But in the work of Yu, C. et al.,121 both Mn2+ and Mn4+ were presented in ZnGa2O4 host as shown by their typical emissions under different excited wavelengths. The Mn2+ and Mn4+ ions seated at the tetrahedral (A) sites and octahedral (B) sites, respectively. The red emission spectrum, excited at 325 nm, shows a peak centered at 666 nm. The other is green emission centered at 508 nm. However, the emission intensities of these phosphors are relatively low.

2.6 Mn4+-doped simple oxide and other material systems

Simple oxides can also be utilized as the host lattices for Mn4+ doped phosphors. Sharp emission lines situated at 14866 cm−1 and 14786 cm−1 have been reported for Mn4+-doped α-Al2O3 by Geschwind, S. et al.130 The Mn4+ valence state was stabilized by charge compensation by Mg2+ ions. The main objective of the work of Jovanić, B. R.132 was to explore the effect of high pressure (up to 10 GPa) on the emission spectrum of α-Al2O3:Mn4+. Besides, other Mn4+-doped oxides, for example, MgO,137 TiO2,136,138 and MnO2139 have been reported. However, only the zero-field splitting and the first excited state splitting and EPR parameters of the compound were investigated.

In addition to the above mentioned simple oxide systems, other more complex Mn4+-activated oxide materials systems have attracted attention in recent years, such as Ba2LaNbO6:Mn4+,146 NaLaMgTeO6:Mn4+,147 La2LiTaO6:Mn4+,18 and Mg7Ga2GeO12:Mn4+.149 La2LiTaO6:Mn4+18 shows a broad deep red emission ranging from 670 to 730 nm with the main peak located at 709 nm under the excitation of 495 nm. The novel deep red-emitting Mg7Ga2GeO12:Mn4+149 phosphor shows bright emission in the 600–750 nm region combined with efficient broad excitation at around 420 nm. Mg6.965Ga2GeO12:0.5mol%Mn4+, Mg7Ga1.990GeO12:0.5mol%Mn4+ and Mg7Ga2Ge0.995O12:0.5mol%Mn4+ phosphors have been prepared and their emission spectra were measured. It was found that the Mg7Ga1.990GeO12:0.5 mol%Mn4+ sample, in which the Mn4+ ions are assumed to enter into the Ga3+ sites, shows much higher emission intensity than the other two samples. This result confirms that Mn4+ should replace the Ga3+ site in GaO6 octahedra, leading to efficient deep red emission of Mn4+ ions. The phosphor also displays a good thermal stability (78% of the initial intensity remained at 250 °C) and therefore, Mg7Ga2GeO12:Mn4+ is considered to be a potential red emitting phosphor for application in WLEDs.

Hence, serious re-absorption that often occurs between (oxy)nitride red-emitting phosphors and yellow (or green) -emitting phosphors in warm white LEDs can be avoided. The red emission is composed of several sharp lines distributed in the spectral range of 610–650 nm with the strongest peak at 631.5 nm due to the spin-forbidden 2Eg → 4A2 transition. 2.8 Mn4+-doped nitride systems

For nitride-based compounds doped with Mn4+ ions, one typical example is Mn4+-doped GaN semiconductors, which have been extensively investigated previously because of their scientific importance, technological applications and ferromagnetism.222,223 This system has been reported to be a ferromagnetic semiconductor with a high Curie temperature due to large p–d hybridization and small spin–orbit coupling. The ferromagnetic properties of GaN:Mn depend strongly on the electronic configuration of the Mn4+ ions.223 Mn-doped and Mn–Mg co-doped GaN were grown by metal–organic vapor phase epitaxy.222 Photoluminescence (PL) and photoluminescence excitation (PLE) spectroscopic investigations indicate that a series of sharp emission lines at 1.0 eV (1240 nm) are observed in Mn–Mg co-doped GaN and attributed to the spin-allowed intra d-shell transition 4T2(F)–4T1(F) of Mn4+ ions. According to the Tanabe–Sugano diagram of a d3 ion (Mn4+) in a tetrahedral crystal field (GaN), PLE peaks observed at 1.79 and 2.33 eV, which is a normal characteristic of the intra center transition, are attributed to the spin allowed intra-d-shell transitions from the 4T1(F) ground state to the 4T1(P) and 4A2(F) excited state, respectively. The intra d-shell excitation processes can be explained as follows: when the pump energy is tuned resonantly with one of its excited states, the Mn4+ ion is excited from the ground state to the corresponding excited state; the excited Mn4+ ion subsequently relaxes non-radiatively to the first excited 4T2(F) state before the 4T2(F)–4T1(F) PL transition at 1.0 eV.

3 Outlook and future prospects

This review highlights the development of Mn4+-activated optical materials. Mn4+-doped alkaline aluminates show promise in white LEDs but the weak UV-blue absorption and low efficiency of their luminescence requires further improvement. K2XF6 (X = Si, Ti) -based materials doped with Mn4+ ions, exhibit the most intense excitation band located at around 460 nm and very sharp red emission lines peaking at about 630 nm. However, their thermal stability seems to be a problem and should be further improved; moreover, in the synthesis process, the required toxic HF solution is very harmful to environment. In contrast, oxide compounds show much higher chemical stability with an eco-friendly preparation procedure, but the blue excitation band cannot meet the needs right now.

As described above, in the search for suitable phosphors, significant effort has been made to develop highly efficient LED devices with good colour rendering properties. Fig. 15 gives the developed scheme of phosphors for WLEDs. The insets are photographs of phosphors under irradiation of a day-light lamp. Table 7 shows the colour of the corresponding phosphors under irradiation of a day-light lamp and a 460 nm blue chip. In recent years, several highly luminous white LEDs have been fabricated based on the combination of a blue LED with YAG:Ce3+ (yellow-emitting) or silicate-based phosphors such as (Sr,Ba)2SiO4:Eu2+ (green-, yellow-emitting), and (Sr,Ba)3SiO5:Eu2+ (orange-emitting), but all of them have a low colour-rendering index and strong thermal quenching. Nitride-based red emitting phosphors such as (Sr,Ca)2Si5N8:Eu2+ and (Sr,Ca)AlSiN3:Eu2+ showing better thermal stability and luminescence intensity than CaS:Eu2+ and (oxy)nitride BaSi2O2N2:Eu2+ blue-green-emitting phosphors have weaker thermal quenching, but they all exhibit rather poor luminescence efficiency. Although K2TiF6:Mn4+ and K2SiF6:Mn4+ red-emitting phosphors have better luminescence efficiency and color purity, their chemical stability is not very good. The combination of the Ga-YAG:Ce3+ yellow-green-emitting phosphor and the K2TiF6:Mn4+ red-emitting phosphor was recently proposed as the optimal mixture for application in ultrahigh CRI (CRI = 95) and high efficiency cool white light (CCT = 5954 K) LEDs. More studies will be performed to illustrate the luminescence mechanism and the distribution of the Mn element in the near future. Briefly, considerable efforts will be devoted to the further improvement of Mn4+-activated phosphors for application in WLEDs, including surface modification by coating and host-lattice modification by ion-doping.
