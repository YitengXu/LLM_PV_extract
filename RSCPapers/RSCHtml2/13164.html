Abstract

Due to the high sodium ion conductivity, sodium super ionic conductors (NASICONs) are among the most promising candidates as solid electrolytes in solid state batteries and have therefore gained enormous attention in recent years. Previous experimental and computational investigations show excellent sodium ion conductivity for Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 with x = 2â€“2.5. In order to elucidate the conductivity maximum at high substitution levels, we investigate the influence of the cation environment on the site energies of sodium ions and the correlated migration using density functional theory. The results reveal that the site energy strongly depends on electrostatic effects. In addition, an increasing fraction of sodium and silicon ions enhances the correlated migration due to the increasing coulombic repulsion of sodium ions and opening of the bottleneck along the migration path. Based on the results, we generate an energy model to predict configurational and migration energy in subsequent Kinetic Monte Carlo simulations. We show that sodium ions are trapped due to introduced silicon ions but percolate in the system for xâ‰¥2.0, which greatly increases the ionic conductivity.

1. Introduction

Clean and sustainable energy is becoming increasingly important as the extensive use of fossil fuels has raised concerns in terms of resource limitations and environmental impact.1â€“3 However, the supply of renewable energies, such as wind and solar power, is not adjustable and there is a large gap between production and demand. Large-scale energy storage and conversion devices are required to integrate renewable energies smoothly into the electrical grid and expand sustainable energy supply.4 Therefore, it is crucial to develop and improve reliable electrochemical energy storage (EES) technologies. In particular, batteries provide high energy density and conversion efficiency combined with long cycle life as well as low maintenance.5,6

One of the most established storage devices are lithium-ion batteries with high energy densities and output voltage.7 Nevertheless, the availability of resources leads to concern about its cost development.8â€“11 Sodium is a promising alternative due to its unlimited abundance and low cost as well as its low redox potential that is only 0.3 V above that of lithium. Admittedly, sodium is larger and heavier than lithium leading to lower energy density.11â€“16 Nevertheless, sodium-ion batteries can be an alternative to lithium-ion batteries where energy density requirements are less demanding, such as stationary EES systems.17â€“20

The efficiency of electrochemical devices depends crucially on the ionic conductivity of the applied electrolyte. Ceramic electrolytes are promising alternatives to conventional liquid electrolytes due to their high thermal stability and mechanical strength as well as wide electrochemical window. Furthermore, they can potentially enable both high energy and power density of batteries. Due to the numerous advantages, sodium based all-solid-state batteries receive increasing attention.21â€“29 However, not only the interfacial compatibility between electrolytes and electrode materials could be poor but also the ionic conductivity of solid electrolytes, which depends on the structure and composition poses a major challenge.21,24,26â€“31

NASICON (Na+ superionic conductor) materials are attracting considerable attention within the group of ceramic electrolytes since the observation of fast sodium ionic conductivity in Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 (0 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 3).28,31â€“33 The flexible framework allows a great compositional diversity that leads to many possible applications.31,34,35 Depending on the composition and temperature, the ionic conductivity of NASICON materials varies by several orders of magnitude.28,31 An extensive literature study of doped NaxM2SixP3âˆ’xO12 NASICONs by Guin et al. shows that materials exhibit highest conductivities when containing 3â€“3.5 sodium ions per formula unit, M cation with an ionic radius close to r = 0.72 Ã… as well as silicon ions substituting phosphorous ions.35 Ma et al. reported one of the highest total conductivities of 5 Ã— 10âˆ’3 S cmâˆ’1 at room temperature for Na3.4Zr2Si2.4P0.6O12.36

1.1 Structure

At room temperature, Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 crystalizes in the rhombohedral phase (Rc), but in the compositional range of 1.8 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 2.2 a monoclinic distortion (C2/c) occurs. The low-temperature monoclinic structure undergoes a second order phase transition to rhombohedral symmetry at around 150 Â°C.32,36â€“45 Both structures consist of a framework of corner sharing PO4 tetrahedra and ZrO6 octahedra with sodium ion sites filling the cavities (Fig. 1). In the rhombohedral structure, there are two different sodium sites, the Na1 (6b) and Na2 (18e) site, whereas in the monoclinic structure there are the Na1 (4d), Na2 (4e) and Na3 (8f) sites.32,40,42,46â€“48 In the structure with x = 0 all sodium ions occupy the energetically more favorable Na1 positions.46,49â€“51 With increasing x the phosphorus ions get substituted by silicon ions and the overall charge is maintained by an increasing amount of sodium ions. In the fully substituted structure with x = 3, all sodium sites are occupied, as shown in Fig. 1. Additionally, transient mid-Na sites located between Na1 and Na2 sites are reported in structures with intermediate Na contents.43,49,51â€“54 Coulombic forces between sodium ions increase with x, which leads to a shift of some Na1 ions to mid-Na positions towards unoccupied Na2 sites. The occupation of mid-Na sites excludes the occupation of adjacent Na1 and Na2 sites. For that reason, the occupation of mid-Na sites tends to zero in structures above x = 2 since there are no unoccupied Na2 sites.43,49,51,52 For sake of simplicity, we focus on the occupation of the equilibrium Na1 and Na2 sites in this work.

1.2 Migration

The excellent ionic conductivity of NASICON materials is attributed to the three-dimensional framework of the structure with multiple sodium positions that provides high mobility of the charge carriers.32,33 The pioneering work of Hong and Goodenough et al. and following experimental32,33,47,48,55 as well as computational51â€“53,56 studies show that the sodium ions migrate via Na1 and Na2/Na3 sites rather than solely Na2/Na3 sites. As suggested by lower energy barriers, the concerted migration mechanism which involves the interstitialcy-like correlated migration of sodium ions on regular lattice sites (Na2/Na3â€“Na1â€“Na2/Na3) is preferred over single ion migration. During the correlated migration, the potential energy is transferred from ions at energetically higher Na2/Na3 sites to ions at energetically lower Na1 sites leading to a reduction of the migration energy barrier.52,53,57,58 Coulomb repulsion between the mobile ions pushes one ion to a neighboring site and causes another pushing-out event. Bottlenecks formed by ZrO6 octahedral and PO4 tetrahedral edges determine the migration energy.28,45,48,51,53â€“55 The high conductivity of compositions with x = 2â€“2.5 is not only attributed to the high concentration of mobile charge carriers, but also to the widening of the bottleneck that promotes the ion mobility.28,32,40,45,47,54 Furthermore, increasing sodium ion concentration enhances the correlated migration by increasing sodiumâ€“sodium coulombic repulsions.49,52 This type of collective migration appears also in fast lithium ion conductors, such as in NASICON-,59,60 LISICON-61,62 and garnet63â€“65 type structures.

The understanding of ionic motion is essential to improve the conductivity of solids. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations and computational simulation techniques enable investigations of sodium ion migration in NASICON structures on multi-scale level as demonstrated in the past.51â€“53,56,66â€“70 Here, we studied the sodium ion migration and conductivity in the NASICON structure with composition Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 (0 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 3) using a combination of density functional theory (DFT) and kinetic Monte Carlo (KMC) simulation. We investigated the different sodium sites and the influence of the cation arrangement on the sodium site energies. In addition, we investigated the migration energy of mobile sodium ions regarding different pathways and compositions. Finally, we applied the ab initio results in subsequent KMC simulations allowing the investigation of ionic conductivity in large systems. Thus, the macroscopic sodium ion conductivity is linked to the local ionic environment and different migration pathways and energies. Our study reveals that not only the increasing amount of charge carriers and coulombic sodiumâ€“sodium repulsion as well as opening of the bottleneck of migration pathway enhances the ionic conductivity but also trapping and percolation effects of the charge carriers due to introduced silicon ions must be taken into account.

2. Method and model

2.1 Density functional theory

General computational setup. Optimization of monoclinic and rhombohedral structures and calculations of sodium ion migration within these structures were performed by Kohn-Sham DFT71,72 using the Vienna Ab Initio Simulation Package (VASP).73â€“76 The exchange correlation contribution was described by the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) parametrized by Perdewâ€“Burkeâ€“Ernzerhof (PBEsol).77 Plane waves with energy cut-offs of 500 eV for constant pressure and 400 eV for constant volume calculations were used and the projector augmented wave (PAW) method was applied.78 After optimization of the cell parameters, the volume was kept fixed in subsequent calculations. The convergence criteria of ionic and electronic relaxation were set to 0.01 eV Ã…âˆ’1 and 10âˆ’4 eV, respectively. A k-point mesh centered at the gamma point according to Monkhorst and Pack scheme was used to sample the Brillouin zone.79 For calculations of conventional cells a 2 Ã— 2 Ã— 2 mesh and for calculation of supercells a 1 Ã— 1 Ã— 1 mesh was chosen. The 2p63s1 electrons of sodium, the 4s24p64d25s2 electrons of zirconium, the 3s23p2 of silicon, the 3s23p3 electrons of phosphorous and the 2s22p4 electrons of oxygen were considered as valence electrons.
Configurational energies. Optimization of volume and lattice parameters was performed using conventional unit cells containing up to 126 atoms. The influence of the cation arrangement on configurational energy was investigated in cubic supercells with up to 168 atoms built from the primitive cell with optimized volume and lattice parameters using the following rotation matrices. If necessary, a homogeneous background charge was introduced to compensate the defect charge.
Migration energies. The concerted migration of two sodium ions as well as the single ion migration was calculated in structures with x = 0, 1, 2, 3. In the case of the unsubstituted system with composition Na1Zr2P3O12 (x = 0), one additional sodium ion was introduced into the cell, while in the case of fully substituted system with composition Na4Zr2Si3O12 (x = 3), one vacancy was created by removing one sodium ion. The resulting defect charge of +1 in unsubstituted and âˆ’1 in fully substituted system was compensated by introducing a uniform background charge to maintain the charge neutrality. A distinction was made between three different migration pathways depending on their angle of about 80Â°, 90Â° and 180Â°. The minimum energy paths (MEP) of sodium ion migration in supercells were calculated by the climbing image nudged elastic band (CI-NEB) method with up to five interpolated images.80

2.2 KMC simulation

KMC methods allow the simulation of ionic conductivity of systems with different composition and at various temperatures.81 KMC simulations were performed with the MOCASSIN program using the null-event algorithm.82,83 The transport of ions is described by the activation barrier Emig that, according to transition state theory, depends on the energy of initial, transition and final state. The energies of the initial and final states depend on the local ionic configuration. As the calculation of the exact energy of all possible configurations by DFT exceeds the computational limit, the configurational energies are described by a pair interaction model. The approximation includes the number of pair interactions Ndij of the cations i and j at distance d and the corresponding interaction energies Îµdij without considering any many-body interactions.

The volume and cell shape of conventional cells of Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 (x = 0â€“3) were optimized for various compositions and configurations. With increasing x the amount of silicon ions and sodium ions increases. For both, the monoclinic and rhombohedral structures, the cell volume expands with x as shown in Fig. 2. 3.2 Configurational energy

In the Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 structures with x = 0 and x = 3 the P5+/Si4+ P/Si sites are exclusively occupied by phosphorous or silicon, respectively, and thus only the distribution of the sodium ions affects the configurational energy. In contrast, different distributions of phosphorous and silicon ions in x = 1 and x = 2 have an additional impact on the configurational energy.
Phosphorus and silicon distribution. Supercells with different phosphorus and silicon distributions were investigated to identify low energy configurations. Five different configurations with increasing clustering of P5+ as well as Si4+ cations but equal arrangement of sodium ions were calculated. Fig. 3 shows the energy of the configurations depending on the number of siliconâ€“phosphorus pairs in distance of up to 5.1 Ã…. The energy of both the monoclinic and rhombohedral phase decreases with increasing number of siliconâ€“phosphorus pairs. Thus, a homogeneous distribution of phosphorus and silicon ions is more favorable rather than clustering in accordance with previous studies.41,68 The finding could be explained by Coulomb repulsion of the higher charged P5+ ions and steric hindrance of the larger Si4+ ions. In addition, clustering is accompanied by a slight distortion of the ionic positions, probably due to the unbalanced ionic interactions and the high level of strain in the crystal system.

3.3 Migration energy

Single ion jumps according to an interstitial mechanism are not considered in detail, since calculations in cells with x = 0, 1, 3 show high migration energies of Emig,0 = 2â€“3 eV. Only the correlated migration of sodium ions are investigated in cells with composition Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 (x = 0, 1, 2, 3) for different cation configurations. During the migration process, one sodium ion moves from a Na2/Na3 site towards the neighboring occupied Na1 site and pushes this sodium ion to another neighboring unoccupied Na2/Na3 site. The sodium ions jump through bottlenecks, which are each formed by three oxygen ions of one SiO4/PO4 tetrahedron and one ZrO6 octahedron as illustrated in Fig. 7.

In the compositions with x = 0 and x = 3 there are no local variations of the cation occupation, and the migration barrier only depends on the migration angle. Energy profiles of the different migration paths in rhombohedral structures are shown in Fig. 9. No significant differences between the monoclinic and rhombohedral structure were found. The results are in agreement with the recent findings of Zou et al.53 for 180Â° and 90Â° in composition x = 0.

The local minimum along the 180Â° migration path in the structure with x = 3 corresponds to the mid-Na site suggesting that this site occurs due to high sodium content and resulting coulombic repulsion.43,49,51â€“54

The migration energy decreases with increasing angle of pathway due to less steric hindrance of the migrating sodium ions. In addition, the alignment of the sodium ions migrating in 180Â° direction, which repel one another, may promote the correlated migration. The energy differences between the three distinct pathways are larger in structures with x = 0 than with x = 3. In the structure with x = 0 there are no sodium ions adjacent to the migration pathway which could sustain the correlated migration in 80Â° and 90Â° direction due to additional coulombic repulsion. For this reason, the 180Â° migration may be particularly favored, due to the alignment of the sodium ions along the migration direction, as described above. In contrast, all sodium sites are occupied in the structure with x = 3. In this case, the migration energies of the different pathways are similar due to similarly strong coulombic repulsion of neighboring sodium ions. Consequently, the change in energy x = 0 to x = 3 is most pronounced for the 80Â° pathways and least for the 180Â° pathways.

Furthermore, the migration energy decreases with increasing substitution level. Increasing sodium ion concentration leads to the increase of electrostatic sodiumâ€“sodium repulsion, which facilitates the correlated migration mechanism. Investigations of the structural changes during the sodium ion migration show that the sizes of the bottlenecks of the three different pathways do not differ significantly. However, the area of the bottlenecks is smaller in structures with x = 0 compared to structures with x = 3 (Fig. S2, ESIâ€ ). With increasing substitution level, phosphorus ions are replaced by larger silicon ions and the concentration of sodium ions increases leading to a larger cell volume and thus to a larger area of the bottleneck. The larger bottleneck facilitates the migration of the ions and thus reduces the energy barrier. These results agree with previous studies which suggest that sodium ion diffusion increases with opening of the bottleneck.40,42,47,54,88

For structures with composition x = 1 and x = 2 and different cation configurations, multiple migration pathways were investigated. The migration energies Emig,0 were extracted from the energy profiles of various configurations according to eqn (2). Shown in Fig. 10, the migration energies differ because of the different local cation environments of the pathways and structural distortions (Fig. S3 and S4, ESIâ€ ). Although the calculated migration energies scatter widely, the averaged values of 80Â° and 90Â° migration pathway are in very good agreement with the interpolated values between those of x = 0 and x = 3. For 180Â° migration, the average values for x = 1 and x = 2 are higher than the interpolated values, which might be due to distortion in different local environments. However, it should be noted that the range of values calculated for these compositions is large.

Interpolation between migration energies for Na3+xZr2Si2+xP1âˆ’xO12 with x = 0 and x = 3 as well as range between minimum and maximum values of migration energies for x = 1 and x = 2 is shown.

In summary, the decrease in the migration energy with x can be explained by the increase of sodium ion concentration, which increases the Coulomb repulsion and activates more correlated jumps. In addition, the framework expands, enlarging the bottlenecks of the pathway, resulting in less structural rearrangement during sodium ion migration. Our results are in accordance with the recent study of Zou et al. that distinguishes between 90Â° and 180Â° migration in rhombohedral structure53 and investigate 180Â° migration in monoclinic structure.54

3.4 KMC simulations

Based on the derived energy model, KMC simulations were performed to investigate the influence of the composition, local site energies and migration pathway on the ionic conductivity in Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12. Four different simulation models were applied to differentiate the effect of different energy contributions. Although the energy model is simple and we applied a fixed volume and attempt frequency, the simulations are expected to reveal the origin of the conductivity maximum at high sodium content that was observed in previous studies. In model 1 no pair interaction energy is considered whereas in model 2â€“4 the pair interaction model according to eqn (4) is applied. Furthermore, the definition of the migration energy Emig,0 differs in the models:

Model 3: Individual Emig,0 for different migration pathways corresponding to the averages of x = 0 and x = 3.

In the following, the results of simulations in the rhombohedral structure are shown as the structure exists in a wider range of compositions and temperatures. Since the conductivity is anisotropic, we show the averaged conductivities from simulations with an electric field in x-, y- and z-direction and compare these values with experimental values of polycrystals. In addition, we discuss the corresponding activation energies and we analyze the energy distribution of the jumps in each direction.

Fig. 11 depicts the conductivity as a function of the composition for all simulation models. The results of model 1 (Fig. 11(a)) show the conductivity maximum at x = 1.5 where half of the Na2/Na3 sites are occupied leading to the maximum number of jump possibilities. The partial conductivities resulting from migration in 80Â°, 90Â° and 180Â° direction are the same and thus contribute equally to the total conductivity (Fig. S5(aâ€“c), ESIâ€ ).

However, the impact of sodium ion trapping is less pronounced compared to the variation of the migration energy. For a more detailed analysis of the impact of substitution and pathway on the activation energy, the energy-dependent number of attempted and succeeded jumps in all directions are considered (Fig. S7â€“S9, ESIâ€ ). As there are no differences in Emig,0 for different angles in model 2, the jump histograms of 80Â°, 90Â° and 180Â° direction are equal. The histograms can be classified in three groups according to the compositions with equal or similar ratio of the amount of charge carriers and vacant positions. The composition with (i) x = 0.2 and 2.8, (ii) x = 0.5 and 2.5, (iii) x = 1.0, 1.5 and 2.0 show identical energy distribution of the jumps and thus identical activation energies, as observed in Fig. 12. With a more heterogeneous energy landscape towards x = 1.5 the range of energies is broadened and shifted to higher values. The number of succeeded jumps in each direction show a minimum at x = 1.5 due to sodium ion trapping. The trends of model 3 are similar but with 80Â° and 90Â° jumps shifted to higher energies. Therefore, at very low and high x, mostly 180Â° jumps succeed thus determining the activation energy. However, with x approaching 1.5 the number of succeeded jumps in 180Â° direction decreases the most, and thus the influence of 90Â° and 180Â° jumps on activation energy is similar. The number of succeeded jumps in 80Â° direction is very small which explains the low conductivity and insignificant influence on activation energy.

In model 4, attempted jumps appear over a wider range of energies according to interpolated migration energies (compare Fig. 10). At low substitution levels, the migration energies of different pathways increase strongly with smaller angle. Thus, the number of succeeded high energy jumps in 80Â° and 90Â° direction is negligible which explains the low partial conductivity and small influence on total activation energy. The number of succeeded jumps in 180Â° direction show a minimum at x = 1.0 due to trapping of sodium ions. For 1.0 < x < 2.0, the number of succeeded jumps in all directions increases slightly even though sodium ions are trapped as the migration energies decrease. When x > 2.0, the number of succeeded jumps increases strongly due to additional percolation effects of sodium ions. At high substitution levels, the migration energies of 90Â° and 180Â° converge and therefore jumps appear at similar energies. Since there are twice as many jump possibilities in 90Â° direction, the number of succeeded jumps is higher compared to those of 180Â°. The number of succeeded jumps in 80Â° is the lowest due to higher energies and thus the partial conductivity and influence on total activation energy.

The determined activation energies are in good agreement with the results obtained from a.c. conductivity measurements,33,37,38,40 especially with the findings of Goodenough et al.,33 and obtained from ab initio molecular dynamics simulations.53 Nevertheless, it is apparent that the literature values fluctuate strongly and a direct comparison is not straightforward.

4. Conclusion

We investigated the sodium ion transport in the rhombohedral and monoclinic structure of composition Na1+xZr2SixP3âˆ’xO12 using DFT calculations and KMC simulations. DFT calculations reveal that the sodium site energy strongly depends on electrostatic interactions with the adjacent cations. Lower sodium and phosphorus content in local environment leads to lower sodium site energy. We constructed a pair interaction model to generalize these findings. Although the configurational energies cannot be described precisely with the model, the physical interpretability is ensured. Furthermore, our calculations reveal that there are three different pathways for correlated sodium ion migration, defined by their angle, with the 180Â° path being most favorable. The migration energies decrease with increasing angle of pathway and substitution levels due to lower steric hindrance and increasing Coulomb repulsion.

The DFT results were applied in subsequent KMC simulations that show highest conductivity at x = 2.5â€“2.8 when directional and compositional dependence of migration energy is considered. Our results reveal further explanations of the behavior and dependence of ionic conductivity in NASICON materials. The maximum of ionic conductivity cannot only be explained by increasing Coulomb repulsion, enlarging of the bottleneck and the direction of the migration pathway. Also, the percolation of sodium ions by adjusting the dopant content need to be considered in order to optimize the NASICON compositions.
