Abstract

Three new conjugated polymers have been designed and synthesized via the alternate copolymerization of the electron-donating monomer benzodithiophene (BDT) and three different electron-accepting monomers: 5,8-dibromoquinoxaline, 5,8-dibromo-2,3-diphenylquinoxaline, 10,13-dibromodibenzo[a,c]phenazine. The synthesized polymers show good solubility in common organic solvents as well as broader absorptions in the visible region and narrower optical band gaps compared to homopolymers made from BDT units. It is found that the absorptions of the copolymers are red-shifted by increasing the electron-withdrawing ability of the co-monomer. In particular, the absorption edge of the P3 film extends to 778 nm, whereas that of the P1 film is only at 636 nm. Theoretical calculation studies of structureâ€“property relations indicated that polymer chains with better coplanarity or Ï€â€“Ï€* delocalization will lead to lower band gaps. Photoinduced charge transfer (PCT) studies disclosed that exciton dissociation of photoexcited PX (X = 1, 2, 3) via electron-transfer may occur at the interface with CHL-C60, enabling the photogeneration of electrons and holes. The P3/CHL-C60 blended film was chosen as a representative sample to test the applicability of the polymer P3 for energy conversion, a 1.76% PCE was finally obtained.

Introduction

During the last few years, organic solar cells have attracted a great deal of interest because of their advantages in offering low-cost solar electricity by using spin-coating, ink-jet printing, or roll-to-roll printing techniques to fabricate large-area photovoltaic devices on flexible and light-weight substrates.1â€“4 Among these cells, bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells,5 in which the hole-transporting conjugated polymers (donor) and electron-transporting molecules (acceptors), such as fullerenes, are blended in the active organic layer, are regarded as highly efficient systems for rapid exciton dissociation and charge transport. The power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of solution-processed BHJ solar cells have recently exceeded 5%,6â€“9 primarily due to the development of low band gap p-type materials10,11 and better control of the interpenetrating electron donorâ€“acceptor network by using processing additives.12â€“14

Recently, polymers based on benzo[2,1-b:3,4-bâ€²]dithiophene (BDT) have attracted great attention for their functions as the electron donor for BHJ solar cells. For instance, some research groups have synthesized the BDT-based copolymers with thiophene, benzothiadiazole, thieno[3,4-b]thiophene, and thieno[3,4-b]pyrazine units.15 Among these polymers, poly(BDT-thieno[3,4-b]thiophene) showed the most promising photovoltaic performance (>7%) due to the aromatic stabilization energy gained from forming the resonance quinoidal structure of the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene units that significantly red-shifts the absorption of the polymers.15bâ€“d

Herein, the present paper reports the synthesis of three new conjugated polymers based on BDT units. Polymers, poly[4,8-bis(2-dodecyloxy)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene-(5,8-diquinoxaline)] (P1), poly[4,8-bis(2-dodecyloxy)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene-(2,3-dipyridine-2-ylquinoxaline)] (P2) and poly[4,8-bis(2-dodecyloxy)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene-(dibenzo[a,c]phenazine)] (P3), were synthesized successfully via the Stille reaction. All obtained polymers show good solubility in common organic solvents as well as broader absorptions in visible region and narrower optical band gaps compared to homopolymers from BDT units. Results and discussion

Thermostability

As revealed by the TGA curves (Fig. 4), the TGA of the polymers exhibited an apparent degradation at approximately 300 Â°C for P1 and P3, and 350 Â°C for P2 with a weight loss of around 5% under nitrogen protection and P1 was first decomposed among these three polymers. This is might be ascribed to the quinoxaline unit on P1 may be less stable than the diphenylquinoxaline and dibenzo[a,c]phenazine units on P2 and P3 respectively. The thermal stability of these three polymers is apparently good enough for their application in polymer solar cells (PSCs) and other optoelectronic devices.

Optical properties

Fig. 5 shows the normalized absorption spectra of the three polymers in CHCl3 solution and their corresponding spin-coated films on quartz substrates. The results from the absorption spectra are summarized in Table 1. The copolymers, P1â€“P3, show the lowest energy absorption edge at a longer wavelength than that of the homopolymer poly[4,8-bis(2-dodecyloxy)benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene] (PBDT) (Table 1), which was presumably due to an intramolecular charge transfer structure in the copolymers. The quinoxaline or phenzine unit is a Ï€-acceptor whereas the BDT unit serves as a Ï€-donor. Thus, we can conclude that incorporating the electron-acceptor unit into the polymer chain can lower the polymer band gap effectively. Additionally, Table 1 shows the fact that the spectra of all these polymers in the solid-state are red-shifted compared to their corresponding absorption onsets in solution. This phenomenon suggests that the polymer backbones in the solid-state have more ordered interchain packing compared to those in solution.19,20 The absorption band-edge of the polymer films extend to 636, 756 and 778 nm, which are red-shifted by 15, 45 and 29 nm compared to those in solutions, respectively. Thus, the optical band gaps (Egopt) are calculated as 1.95, 1.64 and 1.59 eV for P1, P2 and P3, respectively. Obviously, these three electron-deficient units exhibit a strong effect in lowering the band gaps of the BDT-based polymers compared to the PBDT homopolymer (Egopt = 2.13 eV). Compared to PBDT (Î»onset = 582 nm), the absorption spectra of the films of P1 (Î»onset = 636 nm), P2 (Î»onset = 756 nm) and P3 (Î»onset = 778 nm) are red-shifted by 54, 174, 196 nm, respectively. Thus, we can draw a conclusion that the order of bandgap-lowering ability of these three units is: 5,8-dibromoquinoxaline (M1) < 5,8-dibromo-2,3-diphenylquinoxaline (M2) < 10,13-dibromodibenzo[a,c]phenazine (M3). This is ascribed to the rigid structure of the phenazine ring which makes the polymer P3 chains more coplanar. Comparing P1 and P2, the conjugation between dipyridine and quinoxaline in P2 will also lower its band gap and the slight electron withdrawing effect from pyridine also lower the band gap of P2. Therefore, the order of bandgap-lowering ability of these three units is M1 < M2 < M3.

Studies of structureâ€“property relations by theoretical calculation

Method. The band gaps of the BDT-based polymers with diverse structures have been analysed using quantum-chemical methods in order to understand the structureâ€“property relations. All the calculations were performed with the Gaussian-03 program.21 The geometries of the oligomers were optimized at an RHF level using a semi-empirical AM1 method.22 The band gaps were calculated using density functional theory (DFT) at B3LYP/3-21G* and B3LYP/6-31G* levels. The calculated band gaps are plotted against the inverse of the number of monomer units (1/n) and extrapolated to infinite number of units to get the value for the polymer. Two types of basis sets were chosen for comparison of the accuracy of the calculations.
Calculation of band gaps. The band gaps in conjugated polymers are governed by their chemical structures. Therefore, detailed band gap theoretical calculations were carried out on the polymer structures. The theoretical quantity of Eg for direct comparison with the experimental band gap should be the transition (or excitation) energy from the ground state to the first dipole-allowed excited state.23 At the molecular level, Eg corresponds to the adiabatic transition energy.24 However, the crudest estimate, but most widely used due to its low cost, is based on the energy difference between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO), when n = âˆž.23,25,26 Band gaps for the infinite chains (polymer) of P1, P2 and P3 were determined by plotting band gaps of oligomers against the inverse of the number (n) of monomer units and extrapolating the number of units to infinity. The HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps of the oligomers, and the extrapolated band gaps of polymers are presented in Table 2 and in Fig. 6. As shown in Table 2, for the band gaps of P1, P2 and P3, there is a good agreement between the theoretically calculated (P1: 1.88/2.07 eV; P2: 1.54/1.67 eV; P3: 1.39/1.54 eV) and the experimental optical band gap Eg values (counterparts P1: 1.95 eV; P2: 1.64 eV; P3: 1.59 eV). For polymers P1, P2 and P3, the predicted values of the band gaps are in fair agreement with the experimental values, with deviations of about 0.12, 0.03 and 0.05 eV, respectively. Hence, this methodology to determine the band gaps is a good approach for predicting the band gap in benzo[2,1-b:3,4-bâ€²]dithiophene copolymers with alternate Dâ€“A units. The difference between the computed values and experimental values is probably due to the fact that the theoretical calculations are in the gaseous phase, while the experimental band gaps values are obtained in solution or in thin films. Hence, solvent effects and interchain interactions should be considered.27 In addition, the band gaps are the crudest approximation because of the methodology based on the difference between the HOMO and LUMO.

PCT studies

The ability of charge transfer has been investigated by photoluminescence (PL) measurements. As shown in Fig. 10, the mixing of approximately massPX/massCHL-C60 âˆ¼ 1/2 CHL-C60 and PX (X = 1, 2, 3) will result in a quenching of the photoluminescence of the conjugated polymer. The spectra show that the photoluminescence of PX (X = 1, 2, 3) is completely quenched upon addition of CHL-C60. Because energy (exciton) transfer from the electron-donor to electron-acceptor is highly endergonic. We attribute the fluorescence quenching to electron-transfer, although a contribution from increased nonradiative decay (e.g., intersystem crossing) cannot be ruled out. Thus, the results show that the electron-transfer from the donor to acceptor is very close to 100% (Fig. 10aâ€“c). The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels of PX (X = 1, 2, 3) and CHL-C60 relative to the vacuum level, are schematically shown in Fig. 10d, which indicates that exciton dissociation of photoexcited PX (X = 1, 2, 3) via electron-transfer may occur at the interface with CHL-C60, enabling the photogeneration of electrons and holes.

Conclusion

Three low band gap conjugated polymers based on the BDT unit have been successfully synthesized by the Stille coupling reaction. All the obtained polymers are completely soluble in common organic solvents, thus applicable to solution-processing. All three polymers show broader visible absorption bands and narrower optical band gaps than those of homopolymer formed from BDT units. Theoretical studies disclosed that the polymer chain Ï€â€“Ï€* delocalization and the coplanarity have a crucial influence on polymer band gap. Photoinduced charge transfer studies suggested that these polymers P1â€“P3 could be used as electron donors in PSCs. The P3/CHL-C60 blended film was chosen as a representative sample to test the applicability of the polymer P3 for energy conversion, a 1.76% PCE was finally obtained.
