Abstract

The long-standing popularity of semiconductor photocatalysis due to its great potential in a variety of applications has resulted in the creation of numerous semiconductor photocatalysts, which stimulated the development of various characterization methods. This review aims to summarize the main characterization methods for assessing the most important properties of semiconductor photocatalysts, including their chemical composition (elemental composition, and chemical state/structure), physical properties (physical structure, crystallographic properties, optical absorption, charge dynamics, defects, and colloidal and thermal stability), and band structure (band gap, band edges/band edge offsets, and Fermi level). The discussion on each of these methods starts with a concise presentation of its fundamentals followed by carefully selected examples. At the end, a chart correlating the properties of a semiconductor with its potential characterization methods as well as outlook are provided. Overall, the aim of this review article is to help materials chemists and physicists, particularly students, in selecting suitable techniques for the characterization of semiconductor photocatalysts and potentially other relevant materials.

1. Introduction

Sunlight is a sustainable energy source which has played a paramount role in the history of life evolution on Earth. Much more recently, its potential in heterogeneous catalysis has been realized since seminal work by Fujishima and Honda in 1972 devoted to water splitting on a titanium dioxide electrode under UV light and a weak external electric field of 0.5 V.1 Considering that the electrolysis of water requires a potential bias of at least 1.23 V, the use of UV light helped to save a significant amount of electric energy. Since then, semiconductor photocatalysis has attracted tremendous attention, as reflected by the exponential growth of relevant publications (Fig. 1), due to its great potential in a variety of applications ranging from environmental remediation to energy conversion, such as carbon dioxide reduction and water splitting.
Fig. 1 Record count of publications on photocatalysis since 1995. Data were retrieved on November 13, 2018 from Web of Science by entering the topic “photocataly*” (i.e., TS = photocataly*).

The Experimental section of a typical research article on semiconductor photocatalysis consists of three parts, namely, materials synthesis, characterization, and photocatalytic application (testing). An enormous number of articles that deal with these three parts for a particular (group of) semiconductor photocatalyst(s) are readily available in the literature. However, a systematic review solely focused on the characterization of various semiconductor photocatalysts from the perspective of studying their most important properties has yet to come. Since characterization of these properties, including chemical composition, physical properties, and band structure, is important for understanding the photocatalytic activity of semiconductor photocatalysts for specific applications, a critical appraisal of the characterization methods should be of great help.

In the beginning of each section covering the above-mentioned properties, their importance, namely how they influence the photocatalytic activity, is firstly discussed. Next, a concise presentation of relevant characterization methods is provided, followed by demonstration of their practical applications illustrated with recently reported examples. Each of the major sections is further divided into several sub-sections. Specifically, chemical composition includes elemental composition and chemical state and structure; physical properties include physical structure, crystallographic properties, optical absorption, charge dynamics, defects, and colloidal and thermal stability; and lastly band structure includes band gap, valence and conduction band edges, and Fermi level. This arrangement of the discussion is due to the consideration of the fundamental relationship among these three major properties, namely that the band structure stems from the chemical composition and physical properties, although the band structure is the primary physicochemical attribute of a semiconductor photocatalyst. At the end, a summary chart correlating properties of semiconductor photocatalysts with the potential characterization methods is provided, together with a brief outlook. Overall, the primary objective of this review article is to familiarize materials chemists, particularly those who are early in their careers, with techniques that are widely used for the characterization of the most important properties of various semiconductor photocatalysts.

2. Chemical composition

2.1 Elemental composition

One of the first tasks in characterization of a photocatalyst is the determination of its elemental composition, which directly affects the overall photocatalytic activity. One might argue that the constituent elements of a compound can be deduced from the reactants used. For instance, the product of bismuth nitrate calcined at about 350 °C in air is likely to contain bismuth and oxygen with a molar ratio of 2 to 3. However, this subjective speculation is not scientifically acceptable, especially when multiple reactants are involved, such as a reaction involving the formation of a solid solution (which is a mixture of multiple crystalline solids having the same crystal lattice). In addition, the combination of the same elements can result in various compounds with different empirical formulae, such as BiOI, Bi4O5I2, Bi5O7I, and Bi7O9I3,2 which may have quite distinct photocatalytic activities. Furthermore, doping that leads to a new elemental composition can take place even in a fairly routine synthesis. For example, in the preparation of a popular metal-free semiconductor photocatalyst, graphitic carbon nitride, a common solvothermal treatment of melamine prior to calcination was found to cause carbon self-doping.3 Therefore, the elemental composition of a semiconductor photocatalyst needs to be determined experimentally.
2.1.1 Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) is a common technique available on analytical electron microscopes for elemental analysis. Its principle is briefly discussed as follows. When a specimen is irradiated with an electron beam, the electron–specimen interaction results in the generation of X-rays (Fig. 2a). The energy of the released X-rays corresponds to the energy difference between two relevant electron shells and is dependent on the atom's identity; hence these X-rays are known as characteristic X-rays. By measuring the count and energy of the X-rays, EDX spectra can be obtained. As an example, Fig. 2b shows the EDX spectrum of bismuth oxyiodide (BixOyIz) synthesized by reacting a BiVO4/Bi2O3 heterostructure with potassium iodide. Peaks of the compositional Bi, O, and I elements were all identified and their areas were further used to determine the atomic ratio. By applying the same method an atomic ratio of 0.766 of carbon to nitrogen was disclosed for a graphitic carbon nitride sample, verifying the occurrence of carbon self-doping in the material.3 Incidentally, it is noteworthy that the EDX measurement is typically conducted for a selected area of a sample, which results in inaccurate data if the sample is nonhomogeneous; therefore, measurements at multiple spots or additional verification by using other methods are necessary.
Fig. 2 (a) Generation of X-rays by irradiation of a specimen with an electron beam: (i) an electron in an inner shell of a constituent atom is knocked out of its orbit by an incident electron, generating a core hole; (ii) the core hole is filled by an electron from an outer shell, which is accompanied by the release of an X-ray; (b) EDX spectrum of bismuth oxyiodide (reproduced with permission from ref. 2, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH); EDX mapping of (c) WS2/WS0.2Se1.8 (reprinted with permission from ref. 7, Copyright 2018 American Chemical Society), and (d) W18O49/TiO2 (reprinted with permission from ref. 8, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH); and (e) EDX line-scan profile of a Ta3N5/NaTaON core/shell heterostructure shown in the insert (reproduced with permission from ref. 9, Copyright 2017 Wiley-VCH).

In addition to EDX spectra, EDX has been employed for obtaining spatially resolved elemental mapping. The elemental mapping of a WS2/WS0.2Se1.8 heterostructure in Fig. 2c shows that S is primarily distributed in the WS2 region, while Se is hardly observed in this region, which reflects the compositional difference of the constituent materials in this heterostructure and presents a well-defined interface. Interestingly, besides giving the spatial distribution of elements, elemental mapping is even able to reveal structural information. For instance, the elemental mapping of a W18O49/TiO2 heterostructure shows a rectangle-shaped distribution of Ti element, implying a cylindrical TiO2 core with a smooth surface (Fig. 2d). Moreover, regions with the densest distribution of W element form two parallel lines (indicated by the arrows in Fig. 2d), suggesting that W18O49 covers the surface of the TiO2 core.

EDX signals of an element can be recorded in a region, resulting in the elemental mapping as discussed above, or along a line, giving a line-scan profile, which is particularly useful in studying heterostructures. Fig. 2e shows the line-scan profiles of oxygen, sodium, and tantalum for a Ta3N5/NaTaON heterostructure. As can be seen, the signals of O and Na are visible at two separated spots on a line across the Ta3N5/NaTaON heterostructure, whereas that of Ta remains quite constant. In other words, O and Na are present only at the edges of the structure, while Ta in the entire structure, suggesting a core–shell type of heterostructure with a Ta3N5 core and NaTaON shell. Similarly, the hollow nanospherical structure of TiO2/In2O3/MnOx was also resolved by EDX line-scanning of Ti, In, and Mn, the signals of which are concentrated at two separated positions.4

2.1.2 Electron energy-loss spectroscopy. Besides EDX, electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS) is another tool available on many electron microscopes that can be used for elemental analysis. Both EDX and EELS begin with an electron beam focusing on a specimen; in the former technique, the emitted X-rays are detected, while in the latter atomic electron excitations are measured. The electronic excitations are characterized by measuring the loss of kinetic energy of incident electrons, which undergo inelastic scattering due to their coulombic interactions with the atomic electrons of a specimen.5 An EELS spectrum is obtained by plotting the electron count against electron energy loss. As compared to EDX, EELS has much higher energy resolution and is therefore particularly useful for analysis of light elements, such as carbon and nitrogen, as shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†).

Moreover, the high-energy resolution of EELS, which has reached ∼5 meV without sacrificing spatial resolution,6 even makes it capable of studying crystal phases and chemical states. For example, the anatase and rutile phases of TiO2 have been distinguished based on the EELS spectra of a series of TiO2 samples, which exhibit differences in the intensity ratios (b/a and d/c, where a, b, c, d denote peaks shown in Fig. 3) of two excitations of Ti 2p3/2 and 2p1/2 electrons. The change in the oxidation state of Ti in the disordered rutile TiO2 due to the presence of oxygen vacancies was confirmed by the change of both the b/a and d/c intensity ratios and the peak (a, b, c, and d) positions.

2.1.3 High-angle annular dark-field imaging. Scanning transmission electron microscopes can also be used to distinguish different elements by conducting high-angle annular dark-field (HAADF) imaging, during which only elastically scattered electrons are detected.10 The elastic scattering of an incident electron by a specimen is a result of the interaction between the electron and an atomic nucleus.5 This electron–nucleus interaction leads to a significant dependence of the brightness of a HAADF image on the atomic number of the atoms in a specimen (HAADF images are therefore also termed as Z-contrast images), which makes HAADF imaging a useful technique to reveal spatially resolved compositional information. For example, the HAADF image of AuAg/AuAgS/AgInS2 shows that the composite nanoparticles contain two regions of different brightness, and the grey region refers to AgInS2, while the bright region refers to the Au component (Fig. 4a). Furthermore, the elemental difference between the WS2 and the WS0.2Se1.8 regions of a WS2/WS0.2Se1.8 heterostructure could be resolved at the atomic scale by using an aberration-corrected scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM) (Fig. 4b). Se atoms seen as dots in the image are darker than W atoms but brighter than S atoms due to their intermediate electron scattering capability.
Fig. 4 HAADF images of (a) AuAg/AuAgS/AgInS2 (reprinted with permission from ref. 13, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH), (b) WS2/WS0.2Se1.8 (the white line shows the atomic-scale interface; red, green, and yellow arrows indicate W, S, and Se atoms; adapted with permission from ref. 7, Copyright 2018 American Chemical Society), and (c) Rh-doped TiO2 (reprinted with permission from ref. 14, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH).

The ability of HAADF imaging to distinguish many different elements has led to the use of this technique for studying doping and other foreign-atom incorporation processes. For instance, Rh atoms doped in TiO2 are visible as bright dots against a dark background formed by Ti and O atoms (Fig. 4c).

2.2 Chemical state and structure

The study of the chemical state, such as oxidation state and bonding, which helps to determine the chemical structure and formula of a compound, can be considered as an extension to the elemental analysis discussed above. Similar to the elemental composition, the chemical state of an element also has a great influence on the photocatalytic activity. Taking copper as an example, CuO was found to be more photocatalytically reactive toward hydrogen evolution than Cu2O.12 Moreover, the change in the chemical state of an element can even be used to study charge transfer – a decrease in the oxidation state indicates a gain of electrons (or loss of holes), while an increase in the oxidation state indicates a loss of electrons (or gain of holes). Herein, the study of the chemical state by means of spectroscopic techniques is discussed.
2.2.1 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy. When a specimen is illuminated by an X-ray beam with energy greater than the specimen's work function, some of the incident X-ray photons will be absorbed by the inner electrons of the constituent atoms, which is accompanied by the emission of photoelectrons (a process known as the photoelectric effect) and generation of core holes (Fig. 5a).15 By measuring the kinetic energy of ejected photoelectrons, we are able to calculate the electron binding energy with knowledge of the energy of the incident X-rays, which can easily be calculated from their wavelength. The electrons of an atom in a given chemical state have their own binding energies; therefore, a plot of the intensity of emitted photoelectrons versus binding energy, namely an X-ray photoelectron spectrum, can be used not only for identifying the elemental composition of a specimen, but also for determining the oxidation states of the elements. The latter capacity of X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) can be realized by a simple peak matching process. For instance, from the high-resolution XPS spectrum of Bi 4f (an example is shown in Fig. S2, ESI†) metallic bismuth (Bi0) and Bi3+ in bismuth oxide can be identified by matching their peak positions with those of the standard samples (the Bi 4f7/2 peaks of Bi0 and Bi3+ in Bi2O3 are located around 156.9 and 159.5 eV, respectively16).
Fig. 5 (a) Generation of photoelectrons by X-ray irradiation; and (b) two core hole annihilation processes.

However, it is noteworthy that the local coordination environment of an atom in a compound has been shown to affect the binding energy of atomic electrons. For example, the XPS peak of Bi3+ 4f7/2 in Bi2O3, BiVO4, and BixOyIz is located at slightly different binding energies, namely, 160.0, 158.5, and 159.1 eV (Fig. 6a), respectively. Also, the binding energy of Ti 2p3/2 electrons in Fe2TiO5 was shown to be shifted by 0.7 eV as compared with that for TiO2.17 Similarly, a change in the bonding of an element in a molecule causes a shift of the XPS peak. This can be seen in the XPS spectrum of g-C3N4. Namely, sp2-hybridized nitrogen in C–NC (N–C2, Fig. 6b), which is a part of the C–N aromatic ring in g-C3N4, is responsible for a peak at ∼398.6 eV in the XPS N 1s spectrum, while a peak at ∼400.1 eV refers to tertiary nitrogen (N–C3).18 Interestingly, N self-doping in a g-C3N4 sample was found to not only cause a shift in the above-mentioned XPS N 1s peaks, but also produce a peak centered at ∼404.3 eV.18 Finally, shifts of the XPS peaks have been observed in the spectra of heterostructures. Fig. 6c shows that the introduction of Co3O4 nanoparticles on g-C3N4 nanosheets leads to the shift of XPS C 1s and N 1s peaks to higher binding energies due to the electron transfer from the latter semiconductor to the former (namely that the oxidation states of C and N are increased).


Fig. 6 (a) High-resolution XPS spectra of Bi 4f of BiVO4/Bi2O3 and BixOyIz (reprinted with permission from ref. 2, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH); (b) a building unit of g-C3N4 (carbon and nitrogen are marked in grey and pink, respectively); and high-resolution XPS spectra of (c-i) C 1s and (c-ii) N 1s of g-C3N4 and Co3O4/g-C3N4 (reproduced with permission from ref. 20, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH).

The intensity of photoelectrons emitted from element A relative to that of those from element B is dependent on the atomic ratio of A to B in a compound, which justifies XPS's utility in quantitative analysis of elements with not only large but also trace amounts, as commonly found in doped materials, due to the high sensitivity of XPS. For instance, the increase in the percentage of carbon and oxygen in Bi2O2CO3 as a result of CO32− self-doping was detected from the rise of the intensity of the XPS C 1s peak at 288.4 eV and O 1s peak at 530.9 eV.19

Conventionally, XPS is carried out under ultra-high-vacuum conditions due to the extremely short mean free path of electrons (a few angstroms). However near-ambient-pressure XPS has been developed, giving an option to conduct in situ real-time study of the surface characteristics of a material.

2.2.2 X-ray absorption spectroscopy. The photoelectron intensity measured in XPS (discussed above) is correlated to the population of core holes generated in the electron shells of a sample, both of which are proportional to the number of X-ray photons absorbed by the sample and hence may be used to determine the X-ray absorbance. However, photoelectrons produced in the material bulk are likely to be absorbed by the constituent atoms and primarily those emitted from the surface atoms (up to 10 nm in depth) are detected, which makes XPS a popular technique to study the chemical properties of surfaces but the use of photoelectron intensity for determining the X-ray absorbance problematic.15 On the other hand, the core holes will eventually be filled by electrons from shells of higher energies (Fig. 5b), emitting either fluorescence photons (radiative emission) or Auger electrons (non-radiative emission), both of which can be counted by detectors. This allows us to determine the population of core holes, and in turn the number of X-ray photons absorbed (i.e., X-ray absorption). The measured X-ray absorption is then plotted against the energy of incident X-rays, yielding X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS), which has been used to characterize the chemical state of elements not only on the surface but also in the bulk of a compound.

XAFS near the absorption edge is commonly referred to as either X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) for solids and inorganic compounds or near edge X-ray absorption fine structure (NEXAFS) for investigating surfaces, while that extended by hundreds of eV beyond the absorption edge is called extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS).15 XANES or NEXAFS is commonly used for the determination of oxidation states. For instance, by matching the rhodium K-edge X-ray absorption spectrum of Rh-doped TiO2 with those of the reference materials, namely pure Rh metal and Rh2O3 (Fig. 7a), Rh was found to have a +3 charge. As for EXAFS, it has been primarily employed for the study of coordination and chemical bonding properties, such as coordination number and bond length.


Fig. 7 (a) Rh K-edge XANES spectra of Rh-doped TiO2 and the Rh and Rh2O3 reference materials (reproduced with permission from ref. 14, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH), and (b) N K-edge NEXAFS spectra of g-C3N4 and g-C3N4/phosphorene (reproduced with permission from ref. 21, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH).

As discussed in Section 2.2.1, the formation of heterojunctions can affect the oxidation state of a component element, leading to a shift in the XPS spectrum. This phenomenon has also been observed in X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS). Fig. 7b shows the nitrogen K-shell NEXAFS spectra of g-C3N4 and g-C3N4/phosphorene. π* resonances (i.e., electron transitions from 1s to a π* antibonding orbital) were observed at the N K-edge for both samples due to their π bonding orbitals. After coupling with phosphorene, the π* resonance positions of g-C3N4 were shifted to higher X-ray photon energies, suggesting an increase in the electron binding energy as well as the oxidation state of N, which is attributable to the migration of electrons from g-C3N4 to phosphorene.

2.2.3 Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) is a technique commonly used by organic chemists for structural analysis; however, it is also useful to study inorganic materials, either crystalline or amorphous. The characteristic vibrational frequencies in a given FTIR spectrum correspond to specific functional groups, which is used to identify the chemical structure of an unknown (a process that resembles “fingerprinting”). The cumulated Ti–O (i.e., Ti–O–Ti) stretch of the benchmark photocatalyst TiO2 was found to have a vibrational frequency around 500–700 cm−1, and the stretches of N–H, C–N and CN of g-C3N4 have frequencies at 3000–3600, 1240–1640, and 808 cm−1, respectively;22 by matching the positions of the FTIR peaks with these frequencies, the structures of TiO2 and g-C3N4 can be determined. This straightforward fingerprinting method of FTIR has been applied for many other semiconductor photocatalysts in addition to TiO2 and g-C3N4, including heterostructures, to study their surface functionality. However, it is noteworthy that a shift in the positions of FTIR peaks has been observed when heterostructures were formed. For example, when g-C3N4 was coupled with Co3O4, the FTIR peaks of both components were shifted slightly toward higher wavenumbers owing to the charge transfer.20
2.2.4 Raman spectroscopy. As shown in Section 3.3.1, optical absorption by a semiconductor occurs when the wavelength of incident light is smaller than the absorption edge. However, a molecule has actually been observed to be excited by a photon of wavelength (λex, where the subscript “ex” means excitation) larger than the absorption edge from the ground vibrational state to a higher quasi-stationary state j, known as a virtual state (Fig. 8). When the molecule relaxes, there is chance that it returns to an excited vibrational state (such as “m”, namely v = m), emitting a photon of energy h(νex − νv), where hνv corresponds to the energy difference between the ground state and the mth state. Similarly, a molecule in the mth state can be excited to a virtual state j and then relaxes to the ground state, releasing a photon of energy h(νex + νv). Clearly, incident photons may undergo elastic scattering; as a result, the emitted photons have the same energy as the incident. The number and frequency of photons emitted from all of the above-mentioned processes can be measured and plotted in the form of a graph called a Raman spectrum. ±νv = (νex ± νv) − νex are known as Raman shifts, which however are typically represented in wavenumbers. Raman signals are usually within the visible or near-IR region. This allows the usage of glass or quartz cells to hold liquid samples instead of the much more complicated NaCl cells employed in infrared (IR) spectroscopy, which is an advantage of Raman spectroscopy over IR spectroscopy.
Fig. 8 Virtual state-involved excitation and relaxation of a molecule.

Similar to FTIR, Raman spectroscopy also has the ability to study the chemical structure of a material. For instance, the components of a heterostructure can be identified by matching the Raman spectrum of the heterostructure with those of the individual components (Fig. S3, ESI†). Additionally, Raman peaks are known to carry quantitative information. For example, a phase transition of MoS2 from 1T-like (the band gap of which is extremely small) to 2H (semiconducting) due to the increase in annealing temperature was identified based on the weakening of the Raman peak at 330 cm−1 (corresponding to the former phase) and the strengthening of the peaks at 385 and 405 cm−1.23

3. Physical properties

3.1 Physical structure

Bulk, single-component semiconductors are usually less attractive photocatalysts due to their relatively low activity. On the other hand, materials with large specific surface area, such as nanoparticles, nanosheets, and nanotubes, among others, are particularly desirable in photocatalytic applications since they are able to provide a large number of reaction sites, thus increasing the overall reaction rate. In addition to high-specific-surface-area materials, heterostructures have been widely reported to further improve the photocatalytic efficiency by facilitating charge separation. The pursuit of various structures for the sake of better photocatalytic activity necessitates robust structural characterization techniques.
3.1.1 Electron microscopy. The physical structure, including morphology, size, orientation, etc., of nanomaterials is commonly studied by electron microscopy, in which an electron beam is firstly accelerated and then focused on a specimen. A large portion of the incident electrons are backscattered or absorbed by the specimen. However, if the specimen is sufficiently thin (less than 100 nm),10 a considerable fraction of the incident electrons are able to pass through the specimen. The interaction of an electron beam with a specimen generates various emissions, such as secondary electrons (which are emitted from constituent atoms), X-rays (discussed in Section 2.1.1), and Auger electrons (discussed in Section 2.2.2), among others. In general, secondary electrons have small energies (less than 50 eV), and therefore are most likely to be absorbed by the specimen if produced in the bulk while emitted if produced at the surface. Both backscattered and secondary electrons carry surface information of a specimen and their signals detected by an electron microscope can be used to study topography, composition, and crystallography. Fig. 9 shows the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of graphene oxide (GO) and GO/g-C3N4/TiO2. Clearly, the surface of GO is seen to change from smooth to rough after g-C3N4 and TiO2 were inserted between GO nanosheets.22
Fig. 9 SEM images of (a) graphene oxide (GO) and (b) GO/g-C3N4/TiO2 (inserts are AFM images; reprinted with permission from ref. 22, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH).

Electrons that travel through a specimen (transmitted electrons) can be detected by a transmission electron microscope, which has also been widely applied for the study of material morphology. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of a Cu2ZnSnS4/Au heterostructure formed by merging Cu2ZnSnS4 and Au nanoparticles is shown in Fig. 10a-i. As can be seen, the TEM image reveals not only a quasi-spherical geometry of the larger nanoparticles but also a uniform particle size distribution (∼12 nm). Additionally, contrast among different regions within a TEM image has been used to look into compositional information and the internal structure of a material.4,13,17 For example, the hollow spherical structure of TiO2 can be observed in the TEM image as circles with black edges and grey inside (Fig. 10b).


Fig. 10 (a-i) TEM and (a-ii) high-resolution TEM images of Cu2ZnSnS4/Au composite nanoparticles (reproduced with permission from ref. 27, Copyright 2014 American Chemical Society), (b) TEM image of TiO2 hollow spheres (reprinted with permission from ref. 4, Copyright 2016 Wiley-VCH), and (c) high-resolution TEM image of a Mo-doped W18O49 nanowire (reprinted with permission from ref. 28, Copyright 2018 American Chemical Society).

As compared to SEM, TEM generally uses higher electron-acceleration voltages; thus, electrons in the latter are likely to possess smaller de Broglie wavelengths than those in the former. As a result, TEM typically has higher resolution than SEM and is particularly useful for crystal phase identification. The high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of the Au/Cu2ZnSnS4 heterostructure in Fig. 10a-ii shows clearly the lattice fringes and an interplanar spacing of 0.32 nm, corresponding to that of the (002) crystal planes of wurtzite Cu2ZnSnS4, suggesting that the heterostructure was constructed by depositing Au nanoparticles (∼2 nm) on Cu2ZnSnS4 nanoparticles (∼12 nm). Another use of the measured or calculated interplanar spacing is to determine the growth direction of a crystal. For example, the distance of 0.38 nm between the adjacent crystal planes aligning within a W18O49 nanowire indicates that the W18O49 crystal grew along the [010] direction (Fig. 10c).

Incidentally, 3-dimensional (3D) images of a specimen can be created by conducting electron holography on field-emission gun (FEG) transmission electron microscopes that are equipped with a beam splitter.10 One additional application of this technique is to map the distributions of electrical potential and charge density for a specimen.11

3.1.2 Atomic force microscopy. Besides its application in topography (insets in Fig. 9), atomic force microscopy (AFM) has often been used to measure the thickness of a 2D material, which can be useful for understanding the performance of a photocatalyst – thickness on the order of the nanoscale benefits the transport of electrons and holes and thus reduces the chance of their recombination. The principle of AFM for thickness measurements is to monitor the height variation of a specimen during the movement of an AFM probe or sample stage, yielding a line profile.22,24–26 The AFM line profiles of monolayer BiO2−x are shown in Fig. 11b and the corresponding thickness is about 1.0 nm. 3.2 Crystallographic properties

Photocatalysts can be either amorphous or crystalline; however, crystallinity has a substantial effect on their activity and reaction mechanism. A notable example is TiO2, an amorphous structure of which usually shows much worse photocatalytic activity than the crystalline structure due to the severe recombination of electrons and holes. In addition, methylene blue degradation at basic conditions over amorphous and crystalline (monoclinic) bismuth vanadate was found to proceed through two different pathways, namely, demethylation and aromatic ring cleavage, respectively.33 Even for a crystalline semiconductor photocatalyst, its activity differs with various crystallographic properties, such as the crystal phase and crystallite size, among others. For instance, anatase TiO2 is well known to be more photoactive than rutile TiO2, and tetragonal bismuth oxide (β-Bi2O3) has been demonstrated to be more effective than monoclinic bismuth oxide (α-Bi2O3) in photocatalytic removal of methyl orange and 4-chlorophenol.34 Hence, the crystallinity as well as crystallographic properties of a semiconductor photocatalyst should be investigated.

3.2.2 Transmission electron microscopy. The crystallinity of a material can also be resolved by HRTEM. As has been discussed in Section 3.1.1, the HRTEM image of a crystalline material is able to show lattice planes (fringes) and the interplanar distance (d) can be measured or calculated, for example, by a fast Fourier transform (FFT). The resultant d can then be matched with that of a standard sample reported in the literature or PDFs; as a consequence, the Miller index (or indices) of these planes as well as the crystal phase of the material can be determined.

HRTEM images are commonly analyzed in conjunction with selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns, which show the distribution of electrons scattered by a sample. The SAED pattern of a crystalline specimen should exhibit spots or rings in addition to a particularly intense spot at the center that is formed by directly transmitted electrons.10 The distance between a pair of diffraction spots and their angle with respect to the central spot have been used to determine the crystalline composition of a local area of a material.8,19,35 As compared to conventional XRD patterns, which require minutes or even hours to record (however, with the use of synchrotron X-ray sources the time needed can be greatly reduced), SAED patterns can be recorded in less than one second,10 making SAED a handy supplementary technique.

3.4 Charge dynamics

A fundamental feature of semiconductor photocatalysts is their ability to generate electrons and holes after absorbing photons. However, in order to induce redox reactions, electrons and holes produced in the bulk of a material need to arrive at the surface by overcoming various challenges, in a similar way to a human lost in a jungle searching for an exit. Hence, in order to have a decent chance of surviving electrons and holes should have good dynamical properties, such as mobility and lifetime (and therefore diffusion length). Alternatively, an electric field can be applied externally to or triggered internally in a material so that the traveling route of electrons and holes is direction-oriented, just as the human trapped in the jungle chooses to follow the path of the Sun across the sky. In this section, various techniques for the characterization of the population of charge carriers (which can also be in other forms such as current and voltage) are discussed.
3.4.1 Photoluminescence spectroscopy. The recombination of electrons and holes in semiconductors with direct band gaps – that is, electrons in the conduction band and holes in the valence band have the same crystal momentum, excluding the involvement of phonons in the recombination process – is known to result in the release of photons, which have been used in photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy to indirectly study charge transfer properties. Both the count (i.e., intensity) and the wavelength (therefore energy) of emitted photons can be measured, and a plot of intensity against wavelength (or energy) yields a steady-state PL spectrum. The intensity of emitted photons has also been plotted versus the varying wavelength (or energy) of the exciting photons, resulting in a PL excitation spectrum; however, it is less common than the emission spectrum. Fig. 17b shows the steady-state PL spectra of a WS2/WSe2 heterostructure at different spots on a line across the interface. It can be seen that the PL intensity of both WS2 and WSe2 decreases when it is measured at the spot (No. 3) near the interface, which indicates the suppressed recombination of electrons and holes due to the formation of a heterojunction favoring charge transfer. Transient PL spectroscopy, which measures the time dependence of the PL intensity, further enables us to determine the lifetimes of charge carriers using the exponential decay function.4,21,39

When combined with microscopy, PL measurements are even able to give spatially resolved intensity mapping. 3.4.2 Transient absorption spectroscopy. The dynamical properties of a semiconductor have been well studied by transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy. In a typical TA experiment, a fraction of molecules in a sample in the ground state are firstly excited by a pump pulse and the absorbance (Ag) of the pulse is measured. Subsequently, a weak probe pulse is sent through the excited sample and again the absorbance (Ae) of the probe pulse is measured. The absorbance difference (ΔA) is then calculated by taking Ae minus Ag, namely, ΔA = Ae − Ag. Both the delay (τ) of the probe pulse with respect to the pump pulse and the pulse wavelength (λ) are adjustable; as a result, a difference absorption spectrum can be obtained by plotting −ΔA versus τ or λ.

Pulsed lasers with durations on the order of as short as a few femtoseconds have become available,40 rendering TA spectroscopy a powerful technique to study ultrafast charge transfer processes in chemical and biological systems. For example, the TA kinetics data of MoS2 nanosheets annealed at various temperatures shown in Fig. 18 were studied by using the exponential decay function, which resulted in three time constants ranging from hundreds of picoseconds to a few picoseconds, revealing three ultrafast exciton decay processes, corresponding to the trapping, interfacial transfer, and recombination of charge carriers.

3.5 Defects

Defects in the bulk or on the surface of a semiconductor photocatalyst are known to act as electron–hole recombination centers, which are not desirable for the sake of a high photocatalytic efficiency. However, this point is not adequate to justify the superiority of defect-free semiconductor photocatalysts, as has been well demonstrated by the example of TiO2, the defects of which can significantly improve its photocatalytic activity. Both the pros and cons of defects in semiconductor photocatalysts have promoted great interest in relevant research, including defect characterization techniques.

3.5.2 Positron annihilation spectroscopy. Besides ESR, positron annihilation spectroscopy (PAS) is another technique that can be used to study vacancy-type defects. The positron is the antiparticle of the electron and therefore has an electric charge of +1e. The annihilation between a positron and an electron generally releases two gamma photons of energy of about 511 keV (which corresponds to mec2, where me is the electron mass, and c is the speed of light) propagating in two opposite directions (however, an angular deviation could arise from the momentum of an annihilated electron). The detection of vacancies in a crystal lattice by PAS lies in the fact that an incident positron during its thermal motion in the lattice is most likely to be trapped by an existing vacant lattice site due to strong repulsive and attractive interactions with constituent ions, leading to a longer lifetime of the positron.45 In other words, the positron lifetime (τ), namely the time interval between the emission of positrons from a source (such as 22Na) and the detection of gamma photons released from positron–electron annihilation, can be considered as an indicator of the population of vacancies in a material.

A typical positron lifetime spectrum of PAS is a plot of the count of emitted gamma signals versus positron lifetime. The example of monolayer BiO2−x (1.0 nm thick) is shown in Fig. 22. Fitting this spectrum using the exponential decay function resulted in two lifetimes, corresponding to two types of vacancies that were identified as oxygen vacancies (τ = 241 ps) and Bi–O vacancy clusters (where both bismuth and oxygen sites were vacant; τ = 601 ps).

3.5.3 XPS valence-band spectrum. Some types of defects in semiconductors have been observed to produce states in the band gap slightly above the valence band maximum (VBM), which makes their detection possible from XPS valence-band spectra. However, the identity of these defects has been arguable. In most studies, the peaks seen in the XPS valence-band spectra near the edge were ascribed to the existence of oxygen vacancies (OVs). Nevertheless, in the work of Wendt et al.,46 hydration treatment that removed OVs did not noticeably alter the magnitude of the peak that is above the VBM and approximately 0.85 eV below the Fermi level (Fig. 23). Combined with other techniques, including scanning tunneling microscopy and density functional theory, they concluded that the interstitial Ti 3d defects are responsible for the additional XPS valence-band peak.

3.6 Colloidal stability

Many applications of semiconductor photocatalysts require them to be dispersed in solutions/electrolytes containing reactant ions/molecules forming colloidal suspensions. The stability of a colloidal suspension of a semiconductor, which is greatly dependent on the semiconductor's intrinsic properties, such as interfacial potential and size, in addition to various external factors (e.g., temperature, pH, electrolyte concentration, etc.), therefore plays an important role in affecting the overall performance. The interfacial potential (φ0) of a material (a solid) in a liquid is defined as the electric potential at the solid/liquid interface relative to a point in the liquid bulk. φ0 is also termed the surface potential, which is not used in this work to prevent confusion with the surface potential (Vs) discussed in surface photovoltage spectroscopy (Section 3.4.3); the latter is defined as the electric potential at a material's surface with respect to a point in the bulk of the material itself.

Although the interfacial potential is defined at a different location from the zeta potential (ζ, which is the potential at the slipping plane), they are often considered equal or close, particularly for small particles. In terms of colloidal suspension stability, interfacial or zeta potentials with large magnitudes are favorable.

Interestingly, the disturbance based on the knowledge of φ0 and ζ that destabilizes a colloidal suspension has found application in the synthesis of heterostructured photocatalysts. For instance, a Ti3C2/Bi2WO6 heterostructure was prepared by introducing bismuth nitrate to a colloidal suspension of ultrathin Ti3C2 nanosheets that have a negative zeta potential of −35 mV at pH 7, followed by the addition of Na2WO6. During the former step, Bi(III) cations were adsorbed on Ti3C2 nanosheets due to electrostatic attraction.24 Similarly, Co3O4 nanoparticles were deposited on g-C3N4 nanosheets (ζ = −24.7 mV) forming Co3O4/g-C3N4.20

3.7 Thermal stability

The thermal stability of a photocatalyst can be studied by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), the result of which is exhibited as a curve depicting the weight change over temperature. The weight loss profile of a compound is often presented in its first derivative form, and the location of each peak corresponds to the decomposition temperature of a component. The thermal stability of a compound is primarily dependent on its intrinsic composition; however, an accompanying compound can affect the thermolysis of a material, leading to a different decomposition temperature. For instance, the addition of TiO2 to g-C3N4 forming a heterostructure was able to reduce the decomposition temperature of the latter by nearly 50 °C; however, this shift in the decomposition temperature did not affect the determination of the mass fractions of the constituent semiconductors since TiO2 is not decomposable within the temperature range studied.22

Interestingly, the compositional information resolved by TGA has even found utility for the study of doping. The thermal decomposition of bismuth subcarbonate (Bi2O2CO3), which is a semiconductor formed by intergrowth of layers of (Bi2O2)2+ and CO32−, corresponds to a theoretical mass loss of 8.6% at ∼386 °C due to the release of carbon dioxide. However, it was reported that the decomposition of two Bi2O2CO3 samples resulted in weight losses of 9.8% and 10.7% (Fig. 24), which are larger than the theoretical (8.6%), indicating the existence of extra CO32− ions in the structure. In combination with other techniques, including XRD and XPS, it was confirmed that CO32− ions were self-doped into the crystal lattices of Bi2O2CO3.

4. Band structure

The electronic band structure of a semiconductor is typically composed of a valence band (VB) and a conduction band (CB), which are separated by an energy gap called the band gap (Eg). The importance of Eg lies in the fact that it plays a major role in deciding the optical absorption ability of a semiconductor. As can be seen in Fig. 14, semiconductors with band gaps larger than 3.10 eV are not able to absorb visible light. The extrema of the valence and conduction bands, namely, the VBM (Ev) and conduction band minimum (CBM, Ec), determine the redox ability of a photocatalyst. For example, in order to split water into hydrogen and oxygen a semiconductor should have a CBM and VBM with potentials more negative than 0 V (vs. SHE) and more positive than +1.23 V (vs. SHE), respectively (Fig. 25). The Fermi level (EF), which is often measured as the work function (ϕ) or flat-band potential (Vfb) by referencing to the vacuum level or a reference electrode (to be discussed in the following sections), respectively, is another important parameter of a semiconductor and its position relative to the band edges reveals the semiconductor type; that is, semiconductors with EF closer to the CBM (or VBM) due to their majority charge carriers being electrons (or holes) belong to the n- (or p-) type. The relative EF position of the two component semiconductors of a heterojunction decides the direction of the internal electric field formed at the interface, which in turn affects the transfer direction of electrons and holes created in the space charge region.
Fig. 25 Water splitting over semiconductor photocatalysts: (a) not allowed, and (b) allowed. SHE refers to standard hydrogen electrode.

The most powerful technique to study the band structure of a semiconductor perhaps is density functional theory (DFT), which is widely employed by physicists and chemists for understanding the electronic ground-state structures of systems containing electrons and nuclei on the basis of the electronic density distribution. The electronic structure of a semiconductor resolved by DFT is able to provide virtually all important properties pertaining to the band structure. The VBM and CBM correspond to the energies of the highest occupied molecular orbital and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital, respectively. The band gap is essentially the difference between the electron ionization energy and affinity, which can be calculated by DFT as the ground-state energy differences of the (N − 1)- and (N + 1)-electron systems, respectively, with respect to the N-electron system.47 In an electronic structure, the band gap has been conveniently taken as the difference between the VBM and CBM. It is noteworthy that the first and second generations of density functionals, namely, the local spin density and generalized gradient approximations, respectively, are known to underestimate band gaps. However, with the development of hybrid functionals this underestimation has been substantially revised.47

Fig. 26 shows the calculated band structures and density of states of WS2 and WSe2. By locating the band edges, direct (that is, the CBM and VBM are located at the same critical point in the Brillouin zone, K for the present cases) band gaps of 1.86 and 1.56 eV were determined for WS2 and WSe2, respectively. In addition, based on the DFT calculations the work functions of these two semiconductors were assessed. Combined with the positions of band edges relative to the Fermi level (Fig. 26), the WS2/WSe2 heterostructure was found to have type II band alignment.7


Fig. 26 Band structure and density of states of (a) WS2 and (b) WSe2. Energy is referenced to the Fermi level (EF = 0); Γ, M, and K are critical points in the Brillouin zones of the hexagonal WS2 and WSe2 lattices; DOS is the abbreviation of density of states (i.e., number of states per eV) (reproduced with permission from ref. 7, Copyright 2018 American Chemical Society).

Besides DFT, which is a computational method, experimental techniques for the characterization of a particular band structure parameter are discussed in the following sections.

4.1 Band gap

4.1.1 Tauc plot. With the absorption edge (λedge) seen in the absorption spectrum of a semiconductor (discussed in Section 3.3.1), the band gap can be estimated by the following equation: Eg = 1239/λedge. Alternatively, the absorption spectrum can be converted to the Tauc plot using the equation below:
 
(ahν)1/r = A(hν − Eg)(15)
where a is the absorption coefficient (both a and K, as in the Kubelka–Munk function, eqn (8), are symbols of the absorption coefficient recommended by IUPAC), hν is the energy of incident light, A is the proportionality constant, and r = 1/2 or 2 for direct or indirect band gaps, respectively (additional information can be found in ref. 31 and references therein). Based on the Tauc plot, the band gap is obtained as the x-intercept of linear extrapolation. Fig. 27 shows the Tauc plots of BixOyIz and BiVO4/Bi2O3. The band gaps were determined to be 2.13 eV for the former and 2.50 eV for BiVO4 for the latter.
Fig. 27 Tauc plots of BixOyIz and BiVO4/Bi2O3 (reprinted with permission from ref. 2, Copyright 2018 Wiley-VCH).
4.1.2 Photoluminescence and surface photovoltage spectroscopy. Photoluminescence (PL) is primarily due to near band-edge (CBM to VBM) emissions, suggesting that the PL peak locations can be used to calculate the band gap. For instance, the locations of the PL peaks of WS2 and WSe2 at 1.93 eV (640 nm) and 1.60 eV (773 nm), respectively, were found to be very close to the band gaps of 1.86 and 1.56 eV determined by DFT.7 In addition, using PL spectroscopy the band gaps of CdSe quantum dots with different sizes were measured,25 which demonstrated the quantum confinement effect – the band gap of a nanoscale semiconductor increases with decreasing size as can be explained by the particle-in-a-box model.

In analogy with photoluminescence, the surface photovoltage stems from near band-edge excitations. Therefore, the onset of the SPV signal in steady-state spectroscopy (i.e., SPV versus wavelength) can be used to approximately determine the band gap.41 However, the presence of subgap states (which are located slightly below the CBM or above the VBM), or coupling interactions between adjacent particles (particularly at the nanoscale) have been observed to cause a decrease or increase in the onset (in eV) of SPV, respectively.48

4.2 Band edges and band edge offsets

When the XPS spectrum (discussed in Section 2.2.1) of a semiconductor sample is recorded near the Fermi level, an XPS valence-band spectrum can be obtained. The latter has been used for the determination of the valence band maximum (VBM) by linear extrapolation. For a given semiconductor, the resultant VBM is referenced to the Fermi level (EF), which is set as the zero point of the binding energy. In order to compare the VBM of different semiconductors, the XPS valence-band spectra can be calibrated by referencing to the Fermi level of a third material, which needs to be well documented. For instance, silver was used as the reference material to record the XPS valence-band spectra of WO3 and TiO2, and the VBM was measured to be 2.85 and 3.57 eV, respectively, below the EF of silver. In other words, the VBM of WO3 was 0.72 eV above that of TiO2. Combined with the band gaps, the CBM of WO3 was found to be above (0.26 eV) that of TiO2; as a result, type II band alignment was determined for the WO3/TiO2 heterostructure.49

For two semiconductors (SC1 and SC2), the XPS valence-band spectra of which are not calibrated, the following equation has been used to calculate the VBM offset (ΔEVBM):50

 
ΔEVBM = (ESC1CL − ESC1VBM) − (ESC2CL − ESC2VBM) − ΔECL(16)
where CL is the abbreviation of core level, and ESC1CL and ESC2CL are the energy of the core levels obtained from the XPS core level spectra of SC1 and SC2; ESC1VBM and ESC2VBM are the VBM measured from the XPS valence-band spectra of SC1 and SC2; and ΔECL = ESC1,heterCL − ESC2,heterCL, ESC1,heterCL and ESC2,heterCL are the energy of core levels obtained from the XPS core level spectra of the SC1/SC2 heterostructure. Shown in Fig. 28a and b are the XPS core-level and valence-band spectra of anatase and rutile TiO2. The VBM for anatase and rutile was determined to be 2.77 (EanataseVBM) and 2.61 eV (ErutileVBM), and the binding energy of the XPS core-level (Ti 2p3/2) peaks was 458.96 (EanataseCL) and 458.75 eV (ErutileCL). The Ti 2p3/2 core levels of anatase and rutile in anatase/rutile heterostructures differed by 0.44 eV on average (i.e., ΔECL = Eanatase,anatase/rutileCL − Erutile,anatase/anataseCL = 0.44 eV, Fig. 28c). Applying eqn (16), the VBM offset (ΔEVBM) between anatase and rutile TiO2 was calculated to be 0.39 eV (the VBM of anatase was lower than that of rutile), which is fairly close to that (0.47 eV) determined by DFT calculations. Taking into account the band gaps of anatase (3.20 eV) and rutile (3.03 eV) TiO2, the anatase/rutile heterostructure was found to have Type-II band alignment.

An alternative to XPS is ultraviolet (UV) photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS), which uses a monochromatic ultraviolet beam to generate photoelectrons. Due to the smaller energy of UV light, which leads to a better energy resolution of UPS as compared to that of XPS, only low-binding-energy electrons can be excited, making UPS particularly suitable for the study of the valence-band structure of a semiconductor. Additionally, with the use of high-intensity synchrotron-radiation sources the resolution of both XPS and UPS can be substantially improved.

4.3.3 Photoelectrochemical methods. When a semiconductor is brought into contact with a solution, the difference in the Fermi level between the semiconductor and the solution leads to a flow of electrons across the interface (either from the semiconductor to the solution or the other way around), causing bending of the conduction and valence bands of the semiconductor. However, this band bending can be canceled by applying an electric voltage to the semiconductor equal to the flat-band potential (Vfb) of the semiconductor. Vfb of a semiconductor with respect to a reference electrode, such as the standard hydrogen electrode (Fig. 31a), has often been determined by constructing a Mott–Schottky plot, while other photoelectrochemical methods, such as photocurrent–voltage measurements (linear sweep voltammetry) and the pH-dependence of photovoltage in the presence of an electron acceptor (e.g., methylviologen, diazaphenanthrene, etc.), have also been employed.52 The flat-band potential measured against a reference electrode [Vfb (vs. ref)] can be converted to that against SHE [Vfb (vs. SHE)] by the following relation:
 
Vfb (vs. SHE) = Vfb (vs. ref) + Vref (vs. SHE) + 0.0591 × pH(19)
where Vref (vs. SHE) is the potential of the reference electrode with respect to SHE. Since the absolute potential (i.e., against a vacuum) of SHE is 4.44 V, namely, VSHE (vs. vacuum) = 4.44 V, the flat-band potential versus a vacuum [Vfb (vs. vacuum)] can be calculated as
 
Vfb (vs. vacuum) = Vfb (vs. SHE) + 4.44 V(20)
Therefore, the Fermi level, EF(eV), is obtained as follows:
EF = −Vfb (vs. SHE)·e − 4.44 eV
where e represents the electronic charge.

In addition, the positive slopes of the linear parts of the Mott–Schottky plots imply that these two compounds are n-type semiconductors, the Fermi levels of which are slightly (<0.3 eV) below the bottom of their conduction bands. Hence, it can be concluded that the CBM of NaTaON is higher than that of Ta3N5. 5. Summary and outlook

Although it has been extensively studied for several decades, semiconductor photocatalysis is still a popular research area, which may be partly attributed to the continuous emergence of promising applications, ranging from the degradation of pollutants (such as various organic dyes, poisonous molecules/ions, etc.) in the early stage, to energy conversion, and more recently bacterial inactivation, among others. The long-lasting popularity of this research area has led to the discovery or creation of numerous semiconductors and their heterostructures as photocatalysts, such as the benchmark titanium dioxide, metal-free carbon nitride, Z-scheme heterojunctions, and more. In this work, we have discussed the characterization of some of the most important properties of semiconductor photocatalysts, including three major categories, namely chemical composition, physical properties, and band structure, which are further divided into many sub-topics, as have been summarized in Scheme 1.

Nomenclature and abbreviations: EF – Fermi level, ϕ – work function, Vfb – flat-band potential, EDX – energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy, EELS – electron energy-loss spectroscopy, HAADF – high-angle annular dark-field imaging, XPS – X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, XAS – X-ray absorption spectroscopy, FTIR – Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, SEM – scanning electron microscopy, TEM – transmission electron microscopy, AFM – atomic force microscopy, gas ad. – gas adsorption–desorption analysis, XRD – X-ray diffraction, HRTEM – high-resolution TEM, SAED – selected area electron diffraction, DRS – diffuse reflectance spectroscopy, FDTD – finite-difference time-domain method, PLS – photoluminescence spectroscopy, TAS – transient absorption spectroscopy, SPVS – surface photovoltage spectroscopy, PCS – photocurrent spectroscopy, EIS – electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, ESR – electron spin resonance, PAS – positron annihilation spectroscopy, ζ potential – zeta potential, TGA – thermogravimetric analysis, DFT – density functional theory, UPS – ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy, KPFM – Kelvin probe force microscopy, PE – photoelectrochemical methods.

As can be seen from Scheme 1, a given property of a semiconductor photocatalyst usually can be characterized by multiple techniques. In addition to the proper selection of a technique, to some extent it has become standard to use more than one method, which improves the accuracy and reliability of results. For example, EDX is commonly coupled with XPS for elemental analysis because both techniques only give the elemental composition of a partial region of a sample – a selected area for the former and the material surface for the latter.

Recently, great attention has been paid to the development of in situ and operando (a Latin gerund that means operating or working) spectroscopic, scattering, and microscopic techniques, as well as the improvement of the spatial and temporal resolution with the use of synchrotron radiation sources.53,54 The conceptual difference between in situ and operando is that the former focuses on the study of a catalyst under model or true reaction conditions, and the latter involves simultaneous study of a catalyst and its performance during an actual reaction. Using these methods not only helps correlate the characterization results of a photocatalyst with its catalytic performance, but also may reveal new phenomena. Exemplary benefits of in situ/operando experiments are listed below with additional details provided in Table S1 (ESI†):

(1) Development of photocatalysts by monitoring the change in the chemical composition, structure, and crystallinity, among other properties, during their synthesis using in situ techniques such as XPS, XAS, FTIR, Raman, SEM, TEM, and XRD.

(2) Study of the charge transport in heterojunctions. As shown above, XPS and XAS are able to determine the charge transport between two or more components in a heterojunction by comparing the oxidation states of a constituent element in the individual components and the heterojunction, whereas in situ XPS and XAS experiments allow the charge transport to be observed directly by recording the change in the oxidation state of a constituent element after the heterojunction is irradiated with light.

(3) Study of defects, which can be created by light irradiation or reaction with water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and many other ubiquitous molecules,55 resulting in new chemical compositions – at least on the surface – by employing in situ XPS, XAS, FTIR, and Raman spectroscopy.

(4) Determination of the band edges of a photocatalyst in an operating environment containing a solvent by using in situ XPS/UPS, which takes into account band bending resulting from electron transfer between the photocatalyst and the solvent due to unequal Fermi levels.

(5) Direct observation of the reaction-induced motion of a photocatalyst by using in situ electron microscopy, which has potential implications for the study of micro/nano-motors. The motion can be powered by preferred production of a gas at some spots of a photocatalyst with inhomogeneous distribution of reaction sites or at one side of a Janus particle.

(6) Study of the mechanism of a photocatalytic reaction by analyzing intermediates, including radicals and molecules, and products formed using in situ characterization techniques such as ESR and FTIR, among others.

Overall, we hope that this review article will help researchers, particularly students and young investigators, interested in semiconductor photocatalysis and other relevant areas to choose suitable techniques for the characterization of semiconductor photocatalysts.
