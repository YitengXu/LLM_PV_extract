Abstract

We have theoretically examined the geometries, electronic density of states and band structures of cubic and hexagonal A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na, Li) using meta-GGA SCAN-rVV10. The optimized lattice density was found to vary between 2.68 and 4.08 g cm3 for cubic-A2AgCrCl6, with the fundamental electronic bandgap (direct) in the range of 0.660.69 eV. The cell density of hexagonal A2AgCrCl6 was between 2.97 and 3.93 g cm3, but with an indirect bandgap of 0.931.02 eV. The valence band maximum and the conduction band minimum of A2AgCrCl6 were confirmed to be essentially of Cr(3d) character, but the contributions from the orbital states of Cl(3p) to the VBM were also appreciable. Cubic A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K) was identified to possess genuine perovskite stoichiometry, evaluated using various geometry-based indices (viz. octahedral factor, tolerance factor, and global instability index). This was not so for A2AgCrCl6 (A = Na, Li), and was due to the small size of Na and Li cations that caused the critical strain of CrCl6 octahedra and a significant decrease in the cell volume. However, all the five A2AgCrCl6 displayed nearly similar optical properties, including the nature of the oscillator peaks in the dielectric function, absorption coefficient, photoconductivity, reflectivity, and Tauc spectra. The zero-limit of the refractive index was calculated around 2.25 and 2.00 for cubic and hexagonal A2AgCrCl6, respectively, and the extinction coefficient was very small for all cases. The nature of the optical bandgap and transition peaks discussed in this study of cubic and hexagonal Cs2AgCrCl6 agreed well with the experiment. The examination of phonon band dispersion led to the conclusion that cubic-A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb) are the only halide double perovskites of the entire series that are dynamically stable.

1. Introduction

Lead-free halide based double perovskites with the chemical formula A2BBX6 are undoubtedly an important class of materials for photovoltaics, light emission devices and optoelectronics,18 analogous to single halide perovskites.9 In A2BBX6, A is a monovalent inorganic/organic cation (viz. Cs+, Rb+, methylammonium), B is a monovalent transition metal or an alkali metal cation (viz. Na+, K+, Ag+, Cu+, Tl+), B is a trivalent transition metal or a non-transition metal cation (viz. In3+, Sb3+, Bi3+, Cr3+, Au3+), and X is a monovalent halide anion (X = Cl, Br, I).

A great number of halide based double perovskites have been demonstrated in recent years. They are not only stable against heat and light,10 but are also environmentally friendly and nontoxic.11 Indeed, this is in comparison to the most efficient light absorber MAPbI3 (MA = methylammonium).1215 MAPbI3 has been regarded as a blockbuster photovoltaic material, with a certified power conversion efficiency (PCE) that has increased from 3.8% in 2009 to 25.2% in 2020.16

The low PCE, long term stability, and high toxicity characteristics of most lead-based halide perovskites9 have already created a roadmap for discovering new materials. To this end, numerous halide double perovskites have been theoretically proposed using high-throughput computational studies.1720 Many of them have also been experimentally synthesized and their material properties were characterized.6,11,15 Such attempts were made to increase the diversity of metal ion substitutions at B and B sites in A2BBX6 and to examine the resulting changes in the lattice size, shape, bandgap, and optical properties.1720 The underlying reason for such theoretical and experimental attempts was to find materials that are suitable not only for photovoltaics and photocatalysis, but also for the development of phosphor-converted light-emitting devices for bioimaging and biomonitoring, among others.1720,21

The use of a trivalent B-site cation in A2BBX6, which was initially restricted to the pnictogen group (p-block elements, viz. In3+ and Bi3+),14,6,11 was later replaced by d-block transition metal elements. The Cs2AgCrCl6 halide double perovskite is one of those transition-metal based systems.21 While this system is cubic (space group Fmm),18 its hexagonal polytype (Rm) was recently experimentally synthesized, thereby its crystal structure, optical properties, thermal behavior, and magnetic properties are known.21 It was observed that the material is paramagnetic, and displays optical transition features in the near-infrared to the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum. Similarly, Zhao et al.22 have examined a Cr3+-doped halide perovskite called Cs2AgInCl6:Cr3+. It exhibited broadband near-infrared emission ranging from 850 to 1350 nm centered at 1010 nm. Its excitation bands were observed at 353, 565, and 800 nm. These bands were attributed to the absorption of the Cs2AgInCl6 host, the Cr3+ dd spin-allowed transitions 4A2 4T1 and 4A2 4T2, respectively.22

This work theoretically investigates the electronic structure, stability, density of states, and band dispersion of cubic A2AgCrCl6 that have a double perovskite stoichiometry, where A = Cs, Rb, K, Na, and Li. The motivation for this work is to elucidate how alkali substitution modulates the crystal lattice and electronic, geometrical, and optical properties, and to examine whether each of the five members of the A2AgCrCl6 series has a stable perovskite structure. We investigate the orbital origin of the valence band maximum and the conduction band minimum (VBM and CBM, respectively) of A2AgCrCl6 and discuss the effect of the alkali atom at the A site on the mobility of charge carriers. Linear response properties, i.e. the dielectric function, absorption coefficient, photoconductivity, reflectivity and complex refractive index spectra, are evaluated and compared. The phonon-based band dispersion and density of states analyses are performed to explore the dynamical stability of A2AgCrCl6. The same properties simulated for hexagonal A2AgCrCl6 are also included and compared with that known experimentally.21

2. Computational methods

The primitive- and conventional unit-cell geometries of cubic and hexagonal A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na, Li) were relaxed within the framework of the meta-generalized-gradient approximation (meta-GGA) using SCAN-rVV10. SCAN is Strongly Constrained and Appropriately Normed, and is paired with the non-local correlation part from the rVV10 vdW density functional.2328 This pairing was shown to provide superior performance over the non-empirical PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE) functional.29 For instance, the results of SCAN-rVV10 have already assisted in gaining correct insight into the chemical bonding, intermediate-range van der Waals (vdW) interaction, and interlayer binding energies and spacing, as well as intralayer lattice constants.23 In any case, a k-point mesh 8 8 8 centered at was used for sampling the first Brillouin zone. The projector augmented wave method30 was used for all elements of A2AgCrCl6. The energy cut-off used for geometry relaxation was 520 eV. The average forces on ions were minimized to 0.005 eV 1. Hereafter, we label cubic and hexagonal A2AgCrCl6 as c-A2AgCrCl6 and h-A2AgCrCl6, respectively.

Calculations of the band dispersion (Ek diagram) were performed using the same functional as above; the primitive cells were used. The high-symmetry band labels of the Fmm (no. 225) and Rm (no. 166) space groups31 were used for c-A2AgCrCl6 and h-A2AgCrCl6, respectively. These include k-paths X W L and T H2/H1 L S0/S2 F, respectively. The calculation of the density of states (DOS) was performed with the same computational approach, but with a high dense k-point mesh 11 11 11. The tetrahedron method with Blchl corrections detailed in the manual32 of Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)33,34 was used. The DOS/band structures were analyzed using the Pyband35 and Sumo packages.36

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Geometry

Experimentally, the Rm geometry of Cs2AgCrCl6 was confirmed to be paramagnetic.22 Based on this, and given that the system contains a 3d3 transition metal, both spin-polarized and non-spin polarized calculations were performed to energy minimize the antiferromagnetic, paramagnetic and nonmagnetic configurations of c-A2AgCrCl6 conventional cells. Similar to that reported elsewhere for other perovskite systems,18b the total energy was found to be virtually the same for all the magnetic and non-magnetic configurations of each system (all within 1.6 meV per unit formula). The small energy difference between the different configurations of each system may mean that Cs2AgCrCl6 is paramagnetic at room temperature. Thus, the results of spin-polarized paramagnetic configurations of c- and h-A2AgCrCl6, without any specific bias to the ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic orderings, are discussed below.

3.2 Perovskite stability

The combination of the octahedral factor ( = rB/rX)45,46 and the Goldschmidt's tolerance factor t47 has been widely used to assess the geometrical stability and perovskite nature of ABX3 and ABBX6 systems, where rA, rB and rX refer to the ionic radii of sites A, B and X, respectively. For ABBX6 double perovskites, rB = (rB + rB)/2. A t value between 0.813 and 1.107 and a value in the range of 0.410 < < 0.895 (i.e., > 0.41) have been suggested to result in stable perovskite structures.18,45,46,48 Thereby, considering Shannon's radii for the ions,49 our calculations gave a of 0.49 for A2AgCrCl6 (Table 3), which is within the range recommended for .18 On the other hand, the tolerance factor for the first four members of the series, A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na), varies between 0.84 and 0.97 (Table 3). A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K), with GII values between 0.10 and 0.13 valence unit (v.u.), are marginally deviated from ideal perovskite stoichiometry. This does not apply to Na2AgCrCl6 and Li2AgCrCl6. 3.3 Energy stability

Ab initio molecular dynamics simulations were previously performed to understand the thermodynamic stability of solid systems at specific temperatures,85,86 but the energy differences between reactants and products can also provide insights into stability phenomena.9 Experimentally, the quaternary halide perovskite Cs2AgCrCl6 was prepared in the form of dark purple crystals by the reaction of CsCl, AgCl, and CrCl3 at 700 C. Therefore, considering the experimentally reported prospective reactants above, the change in total energy Er (Er = E(product) E(reactants)) between the reactants and products was calculated to assess the thermodynamic stability. The following reaction pathway was assumed: 2(Cs2AgCrCl6) 2(AgCl) + 4(CsCl) + 2(CrCl3). As such, our spin-polarized calculation yielded Er values of 1.65 and 1.63 eV for cubic and hexagonal Cs2AgCrCl6, respectively, where the total energy E per formula unit of the reactants and product was used. This result indicates that the double perovskite systems investigated are much more stable than their constituent fragments, suggesting thermodynamic stability. We have also calculated Er for the other four perovskite systems of the series assuming a similar reaction pathway (e.g., 2(A2AgCrCl6) 2(AgCl) + 4(ACl) + 2(CrCl3) (A = Rb, K, Na, Li)). The Er values were found to be 1.27 (1.05) and 0.57 (0.35) eV for cubic (hexagonal) Rb2AgCrCl6 and K2AgCrCl6, respectively, providing theoretical evidence of their thermodynamic stability. This was not the case, however, for the other two lighter members of the series since the Er were found to be 1.78 (1.19) and 3.07 (0.82) eV for cubic (hexagonal) Na2AgCrCl6 and Li2AgCrCl6, respectively.

While there can be several different decomposing pathways, we have also examined the energies of the c-Cs2AgCrCl6 double perovskite and the fragments in the competing phases following the pathway (2(Cs2AgCrCl6) Cs3Cr2Cl9 + 2AgCl + CsCl) to shed some light on the thermodynamic stability of Cs2AgCrCl6, where each subunit represents the formula unit. Our calculation gave a Er of 0.36 eV for the reaction, indicating thermodynamic instability. The decomposition pathway is similar to what has been reported for other systems, viz. Cs2AgBiI6 (2(Cs2AgBiI6) Cs3Bi2I9 + 2AgI + CsI)9a and MAPbI3 (MAPbI3 MAI + PbI2),9b in which, these perovskite systems were found to be intrinsically unstable with respect to their respective phase separations. Savory and coworkers have demonstrated that whereas these latter systems might be synthesizable, they do suffer from instability issues in devices.9a The authors have also examined a number of such single perovskites (viz. MABX3, CsBX3 (B = Pb, Sn; X = Cl, Br)) and have found that when I in MABI3 and CsBI3 is replaced by Br and Cl, the reactions become endothermic for all these systems regardless of the nature of DFT functionals employed (PBE and vDW), thereby leading to the interpretation that these systems have a lesser tendency for phase separation.

We have also calculated the relative energy stability E between hexagonal and cubic A2AgCrCl6 using the following relation: E = E(Fmm) E(Rm), where E represents the total energy per unit formula of individual species. It was found that the Fmm geometry is slightly more stable than the Rm geometry for A = Cs, Rb, K. The stability trend was opposite when A = Na and Li. In other terms, the relative energies of cubic Cs2AgCrCl6, Rb2AgCrCl6, and K2AgCrCl6 were approximately 0.012, 0.109, and 0.107 eV greater than those of their corresponding Rm analogues, respectively. Similarly, the energies of cubic Na2AgCrCl6 and Li2AgCrCl6 were about 0.294 and 1.129 eV less than those of their corresponding Rm counterparts, respectively.

The dispersive valence band of c-A2AgCrCl6 is doubly degenerate over the spin-up channels. From the parabolic fitting of the band edges,36,37 it was found that the mean effective mass of holes (mh*) is heavier along the X path than along the X W path (Table 5). An exactly similar trend is observed for the spin-up electrons (me*) along these directions, associated with the CBM. For all cases, the hole mass is found is to be negative and the magnitude is larger than that of the electron. The negative sign is not unexpected as the hole moves opposite of the electron, and is ideal for most semiconductors.37 From the data in Table 5, it may also be said that the mobility of spin-up holes/electrons is reasonably slower along X than along the transport direction X W. This insight was gained based on the simple Drude model53 that suggests that mobility is inversely proportional to the effective mass of the charge carrier. There is also a weak shoulder in the 2() curve, appearing around 2.3 eV. This, together with the stronger peak at 2.1 eV, should be regarded as the spin-allowed transitions. While the weak transition is not very transparent in the 2() spectrum in Fig. 4b that was obtained using dense k-meshes, the first onset of absorption is observed roughly around 1.6 eV. This is not close to the non-SCF (direct) bandgap of 0.66 eV (Table 4), but is close to the SCF bandgap of 1.83 eV.8c

Alkali substitution did not significantly change the positional occurrence of the transition peaks. The weak peaks were observed at energies in the range from 2.3 to 2.4 eV, whereas the strong peaks were observed at energies around 2.1 eV. In all cases, the onset of absorption lies between 1.5 and 1.6 eV. This may mean that the optical characteristics do not depend on the perovskite nature of A2AgCrCl6. Rather, these depend on the nature of the atomic compositions involved. The marginal change in the shape, peak position and height due to alkali substitution is concordant with the nature of the variation of the fundamental bandgaps summarized in Table 4 for c-A2AgCrCl6. Note that we have recently examined the A2AgCrBr6 (A = K, Rb, Cs) and Cs2AgCrX6 (X = Cl, I) systems that have double perovskite compositions.8c Their physical, electronic, bandgap, and optical properties were explored on their respective conventional cells. The study did not consider band unfolding to provide an accurate view on the nature of the band structure and charge carriers, yet the dielectric spectra reported using the conventional cell of c-Cs2AgCrCl6 are in decent agreement with that shown in Fig. 4a.

Nevertheless, the nature of the bandgap transitions illustrated in Fig. 4 matches reasonably well with those observed in the UV/Vis/NIR diffuse reflectance spectrum of hexagonal Cs2AgCrCl6.21 In particular, these transitions were observed at energies of 1.6 and 2.2 eV, respectively. It was argued that these are characteristic of dd transitions, arising basically from the octahedrally coordinated Cr3+ ions in weak ligand fields of the Cl ions. Such absorption bands were also observed at 565 and 800 nm (1.55 and 2.2 eV, respectively) in the corresponding spectrum of Cr3+ doped Cs2AgInCl6, and were attributed to the Cr3+ dd transitions of 4A2 4T1 and 4A2 4T2, respectively.22 Our results presented in Fig. 4b clearly suggest that the use of a denser k-point mesh may not always be necessary to infer accurately the characteristics of the important absorption peaks of c-Cs2AgCrCl6.

3.6 Phonon features: nature of dynamical stability

The phonon band structure and (total) phonon density of states of Fmm and Rm A2AgCrCl6 were examined using linear response density functional perturbation theory39 and the finite difference method (FDM).76 To evaluate the finite size effects77 on the nature of phonon dispersion, we used a number of geometries including the primitive unit-cell, conventional unit-cell and the 2 2 2 super-cell of the former for c-Cs2AgCrCl6. These consisted of 10, 40 and 80 atoms, respectively, which were used individually to build and calculate the dynamical matrix of the Fmm structure of Cs2AgCrCl6. For Rm Cs2AgCrCl6, the conventional cell comprising 60 atoms was used for the said purpose. Similarly, for c- and h-A2AgCrCl6 (A = Rb, K, Na, Li), the dynamical matrices were constructed using their respective primitive- and conventional-cells, meaning that these cell geometries were utilized for the evaluation of the second derivative of energy with respect to the atomic displacements. We used k-mesh 4 4 4 for sampling the Brillouin zone in the reciprocal space for the primitive, conventional and supercell structures for Fmm lattices, and k-mesh 3 3 1 for h-A2AgCrCl6. The kinetic energy cut-offs for the plane wave were 520 eV, with a tightly converged electronic wavefunction (within 108 eV). FDM accounted for the only symmetry inequivalent displacements of ions, together with SCAN-rVV10, to compute forces. Both VASP and Phonopy78,79 software were used.

From the phonon band structures and DOS illustrated in Fig. 7a, it is apparent that the Fmm structure of Cs2AgCrCl6 is dynamically stable. This applies regardless of the cell-type and computational approaches utilized. Our claim is based on the fact that there is no soft phonon mode (negative frequency) associated with the acoustic and optical phonons at the zone-center and across the zone boundary. A similar stability feature was obtained when the conventional cell was used (not shown), suggesting that there was no self-interaction of the displaced atom with respect to itself for the supercell used (Fig. 7). Since the primitive cell of c-A2AgCrCl6 contains 10 atoms, each system comprises 30 phonon modes. Of these, three are typically acoustic modes with frequencies close to zero. In fact, our calculations yielded three degenerate acoustic modes at a frequency of 0.02 THz. The use of the 40-atom conventional cells has resulted in a large number of optical modes, thereby causing the phonon dispersion denser. Nevertheless, and in all cases, the longitudinal and transverse acoustic modes are stable throughout the Brillouin zone path, with the minimum found at the center of the zone (-point). Interestingly, the above nature of the phonon modes across the Brillouin zone is not the same as that found for h-Cs2AgCrCl6 and for other members of the same series. This is because there were several soft modes throughout the Brillouin zone, especially at the L- and F-points, leading to the conclusion that h-Cs2AgCrCl6 and its sister analogues are dynamically unstable.

As explained above, systematic replacement of the A-site cation with four other light elements of the alkali-group (one type at a time) results in the critical lattice strain exhibited by GII and (Table 3). A result of this is that c-Rb2AgCrCl6 was still found to be dynamically stable (Fig. 7b), but the low-frequency optical modes are shallow near the center of the zone. These modes are likely to be affected by external agencies such as temperature and pressure. The above features are not the same for the remaining three systems of the series. This is because soft phonon modes have appeared for all of them. These modes became prominent when the lattice changed its properties from perovskite to non-perovskite (largely distorted). For example, soft mode occurs at frequencies of approximately 44.2, 49.6, 67.3 and 68.2 cm1 for K2AgCrCl6. Several such modes were found between 13.0 and 102.1 cm1 for Na2AgCrCl6. The imaginary modes seen at the zone boundaries are clearly the result of instability caused by sub-lattice mismatches and are a common feature of halide-based perovskite structures.8083 These have been linked with anti-ferroelectric distortions caused by rotations and/or tiling of the octahedra in neighboring unit cells.84 It is worth noting that our phonon calculations were performed within the harmonic approximation. Obviously, the effect of anharmonicity on the eigenmodes at the center of the Brillouin zone has not been investigated, so it is not possible to speculate how it will contribute to explain the stability of the A2AgCrCl6 (A = K, Na, Li) lattices investigated.

4. Conclusion

This study examined the physical and optical properties of the series c-A2AgCrCl6 using SCAN-rVV10. The combined analysis of the geometrical indices, including the octahedral factor , Goldschmidt's tolerance factor t, and the newly proposed tolerance factor , has enabled us to demonstrate that the first three members of the series, c-A2AgCrCl6 (A = Cs, Rb, K), possess stable perovskite stoichiometry. Both and t recognized c-Na2AgCrCl6 to possess a perovskite structure, but the new tolerance factor rejected such a possibility.18 On the other hand, c-Li2AgCrCl6 was found to be a significantly unstable distorted perovskite structure. These conclusions were in agreement with that inferred from the Global Instability Index GII, and the application of this index, together with , may be regarded superior over t and in detecting the perovskite nature of unknown ABX3 and A2BBX6 systems. Although h-Cs2AgCrCl6 was recently synthesized, it was found to be energetically less stable than its cubic counterpart.

In addition, we have shown that all the five Fmm systems, c-A2AgCrCl6, exhibit nearly identical electronic and optical characteristics, which are reminiscent of any semiconducting material. In particular, we have shown that they display similar transition features in the dielectric function, absorption coefficient, reflectivity, conductivity, and refractive index spectra. Alkali substitution did not significantly change the magnitude of the bandgap, although it has populated and/or depopulated the occupancies of specific (orbital) energy states responsible for the development of the dispersed VBM. The effective masses of spin-up charge carriers were small, which led to the suggestion that c-A2AgCrCl6 possesses high mobility. The magnitude and direct nature of the bandgap revealed with the meta-GGA functional and the optical transition features revealed using DFPT for c-Cs2AgCrCl6 were in good agreement with the experiment. The Rm Cs2AgCrCl6 perovskite and its sister analogues had similar properties with indirect bandgaps, but were found to be dynamically unstable. Among the lattices investigated, the cubic Cs2AgCrCl6 and Rb2AgCrCl6 double perovskites were shown to be dynamically (phonon) stable within the harmonic approximation, thus welcoming experimentalists for their subsequent synthesis and characterization.
