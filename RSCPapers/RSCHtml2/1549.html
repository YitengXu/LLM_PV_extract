Abstract

To enhance the total emission intensity, particularly the red emission of Yb,Er co-doped nanoparticles for red light activated photodynamic therapy (PDT), we doped Mn2+ ions into the NaGdF4:Yb,Er core, and subsequently coated the NaGdF4:Yb active shell to fabricate coreâ€“shell structured, up-conversion nanoparticles of NaGdF4:Yb,Er,Mn@NaGdF4:Yb (abbreviated as UCNPs). A novel and facile encapsulation method with gelatin has been proposed to transfer oleic acid (OA) stabilized UCNPs into an aqueous solution and simultaneously decorate zinc phthalocyanine (ZnPc) photosensitizer molecules. In the encapsulation process, ZnPc molecules are wrapped in the interlaced net structure of the peptide chain from gelatin, forming the UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc nanocomposite. The nanoplatform has high emission intensity and excellent biocompatibility, as was expected. More importantly, the enhanced red emission of UCNPs has significant overlap with the UV absorbance of ZnPc; therefore, it can effectively activate the sensitizer to produce a large amount of singlet oxygen reactive oxygen species (ROS, 1O2) to kill cancer cells, which has evidently been verified by the in vitro results. Combined with the inherent up-conversion luminescence (UCL) imaging properties, this UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc nanoplatform could have potential application in PDT and imaging fields.

Introduction

In recent years, rare earth up-conversion luminescence (UCL) materials have gained considerable attention for their potential applications in lighting industries, color displays, solar cells, nanoscale bio-probes, and optical devices.1â€“11 Among these, the applications in the biomedical field are especially attractive.12â€“22 This is because rare earth elements have a unique electronic configuration, which allows their compounds to have versatile physical and chemical properties.23â€“25

It is well known that rare earth fluoride materials are good hosts for UCL, due to their low phonon energy, low non-radiative decay rates, and high radiative emission rates.26â€“29 NaGdF4:Yb,Er is an efficient up-conversion phosphor, in which the Yb3+/Er3+ couple displays two main up-conversion emission bands centered at âˆ¼540 nm (green light) and âˆ¼660 nm (red light).30,31 Interestingly, the red light emission promotes the conjugation of Yb3+/Er3+ coupled up-conversion nanoparticles, with the photosensitizer, ZnPc. This occurs when the formed nanocomposite is exposed to 980 nm laser irradiation; the energy of the red light emission can be transferred to ZnPc molecules to produce singlet oxygen (1O2), allowing the nanocomposite to have potential application in photodynamic therapy (PDT).32â€“35 Nevertheless, the present up-conversion nanoparticles always emit much stronger green emission than red emission, while ZnPc molecules mainly absorb the red emission.36â€“39 Additionally, green light is not in the optical transmission window, and therefore has limited penetrating depth, making it hard to detect.40,41 In order to amplify the red emission of the Yb3+/Er3+ couple, Han and co-workers made an attempt to increase the Yb3+ concentration.42 This method increased the cross-relaxation processes between Er3+ ions, and red-to-green (R/G) emissions were therefore increased. However, more Yb3+ dopants in the host lattice will decrease interatomic distance of Yb3+ â†’ Er3+ and thus facilitate back-energy-transfer from Er3+ to Yb3+, resulting in the decreased total up-conversion emission.43â€“45 Liu and co-authors prepared KMnF3:Yb,Er nanoparticles, which have high purity, single-band red up-conversion emission.46 However, the UCL intensity is insufficient at low power density because the host KMnF3 is in the cubic crystal phase, which is not as efficient as the hexagonal phase.47â€“53

Recently, a new technique was developed to enhance the red emission by co-doping limited amounts of Mn2+ into Yb3+/Er3+ coupled up-conversion nanoparticles.54,55 The total UCL intensity can be retained for the efficient lattice host. The increased intermediate energy level from Mn2+ transfers the energy of the green band of Er3+ to the red band, leading to decreased green emission but enhanced red emission. Capobianco et al. reported that the Yb3+ doped active-shell coating can significantly improve the total luminescence intensity, especially red up-conversion emission, compared to core-only and core-inert shell samples without Yb3+ doping.56 However, the improvement is still limited. Enlightened by the above two routes, we envision that the UCL intensity, especially the red emission should be able to be markedly enhanced by simultaneously co-doping Mn2+ ions into the Yb3+/Er3+ coupled up-conversion core, and coating with the Yb3+ doped fluoride active-shell, which would make the produced material suitable for red emission-sensitized PDT and bio-imaging.57,58 Furthermore, OA-coated UCNPs cannot be used directly in organisms because of their low dispersibility in aqueous solution and bad biocompatibility.58â€“61 The conventional methods for transforming hydrophobic nanoparticles into hydrophilic ones have their own drawbacks. For example, the ligand exchange method will change the physicochemical properties of the surface and decrease the long-term stability, and the silica coating route faces the problem of great agglomeration and undegradability of silica in physiological environments. Thus, exploiting novel routes for transforming hydrophobic nanoparticles into hydrophilic ones is highly desirable and promising.

Herein, a coreâ€“shell structure combining a Mn2+ doped core with Yb3+ doped active-shell coating to enhance red up-conversion emission and total UCL intensity was designed and fabricated for the first time. Also, gelatin, which possesses an amphiphilic nature and polypeptide chain structure, has been used for the conversion of hydrophobic UCNPs to hydrophilic ones. Firstly, gelatin extracted from the connective tissue of organisms is non-toxic, non-immunogenic and biodegradable, which ensure good biocompatibility for gelatin-encapsulated UCNPs.62 Secondly, the hydrophobic segments of the gelatin molecule can be attracted to the surface of UCNPs, while the hydrophilic section, with active groups exposed to the water media can be modified with functional groups.63,64 Furthermore, the interlaced net structure of the gelatin layer can tightly wrap the ZnPc molecules during the transferring process. Therefore, transferring the UCNPs and decorating with ZnPc molecules can be achieved in one step. Additionally, the enhanced red emission of UCNPs overlaps the UV adsorption of ZnPc in the red color range, and can be used to generate ROS upon NIR light irradiation, endowing UCNPs with PDT anticancer capability when excited by NIR irradiation. Accordingly, the gelatin-coated UCNPs were used as a multi-functional platform for PDT and UCL imaging. The biocompatibility with L929 cells, anticancer effects on HeLa cells in vitro and UCL imaging were investigated.

Results and discussion

Phase, composition, structure and luminescence properties

A one-step self-assembly method was adopted to fabricate the UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc nanocomposite, as illustrated in Scheme 1. The mixture of cyclohexane solution (containing OA-capped UCNPs and ZnPc molecules) and gelatin with amphiphilic polypeptide chains were emulsified in the water phase under vigorous stirring. With the evaporation of cyclohexane, abundant hydrophobic side chains gradually dragged the peptide chain to the surface of the UCNPs, and the hydrophilic segments were exposed to deionized water. At the same time, ZnPc molecules were wrapped in the interlaced net structure and conjugated with active groups, including â€“NH2, â€“COOH and â€“OH exposed outwardly. Finally, the diluted nanocomposite aqueous solution showed optical transparency. Compared with the existing hydrophilic modification, bio-conjugation and loading processes, this process is time and labor saving. More importantly, the possibility for too many hydrophobic photosensitizers conjugating on the surface of the gelatin layer is avoided, otherwise the biocompatibility would be lowered and the photosensitizer molecules would be easily leaked.

For pure NaGdF4:Yb,Er, it is obvious that the red emission of the nanoparticles is much weaker than the green one. As is known, green light has only a shallow penetration and is hard to detect in biological tissue. Therefore, it is necessary to enhance the red emission for the purpose of detecting and enhancing the ability to activate the red emission-activated ZnPc molecules. It is clear that the red/green (R/G) ratio is increased with increasing Mn2+ concentration. It is clear that the red region was enhanced, while the green region decreased. Moreover, with the increase in Mn2+ ions, more energy transfer between Er3+ and Mn2+ will also lead to decreased up-conversion emission. The change in the red and green emission can also be verified by the decay times. To keep the total emission intensity and simultaneously obtain bright red emission, we adopted the technique of coating an active shell onto a luminescent core. It was found that the lifetime at 655 nm was increased markedly to 394.3 Î¼s after coating with the active shell. As for the final nanocomposite, the lifetime at 655 nm decreased to 264.7 Î¼s; i.e. the energy of the red emission was transferred to the ZnPc molecules. In order to further improve the R/G ratio of the nanocarriers used in this work, we fabricated core nanoparticles with different Er3+ and Yb3+ concentrations and studied their luminescence properties. The emission spectra of NaGdF4:Yb,Er,0.4Mn and coreâ€“shell UCNPs are given in Fig. 3C; as shown, the total emission intensity was markedly enhanced. Moreover, coreâ€“shell nanoparticles with the NaGdF4:Yb active-shell have much stronger UC emission intensity than that with the NaGdF4 inert-shell (Fig. S1B, ESIâ€ ). Fig. 3D displays the UV-Vis absorption spectrum of ZnPc molecules, and the emission spectra of UCNPs and UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc. A strong absorption band at 650â€“690 nm for the ZnPc solution overlaps perfectly with the emission band of UCNPs in the red emission region. Therefore, fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) between UCNPs and ZnPc molecules will occur when irradiated by a 980 nm laser. This result also indicates that the ZnPc molecules were successfully decorated, and the distance between photosensitizers and the surface of UCNPs is no more than 10 nm, which allows for energy transfer. As expected, the red emission intensity of UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc is significantly lower than that of UCNPs upon 980 nm light excitation, which further indicates the efficient energy transfer from Er3+ to ZnPc molecules. The luminescence mechanism and relevant energy level diagrams are depicted in Fig. 4. Firstly, the energy transfer from the 2H11/2/4S3/2 levels of Er3+ to the 4T1 level of Mn2+ contributes to the decreased green emission, and then the following energy back transfer from the 4T1 level of Mn2+ to the 4F9/2 level of Er3+ leads to the enhanced red emission.37 Secondly, the doping concentration in the active shell is 20 mol% and the value of the core is 18 mol%, which means the total concentration of Yb3+ is raised after coating. It has been proved for Er3+/Yb3+ co-doped nanoparticles that increasing the doping level of Yb3+ causes a corresponding relative increase in the R/G emission ratio, which is mainly responsible for populating the red emitting (4F9/2) state (left, Fig. 4). Finally, the increased Yb3+ ions transfer more energy to Er3+, and the Er3+ ions in the 4I11/2 excited intermediate state decay non-radiatively to the lower-lying 4I13/2 state. A subsequent energy transfer from the excited Yb3+ ions brings them directly to the 4F9/2 state. A consecutive transfer can excite the Er3+ ions from the 4F9/2 state to the 2H9/2 state and lead to the observed 2H9/2 â†’ 4I11/2 red emission (right, Fig. 4).

Conclusions

In summary, a UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc anti-tumor nanoplatform has been rationally designed and applied for both UCL imaging and PDT. The use of gelatin with native amphiphilic structure and long polypeptides enables the transfer of the OA-coated UCNPs to the water phase, and the conjugation with ZnPc molecules. The doping of Mn2+ and coating with an active shell can simultaneously retain the total UCL emission intensity and enhance the red emission, which results in more energy being transferred to the photosensitizer, ZnPc, to effectively produce 1O2 for PDT. The as-prepared product has excellent biocompatibility and high anticancer efficiency, indicating that the nanocomposite has the potential for use in cancer diagnosis and therapy.

Experimental section

Materials and synthesis

All the chemical reagents in this experiment were of analytical grade, and were used without any further purification. These include sodium fluoride (NaF), manganese dichloride (MnCl2), hydrochloric acid (HCl), Gd2O3 (99.99%), Yb2O3 (99.99%) and Er2O3 (99.99%) (from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd), oleic acid (OA), 1-octadecene (ODE), 3-[4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl]-2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide (MTT), glutaraldehyde, 4â€²,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI), calcein AM, propidium iodide (PI) and zinc(II)-phthalocyanine (ZnPc) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Co. LLC (China). Gelatin was obtained from Tianjin Kermel Company. Sodium trifluoroacetate (CF3COONa), trifluoroacetic acid (CF3COOH) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) were purchased from Beijing Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd.

Synthesis of the oleic-acid-stabilized Î²-NaGdF4:Yb,Er,Mn core. Oleic-acid-capped NaGdF4:Yb,Er,Mn was synthesized according to the method developed previously.66 Typically, 1.0 mmol of Yb(oleate)3/Er(oleate)3/Mn(oleate)2/Gd(oleate)3 (mol/mol/mol/mol = 0.18:0.02:x:0.8 âˆ’ x), 5 mmol of NaF, and 15 mL of oleic acid (OA)/1-octadecene (ODE) (v/v = 1:1) mixed solvent were added to the reaction vessel and heated to 110 Â°C under vacuum for 30 min, to remove residual water and oxygen. After that, the temperature was increased to 300 Â°C and the reaction system was kept for 1.5 h in a N2 atmosphere. After the reaction system was cooled to room temperature, ethanol and cyclohexane were used to centrifuge the products. The finally obtained NaGdF4:Yb,Er,xMn nanoparticles were dispersed in 5 mL cyclohexane for further use.

Synthesis of OA-stabilized coreâ€“shell UCNPs

The cyclohexane solution of the above NaGdF4:Yb,Er,0.4Mn nanoparticles was added to a three-neck reaction vessel, then 0.4 mmol of Gd(CF3COO)3, 0.1 mmol of Yb(CF3COO)3, 1 mmol CF3COONa and 15 mL of oleic acid (OA)/1-octadecene (ODE) (v/v = 1:1) solvent were added to the reaction vessel. The solvent was then heated to 120 Â°C under a vacuum with magnetic stirring for 1 h and flushed with N2. The temperature was increased to 310 Â°C and kept for 1 h in a N2 atmosphere, and the OA-coated NaGdF4:Yb,Er,0.4Mn@NaGdF4:Yb (abbreviated as UCNPs) were obtained.

Synthesis of FITC modified gelatin

Gelatin (2.4 g) and 6 mg FITC were dissolved in 25 mL Na2CO3 buffer (pH = 9.2). The solution was heated to 40 Â°C with magnetic stirring. After reacting for 8 h in the dark, the gelatin solution was transferred into a dialysis bag with Mw cutoff of 12000â€“14000 Da, and immersed in deionized water at 40 Â°C to remove the free FITC. The dialysis was performed for 3 days and the water was changed 4â€“6 times each day. Finally, the solution was poured into a culture dish and dried at 60 Â°C to obtain the yellow-colored FITC-modified gelatin slices.

Preparation of gelatin-encapsulated and ZnPc/FITC decorated UCNPs

Briefly, 15 mg of gelatin were added to a beaker with 15 mL deionized water, and then placed in an ultrasonic cleaner at about 40 Â°C. After the gelatin was well-distributed in deionized water (about 60 minutes), 5 mL of OA-coated UCNPs (6 mg mLâˆ’1) and 2 mL of ZnPc (2 mg mLâˆ’1) in cyclohexane were put into the beaker and stirred for 24 h to evaporate the organic solvent, following the self-assembly of UCNPs to form nanocomposite spheres. The hydrophobic UCNPs were transferred into water and decorated with ZnPc in a one-step process. The products were washed three times with deionized water to remove the free gelatin and residual ZnPc molecules. The final nanocomposite was denoted as UCNPs@gelâ€“ZnPc. A similar experiment was carried out to obtain UCNPs@gelâ€“FITC.

ROS detection of the nanocomposite

DPBF was employed as a chemical probe to determine singlet oxygen by measuring the absorption via UV-Vis spectroscopy. Typically, 1 mL of ethanol solution containing DPBF (1 mg mLâˆ’1) was mixed with 1 mL of nanocomposite solution (1 mg mLâˆ’1) and put into the 24-well plate. The solution was kept in the dark and irradiated by a 980 nm laser (pump power of 1.44 W cmâˆ’2) for different time periods (0, 2, 5, 10 and 20 min). The solution was then centrifuged at 5000 rpm and the supernatant was collected for UV-Vis detection.
