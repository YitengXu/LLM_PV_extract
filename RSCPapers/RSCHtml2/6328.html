Abstract

Great efforts have been made to design and fabricate low-cost, high efficiency advanced electrode materials for energy storage devices such as batteries and high-performance supercapacitors. Choosing organic and redox active species that increase the faradaic charge storage of electrode systems has thus become a challenge to increase energy density and conductivity. 1. Introduction

The use of renewable energy, such as solar and wind power, is of great importance to meet the energy demands of a growing population, as non-renewable energy sources dwindle and the general public gain more knowledge on environmental pollution. One of the most important challenges in the energy storage industry is the design of energy storage devices from sustainable sources that have high energy capacity and high power capability.1 Electrical energy storage technology involves the storage of various forms of energy, including energy storage in electrochemical double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) and batteries.2 Batteries, including lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), which were the topic of the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2019, present a great breakthrough in science and energy storage technology, and are essential for use in electronics and other technologies. However, there is an increase in demand for alternative improved energy storage technologies due to the poor performance of LIBs, their high cost, the lack of lithium resources, their safety, and relatively low energy levels. In line with the development of materials, electrochemical energy storage technologies now play a more promising role in the global effort to meet sustainable energy supply challenges.3,4 Rechargeable batteries and high energy supercapacitors that have high power capabilities and long cycling lives have been widely investigated.5,6 Hybrid devices, or supercapatteries, have the characteristics of a both a rechargeable battery and supercapacitor combined in one device. Therefore, supercapattery behavior is similar to those of both rechargeable batteries and supercapacitors. However, these devices have a large capacitor that operates with more power energy capacity and a supercharger with longer charging/discharging capability than rechargeable batteries.7 The construction of hybrid devices with the aim of effectively increasing the overall cell voltage is a new approach that has been undertaken in recent years. Carbon materials are good materials for use in supercapacitors as they are easy to synthesize, have high temperature resistance, high cross-sections, good conductivity, high chemical stability, and are non-toxic. For this reason, activated carbon materials, including carbon nanotubes (CNTs), porous carbon, and graphene, have been reported as EDLC electrode materials over the past several years.8 Graphene has attracted a lot of attention due to its unique properties, such as excellent mechanical flexibility, high electrical conductivity and surface area, good thermal stability, and high theoretical capacity (550 F gâˆ’1).9 Various two-dimensional (2D) carbon materials, e.g., graphdiyne, graphene, and graphene,10â€“13 have been successfully synthesized and used in energy storage systems and conversion devices.1 Due to the strong van der Waals interactions between graphene sheets, they are prone to reassemble to form compact aggregates. To overcome this issue, graphene-based hydrogels/aerogels have been prepared that have interconnected 3D frameworks that facilitate rapid ion/electron transfer.9 However, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have a high surface area of more than 7000 m2 gâˆ’1 that of activated carbon, potentially allowing them to challenge the dominance of carbon activated electrodes in EDLCs.14 Due to the promotion of charge transfer within the structure of the MOF and the regeneration behavior of the metal cations, these nanostructures have been successfully used as electrode materials in energy storage devices.15 In this study, a new asymmetric supercapattery is constructed using an azure A (Az)/3D graphene aerogel (GA), Azâ€“GA, as an anode and a trypan (Try) blueâ€“nickel MOF (Tryâ€“Ni-MOF) nanosheet array as a cathode. Both of the new electrodes were synthesized via a one-step hydrothermal process and electrochemically characterized via cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic chargeâ€“discharge (GCD), and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements. The Azâ€“GA electrode exhibits good electrochemical performance and high specific capacitance of 716.06 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1. In terms of the Tryâ€“Ni-MOF electrode, it exhibits a high specific capacitance of 845.43 F gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1. The asymmetric Azâ€“GA//Tryâ€“Ni-MOF supercapattery constructed from these electrodes shows excellent electrochemical performance, a high specific capacity of 319 F gâˆ’1, excellent cycling stability with 92.12% capacitance retention after 5000 cycles at 10 A gâˆ’1, a specific energy of 66.55 W h kgâˆ’1, and a specific power of up to 4.45 kW kgâˆ’1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Chemical characterization and design considerations

Az as a redox material and 3D GA as a solid EDLC material were added together to prepare Azâ€“GA via a simple hydrothermal method using urea as a reducing agent (Scheme 1). In this reaction the hydrophilic amino groups of Az graft onto the GO nanosheets in the 3D GA via strong non-covalent Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking interactions. Such materials can be used in the fabrication of high-performance supercapacitors. The high-capacitive performance of the Azâ€“GA electrode can be attributed to Ï€ interactions, in which the porous structure of sponges such as Azâ€“GA can be used to establish rapid ion transport pathways and the movement of charges across materials as a result of the oxidation/reduction reactions of Az molecules at the GO surface.9 These features lead to the material having excellent long-term cycling stability and power density. The Azâ€“GA/NF electrode was used as an anode in an asymmetric supercapattery and a Ni3Cl2(OH)4/NF electrode was fabricated and used as the cathode. However, to enhance the capacity of the cathode, Try was selected as a linker in the synthesis of Tryâ€“Ni-MOF (Fig. 1). Try 3,3â€²-[(3,3â€²-dimethylbiphenyl-4,4â€²-diyl)didiazene-2,1-diyl]bis(5-amino-4-hydroxynaphthalene-2,7-disulfonic acid) is an organosulfonate compound that contains two hydrophilic amino and four sulfonate groups that is used as a histological dye and as a carcinogenic agent (Fig. 1, compound 1, 2). Try decomposes into three different intermediate compounds (Fig. 1, compounds 3, 4, and 5).20 The hydroxyl and sulfonate groups of the decomposed Try product 4 (5 amino-3,4-dihydroxynaphthalene-2,7-disulfunic acid), binds to nickel ions (Ni2+) to form a MOF (Fig. 1, compound 6). A MOF is a coordination network made up of metal ions and organic linkers that contains potential cavities in the form of 1D cylindrical channels of âˆ¼2 nm in diameter. Molecules form molecular assemblies exclusively in a finite and specific nanostructure, creating ion transport pathways and a large surface area for charge storage. Such easy access to the active materials of the electrode by electrolyte ions leads to the high power of the system. Finally, an asymmetric Azâ€“GA//Tryâ€“Ni-MOF device was fabricated using a 3 M KOH electrolyte with a potential window of 1.60 V. This system has the advantages of being cheap and very easy to prepare. Therefore, by intelligently selecting materials, a new strategy is proposed toward energy storage systems that can are effective and efficient in advancing the development of supercapatteries. Before examining the main topic of this study, the active ingredients in the fabrication of the electrode fabrication are described and evidence of the combination of Az with the 3D GA framework is presented, as well as the creation of a porous MOF structure upon the addition of Try.
