Abstract

Polymer solar cells (PSCs) have garnered considerable attention in recent years. However, improving the fill factor (FF) remains a big challenge. The FF is closely related to the mobilities of the donor. Thus, improving the mobilites of the donor by constructing an enlarged Ï€-conjugated fused ring in polymeric backnones could improve FF and power conversion efficiency (PCE). Here, a fused-ring dithienosilolodithiophene (DTTS)-based low-bandgap polymer of PDTTS with an enlarged Ï€-conjugated system was created and its thermal stability, absorption range, molecular energy, and the corresponding photovoltaic properties were investigated. UV-vis absorption spectra showed that PDTTS with enlarged conjugation on polymeric backbones exhibited a more obvious absorption shoulder peak compared with a similar polymer of PDTS containing a dithienosilole (DTS) unit. Hence, PDTTS had a more planar structure than PDTS and resulted in stronger intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions. X-ray diffraction revealed PDTTS to exhibit greater crystallinity than PDTS, which suggested more ordered Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking in PDTTS backbones. The mobility of PDTTS was more than one order of magnitude higher than that of PDTS, and benefited from the more planar conjugated structure of PDTTS. Photovoltaic devices based upon PDTTS/PC71BM blends exhibited efficiencies up to 6.33% with a higher FF of 65% for as-cast films without processing additives. Under optimized processing conditions, PDTS/PC71BM blends exhibited an efficiency of 3.22% with a FF of 53%. These preliminary results suggest that polymers with an enlarged Ï€-conjugated system possess higher mobility and facilitate effective carrier transportation, and boost FF and PCE values.

Introduction

Polymer solar cells (PSCs) based on a bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) structure have attracted considerable attention due to their great potential in the production of large-area, low-cost, and flexible photovoltaic devices via solution processing.1 In the last 5 years, scientists worldwide have carried out a considerable number of studies towards realizing the high-power conversion efficiencies (PCEs), and PCEs have reached 11â€“13% by conversional, invert or tandem device structures.2 To harvest the maximum photon flux and enable higher short-circuit current (Jsc) for photovoltaic polymers, a low bandgap that absorbs a wide portion of the solar spectrum (in particular photon absorption in the near-infrared region) is needed. By combination of an electron-rich donor (D) unit and an electron-deficient acceptor (A) unit (Dâ€“A type) or a combination of an aromatic (Ar) unit and quinoid (Qu) unit (Aâ€“Q type), classical narrow bandgap photovoltaic donor polymers such as PCDTBT, PCPDTBT, PBDTTBD, PTB7 and PBDTTT-C-T have been developed.3,4 Among these polymers, various good donor moieties (e.g., carbazole, benzodithiophene (BDT), benzodifuran, dithienosilole (DTS)) and acceptor moieties (e.g., benzothiadiazole (BT), thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione, diketopyrrolopyrrole, isoindigo) play important parts in the construction of high-performance polymers.4â€“7 Nevertheless, an improvement in PCE requires not only a high short-circuit current (Jsc) but also a large open-circuit voltage (Voc) and a satisfactory fill factor (FF). Until now, several works have focused on lowering energy levels to achieve high Voc and Jsc, but improving FF remains a considerable challenge. Recently, Marks and co-workers and You et al. showed that FF is closely related to the mobilities of the electron donor.8,9 Thus, improving the mobilites of the donor is becoming a new strategy to improve PCE. The mobilities of the donor depend not only on conjugated building blocks but also the degree of Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking of polymeric backbones. Results from our research team and those of other groups have demonstrated that enlarging the degree of conjugation of building blocks can improve (at least in part) the mobilities of the donor.8,10â€“12 Inspired by those conclusions, in this work, we designed a novel building block based on a good electron-rich unit of DTS.7 By introducing thienothiophene (Tt) units to the DTS core symmetrically to replace thiophene motifs, a fused-ring of DTTS unit was synthesized and copolymerized with a popular electron-deficient acceptor BT to obtain a low bandgap polymer of PDTTS (see Scheme 1).

In comparison with DTS, design of this DTTS unit is aimed for formation of an enlarged Ï€-conjugated structure to promote overlap of intermolecular orbitals and to improve hole mobilities. Correspondingly, the copolymerization of DTTS and a BT unit to obtain a low bandgap polymer and its favorable opticalâ€“electronic properties is expected and potentially applied in PSCs.

Fabrication of polymer solar cells

Except for deposition of the PEDOT:PSS layers, all the fabrication processes were carried out within a controlled atmosphere of a nitrogen dry box containing <10 ppm oxygen and moisture.

UV-vis absorption and X-ray diffraction

UV-vis absorption spectra provided information on the optical properties of the conjugated polymers. Fig. 1 shows the UV-vis absorption spectra of the polymers in dilute chloroform solutions and films cast from chloroform solution on a quartz substrate. The absorption spectra of PDTS and PDTTS showed two broad absorption bands. The short-wavelength absorption bands were at 300â€“500 nm, which could be ascribed to localized Ï€â€“Ï€* transitions. The long-wavelength absorption bands of 500â€“800 nm could be attributed to intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) between the D and A units. In comparison with the main absorption peaks of PDTS and PDTTS in solution, a general red-shifted absorption was observed for both in thin films, which could be attributed to more aggregated configurations in the solid state. The absorption main peaks (Î»max) of PDTS were at 620 and 651 nm in solution and thin film, respectively, and those of PDTTS were at 628 and 658 nm in solution and film. There was a slight red shift of the absorption peaks of PDTTS compared with those of PDTS. This finding implied that introduction of an enlarged Ï€-conjugated structure did not lead to an obvious change in electron-richness. PDTTS showed an obvious shoulder peak located at 698 nm in thin films, but PDTS did not show detectable shoulder peaks. These findings suggested that the fused-ring DTTS motif in PDTTS had a more planar structure than the DTS motif in PDTS and that, correspondingly, the former had stronger Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking than the latter. Interestingly, there was no obvious absorption shoulder peak in PDTTS solution in comparison with PDTTS films, which could be attributed to minor twists caused by n-heptyl side chains in solution. Besides the absorption range, the absorption coefficient of solid films is another important parameter in an active layer material in PSCs. The absorbance coefficients of PDTTS film (0.46 Ã— 10âˆ’2 nmâˆ’1) at the maximum absorption peak was â‰ˆ18% higher than that of PDTTS (0.39 Ã— 10âˆ’2 nmâˆ’1) (Fig. S3, ESIâ€ ). The enhanced absorption intensity of the PDTTS film could be attributed to the increased interchain interactions induced by a larger planar fused-ring structure and benefited the â€œsunlight harvestingâ€ of the photoactive layer. From their optical band gaps (Eoptg), PDTS and PDTTS exhibited similar Eoptg values, which were 1.53 eV and 1.52 eV, respectively, suggesting that Eoptg values were seldom affected if the DTS motif was replaced by a DTTS motif.

Conclusion

We synthesized a fused-ring DTTS incorporating two thieno[3,2-b]thiophene units bridged by a dialkylsilole. Copolymerization with 4,7-dithiophenylbenzothiadiazole by the Stille polycondensation afforded a low-bandgap polymer of PDTTS. In comparison with the absorption spectra of the similar polymeric backbone of PDTS, PDTTS showed a distinctly stronger shoulder peak due primarily to introduction of an enlarged Ï€-conjugated planar structure, and resulted in better Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking. XRD measurements of the distinguished aggregated peaks of PDTTS compared with that of PDTS were in accordance with the phenomena observed in UV-vis absorption spectra. More importantly, the mobility of PDTTS was more than one order of magnitude higher than that of PDTS due to the more planar molecular structure of DTTS than DTS. Photovoltaic-devices based upon PDTTS/PC71BM blends exhibited promising efficiency of up to 6.33% with a FF of 65%. However, under the same optimized processing conditions, PDTS/PC71BM blends had an efficiency of 3.22% and a FF of 53%. These preliminary results suggest that polymers containing fused DTTS moieties with an enlarged Ï€-conjugated system possess higher mobility, which benefited carrier transfer efficiency and can account (at least in part) for the higher FF and Jsc values for PDTTS-based devices. Therefore, a strategy of an extended Ï€-conjugated system on donor backbones could be used to design efficient photovoltaic materials.
