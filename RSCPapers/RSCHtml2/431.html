Abstract

Efficient charge separation of photo-generated electrons and holes is critical to achieve high solar to hydrogen conversion efficiency in photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting. N-type doping is generally used to improve the conductivity by increasing the majority carrier density and enhance the charge separation in the photoanode. However, minority carrier transport is also very important in the process of charge separation, especially in materials that possess inadequate minority carrier mobility. Herein, we take a BiVO4 PEC water splitting cell as an example to demonstrate how to analyze the limiting factor and to formulate the corresponding solutions to improve the hole mobility. The benefits and problems caused by n-type doping (W-doping here) of BiVO4 are analyzed. Codoping with Ti further enhances the charge separation by improving the hole transport and leads to a cathodic shift of the photocurrent onset potential. A high charge separation efficiency (79% at 1.23 VRHE) in a compact BiVO4 photoanode has been achieved without any nanostructure formation. Theoretical results show that Wâ€“Ti codoping has decreased the hole polaron hopping activation energy by 11.5% compared with mono-W doping, and this has resulted in a hole mobility increase by 29%. The calculated adsorption energy and reaction Gibbs free energies indicate that the Ti site is energetically more favorable for water splitting. Moreover, the Ti site possesses a lower overpotential in the Wâ€“Ti codoped sample compared with the mono-W doped sample. The current study indicates that in order to improve the solar energy conversion efficiency, there should be a balanced charge transport of both majority and minority charge carriers. This can be achieved by simply choosing appropriate codoping elements.

1. Introduction

Solar energy is one of the most promising renewable and clean energy sources to overcome the environmental challenges caused by fossil fuel consumption. Photoelectrochemical (PEC) solar water splitting is one of the most promising solutions to convert solar energy into clean chemical energy.1â€“4 Efficient charge separation of photo-generated electrons and holes is critical to achieving high solar to hydrogen conversion efficiency. Doping is an effective way to improve the conductivity by increasing the majority carrier density and enhance the charge separation.5,6 However, minority carrier transport should not be neglected, especially for those having a poor transport ability. In principle, a well-balanced electron and hole transport is crucial for high efficiency;7 however, research in this area is still lacking.

Nanostructuring is known to be an effective strategy to shorten the carrier transport distance from where it is generated to the surface reaction site, which could effectively reduce the photo-generated carrier recombination.6,8â€“12 Creation of heterojunctions is another effective way to enhance the minority carrier transport, using the internal electric field formed between the junction to facilitate the charge carrier separation.13,14 A gradient-doped structure, in which the surface doping concentration is lower than the bulk concentration, is also an effective way to promote the minority carrier to diffuse to the surface.15,16 However, these strategies do not change the mobility of minority carriers. A fundamental understanding of intrinsic minority transport is still lacking.

In addition to bulk charge separation, surface charge injection to oxidize water is also a key step to the energy conversion efficiency. Due to the sluggish oxygen evolution reaction and the presence of surface states, surface recombination is generally inevitable. Thus, these recombination losses lead to low charge transfer efficiency and high overpotential for PEC water oxidation. Numerous approaches including surface treatment or etching,17â€“19 overlayer coating,20â€“24 and electrocatalyst deposition6,25,26 have been applied to effectively suppress the surface recombination. However, there is a lack of understanding of the surface catalytic properties of both pristine and doped BiVO4 in the existing literature.

Here, we take a BiVO4 PEC water splitting cell as an example to demonstrate how to analyze the limitation factor for the energy conversion efficiency, as well as to formulate the corresponding solutions to improve the charge separation. Codoping was employed here to overcome the limitations on the basis of the reported enhancement.27â€“31 The surface catalytic properties of pristine and doped BiVO4 are analyzed from both experimental and theoretical perspectives. Monoclinic BiVO4 is one of the most promising photoanodes for PEC water-splitting. Its 2.4 eV optical bandgap corresponds to a theoretical conversion efficiency of 9.1%. It also possesses a favorable conduction band edge position for water splitting.6,16,32,33 In previous reports, W or Mo doping was found to be effective in enhancing the electronic conductivity.34,35 In this study, we quantitatively analyzed the limiting factor of BiVO4 and pointed out both the benefits and deficiencies caused by n-type doping (W-doping here). It is found that W doping increases the static dielectric constant which lowers the carrier mobility although it enhances the electron density. We find that Wâ€“Ti codoping into BiVO4 can lower the static dielectric constant, which enhances the hole mobility and further improves the charge separation efficiency to 79%. Moreover, this codoping has also caused a cathodic shift of the photocurrent onset potential by introducing active surface sites related to the presence of Ti. An impressively high charge separation efficiency (79% at 1.23 VRHE) and a simultaneous cathodic shift of the photocurrent onset potential has been realized by Wâ€“Ti codoping in a compact BiVO4 photoanode without any nanostructure formation.

2. Experimental

2.1 Preparation of thin films

Bismuth trioxide, ammonium metavanadate, ammonium tungstate hydrate and titanium(IV) bis (ammonium lactato) dihydroxide solution were dissolved in ethylene glycol with an appropriate amount of nitric acid (100 ml solution containing 5 ml nitric acid) to form 0.03 M Bi, V, W, and Ti precursor solutions, respectively. These solutions were mixed according to the stoichiometric ratio for W doped BiVO4 and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4. Citric acid (CA) was also added according to a stoichiometric ratio of CA:M = 1.5:1 (M is the total amount of cation including dopants). After mixing, 70 Î¼l of the precursor solution were dropped on the FTO substrates (1 cm Ã— 2 cm with half of the length covered with transparent tape for electrical contact). The samples were dried at 120 Â°C for 30 minutes, and subsequently calcined at 500 Â°C in a furnace for 30 min to obtain the thin films. An annealing temperature of 500 Â°C was chosen according to the TG-DTA results (Fig. S1â€ ).

Co-Pi decorated Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 was synthesized by electrodeposition. An electrochemical cell with a three-electrode configuration was employed for the CoPi electrodeposition. Pt foil was used as the counter electrode and a Ag/AgCl electrode (in saturated KCl solution) was used as the reference electrode. 0.5 mM Co(NO3)2 was added to 0.1 M KPi solution to serve as the electrolyte, and the pH value was adjusted to 7. For the electrodeposition, a constant current density of 20 Î¼A cmâˆ’2 was applied. The amount of CoPi was estimated to be 5 mC cmâˆ’2. After electrodeposition, the films were washed with deionized water.

3.2 Effect of Wâ€“Ti codoping on the charge separation of BiVO4

Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 films were prepared by the drop-casting method reported before.40 The crystallinity and evidence for successful doping was analyzed by XPS, EDX and Raman spectroscopy (Fig. S4â€“S7 and Table S1, details are given in the ESIâ€ ). We find that after Ti codoping into W doped BiVO4, the performance can be greatly enhanced. After optimization (Fig. S8â€ ) of Ti codoping concentrations, a nominal 2%W and 2%Ti codoped BiVO4 shows much better performance than the W doped BiVO4, corresponding to almost 2 times that of W doped BiVO4 (Fig. 2a). For comparison, the photocurrent of Ti doped BiVO4 is similar to that of the pristine sample. This indicates that Ti mono-doping has no effect on the photocurrent improvement. This is understandable since the electronic conductivity is the limiting factor for pristine BiVO4 and Ti doping has no contribution to this. Based on the series comparison, we conclude that to obtain high solar conversion efficiency, the electronic conductivity has to be improved as the foundation, based on which further enhancement can be designed. Fig. 2b shows the photocurrent of CoPi decorated Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4, in which CoPi is loaded by electro-deposition with a loading amount of around 5 mC cmâˆ’2 according to a previous study.41 The photocurrent reaches to 2.4 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE by suppression of the surface recombination after the decoration of CoPi.

3.3 Effect of Wâ€“Ti codoping on surface catalysis

In Fig. 2a, another feature from codoping with Ti is the photocurrent onset potential. Ti codoping causes a cathodic shift of the photocurrent onset by 100 mV (0.68 VRHE for Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 and 0.78 VRHE for W doped BiVO4). In order to understand this, experiments and theoretical calculations are conducted for further analyses.

Mottâ€“Schottky plots are shown in Fig. 4a. The flat-band potential VF of pristine BiVO4 is estimated to be âˆ’0.32 VRHE, while that of W doped BiVO4 is about âˆ’0.20 VRHE. There is a positive shift compared with pristine BiVO4. However, after codoping with Ti, the flat-band potential is about âˆ’0.31 VRHE, which is 0.11 V negative than that of W doped BiVO4. This flat-band potential change correlates nicely with that of the photocurrent onset potential (0.1 V in Fig. 2a). The flat-band potential is generally considered to be located just under the conduction band of n-type semiconductors.52 Thus, it is reasonable to deduce that the shift of band potential corresponds to the shift of the conduction band. Another feature caused by the Wâ€“Ti codoping is the carrier concentration change. From Mottâ€“Schottky plots the carrier density is 1.2 Ã— 1020 cmâˆ’3 for pristine BiVO4, 4.46 Ã— 1020 cmâˆ’3 for W-doped BiVO4 and 4.89 Ã— 1020 cmâˆ’3 for Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4. W doping has increased the density greatly, while Ti codoping almost has no further enhancement in the density. One would expect Ti to act as an acceptor as it has a valence state of 4+, and thus compensates the extra electrons provided by W6+. However, we find that this is not true from the experiment, which could possibly be ascribed to the fact that the doping elements are at a low level and far separate from each other. Thus, W-doping and Ti-doping do not interfere with each other. Rather, they are able to improve electron and hole transport simultaneously.

It has been reported that the photocurrent onset is closely related to the charging of the surface states which generally exist on the metal oxide surfaces.53 The open circuit potential also proves the existence of such surface states. In the dark, the equilibrium between the photoelectrode and the electrolyte dictates that the measured open-circuit potential reports on the position of the Fermi level.54 Under ideal conditions, in the complete absence of an overpotential, an equilibrium open-circuit potential of 1.23 V would be expected, because the H2O/O2 redox couple dominates the electrochemical potential of the electrolyte in the dark. It was found that the equilibrium potentials of pristine, W-doped and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 were almost the same at around 0.61 VRHE (Fig. S15â€ ), and was far below 1.23 V. Thus, it is supposed that there exist some surface states on the BiVO4 surface. An equivalent circuit was employed here to analyze the photoelectrochemical processes.55 In the equivalent circuit (inset in Fig. 4b), Rs represents the total resistance from the external circuit, Rtrap represents the resistance of the surface states trapping holes, Rss represents the surface charge transfer resistance from the surface states to oxidize water, Cbulk represents the space charge capacitance of the bulk hematite, and Css represents the surface state capacitance, which dominates charging of the surface states and has a close correlation with the photocurrent onset.53 The Css of pristine, W doped, and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 obtained from EIS data is given in Fig. 4b. The values for Css show a Gaussian behavior, and the charging potential follows the order pristine < Wâ€“Ti codped < W doped. The charging potentials of Css somehow also correlate with the photocurrent onsets except for the pristine one, which is possibly due to poor conductivity and large resistance. Further discussion will be made next.

Fig. 4c shows the Nyquist plots of pristine, W doped and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 at an applied potential of 1.23 VRHE, which are used to study the surface charge injection at the interface. The same equivalent circuit (inset in Fig. 4b) was adapted for the analysis. The resistances of trapping holes by the surface states (Rtrap) of pristine, W doped and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 are 13320 Î©, 657 Î© and 35 Î©, respectively. Compared with doped BiVO4, the pristine one has a much larger hole trapping resistance, which requires a higher voltage to overcome the resistance. This explains why the pristine sample has a poor photocurrent onset, although it has a more negative conduction band and lower charging potential of the surface states as shown in Fig. 4b. The surface charge transfer resistances Rss of pristine, W doped and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4 are about 3996 Î©, 2272 Î© and 2218 Î©, respectively. Thus, the doped samples have smaller charge transfer resistances than the pristine and Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4. This trend agrees with the calculated charge transfer efficiencies. To unravel how Wâ€“Ti codoping affects the charge transfer, it is important to explore the surface conditions in the BiVO4. It is known that hydroxyterminated surface states will be formed on metal oxide electrode surfaces in aqueous solutions.53,56â€“58 Thus, hole accumulation or trapping in these surface states can be treated as an oxidation process of these surface states, and thus these active surface states can be characterized by cyclic voltammetry scans, in which the current peak can be seen due to the reduction of these oxidized surface species.58 To verify our speculation, we carried out cyclic voltammetry scans of BiVO4 in Na2SO4 electrolyte at a scan rate of 100 mV sâˆ’1 after holding the electrode at a potential of 1.8 VRHE for 120 seconds under 1 sun illumination. Fig. 4d illustrates the changes in the surface states of BiVO4 caused by mono-W doping and Wâ€“Ti codoping compared with the pristine one. For pristine BiVO4, no obvious reduction peak was observed. One obvious reduction current peak around 0.65 VRHE emerged for the W doped sample. For the Wâ€“Ti codoped sample, two obvious reduction current peaks at around 1 V and 0.5 VRHE were observed. These reduction current peaks are evidence of the surface states.59 As seen, the Wâ€“Ti codoped sample has more active surface states to participate in the water oxidation, which implies that Wâ€“Ti codoping favors the surface catalysis.

From Fig. 5a we can see that there are no obvious changes in the surface structure when doping with W or Ti in the V site. However, the PDOS in Fig. 5b shows that mono-doping on the surface will introduce unoccupied electronic states deep within the BiVO4 bandgap, which is reported to be resulting from the donated electrons to form small electron polarons via self-trapping.50 The deep level states are usually surface trap states, because Î± and Î² orbitals in the deep level states are asymmetric (Fig. S16â€ ). Such trap states usually serve as recombination centers. However, no such kinds of trap states are found in the case of Wâ€“Ti codoping , which indicates that Wâ€“Ti codoping facilitates the surface catalysis.

The calculated Gibbs free energies of the five surface sites during the OER process on the BiVO4 (010) surface are shown in Fig. 5c according to the adsorption of several key species (Fig. S17â€ ), which shows different abilities for water splitting. Comparing the Gibbs free energies on different sites, it is found the Ti site is closer to the ideal OER catalysis process than other sites, indicating that the Ti site is energetically more favorable for water splitting. In order to further understand the surface catalytic ability, the overpotentials of different surface sites were also calculated, as shown in Fig. 5d. Compared with pristine BiVO4, no much improvement has been found in the overpotential in the Bi or W site. However, the Ti site possesses a lower overpotential in the Wâ€“Ti codoped sample (0.68 V). This well explains the observed photocurrent onset potential shift in Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4. Although the Ti site on the Ti doped surface also shows a low overpotential (0.44 V), the experimentally observed performance was poor. This is mainly due to the fact that the Ti mono-doped sample has poor electron conductivity and consumes more voltage to overcome the large resistance, which again proves that Ti doping can only become effective after the poor electron transport has been overcome by W doping.

4. Conclusions

Quantitative analysis shows that the low charge separation (22%) is the limiting factor for the poor performance of pristine BiVO4. W doping, which improves the electron conductivity, enhances the charge separation from 22% to 42% (at 1.23 VRHE). However, the photocurrent of W doped BiVO4 is far below the theoretical value. In this study, we find that Wâ€“Ti codoping enhances the photocurrent to about 2 times the one of W mono-doping, while Ti mono-doping has nearly no effect on the photocurrent improvement. This enhancement is mainly attributed to the better charge separation efficiency (79% at 1.23 VRHE). Furthermore, the enhanced charge separation is found to arise from the enhanced hole transport. Transient absorption spectroscopy shows that the hole diffusion length of W doped BiVO4 is around 250 nm, and it increases to around 370 nm for the Wâ€“Ti codoped BiVO4. This improvement of the hole diffusion length indicates that the Wâ€“Ti codoping dramatically increases the hole mobility which facilitates its transport. Theoretical results show that W doping increases the static dielectric constant which lowers the carrier mobility. Wâ€“Ti codoping lowers the static dielectric constant, and thus, the hole polaron hopping activation energy WH is lowered by 11.5% after Wâ€“Ti codoping. Moreover, Wâ€“Ti codoping causes a cathodic shift of photocurrent onset potential by 100 mV compared with W doped BiVO4. The calculated adsorption energy and reaction Gibbs free energies indicate that the Ti site is energetically more favorable for water splitting. Moreover, the Ti site possesses a lower overpotential in the Wâ€“Ti codoped sample (0.68 V) compared with the mono-W doped sample (1.68 V). The current study sheds light onto ways to identify the key factor(s) and to improve the charge separation and transfer efficiencies of a compact photoelectrode. It indicates that in a photo electrode system, the minority carrier mobility is also an important consideration. High solar conversion efficiency can only be obtained in a well-balanced electron and hole transport system.
