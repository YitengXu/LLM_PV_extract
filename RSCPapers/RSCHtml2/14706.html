Abstract

Supercapacitors are widely accepted as one of the energy storage devices in the realm of sustainable and renewable energy storage. Supercapacitors have emerged as a good alternative to traditional capacitors and fuel cells due to their higher energy density and power density compared to batteries and fuel cells. However, supercapacitors have some drawbacks such as low energy density and poor cycle life compared to batteries. To overcome these issues, researchers are paying much attention to the fabrication of metal oxide nanostructures and their modification by different approaches such as doping, introducing oxygen vacancies, and hybridization with nanomaterials of carbon allotropes for enhanced electrochemical properties. In this review article, we have presented the above-mentioned topics with the aid of recently reported works. Moreover, we have provided theoretical insights from density functional theory for the electrochemical behavior of the electrode materials from the published works. This review concisely presents the advancement in the supercapacitor energy storage field and the different approaches involved in the fabrication of supercapacitor electrode materials, which will be very handy to the researchers working in the field of energy storage. Further, the challenges and future perspectives of this exciting research field are discussed in detail.

1. Introduction

1.1 Energy crisis and alternatives

Energy crisis and climate change are two highly correlated phenomena. The energy demand is growing day by day due to the rise in the population, rapid industrialization, and the increased usage of electronic appliances. Simultaneously, the exhaustion of fossil fuels and other forms of non-renewable energy resources is triggering both economic and environmental crises throughout the globe. Therefore, a majority of the developed and developing countries have taken several timely measures to find out possible alternative paths to address the global energy deficit in the coming years. The available non-renewable energy sources such as crude oil, charcoal, and natural gas are insufficient, and the global energy demand will surely grow catastrophically. It is to be noted that the formation of these kinds of non-renewable energy sources could take at least about a million years or more. Hence, the utilization of renewable energy resources such as solar, wind, hydro, and biomass energy is highly essential to preserve our non-renewable fossil fuel reserves and to fulfil the energy demand in the current scenario. The usage of renewable resources has increased over the past few decades. However, due to the inconsistency in the generation of electricity from these renewable energy resources, effective storage technologies must be implemented to store and distribute the energy as and when required. Electrochemical energy conversion (EEC) and electrochemical energy storage (EES) systems are presently playing a lead role in the global energy platform to tackle sustainable energy and growth challenges.1–4

1.2 Supercapacitors

Among various EEC and EES systems, fuel cells, batteries, and ultracapacitors or supercapacitors (SCs) are considered as the three leading electrochemical energy conversion and storage technologies. In order to overcome the technical issues of batteries, SCs have emerged as promising alternatives. SCs, also known as electrochemical capacitors or ultracapacitors, have a similar cell construction to traditional dielectric capacitors, except the metal electrodes are substituted by porous electrodes with high specific surface areas and the dielectric material is replaced by a suitable electrolytic substance to facilitate ion channelling. SCs have attracted substantial attention in a broad range of applications, such as portable electronics, electric or hybrid electric vehicles, and smart grids. However, SCs exhibit a lower energy density than batteries and fuel cells, which is the major challenge in the current scenario. Therefore, scientists are relentlessly trying to improve the energy density of SCs by developing new electrode materials and electrolytes and ingenious device architectures.5–7Fig. 1(a) illustrates the schematic structure of an SC. A typical SC device comprises two electrodes separated by an ion-permeable membrane denoted as the separator and an electrolyte. Fig. 1(b) presents the comparison of Ragone plots of conventional capacitors, SCs, batteries and fuel cells, as various energy storage and conversion devices, in terms of power density and energy density. The Ragone plot reveals that the power density of the SCs is lower than that of conventional capacitors while the energy density is many times higher. SCs attain the highest power density (1–10 kW kg−1) over batteries and other energy storage devices due to their charge storage mechanism. In other words, SCs can be completely charged or discharged in seconds, whereas batteries normally require several minutes or hours.8 Therefore, SCs can efficiently bridge the gap between batteries and conventional capacitors in terms of both power and energy densities and SCs are excellent in life span, operating temperature, efficiency, cost, and safety.9,10 In order to obtain high-performance metrics in SC, electrode materials should possess high electrical conductivity and high surface area. The electrode materials in SC devices should not react with the electrolyte and remain chemically stable under the working potential window. The microporous separator plays a vital role in allowing the electrolyte ions to pass through them as well as avoiding a short circuit between two electrodes. When an external bias is applied across an SC device, an electrical double layer formation occurs at the electrode surface. Depending on the nature of the electrode materials and charge storage mechanism, SCs are categorized (as shown in Fig. 1(c)) into various types such as (i) electric double layer capacitors, (ii) pseudocapacitors, and (iii) hybrid capacitors.4,11,12

1.3 Supercapacitors-working mechanism

1.3.1 Electric double-layer capacitance. In the case of an electrical double-layer capacitor (EDLC), the charge storage occurs via electrochemical adsorption of ions at the interface between the electrode and electrolyte under an applied cell voltage, and no charge transfer happens across the electrodes, indicating a non-faradic process. The concept of the formation of an electrical double layer (EDL) was first introduced by Helmholtz in the 19th century when he investigated the distribution of oppositely charged ions at the interface of colloidal particles. According to the Helmholtz double layer model, two distinct layers consisting charges of opposite nature, are formed at the electrode–electrolyte interface. The typical thickness of individual layer is equal to the dimension of one atom. Later on, the Helmholtz double-layer model was modified by Gouy and Chapman by introducing the concept of thermal motion of ions near a charged surface, which is referred to as the diffuse layer (Fig. 2(b)). In 1924, Stern merged the Helmholtz double-layer model and the Gouy–Chapman model by considering the hydrodynamic motion of the ionic species in the diffuse layer (the electrode strongly adsorbs ions) and the gathering of ions close to the electrode surface, as illustrated in Fig. 2(c). The concept of the EDLC is a combination of two regions: (i) the Helmholtz double-layer and (ii) the diffuse layer. Thus, the total capacitance (C) can be expressed by the following equation:14–16
 
1/C = 1/CH + 1/CD(1)
where CH is the (Helmholtz) layer capacitance independent of the surface-inactive electrolyte concentration, and CD is the diffuse layer capacitance. The capacitance of the EDLC follows the parallel-plate capacitor mechanism for charge storage:14,17
 
C = εrεoA/d(2)
where εr is the electrolyte dielectric constant, εo is the permittivity of a vacuum, A is the electrode specific surface area, and d is the effective thickness of the electric double layer.

Different types of carbonaceous materials, such as activated carbon, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), porous carbon, graphene, graphene oxides and graphene derivatives, have been widely reported electrode materials for EDLCs.18,19Fig. 2(d) represents the formation of electric double layer on the surface of porous carbon electrode. The mechanism of double layer formation in the case of porous carbon electrodes is more complex in comparison to what is observed in the case of an infinite plane electrode surface. In porous carbon electrodes, ion transportation is affected by various parameters, such as the tortuous mass transfer path, space constraints inside the pores, the ohmic resistance associated with the electrolyte, and the wetting behavior of the pore surface by the electrolyte.14 The cyclic voltammogram (CV) and charge–discharge (CD) profile of carbon SCs are shown in Fig. 2(e) and (f), respectively, which reveals that a double-layer carbon supercapacitor shows a characteristic rectangular shape, with a voltage-independent current, whereas CD processes are almost linear.13

1.3.2 Pseudocapacitors. Despite the exceptional progress in carbonaceous materials properties, their limited energy density usually limits EDLC-based SCs to drive portable electronics and heavy electronic vehicles. Compared to EDLC-based SCs, pseudocapacitors store charge via faradaic processes based on fast, reversible redox reactions at the surface or near-surface of the electroactive materials. According to Conway, the different faradic processes can be distinguished in a pseudocapacitive electrode material, such as (i) underpotential deposition, (ii) surface redox pseudocapacitance and (iii) intercalation pseudocapacitance.20,21 Underpotential deposition happens in the case of different types of metal (Ru, Pt, Au, and Rh) surfaces by forming a monolayer of metal ions due to the faradaic absorption or desorption process (Fig. 3(a and d)).22 For pseudocapacitance based on redox reactions, the charge is stored through the faradaic charge-transfer process where ions are electrochemically adsorbed onto the surface or near the surface of the electrode material (Fig. 3(b and e)). Different types of electroactive materials, including transition metal oxides such as MnO2 and RuO2 and conducting polymers like polypyrrole and polyaniline, exhibit redox pseudocapacitance for charge storage.23–25

Similarly, intercalation pseudocapacitance happens when ions are reversibly inserted into and extracted from the tunnels or layers of a redox-active material associated with the faradaic charge-transfer process without the crystallographic phase change (Fig. 3(c and f)). Mostly, electroactive materials such as nanostructured TiO2, Nb2O5, and MoO3 exhibit intercalation-based pseudocapacitance in organic electrolytes.27 These three different pseudocapacitive mechanisms occur at the electrode surface due to their different physical processes and different types of electroactive materials. The electrochemical features of pseudocapacitive materials are usually examined by CV and CD tests. Fig. 3(g) shows the CV and CD profile of the various types of electrode materials, which reveals that Type B pseudocapacitive is faradaic in nature, while Type C faradaic is not capacitive in nature.26 Generally, pseudocapacitors exhibit higher specific capacitance and energy density compared to the EDLCs. However, pseudocapacitors frequently suffer from poor cycle life due to the bad electrical conductivity and low power density.28,29

1.3.3 Hybrid supercapacitors. The working principle of hybrid SCs (Fig. 4(a, b)) is governed by a combination of the EDLC (non-faradaic) and pseudocapacitor (faradaic) charge storage principles. In other words, a hybrid supercapacitor can try to utilize the relative advantages and mitigate the relative disadvantages of EDLCs and pseudocapacitors to realize better electrochemical performance or charge storage performance. Exploiting both non-faradaic and faradaic process charge storage principles, hybrid SCs have achieved higher energy and power densities than EDLCs and pseudocapacitors without compromising cycling stability and rate capability. Based on electrode configuration, the hybrid SCs can be divided into three categories: (i) asymmetric hybrids, (ii) battery-type hybrids and (iii) composite hybrids.13,30,31
Fig. 4 (a) The fundamental configuration of the hybrid supercapacitor. Reproduced with permission.30 (b) Schematic diagram of various types of hybridization approaches for fabrication of hybrid supercapacitors. Reproduced with permission.13

An asymmetric hybrid supercapacitor can be fabricated by combining an EDLC electrode (usually negative electrode) with a pseudocapacitive electrode (usually positive electrode), which can work up to a higher potential window compared to normal symmetric SCs, to enhance the energy density of the supercapacitor.32 Similarly, the battery-type hybrid supercapacitor has become another class of energy storage device, which can bridge the energy and power density of batteries and SCs. The battery-type hybrid supercapacitor uses battery-type and capacitive-type electrode materials in the supercapacitor device, enhancing the overall electrochemical energy performance.33,34 Composite hybrid SCs can be fabricated by integrating carbonaceous materials (EDLC) with pseudocapacitive materials such as transition metal oxides/hydroxides/chalcogenides/conducting polymers which incorporate both physical and chemical charge storage mechanisms together in a single electrode. The composite hybrid supercapacitor device shows better electrochemical performance than the parent or bare supercapacitor device due to both EDLC and pseudocapacitive charge storage mechanisms' synergistic effect.18,35

1.3.4 Supercapatteries. The term ‘supercapattery’ (supercapacitor + battery) represents the combination of a supercapacitor and a battery, which is different from either SCs or rechargeable batteries in terms of fundamental principles and technological prospects. Therefore, supercapattery devices take advantage of both capacitive and non-capacitive faradaic (or Nernstian) charge storage mechanisms at either the electrode material level or the device level with an improvement in energy and power densities. Based upon the charge storage mechanism of electrodes, the assemblies of supercapattery devices can be divided into four categories;36,37

(i) Capacitive faradaic system + capacitive non-faradaic system (pseudocapacitive + EDLC).

(ii) Capacitive faradaic system + capacitive faradaic system (pseudocapacitive + pseudocapacitive).

(iii) Capacitive non-faradaic system + non-capacitive faradaic system (EDLC + battery).

(iv) Capacitive faradaic system + non-capacitive faradaic system (pseudocapacitive + battery).

2. Metal oxide-based supercapacitors

2.1 Potential materials for supercapacitors

As we know, the massive success of graphene and its derivatives in energy-storage technologies, especially SCs, has triggered a flurry of research activities in investigating its analogues, including a vast diversity of 2-dimensional (2D) nanomaterials and/or compounds such as metal oxides,38 metal chalcogenides,39 metal phosphates,40 metal carbides and nitrides41 and jarosites42 (Fig. 5). These kinds of nanomaterials demonstrate extraordinary properties that differ from those of their bulk lamellar systems. Therefore, the study of 2D nanomaterials has become the main focus in the fields of energy conversion, energy storage, catalysis, and sensors.43

2.2 Various metal oxides

Amongst the numerous nanomaterials used for SCs, metal oxides have gathered significant attention due to their physical and chemical properties. Compared with metal chalcogenides, metal oxides are easy to prepare (and thus facilitate large-scale production), chemically stable, environmentally friendly, and compatible with wide varieties of electrolytes, which are crucial to accomplish durable and cost-effective SC devices. In addition, metal oxides exhibit higher theoretical specific capacitance/capacity, higher energy density, and better electrochemical performance compared to graphene and other 2D materials due to the combined effect of high electrode surface area and multiple oxidation states.43,44 Until now, a broad range of metal oxides, including binary metal oxides such as RuO2, MnO2, TiO2, NiO Co3O4, V2O5, MoO3, and Cr2O3, and ternary metal oxides such as CuCo2O4, NiCo2O4, ZnCo2O4, FeCo2O4, etc., have been successfully prepared via either direct exfoliation from their corresponding bulk counterparts or self-assembly from salt precursor-containing solutions.45 For example, ruthenium oxide (RuO2), and manganese oxide (MnO2) have been extensively studied as electrode materials for SCs due to their fast, reversible redox reactions.46,47

2.3 Desired factors influencing the energy storage performance of metal oxide based supercapacitors

Usually, the supercapacitive performance of metal oxides depends upon few crucial factors such as (i) crystallinity, (ii) morphology, (iii) specific surface area, (iv) electronic conductivity, and (v) mass loading of the electroactive material.48,49
2.3.1 Crystallinity. Generally, most of the metal oxides exhibit the faradaic redox mechanism for charge storage with the exception of MnO2 and RuO2, which show actual pseudocapacitive behavior. The degree of crystallinity is one of the critical factors which can affect the electrochemical activities of the metal oxide materials. Pseudocapacitive materials such as MnO2 and RuO2 often have poor crystallinity and a high density of the grain boundaries with fine grains, leading to efficient diffusion channels for the electrolyte ions and high specific capacitance and energy density. Therefore, amorphous metal oxides show better electrochemical performances as compared to their high-crystalline counterparts.28,50 Recently, Yan et al. discussed the importance of the amorphous structure of metal oxides compared to the crystalline structure in the field of electrochemical energy storage and conversion.51
2.3.2 Morphology. The morphology of metal oxides has a significant influence on electrochemical performance. The term morphology is closely related to the specific surface area, pore-size distribution, and the diffusion pathway of electroactive materials.48,52 SCs, involving 2D structures such as nanosheet, nano-lamellar, and nano-film structures, exhibit better physical and chemical properties such as mechanical stability, flexibility, transparency, and chemical stability compared to other nanostructures (OD and 1D).53 Xiao et al. synthesized NiO nanosheets by the hydrothermal method without using the assistance of any templates and surfactants with an average thickness of about 30 nm (shown in Fig. 6(a and b)).54 The electrochemical performance of the as prepared NiO nanosheet electrode was evaluated in 3 M KOH electrolyte. The CV profile of the NiO nanosheet electrode is shown in Fig. 6(c) and it achieved a specific capacitance of 81.67 F g−1. Xiang et al. prepared Co3O4 nanosheets with varied contents of oxygen vacancies to examine the effects of oxygen vacancies on the intrinsic conductivity, the structure, chemistry, and SC performance.55Fig. 6(d and e) shows the SEM micrograph of Co3O4 nanosheets and oxygen vacancy-rich Co3O4, which indicates that the thickness of nanosheets is about 20 nm. The electrochemical test of the prepared samples was carried out in three electrode systems using 6 M KOH electrolyte. The comparative CD profile of all the samples is provided in Fig. 6(f). The oxygen vacancy-rich Co3O4 obtains the highest specific capacitance of 2195 F g−1 at a current density of 1 A g−1. The specific capacitance of oxygen vacancy-rich Co3O4 is higher than that of the pristine-Co3O4 nanosheet (3.6 times) and H200–Co3O4 nanosheet (2.6 times) electrode. Later on, Qin et al. prepared mesoporous MnO2 nanosheets by a supramolecular bottom-up self-assembly strategy (shown in Fig. 6(g)) to fabricate microsupercapacitors.56Fig. 6(h) shows the AFM image of MnO2 nanosheets, which reveals that the thickness of MnO2 nanosheets is about 10 nm. Further, to evaluate the electrochemical performance, an asymmetric microsupercapacitor was fabricated using MnO2 nanosheets as the positive electrode, and porous VN nanosheets as the negative electrode in a “water-in salt” gel electrolyte. The fabricated asymmetric microsupercapacitor worked in the potential window of 2.0 V with an energy density of 21.6 mW h cm−3.

2.3.3 Specific surface area. Specific surface area is crucial for metal oxides to have better electrode/electrolyte interaction, which is ideal for SC applications. The electrochemical performance of metal oxides depends on the redox reaction or faradic reaction, which mostly occurs on the surface of the electrode material. Therefore, an electrode with a high specific surface area can lead to enhanced electrochemical performance (in terms of both specific capacitance and energy density) due to the availability of more electroactive sites, facilitating multiple redox reactions.57
2.3.4 Electronic conductivity. Conductivity is another vital factor for metal oxide-based materials to be effectively utilized in SCs. As we know, most of the metal oxide compounds (including 0D, 1D, and 2D metal oxides) exhibit poor electronic conductivity, which restricts the ion and electron transfer in the electrochemical charge–discharge process. At high charge–discharge rates, the resistance towards the electronic mobility will be significantly high in the case of an electroactive material (metal oxides), which decreases the specific capacitance, energy density, power density, and rate capability.28 For instance, the experimental value of specific capacitance in the case of manganese oxide is about 350 F g−1, which is very low when compared to the theoretical value (1370 F g−1), which is due to the poor electronic conductivity (∼10−5 to 10−6 S cm−1).58
2.3.5 Mass loading. In view of the practical application of SCs, both high specific capacitance and large mass loading of electroactive materials are desirable to exhibit high energy and power density. However, high mass loading of electroactive materials (metal oxides) results in decreased electrical conductivity and restricted access to the electrolyte ions, leading to poor electrochemical performance. Also, the mass loading factor is inversely proportional to the value of specific capacitance, which means that an increase in the mass loading value would decrease the specific capacitance.59,60 Yang et al. already reported that the specific capacitance of 2D MnO2 nanosheets decreased from 203 to 155 F g−1, when the mass loading increased from 6 to 18 mg cm−2 on the Ni foam substrate.61 Hence, it is challenging for metal oxides to achieve both high specific capacitance and large mass loading in practice.

3. Improvement in the energy storage performance of metal oxides through hybrid structure fabrication

Although metal oxides possess intriguing electrochemical performances based on the faradic redox reaction, they may not be implemented in their pristine form as the SC electrodes due to the following limitations: (i) the electrical conductivity of most of the metal oxides is very poor, which leads to an increase in the sheet resistance and the charge transfer resistance of the electrode, and causes a large IR drop at high current densities in the charge–discharge cycles. This results in a decay in the rate capability and power density of SC devices; (ii) during the cycling of metal oxide based SCs, strain is developed in the metal oxide surfaces, creating structural degradation of the electrode material, leading to poor cycle life, and (iii) it is cumbersome to tailor few properties of metal oxides such as to increase the specific surface area and the pore size distribution. These are the major drawbacks of metal oxide-based SCs.35 To overcome these demerits of metal oxide-based SCs and enhance their electrochemical performance, developing composites and/or hybrid electrode materials is essential by combining metal oxides with carbonaceous materials or conductive polymers.

3.1 Metal oxide/graphene hybrids

Generally, graphene hybrids of metal oxides have great potential in SC applications due to the following reasons: (i) the metal oxide/graphene hybrid structure can result in high conductivity due to the injection of electrons from the graphene layer into the metal oxides, increasing the hole concentration in graphene;62 (ii) metal oxide/graphene hybrids have a high surface area which enhances the interaction between the electroactive material and the electrolyte, resulting in improved electrochemical performance;63 (iii) in metal oxide/graphene hybrids, metal oxide nanosheets are often sandwiched between graphene nanosheets to avoid the restacking of graphene layers. Zhang et al. prepared a 2D MnO2/graphene hybrid through the electrostatic coprecipitation method.64 The morphological analysis (Fig. 7(a and b)) of the 2D MnO2/graphene hybrid shows that the MnO2 nanosheets are dispersed on the surface of the graphene substrate due to the electrostatic interaction. This 2D MnO2/graphene hybrid exhibits better electrochemical performance as compared to the pristine MnO2 and graphene due to the simultaneous contribution of the excellent electrical conduction in the graphene and the pseudocapacitive properties of the MnO2 nanosheets. Fig. 7(c) presents the CV profile of the 2D MnO2/graphene hybrid electrode and it achieved a specific capacitance of about 188 F g−1 at a mass normalized current of 0.25 A g−1.

3.6 Synergic effects in the hybrid structure

The synergistic effects in hybrid structures (metal oxide combined with graphene, or CNT, or AC or conducting polymers) can be briefly highlighted as follows: (i) graphene can play a vital role in metal oxide/graphene hybrid structures, as graphene provides a 2D conductive template to promote an interconnected conductive porous network that would enhance the electrical conductivity and charge transport pathways of metal oxides; (ii) graphene establishes a good interfacial interaction and electrical contact between itself and the metal oxides due to the availability of oxygen-containing functional groups; (iii) metal oxides when combined with AC, and/or CNT, tend to enhance the specific surface area of the hybrid electroactive material, which will facilitate the surface reactions, and the electrolyte can have a good interaction with the electrode (due to its large surface area), resulting in enhanced electrochemical performances in terms of specific capacitance, energy density and power density; (iv) in order to improve the electrical conductivity of bare metal oxides, they are usually combined with several number of conducting polymers to enhance the electrical conductivity of the metal oxide/polymer hybrid as well as to improve the various properties such as flexibility, specific capacitance, and ease of fabrication for practical applications (Table 1).

4. Influence of different factors (oxygen vacancies, additional active sites, doping, conductivity, and mixed metal valence states) on capacitance performance

In general, metal oxide based pseudocapacitors exhibit a higher specific capacitance, but its poor conductivity leads to the gradual decrease of capacitance. These were hybridized with carbonaceous materials such as graphene, carbon nanotubes, and porous carbon to improve the storage behaviour in metal oxides. Currently, creating surface oxygen vacancies in the metal oxide materials is an effective way to enhance the specific capacitance, revealed from the recent theoretical and experimental reports. The introduction of oxygen vacancies in metal oxides has the following three advantages: (i) an optimal content of oxygen vacancies enhances the diffusion of the charge carriers, (ii) surface oxygen vacancies afford enhanced adsorption of hydroxyl ions (OH−) and (iii) they act as active sites for redox reactions and accelerate the surface reaction kinetics.81 In addition, other factors such as additional active sites, doping, conductivity, and mixed oxidation effects on the charge storage of metal oxides are also discussed in this section in detail.

4.1 Oxygen vacancies

Co3O4 is a promising candidate for electrochemical storage among the various transition metal oxides due to its high theoretical capacitance (3560 F g−1), low cost, environmental friendliness, and high abundance in nature.82 Its multiple valence states are beneficial for the redox behavior. This high surface area of the MoO3 electrode enables gathering a large amount of electrolyte ions, which is accountable for the enhanced storage activity.92 Zhu et al. have synthesized benzoic acid-assisted 2D porous Co3O4 thin sheets through the hydrothermal method at 200 °C with different time durations of 6, 12 and 24 h. A high specific capacity was achieved for 12 h duration (598.5C g−1 at 1.0 A g−1) compared to 6 h (497C g−1) and 24 h (399C g−1). The estimated specific surface area evidences the enhancement of the capacitance value (69.96 (6 h), 142.15 (12 h) and 42.07 (24 h) m2 g−1) (Fig. 18(a)). The few sheets are stacked on the surface of thin sheets at lower reaction times, decreasing the surface area, but for longer reaction times, a greater number of sheets are formed over the surface, which also decreases the surface area. So the prepared Co3O4 thin sheets exhibit a higher surface area with a higher number of electroactive sites, which enhances the electron transportation efficiently, improving charge storage performances.

4.3 Choice of dopants

Transition metal doping is a facile and cost-effective process to enhance the supercapacitor performance of the metal oxides. The transition metal dopants induce a greater number of active sites, and large surface area in the host matrix; in addition, they produce efficient diffusion pathways for both electron transfer and ion diffusion. Moreover, doping generates oxygen vacancies in the metal oxides which increases the electrical conductivity.95 Asaithambi et al. have studied the electrochemical behaviour of different transition metal (Fe, Cu and Zn) doped SnO2 nanoparticles. Fe doped SnO2 nanoparticles exhibited a maximum specific capacitance of 270 F g−1 at a current density of 0.5 A g−1 compared with Cu–SnO2 (251 F g−1) and Zn–SnO2 (251 F g−1) electrodes which is attributed to the higher electrical conductivity for the Fe-doped SnO2. Impedance study reveals that the Fe doped electrode has a lower solution resistance (0.21 Ω) than the Cu (0.32 Ω) and Zn (0.38 Ω) doped SnO2 lattice which supports the trend of supercapacitor performances.96 The pristine and transition metal (Cr, Co, Ni and Cu) doped Mn3O4 nanocrystals were synthesized by a hydrothermal method according to Dong et al. The estimated specific capacitance for Cr, Co, Ni and Cu doped Mn3O4 electrodes is 272, 209, 184 and 134 F g−1, respectively at a current density of 0.5 A g−1. Such an enhanced capacitance for the Cr doped Mn3O4 electrode (6.079 Ω) is due to the lower charge transfer resistance (Rct) between the electrode and electrolyte than those of pristine (7.84 Ω) and other doped (Co = 7.038 Ω, Ni = 7.792 Ω, and Cu = 8.153 Ω) Mn3O4 electrodes which is revealed from electrochemical impedance spectroscopy.97

4.4 Metal doping

It is well known that metal oxides store the charge through the faradaic redox reactions. To improve the redox reactions in the metal oxide-based supercapacitor electrodes, doping is an effective way because it increases the conductivity of metal oxide semiconductors, leading to the fast charge transportation which results in effective reversible redox behavior for charge storage. We have discussed here some of the reported works of doped 2D metal oxides for supercapacitor applications. Xu et al. have reported the supercapacitor performance of Co doped NiO nanosheets composited with carbon welded carbon nanotube foam (WCNTF). The electrochemical behavior of the NiO, Co-doped NiO and Co-doped NiO@WCNTF was tested for 2 M KOH electrolyte with the potential range of 0–0.6 V. CV curves display that all the electrodes show a redox peak at 0.45 and 0.35 V, which is due to the faradaic redox reaction between Ni(II) and Ni(III) and the corresponding chemical relation is written as NiO + OH− ↔ NiOOH + e−. The integrated area of the CV curve for Co-doped NiO is greater than that of undoped NiO, which demonstrates that Co doping increases the electrochemical activity of pure NiO, which results in enhanced specific capacitance of Co-doped NiO. After hybridization with the WCNTF, the integral area of the CV curve for Co-doped NiO@WCNTF gets increased. This enhancement is due to the porous Co-doped NiO@WCNTF surface with interconnected open channels offering more effective active sites for faradaic redox reaction.98 Li et al. have studied the behavior of metal doped (Mg, Fe and Ni) δ-MnO2 nanosheets with aqueous electrolyte solutions such as MgSO4 and Na2SO4. The MnO2 structures store the charges via the transition of Mn3+ and Mn4+ oxidation states of Mn. The chemical equation for the charge storage is given by MnO2 + C + e− ↔ MnOOC, where C indicates the cations of the electrolyte. Mg embedded δ-MnO2 nanosheets exhibited a maximum capacitance of 301.8 F g−1 at a current density of 0.2 A g−1 for the MgSO4 electrolyte, about 239.6 and 209.7 F g−1 over the Fe and Ni doped MnO2, respectively. From the characterization results, half of the Mg2+ ions were accommodated in the interlayer by the replacement of K+ ions. The remaining ions were in the MnO2 octahedron, which improves the system's conductivity and enhances the redox reaction. But in Fe and Ni doped δ-MnO2 assembly, the Fe2+ and Ni2+ fully replace the K+ ions.99 An outstanding specific capacitance of 1392 F g−1 at 2 A g−1 was obtained for Mo doped CuO nanosheets by Lv et al. The oxidation and reduction reaction between the Cu2+ and Cu+ ions is responsible for the charge storage performance and the chemical reaction is represented as 2CuO + H2O + 2e− ↔ Cu2O + 2OH−. The conductivity of the CuO system increases significantly because the Mo6+ ions substituted the Cu2+ ions and each Mo atom contributes four free electrons to the CuO lattice. Thus, the capacitance enhancement is observed in the doped system.100 The other doped systems such as Ni doped MnO2 nano arrays,101 Al doped ZnO102 and Cu–Fe codoped cobalt oxide103 show an enhanced capacitance value compared to their pristine samples which is because the metal doping increases the conductivity of the host matrix.

Apart from the doping concept, hybridization with carbon allotropes can also increase the host system's conductivity by enhancing the electron transportation. Li et al. have prepared MnO2 nanosheet/SWCNT hybrid fibers using a simple wet-spinning process. Such nanosheets were anchored along the axial direction on the SWCNT fiber in a uniform way; SWCNTs serve as a conducting substrate to improve the conductivity of the MnO2 and prevent the aggregation of the nanosheets. The SWCNT electrode showed a volumetric capacitance of 39 F cm−3. After the SWCNT was composited with the MnO2 the specific capacitance substantively increased. The optimized mass load (67%) of MnO2 with SWCNTs showed a volumetric capacitance of 428 F cm−3.104 It was reported that NiO nanosheets were directly grown on the surface of CNTs by chemical bath deposition followed by thermal annealing. Bare NiO exhibits a capacitance of 288 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and modest rate capability (72 F g−1 at 20 A g−1) due to the poor conductivity. After NiO deposition over the CNT surface, the specific capacitance reached 996 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and the rate capability was maintained even at 20 A g−1 which infers that the highly conductive CNT core provides a rapid path for electron transport.105 Shi et al. have synthesized a MnO2/CNTs nanocomposite by the electrodeposition method. The optimized deposition time of 800 s for MnO2 loading over the CNT surface shows a maximum capacitance of 531 F g−1 at a current density of 1 A g−1. Such a superior performance of the composite was due to the unique aligned structure of CNTs enhancing the charge transfer path of electrons to the MnO2 and the high in-plane conductivity of the CNT.106 The integration of 2D Co3O4 nanoflakes with graphene on Ni foam was carried out by Tan et al. through chemical vapor deposition followed by a facile hydrothermal method. Among these, the Co3O4 NF13-G electrode exhibited a maximum specific capacitance of 1.75, 1.38, 1.07, 0.82 and 0.69 F cm−2 at the current densities of 1, 2, 5, 8 and 10 mA cm−2, respectively. From the electrochemical impedance plot, the Co3O4 NF13-G electrode has a lower solution resistance (∼0.6 Ω) and charge transfer resistance (∼0.1 Ω) which informs that the binder-free nanoarray of Co3O4 with graphene sheets possess excellent conductivity.107 The Zhao group has synthesized bulk Mn–MOF (metal–organic frameworks), MnO–CNS (carbon nanosheets) and MnO2–CNS and their formation pathway is illustrated in Fig. 19(a). 4.6 The contribution of mixed metal oxides and their valence states

Different metal oxidation states can result in many oxygen vacancies on the surface, accelerating the electrochemical redox process. Oxygen vacancies cause a bigger interlayer gap in metal oxides, which promotes faster charge storage kinetics with intercalation pseudocapacitive behaviour. A mixed valance state facilitates ionic and electronic defect development while also improving the electrode material's intrinsic conductivity for supercapacitor applications. Mixing of one or two metal compounds with the single crystal can facilitate achieving the multiple oxidation states of the material. In a single crystal structure, the presence of two different cations may produce more electrons than single metal oxides, enhancing electrical conductivity. The creation of an intermediate energy level, which leads to fast electron transport, is primarily responsible for the improved conductivity caused by the inclusion of various oxidation state metals. The spinel structured material, for example, NiCo2O4 has an electrical conductivity that is two or three orders of magnitude higher than that of single metal oxide NiO or Co3O4 materials.109

According to Fang et al. tuning the Mn2+/Mn3+ valance state ratio in manganese oxide can promote supercapacitor performance.110 Ling et al. have fabricated the ZnxCo1−xO electrode via atomic-level structure engineering for higher supercapacitive performance. The similar atomic radii of Zn2+ and Co2+ can facilitate the replacement of Co2+ with the Zn2+ ion. Abundant oxygen vacancies can be achieved by uniform Zn doping on the (1 1 1) facet of ZnxCo1−xO which leads to excellent capacitive performance. However, in ZnxCo1−xO NRs, quick oxygen-ion intercalation is insufficient to offer huge intercalation capacity that may be accomplished by increasing the electronic conductivity at the same time (Fig. 20(a)). The estimated density of states (DOS) in Fig. 20(b) shows that the virgin CoO has a broad bandgap of 2.4 eV, implying intrinsically low electronic conductivity. New electronic states emerge in the bandgap of Zn-doped CoO (ZnxCo1−xO) and the increment of DOS shows the enhancement of carrier density in ZnxCo1−xO. When compared to CoO NRs, Zn-doped CoO NRs have a 10-fold increase in charge carriers. This increase in electronic conductivity instantly leads to a large increase in capacitive charge storage in Zn-doped CoO NRs when compared to CoO NRs. In the atomically constructed ZnxCo1−xO NRs, fast ion and electron transports are produced simultaneously and synergistically linked, contributing to their excellent performance. As a result, charge transport promotion in this oxide, with a capacity exceeding 2000 F g−1 without sacrificing power capability, is achieved. To accomplish this ideal oxide electrode, multi-valance state doping in the oxide can be used in energy storage applications.111

5. Influence of electrolytes on specific capacitance

The main disadvantage of supercapacitors is their low energy density. The energy density of the electrode materials depends on the cell voltage and capacitance. The potential window of electrolytes determines the working cell voltage in supercapacitors. Hence, the electrolytes, in addition to electrodes, play an important role in supercapacitor performance and are classified into aqueous (1–1.3 V), organic (2.5–2.7 V) and ionic liquid (3.5–4 V) electrolytes.117

5.1 Choice of electrolytes

In general, electrolytes have their own set of merits and demerits. Aqueous electrolytes can achieve high ionic conductivity and capacitance; however, the working potential is limited by a narrow decomposition voltage. Even though organic and ionic liquid electrolytes hold wide operating cell voltage, the major disadvantages are high costs, poor conductivity (large internal resistance) and flammability. Compared with organic and ionic liquid electrolytes, aqueous electrolytes are environmentally safe, inexpensive, have facile preparation methods, and are better for supercapacitors since they have strong ionic conductivity. Acidic, basic, and neutral electrolytes are the three types of aqueous electrolytes.118 Among them, H2SO4, KOH, and Na2SO4 are the most often utilized electrolytes.

5.2 Ionic size

Ionic size is one of the electrolyte parameters which influences the energy storage performance of the supercapacitor. To get a high capacitance and high-power density, the electrolyte ion size must be equivalent to or smaller than the pore size of the electrode material. Commonly, the solvation of alkali metal ions in water is very strong and their hydration sphere radius order is as follows: Li+ > Na+ > K+. K+ solvated ions have a lower hydration radius sphere of 3.31 Å compared to Na+ (3.58 Å) and Li+ (3.82 Å) ions. The interaction of K+ ions with the electrode material can exhibit enhanced electrochemical performance due to the lower hydrated radius.119 Naeem et al. have investigated the electrochemical behavior of ZnO nanomembranes (NMs). Using the atomic layer deposition (ALD) process, ZnO NMs of varied thicknesses (50, 100, and 200 ALD cycles) were coated on a commercially purchased polyurethane sponge matrix. The electrode was made by uniformly spreading ZnO NM slurry over cleaned Ni foam and the electrochemical characterization was performed through a three electrode system (Fig. 24(a)). The 100-ALD ZnO NM electrode showed a larger specific capacitance of 846–92 F g−1 in 6 M KOH, 465–8 F g−1 in 1 M KCl, and 65–7 F g−1 in 6 M Na2SO4 electrolytes, for a current density of 1 to 5 A g−1 (Fig. 24(b–d)). From the obtained results, the electrochemical behavior of the electrodes in different electrolytes shows that the K+ and OH− ions are excellent exchangers and the greatest performance is found in the KOH electrolyte.120

5.3 Dissociation rate

The different components involved in the electrolyte conductivity are salt concentration, dissociation degree of charge carriers (cations and anions), mobility of ions, and temperature. In general, the dissociation degree for aqueous electrolytes and organic electrolytes is nearly one and less than one, respectively. Hence, the salt has lower solubility in organic solvents, leading to poor ionic conductivity of the organic electrolytes. Moreover, the organic electrolytes have equivalent series resistance (ESR) when compared to aqueous electrolytes. The structure of organic salts such as symmetric or asymmetric, and ionic size (cation and anion) also affect the ion mobility and dissociation degree which regulate the conductivity of the electrolytes.125 Abbasi et al. fabricated a Ni6MnO8 nanosheet based electrode through a hydrothermal method and investigated the electrochemical behavior for aqueous electrolytes (LiOH, NaOH and KOH). The recorded GCD curves for the three electrode systems are displayed in Fig. 26(a). 5.4 Potential window

The potential window determines the energy density of supercapacitors. As a result, the researchers are concentrating on enlarging the potential window to boost the energy density. Because of their unique properties, aqueous electrolytes are commonly utilized in supercapacitors. Theoretically, the estimated voltage window for aqueous electrolytes is 1.23 V which is lower than the experimental potential window (1 V).127 To increase the potential window of the electrolytes, various techniques such as pH adjustment, concentration and dual shuttle ion electrolytes have been applied. These techniques have some complex processes so the practical implementation is very difficult. The researchers recently followed the idea of extending the potential window to increase the energy density through asymmetric device formation to combine the two different potential windows of the two different electrodes. Zhang et al. have fabricated a solid-state flexible asymmetric supercapacitor composed of a CoMn2O4/3D rGO hybrid and 3D rGO film, in which the CoMn2O4/3D rGO hybrid and 3D rGO film serve as the positive and negative electrodes (Fig. 27(a)). This hybrid electrode worked at an extended potential window of 1.8 V and exhibited an energy density of 46.35 W h kg−1 and 89.7% capacitance retention beyond 5000 cycles with excellent structural flexibility (Fig. 27(b)). At the hybrid interface the covalent state of Mn atoms was increased which enhanced the OH− adsorption, resulting in enhanced capacitance. A built-in electric field was formed at the hybrid interface because of the space charge redistribution and the low concentration of free carriers in the semiconductor. It is mentioned that the built-in electric field in the CoMn2O4/3D rGO heterostructure significantly reduces the effect of the applied electric field which enhances the operational voltage window (−0.3 to 0.6 V) of the hybrid system (Fig. 27(c)).128

The ionic conductivity of the electrolyte has a significant influence on supercapacitors' internal resistance. The aqueous electrolytes such as H2SO4 (acid) and KOH (alkaline) have a higher conductivity (up to ∼1 S cm−1) compared to acetonitrile or propylene carbonate (organic electrolytes), providing the device with high power performance. On the other hand, the operating voltage is relatively lower (∼1 V) due to the low water electrochemical stability window (1.23 V); hence, the energy storage in the device is limited. The electrochemical behavior of MnOx/Ni foam was investigated for Na2SO4 and KOH electrolytes according to Aldama et al. The evaluated specific capacity and specific capacitance are larger for KOH (K+) over the Na2SO4 electrolyte (Na+) which is due to the higher ionic conductivity of the alkaline electrolyte (215 mS cm−1) compared to the neutral one (86 mS cm−1).132

6.2 Pore size distribution

Even though the specific surface area of the electrode materials plays a major role in the specific capacitance, the specific capacitance is not directly proportional to the specific area. Pore containing surface area determines the electrode materials' specific capacitance, called active surface area. The electrochemically active surface area depends on the pore size of the electrode material. The pore structures with a diameter greater than 50 nm are macropores, a size between 2 and 50 nm are mesopores and less than 2 nm are micropores, whereas the pores sizes of PC components were categorized into nanopores (<100 nm), supermicropores (0.7–2 nm), and ultra-micropores (<0.7 nm). Therefore, suitable pore size distribution (PSD) determines the specific energy density and power density in supercapacitors. Gogotsi et al. have discovered the pore size impact on the specific capacitance of double-layer carbon electrodes with a pore sizes less than 1 nm which is derived from carbide.141 Huang et al. reported that the (O) C–O–Mn bonded Mn3O4 nanoflake/reduced graphene oxide composite with a moderate pore size showed an elevated specific capacitance of 351 F g−1 at 0.5 A g−1 and excellent cycle life of 80.1% capacitance retention even after 10000 cycles. Mn3O4 has a pore size of 5–10 nm, which is achieved through phase transition and lattice reformation by increasing the annealing temperature.142 According to Wang et al. solvothermally prepared MgCo2O4 twinned-hemispheres have a specific surface area of 63.8 m2 g−1 and the pore size and the mean pore size are 6 nm and 13.5 nm, respectively. The fabricated electrode displayed a higher specific capacitance of 626.5 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and has an excellent capacitance retention of 71.8% at 16 A g−1. 6.3 Electrolyte diffusion

Fast diffusion of electrolyte ions into the electrode surface is a vital process for enhancing the energy density of the supercapacitors. The decrement of electron transportation path length greatly improves the diffusion of electrolyte ions. The 2D core–shell structured nanomaterials favor the rapid diffusion of electrolyte ions to conduct the electrochemical reactions. Hydrothermally prepared 1D MnO2 nanotubes were mixed with Co and Zn precursors and 2-methylimidazole to grow Co–Zn-ZIF polyhedrons over the MnO2 nanotubes which created the core–shell MnO2@CoZnZIF hybrid. The agglomeration of the core–shell hybrid is prevented by the NiCoZn–OH nanosheets which provides the additional active sites in the hybrid system. And the high dispersibility nature of the as prepared material (MnO2 nanotubes) shortens the ion diffusion path. Therefore, the synthesized MnO2@NiCoZn–OH and NiCoZn–OH have the surface area of 149.56 and 131.85 m2 g−1 with microporous and mesoporous structures, demonstrating the improved electrochemical performance. 6.4 Influence of morphology on charge transfer

In general, hierarchical nanostructures of metal oxides offer tunnels for smooth and rapid charge transfer as well as extended cycle stability. In addition to that they prevent the volume expansion during the electrochemical process. The preparation method mainly defines the morphology of the nanomaterials.153 Shinde et al. have prepared the CuO by a chemical bath deposition method, having a nanosheet like morphology, which converted to nanobuds after ionic liquid treatment. The nanobud surface facilitates the rapid charge transportation of ions between the electrode and electrolyte due to the existence of abundant conductive sites.154 Luan et al. reported that the higher surface area of CuO-NWs receives charges from alkaline ions or protons in the electrolyte. The charge/discharge process through pseudocapacitive redox reactions between Cu+ and Cu2+ oxidation states is as follows:
 
Cu2+ + e− ↔ Cu+ or 2CuO + H2O +2e− ↔ Cu2O + 2OH−(3)

Thus, the redox behavior of CuO-NWs results in the improved capacitance of supercapacitors.155 MnO2 is one of the transition metal oxides widely investigated for energy storage application due to its larger theoretical capacitance (1370 F g−1), eco-friendly nature and cost effectiveness.155 According to crystal structures, MnO2 was categorized into 1D-tunnel structure (α-MnO2, β-MnO2, and γ-MnO2), 2D-layer structure (δ-MnO2), and 3D-mesh structure (λ-MnO2). 2D-δ-MnO2 has been formed by coupling of [MnO6] octahedrons with common edges in which the interlayers are separated at 0.7 nm. MnO2 crystal structures and morphologies were controlled by varying the pH (1–12) of KMnO4 through the hydrothermal method. At high temperature KMnO4 was easily dissociated into MnO2, H2O and O2. 7 Density functional theory

Density functional theory (DFT) is an extensively used method for “ab initio” calculations of the bulk crystal structures and their surfaces as well as the interactions between them. In this DFT development, instead of solving the Schrödinger equation by the exact wave functions of electrons, electrons are treated implicitly, and the wave function is substituted by electronic density. The equation can be simplified into a functional form by lowering the electronic density energy (function of a function). DFT depends on the approximation that electrons are not interacting. A term uses the interactions of electrons to correct the result, named exchange–correlation energy (Ex) and is divided into two terms, namely exchange energy and correlation energy. The exchange energy represents electron–electron repulsion minus Hartree energy. The correlation energy is difficult to solve. So approximations are employed for the exchange–correlation function, such as local density approximation (LDA) and generalized gradient approximation (GGA). In GGA, the functional is denoted by both local electron density and gradient in the electron density. Compared to LDA, more physical terms are used in GGA. The Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) approximation is used to improve the accuracy of computational calculations. The accuracy of PBE depends on the materials property and electronic structure. In the case of strongly correlated systems (solid state materials), additional functionals can be used such as hybrid functionals or Hubbard correction. Furthermore, some of the functionals are in the hybrid form. It combines the exact exchange estimated from the Hartree–Fock with either GGA and/or LDA exchange correlation such as the Becke three parameter Lee–Yang–Parr (B3LYP) functional.186,187

8. Final design strategies for enhanced energy storage performance

Supercapacitors are widely used in various applications including their promising use in hybrid electric vehicles (HSD) (cars and trains), electronic devices (smartphones, volatile memory backups, and uninterrupted power supplies (UPS)) and energy harvesting systems like wind turbine systems where they are used in the form of supplementary with the batteries. Similarly, in hybrid electric vehicles, the supercapacitors are used to cope with the energy for acceleration during braking, preserve energy, and protect the batteries at high frequencies during swift charging and discharging processes, i.e., during dynamic operation. The electrochemical performance of the supercapacitors is estimated by specific capacitance, energy and power density, rate capability, and cycling stability. These parameters basically depend on the electroactive material and electrolyte used in supercapacitors. To enhance the electrochemical energy storage performance of a supercapacitor device, the electroactive material should have the following properties: (i) large specific surface area, (ii) good electrical conductivity, and (iii) robust electrochemical stability. The electrolyte has played a key role in enhancing the performance of supercapacitors. The crucial properties considered in the electrolytes are (i) high temperature coefficient, (ii) higher potential window, (iii) good electrochemical stability, (iv) greater ionic concentration, (v) lower solvation ion radius, (vi) less viscosity, (vii) less volatility, (viii) less toxicity, and (ix) cost-effectiveness. In the present review article, we have discussed the effect of oxygen vacancies, doping, tailoring of morphology, surface area, electrolyte and conductivity on enhancing the supercapacitor performance. Different forms of metal oxides like binary and ternary forms with transition metal doping and hybrid combination as well as the role of pristine materials in the energy storage performances are discussed. In the present scenario, the supercapacitors are mainly suffering from energy density and longer cycle life. In order to overcome this drawback, the research will be continued to find out the suitable hybrid combination with outstanding prolonged charge–discharge cycles. The hybrid supercapacitor devices aim to achieve comparable performance to batteries in energy density and preserve the high-power density and good cycle life of supercapacitors, which can be possible by hybridization at either the device or materials level. Different nanostructures with large surface areas and refining pore size distribution benefit the enhancement of specific capacitances but face lot of large-scale production challenges. They have failed to maintain the capacitance retention beyond certain limits.

Furthermore, the electrolytes limit the potential window of the supercapacitor device. With the utilization of organic electrolytes, ionic liquid electrolytes and redox-active electrolytes, the potential window of supercapacitors can be increased up to 3–4 V by enhancing the device energy density. The researchers focus on the fabrication of asymmetric devices by taking advantage of two different electrode materials (positive and negative electrodes) to cover the wide range of operational cell-voltage (beyond the thermodynamic decomposition voltage of electrolytes) to increase the device energy density. Recently most reported works are on the hybrid combination device to achieve the wide potential range instead of playing with the electrolytes. Making the ternary and quaternary hybrids using the metal oxides, carbon allotropes and polymers will provide a wide range of potential windows. The drawback of each one is overcome by others. We can vary the different parameters such as pore size, dopant, oxygen vacancy and electrolyte on these hybrids and end up with high energy density with long cycle life electrode materials for efficient energy storage devices. Instead of spending more time on the fabrication of electrodes with different combinations and performing electrochemical experiments, first, we can study the electronic structure for the different hybrid combination and estimate the energy barrier potential for the different electrolytes with the different hybrids though DFT which will save our time and make our works easier. The ever-growing needs for flexible electronics in the applications of consumer electronics and implantable/wearable healthcare devices require concerted mechanical flexibility in supercapacitor devices. Substantial improvement should be needed to fabricate bendable, stretchable, shape-memory, transparent and self-healing supercapacitor devices. In view of the practical application of supercapacitor devices, integration with multifunctionality will attract more attention for future supercapacitor designs such as electrochromic supercapacitors, alternating current (AC) line-filtering supercapacitors, self-healing supercapacitors, shape memory supercapacitors and self-charging supercapacitors.

9. Conclusion and future directions

Among the various energy storage devices (traditional capacitors, fuel cells and batteries) supercapacitors attracted much attention in the research community because of their special features such as rapid charge–discharge, high energy density and excellent cycle life compared to conventional batteries. They are widely used in electronic devices such as mobile phones and electric vehicles. The present review deals with the metal oxide nanostructures and their enhanced supercapacitor performances due to the induced oxygen vacancy, doping effect, and hybridization with the carbon allotropes and role of electrolytes in the host matrix of binary and ternary metal oxides. In addition, we have discussed the impact of surface area and pore size distribution on the energy storage activity of the electrode materials. DFT works give the pre-idea about a better hybrid combination without performing experiments such as electrode preparation and electrochemical investigations. The selection of electrolytes can also be decided by computing the energy barrier potential between the electrolyte ion and electrode material. Electrolytes have a certain limitation of operational cell voltage; in order to overcome this, we can design asymmetric devices through different hybrid electrodes with wide potential from negative to positive.

It is known that the surface-active sites participate in the electrochemical reactions. Different synthesis strategies are reported for tuning the pore-size and surface area of the metal oxide nanostructures. Based on the optimization, we can achieve a highly porous structure with suitable pore size in the metal oxide nanostructures, leading to shortening of the ion diffusion length. Even though metal oxide nanostructures exhibited excellent specific capacitance with 2D graphene, 1D CNTs, and polymer hybridization, our focus switches on composition with a new type of 2D material MXene (metal carbides/nitrides). Because it has a high intercalation capacitance of 1000 F cm−3 (approximately) in aqueous electrolytes, metal oxide/2D MXene hybrids will be a perfect combination to attain a higher capacitance value. The potential window plays a major role in enhancing the energy density value which will be increased using the suitable eco-friendly electrolytes with good conductivity, like ionic liquid electrolytes. Although significant progress has been made in the field of supercapacitors based on engineered metal oxides and their hybrids, there exist several challenges and future perspectives in this exciting research field. (1) Fabrication of advanced and large-scale supercapacitor devices for practical and sustainable applications, especially in the field of wearable or flexible electronic devices remains a challenge. It is highly desirable to develop green, low temperature, and cost-effective approaches for the synthesis of these materials. Further modification of the material properties to boost the surface area, active sites and wettability of these metal oxides with large scale production is the emerging research area for practical applications of supercapacitors. (2) To enhance the conductivity of the metal oxides, the hybridization with carbonaceous materials and other electroactive materials can have a synergistic effect between the components through improving the specific surface area, creating porosity, easing electron and proton conduction, and providing extra pseudocapacitance. (3) For modern electronics, the design and development of an integrated supercapacitor device capable of energy harvesting, conversion and storage are essential for day-to-day life applications. Due to this reason, self-charging supercapacitors have been found to be emerging candidates for driving various wearable, portable and implantable electronic appliances. Metal oxides have emerged as ideal candidates for self-charging supercapacitors due to their high performance, stability, and tuneable energy storage performance. Further, work needs to be carried out in this direction and there is plenty of room to be explored. (4) The primary goal for supercapacitors is to utilize both research and industry to acquire high power density and high energy density simultaneously by fabricating asymmetric supercapacitor devices. Such a tough job can be accomplished by the smart or flexible design of supercapacitors and the development of electrodes and electrolytes materials. (5) Detailed investigation on mechanisms involved in enhanced energy storage performance of metal oxides by different operando spectroscopic studies with supported theoretical investigations is an emerging research area to unravel different roles of modified metal oxide-based materials. (6) Among the other emerging 2D materials for high-performance supercapacitor applications include MXenes, black phosphorus and transition metal dichalcogenides. Hence, fabricating appropriate hybrid materials of metal oxides with these materials may enhance energy storage capabilities. Further, asymmetric supercapacitors based on these hybrid materials will help achieve high energy storage performance in working voltage windows, energy, power density, etc.

In a nutshell, this review aims to cover recent developments and future perspectives of metal oxides modified by different approaches such as doping, defects, vacancies, and heterojunctions for supercapacitor applications. There still exists scope to improve the energy storage performance of these oxide-based materials for their large-scale production and practical applications.
