Abstract

The progress on technologies for the cleaner and ecological transformation and storage of energy to combat effluence or pollution and the impending energy dilemma has recently attracted interest from energy research groups, particularly in the field of coordination chemistry, among inorganic chemists. Carriers for storing energy or facilitating mass and e− transport are considered significant for energy conversion. Accordingly, considering their properties such as large surface area, low cost, customizable pore diameter, tunable topologies, low densities, and variable frameworks, MOFs (metal–organic frameworks) and their derivatives are well-suited for this purpose. MOFs are an innovative category of porous and crystalline materials, which have gained significant interest in recent years. Thus, herein, we highlight the state of the art progress on MOFs for energy-based applications, as perfect compounds and elements in compound assemblies for converting solar energy, lithium-ion arrays, fuel devices, hydrogen production, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, proton conduction, etc. In addition, the substantial progress achieved in the production of various composites and derivatives containing MOFs with particular focus on supercapacitors and gas adsorption and storage is summarized, concentrating on the correlation between their coordination structural frameworks and applications in the field of energy. The current improved strategies, challenges, and future prospects are also presented in view of the coordination chemistry governing the structural modification of MOFs for energy applications.

The need for energy is growing rapidly around the world, but it must also cope with the significant environmental pollution challenges and depleting resources simultaneously. Thus, to address these essential issues, a large number of research initiatives have been created and carried out. The global demand for energy resources has risen consistently and considerably in recent years, generating major concerns about the long-term viability of fuel supplies and environmental challenges such as climate change, melting glaciers, and rising sea levels. Energy storage and conversion technologies that are renewable, safe, clean, and sustainable, and thus have become a hot research area. There has been a lot of efforts put into reducing the heavy reliance on fossil fuels and resolving environmental challenges. Researchers are working hard to reverse this trend by finding new and more efficient ways to create, transfer, store, and use energy. In this regard, solar cells are considered distinct important initiatives in the 21st century.1–3 Because photosynthesis is such an important factor in solar energy applications, this has motivated scientists to synthesize a range of functional materials that can achieve a similar process (e.g., hybrid materials, molecular dyes, and inorganic semiconductors). Porous materials can efficiently conserve energy sources, resulting in a quick electron and mass transfer for the conversion and various energy storage devices. These materials are designed extensively so that they can be used for many applications based on photochemical and electrochemical properties. The deciding factor in the performance of energy-based materials using MOFs can be expressed with reference to their topology, adjustable pore diameter and large surface area, leading to improved reaction kinetics. Some other properties of MOFs such as hybrid inorganic and organic nature, dynamic multifunctionality, spatial dispersion of materials, etc. make them more suitable for energy applications compared to other standard inorganic porous materials.32,33

Moreover, the well-defined structures of MOFs promote the formation and direct precise rationalization of desired linked framework interactions, which is critical for material optimization. The phenomenal development of MOFs in recent years has drawn widespread attention from a multitude of sectors, fueling the birth of novel applications in the energy sector. 1.1 A theoretical overview of MOFs, their composite materials and derivatives

MOFs, as porous coordination polymers, have been around for more than 20 years, and research is still ongoing to expand their number and applications. To form this novel type of crystalline material, metal complexes as inorganic secondary building units (SBUs) are allowed to react with different organic linkers via coordination interactions. These materials can be simply modified by varying the species, shape, size, and components functioning in the pre-design or post-synthetic modifications (PSM). Over 20000 MOFs have been designed as a result of the developments in synthesis and PSMs, sensing47 and molecule separation.48 MOFs have a large surface area of ∼10000 m2 g−1, controllable pore diameter of up to 98 Å, and density OF ∼0.13 g cm−1,3 due to which they can be used as adsorbents in various energy sectors such as gas adsorption and purification and to preferentially catalyze organic synthesis. Nano-MOFs, which possess adjustable dimensions and topology, also represent a diverse possibility for pure MOFs. In the current trend, the use of MOFs in various energy sectors, such as batteries, fuel cells, photocatalytic reduction of H2, and supercapacitors has sparked considerable interest in the research communities of materials and chemical science.49 MOFs are considered exceptional porous materials, and thus are well suited for the conversion and storage of energy. These functional materials have also been employed as sacrificial components for developing different nanostructures and support substrates for the integration of metal oxides, functional metals, semiconductors, and complexes.

Inorganic materials such as porous carbons, metal sulphides, metals, metal oxides, and multicomponent hybrids have potential applications in energy and the environment. 2. In situ characterization

The detailed characterization of all the intermediate MOF structures formed during operation is a key challenge in the deeper exploration of the energy-based applications of MOFs to understand the mechanism of their structural transition, host–guest and guest–guest interactions, and energetics of their transitions. Consequently, experimental findings are critical for the future development of reliable predictive modelling techniques. Thus, this suggests the need to measure physical characteristics via techniques such as NMR, X-ray diffraction, neutron diffraction, EPR, optical spectroscopy, and TEM under standardized conditions, such as defined ambient temperature, electric and/or magnetic field, hydrostatic pressure, and theoretical time-resolved conditions, where these measurements are referred to as in situ characterization.

2.1 X-ray and neutron diffraction techniques

Because the majority of MOFs are crystalline solids, it is possible to study their crystallization, pore filling, switching, and mechanical stability using the X-ray and neutron diffraction techniques under controlled conditions, such as a gas atmosphere at specific pressures, temperatures and hydrostatic pressures. Consequently, the development of sophisticated in situ diffraction techniques is critical for understanding the main features of MOFs under circumstances as close to future applications and real-world working conditions as possible. Under an X-ray beam, the necessary experiments are performed and PXRD patterns are measured in the circumstances of interest. The high-quality PXRD patterns can also be employed for characterization and subsequent structural analysis of various MOFs. In 2002, Kitagawa and colleagues published the first paper on parallelized gas adsorption with PXRD. Since MOFs were first presumed for gas storage applications, most in situ neutron diffraction experiments included in situ loading of deuterated versions of fuel relevant gases, such as hydrogen and methane, to better understand the preferred adsorption sites and contribute to development of MOFs with improved storage performances. In situ neutron diffraction provides deep structural observations and is best suited for identifying preferred adsorption sites and clarifying the adsorption mechanism in MOFs.

2.2 NMR

The use of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy for the characterization of MOFs is becoming increasingly more common. This approach, which relies on the detection of NMR-active nuclei such as 1H, 13C, 15N, and 31P allows not only the characterization of the MOF lattice but also the adsorbed species inside the porous structure without the necessity of long-range (crystalline) order. The signal intensity in NMR is equivalent to the number of spins in the detected sample volume, making it a quantitative approach. In contrast to optical spectroscopy, the intensities of distinct signals are comparable. In solid-state NMR spectra, the distance-dependent or spatially anisotropic variables such as dipolar couplings, chemical shift anisotropies, and electric field gradient tensors reveal structural information based on calculated parameters such as chemical shifts and J-couplings. MOFs are regarded as effective materials for gas storage and separation, such as the separation of CO2 from other gases such as methane. Consequently, several in situ13C NMR spectroscopy analyses of CO2 adsorbed on MOFs have been published in recent years.

2.3 Optical spectroscopy

Optical spectroscopy is based on the interaction of electromagnetic radiation with matter. Optical transitions are seen in both absorption and emission. In transmission or reflectance mode, absorption spectra can be evaluated. Over the years, many particular theories or case examples have been devised to allow in situ optical spectroscopy under relevant conditions. In situ molecules were developed as a result of numerous instrument advances, allowing gas adsorption research and reaction measurement on heterogeneous catalysts at varying temperatures and pressures. This can be accomplished either by embedding the sample in a matrix, which is commonly achieved by processing the sample into a KBr pellet or by manually pressing the powder into a self-supported layer without the use of a matrix.

2.4 EPR

Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy has emerged as the primary method for studying paramagnetic substances, which is normally applied in a variety of systems and plays an essential role in materials sciences, chemistry, and biology. Transition metal ions such as Ti3+, V4+, Cu2+, Mn2+, Fe3+, Cr3+, and Co2+, as well as rare-earth ions such as Gd3+ and Eu2+, are main classes of paramagnetic species found in MOF complexes. The developed in situ EPR devices work with a continuous gas flow at temperatures of up to 813 K to confine the reactants and can be linked to a gas chromatograph for product analysis in the experimental setup.

2.5 TEM

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is cutting-edge technology for characterizing materials utilizing a nanometer-focused high-energy electron beam. This technique can be used for the analysis of MOF nanoparticles, their dispersion and crystallinity. For example, Yaghi and colleagues produced significant publications on TEM imaging of the MOF-74 family of compounds. Hexagonal channels were clearly visualized and related diffraction patterns were found in MOF-74-type materials. 3D electron diffraction tomography and high-resolution TEM images were used to determine the crystalline phase of the material, which is about a 200 nm crystallite.

3. Energy-based applications of MOFs in emerging fields

When looking at the distinctive properties of these novel frameworks, it is obvious that their high porosity and lack of covered volumes make them excellent for volume-specific applications such as gas purification, adsorption and separation.

3.1 Gas purification, separation and storage

3.1.1 Purification of gases. The elimination of ppm-traces of sulphur constituents from many gases is one of the fields in which MOFs can be useful. MOF assemblies with available open active sites of metal, in particular, are ideally fit to chemisorb electron-rich, odor-generating compounds such as PH3, –NH2, oxygenates, –OH, H2O, and S-containing particles strongly (430 kJ mol−1). For instance, tetrahydrothiophene (THT, odorant) can be selectively reduced from a natural gas. This was experimentally tested in incessant breakthrough trials. Accordingly, 10 to 15 ppm traces of sulphur were collected down to <1 ppm at room temperature in a fixed-bed reactor via electrochemically produced Cu-EMOF,57 as shown in Fig. 4a. Around 70 g THT per litre MOF (total capacity) was achieved by this material. This value is comparatively higher than that of the commercially offered AC materials such as Norit type RB4 and Carbotech type C38/4.

3.1.2 Separation of gases. In the procedure for the separation of gas mixtures, the mixtures are often made up of components with concentrations in the same range, which is different for the methods of gas purification. For the separation of gas mixtures, techniques such as distillation, pressure, and thermal swing adsorption–desorption methods are usually recommended. The existing methods include the separation of several mixtures such as N2 and O2 (air), N2 and CH4, and noble gases (e.g., krypton and xenon) and some of these processes employ zeolitic adsorbents. Recently, MOF-adsorbents have been used for the separation of Kr–Xe and the purification of CH4 in natural gas by means of pressure swing adsorption.58,59 Even MOFs with small pores that can segregate molecules based on their size or on kinetic diameter have also been described,60–64e.g., while H2O adsorption occurs, somewhat bigger molecules such as nitrogen, oxygen and carbon dioxide, and methane are usually blocked from adsorption. The N2 and H2 sorption isotherms for the small pore size MgMOF (Basolite M050) demonstrating this notion are illustrated in Fig. 4b. It can be seen that there is a preference for hydrogen adsorption over nitrogen adsorption. Because of the increased awareness of global warming in recent years, CO2 separation is of tremendous interest. Accordingly, MOFs have been investigated as CO2 adsorbents.65,66 Currently, gas scrubbing is particularly effective at removing carbon dioxide from low pressure fuel gases such as those generated by fossil fuel-fired power plants. In these streams, the CO2 partial pressure ranges from 60 to 130 mbar. This separation is carried out at room temperature utilizing a reversible stoichiometric reaction (chemisorption) of carbon dioxide with NH3 (amine wash). Carbon dioxide in MOFs is also physisorbed with almost similar temperature conditions. Given that MOF adsorbents, such as MIL53(Cr),67 have a lower sorption energy for the physisorption process, they require a higher pressure for carbon dioxide to grasp higher sorption capacities, as shown in Fig. 4c. Thus, MOFs are considered more effective to separate gases such as carbon dioxide at higher pressure. However, they do not perform as an amine scrubber even under the same temperature and pressure conditions.
3.1.3 Storage of gases. Because of the peculiar framework of MOFs, and in particular their lack of dead volume, it is now feasible to improve the volumetric specific storage of gas beyond the known levels. This type of effect can be rather strong and is dependent on the type of gas, temperature, and pressure, as well as the MOF material employed. Once the basic principles are addressed, the mechanism of a greater loading in cylinders filled with MOFs than empty gas bottles is straightforward. Filling a standard gas cylinder is merely a matter of employing physical properties based on the pVT characteristics of the gas being studied. In the case of gas cylinders filled with MOFs, this is usually reinforced through an internal adsorption effect. Because these MOFs are devoid of dead-volume, no significant difference is reported in storage capacity owing to non-accessible volume-impeding space. In summary, pVT-filling combined with adsorption results in increased volumetric storage capacity. The uptake curves arising after this type of effect are illustrated in Fig. 4d for storing CH4 in gas cylinders packed with various MOFs at ambient temperature over a pressure of 200 bar. When the pressure increases, non-linear uptake behavior may occur. The gas release follows a similar pattern when the pressure decreases and the valves are kept opened. At a pressure of around 150 bar, it is obvious that the capacity increased by around 35% above the advanced filling curve. It is worth noting that the advantage of increased capacity of vessels filled with MOFs can only be obtained if the gas is in a genuine gaseous form rather than a liquid state. Furthermore, the loading capacity of MOFs is restricted by the liquid state density. In terms of graphs, an MOF cannot have compressed gas to a density greater than the density of its liquid phase. Recently, Kitagawa demonstrated unusually large uptakes of even acetylene, clearly beyond the packing density typically attained in the current storage systems.68 Obviously, the most challenging problem is H2 storage. The idea of using H2 as a fuel for mobile or portable fuel devices has sparked significant curiosity in H2 storage options. MOF-based hydrogen storage is totally reversible, does not require expensive thermal treatments, and may be recharged in seconds or minutes. This is definitely a benefit over other storage materials, such as metal hydrides. In MOF-5, inelastic neutron scattering was used to reveal the unique positions of adsorbed hydrogen molecules, whereas density functional theory (DFT) calculations suggested that that each Zn4O-cluster could store 16–20 molecules of H2. Recently, the US DOE (Department of Energy) examined the need for alternate fuel sources and energy carriers, as well as its target values.69 At 9 MPa and 77 K, the storage capacity of ∼7.50 wt% H2 in MOFs (e.g., MOF177) was observed. This problem is also being explored by a number of other academic researchers. Ferey's group reported the preparation of MIL-5370 Al salts and terephthalic acid, which showed a hydrogen capacity of 3.8 wt%. Kim71 and Seki72 used the concept of pillaring MOF with R2NH linkers (triethylenediamine) as an effective tool, leading to H2 curves with ∼2.0 wt% at 0.1 MPa and 87 K. Yaghi's group73 also used the same concept and found that these values are comparable with that of MOF505, where copper paddle-wheels were coupled by 3,3,5,5-biphenyltetracarboxylic acid. At 77 K, 1.9 wt% H2 absorption was achieved by double interpenetrated nets of zinc frameworks when they interacted with an NTB organic-based ligand (4,4,4-nitrilotrisbenzoic acid), as reported by Suh.74 Conversely, there are some materials whose surface area is very high (for example MOF5, MIL-100,44, and MOF-177,7), whereas some isoreticular compounds with an average surface area ∼1000 to 1500 m2 g−1 or small pore diameter are not considered to most promising storage media. It is also not clear whether the most promising metal clusters are divalent or trivalent.75a Nonetheless, the comparison with NaX-zeolite clearly demonstrated that MOFs outperform typical microporous inorganic media.75b,76 Our findings on prototype equipment (at 77 K temperature and ∼40 bar pressure) showed the significant contribution of MOF materials to volume-specific H2 storage77 in distinct manners (Fig. 4e).

For the selected MOFs, MOF177 showed the most promising outcomes. This information collected from a far-reaching pattern matches the literature fairly well. Comparing the storage parameters on a volume-specific rather than weight-specific storage capacity will be significantly more industrially relevant for numerous volume-specific handy fuel devices. MOF powders typically have packing densities of about 0.20 to 0.40 g cm−3, which may increase to 0.50 to 0.80 g cm−3 once they are moulded into drugs or in extrudates. Because of the low density of these materials, the weight limitations for applications are often irrelevant. However, this contradicts the use of metal hydrides as a storage medium. The amount of hydrogen that can be released from the storage medium on a tolerable timescale is the most critical problem here. MOFs possess a completely reversible uptake and release characteristic in this environment. Because the storage method is mostly dependent on the physisorption phenomenon, no significant activation energy barriers are reported during the liberation of stored H2. Within just a few seconds, the modest pressure drop achieved by adjusting the opening of valve is enough to drain H2 from the MOFs. The energy density value of about 1.1 kW h L−1, as recommended in the European Hydrogen and Fuel Cell Strategic Research Agenda and Deployment Plan,78 corresponds to a volumetric H2 storage capacity of around 33 g H2 per litre. This figure may be attained by H2 storage in MOF177 at 77 K and at a modest pressure of around 40–50 bar, as proven. Nevertheless, because of the limited degree of interaction amongst the H2 molecules and due to the low adsorption heat, a considerable storage capacity has not been achieved at room temperature to date. Gaining a better understanding of the optimal hydrogen adsorption sites in MOF structures should primarily aid in the projection of their storage capacities. In this regard, molecular modelling tools can be as significant as complex experimental synthesis efforts. Based on the temperature array of a potential use, extremely porous MOFs are desirable at low temperatures, while small pore materials or very appealing and flexible materials may be preferable at higher temperatures,79 which can be chosen for hydrogen storage at room temperature. Recent studies have explored biomimetic approaches, such as directly linking Co4(μ4-O)-(carboxylate)4 units80a towards the configuration of dynamic centres such as iron in hemoglobin. The sorption enthalpies of these materials are relatively high. For example, for H2, it is about 10 kJ mol−1. Gases such as oxygen, carbon monoxide, and methane also possess relatively high sorption enthalpies.

3.2 MOFs and supercapacitors (SCs)

Ultracapacitors (electrochemical capacitors), also recognized as supercapacitors, are high-power capacitors with a long life cycle, which have attracted significant research attention in the past few years. Due to their great porosity, pure supercapacitors based on metal–organic frameworks possess a low specific areal capacitance due to their weak conductivity. The combination of various MOFs with highly conductive systems such as conductive polymers and graphene has been proved to be a novel approach to increase the performance of SCs by boosting the conductivity of MOFs. Choi et al. appropriately doped graphene into 23 crystalline metal–organic systems to create SCs.81 Many nanocrystalline MOFs with diverse pore sizes have been studied thus far. One of the most successful nanocrystals, i.e., {Zr6O4(OH)4(BPYDC)6}n (where BPYDC is 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylate) exhibited the highest SC capability over 10000 cycles with a given stack and areal capacitance of 0.64 and 5.09 mF cm−2, respectively, thereby showing great material strength. Owing to their high porosity, MOFs also possess enormous electro-chemical double-layer capacitor-type capacitance under similar conditions due to which electrolyte ions can easily diffuse through their micro- and mesopores.82

ZIF67 nanocrystals electrochemically interacted with conductive polyaniline (PANI) to improve their conductivity. Consequently, a high areal capacitance of about 2145.9 mF cm−2 at 9.9 mV s−1 was shown by PANI-77 by supporting electron transportation by directly linking the metal–organic system with external simulation. Thus, organic polymers and metal oxides can also be used for integrating MOFs and pseudocapacitor materials.83

In a recent report studying the behavior of an electric double layer capacitor, an advanced conductive MOF, i.e., “{Ni3(HITP)2}n(HITP = 2,3,6,7,10,11-hexaiminotriphenylene)”,84 was also highlighted, as shown in Fig. 7. When there are no conductive materials or organic linkers available, an MOF-based supercapacitor was developed as a single-material electrode system, resulting in a gravimetric capacitance of roughly 110.9 F g−1 at 0.049 A g−1. These results are comparatively more advanced than that of carbon-based nanotubes. These MOF-based SCs generally show a greater surface area. It has a controlled specific capacitance of about 17.9 mF cm−2, which is generally higher than that of carbon-based nanotubes excluding graphene. An experimental study also suggested that after 10000 cycles, the capacity retention factor is very high.84 Properties such as rapid charge and discharge rates, low molecular weight and bipolar operating flexibility are characteristics of carbon-based SCs, which promote the use of MOF-derived nanoporous carbons in capacitive applications. 3.2.1 MOF nanocrystals in SCs. Various cobalt-containing zinc MOFs have been studied as SC electrodes based on known cobalt oxide supercapacitor electrodes.89 Earlier studies suggest that pristine Co-MOF nanocrystals (size of 50–100 nm) were utilised as the reference electrode based on a three-electrode system via doctor blading on an ITO-covered crystal, attaining a specific capacitance of about 205.9 F g−1 over 1000 cycles with only 1.49% loss.90a Supercapacitive performances have also been reported for zinc- and cadmium-based MOFs with interpenetrated structures and a tiny pore size of about 1.4 nm.91 Yaghi and colleagues developed a variety of SCs employing 23 different metal–organic systems based on nanocrystalline materials (depending on the pore diameters and their forms, ranging around roughly 499.9 nm), thereby showing different topologies.92 On a clear titanium substrate, a solution (based on colloidal properties) of nanocrystalline metal–organic system and graphene (3.29 wt%) was spin-coated. The crystallinity and porosity of the nanocrystalline MOFs are usually maintained by depositing thin layers on both edges of a polypropylene separator membrane, and then soaking them in a carbon-based electrolyte solution. The capacitance performance can be improved by changing parameters such as pore diameter, nanocrystal size, and crystallinity, including mixed metal sites, which are redox active. The stack and areal capacitance of metal–organic systems with significant holes and smaller size nanocrystal materials were found to be high. When tested in 10000 cycles, a ZrMOF (nMOF867) outperformed the commercial activated carbon (0.79 mF cm−2) with an areal capacitance of 4.99 mF cm−2, which is comparatively six-times that of other nanocrystalline MOFs. By using this methodology, Wang et al. prepared flexible SCs based on metal–organic system using two approaches, in which ZIF67 nanocrystals (size of 299.9 nm) were deposited on a carbon-based fabric and then electrochemically placed into the cloth.93 The open metal–organic system pores allowed simple electrolyte diffusion.

Polyaniline allowed electrons to reach the MOF surface, thereby showing a high areal capacitance of 2145.9 mF cm−1 at the rate of 9.9 mV s−1 (Fig. 9a and b). Lan and colleagues achieved an outstanding areal capacitance of about 5146.9 mF cm−1 by coating polyoxometalate-based MOF nanocrystals (∼100 nm), NENU5, with pyrrole rings.94 NENU5 is structurally similar to CuMOF/HKUST1 because of the immobilization of PMo12 in its pores, as illustrated in Fig. 9c.

3.2.4 Pristine 3-D MOFs as positive electrodes. 3.2.5 Derivatives of MOFs such as sulfides, metal oxides and metal hydroxides as positive electrode systems. As previously said, pristine MOFs can be employed as SC electrode systems due to their large surface area, structural stability, and other properties. However, by increasing their conductivity and turning them into M(OH)2 and nanoporous carbon, their electrochemical performance can be significantly improved. Various research projects have been carried out based on this principle, resulting in improved electrochemical performances. Continuous research has been done to develop a variety of symmetric and asymmetric supercapacitors to analyze the performance of electrode materials based on MOFs. Compared to conventional symmetric-based electrode systems, ASCs are more appealing in energy storage systems because of their ease of construction, broad potential window (0–1.6 V), non-flammability, low cost138 and high ionic conductivity. Batteries and capacitors are used as electrode materials for building ASCs. For example, pseudo-capacitive-type electrode materials such as RuO2 and other redox-active nanomaterials139 such as FeOOH140 and Fe3O4,141 and NiCo2S4,142 Co3O4143 and MgCo2O4144 are employed as battery-type electrode materials for preparing ASCs. A novel category of hollow porous nickel phosphate compounds, i.e., (NixPoyOz), developed by Bendi et al. from NiMOF via ligand exchange showed consistent SC electrode properties, thereby exhibiting a specific capacitance of 1627.0 F g−1 at 1.0 A g−1 in 2.0 M potassium hydroxide solution electrolyte and a capacitance retention of 53.650% over 10000 cycles. By using this concept, PO43− ions can be used to improve the conductivity of MOFs, allowing the faradaic process to occur across different surfaces. Truncation of the ion diffusion length, augmentation of ion uptake, and protection from substantial volume change during redox processes are some of the unique qualities of loosely connected surfaces, which increase the electrochemical performances. This group145 constructed a symmetric SC with an energy density of 7.950 W h kg−1 at a power density of 500 W kg−1 and 4.440 W h kg−1 at 10000 W kg−1. Despite having a lower energy density than pristine MOF compounds, this novel category of compounds interacted with other systems through the suitable electrochemical property or utilized to create ASCs to produce efficient supercapacitor devices. Despite the fact that it had a greater capacitance retention than pure Ni-MOF, the fundamental downside of this MOF was its low capacitance retention. The main problems for MOF-derived materials are their stability and capacitance retention. It should be highlighted that after using these techniques, the cyclic stability, capacitance retention and specific surface area of the MOF-derived material increased; nevertheless, its ASC performance was poor. This was related to the mass balance of the positive and negative electrodes during the device fabrication. Charge conservation is related to mass balance because the net charge created on the (–) electrode of the device must equal the net charge created on the (+) electrode. Thus, it can be expressed as the following relation: Q+ = Q−, which implies, m+C+ ΔV+ = m−C− ΔV−, where Q, m, C, and ΔV are the total charge stored on the electrode, active mass, specific capacitance, and electrode working potential, respectively. The (+) and (−) signs indicate positive and negative electrodes, respectively.146 Reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is a carbon-based material with high electrical conductivity and EDLC. Both the metal oxide and the carbon-based rGO will contribute to superior SC performances if rGO is coated over the metal oxide. Consequently, the electrochemical performance of these materials is enhanced. rGO wrapped with MoO3 (rGO/MoO3) was produced by Cao et al. in 2015, which could be used as an all solid-state flexible symmetric SC electrode material.146 For performance improvement, they employed MoMOF-generated MoO3 rather than the commercially available MoO3. This MoMOF was allowed to interact with GO sheets and annealed in an Ar-based atmosphere to form rGO-wrapped MoO2, followed by subsequent calcination in air to obtain the rGO-wrapped MoO3 material. Consequently, the mechanical strength and conductivity of the MOF improved, thereby enhancing its structural integrity, charge transfer property, cycle longevity, capacitance retention, and superior rate capability compared to other systems developed by the interaction of pseudocapacitive materials and carbon.147 A decrease in the ion diffusion length including greater redox active surface area148 owing to nano-morphology is an important contribution for the MoO3 nanomaterial, leading to the better electrochemical performance. When tested at 6 A g−1 for capacitors using the rGO/MoO3 electrode material, the capacitance retention was found to be 87.5% over 6000 cycles. 3.2.6 Three-dimensional pristine MOFs as negative electrodes. Interpenetration is a process that occurs during the self-assembly of MOFs. This interpenetration is closely interlinked with the porosity of metal organic frameworks.154a Generally, the porosity or empty space between the linker templates is reduced when metal ions interpenetrate the 3D organic linkers, increasing the interior surface area of the MOF.155 The thermal stability of MOFs is due to their high surface area. Porous MOFs can be fabricated utilizing a variety of approaches, considering the interpenetration phenomenon. Properties such as wettability, surface polarity, and electrochemical conductivity have all been proven to be improved by nitrogen doping.156 Thus, MOFs can be converted to nanoporous carbon if they are doped with a nitrogen-containing organic-based linker. This is tremendously beneficial for various SC applications. Based on this concept, in 2016, Li et al. developed a hierarchical flower-shaped N-doped carbon system for SC electrodes using a 3-fold interpenetrating diamond-shaped CuMOF based on Cu(II).157 A rod-like linker, 1,4-bis (imidazole-1-yl) benzene (bib), was employed to construct a new CuMOFm {[Cu(bib)2(H2O)2](ClO4)2(H2O)}x∞, which possessed a flower-like topology. 3.3 Lithium-based batteries

Li+ ion batteries (LIBs) have been proven to be commercially more successful than other batteries. This is due to many reasons such as they may be used to power portable electronic devices due to their numerous advantages, including high energy density, long life cycle, low toxicity, and also no retention loss during charge and discharge operations. Lithium ions formed via the oxidation of Li-based anode materials flow from the anode to the cathode during discharge, and then return when charged, which is how LIBs work. However, the energy and power densities of LIBs render them unsuitable for the large-scale storage of electrical energy. Consequently, developing electrode and the electrolyte materials for LIBs is critical to increase their efficiency. MOFs, their composites, and derivatives hold promise in the development of innovative electrode and electrolyte devices such as LiO2 and LiS batteries.

Pristine MOFs can be employed as an alternative option to standard graphite anode materials given that they show a large surface area and persistent pores for storing lithium ions and their movement during charge and discharge operations. However, due to their limited cycling capability, several early investigations demonstrated that pristine MOFs, such as MnLCP (LCP = layered coordination polymer) and MOF177 (Zn based), do not promote the transport of reversible Li+ ions.171 Accordingly, unique MOFs may be developed as LIB anode materials because of their high discharge cycles. An intercalated MOF, discovered in 2014, made up of lithium ions and 2,6-naphthalene dicarboxylate may act as an anode for the intercalation of lithium metal.172 This iMOF exhibits a 2e− transfer, which is reversible for the intercalation of lithium ions at a flat potential of about 0.80 V. Notably, the first MOF-based 4 V single cell with iMOF as the anode and LiNi0.5Mn1.5O4 as the cathode172 was constructed and demonstrated a high cell voltage, with notable features such as outstanding cyclability, high specific power and high specific energy. MOFs may be used as anode materials in LIBs, as evidenced by this. However, developing strong MOFs with regeneration or reversible formation, leading to high-performance-based lithium storage with greater cyclability is a problem. 3.3.1.1 Storage mechanism of Li+ ion batteries. A lithium ion battery consists of an anode, cathode, and electrolyte, which converts chemical energy into electrical energy. The electrochemical potential values of the electrodes (μA for anode and μC for cathode) and their positions relative to the HOMO–LUMO energy gap (Eg) of the electrolyte determine the choice of the electrode. For a stable cell, μA must be lower in energy than the LUMO of the electrolyte; otherwise, the electrolyte will be reduced. Alternatively, μC must be greater in energy than the HOMO of the electrolyte to prevent the electrolyte from being oxidized184b (Fig. 25c). With the highest electrochemical potential difference between the anode and cathode, as well as their high lithium intercalation ability, a cell with high energy storage density can be attained, although the stability of the electrolyte should not be disregarded.184c The anode is normally graphite and the cathode is usually layered LiCoO2 as an intercalation host for Li+, which are separated by a porous permeable membrane that only allows Li+ ions to pass through but prevents a short circuit due to direct electrode contact. The lithium metal oxide (e.g., LiCoO2) deintercalates from the Li+-ion, which then flows through the Li-ion conductive electrolyte and intercalates in the layered graphite. The mechanism occurring in an LIB is depicted in the schematic design in Fig. 25d. The reactions that produce lithium ions and electrons are as follows:
 
Cathode: LiCoO2 ⇌ 4 LixCoO2 + xLi+ + xe−(1)
 
Anode: 6C + xLi+ + xe− ⇌ LixC6(2)

To couple with the excess positive charge (Li+) in the electrode, the electrons go through the external electrical circuit. The reverse mechanism occurs during the discharge operation. Another key feature of LIBs is the selection of an appropriate electrolyte for a pair of electrodes. Alkyl carbonates (ethylene carbonate (EC), propylene carbonates (PC), and diethyl carbonate (DEC)) combined with the lithium salt hexa-fluorophosphate (LiPF6) or lithium perchlorate are often utilized as electrolytes in LIBs (LiClO4).184d Besides the energy gap of the solvents, characteristics such as viscosity and dielectric constants for thoroughly dissolving the solutes play a critical role in achieving a stable rechargeable cell. The total charge transferred in the electrode–electrolyte interface is connected to the current carried through the external circuit during the charging–discharging process, which is dependent on the current passing through the external circuit. During rapid charging at a high current, the rate of ion transport across the electrode–electrolyte interface is controlled by diffusion.184e When the Li-ion flow during the charge transfer process exceeds the Li-ion diffusion flux into the negative electrode,184f Li is projected to accumulate on the negative electrode and electrolyte interface. When the lithium ion concentration exceeds a particular saturation level, significant dendritic development of Li can be observed (0.077 mol cm−3). This deposition induces deformation beneath the electrode surface, resulting in an internal short and thermal runaway in a battery. Furthermore, because of the poor diffusion of Li+-ions at the interface, the dead Li deposited on the electrode surface causes internal resistance in the cell, and the changes at the electrode surfaces modify the rate, resulting in irreversible capacity fading. Capacity fading is caused by electrochemical breakdown of the electrolyte at the electrode–electrolyte interface, which forms the solid–electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer on the active material. Although the SEI layer causes irreversible capacity fading in a cell, it also acts as a barrier to prevent additional electrolyte dissolution at the electrode–electrolyte interface.184g,h The fraction of the electrical charge stored during charging that is recoverable during discharge after each cycle is the coulombic efficiency, which is related to the capacity fading, and thus calculated as Qdischarge/Qcharge × 100.

3.4 Storage and conversion of electrical energy

3.4.1 Fuel cells (FCs). FCs are a key aspect of energy storage and conversion technologies because they transform fuels (such as CH3OH, H2 gas, natural gas) into electricity for use in automobiles, portable gadgets and stationary machinery. Nonetheless, existing FCs are still a long way from the US DOE projected price, efficiency, and durability objectives for fuel-cell technology development in 2016. This necessitates the continued development of current electrode catalysts and electrolyte membranes, and the optimization of fuel cell auxiliary facilities. In the field of FCs, metal–organic frameworks, their derivatives, and composites are extremely promising electrolyte materials and electrode catalysts.

3.5 Conduction of protons

3.5.1 At low temperature. Due to their potential on-board applications, PEMFCs (polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells) have attracted significant attention. One of the most significant sulfonated fluoropolymers (namely, Nafion, which is a popular PEM material) has strong proton conductivity with thermal and chemical stability. However, its working conditions necessitate a temperature of less than 80 °C and a moistened environment, limiting its efficiency and utilisation.51 Consequently, it is vital to design unique electrolyte materials with excellent proton conductivity. Developing crystalline MOFs with efficient proton conductivities and low-cost synthetic approaches show potential as fuel cell electrolytes. Proton conduction in MOFs occurs in two ways, namely at low temperatures, i.e., below 100 °C under moist environments, and at high temperatures, i.e., over 100 °C in the anhydrous state. In the low-temperature region, {(NH4)2(adp) [Zn2(ox)3]·3H2O}n (where ox = oxalic acid and adp = adipic acid) was the first proton-conducting MOF studied in 2009. Its performance was similar to Nafion. The carboxylic groups of adipic acid, H2O molecules, and NH4+ ions were later used as conducting media185 (Fig. 26a). This MOF showed a proton conductivity of about 83 × 10−3 S cm−1 at 25 °C and 98% RH (relative humidity). Its activation energy (Ea) is higher than that of Nafion by 0.22 eV, showing that proton conduction occurs via both the Grotthuss and vehicle processes. Implanting various proton carriers into the pores of MOFs using 2-D porous oxalate-based structures (acting as hosts) allowed for the investigation of high proton conductivity. 3.5.2 At high temperature. The most successful strategy in the high-temperature regime is to embed nonvolatile guests into the pores of MOFs, for instance, histamine, imidazole, triazole, benzimidazole, and even H2SO4 and H3PO4, to facilitate proton delocalization channels for enhancing proton conduction at or above 100 °C and in anhydrous conditions.51 By encapsulating imidazole groups (Im) in Al-MOFs, i.e., Im@{Al(m2-OH)(1,4-NDC)}n(1,4-NDC = 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate) and Im@{Al(m2-OH)(1,4-BDC)}n, increased conductivities of ∼2.23 × 10−5 and 1.03 × 10−7 S cm−1, respectively, were achieved at 120 °C compared to other parent MOFs.192 The differing imidazole groups in these two MOFs are responsible for the differences in their proton conductivities. Under anhydrous conditions, b-PCMOF2, a sulfonate-based MOF with 1H-1,2,4-triazole (Tz) encapsulated in its pores, conducts protons with conductivities between 2 × 10−4 and 5 × 10−4 S cm−1 at 150 °C.193 The sample with the maximum proton conductivity in the b-PCMOF2(Tz)x (x = 0.30, 0.45, and 0.60) series was incorporated into a membrane electrode structure to build an H2-air cell. Electromotive force measurements at 100 °C demonstrated a continuous open-circuit value of 1.18 V for 72 h. This breakthrough not only validates the guest-impregnation method for researching novel proton conductors in the anhydrous state, but also covers the path for MOF-based fuel devices to operate at temperatures higher than 100 °C. MOFs have been made directly from inorganic phosphoric acid and by immobilising inorganic phosphoric acid monomers in their pores as nanoparticle sites using a secondary organic binder. It is important to note that a dry H2-air cell was prepared using a membrane-electrode assembly of {Zn(H2PO)2(HTz)2}n (where HTz = 1,2,4-triazole), thereby showing a proton conductivity of about 10−4 S cm−1 at a temperature of 150 °C.194 At 25 °C and 130 °C, the open-circuit voltage of the FC was shown to be ∼0.65 and 0.50 V, respectively. This method allows the easy creation of novel proton conductors at low cost.

3.6.2 Reduction of CO2via photocatalysis. The reduction of carbon dioxide is necessary because carbon monoxide, formic acid, methanol, methane, etc., are some of the energy derivatives leading to the emissions of carbon dioxide at the industrial level. In addition, turning CO2 to fuels may be an answer to the environmental issues triggered by excess carbon dioxide emissions. In the presence of catalysts based on metal–organic systems, researchers are using solar and electrical energy to facilitate carbon dioxide activation and its conversion in fuel devices. Here, we look at the key achievements made using materials based on MOFs in promoting photo-driven carbon dioxide reduction. The direct insertion of a catalytic component of rhenium based complex, i.e., [ReI(CO)3(dcbpy)Cl] (where dcbpy = 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid), into the extremely stable porous UiO-67 yielded the first MOF-based catalyst for the photochemical reduction of carbon dioxide.202 The photocatalytic carbon dioxide reduction performance of [ReI(CO)3(dcbpy)Cl]-doped UiO-67 was considerably higher compared to homogeneous [ReI(CO)3(dcbpy)Cl]. The incorporation of [ReI(CO)3(dcbpy)Cl] active sites in the metal–organic systems led to a unimolecular mechanism for the reduction of photoinduced carbon dioxide on the MOF-based catalyst. We must continue to create long-lasting, high-performance, and low-cost MOF-based catalysts because their photoactivities in photo-induced carbon dioxide reduction are currently poorer than that of inorganic semiconductor materials. Another intriguing method for improving the excitation and charge separation efficiency is to combine semiconductors and MOFs. 4. Impact, outlook and future prospects

The conductivities of MOFs, which are now their most significant barrier in electrochemical and photochemical-based energy applications, should be increased further for daily use. To study the impacts of diverse electrical and chemical environments or structural effects on the functional materials, composites and derivatives of metal–organic systems should be employed. PSM of as-prepared MOF compounds by chemical and electrochemical processes, for example, is a promising approach to enable more advanced functional inorganic materials, design methodologies, and preparation procedures for energy applications. The application conditions, especially severe circumstances such as extreme temperatures, strong pressure, and corrosion, should be considered for developing desirable materials. To reduce the expenses, improve the specific performance, and lengthen the usage lifespan of MOFs, their topology, structure, composition, preparation process, and intrinsic response mechanisms should all be analyzed simultaneously. Advanced characterization methods should be used in conjunction with theoretical simulations to investigate the internal process involving specific components, surfaces, and interfaces. To detect the key factors, new insights into the synergistic impact of multi-component composites should be presented. To reduce time required for the design and preparation of MOF-based materials, and to analyze their useful applications, effective and simple screening techniques for identifying high-performance materials should be developed. Development of medical equipment as part of the application of MOFs should be given much more attention. More continuous and robust efforts in this sector will intensify the possibilities of MOFs for energy applications. As a hole transportation medium, the copolymer poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) was shown to offer considerable potential for cost-effective and extremely efficient photovoltaic devices. There is significant interest in delivering many functionalities in future electronic equipment such as optical and electrical properties, processability and mechanical durability. Furthermore, downsized electronic gadgets with small flexible power sources, such as cable/wire-type flexible micro-batteries, will be a future trend. Due to the restricted surface for electrochemical devices in a cell, a tiny power source results in low energy storage, and batteries must be able to be charged in a matter of seconds with a long life (can be recharged a number of times) to meet their function. Also, the design of cheap, quick, and controlled fabrication procedures for adjustable sensors, electrolytes, and packaging is critical for the large-scale and low-cost production of flexible LIBs. Ongoing efforts should focus on printing technologies (such as conductive ink and digital printing), which can provide large-scale, solution-based, low-cost production methods with high mobility for application developers. Finally, different aspects, for example, the effect of the electrolyte, electrolyte concentration, pH of different solvents, organic linkers, and relevant temperature, on the performance of MOF-based supercapacitors should be investigated, eventually leading to the creation of a long-lasting supercapacitor devices and gadgets.

5. Conclusion and remarks

In this review, the up to date advancement of the energy applications of MOFs was summarized. Presently, exploring the energy applications of MOF-based materials has now become one of the most active research fields. Metal–organic systems, their derivatives and composites play significant roles in electrochemical and photochemical energy storage and its conversion for various purposes such as electron, and charge distribution, processing exoteric energy, enhancing gas diffusion, and promoting reactant activation in terms of storing gas molecules, facilitating mass transfer, and increasing electrical conductivity and durability. Furthermore, combining the porosity character of materials possessing tunable reactivity with different metals and organic-based linkers yields useful tools for energy-related applications. MOFs can encapsulate active functional materials when employed as supports and sacrificial precursors. MOFs are considered potential materials for developing nanostructures because of their appropriate features such as low cost, high yield, low densities, tunable porosity, adjustable pore sizes, high surface area, and controllable structures. These materials can be adjusted to increase the energy storage and conversion efficacy. For improving the performance and practical applications of materials, these criteria are extremely important. Gas storage, its purification, and separation are all promising uses. Fuel cells are an important component of energy storage and conversion technologies, which convert fuels into electricity for use in automobiles, portable gadgets, and other applications. Supplementary progress of microporous crystalline materials as proton conductors may lead to upgraded electrolyte sheets for fuel cells. Among the different batteries, LIBs have been recognized as commercially the most successful. However, because the lithium element in batteries is vulnerable to oxidation, they must be protected from being overheated and totally depleted. Compact, thin, adaptable, durable packing materials with outstanding barrier properties are also required to protect the battery components, electrolytes, and electrochemical processes from the external environment. This outlook also highlighted the use of nanoscale MOFs such as supercapacitors, organic solar devices, photocatalytic fuel production from H2O, and solar energy transformation, comprising studies of photon capture and various other applications of energy systems. This review generally presented many reasons why MOFs are extremely helpful at this nanoscale stage, and thus thought provoking to research forthcoming applications because of their chemical tunability, molecular and elemental diversity, high order, etc. These perspectives also emphasize the advantages of supercapacitors by means of nanorods, nanosheets, and nanocrystals through improved material compatibility, process ability, and performance because of the high exterior surface region, more exposed active area and faster e− and ion transmission. The purpose of this research was to display the trials that metal–organic systems face at the nanoscale level such as restricted conductivity, stability, and uniformity. These materials offer some desirable features such as tailorable structures and high surface area because of the availability of various metal precursors and organic ligands.

MOFs have found utility as novel materials in a broad spectrum of applications due to their high porosity. After a span of extreme investigation by numerous researchers, incredible developments have been accomplished using MOFs, their compounds and products for various energy-based applications in several areas. For supercapacitor applications, 3-D MOFs have been used as a positive electrode in numerous studies; nevertheless, the exploration for a negative electrode constructed using these MOFs has mostly been confined to MOF-generated nanoporous carbon-based materials. Consequently, researchers may focus their efforts on developing innovative MOF-based SC negative electrodes that outperform standard carbon materials in terms of electrochemical performance. The binder technique is used to fabricate the majority of MOF-based SC electrodes. A report implementing the binder-free approach was recently released. Consequently, this binder-free synthetic technique may be used to produce extremely efficient and dependable supercapacitor electrodes. These MOFs should be used as more than just sacrificial templates. Therefore, further study may lead to the development of different MOFs with unique topologies for electrochemical applications. In the case of supercapacitor applications, mixed metallic MOFs are preferable over single metals. Similarly, in the production of three-dimensional MOF-based supercapacitor electrodes, researchers should concentrate on mixed organic linkers. However, few organic-based linkers show insulating property between the metal centres, which is sometimes considered a major drawback. The direct conversion of pure metal–organic systems into their equivalent carbon nanotubes and metal oxides has been presented as a solution to this problem by researchers. In general, electrochemical stability can be improved when MOFs or MOF-based metal oxides or sulfides are hybridized with different organic linkers based on different donor atoms. The rate capability and cycle stability, in addition to specific capacitance, are critical variables in evaluating the actual performance of high-capacity supercapacitors. However, few metal electrodes of MOFs show high capacitance and they exhibit low cyclic stability, whereas some have a low specific capacitance but better cyclic stability. Thus, understanding the synthetic method, crystal growth, electrode material selection, and influence of various composite electrodes on electrochemical activity will aid in the development of high-performance supercapacitor devices capable of real-time applications. In terms of performance, 3-D MOF-based porous nanostructures outperform traditional metal oxides and carbon-based materials. MOFs are also an appealing possibility for these applications because of their capacity to regulate characteristics on a molecular scale with the right metallic core and organic linker. Consequently, 3-D MOF-based nanoporous carbon-based composites and porous metal oxides may be next-generation advanced materials for use in the field of energy storage materials for suitable microelectronics. Accordingly, many strategies for energy applications can be adopted.
