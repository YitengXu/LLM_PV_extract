Abstract

Two-dimensional (2D) mono-elemental materials from group IVâ€“VI have aroused great interest in the scientific community on account of their intriguing optoelectronic properties. Integration of elements from the same group into binary hetero-structures is of great significance for their diversified properties and potential multifunctional applications. In this contribution, a 2D selenium (Se, VI) coated tellurium (Te, VI) nanosheet (NS) binary heterostructure (Te@Se) is constructed and demonstrated as a promising material for a photodetector (PD) in a photoelectrochemical (PEC)-type photodetection system. The experiments confirm that the Te@Se-based PD exhibits pH-sensitivity in a wide pH range from 1 to 14 with many other advantages such as self-powered, repeatable fast dynamic pH response, and long-term stability over 30 days. Theoretical calculations are performed to understand the mechanism of the pH-sensitive optoelectronic effects in 2D Te@Se in the framework of density functional theory (DFT) calculations. It is anticipated that the presented work expands a new concept for the applications of 2D binary heterostructure-based multifunctional devices, combining photo-detection and pH sensing.

Introduction

The breakthrough discoveries of two-dimensional (2D) materials, including hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN),1 graphene,2 transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs),3,4 and a series of mono-elemental 2D materials,5â€“7 have aroused enormous interest of researchers all over the world. Among them, mono-elemental materials from group IVâ€“VI have become promising materials for various applications owing to their outstanding electronic and photonic properties.8â€“10 Graphene, the most studied mono-elemental material from group IV, has an extremely high carrier mobility (âˆ¼105 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1), but the no band gap nature has greatly limited its applications.11â€“14 Black phosphorus (BP), a star mono-elemental material from group V, has been widely investigated for bio-medicine,15,16 energy storage,17,18 electronics,19â€“21 and optoelectronic devices22,23 thanks to its tunable bandgap (0.3â€“2.0 eV), high hole mobility (âˆ¼103 cm2 Vâˆ’1sâˆ’1), and anisotropic characteristics. However, the intrinsic easily-oxidized property of BP has greatly limited its wide practical applications.24,25 As a typical representative of group VI, tellurium (Te) has similar properties to BP, such as a layered folded structure, tunable bandgap (0.35â€“1.05 eV),26 and high carrier mobility (âˆ¼103 cm2 Vâˆ’1sâˆ’1),27 with, however, better environmental stability than BP,28 arising as a promising candidate to replace BP in many fields. Te has a strong tendency to grow into a one-dimensional (1D) nanoarchitecture due to its chain-like structure and the inherent structural anisotropy.29,30 A wealth of previous studies have reported the formation of 1D Te nanomaterials like Te nanotubes (NTs) and nanowires (NWs), while there are fewer for 2D Te and its related properties.31â€“35 The reliable preparation of 2D Te is of great significance for developing new technologies based on this layered chalcogen, and which has become the urgent challenge of frontier focuses. For example, He et al. synthesized hexagonal Te nanoplates (NPs) through the van der Waals (vdW) epitaxy method on flexible mica sheets.36 The Te NP-based photodetector (PD) shows great potential in flexible and wearable optoelectronic devices with a high photo-response after bending 100 times. In 2018, Te nanoflakes (NFs) were prepared via the hydrothermal reduction of sodium tellurite (Na2TeO3) by Ye et al.30 The Te NF-based transistor exhibits a high ON/OFF ratio with an order of 106 and a high carrier mobility of about 700 cm2 Vâˆ’1sâˆ’1. Immediately afterwards, Javey et al. demonstrated that Te NFs can be used as short-wave infrared (SWIR) PDs, and the carrier mobility can be further increased to 1430 cm2 Vâˆ’1sâˆ’1 at 77 K.27 Our group prepared Te nanosheets (NSs) through the liquid phase exfoliation (LPE) method, and the obtained Te NSs possess a lateral dimension ranging from 41.5 to 177.5 nm with a thickness ranging from 5.1 to 6.4 nm, which exhibit good photo-response behaviors from the ultraviolet (UV) to the visible regime in a photoelectrochemical (PEC)-type photodetection system.37 Well synthesized 2D Te offers the base to build 2D Te-based heterostructures, which in turn introduce diversified properties for potentially multifunctional device applications beyond photodetection.

2D heterostructures, which are composed of two or more components, usually possess the advantages of each individual building block and also generate extra novel physical properties that provide opportunities to overcome the limitations of mono-component materials, thereby meeting the demands of fabricating high-performance multifunctional optoelectronic devices.38â€“40 In particular, binary hetero-structures constructed from mono-elemental materials from the same group, such as IVâ€“IV and Vâ€“V heterostructures, are extremely interesting due to their exceptional properties.41â€“43 For example, a 2D graphene/n-type silicon heterostructure (graphene/n-Si) was prepared and applied for photodetection by Fang et al.44 The graphene/n-Si-based PD shows a specific detectivity of 5.77 Ã— 1013 Jones, a high responsivity of 0.73 A Wâˆ’1, and a high ON/OFF ratio up to 107 at 890 nm in a vacuum. Zeng et al. reported 2D black arsenic/phosphorus (Î±-AsP) heterostructures.19 Î±-AsP shows a direct band gap of 1.54 eV and a high electron mobility of 14380 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. More importantly, the maximum power conversion efficiency of Î±-AsP-based excitonic solar cells (XSCs) can reach up to 22.1%. Nevertheless, at present, studies on 2D VIâ€“VI iso-group heterostructures are rare, and the most adopted strategies for preparing heterostructures are usually based on chemical vapor deposition (CVD),45â€“47 physical vapor deposition (PVD),48,49 and the molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) methods,50â€“52 which suffer from high fabrication costs, strict preparation conditions, and the risk of forming undesired byproducts. Therefore, it is desired to explore cost-effective and environmentally friendly methods, like the solvothermal method, to prepare 2D VIâ€“VI heterostructures for device applications with enhanced performance.53,54

In this work, we aim to prepare 2D selenium (Se) coated Te binary heterostructures (Te@Se) through the solvothermal method. It is found that the Te@Se-based PD shows excellent photo-response behaviors in a PEC-type photodetection system, which shows a high photocurrent (Pph) of 19.64 Î¼A cmâˆ’2, a high responsivity (Rph) of 432.72 Î¼A Wâˆ’1, and a specific detectivity (D*) up to 4.82 Ã— 108 Jones. Furthermore, systematic studies reveal that the measured Pph is very sensitive to the pH value of the applied electrolyte in a wide range from 1 to 14. The Te@Se-based PD exhibits a special self-powered pH detection capability due to the emerging built-in electronic field generated at the interface. Moreover, the investigations display the excellent repeatable dynamic pH detection, fast pH response (about 1.5 s), and pronounced long-term stability (over 30 days) of such a prepared pH-sensitive PD. The pH-sensitive photo-response behaviors of Te@Se are further demonstrated by density functional theory (DFT) calculations. We believe that the present work not only provides new ideas for the development of VIâ€“VI heterostructures, but also suggests novel applications of PDs based on 2D materials.

As discussed above, Pph shows an increasing trend with the gradually increasing pH of the electrolyte under SL or selected illumination wavelengths. According to previously reported work, the increased Pph can be attributed to the reduced interfacial resistance between the materials and electrolytes.65,70 To get deep insight into the detailed optoelectronic properties of Te@Se in electrolytes with different pH values, we performed DFT calculations for sandwich Te@Se heterostructures as shown in Fig. 7a. The projected band structure of Te@Se is depicted in Fig. 7b, showing that the Te states and Se states are strongly hybridized. The valence band maximum (VBM) state of Te@Se mainly consists of Te 5p orbitals as well as Se 4p orbitals, while the conduction band minimum (CBM) is mainly contributed by Se 4p orbitals and a part of Te 5p orbitals, indicating that the charge distributions of the CBM and VBM states are separated on the different layers of Te@Se (Fig. S7, ESIâ€ ), which can effectively separate photo-induced electrons and holes to avoid carrier recombination, resulting in the increased Pph of the devices. Therefore, Te@Se-based PDs could have significantly improved optoelectronic performance compared to pristine Te or Se-based PDs. It should also be emphasized that the Se atoms are exposed to the external environment in Te@Se, which provides more possibilities of electronic structure modulation through surface functionalization, external fields, and the pH value of electrolytes. In addition, Fig. S8 (ESIâ€ ) illustrates the band structure of 2D Te and Se, indicating the formation of a type II hetero-junction. Under thermal equilibrium conditions, due to the diffusion of intrinsic carriers in Te and Se, a built-in electric field will be generated at the interface. Under illumination, photoinduced carriers are generated. Driven by the built-in electric field, electrons move toward the conduction band of Se, while holes diffuse to the valence band of Te, which can facilitate photoinduced electronâ€“hole pair separation and suppress the rapid recombination of carriers. In type II hetero-junction-based devices, the photocurrent can transport and recirculate multiple times within their lifetimes, leading to an enhancement in photo-response behaviors.

The observed pH-dependent photo-response behaviors of Te@Se could be explained by the existence of surface O2 chemisorption/desorption. It is well known that O2 chemisorption/desorption play a significant role in modulating the photo-response process, which was found for other materials before.71â€“73 Here, we investigate the adsorption behaviors of O2 molecules on the Te@Se surface under acidic and basic conditions. In acidic electrolytes, the proton H+ can be attached to the Te@Se surface, leading to the formation of Seâ€“H bonds by capturing electrons from Te@Se (Fig. S9a, ESIâ€ ). When the dissolved O2 molecules approach the Te@Se surface, they could be adsorbed by the surface. The adsorption energy is further calculated (see the Experimental section, DFT calculation, ESIâ€ ) with a 2 Ã— 2 Ã— 1 supercell to evaluate the interaction between an O2 molecule and Te@Se. The relaxed configuration and differential charge density of Te@Se with adsorbed O2 under acidic conditions are presented in Fig. S9b and c (ESIâ€ ). The adsorption energy is calculated to be 0.078 eV, suggesting a very weak physisorption of O2 on the surface. The distance between the bottom O atom and its nearest Se atom is 3.008 Ã…. The Oâ€“O bond length is 1.254 Ã…, which is similar to that of the isolated O2 molecule (1.223 Ã…). The differential charge density showed that there is no obvious charge transfer between the O2 molecule and Te@Se. With the gradually increasing pH, the amount of OHâˆ’ increases in the electrolytes, and exceeds the H+ ion content in the basic condition. The OHâˆ’ ions attach to the Te@Se surface to form Seâ€“O bonds with electron migration from Te@Se (Fig. 7c). The O2 adsorption energy is calculated to be 0.433 eV, which is much larger than that in the acidic electrolytes, indicating a much stronger interaction between the O2 molecule and Te@Se. The larger adsorption energy corresponds to a more stable configuration, where O2 molecules are almost parallel to the surface (Fig. 7d). The distances between the O atom and its nearest Se atom are 2.017 Ã… and 1.804 Ã…, respectively, which are shorter than those in the acidic electrolytes (3.008 Ã… and 3.873 Ã…). The Oâ€“O bond is also elongated to 1.539 Ã…. As shown in Fig. 7e, we can observe an apparent charge accumulation region in O2 and Te@Se and an considerable amount of electron transfer from OHâˆ’ terminated Te@Se to the O2 molecule, forming negatively charged ions [O2(g) + neâˆ’ â†’ O2nâˆ’ (ad.), n = 1â€“2]. From the projected DOS, as depicted in Fig. 7f, we can observe the hybridization of O2 2p states and Se 4p (Te 5p) states in a wide energy range below the Fermi energy, reflecting the strong adsorption of O2 molecules on OHâˆ’ terminated Te@Se. In comparison to pristine Te@Se (Fig. 7g), the optical absorption coefficients of Te@Se with adsorbed O2 hardly changed after O2 absorption, spanning the whole regime concerned from infrared to ultraviolet in the alkaline electrolytes. However, in the light process, the adsorbed negatively charged O2nâˆ’ ions could be desorbed from the OHâˆ’ terminated Te@Se to form dissolved O2 molecules in the electrolytes again, releasing electrons, which leads to a significantly increased Pph of Te@Se under basic conditions (Fig. 3aâ€“d).

Apart from Pph, it is found that the photo-response speed of the Te@Se-based PDs is also affected by the pH value of the electrolytes. We intercept one typical ON/OFF signal from level IV of the PDs in different electrolytes and obtain the typical response and recovery time for different pH values. As can be seen from Fig. S9d and e (ESIâ€ ), the response and recovery time gradually increase with the increase of the pH. In the weak acidic electrolyte (pH = 2), the measured photo-response and recovery time are 0.03 and 0.04 s, respectively. As the pH of the electrolyte increases to 10, the response and recovery speed get slower (0.06/0.09 s). The response and recovery time are calculated to be 0.43 and 0.89 s with a further increase of the pH, which are almost 10â€“20 times longer than those in the weak acidic solutions (pH = 2). It is worth noting that the decreased photo-response speed stems from the absorption/desorption of O2. For example, in the weak acidic electrolyte (pH = 2), due to the very weak interaction between the O2 molecule and Te@Se (with a calculated adsorption energy of 0.078 eV), the absorption/desorption process proceeds very quickly, resulting in a fast photo-response speed. In the basic electrolyte, OHâˆ’ ions become dominant, and the calculated large adsorption energy (0.433 eV) suggests that the absorption/desorption process will be slowed due to the strong O2 absorption on the OHâˆ’ terminated Te@Se, resulting in long response and recovery times, which is in good agreement with our observations in experiments.

Conclusions

In summary, a 2D VIâ€“VI Te@Se binary heterostructure was successfully prepared through the hydrothermal method. The structure, composition, and optical absorption of Te@Se were thoroughly investigated. The studies show that Te@Se is considered as a promising nanomaterial for PEC-type photodetection with pH-sensitivity in a wide range from 1 to 14. Additionally, Te@Se-based PDs also possess advantages like self-powered pH-sensitive capability, fast dynamic pH response and pronounced long-term stability (over 30 days). DFT calculations further evidence that the pH-sensitive photo-response behaviors of Te@Se can be attributed to the different O2 chemisorption/desorption behaviors on the Te@Se surface in different electrolytes. It is believed that the presented work paves the way for designing novel VIâ€“VI binary heterostructures for multifunctional applications of both photodetection and chemical sensing.
