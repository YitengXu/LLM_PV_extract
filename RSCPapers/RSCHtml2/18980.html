Abstract

Photoelectrochemical hydrogen (H2) production from water is a key method of addressing energy needs using an environmentally friendly approach. In the last two decades we have witnessed the evolution of many different expensive catalysts, photoelectrodes and related technologies, especially those involving precious metals and use of acidic or basic electrolytes for hydrogen production. Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) is a relatively new candidate in the category of efficient photocathodes, due to its high absorption coefficient and near optimal energy band gap. In this paper, we demonstrate photoelectrochemical viability of CZTS in combination with other photoanodes such as TiO2, BiVO4, and WO3 for H2 production with the use of an electrolyte of near neutral pH, a single redox mediator, and insignificant potential biasing. A systematic study was performed to understand CZTS performance with each photoanode, band energetics of CZTS with other photoanodes, impedance behavior of each photoelectrode, and utility of a CZTS photocell in place of a CZTS photocathode. Our assessment indicates that a protected CZTS photocell performs well when used in a Z-scheme containing TiO2 nanotubular array-CZTS or nanocrystalline WO3–CZTS. Preliminary experiments indicated that apart from band energetics, porosity and effective surface area of the photoanodes play a crucial role in determining the photoelectrochemical performance of the system.

1. Introduction and background

Solar hydrogen production using a semiconductor photocatalyst–electrolyte system is one of the most promising options for clean fuel generation. For water splitting, a substantial minimum amount of energy (ΔG° = 238 kJ mol−1) is required.1 This can be achieved using a semiconductor, which upon irradiation can produce energy2 equivalent to or greater than the minimum energy required for water splitting. For a single semiconductor water splitting system, the potential of the conduction band edge must be more negative than the potential for proton reduction, and the valence band should be sufficiently positive for efficient water splitting. Many n-type materials have a sufficiently large band gap for water oxidation but are not capable of producing a high current density.1 Smaller band gap p-type materials, which cannot provide sufficient energy for single semiconductor water splitting, have conduction bands more negative than the proton reduction potential.1 Most of the non-oxide semiconductors have reduction potentials that are higher than the proton reduction potential. Hence, they are resistant to photo-reduction. Regarding hole oxidation, many p-type cathodes form downward band bending at the semiconductor/water interface, which acts as a barrier for holes.1 The reduction and oxidation potentials for most water splitting semiconductors depend on the pH value of electrolytes.1 The photoanode (n-type) should be stable if the thermodynamic oxidation potential is lower than the valence band minimum.3 For photocathodes, if the cathode material reduction potential is higher than the water reduction potential, it will be reduced before water reduction takes place. It is also recommended that metal oxide to be used as a photo-anode should have a lower oxidation potential than the oxidation potential of water.3 In many cases, it is preferred that the metal atoms in these oxides should be in a higher valence state, otherwise they will oxidize before oxidation of water takes place. Most of the non-oxide semiconductors possess a higher oxidation potential compared to the water oxidation reaction and hence they are likely to become thermodynamically unstable in aqueous solution if used as photoanodes for water oxidation. Photoelectrodes such as TiO2, BaTiO3, and SrTiO3 have been extensively utilized for photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting under UV irradiation.1 Other materials such as BiVO4, WO3, SrTiO3: Rh have also been utilized in the visible spectrum.4–6 Various d10 and d0 class type photocatalysts1,7–23 have been utilized for water splitting. Although various novel materials show promising results, none of them can maintain 1000 hours of stability in typical solutions under solar illumination conditions.24

1.1 Challenges for water splitting photoelectrodes stability and performance

A major challenge related to stability and light utilization is to reduce the current cost of hydrogen production using PV–electrolysis systems, which is currently more than $10 per kg. The current cost of hydrogen production by other methods is ∼$1–4 per kg.25 Amongst non-oxide materials, a GaInP2/GaAs tandem configuration shows high solar to hydrogen conversion efficiency but low stability. I–III–VI materials can be useful due to high photon conversion efficiency and low cost of manufacturing.24 Another factor related to cost is reagent consumption and waste product removal. A shift of ∼59 mV in standard potential generally occurs for each unit increase in pH, making hydrogen production difficult with neutral pH water due to less favorable band energetics.1 The acidic media (concentration ∼ 1 M) is generally used to supply an abundance of hydrogen ions that can be quickly converted to hydrogen gas molecules. The abundance of hydrogen ions makes the process of producing hydrogen gas much easier in acidic pH than at neutral pH. However, the consumption of hydrogen ions from acid enriched solutions requires replenishment of the acid to maintain the high rate of hydrogen production. The added acid contains its associated counter ion, which will accumulate in solution as acid is replenished, thereby necessitating additional processing to remove the counter ion. The need to add acid and to remove accumulated counter ions from acid additions increases the true cost and lowers the overall cycle efficiency. The same principle applies to consumable redox reagents. Addition of high concentrations of acid also increases electrode corrosion and decreases long term performance. Most photocatalyst research has shown water splitting activity with UV irradiation. However, photoactivity using visible light illumination has been limited.1 A material with a band gap of 2.0 eV or more with good chemical environment stability, and a long minority carrier diffusion length is required for single electrode solar hydrogen production.1 Although many potential materials such as Fe2O3 and TaON have shown photocurrent, most of them do not have sufficient photopotential to split water.1 TaON shows good photocurrent but the durability of this electrode is not sufficient. Materials such as SrTiO3 (as a single electrode material) have been utilized for water splitting.5,6,26 However, the high necessary band gap eliminates most of the solar spectrum and thereby reduces practical efficiency.

1.2 Importance of Z-scheme for water splitting and use of CZTS

In a Z-scheme, two different semiconductors are assembled in such a manner that the oxidation and reduction reactions will be carried out by two separate semiconductors.1 Reduction of protons (hydrogen evolution) occurs at the electrode for which the conduction band is located above the potential of the H+/H2 reaction (Fig. 1). In this system, an electron donor species such as Fe2+ will be converted into an acceptor such as Fe3+ by utilization of valance band holes at the hydrogen producing semiconductors. On the other hand water oxidation occurs by valence band holes at the photoanode surface such as BiVO4. The valence band needs to be below the O2/H2O reduction potential line. Most of the metal chalcogenides undergo anodic corrosion and hence cannot generate oxygen.3 Hence, a p–n PEC structure, which contains a photocathode that is stable under the cathodic conditions, can be used to produce hydrogen with photoanodes that are stable under anodic conditions, provided a salt bridge/membrane is used to separate the associated environments. Most of the existing new semiconductor compounds and photoelectrodes suffer from low quantum yield, inefficient solar conversion efficiency, the need for a bias voltage, and high photo-induced corrosion.1,3–6,24,26–33 For high efficiency PEC cells, very efficient p-type photocathodes are required.34–40 Though, many of the p-type electrodes have been investigated, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) or Cu–Sb–Zn–S (CAZS) electrodes have been least explored.25,34,36–39 The relative position of conduction bands of CZTS and its derivatives are appropriate for producing hydrogen by reduction of H+ ions. Close examination of a CZTS band diagram suggests that the oxygen evolution reaction is not possible at this electrode. The conduction band is ∼0.7 V below the hydrogen evolution potential, whereas the valence band is ∼0.55 V below the oxygen evolution potential.3,40 This suggests that unassisted water splitting is not possible using a single CZTS electrode. Hence, additional experiments were needed to evaluate CZTS in combination with oxygen evolving catalysts or photoanodes. In order to achieve this task, two compartment and single compartment experiments were conducted to evaluate hydrogen generation capabilities in augmentation with photoelectrodes such as TiO2, WO3, and BiVO4. In this piece of research, most of the experiments are designed to operate in a neutral pH environment with regenerating redox reagents. The use of CZTS has been demonstrated to meet the need of band alignment, as well as production of hydrogen gas from water in a Z-scheme system without using a biasing potential. In the proposed system the production of hydrogen and oxygen will be balanced to match the stoichiometry of water and eliminate the need for acid consumption, redox consumption, and counter ion removal, thereby reducing overall operating costs. Most of the experiments have been performed using thin film type photoelectrodes, however, some experimental results have been also presented for nanocrystalline powder.

2. Experimental

2.1 Synthesis of CZTS thin film

CZTS thin films were grown on a fluorinated tin oxide coated conducting transparent substrate by an electrochemical method. Cu–Zn–Sn alloy layers were electrochemically co-deposited using a bath with a three electrode system. The bath contains ions of interest and a complexing agent. Other details about film morphology, characterization, and electrochemical performance can be found in earlier reports.41,42 These films, which were electroplated with metal precursors (Cu, Zn and Sn), were annealed in a sulfur environment, using a tube furnace and evaporated elemental sulfur for 2 h.

2.2 Fabrication of CZTS/CdS/i-ZnO/ZnO cell

After confirmation of phase purity and film quality, a thin layer of CdS was deposited over the CZTS layer. The CdS layer was grown using a chemical bath deposition method using acetate media. The film containing the adjacent layers of CZTS and CdS was utilized to fabricate a cell, that was completed when a thin layer of i-ZnO and n-type ZnO were deposited using an RF sputtering technique. Other details about deposition parameters can be found elsewhere.43

2.3 Synthesis of BiVO4 powder and thin film

Synthesis of BiVO4 thin film and powder was carried out using bismuth nitrate and ammonium metavanadate. In a typical synthesis scheme, a stoichiometric quantity of Bi(NO3)3·5H2O and NH4VO3 were dissolved in the ethanol–water mixture with a volume ratio of ethanol and water of (Vethanol:Vwater) = 3:1. Nitric acid solution was added to adjust the pH to 4 under stirring until a yellow-milky gel formed. This gel was coated over a fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) glass substrate and subsequently annealed in tube furnace at ∼350 °C for 15–20 minutes to obtain a BiVO4 thin film. Synthesis of BiVO4 powder was carried out using a process discussed elsewhere.6,30,31

2.4 Synthesis of WO3 bulk and nanoflakes array film

WO3 bulk and nanoflakes array film were synthesized on FTO coated glass substrate using a previously reported solvothermal route with some stoichiometry modifications.44,45 For bulk WO3 film synthesis, 1.25 g of tungstic acid (H2WO4) and 0.5 g of poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) were dissolved in 10 mL of H2O2 (30% by wt, Fischer Scientific) and subsequently coated on the conducting surface of the FTO glass substrate. Further, the resultant substrate was annealed in air at 500 °C for 2 hours and was labelled FTO–WO3-B. For solvothermal growth of the nanoflakes array film,46,47 FTO–WO3-B was used as a primary substrate (considered as a seed layer). An H2WO4 solution for solvothermal use was prepared by dissolving 1.25 g of H2WO4 into 30 mL of deionized water aided by gradual addition of 10 mL of 30% H2O2 while heating at 95 °C on a hot plate with stirring. The resulting solution was diluted using deionized water to 100 mL in order to obtain a molar concentration of 0.05 M. In a typical procedure described elsewhere, a solution mixture comprising 3 ml of 0.05 M of H2WO4 solution, 0.02 g of oxalic acid, 0.02 g of urea, and 0.5 mL of 6 M HCl were added into 12.5 mL of acetonitrile, and the resultant solution was transferred to a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave. The FTO–WO3-B substrate was placed at an angle against the wall of the Teflon-liner with the conducting side (side with the coated seed layer) facing down. The solvothermal synthesis was conducted at 180 °C for 2 h. Post completion of the synthesis, the autoclave was allowed to cool down to room temperature. The substrate was taken out and annealed in air at 500 °C for 1 hour and was labelled FTO–WO3–NF.

2.5 TiO2 nanotube synthesis

Titania nanotubes were synthesized by electrochemical anodization of titanium foils (0.1016 mm thick, ESPI metals, G1 grade) using a previously reported method.48,49 Titanium foils were cut into 1.5 × 1.5 cm coupons, hand-polished with emery paper, and degreased in an acetone–isopropanol (50% v/v each) mixture by ultrasonicating for 30 minutes. Anodization was carried out at 30 V in a fluorinated ethylene glycol solution (0.5 wt% NH4F + 3 wt% H2O) under constant stirring at 60 rpm for one hour using Pt foil as the counter electrode. The anodized coupons were annealed in air at 500 °C for 2 hours.

2.6 Modification of photoanode with Co-Pi catalyst

Cobalt based oxygen evolution catalysts (Co-Pi or cobalt phosphate) were deposited on the surface of photoanodes by photo-assisted electroplating deposition in a neutral phosphate buffer solution containing Co2+ ions. Co-Pi nanoparticles deposition on the photoanodes was achieved by a similar photochemical method as described in previous reports.50 The photoanodes (BiVO4 on FTO glass) were separately placed in a flask containing 50 mL of sodium phosphate buffer solution (0.01 M, pH = 7.0) prepared by dissolving potassium phosphate monobasic (KH2PO4) in deionized water. To this suspension, 1.25 mL of 0.02 M cobalt nitrate (Co(NO3)2·6H2O) aqueous solution was added under stirring. The mixture was subjected to UV light illumination (100 W@365 nm) for 30 min and the resulting precipitate formed on the photoanode substrate, after which it was withdrawn and washed with deionized water thoroughly before drying at 60 °C overnight. It is presumed that such Co-Pi coated photoanodes would exhibit better electrochemical performance than photoanodes without Co-Pi.

2.7 Synthesis of CZTS powder

For CZTS powder synthesis, copper(II) acetate (99.9%), zinc(II) acetate (99.9%), tin(II) acetate anhydrous (≥97%), and thiourea (>99%) were ground to make homogeneous mixture. The molar composition of these salts was chosen in such a way so that stoichiometric ratio of Cu:Zn:Sn in the mixture was ∼2:1:1. This mixture was sintered within annealing tube in the presence of an evaporated sulfur environment for 2 hours at 550 °C. The annealing tube was continuously purged with argon for 25–30 min to displace air, prior to sulfur annealing. Elemental sulfur (99.9%), placed on a quartz boat, was held at a temperature of 150 °C was the primary source of evaporated sulfur. After 2 hours, the product was allowed to cool naturally and was subsequently examined for phase detection. A sponge-type CZTS material was obtained, which easily turned into powder when ground.39 This powder (see Fig. 2p) was washed in KCN (10% by weight in water) solution followed by washing in 50% aqueous solution of isopropyl alcohol to remove residual sulfide phases.39

2.8 ZnO encapsulation

Among several oxides which can be easily coated on CZTS to improve PEC performance, ZnO is best suited for this purpose, as it has adequate transparency and can be easily applied over CZTS nanocrystals. In order to coat CZTS with ZnO, CZTS nanocrystals were dispersed ultrasonically in deionised water for 30 minutes. Then a dilute aqueous solution of 0.04 M Zn(NO3)2 was added into the reaction mixture and subsequently stirred. A sodium hydroxide aqueous solution was added into this mixture until the pH reached 10. At this point segregated ZnO encapsulated CZTS crystals were observed clearly. These nanocrystals were centrifuged and subsequently filtered using filter paper. After repeated washing steps the nanocrystals were dried at in a vacuum oven. It is essential to note that the thickness of CZTS/ZnO encapsulation can be controlled by changing the molar concentration of Zn(NO3)2 solution.

3. Characterization

The various morphologies of the synthesized TiO2, BiVO4, and WO3 thin films and WO3 nanoflakes arrays were characterized using a field emission scanning electron microscope (Hitachi S-4800 SEM) maintained at ultrahigh vacuum (∼10−8 Torr). An intense electron beam from a tungsten filament at 3 kV accelerating voltage and 15 μA emission current setting was used for imaging at very high magnification (∼45000×). Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) mapping was achieved using an Oxford EDX detector attached to the SEM. The EDS studies were performed at 20 kV accelerating voltage and high probe current over a 25 mm × 25 mm sample area. Raman spectroscopy of BiVO4 and WO3 substrates were carried out in order to investigate phase purity and vibrational properties. Raman examination results for CZTS and TiO2 can be found in our earlier reports.49,51 A R3000 QE Raman spectrometer was utilized in backscattering mode. Raman measurements were performed using a laser with a wavelength of ∼785 nm and a power of ∼95 mW. The wavelength stability and drift were examined using standard silicon sample measurements and it was observed to be less than 1 cm−1 over a 12 h period. X-ray diffraction (XRD) was carried out using a Rigaku Miniflex XRD (CuKα = 1.54059 Å) from 2θ = 20 to 80° with a step size of 0.015° and dwell time of 1 degree min−1. The diffraction patterns were analyzed using Rigaku PDXL2 analysis software and indexed with standard JCPDS cards.

In order to examine photoelectrochemical performance of CZTS electrode, a two compartment cell was fabricated using CZTS on FTO photocathode and photoanodes. For a comparison three different photoanodes (Co-Pi coated BiVO4 on FTO, WO3 nanoflakes on FTO, and TiO2 nanotubular arrays) were chosen. For electrolyte, 0.1 M potassium phosphate aqueous solution was used. In order to connect two half-cell compartments, a filter paper soaked with concentrated sodium chloride was utilized as a salt bridge. Electrochemical measurements were performed under simulated sunlight irradiation (ABET Technology, AM 1.5). Fig. 6a shows a schematic diagram of such a water-splitting system. Some experiments were performed without putting external bias while some experiments were also conducted with application of bias voltage. Fig. 6b shows I–V curves of different photoelectrochemical system (CZTS in combination with different photoanodes). It can be seen that with increase in applied bias, photocurrent increases. It was also interesting to observe that without any external bias, change in photocurrent was realized during illumination. Hydrogen evaluation analysis was performed using a portable hydrogen detector, which shows hydrogen production when electrodes were illuminated. Fig. 6c suggests that CZTS–TiO2 nanotubular arrays and CZTS–WO3 system are better choices for solar hydrogen system compared to CZTS–BiVO4. The change in photocurrent while being illuminated was at least ∼100 μA in the case of TiO2 nanotubular arrays photoanode, whereas such a change was lowest for BiVO4 photoanode and the change in current was less than ∼10 μA, when applied bias was ∼1.5 V. It can be seen that such a performance is comparable with many water-splitting systems. In some cases this performance was found to be superior than photoelectrochemical systems such as RGO–CuGaS2 composites.55

4.3.1 CZTS photocell–BiVO4 system for water splitting. Most of the metal oxides have a higher oxidation potential than water oxidation at pH = 0, and they are already oxidized below the water oxidation potential. As a result, they are stable at the water oxidation potential. Hence, they can be used as suitable photoanodes (see Fig. 7). On the other hand, most of the non-oxide semiconductors have lower oxidation potentials than the water oxidation potential at pH = 0. Thus, when these non-oxide semiconductors reach the water oxidation potential, they convert to metal oxides, and are therefore unstable. Therefore, these semiconductors are thermodynamically unstable in aqueous solution. For CZTS, it is expected that its anion component will be oxidized to a neutral or positive valence state (S or SO4−).3 However, the reduction potential of the CZTS is more negative than the standard hydrogen reduction potential, which indicates that it will be stable under reduction conditions during illumination. If a hole-blocker oxide layer is applied on these semiconductors, it can allow photogenerated electron migration from the conduction band to solution via the oxide layer, but holes will be restricted due to the low valence band edge of the oxide layer.3 The inability of the holes to reach the surface will protect the underlying semiconductor from oxidation by solution species. Among transparent oxides, SnO2, In2O3, ZnO, CdO, MgIn2O4, and CdSb2O6 are widely used as transparent conductors with or without dopants.52 Among them, ZnO and SnO2 are cost effective, hence they can be used to prevent oxidation of CZTS.56 Apart from protective layer, the use of CdS thin layer over CZTS coating will provide, additional band bending and photovoltage assistance for water splitting. Hence, additional experiments were conducted using a CZTS photocell in combination with another photoanode (such as BiVO4) for solar water splitting. In this case also, we used a similar configuration to what we used earlier (see Fig. 6a). Photoelectrochemical characterization of CZTS thin films photocell and BiVO4 thin films has been carried out in aqueous solution containing Fe3+/Fe2+ redox mediator at pH 7. 4.4 Band diagram

Fig. 10a shows a band diagram and possible band energetics, drawn from band position information from the literature. It should be noted that it is a wired configuration, and in this case photoelectrode producing relative low photocurrent will mainly govern the operating current density.57 As in this configuration, both electrodes are simultaneously irradiated with AM 1.5 G illumination, it is expected that both electrode will absorb solar photons based on their band gap energy. In case of BiVO4, that has a band gap energy of ∼2.5 eV, it can potentially absorb solar photons up to a wavelength ∼500 nm.57 In case of nanostructured WO3, that has a band gap energy of ∼2.7 eV,58 the absorbance cut-off wavelength will be relatively less ∼460 nm. For, TiO2 NTA, that has a band gap energy of ∼3.2 eV, it is believed that solar photons up to a wavelength of ∼390 nm, will be absorbed. A possible explanation for higher photocurrent in the case of CZTS–TiO2 combination can be understood by examining photoelectrochemical kinetics of these immersed electrodes in solution. When the photoanode is immersed in electrolyte, holes that are produced during photonic excitation are utilized in oxidation reactions, whereas electrons in the conduction band migrate towards the photocathode via an external circuit and will be utilized in the reduction reaction. The top of valence band should be more positive compare to oxygen evolution potential, which is a condition for the photoanode to generate oxygen. Similarly for the photocathode, the bottom of the valence band should be more negative compared to that of the hydrogen evolution potential. It can be seen that our system fulfils these criteria very well. In the case of n-type electrode (such as TiO2 or WO3), in order to equalize the Fermi level, some of the electrons will be depleted from it. The depletion of electrons will cause an upward bending of bands, and depletion width of photoanode will accommodate the positive charge. In contrast, the Helmholtz layer of solution, contains the negative charge. Such a condition, creates a gradient that directs positive holes (photogenerated minority carriers) towards the solution. In case of p-type photocathodes, an analogous process will occur, where an electric field gradient will direct negatively charged photogenerated minority carriers towards the solution. Now, let us re-examine the band-diagram, showing the relative position of all photoanodes used in the experiments. The difference between conduction band edge and water oxidation potential, (or barrier height) can be correlated with the maximum internal energy, which can be extracted from this combination. It can be seen that such a barrier was highest in the case of TiO2.

4.6. Mechanism

The role of CdS in various photoelectrochemical devices have been demonstrated60 including quantum dots based dye sensitized solar cells, where CdS QDs were observed to be highly helpful enhancing the power conversion efficiency for TiO2 nanotubes based devices (sensitized with CdS quantum dots). Similarly, the CdS–CIGS combination demonstrated an efficient solar water splitting system when decorated with Pt clusters. Hence, few experiments were conducted and analyzed where CZTS absorber layers were coated with CdS layers. Photoexcitation of the Z-scheme [n-type SC – CZTS (CdS)] photoelectrode system with subsequent charge transport mechanism can be summarized by the following reactions:
 
CZTS + hν → CZTS(e + h)(1)
 
CZTS(e + h) → CZTS + hν + Δ(heat)(2)
 
CdS + CZTS(e) → CdS(e) + CZTS(3)
 
CdS(e) + Ox → CdS + Red(4)
where eqn (1) and (2) are the photoexcitation and recombination events. It is presumed that the photogenerated electron–hole pairs of CZTS are able to be separated in a region known as charge separating junction (boundary between CdS and CZTS) and act as relatively longer lived carriers.61Eqn (3) shows the electron transfer from CZTS to CdS, and eqn (4) is the electron transfer from CdS to the redox couple (Ox) resulting in reduced products (Red). The electrons generated in CZTS conduction band will travel downward across other bands with favorable band energetics and will be transferred to solution, which in turn leads to the reduction of H+ to H2 gas.
 
2H+ + 2(e) → H2(5)
Scavenging of accumulated holes in the valence band of CdS is important to maintain stability of the absorber layer (eqn (4)). If there is extensive accumulation of holes, CdS will begin to oxidize and result in diminishing photoelectrochemical performance overtime. Sacrificial electrolytes containing Fe2+/Fe3+ ions are used to stabilize chalcogenide sensitizers as they have suitable redox potentials.62 The presence of Fe3+ acts as an electron acceptor (from Fe3+ to Fe2+) whereas the Fe2+ acts as a hole scavengers (from Fe2+ to Fe3+) to facilitate charge carrier localization and hence prolong separation by trapping at energy levels close to the conduction or valence bands, respectively. Therefore, the presence of Fe3+/Fe2+ redox couple could be effective in producing materials that delay electron–hole recombination, thereby increasing the lifetime of the electron–hole separation. In this study, the electrolyte used consists of FeCl3. The Fe3+ ions act as redox mediators, to which the conduction band of the n-type photoanode (TiO2/WO3/BiVO4) donates an electron and as a result, Fe3+ is reduced to Fe2+.
 
Fe3+ + n-type(e) → Fe2+(6)
Fe2+ acts as a hole scavenger and is oxidized to Fe3+ to prevent (eqn (4)).
 
Fe2+ + CdS(h) → Fe3+(7)
The n-type photoanode ensures oxygen production at the anode. Upon illumination on the n-type semiconductor, as the electrons are excited to the conduction band and further make its way to the redox couple, the photogenerated holes cause oxidation of water to oxygen gas. The electrons released in the oxidation of water flow through the external circuit and result in the generation of the anodic current as shown in eqn (8) and (9).
 
H2O + n-type(h) → OH− + H+ → O2− + 2H+(8)
 
O2− → 1/2O2 + 2e(9)
For the second case, uncoated CZTS was used as the photocathode while the n-type photoanode (such as BiVO4) was coated with cobalt based oxygen evolution catalysts (Co-Pi) while phosphate ions acted as the redox mediator. It is also important to note that the interface between the n-type semiconductor and Co-Pi phases in the photoanode system plays an important role in charge transfer as upon illumination,63 holes generated in the n-type semiconductor migrate to the Co-Pi phase. The enhanced activity for oxygen evolution was directly observed from photocatalytic water oxidation over [n-type (Co-Pi) – CZTS] photoelectrode system. We observed that Co-Pi catalyst deposited on the photoanode effectively enhanced the anodic photocurrent. A possible mechanism can be understood based on these equations:
 
n-type + hν → n-type(e + h)(10)
 
n-type(e + h) → n-type + hν + Δ(heat)(11)
 
n-type(h) + Co-Pi → n-type + Co-Pi(h)(12)
 
n-type(e) + Ox → n-type + Red(13)
The equations suggest that Co-Pi played the role of cocatalyst. It help photon driven oxidation occurred via Co-Pi catalyst oxidation (from photogenerated holes from semiconductor) (eqn (12)), leading to the enhancement of electron and hole separation efficiency and longer lifetime of holes. The mechanism, defining the role of Co-Pi is not perfectly understood. In some cases it is believed that charge is being transferred to and stored in the Co-Pi film without an occurrence of water oxidation stable stage.64 It is reported that such a trapping or storage is useful and promote the recombination of electrons in conduction band and conversion of cobalt to a lower oxidation state.64 Another report suggests that Co-Pi treatment results in reduction of back electron/hole recombination events.65 The use of Co-Pi significantly affects the kinetic competition between water oxidation and back electron/hole recombination, by favoring the later process.65Eqn (10)–(13) represent a generalize way to understand the mechanism.

Due to phosphate ions present in the solution, dissociation of phosphoric acid takes place according to the following reactions:

 
H3PO4 ↔ H2PO4− ↔ HPO42− ↔ PO43−(14)
Under illumination, HPO42− further changes to HPO4− as shown in eqn (15), and the electron is donated to the photocathode CZTS conduction band (eqn (16)), which ensures hydrogen production at the cathode as indicated in eqn (5).
 
HPO42− + hν → HPO4−(e)(15)
 
HPO4−(e) + CZTS → HPO4− + CZTS(e)(16)
In the absence of an electron acceptor, electrons and holes generated (eqn (10)) from Co-Pi/n-type photoanode would recombine quickly (eqn (11)), and as a result photocatalytic water oxidation would not take place. In order to prevent this, HPO4− scavenges the electrons (eqn (13) and (17)) from the conduction band of the n-type semiconductor while the Co-Pi cocatalyst traps the photogenerated holes as mentioned earlier, causing oxidation of water as shown in eqn (8).
 
HPO4− + n-type(e) → HPO42−(17)
OH˙ radicals generated from water oxidation can initiate formation of phosphate radicals too (PO4H2˙, PO4H˙−, PO4˙2−) from the reaction of OH˙ with phosphate ions contained in high concentrations in the water matrix.

Thus, a comparison of the two cases, one in which CdS photo-absorber layer is coated on the p-type CZTS with Fe3+/Fe2+ redox couple, and the other in which Co-Pi cocatalyst is coated on the n-type semiconductor with phosphate radicals as mediator, has been presented for efficient photocatalytic water oxidation and subsequent hydrogen production.

Here one question can be asked regarding enhanced efficiency for NT TiO2–CZTS system or CZTS–NC WO3 system compared to that CZTS–BiVO4 system. One common similarity in TNT and NC WO3 system is their enhanced effective surface area of interaction. As can be seen in earlier reports that surface area is one of the leading factors deciding the overall performance of solar water splitting system.66 Such an effect and their leading contributions can be validated using photocathodes made from bulk TiO2 or bulk WO3. In our preliminary experiments we have demonstrated that bulk WO3 is not as effective as a photoanode as NC WO3. Similarly, effect of nanocrystallinity can be demonstrated using TNT electrodes with different effective surface area. A relative increment in surface area of was twice, when inner pore diameter decreases from 80 nm to 20 nm.67 A change in nanotube diameter not only alters the surface area but also the roughness and contact angle of the electrodes.67 In our case we have observed that current density (for two compartment system, with unprotected CZTS) drops to ∼1.3 × 10−4 A cm−2 from 1.6 × 10−4 A cm−2 when inner pore diameter increment was ∼25%.

5. Further scope of development and additional preliminary results

5.1 Porous layer utilization

To understand the effect of porous CZTS structure on charge carrier generation, a schematic diagram is shown in Fig. 12a, in which the paths of light rays are shown. It can be seen that, a light ray would be reflected internally in a porous nanocrystalline structure. Such a morphology enhances the light propagation path length, which enhances charge carrier generation. The SnO2 layer possesses good light harvesting capacity when coated on the CZTS surface. A greater amount of light would be used by the SnO2/CuZnSnS4 core/shell combination for generating the charge carrier when porous material is used. Furthermore, it has been known that the SnO2 nanorods, grown on a conductive glass substrate, can directly transport the excited electrons to the outer surface, utilizing the excellent charge transport property of SnO2 to its full advantage. From the energy band diagram (Fig. 12b), it can be seen that both the conduction band and the valence band of SnO2 are lower than that of CZTS, thereby inducing band bending. It is essential to note that the protective oxide layer should form a staggered type-II band alignment with CZTS. Another important criteria for the protective layer is that the surface in contact with the solution must have favourable reaction kinetics towards the reduction reaction. During irradiation, electron–hole pairs are generated. The photo-induced electrons tend to move from the conduction band of the CZTS to the conduction band of the protective oxide layer and subsequently to the solution. On the other hand photo-induced holes tend to move from the valence band of the CZTS to the valence band of the oxide. Thus, the photo-induced electrons and holes can effectively separate in the heterojunction, which decreases the recombination ratio of photo-induced electrons and holes, thereby leading to improved PEC performance.

Additional experiments were conducted in order to validate the proof of concept for a single compartment impregnated electrode for water splitting. 5.3 Effect of crystal size, doping, and surface modifications

Size has significant effects on electronic, optical and photoelectrochemical properties of photovoltaic material crystals.33 Generally, nanostructures have a wider band gap compared to their corresponding bulk materials.33 Such a band gap enhancement can cause a shift in conduction as well as valence band position. Fig. 14 shows the Mott–Schottky plots for CZTS (nanocrystals and micro-powder) obtained from electrochemical impedance measurements data. Negative slopes of these curve indicates that both materials are p-type. All electrochemical experiments were conducted at near neutral pH conditions. The flat-band potentials can be obtained from the intercepts of the extrapolated lines. A significant difference in the flat-band potentials between nanocrystals and micropowders can be seen, with the flat-band potential of nanocrystals less than that of micropowders by ∼0.45 V.33 Such a measurement suggests that there is a difference of ∼0.45 eV in the valence band edge of the two morphologies. From the band diagram, it can be observed that by controlling the size of CZTS crystals, band offsets (from the oxygen evolution potential) can be modified. These results match well with earlier conclusions made regarding the change in band gap position due to porosity or grain size.33
Fig. 14 Mott–Schottky diagram and band position of CZTS (a) nanocrystalline and (b) microcrystalline powder. Analysis of the Mott–Schottky plot of the CZTS powders were carried out under dark conditions in phosphate buffer solution by polarizing from 2 V to −2 V (vs.Ag/AgCl).

Surface modifications with noble metal such as Pt were observed to be useful for improved hydrogen evolution capability.1 Improvement was also observed when a CdS layer was deposited on a CZTS electrode. However, water splitting without biasing and without use of precious metals is still difficult using CZTS. An improvement in photocurrent was observed when porous CZTS photocathode was used for water splitting.34 A porous structure is useful to promote stronger light absorbance and to shorten the minority carrier diffusion length.34 Porous material allows more electrolyte insertion per unit area and therefore promotes ease of charge transport. Although a relatively small charge carrier diffusion length is a factor for high current response, the crystallinity of sample should be maintained or improved for better performance. A strategy to reduce valence band-offset, without affecting conduction band level was adopted to improve the performance of oxide materials by doping with nitrogen and sulfur. Use of antimony modified (antimony substituted for tin in CZTS) semiconductor (CAZS) can be potentially beneficial as its band gap is ∼2.15 eV.37 It was earlier observed that such an electrode shows a photocurrent response at ∼zero biasing voltage.37 The tops of the valence bands are generally shifted to higher potential energies when nitridation is performed. Thus using simple modifications with nitrogen or antimony, the CZTS band gap can be tuned for optimized performance.

6. Conclusions

In summary, we have demonstrated a Z-scheme system for CZTS-other photoanode combinations. Photoelectrochemical tests have been performed using CZTS photocathode as well as CZTS photocell in combination with TiO2, BiVO4, and WO3. It was observed that CZTS–TiO2 system performs reasonably well, either in case of photocathode or photocell. Solar to hydrogen conversion efficiency was calculated for this system in electrolyte containing Fe3+/Fe2+ and phosphate redox couple at near neutral pH condition. We have observed maximum STH efficiency ∼1.1% at biasing of ∼0.6 V, for CZTS photocell–TiO2. It was interesting to observe that STH efficiency was significant even at near zero biasing condition ∼0.5% for CZTS photocell–TiO2. The efficiency was significantly less (at least one order of magnitude) when CZTS photocathode–TiO2 system was utilized. EIS spectroscopy was also conducted to evaluate the effect of biasing on charge transfer activity of different photoelectrodes. All initial results convincingly indicate that the CZTS augmented water splitting system has a great potential for low cost hydrogen production. This material can be used with other oxygen evolving catalysts for single as well as dual compartment water splitting systems. Electrode based systems appears to be more efficient for water splitting compared to suspension based systems as estimated using preliminary experimental data.
