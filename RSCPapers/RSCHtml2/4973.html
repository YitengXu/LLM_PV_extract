Abstract

We summarize our recent explorations of photophysical mechanisms that may be utilized in solar cells with power conversion efficiency theoretically exceeding the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit. The dominant losses responsible for the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit are below band-gap and thermalization (hot carrier) losses; together, they account for >55% of the total absorbed solar energy. This perspective focuses on two photophysical mechanisms, hot carrier equilibration and carrier multiplication, which may be used to reduce these losses through their utilization in novel solar cell designs. For the implementation of a hot carrier solar cell, we discuss the necessity of hot carrier scattering as well as the photon flux challenge. Although recent experiments have demonstrated the feasibility of hot-electron extraction from photo-excited semiconductor nano-crystals, the photon flux challenge cannot be met in these materials. We propose graphene and related materials as potentially ideal chromophores for hot carrier solar cells. For the multi-exciton solar cell, we focus on the molecular analog called singlet fission. Recent experiments in our lab revealed a quantum coherent mechanism in which photo-excitation of the organic semiconductor pentacene or tetracene creates a quantum superposition of singlet exciton and multi-exciton states. This quantum superposition and the corresponding decoherence time (i.e., singlet fission time) are critical to the competing dynamics of charge or energy harvesting. We discuss design principles for solar cells based on singlet fission materials.

Broader context

We discuss two photophysical mechanisms, hot carrier equilibration and carrier multiplication, in molecular and nano materials and the feasibility for their implementation in solar cells with power conversion efficiency theoretically exceeding the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit. We point out the necessity of hot carrier scattering in realizing a hot carrier solar cell. The low photon flux from solar radiation puts stringent requirements on potential hot carrier chromophores. These requirements include sufficient carrier delocalization to length scales approaching Î¼m, efficient carrierâ€“carrier scattering, and inefficient carrierâ€“phonon scattering. We explore the potential of nano-materials, particularly graphene and related materials, as potentially ideal chromophores for hot carrier solar cells. For the carrier multiplication solar cell, we discuss multi-exciton generation in semiconductor nanocrystals and the molecular analog called singlet fission. The latter is particularly promising because of the high multiplication yield and the potential for designing and synthesizing new singlet fission molecules. Recent experiments in our lab revealed a quantum superposition mechanism for singlet exciton fission and the feasibility of extracting two electronâ€“hole pairs from the quantum superposition or the resulting two triplet excitons. We discuss design principles for solar cells based on singlet fission materials.

Introduction

Ever increasing energy demand and deleterious environmental effects of fossil fuels has led to a search for alternative energy sources.1,2 Solar energy has been argued as a viable alternative in part because of the abundance of solar radiation reaching the earth's surface.3 This perspective focuses on solar energy conversion through the use of photovoltaics (PVs) with emphasis on third generation PVs. These generations were characterized by Green based on the cost per peak watt of the solar cell.4 First generation PVs, comprising mainly of single pâ€“n junction crystalline materials such as gallium arsenide or silicon, are highly efficient but their cost may be too high to compete with fossil fuel, but recent manufacturing advances are reducing the gap and approaching grid parity.5 The use of thin film amorphous or polycrystalline materials in second generation PVs dramatically reduces cost at the expense of efficiency. Third generation PVs aim to maintain or lower the cost of second generation cells while dramatically improving efficiency by introducing novel cell designs and materials to reach a target price per peak watt of approximately $0.20.4,6

Before discussing concepts for third generation PV design, it is necessary to understand fundamental loss mechanisms in single junction solar cells. These losses are typically separated into two types: extrinsic and intrinsic. Unwanted recombination as a result of impurities, series resistance, and absorption from an inactive window layer are examples of extrinsic losses and can, in principle, be eliminated. Intrinsic losses, however, represent fundamental limitations of energy conversion with a given operating principle. Examples of intrinsic losses in single junction solar cells and a brief description of their origin are presented in the subsequent paragraph.

Detailed derivations of the thermodynamic origin of intrinsic losses in solar cells have been described in a number of articles6â€“9 and their results are summarized here. In its most basic form, the solar cell is a heat engine. If no new entropy is generated in doing work, then the power conversion efficiency is that of a Carnot engine operating with the sun as a heat source (âˆ¼6000 K) and the earth's surface (âˆ¼300 K) as a heat sink, or 95%.10 The solar cell is both an absorber and an emitter. Recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs decreases the total number of carriers that can be collected, reducing the overall cell efficiency by âˆ¼1%. Further losses are introduced by the angular inequality of incident and emitted radiation. The sun's rays are essentially parallel when they reach the earth, whereas the absorber reemits light isotropically. This mismatch in angle, called angular entropy or Boltzmann loss, lowers cell efficiency by another âˆ¼9%. The two largest loss channels are: (1) transparency of the solar cell absorber to photon energies below the band gap (EG) (âˆ¼25% loss for EG âˆ¼ 1.3 eV); and (2) thermalization losses (âˆ¼30%) where photons with energy greater than EG create hot electrons and holes that thermalize with the lattice before they are extracted at the band edges. Taking into account all of these losses has led us to the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit for solar-to-electric power conversion efficiency,11 which is âˆ¼32% at the optimal EG = 1.3 eV, and âˆ¼30% at EG = 1.1 eV for silicon. The result of this analysis as a function of band gap is summarized in Fig. 1. The current record for single crystal silicon solar cell is âˆ¼25% and that of gallium arsenide (EG = 1.4 eV) is âˆ¼28%.12

Among the intrinsic channels listed above, the Carnot and emission losses are limited by fundamental physics; Boltzmann, thermalization, and bandgap losses, however, may be reduced with device/material designs and new photophysical mechanisms. Solar concentration, for example, increases the solid angle of the incident light, effectively reducing angular entropy and the Boltzmann loss. The largest gains in efficiency, however, can be made by reducing thermalization and band gap losses. Tandem cells have proven to be an effective method for reducing thermalization and band gap losses by sequentially absorbing photons with decreasing energy in multiple pâ€“n junctions. Record efficiencies as high as 44% have been demonstrated for crystalline semiconductor tandem cells with solar concentration,12 but manufacturing costs are prohibitively high for large-scale applications.

A number of novel concepts have been proposed for third generation solar cells.6,13,14 In this perspective, we focus on the hot carrier solar cell (HCSC) and the multiple exciton generation (MEG) concepts that aim to reduce thermalization and band gap losses (Fig. 2B and C). These concepts are particularly attractive as they may be implemented with nano or molecular materials to meet the cost requirement of third-generation PVs. We focus on fundamental physical principles obtained from mechanistic studies in model systems. We also discuss challenges and opportunities in implementing these novel concepts. Another proposal for exceeding the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit is the intermediate band solar cell (Fig. 2D).15 In this approach, a narrow band of electronic states is introduced into the fundamental gap of the semiconductor material to allow below gap optical excitations. Attempts at introducing the intermediate bands include the use of discrete impurity levels,16 mini-bands from quantum dots,13,17 and highly mismatched alloys.18 This active research area will not be discussed here.

While this perspective focuses on the feasibility of hot carrier and multi-exciton concepts to exceed the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit, there are formidable challenges in implementing these concepts. Molecular and nano materials are intrinsically disordered. The presence of structural and energetic disorder (both static and dynamic) results in not only charge carrier trapping, space charge accumulation, and electronâ€“hole recombination, but also fundamental limits in photovoltaic efficiency.19 In addition, the presence of multiple interfaces for charge carrier separation and collection adds to the complexity and challenge.20â€“23 These materials and engineering issues are of critical importance, but will not be covered within the limited scope of the present perspective.

Hot carrier solar cells

The necessity of carrierâ€“carrier scattering

In a conventional PV cell, hot electrons and holes are generated by photons with energy above the band gap. These hot carriers lose their excess energies to phonons and quickly cool to the band edges before they are captured (Fig. 2A). A hot carrier PV cell captures the carriers with excess energy.24â€“26 As illustrated in Fig. 2B, the central concept of HCSCs is the partial separation of the hot carrier energy from the thermal bath. At a sufficient density of hot carriers within a scattering volume and if hot carrier cooling by phonon scattering is slower than carrierâ€“carrier scattering, the hot electrons (holes) may reach a quasi-equilibrium in the electronic degrees of freedom described by an electron (hole) temperature, Te (Th), which is higher than that of the lattice or the surrounding environment. For an HCSC, a lower band gap absorbs more of the solar spectrum and gives a higher power conversion efficiency. In the limit of zero band gap and maximum solar concentration, a theoretical efficiency as high as 85% has been predicted.6,25 In essence, the HCSC attempts to recover all the energy from bandgap and thermalization losses.

In the implementation of an HCSC, electrons with energy in excess of the Fermi level of the electron conductor will be lost after electron transfer. The same argument applies to the hot hole. To minimize energy loss within the electron (hole) conductor, the hot electrons (holes) should ideally be extracted in a narrow energy window near the Fermi level of each. This requires the presence of an interfacial electronic energy level, which serves as a resonant channel, similar to a narrow â€œband-passâ€ filter, for hot carrier transfer. While an HCSC may appear to be equivalent to a conventional cell with an effectively wider band gap defined by the energy differences of the hot electron and hot hole extraction levels, this is a misconception. Consider a photoexcited electron above the conduction band minimum but below the hot electron extraction level. In the conventional picture, this conduction band electron cannot be extracted. However, in the presence of electronâ€“electron scattering, the electron with energy above the hot electron extraction level can scatter with one below to bring both into resonance with the extraction level and increase photo-current.

These arguments illustrate the central importance of carrierâ€“carrier scattering in achieving the high efficiency of an HCSC. Extracting hot carriers before their excess energy is lost, e.g., in a conventional pâ€“n junction with ultrathin carrier transport layers,27 does not by itself qualify as an HCSC. To realize the separation of electron (hole) â€œtemperaturesâ€ from that of the phonon bath, one must ensure that elastic electronâ€“electron (holeâ€“hole) scattering, including impact ionization addressed below, is more efficient than the inelastic electronâ€“ or holeâ€“phonon scattering. However, electronâ€“phonon scattering is highly efficient in most semiconductor materials, with hot electron lifetimes typically in the picosecond range. Ensuring sufficient electronâ€“electron scattering within the inelastic lifetime of hot electrons puts stringent requirement on the excitation density and the photon flux reaching the light absorbing material.

The photon flux limitation

While the transient separation of electronic â€œtemperaturesâ€ from that of the phonon bath can be easily achieved with pulsed laser irradiation, this is not the case for solar radiation. The power density of solar radiation incident on the surface of the earth is âˆ¼1 kW mâˆ’2, corresponding to a spectrally integrated photon flux of âˆ¼106 photons cmâˆ’2 psâˆ’1 in the broad range from near-IR to UV. Extensive efforts are underway to explore molecular and nano materials for solar energy conversion because they may be compatible with low-cost PV production. However, it is extremely difficult for these material systems to meet the requirements on electronic delocalization and high carrier velocity. A solar concentration of >106 is needed to obtain more than one excitation within the hot electron lifetime. Hot electron dynamics in bulk semiconductors

It is an exceptional challenge to find appropriate materials with attributes for the HCSC: low band gap to absorb a broad solar spectrum, high carrier mobility and delocalization to allow efficient carrierâ€“carrier scattering, as well as slow electronâ€“phonon scattering to enable long hot carrier lifetimes.

There are a number of low bandgap materials that may meet the first requirement. Examples include Ge, InN, and GaSb with Eg âˆ¼ 0.7 eV, or PbS, PbSe, and PbTe with Eg âˆ¼ 0.2â€“0.4 eV. In the limit, zero bandgap materials are theoretically the most efficient absorbers for HCSCs.

The second requirement may be met in materials with very high carrier mobility, e.g., crystalline GaAs. As an example, a photo-excited electron at âˆ¼0.35 eV above the conduction band minimum in single crystal GaAs has a diffusion constant of D âˆ¼ 500 cm2 sâˆ’1 and a lifetime of Ï„ âˆ¼ 10 ps, corresponding to a mean-free-path of âˆ¼1 Î¼m.28,29 With realistic solar concentration (102 to 103), it is possible to achieve hot carrier scattering and transient equilibration necessary for HCSCs. While the estimation presented above predicts that hot electron (hole) equilibration is possible under realistic solar concentration in semiconductor materials with high carrier mobility, this prediction still needs experimental verification. Previous studies on hot carrier scattering in semiconductors have all been carried out with laser excitation, at excitation densities many orders of magnitude higher than that achievable with solar radiation. Under such high intensity laser excitation, hot carriers are known to scatter with each other efficiently to yield a hot electron (hole) equilibrium on time scale scales as short as 100 fs, which is at least one order of magnitude faster than the time scale of electronâ€“phonon scattering.28

The requirement for long hot electron lifetimes is difficult to achieve because efficient electronâ€“phonon scattering is intrinsic to most semiconductor materials. This is particularly true for polar semiconductors where the FrÃ¶hlich interaction results in intra-band cooling of hot carriers by the emission of longitudinal optical (LO) phonons, for both energy and momentum conservation, on time scales of picoseconds or less. Decay into optical phonons, however, does not preclude the possibility of using bulk semiconductors as hot carrier absorbers. This is because further cooling of the excitation requires the decay of optical phonons into acoustic phonons through the anharmonic or Klemens mechanism30 in which one optical phonon decays into two acoustic phonons with nearly the same energy but opposite wave vector to satisfy energy and momentum conservation. This phonon scattering channel may be suppressed if there is a phonon band gap with the acoustic phonon energy less than half of the optical phonon energy. These phonon band gaps exist in some binary semiconductors with a large difference in atomic mass, such as in InN, BiN or SnO,31 or in carbon nanomaterials.32 Suppression of the Klemens mechanism not only slows carrier cooling but would also allow energy scattered into optical phonons to be regained when optical phonons scatter with electrons again.33

Hot electron extraction from semiconductor nanomaterials

Semiconductor quantum dots (QDs), particularly colloidal QDs, are being actively explored for applications in solar energy conversion thanks to their compatibility with low-cost processing and tunable electronic and optical properties.34â€“37 One argument for the use of QDs in HCSCs is the possibility of slow hot carrier cooling due to the â€œphonon bottleneckâ€. This refers to the effect caused by the discretization of electronic bands from quantum confinement which leads to the requirement of multiple phonon emission for intra-band electronic transitions.38,39 Experimental evidence for the phonon bottleneck in colloidal QDs, however, has been scarce due to various hot carrier cooling mechanisms other than multi-phonon emission, such as eâ€“h energy transfer, surface state assisted cooling or recombination.40â€“50 Only when these cooling channels are minimized through careful engineering of coreâ€“shell structures can much longer hot electron lifetimes be observed.51 Nevertheless, this kind of phonon bottleneck likely exists only near the band-bottom (e.g., 1p) where quantum confinement is effective.

To investigate hot electron relaxation and possibility of a phonon bottleneck, we recently applied femtosecond time-resolved two-photon photoemission (TR-2PPE) spectroscopy to directly determine hot electron relaxation dynamics in thin solid films of PbSe QDs capped with ethanedithiol molecules.52 In a TR-2PPE experiment, an intermediate state is resonantly excited by a pump photon and after a controlled time delay photoionized for detection by a probe photon. Fig. 3 shows a TR-2PPE spectrum from 5.4 Â± 0.3 nm PbSe QDs (optical gap Eg = 0.70 eV) excited with a photon energy of 2.20 eV. The data directly shows the ultrafast cooling on the sub-ps time scale of initially excited hot electron at âˆ¼0.8 eV above the conduction band minimum. The absence of a phonon bottleneck is not surprising because the dynamics of highly excited carriers are essentially bulk-like, as shown by Jonas and coworkers based on a detailed analysis of photon energy dependent oscillator strength of PbS QDs.53 These authors argued that hot carriers excited with photon energy âˆ¼3EG are localized on the 1â€“2 nm length scale, which is shorter than or on the order of the diameter of the QDs. These hot carriers move like independent carriers in the bulk and scatter with the QD surface less frequently than they do with optical phonons.

The major obstacle to using QDs as hot carrier absorbers is the photon flux limitation. To achieve hot carrier scattering within their limited lifetimes and under solar radiation, one would need delocalization on the Î¼m length scale. In principle, strong inter-QD coupling may lead to the formation of hot carrier minibands in QD solids, but the inevitable presence of intrinsic disorder makes this unlikely. Sources of disorder in semiconductor QDs may include variations in size, shape, surface termination, capping molecule distribution, and inter-QD arrangement. Any or all of these factors can disrupt the formation of hot carrier minibands. Interestingly, band-like transport has been reported in QD thin films with strong inter-QD coupling resulting from surface chemical control.54,55 It remains to be seen if minibands for hot carrier equilibration and transport may be achieved in such strongly coupled QD thin films.

Graphene as the hot carrier chromophore

A single layer of graphene absorbs 2.3% of incident light, nearly independent of light wavelength in the IR-visible range (deviation only in the far-IR or UV range). Given the high absorption cross section near complete absorption is possible with a small amount of material (a few tens to hundred monolayers). The very high carrier velocities (â‰¥106 m sâˆ’1) give rise to a large scattering volume (area) as well as long hot electron (hole) mean free path on the order of hundreds of nanometers.56,57

Of particular importance to hot carrier thermalization in graphene is its unique band structure. The linear dispersion at the k-point means that energy and momentum are conserved simultaneously in carrierâ€“carrier scattering events, including scattering between a hot electron and a valence band electron below the Fermi level which yields two hot electrons or the elastic scattering between two hot electrons (Fig. 5). The former is often called carrier multiplication. Indeed, a number of theoretical studies have predicted efficient carrier multiplication in graphene.58â€“60 The dynamic competition between carrier multiplication and the Auger process leads to rapid thermalization of hot electrons (holes) in a few tens of femtoseconds in optically excited graphene.61â€“64 In electrically biased graphene, light emission characteristic of black-body radiation at the electronic temperatures as high as 1000 K have been reported,65 and hot carriers responsible for photo-thermoelectric effects are believed to dominate the response of graphene photodetectors.66â€“68

In contrast to the ultrafast equilibration of hot electrons (holes), electronâ€“phonon scattering in graphene is much slower, giving rise to a mean free path of âˆ¼1 Î¼m for hot electrons.56 While hot carrier equilibration with optical phonons occurs in âˆ¼1 ps, further cooling by acoustic phonon emission can take as long as 100 ps, particularly as the hot electron (hole) energy approaches the charge neutral point on the Dirac cone and further cooling becomes inhibited by the phonon band gap.58â€“60 Thus, we can identify two time windows for the efficient extraction of hot carriers: (1) 10 fs < t < 1 ps, in which hot electrons (holes) equilibrate among themselves but are not equilibrated with optical phonons; (2) 1 ps < t < 100 ps, in which hot carrierâ€“optical phonon equilibrium is not cooled down further by acoustic phonon emission.

Hot carrier equilibration may be possible under solar radiation as illustrated with the following parameters. With a realistic solar concentration of 104, the photon flux is 10âˆ’4 photons nmâˆ’2 psâˆ’1 in the IR-UV range. For a graphene nanostructure of 1 Î¼m2, the optical absorption cross-section and carrier scattering area are of the same order. There are >100 hot carriers in this scattering volume (area) within the hot carrier lifetime of âˆ¼1 ps. Based on this analysis, we predict that hot carrier scattering and equilibrium is possible; this prediction requires experimental verification.

Despite the potential of graphene as a hot carrier chromophore, its implementation into a working solar cell remains difficult. From a practical point of view, methods for the manufacturing of monolayer graphene on an industrial scale have not emerged. There is also a physical limitation of using monolayer graphene adsorbed to another bulk semiconductor surface. Hot electrons (holes) in graphene are near the K point in momentum space, while the conduction band minima of common semiconductor electron acceptors, such as TiO2 or ZnO, are at the Î“ point. Transferring a hot electron from adsorbed monolayer graphene to the conduction band of the acceptor may be accompanied by scattering with phonons to compensate for the momentum change. Consequently, there may exist a momentum barrier for hot electron transfer.

Both obstacles discussed above may be overcome with graphene nanomaterials, such as chemically synthesized or processed graphene quantum dots (G-QDs) or ribbons. Chemical methods for the synthesis of graphene nano structures can potentially be scaled up to industrial scale69 and the nano size relaxes the momentum requirement in interfacial charge transfer. In preliminary experiments, we demonstrated hot electron injection from chemically synthesized G-QDs, each containing 48 fused benzene rings, anchored to the TiO2 (110) surface via carboxyl linkers.70 These G-QDs,71 also called large polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons,72 have been shown to possess hot electron lifetimes as long as 100 ps.73 Such a long hot electron lifetime is comparable to the phonon bottleneck. Their ability as a light absorber has been recently demonstrated in dye-sensitized solar cells.71

While the demonstration from the GQD/TiO2 interface established the feasibility of ultrafast hot-electron extraction, the molecular size is too small to allow for multiple excitations in each molecule under solar radiation and the extraction of the carriers is not energy selective (i.e., lack of a bandpass filter). To develop realistic HCSC absorbers, we need to increase the size of graphene structures and incorporate energy-selective states for charge extraction. These challenges must be met.

Multi-exciton solar cells

Carrier multiplication in bulk semiconductors

Carrier multiplication (also called impact ionization) was originally predicted and discovered in crystalline inorganic semiconductors.75â€“77 A carrier multiplication solar cell aims to capture part of the loss from solar radiation by converting the excess energy of hot carriers into additional electronâ€“hole pairs, increasing photo-current (Fig. 2C).

Carrier multiplication is the inverse of the Auger process. It involves the scattering of a hot electron in the conduction band with an electron in the valence band to create an additional electronâ€“hole pair (a similar process exists for a hot hole). The scattering process requires both energy and momentum conservation and occurs most efficiently in particular regions of the band structure. Queisser and coworkers studied the carrier multiplication problem in detail and proposed optimal conditions, including the lowest band gap being indirect while the next gap being direct, strongly absorbing, and asymmetric.78 The last condition, i.e., asymmetry in electron and hole mass, ensures that one carrier of the eâ€“h pair receives most of the excess energy from a high-energy photon. While optimal materials have not been found, crystalline silicon meets part of these requirements and has been the subject of research on carrier multiplication solar cells. Internal quantum efficiencies exceeding 100% at photon energies â‰¥4 eV have been demonstrated for crystalline silicon solar cells.79 However, the high photon energy threshold (âˆ¼4Eg) means that there is minimal gain in power conversion efficiency with carrier multiplication. As pointed out by Queisser in 2010, â€œefficiency enhancement is found to be present but disappointingly smallâ€.78

Carrier multiplication in semiconductor QDs

Nozik proposed that semiconductor quantum dots might exhibit higher carrier multiplication or, as it is often called in quantum dots, MEG, than corresponding processes in the bulk.13 Theoretically, a higher carrier multiplication efficiency in quantum confined materials may be attributed to the relaxed requirement on momentum conservation and the enhanced Coulomb interaction between excitons and multiexcitons.80â€“83

Experimental measurements of high carrier multiplication efficiencies have been reported for a number of QD systems.84â€“94 While the exceptionally high quantum efficiencies in early reports have been revised on the discovery of experimental artifacts,95,96 various experimental measurements have now converged to show modest MEG yields in several QD systems, with threshold photon energy âˆ¼2.5Eg.97,98 Contrasting the above reports, several research groups also reported the absence of enhanced carrier multiplication in QDs;52,99â€“105 these studies pointed to bulk-like properties of hot carriers in semiconductor QDs.

Solar cells with external quantum efficiency exceeding 100% at high photon energies have been reported by two research groups106,107 but the actual effects of multiexciton generations on the power conversion efficiency in QD solar cells are small. One potential way of increasing the carrier multiplication efficiency is to use anisotropic nanostructures such as nano-rods108,109 or platelets,81 where the enhanced Coulomb potential may increase the excitonâ€“multiexciton coupling and, as a result, carrier multiplication yield. A recent experimental report92 and computational prediction94 of high carrier multiplication yields in coupled silicon QDs, with threshold energy near 2Eg, also suggest a promising direction. Whether the application of carrier multiplications in QDs to solar cells can substantially increase the power conversion efficiency97,98 or not110,111 remains an issue of discussion. It is not covered further here; interested readers are referred to the above publications for details. In the following, we focus on the molecular analogue of carrier multiplication called singlet fission (SF). The high quantum yield (âˆ¼200%) of singlet fission in a few molecules under visible light excitation offers exciting potentials for implementation in solar cells.112,113

Singlet fission in organic semiconductors

Singlet fission, the conversion of a singlet into two triplet excitons, was discovered nearly 50 years ago.114,115 One of the main reasons for the high quantum yield is that singlet fission can be both energy and spin allowed. Due to the large exchange energy in molecules, the excitation energy of a triplet can be half or less than that of a singlet. Two coupled triplets can have total singlet spin and singlet fission is spin-allowed. The commonly accepted theoretical framework for singlet fission comes from that for the inverse process of tripletâ€“triplet annihilation116â€“118 and is illustrated in Fig. 7. Here, the electronic coupling of two adjacent chromaphores produces what is known as a correlated triplet pair 1(TT) or a multiexciton (ME) state with overall singlet spin. Subsequent loss of spin coherence within the ME state results in two independent triplet excitons (2T1).

Strategies for harvesting singlet fission

Despite an initial suggestion that singlet fission could be exploited in photovoltaic systems,121 there has been little research activity in implementing the idea. This may be partly attributed to observation of high singlet fission yields in only a small number of systems, namely crystalline tetracene, pentacene, and more recently, 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran.112,113 Enthusiasm for singlet fission grew again following reminders122,123 of the phenomenon's relevance to photovoltaic systems and a quantitative analysis by Hanna and Nozik124 showing that the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit of a conventional single junction solar cell could be increased from 33% to 44% if a sensitizer capable of quantitative singlet fission was incorporated into the device.

To achieve the high theoretical limit, one must use at least two chromophores: a singlet fission chromophore which absorbs high photon energies and produces two lower energy triplet excitons and a conventional chromophore absorbing lower energy photons to produce one exciton (singlet or triplet), as shown in Fig. 9. Note that for carrier multiplication or MEG in inorganic materials discussed above, the two chromophores can be the same material. For singlet fission, the two chromophores can be in contact with a common electron acceptor, such as C60 in a molecular solar cell or TiO2 in a dye-sensitized solar cell, for the harvesting of three eâ€“h pairs from two photons. Chromophore 2 can also be the electron acceptor as demonstrated recently for pentecene/inorganic QD bilayer solar cells.125,126 Alternatively, one can use a trilayer structure containing a singlet fission material as a top layer, complimented with a conventional chromophore for the middle layer that absorbs lower energy photons and also transports triplet excitons generated in the singlet fission process to the bottom layer which consists of a common electron acceptor.127,128 The long-lived triplet excitons transfer via the Dexter mechanism involving direct electronic coupling.129

Insight into the competing loss mechanisms can be gained by looking at the interfacial electronic energy level alignment in each of the strategies outlined above. We first consider the interface between a singlet fission donor and an electron acceptor for electron transfer from the ME or T1 state. Creating an energetic driving force for electron transfer from ME or T1 also results in a favourable situation for electron transfer from S1. Consequently, single electron transfer can compete with singlet fission and multielectron transfer130 and is considered a loss channel in a photovoltaic device as it reduces the total photocurrent and therefore power conversion efficiency. These competitions depend critically on the dynamic time scales involved, as is evident in comparative studies on pentacene and tetracene.119,120

Fig. 10 summarizes the time constants for singlet fission and electron transfer processes at pentacene/C60 and tetracene/C60 interfaces, as determined from our recent TR-2PPE experiments.119,130 These kinetic parameters were obtained for pentacene or tetracene in direct contact with C60. For pentacene, the exoergic singlet fission process is ultrafast (0.1 ps), faster than electron transfer to C60 (0.4 ps) and independent of whether the pentacene layer is in direct contact with or away from C60. Two-electron transfer from the triplet pair (MEâ€² or 2T1) dominates interfacial dynamics and this is a favourable scenario for solar cells. Note that, electron transfer from the nascent triplet pair (MEâ€²) is 0.4 ps, faster than that from individual triplets on longer time scales (5 ps).

In contrast to pentacene, singlet fission in tetracene is much slower (7 ps) and is not competitive with interfacial electron transfer (0.5 ps). As a result, direct electron transfer from the [S1 â‡” ME] quantum superposition state dominates. Only a small percentage of the exciton population can complete the fission process, followed by two-electron transfer to C60. Because electron transfer occurs from the quantum superposition state, both one-electron transfer from S1 and two-electron transfer from ME are possible. From the directly monitoring of charge transfer states,130 we conclude that 40% of the quantum superposition state is responsible for two-electron transfer to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of C60 while 60% undergoes one-electron transfer (from S1) to the C60 LUMO + 1.

A similar dynamic competition is also expected in the trilayer strategy discussed above. For the efficient transfer of triplet excitons from the blue-absorbing singlet fission layer (donor 1) through the red-absorbing conventional chromophore layer (donor 2) to a common electron acceptor, the electronic energy alignment at the donor/donor interface must be such that S1 in donor 1 should be substantially higher than that of donor 2. As a result, electron transfer or singlet energy transfer from donor 1 to donor 2 should compete with triplet energy transfer.

To minimize the loss channels of one-electron transfer or singlet energy transfer (in the case of the tri-layer approach) when singlet fission is slow, one needs to minimize the interface between the singlet fission chromophore and the electron acceptor or the second conventional chromophore by increasing the thickness of the former. In a relatively thick layer of singlet fission chromophore, a higher proportion of excitations would be created away from the interface and would have an increased probability of undergoing fission, followed by triplet diffusion to the interface and electron or triplet energy transfer. While increasing the singlet fission layer thickness would benefit two-electron transfer from triplet pairs, it must be balanced against the diffusion length of the triplet states and tripletâ€“triplet annihilation.

A slow singlet fission process is generally undesirable as it leads to the competition between one- and two-electron harvesting. However, we may also take advantage of a long-lived [S1 â‡” ME] quantum superposition state. If we design an ideal electron acceptor which allows two electron transfer from the triplet pair (e.g., T1 in resonance with LUMO) while keeping one-electron transfer slow (e.g., S1 off resonance with LUMO + 1), the effective singlet fission yield can actually be enhanced in the presence of such an electron acceptor. This is because the effective singlet fission yield is proportional to the ratio between the decay rate of the ME state and that of the S1 state.80

Singlet fission solar cells

How can we build solar cells that incorporate singlet fission to (theoretically) exceed the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit? We do not have a definitive answer yet, but the mechanistic studies presented above suggest a few design principles. At the present time, high singlet fission yields (approaching 200% quantum efficiency) that may be of value to solar cells have been discovered in only three materials, pentacene, tetracene, and the newly designed 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran. These are clearly not sufficient as engineers designing such a solar cell must consider not only singlet fission yield, but also a host of other chemical/physical properties, such as strong light absorption, long-term chemical (and photochemical) stability, sufficient charge-carrier mobility, and appropriate interface formation. We need to greatly expand the toolbox of molecules with high singlet fission efficiency. Some of the principles may include:
Energetics. For efficient singlet fission, the energetic requirement of E(S1) â‰¥ 2E(T1) is usually necessary. A slightly exoergic singlet fission process ensures that the reverse process of tripletâ€“triplet annihilation is less efficient. This is particularly important when we use a relatively thick singlet fission layer and long distance triplet diffusion is required. In addition, the energetics of other spin states must also be considered, as pointed out by Michl and coworkers.112,113
Electronic coupling. What gives rise to strong Coulomb coupling between a singlet exciton and a multi-exciton state in a neighboring molecular pair is not transparent from chemical intuition. Michl and coworkers have addressed in detail this critical issue.112,113 However, the involvement of charge transfer states as a potentially dominant channel for singlet fission, as well as the role of excitonic or electronic delocalization in crystalline materials, complicates theoretical analysis.131
Dynamics. The results presented in the previous section emphasize the critical importance of competing time scales. In principle, ultrafast singlet fission exemplified by the exoergic process in pentacene is desirable. However, a slower singlet fission process, such as that in tetracene, offers the enticing possibility for us to control or enhance the effective yield of singlet fission by directly extracting two electrons (holes) from the ME state in the quantum superposition.
Electron acceptors. Most studies on organic photovoltaics use fullerenes as electron acceptors. There are obvious limitations with this molecule, among them, the low optical absorption cross section. The tetracene/C60 example also shows the undesirable presence of higher lying electron acceptor levels (e.g., LUMO + 1) responsible for one-electron transfer from the S1 state. Recent experiments demonstrated charge separation and model solar cells using pentacene/QD interfaces.125,126 Here the QDs are not only tunable electron acceptors but also strong light absorbers at long wavelengths.
Solar cell structure. The presence of charge and energy transfer from the S1 state can compete with singlet fission. Thus, the interfacial area between a singlet fission chromophore and an electron acceptor or a second chromophore needs to be minimized. This argues against the use of distributed interfaces, such as that in bulk heterojunction solar cells. Indeed, recent successful demonstrations of singlet fission based solar cells all employ bilayer or multilayer structures.125â€“127 The thickness of the singlet fission layer should be chosen based on a balanced consideration of light absorption, singlet fission, triplet diffusion, tripletâ€“triplet annihilation, and charge/energy transfer at interfaces.

Conclusions

This perspective offers our views on exceeding the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit with novel photophysics. We discuss two approaches based on the harvesting of hot carriers or multiple excitons in molecular or nano-materials. The HCSC concept offers the highest gain in power conversion efficiency but is exceedingly difficult to implement due to a number of challenges, in particular the photon flux challenge. We propose graphene and related two-dimensional materials as potential means to meet these challenging. The multi-exciton solar cell concept offers more modest gains, but is also more promising in actual implementation. Recent developments in solar cells based on both MEG in semiconductor nanocrystals106 or singlet fission in organic semiconductors132 have shown external quantum efficiency exceeding 100% in limited spectral ranges. We believe singlet fission is most promising in this regard, because of the known high quantum efficiency (200%) in three molecular systems112,113 and the potential of designing/synthesizing new molecules. Based on recent mechanistic studies, we discuss design principles for the implementation of singlet fission in solar cells. Much lies ahead in the quest to exceed the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit.133
