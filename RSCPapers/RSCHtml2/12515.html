Abstract

To advance the progress of photoelectrolysis, various promising devices integrated with p- and n-type photocatalysts and dye sensitized photoelectrodes have been systematically studied. This review discusses, from theory to practice, an integration strategy for state-of-the-art dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) with potential p- and n-type photo-electrocatalysts or directly with dye sensitized photoanodes and cathodes for hydrogen and oxygen production through water splitting. Thorough insight into the theoretical approach which systematically drives the photoelectrolysis reaction directly or in a coupled mode, with diverse configurations of DSSCs and other photovoltaic (PV) cells, is crucial to understand the underlying fundamental concepts and elucidate trends in such reactions, and will serve as a guide to design new electrocatalysts and their integration with new PV devices, while simultaneously underlining major gaps that are required to address the challenges. Likewise, challenges, opportunities and frontiers in tandem and hybrid perovskite electrolysis processes are also discoursed in the present tutorial review. We illustrate our analysis by encompassing these integrated systems to photo-electrolysis, artificial photosynthesis such as CO2 conversion into value-added chemical reduction-products, where advancements in new catalysts and solution-processed inexpensive PV devices can certainly enrich the overall performance of the renewable production of solar fuels, including solar driven carbonaceous fuels.

1. Introduction

In recent years, the significance of electrochemistry has been recognized for the development of next generation energy based devices. This view was also endorsed by the World Economic Forum by choosing liquid fuels from sunshine as one of the emerging technologies in 2017. With the seminal work of Edmond Becquerel on the photoelectric effect, the research community has been exploiting the idea of utilizing sunlight to harvest electric power or liquid fuels for nearly two centuries. Carbon free energy is now of utmost importance for the stabilization of CO2 emission to an acceptable or preindustrial level. The possibility of converting solar energy directly into chemical energy stored in fuels provides a solution to the challenges of intermittency in renewable energy sources, which suffer from issues such as effective storage and transport. Photoelectrochemistry provides an attractive option to produce carbon neutral renewable products by utilizing suitable electrocatalysts. Light absorption in the semiconductor depletion layer allows electrical charge formation and separation as electronhole pairs. In the case of an n-type semiconductor layer, the holes travel to the surface, while the electrons are transported to the semiconductor layer, and the reverse process can be achieved in the case of a p-type semiconductor. The oxidizing and reducing agents available in the electrolytes capture injected photo-generated carriers and facilitate the reactions. Absorption of one photon produces one electronhole pair, which ideally makes the quantum yield value = 1. Charge-transport limitations are currently seen as an obstacle in these systems, which can be partially solved by the addition of protic species in the corresponding electrolytes. In a similar fashion, through light-assisted photo-electrochemical reactions, several renewable products can be realized by elaborating cost-effective photoelectrodes.

In the presence of sunlight, plants have generated their food by storing chemical energy for over the last 2 billion years through the photosynthetic process. By adopting similar pathways, and mimicking the photosynthetic process, liquid fuels can be generated by harvesting solar energy. Renewable products, such as hydrogen, which is the simplest form of energy carrier, can be produced by means of either photoelectrochemical cells by performing water splitting based on direct photo-excitation at the electrodes or electrolysis cells coupled to photovoltaic (PV) devices in a self-sustainable manner. In the case of water splitting,1 an applied voltage of ca. 1.5 V for current densities of the order of 100 A m2 or higher, i.e. of the same order of magnitude as those provided by solar cells. In accordance with the current density, a prerequisite to provide the thermodynamic driving force (1.23 V) and the required overpotentials at the electrode/electrolyte interfaces, as well as the ohmic losses in the electrode materials and the electrolyte; in particular, the four-electron oxygen evolution reaction involving the coupling of several electrochemical and chemical steps is a kinetically uphill task. It is difficult to supply such voltage by a single PV cell at the maximum power point. Coupling of more than one solar cell to one or more electrolysis cells will be essential. In this respect, solar cells providing open-circuit voltages (Voc) exceeding 1 V are preferred candidates; introductory concepts on this issue are presented in the Appendix (ESI). The possibility of providing the required power using multi-junction solar cells, for example based on IIIV semiconductors, or using Cu(In,Ga)(S,Se)2(CIGS)-based solar cells,2 was exploited in the past; for example, two such cells in series are sufficient for water electrolysis as compared to three amorphous silicon cells, but in most of the cases the low photovoltage remains a challenge. With the recent success of perovskite solar cells (PSCs), and by tweaking the halide content, i.e. in bromide-based PSCs, a Voc of approximately 1.5 V can be easily achieved in a single junction cell.1

In the case of water-splitting, noble metal and noble metal oxide-based electrocatalysts are preferred due to their low charge-transfer overpotentials and good corrosion stability, particularly in acidic electrolytes. However, for future large-scale applications, the development of efficient and stable electrocatalysts based on earth-abundant materials, e.g. Fe, Ni, and C, is of paramount importance. In an ideal case, for the techno-economic feasibility, both the catalysts for hydrogen and oxygen evolution should be active in the same electrolyte; however, the recent development of bipolar membranes has enabled the efficient operation of electrolysers with solutions of different pHs at the anode and the cathode.

The generation and charge separation of photoexcited electrons will not be sufficient to allow the functioning of photoelectrochemical devices. For this, the generated species need to be channeled in such a way that association with an electrocatalyst can be achieved in order to facilitate the electrochemical reaction of interest. The cell in which the photoelectrode forms a charge-extracting junction in conjugation with an electrolyte by achieving equilibrium, thereby eliminating the need for a built-in pn junction, is termed a photoelectrosynthetic cell. In this cell, when a p-type semiconductor comes into contact with an electrolyte, electrons or holes are exchanged from the electrolyte to the semiconductor, resulting in a built-in potential. This effect has the potential for implementation on a large scale; however, photoelectrosynthetic cells need to be filled with an electrolyte, which should be replaced periodically or monitored for degradation. In spite of this realistic approach, monitoring and handling large volumes of liquid electrolytes make photoelectrochemical devices impractical currently.

To overcome such issues, the strategy for utilizing PV cells for electrical current generation, which can then be fed to a separate electrolysis device for fuel generation, can be a viable option. This process, differentiated from photoelectrochemical cell operation with directly illuminated semiconductor/electrolyte interfaces, is also being exploited in a so-called artificial leaf system, where the PV element is immersed into the electrolyte or is placed in close proximity. In this case, chemical species are transported from the solar absorber to the storage site, while in a system relying on light harvesting by PV cells, electrons are transported. A PV-driven based system can intensify the value of current density and thus minimize the amount of catalyst and membranes used.

There is a trade-off between these two approaches. The transport of fluids is cheaper than that of electrical power. Also, electrical power transportation is prone to losses, while the artificial leaf process was suggested to be cost competitive. With the design and integration of a multifunction system, a facile chemical approach can be intended, which can allow for a further diminishment of the cost for solar fuel conversion. To achieve this target, new photoelectrodes, appropriate PV devices and earth-abundant electrocatalysts are being extensively exploited (ref. 1 and 2 and references therein).

Performing the photoelectrochemical reduction of CO2 to yield fuels is another competitive way to obtain renewable energy and simultaneously close the anthropogenic carbon cycle. Despite the uphill electrochemical and associated chemical kinetics of the participating processes.3 This will also provide a solution to long-term energy storage while contributing to a balance of the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere. The reaction pathways can be altered to produce a range of carbon-based products. Research efforts are being made towards increasing the yield of such targeted products to make it cost competitive, with particular emphasis on designing efficient and selective catalysts for electrochemical reduction. Some of the reported electrocatalysts, which are suitable for different reactions, are mainly inactive towards CO2 reduction. For this purpose, activated metal surfaces are being introduced to promote this reaction.13 To understand the underlying process and to control the catalytic activity of such a designed surface, the understanding of the related mechanistic process is of paramount importance.

Two categories of such oxygen evolution catalysts (OECs) have been investigated: molecular OECs and heterogeneous microdispersed OECs. Firstly, molecular OECs will be discussed.

In the case of molecular OECs, DSPELCs are widely investigated by the inorganic chemistry of oxygen evolution in homogeneous or micro-heterogeneous systems, often involving the addition of a strong oxidant as sacrificial electron donor species like Ce4+. The most efficient molecule (OEC) for DSPELCs utilizes ruthenium (Ru-OEC), as reported in several works of the Sun group,7,9,12 the Imahori group7,12 and the Meyer group.23

Recently, Eom et al.25 proposed an organic metal-free dye, 2,2-(benzo[c][1,2,5]-thiadiazole-4,7-diyl)bis(thiophene-3-carboxylic acid) (T2-BTD), for water oxidation. The particular aspect in the studies of Meyer and collaborators13,23 was the use of coreshell type PEs. SnO2 particles were coated with a very thin TiO2 layer (5 nm thickness) that was deposited by atomic layer deposition, which then acts as the active sites for dye chemisorption. The advantage of this particular construction is that the dark recombination current can be decreased due to the fact that the conduction band edge of SnO2 is lower than that of TiO2. This builds an effective barrier to electron transfer from SnO2 to TiO2 as long as EF(PE) lies below Ecb, a condition that is normally fulfilled under the conditions of DSPELC operation.

6. Tandem n/dye-p/dye electrolysis devices

In addition to the pursuit of new and promising materials, device architecture plays a very significant role in renewable hydrogen generation (artificial photosynthesis), which can probably replace conventional electrolysis limited by the use of precious metals. There are basic and advanced device configurations reported, which include three electrode photoelectrochemical cells, photocatalysis and externally biased oxygen evolution and hydrogen evolution catalysts with PSCs, integrative DSPELCs and more recently tandem devices.1,2,36 In tandem cells, two types of photoreactions occur, which require double the number of photons at a single photo-electrode; however, well configured tandem devices offer a broader solar spectral range and ameliorated efficiency. In photo-electrochemical tandem devices, the combination of the photoanode and photocathode results in two distinct water redox half-reactions. Subsequently, the photoanode and photocathode must be n-type and p-type semiconductors with desired ohmic contacts.

Overall, the daunting challenge for hydrogen production is to decrease the cost of the hydrogen production to allow the new technologies for practical applications. In this aspect, there are at least three major requirements which are important to understand and required the pursuit for ideal PE materials, their respective device configurations (tandem) and the possible integration with high performance PV panels. They include: (i) solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency exceeding 20%; (ii) photo-electrode materials stability (5000 hours); and (iii) materials photoactivity at neutral pH.

Though largely the fundamental understanding of the process is being well established, we believe in terms of the above-reviewed work that conflating few important modulations on the PE side can drastically improve the performance of these new integrated tandem device systems. They are:

(i) Choice of high performance PEs: it is important to mention that n-type (PE) and p-type (CE) semiconductors should be optimal. Earlier in the above mentioned tandem devices mostly researchers explored bismuth vanadate systems and NiO PEs; however, there is a great scope to combine high performance photocathodes, such as Al:SrTiO3, Cu2O, and Si nanowires (SiNWs), and photoanodes, such as AlGaN, GaN:ZnO, triclinic copper vanadates, cobalt phosphate (CoPi), TaON, and Nb2O5, with DSSCs and in tandem cell configurations which can significantly improve the overall performance.

(ii) Role of a co-catalyst in tandem devices photoelectrodes: a suitable co-catalyst depending on the nature of the reaction (HER and OER) under photoelectrochemical conditions is indispensable for enhancing the overall efficiency of tandem devices. The co-catalyst can dispense oxidation or reduction active sites, accelerate the surface reactions by decreasing the activation energies and suppress the recombination of photogenerated electron and hole pairs. Earth abundant transition metal oxides, biomimetic enzymes, noble metals and sulphides can act as the best co-catalysts if combined with specific PEs. However, use of co-catalysts depends on various factors such as the choice of materials, and electronic compatibility with semiconductors with regard to energy levels, electronic structure, suitable overloading, shape, and size distribution. In order to accomplish improvement in STH efficiency, design of novel co-catalysts on PEs can significantly impact the overall performance of tandem devices.

(iii) Surface passivation of PEs: generally, a semiconductor surface/interface is a prominent region in photoelectrochemical water splitting systems. Swift surface recombination, steady charge transfer kinetics and, most importantly, appalling photoelectrochemical stability are three colossal challenges which impede the progress of solar-driven water splitting. Recently, various reports have suggested that ultrathin (less than 5 nm) surface passivation layers can dramatically affect surface recombination, improve charge transfer kinetics and protect against the chemical/photo-corrosion of PEs. To improve the overall performance of tandem p- and n-type electrolysis, the surface passivation of the PE could modulate the overpotential and increase the photovoltage and stability of these devices. There are various promising surface passivation layers investigated for semiconductor devices which can be employed in the above mentioned tandem device systems such as SiOx, Al2O3, Ga2O3, In2O3, and In2O3 and self-assembled monolayers.

Here, we want to emphasize that there has been no research coupling DSSCs and direct electrolysis (with metal cathodes and anodes). The efficiency of DSSCs, and in particular of hybrid np DSSCs, is too low for this purpose. Researchers in solar fuels usually prefer high-efficiency IIIV based solid-state semiconductor cells or junctions, or eventually PSCs. PSCs are less efficient than IIIV based semiconductor cells. The subject of coupling DSSCs and photoelectrochemical cells was covered in our previous review and, as far as we know, there has not been substantial progress in this direction during the last two years (ref. 2, 18, 21, 22, and 25 and references therein).

7. Perovskite-electrochemical hybrid devices

Various groups made efforts towards increasing the efficiency of PSCs over 23% using low cost solution processed routes. Perovskite based solar cells can achieve open circuit voltages in excess of 1.5 V, which is sufficient to drive electrochemical water splitting in a hybrid system. The concept of perovskite based hybrid devices for electrochemical water splitting was first conceived by Grtzel et al. in 2014.1 They used earth abundant bifunctional catalysts for the hydrogen and oxygen evolution reactions. The water splitting cell was connected with a PSC and this resulted in 12.3% STH efficiency. They used high surface area NiFe LDH (double layer hydroxide)/Ni foam as a bifunctional catalyst, where 10 mA cm2 current density was achieved by applying 1.7 V.

Bin et al. used a nitrogen doped reduced graphene oxide/nitrogen-doped carbon nanotube (NrGO/NCNT) hybrid as an electrocatalyst, which showed a current density of more than 7 mA cm2 at 1.4 V with 9.02% STH efficiency.43 Photoelectrochemical water splitting was carried out in a tandem device where semiconducting polymer:fullerene coupled with a perovskite planar heterojunction demonstrated 11.8% power conversion efficiency. Recently, Roy et al. demonstrated TiO2/Co doped hematite as an electron transport bilayer which was treated with a perovskite layer (CH3NH3PbI3).44 However, the overall STH conversion efficiency for these electrodes was 2.46%. The STH conversion efficiency is obtained from the combined current densityvoltage measurement curve of PSChematite photo-electrode tandem devices.

Zhang et al.45 developed a coreshell TiO2/BiVO4 photoanode driven by a hybrid PSC for un-assisted water splitting, which exhibited an improved (more than three fold) STH efficiency (1.4 mA cm2) as compared to pristine TiO2 demonstrated in Fig. 10a. For TiO2/BiVO4 electrodes, they investigated individual light harvesting (LHE = 110A, charge separation (sep) and electron transfer (transfer) efficiencies. It was concluded that better charge transfer and light absorption were observed for coreshell type electrodes. The authors argued that the charge transfer (transfer) efficiency can be increased by adding a swift hole scavenger to electrolyte. Cui et al.37 achieved highly efficient solar driven water splitting through a BiVO4perovskite hybrid system. By doing so, they improved carrier diffusion and charge separation multifold by doping Mo:BiVO4 onto the surfaces of nano-engineered nanocones formed through the reactive ion etching (RIE) process. The system was driven by a single PSC and achieved 6.2% STH efficiency unlike the abovementioned LDH based system driven by two cells. The main reason for this high performance was that the geometry of the electrode surface was designed as nanocones coated with Mo:BiVO4, which assisted charge separation and high absorption of photons. To advance the process of solar water splitting, Gurudayal et al.46 reported perovskitehaematite tandem cells for overall water splitting by using single perovskite cells in series. Fig. 10b shows a photoanode composed of haematite nanoarrays. In this device architecture, about 2.4% STH efficiency was accomplished; however, the STH efficiency was limited due to the low onset potential and photocurrent density of the photoanode (haematite). It is important to note that while combining with high photocurrent producing photoanodes the efficiency of these tandem devices can be significantly ameliorated. Due to the high performance (greater than 23% efficiency) of solution processed PSCs, various promising applications of PSCs were explored. Increasing CO2 (400 ppm) in the ecosystem poses various challenges; thus it is important to convert CO2 into value added products such as CO, formic acid, methane, methanol, ethanol and ethylene depending on the nature of reaction (number of electrons). Grtzel et al.47 reported a perovskite integrated hybrid system for CO2 reduction to CO. The system was connected to three perovskite cells to provide a sufficient voltage to catalyst load for considerable reduction. The product formation was analyzed by gas chromatography. Fig. 10c shows the schematic device structure of three combined electrochemical PSCs and a generalized energy diagram of three PSCs.47 During the 18 h period of operation the faradaic efficiency varied between 80 and 90%. Later, Jang et al. also reported a sunlight driven perovskite assisted tandem cell hybrid system combined with a photocathode composed of ZnO@ZnTe@CdTe coreshell nanorod arrays for CO2 reduction into CO without any bias voltage.48Fig. 10d shows a schematic representation of a perovskite driven photoelectrochemical photoanode. The device illuminated under the light for 3 h using Au coupled ZnO@ZnTe@CdTe coreshell nanorod arrays based photoanode in tandem configuration. Jang et al. also used cobalt bicarbonate as a potential cathode replacing the conventional platinum electrode. When high performance photoanode and cathode is applied, this hybrid device has great prospects for practical artificial photosynthesis reactions. Advancing water photolysis and artificial photosynthesis through integrated hybrid PSCs could be a new beginning for practical solutions. However, emphasizing only on PSCs while ignoring major electrode components and electrochemical system design will hinder the progress in STH conversion in such systems. In order to accomplish highly efficient water splitting using solar energy technology, high performance PSCs combined with perfect HER and OER electrocatalysts and hybrid electrochemical cell design can be considered as a practical approach. However, more efforts are needed to design and select suitable electrocatalysts with required functionalities. Earth abundant metal based catalysts, which give high turnover frequency (TOF), and high turnover numbers (TONs), bifunctional in nature, easy to process exhibit, desired overpotential of half-cell, and stable over a wide range of pH and show ideal performance in neutral pH etc., can significantly provide improved STH for hybrid devices. Other aspects to improve this system could be depositing a bifunctional catalyst on the back side of the PSC to develop an integrated system for a more practical solution. It should be noted that such research has not been systematically pursued in the last year, with the investigations of the Grtzel group constituting a landmark in this case. One disadvantage of PSCs is their moisture sensitivity; thus they cannot be used in integrated systems based on water-immersed pn junctions.

8. Concluding remarks

Dye sensitized photoelectrodes have been systematically investigated as direct cathodes or anodes for solar fuel generation during the last few decades. Encouraging preliminary results were obtained due to the ultrafast charge separation occurring in a picosecond range and sluggish back reaction in these systems. However, the energy storage efficiency is below 10% in most of the cases. Traditional design of new dyes for electricity-producing regenerative solar cells is not ideally suited for solar fuel applications, and thus it is vital to undertake the design and synthesis of suitable dyes through a molecular engineering approach. Important factors such as the matching of the energy levels of dyes, the semiconductor properties, and the redox reactions pertaining to fuel generation need to be optimized. Additionally, the electron transfer kinetics between the dye and electroactive species in the electrolyte should be improved by the development of suitable interposed molecular or heterogeneous catalysts. In this aspect, the synergy between photochemistry, photophysics, electrochemistry, materials science, and molecular chemistry promises to contribute substantial advances in the near future. A systematic understanding of underlying theoretical principles and experimental findings is essential in the process of laying a solid foundation for the comprehension of diverse photoelectrochemical reactions in integrated and direct p- and n-type DSPELCs, tandem cells and hybrid perovskite-driven photoelectrosynthetic cells. In tandem devices, in addition to the improvement of charge-transfer kinetics and long-term stability, together with the development of new and robust catalysts, there is tremendous scope for the exploration of the currently available high-performance hydrogen and oxygen evolution catalysts which can deliver promising performance.

Preliminary investigations reveal that this direction towards hybrid perovskite-driven electrolysis has achieved a sunlight-to-hydrogen (STH) conversion efficiency of 12.3%; however, still there are major challenges to be addressed, which include: (i) the development of highly stable and performing perovskite cells, which can drive the reactions in excess of 10000 s h with the required efficiency; (ii) the design of robust, stable and efficient electrocatalysts for both the HER and OER and (iii) the development of catalysts which can demonstrate good performance at neutral pH and are active over a wide pH range.

A perusal of extant paradigms of advances of novel DPSELCs and photoelectrode materials is beyond doubt a first step. However, additionally, various ubiquitous challenges need to be effectively addressed in order to realize creative innovations: for instance, the development of high-performance photoelectrodes; assembly of new architectures, in the initial stage based on the existing efficient catalysts; the improvements of the existing approaches of surface passivation so as to enhance charge separation; and the effective use of potential co-catalysts in order to improve the overall STH efficiency of DSPELCs.

DSPELCs as well as tandem and hybrid perovskite systems have been extended to CO2 reduction to carbonaceous fuels, e.g., carbon monoxide or formate, and serve dual purposes of making our planet carbon neutral and converting the emissions into energy based products. CO2 reduction is a kinetically uphill reaction which involves complex molecular reorganization, with the actual mechanism still under extensive debate. There is an enormous scope for the development of new catalysts that can be integrated with DSPELCs and perovskite systems towards the realization of a variety of C1 and C2 products via reduction of CO2. One particular example could be Cu-based catalysts, which are extensively considered for CC coupling reactions, such as Cu nanocrystals with engineered grain boundaries, Cu complexes, and nanoporous Cu, which can be variously integrated within the aforementioned framework to target C2 products such as ethanol and ethylene.

The recently proposed DSPELCs, tandem system and hybrid perovskite framework can be applied to alternative electrosynthetic reactions such as hydrogen peroxide production, nitrogen reduction, and various other essential photo-electrochemical photocatalytic and electrocatalytic transformations. To completely understand the underlying principles of these systems, a theoretical investigation (ref. 2 and references therein), based on first-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations, and ab initio CarParrinello molecular dynamics (CPMD) simulations, is highly required.49,50
