Sustainable bio-derived materials for addressing critical problems of next-generation high-capacity lithium-ion batteries

Abstract

Sustainable, non-toxic, and low-cost bio-derived materials (BDMs) have interesting structures, complex compositions, and unique functional groups and have been used as electrode materials, separators, interlayers, and binders in lithium-ion batteries (LIBs). More and more BDMs have been used to tackle the critical challenges of high theoretical capacity LIBs, low ionic and electronic conductivity for high-capacity active materials, the large volume expansion of lithium-conversion active materials (e.g., Si anode), the uncontrollable side reactions and lithium dendrites in the lithium metal anode, and the shuttle effect of lithiumsulfur like systems. In this work, we systematically review the preparation, mechanisms, and the applications of BDMs and summarize the role they play in resolving these challenges so that larger capacity, higher power density and longer cycle life of next-generation LIBs can be achieved in a green and sustainable manner.

Introduction

Modern society and economic development are heavily dependent on the continuous energy supply. However, the consumption of limited fossil fuels brings environmental and ecological concerns, which is not sustainable.1 Renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and tides are unlimited and clean, but their naturally inconsistent energy output requires energy storage to preserve the power generated and to supply when needed.2 Apart from the stationary grid energy storage, electrochemical portable energy storage gets crucial with the increasing dependency and demand for the mobile electronic devices and electric vehicles.3 Among the electrochemical portable energy storing systems, rechargeable lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) receive the most significant attention due to their light weight, high gravimetric and volumetric capacity, high power density, and long cycle life.4 However, most commercially available LIBs nowadays that use graphite anodes and LiCoO2-like layered structural cathodes with the lithium-ion intercalation mechanism to store the energy are gradually becoming insufficient for the market needs.5 Hence, tremendous efforts are being continuously made for exploring high-capacity materials for LIBs.6,7

Abundantly available silicon (Si), with a considerably high theoretical specific capacity of 3590 mA h g1 in the Li15Si4 alloying phase at room temperature, is not only low cost and safe to handle, but also has a low average discharge voltage. It is the most well-known alternative for the traditional graphite anode (372 mA h g1).810 However, the great volume change (300%) during lithiation/delithiation hinders the real commercialization of the Si anode.11,12 The stress introduced can cause Si particle pulverization, and in addition to its intrinsically low electrical conductivity and the formingbreakingreformation of the solid electrolyte interface (SEI), can increase both the electrolyte consumption and impedance, resulting in an overall fast capacity fading.1315 Another popular graphite anode replacing candidate is lithium (Li) metal due to its extremely high theoretical specific capacity of 3860 mA h g1, the lowest negative electrochemical potential of 3.04 V (vs. the standard hydrogen electrode), and low density (0.59 g cm3).1618 Regrettably, uncontrollable dendrite formation might cause safety concerns such as short circuit, thermal runaway, and low coulombic efficiency during Li deposition/stripping and therefore limit the industrialization of the Li metal anode.1921

Low-cost sulfur (S) has attracted great attention as one of the most promising cathodes, because it is rich in the Earth's crust and can deliver a high theoretical specific capacity of 1675 mA h g1 with an average discharge voltage of 2.15 V. A LiS cell can potentially deliver an overall theoretical specific capacity of ca. 1167 mA h g1 and theoretical gravimetric energy density of ca. 2510 W h kg1 which has made this system very attractive nowadays.2224 Nonetheless, S cathode commercialization was inhibited by the shuttle effect of polysulfides that could lead to active material loss, serious lithium corrosion and short cycle life.2527

Apart from the active electrode materials of LIBs, other components, like the separator, and binder within the battery, are also crucial for keeping a battery running smoothly. Petroleum-derived polyolefins, like polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), and their blends, are very stable in battery operation and low in cost, which has made them commercially successful in the existing battery systems.28 However, polyolefin separators can lose their mechanical strength and malfunction at a higher operating temperature, which could lead to battery failure and safety worries.29,30 Poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) has been the most successful binder for the electrodes due to its high adhesion and electrochemical stability.31 Unfortunately, PVDF could lose its adhesion when soaked with the electrolyte and be unable to maintain the integrity of the electrodes, particularly with high capacity active materials mentioned above.32

Bio-derived materials (BDMs) could be obtained from living organisms and plants. They are usually green, non-toxic, abundant, low-cost, biodegradable and often referred to as sustainable materials. As an example of BDMs, carbohydrates, the key structural building blocks of cells, could play an important role in enhancing the performance of future LIBs.33,34 The structure of the collaborative carbohydrate molecules can usually be distinctive, orientated, and/or porous and hence they enable potentially low-cost and environmentally friendly electrode fabrication.3537 The compositional complexity of carbohydrates and their functional groups make them unique biopolymeric materials with water-solubility and interesting physiochemical properties meaning that they could be important existing polymeric substitutions in battery construction and fabrication.3840 In this regard, we will briefly review the recent advancement of using bio-derived materials to overcome the critical challenges with the future high-capacity Li-based batteries (see Fig. 1), and start from (1) anode and cathode electrode active material altering, to (2) separator or interlayer revamping, and then (3) binder substitution.

Bio-derived materials as carbonaceous and active materials

The major challenges for the future high-capacity active electrode materials (AEMs) include (1) poor electronic conductivity for nonmetal AEMs, (2) the large volume variation during the lithiation/delithiation process in AEMs, (3) the uncontrollable side reactions and lithium dendrites in the Li-metal anode, and (4) the dissolution of sulfur in the organic electrolyte. Carbohydrates, a BDM, can be easily converted into a highly conductive carbonaceous material through pyrolysis, which is often used to enhance electron transfer and accelerate the electrochemical reactions of the nonmetal AEMs.4143 Furthermore, the unique intrinsic structure of the biomaterial can also act as a scaffold for forming an interesting carbonaceous architecture that holds active materials, such as core/yolkshell like structures, and porous bio-derived carbon and active material composites.4448 These architectures can either encapsulate or trap active materials and tolerate the volume variation during lithiation/delithiation. In addition, organic biomaterials usually consist of oxygen and nitrogen elements, so the carbonized structure can be rich with heteroatoms that facilitate the lithium ion and/or electrochemical intermediate adsorption, like N-doped carbon, are also extensively reviewed.4951 Hence, through careful manipulation, the BDMs can be useful to solve the challenges that high-capacity active materials are facing.
Bio-derived materials for anode electrodes. Silicon has drawn tremendous attention as an anode substitute due to its abundancy, low-cost, safety, high theoretical capacity, and low average discharge voltage, but it suffers from a large volume variation of ca. 300% during lithiation/delithiation with the resulting adverse effects preventing it from achieving large-scale commercialization.13 To mitigate these effects, both nanosizing the Si particles15,52,53 and utilizing smart framework designs, including porous structures5457 and nanowires,58 have been applied in silicon electrodes. Encapsulated composite structures, such as yolkshell,5965 and carbon cages6668 are also capable of retaining the electrode integrity and improving the conductivity at the same time.

Glucose, for instance, has been commonly used as the carbon source to wrap or coat a silicon anode in many studies.6973 For instance, Hu et al. wrapped Si@SiOx with glucose through hydrothermal carbonization that greatly improved capacity retention and cycle life when compared with non-coated nano-Si.69 The ca. 40 nm in size Si@SiOx/C composite exhibits a high reversible capacity of 1100 mA h g1 after 60 cycles with above 99% coulombic efficiency, whereas the uncoated Si nanoparticles can only survive for a few cycles. Their study shows that the glucose-derived carbon coating can enhance the electronic and ionic conductivity and help with the SEI formation to achieve a better cycle life.

BDMs with unique structures are also widely explored and utilized as conductive frameworks for hosting active materials.7477 For instance, Shen et al. fabricated a 3D conductive framework from sugarcane bagasse with a loaded dopamine wrapped Si nanoparticle anode composite (Fig. 2a).74 The as-prepared 3D active carbon/silicon composite and bare Si nanoparticles were cycled at a current rate of 200 mA g1, and the composite electrode showed a stable discharge capacity of 1014 mA h g1 at the 11th cycle which slipped to 503 mA h g1 after 100 cycles whereas it was only 13.7 mA h g1 after 100 cycles for the bare Si nanoparticles electrode. Niu et al. used biomass lignin to crosslink the Si nanoparticles via a facile self-assembly method (Fig. 2b).75 The as-prepared SiNPs@Lignin electrode demonstrates an excellent capacity retention of 89% after 100 cycles at a 300 mA g1 charge and discharge rate and a residual capacity of 3000 mA h g1 that is much better than that of unwrapped Si nanoparticles (Fig. 2c). They further assembled the as-prepared electrode with a LiCoO2 cathode into a flexible full battery to show that even great bending/deformation of the battery will not affect the performance of the anode electrode (Fig. 2d). This indicates that carbonaceous materials can help to mitigate the effect of volume variation during charge/discharge, facilitate electronic conductivity, and then enhance the overall performance of the batteries.

Plants could take up minerals from the soil that may be converted into an interesting combination of natural carbon structure with the active material composite.78,79 The successful conversion of biosilica from unwanted biomass into nano-silicon via the magnesiothermic reduction and its use as the anode in LIBs has been explored for a while.8082 For instance, Xing et al. successfully used magnesiothermic reduction to convert the biosilica within unwanted rice husk into a highly porous Si anode with the large specific surface area for LIBs (Fig. 3a), and achieved a capacity retention of 1220.2 mA h g1 at a current of 1000 mA g1 after 100 cycles.82 Liu et al. utilized a similar reduction method to recover the nano-Si to obtain various shapes of Si in the end.81 The obtained bio-derived Si anode can still deliver a stable 1750 mA h g1 at a rate of 0.5 C after 150 cycles. Yu's group had synthesized a 3D porous SiC structure from the silica-rich reeds that could be found abundantly in the wetland with a glucose-derived carbon coating.79 Apart from the precursor that could be easily accessed, the recovered silicon within the naturally grown 3D structure can also mitigate the pulverization during the charge and discharge process. At the same time, the carbon-coating can enhance the overall structural stability and electronic conductivity. This composite can deliver a high-capacity retention of 1050 mA h g1 after 200 cycles at 0.5 C and could also maintain stable under 10 C and 20 C with the reversible capacities of 745 and 398 mA h g1, respectively. Furthermore, this bio-derived composite can be cycled 4000 times under a current density of 10 C which indicates that the natural structure of reed leaf and glucose-derived carbon coating can both alleviate the effect of the volume expansion and enhance the good electronic conductivity.

Another method worth mentioning is obtaining the active silicon materials via aluminothermic reduction.83,84 Su et al. obtained highly porous silicon from the biomass, corn leaves, with the aluminothermic reduction (Fig. 3b) and the as-prepared CornSi electrode exhibits a capacity retention near 100% and a discharge capacity of 2100 mA h g1 after 300 cycles.83 Kuang et al. converted the commercial bamboo charcoal into 3D porous SiOx/C composites via an in situ top-down synthesis that involves carbonization and aluminothermic reduction (Fig. 3c).84 The as-prepared composite anode had delivered a specific capacity of 1100 mA h g1 after 300 cycles at a loading of 200 mA g1, and 700 mA h g1 after 200 cycles when the loading had increased to 1 A g1. These examples again demonstrate that BDMs can help develop high-capacity LIBs with lower cost and greener processing methods.

A lithiumsulfur battery (LSB) consists of a sulfur cathode and a lithium metal anode with an extremely high theoretical energy density.27,8589 However, major challenges for the lithium metal anode are the degradation by the corrosion of the electrode caused by polysulfides and uncontrollable dendrite formation, leading to serious safety concerns.27 The corrosion and dendrites can be minimized by proper structural designs, i.e., adding a protective layer, which could potentially solve the unwanted dendrites at the same time.9092 For instance, Zhang et al. developed an interesting channel-guided Li metal anode with carbonized wood (C-wood) to mitigate the corrosion and undesired dendrite growth.91 The Li/C-wood electrodes were prepared by lithium infusing the ZnO coated C-wood with highly aligned channels (Fig. 4ae). The as-prepared electrode can withstand a high current density loading of 3 mA cm2 and be cycled for 225 cycles with stable stripping/plating platforms, whereas the bare Li metal cell shows an overpotential increase along with the cycle number. Similarly, Song et al. had used a wood-derived carbon (WDC) frame to fabricate a dendrite-free composite Li anode assisted by silver (Ag) nanoparticles (Fig. 4f) that shows an excellent cyclic performance over 450 h at 1 mA cm2 and over 300 h when the loading had increased to 3 mA cm2.92

Bio-derived materials for cathode electrodes. LSB is a promising candidate for next-generation energy storage devices due to its high theoretical capacity of the sulfur cathode when compared with metal oxide cathodes in commercialized LIBs.93,94 However, before the commercialization of the LSB, certain technical challenges must be addressed.95 LSBs suffer from poor electronic conductivity of the cathodic active material and its discharge product (sulfur and Li2S, respectively), sluggish reaction kinetics related to the solid-state steps of the electrochemical reaction, a large volume expansion of active materials, which leads to structural damage during cycling, and most troubling, the polysulfide (PS) shuttle effect which results in rapid electroactive material loss and capacity fading in the LSB cell.96 Rationally designed sulfur hosts have been widely applied in LSB cathodes to remedy the aforementioned technical challenges by providing an electronically conductive framework for the active material, ample pore volume to accommodate active material expansion and PS retention through physical blocking and chemical retardation of PS migration during cycling.97,98 Biochar, obtained from a carbonization process of a biomass precursor, has many appealing properties for use as sulfur hosts in LSBs, including high electronic conductivity, a suitable distribution of micro/mesopores, inherent heteroatom doping for chemical PS restriction, as well as low environmental and economic costs.99 Typically, for LSB applications, the obtained biochar is subjected to an activation process to provide the material with abundant micropores for enhanced PS restriction at the cathode. Activation is commonly carried out in either two steps (carbonization followed by activation) or a one-step combined carbonization/activation process, generally utilizing KOH as the porogen100 with some exceptions.101,102

Among the wide variety of source materials for the biomass precursors, plant derived biochars, a kind of BDMs, tend to be the most popular. Biomass sources include kapok fiber,103 bamboo,104 catkin,51,105 seaweed,100 agaric,106 lignin,107 and reed flowers108 to name a few. A particularly impressive example of a plant derived biochar application in LSBs was provided by Manthiram's group.109 Natural cotton fibers were annealed in a N2 atmosphere to obtain a free-standing and robust cotton carbon network, which, after catholyte infiltration, allowed for a binder and current collector free cathode system (Fig. 5a). The obtained cells displayed a remarkably high sulfur loading of 61.4 mg cm2 with a reversible discharge capacity of 724 mA h g1 after 50 cycles at 0.1 C.

As a way of further reducing the environmental impact associated with the sources of biomass precursors for LSB biochar, researchers have looked towards waste products. Due to the abundant supply, food waste is a popular choice as a biomass source. Examples include fruit peel,50,110112 vegetable peel,113,114 nut shells,101,102,115 expired milk powder,116 tea leaves from used teabags,117,118 fish scales,119,120 crab shells,121 eggshell membrane,122 and pig bones.123 Zhou and co-workers used peanut shells as a biomass precursor in a one-step carbonization and activation process using K2FeO4 as both the activating and graphitization agent (Fig. 5b).102 The resulting biochar material displayed an ultra-micropore (<0.7 nm) volume of 0.65 cm3 g1 that served as a host for short-chain polysulfides (S24). When cycled with a carbonate electrolyte, the assembled lithiumsulfur cells delivered a specific discharge capacity of 826 mA h g1 at 1 C after an outstanding 1000 charge/discharge cycles. Byproducts of industrial processes are also a rational source of biomass which would otherwise be waste. To this end, Tesio et al. used waste from the beer brewing process as a biomass precursor for LSB cathode hosts.124 After a nonactivating pyrolysis process, the LSBs fabricated from the after brewing waste carbon delivered a discharge capacity of 498 mA h g1 after 400 cycles at the high discharge current of 5 C.

In addition to direct applications as a sulfur host material in LSB cathodes, carbonized biomass has been used as a carbon skeleton in the construction of modified biochar frameworks for sulfur cathodes. Many such examples exist, including a wheat flour biochar modified with g-C3N4,125 wood fiber carbon subjected to atomic layer deposition of Al2O3,126 tissue paper carbon as a growth medium for MoS2,127 among others.128132 Zhong et al. used an instant puffing process to expand the rice precursor into a highly porous macrocellular structure, as shown in Fig. 5c.133 After a carbonization process, the puffed rice carbon (PRC) was obtained, which served as a carbon support skeleton for transition metal catalysts, namely Ni nanoparticles. The rationally designed structure allowed a reversible discharge capacity of 813 mA h g1 to be obtained after 500 cycles at 0.1 C. All in all, biochar derived from the carbonization of biomass precursors are shown to greatly improve the performance of lithiumsulfur batteries, whether they were used directly as host materials or as skeletons for further modification.

Bio-derived materials for separator or interlayers

BDMs show great promise to be used as separators and interlayers because of their abundant functional groups and excellent mechanical properties. In terms of separators in LIBs, several key factors should be considered, including porosity, mechanical strength, thermal stability, chemical/electrochemical stability, wettability, etc. The current commercial separator market has been dominated by polyolefins such as polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), and their blends, which exhibit significant advantages in their outstanding stability in battery operation as well as low cost. However, these commercial separators suffer from severe function failure at high temperature, which may cause safety concerns.134,135 In addition, these commercial separators have inferior wettability with organic electrolytes, which is detrimental to the rate capability of LIBs.136,137 Conversely, BDMs can offset the drawbacks of polyolefin-based separators as they possess good mechanical properties and many polar groups which endow them with good affinity for liquid electrolytes. Recently, a wide spectrum of BDM-based separators has emerged for advanced LIBs. In this section, we will discuss the development of BDM-based separators as well interlayers considering that interlayers can be regarded as extended parts of separators.
Bio-derived materials for separators. Cellulose and cellulose-derived materials are the most commonly used sources for biomass-based separators because of their abundant functional groups, including carboxyl groups (COOH) and hydroxyl groups (OH), leading to superior wettability with liquid electrolytes. More importantly, the cellulose fibers endow the separators with a well-controlled nanoporous structure. As shown in Fig. 6a, compared to the slit-like pores in a commercial PP/PE/PP separator, the cellulose-derived separators exhibited nano-sized pores. Moreover, the porous architectures of cellulose-derived separators are tunable by controlling the water concentration during the preparation process. When applied in lithium-ion batteries, the as-prepared separator showed higher ionic conductivity and improved electrolyte wettability and thermal stability than the polyolefin-based separator due to the polarity and thermal stability.138 However, the unusual pore sizes and low mechanical strength of pure cellulose-derived separators have a great impact on the safety and lifespan of LIBs due to the formation of lithium dendrite and battery self-discharge.139,140 In this regard, Xu et al. adopted a composite membrane composed of cellulose and polysulfonamide (PSA) as a LIB separator.141 The obtained cellulose/PSA composite nonwoven membrane exhibited excellent tensile strength up to 17 MPa, because the cellulose skeleton was well bonded with PSA for improving the mechanical properties (Fig. 6b). Another strategy to improve the mechanical strength of cellulose-based separators is to add inorganic fillers. Kim et al. introduced colloidal silica nanoparticles into cellulose nanofiber paper (CNP) separators for lithium-ion batteries. As shown in Fig. 6c, the incorporation of SiO2 nanoparticles in the cellulose nanofibers (CNF) suspension is an effective way to control nanoscale labyrinth structure (i.e., nanoporous structure) of CNP separators. Because of its highest ionic conduction arising from the well-balanced properties between the porous structure and separator thickness, the cells using CNP separator with appropriate SiO2 content (5 wt%) showed improved C-rate capability and excellent cycling performance.140 Moreover, the rice paper (RP) consisting of tortuous cellulose fibers demonstrated excellent thermal stability and high-voltage stability.142

Chitin is another main source of biomass for separators in lithium-ion batteries. For example, Kim et al. reported a hierarchical regenerated chitin fiber (Chiber) as a nonwoven mat-type Chiber separator to mitigate lithium dendrites in lithium metal batteries. Results suggested that the chitin molecule showed a stronger Li-ion affinity than conventional battery separators due to unique coordination with functional groups, leading to greatly improved coulombic efficiency with Li metal mitigation (Fig. 6d).143 Alginate, obtained from brown seaweeds, is an ideal material for separators for high-voltage lithium batteries owing to its strong polymer binder, good electrochemical stability, and special carboxyl structure. Wen et al. explored the feasibility of alginate as a separator for high-voltage LIBs. They demonstrated that the prepared alginate nonwoven separator (abbreviated as CA separator) exhibited superior electrochemical stability, high ionic conductivity, and improved electrochemical performance. Additionally, because of abundant carboxyl groups in the alginate structure and its capability to chelate with Mn2+, the CA separator could significantly improve the cycling performance of a high-voltage (5 V) LiNi0.5Mn1.5O4 cathode at 55 C (Fig. 6e and f).144 In summary, the biomass-based separators for LIBs show some common properties, including favourable separator surface and interface wettability by electrolyte, high ionic conductivity and large scale production with low cost.

Bio-derived materials for interlayers. Functional interlayers are usually employed in LiS batteries to solve the diffusion of polysulfides and shuttle effect issues, and lithium anodes to prevent lithium dendrite growth. An ideal interlayer is expected to meet the following requirements:145 (1) strong adsorption ability to suppress polysulfide diffusion; (2) efficient diffusion pathway for lithium ions; (3) protecting lithium anode from polysulfide attack; (4) reutilizing the confined active materials by acting as an upper current collector; (5) maintaining electrode integrity by buffering the volume changes; (6) sufficient mechanical strength for preventing pulverization during cycling; and (7) reasonable thickness for easy assembly and high total volumetric energy density.

BDMs are appropriate sources to synthesize functional interlayers for LiS batteries because bio-mass-derived interlayers possess high surface area and unique nanostructure, which is beneficial for accommodating polysulfides. To date, many BDMs have been explored for functional interlayers, including cellulose paper,150,151 leaf,152 eggshell membrane,153 bacterial cellulose,154 cassava,155 fungus,147,156 bamboo,146etc. Inspired by the unique tissue and functions of leaves, Chung et al. synthesized a natural carbonized leaf (CL) as a functional interlayer for LiS batteries. After carbonization under inert gas protection at high temperature, the obtained CL could retain the thin-film shape and exhibited sufficient mechanical strength. The structural gradient and the micro/mesoporous adsorption sites of CL could effectively intercept/trap the migrating polysulfides and facilitate their reutilization.152 Similarly, Gu et al. prepared a bamboo carbon fiber membrane (BCFM) from waste bamboo stick (Fig. 7a). The unique robust fiber network and macro/micropores of BCFM could obtain high electrolyte uptake, facilitate rapid transportation of electrons and ions as well as provide physical space for efficient confinement of sulfur. As a result, the BCFM interlayer could greatly improve the cycling performance and electrochemical performance of LiS batteries.146 Additionally, it is quite easy to fabricate a heteroatom doped carbon material from BDMs. For example, Chai et al. synthesized N and O-doped fungus carbon fiber (GFC) embedded with graphene as interlayer for LiS batteries. The GFC film serves as the conductive interlayer to greatly improve the performance of LiS batteries including capacity retention and rate capability (Fig. 7b).147

Besides carbon materials carbonized from BDMs, some BDMs can be used as interlayer directly. For instance, Tu et al. incorporated a natural polymer of Gum Arabic (GA) onto the conductive support of a carbon nanofiber (CNF) film as a polysulfide shielding interlayer (Fig. 7c). The GA possesses precise oxygen-containing functional groups that can induce a strong binding interaction toward lithium polysulfides and thereby effectively suppress the shuttling behavior.148 Inspired by air filtration, Fu et al. reported a protein-based nanofilter for effectively trapping polysulfides but facilitating Li+ transport. Interestingly, the gelatin protein could drive unique self-assembly of CB particles, resulting in a 3D porous structure (Fig. 7d). Additionally, the backbone oxygen atoms and carboxyl groups from the end chains of the protein showed notable electrostatic interactions with Li ions in polysulfides, which showed significant advantages in improving the battery performance.149

BDMs have also been integrated into artificial solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) films to suppress the dendrite propagation, given that the BDMs show a great ability to manipulate the mechanical properties by macromolecular chains as well as regulate Li ion flux and distribution by lithiophilic functional groups.47 For example, Zhang et al. used a uniform agarose film to protect the lithium anode. The high ionic conductivity, high elasticity, and chemical stability of this natural biopolymer provided a fast Li-ion transfer and feasibility to accommodate the volume change of lithium anode. In contrast to the dead lithium formed on the pure Cu electrode, the tough and elastic agarose polymer film could relieve the volume change and prevent the overgrowth of Li dendrites during the Li deposition and dissolution process (Fig. 8a and b). As a result, a dendrite-free anode and a stable interface were obtained, resulting in enhanced electrochemical performance in full cells.157 Additionally, Kimwipe (KW) paper composed of a 3D fibrous framework of virgin wood fibers could also realize a dendrite-free surface and improve the reversibility of the Li anode. As shown in Fig. 8c and d, due to the abundant polar functional groups in KW paper to adhere to Li ions, the SEI interlayer provided excellent wettability and impeded the aggregation of Li ions around protrusions, resulting in a homogeneous deposition of Li ions on the surface.158 Therefore, the natural polymers with abundant surface polar groups exhibit outstanding ability to regulate lithium deposition and inhibit lithium dendrite growth. However, some issues should be considered in terms of natural polymers in the SEI interlayer, including the compatibility of natural polymers with organic solvents and the chemical/electrochemical stability of natural polymers against lithium metal.

Bio-derived materials for binding

In addition, the precursor design based on the BDMs, the binder, which is the essential component of the electrode, directly uses the biomass materials.46,47 Recent achievements of the binders highlight the increasing competitiveness of high-energy-density electrodes for energy storage.159,160 In this Section, we explore BDM-based functional binders for the fabrication of Si and S electrodes.
Functional binders for Si anodes. As aforementioned, silicon, with the high theoretical specific capacity and suitable average discharge voltage, is considered as the most promising alternative to the graphite at the anode.161 However, the Si material suffers from the large volume variation of Si (for Li4.4Si) during lithiation/delithiation. The stress generated during repeated charge/discharge results in the pulverization of the electrode, eventually leading to the performance decay of the Si-based anode.39,162,163 In addition, such a volume variation is also detrimental for the electrode conductivity due to the lost connection between Si particles and conductive fillers. These difficulties are challenging the industrial prospects of Si-based anodes. To this end, research efforts have focused on the design of functional binders to overcome the above difficulties, which is well buoyed by the low cost of polymeric binders and the good compatibility with conventional battery processing.

In earlier studies for the Si-based anode, some natural materials have been explored for replacing the PVDF binder. Typically, carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) with carboxy groups is demonstrated to be favorable for the enhanced electrochemical behaviors of the Si-based anode. In the year 2011, the battery scientists suddenly became aware that the binder design could be more feasible for high-stability Si-based anodes compared with other creative yet costly wrapping techniques or structural changing methods. Luzinov and Yushin jointly proposed sodium-alginate (SA) be used as the binder for the Si-based anode (Fig. 9ac).164 Meanwhile, the concept of hydrogen bonding between the binder and Si materials has been demonstrated and cited by the battery community. Subsequently, various bio-mass binders with tailor-made functions, such as binding strength, electronic conductivity, flexibility, and wettability, have been explored to improve the cycling performance of Si anodes. For example, Ling et al. reported the application of naturally abundant gum arabic (GA),165 composed of polysaccharides and glycoproteins, as a dual-function binder for the Si-based anode (Fig. 9d). This is attributed to the combination of a large amount of hydroxyl groups, which are essential in ensuring strong binding to Si, and existing long chain glycoproteins providing robust mechanical tolerance to large volume variation by Si nanoparticles upon lithiation. Therefore, the Si anodes can deliver the reversible capacity of ca. 2000 mA h g1 and a long lifespan of 500 cycles.

In addition to the direct utilization of biomass materials as a binder, the bio-derived polymer monomers to form polymer resins are designed to serve as functional binders for the LIB electrode.166168 Liu and coauthors reported a facile strategy of exploiting in situ self-polymerization of furfuryl alcohol to adhere to the Si-based anode (Fig. 9e).166 Regrettably, the resultant poly(furfuryl alcohol) cannot address the destructive volume variation of Si anodes by itself due to the inherent property of high stiffness. Tuning the mechanical properties of the binder is a practical approach for addressing the above problem. When combined with the soft PVA constituent, the resultant 3D network binder has been interweaved to enable a high areal-density Si-based anode with a stable cycling life simultaneously. Such a strategy of interweaving hard and soft polymers has been a demonstration in the research realm of SiOx and metal oxides anode,169 which opens up a new view to develop environmentally benign binder technology employed in the high-performance Li-storage anodes with large volume change.

Beyond the enhanced bonding affinity, the functionality of the biomass binder is worthy of discussion. The electric conductivity could be obtained by partially carbonized polymer binders. Li et al. proposed the increasing electronic conductivity stemming from the electron delocalization over the chain and in the length of the poly-conjugation without sacrificing bonding ability.170 The CMC was thermally treated by an optimum temperature of 250 C in the air atmosphere. When the partially carbonized CMC acted as the binder for the Si-based anode, the specific capacity of over 2000 mA h g1 and areal capacity of 1.39 mA h cm2 can still be achieved in the absence of conductive additive. This partial carbonization binder is feasible to scale up due to the facile heat-treatment process. Similarly, some small-molecule cross-linkers have been recently documented to design robust water-soluble network binders for stable Si anodes. These small molecules have good flexibility and wettability to adhere to the surface of Si particles. These advantages are attributed to extremely abundant chemical structures of the cross-linkers and tuned physicochemical properties such as melting point that greatly affect the mechanical properties of the network binder. Li and coauthors used a small molecule of epichlorohydrin (ECH) to enhance the mechanical strengthen of peach gum with Si particles.171 The resultant PG-c-ECH composite binder has durable interfacial adhesion and outstanding mechanical properties to stabilize the structural integrity of the Si anode.

Functional binders for S cathodes. Sulfur, when used for Li storage, has appealing electrochemical properties, including a high theoretical capacity of 1672 mA h g1 and an average cell voltage of 2.15 V (vs. Li/Li+) being suitable for the cathode.172175 Regrettably, its intrinsic drawbacks, such as low electrical conductivity, large volume change upon lithiation, and the shuttle effect of lithium polysulfide (LiPS) dissolved in the electrolyte, have seriously hindered its practical applications. Many efforts have been devoted to the rational design of S-based nanostructure and overall electrode architectures,176,177 including core/yolkshell morphologies, multistage porosity, and functional interlayer, to name but a few. In parallel, the battery scientists are also increasingly aware of the importance of keeping the S-based cathode integrity via the binder design. Typically, when a gelatin macromolecule was used for LiS batteries,176 biological gelatin has advantages in the preparation of sulfur cathodes. Its rich ionizable groups such as COOH and NH2 allow for the gelatin to have great hydrophilic capability for trapping dissolving LiPSs. In addition, the gelatin comes from the natural sources and thereby is easier scaled up. In earlier studies, various studies of the bio-based binders,89,178180 such as poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) and polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP), have been also investigated for the sulfur cathode, confirming their beneficial roles in enhancing cycling stability of the electrode.

Sodium-alginate (SA), a natural polysaccharide extracted from brown algae, has been widely investigated as the binder for the LIBs.164,181 In 2013, Zhang and co-authors initially investigated its effects in S-based cathodes and exhibited resultant electrochemical evidence.181 Compared with the PVDF binder, the SA binder enables sulfur cathodes with a higher specific capacity and better cycling stability. It is attributed to the benefits of robust mechanical properties, which effectively buffered volume expansion of sulfur particles during discharge/charge processes, and porous structure, being favorable for electrolyte permeation and thus fast Li-ion transfer. Subsequently, Min et al. reported the novel design of gum arabic (GA) as binder for the S-based cathode (Fig. 10a).182 GA is known as a natural polymer derived from Acacia senegal and comprises massive highly branched polysaccharides. These components provide rich hydroxyl, carboxyl, and ether functional groups that not only form the network for the stronger bonding in response to the volume variation, but also chemically function to restrain the shuttle effect of soluble lithium polysulfide (LiPS). Consequently, the GA binder is confirmed to enable the S-based cathode with a high initial specific capacity of 1157 mA h g1 and stable cycle life over 500 cycles (Fig. 10b).182 A further demonstration has been also performed in the case of soy protein (SP),183 which has abundant polar groups, such as amine and carboxyl groups (Fig. 10c). The S-based cathode using the SP binder exhibits a remarkable specific capacity even at high mass loading (Fig. 10d). These studies have also reminded the battery scientists that the vital role of the bio-based binder's polysaccharides in enhancing the electrochemical performance of the S-based cathode should be ignored in the subsequent modifications.

There are some references worthy of discussion in the literature. Zhang and Lin jointly proposed a concept of using incorporation of various biopolymers as a binder network for the S-based cathode. They simply mixed guar gum (GG) and xanthan gum (XG), which are low-cost, non-toxic, sustainable natural products. Benefiting from the intermolecular binding effect between GG and XG, a resultant binder network exhibits the strong bonding affinity between sulfur particles and conductive filler as well as between sulfur particles and current collectors (Fig. 10e and f). In addition, its rich oxygenous functional groups provide the remarkable adsorption for trapping the dissolved polysulfides, and realizing an ultrahigh areal specific capacity of 11.9 mA h cm2 (Fig. 10g).174 These results show that a high-energy-density LiS batteries can be easily obtained using a biomass binder.

Conclusion

Sustainable bio-derived materials with unique structures, compositions, and functional groups for enhancing the performance of LIBs are briefly reviewed in this article. The critical problems of high-capacity LIBs are the generally low ionic and electronic conductivity of active materials, the great volume change in Li-conversion type AEMs (Si anode), the uncontrollable side reactions and dendrites in the Li metal anode, and the shuttle effect of the sulfur cathode.

Bio-derived materials now in various designs and forms can mitigate these problems and potentially bring the realization of those high-capacity active materials a step forward by carbonaceous hosting materials and/or active materials sources, functional spacers and interlayers, and functional binders with desired mechanical properties. As to the further improvement of the overall volumetric capacity density of the batteries, there are more studies required for BDMs, such as better control and manipulation of the carbonaceous architectures to perfect or match the actual size required for anode and cathode materials, reduce the thickness for the separators and/or the interlayers, and lessen the amount of the binding materials used. These all require the enhancement of the fabrication and/or extraction techniques, the understanding of the BDMs in terms of the specific properties and functions that they can bring, and the scale-up trial with quantitative and qualitative analysis because the bio-sources could vary seasonally and locationally. In short, the increasing attention paid to the use of greener and more sustainable bio-derived materials for solving the challenges of replacing the existing battery systems is underway and requires more in-depth studies and research. More bio-derived materials or related green processes/techniques could be invented and/or discovered for high-performance LIBs in the near future.
