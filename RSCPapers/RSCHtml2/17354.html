Small energy loss in ternary organic solar cells with a blend of cascade energy levels: two fullerene-free acceptors as the electron acceptor†

Abstract

Fullerene-free organic solar cells (OSCs) were fabricated using a blend of wide bandgap donor PBDB-T and ultranarrow bandgap acceptor IEICO-4F as binary photoactive layers. The ultranarrow bandgap acceptor 4TIC was selected as the third component due to the shallow lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels, the good compatibility compared to that of IEICO-4F and the wide energy levels offset between the donor and acceptor. The power conversion efficiency (PCE) of ternary OSCs increased from 10.02% to 11.63% when 40% IEICO-4F was replaced with 4TIC, with an enhanced open-circuit voltage (VOC) of 0.82 V and without sacrificing the short-circuit current (JSC) or fill factor (FF). The main contribution of 4TIC can be summarized as the elevated LUMO level of the acceptor blended and enhanced exciton utilization of the ternary photoactive layer due to the second light absorption of 4TIC and efficient energy transfer from PBDB-T to the blended acceptor. Simultaneously, 4TIC can also act as a regulator to adjust the PBDB-T molecular arrangement for weak charge carrier recombination and optimizing charge carrier transport resulting in the lack of any decrease in the JSC and FF values of ternary OSCs even when the absorption spectrum of the ternary photoactive layer does not match the AM 1.5G solar simulator spectrum. Thus, approximately 15% PCE improvement was achieved by adopting the ternary strategy based on PBDB-T–IEICO-4F binary OSCs with a PCE of 10.02%.

1. Introduction

Bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) organic solar cells (OSCs) based on solution processable p-type polymer semiconductors as a donor and n-type small molecules as an acceptor have attracted considerable interest in recent years.1–3 The traditional photoactive layer of OSCs consists of p-type polymer donor and n-type fullerene derivative acceptor materials.4–6 However, there are also certain disadvantages of fullerene derivatives that need to be addressed, such as the absorption peak in the short wavelength region (the absorption peaks are located at 240 nm and 380 nm for PCBM and PC71BM, respectively) and the deep lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels (the LUMO energy levels are 4.1 eV and 3.9 eV for PCBM and PC71BM, respectively).7,8 However, a significant challenge must be overcome in order to match the light spectrum of the AM 1.5G solar simulator and the LUMO energy levels of donor materials. Fullerene-free acceptors have recently been vigorously developed and have replaced the common fullerene derivative small molecule acceptor materials for high-performance OSCs, which play an important role in the broad absorption windows, particularly in the long wavelength and near infrared region, and adjusting the LUMO energy levels according to the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of the donor materials.9–11

Thus, new fullerene-free acceptors were designed and synthesised with thiophene-thieno[3,2-b]thiophene–thiophene as the central donor unit and electron-deficient 3-(dicyanomethylidene)indan-1-one as the terminal acceptor unit to construct a new low-bandgap fullerene-free acceptor, thiophene-thieno[3,2-b]thiophene–thiophene-3-(dicyanomethylidene)indan-1-one (4TIC), which exhibits a near infrared light absorption window (the absorption peak is located at approximately 796 nm) and a shallow LUMO energy level (the LUMO is −3.78 eV).9,12 The other fullerene-free acceptor is 2,2′-((2Z,2′Z)-(((4,4,9,9-tetrakis(4-hexylphenyl)-4,9-dihydro-sindaceno[1,2-b:5,6-b′]dithiophene-2,7-diyl)bis(4-((2-ethylhexyl)oxy)thiophene-5,2-diyl))bis(methanylylidene))bis(5,6-difluoro-3-oxo-2,3-dihydro-1H-indene-2,1-diylidene))dimalononitrile (IEICO-4F), with a shallow LUMO energy level (the LUMO is −4.05 eV) and a redshifted absorption peak (the absorption peak is approximately 835 nm).

To further improve the photovoltaic performance, the ternary strategy (the donor or acceptor materials as third component materials) has also been used to broaden the absorption windows and adjust the LUMO and HOMO of the photoactive layer.13–15 If the advantages in the photovoltaic performance of fullerene-free OSCs and ternary OSCs are recombined into one OSC by employing fullerene-free ternary OSCs, efficient fullerene-free ternary OSCs can be obtained with balanced photovoltaic parameters (VOC, JSC, FF and PCE).

Inverted ternary OSCs were fabricated with PBDB-T as the donor and a blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC as the acceptor. The PBDB-T:IEICO-4F-based binary OSCs exhibit a PCE of 10.02% with a JSC of 20.45 mA cm−2, a VOC of 0.72 V and a FF of 64.9%. The PBDB-T:4TIC-based binary OSCs exhibit a PCE of 8.45% with a JSC of 16.45 mA cm−2, a VOC of 0.84 V and a FF of 61.2%. For the blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC as the electron acceptor, the ternary PSCs are apparently complementary photovoltaic parameters of the two binary OSCs. The PCE of the optimized ternary PSCs (the ratio of IEICO-4F:4TIC is 6:4) reaches 11.63% with a JSC of 21.52 mA cm−2, a VOC of 0.82 V and a FF of 65.9%. Over 15% PCE improvement can be achieved in comparison to the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F-based binary OSCs owing to the simultaneously significantly enhanced VOC and slightly increased JSC and FF. According to the alloyed model based on the good compatibility of the used materials, the VOC values of ternary PSCs are monotonously increased along with the increasing 4TIC content. However, through alloyed model calculations, the enhancement of the VOC value is beyond the increase in the energy level offset between the donor and the blended acceptor (Eg), which is attributed to the decrease of the energy losses (Eloss) of the photoactive layer. It should be a smart strategy to recombine the advantages of binary PSCs into one ternary cell by selecting a third component with appropriate energy levels and good compatibility with the host system.16,17

Fig. 2a shows the normalized absorption spectrum of the pristine donor and the pristine acceptor; the IEICO-4F films exhibit long wavelength absorption windows (the absorption peak of IEICO-4F is located at 835 nm) and the absorption peaks are redshifted approximately 35 nm compared to that of the 4TIC films (the absorption peak of 4TIC is located at 800 nm). When the blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC replaced pristine IEICO-4F (as shown in Fig. 2b), the normalized absorption spectra of both the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films and the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary films exhibit similar main absorption peaks at 615 nm (corresponding to PBDB-T) and slightly blueshifted acceptor absorption peaks (blueshifted from 830 nm to 810 nm). Thus, the near infrared light absorption window of IEICO-4F plays an important role in efficiently harvesting long wavelength and near infrared photons of the photoactive layer.21 In contrast to the AM 1.5G solar simulator spectrum, the maximum light energy focusing on the long wavelength and near infrared regions, the blueshift absorption spectrum of PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films is not beneficial for efficiently harvesting long wavelength and near infrared photons compared with the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary films. Fig. 2c (the absorption spectral difference (ΔAbsorption) between ternary films and binary films) further confirms this result.22 To intuitively observe the effect of the ternary photoactive layer on the EQE spectra of OSC, the EQE spectral differences (ΔEQEs) between the ternary OSC and the binary OSC were employed to investigate the influence of the binary photoactive layer and the ternary photoactive layer on the photocurrent response. As shown in Fig. 2d, when the blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC replaced pristine IEICO-4F as the electron acceptor, the ΔEQE curve of the ternary OSC showed positive values and better photocurrent response in the whole visible light region, which is attributed to the improved utilization of exciton dissociation and charge carrier transport.23 Thus, the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC-based ternary OSC exhibits efficient photocurrent response in the whole visible light region even when inefficiently harvesting long wavelength and near infrared photons compared with the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary OSCs. Next, we will investigate the work mechanisms of PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC-based ternary OSC by exciton dissociation, charge carrier transport and collection as well as charge carrier recombination.

We investigated the exciton dissociation, charge carrier mobility and recombination.

Photoluminescence quenching and photoluminescence emission lifetime measurements provide a straightforward approach to estimate the exciton dissociation efficiency. Fig. 3a shows the photoluminescence (PL) quenching spectra of pristine PBDB-T, IEICO-4F, PBDB-T:IEICO-4F and IEICO-4F:4TIC binary films and PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films. After incorporating the pristine electron acceptor or blended electron acceptor into PBDB-T, significant PL quenching was observed for the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary films, and much more PL quenching occurred for the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary film. Furthermore, there was remarkable PL quenching for the IEICO-4F:4TIC binary films. In the blended films, identical molecules are closely packed, potentially leading to strong photoluminescence self-quenching. Fig. 3b shows the time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) spectra. The emission lifetimes (η) decreased from 11.8 ns (for pristine PBDB-T) to 3.85 ns (for PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary films) and further decreased to 3.46 nm (for PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary film). In addition, for the IEICO-4F:4TIC films, the η value significantly decreased to 1.13 nm (the η value of pristine IEICO-4F is 1.87 ns). This finding together with the more efficient PL quenching and the significant decrease in emission lifetimes for the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary film could be collectively responsible for the more sufficient driving force for exciton dissociation from the donor to blended acceptor phases and the fast rate of charge transfer in the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films.35,36

The sandwich configuration of OSCs was fabricated with the “ITO/photoactive layer/Al” configuration (the pristine IEICO-4F, pristine 4TIC and blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC as the photoactive layers). The J–V curves are shown in Fig. 3c. With the blended films of IEICO-4F and 4TIC as the photoactive layer, a higher JSC value was obtained (the JSC was 0.96 mA cm−2) compared to the pristine IEICO-4F-based OSC (the JSC was 0.61 mA cm−2) and the pristine 4TIC-based OSC (the JSC was 0.55 mA cm−2), which indicates that excitons can be dissociated into free charge carriers at the IEICO-4F/4TIC interface and produce a higher photocurrent.44,45

The PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films exhibit the highest fluorescence quenching efficiency and energy transfer efficiency. The SCLC measurements were employed to evaluate the charge-carrier mobility (the SCLC curve and all the values are shown in Fig. 4 and Table 2, respectively). Ternary SCLC devices (for which the ratio of IEICO-4F:4TIC is 6:4) exhibit the highest electron mobility (μe), with a large gain in the μe value from 2.58 × 10−4 to 4.32 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1 for the binary PBDB-T:IEICO-4F-based SCLC device, while μh slightly decreased from 7.84 × 10−4 to 7.62 × 10−4 cm2 V−1 s−1. It is known that excitons can be dissociated at the donor/acceptor interface (D/A), and the electrons will transport along the acceptor channels as well as holes transporting along the donor channels.13,49 Thus, the introduction of a third component material at the binary photoactive layer can strongly affect the μe and slightly affect the μh. Simultaneously, the ternary photoactive layer enhances the μe value and balances the charge carrier transport between the anode and the cathode (the μe/μh values are 0.33, 0.57 and 0.31 corresponding to the IEICO-4F:4TIC ratios of 100:0, 60:40 and 0:100, respectively), which reduces the electron accumulation at the cathode interface.50,51

Surface energy analysis was employed to investigate the role of 4TIC in the ternary photoactive layer, and the detailed calculation method and relevant parameters are described in the ESI.† The third component material 4TIC has a different surface energy (γ) compared to pristine PBDB-T and IEICO-4F, which might offer driving forces for the rough interface of PBDB-T:4TIC binary films and will reflect the role of 4TIC in the ternary photoactive layer.52,53 The surface energies of pristine PBDB-T, IEICO-4F and 4TIC are summarized in Table S3 (ESI†). The related interfacial surface energies between two materials were estimated and are summarized in Table S4 (ESI†). There are similar γ values for both PBDB-T and IEICO-4F (25.25 and 27.75 mJ cm−2 respectively), and a significant enhancement in the γ value for 4TIC (32.24 mJ cm−2) was seen. These similar surface energy values are collectively responsible for good miscibility in blend films, while the high interfacial surface energy difference between PBDB-T and 4TIC is collectively responsible for poorer miscibility.53 If the wetting coefficient (ω) > 1, the 4TIC molecule will locate in the PBDB-T domain. If ω < −1, the 4TIC molecule will locate in the IEICO-4F domain. If −1 < ω < 1, the 4TIC molecule will locate at the interface between PBDB-T and IEICO-4F. From Table S5 (ESI†), the ω value of PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films was −1.56, indicating that 4TIC is located within IEICO-4F domains.9,54,55

Atomic force microscopy (AFM) imaging and X-ray diffraction (XRD) were employed to study the effect of blended electron acceptors on the roughness and crystallinity. For binary PBDB-T:IEICO-4F and PBDB-T:4TIC, and ternary PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC films, the root-mean-square (RMS) roughness values are approximately 2.16 nm, 3.45 nm and 2.19 nm respectively (as shown in Fig. S2, ESI†). The minor roughness variation in PBDB-T:IEICO-4F and PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC films indicates good compatibility between a large amount of PBDB-T and IEICO-4F and a small amount of 4TIC. The large RMS value of the PBDB-T:4TIC binary film indicates poor compatibility between PBDB-T and 4TIC, which is unfavourable for exciton dissociation at the interfaces and might provide charge carrier recombination centres.56,57 Based on the aforementioned interfacial surface energy results, we find that a small interfacial surface energy should be favorable for good compatibility and smooth morphology for the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary film and the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary film. However, the PBDB-T:4TIC blend film possessed a large interfacial surface energy, which is attributed to the poor compatibility between PBDB-T and 4TIC and causes a rough surface in PBDB-T:4TIC films.

For the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary films, the laminar diffraction peaks located at 5.53° correspond to the PBDB-T molecule and a lamellar distance of 8.0334 Å. For the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films, the laminar diffraction peak shifts to 5.72° (the lamellar distance is 7.7528 Å) and the intensities of the diffraction peaks significantly increase, which indicates quite strong structure order and π–π stacking. However, for pristine 4TIC as the electron acceptor, the PBDB-T:4TIC binary films show strongly decreased diffraction peak intensities and a small laminar diffraction angle (5.35°), which indicates sharp reduction in crystallinity and a large lamellar distance of 8.2618 Å. The presence of the blend of IEICO-4F and 4TIC could improve the ternary photoactive layer crystallization, indicating good material mixing.

Fig. 6b displays the plots of logJSCversus the log of light intensity (I), and the β values are 0.952, 0.958, and 0.934 for the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary OSCs, the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC-based ternary OSC and the PBDB-T:4TIC-based binary OSCs, respectively. The gradually increased β values indicate the reduced bimolecular recombination with the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC ternary films as the photoactive layer.74,75 For the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC-based ternary OSC, another plausible reason for the deviation of the β values from unity can be understood in terms of the build-up of the space-charge in the device due to the unbalanced electron–hole mobility. For the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F and PBDB-T:4TIC-based binary OSCs, the build-up of space-charge in the device is due to the unbalanced electron–hole mobility. The results of the VOC and JSC dependence on light intensity indicate that weak monomolecular recombination and bimolecular recombination occur under open circuit and short circuit conditions in the optimized ternary OSCs.

Under maximum power point condition, all three OSCs exhibit different trends between FF and light intensity. High FF values of OSCs indicate efficient charge carrier transport and collection capability and avoid charge recombination. The PBDB-T:IEICO-4F:4TIC-based OSCs exhibited the highest FF value at different light intensities (from 100 mW cm−2 to 5 mW cm−2) compared to the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F and PBDB-T:4TIC-based OSCs (as shown in Fig. 6c), which indicates suppressed charge recombination and more efficient charge collection.35,74

3. Conclusion

In summary, ternary OSC performance parameters were optimized by regulating the ratio of IEICO-4F:4TIC, resulting in a wide energy level offset between the donor and the acceptor and reduced energy losses. A PCE of 11.63% is achieved in the optimized ternary PSCs with enhanced VOC and synergistically without sacrificing the JSC and FF values. Approximately 15% PCE improvement was achieved by employing the ternary strategy in comparison to the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary OSC with a PCE of 10.02%, which primarily benefits from the third component materials having enhanced LUMO energy levels, good compatibility and suppressed charge carrier recombination. Simultaneously, a synergistic improvement without sacrificing the JSC and FF values was achieved even though the absorption spectrum of the ternary photoactive layer did not match the AM 1.5G solar simulator spectrum. This work also provides guidance to select 4TIC as the third component material with good compatibility and shallow LUMO energy levels compared to those of the PBDB-T:IEICO-4F binary photoactive layer for obtaining highly efficient ternary PSCs.
