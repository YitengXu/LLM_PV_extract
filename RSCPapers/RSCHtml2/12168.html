Abstract

Cross-coupling by dual metal/photoredox catalysis is attractive for producing valuable chemical building blocks, where the photoredox catalysts lay the foundations for an efficient and sustainable operation. Herein a series of imine based hexagonal covalent organic frameworks (COFs) were developed as photoredox platforms to cooperate with a molecular Ni catalyst for the cross-coupling of aryl halides with alcohols. By varying the C2 and/or C3 symmetric molecular subunits, the catalytic performance was effectivity modulated with up to one magnitude difference in the solar-to-coupling product conversion efficiency. Combined (photo)electrochemical measurements and electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy revealed the essence of Ni speciation balance to sustain the level of active Ni(I) and the productive coupling cycle. Specifically, 4-[4-(4-formylphenyl)phenyl]benzaldehyde and planar 4,4â€²,4â€²â€²-(1,3,5-triazine-2,4,6-triyl)trianiline, upon assembling into TTâ€“Ph3 COF, translated to band structures that ensure Ni(II) reduction by conduction band electrons to Ni(I) and minimal off-pathway deactivation, granting the best cross-coupling performance.

1. Introduction

In recent years, metallaphotoredox catalysis that orchestrates light-driven electron/energy transfer and the transition metal mediated construction of carbonâ€“heteroatom bonds has emerged as a powerful approach to building valuable organic building blocks.1â€“12 Pioneered by iridium photosensitizers, diverse light-harvesting molecules and materials (e.g., organoborons,8 quantum dots,13 and polymeric carbon nitrides9,12) have been developed and implemented in metallaphotoredox catalysis for a broad range of (cross)coupling reactions, aimed at improving solar-to-chemical conversion efficiency, (metal) atomic efficiency and operational simplicity. In metallaphotoredox catalytic Câ€“O or Câ€“N coupling of aryl halides with OH/NH group-containing compounds, Ni(II) is developed as the active site, which is reduced by photogenerated electrons, and then enters a cycle of the oxidative addition (OA) and the reductive elimination (RE) (Scheme 1).3,4,14â€“16
Scheme 1 Structural illustration of the hexagonal COF series (TTâ€“Phn) with TT-(C3) and various C2 subunits, together with the working hypothesis for COFs/Ni dual catalysis involving reductive activation of Ni(II), and the Ni(I)/Ni(III) oxidative-addition/reductive-elimination cycle, and off-pathway Ni(I) deactivation.

With regard to their advantages in terms of stability and separation, heterogeneous photocatalysts promise sustained and less waste-intensive protocols. Moreover, their feasible structural engineering offers a great opportunity to regulate the kinetics of key processes (e.g., reductive metal activation and oxidative addition, Scheme 1) to gain enhanced activity.9â€“14,17 Cyanamide modification of carbon nitride, for example, with an extended electron lifetime, substantially boosted the Câ€“N and Câ€“O cross-coupling efficiency.9 The surface functionalities of heterogeneous materials (sensitized TiO2,11 C3N4,9,12 or covalent frameworks5) that chemically link the metal catalysts, by promoting electron transfer to reduce the metal catalyst, typically Ni(II) complexes, exhibits faster conversion and turnover number/frequency. Of note, the accumulation of low-valent Ni species may lead to detrimental off-pathway dimerization or precipitation, which highlights the complexity arising from the interplay of multiple processes in dual metal/photoredox catalysis. Designing heterogeneous photoredox catalysts that can maintain the productive catalytic cycle and/or mitigate off-pathway deactivation is important for an efficient and sustained cross-coupling performance.10,15,16

Covalent organic frameworks (COFs), with ordered spatial arrangements of molecular subunits assembled via covalent bonds, and interlayer stacking, possess distinct energy bands/charge transfer channels.18â€“22 Variation in the condensation monomers (subunit precursor) furnishes COFs with progressively modulated electronic properties, topological structures and charge transport properties that govern their performance under irradiation.23â€“29 In imine-based COFs prepared from triphenylaryl-aldehyde and hydrazine (Nx-COFs), enhanced hydrogen evolution activity was observed with an increase in x from 0 to 3, exhibiting strong correlation with the dihedral angle of the C3 monomer. The planar triazine center promoting charge separation/transport also benefited the selective aerobic oxidation of sulfide in olefin based COFs.30 In addition, the C2 monomers of COFs can play important roles.25,26 These studies highlight the potential of COF editing in modulating both photoredox properties and the closely linked metal cycle to gain optimized performance in metallaphotoredox catalysis.

Herein, imine-based hexagonal 2,4,6-triphenyl-1,3,5-triazine (TT-)COF series were successfully assembled, featuring phenyl-, biphenyl- or p-terphenyl- as the C2 subunit (Scheme 1, TTâ€“Phn, n = 1â€“3). A reference COF, TBâ€“Ph1, where the C3 subunit was replaced by 1,3,5-triphenylbenzene was also prepared. Using the etherification of aryl bromide as the model reaction (Câ€“O cross-coupling), the subunit-dependent activity was established in their collaboration with a molecular Ni catalyst. Other than the charge separation/transport property, the observed dependency is more relevant to the ability of the assembled COFs to sustain the productive Ni cycle, by activating Ni(II) to Ni(I), and/or inhibiting Ni deactivation. The best-performing TTâ€“Ph3 showed good substrate/functionality capability and well-sustained activity in multiple reaction runs.

3.3 Origins of the activity dependency on COF subunits

An Ni/photoredox dual catalysis mechanism has been widely recognized for light-driven speciation evolution to form a Câ€“O bond (Scheme 1). In a nutshell, quinuclidine devoured the photogenerated holes during irradiation, and the photogenerated electrons were available for the reduction of Ni(II) (ligated to bipyridine) to the active Ni(I) state. Ni(I) undergoes oxidative addition to aryl bromides, converting to BrNi(III)Ar, followed by â€“Br/â€“OR exchange and an energetically favoured reductive elimination, affording Arâ€“OR. However, the off-pathway deactivation of Ni(I) to Ni black proceeds alongside.

As shown in Fig. S7,â€  the light-harvesting efficiencies at the irradiation wavelengths of all the COF variants were quite close; and their photogenerated (conduction band) electrons can readily reduce the Ni(II) catalyst to trigger the Ni(I)/Ni(III) (oxidative additionâ€“reductive elimination) cycle that forges Câ€“O bonds. However, according to the onset oxidation potential of quinuclidine (1.1 VNHE),15 which supplies the reductive equivalents for Ni reduction (activation), the TBâ€“Ph1 (EVB = 1.07 VNHE) mediated photoredox cycle is less favoured due to the thermodynamically uphill hole trapping and rapid non-productive charge recombination, which partially account for the low activity. All other COFs would enable smooth photoredox turnovers. In particular, TTâ€“Ph3 bearing a deeper valence band is expected to enable faster hole trapping and more available reducing equivalents for Ni(II) to Ni(I) conversion.

To shed light on the Ni species evolution in the dual catalysis, semi in situ electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy was conducted for TTâ€“Phn/Ni/quinuclidine under irradiation in the absence of aryl bromide. As shown in Fig. 3a, a sharp g = 2.003 signal was observed for all COF variants, characteristic of the intrinsic localized electrons (in line with their n-type feature).43,44 An additional broad, anisotropic S = 1/2 signal in the g = 2.319 to g = 2.039 region appeared after irradiation; a g-tensor with close-to-axial principal values, [gxx, gyy] = [2.231, 2.114], pointing to monomeric Ni(I) with 3d9 configuration (the gzz value is shielded by the strong signal of photogenerated electrons at g = 2.003).45â€“47 TTâ€“Ph3 exhibited the most notable Ni(I) signal followed by TTâ€“Ph2, while TTâ€“Ph1 barely showed an Ni(I) band. The higher level of Ni(I) in TTâ€“Ph3 can be attributed to the valence band position (discussed above), which is beneficial for the oxidative addition to aryl halides and the productive Ni cycle. On the other hand, the deactivation processes in the three catalytic systems were estimated by the amount of Ni deposited in the recycled COFs. It was found that TTâ€“Ph3 contained a much lower level of precipitated Ni black (0.3 wt%) than TTâ€“Ph1 and TTâ€“Ph2 (1.1% and 2.4%, Table S8â€ ). Considering the Ni(I)/Ni(0) reduction potential (âˆ’1.05 to âˆ’1.25 VNHE) and the COF conduction potentials (âˆ’1.13 to âˆ’1.2 VNHE), the lower conduction band potential of TTâ€“Ph3 might account for a less favored Ni(I) to Ni(0) conversion and the subsequent aggregation. Although the minimal Ni deposited in TTâ€“Ph3 could also be related to its larger interior surface that decreases the probability of Ni(I)â€“Ni(I) disproportionation followed by metallic Ni aggregation/deactivation, the difference in the surface area (Fig. S5â€ ) does not correspond to the activity (Table 1 and Fig. S9â€ ), thus surface area is considered not to be the primary influencing factor for Ni speciation. Taken together, the interfacial charge transfer and balance of Ni speciation are essential for the well-cooperating and sustained dual catalytic cycles.

We further conducted transient photocurrent measurements to evaluate the charge separation/transport property of the COF variants (Fig. 3d). TBâ€“Ph1 demonstrates lower photocurrent density than TTâ€“Ph1, which is consistent with the charge transport facilitated by the higher degree of COF conjugation and better crystallinity of planar TT-COFs (the dihedral angle of the TT-molecule is 0Â°, while the value for TB- is 38.7Â°).23 However, the most active TTâ€“Ph3 demonstrated the lowest photocurrent density in the TTâ€“Phn series. The poor correlation of photocurrent density with activity implies that charge separation/transport is a subordinate process in dual COFs/Ni catalysis, trumped by an interfacial charge transfer process in the reaction studied here.

The results presented here collectively suggested for the COFs/Ni-catalysed Câ€“O coupling that the performance was defined predominantly by the interfacial charge transfer processes that govern the Ni speciation under reaction conditions; the internal charge transport properties played a less important role. The energy band position is important to ensure the well-balanced formation and consumption of Ni(I) (by oxidative-addition) via interfacial electron transfer, to sustain both the photoredox and Ni cycles that are essential for efficient metallaphotoredox catalysis.

3.4 Photocatalytic substrate scope

With the best-performing TTâ€“Ph3 recognized, we investigated the substrate scope of TTâ€“Ph3/Ni. Good to excellent isolated yields (78â€“92%) of Câ€“O coupling products were obtained for most aryl bromides with an electron-deficient group in the para-position. Various functional groups, such as nitrile, carbonyl, and ester were all compatible with the photocatalytic protocol (Table 3, 1â€“5). 5-Methoxypyridine-2-carbonitrile (6) was produced in a moderate yield of 62% in the reaction of methanol with a heteroarene bromide, 5-bromo-2-cyanopyridine. Substrates bearing an electron-donating methoxy group in the para position showed poor conversion to 1,4-dimethoxybenzene (7), even with the more easily activated aryl iodide (0.7% for 4-bromoanisole, 0.8% for 4-iodoanisole, 20 h). We also scrutinized the scope of the alcohol partners. Under standard conditions, primary alcohols, including trifluoroethanol, ethanol and benzyl alcohol, underwent smooth coupling with 4-bromobenzonitrile, with 88â€“93% yield of the corresponding ethers 4-(2,2,2-trifluoroethoxy)benzonitrile (8), 4-ethoxybenzonitrile (9) and 4-benzyloxybenzonitrile (12). The reaction with isopropanol gave a relatively lower isolated yield (62%) of 4-isopropoxybenzonitrile (10). Hydroxylation of 4-bromobenzonitrile also proceeded successfully, with 62% 4-hydroxybenzonitrile (11, GC yield) produced in the presence of 20 equivalents of water. When phenol was used, biphenyl ether product 4-phenoxybenzonitrile was produced (GC yield 13.1%) after 20 h of irradiation (14). Pyrrolidine was also tested as a N-coupling partner (13), and 97% 4-(1-pyrrolidinyl)benzonitrile was obtained.

Conclusions

In summary, imine-based COFs were successfully assembled from TT- and C2 symmetric â€“Ph1â€“3 monomers. These COF variants are attractive photoredox catalysts to work with an Ni catalyst, forging carbonâ€“oxygen bonds from various aryl bromides and alcohols/water under mild conditions. Variation of the C3/C2 subunits resulted in different band energy diagrams and interfacial charge transfer thermodynamics/kinetics, which determines the Ni speciation that is relevant to the productive cycle as well as deactivation. TTâ€“Ph3, with balanced interfacial charge transfer processes, well sustains the dual photoredox/Ni cycles, offering the best performance in light-driven Câ€“O coupling. Future work will focus on the design of the next generation of integrated heterogeneous COF platforms with novel metal sites that are resistant to deactivation.
