Abstract

Currentâ€“voltage hysteresis is a critical issue that impacts the photovoltaic performance of perovskite solar cells, and thus, it is imperative to develop high-efficiency perovskite solar cells without hysteresis behavior. Herein, we report the fabrication of efficient planar heterojunction perovskite solar cells employing solution-processed NiCo2O4 as the hole transport layer. The spinel NiCo2O4 thin film obtained via a solâ€“gel process exhibits high optical transparency, well-matched energy bands, smooth film morphology and decent electrical conductivity, much better than NiO, a typical hole transport layer for perovskite solar cells. We herein demonstrated that due to excellent hole extraction, transport, and collection properties of the solution-processed NiCo2O4, the device efficiency of up to 18.16% was achieved for the planar heterojunction perovskite solar cells. More importantly, the devices with NiCo2O4 as the HTL exhibited hysteresis-free behavior regardless of the voltage scan rate, which out-performed NiO-based devices.

1. Introduction

Solar cells based on organicâ€“inorganic lead halide perovskites as the light absorbing materials have attracted extensive interest due to their extraordinary characteristics such as broadband light absorption with a low band gap, high light absorption coefficient, long charge carrier lifetime and admirable charge transport properties.1â€“5 Using the most advanced perovskite solar cells (PSCs), certified PCEs exceeding 23% have been achieved in a single-junction device through extensive efforts in material engineering and device optimization.6â€“8 Compared to typical mesoscopic device structures, planar heterojunction devices with p-i-n structures have received significant attention due to their simple device structure and easy manufacturing process. The use of various materials for the interfacial layer can tune not only the photovoltaic performance but also the stability of the devices;9â€“12 in addition, this is a suitable structure to achieve flexible and transparent devices because of the low-temperature solution processing and high light transparency of the thin interfacial layers. Thus, the p-i-n structure is promising for the fabrication of practical solar cells.13â€“15 More significantly, p-i-n devices have been reported to have negligible hysteresis with the varying scan rate and direction as compared to the n-i-p device;16 thus, a stable power output can be expected from the p-i-n PSC devices.16

To achieve high photovoltaic performance of p-i-n PSCs, the selection of the hole transport material is important because the movement, such as transport and collection, of charges is mainly dominated by the interface between the perovskite layer and electrode; moreover, it is important to quickly transport the hole because the hole travels slower than the electron.17â€“19 A hole transport layer (HTL) for planar PSCs must meet several requirements, such as a pinhole-free surface, high transparency, and well-matched energy levels to both the electrode and the perovskite layer, to deliver maximum photovoltaic performance.20â€“22 In this regard, a variety of semiconductors have been used as HTLs in planar PSCs, and promising PCEs have been achieved.23â€“26 Among them, solution-processed p-type metal oxides possess several advantages over other semiconductors in terms of electronic conductivity, chemical stability, robustness, and optical transparency in the UV-Vis range, which would provide improved hole extraction and transport properties. Nickel oxide (NiO) is an interesting type of metal oxide that has been successfully applied in optoelectronic devices;27 in recent years, NiO has been extensively investigated as a robust HTL for planar PSCs, and promising PCEs exceeding 17% have been demonstrated due to the deep valence band (VB) edge, wide band gap, and hydrophilic surface of NiO.28,29 However, most of the p-type metal oxides have low electrical conductivity and unstable surface defects, which result in inefficient hole extraction and thus accelerated charge recombination. Thus, it is highly desirable to develop a p-type inorganic semiconductor with excellent optoelectronic properties for use in the HTL of planar PSCs.

In recent years, nickel cobalt oxide (NiCo2O4) has been reported as a feasible p-type semiconductor for a variety of optoelectronics such as anodic oxygen evolution catalyst, supercapacitors, infrared transparent conducting electrodes, dye-sensitized solar cells, and sensors.30â€“35 NiCo2O4 with a cubic spinel structure, in which all the Ni2+ ions occupy the octahedral sites and the Co3+ ions are distributed between the tetrahedral and octahedral sites, delivers a superior electrical conductivity (âˆ¼500 S cmâˆ’1) as compared to NiO and cobalt (II or III) oxide. NiCo2O4 possesses a wide optical band gap (âˆ¼2.4 eV) and a deep VB of âˆ¼5.3 eV, which meets the optoelectronic requirements well as an HTL in p-i-n PSCs. Moreover, the core elements of NiCo2O4 are abundant and environmentally friendly; this suggests that NiCo2O4 can be an appropriate replacement of metal-oxide HTLs to achieve efficient p-i-n PSCs. Very recently, Choulis and co-workers have reported the use of NiCo2O4 nanoparticles (NPs) prepared via combustion synthesis in the HTL of PSCs, and the PCEs of up to 15.5% have been achieved.36 Choy and co-workers have also synthesized ultra-small NPs of NiCo2O4 using a deamination reaction of Coâ€“NH3 in the presence of Ni(OH)2.37 The uniform HTL obtained from NiCo2O4 NPs resulted in the PCEs of up to 18.23% with high stability. According to these studies, the preparation of solution-processed NiCo2O4 HTLs via the facile solâ€“gel protocol is still of interest to achieve n-i-p PSCs with high efficiency.

Herein, we report the solution-processed p-i-n PSCs with high photovoltaic performance obtained using solâ€“gel-processed NiCo2O4 as an excellent HTL. The solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 film generates a uniform HTL with high optical transparency and decent electrical conductivity, which are comparable or even better than those of NiO. The optimized solâ€“gel-processed NiCo2O4 HTL exhibited promising PCEs of up to 18.16%; a systematic evaluation of the hysteresis revealed almost negligible mismatch between the Jâ€“V scans of NiCo2O4â€“based devices. Encouragingly, NiCo2O4 generated better photovoltaic performance and comparable ambient stability under continuous illumination as compared to NiO. All these results validate the significant potentials of the solâ€“gel-processed NiCo2O4 HTL for achieving high-photovoltaic performance p-i-n PSCs.

3. Results and discussion

The solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin films were prepared using a precursor solution of nickel(II) acetate tetrahydrate and cobalt(II) acetate tetrahydrate (at the molar ratio of 1 to 2) in 2-methoxyethanol. As is well-known, the morphology of solâ€“gel-processed metal oxide thin films varies with the annealing temperature.38 The influence of annealing temperature on the surface morphology of the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin film was investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Fig. 1). The as-spun NiCo2O4 thin film shows a flat surface without any discernable grain boundaries because no solâ€“gel conversion occurred, and the surface cracks originated due to shrinkage of the film as the solvent evaporated. After the thin film was annealed at 340 Â°C, the film exhibited a fully covered compact surface with well-crystallized and densely packed grains having the grain size of âˆ¼10 nm. The single-crystalline cubic structure of spinel NiCo2O4 was confirmed by the X-ray diffractogram (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ).39 As the annealing temperature increases to 400 and 500 Â°C, the grains grow larger, and thus, the grain boundaries become quite apparent. In addition, the NiCo2O4 thin films annealed at high temperatures possess some big particles that can act as defect sites for charge recombination. The surface morphology of the NiCo2O4 thin film was further studied by atomic force microscopy (AFM). As shown in Fig. 2, the NiCo2O4 thin films exhibited very smooth and compact film surfaces with the root-mean-square (RMS) roughness of 3.1 nm, which was comparable or even smoother than that of the solâ€“gel-processed nickel oxide (NiO) thin film. The flat and uniform film surface is favorable as a robust underlayer for the consecutive solution processing of the perovskite thin film in p-i-n PSCs. Note that the grain size for NiCo2O4 (âˆ¼120 nm) is slightly larger than that for NiO (âˆ¼60 nm), as shown in Fig. S2 (ESIâ€ ). The electrical conductivities of the NiCo2O4 and NiO thin films were compared using conductive AFM (c-AFM) (Fig. 2c and d). A significantly higher current flow is clearly observed for the NiCo2O4 thin film, which corresponds to much higher electrical conductivity, as evident by the sheet resistance measurement (Table S1, ESIâ€ ). Since excellent conductivity is a prerequisite for the interfacial layer to achieve high-efficiency PSCs, NiCo2O4 is a promising candidate for the HTL of efficient p-i-n PSCs.

The charge transport/extraction efficiencies of the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 films were assessed by monitoring the photoluminescence (PL) of the perovskite layer with regard to individual HTLs. Fig. 5c shows that the PL of the perovskite film is quenched significantly in the presence of the NiCo2O4 thin films in the order of the annealing temperature of 340 Â°C > 400 Â°C > 500 Â°C. An effective PL quenching suggests more selective extraction of the hole carriers from the perovskite layer. We further investigated the transient PL quenching using a time-correlated single photon counting system (Fig. 5d). We then conducted electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) to examine the advantage of NiCo2O4 for improved charge transport in the devices. Fig. 8c shows the Nyquist plots for the devices with different HTLs measured in the dark with a 0.75 V bias in the frequency range from 1 MHz to 10 mHz, and the fitting results of the equivalent circuit are listed in Table 2 (The Nyquist plot for the devices under irradiation and the fitting result are also shown in Fig. S6 and Table S4, ESIâ€ ). The high frequency intercept to the Zâ€² axis, which represents the series resistance (Rs), is mainly associated with the serial resistance of the ITO, HTL and the perovskite layer. Specifically, the semicircle in the high-frequency range is correlated with interfacial resistance (Rtrans) occurring at the interface between the perovskite layer and the HTL, whereas the semicircle in the low-frequency range corresponds to the recombination resistance (Rrec) at the interface of the perovskite/HTL. For the device with NiCo2O4, Rtrans was substantially decreased; this indicated quick transport of hole carriers from the perovskite to HTL, and thus, charge recombination at the interface between perovskite and NiCo2O4 could be suppressed. On the other hand, the NiCo2O4-based device exhibits higher Rrec than NiO; this also suggests reduced recombination rate for the device employing NiCo2O4 because the recombination rate is inversely proportional to Rrec.55 Based on the EIS results, it can be demonstrated that charge transfer and recombination in p-i-n PSCs are significantly improved by replacing the conventional NiO with NiCo2O4; this is well consistent with the IMPS and IMVS analyses. Consequently, the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin film is a promising HTL for efficient charge transport and reduced charge recombination, which is responsible for the enhanced photovoltaic performance of p-i-n PSCs.56

To investigate the device stability of the PSCs employing the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 HTL, bare devices without any encapsulation or desiccant were stored under ambient conditions with continuous illumination. As shown in Fig. 8d, the devices using NiO and NiCo2O4 as the HTL exhibited a similar PCE variation upon storage, maintaining 47% and 44% of their initial efficiencies after 96 h, respectively. The variation in other device parameters, such as VOC, JSC, and FF, is shown in Fig. S8 (ESIâ€ ). In another set of stability tests, the NiCo2O4-based devices still exhibited an inferior stability as compared to NiO-based devices (Fig. S9, ESIâ€ ). However, via further optimization of crystallinity, film thickness or electronic structure, the stability of the NiCo2O4-based devices could be improved as much as that of NiO-based devices. Therefore, solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 is a promising candidate for the replacement of NiO to achieve high-performance p-i-n PSCs.

4. Conclusions

We herein demonstrated a simple but effective strategy for the preparation of a promising HTL to achieve high-performance p-i-n PSCs; the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin films exhibited high electrical conductivity, high optical transparency, a well-matched work function and effective hole extraction from the perovskite layer. Based on the abovementioned advantages, the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin film delivered the champion PCE of 18.16% as an HTL in p-i-n PSCs. Encouragingly, the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin film generated a marginal hysteresis at very slow scan rates. In addition, the NiCo2O4-based devices exhibited significantly improved PCEs as compared to the NiO-based devices. All the results analyzed in this study clearly suggest the advantages of using the solâ€“gel-driven NiCo2O4 thin film as a promising HTL to achieve highly efficient and stable p-i-n PSCs for practical use.
