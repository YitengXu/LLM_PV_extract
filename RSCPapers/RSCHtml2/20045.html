Carbon nitrides and metal nanoparticles: from controlled synthesis to design principles for improved photocatalysis

Abstract

The use of sunlight to drive chemical reactions via photocatalysis is of paramount importance towards a sustainable future. Among several photocatalysts, earth-abundant polymeric carbon nitride (PCN, often wrongly named g-C3N4) has emerged as an attractive candidate due to its ability to absorb light efficiently in the visible and near-infrared ranges, chemical stability, non-toxicity, straightforward synthesis, and versatility as a platform for constructing hybrid materials. Especially, hybrids with metal nanoparticles offer the unique possibility of combining the catalytic, electronic, and optical properties of metal nanoparticles with PCN. Here, we provide a comprehensive overview of PCN materials and their hybrids, emphasizing heterostructures with metal nanoparticles. We focus on recent advances encompassing synthetic strategies, design principles, photocatalytic applications, and charge-transfer mechanisms. We also discuss how the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effect of some noble metals NPs (e.g. Au, Ag, and Cu), bimetallic compositions, and even non-noble metals NPs (e.g., Bi) synergistically contribute with PCN in light-driven transformations. Finally, we provide a perspective on the field, in which the understanding of the enhancement mechanisms combined with truly controlled synthesis can act as a powerful tool to the establishment of the design principles needed to take the field of photocatalysis with PCN to a new level, where the desired properties and performances can be planned in advance, and the target material synthesized accordingly.

1. Introduction

The adverse environmental problems caused by the intensive consumption of fossil fuels have led to an increasing interest in the use of alternative energy sources to serve and deliver power to human activities.1–3 Solar energy has an enormous potential as a clean, abundant, and economical resource.4 Therefore, the efficient harvesting and conversion of solar energy has been a worldwide priority target in the past decade.

In this context, the use of sunlight to drive and control chemical reactions (solar to chemical energy conversion) via photocatalysis represents a very promising approach.5 In photocatalysis, the most studied examples are endothermic reactions, such as H2O splitting and CO2 reduction, where the energy of solar photons can be stored in chemical bonds by using semiconductors as photocatalysts.6,7 Even though a lot of progress has been achieved in the development of semiconductor photocatalysts, most systems require solar energy input in the UV region for band gap excitation.8–10 Thus, most photocatalysts present low solar-energy utilization, as solar light is composed of ultraviolet, visible and infrared components (accounting for 5, 43, and 52%, respectively).11 These features illustrate that it is still very challenging to design new photocatalysts that are abundant, stable, facile to fabricate, and show high performances under visible-light or near-infrared excitation.10

The search for suitable semiconductor photocatalysts to optimise existing or design novel chemical processes is one of the persistent goals of sustainable chemistry. An optimal material would combine the ability to absorb light efficiently, especially in the visible and near-infrared range, and be stable in working chemical and thermal conditions. Moreover, it should be non-toxic, preferentially composed by abundant elements, and easily processable. Mostly explored semiconductors are so far constructed from transition metal ions with d0 or d10 electronic configuration, along with counter-anions such as chalcogens.12,13 However, it remains challenging to engineer and tune the electronic properties of metal chalcogenides. As a result, these structures have rarely been targets of systematic optimisation.14,15 Traditional inorganic semiconductors are limited in the nature of their active sites and thus the scope of applications that they can accomplish.16,17 On the other hand, while organic polymeric catalysts have the advantage of flexibility for optimization, they suffer from serious swelling and stability problems.17 Consequently, it is extremely desirable to have an active and stable photocatalyst that could allow large variations in the structure without having to change its overall composition.

Polymeric carbon nitride (PCN) (often wrongly named g-C3N4 in the literature) is a highly active photocatalyst which, at least in principle, meets above-mentioned requirements (Fig. 1). The history of carbon nitride can be traced back to the embryonic form, “melon”, made by Berzelius and named by Liebig in 1834, being regarded as one of the oldest synthetic polymers.14,16,18 The framework topology of linear polymer melon is presumably consisting of interconnected tri-s-triazines via secondary nitrogens 4,19 while poly(heptazine imide)s 5 comprises rings of six heptazine units that are linked by NH bridges to form a 2D extended network.20–22 The “perfect” graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) material is comprised solely of C and N bonds in the form of p-conjugated planar 2D sheets of poly(tri-s-triazines) interconnected via tertiary amines18,23 with no active basicity, due to the lack of electron localized in the π state.24 However, “perfect” g-C3N4 has not been synthesised to this day.21 The closest from g-C3N4 synthesised up to date were the poly(heptazine imide)s 5 and the triazine-based graphitic carbon nitride (TGCN) 6, the last consists of nitrogen-linked triazine units of sp2-hybridized carbon and nitrogen atoms forming a 2D layered material.25,26 Due to the poor understanding of the actual structures of carbon nitride materials, several reports do not allow a precise identification of which mentioned structure is the one obtained. Because of this, we decided to use in this review the more generic term “polymeric carbon nitride (PCN)” to refer to all the carbon nitride based materials (Fig. 1).

In general, most of the synthesis routes lead to predominantly to the linear polymer melon with medium to poor crystallinity and a high degree of disorder, but still showing high photocatalytic activity.14–16 Bulk PCN is typically prepared by the direct thermal condensation of low-cost nitrogen containing precursors (cyanamide, dicyandiamide).27–30 This results in carbon nitrides with several shortcomings, including low specific surface area, insufficient visible light utilization, and, particularly, rapid recombination of photogenerated charge carriers.30–34 High separation/transfer efficiency of photogenerated electron–hole pairs is the most desirable property in PCN-based photocatalysts. Several strategies can be employed to achieve this objective, such as (i) doping the PCN structure with sulphur,35,36 boron,37 oxygen38 and phosphorous;35,39,40 (ii) modifying its synthesis method (e.g. changing and combining different nitrogen containing precursors,18,41,42 introducing mesoporous using hard templates,43,44 microwave-assisted synthesis,45 synthesis in molten salt,46 and synthesis in ionic liquid47); (iii) performing a post treatment (e.g. exfoliation48–51 and thermal treatment18,41,42); (iv) controlling the defects in its structure;52,53 (v) and/or combining it with other structures (e.g. semiconductors,30,54–58 metal nanoparticles (NPs)30,59–61 and metal–organic frameworks (MOFs)29,62–64).

Among the recent strategies in the development of PCN photocatalytic systems, PCN-based heterostructures are one the most promising due to its feasibility and effectiveness for the spatial separation of photogenerated electron–hole pairs.30 The PCN-based heterostructures are capable to suppress the recombination of photogenerated charge carriers while endowing the photocatalysts with unique features that arise from the synergism between PCN and the other components. Therefore, we believe that the rational design of PCN-based heterostructures could provide promising routes for creating highly efficient visible-light driven photocatalysts for chemical synthesis, environmental remediation, and energy applications.

Herein, we aim at providing a general overview on heterostructures comprised of PCN and metal nanoparticles (NPs). The synthesis, applications in photocatalytic reactions, and the mechanisms behind the synergistic effects involving PCN and metal NPs will be covered. This review will also focus on how the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effect of some noble metals NPs (e.g. Au, Ag, and Cu), bimetallic compositions (AuPd, AuAg, AuPt, etc.), and even non-noble metals NPs (e.g., Bi) can enhance the catalytic properties of PCN in several light-driven transformations.

2. Synthesis of PCN

PCN is commonly synthesized by thermal treatment that leads to a combination of polyaddition and polycondensation reactions of the nitrogen-rich precursors such as urea, thiourea, melamine, cyanamide, and dicyandiamide.65–71 This approach is described in Fig. 1. The precursors condensate into dicyandiamide 1 and melamine (triamino-s-triazine) 2, which forms tri-s-triazine units, also known as melem 3, by polycondensation of melamines. Then, the PCN are formed through the further condensation of the tri-s-triazine units or by the further condensation of melanine with smaller nitrogen-rich precursor molecules in the case of TGCN 6.25,34,72

Interestingly, it has been established that the choice of precursors significantly influences the electronic band structures and textual properties of obtained pristine bulk PCN.30 For example, when thiourea is used to prepare PCN, it exhibits a slightly smaller bandgap when compared with the PCN prepared from urea.73,74 On the other hand, the PCN prepared from urea generally presents much higher specific surface area than that prepared from thiourea and melamine.18,73

Another aspect is that the PCN properties strongly depends on the nitrogen-rich precursors and temperature employed during the thermal polymerization, which represents an attractive method due to its simplicity and low cost. The nitrogen-rich small molecules polymerize into PCN in temperatures ranging between 450 and 650 °C.30,73,75 Bellow 450 °C, the polymerization is incomplete. Over 650 °C, the PCN start to decompose.66 Different precursors calcined at different temperatures can lead to a range of band-gaps and specific surface areas. For instance, urea can produce PCN with band gaps varying from 2.68 to 2.78 eV and surface areas from 31 to 288 m2 g−1, while thiourea can lead to carbon nitrides with band gaps from 2.58 to 2.76 eV and surface areas significantly smaller, corresponding to 11–52 m2 g−1.18 Furthermore, even the atmosphere composition used during the pyrolysis process can great influence the nature of the product obtained, producing nitrogen vacancies and consequently altering the PCN band gap.76 It is important to highlight that these examples involve only the modification in the nature of precursors and calcination temperature. Many other approaches can also be employed to tune carbon nitride properties, such as doping the PCN structure,35–40 introduce mesoporous using hard templates,13,43,44 using other synthesis methods apart from thermal condensation,45–47 adding a post-treatment (e.g. exfoliation48–51 and thermal treatment18,41,42), controlling the defects in its structure,52 and combining it with other structures, such as semiconductors,30,54–58 metal NPs,30,59–61 and metal–organic frameworks (MOFs).29,62–64

2.1. Challenges in characterizing PCNs

PCN photocatalysts are commonly characterized especially by X-ray diffraction (XRD), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). However, the carbon nitride-based materials still have a poorly understood structure. By using the abovementioned techniques, is still not possible to state which of the PCN-based structures is the predominant in the generally reported materials. We would like to make the readers aware that some of the reproduced schematic representations reprinted in this review may not be the representation that better match with the characterization presented in the respective reports.

XRD is an important technique due the two characteristic diffraction peaks at ca. 13.0 and ca. 27.4° that appear for the PCN-based materials.18 The first related to in-plane repeats between heptazine (C6N7) units (spacing of 6.91 Å) and the last related to the π–π stacking motif of the same heptazines on top of each other (spacing of 3.20 Å). Despite the characteristic π–π stacking between the heptazine units, the PCN-based materials cannot be classified as graphitic, since its structure is not extended in a 2D network.21,77 The XPS measurements are especially useful to investigate the chemical environment of carbon and nitrogen elements in PCN, evidencing sp2-bonded carbon in N–CN (ca. 288.1 eV) and the sp2-bonded nitrogen in C–NC (ca. 398.7 eV). Furthermore, XPS allows to characterize elemental doped PCN and estimate the degree of defects in the PCN structure by the nitrogen in tertiary nitrogen groups (ca. 400.3 eV) and the presence of amino groups (C–N–H, ca. 401.4 eV). Most XPS results reported in the literature present significant amino peaks, evidencing structures more similar to melon (polymeric) than to graphitic structures like TGCN. Because of the high thermal stability of PCNs, the decomposition temperature obtained by TGA can be a simple way to confirm the proper polymerization of the nitrogen-rich precursors.18 Despite less developed, spectroscopy techniques also are employed to characterize PCN. IR spectra normally show signals in the ∼3000 cm−1, which is related to primary and secondary amine groups, the last probably forming bridges between the heptazine units. This, once again, confirms a more polymeric and less graphitic-like structure.78 Raman also is employed to characterize PCN structures. In general, Raman spectra for PCN presents two relevant peaks, one observed at ∼1345 cm−1 (D band) and ∼1570 cm−1 (G band). The G band corresponds to the symmetric E2g vibrational mode in graphite-like structures and is attributed to graphite-like sp2 microdomains in the products, while the D band corresponds to disordered sp2 microdomains introduced by the linking with N atoms.59 Although useful, the spectroscopic investigation of PCN by Raman still much less developed than other materials, such as carbon nanostructures. While Raman enables to estimate the number of layers in few layered graphene and to predict the diameter of single-walled carbon nanotubes, there are no studies that allow us to infer structural data for PCN-based materials from Raman spectroscopy.

3. Properties

3.1. Morphological features

As a polymer, g-C3N4 has a flexible structure and is suited to form different morphologies with the assistance of different templates during the synthesis as well as post-treatments. However, differently from most polymeric materials, PCN can endure high temperatures (up to 650 °C),79 even in oxidizing atmosphere.73 PCN have been obtained in several morphologies, such as porous PCN (3D nanostructures),43,44,80 hollow spheres,81 nanorods82/nanowires83/nanotubes84 (1D nanostructures), and nanosheets (2D nanostructures).51,85,86

Mesoporous polymeric carbon nitrides (mp-PCN) can be easily obtained by a hard template method. It has been successfully synthesized using various nitrogen-rich precursors in the presence of silica NPs43,44 or ordered mesoporous silica (e.g. SBA-15)80,87 as a hard template. The removal of those silica structures by a treatment with HF then generates a 3D structure of PCN with a large surface area, which can reach up to 373 m2 g−1.18 The mp-PCN synthesis, functionalization, and applications was recently reviewed by Lakhi and colleagues.88

The preparation of hollow PCN spheres is challenging due to its layered structure, which is more susceptible to collapse. However, Sun et al.81 were successful in coating monodisperse silica NPs with thin mesoporous silica shells and then used it as a hard template to prepare hollow PCN nanospheres. This strategy is similar to the one used to prepare mesoporous PCN. The silica was then removed by a post-treatment with HF at the end of the synthesis. PCN in the form of hollow spheres are very attractive because they are able to harvest more incident light through successive reflections within the hollow structure, producing more photoinduced charge carriers serving as light-harvesting antennas.18

1D nanostructured PCN such as nanorods, nanowires, nanobelts, and nanotubes are also attractive morphologies because their length, diameter, and aspect ratio can be tuned. This, in turn, can lead to unique and tunable chemical, optical and electronic properties.18 1D nanostructured PCN displaying porous structures, large surface areas, and charge-carrier mobility can be prepared using different templating (e.g. SBA-15)80,82 or non-templating (e.g. reflux in solvent)84,89,90 methods.

Bulk PCN display a low specific surface area, corresponding to <10 m2 g−1, due to the stacking of PCN layers. Thus, several studies have been focused on exfoliation methods to obtain nanosheets from bulk PCN. The most common strategies include ultrasonication-assisted liquid exfoliation,49,51,85,91–96 acid exfoliation,97,98 liquid ammonia-assisted lithiation,92 and thermal oxidation.49,99 2D PCN nanosheets exhibit interesting features, such as large specific surface areas, prolonged lifetime of charge carriers, enhanced electron transport property, reduced recombination rate of charge carriers, and improved charge transfer and separation efficiencies.34,100,101 Due to the combination of these very attractive features, it represents the most studied PCN morphology.

3.2. Surface basic and acid sites

Among the several features of PCN, the presence of acid and basic sites in its surface is one of the most interesting but poorly understood properties. PCN has Lewis basic sites (Fig. 2a), which can be the nitrogen present in the six-membered ring (A) or nitrogen present as primary (C) and/or secondary (B) amine groups on the terminating edges due to defects in the PCN structure.66 The surface basicity of PCN can be confirmed by temperature-programmed desorption of CO2 (CO2-TPD).66 This powerful technique also allows to differentiate and estimate the amount of the three basic site types. These basic sites can be applied to catalyze several reactions, such as C–C coupling,43 α-C–H activation,37 NO decomposition,102 and Knoevenagel condensations.24,103–105

The formation of Brønsted acid sites on the PCN surface under oxidation conditions was first proposed by Shiraishi et al.106 in 2014 by the approach shown in Fig. 2b. Ever since, many recent studies have confirmed the presence of highly labile hydrogen adsorption sites at the PCN edges under photooxidation conditions (O2 atmosphere and light) in the presence of alcohol, which can be the substrate or the solvent in the reaction.107,108 These Brønsted acid sites were recently confirmed experimentally by post-mortem temperature programmed desorption and in situ X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy analysis (XPS), which indicates Hads weakly adsorbed on the catalyst surface.107 This same study also confirmed the presence of Hads on PCN by semi-empirical MOPAC2016 calculations and estimated the relative adsorption energies of H pairs (two H atoms) on a model crystalline PCN structure consisting of two H-bonded strips with 6 melem units, which evidenced that the Brønsted acid sites are more likely to be present at the edges of the PCN and bonded to N atoms in the same ring adjacent to the bridging N atoms between melem units, as shown in Fig. 2b.107 The Brønsted acid sites can be applied to catalyse a series of reactions, especially those which depend on a dehydration, which include the conversion of nitroaromatics into azo- and azoxyaromatics107 and the photoacetalization of ketones and aldehydes.108 Moreover, Lewis acid sites can be introduced into PCN by elemental doping with boron37,109–111 or phosphorous.35,39,40,112 Despite that fact that Lewis acid sites are less active than the Brønsted ones, they presented helpful synergistic effects in a range of photoreactions, such as H2 evolution,40,109 photodegradation,40,111 photooxidations,110 C–H activations37 and CO2 cycloaddition.112

4. PCN based heterojunctions: non-noble metals, metal oxides, metal sulphides, and metal–organic frameworks

Elemental doping is widely used to engineer the band gap of PCN through the addition of other elements instead of C and N in the PCN structure. This makes it possible to modulate the light absorption and redox band potentials for targeted photocatalytic applications according to Fig. 3.34 As previously mentioned, the most commonly used elements are S,35,36,113 B,37,111 O,38 I35,114,115 and P.35,39,40 However, metal cations also can be included into PCN framework, such as Zn2+, Fe2+,116 Ni2+, Cu2+, Co3+,117 Cu1+,118 Mn3+, K+,119 Na+ and Li+.120–123 The recent review by Zhang et al. provides a detailed discussion of several strategies that can be employed to obtain PCN structures and how they affect photocatalytic activities.124 Another interesting review by Jiang and collaborators overviews the recent advances in doping of PCN for photocatalytic applications.125 Another efficient way to improve the PCN electronic properties is by combining it with other semiconductors and nanostructures to form PCN based heterojunctions.

Similarly to other typical inorganic semiconductor photocatalysts (e.g., TiO2), PCN also suffers several intrinsic drawbacks such as low electrical conductivity, high recombination rates of electron–hole pairs, and limited absorption above 460 nm.126 Highly efficient PCN-based nanostructures must attend some important criteria. These include low recombination rates, enhanced solar light absorption, and high photochemical stability. In this context, the development of PCN-based nanohybrids is attractive for several reasons: (i) in enabling more efficient charge transfer and separation through the Schottky junction (metal/semiconductor heterostructures) or the p–n junction (semiconductor/semiconductor heterostructures); (ii) the presence of cocatalysts lowers the redox overpotential at the active sites; (iii) the visible light utilization can be improved; and (iv) the system stability by protecting the active sites and functional groups on the surface of the semiconductor through proper surface passivation is ameliorated.34

It is well established that charge recombination processes are disadvantageous to the photocatalytic efficiency. When the recombination process takes place, excited electrons return to the valence band, dissipating the energy as heat without reacting with the adsorbed species on the surface of PCN. Thus, many strategies have been investigated to suppress recombination in PCN and consequently improve the photocatalytic performances.34 Among them, the construction of PCN-based heterojunction nanohybrids is particularly appealing has received a lot of attention in the field of photocatalysis. This strategy will be discussed in the subsequent sections.

4.1. PCN and noble-metal free materials

Noble-metal free materials (e.g. PCN, graphene, carbon nanotubes, etc.) have various advantages, such as being low cost and environmentally friendly. Hence, they have attracted tremendous attention to their potential in tackling environmental and energy problems. Thus, it becomes attractive to construct hybridized materials via coupling two noble-metal free materials. For example, graphene, a two-dimensional macromolecular sheet of carbon atoms, has received much attention due to its outstanding mechanical, thermal, easily-obtained, and electrical properties.127,128 Thus, it represents an intuitive noble-metal free candidate to construct heterojunction nanohybrids with PCN.129

Sun et al. prepared graphene/PCN heterojunctions which presented enhanced conductivities and electrocatalytic performance towards the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR).130 Xiang et al. obtained graphene/PCN nanohybrids by a combined impregnation-chemical reduction strategy, and successfully tested it towards the photocatalytic H2-production in the presence of Pt as a cocatalyst under visible light.129,131 Li and collaborators synthesised cross-linked PCN/rGO (reduced graphene oxide) nanohybrids with tunable band structure, which demonstrated significantly enhanced visible light photocatalytic activity.132 In another example, Ong et al. have assembled rGO and PCN for successful CO2 hydrogenation into methane.133 Other successful attempts have been made to explore novel materials coupling carbon nanostructures with PCN, such as MWNTs (multi-walled carbon nanotubes)/PCN,134,135 C60/PCN,136,137 carbon nanodots/PCN138–143 and polymers/PCN.144–149

Another carbon structure that has gained huge attention recently is carbon nanodots. They present excellent optical and electronic features, acting as both light absorbers and electron reservoirs.138 Different methods have been used to synthesize carbon nanodots on PCN, such as hydrothermal150 and pyrolysis.138 Due to the unique nature of carbon nanodots as spectral converters, they allow for the up conversion of infrared light to visible light. The emitted light can then be used by the PCN for various photo-driven transformations. Carbon nanodots can also be employed as a cocatalyst in some reactions. For example, H2O2 is a typical intermediate of photocatalytic water splitting and its adsorption on the surface of the PCN results in the poisoning of the active sites. When carbon nanodots are added on PCN surface, the photocatalyst maintains a high activity during the entire process.140

It is important to mention noble-metal free heterojunctions between carbon nitrides displaying different electronic structures. For instance, Wang et al. obtained two types of host–guest CN/CNS heterojunctions with a facile band alignment by the surface-assisted polymerization: CNS–CN (CN serving as the host) and CN–CNS (CNS serving as the host).151 This heterojunction is called isotype heterojunctions. These two types of CN/CNS heterojunctions have demonstrated significant enhancement in the photocatalytic activity and stability for H2 evolution. Inspired by Wang's work, Dong et al. synthesised layered PCN/PCN metal-free isotype heterojunction by treating the molecular composite precursors of urea and thiourea under the same thermal conditions. This CN-T/CN-U heterojunction was constructed on the basis that the PCN prepared from urea and thiourea have different band structures. Upon visible light irradiation, the photogenerated electrons transfer from PCN (thiourea) to PCN (urea). This is driven by the conduction band offset of 0.10 eV. Also, the photogenerated holes transfer from PCN (urea) to PCN (thiourea) driven by the valence band offset of 0.40 eV.41 These two charge transfer processes are beneficial for overcoming the high dissociation barrier of the Frenkel exciton and for the stabilization of electrons and holes. The redistribution of electrons on the one side of the heterojunction (CN-U) and holes on the opposite side (CN-T) reduces the electron–hole pair recombination, extending the lifetime of charge carriers and resulting in noticeably higher photocatalytic activity. These two examples demonstrated that the rational design and construction of isotype heterojunctions could open new strategies to the development of more efficient visible-light photocatalysts.129

4.2. PCN and metal oxides or sulphides

During the past few years, many groups have reported different types of heterojunction photocatalysts comprised of PCN and metal oxides or sulphides showing improved efficiencies relative to their individual components.30,34,129,152,153 Among abundant, non-noble metal oxides, TiO2 (e.g. 3.2 eV), represents one of the most widely used photocatalyst and it has been employed as a promising candidate for constructing PCN based heterojunctions. Several studies on the formation of TiO2/PCN hybrid nanohybrids have been reported to enhance photocatalytic activities.154–165 Zhou et al. reported the synthesis of TiO2 nanotube array/PCN heterojunction photocatalysts by a facile electrochemical approach.54 Additionally, heterojunctions between PCN and TiO2 with different morphologies (0D nanoparticles, 1D nanowires, 2D nanosheets, and 3D mesoporous crystals)166 and exposed facets ({001} and {101}) have been reported.156,167–171 Besides the incorporation of anatase TiO2 with PCN, other TiO2 crystal phases such as brookite and rutile have been coupled with PCN for photocatalytic applications.172–174

In addition to the TiO2/PCN photocatalysts, ZnO/PCN has also been reported (ZnO presents a band gap of around 3.2 eV).175–178 Several examples on the synergism between ZnO and PCN have been observed, such as the increase in photocurrent of ZnO/PCN hybrid nanomaterials by 5 folds under visible light irradiation (λ > 450 nm)55 and the use of N-doped ZnO/PCN core–shell nanoplates for visible-light photodegradation of Rhodamine B (RhB).179

Differently from TiO2 and ZnO, WO3 has a relatively lower band gap of ca. 2.6–2.8 eV, which lies in the visible light region. Therefore, WO3/PCN heterostructures exhibits the Type II heterojunction charge transfer process, which drastically suppress the recombination of charge carriers.180–182 It is noteworthy that other oxides have also been employed to generate PCN based hybrids, such as Cu2O/PCN (which promoted a red-shifted in the absorption edge from 460 to 600 nm),183 Fe2O3/PCN (makes the material magnetically recoverable and improves photoredox activity),184 and the Co3O4/PCN water oxidation catalysts displaying high stabilities.185 Hybrid photocatalysts containing composite oxides have also been receiving attention. For instance, Ge et al. prepared Bi2WO6/PCN heterostructured photocatalysts, which presented a strong light absorption in the visible region.186 The synergistic effect between PCN and Bi2WO6 promoted the charge transfer and separation due to the joined electric fields, whereby the excited electrons from PCN were injected into the conduction band of the Bi2WO6. Simultaneously, the holes on the valence band of Bi2WO6 were transferred to that of PCN, retarding charge recombination.34

In addition to metal oxides, cadmium sulphide (CdS) is a fascinating semiconductor with a relatively low band gap of 2.4 eV, which makes it able to absorb solar light up to 520 nm or even longer wavelengths.187 The well-matched energy levels of PCN and CdS allows for the formation of heterostructures through the Type II band alignment, which enables charge migration transfer upon light irradiation.34,188,189 To date, CdS/PCN materials, that are composed of two visible light responsive semiconductors, have been fabricated via an in situ precipitation–deposition method developed by Fu et al. The resulting CdS/PCN heterostructure exhibited outstanding visible light photocatalytic in 4-aminobenzoic acid removal.190 Other studies have reported higher stability and inhibition of photocorrosion of CdS when it is combined with PCN.191,192 Two-dimensional (2D) MoS2 nanosheets have become a promising cocatalyst to produce hybrid photocatalysts with PCN. MoS2 nanosheets are robust, highly active (especially for the H2 evolution reaction), can be obtained in the layered morphology, and it is relatively inexpensive.193–195 Interestingly, under the same loading of cocatalysts (MoS2 or Pt), it has been shown that MoS2/PCN outperformed the Pt/PCN for H2 production under similar experimental conditions.57

Mechanistic investigations extensively examined the interface region between PCN and MoS2. These studies concluded that valence band maximum and conduction band minimum of PCN were greater by ca. 0.15 and 0.83 eV, respectively, relative to MoS2. This led to the formation of a Type II heterojunction system.196 As the charge redistribution occurs preferentially at the heterointerfaces, a polarized field is created at the interface region, which inhibited the recombination of charge carriers and prolonged the lifetime of photogenerated charge carriers.34,196 Apart from CdS and MoS2, other sulphides have been employed for improved charge separation in hybrids with PCN. These include WS2/PCN,57,197 SnS2/PCN,198 NiS2/PCN,199 CoS/PCN,200 ZnS/PCN,201–203 and Ag2S/PCN.204

4.3. PCN and metal–organic frameworks (MOF)

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are area composed by metal ions coordinated to organic ligands to form one-, two-, or three-dimensional structures. Similar to what has been described for metal oxide and sulfide semiconductors, MOFs can photogenerate holes and electrons upon visible-light irradiation, which can lead to the transfer of holes and electrons to target reactant molecules to initiate light-driven photoredox processes.34,205 In terms of band structures, the MOF's valence band correspond to the outer orbitals of organic linkers, whereas the empty outer orbitals of the metal contribute to the conduction band. In this context, the incorporation of MOFs on PCN is interesting due to the variety of MOF structures and species as well as their tunable cavities, porosities, and high surface areas. Several successful examples of MOF/PCN hybrid systems have been reported, including MIL-125(Ti)/PCN,63 ZIF-8/PCN,206 and UiO-66/PCN.62 These hybrids have shown improved photocatalytic activities, high porosity and surface area, thermal stability, and accelerated mass transfer processes relative to PCN.64,207

5. PCN based heterojunctions with metal NPs

5.1. Principles

Among all classes of nanomaterials, metals deserve a special place. They represent almost two-thirds of all elements in the periodic table, displaying remarkable chemical, electronic, optical and magnetic properties that have enable novel and/or improved applications in areas such as catalysis, electronics, plasmonics, information storage, and biomedicine.5,208–215

Some metals, such as silver (Ag) and gold (Au), present outstanding optical properties in the visible and/or near infrared ranges. The unique optical properties of Ag and Au in the visible and near-infrared range occur as a result of their localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) excitation. These properties give rise to applications in areas such as surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS), plasmonic photocatalysis, and plasmon-enhanced fluorescence.216 The LSPR refers to the oscillation of free charges on a metallic nanoparticle relative to the static nuclei in response to an oscillating electric field from an incoming electromagnetic wave.217 In this case, upon electromagnetic irradiation, the oscillating electric field component of light imposes a force onto the conduction electrons of the nanoparticle, which results in the formation of an induced dipole moment. In order to counterbalance this excitation, charge redistribution acts to provide a restoring force on the free electrons, thus granting these NPs an associated resonant frequency at a specific wavelength. The LSPR frequency is dependent on size, shape, composition, and structure of the nanoparticle as well as and the dielectric constant of the environment.208,217

It has been recently established that the LSPR excitation in plasmonic nanoparticles can be put to work towards the enhancement in the rates of several chemical transformations.5,218 This field, named plasmonic catalysis or plasmonic photocatalysis, has emerged as a new frontier in the field of nanocatalysis and allows for the utilization of visible light as an abundant and green energy input to drive and, at least in principle, control chemical reactions.215,218,219 Importantly, plasmonic photocatalysis allows one to overcome some of the intrinsic limitations of conventional semiconductor photocatalysts, such as short-range light response, low light absorption, and high photo-generated carrier recombination rates.215,218,219

Two main enhancement mechanisms have been proposed for the observed activities in plasmonic photocatalysis: localized heating and charge transfer of LSPR-generated charge carriers at the metal–molecule interface via direct and indirect mechanisms.220 Upon light LSPR excitation (Fig. 4a),217 the LSPR oscillation decays via non-radiative and radiative pathways.209,218,221 While radiative damping involve scattering and is not beneficial for plasmonic photocatalysis, non-radiative damping (Landau damping) leads to the generation of hot electrons and holes (Fig. 4b). In other words, this creates the probability of finding the excited electrons between the fermi level of the nanoparticle (EF) and EF + hν.220 Further decay via electron–electron and electron–phonon coupling lead to a Fermi–Dirac distribution of charge carriers (Fig. 4c), which also decays via thermal dissipation to the surroundings (Fig. 4d).218,220,222–225

Regarding the enhancement mechanism via charge transfer of LSPR excited hot carriers, two potential microscopic pathways, indirect and direct, can take place. In the indirect pathway, charge carriers are excited from occupied states to higher-energy unoccupied states in the metal particle. This leads to an excited electron distribution. Charge carriers of appropriate energy from this distribution can then scatter through the adsorbate states (orbitals) forming transiently charged adsorbates. The activation of these species then lead to increased reaction rates. The role of LSPR in this process is to increase the rates of charge excitation. On the other hand, the direct transfer pathway involves an LSPR-induced electron excitation from occupied to unoccupied orbitals of the metal–molecule or metal–adsorbate complex that is not mediated by the formation of an excited electron distribution within the metal. Instead, there is the excitation of an electron directly into an unoccupied adsorbate orbital of matching energy.226,227

When metal NPs are deposited on a semiconductor surface, a contact potential difference is generated at their interface due to their different work functions. This potential difference is called the Schottky barrier, and the band bending when a contact is formed after reaching equilibrium is dependent on the relative energies of the work functions of the metal and semiconducting component as illustrated in Fig. 5. This phenomenon can induce the directional migration of photogenerated electrons from the semiconductor to the metal, greatly enhancing the charge separation efficiency.30 Therefore, this system behaves as an effective electron trapping site to suppress the electron–hole recombination. In Fig. 5, this phenomenon is shown only for n-type semiconductors. However, for a p-type semiconductor, as the EF(bulk) is closer to the valence band, which is lower than EF(surf) under disequilibrium, the electrons will transfer from the surface to the bulk, causing downward band bending.228

It is well known that plasmonic NPs can act as both light absorbers and electron traps, enabling the lowering recombination rates when combined with semiconducting materials.231 In the context of PCN, it is clear that the LSPR excitation of noble (Au and Ag)4 and even non-noble (e.g. Bi) metals can expand the optical spectral response of PCN to visible light, while the excellent catalytic activity enabled by the metal NPs can provide catalytic sites for target transformations.34,232–236 Moreover, the metal NPs possess a range of tunable properties (e.g. optical and catalytic properties as a function of shape, size, and composition), which can be exploited synergistically with a PCN for improved photocatalysis. In the following sections, we will start by discussing the synthesis and design in the construction of hybrid materials composed of metal nanoparticles and PCN.

5.2. Hybrids between noble metal NPs and PCN

Au and Ag represent the most investigated noble metal NPs displaying LSPR effect in the visible or near-infrared range. Au-loaded on bulk60,237,238 and exfoliated239 PCN have been reported for various photocatalytic applications.240 Different methods to decorate the PCN surface with the metal NPs are commonly used, such as photoreduction,238,239,241 impregnation,240 and deposition–precipitation.60 Di et al. compared Au/PCN synthesized by photoreduction, impregnation, and deposition–precipitation. They reported a higher photocatalytic activity for the composite prepared by the deposition–precipitation, due to the formation of tight Au–semiconductor heterojunctions effectively promoting the transfer of charge from light-excited PCN.60 The stronger the interfacial contact between Au and PCN, the easier the electron migration process will be. Moreover, the LSPR effect in Au helped in visible light absorption in the hybrid, also contributing to the superior photocatalytic activities. Samanta et al. compared pure PCN with 1 wt% Au/PCN. The plasmonic photocatalyst presented a photocurrent density that was 3000 folds higher than pure PCN. The dramatically enhanced photocurrent density of Au/PCN was ascribed to the strong visible light absorption and boosted charge carrier separation due to the LSPR effect from Au NPs.61 Furthermore, it can be observed that the Au Fermi level can shift towards more negative potentials when the electrons were transferred from the sp2-hybridized π-conjugated PCN network to Au. Essentially, Au played a dual-function role as an electron collector and also as a plasmonic cocatalyst.34

Ag, which presents a characteristic LSPR peak centered at ca. 400–450 nm, has also been employed to form heterojunctions with PCN.59,242–247 It is important to note that in both Au and Ag, the LSPR frequency can be tuned by maneuvering their size and shape.5 Yang et al. reported that Ag/PCN nanohybrids prepared by photodeposition were active under visible light due to the LSPR excitation in Ag NPs as well as the fast transportation and separation of charge carriers.248 It was demonstrated that the LSPR effect of Ag promoted an enhancement of local electromagnetic fields, which improved the generation of electron and holes within PCN. In addition to the LSPR effect, the photogenerated electrons on PCN can be scavenged promptly by Ag NPs, forming a Schottky barrier to inhibit the charge recombination process.34 Further details about Ag modified PCN hybrid synthesis and their applications can be found in a recent review written by Patnaik et al.249

In addition to the plasmonic metals, an interesting example that illustrated the importance of the strength of interaction between the noble metal and PCN to ensure the smooth transfer of photoinduced electrons was reported in the Pt/PCN material by Shiraishi and co-workers. They investigated the influence of two different synthesis methods over photocatalytic activities: (i) reduction of Pt precursor by H2 at a high temperature, and (ii) photoreduction. Both approaches yielded nearly similar Pt particle sizes (ca. 3.5–4 nm). Surprisingly, the catalyst prepared by the high-temperature method exhibited 10 times higher photocatalytic activity for H2 evolution when compared with samples prepared by photoreduction method.250 Several other studies also reported increased photocatalytic activity in Pt/PCN heterojunctions, especially for photodegradation251 and CO2 photoreduction.252,253

Pd/PCN heterojunctions have also been widely investigated. A range of publications have addressed synthesis routes to decorate PCN with Pd NPs.254–259 Among them, it is worth to highlight the results reported by Bai and co-workers on the effect of Pd facet selectivity as a cocatalyst in photocatalysis. They produced uniformly dispersed Pd NPs with different preferential exposed facets, {100} and {111}, on 2D PCN. They showed that reduction of CO2 can better occur on Pd-{111} facets while H2O prefers to generate H2 on Pd-{100}. The authors justified the higher activity of the Pd-{111} facets towards CO2 reduction as a result of the higher adsorption energy of CO2 in these surface facets (0.230 eV) when compared to Pd-{001} (0.064 eV), as indicated by first-principles theoretical calculations.258

5.3. Hybrids between non-noble metals NPs and PCN

Apart from the noble metals, Bi, Ni and Cu have also been used to form hybrids composited with PCN. The semimetal Bi exhibits intriguing properties, being able to act as an electron reservoir and possessing plasmonic properties (which are in essence similar to the noble metals).260 Dong et al. deposited Bi nanospheres on thin PCN layers and observed a wide additional absorption peak at ca. 500 nm, thus improving visible light absorption and the generation of charge carriers. They also observed a higher surface photovoltage (SPV) response for Bi/PCN due to the electric field generated from Bi accompanied by the transfer of electrons from PCN to Bi acting as an electron channel.260 These local electromagnetic fields emerge from the LSPR effect in Bi, which was confirmed by using a finite integration technique simulation.260 Another non-noble metal displaying plasmonic properties which was reported in PCN based hybrids is Cu. To the best of our knowledge, only Zhang et al. have investigated Cu NPs to improve photocatalytic reactions in this context. They observed a red-shift on light absorption due to LSPR effect of Cu NPs and an increment in the H2 evolution rate under visible light irradiation.261 Wang et al. have tested Cu/PCN NPs composite as peroxidase-like to help in glucose detection in blood samples, which allow lower detection limits.262 Studies on Cu/PCN heterojunctions have remained scarce mainly due to the instability of the Cu NPs towards oxidation in air. Hence, most of the reports tested Cu oxides or hydroxides with PCN.183,263,264

Ni has also been studied as a cocatalyst with PCN. Bi et al. reported increment on the SPV due to efficient charge separation at the heterojunction interface of Ni/PCN.265 Kong and collaborators developed a robust and visible light active Ni/PCN catalyst for H2 production. Their mechanistic studies concluded that the hybrid composite presented high visible light activity due to Ni NPs effectively preventing the recombination of the photogenerated electrons and holes of PCN.266 In a recent publication, Wen and co-workers reported that the H2-evolution activity for the system Ni/PCN can be further improved by the addition of NiS. They reported a significant synergistic effect between the multifunctional metallic Ni interface layers and amorphous NiS improving the charge separation and transportation. This effect could enhance both H2-production and TEOA-oxidation kinetics, thus resulting in the significantly boosted photocatalytic H2 evolution over the ternary composite photocatalysts.267 It is worth to highlight the recent Al decorated PCN reported by Choi and colleagues. The incorporation of this inexpensive and abundant metal significantly changed optical and photocatalytic properties and led to increments for H2-evolution and organic pollutant degradation under light excitation.268

5.4. Hybrids between bimetallic NPs and PCN

Compared to monometallic NPs, bimetallic NPs have attracted massive interest in catalysis, in which the synergy between the two metals (electronic effects) have led to improved performances.30,269 In addition to catalytic activity, selectivity and stability have also been dramatically affected in the bimetallic systems.270 Han et al. reported PCN nanohybrids modified with PtCo bimetallic alloy NPs. They were formed on PCN by an in situ strategy based on chemical reduction. This material was investigated towards the photocatalytic H2 evolution. They showed that the bimetallic NPs increased the surface defect density and changed the Fermi level in comparison to monometallic Pt NPs. This lead to the better separation of photogenerated charge carriers and consequently to a more active catalyst for generate H2 under visible light irradiation.271 The same group also reported AuPd/PCN bimetallic photocatalysts with enhancement of visible light absorption and more efficient separation of electron–hole pairs as compared to PCN and their monometallic counterparts.272

The effect of the exposed facet (shape effect) is also important in bimetallic systems. PtCu concave nanocubes displaying high-index {730} surface facets loaded on PCN nanosheets exhibited higher photocatalytic activity and selectivity in CO2 reduction for methane (CH4) production as compared to PtCu nanocubes (enclosed by {100} facets) loaded on PCN. This enhancement was assigned to the presence of more low-coordinated, active metal active sites in the concave nanocubes with high index surface facets, favouring adsorption and activation of CO2.273

5.5. Metal NPs in PCN-based Z-scheme photocatalysts

An important class of application of metal NPs is to work as a conductor in Z-scheme photocatalytic systems. Metal NPs (such as Au, Ag, Pt, Cu, and Bi), as well as carbon materials (such as GO, CNTs, rGO and carbon), can be applied as solid-state electron mediators for PCN-based Z-scheme systems. They can promote a fast separation of the photogenerated electron–hole pairs at the interface of the two semiconductors.274–277 The metal NPs can also serve as a charge transmission bridge. When a conductor is added between two semiconductors, it forms an Ohmic contact with low contact resistance as depicted in Fig. 7.278,279 The photoinduced electrons in the conduction band semiconductor 2 can directly recombine with the holes in the valence band of semiconductor 1 through the Ohmic contact. Consequently, the holes and electrons with more efficient redox ability can be mostly reserved for efficient photocatalytic reactions.280,281 Z-scheme systems have attracted considerable attention because it not only boosts the spatial separation efficiency of photoinduced electron–hole pairs but also minimizes undesirable backward reaction of the photocatalytic process due to two different redox sites.282,283 Moreover, maximum overpotentials can be obtained with this unique Z-scheme system, which benefits from the effective utilization of a high conduction band from semiconductor S2 and a low valence band from semiconductor S1 (Fig. 7).284,285 Many recent advances have been done using PCN in Z-scheme heterojunctions. This topic was recently reviewed by Jiang et al., which covered the preparation and catalytic application of PCN-based Z-scheme systems.280

6. Photocatalytic applications of metal NPs and PCN hybrids

Metal NPs can have a wide range of catalytic applications. In fact, they can be coupled with PCN to accomplish one or more of the following tasks: (i) improve charge separation through heterojunctions with PCN; (ii) improve the visible light utilization; and (iii) act as a cocatalysts lowering the redox overpotential at the active sites and helping in the activation of the reactant molecules.34 This section will focus on how each metal introduce and presented in the last section help to address one or more of those tasks when coupled with PCN in different catalytic reactions together with a detailed discussion of their enhancement mechanisms. 6.1. Photocatalytic water splitting

Hydrogen is considered as an ultimate clean fuel because of its high-energy density (140 MJ kg−1), environmental benignancy, and recycling.1,129,286 The generation of H2 from water by employing a photocatalyst and solar light as the energy input has been considered a promising approach.287,288 A range of semiconductor photocatalysts have been reported to catalyse the evolution of H2 from water. However, the practical applications of this strategy are limited by the prompt recombination of photogenerated electron–hole pairs within photocatalysts.

According to the requirement of thermodynamics, the conduction band potential should be more negative than the reduction potential of H2O (0 V vs. NHE) for H2 generation. Meanwhile, the valence band potential should be more positive than the oxidation potential of H2O (1.23 V vs. NHE) for O2 generation. PCN possess appropriate conduction and valence band positions to catalyse the water splitting reaction.30,289 Moreover, its visible-light-driven properties and high specific surface area makes PCN a promising photocatalyst to promote the H2-evolution from water.289,290 The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) has concluded that photocatalysts for water splitting must have solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency equal or higher than 5% to meet the economically viable price of US$ 2–4 kg−1 of H2. Up to now, one of the photocatalysts that are closer to this efficiency is a PCN-based catalyst comprised of carbon nanodots on PCN.140 Despite all the PCN great properties, it produces 0.1–4 μmol h−1 of H2 with a quantum efficiency (QE) of less than 1% under visible light when it is used to catalyse the water-splitting reaction.34,236,291,292 Hence, it is highly important modify PCN to allow higher rates of H2 production and quantum efficiencies. The promotion mechanism in Ag-modified PCN photocatalysts was investigated for the first time by Bu and collaborators through an electrochemical method.243 The decoration of the PCN surface with Ag NPs improved the conductivity and reduced the energy barrier of the interface reactions. As displayed in Fig. 10, both Fermi levels of PCN and Ag are shifted positively when Ag is in contact with the PCN surface because the conduction band potential of PCN is less positive than the Fermi level of Ag. Therefore, the Fermi level of PCN, which is situated below the conduction band, is shifted to the positive direction. The conduction band of PCN is simultaneously moved in the same direction. Consequently, the conduction band generates an upward band bending near to the interface between Ag and PCN for the transfer of photoexcited electrons from PCN to Ag NPs. This phenomenon leads to the formation of an interfacial electric field. When the electron exchange is held in equilibrium, the flat band potential (Efb) is positively shifted. This enhances the separation efficiency and prolongs the lifetime of electron–hole pairs, accredited to the heterojunction electric field formed on the interface of Ag and PCN.

In fact, when the LSPR absorption of the noble metal partly coincides with the optical absorption of the photocatalyst, the LSPR induces plasmon resonance energy transfer (PRET) from the plasmonic metal to the adjacent semiconductor will excite charge carriers at the interface of noble metal/semiconductor.304,305 Thus, the PRET effect facilitates the generation of charge carriers and, for this reason, it suppresses the charge recombination by reducing the migration distance for the surface catalysis.5 It is noted that the LSPR-induced electric field and the PRET effect will become greater when the distance between the semiconductor and the noble metal is optimised.306 For the Ag/TiO2 system, this optimal distance corresponds to around 5 nm as reported by Linic et al.5 If the semiconductor is located too near the noble metals, the nonradiative energy transfer, Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET), will primarily occur thus quenching the photoexcited semiconductor.307 As a result, the distance between a semi-conductor and a noble metal is essential to balance the PRET and FRET effects. Chen and collaborators, using an approach similar to that reported by Linic's group, investigated the distance-dependent plasmonic influence in the system PCN and Ag NPs. They used a nanogap-engineering approach by loading the Ag@SiO2 plasmonic core–shell NPs onto the PCN nanosheets.244 In view of the distance-dependent plasmonic influence, the nanogaps between PCN and Ag NPs were tuned from 8 to 21 nm by modulating the SiO2 shell thickness (Fig. 11a–f) in order to balance both PRET and FRET effects. The electromagnetic field intensity induced by Ag NPs could be viewed from the finite difference time domain (FDTD) simulation, as shown in Fig. 11g–l. By this the nanogap engineering approach, the optimum activity for the H2 production reported by the authors was for the system with a nanogap of 12 nm. This optimum distance balanced the positive PRET and the negative FRET effects on plasmonic photocatalysis (Fig. 11m).244 This interesting finding will be particularly helpful to designing more effective metal NPs/PCN systems for photocatalytic applications.34

6.2. Photocatalytic reduction of CO2

Over the past century, our society has become heavily dependent on fossil fuel sources such as coal, gas, and oil to produce our daily energy needs and chemicals.318 The CO2 produced by these non-renewable carbon sources has been significantly altering the climate on Earth319 and our heavy reliance make them more expensive and less abundant.318 A larger demand on fossil resources for the next few decades is expected since the global economy will continue to grow steadily.320 Recently, a great effort towards reducing the regional and global CO2 emissions has been pursued. However, a breakthrough technology which allows converting CO2 into energy-bearing products is required. Inspired by the natural photosynthesis, the photocatalytic conversion of CO2 into energy-bearing products has been regarded as an alternative option to address the diminishing of fossil resources and mitigate the greenhouse gas effect to attain solar energy conversion.321,322 In this artificial photosynthesis approach, a photocatalyst directly captures CO2 and solar energy and converts them into hydrocarbon fuels.

The photocatalytic CO2 reduction process is a multielectron transfer process. The possible reaction processes and corresponding redox potentials (vs. reversible hydrogen electrode, RHE) are shown below:

 
CO2 + 2H+ + 2e− → HCOOH (Eredox = −0.20 V)(1)
 
CO2 + 2H+ + 2e− → CO + H2O (Eredox = −0.12 V)(2)
 
CO2 + 4H+ + 4e− → HCHO + H2O (Eredox = −0.07 V)(3)
 
CO2 + 6H+ + 6e− → CH3OH + H2O (Eredox = +0.03 V)(4)
 
CO2 + 8H+ + 8e− → CH4 + 2H2O (Eredox = +0.17 V)(5)
In this case, the CO2 reduction reactions are complex with many pathways and products. Moreover, the CO2 molecule is extremely difficult to be activated and further reduced at room temperature due to its thermodynamic stability. These factors make photocatalytic CO2 reduction very challenging.30

Despite the very low efficiency, pure PCN (without any cocatalyst) can catalyse the reduction of CO2 into other chemicals (e.g. CO,323 CH3CHO324 and CH4324), and the nature of the major CO2 reduction product in the presence of water vapor can be modulated by the band structure of bulk PCN and PCN nanosheets, as reported by Niu et al.324 Other studies about the influence of electronic structure of PCN-based systems on the product selectivity in CO2 reduction were also performed and proposed that not only band position is responsible by this effect, but also the electron sink effect as well as the promoted charge transfer between composite components.30,252,325–329

Similar to photocatalytic water splitting, noble metals can be used as efficient cocatalysts for enhancing the photocatalytic CO2 reduction performance of PCN (as summarized in Table 2). Yu et al. reported a great enhancement on the photocatalytic activity of PCN for reduction of CO2 into hydrocarbon when Pt was added as cocatalyst in a range from 0.25 to 2 wt%. More interestingly, they also reported an important influence on the product selectivity by the Pt loading (Fig. 15a).253 Another research group reported the reduction of CO2 to CH4 using PCN loaded with 0.5–10 wt% of Pt. As shown in Fig. 15b, better yields for CH4 were obtained over the Pt/PCN with an optimal 2 wt% content of Pt. Moreover, this catalyst presented reaction rate 5.1 times greater than pure PCN. The noticeably improved photoactivity of Pt/PCN was assigned to the increased light absorption and effective interfacial electron transfer from PCN to Pt due to its lower Fermi level that prohibited the recombination of electron–hole pairs (Fig. 15c).

In order to better understand the role of noble metals in the photocatalytic CO2 reduction process, Gao and collaborators investigated the photocatalytic CO2 reduction over PCN modified with single-atom of Pd or Pt by using DFT calculation. Comparing the rate-determining barriers of different possible reaction pathways, they found that the HCOOH was the favourable product of CO2 reduction on the Pd/PCN catalyst, while CH4 was favored over Pt/PCN (Fig. 15d and e). This work provided a theoretical support for the empirically observed product selectivity of photocatalytic CO2 reduction.330

Tu and collaborators have studied the impact of tuneable nitrogen vacancies on photocatalytic activities of Pt/PCN towards the CO2 reduction into CO.296 They observed a synergistic effect between nitrogen vacancies and Pt NPs, in which the density of nitrogen vacancies in an optimum range has a positive effect on the photocatalytic activities. This was due to the gradual enhancement of light harvesting, efficient charge separation and transportation, and the uniform binding of Pt on PCN as active reaction sites (Fig. 16). However, further increase in nitrogen vacancies decreased the photocatalytic activity, because the deeper midgap states induced by overdoped nitrogen vacancies could become recombination sites of photogenerated electron–hole pairs.296 Pt NPs cocatalyst on PCN were also used in combination with other heterostructures and tested to reduce CO2 into CH4, such as NaNbO3 nanowires331 and KNbO3.332 Enhancements in the photocatalytic activity were observed in these systems.

The nature of the exposed facet in noble metals (shape effect) can also significantly influence the product selectivity in CO2 reduction, as demonstrated by Bai et al.258 The authors investigated the facet-dependent selectivity of CO2 reduction over Pd cocatalysts in the Pd/PCN system employing Pd NPs with {100} and {111} surface facets (cubes and tetrahedrons, respectively). The photocatalytic CO2 reduction activity was significantly higher for PCN loaded with Pd tetrahedrons. Analysing the facet-dependent CO2 reduction process by first-principles calculations, it was found that the adsorption energy of CO2 on the Pd-{100} (Ea = 0.064 eV) was lower than that for Pd-{111} (Ea = 0.230 eV). Moreover, the activation barrier EB was reduced from 7.15 to 3.98 eV and from 6.79 to 4.15 eV for Pd-{111} and Pd-{100} facets, respectively, when two electrons were brought to the surfaces to initiate the activation of CO2 (Fig. 17). This observation helped to explain why the Pd-{111} facets were more reactive for the reduction of CO2. The Pd/PCN system works essentially similar to the Pt loaded system. When it is irradiated by light, the photoexcitation of electrons took place in PCN. Due to larger work functions of Pd-{100} (5.05 eV) and Pd-{111} (5.23 eV) as compared with the PCN (4.31 eV) (Fig. 17e and f), the photoexcited electrons were rapidly shuttled to the Pd cocatalysts to retard the recombination of charge carriers, resulting in increased CO2 reduction to CH4, CO and C2H5OH.34,258 Similar results were reported by Cao et al. 6.3.1 Liquid phase degradation of pollutants. The removal of pollutants in wastewater effluents, especially organic pollutants (e.g. dyes, antibiotics, bisphenol A, phenols), has been addressed by advanced oxidation processes commonly catalysed by a variety of photocatalysts, which include PCN-based materials. In these studies, the dyes methyl orange, methylene blue, and Rhodamine B (RhB) have been employed as model molecules (so that the reaction can be monitored by a spectrophotometer). In the context of water purification, H2O2 is one of the most widely used green oxidants, which typically requires a catalyst for activation into more reactive oxidizing intermediates.34,344 Cui et al. have demonstrated the metal-free activation of H2O2 by PCN under visible light irradiation, using the decomposition of RhB in aqueous solution as a proof-of-concept reaction. The authors reported that PCN was capable of activating H2O2 to generate reactive oxy-radicals, which in turn led to the mineralization of the dye.344 Interestingly, other studies have used O2 as oxidant. For example, Yang et al. exploited the LSPR effect of Ag NPs in Ag-deposited PCN plasmonic photocatalysts towards the methyl orange degradation. In general, when PCN is excited by visible-light, the generation of ˙O2− and h+ takes place. Here, its band structure does not promote the formation of ˙OH species. It was found that for Ag/PCN systems, ˙OH radicals also took part in the photocatalytic reactions, thus giving the direct evidence to contribute to its increased catalytic performance.248 The same research group later reported the fabrication of Z-scheme plasmonic photocatalyst Ag@AgBr/PCN to further enhance the photocatalytic activity. The high activity achieved in the degradation of methyl orange was attributed to the Z-scheme system, which retained the photoinduced electrons with strong reduction power in the conduction band of PCN and h+ with strong oxidation power in the valence band of AgBr.349

Recently, Ag NPs have been used to improve photocatalytic activities in PCN-based Z-scheme systems.274,316,350,379 For example, Xiao et al. reported enhanced photocatalytic performances for degradation of RhB by a C3N4@Ag–Bi2WO6 material.350 Ag NPs also have been used in a Z-scheme system for RhB316,351,352 and methyl orange (MO)301,353 photodegradation. Several studies have reported the spontaneous photodeposition of Ag NPs during the photocatalytic reaction for Ag compounds/PCN materials due to the Ag+ photoreduction to generate Ag NPs under light irradiation.353,354,380 PCN have been reported to construct Z-scheme systems via the formation of Ag NPs during the photocatalytic reaction with Ag3PO4,338,354–357,380 Ag2CrO4353 and Ag2CO3.359 Generally, the Ag-compounds/PCN system behaves according to a traditional heterojunction. However, the excited electrons have enough reduction ability to convert Ag+ into Ag0. Consequently, the two-phase system is gradually transformed into a three-phase Z-scheme system under light illumination. Notably, Ag NPs can strongly absorb the visible light by LSPR effect.381–383 It has been reported that Ag NPs can simultaneously exhibit the LSPR effect and serve as electron mediators in some Ag-compound/Ag/PCN systems improving its photocatalyst activity.274,316,349,384

Ag doped PCN was also successfully employed to photodegradation of diclofenac (DCF). DCF is an organochlorine anti-inflammatory commonly found in wastewater. It can have significant impact to organisms even in extremely low concentrations (>1 μg L−1), which makes its removal from wastewater critical.385 Li et al. showed that the degradation rate for Ag/PCN was 3.1 folds higher relative to pure PCN, with an excellent stability over the re-use cycles. The authors attributed the enhanced photoactivity to the close contact between Ag and PCN as well as the localized surface plasmon resonance (SPR) effect of Ag NPs.347 Even more impressive results were obtained by Zhang et al. doping PCN with Ag3PO4. In this case, it was reported a 34.8 times higher photodegradation rate for the doped sample when compared with pure PCN.358 The authors did not investigate mechanistically the system in detail. However, the presence of Ag NPs was confirmed, which may have led to the formation of a Z-scheme heterojunction, consequently improving the charge separation efficiency of the photogeneration electron–hole pairs.358

PCN-based Z-scheme systems were also applied for NO oxidation379 and Cr(VI) photoreduction.360 Su et al. decorated SrTa2O6/PCN with Ag NPs as catalysts. An enhancement of the visible light absorption efficiency and a promotion of the photocatalytic activity by a factor of 2.8 for Cr(VI) photoreduction and 4.6 for methyl orange degradation were observed as compared with only SrTa2O6/PCN (Fig. 18a–d). Ag decoration on SrTa2O6/PCN not only extended the visible light absorption region due to LSPR effects but also act as an electron mediator for efficient migration of photogenerated electrons and simultaneously prevent the recombination of photogenerated electron–hole pairs (Fig. 18e).360

Ag NPs also display antibacterial activity. Bing and co-workers successfully improved visible-light-driven antibacterial activity and dispersal of biofilms by embedding Ag NPs on PCN.246 The Ag/PCN hybrids were much more effective to reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation under visible light. The composite was efficient as a bactericidal agent for both Gram-negative (G−) E. coli and Gram-positive (G+) Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) under visible-light illumination.246 Muñoz-Batista et al. tested Ag/PCN biocidal capability against E. coli and it system displayed significantly higher antibacterial capabilities compared to the single components of Ag NPs and PCN.245 Upon UV light illumination, the Ag/PCN system was demonstrated be more effective on charge separation, which left hole-related radicals at the PCN support and electrons on the Ag NPs. Meanwhile, under visible excitation, the LSPR effect of Ag played an essential role in the biocidal action. In this regard, a complex phenomenon involving adsorption, radical attack as well as deactivation process of the interaction between microorganism and photocatalyst took place simultaneously.245 It is noteworthy that the bactericidal action was greatly influenced by both electron and hole-related charge species, which could be confirmed by the EPR measurements of the charge radicals produced from the photoactivity.34

In addition to Ag, Au also has been widely used to improve the PCN photodegradation performance. Cheng et al. reported superior photocatalytic activities for the decomposition of methyl orange under visible-light after decorating PCN nanosheets prepared by ultrasonication-assisted liquid exfoliation of bulk PCN with Au NPs deposited via green photoreduction of HAuCl4.239 Chang et al. reported an Au/PCN nanorods with high activity for photodegradation of a range of pollutants, such as RhB, 4-chlorophenol (4-CP), tetracycline (TC) and reduce (Cr(VI)).386 Au NPs also have been used to decorate PCN combined with other heterostructures (e.g. TiO2,364 Fe2O3365 and CdS)369 for the photodegradation of pollutants. It is noteworthy the results obtained by Zada and co-workers using the ternary component (Au/TiO2/PCN), which presented an improvement of 5.11-folds for photodecomposition of 2,4-dichlorophenol (2,4-DCP) when compared with pure PCN. They reported an exceptional visible-light for the fabricated Au–(TiO2/PCN) nanohybrids, which was attributed to the extended visible-light response due to the Au NPs LSPR and its catalytic function, and to the enhanced charge separation by transferring electrons from PCN and Au to TiO2. This promoted electron transfer for the TiO2 favored the formation of hydroxyl radicals (˙OH), which are very effective in the photocatalytic oxidation process of 2,4-DCP.364 Furthermore, Au NPs also have been widely used in combination with carbon nanostructures, such as CNT366 and GO.368 This carbon nanostructures, in general, work as an electron transporter and acceptor facilitating the charge transfer and avoiding the recombination of photoexcited electron–hole pairs.368 Recently, Leng et al. reported a dramatically enhance of 15 times on the photocatalytic degradation rate constant towards methyl orange (MO) for Au@3D-PCN compared with PCN. Their nanonetwork-structured PCN with well dispersed Au NPs yolks seems to improve the photocatalytic activity due to the positive synergistic effect between the SPR effect of the Au NPs and the high photoelectron-separated efficiency of the 3D nanonetwork structure.363

Despite its more restrict use in photodegradation applications, possibly due to its prohibitive price, the activity of Pt NPs has also been evaluated to degradation of pollutants. In order to minimize the influence of the Pt price in the application feasibility of Pt/PCN for photodegradation, Xu and collaborators wisely coupled the photodegradation of the macrolide antibiotics roxithromycin (Rox) with H2 production reaction. The authors found that macrolide antibiotics present in water act as a sacrificial agent to consume H2O2 (side product from H2 production), itself being degraded, leading to an enhancement of the H2 yield (Fig. 19).387 Pt NPs were also tested on PCN nanotubes towards the photodegradation of p-chlorophenol, p-nitrophenol, methylene blue and RhB, under visible-light irradiation. The authors reported a superior photocatalytic activity as compared to only PCN nanotubes and attributed it to the synergic effect of Pt NPs on the unique tubular nanostructure of PCN nanotubes.251

Bisphenol A (BPA) is another common pollutant. It is widely used in the manufacturing of consumer goods. Owing to its wide application, a large amount of bisphenol A has been released into the environment, leading to ubiquitous presence of bisphenol A in natural water.388 Thus, an extensive research effort has been devoted to the remediation of this problem. Chang et al. investigated the degradation of BPA in water by Pd decorated mesoporous PCN (Pd/mp-PCN).259 The catalyst modified with Pd NPs presented a higher photoactivity compared with only mp-PCN. This higher activity was attributed to the enhanced light absorbance in the UV-VIS region, along with the ability of the Pd NPs to act as electron traps and facilitate the separation of charge carriers.259 Pd NPs were also successfully employed to improve the photodegradation of polychlorinated dibenzo-p-dioxins (PCDDs). PCDDs are one of the most important environmental pollutants due to its toxicity, bioaccumulation, and persistence.389 Ding et al. reported a photodegradation rate constant 3.7 times higher than that of pure PCN for 2-chlorodibenzo-p-dioxin (2-CDD) for their optimal photocatalyst (5%Pd/PCN).362 The authors claimed that the enhanced activity originated mainly from the formation of Schottky barrier between Pd NPs and PCN, which extend the spectrum responsive range and suppressed the charge recombination of PCN.362

There are some successful examples where PCN was decorated with bimetallic NPs in order to improve its photocatalytic activity towards photodegradation reactions. Xue et al. fabricated Au/Pt co-decorated PCN heterostructure by a facile calcination–photodeposition technique, which displayed enhanced photocatalytic activity for antibiotic tetracycline hydrochloride (TC-HCl) degradation. The degradation rates were up to 3.4 times higher than that of pure PCN under visible light irradiation. The authors attributed this enhancement in the photocatalytic activity to the LSPR effect of Au and electron-sink function of Pt NPs, which improved the optical absorption property and photogenerated charge carriers separation of PCN, synergistically facilitating the photocatalysis process.370 Gondal et al. used a different approach. Instead of using co-decorated bimetallic NPs, they used AuAg NPs alloys in order to take better advantage of the LSPR properties of these NPs. As a result, AuAg/PCN photocatalysts exhibited significantly higher activity for the decolorization of RhB in the visible light as compared to the monometallic Ag and Au NPs counterparts on PCN.371 Bimetallic AuPd NPs alloys on PCN was recently applied for photodegradation of carcinogenic phenolic compounds and displayed superior photocatalytic activity compared with the previously reported AuPd/rGO. Photoluminescence results demonstrated that the efficient charge separation and delayed recombination of photo-induced electron–hole pairs in the AuPd/PCN nanocomposite were responsible for its superior activity.372

Besides the noble metals, non-noble metals such as Bi and Cu can be very useful to improve photocatalytic properties of PCN. The most common strategy consists in making use their plasmonic properties to improve visible light absorption and reduce the recombination of electron–hole pairs. This strategy has been reported by Sierra et al. for the Bi spheres/Bi mixed oxides/PCN nanohybrids390 and Gao et al. for the system Cu–PCN synthesised by a supramolecular approach.373 Other interesting example using the low-cost metal Bi to improve progradation was reported by Wang et al.375 The authors deposited plasmonic Bi NPs on PCN@Bi2WO6 microspheres via a hydrothermal method. Bi NPs inserted in the interlayer between PCN and the surface of Bi2WO6 microspheres worked as an electron-conduction bridge enhancing the visible light absorption due to the LSPR effect, consequently facilitating the efficient electron-carrier separation. As a result, they observed an extraordinary enhanced photocatalytic activity for decomposition of 2,4-DCP relative to the PCN, Bi2WO6, and PCN(20%)@Bi2WO6 samples. These observations imply that the heterostructured combination of PCN, metallic Bi NPs, and Bi2WO6 microspheres provided synergistic photocatalytic activity via an efficient electron transfer process. The low-cost metals Bi and Cu were also used to fabricate Z-scheme systems, such as Bi@BiOCl/PCN and Cu2O/Cu/PCN,374 which were applied for photodegradation of pollutants.276

6.3.2 Gas phase degradation of pollutants. The use of metal NPs/PCN heterostructures in the topic of gas phase photodegradation is essentially limited to the oxidation of nitric oxide (NO). Nitrogen dioxide and nitric oxide, also known as NOx, are common air pollutants generated from the combustion of fossil fuels and vehicle exhaust. They are responsible for atmospheric pollutions (e.g. acid rain and urban smog). In view of these facts, there is a growing environmental concern and thereby an intense search for efficient catalysts capable to oxidize NO.34 Sano et al. were the first to report the application of PCN photocatalysts for the oxidation of NO. However, the authors observed low oxidation rates under visible light irradiation.391 Despite the efforts of Wang and co-workers a few years later trying to improve the surface area of the PCN hoping to obtain higher oxidation rates, no satisfactory improvements on the NO oxidation was observed. Thus, the combination of PCN with a cocatalyst seems to be an interesting strategy to improve the photocatalytic activity for NO oxidation reaction.392 Sun et al. resorted to this strategy by hybridizing PCN with Ag NPs. Their results showed that the deposition of Ag NPs not only enhanced the visible light absorption of the hybrid but also benefited the oxidation of NO to the final products (NO2 and NO3−). The authors attributed the visible enhancement in light absorption due to the LSPR of Ag NPs and the increase in the NO oxidation due to improved separation and transfer of photoinduced carriers over Ag/PCN composites, which was supported by the PL spectra.377 Ma and co-workers reported even more significant increment on the activity for NO oxidation in PCN/WS2 decorated with Ag NPs. Their optimized system was 3.70, 1.90 and 1.17 times more active for NO oxidation than that of PCN, WS2, and PCN/WS2, respectively.378

Another promising cocatalyst for NO oxidation due to its low-cost is Bi. Jiang et al. reported the deposition of Bi NPs on PCN. This system was employed as a photocatalyst in removing ppb-level NO in a continuous gas flow under visible light illumination. The Bi NPs significantly enhanced the performance of PCN, and this enhancement arises from the heterojunction and LSPR effects of Bi NPs. However, Jiang et al. also investigated the contribution of each, since they are highly dependent on the size of Bi NPs. By tuning the size of Bi NPs, the authors concluded that Bi NPs with 12 nm represents the optimum size to favor the synergy of these two effects (Fig. 20c and d).376 In another paper, Dong et al. constructed an Bi NPs/PCN displaying highly enhanced visible-light photoactivity and stability for NO purification as compared to that of pristine PCN.260 The enhanced photocatalytic performance was attributed to the co-contributions of the notably improved light-harvesting, owing to the LSPR effects of Bi nanospheres, and the increased separation efficiency of electron–hole pairs due to the electron trapping effect of Bi metal in the heterostructured system. The local electromagnetic field arising from the LSPR effects of Bi was simulated with a rigorous Maxwell's solver based on the finite integration techniques (Fig. 20a), where a significant enhancement of electromagnetic field under visible light could be observed. This electromagnetic field accelerates the photoexcited electrons and holes separation. Furthermore, the nearby Bi NPs can act as electron traps to facilitate the separation of photoinduced electron–hole pairs (Fig. 20b).260

6.4. Other photocatalytic applications

In addition to the transformations presented in the previous sections, metal NPs/PCN have also been employed for other transformations.393 For example, Verma and collaborators studied the photocatalytic C–H activation and oxidative esterification photocatalysed by Pd/PCN. The authors reported excellent yields (>90%) for the conversion of a broad scope of aromatic alcohols into esters.394 Although only preliminary investigations about the reaction mechanism been performed, it is possible to affirm that the reaction proceeds via a C–H activation, which depends on O2 to occur. The activated carbon is then attacked by the alcohol used as reaction solvent, in this case methanol, leading to the ester as final product.394

It is important to note that the applications of carbon nitrides are not limited to only photoreactions. Despite not being the focus of this review, carbon nitrides and metal NPs/PCN hybrids have been put to work in electrocatalysis,395 hydrogenations,396 oxidations,397,398 alkene epoxidation,399 formic acid decomposition400 and very recently to promote cross-coupling reactions.401 Pérez-Ramírez and collaborators reported Pd single-atoms anchored on exfoliated PCN (Fig. 21a and b) efficiently catalysing the Susuki coupling of broad scope of substrates. The authors showed that heterogeneous Pd single-atom catalyst based on exfoliated PCN surpasses the performance of state-of-the-art homogeneous catalysts and conventional heterogeneous catalysts based on nanoparticles or grafted molecular complexes, without evidencing metal leaching or aggregation after the reaction. They attributed the enhanced properties to the adaptive coordination environment within the macroheterocycles of exfoliated PCN (Fig. 21a), which facilitates each catalytic step.401

Moreover, a number of emerging intrinsic properties of PCN allow its novel applications outside of conventional catalysis. Recently, Antonietti et al. compiled these unconventional applications in an excellent review, covering applications of PCN in a range of areas, such as sensing, bioimaging, photovoltaic cells, templating, and the construction of carbon nitride based devices.402

7. Conclusions and perspectives

We presented herein a comprehensive overview of metal NPs/PCN-based heterostructures, focusing on their design principles, photocatalytic applications, and charge-transfer mechanisms. The versatility of the PCN with respect to control over its electronic band structure, composition, crystallinity, vacancies sites, porosity, stability, and active sites at its surface make this material an exceptional candidate for the formation of hybrids with metal NPs. The metal NPs can possess a range of tunable properties (e.g. optical and catalytic properties as a function of shape, size, and composition) that, when combined with PCN, can be harnessed for improved photocatalysis. The recent and intense investigation in the synthesis and application of NPs/PCN-based hybrids confirm that these materials represent an important class of photocatalysts for transitioning towards a sustainable future.

Despite the great progress in the area, we believe that we are still at the early stages in terms of material design and photocatalytic efficiency, which remains low for widespread applications. In order to overcome these challenges, we believe that several issues need to be addressed as described in the following paragraphs.

Firstly, we must be aware and correct the description of the carbon nitride-based materials. Unfortunately, several papers refer to carbon nitride-based materials generically as “g-C3N4”, which implied a precise and ideal stoichiometry. This limits the understanding and abstraction of the real structure responsible for the photocatalysis. Recently, McMillan and collaborators proposed a new a hierarchical scheme for naming these materials based on their hydrogen content and morphology.77 We particularly recommend the adoption of these naming practices for future publications and studies in the field.

A fundamental understanding of the mechanism behind the charge transport in metal NPs/PCN-based photocatalysts is critical. It is essential to unravel the charge cascading process in detail to allow an optimum utilization of light through photogenerated charge carriers for driving charge-transfer reactions. It is required to understand the charge generation, separation, and transportation across the nanoscale. This is especially important for Z-scheme systems, which remain poorly understood. For instance, in situ characterization can be very valuable for probing the electron–hole transfer pathways, being quite promising for enabling the design of more efficient metal NPs/PCN-based photocatalysts having higher quantum efficiencies and thus economic viability. It is also noteworthy that most of the work functions used in semiconducting materials and heterojunctions correspond to the bulk material values. Nevertheless, these values are size dependent, and their precise understanding should involve photophysical measurements of band structures/interfaces.

The interface of metal NPs/PCN-based photocatalyst is a complex system, but very important for photocatalytic activity. Consequently, it is imperative to investigate interface effects in detail, especially regarding metal–support interactions, shape effects, nanoscale assembly, composition, density of defects, vacancies, and bandgap adjustment. Some powerful characterization techniques, such as atom probe tomography and ion scattering, can be more employed to improve our understanding of the interface of metal NPs/PCN systems.

Another challenge consists on the development and utilization of theoretical models and approaches to understand metal NPs/PCN-based systems, such as electronic structure modelling of surface–adsorbate interactions and its integration it with the effect of light excitation. Moreover, many of the theoretical models use for PCN structures actually does not match with the experimental ones. Commonly, these calculations are performed using graphitic carbon nitride as model to represent what should be melon.

Metal NPs/PCN systems exhibit high photocatalytic activities. However, their practical application is limited by the high price and low abundance of noble metals. This limitation can be circumvented by replacing the expensive metal for low-cost alternatives. This review presented some examples of low-cost metals (e.g. Cu, Bi, and Fe) being used as alternative photocatalysts to noble metals. Another strategy is to enhance the atom utilization efficiency of noble metals, for example by synthesizing highly active systems containing noble metals (which enable one to reduce its loading in the reaction) and single-atom noble metal-deposited PCN materials. So far, facile and efficient methods for preparing single-atom noble metal modified PCN systems are challenging and deserve further exploration. Furthermore, the characterization of these systems is specially challenging and basically limited to AC-STEM and EXAFS. It would be very helpful with further development of spectroscopy techniques to probe and support the coordination environment of single-atoms on PCN. The stability of these systems it is also extremely important to make them economically viable. Hence, it is critical to address chemical corrosion and/or degradation of the photocatalysts.

Finally, synergistic efforts in the areas of controlled synthesis, precise characterization (both ex and in situ), mechanistic investigation, and modelling will be needed in order to move beyond the state-of-the-art in the field of photocatalysis and enable the widespread utilization of metal NPs/PCN materials towards sustainable molecular transformations.

It is a consensus that a sustainable economy must have its process driven by sunlight. Hence, photocatalytic systems will have a protagonist role in our journey to a green future. In this context, photocatalytic systems based on earth-abundant elements, such as PCN, are particularly attractive. Furthermore, the synthesis of metal NPs and PCN have been recently successfully scaled-up to the kilogram scale,107,403,404 which make these hybrids photocatalysts, even more, promising for future practical applications.

We believe that the precise understanding of the charge transport mechanism combined with the high level of control over the materials syntheses, their combination, and characterization can take the topic of photocatalysis with PCN to a new level, potentially enabling us to move from an observation-driven to a design-driven approach (Fig. 22). In this case, specific properties and the performance of the photocatalytic systems can be planned in advance, and the target material synthesized accordingly. We are confident that the continuous efforts of the scientific community in synthesis, characterization, and mechanistic understanding, modelling, and assessment in photocatalysis is paving the way for cleaner energy generation as well as more efficient and sustainable processes.
