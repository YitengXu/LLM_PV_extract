Abstract

Aqueous electrochemical energy storage devices have advantages in terms of high safety, low cost, and environmental benignity, yet a major drawback is the low energy density compared to those using organic electrolytes. Here, we report an aqueous Zn-ion hybrid energy storage device (ZIHESD) using poly(4,4â€²-thiodiphenol, TDP)-modified nanoporous activated carbon (AC) as the cathode material. The introduction of this redox-active polymer can largely enhance the energy storage capacity. Compared to the Zn//AC cell, the use of the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cathode leads to not only a wide voltage window, as broadened from 0.2â€“1.8 V to 0.1â€“1.9 V, but also an approximately three-fold increased areal capacity. The capacity retention is 71% after 2000 cycles. Also, a group of ZIHESDs with different AC mass loadings in the cathode are tested. A maximum areal capacity of 1.16 mA h cmâˆ’2 is achieved. Furthermore, a flexible quasi-solid-state cell encapsulated in a flat pouch shows stable electrochemical performance when repeatedly bent at large angles. The cell shows sustained powering capability after being partially cut by using scissors. The energy storage mechanism is discussed in detail.

Introduction

There has been an urgent demand for the development of alternative clean energy sources, such as wind, solar, and ocean energy, to tackle the energy crisis across the world.1,2 Nevertheless, these renewable energy sources are intermittent by nature, which requires energy storage systems as an intermediate station between the energy harvester and consumer. Electrochemical energy storage devices play a vital role in our daily life, and their sizes vary in a large range. From the bulky ones for grid energy storage to the miniature ones for electronic gadgets, the energy storage performances in terms of energy density, rate performance, and cycling life are determined by the type of electrode material and the related manufacturing technology. According to the energy storage mechanism, rechargeable energy storage devices can be classified as batteries and supercapacitors.3,4 In general, a secondary battery has a high energy density but suffers from a low power density. Also, it takes hours for a full charge. A supercapacitor can be charged quickly, able to provide a high power output, and has a super long cycling life, but has inferior energy density because the charge storage relies on physical adsorption of ions onto the electrode surface. With the development of new electrochemical energy storage systems, devices with a hybrid energy storage mechanism have emerged.

Significant research efforts have been devoted to developing high performance supercapacitors by combining a battery-like electrode and a supercapacitor-like electrode, and the device named a hybrid supercapacitor (HSC) has merits of both ionic batteries and conventional supercapacitors. For example, lithium-ion,5 sodium-ion,6 and potassium-ion HSCs have been reported.7 These devices generally use organic electrolytes that lead to wide potential windows. Besides, aqueous asymmetric supercapacitors based on two dissimilar electrode materials were also reported.8â€“11 The asymmetric design is to widen the potential window beyond the water splitting point at âˆ¼1.23 V.12 Though the use of aqueous electrolytes brings about advantages like low cost, easy fabrication, high safety, and high ion mobility, the major drawback is the low energy density compared to the counterparts of Li-ion batteries using organic electrolytes. Therefore, it is imperative to devise a new aqueous energy storage system that has both wide potential window and high capacity.

Very recently, aqueous Zn-ion hybrid supercapacitors using a Zn anode and a porous activated carbon (AC) based cathode were reported.13â€“16 These devices rely on the physical adsorption of Zn ions onto the surfaces of AC to store charge, and the operating voltage was in the range of 0.2â€“1.8 V. Though the divalent Zn2+ ions carry more charges than monovalent ions such as H+, Na+, and Li+ which are often used in other types of aqueous supercapacitors, the physical adsorption of ions is inadequate to offer a high specific capacitance, limiting the specific energy. Herein, we report a novel aqueous Zn-ion hybrid energy storage device (ZIHESD) based on a Zn anode and a polymer-based cathode. In our work, a pseudocapacitive polymer, poly(4,4â€²-TDP), was electrodeposited onto porous AC to prepare the cathode, which was combined with a Zn anode to construct aqueous ZIHESDs. Electrode materials for Zn ion based rechargeable cells can be classified as inorganics and organics.17â€“20 The inorganic ones represented by metal oxides or carbides have high resistance, hence a low mass loading of such an electrode material is preferred to reduce the internal resistance. Aqueous Zn//MnO2 and Zn//V2O5 cells are charged/discharged within voltage ranges of 1.0â€“1.8 V and 0.2â€“1.6 V, respectively.21,22 In our work, not only high mass loadings can be achieved from the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cathode, which show high areal capacities of up to 1.16 mA h cmâˆ’2, but also a broad voltage range, 0.1â€“1.9 V, is achieved by using the poly(4,4â€²-TDP) cathode.

From a practical point of view, a high mass loading of the electrode material is always required to render high capacity. We prepared a group of Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cells with different AC mass loadings in the cathode, ranging from 3.2 to 6 mg cmâˆ’2. Note that the mass gain of electrodeposited poly(4,4â€²-TDP) is also a function of the AC mass loading. For the cathode with an AC mass loading of 6 mg cmâˆ’2, the net weight of poly(4,4â€²-TDP) is âˆ¼2.5 mg cmâˆ’2, and thus the overall mass loading of the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC composite is âˆ¼8.5 mg cmâˆ’2. The rate performances of these devices are shown in Fig. 2e. A trend is that the higher the mass loading of the cathode material, the higher the capacity. However, the capacity would reach a saturation level when the AC mass loading is beyond 5.0 mg cmâˆ’2. It can be understood that the permeation depth of electrodeposited poly(4,4â€²-TDP) in an AC coating is limited, and thus the net weight of poly(4,4â€²-TDP) and the capacity would reach a saturation level if the thickness of the AC coating is beyond a certain value. For the device with an AC mass loading of 6 mg cmâˆ’2 in the cathode, a maximum areal capacity of 1.2 mA h cmâˆ’2 at 1 mA cmâˆ’2 is achieved. Remarkably, even at 10 mA cmâˆ’2, a capacity of 0.85 mA h cmâˆ’2 is retained, about 74% of the maximum capacity. The capacity recovered to 1.1 mA h cmâˆ’2 when the current density dropped from 10 to 1 mA cmâˆ’2, showing good rate performance. Fig. 2f shows Ragone plots of this group of devices. The cell with the thickest AC coating in the cathode shows the highest areal energy density of 1.03 mW h cmâˆ’2, corresponding to a power density of 0.9 mW cmâˆ’2. For comparison, energy and power densities of some other HSCs that were reported previously are provided as well in Fig. 2g. Examples include Ag@Ni0.67Co0.33S//C (0.36 mW h cmâˆ’2),23 Ni3S2@CdS//C (0.26 mW h cmâˆ’2),24 Co0.67Ni0.33 LDH/NiCo2O4//AC (0.32 mW h cmâˆ’2),25 Mn1âˆ’xNixCo2O4//AC (0.14 mW h cmâˆ’2),26 Niâ€“Coâ€“S nanoflakes//polypyrrole (0.11 mW h cmâˆ’2),27 Zn-PTO (0.83 mW h cmâˆ’2),28 Ni0.8Cu0.2S//N-doped graphene (0.14 mW h cmâˆ’2),29 and Zn-PANI (0.2 mW h cmâˆ’2).30 Apparently, our ZIHESDs surpass most other hybrid supercapacitors in terms of both energy and power densities.

To realize different applications, several cells can be connected either in series to boost the voltage or in parallel to multiply the capacity. The GCD curves in Fig. 3a were collected from a single cell and two cells in parallel, respectively. When at an identical current, the discharge time of two cells in parallel is nearly double that of a single cell, indicating doubled capacity because of the parallel connection. In Fig. 3b, the two cells in series can be charged within a wide voltage window from 0.1 V to 3.8 V, which is twice as wide as that of a single cell. Note that carbon cloth used as the current collector is highly conductive and flexible. Some researchers used carbon cloth as the base to fabricate Liâ€“I or Liâ€“S batteries, without demonstrating the device flexibility.31,32 We fabricated quasi-solid-state Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cells with using a gelled electrolyte and studied the effect of device bending on the electrochemical performance. The electrolyte was prepared by mixing gelatin with 2 M ZnSO4 solution. Fig. 3c shows the cycling performance of a typical flexible device at different bending angles. During the cycling process, the flat device was bent at 60Â°, 120Â°, and 180Â°, and then released to the initial status. It can be seen that both capacity and coulombic efficiency of the device remained stable, regardless of bending. Impressively, after repeatedly bending and releasing the device for 50 cycles, the capacity retention was âˆ¼98%. Therefore, our flexible ZIHESD has the potential for wearable electronics, which requires high-standard safety of the energy storage component. In order to demonstrate the high safety, we used ceramic scissors to cut the pouch cell while it was working. To elucidate the effect of poly(4,4â€²-TDP) on broadening the voltage window of the Zn-ion cell, we measured the CV loops within different voltage windows for a bare AC coating and a poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC coating in a three-electrode cell filled with 1 M H2SO4, and the results are shown in Fig. S4.â€  Upon using a Pt counter electrode and an Ag/AgCl reference electrode, the suitable voltage range for the AC electrode is âˆ’0.2â€“0.8 V (Fig. S4aâ€ ), while that for poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC can be widened to be âˆ’0.6â€“1.1 V (Fig. S4bâ€ ). We speculate that ion insertion in poly(4,4â€²-TDP) is responsible for the broadened voltage window. In the H2SO4 electrolyte, H+ would be intercalated into the poly(4,4â€²-TDP) coating when the voltage is below âˆ’0.2 V, suppressing the evolution of H2. During the charging process, anions such as SO42âˆ’ and OHâˆ’ move towards the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC electrode. Meanwhile, H+ ions are extracted from the polymer molecules, causing an oxidation peak at âˆ¼0.5 V over the CV curve due to the conversion from â€“OH to O. When the voltage is beyond 0.8 V, the released H+ from poly(4,4â€²-TDP) is combined with OHâˆ’ at the interface, inhibiting the evolution of O2 until the voltage reaches âˆ¼1.1 V. At the opposite Pt counter electrode, water molecules gain electrons and are decomposed to release H2 and OHâˆ’ into the electrolyte to balance positive and negative charges. Since the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC electrode can lead to a much wider voltage window than the bare AC electrode in H2SO4, when it is combined with a Zn anode in a two-electrode cell filled with ZnSO4, the voltage window would be also wider than that of the Zn//AC cell, owing to the cation insertion in the cathode. In Fig. S5,â€  the two CV loops were collected from a beaker-based Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cell using ZnCl2 and ZnSO4 electrolytes, respectively. Both CV loops exhibit two oxidation peaks and two reduction peaks, indicating a similar redox behavior of the cathode in either ZnCl2 or ZnSO4 electrolytes. Therefore, the oxidation of polymer molecules by SO42âˆ’ is ruled out. However, the CV loop corresponding to the ZnCl2 electrolyte exhibits obvious polarization when the voltage approaches 1.9 V. Presumably, the relatively small Clâˆ’ ions are intercalated into the polymer coating and block the deintercalation of H+, leading to the evolution of O2 on the cathode surface when at 1.9 V.

For this measurement, the cathode after being charged or discharged in a beaker-based two-electrode system was immediately isolated and a small piece was cut off by using ceramic scissors. The cathode piece was rinsed with using deionized water and dried for XPS measurement. The charge storage mechanism of the Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cell is illustrated in Fig. 5. The poly(4,4â€²-TDP) molecular chain contains redox-active carbonyl groups that are responsible for charge storage. It should be addressed that the open voltage of a freshly assembled Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cell is âˆ¼1.1 V, and the 2 M ZnSO4 electrolyte is a bit acidic (pH âˆ¼ 5.4). Hence, in the first discharge cycle Zn2+ and H+ would move towards the cathode. As aforementioned that the poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC electrode was also tested in a three-electrode cell filled with 1 M H2SO4, and the interaction between H+ and this polymer is responsible for its pseudocapacitance as well as the widened voltage window, we believe that the coinsertion of Zn2+ and H+ would occur, causing a pair of reduction peaks as shown in Fig. 2b. A previous report on Zn//MnO2 batteries showed that both Zn2+ and H+ could be inserted into MnO2, causing two voltage plateaus in the discharge curve.21 In our work, the two voltage plateaus are obscure but the two reduction peaks over the CV curve are evident in Fig. 2b. As illustrated in Fig. 5 and by the 3D molecular geometry in Fig. S8,â€  a Zn2+ would be coordinately bound to two neighboring â€“Oâˆ’, and an H+ would be bound to an isolated O to form â€“OH. When the cathode is charged from 0.1 to 1.9 V, the extraction of Zn2+ would first occur to cause the first oxidation peak, as this bivalent ion experiences a stronger electrostatic force and the binding force between Zn2+ and â€“Oâˆ’ is weak. Second, H+ ions are released from â€“OH groups of poly(4,4â€²-TDP) molecules upon increasing the voltage, and the continuous extraction of H+ after the second oxidation peak position (âˆ¼1.65 V) leads to a high voltage limit of 1.9 V. In the following discharging process, as cations and anions encounter at the interface between the electrode and electrolyte, the electrostatic interaction would make them combined, facilitating the growth of Zn4(OH)6SO4Â·5H2O flakes. The released H+ at a higher charging voltage also has a function of etching away these byproduct flakes over the cathode surface. This accounts for the phenomenon that in the first few cycles, Zn4(OH)6SO4Â·5H2O flakes emerged over a discharged cathode but nearly disappeared when the cathode was fully charged.

Conclusion

In summary, we fabricated aqueous ZIHESDs by using a poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cathode to match with the Zn foil anode for enhancing the energy storage capacity. The redox-active polymer that is electrodeposited onto nanoporous AC granules has an effect of not only widening the voltage window but also boosting the areal energy density of the device, achieving a wide operating voltage range of 0.1â€“1.9 V and a high capacity of 1.16 mA h cmâˆ’2. The maximum energy and power densities are 1.03 mW h cmâˆ’2 and 9.0 mW cmâˆ’2, respectively. Therefore, our ZIHESD has merits of both ionic batteries and supercapacitors. In addition, we fabricated quasi-solid-state ZIHESDs that show stable electrochemical performance when bent at large angles. Even after being partially cut by using scissors, the cell shows sustained powering capability. As a demonstration of practical application, two ZIHESDs in series were used to power a mini electric fan. We conclude that both Zn2+ and H+ can be bound to the carbonyl groups of poly(4,4â€²-TDP) molecules, and the alternate insertion and extraction of H+ play a key role in rendering a wide voltage window. Our study suggests a new way to fabricate high-performance aqueous rechargeable Zn-ion cells by combining appropriate redox-active polymers with nanoporous AC to fabricate the cathode.

Experimental section

To fabricate a poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cathode, a slurry made by dispersing AC powder (YP-80, Kuraray Co.), carbon black, and polyvinylidene fluoride at a weight ratio of 8:1:1 into N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone solvent was applied onto a piece of carbon fiber fabric. After a drying process, the AC-coated carbon fabric used as the working electrode was immersed into a three-electrode cell filled with an acidic solution containing 1 M H2SO4 and 0.03 M 4,4â€²-TDP small molecules. By using a Pt foil counter electrode and an Ag/AgCl reference electrode, electropolymerization was conducted by performing a CV test at 20 mV sâˆ’1 within the voltage window of âˆ’0.3â€“1.2 V for 80 cycles. Finally, the obtained poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC electrode was dried in a vacuum oven at 80 Â°C overnight. The net weight of electrodeposited poly(4,4â€²-TDP) was measured by using an analytical balance with a resolution of 0.01 mg.

The soft-packaged aqueous Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cell was assembled by stacking a poly(4,4â€²-TP)/AC cathode, a glass fiber separator, and a Zn foil anode together. The separator was soaked with 2 M ZnSO4 aqueous electrolyte. The whole cell was sealed into an Al-plastic film pouch using hot melting glue. For the quasi-solid-state Zn//poly(4,4â€²-TDP)/AC cell, gelatin was added to the aqueous electrolyte. The assembled device was left under ambient conditions for 1 h for partially drying and then sealed. Electrochemical measurements were performed on a Corrtest CS2150H electrochemical workstation. GCD tests were performed on a LAND CT3001A battery testing system at room temperature.
