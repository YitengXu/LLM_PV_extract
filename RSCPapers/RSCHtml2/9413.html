Abstract

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), an important class of inorganicâ€“organic hybrid crystals with intrinsic porous structures, can be used as versatile precursors or sacrificial templates for preparation of numerous functional nanomaterials for various applications. Recent developments of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures, constructed by more than two components with varied functionalities, have revealed their extensive capabilities to overcome the weaknesses of the individual counterparts and thus give enhanced performance for energy storage and conversion. In this tutorial review, we summarize the recent advances in MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures. The synthetic strategies for preparing MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures are first introduced. Focusing on energy storage and conversion, we then discuss their potential applications in lithium-ion batteries, lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries, supercapacitors, lithiumâ€“oxygen batteries and fuel cells. Finally, we give our personal insights into the challenges and opportunities for the future research of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures.

1. Introduction

Hybrid micro-/nano-structured materials have played an essential role in high-performance energy storage and conversion applications, such as rechargeable batteries, supercapacitors, and fuel cells.1 Despite their complexity, these multicomponent structures hold great potential to overcome the weaknesses of the individual counterparts. Such a unique feature of hybrid-structured materials, along with unrestricted selection of components with different properties, offers an intelligent way to optimize the performance for specific applications.2 To date, a variety of hybrid micro-/nano-structures have been successfully prepared through various synthetic approaches.1â€“3 Precursors and sacrificial templates are normally required to construct some sophisticated hybrid structures, which are difficult to be achieved via conventional methods.1 Recently, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), an important class of porous inorganicâ€“organic hybrid crystals, have attracted intensive interest and been utilized as versatile precursors/sacrificial templates for the preparation of various hybrid micro-/nano-structures with complex compositions/structures and predetermined functionalities.3â€“6

MOFs are constructed via coordination bonds between metal ions/clusters and organic ligands. To date, more than 20000 MOFs with diverse compositions, crystal structures, and morphologies have been reported.7 The unique chemical constitution of MOFs allows the facile tuning of their microstructures and properties through the proper selection of metal ions and/or ligands as the building blocks, making MOFs promising for a variety of applications, including gas adsorption/separation, catalysis, and energy storage/conversion.7 Importantly, in recent years, MOFs have been proven to be versatile precursors or sacrificial templates for the synthesis of functional micro-/nano-materials, such as metal compounds (e.g. metal oxides/sulfides/carbides),8 carbon nanomaterials,9 and their hybrids.10 Generally, the preparation of MOF-derived materials relies on a thermolysis process under a certain atmosphere (e.g. argon, nitrogen, air) at an elevated temperature. The equipment for this process normally involves a high-temperature furnace that is able to afford the decomposition temperature of MOFs (e.g. 500â€“900 Â°C), as well as a gas supply system. Such ordinary requirements for the synthetic process offer an opportunity for the massive preparation of MOF-derived materials for industrial applications.

The simple synthesis of MOFs makes the subsequent preparation of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures convenient, and provides a facile way to design their structural complexity, composition uniformity, and reproducibility. One of the most fascinating advantages of MOFs is their unique composition, in which a regular arrangement of metallic and organic components is presented within the crystalline structures of MOFs. This usually leads to a uniform distribution of different components (e.g. metal nanoparticles and carbon) in the subsequently obtained MOF-derived hybrid structures. Moreover, the presence of organic ligands in MOFs enables the formation of carbon-based nanomaterials, such as amorphous and graphitic carbons11,12 after the calcination process without introducing external carbon sources. Besides, some MOFs with organic ligands containing nitrogen (e.g. methyl imidazole) can generate nitrogen-doped carbon nanomaterials after thermolysis,9 resulting in the enhancement of the electrical conductivity and electrochemical activity of the resulting nanomaterials.

Another appealing advantage of using MOFs as precursors for the preparation of hybrid micro-/nano-structures is their ability to afford nanoporous structures with a uniform pore size, high porosity and controllable morphologies, such as porous carbon or metal oxide nanostructures. The porous structures normally result from the decomposition of organic ligands or the vaporization of the metal content of MOFs at elevated temperatures.13 Note that the pore size of MOF-derived materials can be finely tuned by rationally designing the MOF precursors (e.g. composition and morphology) and extrinsically controlling the thermolysis process (e.g. annealing temperature/time, heating rate, and/or gas atmosphere). Besides, most MOF-derived materials are capable of preserving morphologies similar to their corresponding MOFs under appropriate annealing parameters.9,13,14 Given all the aforementioned advantages, several MOF-derived materials with diverse morphologies and structures have been prepared in recent years, with the aim of realizing optimal performance in certain applications, e.g. energy storage and conversion. It is worth pointing out that a large number of hybrid micro-/nano-structures with complex compositions, structures and functions, have also been achieved by using MOFs as precursors/templates. These MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures usually exhibit enhanced properties as compared to their individual counterparts due to the synergistic effect among different components, making them attractive for high-performance energy storage/conversion applications.

Although several review articles have summarized MOF-derived materials and their applications,15â€“17 they only categorized MOF-derived materials according to their compositions, such as MOF-derived metallic compounds, porous carbons and/or their composites. Given that the performance of hybrid micro-/nano-materials is highly affected by their structures, compositions and preparation approaches, we believe that offering a focused review to summarize these newly emerged MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures with sophisticated architectures and/or multiple components is of great importance for future development of MOF-derived materials. This would help researchers to obtain a deep understanding of the indispensable function of each component within MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures, as well as the strategies for the design and preparation of these hybrid micro-/nano-structures for high-performance energy storage/conversion applications.

In this tutorial review, we aim to provide a detailed and comprehensive overview of what has been achieved in the preparation of various hybrid micro-/nano-structures based on the versatile MOF-based precursors/templates, as well as their promising applications in energy storage and conversion. This review gives a brief introduction of the general synthetic strategies of various MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures, followed by summarizing their applications in energy storage and conversion, including lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries, supercapacitors, lithiumâ€“oxygen (Liâ€“O2) batteries, and fuel cells. Finally, the review is concluded with some personal insights into the future directions in this fascinating research field.

2.1 Structures and compositions of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures

2.1.1 Structures of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures. Porous hybrid polyhedrons are one of the most typical structures of MOF-derived hybrid materials, such as porous carbonâ€“ZnO quantum dot (QD) hybrids10 and microporous carbonâ€“sulfur hybrid polyhedra.13 Benefiting from their unique compositions and structures, most MOFs can be converted into hybrid materials composed of a metal compound (or metal) and carbon via a simple annealing process. The formation of porous structures in MOF-derived hybrids is normally attributed to the decomposition of crystalline MOF structures, leading to the generation of numerous void spaces.

As an attractive class of active materials for high-performance energy storage/conversion devices, hollow materials with spherical or non-spherical structures can effectively elevate the volume expansion and promote the structural stability of electrode materials, leading to a long cycle life. Using MOFs as precursors provides a novel and efficient way to create a variety of hybrid materials with hollow microstructures, such as ZnO/ZnFe2O4/C octahedra,3 CuO/Cu2O polyhedra,18 NiCo2O4/NiO dodecahedra,19 and CoFe2O4 nanocubes.20 Particularly, by rationally designing the composition of MOFs as well as carefully tuning their subsequent calcination process, hollow hybrid materials with more complex structures,21â€“23 such as multi-shelled hollow spheres,22 and cube-in-box hollow structures (Fig. 1c),23 have also been prepared in relatively convenient ways.

Importantly, ultrathin nanosheets with quasi-2D structures, possessing extraordinary features of large surface areas and a high density of edge sites, are promising materials for a variety of applications. MOF-derived 2D hybrids with ultrathin thickness and sheet-like morphology can be achieved by coating MOF films on a graphene template, followed by a calcination process and acid wash to remove the metallic ingredient.24 Recently, 2D MOF nanosheets have also been used as precursors for the preparation of hybrid materials.25,26 For example, a 2D CoS1.097â€“carbon nanocomposite was prepared by the calcination of a porphyrin paddlewheel framework-3 (PPF-3) nanosheet (Fig. 1e), which was then used as a supercapacitor electrode.25 In addition, by using a 2D Cu(HBTC)-1 nanosheet with a thickness of âˆ¼6 nm as a precursor, a copper oxide-based hybrid sheet was obtained.26 Benefiting from the unique structure-dependent properties of MOF-derived 2D hybrids, it is highly possible to achieve their further enhanced performances in certain applications through optimization of their structures. Since the metal ions/clusters of 2D MOF nanosheets normally can be replaced by other ones to form new types of 2D MOFs, more MOF-derived 2D hybrids could be prepared using the formed new 2D MOF nanosheets as the precursors/templates.

Carbon-based micro-/nano-structures have been intensively studied for numerous applications due to their unique properties (e.g. good electrical conductivity, and chemical inertness under ambient conditions), as well as various available techniques to engineer the intrinsic chemical/physical properties of carbon materials, such as morphology, crystalline structure, surface functional groups, and electronic structure. Interestingly, hierarchical carbon micro-/nano-structures, including carbon nanotube frameworks27 and graphene sheet/graphene tube nanocomposites,28 have also been successfully prepared by simply annealing the corresponding MOFs or MOF-based hybrids under proper experimental conditions.

Moreover, many substrate-supported MOF derivatives have been prepared by deposition of MOFs on certain substrates (e.g. carbon-based substrate and nickel foam) followed by a calcination process.5,29,30 This is a convenient and effective way to prepare MOF-derived hybrids with a hierarchical structure, which can be directly used as electrodes for energy storage/conversion applications.

2.1.2 Compositions of MOF-derived hybrid materials. MOF-derived carbon-based hybrids, including the hybrids of carbon with metal, metal oxide or metal carbide particles, are one of the most typical bi-component MOF-derived hybrid materials, which have been intensively studied and have exhibited promising applications in energy storage and conversion. Moreover, MOF-derived binary metal oxide structures and hierarchical carbon nanocomposites with diversified compositions, morphologies or functionalities are particularly interesting for a variety of applications.

Owing to their unique properties, metal oxides are attractive materials in many research fields. The integration of binary metal oxides with different functionalities to form a hybrid structure might afford extraordinary chemical/physical properties as well as synergistic effects between the two components, leading to enhanced performance in certain applications. Utilizing MOFs as precursors/templates provides an effective and interesting way to prepare bi-component metal oxide hybrids,21,23,31,32 such as CuO@NiO,21 and NiO/Co3O4 hybrid spheres.22 As shown in Table 1, binary metal oxide hybrids were usually obtained by calcination of pre-synthesized bimetallic MOFs, or deposition of MOFs on metal oxides followed by thermal treatment.

Other interesting MOF-derived bi-component hybrid structures are the ones composed of carbon materials with varied structures, dimensions or morphologies, such as porous carbon/graphene sheet hybrids,24 and graphene tube/graphene sheet nanocomposites.28 Due to the aforementioned unique features of carbon materials as well as various available methods to engineer their structures and intrinsic properties, it is of great importance to develop all-carbon hybrids via MOF precursors/templates for high-performance energy storage/conversion applications.24,27,28

In addition, numerous MOF-derived micro-/nano-structures with three or more components, such as hollow ZnO/ZnFe2O4/C hybrid octahedra,3 coreâ€“shell structured ZnO/ZnCo2O4/C hybrids,33 and yolkâ€“shell structured ZnO/Ni3ZnC0.7/C hybrid microspheres (Fig. 2a and b),34 have also been reported recently. It is worth mentioning that each component in the MOF-derived hybrids possesses an essential and unique function. Therefore, a rational arrangement of these components within the hybrid structure could stimulate a synergetic effect among them, thus leading to the enhancement of the overall properties of the hybrids. As a typical example, Li et al. demonstrated a complex MOF-derived hybrid structure composed of four components, i.e. rGO sheets, Co nanoparticles (NPs), carbon polyhedra and sulfur (Fig. 2câ€“g).6 Although multiple steps are required for the preparation of this hybrid, i.e. rGO/Câ€“Coâ€“S, each component plays an indispensable and special role in the hybrid. Briefly, the porous nature of carbon matrix along with the formed Coâ€“S bonding can effectively trap the incorporated sulfur species. The rGO sheets not only improved the electrical conductivity of the hybrid, but also served as a physical barrier to prevent dissolution of the polysulfides formed during the Liâ€“S battery test. Owing to the unique feature of each component and the synergetic effects among them, the prepared rGO/Câ€“Coâ€“S hybrid exhibited enhanced properties compared to the individual counterparts, making the hybrid promising for high-performance Liâ€“S battery cathodes. Similarly, the rGO-coated Fe/Fe3C@C porous nanobox reported by Chen and coworkers also demonstrated the essential role of hybrid micro-/nano-structures and the synergetic effect among the four components in enhancing the electrocatalytic activity in the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR).35

2.2 Preparation strategies for MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures

The preparation of various MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures is mainly based on the following three strategies (Fig. 3): (1) preparation of MOFs with predesigned composition/morphology followed by a calcination process; (2) hybridization of MOFs with other materials followed by a calcination process; (3) calcination of MOFs followed by post-treatment.
Fig. 3 Schematic illustration of the general preparation strategies from MOFs to their derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures: (1) preparation of MOFs with predesigned composition/morphology followed by a calcination process; (2) hybridization of MOFs with other materials followed by a calcination process; (3) calcination of MOFs followed by post-treatment.
2.2.1 Preparation of MOFs with predesigned composition/morphology followed by a calcination process. In order to control the morphologies and properties of the obtained MOF-derived micro-/nano-structures, a relatively simple method is to calcinate MOFs with pre-designed compositions/structures. After thermal treatment, the MOFs are normally converted to mixtures of carbon, metal carbides or metal oxides, resulting in the formation of hybrids with pre-designed features. Many synthetic approaches, such as the cation exchange process,21,22 hydro-/solvo-thermal treatment19,33 and self-assembly,36 have been used to prepare MOFs with pre-designed compositional and structural features. For example, a hollow ZnO/ZnFe2O4/C hybrid octahedron with a porous shell was successfully obtained after a one-step annealing process of Fe-doped MOF-5 crystals under N2 atmosphere.3 The Fe-doped MOF-5 crystal was prepared by a refluxing method based on the reaction of Fe(acac)3, Zn(NO3)2, terephthalic acid and poly(vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP) in a mixed solvent of dimethylformamide (DMF) and ethanol. Similarly, ZnO/Ni3ZnC0.7/C hybrid microspheres were prepared based on a similar strategy by thermal treatment of Ni-doped Znâ€“MOFs (Fig. 2a and b).34 By changing the solvothermal reaction conditions, both yolkâ€“shell and solid structured ZnO/Ni3ZnC0.7/C hybrids have been successfully produced. Since the compositions of MOFs can be easily altered through various methods, such as cation exchange and hydrothermal processes, a variety of other MOF-derived hybrid micro-nanostructures with multiple components can be obtained through simple calcination of these MOFs with pre-designed compositions.

The cation exchange process is an effective way to introduce a secondary metal element into MOFs and obtain bimetallic MOFs without altering the primary morphologies of the MOFs. The resulting bimetallic MOFs can be then transformed into MOF-derived hybrids with binary components after subsequent thermal treatment. This synthetic strategy has been widely used in the preparation of yolkâ€“shell and multi-shell structured MOF-derived nanomaterials.21,22 For example, a CuO@NiO hybrid microsphere with a unique ball-in-ball structure was prepared by using a Niâ€“MOF sphere as the precursor (Fig. 4a).21 Briefly, the Niâ€“MOF sphere was first synthesized by the reaction of 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTC) and Ni(NO3)2 in a micro-wave reactor. After a cation exchange reaction of Niâ€“MOF with Cu(NO3)2, a bimetallic MOF was formed (Cuâ€“Ni-BTC, Fig. 4b), which contained mixed Ni and Cu ions in its crystalline structure. A hollow CuO@NiO hybrid microsphere with multiple shells was obtained after thermal treatment of Cuâ€“Ni-BTC in air (Fig. 4c). It is worth mentioning that due to the limited ion exchange rate, the CuO@NiO hybrid microsphere exhibited a unique elemental gradient, i.e. the concentration of Ni element increased from the outer layer to the core (Fig. 4d). The formation of such a multilayer microsphere structure could be attributed to the heterogeneous decomposition of MOFs, which is a result of the temperature gradient from the outer layer to the core during the annealing process. This phenomenon is similar to that observed in the formation of many yolkâ€“shell structures. In other words, the outer surface of the MOFs decomposed first and formed the metal oxide shell before the inner part. Due to the different decomposition temperatures of the Cu- and Niâ€“organic compounds within the bimetallic MOF crystal, a stepwise decomposition and formation of metal oxide layers occurred at elevated temperatures, resulting in the formation of a ball-in-ball CuO@NiO structure. In addition, multi-shell CuO/ZnO, Co3O4/ZnO, and NiO/Co3O4 hybrid microspheres have also been prepared through similar cation exchange reactions of Co- or Zn-based MOFs followed by a calcination process.22 Obviously, numerous other types of binary metal oxide hollow nanostructures can be produced through the preparation of bimetallic MOFs followed by a calcination process.

2.2.2 Hybridization of MOFs with other materials followed by a calcination process. Preparation of MOF-based hybrids followed by a calcination process is an effective way to obtain MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures with multiple functionalities. To date, a number of MOF-based hybrids, e.g. MOFâ€“carbon24,35,37 and MOFâ€“metal oxide hybrids,5,31 have been prepared by various synthetic approaches, such as template-assisted methods,24,29,30,32 ball-milling,38 and incorporation of small molecules into the pores of MOFs.28 The resultant MOF-based hybrids served as versatile precursors/templates for the production of various MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures.

The template-assisted method is powerful in creating complex structures, and has been used in the preparation of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures. The rational combination of MOFs with various carbon nanostructures, such as one-dimensional (1D) carbon nanotubes/fibers, 2D graphene sheets, and three-dimensional (3D) porous carbons, enables the preparation of MOF-based hybrids with various morphologies, hierarchically porous nanostructures, and enhanced electrical conductivity. After thermal treatment of MOFâ€“carbon hybrids, the resulting MOF-derived materials directly contact the conductive substrate, and are promising materials for a wide range of applications, such as active materials in electrochemical systems.29,30

As a typical example, an N-doped porous carbon-coated reduced graphene oxide (rGO) sheet with a sandwich-like structure was prepared by in situ growth of a ZIF-8 film on a GO sheet, followed by the calcination of the ZIF-8/GO hybrid under Ar gas at 650â€“950 Â°C, and then washing in acid to remove the metallic Zn ingredient.24 Interestingly, it was revealed that the PVP additive enabled the homogenous growth of the ZIF-8 film on the GO sheet. This might be due to the amide carbonyl groups of PVP, which increased the adhesive force between GO and the ZIF-8 precursor (i.e. Zn2+ ions and 2-methyl imidazole (2-MeIM)), facilitating the uniform nucleation of ZIF-8 crystals on the surface of GO. Note that the uniform and complete coating of ZIF-8 crystals on GO sheets is essential for the formation of a direct contact between the derived porous carbon and rGO, facilitating effective electron transport between these two components. The resulting porous carbon-based hybrid with a quasi-2D microstructure exhibited an ultrahigh surface area (911 m2 gâˆ’1), making it attractive for a variety of surface-dependent applications, such as supercapacitors and electrocatalysts. Importantly, it is expected that a number of MOF-derived novel hybrid micro-/nano-structures could be obtained based on the aforementioned synthetic strategy using MOFs and 2D materials as building blocks.

As another typical example, our group recently reported the preparation of MOF-derived porous metal oxide-coated 3D graphene composites (Fig. 5).29 Briefly, ZIF-8 or MIL-88-Fe crystals were first grown on a HNO3-treated 3D graphene network (3DGN) through in situ growth (Fig. 5aâ€“c). 2.2.3 Calcination of MOFs followed by post-treatment. MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nanostructure can also be obtained through calcination of MOFs followed by post-treatment, in which the calcinated MOFs serve as building blocks that can be incorporated with other functional materials to form more complex hybrid structures. Many approaches have been developed for the post-treatment of calcination products of MOFs, such as further transformation of the compositions of calcinated MOFs,39 incorporation of other materials into the porous structures of calcinated MOFs,13,40 and decoration of the surface of calcinated MOFs with other species.41 Typically, metal compoundâ€“carbon hybrids, such as a NiSâ€“carbon hybrid,39 can be simply obtained by the hydro-/solvo-thermal treatment of MOF-derived metalâ€“carbon hybrids under proper reaction conditions. For example, a Se-embedded porous carbon hybrid sphere has been prepared through calcination of Ni-BTC MOFs followed by post-treatment (Fig. 6).42 The calcinated Ni-BTC MOFs were first washed by acid to remove Ni to produce porous carbon spheres, which were then subjected to a melt-diffusion process to incorporate Se to obtain the Seâ€“carbon hybrid sphere (Fig. 6a). Based on such a synthetic approach for MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nanostructures, the original morphologies of the calcinated MOFs can be extensively preserved after the post-treatment (Fig. 6bâ€“g). Note that the numerous currently existing material processing techniques, such as mechanical mixing, electrostatic adsorption, electrophoretic/spray deposition, and spin coating, can also be applied to the post-treatment of calcinated MOFs, creating more complex micro-/nano-structures. It can be expected that a variety of novel hybrid micro-/nanostructures with improved properties or predetermined functions will emerge using MOF-derived materials as the building blocks.

3. Applications

Compared to individual counterparts, hybrid micro-/nano-structures can achieve significantly enhanced performance in a given application through the synergistic effect among the components with rationally designed properties. Generally, there are several advantages of using MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures for energy storage and conversion. (1) Most MOF-derived materials exhibit micron-scale structures, which are assembled from diverse nano-sized components. Micron-scale structures are normally tolerant to the aggregation problem as compared to the nano-sized counterparts, which largely preserves the pristine properties of the nanomaterials. (2) When used as electrodes for rechargeable batteries, the interior spaces of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures can effectively accommodate the large volumetric expansions induced by intercalation/de-intercalation of electrolyte ions. (3) MOF-derived porous structures can not only reduce the diffusion length of electrolyte ions by improving the contact effectiveness between electrode and electrolyte, but also provide more active edge sites for the redox reaction. (4) Some components of MOF-derived hybrid structures (e.g. carbon and metal) with high electrical conductivity can improve the performance of the obtained hybrids by enhancing the charge transfer efficiency. Especially, the components with special 3D architectures, such as 3D graphene networks, nickel foams and carbon fiber papers, not only have a large surface area, but also directly serve as current collectors with 3D connected electron pathways.

In this section, the energy storage and conversion applications of various MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures are discussed with emphasis on: (1) how to optimize the performance of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures by rational design of their compositions, morphologies and hierarchical structures; and (2) the advantages of MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures for energy storage and conversion as compared to other kinds of nanostructures.

3.1 Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs)

LIBs are one of the most important and widely used energy storage devices in our daily life, playing an indispensable role in portable electronic devices, laptops, and electrical vehicles. Electrode materials are one of the key elements that determine the overall performance of LIBs. Therefore, enormous effort has been devoted to the design and preparation of novel materials or materials with unique structures as LIB electrodes. The intrinsic porous structure of MOFs, with most being microporous with a pore size of <2 nm, enables the reversible insertion/removal of Li+ ions and preserves the structural integrity of the MOFs during the charge/discharge process. Moreover, lithium storage can also be achieved by the interaction between the MOF ligands and Li+ ions, leading to increased capacity. These features make MOFs potential candidates for LIB electrodes. However, low capacity and poor cycling stability have been observed with MOF-based electrodes due to their instability during the conversion reaction with Li+ ions, as well as the poor electrical conductivity. Fortunately, MOF-derived materials, including carbon materials, metal oxides and their composites, have been demonstrated to be excellent electrodes for LIBs. For example, a ZnO QD-porous carbon hybrid obtained by calcination of IRMOF-1 crystals delivered a high specific capacity of âˆ¼1200 mA h gâˆ’1 at a current density of 75 mA gâˆ’1.10 Moreover, a reversible capacity of 911 mA h gâˆ’1 was achieved on the mesoporous Î±-Fe2O3 with a spindle-like structure after 50 charge/discharge cycles at a rate of 200 mA gâˆ’1, which was prepared from the MIL-88-Fe precursor.14 Even at a high charge/discharge rate of 10 A gâˆ’1, a capacity of 424 mA h gâˆ’1 still remained, indicating the good rate capability of this MOF-derived Fe2O3 electrode. The excellent performance of this MOF-derived electrode could be attributed to its unique porous micro-/nano-structure, which enhances the electrode activity and accommodates the volume change of the active material during cycling. Besides, the surface/interface Li storage may also contribute to the high reversible capacity.

Importantly, MOF-derived materials with more complex structures and compositions have been developed and used for LIBs in recent years. In particular, hollow structured nanomaterials have been prepared by using MOFs as precursors. For example, a binary metal oxide hybrid microsphere with multiple shells (CuO@NiO) was prepared by using a Cuâ€“Ni bimetallic MOF as the precursor.21 Benefiting from such a unique structure and chemical composition, the CuO@NiO hybrid microsphere exhibited a reversible capacity of âˆ¼1000 mA h gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles (Fig. 7a), higher than the theoretical capacities of both CuO (674 mA h gâˆ’1) and NiO (718 mA h gâˆ’1).21 In comparison, the electrode of NiO microspheres derived from the Ni-BTC MOF exhibited a rapid decay of the specific capacity after 70 charge/discharge cycles, which was only âˆ¼150 mA h gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles. The enhanced performance could be mainly attributed to the improved structural stability of the CuO@NiO hybrid microsphere. The lithium insertion voltages for the two metal oxides are different (i.e. âˆ¼1.2 V for CuO and âˆ¼0.7 V for NiO, Fig. 7b and c), leading to the reactions with Li+ ions occurring at different times. In other words, a stepwise lithium insertion process occurred within the binary metal oxide hybrid. This effectively eliminated the mechanical stress within the CuO@NiO hybrid, because the non-reacted component can act as a temporary buffer for the volumetric expansion of the reacted one. Therefore, the pulverization problem of the electrode material was extremely prevented, leading to high electrochemical stability and long cycle life. This work may provide a general way to fabricate porous metal oxides with binary components for high-performance LIBs. Besides MOF-derived hollow spheres, other non-spherical hollow structures have also been successfully prepared from MOF-based precursors/templates and then used for LIBs, such as a CuO/Cu2O hollow polyhedron,18 a Niâ€“Fe binary oxide nanobox and a cube-in-box hollow structure,23 and a NiCo2O4/NiO hollow dodecahedron.19

It is worth mentioning that hierarchically structured electrode materials can also be obtained through the deposition of MOFs on conductive substrates followed by calcination. This fabrication strategy eliminates the requirement for an additional binder, carbon additive, or current collector, which is suitable for fabrication of lightweight energy storage devices due to the reduced total mass and the increased energy density of the cell. Additionally, MOF-derived porous carbons can effectively improve the performance of LIBs when coated onto other active materials (e.g. Si and metal oxides). Owing to the advantages of electrical conductivity and mechanical flexibility of carbon materials, the resultant hybrid electrode materials with improved structural stability can greatly accommodate the volume change during the charge/discharge process, leading to a long cycle life and a high rate capability. More recently, MOF-derived materials have also been investigated as cathode materials for LIBs with enhanced performance. For example, Wang and coworkers demonstrated that the UiO-66-derived ZrO2 served as an effective protection layer for the cathode material of LiCoO2 to improve its rate capability.43 The resultant ZrO2@LiCoO2 hybrid cathode delivered a reversible capacity of 148 mA h gâˆ’1 at a high current density of 2325 mA gâˆ’1 after 100 cycles. In contrast, the capacity of bare LiCoO2 rapidly decreased to âˆ¼20 mA h gâˆ’1. Interestingly, the hybrid cathode also exhibited a reversible capacity of 132 mA h gâˆ’1 after 100 cycles at 55 Â°C, indicating good thermal stability. 3.2 Lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries (Liâ€“S batteries)

Liâ€“S batteries have a high energy density (2500 W h kgâˆ’1) and theoretical specific capacity (1675 mA h gâˆ’1) and rely on cheap and environmentally friendly sulfur cathode materials.40 Therefore, Liâ€“S batteries are considered as promising alternatives to the conventional LIB-based energy storage devices. However, the development of Liâ€“S batteries, in its current stage, is restricted by the well-known problem of the â€œshuttle effectâ€, which originates from the dissolution of intermediate products (i.e. polysulfides) in the organic electrolyte, leading to the de-activation of the electrodes as well as poor cycling performance. To solve this problem, developing novel materials or materials with unique structures, which can effectively trap the polysulfides during charge/discharge process, is of great importance. Porous carbon materials have drawn intensive attention as host materials for Liâ€“S batteries due to their ability to anchor polysulfides through chemical bonding or physical interactions. MOF-derived carbon materials have shown high surface area, hierarchically porous structures and good electrical conductivity, making them attractive candidates for Liâ€“S batteries.

As a typical example, Xu et al. first demonstrated that sulfur-encapsulated porous carbon nanoplates delivered a high initial discharge capacity of 1177 mA h gâˆ’1 at a low rate of 167.5 mA gâˆ’1.40 The hybrid cathode exhibited an excellent cycling performance with a reversible capacity of âˆ¼730 mA h gâˆ’1 after charging and discharging at 837.5 mA gâˆ’1 for 50 cycles. Later on, Low and coworkers further revealed that the performance of MOF-derived carbonâ€“sulfur hybrid cathodes in Liâ€“S batteries is affected by the sulfur concentration within the hybrid and the type of electrolyte.13 The carbonâ€“sulfur hybrid electrodes with sulfur concentrations of 43 and 63 wt%, respectively, exhibited distinct chargeâ€“discharge profiles, suggesting different electrochemical reactions taking place in the two electrodes. More recently, lithiumâ€“selenium (Liâ€“Se) batteries have also been developed based on MOF-derived porous carbon spheres.42 Note that Se possesses much higher electrical conductivity compared to S at room temperature (1 Ã— 10âˆ’5vs. 5 Ã— 10âˆ’30 S cmâˆ’1),42 which can increase the electron transportation rate of the cathode material. The prepared carbonâ€“Se hybrid electrode delivered an initial specific capacity of 588.2 mA h gâˆ’1 at 337.5 mA gâˆ’1, close to the theoretical capacity of Se (675 mA h gâˆ’1).42 Additionally, a reversible capacity of âˆ¼250 mA h gâˆ’1 was also obtained for the hybrid electrode after 500 charge/discharge cycles at 337.5 mA gâˆ’1, corresponding to a decay rate of 0.08% per cycle.

Therefore, further improved performance of Liâ€“S (Se) batteries might be achieved by optimization of the sulfur (selenium) content, pore size distribution and electrical conductivity of MOF-derived hybrid cathodes.

3.3 Supercapacitors

Supercapacitors are one of the most promising energy storage devices with unique features, such as fast charge rate, long cycle life, and high power density. Based on the electrode/electrolyte interface-dominant charge-storage mechanism, the performance of supercapacitors is highly reliant on the surface area and electrical conductivity of electrode materials. It is not surprising that MOFs with high surface area have been investigated in supercapacitor applications in the past few years.44 Owing to their unique porous structures and convenient preparation methods, MOF-derived materials are also attractive as high-performance supercapacitor electrodes, since they are able to provide effective contact between the electrode and electrolyte. For example, nanoporous carbon, prepared through calcinating a mixture of furfuryl alcohol and MOF-5 crystals, exhibited an ideal capacitor behavior with a specific capacitance of âˆ¼100 F gâˆ’1 at a scan rate of 5 mV sâˆ’1.11 Moreover, many MOF-derived hybrid materials composed of carbon and faradaic electrode materials, such as a porous Fe3O4â€“carbon composite,45 a nanoporous Co3O4â€“carbon hybrid,46 and a nitrogen-doped carbonâ€“CoS1.097 hybrid,25 have also been developed and used as electrode materials for supercapacitors. Benefiting from the synergistic effect of different components, these MOF-derived hybrid electrodes exhibited high specific capacitance and energy density.

To improve the electrical conductivity of MOF-derived electrode materials, we developed a conductive wrapping strategy, i.e. the Moâ€“MOF was wrapped by a GO film and subjected to a subsequent two-step annealing process to obtain a porous rGO/MoO3 composite (Fig. 8a).37 As evidenced by TEM images and Raman mapping (Fig. 8b and c), the rGO film with a thickness of âˆ¼20 nm was coated on the porous MoO3 nanostructure, which not only acted as a conductive shell but also enhanced the mechanical strength of the obtained rGO/MoO3 composite. Moreover, the rGO films could also interconnect with each other to form a large electrically conductive network, which is beneficial for the improvement of the rate capability of the composite electrode. After assembling the rGO/MoO3 composite into an all-solid-state flexible supercapacitor, the fabricated device delivered a high specific capacitance of 404 F gâˆ’1 at 0.5 A gâˆ’1. The flexible supercapacitor devices also exhibited good electrochemical and structural stabilities under varied bending statuses (Fig. 8d and e), which benefited from the unique micro-/nano-structure of the rGO/MoO3 composite. As shown in Fig. 8f, three supercapacitor devices that are connected in series can successfully light a light-emitting diode (LED), suggesting the potential application of the rGO/MoO3 composite in flexible energy storage devices. It is worth mentioning that the formation of the GO-wrapped Moâ€“MOF composite could be attributed to the interaction of the MOF and surface functional groups of GO. Therefore, this conductive wrapping strategy could be applicable to the preparation of other GO-wrapped MOF composites and their corresponding MOF-derived porous micro-/nanostructures, which can be used for high-performance energy storage applications.

3.4 Other energy storage and conversion applications

In addition to the aforementioned applications, many MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures have also shown high activities in catalytic reactions, such as the ORR, oxygen evolution reaction (OER), and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). These reactions are essential for some energy storage and conversion devices, including lithiumâ€“oxygen (Liâ€“O2) batteries and fuel cells.27,47â€“50 MOF-derived ORR catalysts, especially heteroatom-doped carbon materials and metal-/metal compound-carbon hybrids, are highly expected to be alternatives to conventional noble metal catalysts (e.g. Pt/C), because of their simple preparation process, low production cost, and improved catalytic stability. As is well known, the properties of materials are somehow dependent on their morphologies. Recent research has further demonstrated that the morphology of a catalyst plays an important role in improving its catalytic properties for the ORR.27,28 Using a cage-containing MOF as the precursor, Li et al. prepared a hybrid catalyst composed of graphene sheets and graphene tubes, which was used as a cathode for Liâ€“O2 batteries.28 The used MOFs with a large cage size of 1.8 nm were prepared by using 4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine as a ligand. Such special structural feature of the cage-containing MOFs facilitated the subsequent incorporation of foreign substances, i.e. dicyandiamide (DCDA) and iron acetate, inside their pores to form the MOF-based hybrid, which was then used as the precursor for the preparation of varied carbon-based catalysts. After the subsequent calcination process of the obtained MOF-based hybrid at different temperatures, three carbon-based nanostructures with diverse morphologies, i.e. the onion-like carbon/Fe3C hybrid, N-doped carbon tubes and N-doped graphene sheet/graphene tube hybrid, were prepared. The formation of these graphitic carbon nanostructures can be attributed to the following factors: (1) the organic ligands and DCDA within the MOF hybrid served as the carbon sources; (2) the decomposition of the MOF hybrid generated Fe/Fe3C, which acted as the catalyst. When used as an ORR catalyst, the performance of the N-doped graphene sheet/graphene tube hybrid is higher than that of the other two prepared carbon-based nanostructures (i.e. the onion-like carbon/Fe3C hybrid and the N-doped carbon tube), which is also comparable to that of the Pt/C catalyst. Notably, this hybrid catalyst also exhibited a high half-wave potential in alkaline electrolyte (E1/2, 0.88 V vs. reversible hydrogen electrode, RHE). The N-doped graphene sheet/graphene tube hybrid catalyst was further used as a cathode material, and then assembled in a Liâ€“O2 battery using LiPF6 in tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) as the electrolyte. The fabricated cell delivered a high initial discharge capacity up to 5300 mA h gâˆ’1 (calculated based on the mass of catalyst), as well as a good cycling stability with 73% capacity retention after 50 cycles at a relatively high current density of 400 mA gâˆ’1, suggesting the potential application of MOF-derived catalysts for Liâ€“O2 batteries.

More recently, Xia et al. developed a facile method to synthesize a nitrogen-doped CNT hollow framework using ZIF-67 as the precursor (NCNTFs, Fig. 10).27 Note that the H2 atmosphere used in the calcination process played an essential role in the formation of CNTs, which enhanced the catalytic activity of the MOF-derived Co species for the formation of graphitic carbon nanostructures (Fig. 10aâ€“c). On the contrary, the calcination of ZIF-67 without H2 only produced carbon-based materials with low graphitization degree (Fig. 10d), which is in agreement with the observations of many other related reports using ZIF-67 as the precursor for carbon materials. Moreover, the organic ligand of ZIF-67, i.e. 2-MeIM, also served as a carbon source for the growth of CNTs. The resultant nanomaterial was then used as an ORR catalyst, exhibiting a high half-wave potential (0.87 V, Fig. 10e), a smaller Tafel slope (64 mV decâˆ’1) and an electron transfer number close to 4, superior to the conventional Pt/C catalyst. The resulting CNT framework also possessed an excellent catalytic stability, exhibiting 96% retention of its initial current density after testing for 100000 s (Fig. 10f).

Generally, the good ORR performance of the aforementioned two MOF-derived carbon hybrid structures could be attributed to their unique morphologies and structures. First, the unique carbon networks, which were constructed by the entangled graphitic carbons (i.e. CNTs and graphene), offered efficient electron transportation pathways to the hybrid catalysts. Second, these MOF-derived hierarchically porous structures possessed a large surface area (e.g. 513 m2 gâˆ’1 for the CNT framework27), which provided a high density of active sites for the ORR, leading to enhanced catalytic performances. Third, the unique compositions and crystalline structures of the MOFs also enabled the uniform dispersion of heteroatoms in the resultant carbon-based hybrid structures, which further enhanced the catalytic properties. Fourth, in comparison to amorphous carbon materials, graphitic carbon usually possesses high mechanical strength, resulting in good structural stability and cycling performance. It is worth mentioning that the residual metallic species in the MOF-derived CNT hollow framework might also partially contribute to the overall catalytic properties. Importantly, using MOF-based materials as the precursors/templates, hierarchical graphitic carbon nanostructures can be obtained. This straightforward preparation strategy can also be extended to other kinds of MOF-based precursors/templates, opening up numerous options to synthesize various novel carbon-based catalysts with enhanced performance. For the future research in this area, introduction of other heteroatoms, such as sulfur and boron, into the MOF-derived carbon structures could tune the electronic structures and the density of active sites, leading to further improved performance of Liâ€“O2 batteries and fuel cells.

Obviously, a number of recently developed MOF-derived hybrid materials have shown promising potential in energy storage and conversion applications, which can be attributed to their unique hybrid micro-/nano-structures. For example, when used as a battery electrode, the void space in MOF-derived hollow spherical or non-spherical structures can effectively eliminate the volume change during the charge/discharge process, leading to a long cycle life. MOF-derived 2D hybrids with ultrathin thickness normally have a high density of surface sites, which are quite active for redox reaction or catalytic processes, such as the OER, ORR and HER. This feature makes MOF-derived 2D hybrids promising for application in supercapacitors and Liâ€“O2 batteries. Owing to the unique chemical/physical properties of carbon nanomaterials, MOF-derived hierarchical carbon micro-/nano-structures are highly attractive for the construction of electrode materials for various energy storage/conversion applications. Substrate-supported MOF derivatives enable the simple fabrication of MOF-derived electrodes with controlled structures, which can be directly used for various applications, such as LIBs, Liâ€“S batteries and supercapacitors. It is expected that more MOF-derived hybrid materials with novel structures and functions can be synthesized through rationally designing MOF-based materials and finely controlling their thermolysis process.

4. Conclusion

Owing to the current demand for high-performance energy storage and conversion applications, the development of advanced materials as electrodes or catalysts is of great importance. Recent research progress in MOF-related materials has demonstrated the powerful capability of utilizing MOF-based materials as precursors or scarifying templates for the preparation of various novel materials or materials with unique structures. To date, numerous MOF-derived materials with various compositions, morphologies, structures, properties and functionalities have been successfully produced and then used for a wide range of applications. MOF-derived materials are attractive candidates for electrode materials of high-performance rechargeable batteries. For example, MOF-derived carbon materials possessing high porosity and uniform pore size distribution can lead to a fast electron/mass transfer rate. The porous metal compounds derived from MOFs are able to greatly eliminate the volumetric expansion and pulverization problem.

MOF-derived hybrids with more complicated micro-/nano-structures have also been demonstrated in recent years, which are normally composed of more than two components assembling in different hierarchical levels. Within such kind of hybrids, each component has as special role and/or possesses a unique function, which can overcome the drawbacks of the individual counterparts and generate a synergistic effect for enhanced performance. Typically, MOF-derived metal oxideâ€“carbon hybrids with hierarchically porous structures possess a number of appealing features, including electrical conductivity, a short diffusion path length for electrolyte ions, and structural stability. Due to their promising features, MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures have been intensively used in energy storage and conversion devices, such as LIBs, supercapacitors and fuel cells. Importantly, MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures are also suitable for many other applications, including solar cells, thermoelectric devices, piezoelectric devices, and water purification.

Previous studies have proven that MOFs can serve as versatile building blocks for the preparation of various materials, which might be difficult to be achieved by using other methods. Compared to conventional synthetic approaches for porous materials, especially those with multiple components or complicated hierarchical structures, using MOFs as precursors/templates has enormous advantages. First, the preparation process for MOF-derived porous materials is convenient and scalable. Second, the structures and compositions of the obtained MOF-derived products can be easily controlled. Third, a uniform distribution of each component in the prepared MOF-derived hybrid structures can be achieved. It can be expected that through rationally designing the preparation procedures or combining various mature synthetic technologies (e.g. wet- and dry-chemical methods), plenty of novel MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures with predetermined functionalities and properties will emerge, which can be used for a variety of applications.

Undoubtedly, rapid progress has been made in the preparation and application of MOF-derived materials over the past few years. However, this research field is still under development. More effort is required to realize the practical usage of MOF-derived micro-/nano-structures. Several challenges and possible research directions for future work in this area are described as follows. (1) Most of the reported studies are conducted by choosing existing and common type of MOFs, such as the ZIF and MIL series, as precursors/sacrificial templates, followed by investigating the properties of the resultant MOF-derived structures and their applications. However, MOFs with exclusively designed features are of great importance to realize the precisely controlled properties, functionalities and performance of MOF-derived structures. (2) Previous works have indicated that synthetic parameters strongly affect the compositions, structures and morphologies of the resultant MOF-derived hybrid nanomaterials. Therefore, a systematic investigation on revealing the relationship between the properties and structures of prepared MOF-derived materials is very important, and will direct the preparation of high-performance MOF-derived materials. Following this, the development of efficient synthetic routes with the capability of precisely manipulating features of MOF-derived materials is also important. (3) As for research using MOF-based hybrids (e.g. MOFâ€“graphene hybrids) as the precursors/templates for MOF-derived hybrid micro-/nano-structures, an in-depth understanding of the interfacial interactions between MOFs and the other components is also very important, since it could provide guidance to optimize the synthetic process of MOF-based hybrids for enhanced performance. (4) Although MOF-derived hybrid materials have shown promising applications in energy storage and conversion, their volumetric energy densities are relatively low. This limitation mainly results from their porous structures with low density. To solve it, optimization of electrode processing techniques, such as engineering the microstructure and ratio of components within the hybrid electrode, is expected to increase the packing density without deteriorating the performance.
