Functional supramolecular aggregates based on BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes: control over the pathway complexity

Zhijian Chen * and Zhaoying Chen
School of Chemical Engineering and Technology, and National Collaborative Innovation Center of Chemical Science and Engineering (Tianjin), Tianjin University, Tianjin, 300072, China. E-mail: zjchen@tju.edu.cn

First published on 20th April 2023

Abstract

Boron-dipyrromethene (BODIPY) and boron-azadipyrromethene (aza-BODIPY) dyes are attractive candidates for versatile building blocks for functional supramolecular dye aggregates owing to their excellent photophysical properties, stabilities, and facilities of chemical modification. This review gives an overview of the recent advances in the self-assembly of BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes, emphasizing the control over the kinetic pathway complexity of this class of dyes. Besides the theoretical models used for single or coupled supramolecular aggregation processes with isodesmic, cooperative, or anti-cooperative mechanisms, representative examples of BODIPY and aza-BODIPY aggregates with a variety of supramolecular interactions and pathway complexity are presented. The pathway-dependent functionalities and potential applications of these supramolecular aggregates are discussed.

1. Introduction

In recent years, supramolecular aggregates19 based on a variety of functional molecules have been constructed by cooperative or independent non-covalent molecular interactions, including hydrogen bonding,1012 stacking,13,14 hydrophobic interactions,1517 metalligand interactions,1820etc., and intensively investigated for their potential applications in a broad range of fields such as nanomaterials,21 organic electronics,22 biomedicine,23,24 and so on. Among the functional molecules, organic dyes constitute an important class of molecular building blocks in the formation of supramolecular aggregates. After the serendipitous and independent discovery of the J-aggregates of a cyanine dye (pseudoisocyanine) by Jelley and Scheibe in the 1930s,1 the J- and H-aggregation properties of various dyes, including cyanine dyes,25 porphyrins,26,27 phthalocyanines,27,28 perylene bismides,29,30 squaraines,31 and chlorophylls,32 were studied in detail. It was observed that these aggregates exhibited collective optical or electronic properties that were altered significantly from those for monomeric dyes, e.g. hypsochromic or bathochromic shifts in absorption and emission bands, changes in spectral shape and intensity, and aggregation-induced fluorescence quenching (ACQ) or enhancement (AIE),33,34 transport of charges and energy,35 and so on. Such new properties upon supramolecular dye aggregation have attracted tremendous attention from academics. In 2000, the concept of supramolecular dye chemistry was elucidated by Wrthner,36 pointing out the importance of the appropriate organization of dye molecules in the system for yielding properties that are not given by the monomeric building blocks. For examples, natural chlorophyll molecules self-assemble into rod-like nano-aggregates for efficient energy transfer between the chromophores in light harvesting.37 Meanwhile, the stacked aggregates of synthetic dyes including cyanine, perylene bisimides, and BODPIYs have been applied successfully as organic semiconductors in organic electronic and photovoltaic devices.3840

In order to obtain supramolecular aggregates with the expected structure, morphology, and properties for targeted applications, it was revealed recently that the control over the assembly pathway complexity4146 of the molecular building blocks is highly important since the competing kinetic assembly pathways may lead to aggregates with diverse supramolecular structures and thermodynamic stabilities. Accordingly, efforts have been devoted to an in-depth mechanistic understanding of the thermodynamic and kinetic aspects of supramolecular polymerization processes for many -conjugated molecular systems. Based on these studies, the strategies of living supramolecular polymerization (LSP)4755 with a chain-growth mechanism have been developed, which offer a more precise control over the supramolecular structures of the products in comparison with aggregation based on spontaneous nucleation and elongation mechanism.56,57

Boron-dipyrromethenes (BODIPYs) are considered as one class of versatile functional dyes.58,59 BODIPY dyes have been known for nearly 60 years since their first synthesis in 196860 and only in recent years have their extraordinary self-assembly properties and optical and electronic properties stemming from aggregation been brought to light, as shown by several review articles.6164 Based on these properties, further applications of this class of dyes including fluorescence imaging and sensing,65,66 photodynamic and photothermal therapy,66,67 as well as stimulus-responsive materials68 have become feasible. Since the synthesis and optical properties of a large number of BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes have been well summarized in a number of reviews,58,59,6972 the current contribution focuses on the newest advances in the self-assembly properties of BODIPY- as well as aza-BODIPY-based supramolecular systems, emphasizing the importance of the control over the kinetic pathway complexity of this class of dyes and their capability of living supramolecular polymerization. In addition, a few examples of assembly pathway-dependent applications of BODIPY- and aza-BODIPY-based supramolecular structures are presented briefly.

2. Self-assembling (aza-)BODIPY dyes and mechanistic aspects of their aggregation process

2.1 Self-assembling BODIPYs and aza-BODIPYs

For the synthesis of (aza-)BODIPYs featuring self-assembly properties, the dipyrromethene chromophores can be modified at various positions (18 for BODIPY and 17 for aza-BODIPY),58,61,69 allowing the introduction of functional groups such as hydrophobic wedges, H-bond donors and acceptors, and ligands of metal ions for modulating the intermolecular non-covalent forces and enabling self-assembly of the dyes. The functional groups at the (aza-)BODIPY cores can be introduced easily by transition-metal-catalysed cross-coupling reactions such as Heck, Sonogashira, Suzuki, and Stille coupling reactions using halogenated BODIPY precursors,58,69,72 such as 2,6-bromo- or 2,6-iodo-substituted BODIPYs.73,74 Moreover, alkynyl Grignard reagents can be used to attach functional groups by replacing the fluorine atoms at the boron.58,75 Other reactions, such as Knoevenagel, condensation and Michael addition reactions, have also been applied in the synthesis of self-assembling (aza-)BODIPY dyes.76,77

2.2 J- and H-aggregation of (aza-)BODIPY dyes

Both the BODIPY and aza-BODIPY cores show strong -electron delocalization due to their effectively conjugated backbone structures.69 Accordingly, intensive optical absorption bands ( > 105 M1 cm1) as well as fluorescence ( > 0.9) have been observed for these dyes. While the majority of BODIPY dyes have their S0S1 transition bands located between 490700 nm,59 aza-BODIPY dyes with the same substituents often displayed absorption and emission bands at longer wavelength in the visible to NIR range.5871 The calculated HOMOs and LUMOs of 1,3,5,7-tetramethyl substituted BODIPY and aza-BODIPY (Fig. 2a) indicated their comparable electronic structures. Upon aggregation, (aza-)BODIPY chromophores often display obvious spectral shifts, which can be elucidated by Kasha's excitonic coupling theory.79 In a simple case (Fig. 2b), the treatment of two aggregated molecules with coplanar inclined transition dipole moments by this theory results in the splitting of the excited state into two levels, corresponding to the in-phase and out-of-phase arrangement of of the chromophores, respectively. For the two energy states, only one of them is transition-allowed. The energy difference between the two states, i.e. exciton splitting energy , is given by equation = 2||2(13cos2)/ruv3, where ruv is the center to center distance between molecules, and is the angle between and the line of the molecule centres. For = 54.7 (so-called magic angle), = 0 is obtained, implying there is no spectral shift in this case. For < 54.7, transition to a lower energy level is allowed and gives a bathochromically shifted absorption band (J-band) while for > 54.7, transition to a higher energy level is allowed, resulting in a hypsochromically shifted absorption band (H-band).1 These results for the dimeric aggregate can be generalized to polymeric aggregates, i.e. the J-aggregates have a highly slipped molecular packing with a small while a face-to-face packing mode is favoured for H-aggregates.

2.3 Mechanistic aspects of dye aggregation

Besides the structure control of the aggregates by tuning the type of intermolecular force, the pathway complexity41 has been reported for a number of BODIPY and aza-BODIPY self-assembly systems. To lay the foundations for control over the pathway complexity of this class of dyes, the in-depth mechanistic understanding of the self-assembly process is crucial. In the literature, different types of kinetic assembly pathway can be summarized for (aza-)BODIPYs dyes (Fig. 3). Many of the self-assembly systems based on (aza-)BODIPY dyes exhibited a single aggregation pathway (Fig. 3a). For the vast majority of these systems, the aggregation of monomeric species (M) follows either an isodesmic or a nucleation-elongation mechanism, forming the thermodynamically stable aggregate species A.
Fig. 3 Energy landscapes illustrating supramolecular systems with (a) a single assembly pathway, (b) two competitive assembly pathways, and (c) two consecutive assembly pathways.

In contrast, some BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dye systems display two-pathway self-assembly characteristics. For such systems, the relationship between the individual pathways can be described as competitive or consecutive.46 The competing assembly pathways (Fig. 3b) lead to formation of the kinetic-controlled metastable aggregate species A and the thermodynamically stable aggregate B. For the transformation of A to B, the disassembly of A into the monomer is necessary. In this situation, species A is identified as the off-pathway aggregate while B is the on-pathway aggregate. In the case of consecutive pathways, the monomers first form the aggregate species A (Fig. 3c), which can be directly transformed into the more stable aggregate species B without disassembly into monomers M. The aggregate A is referred to as an on-pathway aggregate for the final product B.

3. (Aza-)BODIPY-based supramolecular dye aggregates by the single assembly pathway

It has been well known that BODIPY- and aza-BODIPY-based supramolecular aggregates can be constructed by introducing various non-covalent intermolecular interactions into the monomers. In many cases, these systems quickly reach equilibrium and exhibit a single aggregation pathway that leads to the formation of thermodynamic stable supramolecular nanoaggregates. As shown in the literature, the driving force of the single-pathway assembly process of (aza-)BODIPYs dyes include stacking,95100 hydrogen bonding,101108 hydrophobic effects,84,109115 metalligand interactions,116120 electrostatic interactions,121123 and others.124 The molecular structures of representative examples of self-assembling BODIPY dyes are depicted in Chart 1.

In 2012, Fernndez and co-workers reported the aggregation of BODIPY dye 1 driven exclusively by interactions in cyclohexane.97 The aggregation of dye 1 was investigated by means of UV/Vis spectroscopy, SEM, and concentration-dependent and temperature-dependent NMR. The results indicated the isodesmic mechanism of dye 1 aggregation and the equilibrium constant was determined as 1.0 104 M1. Further ROESY NMR experiments revealed the organization of the stacks of dye 1 (Fig. 5a), where the BODIPY and OPE fragments were alternately stacked with a slip angle of 39 and a distance of 3.4 . As shown by SEM as well as fluorescence microscopic studies, dye 1 formed fluorescent 1D micrometric-sized associations consisting of individual alternated -stacks (Fig. 5b). In addition, a columnar rectangular mesophase was also observed for this dye at room temperature.

Besides interactions, hydrogen bonding interactions play an important role in the self-assembly of BODIPY dyes. In 2006, Ziessel et al. synthesized a BODIPY-based organic gelator 2,101 which was functionalized with an intermolecularly H-bonding unit bearing three amide groups. In nonane, the dye 2 exhibited excellent gelation abilities and formed fibrous J-type aggregates with strong photoluminescence, as revealed by the emission spectrum as well as TEM and FTIR studies (Fig. 6). The gelation of dye 2 could be ascribed to the combination of intermolecular hydrogen bonds and interactions between the BODIPY fluorophores.

More recently, we designed and synthesized a series of BODIPY dyes 3ac bearing two uracil groups at the 2,6-positions with ethynyl spacers.75,106 As the transition dipole moments of this dye are in parallel with the direction of the intermolecular H-bonds, a head-to-tail arrangement (J-aggregation) of transition dipole moments with strong excitonic coupling is expected of this dye. Indeed, a narrowed and intensive J-band with a large bathochromic shift of 3060 nm was observed for 3a in MCH and 3b,c in n-hexane as compared with the monomeric dyes, accompanied by obvious colour changes of the solution (Fig. 7a). Temperature- and concentration-dependent studies for 3ac revealed a mechanism of highly cooperative supramolecular polymerization. Further 1H NMR studies indicated strong intermolecular H-bonds between the uracil groups during the aggregation of dye 3ac, leading to the formation of nanowires (Fig. 7b).

The J-aggregates of dyes 3ac exhibited attractive emission properties. For dyes 3bc, J-aggregation induced emission enhancement (AIEE)125 was observed. For 3b the fluorescence quantum yields were largely improved from 0.27 (monomers) to 0.62 (aggregates) in n-hexane. Time-resolved fluorescence spectroscopy revealed a coherent size of 89 molecules for the excitonic coupling in the J-aggregates of 3a,b, indicating that the AIEE of 3b,c is mainly caused by a superradiance effect.126 The application of J-aggregates of 3a in polarized fluorescent materials was further explored by alignment of the J-aggregates with the rubbing method, and polarized photoluminescence with a dichroic ratio of 14.3 was obtained.

Aqueous self-assembly has been demonstrated to be an effective strategy for the formation of nanoaggregates of amphiphilic molecules, where the hydrophobic effect has been considered as the dominant driving force.1517 Accordingly, amphiphilic BODIPY dyes bearing various hydrophobic and hydrophilic segments have been successfully synthesized and used for aggregation studies. For examples, Kuang et al. synthesized a BODIPY amphiphile 4 bearing three oligo(ethylene glycol) chains, and studied its self-assembly properties in a THF/water mixture.111 The amphiphilic BODIPY 4 self-assembled into nanovesicular structures in aqueous medium, accompanied by significant quenching of fluorescence, as confirmed by fluorescence spectroscopic and TEM measurements. In addition, the potential application of self-assembled vesicles in the bioimaging of living cells was studied.

The coordination self-assembly of BODIPY-based organic ligands with metal cations has also been explored.116,127 Recently, Huang and Cook et al. reported two highly emissive platinum(II) supramolecular triangles by a coordination-driven self-assembly of pyridyl-functionalized BODIPY 5.119 The studies with NMR, ESI-MS and 2D DOSY supported the successful construction of the BODIPY metallacycles (Fig. 8a and b). Photophysical studies indicated that the macrocycles exhibited intense fluorescence, which was beneficial for fluorescence imaging applications. Moreover, promising anticancer properties were observed for these supramolecular triangles containing BODIPY-based bridging ligands.

With a larger -conjugated system and longer absorption wavelength than that of BODIPYs, the aza-BODIPY dyes exhibited highly attractive self-assembly properties in recent studies. Recently, Jiao, Hao, and co-workers reported the bisphenanthrene-fused aza-BODIPY dye 6 with extended -conjugation and an absorption maximum at 790 nm.99 The ring-fused dye 6 was prepared by the Suzuki coupling reaction and subsequent oxidative ring fusion reaction. In the mixed THF/H2O solvent, the dye 6 formed H-type aggregates (Fig. 9a) with a morphology of one-dimensional nanowires, as confirmed by the SEM and XRD characterizations. On the other hand, the formation of H-bond-directed J-type of aggregates has been reported for dye 7 bearing alkynyl uracil groups (Fig. 9b).107 The concentration-dependent absorption spectroscopic studies indicated that the formation process of J-aggregates followed the cooperative nucleated mechanism with equilibrium constants of Kn = 2.5 103 M1 and Ke = 5.0 105 M1 respectively for the nucleation and elongation stages.

4. (Aza-)BODIPY-based supramolecular dye aggregates with pathway complexity

The concept of pathway complexity was elucidated by Meijer et al. on the basis of their pioneering work on the self-assembly properties of a dimeric oligo(p-phenylenevinylene) dye.41 While a number of BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes exhibiting a single assembly pathway were reported, the pathway complexity of this class of dyes was observed in several systems and their chemical structures were displayed in Chart 2. In these systems, the formation of kinetic-controlled on-pathway or off-pathway metastable aggregates (Fig. 3b and c) was frequently observed, which could transform to the thermodynamic aggregates under suitable conditions. The control over pathway complexity is crucial for the generation of molecular aggregates with the desired structure and properties, which can be realized through appropriate chemical modifications of the self-assembly building blocks.42,128

For example, Fernndez and co-workers reported a method to control the aggregation pathway of BODIPY dyes by altering the molecular positional isomerism.129 They synthesized two kinds of amphiphilic BODIPY dye 8a and 8b by exchanging the positions (2- or meso-) of the two functional groups (carboxyl and aldehyde). This position variance had little influence on the spectral properties of the two dye monomers. However, the self-assembly behaviour of dyes 8a and 8b in a THF/water solvent exhibited significant differences. While dye 8a exhibited a single assembly pathway for Agg.1 (Fig. 10a), dye 8b first aggregated to form the metastable Agg. A exhibiting comparable spectral characteristics to those of Agg.1, which gradually transformed into the thermodynamic Agg. B. The narrowed and red-shifted absorption band (Fig. 10b) of Agg. B is indicative of typical J-aggregates.

Further mechanistic studies manifested that the formation of both Agg.1 and Agg. B followed a cooperative mechanism while the formation of A followed an isodesmic mechanism. In addition, the average Gibbs energy for Agg. 1, Agg. A and Agg. B was determined respectively as 52.4 kJ mol1, 44.3 kJ mol1 and 49.3 kJ mol1. Furthermore, kinetic studies unveiled that A was an on-pathway species for B, that is, A was converted into B without disassembly into free monomeric species. The emergence of pathway complexity for 10b could be attributed to the relative position (90 or 180) of the carboxyl group and the hydrophilic chains, as indicated by further structural characterization and theoretical calculations.

In another work, four amphiphilic BODIPY dyes 8cf were synthesized to modulate the self-assembly behaviours of the four dyes in aqueous media.130 The two steric-free dyes 8c and 8d and dye 8e all displayed aggregation with single assembly pathways and formed aggregates in a weak exciton coupling fashion. In contrast, two-pathway aggregation was observed for 8f, which first formed a metastable aggregate A with weak exciton coupling, and gradually transformed into J-type aggregate B upon aging for a long time. This could be ascribed to the additional repulsive dipoledipole interactions generated by aldehyde substituents as compared with dye 8e.

In 2021, Ajayaghosh and co-workers reported the synthesis and biphasic self-assembly of BODIPY dye 9 and the regulation of nanomorphology of the aggregates between spherical and 2D lamellar (Fig. 11).131 It was observed that dye 9 assembled into spherical nanoaggregates with a size of 4001200 nm in MCH while 2D supramolecular nanosheets were formed in n-decane. Thermodynamic analysis revealed the enthalpy-driven cooperative assembly mechanism for the aggregation process of 9 in both solvents. More interestingly, distinct emission properties revealed the two types of aggregate. The spherical aggregates exhibited single emission band emission with a maximum at 540 nm whereas the 2D aggregates displayed multiple emission bands at 540, 610, 650, 725 and 790 nm due to long-range cascade energy transfer. Furthermore, reversible conversion between the two types of aggregate was realized by changing the MCH/n-decane composition of the solvent, and thus the modulation of two emission states of aggregates was achieved.

5. Living supramolecular polymerization of (aza-)BODIPY dyes

For molecular assembly systems with pathway complexity, the realization of living supramolecular polymerization (LSP)4755 has been demonstrated to be feasible, which provides control over aggregate structural factors such as length, polydispersity, and molecular sequence. In the established protocol for LSP, the metastable aggregate in a kinetically trapped and inactive state can be used as the starting material while the seeds of the thermodynamic aggregate are added as initiators for the polymerization. Thus, it is crucial to design and synthesize functional monomers that exhibit biphasic self-assembly properties. To this end, the molecular design with variable intramolecular and intermolecular H-bonding patterns has been well-elucidated and applied successfully for a number of LSP systems, including functional dye-based systems,133139e.g. perylene bisimide and corannulene. Likewise, such principles of molecular design were also employed in several LSP systems based on BODIPYs and aza-BODIPYs 1113 (Chart 3).

In order to explore the LSP of aza-BODIPYs in aqueous media, we synthesized the amphiphilic aza-BODIPY dye 13 conveniently by a click reaction.143 Due to its large-sized hydrophilic wedges, the dye 13 is soluble in pure water as well as MeOH. Interestingly, the biphasic self-assembly pathways of 13 can be accessed by using a mixture of water and MeOH as solvent. The thermodynamic and kinetic properties of the aggregation process of 13 were investigated in MeOH/H2O (85:15, v/v), indicating a much higher elongation enthalpy change of Agg. Moreover, dye 13 displayed the expected LSP capability in MeOH/H2O (85:15, v/v), indicating that the utilization of water as an assisted co-solvent to establish the kinetic trapping of 13 could be an effective strategy for the realization of LSP in aqueous media.

6. Pathway-dependent functionalities of (aza-)BODIPY-based dye aggregates

In recent years, remarkable efforts have been devoted to the development of functionalities of BODIPY derivatives.6568,144148 The molecular BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes have been investigated in a diverse range of applications, such as fluorescent probes,149152 ion recognition,153,154 biological imaging,155158 photothermal and photodynamic therapy,159164 organic solar cells,146 and liquid crystalline materials.165 In this part we focus on those applications of (aza-)BODIPY dyes specifically connected with their self-assembly pathways. Such applications could be divided into two categories: one includes the applications of the dyes with a single assembly pathway by utilizing the transformation between the monomeric and aggregation states, and the other includes the applications based on the supramolecular polymorphic transformation of the dye molecules featuring pathway complexity. In the next sections, some representative examples of such applications will be summarized.

6.1 Applications based on the assemblydisassembly of (aza-)BODIPY dye aggregates

For the BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes, the self-assembly of monomers to aggregates and the disassembly of the aggregates to monomers usually cause changes of the absorption and emission properties of the dyes. By utilizing the optical signal changes upon the assembly and disassembly of the BODIPY dyes, a number of applications in biomedical areas have been developed.

For instance, two amphiphilic BODIPY dyes 14 and 15 (Chart 4) bearing ionic hydrophilic groups were synthesized by our group84,166 and their application as turn-on fluorescence imaging probes was developed (Fig. 16). Both of the dyes 14 and 15 are fluorescent and respectively displayed green and orange-coloured emissions. Further studies indicated that these two dyes could co-assemble into nanovesicles with fluorescence-quenching in water containing 0.5% DMSO (v/v).

In another example, by covalently linking a capsaicin moiety to BODIPY, a capsaicinBODIPY (CAP-BDP) dye 16 was obtained and its antitumor activity was studied.167 The BODIPY unit in this dye acted as a hydrophobic moiety to enhance the aggregation tendency in aqueous media. The BODIPY dyes with longer emission wavelengths in NIR are attractive for bioimaging applications. For example, the amphiphilic BODIPY dye 17 bearing hydrophilic poly(ethylene glycol) chains was reported by Xie et al.168 This dye could self-assemble into nano-sized vesicles in aqueous solution with fluorescence quenching. The NIR fluorescence enhancement induced by the dissociation of nanovesicles was observed by in vivo fluorescence imaging studies. Moreover, the nanovesicles were used to encapsulate the antitumor drug decetaexl and exhibited excellent properties of intratumor imaging and tumor inhibition, indicating their potential in imaging-guided drug delivery and cancer therapy.

For the example of aza-BODIPYs, the lipid-conjugated aza-BODIPY 18 was synthesized by Zheng et al. and was applied for the optical imaging of tumors (Fig. 18).169 When co-assembled with phospholipid DPPC, the dye 18 formed highly stable nanovesicular J-aggregates with an absorption maximum in the NIR region (702 nm), a high extinction coefficient (128 mM1 cm1), and high fluorescence quenching. 6.2 Applications based on the supramolecular polymorphic transformation of (aza-)BODIPY dyes

For the BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes with assembly pathway complexity, dimorphic or polymorphic aggregates with distinct structures and properties can be obtained. Moreover, the transformation between different aggregate polymorphs may take place under external stimuli such as heating, light irradiation, mechanical stress, stirring, sonication, etc. Attractive applications based on the supramolecular transformation processes of BODIPYs and aza-BODIPY dyes have been developed.

Ajayaghosh and co-workers reported a series of BODIPY dyes 19ac (Chart 5) and their applications in photonic materials.170,171 For dye 19a, an unprecedented generation and amplification of NIR emission was observed by shearing the film of the self-assembly from n-decane (Fig. 19a). As compared with the aggregates in decane solution, the emission intensity at 738 nm was amplified by 15 times with a change in emission colour from greenish-yellow to orange-red. A similar enhancement in NIR emission was observed for a DMSO gel of 19a (Fig. 19b). These observed pathway-dependent optical properties were elucidated by the spectroscopic and single crystal analysis of a model compound 19b. After shearing, the H-bonding pattern in the molecular packing of the dye molecules was varied and a NHF H-bond-directed, head-to-tail slipped molecular arrangement was formed (Fig. 19c), which was beneficial for strong excitonic coupling.

7. Summary

In the last two decades, BODIPY and aza-BODIPY dyes have received extensive attention as versatile building blocks for functional supramolecular aggregates. A large number of self-assembling (aza-)BODIPY dyes were designed and synthesized and their aggregation properties were investigated in polar and nonpolar organic solvents (MCH, n-hexane, MeOH, THF, DMSO etc.), aqueous solutions, biological environments, and condensed states. Accordingly, dye aggregates or co-aggregates with various morphologies including nanoparticles, vesicles, nanofibers, gels, and 2D nanosheets with attractive optical properties with a wavelength from visible to NIR were obtained.

Moreover, the mechanistic aspects of the self-assembly process of this class of dyes have been studied in detail. While a large number of examples of single-pathway aggregation were reported for (aza-)BODIPY dyes with different types of supramolecular interaction, a few dye molecules exhibited more complicated kinetic-controlled self-assembly behaviour that leads to the formation of metastable and thermodynamic aggregates with structural and property diversity. Such pathway complexity has been further analysed by applying the numerical models for single or coupled aggregation processes and the results indicate that the assembly of (aza-)BODIPYs may follow an isodesmic, cooperative or anti-cooperative mechanism. In general, the (aza-)BODIPYs dyes give aggregation constants K ranging from 103 to 107 M1, and corresponding Gibbs free energy changes from 20 to 60 kJ mol1. Based on these studies, seeded living supramolecular polymerization has been realized for this class of dyes by introducing interconvertible intramolecular and intermolecular H-bonding interactions into the molecules, which offered more precise control of the aggregates structures as compared with the spontaneous assembly process. It is necessary to mention that all the reported LSPs for (aza-)BODIPYs give 1D fibril type nanoaggregates and the realization of 2D LSP44 and supramolecular block copolymerization53 for this class of dyes remains a challenging target.

Besides the structure and properties of the (aza-)BODIPY dye aggregates, functionalities depending on their self-assembly processes have been demonstrated for advanced biomedical as well as photonic materials, indicating the promising potential of the (aza-)BODIPY assemblies in applications. For future research, the development of BODIPY (or analogue) aggregates with tuneable optical properties in the NIR II region (10001700 nm)173 could be attractive for biomedical applications, such as imaging, photothermal and photodynamic therapy, and drug delivery.174 The J-aggregates of (aza-)BODIPYs with outstanding excitonic properties may applied in nanophotonic devices. 2D aggregates of this class of dyes with charge percolation properties could be promising for applications in thin-film devices,1 such as organic field effect transistors or solar cells. On the other hand, the relationship between the dye molecular structure and the pathway complexity needs to be further elucidated, which is crucial for the development of the pathway-dependent applications. It is also worth noting that the supramolecular chirality175 of (aza-)BODIPY aggregates remains to be less explored in comparison with the large number of reported assemblies of this class of dyes, which may inspire the advance of circular polarized luminescence and other chirality-relevant applications as these dyes are known to be excellent fluorophores.
