Abstract

Since the mid 1970's interest in the chemistry and applications of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes has increased steadily. In this perspective, the development of this area is tracked and discussed taking into account new scientific developments as well as novel applications. The interaction between basic and applied research is of particular importance and selected examples are highlighted.

Introduction

Over the last 2030 years ruthenium polypyridyl complexes have been investigated in perhaps greater detail than any other class of luminescent metal complexes. Many thousands of compounds have been prepared and a very extensive literature has evolved dealing with synthesis, photophysics, electrochemistry, solar cells, biosensors, modified electrodes, displays, etc. The enormous popularity of this class of compounds, and also of the related osmium, rhodium and iridium compounds has been driven by the wide range of applications that such compounds have seen and, potentially, will see in the future. Many of these applications are related to the well defined electrochemical and photophysical properties of the compounds. In addition, the extensive synthetic chemistry available allows for systematic manipulation of their physical properties while substituents, which allow immobilisation on a variety of surfaces, can be introduced. Investigation into the chemistry of ruthenium began in earnest in the late 1950s and 60s with the publication of the dinuclear complex [(NH3)5Ru2pz]2+ (pz is pyrazine) by Creutz and Taube1 and the first report of luminescence of the paradigm complex [Ru(bpy)3]2+ (bpy is 2,2-bipyridine) by Paris and Brandt.2 However, the investigation of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes really exploded in the mid to late 1970's with the realisation that the combination of excited state and electrochemical properties of the compound [Ru(bpy)3]2+ should enable it to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. The fact that these observations were made in the middle of an energy crisis was further instrumental in generating such interest. Although it became clear that the initial expectations of photochemical water splitting were not easily realised, the almost ideal combination of physical properties of the compounds was sufficiently promising to encourage the search for potential applications in other areas. In this perspective a personal overview of many different aspects of ruthenium polypyridyl chemistry will be given. The aim of the overview is to outline the research lines that have evolved over the last number of years and show how basic research and application driven investigations have combined in producing a dynamic area of research. The simultaneous emergence of instrumentation, especially in the area of photophysics where femto- and pico-second timescales can now be probed routinely, has supported this expansion. These developments have not only given the scientific community additional instrumentation, but have, more importantly, extended the timescale of events to the edge of what can be called chemistrythe impulsive limit.3 In recent years the focus may have switched somewhat from ruthenium to other metals such as Re, Rh, Os etc. but the lessons learned over the last 3040 years with ruthenium complexes are still very relevant for today's research communitya community forever searching for new properties and materials. This perspective will be based, to a large extent, on work carried out in our laboratories over the last 25 years combined with results obtained by other groups. Issues addressed will be: synthetic chemistry, polymer films and monolayers, supramolecular chemistry, photophysics and electrochemistry of dinuclear compounds, DNA interactions, molecular electronics and applications such as solar cells, sensors and displays which have arisen from these fundamental studies.

Synthetic aspects

One of the reasons that ruthenium polypyridyl chemistry has expanded so enormously is that compounds of this type have an extensive synthetic chemistry. No other metallic compounds, with the possible exception of ferrocene, show similar stability and flexibility. As a second row transition metal, the ligand field is considerably larger than that observed for first row metals. This has the result that higher temperatures are needed to exchange ligands, but overall the compounds obtained are very stable. In addition ligands can be exchanged in a systematic manner, removing some while maintaining the presence of others often with retention of stereo-configuration.4 Melting points are invariably high and the compounds are often stable in solvents such as concentrated sulfuric acid. Importantly they are also stable under conditions normally encountered in synthetic organic chemistry; this, combined with the fact that from a synthetic point of view substituents on coordinated polypyridyl ligands do not behave very differently from their non-coordinated analogues, results in a rich inorganic organic chemistry. The ability to utilise this extensive synthetic expertise has enabled researchers to prepare intricate molecular assemblies, something that is impossible with a first row transition metal such as iron. This ability to synthesize designer compounds has been central to the development of the area.

[Ru(bpy)2Cl2]2H2O and its analogues are well-known precursors and many thousands of complexes have been prepared5 including optically pure compounds.6 The preparation of tris heteroleptic compounds is substantially more challenging but there are now a number of methods available that have opened the path for further extension of multinuclear systems of even greater complexity7,8 Some approaches reported are shown in Fig. 1.

With the increasing complexity of the compounds prepared, the need for purification and characterisation techniques capable of producing and identifying high purity molecular materials has become increasingly important. UV/VIS and emission spectroscopy are in general a good indication for the type of coordination environment of the ruthenium centre, but these techniques are somewhat less useful for the corresponding osmium complexes. Mass spectrometry, especially through the use of electrospray ionisation (ESI-MS) and cold spray ionisation (CSI-MS), is of particular help for the study of multinuclear and labile coordination compounds and for probing non-covalently bound species in solution or in biomolecules.18ac The most detailed information is normally obtained from NMR spectroscopy. For low spin Ru(II) high quality spectra can be obtained. With increasing complexity of the compounds the number of signals will, however, increase and more and more resonances will overlap. One solution is the introduction of deuteriated ligands. With a partial deuteriation of the ligands cleaner spectra can be obtained, which give more information about the more detailed aspects of the NMR spectroscopic data. Importantly, deuteriation can also be used to determine the nature of the excited state in mixed ligand compounds. Results obtained show that to a first approximation, deuteration of ligand A in the hypothetical complex [M(A)2(B)] will lead to an increase in the emission lifetime when the emitting state is based on that ligand. Otherwise the emission lifetime will be unchanged. This behaviour is observed for many mixed ligand compounds.19 It needs to be considered, however, that coupling of excited states (i.e. fast internal conversion between low lying excited states) may take place, in which case the results obtained may be less clear. An additional role for deuteriation of ligands is encountered in resonance Raman (rR) spectroscopy.19,20 When used in conjunction with excited state rR, deuteriation becomes an important tool in determining the nature of the emitting state. Since the vibrational features observed in excited state rR are related to the ligand where the excited state is located, a frequency shift in certain vibrational features upon deuteriation can provide direct evidence that the excited state under investigation is localised on the deuteriated ligand. In certain cases 99Ru NMR spectroscopy can be useful, especially in the identification of coordination isomers.21

Ultimately semi-preparative HPLC is often the only way to achieve ultrapure compounds. The samples are first investigated using analytical HPLC and after a method is developed semipreparative separation can be obtained. For the average ruthenium complex cation exchange chromatography is most helpful,22 although alternative enantioselective separations using column chromatography and other HPLC columns have been reported also.23

Solar energy applications

In the mid 1970's a paper appeared reporting that a surfactant derivative of [Ru(bpy)3]2+ when immobilised on a glass slide was able to split water using sunlight. Although, it was recognised soon afterwards that highly purified samples did not show this effect,24 the application of this type of ruthenium complex to the photoinduced splitting of water became a very active area of research. In particular, since it was realised that thermodynamically such a process should be possible. During the 1980's thousands of new compounds were reported.5 The aim of these studies was to improve the photostability and/or the photoinduced energy/electron transfer properties of the compounds. Extensive studies have been carried out investigating the potential use of catalytic cycles as outlined in Fig. 11.25

There are, however, several problems with such approaches, in particular the fast back reaction between the oxidised ruthenium complex and the reduced methylviologen (MV+). Efficiencies were, therefore, low and much effort was paid to the study of half catalytic cycles using sacrificial agents. Many efforts were made to reduce the effect of back reactions. Investigations in heterogeneous systems based on micelles, bilayers, ion-exchange resins, colloids, polymers, Nafion films and polyelectrolytes were carried out.26 None of these did, however, lead to stable, highly efficient systems. The real breakthrough came in the early 1990s with the work of Grtzel and co-workers who developed photovoltaic cells based on nanocrystalline TiO2 surfaces modified with monolayers of ruthenium complexes27 (see Fig. 12).


Fig. 12 Schematic of dye sensitised solar cell including an immobilised ruthenium complex and the iodine/iodide redox couple needed for regeneration of the oxidised dye.

With this arrangement efficient conversion of solar energy to electricity was demonstrated without the need for sacrificial donors. The efficiency of this device is based on two important components. Firstly, the use of nanocrystalline films with roughness factors of about 1000, leading to high surface coverages and therefore high absorption of incident light. Secondly, the very fast electron injection from the dye into the TiO2 (See Fig. 13) coupled to a back reaction that is many orders of magnitude slower. This very fast electron injection has a surprising consequence.

Thus the compound shown in Fig. 13, which contains two monodentate ligands, is known upon irradiation in solution, to undergo fast ligand exchange, in acetonitrile, the solvent used in the solar cell. This is, however, not observed for the immobilised dye and cells have been shown to be stable for a very long time. This suggests that injection is likely to take place very rapidly from a state close to the FranckCondon state, e.g. an 1MLCT or vibrationally hot 3MLCT state and not from the equilibrated 3MLCT (THEXI) state, which is known to lead to subsequent population of the 3MC state resulting in photodecomposition.

It is generally assumed that a direct chemical bond between the semiconductor surface and the molecular component is required, mostly a carboxy or phosphonate grouping. Importantly, however, it has been shown that for a dinuclear ruthenium/osmium compound injection from the osmium centre which is not attached directly, can take place via through space electron transfer.28 The structure of this compound is shown in Fig. 14.


Fig. 14 Structure of a dinuclear ruthenium/osmium compound (dcbpy is 4,4-dicarboxy-2,2-bipyridine).

Investigation of this compound in solution shows as expected that the emission is osmium based and energy transfer occurs from the ruthenium to the osmium centre. When attached to nanocrystalline TiO2, no emission is observed. Assuming that electron transfer is only efficient when the centre excited is covalently bound to the nanocrystalline surface and that the energy transfer cascade is away from the surface this was, initially, surprising. The osmium centre does not seem to have a through bond deactivation pathway. It seems likely, however, that electron transfer does occur but through space to a neighbouring nanoparticle. Many detailed studies on the behavior of TiO2 and other types of nanocrystalline surfaces modified with ruthenium polypyridyl complexes have been reported.29 In another example a ruthenium complex covalently bound to a methylviologen moiety was immobilised via the ruthenium centre to a nanocrystalline electrode. It was shown that the long lived photoinduced charge separation can be modulated by the potential applied to the electrode.30 Efficient electrochromic devices have also been reported.31

One type of material developed at that time was that of ruthenium containing polymers. Initially these materials were developed for their potential photophysical properties26,32 but also their electrochemical behaviour proved to be of great interest and their investigation together with that of the analogous osmium polymers led to the development of electrochemical and biosensors. This well-defined behaviour coupled with excellent stability of the redox centres allowed for detailed study of the charge transport and determination of the importance of parameters such as electron hopping, polymer movement and ion transport for the overall charge transport process. The fact that ion transport is closely related to the metal based redox process is illustrated in the mass increase (M) of the layer as identified by an electrochemical quartz crystal microbalance (EQCM).36 Electrochemical studies on the effect of temperature, electrolyte concentration, crosslinking,37 layer thickness etc. have been carried out and a detailed understanding has been obtained of the charge transport properties of thin layers of these materials. These studies have shown that the rate determining step of the charge transport process can vary from polymer movement to electron hopping and ion transport depending on the nature of the electrolyte and the distance between the metal centres.38 The latter can be changed by synthetic means (see Fig. 15). In the present example one out of ten pyridine units of the polymer is bound to a metal centre. Important for such studies is the exact nature of the polymer film when in contact with electrolyte. This type of information can be obtained using in situ neutron reflectivity measurements, which can provide direct information about issues such as film swelling as a function of the nature and the concentration of the electrolyte.39

This detailed fundamental electrochemical information together with the stability of the layers has led to the application of electrodes modified with such polymers as electrochemical sensors. The osmium polymers have been studied widely as electron relays in biosensors by Heller and co-workers.40 Enzymes are incorporated into the polymer layer and in a series of elegant studies the properties of these layers for the detection of glucose were investigated. Electrochemical sensors have been developed for analytes such as nitrite,41 nitrate42 and ascorbic acid.43 In the development of these sensors the ability to manipulate the redox potential of the metal centres has been shown to be an important variable and as a result analytical problems can be solved by tuning the redox potentials of polymer and analyte. From these investigations it appears that the electrochemical properties of these polymer layers are in broad terms controlled by two components. The redox potential is controlled by the nature of the metal centre, but the polymer properties control the kinetics and thermodynamics of charge transport through the layer. For example, ion transport is greatly dependent on the amount of swelling of the layer and of the interaction between anion and polymer backbone. This has the result that the fundamental properties of a particular polymer can not be determined. Parameters, such as charge transport rates, activation energies and catalytic behaviour will always depend of the interaction between the polymer layer and the contacting electrolyte, therefore, the results obtained are specific to the measurement conditions.45

The stability of the metal centres involved in these polymer studies, together with the fact that their redox potentials can be changed systematically has also lead to a detailed investigation of their application as self-assembled monolayers. Redox centres of this type modified with pyridine or thiol groupings have been immobilized on gold and silver electrodes and detailed studies of their electrochemical properties have been carried out.45,46

Intercomponent interaction in dinuclear metal complexes; molecular devices

At the beginning of the 1980's the synthetic effort in ruthenium polypyridyl chemistry was directed towards the preparation of mononuclear complexes since at that stage the main aim of the work carried out was to produce dyes for solar applications. By 1990,5,47 less than 100 multinuclear compounds had been reported which showed luminescent properties. However, study of multinuclear compounds became more central, driven by the search for molecular devices and molecular machines.48,49 In this context a molecular machine or motor is defined as a molecular assembly capable of changing its position as a result of an external impulse. Extensive research in this field has been carried out using rotaxanes and catenanes.50 A light driven organic molecular rotor has also been reported.51 In the area of ruthenium chemistry Sauvage and co-workers have utilised the photochemical lability of ligands in ruthenium polypyridyl complexes as the starting point for the design of molecular motors where light driven and thermal movements can be instigated.52

The development of molecular devices was encouraged by the emergence of supramolecular chemistry and by the increased knowledge that became available in the late 1980's concerning the natural photosynthetic centre in bacteria and plants. Suddenly the multi-step energy transfer and charge separation processes observed in nature were better understood and the desire to prepare artificial assemblies and model systems to study these processes took central stage. A good example of this approach is shown in Fig. 6 where a comparison is made between the components in both natural and artificial photosynthetic assemblies.13 As a result of this general interest more than 400 multinuclear compounds containing polypyridyl were listed by 19965a and many more have been reported since.53 The reader is directed to these references for a detailed overview of this area.

The definition generally used to describe a supramolecular arrangement in this field of research is that in such an assembly the individual centres should retain many of their properties when incorporated, but that the assembly as a unit has new properties over and above those observed for the individual components. Examples of such additional processes are energy or electron transfer. The parameters generally used to modulate the intercomponent processes such as energy and electron transfer in multinuclear complexes are the control of the distance between the metal centres and the nature of the bridge. In the following example the importance of these two factors is illustrated. Firstly, the effect of the nature of the bridge is varied while the distance between the metal centres remains the same. Optical sensing

The long-lived excited state lifetimes of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes have been instrumental for their development as luminescence sensors, in particular for the detection of O2.59 The commercial application of this type of sensor, where oxygen concentration is directly related to the emission intensity or lifetime is well underway. The most common approach taken in these studies is the immobilisation of the complexes in a solid matrix. Several environments have been used including polymers, Nafion and Vicor glass. Excellent results have been obtained by the immobilisation of ruthenium complexes in solgel type layers.60 In this approach thin solgel coatings are attached to waveguides or optical fibres and ruthenium complexes are immobilised either by electrostatic means or by covalent bonds. This method of coating is cheap and very flexible. For example, parameters such as thickness and porosity of the layer can be varied by changing the coating parameters. Ruthenium complexes such as [Ru(bpy)3]Cl2 and [Ru(dpp)3]Cl2, dpp = 4,4-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline, have been incorporated into solgel or polymeric matrices and their oxygen sensing capabilities have been investigated. It is important to realise that the photophysical properties of the compounds may be affected by the matrix itself.61 CO262 and pH63 sensors have been developed also by taking a similar approach.

The emission properties of ruthenium complexes have also been employed in anion sensing,64 with typically polypyridyl ligands being modified with anion recognition groupings such as that shown in Fig. 22.

Interactions with nucleic acids and other biomolecules

For more than 20 years there has been great interest in the study of the interaction of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes with nucleic acids.69 While much of the initial interest was stimulated by the Barton group's use of the enantiomers of such complexes as possible chiral probes for DNA,70 it was quickly recognised that, as the photophysical and electrochemical properties of the complex can be systematically varied by appropriate ligand substitution, they also have great potential as luminescent probes and as photochemical reagents.

Changing the ligands of the complexes also provides a means to vary the nature and strength of binding to nucleic acids. Thus while all cationic complexes are expected to be attracted to the negative charged DNA, it can also be imagined that those complexes with extended heteroaromatic ligands might insert that ligand between the base-pairs of B-form double-stranded DNA (that is bind via intercalation.) In agreement with this hypothesis, it was observed that while [Ru(bpy)3]2+ binds relatively weakly to DNA (mainly through electrostatic interaction in one of the grooves), the interaction of [Ru(phen)3]2+ is much stronger.71 The partial intercalative nature of this binding was initially demonstrated using an electrophoretic/topoisomerase method to monitor the unwinding of the helix and the binding of this complex has since subsequently been studied in great detail using hydrodynamic72 and dichroism methods.73

One of the first complexes which was shown to bind significantly more strongly to DNA was [Ru(bpy)2(HAT)]2+, where HAT is 1,4,5,8,9,12-hexaazatriphenylene (for structures see Fig. 25).74 This complex also showed a significantly different absorption spectrum and markedly enhanced emission upon DNA binding-features which are presumably a consequence of the changed environment experienced because of the intercalation of the HAT ligand between the base-pairs of DNA.


Fig. 25 Some heteroaromatic ligands used in DNA intercalating ruthenium complexes.

However the most dramatic effect of increasing the size of one of the heteroaromatic ligands is found for complexes such as [Ru(bpy)2(dppz)]2+, initially synthesised by Sauvage and co-workers75 and examined with DNA by Barton and co-workers.76 As the complex is non-luminescent in water but emits strongly in non-polar solvents or when intercalated into DNA, its use as a light-switching DNA probe has been proposed. The complex photophysical behaviour of such dppz complexes both in aqueous solution and when bound to DNA has been examined in a number of detailed fast reaction investigations, using luminescence measurements, visible transient absorption methods and vibrational (transient resonance Raman and CARS) techniques.77 These have emphasised the role of water in determining the relative stability of the dark and light states and have revealed the dominant control of entropy when the complex is in an aqueous environment.

The analogous dipyridoquinoxaline (dpq) complexes, such as [Ru(bpy)2(dpq)]2+, do not show DNA light-switching behaviour, as the complexes in water luminesce quite strongly.78 Remarkably, amide substitution of the dpq to give [Ru(bpy)2(dpqa)]2+, produces a complex which is quite non-emissive in water. However it does luminesce in organic solvents or when bound to DNA, with a quantum yield, which is significantly greater than that of [Ru(bpy)2(dppz)]2+.79 Probing the amide carbonyl-stretch region using picosecond infra-red measurements has demonstrated that in water the initially formed MLCT state is rapidly deactivated (ca, 7 ps) to a longer-lived excited state (650 ps), which exhibits a strikingly different IR spectrum in the 15001700 cm1 region, suspected to be due to an intramolecular charge transfer state.80

A particularly dramatic effect of substitution on the photophysical and photochemical properties of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes is observed when phenanthroline is replaced by 1,4,5,8-tetraazaphenanthrene (TAP) or HAT. Initial experiments demonstrated that [Ru(TAP)3]2+ was much more efficient at causing single strand nicking of DNA than either [Ru(bpy)3]2+ or [Ru(phen)3]2+.81 Subsequently it was shown that this complex formed covalent photo-adducts with DNA.82 Detailed studies then revealed that such behaviour depends crucially on the oxidising power of the excited state and that such behaviour is found for complexes such as [Ru(bpy)L2]2+ or [RuL3]2+ (L = TAP, HAT) but not for the mono-substituted complex [Ru(bpy)2L]2+.83 Luminescence lifetime and laser flash photolysis experiments confirmed that these excited states could oxidise guanine either as the free nucleotide (5-GMP) or in DNA, but not (except for the most oxidising complexes) the other nucleobases. The formation of adducts was attributed to the reaction of the radical products formed by initial electron transfer and subsequent proton transfer.

The product of the photoreaction of [Ru(TAP)3]2+ with 5-GMP was isolated and identified.84a Subsequently the photoproduct formed on DNA by [Ru(TAP)2(bpy)]2+ was enzymatically excised from DNA (see Fig. 26).84b It was shown that the adduct was of an unusual type, the bond between the metal complex and the DNA being directly between the TAP ligand and the exocyclic NH2-group of the guanine. More recently the Kirsch-De Mesmaeker group has demonstrated that in the photoadduct formed between [Ru(HAT)2(phen)]2+ and 5-GMP a bond is formed between the ligand and the 6-O atom of the guanine.85

The details of the photooxidation of guanine continue to be probed using complexes, such as [Ru(TAP)2(dppz)]2+ which combine oxidising power with strong intercalation into DNA.86 Both the forward and back reaction of this complex show a significant isotope effect when the reaction is carried out in D2O, a fact that has been attributed to the reaction proceeding by proton-coupled electron transfer.

Various approaches have been taken to further enhance the binding of metal complexes to DNA. This includes the use of multinuclear complexes.87 A particularly interesting case is that of ,-[-(11,11-bidppz)(phen)4Ru2]4+, which binds very strongly to double-stranded DNA, but with remarkably slow kinetics.88 Stereoselective interactions between [{Ru(phen)2}2(-HAT)]4+ and nucleotides (5-GMP, 5-AMP) or denatured DNA have been observed, with the meso-isomer interacting much more strongly than the ,- or ,-enantiomers.89 A further interesting example of preferential binding is that exhibited by [{Ru(Me2bpy)2}2(-2,2-bipyrimidine)]4+, which was examined in the presence of the deoxyoligonucleotide d(CCGAGAATTCCGG)2.90 It was observed that the ,-enantiomer bound to the bulge, which is formed in the non-complementary regions of the double-stranded DNA, whereas its ,-isomer did not.

The targeting of specific sequences on DNA is however probably best achieved by linking the metal complex to an oligonucleotide having a sequence complementary to that of the target. This has been used to design a range of sequence-specific luminescence probes.91 A similar approach can be used to photochemically target particular sequences. For example with one characteristic of chronic myeloid leukaemia, it has been shown that visible-light excitation causes oxidative damage only at the guanine G21 located in close proximity to the photosensitising [Ru(phen)3]2+-centre (Fig. 27).92 Linking of [Ru(TAP)2(4,7-Ph2phen)]2+-type centres to oligonucleotides has been exploited to study the distance dependence of the photo-induced electron transfer from guanine to the excited state of the metal complex.93 The formation of photoadducts leads to cross-linking of the two strands and a resultant blocking of exonuclease activity.

It may be seen therefore that the rich chemical and structural diversity of ruthenium polypyridyl complexes has provided the biological chemist with a range of chemical, luminescent and photochemical probes to study DNA and potentially to open up new routes to therapeutic agents.

Molecular electronics

Recently we reported on the application of osmium polypyridyl complexes as potential molecular transistors. Other similar papers have also appeared.94 Of interest is to note that this new development arose from our long standing interest in polymer modified electrodes. This is illustrated in Fig. 28, which shows that the redox active metal center employed is the same as in the earlier polymers. The present work furthermore builds on the extensive work carried out on self assembled monolayers.45,46 The most striking observation obtained in these studies is that self-assembled monolayers of these molecules show transistor like behaviour in situ and at room temperature. The tunneling currents observed are in agreement with theoretical studies and are controlled by the redox properties of the metal center, which allows for systematic variations. Concluding remarks

Although [Ru(bpy)3]2+ was first prepared in the 1930's, research on ruthenium polypyridyl chemistry really got underway following the initial promise of its application to the conversion and storage of solar energy. Many hundreds of compounds were prepared with the aim of enhancing the photophysical and electrochemical properties and at the same time improving the photochemical stability of the compounds. The successful development of the Grtzel cell owes much to this work, although it is somewhat ironic that the compounds now routinely used in this device are actually photolabile in solution. However, the synthetic experience gained has proved invaluable for the design and preparation of complexes for a wide variety of applications including the active components for biosensors, molecular motors, optical displays and molecular electronics. In the last two rather new areas considerable growth may be expected in the near future, as initial results68 are very promising. It may be expected that ruthenium polypyridyl complexes will play pivotal roles in these and other areas of nanotechnology. For the next generation of electronic devices the design of real molecular wiresconnectors that will facilitate interaction between for example donor and acceptor moietiesare needed. At present many rod-like systems have been labelled as molecular wires, however most show a rapid decrease in energy upon electron transfer and there is therefore place for further synthetic efforts to achieve long-range conductivity. Of interest in this area may also be the recent observations made that carbon nanotubes can be coated efficiently with the redox polymers discussed above.95 This observation offers the possibility of the development of an alternative type of molecular wire in the future.

Significant developments are also expected in the application of ruthenium complexes as biological probes and possibly even as the active constituent of phototherapeutic procedures. Equally it is likely, especially given the belated realisation amongst politicians that the world needs to consider alternatives to carbon-based fuels, that we may see a rekindling of interest in artificial photosynthesis and solar energy conversion. Another possible development is of catalytic systems for the photochemical or electrochemical reduction of CO2. It would certainly not be a surprise to see polypyridyl complexes involved in these processes. Indeed it may be possible, and would be very gratifying, to see the promise of the studies carried out over almost 30 years lead to established technology.
