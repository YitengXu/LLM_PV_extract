Abstract

Introducing suitable interfacial chemical bonds into heterojunctions can increase the charge carrier density, propel the charge separation, and facilitate interfacial charge extraction in photoanodes for photoelectrochemical (PEC) water oxidation. However, tuning chemical bonds at heterojunction interfaces and elucidating their influences on band alignment and the associated evolution of PEC performance remain elusive. Herein, Biâ€“S bonds were introduced into the interface of a CdIn2S4 (CIS)/Bi2WO6 (BWO) heterojunction. In situ irradiated X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy and electron spin resonance signals confirm that the Biâ€“S bond transforms the band alignment from type II to the direct Z-scheme, significantly enhancing the carrier separation efficiency. Theoretical calculations show that the Biâ€“S bond not only acts as an atomic-level charge transfer channel, but also changes the migration pathway and distance within the heterojunction. As a result, the optimized CIS/BWO photoanode exhibits a relatively high PEC performance of 4.25 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE (VRHE) and a low onset potential of 0.30 VRHE. This work presents a new avenue to construct comprehensively improved photoanodes by tuning the interfacial structures at the atomic level.

Introduction

In order to alleviate the current energy crisis and environmental pollution, an attractive approach utilizes PEC water splitting to convert the intermittent solar energy to easily accessible hydrogen directly.1â€“3 Photoanodes with sluggish four-electron reactions are the bottlenecks of PEC water splitting.4 Ternary metal sulfides, such as CIS,5 ZnIn2S4,6,7 and ZnxCd1âˆ’xS,8 are considered as promising photoanode materials due to their advantages of tunable band gap and excellent visible light absorption. Specifically, CIS, with tunable band gap (2.2â€“2.5 eV), suitable band position, and diverse and adjustable chemical structures, has been considered as a promising candidate for solar water oxidation, whereas the severe bulk carrier recombination significantly hinders the photocatalytic properties of a single CIS in the PEC field.9

Constructing heterojunctions with suitable band alignment is an effective strategy to promote the bulk carrier separation of photoanodes for PEC water splitting.10â€“12 Recently, constructing Z-scheme heterojunctions, which can separate photo-generated carriers spatially and preserve the redox potential of materials, has emerged as an effective strategy for obtaining highly efficient photoanodes.13,14 The prerequisite for constructing Z-scheme heterojunctions is band structure matching, in which the conduction band (CB) of one semiconductor should be close to the valence band (VB) of the other semiconductor.15â€“17 According to previous reports, the CB of BWO (+0.62 eV) is much lower than that of CIS (âˆ’0.58 eV),18,19 but it is very close to the VB of CIS (+1.51 eV), suggesting that the photo-generated electrons are more likely transferred to the VB of CIS and recombine with photo-generated holes on the VB of CIS, which forms a Z-scheme carrier transfer pathway. However, the interfacial connection between CIS and BWO is generally poor, which prevents the transfer of charge flow. In addition, the gradient band alignment enables the spontaneous formation of a type II heterojunction between CIS and BWO, which makes the energy band of CIS bend downward and the energy band of BWO bend upward. Nevertheless, the construction of type II heterojunctions fixes the charge carrier transfer direction and sacrifices the redox potential of components.20 Therefore, the unfavorable interfacial charge transfer and band bending become obstacles for constructing Z-scheme heterojunctions. Recently, the construction of heterostructures induced by interfacial chemical bonds has motivated the thought of assembling heterostructures with specific atomic-level interfacial contacts.21 Chemical bonds act as atomic-level channels to accelerate the charge transfer between semiconductors.22 Furthermore, the internal electric field within heterojunctions has been considered as an effective strategy to promote the construction of Z-scheme heterojunctions as well. Under the influence of an internal electric field, the photo-generated electrons on the conduction band of semiconductors with a relatively lower Fermi level (EF) can transfer to the valence band of semiconductors with higher EF values, thus modulating the band bending and realizing the Z-scheme charge transfer.23 Furthermore, the existence of interfacial chemical bonds may change the work function of materials forming heterojunctions, which in turn affects the EF and internal electric field of heterojunctions.24 Therefore, an efficient Z-scheme heterojunction can be obtained by reasonably modulating the interfacial chemical bond within the heterojunction. On the one hand, it can promote carrier transport, and on the other hand, it can adjust the internal electric field and band bending to promote the construction of Z-scheme heterojunctions for PEC water oxidation.

Herein, we successfully prepared a composite CIS/BWO heterojunction with a rationally designed Biâ€“S interfacial chemical bond at the interface. The interfacial Biâ€“S bond converts the type II heterojunction into a direct Z-scheme heterojunction, which effectively improves the carrier separation efficiency of the photoanode. Theoretical calculations reveal that the Biâ€“S bonds act as atomic-scale charge-flow highways to promote the interfacial charge transfer. The work function calculations illustrate that an internal electric field is formed when CIS with a high EF and BWO with a low EF are in contact, which results in a Z-scheme charge carrier transfer pathway. As a result, the optimum CIS/BWO photoanode exhibits the highest photocurrent density of 4.25 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE and a low-onset potential of 0.30 VRHE. This work provides a reference for studying the role of chemical bonds inside heterojunction photoanodes.

Materials

Cadmium nitrate hydrate (Cd(NO3)2Â·4H2O), indium nitrate hydrate (In(NO3)3Â·xH2O), and sodium tungstate (Na2WO4) were purchased from Shanghai Aladdin Bio-Chem Technology Co., Ltd. Synthesis of the CIS photoanode

The CIS photoanode was prepared using a facile hydrothermal method. The precursor solution was prepared by dissolving 0.01 M Cd(NO3)2Â·4H2O, 0.02 M In(NO3)3Â·xH2O, and 0.04 M thiourea in 50 ml of deionized (DI) water. A piece of clean fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass was placed into a 25 ml Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave with the conducting side facing down. Then, 10 ml of precursor solution was slowly injected into the autoclave. The hydrothermal reaction temperature and time were set as 200 Â°C and 4 h, respectively. The resulting sample was rinsed with DI water and absolute ethanol more than 3 times and dried under vacuum at 50 Â°C for 12 h.

Synthesis of the CIS/BWO photoanode

The CIS/BWO photoanode was prepared using a facile solvothermal method. 1.9402 g of Bi(NO3)3Â·5H2O was dissolved in 80 ml of ethylene glycol to form solution A. Solution B was prepared by dissolving 0.6594 g of Na2WO4 and 0.1000 g of hexadecyl trimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) in 40 ml of DI water and 40 ml of ethylene glycol. Then, solutions A and B were mixed and sonicated for 20 min to obtain precursor solution C. The CIS photoanode was placed in a 25 ml Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave with the working side facing down. Then, 10 ml of precursor solution C was slowly injected into the autoclave. The solvothermal reaction temperature and time were controlled to regulate the amount of BWO. The composite photoanode was washed with DI water more than three times and dried in air at 80 Â°C for 2 h.

Synthesis of the CISâ€“BWO photoanode

BWO was prepared using a facile solvothermal method as mentioned above. 1.9402 g of Bi(NO3)3Â·5H2O was dissolved in 80 ml of ethylene glycol to form solution A. Solution B was prepared by dissolving 0.6594 g of Na2WO4 and 0.1000 g of CTAB in 40 ml of DI water and 40 ml of ethylene glycol. Precursor solution C was prepared by mixing solutions A and B and sonicating for 20 min. Then, 10 ml of precursor solution C was slowly injected into a 25 ml Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave. The solvothermal reaction temperature and time were 160 Â°C and 10 h, respectively. The powder was collected by centrifugation, washed with deionized water and ethanol several times, and dried at 80 Â°C in air. Then, the BWO powder was dissolved in water for spin coating. The BWO dispersion solution was spin-coated onto the as-prepared CIS photoanode at 3000 rpm for 20 s.

Characterization

The morphology of the samples was characterized by field-emission scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Hitachi SU8010). The microstructure was analyzed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and high-resolution TEM (HRTEM, Tecnai G2 F20 200 kV, FEI, USA). The phase and crystal structure of the samples were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD, Bruker, D8 ADVANCE). The chemical composition and element valence states were determined by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, ESCALAB 250Xi). Raman spectra were recorded using an intelligent Raman spectrometer (Horiba Jobin Yvon, XploRA ONE). A UV-vis spectrophotometer (Shimadzu UV-3600) was used to investigate the light absorption ability of the samples. TRPL spectra were recorded on an Edinburgh Instruments LifeSpec II with a 450 nm laser (FLS1000, laser pulse width 81.9 ps).

A neutral solution of 0.5 M Na2SO4 was used as the electrolyte (pH = 6.7). A simulated solar light of AM 1.5G (100 mW cmâˆ’2) was provided by a sunlight simulator (Newport, 94043A). Computational details

First-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using the Vienna ab initio simulation package.25,26 The projector augmented wave method was used for describing electronâ€“ion interactions,27 and the generalized gradient approximation parametrized by the Perdewâ€“Burkeâ€“Ernzerhof exchangeâ€“correlation functional for solids was used.28 The orbitals of 4d105s2, 5s25p1, 3s23p4, 6s26p3, 5d46s2, and 2s22p4 were explicitly treated as valence electrons for Cd, In, S, Bi, W, and O, respectively. A cutoff energy of 500 eV for the plane-wave basis set and a Î“-centered 4 Ã— 4 Ã— 1 k-point mesh were used. For structural optimization, the computational unit cell contains a vacuum space of at least 15 Ã… in the c direction and atoms in the bottom four layers of the CIS are frozen to mimic a thick substrate, while all other atoms are relaxed until the Hellmannâ€“Feynman forces are less than 0.01 eV Ã…âˆ’1. The convergence threshold of the self-consistent field iteration was set to 10âˆ’6 eV.

To analyze the interfacial charge carrier transfer pathway of CIS/BWO and CISâ€“BWO photoanodes, in situ irradiated X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (ISI-XPS) was used.36 As shown in Fig. 3b, the CIS/BWO photoanode exhibits two peaks located at 34.85 and 37.00 eV without light irradiation, which are attributed to the characteristic peaks of W 3d5/2 and W 3d3/2 of BWO, respectively. Upon light irradiation, the W 3d characteristic peaks positively shift by 0.27 eV, implying the decreased electron density under light irradiation (Fig. 3c). In comparison, the W 3d characteristic peaks of the CISâ€“BWO photoanode shift negatively by 0.30 eV. This phenomenon provides direct evidence that the introduction of Biâ€“S bonds at the interface of CIS and BWO can change the interfacial charge transfer direction. The XPS peaks at 405.36 (Cd 3d5/2) and 412.11 eV (Cd 3d3/2) in Fig. 3d are attributed to CIS measured without light irradiation. The characteristic peaks of CIS/BWO under irradiation shift negatively by 0.55 eV, indicating that the electron density increases and the electrons transfer from BWO to CIS. Meanwhile, the Cd 2d peaks of the CISâ€“BWO photoanode shift positively, suggesting a completely opposite hole transport direction (Fig. 3e). These binding energy shifts of CIS/BWO and CISâ€“BWO photoanodes make the charge carrier transfer directions at the heterojunction interfaces clear under the same light irradiation conditions. Specifically, with the assistance of the Biâ€“S bond, the photogenerated electrons migrate from BWO to CIS while the photogenerated holes transfer from CIS to BWO, which corresponds to the carrier transfer pathway of a direct Z-scheme heterojunction. In contrast, the carrier transfer pathway of the CISâ€“BWO photoanode exhibits the opposite trend, which is consistent with the type II model. In a word, the introduction of Biâ€“S bonds can alter the direction of carrier transmission, and eventually change the type of the heterojunction.

First-principles calculations based on DFT were carried out to further understand the interfacial charge transfer mechanism of the CIS/BWO photoanode.21 The CIS/BWO heterojunction model was simulated based on the CIS (100) slab and the BWO (001) slab, and the heterojunction molecular structure model is presented in Fig. 5a. The electron localization function is computed to reveal the bonding interaction of Biâ€“S bonds and the regions are defined from 0 to 1. The regions close to 1 have strong covalent bonding electrons or lone pair electrons and the regions close to 0 have very low electron density. As shown in Fig. 5b, the large regions between the Bi and S atoms of CIS/BWO suggest the existence of strong interfacial Biâ€“S covalent bonds (length 2.79 Ã…) between the Bi atoms of the BWO (001) facet and the S atoms of the CIS (100) facet. The interfacial chemical bonds can act as atomic-scale charge-flow highways, which could facilitate the speeding up and broadening of the charge flow between components. More importantly, a charge density difference image is presented in Fig. 5c to demonstrate the charge distribution. The yellow and cyan regions represent the electron accumulation and depletion, respectively. Obviously, the electrons tend to accumulate on the CIS side and deplete on the BWO side, suggesting that the electrons flow from BWO to CIS. The band alignment and free electron transfer of a heterojunction can be revealed by calculating the work function of components, which is calculated based on the electrostatic potential across the heterostructure. As shown in Fig. 5d and e, the work function of the CIS (100) surface is calculated to be 4.88 eV, apparently smaller than that of the BWO (001) surface (5.64 eV). Therefore, when the CIS (100) facet and the BWO (001) facet are in contact, they form a compact interface. The free electrons in the BWO (001) surface with a high EF (5.64 eV) will diffuse to the CIS (100) surface with a low EF (4.88 eV) spontaneously to achieve an equilibrium of EF through the interfacial Biâ€“S bond. The free electron drifting enables charge redistribution at the interface of the CIS/BWO heterojunction, which in turn results in an internal electric field pointing from BWO to CIS. Under the assistance of an internal electric field, the photo-generated carrier achieves rapid separation and directed migration. Thus, the Biâ€“S bond could not only act as the atomic-level charge transfer channel but could also change the migration pathway and distance within the heterojunction. Based on the above discussions, a Z-scheme heterojunction charge transfer mechanism is presented in Fig. 5f. When CIS with a high EF is in contact with BWO with a low EFvia the interfacial Biâ€“S bond, the free electrons will transfer from CIS to BWO to achieve EF balance. Then, an electron-depleted layer in CIS and an accumulation layer in BWO are formed. Thus, an internal electric field is formed, which points from CIS to BWO. Meanwhile, the band edge of CIS bends upward and the band edge of BWO bends downward. Under the control of the internal electric field and band edge bending, the photo-generated electrons from the CB of BWO recombine with the photo-generated holes from the VB of CIS. Finally, the Z-scheme CIS/BWO heterojunction is formed, which separates the photo-generated carriers spatially and retains the redox potential, thereby promoting the PEC performance of photoanodes.

Conclusions

In summary, we utilize a facile high-temperature solvothermal method to introduce interfacial Biâ€“S chemical bonds into a CIS/BWO heterojunction. After introducing the Biâ€“S bonds, the composite photoanode shows an inverted carrier transfer direction and the band structure exhibits Z-scheme alignment. The formation of a Z-scheme heterojunction promotes the charge carrier separation and preserves the water redox potential of materials at the same time. Theoretical calculations confirm that the Biâ€“S bonds act as atomic-scale charge-flow highways, which help in the carrier transfer between components. As a result, the composite photoanode exhibits a remarkably enhanced photocurrent density of 4.25 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE with a low onset potential of 0.30 VRHE. This significant improvement in the PEC performance mainly comes from the Z-scheme band alignment for photogenerated carrier separation and the surface exposure of Biâ€“S bonds for charge carrier injection. This strategy demonstrates the possibility of achieving comprehensive improvement via facile introduction of interfacial chemical bonds into heterojunctions.
