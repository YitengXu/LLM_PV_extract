Abstract

In this paper, tris(2,3-dibromopropyl) isocyanurate (TBC) is for the first time as far as we know determined by ultrasensitive photoelectrochemical (PEC) immunoassay using an antibody-modified ternary hybrid CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 nanotube arrays (NTAs) photoelectrode, developed by the pulse electrodeposition technique. The as-prepared hybrid shows enhanced photon absorption and photocurrent response, which subsequently increased the photoelectrical conversion efficiency in the visible region. TBC-antibody (Ab) was developed in rabbits as a result of immunization with the BSAâ€“TBC conjugate and covalently cross-linked onto the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs. Since the photocurrent is highly dependent on the TiO2 surface properties, the specific interaction between TBC and the antibody results in a sensitive change in the photocurrent, which displayed a linear range of 5.0 Ã— 10âˆ’11 to 5.0 Ã— 10âˆ’5 M and a low detection limit of 5.0 Ã— 10âˆ’11 M for TBC determination. This proposed strategy highlights the application of TiO2 nanotube in visible-light-activated photoelectrochemical biosensing, which could largely reduce the destructive effect of UV light on biomolecules.

1 Introduction

Tris(2,3-dibromopropyl) isocyanurate (TBC, Fig. S-1,â€  CAS no. 52434-90-9) is a heterocyclic hexabrominated additive flame retardant, which is resistant to photodegradation, durable, and thermally stable. It is a white to off-white powder specially developed for use in polypropylene, polyethylene and polystyrene. In China, TBC has been widely applied since the mid-1980s in polymer products for fire safety reasons due to its excellent capability to reduce flammability.1,2 However, increasing concerns have been raised on the environmental problems caused by these flame retardant products. Numerous studies showed that these chemicals are persistent, have long distance transport and bioaccumulative abilities, and may potentially harm ecosystems and human health.3â€“5

Therefore, it is essential to develop a sensitive, rapid, accurate, and selective method for determination of TBC. Well established methods include gas chromatography (GC) and liquid chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry (LC-MS).6,7 Although good sensitivity has been achieved, these protocols are relatively expensive, involve time-consuming sample preparation, and they must be performed by highly trained and experienced personnel. So, they are not suitable for field tests. Zhao et al. of our research group proposed an electrochemiluminescence (ECL) technique for the determination of TBC with a limit of detection of 5.0 Ã— 10âˆ’8 M (S/N = 3) and a linear range of 1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’7 to 5.0 Ã— 10âˆ’5 M,8 which is not suitable for the detection of ultratrace TBC in environmental samples because of its low solubility in water.

The photoelectrochemical (PEC) label-free immunoassay is a newly developed analytical method for the rapid and high-throughput biological assay.9â€“11 Compared with conventional optical methods, the PEC immunoassay is simpler in instrumentation and easier to be miniaturized. In the PEC process, the light-induced current is used to quantify the target. Benefiting from the separation of excitation source (light) and detection signal (current), some undesired background signals are reduced and high sensitivity is achieved. Of course, photoactive material and antibody features are the key to influencing the performance of the PEC immunosensor.

Titania (TiO2) is a wide-band-gap semiconductor material that has received intense scrutiny for a broad range of applications, thanks to its intriguing physicalâ€“chemical properties and its cheap, abundant, and reasonably non-toxic nature. Among all the TiO2 nanostructural materials, TiO2 nanotubes (NTs) prepared on Ti foil by electrochemical anodization have attracted a lot of attention due to their facile synthesis process, high surface area, high orientation, uniform interfacial structure and excellent biocompatibility. However, the application of TiO2 is limited by its band-gap energy of 3.2 eV12 since it can only absorb UV light (Î» < 400 nm) which can kill biomolecules. Modification of TiO2 with narrow-band-gap semiconductors is essential for PEC biosensing applications to enhance the absorption in the visible range (400 nm < Î» < 800 nm) and increase the photoelectrical conversion efficiency. CdTe is an active PEC material in the visible range with a band-gap energy of 1.4672 eV (âˆ¼1.5 eV), and has been successfully used in solar cells.13,14

Gold nanoparticles (AuÂ NPs), with unique chemical and physical properties, are extremely suitable for the design of new sensing devices.15 Indeed, the biocompatibility and large surface area of AuÂ NPs provide a favorable microenvironment for the adsorption of biomolecules without denaturation.16 Furthermore, AuÂ NPs have been employed either to act as an electron transfer bridge for direct electrical contacting of redox proteins with electrode supports or to play a crucial role in both immunosensors and DNA sensors.17,18 All of these AuÂ NPs features constitute useful strategies for the preparation of biosensors with developed analytical performance.

Combining noble metals such as Pt or Au with TiO2 has been demonstrated to increase the photocatalytic efficiency.19,20 Through the deposition of metal nanoparticles onto the semiconductor surface, a Schottky barrier can be created at the metalâ€“semiconductor junction, which is due to the difference in the Fermi-level positions of the metal and the semiconductor.21 This leads to a continuous transfer of electrons from TiO2 to the metal nanoparticles until the equilibrium of the Fermi energy levels is achieved. In such metalâ€“metal oxide systems, during photoexcitation, electrons can migrate from the semiconductor to the metal across the Schottky barrier, where they are trapped and the electronâ€“hole recombination phenomenon is suppressed, whereby the hole is then free to diffuse through TiO2 to the surface where it can be used to oxidize organic species. Therefore, the Schottky barrier can serve as an efficient electron trap to avoid electronâ€“hole recombination,22,23 which facilitates the charge separation and subsequently the interfacial charge transfer whilst enhancing the photoelectrical conversion efficiency.

In this work, CdTe/Au nanocrystals (NCs) were co-deposited on the TiO2 NTAs by the pulse electrodeposition technique. The advantage of self-assembled TiO2 NTAs with metal AuÂ NPs and narrow-band-gap compound semiconductor CdTe NPs is that they construct a stepwise structure of band-edge levels in the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode which benefits separation of the electronâ€“hole pairs. The co-sensitized electrode reveals excellent photoactivity. A label-free TBC immunosensor was fabricated by immobilizing the anti-TBC polyclonal antibody on the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs substrate. 3.3 Performance of the immunosensor

Due to the high photoelectrical activity, the 400 sequences CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode prepared by electrocodeposition was used as the substrate of the PEC biosensor. Scheme 1 presents a model energy scheme of the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode. Since the conduction band (CB) level of TiO2 lies at a more positive potential than the CB level of CdTe, electron injection is expected from the photoexcited CdTe into the CB of TiO2, whereas holes can accumulate in the valence band (VB) of CdTe to form hole centers, which can be consumed by participating in oxidation. Thereby, charge carrier recombination can be hindered. However, when the metal Au is coupled with TiO2, the photogenerated electron will gradually transfer from TiO2 to Au until a thermodynamic equilibrium is reached.22,33 At this time, the two Fermi levels are equal. Under the effect of the Schottky barrier formed in the interface between Au and TiO2, the photogenerated electron will continuously transfer across the Auâ€“TiO2 interface to Au, and flow to the external circuit through the conductive Ti substrate. Simultaneously, holes in TiO2 VB transfer to the CdTe VB, accumulate there, and are consumed by participating in oxidation. Thus recombination is effectively suppressed, which is responsible for the high photocurrent in the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode. With the immunoreaction between TBC and the antibody modified on the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode, an immunocomplex was formed, resulting in an increase in the steric hindrance toward the diffusion of quencher molecules and/or photogenerated holes at the electrode interface, and consequently resulted in a decrease in photocurrent.

In order to obtain a high and stable photocurrent, electron donors (or oxidative quenchers) are necessary. An efficient electron donor can capture the hole of the semiconductor, suppressing electronâ€“hole recombination and therefore enhancing the photocurrent intensity. Although KOH is used as the electron donor in strong alkaline solution (pH = 13), a mild pH solution is particularly required in biosensing. Antioxidants including uric acid, glutathione, and AA were investigated as the electron donor and the results are shown in Fig. 5A. The highest photocurrent is achieved in AA solution. The photocurrent increases with increasing AA concentration up to 0.25Â M, and then decreases thereafter (Fig. 5B). The decrease in photocurrent at high AA concentration can be ascribed to the absorbance of the quencher solution, which thus decreases the irradiation intensity and the efficiency of excited electronâ€“hole center formation.9,10

4 Conclusions

Tris(2,3-dibromopropyl) isocyanurate (TBC) was measured for the first time by ultrasensitive PEC immunoassay using an antibody-modified ternary hybrid CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs photoelectrode developed by the pulse electrocodeposition technique. The ternary hybrid CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode shows an enhanced photon absorption and photocurrent generation since the AuÂ NPs accelerate the migration of the photo-generated electrons. The highest photoconversion efficiency observed for the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode is 0.35% as compared with pure TiO2 NTAs (0.07%) under visible light irradiation. The improved photoelectrochemical activity of the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode is attributed to the synergistic effect of CdTe NPs, AuÂ NPs, and TiO2 NTAs. A label-free, highly selective and ultrasensitive PEC immunosensor for TBC was fabricated by successive modification of the CdTe/Auâ€“TiO2 NTAs electrode with anti-TBC Ab and BSA. TBC was quantified by measuring the photocurrent intensity. The sensitive detection of TBC was achieved with a LOD of 50 pM and a linear range of 50 pM to 50 Î¼M. The analysis of TBC in Liuyang River water illustrated the applicability of the immunosensor.
