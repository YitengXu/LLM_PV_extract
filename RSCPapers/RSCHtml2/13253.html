Abstract

In this work, a novel catalyst based on the polydopamine (PDA)â€“Mn3O4 composite was synthesized and optimized by varying PDA and Mn3O4 loadings. PDA was used to immobilize Mn3O4 nanoparticles due to its abundant functional groups and self-polymerization properties. Bifunctional PDA acts as not only a bridge between Mn3O4 nanoparticles and graphite felt (GF) but also a catalyst for the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction. The composite electrocatalyst significantly improves the electrochemical performance of GF on which the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction tends to be sluggish. Interestingly, it was found that there was a synergistic effect in the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite which resulted in better electrochemical catalytic activity than pure PDA or pure Mn3O4. The electrochemical kinetics of the optimum PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF with 4 h PDA polymerization time and 50 ppm Mn3O4 was investigated by cyclic voltammetry and the results showed that the diffusion coefficient of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF was approximately twice that of blank GF. As expected, the optimum composite GF exhibited substantially improved performance in the vanadium redox flow battery (VRFB) single cell test. The electrolyte utilization of the VRFB with PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF was 94% while that of the VRFB with blank GF was 85%. The voltage efficiency (VE) and energy efficiency (EE) of the VRFB with PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF were increased by 5.9% and 5.2%, respectively at high current density (150 mA cmâˆ’2) compared to those of blank GF due to the synergistic effect of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF. Moreover, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF showed outstanding stability in both the stability test and VRFB single cell operation due to the strong interaction between PDA and Mn3O4. Compared with previously reported electrode materials based on modified GF, the novel catalyst described in this work exhibited higher energy efficiency at comparable or higher current density. Furthermore, the superior stability of Mn3O4 on the electrode with the use of bifunctional PDA could improve the lifetime of VRFBs by hindering catalyst loss and dissolution during cycles.

1. Introduction

Renewable energy technologies are of great interest nowadays due to resource scarcity and detrimental environmental concerns raised by the overuse of fossil fuels.1,2 Solar and wind energies are the mostly used renewable energy sources. However, one challenge of these natural energy sources is their uncontrollable intermittency.3 The energy storage system (ESS) storing electrical energy into chemical species and reversibly releasing it according to demand is a promising solution to this problem.4 Among various energy storage systems, vanadium redox flow batteries (VRFBs) first proposed by Skyllas-Kazacos in the 1980s are the most promising and extensively studied systems.5 VRFBs employ the same element with different valence states in the anolyte (V(II)/V(III)) and catholyte (V(IV)/V(V)) and have the advantages of power-energy separation, flexible design, long life span, low crossover, high stability and environmental friendliness.6 Despite the merits of VRFBs, high cell polarization at high chargeâ€“discharge current densities results in poor overall cell performance including low efficiency and low vanadium utilization limiting market applications of VRFBs.7 In VRFBs, polarization is caused by kinetic polarization at the electrode/electrolyte interface, ohmic loss of the electrode and electrolyte and concentration polarization from mass transport limitations.7,8 Electrodes where electrochemical reactions take place are key components affecting the overpotential arising from polarization heavily. The typical electrode of VRFBs is graphite felt (GF) with low cost, good chemical and mechanical stability, high conductivity and high surface area.9 However, GF has poor kinetic reversibility and electrochemical activity especially on the cathode side since the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction is complex, involving several elementary steps, limiting VRFB operation to low current densities (âˆ¼50 mA cmâˆ’2).10,11 Therefore, it's paramount to explore effective electrode materials and electrocatalysts. To date, lots of strategies have been exploited to overcome the cell polarization problem including surface treatments and introducing electrocatalysts onto the electrode to reduce the activation barrier for the redox conversion.7,12 Surface treatments contain thermal treatment in air, acid treatment, plasma treatment, electrochemical oxidation and nitrogen-doping, which can increase the active sites for redox reactions by introducing oxygen or nitrogen functional groups on carbon fiber.13â€“17 In regard to electrocatalysts, many researchers have explored carbon-based (carbon nanotubes, graphene and carbon nanoparticles) and metal-based (metal and metal oxide) materials.18â€“21 Metal catalysts include Pt, Au, Ir, Pd, Ru and Bi.7,22â€“26 However, most of these noble metals are expensive and susceptible to occurrences of the hydrogen/oxygen evolution reaction limiting market applications of VRFBs.27 Therefore, low cost metal oxides (Mn3O4, PbO2, WO3, Nb2O5, etc.) are more attractive to enhance the electrochemical activity of the electrode.28â€“32 In particular, Mn3O4 is promising due to the high abundance of Mn, low cost and environmental benignity and excellent electrocatalytic effect on VRFB redox reactions.33 Mn3O4 is usually synthesized simply by a hydrothermal method onto graphite felt in the literature.29,34 However, there are two challenges for the use of Mn3O4 as an electrocatalyst for VRFBs. One is low electrical conductivity hindering charge transfer on the electrode.34 The other is the weak adhesion of Mn3O4 on the electrode causing it to be washed out by the flowing electrolyte in VRFBs.29,35 Thus, a highly conductive electrocatalyst support is needed to solve these two problems. As described before, the use of heteroatom-doped carbon nanomaterials especially nitrogen doped carbon nanomaterials as electrocatalysts for VRFBs has been gaining popularity and they have shown enhanced battery performance.36â€“38 One of the nitrogen doped carbon nanomaterials is carbonized polydopamine (PDA). PDA shows extraordinary coating capability and high conductivity after carbonization.39,40 PDA is easy to polymerize from the monomer under mild conditions and can bind nanoparticles tightly.41,42 Inspired by the unique properties of polydopamine, we propose to use this polymer as a Mn3O4 electrocatalyst support on electrodes in this work. The presence of catechol/quinone groups on the PDA surface after self-polymerization can bind strongly to the Mn3O4 nanoparticles through coordination bonds.42 Interestingly, PDA also provides nitrogen-doping on the carbon matrix, which introduces defects and vacancies acting as new active sites of redox reactions and promote vanadium redox reactions.38,43 In this paper, we investigate electrocatalytic synergism in carbonized PDAâ€“Mn3O4 for the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction using cyclic voltammetry. As described below, the electrocatalytic activities of the composite electrocatalyst are much higher than those of individual PDA or Mn3O4. The composite electrocatalysts are subsequently applied in VRFBs after optimization. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first attempt to use the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite as an electrocatalyst in VRFBs. It is expected that PDA can immobilize Mn3O4 firmly, and furthermore, the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite electrocatalyst exhibits a synergistic effect which achieves excellent performance in VRFBs.

Mn3O4 was synthesized by a hydrothermal method.29 In detail, 0.8 g manganese(II) acetate was dissolved in 80 mL DI water in an autoclave. The reaction mixture was heated to 150 Â°C for 12 hours. The morphologies of Mn3O4 powder, GF, PDA GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF were characterized by field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, JSM-6701F, JEOL, Tokyo, Japan) at 15 kV and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEM 3010, JEOL, Tokyo, Japan). 2.5. Electrochemical measurements

The electrochemical measurements were performed using a computer connected potentiostat (IVIUMSTAT, IVIUM Technologies, Eindhoven, Netherlands). 2.6. Stability test

To test the adhesion stability of Mn3O4 on a PDA support on GF, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF was exposed in 50 mL DI water under vigorous stirring. A 0.5 mL sample solution was taken out every 30 min. Subsequently, inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS, 7500 series, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, USA) was used to detect Mn in the digested sample solution to determine the quantity of Mn flushed away from the electrode.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) of the VO2+/VO2+ redox couple using blank GF, PDA GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF

The composite electrode was synthesized by using polydopamine (PDA) as the bridge between Mn3O4 and the graphite felt (GF) substrate. The bridging effect results from the catechol/quinone groups which can bind nanoparticles and the substrate via strong coordination bonds.42 Walsh's group reported a nitrogen-doped, reduced-graphene oxideâ€“Mn3O4 composite electrocatalyst which was similar to our materials.45 However, the synthesis process of the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite described in this work is simpler and more environmentally friendly with mild experimental conditions while Walsh and co-workers' method uses a strong oxidizer, concentrated acids and concentrated alkaline solutions with harsh experimental conditions.45,46 Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) was used to investigate the electrochemical properties of the fabricated composite electrodes. To explore the effect of different quantities of PDA on electrochemical properties, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GFs modified with various PDA polymerization times were investigated by CV at a scan rate (Î½) of 10 mV sâˆ’1 with the same amount of Mn3O4 and the results are shown in Fig. 2. As shown in Fig. 2a, it is observable that the positions of the redox peaks correspond to the literature reported previously.29 As can be seen in Fig. 2a, there are a negative shift in the oxidation peak first and then a positive shift in the oxidation peak with increasing polymerization time (oxidation peak potentials from blank to 24 h PDA polymerization time successively are 1.28 V, 1.21 V, 1.18 V, 1.19 V, 1.24 V and 1.27 V). On the contrary, there are a positive shift first and a negative shift subsequently in the reduction peak with increasing polymerization time (reduction peak potentials from blank to 24 h PDA polymerization time successively are 0.49 V, 0.58 V, 0.61 V, 0.6 V, 0.55 V and 0.46 V). Fig. 2b shows the peak voltage separation between oxidation and reduction peaks, which decreases first and then increases with higher polymerization time compared to that of blank GF (0.73 V). The smallest peak voltage separation (0.57 V) comes from PDA modified GF with 4 h polymerization time. Additionally, the corresponding peak currents of these CV curves are shown in Fig. 2c. The peak currents for redox peaks increase first and then decrease with higher polymerization time especially for the oxidation peak. The highest peak currents (454 mA for the oxidation peak and 276 mA for the reduction peak) come from PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF with 4 h PDA polymerization time. Based on these results, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF with 4 h PDA polymerization time possesses the smallest peak voltage separation and highest peak current indicating the most favorable electron-transfer kinetics for the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction.47 The reason for the peak voltage separation decrease and peak current increase before 4 h PDA polymerization time for PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF is due to the catalytic effect of PDA and Mn3O4 on VO2+/VO2+ redox reactions which will be explained in detail later.29,38 With increasing polymerization time after 4 h, the peak voltage separation becomes larger and the peak current becomes lower for PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF. This can be ascribed to too much PDA which may reduce the porosity of the modified GF. A longer polymerization time (>4 h) is not beneficial to enhance the redox activity of the PDA modified GF.

To further examine the effect of Mn3O4 loading on the electrochemical properties of the composite electrocatalyst, various concentrations of Mn3O4 were tried after GFs were modified with 4 h PDA. The CV curves of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GFs modified with various Mn3O4 loadings keeping the optimum PDA polymerization time (4 h) at a scan rate of 10 mV sâˆ’1 are shown in Fig. 3a. It is observable that the onset anodic potential has a negative shift while the onset cathodic potential has a positive shift with increasing Mn3O4 loading before reaching 50 ppm. When the Mn3O4 loading is more than 50 ppm, the shift for oxidation and reduction peaks occurs in the opposite direction. As shown in Fig. 3b, the peak voltage separation between anodic and cathodic peaks decreases first and increases subsequently with higher Mn3O4 loading. Additionally, the peak currents for anodic and cathodic peaks increase first and decrease subsequently with higher Mn3O4 loading as shown in Fig. 3c. Obviously, 50 ppm Mn3O4 is the optimum concentration resulting in the smallest peak voltage separation and highest peak current which imply remarkable electrochemical properties. The increase in electrochemical performance seen from CV curves (before 50 ppm) can be explained by the electrocatalytic effect of Mn3O4 on the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction.29 When the Mn3O4 concentration is higher than 50 ppm, the electrochemical activity decreases with high Mn3O4 loading resulting in large peak voltage separation and low peak current. This is presumably due to the low electrical conductivity of Mn3O4 hindering the electron transfer of the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction to the electrode.34,45 After optimization of the polymerization time of PDA and Mn3O4 loading, GF modified with 4 h PDA polymerization time and 50 ppm Mn3O4 stands out in comparison with other modified GFs. Therefore, the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF used in the following part for further study is fabricated with 4 h PDA polymerization time and 50 ppm Mn3O4.

To elucidate how PDA and Mn3O4 affect the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite electrode, the electrochemical properties of GFs with individual PDA and individual Mn3O4 were investigated using CV. Pure PDA GF was fabricated by putting thermally treated GF in a dopamine solution in Trisâ€“HCl (pH = 8.5) stirring for 4 hours while pure Mn3O4 GF was fabricated by putting thermally treated GF in a 50 ppm Mn3O4 suspension stirring for 1 hour. Both PDA GF and Mn3O4 GF were then carbonized at 900 Â°C for 1 h under Ar protection. The CV curves of blank GF, pure PDA GF, pure Mn3O4 GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF at a scan rate of 10 mV sâˆ’1 are shown in Fig. 4a. As seen in Fig. 4a, it is obvious that the electrochemical activity of the electrode was greatly enhanced after modification with pure PDA, pure Mn3O4 or PDAâ€“Mn3O4. The onset potentials of redox reactions are key criteria for evaluating the electrochemical activity of an electrode. A low oxidation potential indicates high energy efficiency since a low oxidation potential results in low charge voltage in VRFBs.47 As can be seen in Fig. 4a, the onset anodic potentials of all modified GFs exhibited a negative shift while the onset cathodic potentials exhibited a positive shift compared to that of blank GF. Fig. 4b shows the peak voltage separation of these GFs. The peak voltage separation is in the order: blank GF (0.78 V) > PDA GF (0.69 V) > Mn3O4 GF (0.66 V) > PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF (0.57 V). PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF exhibits the best reversibility of the VO2+/VO2+ redox couple since small peak voltage separation indicates better reversibility. As shown in Fig. 4c, the peak currents of both oxidation and reduction peaks of modified GFs are much higher than those of blank GF. A high peak current indicates a large active surface area of the electrode.36 Compared to blank GF, there is an improvement of the electrochemical activity of individual PDA GF or Mn3O4 GF. Among all modified GFs, the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF has the highest peak currents (454 mA for oxidation and 276 mA for reduction) while the peak currents of blank GF are 323 mA for oxidation and 204 mA for reduction. As a result, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF possessing the smallest peak voltage separation and highest peak current displays much better electrochemical activity than pure PDA GF or pure Mn3O4 GF. The great enhancement of the electrochemical activity of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite GF can be attributed to the electrocatalytic effect of the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite towards the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction. To understand the electrochemical catalytic activity of the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite, the proposed mechanism towards the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction is shown in Fig. 4d. As described above, both carbonized PDA and Mn3O4 can catalyze the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction.29,38 CV results in Fig. 4a indicate that there is presumably a synergy between PDA and Mn3O4. As shown in Fig. 4d, PDA and Mn3O4 bind strongly forming the composite electrocatalyst. In charging processes, the negatively charged nitrogen (pyrrolic-N, pyridinic-N or graphitic-N), due to its strong electronegativity from the PDA framework, can adsorb positively charged reactant ions, i.e. VO2+, easily on the active sites.48 The active sites are attributed to the defects and vacancies on the carbon framework, which are caused by the different radii between C atoms and N atoms.49 To prove the N-doping of carbonized PDA on the electrode, elemental analysis was used to measure the content of N in the PDA modified GF. The result of elemental analysis showed that the content of N in the electrode was 1.7%, indicating that N was successfully doped in the carbon matrix. XPS was used to further identify the N species on the surface. As shown in Fig. S1,â€  the N 1s spectrum of PDA GF was deconvoluted into three peaks, corresponding to pyridinic-N (398.3 eV), pyrrolic-N (400.3 eV), or graphitic-N (401.2 eV) forms. The three peaks were similar to those reported previously.38,43 The content of graphitic-N was relatively higher than the other two types of N, which was helpful in VRFBs because graphitic-N is more stable in an acidic environment and less subject to protonation than the other nitrogen forms.10,50 An Nâ€“V transitional state is formed after the reactant ion is adsorbed and the electron on the electrode transfers out of the anti-bonding orbital of VO2+ through Nâ€“V bonding. Subsequently, one H2O molecule coordinates with an empty orbital of VO2+ generating VO2+ and two protons. VO2+ diffuses to the electrolyte solution from the electrode by ion exchange finally. As shown in Fig. 4d, the catalytic mechanism of Mn3O4 in the composite electrocatalyst is similar to that of PDA except the active sites. The molecular structure of Mn3O4 is shown in Fig. 4d, containing one Mn (+2) and two Mn (+3) connected to O atoms. Oxygen atoms act as active sites for the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction. The catalytic process of Mn3O4 for the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction occurs on Mnâ€“Oâ€“Mn active sites consisting of adsorbing reactant ions, electron transfer, coordinating with H2O and generation of product ions. These two catalytic mechanisms of PDA and Mn3O4 contribute to the enhancements of electrochemical activities of individual PDA GF and individual Mn3O4 GF compared to blank GF. Moreover, the electrochemical catalytic activity of the composite PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF is much better than that of individual PDA GF or individual Mn3O4 GF. The synergy between PDA and Mn3O4 is responsible for the enhanced electrochemical catalytic activity of the composite GF. This synergistic effect could be attributed to the strong interaction between PDA and Mn3O4, attributable mainly to intra- and interchain noncovalent interactions, including charge transfer, Ï€-stacking and hydrogen bonding interactions.42 In addition, the highly conductive carbon framework of carbonized PDA can facilitate electron transfer between the electrocatalyst and electrode.

To investigate the redox reaction kinetics, the CVs of blank GF, PDA GF, Mn3O4 GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite GF at different scan rates are shown in Fig. 5. It is observed that the anodic peak voltages of all electrodes shift positively while the cathodic peak voltages shift negatively with increasing scan rate suggesting that the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction is not completely electrochemically reversible (quasi-reversible) on these electrodes.45 As shown in Fig. 5, the ipc and ipa at different scan rates are proportional to the square root of the scan rate indicating that the VO2+/VO2+ redox reactions on the electrode are diffusion-controlled processes.45 By using the Randlesâ€“Sevcik equation shown in eqn (S1) and (S2),â€  the diffusion coefficient (D) values of the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction on blank GF, PDA GF, Mn3O4 GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite GF are calculated and shown in Table 1.51 It is evident that the diffusion process is improved by modification of the electrode due to higher D values. As can be seen from Table 1, there is a significant increase in the anodic diffusion coefficient at the PDA modified GF (5.16 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 8.33 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1) as compared to that of the blank GF (4.17 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 6.73 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1) while there is minimal improvement in the cathodic side. This suggests that PDA is more effective in catalyzing the oxidation of VO2+ to VO2+. On the contrary, Mn3O4 is more effective in catalyzing the reduction of VO2+ to VO2+ as shown by the higher increase in the cathodic diffusion coefficient (4.10 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 6.61 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1) when compared against that of the blank GF (3.57 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 5.76 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1). Moreover, the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite gives the highest diffusion coefficients (8.45 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 13.6 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 for the oxidation process and 5.62 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 to 9.07 Ã— 10âˆ’6 cm2 sâˆ’1 for the reduction process), which are about twice those of blank GF. The diffusion coefficient of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF in this work is 2.02 times that of blank GF which is higher than the diffusion coefficient of ZrO2 modified GF, which is in turn 1.67 times that of blank GF as reported in the literature.51 A higher diffusion coefficient indicates a higher mass transfer rate and hence faster electron transfer kinetics, increasing the reversibility of vanadium redox reactions.52 The high diffusion coefficients of the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction on PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF prove the synergistic electrocatalytic effect of the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite towards the VO2+/VO2+ redox reactions corresponding to the proposed mechanism.

3.5. Adhesion stability of Mn3O4 nanoparticles on the composite electrode

The stability of the electrocatalyst on the electrode is an important factor affecting the lifetime of VRFBs. In this work, Mn3O4 was immobilized onto the PDA film by two processes including the monomer polymerization process and the Mn3O4 binding process with PDA self-polymerization properties and rich functional groups. To evaluate the stability of Mn3O4 nanoparticles in PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF, the stability test was performed and the loss of Mn from the electrode was analyzed by ICP-MS. The stability of Mn3O4 on PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF was compared with that of pure Mn3O4 GF. As shown in Fig. S2,â€  the Mn concentration from pure Mn3O4 GF is always higher than that of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF (318.5 ppb for pure Mn3O4 GF and 135.2 ppb for PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF after 210 min). This result is significantly lower than the previously reported value (100 ppm level) by Kim's group indicating that the Mn3O4 is much more stable on the electrode with the use of PDA.29 The reason for the good stability is attributed to the strong interaction between PDA and Mn3O4 arising from catechol/quinone groups in PDA. Additionally, the self-polymerization properties of dopamine were beneficial for both attaching Mn3O4 onto GF, and establishing a strong binding affinity with the graphite felt. In this way, Mn3O4 nanoparticles are immobilized by the PDA and the PDA can also bind strongly with the GF. The excellent stability would improve the lifetime of VRFBs by hindering catalyst loss and dissolution during cycles.

3.6. VRFB single cell performance

The results for the electrochemical performances of VRFBs with blank GF, PDA GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF at a constant current density of 50 mA cmâˆ’2 are shown in Fig. 9. Pure Mn3O4 GF was not tested in VRFBs due to the poor stability of Mn3O4 on the electrode. Fig. 9a shows the chargeâ€“discharge curves of VRFBs (1st cycle) with blank GF, PDA GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF. The longer chargeâ€“discharge time and smaller voltage plateau separation suggest better performance of VRFBs. Fig. 9b shows the electrolyte utilization of VRFBs with blank GF, PDA GF and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF. The electrolyte utilization is calculated by practical charge capacity (1st cycle) over the theoretical capacity. It is observable that PDA GF (88.3%) and PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF (94.0%) have higher electrolyte utilization than blank GF (85%). The high electrolyte utilization of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF is due to the faster kinetics of the composite electrocatalyst. It is noted that this work aimed to overcome the sluggish kinetics of V(IV)/V(V) redox reactions. Compared with the negative V(II)/V(III) redox couple, the V(IV)/V(V) redox couple sustains a relatively slow reaction due to the rearrangement of the coordination structures of the vanadium ions, influencing significantly the performance of VRFBs. This rearrangement includes the transfer of electrons, the exchange of protons, and the formation of a number of intermediate complexes, according to the pH of the electrolyte and the electrical potential.23 Thus, the V(IV)/V(V) half-cell reaction plays an important role in the overall performance of VRFBs. This is the reason that much attention has been paid to the investigation of effective catalysts and materials for the positive side of VRFBs. In addition, the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 composite electrocatalyst employed in this work may not be suitable for the V(II)/V(III) redox couple. As reported previously, carbonized PDA showed less catalytic activity for the V(II)/V(III) redox reaction and a higher hydrogen evolution current than thermally treated graphite felt.38 Hydrogen evolution in the literature was observed in the negative electrode modified with carbonized PDA of VRFBs, suggesting that carbonized PDA may not be appropriate for use as a negative electrode in VRFBs.38 There is another study with a grapheneâ€“Mn3O4 modified electrode.45 The result showed that the grapheneâ€“Mn3O4 catalyst did not improve negative redox reactions.45 Therefore, this work did not investigate the effect of the composite catalyst on the V(II)/V(III) redox couple and more suitable catalysts should be developed for the negative reaction of VRFBs.

4. Conclusion

In summary, PDAâ€“Mn3O4 modified GF was synthesized and optimized by varying the polymerization time of PDA and Mn3O4 loadings. The optimization was conducted with CV and the optimum PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF was with PDA with 4 h polymerization time and 50 ppm Mn3O4. The electrochemical activity of the optimum PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF can be greatly improved and the mechanism for the improvement is proposed in this work. The CV curve of the PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF shows smaller peak voltage separation and higher peak current compared to those of PDA GF or blank GF suggesting better electrochemical activity. The diffusion coefficient (D) for PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF is about twice that of blank GF. In addition, the charge resistance Rct is the smallest among different electrodes. These improvements including reduced polarization and enhanced electron transfer kinetics are attributed to the synergistic effect between PDA and Mn3O4 in the composite electrocatalyst on the electrode. Consequently, a VRFB employing PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF as the positive electrode exhibited excellent performance especially at high current density. The VE and EE of VRFBs with PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF are 61.5% and 61.1% at 150 mA cmâˆ’2, much higher than those of VRFBs with blank GF. Moreover, the stability of PDAâ€“Mn3O4 GF is outstanding in both the stability test and VRFB operation.
