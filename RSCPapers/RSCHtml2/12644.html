Abstract

It has been a strong desire for researchers to control material growth at the molecular and atomic levels. Molecular and atomic layer deposition (MLD and ALD) are two such techniques. In comparison to a huge amount of studies invested on ALD, the research on MLD is still relatively limited due to the difficulty in finding suitable coupling precursors, but the recently successful applications of MLD are very encouraging in many areas such as surface engineering, new energies, and catalysis. In order to further stimulate more research enthusiasm and educate beginners, we contribute this thorough survey on the progress of MLD. This review is a comprehensive account of MLD processes for organic and organicâ€“inorganic materials, covering precursors, surface chemistry, growth characteristics, film properties, and promising applications. The work provides a complete summary of over 80 MLD processes for growing over 20 types of pure polymeric and metal-based hybrid polymeric materials. Given their similarities in mechanisms, we made a comparative description between ALD and MLD, and discussed the uncountable possibilities to combine both ALD and MLD for advanced materials with desirable properties. To feature and highlight the significance of MLD, this review specially gives some detailed discussions on MLD's applications in several important areas, including novel nanostructured materials, surface engineering, new energies (i.e., batteries, supercapacitors, solar cells, and water splitting), catalysis, and rewritable data storage. With this work, we expect to boost research interest and attempts at advanced materials using MLD as well as ALD.

1. Introduction

Nanoscience and nanotechnology hold great promise in providing solutions for the four big problems1,2 facing our modern society: food,3â€“5 health,6 energy,7 and pollution.8 With increasing research enthusiasm and effort in nanoscale materials, it is critical to fulfil miniaturization. To this end, there have been two technical strategies in practice: the top-down and bottom-up approaches.1,9,10 However, it is becoming increasingly apparent that the top-down approach is subject to drastic limitations for dimensions smaller than 100 nm.1,11 In comparison, the bottom-up approach (credited to Feynman12) as an alternative strategy allows the manipulation of atoms and molecules for building up nanostructures, and opens virtually unlimited possibilities concerning design and construction of artificial molecular-level devices and machines.10,11 On the other hand, such an approach can give invaluable contributions to better understanding the molecular-level aspects of the extremely complicated devices and machines.11 It has been a strong desire for scientists and engineers to manipulate atoms and molecules precisely at will to build ideal nanosized materials for various applications.13 The control of atomic and molecular scale manufacturing is a need for the development of new technologies that will enable working at the nanoscale in a reliable and reproducible manner, constituting the basis for future production processes.10 Among all the methods in the bottom-up strategic category to date, in the past decades two techniques named atomic layer deposition (ALD)14 and molecular layer deposition (MLD)15 have emerged and have been attracting more and more research attention with their unique capabilities in nanofabrication. Both ALD and MLD well meet our desires for precisely controlling material growth at the atomic and molecular levels with high reproducibility.

ALD is a vapor-phase surface-controlled thin film technique based on alternative self-terminating surface reactions.14 It proceeds in a layer-by-layer growth mode and enables material growth controlled at the atomic level. ALD first appeared in the 1970's and was initially invented by Suntola16 for growing ZnS two-dimensional (2D) planar thin films for electroluminescent display panels. ALD experienced some evolutions in its earlier times and its applications were very limited until the 21st century.17,18 In the early 2000's, ALD appeared as an enabling technology in microelectronics manufacturing, particularly for depositing high-k oxides.19 At the same time, ALD's potentials for various nanostructures have also been recognized and this has made it a powerful tool to date for synthesizing nanoscale materials.20,21 Since then, ALD has been widening its applications through offering new solutions with novel nanostructured materials. To this end, many excellent reviews have been documented in the literature for microelectronics,22â€“25 energies (e.g., photovoltaics,26,27 catalysis,28 fuel cells,27,29 batteries,27,30â€“34 supercapacitors,31 water splitting,35 and optoelectronics19,27), medical treatment,36 environmental issues,37 surface engineering,21 and novel nanodevices.38 Recently ALD has accomplished many new successes such as ultralight and ultrastiff mechanical metamaterials39 and superior two-dimensional materials.40,41 As clearly indicated by its name, ALD itself enables material growth at the atomic level.14 With this nature, ALD has been to date exclusively used for synthesizing inorganic materials,42,43 covering single elements,23,44 binary compounds (e.g., oxides,19 sulfides,45 nitrides,23 and fluorides46), ternary and even more complex compounds.47â€“55 In the meanwhile, a large number of novel nanostructures have been developed by ALD from zero-dimensional (0D) to 1D, 2D, and 3D morphologies.21,56 The strong versatility of ALD accounts for the huge number of publications since its delivery (illustrated in Fig. 1a), especially since the beginning of the 21st century.

The first reported such films are an aluminum alkoxide (the so-called â€œaluconeâ€),82 and thereafter there are many more alucones59,83â€“91 developed by MLD. These also ignited research interests in other metalcones (e.g., mangancone,91 zincones,92â€“99 zircones,100,101 titanicones,102â€“105 hafnicones,106 and vanadicone107) and other metal-based polymers.108â€“111 This greatly extended our capabilities in searching for advanced materials in a controllable mode. Given the fact that polymers are composed of multiple repeating units, their growth can only be controlled at the molecular level. Since its delivery, MLD compared to ALD is still in its infancy to date and there are only over 100 research papers totally recorded in the literature (see Fig. 1b). In the past few years, new efforts in MLD processes have also produced many promising hybrid materials such as metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs),112â€“114 lithium terephthalates,115 metal tetracyanoethylenes,116,117 and metal quinolones.118,119 Owing to its unlimited possibilities for new polymers and hybrid materials with precisely controllable growth, MLD thus is drawing an ever-increasing interest. In fact, MLD has laid a new venue for developing new organic and organicâ€“inorganic materials to address many technical challenges in a large variety of applications such as microelectronics,120 catalysis,121 energy conversion and storage,122 organic magnets,120 luminescent devices,60 surface engineering,123,124 and many others.61

Due to the differences in their bonding and elemental composition, inorganic and organic materials exhibit very distinct properties. In many cases, inorganic and organic matters are often complementary in properties. Through combining the ALD and MLD processes, more interestingly, there are new hybrid organicâ€“inorganic materials developed with amazing properties. It is very favorable that the complementary ALD and MLD share many common characteristics in film growth, such as the chemistry based on surface-controlled reactions, conformal film coverage over high-aspect-ratio substrates, low growth temperatures, and high flexibility for large varieties of materials. To date, there are many excellent reviews14,27,30,32,34,38,42,45 for summarizing ALD's mechanism, capabilities, and applications. In comparison, there are also about ten summaries of MLD reported in the literature.15,58â€“61,118,120,122,123,125 However, these reviews of MLD have either been published in a much earlier time or only focused on introducing a special feature of MLD. In particular, in recent years there have been an increasing number of studies on MLD (see Fig. 1b). In this context, it is time for an updated account of MLD processes for clarifying their surface chemistries, growth characteristics, film properties, and potentials for applications. Along with the needs for advanced materials with unusual properties, in addition, there is a new trend to bridge ALD with MLD for advanced hybrid organicâ€“inorganic materials. Hybrid materials (a land of multidisciplinarity126 which traditionally is dominated by solution-based methods) have long been searched for new properties for many important applications.126â€“132 Given the afore-discussed facts, we therefore are inspired to contribute this comprehensive review, in which we make a thorough survey of various MLD processes for organic and organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials. Specifically, this review systematically categorizes MLD processes and clarifies their underlying growth mechanism, growth characteristics, and film properties. In addition, this work summarizes the successful applications of MLD in several important areas such as novel nanoscale functional materials, surface engineering, energy conversion and storage, and catalysis. This new summary lays stress on the possibilities of MLD and specially highlight its processes for pure polymers and metal-based hybrid polymers. In addition, this work introduces the combination of MLD with ALD for nanolaminates with tunable properties and promising applications.

This review covers seven parts. Following this introductory section, we give some fundamental knowledge on both MLD and ALD, present a concise comparison of MLD with ALD in surface chemistry and growth characteristics, and the unique characterization of MLD. The third part focuses on introducing various MLD processes for pure polymers with a full collection of all the polymers as well as their precursors reported, and detail their growth characteristics and the properties of the resultant films. The fourth section continues to introduce MLD processes for metal-based hybrid polymers, divides all hybrid materials into different classes, and describes their growth characteristics, surface chemistry, and precursor selections. In the subsequent fifth part, we specially introduce the flexibility and unlimited possibility for new hybrid materials by combining MLD with ALD. The sixth part emphasizes the recent exciting results from MLD and the combination of MLD with ALD, such as novel nanostructures, surface engineering, energy conversion and storage, and catalysis. In the last part, we give some brief conclusions, and then have some insightful outlooks on future development of MLD by itself and its integration with ALD.

2. Fundamentals of MLD

2.1. Surface chemistry

2.2. Growth characteristics

The growth characteristics of ALD processes are subject to three parameters: precursor, growth temperature, and substrate, as well discussed by Puurunen.42 In MLD processes, the three elements also play important roles in organic film growth. As required in ALD processes, the precursors in MLD processes should also be able to provide a high vapor pressure, chemically stable in a temperature range for deposition, and highly reactive to their coupled precursors. As demonstrated above using EG and HQ for alucones, MLD films are highly related to the precursors adopted, such as polymeric material structures and GPCs. In some cases, substrates have to be pretreated in order to initiate a MLD process. For examples, 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane (APTES, H2N(CH2)2NH(CH2)3Si(OCH3)3) has been used in some studies to modify the Si and SiO2 substrates' surface coverage from â€“OH into â€“NH2 (ref. 67â€“69, 74 and 78) in order to initiate some polymeric film growths. In contrast, octadecylphosphonic acid (ODPA, H3C(CH2)17PO(OH)2) has been applied on Cu substrates to form a protective layer to resist ALD and MLD growths.142,143 As for the effects of MLD deposition temperature, the studies documented in the literature clearly show that almost all the MLD processes exhibit a decreasing tendency with temperature in GPCs. In other words, an increase in temperature commonly reduces the GPCs of MLD processes. In all the cases reported, MLD enables uniform and conformal coatings any shaped substrates (see Fig. 3câ€“e). The film root-mean-surface (RMS) roughness is usually in the range of 2â€“6 Ã… (ref. 79, 80, 88, 90, 98, 109, 110, 116, 138 and 173) (see Fig. 3f, showing a roughness of 5.6 Ã… of MLD-deposited alucone using TMA and glycidol (GLY)88). Organic and organicâ€“inorganic hybrid films from MLD processes are often air-sensitive, and chemically and thermally unstable, but there do have some MLD processes producing stable organic and organicâ€“inorganic hybrid films. This will be discussed in following parts.

In order to characterize the growth characteristics of MLD-resultant organic films, many tools have been employed to measure MLD film surface roughness, thickness, morphology, and composition. X-ray reflectivity (XRR)82,87,89,165 and spectroscopic ellipsometry (SE)82,89 are two techniques for measuring film thickness. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)87,90,97,105,106,147,151,162,163,174 (see Fig. 3câ€“e) is normally applied for verifying film thickness and structure. Atomic force microscopy (AFM)79,83,88,90,97,104,107,116 (see Fig. 3f) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM)101,112,113,173 are used for observing film surface roughness and morphology. X-ray photoelectronic spectroscopy (XPS)67,68,78,80,82,87,92 has been used for analyzing film composition. There are also other analysis instruments involved but not listed here.

6.2. Surface engineering

Surface engineering plays important roles in many scientific and industrial applications, such as friction, wear resistance, corrosion resistance, wettability, biocompatibility, mechanical stiffness, optical properties, and so on. The powerful capabilities of MLD provide numerous candidates of polymeric and hybrid polymeric materials for tuning surfaces with improved properties. The studies so far demonstrated MLD as well as its combination with ALD being a very useful technique in surface engineering.
6.2.1. Wettability. Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) feature structural perfectness and chemically inert nature, and have highly hydrophilic properties. Using MLD, Chen et al.144 made an attempt at modifying CNTs' wettability. Adopting the MLD process of PEâ€“ED, Chen successfully applied a film of polyurea on multiwalled CNTs (MWCNTs). It is still not clear how the MLD precursors are anchored on the inert surface of CNTs, but Chen et al.144 hypothesized that PD might have noncovalently adhered onto the surface of MWCNTs via strong physical adsorption, owing to its planar, phenyl system and negatively charged terminal isocyanate. Therefore, the physisorbed PD reacted with the sequentially dosed ED and formed a more stable complex that does not desorb from the sidewalls of the CNTs. Chen et al.144 demonstrated that the MLD polyurea grows linearly on MWCNTs with a GPC of 4.1 Ã… per cycle at 65 Â°C. The solubility of the uncoated and polyurea-coated CNTs were investigated in different solvents. Chen et al.144 found that, in contrast to the pristine MWCNTs, the polyurea-coated MWCNTs are easy to be dispersed in organic solvents, and the dispersion stability depends on not only whether the MWCNTs were modified but also the polarity of the solvents. They further clarified that the polyurea-coated MWCNTs were dispersed well in high polar solvents including DMF, 1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinone (NMP), and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) to form homogenous black solutions. These solutions could remain stable for weeks under ambient conditions. This is due to the increased polarity of the coated MWCNTs caused by the functional groups of the polyurea. Chen et al.144 also pointed out that the polyurea-coated MWCNTs exhibited poor solubility in less polar solvents such as water, due to the hydrophobic nature of the polyurea coating. This was ascribed to the improved chemical compatibility between the polyurea-coated MWCNTs and the polyurethane, and thereby better dispersion and interfacial adhesion.

6.3. Energy conversion and storage

Renewable clean energies (such as solar radiation and wind power) are essential for addressing and replacing the quickly depleting fossil fuels (i.e., oil, coal, and natural gas) to power our societies in the future.205 In doing so, energy conversion and storage devices are essential, such as rechargeable secondary batteries, supercapacitors, solar cells, and water splitting devices.
6.3.1. Rechargeable secondary batteries. Batteries as electrical energy storage (EES) devices are critical for wide implementation of the renewable clean energies,206 given the intermittent operation nature of solar and wind power. To date lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) represent the most successful EES devices and are dominant in portable electronics such as laptops, cell phones, and many others. However, LIBs are still not sufficient for transportation in terms of energy density, stability, cost, and safety.206 In addressing these challenges, ALD has emerged as a new research thrust and shown strong potential,27,30 exhibiting great capabilities in designing novel electrodes, solid-state electrolytes, and surface coatings for LIBs. Recently, there have been new efforts in ALD for other advanced battery systems such as lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries,172,207,208 sodium-ion batteries,34,209 and lithiumâ€“air batteries.210 Along with the expanding applications of ALD in developing high-performance battery systems, MLD recently appeared as a new tool for addressing issues in LIBs.122 More recently, there have also been new efforts154,211 in using MLD for developing new advanced battery technologies beyond LIBs, such as lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries. The capabilities of MLD for various batteries to date are exhibited in two aspects: (i) surface coatings of electrodes and (ii) electrode designs.
6.3.1.1. Surface coatings. The direct contact between LIB electrodes (i.e., anodes or cathodes) and liquid electrolytes always produces a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI), which consists of decomposed products of electrolytes and lithium. It is important to secure a stable layer of SEI for reliable cyclability of LIBs. Previous studies, as reviewed by Meng et al.30 have demonstrated ALD as a viable technique to modify electrodes with an ultrathin inorganic coating (such as Al2O3, TiO2, and ZrO2 with a thickness of less than 1 nm in most cases) as an artificial SEI film to improve the stability and sustainable capacities of LIBs. MLD's capabilities for polymers prompted researchers to search for new surface coatings as SEI films in LIBs.

An earlier attempt by Leobl et al.153 comparatively investigated the effects of both ALD Al2O3 (using TMA and water) and MLD alucone (using TMA and EG) coatings in suppressing SEI formation and improving sustainable capacities of a LIB carbon anode. Their study disclosed that the ALD Al2O3 coatings performed much better than the MLD alucone coatings, in terms of reducing irreversible capacity and sustaining high reversible capacities. In comparison to the ALD coated LIB cells and the uncoated LIB cells, the MLD coated cells showed a worsened electrochemical performance. Leobl et al.153 believed that the poorer ionic transport through the thicker MLD alucone coatings, in contrast to the better ionic transport through the thinner ALD Al2O3 coatings, underlies the worsened performance of the MLD coated LIB cells.

Si is a very promising anode material and features an extremely high capacity of 3579 mA h gâˆ’1 theoretically at room temperature, nearly ten times higher than that of graphite anode materials (372 mA h gâˆ’1 theoretically).212,213 In contrast to little volume change of intercalation-based graphite anodes, Si is a kind of alloy/de-alloy-based anodes and experiences a huge volume change of âˆ¼300% during chargeâ€“discharge processes of LIBs.212,213 The large volume change of Si brings many issues in LIBs and leads to electrochemical degradation and cell failure ultimately. To overcome this challenge, surface coating has been confirmed being effective to maintain mechanical integration of Si anodes, eliminate SEI formation, and therefore boost their electrochemical performance. To this end, several ALD coatings have been investigated, such as Al2O3 (ref. 214â€“216) and TiO2 (ref. 217). However, these metal oxide coatings lack flexibility with large volume expansion and contraction, and their ability to sustain long-term reliable cyclability of Si anodes is questionable. Piper et al.158 recently utilized a MLD coating of alucone (denoted as AlGL, using TMA and GL using as precursors at 140 Â°C) on Si anodes. Different from the rigid inorganic coatings, the organicâ€“inorganic hybrid alucone coating features mechanical robustness and high flexibility. In particular, the MLD capability in coating high-aspect-ratio structures enables a conformal coating over prefabricated Si anodes consisting of tortuous structures (see Fig. 34). Consequently, Piper et al.158 confirmed that the MLD-AlGL coated nano-Si anodes could realize high performance, in terms of sustainable capacity, cyclability, and coulombic efficiency. It is further disclosed that the AlGL coating helps preserve the electrode network, accounting for a 17.5% volume increase after 20 electrochemical cycles for the coated electrode while it's a 50% volume increase for the bare electrode. The larger volume change with the bare electrode led to loss of fundamental particle contact and structural integrity, and therefore rapid degradation. Thus, AlGL proves to be robust and resilient enough to accommodate the extreme volumetric changes of the Si nanocomposite electrodes, helping maintain an intimately linked conductive network and allowing for faster ionic and electronic conduction.

6.3.4. Water splitting. Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting, first reported in the early 1970s,225 has been attracting much attention for large-scale production of hydrogen to supply clean energy using solar radiation. 6.4.2.1. Porous overlayers. The deactivation of catalysts has been a ubiquitous but notorious issue in various catalytic processes and can be caused by many different mechanisms,229 such as poisoning, coking and fouling, sintering, attrition, and so on. In this regard, ALD has shown being highly effective by depositing a thin overlayer to encapsulate catalysts on their supports.230 However, a dense and pinhole-free ALD coating may reduce the catalysts' activity. 7. Conclusions

This work presents a comprehensive review on the technique of MLD, covering over 80 MLD processes for producing organic and organicâ€“inorganic films. Analogous to ALD, MLD provides excellent uniform coverage over large-scale planar substrates and extreme conformal coatings over high-aspect-ratio complex structures. Exclusively for organic and organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials, MLD therefore is supplementary to ALD and enables an accurate control over material growth at the molecular level. ALD is particularly for inorganic materials with a growth preciseness at the atomic level. With the full capabilities of MLD and ALD, researchers are capable of developing new advanced materials with desirable properties.

As clearly discussed in the work, to date there have been a variety of MLD processes developed for organic and organicâ€“inorganic materials. Given the fact that our society is facing many rigorous challenges in food, energy, health, and pollution, MLD is expected to offer new solutions with organic materials to address them, but the research on MLD to date is far more enough. Based on the studies summarized in this review, even so, it is encouraging that MLD are providing a number of new opportunities in many important applications and the studies on MLD is becoming more and more attractive. In case of new energies, rechargeable secondary batteries are one of the most successful technologies in electrochemistry and become essential for widely utilizing renewable clean energies (e.g., wind and solar power). In addressing the issues in LIBs and Liâ€“S batteries, the recent studies154,158 clearly demonstrated that MLD-deposited organicâ€“inorganic hybrid films have some exceptional properties in tailoring battery interfaces for improved battery performance. Furthermore, Nisula and Karppinen115 developed a lithium-containing organic material (i.e., LTP) as a new anode. These evidence MLD's capabilities in designing high-performance battery systems. In addition, MLD has shown promising applications in other energy devices including supercapacitors, solar cells, and water splitting. In the case of catalysis, MLD exhibits very novel and highly tunable abilities in designing high-efficient electrodes and stabilizing catalysts for extended lifetime. In the case of surface engineering, MLD paves an avenue to tune surface wettability, wearability, corrosion resistance, and so on. In case of novel nanostructured materials, MLD realizes many new materials and new structures that have not been reported. Through pyrolizing MLD deposited polymeric films, for example, Yang et al.173 demonstrated that MLD can synthesize CNTs in a more controllable mode. Moreover, there are other carbon-based composites and porous metal oxides reported. Besides the encouraging applications discussed in this review, there are also new research trends appearing in MOFs,112,113 organic magnets,116,117 luminescent materials,118,119 and so on. All these witness the significant roles played by MLD in searching for new materials for addressing challenges facing us. We would foresee that MLD, as well as its combination with ALD and other nanotechnologies, would greatly expand our capabilities in seeking new solutions for the four big problems of health, food, energy, and pollution.

Although all these exciting developments have been made with MLD, we should also realize that there are a number of new opportunities with MLD for future studies. In comparison to the large family of organic and organicâ€“inorganic materials, first, the research on MLD is relatively undeveloped and more research efforts are needed. In energy storage, for instance, MLD to date is mainly used for surface modifications of battery electrodes. Research efforts of future MLD studies may further deliver new organic materials as electrodes and solid electrolytes to advance our pursuits in flexible and all-solid-state batteries234â€“236 with reliable safety, improved stability, low cost, and high energy. In surface engineering, MLD as well as its combination with ALD offers tremendous potential to develop advanced multifunctional materials enabling excellent corrosion resistance, low friction, high wearability, and tunable wettability.237 Second, MLD processes now are limited in satisfactory precursors. The ideal precursors should meet high volatility, high stability, and high reactivity. In particular, the precursors should be able to avoid double-end reactions. Third, the stability of the organic and organicâ€“inorganic materials by MLD to date remains an issue, sensitive to air, humidity, and/or heat. In this regard, it will be very promising to expand MLD's applications with more robust materials. Last but not least, MLD to date is restricted to a limited range of applications. With the development of ideal MLD precursors and the improvement of resultant organic materials, we expect that the applications of MLD, in addition to those discussed in this review, will be greatly expanded into the areas of semiconductor,238 sensors,239 optics,240 biology,241 and medical242,243 and smart materials.244
