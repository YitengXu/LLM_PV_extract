Abstract

Wide bandgap inorganic perovskites have attracted intensive attention owing to their potential applications in high-efficiency tandem solar cells and indoor photovoltaics. However, the performance of wide bandgap inorganic perovskite solar cells (PSCs) suffers from large energy loss due to a high degree of atom (or lattice) disorder and trap defects induced by phase transformation. Herein, the defect states of 1.82 eV inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite are investigated by thermal admittance spectroscopy, photoluminescence spectroscopy, transient photovoltage, and space-charge-limited current measurement. It is found that the deep-level interstitial defects with an activation energy of 321 meV can be reduced by two orders of magnitude under prolonged storage under low-humidity ambient conditions. Admittance spectroscopy in the low-frequency region also reveals the evolution of activation energy for ion migration. The self-healing process, which is assisted by the ion migration, is proposed to explain the mitigation of the deep-level interstitial defects. Device characterization and drift-diffusion model-based simulation further elucidate the reduction of nonradiative Shockleyâ€“Readâ€“Hall recombination and the enhancement of carrier extraction, both attributed to the mitigation of the trap defects. This work highlights the critical roles of the self-healing process in diminishing the deep-level interstitial defects for high-performance inorganic perovskite optoelectronics.

1. Introduction

Halide perovskites have gained tremendous progress in the photovoltaic field. The most efficient perovskite solar cells are based on low bandgap organicâ€“inorganic perovskites, with power conversion efficiency (PCE) exceeding 25%.1 Meanwhile, inorganic cesium lead halide perovskites (CsPbX3) have also been widely studied due to their inherent thermal stability.2â€“4 Among inorganic CsPbX3, mixed-halide CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx (0 < x < 1) perovskites exhibit tunable bandgaps of 1.70 to 1.92 eV.5 A suitable bandgap offers a versatile opportunity for being integrated with different kinds of low bandgap solar cells such as c-Si, CIGS, and CdTe, as a top cell in a configuration of a series-connected tandem structure.6â€“8 Additionally, wide bandgap CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx (0 < x < 1) perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are also promising for indoor photovoltaic applications due to matching of the spectra with those of ambient light.9,10 Alternatively, CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskites with 0 < x < 1 also possess a low phase transition temperature, which simplifies the preparation process and improves their phase stability under ambient conditions.11 Nevertheless, the performance of wide bandgap inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx (0 < x < 1) PSCs leaves much room for further improvement, mainly due to the large energy loss.12 For example, the energy loss for most of the inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx PSCs lies between 0.6 and 0.8 eV, which is much larger than that of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid PSCs.13â€“16 The origin of the energy loss in wide bandgap inorganic PSCs is closely related to the unavoidable deep-level defects formed in the absorber.

Trap defects are undoubtedly among the most critical physical issues to develop highly efficient PSCs. Compared with organicâ€“inorganic perovskites, defects and impurities are prone to form in inorganic perovskites due to the complicated phase transformation, inevitable grain boundaries, unbalanced stoichiometry, ambient induced decomposition, etc.17â€“19 In inorganic perovskites, possible intrinsic point defects include vacancies, interstitials, and substitutions.20,21 Among different kinds of point defects, vacancy defects are widespread and primarily categorized as shallow-level defects, which do not affect the energy loss according to the high defect tolerance properties. In comparison, deep-level defects, including interstitial and antisite defects, often lead to a severe deficit of carrier extraction for being the dominant non-radiative Shockleyâ€“Readâ€“Hall (SRH) recombination centres to trap free charge carriers.22 Therefore, deep-level defects would affect the device performance and even result in film decomposition. Previously, substantial efforts have been made to partially passivate the deep-level defects to improve the performance and stability of inorganic PSCs.23

Here, we studied the trap defects in wide bandgap inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite using thermal admittance spectroscopy (TAS). TAS is a powerful tool to measure the admittance or impedance of a junction as a function of the frequency and the temperature, giving helpful information of activation energy, capture cross-section, and the trap density of states.24 It was found that the deep-level defects in a fresh CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite film, which were identified as interstitial defects, could be reduced by two orders of magnitude under prolonged storage under low-humidity ambient conditions. The TAS result also revealed the evolution of activation energy for ion migration, pointing out a self-healing process of mitigating the deep-level interstitial defects. Device characterization and simulation further elucidated the suppression of the nonradiative SRH recombination and the enhancement of the charge extraction. With the diminishing of the deep-level defects, highly efficient 1.82 eV wide-bandgap inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx PSCs can be obtained on a SnO2-based n-i-p structure with a PCE of 18.30%, along with a high fill factor (FF) of 0.80, a remarkable open-circuit voltage (Voc) of 1.27 V, and a short-circuit current (Jsc) of 18.01 mA cmâˆ’2.

2. Results and discussion

Fig. 1(a) shows the typical electronic band structure of the inorganic perovskite, where the conduction band minimum (CBM) is determined by the orbital interactions between Pb 6p and X 4s orbitals in the PbX6 octahedra and the valence band maximum (VBM) is governed by the antibonding hybridization between Pb 6s and X 4p. According to density functional theory (DFT) calculation, there are three types of defects in the inorganic perovskite: vacancies, interstitials, and substitutions.21 These defects can be classified into shallow- and deep-level defects. As Fig. 1(b) illustrates, halide vacancies contribute to shallow-level defects near the band edges. Interstitials and substitutions are deep-level defects located at the middle transition levels within the bandgap. Here, we employed TAS to investigate the energetic distribution of the majority-carrier trapping defects of the wide bandgap CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite. TAS was performed for photovoltaic devices with the active layer prepared as the fresh or the aged CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite. CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx was prepared from spin coating of the precursor solution (the molar ratio of CsI:HPbI3:PbBr2 is 1:0.5:0.5) in a N2-filled glovebox. The as-obtained (fresh) CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx film was aged under low-humidity ambient conditions (relative humidity (RH) < 10%) for a timescale of 168 hours. The details of the fabrication process are given in the Experimental section. The fresh and aged films show the same absorbance, with a Tauc bandgap (Etaucg) of 1.82 eV (Fig. S1â€ ). The absorbance spectra suggest that, after the aging process, the CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx film does not degrade or undergo a phase transition. TAS measurement was carried out from 210 K to 300 K to obtain the temperature-dependent admittance spectra for the fresh and aged CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx devices (Fig. 1(c) and (d)). TAS spectroscopy of PSCs typically shows two capacitance signatures at low-frequency (<103 Hz) and high-frequency (103 Hz < f < 106 Hz). According to previous reports, a perovskite film is likely to undergo a self-repairing process under external light stimuli.29â€“31 A similar process may also apply to our case. SEM images in Fig. 2(a) show the transformation from a wrinkled morphology for the fresh film into a flat morphology with grains uniformly distributed after prolonged storage. The appearance of the wrinkle morphology has been ascribed to the buckling effect, which was explained as a result of local compressive stress.32 The compressive stress has been reported to induce trap defects in the film.33 After storing under low-humidity ambient conditions for a long time, the lattice stress is released due to the ion migration, and the release of the lattice strain is confirmed by X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurement. Fig. 2(b) shows that the prominent XRD peaks shift to lower diffraction angles for the aged film. The blue shift of the XRD peaks is ascribed to the lattice expansion, thus implying strain relaxation. Based on the above results, the mitigation of the deep-level interstitial defects to shallow-level vacancy defects in inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite is explained by a self-healing process, which is illustrated in Fig. 2(c). In the beginning, the interstitial defects appear in the fresh CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite due to the compressive stress. After prolonged storage under low-humidity ambient condition, the stress is released by ion migration. The ion migration is accessible in the fresh film due to the featured small Ea (23 meV) determined from the low-frequency admittance spectra (Fig. S3â€ ).34 In the second step, the interstitial ions find their place in the lattice during the ion migration. The driving force of this step is believed to be correlated with the existence of a small amount of water. Although the presence of small quantities of water has been shown to increase the local conductivity through passivating traps at the grain boundaries and play a self-doping role in the organicâ€“inorganic perovskite,35 the effect of water on the already-fabricated inorganic perovskite film during the aging process is different from that when it is introduced as an additive in the perovskite precursor. The statistical data of the device performance show that the introducing of a small amount of water into the inorganic perovskite precursor has a negative impact on the perovskite quality and the device performance (Fig. S4â€ ). It should be noted that the self-healing process is not obviously observed under inert N2 or O2 gas storage, which will be shown later.

During the TPV measurement, a solar cell device is constructed and illuminated under pulse light. There will be photon-generated electrons and holes, followed by photovoltage generation when the light is on. After the light is off, the photovoltage decreases due to the recombination of electrons and holes at defect sites. As expected, the aged device shows a slow voltage decay with a characteristic lifetime of 155 Î¼s, whereas the fresh device exhibits a fast voltage decay with a typical lifetime of 108 Î¼s. The large characteristic lifetime of the aged device manifests the reduction of the deep-level trap defects.

3. Conclusion

We have investigated the defect states of wide bandgap inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx perovskite by TAS, PL spectroscopy, transient photovoltage, and SCLC measurement. It was found that the fresh film shows a high density of deep-level interstitial defects located around 321 meV and a small activation energy of 23 meV for ion migration. These deep-level defects of the film can be self-healed when storing the fresh film under low-humidity ambient conditions for a long time of 168 hours. The self-healing effect is proposed due to the stress relaxation due to the ion migration and the interstitial annihilation assisted by the small amount of polar water. Device characterization and simulation further elucidate the reduction of the nonradiative SRH recombination and the enhancement of the carrier extraction, both attributed to the reduction of the trap defects. Without the judicious selection of the charge transport layers and the use of complicated bulk and interface passivation, a high efficiency of 18.30% can be achieved for 1.82 eV inorganic CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx PSCs with a planar SnO2 based n-i-p structure. This work highlights the critical roles of the self-healing process in diminishing the deep-level interstitial defects for high-performance inorganic perovskite optoelectronics.

4.2 Device fabrication

The ITO glass substrates were cleaned with liquid detergent, deionized water, and IPA for 15 min, respectively. After drying with nitrogen and putting in an oven at 95 Â°C overnight, the ITO substrates were treated with oxygen plasma for 20 min. The SnO2 precursor was prepared by mixing the SnO2 colloidal solution with DI water at a volume ratio of 1:5. The compact SnO2 layer was spin-cast from the SnO2 precursor solution on top of the clean ITO/glass substrate with a spin rate of 3000 rpm for 30 s and annealed at 150 Â°C for 30 min in air. The CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx precursor solution was spin-coated onto the SnO2/ITO substrates at 6000 rpm for 30 s and then annealed at 170 Â°C for 8 min. After cooling, 50 Î¼L PEABr solution was added dropwise onto the as-annealed CsPbI3âˆ’xBrx/SnO2/ITO substrates at 5000 rpm for 30 s. For the aged sample, the as-obtained perovskite film was stored under low-humidity ambient conditions (relative humidity (RH) < 10%) for a timescale of 168 hours. The aging process was carried out in a humidity control storage box without light illumination at room temperature. Then, the spiro-OMeTAD solution, consisting of 72.5 mg spiro-MeOTAD, 35 Î¼L Li-TFSi (260 mg mLâˆ’1 in acetonitrile), 29 Î¼L FK209 (300 mg mLâˆ’1 in acetonitrile), 28.8 Î¼L tBP, and 1 mL chlorobenzene, was spin-coated onto the perovskite film at 4000 rpm for 30 s.
