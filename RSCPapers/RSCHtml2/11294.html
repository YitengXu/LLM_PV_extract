Abstract

Harvesting energy from water is an environmentally friendly way to ease the energy crisis, since water covers about 70% of the Earth. However, traditional hydraulic generators need to be set in some specific place to satisfy the requirements of hydrological and geological conditions. Nanogenerators based on the interaction at the liquidâ€“solid interface can perfectly ignore these limitations, and therefore, show great potential for use in self-powered systems. In this review, the mechanism of electricity generation at the liquidâ€“solid interface, classical designs and experiments, and application of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator are reviewed. Finally, existing problems and future developments of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator are discussed and summarized.

1. Introduction

Rapid developments in industrial technology have resulted in the requirement for huge energy consumption. One of the most feasible and environmentally friendly solutions to resolve this is to harvest renewable energy from water,1â€“4 since water covers about 70% of the Earth and the energy in water can satisfy the global electricity demand.5,6 However, existing hydropower stations that use electromagnetic generators necessitate large areas and need to be built in places where water needs to meet specific requirements with regard to velocity, level, and flow. Quincke et al. discovered the streaming potential in 1859,7â€“9 but underdeveloped nanotechnology limited further research and applications.

The good news is that promising breakthroughs in nanomaterials10â€“12 and nanotechnologies13,14 have enabled the harvesting of energy from water in different forms and it has attracted great attention from all over the world.15â€“21 Heyden et al. reported the influence of pressure gradient, salt concentration, and channel height on the stream current.22 Zhao et al. experimentally proved that voltage could be induced by flowing water in a single carbon nanotube.23 Dhiman et al. demonstrated that energy can be harvested from water flow over graphene.24 Wang's group invented a practical waterâ€“solid-based triboelectric nanogenerator (TENG); the consequent electricity generation can be attributed to the coupling effect between contact electrification (CE) and electrostatic induction (EI).25,26 Guo's group generated electricity by moving the boundary of liquid and gas27 or moving a droplet28 along graphene, resulting from the formation and movement of the electrical double layer (EDL). Recently, carbon black sheets were successfully used to harvest energy via water evaporation.29

In comparison to solidâ€“solid-based nanogenerators, a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator can avoid surface wear, and the fluidity of the liquid enhances the contact area between the solid and liquid. Besides, the liquid (e.g., pure water,30â€“32 sodium chloride solutions,33 and rainwater34) used for electricity generation has a wide source and low synthesis cost. The above advantages endow the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator with higher efficiency, lower cost, and longer service life than a solidâ€“solid nanogenerator. Therefore, the study of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators is worthwhile.

In this review, the basic mechanisms of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator are first reviewed and discussed, including the charge carrier behavior at the liquidâ€“solid interface7,35â€“43 and the mechanism of EI.44â€“46 Second, the classical designs and new progress of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator are revisited, particularly the mechanism and factors affecting the electricity generation performance (EGP; namely, voltage, current, current density, power, power density, and energy conversion efficiency). Third, the applications of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators in sensors (e.g., flow,47,48 concentration,47,49 displacement,50 and temperature26), energy collection unit, self-powered microelectromechanical system,51 and wearable equipment52,53 are presented. Finally, existing problems, the most promising applications, and future development of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators are discussed and summarized.

2. Basic mechanism of electricity generation at liquidâ€“solid interface

At present, studies on electricity generation at the liquidâ€“solid interface mainly focus on the CE, which is based on the EDL theory.28,54 The introduction of EI can enrich the designs of nanogenerators.51,53,55 In this section, the charge carrier behavior (charge carrier type and theoretical development of EDL) at the liquidâ€“solid interface and the mechanism of EI are systematically and comprehensively discussed.

2.1. Charge carrier behavior at the liquidâ€“solid interface

CE is a phenomenon that was discovered more than 2600 years ago.56 When two different materials (solid, liquid, and even gas) come into contact with each other, charge carriers are transferred, resulting in a potential difference. As a classic phenomenon, when a rubber rod is rubbed with fur, it gets negatively charged, while a glass rod rubbed with silk gets positively charged. The charge carrier type is one of the most important aspects in the study of CE. The mechanism of CE between metals has been successfully explained by thermodynamic equilibrium.57,58 Several mechanisms of CE between other solids have been proposed, which can explain some phenomena very well, such as electron transfer,59â€“63 ion transfer,64 and material transfer.65 However, the charge carrier type at the liquidâ€“solid interface has not been well studied. Touchard et al. studied flow electrification and believed that CE at the liquidâ€“solid interface was generated by electrochemical reaction.41 There are small amounts of impurities even in purified hydrocarbon liquids. Wang's group studied the charge carrier behavior at the aqueous solutionâ€“polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) interface in detail.66 Theoretical calculation shows that if there is only ion transfer at the interface between deionized (DI) water and PTFE, the amount of charge in water should be much lower than the experimental value. The outer shell of electrons of the oxygen atom in the water molecule form the electron cloud and the electron acceptor is the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital on the surface of PTFE. Before the water molecule contacts PTFE, electrons are trapped in the potential well because of the energy barrier (EW for the electrons of the oxygen atom in the water molecule and EP for the electrons at the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital on the surface of PTFE). When the water molecule contacts PTFE, electrons of the oxygen atom in water in the high-energy state hop to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital because of overlap among the electron clouds, following which the equilibrium state is achieved. After separation, if the thermal fluctuation of the electrons (ÎºT) in PTFE is less than the energy barrier (EP), the previously transferred electrons are trapped in the potential well of PTFE. Thus, PTFE is negatively charged and water molecules are positively charged. However, if the thermal fluctuation of electrons is larger than the energy barrier at a higher temperature, the electrons escape from the surface of PTFE, resulting in a decrease in the amount of charge on the PTFE surface. In addition, positively charged water molecules can induce the surrounding water molecules to be polarized. As a result, the positively charged water can be more easily separated from PTFE. To further explore the role of electrons in CE processes, the surface potential of PTFE was measured by using ion-free transformer oil to contact with the PTFE film. Results show variations in the surface potential with a change in the contact area between oil and PTFE, which indicates that the CE process can occur even without ions. To sum up, electrons play a dominant role in the liquidâ€“solid CE process (up to 90%) for this particular system, and whether this theory is applicable to other systems is worth exploring.

Since it is believed that electricity generation originates from the distribution change of the charge carrier at the liquidâ€“solid interface, it is crucial to investigate the EDL, which reveals the distribution of charge carriers at the interface.28,35â€“39 When the liquid comes into contact with the solid, the charge carrier in the liquid and solid form the EDL at the liquidâ€“solid interface. The motion of the liquid leads to a dynamic change in the charge carrier, inducing a current. The study of the EDL has a long history and several typical models have been proposed.7,40 In 1879, the Helmholtz model was proposed, which is a quantitative theory for electrokinetic phenomena based on Quincke's idea of the EDL.67 In the Helmholtz model, the opposite charges are equally distributed on both sides of the interface, like a flat capacitor. The distance between the two layers is approximately equal to the ionic radius, and the potential linearly decreases between the two layers.67 Considering the electrostatic attraction and thermal motion, the concept of the diffusion layer was introduced in the Gouyâ€“Chapman model.68,69 Gouy and Chapman believed that the counterions diffused into the bulk solution, which was called the diffusion layer.

In 1924, the Stern model composed of the Stern layer and diffusion layer was proposed,70 which is one of the most commonly used models at present (Fig. 1b). It is believed that ions had a certain size and van der Waals force exists between the ions and solid surface besides the electrostatic interaction. Therefore, the counterions at the interface are strongly attracted to the surface, forming the Stern layer. The charge distribution in the Stern layer is similar to that of the Helmholtz model, and the potential drops from Ïˆ0 (the potential at distance x0) to Ïˆs. The charge distribution in the diffusion layer is similar to that of the Gouyâ€“Chapman model, and the potential decreases exponentially from Ïˆs to zero. Therefore, the surface of the solid is charged. In the second step, the ions adsorb due to the coulombic force, consequently forming the EDL. Their theory emphasizes the dominant role of electrons in electricity generation at the liquidâ€“solid interface, which has been confirmed on some solid surfaces.56

2.2. Mechanism of EI

EI, also called â€œelectrostatic influenceâ€, refers to the redistribution of an object's electric charge under the influence of an external charge.80 The most common phenomenon is that there are always certain slight objects that are attracted to the pet's body, such as pieces of paper and plastic. This is because the pet's body is electrically charged by friction, and slight objects are attracted due to EI.

In general, without the influence of an external positive charge, there is no net charge in each part of an uncharged conductor. However, when an external charge is moved close to the conductor, the charge carrier in the conductor redistributes. The portion near the external positive charge has more negative charges (electrons), and the portion far away from the external positive charge has more positive charges (nuclei left), which are called induced charges (Fig. 1d). If the connection between the induction layer and ground is broken at this time, the positive (negative) charge carriers will remain stable on the surface of the triboelectric layer, resulting in an increase in the surface charge density. During this process, the ground acts as a huge reservoir, which can provide or store electrons.

3. Electricity induced by different liquid sources

When a liquid contacts a solid, the charge carriers in the liquid get regularly adsorbed on the surface of the solid, forming the Stern layer. The motion of the liquid results in the motion of charges, which is one of the reasons for electricity generation. According to the form of water, electricity generation can be induced by droplet, waving, flowing, evaporation, and moisture. Furthermore, the introduction of EI enriches the structure and design of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators. In this section, the mechanisms, recent advances, and factors affecting the EGP of different nanogenerators are reviewed.

3.1. Electricity induced by droplets

Harvesting solar energy has already been achieved, but harvesting â€œrain energyâ€ needs further research, which is the goal of droplet-driven nanogenerators. Yin et al. successfully generated electricity by moving a droplet along graphene, and the maximal pulse voltage and output power were âˆ¼30 mV and 19.2 nW, respectively.28,87 Combining density functional theory calculations and the EDL theory, they proposed the mechanism of electricity generation. When an ionic droplet is static on graphene, the EDL is established, forming pseudocapacitors (CF and CR). The charges are in equilibrium without a potential difference. However, when the droplet moves forward along graphene, this â€œcharge equilibriumâ€ is broken (Fig. 2a and b). At the front of the droplet, the ions are adsorbed onto the interface and a new boundary of EDL will ceaselessly form, â€œpropellingâ€ the pseudocapacitor (CF) and attracting electrons in graphene to charge CF. On the contrary, at the rear of the droplet, the ions are desorbed and the EDL disappears, releasing electrons into graphene. Consequently, a potential difference is caused by a dynamic change in the electrons' density. However, a later study suggested that it was the polymer substrate rather than graphene that attracted the ions in the solution, while the graphene acted as a conductive plate, which could be replaced by any conductive material with a weak shielding effect.33 Yang et al. found that the voltage and current were generated as the sequential NaCl solution droplets hit graphene/polyethylene terephthalate (PET) rather than graphene/polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA).33 The results of the sum-frequency vibrational spectroscopy show that the spectral intensity changes with an increase in the NaCl solution concentration on the surface of PET or graphene/PET but not on the surface of PMMA or graphene/PMMA. The spectral intensity on the surface of PET is slightly higher than that on the surface of graphene/PET, indicating that the surface field can be built on the surface of PET and Na+ ions come to the liquidâ€“PET interface. The attraction of PET to Na+ ions is caused by its surface dipole layer, and monolayer graphene (conductive layer) can be replaced by other conductive materials as long as the conductive layer does not completely block the surface dipole layer's attraction to ions. The induced voltage is proportional to the velocity of the droplet (Fig. 2c), consistent with the work of Park et al.88 Furthermore, the ion specificity of electricity generation was found. The induced voltage increases with a decrease in the atomic number of the solvent, which is attributed to the difference in the ability of attracting electrons in graphene (Li+ > Na+ > K+) and the screening effect (Fâˆ’ < Clâˆ’ < Brâˆ’). However, some studies have shown that even DI water can generate voltage. Zhong et al. generated a voltage of 0.1 V using DI water sliding on graphene with a piezoelectric substrate beneath it, because the shielding effect of water on piezoelectric charges is more hysteretic than that of graphene.89

The most effective and direct method to improve the EGP of a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator is to optimize the materials of the triboelectric layer and substrate. Using a sapphire substrate, MoO3, sulfur powder precursors, a polystyrene supporting film, and a polyethylene naphthalene (PEN) substrate, Aji et al. fabricated a large monolayer MoS2-film-based nanogenerator with a high voltage using chemical vapor deposition (CVD).90 Although the output power is similar to that induced by moving the NaCl droplet along graphene, the motion of the droplet along MoS2 generates a much larger voltage due to the high resistance of MoS2, which is more suitable for driving the diode rectifier circuit. Additionally, different substrates (PEN, sapphire, and SiO2/Si) cause different n-type doping characteristics and different square resistances. The nanogenerator with a PEN substrate has a higher square resistance and generates a higher voltage than that with sapphire or SiO2/Si substrate (Fig. 2d). More interestingly, using PEN as the substrate can generate a larger short-circuit current. Their explanation is that the polymer substrate facilitates the adsorption of ions on the MoS2 surface. Different from the previous study,28 as the speed increases, the voltage and current gradually reach saturation in this work. They attributed this phenomenon to the slow adsorption velocity of ions. By choosing a PTFE substrate, Kwak and his coworkers enhanced the output power of the graphene-based nanogenerator by âˆ¼100 times, reaching 1.9 Î¼W.28,54 In the wet transfer process, the PTFE surface is negatively charged. Hence, holes inside graphene are attracted and electrons are excluded from the top surface of graphene, facilitating the adsorption of ions in the water droplet and weakening the screening effect of counterions in the diffusion layer. The result of the Kelvin probe force microscopy shows the stable surface potential of negatively charged PTFE. More directly, Zhai et al. doped graphene with nitrogen (N) via plasma-assisted hot filament CVD, increasing the induced voltage from 50 mV to 320 mV.93 They found that N doping improved the concentration and mobility of the charge carriers, which is conducive to electron transfer in graphene. The result of the Kelvin probe force microscopy shows that N doping leads to an increase in the surface potential of graphene, which indicates an increase in the Fermi level. The higher surface potential of the N-doped graphene results in a higher induced voltage. Besides, the smaller contact angle of the N-doped graphene is one of the reasons for the increase in voltage due to an increase in the contact area. Moreover, this is inconsistent with Park's work,94 which needs to be further explored.

By introducing an induction layer, a variety of polymers can be used to generate electricity, providing more inspiration for researchers. A water-based TENG (WTENG) was fabricated by using PTFE, Cu, and PMMA.26 Electricity generation can be attributed to the coupling effect between CE and EI at the liquidâ€“solid interface. There are two conditions to generate electricity (Fig. 2e and f). When a pre-charged droplet (generally positively charged due to friction with air or pipes) hits the PTFE, the electric equilibrium is broken, and EI forces the electrons to flow from the ground to the copper to rebuilt the charge equilibrium (Fig. 2e). After the droplet slides away, the electrons flow back to the ground. When the droplet hits the PTFE film again, the same progress happens and continuous impacts produce an alternating current. The other condition is that the droplet is pristine and uncharged (Fig. 2f). When an uncharged droplet hits the PTFE film, the surface groups of the PTFE get negatively ionized, forming the EDL. When the droplet slides away, there is no positive charge to balance the negatively ionized groups on the surface of PTFE. The electrons flow from copper to the ground due to EI. When PTFE is hit by the droplet again, the formation of EDL shields the negative charge on the PTFE surface, causing electrons to flow from the ground to copper. Then, as the droplet slides away, the electrons flow back to copper again. The experimental results show that the impact of a 30 Î¼L droplet from a height of 90 cm can generate an alternating voltage and current of 9.3 V and 17 Î¼A, respectively. Evidently, with an increase in the falling height of the droplet, the transfer charges (proportional to the current) increase and then saturate (Fig. 2g). Moreover, replacing the PTFE film with a nylon film leads to a decrease in the current density, which can be attributed to the liquid residue on the hydrophilic nylon surface. The WTENG with a size of 4 Ã— 4 cm2 can generate a current density of 1.5 Î¼A cmâˆ’2 and a peak-to-peak power density of 20 mW cmâˆ’2, which can light up 20 light-emitting diodes (LEDs), driven by tap water at a flow rate of 30 mL sâˆ’1.

Since the liquid residue on the triboelectric layer can significantly reduce the EGP, it is necessary to explore a method to reduce liquid residues. Chung et al. prepared a rough hydrophobic surface by using an aerosol spray.91 The open-circuit voltage increases with an increase in the top coats. The impact of the droplet may destroy the microstructure of the surface, causing the droplet on the surface to change from the Cassieâ€“Baxter state to the Wenzel state, which affects electricity generation. More top coats are beneficial for withstanding the impact of droplets and protecting the microstructure, thereby improving the voltage and stability (Fig. 2h and i). They used a simple spray method to fabricate a superhydrophobic TENG on the cap brim and light up an LED, demonstrating its excellent applicability (Fig. 2j). In addition to the material, the surface structure of the triboelectric layer has a great impact on wettability. Cho and coworkers investigated the role of the hierarchical structure of heptadecafluoro-1,1,2,2-tetrahydrodecyltrichlorosilane-coated aluminum plates on electricity generation at the liquidâ€“solid interface.92 TENGs with hierarchically structured surfaces and nanostructured surfaces were fabricated. The obtained results show that despite the similar voltage generated by the first hit of the water droplet, the TENG with a nanostructured surface generates decreasingly lower voltage as the number of impacts increases. However, the voltage generated by TENG with a hierarchically structured surface does not vary much (Fig. 2k). High-speed camera images show that the hit of droplets involves diffusion and rebound processes. When the hit energy is greater than the surface tension energy, the droplets will break. Therefore, when the hit velocity of droplets is high, droplets split into smaller droplets. These small droplets cannot fall off the surface, which causes the voltage to decrease. When the next droplet hits, the small droplet on the hierarchically structured surface (Cassieâ€“Baxter state) coalesces with the larger droplet and slides off the surface. However, the droplet on the nanostructured surface (Wenzel state) coalesces with the large droplet and forms a larger water film, resulting in no voltage generation. By changing the structure and wettability of the solid surfaces, a creative TENG was designed by Liu and his coworkers.95 The entire surface contains four regions with different wettability values (Fig. 3a). The contact angles of the four regions are 164.8Â°, 127.85Â°, 107.6Â°, and 92Â°. The wettability gradient of the surface causes spontaneous motion of the droplet, which makes it possible to generate electricity even without any external energy input. Besides, a higher wettability gradient makes the droplets move faster, resulting in a higher voltage. In general, the energy conversion efficiency refers to the ratio of output power to input power. The TENG driven by self-propelled droplets requires no external energy input and the motion of the droplet is determined by the inherent properties of the liquidâ€“solid interface. Therefore, in a way, we can say that the energy conversion efficiency of this TENG is very high. The self-assembly layer of hydrophobic molecules also reduces the liquid residue. Chung and coworkers reported a perfluoropolyether-liquid-based spin-coated TENG containing a self-assembled hydrophobic layer of trichloro(1H,1H,2H,2H-perfluorooctyl)silane (HDFS), which could facilitate the separation of liquid and surface.96 Furthermore, HDFS and perfluoropolyether liquid have high electron affinities, which help to enhance the EGP. Using a lubricant is also a good way to reduce the liquid residue. Wang's group invented a new slippery lubricant-impregnated porous surface-based TENG (SLIPS TENG), which could work normally even in the bent state.97 The specialty of the SLIPS TENG is that a perfluorinated liquid is used as the lubricant to cover PTFE and it can generate electricity by sliding a droplet on the surface as long as the thickness of the lubricant is less than the critical value (Fig. 3b). The presence of the lubricant prevents the dripping droplets from bouncing off the TENG; further, even if the temperature is lower than the freezing point, the dripping droplets can still slide smoothly without a residual liquid.

Furthermore, optimizing the structure of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators is also an effective method to improve their EGP. In the work of Yun et al., the induction layer was designed to be compact and interdigital, using which the amount of triboelectric charge was doubled.103 Xu et al. used the bulk effect to generate electricity rather than the conventional interfacial effect.99 An aluminum electrode is directly connected to indium tin oxide (Fig. 3g). Materials and structures have a great influence on the performance of nanogenerators. Zhu and coworkers fabricated a nanogenerator composed of fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) and two metal electrodes.115 Electricity generation relies on the difference in EI in two metal plates (Fig. 4i). In the initial state, the negative charges are evenly distributed on the FEP surface, and there is no potential difference between the two metal plates. When water comes into contact with FEP on the left, water gets positively charged due to CE. Consequently, the charges on the left of FEP are screened by the positive charges in water, resulting in electron flow from the right metal plate to the left to maintain charge equilibrium. The same shielding effect occurs when water continues to move to the right metal plate, causing the electrons to flow back to the right plate. As water gradually moves to the left, the shielding effect of charges in the water on the right metal plate is weakened, while the shielding effect on the left metal plate is enhanced, resulting in electrons flowing back to the left metal plate again. The current can be affected by the aspect ratio of the TENG (Fig. 4j). This is because as the aspect ratio gets narrower, the interaction distance gets larger, resulting in a higher current. Lin and his coworkers invented a waving-driven nanogenerator composed of a PMMA substrate, a copper film, and a patterned polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) film to harvest wave energy, as shown in Fig. 5a.25 The contact between water and PDMS leads to the ionization of PDMS surface groups, resulting in negatively charged PDMS and positively charged water. These two results indicate that this device can be used as a sensor to detect factors affecting the water dielectric constant and polarity.

3.3. Electricity induced by flowing

When a channel or porous plug with charged walls is filled with an ionic solution, an EDL forms at the channel's inner surface, which usually results in the overlap of the counterions due to the small size of the channel.8,120â€“122 During the solution flow driven by the pressure gradient, a current is generated due to the directional migration of counterions, which is called the streaming current (Fig. 6a). Different surface properties generate different voltages. For example, the silica surface with negative charges generates a positive streaming current, while a polylysine-coated surface with positive charges generates a negative streaming current. The result clearly shows that there is a proportional relationship between the streaming current and pressure (Fig. 6b). To improve the EGP of the streaming-potential-based nanogenerator, great efforts have been made. Malekidelarestaqi et al. suggested that a longer slip length can lead to an increase in the slip velocity, and therefore, more counterions flow along the microchannel (Fig. 6c), resulting in a higher streaming potential and current.123 The enhancement in the slip length from 0 to 144 nm results in an increase in the streaming potential and current by 2.55 and 2.44 times, respectively. Meanwhile, the streaming potential can also be regulated by viscosity, density, conductivity, and dielectric constant of oil, when the ionic solution moves along the oil-filled surface (Fig. 6d).31,124 For example, a surface filled with Dupont Krytox GPL 104 (a general-purpose lubricant whose base oil is perfluoropolyether) can improve the streaming potential, while castor oil inhibits it (Fig. 6e). The influence of surface properties on the streaming potential may be attributed to the differences in interfacial energy, surface charge density, local disturbance, and shear rate. According to the mechanism of streaming potential, Saha et al. packed silica spheres tightly together to make a portable nanogenerator that could generate electricity via flowing water (Fig. 6f).125 By optimizing the parameters of silica spheres, the EGP could be improved. Li et al. used the capillary effect of three-dimensional (3D) graphene membranes to generate electricity and successfully powered an LED.126 The contact between 3D graphene membranes and capillary-driven NaCl solution leads to the formation of EDL. Then, Na+ ions absorb into the 3D graphene membranes, and Clâˆ’ ions are repelled. Thus, the flowing NaCl solution generates a streaming potential.

3.4. Electricity induced by evaporation

Water evaporation is a widespread and important phenomenon and has shown great potential toward generating electricity. Interestingly, carbon nanoparticles modified by four different polymer molecules can successfully modulate the voltage from âˆ’3 V to 3 V.129 Voltage modulation can be attributed to the difference in the surface charge caused by different functional groups. When the liquid passes through nanochannels between the carbon nanoparticles, channels with different surface charges allow only ions with a specific charge to pass through, thereby generating different voltages. Sun et al. reported a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator that comprised a PET substrate, two carbon nanotube electrodes, and a Niâ€“Al layered double hydroxide (LDH) (Fig. 7a).130 The nanogenerator is placed in a closed box and the LDH part is semi-immersed in water. When the humidity in the box reaches saturation, water evaporation drives the nanogenerator to generate a continuous current and voltage after opening the box. The formation of the EDL with a Debye length of 0.96 Î¼m can positively charge the layered LDH flakes. As a result, the nanochannels between the LDH flakes allow only anions (OHâˆ’) and water molecules (H2O) to pass through and cations (H3O+) remain at the bottom of the LDH. Consequently, there is a potential difference between the top (negative electrode polarity) and the bottom (positive electrode polarity) of the LDH. The hydrophilic property and high surface charge density of the LDH facilitate evaporation and enhance the voltage and current. Since a high wind velocity can promote the evaporation of water, there is a positive correlation between the open-circuit voltage and wind velocity (Fig. 7b). A flexible structure can improve the mechanical properties, and the output of the nanogenerator does not decrease even under the bending state. Zhou et al. reported that electricity can also be collected from water evaporation using natural wood.131 Driven by evaporation, water flows through the microchannel and the hydroxyl on the wood microchannel can be hydrolyzed (Fig. 7c). Consequently, the inner wall is negatively charged, absorbing positive ions in water. Therefore, a potential difference can be established along the direction of water flow. The current and voltage of different wood types have been studied; evidently, beech wood is better due to its optimal microchannel size (Fig. 7d). Based on the streaming potential and capillary pressure equations, the microchannel of beech wood is further selected and optimized. After modification with citric acid, five beech wood nanogenerators connected in a series can be used to power a calculator, proving its application value and optimum design based on the mechanisms of electricity generation provides a good example for the development of nanogenerators.

3.5. Electricity induced by moisture

Moisture is one of the ubiquitous forms of water and it has been proven that electric energy can be collected from moisture using nanomaterials with functional groups.132,133 In general, water molecules in moisture are attracted to the surface of the nanomaterial and make the surface functional groups dissociate, resulting in the creation of positive and negative ions.134 Positive and negative ions generally cannot move together for two following reasons: (i) the volume of positive and negative ions is different, and the size of the channel prevents positive or negative ions from passing through; (ii) positive or negative ions can bind to the surface of the nanomaterial.135 Since positive (negative) ions are immovable, free negative (positive) ions will move in a certain direction due to the gradient of concentration (Fig. 8a), resulting in the induction of a current and voltage.136 According to the mechanism, ions should move in a certain direction to generate electricity.21 Therefore, the EGP of nanogenerators can be regulated by controlling the above processes. Since moisture-induced voltage is accurately repeatable, this kind of nanogenerator can be used as a memory. By matching the asymmetric electrochemical oxidation/reduction of two ends with certain information, the nanogenerator can be used to realize information storage. Liang et al. fabricated a simple nanogenerator consisting of a graphene oxide layer and a moisture-insulation substrate to realize the directional movement of ions (Fig. 8b).138 In their work, when the nanogenerator is exposed to moisture, water molecules are absorbed by the graphene oxide layer that subsequently move spontaneously to the side of the moisture-insulation substrate, driving free ions (H+) to move directionally.

Luo et al. reported a moisture-induced self-charging device, which mainly consists of three parts: carbon nanotube electrode that absorbs water molecules, CaCl2 that provides the charge carriers, and electrolyte gel composed of hydrochloric acid/polyvinyl alcohol for ion separation.140 When the carbon nanotube electrode absorbs sufficient amount of water molecules from moisture, CaCl2 dissolves, forming a CaCl2 solution and providing free Ca2+ ions and Clâˆ’ ions. Different mobilities of Ca2+ and Clâˆ’ ions in the hydrochloric acid/polyvinyl alcohol matrix lead to their natural separation.

Biological nanomaterials are rich in functional groups, which can be chemically modified. Yang et al. fabricated a kind of asymmetric ionic aerogel of biologic nanofibrils (NFs) with a bilayer structure to collect electric energy by using 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl-oxidized cellulosic NFs and quaternized cellulosic NFs through a facile freeze-casting method.139 In 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl-oxidized cellulosic NF layer, free positive ions are mainly produced, and free negative ions are mainly produced in the quaternized cellulosic NF layer (Fig. 8c). Due to the gradient of ion concentration, net charges move in a certain direction, resulting in a current. Voltage can be modulated by the thickness ratio of the two layers. The free charge carriers of the two layers are almost equal in unit volume; the optimal open-circuit voltage can be maintained when the thickness ratio of the two layers is âˆ¼1:1 (Fig. 8d). Besides, to ensure moisture and ion transport, the optimal porosity is âˆ¼99.3% (Fig. 8e) and the optimal thickness of the nanogenerator is âˆ¼6 mm (Fig. 8f). This kind of environment-friendly biomaterial has good biocompatibility, which is worthwhile for further exploring its application in the medical field.

4. Application

An electric signal can be generated by the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator via the interaction of the liquid and the solid. In this section, the applications of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators in sensors, energy collection units, self-powered microelectromechanical systems, and wearable equipment are reviewed.

4.1. Sensors

It is a hassle to provide a separate power supply for sensors. Consequently, self-powered sensors based on the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator have great advantages, such as simplistic structure, maintenance-free nature, energy saving, and so on. As early as 2012, Newaz et al. reported an ion concentration sensor based on the sensitivity change in the streaming potential of a graphene field-effect transistor to the ion concentration (Fig. 9a), which could detect a concentration change of 40 nM.47 Jiang et al. reported a liquidâ€“solid TENG and examined the CE process at the oilâ€“waterâ€“solid multiphase interfaces (Fig. 9b).141 Evidently, when the TENG is inserted into the â€œoilâ€“waterâ€ interface, two unique signals are generated: one from CE and EI between the liquid and PTFE film of TENG (VTENG and ITENG) and the other from the EI in copper due to the charge at the oilâ€“water interface (Î”Vinterface and Iinterface). Interestingly, after coating a layer of polydopamine on the PTFE and glass surfaces, two signals show opposite trends. VTENG and ITENG decrease, while Î”Vinterface and Iinterface increase (Fig. 9c and d). Both ITENG and Iinterface have a good linear relationship with the natural logarithm of the concentration of dopamine. Using this linear relationship, TENG can be used as a dual signal sensor for the accurate detection of dopamine concentration. The specific adsorption of Cu2+ and Pb2+ ions on a self-assembled monolayer with a PDMS substrate can induce a much higher voltage than those by other ions.142 Using this property, Yoon et al. fabricated liquidâ€“solid TENG that can be used to detect Cu2+ and Pb2+ ions in water (Fig. 9e); in fact, Cu2+ ions can be repeatedly detected after the device is cleaned by HCl solution (Fig. 9f).

4.2. Energy collection unit

Flowing water can be used to drive the motion elements in solidâ€“solid nanogenerators to generate electricity,145â€“151 but the electricity of these solidâ€“solid nanogenerators is generated by solidâ€“solid interactions, which are less efficient. Therefore, it is worthwhile to develop a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator as a more efficient and durable energy collection unit to collect energy from water.

Combining solar energy with the drawing potential, Tang et al. fabricated an all-weather working nanogenerator (Fig. 10a),152 which could effectively generate electricity on sunny and rainy days. Yoo et al. found that previous electricity generation devices combining solar cells and liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators often used nanogenerators as shells.153 However, the low transmittance of nanogenerators results in a decrease in the energy conversion efficiency of solar cells. Inspired by moth's eye with superior specular transmittance, a moth's-eye-mimicking TENG (MM TENG) was fabricated (Fig. 10b), which has better conversion efficiency. This moth's-eye-mimicking structure can also provide the self-cleaning property, which ensures the long-term performance of the MM TENG. Dao et al. fabricated a Limnobium laevigatum-inspired nanogenerator (LLN) motivated by water evaporation, which could float on water for energy supply all day.154 As shown in Fig. 10c, polystyrene foam is used to provide buoyancy as well as block heat transfer between the LLN and cold water, thereby improving the evaporation efficiency. The voltage and current density induced by LLN can reach 1 V and 1650 mA mâˆ’2, respectively. Because the evaporation is a continuous process, LLN can generate electricity even if there is no light at all (Fig. 10d), which provides a strategy for all-weather generating sets. Ahn et al. fabricated a liquidâ€“solid TENG to convert an unsteady flow of energy into electricity.155 The core of this TENG is the â€œcellâ€, which is a tube with two serially connected hydrophobic film tubes. The cell with polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) and silicon is called an sP cell and the cell with a biaxially oriented polypropylene and silicon is called an sB cell (Fig. 10e). When the TENG works in the single-electrode mode, the charge density varies in different areas of the sP cell after the pipe is supplied with water due to the different electron affinities between PVDF and silicon (Fig. 10f). The axial force generated by the liquid flow leads to the axial movement of ions adsorbed on the inner surface of the sP cell, resulting in a changing number of ions on the inner surface of PVDF. EI in the copper electrode generates the current. The same goes for the freestanding mode, and the surface charge of biaxially oriented polypropylene must be the opposite to that of PVDF so that the current generated by the sP cell and sB cell is superimposed, increasing the EGP. Evidently, the unsteady streaming flow can generate a higher current than that of the steady flow, which is consistent with the work of Cheedarala et al.156 Further, Wang et al. added the liquid to a ring-shaped sealed pipe (liquid does not fill the pipe) to fabricate a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator, out of which the Cu electrode was designed into a ring structure, with two electric brushes bilaterally anchored.157 When the pipe rotates, the liquid flows, generating a direct current. In this way, a voltage of several hundred volts can be generated. These experiments demonstrate the potential of tubular liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators for energy harvesting.

5. Discussion

Electricity generation by the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator originates from the directional movement of the charge carrier at the liquidâ€“solid interface. Since the contact area between the liquid and solid is larger, and the liquid is usually readily available, the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator offers better efficiency, better durability, and lower cost than solidâ€“solid nanogenerators. Water energy is a renewable energy source and takes many forms, such as tide, water droplet, wave, flow, moisture, and even waterfall.164 Therefore, using a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator to obtain energy from water is of considerable research, environmental protection, and commercial value. Over the recent years, considerable efforts have been devoted toward improving the EGP of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators, including novel materials,165 optimizations of the triboelectric layer surface,166 theoretical analysis,167,168 and so on. Table 1 summarizes the progress of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators. The EGP of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator varies with the material of the triboelectric layer, liquid source, structure, and solution type. Different nanogenerators have their own pros and cons. In terms of structure, the nanogenerators based on EDL nanogenerators can be divided into those with and without an induction layer. Nanogenerators without an induction layer usually use conductive 2D materials and their derivatives as the triboelectric layer, which have high cost, poor durability, and unstable output, but they have a simplistic structure. It is very convenient to modify 2D materials, which can modulate the output of the nanogenerator. Nanogenerators with an induction layer usually use polymer materials (such as PTFE, PMMA, and nylon) as the triboelectric layer, which are cheaper, more durable, and more stable. The introduction of EI makes the structure of the nanogenerators more complex, which leads to a variety of structures. Different structures are suitable for different occasions, which is beneficial to promote the development of nanogenerators. In terms of the liquid source, the fabrication of nanogenerators driven by the droplet, waving, and flowing are simple, and all of them can generate voltages ranging from tens to even hundreds of volts. However, droplet-driven nanogenerators with an instantaneous output are severely constrained practically by the weather, while waving-driven nanogenerators and flowing-driven nanogenerators placed in oceans and rivers can work continuously, and they are not restricted by the weather. Nanogenerators driven by evaporation or moisture need to regulate the functional groups to cause a gradient in the charge carrier, which is complex to fabricate. They can only generate a voltage with a few volts at the moment, but these two kinds of nanogenerators can generate a constant direct voltage and current, which are better for powering electronic devices.

There are still several problems that need to be resolved for liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators, and some of the important issues are summarized and discussed as follows:

First, the current of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator is mostly concentrated in the range of 0.1 Î¼A to 1 mA, which cannot drive most electronic devices, such as micro-motors. A higher current can considerably expand the applicability of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators. In some studies, with an increase in the resistance of the circuit, the voltage of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator gradually increases. However, this method accordingly reduces the current. At present, there are several methods that can be used to increase the current of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators: (i) increasing the surface charge density of the triboelectric layer, such as using materials with high electron affinity, ion injection,169,170 and surface functional group modification;125 (ii) optimizing the structure of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator;167 (iii) increasing the hydrophobicity of the triboelectric layer, including etching a micro-hierarchical structure and using more hydrophobic materials. For the method described in (i) above, the material used is one of the most important factors that affects the surface charge density, which is related to the tendency of the material to gain or lose electrons, known as the triboelectric series. In order to improve the surface charge density, materialsâ€”which are â€œfarâ€ from the liquid in the triboelectric seriesâ€”should be selected as the triboelectric layer. For example, most 2D materials are near the negative side and MoS2 shows the most negative charging characteristic, which can be widely used to generate electricity.171 A series of studies on the triboelectric series have been done, showing that the triboelectric series is affected by temperature, gas environment, roughness of the material, and type of counter material.56,172,173 Hence, the existing triboelectric series can only provide qualitative guidance. However, polymer materials are an exception, and the position of polymer materials in the different triboelectric series is almost identical. Therefore, nanogenerators that use polymer materials as the dielectric layer are more stable. Besides, our previous work has shown that less than 5% of the surface charge is involved in electricity generation due to the screening effect of ions in the diffusion layer.174 Hence, how to make all the surface charges â€œparticipateâ€ in the electricity generation process and improve the coefficient is also a problem that needs to be resolved. For the method described in (ii) above, since electricity generation is attributed to the coupling effect between CE and EI, it is important to study the effect of the nanogenerator structure on the coupling effect. Wang et al. introduced the ferroelectric material to the structure of the nanogenerator to increase the coupling effect. The hysteretic dielectric polarization of the ferroelectric material can enhance EI, resulting in an increase in the induced charge.175 Several works have suggested that the introduction of a capacitor into the structure of the nanogenerator can increase the output current.176â€“178 In the working cycle of the nanogenerator, charges can be transferred between the capacitor and nanogenerator, which can considerably increase the coupling efficiency of CE and EI. In addition, some studies have shown that the thinner triboelectric layers have higher efficiency of EI.170,179 Wang's work has shown that the water droplet can bridge the originally disconnected components into a closed-loop electrical system, which can generate a voltage of up to 143.5 V and a current of up to 270 Î¼A.99 This is because the CE and EI are â€œperfectlyâ€ coupled at the moment the droplet hits it, causing rapid changes in the distribution of charge on the surface of the device. In addition, a larger contact area of the triboelectric layer can increase the output of the nanogenerator.175,179 Since the liquid can be in full contact with the solid, we can texture the surface to increase the surface area. For the method described in (iii) above, etching a micro-hierarchical structure to increase the hydrophobicity of the materials can prevent liquid residue on the surface of the triboelectric layer and promote the rapid spread of liquid, which is conducive to the rapid change in the charge distribution in the EDL.

Second, the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator usually generates an unstable alternating current, which is hard to store and directly use. Since small electronic devices use a direct current, it makes sense to store the generated energy and release energy in the form of direct current. The rectifier circuit is considered to be a good way to convert an alternating current into a direct current.32,180 Zhang et al. used a kind of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator to charge the capacitor through a rectifier circuit and the charged capacitor could power an LED for more than 300 s.34 However, capacitor discharge is not a stable process, and the voltage decreases gradually during the discharge process of the capacitor, which is not conducive to the normal working of electronic devices. Since the battery can achieve continuous and stable power output, it is worth trying to use the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator to charge the battery. Existing studies on the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator are focused on increasing the voltage, current, and durability, but there is little research on the charging technology of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators. We believe that the use of a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator to charge batteries stably and efficiently will be a hot topic.

Third, the stability of the triboelectric layer is an important factor to ensure the durability of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator. Triboelectric layers made of 2D materials or etched materials are not stable. Our previous verification experiment based on Guo's work28 showed that not all graphene could generate a voltage, which indicated that a graphene-based triboelectric layer was unstable. The movement of liquid on the solid surface can cause damage to the surface structure of the triboelectric layer.12,166 In addition, to improve the EGP of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator, the triboelectric layer is usually very thin, which can be easily damaged by an external force in practice. Although it has been claimed that a self-repairable triboelectric layer has been realized,49 the repair of the nanogenerator requires heating, which would incur additional costs in future commercialization. Therefore, improving the mechanical strength of the triboelectric layer seems like a better strategy. Recently, Wang et al. obtained a surface with a stable superhydrophobic property by dressing superhydrophobic nanostructures in armor.181 Even after abrasion by sandpaper or by a steel blade, the superhydrophobic property of the surface is retained, which promotes the development of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators. Searching and designing the triboelectric layer with high hydrophobicity, high wear resistance, high mechanical strength, and high electron affinity is the focus of future studies.

Finally, most of the existing studies are in the small-scale and high-cost laboratory stage. It is expensive and inefficient to manually build a large-scale liquidâ€“solid-nanogenerator-based generating set. Automatic production lines can cheaply produce materials used as the triboelectric layer as well as efficiently fabricate commercial liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators, which can significantly reduce the cost per unit. Therefore, the industrialization of commercial nanogenerators (including materials and production mode) is of great significance. However, the industrialization of a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator is a complex and systematic project that necessitates cooperation among various fields.

6. Conclusion and prospect

In summary, liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators are based on the charge carrier behavior at the liquidâ€“solid interface. According to the form of water, nanogenerators can harvest energy from droplets, waving, flowing, evaporation, and moisture. Various liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators have shown great potential in sensors, energy collection units, self-powered microelectromechanical systems, and wearable equipment. However, there are still certain problems in liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators, such as low current, difficulty in using and storing electricity, and difficulty in industrialization. After resolving existing problems, the application prospect of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators would be limitless and amazing. For example, in modern oil drilling, sensors at the bit are often powered by lithium batteries. However, the lifespan of the lithium battery is considerably shortened due to the high temperature and pressure at the bit. If the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator can be installed inside the bit, the high-pressure drilling fluid can drive the nanogenerator that would eventually power the sensors. In fishery, flowing-induced nanogenerators can be attached to the outside of the boat. As the boat moves, the flowing water can drive the nanogenerators to generate electricity. In terms of daily life, with the development of the Internet of Things, smart homes will be rapidly popularized within 20 years. A combination of liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators and solar cells installed on the roof as an all-weather power source would be a great way to power electronics in smart homes. Evaporation-induced nanogenerators can estimate the amount of sunlight in a room based on the current (stronger sunlight promotes evaporation) and automatically control lighting or curtains in the room. With regard to health management, wearable devices based on a liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator can be used for the detection of vital signs. For example, the device can automatically notify the hospital and family when the current pulse caused by the heartbeat is abnormal, which can facilitate the timely treatment of patients. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the liquidâ€“solid nanogenerator toward fluids makes it possible to be widely used in the field of microfluidic control, and a large-scale series and parallel generating set based on liquidâ€“solid nanogenerators can collect ocean blue energy and reduce carbon dioxide emissions from fossil fuels, which can better protect our homeâ€”the Earth.
