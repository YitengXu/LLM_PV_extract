Rational design and applications of highly efficient reaction systems photocatalyzed by noble metal nanoparticle-loaded titanium(IV) dioxide

Hiroaki Tada*a, Tomokazu Kiyonagaa and Shin-ichi Nayab
aDepartment of Applied Chemistry, School of Science and Engineering, Kinki University, 3-4-1, Kowakae, Higashi-Osaka, Osaka, 577-8502, Japan

Received 12th December 2008

First published on 8th April 2009

Abstract

The fundamentals and applications of TiO2 photocatalysis have been extensively studied in recent decades, the central theme being increasing reaction efficiency. A guideline for achieving so-called reasonable delivery photocatalytic reaction systems (RDPRSs) has been presented. This tutorial review summarizes recent developments in the RDPRS-based design of highly efficient photocatalytic reactions by noble metal nanoparticle-loaded TiO2 (M/TiO2). After introducing the conditions required for RDPRSs, the key factors affecting the photocatalytic activity, including Fermi energy in the photostationary state, the metal particle size effect, and solvent effects are discussed. We then describe selected applications of RDPRSs for the conversion of light energy to chemical energy, environmental purification, and low-temperature cleaning of sulfur-poisoned metal catalysts. Future applications of this idea to visible-light photocatalysts, which are currently the subject of intensive research, may lead to feasible green and efficient photocatalytic reaction systems using sunlight as an energy source.

1. Introduction

Since the discovery of the Honda-Fujishima effect,1 TiO2 photocatalysts have attracted much interest as a key material for the decomposition of harmful organic pollutants2 and the synthesis of useful compounds3 by taking advantage of its excellent redox power, and for applications in self-cleaning and anti-fogging coatings owing to its photoinduced superhydrophilicity.4 Generally, in TiO2-photocatalyzed reactions, reduction and oxidation take place concurrently at adjacent reaction sites on the surface, which causes an inherent drawback the recombination of photogenerated charge carriers. The major challenge in this area, in common with the heterogeneous photocatalysis, is therefore to improve the activity by restricting the recombination. For this purpose, the excited electrons in the conduction band (c.b.) of TiO2 must be separated spatially from the holes in the valence band (v.b.), because the characteristic time required for reduction (1 ms) is longer than that for oxidation (100 ns) by a factor of as much as 10000.5 A very promising approach is incorporation of noble metal nanoparticles (NPs) with large work function (i.e., high electronegativity) on TiO2,6,7 which enhances the charge separation due to the interfacial electron transfer from semiconductors to metals.8 Particularly, in view of organic synthesis, selectivity is of great importance, in addition to activity. In order to further increase the efficiency of photocatalytic reactions, we have previously presented the concept of reasonable delivery photocatalytic reaction systems (RDPRSs) shown in Scheme 1, in which M denotes a noble metal NP.9 The first condition is that the oxidation sites (TiO2) and reduction sites (M) should be separated. The second condition is that reductants and oxidants should be supplied abundantly and selectively to the oxidation sites and on the reduction sites, respectively. The third condition is that the product re-adsorption on the photocatalyst surface should be inhibited. Simultaneous satisfaction of these three conditions should lead to highly efficient photocatalytic reaction systems.
Scheme 1 A schematic representation of RDPRSs.

This tutorial review summarizes recent developments in the RDPRS-based design of highly efficient photocatalytic systems. Section 2 presents the fundamental conditions of RDPRS, followed by the key issues of the M/TiO2 photocatalysis: the Fermi energy of M/TiO2 in the photostationary state, the metal particle size effect, and solvent effects. Section 3 describes the applications of RDPRSs in the conversion of light energy to chemical energy, environmental purification, and low-temperature cleaning of sulfur-poisoned metal catalysts.

The remarkable loading effect of Au and Ag NPs on the photocatalytic activity of TiO2 can be summarized as follows. The electrons excited from the v.b. of TiO2 to the c.b. are efficiently transferred to Au or Ag NPs (charge separation effect) and accumulated therein (electron pool effect). The oxidant (PySSPy) and the reductant (H2O) are selectively and abundantly supplied to the reduction sites (Au or Ag) and oxidation sites (TiO2), respectively (reasonable delivery effect). The electron pool effect of metal NPs particularly exerts its power in the multi-electron transfer process. The inhibition of the product re-adsorption on the photocatalyst surface also contributes to the increases in activity and selectivity of the reaction (inhibition of product re-adsorption).

2.2 Fermi energy of M/TiO2 in the photostationary state

In RDPRSs, if the Fermi energy of the metal NPs loaded on TiO2 in the photostationary state (EF) is known, it should be very useful in the design of photocatalytic organic synthesis. Since the EF dictates the photovoltage in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), it is of great importance also in DSSCs.16 In the pioneering work on n-TiO2 electrodes covered with discontinuous Au films, Nakato et al. showed that the photopotential of n-TiO2 is not in agreement with that of Au, while both the potentials cathodically shift.17 This study provides important information about the mechanism of action of the metal loading on semiconductor photocatalysts. However, most of the photocatalytic reactions were carried out in particulate systems. Recently, the research group of Kamat has determined the Fermi energy of irradiated Au/TiO2 particles in organic solvents using a C60/C60 redox pair.18 Thus, organic synthesis using water as a green solvent will increasingly become important from the viewpoint of environmental conservation. The EF value of Au/TiO2 in water is determinable by utilizing the photoinduced sulfur desorption [eqn (2)].19

S8 as well as PySSPy is selectively adsorbed on the surface of Au NPs (adsorption selectivity >99.6%).20 As shown in Fig. 3, the sulfur adsorbed on Au/TiO2 is efficiently reduced to S2 to be desorbed into water at room temperature with irradiation. The ratio of sulfur desorbed from S-Au/TiO2 dispersed in water (X) was measured at various mean Au particle sizes (d). In every sample, the X value abruptly increased on irradiation, reaching a constant value at tp > 20 min. When the suspension containing 0.2 vol% TiO2 particles was irradiated from the upper side of the reaction vessel, light absorption by TiO2 particles was limited to approximately 1 mm of the suspension because the absorption coefficient of TiO2 in the UV region is about 104 cm1. Since Au/TiO2 particles are continuously moving throughout the reaction vessel due to stirring and convection, part of the sulfur desorbed as S2 ions would be readsorbed on the Au surfaces of Au/TiO2 below this region. 2.3 Metal particle size effect

Au NPs loaded on metal-oxide supports have been revealed to exhibit high activities for important chemical reactions, whereas bulk Au is chemically inert.21 The most intriguing point in the thermally activated catalysis of Au NPs is the strong dependence of the activity on its particle size, e.g., the activity for low-temperature oxidation of carbon monoxide reaches a maximum at d 3 nm.22 We have recently found a quite different Au particle size-dependence of the photocatalytic activity of Au/TiO2 for RDPRS when the Au loading amount (x) maintained constant.19

As a first test reaction, the Au/TiO2-photocatalyzed reduction of PySSPy to PySH [eqn (1)] was carried out.10Fig. 6A shows plots of the initial rate of reaction (v0) vs.d, where the v0 value for d = 0 is that for the TiO2 system: x = 0.44 mass%. The photocatalytic activity significantly increases as a result of the Au NP loading. The v0 value increases with increasing d in spite of the decrease in the Au surface area. Au/TiO2-photocatalyzed 6e-reduction of nitrobenzene (NB) to aniline (AN) also belonging to RDPRS was performed as a second test reaction.23 The addition of CH3OH as a hole scavenger causes for the NB reduction to proceed,14 whereas the reduction hardly occurred in water for both the TiO2 and Au/TiO2 systems. Fig. 6B shows the electronic absorption spectral change of the reaction solution containing 0.1 mol dm3 CH3OH with UV-light irradiation in the presence of Au/TiO2 with x = 0.44 mass%. Irradiation yields a new absorption around 230 nm due to AN, accompanied by a decrease in the absorption of NB centered at 270 nm. Since isosbestic points are situated at 210, 220, and 245 nm, the 6e-reduction of NB to AN proceeds almost selectively also in this case [eqn (5)].

In the absorption spectra, Au/TiO2 has a broad absorption around 550 nm due to the surface plasmon resonance of Au NPs along with a strong absorption band below 385 nm due to the TiO2 interband transition. In contrast, in the PL spectrum of TiO2, a broad emission band is present in the range from 380 to 700 nm. The main band at 424 nm (B1) can be assigned to the bandband PL. Also, a sharp band at 470 nm (B2) is attributed to the emission from vacancy levels. Further, a band at 575 nm (B3) may result from impurity levels because pure TiO2 does not have this PL band. There are two kinds of electron-trap sites in the TiO2 particles used in this study. When Au NPs are loaded on TiO2, the relative PL intensity decreases, although the spectral profile is invariant. A similar trend has recently been reported also for the Ag/TiO2 particles.25 Interestingly, the decreasing degree of the PL intensity for Au(d = 13 nm)/TiO2 is greater than that for Au(d = 4.2 nm)/TiO2. The PL intensity is thought to decrease with the AuNP loading because of the reduction in the recombination due to the interfacial electron transfer from TiO2 to Au. Further, the efficiency of the electron transfer is suggested to increase as the d value increases in the range below 13 nm.

As a result of AuNP loading on TiO2, two significant changes are apparent. One is the increase in the ratio of c2 to c1, and another is the decrease in 1 and 2. Also, these tendencies are more pronounced in Au(d = 13 nm)/TiO2. Fig. 7C shows PL spectra of TiO2, Au(d = 4.2 nm, x = 0.44 mass%)/TiO2, and Au(d = 13 nm, x = 0.44 mass%)/TiO2 delayed by 25 ms. Each spectrum has a broad PL band at > 450 nm, of which peak position is close to that of B3 in Fig. 7A. The direct electron-hole recombination is an ultrafast event, occurring at 1 ps.26 Accordingly, B2 and B3 are tentatively assignable to the shorter-lived PL from shallow trap sites at ca. 0.6 eV below the c.b. and the longer-life PL from deep trap sites at ca. 1.0 eV below the c.b., respectively. A new path of the excited electron transfer from TiO2 to Au explains the decrease in the PL life time with the AuNP loading.

In this manner, the same conclusion can be drawn from the kinetic analysis of the PL spectra that the efficiency of the interfacial electron transfer from TiO2 to Au increases as the d value increases. This is further consistent with the experimental results that the EF value increases to improve the photocatalytic activity for the Au/TiO2-photocatalzyed reduction with increasing d. Reducing the size of the metal particle at a given loading amount necessarily increases its number density. It was previously shown by solving the 2D Poisson equation for M/TiO2 that the metal NPs produce the electric field at the metalTiO2 interface to enhance the charge separation.27 However, when too many metal particles are loaded on a single TiO2 particle, the charge separation effect is rather impaired due to the overlap of the interfacial electric field. This reasonably explains the conclusion drawn from the PL measurements that the efficiency of the interfacial electron transfer from TiO2 to Au NPs increases with increasing d in the range 3.0 d 13 nm.

3. Applications of RDPRS

3.1 Conversion of light energy to chemical energy

Semiconductor photocatalysis is also of interest and importance in view of energy storage of light energy in a chemical form. The application of the RDPRS idea enables one to achieve a highly energy-efficient up-hill photosynthetic reaction (G > 0) [eqn (8)].28
(8)
Fig. 9A shows time profiles for the photoinduced reaction of S8 with varying initial concentrations (C0) in the presence of TiO2 and Au(0.46 mass%)/TiO2 at 298 K. The decrease in the S8 concentration with adsorption in the dark was negligible under these conditions. When a deaerated 1.72 104 mol dm3 solution of S8 in ethanol solution containing TiO2 was irradiated, the concentration slowly decreased with increasing tp. With a low level of Au loading (a), the reaction is drastically enhanced, and the S8 completely disappears within 15 min at C0 < 1.72 104 mol dm3 (bd). Regardless of x and C0, the zero-order rate law holds for the reaction, of which the rate constant, k, increases with the Au loading by a factor of as much as 9.4, being very weakly dependent on C0 in the Au/TiO2 system. Both the irradiation and TiO2 (or Au/TiO2) were necessary for the reaction to occur. In the electronic absorption spectrum of aqueous NaOH solutions trapping the H2S evolved during the reaction, an absorption peak appeared at 230 nm due to S2 ions. The ratio of the number of moles of S2 ions generated to that of S8 consumed was ca. 8 irrespective of tp, indicating that S8 is stoichiometrically reduced to H2S [eqn (8)]. Also, the generation of CH3CHO was confirmed as an oxidation product in the gas phase by gas chromatography. Fig. 9B shows the TOF for the Au/TiO2 photosynthetic reduction of S8 as a function of d with C0 = 1.72 104 mol dm3. The TOF significantly increases with increasing d for 3 < d < 12 nm, which is also ascribable to the increase in EF with increasing d.

Most of the azo dyes used widely in the textile industries are carcinogenic and resistant to bacterial degradation, thus requiring effective wastewater treatment. The Pt/TiO2-photocatalyzed 4e-reduction of azobenzene (AB) to aniline (AN) via hydrazobenzene (HAB) proceeds effectively [eqn (11)].34

Fig. 10A shows the variation of the concentrations of AB and products in the presence of TiO2 as a function of tp, with H2OC2H5OH (9/1 v/v) as solvent. AB is slowly reduced to HAB with a selectivity of 97% at 0 < tp < 3 h. Since the TOF is calculated to be ca. 3 at tp = 3 h, this reaction can be regarded as photocatalytic. Fig. 10B shows time courses of the concentrations of AB and products with irradiation in the presence of Pt/TiO2. A drastic increase in the rate of reduction of AB to HAB is observed as a result of the Pt loading [conversion ca. 100%, selectivity (HAB) = 91% at tp = 1 h]. Noticeably, further reduction of HAB to AN occurs [selectivity (HAB) = 19% at tp = 3 h]. In the absorption spectra, the n* band of AB completely disappeared at tp = 1 h, and new absorption bands appeared at 280 and 230 nm at 3 h that are in accordance with the positions for the n* and * bands, respectively. Product analysis by gas chromatography confirmed the generation of CH3CHO and CO2; evidently, C2H5OH acts as a reductant. The adsorption amount of AB increased with loading Pt from 4.3 107 mol g1 for TiO2 to 2.6 106 mol g1 at an equilibrium concentration of 4.0 105 mol dm3, whereas the degree of C2H5OH adsorption was essentially invariant. This finding indicates that AB and C2H5OH are preferentially adsorbed on Pt and TiO2 surfaces of Pt/TiO2, respectively. The interaction between AB and Pt would involve both -bonding [ orbital (AB) d orbital (Pt)] and -backbonding [d orbitals (Pt) * orbital (AB)].

These results clarified the two effects of Pt loading; one is to increase the rate of 2e-reduction (AB HAB) and the other is to progress 4e-reduction (AB AN). Pt loading enhances the adsorption of AB and the charge separation of electron-hole pairs. In addition, selective adsorption of the oxidant (AB) on the reduction sites (Pt) and the reductant (C2H5OH) on oxidation sites (TiO2) is confirmed. Further, the larger solubility into water of AN than that of AB would restrict re-adsorption of the product. In this manner, the remarkable increase in the reduction rate can be explained in terms of the RDPRS criteria. Also, inspection of Figs. 10A and B suggest that Pt loading leads to the absence of an induction period. The interfacial multi-electron transfer is assisted by an electron-pool effect of Pt with a large work function. At present, degradation of azo- and thiazine dyes are frequently targeted for water purification and used as a model pollutant for the activity evaluation of photocatalysts.2 Pt/WO3 has recently been found to exhibit high photocatalytic activity for the decomposition of organic compounds under visible light irradiation, whereas the activity of WO3 is very low.35 This is a good example of the semiconductor photocatalyst coupled with metal NPs acting as a promoter for multi-electron transfer.

3.3 Low-temperature cleaning of S-poisoned metal catalysts

The catalytic activity of Au NPs strongly depends on not only the Au particle size but also the kinds of support, e.g., among various metal oxides tested, the anatase form of TiO2 was shown to be the best support for the Au nanoparticle catalyst in the low-temperature oxidation of carbon monoxide.36 On the other hand, because of the great affinity of Au to S, gold can undergo sulfur-poisoning from sulfur contained in the feed on the ppm order. Heat cleaning is usually employed to clean the surfaces of sulfur-poisoned metal NPs; however, the high-temperature heating causes a reduction in the catalytic activity due to sintering and surface oxidation. Photoinduced sulfur desorption [eqn (2)] can also be used as a low-temperature method for cleaning metal surfaces, which enables reactivation of the catalysts without deterioration.37

A FE-SEM image of Au-loaded TiO2 films is shown in Fig. 11A, indicating that Au nanoparticles (d = 6.9 nm) are deposited on the TiO2 film in a highly dispersed state by the DP method under the same conditions as in the particulate system (Au/TiO2-P) (Fig. 1A).

Fig. 11B shows the photoinduced sulfur desorption ratio (X) for the Au/TiO2-TF (open reaction system) and Au/TiO2-P systems (closed reaction system) at pH 7 as a function of tp. The X value for the thin film system was calculated using the surface S- and Au-atomic concentrations determined by the XPS analysis. The photoinduced sulfur desorption in the thin film system proceeds much more effectively than the particulate system. In the former, the X value reaches ca. 75% at tp = 10 min, whereas 1 h is necessary for approximately half of the sulfurs to be desorbed in the latter. Also, it is noteworthy in the particulate system that the X value saturates near 50% at tp > 1 h. On the other hand, in the thin film system, subsequent to an initial abrupt rise in X, the sulfur desorption is almost completed after the prolonged irradiation. In this open reaction system, the sulfur (once desorbed from the sample surface) should be promptly removed by the flowing water. Another requisite for the cleaning method is that the Au particle size should be maintained after the treatment because it strongly affects the catalytic activity of Au NPs. Fig. 11C and D show FE-SEM images of the photocleaned and heat-cleaned Au/TiO2TF samples, respectively. The size of the Au particles hardly changes after 10 min irradiation, whereas it increases up to as large as 37 nm as a result of the heating at 773 K. These results indicate that this phenomenon could be applied to provide to a low-temperature method for cleaning sulfur-poisoned Au/TiO2 (photo)catalysts something that has actually been confirmed to be effective in the Au/TiO2-catalyzed oxidation of benzyl alcohol to benzyl aldehyde.15 The rate of the photoinduced sulfur desorption for Pt/TiO2 is larger than that for Au/TiO2,38 and thus this method may also be applicable to the re-activation of Pt particle catalysts.

Conclusions

An idea for designing photocatalytic reaction systems called reasonable delivery photocatalytic reaction systems (RDPRSs) has been presented. The synergy of the charge separation and electron pool effects of noble metal NPs (M), the reasonable delivery effect, and inhibition of product re-adsorption can lead to highly active and selective M/TiO2-photocatalyzed multi-electron redox reactions. Also, the metal particle size and the polarity of the solvent are shown to be important parameters affecting the photocatalytic activity of M/TiO2 through the tuning of the Fermi energy of metal NPs in the photostationary state. Future work applying the RDPRS idea to visible light photocatalysts may enable the development of green and highly efficient photocatalytic reaction systems using sunlight as an energy source.
