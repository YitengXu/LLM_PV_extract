Abstract

A high porous molybdenum sulfideâ€“carbon (MoS2â€“C) hybrid film was prepared by using an in situ hydrothermal route. The MoS2â€“C hybrid film served as a low-cost and high efficient platinum-free counter electrode for a dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC). The cyclic voltammetry, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy and Tafel curve analysis indicate that the MoS2â€“C electrode possesses low charge transfer resistance on the electrolyteâ€“electrode interface, high electrocatalytic activity and fast reaction kinetics for the reduction of triiodide to iodide at the counter electrode, which is due to large specific surface area and special structure and compositions of MoS2â€“C film. A DSSC with the novel MoS2â€“C counter electrode achieve a high power conversion efficiency of 7.69% under standard light illumination, which exceeds that of the DSSC with a Pt counter electrode (6.74%).

Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have attracted significant attention in the past decades as a promising renewable energy source due to their relatively high efficiency, environmental friendliness, simple fabrication process and low cost.1â€“4 A DSSC based on traditional platinum (Pt) counter electrode (CE), cobalt(II/III) electrolyte and zinc porphyrin dye has achieved a power conversion efficiency over 12%.4 However, although Pt has high conductivity, catalytic activity and chemical stability, it is one of the most expensive components in DSSCs.2,3 In addition, the Pt can be decomposed to PtI4 in traditional Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox couple electrolyte, which will affect the long-term stability of the DSSC.5,6 Thus, it is necessary to develop an economic, stable, effective and Pt-free CE. Many materials, such as carbon materials,7â€“9 conducting polymers,10â€“12 and inorganic materials including transition metal sulfides, nitrides and carbides,13â€“16 have been attempted to replace the Pt as CE in DSSCs.

As a typical layered transition metal sulfide, molybdenum sulfide (MoS2) is composed of three atom layers: a Mo layer sandwiched between two S layers, and the triple layers are stacked and held together by weak van der Waals interactions.17 Due to its analogous structure to graphene, MoS2 has attracted great interests and was proposed as a potential CE candidate in DSSCs. MoS2 is extensively applied as solid lubricants and spent hydrodesulfurization catalysts.18â€“20 However, only a few reports focused on the application of MoS2 as the CE in DSSCs. Very recently, Wu et al.21 employed MoS2 and tungsten sulfide (WS2) as CE materials for DSSCs and showed high photovoltaic efficiencies of 7.59% and 7.73%, respectively. On the other hand, hybrids of inorganic compounds and carbonaceous materials maybe can improve the electrocatalytic property of the CEs. Li et al.22 reported a composite CE composed of TiN with high electrocatalytic activity and carbon black with good electrical conductivity, and an improved photovoltaic performance of DSSC was obtained. Wu et al.23 reported high photovoltaic performance when MoC and WC particles were embedded in ordered nanomesoporous carbon. Also, Lin et al.24 demonstrated enhanced performance of a MoS2â€“graphene composite catalytic film prepared by simply mixing graphene oxide nanosheets with MoS2. In these works, the electrocatalytic activity of metal carbides, nitrides, and sulfide is improved due to the fast electron-transport network provided by the carbon-based materials.

Here, a high porous molybdenum sulfideâ€“carbonaceous (MoS2â€“C) hybrid material was synthesized by a hydrothermal route. The hybrid was characterized by scanning electron microscopy, cyclic voltammetry, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, and Tafel measurements. The results indicate that the MoS2â€“C hybrid possesses large specific surface area and excellent electrocatalytic activity. A DSSC based on the MoS2â€“C hybrid CE significantly improved its photovoltaic performance.

Experimental

Materials and reagents

Sodium molybdate (Na2MoO4Â·2H2O), thiourea, polyethylene glycol with average molecular weight of 2000 (PEG 2000), glucose, N-methyl-2-pyrrolidinone (NMP), isopropanol, 4-tert-butyl-pyridine (TBP), acetonitrile and titanium tetrachloride (TiCl4) were purchased from Shanghai Chemical Agent Ltd, China. The sensitized dye N719 [RuL2(NCS)2Â·2TBA, L = 2,2-bipyridyl-4,4-dicarboxylic acid, TBA = tetrabutylammonium] was obtained from Solaronix SA (Switzerland). All reagents are of analytical reagent grade and used without further purification. FTO glass substrates (8 Î© cmâˆ’2, Hartford Glass Co., USA) were cut into 2.5 Ã— 1.5 cm2 carefully and ultrasonically cleaned sequentially in detergent, acetone and distilled water for 10 min each and then stored in isopropyl alcohol.

Synthesis of MoS2â€“C hybrid

4.84 g Na2MoO4Â·2H2O, 6.12 g thiourea and 0.5 g PEG 2000 were dissolved in 120 ml distilled water, followed by ultrasonication and stirring for 30 min until a clear and homogeneous solution was achieved. After that, 0.5 g, 1.0 g, 1.5 g, 2.0 g or 2.5 g of glucose was added into the reaction solution, followed by further sonication for another 30 min before being transferred into a 150 ml Teflon-lined autoclave, and then heated in an oven at 200 Â°C for 48 h without intentional control of ramping or cooling rate. The product was collected by filtration and washed with ethanol and distilled water at least 5 times, and dried in a vacuum oven at 100 Â°C for 24 h. The above black precipitates were the final conductivity active materials, which were denoted as MoS2-1.13-C, MoS2-2.23-C, MoS2-3.30-C, MoS2-4.35-C and MoS2-5.38-C. The values 1.13, 2.23, 3.30, 4.35, and 5.38 represent the weight ratio of carbon in all mixed reactants.

Preparation of counter electrodes

Typically, the MoS2â€“C CE was prepared by a slurry coating procedure on the cleaned FTO substrate. The slurry consisting of 80 wt% MoS2â€“C hybrids, 10 wt% acetylene black and 10 wt% polyvinylidene fluoride grinded together and then was dissolved in NMP. Then the slurry was further sonicated for another 30 min, and kept stirring for 12 h. Subsequently, the as-prepared slurry was coated on the FTO substrates using a doctor blade method. In our experiments, the thickness for the CE film was controlled by the transparent adhesive tape layers (one layer thickness of transparent adhesive tape is about 4 Î¼m). The electrodes were dried at 100 Â°C for 24 h in a vacuum oven.

Fabrication of TiO2 anode and DSSC

A TiO2 anode was prepared as described previously.25,26 In brief, a thin TiO2 blocking layer was deposited on the FTO substrate by immersing the FTO in 0.15 M TiCl4 isopropanol solution for 12 h, followed by sintering at 450 Â°C for 30 min in air. Subsequently, a TiO2 layer with a particle size of 10â€“20 nm was coated onto the blocking layer by using the doctor blade method, and then sintered at 450 Â°C for 30 min in air. The resultant TiO2 film was sensitized by immersing the TiO2 film in a 0.3 mM dye N719 ethanol solution for 24 h. Thus a dye-sensitized TiO2 photoanode with thickness of 6â€“8 Î¼m was obtained. After that, the photoanode and a CE were clipped together and wrapped with thermoplastic hot-melt Surlyn. The DSSC was fabricated by injecting the liquid electrolyte (0.05 M of I2, 0.1 M of LiI, and 0.6 M of tetrabutylammonium iodide and 0.5 M of TBP in acetonitrile) in the aperture between the dye-sensitized TiO2 electrode and the CE. The active area of the DSSC was 0.4 Ã— 0.5 cm2.

Characterization and measurements

The micromorphologies of the MoS2â€“C hybrids were observed by using a JSM-7600F field emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM). The morphology of the samples was observed using a JEM-2100 (JEOL, Japan) transmission electron microscope (TEM) equipped with EDS to measure the elements contained in the samples. The compositions of samples were studied using a glancing incident X-ray diffractometer (XRD 6000, Shimadzu Corporation, Japan). The 2Î¸ angular regions between 10Â° and 80Â° were investigated at a scan rate of 4Â° minâˆ’1 with a step of 0.02. The specific surface area (BET) was measured using a JW-K analyzer by nitrogen absorption. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement was implemented, in which an as-prepared CE was taken as the working electrode, one 4 cm2 of Pt sheet as auxiliary electrode and one Pt wire as reference electrode in the acetonitrile solution consisting of 10 mM of LiI, 1 mM of I2, and 0.1 M of LiClO4 in a three-electrode one-compartment cell. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurement was carried out with two identical electrodes, sealed with thermoplastic hot-melt Surlyn and leaving an exposed area of 0.64 cm2. The electrolyte used in the DSSC tests was also injected into the cells. The EIS tests were carried out simulating open-circuit conditions at ambient atmosphere by using an electrochemical measurement system (CHI660D, Shanghai Chenhua Device Company, China) at a constant temperature of 20 Â°C with AC signal amplitude of 20 mV in the frequency range from 0.1 to 105 Hz at 0 V DC bias in the dark. The complex nonlinear least square analyses of the Nyquist plots were analyzed with the ZSimDemo version 3.30d. The spectral response of the DSSC was determined by measuring the wavelength dependence of the incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) by focusing light from a xenon lamp through a monochromator (PXJ43B11, Japan) onto the cell.

The photovoltaic testing of the DSSC was carried out by measuring the photocurrentâ€“photovoltage (Jâ€“V) characteristic curve under white light irradiation of 100 mW cmâˆ’2 (AM1.5) from a solar simulator (XQ-500W, Shanghai Photoelectricity Device Company, China) in ambient atmosphere. Fig. 8 shows the IPCE curves of the DSSCs based on the Pt, MoS2 and MoS2â€“C CEs. The IPCE of the DSSCs with these CEs have similar photoelectric response spectra in the 300â€“750 nm range; the strong photoelectric response of IPCEs at 340 nm was mainly due to the direct band gap photo-electron excitation of TiO2.36 The IPCE of the DSSC using the MoS2 CE has its highest photoelectric response of 44.6% at 520 nm, which is lower than that of the DSSC using the Pt CE (60.9%). It is apparent that the DSSC using the highly porous MoS2â€“C CE exhibits the highest photoelectric response of 67.3% at 520 nm, which is 10.5% higher than that of the DSSC based on the Pt CE, which may result in a higher Jsc than that of Pt CE.

Conclusions

A molybdenum sulfideâ€“carbon (MoS2â€“C) hybrid material was synthesized by using an in situ hydrothermal route, and was used as a Pt-free counter electrode in a dye-sensitized solar cell. The morphology observation, cyclic voltammetry characterization, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy analysis and Tafel curve measurements indicate that the hybrid electrode possesses highly porous structure, large specific surface area, low charge transfer resistance at the electrolyteâ€“electrode interface, good electrocatalytic activity and fast reaction kinetics for the Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox reaction, which is due to the synergistic effect of combining carbon and MoS2. Under optimal conditions, the solar cell based on the MoS2â€“C counter electrode achieved a high power conversion efficiency of 7.69% under simulated solar light illumination at 100 mW cmâˆ’2, which exceeds that of the solar cell based on a Pt counter electrode (6.74%). These results demonstrates the feasibility of using low-cost transition metal sulfideâ€“carbon hybrid materials as Pt-free counter electrodes in dye-sensitized solar cells.
