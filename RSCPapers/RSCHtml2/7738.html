Abstract

In this study, we investigated the potential of CsPbI3 as an absorber material to be used in perovskite solar cells (PSCs). To optimize the device, we used TiO2 as the electron transport layer and copper barium thiostannate (CBTS) as the hole transport layer in the CsPbI3-based PSC, and employed SCAPS-1D software. We initially tested 10 different back metal contacts (BMCs) to identify the most suitable one for the primary device. After optimization of the BMC, the best-optimized device structure, ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni, achieved a power conversion efficiency of 17.91%. We then evaluated the impact of the absorber thickness, acceptor density, and defect density on the device performance. We also analyzed the effect of changing the thickness, charge-carrier density, and defect density of the CsPbI3, TiO2, and CBTS layers, as well as the interfacial defect densities at the CBTS/CsPbI3 and CsPbI3/TiO2 interfaces, to further optimize device performance. This resulted in an improved efficiency of 19.06% for the ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni device with HTL, compared to 18.17% without HTL. We also analyzed the impacts of operating temperature, series resistance, and shunt resistance on the final optimized device performance, as well as its capacitance–voltage, generation and recombination rate, current density–voltage (J–V), and quantum efficiency (QE) features. The results of these simulations provide valuable insights for the experimental fabrication of an efficient CsPbI3-based inorganic PSC.

1. Introduction

Perovskite materials have the potential to be used in high-performance photovoltaic (PV) solar cells and are receiving huge interest from the scientific community.1–5 Organic–inorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have achieved significant advancements in device construction and element control due to their superior optical absorption, low trap density, extended charge-carrier lifetime, high charge-carrier mobility, and low exciton binding energy.6–8 However, the primary challenges for their extensive commercial uses are the volatility and thermally instability of the organic cations.8–16 Conversely, inorganic CsPbX3 (X = Br, I) perovskites have remarkable compositional stability, and therefore, they can greatly contribute to the development of solar cells.17 Especially, CsPbI3 has attracted considerable interest among the inorganic halide perovskites due to its enhanced thermal stabilities and suitable band gap (∼1.7 eV) for application in highly-effective tandem solar cells.18 Over the last several years, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of CsPbI3-based PSC has increased from 2.9% to more than 19%.19,20 Black phases of CsPbI3 are structurally unstable and rapidly deteriorate to the non-perovskite yellow δ-phase at ambient temperature.19,21,22 It has been reported that the reduction of the grain size and doping stabilizes the CsPbI3 in the black phase by utilizing surface energy or changing the tolerance factor. Between them, doping is a widely-adopted method in which suitable element ions are introduced into the CsPbI3 host lattices to modify the perovskite lattice structure to improve the PV performance with various electron transport layers (ETLs) and hole transport layers (HTLs).23–25

The ETL extracts electrons from the perovskite layer and blocks the electron–hole recombination.26,27 A thick ETL may reduce recombination, but the high series resistance may hamper electron transport. Several solar cell designs have been made without an ETL; however, their PCEs are poor compared to the planar PSCs with an ETL.28,29 As of right now, among several metal oxides, TiO2 is commonly regarded to be the preferable ETL for PSCs. This is because its conduction band (CB) is usually better matched with the perovskite, and as a result, it has a high capacity for charge transfer and collection. Besides, TiO2 is a wide bandgap semiconductor that is often used as an ETL in PSCs. It is known for its high electron mobility, stability, and transparency in the visible light range. Its high electron mobility allows it to effectively transport electrons from the perovskite layer to the electrode, thus increasing the overall efficiency of the device. However, the use of TiO2 ETL in PSCs is restricted due to some drawbacks, such as poor conductivity and a high number of defects, which inevitably result in carrier recombination.30–32 To enhance the PSC efficiency, TiO2 ETLs have been the focus of several initiatives such as doping, nanostructure formation, and interface engineering.33–37

The HTL collects photo-generated holes from perovskite and delivers them to the cathode in both n–i–p and p–i–n architectures. It has a strong correlation with the device's functionality and long-term stability.38,39 P-type chemical compounds, including 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis, poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA), poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) polystyrene sulphonate (PEDOT:PSS), and poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA) has been used constantly as the HTL layer in extremely competitive PSCs. However, these organic compounds are costly due to difficult synthesis processes and high purity demands, and they are particularly prone to deterioration in moisture and air. Organic HTL materials cannot maintain a long-lasting superior efficiency because they are chemically harmful to perovskite absorbers, such as acidic and hygroscopic PEDOT:PSS. The need for alternate inorganic HTL materials was prompted by the problems brought on by the usage of organic materials. The mobility and band alignment of the HTL with the perovskite absorber along with material stability and chemical compatibility should be considered while choosing the HTL, as these aspects are crucial for defining hole collection/extraction and carrier separation/injection, which are directly related in controlling the short circuit current (JSC) and open circuit voltage (VOC). These findings imply that there is a pressing need to investigate novel inorganic semiconductors that can serve as reliable and effective HTL materials. Cu2BaSnS4 (CBTS) is known for its high hole mobility, stability, and transparency in the visible light range. Its high hole mobility allows it to effectively transport holes from the perovskite layer to the electrode, thus increasing the overall efficiency of the device. Besides, CBTS has more advantageous defect features for effective PV and photo-electrochemical solar energy conversion due to its noncentrosymmetric crystalline structure and substantial atomic size changes between the constituent cations.40 Its large band gap (∼2 eV) makes it a good tandem solar top-cell absorber.41 Therefore, because of its adequate chemical stability, high mobility (10 cm2 V−1 s−1), and excellent band alignment, (CBTS) thin film with a bandgap of 2.04 eV is promising as an HTL for the CsPbI3 absorber, even if it is not frequently utilized in PSCs.39,42

In our previous study, with the one-dimensional solar cell capacitance simulator (SCAPS-1D) simulation, we found 96 different structures for CsPbI3-based PSC with a diverse set of charge transport layers.43 Among those structures, the CBTS HTL with 6 different ETLs showed noticeable performances, and ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Au was the best device with a PCE of 17.90%, fill factor (FF) of 85.21%, JSC of 21.075 mA cm−2, and VOC of 0.997 V. In this study, we focused into the optimization of this ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Au device through SCAPS-1D. Initially, we explored the BMC variation to improve the PSC efficiency and obtained the optimum device structure of ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni. Then we further optimized the thickness of CsPbI3, HTL, and ETL, charge-carrier density and defect density of absorber, ETL, and HTL, and interfacial defect density. Moreover, we observed the impact of operating temperature, series resistance, and shunt resistance on the final optimized device's performance and also investigated the capacitance–voltage (C–V), generation and recombination rate, J–V, and QE of the final optimized device with and without HTL. Concurrently, we investigated the variation in the device performance without using the HTL.

During the simulation, we found the same PCE of 17.91% for both W and Ni back contacts. On the other hand, Pd, Pt, and Se back contacts showed slightly better PCE of 17.93% (Table 3). The decrease in back contact WF lowered the device's performance. For example, as the Ag back contact has a very low WF, it is hard for the holes to move to the surface of the metal, but it is easy for the electrons to move. This causes the reverse saturation current to go down, and thus, the PSC with Ag back contact showed the lowest PCE of 3.85%. Also, experimentally, it has been observed that the iodide ions of the perovskite layer react with the Ag ions to make AgI. When AgI breaks down in the presence of the perovskite material, the iodide ion can move through the absorber, which deteriorates the device's performance.51 Furthermore, the PCE and FF increased as the back contact WF enhanced, which was because, due to the presence of BMCs with high WF (e.g., Au, Pt, Pd, etc.) in the PSC, the Schottky barrier diminishes that favors the hole transportation.53 Other studies also show that the performance of the PSCs changes when the back contact WF changes.50,54 Because of the abundance, low cost, and efficiency, Ni was chosen as the back contact for the next steps.

During the analysis, the defect densities (Nt) of the absorber, ETL, and HTL, as well as the interfacial defect densities were kept fixed at the initial values.

3.4 Device optimization

3.4.1 Optimization of absorber layer thickness. As the performance of the ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni structure was influenced by the absorber thickness, we increased the absorber thickness from 400 nm to 2400 nm to optimize the device performance. The performance change with the varying absorber thickness of the PSC with and without the HTL has been shown in Fig. 4(a).
Fig. 4 Effect of the thickness variation of the CsPbI3 layer, TiO2 ETL, and CBTS HTL, respectively. (a) Shows the effect of the CsPbI3 thickness variation on VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE with and without HTL; (b) shows the effect of the TiO2 ETL thickness variation on VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE with and without HTL; (c) shows the CBTS HTL thickness variation on VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE.

During the optimization, as the absorber thickness increased, the VOC of the PSC decreased for both with and without the HTL configurations as the reverse saturation current increased with the increasing absorber thickness.53 Again, in both with and without the HTL configurations, the JSC increased with the incrementing thickness as the spectral response at longer wavelengths will go up as the thickness rises.54 However, when the absorber thickness was increased, the structure with and without the HTL showed different FF trends. For both with and without the HTL structures, the reduction in efficiency on both sides of the optimal absorber thickness of 1500 nm was due to the impacts of the photon absorption rate and charge carrier recombination.57 When the absorber thickness is the same as the charge carrier diffusion length, the PSC usually shows the best performance. As the absorber layer thickness rises, a higher number of photons are absorbed with the simultaneous enhancement of the recombination rate. However, for a thin absorber, the carrier extraction at the electrodes is significant and the PCE drops because a thin absorber can only absorb a small number of photons.58 Since too thick perovskite film can only convert a small amount of power, a good balance is needed between the light absorption and moving carriers.59,60 Therefore, for better performance of VOC (0.978 V), JSC (21.98 mA cm−2), FF (85.47%), and PCE (18.37%) need to be an ideal thickness, which was found to be 1500 nm for the CsPbI3 absorber in the study. This optimization result was included in the next stage of the performance evaluation.

3.4.2 Optimization of ETL and HTL layer thickness. With an appropriate ETL, the recombination currents can be lowered, while the transmittance can be raised in the PSC.61 In this work, the thickness of the TiO2 ETL was changed from 10 nm to 500 nm, while all other parameters were kept the same. Fig. 4(b) shows how the ETL thickness affects the PV parameters of the configurations with and without the HTL. There was an insignificant drop in VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE with the increasing ETL thickness, which could be because a thicker ETL partially absorbs light, retarding the charge generation and collection rate. The performance loss gets more severe as the ETL gets thicker because of the drop in transmittance.50 Based on the results, it was apparent that the ETL thickness had little effect on the PV performance, similar to the previous works.62 So, 30 nm was chosen as the best ETL thickness, which was the same as the initial value.

Again, the CBTS HTL thickness was modulated from 50 nm to 500 nm to find the optimum HTL thickness, and Fig. 4(c) illustrates its effect on the device performance. Alike the ETL thickness variation, no significant variation in PV parameters was observed during the modulation of HTL thickness. That's why the optimized HTL thickness was chosen to be 100 nm for further optimization, which was the initial setting of the study. A p-type layer (HTL) needs to be thicker in comparison to the n-type layer (ETL) to reduce the likelihood of recombination as it facilitates the prompt transportation of an equal number of charge carriers to terminal.63 Therefore, the ideal thickness of HTL should be larger than the ideal thickness of ETL, which was followed in this work. Besides, thicker HTL is beneficial as it makes the light-harvesting layer more capable of absorbing photons.63

3.4.3 Optimization of acceptor density of the absorber. To find the effect of the NA of the absorber on the PV parameters, the NA of the CsPbI3 layer was changed between 109 cm−3 and 1018 cm−3. Fig. 5(a) shows how the FF, PCE, VOC, and JSC change in the configurations with and without the HTL as the absorber NA changes. The VOC and FF improved from 0.96 V to 1.053 V and 81.97% to 83.34%, respectively, whereas the JSC and PCE dropped from 21.972 mA cm−2 to 11.099 mA cm−2 and 17.29% to 9.74%, respectively, for the structure with the HTL. In addition, the performance parameters of the structure without the HTL also followed a similar trend when the absorber NA was varied; but the maximum PCE of 18.17% was found at 1015 cm−3NA instead of 1016 cm−3NA. The decrease in the Fermi energy level of the hole is caused by an increase in the absorber NA, which in turn, enhances the VOC.64 As the NA of the absorber goes up, so does the built-in potential. This can be another reason for the VOC to go up because the charge separation also rises. But when the absorber NA value was more than 1016 cm−3, the PCE went down. This is because, as the number of defect states starts to rise when the absorber NA goes up, hence, the PCE goes down.64 So, to get the best performance of VOC = 1.01 V, JSC = 21.42 mA cm−2, FF = 85.31%, and PCE = 18.46%, the optimum value of NA was taken as 1016 cm−3.65 This value was considered for the PSC structure for the next stage of this study.

3.4.4 Optimization of defect density of the absorber. The efficiency of PSCs is significantly influenced by the absorber Nt. Photoelectrons are produced when the absorber layer is allowed to interact with light. However, this might lead to an inadequate perovskite layer coverage on the ETL if the morphology is not good. Inadequate film quality leads to a larger defect density, which in turn, increases the recombination. Fig. 5(b) demonstrates that all PV parameters declined as the absorber Nt was increased from 109 cm−3 to 1018 cm−3 in the configurations with and without the HTL.

The VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE considerably decreased from 1.02 V to 0.78 V, 21.57 mA cm−2 to 16.45 mA cm−2, 86.55% to 66.60%, and 19.05% to 8.53%, respectively, in the configuration with the HTL. The results were also similar for the structure without the HTL. As the absorber Nt reached 109 cm−3 to 1012 cm−3, the device performance remained almost constant, and it started to decrease slowly after 1012 cm−3. Here, σ represents the charge-carriers capture cross-section, τ represents the lifespan of the charge-carriers, and Vth represents the thermal velocity of charge carriers. It is possible to reduce the number of defects in a crystalline perovskite by improving the crystallinity by using the appropriate layer processing conditions that can reduce the SRH recombination.57 In this work, the optimum Nt for CsPbI3 was found to be 1012 cm−3, which is nearly similar to the published literature.64 For the next stage of the simulation, this absorber Nt value was considered for the device.

Again, Fig. 6(b) illustrates the impact of the Nt of TiO2 ETL on the performance parameters of the configurations with and without the HTL. As the Nt of TiO2 was increased from 1011 cm−3 to 1018 cm−3, the VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE remained almost constant in both configurations. 3.6 Effect of resistance and temperature

3.6.1 Effect of series and shunt resistance. Fig. 10(a) represents the effect of series resistance (RS) on the PV parameters of the device with and without the HTL as it was varied from 0–6 Ω cm2, while the shunt resistance (RSH) was constant at 105 Ω cm2. As the RS enhanced, the VOC of the device without HTL increased marginally from 1.0203 V to 1.02 V, whereas the JSC, FF, and PCE dropped from 21.58 mA cm−2 to 21.54 mA cm−2, 86.57% to 75.26%, and 19.06% to 16.54%, respectively. Similar findings were also attained for the device without HTL. Overall series resistance is determined by the resistance of each layer utilized in the cell, and resistance of interfaces between semiconductor–semiconductor and semiconductor–metal. Highly conductive TCO rarely contributes to RS.70 Therefore, RS majorly depends on the resistance of materials, and at the semiconductors–metal interface, RS significantly hampers the FF of the device and sometimes also affects the VOC43 and results in an overall reduction in PCE due to higher power loss.52
Fig. 10 (a) Shows variation in series resistance (RS) on VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE when RSH = 1 × 105 Ω cm2 with and without HTL; (b) shows variation in shunt resistance (RSH) on VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE when RS = 0.5 Ω cm2 with and without HTL; (c) shows the variation of temperature 275 K to 475 K VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE.

Meanwhile, Fig. 10(b) illustrates the effect of RSH on the performance parameters of the device with and without the HTL during its augmentation from 10 to 107 Ω cm2, whereas the RS remained at a constant value of 0.5 Ω cm2. During this enhancement, we found increments in VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE values from 0.215 V to 1.020 V, 20.545 mA cm−2 to 21.572 mA cm−2, 25% to 85.61%, and 1.11% to 18.84%, respectively. When the optimization was carried out without using HTL, we obtained similar conclusions. Manufacturing defects mainly contribute to RSH.43 A greater RSH lowers the p/n junction resistance allowing more current to flow through it. The results of our research ascertain that RSH affects the FF and PCE substantially.43,71 When the RSH was between 10 Ω cm2 to 104 Ω cm2, a significant change in the performance could be seen, and then, the performance was more stable between 105 Ω cm2 to 107 Ω cm2RSH. In a previous study, even though the RSH exceeded 8000 Ω cm2, the FF, PCE, and VOC steadily rose, while the JSC stayed consistent.52

3.6.2 Effect of temperature. Fig. 10(c) represents the effect of temperature variation from 275 K to 475 K on the performance parameters of the device with and without the HTL. During the modulation of temperature, we found variations in VOC, JSC, FF, and PCE of the device with HTL from 1.083 V to 0.568 V, 21.536 mA cm−2 to 21.727 mA cm−2, 87.61% to 74.74%, and 20.43% to 9.22%, respectively. Similar trends could be observed for the device without HTL. The device performance steadily declined with the rise in temperature due to the increase in defects and deformation stress, which is in line with the previous report.72 Furthermore, the increasing temperature also affects the diffusion length and increases RS, which directly affects the FF and PCE of the device.51,73

3.7 Analysis of capacitance and Mott–Schottky values

As the voltage varied from −0.5 V to 0.8 V and the frequency was kept constant at 1 MHz, Fig. 11(a) and (b) show the fluctuation of the capacitance (C) and Mott–Schottky values (MS, 1/C2), each for the final structure with and without HTL. The capacitance increased with increasing voltage. At zero bias, the entire device was exhausted; but when a forward bias of about 0.5 V was applied, the depletion width decreased to a value close to the absorber thickness. With the increase in forward bias, the capacitance increased and began to act according to the MS relationship. Previous research has shown that at low voltages the current is well below the saturation current, but at voltage peaks, the current is only allowed to exceed the saturation current of the contact.74 On the other hand, MS is a popular and trusted way to find out the built-in potential (Vbi) of a device, which represents the contrast between the functionality of the electrode and the doping level. The basis of the MS theory is based on the properties of the p–n junction, where the Vbi of semiconductor devices is usually represented by the intersection of the x-axis. Despite the lower-than-expected results, the slope of 1/C2(V) is usually identified as the concentration of occupied traps. A decrease in MS values is observed with an increase in voltage which is in accordance with the previous findings.43,71,75

3.8 Analysis of generation and recombination rate

With respect to the position in the device, Fig. 11(c) and (d), respectively, show the carrier generation and recombination rate for optimized structures with and without HTL. In the carrier generation step, electron–hole pairs are generated with the excitation of an electron from the VB to CB, leaving a hole in the VB. The discharge of electrons and holes from these PSCs propels carrier generation. The maximum generation rates for the optimized structure with and without HTL were discovered at around 1.6 μm and 1.45 μm, respectively. The generation rate at that range was maximum as the highest number of electrons are generated at that particular position because of the increased absorption of photons. SCAPS-1D calculates the electron–hole pairs generation, G(x) by employing the incoming photon flux, Nphot(λ, x) by following eqn (11):
 
G(λ, x) = α(λ, x)·Nphot(λ, x)(11)
In contrast, during the recombination, the electrons and holes of the CB get combined and become eliminated. The rate of recombination in PSCs is influenced by the charge carrier lifetime and density. Additionally, the defect state of each PSC layer has an impact on electron–hole recombination. The maximum recombination rates for the optimized device with and without HTL were observed at around 0.1 μm and 1.6 μm, respectively. This is because, compared to other locations, a greater number of electrons in the CB crosses the energy gap and reaches the VB to get stable and occupy the position of a hole at that range. As a result, the energy levels produced at that point affected the electron–hole recombination profile. In addition, grain boundaries and imperfections can cause non-uniformity in the distribution of recombination rates.

4. Summary

CsPbI3-based PSCs are attractive because of the excellent optoelectronic properties and thermal stability of CsPbI3 absorber compared to other perovskites This work showcases the optimization of ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Au PSC by considering and optimizing multiple performance parameters for the absorber and charge transport layers and the associated material interfaces, and the following conclusions can be drawn:

(i) Ni was considered as the optimum back contact during the back contact optimization. The ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni device exhibited a PCE of 17.91%, which was further optimized (with and without HTL). Before further optimization, the simultaneous impacts of absorber thickness with acceptor density and absorber thickness with defect density on the device with and without HTL were explored.

(ii) The optimal absorber thickness for both configurations was 1500 nm. During the variation in ETL and HTL thickness, ETL and HTL defect density, and HTL acceptor density, the device's performance was almost unperturbed.

(iii) The optimum absorber acceptor densities of the PSC with and without HTL were 1016 cm−3 and 1015 cm−3, respectively, while the optimum absorber defect density and ETL donor density for both cases were 1012 cm−3 and 9 × 1017 cm−3, respectively.

(iv) The finally optimized ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Ni device achieved a maximum PCE of 19.06%, which is 1.16% higher (in magnitude) than the initial combination of ITO/TiO2/CsPbI3/CBTS/Au. And the efficiency of this final optimized structure without HTL was 18.17%.

(v) Furthermore, capacity and Mott–Schottky showed a similar trend for final optimized devices with and without HTL, in contrast, generation and recombination effects on the optimized device showed significant performance with HTL compared to without HTL.

For validation of the solar cell, we checked the series and shunt resistance, working temperature, illuminated current–voltage curve, and quantum efficiency. The extensive optimization study reported in this study for CsPbI3-based perovskite cells may lead the way in the development of high-efficiency Cs-based PSCs in the future.
