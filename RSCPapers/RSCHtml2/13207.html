A novel approach to synthesize ultrasmall Cu doped Zn–In–Se nanocrystal emitters in a colloidal system†

Jun Ke a, Xinyong Li *ab, Qidong Zhao a, Yong Shi a and Guohua Chen b
aState Key Laboratory of Fine Chemical, Key Laboratory of Industrial Ecology and Environmental Engineering (MOE), School of Environmental Science and Technology, Dalian University of Technology, Dalian, China. E-mail: xyli@dlut.edu.cn
bDepartment of Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering, Hong Kong University of Science & Technology, Clear Water Bay, Kowloon, Hong Kong, China. E-mail: kechengh@ust.hk

Received 20th November 2013 , Accepted 22nd December 2013

First published on 2nd January 2014

Abstract

A facile phosphine-free approach is presented to prepare Cu doped ternary Zn–In–Se nanocrystals with high performance photoluminescence. By doping during nucleation stage, Cu dopant serving as a dominative emission centre was introduced into a weak fluorescence ternary system to modify the emission path. As a result of varying the ratios of zinc to indium, emission of the products is tunable ranging from 620 to 545 nm and a photoluminescence quantum yield of up to 20%. Meanwhile, a large Stokes shift of 467 meV is achieved.

Introduction

Colloidal semiconductor nanocrystals (NCs) with radii smaller than that of the Bohr exciton of the material have received considerable attention because of their preferred optical properties such as excitation in the visible window, large Stokes shift and high quantum efficiency, for fundamental studies and technical applications in biomedical labelling, light emitting diodes, lasers, and sensors.1–4 Semiconducting ternary NCs, being a good choice due to their narrow band-gap and low toxic elements, have been extensively investigated and reported over the past decades. Ternary semiconductor NCs such as CuInS2, AgInS2 and quaternary NCs such as Cu2ZnSnSe4, CuInxGa(1−x)S2, have been synthesized with a direct band gap of bulk materials.5–10 These semiconducting NCs are often utilized to prepare thin films via spin or spray techniques in linear and nonlinear optical devices and photovoltaic solar cells.11,12 Furthermore, the optical and electronic properties of these materials could be accurately tuned by varying their elemental compositions.13–18 Yang's group and Chen's group reported the facile synthesis of different composition ratios (CuIn)xZn2(1−x)S2 NCs with different photoluminescence wavelengths.19,20 In order to synthesize NCs with a high quantum yield, several strategies have been extensively studied. It was reported that passivation of the surface of NCs with a wider band gap semiconducting material could enhance the photoluminescence quantum yield by confining the excitons within the core of NCs and reducing the surface defects.21–26

In ternary semiconductor NCs systems, strong photoluminescence stems from irradiative recombination of excited electrons in Cu or In vacancies, which is easy to change by means of varying the excitation wavelength.27–30 To the best of our knowledge, however, synthesis of ternary NCs with the desirable properties of large Stokes shift, excitation-independent and bright emission have rarely been reported in previous papers. Alternatively, the use of intentional impurities or dopants can also strongly influence the optical behaviour of host NCs. Metal doping strategies have been recently developed to adjust the energy transfer and the irradiative process.31–35 Specifically, Cu dopant could be chosen as a primary emission centre and introduced to modify the optical properties of ternary NCs, which can not only dominate the whole fluorescent emission of ternary alloyed NCs, but also yield a favourable large Stokes shift. Herein, a facile doping route for preparing Cu doped Zn–In–Se NCs without using highly toxic reagents, such as pyrophoric reagents, is present. By varying the ratios of zinc to indium, the emission of the obtained NCs is tunable from 620 to 545 nm. Meanwhile, a large Stokes shift of 467 meV is successfully achieved for the Cu doped Zn–In–Se NCs.

Experimental

Materials

Technical grade 1-octadecene (ODE, 90%), indium chloride (InCl3), copper chloride (CuCl2), zinc stearate (ZnSt2), 1-dodecanethiol (99%), oleic acid (OA, 90%), oleylamine (OAm, 98%) were purchased from Aldrich. Selenium powder (Se) and mercaptopropionic acid (MPA) were purchased from Acros Organics. All other reagents were of analytical grade and used as received.

Preparation of stock solutions

Cu stock solution was prepared by adding 0.1 mmol of CuCl2 into 5 mL of OAm. And then to obtain a clear solution, the mixture was sonicated for 20 min. 30 mL of ODE and 10 mmol Se powder were mixed and then degassed to remove air and water at 120 °C for 30 min and was purged for 30 min using a nitrogen gas inlet. A clear yellow solution was gained as Se–ODE stock. Zn stock solution was prepared by adding 1.0 mmol of ZnSt2 into the mixture solution containing 2 mL of ODE and 2 mL of OAm and then heated to 150 °C under nitrogen gas for further use.

Synthesis of Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs

In a typical synthesis of Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs, indium chloride (0.1 mmol, 0.0293 g), ZnSt2 (0.05 mmol, 0.0316 g), 1-dodecanethiol (2 mL, DDT), ODE (4 mL), OA (1 mL), and 0.05 mL Cu stock solution (0.001 mmol CuCl2) were loaded into 50 mL three-necked round-bottom flask. The reaction mixture was degassed for 5 min before heating to 120 °C and then purging for 30 min with nitrogen gas. Afterwards, 3 mL of Se–ODE stock solution with 2.0 mmol OAm was injected when the temperature was raised to 220 °C. The reaction mixture was kept at the same temperature and monitored through UV-vis and photoluminescence spectra.

Then, 0.5 mL of Zn stock solution was injected dropwise into the above colloidal solution using a syringe while the temperature of the system was maintained at the same value. The second or third addition of Zn stock solution has been injected in an interval of 15 min. The reaction was quenched by removal of heating and cooled down to room temperature. The product of NCs was isolated by precipitating through addition of acetone, centrifuging, and decanting the supernatant. The NCs could be re-dispersed in organic solvents such as chloroform. The precipitation/dispersion cycle was repeated at least three times in order to eliminate byproducts and unreacted precursors.

Characterizations

TEM and HRTEM images were taken on a FEI Tecnai G2 F20 with an acceleration voltage of 200 and 300 kV. Chloroform solutions containing these nanocrystal samples were dropped on carbon-coated copper grids. XRD patterns were obtained using a Rigaku D/max-2500 X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromated Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). The UV-vis absorption spectra were obtained on a Shanghai Tianmei Tech Ltd. The room temperature photoluminescence spectra of the NCs samples were measured using a Hitch F-4500 fluorescence spectrometer. Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterizations of Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs

Fig. 1 shows typical TEM and HRTEM images of Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs. The as-obtained NCs appear to be nearly spherical in shape and fairly monodisperse. The average size of these samples is about 2.5 nm, as determined from TEM images and size distribution (Fig. 1a and S1†), which does not show an apparent contrast due to the ultrasmall size. The lattice fringes are observed clearly (Fig. 1b), which indicates the formation of the NCs with some degree of crystallinity. In the meantime, three broad and obvious peaks observed in the XRD patterns (Fig. 2) suggest the nature of ultrasmall-sized samples and demonstrate that the as-synthesized NCs have the characteristics of zinc blende (ZB) ZnSe (JCPDS 80-0021). Furthermore, XRD patterns of the samples in the absence and presence of Cu species have no apparent differences, which demonstrates that Cu species as dopant have insignificant influence on the crystal structure, similar to Mn doped binary QDs reported in previous papers.37,38 The element compositions of the samples were recorded using ICP-AES. The ratio of Cu to (Zn + In) is 0.3%, which is lower than the initial ratio, 0.6%, in the initial precursor. The absorption and photoluminescence spectra of a typical Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs sample are shown in Fig. 3a. The sharp absorption peak of Cu doped ZnIn2Se4/ZnSe NCs was not observed for any sample around 500 nm, in contrast to the good resolution of absorption spectra in pure binary NCs. The photoluminescence (PL) spectrum of as-synthesized NCs shows a strong and broad emission peak at 616 nm. The big gap between the absorption spectrum and photoluminescence peak demonstrates that emission does not derive from intrinsic band gap emitting. Meanwhile, the photoluminescent emission of the NCs also has been measured under different wavelengths of excitation (Fig. 3b). It shows that the primary emission of the NCs does not vary with the changes of excitation wavelengths. In contrast, in pure ternary NCs, emission derives from the recombination of excitons in copper or indium vacancies, which results in excitation-dependent emission.27 When copper ions as a dopant are introduced into ZnIn2Se4 NCs system, it acts as the primary emitting center and dominate the whole emission of the as-obtained NCs.

Effects of Cu species on the optical properties of NCs

In order to demonstrate the role of Cu species on the optical properties of NCs, the corresponding NCs have been synthesized without Cu species under the same conditions. We observed that the NCs do not have characteristic PL spectra caused by Cu dopant with strong emission but the weak PL related to the band gap (Fig. 4a). Although an apparent difference in emission spectra has been observed, the absence of Cu species did not alter the absorption spectra (Fig. 4b), which is consistent with the XRD results. More importantly, an apparent change of the emission peak was not observed with increasing the concentration of Cu dopant, which demonstrates that the amount of Cu dopant has a low correlation with the peak wavelength of photoluminescence (Fig. 5). However, the initial amount of Cu precursor could to some extent enhance the fluorescence intensity and increase the quantum yield of the samples. Such result is similar to Mn doped binary NCs, that is, the PL QYs of doped NCs could be raised by increasing the dopant concentration without formation of a new crystalline phase.37,39,40 The emission is attributed to the dopant and originates from the Cu center, which is consistent with the apparent Stokes shift shown in Fig. 3.To obtain the valence of Cu ion in a single nanocrystal, ESR spectra under room temperature were recorded (Fig. 6). We observed that the characteristic CuII signal of the ESR spectrum disappeared after the reaction, despite CuII salts being utilized in the initial reaction mixture. In previous studies, a copper element was introduced into the ternary NCs through CuI salts or organometallic precursors.21,41 The ESR spectra of those NCs are expected not to show the feature peak of CuII ions. These results suggest that the Cu ion in the obtained NCs is in oxidation state +1. The reduction of CuII may take place in the presence of fatty amines when the reaction temperature was raised. The time-resolved PL spectra of the sample measured at emission peak wavelength are shown in Fig. 7. The decay curve was fitted by a biexponential function (the inset in Fig. 7). The lifetimes are found to be 7.1 ± 0.1 ns (τ1) and 143.3 ± 3 ns (τ2) with a = 0.2 and b = 0.8, respectively.

Effects of the initial ratio of Zn to In on emission of NCs

Interestingly, apart from variation of the Cu dopant, the wavelength of the emission peak was altered when the initial ratio of Zn to In changed in the initial precursor. From Fig. 8, it can be observed that the wavelength of the emission peak was shifted to higher energy by 61 nm when the ratio of Zn to In increased from 0.5 to 1.0. When the molar ratio of zinc to indium reached 2.0, the characteristic emission almost disappeared, due to failure of ZnIn2Se4 nanocrystals formation.20 Therefore, the emission wavelength could be expected to be tuned through variation of the NCs composition.
Fig. 8 PL spectra of the NCs samples with different ratios of Zn to In.

The epitaxial shell is a necessary structure to enhance the quantum yield of NCs, which has been investigated extensively in the past years.21,42 ZnSe was selected as the useful shell in the present study to passivate the as-obtained NCs so as to improve the quantum yield. The replacement of In3+ ions with Zn2+ ions happened at the initial stage of the addition of Zn stock solution, as shown in Fig. 9. The ion diameter of Zn2+ (r = 0.74 Å) is smaller than that of In3+ (r = 0.94 Å), which makes the replacement of In3+ ions with Zn2+ easy at high temperature. As shown in Fig. 9a, the band gap of the as-obtained NCs samples was enlarged with the increase in reaction time because the replacement of Cu or In ions with Zn ions resulted in a decrease of the core diameter and thus an increase of the energy band gap due to a quantum confinement effect.39 The blue shift of PL emission peak indicated that an ion exchange reaction had taken place with forming of ZnSe shell as displayed in Fig. 9b. The core of Zn–In–Se NCs shrank gradually as the zinc ions replaced the indium ions in the core, which resulted in a blue shift of emission. The blue-shifted phenomenon of PL emission peak has also been observed by other ternary semiconductor NCs, such as CuInS2, when a zinc sulfide shell was deposited on the surface of core NCs.39 Such blue-shifted PL emission peak caused by the formation of the zinc chalcogenide shell is distinguished from the red-shifted PL emission peak in the binary QDs.42 Because of the different reactivity of the metallic-element precursors, the types of existing defects are different. In pure copper doped ZnIn2Se4 NCs, the zinc ion as a critical acid easily binds to 1-dodecanethiol (DDT, soft base) as compared to the indium ion as a hard acid, that is, indium ion easily reacted with selenium precursor.43 Therefore, a zinc vacancy could be source for major surface and deep defects, which is in agreement with those related conclusions.27,44–46 When the shell grew at the outermost of the core by replacement of indium with zinc, the zinc vacancy was gradually reduced, resulting in formation of a ZnSe shell. Finally, copper dopant as an emission center dominated the whole emission of core/shell NCs. The band-gap emission was suppressed simultaneously, as shown in Fig. 9.


Fig. 9 Absorption (a) and photoluminescence spectra (b) of the sample with the 1:2 ratio of Zn to In with and without ZnSe shell under light excitation of 400 nm.

To further identify the potential binary (ZnSe, In2Se3) impurities because of similar XRD patterns to ZnIn2Se4, additional characterization with Raman spectroscopy was performed to confirm the absence of these impurities. Fig. 10 clearly shows that there is no presence of ZnSe and In2Se3 which would have strong Raman peaks at 351 and 196 cm−1, respectively.24,47 Three peaks at 94.5, 142.9 and 235.8 cm−1 are ascribed to Zn–In–Se NCs. Another apparent characteristic peak at 172.1 cm−1 has been observed in the presence of Cu dopant, while it disappears in the absence of Cu dopant. Therefore, the Raman peak could be attributed to the quaternary alloy Cu–Zn–In–Se NCs.48 Meanwhile, by comparing the Raman spectra of doped with undoped NCs, we found the relative intensity of the peak at 235.8 cm−1 decreased, while the peak at 94.5 cm−1 was enhanced. The spectral change might be due to the introduction of copper as well as the ion replacement of indium with zinc, resulting in the slight change from single a crystal phase to an alloy crystal phase.18,24


Fig. 10 Room-temperature Raman spectra of samples with different compositions and structures. λexc = 758 nm.

The corresponding Zn:In ratios of the samples measured by ICP-AES indicate that the ratio of Zn to In reduced from the initial 1:1.94 to the final 1:1.76. As a result of the formation of ZnSe shell, the quantum yield of the as-prepared samples was found to be slightly over 20%, one order of magnitude higher than that of core NCs, 2.02%. It is implied that the growth of the shell reduces the surface and deep defects deriving from Cu and In vacancies and suppresses the leak of excitons and non-irradiative probability, which to a large extent determines the PL QYs and photostability of semiconducting NCs.49–51 A high quantum yield makes these NCs a good candidate for many applications, such as sensing, imaging, LED.

Conclusions

In conclusion, a facile synthesis of stable, cadmium-free, Cu doped Zn–In–Se NCs in ultrasmall size is presented through a doping technique with low toxic precursors in a colloidal solution. Emission of the as-obtained NCs has been successfully modified and dominated by a low amount of Cu dopant, which could easily be reproduced and controlled. Furthermore, the emission of these NCs ranging from 620 to 545 nm could also be tuned by varying the initial molar ratio of indium to zinc in the precursor. A large Stokes shift of 467 meV is successfully achieved for the Cu doped Zn–In–Se NCs. In addition, the as-obtained NCs possess a high quantum yield slightly over 20%, which could meet the demands of biomedical, LED, and sensing applications.
