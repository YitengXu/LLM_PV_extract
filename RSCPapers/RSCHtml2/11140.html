Abstract

Rational structure design of semiconductor photocatalysts is meaningful to enhance their sunlight-driven hydrogen-evolution performance. Herein, we present a facile approach to prepare Cd1âˆ’xZnxS nanopopcorns with zinc blende/wurtzite phase junctions by using excess thioacetamide (TAA). The as-obtained Cd1âˆ’xZnxS samples were used for visible-light photocatalytic H2 evolution without a co-catalyst. The H2-evolution activity reaches 282.14 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1, which is ca. 12 fold higher than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S prepared with an equivalent dose of TAA. Meanwhile, an apparent quantum yield of 64.4% at 420 nm was achieved. Results demonstrated that the Cd1âˆ’xZnxS samples synthesized by using excess TAA improve light absorption and hydrophilicity, which is helpful for the photocatalytic reaction. Moreover, the existence of type-II ZB/WZ phase junctions promotes the separation and migration of photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs. This work provides a novel idea for the rational design of efficient Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based photocatalysts.

1. Introduction

H2 is an ideal substitute for nonrenewable fossil fuels owing to the development of H2 production technology and storage materials.1,2 Converting inexhaustible solar energy to hydrogen energy via semiconductor-based photocatalysis is environmentally friendly and has therefore attracted considerable attention.3,4 For decades, researchers have been increasingly exploring the technology of semiconductor-based photocatalytic H2 evolution (PHE), especially those semiconductors with suitable band structures and visible-light response.5,6

Among diverse semiconductor-based photocatalysts, CdS and its solid solutions (e.g., Cd1âˆ’xZnxS and Cd1âˆ’xMnxS) are becoming more and more popular in the field of photocatalysis owing to their suitable band structures and optical properties.5,7,8 As a ternary solid solution with low toxicity and cost-effectiveness, Cd1âˆ’xZnxS (CZS) has been widely studied because of its flexible and adjustable bandgap and edge position, simple preparation process, strong visible-light response and high resistance to photocorrosion.7,9 Some efforts have been made to explore Cd1âˆ’xZnxS with different x values, morphologies and crystal structures.10â€“16 By adjusting the value of x in Cd1âˆ’xZnxS, the ternary solid solutions possess the advantages of both wide bandgap ZnS (3.6 eV) and narrow bandgap CdS (2.4 eV).10 Qin Li et al. found that the H2-production rate of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS solid solutions varies with controlled Cd1âˆ’xZnxS bandgap via changing the Cd/Zn molar ratio.11 Up to now, Cd1âˆ’xZnxS samples with various morphologies such as nanosheets,12 nanospheres,13 nanorods14 and hollow dodecahedral cages15 have been investigated with regard to their photocatalytic performance. In addition, two crystal structures (zinc blende (ZB) and wurtzite (WZ)) of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS have attracted much attention. It has been reported that ZB-type Cd1âˆ’xZnxS exhibits higher PHE activity than the WZ-type structure.16 Although Cd1âˆ’xZnxS has great potential for application in PHE, some disadvantages such as low solar energy utilization, rapid recombination of photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs and photocorrosion still limit its further application.

Many effective strategies (e.g., element doping, structure designing and co-catalyst loading) have been developed to improve the photocatalytic performance of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based materials.7 Among these strategies, construction of junctions (e.g., heterojunctions and phase junctions) is one of the most important ways. Many Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based heterojunctions without any noble metal co-catalyst such as MoO2/Cd0.5Zn0.5S,17 1T-LixMoS2/Cd0.5Zn0.5S,18 Cu1.94Sâ€“Cd1âˆ’xZnxS (ref. 19) and ZnO1âˆ’x/Zn0.2Cd0.8S (ref. 20) have been successfully constructed to enhance PHE performance. The formation of phase junctions between two crystalline phases of a semiconductor is an effective means to improve the PHE activity, such as Î±/Î² phase Ga2O3,21 anatase/rutile TiO2 (ref. 22 and 23) and hexagonal/cubic CdS.24 Phase junctions can promote the separation/transfer of photoinduced carriers and prolong their lifetime owing to the internal electric field across the phase junction.25 For Cd1âˆ’xZnxS solid solutions, twinned Cd1âˆ’xZnxS with phase junctions between the ZB and WZ segments has become one of the most efficient H2-evolution catalysts so far.26 The twin-induced Cd0.5Zn0.5S with long-range ordered phase junctions has high activity (25.8 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1) and an apparent quantum yield up to 62% without a co-catalyst.27 Moreover, bare Cd1âˆ’xZnxS with ZB/WZ phase junctions stabilized by L-cysteine exhibited high PHE activity (31.3 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1) with a high apparent quantum yield of 65.7%.28 Besides, phase-junction Zn0.5Cd0.5S showed excellent PHE activity (125.27 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1) by controlling the homogeneity and crystallinity under different reaction conditions.16 Recently, the ZB/WZ phase junctions with major ZB and minor WZ phases in Cd1âˆ’xZnxS can effectively improve the separation and transfer of photogenerated charge carriers and thus H2 production (30 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1).29 However, the synthesis of ZB/WZ phase junction Cd1âˆ’xZnxS often needs additional conditions such as ultrasonic wave irradiation,30 high temperature,27L-cysteine assistance31 and a tedious process.32

For a PHE reaction system, the hydrophilicity of catalysts is also a key point to boost the solidâ€“liquidâ€“gas multiphase photocatalytic process. Highly hydrophilic materials exhibit many advantages for photocatalytic reactions in an aqueous medium. First, improving the surface wettability is helpful in promoting the dispersion states of photocatalysts and providing a larger interface with water.33 Second, the better dispersity is beneficial to inhibit charge recombination and increase photocatalytic efficiency.33 Third, increasing the hydrophilicity of catalysts can lower the adsorption energy of water molecules and boost the reaction of charge carriers and water molecules.34 Some hydrophilic materials, such as CTF-HUST-A1,35 P123@SrTiO3 (ref. 34) and ReS2-BzO-TiO2,36 have been found to be very promising for PHE in recent years.

In this work, we synthesized ZB/WZ phase-junction Cd1âˆ’xZnxS nanopopcorns by a facile one-pot solvothermal method using excess thioacetamide (TAA). During the preparation process, TAA acts as a sulfur source and its addition amount has a great influence on the structure and performance of catalysts when using pure ethylenediamine (en) as the solvent. The as-prepared samples were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), water contact angle measurements and so on. The H2-evolution rate of the as-obtained Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 sample reaches 282.14 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1 under visible light illumination (Î» â‰¥ 420 nm), which is about 12 fold higher than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S prepared using an equivalent dose of TAA. Meanwhile, a high apparent quantum yield of 64.4% was achieved at 420 nm. The excellent photocatalytic performance is higher than that of most reported Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based materials. We also explored deep insights into the photocatalytic behavior of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16. Finally, a possible reaction mechanism is put forward from the experimental results.

All of the Cd1âˆ’xZnxS samples were prepared by a simple one-step solvothermal approach. Typically, 10x (x = 0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, and 1) mmol of Zn(NO3)2Â·4H2O (where x corresponds to the Zn/(Zn + Cd) molar ratio of Zn and Cd precursors) was dispersed in 60 mL of ethylenediamine (en) and stirred for 30 min. The reaction mixture kept stirring for 1 h until a homogeneous viscous suspension was formed. The resulting mixture was then sealed in a 100 mL capacity Teflon-lined autoclave and heated up to 180 Â°C for 24 h. After five cycles of experiments (Fig. 6d), there is no obvious decrease of H2-production amount for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 in comparison with the initial cycle, demonstrating its excellent stability during the photocatalytic process. Moreover, the type-II ZB/WZ phase-junctions are not considered as recombination centers for photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs,28,53 but they act as special phase junctions, which are beneficial for effective charge separation and migration.

3.3. Photocatalytic mechanism

The optical properties of a photocatalyst are of importance to understand the photocatalytic process. A comparison of UV-vis absorption spectra of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-16 samples is displayed in Fig. 8a. The absorption edge of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS solid solutions moves from the visible to ultraviolet region when the Zn content increases. The color of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-16 also agrees with their absorption variations (Fig. S9â€ ). Compared with Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10, Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 exhibits an obvious red shift in the absorption edge (Fig. S10â€ ). The bandgap energy of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS can be evaluated according to the Kubelkaâ€“Munk method.54 As shown in Fig. 8b, the bandgaps of CdS-16, Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16, Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 and ZnS-16 were estimated to be 2.45, 2.61, 2.77 and 4.62 eV, respectively. The photoluminescence (PL) spectra of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 and Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 were acquired with an excitation wavelength of 400 nm (Fig. 8c). The weaker intensity of the PL peak for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 reflects the stronger ability of restraining the recombination of photoexcited electrons and holes.55 The PL emission peak shift to a longer wavelength for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 compared to that for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 is caused by their different chemical compositions of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS (Cd0.7Zn0.3S for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 and Cd0.5Zn0.5S for Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10). A higher content of Cd in Cd1âˆ’xZnxS causes a shift of the emission peak to a longer wavelength.28,56 Besides, the absolute fluorescence quantum yield of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 is 1.47% (Table S3â€ ), which is much higher than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 (0.21%), indicating the more efficient separation of photoexcited electrons and holes in Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 than in Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10. Moreover, the time-resolved PL decay spectra are presented in Fig. 8d. The average fluorescence lifetime of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 (1.22 ns) is longer than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 (0.77 ns), demonstrating the more effective separation and migration of charge carriers for photocatalysis.57 Hydrophilicity is also of importance for H2 evolution, and thus we conducted water contact angle measurements over Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 and Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10 (Fig. 9). On the surface of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 and Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10, the contact angles of water droplets were measured to be 11.11 and 20.95Â° at 150 ms, respectively. The surface wettability test demonstrates that the hydrophilicity of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 is better than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-10, which is helpful for the improvement of H2 evolution.33,35

To better explore the photocatalytic behavior of ZB/WZ phase-junction Cd0.7Zn0.3S nanopopcorns in Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16, pure WZ Cd0.7Zn0.3S (ref. 31) and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S (ref. 42) samples were synthesized as reported, respectively. The XRD patterns of WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S samples attest to their successful preparation (Fig. S11aâ€ ). Interestingly, WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S samples exhibit different PHE activities (Fig. S12â€ ), which is likely due to the difference in their conduction bands (Fig. 10a). The bandgaps of WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S were estimated to be 2.52 and 2.50 eV (Fig. S11bâ€ ), respectively. The conduction band (CB) potentials of WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S were measured to be âˆ’0.46 and âˆ’0.31 V (vs. RHE) based on the Mottâ€“Schottky plots (Fig. S11c and dâ€ ). The valence band (VB) potentials of WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S were calculated to be 2.06 and 2.19 V according to the equation: EVB = Eg + ECB. Therefore, the band structure schematic of WZ and ZB Cd0.7Zn0.3S is illustrated in Fig. 10a. Based on our results, a type-II staggered band alignment diagram of ZB/WZ phase-junction Cd0.7Zn0.3S nanopopcorns for visible-light H2-evolution is proposed in Fig. 10b. The type-II ZB/WZ phase-junctions can also be clearly identified from the HRTEM image in Fig. 3c. The type-II staggered band alignment formed between the ZB and WZ segments acts as a special phase junction, which is beneficial for effective charge separation and migration. The CB and VB energy levels of the WZ phase upshift with respect to those of the ZB phase based on our experimental results (Fig. 10a) and the related reports.27,28 Upon irradiation of ZB/WZ phase-junction Cd0.7Zn0.3S nanopopcorns, for both ZB and WZ phases, electrons are photoexcited to their own CB and an equivalent amount of holes remain in their own VB. The photoinduced electrons tend to transfer from the CB of the WZ phase to the CB of the ZB phase via the ZB/WZ phase junction and then reduce H+ to H2, while the photoinduced holes transfer from the VB of the ZB phase to the VB of the WZ phase and then oxidize the sacrificial agent S2âˆ’/SO32âˆ’. As a result, the construction of the ZB/WZ phase-junction in Cd0.7Zn0.3S nanopopcorns leads to an efficient separation and migration of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs, which is greatly beneficial to the improvement of photocatalytic activity.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we present a facile one-pot method to synthesize Cd1âˆ’xZnxS nanopopcorns with ZB/WZ phase junctions using excess thioacetamide (TAA). Without a co-catalyst, the visible-light PHE of Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 reaches a remarkable value of 282.14 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 mgâˆ’1, which is about 12 fold higher than that of Cd0.5Zn0.5S when using an equivalent amount of TAA and better than that of most Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based materials. In addition, the Cd1âˆ’xZnxS phase junctions display a high AQY of 64.4% at 420 nm and excellent stability. The highly efficient photocatalytic performance results from the construction of Cd1âˆ’xZnxS nanopopcorns with ZB/WZ phase junctions. The type-II ZB/WZ phase junctions drive the high-efficiency transfer/separation of photoexcited charges with a prolonged carrier lifetime, boosting the photocatalytic process for H2 generation. Moreover, Cd0.5Zn0.5S-16 improves light absorption and hydrophilicity greatly, which is helpful for the photocatalytic reaction. This work will contribute to the rational design and synthesis of inexpensive and highly efficient Cd1âˆ’xZnxS-based photocatalysts for clean energy conversion.
