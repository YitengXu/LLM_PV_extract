Abstract

The large-scale application of clean electrochemical energy storage and conversion systems such as water-splitting devices and Znâ€“air batteries greatly depends on the development of low-cost and highly efficient electrocatalysts. Transitional metal sulfides (TMSs) have gained great research interest owing to their high intrinsic catalytic activity, adjustable electronic structures, and various crystal structures and phases. Meanwhile, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are ideal precursors for the preparation of TMSs due to the diverse metal nodes, organic ligands, topologies, and morphologies of MOFs. In addition, MOFs could act as hosts for the incorporation of guest molecules, which could further expand the metal types of TMSs. In this review, the recent progress of MOF-derived TMS electrocatalysts for hydrogen evolution reactions (HERs), oxygen evolution reactions (OERs) and oxygen reduction reactions (ORRs) are summarized. After a brief introduction to the reaction mechanisms, the preparation strategies, structures, catalytic performances, and catalytic mechanisms of the TMSs are discussed to give readers a comprehensive understanding of this prosperous field. The strategies such as nanostructure engineering, interface engineering and heteroatom doping used to improve the performance of the electrocatalysts are discussed in each section. In addition, bifunctional catalytic performances of the catalysts for overall water splitting and rechargeable Znâ€“air batteries are illustrated. Furthermore, challenges and opportunities for the future development of this field are proposed. We expect this review could provide guidelines for the design and fabrication of TMSs, and initiate more efforts and new discoveries in this emerging research area.

1. Introduction

The ever-increasing environmental issues caused by burning of fossil has inspired human's awareness of environmental protection, and the realization of carbon neutrality in the middle of this century has been promised by many countries.1â€“3 To achieve this goal, it is necessary to optimize energy structures via developing green and renewable alternative energies.4â€“6 Solar energy and wind power are recognized as promising clean energies. However, their widespread applications are limited in the global energy systems due to their instability caused by time and place factors. Fortunately, it is possible to convert these sustainable energy sources into hydrogen or electrical energy using water-splitting devices and metalâ€“air batteries.7â€“9 Nevertheless, the conversion process is limited by the low energy conversion efficiency due to the sluggish kinetics and large overpotentials of the electrochemical reactions that take place in these energy conversion devices.10â€“12 Therefore, it is important to improve the energy conversion efficiency of the electrochemical devices to realize lower energy consumption during the conversion process.

Generally, the electrochemical water splitting involves two reactions, namely, hydrogen evolution reactions (HERs) and oxygen evolution reactions (OERs). In addition, OERs and oxygen reduction reactions (ORRs) are the primary reactions in rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries. Using highly efficient electrocatalysts to decrease the reaction barriers is one of the main strategies to accelerate the electrochemical reactions.5,13,14 Nowadays, state-of-the-art electrocatalysts are mainly precious metals such as Pt (ORRs and HERs), RuO2 and IrO2 (OERs). However, due to the high cost incurred by their â€œrare earthâ€ status, the wide spread application of these catalysts is severely hindered.15â€“17 Therefore, numerous efforts were devoted to develop highly efficient electrocatalysts based on earth abundant elements.

Recent investigations have demonstrated that transition metal sulfides (TMSs) are very promising alternatives benefiting from their unique physicochemical properties such as various crystal structures, different crystal phases and adjustable band position and band gap.18â€“22 Commonly, TMSs consist of a layered structure (MoS2 and WS2) and a nonlayered structure (Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn etc.), both of which have unique advantages for electrocatalysis. For example, layered TMSs could expose a large number of edge active sites.23,24 In comparison, it has been proposed that there are a number of dangling bonds on the surface of nonlayered TMS, which could bring in novel electronic characteristics.25,26 In addition, TMSs have many different crystal phases (CoS, Co3S4, Co9S8, and CoS2 for cobalt-based sulfides), which provide opportunities for optimizing the conductivity and band gap of TMSs.27â€“29 Moreover, TMSs can be easily doped with heteroatoms or form heterogeneous structures, leading to better electronic structures for the adsorption of intermediates.30,31

TMSs can be prepared via a hydrothermal or a solvothermal method, co-precipitation, chemical vapor deposition, ion exchange method, etc.32â€“36 The electrocatalysis performance of the obtained TMSs has been investigated including phase engineering, composite engineering, and interface engineering. Especially, TMSs derived from metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted widespread attention in the field of energy conversion in recent years.37â€“40 MOFs are a class of porous materials composed of metal nodes and organic linkers, and the composites and structures of MOFs can be well tailored by properly choosing metal centers and organic ligands. Due to the presence of various metal ions in MOFs, they are ideal candidates to prepare metal compounds such as metal oxides, metal sulfides and metal phosphides.41â€“45 In addition, the organic ligands in MOFs can be carbonized into carbon in a N2 atmosphere, thereby forming metal compounds/carbon hybrids. Generally, N-coordinated nitrogen-containing ligands and O-coordinated oxygen-containing ligands can be used to synthesise MOFs, which could not only result in different components, structures, and morphologies of MOFs, but also lead to heteroatom doping in carbon to further improve the catalytic performance of the hybrid catalysts. Moreover, the morphology of MOFs can be inherited during the transformation process, which enables the preparation of MOF derivatives with unique structures such as hollow structures that could provide more surface and interfaces for the electrochemical reactions.46â€“49 Some elegant works have reviewed the progress of porous carbon, metal oxides and metal phosphides derived from MOFs for catalysis and energy storage.43,50,51 Yet to the best of our knowledge, there is no specific review about MOF-based TMS electrocatalysts for the HER, OER and ORR. Considering the increasing research interests and significant progress of MOF-derived TMSs for electrocatalysis, there is a great necessity to provide a systematic comprehension of this potential field.

In this review, the recent progress of MOF-derived TMSs for advanced electrocatalysis including HERs, OERs and ORRs is comprehensively and systematically summarized (Fig. 1). The preparation strategies, structures of the catalysts, catalytic performances and catalytic mechanisms are revealed to give readers a better understanding of this prosperous field. We also paid specific attention to the bifunctional catalytic performance of the catalysts for overall water splitting and rechargeable Znâ€“air batteries. In addition, personal perspectives about the challenges and opportunities for the future development of this field are proposed. We expect this review could initiate more efforts in this emerging research area and new discoveries and breakthroughs could be achieved in the future.

2. A brief introduction to the electrocatalysis processes

As mentioned above, the HER and OER are the basic reactions of water splitting and the OER and ORR are the main reactions of rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries. To design highly efficient electrocatalysts, an understanding of the reaction mechanisms is very important.

The HER is the cathode reaction in water splitting and the process involves two steps. The first step is the Volmer reaction, which is also known as the electrochemical hydrogen adsorption step, where a proton coupled to an electron to adsorb hydrogen atoms on the surface of electrocatalysts. The second step is either the electrochemical desorption (Heyrovsky reaction) or the chemical desorption (Tafel reaction) process depending on the coverage of adsorbed hydrogen.52,53 When the concentration of adsorbed hydrogen is low, adsorbed hydrogen will couple with another electron and combine with another water molecule to release hydrogen, while the two adjacent adsorbed hydrogen atoms combine to form H2 when the coverage of adsorbed hydrogen is high. In an acidic medium, H3O+ in water can be adsorbed on the active sites of catalysts directly and participate in the chemical reaction. While in alkaline media, the decomposition of a water molecule occurs to form adsorbed H on the active sites since there is no direct H3O+. The related reaction mechanisms are listed in Fig. 2. Due to the involved adsorption and desorption of hydrogen, the rate of HER greatly relies on the adsorption free energy of hydrogen (Î”GH*), and an ideal electrocatalyst for HER should have a Î”GH* close to 0 eV.54,55

Compared with HERs, OERs taking place at the anode of metalâ€“air batteries and water splitting are more complicated four electron processes. In alkaline media, the hydroxyl group is absorbed onto the surface of electrocatalysts to form Mâ€“OH and Mâ€“O intermediates, then O2 can be produced by direct combination of two adjacent Mâ€“O intermediates or the combination of Mâ€“O with OHâ€“ to form MOOH intermediate and release O2 further.56,57 Generally, OERs and HERs possess similar evaluation parameters such as onset potential and overpotential, which indicate the potential when the cathode current begins to appear and reach a certain current density. The overpotential at a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 is usually used to compare the performance of electrocatalysts.58 The Tafel slope and exchange current density are also important parameters to evaluate the activity of an electrocatalyst. The Tafel plot can be derived from the LSV curve according to the formula Î· = a + blogj, where Î· is the overpotential, a is a constant related to the temperature, electrolyte and electrode characteristics, and b is the Tafel slope. The Tafel slope not only reflects the reaction kinetics, but also reflects reaction mechanisms.59 When Î· is 0, the obtained j is the exchange current density, which is a key descriptor to evaluate the catalytic efficiency. The turn over frequency (TOF) is usually used to estimate the intrinsic catalytic activity of electrocatalysts, which indicates the number of reactants that can be converted into the target product per active site and per second.60 Stability is also an important parameter, which is directly related to the service life of an electrocatalyst.

The ORR is a cathode reaction in metalâ€“air batteries and fuel cells, and can proceed via a direct four-electron pathway or a two-electron pathway based on the catalytic activity of ORR catalysts. In fuel cells, the direct four-electron pathway is more desirable, and this process also involves many intermediates and multiple steps (Fig. 2).61 Therefore, optimizing the adsorption energy of intermediates is also an effective means to improve the reaction kinetics of ORRs, which is similar to HERs and OERs. The onset potential, half-wave potential and limited current density are important parameters for evaluating the catalytic activity of ORR electrocatalysts. In addition, the reaction kinetics and electron transfer number can be obtained using the Kouteckyâ€“Levich equation based on rotating disk electrode measurements.62

With the development of bifunctional electrocatalysts, overall water-splitting devices and Znâ€“air batteries are usually assembled to evaluate the practical applications of the catalysts. For overall water-splitting devices, the potential used to drive a certain current density such as 10 mA cmâˆ’2 is usually collected to compare the performance of the catalysts. In addition, the faradaic efficiency of the water-splitting devices is usually calculated from collected H2 and O2 by a drainage gas gathering method.63,64 For Znâ€“air batteries, the open circuit voltage, power density, specific capacitance, and voltage gap between discharge and charge potentials at a certain current density are usually collected for comparison.65,66

3. Metal sulfides derived from MOFs for electrocatalysis

3.1 Metal sulfides derived from MOFs for HERs

TMSs are ideal electrocatalysts for replacing Pt in HERs due to their intrinsic high activity, abundant active sites, and high reserve. However, the low conductivity of TMSs limited their performance.67 Constructing TMSs/carbon hybrids is an effective method to improve the electronic conductivity. For the preparation of TMSs from MOFs, carbon can be derived from the decomposition of organic ligands and TMSs can be obtained by directly calcining MOFs in the presence of S-containing compounds. By solid grinding thiourea with Co-MOFs followed by thermal annealing at high temperatures, Li et al. prepared N, S co-doped carbon-encapsulated Co9S8 nanoparticles, and a low overpotential of 86.4 mV was achieved at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 due to the synergistic effect of carbon and Co9S8 active species.68 By thermal annealing ZIF-67 in the presence of S powders, Mujtaba et al. also obtained a Co9S8-decorated N-doped carbon layer, which could effectively catalyze HERs under both acidic and basic conditions.69 He et al. prepared amorphous metal sulfides (CoSx) via controllable electrochemical conversion of ZIF-67, and the optimized catalyst exhibited a small overpotential of 168 mV in a neutral electrolyte.70 In addition, the conversion mechanism and correlation between the concentration of S22âˆ’ sites and the HER activity were revealed, providing a new platform for preparing metal sulfides from MOFs. In another work, Wang et al. found that thiourea could not only lead to the formation of TMSs, but also lead to N doping, and nitrogen-rich CoS2@nitrogen-doped carbon was prepared. Compared with CoS2@nitrogen-doped carbon prepared using a S powder as a sulfur source, N-CoS2@NC exhibited a higher HER catalytic activity due to N doping and more Coâ€“Nx active species.71

Constructing proper nanostructures could improve the catalytic performance of TMSs due to the more exposed active sites and fast mass transport. Using ZIF-67 as the precursor followed by three successive sulfidation processes, Niu et al. prepared CoS2â€“C@CoS2 yolkâ€“shell nanocages. The unique structure endows the catalyst with a high specific surface area of 161 m2 gâˆ’1 and much interior voids (Fig. 3a).72 Combined with the chemical compositions and high conductive carbon substrate, CoS2â€“C@CoS2 exhibited a small overpotential of 79.1 mV in 0.5 M H2SO4 at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 and a small Tafel slope of 51.9 mV decâˆ’1 (Fig. 3b and Table 1).

Doping metal ions into TMSs is an effective strategy to improve their HER performance due to the changed electronic state and band gap of TMSs.73,74 Using MOFs as precursors to avoid the differences in catalytic activity brought by morphology, Du et al. investigated Fe, Ni and Zn-doped Co9S8 nanoarrays via both theory and experiment.75 The H2O adsorption models were optimized and DFT calculations indicated that Niâ€“Co9S8 showed a stronger chemical adsorption of H2O, which could promote the water splitting kinetics (Fig. 3câ€“h). In addition, Niâ€“Co9S8 exhibited a smaller Gibbs free energy of adsorbed hydrogen, suggesting a smaller activation energy of transition state species on the Niâ€“Co9S8 surface during the HER process (Fig. 3i). Moreover, more electrons near the Fermi level were indicated by the density of states (DOS), indicating a better conductivity of Niâ€“Co9S8. As a result, Niâ€“Co9S8 exhibited the best HER performance with an overpotential of 90 mV at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 3j). The effect of metal ion doping was also demonstrated by Huang et al., where Zn, Ni and Cu-doped Co3S4 were developed for HERs using ZIF-67 as the precursor (Fig. 3k).76 DFT calculations indicated that the doping of Zn2+, Ni2+ and Cu2+ could narrow the band gap and lead to the improved excitation of charge to the conduction band, thereby improved conductivity was observed. In addition, Zn-doped Co3S4 exhibited the smallest free energy for H adsorption, suggesting its proper electronic structure for HERs (Fig. 3l). Electrochemical measurements indicate that Zn0.30Co2.70S4 exhibited good HER activity over a wide pH range (Fig. 3mâ€“o).

MoS2 has been considered as a highly efficient HER electrocatalyst due to its similar hydrogen adsorption free energy with Pt.77 Therefore, MoS2-based TMSs are extensively investigated for HERs. Tang et al. prepared MoS2/CoS2 nanotube arrays supported on a piece of carbon cloth (CC) using Co-MOFs as the precursor followed by a two-step sulfurization reaction with (NH4)2MoS4 and thioacetamide (TAA) respectively (Fig. 4a).78 The hollow heterostructure exhibited superior HER performance with small overpotentials of 85 and 90 mV in 0.5 M H2SO4 and 1 M KOH. The interface interaction of MoS2 and CoS2 was revealed by the shift of XPS binding energies of Co, Mo and S. The DFT calculations indicated that more electrons would accumulate around Coâ€“S bonds at the interfaces, and electrons in the fully empty states are localized at the Sâ€“S bridging S22âˆ’ ligands, which could improve the HER activity (Fig. 4b and c). In addition, a reduced Gibbs free energy for H adsorption was observed, further demonstrating the advantages of the heterostructure (Fig. 4d and e). The synergistic effects of CoS2â€“MoS2 and Ni3S2â€“MoS2 were also demonstrated by Hou et al., where polyoxometalate-based MOFs were used as precursors, and low overpotentials for HERs were achieved.79

After further coating MoS2 using Na2MoO4 and TAA, a MoS2/Co3S4 hollow hybrid was successfully prepared. Due to the hollow structure and the interaction of MoS2 and Co3S4, the catalyst exhibited an over potential of 175 mV at 10 mA cmâˆ’2. Co9S8â€“MoS2 composites also exhibited good HER performance, as demonstrated by Ganesan's work, where hierarchical Co9S8â€“MoS2 nanoboxes were prepared using a ZIF-67 nanocube as the precursor.81 The Co9S8â€“MoS2 exhibited a small overpotential of 106 mV in 0.5 M H2SO4 and a small Tafel slope of 51.8 mV decâˆ’1. Combined with the high conductivity induced by the porous carbon, the Co9S8-NC@MoS2 exhibited good performance over a wide pH range (Fig. 4g).

The important roles of nanostructures and components of TMSs were further revealed by Yang and coworkers.83 Using ZIF-67 as the precursor, a yolkâ€“shelled NC-CoS2@CoS2/MoS2 catalyst was developed by an ionâ€“exchange reaction with (NH4)2MoS4 and a sequence sulfurization reaction with sulfur powders. To further enlarge the synergistic effect of various metal sulfides, TMSs with multiple metal sites were developed. 3.2 Metal sulfides derived from MOFs for OERs

Besides HERs, TMSs are promising electrocatalytic materials for OERs due to their proper adsorption energy for O and OH species.87,88 Using ZIF-67 nanocubes as the precursor, Guo et al. prepared hollow CoS2 nanocubes via an ionâ€“exchange reaction in thioacetamide solutions.89 The hollow nanostructure enabled a large electrolyteâ€“catalyst interaction and a rapid mass diffusion pathway. Combined with highly active CoS2, the CoS2 nanocube only needed 290 mV overpotential to reach a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2. Using a similar anion exchange reaction of Na2S with Co3[Fe(CN)6]2, Cai and coworkers prepared a hollow amorphous CoSx catalyst.90 Extended X-ray absorption near-edge spectroscopy indicated that there are partly Coâ€“O bonds in the catalyst, and DFT calculations suggested that the incorporation of oxygen into amorphous CoSx could strengthen the adsorption of O-containing intermediates, thereby improving the OER performance of the catalyst accordingly.

The OER performance of TMSs could be further improved by incorporating carbon substrates to improve the electron transfer during the reaction process. In addition, carbon could further prevent the TMS nanoparticles from aggregation. In a recent work, by coating ZIF on the surface of a CdS nanowire followed by thermal annealing, Wu et al. prepared N, S co-doped hierarchical carbon nanofiber-encapsulated Co9S8 nanoparticles for OERs (Fig. 6a).91 During the heat treatment process, the CdS was reduced to evaporative Cd and S diffused into the carbon framework and reacted with Co, forming Co9S8 and N, S co-doped carbon. The evaporation of Cd could further improve the porosity of the carbon. Due to the synergistic effect of hierarchical carbon and Co9S8, the optimized catalyst Co9S8/NSCNFs-850 could afford a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 at a small overpotential of 302 mV (Fig. 6b and Table 2).

Heteroatom doping is another effective strategy to improve the catalytic activity of TMSs, where the electronic structure of TMSs could be tuned to optimize the adsorption of intermediates.92,93 Wang et al. prepared Fe-doped Co9S8 from Fe-doped Co-MOF nanosheets and investigated the role of Fe doping by DFT calculations (Fig. 6c).94 It has been found that Fe doping could induce a decrease in dxz-orbital density of Co around the Fermi level, probably due to the partial charge transfer from Co2+ to Fe2+ (Fig. 6d). In addition, the d-band center of Feâ€“Co9S8 shifted away from the Fermi level, which could boost the desorption of oxygen-containing intermediates on the surface of the catalyst and improve the OER catalytic activity accordingly (Fig. 6e).

Constructing heterostructures could also alter the electronic structure of TMSs, and the synergistic effect of different components could further improve the catalytic activity.95,96 Using Znâ€“Co-ZIF as a precursor followed by sulphuration with S powders, Chen et al. prepared Co9S8/Znâ€“Coâ€“S heterostructure-decorated carbon nanocages (Fig. 6f).97 The carbon substrate could improve the electronic conductivity of the metal sulfides, and the interconnected nanorods that extruded from the rough surface of the carbon polyhedral could facilitate ion and electron transport (Fig. 6g). In addition, the formation of Co9S8/Zn0.8Co0.2S heterostructures induced the enlargement of the lattice parameter, due to the electron injection from Zn0.8Co0.2S into Co9S8. The electrochemical measurement indicated that the overpotential of Co9S8/Zn0.8Co0.2S for OERs decreased greatly compared with single-component catalysts or a mixture of them (Fig. 6h). DFT calculations suggested that the charge injection could decrease the formation energy of *OOH species, therefore promoting the formation of O2 (Fig. 6i and j). In another work, CeOx/CoS heterostructures with abundant grain boundaries and lattice defects were developed and improved OER catalytic performance was achieved due to the lattice strain induced by the strong coupled CeOx/CoS units and the high-energy interfacial structures induced by high-index CeOx nanoparticles.98

Nickel sulfides are also good candidates for replacing RuO2 as promising OER catalysts and the OER performance of nickel sulfides with different crystal phases was also investigated. In a recent work, Wan et al. prepared a hierarchical Ni3S4 architecture using a Ni-MOF as the precursor followed by an anion exchange reaction with Na2S, and the role of Ni3+ for OERs was revealed by both theoretical and experimental results (Fig. 7a).99 By adjusting the reaction temperature, the Ni3+/Ni2+ ratio could be effectively adjusted and the catalyst with a higher Ni3+ content tends to exhibit a higher catalytic activity. DFT calculations indicated that compared with Ni2+ coordinated with four S atoms, Ni3+ coordinated with six S atoms showed much lower chemisorption free energy, which could facilitate electron transfer to the surface during the OER process. In addition, the hexacoordinated Ni6 exhibited a longer Oâ€“H band distance and a shorter Niâ€“O band, demonstrating improved activation of the Oâ€“H band of the Ni3+ sites (Fig. 7bâ€“d). In another work, Lin et al. prepared a 2D Niâ€“Ni3S2@carbon nanoplate with a thickness less than 20 nm from MOF nanoplates and the catalyst exhibited a low overpotential of 287 mV to deliver a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 7e).100 The good performance could be ascribed to the thin 2D morphology that provided abundant active sites, the high conductive graphite carbon layers facilitating the charge transfer from the inner alloy core and the coexistence of Ni particles to lower the resistance.

Compared with non-self-supporting catalysts, which usually need adhesives to bond them to the working electrode, self-supporting catalysts in situ grown on conductive substrates have several advantages.103,104 On the one hand, the high conductivity of the catalysts could be ensured and the contact area of catalysts and electrolyte are enlarged without the usage of polymer binders. 4. Conclusions and outlooks

Developing low-cost and highly efficient electrocatalysts is a promising strategy for the large-scale application of clean electrochemical energy storage and conversion systems including Znâ€“air batteries and water-splitting devices. TMSs have gained great research interest owing to their high intrinsic catalytic activities, adjustable electronic structures, diverse components, and morphologies. Generally, the performance of TMSs can be improved by increasing the number of active sites via nanostructure engineering and edge engineering, or by increasing the intrinsic activity of each active site via interface engineering, heteroatom doping and so on. MOFs are ideal precursors for the preparation of TMSs due to their various components including diverse metal anodes and ligands. In addition, MOFs could act as hosts to incorporate guest molecules, which could further introduce metal species that are difficult to coordinate with organic ligands. Moreover, the morphology of MOFs can be readily controlled by changing the synthesis conditions such as solvents and temperatures.

In this review, recent progress of MOF-derived TMSs was summarized based on the applications of TMSs including HERs, OERs, ORRs, overall water splitting and Znâ€“air batteries. In each section, the preparation methods, structures and electrocatalytic activities were introduced and the possible mechanisms underlying the improved catalytic activity were revealed to give readers an overall comprehension on the progress of the MOF-derived TMSs.

Although great progress has been achieved in the development of MOF-derived TMSs, several major challenges still exist in both the academic and industrial viewpoints, and great breakthroughs can be achieved from the following aspects (Fig. 15):


Fig. 15 Challenges and opportunities for preparing high-performance TMSs.

(i) Expending the variety of MOF precursors. It is known that one of the main advantages of MOFs is their diversity, which enables MOFs with various metal centers, ligands, and topologies. However, nowadays, most of the MOFs used to prepare TMSs are based on ZIF-8 and ZIF-67, and the metal centers are usually centered on Ni, Co and Mo, which greatly restrict the development of TMSs. Therefore, it is urgent to expend the variety of MOF precursors to develop new TMSs with more diverse morphologies, components, and favorable electronic structures to boost the catalytic performance. Especially, it is proposed that the introduction of In could promote the reversible oxygen electrode reactions of CoIn2S4 and NiIn2S4.144,145 Therefore, developing In-based sulfides from In-MOFs may be a promising strategy. In addition, the carbon substrate derived from the decomposition of organic ligands in MOFs has been demonstrated to have great effects on the performance of metal sulfides/carbon composites. Nevertheless, most of the works are mainly focused on the N species in the resulting carbon. It should be noted that the O atom is also a heteroatom that has distinctive properties from C. Therefore, the role of O species in carbon can be investigated by designing oxygen-containing ligands in MOFs.

(ii) Revealing real catalytically active species. In order to design more superior catalysts, it is important to reveal the catalytic active sites. Till now, theoretical calculations could predict the catalytic active sites on a large extent by optimizing the proper adsorption model and adsorption energy. However, it should be noted that metal sulfides will undergo phase transformation during the catalytic process, especially under oxidation conditions. For example, metal oxides and hydroxides are usually formed after a time of catalytic reaction. However, most of the theoretical models constructed nowadays are still based on metal sulfides rather than metal sulfides with a layer of metal oxides or hydroxides, which may lead to errors in identifying the real active sites. Therefore, it is important to construct more accurate models to predict the active sites.

(iii) Understanding the catalytic reaction processes and reaction mechanisms. As electrocatalysis reactions take place at the three-phase interface and lots of intermediates are involved in the reaction processes, monitoring the reaction intermediates is essential for the understanding of reaction mechanisms. In situ and operando characterization technologies such as Raman spectroscopy, X-ray diffraction (XRD), X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), ambient-pressure XPS, and electrochemical mass spectrometry (MS) may be powerful tools and the related investigation is rare.

(iv) Expending the applications of TMSs. Nowadays, most of the investigations about MOF-derived TMSs for electrocatalysis are focused on HERs, ORRs and OERs. It should be noted that TMSs have a similar structure to nitrogenase, and therefore, the exploration of nitrogen reduction reaction catalytic activity of TMSs is also meaningful. In addition, electrochemical carbon dioxide reduction reactions, which are also a good strategy for the resource utilization of carbon dioxide, involve electron transfer and hydrogenation processes similar to NRRs, and TMSs may have the potential to activate CO2 and transform it into useful chemical fuels.

(v) Developing more advanced bifunctional or multiple functional electrocatalysts. As stated above, HERs and OERs are involved in overall water splitting and OERs and ORRs are involved in rechargeable Znâ€“air batteries. Therefore, the development of bifunctional electrocatalysts is important to simplify the operating systems to reduce the cost. However, the HER, OER and ORR undergo three different catalytic routes, and the increase in one catalytic performance is always accompanied by a decrease in another catalytic activity, which limited the development of excellent bifunctional catalysts. Recently, it has been proposed that single-atom catalysts could realize almost 100% atom utilization; therefore, decorating highly active precious metal single atoms into TMSs may be a good route to improve the performance of bifunctional electrocatalysts. In addition, high-entropy alloys and compounds have shown great potential in diverse fields owing to their distinctive concept and unique properties. By taking advantage of the metallic diversity of MOFs, it is very attractive to prepare high-entropy metal sulfides from MOFs.
