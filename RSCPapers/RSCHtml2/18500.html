Abstract

One of the major issues in tissue engineering is regulation of stem cell differentiation toward specific lineages. Unlike biological and chemical signals, physical signals with adjustable properties can be applied to stem cells in a timely and localized manner, thus making them a hot topic for research in the fields of biomaterials, tissue engineering, and cell biology. According to the signals sensed by cells, physical signals used for regulating stem cell fate can be classified into six categories: mechanical, light, thermal, electrical, acoustic, and magnetic. In most cases, external macroscopic physical fields cannot be used to modulate stem cell fate, as only the localized physical signals accepted by the surface receptors can regulate stem cell differentiation via nanoscale fibrin polysaccharide fibers. However, surface receptors related to certain kinds of physical signals are still unknown. Recently, significant progress has been made in the development of functional materials for energy conversion. Consequently, localized physical fields can be produced by absorbing energy from an external physical field and subsequently releasing another type of localized energy through functional nanostructures. Based on the above concepts, we propose a methodology that can be utilized for stem cell engineering and for the regulation of stem cell fate via nanostructure-mediated physical signals. In this review, the combined effect of various approaches and mechanisms of physical signals provides a perspective on stem cell fate promotion by nanostructure-mediated physical signals. We expect that this review will aid the development of remote-controlled and wireless platforms to physically guide stem cell differentiation both in vitro and in vivo, using optimized stimulation parameters and mechanistic investigations while driving the progress of research in the fields of materials science, cell biology, and clinical research.

1 Introduction

Tissue or organ damage is difficult to heal with traditional medical approaches; tissue engineering has emerged as a promising treatment method over time.1 Seed cells, scaffolds, and growth factors are the three essential elements of tissue engineering, which can be combined to build artificial tissues or organs in vitro and in vivo. Stem cells, including adult and embryonic stem cells, are important seed cells. Adult stem cells, such as hematopoietic stem cells, mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), neural stem cells (NSCs) and muscle satellite cells, are a kind of undifferentiated cells located within a differentiated tissue, organ or organism;2 while embryonic stem cells (ESCs) derive from inner cell mass of blastocyst-stage embryos.3 Various kinds of stem cells have immensely promoted the progress of tissue engineering, even though controllable stem cell differentiation is still a challenge.4–7

The stem cell niche is a diverse, complex, and dynamic microenvironment, and the extracellular matrix (ECM) plays an important role in this niche.8,9 Natural ECM networks consist of functional macromolecules, such as proteins and proteoglycans.10 The ECM provides structural support to the organs and tissues, as the outside layers form the basement membranes and act as the substrates of single cells for migration.10 The ECM has many functions besides supporting cells.11,12 ECM proteins typically include multiple, independently folded domains with conserved sequences and arrangement. Some domains of ECM proteins bind adhesion receptors, such as integrins, which mediate the cell–matrix adhesion and transduce signals into cells, while other receptors bind soluble growth factors and determine their activity, distribution, and transportation. Specific nanostructures of well-organized ECM proteins can integrate numerous complex signals and control cell development and differentiation as well as other behaviors of these cells. Therefore, nanostructured ECM is a crucial platform for cell fate regulation (Fig. 1a).13

Among these receptors, those related to the biomolecule-driven signaling pathways have been widely investigated and their progress has been comprehensively reviewed in several important articles.15 However, biomolecule cues are generally expensive, easy to degrade, and may exert a negative effect on other adjacent tissues. Moreover, it is difficult to locally regulate these biomolecules in clinics.

Some of the stem cell surface receptors respond to the physical environment or to physical signals accepted by the ECM of stem cells. Therefore, stem cell fate can be regulated by the physical signals surrounding the cells or the physical fields to which the cells are exposed.30–32 Physical signals, such as mechanical signals, light signals, thermal signals, electrical signals, acoustic signals, and magnetic signals, have become attractive candidates for the regulation of stem cell differentiation over the past decades owing to their higher safety, lower cost, and tunable localized properties compared with those of biological and chemical signals. These signals are generally either applied directly on cells or by relying on the construction of the cellular microenvironment by nanomaterials.

Some important reviews on the regulation of stem cell fate by nanomaterials and physical signals are summarized in Table 2. These reviews have highlighted the recent progress in tissue engineering and regenerative therapy and have also provided instructions to regulate stem cell fate decisions. Few investigations concerned with physical signals as the sole regulators of stem cell fate have been comprehensively reviewed.33,34 Previous reviews have focused on the mechanical properties of materials or stimulus-responsive platforms, which have been proven to function as macroscopic regulators.34–37 However, in many cases, the macro magnetic, optical, acoustic, and electrical fields do not influence stem cells significantly, likely due to physical signal receptors being located on the cell membrane and acting through the ECM. Moreover, the strength of the physical signals accepted by the surface receptors from the macroscopic physical field is too small to trigger the signaling pathways. Therefore, emitting macroscopic physical signals to the whole cell population may be ineffective, whereas localized physical signals can be easily recognized by the surface receptors, thereby regulating stem cell fate more efficiently. When applying a macroscopic physical field, a weak field is difficult to trigger the signaling pathway of cells, while a strong field may represent a potential risk to the surrounding tissues. To perform noninvasive stimulation of stem cells, it is necessary to apply indirect physical stimulation using materials. Indeed, significant efforts have been made to investigate the role of nanomaterials in stem cell fate decisions, as they can act as effective regulators by mimicking and affecting the ECM of stem cells.35 Furthermore, both nanomaterial-based cell scaffolds and internalized nanoparticles have been reported to influence stem cell differentiation, which work extracellularly and intracellularly, respectively.38,39

Based on the above opinion, the key to triggering the physical signaling pathways in stem cells is to deliver targeted physical signals to the receptor located on the cell surface. Therefore, the only approach to regulate stem cell fate using physical signals is the local application of these signals on the ECM of specific cells without affecting surrounding cells. It is very difficult to create nanoscale electric fields, magnetic fields, pressure, or light spots in vivo via conventionally designed concepts and existing manufacturing technology. Fortunately, recent advances in nanoscale functional materials facilitate the localized stimulation of surface receptors on stem cells based on the energy conversion properties of some specially designed nanomaterials.51,52 For example, localized electric signals can be produced through the piezoelectric property of the nanomaterials driven by ultrasonic irradiation53 and magnetoelectric response54 or the photoelectric property excited by light irradiation,55 while localized stress can be generated by photo-constriction and magneto-constriction effects of nanostructures driven by light irradiation and an alternating magnetic field.56,57 The details of the mechanisms and the associated nanomaterials will be discussed in the following sections.

In this review, we classify the physical signals into six categories: mechanical, light, thermal, electrical, acoustic, and magnetic, according to the form of external physical stimulation sensed by the cells (Fig. 2), and then explain the intrinsic interactions among various physical signals. The effective signals that drive cell differentiation are also discussed in detail in the following six subsections. In each section, we briefly introduce the effects of physical signals, and then the characteristics of nanostructured materials-mediated physical signals are discussed in detail, especially in terms of regulating stem cell differentiation into specific lineages, such as osteoblasts, chondrocytes, adipocytes, muscle cells, and neurons. Finally, we summarize the current challenges and future outlooks in this field as we believe that these nanostructured materials-mediated physical signals show great potential for in vivo and clinical applications.

2 Mechanical signals

Mechanical signals are more easily sensed by cells when cells interact with materials, and their regulatory effects on stem cells are the prime interest of researchers among various physical signals. Mechanical signals can directly influence the commitment of stem cell fate without stimulation signal conversion.58,59 Summarized from previous studies, the mechanical signals for stem cell fate regulation mainly include two aspects: mechanical force—including shear stress, mechanical strain compression, tension and other types of force—and nanostructured materials-mediated mechanical cues, which mainly consist of substrate topography, substrate stiffness, and some other aspects such as cell rigidity (Table 3).

Under specific conditions and mechanical stimuli, the stem cells can receive the signal input and respond to external cues, which leads to different fate decisions such as muscle cell, osteogenic, or neural differentiation.71 Stem cell differentiation toward terminal mature states, controlled by mechanical factors with high efficiency and directional guidance, would provide supportive guidance for tissue repair, stem cell therapy, and other research areas. Since there are many difficulties in accurately controlling and regulating stem fate decisions and functions both in vivo and in vitro, novel mechanical load equipment and nanomaterial processing technologies would notably facilitate fundamental studies by creating a well-controlled biomechanical microenvironment and investigating the effect of biomechanical stimulation in stem cells. In addition, mechanical signals can also be applied and mediated by thermal signals, light stimulation, electric fields, and magnetic fields, which rely on thermo-sensitive materials, photo-crosslinked hydrogels, converse piezoelectric effect, and magnetic-mechanical coupling through nanostructured materials, respectively. These indirect methods can easily implement localized mechanical stimulation of stem cells and regulate their fate lineages.

2.1 Directly exerted mechanical forces

Mechanical force signals are of great significance in the development and function of tissues and organs, including the lung, heart, muscle, cartilage, and bone. The notion that mechanical force plays an important role in stem cell fate commitment has broadened extensive horizons related to manipulating stem cell fate and led to a more intensive comprehension of stem cell differentiation processes. As illustrated in Fig. 3, various types of mechanical forces exist at the cell, tissue, and organ levels inside the body, including shear stress, cyclic stretch, compression, interstitial flow, and tension.72 In the past decades, many in vitro models that mimic mechanical stimuli in vivo have been established to investigate their effect on stem cell fate commitment. In the following section, we discuss the influence of mechanical signals, such as shear stress and stretch forces, on stem cell fate regulation.

2.1.1 Shear stress. Shear stress plays a vital role in many biological processes and has been extensively studied in recent years, particularly in vascular biology. To generate such biophysical signals of shear stress, microfluidic shear devices73,74 or parallel plate flow chambers61 have been developed to investigate stem cell responses. As shown in Fig. 4a, the pulsatile flow bioreactor includes a peristaltic pump, a glass culture reservoir with inlets and outlets for medium circulation and air exchange, a compliance chamber, and a culture chamber.61 The culture medium for circulation was produced using a peristaltic pump. In this study, shear stress for the stem cells was initially set as at 1 dyne cm−2 and gradually increased to 15 dyne cm−2 over a two-day period. The cells on the scaffolds were then stimulated by 15 dyne cm−2 for another two days. The results demonstrated that shear stress significantly upregulated the expression of endothelial cell-related markers, and downregulated smooth muscle-related markers, compared with those in static control group.

Apart from affecting MSCs, the shear stress can also contribute to the fate commitment of ESCs.60,75,76 Using a similar pulsatile flow bioreactor to the one mentioned above, the effect of shear stress on mouse ESC differentiation was investigated. Fluid shear stress upregulated the expression of Runx1 in CD411c-Kit1 hematopoietic progenitor cells, and strengthened their hematopoietic colony-forming potential.60 Another study showed that ESCs could be directed to differentiate into vascular wall cells by shear stress loading in vitro.75

2.1.2 Mechanical strain. Cells can maintain a dynamic balance of cell-ECM force by adjusting their actin cytoskeleton. External mechanical strain signals can be transduced to stem cells through specific receptors expressed on the cell surfaces, such as integrins, which cause the instantaneous imbalance as well as the reorganization of the actin cytoskeleton. Extensive studies have revealed that mechanical stimulation is effective in guiding stem cell differentiation toward vascular smooth muscle cells,77–79 osteoblasts,80,81 and chondrocytes,82 which may contribute to the functionality evaluation process of stem cell therapy for tissue engineering purposes.

The signals transduced by mechanical strain can change cell shape, activate various signaling pathways, and induce gene transcription resulting in a fate change in stem cell. As shown in Fig. 4b, the long-term effect of local cyclic stress through focal adhesions led to oct3/4 gene downregulation in mouse ESCs.62 The undifferentiated cells were stably transfected with green fluorescent protein (GFP) driven by the oct3/4 promoter with the addition of leukemia inhibitory factor. After a constant stimulus of a local stress (17.5 Pa, 0.3 Hz, 60 min), oct3/4 had a ∼35% downregulation in expression within 24 h, and ∼50% downregulation within 72 h, while control group without stress persisted on oct3/4 expression. This result suggests that local cyclic stress was able to drive an ESC to differentiate through focal adhesions.

Mechanical regulation of stem cell differentiation can also be accomplished using stretch force. A recent study reported that a population of unipotent stem cells could directly sense external stretching in vivo, therefore adjusting their fate decision. This mechanosensing was activated through Piezo and cytosolic Ca2+ increase.63 Moreover, stretch could activate the ion channel Piezo1, which directs fate of human NSCs, and mechanosensing showed a similar pathway.83

Usually, the mechanical strain used for the regulation of stem cells is anisotropic, which may also induce an anisotropic effect on stem cells. The cyclic uniaxial strain on elastic substrates may cause cells to align themselves perpendicular to the strain axis and to anisotropically direct stem cell fate. In a recent report, stem cells were stimulated by a 5% cyclic uniaxial strain (1 Hz) for 2–4 days.84 DNA microarray analysis showed general gene expression changes, including decreases in cartilage matrix markers, an upregulation in the smooth muscle marker, and alterations in cell signaling. Moreover, uniaxial strain increased the proliferation of MSCs. Another study compared the differential effects of equiaxial with uniaxial strain on MSCs, and revealed that cyclic equiaxial strain decreased the expression of SM-22α and SM α-actin in human MSCs (hMSCs) on collagen- or elastin-coated membranes after 1 d, and the expression of α-actin in stress fibers were downregulated.85 However, the expression of SM-22α and SM α-actin was transiently promoted by cyclic uniaxial strain after 1 d, and then decreased to basal levels after the cells grew perpendicular to the direction of strain.

2.2 Nanostructure mediated mechanical signals

In recent years, nanostructured materials have been developed as a controllable interaction between cells and the microenvironment. Physicochemical features of nanostructures, such as substrate stiffness86 (or elasticity) and topography,87,88 can exert an important influence on stem cell behavior and build a differentiation-inducing microenvironment on stem cells. As shown in Fig. 5a and b, stem cells can sense the mechanical signals mediated by variations in substrate stiffness and nanotopography, mainly through integrin-mediated focal adhesion signaling and response to the signals by adjusting the degree of actin polymerization.30,89 Therefore, the cytoskeleton of stem cells can be regulated by the stiffness and topography of biomaterial scaffolds, indicating that the mechanical signals mediated by the nanomaterials can regulate signal transduction of stem cells and subsequently guide their differentiation into specific lineages.

2.2.1 Substrate stiffness mediated mechanical signals. Multiple cues can regulate stem cells differentiation, including the elasticity or compliance of the matrix substrate (Fig. 6a).25 Elastomeric polymer cell culture substrate, such as hydrogels, has been commonly used due to their unique physicochemical properties, which can be controlled by simply adapting the density of crosslinks in the network.90–93 The stiffness of hydrogels could be varied from very soft (<1 kPa in elastic modulus, similar to a viscous fluid such as honey) to rather stiff (∼500 kPa, similar to silicone rubber).45 Hydrogels, including polyacrylamide, hyaluronic acid hydrogels, alginate hydrogels, and polyethylene glycol are frequently used as cell culture materials owing to their easy processing properties. Particularly, stem cells tend to efficiently differentiate into specific tissue lineages in which the tissue elasticity is similar to that of the hydrogel substrate for stem cell culture. hMSCs were cultured on variably compliant polyacrylamide gels, and the results showed that soft matrices that mimic the brain (0.1–1 kPa) were in favor of neurogenesis, stiffer matrices that mimic muscle (8–17 kPa) promote myogenesis, and comparable rigid matrices that mimic collagenous bone (25–40 kPa) promote osteogenesis (Fig. 6b).65

The variable stiffness of cell culture substrates can alter cell morphology and phenotype through focal adhesions, especially in two-dimensional cultures.94,95 A recent study showed that stiff matrix promoted hMSC differentiation into smooth muscle cell lineage, while soft matrix (∼1 kPa) promoted hMSC differentiation into chondrogenic and adipogenic lineages. In addition, both matrix stiffness and TGF-β factors were important in defining a unique differentiation pathway.96 Another study investigated the effect of the stiffness of electrospun cell culture nanofiber substrates on MSC fate decisions. The utilized materials were core–shell poly(ether sulfone)–poly(caprolactone) (PES–PCL) fibers (30.6 MPa) and pure PCL (7.1 MPa), and the modulus difference between the two materials was more than four times. The lower modulus PCL fibers provided more appropriate microenvironments for chondrogenesis of murine embryonic mesenchymal progenitor cells, and the stiffer core–shell PES–PCL fibers supported enhanced osteogenesis.97

The effect of a stiffness gradient of 1.0 ± 0.1 Å kPa mm−1 on hMSCs cultured on a hydrogel was also investigated. The hMSCs undergo directed migration, or durotaxis, up stiffness gradients, rather than remain stationary. hMSCs migrated to a stiffer matrix and then differentiated into a more contractile myogenic phenotype. The phenotype of those cells migrating from soft to stiff regions was not completely altered by the stiff hydrogel, as some cells retained the expression of neural marker. This result indicated that stiffness variation, not just stiffness alone, can be an effective regulator of stem cell behavior.98

In 3D culture systems, the stiffness of scaffolds is also considered as an important regulator for the oriented differentiation of stem cells. As shown in Fig. 6c, mouse MSCs (mMSCs) were encapsulated into a 3D hydrogel, which was constructed by alginate polymers and integrin-binding RGD peptides.66 Osteogenic differentiation was favored at middle E (11–30 kPa), while softer (2.5–5 kPa) microenvironments promoted adipogenic differentiation, indicating the significant role of matrix rigidity in regulating MSC fate. Moreover, another study reported the proliferation and differentiation of rat neural stem cells encapsulated within 3D scaffolds, which was contrasted by alginate hydrogels, and the elastic moduli ranged from 0.18 to 20 kPa. The expression of neuronal marker β-tubulin III was upregulated in the cells encapsulated within the softest hydrogels, of which elastic moduli was comparable to that of brain tissue (∼180 Pa).99

Tissue-specific elastic modulus generated from developmental changes in the ECM may be time-dependent in vivo due to tissue progression in some typical cases. To mimic the temporal change of stiffness in vitro, thiolated hyaluronic acid (HA) hydrogels were crosslinked with poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate, and their properties were tuned by varying the molecular weight of the crosslinker.100 When the hydrogel was tuned to comparably heart muscle development, pre-cardiac cells on collagen-coated HA hydrogels exhibited a 3-fold upregulation of mature cardiac specific markers, and ∼60% maturing muscle fibers formed after two weeks, compared with that of cells on polyacrylamide hydrogels.

A major challenge during stem cell culture is the development of optimal culture conditions and culture substrates to maintain the stemness of stem cells. Muscle stem cells (MuSCs) exhibit steady regenerative ability in vivo but rapidly lose that ability in vitro. Using a bioengineered substrate to mimic the biophysical and biochemical ECM features, researchers have found that substrate elasticity is an effective regulator of MuSC fate during cultivation.67 Unlike cultured on rigid plastic dishes (∼106 kPa), MuSCs cultured on soft hydrogel substrates mimicking the elasticity of muscle (12 kPa) maintained their self-renewal ability in vitro and contributed effectively to muscle regeneration.

The substrate topography designed for stem cell culture can account for cellular responses from attachment, self-renewal, migration to differentiation, and production of new tissues. Various nanoengineering tools and synthesis methods, including electron beam lithography, photolithography, electrospinning, hydrothermal synthesis, and other micro/nanofabrication techniques have been successfully developed and utilized to create experimental platforms to understand the role of substrate topography on stem cell behavior. To date, various nanotopographies of cell culture substrates, including nanofibrous,101 nanopits,69 and nanogroove,102 have been reported. Furthermore, the size, order, and dynamic change of topographic cues, which could be accurately controlled by the advanced manufacturing technology, can also impact pluripotent stem cell fate decisions.

2.2.2 Micro-nanotopography mediated mechanical signals. Previously, numerous studies have been performed to explore the regulatory effect of various substrate morphology cues on stem cell fate.103–106 Recently, control of nanoscale symmetry and disorder in nanoscale substrate surface agglomeration was accomplished, allowing understanding of anisotropy and randomness in the regulation of hMSC differentiation.68 Another study revealed that the differentiation process of human adipose-derived stem cells (hADSCs) could be efficiently controlled through one-dimensional hydroxyapatite nanostructures with tunable lengths.107 In addition, biodegradable polylactic acid nanopillar arrays with different diameters was fabricated using anodic aluminum oxide nanowell arrays with different diameters as templates to explore the effect of the nanopillar diameter on stem cell differentiation. Several studies have noted that stem cell fate can be manually regulated by controlling their shape using artificial micropatterned substrates. Geometric profiles of the designed substrate which increase actomyosin contractility can enhance osteogenesis.110

Topographical cues of substrates may also be designed to maintain the phenotype and multipotency of stem cells. A recent study devised a nanoscale substrate fabricated by electron beam lithography and processed it into thermoplastic PCL by hot embossing. Moreover, the ZnO nanorod arrays maintained the stemness of hADSCs without inhibiting their proliferation and oriented differentiation capabilities.

In contrast with other methods that regulate stem cell fate, nanotopography-driven differentiation has a great advantage in obtaining special cell lineages in specific areas with designed nanostructures. Therefore, we propose a strategy to obtain multilineage cells at the same biomaterial by constructing different nanostructures in different areas to form organoids in vitro. We define this method for the construction of organoids as localized committed differentiation of stem cells. The nanotopography can promote osteogenic differentiation with the presence of osteogenic induction medium. For example, Liu et al. constructed a TiO2 nanorod array locally on a well-polished TiO2 ceramic plate to form a TiO2 nanostructure–TiO2 smooth surface periodic stripe pattern through a microfabrication-hydrothermal process.113 They cultured MSCs in the osteogenic induction medium and found that cells cultured on the nanostructured TiO2 strongly differentiated into osteogenic cells, and those on the smooth ceramic parts maintained stemness compared with those on culture plates in the same culture medium (Fig. 9). This confirmed that differentiation can occur locally on specially designed nanostructure parts and can achieve localized committed differentiation of stem cells on the same surface of biomaterials.

As discussed above, the physical properties and nanotopography can regulate stem cell differentiation, which can be used to control the differentiation at localized areas of biomaterials and have great applications in tissue engineering. To date, the mechanism of nanotopograhy-driven regulation of stem cell differentiation and the relationship between physical property/nanotopography and surface receptors of stem cells have not been well understood. It is still very difficult to theoretically predict stem cell fate for a biomaterial with specific stiffness and designed nanotopography. The investigation in this field remains at the experimental level, and interdisciplinary research between biophysics and stem cell biology should be the final approach to solve the theoretical problems of this topic.

2.3 External field responsive materials mediate mechanical signals

2.3.1 Temperature-driven thermo-sensitive material mediate mechanical signals. Topography cues of cell culture substrates have been utilized to mimic the stem cell microenvironment using various methods in an attempt to alter cell shape, spreading, migration, and even differentiation. However, many reported substrates for stem cell culture are usually limited to static models that inadequately imitate naturally dynamic cellular microenvironments. Therefore, designing more intelligent biomaterials is necessary to dynamically reproduce stem cell microenvironments and facilitate definite commitment to stem cell fate. This demands the rational design of spatiotemporal response biomaterials to fulfill the need for dynamic stem cell culture. From the synthesis and control aspects of intelligent biomaterials reported in numerous studies, thermo-sensitive materials may serve as suitable candidates for dynamic response substrates. Thermo-responsive biomaterials for stem cell culture can be divided into shape memory polymer (SMP) and thermo-sensitive hydrogel (TSH). Both types of substrates have been researched for stem cell fate regulation in recent years.

SMPs possess the ability to “memorize” their original shape through covalent or physical crosslinking of two different components. These polymers consist of a fixed phase and a reversible phase, of which the fixed phase is responsible for the storage and recovery of the original shape, and the reversible phase is responsible for the shape change. They can deform by heating the polymer above its glass transition temperature (Tg) and stored in a temporary shape after cooling back down past their Tg. The temporary shape could recover back to its original shape when triggered by various types of cues, including temperature, force, water, and electricity. The SMPs most investigated in the past years for cell culture are thermos-induced SMPs. Lee et al. reported a cell substrate with dynamic surface roughness based on shape memory acrylate networks for differentiation of human MG63 osteoblast-like cells.115 As shown in Fig. 11a, the surface roughness could be reduced to smooth using compression (0 h) and fully recover within 24 h under culture conditions. This strategy of a SMP with dynamic surface roughness may improve osseointegration. Microstructures of SMP-based cell substrates for stem cell fate regulation have also been developed. As shown in Fig. 11b, micro-well substrates were fabricated through thermal lithography with arrays of triangle-, square-, hexagon- or round-shaped microwells to direct MSC differentiation, which were composed of a biocompatible and biodegradable SMP network.116 Moreover, parallel microgroove patterns on the SMP substrate surface were fabricated via thermal embossing lithography (Fig. 11c).117 Dynamically tunable surface microstructures can direct MSCs differentiation.

TSHs are another frequently utilized thermo-responsive biomaterial for cell culture. Among the various kinds of TSHs, poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) (PNIPAAm) and chitosan-based composite hydrogels have been favored because of their good biocompatibility and biodegradability. These TSHs are known to be thermo-responsive, maintain the gel state, and dissolve once they are cooled below its lower critical solution temperature (LCST). PNIPAAm possesses a LCST (32 °C) at which the macromolecular structure transforms from a hydrophilic to a hydrophobic state. The unique properties of this material enabled the 3D culture of MSCs and the regulation of stem cell fate.119,120 Moreover, adjusting the oligomer length and maintaining the same monomer composition and oligomer chemical structure can independently regulate the hydrogel modulus. TSHs with different moduli could regulate the proliferation rate and degree of differentiation (Fig. 11d).118 TSHs combined with electrospun technology have also been developed to regulate stem cell fate. Dang et al. fabricated thermally responsive electrospun nanofibers for MSCs culture and found that the nanostructure of the nanofiber could induce myogenic differentiation, and the thermo-responsive property enabled cell sheet production.121 This strategy provides an effective method for the production of tissue-engineered cell sheets. Brunelle et al. synthesized an electrospun thermos-sensitive hydrogel scaffold for the differentiation of hMSCs.122 The 37 °C-temperature exposure induced phase transition of the hydrogel to a gel state, and thus effectively encapsulated the stem cells in a 3D hydrogel. They found that the 3D hydrogel could enhance the chondrogenesis of hMSCs according to chondrocytic gene and protein expression.

2.3.2 Photo-driven crosslinking degree derived mechanical signals of hydrogels. UV light plays a vital role in the photo-crosslinked hydrogels for two-dimensional (2D) or 3D culture, despite the potential risk of short-time UV irradiation.123

HA is a natural component of the ECM that participates in many biological processes. To obtain crosslinking groups, Guvendiren et al. functionalized HA with methacrylic anhydride (MA) to prepare methacrylated hyaluronic acid (MeHA) hydrogels, and dithiothreitol (DTT) was introduced into the MeHA solution to obtain uniform mechanical properties.124 The MeHA hydrogels can be stiffened by UV irradiation-triggered crosslinking (Fig. 12a), exhibiting tunable Young's moduli. hMSCs were seeded on MeHA hydrogel substrates in situ for 2D culture, in which the spread area and traction can be altered by different stiffness (soft, 3 kPa; stiff, 30 kPa; soft-to-stiff, 3–30 kPa). During long-term cultivation for up to two weeks, the differentiation of hMSCs can be regulated by the moment of stiffening, that is, later stiffening favored adipogenesis, while earlier stiffening favored osteogenesis. These findings clearly illustrate that photo-crosslinked hydrogels can temporally mediate the mechanical properties of stem cell multilineage differentiation in 2D culture models.

2.3.3 Electric field-driven mechanical signals mediated by piezoelectric thin films. Moreover, materials converse piezoelectric effects can be accomplished by applying an excitation electric field to generate mechanical deformation, thus affecting cellular behavior as a substrate. Frias et al. cultured MC3T3-E1 cells on piezoelectric film polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) with alternating current (AC) stimulation of 5 V at 1 Hz and 3 Hz for 15 min and found that piezoelectric materials can be used as efficient mechanical stimulation generators through electrical-mechanical transformation processes (Fig. 12b).125 Converse piezoelectric effects on stem cell fate decision have been rarely investigated until now, making it possible to develop novel piezoelectric biomaterial-based tissue repair strategies through mechano-sensing and mechano-transduction.

Although the direct current can induce mechanical signals by converse piezoelectric effect and affect cell behavior, the complex effect of signals mixed with electricity and stretch compression makes the mechanism complicated and difficult to explain. In addition, this system is difficult to be translated into clinical applications, not only because PVDF is not biodegradable, but also because the essential electric wires in this system will cause serious problems in vivo.

2.3.4 Magnetic field-driven mechanical signals mediated by nanostructures. As mentioned above, mechanical forces play an essential role in regulating stem cell behavior and fate, which has promoted the rapid development of mechanobiology. Apart from the directly applied mechanical forces including compression, traction, shear stress, and some other types of force with mechanical movement and topography-induced localized force, the remote-control mechanical force mediated by magnetic fields and materials may provide additional possibility and predictability for stem cell fate commitment. Based on the functions of specific nanostructures, some magnetic field-responsive nanomaterials can convert the energy of a moving magnetic field into mechanical force, which can be utilized to efficiently regulate cell behavior or fate. Herein, the recent progress in magnetic field-driven mechanical signals mediated by nanomaterials for stem cell fate regulation is briefly reviewed.

The nanostructures that can be driven by a magnetic field to form mechanical signals include magnetic nanoparticles, magnetic hydrogels, and some nanomaterials with magneto-construction properties.

Fe3O4 magnetic nanoparticles are the most popular magnetic materials in biomedical applications because of their good biocompatibility and easy synthesis. The mechanical force can be generated by the effect of a static or alternating external magnetic field on magnetic nanoparticles that are attached to a surface or inside an object. When magnetic nanoparticles are taken in a stem cell or firmly attached to the surface of stem cells, the mode of mechanical load on the stem cells can be different according to the application method of magnetic fields, which can cause different effects of Fe3O4 nanoparticle-mediated mechanical signals. Jiang et al. synthesized Fe3O4/bovine serum albumin (BSA) particles with a diameter below 200 nm for the magnetic control of bone marrow MSC (BM-MSC) stimulation.132 The Fe3O4/BSA particles taken by BM-MSCs combined with a constant static magnetic field could significantly promote the osteogenic differentiation of MSCs, while the individual magnetic particles or external magnetic field did not influence the osteogenic differentiation potential of MSCs. Another study reported that Fe3O4 magnetic nanoparticles together with an external magnetic field were used as stimulation for BM-MSC fate regulation.133 Cells that incorporated magnetic nanoparticles were pelleted and subjected to a static magnetic field and/or magnet-derived shear stress. The results showed that the magnetic-based mechanical force could facilitate chondrogenic differentiation.

Stem cells can sense and respond to the surrounding microenvironment according to changes in physical properties through the binding effect of integrin on the cell membrane.134 By manipulating the binding state between stem cells and implants, it is possible to control stem cell fate decisions. Magnetic control of adhesive ligand presentation, such as RGD, serves as an appealing strategy for the remote manipulation of cell-implant interactions. In recent years, Bian's group has completed a series of investigations on the remote control of RGD ligands using various methods and the regulation effect on the adhesion, spreading, and differentiation of hMSCs.135–137 Their work mainly utilized the combination of superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles conjugated with the RGD ligand and anchored to a glass substrate by a long flexible poly(ethylene glycol) flexible linker as the magnetically tunable oscillatory motion for the regulation of hMSCs. Different strategies based on controllable PEG linker molecular weight, the application of oscillation motion frequency controlled by the magnetic field, and reversible caging and uncaging of the RGD ligand accounted for the tunable adhesion of stem cells on the implant, thus enabling the fate commitment of hMSCs.

Furthermore, the attached proteins, ion channels, or lipids on the stem cell membrane could be designed as mechanotransduction targets to modulate cell fate, as it may induce the initiation of a variety of signaling pathways. By conjugating magnetic nanoparticles with specific antibodies that target cell surface mechanosensitive receptors, the mechanical signals mediated by magnetic nanoparticles and magnetic field would further elicit specific signaling pathways for cell differentiation commitment. The transmembrane ion channel stretch-activated potassium channel (TREK-1) and RGD-coated magnetic particles combined with a magnetic field were utilized as the mechanical signals for human bone marrow stromal cell differentiation commitment.138 The mechanotransduction signal stimulation for bone marrow stromal cells labeled with TREK-1 or RGD magnetic nanoparticles promoted differentiation with different degrees of specific marker expression toward the osteochondral lineage. Another study reported that magnetically targeting and activating two specific mechanical-sensitive cell membrane receptors, platelet-derived growth factor receptor α (PDGFRα) and integrin ανβ3, could also be targets for magneto-mechanical stimulation of human bone marrow-derived MSCs toward osteogenic differentiation.139

In addition to the magneto-mechanical force generated by the magnetic particles at the nanoscale level, the devices of coupling the magnetic nanoparticles into a controllable system also emerge as a potential strategy for stem cell regulation. As shown in Fig. 12c, a 3D magnetic tissue stretcher was developed to mechanically control the differentiation of ESCs.126 The ESCs incorporated with magnetic nanoparticles were capable of sensing the mechanical force generated by the magnetic stretcher. This design enabled ESCs differentiation toward the mesodermal cardiac pathway. Another study incorporated magnetic nanoparticles into microscale hydrogels to probe strain-induced cell responses.140 This magnetic hydrogel enabled the application of a relatively homogeneous deformation following non-contact actuation under external magnetic fields for cell regulation. Park et al. presented a biological microelectromechanical system that is mainly made of an actuator and a cartridge-type chamber to apply a cyclic compressive load on MSCs of various magnitudes.141 The cyclic load for stem cells was applied at 1 Hz frequency for 10 min, twice a day. The culture results showed that the designed system-derived magnetic-driven mechanical force could enhance the chondrogenic differentiation of MSCs, even in the absence of exogenous inducing factor TGF-β.

In addition to the magnetic nanoparticles tightly attached to the surface of the stem cells can affect their fate by magnetic field-derived mechanical force, the nanoparticles endocytosed by the stem cell can also be influenced by the magnetic field and suffer mechanical force inside the stem cells. This effect of mechanical signals on stem cell fate has rarely been investigated, but it should become a very attractive topic in stem cell fields.

As discussed above, mechanical signals that originate from different microenvironments or physical fields play an important role in the regulation of stem cell fate. In fact, although all the above methods based on different physical mechanisms can be used to tune stem cell fate, they each have advantages and disadvantages, which determine their potential application prospects. Directly exerted mechanical forces are the most powerful tool for mechanical signal regulation of stem cell fate; however, its use in clinical applications is improbable because mechanical signal production relies on instruments that are unsuitable for in vivo application. Substrate stiffness and micro-nanotopography-mediated mechanical signals are very promising methods for clinical tissue reparation applications, because the substrate can be chosen as a biodegradable material, induce direct differentiation of stem cells to obtain different functional cells at different parts of the biomaterials, and possibly help construct organoids in vitro. However, it is very difficult to control the mechanical signal applied to the stem cell quantitatively and in a timely manner. The advantage of temperature-driven mechanical signals mediated by thermo-sensitive material is that this method can regulate stem cell fate in vitro because laser heating is easily applied to the cells through thermo-sensitive material, and the redundant heat can disperse to the culture media. If this method is used for tissue regeneration in vivo, the laser heating process becomes complicated or impossible because of the light irradiation depth limitation and the difficulty in avoiding heating effects on the adjacent cells or tissue. The hydrogel photo-driven crosslinking is a unique method to apply mechanical signals to the cells, but it also poses complications when used in vivo. Electric field-driven piezoelectric thin film-mediated mechanical signals is an attractive method for in vitro demonstration of the mechanical signals that affect stem cell differentiation because it is very easy to control the force intensity and time, and to observe the cell morphology over time. However, PVDF is not biodegradable, and electricity-exerting equipment and connection wires are essential in this system, which will bring great difficulty in clinical applications. Magnetic field-driven mechanical signals mediated by nanostructures are one of the most promising methods for the regulation of stem cell fate both in vitro and in vivo. As a non-contact field for driving mechanical signal production, this approach has great potential in vivo and will have great applications in clinical tissue repair applications.

3 Magnetic signals

The magnetic field, one of the basic physical factors in the natural environment, is pervasive throughout the environment owing to modern technological developments. Moreover, the magnetic field signal has a significant effect on cells, tissues, and many physiological processes within organisms, which play essential roles in many biological processes of stem cells, including self-renewal, migration, lineage differentiation, and apoptosis.142 Research on the effect of magnetic signals on stem cell fate decisions can be predominantly divided into three categories according to the origin of the magnetic field: (1) macroscopic magnetic field signals, (2) nanomaterial-mediated-magnetic field signals and magnetic field, and (3) light-driven nanostructure-mediated plasmon-induced magnetic resonance. Specifically, a magnetic field with tunable frequency and field strength for signal input, whether the signals are mediated by nanomaterials, provides a superior and accurate external trigger for stem cell fate regulation. From this perspective, applying magnetic field signals has been utilized as an important approach to control the behavior and response of stem cells noninvasively and precisely, although the mechanism of how and why the magnetic field influences stem cell fate remains poorly understood. The current progress of the magnetic field research on the fate commitment of stem cells will be described in three parts, as mentioned above.

3.1 Macroscopic magnetic signals

Macroscopic magnetic signals are those directly generated by electromagnets or permanent magnets and can be divided into (1) static magnetic fields (SMFs), (2) pulsed electromagnetic fields (PEMFs), (3) alternating electromagnetic fields and (4) rotating magnetic fields.143 Among the different types of magnetic fields, the SMFs create a single magnetic field that can be easily generated using a permanent magnet and enables SMF signals to be feasible for long-term stem cell stimulation. According to the magnetic field intensity, SMFs can be categorized as weak (<1 mT), moderate (1 mT–1 T), strong (1–5 T), and ultra-strong (>5 T).144
3.1.1 Static magnetic fields. Utilizing SMFs as the stimulation signals to stem cells, the regulation effect may vary with the magnetic field density, exposure time, and other parameters. When dental pulp cells were continuously exposed to SMFs at 290 mT in combination with osteogenic induction, the SMF signals could significantly accelerate osteogenic differentiation and mineralization.145 A comparable magnetic field density of 200 mT for 24 h day−1 was applied to stimulate rat bone marrow MSCs, which enhanced effect of osteogenic differentiation and mineralization.146 The effect of magnetic field density at 400 mT on cells was also investigated, and the results also showed a promoting effect on differentiation towards chondrogenesis147 or osteogenesis.148,149 Furthermore, another study explored the effects of moderate intensity SMFs (3, 15, and 50 mT) as biophysical stimulators on the proliferation and osteoblast differentiation of human BM-MSCs.150 Alizarin red staining of the cultured cells at different magnetic field densities is shown in Fig. 13a, and SMFs could promote the osteoblastic differentiation of MSCs, especially the magnetic field density at 15 mT.

The mechanism for the effects of SMFs on various stem cells was similar because the cell membrane possessed diamagnetic properties, and SMF stimulation served to modify the membrane flux. Moreover, the ECM proteins also presented diamagnetic properties, which enabled the SMFs to affect their structures, orientations, and functions.153 These effects on the cells may contribute to the reported mechanism by which SMFs influence stem cell fate by altering Ca2+ concentration or increasing membrane rigidity.149

3.1.2 Electromagnetic fields. Electromagnetic fields (EMFs) also have promotive effects on stem cell differentiation. The EMF generator devices mainly consist of three parts: a program control computer, power amplifier, and solenoid. A typical solenoid is shown in Fig. 13b. There are two parameters that predominately define the electromagnetic field properties: frequency and magnetic flux density.151 Usually, low-frequency and low-energy electromagnetic fields are generated from the stimulation source. In addition, the magnetic flux density applied in most previous studies for stem cells fate regulation varied from 0.1 to 5 mT and the frequencies varied from 7.5 to 75 Hz. For example, homogeneous sinusoidal extremely low-frequency magnetic fields (5 mT, 15 Hz) produced by a solenoid were applied to hMSCs in a cartilage differentiation induction medium for three weeks, and the results showed that EMFs can promote chondrogenic differentiation.154 Another study demonstrated that EMFs (0.5, 50 Hz) accelerate cell proliferation and enhance cell differentiation of mouse bone marrow stromal cells.155 In addition, the effect of low-frequency electromagnetic fields (1 mT, 50 Hz) on the differentiation of human BM-MSCs toward neurons was assessed. The neuron marker (TuJ1) staining results indicated that SMFs efficiently enhanced neuronal differentiation (Fig. 13c).152 Pulsed electromagnetic fields, one of the most commonly used EMFs, produce magnetic fields and electric currents, which also enhance cell differentiation.156–158

However, until now, the precise mechanisms of EMFs influencing stem cell fate are not yet fully understood. It has been suggested that EMFs enhance osteogenic differentiation of bone marrow stromal stem cells through selective activation on Ca2+-related mechanisms.159 Additionally, EMF exposure may induce neural differentiation by increasing intracellular reactive oxygen species concentrations,160 and EMFs could also promote neurogenesis via upregulation of Cav1-channel activity.161 More efforts on mechanism investigation should be paid.

3.2 Localized magnetic signal mediated by magnetic nanostructure

A number of previous studies have reported that external magnetic field signal stimulation allows stem cells to sense the field change and regulate their behaviors, such as cell growth, elongation, migration, and differentiation. However, for the terminal purpose of tissue repair and regeneration engineering, it may not be enough to employ the magnet field as a single treatment. Thus, it is essential to design a proper scaffold to provide support, and contribute to connecting, and communicating functions. In addition, a properly designed substrate for stem cell culture also accounts for the regulation of stem cell behavior. Therefore, a magnetic field combined with a cell scaffold could be developed as a novel strategy with more controllable and functional properties for the fate regulation of stem cells.
3.2.1 Superparamagnetic nanoparticles-based scaffolds. Superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (Fe3O4 or γ-Fe2O3) possess a magnetic field-responsive property, which behave like common materials without an external magnetic field and will be rapidly magnetized to saturation and respond to the magnetic field when exposed to an external magnetic field. Therefore, the combined application of an external magnetic field and scaffolds containing magnetic nanoparticles may have a special effect on stem cells.162 Yun et al. reported the synergism between magnetic scaffolds and SMFs.163 As shown in Fig. 14a, the magnetic scaffold was constructed with Fe3O4 nanoparticles and polycaprolactone (PCL) in different proportions. A neodymium (Nd2Fe14B) disc magnet was placed under the magnetic scaffold for magnetic field stimulation. Primary mouse calvarium osteoblasts were cultured on the surface of the magnetic nanocomposite scaffolds for 10 days. The gene expression and ALP activity experimental results indicated that the magnetic nanocomposite scaffolds synergized with SMFs enhanced osteoblast differentiation of primary mouse calvarium osteoblasts. Moreover, effects of the magnetic scaffold and SMF on the osteogenic differentiation of MC3T3-E1 cells has also been reported.164 The magnetic nanofiber scaffold was constructed using superparamagnetic Fe3O4 nanoparticles and poly(L-lactide) with a mixing and electrospinning method, and a magnetic field of 100 mT was served by a neodymium magnet. Therefore, both the external SMF signals and the incorporation of Fe3O4 nanoparticles can enhance the proliferation and osteogenic differentiation of MC3T3-E1 cells, and the synergism of SMF stimulation and magnetic scaffold could further promote these changes. Another study developed intrinsically superparamagnetic Fe-hydroxyapatite nanoparticles, which replaced the Fe3O4 nanoparticles with Fe2+ and Fe3+ substitution in the material structure to investigate the influence of magnetic materials and magnetic field on differentiation of SaOS-2 human osteoblast-like cells.165 Furthermore, a strategy combining the magnetic scaffold and SMF was also applied and tested in vivo, and the results supported osteogenesis for bone repair in vivo.166 Therefore, the combinatory external SMF stimulation and internal magnetism scaffold can be a promising strategy for stem cell fate regulation and regenerative engineering.

Scaffolds for adjusting the ECM have also been designed to investigate the combined effect of magnetic field signals on stem cells. Lee et al. investigated the influence of extremely low-frequency electromagnetic fields (50 Hz, 1 mT) on the neuronal differentiation of hMSCs on graphene-based substrates.168 Graphene was employed as the cell scaffold as it could support neuronal differentiation of stem cells. By the application of low-frequency electromagnetic fields, the neuronal differentiation efficacy of stem cells grown on a graphene-coated substrate could be synergistically enhanced.

3.2.2 Magnetic field responsive nanomaterials. Magnetic field-responsive nanomaterials have also been explored to assess the influence of the magnetic field on the cells. Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) become transiently magnetized once exposed to specific EMF frequencies, enabling more direct transfer of EMF energy to the target cells. As shown in Fig. 14b, Yoo et al. designed an AuNP-based substrate for fibroblasts to investigate the effect of EMF on induced dopamine neurons differenrtiation.167 They selected mouse tail-tip fibroblasts and transiently transfected them with plasmids that express the dopamine neurons lineage transcription factors in vitro. EMF stimulation activated histone acetyltransferase Brd2, resulting in the acetylation of histone H3K27 and activation of neuron-specific genes. Fibroblasts were reprogrammed to induce dopamine neurons without de-differentiated to induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) under EMF exposure and AuNPs facilitating. The direct reprogramming of somatic cells into other lineages is attractive, and EMF provides a particularly method to non-invasively regulate cell fate with high efficiency and low toxicity. This study may inspire researchers to explore the effect of nanostructures meditated-physical signals on somatic cells reprogramming.

Although magnetic fields have been proven to regulate stem cell fate with or without the mediation of nanomaterials, the physical and biological mechanisms of this effect are not clear. In fact, the generation of a localized alternative magnetic field at the interface between cells and substrate and how the localized alternative magnetic field acts on the surface receptor of the stem cells are still of the essence.

3.3 Electromagnetic signals mediated by light irradiated metal nanostructures based on the localized surface plasmon resonance effect

Localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) is due to the strong absorption of photon energy by nanoparticles or metals, when the incident photon frequency matches the collective oscillation frequency of the noble metal nanoparticles or the conducting electrons of metal. In the LSPR condition, the metal nanoparticles exhibit strong electromagnetic field enhancement and generate hot electrons. The localized electromagnetic enhancement generated by the LSPR effect could act as a magnetic signal to regulate stem cell fate. In addition to noble metal nanoparticles, some compounds, especially chalcogenides, possess a high plasmonic effect.169 Recently, Liu et al. found that micro-electromagnetic field variation based on the plasmonic effect of CuS driven by near-infrared can induce hADSCs to differentiate into neuron-like cells without any growth factor or neural differentiation media, which opened a new door for the regulation of stem cell fate with the plasmonic effect of non-metal compound nanostructures.170 In a photo-induced plasmon system, several physical signals, such as light, thermal, mechanical electrical, and magnetic signals, are all involved and closely related. However, the dominant factor and the minor factor that contribute to stem cell fate regulation are not identified, and the underlying mechanism are also not clear. There remains blank in this promising field and more efforts need to be paid in the future.

4 Thermal signals

An appropriate temperature is one of the most fundamental factors influencing cell behavior, including growth, migration, differentiation, and apoptosis. Generally, temperatures in the range of 35–37 °C are optimal for cell viability both in vivo and in vitro. Deviating from this region, cell metabolism and growth may be affected, even causing cell death. Moreover, when the external temperature is slightly increased higher than 37 °C, the cells also develop an adaptive heat acclimation process to increase heat tolerance. This mild heat has considerable effects on the activity of enzymes, proteins, and various reaction processes inside the cells, which endow the temperature regulation of cell fate. Through decades of research efforts, numerous studies have reported the effects of mild heat on the cells, especially on stem cells, and their underlying mechanisms. In addition, photothermal nanomaterials can mediate localized thermal signals for regulating cell fate under light stimulation, In the following part, the influence of the mild heat and photothermal nanomaterial-mediated thermal signals on the stem cells fate decision will be mainly reviewed.

4.1 Thermal signals in the regulation of stem cell fate

At the cellular level, heat exposure may increase the temperature of both the inside and outside microenvironment of the cells, which may induce the facilitation of biological reactions due to the temperature coefficient effect and thereby regulate stem cell behaviors, including proliferation and differentiation. To maintain cell homeostasis, some specific biological process may occur inside the cells after heat shock. Heat shock proteins (HSPs) are molecules that maintain cellular homeostasis during environmental changes.171 Usually, heat shock protein synthesis is correlated with this tolerance and is constitutively at basal unstressed levels and sensitively responds to various cell stresses such as hyperthermia and anoxia.172 From this aspect of stem cells, HSPs may serve as regulators of stem cell proliferation and differentiation when encountering external heat exposure. A recent study carried out an experiment in which NSCs isolated from forebrain cortices were cultured at either 37.0 °C (control) or 38.5 °C (mild heat exposure) for four days, showing that heat exposure upregulated both HSP27 mRNA expression and p-Akt levels.173 In addition, mild heat exposure promoted the proliferation of NSCs, possibly through the Akt pathway, and promoted neuronal differentiation. Some studies have also investigated the heat shock effect on MSCs, and mild heat shock (1 h at 41 °C, 42.5 °C, or 44 °C) can enhance osteoblastic differentiation of hTERT-immortalized MSCs.174 The influence of HSPs on murine embryonal carcinoma stem cells has also been investigated, and researchers have found that HSPs also account for the regulation of cell differentiation and proliferation.172,175

5 Light signals

Among the various external stimulation methods, the effect of noninvasive light should not be underestimated.182,183 Light with different wavelengths, including ultraviolet (UV), visible light, and infrared light, differ in regulation of stem cell differentiation. Stem cells can sense light irradiation from typical receptors. For example, Wang et al.184 reported that NSCs naturally express blue/red light-sensitive opsins, the melanopsin/transient receptor potential channel 6, which are photoreceptors present within mammalian retinas.185

In addition to light stimulation, rational materials with typical light-responsive properties have also been designed to achieve dynamic physical stimulation of cells with light assistance, such as photo-crosslinking and photothermal response, which have been discussed in the above Sections 2.3.2 and 4.2. With direct light irradiation at different wavelengths, the light can be changed into a special wavelength or wavelength region different from the initial light mediated by light-converting nanomaterials, such as UCNPs and second harmonic generation (SHG) nanostructures.

5.1 The effect of light irradiation on stem cell differentiation

Light irradiation is mainly applied in the form of visible light (380–780 nm in wavelength) to direct cell differentiation. Light signals can be simply generated by various tools and devices, such as diode lasers,186,187 He–Ne lasers,188 and light-emitting diodes (LED).189–193

Low-level laser irradiation (LLLI) was applied to rBMSCs using a 632 nm diode laser to promote myogenic differentiation with 5-azacytidine (5-aza) induction at an energy density of 5.0 J cm−2.186 Another study demonstrated that LLLI from 632 nm LED promoted osteogenesis of rBMSCs with optimized parameters at 15 mW cm−2 and 4 J cm−2, seen as the increased expression of ALP and OCN.189 Furthermore, Shao et al. developed a CRISPR-dCas9 effector device that was activated by far-red light (FRL, 730 nm) and used this optogenetic device to mediate targeted epigenetic modulation, which can efficiently promote neural differentiation of iPSCs, which holds great promise for directing stem cell differentiation and optogenetics-based therapy.193

The above studies focused on the effect of red light, while various wavelengths may contribute differently and are controversial. Higuchi et al. investigated the osteogenic differentiation of amniotic fluid-derived stem cells (AFSCs) when exposed to LEDs (0–2 mW cm−2) in various wavelengths, including 470 nm (blue), 525 nm (green), 600 nm (yellow) and 630 nm (red).192 They found the osteogenic differentiation of AFSCs was significantly promoted by green or blue light irradiation from LEDs, rather than red or yellow light at the same power density. This conclusion was different from other reports,186,189 which may be attributed to the different stem cell types and various irradiation parameters. Moreover, blue light (453 nm) irradiation has been confirmed to initiate the differentiation of human skin cells, providing a therapeutic strategy for skin diseases.190

5.2 Light-converting nanomaterials-meditated optical signals

5.2.1 Up-conversion nanoparticles. Lanthanide-doped UCNPs exhibit the intrinsic properties of converting near-infrared (NIR) light into high-energy light regions with low wavelengths, including UV, visible, and NIR light. Recent research results illustrated that the upconversion nanostructures can regulate stem cell fate both outside and inside stem cells.

In a recent study, silica shell-coated NaYF4:Yb/Er UCNPs (UCNP@SiO2) core–shell nanoparticles were synthesized using a microemulsion method to regulate MSCs fate as a light conversion platform (Fig. 16a).194 The authors designed and constructed a special scaffold by assembling a specially modified UCNP@SiO2 core–shell nanoparticle into a matrix. The surface of UCNP@SiO2 core–shell nanoparticles was modified by two kinds of molecules: a UV-driven photocleavage molecule, which can be broken under strong NIR light irradiation, and the other is a stable polymer molecule, which can capture the stem cells on the scaffold. Under NIR irradiation, UCNPs can convert NIR irradiation into UV, causing cleavage of photocleavage molecules and blocking cell–matrix interactions (Fig. 16b). Upon low-power NIR irradiation (0.5 W cm−2), the differentiation of MSCs was toward adipogenesis due to the detachment of some poly(ethylene glycol) (P1), while toward osteogenic differentiation upon high-power NIR irradiation (6 W cm−2) with more P1 detaching (Fig. 16c). This study proposed a strategy for realizing multidirectional differentiation of MSCs using an upconversion substrate-mediated optical signal and different NIR lasers.

5.2.2 The second harmonic multiplier nanostructures. In addition to UCNPs, lithium niobate (LiNbO3, LN) nanocrystals with SHG nanocrystals have been reported to label the rat MSCs (rMSCs) for imaging cellular internalization, and their influence on stem cell differentiation was also evaluated.195 The results showed that LN nanocrystals were taken up by the rMSCs and their distribution was highly consistent with actin filaments under 910 nm NIR excitation (Fig. 16f). Moreover, rMSCs incubated with 50 μg mL−1 of LN nanocrystals retained their osteogenic and adipogenic abilities. This work provides insights into multifunctional SHG nanocrystals with deep tissue tracking, localized drug release, and stem cell therapy.

Although light signals can regulate stem cell fate and realize directional differentiation, techniques for the use of light should be further investigated in the future. The most important factors in direct use of UV and visible light are the penetration depth and to avoid damaging cells and tissues surrounding the target stem cells. To convert NIR light into visible or UV light inside the cells or round the cells in vivo, although the problem of light penetration depth can be solved, and the required UV/visible light can be obtained, the degradability of the nanoparticles becomes the strongest enemy for clinical applications. Solving the above problems is urgent for the light signal regulation of stem cells and their applications in tissue engineering.

6 Electrical signals

Since cell electrophysiology has been intensively investigated in recent years, electrical signals are now considered as a stimulation approach and potential regulator of stem cell fate, controlling cardiogenic, osteogenic, chondrogenic, and neuronal differentiation. In fact, the electrical field can modulate almost most processes in various stem cell types, such as adhesion, migration, proliferation, and differentiation.198 Although many receptors and pathways have been shown to affect cell fate, how an electrical signal regulates the fate of stem cells is not very clear.31 Many progress has been made in the past decades in cell differentiation and tissue engineering because of the development of convenient electrical stimulation methods and devices.162,199–201 According to the way cells are subjected to exogenous electrical stimulation, these methods can be classified into two categories in this review: direct electrical stimulation and indirect electrical stimulation. In this review, indirect electrical stimulation is defined as materials-mediated electrical stimulation with electricity or charge, including conductive material-mediated electrical signals, fabricated devices based on nanomaterials for applying electrical signals, and the surface charge of typical materials.

In addition, these kinds of stimulation mentioned above are generally invasive for cells and the human body, and electrical stimulation can also be realized in a material-mediated noninvasive manner, relying on the innate properties of typical materials such as photoelectricity and piezoelectricity, which are reviewed in detail in this section.

6.1 Direct electrical stimulation

Direct electrical stimulation can be applied in the form of a direct current (DC) electrical field, alternating current (AC) electrical field, and electric impulse stimulation, which stimulate cells in a contact manner. Electrical signals are often generated or realized by various tools and devices, such as incubation chambers between electrodes,202–205 electric stimulator,206 indium tin oxide (ITO) glass electrodes,207 gap cuvettes,208 microfabricated capacitance sensor arrays,209 vertical nanowire electrode array,210 and electromyography electrodes.211

A steady electrical field was applied to dedicate stem cell differentiation. Elena et al. found that a constant electrical field (1000 V m−1) could be utilized to initiate the differentiation of human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) toward cardiac lineages.202 In this study, researchers used an assembled bioreactor that included incubation chambers and electrodes for steady stimulation of hESCs (Fig. 17a). It has been reported that rMSCs can differentiate into osteocytes induced by a lower amplitude of the electrical field (100 V m−1).205

6.2 Indirect electrical stimulation

6.2.1 Conductive nanomaterial-meditated electrical signals. Cells can sense and respond to electrical stimulation through a conductive nanostructured culture substrate or scaffold connected to electrical stimulation devices during in vitro cultivation rather than direct contact with large electrical stimulation devices, because the localized signal is more powerful than the equipotential electrode.213 In this review, these conductive materials are classified into inorganic nanomaterials,214,215 conductive hybrid polymers,32,216–220 carbon-based nanomaterials,221–224etc., which have been subjected to DC and/or AC electrical stimulation as well as pulses and have been used to stimulate cells. The determination of proper electrical stimulation parameters is important and comparable to direct electrical stimulation for directing stem cell fate, as mentioned above.
6.2.1.1 Inorganic conductive nanomaterials. Inorganic nanomaterials usually undergo AC electrical stimulation to promote osteogenic differentiation. For example, Woo et al. prepared fibronectin-coated Au NPs (∼20 nm) by submersing polyethyleneimine (PEI) precoated cover glass in a Au NP dispersion agent for 1 d and delivered electrical stimulation to hESCs on an Au NP-based conductive substrate through a three-electrode system (Fig. 18a and b).214 This specially designed matrix enhanced osteogenic differentiation of hESCs through 30 min AC stimulation and may help tackle the problems associated with protein or peptide delivery systems for stem cell differentiation. The processing technology of Si wafers is developing rapidly in the field of nanomaterials, obtaining versatile micro/nano-topographical patterns. Huang et al. prepared patterned Si surfaces by etching techniques and utilized these Si scaffolds to promote the osteogenic differentiation of bone marrow-derived MSCs (rBMSCs) with pulsed AC electrical stimulation (150 V m−1, 1 Hz, 2 ms pulse width).215

6.2.1.2 Conductive polymers. Polyaniline (PANI) and polypyrrole (PPy) are typical conductive polymers that are widely used to regulate stem cell fate, such as osteogenesis217,219,220 and neurogenesis218 under indirect electrical stimulation. Poly-L-lactide (PLLA)/PANI nanofibrous scaffolds at a ratio of 85:15 were obtained by electrospinning with a diameter of 195 ± 30 nm (Fig. 18c), which resulted in neurite growth of NSCs with a steady electric field of 100 V m−1 for 60 min.216 Another study demonstrated that moderately conductive PANI substrates were coated with collagen type I (ColI) and/or sulfated hyaluronan (sHya) to mimic the ECM of bone tissue.217 With pulsed electrical field stimulation (7 ms rectangular pulses, 3.6 mV cm−1, 10 Hz), hMSCs on substrates showed stronger osteogenic differentiation tendency. Several researchers have reported that PPy films synthesized from pyrrole and ammonium persulfate favored the osteogenic differentiation of different MSCs under corresponding constant voltage parameters.219,220 Therefore, these two conductive polymers can be effective for direct commitment to both neural and osteogenic lineages.
6.2.1.3 Carbon-based nanomaterials. Carbon-based nanomaterials, which are thought to be degraded in vivo, are an important category of nanostructured inorganic non-metal materials. Recently, the researchers have proved that conductive carbon nanotubes (CNTs),221–223,226 carbon nanofibers,227 graphene,225,228,229 and graphene oxide230 can be used as a mediated matrix to realize electrical stimulation to regulate the fate of stem cells. CNT ropes were prepared by Huang et al. used a chemical vapor deposition method, following a specially designed spin and twisting process (Fig. 18d).223 By applying pulsed electrical stimulation at 5, 0.5, and 25 ms intervals on the CNT rope, the differentiation of mNSCs cultured on the CNT rope was promoted. Another important type of carbon nanostructure used as a mediator for regulating stem cell fate with electrical stimulation is graphene. Li et al. prepared porous three-dimensional graphene foams (3D-GFs) as scaffolds by depositing a graphene layer on the surface of a nickel foam framework, following a chemical etching process.225 They found that the 3D-GF scaffolds enhanced mNSC differentiation toward astrocytes and promoted the differentiation especially to neurons under external pulses electrical stimulation (1–100 ms monophasic cathodic pulses, 10 s intervals, 20–30 mA) compared with that in 2D graphene samples (Fig. 18e).

Although all-carbon nanomaterials possess high conductivity, and carbon nanotubes and graphene have been proven to be degraded slowly in vivo, all-carbon scaffolds are difficult to apply in clinical treatments because of the difficulty in controlling the degradation speed in vivo. Therefore, some researchers designed biodegradable conductive scaffolds by assembling a layer of conductive nanostructures on a biopolymer porous scaffold to overcome the above problems. Liu et al. synthesized a porcine acellular dermal matrix–graphene (PADM–Graphene) hybrid conductive scaffold by chemically assembling reduced graphene oxide (rGO) on the channel surface of a PADM scaffold. The scaffold has the capability to enhance neuronal differentiation of rMSCs and is suitable for electrical stimulation as a biodegradable conductive scaffold.231

The stimulation mode for electrical signals is important for the enhancement effect as a methodology for the regulation of stem cell fate. Normally, the cells are seeded and cultured on the surface of the conductive scaffold, and electric potential is applied to the scaffold. DC or AC and pulse electric field with a specially designed frequency and voltage waveform can be applied on the two tops of the scaffold or using two-electrode and three-electrode modes as well as microelectrode arrays. The different electricity application modes have different effects on stem cell differentiation, which is still unclear. 6.2.2 Electrical signals applied with microfabricated devices. Apart from conductive materials with external potential applied, electrical stimulation can also be applied through nanostructured functional materials with novel physical properties, or through some specially designed electronic devices based on nanomaterials, such as triboelectric nanogenerators (TENGs),232–234 microelectrode arrays (MEAs),235,236 and microfluidic devices.237,238 These devices can apply electrical stimulation locally to specific parts of the stem cells.

Previously, we fabricated a TENG to provide to pulsed electrical signals and a highly conductive poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)–rGO (rGO–PEDOT) hybrid microfiber as a scaffold (Fig. 19a and b).232 Driven by human walking steps, TENG can supply electrical simulation to induce neural-like differentiation of rMSCs on 15% rGO–PEDOT without any bio/chemical factors. A recent study has also designed a self-powered implantable electrical stimulator, which was composed of a TENG and a flexible interdigitated electrode employing PET as the substrate and packaged by PDMS, which significantly promoted the attachment, proliferation, and differentiation of MC3T3-E1, as well as the level of intracellular Ca2+ level after AC and pulsed DC electrical stimulation.233

Microelectrode array (MEA) devices have been used to stimulate stem cell differentiation. Zheng et al. cultured rMSCs on the surface of an MEA chip and applied AC electrical stimulation and found that electrical stimulation can promote the osteogenic differentiation (Fig. 19c).236 Another example is that the hNSCs encapsulated in conductive polysaccharide-based biogel and cultured on the microprinted MEA constructed with conductive polymer array can realize enhanced neural differentiation under biphasic waveform electrical stimulation.235

As a versatile tool for biology research, microfluidic chips have attracted broad interest from researchers in many fields, especially tissue engineering. Well-designed microfluid chips can be manufactured using a microfabrication process that is normally used to fabricate microelectronic mechanical systems (MEMS), which can not only realize in situ electrical stimulation and signal measurement of electrophysiology, but can also realize single-cell research and in situ measurement of secretory proteins when the stem cells are under differentiation. Ni et al. proposed a device that can quickly apply various electrical stimulation to suspend cells in a microfluidic chip.237 Pulsed electrical stimulation was applied to clusters consisting of human cardiac myocytes (hCMs), human fibroblast cells (hDFs), and human umbilical vein endothelial cells (hUVECs), and their electrical responses were recorded, which provided parameter protocols for directing cardiac cell differentiation. Unfortunately, to the best of our knowledge, there is no strategy to induce or enhance the differentiation of stem cells by microfluidic chip-mediated electrical stimulation, providing new pathways for further investigations in stem cell therapy.

In fact, these nanostructured electrodes or devices can act as powerful tools for the timely investigation of electrical signals locally and even quantically. A deeper understanding of the effect of electrical signals on stem cell differentiation can be achieved through these devices, but these devices are difficult to use in clinical applications.

6.2.3 Electrical signals derived from the surface charges of functional materials.
6.2.3.1 Ferroelectric materials. As mentioned above, the release of the surface charge can be accomplished by applying an external potential to the electrode. In fact, surface charges exist on the surface of some synthesized materials because of the intrinsic properties of the materials or specially designed surface chemical properties. The surface charge of these materials can also supply electrical stimulation to stem cells, thereby regulating their lineage fate as a noninvasive stimulation tool. Ferroelectric crystals are one such type of material. As illustrated in Fig. 19d, ferroelectric materials such as LiNbO3 (LN) single crystals possess a spontaneous polarized potential inside the crystal along the c-axis.239 To balance the internal potential along the Z-axis of the LN crystal, opposite charges are absorbed on the two opposite surfaces and form layers of charge on the surface. The surface of the positive pole Z+ side of the LN wafer absorbs negative charges in air, while the negatively charged Z− surface absorbs positive charges (Fig. 19d). We investigated the differentiation of rMSCs cultured on the Z+, Z−, and neutral Y0 surfaces of LN surfaces, and found that positive charge can enhance the osteogenic differentiation of rMSCs compared with negative charge and neutral charge. In addition, we found that endocytosis of ferroelectric LiNbO3 nanoparticles of rMSCs induced enhanced differentiation of rMSCs to osteoblasts, which is also attributed to the surface charge of LiNbO3 nanoparticles.195 In addition to inorganic ferroelectric crystals, the surface charges on poled ferroelectric polymer films, such as polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), have been shown to promote osteogenic differentiation of BMSCs.240
6.2.3.2 Hydrogels with charges. Surface charges are not only derived from the external or internal electrical potential but can also be found on the surface of materials modified with specific molecules with charges. With this point, as a very important kind of biomaterial, hydrogels can be considered as potential biomaterials for the regulation of stem cell fate with their surface charge, because their surface can be modified easily. In recent years, researchers have attempted to fabricate hydrogels with different physical, chemical, and biological cues for regulating stem/progenitor cell differentiation, of which the surface charge of hydrogels plays a key role. In Mahrokh et al.'s study, a biocompatible and biodegradable macromer oligo-(polyethylene glycol) fumarate (OPF) was chemically modified by the incorporation of a cationic monomer [2-(methacryloyloxy)ethyl]-trimethylammonium chloride to fabricate a positively charged hydrogel.241 Rat neuronal cells from dorsal root ganglions (DRGs) anchored to the hydrogels and neurite extension were analyzed, and the neurite lengths of cells on charged hydrogels were significantly longer than those on unmodified hydrogels. Another study developed an OPF-based positively charged hydrogel and electrical conductivity by covalently embedding graphene oxide acrylate and carbon nanotube poly(ethylene glycol)acrylate.242 It showed great biocompatibility and enhanced proliferation and spreading of rat pheochromocytoma (PC12) cells, exhibiting great potential for application in neural tissue engineering. In addition to neural development, synthetic hydrogels can serve as a scaffold with the potential to induce chondrogenic differentiation and relevant tissue because of their soft and flexible properties that mimic natural cartilage tissue. Negatively charged poly(2-acrylamido-2-methyl-1-propanesulfonic acid) (PAMPS) gel, neutral charged poly(dimethylacrylamide) (PDMAAm) gel, and their complexes with different ratios (P(AMPS-co-DMAAm)) were used to induce differentiation of ATDC5 cells.243 The results suggested that PAMPS gel or highly negatively charged P(AMPS-co-DMAAm) gel can induce chondrogenic differentiation of ATDC5 in insulin-free maintenance medium, and neutrally charged PDMAAm gel favored cell aggregation and chondrogenesis under insulin supplementation.

Although the charges on the surface of biomaterials have a regulatory effect on stem cell fate, it is believed that the stiffness of the surface-charged materials also plays an important role in the direction of stem cell differentiation. Normally, hard substrates such as LN single crystal surface charges are favorable for hard tissue repair (i.e., osteogenesis), while soft substrates such as hydrogels-mediated surface charge support soft tissue regeneration (i.e., neurogenesis and chondrogenesis).

The effect of static charges on the surface of surface-charged materials is limited. Therefore, some ferroelectric materials were used to generate periodic stimulation by applying an excitation electric field to generate mechanical deformation, thus affecting cellular behavior as a substrate (reviewed in the Section 2.3.3).125

As mentioned above, static charge on the surface of materials with special properties or artificially constructed microelectrode arrays can regulate stem cell fate; however, practical clinical application would be complicated. The most well-known materials with intrinsic charges are inorganic ferroelectric materials. Although they can be used as perfect models to demonstrate the surface charge regulating stem cell fate, their high stability prohibits their clinical applications due to the difficulty of biodegradation when they are implanted in vivo.

6.3 External field responsive materials mediate electrical signals

6.3.1 Ultrasound-driven piezoelectric nanomaterial-meditated electrical signals. As mentioned above, although active electrical stimulation can produce a periodic electrical signal, connecting wires between the cell culture conductive materials and electrical signal generator is a great obstacle for its practical medical applications. Conversely, ferroelectricity-derived surface static charges can provide a passive electric field, but the surface charges can be easily saturated during the cell culture process. Fortunately, the electromechanical coupling property of piezoelectric materials can overcome these problems. Piezoelectric materials can generate alternative electric fields or alternatively absorb and release charges on the surface of the material under mechanical deformation,244 which have been widely used in piezo generators, sensors, piezo actuators, and transducers.245
6.3.1.1 Piezoelectric nanoparticles. Piezoelectric micro/nanosized particles or films can be driven by sound, ultrasound, or other vibrations, which have been used as pressure sensors, loudspeakers, and nanogenerators.246,247 As a very powerful energy transporting tool, ultrasound can be used to drive piezoelectric micro/nanostructures, which have been used in some biofields.248 As a mediator, piezoelectric nanostructures can convert vibration energy to electrical signals, which provide a perfect opportunity to stimulate stem cells cultured on the surface of the nanostructures and regulate stem cell fate.

Piezoelectric nanoparticles, along with ultrasonic irradiation, fulfill the demand for in vivo electrical stimulation for tissue engineering, and are one of the most important strategies for regulating stem cell fate in biomedical applications. Therefore, some attempts related to the regulation of cell fate by ultrasound-driven nanostructured mediated electrical signals have been proposed.

Hexagonal boron nitride (BN) possesses a graphene-like structure, where B and N atoms alternately substitute the sites of C atoms in graphene. A single-layer BN crystal possesses piezoelectric properties, because the centers of positive and negative charge misalign and cause polarization of the BN single-layer crystal when the BN plane suffers stress and causes deformation.249 However, the double-layered BN could not exhibit piezoelectric properties because the up site of the B atom on one layer is an N atom, which causes the neutralization of the polarization of the two layers. This property is similar to the piezoelectric properties of MoS2 crystals.250 Boron nitride nanotubes (BNNTs) exhibit similar atom arrangements as CNTs, and BNNTs, especially single-layered BNNTs, possess high piezoelectric properties (Fig. 20a).251 Ciofani et al. cultured PC12 cells with BNNTs – 200–600 nm in length and 50 nm in diameter to investigate cell viability and neural differentiation under ultrasound stimulation (40 kHz, 20 W, 5 s, 4 times day−1 for 9 days) (Fig. 20b).252 They found that BNNTs were endocytosed in the cell and could enhance differentiation into neuron-like cells. In this process, the nerve growth factor (NGF) specific receptor TrkA was found to play a major role in BNNT-mediated ultrasound-driven electrical stimulation differentiation. Another study investigated the osteogenic differentiation of primary human osteoblasts with endogenous stimulation mediated by poly-L-lysine (PLL)-coated BNNTs and ultrasound stimulation.253 The highest OPN and OCN protein expression, Ca2+ production, and TGF-β1 synthesis were observed in the 10 μg mL−1 PLL-BNNTs + ultrasound treatment (40 kHz, 5 s, 3 times day−1 for 7 days), indicating that the osteoblast maturation process was driven by BNNTs-mediated electrical stimulation.

Tetragonal barium titanate ceramics have good ferroelectric properties and superior piezoelectricity, and are bioinert, making them nontoxic to cells and tissues. Therefore, barium titanate nanoparticles (BTNPs) have been used as models to investigate the piezoelectric effect on the regulation of cell fate and other bioeffects. For example, BTNPs have been exploited as nanotransducers to provide indirect electrical stimulation to human neuroblastoma-derived SH-SY5Y cells.254 The BTNPs with diameter of 479.0 ± 145.3 nm was dispersed in culture media at a concentration of 50 μg mL−1, and the SH-SY5Y cells were cultured with the under ultrasound stimulation (1 MHz, 5 mm distance from the cells, 5 s, 0.1–0.8 W cm−2). A comparative investigation of piezoelectric and non-piezoelectric BTNPs on the differentiation of SH-SY5Y cells was performed, and it was found that piezoelectric BTNPs can enhance the differentiation of SH-SY5Y cells, and confirmed that the voltage-sensitive channels related to Ca2+ and Na+ transients induced by ultrasound-driven piezoelectric nanoparticle-mediated electrical signals, as shown in Fig. 20c and d, respectively. Marino et al. fabricated a two-photon polymerization of a nanocomposite, Ormocomp/BTNPs composites.256 Ormocomp is a commercial photosensitive resist, and enhanced osteogenic differentiation of human osteosarcoma-derived SaOS-2 cells under ultrasound stimulation (0.8 W cm−2, 5 s every 4 h, 3 times day−1). These nanostructured materials that mediate electrical stimulation can remotely initiate cell differentiation and tissue regeneration.

In addition to inorganic piezoelectric nanostructures, organic piezoelectric materials have also been used to tune the fate of cells. The odd number (e.g., 7, 9, and 11) nylons have intrinsic piezoelectricity, especially nylon-11.257 In our recent study, piezoelectric nylon-11 nanoparticles (nylon-11 NPs) were synthesized by a simple anti-solvent method, exhibiting a uniform morphology (Fig. 20e).255 The amplitude image detected by piezoelectric force microscopy was shown in Fig. 20f, confirming the piezoelectric feature of nylon-11 NPs. It was found that hDPSCs incubated with optimized 200 μg mL−1 biocompatible nylon-11 NPs exhibited the highest osteogenesis efficiency under ultrasound stimulation. We also found that nylon-11 NP-mediated electrical stimulation accelerated osteogenesis of hDPSCs by stimulating Ca2+ influx with ultrasound assistance.


6.3.1.2 Piezoelectric film or bulk materials. Apart from nanoparticles, piezoelectric films or bulk materials can serve as substrates or scaffolds to mediate electrical signals and regulate cell differentiation, they are very important for the clinical design of electrically stimulated tissue repair. PVDF has several crystal forms, of which non-piezoelectric α-phase PVDF has an alternating trans-gauche (TGTG) conformation (Fig. 21a), and piezoelectric β-phase PVDF has an all-trans conformation (TTTT) and exhibits a net dipole moment (Fig. 21b).258,259 The piezoelectric properties of β-PVDF strongly vary with the processing conditions, and their d33 coefficient is −25 pC N−1 by theoretical calculations.260 Hoop et al. cultured PC12 cells on β-PVDF film substrates and applied ultrasound (132 kHz, 80 W, 10 min, 5 times day−1) to stimulate cells with piezoelectric material-mediated electrical signals on surfaces when undergoing vibration (Fig. 21c).261 The effect of piezoelectric signals on neural differentiation of PC12 cells is comparable to that induced by the typical neural differentiation growth factor, NGF. They found that dynamic stimulation mediated by piezoelectric film activated calcium channels, which was similar to the effect of piezoelectric nanoparticles, and induced neurite generation via a cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)-dependent pathway.

6.3.1.3 Magnetic/piezoelectric multiple stimulation. Multiple stimulations hold great promise for the regulation of cell fate. The above examples of ultrasound-driven piezoelectric material-mediated electrical signals for the regulation of stem cell/cell line differentiation are based on nonbiodegradable piezoelectric materials, which is limited for clinical applications. In recent years, some materials constructed using bio-derived natural molecules have been proven to possess piezoelectric properties, which have great potential for clinical applications owing to their biodegradable performance in vivo. PLLA is a typical bio-derived piezoelectric material. Unlike the polarization direction of PVDF, PLLA has a d14 piezoelectric coefficient of −9.82 pC N−1264 and its biodegradable properties provide great potential application in tissue engineering. Binan et al. proved that electrospun poly L-lactic acid/gelatin scaffolds can enhance the differentiation of neuronal stem cells into motor neurons.265 In some occasions, researchers endow piezoelectric materials with other functions to form multiple multifunctional nanostructures. Under well-designed stimulation, PC12 cells can move to a targeted area and differentiate induced by magnetic piezoelectric helical microswimmers, which are composed of PLLA/CoFe2O4 or P(VDF-TrFE)/CoFe2O4 hybrid helical nanostructures, called piezoelectric microswimmers, were used to realize the magnate-induced target cell differentiation of PC12 (Fig. 22a).266 After 7 days, the PC12 cells were already differentiated by the co-stimulation of microswimmers-mediated electric signals under ultrasound irradiation, while those stimulated only with microswimmers or ultrasound remained undifferentiated (Fig. 22b). Effective bone generation was achieved using a built-in P(VDF-TrFE)/CoFe2O4 membrane by remote tuning of the ME microenvironment.267

6.3.2 Light-driven photoelectric nanomaterials-mediated electrical signals. Photoelectric materials are a very large family of functional materials that can convert light energy into electric energy and further convert it to chemical energy. Photoelectric materials have been widely applied in photovoltaic cells (solar cells),270 photocatalysis for organic pollution,271 hydrogen generation,272 and photocatalytic synthesis.273 Therefore, photoelectric conversion, especially near-infrared light-driven photoelectric-material-mediated electrical signals, should be one of the most suitable practical approaches for generating wireless electrical stimulation to regulate cell fate in vivo. However, there is very little research on this topic.

In contrast, only 6% and 5% cells grown on bare silicon substrates and glass coverslips showed calcium peaks under the same light parameters, respectively (Fig. 23g and h).

7 Acoustic signals

Acoustic waves possess a massive amount of energy and have promoted great progress in biomedical and clinical research, especially ultrasound imaging and diagnostic techniques. Acoustic wave stimulation has also been widely utilized in directing cell lineage fate and cell therapy recently, and is devoted to triggering the piezoelectric response of specific nanomaterials owing to their deep tissue penetration, easy controllability, and noninvasive properties (reviewed in the Section 6.3.1).

In this section, we introduce the effects of various acoustic waves and material-mediated physical cues on stem cell differentiation from the following three aspects: the effect of acoustic wave stimulation, and the combination of acoustic waves and nanomaterials.

7.1 Acoustic and ultrasonic signals

Several investigations have established the stimulatory effect of different acoustics on stem cell differentiation, usually in the form of extracorporeal shock waves (ESWs) and ultrasound generated by ultrasonicators or other apparatus with different frequencies, powers, power densities, stimulation time, duty cycles, and/or pulsed parameters.

Recruiting, proliferation, and differentiation of the stem cells were enhanced under ESWs stimulation (0.16 mJ mm−2, 1 Hz, 500 impulses).286 Histological assessment suggested that rMSCs progressively differentiated into osteoblast and chondrocyte lineages. In animal experiments, ESW treatment did not cause any side effects in segmental femoral defect rats. Catalano et al. investigated the effects of ESW stimulation on the osteogenic differentiation of hADSCs and found that ESWs can enhance hADSC differentiation toward osteoblast-like cells under osteogenic induction, and clarified the mechanisms involved.287

Ultrasonography is an important acoustic wave. Under ultrasonic irradiation, microbubble formation and collapse, and a very special physical micro-field form at the interface between the bubble and liquid substrate. Theoretical stimulation results indicated that the pressure at the gas–liquid interface was over 1000 atm, and the temperature inside the bubbles was even 4200 K in a very short period during the formation and collapse process.288 Therefore, ultrasound can regulate specific cell lineage differentiation. In addition to osteogenic differentiation of hADSCs289 and neuronal differentiation of mNSCs,290 researchers have extended dynamic ultrasound treatment to control hepatocyte-like cell differentiation.291

Several researchers have used customized ultrasound, such as low-intensity ultrasound (LIUS)292,293 and low-intensity pulsed ultrasound (LIPUS)294,295 to investigate specific lineage differentiation.

LIUS exposure at an intensity range of 100–500 mW cm−2 can promote the neuronal differentiation and neurite growth of neural stem/progenitor cells (NSPCs) and denser neuronal networks.292,293 Thus, LIUS may serve as a potential tool for neuronal induction differentiation and neural cell therapy.

Furthermore, LIPUS with different pulsed parameters can promote the osteogenic,296,297 chondrogenic298 or multilineage differentiation299 of different types of MSCs as well as neuronal differentiation of gingival stem/progenitor cells.300 In addition, this LIPUS strategy has been extended to regulate the fate of hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells. Xu et al. treated HSPCs with LIPUS (1.5 MHz, 200 μs burst sine wave with repetition rate at 1.0 kHz).301 LIPUS treatment can enhance the viability proliferation of hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells, promote burst-forming unit-erythroid colony formation and favors cell differentiation into early erythroid progenitors without affecting the percentage of CD34+ and CD14+ cells.

Therefore, the use of acoustic energy to regulate stem cell fate remains a significant challenge.

7.2 Acoustic waves coordinated with biomaterials

As mentioned above, acoustic waves do not have selectivity for specific cells and may cause cell death or damage to the cells around the target cells in vivo. To overcome this shortage, researchers designed biomaterials and realized the enhanced regulation of stem cells cultured on the surface of the materials under ultrasound irradiation. The energy of the ultrasonic wave can be localized in a special area or transfer another type of energy mode, which can regulate stem cells cultured on the surface of the materials more efficiently. It should be noted that the nanostructures can be easily driven by the localized extremely high energy generated by the formation and collapse of the bubbles under acoustic irradiation and cause the stress or stain of nanostructures, which affects the behavior of the stem cells attached to the surface of the nanostructured materials. Therefore, acoustic wave stimulation and nanomaterials have a synergistic effect on cell differentiation because the former has noninvasive and non-contact properties, while the latter provides the possibility for transplantation in vivo.

To investigate the synergistic effect of acoustic waves and materials on cell fate, primary osteoblast-like cells were cultured on porous polycaprolactone (PCL), and stimulated by a specific acoustic wave, surface acoustic waves (SAWs), which are generated by a specially designed microcircuit on a piezoelectric material.302 It was found that over 80% of the osteoblast-like cells survived after SAW treatment (20 MHz for 10–30 s, 380 mW), and von Kossa staining images indicated that cells both with and without SAW stimulation could differentiate toward osteoblasts in mineralization-promoting medium after 18 days. Although this study did not provide more detailed or solid evidence for the enhancement of osteogenic differentiation of primary osteoblast-like cells, this method has great potential to regulate cell fate by using the coordinated effect of acoustic waves and nanostructured materials.

Representative ultrasonic types, LIUS and LIPUS, were also utilized to investigate stem/progenitor cell differentiation on material scaffolds. Park et al. selected rabbit MSCs as a model to explore how LIUS exposure affects cell fate both in vitro303 and in vivo.304 They used 3D alginate material to encapsulate rabbit MSCs at different passages and applied LIUS stimulation (1 MHz, 200 mW cm−2, 10 min every 12 h for 1 or 2 weeks). The mRNA levels of chondrogenic markers such as Sox-9, aggrecan, and type II collagen were clearly higher in the LIUS-stimulated group at early passages and decreased overall by the passages.303 Similarly, biodegradable polyglycolic acid (PGA) non-woven mesh was implemented as a scaffold for the construction of rabbit MSCs and then implanted in nude mice.304 Applying LIUS (0.8 MHz, 200 mW cm−2, and 10 min day−1) to the nude mouse model, the size and shape of the implanted constructs changed progressively over 4 weeks with or without LIUS. Their research revealed the positive role of LIUS as a conventional mechanical stimulation in the chondrogenesis of MSCs. The nanostructured materials-mediated mechanical cues including topological surfaces have been reviewed in the section 2.2.2, and topological surface nanostructures were combined with ultrasound stimulation and can mediate physical cues for regulating cell fate in this part. Vertical microtube arrays with topological surfaces of 5 μm diameter and 5, 10, or 20 μm in height were fabricated by laser direct writing via two-photon polymerization of a biocompatible photopolymer.305 The early osteoblastic marker ALP activity increased by up to 50% with LIPUS (40 kHz, 60 mW cm−2, 20 min day−1, 5 days) treatment. The late osteoblastic marker OCN secretion significantly increased on the topographical surface compared with that on the flat surfaces, and the OCN secretion increase was also induced by the synergistic effect of LIPUS treatment and the increase in microtube height. These findings will improve the understanding of osteogenic differentiation and realize the combination of nanostructure-mediated mechanical signals with acoustic cues.

In fact, the investigation on applying acoustic wave to regulate the cell fate for clinical applications has just begun, and will be very “hot” due to the extremely high temperature, electrical field, and electromagnetic field generated at the interface of gas–liquid interface of the bubbles generated by ultrasonic irradiation. Most importantly, various ultrasonic imaging and therapy instruments have been developed, which will promote research related to the regulation of cells by acoustic waves in vitro and in vivo.

8 Summary and future outlook

Applying physical signals, including electrical, mechanical, light, heat, magnetic, and acoustic signals to the stem cells provides a powerful toolbox for the regulation of stem cell fate with timely and energy-controllable stimulation and is expected to have clinical applications in the future. However, most conventional techniques for applying physical signals are only suitable for in vitro experiments as they require large-size signal generators, lack the ability to recognize and specifically regulate the target stem cells, and cannot avoid affecting the surrounding cells or tissues, which prevents the practical application of regulating the stem cell fate using physical signals. A new concept termed as the “regulation of the stem cell fate via physical signals mediated by nanostructures” was proposed in this review after summarizing the findings of previous research studies related to the physical cues of biomaterials guiding the stem cell differentiation.

Based on the concept of “regulation of the stem cell fate via physical signals mediated by nanostructures,” localized wireless physical signals can be obtained at the surface of nanostructured biomaterials under an external physical field, while the physical stimulation can be realized locally, timely, and quantitively in the stem cells cultured on the surface of these materials. This mode of wireless physical stimulation using specially designed functional nanostructures and an external field will have great applications in tissue regeneration.

However, this research field is still very young and significant efforts must be made before it can be widely used for stem cell therapy. Moreover, there are significant challenges in the future research aspects and practical applications of this field. Several aspects related to this field require further elucidation in future studies.

8.1 To understand the mechanisms by which the localized physical signals regulate the stem cell fate

Although various potential receptors and signaling pathways associated with stem cell differentiation have been identified by conventional biological investigation methods, the interaction mechanism between the localized physical stimulation and the specific part of the stem cells is very difficult to understand, as there is no specific tool or methodology to detect or observe nanoscale variations when the receptor is activated. In this regard, there is a great demand for interdisciplinary research involving collaborations among the biophysicists, cell biologists, and researchers from microelectromechanical systems. In this field, micro-devices, nano-measurement technology, micro-physical stimulation affecting surface receptors of stem cells, and the pathway of differentiation is very important. Different fields, like biophysics, molecular biology, and materials science to should be explore to obtain a complete picture of this topic.

8.2 Concept design and research instrument building to realize wireless physical stimulation

There should be different concepts for the regulation of stem cells by nanostructure-mediated physical signals. So far, the published research related to the use of nanomaterial-mediated localized physical signals to tune the stem cell fate is just a drop in the ocean. Some exciting fields related to this research area are awaiting further exploration.

It should be noted that the design concept for generating wireless physical signals to stimulate stem cells includes three factors. (1) The kind of physical signals that should be chosen for the regulation of stem cell fate based on the cell type and direction of stem cell differentiation; (2) the criteria to choose materials with special energy conversion properties, such as mechanical-electrical, photoelectrical, ultrasonic-mechanical, magnetic-mechanical, and magnetic-electrical conversions. The method to design and synthesize nanostructures to realize local stimulation, particularly, the materials that have high biocompatibility and biodegradability; (3) the design of efficient devices or systems to apply external physical fields or irradiation to nanostructures in vitro or in vivo.

8.3 In vivo investigation on tissue regeneration based on the regulation of stem cells via nanostructure-mediated physical signals

To generate proper physical signals for directing the stem cell fate, the external stimulation parameters should be further optimized for both in vitro and in vivo conditions. Some physical signals, especially UV, have high cytotoxicity and may hamper cell survival in the human body and their safety needs to be critically assessed. Based on the success of in vitro research, further in vivo investigations assessing the use of wireless physical stimulation for stem cell differentiation and tissue regeneration should be comprehensively conducted. The effects of surrounding tissues, cells, and the surrounding microenvironment on energy conversions should be studied, which will be beneficial for the clinical applications of this concept. During in vivo investigations, proper balancing of the contradictions between biodegradation and the maintenance time of the energy conversion property should be done.

8.4 Interdisciplinary teams are very important for this topic

As mentioned above, this topic involves different disciplines, such as materials science, cell biology, molecular biology, biophysics, and microelectronics. This research topic has typical characteristics of interdisciplinary research. As such, this research cannot be accomplished by a single well-organized team. To understand the fundamental mechanism and realize the clinical applications of this concept, a large number of biological and physical key problems, difficulties associated with microfabrication and device manufacturing, and in vivo verification problems should be tackled by integrated interdisciplinary teams.

There is still a long way to go from conducting successful experimental studies to exploring the beneficial clinical applications of this concept. Although there are already many obstacles in this domain, it is reasonable to expect that new materials and devices will continue to emerge and be at the forefront of research in the future. There are tremendous opportunities and challenges in this field and we hope that this review will aid in the development of safe, noninvasive, and non-contact platforms, which can be used to guide stem cell differentiation pathways. We also believe that further research in this area will drive progress in many other fields as well, including materials science, cell biology, and clinical therapy.
