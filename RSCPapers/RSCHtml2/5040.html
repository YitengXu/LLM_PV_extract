Decorating carbon nanotubes with metal or semiconductor nanoparticles

Abstract

Due to their large chemically active surface and stability at high temperatures carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have been used as a support material for the dispersion and stabilization of metal and semiconductor nanoparticles (NPs). These hybrid materials have found several applications in catalysis, nanoelectronics, optics, nanobiotechnology, etc. Several ways have been described in the literature to immobilize NPs on CNTs and they can be divided into two main pathways: (a) the formation (and stabilization) of metal NPs directly on the carbon nanotube surface, and (b) the connection of chemically modified NPs to carbon nanotubes or to modified CNTs. A plethora of methods for the synthesis of different NPs have been very recently developed. This know-how is now available for the generation of a large variety of new hybrid products in combination with CNTs. A selection of representative examples of the synthesis, properties and applications of NPCNTs is here reported and discussed.

1. Introduction

The exciting electronic and mechanical properties of carbon nanotubes (CNTs), reported first by Iijima in 1991,1 have generated broad and interdisciplinary attention.2,3 In recent years, many efforts have led to the development of versatile chemical modification methodologies, targeting CNT derivatives with even more attractive features.4 To this end a wide range of derivatives have been prepared and fully characterized that exhibit promising applications in nanobiotechnology, energy conversion/fuel storage, catalysis, nanotechnology, electronic nanodevices etc.5,6

Nanomaterials7 and especially metal nanoparticles8 (NPs) have emerged as a new class of compounds that are particularly interesting for materials science due to their unique electronic, optical, magnetic and catalytic properties. Importantly, these features differ from those of the bulk materials9 and depend on the size and shape of the NP.10 Current issues in NP research focus on the synthesis of NPs of noble metals such as Au, Ag, Pt;11 on studies of their properties;12 and on their applications in several areas from chemistry13 to physics,14 to material sciences,15 to biology and medicine.16 Impressive results have been attained with semiconductor NPs.10a,17 Of particular interest are new synthetic strategies to control the shape and the dimensionality of the NPs.17,18 Intensive research has been devoted to the study of shape-dependence of the fascinating properties and their applications.19 Other classes of nanoparticles are intensively studied, such as iron/platinum (FePt), cobalt/platinum (CoPt),20 magnetic nanoparticles,21 that exhibit novel optical and electrical properties.22

The combination of the two classes of material (CNTs and NPs) may lead to a successful integration of the properties of the two components in the new hybrid materials that present important features for catalysis and nanotechnology.23

The CNT surface serves as a template where NPs are absorbed or, when bearing functional groups, CNTs may be linked through organic fragments,24 to metal or semiconductor NPseither naked or stabilized by a protecting monolayer.

The first report on decorating CNTs with metal clusters dates back to 1994. Ajayan and coworkers25 described the use of single walled carbon nanotubes (SWNTs) as a support material for dispersing ruthenium NPs that act as catalysts in heterogeneous catalysis. In particular, ruthenium 2,5-pentanedionate was spread onto the walls of SWNTs and subsequently reduced under a hydrogen stream. The Ru NPs thus obtained were well dispersed on the nanotube surface as corroborated by detailed TEM analysis. The final material contains 0.2% w/w of Ru. Catalytic assays that include liquid-phase hydrogenation of cinnamaldehyde revealed a particularly high selectivity for cinnamyl alcohol (up to 92%) with an 80% conversion of cinnamaldehyde. In contrast, under similar conditions, Ru NPs of similar size supported on Al2O3 catalyze the formation of cinnamyl alcohol with a selectivity of 2030% only.

Following this promising work, hybrid composites were developed with either metal or semiconductor NPs. This review highlights hybrid composites constituting of CNTs and metallic and semiconductor materials. A selection of examples is here critically reported as representative of the many different synthetic methodologies so far reported. These new hybrid materials have been used as catalysts and comparison with the performance obtained using more traditional catalysts is not always available for the different reaction conditions used. Nevertheless new frontiers are open taking advantage of new properties still under investigation.

2. Carbon nanotubes/nanoparticles

The composites of CNTs with NPs can be achieved through two main pathways. Naked NPs are grown and/or deposited directly onto the CNT surface. In an alternative approach, NPs are pre-formed and connected to CNTs using covalent linking through organic fragments.

2.1 Formation of metal nanoparticles directly on the carbon nanotube surface

Salts of noble metals are commonly used as precursors of NPs, which are obtained by a reduction process. If this process occurs in the presence of CNTs, the NPs can be deposited onto the CNT walls mostly through van der Waals interactions, which in some cases seem to be sufficiently strong to guarantee meaningful adhesion. Various methodsapplication of heat, light, and reducing agentshave been utilized to perform the metal cation reduction. Precious and noble metals (Pt, Au, Pd, Ag, Rh, Ru) as well as Ni26,27 have been used extensively in the literature.2651 These metals are commonly used in heterogeneous catalytic reactions and their properties can be enhanced when CNTs are employed as support materials.23 Applications of these hybrid materials as functional components in supercapacitors,32 gas sensors,33a biosensors, hydrogen storage have been reported.26,33b

Chen and coworkers28 addressed its interest to a variety of metals and described a method for the growth of Pt, Ag, Au, Pd and Cu NPs and fibers34 onto multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWNTs). The metal cations were initially dispersed onto the nanotube surfaces and then reduced under hydrogen stream while heating. Besides acting as a supporting material, CNTs, very likely, play the role of templates in tailoring the size of metal particles. This hypothesis was supported by the fact that the NPs are remarkably larger when the reduction of the metal cations is performed in the presence of graphite or amorphous carbon. Moreover, the number of NPs deposited on graphite or active carbon using the same method is very low with respect to that found in the presence of CNTs.

Lordi and coworkers35 treated SWNTs with diluted nitric acid to create oxygenated functions on the nanotube walls. The surface presents mostly carboxylic groups that can act as anchors in the formation and deposition of Pt NPs. Ethylene glycol (EG) was used as the reducing agent. The final material contained about 10% of Pt NPs, with a mean diameter of about 12 nm (Fig. 1). The PtSWNT catalytic system was probed in the hydrogenation of 3-methyl-2-butenal and its activity started above 75 C. These results seem to be promising even though they did not show any significant catalytic enhancement for the Pt decorated SWNTs compared to traditional catalysts.


Fig. 1 Formation and deposition of Pt NPs directly on oxidized CNTs (EG = ethylene glycol).35

Polymer electrolyte and direct methanol fuel cells (PEFC and DMFC respectively) are new power sources for several applications such as electric vehicles and electronic portable devices. Platinum NPs and alloys immobilized on carbon black are the most utilized electrocatalysts in these devices. However, the high cost that occurred from the use of Pt as a catalyst is a serious disadvantage for the commercialization of such devices. Carbon nanotubes could be an ideal substrate in this application since they offer very attractive characteristics such as high surface area, excellent electronic conductivity and high chemical stability.

Xin and coworkers reported a procedure for the synthesis of PtMWNT nanocomposites in order to study their catalytic efficiency in DMFC.36 Two different reduction procedures were used in the preparation of Pt NPs supported on oxidized MWNTs. A first sample was obtained by reduction of the hexachloroplatinic acid with an aqueous solution of formaldehyde whereas the second sample was obtained by reduction of the Pt precursor with an EG solution. Both samples contained a metal loading of 10 wt% but the one prepared by EG treatment had a more homogeneous dispersion of spherical Pt NPs with a narrow particle size distribution. TEM and XRD analyses show a 2.5 nm average particle size. The products were tested for their efficiency in DMFC, whereas the EG-generated sample displayed significantly higher performance than the Pt loaded on commercial XC-72 carbon. This improvement in catalytic performance was attributed to the greater dispersion of the supported Pt particles when the EG method is used and to possible unique structural and higher electrical properties of the MWNTs once compared to carbon black XC-72 as a support.

A uniform deposition of Pt NPs with a diameter lower than 5 nm was obtained using a sonochemical method.37 The resulting material had a loading of up to 30% as shown in Fig. 2. PtCNT composites were used for cyclic voltammetry measurements in the electrochemical adsorption and desorption of hydrogen, exhibiting much higher efficiency as compared to Pt on carbon black.


Fig. 2 TEM images of high loaded CNTs with Pt NPs at 10, 20, and 30 wt%, respectively, from left to right (reproduced with permission from ref. 37)

Liu et al.38 applied a sensitization step for Pt NP deposition on the surface of highly functionalized CNTs. In this case CNTs were oxidized with HNO3 and K2Cr2O7/H2SO4 and then sensitized by aging in SnCl2/HCl solution. Pt NPs were successfully deposited on the sensitized CNTs by an electroless plating procedure. The sensitization step seems to influence the deposition and the homogeneous distribution of Pt NPs in a substantial way. The PtCNT electrodes showed high electrocatalytic activity for oxygen reduction in a single stack polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) fuel cell. Iridium has also been used as a promoter for the formation of Pt/Ru/Ir alloy NPs with very small sizes (about 1 nm). Their immobilization on CNTs produced a Pt/Ru/IrCNT composite that shows excellent activity in methanol oxidation.39

It has been observed that Ni NPs facilitate the chemisorption of hydrogen on CNTs thus suggesting that NiCNT composites could be used for effective reversible hydrogen storage.16 Xu and coworkers used Pd and Sn as nucleation seeds for the deposition of Ni and Pd NPs on CNTs by electroless plating.27

A different approach to CNT functionalization takes advantage of microwave irradiation to introduce carboxyl, carbonyl, hydroxyl, and allyl terminal groups on the CNT surface without the use of strong acids or ultrasonication. The functional groups serve as the preferred nucleation centers for the reduction of the metal ions present in solution. MWNTs were dispersed into a water solution containing HAuCl4, using EG as reducing agent. After microwave irradiation, gold NPs uniformly dispersed on the CNT surfaces were formed (Fig. 3).40

Fig. 3 Decoration of multiwalled CNTs with Au NPs of 310 nm diameter. (Reproduced with permission from ref. 40).

Dai and coworkers reported an unprecedented result related to the reduction of metal ions in the presence of CNTs.41 The authors observed that Pt and Au NPs could be formed in the presence of CNTs, from metal precursors without any reducing agent. They proposed that a direct redox reaction occurs between the metal ions and CNTs. The NPs formed spontaneously and exclusively on SWNT walls right after the immersion of CNTs in a solution of HAuCl4 (Au3+) or of Na2PtCl2 (Pt2+) for 3 min. The metal ion reduction and SWNT oxidation during this process was probed by measuring the electrical conductance of the SWNTs immersed in solutions. The latter act as the electron donors and the hole injection into SWNTs is expected to generate an increase in the electrical conductance to the already p-type nanotubes due to O2 doping under ambient conditions. This electroless metal deposition was effective only using Au and Pt. Other metal cations such as Ag+, Ni2+ and Cu2+ could not be reduced in the same way because of their lower redox potentials (Fig. 4).

Another method for the electroless deposition of metal NPs on CNTs in the absence of any additional reducing agent has also been reported.42 In this case, metals with lower redox potentials than that of CNTs such as Cu and Ag NPs were included. The reaction process is described in Fig. 5.


Fig. 5 Schematic illustration of the deposition of metal nanoparticles on carbon nanotubes via the substrate enhanced electroless deposition (SEED) process.42

The deposition of metal NPs is achieved via the redox reaction of a galvanic cell, where the nanotube acts as a cathode for metal deposition (e.g. Au) via reduction of metal ions in solution, while a metal substrate (e.g. Cu) serves as an anode where metal atoms are oxidized to the corresponding ions and dissolved in solution. This method can be used for the growth and deposition of any metal NPs on carbon nanotubes as long as the redox potential of the metal substrate is lower than that of the metal ions in solution, indicating a great potential for functionalization of carbon nanotubes with various metal NPs.

Recently, the immobilization on SWNTs of Au, Pt, and Rh NPs obtained by reduction of the precursor salts by polyethylene glycol has been reported (Fig. 6). The addition of oleylamine that passivate the NP surface enhanced solubility of the new materials in organic solvents,43 which can be an advantage for applications in the catalysis of organic reactions.


Fig. 6 Modification of SWNTs by metal NPs and subsequent addition of oleylamine. Inset: a representative TEM image from Pt (oleylamine)SWNT composite.43

Another method used to form metal NPs on CNTs is electrodeposition.44,45 Immersing CNTs in a solution of HAuCl4, K2PtCl4, or (NH4)2PdCl4 and controlling the potential, the pulse and the concentration of the precursor salt in an electrochemical system results in the deposition of Au, Pt and Pd NPs respectively on CNTs. The external surface of the NTs was fully covered by metal and thus the product could be characterized as a metallic nanowire. The electrodeposition occurs to the same extent on the sidewall surface and on the tips of CNTs and the presence of carboxylate, hydroxyl or other groups did not act as activators in the electrodeposition (Fig. 7).


Fig. 7 Schematic illustration of the SWNT as an electrode used for electrodeposition of metal NPs.44

Metal NPCNT composites have often been used as catalysts in a variety of organic reactions showing very good performance. CNTs offer important advantages as a supporting material in catalytic systems. They are a very light but stiff material, with large surface area, high thermal and electrical conductivity and chemical inertness under normal conditions. Metal NPs are in general, easily immobilized on the CNT surface in fine dispersion. In contrast to other supporting materials such as amorphous carbon, CNTs are not a porous material. As a result, most of their surface is accessible to metal deposition and the deposited NPs are exposed to the reactants. Furthermore, the lack of porosity in CNTs facilitates the diffusion of the reactants toward the metallic surface and the removal of the reaction products from the catalytic surface. Indeed, the difficulty in diffusion and recovery of the products are major disadvantages of the most common supporting materials in catalytic reactions.

As an example, Pd and Rh NPs and a Rh/Pd alloy on CNTs have been recently compared for catalytic hydrogenations and coupling reactions.46 A water-in-oil microemulsion technique and the hydrogen reduction of cationic precursors of the metals have been applied to prepare and deposit metal NPs onto CNT surfaces. The metal precursor was added to a waterhexane microemulsion and the reduction by hydrogen gave rise to NPs dispersed on the CNT outer surface. The microemulsion technique leads to a fine dispersion of NPs with small size, in contrast to a simple deposition. The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis of the composite materials on SWNTs showed a metal deposition of about 13% for Pd and 17% for Rh. The metal NPCNT composites were tested as catalysts for organic synthesis reactions. As an example, PdCNT composites were used to catalyze the Heck coupling reaction between iodobenzene and styrene (Fig. 8).


Fig. 8 Catalytic formation of 1,2-diphenylethane from styrene after Heck coupling reaction and hydrogenation in the presence of PdCNT and PtCNT as catalyst respectively.46

The product was obtained with 94% yield after 3 h while under the same reaction conditions the classical Pd/carbon (10 wt%) gave the product with 53% yield in 24 h. Subsequent hydrogenation with PtCNT yielded 1,2-diphenylethane with 99% yield after 10 min using 1 atm of H2 gas at 25 C. In comparison, when commercial Pt catalyst was used, the hydrogenation reached a similar yield after only 1 h. The hydrogenation of anthracene was selected to test the catalytic activity of RhCNT composites. Anthracene was completely hydrogenated after 1 h at 25 C under 10 atm of H2. Using the commercial Rh/carbon catalyst, no conversion of anthracene was detected after 2 h of reaction under the same conditions. RhPd NPs deposited on MWNTs using the microemulsion procedure seem to be more active than the single metal NPs on CNTs in the catalytic hydrogenation of anthracene.

Rh NPs deposited onto CNTs have been also used to catalyze hydrogenation and hydroformylation reactions.47 Highly dispersed Rh NPs were obtained from a [Rh2Cl2(CO)4] complex in the presence of carboxylated MWNT surface by a reduction step (Fig. 9). This catalyst exhibited very high selectivity for both hydrogenation and hydroformylation.


Fig. 9 Formation of Rh NPs on CNTs.47

Hor and coworkers immobilized Pt clusters on CNTs.48 Carboxylic groups on the CNT surface, obtained by oxidation with HNO3 or a H2SO4HNO3 mixture, behave as nucleation sites for a well dispersed deposition of Pt NPs on the surface of CNTs. The authors suggest that such kinds of materials are potentially useful in catalysis, while the formation of these composites through a chemical modification method would also be beneficial to the preparation and applications of new metalnanofiber composites.

PtCNT composites have also been synthesized in supercritical carbon dioxide using Pt(II)-acetylacetonate as the metal precursor (Fig. 10).49 These catalysts showed to possess a high catalytic activity both in the oxidation of methanol and in the oxygen reduction reactions. This performance has been attributed to the large surface area of CNTs and the decrease in the overpotential for methanol oxidation and oxygen reduction reactions. Electrochemical measurements showed that the oxygen reduction reaction at the PtCNT electrode is a diffusion-controlled process. The experimental results show that PtCNT nanocomposites synthesized in supercritical carbon dioxide are effective electrocatalysts for low temperature fuel cells.


Fig. 10 A typical TEM image of CNTs decorated by Pt NPs synthesized in supercritical carbon dioxide (reproduced with permission from ref. 49).

Xu and coworkers have reported that the presence of salts such as sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), sodium tosylate, lithium triflate, or lithium perchlorate in the reaction solution leads to a high dispersion and loading of platinum NPs on CNTs without aggregation (Fig. 11).50 The salt seems to support the heterogeneous nucleation which results in a fine metal dispersion and depresses the homogeneous nucleation which causes the growth of large aggregates of metal NPs.


Fig. 11 Schematic representation of salt-directed selective metal nucleation and growth on CNTs (left part) and the TEM images of PtCNT derivatives (right part) (reproduced with permission from ref. 50).

2.2 Connecting metal nanoparticles and CNTs

Metal NPs can be linked to the CNT surface via covalent or weaker bonds. Therefore, the NPs are prepared and modified with suitable functional groups for the connection to the CNT surface. These linkers can be of two types: (1) the functional groups may be used to form covalent bonds with functional groups present on the CNT surface or (2) the linker simply sticks to the CNT surface through weak intermolecular interactions such as stacking, hydrophobic or electrostatic attractions.
2.2.1 Covalent linkage. Haddon and collaborators51 first reported that oxidation of SWNTs and subsequent reaction with aliphatic amines, to give amides, leads to soluble functionalized SWNTs. Au NPs were connected to oxidized SWNTs using aminothiols or bifunctional thiols, which act as linkers for Au colloids (Fig. 12).52,53

The so-called Bingel reaction has also been used to functionalize the CNT surface with thioether groups.54 These groups then bind gold NPs as shown in Fig. 13.


Fig. 13 Connection of gold NPs to CNTs using the Bingel reaction and the AFM image of the as formed AuSWNT (reproduced with permission from ref. 54).
2.2.2 Hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen bonds. Hydrophobic interactions between the ligands forming the monolayer that passivate the metal surface have been used to immobilize the metal NPs onto CNTs. Acetone molecules adsorbed on CNTs can interact hydrophobically with Au nanoclusters covered with a monolayer of octanethiols.55 These systems may become important for exploring and creating a rich variety of molecular nanostructures for device applications.

A simple procedure has been reported to produce a composite material where MWNTs and SWNTs were decorated with Au NPs protected by a monolayer of dodecanethiol molecules simply by mixing dispersions of both components in dichloromethane.56 It is important to point out that the nanoparticle protective monolayer imparts solubility properties to the new composite. The resulting AuCNT system shows strong electronic coupling, which suggests interesting physicochemical properties. Transient absorption spectra analysis, following photoexcitation, suggests that the Au NPs function as electron acceptors, receiving additional electron density from MWNTs or SWNTs.

A combination of hydrophobic and hydrogen bond interactions has been used to anchor mixed-monolayer protected Au clusters to functionalized surfaces of CNTs.57 Au NPs, with a size of 2 nm or 5 nm, covered by a mixed-monolayer of decanethiol and mercaptoundecanoic acid, were linked efficiently to oxidized CNTs due to the presence of carboxylic acid groups on the surface. Thus, besides the hydrophobic interactions between the alkyl chains and the CNT surface, hydrogen bonds between carboxylic groups of CNTs and those present on the NP surface led to very stable composite materials (Fig. 14). Thermal treatment, for example, at 300 C to remove the capping shells of the Au NPs induced aggregation of NPs that stick strongly to the nanotube surface. Sonication in hydrophobic solvents failed to disassemble the composite material.


Fig. 14 Schematic illustrations of the assembly of mixed-monolayer capped NPs on oxidized CNTs and a characteristic TEM image of the resulting derivative (reproduced with permission from ref. 57).

Very recently, Zhu and coworkers have proposed that bamboo-like carbon nanotubes (BCNTs) can be used as a substrate for the immobilization of Au NPs rather better than normal carbon nanotubes.58 The authors observed that nitrogen atoms present on the wall of BCNTs contribute to the high-density and uniform assembly of Au NPs on BCNTs. The BCNTs were produced by pyrolysis of iron(II) phthalocyanine then were dispersed in toluene by sonication and mixed with a solution of organically modified Au NPs by a simple hand shaking. AuBCNT nanocomposites were isolated by centrifugation and characterized. The lower part of Fig. 15 presents characteristic TEM images of pristine and Au decorated BCNTs. These images clearly revealed that gold NPs are finely dispersed and present a high dense population of NPs on the N doped CNT surface. The observed increase in density of NPs with respect to classical CNTs has been explained by the authors by the presence of specific AuN interactions which determine the uniform assembly of NPs.


Fig. 15 (Upper part) Illustration of the direct assembly of Au NPs on bamboo-like CNTs. (Lower part) TEM images of bamboo-like CNTs (left) and AuBCNT nanocomposites (center and right) (reproduced with permission from ref. 58).

Fig. 16 Ammonium-modified MWNTs and the noncovalent self-assembly of Au nanoparticles with crown ether-modified monolayer (upper part). TEM images of crown-modified Au NPs (a) adsorbed on modified MWNTs and (b) not adsorbed on unmodified MWNTs (reproduced with permission from ref. 60).

Very recently, Fitzmaurice and collaborators reported a predictive model for interactions (charge transfer, van der Waals, osmotic, elastic, non elastic and covalent) between TOAB-stabilized Au NPs (TOAB = tetraoctylammonium bromide) and covalently modified MWNTs with alkyl and alkylthiol chains in order to predict the coverage of CNTs by Au NPs for a range of different experimental conditions.59 For example, comparison between theory and experiments showed that as the length of the alkyl chains at the surface of the MWNTs increases, the coverage decreases. The understanding of the parameters that control the MWNT-templated assembly of NPs has been the basis for the design and control of more complex hybrid materials.

An elegant self-assembly coverage of cation-modified MWNTs with crown-modified Au NPs has been reported by Fitzmaurice and coworkers60 where ammonium cations thread the crown ethers present on the Au NP monolayer surface (Fig. 16).61

2.2.3 -Stacking. Among other aromatic compounds, pyrene derivatives stack onto the CNT surface by interactions.6264 Thus, pyrene molecules, bearing a long aliphatic chain terminated by a thiol group, can connect CNTs to Au NPs via -staking of the pyrene moiety to the CNT surface as shown in Fig. 17(a).62 Spectroscopic experiments on the composite material show that the pyrene fluorescence is completely quenched and the Raman spectrum of the CNT is enhanced. These results imply a charge transfer character between CNTs and Au NPsmediated by the linker. Electron transfer processes between donor groups and CNTs have also been observed to occur on SWNTZn porphyrin hybrid materials.63,64
Fig. 17 Noncovalent attachment of gold or magnetic NPs onto SWNTs using pyrene derivatives as interlinkers.62,65

A similar strategy was adopted to anchor magnetic NP, such as Fe3O4, Co or CoPt to SWNT, using a ligand having a pyrene ring and a carboxylic group at the two ends, respectively. The pyrene is attached on the SWNT surface while the carboxylic group is linked to the magnetic NPs resulting in their homogeneous dispersion on the SWNT surface. Furthermore the presence of an organophilic layer of oleic acid surrounding the inorganic nanoclusters makes the resulting composites highly soluble in organic solvents, such as chloroform, toluene, hexane [Fig. 17(b)].65

Besides the enhanced solubility, an interesting feature of the modified CNT is the modification of their magnetic properties.65 Both properties are illustrated in Fig. 18. Photographs (a) and (b) show the characteristically colored solutions of magnetically modified CNTs in chloroform. Photograph (c) presents the same composite material dispersed in acetone and (d) the response of this dispersion to a permanent magnet. Another important issue, in this study, is that the NPs are not directly and permanently attached to the CNT surface. Due to the linker length, there is a tunable distance between the NP and the nanotube surface, which could be modulated in the study of the electronic and magnetic properties of the modified CNTs. The magnetically coated CNTs have great potential as tips for high resolution Magnetic Force Microscopy (MFM) or for several other applications in biology and nanoelectronics.


Fig. 18 (a), (b) Photographs of magnetically modified CNTs in chloroform at different concentrations, (c) dispersion in acetone and (d) their response to a permanent magnet.65

Another example is the formation of AuCNT nanocomposites in aqueous solutions using 1-pyrenemethylamine as the linker (Fig. 19).66 Au NPs with diameters of 24 nm were densely assembled on the surface of the MWNT. Au NPs were functionalized with 1-pyrenemethylamine. The formation of the composite material was followed by UV-Vis and luminescence spectroscopy. In the modified Au NPs, the absorbance value of pyrene chromophore was decreased and the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) was red shifted from 508 to 556 nm due to interparticle plasmon coupling. The photoluminescence of the pyrene moiety was largely quenched when attached to gold NPs and the pyrene emission intensity was further lowered upon formation of Au NPCNT composites. The present approach to form deposition of high-density gold NPs on the surface of MWNTs has been extended to other molecules with similar structures such as N-(1-naphthyl)ethylenediamine and phenethylamine, demonstrating the generality of this strategy for making AuCNT nanostructure composites.

An interesting application of PtCNT nanocomposites, as anode catalyst for methanol oxidation fuel cells, has been recently described (Fig. 20).67 Pt NPs covered by triphenylphosphine (PPh3) were deposited on the surface of MWNTs. 2.2.4 Electrostatic interactions. Electrostatic interactions have also been used to anchor metal colloids to CNTs.6878 To this end, oxidized CNTs were treated with an ionic polyelectrolyte which serves as an anchor for charged metal NPs. In a typical example, oxidized CNTs were modified with a cationic polyelectrolyte and exposed to negatively charged Au NPs.68 By choosing different kinds of polyelectrolytes the surface of the CNT can be also negatively charged in order to accept positively charged NPs.

A similar electrostatic approach has been used to build mixed Au NPMWNT layers using the LBL (layer-by-layer) methodology.70 Acid-functionalized MWNTs were first covered with a layer of a positively charged polymer [PDDA, poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride)] and then with a layer of a negatively charged polymer [PSS, poly(sodium 4-styrenesulfonate)]. Subsequently, positively charged Au NPs were anchored through electrostatic interactions to the PSS layer. Positively charged Au NPs may also interact directly with MWNTs presenting carboxylate groups on their surface. However, the direct binding happens with a much lower density than that observed for the PDDA/PSS LBL method.

The polyelectrolyte LBL approach has been recently used to prepare a new class of hybrid materials composed of Au nanorods aligned along the walls of MWNTs.71 In this case, the MWNTs were treated with a negatively charged polyelectrolyte (PSS), followed by electrostatic assembly of a positively charged polyelectrolyte (PDDA). Over this charged monolayer Au nanorods, prepared following the method of Nikoobakht and El-Sayed72 and after exchange of CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) with PVP [poly(vinylpyrrolidone)], were deposited. A very uniform string of Au nanorods aligned over MWNTs is formed, where the rods connect through end-to-end contacts. Such morphology results in uniaxial plasmon coupling, but can be used to monitor the degree of alignment of CNTs within polymer films or other nanostructured systems.

Polyethyleneimine (PEI) is an amino-rich cationic polyelectrolyte, which interacts with CNTs via physisorption. PEI has been recently used as a reducing and protecting agent for the preparation of gold NPs. Dong and coworkers73 combined these two features of PEI for the formation of AuCNT composites through in situ reduction of Au cations by PEI adsorbed on the sidewalls of MWNTs (Fig. 22).


Fig. 22 Schematic illustration of the experimental procedure in which PEI possibly interacts with acid-functionalized MWNTs through electrostatic interaction and physisorption processes (reproduced with permission from ref. 73).

The coverage of Au NPs on CNT surfaces can be controlled by several parameters such as the initial concentration of PEI, the Au precursor and MWNTs, the temperature and the duration of the heat treatment.

Jerome and collaborators74 devised an easy route to decorate carbon nanotubes with magnetic NPs (Fe3O4). The two components form a stable composite by electrostatic interactions. MWNTs were functionalized with carboxylate terminated alkyl chains using pre-formed poly(2-vinylpyridine) (P2VP) chains by a covalent bonding technique using a radical metal-free method.75 The chemical functionalization led to an increase of the grafting density of carboxylate groups onto the CNT surface with respect to oxidative methods which cause, also, a severe reduction of the CNT length. The magnetite NPs were treated with a HNO3 solution to obtain positive charges at their surface. The derivatized MWNTs, as well as the charged NPs, were dispersible in water whereas the final composite was not. Fig. 23 shows a characteristic part of the decorated MWNTs where small magnetic NPs are densely immobilized on the carbon surface.


Fig. 23 Grafting of P2VP to CNTs and decoration by Fe3O4 NP. (a) TEM and (b) HRTEM images of the magnetiteCNT nanohybrids (reproduced with permission from ref. 74).

Several studies have focused on the potential applications of CNTs and their derivatives as platforms for biosensors and electronic nanodevices to exploit the ability of CNTs to promote electron transfer reactions with enzymes and other biomolecules. To this aim, Luong and collaborators76 used a PtSWNT composite to improve an electrochemical biosensor for the detection of hydrogen peroxide, an analyte that is released during the oxidation of the substrate by a pertinent oxidoreductase in the presence of oxygen. Nafion was used to modify and disperse carbon nanotubes making their surface negatively charged. Then, Pt NPs were immobilized on the modified carbon nanotube surface mainly by strong charge interactions. Nafion is a perfluorosulfonated polymer with a polar side chain, biocompatible, with unique ion exchange properties. SWNTs modified by Nafion are easily dispersible in water solutions or alcohol.77

The PtSWNT composite has been used to modify a glassy carbon electrode and a carbon fiber microelectrode in order to improve their electroactivity for H2O2. The authors demonstrated that Pt NPs are in electrical contact, through the SWNTs, with the glassy carbon or carbon fiber, enabling the composite structure to function as an electrode. With glucose oxidase as an enzyme model, a biosensor was constructed, more sensitive to glucose than those modified by Pt NPs or CNTs alone. The response time and detection limit of this biosensor was determined to be 3 s and 0.5 M, respectively.76

In addition to the use of metal NPCNT composites in fine dispersion for potential applications as catalytic systems or biosensors, CNTs have been used as templates for the formation of metal nanowires. In a characteristic example CNTs were used as templates for the formation of Au nanowires.78 Au NPs were stabilized with tetraoctylammonium cations and dispersed in chloroform. When immersing MWNTs in this solution, Au NPs were deposited around carbon nanotubes. The authors suggest that Au NPs are adsorbed on the CNT surface similar to the case of Au NPs interacting with fullerenes. Then, the mixed composite was heated so that the metal NPs melt and amalgamate. The result of the heating process was the formation of metal wires with large aspect ratios having CNTs as the cores. This method seems to be applicable to a wide range of metal species.

2.3 Connecting semiconductor nanoparticles and CNTs

Semiconductor NPs and CNTs represent an intriguing combination. The metal nanostructures, commonly named quantum dots (QDs), are NPs composed of groups IIIV or IIVI elements with optical and electronic properties that depend on the size of the NP as a consequence of the quantum confinement effect on the photoexcited species.

QDs are very attractive tools in biological labelling,79 solar cells,80 light emitting devices81 and other photonic applications. However several difficulties need to be overcome for their use. As an example, QDs have been used in solar cells in composites with polymers with quantum yields less than 2%. This percentage is relatively low in comparison to inorganic solar cells. This disadvantage is mainly due to the difficulty in transferring the charge carriers away from QD NPs in the device.82

CNTs decorated by QDs could act as nanowires that promote direct charge transport and efficient charge transfer to QDs, thus increasing the efficiency in solar cells. The above described technique to connect CNTs and metal NPs by using an interlinker has been also used for the connection of semiconductor NPs such as CdSe to CNTs. As an example, QD NPs were stabilized with a mixed-monolayer of trioctylphosphine oxide and 2-aminoethanethiol. The amino-functionalized QDs react with the carboxylic groups of the oxidized SWNTs, forming amide bonds.82 Using a similar route to realize QDCNT, Banerjee and Wong observed a significant charge transfer occurring in QDCNT.83

Similarly, Ozkan and collaborators84 reported the formation of heterojunctions between MWNTs and thiol-stabilized ZnS-capped CdSe QDs (Fig. 24). The surface of MWNTs was purified and functionalized with carboxylate groups by gentle oxidation (i.e., HNO3). The QD NPs were protected with 2-aminoethanethiols and the heterojunction was formed in the presence of EDC (N-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)-N-ethylcarbodiimide) between the acid groups present at the ends of oxidized CNTs and the amino groups of the QDs.


Fig. 24 Scheme of the synthesis of ZnS-capped CdSeMWNT conjugates.84

This strategy was used by Willner and collaborators to assemble a CdS NPCNT system on an Au electrode and to study the photoinduced charge transport of this system in the presence of triethanolamine (as a sacrificial electron donor).85 The generated photocurrent was impressively high, I(l390) = 0.83 mA, for the CdS NPCNT monolayer. From the intensity of the light absorbed by the sample a quantum efficiency for the photon-to-electron conversion of 25% was estimated. The quantum efficiencies increase as the digestion time treatment of the CNTs increases (shorter CNTs) reaching a quantum efficiency up to 70%. The results suggest that the length of the CNT plays a major role in the formation of photocurrents and probably the defects in the CNTs affect the extent of charge separation that follows the photoexcitation of the CdS NPs.

SnO2 is an n-type semiconductor with a wide energy gap. Chemical sensors, solar cells, heterojunctions, transparent coatings, and conductive electrodes are applications where SnO2 semiconductor is involved.

A uniform and thin coating of carbon nanotubes by SnO2 NPs was reported by Zettl and Han.86 Acid treated CNTs were dispersed in a solution of SnCl2 and conc. HCl in water. After a short sonication, SnO2 NPs with a 4 nm average diameter are visible on the nanotube surface.

SWNTs and MWNTs were also linked to CdTe NPs, through electrostatic interactions.87 The CdTe NPs were stabilized by thioglycolic acid. The novel nanohybrids were characterized both in the ground and excited state with specific attention to electron transfer chemistry. These studies provided kinetic and spectroscopic evidence that support a partial transfer of charge density, with rapid formation of microsecond-lived radical ion pair states. This remarkable feature was exploited to construct photoelectrochemical cells. Photocurrents were generated through the implementation of CdTe and SWNTs or MWNTs, which serve as excited state electron donor components and electron acceptors, respectively. Very recently Kamat and coworkers reported the synthesis of CdS NPSWNT composites. This new material shows charge transfer interactions between photoexcited QDNP and SWNTs (Fig. 25).88

An attractive application in the area of nanomedicine has very recently reported by Bottini et al.89 Streptavidin conjugated quantum dots (Str-QD) were adsorbed on the surface of the SWNTs, forming a supramolecular nanostructure fully dispersible in buffer solution. Conclusions

The most important and crucial point for the future expansion of these new hybrid materials for technolological applications is to find powerful synthetic methodologies to produce materials with reproducible properties and performances.

Many approaches have been devised to glue metal NPs to CNTs such as a reduction/deposition and chemical linking processes. By comparing the different ways, advantages and disadvantages can be found in all cases. It is the potential application that indicates which way is preferable each time. The direct reduction/deposition is usually a simple and effective method and a large amount of metal NPs are easily immobilized on the CNT surface. On the other hand, this is not an easily controlled procedure regarding the characteristics of the NP size, shape, and compositionand the percentage of the deposited metal.

Connecting pre-formed NPs with CNTs is a more complicated procedure. Normally, more than one reaction is needed, e.g., formation of the metal NPs, chemical modifications of both components and their connection. In addition, in the case of the covalent bonding, the number of metal NPs that are usually attached to the CNT surface in this way is associated with the number of carboxylates or other chemical functionalities of the CNT surface, and thus limited. The advantages of this pathway are that NPs can be selected by size or shapesince they are formed previouslyand the connection is under certain circumstances reversible.

Also, a fine dispersion with high-density can be achieved when organically modified metal NPs are immobilized on the surface of carbon nanotubes by hydrophobic or electrostatic interactions especially when the nanotubes are initially modified by polymers which enhance these interactions.

A brief description of the synthetic approaches to metal NPCNTs or metal cationCNTs and their potential applications has been presented.

The interaction of CNTs with metallic species produces significant composite derivatives with several potential applications such as in gas sensors, biosensors, electronic nanodevices, polymer electrolyte fuel cells, etc. In many recent reports metal NPCNTs are involved in catalytic systems with noteworthy results. The main advantages of CNTs as a supporting material in general are: the high surface to volume area, the thermal and electrical conductivity, the chemical inertness and the lack of porosity. The results so far reported in the field of the heterogeneous catalysis are very promising and in this area there will be a growing interest toward efficient industrial use of hybrid metal NPCNTs as catalysts. Still many aspects have to be studied more deeply such as the stability and the recovery of the catalyst. Also, a screening of the many reactions that can be catalysed by NPCNT materials is far from being complete. Another key aim is the use of CNTs as a scaffold to realize 1D self-assembled nanowires of metal, semiconductor or magnetic nanomaterials. The properties of these new materials have been disclosed only partially. The electronic communication between NPs and CNTs has opened up new avenues in the design of optoelectronic and light-energy conversion devices. It is easy to foresee that these intriguing systems will stimulate more and more studies aimed at practical applications.
