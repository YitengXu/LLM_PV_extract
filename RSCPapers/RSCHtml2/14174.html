The rapid self-reconstruction of Fe-modified Ni hydroxysulfide for efficient and stable large-current-density water/seawater oxidation

Abstract

The reasonable design of electrocatalysts with rapid self-reconstruction for an efficient oxygen evolution reaction (OER) at commercially required current densities is highly desirable but very challenging. Herein, ultrathin Fe-modified Ni hydroxysulfide (FeNiSOH) nanosheet arrays were grown in situ on Ni foam via a simple two-step oxidation strategy for efficient and stable large-current-density water/seawater oxidation. Systematic insights, including experimental and theoretical analysis, reveal that in situ S leaching from the electrode boosts its self-reconstruction and results in the more-ready generation of highly active Ni4+ species, which benefits from a reduced formation energy. Owing to its excellent physical and chemical properties, the FeNiSOH catalyst requires only low overpotentials of 207, 240, and 268 mV in alkaline water to deliver current densities of 10, 100, and 500 mA cm2, respectively, and it can work stably for 1100 hours at the commercially required current density of 500 mA cm2. Furthermore, it also exhibits excellent seawater oxidation activity and superior resistance to Cl corrosion, since it can run stably at 500 mA cm2 for over 900 hours. This work offers an efficient strategy to build rapidly self-reconstructing electrocatalysts to promote the formation of highly oxidized metal species for efficient and stable water/seawater oxidation.


Broader context

Hydrogen, an ideal renewable energy carrier with high efficiency and zero-carbon emissions, is expected to play an important role in helping mankind on the road to carbon neutrality. At present, hydrogen mainly comes from fossil fuels, known as grey hydrogen, which is in conflict with the original purpose of carbon neutrality. Electrochemical water splitting is a green hydrogen technology with a simple process and high conversion efficiency, which can be coupled with renewable energy sources (wind, solar power, etc.). Unfortunately, the high cost of precious metal catalysts and the poor efficiency of the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) limit its large-scale applications. Therefore, it is urgent to develop high-efficiency and low-cost noble-metal-free catalysts for the OER. Despite the good progress achieved in reducing overpotentials, they are still unsatisfactory in terms of the long-term stability at the commercially required current densities. Hence, we report a simple two-step oxidation strategy to fabricate an Fe-modified Ni hydroxysulfide catalyst with rapid self-reconstruction, which exhibits high efficiency and stable water/seawater oxidation capacity with low overpotentials at a large current density. Impressively, it can work stably for over 1100 and 900 hours in alkaline water and seawater, respectively, at the commercially demanded current density of 500 mA cm2.

Introduction

Hydrogen (H2) originating from electrocatalytic water splitting powered by renewable electricity sources is of high purity and cleanliness, and has been considered one of most effective ways to solve the energy crisis and environmental problems around the world.1,2 Unfortunately, electrochemical water splitting suffers from low energy conversion efficiency due to the sluggish kinetics of the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) on the anode.3 Although noble-metal-based (e.g., Ir and Ru) materials are currently the benchmarks of OER electrocatalysts, their rarity and poor stability prevent their use in large-scale application.4 To address the issue, considerable efforts have been devoted to exploring highly efficient and stable non-precious metal electrocatalysts, such as transition-metal (oxy)hydroxides,5 oxides,6 nitrides,7 phosphides,8 sulfides,9,10 and selenides.11

Among the promising candidates reported so far, the transition-metal (Ni, Fe, Co, etc.) based (oxy)hydroxides are regarded as the most efficient OER catalysts in an alkaline electrolyte.12 Moreover, with the development of in situ or operando characterization techniques, transition-metal (oxy)hydroxides have been identified as the actual active species reconstructed from most original OER catalysts (i.e., pre-catalysts).13,14 Meanwhile, it has also been demonstrated that in situ-generated highly oxidized metal species possess greatly superior activities, by regulating the orbital hybridization between metal d band and O 2p to activate oxygen species for triggering the direct OO coupling of the lattice oxygen mechanism (LOM).15,16 Nevertheless, several challenges still remain, although progress has been achieved in designing efficient electrocatalysts to reduce overpotentials. Firstly, most reconstructed catalysts exhibit a coreshell structure (Table S1, ESI) owing to limited electrolyte diffusion.17 The reconstructed degree of these catalysts may change with reaction conditions,14,17 which makes their structure and performance unpredictable and hinders an insightful understanding of the reconstruction and catalytic mechanisms. By contrast, catalysts with complete reconstruction are expected to exhibit higher durability due to their more thermodynamically stable components,18 which is important for large-current-density seawater splitting. Besides, most of the self-reconstruction process is slow and energy-intensive, as represented by hundreds of CV cycles or long-term operation under constant potentials or current densities.19,20 Secondly, the deep oxidation of metal species is thermodynamically unfavorable, and high energy consumption is ineluctable during the OER, which hinders the formation of highly oxidized metal species.21,22 Therefore, it is highly desirable to design a pre-catalyst with the ability of rapid and complete self-reconstruction for more ready generation of highly oxidized metal species. Thirdly, despite good OER performance at small current densities (100 mA cm2) being achieved in most reported catalysts, they still face an enormous challenge in terms of the long-term stability at the large current densities required for industrial application.

On the other hand, considering the abundance of seawater on our planet, which accounts for 96.5% of the world's total water, direct seawater electrolysis technology could help to alleviate the shortage of freshwater, especially in arid regions.23,24 However, direct electrolysis of seawater at large current density faces great challenges, including the chlorine evolution reaction (CER) that competes with OER at the anode, the corrosion of electrodes by Cl ions and microorganisms in natural seawater, and the deposition of insoluble precipitates [Ca(OH)2 and Mg(OH)2] during seawater splitting.7,25 More recently, some progress has been made in seawater oxidation by developing highly efficient catalysts due to the more favorable thermodynamic potential (480 mV) of OER in artificial alkaline seawater (pH > 7.5).23,26,27 Nevertheless, most of these catalysts remain catalytically active for only a short time (100 hours) in alkaline seawater due to their poor corrosion resistance, especially at high current densities. Therefore, the requirements for effective and durable OER catalysts for long-term seawater electrolysis at large current densities are more demanding, and they must have both high OER activity and excellent corrosion resistance.

To overcome these difficulties encountered in OER electrodes, a pre-catalyst prepared by a simple method has been demonstrated here to achieve rapid self-reconstruction for efficient and stable large-current-density water/seawater oxidation. That is, ultrathin Fe-modified Ni hydroxysulfide (denoted FeNiSOH) nanosheet arrays were grown in situ on Ni foam via a facile two-step oxidation strategy for the first time, which exhibit a large surface area for exposing a massive number of active sites as well as a superhydrophilic surface and excellent gas release behavior to facilitate mass transfer. Meanwhile, the nickel foam acts as both a substrate and Ni source to generate the active material, and this facile connection enables the catalyst to be firmly anchored to the substrate, guaranteeing efficient electron transfer and excellent corrosion resistance in alkaline water/seawater. In addition, systematic insights from ex/in situ characterization and DFT calculations reveal that in situ S leaching from the FeNiSOH electrode not only accelerates its reconstruction process, but also reduces the formation energy of highly active Ni4+ species, making its formation much easier during activation. Therefore, the FeNiSOH catalyst possesses outstanding OER activity in alkaline water/seawater, for which the overpotentials at current densities of 10, 100, and 500 mA cm2 only require 207, 240, and 268 mV in 1 M KOH as well as 213, 263, and 311 mV in 1 M KOH seawater, respectively. Impressively, the FeNiSOH catalyst demonstrates superior potential for industrial application since it can work stably for over 1100 and 900 hours in alkaline water and seawater, respectively, at the commercially demanded current density of 500 mA cm2.

Results and discussion

A facile two-step oxidation procedure to prepare ultrathin FeNiSOH nanosheet arrays is illustrated in Fig. 1a. As the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images show, the smooth surface of Ni foam (Fig. S1, ESI) is etched into many large weedy nanosheet fields (Fig. 1b) after a wet chemical oxidation reaction. The energy dispersive X-ray (EDX) results prove that Ni, S and O are uniformly distributed on these nanosheets to form Ni hydroxysulfide (denoted NiSOH) (Fig. S2, ESI). In this process, the Ni foam substrate serves as an Ni source for the growth of NiSOH nanosheets and provides close contact between the active materials and the substrate, which ensures efficient electron transport and stability. Further anodization results in the incorporation of Fe into NiSOH for the formation of FeNiSOH accompanied by the partial removal of S atoms, with the morphology evolving from large nanosheet fields into many smaller lawns stacked with abundant nanosheets (Fig. 1c). This unique three-dimensional nanostructure provides effective channels for facilitating electrolyte diffusion and the escape of gaseous product, ensuring the stability and activity of the electrode. To determine the optimal condition for anodic oxidation, different current densities and anodic oxidation times were also explored and discussed (Fig. S3 and S4, ESI). This facile approach is simple and efficient, so that it is easy to scale up from 1 cm2 to 100 cm2 (Fig. S5, ESI), demonstrating great potential for practical applications. Moreover, S-free FeNiOH nanosheet arrays were also prepared by the same process using pure nickel hydroxide (NiOH) nanosheet arrays as the precursor (Fig. S6 and S7, ESI).

The phase information for the self-supporting electrodes was analyzed via X-ray diffraction (XRD). It was found that no obvious diffraction peaks can be detected in the XRD patterns of the NiSOH and FeNiSOH electrodes (Fig. S8, ESI), perhaps due to the influence of their poor crystallinity and the signals of the Ni foam substrate (PDF#4-850) being too strong. As for NiOH (Fig. S9, ESI), the XRD pattern mainly matches with -phase Ni(OH)20.75H2O (PDF#38-715) and Ni(OH)2 (PDF#14-117). After anodization, the diffraction peaks for FeNiOH become very weak, possibly due to the incorporation of Fe or the partial oxidation of Ni(OH)2, resulting in an increase in structural disorder. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was further applied to study the structure and element distribution of FeNiSOH. There are no obvious lattice fringes in the high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of the FeNiSOH nanosheet (Fig. 1d and Fig. S10, ESI) due to its poor crystallinity. This can also be verified by the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (inset, Fig. 1d), which is consistent with the XRD results. Fig. 1e exhibits the dark field TEM (DFTEM) image of the FeNiSOH catalyst and its EDX elemental mapping images of Fe, Ni, O, and S, demonstrating the uniform distribution of these four elements in each nanosheet within the fields. Furthermore, as the EDX mapping results show in Fig. S11 (ESI), the atomic ratio of Fe:Ni:O:S is close to 1:1.5:11.2:0.3, where the amount of S is about 1.97%.

The vibrational modes of the as-prepared catalysts were investigated by Raman spectroscopy. The Raman spectrum of the NiSOH electrode possesses two vibration modes located at approximately 291 and 454 cm1 assigned to vibration modes of NiS and NiOH bonds, respectively, while the S-free NiOH catalyst displays only the NiOH stretching mode of Ni(OH)2 at 462 cm1 (Fig. S12, ESI).2831 After the incorporation of Fe, the FeNiSOH electrode exhibits three new characteristic peaks attributed to amorphous FeOOH: the FeOH stretching mode at about 304 cm1 and the FeO vibration modes at about 532 and 675 cm1 (Fig. 2a).3234 As for the S-free FeNiOH, a new vibration mode appears at around 497 cm1, assigned to the NiO bands associated with the presence of disordered or defective Ni(OH)2.35,36 This implies that incorporation of Fe induces partial lattice defects in the NiOH catalyst, which is in line with the XRD results. The surface chemical valence states of the electrodes were studied via X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). As the high-resolution XPS spectra of Ni 2p show in Fig. 2b, the two characteristic peaks of FeNiOH located at about 856.20 and 873.95 eV are attributed to Ni 2p3/2 and Ni 2p1/2, respectively, while there is a positive shift of around 0.19 eV for the two peaks of FeNiSOH. As for the Fe 2p spectra (Fig. 2c), two peaks at around 711.78 and 724.62 eV for FeNiOH can be assigned to Fe3+ 2p3/2 and Fe3+ 2p1/2, respectively,25,37 and there is also a positive shift of around 0.22 eV for the two peaks of FeNiSOH. Moreover, their valence states and electronic structures were investigated by the absorption-edge position of the X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XANES) spectra.38 Compared with FeNiOH, a higher absorption edge position in both Ni and Fe K-edge XANES spectra for FeNiSOH suggests that S atoms modulate the electronic structure around the metal atoms (Fig. S13, ESI), which is in line with the XPS results. Besides, the O 1s XPS spectra (Fig. 2d) show that both catalysts possess metaloxygen (MO) bonds and metalhydroxide (MOH) bonds at around 530.95 and 531.82 eV, respectively, and the peak for FeNiSOH at around 532.85 eV is assigned to chemisorbed water on the electrode surface (H2O).3941 Abundant MOH bonds indicate that metal hydroxides are the main components for both catalysts, while the MO bonds come from metal oxyhydroxides. The S 2p XPS spectrum of FeNiSOH exhibits two peaks located at about 162.70 and 163.88 eV assigned to S 2p3/2 and S 2p1/2, respectively, while the two peaks at about 169.02 and 170.05 eV correspond to SO bonds (Fig. S14, ESI), which may result from residual sulfate groups during the preparation process, or surface partial oxidation when exposed to air.25 Additionally, the Ni 2p XPS spectra (Fig. S15, ESI) and the absorption edge position of Ni K-edge XANES spectra (Fig. S16a, ESI) for NiSOH also exhibit positive shifts compared with those for NiOH, suggesting the modification of electronic structures by S atoms.

The local coordination structures of the four catalysts were investigated by k3-weighted Fourier transform extended X-ray absorption fine structure (FT-EXAFS) spectra (Fig. 2e, f and Fig. S16b, ESI), and the first-shell fitting results are displayed in Fig. S17S19 and Tables S2, S3 (ESI). Compared with the NiO path at approximately 2.04 for NiOH, NiSOH exhibits NiO and NiS paths at about 2.02 and 2.26 , respectively. After anodizing, the coordination environment of the first shell for FeNiOH remains almost unchanged, while FeNiSOH possesses an NiO path at 2.02 and an NiS path at 2.29 at the first-shell pathway. Besides, FeNiSOH shows a decreased average coordination number (CN) for the NiS path and an increased average CN for the NiO path compared with NiSOH, indicating that some of the S atoms are removed and replaced by oxygen atoms or hydroxyl groups during anodic oxidation. As for the coordination structures of Fe, there is only an FeO path at approximately 1.98 in both FeNiSOH and FeNiOH, assigned to Fe oxyhydroxide.42 Remarkably, combining all the chemical structure characterizations, it can be concluded that the FeNiSOH catalyst mainly consists of Fe-incorporated Ni hydroxysulfide and Fe oxyhydroxide, while Fe-doped Ni hydroxide and Fe oxyhydroxide are the main components of the FeNiOH catalyst.

The oxygen evolution catalysis of the as-prepared samples was firstly conducted in 1 M KOH. As presented in Fig. 3a, the Fe-modified catalysts show significantly enhanced OER activity compared with NiSOH and NiOH, illustrating that Fe modification improves the OER activity. For further detailed comparison, Fig. 3b displays the overpotentials of these catalysts at different current densities. Obviously, FeNiSOH requires only a very low overpotential of 207 mV to deliver a current density of 10 mA cm2, which is much superior to those of FeNiOH (259 mV), NiSOH (297 mV), NiOH (387 mV), or benchmark IrO2 (258 mV). As for the larger current densities of 100 and 500 mA cm2, the overpotentials for FeNiSOH are only 240 and 268 mV, respectively, much lower than those for the other electrodes and commercial IrO2. Considering the large surface area of the Ni foam substrate, its total pore area was measured by the mercury intrusion method, and the normalized activity of the catalysts is shown in Fig. S20 (ESI). The LSV curves were further normalized by loading mass of the catalysts, and the FeNiSOH electrode possessed the best OER mass activity with overpotentials of 215, 239, and 264 mV to achieve current densities of 10, 50, and 200 A g1, respectively, much superior to those of other electrodes (Fig. S21, ESI). Additionally, the OER performance of FeNiSOH with different anodizing current densities and times are also compared in Fig. S22 and 23 (ESI), and FeNiSOH exhibits the best OER activity, which confirms the optimal anodic oxidation conditions. Moreover, FeNiSOH possesses a relatively small Tafel slope of 33.5 mV dec1, which is smaller than those of FeNiOH (63.3 mV dec1), NiSOH (86.2 mV dec1), NiOH (150.8 mV dec1), or IrO2 (67.7 mV dec1), demonstrating its rapid OER catalytic kinetics (Fig. 3c). Therefore, the FeNiSOH catalyst possesses outstanding OER catalytic activity, which also outperforms most reported electrocatalysts (Table S4, ESI).

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was further conducted to study the OER kinetics of the fabricated samples. As the Nyquist plots presented in Fig. 3d show, FeNiSOH possesses the smallest charge transfer resistance (Rct) of 0.98 , suggesting its excellent conductivity and outstanding interfacial electron transfer kinetics, which are beneficial for improving the OER efficiency. The electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) can be used to estimate the actual number of exposed active sites and intrinsic activity of the electrodes, which is estimated by the double-layer capacitance (Cdl) derived from the CV curves at different scan rates (Fig. S24, ESI).9 As illustrated in Fig. S25a (ESI), FeNiSOH possesses a larger Cdl of 2.48 mF cm2 than those of FeNiOH (1.88 mF cm2), NiSOH (1.80 mF cm2), or NiOH (1.43 mF cm2). This implies that FeNiSOH achieves the largest ECSA and exposes more active sites, which are beneficial for boosting the OER process. Furthermore, the OER LSV curves normalized by ECSA are shown in Fig. S25b (ESI). It was found that the FeNiSOH catalyst achieves the largest current densities at the same overpotentials, determining its highest intrinsic activity. In addition, the FeNiSOH catalyst exhibits the largest turnover frequency (TOF) value of 0.0630 s1 at an overpotential of 250 mV (Fig. S26, ESI), much higher than those of any other electrodes at the same or even higher overpotential (350 mV), indicating its highest intrinsic OER activity. Therefore, in comparison with the other catalysts, both the number of active sites increases and the intrinsic activity is improved for the FeNiSOH electrode. Additionally, as shown by the contact angle (CA) of the electrodes displayed in Fig. S27 (ESI), the FeNiSOH catalyst possesses a superhydrophilic surface with the smallest CA, which indicates that it has a strong adhesion force to the electrolyte, thus facilitating the adsorption and pyrolysis of reactants. The bubble releasing behavior of the FeNiSOH and FeNiOH catalysts was also investigated (Fig. S28, ESI), where bubbles adsorb on the surface of the FeNiOH catalyst due to its superaerophilicity (Video S1, ESI), while the FeNiSOH electrode exhibits weak adhesion to the bubbles, allowing the gaseous product to escape quickly (Video S2, ESI). These indicate that the FeNiSOH catalyst possesses superior mass transfer kinetics for facilitating the OER process, especially at large current density.43,44

We further evaluated the performance of the electrodes in artificial alkaline seawater (1 M KOH seawater). As the polarization curves of the four electrodes in Fig. 3e show, the FeNiSOH electrode still displays the best OER activity among these catalysts, with low overpotentials of 213, and 263 mV to drive the current densities of 10 and 100 mA cm2 (Fig. 3f), respectively. Moreover, FeNiSOH requires only a very low overpotential of 311 mV to deliver the commercially demanded current density of 500 mA cm2, which is well below the maximum overpotential of 480 mV required to trigger CER for forming hypochlorite. Therefore, an excellent OER selectivity can be realized for FeNiSOH in an H-type electrolytic cell with 1 M KOH seawater, as evidenced by the average oxygen faradaic efficiencies (FE) of 97.8% and 97.6% at current densities of 100 and 500 mA cm2, respectively (Fig. S29, ESI). The outstanding OER activity of the FeNiSOH electrode in alkaline seawater is much superior to those of most reported electrocatalysts for seawater oxidation (Table S5, ESI).

Long-term durability is another key index to assess the performance of an electrode in practical applications. The stability investigation of the FeNiSOH catalyst was operated at different constant current densities in alkaline water/seawater. As illustrated in Fig. S30 (ESI), the FeNiSOH catalyst exhibits excellent stability in 1 M KOH, almost without a decrease in OER activity at current densities of 10 and 100 mA cm2 during 100 h of operation. What is more, the FeNiSOH electrode exhibits excellent OER efficiency with an average O2 FE of 98.9% at a constant current density of 100 mA cm2 (Fig. S31, ESI). The SEM image shown in Fig. S32 (ESI) shows that the nanosheet structure of used FeNiSOH remains good after 100 h of OER operation. Its corresponding Raman spectrum exhibits (NiO) and (NiO) vibration modes of NiOOH at 474 and 553 cm1,45,46 and FeO vibration modes of FeOOH at 687 cm1, indicating the formation of the more active NiOOH in FeNiSOH during the OER process (Fig. S33, ESI). Very impressively, the FeNiSOH catalyst can work stably at the commercially demanded current density of 500 mA cm2, and there is only about a 40 mV increase for the required potential after 1100 h of continuous testing, in which the periodic small fluctuation of the curve is caused mainly by the temperature difference between day and night as well as water supplements (Fig. 3g). Although the OER activity of the FeNiSOH catalyst is not the best compared to published results, few catalysts have been reported to run stably for such a long time at commercial current densities (Table S4, ESI). Moreover, the FeNiSOH catalyst also possesses excellent stability at a constant current density of 100 mA cm2 in alkaline seawater (Fig. S34, ESI), almost without obviously increased overpotentials during 100 h of seawater oxidation, suggesting good resistance to chlorine corrosion. Besides, the nanosheet structure of the FeNiSOH electrode after the stability test remains good, where several new elements (such as Mg, Ca, Na, Cl, etc.) come from the natural seawater (Fig. S35, ESI). More importantly, the FeNiSOH catalyst can operate stably for over 900 hours at the commercially demanded current density of 500 mA cm2, with an increase in the overpotential of only 49 mV (Fig. 3g), indicating its superior stability and corrosion resistance in seawater. The Cl corrosion resistance abilities of the four electrodes were analyzed in-depth via corrosion polarization curves in natural seawater (Fig. S36, ESI), and the lowest corrosion current density for the FeNiSOH electrode suggests its highest resistance to Cl corrosion in seawater. In addition, almost no ClO was produced in the electrolytes after the stability test, as detected by the N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine (DPD) method (Fig. S37, ESI), suggesting its excellent OER selectivity. Therefore, the FeNiSOH electrode possesses highly efficient OER activity and excellent durability for alkaline seawater electrolysis at large current density, both of which outperform most reported electrodes (Table S5, ESI). Moreover, the FeNiSOH electrode still exhibits outstanding stability and OER selectivity in 1 M KOH seawater even when the potential exceeds the threshold potential for the ClO formation reaction (1.72 V vs. RHE),47 and can work stably for 80 hours at an extremely large current density of 2 A cm2 (Fig. S38, ESI), demonstrating a record excellent OER performance in alkaline seawater under large current densities.

Furthermore, a two-electrode electrolyzer for overall water splitting (Fig. S40a, ESI) was assembled in alkaline water/seawater via pairing the FeNiSOH electrode with a previously reported CoP nanorod arrays electrode (Fig. S39, ESI) with good HER activity.32 As expected, the FeNiSOH//CoP electrolyzer requires small cell voltages of 1.527, 1.618, and 1.703 V to deliver current densities of 10, 100, and 500 mA cm2, respectively, in 1 M KOH (Fig. S40b and c, ESI). Additionally, the electrolyzer also exhibits excellent overall-seawater-splitting activity with low cell voltages of 1.553, 1.674, and 1.808 V to drive current densities of 10, 100, and 500 mA cm2, respectively. Furthermore, the FeNiSOH//CoP electrolyzer displays excellent stability in alkaline water and seawater at constant current densities, with almost no decrease in catalytic activity (Fig. S40d, ESI), showing promising application prospects. After long-term overall water/seawater splitting at large current density, the smooth nanorod structure of the CoP electrode becomes rough, and the significantly reduced phosphorus content implies that metallic Co was formed on the surface of the CoP (Fig. S41, ESI), which may contribute to the high activity and stability of the electrolyzer.1 In addition, the signals of Ca and Mg elements can be detected in the CoP after overall seawater splitting, suggesting the Ca(OH)2 and Mg(OH)2 precipitations were deposited on the surface of the cathode.

It is worth noting that the OER activity evaluations determine the superiority of FeNiSOH among the Fe-involved catalysts in alkaline water/seawater. The redox electrochemistry of both catalysts was firstly conducted to investigate the structureactivity correlations. As the steady CV curves show in Fig. 4a, both catalysts display obvious oxidation peaks attributed to the Ni2+ Ni3/4+ oxidation process, along with the Ni(OH)2 NiOOH transformation.15,48 However, the FeNiSOH catalyst possesses extremely large oxidation peak areas in comparison with those of FeNiOH, implying their diverse reconstruction behaviors. Ex situ characterizations were conducted to study the FeNiSOH and FeNiOH catalysts after 20 CVs (denoted FeNiSOHCV and FeNiOHCV, respectively), and the CV curves are shown in Fig. S42 (ESI). As the Raman spectra show in Fig. 4b, FeNiSOHCV is completely converted into active NiOOH and FeOOH, indicating the time-saving and energy-efficient reconstruction behaviors. By contrast, only a new (NiO) vibration mode for NiOOH at about 550 cm1 shows up for FeNiOHCV besides the original vibration modes of Ni(OH)2, implying that only a small amount of Ni(OH)2 is converted to NiOOH, which is consistent with the CV curves. The formation of more NiOOH in the FeNiSOH catalyst is accompanied by the disappearance of S, which can be seen in the high-resolution XPS spectra for S 2p (Fig. 4c). There is no S 2p peak even after etching the surface layer via an argon ion beam for 25 s, suggesting the complete leaching of the S species from the FeNiSOH catalyst. Meanwhile, the Ni 2p XPS spectrum displays a positive shift of approximately 0.19 eV for FeNiSOHCV compared with those for FeNiOHCV, suggesting a higher valence state of FeNiSOHCV, in accordance with the formation of more NiOOH (Fig. 4d). As for Fe 2p, the FeNiSOHCV catalyst exhibits two spinorbit doublets of Fe3+ 2p3/2 and Fe3+ 2p1/2, while the FeOHCV catalyst exhibits two characteristic peaks of Fe2+ 2p3/2 and Fe2+ 2p1/2 at about 710.54 and 723.99 eV, respectively (Fig. 4e).4951 As for the XPS spectra of O 1s, FeNiSOHCV displays the greatly increased MO/MOH peak area ratio (SMO/SMOH) of 2.09 in comparison with the FeNiOHCV having that of 1.36, suggesting more transformation from hydroxides to oxyhydroxides in FeNiSOHCV due to S leaching (Fig. 4f). Moreover, there is a small number of lattice fringes with interplanar spacing of 0.209 nm in the HRTEM image of FeNiSOHCV (Fig. 4g), ascribed to the (105) plane of -NiOOH (PDF#6-75). The EDX results display the uniform distribution of Fe, Ni, O, and K for the FeNiSOHCV catalyst, and the negligible amount (an atomic ratio of about 0.08%) of the S element determines its complete leaching during in situ reconstruction. Therefore, these ex situ characterizations systematically demonstrate that in situ sulfur leaching promoted the conversion of the FeNiSOH catalyst to more OER-active species, namely -NiOOH, thus ensuring excellent OER activity.

To better gain insight into the reconstruction process of the catalysts and determine the real active intermediates, more in-depth structural characterizations of the catalysts were performed through in situ measurements. In situ Raman (Fig. 5a) was firstly carried out to reveal the real-time reconstruction of the electrodes during the OER process, collected from open circuit potential (OCP) to 1.515 V vs. RHE. As for the S-free FeNiOH (Fig. 5b), no noticeable reconstruction occurs when the potentials are below 1.365 V vs. RHE, where the original peak at 499 cm1 cannot be detected, mainly owing to the influence of NiOH vibration modes. When the potential reaches 1.415 V vs. RHE, the NiOH peak at 463 cm1 becomes weak and the peak at 499 cm1 can be observed. Additionally, a new peak appears at 553 cm1, ascribed to the (NiO) vibration modes of NiOOH,35 indicating the partial transformation from Ni(OH)2 to NiOOH. The intensity ratio of these two peaks at 553 and 463 cm1 (I553/463) was used to roughly determine the degree of phase transition (Table S6, ESI). The value of I553/463 remains about 0.60 at 1.515 V vs. RHE, suggesting the S-free FeNiOH was reconstructed into a complex of Ni(OH)2 and NiOOH, with the oxidation from Ni2+ to Ni23+. As for the FeNiSOH catalyst, when operated at OCP, there are two tiny characteristic peaks at about 471 and 527 cm1 attributed to the (NiO) of NiOOH and FeO vibration mode of FeOOH,52 respectively, suggesting rapid reconstruction behavior. The intensity of these two peaks gradually increases with the increase in potential until they are replaced at 1.365 V vs. RHE by two distinct characteristic peaks at approximately 474 and 556 cm1, corresponding to well-defined (NiO) and (NiO) vibration modes of NiOOH.46,53 This indicates the complete transformation of the Ni-species into NiOOH. The peak of the FeO vibration mode becomes invisible due to overlap with these two strong bands, which is consistent with previous work reported by Hu's group.52 Generally, -NiOOH exhibits a higher peak intensity ratio of (NiO) to that of (NiO) (denoted I/) compared with -NiOOH, due to its looser structure.54,55 Meanwhile, the average valency of Ni in -NiOOH is +3.6, which means the presence of highly oxidized Ni4+ species.56,57 It is worth noting that the value of I/ for the FeNiSOH catalyst is as high as 1.92.1 during the reconstruction process, suggesting the formation of -NiOOH with Ni4+ (Table S7, ESI). Moreover, the I/ value reduces slightly with the increase in applied potential originating from the structural defects and lattice disorder owing to incorporation of Fe.35,52,55 Therefore, it can be inferred that in situ S leaching induces the formation of highly oxidized Ni4+ in the FeNiSOH catalyst. Furthermore, the lower formation potential of NiOOH for the FeNiSOH catalyst reveals that S leaching decreases the reconstruction barrier and thus promotes complete reconstruction.

The in situ XAS of the two catalysts was detected at 1.5 V vs. RHE to reveal the valent states and coordination environments of the metals during OER. For the Ni K-edge XANES spectra (Fig. 5c), there is a positive shift of approximately 2.7 eV for the absorption edge of Ni XANES spectra in the FeNiSOH catalyst compared with that in the FeNiOH catalyst with Ni23+, which is consistent with previous reports of Ni2+ being oxidized to Ni34+.42,58 This shift implies the formation of highly oxidized Ni4+ in FeNiSOH during OER, in accordance with the in situ Raman results. Moreover, Fig. 5d illustrates the FT-EXAFS peaks of Ni with a shift to the lower R direction, indicating the formation of shorter NiO bonds in the FeNiSOH catalyst during the OER process. The fitting results exhibits an average NiO bond length of 1.87 for the FeNiSOH catalyst at 1.5 V vs. RHE (Fig. S43 and Table S2, ESI), assigned to Ni34+,59,60 while the average NiO bond length of 2.04 for the FeNiOH catalyst corresponds to Ni23+.56 Additionally, compared with the initial fresh FeNiSOH electrode, the NiS path disappears and the CN of the NiO path decreases slightly, demonstrating that S leaches and then vacancies are left during reconstruction process, which may lower the energy barrier for the formation of Ni4+. As for the Fe K-edge (Fig. 5e), both catalysts exhibit the absorption edge of Fe3+,60,61 indicating that the valence states of Fe-species in the two catalysts are unchanged in the OER process. Furthermore, the two catalysts have the same FeO paths at the first shell (Fig. 5f, Fig. S44 and Table S3, ESI), in agreement with that of FeOOH in the freshly prepared catalysts. It is worth noting that FeNiOHCV contains only Fe2+ species based on the XPS results, from which it can be inferred that the Fe-species in FeNiOHCV is reversible, and Fe2+ in the resting state is oxidized to Fe3+ before OER. This can be proved from the Raman spectra of the CV-activated catalysts after 24 h of exposure to air, where FeNiOHCV transforms back to the initial phase, suggesting the instability of its reconstructed active phase (Fig. S45, ESI). In contrast, FeNiSOHCV possesses stable reconstituted active species with unchanged characteristic peaks. Therefore, all the ex/in situ characterizations reveal that in situ S leaching accelerates the self-reconstruction process and induces the formation of highly oxidized Ni4+. With that in mind, the dynamically generated Ni4+ species play a decisive role in OER activity, and can be considered as real active sites in this system.

Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were carried out to investigate the role of S leaching during activation on generating highly oxidized Ni4+ species. Ni(OH)2, S-doped Ni(OH)2 [SNi(OH)2], and S-doped Ni(OH)2/FeOOH [SNi(OH)2/FeOOH] were optimized as original models (Fig. S46, ESI), and S atoms were further removed to form S-leached (SL) catalysts (Fig. S47, ESI). Fig. 6a presents the free energy diagram from Ni2+ to Ni34+ for Ni species in the catalysts (Fig. S48, ESI) during self-reconstruction. The transformation from Ni(OH)2 to NiOOH requires energy of 1.24 eV, while lower energy is required for Ni2+ in SL SNi(OH)2 (1.16 eV) and SL SNi(OH)2/FeOOH (1.11 eV) to be oxidized to Ni3+. Very impressively, SL SNi(OH)2 and SL SNi(OH)2/FeOOH need only 0.76 and 0.69 eV, respectively to form highly oxidized Ni4+, much lower than that of Ni(OH)2 (1.16 eV). This illustrates that S leaching can accelerate the conversion of Ni(OH)2 to NiOOH and greatly reduce the formation energy of Ni4+. Besides, the incorporation of Fe can further optimize both processes, in line with the experimental results. Therefore, the FeNiSOH catalyst can easily generate more active Ni4+ species during self-reconstruction benefitting from the favorable effect of sulfur leaching, thus achieving excellent catalytic activity. In general, compared with Ni3+, the higher oxidized Ni4+ 3d band easily embeds more deeply in the O 2p band due to the stronger electron transfer from metal to oxygen atoms, resulting in the rise of the O 2p band to increase unoccupied electronic states and generate ligand oxygen holes (Fig. 6b).62,63 This phenomenon can also be observed in this catalyst system from the projected density of states (PDOS) of SL NiOOH/FeOOH during deprotonation (corresponding to Ni3+, Ni34+, Ni4+, respectively) (Fig. S49, ESI). As the amount of highly oxidized Ni4+ increases, the metal d-band center (d) gets further and further away from the Fermi level, while the p-band center gradually shifts to higher energy (Fig. 6c and Table S8, ESI). Additionally, the proportion of O 2p component (the ratio of the state number of O 2p to total orbital) apparently increases, especially above the Fermi energy, suggesting increased unoccupied electronic states and the formation of ligand oxygen holes. These active oxygen species can be experimentally detected by in situ Raman spectra. A broad vibration mode between 850 and 1150 cm1 begins to appear at 1.36 V vs. RHE with the formation of Ni4+ for FeNiSOH, corresponding to active oxygen species (NiOO),64,65 but it does not show up for FeNiOH (Fig. S50, ESI). These activated lattice oxygen ions can trigger the direct OO coupling of LOM that overcomes the thermodynamic limitations of the conventional adsorbate evolution mechanism (AEM), thereby enhancing OER activity.66,67 To further verify whether the lattice oxygen of the catalysts participates in the OER cycling process, 18O isotope-labelled experiments were carried out.6870In situ Raman spectra in Fig. S51a and b (ESI) exhibit that the 18ONiOOH peaks of 18O-labelled FeNiSOH immediately shift back to the position of 16ONiOOH within 1 min, while that of the 18O-labelled FeNiOH shows only a small shift after 20 min. Moreover, the mass spectrometry (MS) results in Fig. S51c (ESI) reveal that the 18O-labelled FeNiSOH can generate 18O-labelled products (16O18O), of which only a very small amount is detected in the 18O-labelled FeNiOH. These straightforward pieces of evidence strongly confirm the LOM in the OER cycling for the FeNiSOH electrode. It should be noted that, although O-isotope exchange can also be observed in the FeNiOH electrode, the extremely weak phenomenon suggests that it mainly follows the AEM pathway rather than the LOM.

Combining all the experimental and theoretical results, the excellent water/seawater oxidation performance of the FeNiSOH electrode can be ascribed to the following features. (1) The nickel foam substrate provides a Ni source to directly generate the active materials, and their tight connection endows the FeNiSOH catalyst with excellent electron transport and corrosion resistance capacity, resulting in its long-term operation in alkaline water/seawater. (2) Most importantly, in situ S leaching from the FeNiSOH electrode boosts its self-reconstruction process, and leads to more ready generation of highly active Ni4+ species due to the reduced formation energy, thus achieving excellent intrinsic OER activity. (3) FeNiSOH possesses a large surface area for exposing a massive number of active sites as well as a superhydrophilic surface and excellent gas release behavior for facilitating mass transfer, both of which are significant for efficient and stable water/seawater oxidation under high current densities.

Conclusions

In summary, this work develops a unique two-step oxidation strategy to fabricate ultrathin FeNiSOH nanosheet arrays grown in situ on Ni foam. Systematic insights reveal that the electrode possesses a large surface area for exposing a massive number of active sites, a superhydrophilic surface and excellent gas release behavior for facilitating mass transfer, a tight connection with the Ni foam substrate for excellent corrosion resistance, and more-readily formed Ni4+ species due to in situ S leaching for enhanced intrinsic OER activity. Therefore, the FeNiSOH catalyst shows outstanding OER activity and robust durability in alkaline water/seawater, operating stably over long periods of time at the commercially required current density of 500 mA cm2. This work provides an efficient strategy for constructing excellent OER catalysts rapidly with more readily formed highly oxidized metal species, which will significantly promote the development of alkaline water/seawater splitting for commercial applications.
