Abstract

This work shows a detailed study of the synthesis, structural characterization, electrical, thermal, and mechanical luminescence behavior of a one-dimensional coordination polymer formed by a Cu(I)â€“I double chain, using 2-amino-4 chloropyrimidine as a ligand. The asymmetric structure of double chain type [Cu(L)I]n, (L = 2-amino-4-chloropyrimidine (aCLpym)) with Cuâ€“Cu distances shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of two copper atoms, and the substantial flexibility of this structure are crucial to understanding its optoelectronic behavior. The electrical conductivity values at 295 K are in agreement with possible semiconductor behavior. Additionally, the luminescence properties of this coordination polymer (CP) were characterized under ambient conditions (ambient pressure (AP) and 295 K) by a non-symmetric high energy (HE) band centered at 465 nm, tentatively assigned to a mixed 3(I + M)LCT. The intensity of the emission increases upon lowering the temperature. A structural contraction can explain this behaviour, clearly observed when solving the structures employing single-crystal X-ray diffraction of the compound at 296 K and 110 K. Strong mechanochromic behavior was observed when hydrostatic pressure was applied, with the appearance of an additional low energy (LE) band at around 650 nm, which for this type of CuI CP is typically associated with transitions in the CuI cluster-core, involving 3IMCT and cluster centered 3d10 â†’ 3d94s1 (3MCC) states. The latter is due to the shortening under pressure of the Cuâ€“Cu distances below the van der Waals limit. Moreover, the study of the mechanochromic behavior versus pressure exerted by grinding shows a decrease in the intensity of the emission due to the increase in the number of defects in the material and a decrease in its particle size.

Introduction

Copper(I)â€“iodine coordination polymers (CPs) with ligands derived from pyrimidines and pyrazines have been studied from different points of view.1,2 Indeed, pyrimidines are an important class of heterocyclic structures found in many synthetic and naturally occurring products with a remarkable spectrum of biological activities by themselves or in combination with metal ions.3 These primary amines with an electron-withdrawing group, such as 2-amino-4 chloropyrimidine, are ligands that can stabilize the Cu(I) oxidation state. In addition, they present the possibility of molecular recognition by forming hydrogen bonds, thanks to the presence of the amino group.4

Structurally, Cu(I)â€“I CPs can present different dimensionality depending on the chosen pyrimidine ligands.5 Their chains can form various topologies, with those with a Cu(I)â€“I double chain (1D-staircase ribbon) being the most common.6â€“8 Most of them have luminescence properties and behave as stimulus-responsive or intelligent materials.2,6,9 Indeed, they can change their properties in response to temperature, pressure, and the presence of gases, among other stimuli.4 An attractive characteristic of these CPs is their partial solubility in some organic solvents such as acetonitrile and the reorganization of the building blocks to the pristine CP through the slow evaporation of the said solvent.2 This unusual characteristic allows fine crystals to be obtained from the solution, useful for single-crystal X-ray diffraction. This technique greatly facilitates the comprehension of their behavior when exposed to external stimuli. Their Cu(I)â€“I chains are extremely sensitive, behaving like springs, resulting in a shortening in the Cuâ€“Cu and Cuâ€“I distances and the Cuâ€“Iâ€“Cu angles when the CPs are subjected to low temperature or to high pressures.10

The thermal stability of these types of CPs, their synthesis in a single step,11,12 the low cost of the ligands, and their interesting optical properties, make these compounds hold much interest in different areas and promising for technological applications, such as data recording devices and motion or stress sensors.13 Moreover, recent studies have shown that these types of CPs can be processed into controlled thickness composite films, improving their mechanical properties and maintaining their optical properties, thus expanding the possibility of creating new optical devices,5 such as14 light converting agents to increase quantum efficiency in solar cells.15 Despite interest in these compounds, research towards understanding the behavior versus stimuli such as pressure is still scarce. Indeed, very few studies have applied hydrostatic pressure and solved the crystal structures of these materials under different pressures via single-crystal X-ray diffractometry.16 Furthermore, although the mechanoluminescence studies of copper iodide clusters by grinding are commonplace, they are primarily associated with a crystalline-to-amorphous conversion.17,18 However, the examples in which crystallinity is maintained are still few.2

This work shows a detailed study of the synthesis, characterization, electrical, thermal, and mechanical luminescence behavior of a 1D coordination polymer formed from a Cu(I)â€“I double chain, using 2-amino-4 chloropyrimidine as a ligand. Its partial solubility in acetonitrile allows single crystals to be obtained to allow the solving of its structure by single-crystal X-ray diffractometry, as well as an understanding of its luminescence behavior versus temperature and pressure variations when solving the structure at different temperatures and pressures. This work supports the reversible behavior of this CP against temperature and pressure, except for the conductivity of the material subjected to grinding. In this case, the study of its morphological transformation corroborates its nano processing, the decrease in grain size, and the increase in the number of defects that explain its luminescence behaviour.

Photophysical properties

Photophysical properties of CPs based on d10 metal centers are of current interest. Mainly those CPs containing Cu(I) as building blocks may show bright luminescence emission, varying from blue to red light, and therefore, they have attracted recent attention towards potential applications.28â€“34

To investigate the fluorescence thermochromism properties of the compound, we first recorded the photoluminescence behavior of the sample under a UV lamp at 300 K and 80 K. As shown in Fig. 2, CP1 emits blue light at RT and green-blue light at 80 K (LT). The compound exhibits fluorescence thermochromism properties, and this thermochromic behavior is fully reversible.


Fig. 2 Emission spectrum (Î»exc = 390 nm) at different temperatures in solid-state 1D-[Cu(aClpym)I]n (CP1), from room temperature (RT, 300 K) (light blue) to liquid nitrogen temperature (LNT, 80 K) (red).

To evaluate these seminal experimental observations in more detail, emission spectra of CP1 at variable temperature were recorded (Fig. 2). Upon excitation at 390 nm, by lowering the temperature of CP1 from 300 to 80 K, it exhibits a very weak broadband at 468 nm at RT, which increases in intensity and shifts to 480 nm upon lowering the temperature to 80 K (Fig. 2) (Î” = 12 nm at 80 K). The compound exhibits broad emission bands with barely any band shifting, suggesting that the emission could be derived from a charge-transfer excited state. The origins of the luminescence emissions could be assigned to metal-to ligand charge transfer (MLCT), iodide-to-ligand charge transfer (ILCT), iodide-to metal charge transfer (IMCT), and cluster-centered (CC*) transition, or an admixture of more than one of the above.35â€“39

The 1D chain polymeric stair-type chain structure adopted by CP1 is typical for Iâ€“Cuâ€“N donor ligands coordination polymers, and the emission origin, centered at around 500â€“630 nm, might be derived from an excited state 3ILCT*, and at shorter wavelengths (like in the CP1 case, which appeared at 468 nm) when they are mixed with copper cluster-centered 3d10 â†’ 3d94s1 (3MCC) or IMLCT. The contribution of the 3MCC transition in the excited state is enhanced when the Cuâ€“Cu distances are shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radius (2.80 Ã…). In this case, the structure adopted by CP1 contains an asymmetrical Cu2I2 rhombic core with one Cuâ‹¯Cu distance less than 2.8 Ã… and another longer (Table S2â€ ). This suggests a higher contribution of the excited state of dâ€“s character (3MCC), shifting the emission wavelength to a higher value. Additionally, as explained later in the mechanoluminescence studies, the emission spectra versus pressure allow us to deduce that the band observed under ambient conditions may arise from a 3(I + Cu)LCT combination, excluding the contributions that arise from the transitions in the CuI cluster.6,30,40â€“44 On the other hand, this emission corresponds to a so-called high energy (HE) band. An additional low energy (LE) band appears to be associated with the transitions in the cluster, that is, IMCT and MCC. The increase in the intensity of the emission observed when the temperature decreases (Fig. 2) can be explained by a structural contraction. This is observed when solving the structures employing single-crystal X-ray diffraction of the compound at 296 K and 110 K (Fig. 1, Tables S1â€“S3â€ ) both in the Cuâ€“Cu and Cuâ€“I distance, as in the angles and in the supramolecular interactions through hydrogen bonds (Table S3â€ ).

Previous studies have shown that the emission energies of Cu(I)â€“iodide complexes are strongly affected by the presence of heterocyclic organic ligands linked to the metal center of the [CunIn] chains, mainly due to structural changes induced by the presence of terminal ligands and/or their supramolecular interactions.45â€“47 Therefore, we decided to compare the solid-state luminescence behavior of CP1, with coordination polymers based on Cu2I2 chains bridged by pyrimidine N-heteroaromatic ligands containing different auxiliary ligands in the 2,4 positions of the ring, such as one amino and one chloride group, respectively (Table S5â€ ). Moreover, to evaluate the possible presence of emission arising from a ligand-centered (LC) transition, the free ligand 2-amino-4-chloropyrimidine was also investigated at 300 and 80 K (Fig. S8â€ ). The 2-amino-4-chloropyrimidine exhibits two clear bands centered at 452 and 500 nm (Î»ex = 356 nm), which increase in intensity when the temperature decreases. These emissions correspond to ligand-centered Ï€ â†’ Ï€* transitions, and we can observe that the emission is affected by the nature of the substituent on the pyrimidine ring. As described previously, the electronic structure of the aromatic ring can significantly impact the emission. In this case, the weak emission of CP1 can be attributed to the aromatic ring of one amino and one chlorine group in the 2,4 positions giving rise to lower intermolecular packing.

The temporal luminescence decay of the emission band of CP1 was recorded at 300 and 80 K under excitation at 355 nm using the third harmonic of a pulsed Nd:YAG laser (Fig. S10,â€  red and blue circles, respectively). As can be seen, the decays were complex (no single exponential) at both temperatures, and they can be well fitted using a double exponential function (Fig. S10,â€  black lines). The different parameters associated with the fitting functions are included in the supplementary information (Table S6â€ ) together with the average decay time <Ï„> (as indicated at the bottom of Table S6â€ ). The average decay time of CP1 at 80 K gives a value of 0.11 Î¼s, while at 300 K exhibits a value of 0.03 Î¼s. This reduction could be attributed to multiphonon relaxation, which may be induced by changes in the bond lengths and angles. The lifetime decrease is also according to the reduction observed in the emission band intensity when the temperature increases (see Fig. 2). In the range of microseconds, the average decay time is the typical range assigned to phosphorescence emission, indicating that this emission band arises from a triplet state.

Solid-state emission depends on the molecular structure, so mechanical stresses alter the arrangement of the inter/intramolecular interactions and modify the Cuâ€“I chains according to the temperature. To study the mechanoluminescence behavior, high-pressure hydrostatic emission was measured for compound CP1, as a microcrystalline powder and using a single crystal. In addition, the behavior of CP1 upon being ground was studied.

The hydrostatic high-pressure technique is a powerful tool that allows the study of the luminescence properties of a system, by continuously varying the different parameters involved, as the bond distances and angles are closely related to the luminescence properties. For CP1, pressure-induced changes in the emission are a clear example of mechanochromic effects.

The pressure experiment was carried out under ambient conditions (AP 300 K), with excitation at 375 nm, where CP1 shows an asymmetric broadband centered approximately at 465 nm (blue). Compressing the sample, drastic changes, even at very low pressure (0.19 GPa), were observed, with an additional band emerging at longer wavelengths with an intensity more significant than the previous one (Fig. 3). These data resemble those observed for other CuI coordination polymers and are described considering two well differentiated bands coined in the literature as HE and LE bands.16,48 At around 3.0 GPa, a third band starts appearing at 445 nm. The evolution of these bands with pressure was analyzed.

Regarding the HE and LE bands, both show similar behavior, with non-linear red-shifts (bathochromic) with pressure, where the maximum variation takes place between 1.0 and 4.0 GPa. Tentative linear fitting was carried out, to give average slopes of âˆ’452 cmâˆ’1 GPaâˆ’1 and âˆ’323 cmâˆ’1 GPaâˆ’1 for the HE and LE bands, respectively. At pressures beyond 4.0 GPa, the shifts are less pronounced and seem to be stabilized (Fig. 4a). On the other hand, no pressure shift for the third band was observed. The intensity decreased by approximately 85% from AP to 6.7 GPa (Fig. 4b), and the same as with the band-shifts, beyond 4.0 GPa some stabilization of the intensity was observed. During the experiment, the relative intensities of the HE and LE bands remained unchanged.

Upon decompressing the sample, the positions of the bands, as well as the total intensity, were almost recovered. However, the relative intensities of the HE and LE bands differ, to the point that at AP the LE band remains, contrary to what happens at the beginning of the experiment.

The AP experimental results show the presence of the HE band alone, which is usually ascribed to a combination of (I + M)LCT bands, and the averaged lifetime of 0.11 Î¼s obtained at 80 K, suggests a phosphorescence mechanism between spin-forbidden tripletâ€“singlet transitions (T1 â†’ S0) with the 3(I + M)LCT transitions. However, the short lifetime obtained for this emission at 300 K in the order of 0.03 Î¼s indicates an abrupt temperature-dependent rise of the non-radiative mechanisms (knr). The non-exponential character of the decay curve (Fig. S10 and Table S6â€ ) supports the presence of more than one emitter state. At shallow pressures (0.18 GPa), another band at a longer wavelength (LE) was also activated, being usually ascribed to transitions within the CuI cluster-core, and considered as a combination of iodide-to-metal (iodide-to-copper) 3IMCT and copper-centered 3d10 â†’ 3d94s1 (3MCC) transitions, with the latter only observed under some Cuâ€“Cu distance requirements (<2.8 Ã…), that is less than the double of the van der Waals radii, established for the Cu atoms as 1.4 Ã….49

In these cases, the interaction between pairs of Cu atoms (cuprophilic interactions) can be considered as non-negligible. From room temperature single-crystal X-ray measurements on CP1, two distinct Cuâ€“Cu pair distances were obtained (Fig. 1b), with values under ambient conditions of 2.695 Ã… (shorter one) and 3.361 Ã… (longer one). Therefore, only the Cu pairs at shorter distances are expected to contribute towards the LE emission. On the other hand, as can be deduced from the variation of the cell parameters and the unit cell volume with pressure, CP1 is highly compressible (Fig. S12â€ ), giving rise to significant shortenings of the Cuâ€“Cu distances, of around 6% and 18% for the shorter and larger ones at 7.0 GPa, respectively. Still, despite these shortenings, they are hard enough to activate LE emission from the larger distance Cuâ€“Cu pairs since the threshold value is reached at more than 6 GPa (2.756 Ã… at 7 GPa). Density functional theory (DFT) calculations for other similar complexes show the Cuâ€“Cu bonding character of the excited state. As the pressure increases, the Cuâ€“Cu distances become shorter, increasing the bonding character and then lowering the energy level and consequently inducing a red-shift in the emission bands.50,51

As can be observed from Fig. S13â€  the variations in these distances are not linear, in accordance with the non-linear red-shifting observed for the emission bands. Focusing on the emission from the shorter pairs, the slope is higher from ambient to 4.0 GPa (Fig. 3), and then tends to stabilize for pressures higher than 4.0 GPa. The same behavior is observed for the integrated intensity of the emission (see Fig. 4b), where the drop in intensity with pressure is again greater in the 0.0 to 4.0 GPa range, where both effects can be correlated with changes in the structure, more evident in this pressure range, which enhances the non-radiative mechanism, knr.

The multicomponent origin of the emission observed upon applying pressure and the presence of a third band, which emerges at around 450 nm under approximately 4.0 GPa, may indicate that the two different components, 3(I + M)LCT, proposed for the HE band, exhibit different pressure behavior, one highly dependent on the conditions, which rapidly red-shifts, the other independent. This fact separates both contributions, giving rise to two different HE bands.

Concerning this study, single-crystal X-ray diffraction on a single crystal of CP1 was carried out to analyze structural changes under hydrostatic pressure. Thus, a crystal of CP1 was introduced into a cell with a liquid medium and was subjected to a gradual increase in pressure from 0 to 9 GPa. From the behavior of cell parameters with pressure, only a small change can be observed in the b-axis at around 2 GPa but without any discontinuity in the volume curve. Therefore, no evidence allows us to conclude a phase transition in this compound (Fig. S12â€ ). The main changes arise from an important deformation of the Cuâ€“I ladders gliding from one side to the other and a moderate reorientation of the pyrimidine rings, where we can observe an asymmetric behavior in the Cuâ‹¯Cu diagonal interactions (Fig. S13â€ ). The increase in pressure causes a contraction of the structure. In particular, the hydrogen bond holding the Cuâ€“I chains together becomes more important as the pressure increases, going from 2.196 Ã… at 0 GPa to 1.960 Ã… at 9 GPa. The distance between the pyrimidine rings and their respective slippage distances are shown in Fig. S14.â€  The volume of the tetrahedral structure formed with Cu as the central atom and the nitrogen and iodine atoms (N1, I1 I1ii, and I1iii) is also affected, as shown in Fig. S15.â€ 

The study of the mechanoluminescence behavior of CP1 upon being ground shows different behavior. In general, the grinding of CPs results in the compounds being crushed, and the generation of granules and defects, where the compounds undergo variations both in the intensity of the emission and in the wavelength peak.52 In this study, CP1 shows an emission intensity that decreases in line with the grinding time (Fig. 5).

Conclusions

This work shows the synthesis in a single step and characterization of the monodimensional double-chain CP 1D-[Cu(aClpym)I]n (CP1), obtained via direct synthesis between CuI and 2-amino-4-chloropyrimidine. This luminescent compound also exhibits possible semiconductor behavior, being thermochromic and reversible fluorescent.

The combination of the emission experiments versus temperature and pressure allowed us to have a more precise idea of the contributions of the different states in the final emission of the compound. The decrease in temperature produces a structural contraction that causes a shortening in distances and angles (Cuâ€“Cu, Cuâ€“Iâ€¦), generating an increase in emission due to an increase in cuprophilia. This response is reversible, showing the great flexibility of this type of structure. In addition, the pressure exerted hydrostatically does not cause phase transitions, shortens the Cuâ€“Cu distances, increases the binding character, decreases the energy level, induces red-shifting, and increases the emission of the compound. However, the pressure exerted by grinding results in a reduction in emission due to the decrease in the grain size and an increase in the number of defects with hardly any loss in crystallinity or structural transformation. Multi-response materials can act as sensors in industries as diverse as food, packaging, or aerospace, and these studies have great relevance for the design and crystal engineering of new Cu(I)â€“I coordination polymers with better and more interesting optical properties, which may be helpful in the creation of new optoelectronic devices with industrial utility.
