Green synthesis of fusiform-shaped Fe-PANI as a phenomenal dual electrocatalyst for overall water splitting and assessment of its sustainability standards

Bakthavachalam Vishnu , Sundarraj Sriram and Jayaraman Jayabharathi *
Department of Chemistry, Material Science Lab, Annamalai University, Annamalainagar, Tamilnadu 608002, India. E-mail: jjayabharathi2022@gmail.com

Received 30th December 2022 , Accepted 23rd February 2023

First published on 23rd February 2023

Abstract

Development of eco-friendly dual electrocatalysts for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) is gaining importance for renewable energy generation. In this study, green-synthesized Fe-PANI is reported as an efficient bifunctional electrocatalyst for the HER and OER processes. Fe-PANI/NF exhibits a lower overpotential () of 261 mV (OER) and 155 mV (HER) at 10 mA cm2 than commercial IrO2 (341 mV) and near to that of Pt (36 mV), and a smaller Tafel slope (OER: 100 mV dec1/IrO2 125 mV dec1; HER: 120 mV dec1/Pt 74 mV dec1). The turnover frequency (TOF) of Fe-PANI (OER: 0.0673 s1/IrO2 0.0179 s1; HER: 0.022 s1/Pt 0.10 s1) was found to be higher than that of IrO2 and near to that of Pt. For solar water electrolysis, Fe-PANI/NF exhibits overpotential of 411 mV and a durability over 100 h with 4.1% potential loss (1 M KOH). As a non-precious electrolyzer, Fe-PANI combined with a solar cell produces H2 gas under sunlight. This methodology suggests that the green-synthesized Fe-PANI electrolyzer outperforms precious electrocatalysts. Hence, cost-effective large-scale H2 production without an artificial current may be possible with a commercial solar cell. Furthermore, the environmental impact of Fe-PANI was evaluated by examining the significant sustainability metrics and socio-economic parameters.

1. Introduction

The increasing population has led to high energy consumption globally with the diminishing of traditional fossil fuels and severe pollution issues.1 Academic communities and industrialists have been investigating supplementary power sources, such as hydropower, wind energy, nuclear fusion and solar irradiation.13 Besides the huge potential for energy manufactured from renewable resources, the utility of both intermittent as well as non-surplus energy sources for large scale applications is hindered. Recent mileposts in the development of hydrogen (H2) production, such as power plants, fuel cells, metalair batteries, etc., have validated it as a promising conversion technology, as well as confirming H2 to be a sustainable, clean, eco-friendly and renewable energy carrier.4 Electrocatalytic water-splitting is a potential alternative for ultrapure hydrogen production. The HER, OER, hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) are at the heart of these technologies for the conversion of chemical to electrical energy. The electrolyzer consists of two electrodes with the OER taking place at the anode and the HER at the cathode.5 The polarization curves of these electrodes consist of equilibrium points of [(0 V): (H2/H+:H2 2H+ + 2e: HOR/HER) and 1.23 V (H2O/O2: 2H2O O2 + 4H+ + 4e: OER/ORR) Vs. RHE]. Although IrO2/RuO2 (OER) and Pt (HER) catalysts are benchmark electrocatalysts, their high costs and instability hinder their use in large-scale applications.4,6 Therefore, an alternative strategy has become urgently needed to achieve large-scale water splitting. The development of non-precious metal-based, low-cost, efficient HER and OER electrocatalysts has been widely studied but still remains a challenge.

Due to its unique -conjugated structure, which is beneficial for electrochemical stability and conductivity, polyaniline (PANI) has attracted great attention as a material for energy storage and conversion.7,8 The nitrogen with a lone electron pair of PANI provides coordination sites to anchor 3d transition metals (TMs), with a TM-N coordinate bond forming owing to strong electron transfer ability and resulting in enhanced conductivity and high stability in a harsh alkali environment.9,10 However, the oxidative polymerization synthetic methodology of PANI produces eco-unfriendly by-products and the formed PANI lacks nanostructure consistency.11 To solve these issues, there is an urgent need to fabricate low-cost, efficient PANI-based composite electrocatalysts for water splitting. Mechanochemical synthesis is the process of initiating a chemical reaction by grinding solid reagents made up of either inorganic or organic materials without the use of solvents, making it an environmentally friendly method.12,13 Due to not requiring solvents, synthesis achieved by grinding (mortar and pestle) has gained increasing attention in the field of green chemistry. This technique does not require advanced laboratory equipment and can be performed in any laboratory.14

Herein, we report Fe/PANI obtained from green-synthesized PANI, which anchors Fe2+ ions using its abundant nitrogen sites. The high-valence Fe centers become easily assembled into the PANI network to form a Fe-PANI spindle-like morphology. The green synthetic route endows Fe/PANI with significant bifunctional activity and stability. Fe-PANI shows excellent activity, with an overpotential of 311 mV (OER) and 100 mV (HER) at 10 mA cm2. We fabricated a two-electrode electrolyzer (Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF) that exhibits water splitting at 10 mA cm2 for 100 h. Also, solar-to-hydrogen (STH) production at 1.64 V in a full-cell set up was demonstrated. The robust bifunctional Fe-PANI spindle electrocatalyst shows outstanding results, with long-term stability.

2. Experimental

Synthesis of Fe-PANI was carried out by hand grinding using a pestle and mortar (Fig. 1).15 For polymerization of aniline under solvent free conditions, FeCl36H2O was employed as both oxidant and dopant.16 About 1.0 mL of aniline and 1 g of FeCl36H2O were subjected to grinding for 5 min and a black colored powder was formed. Further grinding of the black powder led to slurry formation, which turned to a green color after grinding for 10 min. These observations implied the formation of polyaniline as its doped emeraldine salt (ES) together with iron. The reaction mixture was further subjected to 60 min of grinding followed by aging (12 h) at room temperature. The formed Fe-PANI was purified by washing with water, filtering and drying at room temperature (Fig. S1, ESI).
Fig. 1 Schematic diagram of the green synthesis of Fe-PANI.

Reasonably priced FeCl3 was used as an oxidant to polymerize aniline as well as an iron dopant source (Fe-bonded to the functional group of PANI). The sources of carbon, nitrogen and iron in Fe-PANI are from the precursors only without the need for any additional chemical supports. Only grinding changed the polymer spindle into an interconnected carbon network framework via the formation of NFe bonds. Fe is a known conductor with abundant active sites to enhance catalytic activity. The PANI acts as a synergistic stimulant for electrocatalytic activity due to fast electron transport, hence Fe-PANI is a robust electrocatalyst for the hydrogen and oxygen evolution reactions. The distinctive spindle structure of Fe-PANI enhances the active sites by providing a high electrochemical surface area. Its unique morphology increases conductivity and stability, which lead to enhanced activity.

3.1. Activity and stability of Fe-PANI

Electrocatalytic studies on conducting polymers have rarely reported on how the fast electron transfer, excellent conductivity, and heteroatoms (N) in the conducting polymer may promote the OER and HER activity. Thus, the NC bond in the Fe-PANI can act as an electroactive center for the OER and HER process. Since the lone pair of the N atom of PANI serves as a coordination site to hold 3d transition metal ions, the NFe coordination bonds afford abundant electrocatalytic active centers and promote fast electron transfer that results in high performance.33

Motivated by the aforementioned findings and the green synthetic route that leads to the expected composition of Fe-PANI, the OER activity of Fe-PANI/NF was investigated in a three-electrode device (1.0 M KOH).32 The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curve of Fe-PANI/NF shows a lower overpotential of 261 mV (at 10 mA cm2) than a commercial IrO2/NF catalyst (341 mV) and bare NF (401 mV) (Fig. 10). The comparative efficiency of Fe-PANI with recently reported non-noble bifunctional electrocatalysts was confirmed (Table S3, ESI). The Tafel slope of Fe-PANI/NF (100 mV dec1) was found to be lower than those of IrO2/NF (125 mV dec1) and NF (181 mV dec1) (Fig. 10). The low overpotential and small Tafel slope of Fe-PANI/NF reflect its excellent catalytic activity. At 1.60 V, the calculated TOFs of NF, IrO2/NF and Fe-PANI/NF are 0.0076 s1, 0.0179 s1 and 0.0673 s1, respectively. Electrochemical stability is a significant issue for excellent OER catalysts because of the constant gas evolution that occurs during the electrochemical process. The potential change and surface morphology of Fe-PANI/NF were examined via durability testing by plotting a currenttime (It) curve for 100 h (Fig. 10). After 100 h of electrolysis with constant gas evolution, the It curve exhibits significant stability with a 2.0% loss in potential.


Fig. 10 OER polarization curves (no iR correction) of Fe-PANI/NF in 1.0 M KOH; (a) LSV curves of Fe-PANI/NF, IrO2 and NF; (b) Tafel slope and (c) chronopotentiometric durability testing of Fe-PANI/NF.

The electrochemical study of Fe-PANI/NF reveals that iron concentration and Fe(OH)x formation [M(II)/M(III): (MFe)] during the OER process enhance the activity. The Fe-PANI/NF OER reaction route is as follows (Fig. 11):32,42 OH+ * OH* + e; OH + OH* O* + H2O + e; OH + O* OOH* + e; OH + OOH* O2 + H2O + e; where * indicates active Fe-PANI sites and metal centers with open sites, which are well known to be advantageous to the electrocatalytic process. The higher %N of PANI in Fe-PANI (NFe) improved the iron binding energy with PANI and Fe-PANI/NF stability during the OER process, The NFe coordinate bonds offered abundant electrocatalytic active centers for the OER, as confirmed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) data.33 During the OER process, the formation of Fe3+/Fe2+ (Fe(OH)2/FeOOH) enhanced the oxygen evolution, which is consistent with post-OER XRD analyses.43 Also, Fe had the potential to improve the OER process by increasing the electrocatalytic activity at active centers. At 1.39 V, an anodic peak was observed where the interaction of Fe species in the Fe-PANI aided the oxidation of Fe0 to Fe3+.44 The Fe-PANI/NF potential activity indicated that 1.0 M KOH was in close contact with the active regions of the electrocatalyst surface.

The post-OER XRD analysis of the Fe-PANI/NF electrode showed that Fe-PANI has a degree of crystallinity (Fig. 12). The diffraction peaks of Fe-PANI phase were almost invisible but several new broad peaks were observed in the 1040 region due to scattering from the PANI chains. The peaks at 40.1(210), 41.6(140), 43.2(220), 47.2(041), 48.0(230), 49.7(150), 50.5(211), 54.7(240) and 55.9(060) were indexed to FeO(OH) (JCPDS: 81-0464). Fig. 12 depicts the post-OER IR spectra of Fe-PANI/NF, wherein the peaks at 1487 and 1354 cm1 were attributed to the CN (quinonoid) and CC (benzenoid) stretching vibrations of PANI, respectively.45 The distinct peaks at 1160 cm1, 750 cm1, 3305 cm1 and 1056 cm1 were attributed to CN, CH, NH and CH stretching vibrations, respectively.4649 The bands at 678 cm1 and 3456 cm1 confirmed the presence of FeO and OH in the polymer composite.50,51 The post-OER IR spectrum of Fe-PANI was found to be nearly identical to the pre Fe-PANI spectrum, with a slight shifting of bands. The intensity ratio of the quinonoid to benzenoid band changed due to the * stacking and electrostatic interactions between the Fe ion and PANI in Fe-PANI. Post-OER IR spectrum analyses suggested that Fe-PANI underwent a slight phase change after the OER, which is consistent with the post-XRD and SEM results.

Measurements in 1.0 M KOH were conducted to analyze the electrocatalytic activity of Fe-PANI/NF and Pt/C/NF for the HER.43,60 The Fe-PANI/NF polarization curve showed the lowest overpotential of 155 mV in comparison to that of NF (320 mV) and was near to that of the Pt/C/NF electrocatalyst (5% Pt on carbon) (36 mV) at 10 mA cm2 (Fig. 15). Fe-PANI/NF exhibited superior water splitting capability than known inexpensive HER catalysts. Comparable efficiency of Fe-PANI with recently reported non-noble bifunctional electrocatalysts was confirmed (Table S3, ESI). Additionally, the Tafel plot (HER) of Fe-PANI/NF shows a smaller slope (120 mV dec1) than NF (142 mV dec1) and was near to that of Pt/C/NF (74 mV dec1), which implies that that HER kinetics became much faster. The TOFs of Fe-PANI/NF (0.022 s1), Pt/C/NF (0.10 s1) and NF (0.005 s1) at 0.1 V confirmed the intrinsic electrocatalytic activity of Fe-PANI in 1.0 M KOH. Fe-PANI/NF exhibits excellent HER performance with significant H2 production in the given time. The currenttime (It) curve was used to examine the modification of the surface morphology, potential and phase of Fe-PANI/NF over 100 h (Fig. 15). The It curve at 100 mV vs. RHE at 10 mV cm2 shows impressive electrode stability, with 2.2% potential loss after 100 h with vigorous H2 evolution.

The favorable TafelVolmer step (Fe-PANI/NF Tafel slope: 120 mA dec1) assisted the H2 evolution in 1.0 M KOH at low overpotential, and Fe-PANI/NF exhibited high HER activity (Fig. 16).42 2M# H* H2 + M# (Tafel); M# + H2O + e M# H* + OH (Volmer); Mcat Hchem + H2O + e H2 + M# + OH (Heyrovsky). The hydrogen (H*) atoms adsorb on metal active sites (M#), which is highly dependent on the electrochemical properties of the active sites. The H+e transfer process in the HER catalyst promotes strong bonds with adsorbed H, which are broken to release H2 gas (Sabatier theory).42 However, the rate determining step is related to hydrogen absorption instead of water dissociation. Fe-PANI has potent ability to capture water quickly, which results in dissociation to release a lot of hydrogen atoms. The small Tafel slope of Fe-PANI implies that the hydrogen atoms are transferred to the active sites of Fe-PANI to form FeH, which combines with H2O-generated H2 molecules.60 Additionally, desorption of adsorbed OH on the surface is accelerated by Fe(OH)x formation, which is consistent with the post-reaction XRD results. The HER kinetics were improved and the ability to dissociate water was effectively increased by the formation of low-valence iron materials on the Fe-PANI (NFe*) surface.33 The increase in the activity of the HER implies a surge in the prevalence of active sites in Fe-PANI. As a result, Fe-PANI may emerge as a robust material for electrochemical energy conversion. The superior activity of active centers (NFe) may be ascribed to the green-synthesized Fe-PANI deposited on the NF and the active centers on the surface of the Fe-PANI closely interacting with 1.0 M KOH.

The active centers in Fe-PANI influenced the catalytic activity. As an outcome, the electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) of Fe-PANI/NF was determined by measuring the double layer capacitance (Cdl) at 40, 60, 80, and 100 mV s1 scan rates in 1.0 M KOH (Fig. 20 and SI-1, ESI).7779 According to the rectangular shape of the data, the ECSAs of Fe-PANI (13.5 m2 g1) and NF (7.4 m2 g1) were determined to have high double layer capacitance. The slopes of the double layer capacitance were found to be 0.29 mF cm2 (Fe-PANI/NF) and 0.27 mF cm2 (NF) for broad rectangular traces when the scan rate was changed from low to high. The anodic and cathodic current increased to 0.066 mA cm2 (NF). For the OER and HER, strong polarity and a great potential for the formation of new active sites were observed for Fe-PANI. The calculated ECSAs were 7.4 m2 g1 (NF) and 13.5 m2 g1 (Fe-PANI/NF). A significant increase in the ECSA for Fe-PANI/NF over that of NF indicates that Fe-PANI/NF interacts strongly with the electrolyte, generating more accessible active sites to assist the OER and HER processes. The high ECSA may be due to the unique spindle-like nature of Fe-PANI with a high surface area (BET). Table S2 (ESI) shows the ECSA/RF values of Fe-PANI/NF and NF (Fig. 20).

Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF (1.64 V at 10 mA cm2) required a lower voltage than NF//NF (1.95 V at 10 mA cm2). During the electrolysis, vigorous H2 and O2 bubbles were observed on the respective electrodes. The Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF electrolyzer exhibited durability, with 2.0% loss in potential after 100 h of continuous electrolysis at 10 mA cm2 (Fig. 22). The evolution of H2 gas (poorly soluble) was more rapid than O2 gas evolution because of the sluggish OER reaction kinetics. Table S3 (ESI) confirms that Fe-PANI/NF exhibits superior bifunctional catalytic efficiency compared to other iron-based electrocatalysts. The strong association of Fe-PANI with the NF under OER and HER conditions was demonstrated through electrochemical in situ surface oxidation. The synchronous chemisorption of intermediates consisting of oxygen and hydrogen was assisted by Fe in Fe-PANI and led to an increase in electrocatalytic OER and HER activity.

3.2. Solar-to-hydrogen (STH) production of Fe-PANI

We constructed an electrolyzer with a commercial solar panel (5.83 V) to assess the practical performance of Fe-PANI/NF in 1.0 M KOH. The solar-assisted water-splitting setup involved Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF. To adjust the voltage, the positive and negative terminals of the solar panel were connected through copper clips and a digital multi-meter with full cell water splitting setup (Fig. 23).43,60 The fabricated solar water splitting device evolved oxygen and hydrogen gas constantly at 1.64 V (without sunlight). During water electrolysis, 4.57 mmol h1 cm2 of hydrogen was generated (SI-1: Table S4, ESI).42 As a result, the solar-assisted electrolyser developed in this study might be useful for cost-efficient H2 generation in the water-splitting industry.
Fig. 23 (a) Solar full-cell water splitting setup and (b) overall water splitting of Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF.

Thus, Fe-PANI has many advantages for efficient water splitting: (i) the spindle-like geometry of Fe-PANI provides a high surface area-to-volume ratio for rapid charge transfer with appropriate coverage of active sites; (ii) spindle-architectured Fe-PANI with a suitable pore size is considered to be a reactive species for adsorptiondesorption; (iii) Fe-PANI consists of Fe coordination sites bonded with N (FeN), which would be favorable for the formation of active Fe-(oxy)hydroxides, thereby increasing the attraction for intermediates; (iv) the in situ association of Fe-PANI on NF improves the electrode stability and conductivity, also promoting mass transfer by incorporating the active sites and impeding self-aggregation; (v) the bifunctional activity of Fe-PANI can be improved by a complete green treatment (no solvents, direct use of starting materials with simple mortar and pestle grinding). On the basis of these advantages, the potential Fe-PANI electrocatalyst exhibits a low overpotential of 261 mV (OER) and 155 mV (HER) at 10 mA cm2 in 1.0 M KOH. A cell voltage of 1.64 V was required by Fe-PANI to drive an alkali-electrolyzer at 10 mA cm2.

3.3.1. Raw materials. Very small quantities of raw materials (FeCl36H2O and C6H5NH2) were used to synthesize Fe-PANI, which was then washed with H2O and C2H5OH. 3.3.2. Energy consumption. A normal filtration method was used instead of operating vacuum filtration settings and no electricity was used for the synthesis. The Fe-PANI was dried with sunlight (no electrical energy was involved) and the energy intensity was found to be 0.00 kW h kg1 for the laboratory-scale synthesis of Fe-PANI and the energy intensity may be completely reduced by increasing the production scale.
3.3.3. Resulting emissions. No harmful side products were separated out during the synthesis of Fe-PANI; also, extra oxidants were avoided for polymerizing aniline during the synthesis. The calculated environmental factor (0.3) implies negligible emissions and the recovered unreacted salts during repeated washings and H2O can be recycled (minimum quantity of H2O was used for washing).
3.3.4. Toxicity potential. As per the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), FeCl3 is a corrosive skin sensitizer and aniline is toxic to both human as well as aquatic life. In the presence of non-flammable FeCl3in situ polymerization of aniline at room temperature resulted in the synthesis of PANI, which was blended with FeCl3 to form the non-hazardous material Fe-PANI (in accordance with regulation (EC) no. 1272/2008). Hence, the industrialized process can be undertaken with very simple preventive measures.
3.3.5. Disposal of waste. The nickel foam was finally disposed of by a professional waste disposal service. The material was dissolved in combustible solvent and burnt in a chemical incinerator equipped with an afterburner and scrubber.
3.3.6. Affordability. The solar-assisted water splitting set-up demonstrated in this study might be recommended for cost-efficient H2 production in the water-splitting industry because the durable Fe-PANI electrocatalyst was synthesized in a green manner using inexpensive materials.
3.3.7. Social acceptability. The current green strategy for the synthesis of Fe-PANI is eco-friendly, simple, economical, requires low operative effort, produces a product that is suitable for consumers and generates clean hydrogen that has high societal impact. Since the recommended technology does not require the use of highly skilled manpower for implementation as well as maintenance it is highly acceptable for use in the OER/HER.

4. Conclusion

We described a green synthetic technique for developing earth-abundant Fe-encapsulated PANI as an excellent durable electrocatalyst. Fe-PANI with a spindle-like morphology catalyzed the OER (261 mV) and HER (155 mV) with a low overpotential, small Tafel slope (OER/HER: 100/76 mV dec1) and high durability (100 h). The TOFs of 0.673 s1 (OER) and 0.22 s1 (HER) were higher than that of IrO2 (0.017 s1) and near to those of Pt/C (0.0109 s1), respectively. The outperformance of the Fe-PANI/NF electrocatalyst may be attributed to the synergistic effect of the spindle-like morphology and NF hitched electrode architecture. The spindle-like morphology leads to fast carrier transfer and mass transport and outstanding mechanical durability (100 h: OER/HER), with a huge active surface area of 7.4 m2 g1 (Fe-PANI). The Fe-PANI/NF//Fe-PANI/NF electrolyzer exhibits non-stop H2 and O2 evolution, with 1.64 V at 10 mA cm2. Solar-to-hydrogen (STH) production through water splitting (1.64 V) was demonstrated for the first time using bifunctional Fe-PANI/NF. The environmental impact of Fe-PANI/NF was established by evaluating the relevant sustainability metrics.
