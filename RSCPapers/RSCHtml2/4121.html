Abstract

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are crystalline porous materials with a broad range of practical applications. A plethora of MOFs based on a variety of metal ions including s-, p-, d-, and f-block elements have been developed over the past decade. Despite the abundance, cheapness, and recognized non-toxicity, s-block MOFs are less explored than those of MOFs based on transition metals and f-block metal ions. Given that s-block MOFs adopt outstanding features (e.g., low density, dense Lewis active centers and open metal sites, biocompatibility, and high catalytic activity), they have been used for potential applications such as gas storage and separation, chemical delivery, catalysis, electrochemical applications, and sensing. This review includes the recent advances of s-block MOFs. The properties of MOFs based on s-block metal ions are the first aspect in our discussion; the second one is the description of the chemical structures of s-block MOF constituents including organic linkers and metal clusters; furthermore, the function of crystal structures and applications of s-block MOFs are specially addressed in gas sorption and separation, chemical delivery, catalysis, sensing, and others types of practical applications. Providing viewpoints on how to expand the scope of s-block MOF chemistry is considered as our final goal in an attempt to orient the chemistry, properties, applications, and future directions of s-block MOFs as advanced materials for multiple purposes in the future.

1. Introduction

Adopting the intriguing features of architectural rigidity, high porosity, thermal and chemical stability, crystalline frameworks, and limitless structural functionalization, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as promising candidates for energy-related applications1â€“6â€”IUPAC identifies that one of ten innovations in chemistry7 that could change the world is using MOFs for water harvesting;8â€“14 the applicability of MOFs, however, is much further than that.15â€“22 The unified pore structures which can be decorated with targeted motifs by physical and/or chemical inclusion,23â€“25 the ability of organic linker functionalization without disturbing the structural topologies, and the chemical diversity of metal clusters26â€“28 (i.e. organic linkers and metal clusters are two components, termed secondary building units, or SBUs, that construct the hybrid frameworks of MOF materials) are what make MOFs interesting. Particularly, MOFs with hybrid functional structures are advanced platforms for catalysis,20â€“22 adsorption,29â€“31 magnetic applications,22,32 and sensing,19,33 to name a few.34â€“36

Metal-based clusters that MOF chemistry relies on are very diverse: MOF structures consist of organic linkers and almost all metals in the periodic table,37,38 including s-block metals, p- and d-block transition metals, and rare-earth elements. Owing to the pronounced characteristics of transition metals that include visible light absorption, multiple oxidation states, and various coordination geometries, MOFs based on p- and d-block elements are the most dominant. This has been proven by many well-established MOF structures consisting of clusters of Ti(IV),38â€“46 Zr(IV),47â€“52 Cr(III),53,54 Fe(III),55,56 Cu(II),57â€“59 Zn(II),60,61 and Al(III),62â€“64etc. Recently, attention has been paid to rare-earth (RE) MOFs65â€“71 due to the high coordination numbers of RE clusters leading to higher stability and fascinating structures with various topologies. In contrast to p-, d-, and f-block MOFs, MOFs made up of s-block main group elements, alkali and alkaline earth metals, have been less considered in both the fundamental and applied chemistry because of their low stability and most importantly, preconceived chemical features,72â€”i.e., the shortage of the formation of predictive structures and the challenge to reticulate those of active s-block metals into extended frameworks.73 As a result, research efforts, published articles from 1995 to date, on s-block MOFs only hold a small fraction of 1.02% compared to all MOFs (Fig. 1).

Despite the limited structures and difficulties in the structural formation of s-block MOFs originating from the unpredicted coordination behavior of metals in groups 1A and 2A, s-block MOFs adopt many distinctive, appealing, and intriguing features that are suited to many applications. In particular, s-block MOFs inherit the characteristics of s-block main group elements that have (1) naturally high abundance, making them inexpensive; (2) less toxicity, unlike the other MOFs based on transition metals, thus expanding their applicability in various fields, especially in biological processes; and (3) low density, one of the essential factors for gas sorption applications.72â€“74

In order to obtain a comprehensive understanding of the chemistry of s-block MOFs (e.g., the coordination behavior of metal clusters and crystal structures of MOFs that are formed by linking those of metal and organic SBUs) and their practical applications, we summarize the progress, the discoveries, and the uses of s-block MOFs (i.e., discussing the structural features, and interpreting the related applications of alkali- and alkaline-earth-based MOFs) in detail. We finally consider some research directions, as important as the discussion of the chemical features and applications of s-block MOFs, which help diversify and expand the scope of s-block MOFs for the design and synthesis of more complex structures.

2. Properties of s-block MOFs

Although MOFs based on s-block metals have been less considered, they afford several interesting properties:

(i) Some s-block metal ions such as Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, and Ba2+ present important biological properties.75 The biocompatibility makes the aforementioned metals attractive to design biocompatible MOFs.76,77 For instance, Ba is widely used as an X-ray contrast agent;78 therefore, linking such s-block metal ions with biocompatible linkers would be of great interest for medicinal applications.79

(ii) Low-density s-block metal ions (e.g., Li or Mg) can construct MOFs that possess low-density frameworks with a high surface area leading to increased gas uptake capacity.80 For instance, researchers used Li+ for gravimetric modification of MOFs that adopt promising gas sorption properties.81 Moreover, Mg-MOFs have been largely explored for selective CO2 capture and separation.82,83

(iii) MOFs constructed from s-block metals provide open metal sites (OMS) accomplished through elimination of coordinated solvent molecules for gas uptake enhancement. The requirement for using OMS-based MOFs for sorption is that the unsaturated frameworks must be stable and have to maintain permanent porosity after activation.84 Removal of such coordination centers, however, often comes at the cost of structural collapse and loss of porosity.85 In this context, post-synthetic metalation using s-block metals (i.e., doping Li) holds promise to solve such problems.86,87 Given the advantages afforded by Li and other alkali metals, scientists have applied the doping strategy using different alkali metals to MOFs to increase CO2, CH4, and H2 uptake capacity.88â€“91

(iv) Along with the OMS, structures of s-block MOFs often consist of infinite chain SBUs which are strong Lewis acidic centers. Such infinite SBUs not only enhance the gas uptake capacity but also promote organic transformations due to the dense catalytically active sites. Therefore, s-block MOFs are suitable for organic reactions requiring catalysts with Lewis acidic active centers such as Câ€“C bond formation or cycloaddition of CO2.

These interesting properties will be discussed in detail in the application section.

According to the hard soft acid base principle,94 s-block metal ions are hard metals. Consequently, they react with hard heteroatom-based organic linkers to form extended structures with ionic bonds in nature (Fig. 2). There are few reports of using organic linkers with soft atoms (e.g., N of pyridine and imidazole based on carboxylic acids)95,96 to construct extended frameworks with s-block metals. In these cases, s-block metals binding to soft atoms, mainly N-based carboxylate linkers, will generate more stable structures. As in the case of {[Sr(Î¼2-H2mPyMIDC)2(DMF)2]nÂ·H2O}n,94 the versatile connectivity modes of the H3mPyMIDC linker (H3mPyMIDC stands for 2-(pyridin-3-ylmethyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid) to the metal ion facilitate the formation of a 3D chiral MOF consisting of two well-defined 1D helical chains. Apparently, the flexibility of the pyridinylmethyl group of the imidazole-based dicarboxylate linker with dihedral angles between imidazole and pyridyl rings in the range of 0â€“60Â° allows them to rotate freely around the Câ€“C facilitating the MOF formation. The linker adopts a Î¼2-kN,O:kO' mode binding to two Sr2+ ions in a N,O-chelating monodentate fashion. This linkage makes the MOF structure rigid.

Most organic linkers in s-block MOFs are aromatic polycarboxylate anions whose bond strength with alkali (1A) and alkaline earth (2A) ions mainly leads to increased rigidity in s-block MOF structures.72 Particularly, the rigidity of s-block MOFs is based on the strong ionic interaction resulting from the large difference in electronegativity between the s-block metal ions and the donor atoms (e.g., carboxylate functional groups) of organic linkers (an exception is the Be ion which has the highest charge/radius ratio compared to other s-block elements, thus forming covalent bonds in Be-based extended structures).97 This type of bonding differs from that of MOFs based on p-, d- and f-block metal ions, in which diverse structures are formed by covalent bonds.98,99 Limited development of s-block elements as metal centers for MOFs is attributed to their instability in air and aqueous medium. Once exposed to air, Mâ€“O bonds, M stands for s-block metals, adsorb moisture and form Mâ€“OH species, hence protonating the bond and thereby destroying the MOF structure. The hydrolytic behavior is prone to be faster in aqueous medium, especially under various pH conditions. This is caused by the high electronegativity and ionic nature of the metal centers with their carboxylate oxygen atoms. It is thus difficult to exert control over s-block MOF coordination geometry which is determined by the orientation of these functional groups.73,100â€“103 For applications including desalination, separation of organic molecules, drug delivery, and proton conduction, among others, there is an inevitable need for high stability of the MOF in an aqueous environment. MOF-74-Mg for instance losses 80â€“90% of its original porous structure when exposed to water at 95% relative humidity (RH).104 A similar trend is observed in carbonate-based electrolyte medium in which small metal ions like Li suffer instability which notably hinders their development as electrode materials for batteries.105

Thermal stability, which is essential in gas adsorption and separation applications, however, is comparatively high among s-block MOFs. The large radius coupled with high electronegativity increases thermal stability behavior in s-block MOFs with most of them having Td (decomposition temperature) values of ca. 400 Â°C, if not above.101,102 This robust property makes s-block MOFs stable for gas uptake studies similar to other MOFs. Similarly, in a nonpolar chemical environment, s-block MOFs exhibit higher stability due to the Mâ€“O bonds which are strong enough not to be hydrolyzed by such nonpolar solvents and high charge density as well as the large ionic size that accounts for the larger coordination environment. Therefore, finding the way to implement strong linkages into rigid s-block MOF structures is essential for enhancing the stability of resulting frameworks and further expanding the chemistry and applications of s-block MOFsâ€”the last section in this review, future perspectives, covers this encounter.

5. Applications of s-block MOFs

The potential applications of s-block MOFs in gas storage and separation are based on permanent porosity and OMS. Meanwhile the shape, size, functionality of pore structures, and active metal sites mainly correspond to catalytic applications. Most importantly, the biocompatibility of s-block MOFs makes them attractive platforms for chemical/drug delivery and medicinal applications. The following sections describe the interplay between the structures and most common applications of s-block MOFs in detail.

5.1. Adsorption of gases and small molecules

Using MOFs for gas sorption and small molecule uptake has been successfully achieved.1,112 The uptake of small molecules using s-block MOFs, however, has been explored recently by Yan et al. reporting a Ca-based MOF, made up of the H3BTC (H3BTC = benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid) linker and Ca clusters and formulated as [Ca(HBTC)(H2O)]Â·2H2O.99 [Ca(HBTC)(H2O)]Â·2H2O exhibited linear zigzag SBUs which link to the organic linkers to generate open pores with a dimension of 7.9 Ã— 5.6 Ã…. There is only one distinctive Ca2+ ion in the asymmetric unit and this Ca2+ bonds to eight oxygen atoms: seven of them, in two different binding modes, belong to five different carboxylate linkers and the other one is from the water molecule. The resulting polyhedra share edges to form 1D zigzag SBUs. Each Ca2+ in the chain coordinates to three BTC linkers to generate the [Ca(H2O)(BTC)3] chain which further connects to the other six chains via BTC linkers to form a 3D network with solvent accessible pores. This MOF exhibited high thermal stability and retained the structural framework intact after activation; therefore, it was studied for the removal of Hg0 at 150 Â°C. Nevertheless, about 50% of Hg0 was reduced after 50 min, and the concentration of Hg0 was raised again after 75 min. The observed poor adsorption ability of [Ca(HBTC)(H2O)]Â·2H2O was attributed to its small pores.

The second example of using s-block MOFs for small molecule uptake is credited to Mandal et al.113 who synthesized a Ba-MOF, formulated as [Ba3(C9H3O6)2(H2O)4]Â·2H2O denoted by 1-B. [Ba3(C9H3O6)2(H2O)4]Â·2H2O is based on mixed linkers, H3BTC and BIA (H3BTC = benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid; BIA = 5-boronoisopthalic acid). The 3D framework of [Ba3(C9H3O6)2(H2O)4]Â·2H2O consists of trimeric Ba-based SBUs linked to BTC3âˆ’ linkers. [Ba3(C9H3O6)2(H2O)4]Â·2H2O was examined for its applicability in adsorption of cis-diol compounds including glucose, galactose, mannose, and xylose. Boric acid was added to substitute some of the BTC ligands in the MOF structure to create defects, thus, improving the adsorption affinity between the MOF and cis-diol compounds. The adsorption studies of cis-diol compounds decreased in the order of galactose > glucose > mannose > xylose. The MOF regeneration, however, was hindered by its instability in acidic medium.

It should be noted that there are very few studies that report the use of s-block MOFs for the uptake of organic compounds, Hg0 removal, and environmental applications. This limitation is mainly due to the stability and porosity of s-block MOFs, both must be improved for such kinds of applications.

5.2. Chemical/drug delivery

The uses of MOFs to entrap and deliver compounds for specific purposes (e.g., chemical delivery, drug carriers) have been extensively studied126,127 thanks to the high porosity, strong hostâ€“guest interaction, and well-defined pore structures of MOFs. Furthermore, owing to the high degree of flexibilityâ€”structural customization has been controlled at the molecular levelâ€”MOFs can be appropriately tailored to incapsulate and deliver specific compounds under targeted conditions. For instance, Auras et al. synthesized CDMOF-a and CDMOF-b consisting of potassium precursors and gamma cyclodextrin units (Î³-CDs). Both structures showed a hydrophilic interior and hydrophobic exterior.128 Especially, CDMOF-a and CDMOF-b can encapsulate acetaldehydeâ€“acetaldehyde is a water-soluble material, volatile, highly reactive and toxicâ€”and its release can precisely be controlled. Indeed, CDMOF-b with the Î³-CD backbone exhibited better encapsulation and release of acetaldehyde than CDMOF-a and previously reported CD-MOFs.129 This behavior was attributed to the aldol self-condensation reaction occurring in the pores of CDMOF-b bearing the residual hydroxide ions. The encapsulation of acetaldehyde in CDMOF-a, however, resulted in a formation of residual products that was indicated by a color change.

Similar to CD-MOFs, the applications of s-block MOFs for biochemical applications rely on their nontoxicity and biocompatibility of metals, especially Ca and Sr. For example, Joseph et al.130 synthesized a mixed metal CaSr-MOF made up of trimesic acid and Ca and Sr clusters. The CaSr-MOF was investigated for in vivo delivery of Ca and Sr osteoinductive metals to enhance bone regeneration. This study also revealed that the CaSr-MOF could induce in vitro biomineralization from pre-osteoblastic MC3T3 cells and human mesenchymal stem cells (hMSCs). Additionally, CaSr-MOFs were capable of encapsulating dimethyloxalylglycine and inducing the production of vascular endothelial cells from hMSCs.

Furthermore, Sun, Wang, and co-workers reported the synthesis of MOF@CaP,131 a biodegradable nanoscale Mg-gallate core encapsulated by a calcium phosphate (CaP) nanostructured shell, for drug delivery. The CaP shell reduces the degradation of the Mg-MOF core and allows the MOF composite to encapsulate bioactive entities that can be released under low pH conditions. Particularly, MOF@CaP releases the inflammatory microenvironment stimuli-responsive to the anti-inflammatory bioactive protein cytokine interleukin 4 (IL4). MOF@CaP nanoparticles were mixed with a collagen solution (Col) to prepare the MOF@CaP/Col composite. The in vivo study within a period of eight weeks showed that cranial bone defects in IL4-MOF@CaP/Col were successfully recovered to a nearly indistinguishable state compared with the early state of injury.

It is undoubtable that the biocompatibility of alkali and alkaline earth metals makes them attractive to build MOF systems for potential biomedical applications as discussed in several examples mentioned above. In addition, Sr is a non-toxic metal and plays a vital role in human tissue (e.g., bone). It has positive effects on bone stabilization that can stimulate osteoblast differentiation and prevent osteoclast formation and resorption.132 Moreover, Sr lessens cartilage degeneration and chondrocyte apoptosis on the osteoarthritis therapy.133,134 Studies have shown that Sr not only efficiently reduces inflammation but also relieves pain caused by osteoarthritis.135â€“137 In this context, Tang and co-workers138 reported the synthesis of the Sr/HCOOH-MOF and tested its activity for ketoprofen (an anti-inflammatory drug) release. Particularly, loading and releasing experiments showed that only 3% ketoprofen was loaded and nearly 80% was released within 8 h. It was noticed that there was a rapid increase at the beginning of the period of 8 h attributed to ketoprofen being close to the surface and cage walls. Following the rapid release, a slower release between 12 and 36 h was indicative of a release of ketoprofen from smaller pores and the release was stopped after 36 h when the framework collapsed.

Moreover, the anti-inflammatory effect of the Sr/HCOOH-MOF was studied for the in vitro qPCR (quantitative polymerase chain reaction) detection technique of three inflammation-related genes: IL-1Î², iNOS, and RANKL, proving the promising applicability of the Sr/HCOOH-MOF in osteoarthritis treatment.

Like other MOFs adopting permanent porosity and catalytically active sites, s-block MOFs are capable of promoting organic transformation. In this section, we highlight the application of s-block MOFs for organic synthesis by exploiting the OMS or Lewis acidic sites.

5.5. Sensing

MOFs have recently been explored for their potential as chemical sensors; the tunability of MOF structures and properties provides a certain advantage in the field of chemo-sensory materials. Sensing capabilities of several s-block MOFs have been examined and summarized.

Recently, luminescent frameworks have been considered as a direct consequence of several key factors, one of which is the production of a signal that is observable by the naked eye. Several MOFs have been included in this summary. The two MOFs, [Ca(HBTC)(H2O)]Â·2H2O and Rb(HBDC), which were previously mentioned in Section 5.1 were studied for their photoluminescence (PL) properties.99 In Rb(HBDC), each Rb cation connects to six carboxylate linkers. The resulting RbO6 polyhedra share edges to form 1D chains. These infinite chains are connected in a parallel manner via the carboxylate groups of the organic linkers. Each carboxylate linker is shared between two different Rb+ ions from two parallel chains. The photoluminescence properties of the two MOFs were studied at room temperature. Rb(HBDC) exhibited blue photoluminescence at 427 nm upon irradiation at 317 nm. Similar behavior was observed in the free H2BDC linker. This behavior is a result of the ligand-centered Ï€*â€“n transition. Photoluminescence studies revealed a hypochromic blue shift for the [Ca(HBTC)(H2O)]Â·2H2O as compared to the free ligand H3BTC. The shift was attributed to the change of non-bonding orbital energy for oxygen atoms because of coordinated Ca2+ and hydrogen bonding to the carboxylate groups of the linker.

Motivated by the photoluminescence of MOFs based on H3BTC, Lu et al.147 reported [Sr(HBTC)(H2O)]n showing promising results in photoluminescence. The MOF has one crystallographic independent octa-coordinate Sr ion crystallizing in a distorted square antiprism structure. The Sr ion is penta-coordinated to five HBTC units binding through seven connected carboxylate oxygen atoms in different chelating and bridging modes, resulting in the formation of a 2D layered structure. The 2D layers are extended and linked through remaining carboxylate to form a 3D framework. A photoluminescence study was conducted using a pulse laser at different excitation powers, and two main peaks were observed; a strong peak was detected at 330 nm and it was considered as a ligand-based emission, and a weaker peak was detected at 640 nm which referred to ligandâ€“metal charge transfer, representing a direct optical band gap energy of 1.94 eV. This experimental band gap was in good agreement with the theoretical value (2.04 eV) obtained by DFT calculations.

MOF frameworks with high conjugation provide attractive photoluminescence features for sensing applications. In order to obtain such structures, Sun et al.148 designed and successfully synthesized Ba5(ADDA)5(EtOH)2(H2O)3Â·5DMF (H2ADDA = 3,3â€²-(anthracene-9,10-diyl)diacrylic acid), termed UPC-17. The structure of UPC-17 exhibits an asymmetric unit with five Ba2+ ions, five ADDA linkers, two ethanol, and three coordinated water molecules. The five independent Ba2+ ions have coordination numbers from 7 to 10. Each Ba2+ connects to five organic linkers through different coordination modes, three of which are trans isomers while the remaining two are cis isomers. Ba2+ ions links to the carboxylate of ADDA ligands to form 1D rod-shaped SBUs. The 1D SBUs are then bridged by the backbone of ADDA ligands forming the porous 3D framework.

PL measurements of Ba5(ADDA)5(EtOH)2(H2O)3Â·5DMF at room temperature showed an emission at 546 nm when being excited at 330 nm. This transition can be attributed to the transition of Ï€*â€“n or Ï€*â€“Ï€. PL measurements were also varied to detect metal ions in different organic solvents including methanol, tetrahydrofuran (THF), and acetone. In methanol, UPC-17 showed a 48 nm-peak shift as compared to the solid-state PL measurements. By conducting fluorescence titration, the gradual increase of Fe3+ concertation quenched 90% of the initial PL emission (turn-off) indicating high sensitivity toward Fe3+. Nevertheless, only 23% quenching was observed when using acetone indicating lower selectivity. Moreover, a 4.5-fold enhancement for Fe3+ detection in THF was observed. An increase in the PL emission intensity of the fluorescence enhancement (turn-on) was also observed upon the gradual addition of Fe3+. The sensing performance of the MOF was also tested for the detection of Al3+. An increase in the emission intensity was detected upon the gradual addition of Al3+ in THF, however, no effect was observed when using acetone or methanol. Furthermore, a sensing study of different nitroaromatic compounds resulted in varied quenching results. Remarkably, the MOF showed the highest sensitivity for 4-nitrophenol in acetone, THF, and methanol with 80%, 75%, and 60% quenching fluorescence percentage, respectively.

Mandal and co-workers reported the reaction of the H3BTB linker (1,3,5-benzenetribenzoic acid) with three alkaline earth metal ions (Ca, Sr, and Ba),108 which leads to the 3D formation of [H2N(CH3)2][Ca7(BTB)5-(H2O)8(DMF)4]Â·4H2O, [H2N(CH3)2][Sr5(H2O)(BTB)4], and [H2N(CH3)2][Ba(H2O) (BTB)], respectively. Tang et al.111 synthesized [Sr(2,5-pzdc)(H2O)4]Â·H2O whose structure is filled by water molecules pointing toward the center of 1D channels. Fluorescent MOFs have interesting applications as sensors because they possess a high surface area as well as intense and controllable fluorescence. In this regard, Kojima et al.154 prepared a fluorescent Ca-based MOF using the 1,4-NDC linker (1,4-NDC = 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid), {(H3O+)2[Ca(NDC)(C2H5O)(OH)]}4Â·1.1H2O, denoted by CaNDC-MOF. The excitation spectra of 1,4-NDC and CaNDC-MOF were studied. For the linker, the fluorescence peak was observed at 470 nm when excited at 385 nm, while CaNDC-MOF showed a violet fluorescence peak at 395 nm when excited at 350 nm. The authors discussed two possible pathways corresponding to the observed fluorescence in the MOF. First, the detected blue shift in the fluorescence peak of CaNDC-MOF is ascribed to the LMCT transition in the MOF where Ca2+ ions bonding to the linkers resulted in a 75-nm shift in the PL spectrum. The blue shift also confirms that the linker was incorporated into the framework of CaNDC-MOF lengthening distances between molecules. LMCT-based fluorescence MOFs reported so far have exhibited red-shifts, however, the CaNDC-MOF is the first MOF that shows a blue shift attributed to the LMCT transition. Second, the fluorescence originating from the linker and Ca2+ ion did not contribute to the fluorescence spectra. This suggestion was proposed after studying the fluorescence of 1,4-NDC solution in a mixture of water and ethanol which showed a fluorescence peak at 412 nm when excited at 369 nm. These similar results to those of the CaNDC-MOF are ascribed to the linker being dispersed in the crystalline structure of the MOF, which is similar to the solution resulting in similar fluorescence behavior.

Another strategy applied in designing sensing materials is doping metal ions into a MOF structure (PSM method). In this regard, emissive metals are used. For example, Eu ions were doped into [Ba(DPA)2(H2O)2]n termed Eu@BCP (DPA stands for dipicolinic acid) and Tb ions were incorporated into [Ba(DPA)2(H2O)2]n termed Tb@BCP.156 Both Eu@BCP and Tb@BCP showed strong emission upon UV irradiation: Tb@BCP exhibited an intense green emission and Eu@BCP featured a red emission. First, for temperature sensing, the fluorescence intensity of the host lattice was enhanced upon raising the temperature from 20 Â°C to 90 Â°C. 5.6.1. Antibacterial materials. Microbial resistance from pathogenic bacteria is growing at an alarming rate and conventional antibiotics are getting ineffective due to the mutating nature of the resistant bacteria. Nair et al.158 prepared a Sr-MOF layer based on L-dimalatepentahydrate termed DSM with a chemical formula of Sr2(C4H4O5)2Â·5H2O. The anti-bacterial properties of DSM were tested against different bacterial strains including Staphylococcus aureus MTCC 902, Staphylococcus epidermis MTCC 435(SE), Bacillus subtilis MTCC 2756(BS), Escherichia coli MTCC 2622(EC), Klebsiella pneumoniae MTCC 109(KP), and Proteus mirabilis MTCC 425(PM); of which Staphylococcus aureus MTCC 902, Staphylococcus epidermis MTCC 435(SE), and Bacillus subtilis MTCC 2756(BS) are Gram positive, whereas Escherichia coli MTCC 2622(EC), Klebsiella pneumoniae MTCC 109(KP), and Proteus mirabilis MTCC 425(PM) are Gram negative.

The antimicrobial activities (measured as the diameter of the zone of inhibition formed for discs in mm) of DSM were determined by the disc diffusion method against the aforementioned bacteria. Moreover, the activities were compared with the those of standard piperazine. The diameter of the zone of inhibition formed for discs was found to be active except for the Gram-negative Escherichia coli. Compared to the activity of piperazine, Gram positive Staphylococcus aureus was found to be more active while the others show lower activity.

5.6.3. Pyrotechnic applications. Pyrotechnics has always been an essential component of festivities, and its history dated back thousands of years in the ancient civilization. In modern times, the function of pyrotechnics has been expanded to cover a wide range of applications from civilian to military. The reaction in these compounds is basically explosive, and as such they must contain components that are oxidative and easily combustible to enable redox reactions. To a greater extent, such reactions are useful, however, the combustion of chlorine-based pyrotechnics has side effects on human health. Therefore, development of chlorine-free pyrotechnics is widely sought. Among the novel green combustible, azole-based energetic MOFs (EMOF) hold promise. In principle, nitrogen-rich azole EMOFs have promising properties including their high enthalpy of formation that provides the primary energy source and the green combustion reaction that produces greener products. In fact, the generated products allow metal ions to distribute in a monoatomic fashion inside the flame which in turn produces an enhanced color performance. EMOFs based on s-block metals adopt highly oxidizing properties.139 In particular, a Cs-based MOF synthesized from N-(5â€²-amino-1H,1â€²H-[3,3â€²-bi(1,2,4-triazol)]-5-yl)nitramine (HABTNA) ligand, termed EMOF-1, was studied by Wang et al.161 EMOF-1 shows promising energetic and colorant properties that are essential in the development of pyrotechnics. EMOF-1, a highly thermal-stable structure, crystalizes in a 3D structure with abundance of N-based entities and balanced oxygen content. Therefore, it is safe to handle. The authors proved that EMOF-1 is non-hygroscopic, insensitive, and contains high combustible energy, offering a new alternative to replace toxic chlorine-based MOFs.

Most of the conventional compounds used in pyrotechnics are flammable and can cause self-oxidative combustion. They are sensitive to water causing wetting inconvenience along with other health related risks. EMOF-1 paves the way for developing novel research that would make explosive materials more safe and pyrotechnics appealing to the public.

6. Conclusion and future perspectives

This review summarizes the versatile structures and applications of s-block MOFs. Particularly, the properties, coordinative environments of the SBUs of s-block MOFs, linker designs, and the relationship between their structural features and applications in gas storage and separation, adsorption of small molecules, chemical/drug delivery, catalysis, sensing, and many more were discussed comprehensively. Our highlights in this article indicate the groundbreaking features of s-block MOFs which will attract more considerable attention from the research community for multiple applications.

In s-block MOF chemistry, structures rely on ionic bonds and the structural dimensionality depends much on the connectivity between organic linkers and metal ions (e.g., Li, Na). For heavier metal ions, the resulting extended structures tend to be more complex and advocate against interpenetration. Additionally, using s-block metals with a large ionic radius for MOF construction leads to low charge density frameworks, and they are prone to accommodate coordinated solvents; removing such guest molecules binding to metal clusters during activation often results in structural collapse. For MOFs based on alkaline earth metal ions, there is no solid evidence that presents the specific trend in the formation of MOFs. Indeed, like the alkali ions, as the atomic size increases, the coordination number of metals in MOFs based on group 2A increases; and 3D structures are expected to form, although such structures are serendipitously produced rather than systematically designed.

MOFs based on s-block metal ions have not been thoroughly investigated because of the following reasons:

(1) The unpredictable structures originating from the reactive metal sources often prevail.

(2) Balancing the high porosity and stability remains challenging.

(3) The ionic bonds between metal ions and linkers negatively affect the synthetic conditions.

(4) There is a lack of predesigned SBUs that can overcome the synthesis challenges to form multiple and complex structures.

In order to expand the scope of s-block MOFs, we herein present the future perspectives through several questions that should be addressed in sequence.

(1) How to overcome the structural instability of s-block MOFs?

The ability of light s-block metal ions to form a tetrahedral environment makes their frameworks vulnerable and unstable compared to that of d- or f-block metal ions. Moreover, due to the electronic nature and a lack of ligand field stabilization energy of s-block metal ions, s-block MOFs show most instability. In addition, the inability of s-block metal ions to form higher valence preventing them from forming a strong electrostatic interaction with heteroatoms of coordinating linkers results in less stable frameworks. It should be noted that the robustness of the frameworks depends on the geometry and connectivity of the linkers and SBUs. Toward obtaining structures with inherent porosity while maintaining the robustness of structural architectures, a higher coordination number of metals or higher connectivity of SBUs (i.e., high connectivity of points of extension) must be obtained. In order to fulfill this task, the organic linker may contain multiple functional groups instead of only carboxylic acid. Additionally, functionalities that enable Î¼3-based linkages with metal clusters (e.g., O and P) would be interesting to investigate the structural formation of s-block MOFs. Such linkages will enhance the rigidity of MOFs and further avoid coordination of guest molecules, thus improving the stability of s-block MOFs.

(2) How to synthesize more complex s-block MOF structures?

Not many linkers with different connectivity or linkers with multiple functionalities have been investigated for making s-block MOFs. We believe that functionalized linkers with specific properties can adopt a higher coordination environment, thus improving the complexity of s-block MOFs. For example, CD-based linkers react with various s-block metals (K, Ca, Na) to form sophisticated sugar-based structures. Modifying functionalized linkers with similar properties may expand the scope of s-block MOF structures with interesting applications. As we discussed in the structural features of organic linkers used for the synthesis of s-block MOFs, s-block metals binding to heteroatoms (e.g., N-based carboxylate or azolate linkers) will generate stable extended structures. The combination between multiple functionalities and heteroatom-based linkers would solve two problems, stability and multiplicity, of s-block MOFs. Finally, we review recent findings from Frenking and Zhou et al. who firmly state that Ca, Sr, and Ba may use their genuine valence orbitals of (nâˆ’1)d functions to coordinate to ligands that are based on CO or N2 functionality. As a result, Ca, Sr, and Ba can be 8-coordinate units which strongly enhance the robustness and diversity of the framework.162â€“164 These studies imply that there exist opportunities for us to reticulate specific multitopic linkers into extended structures based on s-block metal SBUs mimicking the donorâ€“acceptor interactions of transition metals.

(3) How to expand the scope of s-block MOFs in practical applications?

Expanding the scope of s-block MOFs in practical applications lies at the heart of obtaining more functionalized and complex MOFs. In other words, this task depends much on the structural diversity of s-block MOFs which must be prioritized.

Finally, we think that the future trend for syntheses and uses of various s-block MOFs gathers all aforementioned properties of organic linkers and most importantly, multibinary systems with diverse functionalities should be mainly considered and applied in rational ways for the designed synthesis of s-block MOFs. Especially, functional groups beyond carboxylates are strongly required for obtaining a variety of stable and appealing MOFs based on s-block elements.
