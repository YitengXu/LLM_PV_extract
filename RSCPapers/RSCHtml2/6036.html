Abstract

Over the past decades, there have been many projections on the future depletion of the fossil fuel reserves on earth as well as the rapid increase in green-house gas emissions. There is clearly an urgent need for the development of renewable energy technologies. On a different frontier, growth and manipulation of materials on the nanometer scale have progressed at a fast pace. Selected recent and significant advances in the development of nanomaterials for renewable energy applications are reviewed here, and special emphases are given to the studies of solar-driven photocatalytic hydrogen production, electricity generation with dye-sensitized solar cells, solid-state hydrogen storage, and electric energy storage with lithium ion rechargeable batteries.

1. Introduction

The widely projected depletion of the fossil fuel reserves on earth and the rapid increase in green-house gas emissions and other environmental pollutions have brought about an urgent need for the development and efficient use of renewable and clean energy resources.1–9 A number of approaches have been pursued to tackle the challenges. Among them, the ability to produce electricity and fuels such as hydrogen using sunlight, and the ability to efficiently store them, could help revolutionize how we live our life in the future.1–9 For example, in one proposed scenario of the renewable hydrogen economy, hydrogen, as the energy carrier, is produced through solar irradiation of water, then stored and used in a hydrogen fuel cell car, which produces no pollution except water. In another scenario, electricity is produced under solar irradiation through the use of solar cells, then stored in a battery and used in an electric car, producing no emission. Nevertheless, these renewable energy technologies are not yet cost competitive with the traditional, nonrenewable, fossil energy technologies.

On the other hand, aiming to control and expand the properties of materials on the nanometer scale, scientists have been studying nanomaterials over the past decades.10–15 Nanomaterials are matters with at least one dimension in their physical size ranging between 1 and 100 nm. Nanomaterials appear in a variety of forms such as nanoparticles, nanorods, nanowires, nanofibers, nanotubes, nanoporous framework, etc. As the size of the materials decreases, the percentage of the atoms on the surface and the surface-to-volume ratio increase dramatically.10,12 For example, while less than 0.2% of atoms are on the surface for a 100 nm particle and 10% for a 10 nm particle, around 90% of the atoms are essentially on the surface for a 2 nm particle.10 The atoms on the surface may have one or more dangling bonds; they are generally very active and tend to form bonds with adjacent molecules or particles to reduce the total energy, thus getting stabilized. This can result in an increased chemical activity, lowered melting point, increased phase transition pressure, and higher solubility, for instance, when compared to their bulk counterparts.12 In addition, the intrinsic properties of nanomaterials are affected by their size on the nanometer scale. For example, the electronic band structures of nanoparticles are altered if their size is smaller than certain threshold values.10 For semiconductors, these values are commonly called the Bohr radius, which is in the range from several nanometers to tens of nanometers. The Bohr radius of a semiconductor is normally the average distance between the excited electron and hole after the semiconductor absorbs light with energy larger than its band gap. When the size of a semiconductor crystal is larger than its Bohr radius, the movement of the electron and hole is largely confined by it. However, when the size of a semiconductor crystal is smaller than its Bohr radius, the movement of the electron and hole is largely confined by its physical size, although a small part may leak out due to a quantum tunneling effect. This confinement can cause the electronic band structure of a semiconductor to become discrete or quantized, along with an increase in the band gap between the valence band edge and the conduction band edge. Electronic transitions between the valence band and the conduction band underlie the optical absorption and emission of a semiconductor. So one can tune the optical property of a semiconductor by changing its size within the Bohr radius. In addition to the optical properties, transport of the optically excited electrons and holes across the interface and within the particle can also be modified if the size is smaller than the characteristic mean free path and/or diffusion length.

Many materials, including nanomaterials, have been widely studied for numerous renewable energy applications.6,7,9,16–42 For the present writing, we aim to offer a brief overview of the applications of nanomaterials for solar hydrogen generation, solar electricity generation with dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), solid-state hydrogen storage, and electrochemical electric energy storage. Each section of this review contains a short introduction of the principles of the respective technology, followed by a discussion of the benefits of using nanomaterials, together with selected examples representing recent progress. The emphasis of this review is on the benefits of using nanomaterials for these applications. It is noted that the coverage of this review is far from complete and comprehensive. For a complete grasp of these and other related fields, please refer to more specialized references, such as ref. 21–42. Whereas we do hope that this writing will inspire the interest of the reader in renewable energy technology research, the reader is advised to further reading of the cited articles, reviews, and books.

2. Nanomaterials for photocatalytic hydrogen generation

2.1 Principles of photocatalytic hydrogen generation

The driving force of solar hydrogen generation is the dream of renewable clean fuels.1–5 Imagine that if one can acquire hydrogen gas directly from splitting water using sunlight, then we would have an endless source of clean fuel that has many applications. Hydrogen can be stored in a fuel tank and used for cars powered by a fuel cell where only water is emitted. In such a scheme we would be able to avoid environmental problems currently faced by the use of fossil fuels. Semiconductor materials can act as the key media which help split water into oxygen and hydrogen under solar irradiation.

There are two basic approaches to achieving solar hydrogen generation: an electrode system and a particle system, as schematically shown in Fig. 1. The principles for both systems are similar; a semiconductor acts as the light absorber in which electron–hole pairs are generated upon solar irradiation, while a metal acts as the electron trapper or a co-catalyst.5,6,18–23,40,41 Photo-generated electrons and holes are produced when the semiconductor electrode or particle absorbs sunlight and the electrons are excited from the valence band to the conduction band. The excited electrons can be either transported to the metal electrode connected by an external circuit in the case of an electrode system, or directly transferred to the metal particles deposited on the surface of the semiconductor particle in the case of a particle system. The electronic structure of the metal used for solar water splitting is typically characterized with a half-filled d band. The Fermi level of the metal normally lies below the conduction band edge of the semiconductor. Thermodynamically, transport of the photo-generated electrons from semiconductor to metal is favorable due to the potential difference between the metal and the semiconductor. Before the electrons can move around and get transported, the electron–hole pair from the optical absorption needs to be separated. Meanwhile, the excited electrons need to overcome several barriers in order to transport or migrate to the metal after they are generated. The electrons have to overcome the trapping in the bulk, on the surface of the semiconductor, or at the semiconductor–metal interface, in order to avoid the possibility for meeting the holes to get recombined.

Using the particle system as an example, the processes in the photocatalytic generation of hydrogen are illustrated in Fig. 2.40 They include light absorption of the semiconductor photocatalyst, generation and recombination of the excited charges (electrons and holes), separation, migration, and trap of excited charges, and transfer of excited charges to water or other molecules to produce hydrogen. The amount of hydrogen generated is largely determined by the amount of excited electrons that survived to reduce protons at the water–metal interface. Any processes that consume excited electrons such as trapping and charge recombination should be avoided. Thus, the semiconductor photocatalyst should first have a sufficiently small band gap to absorb as many photons from the sun as possible, and reflection or scattering of light by the photocatalyst should be minimized. After absorbing light, the semiconductor photocatalyst should have a high efficiency in generating excited charges, instead of producing heat via the interaction between the electrons and the lattice (phonons), or emitting fluorescence. Meanwhile, in order to transfer the excited electron to the metal, where hydrogen molecules are produced, the conduction band edge of the semiconductor has to lie above the hydrogen evolution potential and below the conduction band of the semiconductor. Fig. 3 lists the band positions of some oxide and sulfide compounds relative to the chemical potentials for hydrogen/oxygen evolution from water.43 There are many candidate materials which meet the electronic structure requirement for the generation of hydrogen from water. At first glance, the oxide materials have a larger band gap, thus absorb less sunlight, and the sulfide materials have a narrower band gap and can absorb more sunlight. Although the sulfide materials seem to be capable of absorbing more sunlight, they usually undergo oxidation reaction on the surface to become corroded over time under sunlight in water, and therefore quickly lose the capability to generate hydrogen from water. One of the challenges in semiconductor-based photocatalytic hydrogen generation is to design stable narrow bandgap materials which can absorb as much light as possible and match electronic structure requirement for hydrogen generation.

In addition, efficient charge separation and fast charge transport without bulk or surface charge recombination are crucial for photocatalytic hydrogen generation. Any approach beneficial to the charge separation and transport should be taken into account, such as design of a built-in electric field and use of high photoconductive semiconductor materials. The reaction of photo-generated H2 and O2 to form H2O on the photocatalyst surface is usually called “surface back reaction (SBR)”. It reduces the amount of H2 generated from the photocatalytic reaction. The addition of sacrificial reagents to remove the generated holes on the surface of the photocatalyst can help improve the efficiency of photocatalytic hydrogen production.

2.2 The advantages of using nanomaterials for photocatalytic hydrogen generation

Semiconductor nanomaterials have several advantages over their bulk counterparts for solar hydrogen generation due to the following reasons. (a) Higher surface area.10,12,14,21,22,30,32 Higher surface area of nanomaterials can generally provide larger reaction surface area. (b) Higher optical absorption.10,12 As the size of nanomaterials decreases, their optical absorption may increase dramatically. Thus, to absorb an equal amount of light, less material is required. (c) Shorter charge migration length. Due to the small size of nanomaterials, the excited electrons only need to migrate a shorter distance to reach the surface and reduce water into hydrogen. Given that nanomaterials are usually highly crystallized and their size is smaller than the Bohr radius, it is possible that right after light absorption, the electrons are directly pumped to the surface of the nanomaterial.10,12 In this case, charge trapping and recombination in the bulk could be avoided. (d) Higher solubility. Compared to the bulk materials, nanomaterials normally display higher solubility due to their higher surface energy and higher surface area. The higher solubility of nanomaterials can provide a clear solution of catalyst, reducing light-scattering of bigger particles, and increasing the apparent quantum efficiency, which is defined by the ratio of the amount of the hydrogen generated over the amount of light irradiated. (e) Tunable electronic structure.10–15 Owing to the effect of quantum confinement, the electronic structure of nanomaterials can be tuned by changing their sizes. So it is possible that some of the bulk semiconductors with small band gap that are not suitable for solar hydrogen generation can be explored to generate hydrogen if their sizes are controlled to the desired values. (f) Plasmonic resonance assisted charge injection and separation. Small noble metal nanoparticles on the semiconductor surface can help electron transfer from the catalyst to reduce water into hydrogen. The electrons in the conduction band of these noble metal nanoparticles can oscillate collectively from one surface to the other surface under sunlight, thus inducing strong optical absorption, called plasmon absorption. If the structures of such metal–semiconductor nanocomposites are well designed, the plasmon-enhanced absorption of metal nanoparticles can inject electrons to the conduction band of the semiconductor.

2.3 Selected recent progress

In 1972, Fujishima and Honda reported the photocatalytic hydrogen generation on a TiO2 single crystal under UV light irradiation.44 A series of studies have been conducted following that, and TiO2 has become one of the most effective semiconductor photocatalysts.45–62

However, the electronic band gap in the ultraviolet (UV) wavelength regime of TiO2 has largely limited the overall photocatalytic efficiency. Narrowing the band gap of TiO2 is vital to achieve efficient absorption of sunlight and high efficiency for solar hydrogen production. Various metal and nonmetal elements have been doped into the lattice of TiO2 lattice and visible-light absorption has been obtained.63–108 For example, in the early 1990s Choi et al. conducted a systematic study of TiO2 nanoparticles doped with 21 metal ions and found that the photoreactivity, charge carrier recombination rates, and interfacial electron transfer rates were significantly influenced by the metal ion dopants.71 Anpo et al. found that the absorption band of Cr-ion-implanted TiO2 shifted smoothly toward the visible light region and the extent of the red shift depended on the amount of metal ion dopant, while impregnated or chemically Cr-ion-doped TiO2 showed no shift in the absorption edge of TiO2; however, a new absorption band appeared at around 420 nm as a shoulder peak due to the formation of an impurity energy level within the band gap, and its intensity increased with the number of Cr ions.84 The absorption spectra that shifted to a lower energy region with an increase in the dopant concentration were also found for V-, Mn-, or Fe-doped TiO2.68,71,74 This red shift was attributed to the charge-transfer transition between the d electrons of the dopant and the CB (or VB) of TiO2. Metal ion doped TiO2 prepared by ion implantation was found to have a large shift in the absorption band toward the visible light region with various transition-metal ion dopants such as V, Cr, Mn, Fe, and Ni, and the order of the effectiveness in the red shift followed V > Cr > Mn > Fe > Ni.79–82 Nonmetal doped TiO2 normally has a color from light yellow to brown or even light gray, with the onset of the absorption spectra red shifted to longer wavelengths.89–116 Asahi and co-workers calculated the electronic band structures of anatase TiO2 with different substitutional dopants, including C, N, F, P, or S, using the FLAPW method in the framework of the local density approximation (LDA).89Fig. 4 shows the density of states (DOSs) for TiO2 doped different non-metal elements and pure TiO2.89

In Asahi's report, the substitutional doping of N was the most effective in the bandgap narrowing among other N species because its p states mixed with O 2p states, while the molecularly existing species, e.g., NO and N2 dopants, and interstitial species, gave rise to the bonding states below the O 2p valence bands and antibonding states deep in the bandgap (Ni and Ni+s), and were well screened and hardly interacted with the band states of TiO2.89 In N-doped TiO2 nanomaterials, the bandgap absorption onset shifted 600 nm from 380 nm,92,110 with primary optical absorption increase between 400 and 500 nm, compared to the 500 nm absorption due to the oxygen defects in TiO2.109 N–F-co-doped TiO2 prepared by spray pyrolysis absorbed light up to 550 nm in the visible light spectrum.106 Yuan et al. found that in nitrogen (N)-doped TiO2 samples the N was presented in the state of both molecularly chemisorbed N2 and substituted N; while both of them contributed to the response to visible light, the substituted N was responsible for the hydrogen evolution under visible light.110

3. Nanomaterials for solar electricity generation by dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs)

3.1 Principles of dye-sensitized solar cells

The general structure and the operating principle of a DSSC are illustrated in Fig. 18.2,216–219 A nanocrystalline mesoporous TiO2 film with a monolayer of the charge transfer dye attached to its surface is pasted on a transparent conductive substrate, such as a tin-doped indium oxide (ITO) or fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass. A redox electrolyte or an organic hole conductor is placed in contact with the film. Electrons in the conduction band of TiO2 can be injected from the dye by photoexcitation, and conducted to the outer circuit to drive the load and make electric power. The electrolyte, usually an organic solvent containing a redox system, such as the iodide/triiodide couple, will donate electrons to the dye to restore the dye to its original state. The restored dye can transfer electrons to TiO2 by photoexcitation again and generate electricity. The regeneration of the sensitizer by iodide prevents the recapture of conduction band electrons by the oxidized dye. Meanwhile, the electrons migrated through the external load at the counter-electrode regenerate the iodide by the reduction of triiodide. The difference between the Fermi level of TiO2 and the redox potential of the electrolyte produces a voltage under illumination. Overall, electric power is generated from sunlight without the DSSC device suffering any permanent chemical transformation.
Fig. 18 Schematic illustration of the structure and the working principle of a dye-sensitized solar cell.219 Reprinted with permission from ref. 219. Copyright 2009, American Chemical Society.

The change in the electron concentration in the nanocrystalline electron conductor that results from photo-induced charge injection from the dye is the fundamental cause for producing the photovoltage in a DSSC. Thus the photo-induced chemical potential gradients determine photovoltage generated. The difference in electron energies between the redox level and the bottom of the conduction band of the electron conductor gives the maximum photovoltage. The enthalpic and entropic driving forces that exist at the dye–electron conductor interface induce charge separation, and such a driving force at the electron conductor–contact interface facilitates charge transport. There are three key factors for efficient light harvesting and conversion: (i) the very high surface area of nanostructured oxide films for dye adsorption, (ii) efficient injection of electrons from the excited dye to the conduction band of TiO2, and (iii) transport of charge carriers through a porous structure that should take place with minimal recombination loss of electrons.218,219

The overall conversion efficiency of a dye-sensitized solar cell is expressed by the photocurrent density measured at short circuit (Jsc), the open-circuit photovoltage (Voc), the fill factor of the cell (FF), and the intensity of the incident light (Is):

 
ηglobal = JscVocFF/Is(4)

The fill factor FF is defined by the ratio of the maximum power (Pmax) of the solar cell divided by the open-circuit voltage (Voc) and the short circuit current (Isc). It can assume values between 0 and 1.

 
FF = Pmax/(IscVoc)(5)
Pmax is the product of the photocurrent and photovoltage at the voltage where the power output of the cell is maximal. The value of the fill factor reflects the extent of electrical (Ohmic) and electrochemical (overvoltage) losses occurring during the operation of a DSSC. Higher fill factors can be obtained by increasing the shunt resistance and decreasing the series resistance as well as reducing the overvoltage for diffusion and electron transfer.219

Fill factor values of 0.65–0.8 (Jsc: 16 to 22 mA cm−2 and Voc: 0.7 to 0.86 V) can be reached with the-state-of-the-art ruthenium sensitizers, under full sunlight, which corresponds to a standard air mass 1.5 global (AM 1.5 G) spectral distribution and an intensity of 1000 W cm−2. The transfer coefficient β for electron transfer from TiO2 to the electrolyte affects the fill factor of a DSSC. Its rate shows exponential voltage dependence, following a Tafel law. A better fill factor can be obtained with a larger β value for a given Voc. Theoretically, β should be 1 for electron transfer from a semiconductor to a solution redox couple. Smaller β values are usually observed due to the participation of surface states whose energy levels lie below the conduction band of TiO2. A larger β value can be associated with a shallower distribution of the surface defect states.219

The photocurrent density produced in the external circuit under monochromatic illumination of the cell divided by the photon flux that strikes the cell is called the incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE).

 
IPCE(λ) = LHE(λ)φinjηcoll(6)

Here, LHE(λ) is the light-harvesting efficiency for photons of wavelength λ; φinj is the quantum yield for electron injection from the excited sensitizer in the conduction band of the semiconductor oxide; and ηcoll is the electron collection efficiency. The IPCE value is normally plotted as a function of the excitation wavelength, which sometimes is also referred to as the “external quantum efficiency” (EQE).219

3.2 The advantages of using nanomaterials for dye-sensitized solar cells

Nanomaterials are widely applied to the electrode for DSSCs due to the following reasons.2,34–36,216–252 The first is high surface area. The high surface area of nanomaterials can provide a large surface for dye chemisorption, reducing the amount of electrode materials. The second is short charge migration length. A short charge migration length allows effective charge transport from the dye molecules to the electrode possible.220

3.3 Selected recent progress

The driving force for the development of mesoscopic semiconductor materials with an enormous internal surface area is the need for dye-sensitized solar cells to absorb far more of the incident light. Fig. 19A shows an electron micrograph of a nanocrystalline TiO2 film with a grain size in the range of 10 to 80 nm.2 The mesoporous film is interconnected to allow electronic conduction to take place. The mesoscopic pores between the particles are filled with a semiconducting or a conducting medium, such as a p-type semiconductor, a polymer, a hole transmitter or an electrolyte. A junction of extremely large contact area between two interpenetrating, individually continuous networks can be obtained. Charge carriers can percolate easily across the mesoscopic particle network, making the huge internal surface area electronically addressable. Semiconductors with a porosity of 50% have a surface area available for dye chemisorption over a thousand times that of a flat, unstructured electrode of the same size. If the dye is chemisorbed as a single molecular layer, enough can be retained on a given area of electrode to provide absorption of essentially all the incident light. The astounding photoelectrochemical performance of nanocrystalline semiconductor junctions is illustrated in Fig. 19B and C. Both electrodes are sensitized by the ruthenium complex cis-RuL2(SCN)2 (L is 2,2′-bipyridyl-4-4′-dicarboxylate). The IPCE value reaches 88% with the nanocrystalline electrode – more than 600 times of the single-crystal electrode (0.13% near 530 nm). The photocurrent in standard sunlight augments 103–104 times when passing to a nanocrystalline electrode from a single crystal. This striking improvement is due to the far better light harvesting of the dye-sensitized nanocrystalline film as compared with a flat single-crystal electrode, and to the mesoscopic film texture favoring photogeneration and collection of charge carriers.2 The mesoporosity and nanocrystallinity of the semiconductor are important not only because of the large amount of dye that can be adsorbed on the very large surface but also for two additional reasons: (a) they allow the semiconductor small particles to become almost totally depleted upon immersion in the electrolyte (allowing for large photovoltages), and (b) the proximity of the electrolyte to all particles makes screening of injected electrons, and thus their transport, possible. Charge transport in such mesoporous systems is best described by a random walk model. And the performance of the DSSCs made from the randomly oriented anatase nanocrystals can be further improved by organizing the anatase nanocrystals with the Pluronic P123 templated in forming ordered mesoporous TiO2 film.220

One dimensional (1D) nanostructures are proposed to improve the electron transport properties over the randomly distributed nanocrystalline film.220–252 1D TiO2, including nanorods, nanotubes, nanobelts, and nanofibers, has been employed to fabricate the DSSC electrode in replacing TiO2 nanocrystals, demonstrating higher efficiency.220–252 In 2005, Law et al. introduced a version of the dye-sensitized cell in which the traditional nanoparticle film was replaced by a dense array of oriented, crystalline ZnO nanowires.221 The nanowire anode was synthesized by mild aqueous chemistry and features a surface area up to one-fifth as large as a nanoparticle cell. Films constructed of oriented one-dimensional nanostructures, such as nanotube (NT) arrays, aligned perpendicular to the collecting substrate can potentially improve the charge-collection efficiency by promoting faster transport and slower recombination. A comparison of the electron pathways through nanoparticulate and nanotubular structured TiO2 is shown in the schematic diagram of Fig. 20.248 Directional electron mobility and decreased intercrystalline contacts can accelerate electron transport and lower the recombination probability. The extent to which transport or recombination could be affected by an oriented architecture depends on mechanistic considerations, such as the density and location of structural defects, the crystallinity, and the composition of the redox electrolyte.248 In 2006, Grimes et al. demonstrated the use of TiO2 nanotubes in DSSCs,224–226 and conducted a series of studies in the following years.232,233 For example, they prepared highly ordered nanotube arrays of 46 nm pore diameter, 17 nm wall thickness, and 360 nm length, grown perpendicular to a fluorine-doped tin oxide-coated glass substrate by anodic oxidation of a titanium thin film, as shown in Fig. 21A. 4. Nanomaterials for solid-state hydrogen storage

4.1 Principles of hydrogen storage

In general, hydrogen may be stored in the form of pressurized gas, liquefied hydrogen, or can be chemically or physically bonded to a suitable solid-state material. To meet the need for hydrogen storage for transportation applications, the material candidate must at least have the following characteristics: high hydrogen capacity, fast hydrogenation-dehydrogenation, easy activation and minimal deterioration during hydrogenation cycling. Of the three hydrogen-storage approaches, solid-state storage has the highest volumetric density of hydrogen. The fundamental mechanisms for solid-state hydrogen storage include chemisorption and physisorption.217,253–257

Physisorption mainly involves the adsorption of hydrogen molecules on the surface of materials through the van der Waals interaction.253–257 The van der Waals force is a weak intermolecular force. This force between the hydrogen molecule and the surface is normally in the lower kJ mol−1 range. Thus, to achieve a sufficient amount of adsorbed hydrogen, it is necessary to apply low temperatures (e.g. 77 K of liquid nitrogen temperature). Nevertheless, because of the weak molecular forces, release of physisorbed hydrogen can be fast. On the other hand, chemisorption starts with dissociation of hydrogen molecules and chemical bonding of the hydrogen atoms by integration in the lattice of a solid material, e.g. metal hydrides. This process inherently involves large enthalpy changes and normally requires high operation temperatures and a catalyst for fast hydrogen uptake and release. As a consequence, the force is more material specific compared to the case of physisorption. Fig. 25A illustrates the hydrogen adsorption/absorption process in a metal hydride form and Fig. 25B shows the potential energies of 2H + M and H2 + M reactions at a gas–metal interface. The ideal material for hydrogen storage would achieve a compromise between physisorption and chemisorption (Fig. 25C). In physisorption, hydrogen is weakly bonded to the storage material (<10 kJ mol−1), resulting in a low storage capacity at room temperature. In chemisorption, the bonding is too strong (>50 kJ mol−1), thus requiring high temperatures to release the hydrogen. The potential energy for useful molecular hydrogen physisorption on a metal surface at low temperature falls in the range 10–60 kJ mol−1. This is much lower than the energy required for chemisorption of atomic hydrogen that usually occurs at high temperatures. Among the metals/materials that can effectively adsorb and desorb hydrogen are Li, Al, Mg and their alloys whose hydrides can store 4–6 MJ kg−1 gravimetric energy density or 2–3 MJ L−1 volumetric energy density.257

4.2 The advantages of using nanomaterials for hydrogen storage

Nanostructured materials may offer advantages for molecular hydrogen storage by providing high surface area, or encapsulation or trapping hydrogen in microporous media.253–258 An increased surface area and porosity in nanostructures offer additional binding sites on the surface and in the pores that could increase storage mainly through physisorption. The possibility of storing a significant amount of hydrogen on high surface area density materials has been a key driver in the investigation of the hydrogen sorption properties of nanotubes, graphite sheets, metal–organic frameworks and template ordered porous carbons. Nanostructured carbons, zeolites, metal–organic frameworks, clathrates and polymers with intrinsic microporosity are examples of the investigated physisorption materials. However, using porous nanostructured materials, in general, can reduce the gravimetric and the volumetric storage densities.

For chemisorption, the low rate of bulk hydride sorption and the high temperatures required for gas release are among the major obstacles in the development of materials for hydrogen storage.217,253–258 Nanomaterials can increase the kinetics of uptake and release; the sorption characteristics can be fine-tuned by controlling the particle sizes. With a metal hydride (whether the hydrogen is stored interstitially or held by complex ligands) the kinetics of both absorption and desorption, and the heat transfer, can be improved by an order of magnitude simply by reducing the grain size of the metal.259Fig. 26 clarifies why nanomaterials are superior to macroscopic bulk materials in absorbing hydrogen.256,257 For the bulk hydride, the sorption rate is small, release temperature is too high, and the poor heat transfer can lead to process interruption. Fast hydrogen exchange can be achieved by using nanoparticles in reducing diffusion distances for hydrogen. The increased porosity and smaller size lead to increased diffusion-limited rates. Thus, increased kinetics and uptake can be obtained by reducing the grain and particle sizes. Meanwhile, the surface-to-volume ratio increases for nanoparticles. The low coordination surface atoms increase adsorption sites and induce weaker binding energy in small metallic clusters. Surface energies and material properties at the nanoscale offer ways to tune the energetics of absorption and desorption to reduce the release temperature and speed up release process.256,257

5. Nanomaterials for electric energy storage with lithium ion rechargeable batteries

5.1 Principles of a lithium ion rechargeable battery

Rechargeable lithium batteries provide the capability of delivering the stored chemical energy as electrical energy with high conversion efficiency and no gaseous exhausts. The motivation for developing battery technologies based on Li metal as anode is to achieve electric storage systems with high energy density. Li is the most electropositive (−3.04 V versus standard hydrogen electrode) and the lightest metal. The high stored volume and gravimetric energy density of a Li battery has enabled realization of cellular telephones and laptop computers. Nevertheless, the issues that continue to plague the development of Li batteries for the potential mass market of electric vehicles include cost, safety, and charge/discharge rates, in addition to stored energy density and battery life.283–299

A single lithium battery cell is comprised of a positive electrode (cathode), a negative electrode (anode), a separator, and an electrolyte that conducts lithium ions. The separator isolates the cathode from the anode. It is normally a membrane which allows lithium ion transport while preventing electron crossover from one side to the other (lithium ion permeable and electron impermeable). The anode and cathode are connected through an external circuit. To circumvent the safety issues surrounding the use of Li metal, substituting metallic Li for a second insertion material leads to the so-called Li-ion or rocking-chair technology (vs. lithium battery). The principle of rocking-chair batteries is shown in Fig. 34.288,293,296 The rechargeable lithium-ion battery does not contain lithium metal. It is a lithium-ion device, comprising a graphite negative electrode (anode), a nonaqueous liquid electrolyte, and a positive electrode (cathode) formed from layered LiCoO2. On charging, lithium ions are deintercalated from the layered LiCoO2 intercalation host, pass through the electrolyte, and are intercalated between the graphite layers in the anode. Discharge reverses this process. The electrons travel through the external circuit. The performance (cell potential, capacity or energy density) of the battery is related to the intrinsic property of the materials that form the positive and negative electrodes. The cycle-life and lifetime depend on the nature of the interfaces between the electrodes and electrolyte, whereas safety is a function of the stability of the electrode materials and interfaces. Charge rates are typically given in terms of the fraction of the theoretical capacity that can be charged over a period of one hour per gram of active material at a given charge current. The theoretical capacity of the fully lithiated alloy Li4.4Si is 4212 mA h g−1, an order of magnitude higher than graphite (372 mA h g−1, in the form of LiC6). For example, a rate of 1 C corresponds to a charge current of 4.2 A per gram of silicon.289,290,296

5.2 The advantages of using nanomaterials for lithium ion rechargeable batteries

The advantages of using nanostructured inorganic materials for lithium ion batteries include the following. (1) The reduced dimension increases the rate of lithium insertion/removal and electron transport. The small size of nanostructured materials allows shorter transport distances for lithium-ion within the particles. The diffusion time constant is given by t = L2/2D, where L is the diffusion length and D the diffusion constant. The intercalation time decreases with the square of the particle size. (2) The high surface area of nanostructures permits a high contact area of the electrode with the electrolyte and hence a high lithium-ion flux cross-section coefficient on the interface per mass of the electrode material. (3) The chemical potentials for lithium ions and electrons may be modified by tuning the size of the materials on the nanoscale. This can result in a change of electrode potential (thermodynamics of the reaction). (4) Nanostructures can provide facile strain relaxation, tolerate large volume change during lithiation and delithiation to prevent electrode pulverization and maintain effective electrical contact, allowing a large range of solid solutions.287,288,296

5.3 Selected recent progress

In lithium-ion containing liquid organic electrolytes, if lithium is polarized to a sufficient negative potential, lithium ion will be able to react with Si or metal M (M = Sn, Mg, Ca, Al, Pb, etc.) to form intermetallic compounds. Compared with the graphite (372 mA h g−1), lithium alloys such as Li22Sn5 and Li22Si5 have much higher capacity (990 mA h g−1 for Li22Sn5 and 4200 mA h g−1 for Li22Si5).288 Poor cycle stability of the alloy electrode and the pulverization of the material occur due to large volumetric changes induced by the repeated alloying and de-alloying.288 If the electrodes can be engineered with morphologies such as nanowire arrays or ordered mesoporous structures on the nanometer scale to provide the necessary volume of free space to accommodate expansion during alloying, the material stress can be minimized to avoid electrode pulverization and to achieve greater reversibility and cycling stability.288,300

Recent progress on nanostructured silicon electrode is a good example.300–308 The low cycling stability of bulk silicon has limited the commercial use of silicon in lithium cells, although the theoretical capacity of fully lithiated silicon alloy Li4.4Si is 4212 mA h g−1, an order of magnitude higher than graphite (372 mA h g−1). During lithiation, the large volume change (>300%) leads to high internal stress and electrode pulverization. This causes subsequent loss of electrical contact between the active material and current collector, and leads to poor reversibility and rapidly declining capacity. At high charge/discharge rates, the capacity of silicon electrodes decreases due to low lithium ionic conductivity in silicon and sluggish mass transfer at the electrode interface.296,300–308 Silicon films with a strong adhesion to the substrate have been shown to undergo anisotropic volume changes. Silicon films with thicknesses of 50 nm or less exhibit the best reversibility and highest capacities. Fig. 35 shows the electric storage performance of an amorphous n-type silicon film. On the other hand, Olivine LiFePO4 has been considered a promising cathode material for large scale lithium-ion batteries used for hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) or electric vehicles (EVs).283,288,292,317–327 The material has low toxicity, potential for low cost, long cycle ability and high safety. In the early stage of LiFePO4 research, many reported that the rate performance of LiFePO4 was greatly limited by the slow diffusion of lithium ion in LiFePO4, and/or the low electronic conductivity. Transports of lithium ions and of electrons at the interface are quite important for the power performance of LiFePO4. Besides the lithium ion insertion–extraction or alloying processes, a new interfacial charge-storage mechanism has been found in the reactions of transition metal oxides with lithium ions. It involves the formation/decomposition of Li2O, accompanying the electrochemical reduction/oxidation of the metal nanoparticles in the range 1–5 nm:328

 
MO + 2Li+ + 2e− ↔ Li2O + M(16)

Transition metal oxides MO (M is Co, Ni, Cu or Fe) exhibit specific capacities of 700 mA h g−1 with 100% capacity retention for up to 100 charge/discharge cycles. Reversible decomposition of Li2O can be catalyzed by the nanoparticles of the transition metal (in the range 1–5 nm) produced in the charge process. Although the mechanism of the conversion reactions of the transition metal oxides is not fully understood, it is believed that the electrochemically driven size confinement of the nanoscale metal particles is the key point to enhance their electrochemical activities toward the formation/decomposition of Li2O.328 The conversion reactions of the transition metal nitrides,329 fluorides330 and phosphides331 have been reported.

Nanomaterials exhibit the desirable advantages such as improved lithium ion diffusion rate in the solid phase of the host compound; enhanced reaction rate caused by larger interface between the electrode and the electrolyte due to a high surface area and porosity of the nanostructures; and potential new reactions that are not possible with the bulk materials.105 However, the agglomeration of the materials are induced by the large surface area and high surface energy of the nanomaterials. Therefore, the “kinetically stabilized” nanomaterials should be considered, which, in fact, have been developed by using heterogeneous nanostructures and nano/micro hierarchical structures. Meanwhile, the synthesis of the nanomaterials is usually difficult due to complex processes, high costs, and difficulties in controlling the nano-dimensions. The widely applied synthesis methods including sol–gel, radio-frequency magnetron sputtering, mechano-chemical, etc., still need to be optimized to obtain the more competitive battery products. Recent developments in nanotechnology have opened new opportunities for the large scale applications (roll-to-roll, inkjet printing…) in various fields of nanomaterials. Therefore, it is of great importance, but still a challenge to explore the nanomaterials for high performance and practical lithium ion batteries. It has also been noticed that many LiFePO4 producers can produce this material on a commercial base that achieve >160 mAh g−1 of capacity based on half-cell configurations.332

6. Conclusion

Over the past two decades, nanomaterials have been studied intensively for their potentials for solar-driven generation and effective storage of hydrogen fuel and electricity. The fundamental properties of nanomaterials, i.e., their small size and high surface area, bring in tremendous benefits for applications. The small size offers shorter charge migration distance within the particle, whereas the large surface area provides a large reaction area and high charge and ion transfer rates. The very high surface-to-volume ratio of a nanomaterial increases the adsorption sites as well as low coordination of the surface atoms. Tolerance of structure change and strain relaxation exhibited in many nanomaterials can yield a large range of composition tunability and good stability of the material. However, the large surface area and high surface energy may also induce significant side reactions and agglomeration of the material. In addition, large scale fabrication of nanomaterials has been elusive due to the complex processes, high costs, and difficulties in controlling the homogeneity in general; recent development has opened new opportunities for large scale applications through processes such as roll-to-roll and inkjet printing nanomanufacturing. It is of great importance to explore possible cost-effective approaches to solving the challenges faced by the community of nanomaterials for their better applications to renewable energy production and storage.
