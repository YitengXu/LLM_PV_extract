Abstract

Tripletâ€“triplet annihilation upconversion (TTA-UC) has been attracting attention in various fields as a promising tool to efficiently generate a shorter-wavelength photon than incident light. Compared to conventional UC technologies (e.g., lanthanide-doped inorganic nanoparticles), TTA-UC has advantages of tunable spectral range and high UC efficiency (>1â€“40%) at non-coherent solar irradiance (Iex < 1â€“102 mW cmâˆ’2). This review article provides a succinct overview of the historical background and recent strategies for solving a critical problem (i.e. oxygen quenching) of TTA-UC to improve the efficiency and to expand its applicability in various fields. We also introduce recent strategies to develop host materials (e.g., films, polymers, nanocapsules, gels) for overcoming a quenching problem, before we detail recent progress in applications of TTA-UC materials involving bioimaging, biomedicines, photocatalysts, photovoltaics and OLEDs. This review article aims to highlight the challenges and recent advances in the field of TTA-UC, providing the readers with guidance and opportunities to join the research.

1. Introduction

Most luminescent materials show Stokes-emission, absorbing high-energy photons and emitting low-energy photons. In contrast, photon energy upconversion (UC) is an anti-Stokes process in which two or more lower-energy photons are converted into a higher-energy photon. For example, through UC, near-infrared (NIR) light can be converted into visible (Vis) light, or Vis light can be converted into ultraviolet (UV) light. UC has mainly been implemented using lanthanide-based inorganic nanoparticles (e.g., NaYF4:Er3+, Yb3+). However, lanthanide-based UC has some drawbacks such as narrow absorption bands, a requirement for an excitation source with a high power density (Iex > 103â€“105 mW cmâˆ’2), and a relatively low UC quantum yield. To overcome these limitations of inorganic UC, researchers have turned their attention to organic-based UC via tripletâ€“triplet annihilation (TTA-UC).

TTA-UC has appealing characteristics compared to lanthanide-based UC, such as implementation under a noncoherent irradiation source with a low excitation power density (Iex < 102 mW cmâˆ’2), broad absorbance range, high quantum yield, and tunable absorption/emission wavelength. TTA-UC was first reported in 1962 by Parker and Hatchard but remained dormant until the revival by Baluschev and coworkers in 2003.15,16 After then, research on TTA-UC has widened to quantification of detailed TTA-UC mechanisms,19,20 computational simulation related to TTA-UC,23â€“26 and development of new chromophores for TTA-UC28â€“32 within an active research period of less than 20 years. The substance of the developed UC materials is also very diverse, including solutions, polymeric solids, micro-/nanocapsules, nanofibers, and so on. In addition, TTA-UC is applied to various fields utilizing light such as bioimaging, biomedicines, photocatalysts, photovoltaics, and OLEDs (Scheme 1). In this review, we first describe the general mechanism of TTA-UC, and in the following section, recent advances in the host materials and their applicable research fields.

2. The basic theory of TTA-UC and quantum yield

TTA-UC is achieved through several photochemical steps via chromophore pairs consisting of a sensitizer (donor) and an acceptor (emitter or annihilator) (Fig. 1). â‘  Initially, the sensitizer absorbs a photon and then populates a singlet excited state (1S*); â‘¡ then a triplet excited state (3S*) forms through intersystem crossing (ISC). In the absence of a triplet quencher such as oxygen, 3S* spontaneously decays to the ground state with phosphorescence emission, or decays non-radiatively. â‘¢ Furthermore, when acceptors with an appropriate triplet energy level exist with sensitizers, 3S* transfers its energy to 1A0 through tripletâ€“triplet energy transfer (TET), resulting in the formation of a triplet excited state acceptor (3A*). â‘£ 3A* undergoes tripletâ€“triplet annihilation (TTA) with another 3A* generated by the same processes to form a singlet excited state acceptor (1A*). â‘¤ Finally, anti-Stokes delayed fluorescence is observed from 1A* upon its radiative relaxation. During the whole TTA-UC process, molecular oxygen can quench the triplet energy of the UC chromophores; thus oxygen must be removed from the TTA-UC host material.

To successfully implement TTA-UC, several essential prerequisites must be met. First, singlet and triplet states of the sensitizer must exist between the singlet and triplet states of the acceptor, which is sometimes expressed as the energy state of the sensitizer is sandwiched with the energy state of the acceptor. Moreover, the singlet energy level of the acceptor must be lower than two times the triplet energy level of the acceptor (3A* + 3A* > 1A*). Otherwise, the high energy generated by the TTA process cannot reach the singlet state of the acceptor. Furthermore, a high ISC yield of the sensitizer is preferred to produce a sufficient quantity of 3S* and consequently 3A*. The long triplet lifetime of chromophores (more than microseconds) provides enough time for the TET and TTA processes to occur, resulting in effective TTA-UC. Finally, since both TET and TTA are generally based on Dexter-type energy transfer, TTA-UC processes are greatly affected by the diffusivity of the matrix and the chromophore should be within 1 nm.50,51 In general, the higher diffusivity of the chromophores in the matrix results in higher TTA-UC efficiency. This is the reason that many TTA-UC studies have been conducted in liquid solvents, and even in the solid phase, flexible polymers with relatively low Tg (i.e. elastic polyurethane) are preferred as a TTA-UC matrix. However, it is noteworthy that there are several cases that reported TTA-UC in highly diffusion-limited conditions (e.g. crystal, MOFs, on surfaces, etc.) wherein the UC mechanism was based on triplet self-exchange or triplet energy migration between highly concentrated and/or aligned chromophores, rather than the energy transfer by molecular diffusion.52â€“54

The quantum yield which is defined as the incident photon-to-luminescent electron ratio, is a key parameter used to evaluate the efficiency of TTA-UC. The quantum yield used in the TTA-UC field can be classified in various terms depending on which standard for quantum yield is used.58 For instance, the number of upconverted states, the number of upconverted photons generated, and the number of upconverted photons observed can be the standard for quantum yield. During the TTA-UC process, 3A* can decay in two different ways: pseudo-first-order decay including either radiative or non-radiative decay by itself, and second-order decay by TTA. To efficiently implement TTA-UC, the latter is preferred and is greatly affected by the incident power density.19,20,60 At a low excitation power density, the dependency of the emission intensity on the incident power intensity (i.e., excitation power dependency) is quadratic since the concentration of 3A* is not sufficient; thus, then 3A* cannot undergo TTA sufficiently during its lifetime and decays spontaneously. However, at a high excitation power density, enough 3A* is generated, so that the TTA-based second-order decay becomes dominant and the excitation power dependency becomes linear. Upon plotting the incident power density and emission in a logâ€“log graph, the change from a quadratic to linear dependency is observed (Fig. 2A). In other words, in the region where the slope is 2, 3A* is not consumed via TTA, and in the region where the slope is 1, most 3A* is consumed via TTA. This phenomenon is very unique to TTA-UC, and it can be used to determine whether TTA-UC is effectively occurring. The point where slopes 2 and 1 meet is called the threshold intensity (Ith) and is directly affected by the rate of spontaneous decay of the acceptor, the absorption coefficient of the sensitizer, the efficiency of TET, and the rate of TTA.19,20,60,61 More specifically, the aforementioned parameters can be affected by the following factors: the concentration of UC chromophores, the choice of media, the presence of oxygen, the singlet branching ratio, the triplet lifetime of the acceptor, the excitation wavelength, the ISC yield of the sensitizer, and the operating temperature, and so on.19,20 A low Ith means that TTA-UC occurs effectively in that system under a low excitation power density. Similarly, the UC quantum yield also increases according to incident light and then tends to converge to a constant value through the transition region (Fig. 2B).

3. TTA-UC materials

3.1. TTA-UC in macro-/micromaterials

Early TTA-UC studies were conducted using low-viscosity solutions such as dimethylformamide (DMF), acetonitrile, and toluene, which are capable of maximizing the energy transfer of UC chromophores.71â€“73 However, solution-state TTA-UC in these organic solvents suffered from triplet energy quenching by molecular oxygen, resulting in a decrease in the overall TTA-UC efficiency. Therefore, in most studies published between 2000 and 2010, the solution had to be degassed to replace the dissolved oxygen with an inert gas (nitrogen or argon) by gas purging or freezeâ€“pumpâ€“thaw methods. Due to the inconvenience of excluding oxygen, practical applications of TTA-UC in solution are challenging. In 2012, Kim and coworkers first reported efficient TTA-UC without the degassing process in a mixture of hexadecane and polyisobutylene.4 Similarly, in later published studies, TTA-UC under ambient conditions was implemented using oxygen scavengers such as a mixture of soybean oil/bovine serum albumin, hyperbranched unsaturated polyphosphates, and oleic acid.74â€“76 Although using an oxygen scavenging matrix eliminates the need for a deoxygenation process, the low processability and applicability of solution-state TTA-UC were highlighted as problems in exploiting TTA-UC.

Accordingly, studies using solid-state TTA-UC materials have been actively developed since solid-state TTA-UC was first reported by Castellano and coworkers in 2007.77 Although solid media inhibit the diffusion and energy transfer of UC chromophores, energy transfer can occur inside the solid media at the limited boundary, resulting in TTA-UC. Monguzzi and coworkers evaluated the effect of the glass transition temperature (Tg) and diffusion length (LT) by adjusting the polymerization of poly(n-alkyl acrylates). The TTA-UC polymer with a lower Tg showed higher UC efficiency owing to its longer LT.78

Polyurethane (PU) has advantages in that it has an elastic structure and low oxygen-permeability due to its high cross-linking rate and it can be facilely synthesized at room temperature. Accordingly, many studies have adapted PU as a TTA-UC host material.3,18,38,79,80 Kim and coworkers reported notably efficient TTA-UC in flexible and transparent PU films (Fig. 3A). The benchmark red-to-blue UC pair (PdTPBP/perylene) was blended in commercially available PU and a UC film was fabricated using a spin-coater. Then poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) and polyurethane acrylate layers were further spin-coated to block oxygen invasion. The material showed a high UC efficiency of 7% and a long shelf life despite a thickness of 15 Î¼m.9

Although rubbery polymers are favorable for implementing TTA-UC, rigid polymers such as poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) can also be used to implement TTA-UC at a high concentration of UC chromophores. Simon and coworkers suggested a new methodology to achieve TTA-UC in glassy polymers by covalently binding acceptors to the polymeric methyl methacrylate backbone (Fig. 3B). They achieved green-to-blue UC at a low power density of 32 mW cmâˆ’2 in a UC film containing a sensitizer and an acceptor covalently bound to the polymeric backbone.17 Turshatov and coworkers introduced an efficient TTA-UC polymeric host, poly(olefin sulfone), at Tg of 315 K under oxygen-rich ambient conditions (Fig. 3C). They assessed TTA-UC efficiency using four kinds of poly(olefin sulfone), and a maximum quantum yield of 2.1% was measured in poly(1-dodecene sulfone).40

There have also been studies on aqueous-phase TTA-UC in microparticles. Kim and coworkers reported the first encapsulation of TTA-UC oil in a rigid polymer shell using microfluidics. The core-UC oil/shell-polymer structured UC emulsions were stable not only when dispersed in water or but also in the dry state.4 In a follow-up study, they produced a more complicated TTA-UC double and triplet emulsion that maximized the TTA-UC efficiency (Fig. 3D).46 Elsa and coworkers designed a single-step microfluidic emulsification using a brominated hydrocarbon oil-based core to produce ultrathin oilâ€“water-oilâ€“water configured microemulsions, resulting in an enhancement of the UC quantum yield (Fig. 3E).55

However, microparticles synthesized using microfluidic technology had a limitation in that they cannot reload other UC oils. Kim and coworkers reported aqueous red-to-blue TTA-UC in hollow mesoporous silica microcapsules (HMSM) by postencapsulation (Fig. 3F). They infused TTA-UC oil into presynthesized HMSM through the mesochannels, floated it on water, and then centrifuged it to obtain an aqueous suspension of UC oil-laden HMSMs. HMSMs stabilizes the TTA-UC oil in water, and UC oil-laden HMSMs can be washed using an organic solvent and then reversibly filled with another TTA-UC oil.59

3.2. TTA-UC in nanomaterials

The last few decades have seen enormous growth in the field of upconversion materials, from fundamental principal research to applications in biochemical sensing, bioimaging, biotherapy, and energy-environmental technology. In particular, to the achieve multipurpose biological/photonic applications of TTA-UC materials, researchers have attempted several methodologies to fabricate various forms of nanostructures such as silica shell-coated nanocapsules,5,8,81 polymer-encapsulated nanocapsules,14,47,82 electrospun nanofibers,45,83,84 and biomaterial-loaded nanomaterials.35 In this section, we will briefly introduce these preparation techniques for various applications.

As shown in Fig. 4A, to transfer the annihilation upconversion process into a water environment while removing the possibility of oxygen quenching by the fabricated TTA-UC solution embedding nanomaterials, silica encapsulation has been developed for water-soluble nanoparticles. Kwon and coworkers reported multifunctional silica-based nanocapsules to encapsulate two distinct TTA-UC chromophore pairs (PdTPBP and BPEA; PdTPBP and Perylene).8 After self-assembling micelles were formed in the core, silane reagent treatment was used to construct an inorganic outer shell. The solubilized organic materials in the core part were coated with rigid silica shells. The outstanding properties of silica surfaces, such as facile surface treatment, biocompatibility, and large surface area, have been demonstrated in several studies. The synthesized silica shell constructed by the microemulsion method can penetrate the UV-VIS-IR wavelength range, and can be utilized as the source of upconversion. The surface of the silica shell was immobilized with different species of cancer-targeting peptides, and each nanocapsule detected different cancer cells by upconverted emission (BPEA: green, colorectal cancer; perylene: blue, breast cancer).

Recently, polymer shell coating strategies have attracted much attention for facile synthetic processes. General polymer-encapsulated nanomaterials showed remarkable biocompatibility for sufficient cellular uptake, facile surface functionalization for the detection of target molecules, and notable photostability suitable for long-term storage. Based on these advantages, polymer encapsulated nanoparticles were applied in phototriggering polymeric micellar nanoparticles, as reported by Kohane and coworkers (Fig. 4B).14 The upconversion chromophore pair was located in the hydrophobic core of a polymeric micelle self-assembled from the block copolymer poly(d,l-lactic acid)â€“poly(ethylene oxide) (PLAâ€“PEG). For micelle formation, both the reagent photosensitizer-acceptor solution and PLAâ€“PEG were simply mixed and rotary evaporation was used to slowly evaporate the solvent and obtain TTA-UC materials containing micelles. Finally, the obtained PLAâ€“PEG-encapsulated micelles consisted of inner hydrophobic dyes and an outer hydrophilic PEG chain moiety. Utilizing a highly stable and low-toxicity system, further experiments were conducted in aqueous medium.

The above approaches should include organic solvents, which exhibit volatility and toxicity, during the nanomaterial construction process. Various studies have reported limitations, such as encaging organic molecules.5,8,14,41,47,82 Encapsulating upconversion chromophores that were dissolved in an organic solvent prevents molecular collision that leads to energy transfer and photoluminescence efficiency. Along with maintenance of an adequate distance between chromophores under aqueous conditions, to assign biocompatibility, co-assembly of biological peptides and TTA-UC chromophores was attempted by Yan and coworkers (Fig. 4C).35 Multicomponent coordination self-assembly synthesis has been utilized frequently in the field of biomedical nanotechnology and diagnostic systems.85,86 In particular, when photosensitizers are used, aggregation interferes with the proposed reaction. Peptide-modulated self-assembly with upconversion materials can isolate photosensitizers and emitters from each other, resulting in self-aggregation and retaining the photoluminescence intensity. In addition, amphiphilic peptides prevent oxygen quenching of inner organic molecules and leads to a stable state under aqueous conditions. By diversifying the concentration and reaction ratio of molecules and peptides, the upconversion efficiency and upconversion intensity increase because of strong hydrophobicity. The proposed system can be used in photodynamic therapy, bioimaging and biotherapeutic approaches.

In addition, along with applications in biotechnology, upconversion materials have also been developed in the form of nanofibers. For further application, various shapes of the substrates were fabricated. As a liquid matrix, the materials could not be used in films, gels or any other solid materials. By combining the emulsion-solvent evaporation process and electrospinning, nanofibers consisting of both an upconversion material and a polymer (PVA) were prepared by Turshatov and coworkers.45 Electrospun nanofibers embedding upconversion materials provided a double protection layer composed of a nanoparticle shell and a polymer shell (Fig. 4D). These remarkable advantages increased the air stability of the upconversion materials.

3.3. TTA-UC in other materials

In addition to the above TTA-UC application, a variety of new attempts have been made to overcome the limitations of UC materials in aqueous environments such as gels (hydrogels,10,43,87,88 organogels89â€“91), metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs)27,92,93 and binary crystalline solid types.44 Various gel types allow unique air-stable TTA-UC by proper stabilization with hydrogen bonding networks in TTA-UC chromophores.90â€“92,94 Furthermore, Yanai and Kimizuka et al. proposed a new hydrogel type consisting of gelatin, Triton X-100 and emitter/acceptor pairs as biopolymers, surfactants and chromophores.10 General interiors in the aqueous phase, such as micelles and lipid bilayers were used to increase the solubility of hydrophobic TTA-UC dyes, but inevitably, high concentrations of oxygen scavengers or degassing procedures should be added. Herein, the hydrogel is self-assembled in water to give a hierarchical structure with hydrophobic domains. It achieved a UC emission intensity of 13.5% and long acceptor triplet lifetimes of 4.9 ms even in an air-stable state (Fig. 5A). Interestingly, constant UC emission intensity of an air-saturated G-TX-PtOEP-DPAS hydrogel was confirmed even after 10 heating/cooling cycles, which led to a dis/coassembly transition. Moreover, the unique properties of organogel, another type of TTA-UC gel, increased the average diffusion distance of triplet molecules (âˆ¼300 nm) due to all TTA-UC occurrences in the liquid cavities.89,95 Shadab et al. created another form of annihilator with MOFs to reduce phase segregation and dye aggregation. They combined a Pd porphyrin as a fixed sensitizer in the framework walls and DPA as an annihilator, which has mobility in the channels (Fig. 5B).27 Meinardi, who has performed similar research before, promoted diffusion of excitons with nMOF nanocrystals to achieve a negligible spontaneous recombination rate and a threshold-less upconverter.92 Another example of utilizing the migration of triplet excitons through a bimolecular process, similar to the aforementioned MOFs, is binary crystals. Kenji et al. conducted a simple synthesis under rapid drying conditions to generate binary crystalline solids with alkyl-strapped derivatives (C7-sDPA).44 A general DPA emitter shows a spherical structure, while C7-sDPA has rectangular- and petal-shaped crystals (Fig. 5C). The migration speed of triplet excitons could be improved with this crystalline regularity and dense packing of binary crystals. Therefore, these authors identified centimeter-sized large-area UC emissions at an excitation intensity similar to that of sunlight (1 mW cmâˆ’2) and achieved up to 20% UC quantum yield under aerobic conditions.

4. Applications

4.1. Biological applications

4.1.1. Bioimaging (in vitro). The aforementioned advantages of TTA-UC materials are attractive characteristics for biological applications especially for bioimaging.2,35,90,91,96 The stable dispersion state under aqueous conditions and the versatile shell of TTA-UC materials for functionalization and bioconjugation allowed the demonstration of the most common bioimaging applications both in vivo and in vitro. Optical imaging is the most accurate method to observe the metabolism and physiological behavior of cells.97 However, among the whole spectrum of light, living tissues absorb the UV-VIS excitation that is required by most fluorescent probes.2 To avoid this phenomenon, the power of irradiation is increased and background autofluorescence from the tissues increases and reduces the signal-to-noise ratio. Through the wavelength conversion process by upconversion materials, which convert lower-energy irradiation to higher-energy emission, TTA-UC materials have overcome the abovementioned problems.

In this section, the overall research on bioimaging applications will be summarized. Beverina and coworkers prepared water-dispersible TTA-UC-active nanomicelles using highly biocompatible materials for advanced theranostics (Fig. 6A).2 The synthesized nanomicelles showed efficient emission at low excitation power, limiting damage to living tissues. Biocompatible materials consisting of the nanomicells enabled multichannel, high-contrast optical imaging of 3T3 cells using only a single excitation light source (green laser excitation at 532 nm). As shown in Fig. 6B, blue light (upconverted emission from PtOEP and DPA; Î»em = 435 nm) was acquired in the cytoplasmic region around the cell nucleus, and red light was generated from the dye (phalloidin positive F actin). As seen from the merged confocal fluorescence image, the upconversion nanomicelles were successfully internalized into the cytoplasm. In addition, upconversion can also be utilized for imaging perinuclear localization with good stability in biological media.

Furthermore, Lin and coworkers imaged live cells by utilizing the quenching mechanism of the TTA-UC process (2,6-diiodo-1,3,5,7-tetramethyl-8-phenyl-4,4-difluoroboradiazaindacene-Py (DIBP-Py) UC system) and monitored cell viability by imaging apoptotic cells.36 As shown in Fig. 6C, the membrane of the live cells could not be penetrated by upconversion nanoparticles (UCNPs), and there were no stained cells in the fluorescence image. Bonnet and coworkers developed block copolymer-coated upconversion nanomaterials to reduce the oxygen quenching effect and target specific cancer cells.47 By utilizing surface-treated nanomaterials, the location and number of cancer cells could be visualized. The results could help address the sensitivity issue in which TTA-UC was decreased by the presence of oxygen.

Most reported research has focused on bioimaging applications to detect the location and number of cells. TTA-UC materials can efficiently convert the deep penetrating light that possesses low energy (longer wavelength) to higher energy (shorter wavelength). Based on these properties, researchers have tried to apply upconversion materials to in vivo bioimaging. The results indicated the possibility of simultaneous multicolor imaging for numerous cancer cell types.

4.1.3. Biotherapy. Compared with other photoinduced drug-release systems, prodrug activation is limited by poor tissue penetration in in vivo applications.1 To overcome this problem, TTA-UC materials were applied because they require a low excitation power density with minimal photo-damage, offer low risk of injury to the tissue and are capable of deeper penetration. Han and coworkers used the energy of TTA-UC for the regulation of coumarin-chlorambucil (Cou-C), which can suppress tumor growth.1 Upon red (650 nm) LED irradiation of the tissue containing the prodrug-loaded TTA-UC nanocapsule (TTA-CS), the drug was successfully photocleaved and released into the tissue (Fig. 8A). Only when the mice were treated with TTA-CS and a red LED was applied was tumor growth inhibited. In addition to the preloaded prodrug, singlet oxygen can also serve as a medicine in the photodynamic therapy of tumors by inducing apoptosis and producing tumor-specific immunity. Li and coworkers prepared biocompatible host/guest nanorods for highly effective upconversion photodynamic therapy.13 Upon 650 or 808 nm laser irradiation, the prepared Tc/Pc (tetracene/pentacene) nanorods generated 1O2. The in vivo antitumor activity was monitored by the tumor volume and the body weight of mice over a period of a few days after the injection of Tc/Pc nanorods under laser irradiation. Tumor growth in mice treated with 650 nm irradiation was significantly inhibited, and the tumor inhibition rate reached 95% (Fig. 8B). This was the first application of multifunctional upconversion nanorods as an excellent antitumor system.

4.2. Environmental/energy applications

4.2.1. Environmental applications. Various applications of photocatalysts using TTA-UC films have been researched. Photocatalysts are mainly utilized under ultraviolet light irradiation, which has a high proton energy. To maximize the photocatalytic activity, photon upconversion has the potential to act as a key factor. Then, the photocatalyst can use low-energy photons to economically widen the usable solar energy absorption wavelength range. There has been much research on environmental applications aimed at decomposing environmental pollutants in the air or aqueous phase by enhancing the efficiency of the photocatalyst with TTA-UC.

Kim et al. first achieved photocatalytic degradation of indoor air pollution by applying low-energy, sub-bandgap photons with TTA-UC.3 Most indoor light irradiation is not sufficient for photocatalysts. To solve this problem, they designed a device that combines a TTA-UC polymer film and a nanodiamond (ND)-loaded WO3 photocatalyst as shown in Fig. 9A. Furthermore, Ag nanoparticle-SiO2, a plasma particle, was synthesized into the TTA-UC polymer film to enhance the decomposition efficiency of acetaldehyde (CH3CHO) which is a representative indoor air pollutant (Fig. 9B).3 Anna et al. suggested a dual-layer UC film system composed of multiple sensitizers as another promising TTA film application for indoor air purification.38 These multiple sensitizers, which consist of red-to-blue and green-to-blue UC films, can degrade acetaldehyde more than twice as much as a single sensitizer (Fig. 9C). The authors also coated a film system containing ND/K-WO3 onto fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) and measured the photocurrent under white LED excitation. These data indicate that the photocatalyst film can be activated by UC fluorescence absorption. Fig. 9D shows the highest photocurrent value of the dual-layer UC film with PdTPBPâ€“PtOEP as multiple sensitizers, which correlates with the results of acetaldehyde decomposition. The layered film system with broad light absorption is a promising strategy that is influenced by sensitizers that best harvest low-energy emission, acceptors that best activate appropriate devices and optimal ordering of the individual layers.38

4.2.2. Energy applications. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) which consist of anodes, cathodes and electrolytes, can convert solar energy to electricity by using dye molecules. All photovoltaic energy conversion devices waste the most photon energy below the bandgap, especially high-bandgap materials such as amorphous silicon, polythiophenes and Pd-halide perovskites.102 Therefore, since the first research in 2012,103 TTA-UC has been recognized as a new energy conversion technology that can greatly improve the energy conversion efficiency of most solar cell types by expanding the absorption range and by increasing the UC quantum yield under incoherent light irradiation.104â€“107 Furthermore, upconversion can be applied to photo(electro)chemical energy storage by facilitating photoinduced electron transfer.108â€“110 According to recent research, harnessing TTA-UC has made a lot of progress in applications to solar cells by optical or electrical coupling.111 Both approaches have the advantages of long triplet lifetimes and appropriate energy matching between new sensitizers with long triplet lifetimes and a new annihilator with high TTA yields but it needs further improvements due to device-relevance thresholds and commercial applications.112 To optimize hybrid research of TTA-UC and solar cells, it should focus on minimizing the reverse energy transfer from the annihilator to the sensitizer and maximizing the photon output coupling yield from the UC film to the solar cell beyond the high-efficiency UC quantum efficiency.102 Until presenting this point of view, Schmidt et al. had been actively investigating the compatibility of TTA-UC devices with solar cells.113â€“115 Schmidt et al. demonstrated a TTA-UC system that absorbs photons of infrared wavelengths (>1100 nm) and evaluated the potential of TTA-UC to improve the efficiency of solar cells. Schmidt et al. also reported that the solar cell photocurrent increased by up to 0.2% in 19 organic cells by applying the TTA-UC upconverter. Zhou et al. overcame the above transmission limitation between a sensitizer and acceptor by incorporating a singlet sensitizer (SS).6 SS-to-S singlet energy transfer and S-to-A triplet energy transfer allow cross surface energy migration, and TTA and excited-state electron transfer from 1A* to TiO2. Conclusions

In summary, the state-of-the-art TTA-UC synthesis and a wide range of applications were discussed. Although TTA-UC materials have a number of advantages, there are limitations, such as the low efficiency caused by exposure to external conditions. The oxygen quenching effect of TTA-UC emission should be considered for further practical applications designed to overcome the environmental limitations. Researchers have developed novel concepts for the fabrication of TTA-UC materials in the past few decades, such as covering an outer shell around a liquid core, embedding chromophores into a solid matrix (gel, MOF, and polymers), and binding chromophores to a polymeric backbone.

Through those efforts to improve the upconversion efficiency, integration into various fields was enabled. The morphology was investigated using various methodologies from macro-/microscale to nanoscale materials for promising applications such as bioimaging, biotherapy, and energy and environmental technologies. Recently, applications of upconversion materials have expanded from simple imaging of target materials to environmental energy conversion including solar cell technology. In the future, with the increased yield of upconversion and the potential ability to be applied to various fields, upconversion materials will be utilized in diverse ways.
