Abstract

The tannic acid (TA)-based modular-assembly strategy has exhibited great prospects for fabricating inorganicâ€“biological hybrid systems recently, albeit the technique is still at the early stage of development. Herein, we investigate this strategy in different aspects, including the material suitability, loading effects, and biocompatibility. The successful functionalization of InP nanoparticles, Bi nanospheres, and g-C3N4 by TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm and their subsequent modular assembly onto the yeast surface demonstrated the good versatility of this approach toward the different types of nanomaterials. The proper utilization of TA and nanomaterials in the surface-functionalization step was found to be critical for the material-loading effect, which could also be negatively impacted by the excessive amounts of TA and Fe3+ in the modular-assembly process. The biocompatibility of TAâ€“Fe3+-functionalized nanomaterials to living cells could be optimized via adjusting the relative ratio of TA to nanomaterials during the functionalization. A balance between the material loading and biocompatibility could also be achieved via fine-tuning the TA concentration in the biohybrid studies. The presented deep insight into the TA-based modular-assembly strategy would allow the rational construction of inorganicâ€“biological hybrid systems with a feasible pathway for the development of solar-to-chemical conversion technology, and also for the development of microbe-mediated drug delivery, tumor treatment, and environmental remediation.

1. Introduction

The inorganicâ€“biological hybrid system has emerged as a powerful platform to integrate inorganic catalysts and natural systems for efficient chemical production.1 The goals of this integration include utilizing the catalytic properties of some biological systems (e.g., microorganisms) to improve the chemical selectivity and product diversity of inorganic catalysts, and enhancing the energy-harvesting capacity of biological systems through the properties of inorganic catalysts (e.g., photosensitivity).2 In recent years, several living cell-based biohybrid systems have been successfully constructed via different strategies. For example, the microbial detoxification mechanism and the surface display technique were utilized to grow cadmium sulfide (CdS) nanoparticles (NPs) on the surface of living bacteria.3â€“8 Cross-membrane transportation can be employed to bring ligand-tagged quantum dots (QDs) into the cell for in vivo energy delivery.9 In addition, electron shuttles have been used to transfer electrons from the photosensitized NPs into living cells to sustain the metabolism.10 However, these strategies also face various challenges in practice. For instance, nanomaterials fabricated via microbial detoxification are so far limited to heavy metal sulfides. Cross-membrane transportation is potentially only effective with particle sizes that lie within the level of QDs, and the transfer efficiency of electron shuttles would be affected by the long-distance transportation between cells and nanomaterials in an aqueous suspension.2,11,12 Furthermore, the range of microbes suitable for the construction of biohybrid system is also limited to a small number of species, most of which differ from common microorganisms in subtle aspects of cellular biochemistries, such as their specific energy delivery routes, particular enzymes, or uncommon metabolic pathways.13,14 Since it is unlikely that the rational design and selection of photosensitizers and microbes could be achieved based on the above-mentioned strategies, alternative approaches that are easy to perform and benign to living cells are thus needed to interface the living microbes with light-harvesting inorganic materials.

Very recently, the TA-based modular-assembly strategy has exhibited great prospects to build biohybrid systems.15,16 TA is a polyphenol with abundant o-hydroxyl functional groups, which means it can act as a multidentate ligand to coordinate with metal ions, such as Fe3+, to form a pentagonal ring chelate. A one-step assembly method based on the excellent adhesion and film-forming ability of TAâ€“Fe3+ complex was reported in 2013.17 The surface of materials can be conformably coated with a TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm via a relatively easy and rapid process, regardless of the composition, size, or shape of the particles. Quartz crystal microbalance analysis characterized that the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm could be formed in merely 10â€“20 s.18 Therefore, the TAâ€“Fe3+coating can be applied for the surface functionalization of nanomaterials, resulting in various nanohybrid materials for application in catalysis, organic contaminants removal, and lithium-ion batteries.18â€“20 Furthermore, the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm is also beneficial to certain biological systems. TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm was coated on three types of mammalian cells and it was found that it could effectively protect the cells against damage caused by either UV irradiation or cytotoxic compounds.21 Additionally, the proliferation rate of cells could be modulated via programming the formation and degradation of the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm, a process that mimicked the sporulation and germination of the natural proliferation cycle. More importantly, the TAâ€“Fe3+ film could enhance the adhesion of functional materials onto the surface of various templates, which is a key step toward the modular assembling of superstructure composites on living cells.16 Based on this technique, TA-functionalized inorganic photosensitizer indium phosphide (InP) was successfully assembled on the surface of the widely used yeast strain Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and was then utilized for light-driven fine chemical production.22 To assist the assembly process, a biocompatible polymer poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH, which was rich in â€“NH2 groups) was applied to form a positively charged polymer layer on the yeast cell surface. A number of earlier studies also demonstrated that the enwrapping of polyelectrolyte-treated yeast cells with inorganic nanomaterials did not affect the viability of cells.23â€“25 In this milestone study, InP NPs were selected owing to their proper bandgap (Eg = 1.34 eV), excellent stability to oxygen, and good compatibility to biological system. As one of the best microbial factories, S. cerevisiae was chosen owing to its great potential for the production of various valuable chemicals through synthetic biology engineering.23,24 The TAâ€“Fe3+-functionalized InP adhered to the PAH-treated surface of the yeast cells, while maintaining its excellent photosensitizing property and achieving energy transfer.22 Compared to other methods used in the construction of biohybrid systems, the modular-assembly approach has demonstrated promising versatility in the construction of inorganicâ€“biological composites, as a wide range of existing microbial species and functional nanomaterials can be rationally selected to carry out the fabrication. In addition to the solar-to-chemical energy conversion, such rationally designed biohybrids would also possess potential in microbe-mediated drug delivery, tumor treatment, and environmental remediation. However, studies on the TA-based modular-assembly strategy are still at an early stage of development, in which the feasibility of assembling and the performance of the resultant composites require further in-depth investigation.

In this study, we rationally included Bi nanospheres and g-C3N4 in addition to InP, because Bi nanospheres (NSs) have exhibited good photocatalytic performance in recent studies,26â€“30 yet their biocompatibility in cell-based biohybrid systems has not been fully explored yet, while g-C3N4 is regarded as one of the most promising metal-free photocatalysts that has great potential for future applications in living cells-related systems.31â€“33 We systematically explored and tested the versatility of this TA-based modular-assembly strategy to prepare different materialâ€“microbial hybrid systems in three important aspects: (1) the suitability of different inorganic nanomaterials for TAâ€“Fe3+ functionalization and cell surface assembly; (2) the key factors affecting the material loading on individual cells, and (3) the biocompatibility of TAâ€“Fe3+-functionalized materials to living cells.

3. Results and discussion

The TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids were prepared by the modular assembly of TAâ€“Fe3+ surface-functionalized nanomaterials and PAH-treated yeast cells, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Initially, the free TA molecules could rapidly coordinate with Fe3+ in aqueous solutions to form a TAâ€“Fe3+ chelate complex. This complex could form a TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm and adhere to the surface of nanomaterials (Step 1).17 Second, the surface charge of yeast cells was inverted from negative to positive via PAH treatment (Step 2). Finally, the surface-functionalized nanomaterials (TAâ€“Fe3+/NM) were mixed with positively charged yeast cells to facilitate the modular assembly, resulting in the formation of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/Yeast biohybrids (Step 3).

3.1 Material feasibility for TAâ€“Fe3+ functionalization

The XRD patterns of InP NPs, Bi NSs, and g-C3N4 could be perfectly indexed to the standard diffraction peaks of zinc blende InP (JCPDS 32-0452), rhombohedral Bi (JCPDS 44-1246), and planar g-C3N4 (JCPDS 87-1526), respectively (Fig. 2Aâ€“C). The highly crystallite structures featured strong and sharp reflection peaks without additional impurity peaks observed for their TAâ€“Fe3+/NM counterparts, indicating that the TAâ€“Fe3+ surface functionalization had little effect on the crystal structures of the nanomaterials. The surface information before and after functionalization were further provided by the FT-IR spectra. For the spectral profile of the InP NPs (Fig. 2D), the peaks at 3450 and 1605 cmâˆ’1 were assigned to Î½Oâ€“H and Î½CC, respectively, owing to the residual H2O and organics introduced in the preparation process. Comparatively, additional peaks located at 1400, 1100, and 585 cmâˆ’1 were observed for TAâ€“Fe3+/InP NPs, which could be assigned to the characteristic peaks of rOâ€“H, Î½Câ€“O, and Î½Feâ€“O for the TAâ€“Fe3+ compound.34 Similarly, the peaks associated with the TAâ€“Fe3+ compound could also be found for TAâ€“Fe3+/Bi NSs compared with the compound before surface functionalization (Fig. 2E). These results suggest a successful introduction of TAâ€“Fe3+ into the TAâ€“Fe3+/InP and TAâ€“Fe3+/Bi NSs compounds. However, no obvious differences could be found for g-C3N4 before and after surface functionalization, which was maybe due to the similar peak domains of the Câ€“N(â€“C)â€“C bonds between the TAâ€“Fe3+ compound and non-metal g-C3N4.35 The TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs had higher relative intensities of O 1s and C 1s peaks than the native NMs did in the XPS etching images (Fig. 2Gâ€“I and Table S3, ESIâ€ ), which indicated the adherence of TAâ€“Fe3+ on the surface of the NMs in TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs. However, the Fe 2p peak was not evident in TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs, which may be due to the ultralow content of Fe3+ for XPS characterization. The Fe 2p peaks were evident in the spectra of the InP NPs and TAâ€“Fe3+/InP NPs. These were likely introduced in the material preparation process (InP powder milled with steel balls). To help confirm the presence of Fe3+, ICP/OES characterization was conducted, and the results (Fig. S2, ESIâ€ ) showed a slight difference in Fe content between TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs (2.5 Ã— 10âˆ’4 g(Fe) g(M)âˆ’1) and the NMs (0 g(Fe) g(M)âˆ’1), and the ultralow content of Fe3+ in TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs corresponded to the low signal intensities of the Fe 2p peak in the XPS results.

The morphologies of the TAâ€“Fe3+-functionalized TAâ€“Fe3+/NMs were further characterized by TEM images (Fig. 3). The TAâ€“Fe3+/InP NPs appeared as irregular nanoparticles with a translucent thin layer on their surface (Fig. 3Aâ€“C). Moreover, the distributions of Fe and C elements had a wider outline than those of In and P elemental distributions in the EDS mappings, as marked by the dashed lines (Fig. 3D). Besides, the profiles of In and P possesses a clear boundary, whereas those of Fe and C were misty. These observations suggest that the transparent thin layer on InP NPs was the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm. Similar observations also were made in the TAâ€“Fe3+/Bi NSs (Fig. 3Eâ€“H) and TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4 (Fig. 3Iâ€“L), whereby a layer of translucent thin film on the surface of nanomaterials was evident in the TEM images. The elements that originated from the bulk material (e.g., Bi of TAâ€“Fe3+/Bi NSs, and C and N of TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4) resulted in well-resolved EDS elemental mappings that clearly reflected the morphology of the corresponding nanoparticles, while those that originated from the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm (C, O, and Fe) featured an outline without a clear border, with shapes slightly larger than those of the bulk material. These results provide strong evidence for the successful deposition of TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm on the surface of the nanomaterials. Particularly, the loading of TAâ€“Fe3+ was very low in TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4, which was evident in both the TEM and EDS mapping images in Fig. 3L. The transparent TAâ€“Fe3+ layer was extremely thin, and so its signal in EDS mapping was too weak. This may be caused by the ultrathin sheet structure and distinct surface character of g-C3N4, which was harder to adhere to than those of the InP NPs and Bi NSs. However, some other evidence still showed the successful adherence of TAâ€“Fe3+ on g-C3N4. In the new added XPS etching images, TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4 possessed higher C 1s and O 1s peaks than the g-C3N4; while in the ICP/OES results, there was a uniform rise in Fe content in all three materials after TAâ€“Fe3+ functionalization. According to EDS elemental analysis diagram of TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4 in Fig. 3L, atomic ratio of C and N was around 8:1, which was much higher than that of g-C3N4 (3:4), while the atomic percentage of O was as high as 79.45%, accompanied with 1.28% Fe, all of which indicated the successful adherence of TAâ€“Fe3+ on g-C3N4.

3.2 Material suitability for modular assembly on yeast cells

The electrostatic interactions between nanomaterials and the cell surface play a vital role in modular assembly.16,22 As shown in Fig. 4A, the surface potentials of TAâ€“Fe3+/InP NPs, TAâ€“Fe3+/Bi NSs, and TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4 were âˆ’46.0, âˆ’49.6, and âˆ’42.8 mV, respectively, which were more negative than their counterpart before TAâ€“Fe3+ functionalization. The native yeast cells were also negatively charged with a surface potential of âˆ’29.63 mV. In order to facilitate the interactions to TAâ€“Fe3+/NM, the yeast cells were treated with PAH (rich in â€“NH2) to form a positively charged polymer layer on the cell surface. As expected, the surface of the cell was successfully inverted to a positive charge (25.70 mV, Fig. 4B). As a result, the electrostatic attraction spontaneously drives the yeast cells and TAâ€“Fe3+/NM to be in close contact upon mixing. Moreover, Feâ€“O coordination bonds were formed by the introduction of Fe3+ in to the modular-assembly process.16,22 These bonds serve to promote interparticle locking between individual nanoparticles of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM and interfacial locking between TAâ€“Fe3+/NM and the yeast cell surface, both of which eventually assist in the formation of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids.

The morphology of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids was further characterized by SEM observation (Fig. 5). The SEM images clearly indicate that the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM can attach to the surface of yeast cells. This close contact was also confirmed by the corresponding TEM images of thin-sectioned yeast cells (insets in Fig. 5A and C). The C signals from the EDS mapping give the morphological profile of yeast cells. In addition, the In, Bi, and N were in good agreement with the localization patterns of the InP NPs, Bi NSs, and g-C3N4 observed from their corresponding SEM images, respectively. Despite the nanomaterials having different chemical compositions (e.g., metal phosphide InP, metallic Bi, and non-metallic g-C3N4), and various morphologies (NPs, NSs and nanosheets), the biohybrids could be successfully attained, demonstrating the versatility of the modular-assembly method for the biohybrids fabrication.

3.3 Factors affecting the material loading of the biohybrids

To investigate the key factors affecting the material loading of the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeasts, orthogonal experiments were performed on TA, M, OD, Fe3+, where TA and M means the TA and M concentrations in the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM preparation, OD and Fe3+ means the OD ratio of functionalized nanomaterials to yeast cells and the Fe3+ concentration in materialâ€“yeast hybridization process, respectively. The conditions are summarized in Table S5 (ESIâ€ ). The surface-functionalized nanomaterials had a significant influence on the final state of the inorganicâ€“biological hybrids. Thus, the loading effect of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM in terms of the different weight ratios of TA/NM (mg:mg) on the morphology of the biohybrids was investigated by SEM observation (Fig. 6). For convenience, the OD ratio (TAâ€“Fe3+/NM to cell and Fe3+ concentration) and Fe3+ concentration were adjusted to 1:2 and 1%, respectively. With the TA/NM = (2 mg:50 mg), the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM could efficiently cover the surface of the yeast cells. When increasing the amount of TA (TA/NM = 40 mg:50 mg), no distinct differences in the coverage of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM on the yeast cell were observed, suggesting that an excess amount of TA did not enhance the loading amount of nanomaterials. However, when further decreasing the amount of NM (TA/NM = 40 mg:10 mg), the coverage domain of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM on the yeast cell remarkably decreased, indicating that a higher amount of NM to TA in the functionalization step could enhance the loading amount of nanomaterials on the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids.

In the modular-assembly process, modifying the OD ratio and the Fe3+ concentration can affect the Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids. Thus, the material loading over TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast (NM%) was also semi-quantified (Fig. 7). Accurate cell counting was ensured by flow cytometry measurement, which was optimized to differentiate the nanomaterials and cells (Fig. S3, ESIâ€ ). Due to the quantification difficulty for non-metal g-C3N4 by ICP-OES, the TAâ€“Fe3+/g-C3N4/Yeast biohybrids were excluded from the analysis. The results revealed that the overall NM% lay within the range of 10â€“60 Ã— 10âˆ’14 mol per cell. When a higher initial OD of the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM suspension (OD ratio = 1:1) was applied, a higher NM% was achieved (Fig. 7B and D), which was reasonable since more nanomaterials were involved in the modular-assembly process under this condition. The generally lower loading ratio under the TA/NM = 40:50 condition than that under the TA/NM = 2:50 condition revealed that an excess amount of TA may negatively affect the material loading. More studies are needed to reveal the underlying mechanisms of this. In terms of the effect of Fe3+ on the material loading, a negative correlation was established when a high Fe3+ concentration (10% v/v) was used in the TA/NM = 40:50 conditions, in which an excess amount of TA was used. Since the presence of free TA and Fe3+ results in the rapid formation of TAâ€“Fe3+ complexes, an increased Fe3+ concentration likely leads to the deposition of excessive TAâ€“Fe3+ complexes on the surface of yeast cells, which would eventually impact the nanomaterial loading.

3.4 Investigation of the biocompatibility

The biocompatibility at the bioticâ€“abiotic interface is a serious consideration when combining living cells with inorganic nanomaterials. Therefore, the viability of yeast cells in the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids was studied. Cells incubated without nanomaterials were used as the control group. Under the TA/NM = (2 mg:50 mg) and TA/NM = (40 mg:50 mg) conditions, the increase in cell density (Ã— 106 cells per mL) for all biohybrids was slower than that of the control (Fig. 8A and B), whereas the cell growth was not affected when the highest TA/NM ratio (40 mg:10 mg) was applied (Fig. 8C). The FITC/PI staining and flow cytometry analysis revealed that, after 48 h of culture, the relative viabilities of the yeast cells under most of the experimental conditions were above 90%, and some of them even exceeded 95% (Fig. S4, ESIâ€ ). The only exception was that of the Bi NCs, which gave rise to relative viabilities of 89% and 91% (Fig. S4D, ESIâ€ ). These results indicate that a higher TA/NM ratio can lead to better biosafety for the system, and that Bi NSs likely affected the viability of yeast cells to a greater extent than InP NPs did and g-C3N4 did. This was also supported by the results from the DNA/RNA leakage study, where the group with the highest TA/NM had the lowest level of leakage (Fig. 8D). Since the genetic materials (DNA and RNA) are presented in the nucleus and cytoplasm of living cells, the presence of them in extracellular space indicates a compromised integrity of the cell membrane. Therefore, the lower level of DNA/RNA detected in the samples with higher TA:NM ratios implied a positive effect of a higher TA:NM ratio for biohybrid fabrication. The respiration assay is based on the conversion of TTC, a colorless water-soluble tetrazolium salt, into a water-insoluble red formazan, by the mitochondrial dehydrogenases within viable cells.36 In good agreement with the results presented in Fig. S4 (ESIâ€ ), a significantly lower respiration rate was observed for the Bi group (Fig. 8E), suggesting that the biosafety of Bi NSs was less than that of the InP NPs and g-C3N4. Furthermore, g-C3N4 possessed comparable respiration rates with the control group, demonstrating its promising feasibility for biohybrid construction with excellent biocompatibility.37,38 Viable yeast cells maintain an optimal cytosolic potassium (K+) concentration (200â€“300 mM) via the proper functioning of various ion transporters on the cell membrane.39 Since the bioticâ€“abiotic interface is located on the cell membrane, the integrity of cell membrane was also studied via measuring the concentration of leaked potassium ions (K+)40 after the fabrication of the biohybrids. No distinct difference in K+ leakage was identified between the groups using different nanomaterials and preparation conditions (Fig. 8F).

NM = nanomaterials, including InP NPs, Bi NSs, or g-C3N4.

The light-energy-conversion abilities of these biohybrid systems were explored via measuring the intracellular total NAD and NADH levels, since the cycling of this coenzyme plays a vital role in cellular energy conversion and metabolism.27 The intracellular total NAD in the biohybrids of InP NPs and g-C3N4 showed a minor increase after light irradiation (Fig. S5C, ESIâ€ ). The same treatment resulted in a decrease in intracellular total NAD in the biohybrid of Bi NSs (Fig. S5C, ESIâ€ ), which was in good agreement with the decreased biocompatibility of this material, as also confirmed in the other assays. Although the absorbance of the light-irradiated Bi NSs biohybrid sample showed a minor increase over that of the dark sample (Fig. S5A, ESIâ€ ), their intracellular NADH levels were unable to be quantified due to their low absorbance (Fig. S5B, ESIâ€ ). Light irradiation on the other two biohybrids resulted in a substantial increase in intracellular NADH levels, and the effect of InP NPs was more evident than that on g-C3N4 (Fig. S5B, ESIâ€ ). The evidence thus suggests that the light energy had been transformed by the nanomaterials, most likely into free electrons, and then subsequently utilized by yeast cells to supply the formation of intracellular NADH. Therefore, the modular-assembly strategy and nanomaterials are considered relatively safe to the cell membrane integrity. According to the above results, the biocompatibility and nanomaterial loading effects of the TAâ€“Fe3+/NM/yeast biohybrids depended on multiple factors and the optimization of a single factor might not result in the best outcome. Therefore, striking a balance between the appropriate material loadings and reasonable biocompatibility should be aimed for via careful selection of the reaction conditions, especially the amount of TA used in the biohybrid assembly and the toxicity of the nanomaterials.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a promising strategy for fabricating an inorganicâ€“biological hybrid system based on TAâ€“Fe3+ surface functionalization and modular assembly was systematically evaluated for a number of different aspects, including material suitability, loading effects, and biocompatibility. The successful surface functionalization of InP NPs, Bi NSs, and g-C3N4 by the TAâ€“Fe3+ nanofilm and the subsequent modular assembly onto the surface of yeast cells demonstrated the good versatility of this approach toward different types of nanomaterials. Several important factors that could potentially affect the material-loading efficiencies were investigated. It was revealed that the proper utilization of TA and a relatively higher quantity of nanomaterials in the surface-functionalization step are critical for the material loading. The biocompatibility of TAâ€“Fe3+/NM to the living cells is material dependent, which could also be optimized via adjusting the relative ratio of TA to nanomaterials during the functionalization process. More importantly, a balance between the loading of nanomaterials and the biocompatibility can be achieved via fine-tuning the TA concentration in the biohybrid systems. Overall, this study provides a deep insight into the TAâ€“Fe3+-based modular-assembly strategy for the construction of inorganicâ€“biological hybrid systems. With more in-depth investigations carried out in the future, it could be expected that more feasible strategies for constructing such systems will be established, which could significantly drive the development and expansion of the solar-to-chemical conversion based on inorganicâ€“biological hybrid systems.
