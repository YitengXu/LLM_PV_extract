Atomically precise thiolate-protected gold nanoclusters: current advances in solar-powered photoredox catalysis

Abstract

Photocatalysis has been regarded as an emerging technology to convert renewable solar energy to chemical fuels, providing unprecedented opportunities for solving the deteriorating energy crisis and environmental issues in the future. In recent years, atomically precise gold nanoclusters, which have emerged as novel light-harvesting antennas possessing the merits of unique atomic stacking fashion, the quantum confinement effect, enriched catalytically active sites and a discrete energy band structure, have been drawing enormous attention in diverse fields. Despite the advancement, there is deficiency of a systematic, comprehensive, and insightful summary on the gold nanocluster-based photosystems to reinforce our fundamental understanding of the charge transport characteristics of metal nanoclusters in photocatalysis. Herein, our review summarizes the latest progress in gold nanocluster mediated photocatalysis and photoelectrocatalysis for diverse applications including non-selective photocatalytic organic pollutant mineralization, photocatalytic selective organic transformation, photocatalytic hydrogen generation, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, and photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting. Moreover, we discuss the underlying photocatalytic and PEC mechanisms associated with the energy band and electronic structure of gold nanoclusters. Finally, perspectives and challenges of gold nanocluster-based photosystems are outlined. It is anticipated that our review could inspire ideas on how to smartly utilize atomically precise metal nanoclusters for crafting high-efficiency photosystems towards solar energy conversion.

1. Introduction

Catalysis continues to plays a key role in addressing global challenges such as energy shortage and environmental pollution. Among the diverse catalysis methods, photocatalysis has emerged as a frontier in renewable energy conversion and catalysis science. However, so far, photocatalytic conversion of solar energy into chemical energy has still been far from satisfactory, predominantly due to the fast charge recombination rate. In order to develop high-efficiency photocatalysts, many strategies have thus been developed such as enhancing and extending light absorption, reinforcing photogenerated charge separation, and charge transfer kinetics modulation. Despite these endeavors, development of robust heterogeneous photocatalysis remains a grand challenge.

In recent years, atomically precise gold nanoclusters (NCs) have emerged as a novel sector of metal nanomaterials, owing to their unique atomic stacking fashion and electronic structure.1 It is generally believed that the size of gold NCs is generally less than 2 nm (excluding the protective layer of the shell), and the number of gold atoms is less than 150.2,3 Due to the nano-size effect, gold NCs usually exhibit non-metallic behaviors with energy quantization afforded by their highest occupied molecular orbitallowest unoccupied molecular orbital (HOMOLUMO) gap rather than localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) of conventional plasmonic metal nanocrystals.4 Thus, electrons can be photoexcited from the HOMO to the LOMO level of gold NCs upon light irradiation,5 thus generating the electronhole pairs. Noteworthily, the HOMO is featured by a hybrid orbital consisting of atomic orbitals of Au, causing elegant optical absorption.1 The generation of electronhole pairs over gold NCs using solar energy exert substantial influence on the photocatalytic applications of gold NCs\-based photosystems.

Despite these merits, high surface energy inevitably leads to the spontaneous aggregation of gold NCs, which results in the destruction of the original structure, and a remarkably reduced active site number.6 Besides, owing to the quantum confinement effect, increase and decrease of the gold atom number significantly change the electronic structure of gold NCs, affecting their photocatalytic performances.7 Integrating gold NCs with semiconductors provides an efficacious approach to harness the charge carriers of gold NCs in photoredox catalysis, by which the generic photosensitization effect of gold NCs can be optimized with the charge transport pathway finely modulated.810 Although recent years have witnessed a sporadic investigation on gold NC-based photocatalysis, a comprehensive summary of gold NC-mediated photosystems for heterogeneous photocatalysis has been seldom reported, and moreover, charge transport characteristics of gold NCs in photoredox catalysis has still remained elusive and needs to be well elucidated. The information on these aspects is of paramount importance to reinforce our fundamental understanding of the charge transfer mechanism of atomically precise metal NCs.

Motivated by these considerations, our review primarily focuses on summarizing the latest developments in preparation, modification strategies, and photocatalytic mechanisms of atomically precise gold NCs, and furthermore, gold NC based photocatalytic and photoelectrochemical (PEC) systems for diverse applications including photocatalytic hydrogen generation, photocatalytic selective organic transformation, photocatalytic organic pollution mineralization, photocatalytic CO2 reduction and PEC water splitting are specifically discussed. Besides, future perspectives and challenges in this booming research field are elucidated. It is anticipated that our work could provide enriched information on the emerging potential of atomically precise metal NCs for solar energy conversion.

2. Preparation of gold NCs

With the aid of suitable ligands, gold NCs with an accurate atomic number can be synthesized.11 According to the ligand type, there are monolayer protected nanoclusters such as thiol, phosphine, alkyne, selenium, and multiple layer protected (e.g., thiol/phosphine) NCs. Among these ligands, thiol has drawn widespread attention owing to high chemical energy between thiol and gold atoms. In the past few decades, various synthesis techniques have been developed to prepare gold-thiol NCs, including chemical reduction, template synthesis, ligand etching and so on. Aside from the synthetic methodology, revealing the formation mechanism of metal NCs at the molecular level remains challenging. Determining the decisive intermediate species and tracing the evolution routes in the formation process can advance the precise regulation and control of the size and composition of gold NCs. Taking the gold-thiol NCs, for example, the review focuses on four synthesis strategies and reciprocal growth mechanisms of gold NCs.

2.1 Reduction growth

The reduction growth method is the process of reducing a Au(I) or Au(III) precursor to Au by using a reducing agent. The speed of the reduction process and the strength of the reducing agent are of great importance for the successful preparation of gold NCs. For instance, Chen et al. prepared Au25(PPh3)10[SC3H6Si(OC2H5)3]5Cl2 nanoclusters with a high turnover frequency and various active sites by dropwise addition of NaBH4, dispersed in ethanol, to a mixed solution containing Au(PPh3)Cl, MPTES, CH2Cl2 and ethanol.9 Schaaff et al. prepared Aux NCs by mixing thiols (H-SR) with Au(III) salt (e.g., [AuX4] where X = halogen ligand) or phase-transferred Au(III) salt (e.g., [TOA][AuX4], TOA represents a tetraoctylammonium cation), and this leads to extensive formation of oligomeric SR[Au(I)SR]x complexes via eqn (1) and (2).12 To gain a further insight into the growth mechanism of Aux NCs, Luo et al. revealed the detailed synthetic process for reducing Au(I) complex precursors to [Au25(SR)18] for the first time by exploring the time evolution of the Au(I) precursor and Aux NC intermediate species with electro spray ionization-mass spectroscopy (ESI-MS). They found that the growth of Aux NCs is correlated with the formation of multiple 2e intermediate NC species from 2e to 8e.13

2.2 Seed growth

The seed growth method refers to choosing small-size metal NCs as the seeds and making them gradually grow into a larger size. Similar to the reduction growth method, it can be realized by the two-electron transfer-reduction process. For example, Luo et al. reduced Au(I)SR complexes by using CO in the presence of a preformed 8e [Au25(SR)18] seed, generating monodisperse 20e [Au44(SR)26]2, and thus the authors proposed a three-stage growth mechanism.11,14 The growth mechanism includes: (1) a pre-growth stage: Au(I)SR complexes to [Au25(SR)18]; (2) a Au25-assisted growth stage: [Au25(SR)18] to [Au44(SR)26]2; (3) a thermodynamically controlled size-focusing stage; (4) refining the purity of [Au44(SR)26]2.13

2.3 Alloying reaction

The alloying method is to use a certain amount of external molds to gradually exchange the original surface mold of the NCs to achieve metal exchange, so as to obtain alloy nanoclusters doped with different metals. For instance, Yao et al. found that thermodynamically less favorable [Ag32@Au12(SR)30]4 NCs can be synthesized by reacting [Ag44(SR)30]4 with [Au2(SR)2Cl]via a definitive reaction as formulated by using eqn (3), and then proposed an associationdissociation assisted surface motif exchange (SME) mechanism.15 In addition, alloy NCs can also be produced by other methods, such as coreduction,16 metal exchange, and inter cluster reactions.

2.4 Ligand exchange

The ligand exchange method, similar to the alloying method, belongs to the exchange process, and the SME mechanism can be applied in the ligandexchange reaction as well. Apart from preparing alloy NCs by exchanging motifs, it can also exchange peripheral protective ligands to form other ligand-protected gold NCs or multi-ligand protected NCs. The ligand exchange mechanism of NCs also follows the SME mechanism. In addition to the size and composition, recent advances in X-ray crystallography analysis indicate that the stacking symmetry of the core of metal NCs could be adjusted among icosahedron-based, face-centered-cubic (FCC), hexagonal-close-packing (HCP), and body-centered-cubic (BCC) arrangements, by simply tuning the volume of thiolate ligands.

3.1 Au25(SR)18

Among the various gold NCs, Au25(SR)18 was the first one that has been attracting enormous attention owing to its ease of preparation, unique structure, high stability and wide-spread application prospect. Through X-ray crystallography analysis and theoretical simulation, it has been well established that Au25(SR)18 NCs consist of a centered icosahedral Au13 core (Fig. 2a, magenta portion) surrounded by six Au2(SR)3 semi-rings (Fig. 2, cyan and yellow). The cluster core provides three kinds of Au sites (center, icosahedral surface, and semi-ring) and two thiolate environments.19 Additionally, the semi-ring (or surface) Au atoms are also accessible to reactant molecules in photocatalytic reactions (Fig. 2b).17 Generally, many catalytic properties of Au25(SR)18 result from these semi-ring structures. It should be noted that Zhu et al. revealed the difference in structure of the protected semi-rings between oxidized neutral and native anionic forms by monitoring its crystal structure.20 In another case, Chen et al. synthesized atomically precise Au25 NCs, and then they were deposited on the TiO2 substrate. The result suggests that Au25 NCs/TiO2 nanocomposites exhibit excellent selectivity and high activity in visible-light-driven photocatalytic oxidation of amines to imines.9 The turnover frequency (TOF) of oxidation is found to be 1522 h1 which is considerably higher than that obtained utilizing conventional gold catalysts. Furthermore, Au25 NCs demonstrate good recyclability and stability in photocatalytic selective oxidation of a wide range of amines.

3.2 Au38(SR)24

In addition to Au25(SR)18, Au38(SR)24 has also received enormous interest in academic research on gold NCs. As shown in Fig. 2c, Au38(SR)24 NCs are based on a face-fused bi-icosahedral Au23 core which is covered by a second shell composed of the remaining 15 gold atoms, thus forming a coreshell structure. Taking thiolate ligands into account, the NCs may also be considered as a Au23 core with three monomeric staples (RSAuSR, abbreviated as Au(SR)2 below) and six dimeric staples (RSAuS(R)AuSR, abbreviated as Au2(SR)3 below).18 It is worth mentioning that the Au23 core can be regarded as two Au13 icosahedra fused together by sharing a common Au3 face (Fig. 2d).

4. Modification strategies of photoactive gold NCs

In addition to the Au atom number of gold NCs, the surface composition and structure of Au NCs are also responsible for the photocatalytic performance of gold NC-based photosystems, which are specified in the following section from the perspectives of ligand type, gold atom and ligand number, and metalthiolate motifs. In other words, catalytic properties of gold NCs can be modified by regulating several important factors, such as the species of surface ligands (capping agents), molar ratio of ligand/metal ions, or composition.

4.1 Surface ligand modulation

In the synthesis of gold NCs, high surface energy of gold NCs resulting from the ultra-small size makes gold ions prone to aggregation, which requires an effective stabilization approach with the aid of surface ligands. A surface ligand, especially its structure, plays an important role in maintaining the stability of gold NCs, preventing aggregation and modifying its surface activation. 4.1.1 Thiolate. The studies on self-assembled monolayers (SAMs) led to the vigorous development of gold-thiol chemistry in the 1980s because of their extraordinary stability.24,25 Recently, it was reported that glutathione (GSH) protected gold NCs (Aux@GSH) can inject photo-excited electrons into TiO2 under visible light irradiation, producing photocurrent in a Aux NC-based solar cell with analogous efficiency to that of CdS (2% power conversion efficiency). In another case, Xu et al. reported the photocatalytic mechanism of a GSH-protected Aux NC decorated TiO2 nanocomposite in photocatalytic degradation of organic pollutants.26 It was unveiled that the GSH ligand not only protects Aux NCs but also plays a crucial role in boosting photocatalytic degradation.
4.1.2 Phosphine. The attempt of using phosphine ligands to decorate gold NCs can be traced back to the 1970s.27 Since then, enormous efforts have been made to synthesize gold NCs capped by thiolate and phosphine ligands from Au4 to Au24.1,28 For example, Zhao et al. have successfully synthesized phosphine-capped gold NCs that are deposited on the TiO2 support. In the preparation, a phosphine compound was used for tuning the cluster size.29 It was unveiled that the photocatalyst exhibits significant activity for liquid and gas-phase oxidation reactions. 4.2 Gold-based bimetal NCs

Electronic configuration, optical performance, and photocatalytic performances including catalytic activity and stability of metal dppe, can be tailored by substituting one or several gold atoms with heteroatom(s) of other metal elements (e.g., Ag, Cu, Pd, and Pt).3137 Compared with conventional nanoparticles whose structure is unclear enough, atomically precise gold NCs with a well-defined structure, characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD), provide an ideal model for mapping out the structure of the bimetal NCs.38 According to the type of heteroatom, atomically precise alloy gold NCs can be classified into bimetallic, tri-metallic and multi-metallic alloys. Among them, bimetallic alloys are the most commonly used alloy NCs in gold NC-based photocatalysis, which will be briefly discussed below. Usually, strategies of synthesizing bimetal NCs are mainly classified into two categories: (i) co-reduction in situ synthesis of gold NCs and other metal NCs; (ii) doping gold NCs with other metal salts.38 Varying the metal composite ratio can control the number of heteroatoms in the final alloy NCs. Generally, two types of heteroatom substitution behaviors have been elucidated:1 (i) the precise number of heteroatoms after modification; (ii) distribution of heteroatoms, but the total number of metal atoms is still maintained.
4.2.1 AuAg/Cu. For Ag or Cu substitution, here we illustrate the case of heteroatom substitution in Au25(SR)18 since such bimetallic NCs have been investigated for catalysis recently. It is noted that Ag or Cu substitution in Au25(SR)18 NCs with a specific number has not been obtained yet.39 The similarity between Ag and Au readily leads to alloy NCs with a wide distribution of Ag atoms rather than trace doping. As an example, the total structure of AgxAu25x(SR)18 is shown in Fig. 6c,40 in which the central atom remains gold, whereas the icosahedral shell is composed of Au and Ag (Fig. 6a), and heavy doping can also lead to Ag distribution into the external staple motifs (Fig. 6b).

In a typical example, Anna et al. reported the effect of segregation on the properties of (AuAg)(147).41 This type of bimetal NC can be considered as gold NCs templates, and Ag atoms are placed on an aimed location, forming a Ag@Au@Ag configuration (Fig. 6d). Usually, Ag147 is appropriate for photocatalytic reactions resulting from the less unoccupied d-orbitals on the surface atoms compared to Au, whereas Au147 demonstrates more unoccupied d-states than Ag147 at EF. However, alloy structures are more thermodynamically stable at the Gupta level, as these constructions can maximize the interaction between Au and Ag. For instance, Soumyadip Bhunia et al. prepared bimetallic alloy goldsilver NCs (AuAg NCs) by combining Au3+ and Ag+.42 The thus-obtained alloy NCs can emit variably, and the quantum yield varies optimally considering the ratio of Ag in the Au NCs. However, Au25 NCs showed higher selectivity (ca. 70%) and conversion (ca. 80%). Significantly, bimetallic Au25xAgx NCs exhibit a >99% conversion and an almost complete 100% selectivity, which can be ascribed to the synergistic effect between Au and Ag.43,44

At present, this structure has not been widely used in photocatalysis. TiO2 loaded with bimetallic alloy AuPt nanoclusters exhibit a unique photocatalytic property, which is ascribed to the electronic modification of the shell metal through interaction with the core metal and support. 5. Photocatalytic mechanism of gold NCs

Although new developments in exploring suitable gold NC-based photocatalysis for solar energy conversion have been witnessed in the past few years, the underlying reaction mechanism is still elusive. To make a breakthrough in gold NC-based photocatalysis, it is heavily dependent on an in-depth understanding of the intrinsic mechanism. Recently, substantial studies have been dedicated to the exploration of the principle of gold NC-based photocatalysis, including interaction between gold NCs and semiconductors, influence of reaction conditions, and supply of active sites. The reaction mechanism of gold NC-based photocatalysis can be classified into the following parts including (i) excitation and flow of photoinduced charge carriers, (ii) activity improvement and (iii) instability of the gold NC induced mechanism. The studies on the underlying photocatalytic mechanism would advance construction of more efficient and stable gold NC-based photocatalysts in the near future.

5.1 Photoexcitation of charge carriers over gold NCs

As mentioned above, due to their ultra-small size (<2 nm) effect, instead of the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effect of conventional plasmonic metal NPs, atomically precise gold NCs usually exhibit a molecule-like electronic transition with a discrete energy level as demonstrated in their HOMO and LUMO bands. The HOMOLUMO gap of gold NCs is closely related to their photocatalytic properties such as multiple absorption bands and phoredox potential. Initially, gold NCs are placed in an excited state by absorbing photons of energy under light irradiation. In the simplest case, electrons produced over excited-state gold NCs with adequately high energy will be photoexcited from the HOMO to the LUMO level of gold NCs, leading to the emergence of electronhole pairs like for semiconductors.5

In addition, it has been well established that the band gap of gold NCs can be modulated for obtaining excellent photocatalytic performances, which is correlated to the modification strategies, as elucidated in Section 4. For example, Muhammad reported the variation of HOMOLUMO levels of gold NCs with different sizes by precisely regulating the growth kinetics in the reducing precursor.54 In this work, the HOMOLUMO gap of various gold NCs was determined by TD-DFT calculations. The energy diagram of the gold NCs is displayed in Fig. 8a, and the corresponding simulated UV-vis absorption spectra are shown in Fig. 8b and Table 1. The result indicates that the absorption onset of gold NCs becomes redshifted on increasing the size, which coincides with the variation of the band gap due to the quantum confinement effect.

5.2 Flow of charge carriers over gold NCs

Generally, the band gap width of gold NCs is relatively narrow compared with that of the conventional semiconductor-based photocatalysts. Normally, photogenerated electronhole pairs demonstrate a tendency for recombination, which significantly decreases the intensity of charge carries in photocatalysis. Therefore, once the charge lifetime is shorter than that of subsequent chemical interaction, there is no considerable photocatalytic activity. Thus, in order to fully utilize the charge carriers generated from gold NCs, it is necessary to couple with semiconductors to achieve efficient interfacial charge migration and separation. From another point of view, being able to generate photo-excited electronhole pairs, gold NCs are able to serve as a visible light photosensitizer owing to a favorable band-gap. The delivery pathway of charge carriers is similar to that of dye or quantum dot-based photocatalysts.

Using PEC measurements and emission spectra, Chen et al. revealed the photoexcited state interaction between the semiconductor and gold NCs that acted as a photosensitizer.55 As shown in Fig. 9c, photoluminescence emission intensity decreases on increasing the TiO2 concentration, which suggests that electrons photoexcited over gold NCs gradually migrate from the LUMO level to the conduction band (CB) of TiO2. Note that the IPCE result is consistent with the light absorption of Aux NCs, which confirms that photocurrent generation is ascribed to the photo-excitation of Aux NCs (Fig. 9d). In addition, the maximum IPCE and short-circuit current of 3.96 mA cm2 imply the outstanding photosensitization ability of the Aux NCs in capturing incident photons and converting solar energy into electrical energy (Fig. 9d and e). A schematic illustration of the photo-induced excitation and charge transport process of the Aux NCsTiO2 nanocomposite is shown in Fig. 9a. Given that the LUMO potential of Aux NCs is more negative than the CB edge of TiO2 in conjunction with the intimate interfacial contact between them, photo electrons from Aux NCs can be easily injected into the CB of TiO2. Thus, photo-generated electrons and holes produced over Aux NCs are efficaciously separated, and consequently drive the photoredox reactions. In general, acting as a narrow bandgap semiconductor, Aux NCs supported on TiO2 can not only generate considerable charge carriers under visible light, but can also extend the light absorption range of wide bandgap semiconductors (e.g., TiO2).

Aside from generating electronhole pairs under visible light, Aux NCs can also be deposited onto a semiconductor as metal type co-catalysts under UV light. For instance, in most water-splitting semiconductor photocatalysts, loading metal NCs are demonstrated to contribute to effective separation of charge carriers for extending the electron lifetime and enriching active sites.56 As shown in Fig. 9b, photo-generated electrons over a semiconductor photocatalyst are able to move to Aux NCs acting as co-catalysts, and the transferred electrons then reduce protons for H2 evolution.

5.3 Role of catalytically active sites

It is generally accepted that catalytic activities of semiconductors can be improved after loading metal NCs. However, it was proven that surface ligands do not directly influence the catalytic activity by comparing the catalysts modified with different ligands.9 Therefore, as for gold atoms themselves, distinct electronic properties and framework of gold NCs should be responsible for the high catalytic activity. Thus, due to the small bandgap, apart from the absorption of visible and near infrared (NIR) light, gold NCs can improve the photocatalytic activity by providing enriched catalytic active sites as well.

As mentioned above, there is partial ligand removal under the reaction conditions, and thus, gold atoms are accessible to reactants in the core and on the shell. On the one hand, ligand removal leads to the aggregation of gold NCs and formation of Au nanoparticles, resulting in the variation of the photocatalytic mechanism. It should be emphasized that plenty of gold atoms are actually exposed without protection, providing a multitude of catalytically active sites for chemical reactions. In other words, active sites can be deemed as the unprotected gold atoms. Hence, such exposed gold atoms are able to capture reactants, forming the intermediates. Furthermore, forming the intermediates between gold atoms and reactants can properly retard the aggregation of gold NCs. Using the role of TEMPO (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidinyloxy), which can selectively abstract hydrogen from the metal surface instead of from the substrate,57 Chen et al. reported the emergence of AuH species during the photocatalytic process for selective oxidation of amines to imines.9 It is worth mentioning that Cl and phosphine ligands can be more easily separated from the NC surface owing to the lower detachment energy of a Cl (0.9 eV mol1) or PH3 ligand (1.3 eV mol1) than that of the detachment of a thiolate ligand (2.7 eV mol1), thereby providing Au active sites. In fact, possible catalytic sites are not limited in the above conventional accessible area. For instance, Chen et al. synthesized the Au22 NCs protected by six bidentate di-phosphine ligands.58 The Au22 NCs are composed of two Au11 units clipped together by four ligands, while the two additional ligands coordinate to each Au11 unit in a bidentate fashion. Intriguingly, eight gold atoms at the interface of the two Au11 units are not coordinated by any ligands and thus render uncoordinated gold sites, which can be used as in situ catalytic active sites.

In addition to the formation of intermediates, the holes generated from the photoexcitation of Aux NCs are capable of inducing the highly active oxygen species, which can accelerate the photocatalytic reaction. For instance, the generated holes can also be utilized to produce hydroxyl radicals (OH) that are usually the cardinal active species in photocatalytic reactions.59

5.4 Instability of gold NCs

Many modification methods, including electronic structure modulation and amelioration of the reaction environment, have been devoted to retarding the aggregation of gold NCs stemming from generic instability. However, this aggregation cannot be completely eradicated with continuous illumination. Thus, photocatalytic mechanisms of gold NC based photosystems are complicated considering the instability of gold NCs.

It was reported that light yellow Aux NCsTiO2 composites gradually turn purple during the photocatalytic reaction,60 which indicates the aggregated transformation of Aux NCs into Au NPs. As shown in Fig. 10, when Aux NCs are dispersed on the TiO2 surface (Fig. 10e), photo-induced charge carriers over Aux NCs participate in the photoredox catalysis. However, under visible or simulated solar light irradiation, Aux NCs tend to aggregate because of high surface energy (Fig. 10f), implying the generation of Au nanoparticles. This transformation makes the photocatalytic mechanism of Aux NCsTiO2 composites complex. Specifically, photo-induced aggregated Au NPs with an intrinsic surface plasmon resonance (SPR) feature are capable of generating hot electrons that can be injected into the CB of TiO2, and then increase the density of photoelectrons in the whole reaction system.6163 Thus, the co-existence of Aux NCs and plasmonic Au NPs endows the AuxTiO2 composites with a synergistic effect, as displayed in Fig. 10c. In particular, instead of direct electron transfer from Aux NCs to the CB of TiO2, photoelectrons can also be transferred from the HOMO level of Aux NCs to Au NPs, and then to the CB of TiO2 (Fig. 10a), in which Au NPs act as a bridging medium to reinforce the electron flow.

6. Photocatalytic applications

Thus far, gold NC-based photocatalysts have been explored for catalyzing various chemical reactions under UV and visible light irradiation such as photocatalytic dye degradation, photocatalytic selective organic oxidation and reduction, photocatalytic water splitting, PEC water splitting and photocatalytic CO2 reduction, which will be elucidated in the following section.

6.1 Dye degradation

Based on the photosensitization effect of gold NCs, photocatalytic decomposition of organic dye pollutants utilizing gold NC-based photosystems has attracted enormous attention. For example, Golovko et al. prepared atomically precise Ru3Au NCs and then deposited them on the anatase TiO2 substrate.65 The as-prepared composite catalysts exhibited considerably improved photocatalytic performances toward methylene blue degradation under visible light irradiation. With regard to the photocatalytic materials, TiO2 is the most widely used photocatalyst in photocatalytic degradation under UV light.6670 As shown in Fig. 11a, under UV light irradiation, TiO2 shows an efficient degradation rate of methyl orange, and TiO2 loaded with Au25(SR)18 shows no distinctive difference.59 However, under visible light irradiation, Au25(SR)18 loaded TiO2 shows a 1.6 times activity enhancement in photocatalytic degradation of methyl orange compared to that with blank TiO2 (Fig. 11b). This was mainly induced by the photosensitization effect of gold NCs for producing electronhole charge carriers which participate in the photoredox catalysis.

As discussed above, Aux NCs are capable of serving as a photosensitizer because of a distinctive HOMOLUMO gap. In addition to injecting a flow of electrons, photosensitization of Aux NCs can retard the recombination of charge carries, thus retaining more holes. The holes can subsequently transform into hydroxyl radicals, which are beneficial for dye degradation.74 For example, our group fabricated the highly ordered nanoporous layer-covered TiO2 nanotube arrays (NP-TNTAs) modified with Aux NCs (Fig. 11d) by a facile self-assembly strategy and disclosed the crucial roles of active species in situ produced during the photo-degradation of methyl orange under simulated solar light irradiation.71 As shown in Fig. 11c and e, Aux NCs/NP-TNTA shows a superior photocatalytic performance to that of blank NP-TNTA, and the electrons, holes, and hydroxyl radicals in situ produced during the photocatalytic reaction play pivotal roles in enhancing the degradation of organic dye pollutants. 6.2 Photocatalytic selective organic transformation

As an important organic transformation method in both industry and laboratories, photocatalytic selective oxidation and reduction have been widely studied, due to their enormous potential and important roles of versatile intermediates generated from selective oxidation and reduction.
6.2.1 Selective photocatalytic oxidation. Developing a Au25/TiO2 nanocomposite as a photocatalyst for oxidation of amines to imines (eqn (13)) in the presence of O2 under visible light irradiation has been reported by Chen et al.9 The high photocatalytic activity stems from the photosensitization effect of Au25 NCs. As shown in Fig. 13a(I) and (II), an electron from benzylamine is seized by holes from Au25 NCs, which caused the transformation from benzylamine into the benzylamine radical cation. Subsequently, -H of the benzylamine radical cation would be derived by the exposed Au atoms, which leads to the formation of a AuH intermediate. Afterwards, H from the AuH and the amine would be transformed by O2 species into PhCHNH and H2O2 (Fig. 13a(III)), which are extraordinarily active in the photocatalytic oxidation of amine. Fig. 13b shows the effect of the radical scavenger type on the photocatalytic oxidation of amine, implying that photo-generated holes and electrons drive the photocatalytic reactions jointly. As shown in Fig. 13c, conversion of amine to imine increases to 82% for Au25/TiO2, which is roughly 5.86 times higher than that of TiO2 (14%). The turnover frequency of amine oxidation was calculated to be 1522 h1, which is remarkably higher than that of conventional Au-based catalysts.84 The result indicates that photocatalytic selective organic oxidation using a Aux NC-based photocatalyst under visible light irradiation is boosted efficiently, whicTh has also been confirmed by a recent work.85 Alternatively, it has been ascertained that Au38 NCs can act as a photocatalyst for conversion of amines to imines under visible light irradiation ( = 455 nm) in the presence of oxygen with an efficiency of 99% along with 99% selectivity as well as good thermal stability. In addition, these authors also explored the photocatalytic activity of Au38 NCs for selective aerobic oxidation of sulfides to sulfoxides.85 After irradiation with = 532 nm for 12 h, sulfoxides were formed with 100% selectivity at 57% conversion of sulfides. The photocatalytic activity of Au38 NCs for the sulfide-oxidation reaction is attributed to the generation of singlet oxygen.

Consequently, it is anticipated that photocatalytic selective oxidation of sulfides to sulfoxides would be attained over gold NC-based photocatalysts.

6.2.2 Selective photocatalytic reduction. Aside from the selective photocatalytic oxidation reaction, gold NC-based photocatalysts have recently been fabricated to probe the conversion and selectivity of photocatalytic reduction of organic compounds under visible light. For example, as reported by our group,71 Aux/NP-TNTAs can act as a photocatalyst for selective reduction of substituted aromatic nitro compounds to amino derivatives under visible light irradiation. As shown in Fig. 14a, the Aux/NP-TNTA heterostructure exhibits enhanced visible light absorption, which is ascribed to the photosensitization effect of Aux NCs that endows the Aux/NP-TNTAs heterostructure with remarkably enhanced light absorption in the visible region. 6.4 PEC water splitting

Semiconductor-based PEC water splitting, which was launched by Honda and Fujishima in 1972,102,103 provides significant new insights into solar energy conversion. Contrary to heterogeneous photocatalysis, photoelectrocatalysis could effectively restrain the recombination of photo-generated electronhole and remarkably enhance water splitting efficiency by harnessing the synergy of photocatalysis and electrocatalysis.104111 In this regard, due to the distinct energy level configuration and quantum confinement effect, gold NCs exhibit promising potential in photoelectrocatalysis. For example, Tatsuma and co-workers ascertained that all the GSH-protected Aux (x = 15, 18, 22, 25, 29, 33, 39) NCs adsorbed on the TiO2 electrodes can promote the solar-to-electric energy conversion regardless of different electronic structures.112 Among them, Au25 NCs with the HOMO level located between +1.79 V and +1.56 V vs. SHE exhibit the highest incident photon-to-electron conversion efficiency (IPCE) (Fig. 19a and b), and internal quantum yield of Au25-modified TiO2 with electron donors is more than 60% at 400900 nm. The photocurrent action spectra are highly consistent with the optical absorption spectra of the corresponding gold NCs,55,112 which indicated that gold NCs play a decisive role in the PEC responses. Later, these authors analyzed the photocurrents in terms of charge separation at the interface between gold NCs and TiO2.113 As shown in Fig. 19c, electrons in an occupied level (i.e., HOMO level) are photo-excited to the unoccupied level (i.e., LUMO level) under visible light irradiation, similar to the photoexcitation of a semiconductor for charge carrier generation in semiconductor-based photocatalysis. Subsequently, such photo-excited electrons are injected into the CB of TiO2, and the holes produced in the occupied level would be reduced by electron donors. The photocurrent action spectra of all the gold NCsTiO2 electrodes (Fig. 19d) are highly consistent with the corresponding optical absorption spectra (Fig. 19e). In addition, it was evidenced that the band bending edge evolving from the semiconductor heterostructure also facilitates the oriented electron transfer at the interfere between gold NCs and TiO2,71 leading to the enhanced PEC performances (Fig. 19f).

Recently, Chen et al. verified that Aux@GSH NCs can act as photosensitizers for PEC water splitting (Fig. 20b).8 After being photosensitized by Aux@GSH NCs, a mesoscopic TiO2 film as the photoanode demonstrates significantly enhanced photocurrent, which is ascribed to the visible-light response of Aux NCs (Fig. 20b). The reversible reduction (0.63 V vs. RHE) and oxidation (0.97 and 1.51 V vs. RHE) potentials of the GSH-protected Aux NCs make it applicable to drive the PEC water splitting reaction. When the electrolyte contains the sacrificial reagent (e.g., EDTA), a nearly 3 times larger hydrogen evolution rate was observed. The faradaic efficiency of oxygen production over Aux/GQDs/NP-TNTAs was detected to be the same as the theoretical values (Fig. 20e).

Apart from PEC water splitting, Aux NCs can also serve as a photosensitizer for PEC removal of antibiotics. For example, Xie et al. designed an efficient photoelectrode by depositing Au25 NCs onto two types of TiO2 nanotube arrays (TNAs) with different lengths,104 including the short one, in which the TNA electrodes are a short TNA (ca. 0.5 m in length), and a long one (ca. 4.5 m in length) (Fig. 21a). Compared with pristine TNAs, Au25-coated TNA exhibited enhanced light absorption in the visible region and PEC performance towards tetracycline decomposition, where the degradation efficiency increases from 65% to 81% for Au25/long-TNA heterostructures, and from 46% to 73% for Au25/short-TNA electrodes (Fig. 21b). The enhanced PEC performance towards tetracycline degradation was considered to result from the generation of photo-generated electrons and holes over Au25 NCs. The thus-produced photoelectrons can either reduce the adsorbed organic molecules (e.g., tetracycline or byproducts) or be energetic enough to generate hydrogen, or react with dissolved O2 to produce active oxygen species (e.g., superoxide radical anion, O2 and hydroxyl radical, OH), and the holes with oxidation potential react with the adsorbed tetracycline molecules or directly recombine with electrons, or indirectly react with H2O or OH to produce OH (Fig. 21a). The holes and OH allow blank TiO2 or Aux NC-modified TiO2 to completely degrade most organic pollutant molecules to non-toxic and environment-friendly substances (e.g., CO2).

Generally, light absorption of Aux NCs in the visible domain is far from satisfactory for PEC water splitting. Thus, combination of Aux NCs and plasmonic metal nanoparticles (NPs) is beneficial for further extending the light absorption range of Aux NC-based photocatalysts.64 As shown in Fig. 21c, electrons photoexcited from Aux NCs are directly transferred to the CB of TiO2 or transferred to the Fermi level of Au NPs, and then to the CB of TiO2, in which the Au NP ingredient works as an efficient electron bridging medium to promote the charge transport. Simultaneously, hot electrons generated from the plasmonic effect of Au NPs can also be injected into the CB of TiO2, therefore increasing the electron density of the whole reaction system. Additionally, localized oscillation of the electric field afforded by the plasmonic absorption of Au NPs simultaneously facilitates the transport of electrons photogenerated over Aux NCs.116118 Hence, photo-generated electrons from Aux NCs and hot electrons from plasmonic Au NPs jointly resulted in the significantly prolonged lifetime of electronhole pairs (Fig. 21d) and thus, substantially enhanced PEC performances of the Aux/Au/NP-TNTAs heterostructure (Fig. 21e). Besides, Aux-core/Pt-shell bimetallic NCs integrated on the TiO2 surface were investigated by Tada.50 The result indicated that the deposited Pt leads to a red shift in the light absorption maximum of Aux NCs.

As illustrated in 5.4, small-sized Aux NCs tend to aggregate under light irradiation and thus lose their original advantageous photosensitization effect. Therefore, photoinduced instability of Aux NCs in the PEC water splitting reaction cannot be neglected. For example, our group systematically reported a comparison of the electrons photogenerated from Aux@GSH and hot electrons from plasmonic gold nanocrystals (Au NYs) in situ transformed from Aux NCs influencing the PEC water splitting performances.115 The authors ascertained that interfacial charge separation (Fig. 21f) and transfer (Fig. 21g) of photoelectrons from Aux NCs are superior to that of hot electrons from plasmonic Au NYs under the same conditions.60,64,115 In other words, self-transformation of Aux NCs into Au NYs is detrimental to PEC water splitting in a Aux NC-based photocatalytic system.60,64,119

6.5 Photocatalytic CO2 conversion

Apart from the photocatalytic and PEC water splitting reaction, solar-driven CO2 conversion to high value-added fuels (e.g., methane, methanol, etc.) represents a rather promising way to tackle the increasing energy crisis and deteriorating greenhouse effect. Nevertheless, the lack of active sites caused by tight ligand protection on Aux NCs increases the difficulty of CO2 conversion in a Aux NC-based photocatalytic system, although Aux NCs can potentially extend the light absorption to the visible range. Specifically, tight combination of the ligand and Aux NCs restrains the photoelectron transfer from the internal sector of Aux NCs to the external region, cutting off the contact between the electrons with reducibility and CO2 molecules.

To overcome this problem, Xiong et al. developed an effective strategy to graft diverse metal cations on Aux NCs via bridging ligands for creating active sites (Fig. 22a).120 As shown in Fig. 22b, grafted isolated metal cations (Fe2+, Co2+, Ni2+, and Cu2+) enable the electron transport from the internal surface of Aux NCs to the external surface, and then act as catalytic active sites for reducing CO2 molecules. By DFT calculations (Fig. 22c and d), it was ascertained that different single metal sites possess varied d-band centers and binding energy with CO2 molecules, which allows controlling the electron transfer efficiency and CO2 activation energy for tuning the activity and selectivity.120,121 Unfortunately, Aux NC-based photocatalysts exhibit discouraging stability, for example, Aux NCs inevitably agglomerate and thus lose activities under light irradiation. To enhance the photostability, as demonstrated by the authors, metalorganic frameworks were employed as the encapsulation shell to protect the Aux NC-based catalyst inside. This work endows Aux NCs with new opportunities for photocatalytic CO2 conversion and provides an effective strategy for modifying the surface active sites of metal NCs toward solar-to-fuel conversion.

7. Conclusions and perspectives

Overall, we have summarized the latest developments in the growth mechanism and classification of gold NCs, and the photocatalytic mechanism of gold NC-based photosystems along with multiple photocatalytic applications including non-selective dye degradation, selective organic transformation, photocatalytic water splitting, photocatalytic CO2 reduction and PEC water splitting. Although the progress on metal NCs and photocatalysis as well as photoelectrocatalysis is encouraging,122139 solar-driven gold NC-based photocatalysts are still a new research field lacking in-depth study, and moreover, its photocatalytic applications are limited with solar energy conversion efficiency being far from satisfactory. From our perspective, the following several challenges should be highlighted in future research work.

Firstly, size-dependent photocatalytic rules require more in-depth investigation. Basically, gold NCs used in photocatalysis are composed of an accurate number of atoms and ligands. However, there is deficiency of systematic study on the influence of the size structure of metal NCs on the photocatalytic performance such as photoactivity and product selectivity, although nano-chemistry and optical properties of metal NCs with atomic precision have been summed up in great detail in the past few years.

Secondly, although the reaction mechanism of gold NC-based photosystems has been explored, the surface atomic state is not fully understood. For example, whether there is separation between a gold atom and ligand in the photocatalytic process is crucial to determine the active sites. In addition, it is important to determine the binding state of gold NCs with reactants and intermediates for finely tuning the catalytic reaction. Therefore, in-depth mechanism study on gold NC-based photocatalysis should be highlighted by a coupling experiment with theoretical calculations.

Thirdly, up to now, sporadic efforts have been devoted to preventing the agglomeration of metal NCs for enhancing the photostability, such as controlling the reaction conditions and capping robust ligands. Nevertheless, exploring metal NC-based photosystems with long-term photostability remains a challenging task. Thus, how to effectively improve the photostability of metal NCs in diverse photocatalytic systems while maintaining the activity needs more comprehensive investigation. We believe that coupling structure design and rational reaction condition modulation would synergistically achieve excellent long-term photo-stability of metal NCs without losing activity seriously.

Fourthly, the current photo-conversion efficiency of metal NC-based photosystems is not high enough. More modification strategies need to be explored to remarkably enhance the photocatalytic performances of metal NC-based photosystems. For example, substituting one or more gold atoms with heteroatom(s) of other metal elements for fabricating bimetallic NCs provides an exciting approach to tune the electronic structure of metal NCs, which plays an important role in dictating the photocatalytic performances.

Finally, developing Aux nanocluster based photocatalytic or PEC systems is seriously retarded by the preparation of Aux nanoclusters. Thus far, there has still been deficiency of a facile, green, easily accessible, and efficacious synthetic approach to readily prepare Aux nanoclusters under mild and convenient experimental conditions (e.g., ambient temperature, etc.). Notably, predominant synthesis methods developed in the past few years inevitably involve complicated procedures and a tedious purification process along with low separation yield, which are difficult to repeat and access by those who are engaged in Aux nanocluster-based photocatalysis and photoelectrocatalysis. Besides, fine tuning of the electronic structure of Aux nanoclusters in the preparation process to surmount the generically poor photostability of metal nanoclusters represents an emerging and effective strategy to, in essence, tackle this long-term challenging issue. In this regard, we believe that it is highly desirable to develop a truly efficient and convenient method to prepare atomically precise metal nanoclusters in an easy and controllable fashion, which would lay a solid and significant foundation for exploring a large variety of novel metal nanocluster-based photosystems in the field of photocatalysis toward solar energy conversion.

To our great delight, on-going research studies are being performed to conquer these challenges. It is expected that great progress and exciting breakthroughs would be made, and these challenges and difficulties would be perfectly overcome in the near future.
