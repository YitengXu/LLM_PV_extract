Abstract

This paper presents a theoretical investigation of the microscopic mechanisms responsible for heat transport in bulk Si, Ge and SiGe alloys, with the goal of providing insight into design rules for efficient Si-based nanostructured thermoelectric semiconductors. We carried out a detailed atomistic study of the thermal conductivity, using molecular dynamics and the Boltzmann transport equation. We investigated in detail the effects of the physical approximations underlying each approach, as well as the effect of the numerical approximations involved in the implementation of the two different methods. Our findings permitted us to understand and reconcile apparently conflicting results reported in the literature.

1 Introduction

Efficient thermoelectric energy conversion and thermal management in nanoscale devices critically depend on the design of materials with tailored thermal transport properties. Decreasing the thermal conductivity of bulk semiconductors by nanostructuring represents a promising strategy in the search for efficient thermoelectric materials.1–3 For example, considerable improvements in the thermoelectric efficiency of semiconducting nanowires with increased surface roughness,4,5 and of superlattices6,7 and nanocomposites,8 have been attributed to a significantly reduced thermal conductivity (κ). On the other hand, nanostructured materials with a high thermal conductivity could be used to efficiently extract heat from specific regions of micro- and nano-electronic devices.1

Fabrication and synthesis of nanostructures for thermoelectric and thermal management applications are formidable experimental tasks.1,9 Measuring the thermal transport properties of nanoscale materials is even more challenging.1 Atomistic simulations may complement experimental efforts and help to advance our understanding of thermal transport processes in nanoscale materials, as well as predict new routes for their control. Unlike continuum approaches, atomistic calculations can describe complex morphologies at the microscopic level, including interfaces, surfaces, impurities and defects.

In recent years, several atomistic methods (e.g. molecular dynamics,10 Boltzmann transport equation,11 and Green's function approaches12) have been used to address different aspects of thermal transport phenomena in bulk and nanoscale materials. However, the effect of the physical approximations of each method on computed properties, e.g. the thermal conductivity, is not completely understood; in addition the control of numerical approximations in thermal transport calculations is still challenging, although some notable progress has been achieved. For instance, Schelling et al.10 and He et al.13 compared the performance of equilibrium and non-equilibrium molecular dynamics (EMD and NEMD) approaches for bulk crystalline Si and amorphous Si, respectively, and reported a good agreement between the two methods. Equilibrium MD simulations have also been compared with the Boltzmann transport equation (BTE) in the relaxation time approximation11 (RTA), with lifetimes obtained from the autocorrelation function of the potential energy of each phonon mode calculated within MD.14 A good agreement between the BTE-RTA and MD methods has been observed for bulk crystalline argon and silicon, and carbon nanotubes;14–17 however, such comparisons have so far been limited to supercells of relatively modest sizes (several thousand atoms14,15,17).

One of the techniques commonly used to solve the BTE relies on a perturbative treatment of the phonon–phonon interactions, where the contributions of four or higher phonon scattering processes are neglected.11 To obtain phonon lifetimes in ordered materials, this method is in principle less computationally expensive than calculations of the potential energy autocorrelation functions needed, e.g. in the EMD approach, and allows for simulations of systems with a much larger effective number of atoms. Hereafter, we will refer to the aforementioned pertubative treatment of the BTE as simply the BTE.

MD and BTE approaches treat the effect of disorder and phonon–phonon interactions at different levels of accuracy: in MD, both effects are described exactly (to all orders in the interaction) within a perturbative treatment of the heat current; in the BTE, the two effects are accounted for approximately. On the other hand, the phonon distribution is treated classically within MD simulations, while in the BTE the correct quantum distribution is used. It is therefore important to understand the relative performance of these methods and how their limitations affect the calculated values of the thermal conductivity.

In addition to physical approximations, numerical approximations need to be controlled, in order to compare the results of different techniques. These include size effects (i.e. the size of the chosen simulation cells), simulation times in the case of MD and Brillouin zone (BZ) sampling in the case of the BTE.18–20 In many semiconductors, low frequency phonons have long lifetimes and mean free paths that may contribute significantly to the computed thermal conductivity.9 Consequently, large supercells and large sets of reciprocal space vectors should be used in MD simulations and BTE calculations, respectively, to ensure convergence. However in the literature convergence tests have often been limited to relatively small system sizes, especially in the case of MD simulations, due to high computational costs. This is definitely one of the reasons for a variety of different κ values reported for the same material, studied with the same method but with different numerical approximations.

In this paper, we present a comprehensive comparison of EMD, NEMD and BTE-RTA approaches to the calculation of the thermal conductivity of bulk crystalline Si, Ge and SiGe alloys at room temperature, using Tersoff empirical interatomic potentials.21,22 We discuss physical and numerical approximations on the thermal conductivity of these materials. In the case of crystalline bulk Si and Ge, we found that systems as large as several hundred thousand atoms are required to obtain converged thermal conductivity values using either EMD or NEMD. We show that for crystalline Si, the NEMD approach for samples with a cross section of (2.2)2 nm2 and lengths up to 250 nm gives a value of κ in agreement with EMD at 1000 K, but 50% larger than that obtained with the EMD method at 300 K; however, when compared with EMD results for (2.2)3 nm3 cubic cells, the NEMD method gives only a 17% larger value of κ at 300 K. Using the BTE-RTA approach, we show that accounting for classical phonon statistics (as adopted in MD) instead of the quantum one leads to an underestimate of the calculated κ. This effect becomes more appreciable as the temperature decreases with respect to the Debye temperature (ΘD) of the material (e.g. in the case of Si, the underestimation is ∼13% at T = 300 K for the Tersoff potential). Neglecting higher than three-phonon scattering processes in the BTE-RTA leads to an overestimation of κ with respect to EMD (e.g. in the case of Si, the overestimation is ∼38% at T = 300 K). The differences found in the case of Si at room T (classical vs. quantum, three-phonon vs. all order phonon interactions) are less pronounced in the case of Ge since the ΘD of Ge (360 K) is closer to room temperature than that of Si (645 K). In the case of SiGe alloys, samples with ∼2 million atoms are required to approach convergence of EMD simulations. Using samples larger than ∼2 million atoms would require simulation times longer than >10 ns, at present limited by the statistical noise on the long range decay of the computed heat current autocorrelation functions. In contrast, we argue that the BTE-RTA approach applied to disordered SiGe supercells will give zero thermal conductivity in the limit of infinitely large disordered cells and we suggest that a level of theory higher than the BTE needs to be used to obtain a finite non-zero value of κ. However, physically well behaved BTE-RTA results can be obtained using a virtual crystal approximation23 and the calculated κ is ∼100% larger than that obtained with EMD.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows: in Section 2, the atomistic simulation methods and interatomic potential used in this work are briefly described; in Section 3, the computed values of κ of bulk crystalline Si, Ge and SiGe alloys using different atomistic methods are discussed, and compared with existing theoretical and experimental results. Finally, we summarize our work in Section 4.

2.4 Interatomic potential

Interatomic potentials for MD and lattice dynamics calculations may be chosen in two different ways: (i) from first principles (without resorting to any free parameters), e.g. using density functional theory (DFT);29 and (ii) empirically, by assuming an analytical form to describe the potential with parameters fitted to experimental data so as to reproduce desired physical properties. Recently, first principles approaches have been successfully combined with the BTE to calculate the thermal conductivity of several semiconducting materials30–33 with small unit cells. Coupling first principles Hamiltonians and MD thermal transport simulations is a much more challenging task, especially so in the case of equilibrium MD, where conceptual difficulties exist in defining a local energy (eqn (3) and (5)). Recently, a few attempts to carry out phonon lifetimes calculation using EMD have been reported, as well as thermal conductivity calculations using NEMD with Kohn–Sham Hamiltonians;34,35 these calculations were limited to small system sizes (up to 512 atoms) and short simulation times (30 ps).

At present, empirical interatomic potentials are the only suitable class of potentials to compare results on the thermal conductivity of semiconducting systems using different levels of theory, in particular MD and the BTE. For Si and Ge several empirical interatomic potentials have been proposed in the literature: Stillinger–Weber,36 Pearson et al.,37 environment dependent interatomic potential (EDIP)38 and Tersoff.21,22 The Tersoff potential has been used to study surface reconstructions and the phase diagram of SiGe alloys,39 and has given results consistent with experiment.40 In addition, the Tersoff potential describes accurately the properties of nontetrahedral forms of Si.21 Therefore, the Tersoff parametrization has been the potential of choice in this work.

3 Results and discussions

3.1 Thermal conductivity of bulk crystalline silicon

Experiments and previous simulations. Bulk crystalline silicon (c-Si) is not a good candidate material for thermoelectric power generation, due to its large lattice thermal conductivity. Recent experimental and theoretical studies have shown that nanostructuring can tremendously enhance the thermoelectric efficiency of Si-based materials by reducing the lattice thermal conductivity without degrading the electronic properties.41,42 Since Si is an Earth abundant, cheap and nontoxic material, many research efforts in the field of thermoelectrics have focused on Si-based materials. At room temperature, the heat transport in Si is mainly dominated by lattice vibrations (phonons),43 and thus the study of the lattice thermal conductivity of c-Si is a key prerequisite to understand heat transfer in bulk elemental ordered semiconductors, as well as to provide a basis to devise design rules for efficient Si-based nanostructured thermoelectric semiconductors.

The measured values of κ of natural silicon at 300 K range from 130 to 150 W m−1 K−1.43–48 In 1997, Capinski et al.46 used an optical pump-and-probe technique to measure κ of isotopically pure Si. Their ∼11 μm thick sample was made from 99.7% 28Si, and obtained by liquid phase epitaxy. They found a 60% increase in κ of isotopically pure silicon compared to that of natural samples at 300 K. Later Ruf et al.49 measured the κ of highly enriched 28Si (99.8588%) samples with the volume of ∼2 × 2 × 20 mm3 by means of a steady-state heat flow technique at room temperature. They obtained a result consistent with that of Capinski,46 but subsequently they reported a corrected 10% ratio between the κ of isotopically pure and natural Si.50 In 2004, Kremer and co-authors47,48 measured the κ of isotopically enriched 28Si independently in three laboratories on four samples of different shapes and degrees of isotope enrichment, and sizes of ∼2 × 2 × 20 mm3. They reported good agreement between the results obtained in different laboratories. They found that at room temperature the thermal conductivity of isotopically enriched 28Si exceeds the thermal conductivity of natural Si by only 10 ± 2%. As of yet no complete consensus has been reached about the measured value of κ of isotopically enriched 28Si. Recently Johnson et al.51 demonstrated that at room temperature, the thermal conductivity of c-Si samples of micron size significantly deviates from the one predicted by a simple diffusion model; they thus concluded that to achieve an accurate measurement of the κ of bulk Si, one may not use samples of micron size, but larger samples are required. On the theoretical side, the κ of c-Si using MD techniques was first computed by Volz and Chen25 in 2000. By direct integration of the heat current autocorrelation function obtained from the Green–Kubo formula, these authors computed κ using the Stillinger–Weber (SW) potential36 and equilibrium molecular dynamics. They found a strong size-dependence of the computed conductivity. For example, the calculated κ at 500 K could not be converged even when using cells with 8000 atoms. To obtain a converged result, Volz and Chen extrapolated the computed values using an estimated relaxation time obtained from the heat current autocorrelation function. The fitted results at 300 K are about 70% higher than the measured κ of natural Si. However, using the same empirical potential (SW), several authors (e.g. Schelling et al.,10 Sellan et al.20 and Goicochea et al.16) later reported a weaker size dependence of κ of c-Si. In particular Schelling claimed that a converged value of κ (≈66 W m−1 K−1 at 1000 K) may be obtained by performing EMD simulations with only 1728 atoms. Goicochea et al.16 found a value of κ of about 350 W m−1 K−1 at 300 K by using cells with 512 atoms (130% higher than measured ones). Convergence tests up to 4000 atoms were conducted in ref. 10, 16, and 20. A study of domain size effects on κ of c-Si was also reported by Sun and Murthy.19 Using the environment dependent interatomic potential (EDIP)38 and systems with up to about 30000 atoms, these authors claimed that a converged value of κ at 300 K may be obtained using a small system with 216 atoms. The first atomistic BTE calculations of the thermal conductivity of c-Si reported in the literature used an iterative solution of the linearized Boltzmann equation, but still relied on a relatively simple pairwise central interatomic potential.57 Subsequent papers53,58 explored in more detail the ability of different empirical interatomic potentials to describe the κ of c-Si, and obtained values that are at least 100% larger than experiment. Recently, first principles DFT Hamiltonians have been employed to self-consistently solve the BTE,30,32 yielding a very good agreement with the measurements of ref. 47 and 48. Other first principles BTE studies31,56 used the computationally less intensive relaxation time approximation and reported values similar to those obtained using a self-consistent procedure30 (see Table 2).

Equilibrium MD results. All our EMD simulations were carried out using the LAMMPS code,59 with periodic boundary conditions applied to all three directions, and a time step of 0.5 fs. The initial configurations of all bulk crystalline silicon (c-Si) samples considered in this study were obtained by replicating a cubic diamond unit cell with lattice constant a = 5.4 Å (experimental value). Each sample was equilibrated at 300 K and zero pressure (P) using the NPT ensemble first, to determine the equilibrium density, followed by simulations in the NVT ensemble. After equilibration, the heat current was obtained within the NVE ensemble using eqn (6), and the thermal conductivity was calculated by eqn (1). The heat current autocorrelation function was computed using fast Fourier transforms.60 The convergence of κ of c-Si as a function of simulation time was carefully tested. Our results showed that simulations of the order of 10 ns are necessary to obtain a converged value of κ for all sizes. Therefore, all MD runs for c-Si reported in Fig. 3 were carried out for 10 ns.

We also tested the convergence of κ with respect to the truncation time used in the integration of the heat current autocorrelation function <J(t)J(0)> = CJ(t) in eqn (1). Fig. 2 shows the computed CJ(t) as a function of the correlation time and κ as a function of the truncation time for a silicon sample with 4096 atoms. A truncation time of 200 ps yielded well converged values. In order to test size effects, we carried out simulations for supercells of various sizes. We find that to obtain a converged value of κ of c-Si, one has to use cells with at least ∼64000 atoms. Convergence is not monotonic, as it depends on the quality of sampling phonons that act as heat carriers and phonon scatterers at the same time. As shown in Fig. 3, the calculated values of κ using supercells with 64000, 216000 and 1000000 atoms are the same within error bars. Our results on size convergence may explain the conflicting results for κ reported in the literature.16,19,25 Our converged value of κ of c-Si is 196.8 ± 33.3 W m−1 K−1. Given the complexity of real silicon materials (including defects, grain boundaries, and impurities), we consider the agreement with experiment satisfactory, although it clearly represents an overestimate of the experimental value.

Non-equilibrium MD results. We also computed κ of c-Si using the NEMD method. This approach is analogous to experimental methods, where a temperature gradient is created within the material, and the thermal conductivity is obtained from the ratio between the measured heat flow and the temperature gradient. In order to use Fourier's law (eqn (7)) to calculate the thermal conductivity, one has to determine the linear response regime in which the inverse of the thermal conductivity linearly depends on the inverse of the sample length.

In this work, we chose c-Si samples with a cross section of (2.2)2 nm2 at both 300 K and 1000 K. For the same cross section, the heat current is inversely proportional to the number of time steps (w) by which the velocities of cold and hot atoms are exchanged (see Fig. 4). By computing κ with various values of w (w = 20, 40, 60, 80, 100, 140 and 180) for a sample of dimensions 2.2 × 2.2 × 50 nm3, we found that linear temperature profiles can be obtained by using w = 100 steps, i.e. 50 fs.

At 300 K, as we can see from Fig. 7, only the values obtained for the cells with lengths larger than ∼100 nm appear to be linear. The linearly fitted value of κ at 300 K is 295.8 ± 20.1 W m−1 K−1, which is not in agreement with our EMD converged result (196.8 ± 33.3 W m−1 K−1); however it is compatible with the value of κ = 253.3 ± 14.2 W m−1 K−1 obtained with EMD simulations and samples with 512 atoms (size: (2.2)2 nm2). The differences between EMD and NEMD results indicate that the κ of c-Si at room T, calculated by NEMD, depends both on the cross section and the length of the simulation cells. Sellan et al.20 argued that the linear extrapolation procedure used in NEMD is accurate only when the minimum size is comparable to the largest mean-free-paths of the phonons that dominate the thermal transport. They suggested that to obtain accurate κ of bulk silicon using NEMD, one needs to simulate systems with lengths of 3.3 μm at 500 K, and 1.5 μm at 1000 K, indicating that at 300 K NEMD simulations may need samples with length even larger than 3.3 μm. This claim is supported by the recent experiment of Johnson et al.51 Although the experimental study of Ju and Goodson61 reported that the effective mean-free path of the dominant phonons of c-Si at room temperature is ∼300 nm (comparable to the lengths used in our NEMD simulations), Johnson et al.51 claimed that at 300 K phonons with mean free paths larger than 1 μm could contribute to half of the value of the thermal conductivity of c-Si. Therefore, we concluded that the determination of the appropriate supercell size (in terms of both length and cross-section) needed in NEMD simulations to recover the regime of applicability of the Fourier law, and obtain converged κ at 300 K, is still an open question.

Finally we note that the rate of change of 1/κ with 1/Lz is not temperature-dependent in our simulations, at variance from the findings of Schelling et al.10 who used a Stillinger–Weber potential. These discrepancies may be explained, at least in part, by the different empirical potentials and the sizes of simulated systems used here (Lz up to 200 nm at 300 K in this work, and up to 150 nm at 500 K in ref. 10).

To understand this result in more detail, we show classical BTE-RTA and EMD lifetimes as a function of frequency in Fig. 9(b). EMD lifetimes for each normal mode were obtained from the exponential decay of the autocorrelation function of the potential energy.14 Such simulations are computationally very intensive and here we show EMD lifetimes for a relatively small supercell (1000 atoms). Size effects are apparent in the low frequency part of the spectrum which are not properly probed by EMD with small cells. Nevertheless, the classical BTE-RTA and EMD lifetimes are comparable in the entire range of frequencies accessible to the EMD calculation. We speculate that at very low frequencies, lifetimes obtained with only three-phonon terms may be overestimated with respect to those we would obtain with calculations at all orders, thus leading to a higher value of κ within the BTE-RTA than that in EMD.

We note that our calculated quantum BTE-RTA value of κ, 310 W m−1 K−1, is smaller than that reported in ref. 53 and obtained with the same potential and using the same physical approximations (κ ∼ 370 W m−1 K−1). One of the possible reasons for this discrepancy is the sparse q point quadrature grid used in ref. 53 that may exhibit a κ convergence trend opposite to ours (larger q point meshes give smaller κ, as reported in Fig. 1 of ref. 53). Therefore the value reported in ref. 53 may not be fully converged. The value of κ of c-Si obtained in our work (310 W m−1 K−1) is approximately 2 times larger than the experimental values47,48 and than that obtained with first principles BTE simulations.30,31,56 Our preliminary first principles BTE results63 suggest that the inadequacy of the Tersoff potential (and short-range potentials in general) to describe the transverse acoustic branches is the main reason for this disagreement.

3.3 Thermal conductivity of bulk silicon germanium alloys

Experiments and previous simulations. Silicon germanium (SiGe) alloys are Si-based systems with structural order and mass disorder. They are promising candidates for thermoelectric applications due to a much lower thermal conductivity than Si and Ge, and they have long been used to build thermoelectric modules for the international Solar Polar mission to convert radio-isotope heat into electricity.70,71 However, the efficiency of thermoelectric devices built from bulk SiGe alloys has its peak at high temperature (∼1100 K) and is not yet high enough to make them competitive with conventional generators. Such devices can only work efficiently at high temperature. Recent progress in nanotechnology has triggered interest in trying to reduce the thermal conductivity of bulk SiGe alloys by using low dimensional and nano-confined structures.2

In general, SiGe samples used in thermal conductivity measurements may have a different degree of crystallinity: the zone-leveled samples are closest to ideal single crystals, followed by those grown by the die-casting technique, and finally, the pressure sintered samples contain large amounts of grain boundaries. The measured thermal conductivity is expected to increase with a higher degree of crystallinity. Furthermore, the level of doping (from heavily n- to heavily p-doping) greatly varies in different samples, and this also affects the thermal conductivity values, as it affects the electronic contribution to κ. Here we considered single crystalline undoped samples, and therefore we compared our results with the measurements on lightly doped zone-leveled or die-cast samples. In such samples, the electronic contribution to κ is likely to be negligible. In Table 5 we summarized the relevant experimental thermal conductivities at room T for bulk SiGe with 65 to 75% of Si.

Large statistical noise in the tail of the HCACF is another issue related to the convergence of SiGe κ using EMD. It was previously established that the short time scale of HCACF is associated with high frequency vibrations, and the longer one is associated with low frequency vibrations.78 We found that the decay rate of the short time scale is similar for the different system sizes, while the decay rate of the longer time scale is significantly different, and gradually increases as the size of the systems increases. To investigate whether mass disorder is the main effect in determining the low thermal conductivity of Si0.5Ge0.5, two simulations were carried out for a chosen cell size (216000 atoms): we considered one system with the same mass disorder as that in Si0.5Ge0.5, but whose Si and Ge bonds are described by the Si Tersoff potential; we then considered a system with the same mass disorder as that in Si0.5Ge0.5, but whose Si and Ge bonds are described by the Ge Tersoff potential. The results show that the difference in chemical bonds between Si and Ge plays a minor albeit not negligible role in determining the reduction of the value of κ, while mass disorder has a major effect (see Fig. 14).

Most of the experimental values of κ pertain to Si1−xGex samples with x = 0.2–0.3. In order to compare with experimental results, we estimated the κ of Si1−xGex for x = 0.2–0.5 using a small system with 4096 atoms. Although the absolute values obtained in this way are unconverged and underestimate the converged values, the trends as a function of concentration are expected to be qualitatively correct. Our results show that κ of Si1−xGex alloys becomes composition-independent at 0.2 < x < 0.5, consistent with experiment. Therefore, we conclude that the calculated κ of Si0.5Ge0.5 is representative of and compatible with the experimental results23,73 at lower concentrations.

Lattice dynamics results. To compute the BTE-RTA thermal conductivity of SiGe alloys, one should consider large cubic supercells that explicitly account for mass and chemical bond disorder, and then increase the size of the supercell and the wave vector grid until convergence is achieved, similar to what is done in EMD simulations. This is a challenging computational task since the BTE algorithm scales as (N4Nq2).79 To overcome these difficulties, we implemented two approaches to calculate the thermal conductivity of large systems: (i) we fitted the lifetimes of small supercells to a power law (τ−1 = Aωb) for frequencies up to 200 cm−1 (higher frequencies contribute to the total κ less than a few percent e.g. for supercells with 512 atoms this contribution is 1%); we then used fitted lifetimes to compute κ of large systems through eqn (12); (ii) we developed an atomistic Monte Carlo approach to solve the BTE in the RTA.80 Both approaches give essentially the same results as the BTE-RTA method using exact integration for small supercells, but they are computationally less intensive than exact integration and allow for computations for large systems with supercells of several thousands of atoms.

Fig. 17 shows our thermal conductivity results obtained using the procedure (i) described above as a function of the Si0.5Ge0.5 cubic supercell length. The convergence of κ is not achieved even for supercells as large as 13824 atoms and q point grids that amount to an effective number of atoms as large as 7 million. This is a consequence of the fact that, although lifetimes converge as a function of increasing cell size, group velocity decrease and eventually vanish for infinitely large disordered supercells27 (except for the zero frequency one, that does not contribute to heat conduction). The BTE value for κ in the limit of infinitely large disordered cells should therefore be zero, as pointed out in ref. 27. This finding suggests that higher-level lattice dynamics theories than the BTE need to be employed to obtain a finite non-zero κ for disordered systems.

The convergence issue found here in solving the BTE for alloys (also reported in ref. 55) may be commonly circumvented by using a so called virtual crystal approximation (VCA).23 The basic assumption of this model is that the effect of mass disorder on SiGe κ is weak enough so that one may use the virtual crystal approximation. The virtual crystal is described by the interatomic potential, masses and lattice parameter interpolated between those of Si and Ge. Virtual impurities are accounted for via different masses than those of the virtual crystal (i.e. impurities have masses of Si or Ge). By treating these mass differences as weak perturbations, a phonon lifetime term due to mass disorder is derived and combined with three-phonon lifetimes (given in eqn (16)) via Matheissen's rule.31 Consequently, the group velocities are well defined in this model and converged κ values may be obtained.31,76 We have performed calculations similar to the VCA, using the interatomic potential of Si or Ge (so called average mass approximation). As shown in Fig. 17, our classical BTE-RTA results are approximately a factor of 2 larger than the EMD values. Reconciling this approach with more accurate lattice dynamics approaches that can account explicitly for disorder remains an open problem.

4 Conclusions

We explored the consequences of using different physical and numerical approximations to compute the thermal conductivity of bulk crystalline Si, Ge and SiGe alloys. Our results showed that equilibrium molecular dynamics is a robust method that can describe thermal transport in both ordered (Si, Ge) and disordered (SiGe) materials provided that large samples (105−106 atoms) are simulated over a long period of time (≥10 ns) at temperature close to or above the ΘD. Based on our comparison between results obtained with EMD and the quantum BTE-RTA approach, we conclude that EMD simulations using a classical statistics for the phonons tend to underestimate the thermal conductivity for T < ΘD, possibly as much as ∼15% in the case of Si at room T (although a precise value of this underestimate could not be obtained from such comparison). For crystalline Si, we showed that non-equilibrium molecular dynamics for samples with small cross-sections of the order of (2.2)2 nm2 gives slightly larger values than those of EMD on cubic samples with the same cross-section, and ∼50% larger values than converged EMD calculations at 300 K. These results, combined with the finding that to converge EMD simulations, tens of thousands to million atoms samples are necessary, suggest that extremely large samples (with over 106 atoms) with large cross-sections should be used in non-equilibrium simulations to obtain reliable thermal conductivity values. Therefore although appealing for its simplicity and its ease of generalization to include ab initio Hamiltonians, NEMD appears to pose serious efficiency issues, and to be much more computationally demanding than EMD.

The lattice dynamics based Boltzmann transport approach in the relaxation time approximation gives converged thermal conductivity values for ordered Si and Ge materials for samples of effective sizes comparable to equilibrium molecular dynamics, but at a much lower computational effort. In the case of SiGe alloys, our simulations showed that the approximations used to derive the BTE within the RTA and even the linearized BTE are too crude to describe the thermal conductivity of mass disordered alloys. Unless further approximations (such as virtual crystal) are introduced to overcome the problem of vanishing group velocities for infinitely large cells, the BTE-RTA may not be used to compute conductivity of completely disordered alloys. In the case of ordered systems, one of the main advantages of the Boltzmann approach compared to EMD is the ability to compute phonon lifetimes and group velocities of large samples, and thus provide a detailed, atomistic interpretation of the various contributions to the conductivity and ultimately also of the EMD results. However, phonon lifetimes calculated with the BTE-RTA approach account for three-phonon scattering rates only, and we showed that higher phonon processes may give a non-negligible contribution to κ. e.g. in the case of c-Si, when using the Tersoff empirical interatomic potential.

In summary, we have presented a comparative analysis of the thermal conductivity of Si, Ge and SiGe alloys obtained by using MD and the BTE-RTA, which establishes the limit of validity of the physical approximations underlying each approach. We have also presented an analysis of the numerical approximations involved in each calculation. Our findings provide important guidance for the comparison of simulation results with experiments as well as for the comparison of computed values of the conductivity in bulk and nanostructured SiGe based materials.
