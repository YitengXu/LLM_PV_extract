Abstract

Luminescent solar concentrators (LSCs) are luminescent waveguide layers that convert sunlight into specific wavelengths which are then guided by total internal reflection to a PV device located at the edges of the LSC. Their ability to concentrate sunlight onto small areas makes LSCs a useful complement to silicon-based PVs in a series of applications, such as urban integration and flexible fabrics towards mobile solar-energy. Challenges for the luminescent layer include the use of low-cost and sustainable nature-based organic molecules. We report novel chlorophyll-based LSCs with emission properties in the red/NIR spectral region. Here, chlorophyll molecules extracted from Spirulina maxima, an abundant cyanobacterium and an attractive natural source, are immobilized in organic–inorganic di- and tri-ureasil matrices enabling the production of sustainable LSCs. At low chlorophyll concentrations (<3 × 1017 molecules per cm3), the photophysical properties of the chlorophyll molecules after incorporation into the hybrids closely resemble those in ethanolic solution (with an absolute emission quantum yield of ∼0.16 and a fluorescence lifetime of ∼8 ns). The LSCs were coupled to a Si-based commercial PV device revealing optical conversion efficiency and power conversion efficiency values of ∼3.70% and 0.10%, respectively, illustrating the potential of this approach for the development of nature-based LSCs meeting the requirements of reliable, sustainable and competitive energy systems.

Introduction

The reliable supply of clean and affordable energies is a crucial challenge of the 21st century. This implies an increase in the use of renewable energies, preferentially those involving primary sources such as in the case of photovoltaics (PV).1,2 Among the several approaches in the literature to boost the PV cell performance, emphasis is given to luminescent solar concentrators (LSCs), whose number of related reports has exploded in the last few years,3–9 since the first reports in the 1970s.10–12 LSCs are complementary PV devices comprising a transparent matrix embedding optically active centres that absorb incident radiation, which is then re-emitted at a specific wavelength and transferred by total internal reflection to PV cells located at the edges of the matrix.10,12 The LSC's ability to concentrate sunlight onto small areas can contribute to reduce the size and cost of PV cells.5 The coupling of PV cells to the LSC edges is a very interesting strategy to PV urban integration, as LSCs could be embedded in façades, roofs, windows and walls and coupled to efficient and small PV cells, allowing everyday buildings to be transformed into harvesting machines9 and embedded in wearable flexible fabrics towards low-cost and mobile solar-energy harvesting.13

Featuring the LSCs' role as a complement to PV cell development,5 the design of efficient LSCs requires knowledge of the target PV cell, in particular of the material band gap energy that determines both the region of the solar spectrum that is absorbed and the efficiency of the PV cell, readily establishing the desired optical characteristics (absorption and emission range) of the LSC layer.14 Nowadays, c-Si PV cells are the leading technology in the PV market with a maximum reported efficiency of 26.7%,15 which is very close to the theoretical maximum established by the Shockley–Queisser limit of ∼33%.16,17 To improve the PV conversion efficiency based on the development of new solar cells we note the emerging field of perovskite-based PV cells that, over the last few years, have gained visibility due to the unprecedented increase in their efficiency values in such a short period of time (∼6 years).18,19 The 23.6% record for a tandem PV cell composed of perovskite and silicon was recently reported by K. Bush et al.20 Despite these promising advances, efficiency is still low, especially in the UV spectral region and improving their stability is now an important issue, since they degrade under ambient conditions, when exposed to air, moisture and UV radiation.21,22

Both c-Si and perovskite PV cells display large external quantum efficiency (EQE) in the red/NIR spectral region (∼700 nm to 1100 nm), and, therefore, red/NIR emitting LSCs are looked-for to maximize the performance of these PV cells. Despite some examples mentioning the potential of NIR-emitting quantum dots (QDs),23–27 for which the optical conversion efficiency (ηopt defined as the ratio between the output optical power, Pout, and the incident optical power, Pin) could reach values up to 14.6%,24 lower experimental ηopt values have been reported so far.2,28–33 For single-edge planar NIR-based LSCs based on QDs, namely PbS,28,29 PbS/CdS30 and CISeS/ZnS,2ηopt is below 6.1%,30 and for LSCs based on organic dyes, such as silicon 2,3-naphthalocyanine bis(trihexylsilyloxide) (SiNc) immobilized in an organic–inorganic tri-ureasil matrix, ηopt = 1.5%.33 Larger values for red/NIR emitting LSCs were only reported for cylindrical devices based on PbS QDs (ηopt = 6.5%).28

The potential replacement of QDs and synthetic organic dyes with red/NIR luminescent nature-based organic molecules extracted from natural pigments could make LSCs cheaper and sustainable, as well as providing other intrinsic properties such as synthetic versatility, and high absorption coefficients and emission quantum yield.34

Aiming at following a sustainable route for designing LSCs, photosynthesis may be an inspiring natural mechanism if PV solar energy conversion is compared to an artificial photosynthesis process which is stopped short.35 The optical and electrical functions of LSCs and their attached PV cells mimic the light collection and charge generation processes, respectively.35 In photosynthesis, one key molecule is chlorophyll as it is responsible for sunlight harvesting36 with emission properties in the red/NIR spectral region. The attractive properties of nature-based dye molecules for LSCs have been poorly explored. Emphasis has been given to exploring the Förster energy transfer to minimize re-absorption losses. Examples include LSCs using the BODIPY emitter covalently bound to oligofluorene dispersed in a polymer matrix of lauryl methacrylate and ethylene glycol dimethacrylate with a maximum EQE of ∼2.44% (ref. 34) and phycobilisomes in acrylamide films35,37 with optical quantum efficiency (defined as the fraction of incident photons that are emitted from the edges of the substrates) quantified through an integrating sphere of 12.5%.35

In this work, novel chlorophyll-based LSCs are processed and optically characterised. The chlorophyll molecules were extracted from Spirulina maxima which is an abundant cyanobacterium and an attractive natural source. To enable easy and controlled processing of planar LSCs, the chlorophyll molecules were incorporated into amine-functionalized organic–inorganic hybrids (di- and tri-ureasils) with the added advantages of avoiding the formation of non-luminescent dye-clusters or dye-aggregates,33 enhancing quantum yield and photostability.38 The chlorophyll-based LSCs were coupled to a Si-based PV device, revealing ηopt and power conversion efficiency (PCE) values around ∼3.70% and 0.10%, respectively, demonstrating the huge potential of nature-inspired LSCs for sustainable PV energy conversion. A quantitative evaluation of the chlorophyll-based LSC performance was corroborated by Monte Carlo ray-tracing simulations, indicating that the optical efficiency can still be maintained for larger devices.

Experimental

Materials and methods

Synthesis and processing. Jeffamine® ED-600 (97%, Huntsman), Jeffamine® T-5000 (97%, Huntsman), 3-isocyanatopropyl triethoxysilane (ICPTES, 95%, Aldrich), ethanol (EtOH, 99.8%, Fluka Riedel-de Haën and Fisher Scientific), tetrahydrofuran (THF, 99.9%, Sigma-Aldrich), hydrochloric acid (HCl, 37%, Sigma-Aldrich), and chlorophyll extracts (from Spirulina maxima) were used as received. High purity distilled water was used in all experiments.
Non-hydrolyzed hybrid precursors, d-UPTES(600) and t-UPTES(5000). Both precursors, d-UPTES(600) and t-UPTES(5000), were synthesized by reacting each of the –NH2 groups of Jeffamine® (2 from ED-600 and 3 from T-5000) with the –NCO group of ICPTES (1:2 molar ratio for ED-600 and 1:3 for T-5000), in THF (2:1 v/v THF/Jeffamine®), at 82 °C under reflux and magnetic stirring for 18 h. The t-UPTES(5000) precursor was obtained as a viscous transparent liquid after rotary evaporation to remove THF. The same purification process yielded a viscous yellowish liquid for d-UPTES(600). Both suspensions were transferred to a capped transparent glass bottle and stored at 10 °C.
Extraction and purification of chlorophyll a from Spirulina maxima39. Chlorophyll was obtained from Spirulina maxima via solvent extraction and purified by column chromatography. An amount of 5 g of Spirulina maxima powder (supplied by a local Brazilian pharmacy shop) was dispersed in 50 mL of dichloromethane (Synth PA-ACS) in a 250 mL Erlenmeyer flask at room temperature. The suspension was kept under magnetic stirring for 20 minutes and thereupon, filtered on standard filter paper (pleated fold). The liquid phase, containing chlorophyll-a (chl-a), β-carotene and xanthophylls, was transferred to an amber flask and stored in a refrigerator. The solid phase was re-suspended in 50 mL of dichloromethane and underwent the same extraction process 3 additional times. The extract solution (200 mL) was concentrated to a final volume of 5 mL on a rotary evaporator under reduced pressure. Due to the presence of light in the environment where the extraction and purification processes are carried out, all the flasks containing the extract must be lightproof to avoid photo-degradation of chl-a. The extract purification was performed on a silica gel (200–400 mesh, 2 cm in diameter and 15 cm high) chromatographic column, packed with toluene. To prevent oxidation of the chlorophyll molecules, the column was wrapped with a black plastic film and the eluates were collected in amber flasks. The first elution was performed with the same column packing solvent (150 mL of toluene) to remove carotenes and xanthophyll, which are detected by the presence of yellow and orange colours. The elution of the dark blue/green solution, rich in chl-a, was performed with dichloromethane. Dark green chl-a crystals (ChE) were obtained from the dichloromethane solution, by removing the solvent in a rotatory evaporator (over the course of the process, the solution was kept in a lightproof flask). The ChE were transferred to lightproof Petri dishes and stored within a vacuum desiccator at 15 °C to avoid auto-oxidation processes.
Synthesis of the chlorophyll-doped hybrid thin films and monoliths. The ChE were dissolved in ethanol (P.A. ACS reagent 96%) under magnetic stirring for 15 minutes at room temperature. The d-UPTES(600) (or t-UPTES(5000)) precursor suspension was added to this solution and kept under magnetic stirring for 30 minutes at room temperature. Distinct concentrations of ChE were calculated since ChE presents a 95% purity (predominantly chl-a) and a molar mass of 893.51 g mol−1 (Table 1). The volume of ethanol used to dissolve each mass was 2 mL and the mass of the viscous suspension of each ureasil precursor was 3 g. The molecular density of ChE in the final materials (Table 1) was calculated based on the premise that all the solvent (added or produced over the sol–gel condensation) evaporated after the processing, and that the density of the resulting hybrids was ∼1.0 g cm−3. To obtain the monoliths and thin films, part of the as-prepared ChE-containing suspensions was submitted to a sol–gel transition, by adding an ethanolic solution of HCl (1.2 M) as a catalyst [400 μL for d-UPTES(600) and 200 μL for t-UPTES(5000)]. Part of the volumes was cast onto polystyrene molds (1.0 × 1.0 × 4.0 cm3), covered with Parafilm, and kept at 40 °C for 3 days, yielding greenish-transparent monoliths. The remaining volumes of the suspensions were deposited on glass substrates (NORMAX, 7.6 × 2.6 × 0.1 cm3) by a one-step spin-coating process (SPIN 150-NPP, APT) at 1000 rpm for 60 s, under ambient conditions. The resulting films were heat-treated at 45 °C for 24 h to remove the residual solvents. Refractive index dispersion curves and thickness (10–13 × 10−6 m) of the films were measured by spectroscopic ellipsometry (see ESI for details, Fig. S1, S2 and Table S1†). The reported values for the thickness and refractive index are the average of three measurements performed for each sample with a maximum standard deviation of 1%. Results and discussion

The local-structure of the chlorophyll-based organic–inorganic hybrids was studied by X-ray diffraction (XRD) and 29Si magic-angle spinning (MAS) NMR spectroscopy, revealing that the local structure of the organic–inorganic hosts, d-U(600)43,44 and t-U(5000),45,46 remained essentially unaltered after the inclusion of the chlorophyll molecules (Fig. S3 and S4 in the ESI†). The preservation of the hybrids' local structure after the incorporation of organic dye molecules was also observed for tri-ureasils doped with SiNC, revealing that ureasils are efficient hosts for dye molecules.33

The electrostatic interaction between the d-U(600) and t-U(5000) hosts and the chlorophyll molecules was further studied by ATR/FT-IR spectroscopy (Fig. 2 and S5 in the ESI†). The intrinsic molecular electron density distribution in chlorophyll molecules is characterized by (i) polar bonds to magnesium located on the chlorin ring; (ii) a few polar functional groups and (iii) a hydrocarbon tail, and thus, the interaction with the hybrids' amide non-polar groups by means of London forces is expectable. In this sense, the ATR-FTIR analysis focuses on the amide I (1700–1600 cm−1) and amide II (1600–1500 cm−1) regions,47Fig. 2(a). The hydrogen bonding in amide I and II regions arises from interactions between urea groups of different chains, and between bonded urea groups and the PEO chains.47 The amide I region provides information about the degree of hydrogen bonding interactions, mainly associated with νCO (1637 cm−1), Fig. 2(b and c), which are involved in more ordered hydrogen-bonded urea–urea associations.47 The absence of the peak at 1750 cm−1 in the chl-doped hybrids indicates that there are no free urea groups (not associated by means of hydrogen bonds). After the incorporation of the chlorophyll, the position of the energetic peak of amide I remains constant (1637 cm−1), Fig. 2(b and c), indicating that the urea–urea aggregates are unaffected as the chlorophyll concentration increases.48 However, a narrowing of the peak full-width-at-half-maximum (fwhm) is observed as the concentration of chlorophyll increases, Fig. 2(d). The larger variation of the fwhm in the t-U(5000)-based hybrids suggests a stronger interaction between the chlorophyll molecules and t-U(5000) compared with that occurring with d-U(600).


Fig. 2 (a) ATR-FTIR spectra of d-U(600) and t-U(5000) hosts (black lines) and of selected chl-doped hybrids (red lines). (b) and (c) are amplifications of the amide I region. (d) Amide I fwhm peak as a function of the chl concentration; the dashed lines are visual guides.

Focusing on amide II, the energy and fwhm of the dominant peak at 1560 cm−1 remain unchanged, as the chlorophyll concentration increases in both hybrids (a negligible red-shift below 1.6 cm−1 was detected for the d-U(600)-based materials). Thus, it can be concluded that chlorophyll (or pheophytin) molecules interact with oxygen atoms from the carbonyl groups, which also leads to a reorganization of hydrogen bonds in the amide I environment,48 in which the observed narrowing, Fig. 2(d), can be associated with a more organized environment induced by the chlorophyll incorporation.

The predominant interaction of the chlorophyll molecules with the oxygen atoms of the urea groups rather than with those from the PEO chains is also inferred from the ATR/FT-IR spectra acquired at lower frequencies (Fig. S5 in the ESI†). In this energetic region, the transitions are mainly associated with the PEO chains, namely at 1350 cm−1 (ωCH2) and 1250–1195 cm−1 (τCH2), the siliceous nanodomains at 1083 cm−1 (νCO and νSiOSi for SiO2 clusters) and polar groups attached to the chlorin ring of chlorophyll and pheophytin at 1031 cm−1 (νCO and νCOCH3) and 918 cm−1 (νCC and ρCH2). Apart from a minor blue-shift (∼7 cm−1) of the maximum frequency of the peak at 1083 cm−1 and a decrease in the relative intensity between this peak and that at 918 cm−1 for d-U(600) samples, no significant changes are observed after the chlorophyll incorporation.

The light-harvesting ability of all the chlorophyll-based hybrids was studied by absorption spectroscopy, Fig. 3 and S6 in the ESI†. To enable an accurate interpretation, the same analysis was performed for the isolated chlorophyll molecules by diluting a Spirulina maxima extract in ethanol, as illustrated in Fig. 3(a). The Spirulina maxima-related spectrum reveals two main components at 415 nm and 665 nm ascribed to the monomer form of chl-a, and two shoulders around 465 nm and 648 nm attributed to the contribution of chlorophyll b (chl-b) monomers. The larger relative intensity of chl-a, compared with that of chl-b points out a larger concentration of the chl-a species. The two main absorption bands of chl-a are ascribed to π–π* transitions, where the high-energy component in the blue region (415 nm) is termed a Soret band, and the low-energy one in the red spectral range (648 nm) is ascribed to the S0(0)–S1(0) transition.49,50


Fig. 3 Absorption spectra of (a) chl-a, chl-b,50 and chlorophyll extract in an ethanolic solution [10−5 M] and of (b) tU5–chl-3 and dU6–chl-3.

After the incorporation of the Spirulina maxima extract into the hybrid hosts, the spectra reveal minor changes, when compared with those of the solutions, as illustrated in Fig. 3(b) for selected hybrid materials. Such minor changes can be correlated with the acid environment, to which the precursor suspensions were submitted over the course of the acid catalysed sol–gel transition. A slight blue shift is observed for the maximum wavelengths of the UV-Vis peaks in the spectra of the chlorophyll extract, assigned to the conversion of chl-a to pheophytin-a, when the porphyrinic Mg2+ is replaced by two H+ at low pHs.39

The spectra are clearly dominated by the chl-a contribution, reinforcing that the chlorophyll extracts are mainly from chl-a. Whilst for the Soret band a blue shift of ∼474 cm−1 (∼8 nm) is detected, the S0(0)–S1(0) transition broadens and deviates towards the red ∼159 cm−1 (∼7 nm) for both hybrid hosts, with respect to the absorption spectrum of the chlorophyll extract solution. This red-shift of the S0(0)–S1(0) transition points out the formation of dimers49,51 and the emission broadening observed after the incorporation of the chlorophyll in the hybrids is probably related to changes in the local structure of the chlorophyll molecules due to the rigidity of the host,46 as demonstrated by ATR/FT-IR (Fig. 2). The absorbance around 615 nm can be ascribed to the presence of dimers in the solid state. We note the presence of excited states within 450–550 nm (marked with an asterisk in Fig. 3(b)), most likely due to the presence of carotenoids as contaminants.52

When excited within the region of the absorption spectra, the emission of the chlorophyll-based hybrids is visible to the naked eye in the red spectral range (Fig. 4).

To discern the contribution of chl-a and chl-b, the fluorescence spectra of the ethanolic solution with Spirulina maxima extract incorporated into the hybrids were compared with those arising from diluted ethanolic solutions containing only chl-a and chl-b molecules, Fig. 5(a and b).


Fig. 5 Emission spectra of (a) chl-a and chl-b in diluted [10−5 M] ethanolic solution from ref. 56 and 57 and chlorophyll extract and (b) dU6–chl-4 and dU6–chl-1 excited at 360 nm. Excitation spectra of (c) chl-a and chl-b in diluted [10−5 M] ethanolic solution from ref. 58 and chlorophyll extract and (d) dU6–chl-4 monitored at 460 nm and 675 nm.

In good agreement with that suggested by absorption spectroscopy measurements, the emission spectra of the Spirulina maxima extract resemble those of chl-a, whose emission peak lies at around 675 nm. We note that the typical emission of chl-b around 650 nm is not discerned. The broadening of the chl-a emission spectrum for the case of Spirulina maxima suggests that chl-a experiences distinct microenvironments in the extract.53 After the extract incorporation into the hybrids, the emission spectra depend on the chlorophyll concentration, Fig. 5(b). For the less concentrated samples, the room-temperature emission spectra are dominated by the overlap between the blue-green emission attributed to electron–hole recombination occurring in the urea groups and within oxygen defects in the siliceous skeleton of the hosts43,54 and the chl-a fluorescence peak in the red spectral region,49 as illustrated in Fig. 5(b). Increasing the chlorophyll concentration, the relative intensity of the hybrid host emission decreases, Fig. 5(b), suggesting the presence of efficient hybrid-to-chlorophyll energy transfer since more molecules are located near the hybrid's emitting centres, as suggested by ATR/FT-IR data (Fig. 2). This means that there is a higher number of amide-based emitting centres transferring energy to the chlorophyll molecules, thus contributing to a continuous decrease of the hybrid's radiative transitions. The emission spectra of the lowest concentration doped samples (Fig. S7 in the ESI†) make it obvious that the t-U(5000)-based samples show a more efficient energy-transfer process between the host and the chlorophyll molecules than the d-U(600)-based ones, in good agreement with the ATR/FT-IR analysis (Fig. 2) which suggests a larger chlorophyll interaction over amide groups in t-U(5000)-based hybrids compared with the d-U(600) ones. The presence of two distinct chl-a related components (∼675 nm and 720 nm) in all the emission spectra points out the formation of aggregates in the solid state (Fig. 5(b) and S7 in the ESI†).51 Also, by increasing the chlorophyll concentration it is possible to observe a red-shift in the emission peak, relative to the absorption spectrum, suggesting the presence of J-dimers55 (Fig. S7 in the ESI†).

Focusing our attention on the chlorophyll characteristic emission, the presence of a main component at 675 nm and a shoulder at 720 nm is noticeable, ascribed to the chl-a monomeric and dimeric contributions, respectively.49,59 Another component is present at 645 nm (most evident for the d-U(600)-based samples, see Fig. S7 in the ESI†) and is also ascribed to the microenvironments of chl-a emission.56 On lowering the temperature to 11 K, the spectra of d-U(600)-based samples are dominated by the host contribution, whereas for the t-U(5000) based samples, apart from an increase in the relative intensity of the hybrid's intrinsic emission, the spectra are analogous to those acquired at 300 K (Fig. S8 in the ESI†).

Comparing the absorption and the emission spectra of the chlorophyll solutions the Stokes-shift is ∼314 cm−1 (∼14 nm, Fig. S9 in the ESI†). Upon the incorporation of chlorophyll into d-U(600) and t-U(5000), there is a decrease in the Stokes-shift to values below ∼66 cm−1 (∼3 nm) and ∼133 cm−1 (∼6 nm) for the t-U(5000) and d-U(600) based samples, respectively (Fig. S9 in the ESI†) tentatively attributed to an increase in the rigidity of the chromophore environment that excludes non-fluorescent relaxation to a ground state.60,61 Although the Stokes-shift for the hybrid-based samples is significantly lower than that measured for the chlorophyll solutions (Fig. S9 in the ESI†), the Stokes-shift increases with increasing concentration for both hybrids, indicating that greater intermolecular interactions occur between the hybrid and the chlorophyll chromophore molecules.

Fig. 5(c and d) show the excitation spectra of a diluted ethanolic solution of chl-a and chl-b and the excitation spectrum of an ethanolic solution with Spirulina maxima extract. All the spectra resemble the absorption spectra (Fig. 3), revealing essentially the main bands ascribed to chl-a excited states. Fig. 5(d) illustrates the typical excitation spectrum monitored within the chlorophyll maximum emission peak (∼675 nm) and within the hybrid emission band (∼460 nm). The former reveals the characteristic bands of chl-a, as already noticed in the absorption spectrum (Fig. 3(b)), despite a minor blue-shift when compared with the excitation spectrum of the solution of chlorophyll extract (Fig. 5(c) and S10 in the ESI†). Moreover, we note that the contribution of the hybrid host excited states to chlorophyll-related emission cannot be neglected as the excitation spectrum monitored within the host emission broad band overlaps with that monitored within the chl-a band, in particular in the UV spectral region (250–400 nm). When the excitation spectrum is monitored within the hybrid emission band (∼460 nm) the hybrid excited states appear as expected for both d-U(600) and t-U(5000) (Fig. S10 in the ESI†) showing excitation maxima peaking between 350 nm and 370 nm, depending on the monitoring wavelength.43,46 The presence of the hybrid states in this spectral region is in good agreement with the hypothesis of hybrid-to-chlorophyll energy transfer. The excitation spectra of both chlorophyll-based hybrids remain practically unchanged when the concentration varies, apart from minor changes in the relative intensities (Fig. S10 in the ESI†).

Further evidence of hybrid-to-chlorophyll energy transfer was found by time-resolved fluorescence measurements of the chlorophyll solution incorporated into both hybrids, namely, through the calculation of the fluorescence lifetime for selected samples. The emission decay curves were selectively monitored for the hybrid host (445 nm) and the chl-a excited states at 675 nm. In all cases, the experimental curves are well described by single exponential functions (Fig. S11 in the ESI†). Another relevant parameter for LSC performance is the trapping efficiency (ηt) that accounts for emission losses at the surface.8 The ηt values depend on the refractive index (n) of the optically active layer at the emission wavelength. By investigating the dispersion curves of the chlorophyll-based hybrids, (1.49 < n < 1.50 at 675 nm), trapping efficiency values ∼75% are predicted (Fig. S2 in the ESI†). The light emission efficiency and light harvesting ability can be related by the molar brightness (B), given by B = q × ε,65 allowing both properties to be compared across distinct samples. Modelling

Simulations of the LSC performance (ηopt) were carried out using a Monte Carlo ray-tracing approach, in which the photon propagation follows geometrical optical laws.71 The thickness of the chl-based layer (∼10−3 m) is much larger than that of the light coherent length (∼10−7 m), and thus, interference effects were neglected. The stochastic nature of the model is reflected in the fact that the propagated rays cannot split when reaching an interface but rather either transmitted or reflected, as predicted by Fresnel laws. We also note that incident light polarization is not taken into account. Modelling the path of each propagated ray it is possible to know whether the photons are trapped inside the LSC, absorbed by the luminescent species, lost due to the escape cone or absorption and if they were collected at the edges of the LSC.

The inputs of the Monte Carlo ray-tracing simulation consist of the solar spectrum AM1.5G (280–1600 nm), the absorption and emission spectra, the absolute emission quantum yield and dispersion curve of each LSC. Furthermore, we consider a thin layer of air underneath the LSC.

This model does not include individual molecules or optically active centres but applies statistical averaging of the absorption. First the absorption probability is calculated (pabs = 1–10−αd, where α is the attenuation coefficient (units cm−1) and d is the photon desired step size (0.02 cm) in each iteration) as a function of the wavelength and compared with a random generated number between 0 and 1 for each launched photon. If pabs proves to be higher than the random number the photon is absorbed otherwise, the photon continues to propagate accordingly to its direction vector. Once a photon is absorbed, the subsequent fate of the excitation (that is, emission or non-radiative relaxation) is again determined by the Monte Carlo sampling according to the emission quantum yield. The direction of the emission is randomly distributed and the emission wavelength is sampled randomly from the normalized emission spectrum. The ultimate fate of each photon is either loss due to non-radiative recombination or escape from the LSC from one of the interfaces. When a photon hits the PV interface, it counts as a PV cell absorption and the wavelength of the photon and its position on the PV interface are recorded.

The ηopt for simulation purposes is defined by the energy emitted (per unit of time) from the edge of the LSC divided by the solar energy falling on the LSC (per unit of time), as stated in eqn (1). The output of the simulation yields ηopt = 3.47 ± 0.01% for the dU6–chl-2 LSC with 1.00 × 1.00 × 0.25 cm3. This value is very close to that found experimentally (∼3.70 ± 0.01%) and to the value estimated through the ηopt definition based on the LSC main losses (eqn (S2) in the ESI†).

The good-agreement between the ray-tracing prediction and the experimental results points out that the simulation describes the main optical features and allows us to use the experimental device parameters to estimate the optical performances potentially achievable using waveguides of increasingly larger area or slab thickness.71,81

The ηopt was also estimated for lager area LSCs to discuss the feasibility of scaling-up the chl-based devices. A constant thickness of 10−3 m (typical value for a glass window) was considered in all the simulations and As was varied (10−4 < As < 10−2 m2). For these dimensions, ηopt is approximately constant (4.5%, Fig. S13(a) in the ESI†), which indicates that optical conversion efficiency can still be maintained at a relatively high level for larger devices. The PCE can be related to ηopt through the efficiency of the PV solar cells (PCE = ηPVηopt). Therefore, based on the experimental PCE value of 0.1%, we may estimate the As value expected to produce the desired Pelout, through

Conclusions

In this work, nature-based LSCs made of chlorophyll molecules dispersed in organic–inorganic ureasil hybrids were studied. The chlorophyll-based active layer displays a large overlap with the solar irradiance on the surface of the Earth, (absorption at 300–450 nm and 600–850 nm) and emission spectra centered around 675 nm. At low chlorophyll concentration values (1017 molecules per cm3), the optical properties (fluorescence lifetime, quantum yield and brightness) are preserved after the incorporation into the hybrids hosts. The optimised hybrid sample (ηopt = 3.70 ± 0.01%) was tested as an LSC coupled to commercial Si-based PV devices, revealing an effective contribution to its performance, namely, an increase in the PCE (0.10 ± 0.01%). The use of natural red/NIR emitting dyes, such as chlorophyll molecules, as active layers in LSCs, demonstrates the huge potential of nature-inspired LSCs as a relevant step toward sustainable PV energy conversion.
