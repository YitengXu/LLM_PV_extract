Introduction

SnSe is a native p-type semiconductor with indirect and direct bandgaps of 0.9 and 1.3 eV, respectively. Similar to SnS, SnSe is a promising alternative to lead chalcogenides in photovoltaic (PV) applications because of its environmental sustainability. To maximize the conversion efficiency, the bandgap of an ideal PV absorber layer should be around 1.3 eV for the single-junction cell and 1.0â€“1.9 eV for the two-junction cell.1,2 These requirements can be achieved by tuning the bandgaps of PV materials via adjusting their size or chemical compositions.3â€“12 It is difficult to tune the bandgaps of SnSe and SnS nanocrystals through control of the nanocrystal's size within the quantum confined regime because of their layered crystal structure which readily results in the formation of a sheet-like morphology instead of 0-dimensional particles.9,10,13,14 Therefore, studies about the bandgap control of SnSe and SnS by modulating their chemical compositions are vital.

Recently, a few studies about the tunable bandgaps of SnSxSe1âˆ’x (ref. 12) and SnxGe1âˆ’xSe15 nanocrystals synthesized by a hot-injection method have been reported. However studies for the synthesis of SnSe nanocrystals alloyed with other cations via a simple route still remain to be explored. The ion radius of Sb3+, 0.076 nm, is close to that, of Sn2+ (0.093 nm).16 It is anticipated that the substitution of Sb for Sn in SnSe would widen the bandgap of SnSe according to the Bursteinâ€“Moss effect17â€“19 because Sb has five valence electrons which is one valence electron more than Sn. The synthesis of Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe nanocrystals is not reported yet. In this work, we have synthesized Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.6) nanocrystals in a one-pot system via a solution-phase process and explored the effects of Sb on the phase transformation, morphology evolution and tunable bandgap of Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe nanocrystals. The results reveal that the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.1 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.2) nanocrystals with a tunable morphology and bandgap are potential candidates as photovoltaic materials.

Results and discussion

For the undoped SnSe samples, the single SnSe (JCPDS 01-075-6133) phase, orthorhombic with a = 1.1501, b = 0.4153, and c = 0.4445 nm, hereafter referred to as SnSe(1), could be synthesized at 230 Â°C for 10 h as shown in Fig. 1. The temperature for the growth of the single SnSe(1) phase in the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.1 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.2) samples increased with the amount of Sb. Meanwhile, the XRD peaks of the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.1 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.2) samples shift to higher diffraction angles relative to that of the SnSe sample, respectively, with the extent increasing with the amount of Sb, revealing that Sb3+ substitutes for Sn2+ in the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.1 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.2) lattice. The lattice constants of the SnSe, Sn0.9Sb0.1Se, and Sn0.8Sb0.2Se alloys calculated from their XRD patterns are a = 1.1496, 1.1454, and 1.1288 nm, b = 0.4152, 0.4142, and 0.4120 nm, and c = 0.4428, 0.4385, and 0.4344 nm, correspondingly. From the XPS analyses, the binding energy of Sb3d5/2 (530.0 eV) in the Sn0.9Sb0.1Se and Sn0.8Sb0.2Se samples was close to the reported value of Sb3+. One example is shown in Fig. 2. The difference in the ion radius between Sb3+, 0.076 nm, and Sn2+, 0.093 nm, is about 18%.16 This mismatch is somewhat larger than the criterion for substitution solubility noted by Hume-Rothery who pointed out that an extensive solid solubility of one metal in another only occurs if the diameters of the metals differ by less than 15%.20 A similar result for the SnxGe1âˆ’xSe alloy was also reported,15 where the difference in the ion radius between Ge2+, 0.073 nm, and Sn2+, 0.093 nm, is about 21%.

In order to clarify the factors responsible for the phase transformation from SnSe(1) to SnSe(2), both the Sn0.8Sb0.2Se and Sn0.7Sb0.3Se samples were synthesized under the same conditions, i.e., at 250 Â°C for 36 h. The XRD patterns in Fig. 4 show that the single SnSe(1) phase grew in the Sn0.8Sb0.2Se sample, while SnSe(2) grew in the Sn0.7Sb0.3Se sample, where little SnSe(1) was possibly present. Furthermore, the same result still occurred in the Sn0.8Sb0.2Se and Sn0.7Sb0.3Se samples synthesized at 260 Â°C for 10 h and 270 Â°C for 20 h, respectively. These data reveal that the phase transformation from SnSe(1) to SnSe(2) is due to the introduction of more Sb and is independent of the synthesis temperature.

As seen in Fig. 3, the diffraction peaks become more and more broad when increasing the amount of Sb, indicating that the introduction of Sb induces some defects in the crystals. From the condition for electroneutrality, the introduction of two SbSn+ can induce the formation of one VSn2âˆ’, i.e., a Sn vacancy, and thus the [VSn2âˆ’] increases with the [SbSn+]. When the amount of added Sb exceeds the limit of substitution solubility, the extra Sb can form other defects such as interstitial solute atoms which may play an important role in the phase transformation from SnSe(1) to SnSe(2) for the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.3 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.6) samples.

As shown in Fig. 1 and 3, the temperature and time for the growth of the single SnSe phase in the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.1 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.4) samples increased with the amount of Sb. For the Sn0.8Sb0.2Se sample synthesized at 250 Â°C for 10 h and the Sn1âˆ’xSbxSe (0.5 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 0.6) samples synthesized at 275 Â°C for 24 h other phases such as Sb and Sb2Se3 were also observed, respectively. In order to compare the reactivity between Sn2+ and Se2âˆ’ with that between Sb3+ and Se2âˆ’, Sn-rich Sn0.9Sb0.1Se and Sb-rich Sb1.8Sn0.2Se3 with the same cation ratio, 9:1, and Sb2Se3 samples were synthesized at 230 Â°C for 10 h. The pure single SnSe(1) phase was acquired for the Sn0.9Sb0.1Se sample as shown in Fig. 1. However, for the Sb1.8Sn0.2Se3 sample the Sb2Se3 phase (JCPDS 01-072-1184) coexisted with the residual Sb (JCPDS 00-035-0732) as shown in Fig. 5.
