Abstract

Lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries have recently attracted enormous attention in the energy-storage sector owing to their high theoretical capacities (1675 mA h gâˆ’1), high theoretical energy densities (2600 W h kgâˆ’1), and cost-effectiveness compared to the state-of-the-art Li-ion batteries. Despite these merits, the practical application of Liâ€“S battery technology is hindered by certain severe drawbacks. Therefore, current challenges need to be diagnosed in order to find effective solutions to these Liâ€“S battery-commercialization obstacles. To help direct future work, we not only summarize current research trends, but also highlight an effective solution for the practical applications of Liâ€“S batteries. In this review, we thoroughly summarize current research work on three kinds of Liâ€“S battery systems based on different cathode materials, namely sulfur (S8), lithium sulfide, and sulfurized-polyacrylonitrile. In addition, we also provide insight into the current challenges associated with Liâ€“metal anodes in Liâ€“S batteries. We then summarize and discuss an effective solution to these issues, namely the use of Liâ€“metal-free anodes or all-solid state electrolytes in Liâ€“S full cell systems.

1. Introduction

Rapidly growing population and associated energy problems are currently major global issues. An increase in the use of traditional fossil-fuel energy raises further concerns about environmental pollution. To reduce the dependence on fossil fuel and to mitigate its environmental impact, renewable energy sources urgently need to be developed, including solar power, geothermal energy, and wind energy.1â€“4 However, these types of energy sources only provide power intermittently, therefore cost-effective and environmentally friendly energy-storage devices need to be developed. Rechargeable batteries, such as metal-hydride, lead-acid, and Li-ion batteries (LIBs) have played significant roles for more than a century in applications that include portable electronic appliances and as backup power supplies, owing to their high energy densities, good cycle lives, user-friendliness, and low maintenance costs.5â€“7

Over the past three decades, LIBs have dominated the energy-storage market due to their high energy densities (e.g., 387 W h kgâˆ’1 for a LiCoO2/graphite battery). However, as a result of rapidly developing electric vehicles (EVs) and hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs), further increases in the energy densities of LIBs are urgently being sought. Hence, electrode materials with either higher charge-storage capacities or higher cell operating voltages are required. However, existing cathode materials, including LiCoO2, LiMn2O4, and LiFePO4, are mainly based on insertion-type mechanisms and have low specific capacities of less than 200 mA h gâˆ’1, which limits the ability to improve LIB energy density.8â€“15 Sulfur cathode materials, present in lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries, perform two-electron conversion reactions and have high theoretical specific capacities (1675 mA h gâˆ’1).16 As a consequence, Liâ€“S batteries possess high energy densities and are among the most promising energy-storage devices. Moreover, the low cost of sulfur and its environmental friendliness make Liâ€“S batteries better candidates than the state-of-the-art LIBs.17â€“21

In spite of these merits, the practical application of a Liâ€“S system is still hindered by the following challenges: (1) the large volume change (80%) associated with conversion reactions that take place in sulfur cathodes; (2) the insulating nature of sulfur and its reaction products (Li2S2/Li2S); (3) the shuttle reaction resulting from dissolved Li polysulfides (Li2Sx, x > 2) in electrolytes;22â€“24 (4) the highly active Li metal anode, which changes its morphology and structure through dendrite formation during continuous cycling, causing short circuiting and serious safety issues;25â€“28 and (5) the evolution of gasses such as N2 and N2O, in addition to CH4 and H2, as volatile products, which results in the degradation of electrochemical performance.29 In order to solve these problems, various strategies have been implemented, including: (1) the design of porous and highly conductive carbon host materials that encapsulate sulfur;30 (2) the preparation of polar host materials that chemically trap polysulfides;31 (3) the exploitation of robust multifunctional binders;32 (4) the use of functional interlayers/separators that restrain the shuttle effect;33 (5) the construction of specially structured electrodes including 3D current collectors, as well as sandwich and layer-by-layer structured cathodes;34 (6) the exploitation of solid-state electrolytes for preventing polysulfide dissolution;35 (7) the preparation of anodic protection layers;36 and (8) the use of Liâ€“metal-free anodes that tackle safety concerns involving metallic Li.37

Although numerous approaches that address current cathode issues in Liâ€“S batteries have been explored by both the scientific community and industry, problems surrounding Liâ€“metal anodes still remain and urgently need to be overcome for the practical application of Liâ€“S batteries. Several approaches, such as the application of electrolyte additives,38 gas-phase doping on the Li metal,39 atomic layer deposition on the Li metal,40 and polymeric coatings,41 have been used in attempts to protect the Liâ€“metal surface. However, these strategies fail at high chargeâ€“discharge currents in high-sulfur-loaded Liâ€“S batteries. Meanwhile, the assembly of Liâ€“S batteries with highly active Liâ€“metal anodes is a major commercialization challenge. For this reason, Liâ€“metal-free anodes are more suitable for the construction of reliable Liâ€“S battery systems, and to avoid the challenges associated with Liâ€“metal anodes. Furthermore, when a high-specific-capacity anode is used, the full cell system can possess an energy density that is six-to-seven times higher than that of the existing LiCoO2/graphite system.42 Hence, this battery technology can fulfil the demands of sophisticated electronic equipment, technological advances in electric vehicles (travel ranges of up to 500 km), and high-energy-storage grids.43â€“48

In this review, we first present recent developments and operating mechanisms of three types of Liâ€“S system composed of sulfur (S8), lithium sulfide (Li2S), and sulfurized-polyacrylonitrile (SPAN). We then summarize and discuss the challenges associated with Liâ€“metal anodes in Liâ€“S battery systems. Finally, we discuss strategies for enhancing capacity and energy density, and for overcoming safety issues involving Liâ€“S batteries, and provide suggestions for future guidance. This review provides important insight into the scientific and practical issues involved with the development of Liâ€“S batteries.

2.1 S/C-composite cathodes

Owing to the insulating natures of sulfur and Li2S2/Li2S, a conductive host or matrix is required for improved electron transport.54,55 A sulfurâ€“carbon composite is synthesized by the melt-infiltration method in which elemental sulfur is melted between 119â€“159 Â°C. In this method, carbon materials are added to a sulfur-CS2 solution at room temperature, followed by solvent evaporation at room temperature. The residual powder is then ground and heated at 155 Â°C to enhance sulfur distribution inside the carbon matrix.57

Nazar's group initially demonstrated the encapsulation of sulfur (sulfur content < 55 wt%) in a CMK-3 carbon host, and reported a high specific capacity of 1320 mA h gâˆ’1.19 Many publications have since appeared that report improvements in the capacities and cycle lives of Liâ€“S batteries using conductive carbon matrices,58â€“66 while numerous S/C-composite cathodes exhibit stable cycling performance up to 500 cycles.67â€“72 Despite these achievements, the areal mass loadings of sulfur in the reported S/C-composite cathodes are normally less than 2 mg cmâˆ’2.73â€“79 To increase the energy densities of Liâ€“S batteries, one trend involves enhancing the mass loading of sulfur on the electrode, and increased sulfur mass loadings (2â€“10 mg cmâˆ’2) have been reported in recent years.80â€“111 However, these areal mass loadings are still incapable of generating sufficient energy density to meet the requirements of modern technological applications. As state-of-the-art LIBs require 2 mA h cmâˆ’2 to operate HEVs, whereas EVs require 4 mA h cmâˆ’2, and considering that the operating voltage of a Liâ€“S battery is 2.1 V, a Liâ€“S battery requires a higher areal specific capacity to achieve the same travel range as an LIB. To further increase the transportation, range beyond 500 km, the areal capacity of a sulfur cathode needs to be more than 7 mA h cmâˆ’2, which can be achieved by increasing the sulfur areal mass loading to more than 10 mg cmâˆ’2. 2.3 S/PAN-composite cathodes

Liquid sulfur infiltration, which involves the melting of elemental sulfur at 155 Â°C and its subsequent infiltrate into porous carbon architectures, has been widely used to prepare carbonâ€“sulfur-based composites. Apart from liquid infiltration, vapor infiltration has also been used to synthesize sulfurâ€“carbon composites; however, this method results in weak interactions between elemental sulfur and the carbon matrix, leading to unevenly distributed sulfur and poor electrochemical performance of the corresponding Liâ€“S battery.161,162 In addition, polysulfide dissolution causes the viscosity of the electrolyte to increase, while lowering ionic conductivity and clogging the separator membrane. In order to resolve these issues, sulfurized polymer-composite-cathode materials show great promise for incorporation into high-performance Liâ€“S cell designs. The SPAN composite, which is a sulfurized polymer, uses interactions between the nitrile groups of the polymer and elemental sulfur to destabilize PAN and promote dehydrogenation and ring formation.163 After thermal treatment at high temperature in an inert atmosphere, sulfur species are covalently bound throughout the composite, which results in a combination of physical entrapment and chemical conjugation of elemental sulfur in the host PAN matrix.164 In addition, the lone electron pairs present in the nitrile groups of PAN readily interact with Li through coordination bonding. Interactions between Li2S and the PAN polymer result in a stiffening of the PAN framework as the Li2S is uniformly dispersed. Here Li2S acts as a cross-linking agent that interconnects the PAN network. Such a material-design strategy improves the electrochemical performance of Liâ€“S batteries. In this section, we present recent developments in the field of SPAN-based composite cathodes for Liâ€“S batteries.

Low sulfur content in SPAN-based composites is a major barrier to achieving high areal capacity and energy density. In addition, the low operating voltage of 1.8 V further reduces the energy density of SPAN-based Liâ€“S batteries. In this context, an optimized cell design with a high mass loading of sulfur that improves the specific areal capacity, or a reduction in additional electrode weight in order to increase gravimetric specific capacity, is required. Research on SPAN-cathode-based Liâ€“S batteries began with Wang et al. who delivered a battery with a specific capacity of 1000 mA h gâˆ’1 using a 65% sulfur content in the composite.165 The same group explored SPAN composites in a PVDF-gel electrolyte that delivered a specific capacity of 600 mA h gâˆ’1 after 50 cycles, which is five times higher than that of a LiCoO2-based cathode.166 Yu et al. also synthesized conducting sulfur-containing electrodes by heating PAN with sulfur at a high temperature, which delivered a specific capacity of 480 mA h gâˆ’1 after 240 cycles with a capacity retention of 92%.167,168 Fanous et al. showed that a SPAN composite synthesized at 330 Â°C with Sâ€“C bonds delivered a specific capacity of more than 1000 mA h gâˆ’1, based on the sulfur mass, after 40 cycles in carbonate-based electrolytes.169 Other SPAN-based cathode materials for Liâ€“S batteries have also been employed in order to further enhance capacity and cycling stability.51,170â€“172

Recently, Frey et al.173 developed a simple and scalable synthesis method for poly(methyl methacrylate)/poly(acrylonitrile) (PMMA/PAN) fibers. Fibrous SPAN was obtained by the thermal conversion of PMMA/PAN with sulfur at a high temperature and a 46% mass-loading of sulfur. The fibrous morphology with cylindrical macropores helped to form electronic conduction networks in the cathode, providing directed diffusion pathways for ions. As compared to particulate SPAN (400 mA h gâˆ’1 at 2C), the fibrous SPAN composite exhibited a better rate capability (800 mA h gâˆ’1 at 2C), and a good capacity retention of >800 mA h gâˆ’1 at 0.5C was observed after 1200 cycles. 3. Lithium metal anode: challenges

Liâ€“metal foil is generally used as the anode in a Liâ€“S battery due to its high theoretical specific capacity of 3860 mA h gâˆ’1 and its low negative electrochemical potential of âˆ’3.040 V. However, some serious problems associated with Liâ€“metal anodes used in Liâ€“S batteries remain; they include: (1) the formation of solid-electrolyte-interphase (SEI) layers at Li/electrolyte boundaries during electrochemical reactions that are not mechanically robust, leading to continuous formation/dissolution and inefficient Liâ€“S-battery performance. (2) Exothermic reactions between Li metal and non-aqueous flammable electrolyte solvents that create risks of overheating, while the low melting point of Li may cause thermal runaway or battery explosion. (3) The formation of Li dendrites and their detachment from the Li metal can cause losses of active material and coulombic efficiency. (4) Dendritic Li growth can pierce the separator and extend to the cathode, resulting in short circuiting and the risk of explosion.

3.1 Sâ€“CPAN-composite cathodes

The Liâ€“metal anodes used in Liâ€“S batteries suffer from huge volume changes, a consequence of the heterogeneous deposition of Li during continuous cycling, which results in changes in surface morphology. The degradation of the Liâ€“metal anode also generates a rough surface and Li-dendrite growth in random directions. Changes in the surface properties of the Li metal cause the formation of dendrites that grow further and can pierce the separator, resulting in a short circuit; this is a serious safety issue for Liâ€“S batteries.

Generally, low sulfur mass loading masks the Liâ€“metal-anode issue. When a high-loaded sulfur cathode is used to generate high energy density, the instability of the Liâ€“metal anode is more pronounced. The interphase growth of Li metal has been demonstrated to be extremely porous, and this condition becomes more severe with increasing current density. Moreover, the interphase grows toward the inside of fresh Li metal and accumulates on the Liâ€“metal anode, which can cause an increase in cell impedance.88,200 Cracks appear and Li-anode corrosion takes place. The formation of a thick interphase due to side reactions between the newly formed porous Li metal, the electrolyte, and the soluble polysulfides lead to the loss of sulfur, an increase in cell impedance, and electrolyte consumption. This is a major drawback and a key challenge that needs to be addressed when improving the energy densities of Liâ€“S battery systems.6

Furthermore, the role of the Liâ€“metal anode is much more important when a Liâ€“S pouch cell is constructed. In such a pouch cell, the high areal mass loading of sulfur generates high current and causes serious polysulfide shuttling, which damages the Liâ€“metal anode. The high current generated in a pouch cell creates an inhomogeneous distribution on the surface of the Liâ€“metal anode, where some specific sites accumulate more current, resulting in the formation of more dendritic Li and electrolyte decomposition.201â€“204 High areal current also leads to a large Li-ion concentration gradient at the electrode/electrolyte interface, which damages the SEI layer on the Liâ€“metal surface, intensifies dendritic Li growth, and also diminishes the role of the LiNO3 additive.205,206 As this process is repeated, the contact area between the electrolyte and the Li metal increases, which causes further electrolyte consumption, which enhances the viscosity of the electrolyte while increasing Li-ion diffusion resistance and cell polarization.201 Irreversible electrolyte consumption during continuous cycling can also dry the cell, resulting in severe capacity fading. Even if the cell is refilled with electrolyte, the Liâ€“S pouch cell remains inoperative due to the fatal deterioration of the Liâ€“metal anode. In addition, dendritic Li becomes detached from the anode surface and dead Li powder is formed, which reduces reactivity during cycling when side reactions between the electrolyte/Li polysulfide and the Liâ€“metal anode take place.207â€“210 Various research groups have proposed growth mechanisms for dendrite formation.208,209 Bai et al.208 grew Li dendrites in a glass capillary cell and demonstrated that a change of mechanism, from root-growing mossy Li to tip-growing dendritic Li, occurs at the onset of limited electrolyte diffusion. They also showed that mossy Li is blocked by nanoporous ceramic separators, while dendritic Li can easily penetrate nanopores, resulting in a short circuit. Lopez examined the morphological transition of a Liâ€“metal surface, from flat and smooth into a rough structure.209 This study revealed that three different surface morphologies form during the cycling process, namely a dendritic layer at the top, a porous intermediate layer, and a residual layer. This study also showed that cell failure is related to electrolyte depletion and the production of a porous layer during the reaction of active metallic Li.

Even if SPAN-based cathodes can avoid the shuttle phenomenon and polysulfide dissolution, thereby preventing the loss of active sulfur, the high mass loadings of sulfur in SPAN composites still present problems similar to those encountered in conventional Liâ€“S batteries. The practical application of Liâ€“S batteries requires high sulfur mass loadings that generate high chargeâ€“discharge currents, which (again) causes Liâ€“metal degradation. A variety of techniques have been used to resolve the dendrite issue, including electrolyte additives that form stable SEIs on Liâ€“metal surfaces. The formation of an SEI layer can reduce the precipitation of Li2S- and Li2S2-reaction products on the surface of the Liâ€“metal anode and alleviate dendritic growth, thereby enhancing the cycling performance of a Liâ€“S battery. Electrolyte additives such as lithium nitrate (LiNO3),24,28,211,212 phosphorus pentasulfide (P2S5),145 lithium iodide (LiI),213 and indium iodide (InI3),214 among others, have recently been used to protect Liâ€“metal anodes and prevent dendritic growth. An anodic protection layer has also been used to cover the Liâ€“metal surface and produce a stable artificial SEI layer. Li nitride (Li3N),87 PEDOT-co-PEG,112 and Al2O3 layers,215 have all recently been used to protect Li anodes.

Electrolyte additives and the formation of an anodic protection layer can reduce Liâ€“metal corrosion from polysulfides and alleviate dendritic growth through the formation of a stable SEI layer, however assembling Liâ€“S batteries using Liâ€“metal anodes is a major challenge for the commercialization of Liâ€“S batteries. Furthermore, future requirements of high-energy-density Liâ€“S batteries require high areal sulfur mass loadings, which enhance the polysulfide concentration in the electrolyte and the current density on the anode surface. Therefore, it is very difficult to commercialize Liâ€“S batteries based on Liâ€“metal anodes for applications in high power and energy equipment. Consequently, the development of Liâ€“metal-free anodes for Liâ€“S batteries is a worthwhile objective.

3.2 Prevention of lithium-dendrite growth

The lithiumâ€“metal anode is important for the safe operation of a Liâ€“S battery; therefore, its degradation and dendritic growth, which deteriorate electrochemical performance, need to be prevented. Dendritic lithium growth is suppressed by circumventing lithium dendrite nucleation, which can be inhibited at the root.24,216 Modification of the three-dimensional anode architecture by tuning the surface energy is another way of suppressing dendritic growth.218,227 Surface treatment of the anode circumvents potentially active dendritic-growth sites. Recently, a multi-layered graphene (MLG) coating improved lithium-anode performance due to the separated formation of the SEI layer and the suppression of lithium-dendrite growth.219 A Li3PO4 thin-film coating also suppressed lithium-dendrite growth,220 while a cross-linked polymer, as an interfacial layer, was also employed to suppress lithium-dendrite formation through surface coverage.221 A protective film of Li3N, which is formed through the reaction of lithium and nitrogen gas at room temperature, has also been used to suppress lithium-dendrite formation, while Ma et al.87 showed that a three-dimensional porous anode architecture is another modification that improves Liâ€“S battery performance.222,223 In contrast, the growth of 3D atomic crystals of boron nitride and graphene on copper has been used to hinder lithiumâ€“metal dendrite formation. Metallic Ni foam has also been employed to pre-store lithium, which formed a Liâ€“Ni-composite anode and prevented lithium-dendrite growth;224 this three-dimensional architecture provided an abundant electrode/electrolyte interface that lowered current density and enhanced electron transfer and ion diffusion. A novel, mechanically strong, porous separator was also employed to prevent lithium-dendrite formation.217,225,226 Recently, PE separators coated with ZrO2/POSS multilayers, through layer-by-layer techniques, have been used to improve cycle lives. This novel separator reduced electrode polarization and suppressed lithium-dendrite growth, which increased coulombic efficiency.227 Cellulose-based membranes containing nanopores and nanofibers have also been used as separators that inhibit lithium-dendrite formation and improve electrochemical performance.228

4. Electrolytes for Liâ€“S batteries

Apart from the electrode, the electrolyte is another important component in a Liâ€“S battery. The main function of an electrolyte is the transfer of ions between the electrodes, while also facilitating the formation of an SEI layer on the electrode. An organic liquid electrolyte mainly consists of solvents, salts, and some additives. The voltage window in a Liâ€“S battery is less than 3 V, which facilitates the use of organic solvents as electrolytes. Numerous polysulfides are formed during the charging and discharging processes, and these polysulfides can react with organic solvents. Consequently, the available range of solvents for Liâ€“S batteries is very narrow. One approach to improving Liâ€“S battery performance involves the design of effective electrolytes that provide good capacity and cycling stability. Electrolyte species, such as the type of solvent, concentration, nature of the lithium salt, and functional additives, can improve the performance of Liâ€“S batteries. Generally, electrolytes consisting of lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide ([(CF3SO2)2N]Li, LiTFSI) in a solvent mixture of 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME) and 1,3-dioxolane (DOL), containing lithium nitrate (LiNO3) as an additive, have been used. The advantage of TFSI is mainly associated with its highly delocalized negative-charge distribution, which increases the dissociation and solubility of LiTFSI and improves ionic conductivity. Furthermore, the DOL-based solvent forms an SEI layer, which prevents dendritic growth and accommodates electrode volume changes, resulting in good cycling performance. The lithium nitrate additive used in the Liâ€“S battery facilitates SEI-layer formation on the surface of the lithiumâ€“metal anode, which stabilizes the lithium metal and suppresses polysulfide redox shuttling, thereby increasing the battery's coulombic efficiency and cycling stability. Recently, Zhang et al.229 demonstrated the catalytic effect of LiNO3 on the conversion of highly soluble polysulfides into slightly soluble sulfur. Different concentrations of the lithium nitrate additive have been used to enhance battery performance.230â€“233 Furthermore, the low solubilities of lithium polysulfides in fluorinated electrolytes, which limit parasitic reactions of lithium and alleviate self-discharge, have seen these electrolytes recently used in Liâ€“S batteries. The use of the LiNO3 additive in a fluorinated electrolyte also suppresses self-discharge owing to the formation of SEI layers on the lithium anode and the sulfur cathode. The synergistic effect between the additive and the fluorinated electrolyte prevents redox shuttling, thereby improving the coulombic efficiency of the Liâ€“S battery.234â€“236

Organo-fluorine compounds, such as 1,1,2,2-tetrafluoroethyl-2,2,3,3-tetrafluoropropyl ether (TTE), have been applied as electrolyte solvents in Liâ€“S batteries, leading to reduced shuttling, and improved cycling stability and coulombic efficiency. The SEI was formed through the participation of TTE in redox reactions on both electrodes, which inhibited parasitic reactions and resulted in the better use of electrode materials.234 A fluorinated-electrolyte mixture composed of TTE and LiNO3, as the additive, was used by Azimi et al.;236 this electrolyte combination exhibited good electrochemical performance with no self-discharges observed after long hours of rest at elevated temperatures. A fluorinated bis(2,2,2-trifluoroethyl)-ether-containing (BTFE-containing) electrolyte also alleviated self-discharge at elevated temperatures, at both low and high sulfur loadings, due to the formation of a vigorous protective film on the anode.235 Recently, Gao et al.237 demonstrated a Liâ€“S battery containing a porous carbon/S-composite electrode and a symmetrical fluorinated diethoxyethane electrolyte. The electrolyte solvent, composed of a mixture of 1,2-bis(1,1,2,2-tetrafluoroethoxy)ethane (TFEE) and DOL suppressed polysulfide dissolution and shuttling, resulting in a capacity retention of 99.5% per cycle over 100 cycles. Talian et al.238 compared Liâ€“S battery performance using a tetraglyme electrolyte with that using a TFEE-based electrolyte at low electrolyte loading. Polysulfide solubility was lower in the TFEE-based electrolyte owing to the different Li+-solvation mode. 1,1,2,2-Tetrafluoroethyl 2,2,2-trifluoroethyl ether (HFE), 1,1,2,3,3,3-hexafluoropropyl ethyl ether (HEE), and ethyl 1,1,2,2-tetrafluoroethyl ether (ETE) have also been used in Liâ€“S batteries.239 Apart from fluorinated electrolytes, the development of highly concentrated electrolytes for use in Liâ€“S batteries has also been studied. The salt concentration in a liquid electrolyte is usually less than 1.2 mol Lâˆ’1. Recently, Suo et al. introduced a high-concentration electrolyte, referred to as a â€œsolvent-in-saltâ€ electrolyte, which exhibited a high lithium-ion transference number (0.73) in a Liâ€“S battery. This electrolyte synergistically enhanced cycling performance and safety by inhibiting dendritic lithium growth. In addition, the inhibition of lithium polysulfide dissolution further enhanced the cycling performance and coulombic efficiency of the Liâ€“S battery.240

We emphasize the various kinds of electrolytes used in Liâ€“S batteries and also discuss all-solid-state lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries that use solid electrolytes. For practical application, high-loading electrodes are a precondition to achieving high energy densities. Hence, we first presented recent developments in high-loading sulfur cathodes based on S8, Li2S, and SPAN. From the viewpoint of Liâ€“S full-cell battery configurations, numerous challenges regarding cycle life and energy density still need to be addressed. (2) The need to further optimize a high-loading anode, with high capacity and long cycling life, for use as a Liâ€“metal-free anode. A good three-dimensional Liâ€“metal-composite electrode needs to be designed. Strategies that are beyond those conventionally used, such as high-temperature melting and pre-lithiation by electrochemical methods, need to be investigated. The mass balance between the lithiumâ€“metal anode and the sulfur cathode needs to be optimized in order to achieve a high-energy-density Liâ€“S battery, since excess lithium on the anode side reduces the energy density of the entire Liâ€“S battery. (3) The extra weight and volume related to host materials, conducting agents, electrolyte, and binder, among others, need to be minimized in order to improve the overall energy density of the cell. (4) SPAN-based Liâ€“S full cells have only been reported once in the literature; therefore, further research is required in this area. (6) The electrochemical performance of the coin-cell format does not precisely reflect the performance of pouch-cell-type Liâ€“S batteries.
