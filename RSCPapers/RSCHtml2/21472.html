Abstract

Employing upconversion hosts (which convert lower-energy light into higher-energy light) and plasmonic Au nanoparticles, this work deals with the synthesis of a novel Au/NaYF4:Yb,Er/WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 {denoted as A/NYYE/WW} heterojunction via a hydrothermal method followed by a photo-reduction method. Also, A/NYYE/WW exhibited the optimum photocatalytic activity and stronger photocatalytic stability for the degradation of nitrobenzene aqueous solution in comparison with a single WO3Â·0.33H2O or NYYE/WW component. Based on the results of X-ray diffraction patterns, field emission electron microscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectra, UVâVis diffuse reflectance spectra, upconversion luminescence spectra, luminescence decay and photoelectrochemical measurements, it could be considered that a Z-scheme heterojunction was generated over A/NYYE/WW, in which a highly efficient FÃ¶rster resonant energy transfer from NYYE to WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 was observed and the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effects of Au NPs and W18O49 could induce the utilization of near infrared light to achieve broadband light absorption. On the other hand, Au NPs also played the role of an electron sink for enhanced charge carrier separation and utilization. Thus, the heterojunction showed the synergetic effects of both upconversion and LSPR features, which could broaden the absorption spectrum to the near infrared region and give rise to a higher yield. It provides a pathway to develop broadband absorption materials in photocatalytic systems.

1. Introduction

Nitrobenzene (NB) as an important chemical raw material is widely used in the manufacture of explosives, pesticides, and dyes and in other fields.1,2 The maximum permissible concentration of NB in wastewater is not more than 1 mg Lâ1.3 However, with the increasing demand for nitrobenzene, large amounts of wastewater containing nitrobenzene or its derivatives pose a serious threat to the environment and human beings. Therefore, a variety of possible treatment processes have been adopted to remove NB from industrial effluents, including adsorption,4 ozonation,5 photocatalytic degradation,6 and so on. Among these proposed methods, as one of the promising ways, semiconductor photocatalytic technology has been extensively studied for solving the problem of environmental pollution and alleviating the energy crisis.7â9 However, most semiconductors only absorb ultraviolet or visible light from sunlight, and nearly 50% of near infrared light (NIR) has not been exploited. So, it is still a challenging mission to exploit photocatalysts that can harvest NIR light.

In order to harness utmostly a wide range of sunlight energy, many methods have been used to modify semiconductors, such as loading noble metals,10 doping nonmetal elements,11 combining with other narrower bandgap semiconductors12,13 and constructing Z-scheme photocatalysts.14,15 However, the NIR light of the total solar spectrum is still rarely utilized. In order to extend the absorption to the NIR region, combining semiconductors with upconversion (UC) luminescent materials is one of the most efficient ways.16 UC materials can convert the light of low energy into that of higher energy,17,18 and have been used in bioimaging,19 dye-sensitized solar cells20 and photocatalytic systems.21,22 In particular, NaYF4 (NYF), as a kind of host lattice with lower phonon energy, can achieve efficient down-conversion or upconversion luminescence of doping lanthanide ions.23

Recently, the loading of plasmonic Au nanoparticles (NPs) and semiconductors with oxygen vacancies has been studied as a new research frontier for improving the photocatalytic activity using the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) under visible infrared light excitation due to enhanced photon absorption and utilization by means of plasmonic âhot electronâ injection.24,25 Here, the LSPR effect in the W18O49 nanostructure, which is a type of nonstoichiometric tungsten oxide, originates from the collective oscillations of excessive free carriers due to abundant oxygen vacancies on its surface.26 W18O49 possesses a very broad absorption band ranging from the visible to IR region.27 Furthermore, the photoluminescence of UC NPs can be improved by the LSPR effect.28,29 The higher energy level in NaYF4:Yb,Er (NYYE) can emit visible light, which in turn excites the Au NPs and tungsten oxide semiconductors via the FÃ¶rster resonant energy transfer (FRET).30 The plasmonic coupling between Au NPs and W18O49 possibly promotes the generation of plasmonic âhot-electronsâ.31 On the other hand, during the plasmonic energy-transfer process, the photo-generated electrons would transfer from the semiconductors to the surfaces of Au NPs, facilitating efficient charge separation in the heterostructure.

In this work, we designed a unique visible-near infrared (Vis-NIR) light driven photocatalyst of a Au/NaYF4:Yb,Er/WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 (A/NYYE/WW) heterostructure with UC and LSPR functions, which was applied in the broadband light absorption range and it showed more exceptional photocatalytic performance compared to bare WO3Â·0.33H2O and NYYE/WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 (NYYE/WW) for the degradation of NB aqueous solution. It is notable that the degradation rate of the heterostructure was 2.7 and 2.5 times those of WO3Â·0.33H2O and NYYE/WW, respectively, when they were focused on a xenon lamp (Î» > 420 nm) to initiate plasmonic and IR-excited catalysis.

2. Experimental

All the chemicals used in the experiments were of analytical reagent grade and used without further purification.

2.1 Synthesis of NaYF4:Yb,Er

NaYF4:25% Yb,2% Er nanocrystals were prepared by a hydrothermal method. Firstly, 5.22 mmol Y(NO3)3Â·6H2O, 1.57 mmol Yb(NO3)3Â·6H2O and 0.10 mmol Er(NO3)3Â·5H2O were mixed in 20 mL of deionized water. Then 30 mL of 0.23 M EDTA-2Na water solution was added dropwise to the above solution. Also, 30 mL of 1.22 M NH4F solution was added dropwise to the above mixed solution, and the pH of the whole solution was adjusted to 3 with nitric acid. After vigorous stirring for 1 h at room temperature, the whole mixed solution was transferred into a 100 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 180 Â°C for 12 h. The obtained sample was washed three times with deionized water and ethanol and vacuum dried at 60 Â°C for 10 h. NaYF4:25% Yb,2% Er was denoted as NYYE.

2.3 Preparation of Au/NaYF4:Yb,Er/WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49

The Au-loaded NaYF4:Yb,Er/WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 sample (denoted as A/NYYE/WW) was prepared by a photo-reduction method, taking HAuCl4Â·4H2O as the Au precursor. 2.4 Photocatalytic studies

To determine the photocatalytic activities of the as-prepared samples, nitrobenzene was used as a simulated pollutant. Degradation of nitrobenzene aqueous solution was carried out in a quartz reactor. 30 mg of the desired catalyst was dispersed in 50 mL of 20 ppm nitrobenzene solution, using a 300 W xenon lamp (Î» â¥ 420 nm) as the initiated light source. Prior to irradiation, the suspension consisting of catalysts and nitrobenzene was stirred in the dark for 40 min to reach adsorptionâdesorption equilibrium. During the photocatalytic reaction, 3â4 mL of the suspension was extracted and centrifuged at a certain interval. The supernatant was analysed using a UV-vis spectrophotometer (UV-6300 double beam spectrophotometer). The efficiencies of degradations (Î·) can be calculated from the following equation: Î· = (1 â Ct/C0) Ã 100%, where C0 and Ct represent the initial concentration and temporal concentration, respectively.

To further investigate whether the electrons were transferred from the conductive band (CB) of W18O49 to Au rather than from the CB of WO3Â·0.33H2O to Au, the flat band (Efb) potential was measured using MottâSchottky plots at a frequency of 1000 Hz in the dark. In general, for the n-type semiconductor, the flat-band potential is located near the CB. As shown in Fig. 9a, a positive slope on the potential axis indicates the n-type characteristic state of the designed photocatalysts.55 The values of Efb were calculated to be â0.28 V and â0.27 V (vs. NHE) without an obvious positive shift, corresponding to WO3Â·0.33H2O and A/NYYE/WW, respectively. Based on the above analysis, the proposed possible photocatalytic mechanism of A/NYYE/WW can be summarized into three main processes, as shown in Fig. 9b: (a) NYYE can absorb NIR light, where the Yb3+ ions act as the sensitizer and the Er3+ ions act as the activator. The excited state (2F5/2 level) of Yb3+ is formed from the ground state (2F7/2 level) via excitation under NIR light irradiation. Subsequently, the successive energy transfer from the excited Yb3+ ions to the Er3+ ions occurs to populate the corresponding 2H11/2, 4S3/2 and 4F9/2 levels of Er3+ ions in the exited state.56 (b) On the other hand, the green emissions centered at 521 and 538 nm match well with the SPR band of Au NPs and W18O49. The energy from the Er3+ ions in the higher energy levels possibly transfers directly to NYYE and W18O49 through the FRET process, which in turn induces the occurrence of the LSPR effect.30 Au NPs act as an electron reservoir and LSPR-sensitizer, which favor the electron transfer from the VB of WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49 to the surfaces of Au NPs. (c) According to the DRS and UC luminescence spectra, the generated green emission and red emission of the Er3+ ions can be reabsorbed by the adjacent WO3Â·0.33H2O and W18O49, and then the excited semiconductor can produce the charge carriers in favor of the photocatalytic degradation of NB. The transferred electrons can react with O2 to produce ËO2â radicals, and the remaining holes in the valence band of W18O49 can react with OHâ/H2O to generate ËOH radicals. The NB in aqueous solution is mineralized by these active radicals, described by a Z-scheme mechanism. In this way, the NIR light can be indirectly utilized by Au NPs and WO3Â·0.33H2O-W18O49via the energy transfer process followed by the energetic electron transfer process.

4. Conclusions

In summary, both the UC function and LSPR effect of Au NPs and W18O49 were applied to modify semiconductors to largely achieve broadband light absorption and enhanced photocatalytic activity. The effective energy transfer through the UC process can convert NIR light into shorter wavelength light. On the other hand, Au NPs also play the role of an electron sink to effectively promote the separation of the photogenerated electrons and holes. Thus, the synergistic effects of the factors result in a higher yield of photocarriers. The newly designed A/NYYE/WW photocatalyst showed superior photocatalytic performance and excellent stability for the degradation of NB aqueous solution under Vis-NIR light irradiation. The established A/NYYE/WW photocatalytic heterostructure system as a promising material paves the way for greatly improving the efficiency of solar-energy utilization, indicating its further applications in the removal of photocatalytic environmental pollutants.
