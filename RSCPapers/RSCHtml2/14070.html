Abstract

Nickelâ€“zinc iron oxide (NZF) was introduced into a polyaniline (PANI) matrix by an in situ chemical oxidation polymerization approach. The surface composition and chemical states were investigated by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), which revealed an Fe 2p spectrum with the two peak positions of Fe 2p3/2 and Fe 2p1/2 at 711.00 and 724.48 eV, respectively. Deconvolution of the Fe 2p3/2 peak revealed two components with binding energies of 713.98 and 718.16 eV, corresponding to the presence of Fe cations in the octahedral and tetrahedral sites. Additionally, the Rietveld refinement of NZF showed a cubic system with the Fd3m space group. High-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) analysis showed that the NZF material strongly interacts with polyaniline, while the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern perfectly matched with the XRD data. Lognormal distribution was used to determine the particle size, which was found to be in the range of 1â€“100 nm. A flexible photodetector device utilizing the NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid was fabricated on an environmentally friendly, biodegradable cellulose paper substrate and the device exhibited excellent performance, i.e., a responsivity of 0.069 A Wâˆ’1 and detectivity of 7.258 Ã— 1010 Jones at a very low voltage of 0.1 V. The non-stretched device showed a responsivity of 24.980 A Wâˆ’1 at 5 V, whereas at 2 cmâˆ’1 bending curvature, the device showed a responsivity of 20.175 A Wâˆ’1, which was much higher than the responsivity of a commercial photodetector (<0.5 A Wâˆ’1).

1. Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials (OIHMs) have received a lot of attention due to their superior performance in photodetectors, lasers, and photovoltaics, organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite materials (OIHPMs) are presently gaining a lot of interest in the scientific and industrial communities.1â€“7 Recently, it has been reported that a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 22% was achieved with solid thin film based solar cells made with an OIHPM, suggesting that OIHPMs could be promising materials for commercial optoelectronics.1â€“4,10 It is widely known that a high-quality photodetector device must not only function effectively but must also be inexpensive, stable, and environmentally benign.2â€“4,6â€“8 The development of earth-abundant materials for optoelectronics and photovoltaics have the potential to improve both the sustainability and scalability of devices.1â€“4 Therefore, developing low-cost, biodegradable, environment-friendly organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials is very necessary. Photodetectors, which convert incident light into electric signals, are used in a variety of applications, including optical communication, pollution monitoring, sensors, and water treatment.5,6 Flexible light detector applications across a wide range of areas including stretchy, implantable, small, wearable and printed electronics as well as optoelectronics have gained a great deal of interest in recent years. Photodetectors generally need to operate with an external power supply; however, recently there has been an immense demand for self-powered devices, such as in optics, flammable detection and environment monitoring.7â€“12 Self-powered photodetectors that benefit from energy-saving technology and that can function anywhere, even in the absence of electricity, are highly desired. Owing to their unique optoelectronic properties, organicâ€“inorganic hybrid materials have proven to be promising candidates for photodetectors due to their high absorption coefficients, suitable band gap and broad absorption range.13,14 These merits enable the use of hybrid materials to construct photodetectors with high responsivity and fast response speed as well as broad-spectrum absorption, ranging from the ultraviolet (UV) to the visible solar light spectrum.5,15â€“18

Such hybrid materials containing organic and inorganic components are considered potential platforms for diverse areas and are known as functional materials.19â€“21 Functional materials are substances with one or more features that can, in a controlled way, be significantly changed by external stimuli (temperature, electric/magnetic field, etc.); therefore, they are applied in a wide variety of technological devices such as memory, display and telecommunication devices.19â€“23 Currently, organic conductive polymers such as polyaniline,19,20,24 polypyrrole,21,22 polyacetylene,23,25 polythiophene, and poly(3,4-ethylene dioxythiophene)26,27 are preferred for fabricating flexible devices and have been gaining huge attention due to their unique properties, such as low-cost, easy processing and eco-friendliness.22,23 An important class of hybrid materials is those in which the organic fractions are composed of conducting polymers such as polyaniline and polypyrrole.22,23,25â€“30 Because of its potential uses in photovoltaics, memory devices, gas sensors, and as a crucial component of plastic electronics, polyaniline (PANI) has attracted a lot of interest as a futuristic material.28,29 Polyaniline exhibits many alluring properties, such as a wide bandgap (2.5 eV), short response time and excellent merits under room-temperature operation.30 NZFâ€“conducting polymer based hybrids have been synthesized by various techniques, and the experimental procedure applied fundamentally controls the properties of the resulting material. Variables such as the size and shape of the NZF nanostructures, the degree of dispersion, and the kinds of interaction and interface between the organic and inorganic phases, among others, have a direct influence on properties such as conductivity, piezoresistivity, and photocurrent.31

Previously reported results of graphene quantum dots in a polyaniline matrix,23 ZnO nanorods assisted with polyaniline,26 2D self-healable polyanilineâ€“polypyrrole nanoflakes,27 and in-situ conformal coatings of polyaniline on GaN microwires28 showed that different experimental routes can be successful in producing organicâ€“inorganic hybrids of polyaniline. Iron, with its low elemental cost, high earth-abundance, and environmentally benign character, has broadened the scope for material development and is a suitable material for constructing next-generation technology.1â€“4 When earth-abundant ferrites are incorporated into the polyaniline matrix, they provide many virtues to the final nanohybrid material, such as high mechanical strength and long-term stability, since they function as building blocks. Organic and inorganic semiconductors have very different compositions and structures, which results in major differences in their mechanical, electrical, and optical capabilities. Metal oxides and metal chalcogenides have been extensively explored for sensing, photoconductors, and photoconductive light switches, although iron oxides are the least investigated nanomaterials for photodetector applications.32â€“34 Iron oxide and its composites combined with the conducting polymer polyaniline can be used in many different applications, such as electromagnetic shielding,35 supercapacitors,36 energy storage36 and optoelectronics.22,23,26â€“29 In photodetectors, a change in electrical resistance occurs under exposure to light. For example, the combination of the n-type semiconducting NZF material with the p-type polyaniline has been reported to improve the hybrid photocurrent due to the occurrence of exciton dissociation at the interface.37,38 A citric acid-assisted solâ€“gel method was used to make NZF in this study because it can produce high-purity nanomaterials with small crystallite sizes. Later, the produced material was employed in the aniline monomer polymerization process. NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrids were processed by the in-situ polymerization method and were used to fabricate a metalâ€“semiconductorâ€“metal (MSM) photodetector device on a flexible substrate, which was further used to perform photoresponse studies and revealed a highest photoresponsivity of 24.980 A Wâˆ’1 at 5 V.26,39â€“42

2.1. Materials and reagents

Nickel nitrate (Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O; 99.9%), zinc nitrate (Zn(NO3)2 6H2O; 99.9%), ferric nitrate (Fe(NO3)3 9H2O; 99.9%) and aniline (C6H5NH2; 99.9%) were purchased from Merck-India and used as received without any further purification. 2.2. The synthesis of the NZF material

NZF was prepared by a citric acid-assisted solâ€“gel method because it results in small crystallite size and high purity of the samples. Citric acid is used as the chelating agent that reacts with metal ions to form a stable, water-soluble complex. NZF was prepared by mixing 1 M iron nitrate nonahydrate in 50 ml DI water, 0.2 M nickel nitrate hexahydrate in 50 ml DI water and 0.8 M zinc nitrate hexahydrate in 50 ml DI water separately by mechanical stirring for 1 hour each. After the formation of homogenous solutions, all three solutions were mixed and stirred for three hours. All the precursors were mixed in an aqueous solution with a 1:1 molar ratio of metal ions (Ni(II) + Zn(II) + Fe(III)) to citric acid. The pH of the solution was raised to 7 using ammonia solution, and the mixture was placed on a hot plate at 200 Â°C with stirring until a xerogel was formed, which then self-generated NZF loose powder. The gel, on the other hand, produced a rapid flameless auto-combustion reaction with the release of a significant amount of gases, which began in the hottest zones of the beaker and spread like a volcanic eruption from the bottom to the top. The reaction took 20â€“30 seconds to complete, yielding a dark grey voluminous product with a branched tree-like structure. In this case, the thermal treatment caused a gradual reaction that resulted in a reddish-brown powder being collected in about 10 minutes. Then, the sample was calcined at 873 K for 2 hours in a programmable electric furnace, after which the samples were crushed using a mortar and pestle. 2.3. The synthesis of the NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid material

To synthesize the NZFâ€“PANI hybrid material, we used the in-situ chemical oxidation polymerization approach, as shown in Fig. 1b. 0.1 M p-TSA in 50 ml DI water was mechanically stirred for 30 minutes, which acted as a dopant, and this solution was then divided into two separate beakers. The as-prepared NZF and 5 ml aniline monomer were mixed in one solution and APS in the other one, with continuous stirring for 45 minutes. Next, 1 M ammonium peroxy-di-sulfate was added dropwise to the solution while maintaining its temperature at 0â€“5 Â°C, after which it was mechanically stirred for three more hours and left to stand for a day to polymerize properly with the NZF material. After 24 hours, the precipitate was filtered out by centrifugation and washed with DI water till all the impurities and unreacted monomer were removed from the nanohybrid material. Then, the precipitate was dried at room temperature for 72 hours and stored for further utilization. The characterization details and fabrication of the photodetector device are explained in the ESI.â€ 

The possible reaction mechanism of the NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrids prepared by the in-situ chemical oxidation approach is outlined in Fig. 1c. In the p-TSA medium, pure NZF is positively charged, so the anions of p-TSA get adsorbed on the surface of pure NZF.43 Due to electrostatic interaction between the anions and cations, the aniline monomers are transformed into anilinium cations.43 The NZF material is already dissolved in the p-TSA solution; thus, on adding APS, the anilinium cations start to polymerize from the NZF material surface.

When light photons interact with the nanohybrid material, it creates bound electronâ€“hole pairs [hÎ½ â†’ eâˆ’ + h+].68,69 Under light illumination, a few electrons gain enough energy to pass the potential barrier and transition from the valence band to the conduction band. These photogenerated holes combine with O2âˆ’ ions and convert them into O2 molecules. When solar light with an intensity of 50 Î¼W cmâˆ’2 is incident on the surface of the device, these adsorbed oxygen molecules desorb from the surface, i.e., [h+(hÎ½) + O2âˆ’ â†’ O2(desorption)], as shown under light conditions in Fig. 6.68 These oxygen molecules finally get desorbed from the surface of the nanohybrid material; consequently, the total conductivity rises, and the photocurrent levels rise as well. The remaining uncoupled electron travels into the conduction band, causing the depletion width to shrink.44,70

When the incident illumination was off, the photocurrent sharply decreased to its initial stage.71 Hence, the stability of the fabricated device was confirmed by various on/off cycles;72 the rise and decay time of the device could also be measured from these on/off cycles. The increased electrical conductivity was due to the production of more photogenerated electron and hole pairs, which resulted in a faster rise time. The delayed decay period was due to two factors: first, the oxygen adsorption process of trap electrons under dark condition, and second, the photogenerated holes are captured during oxygen photodesorption.73 The device shows a range of variation of photocurrent when the applied potential drift varies from 1â€“5 V, as shown in Fig. 7a. It was observed that the device could be operated at a very low applied potential drift, i.e., less than 1 V. Hence, the photodetector device also responds very well at drifting biases of 0.5 and 0.1 V, as shown in Fig. 7b and c, respectively. Thus, this metalâ€“nanohybridâ€“metal device could be seen as a nearly self-powered device, suggesting that devices based on these types of materials could be fruitful for further research in the domain of organic photodetectors.

The mechanism of the photocurrent response of the NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid under the illumination of solar light with an intensity of 50 Î¼W cmâˆ’2 is presented in Fig. 10d. The photons of light generate a large number of electronâ€“hole pairs at the surface of NZF under the illumination of solar light. Consequently, such excited electronâ€“hole pairs are smoothly separated via the built-in potential, resulting in the photogenerated holes in the NZF valence band drifting in the same direction as the built-in electric field in the junction and then jumping to the HOMO of PANI through the junction. At the same time, an exciting rectifying behaviour of the Iâ€“V curve was recorded in the photoresponse under illumination.

4. Conclusion

We have successfully demonstrated the synthesis of a NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid in two steps: in the first step, NZF is prepared by a chemical method, and in the second step, NZF is used during the polymerization of aniline. The as-prepared NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid based solar photodetector exhibits a short rise/decay time of 0.9/0.8 seconds, high responsivity of 0.069 A Wâˆ’1, high detectivity of 3.735 Ã— 1012, LDR of 6.920 dB and NEP of 1.641 Ã— 10âˆ’6 at 0.1 V. More importantly, the flexibility of the device was checked at different curvatures, and the device maintained a high responsivity at different bending curvatures. In terms of their mechanical qualities, organic semiconductors are often soft due to the weak van der Waals contacts between discrete molecules, as opposed to hard and robust inorganic semiconductors. Nanohybrids are more absorptive than inorganic semiconductors in terms of their optical characteristics. The excellent performance and potential of the NZFâ€“PANI nanohybrid based photodetector device can be attributed to: (a) the use of a low-cost and earth-abundant material based photodetector, suitable for future technologies, exhibiting high responsivity and high detectivity; (b) its environmentally benign and biodegradable nature, which may be popular in upcoming generations since it reduces pollution; and (c) its very short rise/decay time and exceptional stability of 600 seconds at 0.1 V.
