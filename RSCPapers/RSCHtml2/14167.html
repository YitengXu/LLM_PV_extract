Abstract

We report a surface ion transfer method to synthesise ternary alloy CdS1âˆ’xSex (0 â‰¤ x â‰¤ 1) quantum dots (QDs) in situ on TiO2 nanoparticles. By tuning the content of selenium in such quantum dots, the optical absorption spectra can be controllably widened to cover the most of the visible light range. The electron transport of such QDs can be modulated by changing the interfacial electronic energy between CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs and TiO2 nanoparticles. The QDs with optimized selenium content (x = 0.72) give a balance between a broad optical absorption and a suitable energy band alignment. 1. Introduction

Semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) hold the promise of light harvesting due to their high extinction coefficients1,2 and size dependent bandgaps.3â€“5 Various binary QDs, such as CdS,6â€“8 CdSe,9â€“12 CdTe13,14 and PbS,15â€“17 have been thoroughly investigated as light harvesters in quantum dot sensitized solar cells (QDSSCs). Among these QDs, the tuning span of optical absorption by the size-dependent quantum confinement effect is usually limited to within 100 nm in the visible light range. For example, the absorbent peak of CdS QDs was adjusted between 380 and 480 nm,4 while that of CdSe QDs was tunable from 505 to 580 nm.5 The scaling law18,19 that emerges upon spherical nanocrystals suggests that the bandgaps of QDs are larger than those of the corresponding bulk materials. Therefore, greater energy is needed to excite the electrons in QDs, which leads to a shift of the absorption peaks towards ultraviolet (UV) light. One way to realize dual-directional regulation of optical absorption is to employ ternary QDs. By continuous variation of their composition, we could gain a novel approach to tune the bandgap toward the infrared, broadening the optical absorption in the whole visible light spectrum region.

Far more than tuning the bandgaps, a suitable alignment of energy levels is necessary for efficient electron injection from a photoexcited QDs to a wide bandgap semiconductor in the photosensitizing architecture. On one hand, the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels of sensitizers have to lie sufficiently above the conduction band minimum (CBM) of wide bandgap semiconductor where the density of states (DOS) is large enough to inject electrons into the semiconductor.20 On the other hand, the excess energy of the photoinjected electrons that is over the CBM is rapidly thermalized by electronâ€“phonon scattering and cannot be recovered as electric energy.21 The ability to tune the electronic energy alignment at the semiconductor and QD interfaces contributes to maximising the photocurrent of solar cells.

The Cd chalcogenide family of QDs is being investigated for sensitized solar cells because the band diagram of such dots matches that of TiO2, thus offering an efficient pathway for charge injection.22â€“25 Compared with CdSe QDs, CdS QDs have a higher monochromatic incident photon-to-electron conversion efficiency (IPCE),26 which is attributed to higher conduction band edges with respect to those of TiO2. However, the relatively large bandgap of CdS (2.25 eV in bulk) triggers a narrow light absorption below 550 nm, compared to that of CdSe (1.70 eV in bulk). This difference raises the possibility to better engineer the QDs by creating the ternary alloy CdS1âˆ’xSex to simultaneously optimize both band alignment and light harvesting over the whole visible light spectrum range.

The difficulty in synthesis of ternary alloyed QDs lies in the control of their crystal structure, be it homogeneous or segregated nanocrystals. To achieve a homogeneous alloy, the growth rates of the constituents must be equal.27 As the solubility product constant (Ksp) of CdSe (1.4 Ã— 10âˆ’35) is much smaller than that of CdS (8 Ã— 10âˆ’27), synthesizing a homogeneous CdS1âˆ’xSex alloy by mixing all components in precursors would be extremely difficult, although some attempts have been reported by Jang et al.28 Instead of generating alloyed CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs, segregated structures have been formed by current research.29,30 Other compound QDs, like coreâ€“shell CdS/CdSe QDs have been reported.31 The two different zinc blende structures were synthesized without the presence of any defect or polytypism. In addition, another interesting structure of CdSe/CdS dot-in-rod nanocrystals has been synthesized by G. RainÃ² et al. These heteronanostructures offer new prospects to tailor their optical properties, as they take advantage of a small conduction band offset allowing tunability of the electron delocalization from type-I toward quasitype-II.32 The heterostructure of quantum dot-quantum well nanocrystals has also been investigated in recent years, which holds promise of tunable photoluminescence (PL) over the whole visible range.33 To date, reports of facile methods to produce homogeneous ternary CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs have been exclusively via an organic solvent method.34 The QDs prepared via this approach have a linear change in lattice parameters but are wrapped by surface ligands of tri-n-octylphosphine oxide (TOPO), which prevent charge injection from QDs to TiO2 nanoparticles in QDSSCs.35

To surmount the imperfections induced by surface ligands, here we report a novel surface ion transfer method to grow ternary alloy CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs in situ on TiO2 nanoparticles. By tuning the content of selenium in such QDs, the optical absorption band-edge can be controllably widened to 650 nm. We investigated quantitatively the electronic energy alignment at the interface between CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs and TiO2 nanoparticles, while the QDs with optimized Se content (x = 0.72) gave a balance between a broad optical absorption and a suitable band alignment. The electron transport was determined by employing intensity-modulated photocurrent spectroscopy (IMPS). The relationship between electron transport time and interfacial electronic energy alignment provided us a way to modulate the electron transfer by tuning the CBM of QDs. The homogenous alloyed CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs generated a maximum short circuit current density of 10.19 mA cmâˆ’2, which is higher than those of binary CdS and CdSe QD-sensitized devices.

2. Experimental

2.1 Preparation of TiO2 photoanodes

A dense TiO2 nanoparticle film (âˆ¼10 Î¼m in thickness, 20 nm in diameter) and a scattering layer (âˆ¼5 Î¼m, 400 nm in diameter) were prepared by a screen-printing method onto fluorine doped tin oxide (FTO) glass. The substrate was sintered at 500 Â°C for 1 h to form anatase nanocrystals. After sintering, the TiO2 electrode was immersed in TiCl4 (50 mM) aqueous solution at 70 Â°C for 30 min. The film was then annealed at 450 Â°C for 30 min.

2.2 In situ synthesis of binary CdS QDs

The TiO2 electrodes were first treated by dipping into thioglycolic acid, kept for 5 min and dried in a nitrogen flow. The electrodes were then immersed in ethanol and methanol based solutions for 1 min each, the first containing Cd(NO3)2 (0.1 M) and the second containing Na2SÂ·5H2O (0.1 M). Following each immersion, the electrodes were rinsed for 30 s in order to remove the excess of each precursor. This alternate dipping procedure is referred to as one cycle. After 5 cycles, the CdS QDs were successfully deposited onto the TiO2 films.

2.3 Controlled growth of ternary CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs

The gradient CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs were obtained by surface selenization of a CdS QD-decorated TiO2 substrate. A Se2âˆ’ source solution was prepared by dissolving 5 mM of NaBH4 and 2.5 mM of Se in distilled water under Ar gas protection. Gradient CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs were formed by immersing the CdS QD decorated TiO2 substrate in the Se2âˆ’ source solution at 50 Â°C with reaction times from 30 s to 3 h. The samples were then washed with deionized water and dried in a nitrogen flow. We annealed the samples at a temperature between 150 and 300 Â°C under Ar atmosphere to transfer the gradient CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs into homogeneous ternary alloys.

2.4 Characterization of ternary CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs

Morphological and phase structural studies were performed with an SEM (JSM-6500F) and a TEM (JEOL-2100F). Elemental mapping and composition analysis on the nanoscale was carried out by the EDX integrated in the SEM and TEM, respectively. Absorption spectra were recorded with a Lambda-750 UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer). Photo-electron spectroscopy (AC-3, Riken) was used to study electronic structures of the interfacial deposits.

2.5 Light harvesting efficiency (LHE) measurement

The LHE study was carried out on thin cover glass (0.14 mm) to avoid scattered light leaking from the glass substrate side.36,37 TiO2 was deposited onto a thin glass plate and covered by a drop of aqueous solution and the other thin glass plate. Aqueous solution was employed to represent the electrolyte solvent in quantum dot sensitized solar cells. Both transmittance and absorbance spectra of bare and QDs sensitized TiO2 films were measured by the integrating sphere detector in a Lambda-750 UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer).

2.6 Preparation of counter electrode

The Cu2S counter electrode was fabricated by a chemical bath deposition (CBD) process on FTO glass. Firstly, the 25 mM Na2S2O3Â·5H2O and 25 mM Cu(SO4)2Â·5H2O was mixed in aqueous solution. Then, the clean FTO glass was immersed into the solution and kept at 70 Â°C for 1 hour under Ar gas protection. Finally, the second step was repeated twice by changing aqueous solution to achieve a desired thickness of Cu2S film.

2.7 Assembly of solar cell devices

A thin ZnS layer was coated onto each CdS1âˆ’xSex QD-decorated TiO2 photoanode before device construction, to passivate the charge carrier traps induced by dangling orbitals and surface defects. The coating of ZnS was accomplished by dipping the CdS1âˆ’xSex QD decorated TiO2 substrates alternately into Zn(NO3)2 (0.1 M) and Na2S (0.1 M) aqueous solutions. This dipping procedure was repeated once. The assembly of complete photovoltaic cells was accomplished by hot pressing the TiO2/CdS1âˆ’xSex electrode, Cu2S coated counter electrode, and 30 Î¼m thick sealing spacer (Surlyn, Solaronix). The redox pair in this study was polysulfide (S2âˆ’/Sx2âˆ’) with the following composition: Na2S (1 M), S (1 M), and NaOH (0.1 M).

2.8 Device measurement

Current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) characteristics of the solar cells were measured by using a black metal mask with an aperture area of 0.2304 cm2 under standard air mass 1.5 sunlight (100 mW cmâˆ’2, WXS-90S-L2, Wacom Denso Co., Japan).10 The scan mode was conducted from Jsc to Voc. IPCE were measured with mono-chromatic incident light of 1016 photon per cm2 under 100 mW cmâˆ’2 white bias light in DC mode (CEP-2000, Bunko-Keiki).

The bright field transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image demonstrates that the diameters of CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs range from 3 to 5 nm (Fig. 1b). To evaluate the lattice change between binary and ternary QDs, we examined the atomic structure in CdS, CdS1âˆ’xSex and CdSe QDs in high resolution TEM (HRTEM) images (Fig. 1dâ€“f). The fringe spacing of 0.24 nm in the crystalline structure of a single CdS QD (Fig. 1d) matches well to the interplanar spacing of the {004} planes of anatase TiO2,39 whereas the fringe spacing of 0.29 nm in the QD matches the interplanar spacing of the {200} planes for cubic CdS. In the HRTEM image of a QD consisting of a CdSe nanoparticle (Fig. 1f), the fringe spacing of 0.21 nm matches well to the interplanar spacing of the {220} planes of cubic CdSe. In the crystal lattice of ternary alloyed CdS1âˆ’xSex (x = 0.72) QDs (Fig. 1e) we attribute the fringe spacing of 0.22 nm to the interplanar spacing of the {220} planes of alloy cubic CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs. The change in interplanar spacing indicates the lattice change in ternary CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs, compared to those of CdS and CdSe QDs.

We investigated the actual Se ratio (Se/(S + Se)) of the ternary CdSxSe1âˆ’x QDs by EDX analysis (ESI, Fig. S1â€ ). The relative intensity of Se and S peaks varied in each QD prepared with different ion exchange times. The spectrum allows a quantitative analysis to determine the Se ratio (x value) in the QDs. The relationship between the incorporation of Se content in the CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs and ion exchange time was nonlinear (Fig. 2a). Presumably, this nonlinearity results from the entropy change in ternary QDs. The ion exchange rate was extremely fast in the beginning due to the large surface energy of small diameter QDs, which leads to instability at the surface region. With increasing ion exchange time, the ion transfer rate will slow as the surface of the QDs becomes stabilized, leading to a decrease in entropy inside the QDs.

The electron transport time decreases with increasing light intensity in a manner consistent with a power law relationship. Fill factors (FF) of solar cells are dependent on the series resistance (RS) of the devices. The smaller RS will contribute to higher FF. 4. Conclusions

In summary, we used a novel surface ion transfer method to synthesize ternary alloy CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs; the method facilitates continuous tuning of optical absorption and electron transport. The suitable Se content (x = 0.72) in CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs struck a balance between optical absorption and band alignment. An amazing LHE beyond 90% has been achieved for CdS1âˆ’xSex QDs in the range of 430â€“530 nm. We obtained a short circuit current density of 10.19 mA cmâˆ’2 with a ternary CdS0.28Se0.72 QDSSC, which is 2.6- and 1.4-times those obtained with QDSSCs with CdS and CdSe, respectively. Ternary QDs are novel materials that would be useful in development of highly efficient devices because of the ability to tune both optical bandgap and electronic energy alignment.
