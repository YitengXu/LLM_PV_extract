Abstract

Recently, heteroatom doped coreâ€“shell nanostructures (HCSNs) have been widely used as superior electrocatalysts for the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) owing to their enhanced ORR performance and stability under harsh environmental conditions. In this review, we provide the importance of HCSNs and explain how the ORR performance can be enhanced by various heteroatom dopants, such as nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorous, boron, and combinations of two or more heteroatoms. Various types of nitrogen doping were performed with different forms of nitrogen-containing organic compounds in CSNs, such as metalâ€“organic frameworks, zeolitic imidazolate frameworks, and transition metals containing nitrogen sources, which have been used widely for the ORR because they form a high surface area, a facet surface structure, and reactive active sites in the presence of elements that are useful for the ORR catalytic activity. Furthermore, we briefly discuss the synthesis and fabrication of highly efficient ORR electrodes using a combination of di-, tri-, or multi-heteroatom-doped CSNs. Finally, we discuss the superior ORR activities of the HCSNs reported in recent literature and compare the activity with various reactive descriptors and the broad scope of these HCSNs for practical applications, along with their drawbacks and future demands.

1. Introduction

The continuous demand for energy conversion and storage (ECS) systems such as electric vehicles such as bikes, cars, and buses, rechargeable batteries, flexible displays, heating and cooling systems in buildings, electrochromic smart windows, and electrochemical reactors for fuel cells and chemical production has necessitated the development of novel materials that are economically viable, safe, and robust.1â€“3 Electrocatalysts based on nanohybrid materials play vital roles in the synthesis and development of novel materials for ECS applications.2 In the past few decades, much effort was dedicated to the development of such materials and their introduction in various ECS systems such as green-energy electric vehicles using solar panels, hydrogen fuel cells and direct methanol fuel cells, and metalâ€“air and lithium-ion batteries.1â€“4 Electrocatalysts for the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) play an effective role in ECS systems such as metalâ€“air batteries and fuel cells.5,6 Among the various metal sources, platinum (Pt) is broadly used as the electrode material in the ORR because it has a higher electrical conductivity than various noble metals and Pt-group metal (PGM)-free transition-metal oxides.7â€“10 On the other hand, Pt-based electrodes exhibit poor stability and methanol tolerance, which are major drawbacks for the ORR as well as for commercial products because they lead to reduced performance under harsh environmental conditions.8,11 Pt-based electrodes also have other drawbacks in the ORR: they show instability due to their slow kinetics and degradation behaviors.8,11 In several studies, these drawbacks were mitigated through techniques such as alloying of the Pt-based material with various earth-abundant metals and the mixing of the Pt atom in the core or shell with one or more transition metals.12â€“14

The electrode performance in the ORR has been significantly improved by doping with heteroatoms or carbonizing the sample and loading a thin layer of platinum or platinum with other transition-metal oxides on the shell.15,16 Moreover, these methods gradually enhance the stability and tolerance against harsh conditions and result in enhanced electrocatalytic behaviors.17,18 Meanwhile, recent studies have dedicated significant effort to determining the role of the core and shell in the ORR because it might be difficult to predict whether a core or shell containing a metal source plays a vital role in the ORR.19â€“21 To overcome this difficulty, surface modeling studies based on methods such as density functional theory (DFT) were recently performed to selectively predict the role of the core and shell as well as their thickness in the ORR.22,23 Several reviews and research studies focused on the numerous possibilities of developing electrocatalysts for the ORR.24â€“26

Coreâ€“shell nanostructures (CSNs) have been reported to play an important role in the electrocatalysis of reactions such as the oxygen evolution/reduction reaction (OER/ORR), hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), and overall water splitting for proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells and battery applications owing to their large available surface area and abundant active sites and defects.19â€“21,27 Various metals, metal oxides, single as well as bimetallic materials, and carbon based and heteroatom doped materials were used to synthesise the CSNs by introducing either in the core or shell structures.28,29 Chaudhuri et al. comprehensively reviewed the various aspects of CSN synthesis with different approaches and methods.30 They emphasized the important advantages and disadvantages of these approaches and reviewed analytical approaches to predict the successful development of CSNs and their applications in various fields. Further, they reported that the choice of materials depends on various factors such as performance, long-term stability, cost, ease of mass production, and tolerance behavior against acids, bases, and organic compounds. CSNs have also been applied in various other applications such as drug delivery, catalysis, metal adsorption, energy and environmental conservation, and biomedical engineering.31â€“33 In particular, extensive studies on CSNs in recent years have led to various approaches for the improvement of electrodes for the ORR.19

As for CSNs, a vast amount of research has been conducted on heteroatom doping in carbon or other supports to fabricate an advanced electrode with improved properties.34,35 In these studies, CSNs have attracted much attention in the fabrication of electrodes for the ORR because they make the tuning of the metal composition of the core or shell surface easier. The tuning of the metal composition of the core or shell minimizes the use of Pt and enhances performance, in addition to reducing the total cost of the product. A considerable amount of research studies has proved the improved electrocatalytic ORR activity using well-designed CSNs with a controlled architecture.36â€“40 Among them, heteroatom-doped CSNs (HCSNs) also demonstrated excellent electrochemical activity for the ORR in recent years.42â€“44

2. Oxygen reduction reaction (ORR)

3. Coreâ€“shell nanostructures for the ORR

Recently, CSNs have attracted considerable attentions in various fields especially for electrocatalysis for ECS applications (Fig. 3).19,22,62â€“65 CSNs have also been widely synthesized by tuning the metal composition to enhance the electrochemical mass activity and stability for the ORR.66 Numerous studies were conducted in this regard; examples include the synthesis of PtPdâ€“NF/SG,40 Oâ€“PtCuNF/C,61 Ru@Pt,66,67 Pt in PtIr/Pd/C,68 Pdâ€“Pt,69 Irâ€“Pt,70 PtxGd,71 PtML/AuNi0.5Fe,72 Pdâ€“Fe@Pt/C,73 Pt3Co/C,74 Pd@Pt/CNT,75 Co@Pdâ€“Pt/CNT,76 Pt3Fe/C,77 PtxNiy,78 Pt@Ni,79 Cu/FePtCu,80 Ni(OH)2@Pt/C,81 PdCu2@Pt,82 PdCu@PtCu,83 and AuNi@Pt,84 based on CSNs for enhancing the mass and specific activities as well as stability for the ORR. The excellent performance of these electrocatalysts for the ORR was due to their high surface area, high reactivity, good electron transfer ability for oxygen reduction, and ability to accommodate large numbers of active sites.40,61,66â€“84 In most cases, the ORR activity mainly occurs at the surface of the catalyst, so the size and shape of CSNs are much important parameters to achieve an outstanding catalytic activity.85â€“87 The electrocatalyst having uniform size and shape can deliver better electrochemical ORR activity and highly stable properties.88 Zhang et al. analyzed the effect of size and shape of Au@Ni/Pt CSNs for the ORR using a Au core (diameter 2.2 to 10.3 nm) containing a Ni/Pt shell and demonstrated that the CSNs with a Ni/Pt shell thickness above 1 nm can have excellent ORR activity.89 Moreover, CSNs reduce the cost of the electrocatalyst by reducing the amount of Pt loading or mixing with other metals or heteroatoms and carbon. In addition, CSNs improve the stability and performance of the ORR. We also discuss the effective role of mono-, bi-, and tri-metallic CSNs in the electrocatalysis of the ORR.82,90â€“92 An important note to the reader of this review article: in some places, the name of CSNs was not mentioned uniformly because we just follow the name of the CSNs according to the referenced papers in order to avoid confusions between this review article and published paper.

4. Heteroatom doping for the ORR

Recently, doping with single heteroatoms such as N, S, P, and B and the dual doping of two or more heteroatoms have received tremendous attention for creating defects and abundant active sites for ECS applications.19,93,94 In addition, the doped electrode material can show superior electrocatalytic activity because of the charge polarization caused by the dopant. Zhang et al. provided a broad overview of doping methods for transition metal compounds (TMCs) with their technical advancement for various electrocatalysis applications.95 Heteroatom doping in carbon supports can be widely used in numerous applications. For example, nitrogen-doped graphene exhibits three configurations: graphitic N, pyridinic N, and pyrrolic N.34 These structures can exhibit more defects at the edges of graphene networks than in the middle of the plane.34 The defects at the edges facilitate contact with oxygen molecules and enhance the ORR activity. The spin-density redistribution of the carbon network and changes in the local charge density of the doped material can enhance the ORR activity. Moreover, the dopant can enhance the interaction between the carbon support and metal nanoparticles, which also leads to the enhanced catalytic activity and stability of the active material. The electrons in the sp2 hybridized carbon atoms are too inert for instantaneous use in the ORR.34 These inert electrons can be activated by doping with heteroatoms (N, S, P, B, etc.). In this manner, the O2 molecule might be reduced on the positively charged atoms.96 If a heteroatom is bonded to the carbon framework, it can create some defects on the adjacent site because of the difference in the bond length and atomic size.97 For example, as carbon exhibits a higher electronegativity (2.55) than B (2.04), a positive charge is generated on the B atoms, which become conducive to capturing O2 molecules.98 Additionally, owing to the B doping in the carbon matrix, the electrons from the Câ€“C Î * orbital shift to the vacant 2pz orbital of B. Consequently, a charge transfer occurs in oxygen, causing the stretching of the Oâ€“O bond. Thus, oxygen reduction occurs more efficiently. This type of cost-effective and highly efficient catalyst has attracted tremendous attention as a catalyst based on Nâ€“B codoped carbon materials.99 The development of a non-precious single or bimetallic site coordinated heteroatom-doped carbon based electrocatalyst has displayed tremendous advantages in ORR activity as well as improvement in its stability due to the monodispersed catalytic Mâ€“Nx sites on the carbon support as well as maximum use of the atom in the electrocatalytic activity.100 The bimetallic coordinated N-doped carbon exhibits much improved electrocatalytic ORR activity as compared with single metal coordinated N-doped carbon due to much suitable active sites for cleaving the bond in oxygen during the ORR.100 Guo et al. briefly proposed the two types of mechanisms of the heteroatom doped carbon for the ORR.101 Initially, the carbon atom present near pyridinic N can adsorb the oxygen molecule and deliver two possible mechanisms such as a 4-electron (4eâˆ’) mechanism in a single step and 2-electron (2eâˆ’) mechanism in two steps (Fig. 4a).101 In the 4eâˆ’ mechanism, 2H+ attached to the 2O2 atoms can break the Oâ€“OH bond and produce an OH moiety. The remaining H+ reacts with the OH group and produces H2O. In contrast, the 2eâˆ’ mechanism occurs on the adsorbed OOH group by reacting with another H+ to produce H2O2. However, further readsorption of H2O2 and the reduction of 2H+ may lead to the formation of H2O. Guo et al. also described that the presence of pyridinic N with Lewis basicity closer to the carbon atom would play a pivotal role for the adsorption of oxygen molecules in the ORR mechanism.101Fig. 4b also shows the two pathway mechanisms of the heteroatom doped coreâ€“shell carbon nanostructure for the ORR.

Recently, considerable attention has been paid to the introduction of homogeneous catalytically active sites using heteroatoms on a porous organic polymer (POP) skeleton without further modification.102,103 These heteroatom-doped POPs can display outstanding electrocatalytic activity and stability in both acidic and basic media in the ORR owing to their high surface area and tunable surface structure.103,104 Nanoporous carbon materials developed using POPs as templates or precursors can also display excellent catalytic activity because of the special confinement originating from controlled open channels and nanoscale cavities.104 Moreover, such POPs contain some types of heteroatoms based on the choice of the ligand and organic compounds. Based on the nature and choice of heteroatoms, the developed catalyst can exhibit different electrocatalytic activities in ECS.

5. Structural performances and advantages of HCSNs for the ORR

The HCSNs have great advantages due to the presence of single or bimetallic materials as well as the presence of heteroatoms and carbon with a uniform and hierarchical morphology, higher surface area, active sites and electronic structure, and durability under acidic and alkaline conditions.105 The HCSNs having a hierarchical porous structure with carbon networks have demonstrated excellent electrocatalytic activity due to generation of reasonable surface area, highly active heteroatoms, excellent electrical conductivity, and abundant edge defects.106 Similarly, the development of a highly hierarchical porous structure with macroâ€“mesoâ€“microporous frameworks or hollow structures and heteroatoms lead to an outstanding electrocatalytic ORR activity.107 The presence of heteroatoms can deliver a faster charge transfer rate at the electrode/electrolyte interface which facilitates the electron and proton transfer in the electrocatalysts.108 The introduction of defects as well as heteroatoms on CSNs with a carbon support has achieved superior ORR activity as compared with various materials in both alkaline and acidic electrolytes.106,109 An enhanced electrocatalytic activity of HCSNs was also due to better charge and electron transfer behaviours as well as stronger interactions between the heteroatomâ€“metalâ€“carbon structure as well as special structural characteristics with CSNs.110 Recently, the HCSNs derived by the electrospinning method have drawn great attention due to the easier process, formation of a uniform structure, and also act as a template for growing the nanomaterials along with the polymeric nanofibers.41,108,111 Pyrolysis of these nanofibers can result in excellent electrocatalytic activity for the ORR due to the uniform distribution of the metallic material along with heteroatoms and carbon and also the presence of higher surface area and active sites in the electrocatalyst.108 At the same time, CSNs containing a transition metal carbide core and graphitic carbon shell were used as one of the best electrocatalysts for the ORR in both acidic and alkaline environments.111,112 Moreover, the presence of the carbon shell would safeguard the leaching and contact of the metal core from the acid electrolyte.111,112 The better ORR electrocatalytic activity of these kinds of electrocatalysts was due to the equal balance between the graphitization degree, interconnected network structure, and the type of heteroatom dopant in the electrocatalyst.41 The shell thickness on the core surface also plays a vital role in better electrocatalytic ORR activity because the catalytic activity and performance can be easily tuned based on their shell thickness in addition to the doping agents.113 The electrochemical ORR performance of the coreâ€“shell nanostructure can also improve based on the selection of materials and controlled synthesis of CSNs with a uniform shell thickness. The size of the coreâ€“shell particles, controlled shell thickness, and stabilization of the core with a minimum amount of noble metal play a vital role in long-term stability as well as for enhancing the ORR activity.20 A considerable amount of research studies was focused on the effect of the shell thickness in CSNs with respect to the ORR activity.20,36,89,114,115 Zhang et al. synthesized Au-d@NimPtm based CSNs using a Au core and NiPt shell and studied the effect of the NiPt shell thickness for the ORR.89 They observed that the durability of the Au-d@NimPtm electrocatalyst was improved by increasing the NiPt shell thickness. Zhou et al. also studied the role of the CoO shell thickness in the Fe3O4@CoO CSNs and found that the ORR performance was totally controlled based on the optimised CoO shell thickness.115 The synthesis of intermetallic CSNs, also considered as one of the best electrocatalysts for the ORR because of the stability and electrocatalytic activity, was enhanced by using the intermetallic CSNs as compared with the commercial Pt/C electrocatalyst.116,117 Jiang et al. reported the excellent ORR activity and durability of Niâ€“Pd coreâ€“shell nanoparticles in both acidic and alkaline electrolytes.118 The Ni@Pd3/C nanoparticles were synthesized by using a Ni core surrounded by the Pd shell which when further immobilized on carbon black can deliver an outstanding ORR activity and stability due to the maintenance of the structural stability of the electrocatalyst even after several cycles. The Ni@Pd3/C electrocatalyst delivers superior catalytic activity with onset potential (Eonset) and half-wave potential (E1/2) values of 0.99 V and 0.87 V for the 0.1 M perchloric acid (HClO4) electrocatalyst. On the other hand, the electrocatalyst also exhibits the Eonset and E1/2 values of 0.98 V and 0.86 V in the 0.1 M potassium hydroxide (KOH) electrolyte. The Ni@Pd3/C electrocatalyst exhibits an average electron transfer number (n) of 3.91 and 3.98 in both acidic and alkaline electrolytes.

6. Synthesis and fabrication of electrodes based on heteroatom-doped coreâ€“shell nanostructures for the ORR

HCSNs have been synthesized through various approaches such as the solâ€“gel method, in situ addition of heteroatoms such as N, B, S, and P, and combinations of one or more heteroatoms with other metal compounds or metal oxides and carbon-based materials, followed by pyrolysis or solution mixing, blending, and the addition of suitable heteroatom-containing ligands and polymers.119â€“121 Heteroatom doping in CSNs leads to enhanced ORR activity and stability as well as better performance in both acidic and basic media.122,123 In this section, we discuss the synthesis of various HCSNs in detail as well as fabrication approaches for stable electrodes based on these HCSNs for the ORR.

6.1 Nitrogen-doped CSNs

In the recent past, considerable attention has been paid to N doping in various supports, especially carbon (C) supports such as porous and activated carbon (AC), graphene, and CNTs, for electrocatalysis and ECS applications.124,125 This is because the electronegativity of N (3.04) is higher than that of C (2.55), while the radius of the N atom (0.74 Ã…) is almost equal to that of the C atom (0.77 Ã…).126 These properties facilitate the doping of the C support with N atoms. Moreover, N is an electron donor that is capable of increasing the conductivity of C, which is also responsible for changes in the structural and electronic configuration for enhancing the ORR kinetics. N doping is the most widely used heteroatom doping method for the ORR because of the easier doping process and greater stability as compared to other dopants.127 Various organic compounds and polymers containing nitrogen sources, ammonia vapor, or nitrogen gas were used as N dopants on C or other supports for developing active electrocatalysts for the ORR.128,129 The ORR activity is also influenced by the loading amount of the N dopant, type of the N source, and reaction conditions.128,129 In addition, the catalytic activity of the N-doped C electrocatalyst is nearly identical to and greater than that of the commercial Pt/C electrocatalyst in alkaline and acidic media, respectively, under the same conditions; the enhanced catalytic activity in acidic media is due to the higher amount of active sites and defects in the N-doped electrocatalyst.126 As discussed above, three types of N doping are possible on C supports. Pyridinic N shows more favorable ORR catalytic activity than other types of N dopants. This may be due to the fact that pyridinic N is formed mostly at the edges or defects of carbon materials. Doping with pyridinic N on a carbon support was increased using the ball-milling method to enhance the edges or defects on the N dopant.126 The N doping of CSNs has attracted considerable attention in relation to the ORR catalytic activity as well as for further applications in fuel cells and batteries owing to the enhanced catalytic activity and stability. Moreover, N doping plays a more dominant role than several other dopants in the electrocatalysis of the ORR. N-doped CSNs were synthesized using various methods, such as hydro- and solvo-thermal methods, pyrolysis, the solâ€“gel method, simple metalâ€“ligand coordination, solution mixing, and blending with one, two, three, or multiple heteroatoms, based on the type of supporting materials, ligand, and metal sources.130â€“133

Several studies suggest that graphitic N also plays an important role in improving the electrocatalytic activity for the ORR.50 Ejaz et al. discussed the important role of pyrrolic, pyridinic, and graphitic nitrogen in the palladium nanoparticle loaded N-RGO and their importance in ORR activities.182 Generally, these active structures were developed by incorporation of nitrogen atoms to the basal plane of carbon.183 Pyridinic-N and graphitic-N both show a sp2 hybridized carbon structure, whereas, pyrrolinic-N can have a sp3 hybridized carbon structure. To overcome the issue, the preparation of the hybrid material containing the mixture of pyridinic-N and graphitic-N in the heteroatom doped carbon material can deliver much improved ORR activity.186,187

The sulfur (S) atom is more electron-rich than carbon. Perivoliotis et al. designed CSNs obtained by the formulation of a Ni core with a Pd shell which is wrapped on the S-doped graphene surface which notably improved the electrocatalytic ORR performance and was also further applied for methanol oxidation.194 The material was fabricated in a three-step synthesis pathway; for example, S doping was performed by first modifying GO through a reaction with a S-based compound (Lawesson's reagent) in diethylene glycol methyl ether via sonication, followed by refluxing at 185 Â°C for 48 h in a nitrogen atmosphere (Fig. 9a).194 In the second step, the polyol-assisted synthesis method was employed to grow Ni nanoparticles as a core material on S-doped graphene (SG). 6.3 Phosphorus-doped CSNs

6.4 Boron-doped CSNs

Carbon-based materials (CNMs) have attracted significant attention owing to their fascinating optical, catalytic, and electrical properties.203 As for N doping, boron (B) doping on carbon supports has also been studied broadly for enhancing electrocatalytic properties for the ORR because the B atom acts as an electron acceptor.204 Moreover, B doping may increase the number of hole-type charge carriers, which activate two-dimensional carbon materials through the conjugation of the carbon Ï€ electrons with the electron-deficient B atom. 7.2.1 Effect of active sites of HCSNs for the ORR. Active sites play an important role in electrocatalytic activity. Doping heteroatoms on the carbon matrix or metal support or doping trace amounts of metal in the carbon support can develop the active sites on the surface which enhances the ORR activity. Masa et al. discussed the important role of trace metals with N-based heteroatoms in the superior ORR activity.298 Various kinds of active sites such as non-noble metal-based active sites, Pt-based active sites, N-, S-, B-, and P-based active sites, and trace metal-based active sites were used to improve the ORR activity.299 Sa et al. synthesised a carbon nanotube decorated thin layer of porphyrinic carbon (CNT/PC) by pyrolysis of a mixture of CNTs and iron-containing porphyrin precursor to develop an active site of Feâ€“Nx in the CNT/PC electrocatalyst (Fig. 15a).300 At the same time, the authors also found that the decoration of the uniform porphyrinic carbon layer (Feâ€“Nx active site) on the CNT was achieved with the use of a silica layer for CNT/PC as compared to the absence of the silica layer (Fig. 15a).300 This indicates the formation of higher density active sites which are responsible to enhance the ORR activity and also useful in alkaline exchange membrane fuel cells (AEMFCs). Chen et al. also synthesised N and S doped hierarchical CNTs which was surrounded by an atomically dispersed Feâ€“Nx species.301 The presence of the Feâ€“Nx species and the formation of a hierarchical structure by the decoration with N and S atoms in addition to the active CNTs would facilitate the development of more active sites which obviously lead to the enhancement of the catalytic activity and electrical conductivity and an outstanding electrocatalytic performance in the ORR with more positive E1/2 and higher limiting current density.301 Furthermore, the fabricated electrode derived from the synthesised materials also exhibited excellent properties for Znâ€“air batteries. 7.2.2 Defect engineering of HCSNs for the ORR. The creation of defects on carbon and various other materials can deliver excellent catalytic activity for the ORR as well as other electrochemical reactions.15,106,311 Jiang et al. studied the role of defects in the carbon nanocages (CNCs) by DFT calculations and suggested three kinds of defects on the CNCs such as pentagonal defects in the corners, edge defects due to the broken fringes, and hole defects to the micropores.312 The CNCs with pentagonal defects and zig-zag edge defects predicted from DFT calculations showed lower energy barriers. 8. Conclusions and perspectives

Recently, CSNs have been widely employed in numerous applications because of their outstanding properties such as high surface area, controlled surface architecture, and uniform morphology and particle size, as well as the ability to tune the coreâ€“shell compositions based on the need, cost, and application. Similarly, hollow cores consisting of transition metals, metal oxides, and noble metals as well as shells with heteroatom doping (N, S, P, B, and others) and uniform surface coverage can express better catalytic activity as like or higher than the commercial Pt/C catalyst for the ORR. In this review, we briefly explained the various synthesis pathways of highly stable electrocatalysts using HCSNs for the ORR. We also discussed the major advantages and drawbacks of HSCN-based electrodes for electrocatalysis as well as possible ways to improve their properties, with numerous relevant examples from recent literature. In addition, we discussed the various possibilities of modifying the surface properties of HCSN-based electrodes for further enhancing the electrocatalytic ORR activity. We also focused on various types of heteroatom doping in CSNs and the possibilities for their use in multiple applications. Among the various electrocatalytic reactions, studying and improving the catalytic properties of ORR electrodes are highly important for various applications. So, the outstanding electrocatalytic activity for the ORR was achieved by the following ways.

(1) The use of HCSNs based on low-cost and earth-abundant materials with highly stable and alcohol-tolerant properties is necessary for developing industrial energy-conversion and energy-storage devices in order to achieve superior properties in an affordable way. Likewise, the development of CSNs with a light metal core can provide a low overpotential to the fabricated electrode due to the low *O and *OH binding energies, so that these material can deliver exceptional catalytic activity as compared to the Pt based electrodes. In addition, the use of heteroatoms in the CSNs also improves the electrocatalytic ORR activity.

(2) The development and exploration of different HCSNs with various coreâ€“shell compositions, shapes, sizes, and morphologies are in continuous demand to enhance the electrocatalytic properties in numerous applications, and studies have paid an increasing amount of attention to these aspects. The heteroatom doping on CSNs can create more active sites, deliver faster electron transfer and lead to better electrocatalytic activity.

(3) HCSNs can be synthesized with improved stability, robustness, and tolerance properties by simply tuning the surface properties through either direct doping with heteroatoms or in situ solution growth of uniform nanoarchitectures on the inner and outer surfaces of CSNs, the fabrication of thin carbon shell layers covering the core surface through the direct mixing of carbon-based materials, or the mixing and pyrolysis of carbon-based compounds in an inert atmosphere to improve the conductivity and electron transfer properties. Moreover, there are many possibilities to explore in the electrolyte solution to facilitate electron transfer between the catalyst electrode and electrolyte. The control of the porosity and surface area of HCSNs can also improve the contact of the catalyst and enhance the electrocatalytic behavior.

(4) The introduction of a thin Pt coating on a conducting core improves the electrical and catalytic properties as well as the stability for the ORR. However, CSNs also have some drawbacks that reduce their performance in electrocatalytic reactions; in particular, the insufficient surface coverage of materials in the shell or blockage of the core with non-conducting or low-conductance materials in the shell reduces the contact of the catalyst and electron transfer from the core. These drawbacks depend on the types of shell materials and various other factors, such as porosity, surface area, and number of active sites. The proper selection of highly conducting materials in either the core or shell with excellent electron transfer properties can lead to improved electrocatalysis performance for electrochemical reactions. In most cases, well-controlled conductive materials on the shell with superior properties, such as more active sites and edge defects on the CSN surface, can lead to significantly improved ORR catalytic activities.

(5) Another important aspect of CSNs is the maintenance of structural properties under thermal and mechanical stress. Therefore, the choice of materials for the core and shell is highly important to achieve a highly stable and robust electrocatalyst. Such stable CSNs can be synthesized through one-pot chemical synthesis, seed-mediated growth, and post-selective oxidation treatments, respectively.

(6) At present, only a few research studies were focussed on the important role of various descriptors used to enhance the ORR electrocatalytic activity on CSNs and HCSNs. The detailed theoretical role of HCSNs in the ORR needs to be addressed properly with different reactive descriptors in order to empower the outstanding electrocatalyst at a reasonable cost. The important aspects of various descriptors on HCSNs also need to be studied for enhancing the electrocatalytic ORR activity.

(7) Recently, superaerophilic surfaces manifested much attention for the ORR due to the complete wettability of gas bubbles with an underwater gas bubble contact angle less than 10Â°. Numerous experiments were performed in these areas to study the effectiveness of the superaerophilic surface for various electrocatalytic applications.316 However, HCSNs with superaerophilic properties can also have a huge scope for improving the electrocatalytic activity for the ORR.317 At high current density, superaerophilic surfaces can also reduce the energy loss. In addition, the presence of heteroatom doping with a microâ€“nano hierarchical structure provides abundant active sites which helps to provide enough oxygen for superior ORR activity.

(8) Furthermore, research on HCSNs for other ECS applications is slowly gaining momentum owing to the excellent properties and applicability of HCSNs. Therefore, continuous research on HCSNs is highly important to improve the electrocatalytic properties for various electrochemical reactions. The future demand for these types of HCSNs can be met by improving the synthesis methodologies, reducing the product cost through the appropriate selection of materials, and enhancing the performance and stability in both acidic and alkaline media to produce superior electrocatalysts for commercial products.
