Abstract

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as particularly exciting inorganicâ€“organic hybrid porous materials which can be simply self-assembled from their corresponding inorganic metal ions/clusters with organic linkers. MOFs can combine the inherent physical and chemical properties of both inorganic and organic photonic units due to their inorganicâ€“organic hybrid nature. Furthermore, the pores within MOFs can also be utilized to encapsulate a large number of guest species as photonic units. The vast combination possibilities, synergistic effects, as well as controllable and ordered arrangements of multiple photonic units (MPUs) have distinguished MOFs from other inorganic and organic photonic materials and enabled them to be a promising platform to realize novel photonic functional applications. In this review, we summarize the recent and important progress in the design and construction of photonic MOFs, as well as their various applications in luminescence sensing, white-light emission, photocatalysis, nonlinear optics, lasing devices, data storage, and biomedicine. In addition, we highlight the construction strategy and the synergistic effects of MOFs towards achieving high performance and novel photonic functions. Finally, we also outline the challenges in these fields and put forward the prospects and directions for future development.

1. Introduction

Photonics is the science of generating, controlling, and detecting light waves and photons, which plays an essential role in many fields including communication technologies, information processing, military, and biomedicine.1â€“3 Since photons exhibit great superiority over electrons as information carriers in terms of speed, bandwidth and capacity, photonics provides a promising solution to the current bottlenecks that limit the further improvement of traditional electronics. The photonic devices are making huge impacts on the world today and the flourishing development of optical fibre technology is an excellent example. So far, various photonic devices and applications including luminescent sensors, lighting, nonlinear optical devices, micro/nano laser, 3D patterning and data storage, and photoconductors have been widely demonstrated by utilizing inorganic or organic materials, such as ZnO, CdSe, LiNbO3, Ï€-conjugated macromolecules, and so on.2,4â€“12 In this respect, the development of novel photonic materials with desired properties for functional applications is of crucial importance and these materials will be the most dominating forces in the field of photonics.

Recently, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) or coordination polymers (CPs) are being intensively studied as an intriguing class of hybrid inorganicâ€“organic materials.13â€“62 MOFs offer many intriguing properties such as ultrahigh surface area, tunable porosity, and modifiable chemical functionality and thus lead to numerous potential applications in gas storage and separation, catalysis, proton conduction, chemical sensing, and drug delivery.17,20,63â€“76 In efforts to develop better photonic functional materials, the MOFs have attracted much attention and achieved great experimental success, thus leading us to propose a new concept of â€œmultiple photonic unitsâ€ (MPUs) to highlight the unique feature of MOFs in this emerging field. MPUs refer to the photo-responsive components and can take different forms, including inorganic metal ions/clusters, organic bridging ligands, and guest species (Fig. 1). Generally, photonic MOFs can be easily synthesized by selecting photo-responsive metal ions/clusters and organic ligands as building blocks. Furthermore, the pores within MOFs can also be utilized to encapsulate a large number of photonic guests such as nanoparticles, metal complexes, and organic dyes to construct photonic MOFs.77â€“79


Fig. 1 Conceptual representation of MPUs toward photonic MOFs.

It is not difficult to envision that the advantages of MOF materials for photonic functional applications are numerous. First, considering the vast combination of inorganic and organic photonic units, the number of potential synthetically accessible MOFs is enormous. Through the deliberate design and selection of photonic units, various photonics applications such as luminescence sensing, tunable light emission, visible light-driven photocatalysis and photodynamic/photothermal theranostics have been successfully demonstrated in MOFs. Second, the MOFs can not only retain the intrinsic properties of each photonic unit, but also further improve and optimize the photonic functionality due to the synergistic effect among MPUs. For example, the 4fâ€“4f transitions of lanthanide ions are parity forbidden, which can be sensitized by the organic ligands through ligand-to-metal energy transfer.51 This advantage has been widely used for luminescence sensing and light emitting applications of MOFs. Finally, it is indicated that the photonic properties of assembled organic molecules depend not only on their molecular structure, but also on their aggregation morphology. The coordination geometry and pore confinement of MOFs will cause the packing of organic ligands and/or guest molecules along a preferential direction and even result in new aggregation morphologies which are radically different from their bulk or solution counterparts. Consequently, compared with conventional amorphous porous materials and polymers, the controllable and ordered arrangements of MPUs within MOFs undoubtedly afford an opportunity to generate novel photonic functionalities. In fact, this has been successful in achieving coordination-induced emission, lasing, and nonlinear optical response in MOFs. In addition, because the pore size and pore shape of MOFs can be easily tuned to match the guest substrates and change the intermolecular distance, and thus to control the energy/charge transfer process among MPUs, the MOFs will bring much flexibility in the realization of complex photonic functional applications.

The vast combination possibilities, synergistic effects, as well as controllable and ordered arrangements of MPUs make MOFs an optimal platform to design and synthesize functionally tailored photonic materials (Fig. 2). MOFs have opened a land of promising applications in photonics fields where conventional inorganic or organic materials might not be suitable. It is necessary to summarize the advances and relationships among framework structures, pore confinements, intermolecular interactions and photonic properties to further direct the design and construction of photonic MOFs. In this review, we will first review the design and construction strategies using MPUs towards photonic MOFs, and then summarize the latest progress in MOFs featuring MPUs for luminescent sensors and highlight the sensing strategy based on different photonic units. The subsequent section discusses the examples of incorporating MPUs within MOFs through rational combination and synergistic effects to achieve tunable luminescence and high-performance white light-emitting diodes (LEDs). Then, the recent advances in photocatalytic MOF materials using MPUs as catalytic centers and photosensitizers are surveyed. In the following section, we put emphasis on the orientation behaviours, packing morphologies and the energy/charge transfer process of the MPUs towards achieving novel or enhanced nonlinear optical response. The progress in MOFs for biomedical photonics including luminescence biological imaging, biosensors and photodynamic therapy is then discussed. Finally, we identify the critical challenges in photonic MOFs and put forward the prospects for future development.

2. Design and construction strategies towards photonic MOFs

The library of MPUs is abundant, including lanthanide metal ions, metal clusters, dyes, quantum dots (QDs), organic chromophores, luminescent organic groups, perovskites, etc. Some of these can be designed or selected as the construction units for the framework as secondary building units (SBUs) and linkers while others can be encapsulated into the channels of the MOFs as photonic guests to assemble photonic MOFs. In this section, we will discuss the design strategies towards photonic MOFs according to the three construction units of MOFs, namely SBUs, organic linkers and guests.

2.1 Incorporation of photo-responsive metal ions/clusters

Metal ions/clusters offer the key link nodes for constructing MOFs through coordination with the organic ligands (Fig. 3). The resultant periodic metalâ€“oxygen/nitrogenâ€“carbon clusters are also defined as SBUs.80
Fig. 3 Strategies for incorporating photo-responsive metal ions/clusters to construct photonic MOFs.

Lanthanide ions are a series of photo-responsive metal ions with excellent luminescence performance because of the sharp emission spectra and insusceptibility to the environment of the 4fâ€“4f transition. Moreover, the emission wavelength of lanthanide ions is wide enough to cover from blue light to near-infrared: Tm3+, Tb3+, Sm3+ and Eu3+ emit blue, green, orange and red light, respectively, while Yb3+, Nd3+ and Er3+ display near-infrared luminescence.39,52 However, the forbidden fâ€“f transitions make the lanthanide ions difficult to be directly excited. When forming MOFs, the lanthanide ions can be sensitized by the â€œantenna effectâ€: the light is absorbed by the ligands and then the absorbed energy is transferred to the excited energy level of the lanthanide ions, and subsequently, the luminescence is generated from the lanthanide ions. Incorporation of lanthanide ions into MOFs affords Ln-MOFs, which were largely investigated in the past decade.31,39 Because of the very similar chemical properties of lanthanide ions, the Ln-SBUs can be easily replaced by each other, affording mixed-Ln-MOFs, which may display the characteristic emissions of each lanthanide ion in the meantime. For example, by doping Eu3+ into an isostructural Tb-MOF to form a mixed-Ln-MOF EuxTb1âˆ’xDMBDC (DMBDC = 2,5-dimethoxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate), a targeted self-referencing luminescent thermometer based on the emissions of Tb3+ and Eu3+ was realized in a wide temperature range from 10 to 300 K.81 Even more lanthanide ions are able to be incorporated into MOFs to realize colour tuning and up-conversion materials.82,83 Different from the traditional solid-solution type mixed-Ln-MOF mentioned above, coreâ€“shell type mixed-Ln-MOF hierarchical single crystals were also achieved via a solution-mediated epitaxial growth method.84 In the resultant MOF crystals, each component can maintain its own luminescence property, and multicolor to white light emissions are realized through the color mixing technique by controlling the domain and orientation of the hierarchical crystals (Fig. 4).

Although rarely reported, the 5dâ€“4f transitions of lanthanide ions may also be utilized to construct photonic MOFs, which show a much higher dependence on the coordination environment of the metal ions compared to fâ€“f transitions because of the participation of d states. They provide broader absorption and emission bands without the antenna effect, as the 5dâ€“4f transitions are parity allowed. Some examples in this field were reported by MÃ¼ller-Buschbaum et al.85â€“87 By reactions of the metals Eu and Sr together with a melt of 1H-imidazole (Im), 3âˆž[Sr1âˆ’xEux(Im)2] was obtained where both metal ions were divalent. Upon excitation, a broad emission band attributed to divalent europium was observed, indicating the transitions between the 5d energy levels and the 4f 8S7/2 levels. Similarly, a Ce3+-based MOF 3âˆž[Ce(Tz)3] (Tz = 1,2,3-triazolate) exhibited an intense, blue dâ€“f-emission, which was reported by the same group.

Recently, the utilization of photo-responsive metal clusters to construct photonic MOFs has aroused research interest. Inspired by the excellent luminescence properties of copper bidentate ligands, Li et al. assembled the 0D-Cu4I4 cluster into 1D-, 2D-, and 3D-frameworks via strong Cuâ€“N bonds, achieving high performance hybrid lighting phosphors.88,89 Among these hybrid organicâ€“inorganic compounds, for those containing aromatic ligands, the atomic states that make up the conduction band minimum (CBM) are mostly from organic ligands (C 2p and N 2p), while the inorganic components (Cu 3d and I 5p) offer the atomic states in the valence band maximum (VBM) region. Therefore, the luminescence is originated from the metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT) or halogen to ligand charge transfer (XLCT) process. For those compounds built of aliphatic ligands, both CBM and VBM are composed of inorganic atomic states, and thus the emission is primarily cluster centred. Silver(I) chalcogenide/chalcogenolate clusters can also be utilized as photonic metal nodes for the construction of MOFs, which is presented by Zang, Mak and coworkers.90 The luminescent Ag12 cluster deteriorated very fast in air; however, when forming MOFs with the organic ligand bpy (4,4â€²-bipyridine), the Ag12bpy crystals retained the crystallinity for one year under ambient conditions. Interestingly, Ag12 emits weak red light (620 nm) in air, while the emission peak of Ag12bpy is located at 507 nm, which indicates that formation of MOFs may change the excited state. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations demonstrated that luminescence of Ag12 originates from the mixture of LMCT and metal-centred transitions. In contrast, the excited states of Ag12bpy belong to MLCT (Ag(I)-to-bpy) with some mixing of ligand (S,O)-to-bpy.

2.2 Functionalization of organic ligands

Compared to the relatively limited choices of photonic metal ions/clusters, photonic responsive organic ligands are numerous and can be easily modified. Functionalization methods for the organic ligands include extending the length of the ligands, solvent-assisted linker exchange and adding or replacing functional groups on the ligands, as illustrated in Fig. 5.91

Yaghi and coworkers presented a preeminent example of synthesizing isoreticular IRMOF-74-I to IRMOF-74-XI by extending the length of the organic ligands.61 Specifically, expanding the ligand of MOF-74 from one phenylene ring to two, three, four, five, six, seven, nine and eleven affords IRMOF-74-II, III, IV, V, VI, VII, IX and XI. A similar synthesis strategy was then successfully applied to the NOTT-series,92 fcu-MOFs,93 UiO-series,94 and so on. Adjusting the length of the ligands can not only adjust the pore size of the photonic MOFs which makes MOFs suitable for encapsulating various photonic species, but also enable the multiple functions of MOFs. For example, Lin et al. designed a series of isoreticular chiral MOFs [L1Cu2(solvent)2], where L1 is the chiral tetracarboxylate ligand derived from 1,1â€²-bi-2-naphthol.73 On functionalization with Ti(OiPr)4 through post-synthesis, chiral Lewis acid catalysts were generated and the resulting materials can convert aromatic aldehydes into chiral secondary alcohols for diethylzinc and alkynylzinc additions. Importantly, by tuning the size of the channels, the enantioselectivities of these reactions can be modified through the alteration of the diffusion rates of the organic substrates.

The ligand exchange process was developed to overcome the difficulty of direct synthesis of some photonic functional MOFs. It is a very promising strategy because it represents a potentially universal method for constructing new MOFs which may be thermodynamically unfavourable. Cohen and coworkers investigated the anionic carboxylate ligand exchange in robust MOFs, including MIL-53(Al), MIL-68(In), UiO-66(Zr) and ZIF-71.95 Through the solvent assisted ligand exchange process, a dual ligand MOF MIL-53(Al)-Br/NH2 can be obtained from the mixture of MIL-53(Al)-NH2 and MIL-53(Al)-Br. A similar phenomenon was observed in the case of MIL-68(In), UiO-66(Zr) and ZIF-71. The ligand exchange can also be applied to adjust the pore size of MOFs. Cubic crystal bio-MOF-100 consists of zinc-adeninate clusters linked by 4,4â€²-biphenyldicarboxylic acid (H2BPDC), where BPDC can be exchanged by the slightly longer ligand azobenzene-4,4â€²-dicarboxylate (ABDC) and the much longer ligand 2â€²-amino-1,1â€²:4,1â€²â€²-terphenyl-4,4â€²â€²-dicarboxylate (NH2-TPDC), affording bio-MOF-102 and bio-MOF-103.96 The cell lengths of the analogue cubic bio-MOF-100, bio-MOF-102 and bio-MOF-103 are 68.90, 75.24 and 82.25 Ã…, respectively.

There are numerous photonic functional organic groups, such as amino group, conjugate aromatic group, and azide, that can be introduced in the ligands. It is easy to functionalize organic ligands with these organic groups without destructionÂ  of the frameworks through in situ synthesis, linker exchange or post-synthesis. For example, Eddaoudi and coworkers reported a 12-connected rare-earth SBU based fcu-MOF, linked by 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate (1,4-NDC).97 Afterwards, by replacing the ligand 1,4-NDC by 2-aminoterephthalate (BDC-NH2), 2-fluoroterephthalate (BDC-F), or 2-nitroterephthalate (BDC-NO2) in the reaction mixture, a series of fcu-MOFs were obtained under similar reaction conditions.98 Post-synthesis of functional groups is another widely applied approach to functionalize organic linkers. Generally, an active site on the ligand is a prerequisite which can afford chemical reactions for the transfer of another functional group. Qian et al. functionalized the pyridyl sites within MOF-867 with methyl groups, affording a cationic framework ZJU-101 for the removal of anionic Cr2O72âˆ’.99 Through a diazotization route, the amino groups on IRMOF-3 were successfully converted to azide, which can be reversibly reduced into amino by H2S, leading to a turn-on fluorescence H2S probe.100

Forgan et al. investigated the influence of organic groups on the structures and properties of MOFs in a series of isostructural Zr-MOFs.101 It was found that naphthyl and benzothiadiazolyl modified Zr-MOFs can act as fluorescent water sensors, introduction of dimethyl units yields MOFs that display a temperature dependent phase change, and the tetrafluorinated analogue exhibits superhydrophobicity. However, the understanding of the effect of ligand functionalization on the framework is still very limited. A study of the effect of polar functional groups on the dynamics of the frameworks based on the isostructural ZIF-8, ZIF-90 and ZIF-65 revealed that high pressure can convert ZIF-90 to a â€œgate openâ€ phase, while ZIF-65 undergoes a transition to a â€œgate closedâ€ structure, which was demonstrated by Moggach et al.102 The postsynthetic modification (PSM) of ligands was demonstrated by Rosi et al. using a porous pcu MOF platform based on the Ln4(Î¼3-OH)4(COO)62+ cluster (Fig. 6).103 Through the covalent modification at the amino group sites within a near-infrared emitting MOF, MOF-1114(Yb), the excitation energy for Yb3+ sensitization can be carefully adjusted to lower energy, thus successfully shifting the excitation wavelengths into the range suitable for biological imaging applications. The introduction of organic groups may change the excited state or energy level of the ligands and the energy transfer processes within MOFs, resulting in numerous photonic functional MOFs, which will be discussed in the following sections.

2.3 Encapsulation of photonic species within MOFs

Apart from the intrinsic luminescence of the frameworks, encapsulating photonic species is a very important strategy to enlarge the luminescence modes of MOFs, taking advantage of their large and ordered pore structure. There are plenty of light emitters that have been successfully doped into MOFs, including lanthanide ions,104 quantum dots (QDs),105,106 organic dyes,107,108etc.

MOFs without special design may not tightly encapsulate the photonic species into the pores through the simple adsorption processes (Fig. 7a); however, this does not affect the most photonic applications of MOFs. Falcaro and coworkers incorporated highly luminescent semiconductor QDs into MOF-5 through a one-pot method, affording QD@MOF-5 composites.105 Investigations into the QD distribution within the QD@MOF-5 showed that these inclusions were homogeneously distributed in the framework. Combining the sieving properties provided by the MOF and the QD as a luminescent probe, these composites exhibited the quenching effect on ethanethiol and n-isopropyl acrylamide/acrylic acid/t-butyl acrylamide mercaptane. Recently, Yan et al. investigated the photonic performances of micrometer size isostructural crystalline Tb-BTC (BTC = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate), Eu-BTC and Eu doped Tb-BTC.109 All the three Ln-MOFs exhibited an optical waveguide effect with a low waveguide loss coefficient and high photoluminescence quantum yield, which indicates that the Eu3+ in the pores have similar photonic functions as the Eu3+ in the SBUs in this situation.

This is largely investigated in COFs, supramolecular and porous organic polymers.110â€“113 Because of the significant structural flexibility of MOFs, the design of the hostâ€“guest system should also be taken into account. For instance, Huo and coworkers found that polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) modified nanoparticles can be well dispersed and fully confined within the ZIF-8 crystals.114 Besides, the spatial distribution of nanoparticles within ZIF-8 can be controlled by adjusting the time of addition of the nanoparticles during the MOF-formation reaction. Further experimental results demonstrated that the PVP-modified nanoparticles can be successively adsorbed on the continuously forming fresh surfaces of the growing coordination-polymer spheres. Another example was presented by Qian et al.: the pore size of the as-synthesized ZJU-68 was exactly matched with the size of the dye 4-[p-(dimethylamino)styryl]-1-methylpyridinium (DMASM), therefore the dye molecules were tightly confined and highly oriented, leading to a polarized three-photon-pumped laser in single solid-state microcrystals.107

To improve the loading efficiency of photonic species within MOFs, redundant coordination sites on the frameworks are often utilized. The coordination between the guest species and the frameworks can also lead to a more efficient energy transfer between hosts and guests. Qian et al. fabricated CPM-5 and MIL-100(In) films and then loaded Tb3+ under the same condition, respectively.115 MIL-100(In) has uncoordinated carboxylate groups which can chelate with Tb3+, while CPM-5 can only physically absorb Tb3+. The quantum yield of Tb3+ coordinated MIL-100(In) is about 16.8%, whereas that of Tb3+ absorbed CPM-5 is only 1.1%. The much higher quantum yield of the former material is ascribed to the shorter donorâ€“acceptor distance due to the coordination structure. Similarly, Yan and coworkers coordinated Eu3+ on the N,N-chelating sites of the ligand biphenyl-5,5â€²-dicarboxylate (bpydc), realizing a fluorescent MOF-based ammonia sensor.116

The electrostatic force between hosts and guests has also been investigated in the last few years. For example, DMASM is a cationic dye with red emission at 628 nm. It can be introduced into the anionic framework bio-MOF-1 via an ion-exchange process and the electrostatic force helps to anchor the dye molecules within MOF pores.117 Furthermore, a 3D framework ZJU-21 with polarized benzothiadiazole moieties on the ligand showed a very efficient absorption behaviour towards DMASM.118 Very recently, Li and coworkers reported the first example of incorporating perovskite nanoparticles into MOFs through a direct conversion process triggered by a halide salt, n-butanol solution containing CH3NH3Br, which expanded the encapsulation strategies of photonic guest species within MOFs (Fig. 7e).119

The encapsulation of carbon nanodots (CDs) into MOFs was also realized by Zhang et al. by impregnating porous MOFs with glucose and calcination of the glucose at low temperature.120 Glucose is an economical carbon source with a fairly small size, which can be loaded into the pores of MOFs from the liquid phase and can be carbonized at low temperature (Fig. 8). After the glucose has infiltrated the pores of the as-prepared MOF, the glucose-loaded MOF is heated at 200 Â°C in a nitrogen atmosphere. Because the framework is stable at this temperature, the CDs formed by calcination are confined to the MOF pores, thus yielding a well-defined CD-loaded MOF. The CD-loaded MOF thin film was also grown on quartz glass and provided a good optical-limiting property at 532 nm.

3. MOFs for luminescent sensors

Compared with the traditional sensing approach, luminescence sensing can offer faster response, higher sensitivity, better immunity to electromagnetic interference, safer operation in an explosive or combustive atmosphere, and the possibility of remote monitoring. Benefitting from the fruitful energy/charge transfer process among MPUs and analytes, the luminescence properties of MOFs are very sensitive to their structural characteristics, coordination environment, nature of the pore surfaces, and their interactions with guest species through coordination bonds, Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions, hydrogen bonding, etc., thus providing the solid rationale to develop luminescent MOF sensors.31,39,49,122â€“129 In addition, the MPUs within MOFs are very useful to design and develop ratiometric sensors to eliminate external interference.13,122,130â€“132 The advantages and challenges of MOFs for luminescence sensing have been well summarized by several previous review articles.31,39,52,132 Therefore, we will not simply list a large number of examples of the MOF sensors, and will concentrate on exploring the remarkable sensing strategy of these materials, which is essential to further guide the photonic MOF design and synthesis to meet the grand challenges of practical sensing applications.

3.1 Functional sites within MOFs for selective luminescence sensing

Incorporating functional sites such as open metal sites, Lewis acidic sites, and Lewis basic pyridyl sites can improve the sensitivity of MOFs to environmental changes and the selectivity to analytes.133,134 One of the earliest examples utilizing open metal sites for luminescence sensing of small molecules was realized by Chen, Qian and coworkers.135 The open Eu3+ site was obtained by calcining Eu(BTC) at 140 Â°C under vacuum for 24 h. When the N,N-dimethylmethanamide (DMF) or acetone solvent was gradually added into the Eu(BTC) 1-propanol emulsion, the 5D0 â†’ 7F2 emission intensity of Eu3+ exhibited respectively enhancement or decrement, indicating that the weakly coordinated 1-propanol molecules on the Eu3+ open sites were gradually replaced by DMF or acetone molecules. Similarly, owing to the interaction of NH3 with open metal sites, Dinca et al. reported two MOFs, Zn2(TCPE) (TCPE = tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)ethylene) and Mg(H2DHBDC) (H2DHBDC = 2, 5-dihydroxybenzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid), for the selective detection of gaseous ammonia at elevated temperatures.136 Zhang and coworkers also reported a Eu-MOF, which was formed by single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation, that showed multi-luminescence sensing of acetone, aliphatic alcohol, explosive 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) and Cu2+, as the incorporated accessible Lewis basic sites and coordinated water molecules act as signal transmission media.128

Although researchers realized that functional sites play an important role in the fluorescence sensing properties, most early research studies on MOF sensors failed to provide a deep illustration of the sensing mechanism. However, with the rapid development of MOF sensors, the investigation of the sensing mechanism is extremely urgent. Recently, Jiang and coworkers reported Tb(BTC) nanocrystals utilizing the quenching effect for the sensing of picric acid (PA).137 They proposed a mechanism in which the excited electrons in Tb(BTC) are prone to transfer into the PA molecules due to the lower LUMO (lowest unoccupied molecular orbital) level of PA compared to other analytes and the strong supramolecular interaction, such as hydrogen bonding, between Tb(BTC) and PA molecules. In 2015, Li et al. deeply discussed the electron transfer process between MOF sensors and analytes for fast and sensitive sensing of Aflatoxin B1.138 The MOF LMOF-241 was synthesized by using mixed-ligand H2BPDC and 1,1,2,2-tetrakis(4-(pyridin-4-yl)phenyl)ethane (TPPE) and it exhibited a channel diameter of 16.6 Ã…, which is very suitable for the concentration of Aflatoxin molecules. 3.2 Turn-on luminescence sensing

Up to now, the majority of luminescence-based MOF sensors operate by the turn-off mechanism of the luminescence quenching effect. Many disturbing factors, such as the nonradiative energy transition and luminescence self-absorption, may lead to fake detection results for luminescence quenching based sensors.136,150 In contrast, the turn-on detection, in which the interaction of the MOFs with analytes can turn a relatively â€œdarkâ€ material into a luminescent one, can avoid the fake detection results caused by nonradiative transition, thus enhancing the signal to noise ratio and making them more sensitive to lower concentration of analytes. However, the use of MOFs for turn-on luminescence sensing is still in its infancy. To realize such turn-on sensing, the MOFs must be deliberately designed for the unique energy transfer process which can be positively tuned by the analytes.

Generally, most organic chromophores are highly emissive in solution but become weak or even totally annihilated when the molecules are aggregated. As illustrated by Briks in 1970, such aggregation-caused-quenching (ACQ) effect is common to most aromatic hydrocarbons and their derivatives.151 Since the common structure of organic chromophores is based on Ï€-conjugation, the big conjugate ring increased the chance for such luminophores to form excimers or exciplexes, which indeed will quench the emission of chromophores. However, some organic molecules can exhibit the opposite effect: they do not show emission when dissolved in good solvents but become highly emissive upon aggregation in poor solvents or in the solid state. This process is termed as aggregation-induced emission (AIE), which makes it possible to actively utilize the aggregation process, instead of passively working against it. In 2011, Dinca et al. described the new matrix coordination-induced emission effect (MCIE) through coordinative immobilization of an AIE ligand TCPE within rigid porous MOFs.152 The MOFs Zn2(TCPE)(H2O)2Â·4DEF (DEF = N,N-diethylformamide) and Cd2(TCPE) (DEF)(C2H5OH)2Â·DEF exhibit strong â€œturn-onâ€ emission at 480 nm and 455 nm, respectively, similar to that of the solid ligand H4TCPE, whose maximum emission wavelength is 480 nm. In addition, these MOFs exhibit biexponential fluorescence decay behaviors with values similar to those observed for solid H4TCPE. This result demonstrates that anchoring AIE-type molecules within MOFs is an effective method to turn on fluorescence by restricting the rotation of the phenyl rings, which may be useful to develop new turn-on luminescent MOF sensors.

The guest species incorporated into the pores of MOFs can affect the energy transfer between metal ions and ligands, providing a possible way for designing turn-on luminescent sensors. Song and coworkers reported a MOF, [Eu2L43(H2O)4]Â·3DMF (L4 = 2â€²,5â€²-bis(methoxymethyl)-[1,1â€²:4â€²,1â€²â€²-terphenyl]4,4â€²â€²-dicarboxylate), which after being activated with distilled water can act as a turn-on luminescent sensor for DMF vapor.153 When exposed to the DMF vapor, the activated MOF exhibits more than eightfold enhancement of luminescence intensity. Such a significant turn-on effect triggered by DMF is partly attributed to the replacement of the H2O, which may lead to the quenching effect because of the stretching of the Oâ€“H bond. Furthermore, the DMF molecules incorporated into the channels can modulate the energy levels of the ligand, and thus promote the efficiency of the ligand-to-metal energy transfer (LMET) process. Increasing the concentration of guest species in the pores of MOFs is an efficient way to improve the sensitivity of MOF sensors. Ghosh and coworkers designed a cationic porous framework utilizing a chelating N-donor ligand for anion sensing.154 The cationic framework, [Zn(L5)(H2O)2](NO3)2Â·2H2O (L5 = (1E,1â€²E)-N,Nâ€²-(ethane-1,2-diylbis(4,1-phenylene))bis(1-(pyridin-2-yl)methanimine)), was obtained by single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation, and the coordinated NO3âˆ’ guest anions can be replaced by N(CN)2âˆ’ and ClO4âˆ’ anions through an anion exchange process. The N(CN)2âˆ’ and ClO4âˆ’ anion exchanged materials showed a high enhancement of fluorescence, which may due to the enhanced intra-ligand interactions by the change in the structure of anion exchanged frameworks.

In 2015, Manos et al. reported a Mg2+-based MOF for detecting traces of water in organic solvents through turn-on luminescence.155 The as-synthesized three-dimensional AEMOF-1Â·DMAc (DMAC = N,N-dimethylacetamide) displays a 1D channel and possesses breathing capability by removal/readsorption of DMAc guest molecules. The guest-free compound, named AEMOF-1â€², showed two types of signal transduction for water in THF (tetrahydrofuran): the enhancement of luminescence and the shift of the emission peak. Remarkably, a considerable change in the emission spectra of AEMOF-1â€² can be observed at a water concentration as low as 0.05% v/v. They further investigated the sensing mechanism through calculation and found that the occurrence of redistribution electron density after the hydrogen-bond formation affected the energy transfer process within the framework.

In recent years, many researchers seek for clever ways to realize luminescent turn-on MOF sensors. Chi and coworkers synthesized a Ru(bpy)32+-functionalized bio-MOF-1 fluorescence sensor with a LOD of 8.2 pM for Hg2+ in water.156 The dopant Ru(bpy)32+ species were immobilized at the frameworks via electrostatic interactions between the negatively charged bio-MOF-1 framework and positively charged Ru(bpy)32+. With the addition of Hg2+, the adenine can strongly and selectively bind with Hg2+, which increases the positive charges of the framework, causing the disassembly of the outside layer of Ru(bpy)32+ and the exposure of the inner part of the framework to Hg2+, which triggers the further collapse of the framework. The release of large amounts of Ru(bpy)32+ species into the solution leads to a great enhancement of luminescence intensity. More recently, Qian et al. proposed a quench-turn-on strategy to detect hydrogen sulfide.158 As shown in Fig. 11, they incorporated Eu3+ and Cu2+ into the UiO-66-(COOH)2 framework simultaneously, in which Cu2+ can totally quench the luminescence emission of Eu3+. Zang, Mak and coworkers also reported a quench-turn-on strategy to develop a luminescent sensor using a silver-chalcogenolate cluster-based MOF.90 They prepared a luminescent dodecanuclear silver chalcogenide cluster (Ag12) and then used bpy to replace the coordinated CH3CN of Ag12 to construct MOF Ag12bpy. 3.3 Self-referencing luminescence sensing based on dual-emitting MOFs

Although great research efforts have been made for MOF luminescent sensors, most examples reported including turn-on sensors are based on the intensity changes of the single emission. However, intensity changes may not always be the precise mode of recognition because they may include false responses or an erroneous observation triggered by changes of the external environment. In addition to the different absorption and scatter efficiency of different samples, even if the overall experimental conditions are kept constant during the measurements, the luminescence intensity may vary from sample to sample, which may lead to the erroneous conclusions. Considering all these, the ratiometric luminescent sensors, which utilize the ratio between the intensity of two MPUs within the same luminescent material, are developed for overcoming the main drawbacks of the single emission-based luminescence sensors. Fig. 13 illustrates the general phenomenon and mechanism of dual-emitting sensing. Usually there are energy transfer processes between MPU-A and MPU-B in dual-emitting MOFs (Fig. 13a). When the external environment (temperature, solvents, ions, etc.) changes, the energy transfer processes may be promoted or hampered, resulting in energy reallocation between MPU-A and MPU-B, which changes the ratio of luminescence intensity IA/IB. However, in some systems, there is no energy transfer between MPUs and the dual emissions are originated from either two independent photonic units (Fig. 13b) or two independent excited energy levels within one photonic unit (Fig. 13c). In these systems, the analytes may affect the emissions of MPUs independently, which also results in the change of IA/IB.

Dual-emitting MOF sensors are firstly and henceforth largely developed as thermometers because most of the energy transfer efficiency among MPUs of MOFs is strongly dependent on the temperature (Table 1).81,159,160 Luminescent MOF thermometers also possess unique advantages over some conventional thermometers, such as fast response, high sensitivity, inertness to strong electric or magnetic fields, noninvasive operation and biological application.13,122 Doping different lanthanide ions as MPUs into isostructural MOF materials to construct mixed-Ln-MOFs is an effective strategy to design dual-emitting MOFs for ratiometric luminescence sensing. However, the MOF Eu0.5Tb1.5(FDA)3 exhibits a very unusual volume ratio dependent luminescence: the emission intensity of the Tb3+ ions in Eu0.5Tb1.5(FDA)3 decreases, and that of Eu3+ increases as the volume ratio increases (Fig. 16). Wu and coworkers reported another mixed-Ln-MOF, Eu0.35Tb0.65TCM, for probing VOCs.180 Upon immersion in different solvents of benzene, styrene, benzyl alcohol, nitrobenzene, chlorobenzene, aniline, methanol, and DMF for 1 hour, the composite displays solvent-dependent luminescence. Among them, DMF exhibits an enhancing effect for the emission of both Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions, while chlorobenzene presents an enhancing effect for the emission of only Eu3+ ions. These results indicate that the induced VOCs can not only change the energy transfer efficiency from the organic ligand to Eu3+/Tb3+ ions, but also adjust the energy allocation between Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions in the mixed-Ln-MOFs by induced-fit-type hostâ€“guest interactions.

4. MOFs for solid-state lighting and information display

The near infinite choices of MPUs have enabled us to synthesize novel MOFs and purposely tune their luminescence properties. As a result, MOFs can be designed to meet the application of solid state lighting materials in different cases. In the past decade, various MOFs have been developed for luminescence tuning, barcode pattern, information encryption, and white light emitting devices. Their tunable luminescence and myriad structures with diverse properties enable the emission wavelengths covering the entire visible spectrum and white light emission from a variety of mechanisms. In these examples, the quantum yield is affected by the energy transfer efficiency from the singlet state to the lowest triplet state of the ligands, as well as from the triplet state of the ligands to the excited energy level of the lanthanide ions, the mostly utilized construction units of lighting materials.38 Thus, the selection of the organic ligands with suitable excited energy levels is of vital importance to achieve high quantum yield. In addition, most of the reported MOFs for white-light-emitting devices utilize the ligand-based emission as one of the primary blue color sources. This implies less energy transfer from the ligand to the lanthanide ions, thus resulting in lower quantum yield. Many researchers have proposed several novel ways to realize solid-state lighting MOF materials in expectation to improve their quantum yield.

4.1 Luminescence tuning

Due to the promising applications in the fields of lasers, optoelectronic devices and light display devices, attentive efforts have been dedicated to fabricating luminescent tunable MOFs under single wavelength excitation. Lanthanide ions, with the forbidden 4fâ€“4f transitions, can emit long-lived narrow and characteristic emissions. Additionally, the similar chemical properties allow mixing and co-doping of different lanthanides in a broad range, resulting in convenient luminescence tuning properties. Thus, lanthanide ions are widely utilized in luminescence tuning. In 2010, Zhang and coworkers reported a Eu1âˆ’xTbx-MOF film whose luminescence can be tuned by modifying the ratio of Eu3+ and Tb3+.181 The intensity of three photoluminescence peaks located at 540, 589 and 615 nm is assigned to the 5D4 â†’ 7F5 transition of Tb3+, and 5D0 â†’ 7F1 and 5D0 â†’ 7F2 transition of Eu3+ ions, respectively. Due to the existence of energy transfer between Eu3+ and Tb3+, the luminescence intensity at 540 nm can be tuned by changing the Tb3+/Eu3+ ratio, leading to the color tuning of this MOF film. Introducing more kinds of Ln3+ ions into MOFs may lead to a larger range of color coordinates and brightness tuning, which was reported by Guillou and coworkers.82 By introducing La3+ as dilution ions and changing the Tb3+/Eu3+ ratio, they synthesized a series of mixed-Ln-MOFs, [Tb2xEu2yLa2âˆ’2xâˆ’2y(bdc)3(H2O)4]. The color of emission can be tuned by modifying the ratio of Tb3+/Eu3+ while the emission brightness can be adjusted by optical dilution with the introduction of La3+. Zhang and coworkers reported another MOF Tb(1,3,5-BTC)(H2O)Â·3H2O and incorporated different concentrations of Eu3+ ions.182 By changing the doping concentration of Eu3+ ions, the photoluminescence color can be tuned from green to green-yellow, yellow, orange and red-orange. A practical application of color tunable mixed-Ln-MOFs for barcode fabrication was proposed recently.183 The easily controlled color tuning of MOFs meets the demand of barcoded material, such as easy preparation, innumerous encoding combination, high reproducibility and straightforward recognition. Additionally, lanthanide MOFs can also act as a host for incorporation of lanthanide ions, and the luminescence can be tuned by changing the concentration of coordination ions.

Apart from Ln-MOFs, some other porous MOFs encapsulating lanthanide ions can also realize luminescence tuning. Xu and coworkers reported a newly synthesized Zn-based MOF, and introduced lanthanide ions into the pores of the MOF by soaking in DMF solutions of nitrate salts of Eu3+ and Tb3+.184 Upon UV lamp (Î» = 365 nm) excitation, the Eu3+/Tb3+ co-doped MOF exhibited the characteristic sharp multiband emissions corresponding to the encapsulated Eu3+ and Tb3+, respectively. Interestingly, the co-doped MOF also showed Eu3+/Tb3+ ratio-tuned intermediate colors, which can be observed with the naked eye. Following the same strategy, a porous Cd(II)-MOF constructed from the heterocycle-bridge ligand 3,5-bis(3-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole, 4,4â€²-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane and Cd(NO3)2 was reported by Dong and coworkers.185 By easily encapsulating and replacing Ln(H2O)83+ species, the Ln(H2O)83+âŠ‚Cd(II)-MOFs exhibit tunable luminescence emission by adjusting the ratio of incorporated Eu(H2O)83+ and Tb(H2O)83+ species.

Xu and coworkers also reported an example utilizing the guest molecule as the photonic unit to tune the luminescence. They synthesized a Zn-MOF by solvent-induced single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation with appropriate cages for encapsulating Alq3 (tris(8-hydroxyquinoline)aluminum) molecules.186 Under the 360 nm excitation wavelength, the Alq3 in DMF solution shows a characteristic luminescence emission at 525 nm. Upon introduction into the Zn-MOF, the peak showed blue shift along with increasing MOF amount, which was due to the higher ordered arrangement of Alq3 within MOFs.

4.2 Barcode pattern and information encryption

Barcoded materials have been a considerable focus for bioanalytical science and anti-counterfeiting application. With the luminescent barcoded materials, the anti-forgery can be achieved by monitoring the emission readout. The demand for barcoded materials includes high stability, easy preparation, innumerous encoding combination, high reproducibility and straightforward recognition. The first luminescent MOF barcode was developed by White, Rosi and coworkers, based on mixed-Ln-MOFs that emit several independent NIR signals arising from different lanthanide cations.187 The resultant mixed-Ln-MOFs, ErxYb1âˆ’x-PVDC-1 (x = 0.32, 0.58, 0.70 and 0.81, H2PVDC = 4,4â€²-[(2,5-dimethoxy-1,4-phenylene)di-2,1-ethenediyl]bisbenzoic acid), can be sensitized by the same ligand PVDC. These MOFs displayed sharp signals from both Er3+ (1530 nm) and Yb3+ (980 nm) and the NIR emission intensity of Er3+ and Yb3+ varies linearly with their concentration in the MOF. This features their potential usage as NIR barcodes.

The use of mixed-Ln-MOFs for barcode application was also reported by Su et al. and Yuan et al.183,188 One can design as many code combinations as possible in mix-Ln-MOFs, and the easily controlled color tuning of mix-Ln-MOFs meets the demand of barcoded materials. Yan et al. developed an encoding strategy based on tuning the emission intensity of the Ln3+@MIL-100(In) film via the encapsulation of a screen layer containing different amounts of organic dyes.189 In the presence of the filtered dyes, fluorescein isothiocyanate and methylene blue, the emission intensities of Eu3+/Tb3+@MIL-100(In) decrease due to the reabsorbed light process. Additionally, the filtered emission intensities can be adjusted by altering the loading concentration of the organic dyes, giving rise to distinct ratiometric optical codes.

In 2015, Su et al. reported a single-component homo-metallic Pr-MOF for a dual-channel readable barcode in both visible and NIR region.190 On varying the excitation from 295 to 315 nm, Pr-MOF emits the characteristic fâ€“f transition emissions of Pr3+, while upon excitation from 320 to 340 nm, two additional broad bands covering 400â€“450 and 450â€“600 nm appear, owing to the intraligand charge transfer from the ligand. The temperature dependent luminescence study was carried out on 5%Sm,95%Tb@MOF-253_tfac, and it turned out that a distinguishable color change was observed in a narrow temperature range (280â€“320 K).

4.3 MOF-based white LEDs

The optimum white-light illumination requires a source with the Commission International de lâ€™Eclairage (CIE) coordinates (0.333, 0.333), with the color rendering index (CRI) above 80 and the correlated color temperature (CCT) between 2500 and 6500 K. Currently, most commercially available white LEDs fall into two groups according to their fabrication: (a) those obtained by mixing red, green and blue LEDs, also called multichip white LEDs (MC-WLEDs); (b) phosphor-converted white LEDs (PC-WLEDs).194,195 MC-WLEDs can provide good color-rendering properties, but it is challenging to balance the luminous intensity of individual chips. Therefore, the single-component strategy (PC-WLEDs) is a potential way to overcome such drawbacks, and MOFs can provide an excellent platform by combining trichromatic MPUs (Fig. 19). One of the representative commercially available phosphors is cerium(III)-doped yttrium-aluminum garnet (YAG:Ce), whose quantum yield can be above 90%.196 However, the lack of red light in the spectrum makes narrow spectral widths, leading to low CRI, and the emission intensity is also unstable at high temperature.197 This is one of the reasons that new phosphors are still largely investigated. As discussed before, MOF-based phosphors are very attractive in this field because of the large choice of MPUs. However, incorporating many construction units may also make the energy transfer process within MOFs very complicate, and thus the quantum yield and spectral width of MOF-based white LEDs still need to be improved.198,199

5. MOFs for photocatalysis

Exploring alternative energy sources has been a pressing challenge due to the dwindling fossil fuel supplies and increasing energy demand of human beings. Solar energy is considered as the most promising alternative energy source with its unlimited energy influx.212 Solar energy can be transformed into chemical fuels through green plants via photosynthesis. Inspired by this, scientists have developed various systems and devices to achieve artificial similar photochemical reactions.213 Fujishima and Honda reported the first example of artificial photosynthesis in 1972 using TiO2 to achieve water splitting.214 Henceforth, a large number of different systems and materials have been developed for artificial photocatalysis; yet low transformation efficiency still remains a great challenge in solar energy utilization.

As inorganicâ€“organic hybrid porous materials, MOFs can contain catalytic centers and photosensitizers in a single crystal due to the unique feature of MPUs.215 In the last decade, many MOFs have been proved to be semiconductors.216,217 Moreover, the porous structure of MOFs can also provide sufficient area for chemical reaction or serve as a template to form porous catalysts which can theoretically improve the efficiency of photochemical reactions.218â€“222 Thus, utilizing MOFs in light-driven chemical reactions is very promising in the field of photocatalysis.

5.1 Hydrogen evolution reaction

Hydrogen is considered as the alternative fuel energy in future due to its abundant reserves and non-polluting reaction products. Solar-driven water splitting to generate hydrogen is a convenient and green way to convert solar energy into chemical energy.223 During the past decade, many examples of MOFs have been reported for light-driven hydrogen evolution reaction.213 In early research studies, MOFs usually acted as an activity site and provided sufficient reaction space for the photocatalytic process. For example, Mori and coworkers reported the first porous MOF [Ru2(p-BDC)2]n for the visible-light-driven hydrogen evolution in the presence of Ru(bpy)32+, N,Nâ€²-dimethyl-4,4â€²-bipyridinium (MV2+), and Na2EDTA.224 In this system, Na2EDTA acts as a sacrificial agent, Ru(bpy)32+ as a photosensitizer, and MV2+ as an electron relay. Lin, Zhang and coworkers encapsulated proton reduction catalysts polyoxometalates into MIL-101, and the resulting materials showed excellent photocatalytic proton reduction efficiency in the presence of photosensitizer Ru(bpy)32+.225 Encapsulating polyoxometalates into MIL-101 not only arranges them uniformly in the 3D matrix, but also allows the photosensitizer to surround around polyoxometalates to make the electron transfer process more effective.

MOFs can be used as light harvesting materials through the design of ligands.226,227 Rosseinsky et al. synthesized H2TCPP[AlOH]2(DMF3(H2O)2) (H2TCPP = tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)-porphyrin) with porphyrin and formed the MOF/MV2+/EDTA/Pt system which realized hydrogen evolution. Herein, the porphyrin acts as a photosensitizer and the generated electron is transferred to Pt with the mediation of MV2+ which performs H2 evolution. Lin and coworkers reported another similar visible-light-driven hydrogen evolution system utilizing photoactive MOFs.228 By doping bis(4-phenyl-2-pyridine)(5,5â€²-dicarboxyl-2,2â€²-bipyridine)iridium(III) chloride into the UiO-67 framework, they prepared Zr6(Î¼3-O)4(Î¼3-OH)4(biphenyldicarboxylate)5.94(bis(4-phenyl-2-pyridine)-(5,5â€²-dicarboxylate)-2,2â€²-bipyridine)-iridium(III) chloride)0.06 (1). The UiO-framework, 2, was synthesized with the bis(4-phenyl-2-pyridine)(5,5â€²-di(4-phenylcarboxylate)-2,2â€²-bipyridine)-iridium(III) chloride ligand and Zr6(Î¼3-O)4(Î¼3-OH)4(carboxylate)12 SBU. As a well-studied photocatalytic element, titanium compound-based photocatalysts were largely reported.239,240 Among them, the targeted photoactive catalyst NH2-MIL-125(Ti) for CO2 reduction under visible light irradiation was firstly reported by Li and coworkers.241 With triethanolamine (TEOA) as a sacrificial agent in MeCN solution, HCOOâˆ’ was detected in the liquid phase under the irradiation of visible light in the presence of NH2-MIL-125(Ti), and an isotope-labelling method demonstrated that the HCOOâˆ’ was transferred by CO2. Utilizing the superior water-stability of Zr-based MOFs, Jiang and coworkers reported a robust MOF PCN-222 as visible-light photocatalyst for CO2 reduction.244 PCN-222 with dramatic CO2 adsorption ability was constructed based on the catalytic Zr6 clusters and H2TCPP, in which H2TCPP can act as a visible-light-harvesting unit. 5.3 Degradation of organic compounds

With the development of industries, increasing amounts of wastewater containing toxic organic compounds are generated and discharged without further treatment, which have caused severe environmental problems.245 Unfortunately, the current industrial wastewater treatment approaches such as adsorption or separation methods do not degrade the toxic organic compounds into environmentally friendly compounds. Thus many researchers have focused on developing novel photocatalysts for degradation of organic compounds. Considering the practical application, many water stable MOF-based photocatalysts such as MIL-53, MIL-88, MIL-100, MIL-101 and NTU-9 are developed.

Garcia and coworkers reported the first example of photocatalyst MOF-5 for degradation of organic compounds under the irradiation of UV light.246 Encouraged by this work, Chen and coworkers reported a doubly interpenetrated semiconducting MOF, UTSA-38, which exhibited photocatalytic activity for the degradation of methyl orange (MO) in aqueous solution.247 The MO was gradually degraded into colorless small molecules under the irradiation of visible light by this compound. It retained the photocatalytic property after seven cycles, indicating the good stability during the catalytic process. Moreover, the UV-visible light irradiation can accelerate the degradation of MO. Upon irradiation of UV-visible light, the electrons (eâˆ’) are excited to the conduction band, leaving the holes (h+) in the valence band. Then the electrons migrate to the surface of UTSA-38 and reduce the oxygen (O2) finally into hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH), while the holes oxidize the hydroxyl groups (H2O) to hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH). The generated hydroxyl radicals can decompose MO effectively.

In 2014, Zhang and coworkers reported a p-type Ti-based MOF for use as a visible light photocatalyst for RhB and MB degradation.248 The newly synthesized MOF, NTU-9, with an energy band gap of 1.74 eV, was constructed from Ti as metal nodes and H4DOBDC as the ligand. Ti-Containing compounds are usually good photocatalysts. Combined with the excellent stability of Ti-based MOFs, it is promising to be utilized as a photocatalyst. With the inclusion of NTU-9 in the RhB and MB aqueous solution, the degradation of RhB and MB was complete in 80 min and 20 min under visible light irradiation, respectively. When this compound is subjected to solar irradiation, the electrons on CuI jump to the neighboring C and N atoms, making CuI the active point to provide holes and its neighboring C and N atoms as the sites to accept electrons. The catalytic property of [CuICuII2(DCTP)2]NO3Â·1.5DMF in the decomposition of organic compounds was studied in an aqueous solution containing 10 mg Lâˆ’1 MB, and 70% of MB was decomposed without visible light, while up to 80% MB could be decomposed under visible light irradiation. Roeffaers and coworkers reported a group of visible light photocatalysts containing robust Fe3-Î¼3-oxo clusters for degradation of R6G.251 They studied a series of Fe-based compounds, including MIL-100(Fe), MIL-101(Fe), MIL-88B(Fe), amino-substituted MIL-88B-(Fe), and commercially obtained Fe-MOF, Basolite F300. 6. MOFs for nonlinear optics

Nonlinear optics (NLO) describes the nonlinear relationship between the induced dielectric polarization P in a material and the incident light field E. When the high-intensity laser light interacts with a material, the induced polarization P can be written as a Taylor series expansion in the electric field component of the light wave E, as

In general, the NLO effects include frequency doubling or mixing, such as second-harmonic generation, third-harmonic generation, and parametric amplification, the electrooptic effect, nonlinear refraction and multiple-photon absorption. 6.1 Second- and third-harmonic generations

The second-harmonic generation (SHG) and third-harmonic generations (THG) refer to the processes where two and three photons are combined to one with two and three times of the frequency, respectively.267 In order to obtain SHG-active MOF materials, the rational design and synthesis of noncentrosymmetric MOFs is a straightforward strategy. Using asymmetrical or low symmetrical or chiral organic ligands is a simple synthetic approach to construct the noncentrosymmetric MOFs.268,269 Xiong et al. synthesized a chiral ligand H3tzpbin which subsequently reacted with Cd2+ ions to construct a 1D infinite molecular box [Cd(H2tzpbin)2(H2O)2]n (H2tzpbin = trans-2,3-dihydro-2-(4â€²-pyridyl)-3-(3â€²â€²-cyanophenyl)benzo[e]indole), giving a very strong SHG response 80 times that of urea.270 This may be one of the MOFs displaying the strongest SHG response among the chiral ligand based MOFs. The chiral template can induce chirality in the homo-chiral MOFs even when constructed from achiral building components.271 Du et al. synthesized two homo-chiral anionic MOFs constructed from achiral components by using enantiopure L- or D-APA (APA = 2-amino-1-propanol) as chiral inducing agents. Moreover, the ion exchange of counter cations with chiral or polar cationic molecules can enhance the SHG activity of frameworks, which can be utilized to enantioselectively detect chiral alkamines. As for the framework structure, some diamondoid or octupolar as well as a number of 2D grid- or 1D chain-type MOFs display outstanding SHG properties.272 Lin and coworkers carried out the pioneering work in some of these fields. In terms of the 3D diamondoid system, they synthesized a chiral interpenetrated diamondoid MOF sample,273 and varied the length of the building linkers to construct a series of noncentrosymmetric diamondoid MOFs, which exhibit impressive SHG responses ranging from 1.5 to 400 times as large as that of Î±-quartz.274,275 In the case of 2D grid-type MOFs, they utilized an unsymmetrical linking group and Cd2+ salts to yield a 2D rhombohedral grid [Cd((E)-4-pyv-3-bza)2], which is three-layered-interpenetrated and exhibits ultra-strong SHG activity (1000Ã— Î±-quartz) compared with the technologically important lithium niobate (LiNbO3, 600Ã— Î±-quartz).276

Up to now, most of the second-order nonlinear optical MOF materials are constructed from dipolar organic linkers. This traditional strategy usually suffers from unpredictable resulting MOF structures which do not necessarily fulfil the requirement of noncentrosymmetric crystal packing. Different from the approach of directly constructing noncentrosymmetric MOFs, Qian, Cui and coworkers developed a novel approach to construct NLO MOF materials by orderly aligning the pyridinium hemicyanine dyes as photonic units into the 1D channels of the MOF ZJU-28.277 SHG measurements indicate that the NLO intensities of the dye-encapsulated MOFs are heavily dependent on the included guest dyes: the longer the terminal alkyl chains of the included hemicyanine dyes, the stronger the NLO intensity of the resulting MOF materials (Fig. 30). In addition, the DPASD-encapsulated MOFs NOTT-210âŠƒDPASD (DPASD = 4-(4-(diphenylamino)styryl)-1-dodecylpyridinium) with 3D intersecting channels and bio-MOF-1âŠƒDPASD with larger 1D pore channels do not exhibit any NLO properties, which is attributed to the poor pore channel confinement for the dye DPASD. These results indicate that exact pore/channel matching with the included MPUs and strong interactions between the host frameworks and the MPUs are important for stabilizing the highly ordered MPUs to generate second order NLO properties.278

With the development of the laser technology, an elaborate investigation of THG activity must be addressed.

6.2 Multi-photon process

The multi-photon process is a typical phenomenon of non-linear optics in which at least two photons are simultaneously absorbed to pump an electron to an excited state and then drop down to the ground state accompanied with a photon of higher frequency emission.12 Such process can offer stronger spatial confinement, deeper penetration and less Rayleigh scattering, which are very promising for applications in biological imaging, optical data storage, photo-delivery and more.285,286 Recently, Vittal et al. synthesized a MOF exhibiting multiphoton-excited photoluminescence (MEPL) using the NLO active chromophores as ligands.287 Such a MOF can simultaneously absorb up to four photons and the MEPL performance can be further enhanced by loading guest molecules of high quantum yield into the pores of the MOF material. Afterwards, this group systematically studied the structureâ€“property relationship with respect to the two-photon excited fluorescence in MOFs.288 On the basis of three organic dyes, An2Py, trans,trans-9,10-bis(4-pyridylethynyl)anthracene (AnEPy), and 1,4-bis[2-(4â€²-pyridyl)ethenyl]-benzene (BPEB), and auxiliary ligands, nine Zn-MOFs were synthesized. It was found that the ligand is critical for the two-photon absorption (TPA) cross section of MOFs, and the structural aspect can also affect it. Generally, MOFs constructed from the ligands with higher two-photon absorption cross section also exhibit higher TPA cross section; however, the rotation flexibility and conjugation degree of the ligand within MOFs may influence the nonradiative decay efficiency, changing the two-photon excited fluorescence.

Compared with the MEPL, multiphoton-pumped lasing will require much more prerequisites including a high multiphoton absorption (MPA) cross-section, enough lasing efficiency and a suitable resonant cavity. Qian, Chen et al. demonstrated a new two-photon-pumped lasing by encapsulating the cationic pyridinium hemicyanine dye DMASM with a large TPA cross section into an anionic MOF, bio-MOF-1.117 Because of the pore confinement of DMASM molecules within bio-MOF-1, the dye-encapsulated MOF bio-MOF-1âŠƒDMASM exhibits a much stronger emission at 610 nm with a fluorescence quantum efficiency of 25.87% than that of the DMASM solution. The high luminescence efficiency and the large TPA cross section of the DMASM dye enable the bio-MOF-1âŠƒDMASM to generate significant two-photon excited fluorescence and stimulated emission under excitation with a Nd:YAG laser at 1064 nm (Fig. 32a and c). More importantly, because the opposing top and bottom faces of the MOF crystals indeed function as two reflectors of a Fabryâ€“Perot cavity and provide strong self-cavity optical confinement and optical feedback, the two-photon-pumped lasing is observed around 640 nm when pumped under a 1064 nm pulse laser with a threshold of 0.148 mJ. This strategy not only combines the crystalline benefit of MOFs and the luminescence behaviour of organic dyes but also creates a new synergistic two-photon-pumped lasing functionality, opening a new perspective for the design of highly efficient two-photon-pumped micro-lasers for integrated photonic circuits.

For the higher-order multiphoton pumped lasing, to obtain the enough optical gain will be more difficult due to the much smaller MPA cross-section and lasing efficiency. It is thus necessary to increase the concentration of the gain medium but without significant luminescence quenching. In addition, in order to efficiently excite the gain medium, the realization of the gain medium alignment/orientation is very important given the fact that the pump light and the multiphoton absorption of the gain medium are usually polarized. Very recently, Qian, Chen et al. reported the first polarized three-photon-pumped lasing in a solid-state microcrystal upon the excitation of 1380 nm.107 As shown in Fig. 32b and d, they synthesized a new anionic MOF ZJU-68, whose pore morphology and size can exactly match that of the dye molecule DMASM. They successfully aligned the DMASM molecules into the 1D channels of ZJU-68 via the in situ self-assembly approach to generate ZJU-68âŠƒDMASM. ZJU-68âŠƒDMASM exhibits a relatively high quantum yield of 24.28% with an extremely high dye concentration of more than 0.4 M. Besides the natural crystal facets serving as the resonant cavity and the suitable refraction index, the uniform highly aligned DMASM molecules of high content fulfilled the high-gain lasing with highly polarized excitation response, and showed a perfect polarized emission with degree of polarization (DOP) > 99.9%. Combining both polarization and high-order multiphoton excitation, this material is potentially useful for a new kind of biological imaging due to its ability to reduce multiple scattering, enhance the contrast and improve tissue imaging resolution.

In 2013, Walton et al. reported the first MOF containing a photochromic guest dithienylethene (DTE) with the color changed and reversed upon irradiation of UV and visible light, respectively.289 Such photo-reversible behavior makes the hybrid material a potential candidate in the application of photo-switchable devices and erasable data storage. In these examined systems based on the one-photon triggered reaction process, the spatial selectivity could not be realized because the photochemical responses take place either on the surfaces or in the whole crystals of MOFs. By utilizing the photoactive molecule 2,5-bis(3,5-dicarboxyphenyl)-1-methylpyridinium hydroxide as the linker, Qian and coworkers demonstrated a MOF for 3D patterning and two-photon imaging.290 When the single crystals of ZJU-56-0.20 were exposed to a UV lamp at a wavelength of 365 nm, the emission band around 450 nm decreased rapidly, while a new band centered at 550 nm emerged and increased gradually. 1H NMR results indicate that the change of luminescence may be attributed to the nucleophilic addition and subsequent oxidation of the pyridine ring in linkers under UV light irradiation. Similar to the response to UV light illumination, the MOF exhibits a significant two-photon excited fluorescence change in response to an infrared femtosecond laser of 900 nm. This unique two-photon response allows for two photon patterning and imaging in a high-resolution (1 Ã— 1 Ã— 5 Î¼m3) 3D structure (Fig. 33). Because of the irreversible photochemical process, the fluorescence pattern is much more stable and does not show much decay even after 30 days. The incorporation of photoactive linkers to tune the luminescence of MOFs for 3D patterning and two-photon imaging will enable MOFs to be useful materials for sensor arrays and data storage media.

The NLO properties of MOFs depend not only on those of chromophore molecules constituting them, but also on the orientation and morphology of their assemblies. As mentioned before, the ordered confinement of pyridinium hemicyanine chromophores within the pores of MOFs can generate high second-order NLO activity and multiphoton pumped lasing. In addition, the fruitful excited state photophysical properties and energy/charge transfer processes of MPUs enable the realization of novel NLO effects. Therefore, a thorough understanding of the photophysical properties and energy transfer processes is very essential to direct the NLO MOFs design and synthesis. Lin and coworkers investigated the step-by-step random hopping model which is usually used to explain the Dexter mechanism and the FÃ¶rster mechanism.77,291 They found that singlet exciton migration that occurs in MOFs through the FÃ¶rster mechanism also involves one-step jumping over a long distance, which cannot be explained by the step-by-step random hopping model. In such a system, the truxene-tribenzoate ligands serve as the antenna array in which excitons move via ligand-to-ligand energy exchange. The coumarin dye molecules within the MOF cavity act as acceptors of energy. Thus the emission ratio between the coumarin dye and ligands corresponds to the exciton migration rates. The exciton diffusivities were found to be 1.8 Ã— 10âˆ’2 and 2.3 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 sâˆ’1 in two as-synthesized MOFs respectively, which demonstrates that exciton jumping beyond nearest neighbors happens in these systems.

Howard, Richards et al. examined the tripletâ€“triplet annihilation (TTA) based photon upconversion process in a MOF with a three-layer ordered structure.292 Generally, the TTA-based upconversion system comprises a pair of chromophores: sensitizer and annihilator. Upon photoexcitation of a sensitizer, the singlet state sensitizer undergoes intersystem crossing (ISC), giving rise to its triplet state. Then, tripletâ€“triplet annihilation between two excited triplet annihilators generated by tripletâ€“triplet energy transfer eventually creates one excited singlet annihilator. Subsequently, the upconversion luminescence is observed. Howard et al. proposed the three-layer Aâ€“Bâ€“A structural MOF and fabricated the sensitizer layer â€œBâ€ using Pd(II) 5,15-diphenyl-10,20-di(4-carboxyphenyl)porphyrin linkers on top of an emitter layer â€œAâ€ consisting of 4,4â€²-(anthracene-9,10-diyl)dibenzoate linkers. The chromophores within the layer B are excited with green (532 nm) photons to generate triplet states in B, which will cross the Bâ€“A heterojunction by Dexter electron exchange and be collected in the layer A (Fig. 34). Subsequently, pairs of triplets can decay via TTA-upconversion, emitting higher-energy blue photons. More importantly, with a thicker B layer which can absorb more photons to generate more triplets and a thinner A layer which may improve the tripletsâ€™ concentration, the photon unconversion threshold can be tuned to a lower state. These works present an insight into the exciton transfer mechanism in MOFs and pave the way for designing efficient energy transport networks.

As porous materials, MOFs were initially investigated as hosts for drugs which need to be delayed released in the field of biomedicine.295â€“297 With the introduction of MPUs into MOFs, such a material exhibits many unexpected applications in photobiology, such as biological imaging, biosensors and photodynamic therapy and photothermal therapy.298â€“300 The ability of flexible design enables MOFs to be compatible with the complicated environment of organisms. 7.1 Nanoscale MOFs

Recently, scaling down the MOFs has afforded an exciting new class of materials known as nano-MOFs. Compared with other nanomaterials, nanoscale MOFs possess several advantages that make them superior for biological and biomedical applications:302â€“305 (1) the good crystallinity, structural controllability and post-modification allow nano-MOFs to be rationally designed for specific applications. (2) The periodic and high porosity allows nano-MOFs to achieve high and uniform loadings of imaging/therapeutic agents, minimize the self-quenching effect of dye molecules and quickly diffuse small molecules. (3) The relatively labile metalâ€“ligand bonds make nano-MOFs intrinsically bio-degradable in the long term.

Apart from a few MOFs, such as UiO-66-derivatives, UiO-67-derivatives, MIL-derivatives, ZIF-8, etc., most MOFs need to be scaled down into nano-MOFs to meet the demand of biological applications. In general, nano-MOFs can be synthesized through the following methods:306 nanoprecipitation method, mechanochemical synthesis, solvothermal/microwave-assisted synthesis, sonochemical synthesis, and surfactant-involved synthesis. The first two approaches often can only produce amorphous materials, while the other methods can also afford crystalline materials, due to the better control on nanoscale nucleation and growth kinetics. In addition, the last method includes surfactant-templated synthesis and reverse microemulsion synthesis, requiring surfactants as assistants to control the synthesis process as well as stabilize and disperse the nanoscale particles.

The utilization of the water-in-oil, or reverse, microemulsion method for nano-MOF synthesis has been reported by Lin and coworkers.307 They prepared a nano-MOF Gd(BDC)1.5(H2O)2 by reacting GdCl3 and bis(methylammonium)benzene-1,4-dicarboxylate in the cationic cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB)/isooctane/1-hexanol/water system. By varying the w value (defined as the water/surfactant molar ratio) of the microemulsion systems, the morphologies and sizes of the nano-MOF can be tuned. Furthermore, the particle size of nano-MOF is also affected by the reactant concentration and the reactant ratio. The crystalline nanoplates of [Gd(1,2,4-BTC)(H2O)3]Â·H2O, with a diameter of âˆ¼100 nm and an average thickness of 35 nm, have also been prepared using the reverse microemulsion method. 7.3 Biosensing

Luminescent nano-MOFs have become very appealing candidates for measuring the levels of bio-sensing or biochemical compounds, owing to the wide range of selectable MPUs, and various pore/channel structures that can be utilized to tune the optical, adsorbed, and specifically selective properties for the required analysis.318 Lin and coworkers covalently conjugated fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) to a UiO-type nano-MOF affording the first nano-MOF example F-UiO for real-time intracellular pH sensing in live cells, with the desired properties of high pH response sensitivity, structural stability, and efficient cellular uptake.319 This study also provided the first insight into the endocytosis and intracellular trafficking process of nano-MOFs, paving the way for further research studies. Very recently, they presented the first example of nano-MOF based intracellular oxygen sensor R-UiO, which is based on the Pt(II)-porphyrin ligand as an O2-sensitive probe and the Rhodamine-B isothiocyanate ligand as an O2-insensitive reference probe, exhibiting excellent phosphorescence/luminescence dual-emission for the ratiometric detection of O2.320

Early diagnosis of virus nucleic acid is very important for quarantine and disease control.321 Chen et al. reported a water stable Cu-based MOF, which can form electrostatic, Ï€-stacking, and/or hydrogen-bonding interactions with carboxyfluorescein (FAM)-labelled single-stranded DNA sequences (P-DNA), as an effective turn-on fluorescent sensor for detecting human immunodeficiency virus 1 (HIV-1) double-stranded DNA and Sudan virus (SUDV) RNA sequences with detection limits of 196 and 73 pM, respectively (Fig. 38).322 The fluorescence of FAM can be quenched by the MOF through the photoinduced electron-transfer process. With the addition of target DNA, P-DNA is released from the MOF and thus the fluorescence intensity of FAM can be recovered. They found that the MOF with bigger pores from flexible zwitterionic carboxylates of low symmetry may provide a possibility to detect longer nucleic acid sequences. More recently, the rise of a very acute virus infection, the Ebola Virus Disease (EVD), received a great attention for its high infectivity and high mortality rates. Chen and coworkers constructed a water-stable dysprosium-based MOF containing the probe ss-DNA by non-covalent interactions, serving as a very efficient sensing platform for complementary Ebolavirus RNA sequences with a detection limit of 160 pM.323

In addition, the nano-MOFs as well as their hybrid materials have been reported for use as biosensors in many other fields, such as intracellular ion sensing, small molecule sensing and albumen detection. 7.4 Photodynamic therapy

PDT, which uses photosensitizers (PSs) to produce highly cytotoxic reactive oxygen species (ROS) such as singlet oxygen (1O2) by light activation, is a promising anti-cancer approach to selectively kill tumor cells via the localized irradiation at tumor sites, and is gradually becoming an alternative to traditional cancer treatments for its minimal invasiveness and low toxicity to normal tissues.326,327 Porphyrin is one of the most utilized photonic units in constructing MOFs for PDT, because its strong absorption in the red region locates in the biologic window and high intersystem crossing probability leads to effective population of the triplet state, coupled with an efficient generation of singlet oxygen.

Lin and coworkers designed the first Hf-porphyrin-based nano-MOF example (DBP-UiO), as a highly efficient PS for the photodynamic therapy of resistant head and neck cancer.328 The DBP-UiO can efficiently generate 1O2 owing to the construction components and the porous morphology: (1) porphyrin-based linkers offer the site isolation of the PS; (2) Hf centers enhance the intersystem crossing; (3) the porous DBP-UiO nanoplates facilitate the diffusion of 1O2. More recently, Lin's group partially reduced the DBP ligand to the ligand 5,15-di(p-benzoato)-chlorin (DBC) and utilized the latter ligand to construct the framework with Hf ions, demonstrating the first chlorin-based nano-MOF DBC-UiO with much improved performance than the previous nano-DBP-UiO.329 Besides possessing the same crystallinity, stability, porosity, and nanoplate morphology as DBP-UiO, the partially reduced DBC ligand in DBC-UiO results in a 13 nm red-shift and an 11-fold increase in the extinction coefficient of the lowest-energy Q band, leading to the much higher PDT cytotoxicity with 1O2 generation 3 times as efficient as that of DBP-UiO. Considering that the X-rays can penetrate deeply into tissue, this group synthesized Ir[bpy(ppy)2]+- or Ru(bpy)32+-derived Hf-MOF, where heavy Hf atoms in the SBUs can efficiently absorb X-rays and transfer energy to Ir[bpy(ppy)2]+- or Ru(bpy)32+ moieties to induce PDT by generating ROS.330 As a result, high 1O2 quantum yield of the compound was realized, and this PDT treatment led to a 90% reduction in tumor volumes.

Zhou et al. focused on the reversible control of 1O2 generation.331 Very recently, they introduced the PS and photochromic (PC) units as building components into the Zr-based nano-MOFs via the in situ synthesis approach, achieving the reversible control of 1O2 generation for PDT to the greatest extent by optimizing the ratio of PS and PC units.332 As shown in Fig. 39a and b, the chemical structure of the switch 1,2-bis(5-(4-carbonxyphenyl)-2-methylthien-3-yl)-cyclopent-1-ene (BCDTE) can be photoisomerized thus tuning the energy level. When the BCDTE switch is in the open state, the energy transfer occurs from the T1 energy level of TCPP to 3O2, leading to 1O2 generation. In contrast, the closed BCDTE can quench 1O2 generation due to the energy transfer to the BCDTE. Furthermore, they evaluated the applicability of this system in live B16 cells. As shown in Fig. 39câ€“e, the open state sample exhibited remarkable red emission while the closed state sample shows negligible fluorescence, demonstrating that the excited energy of TCPP was not disturbed by the open switch while the closed form of BCDTE turns off the 1O2 generation, as proposed in Fig. 39b. Based on the experimental research, Zhou and co-workers found that the size optimization of nano-MOFs as PSs may play an important role in improving cellular response.333 They successfully tuned the size of PCN-224 nanoparticles into the biologically interesting range of 30â€“190 nm and eventually found that the 90 nm-PCN-224 possesses the highest PDT efficacy. Moreover, the active targeting modality of the PCN-224 nano-platform for further enhancement of PDT efficacy can be obtained via the post-synthetic FA modification onto the Zr6 cluster. A recent example of H2S triggered nMOF photosensitizer for PDT with controllable 1O2 release was realized by Tang and coworkers.334 The Zn-TCPP was selected as a photosensitive bridging ligand to construct a nMOF with Cu2+, which can significantly minimize the ROS production efficiency of the PS. However, with the appearance of H2S, Cu2+ ions were taken out and the ROS production efficiency recovered, leading to a H2S triggered photosensitizer. These results will promote the design of nano-MOFs at the molecule scale for the specific applications of PDT.

Though porphyrins and derivatives were widely utilized as PSs for PDT, the extinction coefficients of them in the therapeutic window are not very high. The Xie group reported a non-porphyrin-based MOF for PDT.335 In their work, the non-porphyrin PS, BODIPY, which is considered as an ideal PDT agent because of low dark toxicities and high extinction coefficients, was incorporated into UiO-66 through a ligand exchange process. In vitro phototoxicity studies on B16F10 mouse melanoma cells demonstrated high phototoxicity for both UiO-BODIPY and free BODIPY, while UiO-BODIPY showed better photostability.

In addition to focusing on the generation of singlet oxygen, more and more reports concentrate on the synergy of PDT with other treatments based on the multi-functional nano-MOFs. Zhang et al. assembled a dye-labelled peptide, a folate targeting motif and a porphyrin into the cages of a nano-MOF, not only significantly increasing the 1O2 quantum yield of porphyrin by 6.2 times, but also achieving highly efficient cancer therapy and in situ therapeutic monitoring, which exhibited great potential in the integration of cancer diagnosis and treatment.336 Chen et al. reported the first graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4, a PDT agent) nanosheet@ZIF-8 coreâ€“shell nanoparticles for photo-chemo combination therapy (Fig. 40).337 The combination of chemotherapeutic effects of DOX and the PDT effect of g-C3N4 nanosheets leads to significantly enhanced efficacy. By encapsulating UCNPs and DOX into porphyrin-based nMOFs, Yan et al. firstly realized a MOF for chemotherapy and NIR-induced PDT.338 The absorption spectrum of the porphyrin-based-MOF is overlapped with the emission spectrum of UCNPs over a broad range, enabling the energy transfer between them. As a result, upon excitation at 980 nm, the ROS can be generated. In vivo antitumor activity was investigated in the 4T1 breast tumor-bearing mice. The tumor growth of the Dox/UCMOFs + NIR treated group was completely suppressed, demonstrating the excellent synergistic therapeutic effect of chemotherapy and NIR-induced PDT. Very recently, Liu et al. reported a nano-MOF rationally designed with organic ligand TCPP as a PS to allow PDT and the hafnium (Hf4+) center serving as a radio-sensitizer to enhance radiotherapy (RT).339 These works present a new concept of developing multi-functional nano-MOFs, in which the building components, post-modification and guest molecules can contribute to the therapeutic effect.

7.5 Photothermal therapy

Photothermal therapy (PTT) employs photoabsorbing agents to generate heat from light, especially NIR emission, for thermal ablation of cancer cells, and has attracted wide attention owing to its minimal effect to biological tissue. With the photonic units as photoabsorbers, nano-MOFs can be designed as PTT agents, which is an emerging research topic nowadays.

A pioneering study in this field was reported by Jing and coworkers.341 The hybrid UiO-66@PAN, constructed from UiO-66 and NIR-absorbing polymer polyaniline (PAN), combines the advantages of stability and excellent photothermal activity for the effective photothermal therapy of colon cancers. In vivo experiments were carried out in colon cancer bearing mice. The tumors treated with UiO-66@PAN and NIR irradiation exhibited complete tumor regression compared to the control groups after 10 d, demonstrating the excellent PTT treatment effect. Considering the excellent photothermal stability and high heat generation rate of Au nanorods, they are usually utilized as PTT agents modified with CTAB. Chen and coworkers reported ZIF-8 coated Au nanorods for enhanced photothermal therapy and stability, overcoming the high cytotoxicity and aggregation trend of CTAB coated Au nanorods.342 As a result, through treatment with laser in PBS, the temperature of the sample with ZIF-8 coated Au nanorods sharply increased from 20.0 to 47.6 Â°C, while the Au nanorods showed much lower photothermal effects.

Combining MRI and PTT, Liu's group fabricated a nano MOF via the self-assembly of Mn2+ and IR825 bridging ligands, which can offer strong MR contrast and high NIR absorbance, respectively.343 The MOF was further modified with polydopamine (PDAM) and polyethylene glycol (PEG), affording Mn-IR825@PDAM-PEG with excellent physiological stability (Fig. 42a). Upon intravenous injection of Mn-IR825@PDAM-PEG, T1-weighted MR signals gradually showed up in the tumor, as shown in Fig. 42b, indicating time-dependent tumor accumulation of the MOF. Additionally, the in vivo PTT efficiency of Mn-IR825@PDAM-PEG was evaluated in 4T1 tumor-bearing mice. As shown in Fig. 42c, the temperature of tumors in mice 24 h after intravenous injection with Mn-IR825@PDAM-PEG would rapidly rise from âˆ¼34 to âˆ¼52 Â°C within 5 min under the irradiation of an 808 nm laser at 0.6 W cmâˆ’2, while the control group showed only a âˆ¼4 Â°C increase under the same condition. More importantly, it was found that the local heating could promote the release of the drug, which amplifies the treatment effect for cancer. Similarly, by incorporating graphene quantum dots into DOX-loading ZIF-8, Zhu et al. realized synergistic chemotherapy and PTT.346 With breast cancer 4T1 cells as a model cellular system, the results indicated that the combined chemotherapy and PTT exhibited a significant synergistic effect, leading to a higher efficacy to kill cancer cells compared with monotherapy.

8. Conclusions and future outlook

Photonic functional MOFs, as we have outlined in this review, have attracted tremendous attention due to their great advantages of flexible design, high porosities, tunable pores, and diverse functional sites. In fact, they are now active in luminescence sensing, white-light emission, photocatalysis, nonlinear optics, lasing devices, data storage, and biomedicine. In these examples, the photonic units of MOFs play an important and active role in generating, transporting, and storing photons or charge. Considering the vast combination possibilities, synergistic effects, as well as controllable and ordered arrangements of MPUs, we believe that there will be a brighter future for MOFs as photonic functional materials. However, there are still several interesting or important fields that need to be explored.

The rational design and synthesis of isoreticular MOFs with predictable structures/topologies and porosities is very important to systematically tune the photonic properties. A number of isoreticular MOFs have been realized by using M2(COO)4 (M = Zn2+, Cu2+, Ni2+, and Co2+) and Zn4O(COO)6 as SBUs, but it is still a significant challenge to synthesize isoreticular lanthanide MOFs with controllable and predictable structures due to the high coordination flexibility and the lack of preferential directionality of lanthanide ions. The molecular building block (MBB) approach recently proposed by Eddaoudi et al. is one of the breakthroughs in the construction of isoreticular lanthanide MOFs.34,93,97,348â€“350 This approach indicates that some polynuclear lanthanide clusters can be utilized as SBUs to construct a family of isoreticular lanthanide MOFs with tunable pore sizes and thus may assist chemists and material scientists to rationally develop lanthanide MOFs with desired photonic functionalities.

The photonic performance of MOF materials depends not only on their framework structures, but also on the synergistic and cooperative interactions among different photonic units such as metal nodes, organic linkers and guest molecules. For this reason, systematic investigations on the relationship among fabrication strategy, structural modulation, synergistic effects and the corresponding photonic properties of final products will be necessary and will speed up the development of photonic functional MOFs.

The research on luminescent MOF sensors is a relatively young field and still falls far behind that of their gas storage and separation applications. In MOF sensors, luminescence signals arisen from lanthanide ions, organic linkers, and charge transfer processes have been widely utilized to demonstrate the detection of small molecules, ionic species, VOCs, explosives, temperature, pH value, and so on. However, most of them are fulfilled based on the intensity change of one transition. This may lead to erroneous conclusions because the luminescence intensity can be heavily affected by the quantity of MOFs, excitation power, and the drifts of the optoelectronic system. To solve this problem, the ratiometric sensing approach based on dual-emitting MOFs has been established and thus opened a new strategy for high-accuracy luminescent sensors. In addition, developing a new multi-dimensional sensing strategy using intensity, lifetime, wavelength and polarization may be an appealing future direction to enhance the sensing accuracy.

The development of MOF-based photonic biosensors capable of intracellular sensing deserves much more attention, which will yield significant advancements in biomedical diagnostic and therapeutic science. Further efforts should be focused on the preparation of biologically compatible nano-MOFs with controllable size and morphology and the integration of different functionalities such as molecular targeting, luminescence sensing, cellular imaging, and so on. This ongoing research requires joint effort from different disciplines such as chemistry, materials science, photophysics, biology, and biomedicine. It is thus anticipated that the close collaborations will eventually lead to some useful photonic MOFs for commercial applications in the near future.
