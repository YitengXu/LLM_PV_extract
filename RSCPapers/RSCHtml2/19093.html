Abstract

Metal halide perovskite materials have demonstrated unique properties for applications in optoelectrical devices such as solar cells, light-emitting diodes and photodetectors. The unprecedented world-record energy conversion efficiency of over 25% achieved with solution-processed perovskite solar cells is escalating research towards the commercialisation of the perovskite materials. Nevertheless, the use of large amounts of hazardous and carcinogenic solvents such as N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP), and skin-penetrating dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) in the current perovskite synthesis protocols are limited for the commercial uptake of perovskite technology as these detrimental solvents can cause environmental, health and safety issues. Therefore, the development of alternative environmentally-friendly, green solvents for the synthesis of perovskite materials including thin films, nanocrystals or single-crystals with desirable properties is critical for the commercialisation of perovskite technologies. In this review, we summarize the research progress of solution-processable perovskite material using low-toxic and green solvent alternatives. We discuss the fundamental properties that need to be considered in screening solvents for the solution processing of metal halide perovskites. By carefully analysing the common features of solvents and anti-solvent systems that have been reported for making perovskite precursor inks for applications in solar cells and light-emitting diodes, we provide insight into the relationship between the solvent molecular ligand properties of perovskite materials device performance. We discuss the advantages and challenges of each type of green solvent based on their performance in metal halide perovskite technology, effects on the environment, human health and safety, aiming to provide guidelines towards green metal halide perovskite technology.

1. Introduction

Semiconductor materials based on metal halide perovskites, which can be described by the general chemical formula of ABX3 (A = methylammonium (MA+), formamidinium (FA+), Cs+; B = Pb2+, Sn2+; X = Cl, Br, I) have attracted significant attention in the scientific and industrial communities due to their exceptional performance in solar cells and other optoelectrical devices such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs). Solar cells that use metal halide perovskites as the light-absorbing materials have demonstrated very competitive power conversion efficiencies (PCE), with the current world record efficiency of over 25% being achieved with low-cost solution processing methods, making it one of the most attractive photovoltaic technologies that can deliver cost-effective solar electricity in the future.1 Solution-processed perovskite-based LEDs with tuneable colour have also achieved remarkable efficiencies. The recorded external quantum efficiency of 21.6% was reported for near-infrared perovskite LEDs, and 23.4% was reported for green perovskite LEDs.2,3 These breakthroughs are attributed to the unique physical and chemical properties of the perovskite materials, including strong light absorption over a broad solar spectrum, long charge carrier life-times,4 high charge carrier mobility and radiative efficiencies,5 low exciton binding energy,6,7 and high tolerance to defects, to name a few.8,9 High-quality perovskite materials in the form of thin films or nanocrystals made by cost-effective solution processing involves dissolving a high concentration (e.g., >0.5 M) of solid perovskite precursor such as lead halides (PbX2: X = I, Br, Cl) and AX (A = MA+, FA+, Cs+; X = I, Br, Cl) in a suitable solvent, which is then used to deposit perovskite films with controlled thickness by spin coating or other printing technology.10 The solvent(s) is then removed by heating the perovskite precursor films at mild temperature (up to 350 C). The hazardous solvents used in the current protocol for the solution-processing of high-quality perovskite thin films can cause undesirable effects on human and environmental health, such as fertility impairment by N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), skin-penetration by dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), or carcinogenic N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP), chlorobenzene (CB) and toluene (TL).11,12 These health and safety concerns associated with exposure to these solvents in the solution processing of perovskite materials may restrict the commercialisation of perovskite-based technologies.

Other deposition techniques like thermal evaporation can also be used to make perovskite films without using hazardous solvents. For example, thermal evaporation of perovskite precursors such as PbI2 and MAI have been developed to fabricate perovskite films. However, the performance of the device is generally lower as compared to solution-based devices.1316 Considering the simplicity and cost-effectiveness of solution processing, it is anticipated that the large scale production of perovskite film-based devices will be mainly done by solution deposition techniques like slot-die coating or roll-to-roll printing, which are practically and economically more attractive. Although vacuum-based fabrication procedures such as thermal evaporation have also been reported for perovskite film fabrication, the shortcomings of vacuum-based deposition method are the large energy consumption, and the size of the device is restricted by the vacuum deposition chamber. These drawbacks make them less attractive, thus less likely to be the main method for the commercialization of perovskite-based technology. Therefore, the development of solution processing routes that use environmentally benign, biocompatible solvent alternatives with minimal health and safety concerns is of high importance for the large-scale production of perovskite materials for various applications. Recently, alternative less toxic solvents or solvent systems for solution processing perovskite materials have been explored. Understanding the chemistry of the perovskite precursor solution, in particular the correlation of the molecule structure of the solvent with the corresponding properties of perovskite thin films or crystals is critical for the development of green solution processing of perovskite technology. The use of lead is another environmental concern in metal halide perovskite technology. Research on the development of lead-free perovskite materials has been well reviewed in the literature.1720 Since it seems the presence of lead is essential for making high-performance perovskite-based optoelectrical devices including solar cells and LEDs, this review focuses on the research progress in the use of green solvents for the synthesis of metal halide perovskites (MHP) by solution processing technology. It should be noted that the deposition of charge transport layers in perovskite-based optoelectronic devices can also involve solvents and some of the solvent could be hazardous, depending on the materials. Due to the unique properties of the perovskite materials, they have also demonstrated promising performance in many other areas beyond optoelectronic devices such as energy storage devices, electrocatalytic water splitting, etc., where charge transport materials may or may not be involved. Since the perovskite material is the core component in all these applications, this review only focuses on perovskite synthesis by solution processing, in particular, the development of environmentally friendly solvents for use in solution-processed perovskite fabrication. We first discuss the general material properties and synthesis protocol of MHP. This is followed by the discussion of the fundamental properties of solvents associated with their use in solution processing MHP material synthesis and the development of solubility theory for solvent screening for MHPs. We then provide a detailed summary and discussion of green solvents or solvent systems that have been used to make precursor solutions for perovskite materials, or used as anti-solvents to enhance the morphology and crystallisation of perovskite films. The green solvents or solvent systems for solution processing perovskite materials are classified based on the characteristic chemical ligands in the solvent molecule. The relationship of the molecular structure of solvents with their solvation capability for solid perovskite precursors including lead halides and halide salts AX (A = MA, FA, Cs), and the effect of the characteristic chemical ligands of an anti-solvent molecules on the nucleation, grain growth and crystallisation of perovskite films are discussed. Finally, we remark on the challenges and strategies for creating the safe and sustainable fabrication of metal halide perovskite technology.

2. Metal halide perovskite material properties and synthesis protocol

As mentioned above, metal halide perovskites (MHP) have a general chemical formula, ABX3, which usually adopts a stable cubic crystal structure. As shown in Fig. 1a, A is a monovalent cation that occupies the centre void of a cubic framework formed by the [BX6]4 species. A can be an organic component such as methylammonium (MA+), formamidinium (FA+), or inorganic such as cesium (Cs+); B is a divalent metal cation, e.g. lead or tin and X is a mono-anion such as a halide or a mixture of halides (iodide, bromide or chloride). In addition to the cubic 3D structure, low-dimensional perovskite-type materials with large A-site cations (e.g. butylammonium, BA+) have been reported, in which the metal halide octahedra form edge-, face-, and/or corner-sharing networks leading to 2D layered perovskites, 1D chains or 0D isolated octahedra (Fig. 1a).21 These low-dimensional perovskites often exhibit larger exciton binding energies due to strong quantum confinement and site isolation, which is more favourable for applications requiring exciton radiative recombination such as light-emitting diodes and pumped lasers.2123 MHP in the form of thin films, nanocrystals (NCs) or single-crystals have been made for different applications (Fig. 1bd). Regardless of the material composition, the most widely used method for the synthesis of the MHP is based on solution processing. To make MHP thin films, spin coating or printing techniques are often used to deposit perovskite precursor solutions containing PbX2 (X = I, Br, Cl) and MA (FA, Cs)X in a polar aprotic coordinating organic solvent or solvent mixture, usually DMF and DMSO, on a suitable substrate. In order to enhance the grain morphology of the perovskite thin films with large grain sizes and uniform coverage, anti-solvent treatment is used as an additional step during solution processing. The anti-solvent is usually an organic (non-polar) solvent such as diethyl ether (DE) or chlorobenzene (CB), which does not dissolve the perovskite.24 These organic solvents evaporate during subsequent film heating to form the perovskite compound. The detrimental effect of the solvent vapour not only causes air pollution, but exposure to the vapour contaminated atmosphere also causes health issues for the operators and other people in the workplace.2527 As evaluated by the life-cycle assessment of solution-processed perovskite photovoltaic modules, the direct emission of organic solvents during the manufacturing of perovskite solar cells (PSCs) contributes up to 85% of photo chemically generated summer smog by the whole module.28 Regarding the health effects of these solvents, the use of DMF in industry is restricted for women due to its impact on the reproduction of women while DE and CB are classified as highly hazardous in the pharmaceutical industry due to their toxic effects on the human brain, liver and kidneys.25 High exposure to DE and CB can cause unconsciousness and even death.29,30

In the solution processing of perovskite materials, the solvent plays an important role, controlling the morphology, crystallinity and phase purity of the perovskite films, and therefore their performance in PSCs and other optoelectronic devices. A high concentration of PbX2 in perovskite precursors (usually 0.52 M or 230922 mg ml1 for PbI2, for instance) is required to deposit a reasonably thick film for efficient light absorption in PV applications. The most widely used deposition methods for the solution processing of perovskites are based on spin coating for small-sized PSCs (<1 cm2). For large-sized devices that are needed in practice, coating technologies such as slot-die coating,34 inkjet printing,35 spray coating,36 as well as roll-to-roll printing,37 have been explored to make the MHP films. As depicted in Fig. 2, large amounts of noxious solvent(s) such as DMF, DMSO, NMP or dimethylacetamide (DMAc) are used to dissolve PbX2 and AX to make perovskite precursor solutions in all these solution processing fabrication methods.3842

3. Solution chemistry in perovskite precursor inks

Understanding the nature of the species present in the perovskite precursor solution is crucial for obtaining precise control over the nucleation and crystallization of perovskite grains and their film morphologies, which ultimately impact the device performance. As described above, the main components in perovskite inks are the salts of divalent Pb or Sn metals that exhibit a high propensity to form tri-halide anions (MX3; M = Sn(II), Pb(II); X = halide). The divalent lead cation (Pb2+) is prone to undergo various complexations with O-, N-, or S-donor ligand of solvents, as well as with halide ions.43 Despite having an electron configuration of [Xe] 4f14 5d10 6s2 with fully occupied d-orbitals and thus no dd transitions, they exhibit intraatomic and charge transfer transitions, making the probing of Pb(II) chemistry accessible by spectroscopic methods. Additionally, the 6s2 electron lone pair and strong spinorbit coupling result in various interesting and useful electronic and structural effects manifested in the unique optoelectronic properties of hybrid perovskites.44 Recently, lead chemistry in the perovskite inks was investigated by spectroscopic techniques such as UV-vis absorption,45 photoluminescence46 and Raman spectroscopy47 to gain more insight into metalsolvent interactions. Studies on aqueous lead iodide solutions with the addition of iodide salt go back to the 1920s when it was observed that the addition of iodides facilitated the solubility of PbI2.48 Lanford and Kiehl presumed two possible equilibria involving the formation of the complex anions PbI3 and PbI42 when PbI2 and KI were dissolved in water.49 These complexes were investigated by Horvath and Miko in acetonitrile (ACN) solution via absorption spectroscopy showing characteristic ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) transitions at 366 and 408 nm, respectively. Furthermore, photolysis experiments indicated that under irradiation, the triiodide anion I3 was formed as the predominant species in the presence of oxygen.50 These findings are very important, considering the solution aging effects of the perovskite inks on the resulting thin film quality. In the context of hybrid perovskite research, Kamat and co-workers have reported on the absorption spectra of non-stoichiometric MAPbI3 DMF solutions, confirming the early reported iodoplumbate equilibria:

4. Criteria for suitable solvents in the solution processing of perovskites

In the first report of MHP for photovoltaic application, Kojima et al. employed DMF and GBL as solvents to prepare MAPbBr3 and MAPbI3 perovskite precursors59 by taking advantage of the strong dissolving capability of DMF for solid PbI2 and PbBr2. A study by Jung et al. using single-crystal X-ray diffraction and Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy confirmed that DMF molecules coordinate with PbI2 or MAIPbI2 to form intermediate complexes (PbI2DMF and MAIPbI2DMF).60 The intermediates are transformed into perovskite under the subsequent heating of the precursor films. The intermediates also play an important role in the Ostwald-ripening process for the grain growth of the perovskite films.61 However, perovskite films formed from neat DMF often show poor morphologies with many pinholes and high roughness, which is caused by undesirable nucleation and grain growth.62 Fundamentally, the nucleation and grain growth of perovskites are driven by the supersaturation of nuclei in a solution system when the solvents are either removed, or by the addition of an anti-solvent. This process is described by the LaMer mechanism for the formation of classic colloids.60 As mentioned before, previous research has revealed that the nature of the perovskite precursor is a colloidal dispersion in the mother solution, with particle sizes up to the mesoscale, rather than a real solution. The colloid consists of soft coordination complexes in the form of lead polyhalide frameworks such as[PbI3]1, [PbI4]2, [PbI5]3, and [PbI6]4.58 Thus, controlling the colloidal characteristics is critical to achieving high-quality perovskite films with optimal morphology and crystallinity.63,64

In PV applications, a good solvent should be able to dissolve high concentrations of PbX2 (>0.5 M) in order to make perovskite films with sufficient thickness (>500 nm) for adequate light-harvesting. Although the organic halide component of the MHP dissolves in most solvents, finding a solvent that can dissolve the metal halide component is quite challenging. In the early stage of MHP research, finding suitable solvents for solid perovskite precursors largely relied on an experimental trial-and-error method. Solvents such as DMF, DMSO, NMP and DMAc were found to have a good capacity to dissolve solid PbX2 and MAX in the preparation of perovskite precursor solutions.39 Certain solvents such as GBL poorly dissolve solid PbI2 alone; however, PbI2 and MAI can be dissolved well together in GBL. This is attributed to the formation of a MAIPbI2GBL complex.65

Solvent properties like the dielectric constant, dipole moment, atomic charge, volume, and mass influence the solubility of PbX2. The Hansen solubility parameters model (HSPM), which was developed by Charles M. Hansen to predicate the solubility of one material in another, has been used to predict the dissolving ability of a solvent toward solid PbX2.66 HSPM considers three parameters including (i) dispersion (d), (ii) intermolecular dipole interactions (p), and (iii) hydrogen bonding (h) between a solute and a solvent molecule. In the area of MHP synthesis, Gardner et al. firstly used HSPM in their search for possible non-hazardous solvents for the solution processing of perovskite.67 They identified a solvent and a solvent-mixture system consisting of GBL and alcohol-based protic solvents such as ethanol, 1-propanol and acetic acid. The three calculated Hansen solubility parameters, matrix (dispersion (d), polarity (p), and hydrogen bonding (h)) of the solvent or solvent systems for dissolving solid lead acetate/PbCl2/MAI with at least 1 M PbX2 and 3 M MAI are shown in Fig. 7a. Most of the protic solvents (ethanol, 1-propanol and acetic acid) could be mixed with GBL up to 40% volume while still maintaining a clear perovskite solution. Above this concentration, colloidal separation and precipitates were formed (Fig. 7b). Similarly, Babaei et al. also applied the Hansen solubility theory in their exploration of suitable nonhazardous solvents to dissolve solid PbBr2 and PbI2. Among the 63 nonhazardous solvents investigated, they found that a commercial product, PolarClean, which is a diester blend polar solvent (with methyl ester concentration >70%) with a high boiling point temperature (280 C), was able to dissolve 40 mg ml1 PbI2 at 60 C. The formation of the MAPbI3 perovskite film made by a two-step spin coating of PolarClean perovskite solution was claimed based on the optical bandgap measurement by UV-visible spectroscopy.68 Nevertheless, other properties of the perovskite film such as morphology, composition and its performance in solar cells were not reported in this work, which makes it hard to assess the usefulness of this solvent in practice.

Nevertheless, the usefulness of HSPM in solution processing MHP synthesis is limited because the theory does not consider the important ionic interactions of salts and the dative bonding of the solvent, which occurs in precursor solutions of perovskites. Therefore, HSPM is not adequate to fully explain and predict the solubility of PbX2 and MAX in a solvent. For example, GBL is considered to be able to dissolve lead halides according to the Hansen model but experimentally, the solubility of PbI2 and PbCl2 in GBL is very low (less than 4 mg mL1).65 To address this fundamental flaw, modified Hansen solubility theories have been developed to understand the solubility behaviour of PbX2 and MAX salts in solvents. Huang et al. proposed a concept that combines the Hansen solubility parameters and Gutmann's donor numbers of the solute and solvent molecules to screen less toxic solvents for the solution processing of MAPbI3 materials.69 Gutmann's donor number, which measures the tendency to donate electron pairs to the acceptor has been proven to be a good metric showing the coordinating ability of the solvent with the lead halide precursor.56,70 Based on this consideration, Huang et al. calculated the Hansen distance (Ra) and donor number of each solvent. The donor number is used to quantify the possibility of forming complexes between the solute and the solvent (adduct), which is not considered in conventional HSPM. By using this modified theory, the authors have found that the solubility of PbI2 and MAI in a solvent is possibly determined by both the Hansen distance and the donor number. As shown in Fig. 7c, the dependence of the solubility of PbI2 on the donor number and Hansen distance can be separated into three distinct regions: In region 1, solvents with low Hansen distance (Ra < 6) can dissolve perovskite materials well, regardless of the donor number. In region 2, solvents with a higher Hansen distance (Ra > 6) and larger donor number (n > 30) can acceptably dissolve perovskite precursors, while solvents in region 3 cannot dissolve PbI2 and MAI due to their large Hansen distance (Ra > 6) and smaller donor number (n < 30). A solvent with a low Hansen distance is critical for its dissolving ability toward solid PbI2 and MAI, while strong coordination between the solvent molecule and solute can compensate for the relatively poor dissolving ability of solvent with moderate Hansen distance.69

Stevenson et al. proposed a different way to enhance the applicability of HSPM in explaining and predicting the solubility of solid PbX2 in a solvent. They showed that the Mayer bond order of a solvent's polar atom, such as N or O, is a more reliable parameter for screening solvents for the solution processing of perovskites than the relative polarity in the Hansen model.71 The Mayer bond order quantifies the electronic state of a solvent's most electronegative atoms such as N or O, which is directly correlated with the Pb2+-solvent complexation in the perovskite precursor. Using this model, the authors carried out ab initio calculations of enthalpy for forming Pb2+: solvent complexes (Hsolv:Pb2+) of different solvents. The results showed that the solubility of PbX2 with different halides follows the trend of PbIn > PbBrn > PbCln. Among the different solvents investigated (DMSO, DMF, NMP, acetone (ACE), GBL, ACN, methacrolein (METH)), the enthalpy for the complexation (Hsolv:Pb2+) follows the order of DMSO < DMF < NMP < ACE = GBL < METH < CAN (Fig. 7d), which is consistent with the trend of the Mayer bond order of the solvent. The solubility of PbX2 in these different solvents was also in agreement with the experimental results.71 These results showed that the Mayer bond order may be a useful tool for solvent screening for the solution processing of perovskite films. It is worth noting that the effects of organic halides on the solubility of PbX2 in the solvent were not considered in this model. Thus, there is still an urgent need to develop a new theory that can accurately predict the solubility and reaction of solid perovskite precursors in the solvents under different circumstances to unravel the underlying mechanism of the solution chemistry.

In the following, we demonstrate the research on the less toxic solvents or solvent systems that have been reported in the literature for perovskite synthesis by the solution processing method.

3.1 Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)

DMSO is a polar aprotic solvent that has been frequently used as a solvent in organic and biochemical material synthesis. The median lethal dose of DMSO is higher as compared to ethanol, and some even define DMSO as a non-toxic solvent. DMSO has been widely used in medical treatments due to its ability to increase the rate of absorption of some compounds by biological tissues.72 DMSO has a strong dissolving ability for PbX2. The experimental solubility of PbX2 in pure DMSO is 600 mg mL1 for PbI2, 560 mg mL1 for PbBr2 and 310 mg mL1 for PbCl2.71 Wu et al. compared the performance of PSC based on MAPbI3, which was made by using a DMSO-based precursor solution with a DMF counterpart. They found that similar to DMF, DMSO reacted with the PbI2 and MAI to form an intermediate phase of PbI2DMSOMAI, which slowed the crystallization process of the perovskites. Benefiting from the retarded crystallization, the MAPbI3 film derived from the DMSO-based precursor solution exhibited enhanced morphology with a smoother surface and uniform particle size distribution as compared to films deposited from a DMF-based precursor. As a result, the corresponding PSC delivered an average power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 12.5%, while the DMF-based devices showed only 9.7% on average.73 In contrast, Wang et al.65 showed that perovskite films made from a DMSO-based precursor solution had incomplete surface coverage due to the strongly retarded crystallization of perovskite in DMSO, which is presumably due to the difficulty of removing DMSO solvent, which has a high boiling point (189 C). To address this issue, they added 2-methylpyrazine as a co-solvent to accelerate the perovskite film drying process. This solvent quenching strategy favours the nucleation of perovskite particles, contributing to the formation of denser perovskite films. Consequently, the PCE of PSC was enhanced from 10.0% for the DMSO-only solvent to 16.2% for the methylpyrazineDMSO co-solvent system.65 Inspired by this finding, researchers have explored other co-solvents for use with DMSO in the solution processing of perovskite films. For example, GBL was used as a co-solvent to control the formation kinetics of MAPbI3 perovskite films. The PSCs with decent photovoltaic performance (PCE up to 18%) were made by using the doctor blading deposition of a perovskite solution made with a DMSOGBL solvent mixture.69,74,75 This phenomenon was explained by the Hansen distance and donor number of the solvent/solvent system. As can be seen in Fig. 7c, DMSO has a high donor number and exhibits strong interaction with solid PbX2 perovskite precursors to form adducts, while GBL has a lower donor number, which helps to form less-stable complexes and improves the evaporation kinetics. In the co-solvent system, the ratio of solvents in the solvent mixture governs the surface tension of the precursor solution, convection, and evaporation rates of the solvent, which in turn affect the crystallisation, grain growth, and thus the quality of the perovskite thin films. Starch-based biopolymers were used as a rheological modifier for DMSO-based perovskite precursor solutions. The interaction between the OH groups of the starch and the perovskite-DMSO complex led to starch gelatinization, which affects the overall viscosity of the perovskite precursor. Using this gel-like perovskite-starch composite, Giuri et al. fabricated MAPbI3-based perovskite solar cells with PCE of 17.2% and enhanced stability.76 However, the shortcoming of DMSO is that it can easily penetrate the skin, and substances dissolved in DMSO can quickly be absorbed by human tissue, causing health and safety issues. In the case of solution processing perovskite material synthesis, heavy-metal lead and small molecular amines are hazardous to human health. Thus, precaution is needed when handling DMSO-based perovskite solution to avoid direct contact with skin.

3.2 Gamma-butyrolactone (GBL)

GBL is considered to be a less toxic solvent than DMF. GBL has been widely used as a solvent in commercial products such as paint strippers, nail polish removers, stain removers and circuit board cleaners, etc. However, GBL has the potential for drug abuse and is classified as a drug in many countries.77 In the early development of PSC technology, GBL was used extensively as a solvent for perovskite precursors7881 and PSCs using pure GBL often showed poor performance due to poor film morphology. It has been found that compared to DMSO or DMF, GBL exhibits relatively weak interactions with lead ions (Pb2+) due to its weaker binding strength. Without the presence of MAI or other ionic counterparts, the GBL molecule has very low solvation capacity for solid PbI2. Experimentally, it has been shown that the solubility of PbI2 in pure GBL is less than 4 mg mL1 and for PbCl2, the solubility is even lower (<2 mg mL1).71 In the presence of MAI, the solubility of PbX2 in GBL was enhanced by the adduct formation. Adducts of (MA)2(GBL)2Pb3I8 and ordered/semi-ordered (MA)8(GBL)x[Pb18I44] clusters have been confirmed in GBL-based perovskite solution, which are believed to be responsible for the resultant undesirable film quality.47 Fateev et al. found that these undesired adducts could be avoided by adding excessive amounts of iodide ions (MAI or KI) to the precursor, which leads to the complete dissociation of these lead oligomer complexes into simple ions like PbI3 and PbI42. By using excessive MAI, the authors fabricated MAPbI3 perovskite films with homogeneous surface coverage and high crystallinity from neat GBL solvent.47

Generally speaking, due to its low capacity to dissolve solid PbX2, GBL is often introduced into other solvents such as DMSO to act as a co-solvent, controlling the solvent evaporation rate and crystallization of perovskite material to enhance the film quality.69,74,75,82 Gardner et al. showed that by using lead acetate with a small amount of PbCl2 as the lead source, GBL could dissolve a high concentration (>1 M) of the lead salts in the presence of MAI (PbCl2:PbAc2:MAI = 0.2:0.8:3.0, molar ratio). Nevertheless, poor surface coverage and large pin holes appeared in the perovskite film. Under the guidance of the Hansen solubility model, the authors found that several alcohols could mix well with GBL up to 40% (v/v) without sacrificing the solubility of PbX2-MAI. A solvent mixture composed of GBL/ethanol/acetic acid = 60/20/20 (v/v/v) produced the best perovskite film without pinholes. The performance of the corresponding PSC (13.5%) was similar to the device made by DMF-based precursor (13.6%). For small cells (0.13 cm2), the PCE of 14.5% was achieved using this solvent system.67 Similarly, Cao et al. reported a solvent mixture of GBL with polyethylene glycol (PEG) for dissolving PbBr2 to make CsPbBr3-based PSCs by a two-step sequential deposition. The addition of GBL was found to help adjust the high viscosity of the PEG. The resulting inorganic CsPbBr3 perovskite film showed a remarkably smooth morphology, which led to PSCs with a PCE of 8.1%.83

3.3 Acetonitrile (ACN)

ACN is a strong polar aprotic solvent that has a dipole moment comparable to DMF and DMSO. Compared to DMF, it has modest toxicity and is used in industrial products such as perfumes, pharmaceuticals, batteries and pesticides, etc. Nevertheless, ACN has a very poor dissolving ability for PbI2 (<0.1 M).84 DFT-based calculations showed that the chemical binding in the complex formed between ACN and Pb2+ is weak.71 To overcome this issue, Noel et al. used methylamine gas to enhance the solvating power of ACN for MAPbI3 precursor salts.85 As shown in Fig. 8a, the precursor containing MAI:PbI2 (1:1.06 molar ratio) is completely insoluble in neat ACN. Upon treatment with methylamine gas, the precipitate dissolved completely, forming a pale-yellow solution. The ACN/methylamine solvent could dissolve up to 0.5 M of MAPbI3 perovskite. This is ascribed to the formation of the MAPbI3-xMA complex, which is miscible with ACN. The MAPbI3-xMA complex is formed by the uptake of amine molecules into MAPbI3 compound due to the coordination of the N atom in the methylamine with the PbI6-octahedra in the layered PbI2 structure. This interaction leads to a complete collapse of the solid perovskite phase (Fig. 8b and c).86,87 Pinhole-free, smooth MAPbI3 films were obtained upon evaporation of the solvent (Fig. 8d). These films consist of densely packed crystal grains and a smooth surface with a roughness of only 7.8 nm over a large area. Furthermore, this MA-assisted method also provides a healing effect to the perovskite films, which improves the overall film quality,87 significantly improving the charge-carrier lifetime of the perovskite films. As a consequence, the performance of the as-fabricated film evaluated in PSCs with both n-i-p and p-i-n architecture delivered a maximum PCE up to 19.0% (for n-i-p) and 16.8% (for p-i-n), respectively.85 Other amine gases, including propylamine, butylamine and pentylamine, can also be used to assist with dissolving perovskite precursors in ACN. However, a pure MAPbI3 film cannot be fabricated by using these large organic amines. Instead, these large amines possibly lead to the formation of two-dimensional RuddlesdenPopper-based perovskite compounds.

In another study, Noel et al. used the ACN/methylamine solvent system to deposit smooth, highly crystalline films of MAPbBr3. In their work, MAPbBr3 material was firstly dispersed into ACN solvent. After this, methylamine gas was purged into the solution until saturation, which resulted in a stable, clear precursor solution.88 The deposited MAPbBr3 thin films from the ACN/methylamine solvent system possessed much-improved surface morphologies with increased texturing and grain orientation as compared to the film deposited from neat DMF. With choline chloride additives, the film achieved photoluminescence quantum efficiencies of up to 5.5% and charge-carrier mobilities of 17.8 cm2 (Vs)1. The large band gap of MAPbBr3 gave a PCE of up to 8.9% in PSC. The interaction between amines, PbI2 and the formed complex, as well as the overarching influences on the material solubility, were discussed by Kerner et al.89,90 Burkitt et al. added HCl to the ACN/methylamine solvent system in their preparation of the perovskite precursor for the fabrication of MAPbI3 film by slot-die coating. They found that the solution was very suitable for slot-die coating at high line speeds. Thanks to the high evaporation rate of ACN/methylamine, the high-speed slot-die coating could produce PSCs with much-enhanced performance as compared to the spin coating deposition method that is widely used in the laboratory for the fabrication of small-sized devices.91,92

The methylamine-assisted ACN solvent system has also been used for the synthesis of cubic MAPbI3 nanocrystals (NCs) through a ligand-assisted reprecipitation method. The MAPbI3 nanocrystals showed photoluminescence quantum yields exceeding 93%.93 In contrast to coordinating solvents like DMF or DMSO, non-coordinated solvents, like ACN, do not bond to PbI2, leading to the formation of defect-free MAPbI3 nanocrystals upon crystallization, a property that is highly desirable in optoelectronic devices such as light-emitting diodes.94 An issue that needs to be considered for ACN/methylamine solvent systems is that the ACN solvent possesses a very low viscosity (0.25 cP) and a low boiling temperature point (80 C), resulting in very fast crystallization of the perovskite during spin coating deposition. It is known that the retardation of perovskite crystallization often helps to form better perovskite crystal and film quality with fewer defects, pinholes and surface roughness. To tackle this issue, Deng et al. added 2-methoxyethanol (2-ME) to ACN to prepare precursors for MAPbI3 perovskite films deposited by blade coating.84 Due to the very weak coordination capability and high volatility of these solvents, the fabricated perovskite film had a low crystallinity and poor contact with the substrates. An optimized ratio mixture of 2-ME and ACN (2:3; v/v) can deliver a better perovskite film with compact and smooth morphology but the grain size was small (only several hundred nanometers) and the physical contact with the underlying poly(bis(4-phenyl) (2,4,6-trimethylphenyl) amine (PTAA) was poor. The authors added a small amount of DMSO to slow the crystallization, which consequently led to perovskite films with high crystallinity, large grain sizes (up to 12 m) and improved contact with the substrate. As a result, the PSC fabricated at room temperature delivered a PCE of 21.3% for small areas and 16.4% for a cell with an aperture area of 63.7 cm2.84 Nevertheless, there are practical challenges for employing the ACN/methylamine solvent system in commercial applications as ACN has a high vapor pressure and methylamine gas is extremely flammable, which can make it difficult to control the reaction conditions along with health and safety issues.

3.4 Room temperature molten salts ionic liquids

In the search for green solvents for perovskite solution processing, ionic liquids (ILs) have recently attracted the attention of researchers. Ionic liquids, which are also called room temperature molten salts, are arguably considered green solvents for many industrial applications such as organic synthesis, batteries, and solar thermal energy. Compared to conventional solvents, ILs have many advantages, including a non-volatile nature at room temperature or below, non-flammable, high thermal stability, strong dissolving ability for various organic and inorganic compounds. Besides being used as additives to control the crystallization of perovskites,9598 ILs have also been successfully demonstrated as promising solvents for the preparation of perovskite precursors. Moore et al. first showed that methylammonium formate (MAF) could be used as a solvent medium to grow MAPbI3 perovskite. By controlling the reaction time and temperature, a single-phase perovskite was made. However, the morphology of the film was poor with a high surface roughness as compared to other solvents.99 In addition, MAF is unstable and can easily go through condensation reactions with the methylammonium cations in the perovskite precursor to form DMF and water.100 Our recent reports with freshly prepared methylammonium carboxylate protic ionic liquids (e.g., methylammonium formate (MAF), methylammonium acetate (MAAc) and methylammonium propionate (MAP)) showed the in situ formation of perovskite by the addition of PbI2 to the ILs at room temperature, which substantially decreased the annealing temperature required for the formation of polycrystalline perovskite thin films without compromising the final film quality.100 In contrast to the classical aprotic ionic liquids, the protic ionic liquids (PILs) possess an acidic proton on the cation capable of forming hydrogen-bonded aggregates and network structures in solution.

PILs generally possess lower thermal stability, chemical stability, and non-negligible vapor pressure, which can be considered as advantages for the solution processing of hybrid perovskites, where the removal of residual solvents is a critical criterion for achieving high material purity. In order to verify and further investigate these findings, room-temperature PILs synthesized MAF, MAAc and MAP were tested either as pure solvents or in blends with regularly non-applicable green solvents such as water, alcohols, and acetonitrile (Fig. 9). All room-temperature synthesized PILs can dissolve the required high amounts of PbI2/PbBr2 and methylammonium halide. The high solubility of the perovskite precursors was maintained upon blending with the co-solvents. Therefore, these novel solvent systems fulfil the requirements outlined above for the green processing of perovskites. The obtained perovskite materials were phase-pure and showed preferential crystal orientation ((110) vs. (202)) dependent on the co-solvent (Fig. 10). Moreover, the binary and ternary blends of PILs with green solvents like water, ethyl alcohol and isopropyl alcohol can be utilized for hybrid perovskite processing by replacing high-boiling aprotic polar solvents, significantly expanding the range of applicable solvent candidates. Our preliminary studies show that the perovskite inks containing PILs with different anions show good compatibility with solution processing of perovskite, revealing the influence of the PIL anion on the solvation behavior of lead(II) halide precursors. By using a MAPDMSOacetonitrile solvent mixture, we successfully fabricated PSCs with PCE of 15.46% based on (MA0.15FA0.85)Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3. An ink system of the perovskite precursor was also developed for the large-scale fabrication of perovskite by ink-jet printing.100 The role of PILs in the formation and stabilization of perovskite crystals and, consequently, perovskite inks will require further study.

We further investigated the effects of anions in MA-based ILs with different carbon chains (methyl formate (MAF), methyl propionate (MAP), methyl butyrate (MAB), methyl valerate (MAV) and non-linear carbon chain methyl benzoate (MABe)) on the morphology and photoemission properties of the corresponding MAPbBr3 perovskite crystals (Fig. 10c). We found that the PILs with long carbon chains such as MAB benefited the photoemission of MAPbBr3 perovskite crystals. This is explained by the protection of perovskite nanocrystals from aggregation by the long alkyl chains of the ILs. Photoluminescence quantum efficiencies of up to 50% were achieved with MAB-based perovskite crystals (Fig. 10d). The alkyl chain was also found to influence the morphology of the perovskite crystals. Instead of a cubic shape formed by MAP, nanorods of MAPbBr3 were obtained when using MAB as the solvent (Fig. 10c).101 Similar PILs based on methylammonium acetate (MAAc) were used for the synthesis of inorganic perovskite CsPbBr3. It was reported that the addition of MAAc improved the solubility of solid CsBr and helped to produce high-quality perovskite films with reasonable thickness. The CsPbBr3 PSC made by this method showed a PCE of 7.37% and decent stability.102

To tackle the viscous nature of the PILs, Chao et al. developed an annealing-assisted solution process for the fabrication of perovskite film from MAAc precursors. By using a warm substrate during the spin coating process, they produced smooth and pinhole-free perovskite films. The authors also stated that the MAAc solvent could promote the rapid crystallization of perovskite and protect the perovskite from moisture. As a result, high-quality perovskite films were fabricated under ambient conditions (humidity ranged from 30% to 80%) and the corresponding MA-based perovskite solar cells showed power conversion efficiencies up to 20.05%.103 The MAAc ILs allowed the quick liquid-to-solid transition of perovskite through a deformed perovskite intermediate structure, which is different from the phase transformation involving complex intermediate in conventional polar solvents such as DMF and DMSO. Dynamic light scattering analysis showed that the MAAc-based perovskite precursor possessed a more uniform distribution of micellar and higher total colloid concentration as compared to the DMF-based counterparts. As can be seen in Fig. 11a, the micellar size in MAAc and DMF increased with increasing concentration. However, the size distribution was not uniform in the DMF solvent, while the micellar size in MAAc was centred in a narrower range, demonstrating the uniform distribution of the micelles. This was explained by the weak interactions between MAAc with PbI2 and MACl, thus hindering agglomeration. In contrast to the intercalation of DMF or DMSO into layered PbI2 crystals, MA+ and Ac ions tended to form MAPbI3xAcx alloys with PbI2, which further improved the stability of MAAc-based precursors in ambient conditions. Another advantage of using this PIL is that a reasonably thick perovskite film can be made from MAAc at low precursor solution concentrations (300400 mg mL1, half of the DMF solution), which helps to reduce the module price and material waste management in large-scale production.

Wang et al. applied MAAc ILs in the precursor solution for the fabrication of CsPbIxBr3x (0 x 3) inorganic perovskite solar cells.104 Differing from the assumption by Chao et al. about the weak interaction between MAAc with PbI2 in ref. 103, they found that compared to DMF and DMSO, MAAc induced different local coordination environments with Pb2+ due to the strong interactions between PbI2 and MAAc molecules, forming PbO bonds and NHI hydrogen bonds. These interactions stabilize the solution and suppress the oxidation of I in the perovskite precursor. The different working mechanisms of the solubility of PbI2 in DMF/DMSO and MAAc are depicted in Fig. 11b. The high solubility of solid PbI2 in DMF and DMSO molecules is related to the intercalation of these solvent molecules into the layered structures of PbI2, inducing the expansion of the PbI2 lattice. In contrast, the stronger interactions between MAAc ILs and PbI2 mean that each PbI2 is surrounded by the NHI hydrogen bonds and PbO bonds network, which weakens the PbI ionic bond, thus enhancing the solubility of PbI2 in the MAAc solvent. Because of this characteristic coordination environment, the phase transformation for forming perovskite compounds, in particular the inorganic perovskite in the MAAc system, is different from the DMF/DMSO system. According to Wang et al., the formation of CsPbI3 in the MAAc-based precursor solution experiences 4 different stages depending on the annealing temperature. In stage I (<80 C), an intermediate perovskite phase Cs1xMAxPbAcxI2xBr is formed. On increasing the temperature to 100120 C (stage II), MAAc slowly evaporates from the intermediate perovskite phase. More CsI and PbI2 can be incorporated into the lattice. At a higher annealing temperature of 120150 C (stage III), MAAc evaporates continuously, the final perovskite phase is formed, and the intermediate phases disappear. In stage IV (>150 C), MAAc is removed completely and the pure CsPbI3 inorganic perovskite phase is formed. This retarded crystallization greatly contributes to the formation of a smoother morphology of the perovskite film with reduced trap states. Consequently, the all-inorganic (CsPbI2.5Br0.5) wide band gap PSC with PCE up to 17.1% was achieved.104

In an extreme case, Petrov et al. developed a solvent-free preparation of MAPbI3 (and/or FAPbI3) perovskite films by using in situ formed polyiodide melting salt.105 By mixing MAI (and/or FAI) with iodine powder, they created a highly viscous polyiodide ionic liquid. The MAI3+x and FAI3+x polyiodides exhibited very high reactivity with metallic lead and PbI2. Even a 260 nm thick Pb film could be readily converted into perovskite film upon contact with the polyiodides ILs. The phase diagram of the ternary system MAIPbI2 is shown in Fig. 12. The Gibbs triangle demonstrates the compositional range that can form perovskites, which can be liquid, liquidsolid, or single-phase, through controlling the Pb/MA/I2 atomic ratio. The polyiodide was used to make perovskite films including -FAPbI3, MA-stabilized FAPbI3, MAxFA1xPbI3yBry and MAPbI3xBrx perovskites by simply reacting metallic lead or lead compound (PbI2 or PbO). The advantage of this method is that only high concentrations of solid perovskite precursors are used for perovskite formation, without the need for any other solvents, which enhances the material usage with minimum environmental contamination and health impact. If high energy conversion efficiency can be demonstrated, this method is attractive for large-scale production.84

3.5 Water

Water is the most sustainable, environmentally friendly solvent. It is generally thought that water is not suitable for the synthesis of perovskite materials due to the poor solubility of solid PbX2 in water and the decomposition of lead halide perovskites in water. Nevertheless, in contrast to the common thought, Liu et al. demonstrated that the presence of water in the perovskite precursor did not necessarily negatively impact the perovskite film preparation process. The study showed that the perovskite precursors (DMF and DMSO solvent) are homogeneous and clear with a water content of 25%. This is ascribed to the ability of water to prolong the film drying process, leading to the formation of perovskite films with large grain size and a reduced wettability when water is completely removed during film annealing.107 Hsieh et al. attempted to apply aqueous lead nitrate precursors in the sequential solution deposition of perovskite film. The study revealed that perovskite crystals could be formed in an aqueous Pb(NO3)2 solution via a two-step reaction: (i) the quick reaction of Pb(NO3) with MAI to form PbI2, and (ii) the slow transformation of PbI2 into MAPbI3 by reacting with MAI. The corresponding MAPbI3-based PSCs showed a PCE of 12.58%. The performance was a bit lower as compared to the DMF-based PSCs (14.38% PCE), which was ascribed to the poorer surface coverage of the perovskite film.108 Despite this, this work has led to encouraging true green solution processing for the synthesis of perovskite materials. 3.6 Other green solvents for the solution processing of perovskites

Besides the solvents for solution processing perovskite shown above, other low-toxic solvent systems that are much less studied have also been demonstrated for the synthesis of perovskite materials. Akkerman et al. demonstrated a CsPbBr3 nanocrystal (NC) ink by using a solvent mixture composed of isopropanol, hexane, and butylamine. At room temperature, the CsPbBr3 compound directly nucleated and grew in the form of nanocrystals with sizes of 1520 nm in the solvent mixture. Benefiting from the short propionate ligand for efficient charge transfer, the NCs can be directly applied in PSC fabrication protocols, which led to a device with a PCE of 5.4% and a very high open-circuit voltage (1.5 V). This was considered one of the best performing PSCs devices based on CsPbBr3 at the time.112

It is worth pointing out that the effect of the properties of the solvent on the formation of the perovskite precursor solution depends on the composition of the perovskites. For instance, owing to the greater Lewis acidity of Sn2+ than Pb2+, MASnI3 crystallized much more rapidly as compared to MAPbI3 in DMF.113 Although methyl acetate (MeAc) has a poor dissolving ability for lead halides or lead-based perovskites, it can be a green solvent for the fabrication of lead-free perovskites such as MA3Bi2I9 as demonstrated by Jain et al. By using a precursor containing MAI and BiI3 salt in MeAC, MA3Bi2I9 thin films with good crystallinity were obtained by the spin coating deposition method. The efficiency of the champion PSC device was 1.62%, which is remarkable for bismuth-based perovskites.114 Since propylene carbonates can readily dissolve bismuth salts, inorganic Cs3Bi2I9 NCs were synthesized by using propylene carbonates as the solvent via a sonication-assisted method in which a probe sonication was applied to a precursor of bismuth acetate, cesium iodide, and capping ligand in propylene carbonates, followed by the formation of Cs3Bi2I9 NCs directly in the solution. Despite a modest photoluminescence quantum efficiency (PLQY = 0.3%) demonstrated by the materials,115 these studies have provided alternative routes for the sustainable synthesis of perovskite materials.

4. Green anti-solvent used in perovskite material synthesis

4.1 Anti-solvents used in solution processing perovskite synthesis

Anti-solvents are solvents in which a compound (perovskite in this case) is much less soluble. As mentioned above, in solution processing perovskite synthesis, an anti-solvent treatment is commonly used to facilitate the nucleation of perovskite via creating an instantaneous local supersaturation on the substrate. It has become an important step to make dense, pinhole-free perovskite films. Non-polar solvents like chlorobenzene (CB), toluene (TL) or diethyl ether (DE) are commonly used to treat perovskite precursor films during spin coating to accelerate the heterogeneous nucleation. As firstly demonstrated in perovskite fabrication by Jeon et al., one role of the anti-solvent step is to remove excess DMSO residue on the perovskite substrate, leading to the rapid formation of a perovskite-DMSO intermediate phase, which is critical for the formation of extremely homogeneous final perovskite films.116 After this report, anti-solvent treatment has become a popular step in solution processing perovskite to fabricate perovskite films with desirable morphologies for high-performance PSCs.

The criteria for the selection of an anti-solvent are that (i) the anti-solvent should be able to wash away the excess solvent used in the perovskite precursor solution; (ii) the anti-solvent should not dissolve the perovskite or react with the perovskite or decompose it. It has been experimentally defined that solvents with polarity in the approximate range from 2.0 to 4.5 are suitable anti-solvents for solution processing perovskites.117 The polarities and boiling point temperatures of the commonly used solvents are shown in Fig. 14. As can be seen, besides chlorobenzene, diethyl ether and toluene, alcohol-based solvents in the light blue zone may also be suitable anti-solvents in perovskite synthesis.


Fig. 14 Diagraph of the boiling point versus polarity of commonly used solvents defining suitable solvents for the anti-solvent step. Reprinted with permission from Bu et al.117 Copyright Wiley-VCH GmbH 2017.

According to the precipitation law, crystal nucleation and growth of a material are determined by the supersaturation degree.118 The anti-solvent treatment step creates a heterogeneous supersaturation condition by removing excessive solvent on perovskite precursor films, forming a large density of nuclei.24 Lee et al. studied several common anti-solvents used in perovskite synthesis and they showed that TL was the most suitable for fabricating high quality MAPbI3 films because it drives the perovskite precursor into a metastable zone instead of a supersaturation zone. As shown in Fig. 15a, TL treatment induces a consistent rate of nucleation and grain growth, yielding uniform perovskite film consisting of large grain sizes. In comparison, other anti-solvents such as chlorobenzene (CB), chloroform (CF), dichlorobenzene (DCB), and isopropyl alcohol (IPA) generate high nucleation density, resulting in smaller grain sizes. Because the ability to remove or dilute perovskite precursor depends on the dielectric constant and dipole moment of the anti-solvent,39,116 the perovskite precursor film treated with anti-solvent can be driven into different zones including the under-supersaturation zone, metastable zone and supersaturation zone as depicted in Fig. 15b. When the perovskite precursor solution is treated with TL, the perovskite layer is transparent, indicating the existence of a non-stoichiometric intermediate phase in the metastable zone. In the case of CF, CB, or DCB, the perovskite precursor is pushed into the supersaturation state, generating a higher density of nuclei, resulting in perovskite film composed of smaller grains. The PSC using TL as the anti-solvent resulted in the highest PCE of 13.12%, surpassing other devices treated with CF, CB, DCB or IPA (Fig. 15c).119

For the triple cation perovskite Cs0.05(MA0.17FA0.83)0.95Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3, diethyl ether (DE) was found to bring the perovskite to the metastable phase, which contributed to a higher PSCs performance as compared to other anti-solvents.120 This indicates that the key role of an anti-solvent step is to bring the perovskite intermediate phase quickly into the metastable zone instead of the supersaturation zone, providing a steady control of the nucleation and crystallization rate to produce film with large grains. Depending on the composition of the perovskites and properties of the anti-solvent molecule, the experimental conditions in the anti-solvent treatment step such as anti-solvent dripping time and dripping volume need to be delicately controlled to make perovskite films with optimum morphology.119121 Practically, the currently most widely used anti-solvents like CB, TL are not favourable for industrial production due to their detrimental effects on both human health and the environment.11,29,122 Alternative green anti-solvents such as alcohols and acetates are preferable for the large-scale commercial production of perovskites. In the next sections, the alternative green anti-solvents that have been developed for solution processing MHP technologies are discussed. We group the solvents according to their chemical classification.

4.2 Alcohol-based green anti-solvents

Isopropanol alcohol (IPA) is a type of alcohol that has been widely used in hand sanitizers. In the area of metal halide synthesis, IPA has been used as a solvent in precursor solutions in the two-step sequential deposition of MHP layers. However, PSCs made with IPA anti-solvents exhibited inferior performance in comparison to other anti-solvents.117,119,123 Bu et al. found that the use of alcohols (iso-propanol and iso-butanol)-based anti-solvents in the synthesis of the double cation perovskite FA0.85MA0.15Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3, led to perovskite films with rough surfaces and some pinholes. The authors proposed that this was due to the dissolution of organic components in the perovskite compound such as methylamine iodide, methylamine bromine, formamidine iodide in the alcohols, leading to the decomposition of the perovskites.117 In order to use the alcohols as anti-solvents, excessive organic salt is needed in the perovskite precursor to compensate for the loss of the organic salt in the anti-solvent treatment step. Zhang et al. used 2-butyl alcohol as an anti-solvent in the MAPbI3 perovskite film synthesis. They obtained uniform, smooth MAPbI3 thin films from the precursor solution containing an excessive amount of MAI (PbI2:MAI = 1:2 M/M). The process required an extra washing step to accurately control the content of MAI. MAPbI3 PSCs made by this method produced a maximum PCE of 14.3%.124 Another option for addressing the loss of organic components in alcohol is to directly add the organic compounds into the anti-solvent to reduce the dissolution of the FAI/MABr from the perovskite films during the anti-solvent treatment. Tian et al. added a moderate concentration of FAI/MABr into the anti-solvent bath in their fabrication of FA0.85MA0.15Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3 perovskite films. Among the different anti-solvents investigated, they found that n-butyl alcohol (n-BA) is a more suitable anti-solvent as compared to other anti-solvents including CB, DE, ethyl acetate (EA), methanol, ethanol and IPA.125 The anti-solvent treatment was performed by soaking the spin-coated perovskite film in the anti-solvent bath instead of dripping the solvent on top of the spinning perovskite substrate. In this way, the perovskite film was surrounded by excessive anti-solvent, which caused the inter-diffusion of the anti-solvent diluting the perovskite wet film. Thus, the perovskite film was formed slower in wet conditions, producing smoother and denser films, resulting in PSC with PCE of 17.67%. EA-based anti-solvent systems have also been tested but reproducibility was reported as an issue due to the film's sensitivity to the soaking time.125

Alcohol with medium polarity, by controlling alkyl chain length in the solvent molecule, may suppress the issue of the loss of organic cations in conventional alcohol anti-solvents. Li et al. reported that 2-pentyl alcohol (2-PA) with lower polarity than IPA can remove only unbound MAI and residual solvent, inducing the formation of lead polyhalide soft frameworks for the growth of perovskite films consisting of large crystal grains, excellent smoothness and crystallinity (Fig. 16). The authors also compared the effect of DE, CB and TL on the perovskite film morphology and device performance. Despite having comparable polarity to 2-PA, the low solubility of DE in DMSO makes it difficult to penetrate the perovskite precursor film; as a result, the films showed poorer crystallinity. The weak extraction ability of non-polar solvents like CB and TL to DMF/DMSO solvent led to perovskite films with pinholes (Fig. 16). As a result, the MAPbI3 PSCs device fabricated with the 2-PA anti-solvent achieved the best PCE of 17.14%, which outperformed devices made with DE, CB and TL anti-solvents.123

4.3 Ester-based green anti-solvents

Ethyl acetate (EA), the ester of ethanol and acetic acid, is also considered a green solvent and has been widely used in practice. EA-based anti-solvents have been shown to produce perovskite thin films with better morphologies as compared to CB (Fig. 17a). Bu et al. used EA as the anti-solvent for the synthesis of perovskite thin films and as the solvent to prepare hole transporting materials (HTM) solution to improve the interface property between the perovskite and the HTM layer. They achieved the FA0.85MA0.15Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3 PSC with a PCE of 19.43%. The performance was slightly higher than the device with CB-based anti-solvent.117 To understand the interaction between the anti-solvent EA and the perovskite film, Zhang et al. determined the molecular electrostatic potential of the solvent based on DFT calculations. The results showed that the intermolecular hydrogen bonds formed between the EA anti-solvent and DMSO solvent in the perovskite precursor effectively prevented EA from evaporation after anti-solvent dripping treatment, thus avoiding the irregular growth of the perovskite intermediate phase (MAIPbI2DMSO) during the subsequent drying process (Fig. 17b). Compared to DE, EA-based anti-solvent dripping can further slow the perovskite crystallization, resulting in MAPbI3 perovskite films with larger grain sizes and smoother surfaces. The corresponding PSC devices showed improved performance and stability.126 Nevertheless, the effect of the anti-solvent is dependent on both the composition of the perovskite and the amount of anti-solvent used. Optimisation of the perovskite composition and volume of the EA anti-solvent used during the fabrication of perovskite resulted in an impressive device performance up to 20.93% being achieved with (MAPbI3)1x(FAPbIBr2)x perovskite. The perovskite film showed m-sized grains and reduced charge trap density with optimal volume of EA anti-solvent.127 The effect of acetate with different alkyl chains, including methyl-, ethyl-, propyl-, and butyl-acetate, on the crystal growth mechanism was investigated by Yang et al. The results showed that among the different alkyl acetate solvents, methyl acetate is the most suitable anti-solvent to deliver a dense and pinhole-free MAPbI3 film.128 This is attributed to methyl acetate having the highest vapor pressure as compared to the other acetates, which accelerates the evaporation rate of excess DMF and controls the perovskite nucleation process. In addition, the higher water solubility of methyl acetate decreases the moisture effect on the perovskite intermediate phase since the methyl acetate can act as a moisture absorber. By using methyl acetate-based green anti-solvent, PSCs with MAPbI3 produced the highest PCE of 16.3%.128 PSCs with remarkable performance were also achieved with propyl acetate-based anti-solvent. Gao et al. used propyl acetate as an anti-solvent in the fabrication of MAPbI3 PSCs with a configuration of FTO/c-TiO2/MAPbI3/spiro-MeOTAD/Ag. They obtained a PCE of up to 22.38%, one of the highest device performances among PSCs fabricated by green anti-solvents.129

The methyl ester of benzoic acid, methyl benzoate (MB), was used as a non-toxic bi-functional anti-solvent in the fabrication of (FA0.85MA0.15)Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3-based PSCs. A significant enhancement in the perovskite film quality was observed when MB was used for anti-solvent treatment. Specifically, the MB-treated film exhibited better smoothness, larger grain size and less residual PbI2 on the film surface as compared to the conventional anti-solvents like EA and CB. This was attributed to the better dissolving ability of MB for the perovskite precursor than EA and CB, resulting in the recrystallization of the small crystals into larger ones. In this way, MB plays a dual role as both an anti-solvent and digestive-ripening agent for perovskite grain growth. Using this strategy Yun et al. achieved a PCE of up to 22.37% with the MB-based PSCs, whereas the PSCs made from the EA and CB-based anti-solvents produced lower PCEs of 20.77% and 19.38%, respectively.130

Although not a classic ester, tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS), which is also called tetraethoxysilane, is another environmentally benign anti-solvent. In the comparison with CB and TL, TEOS allows relatively relaxed fabrication conditions for PSCs since the quality of the perovskite film made from TEOS anti-solvent is less sensitive to experimental conditions such as dripping time and volume. This has many benefits in the practical fabrication of PSC because simple operation conditions and higher device reproducibility are two of the criteria for the commercial success of PSC technology. The advantages are ascribed to the difference in the miscibility of TEOS with DMSO, along with the volatilization of the anti-solvent. The MAPbI3-based PSCs with high reproducibility showed a PCE of 17.02% with the TEOS based low-toxic anti-solvent, which even outperformed the PSCs made with CB and TL (15.4% and 15.6%, respectively) under the same conditions.131 Recently, tetraethyl orthocarbonate (TEOC), which has been mainly used in the synthesis of pharmaceutical intermediates due to its low toxicity, has been reported as a more suitable anti-solvent for PSC fabrication. TEOC has a high boiling point (161 C), high viscosity (1.06 mPa s) and strong interaction with DMF/DMSO through hydrogen bonding. It can effectively delay the drying process of a perovskite film, leading to enhanced film morphology. The fabricated MAPbI3 PSCs exhibited 18.15% PCE in a device structure that used PCBM as an electron transport layer. In comparison, the PSCs made with CB anti-solvent showed a PCE of merely 15.38% with a similar fabrication process.132

4.4 Ethers-based green anti-solvents

Various ethers with relatively low toxicity have demonstrated good performance in solution processing perovskites. Among them, diethyl ether (DE) has been widely used for the fabrication of high-efficiency PSCs.42,134,135 In particular, it exhibits outstanding performance in triple-cation-based PSCs as compared to other kinds of anti-solvent due to its ability to push perovskite precursors into the metastable zone.120 In terms of MAPbI3 PSCs, Wang et al. showed that di-isopropyl ether (DIE) could be a more suitable candidate for the anti-solvent. The PCE of the MAPbI3 PSCs made with a DIE anti-solvent reached 17.09%, surpassing the PCE of PSCs fabricated with other anti-solvents, including DE, chloroform, CB, and TL. This is attributed to the excellent stability of the MAIPbI2DMSO complex in the DIE anti-solvents while other anti-solvents, such as DE, cause the decomposition of the MAIPbI2DMSO complex into PbI2(DMSO)2. The stable MAIPbI2DMSO complex in the presence of anti-solvent is crucial to retain the balance ratio of perovskite cations and anions in the final film.136

Methoxybenzene, which is commonly known as anisole (ANS), is a low hazardous solvent that is often used as a fragrance in cosmetics and foods. ANS is considered a potential green solvent to replace CB.137 Zhang et al. prepared MA and FA mixed cation perovskite films with ANS-based anti-solvent. They found that compared to the film made with CB-based anti-solvent, the perovskite films made by ANS anti-solvent possessed smoother surfaces and larger grains. Accordingly, the corresponding PSC showed a PCE of 19.42%, which is slightly higher than the CB-processed PSCs (19.09%).137 A study of the binding modes of the ANS molecule on the (001) surface of MAPbI3 perovskite showed that the ANS molecules usually attach to the perovskite surface by the electrostatic interactions between CH3O methoxy groups and the PbI6 octahedra on the perovskite surface.138 However, it is unclear whether the same mechanism applies when ANS is used as an anti-solvent in solution processing of perovskite, in which the perovskite precursor is in contact with ANS. Nevertheless, the perovskite film made by using anisole as a green anti-solvent either in the anti-solvent bath method or anti-solvent dripping method showed impressive performance in PSC devices. Depending on the perovskite composition, a PCE of 20.3% for FA0.75Cs0.25Sn0.5Pb0.5I3/FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I0.5Br0.5)3 tandem PSCs,139 17.8% for MA0.4FA0.6Sn0.6Pb0.4I3 PSCs,140 and 18.2% for FA0.85MA0.15Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3 PSCs141 have been reported for the corresponding PSCs. Anisole also offers advantages for PSCs fabricated in atmospheric conditions instead of a glovebox. Yavari et al. fabricated Cs0.05(MA0.17FA0.83)0.95Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3 in both an N2 gas environment and ambient air with different anti-solvents including ANS, CB and TL.133 They found that under ambient conditions the TL anti-solvent caused aggregation, resulting in a thin film with pinholes, while using ANS or CB resulted in relatively uniform and smooth films (Fig. 17c). A comparison of the device performances showed that ANS has no notable disadvantages in terms of photovoltaic performance as compared to CB and TL. The PSC fabricated by using ANS as the anti-solvent for the perovskite precursor solution and a solvent for hole transport material reached an impressive stable PCE of 20.2%.

Wang et al. systematically compared the effects of the polarities of different ether-based anti-solvents including DE, ANS, DIE and dibutyl ether (DBE) on perovskite films. The polarities of the solvents are in the order of ANS > DE > DIE > DBE. Among these four solvents, DIE could completely extract DMF solvent during the anti-solvent treatment step without decomposing the MAIPbI2DMSO complex intermediate phase. This is ascribed to the suitable polarity of the DIE molecule. Solvents with higher polarities than DIE such as DE and ANS resulted in the perovskite films with high concentrations of PbI2, while the DBE with lower polarity than DIE led to a film with low crystallinity due to presence of residual DMF. The compatibility of DIE with different perovskite compositions was demonstrated, including Cs0.05(MA0.17FA0.83)Pb(I0.83Br0.17)3, (FAPbI3)0.9(MAPbBr3)0.1 and FA0.83Cs0.17PbI2.7Br0.3. The corresponding PSCs showed PCEs up to 21.26% 20.15% and 20.05%, respectively.142

4.5 Hydrocarbon-based green anti-solvents

Hydrocarbon-based solvents are considered to be greener solvents than their chlorinated counterparts such as chlorobenzene, dichlorobenzene and chloroform. Several aromatic hydrocarbons including o-xylene, p-xylene and 1,2,4-trimethylbenzene have been investigated to replace halogenated anti-solvents like CB in perovskite solution processing. The study of using o-xylene and 1,2,4-trimethylbenzene as anti-solvents in the fabrication of MAPbI3-based PSCs showed that both alternatives can produce high-quality perovskite film with similar film quality to that made by CB. With the PC61BM additive in the anti-solvents, they made PSCs with PCEs of 16.82% for o-xylene and 16.83% for 1,2,4-trimethylbenzene based anti-solvent.143 Similarly, p-xylene anti-solvent was used to fabricate (FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbI3)0.15 PSCs, yielding a PCE of 17.8%. However, the device with p-xylene anti-solvent was outperformed by devices made with other anti-solvents due to the low miscibility of p-xylene with DMF/DMSO.144 Petroleum ether (PE), which is classified as a hydrocarbon solvent, has also been used as an anti-solvent in perovskite synthesis by solution-processing. PE is highly volatile and is commonly used as a solvent in material synthesis. However, when PE is used alone as an anti-solvent in perovskite solution processing, the perovskite film showed a discontinuous morphology due to the ultra-rapid extraction of DMF solvent by PE, leading to poor device performance of merely 10.15% for MAPbI3-based PSCs.145 To address this issue, solvents with stronger polarities such as EA were added into PE to balance the DMF extraction rate of the anti-solvent system. This strategy successfully enhanced the morphology of MAPbI3 perovskite films and thus, the device performance.145

N-Hexane is a cheap, relatively safe hydrocarbon solvent. MAPbI3 PSCs made with N-hexane anti-solvent were demonstrated by Lin et al. who achieved a PCE of 11.7%.146 Most recently, Gao et al. compared iso-octane anti-solvent with other anti-solvents including propyl acetate, 2-methyl-1-propanol, ethyl ether and chlorobenzene. They found that iso-octane can promote the more rapid growth of perovskite crystals, leading to perovskite films with extremely smooth surfaces and large grain sizes. Consequently, the MAPbI3 PSC made with the iso-octane anti-solvent achieved a PCE of 22.78%, which is among the highest performance PSCs fabricated with green anti-solvents.129

5. Summary and outlook

Metal halide perovskite-based technologies, including solar cells and other optoelectronic devices, are on the edge of commercialisation. The synthesis of perovskite materials by solution processing offers many advantages in terms of cost-effectiveness and scalability. In this process, solvents are ubiquitous and critical for the quality of the perovskite films and their applications. Hazards associated with the solvents used in metal halide perovskite solution processing cannot be ignored, and could become a limiting factor for the large-scale production of perovskite materials either as thin films or powders (crystals). Therefore, it is of great importance to develop green synthesis protocols for metal halide perovskite materials by using less toxic or non-toxic solvents that have minimum health and environmental effects.

Currently, there is still a lack of fundamental guidelines for solvent screening for metal halide perovskite solution processing. The traditional Hansen solubility model is not adequate to explain or predicate the solubility of solid PbX2 in a solvent due to the lack of consideration of ionic and dative interactions between the tetraethoxysilane solvent and the perovskite precursor solute. A modified Hansen solubility model has made some progress, but the development of new theories that can help in understanding the solution chemistry, rheology, and drying microfluidics of perovskite precursor is still urgently needed to better control the final film quality including morphology, grain size, crystallinity, and phase purity under different experimental environmental conditions. Another important factor is the coordination interactions of the solvent with solid perovskite precursors. Abundant experimental evidence has shown that the ability of a solvent to form complexes with perovskite precursors such as PbX2 and MAX plays a key role in controlling the perovskite morphology. Since the perovskite precursors for solar cells are generally colloidal dispersions in a mother solution, knowledge of the colloidal chemistry of the perovskite precursor solution is needed to guide coordination engineering to achieve better perovskite materials.

To date, most of the reported environmentally benign solvents for metal halide perovskites contain methylamine ligand in the solvent. This is because the compound containing methylamine ligand has a low melting point (ionic liquids) and can be directly used to form perovskite compounds that contain MA. In contrast, the reports on green solvents for highly favourable inorganic perovskite material are quite limited. Among the reported environmentally friendly solvents for perovskite precursors, ionic liquids (ILs) have a remarkable combination of desirable properties such as high polarity, high ionic conductivity, excellent thermal and electrochemical stability and low vapour pressure. The ability to retard the reaction rate of perovskite precursors by the high-viscosity ILs creates an excellent environment for the crystallization of perovskites. Ionic liquids also impede the diffusion of small clusters to meet and form nanoparticles, resulting more homogeneous film formation. Inorganic PSCs fabricated from ILs-based solvents have been demonstrated but the performance still requires further improvement as compared to the state-of-the-art DMF/DMSO-based solvents. ILs can also be used in perovskite technologies as solvents, additives, and interface modifiers in electron transport layers. The flexibility of tuning the cations and anions of ILs to achieve desirable properties makes ILs promising green solvents for commercial production.

Research on the development of the less hazardous anti-solvents in perovskite solution processing has shown several promising candidates including hydrocarbons, ethers, esters, and alcohols. These solvents are generally considered safe with limited exposure doses, although they are not non-toxic. Ideally, a one-step solution deposition of perovskite without using the anti-solvent step is more favourable for industrial applications in terms of processing simplicity and lower module price. If this is not avoidable, the selection of a green solvent should align with the government-regulated permissible exposure limits, recommended exposure limits, and other industrial limiting factors (immediate danger to life and health, lower explosion limit). These solvent systems may be safe on the laboratory scale but are not industrially relevant due to other factors. Toxicology studies to assess their eco-friendliness are necessary.

Lastly, the capability of the green solvents in large-scale production for various applications needs to be demonstrated. With in-depth knowledge of the solution chemistry, approved toxicology and the ability to deliver high-quality perovskite crystals, these green solvents will be the key to the commercialization of perovskite materials.
