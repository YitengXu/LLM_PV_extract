Abstract

An expedient approach to the synthesis of well soluble symmetrical dialkyl-substituted Î±-oligofurans containing up to 8 Ï-conjugated furan heterocycles is reported. An ultimate symmetry and high solubility of these Î±-oligofurans were guaranteed using the 3,3â²-diheptyl-2,2â²-bifuran core and its symmetrical elongation through SuzukiâMiyaura or Stille cross-couplings. 3,3â²-Diheptyl-2,2â²-bifuran was prepared from 2,2â²-bifuran-3,3â²-dicarbaldehyde by the Wittig olefination and subsequent Pd/C-catalyzed transfer hydrogenation. The most appropriate access to 2,2â²-bifuran-3,3â²-dicarbaldehyde was achieved through a regioselective lithiation of 3-furanaldehyde acetal followed by CuCl2-induced homocoupling and deprotection. Single crystal X-ray analysis of 2,2â²-bifuran-3,3â²-dicarbaldehyde revealed anti-arrangement of the furan rings in planar molecules and an unexpected tight herringbone-type packing in crystals.

Introduction

Since the discovery of highly conducting polyacetylene in 1977,1 Ï-conjugated organic polymers and oligomers featured with electron delocalization along the conjugated backbone have attracted immense attention due to their important applications in numerous types of organic electronic devices.2,3 Advantageous electronic properties and good synthetic availability of variously substituted Î±-oligothiophenes and Î±-polythiophenes led these compounds to be the most popular Ï-conjugated materials.3 However, unsubstituted Î±-poly- and Î±-oligothiophenes starting from Î±-sexithiophene are practically insoluble and hardly processable.3,4 Attachment of solubilizing substituents at positions 3 and/or 4 results in twisting of the thiopheneâthiophene chain from a coplanar conformation that is ideal for Ï-conjugation.5 Thus, an inherent low planar rigidity hampers significant optimization of the thiophene-based electronic materials.

Despite chemical dissimilarity between aromatic thiophene and more cyclic diene-type furan heterocycles, theoretical analysis predicted an essential similarity in the structural and electronic properties of the long Î±-oligothiophenes and Î±-oligofurans.6 In 2001 Curtis et al. reported that head-to-tail regioregular Î±-poly(3-octylfuran) exhibited a conductivity comparable to that of the corresponding polythiophenes.7 However, a presumption of the intrinsic instability of Î±-oligofurans comprising more than four furan rings8 survived until the year 2010 when Bendikov et al. disclosed synthesis of unsubstituted Î±-oligofurans in up to nine furan units length.9,10 Computational studies predicted elevated planar rigidity of the Î±-oligofuran chain compared to Î±-oligothiophenes.9,11 Indeed, unsubstituted Î±-oligofurans are highly fluorescent and have structured absorption and emission spectra, suggesting the coplanar-type conformation in solution.9,10 Raman spectroscopy evidenced that Ï-conjugation in Î±-oligofurans does not reach saturation up to Î±-octifuran.12 In the planar ferrocene-capped Î±-oligofurans an excellent charge delocalization was observed.13 The first experimental organic field-effect transistors with the active layer fabricated from unsubstituted Î±-octifuran, hexyl-capped Î±-sexi- and Î±-septifurans as well as from styryl-capped Î±-quaterfurans demonstrated high hole mobilities and on/off ratios akin to the observed in the corresponding oligothiophene-based devices.14,15

One of the major practical benefits of Ï-conjugated furan-based materials consists of enhanced solubility in comparison with the thiophene-based materials. For example, unsubstituted Î±-sexifuran is 20 times more soluble in chloroform than Î±-sexithiophene, but the solubility of Î±-septifuran and the higher analogs is still transient.9 Taking advantage of comparable electronic properties of the thiophene- and furan-based polymers with better solubilization of the latter's, FrÃ©chet's16 and Janssen's groups recently applied oligofuran-diketopyrrole copolymers for fabrication of remarkably efficient bulk heterojunction solar cells with 3â5% power conversion performance.17

The valuable optoelectronic properties and a reasonable processability of Î±-oligo- and Î±-polyfurans prompted to consider this type of compounds as an emerging alternative to the thiophene-based Ï-conjugated materials.9â17 Yet, an assortment of the suitable furan-based Ï-conjugated compounds for organic electronic applications and convenient methods for their preparation is very limited and should be essentially extended. To address this quest, a study on the synthesis of the well soluble symmetrical disubstituted long Î±-oligofurans was initiated. This year, the first variant of the synthesis of dihexyl-substituted long Î±-oligofurans A, which was based on the Ni(II)-catalyzed KumadaâCorriu cross-coupling of 3,3â²-dibromo-2,2â²-bifuran (1) with hexylmagnesium bromide followed by bromination and the subsequent Stille cross-coupling, has been published (Scheme 1).18 Herein, we disclose the details of an expedient complementary approach to the synthesis of similar symmetrical dialkyl-substituted Î±-oligofurans A, for example di-n-heptyl-derivatives, starting from furan-3-aldehyde (2). We demonstrate the possibility to synthesize these promising well-soluble Ï-conjugated oligomers by avoiding highly toxic Ni(II)-complexes and organic tin reagents without essentially affecting the overall synthetic efficiency. A direct comparison of a scarcely exploited Pd(II)-catalyzed SuzukiâMiyaura cross-coupling17,19 for formation of the Î±,Î±â²-furanâfuran junction and the only applied Stille cross-coupling8,9,12â16,18,20 for assembling of the Î±-oligofuran chain revealed a comparable usefulness of both processes with a superior simplicity of the product purification in the first case.

Results and discussion

Our study was directed toward elaboration of a general approach to synthesis of symmetrical disubstituted Î±-oligofurans from simple commercially available furan reagents. In this paper, we describe preparation of the representative di-n-heptyl-substituted derivatives, which contain linear alkyl substituents suitable for efficient solubilization of the molecules. From the very beginning, we considered previously unknown 3,3â²-dialkyl-2,2â²-bifurans of type B (Scheme 1) as valuable central entities, which could be symmetrically elongated to give the soluble long Î±-oligofurans A. However, no reliable methods for preparation of the bifurans B have been reported in the beginning of this work. Thus, though dibromide 1 was reported in the year 1978,21 any reactivity of this compound was unrevealed before the year 2014.18 As a model, KumadaâCorriu cross-coupling of the parent 3-bromofuran (5) with alkylmagnesiums to form the corresponding 3-alkylfurans was documented to be inefficient.7,22 A complementary bromineâlithium exchange in 5 followed by alkylation with alkyl halides afforded 3-alkylfurans in modest yield.23

Based on the precedent of a viable CAN-induced head-to-head dimerization of 3-(pyrrol-2-yl)furans to 3,3â²-bis(pyrrol-2-yl)-2,2â²-bifurans,24 at the outset of this work we considered an option of the most straightforward access to the bifuran B directly from 3-alkylfuran 3. Following the reported low-yielding procedures for 3-octyl-7 and 3-propylfurans,22 the starting 3-n-heptylfuran (3) was initially prepared by cross-coupling of 3-bromofuran (5) with a freshly generated n-heptylmagnesium bromide in the presence of (dppp)NiCl2 or (dppe)NiCl2 in 20% and 24% yield respectively (Scheme 2). Alkylation of 5 through a low-temperature bromineâlithium exchange with n-BuLi in THFâHMPA followed by alkylation with alkyl halide23 failed completely with n-heptyl bromide and gave only 27% of 3 with n-heptyl iodide. On the other hand, Wittig olefination of the aldehyde 2 with n-hexyltriphenylphosphonium bromide (6) and LDA with subsequent Pd/C-catalyzed transfer hydrogenation25,26 of the 3-alkenyl-intermediate 7 provided 3-n-heptylfuran (3) in reasonable yield (Scheme 2).

At this stage, we considered an alternative formation of B from 2,2â²-bifuran-3,3â²-dialdehyde (4) using the same olefination/hydrogenation sequence as for the preparation of 3 from aldehyde 2 (Scheme 2). Both applied Stille and SuzukiâMiyaura cross-couplings provided very moderate yields of the soluble long Î±-oligofurans 20 and 21 (Scheme 7). From a practical point of view, faintly lower yields in the developed SuzukiâMiyaura reactions are overcompensated by avoiding the poisonous organic tin compounds as well as by the less laborious isolation of the products (only 2â3 FC were required for purification in the case of boron instead of 5â6 FC in the case of tin).

The long Î±-oligofurans 20 and 21 are reasonably stable, high melting point deep-yellow and yellow-orange solids respectively, soluble in most organic solvents. For example, the solubility of 20 and 21 in C6D6 is more than 10 mg mLâ1. A similar solubility was observed in 1,4-dioxane and in propylene carbonate, which could also be used for a formulation of relatively stable solutions.

Diheptyl-substituted sexifuran 20 and octifuran 21 were characterized by 1D and 2D (COSY, HSQC and HMBC) NMR, and by the field desorption (FD) HRMS, which gave intensive [M]+-peaks. The UV/Vis and fluorescence spectra of diheptyl-substituted Î±-sexifuran 20 and Î±-octifuran 21, which are essential for characterization of Ï-conjugated compounds, were found to be very similar to the thoroughly discussed data for dihexyl-substituted analogues.18 Evaluation of the synthesized Î±-oligofurans 20 and 21 as potential organic electronic materials will be reported in due course of the study.

Conclusions

In summary, we elaborated a convenient 7-step synthesis of symmetrical and well soluble dialkyl-substituted long Î±-oligofurans from the commercially available furan-3-aldehyde. In the key step of the synthesis, acetal of furan-3-aldehyde was subjected to regioselective heteroatom-directed lithiation followed by CuCl2-induced homocoupling to give the head-to-head dimer. A mild deprotection of the latter afforded 2,2â²-bifuran-3,3â²-carbaldehyde. High yielding attachment of the n-heptyl substituents to give 3,3â²-diheptyl-2,2â²-bifuran was accomplished through a Wittig olefination followed by Pd/C-catalyzed transfer hydrogenation. Subsequent bromination led to the corresponding 5,5â²-dibromo-2,2â²-bifuran. It was converted to the final diheptyl-substituted long Î±-oligofurans using either rarely applied, for assembly of the Î±-oligofuran junction, SuzukiâMiyaura cross-coupling or conventional Stille cross-coupling.
