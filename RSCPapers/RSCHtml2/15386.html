Heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts for zinc–air batteries: progress, mechanism, and opportunities

Xiaofeng Zhu† , Chuangang Hu† , Rose Amal , Liming Dai * and Xunyu Lu *

Abstract

Zinc–air batteries are regarded as promising candidates for next-generation clean and sustainable energy storage devices, due to their low-cost, safety, eco-friendliness, and high specific energy density. In zinc–air batteries, the air catalysts accelerate the sluggish oxygen electrocatalysis and largely govern the overall battery performance. Among the air catalysts, carbon-based materials have attract great attention, owing to their high conductivity, chemical robustness, porous structure, and tunable composition. Herein, this review presents the recent progress in bifunctional heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts for zinc–air batteries, especially for rechargeable and flexible batteries. The review will start with a brief introduction of the development, advantages, and types of zinc–air batteries. Then, the application of bifunctional heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts for aqueous and solid-state/flexible zinc–air batteries will be summarized. In the review, an emphasis is given for the investigations on reaction mechanisms, along with corresponding discussions on the role of non-metal and metal dopants. Theoretical predictions will also be discussed to guide the design and fabrication of future bifunctional carbon-based catalysts. Finally, a general perspective on the challenges and opportunities for the future innovation of heteroatom-doped carbon-based catalysts for zinc–air batteries is presented.

Broader context

Due to their low-cost, high energy density, and eco-friendliness, Zn–air batteries (ZABs) have gained extensive interest from both academic and industrial communities in recent years. With the combined features of fuel cells and lithium-ion batteries, the ZABs provide a promising solution to future sustainable and clean energy storage, especially in portable and flexible devices. To overcome the sluggish kinetics associated with the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), heteroatom-doped carbon materials are widely used as catalysts in ZABs. As the thermodynamic properties and catalytic capabilities of the carbon materials are largely governed by the heteroatom dopants, the rational doping strategies and the elucidation of the underlying mechanism are of significant importance. In the present review, the application of bifunctional heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts for aqueous and solid-state/flexible ZABs is discussed, and the development of corresponding theories and mechanisms for ORR and OER in rechargeable ZABs is also highlighted. By reviewing this emerging field, we hope to offer critical insights to guide the future development of ZABs as well as other related energy researches.

1. Introduction

The growing pollution and economy have resulted in a surge in the demand for electrical energy. However, 64.5% of electricity is still generated worldwide by using fossil fuels, which consequently leads to environmental pollution and global warming.1,2 Therefore, one of the most urgent research tasks is to replace fossil power by sustainable and green energy technologies to generate electricity for electronic devices/vehicles and grid-powering.2 Among all the different types of electricity storage devices, lithium-ion batteries pioneered by Nobel-laureates J. Goodenough, M. S. Whittingham, and A. Yoshino have long dominated the market due to their considerable energy density and high power density.3–9 Despite its tremendous commercialization success, the lithium-ion battery technology is currently restricted by the insufficient energy density (generally, below 350 W h kg−1), high-cost of anode materials, and potential safety issues.10,11 To address these concerns, efforts have been dedicated to finding safer and more efficient energy conversion and storage solutions with the cost benefits. Recently, metal–air batteries have attracted extensive interests amidst commercial companies and governments.12–14 Metal–air batteries store and reproduce electricity via redox reactions between metal anode and oxygen from external air at the cathode, combining the features of fuel cells and conventional lithium-ion batteries.5,15 Strikingly, the theoretical energy density of metal–air batteries is about 2–10 folds higher than that of lithium-ion batteries.16,17 Moreover, the utilization of free and abundant air (O2) as the cathodic reactant enables lighter weight and lower cost of the metal–air batteries with respect to fuel cells. Similar to fuel cells, metal–air batteries have a long lifetime and flat discharge voltage with zero-carbon emission, making them promising alternatives for lithium-ion batteries.18 Commonly, non-noble transition metals, such as zinc (Zn), lithium (Li), sodium (Na), potassium (K), magnesium (Mg), aluminum (Al) and iron (Fe) are used as anodes in metal–air batteries.14 Among them, Li–air batteries possess the highest energy density (5928 W h kg−1) and relatively large voltage output (2.96 V).14 Like Na and K–air batteries, however, the incompatibility of metallic Li to air and water would bring challenges in battery design and operation safety. Mg– and Al–air batteries, on the contrary, are stable in air and aqueous electrolytes with similar energy densities and cell voltages to Li–air batteries. Unfortunately, they suffer a self-discharge when exposed to alkaline electrolytes due to the lower reduction potentials of Al (E0 = −1.66 V) and Mg (E0 = −2.38 V) than that of water (E0 = −0.83 V), leading to a severe battery capacity loss.19 On the contrary, metallic Zn is highly compatible with air and water and its abundant reserve in the earth's crust guarantee the safety and low-cost in Zn–air batteries (ZABs). Meanwhile, ZABs can offer a relatively high energy density (ca. 1086 W h kg−1) and good cell voltage (1.65 V). In this context, the ZAB is considered as a better and feasible choice among all the metal–air batteries,20 thus receiving far more attention from the scientific community.

As an over-a-century-old technology, the concept of ZAB was firstly reported by Smee in 1840.21 Supported by the substantial technological innovations,22 the ZABs started to enter the market and conventionally used in medical applications (e.g., hearing aids).16,23 Subsequently, a milestone was achieved by Zorič and his team, who constructed the first rechargeable ZAB and applied it for small-sized buses.24 This breakthrough inspired the public acceptance of the first commercial rechargeable ZAB produced by NantEnergy in 2012, though with an unsatisfactory energy density (ca. 35 W h kg−1).10,25 While a more detailed history on ZABs with traditional metal-based air-cathodes was well summarized in the excellent review published by Harting et al. in 2012,15 N and P co-doped porous nanocarbon was first reported in 2015 as a metal-free bifunctional catalyst for rechargeable Zn–air batteries with superior performance to the counterparts based on precious metal catalysts (Pt for ORR and RuO2 for OER).26 This, together with the first metal-doped carbon catalysts (Fe–N–C) for a ZAB pioneered by Chen et al. in 2017,27 led to a new era of ZABs based on heteroatom-doped carbon cathodes, which has been embraced by the scientific community worldwide.

Some previous publications have comprehensively reviewed the progress and challenges in the field of ZABs by focusing on the development of ZABs and their performance, which is strongly dependent on the active electrode materials and the associated prompting effects,5,14 and the catalysts used at the air electrodes.17,19,28 Besides, practical concerns on ZABs related to their large-scale industrial and commercial applications have also been previously reviewed.10 An ideal oxygen electrode for ZABs should have a highly conductive porous structure to facilitate both electron and oxygen transports, along with a large specific surface area for high uptake of the reaction products, and hence the high energy density.29,30 By rationally designing carbon-based cathodes with well-defined three-dimensional (3D) hierarchical structures and abundant well-defined doping-induced catalytic sites, high-performance ZABs could thus be developed.31 In this regard, heteroatom-doped carbon materials are one class of the most promising candidates for air electrodes of ZABs, due to their high conductivity, robust chemical resistance, tunable porous texture, and controllable chemical composition and surface/bulk morphologies.

In view of the importance of ZABs based on heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts, a huge amount of investigations and the number of publications is still rapidly increasing every year since it has been firstly reported in 2015.26 A timely review on rapidly growing field of such significance is highly desirable, which, however, to the best of our knowledge, has rarely been systematically and specifically reviewed. Herein, a concise, but comprehensive and critical, review of recent advances in the field of ZABs is provided. The present review first summaries various non-metal and metal heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts developed for ZABs in Fig. 1. Then, the principle and implementation of carbon-based materials in ZABs, especially mechanisms of heteroatom-doped carbons for ORR and OER in rechargeable ZABs, including the triple-effects, d-band, and eg-filling theories, are highlighted. The restriction of the Scaling relations to carbon-based catalyst designs is also discussed, which, together with theoretical simulations, will provide guidelines for the future catalyst design. Finally, current challenges and future perspectives of the heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts for ZABs are outlined. This article aims to provide a timely, concise, and critical review by summarizing recent advances in ZABs based on heteroatom doped carbon cathodes and presenting important issues in this emerging field. Through such a critical account, our understanding of carbon-based catalytic materials and the associated catalysis processes for various energy conversion and storage reactions will be significantly enhanced, as with the interests of a broad range of scientists and insights for the future development and commercialization of ZABs.

On the other hand, atmospheric O2 diffuses into the air electrode through the gas diffusion layer and is reduced into OH− groups by electrons from the anode via the ORR (reaction (2)). Besides, the generated OH− groups migrate from the air electrode in electrolyte via a separator, forming Zn(OH4)2− species and then dehydrated into ZnO, as shown in reaction (3). The primary ZABs that only allow for the forward reaction (3) (discharging process) whereas the reverse reaction (OER) also occurs in rechargeable ZABs for charging the batteries.

The adsorption/desorption of these intermediates significantly influence the activity of the active heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts, which will be highlighted in the following sections.

2.1. Aqueous planar ZABs

Based on the different electrolyte and battery configurations, ZABs can be mainly categorized into two types, namely aqueous planar ZABs and solid-state ZABs, as presented in Fig. 2b and c. The aqueous planar ZABs are constructed and sealed with Zn electrode catalyst layers and alkaline electrolytes (Fig. 2b). Most of the ZAB performance tests in laboratories usually employ this configuration due to its simple assembly. As an essential part of ZABs, the air electrode consists of a catalyst layer and a gas diffusion layer (GDL), which facilitates the diffusion of oxygen from the air to the catalyst layer during ORR. To this end, porous and conductive substrates, such as carbon papers, are widely used as the GDL. By coating with electrocatalysts, it forms a gas–liquid–solid triple-phase boundary for oxygen electrocatalysis.35–37 Apart from the Zn and air electrodes, current collectors (metal meshes or foams) for electron harvesting and current delivery and the electrolyte (usually, 6 M KOH) are also well-sealed in the chamber. The ill-sealed ZABs would accelerate the reaction between atmospheric CO2 and alkaline electrolyte, forming carbonates and then jeopardizing the ZABs performance. In this case, a combination of plastic plates shell and screws with gasket kits are commonly adopted in research practices, further simplifying the assembly steps.38–40

Aside from the above system, flow-cell ZABs with circulating electrolytes flowing in and out of ZAB chamber were proposed based on the aqueous configuration. This design is based on the concerns of the degradation of alkaline electrolyte and Zn electrodes.14 The circulated electrolyte can effectively avoid the accumulation of carbonates in aqueous planar ZABs, ensuring the long-term operation of ZABs with a high energy output. Thus, the performance and life-time of ZABs can be largely improved. Nevertheless, flow-cell ZABs with carbon-based catalysts have been rarely reported so far.

2.2. Solid-state/flexible ZABs

For portable and wearable applications of ZABs, the above aqueous alkaline electrolytes have caused some concerns. For instance, the heavyweight of liquid-phase electrolyte discourages the spread use of ZABs in portable devices, and the design (Fig. 2b) requires a perfectly sealed outer casing to avoid safety concerns. Therefore, solid-state electrolytes have been developed to replace aqueous electrolytes, as shown in Fig. 2c.41–43 To fabricate solid-state electrolytes, polymers (e.g., polyethylene glycol, PEG, and polyvinyl alcohol, PVA) are currently used to maintain basic functional groups and water.44,45 Both the PEG and PVA type solid-state electrolytes function as the aqueous electrolyte and the separator, thus further simplifying the design of ZABs. More importantly what's more, solid-state ZABs can be easily engineered into various shapes and their sealing requirement is not as harsh as their aqueous counterparts, enabling the application of the solid-state ZABs in flexible/wearable devices.43,45 However, low content and weak retention of water in solid-state electrolytes would lead to fast electrolyte degradation, and thus a shorter cycle-life for rechargeable ZABs as compared with their liquid-state counterparts.46–49 Furthermore, the continuous loss of basic ingredients from the solid-state electrolytes and their insufficient interaction with electrodes could also cause a large ohmic polarization of the solid-state ZABs, thereby delivering unsatisfying battery performance.47,50

ZABs can generate electricity via redox reactions of Zn and oxygen, and different types of ZABs with specific configurations have been devised for various applications. For all of them, the battery performance highly depends on the catalytic properties of the air electrode, where kinetically sluggish ORR/OER processes occur. Therefore, the well-design of electrocatalysts is the key to better-performed aqueous and solid-state rechargeable ZABs.

3. Air electrode catalysts

As the most important part of an air electrode, the electrocatalyst and gas diffusion layer are combined into a bridge between the gaseous air environment and liquid/solid-phase electrolyte for ORR and OER.53–55 Therefore, active and durable catalysts are needed for achieving good performance of ZABs. Among the conventional catalysts, platinum (Pt) is known as one of the best catalysts for ORR and has been used as the benchmark catalyst in ZABs.56,57 However, the commercial use of Pt in rechargeable ZABs is restricted by its scarcity and instability as well as its poor activity for OER due to the formation of the electrical non-conductive oxide layer(s).58 Likewise, precious metal oxides, such as iridium oxide (IrO2) and ruthenium oxide (RuO2) are considered as benchmarks for OER, while their ORR activities are inferior.59 To reduce the expenditure of precious metal catalysts, earth-abundant transition metal compounds, such as metal oxides, sulfides, selenides, nitrides, carbides, phosphides, borides, hydro(oxy)oxides, and their alloys or complexes, have been demonstrated to be promising for ORR and/or OER electrocatalysis.60–69 Although most of them (i.e., MnOx and CoOx) exhibit the inferior ORR performance to the Pt-based catalysts, their considerable OER activities make them popular choices for rechargeable ZABs.70,71

Along with the intensive investigations of metal compound catalysts, carbon-based materials have been extensively developed as catalysts for the air electrode. Their robust chemical stability and good electrical conductivity ensure the long-term application and efficient electron transfer, while the high surface area and porous structure tunability facilitate the exposure of active sites and the mass diffusion kinetics. Thus, carbon-based catalysts have received extensive attention for the research and development of rechargeable ZABs.

3.1. Metal-free heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts

Pristine carbon materials, such as carbon black, carbon nanotubes, carbon nanofibers, carbon nanosheets, and graphene, are generally inactive for ORR and OER, due to the lack of active sites for the adsorption and activation of O2 and/or intermediates.72 Recently, tremendous efforts, such as heteroatoms doping, surface modification, and defect engineering, have been devoted to functionalizing the carbon matrix.73–75 Amongst them, the most commonly used and effective strategy is heteroatom doping. The foreign atoms can be chemically introduced into the carbon scaffolds via either in situ doping with carbon precursors during the synthesis of carbon materials or post-treatment of preformed carbon materials with dopants.76Via these methods, carbon-based materials can be doped with both mono- and multiple heteroatom(s). So far, many non-metal atoms have been successfully doped into carbon skeletons to improve the ORR/OER performance of carbon materials, including the extensively-reported nitrogen (N), boron (B), fluorine (F), phosphorous (P), and sulfur (S). Doping heteroatoms into carbon can regulate charge distribution in the sp2 electronic configuration of the pristine carbons.77 Given that the differences in the atomic size, binding state, and electronegativity between carbon atoms and heteroatoms, the charge and spin distribution can be refined through the delocalization of π electrons of carbon atoms near the heteroatom dopants to impart new properties to carbon materials, including the highly enhanced ORR/OER catalytic activities for ZABs.78
3.1.1. Mono-heteroatom-doping strategy. N atoms have a similar atomic size to that of carbon that allows its facile replacement of the carbon atoms without significant lattice distortion. The electronically modified structures of the N-doped carbon materials are favorable for various electrocatalytic applications,77,79 making them the most extensively studied metal-free catalysts. However, the higher content of N dopants in the carbon matrix does not necessarily lead to better catalytic performance. Combining the first-principle density functional theory (DFT) calculations with experimental results, Kwak et al. have proposed that N content above 30 at% would destroy the hexagonal graphene framework while simultaneously become ORR-inactive.80 For example, as a recently popular semiconducting material, graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) is restricted by its low conductivity in electrocatalytic application.81,82 As a result, the carbons with a N doping of below 10 at% retain both conductivity and activity, thereby being favorable for ORR and OER.

As well-known now, in situ N doping strategies allow the direct use of precursors containing both C and N elements, such as organic biomass,87 industrial wastes,83 and a variety of chemical products, including melamine,88 ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA),89 and dicyandiamide (DCDA),90 to straightforwardly introduce N atoms into the carbon matrix (Fig. 3a). Moreover, polymerization of C and N co-contained monomers, such as dopamine (polydopamine),84 aniline (polyaniline),91 and melamine (melamine resin),92,93 in the presence of templates (e.g., silica nanoparticles) is also extensively utilized to achieve the homogeneous distribution of N. After proper pyrolysis under a protective atmosphere, these precursors can be converted into N-doped carbons (Fig. 3b). On the other hand, in the post-treatment strategies, the N-doped carbons are prepared by treating mixtures of crude carbons and reactive N sources at high temperatures (Fig. 3c), such as aniline, melamine, urea, DCDA, nitric acid and ammonia.85,94,95 Unlike the in situ N doping strategies, the carbon substrates are firstly prepared, followed by introducing N heteroatoms from the N-containing sources into the carbon network. Apart from the chemical vapor deposition (CVD), plasma treatments are also useful methods to directly incorporate N atoms into carbon skeletons (Fig. 3d).86,96,97 To address the role of Fe in the Fe–N–C catalysts (vide infra), N-doped carbon on pure alumina with and without impregnated Fe or Ni (remaining metal contamination is lower than 1 ppm) was reported by Ozkan's group in 2006 to be active towards acidic ORR, though the exact role of N was unknown and the possible effect of the metal residual could not be ruled out.98 In 2009, a breakthrough was achieved by Dai et al.,99 who have first reported vertically aligned N-doped carbon nanotubes (VA-NCNTs) that can act as a metal-free catalyst for ORR in alkaline media (Fig. 4a). The ORR activity, durability, and tolerance to CO-poisoning and MeOH-crossover effects of the VA-NCNTs even outperformed those of the state-of-the-art Pt/C benchmark. Their quantum mechanism calculations revealed the high positive charge density of carbon atoms adjacent to N doped atoms (Fig. 4b), which induced the change of chemisorption mode of O2 reactant from the end-on to side-on (Fig. 4c) and thus effectively promoted the cleavage of O–O bonds. This work has inspired worldwide investigations on heteroatom doped carbon catalysts for ORR and beyond.100–102

Before further promoting the N-doped carbons for oxygen catalysis in ZABs, efforts have been made to unravel the origin of catalytic activity in N-doped carbon materials. As is well known, the N dopants in carbon-based materials are mainly existed in four different forms,100–103 namely graphitic N (quaternary-N), pyridinic N, pyrrolic N, and oxidized N at pyridinic sites (Fig. 4d). These N dopants are often simultaneously presented in carbon-based catalysts, while their contents strongly depend on the chemical nature of the precursors and the pyrolysis temperatures used to prepare the carbon catalysts. In an investigation of identifying active sites for ORR and OER in N-doped hollow mesoporous carbon spheres (NHCSs), it was found that the NHCSs-1000 (annealed at 1000 °C) exhibited better ORR activity in basic media than its counterparts derived at lower-temperatures.101 The activity enhancement was ascribed to the higher relative contents of graphitic N and pyridinic N in NHCSs-1000, as the pyrrolic N moieties have been partially transformed into the graphitic N and pyridinic N species at high temperatures (>800 °C). Besides, by systematically controlling the types of N species on the carbon matrix, a subsequent study further confirmed that graphitic and pyridinic N-doped carbon catalysts could better catalyze the ORR process than the pyrrolic N-doped ones.104 Similarly, Yang and co-workers have further improved the N content to 9.69 at% with a high percentage of graphitic N and pyridinic N (69.2%) in 3D N-doped reduced graphene,105 which exhibited favorable ORR performance and enhanced stability compared with that of commercial Pt/C (20 wt%). By contrast, some investigations also suggested that the ORR activity of N-doped carbon was determined by pyrrolic N.106,107 The debates were ongoing for a long time until Nakamura and co-workers used highly oriented pyrolitic graphite (HOPG) as a model to produce pyridinic N-dominated HOPG (pyri-HOPG), graphitic N-dominated HOPG (grap-HOPG), and N-free HOPG for identifying active sites (Fig. 4e and f).108 These authors observed an increased limiting current density and positively-shifted onset potential towards ORR with increased pyridinic N content in N-doped HOPGs, demonstrating that pyridinic N rather than graphitic N mainly contributed to the ORR activity. Meanwhile, the CO2 temperature-programmed desorption (TPD) depicted the Lewis basicity of pyridinic N-HOPG, further revealing that the ORR active sites are induced by pyridinic N (Fig. 4e). Encouraged by this study, Lv et al. recently developed a pure pyridinic N-doped graphdiyne with a high N content of 7.3 wt%.109 As expected, its onset potential, half-wave potential (E1/2), and limiting current density for ORR were 1.0 V, 0.84 V, and 5.5 mA cm−2 in alkaline electrolyte, respectively, even overwhelming the Pt/C benchmark. The excellent catalytic activity led to well-performed rechargeable ZABs with a high power density (130 mW cm−2) and long-term cycling stability (150 h). Nevertheless, some other studies also suggested that the ORR activity of N-doped carbon was determined by pyrrolic N.106,107 Thus, the debate on the active N types still remain, though the pyridinic N being a more favorable ORR active site.

Compared with ORR-active N-doped carbon catalysts, the mechanistic investigations on the OER capability for N-doped carbon are less reported. Nakanishi and co-workers firstly reported a N-doped carbon material that was capable of efficiently catalyzing OER in alkaline media.110 The N-rich polymer-derived catalyst exhibited a low over-potential for 10 mA cm−2 OER current (EOER:j=10) of 1.61 V, which was comparable to that of Ir-based catalysts. Also, it has been uncovered that the OER activity could be accordingly enhanced with the enrichment of pyridinic and graphitic N contents, indicating that pyridinic and graphitic N would be responsible for the OER activity in N-doped carbons. Likewise, the multi-walled carbon nanotubes doped with N heteroatoms were also demonstrated to show a low EOER:j=10 of 1.55 V in 1.0 M NaOH electrolyte, and the appealing OER performance was also attributed to the electronic effects induced by pyridinic N moieties.111 To elucidate the independent role of the different types of N dopants, Liu and co-workers proposed that quaternary N (graphitic N) sites were responsible for ORR due to their electron-donating nature, while the electron-withdrawing pyridinic N acted as OER active sites (Fig. 4g) in alkaline media, as evidenced by the change of XANES spectra for N K-edge before and after ORR/OER (Fig. 4h).51 By optimizing the distribution of quaternary and pyridinic N in graphene nanoribbon networks, exceptional rechargeable ZABs were developed with an open-circuit voltage of 1.46 V, the specific capacity of 873 mA h g−1, and a 30 hour stable recharge cycling. Similar findings are also experimentally confirmed by Yang's group.112

The above fundamental studies have further promoted the application of N-doped carbons for bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysis and rechargeable ZABs. In this context, Yu's group carried out alkali activation and N doping to produce edge-rich N-doped carbon materials (NKCNPs-900), which displayed bifunctional oxygen electrocatalytic activities in alkaline media (Fig. 5a).113 Moreover, defective structures, including the edge-defects, have been recently demonstrated as active sites for oxygen electrocatalysis, which could also tune the electronic and chemical properties of carbon materials. In the above-mentioned work, the edge defects of carbon atoms offer a portion of the active unpaired π electron to effectively boost electron transfer. The N-doping near the edge sites further contributes to the overall electrocatalytic ability, thus enhancing both ORR and OER activities and affording a relatively low ΔE, the difference between EOER:j=10 and half-wave potential for ORR (EORR:half-wave), of 0.92 V. As a result, a good rechargeable ZAB performance with a peak power density of 131.4 mW cm−2, the energy density of 889.0 W h kg−1, and stable 575 charge–discharge cycles were achieved (Fig. 5b and c). In other similar but independent experiments, N-doped carbon materials with bifunctional ORR and OER activities have also been successfully developed (Fig. 5d–f) for rechargeable ZABs with comparable performance to that of the Pt/C.109,114 However, the rechargeable ZABs using N-doped carbons mostly suffer from a large over-potential for the charging process (OER), requiring voltages over 2.0 V for substantially charging the battery. The high over-potential for OER is therefore regarded as one of the major challenges for the application of N-doped carbon catalysts in rechargeable ZABs.

As aforementioned, the N-doping-induced charge redistribution can reduce the adsorption energy for reactant/reaction intermediates.51,99 Apart from N, therefore, all atoms more electronegative than C atom could in principle be used as effective dopants to activate graphitic carbon materials into metal-free catalysts. Insights thus gained can be used to guide the design and development of other heteroatom-doped carbon-based catalysts. Indeed, a F-doped carbon catalyst prepared by ion-exchanging, coupled with the pyrolysis of the Vulcan carbon and ammonium fluoride mixture, displayed the Pt/C-comparable ORR performance with a high current density (ca. 6.0 mA cm−2) and long-term operation stability (12000 cycles) in alkaline electrolyte.115 Besides, a simulation revealed that S-doped carbons were also potentially active towards ORR, though S has almost the same electronegativity as that of C, and hence with no doping-induced charge transfer.116 Unlike F and N, the role of S for improving ORR activity was basically attributed to the high spin density on the nearby carbon atoms (Fig. 6a).117–120

Doping atoms with a lower electronegativity than that of C were also discovered to promote the ORR activity. For example, doping B atoms into carbon backbones showed an excellent electrocatalytic activity towards ORR in alkaline media.121,122 Given that B atom has a lower electronegativity than carbon atoms, the theory of electron-withdrawal effects induced positive-charged carbon atoms as active sites become hardly applicable for B-doped carbon catalysts. Nevertheless, there are two possible explanations:123 firstly, the electro-neutrality-breaking strategy is still applicable and the electron-deficient B dopants served as positively polarized centers for O2 adsorption. Secondly, the π electrons transfer from carbon to vacant 2pz orbitals of B atoms thereafter helping with the ORR process (Fig. 6b). Similar to B dopants, P atoms were also considered as the active sites, which adsorbed oxygen molecules with a side-on mode and thereby weakened the O–O bonds via an electron donation from P atoms to 2π* states of O2.124 Experimentally, a P-doped graphite layer realized by the pyrolysis of triphenylphosphine and toluene was thus highly active for electrochemical ORR in alkaline media compared with the P-free carbon counterpart.125 Compared with B, however, the P dopants with a stronger affinity to O atoms possibly are existed as phosphate, phosphite and/or other oxyphosphorus species in carbon backbones. In this case, the specific roles of P dopants for ORR in carbons call for further elucidation.

Similarly, the non-N doping strategy has also been used for improving OER performance. Suib's group reported a S-doped carbon nanotube-graphene nanolobe catalyst prepared via a sequential two-step method for OER and ORR in alkaline electrolytes.126 Like the ORR mechanism, carbon atoms near the heterocyclic sulfur dopants at the edge sites were attributed as the active species for OER, facilitating the formation of peroxide intermediates and thus promoting the overall oxygen evolution (Fig. 6c). Moreover, an edge-selectively phosphorus-doped graphene catalyst was also developed by Wang et al., which only required 330 mV to reach a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in 1 M KOH.127 They suggested that the enhanced OER activity was related to the P dopants, though the specific role of the P atoms remained unknown.

Notably, the defects (edges) were also recognized and considered as contributors to OER performance in the above work, and the heteroatoms preferentially located at the edges were revealed as OER-active sites.126,127 Similarly, it was found that the zigzag edges and doped armchair edges (Fig. 6d) with high asymmetry spin/charge densities are highly active towards ORR, according to the first-principles calculations.128 Meanwhile, the formation of such armchair and zigzag edges is significantly dependent on the morphology and porosity control, which not only provides numerous accessible reaction sites and accelerates the diffusion process but also generates more edges in the carbon framework. Thus, defect and porosity engineering is also of great importance for improving ORR, OER, and the performance of ZABs. In this regard, the metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are also excellent precursors for the syntheses of N-doped carbon catalysts.129–131 By taking advantage of the low evaporation temperature for the Zn metal in Zn-based MOFs, metal-free catalysts of porous N-doped carbons are ready prepared via a facile pyrolysis process. With evaporation of the Zn metal during thermal treatments, numerous porous and accessible carbon structures can be created, which are considered to be beneficial for both ORR and OER. For instance, the N-doped carbon catalyst derived from SCUT-11 showed a superior ORR activity with the high onset and half-wave potentials (1.0 and 0.88 V)132 attractive for potential applications in ZABs.

Although the defects may not be regarded as heteroatom dopants, the preparation of the heteroatom-doped carbon materials would inevitably introduce defects into the carbon framework and the introduced defects are usually active towards ORR. The defect-induced catalytic activities are also arising from a non-homogenous charge/spin distribution associated with the introduction of defects, as is the case for heteroatom-doping. Thus, the defect engineering is also important in heteroatom-doped carbon for ORR/OER and ZABs.

As can be seen from the above discussions, the mono-non-metal doping can significantly enhance the catalytic activity of the pristine carbons by breaking the electro-/spin-neutrality, which thereby improves ORR and OER performance to a large extent, as compared to non-doped carbons. Consequently, the excellent catalytic activity enables their applications in primary and rechargeable ZABs. With the exception of N-doped carbons, however, the use of heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts in rechargeable ZABs is still limited by the lack of a good OER activity. So far, only very few B-doped and P-doped carbons have been reported for ZABs, especially for rechargeable ones.133,134 Moreover, unlike N-doped carbons, the identification of active sites for S-, P- and B-doped carbons and the origin of activity remain unclear. To further enhance the overall catalytic performance, the textural and defect engineering can be used to modify the electronic configuration of carbon and to enrich active sites. Nevertheless, it is clear that most non-N-doped carbon catalysts are inactive towards OER, which would restrict the application of these carbon-based materials in rechargeable ZABs. Thus, the multi-doping strategies employing more than one heteroatoms would be promising to extend the catalytic capabilities of doped carbon materials towards ORR, OER, and hence rechargeable ZABs.

Based on the progress of the mono-heteroatom-doped carbons, multiple (binary, ternary and more) doping strategies have also been conducted to further boost electrocatalytic activities of carbon-based materials (Fig. 7a). To gain mechanistic insights into the ORR and OER bifunctional electrocatalysis of multi-doped carbon catalysts, Dai and co-workers have carried out DFT calculations on N,P co-doped carbon (NPMC) to determine the modified electronic structures of the N,P co-doped active centers.26Fig. 7b and c show the resultant volcano plots for ORR and OER, revealing the minimum overpotentials for ORR (OER) of 0.44 (0.41), 0.47 (0.49), and 0.47 (0.39) V for the N-doped, P-doped, and N,P-coupled structures, respectively. Therefore, the overall minimum overpotentials of N,P co-doped graphene for ORR and OER were determined to be 0.44 and 0.39 V, respectively, which even outperformed the precious metal-based counterparts (Pt for ORR and RuO2 for OER). Following the calculations, these authors used aniline (N source) as the monomer for polymerization in the presence of phytic acid (P source) with proper pyrolysis (Fig. 7d) to produce the three-dimensional N,P co-doped mesoporous carbon foam (NPMC-1000) with a porous texture and a specific surface area as high as 1663 m2 g−1. The NPMC-1000 exhibited extraordinary bifunctional ORR and OER activities in alkaline electrolyte, which made it an excellent metal-free bifunctional catalyst for the air cathode in rechargeable ZABs with a high open-circuit voltage of 1.48 V, high energy density of 835 W h kgZn−1, and stable charge–discharge cycling for over 240 h at 2 mA cm−2, though the output power density was relatively low (55 mW cm−2) (Fig. 7e and f). As mentioned above, the oxygen catalytic performance of mono-atom-doped carbon materials can be explained by doping-induced charge/spin redistribution. However, when it comes to multi-atom-doped carbons, the explanations are rather ambiguous, especially for the carbon catalysts with both electron-rich and -deficient atoms (e.g., N,B-doped carbons). Therefore, the fundamental understanding of the active sites in oxygen electrocatalysis remains unclear. Also, recent investigations on multi-heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts attributed the enhanced activity to the increased amount of active sites and/or defects.136,137,141 The reported synergistic effects between the doped atoms and carbon hosts might also facilitate the intrinsic activity for oxygen electrocatalysis.138,140 To elucidate the roles of heteroatoms, by employing the heteroatom-doped graphene models, Wei and co-workers proposed a “triple effect” theory to interpret the origin of the catalytic activity, as shown in Fig. 9.142 The triple effect consists of three components, including charge density, spin density, and ligand effect (i.e., coordination effect), which all would tune the binding with reaction intermediates. The charge density has been discussed throughout the previous section, which can be divided into negative charge effects (e.g., N-doped carbon) and positive charge effects (e.g., B-doped carbon), both of which would modify the adsorption of OOH and OH intermediates. Similarly, spin density associated with the lone electrons can also influence the catalytic activity from two aspects, i.e., the low spin density effect and high spin density effect. Lowering spin density can weaken the binding energy of OOH and vice versa. As per the ligand effect, it is usually induced by coordination unsaturation (defects) and lattice disorder induced by the heteroatom doping. The contribution of three effects towards ORR by enhancing the binding energies of *OOH or *OH increases in the order of negative charge effect < positive charge effect < low spin effect < ligand effect < high spin effect. Therefore, it is achievable to intentionally introduce two or even three effects in the heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts to tune their catalytic reactivities and even generate bi/multifunctional properties.

Therefore, doping more heteroatoms into carbon catalysts could further enhance the ORR activity when compared with the mono-doped ones. Nevertheless, most of the non-metal-doped carbon-based ZABs show relatively low peak power densities (mostly below 150 mW cm−2), and some of them even lack the good rechargeable stability, especially for those with non-N-doped carbon cathodes. To better understand the underlying mechanisms for the non-metal-doped carbons, an electro-neutrality-breaking theory and more comprehensive triple-effect theory are proposed to elucidate the prompting effects of non-metal dopants. It was found that the spin effect can tune the binding of OOH intermediate to a large extent, implying that ORR/OER and ZABs performance can be substantially enhanced by modifying the spin density. Moreover, the spin density of doped carbons can be usually engineered and influenced by the electronic structure of transition metal heteroatoms, which brings us to another episode of ZAB catalysts, cataloged as metal-doped carbon catalysts.

3.2. Metal-doped carbon catalysts

One of the main shortcomings of the metal-free doped carbon catalysts is that most of them are insufficiently active towards OER in basic medium. Transition metal compounds, such as layered double hydroxides (LDHs), metal sulphides, and phosphides,62,143–145 often show better OER activities with a higher current density compared with that of metal-free doped carbons. Nevertheless, few of these metal compound catalysts are able to efficiently catalyze ORR, due to the deficiency in conductivity.145 Therefore, researchers consider incorporating metal atoms into the carbon matrix to achieve good bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts for ZABs. Unlike transition metal compounds, the metal-doped carbons can achieve almost 100% utilization of the metal atoms, while only the surface atoms are accessible for catalytic reactions in transition metal compounds. Besides, the metal dopants in carbon will generate an under-coordination environment, which thereafter provides dangling locations for reaction intermediates with tuned adsorption energy.146 Moreover, the introduction of metal atoms can modulate the spin redistribution, which can also enhance the intrinsic activity of OER and ORR for carbon catalysts.142 Owing to the single atomic presence of metal atoms, metal atom-doped catalysts are regarded as single atomic catalysts (SACs).147 However, direct fabrication of metal atom-doped carbon materials (M-doped carbon SACs) using the aforementioned in situ or post-treatment strategies would generally lead to the formation of metal and/or metal carbide nanoparticles due to the high surface energy of isolated metal atoms.148 Creating strong interaction between isolated metal atoms and targeted doped carbons is the key to acquiring high-performance M-doped carbon SACs.

To stabilize single metal atoms in carbons, various strategies, including bottom-up and top-down methods, have been developed to prepare SAC. Detailed descriptions of the SAC preparation methods are comprehensively summarized in a previous review article.148 As an important class of SACs, metal–nitrogen–carbon (M–N–C) catalysts have received increasing attention, due to their extraordinary electrochemical activities for ZABs. In the M–N–C catalysts, N atoms possess lone pairs of electrons, which can form strong interaction with metal atoms to facilitate the immobilization of metal atomic dopants in carbon skeleton.148 Because of the spin density and charge density modification induced by metal and N atoms in the carbon framework, the catalytic capability of resultant catalysts would be significantly promoted. Thus, recent advances for M–N–C catalysts will be mainly discussed in this subsection.

3.2.1. Metal–N–C single atomic catalysts. The discovery of M–N–C catalysts and their application for ORR can be traced back to decades ago,149 when researchers attempted to replace the precious metals in fuel cells, due to their high price, poor stability, and vulnerability to fuel crossover effects. In the initial exploration, transition metal phthalocyanine was found to show an excellent ORR activity. Subsequent work on promoting the stability of phthalocyanine led to improved catalytic activity and durability towards ORR by the derived transition metal and N-doped carbons.150 As revealed by the further analysis of X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), Mössbauer spectroscopy, and electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS), the coordinated metal–Nx complex was recognized to function as the ORR-active sites in the thermally-treated phthalocyanine, as shown in Fig. 10.151 To fabricate the M–N–C materials, researchers have recently developed several methods using inorganic metal precursors and N-containing chemicals, such as aforementioned MOFs, covalent organic framework (COFs) and polymer monomers, to replace expensive macrocyclic compounds. The harvested materials are M–N–C catalysts with N-coordinated metal sites in carbon backbones after proper pyrolysis. Compared with non-metal-doped carbon catalysts, M–N–C catalysts usually show better ORR performance in alkaline electrolytes.152 Given the promising bifunctional ORR and OER activities, M–N–C catalysts are potentially capable of being air electrode catalysts for rechargeable ZABs, leading to many related research activities.

To this end, Wei and co-workers encapsulated Fe atomic species within the interior cavities of a ZIF host, and subsequently obtained Fe–N–C catalysts with a high metal content of 3.8 wt%.163 The resultant Fe–N–C catalyst exhibited an enhanced ORR activity with a half-wave potential of 0.895 V in alkaline electrolytes, which outperformed the commercial Pt/C benchmark. Unlike Fe–N–C materials, Co-based catalysts are relatively inert to Fenton reaction and can stably function under the ZAB operating conditions.165 Thus, Co–N–C catalysts have been developed to fulfill the stability needs for practical ZABs. Compared with the Fe–N–C counterparts, the Co–N–C catalysts can deliver considerable OER activity, though their ORR activity may still be slightly inferior. In previous studies, MOFs (e.g., ZIF-67) have been utilized as precursors for the fabrication of Co–N materials (Fig. 13a).152 ZIF-67 could be converted into Co–N–C atomic catalysts through proper pyrolysis and chemical leaching of residual Co nanoparticles. The resultant Co–N–C catalysts thus produced a desirable bifunctional ORR and OER activity with a small ΔE of around 0.75 V.166 As a result, the Co–N–C catalysts are believed as better air catalysts for rechargeable ZABs than Fe–N–C catalysts (Fig. 13b).167,168 Furthermore, Qiu et al. applied the MOF-derived Co–N-doped graphitic carbon (Co–NGC) as the electrocatalyst in an aqueous planar rechargeable ZABs (Fig. 13c), offering an open-circuit voltage of 1.45 V, a peak power density of 109 mW cm−2, and a large specific capacity of 617 mA h gZn−1 at a current density of 2 mA cm−2.169 Consequently, the ZABs can continuously undergo charge–discharge cycles for 56 h with a charge voltage of ca. 1.9 V and discharge voltage of 1.3 V. Similar battery performance was also recently realized using Co–N–C atomic catalysts even at a higher cycling current density (10 mA cm−2).170 Like the Fe–N–C catalysts, it is widely believed that Co–Nx centers are the active species for the oxygen electrocatalysis. In this case, Wu et al. dedicated to further promote the ZAB performance of Co–N–C materials (SCoNC) by enriching the population of Co atomic sites in the carbon matrix.171 A salt-templating method was adopted to prepare the monodispersed atomic Co on a N-doped graphene-like carbon with a record high Co content of 15.3 wt%. Owing to the densely populated Co isolated sites, the resultant catalyst not only outperformed the Pt/C towards ORR but also overwhelmed IrO2 for OER under the same conditions, resulting in the lowest ΔE of 0.63 V reported so far. Its excellent bifunctionality for oxygen electrocatalysis endows the as-constructed ZAB with a high peak power density (194 mW cm−2), large specific capacity (690 mA h gZn−1) and durable cyclicality (20 h), as shown in Fig. 13d–h.

Given the outstanding rechargeable ZAB performance in the aqueous planar configuration, the Co–N–C catalysts were subsequently used in solid-state/flexible ZABs. For example, Mu and co-workers installed a solid-state planar ZAB with Co–Nx/C nanorod arrays derived from 3D ZIF nanocrystals (Fig. 14a).172 The assembled solid-state ZAB displayed a good battery performance with an open-circuit voltage of 1.265 V (Fig. 14b). Meanwhile, cycling stability is another issue for the solid-state ZAB with Co–Nx/C nanorod arrays. It was severely deteriorated with a discharging retention of 51.3% only, while the aqueous ZAB can carry out 80 h cycling with negligible deactivation. As a more advanced design for solid-state ZABs, the wearable ZABs require a good flexibility for all constituent components of ZABs and efficient function of air catalysts under mechanical deformations, which is highly challenging. These novel designs allowed the batteries to deliver a good rechargeable stability during the bent-flat shift (Fig. 14e). Aside from the Fe– and Co–N–C catalysts, attempts have also been made to introduce other transition metals, such as Mn, Zn, and Cu, into N-doped carbon frameworks.178–181 Although most of them showed attractive ORR performance, the OER activity was hardly improved. Thus, the Zn, Mn, and Cu–N–C catalysts have been reported to be only feasible for primary ZABs till now.178,179,181 By contrast, Ni–N–C catalysts can provide both ORR and OER activities for rechargeable ZABs, as exemplified by a CVD-generated Ni and N co-doped graphene with an ultra-high Ni content of 23 wt%.182 As a result, the OER and ORR performance of the Ni–N–C catalyst was comparable to and even outperformed the commercially-available precious metal catalysts with a ΔE as small as ca. 0.60 V. Consequently, the assembled solid-state ZAB delivered an approximately 45 h long-term rechargeable stability and 3 h bending stability. Therefore, there are great opportunities to further modify Ni–N–C along with Zn and Cu–N–C catalysts for future rechargeable ZABs.

As compared with non-metal-doped carbon catalysts for ZABs, the metal-doped carbons tend to offer better ORR activities, thus resulting in a leap in the maximum power density output of ZABs (usually above 200 mW cm−2), as listed in Table 2. The M–Nx moieties embedded in the carbon matrix are commonly recognized as active sites in M–N–C catalysts for the outstanding catalytic activity,160–164 and the critical role of metal centers has been unveiled by poison experiments using strong metal-chelating agents (e.g., CN− and SCN−) to deactivate the ORR activity of the M–N–C catalysts.183,184 As is the case with non-metal-doped carbon catalysts, the enhanced catalytic activity for the M–N–C catalysts is caused by the tuned adsorption of reaction intermediates, including *O, *OOH, and *OH, on the metal sites. The ORR mechanism on the M–N–C catalysts involves the following sequence: O2 → *OOH → *O → *OH → OH− in alkaline solution (Fig. 15a), and the reverse reaction occurs for OER.161,166,169,170 Therefore, the adsorption of these intermediates on metal sites is the main factor that governs the catalytic activity of M–N–C catalysts for ORR and OER. Besides, the use of different transition metal centers leads to different ORR/OER activities.20

To explain the origin of the different activities, the Gibbs free energy of intermediates adsorption (ΔG) is often used as a descriptor to estimate the electrochemical activity in many studies.161,165,166,169,170 The optimal ΔG is expected to be moderate, which is denoted as the Sabatier Principle, as either too strong or too weak adsorption of intermediates on catalytic active sites is unfavorable to the reaction. This principle consequently leads to the birth of volcano-plot relationship profiling the linear correlation between different transition metals and catalytic activities, as shown in Fig. 15b.57 To further demonstrate the correlation between activities and intrinsic physical properties of metal centers, the d-band theory was proposed.185 It reveals the underlying relationship between the adsorption energies of intermediates and the electronic structure of metals. Specifically, for oxygen electrocatalysis, once the O-containing intermediates formed and adsorbed onto the catalytic metal sites, the 2p electrons from O could be coupled with 3d electrons of metals generating chemical binding with different strengths to form the so-called p–d coupling. The p–d hybridization leads to the formation of bonding (σ) and antibonding states (σ*), as shown in Fig. 15c. The filling of σ states strengthens the interaction between metals and intermediates, while the filling of the σ* states would destabilize the binding. As for 3d metals (Fe, Co, Ni, etc.), the σ state is full, while the filling of the σ* state depends on the local electronic configuration of metal atoms. Therefore, based on the theory, with respect to Fermi level (EF), the relative position of the d-band center (ED) conclusively affects the filling of the σ* state and thereby tunes the ΔG. As a result, the different catalytic performance delivered by M–N–Cs can be attributed to the distinct d band structures of metals. Then, what is the best relative position of EF and ED for oxygen electrocatalysis? To quantitatively answer this question, Yang and co-workers hypothesized an appealing eg-filling theory to further enrich the d band theory for ORR and OER in the ABO3-type perovskite scheme.186,187 Generally, d-orbital of central metals is split into 2 energy levels, i.e., eg and t2g orbitals. In their scheme, the transition metal sites (B sites) are assumed to absorb O-containing groups and eg strongly govern the σ and σ* bonding. Thus, the eg occupancy of B-site metals well determines the adsorption strength of intermediates, and thereby the catalytic activity. According to their results, the value of ca. 1 for the eg-filling indicates the best ORR activity (Fig. 15d). However, this hypothesis is based on the perovskite models, which endows well-defined crystalline structures and long-range order textures. However, the heteroatom-doped carbon materials are lack of the ordered lattice with various underlying adsorption sites being obstacle for the application of the eg-filling theory. Thus, the wide adoption of this theory to M–N–C catalysts still requires more investigations.

Since there would be an optimal d-band structure or eg-filling value for transition metal catalysts, how to manipulate it chemically becomes the focus of research. It is found that the d-band structures of central metals in M–N–C catalysts can be regulated by altering their coordination environments,188 which can be realized by varying the preparation conditions, including temperatures, gas atmospheres, and precursors. Except for the thermodynamically unstable M–N1 model, other M–N2–6 models have also been reported in the literature, as shown in Fig. 16. The presence of the Fe–N2 in the edge sites and in-plane sites were also experimentally and theoretically verified.188–190 It was found that the Fe–N2 sites can strongly adsorb oxygen with longer O–O bonds, indicating higher ORR activities due to the easier cleavage of O–O bonds. The bi-N-coordinated metal sites were also discovered in Co–N–C and Cu–N–C catalysts.191–193 Besides, the well-known M–N4 structure is widely used to elucidate the reaction mechanism for M–N–C catalysts.161–164,170,171 According to previous reports, the M–N4 moieties can be divided into two configurations. One is the porphyrin-like M–N4 structure in-plane of the carbon matrix, while the other is the M–N2+2 complex in the edges (Fig. 16b),194 and both of them can efficiently catalyze ORR in alkaline electrolytes. The M–N4 structure can modulate the chemisorption bond strength of oxygen molecules and other intermediates via the modified eg-orbital, endowing better ORR activity. Notably, the M–Nx (x = 2–4) structures possibly exist in the catalysts simultaneously with different proportions. Thus, it would be difficult to clarify the intrinsic activity for each M–Nx site.192 Although being rarely reported, M–N5 and M–N6 configurations (Fig. 16c and d) have been recognized as the active sites for oxygen electrocatalysis in previous literature.195,196 It is worth pointing out that the M–Nx complexes are all reported to be active for ORR, while their roles for OER are barely studied, which requires further elucidation.

The enhanced activity of M–N–C is usually attributed to the optimized adsorption of O2 and reaction intermediates of the oxygen electrocatalysis on the active metal sites, regulated by the d-band configurations. While the adsorption of reaction intermediates is modulated by the coordination configuration of metal atoms in M–N–C catalysts, the binding of O-containing intermediates follows the Scaling relations (Fig. 16e).197–199 It suggests that the binding strengths of *O, *OOH, and *OH on the metal sites can be adjusted simultaneously, which is because if the binding of one intermediate gets stronger, the other intermediates would bind stronger as well and vice versa. Thus, it is possible when the weak binding of one intermediate is strengthened intentionally, the binding of other intermediates may become too strong to undergo ORR/OER efficiently. Apart from this thermodynamic bottleneck, the unsatisfied OER activity is another shortcoming needed to be addressed in future research.

3.2.2. Metal-doped carbon catalysts beyond M–N–Cs. Based on the substantial progress achieved on M–N–C catalysts, researchers have made efforts to further enhance the ORR activity of M–N–C materials as well as their OER performance for rechargeable ZABs. Inspired by the outstanding OER activity of metal compounds (metals and metal sulphides), bifunctional electrocatalysts have been developed by combining ORR-active M–N–C catalysts with OER-active metal compounds.200 Meanwhile, the underlying synergy between the metal compounds and M–N–C species may further promote the intrinsic activity of both.201,202 In this regard, Zhang and co-workers combined OER-active Co4N nanoparticles and ORR-active Co–N–C on carbon fibers as a free-standing and flexible electrode for flexible ZABs (Fig. 17a).47 Besides, Fu and co-workers synthesized Co nanoislands planted on carbon nanosheets with abundant Co–Nx complex (Co/Co–N–C),200 which showed an excellent OER activity, while the Co–N–C nanosheets delivered superior ORR performance at the same time. Thus, the Co/Co–N–C represented a bifunctional oxygen electrocatalyst with good ORR and OER activities, ensuring better battery performance for the aqueous ZAB (a stable 1000 charge–discharge cycles for around 330 h and a peak power density of 132 mW cm−2) than those of the Co–N–C control sample. By using the ORR and OER bifunctional electrocatalyst of Co/Co–N–C, a cable-type solid ZAB was assembled to exhibit a high open-circuit voltage of 1.41 V and 10 h cycling robustness even under continuous mechanical bending, as shown in Fig. 17d–i. Likewise, a combination of molybdenum (Mo)–N–C and MoS2 (Mo–N/C@MoS2) was also reported by Amiinu et al.203 As an OER and ORR-bifunctional catalyst for a solid-state ZAB, Mo–N/C@MoS2 showed a high open-circuit voltage of 1.342 V and a stable cycling profile with charge and discharge potentials of around 1.99 and 1.24 V, respectively, which outperformed the commercial benchmarks with Pt/C for ORR and IrO2 for OER.203

Another feasible way to further improve the M–N–C catalysts is to introduce more heteroatoms (non-metal atoms) into the carbon backbone, as commonly adopted in metal-free heteroatom-doped carbons. For example, Chen et al., doped extra N and S into a carbon matrix embedded with Fe–Nx species.27 The synergy between N,S co-doped carbon, and Fe–Nx sites further improved the intrinsic activities for bifunctional electrocatalysis of ORR and OER. Therefore, the aqueous ZAB employed with this catalyst displayed a good peak power density of 102.7 mW cm−2 and a stable charge–discharge capability (over 100 cycles). Similarly, Li and co-workers developed single Fe atomic sites supported on the N, P, and S co-doped hollow carbon polyhedron (Fe-SAs/NPS-HC) derived from a combination of metal–organic framework and polymer composite.205 As evidenced by XAS analysis, the P and S dopants were mainly located in the vicinity of Fe–Nx moieties. More interestingly, the electron donation from the surrounding S and P to the metal center Fe (Feδ+) was revealed and consequently made the Fe less positively charged, thus altering the combination with OH intermediate and leading to an enhanced ORR activity. Although the OER activity of the Fe-SAs/NPS-HC catalyst has yet been thoroughly studied, a ZAB with the Fe-SAs/NPS-HC catalyst already showed a good peak power density of 195.0 mW cm−2 and stable charging/discharging stability without any variation in voltage for 200000 s. In addition to the synergistic effect on the M–Nx sites, the intrinsic activity of M–N–C moieties could be further enhanced via the formation of M–N–X–C (X = P and S) structures by doping with extra heteroatoms to coordinate with metal centers. In this context, Amal and co-workers reported an ORR and OER-active carbon hollow spheres with highly dispersed N and P co-coordinated iron atoms (FeNPC).204 As revealed by XAS and XPS, the doping of P led to extra coordination with Fe–N4 moieties, forming the FeN4P1 complex to act as active-sites in catalyzing ORR and OER, as evidenced in Fig. 17b and c. The ZABs based on the FeNPC bifunctional catalyst exhibited a high peak power density of 233.2 mW cm−2 and an excellent cycling stability for over 15 h. Other studies have also shown that the non-N heteroatom dopants could likely substitute the N atoms in Fe–N4 sites, thus generating Fe–N3S and Fe–N3P moieties in the carbon framework.206,207 According to Yuan and co-workers,207 carbon nanosheets embedded with P and N dual-coordinated with Fe active sites (Fe–N/P–C) were prepared and the 4-coordinated FeN3P1 atomic species possibly existed. Their combined experimental and theoretical results indicated that the replacement of P for N in the pristine Fe–N4 complex could kinetically accelerate the O2 sorption process, which therefore promoted the oxygen electrocatalysis for ZABs. Consequently, ZABs with the optimized Fe–N/P–C-700 catalyst exhibited an impressive open-circuit voltage of 1.42 V, a large specific capacity of 723.6 mA h gZn−1, and a maximum power density of 133.2 mW cm−2 at a current density of 219.6 mA cm−2, which largely outperformed those of the commercial Pt/C benchmark.

Another class of metal-doped carbon catalysts was developed very recently by introducing secondary active metal (M2) into M–N–C catalysts. After the introduction of an M2, it would be very likely to form another M2–N–C embedded in the carbon framework with a different M–N–C complex (M1–N–C). For instance, Jiang and co-workers used DCDA and carbon nanotubes as the precursor and conductive support to fabricate bimetallic Ni and Fe atomic catalysts for both ORR and OER in alkaline solutions (Fig. 18a).208 XAS analysis unraveled that both Fe and Ni atoms in the as-prepared catalysts were immobilized by the coordination of nitrogen, and there was an interaction between Fe–Nx and Ni–Nx as evidenced by a positive shift of Ni L-edge absorption peak. As a result, the catalysts showed outstanding performance for reversible OER and ORR, achieving a small ΔE of 0.81 V. Recently, Chen et al. allocated single atomic Ni and Fe sites on the inner and outer walls of graphene hollow nanospheres (Ni–N4/GHSs/Fe–N4), integrating ORR-active Fe and OER-active Ni to form a Janus catalyst (Fig. 18b).209 As evidenced by EXAFS, the atomic Ni and Fe sites were coordinated with four N atoms in a manner of planar Ni–N4 and Fe–N4. Unlike other dual SACs, the competition between the rate-limiting steps of ORR and OER can be balanced via the separation of Fe and Ni species, thus displaying a good bifunctional catalytic ability and excellent performance for rechargeable ZABs.

However, both the M–N–X–C and the M1–N–C + M2–N–C catalysts are essentially single atomic M–N–C catalysts with heteronuclear or modified metal centers. Although the M1–N–C + M2–N–C catalysts can provide additional adsorption and reaction site for intermediates, these sites on the two separated different-type catalysts (i.e., M1 and M2) would independently undergo the reactions because the adsorbed intermediates hardly move between them due to their long separation distance, and hence the Scaling relations cannot be overcome in the M1–N–C + M2–N–C catalysts. Therefore, they are still limited by the linear scaling relationship among different adsorption energies of reaction intermediates (*OH, *O, and *OOH), which thereafter become a fundamental limitation to the overall catalytic performance and further applications.210 To address this problem, innovative M1–M2–N–C models with M1–M2 dual-metal pairs stabilized via N-coordination in carbon backbones have been proposed. As compared with the M1–N–C + M2–N–C catalysts, the reaction intermediates are adsorbed on the dual-metal sites, rather than single atomic metal sites, in the M1–M2–N–C catalysts. Hence, the catalysts can provide dual-sites for intermediates in different adsorption manners, and meanwhile, the strong synergy between the metal atoms can largely regulate the adsorption behaviors. Thus, the development of M1–M2–N–C catalysts would be a practical solution to break the Scaling Relation restriction associated with M–N–C SACs. However, the preparation of M1–M2–N–C catalysts is the main challenge due to the electro-repulsion between metal ions during one-pot strategies. In order to prepare M1–M2–N–C dual-metal catalysts, some novel wet-chemistry methods have been developed. For instance, Lu et al. firstly synthesized an isolated Zn–Co dual-metal pairs in N-doped carbon catalyst (Zn/CoN–C) derived from chitosan.211 The Zn–Co bimetal bonding enhanced the adsorption of oxygen and activated the O–O bonds, thus exhibiting an excellent ORR activity with a half-wave potential of 0.861 V. Besides, the primary ZAB assembled with the Zn/CoN–C catalyst exhibited a peak power density of 230 mW cm−2. Meanwhile, Wang et al. also developed a Fe–Co dual-metal pair carbon catalyst ((Fe,Co)/CNT), which was used as the air catalyst in ZABs, due to its outstanding ORR performance with a half-wave potential of 0.954 V.212 The resultant ZAB showed a peak power density as high as 260 mW cm−2 and a specific capacity of 774 mA h gZn−1. Our previous work extended the application of M1–M2–N–C atomic catalysts to both ORR and OER by embedding atomic Fe–Ni dual metal pairs into N-doped carbon hollow spheres (Fe–NiNC-50) through dual-solvent methods.20 Excellent ORR and OER bifunctional catalytic performance was achieved, delivering a small ΔE of 0.72 V in alkaline electrolytes. Consequently, a rechargeable ZAB with an optimized Fe–NiNC-50 catalyst for ORR and OER was constructed (Fig. 18c), showing a high peak power density of 220 mW cm−2 and large specific capacity of 752.14 mA h gZn−1 (energy density: 932.66 W h kgZn−1) (Fig. 18d–f), outperformed other peer catalysts (Fig. 18g). More significantly, the rechargeable cycles were able to be stably carried out for over 100 h without any obvious deactivation and continuously powered an LED light, as shown in Fig. 18h and i. Although it was also revealed that the neighboring Fe and Ni sites of Fe–Ni dual-metal pairs in the Fe–NiNC-50 catalysts acted as ORR and OER reaction active sites, respectively (Fig. 18j and k), both of which were involved in the adsorption of intermediates. Thus, their binding can be regulated separately free of the Scaling relations.20 Following this work, another Fe–Ni embedded N-doped carbon was further fabricated for rechargeable ZABs, affording a high specific power density and good cycling stability.213 Since then, several dual-metal atomic catalysts, including Fe–Mn and Co–Ni, have been developed for rechargeable ZABs.214,215

Obviously, unlike M1–N–C + M2–N–C catalysts, reaction intermediates can be adsorbed on nearby metal atoms in the M1–M2–N–C catalysts with desirable binding energies, and thereby overcome the limitation of Scaling relations. Therefore, the development of dual-metal pair-doped carbon catalysts could address the drawbacks of M–N–C SACs, which will become a new frontier in the design and development of heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts. Till now, the investigation of dual-metal atomic catalysts is still at its early stage as a comparison with the big family of M–N–C catalysts. However, we believe that dual-metal atomic catalysts have great prospects for rechargeable ZABs in the future.

4. Theoretical predictions

Uncovering the ORR/OER mechanisms in heteroatom-doped carbon is important, which has been supported by numerous experimental observations and theoretical calculations, as the same principle could be applied to the development of various high efficient ORR/OER bifunctional carbon-based cathodes for ZABs. Taking ORR as a probe reaction, the texture and origin of the catalytic activity for different heteroatom (B, N, O, P, and S)-doped graphene catalysts (Fig. 19a and b) have been investigated.78,216 The graphene samples doped with different heteroatoms exhibited various electrochemical activities towards ORR, depending strongly on the nature of the dopants. The theoretically predicted exchange current density (j0) for heteroatom-doped graphene (X-graphene) should be at the top of the volcano (Fig. 19b, defined by the cross-over point of the red lines), which is ca. 6 times that of Pt/C catalyst (blue line), indicting that heteroatom-doped carbon nanomaterials hold great promise for producing efficient carbon catalysts to substitute the noble metal catalysts.

Further DFT calculations indicate that the location of the heteroatoms (e.g., N) plays an important role in enhancing the OER/ORR performance simultaneously. Xia and co-workers have demonstrated theoretically that the substitution of carbon at graphene edge by nitrogen results in the best performance in terms of overpotentials (Fig. 19c).217 Specifically, the OER active sites were identified on the armchair nanoribbons at the carbon atoms near the nitrogen atom, while the ORR active sites are those on the edge carbon near the nitrogen atom. The armchair nanoribbons with N dopants near the edge have the minimum theoretical OER overpotential, which was estimated to be 0.405 V, while the minimum overpotential for ORR was calculated to be 0.445 V (Fig. 19d and e). These values of ORR and OER overpotentials are comparable to those of precious metal-based catalysts (Pt for ORR and IrO2 for OER), suggesting that engineering the edge structures can increase the efficiency of the heteroatom-doped carbon electrocatalysts for efficient OER/ORR in ZABs.

Doping carbon materials (e.g., CNTs, graphene, even graphite) with different heteroatoms, including electron-donating atoms (e.g., P) and electron-accepting atoms (e.g., N), to induce synergistic effects associated with the electronic interactions between different heteroatoms and surrounding carbon atoms is one of the most effective strategies to create effective bifunctional electrocatalysts for ORR and OER.135,218,219 To quantitatively predict bifunctional ORR/OER activities of dual-element doped C-MFECs, Xia and co-workers introduced an activity descriptor associated with the intrinsic material property, which is defined as the product of electronegativity (EX) and electron affinity (AX): Φ = (EX/EC) × (AX/AC), a dimensionless factor, to represent the effect of these characteristics, where the subscript represents the element.220 By calculating overpotentials for all the doping structures, i.e., single N, single X, and N–X clusters (X = N, B, P, S, Cl, etc.), over the co-doped graphene network, the lowest value was chosen as the lower limit of the overpotentials (Ulimit) for the co-doping structure defined by the corresponding descriptor. Compared with the mono element doping, the overpotentials for the N–X (X = N, B, P, S, Cl, etc.) codoping were reduced by 10–40%, depending on the type of co-dopants. Interestingly, when normalized by the overpotential for N–N doping, the overpotentials for N–X codoping clusters vs. the descriptor Φ yielded an inverted volcano relationship (Fig. 19f), indicating co-doing vs. mono-doping can significantly reduce the overpotential to facilitate the reaction.220 In general, the larger the difference in Φ between the “N” and co-dopant “N–X” cluster is, the lower the overpotential becomes. Moreover, it is also important to control the distance between the two dopants in co-doped carbon-based catalysts to create the synergistic effect for efficient multifunctional electrocatalysis.221,222 These findings provide a powerful guidance to design and develop various new high-performance bifunctional OER/ORR catalysts from earth-abundant carbon materials and heteroatom dopants.

For metal-atom doped carbon materials, the performance of the active sites in the carbon skeleton can be further tuned by the coordination environment of metal ions (Fig. 19g and h). Using the first-principles methods based on DFT, Zhang et al. demonstrated that the activity on Co site of CoNx-gra towards ORR and OER would be affected by both the N-dopant concentration and configuration.223 The origin of the activity stems from the moderate hybridization between the Co 3d orbital and p-orbital from O species, governed by the neighboring N coordination environment. The overpotential values for both ORR and OER were roughly reduced with increasing the doping concentration of N, and were also dependent on the doping configuration (Fig. 19h). Among all the CoNx-gra (x = 1, 2, 3, 4, Fig. 19g), the CoN4-gra is identified as the best candidate with the calculated overpotential of 0.69 and 0.47 eV for the OER and ORR, respectively. The results for density of states (DOS) also indicate that the hybridization interaction between the Co-d and O-p states is more pronounced for the CoN4-gra than for other catalysts investigated. These results might be potentially helpful to design the bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts based on the transition metals. In addition to the effect of coordination elements on the activity of the active sites, the introduction of other foreign metal elements can also lower the reaction energy barrier and further improve catalytic performance. For the hybrid catalyst of NiCo/Pyri-NG, the surface Co and its neighboring Ni synergistically promote the efficiency for both ORR and OER (Fig. 19i).224 The optimized hybrid manifests outstanding reversible oxygen electrocatalytic activities, leading to an overpotential different ΔE among the lowest value for highly efficient bifunctional catalysts, surpassing most of the reported bi-functional electrocatalysts. This will promote the rational design of efficient hybrid electrocatalysts at the atomic level for diverse applications in renewable energy storage and conversion.

5. Outlook

Compared with the great commercial success achieved by Li-ion batteries, the ZABs are still demanding substantial progress to further enhance their performance for practical applications. This review summarizes the development of heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts and their outstanding applications for ORR and OER in ZABs. Recent progress in the M–N–C materials promotes all-solid-state and flexible ZABs for portable and wearable applications. Meanwhile, the mechanism of ORR and OER on various carbon-based catalysts is comprehensively highlighted to uncover the underlying relationship between the structure of active sites and their reactivity for ORR and OER. Recent research efforts are concentrated on the promotion of catalytic activities via the modification of the coordination structure of the heteroatom (including metal)-doped sites to tune the charge/spin redistributions and to break the restriction of Scaling relations.

Despite the extensive efforts and progress that were discussed, however, there are still spacious potentials for further improvement of rechargeable ZABs. Specific challenges and innovative approaches to advanced electrocatalysts are briefly described below:

(1) Bifunctional doped-carbon-based catalysts for efficient ORR and OER. The improvement of their intrinsic activity can further facilitate the sluggish ORR/OER. Moreover, the nature of dopants and doping content should be carefully considered, when developing a bifunctional oxygen catalyst. Also, enriching the population of the single atomic sites in M–N–C catalysts is verified as a feasible method to promote the overall performance of ZABs. Besides, incorporating noble metal atoms in the M–N–C configuration would be an attractive option as it can not only largely reduce the cost but also further boost their excellent intrinsic activity. Simulations and theoretical calculations would also help with the design and optimization of the catalysts. Furthermore, defect and porosity engineering are considered as feasible strategies not only to benefit the diffusion of reactants/products/electrolytes but also to increase the number of active sites for ORR and OER from the properly located defects.

(2) The fabrication of dual-metal catalysts and other novel catalysts are worthwhile to be explored. The M1–M2–N–C catalysts are developed to break the restriction of Scaling relations, which thermodynamically limits the further enhancement of the activity for M–N–C catalysts. The investigation of M1–M2–N–C catalysts is still at its early stage, extensive work is strongly encouraged to achieve the high power density, stability, and high round-trip efficiency of ZABs. Meanwhile, based on either the M–N–C or M1–M2–N–C configuration, the proper coordination modification is also an effective approach to regulate the electronic structure of the metal atoms, and thereby improve their catalytic activities.

(3) The further elucidation of the reaction mechanism for ORR/OER by combining advanced techniques with DFT simulations. Although various theories and hypotheses have been proposed to explain the correlation between the coordination environment and the catalytic performance, the favorable electronic configurations of the metal sites and suitable coordination structures are still unclear and inapplicable in some scenarios. For example, the eg-filling theory requires a well-defined crystalline structure like metal oxides, but M–N–C catalysts are often lack of the long-range order. Furthermore, some studies also revealed that the coordination structures of the metal atoms are actually under dynamically changes during the reactions.20,225 It indicates the simultaneous variation of the electronic configurations with the adsorption of intermediates. Therefore, the fundamental understanding of the reaction mechanism and the preferred coordination is strongly required through utilizing in situ and operando techniques in conjugation with theoretical calculations. Also, the change in the density of states during reactions remains to be further unveiled. The elucidation of the dynamic change for the d band structure of active sites would lead to an in-depth understanding of the correlation between the band structure and activity.

(4) Battery constructing protocols and key indicators of battery tests. Zn–air batteries reported in literature are constructed in different configurations and sealed with varied electrolytes, thus it is very challenging to carry out the performance comparisons due to the lack of a universal standard. Therefore, it is highly encouraged to specify the assembly and test conditions for a more objective comparison, including the type of the separator, gas flow rate, operating temperature, electrode distance, reaction area of the electrode, and the volume of the electrolyte used in the cell.226 For battery testing, apart from the conventionally used indicators, such as open-circuit potential, peak power density, and specific capacity (energy density), it is highly recommended to evaluate some important descriptors, including (a) round-trip efficiency, (b) gravimetric and volumetric energy densities, (c) parameters of cycling measurements [e.g., a proper current density (10–20 mA cm−2), discharge/charge period (above 1 hour) and cycling duration (above 20 cycles) for accurately depicting the real cycling ability of the rechargeable ZABs], (d) averaged Coulombic efficiency,227 and (e) failure time of the batteries. Furthermore, the maximal number of cycles (or duration) before the failure of batteries could be considered as an important descriptor to estimate the lifetime of the ZABs. The evaluation of these key indicators would help researchers to test ZABs thoroughly and objectively.

(5) The air catalysts should be fabricated into a membrane electrode assembly (MEA) for practical applications in solid-state ZABs. Most of the carbon-based catalysts are generally coated on conductive substrates when constructing ZABs, while limited research is conducted towards MEAs for ZABs. One of the main reasons is the lack of good MEAs consisting of both solid-state electrolytes and catalysts. Notably, the performance of the solid-state ZABs is considerably inferior to their aqueous counterparts, predominately due to the ill-contact between electrolytes and catalysts. Therefore, feasible and efficient methods for the fabrication of MEAs and the preparation of binder-free catalysts are highly desirable.

(6) Aside from the air electrodes, optimizations of Zn anodes and solid-state alkaline electrolytes for ZABs are also worthwhile to be accomplished. Specifically, the uneven current density at electrodes results in the formation of Zn dendrites, which may destroy separators and even short-circuit the whole batteries. Thus, the composition modification, structural adjustment, and surface engineering of the Zn electrodes are highly demanding. In addition, the fabrication of solid-state alkaline electrolytes is also important for flexible and wearable ZABs (Table 3). Furthermore, the development of rational ZAB design and packing configurations for further enhancing battery performance is also encouraged.

Owing to the low-cost, safety, and high energy density, ZABs are becoming the most likely candidate for replacing lithium-ion batteries as one of the sustainable energy storage technologies. The extension from the liquid-phase ZABs to the solid-state ZABs exclusively ensures the commercialization of ZABs for wearable and portable applications. Therefore, sufficient efforts from the academic and industrial sectors are highly encouraged to promote the transition from a fossil fuel-dependent economy to a green and low carbon emssion based on ZABs.
