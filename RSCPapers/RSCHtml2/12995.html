Abstract

Biological materials have robust hierarchical structures capable of specialized functions and the incorporation of natural biologically active components, which have been finely tuned through millions of years of evolution. These highly efficient architectural designs afford remarkable transport and mechanical properties, which render them attractive candidates for flexible electronic sensing technologies. This review provides a comprehensive overview of the fundamental aspects and applications of biological materials for flexible electronic devices and discusses various classes of biological materials by describing their unique structures and functions. We discuss the effect of the biological activity of biological materials on the improved properties in detail, because this effect overcomes the limited bioavailability and restricted morphology of materials generally encountered in traditional flexible electronic devices. We also summarize various approaches for the design and functionalization of natural materials and their applications in flexible electronic devices for use in biomedical, electron, energy, environmental and optical fields. Finally, we provide new insights and perspectives to further describe trends for future generations of biological materials, which are likely to be critical components (building blocks or elements) in future flexible electronics.

1 Introduction

Flexible electronic devices are of critical importance for medical devices, portable electronics, smart textiles, energy storage, communication and sensor systems.1–10 There is a growing demand for electronic devices that are highly flexible, lightweight, biocompatible, easy to fabricate and inexpensive. Particularly, attention is given to the flexibility and biocompatibility of sensing materials, because these materials are predicted to have widespread use in high performance and environmentally friendly practical applications.4,11–17 Current flexible electronic technologies are dominated by inorganic/organic compound-based materials, such as carbon materials,18–23 metal oxide semiconductors,2,24–27 and polymers.28–32 Despite significant developments over the past few decades, their intrinsic mechanical properties, high cost, and non-biocompatibility limit the widespread use of these materials.

Biological materials are complex actuators and possess beneficial characteristics,33 such as exceptional topographic features,17,34–39 satisfactory in situ tailorability of the chemical composition,39–43 and remarkable mechanical properties.17,35,43–51 In addition to the structural features, natural biological materials have superb biological characteristics (such as abundant and renewable supply, low cost, water solubility, biodegradability, self-adhesive and self-cleaning properties, anti-microbial activity, biocompatibility, renewability, non-toxicity and adsorption properties) beyond the limits of synthetic micro/nanomaterials, as these materials have been naturally optimized over millions of years of evolution to fulfill specific purposes and demands. For example, the immune system contains nano-biointerfaces that possess specific recognition ability for cancer cells.52,53 Butterfly wings have photonic crystal arrays supplying structural color and photonic crystals.54–57 And lotus leaves have superhydrophobic surfaces that provide self-cleaning properties and low water adhesion.58,59 The structures and performances of natural materials not only spark fascination, but also prompt the question as to why certain structures or surfaces exhibit outstanding properties, as well as encourage us to look for new insights from nature to design novel materials.

Inspired by their structures and properties, biological materials provide significant benefits and are attractive building blocks for developing environmentally friendly and biosustainable flexible electronic devices, such as sensors,18,43,48,50,60–64 transistors,48,65–68 display devices,69–71 supercapacitors,48,72–74 lithium-ion batteries,75–79 and solar cells.80–83 These benefits are as follows: (1) their natural 3D structure (such as in natural honeycomb and natural cellular materials) affords a tunable elastic modulus to provide excellent mechanical flexibility and robustness, such that the material can be effectively and congruently fit to various curved and dynamic surfaces and interfaces, such as human organs and biological organism. (2) Super-hydrophobic and self-cleaning surfaces can efficiently reduce the adhesion of dust particles and water as well as the contact area, which can aid in the anti-interference of devices with very precise performances in various environments. (3) Natural materials can afford a variety of functionalities (e.g., recognition, selective adsorption, and sensing) due to their specific compositions, which contain many functional groups, such as hydroxyl, carboxyl, amino, and amine groups, resulting in properties beyond those found in synthetic and man-made materials. (4) The non-toxic, low cost, and naturally abundant materials are biocompatible and can accommodate large-scale fabrication of flexible electronic devices, which makes these materials environmentally friendly. (5) Their lightweight features could reduce the discomfort of external devices meant for long-term integration with the human body and thus enable the long-term and continuous use of flexible sensing platforms for health-monitoring applications.

In addition, natural biological materials are easily functionalized by numerous synthetic methods due to their diverse chemical composition and structural features.18,39,43,50,89 As a result of their highly tailorable composition and structure, natural biofunctional surfaces can usually fulfill several functions.90 Modifying the chemical structure is a useful route to meet the demands of specialized applications by adjusting the mechanical properties, conductivity, electron transfer rates, solubility, capacity, and crystal structure. Thus, natural biocomposites are beneficial to obtain the optimized performance of a wide variety of flexible electronic devices. For example, multiple research groups have utilized natural biological materials to fabricate flexible devices, such as plants (wood,91,92 flower,93 leaf,94 pollen,18,39,50 and silk48,87), active organisms (butterfly,43,95 fish,96 insect,97 gecko,98 bacteria,77 peptide,99 and DNA100), and biological molecules (chitin,101,102 polydopamine,78,85,103,104 oligomeric proantho cyanidins,105 terpenes,40 and fatty acids106). Biological molecules were initially tested as the active component, followed by many other biological materials and biological composites. The properties and broad applications of biological materials have rapidly developed in recent years, as shown by the large number of publications from their advent as active components in 2000 to date (Fig. 1). This development distinctly suggests the significance of biological materials and the intense attention and interest for scientific research in various areas in electronic, optical and energy fields. Notably, early studies mainly focused on the design of biomaterials for use in medical devices, and several reviews have given a brief overviews of some biomedical applications of biological materials.99,107–109 Since 2012, biological materials have undergone significant expansion into various fields and become a hotspot research field. There was a dramatic increase in publications listed when searching for “biomaterials and application” as the key phrase in the “Web of Science” on 15 July 2017. Despite these advances, there is still a lack of systematic examination of the biological and physicochemical characteristics and broad applications of biological materials in other research fields. Herein, we provide an overview of the current state of natural biological materials in flexible electronic devices for applications in various fields (Fig. 2). Our goal is to highlight the importance and development of biological material-based flexible electronic devices in which biological materials derived from nature serve as biologically active components. First, the preparation and modification of active materials, developed through nature-inspired exploration, are introduced. In addition, we discuss the unique biological and physicochemical characteristics of biological materials as promising candidates for high-performance flexible electronic devices. Following this section, we detail some typical composite materials based on biological materials on the basis of their biological and physicochemical properties, with a focus on various biological-based composite materials, including carbon/biological composite materials, polymer/biological composite materials, metal oxide/biological composite materials and biological hydrogels. As this is an emerging nanoscience research field, we indicate the current limitations of conventional functional nanomaterial-based flexible electronic devices as well as the advantages of using biological materials and biocomposite-based flexible electronic devices for future advancements. Selected examples from a wide variety of application fields, mainly in the field of flexible electronics, indicate how such biological/biosynthetic materials can provide new and advanced solutions to diverse problems. In the last part, we describe future developments and trends of biological materials in flexible electronic devices.

2 Fundamental properties of biological materials

Biomaterials, which are conventionally described as materials utilized in medical devices, have been used since ancient times. However, in the recent time, their levels of complexity have been enhanced considerably. Biomaterials discussed currently are mostly rich in information and feature components that are biologically active and obtained from nature.111,112 Therefore, thorough comprehension of the physicochemical and biological properties of biological materials might inspire further developments in different research fields to hasten diversification and extension as well as expand the potential applications of biological materials in the future. Through natural selection, biological materials have developed remarkable functions that closely approach perfection to adapt to their surrounding environments (Fig. 3). In this section, we overview the fundamental characteristics of biological materials, while acknowledging that not all of the properties of biological materials have been completely defined.

2.1 Hierarchical 3D biostructures

In nature, flora and fauna possess robust hierarchical structures capable of specialized functions. In particular, structure-driven properties serve important functions in nature and have inspired numerous technological innovations.50 Biological materials are complex composites that exhibit structural diversity spanning many orders of magnitude in length.114 Their exceptional properties are believed to be due to a functional adaptation of their structure at all hierarchical levels. Notably, hierarchical 3D structures show remarkable repetition formed from structural design elements such as small fibers, tubes, open- and closed-cell foams and plates. These structures can be classified into eight categories depending on their structural design elements, which have been thoroughly investigated and reviewed by Marc A. Meyers35 and Espionsa et al.:115,116 fibrous structures, helical structures, gradient structures, layered structures, tubular structures, cellular structures, suture structures, and overlapping structures. These categories and the SEM image of various multiscale structures are shown in Fig. 4. For example, wood, which is composed of open spaces (lumens) and interconnected cells (tracheids), is a composite material with a natural cellular structure.92,117 Due to the varied arrangement and interior structure of the spaces, various wood species possess different porous microstructures.118 For example, soft wood species (e.g., pine) are composed of uniform close-packed rectangular cells, which are beneficial to the infiltration of solutions through the uniform porous structure (Fig. 5a).91 In contrast, a large number of close-packed tubular cells with sizes of 1–100 μm, including growth ring boundaries (1), earlywood vessels (2), latewood vessels (3), fibers (4), rays (5), axial parenchyma cells in marginal bands (6), have been found in hard wood species (e.g., poplar) (Fig. 5b).119,120 As evidenced by the unique structural characteristics, biological materials have benefited from their numerous distinct architectural features, which adjust the transport and mechanical properties. Such structural features include their spatial and orientational distribution, their detailed 3D architecture, the geometry of building blocks and the number of hierarchical levels. Natural hierarchical materials (seashells, bones, wood, bamboo, arthropod exoskeletons, and glass sponge skeletons) with high pore volume ratios, large specific surface areas, excellent permeability, high storage capacities, and facile mass transport properties have been used in various applications in catalysis, adsorption, separation, sensors, energy, food, industry and life sciences. For example, a gecko foot is composed of a large number of well-arranged microsized hairs with a density of approximately 500 mm−2, exhibiting a unique multiscale hierarchical structure. Interestingly, these hairs are also composed of numerous smaller hairy nanoscale structures (100–1000 per single hair in density).121,122 The multiscale structural hierarchy in gecko feet allow them to exhibit multiple functions, including their excellent adhesive, superhydrophobic and self-cleaning properties.123–125 Therefore, the hierarchical structures of gecko feet act a strong, versatile and effective adhesive to enable the intriguing ability to run and climb freely on vertical walls (Fig. 5c).111,126,127

Another prime example of a unique hierarchical structure can be found in seashells (e.g., abalone shells), which exhibit two layers with different microstructures (Fig. 5d).134 Such layers are capable of dividing into prismatic calcite layers and inner nacreous aragonite layers. The calcite layer is significantly harder and is more prone to brittle failures. It is also mostly applied in preventing infiltration of the shell. Contrastingly, the inner nacreous layer is softer, and is capable of withstanding larger inelastic deformations. Besides, it is capable of also dissipating considerable mechanical energy owing to the well-organized self-assembled layer on the higher mesoscale level. Its structure comprises a large number of microscopic aragonite polygonal tablets, of about 5–8 μm diameter and 400 μm thickness as well as a layer of organic materials with a thickness of about 20–30 nm.135 Due to its highly prearranged hierarchical structure as well as its mortar structure, the nacreous layer shows exceptional mechanical properties.136–138 As described above, owing to the specific functions of biological materials resulting from their various hierarchical structures, they are promising materials for various applications.

2.2.1 Mechanical properties. The mechanical properties of biological materials include flexibility, stiffness, strength, fracture toughness, etc., and are of importance for their applications in a variety of fields. In this section, we discuss and study the flexibility, toughness and tensile strength of biological materials that are very attractive for wearable “green electronics”. The remarkable characteristic of biological materials is that they display well-ordered 3D hierarchical structures from the nanoscale- to the microscale. Notably, such a unique structure possesses specific properties, among which the microstructure commonly has an important influence on the mechanical properties. This distinct mechanical character allows biological materials to act as active units to meet specific demands. Meyers and co-workers noted that different structural design elements led to different mechanical properties in biological materials.35 For example, chiton exoskeletons139 are composed of well-organized overlapping structures with remarkable mechanical flexibility, consisting of a number of individual scales (Fig. 6a). Even though the geometries as well as the resultant mechanics of the various overlapping structures are capable of varying considerably among species, these exceptional structures can offer all organisms a certain degree of joined potency as well as flexibility.141 For highly efficient flexible electronic devices, natural spider (Arachnida) silk (tens of microns in diameter) is a good example of a hierarchical structure composed of numerous aligned fibers.128 Fibrous structures exhibit a dichotomy of strength, high tension, and low to effectively no compression. 2.2.2 Structural coloring. The various color features of biological organisms, generated via iridescence structural color, pigmentation color, or a superposition of the two classes of colors, have attracted extensive research interest. Among them, structural color is a typical natural characteristic caused by the interaction and transportation of highly precise and periodical hierarchical structures with light. As a result, structural color presents amazing functions and has much more efficient light use and energy consumption, which are unavailable when using pigmentation. Structural color also exhibits unlimited lightfastness and is environmentally friendly due to the lack of chemical dyes. In particular, tunable structural color is used by many living creatures as a natural signal to adapt to the surrounding environment, for example, through communication, predation, camouflage, etc.54,82,166,204,250

Beautiful examples can be found in various butterfly wings, some plant, flowers and leaves, birds, insects, fish, algae, berries, etc. (Fig. 7). Some butterfly wings possess various beautiful and amazing iridescence color due to their well-organized periodic and hierarchical structure. For example, the Morpho sulkowskyi butterfly possesses an alluring dual color on the top and underside surface of its wings that is brilliant blue coloration and camouflaged brown coloration, respectively.251 When they fly, the brilliant blue coloration on the top surface of the wings matches the color of the sky. Similarly, when they land, the ventral side of the wings exhibits a brown color to match their surroundings.126 Such structural color can efficiently avoid predators, such as some birds, snakes, frogs, geckosand lizards.160 Parker and co-workers first discovered that Aphrodite, a type of shallow tropical water worm, possesses bright structural colors. They determined that this color was derived from cylinders that collectively diffract light as the hair of Aphrodite possesses a large number of longitudinally oriented, close-packed and hollow cylinders 230 nm in diameter.97 Gu and co-workers found that some fish (such as paracheirodon innesi) provide another example of the utilization of structural color in response to the surrounding environment.166Paracheirodon innesi alter their structural colors in response to many different external stimuli (e.g., predators, flow fluid, and temperature, etc.), and the variation in the spacing of the platelets in their skin cells is responsible for their interference colors. Generally, Paracheirodon innesi exhibit a structural color of cyan. When chased by pursuers, the structural color of this fish rapidly changes from cyan to yellow due to the simultaneous change in the spacing of adjoining reflecting plates (Fig. 8a). Other external stimuli, such as water, can lead to a change in the structural color. The intense inflow of water on the surface of Paracheirodon innesi can cause the skin cells of the fish to swell, resulting in the increased spacing of adjacent platelets, prompting the swell-induced spacing variation mechanism.168 In addition to some fishes, other insects (e.g., Hercules beetle and tortoise beetle)173,252 also exhibit structural color changes through similar processes (Fig. 8b). Such natural structural color features combined with their special biological functions provide enormous potential for energy, optical and electronic applications.

2.2.3 Hydrophobic properties. In general, hydrophobicity is the most common natural characteristic of biological materials and is extremely dependent on the topography and morphology of the surface, as defined by the Wenzel and Cassie–Baxter states (Fig. 9a). Superhydrophobic materials are in extreme demand due to their various potential applications, such as for printing, microfluidic devices, anti-corrosion coatings, nanoparticle assembly, waterproofing, oil/water separation, batteries, and high-sensitivity sensors and optical devices. Therefore, many synthetic materials have been generated to replicate the 3D hierarchical architecture and biological functions of biological materials over the past decades. Although great advancements have been made, key challenges remain, such as limitations in mimicking or accurately recreating biological materials. Therefore, feasible strategies to gradually replace traditional synthetic materials with biological materials are of extreme significance. Fig. 9b shows that many biological materials existing in nature possess superwettability. For example, lotus leaves show superhydrophobicity with water contact angles of approximately 150° and self-cleaning properties (Fig. 9c).198,199 The Cassie–Baxter equation (cosθ = ψs(cosθY + 1) − 1, where ψs and (1 − ψs) are the solid and air fractions, respectively) can be used to describe the self-cleaning properties, which remove dust and particles through the movement of water droplets.253,254 In addition to the self-cleaning properties, Mele209 and Feng58et al. reported that the surface of Strelitzia reginae and Oryza sativa (rice) leaves, which are composed of parallel microgrooves, exhibit superhydrophobic properties. Due to the anisotropy of superhydrophobic properties, the droplet could be displaced in the direction parallel to the microgrooves. This behavior could be used to develop fluid-transport systems with practical applications. In nature, biological materials always have unique superhydrophobic properties to meet or withstand various living environments.

In our recent work, we fabricated an electronic skin made of pollen, as a biological material with hydrophobic properties, with a water contact angle of approximately 100° and determined that the hydrophobic properties have a significant influence on the electronic signal stability and water resistance (Fig. 9d).50 We also tested the long-term water penetration of pollen materials over 0 to 30 min, and the results showed that the water droplet exhibited no obvious change over time. This result indicated that biological materials have many applications as active units that require both good mechanical properties and hydrophobic properties.

2.2.4 High adhesion. Strong and robust adhesion to almost all types of surfaces is another attractive feature of biological materials. In nature, many biological materials possess robust adhesion, such as biological molecules,257 the skin of animals,174,176 and some plants. As materials with high and strong adhesion or cohesion, they are not expected to delaminate from the substrate, which results in stable devices applicable in various areas. The Dauskardt laboratory extensively described the adhesive and cohesive energies of organic semiconductors and devices.258–261 Adhesion depends on chemical bonding, van der Waals interactions, and chain entanglements.

Polydopamine is the most famous natural adhesive, which relies on both noncovalent and covalent bonding. Polydopamine adheres strongly on the organic surfaces with covalent coupling under alkaline conditions possibly via Michael-type addition reactions or via aryl–aryl coupling.85 This process depends on the oxidation of the catechol present in polydopamine to quinones. Though the binding mechanism is not completely understood, research on polydopamine has not ceased. The robust adhesion of polydopamine has resulted in increasing attention in fabricating hybrid materials and functional substrates (Fig. 10a–c).85 Additionally, for the past ten years, great importance has been attached to gecko lizards for their capacity to adhere to surfaces with different roughnesses and orientations (Fig. 10d and e).98 Such intriguing adhesion properties result from arrays of millions of fine microscopic (setae) foot hairs, which are split into hundreds of nanoscale ends (spatulae), by strong adhesive van der Waals forces to form a conformal connection on different surfaces (Fig. 10f).

2.3 Biocomponents

The numerous functional groups found in biological materials can facilitate the grafting of some chemical species to achieve specific functions (surface functionalization) in an eco-friendly manner. Such surface functionalization allows the surface chemistry of the materials to be tailored to recognize different molecular or external stimuli, for example some specific gases, water, proteins, and bacteria, for bioapplications. The recent chemical analysis of Wang and co-workers further revealed that chitosan is a new class of linear polysaccharides obtained from natural Lebadea Martha parkeri Knight butterfly-wings (Fig. 12a).43 The composition of the amino acids in the polysaccharide chain exhibits high recognition ability for specific gas molecules (Fig. 12b and c). The surface of natural cellulose from, for example, natural wood, plants, tunicate, algae and bacteria possesses a large number of –OH side groups, which allows other types of chemical species (e.g., nanoparticles, proteins, water, organic molecules and so on) to be grafted onto the natural cellulose surface under certain conditions to afford different surface properties.92,267 For instance, a high-strength material was shown to form via hydrogen bonding between the hydroxyl groups of cellulose. In addition, cellulose composites were obtained by submersion in an acrylic resin matrix under a reduced pressure of about 0.09 MPa for 12 h.69 These reactions in natural cellulose are of important significance because they provide a versatile, simple and novel route to improve the material properties including transparency, tensile strength, flexibility, coefficient of thermal expansion, thermal stability and optical properties. Additionally, inverse opals of silk, cellulose and chitosan were shown to be sensitive to infiltration with water, the glucose concentration in aqueous solution, organic solvents, pH, and humidity.268,269 For instance, the chitosan inverse opal's reflectance peak transforms as per the improving absorption of different alcohols in water, from pure water to pure alcohol, with an overall general change of about 100 nm (Fig. 12d).270 Results of a more recent chemical analysis of cuticles (Tetrodontophora bielanensis) performed after molting were consistent with these present results, further confirming the chemical constituents of the vertically layered structure, that is, from outside to inside, a protein-rich epicuticle covering a chitin-rich lamellar procuticle (such as a comb-forming structure) as well as the furthest or the outermost lipid rich layer with a thickness of several nanometers (Fig. 12e).264 The wax-like surface coating had been confirmed indirectly 50 years ago by use of chloroform extraction suggested by Noble-Nesbitt.249 It was also accomplished through lanthanum staining that was proposed by Ghiradella and Radigan.271 These two distinct research studies summarized that the layer is capable of assuring hydrophobicity in wet environments as well as preventing desiccation in arid environments.

2.4 Biocompatibility and biodegradation

Biocompatibility is a crucial component for establishing the appropriateness of a given material for particular applications in the biomedical as well as industrial fields, more so for “green” electronics applications.12 In particular, the continual accruals of non-biocompatibility or non-biodegradable external vehicles are greatly unfavorable to the human body as well as other objects. Therefore, exceptional biodegradability and biocompatibility are vital for the design and advancement of new physiologically friendly materials. Different from the traditional man-made or synthetic materials, biological materials expected to exhibit excellent biocompatibility are widely available in nature. They are capable of considerably decreasing the incidences of severe negative impacts brought about by the man-made or synthetic materials' administration. Polydopamine, a predominant pigment of organically occurring melanin, is composed of several operative groups, including catechol, amine, as well as imine, which makes it biodegradable as well as biocompatible.272 Studies carried out by Liu et al. confirmed that polydopamine-melanin exhibits low toxicity and excellent biocompatibility by observing the healthy behaviours, including exploratory behaviour, activity, grooming, eating, drinking, urination, or neurological status of rats after intravenous injection of a single dose over one-month period.273 These examinations indicated that polydopamine-melanin is not capable of stimulating clear cytotoxic impacts when attached to mouse 4T1 breast cancer cells as well as human cervical cancer cells (HeLa cells), even at extremely high doses. Amazingly, many examinations have shown that polydopamine-coatings encourage cell adhesion as well as propagation on substrates in material-independent ways, which is different from pristine substrates. These studies offer convincing confirmation of the insignificant cytotoxicity of polydopamine. In fact, many biological materials in nature are biocompatible and biodegradable. For example, silk, a natural material that has been widely used for a long time, is composed of sericin and fibroin and is biocompatible,142,274 bioresorbable275 and water soluble,276 with programmable rates of dissolution.277 Huang and co-workers demonstrated silk-based hydrogels with good mammalian cell adhesion and low cytotoxicity to exhibit excellent biocompatibility.278 They conducted live/dead staining to assess the biocompatibility by growing human mesenchymal stem cells (hMSCs) on hydrogel surfaces. As a result, hMSCs seeded on the silk-elastin-like protein (SELP)-based hydrogels exhibited similar adhesion to the control tissue culture plate (TCP) surface and supported cell growth and proliferation for 2 weeks by observing fluorescence imaging of live/dead staining (Fig. 13a). Importantly, all tested surfaces show a healthy growth of hMSCs after 14 d by observing an elongated morphology of hMSCs. The results indicated that the SELP hydrogels have minimal cytotoxicity. Similarly, natural chitosan is also a typical biocompatible and non-toxic material, as verified by Arai and Kumirska. Because these compounds have very low toxicity, the LD50 of chitosan in laboratory mice is 16 g kg−1 body weight, which is similar to that of sugar and salt. In rats, chitosan is safe up to 19% in the diet.42 Biodegradation is necessary for materials used in biomedical applications since the long-term retention of foreign substances in the body is likely to cause severe adverse influences. Polydopamine nanoparticles showed reduced absorption along with color fading, suggesting the degradation of polydopamine nanoparticles. In addition, studies have examined the thorough degradation of melanin by hydrogen peroxide, which produces pyrrole-2,3-dicarboxylic acid and pyrrole-2,3-dicarboxylic acid.279 Furthermore, some microorganisms can produce the enzymatic degradation of polydopamine. For instance, natural proteins, cellulose and starch biological molecules with hydrolyzable linkages are generally susceptible to biodegradation by hydrolytic enzymes produced by microorganisms.280 Wheat gluten-based plastics were reported to fully degrade within 36 days through aerobic fermentation and 50 days in farmland soil, without the production of toxic products during the biodegradation period.281 Similarly, Benjamin P. Partlow et al. reported degradable natural silk materials.282 Silk protein-based hydrogels were implanted subcutaneously in mice and examined histologically at one (Fig. 13d), two (Fig. 13e) and four (Fig. 13f) weeks post-implantation. The results demonstrated that after four weeks of implantation the silk-based hydrogels showed a less distinct interface between the gel and adjacent tissues as the silk gel became integrated and had degraded their shapes. In addition, many biocompatible, bioresorbable, or biodegradable biological materials, such as polyphosphates, polylactides, polyethers, polyesters, and polyols, widely exist in nature. Combining newly emerging synthetic technologies and readily available biological materials will lead to the development of an increasing number of biodegradable and biocompatible biological materials as functional building blocks for various applications.283–285

2.5 Other properties

In addition to the above mentioned alluring properties, other relevant properties of biological materials should also be discussed, such as their lightweight,286 and abrasion-resistant,287,288 paramagnetic,89,289,290 self-healing291 and tailorable optical properties.82 For example, silk, chitin, wood, and juglone are light weight and exhibit good flexibility and roughness. A cellulose-based aerogel possesses a porosity of 99.6% and a density of 5.6 mg cm−3 and exhibits an excellent compressive stress of 8.9 kPa at 80% strain.292 Furthermore, biological materials show various biofunctions of abrasion resistance, such as the radular teeth of chiton, which are composed of closely packed magnetite rod-like structures and are used for grazing algae from rocky surfaces.289 The scales of an ancient Polypterus senegalu fish (P. senegalu) contain rod-like, pseudo-prismatic crystallites of apatite in their ganoine layer and provide a defense mechanism against the attack of predators.293 In addition, melanin also displays paramagnetism and tailored optical properties.294 More importantly, novel functionalities, such as anticounterfeiting, catalytic, electrical, and chemical performance, can be integrated into biological materials. These unique properties afford biological materials with novel uses in a variety of fields.

3 Biological materials as components of hybrid structures

Biological materials have been widely accepted as one of the most attractive candidates for medical materials, as they possess unique biological activity unavailable in bulk materials. However, the intrinsic low conductivity of biological materials remains one of the greatest obstacles to their practical use in electronic materials. Due to their structural and compositional diversity, biological materials can be functionalized with relative ease by numerous synthetic methods. This functionalization may create unprecedented properties and functionalities to meet the demands of specific applications by altering the conductivity, mechanical properties, crystal structure, and solubility. Consequently, the features of biological composites might be elaborated more than those of non-natural materials. In developing high-performance composites motivated by nature's design principles, it is therefore needful to deem the structure–function relationships in hybrid biocomposites, to permit us design and produce novel hybrid biocomposites with most favorable natural architectures as well as functions. In this context, our primary concerns are the rational design and fabrication of hybrid biocomposites to tailor their unique structure, mechanical properties, and complex chemistry at multiple length scales to optimize their functional properties to produce ideal abilities.

Addressing this issue through systematic research will eventually open new directions and opportunities for the development of bioinspired composites with superior functional properties.177 Some examples of biologically derived materials from the latest literature are provided below to emphasize some of the modern efforts to comprehend this topic. Developed classification techniques that have permitted us to further understand the advantages of biological materials for specific functions at multiple length scales are first discussed (Section 3.1), followed by various case studies on the design of ‘smart’ materials combined with natural building blocks to afford outstanding properties (from Sections 3.2–3.5).

3.1 Properties of biological composites

The biological activity of living organisms, biological molecular and natural materials to assemble biological composites with remarkable properties using multiple weak hydrophobic effects, hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions, etc. chemical interactions or physical methods (e.g., freeze-drying)134 has produced a series of structural, chemical, and functional advantages, as follows:

(1) The natural topographic structures of biological building blocks at multiple length scales are designed to optimize the functional properties, which can provide biocomposites with excellent mechanical properties, hydrophobic surfaces with self-cleaning properties, and so on. Such structural features include, for instance, their detailed 3D architecture, the number of hierarchical levels, the topographic structures of the building blocks, the type and distribution of chemical bonds, and their spatial and orientational distribution.

(2) Modifying the chemical structure is a facile route to meet the demands of specific applications by adjusting the mechanical properties, conductivity, electron transfer rates, solubility, capacity, and crystal structure. For example, biological materials (e.g., pollen, butterfly, wood) coated with carbon materials (e.g., graphene, carbon nanotube, carbon dots) show excellent conductivity and offer ultrasensitive sensing platforms for the development of biosensors, including enzyme biosensors, protein biosensors, cancer biosensors, and air pollutant sensors.

(3) The multiple adjustable functions of biological materials (e.g., their light weight, natural abundance, biocompatibility, bioresorbability, and biodegradability) are ideal to produce new functional systems. The introduction of new properties derived from nature can endow biocomposites with multiple functions, for example, facile charge transfer, highly effective photon-harvesting and fast molecular transport. Hybrids with multiple functions can be designed to create materials with the properties of biospecies, nanomaterials, and atoms. As such, the combination could achieve “synergistic” results.

Biological composites can provide site-specific structure–function relationships that satisfy various demands and are environmentally friendly, thus providing an economic strategy to develop a novel biological composite with external functional properties by making efficient use of natural building blocks. Moreover, composites allow for the site-specific adjustment of the mechanical, optical, and electronic properties of a material to fit various applications. Examples of biological composites produced through the intimate incorporation of inorganic and organic building blocks are mineralized fish scales, teeth, byssus threads, mollusc shells, and bone.

3.2 Carbon–biomaterial composite materials

Carbon nanomaterials, including carbon nanotubes (CNTs) graphene (GO and rGO), and carbon dots, are particularly promising for various applications due to their unique electronic properties, mechanical properties, and chemical stability.295,296 First, both CNTs and graphene are molecular chemicals made entirely of sp2-hybridized carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb lattice, thus offering natural compatibility with biological materials.297,298 Second, these crystalline carbon nanomaterials exhibit extraordinary electronic properties with high stability and chemical flexibility due to their π–π conjugated skeletons.299–301 Third, they are easily produced over large areas through bottom-up chemical approaches and can be easily micro-/nanofabricated onto a wide range of substrates with high accuracy. Finally, another unique feature of these nanocarbons results from the fact that their low-dimensional nature is on the molecular scale, ensuring the number of bridged molecules down to the single-molecule level. Due to these features, carbon nanomaterials are well suited as nanoscale electrode materials for flexible electronics.302

Wang and co-workers first reported the preparation of a 3D hierarchical and hollow biological composite composed of biological L. clavatum sporopollenin pollen grains with reduced graphene oxide, rGO, as shown in Fig. 14a.39 In the work, a thin layer (<10 nm) of rGO was coated on the surface of the L. clavatum sporopollenin exine capsules using a multiple weak interaction method (e.g., electrostatic interactions and hydrogen bonds). The structure and properties of the biological composite were statistically studied. The natural cellular structure of the L. clavatum sporopollenin exine capsules was retained, and the biological composite was highly conductive. Notably, this synthetic method can also produce other graphene-based biocomposites, such as graphene-sunflower pollen,18i.e., sunflower pollen/graphene with a perfect natural hierarchical structure and high conductivity. In addition to hierarchically structured pollen, some electro-active carbonyl compounds have been employed as a class of electrode materials, as shown in Fig. 14b.76 For example, natural juglone with redox-active quinone carbonyl groups, derived from walnut epicarp, has been proven to be capable of sodium-ion insertion/desertion and reversible electron transport (Fig. 14b). A rationally designed rGO/juglone biological material was fabricated by a facile self-assembly method without any additional conductive and binder agent. Natural juglone biological molecules were immobilized on rGO via noncovalent bonding with π–π interactions,303–306 which was confirmed via optical and photoelectron spectral analysis. Interestingly, this biological composite could prevent the dissolution of organic materials in the electrolyte and improve both the conductivity of the electrode and the sodium-ion accessibility.307 Additional research into carbon nanotubes (including MWCNTs and SWCNTs) and their integration into natural materials has provided a synthetic route involving weak hydrogen-bonding interactions. Recently, Wang and co-workers reported a synthetic method using weak hydrogen-bonding interactions for the fabrication of MWCNT-based biological composites.50 Structural features such as the natural hierarchical and hollow structure were shown in this MWCNT-based biological composite. Because of the tunable electronic properties of MWCNTs, the MWCNT-based biological composite is predicted to be an attractive material for electrical applications. One year later, they further used MWCNTs with PDMS and sunflower pollen to design a novel PDMS/MWCNT/pollen biological composite using the same synthetic method with a weak hydrogen-bonding interaction synthetic method (Fig. 14c). The porous MWCNT-based biological composite films exhibit high conductivity, permitting a response to external pressure stimuli, thus resulting in a significant development for the application of flexible electronic devices. Furthermore, honeycomb-shaped single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs) were combined with Morpho sulkowskyi butterfly wings.251 These honeycomb-shaped SWCNT/(M. sulkowskyi) butterfly wing biological composites exhibited multiple functions with high electrical conductivity and laser-induced remote heating due to the highly intertwined, conductive and photothermal properties of the SWCNT/honeycomb networks.

Carbon quantum dots (QDs) have been extensively explored to endow biological materials with novel functionalities to greatly expand the applications of biological materials.179,308–311 For instance, Lee et al. reported the combination of photoluminescent graphene quantum dots (GQDs) with mussel-inspired polydopamine biological composites fabricated by means of catechol chemistry.312 The coated biological composite exhibits an excellent and stable photoluminescence intensity, which can be well retained for 14 days in diverse buffers solutions, including those under acidic, neutral, and basic pH conditions. In contrast, the initial photoluminescence intensity of GQDs without polydopamine was reduced by 45% after 14 days of incubation in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) solution. The polydopamine coating was shown to protect GQDs from protonation, which leads to an improvement in the photoluminescence steadiness. Most significantly, the polydopamine coating predominantly improved the biocompatibility of GQDs. After administering into mice, the GQDs with polydopamine-coated biocomposites exhibited excellent long-term stable luminescence signals in the organs as compared to the GQDs without coating. Luminescent carbon nanodots were also coated on cellulose-based paper by an immersion method. They utilized the water-jet printing technology to create ink-free printing luminescent paper and may be made for anti-counterfeiting and information storage fields.313

3.3 Polymer–biomaterial composites

To combine biological activities for the creation of novel active materials, many polymers have been introduced into biological materials systems. These biological composite systems have found abundant applications in biosensing, energy, antibacterial action, etc., as revealed in Section 5. Huang and Chen et al. presented a well-controlled coating process for the fabrication of a natural oligo-proanthocyanidin/amphiphilic polydimethylsiloxane-modified N,O-carboxylic chitosan (PMSC) biological composite.105 The resultant oligo-proanthocyanidin-decorated polymer composite had a biostable 3D anti-inflammatory neural interface, as the semi-hydrophilic OPC can act as a structural stabilizer due to its abundant hydroxyl groups and aromatic architecture. In addition, PMSC self-assembled into spherical network nanostructures with sizes of approximately 150–200 nm, where the inner intertextures composed of hydrophobic and hydrophilic biological composites showed both large surface areas and biological structures. Because polymers can be tailored to provide excellent conductivity, in a very recent study, Chen et al. reported a simple route to fabricate juglone molecule/polypyrrole (PPy) biological composites using galvanostatic polymerization.73 Compared to smooth bare carbon fibers, the galvanostatic polymerization produced interlayer interactions between the carbon fiber and juglone/PPy, thus yielding a non-uniform facial layer consisting of granular clusters. PPy is a conductive polymer that possesses good charge storage capabilities, promoting the fast redox charge transfer of juglone by using its high electronic conductivity.

In addition to using biological molecules as simple building blocks in composite matrices, other biomolecules, including polysaccharides, proteins, viruses, DNA, and peptides, have been employed to prepare biological composites using a simple synthetic method. For example, among the many different organic templates for various nanomaterial fabrication and design strategies, M13 viruses have been given special attention because of their attractive and alluring properties, such as a high geometric aspect ratio with diameters of approximately 6 nm and lengths of approximately 880 nm, genetic tunability of the surface protein, and easy replicability.315 Recently, Zhang and co-workers developed a facile route to prepare biological composites through surface bonding and polymerization to produce polymethylacrylic acid (PMAA) on the surface of natural butterfly wings (Morpho), as illustrated in Fig. 15.95 Initially, the MAA monomer is introduced into the Morpho butterfly wing biotemplate which would interact through weak hydrogen bonding between the amino groups (–NH2) of chitosan in Morpho butterfly wings and the carboxyl groups (–COOH) of the MAA monomer. Subsequent PMAA was homogeneously coated on the hierarchical 3D architectures of the Morpho butterfly wings through a polymerization process. This biological material is structurally stable, and this work highlights a strategy for the development and design of a novel class of biocomposites with honeycomb and hierarchical 3D structures and provides an effective way for integrating functional polymers with biological materials for potential use in many fields.

3.5 Bio-hydrogels

Biological materials based on biohydrogels are composed of hydrophilic networks that are renewable, biocompatible, biodegradable, and of low cost and are potentially useful for various applications (such as sensors, biological devices, and separation systems).327,328 Different methods are available to fabricate biohydrogels.329 Two main synthetic methods are demonstrated in this section and can be distinguished according to different cross-linking reactions, physical synthetic methods and chemical polymerization methods. A large number of biological-based hydrogels have been successfully prepared, such as chitosan, polydopamine, silk, cellulose, peptide, fibrin, collagen, actin and so on. 4 Biological materials for flexible electronic devices

Inorganic materials have applications in various fields of modern technology, such as energy, sensors, LED or flexible displays, lasers, detectors, catalysis, circuit elements, micro-fabricated memory elements, light-emitting diodes, photodiodes, solar cells, etc.1,331 However, applications of these intensively studied inorganic nanomaterials are frequently limited in some particular and industrial fields owing to their harsh synthesis, high cost, cytotoxicity, and non-degradability. In addition, developments in the fields like nanomedicine as well as bioelectronics, together with the imminent upheaval of ‘ubiquitous sensor networks’ need novel functional materials which are biocompatible, inexpensive, mechanically strong and adaptable enough for incorporation into tissues, building structures, fabrics as well as other hosts, apart from having least implanted manufacturing energy and very low power consumption. Nature is an exceptionally effective energy consumption engine that we can utilize for infinite inspirations. The diversity of biological materials offers a large number of unexplored possibilities to researchers, and has already started opening new opportunities in materials engineering, physics, and chemistry fields among other fields. Thus, in this section we focus primarily on advancements in conventional functional nanomaterials-based flexible electronic devices and discuss the current problems that limit the use of these conventional functional nanomaterials in the context of flexible electronic devices. After addressing the above issues, we summarize the current progress in creating biological materials derived from nature with various advantages and benefits in flexible electronic devices.

4.1 Problems of conventional functional nanomaterial-based flexible electronic devices: limited mechanical flexibility, high cost and biocompatibility

Flexible and wearable devices should preferably be flexible, easy to produce, biocompatible, inexpensive, lightweight, and multifunctional sensing platforms.291,332–334 Much attention is being paid on how to endow wearable devices with as much new functionalities as possible. Due to the increasingly high standards and demands for flexible, high-performance and environmentally friendly electronic devices, flexibility and biocompatibility, in particular, are being given much attention. Namely, high mechanical flexibility and biocompatibility not only allow conformation to curved and dynamic surfaces and the creation of compatible interfaces with curvilinear and soft biological tissues/organs with seamless fidelity, but also alleviate serious adverse effects and potential detriments resulting from the long-term retention of foreign substances in the body.48 Therefore, high mechanical flexibility and biocompatibility are required for the development of next-generation flexible and wearable electronics, especially for biological-related applications, such as biomedical diagnosis and therapy, electronic skin sensors, and brain/machine interfaces. In fact, functional nanomaterials have been widely investigated and employed in flexible and wearable devices over the past few decades due to their excellent electrical, structural, and optical properties. Even so, these intensively studied functional nanomaterials have limited applications in many fields, such as in food, medicine, biology, and large-scale industrial applications, due to their harsh and complicated fabrication steps and methods, low mechanical flexibility, high cost, toxicity, non-compatibility, and non-degradability.90

(1) Specifically, the limited availability of mineral resources hardly satisfies the increasing requirements and subsequent production quantity of energy-storage devices over the long term, which will necessarily result in price increases.

(2) The non-biodegradability of current electronic and energy-storage devices after their service lifetime will produce tremendous amounts of electronic garbage.

(3) Another issue encountered with current electronic and energy-storage devices arises from the laborious preparation processes of these synthetic materials and man-made functional materials, which usually require extreme conditions, which limits their low-cost large-scale fabrication capability.

(4) Although various 3D synthetic biomimetic man-made functional materials have been produced with much progress, key challenges remain, with limitations in mimicking or accurately recreating biological materials to obtain excellent mechanical flexibility.

The flexibility of functional nanomaterials is strongly dependent on their mechanical features, relating exactly to the structure as well as the size of the building blocks.336 Due to the yield stress of the crystalline material being inversely proportional to the square root of the grain size in the structural hierarchy, the innately brittle nanostructured materials are capable of producing functional nanomaterials with tunable efficient elastic modules. In particular, most bulk functional materials exhibit extremely limited deformability to failure. Therefore, excellent deformability with a low elastic modulus is required to improve the mechanical properties. Well-organized hierarchical and porous structures benefit the creation of mechanically flexible materials for a wide variety of applications, especially for flexible electronic skins that respond to external stimuli, which is one of the most common applications.32 There are numerous synthetic approaches and strategies for preparing multiscale hierarchical surfaces or 3D porous structures. The existing methods can be divided into various bottom-up, top-down, and hybrid approaches.126 Studies into these approaches suggest the formation of multilevel and complex structures, for example, honeycomb, multishell hollow,32,50 urchin-like,337 brush-like,304 flower-like,338,339 tree-like,340 and branched structures.341,342

The fabrication of conventional functional nanomaterials usually has high energy requirements and releases toxic materials, which pollute the environment. Therefore, the high cost and limited biocompatibility hinder the widespread use of these synthetic functional materials in many fields. In particular, the lasting buildup of non-biocompatible or non-biodegradable external vehicles is greatly disadvantageous to the human body as well as other objects. Therefore, exceptional biodegradability as well as biocompatibility is crucial in the design and advancement of novel physiologically friendly materials.283 One of the most promising strategies for obtaining environmental benignity as well as high performance, in addition to the low-cost large-scale fabrication capability, is by using a class of biocompatible and biodegradable materials. Biocompatible materials can meaningfully reduce the occurrence of serious adverse effects caused by the administration of man-made or synthetic materials. In most cases, nature-inspired functional materials are verified to possess good biocompatibility. Depending on the chemical composition and natural properties, various biomimetic polymer materials can be employed, such as chitosan, cellulose, paper, etc.343–349 One of the most interesting potentials of biocompatible materials is their employment in biointegrated electronic devices for biological-related applications, such as brain/machine interfaces, biomedical diagnosis and therapy, and sensor skins.

Though many useful strategies have been developed to address these problems over the past few decades, many challenges remain in developing new materials with low cost, excellent mechanical flexibility, and biocompatibility. Therefore, the key focus in this field is the development of materials with as many new properties and functions as possible.

4.2 Benefits of biological materials as active components in flexible electronic devices

Considering the numerous unique properties of biological materials, it should come as no surprise that they are currently, without a doubt, the most extensively studied materials for a wide range of applications, not only as convenient active synthetic precursors but also as complex actuators for electronic devices. Hitherto, several notable reviews have emphasized the advantages of using biological materials (such as silk, wood, polydopamine, etc.) in flexible and wearable electronic, energy, biological devices.12,48,89,91,351,352 What exactly makes biological materials interesting and beneficial subjects for biological reasons? As we discussed in previous sections, biological materials are structurally and functionally diverse with outstanding potential for fine-tuning, and these materials can serve as active building blocks and are easily functionalized through numerous synthetic methods. Thus, one may create combinations of multiple functional properties, including superhydrophobicity, biocompatibility, biodegradation, good mechanical properties, abundant renewable supply, etc., and these properties are not the limit. Applications of biologically active biological materials or biological-based composites in flexible electronic devices could overcome the problems of conventional synthetic materials:

(1) The unique hierarchical structures provide greater surface area and tailorable mechanical behaviors compared to other man-made or synthetic materials. On one hand, the high surface area ensures effective contact of the external stimulus (electrolyte, biomarkers, gas molecules, etc.) with the material surface, facilitating charge transfer across the sensing layer–target molecules interface. On the other hand, the diverse mechanical properties allow the creation of materials with tunable effective elastic moduli, thus endowing them with high performance.

(2) Highly organized hierarchical porous nanostructures create unique functionalities, such as structural color, superhydrophobicity, selective filtration capacity, directional adhesion, antireflective characteristics, etc., which endows their flexible and wearable electronic devices with multifunctionality or versatility.

(3) Biocompatibility, biodegradability, and lightweight characteristics reduce the risk for human environmental exposure as well as reduce the discomfort of users, allowing the continuous and long-term use of flexible and wearable electronic devices, thus endowing them with good long-term stability together with environmental friendliness.

(4) The abundant renewable supply and low cost of biological materials make them promising candidates for the development of flexible and wearable electronic devices for various applications and endow them with low-cost large-scale production capability.

(5) A large number of active groups on the surface of natural biological materials are easily functionalized and modified through many synthetic methods. As a consequence, natural biofunctional surfaces often have to fulfill several functions, which are achieved through their highly tailorable composition and structure. Changing the chemical structures is an ultimate way of meeting the needs of particular applications by transforming the capacity, solubility, crystal structure, electron-transfer rate, ionic conductivity, as well as mechanical features.

(6) The hierarchical structures are clasped through a combination of strong as well as weak tunable bonds in affording excellent fixed properties whereas still capable of adapting, remodeling, and self-healing their architecture and exploring other design spaces in regard to vigorously transforming external stimuli.

(7) The building blocks are collected into multiscale hierarchical structures, and then respond considerably to the external stimuli (such as the mechanical stress) for further improving the functional features of the material.

In the following sections, we describe recent progress in the application of biological materials or biological-based composites in flexible or wearable electronic devices.

5 Application of natural biomaterials as active components

Based on the above advantages of biological materials as active components in composites, in this section, we summarize current progress in the application of these materials in flexible and wearable electronics. We focus primarily on the fabrication of flexible substrate systems derived from nature (Section 5.1) and then outline some applications in biosensors (Section 5.2), electronic sensors (Section 5.3), energy conversion and storage devices (Section 5.4), and other applications (Section 5.5).

5.1 Flexible substrates

Substrates play an important role in electronic devices, such as flexible displays, energy, and sensing outlets. Flexible plastics, rubbers, and polymers (e.g., poly(ethylene terephthalate) (PET), polyimide (PI), and poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS)) are widely used as substrates due to their great mechanical flexibility, good chemical resistance, stability, etc.355,356 Unfortunately, most current substrates are nonbiodegradable, nonrecyclable and have low biocompatibility. For example, plastic requires 450 years to decompose. However, the lifetime of many sensors and flexible displays, such as cellphones, is on average 18 months.357 As a result, electronic applications generate a large amount of waste. With further understanding of sustainable development, biodegradable and abundant materials are expected to replace nonbiodegradable materials in flexible electronic device applications.358 In this case, the two key issues to be considered when designing flexible electronics are (1) overcoming the mechanical instability of active films subjected to strain/stress and (2) identifying suitable substrates and permeation barrier layers to allow operational stability and resistance to degradation.

Highly mechanically flexible, robust and biodegradable substrates have been intensively investigated to reduce waste and minimize environmental pollution. In addition, biocompatibility and biodegradability are critical for biofunctional and bioimplantable electronics. Biological materials are the largest material systems in nature, with the features of excellent biocompatibility, biodegradability, versatility, sustainability, and low cost. Various biological materials can be used to make substrates exhibit excellent transparency, flexibility, structural robustness with small thermal expansion coefficients, stable mechanical flexibility and surface roughness, as illustrated in Fig. 19 and 20. For example, Rogers reported in 2012 that silk is a desirable natural polymer for biocompatible passive support and packaging materials for flexible and wearable electronics because it is mechanically robust, flexible, insulating, optically transparent, biocompatible, and amenable to chemical and biological functionalization.11 The inspiration to use silk as a substrate material first came from Bombyx mori cocoons.5,359 It was demonstrated that Mg inductive coils joined with other resistive microheaters made up of doped Si nanomaterials that are incorporated on silk substrates and then housed in silk packages, which are capable of providing transitory thermal therapy for the control of surgical site infections as nonantibiotic, programmable bactericidal appliqué that vanishes when the patient improves past the greatest risk period (Fig. 19a and b). Furthermore, the natural silk has significant biodegradability as well as bioresorbability features (Fig. 19c–e), which permits its programmable dissolution as well as its biodegradation rates and formation of noninflammatory degradation products, together with adaptable processability under favorable conditions, as shown in Fig. 19d. Accordingly, silk as an efficient medium has been widely applied in implantable devices which can transfer active electronic modules onto different objects' surfaces including the tooth, brain, skin as well as other organs in performing enviable functions with conformal contact.

5.2 Biosensors

Biosensors are analytical devices, representing one of the most important and widely investigated areas for the application of biological materials. Biological materials have become famous because they display unique biological activities with remarkable hydrophilicity and biocompatibility and can undergo secondary reactions with various molecules, providing an important sensing platform to produce a variety of hybrid materials with certain functionalities. Therefore, a review by Cespedes emphasized the use of biological composite materials in biosensors. However, biological materials feature favorable physicochemical versatility, which results in numbers of biological and biomedical applications. Hence, different biological materials and biological-based composites are summarized in this section in terms of biosensor applications.

5.2.2 Flexible electrochemical biosensors. Many biological materials, as ideal materials for electrochemical biosensors, have also been widely studied for flexible electrochemical biosensors due to their excellent biocompatibility and robust mechanical flexibility. While biological materials exhibit great promise, biological-based composites have recently attracted great attention for their applicability to electrochemical sensing. Particularly, various carbon materials, including carbon nanotubes, graphene, and semiconductor nanoparticles, are already widely used in electrochemical biosensors.

Recently, Javey and co-workers reported fully integrated wearable biosensor array based CNT/chitosan biocomposite materials for the detection of electrolytes (such as sodium and potassium ions) and sweat metabolites (such as glucose and lactate) based on the skin temperature (Fig. 23a).110 A photograph of the wearable electrochemical biosensor and a schematic of the electric circuit are illustrated in Fig. 23b and c. In particular, chitosan is an ideal candidate for the immobilization of bioactive molecules onto electrodes as it has excellent biocompatibility and film-forming capabilities.362,363 As a result, this biological composite film exhibited a high sensitivity for glucose and lactate of 2.35 nA μM−1 and 220 nA mM−1, respectively (Fig. 23d). Moreover, the biosensor displayed excellent long-term stability and good repeatability, as the sensitivities of the biosensors were consistent over a period of at least four weeks. Furthermore, the effect of mechanical deformation was studied by detecting the mechanical properties of both the flexible printed circuit board (FPCB) and the sensor array in different bent states with radii of curvature of 1.5 and 3 cm, respectively, and a negligible signal change and robust mechanical flexibility were observed. As a result, this work can realize personalized medicine (Fig. 23e). The resulting chitosan-based composite sensing film exhibited good reproducibility with 4.7% standard deviation for six successive measurements, an amperometric response with a linear range from 2 to 14 mM, and a low limit of detection of 180 μM for glucose.

5.3 Electronic sensors

In recent scientific and technological developments, increased attention has been given to environmentally friendly electronic devices. In particular, ‘green’ electronics is not only an emerging area of research but also a novel scientific field aimed at identifying compounds of natural origin and establishing economically efficient routes for the production of synthetic materials applicable to environmentally safe (biodegradable) and/or biocompatible devices.12 Natural biological materials, including various flora and fauna, such as plants, flower spores, butterflies, natural living particles, bacteria, DNA, proteins, etc., are earth abundant, sustainable, biocompatible, strong, biodegradable, and chemically accessible for modification, which offers abundant advantage for applications in ‘green’ flexible electronic devices. Research into the emerging class of ‘green’ electronics based on biological materials may not only deliver low-cost and energy efficient materials and devices but may also afford unimaginable functionalities by integrating benign biological materials or biological-based composites into daily life and the environment. This section highlights recent research advancements in emerging biological or biological-based composite materials and their integration in various flexible electronic devices.
5.3.1 Electronic skin sensors. Due to its ability to sense tactile forces, electronic skin (e-skin) is able to simulate the properties of human skin and enhance the capabilities of human skin by incorporating different sensors that are integrated with elastomeric and/or plastic substrates, allowing the signal capture and property simulation of the mechano- and thermo-receptors of skin tissues.366–368 Human skin is considered a benchmark for the performance of artificial tactile systems. Recently, the growth of e-skin based on material selection and the capability of transferring the mechanical properties (flexibility, low modulus and stretchability) of human skin to an artificial counterpart, such as conducting polymers,32,369–371 carbon materials372,373 and low-dimensional materials,374,375 has been considered. Natural biomaterials have diverse mechanical properties and, with proper material choice, can have tunable effective elastic moduli to achieve high performance. Recently, Wang et al. designed flexible pixel-type e-skin arrays that are highly sensitive and responsive in a very short time (Fig. 24).50 Their work demonstrated the development of a wearable, highly sensitive e-skin sensor based on the integration of natural elastic sunflower pollen microcapsules within hollow and hierarchical structures (Fig. 24a and b). The device functions well in discriminating between a variety of spatiotemporal tactile stimuli under both dynamic and static pressure states and exhibits a low-enough detection limit (1.6 Pa). A flexible 12 × 8 pixel sensor array 18 × 12 cm2 in size integrated with a 3D rubber composite film on a micro-structured PDMS substrate was also fabricated. Pressure changes in the contact region can be mapped by recording the relative current changes, as demonstrated by installing the sensor array on a human hand and other objects (e.g., the letters ‘N’, ‘T’, and ‘U’) (Fig. 24d–f). According to their explanation, noticeable changes in the resistance result from the tunable effective elastic modulus of the hollow spheres. Importantly, because SFP possesses natural superhydrophobic properties and a strong hierarchical structure, the resulting interlocked structure enables resistance to water absorption, as well as other structural and functional properties that are characteristic of hydrophobic surfaces (Fig. 24g). Therefore, the device displayed high stability, and the inclusion of the natural microcapsules imparted hydrophobic characteristics that facilitated waterproofing for all-weather use.

Biocompatibility is a significant consideration for such devices because applications of e-skins are closely related to biological interfaces. Ideally, e-skins should be fabricated from highly biocompatible precursors; however, biological reactions in the human body are so complex that the prediction of the interaction of a single substance with the body is difficult. For instance, CNTs have been identified to exhibit asbestos-like pathogenic behavior because of their needle-like morphology and small volume.376 Similarly, some reports have also demonstrated that high concentrations of CNTs are highly toxic to cells.377 Further, other reports have indicated that the immunogenic response due to the incorporation of CNTs into electronic devices is dependent on the nano-topology.355 In fact, the determination of biocompatibility involves many factors, such as the surface charge, concentration, morphology, chemical composition, and pH, indicating that the careful control of material design and proper in vivo testing are crucial to determine the biocompatibility of a particular material in a given application. A safe method to achieve biocompatibility is to choose materials that occur naturally, such as silk, chitosan, starch, and DNA. Silk fibroin has attracted wide attention for flexible electronic devices due to its matrix.48 In 2017, Zhang and co-workers fabricated a skin-like pressure sensor incorporated with silk fibroin-derived active materials through a large-scale and low-cost method (Fig. 24h).365 Flexible and positive pressure sensors can be produced from unstructured PDMS films and transparent carbonized silk nanofiber membranes through a large-scale and cost-effective method. As a result of the incorporation of unique N-doped carbon nanofibers with the interconnected structure of CSilkNM, the sensor displayed superior performance and showed several advantages, including an ultra-high sensitivity over a broad pressure range (34.47 kPa−1, 0.8–400 Pa and 1.16 kPa−1, 400–5000 Pa), an ultra-low detection limit of 0.8 Pa, a fast response time of <16.7 ms, and good durability and stability over >1000 cycles, showing a comprehensive advantage over previously reported pressure sensors. The advantageous performance of this device allows applications in the monitoring of human physiological signals, perceived micro-touches, the spatial distribution of pressure and other aspects (Fig. 24i).

In another study, Yang et al. reported a low-cost, simply prepared, and flexible graphene-silk pressure sensor based on a soft silk substrate via thermal reduction.378 In their work, silk was used as the support, and the device formed a 3D orderly multi-layer structure allowing for the detection of a large range of pressures. In addition, molecules such as DNA were also adopted as dielectric materials,379 and naturally sensing platforms (e.g., carotenoid polyenes and indigo) were used as flexible substances.380 In addition, due to the stable hydrogen-bonding π-stacked structure of natural dyes, the presence of hydrogen-bonded analogues of linear acenes was recently reported.381 Transient electronics, which can be decomposed or re-absorbed into the human body at a programmed rate, is an additional concept resulting from the synthesis of natural compounds.382,383 For instance, Bao et al. demonstrated a biocompatible and biodegradable pressure sensor patch composed of a capacitive sandwich structure with a biodegradable material, poly(glycerol sebacate), as the dielectric layer and Mg metal as the electrode (Fig. 25a).13 The device can be completely degraded after use, thus avoiding further surgical interference and reducing the generation of waste. Resorbable biological substrates can also play a key role in interactions with the body. Kim and co-workers created devices for cardiac electrophysiological and electrocorticography instruments on mechanically compliant matrices (Fig. 25b).5 The adaptability of flexible sensors was further enhanced through the use of bioresorbable silk substrates, which can be deposited on the surface of the brain or implanted into the brain. In general, great advancements have been achieved in the development of biodegradable and biocompatible equipment, enabling the integration of e-skin with the human body.

5.3.2 Flexible photodetectors. Photoelectric detectors with broadband light detection have been employed in biomedical imaging, remote sensing, and spectral and optical communications.2,385,386 In recent years, the biological response to light in organisms such as frogs, beetles, and octopuses has inspired the combination of intelligent materials with delicate optical structures in various biological materials or artificial sensors, leading to better performances over conventional photoelectric detectors.173,387,388 Moreover, the inorganic/biomolecule nanohybrids can be inorganic nano-structure and excellent electronic and optical properties of biological molecules and the combination of optoelectronics, its performance than conventional material performance can be realized. Biological macromolecules, such as DNA and proteins, are promising alternatives to synthetic polymers, and their heterojunctions, formed through covalent bonding with carbon and other nanostructures or noncovalent bonding, show great advantages. Therefore, inorganic/biomolecule optoelectronic nanohybrids allow atomic-scale to device-scale design of photon absorption, exciton dissociation, and charge-carrier transfer and transport by combining the unique electronic and optoelectronic properties of the materials. These nanohybrids present great opportunity for new optoelectronic devices with high quantum efficiency. In a recent study, Wu and co-workers explored the biomolecule cytochrome c (Cyt c, PDB ID: 1HRC) adsorbed on s-SWCNTs to form a molecular block.389 The high response rate and high external quantum efficiency (EQE) obtained in near-infrared (NIR) detection were as high as 90% due to the high exciton absorption of s-SWCNTs and the effective exciton separation of the sSWCNT/Cyt c interface. Although the EQEs obtained in previously reported studies of CNT-based nanocomposites show improvements of over two orders of magnitude, several basic problems remain. Other biomolecules, such as peptides and amino acids, have excellent charge-transport properties and great molecular dipoles that play a large role in the operation of electronic devices. Deng and co-workers adopted a mild, environmentally friendly strategy to create GO sheets functionalized with amino acids (Fig. 26a–c).384 The novel compounds were extensively adjusted with poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):polystyrene (PEDOT:PSS) to match the energy levels of various semiconductors. The functionalized organic photodetector shows the highest normalization capability at −0.1 V.

Cellulosic structures, such as paper, have recently gained research interest for wearable photodetectors. Although polyethylene (PET) films and glass are still employed in the industry, the structure of cellulose makes it possible to lower the manufacturing cost and impart environmental friendliness. Lin et al. demonstrated a simple sandwich structure to fabricate a flexible UV photodetector based on ZnO nanocrystals on cellulosic materials (Fig. 26d–f).68 The active material was embedded on both sides of the structure. Flexible photodetectors have good sensing properties for UV light, and the large volume ratio of such structures benefits the adhesion of active nanomaterials, thereby reducing the deposition of materials. The high porosity from the staggered cellulose structure also allows light scattering through the structure, thereby increasing the absorption of the embedded active material. There is no doubt that due to the large number of bio-cellulose structures in nature and the developed man-made cellulose structure in the industry, it is possible to manufacture such equipment with a wider UV detection range at a lower cost.

5.3.3 Flexible transistors. In recent years, field effect transistors (FETs) have been one of the most important and basic components in electronic devices and have been a hotspot research topic.1,391 FETs have great potential in low-cost, large-field and flexible electronic devices.355,392 In recent years, biomaterials, such as silk, cellulose nanofibers and other composite biomaterials, have been extensively studied as building blocks for flexible FETs.18,48,91,393 In addition to the effects of inorganic/organic materials in the active layer, the nature of the dielectric material and its interface with the active layer play important roles in determining the mobility and operating voltage.394,395 Moving away from oxide dielectrics, such as SiO2 and Al2O3, in flexible transistors is not only cost effective but also provides great advantages in improving the mobility and stability of the equipment. To achieve a stable and low operating voltage for a flexible transistor, the active surface should be hydrophobic and must provide a relatively high capacitance, which is usually a challenge for many polymer dielectrics because they have inherently low dielectric constants. For example, Guha and co-workers employed diphenylalanine nanostructures as the gate dielectric.396 In their study, the nanostructure form of the peptide layer is a necessary condition to resist the bias pressure effect. The on–off ratio and the loading rate of switching were determined by using 102 and 2.5–3 cm2 V−1 as the dielectric layers, respectively. This device had a stable performance, even in air, over a few days. In addition, to further improve the organic field-effect transistor (OFET) performance, they incorporated nanotube structures and reduced the thickness of the layer, which greatly reduced the surface roughness and improved the interface between the conjugated molecules/polymer and the dielectric layer. The combination of organic and inorganic semiconductors demonstrates a new group of biocompatible materials for organic electronics.

Another interesting biomaterial is cellulosic fiber, which can be employed as a substrate for transistors or functional dielectric layers. Nanofibrillated cellulose (NFC)-based nanopaper is a renewable and environmentally friendly material. Nanopaper transistors have the unique features of very prominent light transmittance and flexibility. Hu et al. demonstrated a flexible OFET with high transparency fabricated on specially made nanotubes and observed beneficial good mechanical flexibility and electrical characteristics (Fig. 27a).67 The large binding energy between the cellulose nanomaterials and the polymer medium, as well as the effective stress release of the fibrous substrate, is believed to promote these beneficial properties. Interestingly, only a 10% difference was observed in the nanopaper transistors in a bent and folded state. The optical transmittance of the nanopaper transistors was as high as 83.5%. The flexible bioelectronic device configuration can be altered by employing many different semiconductor materials (Fig. 27b and c). In another study, Zhang and co-workers reported a MoS2 phototransistor fabricated on a flexible, transparent, biodegradable substrate with an electrolyte gate dielectric (Fig. 27d).390 The device used highly transparent, bendable, biodegradable nanomaterials as the support and passivating layers. The device showed a very good light reflectance of approximately 1.5 with 1/10 nW of lighting power, which is much higher than that in other reports of typical backdoor phototransistors (Fig. 27e and f). The device also showed a wide spectral range and good stability under continuous voltage bias and illumination.

5.3.4 Flexible gas sensors. Flexible gas sensors hold significant potential for environmental and healthcare applications, and the development of biocompatible, flexible and wearable, sensing platforms for high accuracy detection of toxic and noxious gas molecules remains an elusive goal. Traditionally, metal oxide semiconductors (MOSs), such as stannic oxide (SnO2),398–400 ferric oxide (α-Fe2O3/Fe3O4),401–403 zinc oxide (ZnO),371,404 cobaltosic oxide (Co3O4),405,406 nickel oxide (NiO)407–410 and copper oxide (CuO/Cu2O),378,399 carbon materials, such as graphene,411,412 carbon nanotubes,413 and carbon quantum dots,414 and some polymers, such as polythiophene (P3HT),415 polyaniline (PANI),416 and polypyrrole (PPY),417 serving as sensing materials, have been widely used in nonflexible gas sensors. Compared to conventional sensing materials, such biological or biological-based composite materials have a large number of advantages as efficient sensing materials to create a ‘green’ flexible gas sensor, including excellent mechanical flexibility, large specific surface area, biocompatibility, biodegradability, light-weight, low cost, etc.43 The use of biological materials as a building block in sensing platforms allows for inexpensive gas devices for point-of-care diagnostics. For example, recently, Wang et al. demonstrated a biological-based flexible gas sensor based on a biological material, Knight butterfly wing, with a surface chemical, functional-graphene (Fig. 28a).43 The Knight butterfly wing functions as natural building blocks which are functionalized with crumpled rGO and then deposited on a biocompatible common laboratory filter paper surface, yielding a three-dimensional architecture with electro-sensing active properties for highly selective detection of diabetes-related volatile organic compounds (VOCs) (Fig. 28b). Because of a high proportion of active sites (–NH2) on the surface of the butterfly wing, the butterfly-based flexible gas sensor achieved rapid gas detection (≤1 s) of diabetes-related acetone vapours in exhaled breath down to a ultralow concentration of 20 ppb. Superior mechanical properties and stability were obtained by using this device under various tension conditions (Fig. 28c and d). As a result, the findings in this work demonstrate the promise of incorporating natural biological materials into high-performance sensors, with excellent potential for wearable and flexible sensors.

5.4 Energy conversion and storage devices

The goal of flexible electronic devices is to produce portable, bendable, lightweight, foldable, flexible and even wearable products.7,418 Nonetheless, existing energy-storage devices, including lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) and supercapacitors (SCs), are generally too bulky, too hard and too large to meet the special demands of wearable electronic equipment, which is seen as the main energy-storage technology to deliver sustainable power to a variety of flexible and wearable electronic products in modern society. Therefore, there is high demand for improving current state-of-the-art rechargeable batteries and supercapacitors in terms of their weight, electrochemical performance, sustainability, manufacturing costs, environmental friendliness and mechanical properties, and solving these problems has become an important research topic. Energy-storage and conversion systems in nature have evolved over billions of years to produce efficient and suitable cellular ventilators for external energy absorption, metabolism and distribution. As a result, natural biomaterials have drawn increasing interest in the field of energy storage and conversion as a novel and effective strategy to address these challenges. In this section, we review some of the latest advancements in natural biomaterials in the field of LIBs, SCs, solar cells and generators.
5.4.1 Flexible lithium ion batteries (LIBs). Because of their long cycle life, high energy density, low cost and good performance, LIBs are rapidly becoming popular in portable electronic products and emerging energy-storage devices.1,419–422 Recent attempts to further expand the practical applications of LIBs in flexible and wearable electronic equipment have produced a large amount of batteries that are flexible, foldable, and bendable, mainly resulting from various electrode configuration designs or integrated battery systems and nanomaterials and showing excellent electrochemical and mechanical properties.423–425 However, their performance standards in different fields, including cycle life, power density, renewable energy, biocompatibility and safety issues, require further improvements for future market demand. Biomaterials or biological materials with unique biological structures exhibit appropriate features for new flexible LIBs, which suggests urgent demand for the development and popularization of flexible electronics.78,426–429 However, so far, there have only been a few reports of flexible LIBs based on biomaterials.

The active electrode material is the most significant component in flexible LIBs, as this component controls the electrochemical performance of the energy-storage device. As a result, the sustainability, energy density, biocompatibility and cost of the active materials have been closely examined. In this regard, inspired by the metabolic processes of organisms that depend on ion transport and energy exchange between biomolecules, in addition to the self-assembly of specific functional groups or biological templates, some natural biomass and its derivatives can produce more sustainable flexible energy-storage devices in a variety of designs. The biological approach to electronic equipment manufacturing is no longer an early proof of concept but has now begun to provide new methods to improve the device performance. For instance, naturally derived redox biomolecules have been utilized. Guo and co-workers produced a biological-based electrode using a retractable telescopic conductive agent. Juglone is a renewable biomolecule extracted from discarded walnut epicarp and can be immobilized on rGO nanomaterials due to the intensive π–π interactions between the aromatic structures and the carbon scaffold (Fig. 29a and b).76 In combination with the redox activity of the quinone groups in juglone and the high conductivity of rGO nanomaterials, the regenerable hybrid electrode showed excellent cyclic stability and high storage capacity for flexible sodium-ion energy-storage devices (Fig. 29c). Sustainable biotechnology electrodes have greater energy-storage characteristics, such as rate capability, cycle stability and specific capacity. After 100 cycles, the high-gravity capacity density reached 305 mA h g−1 and 280 mA h g−1 (Fig. 29d). In addition, this versatile method can be used to construct electrodes of any size and shape, with high alignment accuracy and resolution on a particular substrate, demonstrating its possible application in the complicated and exact fabrication of prospective biocompatible electronic devices (Fig. 29e and f). Other regular redox components, such as flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD), are present in living organisms, which have also been reported in biomolecule-based electrodes for direct energy storage.432

The results showed that mesoporous and soft wood-based substrates can be used as a new platform for flexible batteries with low-cost.

In addition to active electrode materials, biomaterials can also act as binders or separators for flexible batteries to improve the mechanical sustainability, self-healing ability, and electrochemical behavior. Recently, Bao and co-workers demonstrated that binders composed of bio-repair materials have excellent performance in flexible rechargeable batteries (Fig. 31e).431 Inspired by biological systems with intrinsic self-healing abilities, artificial intelligence materials that can repair internal or external damage have been developed over the last decade. Compared with conventional polymeric binders, a binder composed of a self-healing material can be stretched and spontaneously repair mechanical damage and cracks in the electrodes (Fig. 31f–i), thereby stabilizing the electrical and mechanical connections between the silicon particles and increasing their electrochemical cycle life. As expected, at a density of 0.4 A g−1, an electrode composed of silicon microparticles and the self-healing adhesive can produce up to 2617 mA h g−1 of energy in the first cycle, which is about six times the theoretical capacity of graphite. In addition, the electrode had good cycle stability compared to 14%, 27% and 47% PVDF, CMC and alginate-based binders and maintained 100% of its initial capacity after 20 cycles.

Moreover, the wettability of the separator is a key factor in determining the performance of its rechargeable battery. The use of natural processes to transform the separator effectively improves the hydrophilicity and subsequent ion diffusion.

5.4.2 Flexible supercapacitors. Supercapacitors (SCs) are another promising component of energy-storage equipment and have attracted widespread attention due to their rapid charge and discharge rates, high power density and long life.435–438 In particular, to meet the growing demand for portable electronic devices, we have been committed to developing lightweight, flexible, and miniaturized supercapacitors.439–441 Like flexible LIBs, flexible SCs also require high energy and power density, long life, renewability, biocompatibility and safety features. In addition to the configuration, the generation of new green supercapacitors from materials rich in natural reserves by exploring renewable biological materials is necessary.442 Organic materials have the beneficial properties of lightweight and easy handling but are not advantageous due to their low conductivity and rapid dissolution into the electrolyte.73 Several effective methods can be used to improve the low conductivity and large-scale dissolution of organic molecules.443–445 One is to construct unique nanostructures of these materials and to hybridize them with flexible carbon materials, which has proven to be an operative method for solving the issue of declining capacity. Chen and co-workers reported all-solid-state symmetrical supercapacitors, obtained through the polymerization of regenerable converging molecules on conductive carbon fibers that exhibited excellent electrochemical performance (Fig. 32a).73 The results showed that the initial permittivity of the device was 78%, that is, 1.72 mF−1, as the scanning speed increased from 5 to 200 mV−1 (Fig. 32b). Fiber optic supercapacitors also show excellent flexibility, with no significant deviation under different bending conditions (Fig. 32c). Integrated supercapacitor electric-wire-based energy transmission systems can afford synchronous energy transmission and storage, as verified by circuit analyses. The MCAs showed a current density of 333.1 F g−1 in a 6 M solution. The MCAs also exhibited excellent cycle stability and were able to retain 96% capacitance after 1000 charge and discharge cycles.

Another method is to simulate the structure of natural products, and if the structure can be reasonably configured, the rational design of composite electrode nanostructures can effectively improve the structural stability and long-term electrochemical cycle life. Inspired by the geometry of bamboo, whose cross-section contains a periodic internal pore distribution as well as graded pore structures, Sun et al. reported multi-scale, stratified carbon nanofibers (Fig. 32d).72 As shown in Fig. 32e, the prepared carbon nanofibers consist of uniform, discontinuous, hollow internal and graded nanopores. Due to this unique structure, the nanofibers have high mechanical durability and flexibility, providing high surface contact for the electrolyte and low ion-transport resistance. The nanofiber-based all-solid-state supercapacitors showed an energy density of 61.3 kilowatts for 2.37 kilograms, which is much greater than that of traditional carbon-based active materials. In addition, the supercapacitor operates under different mechanical deformation conditions without performance degradation (Fig. 32f).

Typically, when flexible materials are used in practical applications, the electrode material is susceptible to structural breakage during bending or charging and discharging, and flexible polymer substrates can suffer mechanical damage over time or by accidental structural damage. These two failures will seriously affect the reliability and lifetime, resulting in electronic equipment failure and a large amount of e-waste, in addition to inconvenience and security risks.447–449 Due to the significant lack of current research into the mechanical sustainability of these supercapacitors, there is scientific and technical importance for the study of powerful supercapacitors with controllable expiration. In living organisms, wounds are usually treated by a regenerative system. Self-healing electronic equipment is highly desirable because it can enhance the service life of future flexible SC equipment and overcome the problem of electronic damage. In a study by Peng et al., carbon nanotubes were coated on the surface of self-healing polymer fibers to attach the carbon nanotubes.450 After fracture, these conductive fibers can effectively recover their high conductivity by physical contact with the re-established carbon nanotubes in the sheath and restore the mechanical strength by self-repair of the core polymer. The prepared linear supercapacitors can provide a high specific capacitance of 140.0 F g−1 or 1.34 mF cm−1 and can recover 92% of the initial capacitance after fracture and self-healing. Some similar works have also been reported.451,452 The successful manufacturing of self-healing flexible SCs will greatly extend the lifetime of future energy-storage devices, providing good economic and operational safety performance.

5.4.3 Flexible solar cells. Solar energy as a green energy has attracted great attention.80 Solar cells are one of the most studied devices that can capture sunlight and convert it into electrical energy.453,454 Here, we review the latest advances in flexible solar cells based on biomaterials. Improving the efficiency of optical energy conversion is the key to improving the performance of solar energy systems. Many organisms exhibit great efficiency in the use of light diffusion to enhance light collection.455 The optical structure in these biological systems provides inspiration for the design and manufacture of efficient artificial optical energy devices. In addition, biological systems have been given great attention in the study of traditional flat flexible solar cells and fiber-based wearable electronic devices due to the powerful biological flexible stress release properties and the unique function of space regulation.456 Augmenting light absorption is a fundamental factor in improving the energy conversion efficiency of a solar cell. In solar cells, retroreflective coatings can reduce the high reflection losses between the solar cell and air. Multilayer antireflective coatings and conventional monolayers are generally confined to a small range of incident angles over a narrow bandwidth, which limits the performance of the solar cell. Another widely used approach involves the coating of antireflective surfaces on different types of solar cells to improve the efficiency of the solar cell by suppressing the Fresnel reflection over a wide range of wavelengths. In a recent report, a double-sided nanoimprint lithography technique employing a polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxane-based (POSS) antireflective moth's eye nanostructure was developed.457 These retro-reflected nanostructures show quasi-omnidirectional antireflective properties and excellent broadband range, increasing the light transmittance of the glass to 98.2%. Recently, Lee et al. used alumina as a template to construct PMMA nanostructures via hot embossing and obtained excellent wide-angle antireflective properties (at 400–2000 nm, the average reflectance was 8–45 with an average reflectance of 0.5%). A layer of deposited hydrophilic silica nanoparticles showed superhydrophilic anti-fogging functionality, and as biological templates they are interesting and as natural molds, they can be duplicated to yield bio-simulated polymer antireflective nanostructures. For instance, Ko and co-workers used a soft lithographic technique to reproduce the original surface nanoscale morphology of an Attacus moth's composite eye. Both the natural moth's eye and replica show less than 1% reflectivity in the visible region (Fig. 33a–e).433

In addition, a variety of other bio-morphological structures have been studied to enhance the performance of solar cells, especially in terms of improving the efficiency of flexible cells with solar dyes. In solar cells, an optimized grading in the porous morphology of the photoanode is the key to enhancing the light extraction efficiency by increasing the surface area and the optical path length of the dye to enhance the light extraction efficiency. The conductive carbon cloth that is acquired by readily available cellulose fabric's single-step thermal decomposition was used as a counter electrode for flexible dye-sensitized solar cells to contend with the expensive platinum/FTO system. In addition, inspired by the structure of butterfly wings (Fig. 33h and i),83 a layered titanium dioxide lattice structure exhibited a larger surface area and improved light absorption over conventional titania photoanodes, suggesting that the solar cells were more efficient.

5.5 Other applications

In addition to the above applications, biological materials with unique structures and functions have been widely applied in other applications. The unique optical properties of some organisms, such as the brittle star O. wendtii, the beetle C. gloriosa, various butterfly wings, and the moth Cacostatia ossa, were utilized by Zhang and co-workers to fabricate optical sensors.82 For example, Potyrailo and co-workers reported that the natural hierarchical structure could be intensified by filling nanotubes into the empty spaces of the butterfly scales to produce a unique infrared sensor (Fig. 34a–c).458 Their experiments confirmed the fact that the complicated geometry of Morpho butterfly wings is capable of producing an observable specific optical response to temperature, especially as a result of thermal expansion as well as variations in the refractive index, which leads to the ‘wavelength conversion’ of MWIR radiation into observable iridescence with a spatial resolution possibly limited to characteristic ridges on a butterfly scale.458 The Morpho butterfly wing structure simulates the design of an artificial sensor with enhanced sensitivity as well as spatial resolution whose structure only relies on the utilization of our new approaches or methods. For example, Ling and co-workers reported a new route to directly exfoliate silk fibers at the single silk nanofibril level. This process retains the natural structure and functions of silk fibers and exhibits excellent optical properties.504 Additionally, the vacuum-filtration approaches to functionalize silk provide excellent physical properties and could be used in bio-optic and electronic devices. With respect to natural biological molecules, Yan and co-workers indicated that polydopamine coatings can improve the UV and visible-light photoluminescence of ZnO.505 These researchers were of the opinion that the improvement mechanism was the same as that of dye-sensitized solar cells. Polydopamine acted as a dye owing to its wide absorption and produced electrons after photoexcitation. The photogenerated electrons were capable of being transferred to the conduction band of the ZnO nanorods, thereby potentially enhancing the overall number of electrons which play a role in the process of photoluminescence.

6 Concluding remarks and perspectives

Biological materials have attracted increasing attention due to their unique hierarchical 3D structure, remarkable biological activity, tailorable chemical composition, biocompatibility, biodegradation, low-cost and abundant renewable supply. To fully exploit the potential of biological materials as components or active building blocks for practical functional electronic devices on a large scale, surface functionalization with other materials (e.g., carbon materials, polymers, metal oxides, and hydrogels) can be used to controllably assemble these biological materials into ‘smart’ and multifunctional composites. As a result, natural biofunctional surfaces often have to fulfill several functions to meet the requirements of specific applications, which can be achieved through their highly tailorable composition and structure, such as by crystal structure, electron-transfer rates, changing the mechanical properties, ionic conductivity, capacity and solubility. In this article, we summarized the applications of biological materials and biological-based composites as ‘smart’ materials for flexible electronic applications, including flexible substrates, flexible biosensors, flexible electronic sensors, and flexible energy conversion and storage devices. Due to their structural diversity and incorporation of biologically active components derived from nature, biological materials have great potential as active components for flexible electronic devices, especially because they can realize sustainability and ‘green’ goals in the field of electronics. The number of publications in flexible electronic fields is greatly increasing, reflecting the strong demand for a sustainable society. Although much progress has been achieved, key challenges remain with limitations in thorough understanding of their structure–property relationships. Actually, the aim of more insightful comprehension of the relationship between the material structure, including the physicochemical and biological properties, and the device performance is to improve the flexible device performance (e.g. mechanical flexibility, electron or ion transport, reactivity, etc.). Thus, optimizing the natural biological materials to maximize the contribution resulting from structures and functions is always expected. For example, pore-rich and hierarchical biological materials being used in flexible electronic skin, supercapacitors, lithium ion batteries, etc., is anticipated to provide excellent flexibility and elasticity for mechanical deformation, sufficient contact surface for electronic transformation, and large pore size for ease of electrolyte diffusion. Undoubtedly, biological materials provided a facile route to develop next-generation flexible and wearable electronics that are of low cost and environmentally friendly.
