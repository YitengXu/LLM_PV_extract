Abstract

Thiophene-bridged and thiazole-bridged diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) polymers for near-infrared (near-IR) photovoltaic applications have been investigated via density functional theory (DFT) and Marcus charge transfer theory. Compared with thiophene-bridged DPP polymers, thiazole-bridged polymers have higher ionization potentials (IPs) but poorer optical absorption and worse charge transport capability. Different beneficial substituents replaced the hydrogen atoms (H) on the thiazole rings for the sake of reversing the disadvantages of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers and making these compounds better near-infrared absorbing materials. In order to gain deep insight into the impact of Ï€-bridge modification on the photoelectronic properties of DPP polymers, their electronic structures, absorption capabilities, intramolecular charge transfer properties and charge transport performances have been analyzed. The calculated results reveal that Ï€-bridge modification is a feasible way to improve the light-absorbing capability, electron excitation properties and charge transport performance of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers. It is expected that Ï€-bridge modification can also work for other polymers containing Ï€-bridge units. We hope that our research efforts will be helpful in the designing of new near-IR absorbing materials and could motivate further improvement of organic solar cells.

1. Introduction

Organic solar cells (OSCs) have been considered as a next-generation green technology1 because of their environmental friendliness, lightweight, flexibility, low-cost and solution processability.2â€“5 In the past few decades, benefitting from the joint efforts of synthetic chemists and theoretical chemists, the performances of OSCs have been significantly improved.5â€“7 Recently, the best photovoltaic devices that utilize medium band gap polymers have achieved an efficiency of up to 12.05%,8 which is close to the threshold for commercial application.9 Though such devices have already achieved tremendous success, their optical absorption usually does not extend to the near-infrared region.10,11 Taking full advantage of that unused part of the solar spectrum may be conducive to the further improvement of photovoltaic device power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) for use in industrial production, and this calls for designing new ultra-small band gap materials that possess strong spectral responses in the near-infrared region.11 The materials meeting the requirements are rare, and diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) polymers are an outstanding species among them owing to their high charge carrier mobilities and less complex synthesis and other advantages.12 Generally, DPP polymers consist of the following three principal building blocks: a DPP core, a Ï€-bridge unit and a donor moiety (see Fig. 1).
Fig. 1 Basic structure of DPP polymers.

The strong electron-donating capability of thiophene can provide DPP polymers with absorption onsets in the near-IR region, so thiophene-bridged DPP polymers are quite common in the near-IR OSC field.11,13,14 For example, through copolymerization of thiophene-bridged DPP with dithienopyrrole (DTP), Koen H. Hendriks and coworkers synthesized PDPPTDTPT (P1)10 in 2014, which coupled with phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester ([60]-PCBM) provided a PCE of 3.8% and an external quantum efficiency (EQE) of up to 50% at 1000 nm. However, thiophene-bridged DPP polymers have low ionization potentials and small optical band gaps (Eg), which led to large energy losses (Eloss) and small open-circuit voltages (Voc), and hence small PCEs, for the resulting photovoltaic devices. The development of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers has solved this problem. In 2015, Weiwei Li and coworkers using thiazole as a Ï€-bridge synthesized PDPP2Tz-DTP (P2),15 which offered a PCE of 5.6% with a high Voc of 0.69 V but a smallest Eloss of 0.59 eV. Such a brilliant performance convincingly indicates that thiazole-bridged DPP polymers are a promising near-IR absorbing material.

However, this kind of material is not flawless. In comparison to the analog with thiophene as a Ï€-bridge (that is, P1), thiazole-bridged DPP polymers (P2) have poorer optical absorption and charge carrier transport capability, which is associated with the difference of their Ï€-bridge units;10,15 in addition, the matched acceptor PCBM has relatively weak absorption in the visible region of the solar spectrum and limited tunability of its electronic and optical properties,16 the drawbacks which constrain the performances of these devices. Hence, modulating the orbital energy levels of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers to make these polymers better match other excellent acceptors is imperative. Substituting the donor moiety in the polymer backbone, which is the most common approach to designing new donor or acceptor material molecules,17â€“19 can do that, but such a replacement would bring a lot of uncertain factors into the morphologies of blends. Particularly, in view of the favorable morphological features exhibited by the blends containing P2, the effect of the adjustment method on the morphological characteristics should be minimized.

With these concerns, we decided to choose the method of Ï€-bridge modification, which is substituting the remaining hydrogen atoms (H) on thiazole rings with the electron-donating group, methyl (Me), or the electron-accepting fluorine atom (F), in this work, to regulate the intrinsic properties of the thiazole-bridged DPP polymers and make these polymers better near-IR absorbing materials (see Fig. 2). Both Me and F have been reported not to impose much steric hindrance on the intermolecular packing.20â€“23 Moreover, their opposite electronic effects could help us to have a closer examination of Ï€-bridge modification's capability of energy level regulation. To the best of our knowledge, there has been no precedent research about how chemical modification on the Ï€-bridge units would affect the photovoltaic performances of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers. We intend to explore this subject in our work via quantum chemistry calculations, an economical and accurate tool which is widely used in the field of materials science,24 thereby establishing the structureâ€“property relationships of DPP polymers and elucidating the role and characteristics of Ï€-bridge modification. We expect that the study of this method can also shed some light on the fundamental research of all polymers containing Ï€-bridges and help in the development of near-IR OSCs.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Electronic structure

As for Ï€-conjugated polymers, the delocalized frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) go hand in hand with the charge carrier transport properties. Generally, the more the delocalized FMO, the faster the charge carrier transport. This is because delocalization can reduce the intramolecular reorganization energy and increase the electronic coupling of adjacent molecules.54 As shown in Fig. 3, all the polymers shared analogous FMO (including the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)) distribution diagrams: the HOMOs are spread on the whole molecule (over the DTP, DPP and adjacent bridge units), while LUMOs are mainly localized on the DPP and the adjacent bridge units. Compared with the distributions of LUMOs, the HOMOs are more delocalized for all the studied polymers, which denotes that FMO delocalization is more favorable for the hole transport than the electron transport.54 Overall, the substituent groups on the thiazole rings had no obvious influence on the distributions of FMOs, but did cause some minor changes. The electron-donating capacity of the Me group made the HOMO wave function of P3 have larger values on the thiazole rings, while the F atoms were directly involved in the formation of the HOMO of P4; both of them are in favor of polymers possessing more delocalized HOMOs.
Fig. 3 Spatial distributions of FMOs for all polymers.

As prerequisites for ideal near-IR absorbing donors, suitable FMO energy levels are crucial to guarantee a strong photo-response in the near-IR solar spectrum and the occurrence of a photo-induced electron transfer process.10 Therefore, appropriate chemical modification must possess the ability to precisely manipulate the orbital energy levels of material molecules to let them absorb solar radiation at longer wavelengths or adapt to different acceptors. We visualized the FMO energy levels of all the polymers through their images to directly observe their energy level arrangement and the impact of Ï€-bridge modification. The HOMO levels, LUMO levels and band gaps of all the polymers are shown in Fig. 4.


Fig. 4 Energy diagram.

As shown in Fig. 4, the calculated frontier molecular orbital energy levels and band gaps of P1 and P2 are just a little different from the experimental data, which enables us to believe that the computational methods we used can accurately predict the electronic structures of the newly designed polymers P3 and P4. Because the electron-donating methyl group could reduce the ionization potential and the electron affinity (EA) of the polymer, both the HOMO and LUMO levels of P3 are increased in comparison to those of P2. Moreover, the HOMO level is increased more than the LUMO level (the HOMO level increases by 0.1 eV and the LUMO level increases by 0.07 eV), which means compound P3 has a smaller band gap so that it can absorb the solar radiation at longer wavelengths. As for P4, since the LUMO is mainly localized on the DPP unit and the adjacent bridge units, the substitution of F would have a greater effect on the LUMO. Thus, the HOMO slightly decreased by 0.04 eV, while the LUMO drastically decreased by 0.1 eV, causing P4 also to have a smaller band gap than P2. The results of FMO analysis suggest that Ï€-bridge modification can effectively fine-tune the frontier molecular orbital energy levels of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers and make them better match to those of different acceptor materials.

The gap in the light-harvesting performances of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers and their thiophene-bridged analogs has been bridged to some extent by means of the reasonable modification in the molecular Ï€-bridge units.

3.4 Charge transport properties

Charge carrier mobility is a key parameter to assess the charge transfer process,31,42 which plays a vital role in the charge photogeneration process of OSCs. Generally, the higher the charge transfer efficiency the greater the charge carrier mobility. Fast charge transfer promotes a high short-circuit current density, a large fill factor (FF) and a high PCE. In order to quantitatively evaluate the charge transport performances of materials, the charge carrier mobilities were predicted. Previous studies have shown that the inherent triangular pyramid configuration of a nitrogen atom can lead to large intermolecular distances.60,61 This adverse effect on the morphology in conjunction with the poor optical absorption led P2 to have a low JSC and FF. However, due to the small difference between P1 and P2 with regard to the side chains on the DTP units, we cannot directly see the effect of the thiazole Ï€-bridge on their morphology (the nitrogen atoms of P2 may give rise to an increase in intermolecular distances). Thus, consistent alkyl side chains are introduced into all studied compounds to eliminate any complication of the side chain effect on the transport properties.22,62,63 In this way, we could also explore the effect of Ï€-bridge modification on the morphology.

As both the observed blends of P1 and P2 in the experiments contain fibrous crystal structures according to transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images,10,15 we calculated all the mobilities in the crystals. The method of predicting the crystal structure was confirmed by Hui-xue Li and coworkers64 and was used by other research teams,42,65 and it was also applied in this work. We used Materials Studio66 to calculate the crystal structures of all compounds. In the first place, a single molecule was optimized in the Dmol3 module using the Perdewâ€“Burkeâ€“Ernzerhof (PBE) generalized gradient approximation (GGA). Furthermore, the crystal structure was established in the Polymorph Predictor module using the Dreiding force field, which is considered to be the most reliable force field for molecular crystal prediction. Without prior knowledge of the crystal symmetry, we ran the calculations for the ten most common space groups, namely P21/C, P1, P212121, C2/C, P21, PBCA, PNA21, CC, PBCN and C2. The obtained crystal structures were ranked by their total energies and those with the lowest energies were selected to calculate the hole mobilities. It is worth mentioning that the theoretical models we used to represent the studied compounds are based on some sound approximations and simplifications, so the predicted crystal structures may deviate from the true ones. Hence, the main purpose of this work is not the precise description of charge transport, but to reveal the structureâ€“property relationship between these compounds, and thereby elucidate the effect of Ï€-bridge modification on the morphology. On the basis of the predicted packing motif, we chose a molecule as a carrier donor and regarded its adjacent molecules as paired elements so that we could center the dimer selections with respect to a fixed molecule (see Fig. 6). Such a procedure would be beneficial, as is well-known that some dimers are equivalent because of the periodic nature of the molecular crystal. And so we should identify the different types of dimers to find all of the charge transfer pathways in the center molecule without duplication. 4. Conclusions

In this contribution, based on past experimental molecules, we designed two new high-performance polymers P3 and P4via Ï€-bridge modification. They have higher ionization potentials, and thus can provide larger open circuit voltages; they have excellent charge transfer performances, which is an essential feature for excellent solar cell semiconductors. In addition, they have stronger and broader absorption spectra in the near-infrared region. We consider the exploration of such polymers crucial for further improvement in the efficiencies of OSCs. There is no doubt that P3 and P4 are promising candidates for near-IR organic solar cells. What's more, we theoretically calculated the electronic structure, optical properties, intramolecular charge transfer properties and charge transport properties of P1, P2, P3 and P4 using DFT and Marcus theory, thereby summarizing the role and characteristics of Ï€-bridge modification, which is itemized as follows:

(1) Ï€-Bridge modification is an effective method to regulate the frontier molecular orbital energy levels of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers, and has some predictability. For example, the use of electron-donating groups would cause the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of polymers to increase, especially the HOMO energy levels; the use of electron-accepting groups would result in a decrease of the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of polymers, particularly the LUMO energy levels.

(2) Ï€-Bridge modification could modulate the optical absorption properties of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers. In this work, it made polymers P3 and P4 have wider and stronger absorption spectra, which is conducive to improving short-circuit current density.

(3) Ï€-Bridge modification altered the intramolecular charge transfer properties of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers. The substitution of a fluorine atom enhanced the ICT properties of P4, thus resulting in improved charge transfer and longer charger transfer distance.

(4) Ï€-Bridge modification could affect the charge transport capabilities of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers. It could reduce the internal reorganization energy or enhance the intermolecular coupling strength, resulting in better charge transport performance.

(5) Last but not least, the Ï€-bridge modification is not just a replacement for the polymer building blocks, but a kind of subtle modification on the bridged units. Ï€-Bridge modification is proposed for further improvement of device performance on the basis of replacement; in other words, it is the follow-up of replacement.

In summary, we have enlarged upon the photovoltaic performances of four Dâ€“Ï€â€“Aâ€“Ï€ type DPP polymers to confirm the feasibility of the Ï€-bridge modification method. Accurate reproduction of the experimental data ensures the reliability of our predicted results. We have reasons to believe that Ï€-bridge modification is an effective way for adjusting the photoelectric properties of thiazole-bridged DPP polymers and even all polymers containing Ï€-bridges. And we do hope that this research could provide some theoretical guidelines for further improving the performance of OSCs.
