Interfacing green synthesized flake like-ZnO with TiO2 for bilayer electron extraction in perovskite solar cells

Abstract

To improve the performance of PSCs, it is essential to prevent the carrier recombination losses at the interfaces of the transparent metal oxide electrode/electron transport layer (ETL)/active absorber perovskite layer. This present work reports on the green synthesis approach used for the preparation of flake like-ZnO nanostructure (GF-ZnO NS), naturally extracted from the leaf of Albizia Amara, used as a reducing cum capping agent. Herein, we have introduced an n-type GF-ZnO NS material as an efficient electron transport interfacial layer (bi-ETL) at the ETL/perovskite junction in the fabricated perovskite solar cells (PSCs). The structure of the fabricated PSC device is as follows: glass/ITO/bi-ETL (c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs)/CH3NH3PbI3−xClx/spiro-MeOTAD/Au. A comparative study has also been carried out by deploying electron transport materials such as c-TiO2 and GF-ZnO NSs separately. From this, it has been found that the bi-ETL perovskite solar cell devices achieved a maximum power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 7.83% with an open-circuit voltage (VOC) of 0.728 V, a short circuit current density (JSC) of 20.46 mA cm−2 and a fill factor (FF) of 52.61% whereas, the c-TiO2, GF-ZnO NSs and the chemically reduced CR-ZnO ETL based devices achieved a PCE of 4.84%, 5.82% and 6.81%, respectively. The better performance obtained for the bi-ETL based devices is ascribed to the enhanced carrier extraction and the reduced recombination losses at the interface between the ETL and the active perovskite layer.

1. Introduction

In the past two decades, perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have become the rising star in third-generation thin-film photovoltaic (PV) technology, attracting broad attention from both the academic and industrial communities.1,2 Perovskite materials used as an absorber layer in solar cells are well-known for their low-cost, solution based synthesis, optimal energy bandgap value (∼1.1–1.55 eV), powerful absorption coefficient (104 cm−1), long charge diffusion length (∼1 microns), and outstanding charge-carrier mobility (∼5–10 cm2 V−1 s−1).3,4 The best performing perovskite solar cells discovered to date has a notable power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 25.2%.5–7 However, the major drawback of PSCs is the problem with its long-term stability which has to be overcome if they are to gain commercial viability.8,9 In PSCs, the inorganic electron transporting layer plays a pivotal role in transporting electrons and blocking holes, to enhance the charge separation.10–12 Among the various electron transporting layers, TiO2 has been considered to be one of the most widely used electron extraction material in planar PSCs owing to its optimal energy bandgap, suitable optical absorption and chemical stability. However, its low electron mobility and low charge extraction ability may result in low charge separation and high charge accumulation at the ETL/perovskite interface which will reduce the device performance.13–15 Therefore, many researchers are working to develop and use various alternative inorganic ETL materials such as transition metal oxides (SnO2, ZnO, WO3, etc.), Nb2O5-lanthanum oxide and metal sulphides (MoS2, SnS2, In2S3, etc.) because of their good ohmic contact which is required for efficient electron extraction and transport, optimal optical behaviour, high charge carrier mobility and efficient air stability.9,16,17 Moreover, they are of low-cost and have tunable optoelectronic properties and their energy levels match with perovskite materials which make them a suitable electron extraction material.18–25

ZnO with its superior merits such as remarkable optical transmittance in the visible region, optimal bandgap value (∼3.3 eV), excellent electrical properties and higher charge carrier mobility compared to TiO2 is an ideal choice as an alternative ETL in PSCs.26–28 TiO2 and ZnO nanostructure based bilayer ETMs can be used in PSCs which can provide an enhanced device performance by solving the TCO/ETL/perovskite interfacial issues.29,30

The use of a single TiO2 electron transporting material for the fabrication of PSCs without pin-holes is still a challenging task. Pin-holes present in the single ETL will provide direct contact between the absorber and the transparent conductive oxide layer (TCO) and this may lead to high leakage current and serious charge recombination at the ETL/TCO interface.31 When single TiO2 material is used as ETL, the oxygen related defects at the c-TiO2 surface may react with the perovskite layer which may induce the decomposition of the perovskite material at the ETL/perovskite interface which will degrade the photovoltaic performance.17 From the above-mentioned limitations, it is understood that a single ETL may not act as an effective hole blocking material in PSCs and it is not possible to reduce the charge recombination efficiently. To solve this perovskite degradation issue and achieve long-term stability, it is essential to modify the surface of a single TiO2 ETL by using some other n-type materials.32 In order to overcome these issues, a dense, pin-hole free and electrically conductive TiO2/ZnO electron transport bilayer can be used at the c-TiO2/perovskite interface which has the advantages of improved electron extraction and low interfacial recombination.33

In 2019, Xie et al. have reported about using TiO2/SnO2 bilayer as an efficient electron extraction layer in PSC device and have achieved a PCE of 18.85% compared to that of individual TiO2 or SnO2 ETL based devices.34 Khalid et al. have used polyethyleneimine polymer coating on the surface of a ZnO based bilayer ETL film consisting of ZnO nanosheets/ZnO nanoparticles in PSCs and have obtained a PCE of 16.39%. From this, they have concluded that the devices based on such ZnO bilayer films are always superior compared to single-layer ZnO because of the improved morphology and better crystalline quality.29 You et al. have found that a bilayer TiO2/WO3 film is beneficial for extracting electrons from perovskite and the fabricated cell exhibited a PCE of 20.14%, which is better than those of the single WO3 or TiO2 ETL based devices.7 Kumari et al. have fabricated devices with TiO2/ZnO as bilayer ETL, and the bilayer was found to be helpful in achieving high electron extraction and low interfacial recombination which resulted in achieving a PCE of 7.39%.35 Faisal et al. have recently reported about the hysteresis-less mesostructured PSCs consisting of a novel barium hydroxide hybridized boron-doped ZnO (B:ZnO) bilayer thin film as the ETM and the fabricated device with this ETM exhibited a PCE of 20.62%.36 Deng et al. have described the fabrication of PSCs using a hydroiodic acid (HI) modified TiO2/SnO2 bilayer and they reported that the devices exhibit good stability maintaining their initial PCE (η = 16.74%) up to 85% and 83% after 100 ± 5 °C heating for 22 h and under 1 sun illumination for 6 h, respectively.17 Denegri et al. have used a bilayer SnO2/TiO2 ETL in PSCs and have found that the introduced bilayer can reduce the leakage current and improve the charge transfer at the ETL/perovskite interface.37 Hsuan et al. have designed a ZnO/Al-doped ZnO (AZO) bilayer thin film based device and have reported that there is an increase in the PCE value from 12.3% to 16.1% when AZO is used as a buffer layer.38 Yi et al. have used SnO2 bilayer as the ETL and the device showed a maximum PCE of 16.84%.1 Escobar et al. have found that the introduction of insulating ZrO2 nanocrystals onto c-TiO2 for preparing a bilayer ETL may inhibit the direct contact between the c-TiO2 and perovskite layer. This will reduce the recombination losses at the interface and the device fabricated using this bilayer exhibited a maximum PCE of 17.9%.39 Kogo et al. fabricated a bilayer PSC comprised of amorphous SnOx and mesoporous brookite TiO2 as the ETL and it exhibited good mechanical stability against cyclic bending in which the fabricated cells yielded a PCE of 13.4%.40 By optimizing the process time, Chen et al. have obtained an impressive PCE of 16.38% using a TiOx/In2O3 bilayer ETL and the fabricated cell had a good fill factor because of the introduced bilayer.41 Shahiduzzaman et al. have made an important advancement in the design of photovoltaic devices with respect to charge transport and electron–hole recombination by using a c-TiO2 on the anatase phase TiO2 ETL and the device exhibited a maximum PCE of 17.05%.42

There are many previous reports available about the preparation of metal oxide nanostructure based electron transporting materials using expensive physical processing techniques and by using simple chemical methods but hazardous capping and reducing agents (HMT, CTAB, KOH, and NaOH) are used in the chemical methods which can create health risks and threat to the environment. So as to overcome all these issues, in recent years, researchers have started moving towards the low-cost green approach method because of its eco-friendliness. In this green approach technique, the raw materials obtained from the biological system such as plant parts and microorganisms are used to prepare various metal oxide (ZnO, TiO2, etc.) nanoparticles and nanostructures.43–53

In this present study, a flake like-ZnO nanostructure has been synthesized by using a novel green approach in which the leaf extract of Albizia Amara has been used to synthesize the ZnO nanostructure which is used as an electron transport material in bilayer ETL based PSCs. A comparative study has also been made using a single layer (c-TiO2 and GF-ZnO NSs) ETL along with the bilayer (c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs) ETLs. Further, the devices fabricated using the green synthesized ZnO have been compared with the devices fabricated using chemically reduced ZnO based ETLs. Among them, the best performing bi-layer green synthesized ETL based perovskite device yielded a maximum PCE of 7.83%.

The observed emission peak matches well with the bandgap of the prepared perovskite and confirms that there is a negligible amount of non-radiative defects in the prepared perovskite material. From this, it could be inferred that micro-sized pinholes present in the single layer ETL have been reduced into nano-sized pin-holes with a continuous perovskite film over the bilayer c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs ETLs. Since, the micro-sized pinholes present in the surface of the c-TiO2/perovskite film may have the primary negative influence on the photovoltaic performance of perovskite devices due to the serious carrier recombination at the interface caused by the direct contact between ITO and perovskite layers.93,103

This phenomenon of charge extraction and carrier recombination at the interface due to the presence of pin-holes has been schematically illustrated in Fig. 14(a and b). As shown in Fig. 14(a), the larger pinholes present in the single layer electron extraction material based device can act as a recombination centre which induces more charge recombination at the interface of TiO2/perovskite. However, for the devices having a bilayer electron extraction material (given in Fig. 14(b)), the oxygen defects present in ZnO act as a recombination barrier at the interface of ETL/perovskite. Besides the oxygen vacancy defects (VO and Oi), the hydroxyl groups present in the surface of ZnO prepared using the green approach play a key role in controlling the decomposition of the perovskite layer and facilitate charge extraction and thus enhancing the photovoltaic behaviour in ZnO based PSCs.104

For the devices having a single ETL, the pinholes present in the ETL may lead to providing a direct pathway between perovskite and FTO layer, which can obviously increase the risk of recombination between the photogenerated charge carriers, while for the devices having bilayer ETL (TiO2/ZnO), the better hydrophilic nature of the TiO2 layer helps in providing a pinhole-free ZnO layer which may cover the FTO layer uniformly. As a result, there is less recombination loss in the bilayer ETL device, leading to increased VOC, JSC, and FF.7

Herein, the present work is mainly focused on reducing or if possible eliminating the recombination and backflow of electrons at the ETL/perovskite interface using the bilayer ETL (c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs) in the PSC devices, which can reduce pinholes and thereby result in an enhanced photovoltaic performance.7,103,105

2.4 Energy level work function of the constructed PSC device

Fig. 14(c) displays the cross-sectional FESEM image of the constructed PSC device having the structure of – glass/ITO/c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NS/spiro-OMeTAD/Au. The thickness of the respective layers is found to be optimal for achieving enhanced photovoltaic performance. The schematic representation of the energy level and the work function of the corresponding materials used to fabricate the complete bilayer ETL based perovskite device is shown in Fig. 14(d). Energy is expressed in terms of electron volts (eV), using the electron energy in a vacuum as a reference. The energy level of the conduction band (CB) edge of the corresponding materials – ITO, TiO2, ZnO and perovskite – are −4.7 eV, −4.1 eV, −4.0 eV and −3.9 eV, respectively,9,106,107 whereas the valence band (VB) edge of TiO2, ZnO, perovskite, spiro-OMeTAD and gold (Au) are found to be −7.1, eV, −7.2 eV, −5.40 eV, −5.20 eV and the Fermi level of Au counter electrode is −5.1 eV, respectively.33,108

When sunlight falls on the working cell, the free electrons in the perovskite layer jump to the CB edge of the electron transport TiO2/ZnO layer and are finally collected at the ITO anode. Meanwhile, the holes formed at the VB of the perovskite are extracted by the spiro-OMeTAD and then move into the Au electrode.109–112

Besides this, the performance of the perovskite solar cells fabricated using the chemically reduced ZnO electron transporting material has been compared with the performance of the cells fabricated using green synthesized ZnO based electron extraction material of the present work and the chemically reduced ZnO based perovskite device exhibited a PCE of 6.81% with JSC of 18.71 mA cm−2, VOC of 0.716 V and FF of 50.81%.

The better JSC, VOC and FF values obtained for the bilayer ETL devices indicates that the double electron transport layer plays a pivotal role in the better blocking effect with improved interface contact which results in reduced recombination losses,1,7,9,10,29,33,107 thereby resulting in achieving better device performance than the single ETL devices.

From this, it could be inferred that the bilayer ETL device exhibited slightly higher short-circuit current density (JSC), open-circuit voltage (VOC) and fill factor (FF) compared to those of the single layered ETL devices, thus giving rise to a better device performance. In contrast, the single layer c-TiO2 and GF-ZnO NS based device showed the lowest performance due to the obtained poor JSC and FF values which may be attributed to the large size pin-holes present in the absorbing material or the charge recombination taking place at the ETL/perovskite interface which may be responsible for its lower performance.113–116

3. Experimental

3.1 Materials

The pre-patterned indium tin oxide (ITO) glass substrates of about 12 × 12 × 11 mm dimensions having a sheet resistance of ∼16 Ω sq−1 were purchased. Methylammonium iodide (MAI, 98%), lead(II) chloride (98%), anhydrous ethanol (ACS reagent, 99.9%), diethyl ether, N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), titanium diisopropoxide bis(acetylacetonate) (75 wt% in isopropanol), 2,2′,7,7′-tetrakis(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenyl-amine)9,9′-spirobifluorene (spiro-OMeTAD), 4-tert-butyl pyridine (tBP), lithium bis(trifluoromethane sulfonyl)imide (Li-TFSI), chlorobenzene (99.8%) and zinc nitrate hexahydrate Zn(NO3)2·6H2O purchased from Sigma-Aldrich have been used. In addition, Dyesol 18 NR Titania paste purchased from Greatcell Solar was used as received.

3.2 Preparation of Albizia Amara leaf extract

The fresh Albizia Amara leaves were collected from a village in the Coimbatore region in Tamil Nadu, India. From this, 10 grams of the Albizia Amara leaves were taken and cleaned with tap water and then with double distilled (DD) water. The leaves were then chopped into pieces and were put into a 100 ml beaker. 75 ml of DD water was poured into the beaker containing leaves and was heated at 60 °C ± 5 °C for 3–4 hours.117 The heated leaf extract solution was filtered using Whatman filter paper and the filtered solution is to be used as a reducing and capping agent for the preparation of a flake-like ZnO nanostructure.

3.3 Green synthesis of a flake-like zinc oxide nanostructure (GF-ZnO NSs)

The preparation of green synthesized ZnO NSs was carried out as per the previous report of Senthil et al.57 To prepare the GF-ZnO NS precursor solution, 0.1 M (196 mg) of zinc nitrate hexahydrate (Zn(NO3)2·6H2O) was dissolved in 40 ml of double-distilled (DD) water and was stirred (at 300 rpm) overnight at room temperature. Then 25 ml of the leaf extract was added dropwise as a reducing and capping agent into the above prepared colourless solution and then was heated at 90 ± 5 °C for 60 min. The colour change of the solution from clear white to yellow confirms the formation of ZnO nanostructure. Further, the samples were annealed at 400 ± 5 °C for 180 minutes. The finally obtained sample was used as an electron extraction material in the fabrication of bilayer PSCs.

3.4 Perovskite device fabrication

The purchased ITO substrates were cleaned by using acetone, methanol and finally with isopropanol for 10 minutes each. To deposit compact-TiO2 (c-TiO2), precursor solution was prepared by mixing (1:10 v/v) titanium diiosopropoxide bis(acetylacetonate) in absolute ethanol and stirred for 30 minutes. To form compact TiO2, 3.3 ml of the above solution was sprayed onto the 400 °C preheated ITO substrate and the coated films were then annealed at 450 °C for 10 minutes. The above prepared colloidal green synthesized suspension of the GF-ZnO NSs ETL was deposited onto the c-TiO2 by mixing GF-ZnO NSs with ethanol at 2:7 weight ratio along with a drop of Triton X-100 and stirred vigorously for the formation of colloidal GF-ZnO NSs solution. From this, 50 μl was taken and spin-coated onto the c-TiO2 layer at a spin rate of 4500 rpm for 30 s followed by an annealing process at 400 °C for 60 minutes.

The active perovskite layer solution was prepared by dissolving 294.9 mg of methylammonium iodide (MAI) prepared as per our previous report92 in 700 μl of DMF and was stirred. Then 171.1 mg of lead (II) chloride was added to the stirred solution containing MAI and the stirred solution was heated at 90 ± 5 °C for 2 hours. Then the prepared perovskite precursor solution (50 μl) was taken and spin-coated onto the bilayer ETL at 2000 rpm for 30 s and was annealed at 120 ± 5 °C for 45 minutes. To deposit hole transporting layer (HTL), 1000 μl of spiro solution (97 mg ml−1 of spiro-OMeTAD in chlorobenzene) was added to 9.7 μl of 4-tert-butyl pyridine and 30.2 μl of a lithium salt (175 mg ml−1 of Li-TFSI solution in acetonitrile) and then mixed and stirred for 30 minutes. The prepared HTL precursor solution (30 μl) was spin-coated at a spin rate of 2000 rpm for 30 s onto the deposited perovskite layer and was kept in the dark overnight. To complete the device, a ∼80 to ∼100 nm layer of gold was deposited at ∼10−6 bar vacuum condition via thermal evaporation onto the spiro-OMeTAD layer coated film to ensure electrical contact. Hence, the overall fabricated device structure is glass/ITO/c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs/CH3NH3PbI3−xClx/spiro-OMeTAD/Au and the active device area is about 0.042 sq cm as shown in the inset of Fig. 15.

3.5 Characterization techniques

The crystalline structure was analyzed using an X-ray diffractometer (Bruker D8 ADVANCE ECO). The presence of functional groups was identified by recording the Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectrum in the range of 4000–500 cm−1 using a Bruker FTIR spectrometer. The optical absorption spectra were recorded using a UV-Visible spectrophotometer (Jasco V-670). The photoluminescence (PL) studies were carried out using a PerkinElmer (LS 45) system. The morphology of the samples was studied using a field-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM, Zeiss, Sigma, UK). The high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images of the prepared samples have been recorded using an electron microscope (JEOL JEM 2100). Raman spectroscopy studies were carried out using a laser confocal Raman microscope (Renishaw, UK, Model: Invia). The current density–voltage (J–V) curves for the fabricated perovskite solar cells were obtained using a (Keithley 2420) source meter under illumination with an ORIEL Solar Simulator having a 1000 W xenon arc lamp with AM 1.5 G filters. The J–V scan was performed from VOC −2 to +2 V with a time delay of 30 ms with a step size of 10 mV and a scan rate of 50 mV s−1. At least three sets were taken for observing the current density–voltage (J–V) at 25 ± 5 °C at 70 ± 5 °C % humidity.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have interfaced a green approach synthesized flake-like ZnO nanostructure (GF-ZnO NSs) between compact TiO2 and perovskite layer to form bilayer (c-TiO2/GF-ZnO NSs) ETL perovskite solar cells. Compared to the single c-TiO2 and GF-ZnO NSs ETLs, the introduced bilayer ETL film efficiently covered the ITO substrate, avoiding the direct contact at the interface of ITO/perovskite layers. The bi-ETL PSC device achieved a maximum PCE of 7.83% with a VOC of 0.728 V, a JSC of 20.46 mA cm−2 and an FF of 52.61%. Whereas, the c-TiO2, GF-ZnO NSs and the chemically synthesized ZnO based ETL devices achieved a PCE of 4.84, 5.82 and 6.81%, respectively. The better performance of the bi-ETL based devices is ascribed to the enhanced carrier extraction and the reduced recombination losses at the interface between the ETL and the active perovskite layer due to the obtained flake like structured ZnO nanomaterial.
