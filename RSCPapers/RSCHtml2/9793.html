Graphene on {116} faceted monocrystalline anatase nanosheet array for ultraviolet detection

Abstract

A structure composed of a nanosheet array may trap photons, which could be used to enhance the optical response via resonances; this may be highly useful in ultraviolet (UV) detection, photocatalysis, solar cells, etc. Moreover, anatase nanosheets exposed with the active {001}, {111}, and {116} facets are promising in applications such as Li-ion battery, photocatalysis, and electrochemistry. Therefore, in this study, the {116} faceted single-crystalline anatase nanosheet array with the sheet spacing in the range of several hundred nanometers was directly grown on the transparent conductive substrates. A photo-detector was fabricated by transferring a graphene electrode to the top of the anatase nanosheet array. The resultant device is inactive to visible-light irradiation and instantly responds to the UV light, which is due to the unique UV absorption property of the anatase nanosheet array and the advantage of the Schottky junction between the interfaces of graphene and anatase. The energy barrier between the two materials is 0.122 eV. We have provided a thorough research on graphene/monocrystal anatase-NSs.

1. Introduction

Ultraviolet (UV) detectors have wide applications in various fields, including medical, genetics research, space communication, virus monitoring, and military surveillance. Fast and broadband response in UV, toughness to the incident light and less affection to the visible light are few characteristics required for some specific applications.13 Traditional broad bandgap semiconductors, particularly metal oxides, such as tin oxide (SnO2), titanium oxide (TiO2), and zinc oxide (ZnO), have been extensively studied.46 Among them, anatase TiO2 draws vast attention due to its high dielectric constant, high refractive index, and excellent chemical stability. Moreover, anatase is a weak n-type semiconducting material, which makes it an ideal material to produce the composite junctions with other p-type materials.7

Recently, graphene-based photo-electric devices have drawn great attention because of its superior optical and electrical properties.8 Graphene has an almost flat absorption to the specific optical spectrum and has ballistic electronic transport and ultrahigh carrier mobility, which may lower the recombination possibility of electrons and holes.9,10 These properties make graphene an ideal material for photo-detecting devices when it is combined with other photo-sensitive materials.11 For example, Yu et al. used graphene and ZnO nanorod array to detect UV light. Taking the advantages of a single crystalized ZnO nanorod, they fabricated a UV photo-detector that was capable of monitoring a fast switching light with a frequency as high as 2.25 kHz.12 Luo et al. employed the anodization method to obtain a polycrystalline anatase nanotube array and covered it with a monolayer graphene. They conducted a comprehensive study of the fabricated UV detector and proposed an unique physical mechanism based on the band structure.13 However, the polycrystalline TiO2 nanotubes own the characteristics of low mobility and numerous defects, leading to the slow photoresponse.

Apparently, the room for further improvement in the performances of the photo-electric devices based on the graphene/anatase heterojunctions is still large if using single crystalline nanocrystals with various morphologies, such as nanorod, nanoflake, nanosheet, and nanoflower, is considered.1416 Among various nanostructures, nanopore and nanosheet have greater advantages in both photoelectric conversion and solar-energy harvesting because of their flat and continuous walls, which could be used to enhance the light absorption by light-trapping due to multiple reflections.17 In fact, photoelectric devices based on single-crystalline nanocrystals may present a higher performance than that with polycrystalline nanocrystals. The grain boundaries in polycrystalline nanocrystals scatter and recapture the charge-carriers and may provide more secondary recombination sites than those in the single-crystalline nanocrystals.1820 Therefore, in order to further optimize the device performance of the anatase-based UV photodetectors, we intentionally choose single-crystalline anatase nanosheets (NSs) with the {116} facets. In particular, the reasons to select this high Miller-index {116} facet include the exhibition of a much higher reduction capacity and a better electrochemical reversibility than those of anatase with other low index facets (including P25). Therefore, this unique combination of single-crystalline, nanosheet, and the {116} facet in this study may provide the better performances discussed above not only for the UV detectors, but also for the future opto-chemical sensors.21 We synthesized graphene by low-pressure chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and hybridized the graphene with the {116} facet anatase NSs. The UV photo-detector, which we fabricated herein, has a fast response to the UV light, but unresponsive to the visible light. The rising time and recovery speed values of the UV detector are less than one second, which makes the device more practical in the applications.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Device fabrication

The anatase NSs were synthesized by a hydrothermal method following that reported in previous studies.21 The fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) substrate was sequentially cleaned in acetone, ethanol, and deionized water. Then, it was immersed in a KOH solution for 24 h to obtain a clean surface. Subsequently, the FTO substrate was rinsed with deionized water and dried using a high-pressure nitrogen gas gun. We first spin-coated an anatase seed layer on the as-prepared FTO substrate for further growth of the anatase NSs. The precursor solution was made by adding 3 mL titanium butoxide dropwise to 20 mL toluene and then, the mixture was stirred for several minutes, followed by the addition of 1.2 mL hydrofluoric acid. After fully mixing, the as-prepared FTO coated with a seed layer was placed vertically inside a 50 ml Teflon-lined autoclave, which was kept at 180 C for 24 h. Following this, the sample was washed with ethanol and deionized water for several times. Monolayer graphene was synthesized by a low-pressure CVD method using Cu as the substrate.22 Cu sheets (Alfa Aesar, CAS: 7440508) were cut into small pieces and pretreated with acetic acid and washed with deionized water before loading into the CVD reactor. Then, the chamber was evacuated to a low pressure and slowly heated up to 1030 C. After being annealed at 1030 C for 60 min, a mixed gas of 1 sccm methane and 100 sccm hydrogen was introduced. Graphene was synthesized during the next 30 min and then, the reactor was rapidly cooled to room temperature. The as-prepared graphene/Cu sheet was cut into small pieces, etched, and rinsed in deionized water.23

Fig. 1(a) shows the structure and the picture of our as-fabricated UV-photo detector, which is composed of anatase NSs and monolayer graphene. In the process of device fabrication, a polyethylene terephthalate (PET) dielectric film was used to prevent upper metal electrode from directly contacting the anatase layer in order to get a better contact of the junction of anatase and graphene. The Au electrode was sputtered onto the PET layer. Both the Au electrode and FTO were connected to an external electric circuit by silver paste and copper wires. The contact area of the junction was 0.5 mm2. To generate a good contact, the TiO2 NSs wafer was annealed in the air before use to get rid of the surface residues. The PMMA-supported graphene was transferred onto the nanosheet array by a wet transfer method. After Cu was etched, the PMMA-supported graphene was floated on the surface of DI water and was then salvaged by the TiO2 NSs wafer. There exists water between graphene and TiO2 NSs as a buffer layer. Then, the device was dried in the air and in an oven in turn. After the device was totally dried, graphene was covered on the TiO2 NSs like a thin layer of gauze kerchief.

2.2 Structural analysis and device characterization

The morphology of the anatase nanosheet array was studied by field emission scanning electron microscopy (SEM, JSM-6700F). High-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image was recorded using a JEM-2011 (JEOL, Japan). High-resolution X-ray diffraction (XRD) experiments were performed on a Rigaku four-circle diffractometer with a Ge(220) 2-bounce incident-beam monochromator with Cu-K radiation ( = 1.5406 ). All electric measurements were performed under ambient conditions. The electric characteristics of the devices were measured with/without illumination using the Keithley 2400 sourcemeters and the low-temperature electric characteristics were investigated using a Physical Property Measurement System (PPMS, Quantum Design). Ultra-violet illumination was carried out using UVLMS-38. The Raman spectrum was recorded on an inVia confocal Raman microscope ( = 532 nm, Renishaw).

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterization

Fig. 2 presents the SEM images of anatase NSs on the FTO substrate. The top-view images (Fig. 2ac) show very dense and uniform arrays of anatase NSs in a very large area. All sheets are of about 1 m width and the thickness is less than 100 nm. Fig. 2(d) is the top-view SEM image of anatase NSs with graphene on the top. The magnification is the same as that for Fig. 2(c). We can observe that there is a gauze kerchief-like layer on the top of the anatase NSs. The SEM image shown in Fig. 2(d) was recorded without depositing a Pt layer. Comparing these two SEM images, we can observe that the resolutions are almost the same. This confirms that there is a conductive layer on the top of the anatase NSs in Fig. 2(d), which indicates the existence of graphene. From the cavities between anatase NSs, the depth of the field of Fig. 2(d) is weaker, which also proves that there is a layer of graphene blocking the electrons. Together with the Raman spectrum shown in Fig. 3(c), we are certain that graphene is well-contacted with anatase NSs. The cross-sectional SEM image in Fig. 2(e) shows that the average height of anatase NSs is about 2 m with a 300 nm seed layer. The dimensional size of the NSs can be well-controlled by the preparation conditions. As shown in Fig. 2(c), the anatase NSs are almost perpendicular to the FTO substrate. They also cross and support each other to avoid the agglomeration and collapse, making this structure have a good stability. The distance between each nanosheet is approximately equal to the wavelength of UV light. The film surface appears to be smooth, which is more convenient for the future device preparation to trap the photons.

To further evaluate the crystallinity of the nanosheets, HRTEM and XRD techniques were adopted. Fig. 3(a) reveals the pristine single-crystalline NSs. The lattice constant of two-dimensional lattice fringes is 0.35 nm, which is attributed to the {101} plane of anatase.21 The corresponding XRD pattern (Fig. 3(b)) further demonstrates the anatase phase of NSs (according to JCPDF no. 89-4921, a = b = 0.3777 nm, and c = 0.9501 nm). The (220) and (211) diffraction peaks are greatly enhanced. In addition, the strongest diffraction peak is the (220) diffraction peak, indicating that the vertical growth direction of the nanosheets is along the axial direction of <10>, which is perpendicular to the {110} plane. The XRD pattern and the HRTEM fringe are identical to those in our previous reports, confirming that the nanosheets are exposed with the {116} crystal facet.21 Four Raman peaks at 145, 397, 514, and 631 cm1 are attributed to anatase-phase TiO2.7,24 In addition, D band (1348 cm1), G band (1591 cm1) and 2D band (2678 cm1), which are the characteristic peaks of graphene, are also observed.25 We also show the Raman spectrum of graphene on SiO2 to compare with graphene on the TiO2 NSs. According to the spectrum shown in Fig. 3(c), we can observe that the D band is not very clear before the transformation. The presence of the D band indicates the presence of are some sp3 carbon bonds. The 5 cm1 up-shifting of the 2D band reveals the interlayer coupling effect between graphene and anatase NSs.26 The 2D/G intensity ratio is greater than 2, which indicates that graphene on both SiO2 and TiO2 is single-layered.

The IV curve of the monolayer graphene/anatase-NSs-Schottky junction (Fig. 5(a)) shows the rectifying characteristic. The Schottky junction acts as high-resistance state when the current flows from the graphene to the anatase NSs, thus leading to very small current. On the contrary, the Schottky junction acts as a low-resistance state when the current flows from the anatase NSs to the graphene, which results in a relatively large current. At the forward bias, the diode is already in an ON state. If the external electric field is the same direction with the Schottky junction, the photo-generated current contribution is negligible for the total current under the forward bias. 3.2. Physical mechanism

To reveal the physical mechanism of the UV photo-detection, an energy level diagram of the graphene/anatase-NSs Schottky junction is shown in Fig. 7(a).13 Due to the wet transfer method, the CVD derived graphene is usually p-doped because of the influences of the etchant, organic residue, and the substrate.37 At the interface of graphene and anatase, a Schottky junction is formed, which makes the device work as a diode. When the metallic graphene contacts the semiconducting TiO2 layer, free electrons will transfer and flatten the Fermi level at the interface region. As a result, an internal field builds up and the vacuum level, the valence band, and the conduction band are bent. A negative bias voltage (Vr) was applied during the photoresponse experiments, which shifted the Fermi levels across the junction. The photo-induced electrons and holes will separate with the help of the junction, which will increase the current. A built-in electric field is formed at the space charge region. When a forward bias voltage was applied, the electrons were driven to the space charge region, making the Fermi level upward. Thus, the built-in electric field is smaller, which makes the Schottky junction act on the low-resistance stage. As a result, the forward current passes through the junction without any barrier. On the contrary, a backward bias voltage will lower the Fermi level and enlarge the built-in electric field, making the Schottky junction act as an OFF state of a diode. When the UV light is illuminated on the device, the photo-induced electrons and holes are generated in the anatase NSs layer and separated by the built-in electric field at the interface of graphene and anatase. The photo-induced electrons are driven to the space charge region, making the junction smaller. This leads to the generation of a large backward current. Therefore, the photo-current is generated at the backward bias voltage. We have mentioned previously that the Schottky barrier height B between graphene and anatase NSs is only 0.122 eV, which is smaller than the value previously reported in the literature.13 Because the width of the junction is shorter if the Schottky barrier is smaller, the photo-generated carriers only need to move a shorter distance to be collected, which may decrease the response time and lead to the better performance. This leads to a fast response speed when the UV illumination is turned ON or OFF. Moreover, as illustrated in the red dashed box at the top right corner in Fig. 7(a), the vertical stack of the anatase NSs produces a large number of photon wells. This structure with the graphene layer acting as a lid can trap more photons, which makes our detector more efficient. In order to figure out the benefit of NSs, we simulated the absorption spectrums of NSs and NRs using the same parameters as discussed before. Fig. 7(b) and (c) show the basic units of the periodic arrays, such as the length and width. Fig. 7(d) shows the simulated absorption results. The absorption data are normalized to the volume of anatase in both structures. We can observe that the absorption of NSs structure is clearly higher than that of TiO2 NRs in the UV region from 300 nm to 400 nm.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, we took the advantages of the anatase NSs with the high index facets grown by a hydrothermal method, which own numerous merits such as the rich amount of photon wells, single crystals, and stable physical and chemical properties. The anatase NSs array was combined with monolayer graphene synthesized by CVD to fabricate the UV detection device. Due to the built-in electric field generated at the Schottky junction, the electrons and holes generated by the anatase absorbed ultraviolet photons were effectively separated. We studied the temperature influence on our as-fabricated UV detector and obtained the Schottky junction barrier height of only 0.122 eV. The response and the recovery time of our as-fabricated device to the UV light at the different wavelengths were both less than 1 second. The UV detector effectively utilizes the optical property of the monocrystalline anatase. It is UV sensitive, but not response to visible-light irradiation. Through our experiments, it is proven that this type of structure prepared by single-crystalline anatase NSs and monolayer graphene exhibits a good performance in UV detection.
