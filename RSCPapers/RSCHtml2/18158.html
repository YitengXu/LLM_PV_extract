Toward practical solar hydrogen production – an artificial photosynthetic leaf-to-farm challenge

Abstract

Solar water splitting is a promising approach to transform sunlight into renewable, sustainable and green hydrogen energy. There are three representative ways of transforming solar radiation into molecular hydrogen, which are the photocatalytic (PC), photoelectrochemical (PEC), and photovoltaic–electrolysis (PV–EC) routes. Having the future perspective of green hydrogen economy in mind, this review article discusses devices and systems for solar-to-hydrogen production including comparison of the above solar water splitting systems. The focus is placed on a critical assessment of the key components needed to scale up PEC water splitting systems such as materials efficiency, cost, elemental abundancy, stability, fuel separation, device operability, cell architecture, and techno-economic aspects of the systems. The review follows a stepwise approach and provides (i) a summary of the basic principles and photocatalytic materials employed for PEC water splitting, (ii) an extensive discussion of technologies, procedures, and system designs, and (iii) an introduction to international demonstration projects, and the development of benchmarked devices and large-scale prototype systems. The task of scaling up of laboratory overall water splitting devices to practical systems may be called “an artificial photosynthetic leaf-to-farm challenge”.

1. Introduction

A fundamental solution to ever-growing concerns on global warming, air pollution, and energy security is to replace the current carbon-rich fossil fuels with renewable and environment-friendly carbon-free or carbon-neutral energy sources.1,2Fig. 1 provides a brief view of the energy reserves available on the earth, which demonstrates the dominant position of solar energy among all renewable and non-renewable energy sources. The total sum of recoverable energy from all the reserves is around 1% of the solar energy supplied to the earth surface by the sun. Moreover, the solar energy (173000 TW)3 supplied to the total surface (land and water) of the earth is nearly 9600 times larger than today's total energy consumption of the world (17.91 TW in 2017).4 Evidently, solar energy is much more than enough to meet all energy needs for the lifetime of the solar system, but the challenge lies in the development of economically viable technologies for its harnessing, storage, and utilization.

There are several technologies available for solar energy utilization – solar thermal (Fig. 1c), photovoltaic (PV) (Fig. 1d and e), and solar fuels (Fig. 1f and g). Today, PV represents the most widely disseminated technology and is one of the fastest growing industries.7 But PV produces electricity, which has an intrinsic difficulty in terms of storage. Considering the intermittent nature of solar energy, converting solar energy into easily storable chemical energy or a fuel has emerged as an ideal option of solar energy utilization. Among the potential solar fuels, hydrogen (H2) generated from solar water splitting (Fig. 1g) has received attention as the most promising fuel especially for powering fuel cell vehicles.

Hydrogen has a higher gravimetric heating value (141.9 MJ kg−1) than most of the conventional fossil fuels (methane 55.5 MJ kg−1, gasoline 47.5 MJ kg−1, diesel 44.8 MJ kg−1, and methanol 20.0 MJ kg−1), and burns with zero emission producing only harmless water. But H2 is industrially produced mostly by the reforming of fossil fuels (most commonly natural gas containing mainly CH4) that emit a large amount of CO2. Hence, there is an urgent need to establish a sustainable, carbon-neutral, and high efficiency H2 production system on an industrial scale at a reasonable cost.8–13 PEC water splitting has a high potential to become such a solar hydrogen production pathway. Extensive techno-economic analysis (TEA) reports have been recently published to evaluate the viability of H2 production by using renewable energy sources including water electrolysis.14,15

There have already been several comprehensive reviews published on sunlight-driven water splitting with an emphasis on materials development,1,16–19 involved physical chemistry,20 and systems.20–25 However, our present review focuses more on technological and engineering aspects to establish a large scale solar hydrogen production system. We discuss three solar-driven water splitting systems based on photocatalytic (PC), photovoltaic–electrochemical (PV–EC) and photoelectrochemical (PEC) cells. We attempt to evaluate the viability of the practical large scale PEC system from technical, economical, and operational points of view and discuss practical cell architectures and conceptual designs. Large-scale demonstration projects that have taken place worldwide are also introduced. Some of the miniature laboratory devices for solar hydrogen production have been called “artificial leaves”.26,27 Then the subject of this review regarding upscaling to practical systems may be termed “artificial plants” or “solar fuel farms”, thus here we call it “an artificial photosynthetic leaf-to-farm challenge”.

2. Photocatalytic and photoelectrochemical water splitting

2.1 Principle and mechanism

Overall water splitting into H2 and O2 molecules is an energetically uphill reaction requiring a standard free energy change (ΔG°) of 237.2 kJ mol−1, or a potential of 1.23 eV per electron. Two electrons are needed to produce a H2 molecule and four holes to produce an O2 molecule according to eqn (1) and (2):
Absorption of light: SC + hν (>Eg) → e−cb + h+vb
 
Water oxidation (OER): 2H2O + 4h+vb → O2 + 4H+(1)
 
Proton reduction (HER): 2H + 2e−cb → H2(2)
 
Overall water splitting: 2H2O → 2H2 + O2 ΔG° = 237.2 kJ mol−1(3)

SC indicates a semiconductor which acts as a light absorber. Therefore, to perform water splitting, photons with an energy of >1.23 eV (corresponding to λ < 1010 nm) are needed to generate photo-excited charge carriers and carry out redox reactions at the respective surface sites of a photocatalyst as shown in Fig. 2. In practice, the photons are supplied from sunlight with an energy spectrum shown in Fig. 2a. Thus, a photocatalyst must have a band gap energy (Eg = ECB − EVB) large enough to split water, as well as appropriate band positions to drive the two half reactions by using the photogenerated electrons (ecb−) and holes (hvb+) upon light irradiation. Since both half reactions require significant amounts of kinetic overpotentials, a practical photocatalyst should have a band gap energy in the range of 1.6–2.4 eV to drive the overall water splitting.

If the overall water splitting takes place in a PEC cell made of a photoanode (n-type semiconductor) and a photocathode (p-type semiconductor) as shown in Fig. 2c, the process proceeds stepwise: (i) light-induced generation of electron–hole pairs that induce the photocurrents of an n-type semiconductor material as a photoanode, resulting in the formation of electronic charge carriers (excited electrons and holes); (ii) oxidation of water at the photoanode by holes; (iii) transport of H+ ions from the photoanode to the photocathode through the electrolyte, and electron transport through the external circuit to the photocathode; (iv) reduction of hydrogen ions at the photocathode by the electrons. If the semiconductor is immersed in an aqueous electrolyte, band bending near the semiconductor/electrolyte interface provides a driving force for the separation of the photogenerated charge carriers. Band bending can also be generated through the use of a p/n junction or other solid state junctions analogous to a solar cell. The excited holes must reach the surface of the photoanode to drive the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) while the electrons are consumed by the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) at a counter electrode.

A simplified schematic description of the overall water splitting and related technical challenges are summarized in Fig. 2d. Major factors to consider are high solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency, cost competitiveness relative to other H2 production technologies, and stability, which are the major challenges in general for realizing a practical solar H2 production system.28–30 The system is usually composed of 4 components: (i) a photo-electrolysis device, (ii) an electrolyte, (iii) a reactor and (iv) a membrane or separator. The photo-electrolysis device, the core of the system and the main subject of this review, has two major issues of current research – criteria for material selection and efficiency-related factors. The criteria of material selection include the fabrication method, earth abundancy of the elements, cost, and environmental effects. Regarding environmental effects, the solar H2 production does not emit CO2 and is regarded as eco-friendly resembling natural photosynthesis. Extensive reviews and perspectives31,32 are available on strategies and factors to enhance the efficiency, and thus this review will not cover them in detail, but bring the established efficiency and evaluate its viability for practical applications.

There have been a number of studies and reviews20,33,35,40 that report theoretical efficiencies for solar-driven water splitting cells of various configurations. A maximum ηSTH efficiency of ∼31.1% was calculated for PEC water splitting with two light absorbers. The PV–EC system can reach a maximum ηSTH of 28.7% using an efficient bipolar alkaline electrolyzer (73% efficiency) and 2jn PV cells with ∼41% power conversion efficiency (PCE), and an ηSTH of 31.8% using the same efficient electrolyzer and 3jn III–V material-based PV cells with a PCE of ∼43.5%.41 Hisatomi et al.19 showed that the maximum achievable ηSTH is 1.7% for a PC system with a four photon configuration at an apparent quantum yield (AQY) of 1%. However, AQYs of 62, 40 and 30% were needed to achieve an ηSTH of 10% at wavelengths of 600, 700 and 800 nm, respectively.19 The experimental STH efficiencies are discussed in detail in Section 3, but the trend is clear that the highest efficiencies are observed for PV–EC systems, followed by PEC systems, and PC systems show the lowest ηSTH values.

An important analysis was reported by Lewis and coworkers,41 who calculated the maximum efficiency and optimal band gap of light absorbers for PEC tandem systems with three configurations as depicted in Fig. 3; a single light absorber (S2), a side-by-side dual photoabsorber (D4), and a stacked dual photoabsorber (D4).20,40 Previous works reported that the S2 and D4 systems could reach theoretical efficiencies of 29–31% and 40–41%, respectively, thermodynamically without any account of losses.38,40,42 In the S2 system shown, the absorption of a photon (hν) by a semiconductor with a band gap (Eg) creates an electron–hole pair that can be separated by the space charge layer to generate a photo-potential as indicated by the splitting of the Fermi level in Fig. 3a. The maximum theoretical ηSTH for this S2 system is 11.2% for Eg of 2.26 eV. Note that this value falls far short of the thermodynamic limit of ∼30%, highlighting the significant losses associated with reaction overpotentials and the need for better catalysts.

The combination of two absorbers increases the available free energy (Δμex). This free energy must be greater than the energy needed for water splitting (1.23 eV) plus the overpotential losses at both anode and cathode, ηOER and ηHER, for the water splitting reaction to occur. Schematics in Fig. 3c and e show the relation of band gaps and photo-voltages for an ideal two light absorber (side-by-side and stacked configurations) tandem photoelectrolysis device in which the anode is the top absorber. Free energy losses in each absorber (Vloss) and catalytic overpotentials for each half-reaction (η) are fundamental loss pathways. The maximum ηSTH is 15.5% for a dual side-by-side light absorber with photoabsorber(s) of the same band gap of 1.59 eV. In comparison, the maximum ηSTH for a dual stacked light absorber system is 22.8% with bottom and top photoabsorbers having Eg of 1.23 eV and 1.84 eV, respectively. These values provide a baseline for reasonable device efficiencies, which can be projected for materials designs within these ranges of band gaps.40

2.3 Types of solar driven water splitting devices

Sustainable solar H2 production systems are based on three water splitting systems19,23,43–46 as schematically depicted in Fig. 4; (i) particulate PC systems, (ii) PEC water splitting cells, and (iii) PV–EC systems. In a particulate PC system, the photocatalyst powders are dispersed in the medium, and thus the charge transfer pathway is significantly shorter (several micrometers) than usual charge collectors in the other systems (sometimes tens of meters). Since both H2 and O2 are generated from each of the PC particles, a separate gas separator is needed. The system is the simplest with only a light absorber and an electrocatalyst. But when the Z-scheme is used, the system becomes D4 and needs a charge mediator adding complexity.

Typical performance data acquired by these systems are also shown – H2, O2 gas evolution from PC, current matching of the photoanode and photocathode for PEC, and current matching of the PV cell and electrocatalyst (OEC, HEC combined) for the PV–EC system. They also show how to determine the STH efficiency from each system: (a) PC system requires measuring H2 produced per illumination area and time (μmole cm−2 h−1). (b) PEC (photoanode–photocathode) system requires two electrode J–V curves, and the operating point is the photoanode–photocathode cell's short circuit current density, which corresponds to the current density at 0.0 V bias. (c) PV–EC system requires J–V curves of PV and EC, and operating point will be the PV–EC cell's short circuit current density. All data were assumed values for demonstration purposes. For systems like (b) and (c), faradaic efficiency of the reaction is usually confirmed before calculating ηSTH from the current density generated from the system.

In a PV–EC system, independent power is generated by a PV device, which is connected to electrocatalysts. Thus, the performances of PV and electrocatalysts are of primary importance, while charge transfer between them does not matter. The light absorber is completely out of water, and hence, stability against corrosion in the aqueous medium is of little concern. Also, sizes of PV and EC components can be freely modulated because they have very different balance of system (BOS). The BOS denotes auxiliary components needed for operating the device such as piping, controller, fuel or charge transfer roads, which will be depicted in more detail later. A disadvantage of the PV–EC system is the highest complexity in PV designs, but most of the related technologies are already mature and commercialized.

In a PEC cell, photoelectrodes are connected to charge collectors, and only the light absorber side of the photoelectrode contacts the liquid medium while the charge collector is isolated. Fuel separation can be achieved spatially because H2 and O2 evolve from different electrodes. Band bending is quite obvious relative to PC along the photoanode and photocathode for facile charge separation and transfer. A unique semiconductor–liquid junction (SLJ) is established as described above, but it is also possible to use a semiconductor with a buried junction. For these cases of semiconductor–semiconductor junction (SSJ) (e.g. Pt/TiO2/p/n-Si), metal–insulator–semiconductor (MIS) (e.g. Pt/SiO2/n-Si), or electrocatalyst–metal oxide–semiconductor (e.g. Ni/SnO2/p/n-Si as photoanode),47 their driving force of charge transfer comes from the built-in potential from the interface of solid state materials, not from the semiconductor|electrolyte interface. An extensive review on such principles and examples has been published recently by Bae et al.48

For instance, (i) gas evolution for PC, (ii) J–V curves of photoelectrodes and operating point of PEC cells, and (iii) J–V curves of PV cells and electrochemical cells composed of electrocatalyst-based electrodes are presented as typical examples in Fig. 4.

The PEC cells can be operated in different configurations as summarized in Fig. 5. The basic form is either an n-type photoanode–H2 evolution catalytic cathode (HEC) (Fig. 5a), or a p-type photocathode– O2 evolution catalytic anode (OEC) (Fig. 5b). But these single light absorber PEC systems need bias voltage to compensate for the overvoltages and other losses. 3. Current status of solar water splitting at laboratory scales

Extensive research studies of the past 45 years have observed great advancements in converting sunlight to H2 as a future energy carrier by PC, PEC and PV–EC. In addition to a fundamental understanding of the photon-assisted water splitting reaction with photocatalytic semiconductors, high ηSTH's have been achieved enough to consider practical solar hydrogen production through scale-up of laboratory results. In this section, we summarize and evaluate the past advancements and current status of the photon-driven water splitting at laboratory scales.

3.1 Particulate photocatalysis

The PC water splitting is the simplest solar hydrogen production system with just light and water. As in the schematic image of Fig. 6, the photogenerated charge carriers (h+ and e−) should diffuse rapidly within their lifetimes to respective active sites (HER and OER) on the surface of the semiconductor usually decorated with co-catalysts (HEC and OEC) for high efficiency. Even though the PC system needs the smallest complexity for achieving OWS, there are still many requirements for the light absorber of a proper band gap, band edge potentials, and stability in aqueous medium. In addition, it is difficult to control the charge carriers since there is no applied bias.50 Hence, viable light absorbers for PC systems are extremely limited compared to PEC and PV systems, and their efficiency is relatively much lower as a result of this difficulty.

It is also necessary to mention the importance of selectivity functions of photocatalysts in PC water splitting systems. Since HER and OER of water splitting occur on the same PC particle in most cases, backward reactions of HOR (hydrogen oxidation reaction) and ORR (oxygen reduction reaction) occur readily, which are energetically more favorable. Osterloh51 illustrated this issue by explaining two possible reaction pathways (Fig. 6) – usual photocatalysis that includes all possible reactions and “artificial photosynthesis” that we specifically want. While photocatalysts can catalyze both energetically uphill (e.g. H2 production) and downhill (consumption of H2 by hole) reactions, application of HEC and OEC can greatly reduce the chance of the backward reactions to occur. The application of these co-catalyst is in fact quite general in photocatalysis and especially application of HEC is often mandatory to have a high efficiency.

BiVO4 is a well-known water oxidation photocatalyst with a modest band gap (2.4 eV). However, the more positive conduction band (CB) position than the hydrogen reduction potential (H+/H2, 0 VRHE) makes it incapable of overall water splitting. Jo et al.56 simultaneously substituted In3+ for Bi3+ and Mo6+ for V5+ in the host lattice of m-BiVO4 to form Bi1−xInxV1−xMoxO4. The material had a slightly larger band gap energy (2.6 eV) showing a greenish color, but more importantly the CB position was pushed up to become more negative than the hydrogen reduction potential. As a result, the material became an overall water splitting photocatalyst (Fig. 7d), showing an apparent quantum yield (AQY) of 3.2% under visible light irradiation, or H2 production of 17 μmol h−1 H2 with RuO2 photo-deposited as a co-catalyst.56

A single photocatalyst for achieving OWS is extremely difficult to find, thus an ingenious strategy is to employ two photocatalysts to increase their overall OWS efficiency, which is often called as the “Z-scheme” for its resemblance to the natural photosystem (PSII/PSI).50 As a particulate D4 PC system, the Z-scheme OWS evolves H2 and O2 separately from two different photocatalysts (Fig. 8a). Thus photogenerated electrons in a hydrogen evolution photocatalyst (HEP) reduce H+ to H2, whereas holes in an oxygen evolution photocatalyst (OEP) oxidize H2O to O2 and H+. The unused electrons and holes in OEP and HEP, respectively, undergo recombination via an aqueous shuttle redox mediator or solid charge conductive materials.50 However, Jia et al.57 reported electron mediator-free Z-scheme PC water splitting by making an efficient BiVO4–Ru/Rh:SrTiO3 composite as depicted in Fig. 8b. The crystallinity and morphology of m-BiVO4 particles played a decisive role for the high activity of the composite photocatalyst, which showed an AQY of 1.6% at 420 nm under AM1.5 solar irradiation.57

3.2 Photovoltaic–electrolysis (PV–EC) system

The technology is straightforward in that electricity generated from a solar cell is used to convert water into H2 and O2. Other renewable energy sources such as wind72 or geothermal energy could also be used. But solar energy is the most important because it is the most abundant source of energy available (Fig. 1a). The major advantage of PV–EC in comparison to PC and PEC is that PV and water electrolysis are already well-established technologies and a high ηSTH of >10% is easily achievable.73 As a result, partial commercialization of the system was already realized (see part 7, Table 4).

In this system, PV cells and the electrolyzer are connected in series making the operating current and voltage the same for the two devices. Therefore, the crossing point between current (J)–voltage (V) curves for the two devices determines the current (Iop) and the voltage (Vop) under operation. Commonly, the PV junction is electrically wired to the HER and OER catalysts in PV–EC wired systems (Fig. 9a), or the catalysts are directly deposited on the PV component often as a thin nano film or as nanoparticles.46,74 The PV device is used only for the light absorber to generate charge carriers (electricity), which are transferred as direct current to the EC cell specifically to the anode (OEC) and cathode (HEC) for OER and HER. These PV cells are kept out from the water electrolyte to avoid the degradation by water. Unassisted water splitting could be realized with a cell voltage of ∼1.8 V, which is totally supplied by the PV cell. Therefore, the key aspects of the PV–EC device are the open-circuit voltage (Voc) of the PV and the overpotentials of water splitting reactions, which are influenced by HEC and OEC.43,75

The primary consideration for a PV–EC system is highly efficient and stable PV cells and low overpotentials of the EC cell. Fig. 9b shows geometry of various solar cells with record power conversion efficiencies (PCE) above 20%; crystalline n-Si with a high quality passivation layer (TOPcon design), interdigital back contact (IBC) design, silicon heterojunction (SHJ), and a passivated emitter, rear cell (PERC) p–n junction design, GaAs high-band gap layers, CIGS with a transparent conducting layer (TCL), CdTe with interfacial layers, and perovskite with hole-selective top layers.76Fig. 9c shows the Shockley–Queisser efficiency limit of a single junction solar cell under standard conditions of 1 sun illumination.76 The maximum efficiency of 33.7% could be achieved for a semiconductor with a single junction with a band gap of 1.34 eV. With multi-junction solar cells, such efficiency can be higher, and to date, the highest efficiency of 38.8% has been achieved with a GaInAs/GaInP/GaAs/AlGaInAs/AlGaInP five-junction tandem geometry under simulated 1.0 sun condition.76 Fundamentally, the PV–EC system's efficiency comes from the power output of PV, thus less than ∼30% of STH is expectable from PV–EC systems under 1.0 sun condition. However, under concentrated sun conditions (e.g. ∼500 sun), the efficiency can go up to 86.8% in theory, while the highest record achieved is currently 46.0%.76 Unlike PC and PEC systems, PV has the advantage of utilizing highly concentrated illumination, which reduces the actual size of PV, which accounts for the majority of cost in PV–EC systems as discussed in depth in part 4.

Besides PV, EC should also come with a high efficiency with a low potential requirement for conducting water splitting by using efficient electrocatalysts.1,77,78 Water splitting involves HER and OER, which require overpotentials to drive a meaningful degree of reaction. As shown in Fig. 9d, at least 1.6 V is needed to conduct overall water splitting even with highly efficient electrocatalysts. This is a lot higher than 1.23 V of theoretical water dissociation energy. Since the overall efficiency of PV–EC depends on both the power conversion efficiency of PV and the over-potential requirement of EC, reducing such a potential requirement is crucial for maximizing ηSTH of PV–EC.

The generalized J–V diagram in Fig. 9e of a directly coupled PV–EC device graphically identifies the power flows relative to the total incident solar power. The plot represents a highly efficient solar cell (ηpv > 30.8%) with an open circuit voltage Voc and a short-circuit current density Jsc; the total plot area is scaled to the incident solar power (100 mW cm−2). The PV–EC system operates at the intersection of the J–V curves of the solar cell and OECs. At the operating current density Jop, power (Jop ·Vop) is dissipated whereas energy is stored at a rate of Jop·μth, where μth is the thermodynamic reaction potential. The difference represents overpotential losses. Coupling loss is defined as the difference between the solar cell maximum output and the power dissipated in the electrochemistry. These losses are represented by the region J > Jop.75

A very good benchmarking study of electrocatalysts for overall water splitting was done by McCrory et al.79 by comparing various electrocatalysts as shown in Fig. 10. In general, HEC is favored in acidic media while OEC is favored in alkaline media with both low overpotentials (∼0.1 V for HER, ∼0.4 V for OER) and longer stability. The work also emphasized the importance of a prolonged (2 h or more) testing time to ensure the validity of an electrocatalyst's capability. Besides traditional precious metal-based electrocatalysts (Pt, Ir, Ru), there are many recent works reporting base metal-based catalysts of comparable or even better activity than that of precious metals.

Fig. 10c shows the electrochemical properties of earth abundant nanostructured cobalt phosphosulphide (CoPS) electrodes and their HER catalytic performance in 0.5 M H2SO4. The CoPS electrodes made as films, wires, and plates achieved geometric current densities of −10 mA cm−2 at very low overpotentials of −128 mV, −61 mV and −48 mV, respectively, versus RHE. As compared to CoPS films and CoPS nanowires, CoPS nanoplates were more stable for >35 h without any degradation in 0.5 M H2SO4.75 Active OECs are generally metal and metal oxides,78 and IrO2 is the most common practical OER catalyst in acids, which is explained on the basis of an optimum binding energy of the reaction intermediates. Earth abundant OEC materials like NiFe-LDH are active and cost effective.78,80 Corrosion engineering employed to form NiFe-LDH on iron substrates resulted in an electrocatalyst with the best stability (6000 h) in bases ever demonstrated for non-noble metal catalysts.81 The result shows the feasibility of replacing the noble metal electrocatalysts in bases. Fig. 10d shows the iR-corrected polarization curves of NiFe-LDH measured in 1 M and 10 M KOH.81 The OEC can afford current densities of 500 and 1000 mA cm−2 at 300 and 400 mV in 1 M KOH, while in 10 M KOH it requires only 257 and 280 mV to generate the same current densities. The performance is close to that of practical water–alkali electrolyzers and hence meets the requirement of practical large-scale water splitting technology.

Chang et al.83 discussed the design principles for combining PV cells, electrolyzers, and electrocatalysts. All these components can be integrated into solar water splitting devices while minimizing their power loss during the conversion of electrical energy into fuel. The effects of exchange current density, Tafel slope, system resistance, electrode area, light intensity, and PCE of PV are the governing factors. More importantly, an effective combination of independent PV and EC cells is the key for improving the STH efficiency of the whole PV–EC system. Modelling of the PV–EC device showed a maximum STH conversion efficiency of up to 30.8% using a single light absorber.75 Different solar cells such as c-Si, CIGS, GaAs, CdTe and a-Si could reach theoretical limiting STH efficiencies of 26.9, 26.1, 19.7, 19.2 and 26.8%, respectively. The maximum ηSTH for a tandem absorber + electrocatalyst configuration was calculated to be 28.7% with band gap combination of 1.60 eV/0.95 eV and electrolyzer efficiency of 73%. However, ηSTH can be increased up to 31.8% using a 3jn PV cell, and to 62% with an efficient 4jn PV cell.41,83 These have to be considered for practical solar hydrogen production by the PV–EC technology.

A crystalline silicon (c-Si) 3jn solar cell was integrated with NiMoZn (as cathode) and Ni-Bi (as anode) of an EC cell (Fig. 11a), which showed ηSTH greater than 10%.84 Connecting OEC to the p-type terminal and HEC to the n-type terminal of the PV cell, the current–voltage of the PV–EC system has to be equal regardless of the mode of implementation, i.e. wired or wireless. The PV–EC cell was composed of multiple series buried junctions of c-Si solar cell–Ni-Bi (OEC)/NiMoZn (HEC) for both wired and wireless systems. The point of intersection determines Jop and ηSTH of the PV–EC system. The ηSTH was estimated as 2.8% for the 3-cell module and 10% for the 4-cell module of the c-Si solar cell. The maximum current density for H2O oxidation at the anode was 8.13 mA cm−2 with an ηSTH of 9.8%. This approach allows independent modular optimization, after which the PV cell, anode and cathode could be integrated into a monolithic design.84 A typical wired Si PV – membrane electrode assembly (MEA) electrolyzer is shown in Fig. 11b.

Recently, Jia et al.88 achieved the highest ever ηSTH (>30%) with a PV–EC system of InGaP/GaAs/GaInNAsSb 3jn solar cell connected to 2 polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolyzers in series with Pt–Ir as the cathode–anode coated on a Nafion membrane under 42 sun (Fig. 11f). The PEM electrolyzer is composed of a thin solid state ion conducting membrane (e.g. Nafion) instead of an aqueous solution between two electrodes with precious metal electrocatalysts such as Ir, Ru, and Pt. Its operating conditions (20–200 °C, 200–2000 psi) are harsher than those of well-established alkaline electrolyzers (20–80 °C, <500 psi) using cheaper base metal electrocatalysts like Ni, Co, and Fe.72,73 Water flows in the anode compartment of the first electrolyzer and then into the anode compartment of the second electrolyzer, and in the same way, H2 flows from the cathode of the first electrolyzer to the cathode of the second electrolyzer. The current–voltage characteristics of the PV–EC system show beginning of operation (BOO) and end of operation (EOO). This efficient system was stable up to ∼48 h without interruption in pure water. Efficient non-precious metal electrocatalysts have been extensively explored to replace expensive noble metal catalysts like Pt, IrO2, and RuO2 as OEC and HEC for water splitting. For example, NiFe-LDH (OEC) is efficient90 as well as durable up to 6000 h (8 months)81 as discussed earlier. A transition metal hexacyanometallate (MHCM)–NiMoS catalyst as anode–cathode powered by a commercial III–V (InGaP/GaAs/Ge) 3jn solar cell demonstrated an ηSTH of 17.9% and stability for 100 h at neutral pH.91 A wireless and integrated PV–EC device (a-Si:H/c-Si:H 1jn and 2jn solar cell–Ni–Mo or Ni–Co) showed an ηSTH of 3.9% under bias free operation of 40 h in 1 M KOH. The concept was successfully scaled-up up to 64 cm2 device area.92 A miniature device concept was presented by a simple, small and stand-alone bias-free device. A small membraneless solar driven water splitting device (1.35 cm2) consisting of series-connected spherical Si solar cells and non-noble metal electrocatalysts (NiMo–NiFe as cathode–anode) showed ηSTH values of 7.4% and 6.4% using 3-series and 4-series PV solar cells, respectively. This stand-alone and easily-scalable modular PV–electrolyzer device performed a stable operation for 2 h in 0.5 M KOH.93 A PV–electrolysis system composed of a c-Si/a-Si:H heterojunction solar cell (1.9 cm2 for single cell, and 3 series connected for total illumination area 3p c-Si, ∼5.7 cm2) and an MEA electrolyzer with microstructured Ni foam-based earth abundant electrocatalysts showed ηSTH up to 14.2% (Fig. 11b).89 This system containing a Pt–IrOx catalyst layer on a Nafion membrane also demonstrated stable ηSTH of 14.2–14.5% for >100 h, and also comparable ηSTH with Ni–Ni foam electrocatalysts for an alkaline electrolyzer in 1 M KOH. Just usual Ni foam was in fact efficient enough for achieving a high ηSTH. They also showed the size dependence of Ni foam (1–20 cm2) and evaluated stability and efficiency in various electrolytes with pH ranging from 7.0–14.0, showing that pH 14.0 was the best condition.

3.3 Photoelectrochemical (PEC) system

As mentioned earlier, the PEC cell is located between PC and PV–EC in its complexity, choice of material (light absorber), and obtained overall efficiency. In this section, a number of examples of photoanodes and photocathodes are discussed for PEC water splitting cells. Usually photoelectrodes have the following configuration:

Photoanode = OEC/protection layer/dopant:light absorber/substrate

Photocathode = HEC/protection layer/dopant:light absorber/substrate

The substrates are commonly transparent conductive oxides on glass, i.e. fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) or indium tin oxide (ITO). However, metal-based substrates are used in some cases, which are specified as appropriate. Since there are various types of PEC cells, strategies used to develop them are different from each other.102 Therefore, we would like to address only common general backgrounds behind their performance.

3.3.1. Photoanodes. Various n-type metal oxide semiconductors (TiO2, WO3, Fe2O3, BiVO4, etc.) are candidates for practical photoanode owing to their inherent stability in water, which requires lower complexity and less stringent protection.16,31,103 However, these material classes have two major challenges. First, they have relatively large band gaps with poor optical properties. For instance, BiVO4 is currently the best oxide-based photoanode material, but has a relatively large band gap of 2.4 eV,16 while the ideal band gap for the D4 tandem cell's photoanode is 1.84 eV.40 In addition, the absorption properties of the majority of metal oxides are rather poor because of their pronounced indirect bandgap characteristics in comparison with PV grade materials. The second challenge is their poor conductivity, low charge carrier transfer efficiency and small mobility. The charge mobility of metal oxides is only 1.0–0.0001 cm2 V−1 s−1, while that of typical PV grade materials is 100–10000 cm2 V−1 s−1.16,102 A number of strategies have been employed to address these two issues; developing advanced synthesis methods and improving charge carrier dynamics by (i) intrinsic/extrinsic doping, (ii) fabricating a heterojunction of an electron transfer layer (ETL) or hole transfer layer (HTL), (iii) co-catalyst loading (OEC, HEC), (iv) nano-structuring, and (v) surface passivation.103,104 Herein, we introduce some of the highest performing metal oxide semiconductors to assess their viability for practical applications.

TiO2 (3.0–3.2 eV) and WO3 (2.6–2.8 eV) have good electrical properties and stability, but they show moderate PEC performance (Jph of 1–3 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE),16 because of their too large band gaps. Hence here we cite only a few examples for these materials that nicely demonstrated the modification strategies mentioned above. A nanoplate array film of WO3 exhibited unprecedented high PEC performance (3.7 mA cm−2 @ 1.23 VRHE) without adding any cocatalyst or sacrificial reagent.105 Co-doping of tungsten and carbon into TiO2 nanowires, W, C:TiO2 NWs, showed water splitting performance (1.81 mA cm−2 @ 1.23 VRHE) due to the synergistic effects of co-doping. No obvious degradation of the photocurrent was observed during 30 min experimental time interval, and even after over 6 months of storage in a strong base (2 M KOH) solution, indicating excellent chemical stability.106

Thus the major focus has been moved towards narrower band gap materials like BiVO4 and Fe2O3. Selected examples of state-of-the-art photoanodes based on BiVO4 and Fe2O3 are presented in Fig. 12. Among metal oxide-based photoanodes, most remarkable progress has been made in the performance of BiVO4 since the discovery of its photocatalytic water oxidation capability by Kudo et al.107 Its photoelectrochemical and charge transfer properties are not necessarily favorable as an efficient photoanode material. Hence, multiple modification strategies have been addressed including morphology control, formation of composite structures, composition tuning, and coupling with oxygen evolution catalysts.104 In particular, in situ metal organic deposition (MOD), electron transport layers (ETLs) as well as scaffolds such as WO3, and electrocatalysts such as cobalt phosphate (Co-Pi), FeOOH and NiFeOx have contributed greatly to the development of this photoanode material. The current state-of-the-art, solution-processed BiVO4 photoanodes demonstrated by Choi and coworkers108 (double OEC NiOOH/FeOOH109 loaded, nanoporous, N2 gas treated BiVO4) routinely record a photocurrent density of ∼5.0 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE, ABPE of ∼2.1% and high stability of ∼30 h.

In a PEC cell with a NiOOH/FeOOH/N2 gas-treated BiVO4 photoanode (Fig. 12a),110 Lee et al. demonstrated that the use of a V5+-saturated electrolyte (KB + V) inhibited the photooxidation-coupled dissolution of BiVO4, thereby effectively suppressing the anodic photocorrosion of BiVO4. The V5+ species in the solution can also be incorporated into the FeOOH/NiOOH OEC layer present on the BiVO4 surface during water oxidation to further enhance water-oxidation kinetics. The J–V plots measured in the KB and KB + V electrolytes shows a similar trend, suggesting that the presence of H2VO4−/HVO42− species does not interfere with the interfacial charge transfer of holes from BiVO4 to water. The J–t curve shows the photostability of NiOOH/FeOOH/BiVO4 at 0.6 VRHE in KB + V electrolyte over 500 h. The formation of a BiVO4/WO3 heterojunction111 turned out to be one of the most efficient strategies to obtain highly active BiVO4-based photoanodes for improved bulk and surface charge transfer efficiency. In Fig. 12b, a WO3@W:BiVO4 core@shell nanowire (NW) photoanode was fabricated by coating a thin layer of BiVO4 on a vertical array of electrically more conductive WO3 nanowires. Thus WO3 NWs acted as an electron conductor while W:BiVO4 acted as the light absorber. A combination of both achieved high light absorption and a photocurrent of 3.1 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE under 1 sun. This photoanode was stable for 1 h without degradation in 0.5 M KPi at pH 7.112 Light management by nanostructured substrates can also be achieved by using a scaffold such as nanocone or pillar-type TCO. Nanoporous Mo:BiVO4 on top of light trapping nanocone architectures was fabricated by a two-step process113 as shown in Fig. 12c. In the first step, SiO2 nanoparticles of controlled diameters were densely arranged by the Langmuir–Blodgett method and 3D nanocones were formed by a reactive ion etching process. Next, nanoporous Mo:BiVO4 was formed on the nanocone arrays by a sol–gel method. The optimized Mo:BiVO4 nanocone exhibited a very high photocurrent density of 6.05 mA cm−2 at 1.23 V RHE in phosphate buffer solution containing 0.5 M sodium sulfite (Na2SO3) as the hole scavenger. The NiOOH/FeOOH/Mo:BiVO4 nanocone photoanode showed photocurrents approaching this value under water oxidation conditions and a good stability for 5 h. Kuang et al.114 also demonstrated yet one of the longest stability values of BiVO4 (∼1100 h) at 0.4 VRHE by adding Ni cations for regenerative healing of the surface of NiFeOx OEC.

Hematite (Fe2O3) has been one of the most popular materials of choice with BiVO4 for metal oxide-based photoanodes. With a band gap of 2.1 eV (indirect), its theoretical ηSTH is as high as ∼15%. Iron is the 4th most abundant element on the earth and has inherent stability in alkaline water. These attributes would make it an excellent candidate for a practical photoanode for PEC water splitting cells if its poor optical and electrical properties are overcome. In particular, it has an extremely short hole diffusion length of 2–4 nm, which is much shorter than those of BiVO4 (∼70 nm) and WO3 (∼500 nm).24 However, steady improvement in PEC performance of hematite has been made over the past few decades through extensive research led by the research groups of Grätzel and Sivula,23,115–117 making it a highly performing metal oxide photoanode material, yet still having much room for further improvement.32

Fig. 12d shows that photoelectrodeposition of Co-Pi on Fe2O3 photoanodes improves the photocurrent density to 2.8 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE under 1 sun at pH 13.6.118 Both Fe2O3 and Co-Pi/Fe2O3 films showed comparable cathodic shifts of their photocurrent onset potentials. The FeOOH/TiO2/Ti:Fe2O3 nanorods (NR) and branched nanorods (BNR) were fabricated by the hydrothermal method, annealing, doping, interlayer deposition and cocatalyst loading as shown in Fig. 12e.119 The J–V curves of TiO2/Ti:Fe2O3 NR, and TiO2/Ti:Fe2O3 BNR and FeOOH/TiO2/Ti:Fe2O3 BNR photoanodes exhibit photocurrent densities of 1.5, 2.5, and 3.1 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE, respectively, in 1 M KOH under 1 sun illumination. The FeOOH/TiO2/Ti:Fe2O3 BNR also showed noticeable stability by maintaining 95% of the performance after 50 h of operation. Platinum-doped and Co-Pi surface modified wormlike hematite photoanodes are also promising. Stable and efficient hematite-based photoanodes were prepared by a solution method followed by high temperature (800 °C) annealing, and the resulting Co-Pi/Pt:Fe2O3 photoanode showed a record performance of 4.32 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE under 1 sun irradiation.120 Multi-dimensional nano/micro-architectures of Fe2O3 nanoflakes decorated by CoOx OEC showed high photoactivity (3.25 mA cm−2 @ 1.23 VRHE) as a photoanode for water oxidation under 1 sun.121 More recently, Kim et al.6 reported polymer-assisted decomposition deposition (PADD) to make nanoporous (particle size: ∼20 nm) Fe2O3, and Ti doping and post H2 treatment (by decomposition of NaBH4) were applied to increase the bulk charge separation efficiency (Fig. 12f). Eventually, a Ni2FeOxHy/TiO2 overlayer/H2-treated Ti:Fe2O3 photoanode showed a photocurrent density of ∼4.5 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE in 1.0 M KOH, which is yet the highest Jph achieved for a Fe2O3 photoanode. Since Fe2O3 has a theoretical Jph of 12.4 mA cm−2, there is still more than 2 fold room for improvement even though the progress is extremely slow.

Another class of photoanodes is the buried junction, PV-grade semiconductors composed of elements like Si, Ga, In, Ge, Zn, Cd, Cu, P, S, Se, As, Sb, Te, etc.102 which are protected against corrosion. 3.3.2. Photocathodes. Water splitting can also be achieved with a p-type semiconductor as a photocathode for electron transfer toward the electrolyte. The ideal band gap for the D4 tandem cell is 1.23 eV for the photocathode,40 thus lower band gap materials compared to photoanodes are preferred for photocathodes. Fig. 15 shows state-of-the-art examples of photocathodes made from metal oxides, oxy (nitrides), perovskites and PV grade materials. Only a very limited number of metal oxide-based p-type semiconductors are used for photocathodes like Cu2O, CuFeO2, CuBi2O4etc., which show only modest performances.136,137 The most intensively studied metal oxide-based photocathode is Cu2O, which has a band gap of 2.0 eV, and thus can theoretically deliver an ηSTH of 18%. But it is unstable against reduction by photoelectrons and needs proper surface protection for stable operation. Conformal TiO2/AZO protection (buried junction) of Cu2O was first demonstrated by Paracchino et al.138 Electrodeposition of Cu2O on various substrates (Au, Cu, Cu:NiO as a hole transfer layer, HTL) with surface protection resulted in the highest performance for metal oxide-based photoelectrodes of about ∼5 mA cm−2 @0.0 VRHE and stability of ∼2 h.198,199 Luo et al.139 showed that the nanowire morphology of Cu2O greatly increased Jph (10 mA cm−2 at −0.3 VRHE) as compared to planar Cu2O (7.8 mA cm−2) as well as led to marked stability over >55 h by firm protection with RuOx/TiO2/AZO. Very recent demonstration of Pan et al.140 showed an outstanding performance for a metal oxide-based light absorber achieving ∼10 mA cm−2 of Jph as well as Vph near 1.0 V by NiMo/TiO2/Ga2O3/Cu2O as a photocathode. 3.4 Unassisted photoelectrochemical water splitting

As discussed above, the performances of photoelectrodes for OER (photoanodes) and HER (photocathodes) are evaluated under external bias. In practice, we need an unbiased, only solar energy-driven overall water splitting (OWS) system, which can be accomplished by using multiple light absorbers including PV. More specifically, unassisted PEC OWS systems are usually realized by constructing tandem cells of photoanode–photocathode, photoanode–PV, or photocathode–PV. These systems are models of practical large scale solar hydrogen production facilities.
3.4.1. Photoanode–photocathode tandem cell. For this type of tandem PEC cell, a photoanode (n-type for OER) and a photocathode (p-type for HER) are integrated into a cell through a conductive metal wire or wireless connection with a good ohmic contact. The “wired” configuration is convenient for assembling the components, whereas the “wireless” configuration (also known as “artificial leaf”) is more suitable for a compact design. Two main configurations for the photoanode and photocathode have been developed, one is side-by-side or parallel illumination mode (Mode P), and the other is in series, or tandem illumination mode (Mode T).153 Mode P exposes each photoelectrode to the full solar spectrum without requiring a transparent substrate, whereas Mode T more efficiently utilizes solar energy, with the photoanode positioned in front of the photocathode. Modes T and P illumination-based PEC cells are summarized in Table 3 and Fig. 16. Theoretically a PEC tandem system of photoanode–photocathode configuration can achieve a maximum STH conversion efficiency of 29.7% at a band gap combination of 1.60 eV/0.95 eV.41 In reality, it is very difficult to find such ideal materials of suitable band gaps, band positions, and charge transfer characteristics, and thus measured efficiencies are much lower than the theoretical value.

3.4.2. Photoelectrode–photovoltaic cells. As mentioned, PEC tandem cells of photoanodes and photocathodes depend on well-matched bandgap energies and band edge positions of p-type photocathodes and n-type photoanodes. In contrast, photoelectrode–photovoltaic (PEC–PV) tandem cells are more attractive because there is no such requirement. In the PEC–PV configuration, the band levels of the photoelectrode have no direct relationship with the water redox potentials, because PV cells could supply the required potential, and thus there is less limitation on the material type. However, balancing solar light absorption between the PEC and PV cells should be important for optimization. Thus, a PEC–PV tandem cell with an n-type front light absorber is the most common and favorable geometry, since it can grant high photovoltage to the OER that accounts for the majority of overpotential for water splitting.1

Sivula and coworkers realized various PEC–PV tandem cells, e.g. Fe2O3-DSSC (ηSTH = 1.17%) and WO3-DSSC (ηSTH = 3.1%) for spontaneous overall water splitting without any bias.160 There are several reports on the WO3-DSSC tandem device.161 The results illustrate that developing n-type metal oxide semiconductors and their surface engineering are key factors to achieve better ηSTH. Fig. 17a shows the general scheme of a WO3-DSSC tandem cell, which showed unassisted water splitting with 9 h stability. Hydrogen evolves from the Pt surface attached to the DSSC and oxygen directly from the surface of the WO3 photoanode. The crossing point of J–V curves gives an ηSTH of 3.1%. Fig. 17b shows the unassisted water splitting by using an Al2O3/Si:Fe2O3–MAPbI3 perovskite solar cell tandem. The hematite absorbs high energy photons as a front light absorber and low energy photons are transmitted to the perovskite solar cell. The photo-generated holes are transferred to the OER catalyst to evolve oxygen, while the excited electrons flow to the ohmic contact with the perovskite solar cell. Light absorption in the perovskite cell also generates electron/hole pairs. The holes are driven to the back contact to recombine with the electrons from the hematite photoanode. The excited electrons in the perovskite cell are transferred through a wire to a NiMo hydrogen evolution catalyst to evolve H2 to complete the water splitting. This PEC–PV tandem device (NiFeOx/Al2O3/Si:Fe2O3 and MAPbI3/TiO2 solar cell) demonstrated an ηSTH of 1.9% at 1 VRHE with 8 h stability in pH 13.6 electrolyte.162

The PEC–PV tandem cells need a PV cell that produces bias power using light transmitted through the transparent photoelectrode. Usual PV materials are more expensive than materials for photoelectrodes, hence the cost of the PV component would determine the economic viability of the PEC–PV system. The perovskite solar cells (PSC) attract great attention as a next-generation PV cell because they have a rare combination of high efficiency and low price, although their low stability against moisture delays practical applications. Hence, it is natural to attempt to apply PSC to PEC–PV tandem cells.43

In 2013, Mathews and coworkers165 successfully applied a CH3NH3PbI3 (Eg ∼ 1.55 eV) perovskite PV cell in a PEC–PV tandem cell with a Fe2O3 photoanode for unassisted water splitting to obtain an ηSTH of 2.4% in 1 M NaOH electrolyte, which is twice as high as the 1.17% ηSTH of the Fe2O3-DSSC tandem cell reported by Sivula et al.160 The PEC–PV tandem cell contained a single PSC and a Mn-doped hematite photoanode. However, PSC must be immersed in water electrolyte, which induces an inherent stability problem. As a result, these perovskite-based PEC–PV tandem devices cannot be operated for longer than 1 h. Kim et al.166 fabricated a MAPbI3 perovskite PV cell completely sealed in glass and epoxy for a PEC–PV tandem cell with a BiVO4 photoanode modified with extrinsic (Mo)/intrinsic (H2-treatment) dual doping and a cobalt carbonate (Co-Ci) catalyst. The ηSTH was as high as 4.3% in a “wired” configuration and 3.0% in “wireless” configuration, and the cells were stable for at least 10 h. Groups of Kamat167 and Shen168 also fabricated a MAPbI3 PSC-based PEC–PV tandem cell using Co-Pi loaded BiVO4 or BiVO4/TiO2 as a photoanode, which yielded an ηSTH of 2.5% or 1.24%, respectively. Their activity, however, decreased continuously indicating poor stability. A PEC–PV tandem cell (Co-Pi/W:BiVO4–2jn a-Si) was demonstrated with an ηSTH of 4.9% and 1 h stability at pH 7.3.169 A NiOOH/FeOOH/Mo:BiVO4 – PSC tandem cell showed an ηSTH of 6.2% using single PSC with stability of 5 h.113 Interestingly, Luo et al.170 inverted the PEC-PV configuration from the usual alignment to a PV-PEC configuration using a semitransparent perovskite MAPbBr3 (Eg ∼ 2.3 eV) PV cell in front and a CIGS (Eg ∼ 1.1 eV) as the photocathode. The well matched band gaps enabled efficient panchromatic harvesting of the solar spectrum, resulting in an ηSTH of approximately 6.3%.170

In general, PV–EC systems clearly show the highest ηSTH (up to 20% by III/V, ∼10% by Si), followed by PEC (3.7% for PE–PE, ∼8.1% for PE–PV, ∼19% for PE buried), and then PC (mostly around 1.0%) systems. Also there is an appreciable trend of steady improvement in ηSTH over the last two decades. The PEC systems show the largest variation of ηSTH because of the largest system variation. Thus, the systems of PE buried show the most complexity and the highest ηSTH, while PE–PE systems of the minimum complexity show the lowest ηSTH. Unfortunately, none of those experimentally studied systems meet the criteria for practical solar hydrogen production, i.e., a high efficiency above 10%, a long lifetime over 10 years, and scalability up to several km2, which we discuss in more detail in later parts of this paper.

Benchmarking stability is not simple because the reported period of stability does not necessarily mean the lifetime of the device. But the trend is clearly seen. The best stability is observed with PC or PEC systems using intrinsically stable oxide semiconductors or C3N4. However, there are few p-type semiconductors that are stable under photocathode conditions. Many successful cases of protection layer strategies have been reported for unstable p-type or PV grade materials. However, the practical devices require durability as long as 10 years, and longevity of the protection layers for such a time scale is of concern. There is a review specifically addressing the stability issue of photoanodes.48 It also should be mentioned at this point that efficiency and durability are only two of the many factors that determine the economic competitiveness of the system as will be discussed in later sections.

4. Scaling-up of solar water splitting system: artificial leaf to tree and to farm

Due to the rapid progress of past decades in the basic research of efficient and durable solar water splitting materials and systems, it is about time to consider practical applications through scale-up.193 Wind or PV-powered PEM electrolyzers have been well investigated to semi-commercial levels,72 but PEC cells and PC systems have not been explored in such a depth of development. Hence, development of cost-effective, functional, scalable, deployable and practical solar hydrogen production system is in dire need considering the ever-increasing demand for a climate-friendly energy carrier. The PEC water splitting has the potential of meeting the above requirements, yet numerous challenges are involved such as: (i) improved efficiency of photoelectrode materials; (ii) reduced internal resistance losses; (iii) long lifetime, especially of the photoelectrodes; (iv) maximal photon absorption; (v) efficient gas separation and storage; (vi) scaling-up and (vii) reduced plant capital costs.194 All the requirements are needed for reduction of H2 production cost to be competitive with those of the current commercial processes of steam reforming of natural gas (∼1 $ per kg) or electrolysis of water (∼5.5 $ per kg14). DOE's target for the cost of solar H2 production is 4 $ per kg by 2020 for commercialization.15 In this section, we discuss the feasibility of scale up of the laboratory results addressing techno-economic aspects of the solar water splitting technologies and introducing large-scale prototypes and demonstration projects.

4.1 Factors to consider in scale up

First consideration in the scale up is that the overall efficiency of the scaled up device is inevitably lower than the small scale laboratory devices regardless of system type due to unique factors involved in the process as qualitatively discussed in Fig. 22. In Fig. 22a and b, a photoanode and photocathode pair with band gaps of 1.84 eV and 1.23 eV and their tandem cell (D4) are shown, which would achieve an ηSTH of 22% theoretically according to calculation by Pinaud et al.40 The assumed “ideal device” has a total photovoltage of 1.68 V and thus shows an onset potential of −0.45 V in a two electrode mode. Faradaic efficiency of gas evolution was assumed to be 100%. The performance of a real laboratory scale device (scale of 1–10 mm and an assumed size of 0.1 cm2) is lowered by overpotentials of OER (∼0.3 V) and HER (∼0.1 V),77 charge recombination in the bulk/surface of the semiconductor, surface Fermi level pinning (which will be dominant if the light absorber forms an adaptive junction), decreased light harvesting by reflection, and so on. Thus experimentally achievable STH efficiency is reduced to 15% with reduced photovoltage by ∼0.15 V and current loss by 35% (Fig. 22c). It should be noted that the total current load for the device is only ∼1.2 mA, which would not induce any significant potential gradient by reactants (H+), and the distance between electrodes is rather small, thus a laboratory scale device is often free from a large ohmic loss.

When the device is scaled up to a scale of ∼1 m or much larger (10 m2 panel, 106 times larger than laboratory scale), there are problems that are not usually considered at small scales (Fig. 22d). First, making a bigger device requires different fabrication methods, and usually accompanies spatial defects195 and increased total Ohmic resistance,196 which would decrease the efficiency lower than 15%. Another thing that large solar hydrogen production systems need is fuel separation, which is usually done by a membrane separator. Only a few studies are available28–30,41,172,197 on membranes applied for PEC cells, not like the extensive studies in the fields of fuel cells and PEM electrolyzers. Studies showed surprisingly high energy loss from simply applying a good membrane and large potential and current losses are expected from the fuel separator. Also, the light intensity is usually less than the standard 100 mW cm−2 in many places of the world and not uniform over a large area, which will further lower the overall performance of the device.195,196

Finally, the above factors would affect to different degrees depending on cell architectures and materials (light absorbers, electrocatalysts, substrates, housing, electrolytes, etc.) of a scaled up PEC system. Thus from a theoretical (ideal) device to experimental (laboratory), and then to large-size scaled-up devices, continuous efficiency loss is inevitable (Fig. 22e), and it is one of the major tasks of scale up to minimize the losses. Here we assume modest loss in current (33%) and potential (∼0.15 V), which results in STH efficiency of ∼10% for the panel size device, which is the commonly noted efficiency for practical solar hydrogen production by a PV–EC device.

4.2 Techno-economic assessment

There are attempts to assess the state of the solar hydrogen production in comparison with existing technologies of hydrogen production. Ardo et al.198 recently proposed readiness perspective about solar hydrogen production in the time scale of 2050 in comparison with already existing ones. They also tried to identify pathways to implementing solar-hydrogen technologies. Particularly, the fields of market opportunities for short term (10 years of service time) are also noted such as grid-level energy storage, transportation, local or isolated permanent energy systems, military industry, agriculture sector, high-margin products, and for long term like space industry. For specific examples of solar to hydrogen production systems, PV–EC (e.g. Jia et al., ηSTH of 30%88) and PEC (e.g. Verlage et al., ηSTH of 10%175) were compared for a similar light absorber class of III–V semiconductors, which is quite complex but highly efficient. It was shown that the PV–EC system clearly outperformed the PEC cell at the technology readiness level.198

Recently, the Royal Society conducted a technology readiness level (TRL) investigation to show the current state of solar fuel production technologies in comparison with conventional technologies.199 Steam methane reforming and the alkaline electrolyser were rated as TRL 8 and ‘mature’, and solar fuel production was rated at a low TRL of 1–3, meaning it is still in the stage of basic technology research. It is even lower than PEM electrolysis (TRL: 5–7). Yet, they predicted that solar fuel production, especially by PEC or PC, would present lower capital costs. In addition, CO2 tax is also going to be a major issue, for which solar-to-fuel technology is an option that is fully free from CO2. Nuclear energy is also carbon-free,200 but it is out of the scope of this review (Fig. 23).

4.3 Selection of materials

For practical large scale systems, the criteria of materials selection should be different from those of laboratory devices because the cost competitiveness should be a primary concern. Complexity of the device is considered as a major factor for differentiating PV–EC and PEC, which often employ similar materials.190,207 Jacobsson et al.190,207 made a simple criterion whether the photoelectrode or the light absorber works inside or outside of the electrolyte. Recently Yao and Li102 proposed that the materials should be classified as PV grade (c-Si, a-Si, GaAs, GaP, InP, GaInP, CdS, CdSe, CdTe, ZnSe, ZnTe, CuInS2, CuInGaSe2etc.), and non-PV grade (TiO2, WO3, Fe2O3, BiVO4, GaN:ZnO, TaON, Ta3N5, BaTaO2N, Cu2O, CuWO4, CaFe2O4), since PV grade material-based photoelectrodes in fact often share or even have exactly the same structure and geometry as those of PV materials. Ronge et al.15,21 also cited lower complexity as a potential merit of the PEC system over PV–EC, which would directly affect the capital cost of the scaled up system.

The material's complexity could be represented by “layers”, and reducing the number of layers would be a direction of photoelectrode development for scale up with capital cost saving. As schematically shown in Fig. 30, highly active photoelectrodes (photocurrent above ∼10 mA cm−2 at short circuit voltage) always involve more than 2 layers (protection + electrocatalyst) and often 3 layers (charge collector + protection layer + electrocatalyst). This is owing to the fact that light absorbers with good charge mobility and light harvesting capability are PV grade non-oxide materials, which are generally expensive as well as unstable in water and thus need protection.48,208 To reduce the complexity of photoelectrodes, light absorber itself is better to be air- and water-stable requiring less degree of protection.16 Hence, a material's capital cost expected from its complexity should be balanced out for its efficiency. Of course, an efficient, stable, and cheap material would be ideal for scale up, which is not yet available, and in reality, we need to make a smart compromise.

But as shown in Fig. 30, it is rare to find materials other than PV grade that achieved Jph above 8.0 mA cm−2. The value is above the theoretical limit of BiVO4, the best metal oxide-based photoanode at the moment. As the photocathode, Cu2O achieved the highest Jph (10 mA cm−2 at 0.0 VRHE) as well as a photovoltage of ∼1.0 V, which are remarkable for a metal oxide light absorber. Semi-stable Ta3N5 recorded Jph ∼12 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE. Besides them, most of the photoelectrodes showing Jph above 8.0 mA cm−2 are based on PV grade light absorbers with buried junctions and protection layers, which make their complexity rather high. Notably, p- or n-Si, III/V based semiconductors, CIS and CIGS achieved Jph at short circuit above 15–20 mA cm−2. But their photovoltages are rather low, around 0.6–1.0 V. In conclusion, photoelectrodes showing high ηSTH above 10% are limited mainly to PV grade light absorbers of limited stability.

4.4 Fabrication techniques on a large scale

In addition to the choice of materials, the fabrication method should also be suitable for scale-up.175 For scaling-up the PEC water splitting systems, we have to consider first whether we can apply the laboratory fabrication techniques on a large scale such as metal organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD),121,234 molecular beam epitaxy (MBE),60,126,235 atmospheric pressure chemical vapor deposition (APCVD), atomic layer deposition (ALD), pulsed laser deposition (PLD), sputtering, sol–gel or solution based coating (MOD, PADD) via spray pyrolysis, screen printing and spin coating, etc. The technique giving the highest efficiency is not always the choice in large scale fabrication. In general, solution-based methods near ambient conditions are desirable without harsh pressure, temperature, vacuum, and inert gas environment.31,104 Fabrication of PV grade materials (GaP, GaAs, InP, GaInP2, c-Si, a-Si, CIS, CIGS etc.) in a large size requires different strategies as well established in PV applications. However, it should be noted that the III/V material classes made by MOCVD236 and similar methods requiring O2-free atmosphere contributed significantly to the development of high efficiency PEC and PV–EC systems (Tables 2, 3 and Fig. 21).

Many metal oxide semiconductors are inherently stable in air, and nearly the whole synthetic process could be conducted under ambient conditions. 4.5 Charge collectors or substrates

Another basic component of the PEC cell is the charge collector or substrate such as transparent conductive oxides (TCO) like F:SnO2 (FTO), In:SnO2 (ITO), and Al:ZnO (AZO), as well as metals like Ti, Au, and Mo. The TCOs are preferred substrates in order to construct a tandem system of multiple light absorbers for unassisted water splitting. The importance of the selection of the substrate for photoelectrodes has been greatly underrated especially when PV-grade materials are used, because the light absorber–substrate combination optimized for PV applications is harnessed in the photoelectrode fabrication as well (e.g. soda lime glass/Mo for CIGS and sapphire or Ge for III/V, etc.). However, the substrate is an important price factor of the whole PEC cells and efficiency loss (by ohmic loss) from the substrate is significant. Since a photoelectrode always comes with a substrate of equivalent size to the device, a large device needs a large substrate accompanied by high price and high ohmic loss.

Holmes-Gentle et al. estimated the loss by the FTO substrate for selected model photoelectrodes.196 Ohm's law (J = σE) and the continuity of charge were used in order to simulate the electric currents through a conductive medium under an electric field. Here, J is the current density, σ the conductivity (=1/resistance), E the electric field, and Φ the electric potential. The term Q is the current sources/sinks within the medium. Loss can be numerically calculated from the formula ∇J = Q and E = −∇Φ. The analysis assumes 2D geometry of σ since the thickness ranges from 150 nm to 10 μm, and thus the major σ factor would be TCO itself (2.2 × 10−6 Ω m−1). Fig. 33 shows that a large size (L = 5–50 cm) gives a large potential drop for the same photoelectrode. For all model photoelectrodes, efficiency achieved on the laboratory scale would decrease to nearly 15% for a 50 cm device losing nearly 80% of current density. Thus such a potential drop is indeed a serious problem.

4.6 Stability

As discussed above, there are many semiconductor materials (especially PV-grade materials) that show ηSTH way above 10%. But in practice, a more important question is whether such inorganic light absorbers are stable in aqueous electrolytes under irradiation. A highly efficient, low band gap material that is also stable at the same time is yet to be found. For photoanode materials, it is very common that destructive oxidation of the material itself by holes (e.g. CdS + 2h+ → Cd2+ + S) occurs instead of oxidation of water. A material's inherent chemical stability can be predicted from its own oxidation/reduction potential as illustrated in Fig. 35. Thus, when photoelectrons/holes can move to reduction/oxidation potentials, the material becomes unstable due to electron/hole attacks. These routes are blocked for metal oxides like the first case of Fig. 35a and stable for both HER and OER.208 Other non-oxide materials allow either or both of these attacks. This instability problem of non-oxide materials could be mitigated by using a protection layer and efficient co-catalysts like Ni/TiO2/p–n Si or Ni/TiO2/p–n GaAs.175 But the protection measures increases the complexity of the photoelectrode, which will increase the capital cost of the solar hydrogen production device.16

The work also emphasized the importance of anions in the electrolyte, which greatly affected the stability of the photoanode. As mentioned above, Lee and Choi211 fabricated all solution-based photoanode–NiOOH/FeOOH/N2:BiVO4/FTO in vanadium-dissolved electrolyte under a slightly basic condition (pH 9.2) to achieve ∼500 h of stability. But this also requires dissolution of vanadium, which increases the cost of operation. Fe2O3 was also reported with the highest stability of ∼1000 h187 under alkaline conditions (pH 13.6), reported by the PECDEMO project.255 However, all of these recently achieved results indicate meaningful progress toward photoelectrodes stable over the scale of years.

The stability problem is more serious in photocathode materials. Hence, the majority of photocathodes come with protection and charge collecting layers between semiconductor||electrolyte interfaces. For example, RuOx/TiO2/Ga2O3/Cu2O achieved nearly 100 h of stability, which is a significantly improved record from its first demonstration.138 As mentioned, Lewis's group successfully demonstrated ∼1000 h scale stability for both photoanodes and photocathodes made from PV grade materials.131,134,175,259 A TiO2 protected, Pt electrocatalyzed Si photocathode achieved stability of up to 40 days in a highly acidic electrolyte.260 In spite of these successful demonstrations of adequate stability of the protected photocathodes, their practical application in year-scale operation has to be investigated further because exposure of the materials to electrolytes by cracks or pin holes of the protection layer would kill the device almost instantly. In addition, the protective layers, such as Al2O3 or TiO2, are fabricated employing sophisticated and expensive methods instead of cheap solution-based methods.

4.7 Fuel separation and membranes

Fuel separation and transfer is an important factor in practical large scale solar hydrogen production, but has been rarely studied. Fuel separation is mostly accomplished by a membrane electrolyte.172 In laboratory investigations, the performance of the membrane does not affect the overall performance of the device because of the short proton exchange pathway. Of course, this pathway becomes much longer as the device size increases and then the membrane performance becomes much more important. A simple porous ceramic brings gas crossover and unwanted back reactions, as well as possible safety issues. There are two types of ionic membrane; cation-exchange membrane (CEM, e.g. Nafion) and anion-exchange membrane (AEM, e.g. Selemion). The expected duration of the membrane's functional time should be counted as an operational factor, since the price for membrane and fuel separation is far from negligible (for usual planar type, nearly 20% of the light absorber cost).14,29

The importance of membranes was investigated by Jin et al.261 through experiments and simulation. Thus, a “membrane-less” system showed a significant pH gradient and a high crossover of H2 gas (40% in O2 chamber!) as shown in Fig. 37a. Thus, a membrane or at least a similar system is needed especially for the case of high current density and a large amount of fuel like a practical large system.

Usually, OER favors an alkaline electrolyte, while HER favors acidic medium. A way to solve the electrolyte discrepancy problem in the same PEC cell is a bipolar membrane (BPM) made of double-sided two different types of membrane, which sustains different pH of two different electrolytes. Luo et al.99 showed a 2p PSC based PV–EC system operating with CoP HEC in pH 0 while NiFe LDH in pH 14, which are optimum pH for individual components. Similar work done by Jin et al.261 with a CoP/Ti mesh cathode in H2SO4 (pH = 0) and a photoanode of Ni/TiO2/InGaP/GaAs in KBi (pH 9.3) with BPM demonstrated significant stability of ∼100 h (Fig. 37b). Calculation showed a large pH gradient inside of the membrane.

Efforts are also underway to avoid the usage of membranes by using a clever geometry of the system. Recently, Landman et al.262 demonstrated that an auxiliary electrode NiOOH/Ni(OH)2-based capacitor connecting two water electrolysis cells could be operated without a membrane. Powered by Si PV, two different reactors without any membrane achieved an ηSTH of 7.5%.

Planar panel design. In this most common and simplest design, light absorbers are piled up back to back with an internal charge collector. The overall geometry resembles a solar panel dipped in an electrolyte. This geometry, however, requires a good transparency of the two light absorbers and substrates, which would limit the material selection. In particular, p-type materials use a hole transfer layer of low transparency, e.g. CIGS-based photocathodes often use opaque Mo/SLG as a substrate.141,154,178,264 An alternative is a “cappuccino cell”, in which front and back light absorbers face each other and only the front absorber is supposed to be transparent. But this also brings a greater issue for anion/cation exchange since the membrane is between the two light absorbers. Proper selection and design of membrane is needed to minimize the pH gradient. For example, a recent study about BiVO4 (pH 7) with a bipolar membrane and a cathode in different pH (pH 0) showed a moderate current density of 2.0 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE without significant potential gradient loaded to the PEC cell and showed the importance of membranes in such a planar design.265 Xiang et al.197,261,266 implemented a “tilted” design to compensate for this problem at least in part. As mentioned, many studies are of the opinion that PEC devices need their unique geometry rather than traditional planar solar panels because of the necessity of fuel separation and chemical gradient compensation.
Reflection. Pihosh et al.163 proposed that a photoanode facing up and a photocathode or a PV module at 90 degrees to the cell (Fig. 38d) can get reflected light from the photoanode. The position of the membrane was not discussed but it can be spatially separated for the case of PV as the bottom light absorber (HER in the dark). This scheme has less stringent material requirement for the front light absorber since transparency is not important.
Light concentrator. Simulation studies about PEC cells for scale-up showed that the light absorber is the key factor for the overall system's energy balance and expected benefits.40,266 Naturally, reducing the size of the light absorber is the easiest way to reduce the cost. For example, usage of a light concentrator, with 10 times concentrated light, significantly reduced the expected price of hydrogen compared to the planar device.
Vapor feed of water. Vapor feed of water as the reactant may present an advantage for materials and cell operation. For PV–EC, it was already proven that the reaction scale of ∼10 mA cm−2 was viable for the vapor feed system, which had significantly better stability and provided the possibility of using sea water, which would make scaled up device operation greatly cheaper. But the system requires a very different material configuration, reactant permeable membrane||photoelectrode (for PV–EC, electrocatalyst) interface should be well suited, and mass transfer should have much greater dominance. Centralized operation of solar H2 production considers reduced land price and better maintenance for centralized facilities and fuel distribution located in areas of high intensity solar illumination. Thus, centralized solar H2 production would have a similar grid system to PV technology. Thus, the Desertec Initiative proposed a network of concentrated solar and other renewable power plants in Northern Africa and around the Mediterranean region, where solar illumination of high intensity and low seasonal variation is available.15 There is even 1600 km of H2 pipelines already installed in Europe. On top of utilization as fuel for FCV, H2 can also be used for hydrogenation of CO2 to produce hydrocarbons, which also has fuel values. The hydrocarbons are easier for transportation than H2. Large capacity plants are required for centralized application such as large-scale solar H2 plants or solar refinery for solar fuel and chemical production by mimicking natural photosynthesis using CO2 produced from traditional power plants (Fig. 39a).267

Maeda and Domen52 also provided a perspective about solar hydrogen plants to provide a third of energy demand in 2050 as shown in Fig. 40. Estimation showed about 250000 km2 area of solar plant with an ηSTH of 10%. With an individual plant of 5 km × 5 km, 10000 of them would be needed. The 250000 km2 area corresponds to ∼1% of the earth's desert area and produces 570 ton of H2 gas per day.

In spite of the advantages of the centralized solar H2 production facility, the intrinsic merits of solar energy harvesting and storage lie in de-centralized, small capacity systems. Small capacity systems are suitable for residential use (single home application) and storage systems, as in Fig. 39d, whose main elements are rooftop solar energy panels to produce electricity for heating, cooking, lighting, and charging the batteries of the owner's electric cars. An electrolyzer using excess power can convert water into H2 for storage and as a fuel for a residential fuel cell.269,270 Ronge et al.15 also remarked that only ∼8.6% of all urbanized areas are enough for solar panels to meet the world's demand of energy. The decentralized H2 production also mitigates transportation issues of H2 gas.271

Another good example of a decentralized solar H2 production facility is the H2 refueling station for FCV. Fig. 41 shows a conceptual design of solar H2 station that could work around the clock. During the day, sunlight is used to produce solar H2 that is directly used for FCV, and electricity generation by fuel cells and the rest is stored. At night, H2 stored during the day is used for FCV and electricity generation.

Utilization of solar energy on the large scale requires a huge amount of land and competition for space limits the scale up of solar H2 production.199 When land is limited, sea could be used instead for solar H2 production. Floating solar farms for electricity production (Fig. 42) have already been realized in China, Japan, United Kingdom, India and recently Singapore.273,274 Since the majority of the earth's surface is occupied by sea (∼70%), centralized solar H2 production on the sea could be a viable option. Since all solar H2 production needs water, use of sea water as an electrolyte would have great cost advantage. Recent development of electrocatalysts174 and photoelectrodes243 working properly in sea water (pH 8.0) instead of usual electrolytes suggests high promise of using sea water as a potential electrolyte. The device could perform water electrolysis without pumps or membranes and could lead to low-cost sustainable hydrogen production.

A photovoltaic-powered electrolysis device was developed that can operate as a stand-alone platform that floats on open water described as a “solar fuels rig”.275 The device relies on an electrode configuration that allows the gases to be separated and collected using the buoyancy of bubbles in water. The prototype is the first demonstration of a practical membrane-less floating PV–electrolyzer system, and could inspire large-scale ‘solar fuels rigs’ that could generate large quantities of H2 fuel from abundant sunlight and seawater without taking up any space on land or competing with fresh water for agricultural uses.

6. Large-scale demonstrations of PEC water splitting

For solar H2 production, PV–EC has already been demonstrated on large scales. It is rare to find actual demonstration of large scale PEC cells. But owing to the rapid development, in the past decade, of the laboratory scale PEC water splitting technology, there are small but increasing numbers of PEC demonstration projects that we like to introduce in this section. These case studies are invaluable trials of field operation at this early stage of technology development and have a goal to implement prototypes to test the techno-economic feasibility of practical larger commercial scale PEC water splitting plants or solar H2 farms.

As mentioned already, the first barrier of the scale up is that the bigger photoelectrode gives greatly reduced overall efficiency owing to the sheet resistance of the substrate. The substrate with a conductive grid reduces the efficiency loss as practiced for solar cells. Lee et al.256 demonstrated WO3/FTO films up to size of ∼130 cm2 by screen printing of WO3 nanoparticle paste synthesized by sol–gel precipitation (Fig. 43a). Enlarged WO3/FTO suffered great performance decrement – Jph at 1.23 VRHE decreased from 2.63 mA cm−2 to 1.5 mA cm−2 to 1.18 mA cm−2 for photoanode sizes of 0.36, 4.8 and 130.6 cm2. In the meantime, an Ag grid embedded in the film was greatly beneficial – for the 4.8 cm2 photoanode, nearly 130% enhanced photocurrent density was observed relative to a photoanode of the same size with no Ag grid. With the optimized Ag grid-applied WO3 photoanode, a tandem cell of WO3-DSSC achieved a high photocurrent of 150 mA/130.56 cm2 (ηSTH of 1.41%). It is much lower than the laboratory scale ηSTH of 3.24% and 2% for 0.36 cm2 and 4.8 cm2 WO3 photoanodes, but the efficiency is quite high relative to other reports with similarly sized photoanodes. Under 1 sun, the tandem cell of the large area photoanode (130.56 cm2) produced 3 mL min−1 of H2. This work demonstrates the importance of charge collector optimization to minimize efficiency loss of large scale devices.

A full study of actual large scale PEC cell operation was conducted by Vilanova et al.,187 which nicely reflects the majority of issues for scale up of PEC cells that were discussed so far (Fig. 44). They designed a PEC cell with a planar cell architecture named a “CoolPEC cell” and observed fundamental factors related to the operation of PEC cells; (i) fluid dynamics for gas separation, (ii) temperature profile, and (iii) actual large sized Fe2O3/FTO photoanode (50 cm2) operating up to 1008 h, one of the longest successful operations of the photoanode with 0.45 mA cm−2 at 1.6 V, which was provided by a Si solar cell. First, a PEC cell was devised with a housing composed of a stainless steel frame and acrylic body, double side window used as the reactor, working electrode (Fe2O3/FTO as backside window), Pt loaded Ti as the cathode, and an ion exchange membrane. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and actual observation of electrolyte flow was studied to reduce bubble effects under gas generation corresponding to 5.4 mA cm−2, demonstrating that their CoolPEC cell showed negligible effect of light blocking by gas bubbles, which was a serious problem for large scale operation. The CoolPEC cell also maintained a moderate temperature of 45 °C without a significant gradient in the reactor with a constant flow rate of 0.5 L min−1 electrolyte feeding.

The above reactor design and study conducted by Vilanova et al.187 was well implemented in PECDEMO's demonstration of a large scale PEC cell.255 The project investigated a multitude of aspects of PEC cell scale up including development of photoelectrode materials, simulation of the cell and a large scale system. First, large sized photoelectrodes of BiVO4, Cu2O and Fe2O3 were fabricated. For the large area BiVO4 electrode (>50 cm2), Mo metal grids were deposited on FTO glass to improve the charge collection efficiency as shown in Fig. 45a. They performed 4 times of mosaic spray pyrolysis (3.6 × 3.6 cm2) with mask to obtain a modest current of ∼70 mA at a bias of 1.23 VRHE, which was much lower than that observed with a 1.0 cm2 scale device. The PECDEMO project group also designed a 4-stripe type Cu2O photocathode with metal grid lines (Fig. 45c). Two metal grids of Au and Ag showed a significant improvement in the performance of the large scale photocathode relative to a plane photocathode, again indicating the importance of the substrate design.

The large area (50 cm2) photoanodes showed a bit lower performance than laboratory scale (∼1.0 cm2) as expected, but a more serious problem was the instability of BiVO4. Etching of BiVO4 on FTO was observed visually, which is an indicator of corrosion. But when the W:BiVO4 photoanode with Co-Pi as OEC in 0.1 M KPi as electrolyte was replaced with a Co-Ci/W:BiVO4 photoanode, near 70–100 h of stability in bicarbonate electrolyte was observed, emphasizing the importance of good OEC and electrolyte. Moderate stability with the same OEC and electrolyte combination was also reported by Kim et al.166 on a laboratory scale.

Tandem cells of a BiVO4-heterojunction with intrinsic thin layer (HIT) Si PV or Fe2O3–HIT Si PV were tested under outdoor sunlight using a planar “vertical” or solar concentrator based on angled illumination as shown in Fig. 46. The vertical device tested at the test facility SoCRatus of German Aerospace Center (DLR) showed maximum unbiased current density of 1.88 mA cm−2 (ηSTH =2.3%) for BiVO4–HIT Si PV and 0.52 mA cm−2 (ηSTH = 0.74%) for Fe2O3–HIT Si PV.

For the “angled” device tested at UPorto, actual illumination was much larger than the size of the device (∼200 cm2) since a reflection concentrator was used (Fig. 46a and b). Operation was conducted for 15–48 h with no significant degradation of efficiency. Unfortunately, the efficiency observed in laboratory for the 50 cm2 device could not be achieved. Thus, the BiVO4–HIT Si PV tandem cell showed an ηSTH of 0.2% and the Fe2O3–HIT Si PV tandem cell only 0.058% for only the “angled” device (Fig. 46e and f). Even though the light concentrator-based system was expected to achieve much better system operation from system assessment and actual observation for III/V light absorbers, this was not the case here. Indeed, numerous factors exist for scale up of PEC cells, including very large intermittence of illumination. In Fig. 46f, for instance, the variation of ηSTH ranges from 0.42% to 0.12% for 8 days, or by a factor of almost 4.

8. Summary and outlook

Solar overall water splitting has been widely and intensively studied for the past 50 years using photovoltaic, photocatalyst and photoelectrode-driven systems. These technologies achieved significant improvements during the last 10 years reaching solar-to-hydrogen conversion efficiencies ηSTH of nearly 2% (PC), 3% (PE–PE), 8% (PEC–PV hybrid), 19% (buried junction PV) and 30% (PV–EC with PEM electrolyzer), and are still rapidly under progress for under-developed PC and PEC technologies. A technology map is plotted in Fig. 48 for efficiency vs. system complexity. The trend is quite clear; intrinsically stable metal oxide materials in general have low efficiencies and low complexity. The PV-grade materials exhibit high efficiencies, but their higher complexity means higher cost. Thus, efficiency is one of the many considerations to make in the selection of solar hydrogen production technology.
Fig. 48 Technological map showing various photon-driven water splitting approaches for solar energy conversion and specific experimental demonstrations. With achievements in enhancing ηSTH to approach the target value of at least 10%, attention has been paid to the scale up of the solar hydrogen production technologies. In this aspect, the PV–EC technology is way ahead of PC or PEC technologies because many solar hydrogen refueling stations are under commercial operation in many parts of the world. Yet, there are several demonstration projects ongoing in order to scale up PEC solar hydrogen production systems. So far, demonstrations of PEC cells have been done only around 1 m2 scale, but many expected problems and challenges have already been identified in scaling up of materials themselves, balance of systems, and operation issues for serious consideration. The results of those scale-up projects and techno-economic assessments indicate that, at the current level of development, PEC water splitting cannot compete with PV–EC devices. To make the PEC cells competitive, their ηSTH should go above 10%, and lifetime longer than 10 years. This suggests that material developments have to go in parallel with the scale up and demonstration projects for practical application of PEC hydrogen production technology on a large scale.
