Abstract

Solar water splitting is a promising approach to transform sunlight into renewable, sustainable and green hydrogen energy. There are three representative ways of transforming solar radiation into molecular hydrogen, which are the photocatalytic (PC), photoelectrochemical (PEC), and photovoltaicâ€“electrolysis (PVâ€“EC) routes. Having the future perspective of green hydrogen economy in mind, this review article discusses devices and systems for solar-to-hydrogen production including comparison of the above solar water splitting systems. The focus is placed on a critical assessment of the key components needed to scale up PEC water splitting systems such as materials efficiency, cost, elemental abundancy, stability, fuel separation, device operability, cell architecture, and techno-economic aspects of the systems. The review follows a stepwise approach and provides (i) a summary of the basic principles and photocatalytic materials employed for PEC water splitting, (ii) an extensive discussion of technologies, procedures, and system designs, and (iii) an introduction to international demonstration projects, and the development of benchmarked devices and large-scale prototype systems. The task of scaling up of laboratory overall water splitting devices to practical systems may be called â€œan artificial photosynthetic leaf-to-farm challengeâ€.

If the overall water splitting takes place in a PEC cell made of a photoanode (n-type semiconductor) and a photocathode (p-type semiconductor) as shown in Fig. 2c, the process proceeds stepwise: (i) light-induced generation of electronâ€“hole pairs that induce the photocurrents of an n-type semiconductor material as a photoanode, resulting in the formation of electronic charge carriers (excited electrons and holes); (ii) oxidation of water at the photoanode by holes; (iii) transport of H+ ions from the photoanode to the photocathode through the electrolyte, and electron transport through the external circuit to the photocathode; (iv) reduction of hydrogen ions at the photocathode by the electrons. If the semiconductor is immersed in an aqueous electrolyte, band bending near the semiconductor/electrolyte interface provides a driving force for the separation of the photogenerated charge carriers. Band bending can also be generated through the use of a p/n junction or other solid state junctions analogous to a solar cell. The excited holes must reach the surface of the photoanode to drive the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) while the electrons are consumed by the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) at a counter electrode.

In a PEC cell, photoelectrodes are connected to charge collectors, and only the light absorber side of the photoelectrode contacts the liquid medium while the charge collector is isolated. Fuel separation can be achieved spatially because H2 and O2 evolve from different electrodes. Extensive research studies of the past 45 years have observed great advancements in converting sunlight to H2 as a future energy carrier by PC, PEC and PVâ€“EC. 3.1 Particulate photocatalysis

The PC water splitting is the simplest solar hydrogen production system with just light and water. As in the schematic image of Fig. 6, the photogenerated charge carriers (h+ and eâˆ’) should diffuse rapidly within their lifetimes to respective active sites (HER and OER) on the surface of the semiconductor usually decorated with co-catalysts (HEC and OEC) for high efficiency. Even though the PC system needs the smallest complexity for achieving OWS, there are still many requirements for the light absorber of a proper band gap, band edge potentials, and stability in aqueous medium. In addition, it is difficult to control the charge carriers since there is no applied bias.50 Hence, viable light absorbers for PC systems are extremely limited compared to PEC and PV systems, and their efficiency is relatively much lower as a result of this difficulty.

BiVO4 is a well-known water oxidation photocatalyst with a modest band gap (2.4 eV). A single photocatalyst for achieving OWS is extremely difficult to find, thus an ingenious strategy is to employ two photocatalysts to increase their overall OWS efficiency, which is often called as the â€œZ-schemeâ€ for its resemblance to the natural photosystem (PSII/PSI).50 As a particulate D4 PC system, the Z-scheme OWS evolves H2 and O2 separately from two different photocatalysts (Fig. 8a). Thus photogenerated electrons in a hydrogen evolution photocatalyst (HEP) reduce H+ to H2, whereas holes in an oxygen evolution photocatalyst (OEP) oxidize H2O to O2 and H+. The unused electrons and holes in OEP and HEP, respectively, undergo recombination via an aqueous shuttle redox mediator or solid charge conductive materials.50 However, Jia et al.57 reported electron mediator-free Z-scheme PC water splitting by making an efficient BiVO4â€“Ru/Rh:SrTiO3 composite as depicted in Fig. 8b. 3.2 Photovoltaicâ€“electrolysis (PVâ€“EC) system

The technology is straightforward in that electricity generated from a solar cell is used to convert water into H2 and O2. Other renewable energy sources such as wind72 or geothermal energy could also be used. But solar energy is the most important because it is the most abundant source of energy available (Fig. 1a). The major advantage of PVâ€“EC in comparison to PC and PEC is that PV and water electrolysis are already well-established technologies and a high Î·STH of >10% is easily achievable.73 As a result, partial commercialization of the system was already realized (see part 7, Table 4).

In this system, PV cells and the electrolyzer are connected in series making the operating current and voltage the same for the two devices. Therefore, the crossing point between current (J)â€“voltage (V) curves for the two devices determines the current (Iop) and the voltage (Vop) under operation. Commonly, the PV junction is electrically wired to the HER and OER catalysts in PVâ€“EC wired systems (Fig. 9a), or the catalysts are directly deposited on the PV component often as a thin nano film or as nanoparticles.46,74 The PV device is used only for the light absorber to generate charge carriers (electricity), which are transferred as direct current to the EC cell specifically to the anode (OEC) and cathode (HEC) for OER and HER. These PV cells are kept out from the water electrolyte to avoid the degradation by water. Unassisted water splitting could be realized with a cell voltage of âˆ¼1.8 V, which is totally supplied by the PV cell. Therefore, the key aspects of the PVâ€“EC device are the open-circuit voltage (Voc) of the PV and the overpotentials of water splitting reactions, which are influenced by HEC and OEC.43,75

The primary consideration for a PVâ€“EC system is highly efficient and stable PV cells and low overpotentials of the EC cell. Fig. 9b shows geometry of various solar cells with record power conversion efficiencies (PCE) above 20%; crystalline n-Si with a high quality passivation layer (TOPcon design), interdigital back contact (IBC) design, silicon heterojunction (SHJ), and a passivated emitter, rear cell (PERC) pâ€“n junction design, GaAs high-band gap layers, CIGS with a transparent conducting layer (TCL), CdTe with interfacial layers, and perovskite with hole-selective top layers.76Fig. 9c shows the Shockleyâ€“Queisser efficiency limit of a single junction solar cell under standard conditions of 1 sun illumination.76 The maximum efficiency of 33.7% could be achieved for a semiconductor with a single junction with a band gap of 1.34 eV. With multi-junction solar cells, such efficiency can be higher, and to date, the highest efficiency of 38.8% has been achieved with a GaInAs/GaInP/GaAs/AlGaInAs/AlGaInP five-junction tandem geometry under simulated 1.0 sun condition.76 Fundamentally, the PVâ€“EC system's efficiency comes from the power output of PV, thus less than âˆ¼30% of STH is expectable from PVâ€“EC systems under 1.0 sun condition. However, under concentrated sun conditions (e.g. âˆ¼500 sun), the efficiency can go up to 86.8% in theory, while the highest record achieved is currently 46.0%.76 Unlike PC and PEC systems, PV has the advantage of utilizing highly concentrated illumination, which reduces the actual size of PV, which accounts for the majority of cost in PVâ€“EC systems as discussed in depth in part 4.

Besides PV, EC should also come with a high efficiency with a low potential requirement for conducting water splitting by using efficient electrocatalysts.1,77,78 Water splitting involves HER and OER, which require overpotentials to drive a meaningful degree of reaction. As shown in Fig. 9d, at least 1.6 V is needed to conduct overall water splitting even with highly efficient electrocatalysts. This is a lot higher than 1.23 V of theoretical water dissociation energy. Since the overall efficiency of PVâ€“EC depends on both the power conversion efficiency of PV and the over-potential requirement of EC, reducing such a potential requirement is crucial for maximizing Î·STH of PVâ€“EC.

The generalized Jâ€“V diagram in Fig. 9e of a directly coupled PVâ€“EC device graphically identifies the power flows relative to the total incident solar power. The plot represents a highly efficient solar cell (Î·pv > 30.8%) with an open circuit voltage Voc and a short-circuit current density Jsc; the total plot area is scaled to the incident solar power (100 mW cmâˆ’2). The PVâ€“EC system operates at the intersection of the Jâ€“V curves of the solar cell and OECs. At the operating current density Jop, power (Jop Â·Vop) is dissipated whereas energy is stored at a rate of JopÂ·Î¼th, where Î¼th is the thermodynamic reaction potential. The difference represents overpotential losses. Coupling loss is defined as the difference between the solar cell maximum output and the power dissipated in the electrochemistry. These losses are represented by the region J > Jop.75

3.3 Photoelectrochemical (PEC) system

As mentioned earlier, the PEC cell is located between PC and PVâ€“EC in its complexity, choice of material (light absorber), and obtained overall efficiency. In this section, a number of examples of photoanodes and photocathodes are discussed for PEC water splitting cells. Usually photoelectrodes have the following configuration:

Photoanode = OEC/protection layer/dopant:light absorber/substrate

Photocathode = HEC/protection layer/dopant:light absorber/substrate

The substrates are commonly transparent conductive oxides on glass, i.e. fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) or indium tin oxide (ITO). However, metal-based substrates are used in some cases, which are specified as appropriate. Since there are various types of PEC cells, strategies used to develop them are different from each other.102 Therefore, we would like to address only common general backgrounds behind their performance.

3.3.1. Photoanodes. Various n-type metal oxide semiconductors (TiO2, WO3, Fe2O3, BiVO4, etc.) are candidates for practical photoanode owing to their inherent stability in water, which requires lower complexity and less stringent protection.16,31,103 However, these material classes have two major challenges. First, they have relatively large band gaps with poor optical properties. For instance, BiVO4 is currently the best oxide-based photoanode material, but has a relatively large band gap of 2.4 eV,16 while the ideal band gap for the D4 tandem cell's photoanode is 1.84 eV.40 In addition, the absorption properties of the majority of metal oxides are rather poor because of their pronounced indirect bandgap characteristics in comparison with PV grade materials. The second challenge is their poor conductivity, low charge carrier transfer efficiency and small mobility. The charge mobility of metal oxides is only 1.0â€“0.0001 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, while that of typical PV grade materials is 100â€“10000 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1.16,102 A number of strategies have been employed to address these two issues; developing advanced synthesis methods and improving charge carrier dynamics by (i) intrinsic/extrinsic doping, (ii) fabricating a heterojunction of an electron transfer layer (ETL) or hole transfer layer (HTL), (iii) co-catalyst loading (OEC, HEC), (iv) nano-structuring, and (v) surface passivation.103,104 Herein, we introduce some of the highest performing metal oxide semiconductors to assess their viability for practical applications.

TiO2 (3.0â€“3.2 eV) and WO3 (2.6â€“2.8 eV) have good electrical properties and stability, but they show moderate PEC performance (Jph of 1â€“3 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE),16 because of their too large band gaps. Hence here we cite only a few examples for these materials that nicely demonstrated the modification strategies mentioned above. A nanoplate array film of WO3 exhibited unprecedented high PEC performance (3.7 mA cmâˆ’2 @ 1.23 VRHE) without adding any cocatalyst or sacrificial reagent.105 Co-doping of tungsten and carbon into TiO2 nanowires, W, C:TiO2 NWs, showed water splitting performance (1.81 mA cmâˆ’2 @ 1.23 VRHE) due to the synergistic effects of co-doping. No obvious degradation of the photocurrent was observed during 30 min experimental time interval, and even after over 6 months of storage in a strong base (2 M KOH) solution, indicating excellent chemical stability.106

Thus the major focus has been moved towards narrower band gap materials like BiVO4 and Fe2O3. Selected examples of state-of-the-art photoanodes based on BiVO4 and Fe2O3 are presented in Fig. 12. Among metal oxide-based photoanodes, most remarkable progress has been made in the performance of BiVO4 since the discovery of its photocatalytic water oxidation capability by Kudo et al.107 Its photoelectrochemical and charge transfer properties are not necessarily favorable as an efficient photoanode material. Hence, multiple modification strategies have been addressed including morphology control, formation of composite structures, composition tuning, and coupling with oxygen evolution catalysts.104 In particular, in situ metal organic deposition (MOD), electron transport layers (ETLs) as well as scaffolds such as WO3, and electrocatalysts such as cobalt phosphate (Co-Pi), FeOOH and NiFeOx have contributed greatly to the development of this photoanode material. The current state-of-the-art, solution-processed BiVO4 photoanodes demonstrated by Choi and coworkers108 (double OEC NiOOH/FeOOH109 loaded, nanoporous, N2 gas treated BiVO4) routinely record a photocurrent density of âˆ¼5.0 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 VRHE, ABPE of âˆ¼2.1% and high stability of âˆ¼30 h.

3.4 Unassisted photoelectrochemical water splitting

As discussed above, the performances of photoelectrodes for OER (photoanodes) and HER (photocathodes) are evaluated under external bias. In practice, we need an unbiased, only solar energy-driven overall water splitting (OWS) system, which can be accomplished by using multiple light absorbers including PV. More specifically, unassisted PEC OWS systems are usually realized by constructing tandem cells of photoanodeâ€“photocathode, photoanodeâ€“PV, or photocathodeâ€“PV. These systems are models of practical large scale solar hydrogen production facilities.
3.4.1. Photoanodeâ€“photocathode tandem cell. For this type of tandem PEC cell, a photoanode (n-type for OER) and a photocathode (p-type for HER) are integrated into a cell through a conductive metal wire or wireless connection with a good ohmic contact. The â€œwiredâ€ configuration is convenient for assembling the components, whereas the â€œwirelessâ€ configuration (also known as â€œartificial leafâ€) is more suitable for a compact design. Two main configurations for the photoanode and photocathode have been developed, one is side-by-side or parallel illumination mode (Mode P), and the other is in series, or tandem illumination mode (Mode T).153 Mode P exposes each photoelectrode to the full solar spectrum without requiring a transparent substrate, whereas Mode T more efficiently utilizes solar energy, with the photoanode positioned in front of the photocathode. 3.4.2. Photoelectrodeâ€“photovoltaic cells. As mentioned, PEC tandem cells of photoanodes and photocathodes depend on well-matched bandgap energies and band edge positions of p-type photocathodes and n-type photoanodes. In contrast, photoelectrodeâ€“photovoltaic (PECâ€“PV) tandem cells are more attractive because there is no such requirement. In the PECâ€“PV configuration, the band levels of the photoelectrode have no direct relationship with the water redox potentials, because PV cells could supply the required potential, and thus there is less limitation on the material type. However, balancing solar light absorption between the PEC and PV cells should be important for optimization. Thus, a PECâ€“PV tandem cell with an n-type front light absorber is the most common and favorable geometry, since it can grant high photovoltage to the OER that accounts for the majority of overpotential for water splitting.1

4. Scaling-up of solar water splitting system: artificial leaf to tree and to farm

Due to the rapid progress of past decades in the basic research of efficient and durable solar water splitting materials and systems, it is about time to consider practical applications through scale-up.193 Wind or PV-powered PEM electrolyzers have been well investigated to semi-commercial levels,72 but PEC cells and PC systems have not been explored in such a depth of development. Hence, development of cost-effective, functional, scalable, deployable and practical solar hydrogen production system is in dire need considering the ever-increasing demand for a climate-friendly energy carrier. The PEC water splitting has the potential of meeting the above requirements, yet numerous challenges are involved such as: (i) improved efficiency of photoelectrode materials; (ii) reduced internal resistance losses; (iii) long lifetime, especially of the photoelectrodes; (iv) maximal photon absorption; (v) efficient gas separation and storage; (vi) scaling-up and (vii) reduced plant capital costs.194 All the requirements are needed for reduction of H2 production cost to be competitive with those of the current commercial processes of steam reforming of natural gas (âˆ¼1 $ per kg) or electrolysis of water (âˆ¼5.5 $ per kg14). DOE's target for the cost of solar H2 production is 4 $ per kg by 2020 for commercialization.15 In this section, we discuss the feasibility of scale up of the laboratory results addressing techno-economic aspects of the solar water splitting technologies and introducing large-scale prototypes and demonstration projects.

4.1 Factors to consider in scale up

First consideration in the scale up is that the overall efficiency of the scaled up device is inevitably lower than the small scale laboratory devices regardless of system type due to unique factors involved in the process as qualitatively discussed in Fig. 22. In Fig. 22a and b, a photoanode and photocathode pair with band gaps of 1.84 eV and 1.23 eV and their tandem cell (D4) are shown, which would achieve an Î·STH of 22% theoretically according to calculation by Pinaud et al.40 The assumed â€œideal deviceâ€ has a total photovoltage of 1.68 V and thus shows an onset potential of âˆ’0.45 V in a two electrode mode. Faradaic efficiency of gas evolution was assumed to be 100%. The performance of a real laboratory scale device (scale of 1â€“10 mm and an assumed size of 0.1 cm2) is lowered by overpotentials of OER (âˆ¼0.3 V) and HER (âˆ¼0.1 V),77 charge recombination in the bulk/surface of the semiconductor, surface Fermi level pinning (which will be dominant if the light absorber forms an adaptive junction), decreased light harvesting by reflection, and so on. Thus experimentally achievable STH efficiency is reduced to 15% with reduced photovoltage by âˆ¼0.15 V and current loss by 35% (Fig. 22c). It should be noted that the total current load for the device is only âˆ¼1.2 mA, which would not induce any significant potential gradient by reactants (H+), and the distance between electrodes is rather small, thus a laboratory scale device is often free from a large ohmic loss.

When the device is scaled up to a scale of âˆ¼1 m or much larger (10 m2 panel, 106 times larger than laboratory scale), there are problems that are not usually considered at small scales (Fig. 22d). First, making a bigger device requires different fabrication methods, and usually accompanies spatial defects195 and increased total Ohmic resistance,196 which would decrease the efficiency lower than 15%. Another thing that large solar hydrogen production systems need is fuel separation, which is usually done by a membrane separator. Only a few studies are available28â€“30,41,172,197 on membranes applied for PEC cells, not like the extensive studies in the fields of fuel cells and PEM electrolyzers. Studies showed surprisingly high energy loss from simply applying a good membrane and large potential and current losses are expected from the fuel separator. Also, the light intensity is usually less than the standard 100 mW cmâˆ’2 in many places of the world and not uniform over a large area, which will further lower the overall performance of the device.195,196

Finally, the above factors would affect to different degrees depending on cell architectures and materials (light absorbers, electrocatalysts, substrates, housing, electrolytes, etc.) of a scaled up PEC system. Thus from a theoretical (ideal) device to experimental (laboratory), and then to large-size scaled-up devices, continuous efficiency loss is inevitable (Fig. 22e), and it is one of the major tasks of scale up to minimize the losses. Here we assume modest loss in current (33%) and potential (âˆ¼0.15 V), which results in STH efficiency of âˆ¼10% for the panel size device, which is the commonly noted efficiency for practical solar hydrogen production by a PVâ€“EC device.

4.3 Selection of materials

For practical large scale systems, the criteria of materials selection should be different from those of laboratory devices because the cost competitiveness should be a primary concern. Complexity of the device is considered as a major factor for differentiating PVâ€“EC and PEC, which often employ similar materials.190,207 Jacobsson et al.190,207 made a simple criterion whether the photoelectrode or the light absorber works inside or outside of the electrolyte. Recently Yao and Li102 proposed that the materials should be classified as PV grade (c-Si, a-Si, GaAs, GaP, InP, GaInP, CdS, CdSe, CdTe, ZnSe, ZnTe, CuInS2, CuInGaSe2etc.), and non-PV grade (TiO2, WO3, Fe2O3, BiVO4, GaN:ZnO, TaON, Ta3N5, BaTaO2N, Cu2O, CuWO4, CaFe2O4), since PV grade material-based photoelectrodes in fact often share or even have exactly the same structure and geometry as those of PV materials. Ronge et al.15,21 also cited lower complexity as a potential merit of the PEC system over PVâ€“EC, which would directly affect the capital cost of the scaled up system.

The material's complexity could be represented by â€œlayersâ€, and reducing the number of layers would be a direction of photoelectrode development for scale up with capital cost saving. As schematically shown in Fig. 30, highly active photoelectrodes (photocurrent above âˆ¼10 mA cmâˆ’2 at short circuit voltage) always involve more than 2 layers (protection + electrocatalyst) and often 3 layers (charge collector + protection layer + electrocatalyst). This is owing to the fact that light absorbers with good charge mobility and light harvesting capability are PV grade non-oxide materials, which are generally expensive as well as unstable in water and thus need protection.48,208 To reduce the complexity of photoelectrodes, light absorber itself is better to be air- and water-stable requiring less degree of protection.16 Hence, a material's capital cost expected from its complexity should be balanced out for its efficiency. Of course, an efficient, stable, and cheap material would be ideal for scale up, which is not yet available, and in reality, we need to make a smart compromise.

4.7 Fuel separation and membranes

Fuel separation and transfer is an important factor in practical large scale solar hydrogen production, but has been rarely studied. Fuel separation is mostly accomplished by a membrane electrolyte.172 In laboratory investigations, the performance of the membrane does not affect the overall performance of the device because of the short proton exchange pathway. Of course, this pathway becomes much longer as the device size increases and then the membrane performance becomes much more important. A simple porous ceramic brings gas crossover and unwanted back reactions, as well as possible safety issues. Planar panel design. In this most common and simplest design, light absorbers are piled up back to back with an internal charge collector. Light concentrator.
