Abstract

Hybrid halide perovskites have progressed as eminent semiconducting materials pioneering in the domain of low-cost thin film photovoltaics. Nevertheless, the intrinsic instability of the dominant three-dimensional (3D) perovskites impedes their potential applications in industrial-scale production and commercialization. Two-dimensional (2D) perovskite structures have surfaced as an achievable resolution to this contention. In 2D perovskites, large organic cations have been introduced as spacing cations to isolate the inorganic metal halide octahedra layers and correspondingly promote the formation of quantum well superlattices. The photophysical properties of 2D perovskites can be widely tailored through the manipulation of composition, structure, and dimensionality, which provides a compelling field for researchers working in the fields of both chemistry and physics to unveil 2D perovskite materials for high-performance devices. The advancement of low-dimensional Dionâ€“Jacobson (DJ) phase perovskites has lately attracted considerable attention in view of their appealing stability against harsh environmental conditions as well as their competitive performance in optoelectronic applications. Although the initial reports on photovoltaic solar cells (PSCs) based on DJ perovskites have exhibited comparatively poor performance, recent investigations have revealed that they have the capability to realize a high power conversion efficiency above 20%. In this review, we discuss the structural and optoelectronic characteristics of DJ perovskites, followed by a comprehensive investigation on the recent development in DJ PSCs and photodetectors. The conceivable approaches that can be deployed to expedite the advancement of DJ-based optoelectronic applications are also discussed.

1 Introduction

Substantial attention has been concentrated on organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites in view of their compelling and potentially advantageous electrical, optical, and magnetic properties that stem from these materials. In particular, the research on the production of two-dimensional (2D) layered hybrid perovskites through the incorporation of bulky alkyl or aromatic ammonium cations into their inorganic frameworks has revolutionized the field of optoelectronics.1â€“4 Such layered structures not only give the 2D perovskites superior stability, in contrast to their three-dimensional (3D) counterparts, but also exhibit immense possibilities for optoelectronic applications.5â€“10 For instance, solar cells with a high power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 22.9% have been conceived by exploiting octylammonium iodide (OAI) treated 2D/3D perovskites.11 Different from small cations (such as methylammonium (MA+), formamidinium (FA+), cesium (Cs+)) in 3D perovskites, the large bulky organic cations in 2D perovskites present a steric barrier for surface moisture adsorption, which provides an effective route to suppress moisture invasion to the 2D crystal lattice. Thereupon, it is anticipated that replacement of the small hygroscopic organic cations with large hydrophobic organic cations will promote a prolonged lifetime of the perovskites (both materials and devices) under atmospheric conditions. Thus far, 2D perovskites based on various organic ammonium salts have been successively explored, such as the (BA)2(MA)2Pb3I10 (BA: n-butylammonium) Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) 2D perovskite,12 (PEA)2(MA)2Pb3I10 (PEA: 2-phenylethylammonium) layered perovskite,5 and (PDMA)FA2Pb3I10 (PDMA: 1,4-phenylenedimethanammonium) Dionâ€“Jacobson (DJ) 2D perovskites.13 Nevertheless, the 2D perovskites are normally not excellent candidates as light-absorbers for photovoltaic applications in view of their broad optical bandgap and limited charge-carrier transportation in the out-of-plane direction within the 2D structure. Accordingly, it is still necessary to explore the incorporation of novel potential ammonium salts into 2D perovskites to accomplish high-performance and long-term stable optoelectronic devices.

At present, research on the RP phase perovskites is relatively mature, and much effort has so far been devoted to 2D RP perovskite solar cells by advancing the crystal orientation and optoelectronic properties to boost the device efficiency, where some of these systems have shown a performance commensurate with that of their 3D counterparts.14â€“17 The schematic illustration of the evolution from 2D perovskite to 3D perovskite with key components is shown in Fig. 1. 2D RP perovskites have a general formula of (RNH3)2Anâˆ’1MnX3n+1 (n = 1, 2, 3,â€¦), where RH3 represents a large aliphatic or conjugated aromatic alkylammonium spacer cation. It is verifiable that the molecular configuration and the characters of the spacer cations as well as the n values of the inorganic sheets are decisive for the efficiency and stability of 2D perovskite solar cells (PSCs). Researchers have studied the functioning mechanisms of 2D RP perovskites; they have also produced a range of RP perovskites by manipulating the cation type and investigated the influence of the cation on the behavior of PSC. Different from the 2D RP perovskites in which monovalent organic ammonium is deployed as the spacing cation, the 2D DJ perovskites with the common chemical formula of (NH3RNH3)Anâˆ’1MnX3n+1 (n = 1, 2, 3,â€¦) have a relatively shorter distance between the neighboring inorganic slabs, owing to the formation of a monolayer of divalent cations between layers of corner-sharing lead halide octahedra ([PbI6]4+), of both the (001) and (110) orientations. The decreased interlayer distance and the resulting weakened quantum confinement effect as well as the narrowed bandgap will be favorable for charge separation and/or transport as well as light harvesting.

As newly reported halide perovskites, the DJ phases have been developed with several known examples. Since the initial demonstration of 2D RP PSCs (with PEA+ as the spacer cation where a PCE of 4.73% was achieved) by Smith et al. in 2014,5 there have been several investigations with the intention of developing new 2D perovskites as well as tuning their film morphology, improving their crystallinity, and regulating their phase orientation through a variety of approaches, such as solvent engineering, the addition of additives, and tailoring of organic spacing cations.12,18â€“20 2D RP perovskites contain pairs of interdigitated organic spacers between adjacent inorganic sheets, where a van der Waals gap emerges in the bulky spacersâ€™ bilayer construction. By contrast, there are no weak van der Waals interactions in 2D DJ perovskites due to the diammonium groups of the organic spacing layer connecting the adjacent inorganic sheets with a short interlayer distance by strong hydrogen-bonding interactions, thereby excluding the drawbacks of RP perovskites. The structural comparison between the n = 3 crystal structures of RP phases and DJ phases is shown in Fig. 2. The strong interactions between organic spacers and inorganic sheets show the potential for constructing a more rigid structure of DJ perovskites, and hence improved structural stability can be anticipated. These advantages of 2D DJ perovskites over RP perovskites makes them a promising candidate for developing 2D PSCs with high efficiency and long-term stability.

In this review, we discuss the progress of 2D DJ perovskites for a variety of optoelectronic applications. First, we examine the assortment of the dication spacers for the formation of 2D DJ perovskites and discuss the optoelectronic properties of 2D DJ perovskites, including their band structure, charge transport, and optical properties. We also summarize the recent achievements of deploying 2D DJ perovskites in photovoltaic devices and photodetectors. In the last section, we access the challenges and outlooks for further expanding 2D DJ perovskites to an extensive range of applications.

2 Dications for the assembly of 2D DJ perovskites

The 2D DJ perovskites possess great scope for the manipulation if their properties by adjusting the functional organic cations or increasing the perovskite layer thickness. A majority of the organic moieties incorporated in the formation of metal hybrid perovskites have been nonfunctional moieties such as alkyl chains that merely play a bifunctional role by assisting in improving the processability of the hybrids. Incorporation of functionalized organic groups within the perovskites may give the materials new and unusual properties. Examples of this can be seen in many publications.21,22

Investigations on 2D DJ perovskites have been mostly focused on using alkylammonium dications as the spacing cations, although there has also been a general trend in this field of perovskites that consists of incorporating Ï€-conjugated systems as spacer cations.23â€“26 With the relatively high rigidity of the dication monolayer structures, compared with the flexible bilayer of monocations in RP perovskites, the more distorted lead halide octahedra of DJ perovskites are formed. This uncommon structural motif of intensified octahedral distortion induces the broad-band emission of perovskites, which has been observed in their photoluminescence (PL) spectra, in both the (001) and (110) orientations.27,28 However, the rigidity and size of the dications that can be incorporated within 2D perovskites are limited.25,29 Phenylene-p-diamine and a family of similar rigid conjugated aromatic diamines have been demonstrated to be capable of forming a one-dimensional network of lead iodide octahedra.30 Taking this into consideration, some conjugated systems appear to have an over-intensified rigidity, which is too extreme for the lead halide assembly to construct a 2D structure. Research reported by He et al. and Mao et al. has revealed the utilization of asymmetrically methylated N,N-dimethylethylenediamine leads to the formation of 2D perovskites, while another report presented the formation of 1D perovskites based on N,Nâ€²-dimethylethylenediamine, which has a similar diamine structure with symmetric methylation.29,31,32 The balance maintained by these materials to form 2D or 1D assemblies can be ascribed to the varied distance between the ammonium moiety and the halide plane. The alternating methyl units in the asymmetric N,N-dimethylethylenediamine could induce the distortions involved for the stabilization of the 2D perovskite structure, which is consistent with the demonstrated assumption that the closer distance of the ammonium groups to the halide plane would cause larger distortions in the Pbâ€“Xâ€“Pb layer.33,34

3 Optoelectronic properties of 2D DJ perovskites

In the 2D halide perovskites, the organic interlayers function as insulating barriers confining the charge carriers in two dimensions, while they also perform as dielectric moderators that regulate the electrostatic forces exerted on the electronâ€“hole pairs generated from photoexcitation. The introduction of organic spacers promotes the perovskite to form a natural multiple quantum well structure, in which the inorganic layer acts as a â€œwellâ€ and the organic insulating layer acts as a â€œbarrierâ€. The crystal structure of layered perovskite-type quantum well materials with incorporated naphthalene together with the energy diagram of the materials are illustrated in Fig. 3.35 The quantum well width is determined by the value of n, while the quantum well barrier thickness depends on the length of the organic spacer cation and the arising spatial stereoscopic effect. On account of the large organic/inorganic dielectric contrast, the metal halide perovskite materials engender high electron/hole binding energies.

The strong chemical bonding between organic spacing cations and inorganic frameworks has been demonstrated to enhance the structural stability of 2D DJ perovskites.36 Additionally, the reduced distance between inorganic sheets in the DJ perovskites from the monolayer spacer cations can be commensurate with or even two times smaller than the van der Waals radius of halide atoms. This establishes a much more energetic electronic coupling between neighboring inorganic layers, which has been reflected by more efficient charge-carrier transport and larger carrier mobilities/lifetimes compared with the predominant 2D RP perovskites.37

More complicated 2D DJ perovskites with functional organic cations have been reported with interesting optoelectronic properties. It has been reported conjugated oligomers could absorb a complementary proportion of the solar spectrum to that of the inorganic composition while Ï€-conjugated organic cations could enhance charge carrier transport in the 2D RP perovskite light absorbent.23,35,38,39 A quaterthiophene derivative, 5,5â€²â€²â€²-bis(aminoethyl)-2,2â€²:5â€²,2â€²:5â€²â€²,2â€²â€²â€²- quaterthiophene (AEQT), has been introduced into the 2D perovskite structure and demonstrated a strongly influence on the optical properties of the resulting perovskite.23 The quenched PL from AEQT was observed for AEQTPbX4 when the excitonic transition of the inorganic layer presented a high energy of 3.7 eV (X = Cl), which is larger than the singlet and triplet transition energies of the organic AEQT, while the offset between the energy levels of the AEQT layer and the inorganic sheets led to energy transfer and/or charge separation, denoting the formation of an inverted Type I heterojunction of AEQTPbCl4 in which the organic framework had a narrower bandgap than that of the inorganic framework, as shown in Fig. 4a. Changing from Cl to Br to I increased the energy of the valence band, and AEQTPbI4 feasibly formed a Type II heterojunction (Fig. 4b). However, to what extent the Ï€-conjugated organic spacer cations contribute to the optical absorption and emission of 2D perovskites is still under debate.33,39 Earlier investigation also demonstrated that the alignment of the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels of the organic and inorganic layers affected the charge-carrier transfer between them in the metal hybrid perovskites to some extent.40 Recent theoretical work has revealed that the orbitals of the organic spacer cations, especially the aromatic ones, may participate in the energy band structure of the hybrids.26

Hitherto, aliphatic diammonium cations with varied lengths of alkyl chains have been widely investigated in 2D DJ perovskites and have shown exceptional optoelectronic properties. Notwithstanding, it is prevalent that the aliphatic diammoniums are electrically insulating in view of their low dielectric constants. By contrast, the aromatic spacers exhibit a larger dielectric constant compared with aliphatic spacers of a similar size, which could decrease the dielectric mismatch between the organic layer and the adjacent corner-sharing [PbI6]4âˆ’ slabs and diminish the corresponding dielectric confinement in the quantum well.1,42 Kanatzidis and coworkers reported that the aromatic dication spacers based on pyridinium exhibited a smaller exciton binding energy compared with the aliphatic families on account of the increased dielectric constant, resulting in improved performance of the photovoltaic devices.43

4 Layered DJ perovskites in photovoltaic applications

By circumspectly regulating the stoichiometry of the initial precursors, layered DJ perovskites can be prepared with fixed value n, which is characterized as the number of 2D perovskite slabs (defined as the [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedral sheets sandwiched between bilayers of spacer cations). As a critical factor in regulating the optoelectrical properties of low-dimensional perovskites, the value of n influences the bandgap and the absorption in conjunction with the conductivity/mobility and the stability of the perovskites. In most reported PSCs, the value of n is above 3 for devices with an efficiency higher than 10%. It is challenging to achieve phase-pure perovskites with high n values, due to the smaller variations of thermodynamic stability in the higher-n schemes. It is worth mentioning that n = 1 represents the pure 2D structure, while 1 < n â‰¤ 5 is often recognized as a quasi-2D construction. Based on the phase purity, layered perovskites for applications in photovoltaics are normally divided into three divisions: (i) phase-pure layered perovskites, (ii) mixed 2D/3D perovskites, and (iii) 2D perovskite passivation layers for 3D perovskites. Up to now, spacer cations such as alkyl chain diamines, piperidinium derivatives and their counterparts, benzene dimethanammonium, and thiophene-based dications, have been most employed for DJ perovskites.

4.1 Alkyl chain diamines

To achieve high-performance 2D DJ PSCs, the rational design of bulky dication spacer compounds and device engineering are in high demand to achieve high-quality perovskite films along with crystal preferred vertical orientation. So far, aliphatic diammonium cations with different lengths of alkyl chains have been employed in 2D DJ PSCs and have shown significant photovoltaic performance. Selection of the aliphatic spacer cation with a suitable length is one of the rational approaches to manipulate the optical and electronic properties of DJ perovskites. Increasing the cation length leads to variations in the electron and hole confinement potentials in view of the structural distortion, which directly regulates the bandgaps of the related perovskites and influences the charge-transport properties. It was reported that DJ perovskites with organic ammonium spacers with different chain lengths showed different quantum well barrier thicknesses thereby different control of the orientation and quantum well distribution.44 DJ perovskites based on organic spacer cations with a short length, such as 1,3-propanediamine (PDA) and 1,4-butanediamine (BDA), possessed applicable quantum well barrier thicknesses, which exhibited exceptional orientation and more orderly quantum well distribution, granting access to a smooth bandgap transition that led to a lower defect density, longer carrier diffusion length, and higher charge mobility. By contrast, DJ perovskites based on organic spacer cations with a long length, such as 1,5-pentamethylenediamine (PeDA) and 1,6-hexamethylenediamine (HDA), showed thicker barriers, resulting in inferior orientation and multiple phases of the DJ perovskite.

Thus far, BDA has been widely investigated as the spacer cation for 2D DJ perovskites. In 2016, Zhao et al. demonstrated the initial undertaking of using BDA in the formation 2D/3D DJ perovskites.45 A moderate PCE of 9.75% was achieved for the BDA-based 2D/3D DJ PSCs. Later on, Safdari et al. reported the incorporation of BDA in 2D DJ perovskites.46 The BDA-based 2D perovskites were investigated as light absorbing materials in solar cells and a champion PCE of 1.08% was reported. Later, a study investigated by the same group revealed that using BDA as the dication or anion source mixed with lead iodide (PbI2) through different synthetic routes led to the production of 2D (BDA)PbI4 and 1D (BDA)Pb2I6.47 Crystal structures of 1D (BDA)Pb2I6 and 2D (BDA)PbI4 are shown in Fig. 6. Solar cells were fabricated based on these two materials and used as light absorbents in solar cells, with a similar PCE of 1.1% being obtained for both materials. Recently, Niu et al. demonstrated that BDA-based low-dimensional DJ PSCs could exhibit a competitive PCE and a stability comparable to those of established monoamine-based RP PSCs.48 The single-layer BDA enabled the low-dimensional perovskite films to present a high charge-carrier mobility, a low trap-states density, and a near single-crystalline phase crystal. The best-performing PSCs based on BDA showed a PCE of 17.91% with negligible hysteresis, and the PSCs also exhibited a promising stability with 84% of their initial PCE being maintained after examination under ambient conditions and with no encapsulation being applied for the devices. Another recent report by Zhao et al. demonstrated a postdeposition strategy of solvent vapor annealing (SVA) for BDA-based perovskites, with preferential vertical orientation and enhanced grain growth achieved to reduce both bulk and surface defects.49 An NREL-certified efficiency of 18.00 Â± 0.30% was obtained for a PSC containing SVA-treated (BDA)(Cs0.1FA0.9)4Pb5I16. Meanwhile, excellent humidity, thermal, and operational stabilities were also realized for PSCs comprising SVA-treated BDA-based 2D perovskites.

On the other hand, relatively long chain alkyl diammoniums have also been demonstrated as spacer cations to prepare 2D DJ perovskites.44â€“46,53 In the report by Lv et al., HDA was introduced to produce a 2D DJ perovskite capping layer in a 2D/3D perovskite stacking structure.53 The HDA-based 2D DJ perovskite with n = 1 showed a UV stability that was more remarkable than that for a 3D counterpart and a 2D RP perovskite in a 2D/3D perovskite configuration. 4.2 Piperidinium derivatives and analogues

Mao et al. pioneered in the investigation of 2D DJ perovskites based on piperidinium derivatives functioning as the spacer cations.41 The studied piperidinium derivatives were 3AMP (3-(aminomethyl)piperidinium) and 4AMP (4-(aminomethyl)piperidinium). Due to the varied position of the functional moiety in the spacer cations of 3AMP and 4AMP, the as-prepared DJ perovskites exhibited different optical properties, with the 3AMP families showing smaller bandgaps than those of the 4AMP families. 3AMP-based perovskites also showed an enhanced charge-transport performance compared with the 4AMP-based perovskites. Correspondingly, the 3AMP families showed a more advanced PV performance (about 7% champion efficiency for n = 4) than the 4AMP families (about 5% champion efficiency for n = 4).

4.5 Lead-free DJ perovskites

The potential toxic impact of Pb on the environment and public health is one of the hurdles that hinder the development of Pb-based perovskites. One research domain in the halide perovskite area is evaluating Pb substitutes to establish lead-free halide perovskites. Among lead-free perovskites, tin (Sn)-based perovskites possess competitive optoelectronic properties, such as optimal optical bandgaps and high charge-carrier mobilities.64,65 The antecedent example of DJ Sn-based halide perovskites for photovoltaic applications was demonstrated by Chen et al.66 The (4AMP)(FA)3Sn4I13 perovskite showed impressive absorption features, with the absorption edge at 860 nm. An impressive carrier lifetime as large as 18.57 ns was observed for (4AMP)(FA)3Sn4I13, which was considerably longer than the values reported for RP Sn-based perovskites. An intermediate PCE of 4.22% was achieved for PSCs based on (4AMP)(FA)3Sn4I13 without hole-transporting in a mesoscopic structure. Most recently, the same group reported that the incorporation of an optimized amount of 4AMP into FASnI3 thin films promoted the performance of the resulting devices to a higher level.67 It was revealed that with an optimized amount of 4AMP in the thin films the predisposition toward the formation of low-dimensional perovskite phases had diminished. The authors proposed that using the optimum amount of 4AMP (15 mol%) led to an insufficient concentration of 4AMP for forming considerable amounts of unsuitable and unrestrained low-dimensional DJ phases. However, the employed 4AMP provided enough affinity to cover the grain surfaces and grain boundaries of FASnI3, resulting in near fully covered functionalization, as shown in Fig. 13. The obtained thin films showed improved stability against oxygen and moisture as well as decreased nonradiative recombination of photocarriers, indicating the suppressed generation of Sn-defects. A champion PCE of 10.9% was reported for the PSC based on augmented 4AMP-functionalization applied for FASnI3 perovskite thin films. Meanwhile, the PSC showed excellent operational stability with 77% of the initial PCE being maintained after 500 h exposure to continuous 1 sun illumination.

5 2D DJ perovskites in photodetection applications

Generally, both the 2D halide perovskites of RP phases and DJ phases are applicable as the active materials employed in photodetection applications on account of their distinct optical bandgaps, small exciton binding energy, high carrier mobility, and large absorption coefficient.70â€“74 For instance, photodetectors with a tunable photoresponse and short response time (milliseconds) have been reported based on (BA)2(MA)nâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 (n = 1, 2, 3) perovskites.75 Another important feature is the flexibility of the 2D layered structures, which has been demonstrated to give the resulting photodetectors new functionalities. A self-powered photodetector was reported by Liu et al. by using ferroelectric (EA)2(MA)2Pb3Br10 (EA: ethylammonium).76 It was revealed that the strong internal inversion symmetry breaking in (EA)2(MA)2Pb3Br10 led to a high built-in electric field inside the ferroelectric devices, and thereby electrically tunable self-powered photoactive performance was enabled.

Compared with RP phases, demonstrations of DJ-type perovskites for photodetection applications remain limited. The typical shortening of the interlayer distance of DJ phase perovskites could remarkably improve the out-of-plane electron transport, which may be constructive for photodetection technologies. Fu et al. reported the incorporation of 2mept (2-methyl-1,5-diaminopentane) as the spacer cation for the formation of 2D DJ (2meptH2)(MA)Pb2I7 perovskites.77 (2meptH2)(MA)Pb2I7 showed an impressive photoresponse with a remarkably high photoresponsivity (13 A Wâˆ’1 under a bias of 10 V) and a considerable switching ratio (>103), which indicates that (2meptH2)(MA)Pb2I7 can function as a promising candidate for high-efficiency photodetection applications. DJ perovskites based on EDA as the spacer cation were also investigated in photodetection applications.78 When (EDA)(MA)3Pb4I13 perovskites were employed in photodetectors, a champion photoresponsivity of 125 mA Wâˆ’1 under 532 nm irradiation was obtained. A feature worth noting of the EDA-based DJ perovskites films is their great mechanical flexibility, due to which the photocurrent was observed to increase after bending the perovskite films to the radius of 2 mm and 10000 bending cycles on account of the unique binding nature of the perovskites (Fig. 15).

6 Conclusion and future perspectives

Since the pioneering demonstration of the 2D DJ perovskite absorber in solar cells in 2018, the champion efficiency of 2D DJ PSCs has been promoted to over 20% so far, which is comparable to that of 2D RP PSCs. This identifies the significant promise of incorporating 2D or 2D/3D DJ perovskites into photovoltaic applications. Additionally, 2D DJ perovskites also show tremendous potential in photodetection applications. These remarkable accomplishments stem from the progression in the synthetic-chemistry approaches of 2D DJ perovskites and the construction approaches of related solar cells, along with advancements in the scientific understanding of the fundamental properties of the materials. To further propel the performance of 2D DJ perovskites in optoelectronic applications, more endeavors are required to concentrate on fine-tuning of the optoelectronic properties of DJ perovskites by materials design, which can be achieved by the appropriate selection of organic spacer cations, the selection of metal cations, A-site cations, and halides, and alteration of the perovskite sheet thickness. On the other hand, the systematic and decisive theories for the construction of 2D perovskites with well-controlled structures have not been well established yet. The crystal growth control of DJ perovskites through a benign and facile process to attain the preferred orientation of crystals is also of predominant significance since the charge-transport properties of DJ perovskites are highly associated with the orientation of the inorganic sheets within the perovskite films. The demonstrated successful synthetic strategies of inducing the orientation of 2D lattice as well as regulating the packing of crystals in DJ perovskites include A-site cation alloying, the use of additives, and implementing specific solvents in the precursor solution. It is also essential to further understand the fundamental physical and chemical properties of 2D DJ perovskites. Up to now, the understanding of the features of the excitonic properties of charge carriers and conversion of excitons to free charge carriers in 2D DJ perovskites is extremely limited. A systematic theoretical study on the improved long-term stability of PSCs based on 2D DJ perovskites is urgently required as well. Other than PSCs and photodetectors, there are numerous other potential optoelectronic applications such as light-emitting diodes, transistors, and lasers yet to be developed with the incorporation of 2D DJ perovskites. Based on the charge-transport features along both the out-of-plane and in-plane directions for 2D perovskites being very different, methodical investigation in such area is needed for advancing research into various optoelectronic applications based on 2D DJ perovskites.

The unique combination of divalent ammonium cations with perovskite slabs through chemical bonding in DJ perovskites produces DJ perovskites with competitive advantages such as excellent stability and electrical properties. Given the wide varieties of spacer cations with different sizes and functional groups, establishing 2D DJ perovskites with high performance deserves further investigation and presents wide scope for chemical engineering. However, the achievability of integrating diamine spacer cations in DJ perovskites for optoelectronic applications is limited, and only four categories of spacer cations have been reported. On the synthetic-chemistry front, the fluorination of aromatic spacer cations to boost charge transport in 2D RP perovskites appears to be reassuring, and detailed examination has revealed the improved photovoltaic performance of solar cells based on organic cations upon fluorination.90â€“94 It is expected that advancement in the synthetic chemistry of fluorinating organic dication spacers for 2D DJ perovskites will provide a leap forward for research into low-dimensional perovskites. Integrating dication spacers with a Ï€-conjugated functional moiety into DJ perovskites has been demonstrated as an efficacious approach to produce DJ perovskites with exceptional electrical and optical properties. Since the quantum-well barrier thickness plays an imperative role in charge transport in the DJ perovskite layer, the introduction of novel short-length dications with an aromatic unit for DJ perovskites presents the possibility that the resulting DJ perovskites will exhibit a small exciton binding energy because the high dielectric constant of the aromatic cation will lead to a small dielectric confinement effect. This is expected to improve the performance of the related photovoltaic devices because of the improved charge separation and transport. The compositional engineering of mixed A-site cations (even triple cations) in the perovskite sheet, which has been successfully developed for 3D and 2D RP perovskites with a highly improved photovoltaic performance,95â€“98 has also been deployed in 2D DJ perovskites with improved efficiency and stability achieved for DJ PSCs. The synthetic strategy of mixed bulky dications in 2D DJ perovskites has also been reported. Combined with A-site cation alloying, the structural features and optoelectronic properties of the resulting perovskites were fine-tuned, indicating that dual alloying provides a powerful synthetic method for property optimization and promoting the performance of the devices. It is noteworthy that the combination of as-discussed synthetic approaches toward effective property manipulation of the 2D DJ perovskites is anticipated to improve the optoelectronic properties and the performance of the corresponding optoelectronic applications.

The main reason for the inferior performance of 2D DJ perovskites, compared with 3D perovskites, is the hindered charge transport between the perovskite slabs by the insulating spacer cation components. Phase engineering of constructing a 2D/3D perovskite hybrid structure has emerged as a solution to this challenge, which combines the advantages of both components and shows potential in fabricating 2D/3D solar cells based on DJ perovskites with both improved stability and efficiency. However, reports on 2D/3D DJ perovskites are limited. Including 2D/3D RP perovskites in solar cells has given high stability and excellent efficiency, indicating that more efforts towards the optimization of DJ perovskites through preparing the 2D/3D structure will pave the way for achieving considerable advancement in device applications.

Extensive discussions have been carried out to suggest the prominence of the spacer cations and metal halide sheets on the electronic structures of 2D RP perovskites. Lessons learned from research on RP perovskites are that the configuration of the spacer cations regulates the electronic structure of the layered perovskites through the strain on the inorganic structure, which is determined by how the cation fits in the interstitial space between the inorganic octahedra, and the dielectric constant of the spacer cations regulates the dielectric confinement therefore tuning the electronic bandgap and exciton binding energy of 2D perovskites, as mentioned above in the discussion on DJ perovskites. The halide anions and metal substitution in the inorganic sheets can regulate the electronic structures of 2D perovskites as well. Thus far, such research on DJ perovskites is still in its infancy. It has been demonstrated for RP perovskites and 3D perovskites that replacing iodide with bromide or chloride leads to an increase in the bandgap, as it may do in 2D DJ perovskites. Mixed halide systems have been developed to further modify the optoelectronic properties of RP perovskites,99 which presents the possibility of fine-tuning the optoelectronic properties of DJ perovskites through mixed halides. Metal substitutions have also been employed to further manipulate the properties of 2D perovskites. Pb possesses a smaller Pauling electronegativity than Sn, so the Pb electronic states exhibit a higher band structure.100 Sn-Based 2D perovskites have been revealed to have smaller bandgaps than those of Pb-based 2D perovskites, which contain the same organic cation.101

The use of organometal halide perovskites in optoelectronic applications have been under intense study relatively recently. 2D (quasi-2D or 2D/3D) RP and DJ perovskites have also been universally used as light-active materials for photovoltaics, light-emitting diodes, and photodetection. It has been discussed in detail that future research into 2D DJ perovskites includes both the intensification of material design and device fabrication for photovoltaic devices and photodetectors and a thorough fundamental understanding of the physical and photophysical characteristics of DJ perovskites, which conceivably paves the way for other applications in the field of optoelectronics.
