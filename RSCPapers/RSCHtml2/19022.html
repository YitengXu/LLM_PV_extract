Recent advances in electron acceptors with ladder-type backbone for organic solar cells

Jiangsu Key Laboratory for Carbon-Based Functional Materials & Devices, Institute of Functional Nano & Soft Materials (FUNSOM), Soochow University, Suzhou, Jiangsu 215123, China. E-mail: zqjiang@suda.edu.cn; lsliao@suda.edu.cn

Abstract

Ladder-type non-fullerene acceptors have developed very rapidly since 2015 as they have tunable structure, a broad absorption region and good morphology control. The power conversion efficiency (PCE) of non-fullerene organic solar cells (NF-OSC) increased from below 6% to over 13% and, in this review, we analyze the fundamental concepts of organic bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells and different building blocks of non-fullerene acceptors to have an overview of high-performance photovoltaic materials. Meanwhile, we also discuss several design guidelines for chemical construction and modification, which primarily focus on backbone variations, side-chain engineering, and end-cap group substitution. The PCE of several backbones, such as IDT, IDTT, BDCPDT, etc. have successfully exceeded 10% PCE, which demonstrates great potential for future optimization. Side-chains can affect intermolecular packing by forming a 3-D conformation along with the rigid backbones. End-cap groups can regulate electronic properties of the acceptors directly and provide the main electron-transport channels which influence frontier orbital properties. Although design principles and mechanistic understanding of ladder-type acceptors have been explored, there are also many opportunities and challenges of device mechanisms and molecular optimization which need to have deep study and investigation. We hope this review will assist readers to better understand the design principles of high-performance photovoltaic materials, molecular design, and structureproperty relationships.

1. Introduction

We cannot escape hearing the news about energy crisis, climate change, and global warming, which are viewed as the big challenges for sustainable development for human beings.1,2 To prevent global warming by using renewable energy sources is promising and significant for preserving the ozone layer from harmful emissions of burning fossil fuels.3,4 The amount of sunlight that falls on the surface of the earth ranges in amounts of 89000 terawatts per year.5 Thus, solar energy is a green, safe, and sustainable energy source without worrying about a shortage of supply from sunlight.

Although currently silicon-based solar cells are practical in people's daily life, their high cost and fragile features may be problematic for further wide applications.68 An organic solar cell (OSC) which uses organic molecules or polymers to form an active layer could be an important alternative for future renewable energy because the organic compounds are inexpensive and rollable on flexible substrates, which is quite different from a silicon solar cell.914 One major requirement for commercializing organic solar cells is to enhance power conversion efficiency (PCE) of them. In 1986, the first organic solar cell was invented by C. W. Tang with barely 1% PCE.15 During the past two decades, the discoveries of new organic molecular systems, including materials in the active layer and the interfacial layer coordinated together, have boosted PCEs to over 13%.1337 The device fabrication process also changed from vacuum deposition to solution spin-coating; the latter, which is well-known as the bulk heterojunction (BHJ) developed by groups at Cambridge25 and at Santa Barbara,26 contains a blend of a donor and an acceptor to produce a single active layer. This technique allows the formation of a phase-separated interpenetrating donor/acceptor network which offers a continuous pathway for charge carrier transport and wide range for exciton dissociation.27

Nowadays, the rising-star of materials in the active layer of an OSC is the non-fullerene acceptor, as shown in Fig. 1. The term non-fullerene refers to acceptor molecules that do not belong to fullerene derivatives, such as PC61BM and PC71BM, which dominated this field for a long time because of their superior electron transport ability.28,29 After being matched with narrow-bandgap donor materials, the best fullerene-based single junction OSC can exceed 11% PCE.30 Despite this performance, poor absorption, limited variation in energy levels, and relatively high cost are some major drawbacks that deter further improvements in fullerene acceptors.31 Thus, research of non-fullerene acceptors has been experiencing very rapid growth and many effective molecular systems have been developed. At the beginning, an imide-based material is the pioneer of non-fullerene acceptors and many useful units have been developed, such as perylene diimide (PDI)3236 and naphthalene diimides (NDI) blocks.3742 Currently, electron acceptors with ladder-type backbones bearing an AcceptorDonorAcceptor (ADA) motif draw great attention because of their easier synthesis and higher efficiency (>13%).4349

Thus far, several reviews have partially summarized development of some non-fullerene acceptors for OSC applications.44,5055 The target of this review will mainly focus on the design and synthesis of ladder-type acceptors with aspects of backbone modulation, side chain engineering, end group modification, and other effective ways to tune and optimize this new material system. Hopefully, this review will assist readers to better understand the molecular design and structureproperty relationship towards future rational designs of high-performance photovoltaic materials. As for non-fullerene acceptors without the ladder-type backbone or other related areas in OSC, we refer the readers to several more comprehensive reviews.5666 In this review, we first introduce the fundamental concepts of organic BHJ solar cells and their function with a non-fullerene acceptor (Section 2). Finally, we define the ongoing challenges and opportunities in this rising area to move the field forward (Section 5).

2. Physical principles of organic solar cells

2.1 Operating principles of non-fullerene organic solar cells

Generally, there are three steps in the process of the conversion of photon energy to electrical energy in non-fullerene acceptors based organic cells: (a) donor and/or acceptor materials in the active layer absorbs photons and then generates excitons; (b) excitons migrate to the donor/acceptor interface and dissociate to form free charge carriers (electron and hole); (c) free charge carriers transport to and are collected at their respective electrodes, as shown in Fig. 2.67 Due to the short diffusion lengths of excitons, typically 1020 nm in OSC active blend films, donor and acceptor should have a larger interfacial area to produce more photocurrent.

Jmax and Vmax are the current and voltage at the maximum power point on JV curves, respectively. For an ideal cell, the JV curve should have a rectangular shape, and therefore, the FF approaches unity.

V oc generally follows the energy difference between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the donor material and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the acceptor material. It is also affected by the rates of carrier generation and recombination, presence of energetic tail states or trap states, and some other factors that can influence the donor ionization potential or acceptor electron affinity such as the film morphology, surface composition, or the alignment of interfacial dipole moments. Thus, Voc depends on the donor or acceptor materials used, while the other factors also play important roles. Generally, a Voc loss can be observed in an OSC due to the need of driving force to separate the excitons and recombination energy loss.68

J sc is closed relevant to the process of charge generation, which depends on the generation of excitons after light absorption and the process of excitons transfer to the interface of donor/acceptor then separating to free carriers. Thus, other factors that can change the charge generation, charge transportation, charge recombination and charge collection can also influence the Jsc. Materials with increased mobilities can reduce charge accumulation and recombination and could result in enhanced Jsc. Thermal annealing, solvent annealing, and introducing an interfacial layer are also found to be effective for Jsc improvement.69

FF depends on efficient extraction of photo-generated carriers from the device. Besides, FF is least understood among OSC performance parameters and it is also challenging to achieve a high FF. It is found that higher charge mobility, better active layer morphology, and less charge recombination are of significance to increase FF.70

In the following Section 2.22.4, relationships between these several performance parameters of OSCs and energy level, charge mobility, or energy loss will be discussed.

2.2 Energy level tuning of non-fullerene acceptors

Energy level tuning of non-fullerene acceptors is an efficient method to improve non-fullerene acceptors (NFAs) based organic solar cells because the Voc is dependent on the difference of energy level between LUMO of the acceptor (LUMOA) and HOMO of the donor (HOMOD) and Jsc is associated with the band gap (Eg) of donor or acceptor. To get a larger Voc, usually a shallower LUMOA or deeper HOMOD are required so lower band gap of donor-materials (or acceptor-materials) are designed to increase absorption of the solar irradiation spectrum. However, there is a contradiction existing in Voc and Jsc as shown in Fig. 3. If we elevate LUMOA or reduce HOMOD to get larger Voc, then a larger band gap will be formed corresponding to lower Jsc, and vice versa.
Fig. 3 The relationship between Voc and Jsc.

Therefore, it is of significance to explore how to effectively tune the energy levels of non-fullerene acceptors while balancing Voc and Jsc. In ADA type non-fullerene acceptors with a ladder-type backbone, we can modify either the D or A part to tailor the energy levels.7175 For example, incorporating F at the typical A moiety dicycanovinylindan-1-one (IC) would improve intramolecular interaction due to the electron-withdrawing effect of F, and downshift the LUMO energy levels and reduce the bandgap, which is beneficial for light harvesting and enhancing Jsc. However, the downshifted LUMO will cause a smaller Voc.76 But, if the weak electron-donating methyl group is appended to the IC, it can slightly elevate the LUMO level and hence improved Voc in PSCs.77 Adding electron-donating side-chains such as alkoxyl groups to the D moiety is also an effective way to increase the HOMO of an acceptor for a narrow bandgap, but a caution should be taken in case the HOMO of the acceptor could surpass the HOMO of the donor and lead to destructive exciton recombination.78

2.3 Improving charge mobility of non-fullerene acceptors

High charge mobility can improve charge transport while reducing recombination and then achieving a higher FF, thus increasing PCE.79 High-performance non-fullerene acceptor-based OSCs with enhanced charge mobility can be achieved by side-chain engineering, conjugation length extension, fluorination, heavy atom effect (e.g., a selenium atom) and better film processing control. For example, ITIC-Th exhibits a higher electron mobility than its counterpart ITIC with phenyl side chains because the easy polarization of a sulfur atom and sulfursulfur interaction can enhance the intermolecular interaction.80 By incorporating another group (e.g., by extending the conjugation length of an indacenodithiophene (IDT) unit or introducing a Se atom into an IDT unit), Liao et al. designed and synthesized two NFA-based fused 10-heterocyclic ring IDTIDT-IC and IDSe-T-IC, respectively, which has a higher charge mobility than the referent IDT-based NFAs, thus producing a higher PCE.81,82

A thienyl-fused indanone end-group, ITCC, was also rationally designed and synthesized by Hou's group, which shows a closer stacking distance and a higher electron mobility compared to that of ITIC, which contains phenyl-fused indanone end-groups.83 Therefore, to improve the electron mobilities of ITIC, it is also imperative to finely tune the terminal acceptor unit to increase the local intermolecular pp interaction and LUMO distribution on the acceptor unit.84 Finally, through controlling blend ratios, film thicknesses, thermal annealing times, solvents and times for solvent vapor annealing, and additive concentrations, an optimal morphology can be produced which is beneficial for a higher charge mobility and higher fill factor, and thus higher PCE.

2.4 Reducing energy loss of non-fullerene acceptors organic solar cells

Another terminology associated with device performance is energy loss (Eloss). It will be introduced briefly next and more information can be found in a ref. 68. Usually, Eloss can be defined as Eloss = Eg eVoc, where Eg is the lowest optical bandgap of the donor or acceptor as shown in Fig. 4.85,86 Therefore, to increase the PCE, the Eloss should be decreased by reducing the driving force through simultaneously increasing charge transfer energy and decreasing the recombination as much as possible.

For example, a record PCE of 13.1% was achieved for a state-of-the-art non-fullerene OSC by Hou and co-workers, which was fabricated using a fluorinated donor (PBDB-T-SF) and acceptor (IT-4F).87 An interesting fact for ladder-type acceptors in devices is that they seem to have Eloss below 0.6 eV which is smaller than the traditional PCBM-based BHJ OSCs.8892 Usually, HOMOHOMO and LUMOLUMO offsets should be above 0.3 eV to have an effective exciton separation.93 However, some systems having a smaller offset than 0.3 eV (even a near zero HOMO or LUMO offset) have been reported recently. For example, Liao et al. reported IDTIDSe-IC which enabled a PCE of 8.02% with a Voc of 0.91 V when combined with polymer J51 as donor. What's more, the HOMO difference between J51 and IDTIDSe-IC is as small as 0.1 eV and the hole transfer from IDTIDSe-IC to J51 can still be efficient.94 Another work is reported by Yan et al. which has a high PCE of 9.5% and internal quantum efficiency (IQE) of nearly 90% in OSCs when the LUMO difference is negligible.90

3. Performances of OSCs based on ladder-type electron acceptors with different backbones

A ladder-type backbone is the core to constructing new acceptors and we can classify the reported molecules in this aspect. A tricyclic backbone is perhaps the smallest unit in this family. A free rotated biphenyl unit, for example, can be fused by carbon, silicon, or nitrogen to form corresponding fluorene, silafluorene, and carbazole blocks which showed obviously better planarity than the parent biphenyl unit. In 2015, S. V. Bhosale et al. reported a non-fullerene electron acceptor N5 as shown in Scheme 1.95 The core of the N5 is a fused dibenzosilole and two 2,3-dihydro-1H-indene-1,3-dione groups acted as electron withdrawing groups (EWGs). It is should be noted that two thiophene groups were inserted between the donor part and acceptors; these thiophene units can be viewed as bridges between DA, and surely, they could also minimize the energy gap for their electron-rich features. The thermal stability of N5 was excellent decomposition temperature ca. 393 C from DSC analysis, and the authors used it to match with a classical donor polymer, P3HT. The blended film of P3HT:N5 was a good nanoscale interpenetrating network with a broad spectrum IPCE over the visible range and PCE was 2.76%, which represented the highest value reported at that time for solution-processable BHJ devices using DBS-based non-fullerene acceptors.

As compared to silafluorene, the fluorene unit is more accessible in organic synthesis. Wan and Chen et al. reported a non-fullerene acceptor, DTBTF, whose core was an alkylated fluorene.96 This DTBTF adopted two thiobarbituric acids as terminal groups and thiophene, the same as N5, as the electron-rich bridge. An all-small-molecule organic solar cell based on DR3TSBDT:DTBTF blended film showed a PCE of 3.84%. Additionally, a Voc as high as 1.15 V with a low energy loss of only 0.59 eV was obtained. Based on the fluorene core, Zhang and Chen et al. also reported other DICTiF97 and DCF-2HT,98 in which the latter molecule had additional hexyl side chains on the thiophene bridge. With the same donor polymer PBDB-T, DICTiF can achieve higher PCE of 7.11% than that of DCF-2HT, which was mainly due to the higher FF of the DICTiF-based device, but the authors did not give further analysis on this difference. Two fluorene units can be arranged to be a 9,9-spirobifluorene to form a 3D conformation. Based on this new unit, a series of molecules have been developed. Zhan et al. systematically synthesized two isomers, SBF1 and SBF2, but both exhibited very low performance (PCE < 1%). The linear conjugated SBF2 has a little higher Jsc, which could be ascribed to its wider absorption. Grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (GIXRD) shows that SBF2 neat film has strong (100) and (010) diffraction peaks because of its high crystallization while SBF1 is amorphous, and the electron mobility of SBF2 (7.2 104 cm2 V1 s1) is higher than that of amorphous SBF1 (6.1 105 cm2 V1 s1). That is mainly because the coplanar molecular structure of SBF2 makes it easy to be crystalline due to stacking among molecules.99 Yan and Peng also adopted this linear conjugated backbone, but they changed the bridge and EWG. The authors believed that the bulky 3D spirobifluorene unit can suppress molecular aggregation of SFBRCN. This acceptor exhibited a relatively wide optical bandgap of 2.03 eV, which provided a complementary absorption to commonly used low bandgap donor polymers, such as PTB7-Th. As a result, a high PCE of 10.26% was achieved.100 Cho and Choi et al. utilized an unconventional block indenothiophene with asymmetrical conformation to construct the acceptor PhITBD. The asymmetric structure was employed to enhance the internal morphology and efficient tuning of the energy levels. Another symmetrical acceptor, IDT-2BM, was also synthesized for comparison. Under optimized conditions the PhITBD blend exhibited a high PCE of 6.57% with a Jsc of 14.07 mA cm2, whereas the IDT-2BM blend showed a PCE of 3.97% and a Jsc of 9.29 mA cm2.101

After further enlarging the fused ring system to five-cycle ladder-type acceptors, the device performance exhibited big progress. The reason why the four-cycle ladder backbone is absent in this design is probably due to a synthetic difficulty to balance the arrangement of soluble side-chains and mobility from the planar backbone. Herein, the most reported and effective backbone in ladder-type acceptors with five-cyclic rings is indaceno[2,1-b:6,5-b]dithiophene (IDT). In 2015, Zhan et al. reported a novel acceptor DC-IDT2T based on this IDT backbone, in which they pointed out that the resulting material by appending a 1,1-dicyanomethylene-3-indanone (DC) group could have strong electron-withdrawing ability and seemed appropriate for electron acceptor application, which is shown in Scheme 2. Finally, BHJ OSCs based on the PBDTTT-C-T:DC-IDT2T blend yielded a PCE as high as 3.93%. This work demonstrated the great potential of the IDT and DC moieties for use as building blocks for constructing novel non-fullerene acceptors.102 Afterwards, Zhan and Krebs collaborated on using this system in roll-coating fullerene-free OSCs. The best PCE of 1.02% was achieved on a large device area (1 cm2), flexible substrates, ITO free, vacuum-free, and ambient conditions. As compared to the fullerene control devices, the polymer/non-fullerene device showed much better stability (maintained 85% PCE > 50% PCE after 180 h). These preliminary results demonstrated that non-fullerene small molecule acceptors could be promising for industrial manufacture of high-performance OSCs.103 Then, since they used similar technology but a different material system (PTB7-Th & IEIC), they improved the PCE up to 1.79% on an ITO-free substrate, indicating the potential for large scale production of high performance OSCs.104 Here, a new acceptor IEIC was also previously reported by Zhan et al., which differed DC-IDT2T from the two alkyl side-chains on the bridge thiophenes and kept the central ladder-type IDT backbone and end-cap groups constant. IEIC exhibited good thermal stability, strong absorption in the 500750 nm region with an extinction coefficient of 1.1 105 M1 cm1 at 672 nm, deep LUMO energy level (3.82 eV) close to those of fullerenes, and a relatively high electron mobility of 2.1 104 cm2 V1 s1. As a result, a maximum PCE of 6.31% was achieved without any post-treatments, which was the highest PCE then for PSCs based on small molecule non-fullerene electron acceptors. It should be noted that this performance was obtained under a low FF of 0.48, implying there was room for IEIC to improve performance.105 This IEIC acceptor was further investigated by Yan et al.; they used the polymer PffT2-FTAZ-2DT developed in their group as donor and demonstrated a high efficiency of 7.3%. To be specific, they obtained a FF of 0.59, obviously larger than that in Zhan's work, indicating a relatively good morphology and charge collection ability of the solar cell device.106 And the IEIC was also suitable for other polymer systems like diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) units and tandem solar cells.107,108 Around the side-chain on the bridge thiophene a systematic study was carried out by Zhan et al. involving different lengths of the straight chains. Via a steric hindrance effect, an increase in carbon number of the side chains slightly downshifted HOMO energy levels, upshifted LUMO energy levels, and thereby gradually increased Voc from 0.84 to 0.95 V.109 Zhan et al. also developed another similar acceptor, DC-IDT2F, by replacing the thiophene bridge with a furan unit, but its performance was inferior to its thiophene analogues, which is shown in Scheme 2.110 Hou et al. reported IEICO by introducing alkoxy side chains on the bridge thiophene; because of the strong electron-donating property of the alkoxy groups, IEICO exhibited a very narrow optical bandgap of 1.34 eV with a high-lying LUMO level of 3.95 eV. By adopting PBDTTT-E-T as the donor material, the IEICO-based fullerene-free PSC device showed a maximum PCE of 8.4% with a relatively high Voc of 0.82 V, and a high EQE of 66% was recorded at 810 nm. Furthermore, a PCE of 10.7% was recorded in a tandem PSC based on PBDTTT-ET:IEICO as a rear cell. As demonstrated in this work, the introduction of strong electron-donating groups (EDG) like alkoxyl chains provided great opportunities for molecular design of low bandgap non-fullerene acceptors without sacrificing Voc, as shown in Fig. 5.111 Following this work, they adopted the PC71BM in tandem OSC device with an active layer of PBDD4T-2F:PC71BM (front cell) & PBDTTT-ET:IEICO (rear cell), thus producing an outstanding PCE of 12.80%, with a Voc of 1.71 V, a Jsc of 11.51 mA cm2, and FF of 0.65, which was achieved under optimized conditions.112 The intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) can be further enhanced in a fluorinated acceptor IEICO-4F, and this new acceptor had an ultra-narrow bandgap, as low as 1.24 eV. Use of six different polymer donors in the OSC devices all yielded impressive Jsc values over 20 mA cm2 despite low energy losses of approximately 0.5 eV. The optimal ternary OSC device gave a high PCE of 10.9%, demonstrating the great potential of near-infrared (NIR) acceptors with such narrow bandgaps.113

Liu and Zhu et al. also focused on NIR acceptors; they developed new, deep-absorbing, small-bandgap electron-acceptor materials ATT-1 and ATT-2 and used them in semitransparent OSCs applications. It is known that the maximum photopic (vision in bright light) human eye sensitivity is 555 nm and the maximum scotopic (vision in dim light) sensitivity is 507 nm. Thus, the key to designing this series of materials is the introduction of a thieno[3,4-b]thiophene group, which was previously prevalent in constructing low bandgap polymers.4 Both materials when combined with PTB7-Th and the ATT-1/ATT-2 achieved peak PCEs of 10.07%/9.58% respectively.114,115 The idea of a conformational lock also worked for designing IDT-based acceptors. Xu and Bo et al. developed a material, IDT-BOC6, in which the bridge is a phenyl ring. The relative work was always a thiophene bridge because the phenyl group was considered as a twisted factor that would undermine the planarity. However, in this work, a supramolecular interaction can lock the flat conformation of an IDT-BOC6 acceptor, which can significantly extend the effective conjugation and broaden the absorption spectrum because of the existence of OS and OH interactions. These phenomena were also proved by comparison with the referenced molecule IDT-BC6 without alkoxy chains. As shown in Fig. 6, polymer solar cells (PSCs) based on IDT-BOC6 afforded a PCE of 9.6%. In contrast, PSCs based on similar acceptor IDT-BC6, which cannot form a flat conformation, only exhibited a low PCE of 2.3%.116

Using an IDT unit as a core and 5-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazol-4-ylmethylene)-3-ethyl-2-thioxothiazo-lidin-4-one (BR) units as end-capping EWGs, a new non fullerene acceptor, IDT-2BR, was designed and synthesized by Zhan et al. This acceptor was combined with the classic donor P3HT and 1-chloronaphthalene (CN) was adopted as an additive in the solution to adjust the morphology of a blended film. As shown in Fig. 7, addition of 15% (v/v) CN improved the Jsc from 6.38 to 7.538.91 mA cm2 with little change of Voc and FF. An FF of 68.1% and PCE of 5.12% were the highest values reported in 2015 for P3HT-based fullerene-free solar cells. An important discussion of this ladder-type system was that they believed the hexylphenyl groups on the IDT moiety exhibited a dihedral angle of ca. 115 to the backbone plane, which was beneficial for forming favorable interpenetrating networks without severe self-aggregation when mixing with P3HT. This becomes a critical design principle in the following acceptors' design.117

By changing the aryl side-chains to alkyl side-chains, Holliday and McCulloch et al. reported O-IDTBR and EH-IDTBR; the difference was that the former had straight n-octyl chains and the latter had branched 2-ethylhexyl chains. As shown in X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns for both materials, the increased planarity of the backbone promoted crystallization and the formation of pure acceptor domains. GIXRD was used to study their crystal packing with P3HT in films, respectively. It was found that the linear (O-IDTBR) chains could promote stronger intermolecular packing, which was particularly enhanced by thermal annealing, relative to branched (EH-IDTBR) chains. One effect of this was to further red-shift the absorption of O-IDTBR relative to the branched counterpart, which resulted in a broader EQE profile, higher Jsc, and an increase in PCE from 6.0% to 6.4% in P3HT-based OSCs.118 Zhan et al. also reported a similar work by using shorter hexyl side-chains; the inverted OSCs based on PTB7-Th:IDT-2BR1 yielded a high PCE of 8.7% which was higher than the previous IDT-2BR performance. The authors found that the IDT-2BR1 had a narrower bandgap, higher HOMO and LUMO, and exhibited a more self-organized film. This might be related to the nearly flat backbone and perpendicular hexyl chains of IDT-2BR1 while the IDT-2BR had increased steric hindrance and weaker molecular packing.119 Ying and Huang et al. modified the IDT-2BR by introducing a fluorine atom. They aimed to promote intermolecular interactions that can lead to appropriate phase separation that was beneficial for exciton dissociation and charge carrier transport, potentially leading to enhanced overall photovoltaic performances. The HOMO/LUMO energy levels were calculated as 5.31/2.89 eV and 5.69/3.64 eV for PBTA-BO, the donor material in this system and IFBR, respectively. Thus, there will be a desired cascade energy level alignment from the diagram of PBTA-BO, IFBR, and PC61BM, so a ternary device could be achieved. The binary devices exhibited only 5.7% (with IFBR) and 3.85% (with PC61BM) PCEs, and the subsequent ternary devices can easily exceed 8% PCE, indicating IFBR played a bridging role and provided an additional pathway for charge transfer in the bulk-heterojunction films.120 Zhou et al. end capped the IDT backbone with thiazolidine-2,4-dione, and the resulting BTA2 was matched with P3HT. The fullerene-free organic solar cells based on P3HT:BTA2 realized a doubled Voc of 1.22 V compared to that of the P3HT:PC61BM system. In addition, P3HT:BTA2 (0.6:1, w/w) blend film demonstrated high and balanced electron mobility (3.4 104 cm2 V1 s1) and hole mobility (4.9 104 cm2 V1 s1) by thermal annealing and solvent additive, which resulted in a relatively high PCE of 4.5%.121

Yip and Xu et al. reported a new acceptor, IDT-BT-R-CN, by tailor engineering in which the 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)-3-ethyl-rhodanine (R-CN) replaced the rhodanine end group from the IDT-BT-R acceptor. It was reported that one way to increase the Voc was to lower the energy loss which resulted in better device performance.122 Without the a double-bond between the central ladder-type backbone and the end-capped acceptor, it is still also possible to prepare non-fullerene acceptors. Zhan et al. made a try by using the IDT to append directly to the half-perylene diimide (PDI) blocks and, although the maximum PCE of C13 was just 2.36%, they still proved that the ladder-type backbone played a critical role in improving the performance of non-fullerene acceptors while other reference materials with a simple thiophene core presented PCE as low as 0.58%.123 Sun and Zhan collaborated together to refresh the record of these IDT-based materials by designing IC-C6IDT-IC in which the bridged -unit was removed. The as-cast PDBT-T1: ICC6IDT-IC blended film showed a very smooth surface, strong face-on ordering, appropriate phase separation scale, and relatively good charge transport. The as-cast PSC based on the PDBT-T1:IC-C6IDT-IC blend showed efficient exciton dissociation and negligible bimolecular recombination. The as-cast PSCs yield PCEs of up to 8.71%, which was the highest value for fullerene-free PSCs then.124 This acceptor can make great success in small molecule OSCs and over 9% PCEs were achieved by several groups.125,126 Afterwards, Zhan et al. systematically studied IDT oligomers. As the number of IDT units increase, the LUMO energy level stayed similar (around 3.8 eV), while the HOMO level increased from 5.91 to 5.29 eV, and the absorption spectrum red shifted. The IC-1IDT-IC with just one IDT unit exhibited the best D/A harmony and the best PCE of 7.39%.127 They also collectively studied the acceptor IDIC with five polymers showing distinct chemical structures to prove its superiority and versatility as a non-fullerene acceptor, and the PCE of PDBT-T1:IDIC and PTFBDT-BZS:IDIC devices reached 10.311% after using additive DIO to improve crystallinity and phase purity and optimize phase separation of blended films. Going through the acceptors having 56 fused cycles, we can discuss the most prevalent ladder-type system with seven fused units. The story should be started from ITIC, the star material first reported by Zhan et al. in 2015, which is shown in Scheme 4. This molecule had a bulky seven-ring fused core of indacenodithieno[3,2-b]thiophene (IDTT), end-capped with 2-(3-oxo-2,3-dihydroinden-1-ylidene)malononitrile (INCN). The pushpull structure in ITIC can induce intramolecular charge transfer and extend absorption. Furthermore, the four rigid 4-hexylphenyl substituents out of the IT main plane can restrict molecular planarity, aggregation, and large phase separation in BHJ blend films. The ITIC film exhibited electron mobility of up to 3.0 104 cm2 V1 s1, and an average value for five devices was 2.6 104 cm2 V1 s1. This electron mobility was close to those of fullerene derivatives and guaranteed effective charge carrier transport to the electrodes. ITIC in dichloromethane solution (106 M) gave strong absorption in the 500750 nm region with a maximum extinction coefficient of 1.3 105 M1 cm1 at 664 nm. Relative to its solution, absorption of an ITIC film showed a significant bathochromic-shift of 38 nm and a strong shoulder peak, suggesting that there was some molecular self-organization in the film. The ITIC had a bandgap of 1.59 eV and HOMO/LUMO of 5.48/3.83 eV. Using the structure ITO/PEDOT:PSS/PTB7-TH:ITIC/PDIN/Al, the blend at a D/A weight ratio of 1:1.3 gave the best performance: Voc of 0.81 V, Jsc of 14.21 mA cm2, FF of 0.591, and PCE of 6.80%.130 ITIC-Th with thienyl side-chains was also developed by Zhan et al., which was a representative work on side-chain engineering for this type of IDTT-based acceptors. They tried this ITIC-Th with two polymer donors, PTB7-Th and PDBT-T1; the former was a narrow bandgap donor and the latter was a wide bandgap donor. And in these two systems, the additive CN improved Jsc, FF and PCE. In the end, compared with the PTB7-Th:ITIC-Th system, PDBT-T1:ITIC-Th-based devices with 1% CN presented a better PCE of 9.6% and a certified PCE of 8.85%.131 Both ITIC and ITIC-Th developed in Zhan's group were further exploited by searching for various donors to match them; for example, Zhang and Li et al. designed and proposed a new series of wide bandgap polymers: J51, J61, and J71. To understand the morphology of the active layer, AFM was used to obtain surface images of active layers, which are smooth with a root-mean square roughness of 0.540.9 nm. Then, a GIWAXS measurement was used, indicating that the blended film of FTAZ and INIC3 exhibit highest crystallinity and maintain both FTAZ and INIC3 semi-crystalline packing at the same time, which is beneficial for film mobility. GISAXS measurements were also used to reveal that the suitable crystallinity of these four materials does no harm to film phase separation and exciton dissociation. These results provided a great potential of the new IBDT and fluorinated IC building blocks for constructing high-performance non-fullerene acceptors.76

So, the as-cast devices based on PTB7-Th:IUIC exhibited higher EQE, Jsc, and PCE than that of PTB7-Th:ITIC4.176

According to the aforementioned progress based on ladder-type acceptors, we now can summarize the modification techniques used in tuning structureproperty relationships. The first efficient ladder-type electron acceptor ITIC, it is a good example for structural analysis (but not limited in this structure), which is shown in Scheme 7.

We can carefully disassemble an ITIC molecule into the following units: (1) the conjugated fused backbone; (2) the end-cap EWG; (3) the sp3 hybridized side-chains at the cyclopentane part; (4) the substituents at the middle phenyl position of the backbone, where it should be noted that there is no report on this kind of modification for ITIC series; and (5) the -position of the end thiophene, but only one work has been reported so far.215 From these five viewpoints, we will continue to discuss the construction ways in current and next molecular designs.

4.1 Construction of ladder-type backbones

Multifused arenes play a key role in modulating properties and variations on the backbone, which is the most conspicuous change. Because a thiophene has less steric hindrance than benzene, it is indispensable in designing planar backbones for highly efficient acceptor molecules. To replace the thiophene with benzene at the backbone, it was found the resulting molecule has an increased bandgap; thus, we can conclude that the introduction of benzene is critical to tune energy levels. Benzene is also important to provide suitable fused positions with side-chain derivations. Normally, fused ring backbones are accomplished by a FriedelCrafts reaction under acidic conditions, and generally the remaining CH bonds on the benzene are far enough away from neighboring bonds to avoid any planar torsion. In Scheme 8, it is clear to see how the IDT and IDTT blocks form under similar conditions.216,217
Scheme 8 Synthetic routes of IDT and IDTT.

Like the syntheses of IDT and IDTT, other backbone units even without a benzene (for example, thieno[3,2-b]thiophene unit) also adopt a similar synthetic strategy. This kind of ring-closure step is effective but now is only limited to forming aryl substituted ladder-type backbones. This is because of the by-products from the elimination reaction if alkyl chains are used instead of aryl chains. To get the same backbone with alkyl chains, researchers have to use a time consuming route consisting of FriedelCrafts acylation, deoxygenation, and then nucleophilic substitution. So, there is much room for optimizing this synthetic route in the future.

Generally, the ring-closure step forms five-membered cycles, but this FriedelCrafts way is also useful for constructing six-membered cyclics. Very recently, Jen and Liu et al. reported a new ladder-type framework, in which the key step is ring-closing toward a two-dimensional perylene core in a six-membered closing manner. As a result, the multiple-fused materials bearing a highly planar backbone achieved an optoelectronic response over 1000 nm with the incorporated nitrogen atom.218

4.2 Modification of end electron-withdrawing groups

To date, modification of end-cap groups has proved to be very effective for tuning the performance of materials. The property of an EWG can directly affect the LUMO energy levels, which is critical for charge dissociation and Voc. The IC is the most widely used EWG, with two cyano groups to strengthen the electron-poor feature of 1H-indene-1,3(2H)-dione. The benzene ring offers four positions for modification; herein, appending a weak EDG-like methyl can increase the Voc and EWG-like fluorine can achieve a narrower bandgap for light harvesting. A systematic study on position difference effects was also reported,76,77 and replacing the benzene with thiophene isomers also works in constructing effective acceptors.83

Other end groups were also reported using a similar construction strategy and these are included along with the IC in Scheme 9. It is interesting to point out that a double bond is generally used to connect the backbone and the EWG, and this position can also be adjusted. Recently, Zhang and Li et al. adopted a tandem double bonds system to extend the conjugation length which is shown in Fig. 16. In previous reports, two molecules based on IDT and IDTT cores with double bond -bridges were designed and synthesized. By introducing double bond -bridges, the film of these new molecules showed significantly red-shifted absorption, tuned crystallinity, and improved electron mobility, which made their blended film with a polymer donor easier to form appropriate phase separation.219,220

4.3 Side-chain engineering

Existence of a cyclopentane moiety in the backbones in most reports is quite important for appending suitable side chains to solubilize the resulting acceptor. An alkyl chain is the first choice in many examples of conjugated materials as shown in Scheme 10. However, aryl chains instead of alkyl chains are more popular in building such ladder-type acceptors. We believe this is because the synthetic routes, with ester groups existing in the precursors, can simplify the backbone forming process. That is, the aryl lithium reagent attacks the ester to form a tertiary alcohol, followed by intramolecular cyclization. If an alkyl lithium or alkyl Grignard reagent is used in this step, then the next elimination process competes with the cyclization, which leads to difficult work-up and low yield. Thus, the most feasible way for appending alkyl chains to a multi-cyclopentane system is by using a nucleophilic substitution reaction, and this requires a ketone as the precursor for de-oxygenation. And, the ketone precursor can be also transferred from an ester group by FriedelCrafts acylation. Obviously, this synthetic route is more complicated. In addition, another problem for this route is low solubility of the rigid ketone. An alternative way to reduce the synthetic difficulty of fully alkyl chains is using mixed side chains, called asymmetrical sides. Xu and Bo et al. firstly developed this method by using dialdehyde compounds instead of general diester precursors to simplify the synthesis.221
Scheme 10 Common used side chains.

As for the aryl side chain, a current study was done on the meta and para positions for the phenyl group or substantially replacing the phenyl group by thiophene.155,156,160 But in theory, the short alkyl chains involved in removing a conventional benzene group should produce better intermolecular packing, which has been partly proven in an IDT system. Until very recently, the popular IDTT system had adopted the same modification, and a new acceptor, C8-ITIC, which has linear alkyl side chains on an IDTT core, was synthesized through a heterocycle extension strategy. The advantage of this synthetic route is that all intermediates are soluble and readily purified and have general applications in chemical synthesis. Compared to reported ITIC with phenylalkyl side chains, C8-ITIC exhibits a reduction in the optical band gap, higher absorptivity, and an increased propensity to crystallize, which shows the important role of side chains on the properties of acceptor molecules.45

4.4 Short-axis substitution

Many researchers may notice that there are possible substituted positions on the central benzene of IDT or IDTT cores; however, no related reports are available probably due to synthesis difficulty. Another kind of BDCPDT-core based material can easily make this modification by using different side-chains in the precursors.78,169172 Unlike the aforementioned side-chains on the sp3 hybridized carbon that make them point out of the backbone planes, these kinds of side-chains are appended to the conjugated backbone; thus, they can directly affect energy levels especially the HOMO. This impact is quite similar to that of end-cap groups which are placed along the molecule's long-axis, so it could be described as a short-axis substitution.

Recently, Jiang, Zhang and Li et al. found this short cut. It is predicable that the other relative system bearing central unsubstituted hydrogen can adopt this modification to further improve the materials' performance, as shown in Fig. 17.222 In this work, two new fused-ring electron acceptors (CBT-IC and SBT-IC) were designed and developed by utilizing n-hexyl and n-hexylthio as short-axis substitutions based on the BDCPDT system. The short-axises could readily modify absorption spectra and energy levels of CBT-IC and SBT-IC due to different electron-donating/-withdrawing natures of n-hexyl and n-hexylthio substitutions. Furthermore, compared with BDT-IC without any short-axis substituents, both CBT-IC and SBT-IC exhibited higher maximum absorption coefficients. In the film, however, crystallinity and charge mobility of acceptors were decreased since the short-axises were introduced. Combined with a medium bandgap polymer donor, J71, both of CBT-IC and SBT-IC based OPV devices exhibited a higher PCE over 11%, and higher maximum EQE over 75% than a BDT-IC based device. This work demonstrated that a short-axis could be a very useful approach to optimize materials which may give a way to further designing better non-fullerene acceptors.

4.5 -Substitution on the end-thiophene

The last rings at each side of a backbone are always thiophene, and EWGs are appended from the -positions. However, the -position of an end-thiophene is rarely developed when designing new acceptors. Attention should be paid that substitution on the -position is unfavorable for polymer donors because that substitution could make a twisted conformation to the neighboring rings. However, this is no longer a problem for an acceptor because this is the end group in the ladder-type system.

Very recently, Tang et al. reported a new non-fullerene acceptor, ITC6-IC (in Fig. 18), with long alkyl chains in the terminal electron-donating building blocks to enhance steric hindrance, which made the molecule have a planar structure with a high degree of unified configuration and better solubility in chemical solvents. What's more, the alkyl chains could reduce the structural order of ITC6-IC film, thus enhancing the compatibility of ITC6-IC with polymer donors and facilitating the tunability of blended films. These new results showed that the regiochemistry in OSCs is an area worth studying, and it may provide a new strategy to design high performance materials.215

5. Ongoing opportunity and challenges for devices

While this review has shown considerable progress with OSCs based on ladder-type acceptors in recent years, and also discussed current design principles and possible modifications with this kind of acceptor, further insightful considerations on physical properties and devices are also needed for next improvements of OSCs. The acceptor is not a unitary material in the active layer in a device, so it is of equal importance to develop highly efficient donors to keep up the pace of emerging effective acceptors.

A number of reasons could explain why this kind of ladder-type molecule is an effective OSC material: its planar backbone has a strong -orbital overlap that can enlarge the absorption range, it has a relatively low energetic disorder, it may form pure domains but still maintains efficient charge generation, and the out-of-plane side-chains on the fused core can be beneficial for the formation of a favorable interpenetrating donor/acceptor network with a suitable degree of phase separation in the active layers. Thus, these new materials bring many opportunities and challenges in this active research area.

First, lots of researches relating to OSCs have been focused on polymeric donor materials, as they usually have better film morphology and film-forming properties than their monomers. As for the morphology of an active layer, AFM, TEM, R-SoXS, and GIWAXS were used to obtain insights into the behaviors of aggregation and crystallization of donor and acceptor materials. Improved molecular packing and enhanced crystallization of materials can contribute to improvements of Jsc and FF, as improved molecular packing is conducive to an increase in charge-transport characteristics and a reduced recombination. Although semiconducting polymer materials have these morphological advantages, they suffer from difficult purification procedures and synthetic reproducibility, which restricts their commercial development. Therefore, it's a good research direction to develop some small molecular donor materials which are easily synthesized with low cost to build all small molecule blends for OSCs with vast potentials for future development. Some groups126,227231 have taken a lead in this area but now the most efficient OSCs based on all small molecules still lag behind the widely-studied polymer: ladder-type acceptor OSCs.

On the contrary to the all small molecule OSCs, some researchers realize these ladder-type blocks are also useful for building polymer acceptors, which have been dominated by imide-based acceptors for a long time. For example, the most popular acceptor, N2200,223 is constructed by these and has the best efficiency. But as Zhang et al. reported, a new high efficiency polymer acceptor with a PCE over 9% was designed and synthesized from being inspired by IDIC which has a planar structure, high electron affinity, as well as high absorbance.224 This demonstrates the great potential of a polymer acceptor in coupling with polymer acceptors for high performance all polymer PSCs.

A rigid conjugated backbone endows the ladder-type acceptor with the possibility to extend their absorption in the NIR region. It is well known that about 47% of sunlight is in the region of visible light while 53% of it is in this region. It would be very helpful to improve the efficiency of OSCs if near-infrared light could be effectively utilized. If this acceptor is combined with another narrow bandgap donor, then a semitransparent device could be achievable as shown in Fig. 19. What's more, they could be widely used in tandem OSCs, bioimaging, senor, military equipment and so on.

Vacuum sublimation as a viable option should not be ignored. As we know, reproducibility and large-area device's implementation for evaporation techniques are quite superior to the spin-coating technique. Additionally, the organic light emitting diodes (OLED) industry has built up a stream line for large area panels and has great likelihood for success if the OPV materials can be vaporized with high efficiency. Now, the alternatives to fullerenes for vacuum-deposited small-molecule organic solar cells still mainly are comprised of perylene derivatives or halogenated (sub-) phthalocyanines, with the highest PCE of 8.4%.225 How to design ladder-type non-fullerene acceptor materials suitable for vacuum-deposited small-molecule organic solar cells thus is of interest for industrial researchers.

Moreover, it is a big challenge for NFA-based OSCs to maintain their stability for a long time. Although organic materials show good thermal stability and photo-stability, their morphological stability still needs to be improved. Most ladder-type based solution-processed solar cells with high performance require additional complicated post-cast treatments or additives to optimize their morphology, which can lead to irreproducibility and increased cost.75,124 These intricate treatments are unfit for large area production and further development. It's of great significance to develop high performance materials without additional treatments in a device, which can lower the cost and increase the stability.

And lastly, there are still some mechanism issues needing deep study and investigation. It has been reported that some systems8794 can achieve a high Jsc, fill factor, and PCE with low Eloss. But why is it that this only works in certain systems and how does the reduced non-radiative recombination rate contribute to low Eloss? And how can Eloss be further decreased in a more effective way which can be used in most NFA systems? Recently, Hou et al. tentatively proposed that the molecular electrostatic potential maybe is the reason why charge generation can be effective with a small driving force between donor and acceptor.232 It's essential to further set up appropriate theoretical models of the charge transfer phenomenon to have a deep understanding of Eloss and explore efficient ways to decrease it.

6. Conclusions and outlook

Since 2015, rapid advances have been achieved in ladder-type non-fullerene acceptors; thus in this review, we summarized recently reported non-fullerene acceptors in this system according to different backbones and highlighted some important studies. As a very promising subbranch of non-fullerene acceptors, ladder-type materials have several advantages: (i) various tuning modes in molecular design; (ii) a broad absorption region to harvest solar energy; and (iii) good morphology control. Based on these intrinsic features and the great efforts from many groups, the PCE of NF-OSCs increased from below 6% to over 13% and we were able to outline this rapid-growing research area in this review.

Meanwhile, design guidelines for chemical construction of ladder-type acceptors were also discussed. We noted that modifications primarily focus on backbone variation, side-chain engineering, and end-cap group substitution. Based on these reports, several backbones, such as IDT, IDTT, BDCPDT, PT, etc. have successfully exceeded 10% of PCE, indicating great potential for future optimization. As a comparison, backbones shorter than five-fused rings or longer than nine-fused rings are now inferior to the above-mentioned blocks. A short backbone, in many studies, was proved to possess relative low electron mobility which may inhibit performance in devices.96101 A long backbone, however, shows at least comparable or enhanced electron-mobility.216218 But, we should point out that acceptors with short backbones have a great potential for building wide bandgap acceptors and have achieved some good results. Side-chains also play an important role in tuning the properties of acceptors.45,160,221 Unlike a mono-substituted side chain on the nitrogen atom of traditional imide-based acceptors, the side-chains formed a 3-D conformation along with the rigid backbones and affected intermolecular packing. The end-cap group can directly tailor the electronic properties of acceptors so now it perhaps is the most efficient tool for improving a material's performance.76,77,83 The modes we discussed above are normally based on a symmetrical conformation; actually, an asymmetrical conformation should also be useful in constructing ladder-type acceptors by introducing different side-chains, end-groups, or substantially using an asymmetrical backbone and this definitely needs further investigation in the future.

To construct an ideal NFA-based OSC system, selection of a concerted donor and the NFA is of vital importance. On one hand, the energy level of donors and acceptors should be matched to insure they have complementary absorption in the light spectrum as well as an appropriate driving force for excitons separation. On the other hand, intrinsic morphologic miscibility between donors and acceptors is supposed to be acceptable to make sure the morphology of the active layer is smooth and homogeneous. As for donor materials, they should not only have good stability in organic solvents, but also have a tendency towards aggregation and self-assembly when forming a film in a device fabrication process. Yang et al. proposed that developing NFA materials with strong absorption in the NIR region and blue region is the key for further development of NFA solar cells, especially for tandem cells.43 Accordingly, donor materials with medium and wide band gaps deserve further future attention.

Ladder-type acceptor research has been extensively explored in just a few years, which soon became a very hot topic for fundamental studies. Although there has been an accumulation of information on the design principles and mechanistic understanding of these materials, there will be plenty of room for next opportunities and challenges. Many possibilities still remain to be explored and hopefully we have listed part of them by analyzing the publications of many excellent papers in various journals. We enthusiastically look forward to the new advancements in this exciting area of research.

In the end, we wish that this review may provide a clear panorama of these novel functional materials for OPV academia and attract more chemists and physicists to move them towards massive industrial applications.
