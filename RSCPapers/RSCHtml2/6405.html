Abstract

Metalâ€“organic framework derived nanostructures have unique properties, such as large specific surface area, exclusive porous networks, numerous active sites, and exceptional electrochemical properties, when compared to traditional nanostructures. 1. Introduction

The progress of sustainable and eco-friendly energy storage systems has drawn imperative research interest in the scientific community to overcome the energy demands all over the world. Among the numerous electrical energy storage devices, supercapacitors (SCs) have been considered to be the most promising because of their fast chargeâ€“discharge characteristics, high-power density merits, excellent intrinsic safety, and ultra-long cycle life.1 Therefore, SCs have been intensively used in modern electronics such as high-power electric vehicles, military devices, portable electronics, and memory devices.2,3 However, SCs have only been used as conventional battery backups due to their poor energy density when compared to batteries, which is a major drawback for energy storage device applications.4 This problem may hinder their commercial utilization possibilities.5â€“7 Commonly, the enhancement of energy density is ascribed to the design and fabrication of highly active electrode materials, which play an imperative role in determining the energy density of SCs and facilitate the operating potential window and cycling stability.8,9 In recent years, solid-state SCs have emerged as a new type of energy storage device and warrant substantial consideration.10,11 Specifically, solid-state SCs have numerous advantages, such as excellent stability, flexibility, and environmental friendliness, when compared to conventional SCs. The exceptional advantages of solid-state SCs can expand their energy storage applications into commercial sectors.12 Based on their energy storage properties, electrode materials have been classified into those with capacitive-type electroactive materials (i.e., activated carbon, carbon nanotubes, graphene, etc.), and pseudocapacitive-type electroactive materials (MnO2, V2O5, Co3O4, CuO, NiO, etc.).13 The optimum electrode materials should have unique properties, such as hierarchical architectures, enlarged surface area, exclusive porous networks, strong interaction with the current collector, excellent electrochemical properties, simple fabrication methods, and low costs.14 In order to address the energy density problem, fabrication of asymmetric SCs (ASCs) is an innovative approach that hybridizes a capacitive type material with pseudocapacitive type materials. ASCs significantly enhance the energy storage properties, which include the operating potential window, and energy and power densities.15â€“17

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted much attention for their potential applications in water splitting, fuel cells, solar cells, Li-ion batteries, SCs, sensors, and biosensors, owing to their unique properties of a highly crystalline structure and chemical diversity.18,19 Recently, numerous research studies have focused on the design and fabrication of MOFs with a tunable morphology. Nevertheless, the design and fabrication of MOFs is not a realistically facile, scalable, or cost-effective methodology, which hinders their practical applications in commercial sectors. The structure, morphology, mechanism, and fabrication approaches have been explored to attain greater cost effectiveness, simplify designs and fabricate MOFs with high electrochemical performances. MOFs have been used as templates to prepare porous carbon/metal oxides and the as-obtained electroactive materials have been applied to energy storage devices.20,21 The resultant materials were shown to possess a high specific surface area and unique porous nature, and exhibit high SC performance. However, the original structure of MOFs can be destroyed during the calcination process, which leads to a poor specific surface area. In order to overcome this problem, it is important to introduce guest molecules to improve structural stability.22,23 On the other hand, MOFs have been directly employed as electroactive materials for energy storage applications to achieve high electrochemical performance, because of their high surface area and unique porous nature. There have been a few reports of pristine MOFs used as electrode materials for SCs, but their specific capacity and rate performance were relatively low. In this context, enhancement of the electrochemical properties of pristine MOFs utilizing various metal ions and ligands is one of the key challenges for the scientific community.24,25 For example, Jiao et al. reported mixed-metallic MOF-based electrode materials for hybrid SCs, resulting in high specific capacity with superior rate performances.26 Jayakumar et al. reported generation of graphene supported hollow cage-like NixCo3âˆ’xO4 from MOFs and the resultant materials showed high super-capacitive performances.27 Recently, Zhang et al. reported the fabrication of double-shelled Znâ€“Coâ€“S nanostructures by chemical etching methods, followed by an effective sulfurization process. The resultant Znâ€“Coâ€“S nanostructures were employed as an electrode for hybrid SCs to achieve high specific capacitance, excellent rate capability, and a long cycle life.28 The fabrication protocols are suitable for synthesis of MOF-based nanostructures in a powder form, which need polymer binders and conductive additives for SC applications. These binders and additives can be a real barrier for their potential use in flexible electronics because they reduce the active sites and result in inflexibility of the electrodes.29â€“31 To avoid this problem, design and fabrication of MOF-based nanostructures on highly porous 3D substrates has numerous advantages, such as enlarged electroactive sites, shortened ion/electron transport pathways and enhanced synergistic interaction between electroactive materials and the current collector.32 However, there have been few reports of the application of MOF-derived mixed metal sulfides over 3D Ni foam as free-standing and binder-free electrodes for solid-state SCs to date.

Herein, for the first time, we report a novel approach for the design and fabrication of MOF derived ultra-thin CuCo2S4 NS arrays with hierarchical nanostructures for solid-state SCs. The sheet-like CuCo2S4 electrode displayed a high specific capacity of âˆ¼409.2 mA h gâˆ’1 at a current density of 3 mA cmâˆ’2 and excellent rate capability (âˆ¼77.9% capacity retention at a higher current density of 50 mA cmâˆ’2) in aqueous KOH electrolyte. The cycling performance test revealed that the sheet-like CuCo2S4 electrode showed an exceptional cycle life, with nearly âˆ¼94.2% capacity retention after 10000 cycles at a current density of 30 mA cmâˆ’2. Most importantly, the sheet-like CuCo2S4 and iron oxide anchored nitrogen-doped graphene (Fe2O3/NG) aerogel are employed as positive and negative electrodes, respectively. The assembled solid-state ASC exhibited a maximum operating potential window of 1.6 V, high volumetric capacity, exceptional cycling stability, and ultra-high energy and power densities. Thus, we believe that our present synthesis methodology can be easily transformed into a general and cost-effective strategy for the design and fabrication of MOF derived ternary metal sulfides over a porous 3D substrate for practical energy storage and conversion applications. A red-light emitting diode (LED) powered using two CuCo2S4 NS//Fe2O3/NG ASC devices connected in series demonstrated the practical application of all solid-state SCs.

Fabrication and structural analysis of the CuCo2S4 NS arrays

The design and fabrication strategy for the CuCo2S4 NS and Fe2O3/NG aerogel is schematically presented in Fig. 1. First, CuCo2 LDH was derived from an MOF by a simple and scalable hydrothermal method. Then, CuCo2 LDH was transformed into its corresponding CuCo2S4 NS arrays through an ion-exchange process. In detail, an anion exchange reaction occurred between S2âˆ’ (from Na2S) and O2âˆ’ (from CuCo2 LDH) to attain a hierarchical CuCo2S4 NS. It is well known that the anion-exchange technique is one of the best techniques to retain the original structure and enhance the electrical conductivity and the catalytic activity of the NS architectures. After the ion exchange process, the CuCo2 LDH was ultra-thin and became highly porous, which may provide a wide space to reduce the ion/electron transport pathway and avoid the dead volume of the electroactive materials, resulting in high rate performance and an ultra-long cycle life.

4. Conclusion

In summary, for the first time, we have successfully developed a new strategy for the fabrication of a sheet-like CuCo2S4 NS from a MOF and used it as an advanced free-standing and binder-free electrode for solid-state ASCs. The design and fabrication of hierarchical CuCo2S4 NS arrays used a two-step synthesis protocol, whereby the CuCo2 LDH precursor was formed, followed by an effective sulfurization process. The sheet-like CuCo2S4 NS electrode exhibited an ultra-high specific capacity of 409.2 mA h gâˆ’1 and areal capacity of âˆ¼0.96 mA h cmâˆ’2 at a current density of 3 mA cmâˆ’2 with exceptional rate capability and an ultra-long cycling stability (âˆ¼94.2% capacity retention after 10000 cycles). Note that the Fe2O3/NG aerogel exhibited a high specific surface area of âˆ¼521.2 m2 gâˆ’1 and can be used as a negative electrode alternative to commercially available activated carbon. Most importantly, the assembled CuCo2S4 NS//Fe2O3/NG solid-state ASC delivered an ultra-high volumetric capacity of âˆ¼2.1 mA h cmâˆ’3 at a current density of 3 mA cmâˆ’2, an excellent energy density of âˆ¼89.6 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of âˆ¼663 W kgâˆ’1, and an ultra-long cycle life. The assembled CuCo2S4 NS//Fe2O3/NG ASC device revealed superior electrochemical performance when compared to recently reported MOF-derived metal nanostructure-based ASCs. The current impressive results open a new pathway to design and fabricate sheet-like ternary metal sulfides from a MOF and construct energy storage and conversion devices for use in industrial sectors. Furthermore, two series of devices were connected to light a red LED, demonstrating outstanding practical applicability for modern portable devices. These results reveal a promising design for all energy storage devices using a simple, scalable, cost-effective and environmentally friendly process to synthesize materials that may open up the prospect to fabricate and design nanostructured energy storage and conversion devices for modern electronics.
