Abstract

We proposed based on first principles density functional theory calculations that a nano-scale thin film based on a polarâ€“nonpolar transition-metal oxide heterostructure can be used as a highly-efficient photocatalyst. This is demonstrated using a SrTiO3/LaAlO3/SrTiO3 sandwich-like heterostructure with photocatalytic activity in the near-infrared region. The effect of the polar nature of LaAlO3 is two-fold. First, the induced electrostatic field accelerates the photo-generated electrons and holes into opposite directions and minimizes their recombination rates. Hence, the reduction and oxidation reactions can be instigated at the SrTiO3 surfaces located on the opposite sides of the heterostructure. Second, the electric field reduces the band gap of the system making it photoactive in the infrared region. We also show that charge separation can be enhanced by using compressive strain engineering that creates ferroelectric instability in STO. The proposed setup is ideal for tandem oxide photocatalysts especially when combined with photoactive polar materials.

Introduction

By directly converting light to chemical fuels, photoelectrochemical cells (PECs) hold great promise for making solar power meet our future energy needs. The catalytic reactions in PECs are instigated by photogenerated holes and electrons after light illumination on a photosensitive material. The generated holes are responsible for initiating the oxidation pathway while the electrons are responsible for initiating the reduction reactions.

The main issues in conventional PECs are the low efficiency of solar energy conversion in addition to the high cost and device longevity. The light conversion efficiency is mainly determined by three factors. The first factor is related to the light absorption efficiency to generate electronâ€“hole pairs, which could be increased using semiconductors with a band gap <2 eV. This is because the energy of the solar spectrum is roughly 44% visible light (1.3â€“2.1 eV), 48% infrared, and 8% ultraviolet. Standard photocatalytic materials such as TiO2 have a large band gap (>3 eV).1â€“3 To reduce the TiO2 band gap and improve visible light absorption, several schemes are investigated for band engineering such as using cation or anion (co)doping, and synthesizing nano-crystals but with only limited success.4â€“10 The second factor that limits the PEC efficiency is the recombination rates of the generated charge carriers, which can be significantly alleviated by breaking the translational symmetry between the generated holes and electrons. This can be achieved, e.g., by using an external field that couples to the charge of the photo-induced carriers as in an nâ€“p junction that is typically used in conventional semiconductor PECs. Other novel approaches to minimize recombination rates such as using a ferroelectric material or advanced heterostructures are also currently being investigated.11,12 Finally, the last factor that determines the PEC performance is associated with the efficiency of chemical reactions that are instigated by the photo-induced charge carriers. To date, successful materials that are particularly employed in conventional photocatalysts include metal oxides such as TiO2 and SrTiO3 (STO).13â€“20 The major challenge in PEC optimization is in designing photochemical cells that can successfully circumvent all of these limitations.

Here, we study the photocatalytic reactivity of nanocapacitor-like heterostructures by inspecting their electronic structure and optical properties using first principles calculations. Our results imply that this material can be a candidate for a new photocatalyst. Namely, the proposed nano structure combines two ultrathin oxide films that act as photo-cathode and -anode materials, and a polar material with an intrinsic electric field that breaks translational symmetry and inhibits carrier recombination rates. The decrease in the band gap of the system, mainly in the interface region between the polar and non-polar materials, improves substantially the absorption in the near-infrared to visible light regions. We demonstrate the properties of this heterostructure based on STO and LaAlO3 (LAO), as shown in the schematic of Fig. 1. Additionally, we show that compressive strain can polarize the STO layers inducing an electric field in the whole heterostructure, which enhances charge separation even with thicker STO slabs.

Heterostructures composed of perovskite oxides such as STO and LAO, epitaxially stacked along the (001) direction have generated a lot of interest in recent years.21â€“25 The exciting properties stem from the polarâ€“non-polar nature of STO/LAO as can be seen by examining the nominal charges of the layers, which are +1 (LaO) and âˆ’1 (AlO2) in LAO (001) layers, and neutral for the TiO2 and SrO layers of STO (001). Hence in LAO(001)/STO(001) heterostructures there are two possible interfaces, namely n-type (LaO)+/(TiO2)0 and p-type (AlO2)âˆ’/(SrO)0. Further, the charge modulation in LAO results in the buildup of a voltage that is nearly proportional to the number of LAO layers and thus diverges linearly with the LAO thickness. This leads to a â€œpolar catastropheâ€ that can be avoided by transfer of half an electron (hole) per 2D unitcell across the n-type (p-type) interface.26

The nanocapacitor-like STO/LAO/STO heterostructure has an n-type interface on one side and a p-type interface on the other, as shown in Fig. 1. This ensures the existence of an unidirectional electric field in the polar LAO region. The macroscopic electric field in LAO increases the local energy as shown in Fig. 1 such as the energy gap of the entire system is determined by the difference between the CBM of STO on the left and VBM of STO on the right. As the LAO thickness increases, the voltage across LAO increases as well leading to the decrease of the band gap of the system and inducing an insulator-to-metal transition at a critical thickness of LAO. Here the buildup of the electric field in the polar LAO material has two main effects on the properties of the heterostructure especially when applied as a PEC. First, the electric field decreases the band gap of the STO/LAO/STO heterostructure, and we show that this enhances the absorption efficiency in the infrared. Second, the intrinsic electric field in LAO breaks the translational symmetry and inhibits the recombination rates. Further, by using ultrathin STO films, photogenerated charges can instigate surface reactions at the bottom and top STO surfaces.

Results and discussion

We first determine the band gap of the STO/LAO/STO heterostructure. Here, the interface region between STO and LAO is nâ€“p with (LaO)+/(TiO2)0 on the right side of the junction and (AlO2)âˆ’/(SrO)0 on the left side, as shown in Fig. 1. Because of two possible terminations SrO and TiO2 of the STO (001) surface, there are four possible heterostructures depending on the STO surface layer, namely SrOâ€“SrO, SrOâ€“TiO2, TiO2â€“SrO, and TiO2â€“TiO2. We use 1â€“2 unitcells (ucs) of STO overlayers respectively, as shown in Fig. 2(a). Because the thickness of LAO is an important factor that controls the electronic band structure of the interface,27â€“29 we have additionally investigated the heterostructures for different LAO thicknesses. Fig. 2(b and c) show that the band gap decreases as the LAO thickness increases, which is similar to the case of STO/LAO.30 Also, in agreement with STO/LAO,30 we find that STO/LAO/STO has an indirect band gap (see the ESIâ€ ), which closes after a critical thickness with a concurrent charge transfer from the VB to CB. As seen in Fig. 2, the critical thickness is the smallest for the TiO2â€“TiO2 system (3uc) and the largest for the SrOâ€“SrO system (6uc). From the schematic map in Fig. 1, the band gap of nâ€“p type STO/LAO/STO is determined by the band alignment of the two STO layers and can be estimated as Egap = Egap_STO âˆ’ Î”VLAO, where Egap_STO is the band gap of STO and Î”VLAO is the electrostatic potential induced by polarized LAO. Î”VLAO depends on the LAO thickness. One can see that before the charge transfer from the VB to CB, Egap decreases almost linearly with the LAO thickness, which is consistent with previous first-principles calculations.30 After the closing of the bandgap, the charge transfer from the VB to CB balances the increasing electrostatic potential. As shown in Fig. 2(a), the VB and CB, which are the hole and electron acceptor bands, are mainly localized on the (001) and (00) surfaces respectively because of the built-in electrostatic field. The VB is mainly due to the oxygen p-band of oxygen while the CB is due to the Ti d-bands, which contributes to the 2D electron gas at the interface, as reported previously30,31

One key factor for photocatalytic water splitting is the electron transfer between the photocatalyst and the water redox species. Thermodynamically, this transfer takes place with a proper band alignment at which the conduction band minimum (CBM) of the p-type photocathode is more negative in potential than the water reduction potential VH+/H2, and the valence band maximum (VBM) of the n-type photocathode is more positive in potential than the water oxidation potential VO2/H2O, which can be obtained from the half reactions 2H+ + 2eâˆ’ â†” H2 (VH+/H2) and O2 + 4H+ + 4eâˆ’ â†” 2H2O (VO2/H2O).32 In principle, the H2O redox potentials can be calculated by the molecular dynamics simulation or the mean-field approximation considering the interaction between the liquidâ€“solid interface.33â€“35 However, this is beyond the scope of this work and we use instead the experimental values.32 Similar band alignment analysis was applied before for semiconductors as well as for the newly developed two-dimensional nano materials.36,37 In our study, we use the HSE functional to get a more accurate band alignment to the vacuum level in the system, at least compared to local and semi-local functionals (see Fig. S1 in the ESIâ€ ). The results are summarized in Fig. 3 where VO2/H2O and VH+/H2 are shifted by the vacuum potential (Evac) and the Fermi energy is set to zero. One can see that Evac of the opposite sides are determined by the terminations and aligned by the electric field in LAO.

The oxidation (reduction) power of the photocatalyst is defined as the energy difference between VO2/H2O(VH+/H2) and the VBM (CBM) level of the material. From Fig. 3, we see that SrOâ€“SrO and SrOâ€“TiO2 heterostructures can be only used for H2O reduction. This is because VH+/H2 is located below CBM that thermodynamically favors charge transfer, while VO2/H2O is above VBM and thus charge transfer is inhibited. On the other hand, TiO2â€“TiO2 and TiO2â€“SrO heterostructures can be used for both H2O reduction and oxidation as the water redox potentials fall in the bandgap of the system. As seen from the figure, the oxidation and reduction powers for these heterostructures are respectively 0.8 and 0.6 eV for TiO2â€“TiO2 termination, and 0.6 and 2.6 eV for the TiO2â€“SrO termination. The nearly balanced oxidation/reduction powers for the TiO2â€“TiO2 heterostructure is expected to minimize the difference between the reduction and oxidation reactions, and thus can potentially improve the photocatalytic efficiency. The large reduction potential for TiO2â€“SrO termination is also of interest as the main purpose of the photocatalytic reaction is to obtain hydrogen gas. The energy alignment with respect to H2O redox potentials are only shown for the 3uc LAO heterostructure, nevertheless the band alignments are very close to other structures with different thicknesses, and hence our conclusions are independent of the LAO thickness (see the ESI, Fig. S5â€ ). In addition, the band alignment of TiO2â€“TiO2 heterostructures is very similar to the STO slab with TiO2 termination (Fig. S1â€ ). These results show that the band alignment mainly depends on the termination, and is unlikely to be affected by the electric field in LAO. Therefore although here we only calculate the band alignment in a vacuum, we expected that the nâ€“p type STO/LAO/STO works similar to STO for H2O oxidation and reduction in a real photocatalytic system.37

The requirement for ultrathin STO layers can be alleviated using strain engineering. It has been previously shown that compressive strain can induce a ferroelectric instability in STO.39 Thus, by tuning the in-plane lattice constant, it is expected that the heterostructures can be designed to have a macroscopic electric field in the LAO and STO layers that drives the photo-generated charge carriers in opposite directions. We demonstrated this for the nâ€“p STO/LAO/STO heterostructure using a 3% compressive strain with an in-plane lattice constant of 3.78 Ã…. Fig. 5 shows the resulting projected layer DOS obtained using HSE06 as described before. The energy shift of the VB in the STO and LAO layers indicates that the heterostructure supports as a whole a polarization field, and not only in the LAO region as in the unstrained case shown in Fig. 3. We showed that with the strained case, the heterostructure can also have a large absorption coefficient Î±(E) in the infrared region (see Fig. S5â€ ), and thus the system remains photocatalytically active as in the unstrained case. In addition to the effect of the electric field on charge separation, the polarization of the system is expected to improve on the band alignment with the water redox potentials as was demonstrated on TiO2 supported on a ferroelectric STO system.40

By studying the nâ€“p-type STO/LAO/STO heterostructure as an example, we demonstrate a new perspective of oxide heterostructure applications in photocatalysis as a nanosize PEC. The crucial factor here is the polar nature of LAO, which increases the light absorption in the infrared region and effectively separates the generated charge carriers. There have been active debates about the existence of the electric field in LAO.25,41â€“63 The internal electrostatic field in LAO could be affected by oxygen defects and interface inter-mixing in the oxides.41,47,56,59,62 However, there have been several direct and indirect evidence supporting the existence of a built-in electric field,42,44,51,52,54,57,60,61 For example, experimental and computational studies showed that the internal electric field in LAO/STO/Au can be as large as 80 mV Ã…âˆ’1.51 (See the ESIâ€  for more details.) Additionally, we showed that by using a compressive strain, STO can also be polar, and thus charge separation in STO/LAO/STO will be enhanced even with thick STO layers. In this report, we show the internal electric field that can enhance the charge separation, the photo-generated electron/hole dynamics to show the process of charge carrier separation and being trapped by the adsorbed molecules is an interesting topic for future investigation, but beyond the scope of the current work.

It is known that oxygen evolution reaction is one of the most important factors for H2O splitting. A recent study of H2O oxidation on TiO2 supported on a ferroelectric STO demonstrated that the electric field in strained STO also helps to reduce the thermodynamic barriers and improve the reaction efficiency.40 We expect that the electric field in LAO in the nâ€“p-type STO/LAO/STO nanostructure has similar effects and we plan to investigate it in our future work.

Recently, Li et al. proposed a metal-free photocatalyst based on a BN bilayer decorated with F and H atoms on two sides of the BN layer, which is also found to be active for near-infrared light.64 This has some similarities with our proposed model except that in our case STO is used as a support for the photocatalyst, which is expected to be advantageous because the exposed STO is a well-known photocatalyst. Additionally, the oxides are amenable to a diverse array of techniques for enhancing their catalytic activity such as by depositing noble metal cluster and cation (co)doping.65 Finally, the STO/LAO heterostructure with an atomic sharp surface and interface has been synthesized using pulsed laser deposition (PLD) and molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) techniques. Using such techniques, the thickness of LAO can be controlled precisely.30

Another advantage of oxide heterostructures is that we can flexibly combine different polar non-polar oxides. For example, it is possible to replace STO by TiO2. As reported by Kennedy et al.,66 anatase TiO2 (001) can be grown on LAO and STO substrates. The photocatalysis efficiency might be improved because of the high surface reactivity of anatase TiO2(001).67 Also, as a widely used photocatalyst, TiO2 works well under the acidic and the oxidative conditions. We have determined the electronic structure and photo-absorption properties of nâ€“p type TiO2/LAO/TiO2 (001) (Fig. S7â€  shows the structure and absorption spectra), and they are similar to the STO/LAO/STO heterostructure. The non-polar STO or TiO2 is also possible to be combined with another polar metal oxide, for example LaVO3 or GdTiO3.68,69 With different polar metal oxides, the internal electrostatic field can be different. Additionally, by using a polar material with a small band gap like LaVO3, the absorption efficiency can be increased substantially because in this case photocarriers can be generated in the bulk polar region11 in addition to the interfacial region between the polar and non-polar materials. By tuning this factor, it is possible to design tandem oxide heterostructure photocatalysts active for light absorption with different wavelengths. We expect that the facility of growing oxide heterostructures, together with our proposed model, will enable the discovery of new photocatalysts with high efficiency.

Conclusions

In summary, we propose that nanocapacitor-like heterostructures based on non-polar and polar oxides can be used as an efficient photocatalytic material. The proposed heterostructure combines two ultrathin oxide films which act as photo-cathode and -anode materials, and a polar material with an intrinsic internal electric field which enhances the charge carrier separation and reduces the band gap. The decrease in the band gap of the system improves substantially the absorption in the near-infrared to visible light regions. We demonstrate these properties based on first principles calculations of nâ€“p type STO/LAO/STO. Additionally, we show that compressive strain can polarize the STO layers inducing an electric field in all of the heterostructure, which enhances charge separation even with thicker STO slabs. This design can also be used for other combinations of oxide heterostructures to accommodate diverse requirements.

Computational method

The proposed photocatalytic properties are elucidated using first-principles density functional theory (DFT) calculations, as implemented in VASP.70 The ion-electron interactions are described by the projector-augmented wave method.71 We used the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with PBE functional.72 Static local electronic correlations are added to the GGA exchange correlation potential in the GGA + U method with U = 10 eV and J = 0.64 eV (Ti3d).73 Using these U and J values, the obtained band gap of bulk SrTiO3 is 3.19 eV that is in excellent agreement with the experimental value.74 To further validate the GGA + U calculations, we use the screened hybrid functional HSE0675 to obtain more accurate band structures and alignments of the heterostructures.76 We used a Monkhorstâ€“Pack grid of (7 Ã— 7 Ã— 1) k-points and 400 eV energy cutoff for the structure optimization, and (9 Ã— 9 Ã— 1) and 500 eV cutoff for the electronic structure calculation. In order to avoid interactions between the neighboring slabs, 15 Ã… of vacuum is inserted between the neighboring slabs along the non-periodic direction, in conjunction with the dipole correction.77 In our calculations, all of the atomic positions are relaxed until the force on each atom is less than 0.05 eV Ã…âˆ’1. The lattice constants of LAO and STO are 3.789 and 3.905 Ã… and there is 2.9% lattice mismatch.25 We used the experimental lattice constant of STO 3.905 Ã… to fix the in-plane lattice constant of the heterostructure.
