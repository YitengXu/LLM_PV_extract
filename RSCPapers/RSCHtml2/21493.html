Abstract

A 4-octyloxy-carbazole-2,7-diyl unit was newly developed, and was combined with dithienylbenzothiadiazole derivatives by using the Suzuki-coupling reaction to synthesize good processable donor-Ï€-acceptor type copolymers. The monosubstitution at the 4-position of carbazolylene was not only effective to solubilize the narrow band gap copolymers in usual organic solvents but also could greatly improve their processability for device fabrication. All the polymers maintained the fundamental properties of carbazoleâ€“dithienylbenzothiadiazole copolymers such as good thermal stability, a narrow band gap of about 1.8 eV, a polymer interchain distance of 16â€“17 Ã…, a Ï€-stacking distance of 3.5â€“4.1 Ã… and hole mobilities of 8.6 Ã— 10âˆ’4â€“5.5 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. A bulk heterojunction organic photovoltaic device composed of the fluorinated copolymer and fullerene (PC70BM) with the active layer thickness of about 100 nm exhibited a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 4.4% with a short-circuit current density (Jsc) of 11.6 mA cmâˆ’2, a fill factor of 0.56 and an open circuit voltage of 0.670 V. This polymer based device could sustain its photovoltaic performance even at a larger thickness of the active layer of about 200 nm, i.e. a PCE of 4.50% with a Jsc of 14.44 mA cmâˆ’2. The maximum incident photon to current conversion efficiency of this device was 80% at about 530 nm, which was the highest among other carbazole-copolymer-based devices.

Introduction

Poly(carbazole-2,7-diyl)s have been investigated as light emitting materials for organic light-emitting diodes due to their high quantitative fluorescence quantum efficiency, good solubility in organic solvents and superior EL performance.1â€“4 Recently, carbazole derivatives have been gaining much attention as donor-building blocks for donor-Ï€-acceptor (D-Ï€-A) narrow band gap copolymers for bulk heterojunction (BHJ) organic photovoltaic cells (OPVs).5 According to the D-Ï€-A strategy, carbazole based narrow band gap copolymers have been developed by incorporating various strong electron-accepting units to improve the photovoltaic performance.6â€“10 Above all, poly(2,7-carbazole-alt-dithienylbenzothiadiazole) (PCDTBT) shown in Fig. 1 is one of the most successful D-Ï€-A type copolymers because of its low energy level of the highest occupied molecular orbital (EHOMO) and high stability against oxidation.11â€“13 It has been reported that the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of PCDTBT based BHJ solar cells with the standard device architecture was 4.6%,13 which could be improved by controlling the morphology of the active layer.14 However, despite the outstanding high PCE, PCDTBT has a rather low hole mobility (Î¼h) and a large band gap (Eg)12 compared to typical narrow band gap copolymers that exhibited high photovoltaic performance,5 which led to the unfavourable small short-circuit current density (Jsc).11â€“13 According to the D-Ï€-A strategy, introduction of a stronger electron-accepting unit, such as benzobisthiadiazole,15 [1,2,5]thiadiazolo[3,4-g]quinoxaline15 and naphthobisthiadiazole,16 than 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole is one of the powerful methods to construct narrower band gap polymers. Meanwhile, these thiadiazole based copolymers are hardly soluble in common organic solvents because of strong intermolecular interactions between the polymer chains into which effective solubilizing side chains could be hardly introduced. Due to their poor solubility, weight average molecular weight (Mw) values of D-Ï€-A type copolymers composed of carbazole and [1,2,5]thiadiazolo[3,4-g]quinoxaline used for OPVs were only less than 5100 Da, although these polymers showed a sufficiently narrow Eg of about 1.1 eV.17 Therefore, the development of a carbazole unit which can enhance the solubility of the polymer in organic solvents is necessary in order to obtain good soluble D-Ï€-A type narrow band gap copolymers composed of thiadiazole derivatives. As one of the approaches for solubilizing polymers, introduction of alkyl chains into 3,6-positions of carbazole has been reported.18 Poly(3,6-dimethyl-9-alkyl-9H-carbazole-2,7-diyl)s were sufficiently soluble in organic solvents such as THF, CH2Cl2 and CHCl3, whereas the modification at 3,6-positions resulted in reducing Ï€-conjugation along the main chain due to the large steric hindrance and widened Eg of the polymers. Under these circumstances, we consider that modification at the 4-position of carbazole is an approach to improve the solubility of the polymers without large steric hindrance, which allows one to obtain processable narrow band gap copolymers consisting of various thiadiazole derivatives by overcoming the drawbacks of PCDTBT.

In this paper, we designed a novel 2,7-dibromocarbazole monomer with an octyloxy side chain at the 4-position, and synthesized two D-Ï€-A type conjugated copolymers, poly[9-(heptadecan-9-yl)-4-octyloxy-9H-carbazole-2,7-diyl-alt-(4,7-bis(thiophene-2-yl)-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)-5,5-diyl] (PCOC8-TBT) and poly[9-(heptadecan-9-yl)-4-octyloxy-9H-carbazole-2,7-diyl-alt-(4,7-bis(thiophene-2-yl)-5-fluoro-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)-5,5-diyl] (PCOC8-TBTF1) as shown in Fig. 1, to confirm the fundamental properties and characteristics of photovoltaics.

Results and discussion

Theoretical calculations

The optimized geometries of C-TBT, COC1-TBT and COC1-TBTF1 as models of PCDTBT, PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 were calculated by density functional theory (DFT), which are shown in Fig. 2. The dihedral angles between the carbazole and the thiophene units of C-TBT, COC1-TBT and COC1-TBTF1 were 27.52Â°, 28.10Â° and 27.72Â°, respectively, and were almost the same. Thus, it is considered that the alkoxy group at the 4-position of carbazole does not induce large torsion between the carbazole and thiophene units. In addition, the dihedral angle between the thiophene and benzothiadiazole units of COC1-TBTF1 was 1.02Â°, which is smaller than that of COC1-TBT. Generally, the planar conformation of the polymer backbone contributes to expand Ï€-conjugation and facilitate Ï€-stacking, which is favorable to achieve narrow band gap and high carrier mobility. It has been reported that the fluorinated oligothiophene exhibited the small dihedral angle (less than 1Â°) between the neighboring thiophene units probably due to the inductive effect of fluorine.22 Similarly, several theoretical and experimental studies have shown that fluorinated dithienylbenzothiadiazole units favour planar conformation.23,24 Therefore, it is expected that fluorine at the benzothiadiazole unit could contribute to take planar conformation of PCOC8-TBTF1.

Synthesis and fundamental properties

A route for the syntheses of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 is shown in Scheme 1. Compound 1 was synthesized from 2-fluoro-4-bromoiodobenzene in the presence of t-BuOK by nucleophilic substitution of 1-octanol. Lithiation of 1 followed by monoborolation selectively yielded 2. The Suzuki coupling reaction between 2 and 2,5-dibromonitrobenzene in a 1:1 ratio yielded precursor 3, which was cyclized by a ring closure reaction to form 4. An alkylation of the nitrogen position of 4 with 9-heptadecane p-toluenesulfonate yielded 5, which was successively diborolated in the presence of Pd(dppf)Cl2 to give the key monomer M1. PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 were obtained over 80% yields by Suzuki coupling from the corresponding monomers. After polymerization, the terminal tails of the polymers were capped with a phenyl group.

PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 were highly soluble in common organic solvents such as oDCB and 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene. For example, 2 mg of PCOC8-TBT was fully soluble in 0.2 ml of oDCB, whereas 2 mg of PCDTBT (Mn = 15.0 kg molâˆ’1) was not completely soluble in 0.2 ml of oDCB at 40 Â°C. Energy levels of the polymers

The EHOMO values of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 were estimated from their onset of photoelectron spectra in the atmosphere (Fig. 2S in the ESIâ€ ), and the results are summarized in Table 1. The EHOMO of PCOC8-TBT was estimated at about âˆ’5.15 eV, which was higher than the EHOMO of PCDTBT (âˆ’5.35 eV). The strong donating ability of the octyloxy group at the 4-position of carbazole brings about the shallow EHOMO. This result is in accord with the tendency to shallow EHOMO of the polymers with alkoxy groups.6,29 Unexpectedly, PCOC8-TBTF1 exhibited a higher EHOMO of âˆ’4.99 eV than that of PCOC8-TBT, although it has been recognized that introducing a fluorine group is one of the effective way to deepen the EHOMO of the polymers.23,30,31 The shallow EHOMO of PCOC8-TBTF1 is probably due to the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking of the polymer backbone, which simultaneously narrows the Eg and shallow the EHOMO. The energy levels of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (ELUMO) of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 estimated from their Eg and EHOMO were situated at âˆ’3.31 and âˆ’3.23 eV, respectively, which are sufficiently higher than the ELUMO of PCBM (âˆ’4.3 eV). Accordingly, it is expected that charge separation could occur at interfaces between the polymer and PCBM.

Firstly, the photovoltaic characteristics of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 based BHJ OPVs with the active layer thickness of less than about 100 nm were investigated. When the composition ratio of PCOC8-TBT to PC70BM was 1:3, the average PCE (PCEav) of five OPV cells with the active layer thickness of 79 nm was 3.3% with a Jsc of 9.0 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc of 0.82 V and FF of 0.44. The PCEav increased to 4.0% when the composition ratio was changed to 1:4, which is due to the increase of Jsc and FF. Accordingly, the continual route for electrons and holes to reach the electrodes was formed by optimizing the ratio of PCOC8-TBT and PC70BM mixtures. On the other hand, the BHJ OPV consisting of PCOC8-TBTF1 and PC70BM (1:3) with an active layer thickness of 97 nm showed a PCEav of 4.5% with a Jsc of 11.7 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc of 0.666 V and FF of 0.57, which was almost the same as PCEav of the device with the blending ratio of 1:4. In comparison with PCOC8-TBT, the BHJ OPV consisting of PCOC8-TBTF1 exhibited higher Jsc and FF, which led to higher PCEav. However, Voc was still low, which could be due to the high lying EHOMO of PCOC8-TBTF1. In addition, these results are comparable to the optimized performance of PCDTBT based devices with the same architecture characterized by the relatively high Voc and rather low FF (PCEopt = 4.57%, Jsc = 10.22 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 0.89 V and FF = 0.51, active layer thickness = 70 nm).13

In order to investigate the values of Jsc and FF of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 based BHJ OPVs, surface morphologies of spin-coating films of the polymer:PC70BM (1:4) blends with the thickness of less than about 100 nm were observed by AFM. Both the AFM topographic images in Fig. 7 show refined morphologies without pronounced phase separations on the microscale. The BHJ structure must have a comparable nanoscale structure to be an effective solar cell since the exciton diffusion length of organic semiconductors is only about 10 nm.32 Accordingly, efficient charge separation of excitons at the interfaces between the polymer and PC70BM is expected to occur in these blend films with phase separation on the nanoscale.

To gain more insight into the different performances between the devices based on PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1, the effect of carrier mobility was investigated. The Î¼h of the polymers were measured by the method of OFETs, and these transfer characteristics are shown in Fig. 8. The OFETs based on these polymers exhibited typical p-type semiconductor behaviours with good linear and saturation characteristics. The Î¼h of these polymers were calculated from the slope of the plot of the square root of the drain current versus the gate voltage, and PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 showed Î¼h of 8.6 Ã— 10âˆ’4 and 5.5 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 with on/off ratios of 1.3 Ã— 104 and 2.4 Ã— 104, respectively. It is considered that the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking effect observed for PCOC8-TBTF1 shown in Fig. 3 and 5 might contribute to enhancing the charge transport. The low hole mobility is responsible for increasing the recombination of separated charges and series resistance in an active layer of BHJ OPVs, which lead to low Jsc and FF. Thus, the higher Jsc and FF of the BHJ devices using PCOC8-TBTF1 are ascribed to the higher Î¼h than that of PCOC8-TBT.

Since BHJ OPVs that can constantly work with high PCE without depending on the thickness of the active layer are desired for practical application,33 the effect of the active layer thickness on the performance of PCOC8-TBT and PCOC8-TBTF1 based devices was investigated. The device consisting of PCOC8-TBT and PC70BM (1:4) with the active layer thickness of 104 nm showed a PCEav of 2.8%, which is lower than that of the device with the active layer thickness of 77 nm as listed in Table 2. It has also been reported that the PCE of the PCDTBT based OPV decreased from 4.22% to 3.43% by making the BHJ layer thicker from 80 to 100 nm.13 The decrease in PCEav is mainly caused by the lowering Jsc and FF, which might be ascribed to increased collection losses of the generated charge carriers by increasing the charge recombination and series resistance in the thicker active layer. Thus, a low Î¼h of electron-donating conjugated polymers often limits the thickness of the active layers in BHJ OPVs.34 However, PCEav of the OPV devices using PCOC8-TBTF1 were maintained even though the thickness of the active layer increased up to about 200 nm as listed in Table 2. When the active layer of PCOC8-TBTF1:PC70BM (1:4) device was thickened from 102 to 206 nm, FF decreased from 0.56 to 0.481 whereas Jsc increased from 11.6 to 14.44 mA cmâˆ’2. Consequently, these changes led to the improvement in PCEav from 4.4 to 4.50% (Fig. 6). This improvement is mainly due to the increase of the absorbed amount of photons by making the active layer thicker. Additionally, the higher Î¼h of 5.5 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 for PCOC8-TBTF1 than PCOC8-TBT and PCDTBT (Î¼h = 1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1)13 must allow the PCOC8-TBTF1 based BHJ OPV to maintain its performance even in the thick active layer.

Lastly, the ratio between the number of signal electrons and the number of all the incident photons at a given wavelength for the BHJ devices of PCOC8-TBT:PC70BM (1:4) and PCOC8-TBTF1:PC70BM (1:4) were measured, and their IPCE curves are shown in Fig. 9. These devices were found to show efficient sensitization over the whole visible light region. The PCOC8-TBTF1 based device with the active layer thickness of 102 nm displayed higher IPCE response (maximum IPCE = 68%, Jsc = 11.6 mA cmâˆ’2) than that of PCOC8-TBT (maximum IPCE = 65%, Jsc = 9.9 mA cmâˆ’2), which is due to the narrower Eg and the higher Î¼h of PCOC8-TBTF1. The PCOC8-TBTF1:PC70BM device with the thickness of 206 nm exhibited maximum IPCE of 80.36% (Jsc = 14.44 mA cmâˆ’2) at 530 nm, and IPCE covered over 70% in the range of 370â€“600 nm, which is higher than that of PCDTBT based BHJ OPVs, e.g., it has been reported that the PCDTBT based device using TiOx as an optical spacer showed the maximum IPCE of at most 70% (PCE = 6.1%, Jsc = 10.6 mA cmâˆ’2, Voc = 0.88 and FF = 0.66).11 These results demonstrated that the PCOC8-TBTF1:PC70BM device can efficiently convert absorbed photons into current over a range of visible light.

Conclusions

PCDTBT is a typical D-Ï€-A type copolymer composed of the carbazole and dithienylbenzothiadiazole (TBT) units, and has exhibited a PCE of OPV over 7%. However, the low solubility of PCDTBT in organic solvents has caused limitation of polymer design and synthesis, which leads to stagnation of the OPV performance. Improvement of the solubility by retaining the characteristic features of PCDTBT must break through the stagnation on the performance of the PCDTBT based devices. An idea of developing a solubilizing donor unit was a starting point of this work, and it was decided to synthesize an N-alkylcarbazole-2,7-diyl unit with a substituent at the 4-position that must serve as the solubilizing part. The DFT calculation supported that the 4-substituted carbazole could construct a planar D-Ï€-A copolymer as well as PCDTBT.

An N-(1-octylnonyl)-4-octyloxycarbazole-2,7-diyl unit was successfully synthesized by the stepwise method for the first time, and it was polycondensed with the TBT unit to obtain PCOC8-TBT. Similarly, PCOC8-TBTF1 having a fluoro group at the 5-position of TBT was also synthesized. The fundamental properties of PCOC8-TBTs were examined and compared with those of PCDTBT. PCOC8-TBT showed better solubility while its extension of Ï€-conjugation and polymer structures evaluated by UV-Vis and XRD analyses were comparable to those of PCDTBT as expected. Furthermore, it was revealed that PCOC8-TBTF1 had superior characteristics with the narrower band gap (Eg = 1.76 eV) and higher hole transportability (Î¼h = 5.5 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1) compared to PCDTBT and PCOC8-TBT, which could be interpreted by the closer Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking of the polymer backbones with higher coplanarity.

All the BHJ solar cells fabricated with PCOC8-TBTs and PC70BM exhibited good photovoltaic performances showing PCEav in the range of 2.8â€“4.5%. Especially, the PCOC8-TBTF1 based OPV device could maintain its photovoltaic performance, or rather, improve in PCEav from 4.3% to 4.5%, when the active layer was made thicker from ca. 100 to 200 nm. This result could be ascribed to the higher IPCE of over 70% in the whole visible light region and the higher Jsc value of over 14 mA cmâˆ’2 observed in the thicker device. Hence, it is concluded that the additional introduction of a substituent at the 4-position of the carbazole unit was proved to be effective to obtain processable carbazole-based semiconducting polymers which could exhibit the characteristics and functions required for high performance organic devices.
