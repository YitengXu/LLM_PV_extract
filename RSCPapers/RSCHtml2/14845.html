Abstract

Developing cost-effective and earth-abundant noble-metal-free oxygen evolution reaction (OER) electrocatalysts with high activity and durability is highly desirable for future renewable energy systems. Herein, we develop a facile and controllable approach to engineer a series of structurally tiered bimetallic Niâ€“Fe phosphide nanocomposites with well-designed architectures and compositions through the topological conversion of the flowerlike precursor of Niâ€“Fe layered double hydroxides (LDHs). Remarkably, benefitting from their special physicochemical features and desirable synergistic effect, the resultant bimetallic phosphides can serve as a class of advanced high-performance electrocatalysts for water oxidation with an extremely low overpotential of 233 mV to deliver a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2, a small Tafel slope of 42.5 mV decâˆ’1, and strong durability (30 h of continuous water electrolysis), favourably representing the state-of-the-art level. This work elucidates the promising electrocatalytic performance of multinary transition-metal phosphides with controlled structures and compositions derived from LDHs for the OER.

1. Introduction

Molecular hydrogen (H2), as a sustainable and clean energy supply with a high calorific value and zero carbon emission, is regarded as an ideal candidate to substitute non-renewable fossil fuels in the future.1â€“3 Assuming that electricity can be acquired from solar power, direct electrolysis of water is then a promising and feasible route to produce high-purity H2 on a large scale.4 Generally, the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) are two half-cell reactions of electrochemical water splitting. The OER, with a complex four-electron transfer process, is recognized as a bottleneck in water splitting because it is thermodynamically unfavourable and kinetically sluggish. Thus, effective electrocatalysts are required for the OER to achieve a high current density at a low overpotential.5,6 To date, the state-of-the-art catalysts for the OER are largely based on noble metals (e.g., IrO2 and RuO2),5,7 but the high cost and scarcity of these metals significantly limit their large-scale practical application. As a consequence, a great deal of effort has been made to develop effective alternatives,8â€“15 preferably using low-cost transition-metal oxides,16â€“18 hydroxides,19 chalcogenides,20â€“22 nitrides,23 and heteroatom-doped carbon-based materials.24,25

Transition-metal phosphides (TMPs) have recently emerged as a class of promising earth-abundant electrocatalysts not only for the HER but also for the OER.26â€“33 Impressively, it has been found that their electrocatalytic activities in the OER could be even superior to those of their corresponding oxide/hydroxide counterparts.26,34,35 However, compared to the well-established noble-metal-based catalysts, future applications of TMP-based electrocatalysts are hampered by their complicated preparation processes and limited electroactive sites due to technical difficulties in controlling the chemical composition and optimizing the structural properties.36 Thus, developing facile synthetic methodologies to fabricate active and robust TMP-based electrocatalysts with well-designed architectures and compositions for the OER is highly desirable, yet remains a great challenge.

On the other hand, layered double hydroxides (LDHs) are a class of 2D anionic clays, which are composed of positively charged brucite-like host layers and charge-balancing anions in the interlayer space.37,38 In addition to their economic advantage, their metal cations are atomically dispersed, and the chemical compositions of LDHs are designable within a wide range, promising a variety of functionalities. For example, the incorporation of electrochemically active metal cations (e.g., Mn, Fe, Co, and Ni) can endow LDHs with electrocatalytic OER activity.39â€“41 Particularly, it has been well known that NiFe-based LDH materials are a type of state-of-the-art OER catalyst with precious-metal-free compositions. Nevertheless, demanding preparation procedures including intercalation and exfoliation of LDHs are generally required to achieve a commendable activity, which apparently hinder their potential large-scale applications.41â€“43

In order to take advantage of the high efficiency of TMPs, it seems reasonable to speculate that direct topological phosphidation of LDHs might be a viable route to produce mixed bimetallic phosphides for further enhanced electrocatalytic performance. To test this hypothesis, the process feasibility is explored. As shown in Scheme 1, herein, we report our recent research endeavor in transformative phosphidation of NiFe-LDH to bimetallic Niâ€“Fe phosphides (denoted as NiFe-P hereafter for simplicity). Interestingly, the transformation reaction is topological and highly controlled. With the elegant control over the structure and chemical composition, the resulting nanostructured NiFe-P exhibits extraordinarily high electrocatalytic activity and stability for the water oxidation reaction, which is not only superior to its precursor NiFe-LDH, but also even comparable to the state-of-the-art transition-metal-based and noble-metal-based OER catalysts.

2.4. Electrochemical measurements

The electrochemical measurements were carried out using a computer-controlled electrochemical workstation (Autolab, PGSTAT 302N) with a standard three-electrode system, in which a catalyst-modified glassy carbon electrode (GCE, 3 mm in diameter) was used as the working electrode, a Pt plate as the counter electrode, and an Ag/AgCl (filled with 3 M KCl solution) electrode as the reference electrode. The preparation of the working electrode was as follows: the solid catalyst (3 mg) and Vulcan XC-72 carbon (3 mg) were dispersed in 977.6 Î¼L of water, 244.4 Î¼L of ethanol and 50.92 Î¼L of 5 wt% Nafion solution. Then a homogeneous catalyst ink was obtained by ultrasonicating the mixture for about 30 min. Next, 3 Î¼L of the catalyst ink was loaded onto the GCE (loading amount for all the samples: about 0.1 mg cmâˆ’2). Finally, the electrode was dried at room temperature overnight. Prior to all the experiments, the electrolyte solution (1.0 M KOH) was purged with O2 for 30 min. Before the electrochemical catalytic measurements, all the working electrodes were characterized by continuous cyclic voltammetry (CV) several times at a scan rate of 50 mV sâˆ’1 until the signals were relatively stabilized. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was performed with a scan rate of 5 mV sâˆ’1 to obtain the polarization curves. All the polarization curves were corrected with iR-compensation. A long-term stability test was performed by chronopotentiometric (CP) measurement in 1.0 M KOH electrolyte with the current density at 10 mA cmâˆ’2.

In this work, the electrochemical measurements were carried out on at least four working electrodes to check the reproducibility and their average was taken into account. The working electrode was continuously rotated at 1600 rpm to get rid of the oxygen bubbles. All the current densities were normalised to the geometrical area of the GCE, and all the measured potentials vs. Ag/AgCl (3.0 M KCl) were converted to a reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) scale according to the Nernst equation: E(RHE) = E(Ag/AgCl) + 0.210 + 0.0591 Ã— pH.

3.1. Preparation and characterization of catalysts

The NiFe-LDH precursor was fabricated via a one-pot solvothermal process using metal nitrates as metal precursors, urea as the base, NH4F as the mineralizer, and methanol as the solvent (Scheme 1). In the case of NiFe(3:1)-LDH, as revealed by a morphological investigation (SEM and TEM techniques; Fig. 1a, b and S1â€ ), the as-synthesized NiFe(3:1)-LDH exhibits a flowerlike hierarchical structure (ca. 1â€“1.5 Î¼m in particle size) formed by a large number of nanoscale petals connected with each other. It is worth mentioning that the solvent plays a critical role in the formation of the above nanosheet assemblages with hierarchical structures. For example, by using pure deionized water, aggregated flakes together with small clusters are obtained (Fig. S2a and bâ€ ); by using ethanol or isopropanol as the solvent, only irregular and severely aggregated particles can be produced (Fig. S2c and dâ€ ). 3.2. Electrocatalytic performance of the catalysts

The electrocatalytic performance of NiFe(3:1)-P for the OER was evaluated in a standard three-electrode system with 1.0 M KOH as the electrolyte (see the details in the Experimental section). In comparison, NiFe(3:1)-LDH, NiFe(3:1)-LDO, NiPx and FePx with a similar flowerlike morphology were also studied as control catalysts. Fig. 5a displays the polarization curves. As a simple conductor, the bare GCE shows no catalytic contribution to the OER. The operating potential required to achieve a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 is a commonly adopted index to evaluate the OER activity of a catalyst, as it is approximately the current density for a 10% efficient solar-to-fuel conversion device.5 As clearly demonstrated (Fig. 5a and c), the NiFe(3:1)-LDH precursor, with its hierarchical architecture built of interconnected nanopetals, requires a low overpotential of 280 mV to reach 10 mA cmâˆ’2, an excellent activity even better than those of highly active LDH-based catalysts obtained via tedious exfoliation procedures.40,41 After the facile topological phosphidation, the as-obtained NiFe(3:1)-P demonstrates a remarkably enhanced OER activity. A much larger current density can be delivered by NiFe(3:1)-P at the same operating overpotential (e.g., 114 and 20 mA cmâˆ’2 at 300 mV for NiFe(3:1)-P and NiFe(3:1)-LDH, respectively; Fig. 5e). In particular, NiFe(3:1)-P only needs an overpotential of as low as 233 mV to reach a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2, which corresponds to a negative shift of 47 mV compared with that of the NiFe(3:1)-LDH precursor, revealing that the transformation of LDHs through phosphidation treatment is indeed a promising strategy to further promote the OER activity. The remarkable OER activity of NiFe(3:1)-P is also much better than that of the commercial RuO2 benchmark, which requires 342 mV to reach 10 mA cmâˆ’2 (see the LSV curve in Fig. S15aâ€ ). Significantly, it is worth noting that NiFe(3:1)-P presented here is among the best TMP-based OER catalysts reported to date in the literature,26,34,35,44,48 and it also rivals most highly active electrocatalysts based on transition metals and noble metals.5,49â€“51 A detailed comparison with different OER catalysts is provided in Table S3.â€

On the other hand, the monometallic phosphides, NiPx and FePx, need a much larger overpotential to attain 10 mA cmâˆ’2 (305 and 420 mV, respectively). The superior performance of our NiFe(3:1)-P to the corresponding monometallic counterparts is probably attributable to the synergistic coupling effect between the two different metal phosphides with optimized local electronic states owing to the presence of heterojunctions (namely between Ni2P and FeP). To better elucidate this claim, we further performed a comparative XPS analysis on the two control samples NiPx and FePx, which indeed sheds light on the electronic structure modulation behavior of our NiFe(3:1)-P sample. As displayed in Fig. S16,â€  the Ni 2p2/3 binding energy in NiFe(3:1)-P exhibits a positive shift of 0.3 eV relative to monometallic NiPx, whereas the Fe 2p2/3 binding energy shows a negative shift of 0.4 eV relative to monometallic FePx, suggesting the occurrence of appreciable electronic modulation in the NiFe(3:1)-P. Recent theoretical computations and experimental validations revealed that the incorporation of the second metal species can finely tailor the coordination environment and electronic structure of the catalytically active centers, thus dramatically boosting the electrocatalytic activities of transition-metal-based materials.8,13,15,52â€“54 On the basis of the above results, therefore, we propose that in our bimetallic NiFe-P samples, the intimate contact between Ni2P and FeP phases and the inter-doping of Ni and Fe species can optimize the electronic structure of catalytic sites, thereby facilitating the adsorption and desorption of reaction intermediates and promoting their OER activity. Additionally, the calcination-derived sample, NiFe(3:1)-LDO, gives a moderate OER activity with an overpotential of 320 mV at 10 mA cmâˆ’2. Obviously, the NiFe(3:1)-LDO catalyst underperforms the NiFe(3:1)-LDH precursor and NiFe(3:1)-P too. The above results are consistent with the previous finding that TMPs, with well-designed chemistry and structures, are capable of outperforming their corresponding hydroxides/oxides in the OER.26

The Tafel slope was further determined to evaluate the OER kinetics. As illustrated in Fig. 5b and d, the Tafel slope of the NiFe(3:1)-P sample (42.5 mV decâˆ’1) is the smallest among all the samples including NiFe(3:1)-LDH (52.0 mV decâˆ’1), NiFe(3:1)-LDO (58.3 mV decâˆ’1), NiPx (62.0 mV decâˆ’1), and FePx (120.1 mV decâˆ’1). Notably, this value is also comparable to those of the most active OER catalysts reported in the literature (Table S3â€ ), indicative of favourable OER kinetics of the NiFe(3:1)-P electrocatalyst.

3.5. Durability and catalytic mechanism

Since the durability is another important indicator for the OER catalyst, the stability of NiFe(3:1)-P was further examined by long-term electrolysis at a constant current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2. As shown in Fig. 8a, the high activity was retained well over a galvanostatic measurement of 30 h, confirming the excellent stability of NiFe(3:1)-P in the prolonged electrochemical process. The morphology of NiFe(3:1)-P did not change pronouncedly before or after the electrolysis (Fig. 8bâ€“d), although some flakes emerged to encapsulate the spent sample. These small flakes are likely to be the in situ generated surface Niâ€“Fe oxo/hydroxides during the electrolysis, which will be discussed below in detail.

To gain insight into its structureâ€“performance relationship, the spent NiFe(3:1)-P electrocatalyst after the stability test was further characterized using XPS and EDX elemental mapping techniques. As shown in Fig. S22a and b,â€  the peaks at 853.2 eV (Ni 2p) and 707.0 eV (Fe 2p) assigned to the surface metal phosphide species are almost invisible, while the intensities of the peaks corresponding to oxidized Ni and Fe species increase significantly, suggesting the formation of superficial oxidized metal species. Consistently, serious weakening of the overall P signal is observed in the P 2p spectrum (Fig. S22câ€ ). Meanwhile, the peaks at 129.4 and 130.5 eV for Pâ€“M (M = Ni and Fe) species disappear, and only the weak band at around 133.7 eV assigned to Pâ€“O species is barely observable, verifying the surface oxidation of NiFe(3:1)-P. Besides, from the EDX elemental mappings of the spent NiFe(3:1)-P (Fig. 8e), it can be found that the O signal becomes quite strong, while P element is mainly confined in the inner region, further revealing the formation of a quasi-coreâ€“shell structure with Niâ€“Fe oxo/hydroxide species on the outer shell. Similar phosphorus replacements by oxygen have also been observed in the other TMP-based OER systems.26,55,56 Taken all together, it is now known that the surface of NiFe(3:1)-P has been largely converted to Niâ€“Fe oxo/hydroxides during the OER process. Such transformation is likely responsible for the observed enhanced OER activity.

On the basis of the above results, the excellent performance of NiFe(3:1)-P can be related to its unique physicochemical and structural properties: (i) the hierarchical flowerlike architecture attained by stacked nanoscale petals ensures excellent electrical and mechanical contact; (ii) the porosity in the flowerlike catalysts offers a fuller exposure of electroactive sites and an easier mass diffusion for the OER; and (iii) the synergistic coupling effect between different metal species in the Niâ€“Fe phosphide nanocomposite together with the in situ generated quasi-coreâ€“shell heterostructure (i.e., nanocomposite) of phosphide and oxo-hydroxide can further boost the electrocatalytic activity.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have reported the engineering and fabrication of hierarchical bimetallic Niâ€“Fe phosphide nanocomposites with well-controlled structures and compositions through the facile topological phosphidation of the corresponding flowerlike LDH precursor. A series of key synthetic parameters are optimized, and their impacts on the physicochemical structural properties as well as the electrocatalytic OER performances of the as-obtained products are systematically investigated in detail. Notably, the optimal NiFe(3:1)-P catalyst is able to perform as a type of advanced OER catalyst with an overpotential of as low as 233 mV at 10 mA cmâˆ’2, a small Tafel slope of 42.5 mV decâˆ’1, and remarkable working durability. It presents a much better OER catalytic activity than the corresponding NiFe-LDH precursor, NiFe-LDO, and individual counterparts NiPx and FePx; it is also comparable or even superior to the state-of-the-art electrocatalysts reported to date. The outstanding electrocatalytic performance of NiFe-P can be associated with its well-controlled architectural and compositional features, the synergistic coupling effect between different metal species, and the in situ transformation to the phosphide/oxo-hydroxide heterostructure during the OER. The work presented here provides a promising and efficient route to explore highly active and durable noble-metal-free electrocatalysts based on TMPs for the OER and other renewable energy applications.
