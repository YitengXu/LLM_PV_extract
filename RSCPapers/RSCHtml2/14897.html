Abstract

As a promising technology, electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries have gained significant attention in the past few years. Herein, in this review, we focused on the main challenges of the electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries in alkaline electrolytes and the up-to-date progress from materials to technologies towards overcoming these technical barriers. We first overviewed the design and working mechanism of the battery and classified the hindrances into dendritic growth at the anode, lack of higher performance bifunctional catalysts at the air electrode, and electrolyte-related problems. Then, detailed discussions have been provided on the latest progress to address these technical issues based on the nano/micro-materials. Flexible zincâ€“air batteries as a new development have also been discussed in a separate section. Finally, conclusions have been provided followed by future perspective.

1. Introduction

Advanced materials for the efficient harvesting, storage and utilization of renewable energy are at the heart of current energy research.1â€“9 To date, Li-ion batteries have been the most successful energy storage solution and widely used in portable electronics and electric vehicles (EVs) since their first report in 1991. Unfortunately, their limitations of high cost, insufficient energy density, and blemished safety have held back the large-scale applications of Li-ion batteries in the automobile industry, especially for the extended range EVs.1,10,11 To this regard, many post-lithium-ion technologies including Liâ€“S, Na-ion and metalâ€“air batteries have been proposed and actively pursued. Recently, metalâ€“air batteries have become a promising power source, based on the nature of high theoretical energy density and free-oxygen fuel from the atmosphere.12â€“15 Among various metalâ€“air batteries, alkaline metalâ€“air batteries, especially lithiumâ€“oxygen, have been intensively investigated due to their high specific energy density up to 5200 W h kgâˆ’1.16â€“23 However, the rechargeability, safety and cost issues make them hard to be commercialized. Because of these currently unconquerable limitations, rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries have attracted significant attention as an alternative in the past few years.11,24

As one of the most abundant elements in the earth's crust, zinc has been the chosen electrode material for several types of primary batteries ever since the first battery prototype developed by Volta more than 200 years ago. Primary zincâ€“air batteries were first introduced to the science community in the late 19th century, and their commercial products started to enter the market in the 1930s.25 Among all metalâ€“air systems, zincâ€“air batteries are the most practically viable choice. They possess some desirable features such as a high theoretical energy density, low cost, great safety and environmental friendliness.26 The theoretical energy density of zincâ€“air is 1086 W h kgâˆ’1, about 5 times higher than that of Li-ion; its operational cost is estimated to be less than 10 $ kWâˆ’1 hâˆ’1, only a small fraction of that Li-ion.26,27 The primary zincâ€“air battery is a mature technology that has already been successfully applied to the fields of hearing, medical, navigation and railway signal devices. Very recently, electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries have emerged and undergone rapid development.17,28,29 Their rechargeability is realized by developing new bifunctional air cathodes and bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysts that can facilitate both the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) during discharge and the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) during charge.30â€“32 In addition, zincâ€“air batteries can also be mechanically recharged by physically substituting the zinc electrode and electrolyte. There is no major challenge with mechanical recharging, and it allows fast refueling like a conventional petrol refill. Furthermore, flexible and stretchable designs have been developed since 2015. Nevertheless, electrically rechargeable or mechanically flexible zincâ€“air batteries are still in their infant stage and confronted with many challenges.

There are several previous review articles related to zincâ€“air batteries that provide excellent discussions on the issues from individual components to the whole system.11,13,17,21,26,33â€“35 Nevertheless, in light of the increased research activities, especially on bifunctional electrocatalysts for the air cathode and flexible battery designs, an up-to-date and comprehensive account of the current development status, challenges and possible solutions is highly necessary. Here in this review, we focus on the recent progress and main challenges of the electrically rechargeable and/or flexible zincâ€“air batteries in alkaline electrolytes and the solutions towards these technical barriers. We firstly overview the design, components and working mechanism of the rechargeable zincâ€“air battery and identify the problems leading to the dendritic growth at the anode, lack of a higher performance bifunctional air cathode and electrolyte related issues. Then, detailed discussions are provided on the latest progress to address these technical issues. The flexible zincâ€“air battery is also overviewed in a separate section. Finally, conclusions are given followed by a brief future perspective.

2. Fundamentals of electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries

2.1. Battery design and operation principle

As shown in Fig. 1, electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries typically consist of a metallic anode, membrane separator, electrolyte, and bifunctional air cathode. The electrolyte used is usually alkaline solutions, such as concentrated KOH or NaOH. Upon battery discharge, the metallic zinc anode gets oxidized and reacts with OHâˆ’ to yield soluble zincate ions (Zn(OH)42âˆ’). When these zincate ions become supersaturated in the electrolyte, they undergo spontaneous decomposition to form an insoluble zinc oxide, as described below.26,36 In parallel to this, self-corrosion of the zinc may occur with the generation of explosive hydrogen gas, which not only lowers the active material utilization and but also causes safety problems. At the cathode, external oxygen gas diffuses into the porous electrode driven by its concentration gradient. ORR then takes place at the triple phase boundary among the solid working electrode, the liquid electrolyte and the gas phase, similar to the cathodic process on hydrogen fuel cells.37,38 The generated hydroxyl ions migrate in the electrolyte from the air cathode to the metallic anode and complete the battery reaction. According to the reaction mechanism, the theoretical working voltage of zincâ€“air batteries is 1.65 V. However, their practical working voltages on discharge are lower, typically less than 1.2 V in order to sustain a reasonable discharge current density. A recharge voltage of around 2 V or even higher is required to reverse the reaction. Such obvious deviation from the equilibrium value is mainly caused by the large overpotentials of the oxygen reaction at the air cathode.26,39,40 The factors responsible for this are mainly the activation loss and dendritic formation, which are associated with the air cathode and zinc anode.41 Activation loss occurs due to the slowness of the reactions on the surface of the air cathode, which can be minimized by developing porous high-efficiency bifunctional catalysts. Dendritic growth at the zinc anode is another big challenge, which not only leads to large overpotentials but also a short cycle life. For the existence of soluble zincate ions, the dendritic structures always formed during extensively chargeâ€“discharge cycles and blocks the flow of the electrolyte and the reactions at the zinc electrode.26,41 This is a complex problem associated with the design of the zinc anode, electrolytes, separators and even electrocatalysts.

2.2. Oxygen reduction reaction and oxygen evolution reaction

Rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries require bifunctional air cathodes to accelerate the sluggish reaction rates of both ORR and OER. The ORR process in a zincâ€“air battery contains a few steps: oxygen diffusion through the porous electrode, oxygen absorption to the catalyst surface, electron movement from the anode to the oxygen molecules, weakening and breaking of the oxygenâ€“oxygen bond and the hydroxyl ion transfer through the electrolyte to the metallic anode.11,26,42 Though the mechanism of the ORR involves a series of complicated reactions, it is believed that a direct four-electron pathway or a peroxide two-electron pathway may proceed for the oxygen reduction depending on the adsorption type.13 Even for the same catalyst, the ORR behaviours may be different depending on its structure. In a four-electron pathway, the oxygen directly reduced to OHâˆ’ under bidentate O2 adsorption, where two O atoms coordinate with the catalyst. On the contrary, oxygen indirectly reduced to OHâˆ’via HO2âˆ’ in a two-electron pathway under end-on O2 adsorption where one O atom coordinates perpendicularly to the catalyst. Then, this two-electron oxygen reduction may be followed by either a two-electron reduction of peroxide or the chemical disproportionation of peroxide. The four-electron pathway is preferable, because the two-electron pathway forms corrosive peroxide species and shows low energy efficiency.32 It is widely believed that the four-electron reduction predominates on noble metal catalysts, whereas the two-electron reduction primarily participates on carbonaceous materials. For other materials, such as transition-metal oxides, different ORR pathways may occur because of their specific crystal structure and molecular composition. The oxygen evolution reaction plays a key part in electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries.43â€“45 Though the ORR is strongly irreversible, the OER in a rechargeable zincâ€“air battery during charging reverses the ORR by electrocatalysis. Same as ORR, the OER is also a complex process involving several reactions. As oxygen is generally evolved from an oxide phase, the mechanism largely depends on the electrode materials and the site geometry of the metal cation.46 Ideally, the large amount of zincate ions should be confined to the anode for oxygen evolution. However, the zincate ions in the alkaline electrolyte decompose to ZnO spontaneously after accessing the saturation point. These ZnO powders have low conductivity, solubility and poor electrochemical reversibility, which significantly reduce the battery's cycle-life.11,13 It is important to develop new bifunctional electrocatalysts to fulfil the requirements of high ORR and OER performance, low cost and environmental friendliness.11

3.1. Air electrode design and bifunctional catalysts for ORR & OER

During the battery discharge of the zincâ€“air battery, ORR occurs at the boundary among the triple phase, where the electrode, the ion-conducting electrolyte and the oxygen are in close contact.13 Since the solubility and diffusivity are much lower in electrolytes, oxygen diffusion is much faster in the gas phase than in the electrolyte. Thus, the design of a porous structure to replace the conventional electrode architectures for air electrodes has not been altered until now. Typically, an air electrode consists of a gas diffusion layer and a catalytic active layer, which is prepared by laminating the layers together with a metallic current collector. The gas diffusion layer is usually composed of a carbon material and a wet-proofing binder such as polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE).26

It is important to maintain the hydrophobicity, which supports oxygen diffusion without any electrolyte leakage. The catalytic active layer, where ORR and OER take place, includes bifunctional catalysts, carbon materials, and the binder.50 Obviously, the bifunctional catalyst plays a key part in the air cathode for battery performance, and it is critical to develop new bifunctional catalysts with high efficiency, low cost and zero pollution.51â€“53 A selection of typical bifunctional electrocatalysts for zincâ€“air batteries is summarized in Table 1, with the applied electrolyte and related battery performance. Meanwhile, corrosion of the catalyst and carbon substrate should also be considered. Because of the large surface area of the carbon substrate, the catalyst and carbon material can be severely attacked and oxidized by the highly reactive oxygen generated from the OER.9

3.1.1. Nobel metals and metal alloys. In the early days, precious metal catalysts such as platinum were widely used for primary zincâ€“air batteries.11,21,54,55 Other precious metals such as silver, gold and their alloys have also been considered as catalysts for metalâ€“air batteries.56,57 Su and coworkers systematically investigated gold nanocrystals with variable index facets as bifunctional catalysts for Liâ€“air batteries.57 In particular, the trisoctahedral gold nanoparticles enclosed by 24 high-index {441} facets showed the lowest overpotential and highest capacity of 20290 mA h gâˆ’1. The authors also found that the high-index facets exhibited much better catalytic activity. Due to the advantages of low cost (1% of platinum), long-term stability and comparable overpotential (50â€“100 mV larger than platinum), silver is particularly attractive.58â€“61

Thus, silver alloys have been intensively investigated, where cobalt, palladium, nickel and copper are typical doping elements for Ag based catalysts for ORR.11,62 However, most of the investigations were focused on the ORR activity of the primary zincâ€“air batteries. More recently, Lei et al. reported an electrocatalyst of novel Agâ€“Cu alloyed nanoparticles embedded in amorphous Cu film prepared by a pulsed laser deposition technique.63 Besides good ORR catalytic activity, this rechargeable zincâ€“air battery also exhibited low chargeâ€“discharge voltage polarization of 1.1 V at 20 mA cmâˆ’2 and good cycle stability over 100 runs. Jin et al. presented work on the direct growth of Agâ€“Cu bifunctional catalysts on a nickel current collector via a cost-effective, environmental friendly and facile process called a galvanic displacement reaction.64 As the Ag and Cu atoms were in their metallic state, the formation of hindrances such as CuO and Cu2O were inhibited. The full battery showed a round trip efficiency of 53.08% after 100 cycles (20 mA cmâˆ’2 current density, 20 min cycle time), which was a 0.82% decrease compared to the initial 53.9%. Wu and coworkers reported a Agâ€“Cu metallic glass electrocatalyst fabricated via pulsed laser deposition technique, which showed excellent control over the surface microtopography and good ORR and OER activities.65 After dealloying treatment in N2 saturated 0.1 M KOH, this catalyst showed a superior half wave potential of 0.78 V (vs. RHE) at 1600 rpm. The full battery exhibited a stable discharge voltage of 1.0 V, even after 1000 charge/discharge cycles (20 mA cmâˆ’2 current density, 20 min cycle time).

3.1.2. Metal oxides. Precious metals have excellent ORR electrocatalytic activities; however, their widespread application is prohibited by their low OER activities, high price and scarcity.26,37,60 Especially for the high price and scarcity, these two problems are hard to solve. Compared with precious metals, non-precious metal oxides are more desirable for their lower cost.26,32 Transition metal oxides in the form of spinel, perovskite and other structures are under extensive investigations.56,66â€“68 Among them, manganese dioxides have been recognized as attractive candidates for ORR due to their rich oxidation states and crystal structures, low cost, low toxicity and environmental friendliness. In addition, manganese dioxide exhibits a higher catalytic efficiency in alkaline solutions, which suits the zincâ€“air battery systems. However, the electric conductivity of MnO2 is very poor. 3.1.3. Carbon based materials. Precious and nonprecious metals and metal oxides have been studied as bifunctional electrocatalysts for a long time. In the past few years, carbon based electrocatalysts have been introduced because of the advantages such as high porosity, large specific surface area, superior conductivity and relative chemical stability.31 In addition, the carbon materials derived from biomass waste, such as corn silks, leaves and crab shells, could also significantly reduce the cost of electrocatalysts.30,82 However, conventional carbon materials show poor ORR performance through a predominant two-electron pathway with the formation of peroxides in an aqueous electrolyte.26 Chemical modification, such as N-doping, is needed to improve their ORR activity via increased structure-disorder or formation of heteroatom functionalities. For instance, Wan and coworkers recently reported a cheap, accessible and recyclable N, P, and Fe-tridoped nanoporous carbon catalyst derived from corn silk, which had a much higher ORR activity and superior stability in alkaline media.83 Meanwhile, the highly porous carbon material also works as a substrate to provide the oxygen diffusive path during charge/discharge and supports the mechanical structure of the air electrode.31 Therefore, carbon materials show dual functions of electrocatalytic active materials and the substrate of the air electrode. So far, there are two types of carbon catalysts. One is the carbon/metal oxide catalysts, and the other is the metal-free carbon catalyst.

Though carbon materials show better stability over metal and metal oxides, the overall catalytic activities are poor, especially for the OER.31,82,91 Thus, scientists walked one step forward to develop carbon/metal oxide composites, which inherit both advantages of carbon and metal oxides. Various carbon materials have been investigated to address the sluggish kinetics of ORR and OER, such as carbon nanofibers (CNFs), carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and graphene materials.51,53,92â€“100 One example is the CNFs arrays with RuO2 coatings proposed by Guo and coworkers in 2016.101 These arrays have an ordered mesoporous structure and were derived from a natural crab shell. The full battery tests showed a peak power density of 265 mW cmâˆ’2, a small overpotential of 0.7 V and long stability. An intertwined core-corona shaped bifunctional catalyst (referred to as IT-CCBC) was introduced by Lee et al., where LaNiO3 nanoparticles were encapsulated by N-doped CNTs to produce a high performance catalyst.104 The morphology of IT-CCBC not only enhanced the electron conductivity but also provided a highly porous diffusion path and eventually improved the catalytic performance for both ORR and OER. The full battery tests displayed an acceptable overpotential of 1.1 V and an improved cycle life of 40 h at a large current density of 50 mA cmâˆ’2 (2 h discharge followed by 2 h charge). In addition, Wang et al. reported the successful synthesis of high-density iron/nitrogen-doped carbon (Fe@Nâ€“C) coreâ€“shell nanoparticles with superior ORR and OER performance in alkaline electrolytes.100 The full battery tests showed a peak power density of 220 mW cmâˆ’2 and good cyclability over 100 cycles (10 min discharge followed by 10 min charge). Likewise, Wang and coworkers reported an efficient electrocatalyst, made of Co@Co3O4@PPD coreâ€“bishell structure, obtained by a coherent process involving hydrothermal synthesis, high temperature calcination under nitrogen atmosphere and gentle heating in air.90 After heating, the Co@Co3O4 NPs were encapsulated by ultrathin and highly graphitized N-doped carbon and this catalyst showed good ORR activity. After integrating into the primary zincâ€“air battery, the battery delivered a stable voltage of 1.2 V over 40 h (at current density of 20 mA cmâˆ’2).

In addition to nonprecious metal/carbon catalysts, metal-free bifunctional catalysts are of great interest. A series of metal-free N-doped and N, P-co doped carbon electrocatalysts have been investigated for ORR and OER.5,91,105â€“107 Metal-free carbon catalysts derived from eggplant, bacterial cellulose and polymer precursors have been demonstrated for zincâ€“air batteries.108â€“110Fig. 4f shows a typical OER performance of A-EPC-900 (eggplant-derived microporous carbon) compared to GC and Pt/C.108 Unfortunately, the catalytic mechanism is still unclear, especially for the OER. To this extent, effort should be made to fully understand the catalytic mechanism in order to guide the fabrication of a new catalyst with sufficient active sites.91 By fabrication of polyaniline (PANi) aerogels and subsequent pyrolysis, Dai's group recently produced a 3D N, P co-doped mesoporous nanocarbon (NPMC) catalyst with a large surface area of 1663 m2 gâˆ’1 and showed good electrocatalytic performance over ORR and OER.111,112 The full battery exhibited a superior open circuit potential of 1.48 V, a specific capacity of 735 mA h gâˆ’1 and a peak power density of 55 mW cmâˆ’2 for primary batteries. The secondary battery could be operated over 180 cycles over a small current density. However, a deteriorating performance could be clearly observed from the long-term cycling test, due to the different ORR and OER performance of the same catalyst. Consequently, the authors constructed a three-electrode battery (as shown in Fig. 4d) to investigate this issue, where the ORR and OER took place at different electrodes. Fig. 4e revealed that this three-electrode rechargeable Znâ€“air battery had a longer stability of over 600 cycles (100 h). Later in 2016, a metal-free three-dimensional (3D) graphene nanoribbon network (N-GRW) doped with nitrogen (as shown in Fig. 4b) was reported from the same group, which showed better bifunctional electrocatalytic performance.91 They observed that the electron-donating quaternary N sites were responsible for the oxygen reduction reaction. Meanwhile, the unique 3D architecture provided a high density of active sites to facilitate the ORR and OER. Compared to their previous work, the full battery showed a higher specific capacity of 873 mA h gâˆ’1 and an improved peak power density of 65 mW cmâˆ’2.

Despite all the progress above, the long-term stability of an air electrode in general is still far from being commercialized. At practical current densities, the battery performance starts to degrade within few cycles. There are still plenty of challenges to develop high-performance bifunctional catalysts with low cost.

3.2. Electrolyte

Recent literature has demonstrated that ionic liquids can be used as electrolytes for zincâ€“air batteries due to their benefits such as no water leakage and dendrite-free anode during charge/discharge.113â€“116 However, the development of these non-alkaline electrolytes are still in the very early stages for their low conductivity and high viscosity. Commonly used electrolytes for zincâ€“air batteries are LiOH, NaOH, KOH and neutral NH4Cl. Though the near-neutral chloride electrolytes provide a safer and more robust alternative to traditional ones, KOH is still the most widely used electrolyte in zincâ€“air batteries due to its superior ionic conductivity, higher oxygen diffusion coefficiency and lower viscosity.26,117 In addition, the side reaction products of KOH with CO2 have a higher solubility than the sodium counterparts. This could therefore ease the carbonate precipitation problem for zincâ€“air batteries. One way to reduce the resistance of the electrolyte is to increase the concentration of KOH; however, the higher concentration of KOH can lead to an increased viscosity in the electrolyte. The higher concentration of the electrolyte can also lead to the formation of ZnO, which increases the viscosity indirectly and may cause dendritic formation.118 So, 6 M of KOH is widely used for its reasonable ionic conductivity and viscosity.

Because zincâ€“air batteries are operated in an open system, there are two major problems related to the electrolyte. One is carbonate precipitation caused by the atmospheric CO2.119,120 The alkaline solution can react with hydroxyl ions to form carbonate (CO32âˆ’ or HCO32âˆ’), which reduces the concentration of the OHâˆ’ in the electrolyte. This decreases the electrolyte conductivity. In addition, the formation of carbonate crystals clogs the porous air electrode. Eventually, the carbonate precipitation in alkaline electrolyte causes linear deterioration of the cell capacity and a shortened battery lifetime. Though physico-chemical adsorption could minimize the carbonate formation, the high cost of the materials and management hinders wider application. One simple solution is to change the electrolyte.38 Another is to study the CO2 management in the alkaline electrolyte. Drillet and coworkers also observed that the lifetime of the air electrode reduced from 2500 h to 270 h by increasing the CO2 concentration from zero to 1000 ppm.120 More recently, Krewer et al. set up a mathematical model to simulate the impact of a CO2 impurity, which gave useful predictions for practical zincâ€“air batteries.121 According to the equilibrium theory, the more CO32âˆ’ ions present in the electrolyte, the slower the carbonation reaction rate will be. Taking this fact into account, the same group presented other work by intentionally adding K2CO3 to the KOH electrolyte.122 While up to 28.6 mol% K2CO3 was added to a high molar KOH electrolyte, the battery performance remained stable with no obvious decrease in the practical energy density, cell potential and long-term stability was observed.

The second is the water leakage. As the existence of wet-proofing binder in the air electrode, water can indirectly leak to the environment in its gas phase. Gelling of the electrolyte is one possible solution to minimize water loss and enhance battery performance and life. A hydroponics gel was first introduced to immobilize the KOH electrolyte by Othman and coworkers as a gelling agent.123 In early 2016, Chen's group developed a nanoporous alkaline-exchange electrolyte membrane from natural cellulose nanofibres, exhibiting a high ionic conductivity of 21.2 mS cmâˆ’1 and 95.6% water retention as well as excellent bending flexibility.124 Except for the two major problems above, the oxygen bubbles generated from oxygen evolution are another minor problem. These bubbles involved in the electrolyte could reduce the conductivity of the electrolyte and adhere to the charging electrode surface.125 So oxygen bubble coalescence limits the capacity of the rechargeable zincâ€“air battery. Fast removal of these oxygen bubbles is of great importance to improve the charging performance. The clear mechanism of the bubble formation is still not clear, but it is closely related to the surface wettability of the electrode.126,127

Comparing the massive investigations on bifunctional catalysts in zincâ€“air batteries, electrolytes are not in the core of the research. However, electrolyte problems are not negligible, and it is necessary to develop all solid-state electrolytes for flexible devices, which display a promising future with no water leakage and carbonate precipitation.

3.3. Separator

As an electrochemically inactive component, the function of the separators is to physically set the zinc electrode and air electrode apart, with the capability of shuttling hydroxyl ions between the electrodes.34 Because of the direct impact on battery parameters, separators for the zincâ€“air battery must have sufficient chemical resistance in alkaline electrolytes, low ionic resistance and high electrical resistance. They should also have a high absorption capacity and appropriate porosity to sustain electrolytes in the pores. Moreover, the selective passage of hydroxyl ions other than zincate ions is another important feature for electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries.11,26

For zincâ€“air batteries, commonly used separators are made of polyethylene (PE), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), polyamide (PA), polyetherimide (PEI), polyacrylic acid (PAA) and polypropylene (PP).33,34,128â€“131 The fine porosity in these fibrous polymers offers good electrolyte retention and low ionic resistance. Laminated nonwoven materials such as CelgardÂ® 5550, with a PP/PE/PP trilayer structure, are typical separators for commercial zincâ€“air batteries.33 These separator membranes are often fabricated via a dry process and coated with surfactants, where PP is designed to maintain the integrity. The PE core is the smart layer that can shut down the battery once overheated. Saputra et al. reported a facile method to fabricate a 5 Î¼m inorganic microporous MCM-41 membrane. The zincâ€“air battery assembled with this MCM-41 separator in KOH electrolyte delivered a peak power density of 32 mW cmâˆ’2.131

However, the porous separators allow the crossover of soluble zincate ions through separator membranes to the air cathode, which significantly decreased the capacity and cycling efficiency of the battery.13,26,132 During the charge process, zincate ions should be located preferentially around the anode. However, the zincate ions are forced to migrate to the air electrode through the separator due to the concentration gradient.13 Meanwhile, the possible formation of ZnO could clog the porous air cathode. Thus, a promising anion exchange membrane has been introduced. Lee's group reported a composite ERC (electrospun nanofibre mat-reinforced permselective composite) membrane separator that was fabricated via impregnating PVA into electrospun PEI nanofibre mat.132 In this design, the PEI nanofiber worked as a framework to maintain dimensional stability and mechanical strength, and the PVA matrix provided permselective ion pathways. Comparing to the commercial CelgardÂ®3501, as shown in Fig. 5c and d, the permselectivity of hydroxyl ions over zincate ions was tripled to 2800 S min cmâˆ’3. Notably, the second discharge capacity of this membrane in a full cell was 213 mA h gâˆ’1, which was nearly 7 times higher than that of the CelgardÂ®3501. Shortly in 2016, the same group reported a new class of polymer blend electrolyte membranes to address this ion transport issue in Fig. 5a and b.133 This separator consisted of crosslinked PVA/PAA nanofibers as an anion conducting continuous phase and impregnated Nafion as an anion-repelling continuous phase. The crossover of zincate ions was suppressed by a continuous Nafion phase and the transportation of the hydroxyl ions was through the PVA/PAA nanofiber mat. Interestingly, a full cell integrated with this membrane separator showed a better rechargeability over 2500 min at 20 mA cmâˆ’2 and 10 min cycle period than that with conventional CelgardÂ®3501.

3.4. Anode materials

As mentioned in the introduction section, metallic zinc has already been used as an anode material for a long time because of the benefits such as high specific energy density, constant discharge voltage, low cost and environment friendliness.21 As pure zinc metal is used as an anode in zincâ€“air batteries, few studies have been focused on improving the zinc electrode. Generally, the surface morphology of zinc is of great importance in achieving a high reaction rate in an alkaline electrolyte. In addition to bulk zinc, powders and other large-surface-area zinc electrode materials, such as spheres, flakes and fibres, have also been explored.134â€“137 The large surface area of zinc structures are preferred for devices requiring large discharging currents. The scheme of the separator design and SEM image are shown in Fig. 5a and b. Bearing the advantages of good conductivity and stability, fine porosity and effective surface area, a zincâ€“air battery integrated with a fibrous zinc electrode showed a 40% increase in capacity and 50% more energy with large discharging currents.135 For the bulk zinc electrode, the parasitic reaction of Zn + 2H2O â†’ Zn(OH)2 + H2 is not a concern.

However, the increased surface area gives rise to a higher corrosion rate and leads to a decrease in the battery lifetime and utilization efficiency of zinc as well as safety issues.35 Early treatments were performed via a zinc-rich amalgam to reduce the hydrogen evolution. With the addition of mercury to 100 mg cmâˆ’2, corrosion of this zinc alloy was reduced to 40% in comparison to the untreated zinc.145 With the concerns of toxicity and environmental impact, zinc alloys with other metals (such as nickel and indium) have been pursued instead of mercury, lead and cadmium.146,147

For electrically rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries, the major challenge is the non-uniform dissolution and deposition of zinc during charge, as shown in Fig. 6.153 Though the electrochemical reversibility of zinc is good, the shape change and dendritic structure formed during the discharge and charge process severely affected the cycle life of the battery. In addition, as we stated in the Section 2, passivation problems caused by the formation of ZnO during OER also reduce the performance of the zinc anode after a few dischargeâ€“charge cycles.118 The challenges mentioned above limit the surface contact between zinc and the electrolyte and reduce the conductivity of the anode. The secondary battery cannot access 100 cycles in a reasonable cycle time and current densities with large overpotential from 0.8 to 1.5 V.101 At this point, the rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries are far behind other commercialized secondary batteries and primary zincâ€“air batteries.35

To improve the cyclic life, it is crucial to fully understand the formation mechanism of the dendritic morphology of electrodeposited zinc. Unfortunately, the exact mechanism and affecting factors are still not clear after 50 years' of study.138,139 To investigate this issue, a few physical models and simulation methods have been developed. Matsushita and coworkers applied the Wittenâ€“Sander-diffusion-limited-aggregation model to explain this issue and found that the fractal dimension of the dendrite increased linearly as the applied voltage rose after a critical voltage of Vc.139 Later, a thin-interface phase-field model for this electrochemical process was developed to study the effect of the applied voltage on the zinc morphology.140â€“142 Another nonlinear phase-field model was proposed by Chen and coworkers to simulate the microstructure evolution of non-equilibrium processes.143 Most recently, Wang and coworkers established a phase-field model in the COMSOL MultiphysicsÂ® software and simulated the formation process of the dendritic structure.138,144 They found that the morphology of electrodeposited zinc was dependent on the activation control (at early stage) and diffusion-limited control (at the late stage). Achieving a low activation overpotential with a pulsating current and electrolyte flow could be the solution to inhibit or retard the dendrite formation.

Meanwhile, great practical efforts have been made in the past decades. More recently, Liu and coworkers presented a work on preparing dendrite-free nanocrystalline Zn deposits from zinc triflate containing ionic liquid and nickel triflate additives, as shown in Fig. 7c and d.148 The formation of Znâ€“Ni alloy (Î·- and Î³-phases) on the surface played a key role in cycle life improvement. Alternatively, inorganic and polymer additives could also improve the corrosion resistance and cyclic life to some extent.149â€“152 Interestingly, Banik and Akolkar reported that the use of polyethylenimine (PEI, M.W. = 800), an electrolyte additive, inhibited the activation-controlled dendrite tip propagation in alkaline electrolytes, and the efficacy reached a maximum in excess of 50 ppm.153 In addition, the structure and surface modifications of the zinc anode offer an effective strategy for performance improvement.154â€“157 Gan et al. proposed a design of a zinc anode by merging ZnO nanoparticles into an interpenetrating polymer network for rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries.154 In this design, the well-fined 3D polymer network contained a polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) matrix and poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA) anion charge carriers. It showed a super stable discharge specific capacity of 317 mA h gâˆ’1 after 60 cycles at a current density of 50 mA cmâˆ’2. As shown in Fig. 7a, b and e, Parker and coworkers designed and fabricated a 3D zinc anode with rugged monoliths from emulsion-cast sponges of zinc powder, which exhibited a superior specific capacity of 740 mA h gâˆ’1 for primary batteries, low cell resistance of 0.75 Î© cmâˆ’2 after a 90% deep discharge and good cyclability over 80 hours.156 Another work from the same group demonstrated that a 3D zinc sponge architecture applied in a full cell could exhibit excellent cyclability under deep discharge for over 90 hours.157 Although the efforts made from both theory and practice provide significant directions on anode material design and reducing electrode shape change, none of these reported anode designs display excellent performance at all the relevant metrics. 3D sponge electrodes lack mechanical robustness, which would lead to shallow depths of discharge and short cyclic life. Meanwhile, the electrodes modified with discharge-trapping additives show relatively low capacity densities due to the large proportion of non-active additives. In addition, researchers may need to demonstrate their ideas for larger electrode thicknesses and longer testing periods for wide-ranging battery applications.

4. Flexible design of rechargeable zincâ€“air battery

As the fast development of flexible optical electronic devices in recent years, great attention has been raised in flexible and stretchable energy storage/conversion devices, which may be applied to wearable cell phones, human-like electronic skins and intelligent bracelets in the not far future.158â€“160 As a result, there is a high demand to develop power sources, such as batteries and supercapacitors, that share the properties of flexible, stretchable and even wearable.47,48,161 Considering the features, such as low cost, safety, relative high specific energy density and environmental friendliness, zincâ€“air batteries hold a good promise for portable and flexible applications.26,162 However, the conventional designs of zincâ€“air batteries are flat, rigid and stack-designs with aqueous alkaline electrolytes, which do not meet the requirements of portable and flexible applications. The bulk zinc electrode and classic 6 M KOH aqueous electrolyte, which are the most widely used, both need to be substituted by new designs.

As shown in Fig. 8, two types of flexible zincâ€“air batteries have been reported since 2015, fibre-shape and stack-shape zincâ€“air batteries, respectively.48,163,170 The currently reported flexile zincâ€“air batteries are summarized in Table 2 with their applied electrocatalysts, electrolyte and corresponding battery performance. Based on the cable-shape architecture applied to energy storage devices designed by Peng's group, Cho's group proposed the first all-solid-state cable-shape flexible zincâ€“air battery.49,164â€“169 This system was composed of a spiral zinc anode, free-standing gel polymer electrolyte (made from gelatin and 0.1 M KOH) and nonprecious Fe/N/C electrocatalyst loaded air cathode, as shown in Fig. 8a. In Fig. 9e, the effect of the Fe/N/C electrocatalyst was also studied. The flexible gel polymer electrolyte showed a good ionic conductivity of 3.1 mS cmâˆ’1, and the full cell loaded with the electrocatalyst presented a 0.92 V voltage plateau and 9 hour discharge duration. However, this battery cannot achieve the requirements of stretchable, wearable and rechargeable. Later, Xu and coworkers reported a fibre-shape flexible, stretchable and rechargeable battery, which was composed of a zinc string, hydrogel polymer electrolyte (made from 8.3 wt% PVA, 0.83 wt% PEO and 8.3 wt% KOH) and RuO2â€“carbon nanotube (CNT) electrocatalyst loaded air cathode.48 The scheme is shown in Fig. 8c. The cross-stacked and porous CNT sheets behaved as a gas diffusion layer and a current collector with good ORR catalytic properties. The cross-linked electrolyte showed excellent mechanical properties with a high ionic conductivity of 0.3 S cmâˆ’1. As shown in Fig. 9f, this fiber-shape battery displayed excellent discharge and charge performance at a high current density of 2 A gâˆ’1 for 20 cycles with superior flexibility and stretchability, which held great promise for power portable and wearable electronic devices.

Hilder and coworkers demonstrated a paper-based primary zincâ€“air battery via inkjet print method in 2009.159 However, the full battery performance is poor with a 0.8 V discharge potential at very small current densities. More recently in 2015, Chen's group proposed a new paradigm of an efficient flexible stack-shape rechargeable zincâ€“air battery (shown in Fig. 8b) via a facile solid state approach.162 This architecture consisted of a zinc film attached to a copper-foil substrate of 0.5 mm, poly(vinyl alcohol)-gelled electrolyte, LaNiO3 nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes (NCNT) bifunctional catalyst and a carbon cloth layer as the air cathode. Efforts should be made toward improving the electrolyteâ€“electrode interfacial properties on the way to replace liquid electrolytes by the versatile solid-state ones.

5. Conclusions and outlooks

In this review, we overviewed the design, components and working mechanisms of the rechargeable zincâ€“air battery and classified the hindrances into dendritic growth at the anode, lack of higher performance bifunctional catalyst loaded air electrode systems and electrolyte related problems. The recent developments of zincâ€“air batteries were highlighted, and the rechargeable and flexible trend was emphasized. Indeed, zincâ€“air batteries, as promising energy storage devices beyond Li-ion batteries, will play a key role in substituting old lead-acid and other high-pollution low-efficiency batteries. The performance of the battery largely depends on ORR and OER. On the one hand, the lack of high-efficiency, low-cost bifunctional catalysts severely effects the battery performance. Thus, bifunctional electrocatalysts, such as transition metal oxides, metal oxide/carbon and metal-free carbon materials, are under tremendous investigation. On the other hand, the formation of dendrites at the anode limits the surface contact between the zinc and the electrolyte and reduces the conductivity of the anode, which eventually decreases the battery cyclability. Solutions such as anode redesign, electrolyte additives and new electrolyte and separators are provided to minimize this effect. Now, it is possible to achieve a high peak power density of 350 mW cmâˆ’2 for primary zincâ€“air batteries at room temperature. The rechargeable batteries were stable over 1500 cycles (500 h) in lab tests.

Although the current research improves the performance of rechargeable zincâ€“air batteries, specific needs in materials science and engineering are still urgent. First of all, inexpensive bifunctional catalysts with high performance are still in further development. Metal-free carbon materials are a new direction for this research suggested by researchers. Secondly, new air cathode design is necessary to minimize the effect of carbon material oxidation. Direct growth of bifunctional catalysts on metallic foams or corrosion-resistant carbon may be new strategies. Meanwhile, a reversible zinc anode with a small shape change/dendrite formation during the chargeâ€“discharge cycles is needed to extend the battery lifetime. Methods such as surface modification and chemical additives have proven to be feasible solutions. In addition, battery design and electrolyte problems are not negligible. Three electrode design has been proposed as an effective way to increase the battery cycle life. Investigations on electrolytes (such as ionic liquid) and polymer separators should be paid more attention in future studies. Importantly, the solid-state electrolyte is of great importance for the flexible zincâ€“air battery, where efforts should be made toward improving the electrolyteâ€“electrode interfacial properties for better battery performance.

Overall, among different metalâ€“air batteries, zincâ€“air has become the most capable type to replace Li-ion batteries for electric-vehicle application. Moreover, recently developed all-solid-state zincâ€“air batteries hold a good promise for portable and flexible applications, such as human-like electronic skins and intelligent bracelets. Nevertheless, the electrically rechargeable batteries are still behind their primary batteries due to their relatively low rate capability and short cycle-life.
