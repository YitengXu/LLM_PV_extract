Abstract

Phenothiazine (PTZ) is one of the most extensively investigated S, N heterocyclic aromatic hydrocarbons due to its unique optical, electronic properties, flexibility of functionalization, low cost, and commercial availability. Hence, PTZ and its derivative materials have been attractive in various optoelectronic applications in the last few years. In this prospective, we have focused on the most significant characteristics of PTZ and highlighted how the structural modifications such as different electron donors or acceptors, length of the -conjugated system or spacers, polar or non-polar chains, and other functional groups influence the optoelectronic properties. This prospective provides a recent account of the advances in phenothiazine derivative materials as an active layer(s) for optoelectronic (viz. dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), perovskite solar cells (PSCs), organic solar cells (OSCs), organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), organic field-effect transistor (OFETs), chemosensing, nonlinear optical materials (NLOs), and supramolecular self-assembly applications. Finally, future prospects are discussed based on the structureproperty relationship in PTZ-derivative materials. This overview will pave the way for researchers to design and develop new PTZ-functionalized structures and use them for various organic optoelectronic applications.

1. Introduction

Optoelectronics is one of the fast-growing technologies that deal with applying electronic devices to the sourcing, detection, and control of light.14 This technology plays a critical role in survival and innovation in order to meet the electronic demands of the current generation.5,6 To transform light into an electrical signal or vice versa, optoelectronic devices depend on light matter interactions and the electronic properties of matter.7,8 These devices are used for numerous purposes such as telecommunications,9 military10 bio-medicals,11 microwave photonics,12 sensing,13 and other industries.1416 To create better optoelectronic devices, there has always been a push to enhance lightmatter interactions in semiconductor materials. In this context, better light-harvesting and good electron-carriage capable semiconducting material are well-suited for optoelectronics.17,18 In this regard, organic and inorganic materials have received significant attention due to their electronic as well as optical properties.19,20 However, in the past 25 years, there has been a surge of interest in the area of organic optoelectronics due to significant improvements in the material design, purification, cost, and room temperature solution processable deposition on large area or flexible substrates that have led to a significant increase in the material performance.21,22 In this aspect, many organic small molecules and polymers have been developed with various configurations such as DD, DD, DA, AA, and ADA.2326 These patterns are made using various electron donors (triphenylamine, triazines, carbazole, phenothiazine, phenoxazine, benzo-dithiophene, etc.),27,28 acceptors (diketopyrrolopyrrol, perylenediimide, naphthalimide, isoindigo, etc.),2931 spacers, (ethynyl, ethylene, phenyl, thiophene, furan, and porphyrin, etc.),3234 side chains (alkyl, alkoxy ethers, crown ethers, etc.),32,35,36 and anchoring groups (CN, COOH, NO2, and other withdrawing groups).37,38 In fact, the aforementioned groups influence the physical, optical, electrical, electrochemical, and topological properties, changing the overall performance of the organic material as a result.28,35,36 Hence, chemical structure or pattern is a crucial factor in designing new organic materials for respective optoelectronic applications such as organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), nonlinear optics (NLO), organic field-effect transistors (OFETs), bulk heterojunction solar cells (BHJ), dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), organic solar cells (OSCs), perovskite solar cells (PSCs), molecular motors, batteries, biomedical, and sensors.23,39 In all these applications, the central core or donor strongly impacts their optoelectronic properties. In these aspects, S, N heterocyclic molecule, namely, phenothiazine (PTZ), is a well-known building block, which was firstly prepared from the reaction of diphenylamine with sulfur by Bernthsen40 in 1883.

Later on, many synthetic strategies were developed to build PTZ and its motifs with inexpensive starting materials for desired low-cost PTZ functionalized materials.41 In addition, PTZ has the following distinct structural characteristics, making it a well-known building block for optoelectronic applications.

1.1 Shape

The molecular shape has major impacts on the thermal stability, morphology of the film, packing, crystallinity, intermolecular interactions,23,39etc.; as a result, huge variation can be observed in their optoelectronic properties. Phenothiazine is a butterfly or bent-type non-planar conformation42 (see Fig. 1 side view) and these types of structural features hamper the formation of ground state aggregates and excimers. For instance, this kind of non-planar structure decreases the charge recombination and improves the DSSC device efficiency.43

1.2 Cost

Phenothiazine donor is a commercially available low-cost (0.07 $ per g) precursor when compared to other donor materials; triphenylamine (1.8 $ per g), carbazole (0.4 $ per g), phenoxazine (30 $ per g), indole (0.5 $ per g), benzo-dithiophene (187 $ per g).

1.3 Versatility of the chemical structure

Due to the simplicity of their chemistry, transparency, lightweight, etc., the design of organic materials by the respective applications is simple compared to inorganic materials.44,45 However, phenothiazine (PTZ) is an S, N heterotricyclic organic scaffold having multiple functional sites such as C-2, C-3, C-7, C-8, N10, and sulphur of the thiazine ring. Usually, the C3 and C7 positions are decorated with either electron-donating or electron-withdrawing groups and/or one electron-donating and one electron-withdrawing group; this design is based on its application, for example, D-PTZ-A and D-PTZ-D type chemical structures are most common in light-absorbing and hole-transporting materials in DSSC and PSCs, respectively.43,46 DFT calculated HOMO and LUMO energy levels of PTZ are illustrated in Fig. 1 and their values are 5.24 and 0.68 eV, respectively, which are in good agreement with the experimental cyclic voltammetry results HOMO: 5.2 eV, LUMO: 1.6 eV).47 It is easy to tune the required energy levels for various optoelectronic devices by varying the substitutions on phenothiazine. Also, the N-10 active site of the PTZ core can be decorated with suitable solubilizing groups such as alkyl, oligo, aryl, or aralkyl to achieve the desired solubility in organic solvents and solution-processing properties. Furthermore, the PTZ core is easily oxidizable, especially at S, which transforms into sulphide, sulfoxide, or sulfone and can clearly indicate the loss of electrons from the yellow color region of the ESP maps23,39 (see Fig. 1). These chemical changes on PTZ generate a plethora of derivatives with tuneable optoelectronic properties.

With these preliminary features of PTZ, several functional materials can be produced that can be used in a huge number of applications. However, in this prospective, we have focused on a recent account of the development of PTZ-conjugated functional materials and structureproperty relationships for active optoelectronic applications (including brief working principle). In particular, we focused on light-harvesting materials for DSSC and OPV, hole transporting materials for PSC, light-emitting materials for OLEDs, and hazardous material detection problems for sensing applications, also providing a brief overview of PTZ materials in OFET, NLO, and self-assembly applications. Finally, we discuss the most recent ideas for potential research directions in this area, as well as how the most promising molecular PTZ strategies can be tweaked to boost the performance of optoelectronic devices. In this prospective, we have indicated the codes PTZn (n = 1, 2, 3, ) for phenothiazine-based materials and NPTZn (n = 1, 2, 3, ) non-phenothiazine-based materials (only provided the photophysical data and not the structures).

2. Optoelectronic applications

2.1 Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs)

A DSSC is a complex system for light-to-electricity conversion, as with all solar cells. Fig. 2 demonstrates the basic components of the DSSC along with its operation mechanism. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are cheap, cost-effective, high-performance thin-film solar cells that operate on the basis of a semiconductor and an electrolyte.48 DSSC is an assembly of a working electrode (soaked with a dye) and a counter electrode (soaked with a thin layer of electrolyte) sealed with the help of a hot melt tape to prevent the leakage of the electrolyte. When exposed to sunlight, the dye sensitizer gets excited, from which an electron is injected into the conduction band (CB) of the mesoporous oxide. These generated electrons diffuse to the back contacts. Through the external circuit, the electrons reach the counter electrode, which enables the direct reduction of the electrolyte itself (I/I3) and subsequently reduces the oxidized dye molecules to their initial state.49,50
Fig. 2 Typical DSSC component illustration and the working principle.

However, the dye or the sensitizer is one of the essential components of the DSSC, which is responsible for the maximum absorption of incident light, resulting in better electrical current output to make an efficient DSSC. The general requirements for an ideal sensitizer are:51 (i) peripheral acid anchoring groups that allow firm adhesion to the surfaces of the semiconductor; (ii) the HOMO, which is far from the surface of CB of TiO2 as well as below the redox couple's energy level, to promote dye regeneration; (iii) high photo, thermal, and electrochemical stability; (iv) should be solution processable; (v) easy to synthesize and purify; (vi) should be low-cost and non-toxic, i.e., eco-friendly; (vii) hydrophobic in nature; (viii) to achieve effective solar photon harvesting, it should cover a wide portion of the visible spectrum and reach up to the NIR range with high molar extinction coefficients. Currently, dye sensitized solar panels transform 14% of the sunlight that hits them into electricity.52 Researchers are attempting to increase the efficiency by maintaining the above characteristics of the dye by its design and make it comparable to silicon-based solar panels. So far, inorganic ruthenium (Ru), metal-free organic, quantum-dot, zinc (Zn)porphyrin, perovskites, and natural dyes are mainly used as the sensitizers due to their photovoltaic properties.43,53,54 Among them, Znporphyrin and metal-free organic sensitizers have proved their track record in terms of the efficiency and durability. Notably, phenothiazine-based metal-free and porphyrin sensitizers have drawn attention due to their distinct features such as strong electron donor, inexpensive, eco-friendly, non-planar butterfly structure, and simply tunable structures. Though the basic structure of sensitizers, i.e., DA (donoracceptor) has been established, there is a continuous effort and substantial progress has been made in new design architectures, for example, DAA, DDA, DAA, D(A)n, and (DA)n, for efficient DSSC.55,56 Jia et al., developed three (PTZ1, PTZ2, and PTZ3) stereoscopic DAA based phenothiazine dyes, with phenothiazine as the electron donor; spirobifluorene and octyl chains on phenothiazine were utilized to lessen the aggregation and suppress the charge recombination; benzothiadiazole unit (BTD) was utilized to expand the spectral responsiveness of these dyes; phenyl as well as thiophene were used as spacers and different anchoring groups, i.e., carboxylic acid in PTZ1, cyanoacrylic acid in PTZ2, and pyridine in PTZ3 dyes were prepared for the utilization in DSSC.57 This design strategy examined the optical, electrochemical, and photovoltaic performance of these dyes to scrutinize the influence of the above anchoring units. This study claims that cyanoacrylic acid group, followed by the carboxylic acid group, are efficient anchors to TiO2 while pyridine is a poor anchor to TiO2; as a result, PTZ2 shows good PCE of 7.34% than PTZ1 (6.69%) and PTZ3 (3.05%). Unlike, the above strategy, Nagarajan et al., introduced another type of structural architecture, viz., ADA, where an auxiliary-acceptor (naphthalimide) is in one terminal and cyanoacrylic acid anchor in another terminal of the C3 and C7 position of the donor N-hexylphenothiazine.58 They have prepared two dyes by changing the changed alkyl chain length, i.e., ethyl (PTZ4) and propyl (PTZ5) on the nitrogen atom of naphthalimide, as shown in Chart 1. As a result, an improved JSC from 10.29mA cm2 (propyl) to 12.21mA cm2 (ethyl) was observed and efficiencies of 4.91% and 4.57%, respectively, were obtained. In another study, they used an ethynyl-pyrene unit at C-7 and cyanoacrylic acid or rhodanine-3-acetic acid anchoring units at the C-3 position of the C6 or C8 alkyl phenothiazine (PTZ611).59 Consequently, extending the conjugation results in enhanced absorbing ability and improved photovoltaic performance. Among them, PTZ7-based DSSCs exhibited high PCE of 7.45% and 12.10% in the absence and presence of the CDCA co-adsorbent, respectively (see Table 1).

2.2 Perovskite solar cells (PSCs)

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are a new paradigm for efficiently capturing solar energy. Within the span of 10 years, the solar conversion efficiency was increased from 3.8% to 25.5%.70,71 The operation mechanism of a PSC depends on the device configuration. Although it was initially proposed to be similar to a DSSC cell where a single dye layer is absorbed on the mp-TiO2, in a PSC, the perovskite is usually filled into the porous TiO2. As a result, electrons can be transported via both the perovskite layer and the mp-TiO2 layer.72 The schematic picture of the working mechanism and the charge transfer processes of a normal planar PSC is demonstrated in Fig. 5. The common PSC device architecture consists of 5 components:26 (i) transparent electrode, (ii) Electron Transporting Material (ETM), (iii) light-active perovskite, (iv) Hole Transporting Material (HTM), and (v) metallic electrode. However, ambipolar behavior perovskites led to the development of new configurations and materials for PSCs. In this sense, perovskites could be used cumulatively as light absorber, electron conductor, and hole conductor. Therefore, the PSC architecture can be classified as either regular (nip) or inverted (pin) configurations, based on the properties of the first material encountered by the incident light, or the first deposited material (HTM or ETM) on FTO/ITO, which is adjacent to the perovskite layer.

Many research groups have been focused on improving the efficiency by either changing the hole or electron transporting materials, modifying the light harvesters, or using the latest deposition techniques. In fact, the hole transporting material (HTM) is one of the main components in the PSC since it is not only involved in charge transport but also protects the perovskite. The widely used HTM in PSC is spiro-OMeTAD and the device efficiency has crossed 25%. However, a major hurdle for spiro-OMeTAD is its high expense owing to tedious synthetic protocols, and its thermal instability. Hence, the development of an ideal HTM is required for further improvement in efficient and stable perovskite solar cells, having the following properties:73 (i) suitable HOMOLUMO energy levels; (ii) high hole mobility/conductivity; (iii) capable of smooth film formation; (iv) should be solution processable; (v) easy to synthesize and purify; (vi) should be low-cost and non-toxic, i.e., eco-friendly; (vii) hydrophobic in nature; (viii) chemically and thermally stable.

In recent years, PTZ can be used as a substitution or central core for the molecular engineering of efficient HTMs.46 Also, S,N-based heterocycles structure serving as molecular locks to network strongly with under-coordinated Pb2+ ions were recently reported to be appropriate additives for efficient PSCs.74 Versatile structural modification of phenothiazine directs the development of various topological structure-based hole transport materials,26viz., DD, DA, DAD, DDA, DADA, DAD, DADAD, and ADDA, where, D-donor, A-acceptor, and -electron conjugation spacer. Zhao et al., introduced three HTMs with the DAD structure, in which dithienopyrrolobenzothiadiazole acts as an acceptor and phenothiazine (PTZ42, see Chart 4) or triarylamines (NPTZ1 & NPTZ2) are the donors.75 As expected, the phenothiazine unit holds its planarity, the alkyl groups present on PTZ improve the interaction, and PbS bonding occurs, leading to a device efficiency for PTZ42 of 14.2%. Under similar conditions, the PCE was 7.0%, 10.2%, and 14.9% for NPTZ1, NPTZ2, and spiro-OMeTAD, respectively. As per earlier discussions, another possibility of functionalization on the PTZ unit is the conversion of an electron-donating sulfur atom to an electron-withdrawing sulfone. With this concept, Cheng et al., introduced two new phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide (PDO)-based inexpensive HTMs, PTZ43 (62.1791 $ per g)) and PTZ44 (61.2761 $ per g) (see Chart 4). In this design, they varied the N-position of POD with anisole and 4,4-dimethoxytriphenylamine but retained the 4,4-dimethoxydiphenylamine as the side arms (3- and 7-positions); consequently, the resulting molecules possess a donoracceptordonor (DAD) structure.76 With the introduction of sulfuryl, particularly appropriate energy level alignment and excellent charge carrier mobility were obtained. The device was fabricated with the device structure of FTO/c-TiO2/SnO2/P(FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbBr3)0.15/HTM/Au), where PTZ43 and PTZ44 were used as the HTMs. The observed PCEs were 16.7%, 20.2%, and 19.8% for the PTZ43, PTZ44, and spiro-OMeTAD, respectively. A high PCE of the PTZ44-based PSC is ascribed to the more planar geometry of the PDO core group as well as much stronger molecular stacking behavior. Device stability tests of the respective PSCs were performed (relative humidity of 3045%, rt) up to 480 h aging without encapsulation; as a result, PTZ43, PTZ44, and spiro-OMeTAD based PSCs sustained 84.6%, 84.7%, and 80.1% of the initial PCEs, respectively.

Regrettably, the dopants used and the related oxidation reactions have been shown to severely degrade the long-term stability of the device.77 Thus, PTZ-based dopant-free PSC has drawn great attention. Very recently, Tsang et al. introduced two new DD HTMs, coded as PTZ45 and NPTZ3, which consist of 4,8-di(hexylthio)-benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b]dithiophene (BDT) as the -conjugated unit with N-(6-bromohexyl) phenothiazine (PTZ) and N-(6-bromohexyl)phenoxazine (POZ) as the donor side arms, respectively.78 They were used to make the dopant-free inverted MAPbI3 devices using (PTZ45) as the HTM, exhibiting a PCE of 18.26%; that of NPTZ3 was even higher, reaching 19.16%. These results further demonstrate the potential of the inexpensive NPTZ3 ($44.57) and PTZ45 ($59.22) as promising HTMs in PSCs. On the other hand, our group reported the new phenothiazine appended porphyrin macrocyclic based HTMs with and without the dopant PSC. With the DD pattern, N-octyl phenothiazine as the donor, and octyl alkoxy porphyrin as the -conjugated linker PTZ46 (free-base) and PTZ47 (Zn) by varying the metal centers. Screen-printing processed device fabrication of dopant-free PTZ46 and PTZ47 HTMs gave a higher Jsc of 19.66 mA cm2, Voc of 1.00 V, and PCE of 11.26% when compared to PTZ46-based PSC (Jsc of 16.59 mA cm2, Voc of 0.954 V, and PCE of 9.68%).79 This was the best reported PCE at the time in a porphyrin hole-transport layer (HTL)-based PSCs using a carbongraphene composite as the cathode. Later on, Cu metal was introduced into the same system and these molecular systems were introduced into pre-made PSC-without a HTM by a simple infiltration process via the front contact layer. Among them, PTZ48 PSC displayed the highest PCE of 11.76% without dopant and 12.88% with dopants when compared to other HTMs (PTZ46, PTZ47).80

Finally, the modified devices displayed an improved PCE of 15.50% as compared to that of the control device (14.38%). This study points to the synergetic effect of PTZ on the growth and passivating defects for obtaining efficient PSCs.

2.3 Organic photovoltaic cells (OPVs)

An organic photovoltaic cell (OPV) is one of the most promising photovoltaic revolutions to increase the performance and resilience of solar cells owing not just to its low expense, solution-processing, lightweight, stability, etc., but also to a dramatic improvement in its certified efficiency (18.2%).83 A standard OPV system consists of one or more photoactive materials sandwiched between two electrodes (see Fig. 7) and generates photocurrent by the following process.84,85 (i) Light absorption: photoactive materials absorb sunlight and form the excitons, in which the electrons are excited from the valence band/HOMO into the conduction band/LUMO; (ii) exciton diffusion: excitons diffuse to the donor/acceptor interface due to the concentration gradient; (iii) charge separation: separate into free holes and electrons; (iv) charge extraction: holes and electrons travel to the corresponding electrodes by following either donor or acceptor phase; as a result photocurrent is produced.86

Typical OPVs comprise of p-type (polymer or small molecule donors) and n-type (fullerene and non-fullerene acceptors) semiconductors, blended in a bulk heterojunction (BHJ) configuration.87 D/A heterojunctions can be created with two main types of architectures, namely, bilayer heterojunction and bulk heterojunction (BHJ). In fact, the first OSC reported by Tang in 1986 with a bilayer structure had copper phthalocyanine as the donor (D) and perylene derivative as the acceptor (A); as a result, the device exhibited a PCE of 0.95% under simulated AM2 illumination of 75 mW cm2.88 From then, the efficiency OPVs has been improved considerably in the past 18 years, from 4% in 2002 to 18% by 2020.89 For this achievement of OPVs, considerable contribution was demonstrated by PTZ-based materials. As discussed in Section 1, the distinct features of PTZ make for the design of its derivatives either as p-type or n-type materials in OSCs. Polymers have various advantages such as they form quality films, have smooth morphology, and can easily be used to make large-area flexible solar panels. Various organic chromophore-functionalized polymeric materials have been used as electron donors to achieve a high PCE (14%) of OSCs. PTZ-based polymers were used as a donor (for the first time in the year 2006) in BHJ solar cells and reported by Shim and co-workers. They developed a series of PTZ-copolymers PF-PZBx (x = 10, 30, 40, 50) comprising fluorene and cyanovinylene-functionalized hexylphenothiazine materials as the electron donors.90 By the combination of a blend of PTZ54 and the fullerene derivative [6,6]-phenyl C61 butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM), the D/A system showed a PCEs up to 0.53%. Alternatively, small molecules have earned considerably attention in the past few years due to their well-defined molecular structure, definite molecular weight, facile tunability, easy purification, better synthetic reproducibility, excellent light harvesting properties, and high molar extinction coefficients. Recently, Revoju et al. reported three small molecules PTZ56, PTZ57, and PTZ58 as electron donors with PTZ as the core building block connected to benzothiadiazole and the 3-ethylrhodanine acceptor group via the thiophene -spacer92 (see Chart 6). 2.4 Organic field-effect transistor (OFET)

In the year 1930, the phenomenon of a field-effect transistor (FET) was first proposed and received a patent by Julius Edgar. Later, Tsumura et al., reported an organic thiophene polymer-based FETs, also known as an organic field-effect transistor (OFET).99 An OFET is a three-electrode switching system controlled by voltage. The device consists of an organic semiconductor, an insulator between the semiconductor and gate electrode, and three terminals (source, drain, and gate).100 The general device architecture is shown in Fig. 10 and functions as follows. When a gate voltage is pulsed, the carriers assemble near the dielectric layer or organic layer interface and form a conductive channel. From the source electrode, carriers are then injected into the organic layer and transport takes place via a conductive channel to the drain electrode.
Fig. 10 General schematic device architecture of the OFET.

OFETs are an integral element of organic electronics for the next generation. They are dynamic components in display technology, wearable electronics, smart bandages, electronic papers, radio frequency identification tags (RFIDs), and various sensing applications. Both p-and n-type OFETs are crucial for the application of organic electronics. In this context, most of the high mobility polymers and organic small molecular DA systems were reported, where, donors (D): phenothiazine, carbazole, etc., and acceptors (A): naphthalenediimide (NDI), naphthalene monoimide (NDI), perylene diimide (PDI) diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP), isoindigo (IID), etc.101 However, many factors such as the HOMO/LUMO energy level, packing mode on the film state, size/molecular weight, impurities of organic semiconductors including device configuration, morphology, and interface influence the OFET properties; however, there is no doubt that the key factor is the semiconductor itself.

Kim et al. reported a series of p-type semiconductors based on phenothiazylene vinylene and bithiophene copolymers (PTZ70, PTZ71, and PTZ72) for organic thin-film transistors (see Chart 8).102 A readily accessible dialkoxy-substituted phenylene ring was inserted into the phenothiazine units to improve the solubility of the polymers. These polymers showed outstanding thermal stability (see Table 4) and all showed decomposition temperatures above 400 C. Two strong absorption peaks in the wavelength ranges of 306325 nm from the phenothiazine unit and 430480 nm from the transition were observed in both the solution and thin film spectra. The optimized field-effect mobilities for PTZ70, PTZ71, and PTZ72 were calculated to be 1.8 104, 5.7 104, and 2.5 107 cm2 V1 s1 with the equivalent on/off ratios of 5 102, 1 104, and 5 102, respectively; the PTZ72-based device showed the lowest carrier mobility when compared to that of PTZ70 and PTZ71, which may be attributed to the twisting between the 3,31-position of the dialkylated thiophene rings. In continuation, the same group developed two new PTZ-based organic semiconductors by introducing the benzothiadiazole auxiliary acceptor between the two thiophene rings in the above PTZ71 and PTZ72 polymers to get low-band-gap polymers (PTZ73 and PTZ74), respectively.103 The FET devices were processed in solution and made a bottom contact configuration for the above polymers. PTZ73 and PTZ74 showed excellent hole mobilities of 7.5 105 and 1.0 104 cm2 V1 s1, respectively, with on/off ratios of about 104. 2.5 Organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs)

Since the groundbreaking research of Tang and Van Slyke in 1987, organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) have received increasing attention due to their incredible applications in display and lighting technologies.106 An additional benefit of OLEDs lies in bendable and adaptable (flexible) display applications, which is crucial for the recent transformation in the paradigm toward flexible electronics. However, it is necessary to further improve the durability of the large area displays to maintains quality images. OLEDs can be divided into three sets:107 (i) fluorescent OLEDs (FLOLED), (ii) phosphorescent OLEDs (PHOLED), and (iii) thermally activated delayed fluorescence OLEDs (TADF-OLEDs). Usually, all these devices are assembled from the following layers: cathode, electron injection layer (EIL), electron transport layer (ETL), emission layer (EML), hole transport layer (HTL), hole injection layer (HIL), and anode. As shown in the schematic representation of the OLED device (Fig. 12), where voltage is applied to the OLED electrodes, holes are injected and transported from the anode, whereas electrons are injected and transported from the cathode. Excitons (hole and electron pairs) are combined in the emitting layer (EML) and those produced at this time are changed to ground state and generate light.108

In reality, for effective and long-life OLEDs, the molecular structure, solubility, thermal stability, morphology, energy levels, and good transport properties of each layer are of a major concern.109 In this regard, PTZ derivatives have greater interest due to their strong emissive (cover almost all the CIE color space), hole, and electron transport properties. (ii) Phosphorescent OLEDs (PHOLED). In commercialized OLEDs, phosphorescent emitters have been commonly used because they can achieve nearly 100% internal quantum efficiency (IQE) by utilizing all the triplet and singlet excitons.114 In particular, phosphorescent OLEDs (PhOLEDs) are superior and have been used extensively in mobile phones. It is now widely believed that PhOLEDs, possessing transition metal compounds as emitters, have drawn much interest since they can maintain high efficiency of light emission by harvesting all singlet and triplet excitons. However, despite the massive development made so far, PhOLEDs still face some challenges and issues, such as their low efficiency, short lifetime, high-quality blue emission light, complicated manufacturing, and quest for improved photo-functional OLED materials.115 The choice of the right host is the key to achieving high-performance PhOLEDs as hosts are responsible for balancing the transfer of charges and containment of hole-electron pairs within the emitting layer (EML) (Fig. 14). As of now, three types of hosts are considered to realize efficient PhOLEDs, including monopolar hosts (mCP and CBP), bipolar hosts (carbazole, benzimidazole, phenothiazine,), and co-hosts. Recently, the third generation of organic electroluminescent materials, TADF materials, have many advantages compared to other OLED materials, such as 100% internal quantum efficiency, no heavy metal doping, and avoiding the scarcity of ordinary fluorescent and phosphorescent materials.123 PTZ is usually used as an electron donor and it possesses low EST values between the triplet (T1) and singlet (S1). In 2019, Chen and his co-workers introduced three TADF emitters, where, 1,8-naphthyridine (ND) was flanked between two units of dimethylacridan (NPTZ10), phenothiazine (PTZ99), and phenoxazine (NPTZ11).124 Among these, PTZ99 exhibits fluorescence and phosphorescence at 548 nm and 567 nm, respectively. 2.6 Chemosensing application

Anions, cations, and heavy metal ions widely spread in our environments play a crucial role in our daily physiological life.129,130 These are the most life-threatening species if they exceed their biological limits in the body, which could cause serious human health and environmental damage. Hence, the fast and efficient detection of various hazardous ions in drinking water and environments is very important to provide guidance for assessing hazard and pollution management. For this, it is necessary to design and develop efficient sensing probes with good selectivity and sensitivity, which has become the desired work in sensing chemistry. However, many types of sensors and detectors (electrical, optoelectrical, or electronic) devices are available for detecting the physical presence of flame, metals, leaks, or gas and chemicals, etc. Among them, chemosensing is a popular technique, in which molecules or sensing probes are primarily planned and developed based on the principles of complementarity, co-operation, and pre-organization.131 The general strategies for the development of chemosensors (see Fig. 15) are the coupling of the signaling unit and a recognition unit either directly or via suitable spacer units. During the last few years, many phenothiazine-sensing probes have been developed for the detection of various hazardous materials due to their low-cost and ease of chemical modification.

In the PTZ molecule, more electron density is concentrated at the S group and it can be easily oxidized as sulfoxide and sulfone type PTZ structures. Recently, Soni and co-workers developed a photo-induced electron transfer-based PTZ-BODIPY donoracceptor (DA) dyad132PTZ104 and used it as a selective probe for the detection of hypochlorite (HOCl) over other ROS under simulated physiological conditions. In this PTZ104, PTZ acts as an electron donor as well as a HOCl reactive group, while BODIPY acts as an electron acceptor as well as a signaling unit. Upon the excitation of the BODIPY unit, the emission of PTZ104 was quenched (90%) owing to photo-induced electron transfer from PTZ to 1BODIPY*. Though, by the addition of NaOCl, the fluorescence of PTZ104 was enhanced, suggesting inhibition of the reductive PET mechanism from PTZ to BODIPY, which was rapidly bring back upon treatment with H2S, suggesting the reversibility of the probe for the continuous detection of hypochlorite (LOD of 0.72 mM). In this design, branched ethylene glycol chains on the PTZ unit and carboxylic acid functional units on the BODIPY unit enhanced its solubility in water.133 In the existence of different competing ROS, cations, and anions, the probe was examined by tracking the fluorescence and it was found that PTZ105 selectively detects OCl with an LOD of 26.7 nM. The PTZ108 chemosensor overcomes the limitations of the sensors tested so far and has been used to image Hg2+ in environmental samples and biological systems, rather than the identification of a single environment. Interestingly, PTZ108 was used in real water samples and living cells to detect the presence of Hg2+. So far, various PTZ probes have been developed due to their inexpensive and facile structural modification. Because of the strong donating ability of phenothiazine, a space for new PTZ-based probes can be created for the naked-eye detection and imaging of various toxic analytes by the ICT mechanism.

2.7 Non-linear optics (NLO)

Non-linear optics (NLO) is one of the sub-disciplines of contemporary optics and these studies involve a variety of non-linear effects that occur in lightmatter interactions. NLO is extensively used in various applications and fields such as laser technology, optical computing, optical switching, image processing, information processing, and data storage.138 During the past few years, organic materials have displayed attractive NLO properties and have been widely studied since their several advantages due to their low cost, low toxicity, ease of solution processability, flexibility in their structural modification, and flexibility for device fabrication.139 Moreover, the recent advances in the development of organic -conjugated systems with excellent NLO properties arise due to the ease of chemical synthesis, fine-tuning of the NLO susceptibilities/coefficients flexibility, and solution processability. Even then, we still need to overcome some obstacles, such as energy efficiency, optical loss, bandwidth, drive voltage, and signal distortion associated with active photonic devices. To date, the study of NLO organic -conjugated chromophores (phenothiazine, carbazole, triphenylamines, corroles, porphyrins, phthalocyanines, organicmetal hybrid systems, MOFs, etc.) has made incredible development for their potential applications in photonic devices.140 However, PTZ-based -conjugated organic compounds have been of great interest since the 1990s because of their low ionization potentials and ease of modulating their optical, NLO susceptibilities/coefficients, and electronic and chemical properties by tuning their molecular structure.141

Chandrasekharan and his group have extensively worked on phenothiazinemetal nanocomposites/nanoparticles. In 2017, they synthesized PTZAu nanocomposite (NC) and studied their NLO properties using the single-beam Z-scan technique.142 PTZ made a substantial enrichment (67 times) in non-linear optical absorption (NLA) in the PTZAu composite when compared to the constituent units (Au-NPs) and (PTZ). The improvement was credited to both the local field effect and electron/energy transfer. From the Z-scan analysis (see Fig. 18(a) and (b)), it was observed that the cause for NLA and optical limitation (OL) of PTZ was TPA, while that of the NC was excited state absorption (ESA) along with TPA. In the development of Au-NPs, PTZ plays a significant role as a reducing and stabilizing agent. Energy transfer through the overlap of the PTZ emission spectra and Au NP absorption spectra plus the interaction of Au NPs between PTZ and surface plasmon resonance (SPR) resulted in a good delocalization of the charge between them and hence the system's dipole moment was greatly enhanced, as demonstrated in Fig. 18(b) and (c). This results in a substantial increase in the NLO properties of 8.7 1012 esu for PTZAu NPs relative to that of pure Au-NPs (7.1 1012 esu) and PTZ (3.1 1012 esu), respectively, and boost in the optical limiting (2.59 J cm2) action of the PTZAu NPs at an input intensity of 0.82 GW cm2 compared to that of pure Au NPs and PTZ. In continuation, another PTZAg hybrid system was prepared via a pulsed laser ablation technique (photoionization of PTZ and the ablation of the Ag).143 The substantial improvement in the NLO properties of 14.2 1012 esu for PTZAg relative to pure Ag (4.7 1012 esu) and PTZ (3.1 1012 esu), respectively, and boost in the optical limiting (3.43 J cm2) action of PTZAg at an input intensity of 0.83 GW cm2 related to pure Au NPs and PTZ, respectively, were observed. The observed enhancement (even related to PTZAuNC) was explained by the strong charge transfer between PTZ and Ag NPs and the local field effect induced due to the SPR of Ag NPs. Recently, Zhu et al., developed two S,N-co-doped carbon quantum dots (CQDs) from PTZ and NLO/optical power limiting (OPL) properties studied using the Z-scan technique at 532 nm, taking C60 as the standard ref. 144. 2.8 Self-assembly

Self-assembly is an essential process in nature and is important in the fields of material science, biology, and chemistry.149 Molecular self-assembly can be defined as the spontaneous organization of molecular building blocks into ordered structures due to non-covalent interactions.150 Self-assembly in the aspect of materials indicates that constituents are designed to create a functional ordered structure with minimal support from humans or machines. This process is observed on any scale ranging from nano- and micrometer scale to macroscopic dimensions.151 These simple structures are formed using hydrogen bonding, stacking, metalligand interactions, electrostatic forces, strong dipoledipole association, hydrophobic forces, and steric repulsions. As a result, a plethora of supramolecular assemblies such as gels and liquid crystals, several soft nanoribbons, nanotubes, nanorods, and nanowires of low dimensionality of 1D, 2D, 3D have been obtained.152 These nanostructures can enhance the charge transfer efficiency when incorporated into organic electronic devices and find a wide range of organo-optoelectronic applications.

Prasad and his group studied the self-assembly of phenothiazine-based oligo(p-phenylenevinylene) (PTZ110115) building blocks by changing various substitutions153 (see Chart 13). All these compounds exhibit significant solvatochromism, in particular, non-boron molecules show higher PL shift than those comprising the boron unit owing to the presence of sterically-crowded substituents (BMes2). Moreover, through self-assembly via hydrophobic interactions of the long hexadecyl chains and -interactions of their aromatic counterparts, PTZ113 underwent gelation and showed hierarchically ordered 1D fibers in a range of organic solvent mixtures. In another study, they introduced two dendron-based phase selective gelators (DPSGs) PTZ116 and PTZ117, which are derived from acylhydrazone linkage with alkyl PTZ, and can be used as PSGs for fuel oils.154 Compounds PTZ116 and PTZ117 form yellow-colored gels in non-polar and aromatic solvents including solvent mixtures, which are identified by their unique characteristics in the FTIR, UV, and PL studies when compared to normal solutions. The morphology of the self-assembled systems is identified as a helical fibrous structure in hexane and fractal type structure in solvent mixtures (see Fig. 19). The findings indicate that the solvent plays a crucial role in determining the self-assembly morphology by monitoring the solutesolute and solutesolvent interactions. It was observed that the gelation strength increased with an increase in the number of -aromatic rings. Hence, gelator PTZ117 was found to be more efficient than gelator PTZ116.

3. Conclusion and perspective

In the past few years, the study of PTZ-based materials has perceived huge attention apart from their usefulness as a donor for a variety of optoelectronic applications. The multiple positions to modify their chemical structure provide synthetically tunable or adaptable electronic, physico-chemical, optical, thermal properties, and their supramolecular self-assembly via various non-covalent interactions. It is not surprising, given their flexibility and uses, that several reviews have been written from various angles. In this review, we provided a broad literature survey of PTZ derivatives for various applications (including brief working mechanism) such as dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), perovskite solar cells (PSCs), organic solar cells (OSCs), OLEDs, OFETs, chemosensing, NLOs, and supramolecular self-assembly. In particular, the PTZ molecules presented in this review demonstrate how small modifications in the chemical structure such as different electron donors or acceptors, length of the -conjugated system or spacers, polar/non-polar chains, and other functional groups on PTZ can lead to substantial changes in the opto-electronic properties of the PTZ materials, as a result, enhancing the electronic device performance.

The development of phenothiazine functional materials with adjustable optoelectronic characteristics can be aided by careful molecular engineering of the PTZ unit in terms of the shape, molecular structure, and inexpensive synthetic protocols with desirable yields. Several investigations have shown that even minor structural changes to the PTZ unit or the conjugate backbone may cause significant changes in the molecular characteristics and influence the device performance.

The molecular shape of PTZ can be altered by oxidizing sulphur and then its geminate dithionation using Lawesson's reagent or elemental sulphur, resulting in a planar structure that further reduces the HOMOLUMO gap and enhances the thermal and photostability.

Noteworthily, DA, DD, ADDA, DAD, DAAD, etc., based molecular structure or configurations are not limited to particular applications; however, they were restricted to very few molecules until now. Interestingly, the variation of acceptors, donors, -bridges, alkyl or glycol chains, and amide groups in the PTZ derivatives can induce remarkable changes in the photophysical, electrochemical, and charge transport properties. Such modifications provide PTZ-based functional materials desirable features such as solubility, thin-film crystallinity, morphology, broader absorption/emission wavelengths, low band gap, and balanced charge transport.

Though the commercial price of PTZ is very low, the desired PTZ functional materials cost is too high since their synthetic techniques employ scarcely available Pd-metal catalyst and CC/CN based coupling processes. These strategies fail to meet sustainability standards and industrial-scale up. These challenges are being tackled by different efficient synthetic strategies such as metal-free or low-cost metal-based CH activation, heterogeneous catalysis, photochemical, and electrochemical approaches.

Thus, the review on PTZ materials and their wide variety of applications promote the design of novel phenothiazines derivatives and study in detail from the aspects of electronic- and biological applications.
