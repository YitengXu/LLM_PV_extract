Abstract

By considering facile molecular diffusion and optically generated electrical polarization, this article reports, for the first time, that directly introducing optically polarizable non-fullerene electron acceptor ITIC molecules into organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) films with uniform and non-uniform gradient distributions can surprisingly enhance spinâ€“orbital coupling (SOC) with the consequence of increasing the conversion from photogenerated spin-singlets to spin-triplets, boosting the spin states available for photovoltaic actions and enhancing the power conversion efficiency (PCE) from 15.3% to 17.2% with a Jsc of 20.2 mA cmâˆ’2, a Voc of 1.08 V, and a FF of 79% in perovskite solar cells [ITO/PEDOT:PSS/MAPbI3âˆ’xClx/PCBM/PEI/Ag]. Specifically, we find that dispersing ITIC molecules can largely decrease the Î”Jsc (photocurrent difference) generated by switching the photoexcitation from linear to circular polarization with the same intensity. This phenomenon provides direct evidence that dispersed optically polarizable ITIC molecules can increase the SOC in organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites through interaction between introduced electrical polarization and SOC. Furthermore, impedance studies indicate that dispersing ITIC molecules can decrease the density of trap states by passivating the grain boundary defects and simultaneously increases the carrier mobilities. Consequently, non-radiative recombination is reduced upon decreasing the density of trap states through dispersing the ITIC molecules, according to transient photoluminescence studies. The UV-Vis and FTIR spectral studies indicate formation of a bond between Pb, N and O, showing a direct interaction between the hybrid perovskite MAPbI3âˆ’xClx and dispersed ITIC molecules. Clearly, introducing optically polarizable non-fullerene electron acceptor molecules presents a new opportunity to enhance photovoltaic actions in perovskite solar cells through SOC and grain boundary passivation effects.

Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites have drawn significant research interest to understand remarkable photovoltaic actions with power conversion efficiencies quickly reaching 22.1% based on the coexisting semiconducting, electrical polarization, and strong spinâ€“orbital coupling.1â€“5 Doping provides a convenient method to enhance the device efficiencies. Different materials have been explored including high-molecular-weight polymers and low-molecular weight molecules serving as n-type and p-type dopants to enhance photovoltaic efficiencies.6â€“9 In general, doping can passivate grain boundary defects, change domain sizes, and improve film quality with the consequence of decreasing non-radiative recombination and balancing ambipolar transport.10â€“14 Here, we explore a new doping opportunity by introducing optically polarizable molecules to enhance the photovoltaic actions in perovskite solar cells. Specifically, the non-fullerene electron acceptor [3,9-bis(2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)-indanone))-5,5,11,11-tetrakis(4-hexylpheny1)-dithieno[2,3-d:2â€²,3â€²-dâ€²]-s-indanceno[1,2-b:5,6-bâ€²]-dithiophene], named ITIC, was selected as the optically polarizable molecule. In particular, the ITIC molecules contain both electron donating and withdrawing structures, indacenodithieno[3,2-b]thiophene (IT) and 2-(3-oxo-2,3-dihydroinden-1-ylidene)alononitrile (INCN).15 Upon photoexcitation, the electron donating and withdrawing units can become positively and negatively charged units through intramolecular charge transfer, leading to optically induced dipoles in ITIC molecules.16,17 As a consequence, the non-fullerene electron acceptor ITIC molecules can demonstrate optically induced polarization under photoexcitation. Clearly, with optically induced polarization, the non-fullerene electron acceptor ITIC molecules provide new opportunities to explore the deeper doping effects on perovskite solar cells. On the other hand, non-fullerene electron acceptor ITIC molecules can exhibit high diffusivity in a polycrystalline perovskite network due to a low dielectric constant. In this study the ITIC molecules were dispersed into perovskite MAPbI3âˆ’xClx films with uniform and gradient distributions by using mixing and dripping methods to further reveal the underlying mechanisms responsible for doping effects. By combining optically induced dipoles with facile molecular diffusion, optically polarizable non-fullerene electron acceptor molecules present a unique platform to explore deeper doping effects in perovskite solar cells. Experimental studies were performed by using circular photoexcitation-induced photocurrent, impedance, and photoluminescence measurements to address doping effects on spinâ€“orbital coupling, non-radiative recombination and ambipolar transport.

We should note that introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules has little influence on the absorption of perovskiteâ€“ITIC devices, as shown in Fig. S5 (ESIâ€ ). Our early studies found that organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites possess an electricâ€“magnetic coupling through the interaction between electric dipoles and intrinsic SOC.19 This electricâ€“magnetic coupling provides the possibilities to change the intrinsic SOC through optically induced dipoles when the ITIC molecules with optically generated polarization are dispersed into perovskite films. Here, we can hypothesize that dispersing ITIC molecules can increase the SOC and consequently change spin states, enhancing internal photovoltaic actions.

Here, we use circular-photoexcitation induced photocurrent studies to explore the effect of optically polarizable ITIC molecule dispersion on spin states in perovskite solar cells.20,21 As schematically shown in Fig. 3a, circular photoexcitation generates all spin-polarized spin-singlets Ï•ml=+1Ï•â†‘eâ†“h with same-directional orbital momenta (ml = +1 for each spin state), providing a higher total orbital momentum to flip the photogenerated spin singlets Ï•ml=Â±1Ï•â†‘eâ†“h into spin triplets Ï•ml=0Ï•â†‘eâ†‘h through SOC effects. In contrast, linear photoexcitation, equivalent to the combination of left-hand (LH) and right-hand (RH) circular polarizations, generates spin-polarized spin-singlets Ï•ml=+1Ï•â†‘eâ†“h and Ï•ml=âˆ’1Ï•â†‘eâ†“h, with opposite orbital momenta (ml = +1 and ml = âˆ’1). The combination of two opposite orbital momenta provides a lower total orbital momentum to flip spins though SOC. Clearly, switching the photoexcitation between circular and linear polarizations can change the intensity of spin flipping, consequently shifting the spin population between spin singlets and triplets. Because the spin-singlets and spin-triplets are allowed and forbidden to recombine, increasing the spin-triplets can boost the spin states available for generating photocurrent. Therefore, we can theoretically expect that linear and circular photo-excitation leads to lower and higher Jsc, leading to Î”Jsc. We should point out that the SOC can increase the conversion from photogenerated spin-singlets to spin-triplets by flipping the spins, decreasing the Î”Jsc generated by switching the photoexcitation from linear to circular polarization. Therefore, upon switching the photoexcitation between linear and circular polarizations, measuring the Î”Jsc upon doping the ITIC molecules can reflect whether doping can change the SOC in the doped hybrid perovskites. Specifically, small/large Î”Jsc corresponds to strong/weak SOC while switching the photoexcitation between linear and circular polarizations. Fig. 3bâ€“d shows the Î”Jsc with switching linearly and circularly polarized photoexcitation based on PHJ, UBHJ and GBHJ perovskite solar cells. We find that the Jsc with circular photoexcitation is larger than that of linear photoexcitation. This means that Jsc can be essentially increased by shifting the spin-singlets toward spin-triplets, boosting the spin-triplets available for photovoltaic actions in the bulk heterojunction perovskiteâ€“ITIC solar cells. More importantly, the Î”Jsc, generated by switching photoexcitation between linear and circular polarization, decreases from 1.5% (PHJ) to 0.5% (UBHJ) and 0.2% (GBHJ), respectively. A weaker SOC effect makes the conversion of spin-singlets to spin-triplets more difficult, resulting in a larger Î”Jsc. In contrast, a strong SOC effect insures that spin-singlets can easily convert into spin-triplets, leading to a smaller Î”Jsc, and also a better photovoltaic performance. Clearly, the result of decreasing Î”Jsc shown in Fig. 3bâ€“d provides the experimental evidence that introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules into perovskite can increase the intrinsic SOC and boost the spin-triplets available for photovoltaic actions in the organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite. To further confirm that introducing optically polarizable ITIC can boost the intrinsic SOC in hybrid perovskites, we explore the dependence of Î”Jsc on photoexcitation energy upon switching linear and circular polarizations at different laser wavelengths. Here, we can expect that high and low photoexcitation energies generate high and low orbital polarizations in optically generated spin-singlets. Consequently, high and low orbital polarizations can cause strong and weak interactions between orbitals, leading to more and less spin flipping in optically generated spin-singlets. Specifically, with high-energy photoexcitation the linear and circular polarizations lead to more different total orbital momenta due to the stronger interaction between orbitals as compared to the situation with low-energy photoexcitation, according to the diagram shown in Fig. 3a. As a result, at high-energy photoexcitation, switching linear polarization to circular polarization causes a larger Î”Jsc relative to a low-energy photoexcitation. As compared to doping effects, introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules can increase the intrinsic SOC to flip spins in optically generated spin-singlets, which minimizes the Î”Jsc caused by switching the photoexcitation between linear and circular polarizations at a given energy, as shown in the results in Fig. 3. Here, the Î”Jsc dependence on photoexcitation energy provides further evidence to support the SOC effect of spin flipping in optically generated spin-singlets in hybrid perovskites upon introducing the optically polarizable ITIC molecules.

It is interesting to see that doping ITIC can influence the SOC effect in perovskites. Our earlier studies have shown that the SOC can externally be induced by applying the spins from ferromagnetic Co onto the surface polarization of a hybrid perovskite film.22 Here, we should note that the ITIC consists of an Aâ€“Dâ€“A structure, where A represents the acceptor (INCN), and D stands for the donor (IT). Under photoexcitation the A and D structures become negative and positive units, leading to an optically induced electrical dipole between Aâˆ’ and D+. This optically induced dipole in the dispersed ITIC molecules can interact with the intrinsic SOC in the hybrid perovskites. This provides a mechanism to increase the SOC upon doping in perovskite solar cells. We have recently shown that photoinduced polarization can be studied by capacitanceâ€“voltage (Câ€“V) characteristics in hybrid perovskites.23 Specifically, the Câ€“V measurements continuously increase the DC voltage, leading to a continuous drifting on cations/anions while an AC voltage detects the dielectric response. The continuously moving cations/anions cannot respond to a low AC voltage (30 mV at 100 kHz). In this situation, only bulk dipolar/orientational polarization can respond to a low AC voltage, presenting a convenient method to detect bulk polarization in organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites where mobile ions exist. Here, we observe that doping non-fullerene electron acceptor ITIC molecules can enhance photoinduced bulk polarization based on capacitanceâ€“voltage (Câ€“V) characteristics. Fig. 5 shows the Câ€“V characteristics for three devices by scanning the DC voltage from âˆ’0.1 V to +1.3 V at a low AC voltage of 30 mV (100 kHz) at different photoexcitation intensities. Increasing the photoexcitation intensity generates an interesting phenomenon: the amplitude of the Câ€“V signal within the depletion zone from âˆ’0.1 V to +0.5 V is increased by 3.2%, when the excitation increases to 100 mW cmâˆ’2. This phenomenon evidences that photoexcitation enhances bulk polarization in the perovskite (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) film. Compared with PHJ devices, the ratio of the capacitance signal is increased to 6.0% and 7.2% for UBHJ and GBHJ devices under 100 mW cmâˆ’2 excitation, respectively. It is known that the total polarization (Ptotal) measured in the depletion zone combines the polarization (Pdark) under dark conditions and the polarization (Pphoto) upon photoexcitation: Ptotal = Pdark + Pphoto, where the photo-induced bulk polarization is given by . By comparing the photoinduced bulk polarizations before and after dispersing ITIC molecules, we can clearly see that introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules into perovskite film increases photoinduced bulk polarization. This phenomenon suggests that introducing optically polarizable non-fullerene electron-acceptor ITIC molecules can decrease the defects at grain boundaries, leading to a passivation effect in the perovskite (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) film.

Now we study the passivation effects of introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules into perovskite films by measuring the trap density of states (tDOS) by thermal admittance spectroscopy.24,25 As shown in Fig. 6a, the PHJ device possesses the largest tDOS on the order of 1017â€“1019 cmâˆ’3 eVâˆ’1, which is similar to the studies performed elsewhere.26 After doping ITIC into the perovskite, the tDOS of UBHJ devices decreases by 25% at a low energy depth (0.25â€“0.3 eV); however, the tDOS of GBHJ devices only slightly decreases in the low energy regime. Instead, we notice that tDOS of GBHJ devices shows a significant decrease by 55% in a high energy depth regime (0.4â€“0.5 eV). These results indicate that uniform distributed ITIC can effectively passivate the shallow traps that are located in the perovskite bulk, while gradient distributed ITIC is more efficient in passivating the deep traps which mainly occur at grain boundaries and crystal surfaces. As a result, the non-radiative recombination can be decreased upon doping optically polarizable ITIC into the perovskites. At last, we discuss the underlying passivation mechanism of ITIC for the defects in perovskites. Due to decomposition of MAPbI3 at an elevated annealing temperature (>80 Â°C), halide (iodine) loss can generate vacancies and under-coordinated Pb atoms and Pb clusters.30 These under-coordinated Pb results in the positive charges on the atom, which act as the electronic trap centers, and are also known to be Lewis acids. On the other hand, ITIC contains electron-withdrawing groups such as cyano (CN) and carbonyl (CO), in which N and O can donate electrons and essentially act as Lewis bases. Therefore, the under-coordinated Pb atoms form coordinate bonding with the N and O donors of ITIC through Lewis acidâ€“base adduct reaction as shown in Fig. 8a. In order to examine the coordinate bonding of Pb with N and O, we measure the absorption spectrum of pure ITIC and ITICâ€“PbI2 mixed solution, shown in Fig. 8b. We observed a spectral shift of 5 nm in the ITIC absorption peak at a low concentration (0.5 wt%) of PbI2, which indicates a strong bonding interaction between PbI2 and ITIC. Furthermore, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) is employed to analyze this interaction. Fig. 8c represents the IR spectra of ITIC, MAPbI3 and the ITICÂ·MAPbI3 blend. The symmetric stretching vibrations of CO and Câ‰¡N bonds in pure ITIC appear at 1703 and 2218 cmâˆ’1, respectively. After normalizing these two characteristic vibrational peaks with the maximum peak (1540 cmâˆ’1) assigned to the vibrations of CC bonds, which is less influenced by Pb ions, the CO and Câ‰¡N peak intensities were reduced by 79% and 81% in the ITICÂ·MAPbI3 blend. This significant decrease of the vibrational peak intensity indicates that the carbonyl group (CO) and the cyano group Câ‰¡N of ITIC can react with the perovskite through the Lewis acidâ€“base adduct. Therefore, ITIC molecules can be used to passivate defects at grain boundaries of perovskite films using the method of Lewis base passivation,31 consequently enhancing the photovoltaic performance of organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells.

Conclusions

In summary, by introducing optically polarizable non-fullerene electron-donorâ€“acceptor ITIC molecules with uniform and gradient dispersions, we found that the SOC can be surprisingly enhanced in hybrid perovskite (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) films by using circularly polarized photoexcitation induced photocurrent measurements. Specifically, we observed that the Î”Jsc measured by switching the photoexcitation between linear and circular polarizations is decreased from 1.5% to 0.2% by introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules into the perovskite film. This provides evidence that the dispersed non-fullerene electron-acceptor ITIC molecules can increase the SOC in the hybrid perovskites under photoexcitation. We hypothesized that the optically generated dipoles in the ITIC molecules can directly interact with the SOC in the hybrid perovskites, leading to an increase in the SOC. This hypothesis was verified by photoinduced polarization in the hybrid perovskites upon dispersing the ITIC molecules. Therefore, our results present an interesting method to change the SOC in organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites by introducing optically polarizable non-fullerene electron-acceptor molecules. Furthermore, increasing the SOC can essentially increase the conversion from photogenerated spin-singlets to spin-triplets, boosting the spin states available for generating Jsc. On the other hand, our impedance study indicates that dispersing ITIC molecules can decrease the densities of both deep and shallow traps in the hybrid perovskites. The SCLC measurements show that ITIC dispersion can increase electron mobility by a factor of 2 or 3, leading to balanced ambipolar mobilities with the consequence of increasing the FF in perovskite solar cells. Our TRPL measurement provides the direct information that ITIC molecules can suppress non-radiative recombination by decreasing traps at grain boundaries. Suppressing non-radiative recombination can also increase the probability of excitons dissociating to generate photocurrent. Therefore, introducing optically polarizable ITIC molecules leads to an enhancement in the PCE from 15.3% to 17.2% in perovskite solar cells.
