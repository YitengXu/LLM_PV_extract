Green energy storage chemistries based on neutral aqueous electrolytes

Abstract

Widespread use of fossil fuels has resulted in many environmental problems. There is an urgent need to search for reliable green energy storage technologies. The electrical energy storage systems based on neutral aqueous solutions are attractive candidates compared to the present energy storage systems utilizing flammable and expensive organic electrolytes because of their improved safety, low cost and environmental friendliness. What is more, high-rate performance can be achieved due to the high ionic conductivity of the aqueous electrolytes. However, the lower electrochemical window of water limits the possible high output voltage and high energy density. Recently, many new concepts based on neutral aqueous energy storage chemistries including aqueous rechargeable batteries and supercapacitors have exhibited high power and energy densities, excellent cycling life and high Coulombic efficiency. The present paper reviews the latest advances in these new chemistries based on neutral aqueous electrolytes, and the challenges and outlooks in this field are briefly commented on and discussed.

1 Introduction

Recently, the rapid depletion of fossil fuels and the increasing emission of greenhouse gases causing air pollution have become serious issues. There is a clear urgent need for the development of reliable green energy storage technologies to achieve a secure and reliable energy supply. Electrical energy storage (EES) systems including batteries and supercapacitors, an intermediate platform to the versatile and efficient deployment of renewable energy such as solar, wind and tide, have received worldwide attention. What is more, they play a prominent role in our future modern lives. Since the emergence and extensive use of many new types of portable electronic devices and electric vehicles (EVs) which are promisingly expected to significantly contribute to solving the problems of global warming and environmental pollution, the demand for good EES systems is becoming more and more urgent.15

Lithium ion batteries (LIB) have been well acknowledged as an EES system with high energy density and long cycling life, and are superior to other conventional batteries.610 However, their inherent safety issue related to the use of high cost, toxic and flammable organic electrolyte and superfast charging performance are still two challenges for their applications in large-scale EES systems such as electric vehicles and smart grids.11,12 Compared with the combustible organic electrolytes of LIBs, operating in environmentally friendly neutral aqueous electrolytes with high ionic conductivity, about 2 orders of magnitude higher than those of the nonaqueous ones, is attractive for electrochemical energy storage in terms of safety and cost.1316 If both high specific power and energy densities are achieved, such EESs would be useful for power quality applications, such as to assist propelling electric vehicles that require fast acceleration and intense braking.17 Nevertheless, the previous energy storage devices using aqueous electrolytes based on different chemistries such as NiCd, NiMH and lead acid batteries are limited in applications due to their serious ecological threat, poor cycling life and low energy density.1820 There are a lot of endeavors to explore new EES systems.2124

Since 2007, aqueous rechargeable lithium batteries (ARLBs) using positive electrode materials from commercial LIBs have attracted wide attention as a promising system because of their low capital investment, environmental friendliness and good safety, and they can be a suitable alternative because their energy density can be above that of lithium ion batteries.14,15,2530 Different flexible EES systems based on aqueous electrolytes sprang up in recent years: there are a variety of novel aqueous alkali-ion battery chemistries31,33 and supercapacitors.32 They are potentially advantageous owing to their good safety, high ionic conductivity and the low cost of aqueous electrolytes.

In this review, we briefly discuss the recent progress and challenges associated with the EES systems based on neutral aqueous electrolytes including aqueous rechargeable batteries and supercapacitors with an emphasis on the most notable work.

2 Aqueous rechargeable batteries

Aqueous rechargeable batteries mainly include aqueous rechargeable lithium batteries (ARLBs) and aqueous rechargeable sodium and potassium batteries.

2.1 Aqueous rechargeable lithium batteries

An ARLB should meet the following three conditions:14

(a) It uses lithium intercalation or lithium-containing compound(s) as one or two of the electrodes;

(b) Its charge and discharge processes are based on redox reactions accompanying the gain and loss of electrons instead of absorption and desorption;

(c) It uses a lithium-containing aqueous solution as an electrolyte.

ARLBs can overcome some disadvantages of lithium ion batteries such as the high cost and safety problems, and environmental pollution problems of lead acid or NiCd batteries. It may be an ideal energy storage system to satisfy the demands of a grid-scale application due to its excellent safety and reliability.14,3436 However, since the introduction of ARLBs in 1994, the progress on improving their performance was very limited. Recently, there have been many breakthroughs which show clearly that they can present very high reversible capacity, good cycling performance, and rate capability, especially super-fast charge performance which can be comparable to filling cars engines with gasoline.1316,28,3741 Of course, they include positive and negative electrodes.

As for the negative electrode materials of ARLBs, vanadium oxides, molybdenum oxides and some lithium intercalation compounds have been reported, and some performances are also summarized in Table 1. Research about the negative electrode materials is quite limited owing to more challenges concerning dissolution of the active cations so that some conductive polymers or carbon are coated on the surface of negative electrode materials.45,5557 It is well known that polypyrrole (PPy) is one of the most popular conducting polymers,58,59 which has been used to modify electrode materials for lithium ion batteries. 2.2 Aqueous rechargeable sodium and potassium batteries

The price of raw materials in ARLBs could still be a limitation for large-scale EES systems, therefore aqueous rechargeable sodium batteries (ARSBs) appear to be a promising and attractive alternative to their lithium counterparts because of the wide availability and low cost of sodium resources.67 However, ARSBs are rarely reported except a few Na-intercalation materials. On the one hand, the radius of Na+ (0.102 nm), especially the hydrated sodium ion (0.358 nm), is much larger than those of H+ and Li+, making the intercalation reactions more difficult and leading to the low capacity of the active materials. On the other hand, the bulky sodium ion during the intercalation leads to lattice deformation, resulting in the collapse of the crystal structure and poor cycling stability. In the case of aqueous rechargeable potassium batteries (ARPB), there are even fewer reports. Since they are batteries, they include positive and negative electrodes.

2.3 High energy density ARLBs

The main disadvantage of the aforementioned ARLBs, which can be called the first generation of ARLBs (1G ARLBs), is that their output voltage and energy density are still much lower than those of conventional lithium ion batteries due to the limit of the electrochemically stable window of water.14,16,28 Since the output voltage of the new ARLBs is much higher above this window and can be above 4.0 V, these ARLBs are discussed in this separate section. Of course, they can also be called the second generation (2G) ARLBs. The main feature of these 2G ARLBs is that they use stable lithium metal as the negative electrode.
2.3.1 Stable lithium metal. If negative electrode materials of lower redox potentials can be stable in aqueous electrolytes, high energy density systems will be feasible. For the traditional systems, the removal of oxygen is very crucial.36 Recently, our group invented a coated lithium metal that is stable in neutral aqueous solution and the removal of oxygen is not needed.8789 It is well-known that alkali metals are very active, and belongs to the fire category. Aqueous solutions surely belong to water in the traditional five elements theory of China. It is known that fire and water are not compatible. To make them compatible, a wood is needed. Wood consists mostly of polymers. Then we put a layer of polymer (GPE) on the surface of the lithium metal. The GPE consists of our homemade sandwiched polymer membrane, such as PVDF (poly(vinyl difluoride))/PMMA (poly(methyl methacrylate))/PVDF structure, with organic electrolyte. However, water molecules can slowly dissolve the lithium salt and organic solvents in the gel polymer electrolyte, and lithium ions cannot transport between the lithium metal and the aqueous solution. Another layer, a glass-ceramic layer (LISICON from Ohara, Japan) which belongs to earth, is used. LISICON film is a solid electrolyte (Li+ > 104 S cm1) which can also act as a separator and allows only the pass-through of Li+ ions between both sides of the film while the pass-through of protons, water, hydrated and solvated ions is forbidden. This composite layer makes the lithium metal very stable, whose structure is schematically shown in Fig. 9a. In 0.5 mol L1 Li2SO4 electrolyte, the coated lithium metal ensures good electrochemical stability; only Li+ ions can transfer between Li metal and the outside via the LISICON film and the GPE, and dissolution and plating of Li can reversibly happen at about 2.70 V (vs. SCE) (Fig. 9b). When Li metal is only coated by the membrane of the gel polymer electrolyte, the coated lithium metal is still stable in the aqueous solution even when the temperature is up to 60 C.

3 Aqueous supercapacitors

Supercapacitors or electrochemical capacitors (ECs) are energy storage devices which can provide higher power density as compared to batteries and higher energy density than conventional capacitors. They are regarded as promising energy storage devices for electric vehicles, and have attracted intensive attention.104106 Here we focus on neutral aqueous medium, i.e. lithium, sodium and potassium solutions in a wide range of concentrations, which are promising electrolytes for supercapacitors because they are cheap, non-corrosive and allow for application of a diverse range of current collectors, making the supercapacitor assembling process much easier and cheaper. Additionally, such electrolytes are electrochemically stable and environmentally friendly.107

Depending on the energy storage mechanism, supercapacitors can be classified into two general categories: electrochemical double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudocapacitors.108 EDLCs store charges electrostatically via reversible ion absorption/desorption at the electrode/electrolyte interface, which commonly use carbon-based active electrode materials with high surface area.109 In contrast, pseudocapacitors use reversible redox reactions at the surface of electroactive materials for charge storage.110 Typical active pseudocapacitive materials include transition metal oxides and electronically conducting redox polymers such as polyaniline (PAn), polypyrrole (PPy), polythiophene (PTh) and their derivatives.57 Here we discuss their electrode materials according to their different mechanisms.

3.1 Electrode materials based on electrochemical double layer absorption/desorption

In neutral aqueous solutions, so far there are only carbon materials with high surface area that display electrochemical double layered capacitive behavior. To date, various forms of carbonaceous materials such as activated carbons (ACs),111 mesoporous carbons,112 activated carbon fabrics113 and carbon nanotubes (CNTs)114 have been utilized for both symmetric and asymmetric supercapacitors. These carbon-based materials offer some advantageous features including low cost, high electronic conductivity, good electrochemical stability with long cycling life and tunable porous structures. Most recently, graphene115 and graphene-based composites116118 have been investigated because of their unique structures and high specific surface area (2630 m2 g1).

In the case of ACs, the pores including micro-, meso-, and macro-pores are randomly distributed in the ACs, the pore sizes have a significant influence on capacitance which is closely related to the electrolyte.119,120 In neutral solutions, the ion size presents different behavior at different current or power densities. For example, in 0.5 mol L1 Li2SO4, Na2SO4 and K2SO4 aqueous electrolytes, the rate behavior of the AC improves in the order of Li2SO4 < Na2SO4 < K2SO4 (Fig. 11a). This can be attributed to the different hydrated ionic radius contributing to the different charge densities and different migration speeds.120 The hydrated ionic radius of K+ is the smallest and its ionic conductivity is the highest, consequently its access to the inner pores of AC is much easier, and the relaxation time for the migration of the hydrated K+ is the shortest at the high scan rate. In addition, the equivalent series resistances (ESR) for the three electrolytes decrease in the order of Li2SO4 > Na2SO4 > K2SO4 (Fig. 11b). As a result, K2SO4-based electrolyte exhibits the largest capacitance at the high scan rates (Fig. 11c). At the low scan rate, the capacitance is mainly determined by the size of un-hydrated cations, which decreases in the order of Li+ < Na+ < K+. As a result, the capacitance increases in the same order, i.e. Li+ > Na+ > K+. They present very stable cycling in the neutral electrolytes, which opens a platform for aqueous supercapacitors based on neutral electrolytes.

3.2 Electrode materials based on absorption/desorption and redox reactions

Any material has some specific surface area, and absorption/desorption is unavoidable. It is well known that some transition-metal oxides such as amorphous RuO2,122 MnO2,105,123 V2O5 (ref. 56, 81 and 124) and MoO3,45,104,125 as well as some polymers such as PAn, PPy, PTh and their derivatives exhibit pseudocapacitive properties.57 Their large specific pseudocapacitance (typically 3001000 F g1) exceeds that of carbon-based materials (typically 100250 F g1) based on double layer charge storage. Of course, the positive electrode materials for ARLBs can also be good positive electrode materials for aqueous supercapacitors since they also present good and reversible redox reactions, which present good pseudocapacitance. Since they are discussed in Section 2.1.1, here they will not be repeated.

Material morphology is closely related to the specific surface area and the diffusion of ions. Pseudocapacitive technology has been benefited greatly by moving from conventional to nanostructured materials.110 The nanostructured materials have drawn intense attention to develop better supercapacitors because of their high surface area, nanosize effects, significantly enhanced kinetics and high capacitance. For example, a nanocomposite of molybdenum trioxide104,126 (-MoO3) nanobelts coated with polypyrrole (PPy), whose morphology is shown in Fig. 13a and b, presents one pair of redox peaks at 0.45 and 0.32 V (vs. SCE), respectively, corresponding to the reversible intercalation/deintercalation of K+ ions into/from the MoO3 matrix in its CV curves (Fig. 13c), indicating a good negative electrode for supercapacitors. Even at the high scan rate of 40 mV s1, the redox peaks are still evident. Of course, the nanocomposite can also present good reversible intercalation/deintercalation of Li+ and Na+ ions into/from the MoO3 matrix.

4 Summary and outlooks

As the most ideal electrolyte solvent, water is abundant and environmentally friendly. The above discussion shows clearly that the latest explored energy storage systems based on neutral aqueous electrolytes provide several advantages over the traditional energy storage systems:

Low cost: inexpensive and good availability of salts such as Li2SO4 and LiNO3 instead of expensive LiPF6, which do not need rigorously strict humidity control of the assembling environment as batteries based on organic electrolytes do and their requirements on separators are low.

Suitable for charging and discharging at high rates: high ionic conductivity of the aqueous electrolytes.

Satisfactory or high energy density based on both electrodes.

High power density competitive with fuel cells and capacitors.

Long cycling life.

High Coulombic efficiency.

Environmental friendliness: completely green, which is superior to lithium ion batteries having serious HF acid pollution to the environment due to the reaction of the used LiPF6 with water.

More safe: no combustibility or explosion compared with the energy storage systems based on flammable organic electrolytes.

Of course, these advantages are achieved due to the new preparation technologies, especially coating and nanostructures. New concepts such as aqueous rechargeable lithium batteries of high energy density (2 G ARLBs) are also important to break through the electrochemically stable window of water, which provide new directions for post lithium ion batteries. Introduction of nanostructures together with the traditional intercalation compounds leads to an increase of energy and power densities and excellent cycling life of supercapacitors.

The above characteristics are also important for these systems in storing energy from electrical grids connected with intermittent energy sources such as solar, wind and hydropower. It is also important to lower the cost and meet the stringent safety requirements of batteries for pure electric vehicles. The challenges to significantly increase the specific output power and energy density of batteries urgently need to be solved.100,144 Some directions are very important for the further development of energy storage systems based on neutral aqueous electrolytes:

(1) New energy storage chemistries are still needed to explore. For example, MnO2 can be the reversible host for some divalent cations such as Zn2+, Mg2+, Ca2+ and Ba2+ in aqueous electrolytes,130132 and the +2 cations can suppress the activation energy barrier for the interfacial charge transfer of a prussian blue analog thin film electrode.133,134 The Al3+ ion also presents reversible storage behavior in anatase TiO2 nanotube arrays.135 The mixed system (Li,Na)FePO4//FePO4 is also possible.136,137 Aqueous rechargeable batteries such as Ni//Fe,138,139 Co(OH)2//Ni(OH)2,140 TiO2//Ni(OH)2,141 and aqueous Li//S rechargeable batteries142 have been proposed. The concept of a desalination battery with a simple construction and a promising energy efficiency is fascinating since it has fewer corrosion problems than the existing desalination technology by using readily available materials.143 Undoubtedly, such efforts are important and need more encouragement. Combination of batteries with supercapacitors is also a good way to realize the balance between energy and power densities.

(2) New materials are crucial to push forward the application of these energy storage systems. For example, solid electrolytes need much improvement in mechanical processing, ionic conductivity and stability in different aqueous electrolytes.146149

(3) More preparation or modification methods for electrode materials are still required. For example, electrode materials with good crystal orientation to realize much faster charging or discharging behavior require good templates.13,43 Micro-/nano-structured materials with excellent electrochemical performances are another focus since nanostructures provide short diffusion pathways and large specific surface area and the microstructure provides good processing and stability.144,145
