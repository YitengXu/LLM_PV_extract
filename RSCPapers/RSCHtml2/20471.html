Abstract

Methoxy-free donors are an emerging class of alternative methoxy triphenylamine materials toward stable organic hole-transporting materials (HTMs). However, low cost, stable and efficient methoxy-free donors are scarce. Moreover, the lack of a molecular design strategy limits the further development of methoxy-free donor-based HTMs. This study reports a newly developed low cost hybrid methoxy-free donor (viz., indeno[2,1-b]carbazole) as well as a triple Ï€-bridge strategy to enhance the performance of methoxy-free donor-based HTMs. Based on this strategy, two new indeno[2,1-b]carbazole HTMs, termed M136 and M138, were tailor-made and tested in perovskite solar cells (PSCs). Upon incorporating the triple Ï€-bridges (i.e. thiophene-dithieno[3,2-b:2â€²,3â€²-d]pyrrole-thiophene and thiophene-3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene-thiophene), the resulting indeno[2,1-b]carbazole HTMs (M136 and M138) exhibit a significantly increased hole mobility, improved charge extraction and minimized trap-assisted recombination as compared to M135 and M137 with unitary and binary bridges, respectively. Consequently, the optimized devices based on M138 achieved an efficiency of 21.37%, which is among the top efficiencies for PSCs based on methoxy-free donor HTMs. The findings demonstrate that indeno[2,1-b]carbazole is an excellent methoxy-free donor. In addition, this work underscores the effectiveness of Ï€-bridge engineering to improve the performance of methoxy-free donor-based HTMs.

1 Introduction

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have attracted tremendous attention in the field of photovoltaics (PV) due to their outstanding optoelectronic properties.1â€“12 n-i-p PSCs consist of an electron-transfer layer (ETL), a perovskite material and a uniform layer of thin hole-transporting materials (HTMs), and these layers jointly determine the solar cell performances. Commercial spiro-OMeTAD is a prevalently used HTM due to its high performance and reproducibility. Unfortunately, this HTM suffers from expensive purification processes and low hole mobilities, which has motivated intensive research efforts in finding new efficient HTMs such as small organic HTMs,13â€“57 polymers,58â€“61 and metalâ€“organic compounds.62â€“65 The small organic HTMs have gained great attention in the rational molecular design of HTMs due to their facile synthesis and excellent hole transporting capability.

Combining peripheral arylamine donors with central scaffolds has been identified as the most common way to construct small organic HTMs.15,26 Numerous central scaffolds such as spiro-compounds, arylamines, thiophene-containing segments and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons have been explored to modulate the carrier mobility, energy level and morphology of HTMs. In contrast, the exploration of new peripheral arylamine donors is currently rather limited. So far, the most efficient HTMs still rely on methoxy-substituted triphenylamine (OMe-TPA) and OMe-substituted diphenylamine (OMe-DPA).15,26 However, despite their high performance, OMe-TPA and OMe-DPA are not perfect. For example, methoxy usually leads to a higher HOMO level and lower glass transition temperature (Tg) of HTMs, which may result in a decreased open-circuit voltage and device stability.33 To overcome the bottlenecks from the methoxy group, it is necessary to develop new donors as alternatives to OMe-TPA and OMe-DPA.

Strategies including the partial substitution of the methoxy group,28,66â€“68 fluorination of the donor33 and removing the methoxy group24,54,69,70 were employed for the design of better donors (Scheme 1). Despite the initial reports, obtaining a high efficiency methoxy-free donor by getting rid of the methoxy group remains a challenge. Originally, Nishimura et al. introduced oxygen-bridged triarylamines as quasi-planar scaffolds for an azulene based HTM, which achieves a power conversion efficiency of 16.5%.69 Then, Li and co-workers70 developed dopant-free HTMs based on 9-phenyl-9H-carbazole. After that, acenaphthylene was used successfully as the end-capping unit, and the ACE-QA-ACE HTM exhibits high performance showing a champion efficiency of 18.2%.24 Pola and co-workers reported two dopant-free HTMs based on the benzothiophene donor.31 These studies demonstrated that the introduction of donors with a planar backbone could reinforce intermolecular interactions and Ï€âˆ’Ï€ stacking, which is beneficial for improving charge transport abilities even though HTMs lack methoxy groups. Previously, we have reported indeno[1,2-b]carbazole54 based HTMs for PSCs, and found that the methoxy-free hybrid donor contributed to both the efficiency and the stability of devices simultaneously. Nevertheless, to date, the development of a methoxy-free donor for HTMs is still in its infancy, and the design strategy for methoxy-free donor based HTMs needs to be further established.

In this work, a new methoxy-free hybrid donor, i.e. indeno[2,1-b]carbazole group (Fig. 1), is reported. This building block is commercially available (CAS number 1257220-47-5) and the price is not high (the commercial price is about 25 $ gâˆ’1, Energy Chemical, China). In addition, the cost for lab synthesis of an indeno[2,1-b]carbazole-based HTM M138 is estimated to be about 51 $ gâˆ’1 (please find details in the ESIâ€ ), which is much lower than that of spiro-OMeTAD71 (â‰ˆ92 $ gâˆ’1). Compared to indeno[1,2-b]carbazole we reported recently, indeno[2,1-b]carbazole is cheaper. This is importance for substantial reduction in fabrication costs. Indeed, methoxy-free hybrid donors contribute to the stability of HTMs. Methoxy removal, however, is accompanied by a decreased interaction between the HTM and perovskite absorber, which plays a crucial role in reducing interfacial recombination losses and charge extraction. Nonetheless, the interaction between the HTM and perovskite absorber can be tuned by adjusting the Ï€-bridge. Thus, seeking Ï€-bridges which have rich interaction sites with metal cations of the perovskite absorber is of great importance to improve the performance of methoxy-free donor-derived HTMs.

Herein, two indeno[2,1-b]carbazole-HTMs M136 and M138 have been designed and synthesized by incorporating a triple Ï€-bridge based on thiophene, dithieno[3,2-b:2â€²,3â€²-d]pyrrole (DTP) or 3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene (EDOT) (Fig. 1). It is interesting to find that both HTMs show an impressive efficiency of over 20% under 100 mW cmâˆ’2 AM 1.5 G solar illumination. Unexpectedly, the replacement of thiophene-DTP-thiophene by a thiophene-EDOT-thiophene bridge gave amazing results. Upon systematic optimization of the HTM layer, meso n-i-p structured PSCs based on doped M138 achieve a champion PCE of 21.37%, which is the best result for PSCs employing methoxy-free donor-HTMs reported so far. To deeply understand the relationship between the methoxy-free hybrid donor and the triple Ï€-bridge, we have also systematically investigated the influencing factors of the donor/bridge, on the opto-electrical, hole mobility, film morphology and thermal stability of HTMs as well as the performance of devices. These results further signify that decorating the methoxy-free donor with a triple bridge is a universal and effective strategy for highly efficient methoxy-free donor-derived HTMs.

2 Results and discussion

2.1 Design and synthesis

The rationale for developing the methoxy-free hybrid donor is as follows: (1) the donor can be judiciously designed via functionalization of any chemical moiety. Thus, it is feasible to tune the properties of donors. (2) The two-dimensional geometry of the molecule would be advantageous for effective delocalization and intermolecular stacking.25 (3) The extended conjugation backbone of the hybrid donor could significantly improve the glass transition temperature (Tg) and decomposition temperatures (Td), and eventually the stability of the resulting PSCs. In this report, bulk indeno[2,1-b]carbazole combined with dimethylfluorene and carbazole units is expected to reinforce intermolecular interactions, increase the thermal stability of HTMs and improve the film quality. On the other hand, thiophene-DTP-thiophene7 and thiophene-EDOT-thiophene42,72 bridges were introduced so as to improve the performance of methoxy-free donor-derived HTMs. Both bridges have rich interaction sites (oxygen and sulfur atoms) with metal cations of perovskite. To reveal the superior performance of the triple bridge compared to the unitary and binary bridges, M135 (with the DTP bridge) and M137 (with the bithiophene bridge) were introduced as the reference HTMs. The detailed synthetic routes and characterization results (1H NMR and 13C NMR) of M135â€“138 are depicted in the ESI.â€

2.3 Hole mobility and film morphology

The hole transporting properties of the HTMs are investigated by the space charge-limited-current (SCLC) method (Fig. 4a), where a hole only device with a structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/HTMs/Ag was used. The results revealed that the central bridge significantly affected the hole transporting properties of the HTMs. The measured hole mobility value of M135 with a DTP core is 1.93 Ã— 10âˆ’4 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. Meanwhile, M137 featuring the binary bridge bithiophene shows the lowest hole mobility (8.85 Ã— 10âˆ’5 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1) among these HTMs. Noted that, the hole mobility of M136 increased by around 8.2-fold from 1.93 Ã— 10âˆ’4 to 1.58 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 as the DTP core was replaced by the thiophene-DTP-thiophene triple bridge. In addition, the mobility of M138 (1.85 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1) was further improved as DTP was replaced by EDOT. Clearly, incorporating the triple bridge into indeno[2,1-b]carbazole based HTMs is beneficial to efficient hole extraction and transport due to extended charge delocalization and enhanced intermolecular aggregation. The triple Ï€-bridges based on thiophene, DTP and EDOT offer potential advantages in charge transportation because these units can induce additional intermolecular Sâ€“S and Sâ€“Ï€ interactions, thus reducing the intermolecular distance and increasing the hole hopping channels.18 Note that, the mobility values of M136 and M138 exceed that of spiro-OMeTAD (hole mobility = 1.26 Ã— 10âˆ’4 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1).74 This can be attributed to the flat configuration of the molecule and the coplanar Ï€-extended indeno [2,1-b]carbazole as well as the triple Ï€-bridges (Fig. S1â€ ) which are beneficial to improve the intermolecular charge transport via enhanced Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking.18 Despite that, there is room left for further enhancement by chain engineering or introducing a strong acceptor to improve the intrinsic hole mobilities of indeno[2,1-b]carbazole-based HTMs. We also measured the conductivities of these HTMs (Fig. S2â€ ). It turns out that M136 (5.09 Ã— 10âˆ’6 S cmâˆ’1) and M138 (6.52 Ã— 10âˆ’6 S cmâˆ’1) with the triple Ï€-bridge are superior to M135 (4.69 Ã— 10âˆ’6 S cmâˆ’1) and M137 (2.19 Ã— 10âˆ’6 S cmâˆ’1) regarding the conductivity. 2.6 Hole extraction at the perovskite/HTM interface

To further investigate the hole extraction capability of the HTMs, steady-state photoluminescence (PL) measurements were carried out. The steady-state PL spectra of the perovskite layers without and with capped HTMs are shown in Fig. 7a. After the introduction of the HTMs, a severe PL quenching was observed due to hole injection from the photoexcited perovskite into M135â€“138. As presented in Fig. 7a, the PL intensity was reduced to 11.7%, 7.5%, 25.7% and 7.1% of that of the pristine perovskite films with M135, M136, M137 and M138, respectively. Clearly, M136 and M138 bearing the triple bridge could quench the PL of perovskite more efficiently in comparison with M135 and M137. To understand the difference in the PL intensity further and to evaluate the charge extraction kinetics at the perovskite/HTM interface, we recorded the time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) decay, which was modeled in terms of a bi-exponential equation. The fast lifetime (Ï„1), the long lifetime (Ï„2) and the average lifetime (Ï„t) are summarized in Table S2.â€  The Ï„1 is shortened to 1.5 ns for M136-based device compared to that of M135 (133.8 ns) and M137 (213.5 ns). 2.7 Long-term stability of devices

Long-term stability is crucial for the widespread applications of PSCs. We investigated the device stability of M135-, M136-, M137-and 138-based PSCs (Fig. 8). The stability test shows that the M136-and M138-based devices can retain 90% and 89% of their initial PCE after 480 h, respectively. Under the same working conditions, the devices based on M135 and M137 kept around 86% and 79% of the initial efficiency, respectively. The good stability of doped M136-based PSCs can be partially attributed to the enlarged water contact angle. 3 Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully designed and synthesized four new organic HTMs (M135â€“138) based on a low cost, stable and efficient methoxy-free donor indeno[2,1-b]carbazole. Due to the carbazole-fluorene hybrid structure, the indeno[2,1-b]carbazole HTMs exhibit a high Tg, uniform surface morphology and low HOMO level. In addition, a triple Ï€-bridge strategy is introduced to construct high-efficiency methoxy-free donor-based HTMs. Compared with the indeno[2,1-b]carbazole HTMs with a unitary and binary bridge, those HTMs with a triple Ï€-bridge demonstrated significantly enhanced hole transporting/extraction capability and minimized trap-assisted recombination. As a result, the PSC devices fabricated with M136 and M138 demonstrate outstanding PCEs of 20.56% and 21.37%, respectively, with good reproducibility. Importantly, the M138-based devices revealed an excellent long-term operational stability after aging for over 480 h. These results not only suggest the effectiveness of the triple Ï€-bridge strategy for improving the performance of methoxy-free donor HTMs, but also demonstrate the promising applications of methoxy-free donor HTMs for efficient and stable PSCs.
