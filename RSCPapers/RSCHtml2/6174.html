Abstract

Thermoelectric (TE) materials have attracted tremendous research interests over the past few decades, due to their application in power generation technology from waste heat, almost without producing any pollution in the environment. The energy conversion efficiency depends on a dimensionless quantity called the “Figure of merit” (ZT) of the material. The higher value of ZT indicates a material to be a good thermoelectric. So the aim is to find materials with a high ZT value and can commercially be used in devices like thermoelectric generators (TEGs) or thermoelectric coolers (TECs). Some decades ago, the benchmark value of ZT for the available TE materials was ∼1. Still, the application of various novel strategies made it possible to set the target to achieve ZT > 2 or even higher. This review will discuss the traditional and modern strategies for the generic improvement of thermoelectric materials and the progress in thermoelectric performance of Tin and Germanium chalcogenides (SnTe, SnSe, SnS, GeTe, GeSe, and GeS-based materials) over the past few years. Lead chalcogenides have already been one of the best representative materials for TE applications in mid and high-temperature regions for the past few decades. But the toxicity of Pb and related ecological problems restrict their usage for commercial purposes. Sn- and Ge-chalcogenides belong to the same class of (IV–VI) Pb-chalcogenides but cause no serious environmental issues. This article will provide an overview of the progress of Sn- and Ge-chalcogenides as TE materials.

1. Introduction

Nowadays, demand for energy in human life is increasing by leaps and bounds, but our conventional energy sources are minimal, so nonconventional energy sources are drawing more attention. Environment pollution is also a big problem now, so pollution-free energy generation or green energy generation is preferable. Much of the total consumed energy in various modern industries and process plants is dissipated as waste heat. A proportion of this waste heat can be converted into electricity by applying thermoelectric power generation technology. Materials with low electron scattering, therefore high electrical conductivity, and strong phonon scattering are preferable for better TE application. Based on these requirements, crystalline materials of the “phonon-glass electron-crystal” (PGEC) type was proposed as suitable TE materials by Slack.7 According to this proposal, the TE materials should possess an intriguing structure that effectively allows the propagation of charge carriers while blocking phonon propagation for better TE performance. Depending on this proposal, various new strategies, concepts, and material systems have been established in past decades. Mainly Cu-based chalcogenides have emerged as a new class of promising TE materials based on this concept.8–15 It is not an easy task to optimize S, σ, and κ according to the requirement because these three quantities are strongly correlated with each other. No quantity can be modified without affecting at least any other quantity in a negative way. So first, we have to decouple these quantities and then modify them. Rapid improvement in the ZT (>1) value of TE materials has resulted from various newly invented strategies. The creation of nanostructured materials made it possible to improve TE performance tremendously.16–22 Materials in low dimensional forms, such as superlattices (2D structures), nanowires (1D structures), quantum dots (0D structures), etc., can achieve a considerably higher ZT, that was beyond expectations from their traditional bulk forms. The new variable of length scale comes into play with a reduction in the dimensionality of the material, which effectively controls materials' property.18 Thermoelectric properties of traditional bulk materials have been improved by introducing the distribution of nanoscale constituents in the entire sample. This way, to achieve better TE applications, low-dimensional and bulk materials were brought together.17,18 Not all nanostructured materials are efficiently applicable for large-scale commercial uses as they are expensive and slow to be fabricated or can usually be fabricated in a small quantity. Among various nanostructured materials, bulk nanostructured materials can be fabricated via bulk processes rather than those costly and slow nanofabrication processes and are most promising for large-scale commercial purposes.21

Not only inorganic but also organic TE materials are very popular nowadays. The ZT for organic TE materials [polyaniline (PANI), polyacetylene (PA), polypyrrole (PPy), polylactic acid (PLA), polythiophene (PTH), poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT), poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiohene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS), etc.] are usually lower than that for inorganic TE materials due to their low electrical conductivity, Seebeck coefficient, and PF. Doped, dedoped organic materials and organic/inorganic nanocomposite materials23–27 show remarkable TE performance. Bi2Te3,25,26 Bi2Se3,24etc., are efficient inorganic compounds used in organic–inorganic composite materials for TE applications. Organic TE materials have the advantage of low density, lightweight, flexibility, low κlattice and can easily be fabricated on a large scale. On the other hand, inorganic TE materials have a high Seebeck coefficient and electric conductivity. To take advantage of both classes of materials, “hybrid organic–inorganic thermoelectrics” is introduced in the field of TE materials.

Among inorganic metal chalcogenides, the IV–VI metal (Pb, Sn, Ge) chalcogenide materials have some inherent properties that make them suitable for TE applications. Firstly, metal chalcogenide materials have higher atomic weight, which is essential to reduce thermal conductivity. Secondly, the less covalent nature of the bonds arising from low electronegativity increases the PF. Thirdly, these materials can be doped easily and made n-type or p-type according to the need, which is essential to use the materials in TEGs or TECs. Fourthly, these materials can be formed in different structural forms and dimensions. These inherent properties open ample scope for improving their TE performance using various modern and traditional strategies. For the past few decades, Pb-chalcogenides have been among the best representatives of inorganic TE materials for TE applications in mid and high-temperature regions. Still, environmental concerns due to the toxicity of Pb are shrinking their large-scale usage. So, the usage of Pb-free metal chalcogenides in various fields, including electrodes, LEDs, solar cells, memory devices, TE devices, etc., is increasing day by day. Sn- and Ge-chalcogenides (SnTe, SnSe, SnS, GeTe, GeSe, GeS) belong to the same class of (IV–VI) Pb-chalcogenides and have already been proved to be promising TE materials. Tellurium (Te) is the heaviest non-radioactive representative among the chalcogens, and selenium (Se) stands next. Among non-radioactive chalcogens, Te has the least electronegativity (2.1, Pauling scale), and then comes Se (2.4, Pauling scale). Lesser ionic bonds for tellurides and selenides lead to enhanced carrier mobility that helps to achieve high electrical conductivity. Reduction in thermal conductivity and increment of electrical conductivity is advantageous to improve thermoelectric performance. However, the toxicity of Te/Se would be a matter to investigate for their practical application in devices. Many excellent recent reviews reported until now focused on the TE and other properties of any particular metal chalcogenide material or the overall progress of TE materials.28–31 In this review, we aim to provide an overall view on the various novel and traditional strategies to improve TE performance of materials and the outcomes of applying those strategies on the tin (Sn) and germanium (Ge) chalcogenides to improve their TE performance. From the comprehensive study, we predicted some new possible routes to improve the TE properties of those materials further and more effectively use them in green energy generation.

The general strategies to improve the TE performance will be discussed in two parts. The first part will contain strategies to increase the power factor, and the later one will include strategies to decrease the thermal conductivity.

Resonant levels are formed when the CB or VB of the host semiconductor resonates with one energy level of a localized atom in a semiconductor matrix.42 But resonant-state formation by impurities also has a negative impact on σ, because impurities remarkably reduce the carrier mobility due to additional carrier scattering.

However, distortion in DOS is an effective way to enhance the Seebeck coefficient. Different mechanisms to distort DOS are schematically represented in Fig. 5. The formation of resonant impurity levels distorts the DOS effectively. The presence of impurities can heavily modify the TE properties of a material.44–48 If there are quasi-local states called “resonant levels” of impurity atoms in any allowed energy band of the host material, some extraordinary phenomena can occur. Firstly, carriers can jump on those quasi-local states from the band. After residing there for some time, the carriers ejected back to the band with a different momentum; this is called ‘Resonant scattering’. The finite lifetime of the carriers in the impurity state broadens the energy level (though the broadening may be caused by different reasons also).7 So, the carriers with energies close to the level will be predominantly scattered, making the resonant scattering relaxation time strongly energy-dependent if the broadening is not too significant. This increased energy dependence can increase or decrease τ-and hence S-accordingly.

2.5 ‘Diffusion of carriers’ and ‘phonon-drag’

‘Diffusion of carriers’ and ‘phonon-drag’ both enhance a particular material's Seebeck coefficient. Over the past few decades, the ZT value of TE materials has been successfully enhanced above 300 K. Still, a relatively small Seebeck coefficient and high thermal conductivity at low temperatures resist that enhancement of ZT. ‘Phonon-drag’ is dominant at lower temperatures and is weak at higher carrier concentrations. In the expressions for the Seebeck coefficient, the effect of phonons flowing between the two ends is not considered. But the fact is, phonons travel from the hot side to the cold side due to the temperature gradient between the hot and cold ends. During this travel, phonons and charge carriers would interact, and part of the momentum from phonons will transfer to the charge carrier. The charge carriers would gain extra momentum from phonons in the forward direction and travel from the hot side to the cold side by contributing to extra current to be balanced out by the generated electric field and an increased Seebeck coefficient. Phonon-drag increases the effective mass of carriers (electrons or holes) due to interactions with the crystal lattice through which the carrier moves. This effect leads to a decrease in carrier mobility, increase in S and a reduced κ. Phonons contributing to phonon-drag are with longer mean free path and lower frequency. Still, phonons with a shorter mean free path and higher frequency contribute more to thermal conductivity than phonon-drag.7,33,56–61 The ‘Phonon-drag’ effect is significant only in materials where phonons are not strongly scattered and have long mean free paths and the strong electron–phonon interaction.99 Phonon-drag has a more significant contribution from the longitudinal phonon modes than the transverse modes due to the greater group velocities in the former mode. But overall, the diffusion of carriers is the most common process that contributes to the Seebeck coefficient of material and dominates at higher temperatures.56–61 First-principle computation of the phonon-drag effect was reported by Zhou et al.60 Fauziah et al.57 observed size dependency of the phonon-drag factor of the Seebeck coefficient due to enhancing the phonon-boundary scattering in micro/nanometer-scale Si wires. Applying linear response theory in the presence of impurity band, beyond Boltzmann transport regime, Matsuura et al.56 studied the effects of phonon-drag on resistivity and the Seebeck coefficient of the FeSb2 sample.

The use of numerous interfaces (2D) is to scatter phonons more effectively than electrons and to scatter, preferably those phonons that contribute most strongly to the thermal conductivity, not those contributing to phonon-drag. For any material κlattice reaches its lowest value at the amorphous phase with the same chemical composition, which is the “amorphous limit”, but it is very challenging to reach that point or beyond it. “Superlattice” structures are specially designed to eliminate this problem and are usually layered phononic materials. Two strategies were found that reduce κlattice remarkably in low dimension materials. 2.7 Some other strategies to improve PF

Energy filtering83–89,112 and charge density wave formation86,87,113 are other strategies to increase the PF[S2σ]. Carriers with low kinetic energies get stopped by the energy barrier generated by introducing nanometric precipitates at nanoprecipitate phase boundaries, but high-energy electrons pass through them; this mechanism is named energy filtering. Kim et al.90 reported experimental evidence of such carrier energy filtering effect in Te-nanoparticle-dispersed Bi0.5Sb1.5Te3 films. They observed that S-increased significantly from 169 μV K−1 to 248 μV K−1 when Te-nanoparticles of 15 nm diameter were increased up to 15 vol%. Though this increment in S-was accompanied by a decrement in σ, the overall PF was improved by 30%. Charge density wave (CDW), caused by the local lattice distortion, is a low-dimensional transport phenomenon involving strong electron–phonon coupling and reduces lattice thermal conductivity by breaking the translational symmetry of lattices. This method was investigated on CuI-doped Bi2Te2.1Se0.9 compounds.87 Tian et al.112 reported, nanoscale/microscale Ag precipitates in Ag-plated SnTe bulk introduce Ag/SnTe interfaces to enhance the Seebeck coefficient via energy filtering effect.

3.1 Reduction of specific heat (CV) of the material

According to classical statistics CV per atom is 3 kB for all solids at all temperatures (Dulong–Petit's law). But later it was concluded that CV approaches Dulong–Petit's limit in solids at high temperatures only. So specific heat is a built-in property of a material and is difficult to manipulate. But the incorporation of liquid nature in a crystalline solid is helpful to modify their CV. Liquids restrict the propagation of transverse mechanical waves, and thus contribution from transverse phonon mode to the overall specific heat gets reduced, thereby leading to a significant reduction in CV. Many elemental, as well as complex liquids with N-atoms, show that their CV, decreases with temperature, from about 3N kB at the melting point to (2–2.5)N kB at high temperature.114,115 Copper chalcogenide materials at high temperatures exhibit superionic cubic phases, in which Cu-atoms flow through the rigid framework of chalcogen atoms. Liquid-like behavior [“phonon-liquid electron-crystal” (PLEC)] of Cu-chalcogenides provided a route to reduce CV below Dulong–Petit's limit even at high temperature. Liu et al.8 achieved ZT ∼ 1.5 at 1000 K in the Cu2Se sample. In this case, Se-atoms form an FCC rigid sublattice and provide an effective pathway for charge carrier transport. On the other hand, Cu-ions' sublattices are highly disordered around the Se-sublattice. At high temperatures, Cu-ions can travel among the interstitial sites, exhibiting a liquid-like character and results κ < 0.1 Wm−1 K−1. He et al.9 reported that a reduction in CV of lattice vibrations is more prominent in Cu2S than in Cu2Se. As a result, κ < 0.6 Wm−1 K−1 in the whole temperature range and a maximum ZT ∼ 1.7 at 1000 K was achieved. ZT ∼ 1.1 at 1000 K was achieved in high-density Cu2Te samples synthesized via direct annealing without a sintering process.10 Reduction in lattice thermal conductivity was observed in other materials with liquid-like nature like Li0.09Cu1.9Se,11 Cu5FeS4,12 Cu7PSe6,13 CuCrSe2,14 Cu1.94Se0.5S0.5,15 AgCrSe2,116etc. Roychowdhury et al.117 reported that scattering of heat-carrying acoustic phonon modes by soft phonons causes ultralow κlattice ∼ 0.35 Wm−1 K−1 and ZT ∼ 1.6 at 670 K in AgCuTe. 3.2.1.1 Point defects. Point defects affect the transport properties of the crystal lattice at a length scale of interatomic distance. The strong vacancy phonon scattering between Sn-atoms and lattice vacancies enables a significant suppression of phonon propagation when combined with the scattering from In-rich nano-precipitates.121 Enhancement of phonon scattering without any detrimental influence on electronic transport by Cu-interstitials causes low κlattice ∼ 0.4 Wm−1 K−1 in SnTe–GeTe–MnTe alloys.75
3.2.1.2 Line defects. The density and position of the dislocation in a material can affect phonon transport significantly. An increase in dislocation density in TE materials can be achieved by plastic deformation, liquid-phase sintering, clustering of vacancies or interstitials in the grain of a single-phase material, etc. Wu et al.122 explained the evolution and migration at Grain Boundaries in SnTe. Sn0.995In0.005Te samples were synthesized by three different methods [water quenched ingot as cast (IAC), hand-milled + spark plasma sintered pellet (HMS), and ball milled + spark plasma sintered pellet (BMS)]. Dislocations were observed to migrate from the interiors of the grains in the IAC sample and form arrays at grain boundaries in the HMS and BMS samples. Characterization and model simulation suggests that the in-grain dislocations have less impact on heat-carrying phonon scattering than dislocation arrays at the grain boundaries. The in-grain dislocation is more efficient than dislocation arrays at the grain boundaries for charge carrier scattering. Guo et al.97 synthesized SnTe–MnTe alloys by annealing and hot pressing. In this case, the high-density dislocation cores were observed in Sn0.94Mn0.09Te. High-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image and its inverse fast Fourier transform (IFFT) images show several dislocation cores inside. 3D strain clusters found in the material should be dislocation clusters associated with Mn-interstitial atoms, which induces clustered strain fields. Enhancement in the lattice strain causes a reduction in the κlattice. Dislocations-induced lattice strain can locally change the phonon frequencies and reduce the group velocity of phonons. Kim et al.123 achieved sufficiently low κlattice in Bi0.5Sb1.5Te3 sample by producing the periodic dislocations at low-energy grain boundaries. They synthesized the sample using a liquid-phase compaction process. But the synthesis technique was a bit different from the traditional liquid-phase sintering process and included applied pressure and transient flow of the liquid phase during compaction because traditional sintering techniques also accompany a reduction in s. Finally, they achieved κlattice ∼ 0.33 Wm−1 K−1 at 320 K.
3.2.1.3 Plane defects. Low-frequency phonons are in the target region of interface scattering. Since the heat-carrying phonons usually have low frequencies at low temperatures, so interface scattering is more effective for reducing κlattice at low temperatures. Dense interfaces can be created in a material by introducing nanoscale precipitates, nanocomposites or reducing the grain size of materials. GeTe/Bi2Te3 superlattice-like materials are promising systems where plane defects can successfully reduce κlattice. Lower value of κlattice ∼ 0.162 Wm−1 K−1 was achieved due to the effects of interfaces and Bi atoms in Bi2Te3.124 Alloying Bi2Te3 in GeTe can generate vast Ge-vacancies in the GeTe lattice.125 Through proper heat treatment, Ge-vacancies produce nanoscale van der Waals gaps, and these vacancy gaps induce nanoscale ferroelectric domains with 180° domain walls. Finally, a hierarchical “ferroelectric” domain structure arises that was found beneficial to reduce κlattice.125 Grain boundaries, nanoscale precipitates, stacking faults caused low κlattice in the Ge0.89Sb0.1In0.01Te sample.126 The presence of grain boundary and stacking faults play an essential role in the reduction of κlattice of Cd/Bi co-doped GeTe.127 Ball milling, melt spinning, spark plasma sintering can effectively decrease the grain size and increase the density of the interface. The presence of grain boundary and the Si/Ge dopants can effectively scatter the phonons and reduce κlatticein 2D-SnSe sample.225

Bonding heterogeneity and lone-pair induced anharmonicity can cause ultralow thermal conductivity. During thermal vibration, interactions between nonbonding lone-pair electrons and bonding electrons of the neighboring atoms due to the overlapping wave functions occur. These overlaps induce nonlinear electrostatic repulsive forces, which resist lattice vibration. As a result, the outermost electron shell containing a lone pair of electrons gets deformed largely by lattice vibrations. Thus strong anharmonicity comes into play due to remarkable change in nonlinear terms in total energy associated with the crystal.131,140–143 Group-V elements As, Sb, Bi have s2-lone-pair. Stereo-chemically active lone-pairs can cause anharmonicity in lattice and lower the thermal conductivity of solids. Nielsen et al.142 theoretically and experimentally found that presence of group-V elements in the whole class of simple high-symmetry crystalline solids of composition ABX2 [A = Cu, Ag, Au, Na, K, Rb, Cs; B= As, Sb, Bi; and x= S, Se, Te] generates highly anharmonic acoustic phonons. The s-electron shell of the group-V elements can be easily deformed by lattice vibrations and leads to a strong anharmonicity. Anharmonicity in some of the large-grained polycrystals like AgSbSe2, AgBiSe2, Ag0.73Sb1.12Te2, NaSbSe2, NaBiTe2, and NaSbTe2 can be so strong that phonon scattering reduces κlattice to the amorphous limit. According to Skoug et al.,140 Cu3SbSe4, CuSbSe2, and Cu3SbSe3 exhibit different Se–Sb–Se angles. Mutual interaction between the 5s lone pair electrons of Sb and the adjacent valence electrons during thermal agitation leads to large lattice anharmonicity in the lattice. In the case of AgPbBiSe3, lone pairs of Bi and Pb-atoms strengthen their bonds with Se by off-centering and weaken Ag–Se bonds.144 A combination of chemical and structural heterogeneity arises at atomic scales that reduce τ of acoustic phonon scattering and hence reduce κlattice.144 Comparative study on κlattice of different Ga and In-monochalcogenides by Nissimagoudar et al.143 reveals that anharmonic parameters play a more vital role than harmonic parameters in these monolayers. As in this case, GaS (InS) has a higher κlattice than GaTe(InTe) and GaSe(InSe) it can be concludes that anharmonic scattering rate in GaS(InS) is lower than that in GaSe(InSe) and GaTe(InTe). But the actual picture turned out to be completely different. GaS, InS were found to have a higher anharmonic scattering rate than other group-13 monochalcogenides because large anharmonic interatomic force constants (IFCs) are associated with strong interaction between nonbonding lone-pair s electrons and bonding electrons of adjacent Ga/In atoms. In SnS0.5Se0.5, 5s2 lone-pairs of Sn are more localized than in SnS and strengthen the anisotropy of Fermi surface and lattice anharmonicity.145

Generally, strong anharmonicity is observed in materials with a distorted crystal structure. Still, there are so many highly symmetric crystal structures like SnTe, PbTe, Bi2Te3 (rock-salt structure), etc., that also exhibit strong anharmonicity.135,136,146 Structural instability, softening of transverse optical phonon modes, and long-range interatomic interaction due to the presence of resonant bonds contribute to large lattice anharmonicity and strong phonon–phonon scattering in materials having symmetric crystal structure.117,146–149 Long-range interaction along 〈100〉 direction was observed in rock-salt structured materials due to resonant bonding.136,150,151 The importance of this interaction decreases with increasing distortion of the crystal structures. Unlike Coulomb interaction, the strength of long-range interaction does not decrease monotonously with increasing atomic distance, and instead, the strength fluctuates in a pretty irregular manner. Long-range interaction induces near ferroelectric instability in IV–VI metal chalcogenides.136,152 Ferroelectric instability softens optical phonon modes. Soft phonon modes help to reduce κlattice by introducing strong anharmonic scattering and a large scattering phase space volume.151 Using inelastic neutron scattering (INS) and first-principles simulations, Li et al.149 confirmed strong anisotropic nature and giant anharmonicity of phonons in SnSe. They identified that strong anharmonicity in SnSe originates from a bonding instability from the long-range resonant p-bond network of Se-atoms, coupled to stereo-chemically active lone-pair of Sn atom.

Solids possess 3N vibrational modes, among which 3 modes are acoustic, and 3(N–1) are optical.115 The optical modes store most thermal energy, but they contribute little to heat conduction due to their low group velocity. In addition, the heat-carrying acoustic modes tend to interact with and are suppressed by the optical branches.154 An increase in ‘N’- means an increase in crystal's complexity and maybe a larger unit cell. So complex crystals increase the number of optical modes that, in turn, reduces κlattice. Phonon–phonon scattering is a consequence of the anharmonicity of the chemical bond. Acoustical to optical phonon scattering effectively can reduce κlattice. From the dispersion relation of optical and acoustic phonon branches, we notice a “frequency gap” between them at the BZ boundary, which reduces with a reduction in mass ratio between the constituent atoms of the unit cell, as shown in Fig. 12. That lattice restricts the propagation of any vibrational mode that falls within that gap. The extent of the frequency gap between the acoustic and optical branches regulates the Umklapp scattering rate. A small gap size corresponds to the high probability of longer wavelength phonons taking part in the Umklapp scattering process. That means a small frequency gap leads to higher group velocity (longer wavelength) phonons scattered into the optical phonons, and optical phonons have negligible impact on the thermal conduction as they have near-zero group velocity .155,156 From INS measurements and first-principles computations of the phonons, Delaire et al.146 identified strong anharmonic coupling between the transverse optic mode and the longitudinal acoustic modes of phonon in PbTe is the cause of its low thermal conductivity though having a simple rock-salt type crystal structure. According to Tian et al.,147 the softening of optical phonon modes plays an essential role in the thermal conduction of bulk PbSe and PbTe and serves as important scattering channels for acoustic phonons. In a recently reported work, Wu et al.157 theoretically showed that coupling of spin–orbit interaction with phonon anharmonicity has a significant effect on κlattice of SnSe.

Another source of anharmonicity is the presence of “Rattling modes” that were first found in materials with cage-like structures such as clathrates,158,159 skutterudites,160 and cobaltates.161 Materials with open crystallographic positions that can serve as an efficient “host” for inserted atoms are weakly bonded with the basic structural frame. As a result, they can vibrate around the central position (known as rattlers). In most of the bulk materials, the optical phonons have a lesser contribution in lattice thermal conductivity. When the system size reaches the nanoscale, the contribution of optical phonons in lattice thermal conductivity increases.162 But contribution in thermal conduction by acoustic phonon modes cannot be entirely excluded by introducing structural complexity only. Resonant phonon modes originated from “rattling” atoms, or atom clusters can reduce the velocity of the remaining acoustic phonons.154 In resonant bonding, coupling between optical and acoustic phonon modes happens mainly due to the softening of the optical branch, but according to the rattling model, both types of modes get softened.150 The Skutterudite system consists of voids in the structure. Filling those voids with the suitable type of atoms yields a remarkable reduction in the κlattice. Because atoms residing in the void space “rattle” there substantially affect the phonon propagation through the lattice.163 Clathrates emphasize the spatial separation of two substructures known as host clathrate framework and rattling guests. The host framework eases the transport of charge carriers and enhances PF, whereas the rattling of guests inside oversized cages of the host framework reduces κlattice by introducing scattering of acoustic phonons or reducing phonon group velocity.158,159 According to further research works, not only caged materials exhibit rattling modes. Without the presence of an oversized cage in LaOBiS2−xSex, “planar rattling” motions of Bi atoms can scatter phonon-like rattling in the case of caged compounds.164 NaxCoO2,161 CuP2,165 AgIn5S8,166 CsAg5Te3, AgBi3S5, BiCuSeO, Cu3SbSe3, Zintl tellurides such as TlInTe2 and InTe,117etc. also shows low κlattice originated from the presence of “rattling modes”. Jana et al.141 reported ultralow κlattice in mixed (In+/In3+) valent InTe. The rattling In+ cation with 5s2 lone-pairs leads to the generation of strongly anharmonic phonons, which couple with the heat-carrying acoustic phonon modes and lead to an ultralow κlattice.

3.3 Reduction of phonon group velocity

An effective strategy to reduce the κlattice, regardless of other scattering mechanisms present, is to slow down heat conduction by phonons lowering their group velocity. To discuss the strategies for reducing phonon group velocity, we take a simplified picture of a lattice. Where successive atoms with an average mass is connected by a spring (chemical bonds) of length a and restoring force constant is K (strength of chemical bond), Phonon group velocity (v) is 115 The expression of phonon group velocity indicates that weak restoring force, as well as the heavy atomic mass of the constituent elements, are advantageous to reduce v. A more detailed discussion leads to the concept of low phonon group velocity or low sound velocity in materials with global or local soft bonds. Global soft bonds reduce group velocity overall, while local soft bonds induce low-frequency optical modes that interact locally with high-frequency acoustic modes.167 Ying et al.168 proposed α-MgAgSb as promising TE material in low-temperature range (300–550 K) and predicted coexistence of global and local weak chemical bonds to be the origin of the intrinsically low κlattice. In this case, Mg–Ag–Sb bonds in α-MgAgSb vibrate locally and induce low-frequency optical phonons, resulting in “rattling-like” thermal damping to further reduce the κlattice. Additionally, valence electron count (VEC) = 8 for α-MgAgSb causes its weak bonding nature. The low VEC directly leads to weak interatomic interactions and global chemical bonding. It also causes the transformation to the low symmetry phase, which possesses three-center bonds and weak local chemical bonding. Low phonon velocity ∼ 1921 m S−1 and low κlattice ∼ 0.6 Wm−1 K−1 at room temperature was reported for α-MgAgSb. In the case of clathrates, the rattling of guest atoms is the main reason for reducing κlattice. There are some features of clathrates that reduce κlattice, e.g., vacancy formation, formation of superstructures, mass alternation, etc. Vacancy formation within the clathrate framework makes it less rigid, leading to a reduction in phonon group velocity. The formation of superstructures gives rise to high unit volumes causes less coordinated vibrations of framework atoms. Mass alternation within guest substructure alters phonon mean free path without affecting individual rattling modes. Mainly, rattling modes lower phonon group velocity because of avoided crossing acoustic modes or resonant scattering of phonons.159 Such an avoided crossing effect has been observed in Ba8Ga16Ge30 clathrate, where the presence of guest Ba-atom in the cages largely reduces the group velocity of high-frequency acoustic phonons.169 GeTe/PbTe co-alloying leads to significant lattice softening by intense internal stains, resulting from high-density dislocations at the grain boundaries. Xie et al.98 measured sound velocities through SnTe, Sn0.8Ge0.1Pb0.1Te, Sn0.6Ge0.2Pb0.2Te and Sn0.5Ge0.25Pb0.25Te samples to be 1946 m S−1, 1828 m S−1, 1670 m S−1 and 1500 m S−1 respectively. Reduction in sound velocity with increasing proportion co-alloying agents is a sign of lattice softening. The lattice softening leads to a low lattice thermal conductivity. In/Mn co-doping softens chemical bonds in SnTe. Softened chemical bonds lead to lower κlattice. As a result of In/Mn co-doping, ZT = 1.03 was achieved in Sn0.89In0.01Mn0.1Te at 923 K.171 Se- substitution caused larger atomic mass and smaller force constants and, therefore, low phonon group velocity for SnS0.5Se0.5.145

3.4 Nanostructure engineering

Nanostructure engineering, such as introducing quantum dots, quantum wells, nanowires, nanotubes, and thin films, has been widely utilized in TE materials to reduce the κlattice. Nanostructures can scatter medium-wavelength phonons and improve TE properties. Nanostructuring in bulk materials was first implemented in AgPbmSbTe2+m (LAST-m) system.17κlattice of PbS can be significantly reduced by introducing nanostructured second phases. Doping with SrS and CaS form a mixture of a nanostructured/solid solution material. Nanoscale precipitates and point defects both play an important role in reducing the κlattice.172 When the nanoprecipitates are coherently embedded in the matrix, it is called endotaxial nanostructures. Tan et al.208 reported that endotaxial CdS or ZnS nanoscale precipitates reduce κlattice of SnCd0.03Te remarkably with no effect on the PF. Sootsman et al.173 prepared bulk PbTe with nano inclusions of Sb, Bi, and InSb embedded in the matrix, which led to remarkably reduced κlattice. Low κlattice ∼ 1.1 Wm−1 K−1 due to collective phonon scattering from mesostructured grain boundaries, nanostructured precipitates, nanoscale defect layers, and solid solution point defects was achieved in Ge0.90Bi0.10Te.174 Nanostructured In-doped SnTe,175 nanostructured p-type Gex(SnyPb1−y)1−xTe alloys,176 (GeTe)75(AgSbTe2)y(AgSbSe2)x,177 TAGS-materials,178 solid solution series of Ge12In2Te15 and Ge12Sb2Te15 also show promising TE performance due to various nanostructuring.179

3.5 All-scale hierarchical architectures

The periodic arrangement of atoms in crystalline lattice results in periodic dependence of frequency ω() on the wave vector . Different types of waves of atoms' displacements can propagate in crystals, which can differ in the motions of atoms within one crystal cell. Consequently, several types of phonons in crystals are characterized by a specific dependence of energy on momentum. Recently, hierarchical phonon scattering has been suggestive of being effective in reducing the κlattice and hence, enhancing ZT of heavy-band TE materials.180 Hierarchical phonon scattering centers scatter the phonons with different frequencies. The hierarchical scattering centers should be at a shorter distance than the phonon mean free path (lph) but at a longer distance than the carrier mean free path (lc) so that phonons are more strongly scattered than charge carriers. The sub-microscale grain boundaries usually target low-frequency phonons, while more atomic-scale point defects can be designed to further enhance the scattering of high-frequency phonons. 4. Recent strategies to search for highly efficient TE materials

A simple model based on effective mass approximation predicts that inelastic scattering in 2D semiconducting materials with step-like DOS can ensure a high power factor.190 The inelastic processes delay the onset of scattering and maximize the utilization of the Fermi window for transport. The simulations showed that the materials with dominant inelastic processes, specifically when optical phonon energy Eph ∼ 5 kBT, improve the power factor. Thus, the material selection rules formulated in such a manner may trigger the researchers to predict and preselect 2D materials, thereby reducing the time and resources in the involved processes. Another crucial factor is atomic disorder, which is present in most high-performance TE materials, and plays an essential role in modulating electronic and thermal transport. The disorder-induced electronic localization has contradictory behavior on σ and S. As the localization increases with increasing disorder, σ-decreases, whereas S-increases Fig. 14. Effectively tuning the disorders can lead to a high PF. Recently, Roychowdhury et al.191 have established the positive effect of the disorder tuning on electronic and phonon transports, simultaneously optimized toward high thermoelectric performance in Cd-doped polycrystalline AgSbTe2. Doing Cd in AgSbTe2 enhances cationic ordering mainly. Enhanced cationic ordering improves electronic properties due to increased localization of electronic states and reduces κlattice by introducing nanoscale superstructures and coupling soft vibrations localized within nanometer range around dopant sites with local strain modulation. They finally achieved ZT ∼ 1.5 near 300 K and a maximum ZT ∼ 2.6 at 573 K in 6 mol% Cd-doped AgSbTe2. Tang et al.192 reported remarkable improvement in TE performance of Sn1/3Pb1/3Ge1/3Te solid solution due to a reduction in κlattice caused by massive disordered cations in this system. The cationic disorder introduced significant mass/strain contrasts for broadening the phonon dispersion and low κlattice was achieved for the entire temperature range. Organic–inorganic composite materials are also promising for TE application. Ju et al.193 prepared SnSe Nanosheets (NSs) by hydrothermal lithium intercalation combined with an exfoliation process from SnSe ingots and then fabricated SnSe NS/PEDOT: PSS composites. 5. Sn chalcogenides as TE materials

The 16th group elements in the periodic table are usually known as chalcogens. Compounds containing a chalcogen element as an anion and any metal as a cation are commonly called metal chalcogenides. In this review, we will discuss the improvement of TE performance of Tin chalcogenides (SnTe, SnSe, SnS) and Germanium chalcogenides (GeTe, GeSe, GeS) due to the application of various traditional and novel strategies that we have discussed in previous sections. 5.1 SnTe-based materials

5.1.1 Crystal structure and general transport properties of SnTe. SnTe is an extrinsic (p-type) semiconductor. Among chalcogenides of Sn, only SnTe has a rocksalt crystal structure (Fmm) and isotropic thermoelectric properties.194,195 The rocksalt crystal structure of SnTe is shown in Fig. 15a. SnTe has a ferroelectric rhombohedral (R3m) phase which transit to the cubic (Fmm) phase at a carrier concentration-dependent transition temperature, roughly ∼100 K.194 Knox et al.196 reported that SnTe exhibits a crossover to an “emphanitic phase” above 300 K where the sample becomes locally distorted (Sn-off centering), keeping the global symmetry as cubic. SnTe has topological crystalline insulating properties.197–199 Two alternative models have been proposed for the band structure of SnTe.170 The first one is the two-valence-band model (suggested by Allgaier and Scheie).,200 and the second one is the semimetal model (proposed by Sagar and Miller).201 SnTe has a high concentration of Sn-vacancies, and those vacancies act as acceptors. The carrier concentration normally ranges from 1020 cm−3 to 1022 cm−3.202,203 Because of the high concentrations of Sn vacancies, phonons are scattered both by three-phonon Umklapp processes and by the Sn vacancies, this causes κlattice-to vary with temperature as T−1/2 instead of T−1.202 High carrier concentration results in high κlattice ∼ 2.88 Wm−1 K−1 but ultimately Seebeck coefficient reduces and results in a poor ZT (∼0.5 at 873 K).184 At room temperature S-does not decrease with an increase in hole concentration (n) as it generally should be. Instead, it increases from 5–8 μV deg−1 at n = 1–2 × 1020 cm−3 to a maximum of 34 μV deg−1 at n = 8 × 1020 cm−3, after which it decreases to 20.5 μV deg−1 at n = 1.8 × 1021 cm−3 and the maximum value gradually disappears with increasing temperature. At comparatively higher temperature (673–723 K) S-decreases gradually with increasing hole concentration, due to the presence of two nondegenerate VBs [light VB (L) and heavy VB (Σ)].202 The direct band gap is tiny (0.18 eV at room temperature), as shown in Fig. 15b, which leads to a significant Bipolar transport of carriers. The energy difference between light and heavy VB is ∼0.3 eV at room temperature, resisting the heavier holes to contribute to the Seebeck coefficient.195 Under ambient conditions, n-type SnTe has better S-and ZT-than p-type SnTe. An increment in external pressure on n-type SnTe causes an enhancement of its TE performance up to a maximum of 5 GPa then degrades afterward.204 Peng et al. reported the synthesis of n-type SnTe through Pb/I doping. Pb-helped to suppress the high vacancy concentration of SnTe and its electrical transport property was optimized by I-doping.203 Ferroelectricity arises in non-centrosymmetric crystal structures. A local ferromagnetic ordering has been reported above room temperature in rock salt SnTe (centrosymmetric).205 It was concluded that there are two reasons behind this anomaly. One is the local structural distortion due to Sn-off centering, and another is due to the existence of rhombohedral strain in the cubic phase of SnTe. Chang et al.206 thinned SnTe up to 1 unit cell and found a surprising rise in the ferroelectric transition temperature from <100 K in bulk SnTe to 270 K in this ultrathin SnTe film.

5.2 SnSe-based materials

5.2.1 Crystal structure and general transport properties of SnSe. SnSe-based materials are applicable as optoelectronic,246,247 photovoltaic, phase change memory alloys, and in TE devices. Unlike cubic SnTe, SnSe possesses a layered orthorhombic (a ≠ b ≠ c, α = β = γ = 90°) crystal structure (space group Pnma) at room temperature.248–250 Crystal structure in this phase can be derived from a three-dimensional distortion of the rock-salt crystal structure, as shown in Fig. 17a–c.
Fig. 17 Crystal structure of SnSe at Pnma phase (a) projection along c-axis, (b) projection along b-axis, (c) projection along a-axis (data taken from ref. 248). (d) Coordination polyhedron of Sn-in Pnma phase up to 4.1 Å.

Each atom in this phase has the coordination environment of a heavily distorted octahedron, and the lattice is deformed NaCl type and shows anisotropic TE properties and strong phononic anharmonicity.137 SnSe is an intrinsic p-type semiconductor having an indirect band gap of ∼0.9 eV and a direct band gap of ∼1.3 eV.251,252 The lattice parameters reported for the Pnma phase are a ≈ 11.501 Å, b ≈ 4.153 Å, and c ≈ 4.445 Å.248 The band structure of SnSe is very complex. From reported DFT calculation for the Pnma phase shows that the energy difference between the first and second VB is ∼0.06 eV while between the first and third is ∼0.13 eV. From the same DFT calculation, the effective mass on different VB was estimated to be for the first VB band and for the second VB. So it is clear that the second VB is heavier than the first VB.253 Each cell contains two double layers perpendicular to the longest axis (here a-axis). Each layer is formed by zigzag planes of Sn–Se chains extending in two dimensions. The double-layer is held by a network of covalent bonds, whereas the interaction between two double layers is weak. The bonding between the layers is weak van der Waals type. The easy cleavage in this system is along with the (l00) planes. At the Pnma phase, each Sn atom shares six different types of interactions with its neighboring atoms (seven Se atoms and two Sn atoms and vice versa for the neighboring of each Se atom). Due to lone-pair, the structure contains highly distorted SnSe7 coordination polyhedra (containing three short and four very long Sn–Se bonds) due to lone-pair.254 Two types of interaction among those are strong. They involve short Sn–Se distances (2.7–2.8 Å) as d1, d2 (twice), two interactions involve intermediate Sn–Se distances (3.3–3.4 Å) as d3 (twice), d4, one interaction of distance (3.5 Å) as d5 (twice) between Sn–Sn contacts and an additional Sn–Se interaction of distance (4.1 Å) as d6, as shown in Fig. 17d.

As temperature increases, a continuous displacive structural phase transition occurs towards the Cmcm phase, spreading over 200 K below the critical temperature of 807 K. Around 807 K SnSe enters a five-fold coordinated Cmcm phase, as shown in Fig. 18a–c. Lattice parameters in this phase are a ≈ 4.310 Å, b ≈ 11.705 Å, c ≈ 4.318 Å, and the crystal structure has higher symmetry.250,255 From the first principle calculation, Dewandre et al.256 reported that this Cmcm-phase is only stabilized when the anharmonicity is considered, and the origin of enhancement of carrier concentration and the nature of the phase transition was also explained. Using first-principle calculation and Boltzmann transport equation, Wu et al.157 studied SnSe and found that spin–orbit coupling (SOC) significantly influences phonon anharmonicity. SOC-weakens phonon anharmonicity and reduces the delocalization of the resonant bonding network. As a result κlattice is enhanced up to ∼60%. In the Cmcm phase, the atoms form double layers similar to those in the Pnma phase. Each atom is now coordinated to four neighboring atoms at an equal distance,254 but in each layer, all Sn–Se bonds are stacked parallel, and the stacking is continued perpendicular to the b axis along with the layer (with d2 = d3 and d4 = d6), as shown in Fig. 18d. Due to this structural phase change, SnSe is applicable as phase-change memory alloys. The anisotropy of the crystal structure shows an effect on the electronic and thermal transport properties. The complex crystal structure of SnSe is the leading cause of its low lattice thermal conductivity. Shi et al.257 calculated the Pnma phase to have an indirect band gap of ∼0.829 eV and Cmcm to have a direct band gap of ∼0.464 eV, as shown in Fig. 19. The significantly smaller band gap in the Cmcm phase leads to increased bipolar conduction at high temperatures, declining its Seebeck coefficient. SnSe and similar class compounds were previously ignored in thermoelectrics due to their low electrical conductivity and ZT values. The decrease in lattice thermal conductivity with increasing temperature along all crystallographic directions suggests that lattice anharmonicity is an important phonon scattering mechanism in this compound.138,149,258

At ambient conditions, SnSe crystallizes in the orthorhombic Pnma phase. Not only temperature but also externally applied pressure affects the phase transition of SnSe. In earlier times, Chattopadhyay et al.262 reported that there would be no phase transition if the external pressure is applied on SnSe up to 34Gpa. Still, later Alptekin reported a phase transition at 7 GPa from orthorhombic Pnma to Cmcm phase.263 Agarwal et al.264 observed a pressure-dependent electrical resistance and Seebeck coefficient and found the behavioral change of these parameters at 6.5 GPa. Loa et al.265 found evidence of second-order structural phase change towards a higher symmetry space group under the high external pressure of 10.5(3)GPa in SnSe. Later, de Souza et al.266 observed nanostructured SnSe samples under a pressure variation up to 18 GPa. The study of cell parameter variation and enthalpy calculation found a phase transition near 4 GPa. After 8.2 GPa up to 18 GPa, there was no sign of phase transition. Ghosh et al.,267 in 2016, theoretically investigated the effect of high pressure on fully-relaxed samples of SnSe and got 4 GPa as the critical pressure for structural phase transition. Additionally, they found that the resistivity is highest along a-axis and lowest along b-axis, although conductivity increases with increased pressure. The semimetallic nature followed by the orthorhombic → monoclinic structural transition of SnSe above 12.6 GPa was reported by Yan et al.268 That semiconducting-semimetallic transition brought an abrupt change in carrier concentration as well as mobility of the charge carriers. Later, Xie et al.269 reported a structural phase transition ∼8 GPa and a semiconductor to semimetal electronic transition followed by a Pnma to Cmcm structural transition ∼12 GPa. According to Yang et al.,270 there is a phase transition from Pnma to Cmcm at 22 GPa. With further increase in external pressure, various novel crystal structures of SnSe were found (Pmm, C2/m, and Cmmm), only Pnma phase is semiconductor among them, and other phases are metallic. Despite so many theoretical and experimental studies, the critical pressure to initiate the phase transition is still ambiguous; this may be due to inherent microstructures or defects in the system. Monolayer (2D) SnSe also attracted tremendous attention in recent years for its promising TE properties.

Hu et al.271 designed five allotropes (honeycomb structured) of monolayer SnSe, namely α-, β-, γ-, δ- and ε-SnSe. Except ε-SnSe other allotropes are indirect band gap semiconductors. Though TE properties of α-SnSe were reported before, using the ab initio DFT method, they found all the new allotropes (β, γ, δ) have even better TE properties. The predicted ZT value for the β-SnSe phase was ∼2.06 at 300 K, which was remarkable. Later Haq et al.272 reported their study on optoelectronic and photovoltaic applications of these honeycomb allotropes. TE properties of SnSe in the mid-temperature range can be further improved by applying a slight hydrostatic pressure or uniaxial stress. In 2016 Zhang et al.273 reported their work on improving TE performance of Pnma-SnSe in all three crystal directions by applying external pressure. They showed that below 700 K applying external pressure of 6 GPa, the ZT values of p-type materials could reach ∼2.5 and ∼1.7 along b- and c-directions respectively and at the same pressure and 600 K temperature n-type conduction resulted along a direction with a ZT value of ∼1.7 at 600 K. In 2017 Rehman et al.274 reported the change in structural and optical (near/mid-infrared region) properties of cubic-SnSe due to applying external pressure up to 40 GPa. According to the report, the structural parameters decrease gradually with increased pressure in the range of 0–40 GPa. For cubic-SnSe, the band gap showed direct nature at 0 GPa, indirect nature from 5 GPa to 30 GPa, and metallic nature from 35 GPa to 40 GPa. Application of pressure can reduce the Pnma to Cmcm phase transition temperature effectively. Yu et al.275 in 2016 noticed a reduced transition temperature to ∼500 K at 4 GPa hydrostatic pressure. Band gaps of both phases vary inversely with pressure, and at 8 GPa, the SnSe band gap vanishes. This external pressure reduces the Grüneisen parameter, and the maximum reduction takes place along a-axis. Hence, κlattice increases due to the increase in hydrostatic pressure. External pressure does not affect κel remarkably as its value in Pnma-phase was inherently very small. 4 GPa pressure cannot change κtotal significantly which is advantageous to increase ZT. Uniaxial stress > 3.5 GPa along c-axis also can cause Pnma to Cmcm structural phase transition. Another work by Gusmão et al.276 reported that ZT reduces due to the external pressure. They studied SnSe-Pnma crystals below 750 K temperature under P = 0, 4, 7, 10 GPa. Improvement in TE performance of single-crystal and polycrystalline SnSe based materials due to applying different strategies is discussed in the next section.

5.2.2 TE performance of SnSe based materials.
5.2.2.1 p-Type single-crystal SnSe. Single-crystal samples are advantageous than polycrystalline samples in some aspects. In single-crystal, the disturbance of grain boundary scattering and “texture” on the effects of dopants can be avoided. In a comparative study, Jin et al.277 reported a higher ZT ∼ 0.92 for single-crystal SnSe than ZT ∼ 0.5 for polycrystalline SnSe near 793 K. Agarwal et al.278 fabricated Single crystals of SnSe by a direct vapor transport and a chemical vapor transport method. They observed anisotropy in D.C. electrical resistivity for the first time in layered SnSe crystals. Shi et al.257 reported detailed band structure and various TE parameters of single-crystal SnSe. Later single-crystal anisotropic SnSe has attracted significant attention due to its record high value of ZT = 2.6 ± 0.3, 2.3 ± 0.3 and 0.8 ± 0.2 at 923 K (at Cmcm phase) measured along the b-, c- and a-axis respectively of the room-temperature Pnma-orthorhombic unit cell by Zhao et al.138 In that work, crystal parameters in the Pnma phase were taken as a = 11.49 Å, b = 4.44 Å, c = 4.135 Å, and in the Cmcm phase, those as a = 4.31 Å, b = 11.70 Å, c = 4.31 Å. Larger values of the Grüneisen parameter, i.e., strong anharmonicity in bonding, give rise to κlattice in ordered crystal structures, high carrier mobility, large Seebeck coefficient, and extremely κlattice along b- and c-axes leads to higher ZT values. The plot shows that the average Grüneisen parameter is the maximum along a-axis. In any direction κtot-decreases with increasing temperature, and at 973 K, all measured values were <0.4 Wm−1 K−1. Hole-doped SnSe is a promising thermoelectric material in the temperature range 300 K to 773 K. SnSe has non-parabolic and complex multiband valence states. Heavy p-type doping presses the Fermi level into the multiple VBs, makes them participate in the conduction process, and effectively enhances the Seebeck coefficient. The Seebeck coefficient increases from ∼+160 μV K−1 to ∼+300 μV K−1, electrical conductivity decreases from 1486 S cm−1 to 148 S cm−1 and PF also decreases from ∼40 μW cm−1 to ∼14 μW cm−1 as temperature increases from 300 K to 773 K. The highest ZT ∼ 1.34 was achieved in a device application with the hot side at 773 K and the cold side at 300 K.253 Hole doping in single-crystal of SnSe by using Na atoms as acceptors shows an increment in hole concentration up to two orders of magnitude and ZT up to ∼0.7 along the b-axis at 300 K and ZTmax ∼ 2 at 773 K.253 Enhanced thermoelectric performance was observed in the temperature range from 300 K to 800 K by doping p-type single crystals of SnSe with Na and Ag. Doping SnSe is difficult because of its layered anisotropic structure, where each SnSe layer is two atoms thin, and locally distorted bonding is present around the Sn and Se atoms. This doping lowers the Fermi level and increases the number of carrier pockets in SnSe, which collaboratively optimize the Seebeck coefficient and the electrical conductivity. The Seebeck coefficient of Na-doped crystals is comparatively smaller than the Seebeck coefficient of Ag-doped crystals. Additionally, the Seebeck coefficient of Sn0.99Na0.01Se is greater than that of Sn0.98Na0.02Se and Sn0.97Na0.03Se, which means increased doping concentration with Na leads to a reduced Seebeck coefficient in single-crystal SnSe. Still, the higher electrical conductivity of the Na-doped samples yields much larger power factors than the Ag-doped SnSe crystals. For the highest 3% Na-doped Sn0.97Na0.03Se crystal, PF reaches as high as ∼2.8 mW m−1 K−2 at room temperature and is greater than 1.38 mW m−1 K−2 in the whole working temperature range. The κlattice ∝ T−1, which is the sign of the dominance of Umklapp phonon processes. Over the temperature range from 300 K to 800 K, the ZTavg ∼ 1.17 is obtained for the Sn0.97Na0.03Se crystal with a maximum ZT ∼ 2 at 800 K.279 Later in another work, Wang et al.280 studied the result of Na and Ag doping on single-crystal SnSe prepared by a modified Bridgman method at a low-temperature regime. They found that Na is more effective in increasing the carrier concentration of SnSe. 1 at% Na-doping increases the carrier concentration up to ∼1.8 × 1019 cm−3 at 300 K and causes a ZT ∼ 0.33 at 350 K. Ag-doped SnSe single-crystal was prepared by a novel horizontal Bridgman method. Still, doping of single-crystal SnSe with Ag has a lesser effect on lattice thermal conductivity than doping with Na because Ag (z = 49) and Sn (z = 50) atoms have almost an equal size. Therefore Ag atoms have minimal effect on the phonon scattering mechanism. On the other hand, the size difference and mass difference of Na and Sn atoms significantly affect phonons' scattering. The highest ZT along a-axis was measured for Sn0.97Ag0.03Se to be ∼0.95 at 793 K, mainly due to the low κlattice near the Pnma–Cmcm phase transition temperature.281 Wei et al.282 prepared very high quality, fully dense SnSe single-crystals. DFT calculations showed an increase in band gap with an increase in temperature and metal to semiconductor transition at 600 K. Maximum ZT ∼ 1.0 at 850 K was observed along b-axis. Liu et al.283 reported the effect of Te doping on the Se-sites of single-crystal SnSe. They prepared Sn-rich Te doped SnSe single-crystals by a modified Sn self-flux method. Te-doping showed an effect on band gap as well as the carrier concentration. Though having almost the same cation/anion-ratio in Sn1.17Se and Sn1.17Se0.97Te0.027 have a significant difference in κlattice. Lowest κlattice was achieved in the Sn1.17Se0.97Te0.027 sample, and considering the result, for other samples, it was concluded that, κlattice-decreases with increasing Sn/(Se + Te) ratio.
5.2.2.2 n-Type single-crystal SnSe. The undoped SnSe is p-type with a carrier concentration of ∼5.3 × 1017 cm−3 at 300 K.284 Duong et al.285 fabricated p-type SnSe with carrier concentration of ∼5.2 × 1018 cm−3 at 773 K and reported that Bi-doping on Sn-sites turns it into n-type SnSe single crystals with carrier concentration that depends on Bi-content. In this case, a maximum ZT of ∼2.2 was achieved along b-axis (here, a = 11.49 Å, b = 4.44 Å, c = 4.14 Å) at 733 K with an n-type carrier concentration of 2.1 × 1019 cm−3 at 773 K. Pb dopant is also used to synthesize n-type SnSe single crystal successfully via a facile Sn-flux route. For the greater atomic radius of Pb (Z = 82), the formation of SnSe antisite defects is reduced, and hence it induces more Se vacancy (VSe). These vacancies are generated more with increasing Pb content in the samples and act as donors making the system show n-type conduction. Due to this doping, the majority of carriers are changed from hole to electron. The carrier concentration decreased from 4.2 × 1017 cm−3 in p-type SnSe to 3.3 × 1017 cm−3 in p-type Sn0.85+5Pb0.15Se and then increased to 4.1 × 1017 cm−3 in n-type Sn0.1+5Pb0.9Se. A maximum PF of ∼1.2 μW cm−1 K−1 was obtained at room temperature.286 Using DFT calculations, Tang et al.286 showed that due to the more robust metallic nature of Pb, the electrons become delocalized around Sn-atom, suggesting a much higher degree of electron delocalization in the Pb doped SnSe. Chang et al.287 reported an ultralow thermal conductivity with an outstanding PF and a maximum ZT ∼ 2.8 at 773 K in out-of-plane Br doped n-type SnSe crystal. 6. Ge chalcogenides as TE materials

Germanium chalcogenides (GeTe, GeSe, and GeS) are important representative materials for TE application in the mid-temperature region. This section has discussed the progress of Ge-chalcogenide-based materials for TE application in past years.

6.1 GeTe-based materials

6.1.1 Crystal structure and general transport properties of GeTe. GeTe is one of the most efficient TE materials for mid-temperature (600–923 K) and near-room temperature applications.349 GeTe has a rocksalt crystal structure (Fmm, paraelectric, centrosymmetric) when T > 720 K with lattice constant a ≈ 6.01 Å. Near 720 K without any change in the number of atoms per unit cell GeTe undergoes a phase transition from a high-symmetry cubic-phase (c-GeTe or β-GeTe) at higher temperature to a low-symmetry rhombohedral-phase (a = b = c, α = β = γ < 90°, R3m, ferroelectric, noncentrosymmetric, r-GeTe or α-GeTe) at lower temperature through slight distortion along the [111] direction,350,351 accompanied by an angular distortion of the unit cell from 60° to about 57.51° as shown in Fig. 26.
Fig. 26 Schematic crystal structure for the cubic-to-rhombohedral phase transition in GeTe. Reproduced with permission from ref. 378. Copyright © 2014, American Chemical Society.

This r-GeTe has lattice parameter a = 5.985(2) Å and rhombohedral angle α = 88.17(3)° near 300 K.350 These phase transitions are an ideal example of displacive ferroelectric phase transition due to the relative shift of the cation and the anion sublattices caused by the softening116,352 of the transverse optic (TO) phonon mode at the zone-center.350,353 Usually, high-symmetry materials have the advantage of high band degeneracy which causes a good improvement in PF. On the other hand, low-symmetry materials usually show low κlattice. In a reported work, Li et al.354 proposed that the positive contributions of both (r- and c) phases for enhancing ZT can be enjoyed simultaneously by slight symmetry breaking. During phase transition in GeTe, the symmetry breaking is minimal, so it causes both the advantages to be utilized in the same material simultaneously. The band structure of direct band gap c-GeTe is very similar to SnTe with two VBs (L and Σ), and this two VB configuration is also applicable for r-GeTe but switch in energy levels between the L and Σ bands. If ΔEL–Σ is the energy offset between two VBs, then ΔEL–Σ = −0.174 eV for r-GeTe and ΔEL–Σ = +0.064 for c-GeTe. For L-band of c-GeTe, the band degeneracy found to be NVL = 4 similarly for Σ-band it is NVΣ = 12. Due to the symmetry reduction in r-GeTe, those 4L pockets split into 3L + 1Z, and those 12Σ pockets split into 6Σ + 6η, so it seems to be difficult to achieve effective band convergence, but the added new degrees of freedom is enough to achieve effective band convergence. This makes r-GeTe as good thermoelectric material as c-GeTe or even better.355

Jeong et al.356 identified four possible crystal structures of GeTe using DFT calculation during its phase transition. Among these, R3m and Fmm were already known, but the other two phases P1 and Cm, were identified for the first time. From band structure calculation, an equal band gap (∼0.80 eV) was reported for both R3m and P1. While Cm was reported to have a lower band gap of ∼0.66 eV and Fmm to have a band gap of ∼0.34 eV. Band structures of all possible phases are shown in Fig. 27. Askarpour et al.357 used the first-principles modelling and reported unusual thermoelectric transport anisotropy in rhombohedral GeTe. According to their report Seebeck coefficient is lesser anisotropic than electrical conductivity and PF. They took parallel and perpendicular to [111]-direction as cross-plane and in-plane directions, respectively. They found that in n-type GeTe, the cross-plane electrical conductivity is larger than that for in-plane, and for p-type, the nature is opposite. This anisotropy causes ZT to differ depending on the direction. But pristine p-type degenerate GeTe is not considered a good thermoelectric material. It has an extremely high carrier concentration (∼1021 cm−3) due to intrinsic Ge-vacancies caused by the large population of the occupied anti-bonding states.358 The high carrier concentration of GeTe reduces the Seebeck coefficient and hence PF. From the phase-diagram analysis of GeTe, Miller,359 Lubell and Mazelsky360 proposed the presence of only one type of defect. According to them, each defect acts as an acceptor and supplies two holes to the VB. Later Damon et al.361 proposed the defects to be Ge-vacancies. In contrast to that, analyzing Hall measurements, Kolomoets et al.362 reported the presence of more than one type of defect. Later Lewis et al.363 analyzed transport properties of GeTe as a function of composition based on a two-carrier model. In recent work, Liu et al.364 clarified the role of Ge-vacancies in structural change and TE performance. Using first-principles calculations and Boltzmann transport theory, they found that Ge-vacancies are more likely to form spontaneously in the cubic phase than in the rhombohedral phase, plays a vital role in stabilizing the cubic phase, and Ge-vacancies modify the band structure in a way that reduces power factor.364 From reported data, we can have an idea about the different TE parameters of GeTe. Pristine GeTe exhibits the σ value of ∼8067 S cm−1 at 300 K, which decreases to ∼2158 S cm−1 at 708 K. S-value of ∼34 μV K−1 at 300 K increases with temperature, then reaches ∼153 μV K−1 at 708 K. GeTe has κlattice ∼ 2.6 Wm−1 K−1 and κtotal ∼ 8.3 Wm−1 K−1 at 300 K, which reduces to ∼3.43 Wm−1 K−1 at 673 K. ZT of pristine GeTe increases with temperature from ∼0.03 at 300 K to ∼0.8 at 720 K.365 The size-dependence of the amorphous to crystalline phase transitions in GeTe nanoparticles increases the possibility of designing memory devices with GeTe at the nanoscale. Yarema et al. reported that crystallization temperature increases with decreasing size of the GeTe nanoparticles below 10 nm.366 Recently, Shekhawat et al.367 have reported that Se-substitution in GeTe, increased the band gap in the as deposited films and decreased in the annealed films. These results have proved that Se-substitution can effectively be used to tune the bandgap and transition temperatures of GeTe systems. It will be interesting to explore the transport properties of these GeTe1−xSex (0 ≤ x ≤ 1.0) alloys both by experimentally as well as theoretical DFT method.

In 2006 Matsunaga et al.400 showed that GeTe(1 − x)-Sb2Te3(x) pseudo-binary compound films maintain rocksalt crystal structure over a wide range of compositions for a range 0 ≤ x ≤ 2/3 when subjected to instantaneous heating by laser irradiation followed by equally rapid cooling. Many works reported that GeTe rich Sb2Te3 pseudo-binary [Sb2Te3(GeTe)n]-type materials show excellent TE properties in the mid-temperature region. Temperature-dependent phase transition of these classes of materials with n = 6, 12, 15 were studied by Schneider et al.401 Rosenthal et al.402 described this structure as a mixture of tetradymite-type Sb2Te3 and GeTe, and this results in the pseudo-binary homologous series Sb2Te3(GeTe)n with distorted rocksalt-type unit-cell of a thickness depending on the richness (n-value) of GeTe in the sample, and each cell is separated from the adjacent cell by the van der Waals gap. The Te–Te layers are separated by van der Waals gaps for every n block of the Ge–Te layer in the Ge–Sb–Te structure; this is absent in the traditional Sb doped GeTe samples. Samples with n = 2 and 19 were found to achieve ZT ∼ 1.3 at high temperatures. But, the lack of long-time stability of those samples above room temperature, these materials were not so effectively used in actual TE devices. On the other hand, ZT reached only up to ∼0.7 in that temperature region where the samples were long time stable and applicable in devices. Fahrnbauer et al.403 demonstrated the effect of CoGe2 precipitates on the TE properties of (Ge–Sb–Te) samples. A simultaneous increase in PF and decrease in κlattice due to homogeneously distributed CoGe2-precipitates resulted in ZT ∼ 0.9 at 523 K and ∼1.8 at 723 K in the case of (CoGe2)0.22Sb2Te3(GeTe)19 heterostructure. At the same time, peak ZT ∼ 1.9 at 723 K was recorded for (CoGe2)0.20Sb2Te3(GeTe)17. Vacancy engineering on Sb2Te3(GeTe)17 leads to a high ZT. In their work, Xu et al.404 varied the annealing time of the sample and found ZT ∼ 2.4 at 773 K and ZTavg ∼ 1.51 for 7 days' annealing. The carrier concentration of the same sample reduces after annealing. The microstructural origin of this carrier concentration reduction lies in the migration and reconstruction of intrinsic Ge vacancies present in the system during the annealing process, which in turn forms more stable defects named long-range van der Waals gaps. Reduced carrier concentration reduces κel. Recently, Rhenium (Re) doped Sb2Te3(GeTe)12 has been reported as good TE material of Ge–Sb–Te class by Lou et al.405 with a ZT ∼ 2.25 at 773 K in Sb2Te3(Ge0.988Re0.012Te)12. Tsai et al.406 reported that TE performance of Ge1−xSbxTe and (GeTe)1−y(Sb2Te3)y samples can be drastically enhanced by using a two-step cooling procedure. For example, Ge0.95Sb0.05Te alloy synthesized by this procedure with an intentional suspension in cooling for an hour at 673 K and resulted in a ZT ∼ 2.0 at 720 K, which is greater than that achieved in another work mentioned in this report previously. When these samples were further doped with Sb2Te3, their TE performance was enhanced. For (GeTe)0.95(Sb2Te3)0.05ZT reached ∼2.5–2.9 at 720 K. The reason behind this enhancement is the composition fluctuation of alloys below a specific temperature, which is a sign of spinodal decomposition. Sb/In co-doping reduces the phase-transition temperature to extend the better thermoelectric behavior of cubic GeTe to low temperature. ZT ∼ 2.3 was achieved above 650 K in Ge0.89Sb0.1In0.01Te.126 Qiu et al. reduced the phase transition temperature of GeTe from 660 K to 523 K via entropy engineering. Multiple doping by In, Pb, Sb, and I increased the configurational entropy of GeTe. ZT ∼ 2.1 at 800 K was achieved for the highest entropy species Ge0.84In0.01Pb0.1Sb0.05Te0.997I0.003. The increased configurational entropy improved TE performance in many ways. It helped to form short-range disordered microstructures and provided all-scale phonon scattering centers that consequently reduced the κlattice. The large effective mass of the high entropy sample resulted in a high Seebeck coefficient.407


6.1.2.2 Research progress on TAGS materials. Since the past few decades, TAGS [generally expressed as (AgSbTe2)100−x(GeTe)x or TAGS-x] materials have been proving themselves as good TE materials to use mainly in the mid-temperature regime. TAGS-80 and TAGS-85 were considered the two most promising systems of this class.178,408–410 Undoped TAGs-75 has a ZT ∼ 0.7 at 625 K. Zybała177 reported that ZT value increases to a maximum of ∼1.2 at 625 K when TAGs-75 is optimally doped with AgSbSe2 with a composition (GeTe)75(AgSbTe2)23.75(AgSbSe2)1.25. Christakudis et al.411 achieved a maximum ZT ∼ 1.68 at 700 K in (GeTe)0.8 [(Ag2Te)0.4(Sb2Te3)0.6]0.2. TAG-85 is rhombohedral at room temperature and transforms to cubic after a certain higher temperature. Kumar et al.408 prepared cubic TAG-85 even at room temperature by following a specific cooling procedure during synthesis. They reported that TAGS-85 works best when spontaneous precipitation of Ag8GeTe6 is minimized. In recent years researchers found interest in improving further TE performance of this class of samples using various strategies. Levin et al.409 reported that doping with rare earth Dy improves TE performance of TAGS-85. For undoped TAGS-85, a ZT ≤ 1.3 was recorded at 730 K. At the same temperature, the TAGS-85 sample doped with 1% Dy for Te showed ZT ≤ 1.5, and TAGS-85 doped with 1% or 2% Dy for Ge showed ZT ≥ 1.5. TAFS-85 becomes a dilute magnetic semiconductor when doped with 1 at% Ce or Yb. Though this doping increases κlattice but the comparatively higher increment in S compensates for the drawback, and as the net effect, a high ZT ≈ 1.5 at 700 K was achieved in TAGS-85 + 1% Ce and TAGS-85 + 1% Yb.410 When the Ag/Sb ratio is changed from unity, TE performance is improved than the conventional TAGS. Doping TAGS-75 with AgSbSe2 improves the TE performance due to a reduction in κlattice and increment in Seebeck coefficient.178
6.1.2.3 Other effective dopants and techniques. In another work by this research group 1.5 at% BiI3-doped Sb2Te3(GeTe)17 has been reported to have a good ZT value of ∼2.2 at 723 K and ZTavg ∼1.4 in the temperature range of 323–773 K. Doping with BiI3 induces anion site (Te) vacancy clusters which combine with cation site (Ge) vacancies resulting in significant phonon scattering and hence a reduction in κlattice.412 Among IIIA elements (Al, Ga, In, Tl), doping with In-has been proved to be most efficient in lowering the carrier concentration, enhancing the Seebeck coefficient, reducing κel to result in an enhanced ZT in GeTe. Solid solutions of composition (In2Te3)x(GeTe)3−3x were prepared by Sun et al.413 and proposed as an effective substitute for TAGs as a p-leg material for TEGs or TECs. ZT ∼ 1.35 at 823 K for x = 0.05 was achieved. The energy difference between the light VB and heavy VB decreases with increasing In2Te3 content. Increasing DOS causes enhancement in the Seebeck coefficient but a reduction in electrical conductivity. In the rhombohedral phase, In-doped GeTe shows very similar resonant levels as those in undoped GeTe. An enhanced total DOS in both phases originates from the host–dopants interaction irrespective of crystal structure or temperature. The deep defect states (DDSs) above the VBM are around the Fermi levels, and the DOS changes cause an enhanced Seebeck coefficient. A maximum ZT ∼ 1.3 at a temperature about 628 K in Ge1−xInxTe class of samples when x = 0.02 was achieved.414 Co-doping With In/Se is also effective to synergistically enhance the TE performance of GeTe. For Ge0.99In0.01Te1−ySey (y = 0.05 and 0.10) class of samples ZT ∼ 1.38 was achieved for y = 0.05. In dopants incorporates resonant levels and increases PF while Se decreases it. But as Se decreases κ- also, the net result is enhanced ZT.415 From the calculated carrier concentration of different metal-tellurides, it is clear that the carrier concentration increases with a decrease in cation size. That's why among PbTe, SnTe and GeTe, the last one has the highest carrier concentration, which is a drawback for GeTe. Li et al.416 proposed that effective size enhancement of cation can improve TE performance of GeTe. Ge-vacancy has the lowest formation energy and the biggest cause of its high carrier concentration. Doping with PbSe increases cation size, reduces anion size, uplifts the formation energy of Ge-vacancies, and helps to reduce high carrier concentration. Finally, ZT ∼ 2.2 has been achieved in GeTe–PbSe alloys. Al-dopants increase the hole concentration, increase energy separation between L and Σ band, and reduce the band convergence, resulting in a reduced Seebeck coefficient and ZT. Additionally, they found IIA element Ba-is also not a good dopant to increase TE performance of GeTe. In a recent work, Gao et al.418 doped GeTe with transition element Y and achieved a ZT ∼ 1.2, further enhanced by doping with Bi and Sb to ZT ∼ 1.8 and ZTavg ∼ 1.1 from 300 K to 723 K. When GeTe is doped with SnSe and SnS, the system acts as a solid solution at a lower concentration of SnSe and SnS, and at their higher concentration, the coexistence of solid solution and phase separation is observed. Further doping with Sb-optimizes carrier concentration, increases the solubility of SnS– SnSe in GeTe. An abundance of point defect is also increased, and micron-sized Ge-rich precipitates are introduced. So κlattice is further reduced due to an increased phonon scattering in Sb-doped (GeTe)1−2x(SnSe)x(SnS)x system. At 300 K κlattice ∼ 0.8 Wm−1 K−1 and ∼0.3 Wm−1 K−1 were achieved for (GeTe)0.95(SnSe)0.025(SnS)0.025 and (Ge0.90Sb0.10Te)0.95(SnSe)0.025(SnS)0.025 systems respectively. ZT ∼ 1.9 at 710 K was achieved for the second system.419 Recently an enhancement in PF has been repor ted by charged ferroelectric domain walls (DWs) in GeTe.420

6.2 GeSe-based materials

6.2.1 Crystal structure and general transport properties of GeSe. GeSe is an inherently p-type semiconductor with a layered orthorhombic crystal structure when crystallized under ambient conditions.421 At low temperature, it remains in the Pnma phase with lattice parameters a ≈ 10.825 Å, b ≈ 3.833 Å, c ≈4.388 Å.250,422 GeSe is an indirect band gap semiconductor with an experimentally measured band gap of 1.14 eV.423 GeSe has complex VB structures. According to the reported DFT calculation, the energy difference between the first–second and the first–third VB are ∼0.13 eV and 0.16 eV, respectively. From the same DFT calculation, the effective mass on different VB for orthorhombic GeSe was estimated to be for the first VB band and for the second VB. Similar to SnSe, in this case, also the second VB is heavier than the first VB.424 Gashimzade et al.425 reported a phase transition in GeSe from simple orthorhombic to body-centered orthorhombic (Pnma → Cmcm) at 29 GPa. Under some specific crystallization condition, GeSe can also be crystallized in two other phases, and those are cubic (Fmm) and rhombohedral (R3m), respectively. Sist et al.426 carried out a complete structural study of GeSe from 120 K up to 940 K using high-resolution synchrotron powder X-ray diffraction and found a structural phase transition to a rocksalt crystal structure (Fmm) at 907 K. According to Wiedemeier and Siemers, the first-order phase transition from orthorhombic-GeSe to cubic-GeSe occurs at 924 ± 5 K under ambient pressure. It remains in the cubic phase till the temperature reaches its melting point.427,428 This high-temperature cubic phase was inherently unstable due to imaginary vibrational modes centered at the BZ center, and the cubic phase was not available under ambient temperature and pressure. An increment in external pressure leads those imaginary vibrational modes to disappear, and the phase transition occurs under ambient temperature.429 Bhatia et al.430 reported a pressure-induced first-order transition from layered crystalline semiconductor-GeSe (α-phase) to a metallic-GeSe (Fmm) at 6 GPa with a ≈ 6.42 Å. But analyzing in situ x-ray-diffraction measurements, Onodera et al.431 observed that GeSe remains in the orthorhombic structure up to 82 GPa. Von Rohr et al.432 synthesized and characterized a new, unusual β-phase of GeSe, a polymorph of α-GeSe. This β-phase also has a layered orthorhombic crystal structure. The intralayer Ge–Se distance is ∼2.59 Å, and that for interlayer is ≈3.27 Å, where each layer consists of six-rings with the vertices arranged in a “boat” conformation, and it is a semiconductor with a moderate band gap. Later Xu et al.433 worked on this new β-phase of GeSe and investigated the electronic, optical, and transport properties. In 2018 Yu et al.434 discovered two novel intermediate phases between the phases reported by Von Rohr et al. (α-phase → R3m → Fmm → β-phase). Where the low-pressure R3m phase shows ferroelectricity and high pressure (6 GPa) Fmm phase acts as a 3D topological insulator. In recent times by analyzing the phonon dispersion curves, Kagdada et al.435 reported that the Pnma → Fmm phase transition takes place at 7.3 GPa. There is a conflict regarding the actual value of the external pressure, though, from the reported data, it can be predicted to lie somewhere above 6 GPa. A minimum κlattice ∼ 0.39 Wm−1 K−1 was speculated for GeSe,421 which was lower than that of GeS (0.52 Wm−1 K−1) and SnS (0.45 Wm−1 K−1). In practice, the low carrier concentration is a barrier for pristine GeSe to achieve promising TE performance.
6.2.2 TE performance of GeSe based materials. Hao et al.424 predicted that ZT for hole-doped GeSe along crystallographic b-axis can be enhanced up to 0.8 at 300 K and 2.5 at 800 K (Fig. 29) if the carrier concentration can be increased from 4 × 1019 cm−3 to 6.5 × 1019 cm−3. Those dopants which can increase the carrier concentration of GeSe are suitable to enhance the TE performance of GeSe. Ag doping with GeSe on the Ge sites is an effective way to improve its TE performance. Zhang et al.436 showed that doping with Ag can enhance the carrier concentration ∼1018 cm−3 (which still does not meet the requirement). Still, other dopants like Na, Cu, La, Se cannot significantly increase the carrier concentration, even like Ag. For Ge1−xAgxSe systems, the Hall carrier concentration increases with increasing x and reaches a saturation value at x = 0.002. Further doping with Sn increases carrier concentration a little more. ZT ∼ 0.2 at 700 K was achieved in Ge0.79Ag0.01Sn0.2Se. According to some reports, Na can also act as an effective dopant for GeSe to reduce thermal conductivity. Polycrystalline samples of composition Ge1−xNaxSe with x = 0.00, 0.01, 0.02, and 0.04 synthesized by Shaabani et al.437 reported that the value of κ decreases with increasing Na content and becomes lowest ∼ 1.06 Wm−1 K−1 at 300 K and lowest κlattice ∼ 0.44 Wm−1 K−1 at 573 K among the synthesized samples at x = 0.04. Previously it was predicted that the cubic phase of GeSe could not be stable under ambient conditions. Still, later Roychowdhury et al.438 showed that alloying pristine GeSe with specific compound AgBiSe2 could stabilize its cubic phase even under ambient conditions. For (GeSe)1−x(AgBiSe2)x class of samples [0.35 ≤ x ≤ 0.50] this stable cubic phase exists. Additionally, this alloyed material showed n-type conduction, a counterpart of inherent p-type GeSe, and was urgently needed to use GeSe in commercial TEG or TEC devices. As a result of this alloying, with the increase in the AgBiSe2 content, band gap gradually decreases from 1.1 eV (orthorhombic phase) to 0.05 eV (for 0.08 ≤ x ≤ 0.29, rhombohedral phase) and then increases to 0.3–0.4 eV (for 0.30 ≤ x ≤ 0.50, cubic phase). A maximum ZT ∼ 0.45 at 677 K was achieved for the n-type cubic sample for x = 0.50. In 2019 Cui et al.439 theoretically predicted a decent power factor in the cross-plane direction for n-type GeSe. More delocalized interlayer carrier density was assumed to cause the highly normalized electrical conductivity and hence PF. Additionally, they found group VA and VIIA elements to be effective n-type dopants for GeSe. However, VIIA elements were more effective than VA elements to normalize the PF towards a higher ZT. Different phases of GeSe also have properties that are helpful to use as better TE material than pristine GeSe. A way to improve the TE performance of orthorhombic GeSe is to alloy with some particular material to stabilize a new rhombohedral structure (like GeTe) that will have multi valley Fermi surface increased carrier concentration. Two such promising alloying agents are AgSbSe2 and AgSbTe2. Huang et al.440 found a transition from orthorhombic phase to rhombohedral phase in pristine GeSe when doped with AgSbSe2. This alloying leads to an abrupt increase of ∼1020 cm−3 in carrier concentration of GeSe. ZT ∼ 0.86 at 710 K was achieved in the GeAg0.2Sb0.2Se1.4 system. Also, alloying with AgSbTe2 can synergistically improve the TE performance of GeSe. A maximum ZT ∼ 0.96 at 754 K was achieved in GeSeAg0.2Sb0.2Te0.4, and the ZTavg ∼ 0.64 was recorded between 301 K and 754 K by Yan et al.441 Synthesis of p-type crystalline cubic GeSe was done by Yan et al.442 by alloying with Sb2Te3. Study of (GeSe)1−x(Sb2Te3)x alloys at room temperature. The study revealed how different phases of SnSe could be fabricated by changing the percentage of Sb2Te3 in the alloys. When x = 0%, the sample remains in the Pnma phase. At x = 5%, there is a coexistence of Pnma and cubic phases. When x > 8%, the sample turns out to be in the complete cubic phase. From this study, ZT ∼ 0.73 was achieved in (GeSe)0.92(Sb2Te3)0.08 at 710 K. Under ambient conditions, Sarkar et al.443 stabilized the rhombohedral phase of GeSe by allying with 10 mol% AgBiSe2. A remarkably high ZT ∼ 1.25 at 723 K was achieved parallel to the growth direction in the p-type rhombohedral (GeSe)0.9(AgBiSe2)0.1 crystals synthesized by the Bridgman method. Theoretically, it was predicted that this alloy has some ferroelectric instability, which softens the polar transverse optical phonon modes and scatters more heat-carrying acoustic phonons. Which results in a reduction of κlattice, and leads to show a better TE performance. In another work by Sarkar et al.444R3m-phase of GeSe was stabilized under ambient conditions by alloying GeSe with AgBiTe2. This phase change comes along with a change in the nature of bonding from covalence to metavalence. Rhombohedral (GeSe)0.9(AgBiTe2)0.1 has some metallic character and closeness to ferroelectric instability. Hence, its band gap, electronic structure, and low κlattice are preferred to be used as a good TE material. A large PF of 12.8 μW cm−1 K−2 and ZT ∼ 1.35 was achieved in (GeSe)0.9(AgBiTe2)0.1 at 627 K. Wang et al.445 studied the structure-dependent TE properties in GeSe1−xTex (0 ≤ x ≤ 0.5) samples. Orthorhombic, hexagonal, and rhombohedral phases were obtained for different Te content in the compounds. Pure orthorhombic phase exists for x ≤ 0.05, for x = 0.10 and 0.15 both orthorhombic and hexagonal phases coexist, pure hexagonal Ge4Se3Te phase for 0.20 ≤ x ≤ 0.35 and rhombohedral phase for x ≥ 0.40. Different structures had different carrier concentrations, e.g., for the Ge-rich orthorhombic phase, the carrier concentration is ∼1016 cm−3, and for the other two Ge-poor phases, those are ∼1021 cm−3. Albeit having an equal range of carrier concentration rhombohedral phase has a higher Seebeck coefficient than the hexagonal phase due to the favored band structure. At 778 K, a maximum ZT ∼ 0.69 was achieved in GeSe0.55Te0.45 after transitioning from the rhombohedral phase to the cubic phase. Using the first-principles-based Boltzmann transport equation approach, Yuan et al.446 predicted ZT values of p-type orthorhombic GeSe along a-, b- and c-direction can be enhanced up to 1.54, 1.09, and 1.01 at 700 K and 8 GPa, respectively. Predicted values are remarkably higher than those at zero pressure at an experimental carrier concentration of ∼1018 cm−3. Yuan et al.447 again investigated electronic and phononic transport properties along with the dynamical and mechanical stability of GeSe in its R3m phase. Along the interlayer direction at 800 K, the predicted maximum ZT values for p-type and n-type doping were 2.02 and 2.37, respectively. According to Li et al.,448 strain-engineering is an effective way to improve the TE performance of 2D monolayer GeSe. The effects of different kinds of strain like uniaxial, biaxial, tensile, compressive, armchair-compressive strain are demonstrated in this work. It was predicted that the PF could be enhanced four times at room temperature for both p-type and n-type 2D monolayer GeSe at the −6% strained condition. Reduction in structural dimension can improve TE performance of GeSe. A recent investigation on 3D-GeSe and 2D polymorphs of GeSe (α-GeSe, β-GeSe, and γ-GeSe) showed that both 3D and 2D structures are equally stable and can break the benchmark limit of ZT ≈ 1 at room temperature. At room temperature, ZT ∼ 1.02 for 3D- GeSe and ZT ∼ 1.10 for γ-GeSe were achieved. 6.3 GeS-based materials

GeS has the same layered orthorhombic crystal structure as SnSe, SnS, or GeSe with lattice parameters a = 10.481 Å, b = 3.646 Å, c = 4.299 Å for low-temperature Pnma phase.250,449 There is a structural phase transition from the Pnma phase to the Cmcm phase at 863 K.423 GeS has an experimentally calculated band gap of ∼1.65 eV.450 The energy offset between the first and second VB is ∼ 0.11 eV. The estimated effective masses are for the first VB band and for the second VB.424 The first VB is heavier than the second one, which was not in the case of GeSe or SnSe. So GeS shows some conduction properties that are different from GeSe and SnSe. Using a constant-pressure ab initio technique, the gradual phase transition towards the Cmcm-phase of the layered Pnma-GeS was found by applying external hydrostatic pressure about 35 GPa.451 The work also predicted the possibility of further transition into a rocksalt-structured phase from Pnma and Cmcm by sliding the layers relative to each other along c-direction. In 2019 Haq et al.450 reported the presence of two new thermodynamically stable polymorphs (γ-GeS and ε-GeS) of monolayer GeS along with the normal α-GeS. These two new polymorphs have extremely high DOS near the Fermi level and exhibit a high Seebeck coefficient and sufficiently high electrical conductivity. The plots of DOS and different TE parameters of those polymorphs of GeS concerning chemical potential are depicted in Fig. 31. Using DFT and Boltzmann transport theory, the ZT reaches 1.67 for γ-GeS at 500 K and 1.07 for ε-GeS at 1000 K. Ding et al.421 and Shafique et al.453 also studied the thermoelectric and phonon transport properties of GeS along with some other promising IV–VI metal chalcogenides.

Ge-chalcogenides are promising materials for TE application. The high intrinsic p-type carrier concentration of GeTe is a drawback of this material. Research to find suitable dopant and doping techniques are encouraging for this material to optimize carrier concentration and achieve an efficient n-type counterpart for device application. GeTe is the only candidate in IV–VI class of chalcogenides that exhibit a rhombohedral (ferroelectric, noncentrosymmetric) phase at ambient conditions. The transition from ferroelectric to paraelectric (cubic) phase has great importance in the thermal transport properties of this material. Critical analysis of the thermal behavior of this material in the transition region is required. The large band gap and strong covalent bonding in orthorhombic GeSe harm its TE performance. Change in phases (rhombohedral, cubic) tailors the nature of bonding and improves TE properties of GeSe. More research can be pursued to tune and stabilize the suitable phase of GeSe for TE applications. GeSe and GeS have a similar crystal structure as SnSe; their promises in organic–inorganic hybrid TE materials are to be investigated. Techniques to improve the mechanical stability of these materials for real-life applications are to be researched more.

The current discussion focuses on the experimental and theoretical development of the thermoelectric properties of Ge chalcogenides. These materials in the thermoelectric module for power generation technologies may face additional commercialization challenges due to germanium, an expensive material. The device efficiency and commercial feasibility of these materials are yet to be thoroughly investigated.

7. Perspective and future aspects

In this review, we have briefly discussed various novel and traditional strategies that are being used nowadays to improve TE performance beyond the classical benchmark by optimizing the electrical and thermal transport properties of suitable materials. Various strategies, as, increase in local DOS and distortions in DOS by the resonant level formation, relaxation time modulation for charge carriers, lowering of deformation potential, lowering of band effective mass and increment of band degeneracy, modulation of ‘Diffusion of carriers’ and ‘phonon-drag’, reduction of dimensionality of the materials, carrier energy filtering, charge density wave formation to improve the power factor of materials and reduction of specific heat of the material, reducing phonon relaxation time by increment in scattering rate of phonons or large anharmonicity, reduction in phonon group velocity, nanostructure engineering, all-scale hierarchical architectures, reduction of bipolar thermal conduction, tuning of atomic disorders to reduce thermal conductivity of a material have been discussed here for general TE materials. We also have discussed how these strategies have been validated on eco-friendly Pb-free IV–VI metal chalcogenide (SnTe, SnSe, SnS, GeTe, GeSe, and GeS) materials to improve their TE performance. Despite colossal research progress for some candidate materials (SnTe, SnSe, GeTe) of this class, still, some routes for further improvements in the TE performance of these materials in the future can be proposed.

(i) Strongly coupled TE parameters in 3D-bulk TE materials are problematic to decouple. Meanwhile, TE parameters of 2D and quasi-2D materials can be decoupled more easily. So, a reduction in the dimensionality of the materials can help to improve the TE performance. Already single-layered or bi-layered (2D) SnSe, SnS, GeSe, GeS have shown promising TE performance as their 3D-bulk counterpart and sometimes possess equally stable crystal structures in both dimensions. This technique can be explored on other metal chalcogenide materials to check their efficiency in lower dimensions.

(ii) It is essential to fabricate p-type and n-type counterparts of a material to use in TE devices. Both counterparts for some materials (SnSe, GeSe) of this class have already been researched widely, and the rest are yet to be explored more.

(iii) From our discussion, we have seen that a change in fabrication technique can significantly change TE performance in the same composition material. Changing the synthesis method can lead to a better TE performance. More research on the synthesis techniques can be pursued to improve the purity of the desired samples to achieve better TE performance from materials.

(iv) Environmental concerns due to the toxicity of inorganic materials are increasing the research interest in organic TE materials. Using various conducting polymers, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), graphene, etc., as a matrix for the degenerate semiconductor nanoparticles, a new direction has already been evolved to find efficient TE materials for eco-friendly commercial use. Bi2Te3, Bi2Se3, etc., are efficient inorganic chalcogenide compounds used in organic–inorganic composite materials for TE applications. Recently, SnSe NS/PEDOT:PSS composites have been reported to have promising TE performance. Some other IV–VI metal chalcogenides have similar layered lattice structures like SnSe. The TE performance of organic–inorganic nanocomposites with those materials is yet to be explored.

(v) Best operating condition and best structural stability may not fall in the same temperature region [e.g., SnTe, Sb2Te3(GeTe)n]. The lack of long-time stability of such samples in the best operating temperature regime resists applying those materials in actual TE devices. It is to be inspected how the temperature region of stability and best operation can be merged and if organic TE materials can be used for this purpose.

(vi) Phase transition significantly impacts the transport property of materials. The phase with high symmetry usually has the advantage of high band degeneracy and high density of state effective mass, which helps to achieve an elevated Seebeck coefficient. The low symmetry phase usually has the advantage of low lattice thermal conductivity.354 Phase transition of a TE material in the operating temperature range can be harmful to practical applications. The immediate change in the thermal expansion coefficient during phase transition will generate high internal stress between the devices' materials and outside contacts. It would decline the performance of the device. GeTe is a promising material for mid-temperature TE applications. Still, the phase transition in GeTe from a rhombohedral structure to a cubic structure near ∼700 K hinders its suitability of applications for energy harvesting. There are already some reported works on doping, multiple doping, entropy engineering, etc., to reduce the phase transition temperature of GeTe.373,384,385,387,388,399,407 These techniques can be applied to other materials that face similar phase transition problems in their operating temperature range.

(vii) It is desirable to understand the structure of the probable promising material for thermoelectric applications at the atomic scale using some advanced spectroscopic techniques combined with insights rendered by the results of first-principles calculations and machine learning interatomic potential approach. This will focus on identifying the accurate and appropriate contributions of the lattice part of thermal conductivity from the moment tensor potential (MTP) of the machine learning language approach. As a result, it is expected that the figure of merit (ZT) of such intriguing 2D materials may be compared or projected to the values obtained from experiments after suitable synthesis routes.454–456
