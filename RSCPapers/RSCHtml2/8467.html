Abstract

The rapid development of nonlinear photonic devices requires the exploration of novel materials with superior nonlinear optical (NLO) properties. Colloidal semiconductor nanocrystals (NCs) exhibit size-tunable exciton resonances and excellent NLO properties. Besides, the preparation methods of the colloidal semiconductor NCs are facile and NCs can be easily assembled into films by low-cost and large-area solution processing. These characteristics make colloidal semiconductor NCs promising for application in nonlinear photonic devices. This review provides the recent advances of colloidal semiconductor NCs in nonlinear optics and ultrafast photonics. The main liquid-phase synthetic methods, third-order NLO properties, and related nonlinear photonic device applications of several colloidal semiconductor NCs are discussed. The potential challenges and a perspective of the future research areas of these colloidal semiconductor NCs for nonlinear optics and ultrafast photonics are presented.

1. Introduction

The research on nonlinear optics has received much interest since the birth of lasers in the 1960s. Nonlinear optics mainly involves the study of nonlinear effects produced by the interaction between light and materials. Nonlinear optical (NLO) technology not only helps people understand the NLO phenomena, but also enables a variety of novel nonlinear photonic devices such as lasers,1 all-optical switches,2 optical limiters,3 optical modulators,4 Kerr shutters,5 photodetectors,6 and optical memories.7 The nonlinear photonic devices play important roles in light generation, propagation, and detection. For instance, compared with the traditional electro-optical switch, the all-optical switch has the advantage of ultrafast time response, which can meet the needs of high-speed all-optical communications.8 Optical modulators can be used for the generation of ultrashort pulsed lasers, which are useful in medicine9 and processing of industrial materials.10 Current nonlinear photonic devices are mainly based on traditional NLO materials such as crystals and semiconductors.11–17 However, there are some limits in using the traditional NLO materials. For instance, nowadays the molecular beam epitaxy-grown semiconductor saturable absorber mirrors are widely used for the generation of ultrashort pulsed lasers due to their high flexibility and stability.1 But semiconductor saturable absorber mirrors require complex preparation protocols and suffer from high preparation cost as well as narrow operation bandwidth. Moreover, for better integration into chips in the future, smaller nanoscale photonic devices need to be designed. Therefore, developing novel candidate materials with facile fabrication and integration, broadband operation bandwidth, and outstanding NLO performance is highly desirable for next-generation photonic nanodevices.

Colloidal semiconductor nanocrystals (also known as quantum dots) have been widely studied for several decades due to their facile preparation, unique physicochemical features, and extensive applications.18 In terms of fluorescence properties, colloidal semiconductor NCs show high luminescence efficiency, narrow luminescence bandwidth, and wide color gamut. In addition to their fluorescence properties, colloidal semiconductor NCs also exhibit outstanding NLO performance. Colloidal semiconductor NCs show size-tunable exciton resonance, strong NLO properties, and ultrafast relaxation time, as well as facile preparation and integration, etc., making them a class of promising NLO materials for photonic nanodevices.19–25

There are a few review articles on colloidal semiconductor NCs.26–36 However, the recent advances on the NLO properties and related nonlinear photonic device applications of colloidal semiconductor NCs have not been systematically summarized. The research on the nonlinear optics and ultrafast photonics of colloidal semiconductor NCs is still increasing and a related comprehensive review is urgently needed. In this review article, the recent advances of colloidal semiconductor NCs for third-order NLO properties and the related NLO device applications are summarized. Four classes of colloidal semiconductor NCs are introduced, including the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs (ME, M = Cd, Zn, Pb, and E = S, Se, Te), emerging plasmonic semiconductor NCs (e.g., Cu2−xS and Cu2−xSe), metal halide perovskite NCs (e.g., CH3NH3PbBr3, CsPbBr3, and CsPbI3), and quantum dots (QDs) derived from two-dimensional (2D) layered materials (e.g., black phosphorous, MoS2, and Ti3C2 MXene). The structure and bandgap of these colloidal semiconductor NCs are provided. The liquid-phase synthetic methods for these colloidal semiconductor NCs are introduced. In the following, the third-order NLO properties of these colloidal semiconductor NCs are summarized. Afterward, the related nonlinear photonic device applications of these colloidal semiconductor NCs are demonstrated. Finally, the potential challenges and prospects of these colloidal semiconductor NCs for NLO properties and practical optical device applications are discussed (Fig. 1).

2. Basic properties of several colloidal semiconductor NCs

Colloidal semiconductor NCs have been widely studied for several decades due to their unique physicochemical features and widespread applications. Most of the colloidal semiconductor NCs maintain similar lattice structures with their bulk counterparts and may have improved or novel physicochemical properties due to the quantum confinement effect. Four classes of colloidal semiconductor NCs have been extensively studied in the field of nonlinear optics and ultrafast photonics, including the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs, emerging plasmonic semiconductor NCs, metal halide perovskite NCs, and QDs derived from 2D layered materials (2D-QDs). The structure and bandgap energy are two fundamental factors that affect the optical properties of colloidal semiconductor NCs. Therefore, in this section, we introduce the basic properties of these four classes of colloidal semiconductor NCs from the aspect of structure, bandgap energy, etc.

2.1 Conventional metal chalcogenide NCs

Conventional metal chalcogenide semiconductor NCs mainly refer to II–VI or IV–VI compounds, CdE, ZnE, and PbE (E = S, Se, Te). CdE and ZnE compound semiconductors exist in either the cubic zinc blende or hexagonal wurtzite structure. PbE compounds exhibit a face-centered cubic structure at room temperature, as shown in Fig. 2(a).37 These conventional metal chalcogenide semiconductor NCs have covalent chemical bonds, making their crystal structures stable in common solvents such as water and toluene, which is important for practical applications. The bandgaps of bulk ZnS, ZnSe, ZnTe, CdS, CdSe, and CdTe are around 3.16–4.25 eV, 2.58 eV, 2.26 eV, 2.42 eV, 1.74 eV, and 1.61 eV, respectively.38 PbS, PbSe, and PbTe have narrow bandgaps of about 0.41 eV,39 0.3 eV,40 and 0.32 eV,41 respectively.

The conventional metal chalcogenide semiconductor NCs are one of the most studied NCs in the past thirty years. The first synthesis of the colloidal semiconductor NCs was the preparation of CdS NCs in an aqueous solution by Brus and coworkers in 1983.42 In this report, the size-dependent electronic properties were also investigated.42 Since then, a lot of research has been performed on colloidal semiconductor NCs. In general, when the size of the semiconductor NCs reduces, the corresponding bandgap would increase due to the quantum size effect. Besides, changing their elemental compositions, introducing dopants, and constructing core/shell heterostructures (type I, type II, and reverse type I) or composite heterostructures can also adjust the bandgap of the NCs. Overall, the conventional metal chalcogenide semiconductor NCs are generally direct bandgap semiconductors with bandgaps in a broad range from about 4.68 eV43 to 0.3 eV.44 Furthermore, it is known that quantum confinement is realized when the radius of the semiconductor nanoscale materials is comparable to their exciton Bohr radius in bulk materials.45 PbS, PbSe, and PbTe have large exciton Bohr radii of 18 nm,39 46 nm,46 and over 100 nm,47 respectively. Thus, the large exciton Bohr radius of PbE (E = S, Se, Te) makes the quantum confinement effect achievable with a relatively large size.48 Due to their attractive optical characteristics, the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs are usually applied in biomedical49 and optoelectronic areas.50,51

2.2 Emerging plasmonic semiconductor NCs

Some heavily doped semiconductor NCs show a strong absorption band in the infrared (IR) wavelength region due to their localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) properties.52 These LSPR-active semiconductor NCs can be named plasmonic semiconductor NCs.29,30,52 Plasmonic semiconductor NCs mainly include copper chalcogenide (e.g., Cu2−xS), and metal oxide (e.g., WO3−x, MoO3−x, In:CdO, Al:ZnO, and Sn:In2O3) NCs.30 Among them, Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs are the most studied. Therefore, here we mainly focus on the Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs. Although much work on the preparation of Cu2−xS NCs had been performed earlier, it was the report in 2009 by Zhao and coworkers that initiated the research in the field of plasmonic copper chalcogenide NCs.53 Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs are self-doped NCs, where the vacancies in copper result in p-type doping.54 The crystal structure of Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) is stoichiometry dependent.55,56 For example, there are eight different crystal structures in Cu2−xS according to the different copper vacancy concentrations, which are listed in a recent review article.30 For example, Fig. 2(b and c) show the crystal structures of Cu1.8S with a cubic phase57 and CuS with a hexagonal phase.22 The bandgap of Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) also exhibits stoichiometry dependence, for instance, when the factor (2 − x) in Cu2−xS is varied from 1 and 2, the bandgap reduces from 2 to 1.2 eV.56

One main feature of Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs is that the LSPRs and quantum-confined excitons can be realized within the same NCs, which provides the possibility for strong interaction between photonics and electronics, with implications for applications in many areas.55,58,59 For instance, the exciton peak of Cu2−xS NCs with an average size of 2–6 nm blue shifts compared with their bulk counterparts.55 Meanwhile, these NCs show LSPR properties in the IR window, which arise from p-type carriers in vacancy-doped Cu2−xS NCs.55 Besides, the Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs generally have a lower free carrier density compared with the common noble metal nanoscale materials (e.g., Au, Ag), resulting in Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs with tunable LSPR wavelengths in the IR spectral window from about 1 to 7 μm by simply changing the doping concentration.55,58,60–64 The easy accessibility and wide tunability of the LSPR peak in the IR spectral region enable the Cu2−xE (E = S, Se, Te) NCs as promising candidates for applications in various fields such as photothermal therapy63 and luminescence enhancement.30

2.3 Emerging metal halide perovskite NCs

Metal halide perovskites have a history of more than 120 years. However, only in 2009 they began to attract increasing interest worldwide due to their outstanding performance in solar cells.65 3D metal halide perovskites have the chemical formula of ABX3, where A is CH3NH3+ (MA), CH(NH2)2+ (FA), Cs+ or Rb+, B is Pb, Sn, or Ge ions, and X is Cl, Br, I, or mixed halogen ions. Metal halide perovskite NCs include organic–inorganic hybrid and all-inorganic metal halide perovskite NCs. The crystal structure of the 3D cubic phase metal halide perovskite consists of a 3D network of corner-sharing [BX6]4− octahedra, where B ions locate in the center of the octahedra, and A ions fill the voids created by the eight nearest octahedra (Fig. 2(d)).66

Colloidal organic–inorganic hybrid MAPbBr3 perovskite NCs were first prepared by Schmidt et al. in 2014.67 Soon after, all-inorganic CsPbX3 (X = Cl, Br, I, mixed halogen) perovskite NCs were prepared by Protesescu et al. in 2015.68 One attractive characteristic of metal halide perovskite NCs is that the direct bandgap of metal halide perovskite NCs can be easily tuned from about 1.3 to 3.02 eV by changing their elemental composition.68–70 Specifically, the bandgap of perovskite NCs is tunable from 1.69 eV (MAPbI3) to 3.05 eV (MAPbCl3),71 1.77 eV (CsPbI3) to 3.02 eV (CsPbCl3),68 and from 1.68 eV (FAPbI3) to 3 eV (FAPbCl3).72 Compared with the influence of the X-site ion on the bandgap of the perovskite NCs, the A-site cation has less effect on the bandgap. For example, (EA)x(MA)1−xPbBr3 NCs (x = 0–1) have bandgap tunable from 2.38 eV to 2.94 eV by changing their x value.73 Furthermore, the B-site cation also plays a role in adjusting the bandgap of the perovskite NCs. For instance, when Pb2+ is replaced by Sn2+, the bandgap obtained from the emission wavelength of the resulting CsSnX3 perovskite NCs decreases from 2.8 eV (Cl) to 1.3 eV (I).70 Metal halide perovskite NCs exhibit excellent optical properties such as large absorption coefficients and high emission efficiency74 and have potential applications in optoelectronics75–77 and photovoltaic devices.78 However, metal halide perovskite NCs suffer from stability problems when they are in contact with air, water, heat, or light. Therefore, a great deal of research has been devoted to improve their stabilities, such as using other cations with suitable tolerance factors (e.g., FA, Cs) to replace MA ions.79 In addition, the surface passivation and encapsulation strategies are also used for improving the stabilities of metal halide perovskite NCs, as mentioned in a recent review article.80

2.4 Emerging 2D-QDs

2D-QDs are smaller forms of their native 2D layered materials in terms of the lateral size.81 The first report on 2D-QDs was the colloidal MoS2 and WS2 quantized nanoclusters with particle sizes in the range of 1–3.5 nm, which were fabricated by the cleavage of their van der Waals (vdW) layers via penetration by acetonitrile solvent molecules.82 2D-QDs exhibit excellent physical, chemical, and biological properties due to quantum confinement effect and edge effect while maintaining most intrinsic properties of their 2D layered forms, and have aroused intensive interest in the past few years.81,83 Until now, lots of 2D-QDs have been theoretically predicted and experimentally prepared,81 showing applications in catalysis,83 energy,84 photonic and optoelectronic devices,45,85 and medicine.86 Here, we introduce the commonly studied 2D-QDs in the fields of nonlinear optics and ultrafast photonics, which mainly include graphene QDs (GQDs), black phosphorous QDs (BPQDs), antimonene QDs (AQDs), bismuth QDs (BiQDs), transition metal chalcogenide QDs (TMD QDs), and transition metal carbide or nitride MXene QDs (MQDs).

Generally, 2D-QDs maintain similar lattice structures to their 2D counterparts. The 2D layered materials possess intralayer covalent bonds and weak interlayer vdW interactions. Graphene has a flat hexagonal honeycomb structure with single-atom thickness (Fig. 2(e)).87 Unlike graphene with a flat structure, phosphorene shows a folded structure (Fig. 2(f)).88 Each P atom binds with three other P atoms in the same layer, while it binds with the fourth P atom at a parallel adjacent layer, resulting in an in-plane anisotropic structure of phosphorene.89 However, BP is easily oxidized in air. Antimony and bismuth also belong to the group V elements. Theoretical calculation results show that 2D antimonene and bismuthene are stable under ambient conditions.90,91 Different from the stable α phase orthorhombic structure of phosphorene, the most stable form of antimonene and bismuthene is the β phase rhombohedral structure with a hexagonal lattice with the buckled surface.90,91 The crystal structure of layered rhombohedral antimony is shown in Fig. 2(g).92 Theoretical calculation results show that there is a change from a semimetal to an indirect semiconductor when the thickness of gray antimony is reduced to a monolayer.90,93 Similar to antimony, bulk bismuth is a semimetal, while the single-layer bismuthene is a semiconductor.94,95 TMD is a type of 2D layered material with the chemical formula of MX2, in which M is a transition metal element (e.g., Mo, W, or Re), and X is S, Se, or Te. In the X–M–X unit layer, the M atomic layer is sandwiched between two X atomic layers (Fig. 2(h)).96 Some TMDs (e.g.; MoS2, WS2, MoSe2, and WSe2) are semiconductors with direct bandgaps in monolayers and indirect bandgaps in bulk materials.97,98 MXenes have the general chemical formula, Mn+1XnTx, in which M is a transition metal element, X is C or N, Tx is O, OH, or F, and n is 1, 2, and 3.99 Ti3C2Tx MXene was first isolated in 2011100 and several different MXenes have been experimentally prepared or theoretically predicted considering different factors such as composition and surface groups. From the side view, the MXene monolayer is composed of 2n + 1 sheets (n = 1, 2, and 3), where the X sheets are sandwiched by two M sheets (Fig. 2(i)).101

For 2D-QDs, the lateral size has influence on the bandgap energy due to the quantum confinement effect. For instance, graphene is a semimetal with a zero bandgap, but GQDs show semiconductor properties with a discrete bandgap.102 The bandgap of MoS2 QDs is ∼4.56 eV, which is higher than that of 2D MoS2.103 The bandgap of bulk MXene was about 0.1 eV, while that of Ti3C2Tx MQDs was about 2.84 eV, obtained from the absorption result.24 Recently, the influence of size on the bandgap of Ti3C2O2 QDs has been studied theoretically. Results showed that the energy gap of Ti3C2O2 QDs with H passivation changes from 2.76 to 1.14 eV when the lateral size increases.104 In addition to size, the bandgap of some 2D-QDs can also be tuned by other factors such as doping,105 and surface functionalization.106 For instance, Na doped AQDs were prepared with a direct bandgap of 0.88 eV.107 2D-QDs show a broad range of bandgap energy from the ultraviolet (UV) to IR spectral region, suggesting their potential applications in a broad spectral region.

3. Synthesis

Various methods have been used for the preparation of colloidal semiconductor NCs, such as the liquid-phase method and vapor phase epitaxial growth method. Although the vapor phase epitaxial growth method has been successfully used for the synthesis of size-tunable NCs, there are some drawbacks such as the use of expensive experimental instruments and difficulty in separating NCs from the substrates. The liquid-phase preparation method is facile and has been widely applied for the preparation of various monodispersed NCs in different solvents. The liquid-phase synthetic method consists of the bottom-up and top-down methods. In the following, the main liquid-phase preparation methods of colloidal semiconductor NCs are provided.

3.1 The hot-injection (HI) method

The HI method is one of the most commonly used bottom-up methods for the synthesis of colloidal semiconductor NCs. The HI method was first reported for the synthesis of monodispersed CdM (M = S/Se/Te) NCs by Murray et al. in 1993.108 Later, one great improvement for greener synthesis was reported by Peng et al., who used little toxic CdO instead of Cd(CH3)2 to prepare CdM (M = S/Se/Te) NCs.109,110 Typically, the precursor solution is rapidly injected into a preheated mixture solution containing the remaining precursors, ligands (or surfactants), and organic solvents, followed by a fast and complete mixing of the mixture under vigorous stirring (Fig. 3(a)111). The HI method has been used for the preparation of various colloidal semiconductor NCs including CdE,111–114 ZnE,115–117 PbE,47,118,119 Ag2E (E = S, Se, Te),120–122 InP,123 CuInS2,124 AgInS2,124 Cu–Sn–S,125 Cu2−xS,55 Cu3−xP,58 Sn:In2O3,126 CsPbX3,68 Cs4PbX6,127 MAPbBr3,128 MAPbI3,128 FAPbBr3,129 FAPbI3,130 FA0.1Cs0.9PbI3,130 FAPb(Cl1−xBrx)3,131 and Cs2SnI6132 NCs. Alloyed, doped, and heterostructure NCs such as PbS/graphene oxide,133 PbS/graphene,134 CdSe/ZnS/CdSe,135 Ag2SxSe1−x,136 InP/ZnS,137 Mn doped ZnSe,138 PbS–MAPbI3,139 and PbS–FAPbI3140 NCs can also be obtained. By changing the ratio of the chemicals (precursors, ligands, and solvents),55,141 the reaction time110 and temperature,142 the size of the metal chalcogenide NCs prepared using the HI method can be adjusted. In contrast, the nucleation and growth kinetics of the metal halide perovskite NCs are very fast (usually within a few seconds) due to the ionic nature of the chemical bonds in metal halide perovskite NCs. Therefore, the size of the CsPbX3 NCs prepared using the HI method can be adjusted by changing the type and ratio of the added chemicals,129 and the reaction temperature.68,129

The nucleation and growth mechanism based on the LaMer model is widely used to describe the kinetics of colloidal semiconductor NCs.143 A general procedure for the synthesis of monodispersed and uniform NCs requires fast nucleation and a relatively slow growth process. The nucleation time depends on the injection rate and the mass transfer in the reaction systems.144 Therefore, rapid precursor injection and vigorous stirring would lead to a short nucleation time.144 Generally, the formation process of NCs consists of three stages.56,145 In the first stage, the precursors react immediately to form monomers in the reaction system. In the second stage, the supersaturated monomers aggregate into nuclei until the monomer concentration drops below a critical threshold. In the third stage, the pre-formed nuclei grow into NCs without the formation of new nuclei. However, the LaMer mechanism did not describe the particle size evolution during the growth stage. Soon after that, Reiss and coworkers reported a diffusion model and the mechanism of focusing the particle size distribution in the growth stage, that is, the smaller particles grow more quickly than the bigger particles when there are plenty of monomers.146 If the growth is stopped when it is still in the size-focusing regime, NCs would have a narrow size distribution.74 However, sometimes, other competition growth processes such as the Ostwald ripening process should also be considered.56

By employing the HI method, monodispersed colloidal compound semiconductor NCs with high crystallinity, narrow size distribution, and good control over composition and size can be obtained. However, the HI method usually requires high temperature and is not suitable for large scale preparation at one time. Additionally, the NCs obtained using the HI method are usually dispersed in an organic solvent and further modification steps are usually required to make them well-dispersed in an inorganic solvent such as water for special applications.

3.2 Aqueous synthesis

To prepare water-soluble NCs, the aqueous bottom-up synthetic method is usually employed. The earliest method used to synthesize colloidal semiconductor NCs was to prepare CdS NCs in an aqueous system in the 1980s.42,147 Generally, the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor NCs is realized through a precipitation reaction between cationic and anionic precursors in the presence of stabilizers under flux conditions with a suitable solution pH value.148 A water-soluble thiol molecule like thioglycolic acid or 3-mercaptopropionic acid was usually used as the stabilizer. The hydrothermal reaction (Fig. 3(b)),149 microwave-assisted heating,150 or photochemical synthesis22 was also employed for improvement of the aqueous synthesis of colloidal semiconductor NCs. In the hydrothermal method, the precursors and suitable solvents are transferred to a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and the reaction takes place at high temperature and high pressure. The hydrothermal synthesis is helpful for materials that are difficult to crystallize at lower temperatures. By using the microwave strategy, heat can be distributed more evenly throughout the reactor and the material synthesis usually occurs in a very short time, even in minutes. In photochemical synthesis, chemical reactions could take place under photo-irradiation at a mild synthesis temperature without heating. The aqueous synthetic method has been used for preparing various colloidal semiconductor NCs, including CdE,151–153 ZnE,154–156 Ag2E157–159 (E = S, Se, Te), PbS,160 PbSe,161 AgInS2,162 CuInS2,163 Cu2−xS,22 Cu2−xSe,164 graphene QDs,165–167 Mo2S QDs,168–176 and MXene QDs.177–182 In addition, alloyed, doped, and core/shell heterostructure NCs such as Zn1−xCdxSe,156 Ag:CdTe,152 and CdTe/CdS183 NCs were also prepared via the aqueous route. By adjusting the type of stabilizer,184 the concentration of the chemicals,185 the reaction temperature186 and time,187 the size of the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs prepared using the aqueous based synthesis can be changed.

The hard and soft (Lewis) acids and bases (HSAB) theory could be used to qualitatively understand the thermodynamics of the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor NCs. Based on HSAB theory, the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor NCs is mainly based on the Lewis acid/base reactions. Generally, hard acids react more quickly and form stronger bonds with hard bases, while soft acids react more quickly and form stronger bonds with soft bases. In this context, HSAB theory is helpful to qualitatively understand the reactions involved and the stability of compounds. Furthermore, the classical LaMer model containing three stages is also widely used to understand the growth kinetics of the aqueous synthesis of semiconductor NCs.143 However, the cation and anion as major monomers can react very fast after the injection of the precursor solution, leading to the rapid consumption of the monomers. The Ostwald ripening process would be dominated in the growth stage if there are not plenty of monomers, resulting in NCs with a broader size distribution.56

The aqueous method usually uses more environmentally friendly materials and the experimental temperature is usually lower than that of the HI method. The products could be dispersed in water. However, the size distribution of the NCs is usually broader than that obtained using the HI method. The optical properties such as the luminescence quantum yield of the products obtained through the aqueous routes also need to be improved.

3.3 Ligand-assisted reprecipitation (LARP) method

The bottom-up LARP method is often used for the preparation of metal hybrid perovskite NCs. In 2015, Zhong's group reported the LARP synthesis of MAPbX3 QDs for the first time.189 Typically, a mixture of MABr and PbBr2 was dissolved in the polar solvent DMF (good solvent) with the ligands n-octylamine and oleic acid. The mixed solution was added dropwise into the nonpolar solvent toluene (poor solvent) under intense stirring (Fig. 3(c)).188 The solvents induce an instantaneous supersaturation and lead to rapid nucleation and growth of metal halide perovskite NCs. The as-prepared MAPbBr3 QDs are nonstoichiometric with a Br-rich surface. Besides, MAPbCl3 and MAPbI3 QDs were also prepared by changing the precursors or cation change. The LARP method has been used for the synthesis of both the organic–inorganic hybrid and all-organic metal halide perovskite NCs, including MAPbX3,189 FAPbX3,190 CsPbX3,191 Cs3Bi2X9,192 CsxFA1−xPbX3 (X = Cl/Br/I),193 MA3Bi2Br9,194 Cs3Sb2Br9,195 CsPb2Br5,196 and FAxMA1−xPbX3 (X = Br/I).197 By varying the precursor198 and ligand concentrations,199 the size of the metal halide perovskite NCs can be changed. The LARP method is based on the supersaturated recrystallization mechanism. Ions can be spontaneously separated from the solution and a new crystal would be formed when the ion concentration suppresses the saturated concentration. The experiment is performed at room temperature. However, the concentration of metal halide perovskite NCs obtained using the LARP method is low. Additionally, large amounts of toxic solvents are usually required in this method.

3.4 Top-down liquid-phase exfoliation method

The top-down liquid-phase exfoliation method is usually used to prepare 2D-QDs. While the bottom-up synthesis of 2D-QDs makes use of small-molecule precursors, the top-down method mainly uses the bulk 2D layered materials as the precursors. In the bottom-up synthesis of 2D-QDs, small-molecule precursors are aggregated to form 2D-QDs in aqueous or nonaqueous solvents through hydrothermal/microwave (introduced in Section 3.2) or solvothermal route. In the top-down methods, 2D-QDs are usually exfoliated from their native 2D layered materials via chemical or physical routes, including ultrasonication, electrochemistry, pulsed laser ablation, hydrothermal/solvothermal/microwave, and intercalation methods. Generally, functional groups and defects were first created on 2D nanosheets in these routes, resulting in the exfoliation of 2D layered materials into smaller 2D-QDs.81 Here, we mainly introduce the top-down liquid-phase exfoliation method for the synthesis of 2D-QDs.

The ultrasound-assisted technique is one common method used for the exfoliation of various bulk 2D materials into 2D-QDs. In this method, the bulk 2D material sources are mixed with suitable solvents under ultrasonication conditions. The ultrasonication energy breaks the vdW forces of the 2D layered materials, resulting in the exfoliation of the bulk 2D layered materials into 2D-QDs (Fig. 4(a)).200 During ultrasonication exfoliation, an ice bath is often used to maintain a low temperature. Besides, choosing a suitable solvent is important to determine the quality of the final product. The surface energy of the solvent is required to match that of the bulk 2D materials, resulting in effective exfoliation with a minimal energy cost.201,202N-Monthylpyrrolidinone (NMP) is a common solvent used for ultrasonication exfoliation.203,204 In addition to NMP, other organic or inorganic solvents were also employed for the exfoliation of 2D-QDs.205–207 The ultrasonication method usually requires a long time, and the yield of 2D-QDs is usually low. Therefore, sometimes, hydrothermal/solvothermal/microwave treatment, grinding, or probe sonication is used in combination with ultrasonication to help enhance the exfoliation efficiency of 2D-QDs. Various 2D-QDs such as GQDs,208 BPQDs,209 AQDs,210 BiQDs,211 TMD QDs,204 and MQDs212 were prepared. 2D-QDs prepared by using the ultrasonication exfoliation technique at a higher ultrasonication power or longer time usually exhibit smaller lateral sizes.213 In addition, as the centrifugation rate of the 2D-QD dispersions increases,214 2D-QDs with a smaller lateral size can also be obtained.

Other top-down liquid-phase exfoliation methods have also been used for the synthesis of 2D-QDs. In the electrochemistry method, the generation of free radicals leads to the exfoliation of the bulk 2D material into QDs. The electrochemistry methods have been used for preparation of GQDs,215 TMD QDs,216 phosphorene QDs (Fig. 4(b)),217 Na-doped AQDs,107 fluorinated BPQDs,218 and N-containing phosphorene QDs.217 The top-down hydrothermal/solvothermal/microwave method has been used for the fabrication of GQDs,219 TMD QDs,220 MQDs,221 and BPQDs (Fig. 4(c)).222 Moreover, S and N-codoped, N-doped, and S-doped MQDs were also obtained by using a top-down hydrothermal process.223,224 The pulsed laser ablation method has been used for the preparation of GQDs,227 BPQDs,228 MoS2 QDs,229 and WS2 QDs (Fig. 4(d)).225 In the intercalation-assisted method, the alkali metal intercalates into the interlayers of the 2D materials, resulting in a larger layer distance and weaker vdW force. The intercalation-assisted method has been used for synthesis of TMD QDs (Fig. 4(e)),226 MQDs230 and monolayered GQDs.231

The ultrasonication assisted method is simple; however, it usually requires a long sonication time and the yield is usually low. The hydrothermal/solvothermal/microwave method is simple and controllable. However, the disadvantage of the hydrothermal/solvothermal/microwave method is that it usually requires a high temperature. The pulsed laser ablation strategy is simple and ultrafast. However, this method needs a pulsed laser equipment. The electrochemistry method has the advantage of low cost for mass production. However, the electrochemistry method usually requires a complex post-treatment process. The intercalation-assisted method is suggested to be the most suitable method for the large-scale synthesis of monolayer 2D-QDs. However, the intercalation-assisted method may introduce extra elements.

4. NLO properties of colloidal semiconductor NCs

Nonlinear optics has aroused much interest since the observation of optical harmonic generation by Franken et al. in quartz in 1961.232 The third-order NLO effects such as saturable absorption (SA), two-photon absorption (TPA), and optical Kerr effect are the commonly observed processes and are thus widely used in various fields such as ultrashort pulse laser,233 imaging,234 and optical switch.235 Compared with their bulk counterparts, semiconductor NCs usually exhibit stronger NLO response due to the modulation of their electronic structure and electronic density of state by structural low dimensions.236 In this section, the recent progress on the third-order NLO properties of the colloidal semiconductor NCs is introduced.

4.1 Fundamentals of nonlinear optics and experimental methods

Under the excitation of an intense laser, the electric polarization intensity vector P of the material can be written as follows:
P = ε0χ(1)E + ε0χ(2)E2 + ε0χ(3)E3 +⋯
where, E is the incident electric field, and ε0 is the vacuum dielectric constant. χ(1), χ(2), and χ(3) are the linear, second- and third-order nonlinear susceptibilities, respectively. χ(1) describes the linear optical effects. If the incident light field is high, χ(n) (n ≥ 2) becomes significant. χ(2) is related to second-order NLO effects. χ(3) is related to third-order NLO effects. χ(n) (n typically larger than 5) is related to n-th-order NLO effects. Generally, the interaction strength of the NLO process decreases with n. The second-order NLO effects occur in a material that lacks the inversion symmetry center. On the contrary, third-order NLO effects are allowed in all materials. In this section, we mainly introduce the third-order NLO effects and corresponding parameters. The third-order NLO susceptibility χ(3) is a complex number with a real and imaginary part. Re(χ(3)) (the real part of χ(3)) is related to the nonlinear refraction (NLR) effect of the material, while Im(χ(3)) (the imaginary part of χ(3)) is related to the nonlinear absorption (NLA) effect of the material. The relationship between Reχ(3), Imχ(3), NLA coefficient β, and NLR index n2 is as follows:237 Im(χ(3)) = n02ε0c2β/ω, and Re(χ(3)) = 2n02ε0cn2. Here, n0 is the linear refraction, ε0 is the vacuum dielectric constant, c is light velocity, and ω is the light frequency.

SA and reverse SA (RSA) are two kinds of NLA response. In the SA effect, the absorption coefficient of the material gradually decreases and tends to saturation when the incident intensity is large. SA is useful for ultrashort pulse laser generation applications.233 For semiconductors, SA generally happens in the resonant region where the photon energy is larger than or equal to the bandgap of the materials through single-photon absorption238 and can be explained using the Pauli blocking principle.239,240 Under the resonant conditions, electrons in the valence band can jump to the conduction band through single-photon absorption. With increasing light intensity, the upper energy states are gradually occupied by the electrons from the valence band. Since it is impossible for two electrons to occupy the same energy state, further light absorption is blocked and SA occurs. However, sometimes SA also occurs in the nonresonant region where the photon energy is lower than the bandgap of the materials.241–245

In the RSA effect, the absorption of the material increases with increase in incident intensity. The absorption becomes larger and the transmission lowers when the incident laser intensity exceeds the threshold. RSA can be used in areas such as optical limiters.246 The RSA properties of nanostructured materials is complicated and mainly due to the two-photon absorption (TPA), excited state absorption (ESA), or free carrier absorption (FCA) mechanism. TPA effect refers to the phenomenon that electrons absorb two photons at the same time and jump from one state (usually the ground state) to a higher energy (usually an excited state). The FCA is an intraband absorption process under single-photon excitation. FCA requires a pre-existing carrier in the excited band (an electron in the conduction band, or a hole in the valence band), where the carrier can move freely. FCA needs the assistance of phonons or impurities to conserve the momentum.247 ESA is a process in which the electron is excited from an excited state to a higher energy excited state. One difference between ESA and TPA is that ESA can occur through single-photon excitation while TPA cannot.

The NLR property is also called the optical Kerr effect. When the incident intensity is low, the refractive index of the material does not change. When the material is excited by a strong light, the refractive index of the material will no longer be a constant. The relationship between the refractive index of the material (n) and the incident light intensity (I) is as follows: n = n0 + n2I. Here, n0 and n2 are linear and nonlinear refraction indices, respectively. Due to the Gaussian distribution of the laser power densities, the light field intensity in the center of the material is different from that at the edge when the laser passes through the material. When the sign of the NLR index n2 is positive, the material will focus the laser beam (self-focusing effect). When the sign of n2 is negative, the material will make the beam diverge (self-defocusing effect). The NLR process is attributed to the electronic Kerr effect, or the thermal lensing, etc.

The NLA effect has been described using different models. In the simplest equation, absorption coefficient (α) of the sample can be described as follows: α(I) = α0 + βI, where α0 and β are the linear absorption and NLA coefficients, respectively. A model describing the SA process can be described as follows:240α(I) = αs/(1 + I/Is) + αns, where Is is the saturation intensity, I is the incident intensity, αs and αns are SA and non-saturable absorption coefficient, respectively. When the incident intensity is low, α is equal to (αs + αns), while α is equal to αns when the intensity is strong. The maximum change of the absorption is equal to αs, which is also known as the modulation depth ΔR. Is, ΔR, and αns can be used to describe the SA performance of materials. With the concurrence of SA and TPA, the above expression can be written as follows:248α(I) = αs/(1 + I/Is) + αns + βI. In this expression, the term (βI) is related to TPA. Moreover, considering both the NLA and NLR processes, a figure of merit (FOM, FOM = n2/βλ)152 can be used to describe the NLO characteristics of the materials.

There are many methods to measure the third-order NLO susceptibility coefficients, such as the optical Z-scan measurement249 and the balanced twin-detector method.250 By using the open- and closed-aperture Z-scan techniques, the values and signs of β and n2 can be achieved simultaneously. Then, Im(χ(3)), Re(χ(3)), χ(3), and FOM values can be calculated using the above mentioned formula. ΔR, Is, and αns can also be obtained. The sample is placed after the lens and can be moved around the focal point of the lens in the Z-direction (Fig. 5(a)242). For the open-aperture Z-scan technique, all the signal transmitted through the sample is collected by a detector. Through the open-aperture curve, the NLA coefficient β can be obtained. For the closed-aperture Z-scan measurement, an aperture is located in the center of the beam before the detector. Only a small portion of the signal from the center of the transmitted beam can be collected by the detector. The signal collected by the detector is thus not only dependent on the transmission of the material but also the beam diameter after the material, which is related to the beam induced refractive index change of the material. After acquiring the measured open- and closed-aperture Z-scan curves, the NLR index n2 of materials can be obtained. When a single laser beam passes through an NLO material, the transverse distribution of the beam causes an additional phase. The measurement of the additional phase using the Z-scan technique is realized by measuring the amplitude change of the incident laser at different positions by diffraction conversion. The Z-scan technique is one of the most commonly used methods to measure the NLO properties of materials. With the balanced twin-detector system (Fig. 5(b)250), the SA characteristics of materials including ΔR and Is can be obtained simultaneously.251 The balanced twin-detector measurement method is easy to operate and is one of the usually applied techniques for measuring the SA characteristics of materials.

Recent studies on the NLO properties of conventional metal chalcogenide NCs have mainly focused on CdSe, PbS, and PbSe QDs. When the intensity of the incident laser (532 nm, ns) increases, the NLO process of the CdSe QDs changes from SA to RSA (Fig. 6(a–d)).3 The SA properties are due to ground state bleaching and the RSA is caused by FCA of CdSe QDs.3 PbS QDs with a small size (3.2 nm) show TPA and NLR properties in the wavelength range of 1.3 to 2.0 μm (Fig. 6(e)).252 This can be explained as follows. The bandgap of PbS QDs with a smaller size of 3.2 nm can be estimated to be around 1.27 eV, according to the approximate formula, which is larger than the incident photon energy.252 Electrons cannot be excited to the higher level through the single-photon process. With the energy of two photons in the wavelength range of 1.3 to 2.0 μm, electrons can jump to the higher energy level through the TPA process. Therefore, TPA process can be observed in this work. PbSe QDs (7.3 nm) exhibit TPA and NLR properties in the wavelength range of 1.6 to 2.0 μm (Fig. 6(e)).252 The bandgap value of PbSe QDs obtained from the luminescence spectra is about 0.75 eV. When the incident wavelength is in the range of 1.7 to 2.0 μm, the incident energy is lower than the bandgap of PbSe QDs, indicating that electrons cannot be excited to the higher energy level through single-photon absorption but TPA process.252 However, no SA effect is observed when the incident wavelength is 1600 nm, which is close to one-photon transition. This is due to band anisotropy, where the breaking of the inversion symmetry of the wave functions has to be included, as theoretically predicted for PbS and PbSe QDs by Nootz and coworkers.254 In some other reports, the PbS QD-based film exhibits SA properties under excitation wavelengths of 1550 nm (Fig. 6(f)),253 1565 nm,255 1566.58 nm,256 and 1568 nm.257 The PbSe QD-based film shows SA properties under excitation of 1064 nm.258 In ref. 255–257, the estimated bandgaps (0.73 eV, 0.72 eV, and 0.72 eV) of PbS QDs (6.8 nm,255 7 nm,256 and 7 nm257) are smaller than the incident energy. So, the SA process can be observed in these three studies. In ref. 258, the incident energy is 1.165 eV, which is larger than the bandgap of PbSe QDs (1.02 eV) as estimated from the approximate formula between the bandgap and the size.252 So, SA occurred in this study.

The NLO properties of colloidal metal chalcogenide NCs have been reported to be affected by factors that are related to the materials such as size, composition (pristine, doped, multicomponent), structure (core/shell, core/shell/shell) and state (dispersion, film). For instance, CdSe QDs with various sizes (2.1, 3.3, 4.7 nm) show similar NLA performance; however, the values of the measured NLA coefficients were different.111 CdSe QDs with a size of 3.3 nm have a larger NLO susceptibility of 1.81 × 10−10 esu because of resonance absorption. In another study, CdSe/ZnS/CdSe core/shell/shell QDs exhibit a larger TPA coefficient and NLR index compared with that of CdSe and CdSe/ZnS QDs under excitation of 532 nm, 30 ps pulse laser.135 The larger NLA coefficient of CdSe/ZnS/CdSe core/shell/shell QDs is due to the quantum confinement effect, as well as the localization of charge carriers and electronic passivation of QD surface in the core/shell structure. Au or Ag nanoparticles exhibit LSPR properties and the enhanced localized electric field is one way to enhance the NLO properties of NCs. For example, the NLR index near the band edge is enhanced more than 35 times by Ag ion doping.152 The enhanced NLR is due to the increase in the excitonic absorption and local electric field in the Ag-doped CdTe NCs.152 The fabrication method,259 dispersant,260 or stabilizing agent261 also has effects on the NLO properties of colloidal metal chalcogenide NCs. For example, PbS QDs prepared using a cation-exchange method show RSA properties and self-focusing properties at 532 nm.259 The RSA properties of the prepared PbS QDs may be due to FCA. The difference in the NLO activity of PbS QDs prepared using different preparation methods is probably due to the difference in the intrinsic crystal nature. The excitation conditions such as wavelength (resonant or nonresonant) and pulse width also exert influence on the NLO properties of the colloidal metal chalcogenide NCs.

5. NLO devices based on colloidal semiconductor NCs

NLO devices fabricated based on the optical nonlinearities of materials have promising applications in many fields. For instance, materials with SA properties could be used as saturable absorbers (a type of material with SA properties) to obtain ultrashort pulsed lasers.233 The ultrashort pulsed laser plays important roles in medicine,9 and industrial materials processing10 benefiting from its narrow pulse width, large peak power and pulse energy. Many colloidal semiconductor NCs exhibit excellent SA properties, indicating that they are promising candidates as saturable absorbers for the generation of pulsed laser. Since 2016, ultrashort pulsed lasers generated by using the colloidal semiconductor NCs as the saturable absorbers have been developed rapidly.58,59,245,255,264,285,286 In this section, we mainly introduce the pulsed lasers (fiber and solid-state) achieved based on the colloidal semiconductor NCs as saturable absorbers. In addition, other NLO devices including the lasing devices, as well as Kerr switches and wavelength converters based on the colloidal semiconductor NCs are also mentioned.

5.1 Saturable absorbers and pulse laser technologies

The fiber and solid-state lasers are two main methods to obtain pulse lasers. The fiber laser uses fibers as the gain media while crystals, glasses, or ceramic materials are usually employed as the gain media in the solid-state laser. In both lasers, there are two main modulation technologies (active and passive) to turn the continuous-wave (CW) output into a train of optical pulses.1,287 In the active modulation technology, electronic devices controlled by an external driving power source are used. In contrast, in the passive modulation technology, a saturable absorber is inserted into the laser cavity to transform CW into a pulsed laser output by either Q-switching or mode-locking. The passive modulation technology has the advantages of being simple, compact, and inexpensive. Therefore, passive Q-switching and mode-locking are currently two of the main technologies to obtain ultrashort pulsed lasers.

The passive Q-switching technology can be used to obtain high energy pulses (usually μJ to mJ). In the passive Q-switching technique, the cavity loss and the Q-factor are periodically adjusted so as to obtain high energy pulses. In the early stage, the Q-factor is low when the SA performance of the saturable absorber material does not occur; energy can only exist in the form of particle number inversion and continuously accumulates. This would lead to the SA performance of the saturable absorber and a sudden increase of the Q-factor. The gain of the laser will greatly exceed the loss, and the accumulated energy will be released to produce a high energy pulse. There is a formula for the criterion of a passive Q-switching laser.324,325 From this criterion, it can be seen that the properties of the laser crystal (long upper-level lifetimes), saturable absorber (relatively long recovery times and large modulation depths), and short linear cavity are required to be considered. However, blindly increasing the modulation depth of the saturable absorbers would increase the cavity loss; so, it is necessary to keep a balance between cavity gain and loss.

The mode-locking technique is usually used to obtain an ultrashort laser pulse (usually ps to fs). When a saturable absorber is put in the cavity, only the “spike” components of the spontaneous emission noise from the gain medium survive. These components are then amplified by the gain medium and reshaped by the saturable absorber. After iteration of this process, a pulse train is produced.1 In the frequency domain, when the optical phase of each longitudinal cavity resonance mode keeps constant or locked to each other, an ultrashort laser pulse is obtained. There is also a theoretical formula for the criterion of a passive mode-locking laser.324,325 According to this criterion, suitable parameters of the saturable absorber (modulation depth, saturation intensity/energy/fluence), gain medium, and the laser cavity are important for mode-locking lasers. The product of modulation depth and saturable fluence should satisfy the criterion. In addition, two other conditions also need to be satisfied, i.e., the bandgap of the saturable absorber should be smaller than that of the operating laser and the carrier relaxation time should be shorter than the cavity round-trip time.

The advantages of colloidal semiconductor NCs as saturable absorbers for the generation of pulsed lasers mainly include the tunable and broadband bandgap, good SA performance, ultrafast relaxation, and ease of synthesis and integration. First, the bandgap of colloidal semiconductor NCs can be tuned in a broad range from the UV to the MIR spectral region by simply changing their size or composition. Second, lots of colloidal semiconductor NCs show good SA performance. In addition, compared with their quantum-well counterparts, colloidal semiconductor NCs could, in principle, exhibit lower saturation fluence.292 For instance, the saturation intensity of the CdTe/CdS core/shell QDs (3.0 nm) is smaller than that of graphene and MoS2 dispersions.293 Third, some colloidal semiconductor NCs exhibit an ultrafast relaxation time. Many colloidal semiconductor NCs are direct bandgap materials and their corresponding carrier lifetimes are relatively short, suggesting that the direct bandgap materials have superiority in realizing ultrashort pulses. For example, Cu2−xS NCs show an ultrafast response (∼315 fs at 1.3 μm),59 which offers a great advantage for the modulation of ultrashort optical signals. Finally, the preparation and integration methods of colloidal semiconductor NCs are facile.

Once the colloidal semiconductor NCs are prepared, their integration into fiber or solid-state laser cavities to form saturable absorbers is a key issue that affects their subsequent pulsed laser generation applications. The easiest method to construct a saturable absorber is to deposit the colloidal NCs onto the mirrors directly. Saturable absorbers fabricated by this approach are often used for solid-state lasers. For fiber lasers, different techniques have been employed to incorporate the NC-based saturable absorbers into the fiber laser system. Here, three common techniques are introduced. One simple method is directly depositing the NCs onto the fiber end facet or onto a tapered fiber. There are two ways for the direct deposition method. One way is that the NC solution is drop cast on the fiber connector end using a syringe, followed by evaporation. The other way is that the fiber ferrule connector end is flooded into NC suspension, followed by keeping the connector vertical during drying. The direct deposition procedure is simple for integration of colloidal semiconductor NCs into fiber. However, the NC film is not uniform and the thickness of the NC film cannot be controlled. Polymers are often introduced to help fabricate a uniform NC film. The second method is that the NCs are mixed with polymer to construct a uniform NC film. This type of saturable absorber has a simple structure; however, it suffers from a low thermal damage threshold due to the polymer host. To enhance the thermal damage threshold, the third method is based on microfiber. The NCs are coated onto the cross section of the polished microfiber based on the evanescent field interaction. When using this method, the linear transmission spectra and balanced twin-detector measurement technique are usually used to further characterize the integrated microfiber based saturable absorber to evaluate whether the integration process was successful or not.

Passive mode-locking fiber lasers at around 1 μm and 1.55 μm were achieved by using the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs as the saturable absorbers. In 2017, CdSe QDs296 and PbSe QDs258 were used as the saturable absorbers in mode-locked Yb-doped fiber (YDF) to generate pulse lasers centered at around 1 μm. In the following report, PbS QDs were used as the saturable absorbers in Er-doped fiber (EDF).253 In previous work, the long-time operation of laser pulse based on the QDs has seldom been reported. The reason may be that QDs are unstable due to the trap states on their surface. Constructing a core/shell structure is an effective way to passivate the trap state and improve the photostability of QDs. Core/shell structures such as PbS/CdS QDs were prepared and used as saturable absorbers for the generation of fiber lasers.297 The laser pulse spectra were measured over 10 h and a long-time stable mode-locked operation was obtained. PbS/CdS core/shell QDs have a type-I structure, in which the bandgap of PbS core is confined by the CdS shell. The electron and hole states are confined in the PbS core and the trap states at the PbS surface are passivated, improving the optical stability. Up to now, the shortest pulse duration of the generated mode-locked fiber laser based on the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs was 559 fs with PbS QDs as the saturable absorber in the EDFL.253 The maximum repetition rate of the generated mode-locked fiber laser by using the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs was 14.5 MHz with CdSe QDs as the saturable absorber in the EDFL.296 These results show that the conventional metal chalcogenide NCs could be used as the saturable absorbers for the generation of passively mode-locked fiber lasers in the NIR spectral region.

Plasmonic semiconductor NCs were used as saturable absorbers for the generation of passive mode-locking fiber lasers at around 1.55 μm. In 2016, Guo and coworkers prepared Cu2−xS NCs with an LSPR peak centered at around 1300 nm.59 The plasmonic semiconductor NCs show ultrafast NLO response and large modulation depth as large as 63.3%. Mode-locking fiber laser at 1562.6 nm was first achieved.59 In addition, the generated laser pulses remained stable over 2 h. In the following report, Cu3–xP NCs were used as a saturable absorber to generate mode-locked fiber lasers at 1570 nm with a repetition rate as large as 31 MHz.61 The preparation of Cu3–xP and Cu2−xS NCs often requires a high temperature and an air-free environment. Recently, it has been shown that CuS NCs prepared using a photochemical method show good stability and broad absorption in the visible to NIR region.22 A mode-locking fiber laser at 1.55 μm was obtained based on CuS NCs. So far, the shortest pulse width and the maximum repetition rate of the mode-locking fiber laser with the plasmonic semiconductor NCs were 295 fs (with Cu2−xS NCs based saturable absorber59) and 31 MHz (with Cu3–xP NC based saturable absorber61). These results indicate that the plasmonic semiconductor NCs also have promising applications in the generation of passive mode-locking fiber lasers in the NIR region.

When using metal halide perovskite NCs as the saturable absorber, mode-locked fiber lasers centered at the NIR and MIR spectral region (around 1 μm, 1.6 μm, and 2 μm) were achieved. All the studies based on the metal halide perovskite NCs as the saturable absorbers employ CsPbBr3 NCs because of their structural stability. Specifically, In 2016, CsPbBr3 NCs were prepared and used as a saturable absorber for mode-locked Yb-doped fiber laser (YDFL) for the first time by Zhou and coworkers.245 Mode-locking single pulses centered at 1076 nm with a duration of 216 ps are obtained in a polarization-maintained YDFL. In the following report, a dissipative soliton with a pulse duration of 14.4 ps at 1600 nm was achieved in the mode-locked EDFL based on CsPbBr3 QDs.273 With a 25 m single-mode fiber, the pulse width can be compressed to 1.046 ps. Recently, CsPbBr3 NCs have been used as a saturable absorber for mode-locking fiber laser at 1992.9 nm (Fig. 10).274 Until now, the shortest pulse duration of the generated mode-locked fiber lasers based on CsPbBr3 NCs as the saturable absorbers is 1.046 ps at 1600 nm.273 The maximum repetition rate of the obtained mode-locked fiber lasers with CsPbBr3 NC based saturable absorber was 16.6 MHz in the Tm-doped fiber laser (TDFL) at 1992.9 nm.274 These studies suggest that the metal halide perovskite NCs are other potential candidates to achieve a passively mode-locked fiber laser output in the NIR and MIR wavelengths.

The usage of 2D-QDs as the saturable absorber for pulsed laser generation was first reported in 2016 by Xu and coworkers.264 In their report, ultrasmall BPQDs were employed as saturable absorbers for pulsed laser generation at the telecommunication band in a passive mode-locking EDFL. The results confirm the creation of an ultrashort laser pulse with a width of about 1.08 ps at 1567.5 nm. Since the first report, various 2D-QDs have been applied as the saturable absorbers for pulsed laser generation applications. Very recently, it has been shown that MXene Ti3C2Tx QDs show wideband SA properties from 800 to 1550 nm.24 MXene Ti3C2Tx QDs were used for the generation of ultrashort pulses in mode-locking EDFL or YDFL with a pulse duration as short as 170 fs. Additionally, an ultranarrow fiber laser with a full width at half maximum of 5 kHz is obtained. Until now, the shortest pulse duration and the maximum repetition rate of the obtained mode-locked fiber laser based on 2D-QDs as the saturable absorbers were 115 fs (from GaTe QDs) at 1530.9 nm323 and 19.347 MHz (from WS2 QDs) at 1561.1 nm,319 respectively. These results show that 2D-QDs are promising saturable absorbers for nonlinear photonic applications in mode-locked fiber lasers in the NIR spectral window.

5.4 Colloidal semiconductor NCs as saturable absorbers for solid-state lasers

BPQDs can also be used for all-optical signal processing applications. The nonlinear optical Kerr effect and the corresponding optical signal processing application based on BPQDs were reported in 2019.235 By using the BPQD-decorated microfiber, an all-optical Kerr switch with an extinction ratio of ∼20 dB and an FWM-based wavelength converter with a −40 dB conversion efficiency were demonstrated. 6. Conclusion and outlook

The research and application of colloidal semiconductor NCs have attracted increasing interest in recent years. This review summarizes the recent advances of colloidal semiconductor NCs, including the liquid-phase preparation methods, third-order NLO properties and corresponding NLO device applications. The study focuses on four classes of colloidal semiconductor NCs including conventional metal chalcogenide semiconductor NCs, plasmonic semiconductor NCs, metal halide perovskite NCs, and 2D-QDs. The basic properties (structure and bandgap energy) of these colloidal semiconductor NCs are provided. The liquid-phase synthesis methods for preparing colloidal semiconductor NCs are introduced, including the HI, aqueous synthesis, LARP, and the top-down liquid-phase exfoliation methods. Then the recent advances of the third-order NLO properties (mainly SA, RSA, and optical Kerr effect) of colloidal semiconductor NCs are summarized. Based on these NLO properties, many NLO device applications are demonstrated. We mainly focus on the ultrashort pulsed laser generation applications that benefited from the excellent SA properties of the colloidal semiconductor NCs. In addition, other NLO devices such as the optical gain medium in frequency upconverted stimulated emission (lasing) and all-optical signal processing devices are also mentioned.

The extensive NLO device applications of colloidal semiconductor NCs have experienced rapid development and a series of important results have been achieved. However, some challenges remain to be addressed. First, how to precisely control the parameters of the materials (e.g. size, composition, structure, and dispersants) by changing their preparation conditions (e.g., the synthesis method, reaction temperature, reagent types and concentration, the pH value of the solution, and reaction time) to achieve the required NLO properties, so as to meet the application requirements of specific NLO devices. On one hand, for special NLO device applications, some important NLO parameters of colloidal semiconductor NCs should be considered. With the design criterion of passive Q-switching and mode-locking lasers, suitable SA parameters should be explored for passively Q-switched (large modulation depth and long carrier relaxation) or mode-locked lasers (suitable modulation depth, saturable intensity, and recovery time). For all-optical switches, materials with a large NLR index (n2) and a small NLA coefficient (β) are usually needed to satisfy the requirement of good nonlinear FOMs. On the other hand, the material parameters that influence the NLO performance of colloidal semiconductor NCs mainly include their size, composition, structure, and dispersant. Therefore, the design and preparation of colloidal semiconductor NCs with precise material parameters are beneficial to obtain colloidal semiconductor NCs with controllable NLO performance. In addition, it is significant to study the nonlinear optics theory of colloidal semiconductor NCs in depth for improvement of their NLO properties and development of NLO device applications. Second, how to prepare colloidal semiconductor NC-based NLO devices with high quality. For practical device applications, semiconductor NC dispersions cannot be used directly and the preparation of NC-based films is usually required. The thickness, uniformity, and high damage threshold of the NC-based film need to be considered. Third, how to extend the application scope of colloidal semiconductor NCs. Some colloidal semiconductor NCs show excellent SA characteristics and can be used as saturable absorbers for the generation of ultrashort pulse laser. However, the current research is mainly in the NIR wavelength region. The research in other wavelength regions, especially the MIR region, is still not enough. Developing more colloidal semiconductor NCs with a suitable bandgap or strong absorption in the MIR region is needed. Fourth, the commercialization of NLO devices based on colloidal semiconductor NCs is still challenging. This challenge mainly lies in the long-term stability of device performance and low manufacturing cost. Some facile techniques for synthesizing colloidal semiconductor NCs with high quality, as well as for coating and packing the NCs, need to be explored for improving the stability of NLO device performance and reducing the cost.

For further development of the colloidal semiconductor NC-based photonic nanodevices, the following aspects may be considered. (1) Attempts to explore novel colloidal semiconductor NCs with excellent NLO properties, tunable and broad bandgap, and high damage threshold are still needed. (2) The core/shell structure or vdW-integrated heterostructure made by two or more suitable materials is expected to have excellent properties and studies on NC-based core/shell structures or vdW heterostructures are also exciting. (3) Exploration of efficient methods to obtain high quality NC film. It is reported that the NC film was prepared by using the electrospinning technique276 or the in situ inkjet printing strategy,331 indicating efficient ways to prepare uniform NC films. In addition to the electrospinning method and in situ inkjet printing strategy, other strategies are also required to be explored to fabricate high quality NC film. (4) More attempts to improve the NLO device parameters are expected. For example, the output pulsed laser performance could be further improved via many routes, such as improving the stability of the semiconductor NC-based saturable absorber, developing a suitable incorporation method, and optimizing the cavity design.
