Abstract

Due to its ultrabroadband optical absorption, large theoretical surface area, outstanding thermal conductivity and massless electron transportation, graphene has been considered as a prospective candidate in the applications of energy conversion and storage. However, the gapless energy band structure and the limited electrical conductivity restrict the capability of pristine graphene in catalytic reactions, which holds the key in energy conversion applications. Doping graphene with metals and non-metals individually or synergistically could create a highly porous structure and open a critical bandgap to enhance the electrical and photocatalytic properties of graphene, and thus enable a broad range of energy-related applications. Herein, we comprehensively review the recent advances in the research field of doping graphene and related applications in energy conversion and storage. We first introduce the motivation for doping graphene, and then present an in-depth understanding of different doping mechanisms, and discuss the resultant structural modifications and novel properties of doped graphene materials. The review also discusses and compares the principles and the developments in various synthesis methods for efficiently doping different types of graphene materials, including pristine graphene and graphene oxide. Following that, the recent applications of doped graphene in energy conversion and storage devices, namely hydrogen generation, fuel cells, CO2 reduction, secondary batteries, and supercapacitors, are demonstrated. Finally, the remaining challenges in the research field and an outlook on the future research directions of doped graphene for energy-related applications are presented.

1. Introduction

The energy crisis has emerged as a widespread global concern. To address this critical issue, extensive efforts are devoted to exploring state-of-the-art energy conversion applications, such as hydrogen generation to provide solar-to-hydrogen power,31 portable fuel cells converting chemical potential energy to useable electricity,39 and electrochemical CO2 reduction to produce hydrocarbon fuels and useable chemicals.17 Most feasible power conversion devices require well-suited catalysts with high photo-absorption, a large number of active sites, decent stability and fast charge transfer for highly efficient photochemical reactions. Apart from that, high-performance energy storage devices have also been investigated, notably supercapacitors5052 and secondary batteries.13,32,53 Electrode materials in storage devices necessitate good specific capacity, a highly conductive porous structure for better ion transportation, and good electrode/electrolyte wettability. Therefore, the development of cutting-edge materials with outstanding characteristics for both energy conversion and storage is highly desirable.

Carbonaceous materials, featuring excellent conductivity, low cost, good stability and a large potential window, play a pivotal role in both scientific research and industrial applications. Carbon has been explored in different allotropes, namely graphite, amorphous carbon, diamond, fullerene, carbon nanotubes, carbon onions and graphene.54 Graphene, a single atomic layer of graphite, is the thinnest, lightest and strongest material. The high transparency, large specific surface area (2630 m2 g1), and structural flexibility endow graphene-based materials with outstanding thermal and electrical conductivities, as well as unique physicochemical, photo/electrochemical and photo/electrocatalytic properties.55,56 Given these merits, graphene-based materials play a critical role in next-generation energy conversion and storage applications, including hydrogen generation, fuel cells, CO2 reduction, secondary batteries, and supercapacitors.17,5762

Recent developments in graphene-based materials for energy conversion and storage devices have highlighted the need to further improve their electronic and photocatalytic properties. However, zero-bandgap pristine graphene behaves as a semimetal with massless electrons because the valence band is adjacent to the conduction band. This means that there is no electronic state and electronhole separation inside the pristine graphene energy structure, and catalytic reactions for energy conversion cannot occur. Additionally, the defect-free structure of pure graphene is unfavourable for ion transportation in storage applications.63 To improve the performance of graphene for energy conversion applications, it is desirable to regulate the electronic structure and achieve more efficient charge separation and more reactive sites participating in catalytic reactions. For energy storage, a highly porous structure with excellent conductivity is more favourable for charge and ion transportation through the graphene network, leading to a higher energy/power density of devices. As well documented, chemical doping is an effective strategy featuring low cost, scalable production and high efficiency. It can create more charge carriers (electrons and holes) by shifting the Fermi level and creating a sizable bandgap in the graphene electronic structure.45,6466 The foreign elements can bond with carbon atoms by either sharing valence electrons or transferring electrons to make graphene more conductive, create efficient electrochemically active sites and defects in the carbon backbone. Consequently, the charge carriers at active sites directly participate in chemical reactions to boost catalytic abilities for energy conversion, whereas higher conductivity and structural imperfection inducing channels for ion diffusion and transportation are suitable for energy storage.

To date, various types of dopants, including metalloids (e.g., B and Si), non-metals (e.g., N, P, and S), halogens (e.g., F, Br, and I), and metals (e.g., Co, Fe, Mn, Ni, Au, and Bi), have been studied.25,6776 There have been several review articles on the synthetic approaches, properties and applications of doped graphene for energy conversion and storage.75,7780 Nonetheless, they only covered the doping mechanism and properties of doped graphene in brief, focusing the discussion mainly on the conventional synthesis approaches and applications of metal-free dopants, such as B, N, P, and S. A more focused review in terms of fundamental doping mechanisms and related energy applications is critical and urgently needed for the field but remains missing.

In this article, we aim to cover not only substitutional metal-free dopants but also metal dopants, providing an in-depth understanding of structure and property transformation based on different doping mechanisms. Next, we also review the latest synthesis methods, photo/electrochemical and photo/electrocatalytic properties, followed by the comparison of the performance of doped graphene for energy conversion and storage.

As shown in Fig. 1, the main objective of this review is to provide an insight into two major mechanisms of chemically doped graphene, which are substitutional doping and surface transfer doping.80 It will then go on to the structure and properties of doped graphene and recent synthesis methods. Several latest applications of doped graphene, particularly in energy conversion and storage areas, such as hydrogen generation, fuel cells, CO2 reduction, secondary batteries, and supercapacitors, are systematically summarized. Finally, the conclusion and prospects for future research on doped graphene in energy-related fields are identified.

2. Doping mechanism, structure and properties

2.1 Mechanism

Pristine graphene originally is a gapless semiconductor having the Fermi level at the precise Dirac point of the energy band structure (Fig. 3a). This Fermi level can be downshifted or upshifted through chemical doping, forming either p-type or n-type doped graphene and creating a tuneable bandgap between the valence and conduction bands.66,81,82 Besides, the incursion of dopants in the carbon lattice could modulate the structure that influences the electrical, electrochemical, optical and photo/electrocatalytic properties of doped graphene.83 Therefore, a profound understanding of the mechanisms, structure and properties of doped graphene is of essence for the future commercialization of graphene-based materials. This section reviews the key aspects of doping mechanisms that affect the structure and electrical and photo/electrocatalytic properties of doped graphene, which determine their performance in energy conversion and storage applications.
Fig. 3 Illustration of the energy diagram of (a) p-type doped graphene and expanded work function; (b) n-type doped graphene and reduced work function. Structure of doped-graphene by: (c) substitutional doping; (d) p-type surface transfer doping; (e) n-type surface transfer doping.

Chemical doping refers to covalent or non-covalent incorporation of heteroatoms into the graphene network. It can be categorised into: (i) substitutional doping and (ii) surface transfer doping.81,84 Both mechanisms are illustrated by energy diagrams in Fig. 3a and b. Substitutional doping means substituting carbon atoms with heteroatoms, which form covalent bonds between host carbon and doped atoms. Capable heteroatoms for substitutional doping include metalloids and non-metals, such as B, Si, N, P, S, F and Cl. The number of valence electrons of these elements compared to that of C atoms decides the formation of p-type and n-type doped graphene semiconductors. Particularly, B, with fewer valence electrons as seen in Fig. 3a, induces charge polarization in the carbon network and generates holes in the valence band, shifting the Fermi energy towards the valence band, results in p-type doping. Conversely, most non-metal elements (such as N and P) with more valence electrons than C will serve as electron-donors to the conduction band of graphene, shifting the Fermi level upwards (Fig. 3b), resulting in n-type doping. Accordingly, the work function (WF), defined by the difference between vacuum energy and the Fermi level, is expanded if the Fermi energy downshifts or narrowed if the Fermi energy upshifts. It was confirmed by Lazar et al. that the WF increased by 1.26 eV after p-type B-doping and the WF decreased by 1.21 eV after n-type N-doping.64 Freire and co-workers found that Fermi energy was ineffectively altered by Si doping, with only 10% reduction of the WF.85 It might be attributed to the similar number of valence electrons in Si and C. The variation of the WF is one of the essential parameters to develop a catalytic material for specific energy applications, for instance, the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) in fuel cells.8688 Cheon et al. presented that lowering the WF of graphene by chemical doping can reduce the energetic barrier for electron withdrawing from the catalyst surface to reduce adsorbed O2, thereby enhancing the activity of the ORR.87

In contrast to substitutional doping, surface transfer doping relies on charge separation at interfaces by adsorbing dopants on the graphene surface. Electrons can be donated or extracted from carbon atoms to nearby adsorbates, so carbon atoms and dopants are non-covalently bonded. Most metals, metal compounds (e.g., metal oxides and metal sulfides) and heavy halogens (Br and I) can physically adsorb onto graphene through surface transfer doping by hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interaction, -stacking interaction and van der Waals force.71,72,8993 Additional elements can convert graphene into p-type or n-type semiconductors based on the energy positions of molecular orbitals compared to the Fermi energy of graphene. If the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of dopants is lower than the Fermi energy of graphene, these doping atoms will extract electrons from the graphene energy structure to form p-type semiconductors, lowering the Fermi level and expanding the WF (Fig. 3a). On the other hand, graphene will receive electrons from dopants to behave as an n-type semiconductor if its Fermi level is lower than the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of dopants, leading to the reduction of the WF (Fig. 3b). The non-covalent bonds between adsorbates and carbon elements do not disturb the graphitic structure and thus induce fewer defects as compared to substitutional doping.94 On the other hand, these non-covalent bonds still maintain a high surface area and induce active sites for excellent electrocatalytic activity. However, the main limitation of surface transfer doping is the instability of chemical adsorbates at elevated temperature because dopants may detach from the graphene surface.81

2.2 Structure

The structure of doped graphene, including dopant configurations and concentration, can be tailored to modulate the electrical properties for energy storage and engineer a crucial bandgap for energy conversion.36,45,66,95 The structure of doped graphene is varied with different doping atoms and mechanisms.

In substitutional doping, doped atoms typically supersede localized carbon atoms in-plane, on edges and on defect sites of the graphene lattice (Fig. 3c). Depending on their electronegativity and atomic sizes, dopants can generate different configurations and bond lengths with carbon. Table 1 compares of valence electrons, sizes, electronegativity, bond lengths, and possible configurations between C and various substitutional doping elements, including P, Si, N, P, S, F, and Cl. As provided in Table 1, B, N, and F with resemblance in atomic size to the C atom can easily incorporate into the carbon network and cause less distortion than other doping elements owing to their shorter bond lengths with C. In contrast, Si, P, S, and Cl can form a longer bond with C, so they induce more structural distortion in the graphene network. Chang et al. observed that B and N elements tend to replace in-plane C atoms, while S and Si inevitably protrude out of the lattice due to their longer bond lengths. Besides, different electronegativity can induce polarization, thereby affecting the electrical and photocatalytic properties of doped graphene.96

N-Doping into the graphene structure can also result in five possible configurations: graphitic-N, oxidized-N, pyrrolic-N, nitrilic-N, and pyridinic-N (Fig. 5a).4,15,97 Tai et al. presented that graphitic-N and pyridinic-N were more reactive than pyrrolic-N for the ORR.97 Among them, pyridinic-N in thermal-annealing derived N-doped graphene was also more reactive for the ORR. According to Miao et al., pyridinic-N can act as an active centre to facilitate the ORR through a four-electron pathway (Fig. 5b).23 Zhan et al. demonstrated that graphitic-N and pyridinic-N both significantly enhance the total capacitance for electrochemical double-layer capacitors (EDLCs).98 Hence, controlling the configuration of doped graphene could lead to desirable properties for targeted energy applications.

To date, doping configurations can be controlled through varying synthesis parameters, such as the dopant precursor and operation temperature.16 Santhosh et al. altered the plasma environment and obtained high graphitic-N and pyridinic-N configurations (Fig. 5c).15 They observed that the treatment of NH3 plasma can lead to a higher N concentration in comparison with N2 plasma (8.0 at% vs. 2.8 at%, respectively). Zhang et al. successfully synthesized N-doped graphene dominated by graphitic-N at 230300 C through the CVD method.45 In contrast, the sample was dominated by pyrrolic-N at 400600 C (Fig. 5d). The sample dominated by graphitic-N exhibited stronger n-type behaviour and higher carrier mobilities as compared with the pyrrolic-N dominated counterpart owing to the presence of fewer vacancies and defects.

In contrast to substitutional doping, surface transfer doping results in the interchange of electrons between dopants and carbon. Heavy halogens (e.g., Br and I) can physically adsorb on the graphene surface and cause minimum disruption of the basal plane of graphene.12,38,91,99 However, they still enrich reactive sites for efficient electrocatalytic reactions. Kalita et al. presented that I-doped graphene can retain the sp2 hybridized structure, evidenced by the unchanged graphene interlayer spacing of 0.34 nm (Fig. 6a).12 Hamdan et al. revealed that the intercalation of Br atoms inside the graphene structure did not disturb the honeycomb carbon network.91 Br atoms were inserted into carbon layers, forming ionic bonds with C atoms via the surface transfer doping mechanism.24 Likewise, surface transfer doping is also the main mechanism to introduce metal ions into the graphene structure and dope metal nanoparticles into doped graphene. The surface transfer or charge transfer (CT) mechanism of Au ions into the graphene surface is described in Fig. 6c.38 Electrons were extracted from graphene to reduce AuIII to Au nanoparticles (AuNPs), creating p-type Au-doped graphene without the infliction of damage to the graphene structure. In another study, ruthenium (Ru) cations were strongly adsorbed into graphene oxide (GO).46 They interacted with the abundant oxygen functional groups of GO, while N atoms interfered in the GO structure and created most defects in Ru/N-doped graphene (Fig. 6d). The crumped morphology of Ru/N-doped graphene and the existence of Ru atoms are clearly shown in Fig. 6d.

Recently, the synergistic effects brought about by the co-doping of different dopants have drawn extensive research interest.34,100102 Co-doping elements with different electronegativity (according to the Pauling scale) can alter the charge distribution and lead to a tuneable electronic band structure, the so-called synergistic coupling effect.100 For example, N and B dual-doped graphene can create a BNC structure, neutralizing the electron withdrawing and donating characteristics of B and N, respectively.103 Besides, the presence of co-dopants in carbon networks can facilitate the charge transfer within the graphene network.3 Wang et al. observed that N,S dual-doped graphene displays a looser structure with interconnected pores (inset A of Fig. 7a), as compared to N-doped and S-doped samples (insets B and C of Fig. 7a).3 Such a highly porous structure enables faster charge transfer and favours the application of doped graphene as an electrode for energy storage devices. Zhou et al. revealed the higher specific surface area (171.4 m2 g1 at a relative pressure of 0.9) (Fig. 7b), richer porosity with a large average pore size (28 m) and large total pore volume (1.28 cm3 g1) of N,S co-doped graphene as compared with mono-doped ones.20Fig. 7c compares the pore size distributions of N,S co-doped graphene, N-doped graphene, S-doped graphene and undoped reduced graphene oxide (rGO), of which mesopores and macropores dominated in the dual doped samples. The successful introduction of both S and N was verified by XPS spectroscopy and the concentration of N and S was even higher (N: 5.4 at%; S: 3.9 at%) than that of mono-doped ones (N: 5.1 at%; S: 0.6 at%). Besides, the dual-doped sample showed a higher C/O ratio (Fig. 7d), which indicates more effective reduction of N,S-graphene. Recently, multi-doped graphene with B, N, P and Fe for ORR electrocatalysts and metal-doped graphene with Mn, Fe, Co, and Ni were also reported.71,77 Multi-doped graphene samples, especially with the addition of transition metal atoms, outperformed mono-doped and dual-doped samples. The introduction of metal elements not only improves the content of doped heteroatoms but also increases the electrochemical and electrocatalytic performance of doped graphene because the charge transfer between metal atoms and graphene is promising to open a suitable bandgap, while retaining electron mobility for energy-related applications. However, the specific surface area may slightly decrease due to an excessive number of heteroatoms, which may create clusters and block some effective porosity in the graphene structure.

2.3 Properties

2.3.1 Electrical and electrochemical properties. Doping with different foreign atoms not only modifies the structure but also intrinsically manipulates the electrical and electrochemical properties of doped graphene, which is critical for its applications in energy conversion and storage.2,66 In general, the electrical and electrochemical properties of doped graphene can be evaluated from the electrical sheet resistance, the charge transfer resistance and the charge carrier mobility.

As well documented, the difference in electronegativity between C and dopants effectively promotes charge transfer, improves the electrical conductivity and reduces the energy barrier for ion intercalation. Wen et al. demonstrated the enhancement of electrical conductivity of N-doped graphene paper (6.81 at% N) for Li-ion batteries.2 As can be seen from Fig. 8a, the semicircles in the Nyquist plots of N-doped graphene are smaller than that of undoped graphene paper, implying smaller impedance, better ionic conductivity and lower charge transfer resistance (Rct). Calculations from the equivalent circuit of N-doped graphene revealed a decrease of up to one-half (from 110.56 to 54.97 ) of the total impedance resistance and interface resistance. The electrical conductivity of graphene paper also improved from 900 S m1 to 2000 S m1 after N doping. Likewise, Thirumal et al. discovered that the Rct of graphene decreased to one-half after doping of 2.56% B (from 7.22 to 3.52 ), implying facilitated charge transfer and improved conductivity as compared to the undoped counterparts.52 N-Doped graphene (1.4 at% N) with a high carrier mobility of up to 8600 cm2 V1 s1 was fabricated even at room temperature by Lin and co-workers.111 This improvement can be attributed to additional hole or electron impurities in the energy band structure of graphene, facilitating the movement of electrons from the valence band to the conduction band.

It is worth mentioning that both n-type and p-type doping from substitutional and surface transfer doping also improve the electrical conductivity of graphene owing to the charge redistribution after doping. The electrical conductivity is closely related to the synthesis parameters and dopant sources. The electrical conductivity of N-doped carbon paper (2.1 S cm1) under a high pressure of 600 kPa was proved to be 35 times higher than that of doped samples without compression (0.06 S cm1) on account of its higher density.18 With a highly dense structure, N atoms prioritized to dope on the edges and defect sites of graphene honeycomb networks and generate more pyridinic-N, which had a lower energy barrier and served as the most effective position to improve energy storage performance. Thus, an N-doped graphene film at 600 kPa (NCP-600k) showed much lower resistance, faster charge transfer and higher electrical conductivity (Fig. 8b). In another research study, Zhu et al. prepared B,N co-doped rGO (BN-rGO) with massively reduced resistance (Fig. 8c).34 A study of N,S dual-doped rGO by Zhou et al. reported a significant improvement in electrical conductivity, compared to mono-doped graphene (Fig. 8d).20 The reason is that it possesses a highly porous structure and synergistical dual-doping effect. Both N and S heteroatoms were inserted into the graphene honeycomb structure, decomposed a larger number of oxygen functional groups and formed more defects than mono-doped and undoped graphene.

2.3.2 Optical and photocatalytic properties. For energy conversion applications, the photocatalytic properties of materials play a critical role. The typical photocatalytic reactions involve two steps: (i) catalyst absorbs the energy of light that is higher than its band gap to generated electronhole pairs and (ii) photogenerated electrons and holes reach reactive sites and participate in catalytic reactions which enable energy conversion similar to electrocatalysis. The former requires a suitable band gap for effective light absorption, while the latter is dependent on the mobility of photogenerated electronhole pairs, i.e., the light conversion efficiency. Under light irradiation, the valence electrons of doped graphene can absorb the photon energy of light and be excited to higher energy levels, leaving holes in previous positions. The photon energy that can be absorbed depends on the number of free electrons. Therefore, doped graphene with more charge carriers via both substitutional and surface transfer from heteroatoms and metal nanoparticles can control the bandgap width and induce more electronhole pairs with the same amount of irradiation, leading to the enhancement of optical absorption and catalytic activities for high energy conversion efficiency. The light absorption of doped graphene can be extended from the ultraviolet to the visible region by changing the absorption intensity, frequency and spectral structure and was investigated by theoretical and computational studies.66,112 This can be effectively controlled by dopant types and concentrations. Rani et al. observed that B and N co-doped graphene can cause a significant red-shift in the visible spectrum.112Fig. 9a compares the absorption spectra of GO and B-doped rGO (BR-GO).10 The absorption peak shifted from 232 nm to 268 nm after B doping, indicating the restoration and deoxygenation of the conjugated structure of GO.10 The absorption spectra and the bandgap of GO, N-doped, B-doped and N,B co-doped graphene are investigated.21 As illustrated in Fig. 9b, light absorption after B, N and NB doping has been significantly improved. Among them, N-doped graphene displayed the strongest absorbance in the UV to visible light region. The co-doped graphene showed the largest band gap (2.04 eV) as compared with B-doped and N-doped graphene (1.9 eV and 1.68 eV, respectively), which restricted the optical absorption of graphene, adversely affecting the photocatalytic ability. Previous studies indicated that a smaller band gap value is preferred for photocatalysts and it should be between 1.6 and 2.2 eV for high efficiency.113

After charge carrier separation, they will migrate to reactive sites on the surface of the catalyst and participate in chemical redox reactions which enables energy conversion. The mobility of charge carriers decides the energy conversion efficiency because the recombination of pairs may limit the catalytic activity of doped graphene. A previous study presented that the combination of B-doped rGO and ZnO created a pn heterojunction, which lowered the recombination rate of electronhole pairs, resulting in superior photocatalytic activity.114

The photocatalytic ability of doped graphene can be quantified by the quantum efficiency or incident photon to current efficiency (IPCE), which refers to the incident light to current conversion efficiency of materials. Wu et al. recently found that the IPCE of B-doped graphene could be around 60% at wavelengths from 750 nm to 980 nm (Fig. 9c), suggesting the outstanding light response of B-doped graphene.36 By contrast, pure graphene did not show any response under visible light irradiation during photocatalytic reactions due to the deficiency of the energy bandgap. The same trend was observed by Mohamed et al. for N-doped graphene with the highest IPCE of 60% at 600 nm, and doped graphene samples showed the highest charge concentrations between 450 and 850 nm (Fig. 9d).21 In general, B and N dopants served as charge accepting and donating centers, respectively, inhibiting the recombination rate of electronhole pairs. Their presence not only tailors the bandgap but also enhances the quantum efficiency of doped graphene, which plays an important role in photocatalytic reactions in energy conversion.

3.1 Chemical vapour deposition

Chemical vapour deposition (CVD) has been a prominent technique to prepare high-quality pristine graphene and doped graphene thin films over large areas.122,123 The flexible, highly transparent conductive and uniformly doped graphene thin films from CVD are mostly suitable for hydrogen evolution technologies, transparent electrodes for solar cells and smart windows.116,124,125 The resultant doped graphene is formed via substitutional doping by CVD. As illustrated in Fig. 10a, CVD offers various choices of precursors (gas, solid, and liquid raw materials). These precursors are put into a vacuum quartz tube, and then decomposed to radicals under decomposition conditions. The temperature required to decompose common precursors is in the range of 9001000 C. Therefore, metal substrates (such as copper and nickel foil) are typically used for radical absorption to lower the growth temperature, making it more feasible to grow high-quality doped graphene. Moreover, the dependence of doping concentration on the precursor types, flow rate, surrounding pressure, and deposition temperature is also reported by some research groups.116,126128

To fabricate and improve the quality of doped graphene-based materials for energy harvesting devices, lowering the deposition temperature for the CVD substrate below 1000 C is the key target to reduce the limitations of CVD. Reducing the growth temperature can prevent damage to the substrate and induce a sufficient doping level since the high temperature of 1000 C prioritizes CC formation over the bonding between C and doping atoms.28,129 Additionally, structural contamination and the disruption of graphene layers when transferring from a metal catalytic substrate to its insulating counterpart are also critical issues.130 Thus, to lower the growth temperature, CVD approaches are modified, through using low temperature-activated precursors, a metal-free substrate and providing a CVD chamber with a plasma environment. Lu et al. utilized 1,3,5-triazine as a single source of both carbon and the dopant on account of its lower activation energy to prepare N-doped graphene via CVD (Fig. 10b).16 N concentration increased from 2.1 at% to 5.6 at% by reducing CVD growth temperature from 900 C to 700 C, confirming the preference of CC formation at the higher temperature. Pyridine was also employed as an individual precursor to produce N-doped graphene at a very low temperature of 300 C due to its low dehydrogenisation temperature (Fig. 10c), achieving a small defect density of N-doped graphene and an extremely high N concentration of 16.7 at%.28 Zhai et al. synthesized B-doped graphene on a glass substrate by hot-filament CVD (HFCVD), as described in Fig. 10d.40,116 The carrier gas (H2) and precursors (C2H2 and B2H6) were mixed in a gas shower and decomposed to reactive radicals under the hot filament. These radicals then diffused to low-temperature glass substrates to form B-doped graphene by opening the shutter. This new metal-free CVD method can provide a relatively low deposition temperature and high growth rate of doped graphene. Alternatively, Wei et al. proposed plasma enhanced CVD (PECVD) that promotes low-temperature growth of N-doped graphene on a dielectric substrate (SiO2/Si) at only 435 C.47 The PECVD setup is described in Fig. 10e. The presence of plasma coil can provide a high energy environment in the CVD chamber to reduce the growth temperature of precursor species on metal and even dielectric substrates. Despite advantages, a challenge for these metal-free methods still exists, i.e., the insufficient deposition of precursors on insulating substrates. Besides, CVD methods suffer from drawbacks such as a high cost, high operation temperature, and low production yield with toxic precursors involved, thereby hindering their application for industrial-scale production of doped graphene.

3.2 Solvothermal reflux

Solvothermal (hydrothermal) reflux has been widely employed as an economical method to synthesise doped graphene directly at the gram scale and under mild conditions (relatively low temperature and pressure). During solvothermal and hydrothermal processes, doping atoms can covalently bond with C atoms, forming reaction intermediates. These groups subsequently assemble into doped graphene, resulting in substitutional doping. Additionally, metal ions can also absorb onto the surface of substitutionally doped graphene, forming a hybrid structure. A solvothermal approach in the production of graphene was first introduced by Choucair et al.131 As shown in Fig. 11a, a liquid substance containing carbon and dopant sources is used as the precursor and sealed in a high pressure stainless-steel autoclave heated with controllable reaction time and temperatures. Doped graphene via solvothermal reflux can be prepared from either a mixture of different doping precursors or single precursors. For instance, S-doped graphene was produced from a mixture of GO and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) (Fig. 11b), or from only DMSO as a single precursor for both C and S sources.9,120,132 S-Doped graphene was produced via the substitutional doping mechanism through the assembly of CC, CSC and CS intermediates. Recently, Bhushan et al. presented that a long reaction duration, mild treatment temperature and high vessel pressure are more favourable to introduce dopants.118 Sun et al. applied a hydrothermal, followed by calcination route to prepare CoNi pyrophosphates/N-doped graphene at only 140 C (Fig. 11c).26 Under hydrothermal conditions, PO43+, NH4+, Ni2+, and Co2+ well interacted with oxygen functional groups on the GO basal plane. Simultaneously, the O content was reduced, leaving an intermediate product for a further calcination stage to obtain CoNi pyrophosphates/N-doped graphene.

In comparison with other methods, the solvothermal approach is potential and repeatable owing to its relatively mild and simple conditions and low cost along with gram-scale production.120,131,132 The yield is only restricted by the capacity of the flask or autoclave and the doping concentration mainly depends on the selected precursors. Typically, the doped graphene prepared from solvothermal reflux shows more defects and comprises a large number of active sites with a broad interlayer spacing.120,161 These active sites and interlayer distance can accommodate intercalated ions and boost the catalytic capabilities of doped graphene. Therefore, solvothermal-derived doped graphene is suitable for future energy-related technologies. The main limitation of solvothermal methods is that they are time-consuming and toxic chemical dopants are involved.

3.3 Arc discharge

Arc discharge is another feasible, low-cost and high-yield approach to synthesize pure graphene and doped graphene by the evaporation of solid-phase precursors, such as graphite powder and GO powder, at high temperature. The doping mechanism under arc discharge is substitutional doping. Since it was first introduced in 1990 by Krastchmer's group, arc discharge has been applied to fabricate a broad range of carbonaceous materials, including carbon nanotubes and doped graphene materials.133 The scheme of arc discharge synthesis is depicted in Fig. 12a.1 The system comprises two electrodes (anode and cathode) in a vacuum chamber filled with pure helium. A resistance spring made of NiCr is used to generate an arc discharge voltage. Typically, external DC power supply is applied at a specific arc current and the product can be collected at the substrate within only a few minutes.

Luan et al. prepared an F-doped graphene anode for lithium-ion batteries by arc discharge (Fig. 12b), and obtained 2 g F-doped graphene, suggesting the potential of this method for large-scale production. Likewise, arc discharge can be effectively utilized to introduce multiple dopants.22 Dey et al. produced B,N co-doped graphene quantum dots via the arcing methodology.37 As described in Fig. 12c, B-doped and N-doped graphene were first fabricated through arcing (step 1), followed by chemical scissoring by acid treatment of H2SO4 and HNO3 (step 2). Pham et al. prepared N-doped graphene from GO and polyaniline by arc discharge (Fig. 12d) and N concentration reached 3.5 at%, far above the value reported earlier (i.e., only 11.5 at% N).44

The arc discharge method enables the homogenous distribution of heteroelements into the graphene lattice. The main advantages of this doping method are a short duration, a simple procedure with scalable products, and the absence of any catalyst or toxic by-product. Nevertheless, gaseous and liquid-phase precursors are not available to prepare doped graphene via this method. Besides, low doping concentration, low specific surface areas, and high values of the DC current and voltage also limit this method for industrial production.

3.4 Thermal annealing

Thermal treatment, especially thermal annealing of GO, is extensively applied to fabricate three-dimensional electrodes in high yield for energy conversion and storage devices. In general principle, it requires initial exfoliation of GO mixed with the dopant precursor, followed by an annealing process at a very high temperature around 6001000 C to obtain heteroatom-doped graphene. Under elevated temperature (in the range of 200800 C), most of the oxygen-containing groups decompose into active sites to anchor dopants. Therefore, GO can be effectively reduced with the most sp2 structure recovered, providing both high electrical conductivity and catalytic activities which are favourable for energy-related applications.

Yeom et al. applied two-step thermal annealing of GO mixed with B2O3 as a boron precursor to produce B-doped graphene nanoplatelets (BT-rGO) via the substitutional doping mechanism (Fig. 13a).7 The concentration of the B dopant was reported to be as high as 6.04 at%. Thermal annealing can also be an effective method to produce multi-doped graphene. Zhang et al. annealed a mixture of NH4SCN and Co(OAC)2 to prepare CoS/N,S multi-doped rGO (CoS/NS-rGO), as shown in Fig. 13b.19 In the structure of the resultant sample, N and S were doped into the graphene network via the substitutional doping mechanism, whereas Co atoms were trapped into the graphene surface via surface transfer doping.

In thermal annealing, the type and mass ratio of precursors, and annealing temperature and duration significantly influence the doping efficiency, doping concentration and configurations of doped graphene.7,134,135 Although a high annealing temperature is necessary for the synthesis of doped graphene, overheating could lead to unstable bonding between C and dopants and limit the doping concentration. Moreover, Ngidi's work showed a decrease in the surface area of N-doped graphene at 800 C in comparison with the same sample treated at 600 C due to the collapse of the graphene backbone upon thermal annealing.128 For this reason, thermal-driven reduction has been modified to produce high-quality doped graphene, such as microwave-assisted thermal annealing, laser writing and flash heating.35,43,49,136141 These methodologies are proven as non-toxic and highly efficient approaches to simultaneously dope and reduce the mixture of GO with heteroatom-enriched sources. Umrao et al. proposed microwave-assisted thermal annealing to prepare B,N co-doped graphene.35 As described in Fig. 13c, the product was simultaneously reduced and doped in a very short duration at a high doping concentration by microwave irradiation without the use of hazardous reducing agents. Recently, Yoo et al. introduced an ultrafast flash heating method to prepare flash-heated N-doped graphene from GO and melamine in a few milliseconds.43 The flash-heated N-doped graphene showed a much higher N concentration (26 at% N) in comparison with the normal thermal-heated N-doped graphene (0.8 at% N) (Fig. 13d). Alternatively, Fu et al. introduced laser writing photoreduction of GO and urea to produce N-doped GO with a N content of 6.37 at%.49 The schematic illustration of the laser writing setup is provided in Fig. 13e. Under a high power laser, the as-prepared GO/urea mixture was reduced and doped, resulting in N-doped rGO.

In general, thermal annealing is an effective pathway of both reducing and doping GO with a controllable doping content and various choices of precursors (solid, liquid, and gaseous sources). Doping atoms either can insert into the graphene honeycomb network via substitutional doping or induce charge-transfer by annealing. On the other hand, the required high temperature for the normal thermal annealing method might lead to large energy consumption for industrial-scale energy-related technologies. Modified thermal-driven strategies with a short duration and high yield will be potential alternatives for future graphene doping.

3.5 Plasma treatment

In contrast to thermal annealing, plasma treatment has been widely used as a low energy consumption route to prepare doped graphene within only a few minutes at low pressure. On one hand, plasma treatment is widely used to produce doped graphene via substitutional doping. On the other hand, plasma technology can also modify the surface properties of graphene through etching. Therefore, deposition under a plasma environment can potentially be used for surface transfer doping. The plasma treatment method is a potentially alternative strategy to develop doped-graphene materials via substitutional doping and surface transfer doping mechanisms for energy technologies such as fuel cells, supercapacitors because of environmental sustainability, a short plasma exposure time and low energy consumption.34,143,144 Nonetheless, the limitation of plasma treatment for doped graphene is a low doping concentration as compared with other conventional methodologies.

3.6 Chemical treatment

Because GO is water-dispersible, a solution-based chemical reaction method has been widely used to produce doped graphene owing to its versatility and simplicity. Under chemical treatment, the oxygen functional groups in the GO lattice can be reduced and doping atoms can either easily get trapped into the graphene honeycomb framework via substitutional doping or form a charge transfer complex via the surface transfer mechanism.

Recently, Guo et al. used hydrosulphide hydrate (NaSHxH2O) as the reagent and S source to prepare S-doped graphene at room temperature (Fig. 15a).8 The substitution of S atoms in graphene networks (2.5 wt% of S) was confirmed by a clear S 2p signal through XPS analysis. The mechanism of S doping can be illustrated in Fig. 15b: (1) the SH of the dopant first reacted with the carbonyl group of GO to form thiol and hydroxyl groups; (2) these groups detached from the graphene network, generating carbocations; (3) the CH bond was broken to form a new carbocation; (4) CSH attached to the as-produced carbocation to form an S configuration.

The presence of N precursors, e.g., hydrazine hydrate and 2,3-diaminopyridine, helps to simultaneously reduce most of the oxygen functional groups on the graphene structure and introduce N atoms into the graphene structure. Chang et al. confirmed that 2,3-diaminopyridine with enriched pyridinic-N moieties removed most of the oxygen functional groups in the GO structure and attach to the graphene network via a wet chemical reaction at 110 C (Fig. 15c).27 Bi et al. fabricated Bi-doped rGO by mixing GO, hydrazine hydrate and BiCl3.72 Because of the highly hazardous characteristics of hydrazine hydrate, Ahmed et al. proposed human urine as a simple, harmless and cost-free natural N source. The N-doped product attained a doping concentration of 11.1 at% and can be applicable in fuel cells.

As compared to other methods, chemical treatment is a low-cost approach to dope graphene on a large scale with little demand of thermal treatment and special equipment. However, shortcomings still exist such as low homogeneity, high toxicity and instability of doped graphene under excessive addition of reagents.145 Hence, it is necessary to find alternative dopants with a good reduction degree and less toxicity to develop sustainable solution materials for future energy devices.

4.1 Energy conversion

4.1.1 Hydrogen generation. Hydrogen energy represents a sustainable alternative to commercial fossil fuels on account of its high calorific value (144 MJ kg1) and high mass-energy density.146,147 Hydrogen generation in water splitting allows the conversion of electricity to hydrogen energy through electrocatalytic and photocatalytic processes. Electrocatalysis is a conventional catalytic process, including the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER), conducted at an electrode/electrolyte interface with the participation of electrocatalysts. A small overpotential, which refers to the energy barrier of these redox reactions, is essential to facilitate energy conversion.148 Doped graphene has been identified as a promising electrocatalyst to decrease the overpotential for highly efficient hydrogen generation. Apart from electrolysis, photocatalytic water splitting could directly generate hydrogen from water by using sunlight. In photocatalysis, doped graphene serves as a semiconductor photocatalyst excited by photons from sunlight to generate electrons and holes. These photogenerated charge carriers reach the reactive sites and facilitate water splitting reactions to produce hydrogen similar to electrocatalysis.149

Although noble metals (Pt) and transition metals (Co, Ni, etc.) have shown superior photo/electrocatalytic performance for water splitting reactions, their scarcity, high cost, corrosion, and passivation greatly hamper their practical application.150 Therefore, heteroatom-doped graphene and its hybrids with metals and metal compounds, such as metal oxides, metal sulfides, and metal alloys, have been the subject of extensive investigation to achieve excellent photocatalytic performance for hydrogen production.146,151154 Doped graphene and its hybrids can provide geometric defects and a highly active surface for efficient photo/electrocatalysts towards the HER and OER.57,148,155 Particularly, dopants can activate the surrounding C atoms in the graphene lattice, supplying abundant active sites for the HER and anchoring sites for metal components owing to the modification of valence orbital energy levels. The cooperation of doped graphene with metal components is expected to not only enhance the light absorption, but also improve electrocatalytic activity towards the HER.57,156 Zhao et al. revealed an improvement in the photocatalytic hydrogen generation of a Ni/N-doped graphene cocatalyst up to 3.4 times with 0.0013 wt% Ni, compared to N-doped graphene.157 The presence of Ni/N-doped graphene coupling with CdS significantly reduced the photoluminescence intensity. As a result, the recombination rate of charge carriers was hampered. Dong et al. presented that the incorporation of N atoms and MoS2 nanoparticles into the graphene structure exhibited a small onset overpotential and Tafel slope (112 mV and 44 mV, respectively), demonstrating efficient electrocatalysis.158 Moreover, the introduction of these dopants into the doped graphene honeycomb structure regulates the charge density and distribution of neighbouring C due to their differences in electronegativity between metal elements and C atoms, subsequently lowering the activation energy required for both the HER and the OER. So far, developing strong interaction between metal nanoparticles and doped graphene has still been a challenge to obtain long-term stability.

Tsai et al. obtained N-doped graphene quantum dots (N-GQDs, 6.85 at% N) with enhanced photocatalytic activity for hydrogen production.5 Under illumination, the N-GQDs exhibited a two times higher hydrogen production rate than the undoped counterpart (Fig. 17a). This can be attributed to CN bonds. As shown in Fig. 17b, all N-GQD samples display a tenfold increase in the photocurrent generation, compared to undoped GQDs. In another research study, Tian et al. utilized solvothermal doping, followed by plasma etching in Ar, to maximize the activity of S-doped graphene (Fig. 17e).25 As a result, doping structures were created by S doping and topological defects were maintained by plasma etching technology, leading to the improvement of HER performance. As shown in Fig. 17c, plasma-etched S-doped graphene (SG-P) showed the smallest overpotential, indicating its superior activity towards the HER (Fig. 17c and d). Woldetinsay et al. developed a Pd/MoS2/N,S dual-doped graphene hybrid for efficient hydrogen generation.159 The co-doped graphene exhibited an unprecedentedly small overpotential of 42 mV and Tafel slope of 38 mV dec1. From Table 3, co-doped graphene and doped graphene hybrids with metal compounds show better electrocatalytic activities, represented by a smaller overpotential and Tafel slope, suggesting promising approaches for future development of doped graphene catalysts for hydrogen generation.

Barakat et al. also proved the remarkable electrocatalytic activity and long-term stability of FeNi-decorated N-doped graphene (FeNiN-Gr) for the ORR in an acidic electrolyte.178 As well explained, N atoms showed good affinity to hydrogen ions due to their electron-donating nature; metallic atoms, Fe and Ni, attracted more molecular oxygen. The combination of the as-adsorbed hydrogen and oxygen subsequently formed water for the completion of the ORR. Moreover, FeNi alloys with high corrosion resistance can significantly improve the stability of the electrocatalyst (a retention of 98% after 1000 cycles).

In summary, graphene tailored by mono-doping and multi-doping with metal nanoparticles has become a long-term solution for the development of fuel cells. Future research can focus on the development of doped graphene catalysts under both under alkaline and acidic conditions.

4.1.3 CO2 reduction. The CO2 reduction reaction (CRR) refers to a potential energy conversion technology to alleviate the energy crisis by capturing and converting atmospheric CO2 into useable chemicals and hydrocarbon fuels (such as carbon monoxide, formic acid, methanol, ethanol, ethylene, etc.).68,93,146 To date, the prospects of direct catalytic conversion of CO2 to one-carbon (C1) products under mild temperatures (lower than 200 C) have been promising, while two-carbon (C2) products remain challenging.192 The mechanism of the CRR includes three main steps: (i) CO2 absorption on the catalyst surface, (ii) CH bond formation and CO bond destruction via proton-electron transfer, and (iii) desorption and product formation.93 Therefore, the efficient CRR performance necessitates high-performance catalysts with strong interaction with CO2. So far, doping graphene with both metal-free atoms (e.g., B, N, and Si) and metal nanoparticles (e.g., Ni, Fe, and Cu) has been widely studied to tune electronic properties and generate catalytic functional groups for graphene-based materials for the CRR.93,174,193

Ju et al. reported transition metal- and N-doped nanoporous carbon (MNC, where M can be Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, and Cu) as potential catalysts towards the CRR.194 All MNC catalysts are reported with a macroporous structure and average pore distribution of 0.70.8 nm, which is 2.52.9 times larger than that of CO2 molecules, allowing favourable space to trap CO2. Zhang et al. synthesized an Fe/N-doped graphene catalyst for CO2 reduction to CO via thermal annealing (700800 C) with GO, FeCl3 and NH3 precursors (Fig. 19a).17 The as-prepared Fe/N-doped graphene manifested a small overpotential of 0.60 V vs. the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) and a comparable faradaic efficiency of 80% towards CO, indicating the major CO product after reduction (Fig. 19b). Remarkably, the Fe/N-doped graphene catalyst showed high selectivity and excellent stability throughout 10 h testing. This enhancement is due to the presence of N, which lowered the energy barrier of the CRR process due to high charge density and charge delocalization of the doped graphene structure. The combined effect of N and Fe atoms on the improvement of CO2 absorption and activation is also uncovered by free energy diagrams, as shown in Fig. 19c. In short, FeN4 moieties in Fe/N-doped graphene acted as active centres to trap toxic CO2 molecules. The absorption of CO2 on the catalyst surface initiated COOH* intermediate and CO* adduct formation through proton-electron transfer. Then, CO was created after the desorption of CO*. Likewise, Su et al. demonstrated Ni/N-doped graphene revealing outstanding CRR performance to produce the CO product.195 Under optimized conditions, the faradaic efficiency of Ni/N-doped graphene can reach up to 90% towards CO with a low onset potential value of 0.5 V and a small Tafel slope of 126 mV dec1. In the CO2 reduction reaction, the doping type and configuration of doped graphene catalysts will decide hydrocarbon products. For example, Si chain configuration in Si-doped graphene is capable of reducing CO2 to C2H5OH and C2H4 based on the multiple active sites on the doped chain at graphene edges, whereas single Si atom configuration provides only active sites to interact with CO2 and form C1 products, such as CO, HCOOH, and CH4.68 Therefore, controlling the preparation parameters of doping graphene with precise configurations for targeted hydrocarbon products is a challenge for future research. Additionally, highly durable and low-cost doped graphene catalysts are desirable for further economical CO2 conversion.

In general, doped graphene via substitutional doping and surface transfer doping can increase its catalytic performance towards the CRR owing to the enhancement of electrical and optical properties. Multi-doped graphene, especially with transition metal atoms, has recently shown potential to improve CO2 conversion efficiency.

5. Conclusion and prospects

In summary, doped graphene has attracted enormous interest in energy conversion and storage areas. The doping has altered the electrical, electrochemical, and photo/electrocatalytic behaviours of pristine graphene, which contributes to an outstanding performance for future energy-related devices. This review discusses doped graphene based on two doping mechanisms, including substitutional doping and surface transfer doping. The properties and structural modification of doped graphene have been compared to that of its undoped counterpart to clarify differences and important features favourable for energy-related applications. The prevailing doping methods have also been highlighted in this review to achieve doped graphene with high dopant concentration and desirable configurations. In addition, the recent energy-related applications of doped graphene have been summarized, and a significant improvement in the performance and stability of doped graphene materials has been demonstrated.

Despite the superior electrochemical and catalytic performance of energy-related devices using doped graphene, there are still several remaining challenges. At present, co-doping graphene with substitutionally doped atoms could enhance the performance of energy-related devices, but an excessive number of dopants may create an unnecessarily wide band gap for the required photocatalyst. These excessive dopants may also block the effective porosity, inhibit the charge transport in doped graphene, and create an exceedingly large number of defects in the graphene structure, suppressing some critical optical properties of graphene. For example, doping GO with N and B individually could narrow its bandgap from 2.6 eV to 1.69 and 1.9 eV, respectively, whereas the bandgap of N,B co-doped GO is still relatively large (2.04 eV), restricting the light harvesting abilities of catalyst materials.21 In terms of energy conversion and storage, safety issues play a critical role. Overheating doped graphene during the synthesis process may cause an explosion and thermal runaway, which refers to the failure of the energy storage system at high temperature. Therefore, thermal management, especially reducing the synthesis temperature, is always critical. However, the correlation between thermal properties, the structure of doped graphene, and the thermal deformation of doped graphene during the synthesis process is still rarely discussed.

Further studies on doped graphene for energy conversion and storage may focus on the following aspects:

5.1 Doping graphene with the existence of metal atoms

Doping graphene needs to open a suitable bandgap, while maintaining the electron mobility for energy storage, and obtain highly efficient catalytic properties for energy conversion. For example, doping graphene with metal elements can be a promising strategy. Metal compounds, such as metal oxides/hydroxides and metal alloys from the transition metal group (e.g., Ni, Fe, FeNi, Co, CoTa2O6, MoO3, and ZnNiCo hydroxide), are highly recommended to support substitutionally doped graphene.178,244247 They could adsorb into graphene lattices and cause less distortion than substitutional dopants, but still maintain most of the salient properties and excellent stability for conversion and storage applications. Doped-graphene integrated with coreshell nanoparticles on the surface is also reported as a bifunctional catalyst, enhancing the overall efficiency of electrocatalytic reactions.248

5.2 Investigation of new energy-efficient and fast doping methods

Recently, new synthesis methods such as microwave irradiation, laser writing, and flash heating stood out with a lower cost, short duration, better safety, and lower energy consumption, and would serve as novel doping strategies worthy of future investigation. The production of doped graphene through these routes on a large scale is a challenging task and needs to be further developed.

5.3 Improving electronhole generation and mobilities of doped graphene

The mobility of electronhole pairs of doped graphene is a prerequisite for the occurrence of energy conversion reactions and it guarantees the high efficiency of catalytic performance. Hence, enhancing the carrier separation and its mobility is essential. Future research efforts may focus on dual-doping both n-type and p-type elements on graphene to enable pn nanoscale heterojunction formation for more efficient electronhole separation.114 As reported by Zhang et al., N-doped graphene dominated by graphitic-N possesses higher carrier transport characteristics than its pyrrolic-N dominated counterpart.45 Therefore, controlling the dominant doping configurations is a feasible approach to not only enhance electronhole mobilities but also boost the electrical conductivity. The improvement in electronhole generation and mobilities reduces the recombination rate of charge carriers for more efficient photo/electrocatalytic reactions.
