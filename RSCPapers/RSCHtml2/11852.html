Abstract

The progressive development of technology and industry in the new millennium emergently requires finding a balance between economic progress and new smart materials. Graphene as a carbon allotrope since the discovery has attracted enormous attention due to several extraordinary properties that originate from its physical and chemical assets. However, the most important obstacle in graphene assembly still is the production of high-quality graphene on a large scale using cost-effective methods. The presented review article aims to give information on a turning point in graphene synthesis, with reference to a detailed analysis of electrochemical exfoliation, rapid thermal annealing and biomass pyrolysis. Selected methods for graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials production were not covered in the literature to their maximum potential, from our point of view. Through an overview of the selected synthesis methods, we explored their prospects and limitations regarding precursor type and reaction conditions. Furthermore, through several important characterization techniques for graphene, we reveal the details of the properties of graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials obtained through selected methods. Finally, a particular potential application depending on nanomaterial properties obtained through selected methods was pointed out.

Introduction

Over the years the research focus on nanoscaled materials with sizes ranging from 1 to 100 nm increased in various fields due to their unique properties originating mostly from their size, morphology and dimensionality.1,2 Carbon in its elemental form is one of the most abundant materials in nature with many allotropes already proven to be extremely useful to mankind. Nanoscaled carbon allotropes can be found in different forms, including graphene, graphane, graphyne, fullerenes, single or multiwalled carbon nanotubes, carbon nanohorns, carbon nanodiamonds, carbon quantum dots and carbon nano-onions, among others.39 Because of the physicochemical properties, carbon hybridization, structure and dimensionality, these classes of carbon nanostructures show a variety of applications. Graphene fundamental aspects

Graphene represents a single-atom-thick sheet of sp2 hybridized carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal honeycomb structure. Beside single-layer graphene sheet (SLG)21 with a thickness of 0.34 nm, graphene can also form bi- (BLG),22 few- (FLG)11 and multi-layer graphene sheets (MLG)23 (up to 10) of well-defined stacked graphene layers connected by van der Waals forces.12 Each carbon atom in graphene is connected through strong covalent bonds with three surrounding atoms of carbon. The fourth valent electron, however, does not participate in the formation of a covalent bond, but it is perpendicular to the graphene sheet forming a conducting band, which gives graphene special chemical and electronic properties.13 The material dimensionality classification, based on the number of dimensions outside the nanoscale (<100 nm) range, categorizes graphene as a 2D nanomaterial. The zero-dimensional (0D) nanomaterials have no dimension larger than 100 nm, while in one-dimensional nanomaterials (1D), one dimension is outside the nanoscale. Comparing 0D, 1D and three-dimensional nanomaterials (3D), which are not confined to the nanoscale in any dimension, graphene as a 2D nanomaterial has two dimensions outside the nanoscale which gives it a plate-like shape allowing it to form a nano-layer/film (Fig. 1).

Graphene is extremely strong nanomaterial with Young's module close to 1 TPa. The optical transmittance of graphene is approximately 97.7% with negligible reflectance (<0.1%). Another interesting property of graphene is the high theoretical specific surface area of 2630 m2 g1 and intrinsic mobility at Dirac point of 200000 cm2 V1 s1.24 Graphene also possesses high thermal conductivity of approximately 5000 W m1 K1 and charge carrier mobility at room temperature up to 106 cm2 V1 s1 for suspended graphene.25,26 Such arising properties enable wide possibilities of graphene applications from energy storage and conversion, electrochemical catalysis to environmental remediation.2732 These properties are strongly dependent on the number of graphene layers, the angle of stacking, as well as on the sheet size and quality.33 One of the most attractive assets of graphene is that, unlike most 2D materials, graphene is a semiconductor with valence and conduction bands symmetrically distributed on the two sides of the Fermi level. Therefore, graphene is considered a zero band gap semiconductor.34,35 These extraordinary properties connected to graphene nanomaterial opened the gate to a wide range of possibilities in terms of potential applications. Graphene characterization techniques

Detailed characterization of graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials is essential for the determination of precise properties of synthesized nanomaterial. Resolving the number of graphene layers, their size, quality/defectiveness and homogeneity, among others interesting graphene properties through characterization, the full potential of produced graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials can be explored in different fields of application. Here, we will focus on the introduction of several characterization techniques frequently applied, based on the literature survey, for graphene and graphene-based nanomaterial characterization.

As a non-destructive method for the characterization of carbon nanomaterials, researchers mostly focus on Raman spectroscopy analysis, to begin with, to establish the number and quality of formed graphene layers.36 The Raman spectrum of graphene layers shows three predominant bands located at 1350, 1580 and 2700 cm1 assigned as D, G and 2D bands, respectively. The origin of the G band comes from the sp2 hybridized carbon atoms in-plane vibration mode, while the 2D band arises as a result of two-photon lattice vibration processes in graphene.3739 Analysis of the shape, intensity and position of these bands provides substantial information regarding the crystallite size by employing the Tuinstra-Koenig relation.37,40 Raman spectroscopy offers a precise determination of the number of formed graphene layers by calculation of the 2D to G peak intensity ratio (I2D/IG) and full width at half the maximum of the 2D band (FWHM (2D)). The single and sharp 2D band with large intensity relative to the G band (I2D/IG > 2) is characteristic for SLG, while with an increase in the number of formed graphene layers, the 2D band is more asymmetric and the 2D to G band intensity ratio decreases (I2D/IG < 2).41 Aside from the existence of G and 2D bands, with an increase in the number of graphene layers, additional bands in the Raman spectrum of graphene arise. These bands are assigned as the 2D band located at approximately 3248 cm1, and weak G* band at approximately 2450 cm1. The third D band in the Raman spectra is associated with the breathing oscillation of carbon rings that are not allowed in a perfect hexagonal carbon lattice. Consequently, it is not visible in the Raman spectrum of graphene with the perfect hexagonal carbon lattice. Therefore, the presence of the D band indicates the defective nature of graphene structure or the presence of amorphous carbonaceous phases, while the intensity of the D band is directly proportional to the level of defects present. The quality, i.e., defect level of graphene thin films is usually determined through the D to G intensity ratio (ID/IG).20,42

The microscopy techniques, such as atomic force microscopy (AFM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) are the most used methods for the morphology, size and thickness analysis of formed graphene layers. AFM, as a non-invasive technique enables visualization of the sample surface in three dimensions offering information about the material diameter and height, as well as physical characteristics such as surface morphology, roughness and texture.43 SEM as a non-destructive method also provides enough information in confirmation of the number of layers and the size of the formed graphene sheets based on image contrast. However, due to SLG transparency, there is the possibility of transmission of a high-energy beam, which would result in obtaining information about the morphology of the substrate rather than the sample itself. This is where TEM analysis comes as a very useful technique providing the possibility to observe the morphological features of graphene thin films while giving accurate information on the number of graphene layers.44

Graphene surface chemistry characterization is often performed using X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). XPS offers information about surface chemical states, elemental composition, empirical formula, and electronic state of the detected elements present. For example, the typical XPS spectrum of defect-free graphene possesses only one sharp band at 284.5 eV from carbon (C).45 The deconvolution of this band revealed that only sp2 hybridized C atoms are present. In the presence of oxygen-containing functional groups, the XPS spectrum will show two bands centered around 284 and 533 eV, where the first stems from C atoms while the second one is characteristic for oxygen (O).46 The analysis of these bands through deconvolution indicates that C atoms are sp2 (CC) and sp3 (CC, CO and COO) hybridized, while O atoms are located in CO, COH/COC, COOH and H2O/O2 groups.47 The presence of heteroatoms in the graphene structure can be detected by XPS as well. X-ray diffraction (XRD) is suitable for analysis of thin-film structures on a nanometre scale allowing an investigation of the in-plane and out-of-plane crystalline structures of thin films. The XRD method is used to analyze structure parameters such as micro-strain, crystallite size and dislocation density.48

Graphene production techniques summary

Though graphene was initially studied in 1947, at the time only theoretical studies were performed with only a few predictions of the electronic structure.49 Afterward, a process for graphene extraction using simple mechanical exfoliation to produce graphene from graphite with Scotch tape was suggested by Ruoff et al.,50 However, this attempt led to the separation of MLG. Geim and Novoselov in 2004, succeeded in the separation and identification of graphene as the perfect atomic lattice by the same approach. They were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics (2010) for groundbreaking experiments regarding the two-dimensional material graphene.51,52 The upscaling research progress in graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials encouraged research optimists to even say that the research community over the past 20 years has entered the carbon age.

Graphene production over time has been divided into two key categories, top-down and bottom-up, which offered the simplest state-of-the-art technologies for graphene synthesis methods. However, the impact on the large-scale industrial production is closely connected to the development of methods for scalable synthesis of high-quality graphene. The literature survey on the Scopus database (Fig. 3) offers data that show the impact of several methods on graphene synthesis in the past 10 years. Methods such as chemical synthesis or chemical, electrochemical and mechanical exfoliation along with the definite leader in the field, the CVD method, with more than 1090 research papers published over the past 10 years, are the most explored/applied methods for graphene synthesis chosen by the research community.5357 However, each of the mentioned methods shows several extraordinary advantages in graphene synthesis but also some substantial drawbacks.

Mechanical exfoliation as a top-down, low cost and simple method for graphene synthesis has been acknowledged from the beginning by the carbon community for the explosive growth of interest in graphene synthesis.58 A method for the simple separation of graphene layers from bulk graphite through multiple exfoliation steps using Scotch tape is based on overcoming the van der Waals forces between adjacent layers of graphene.59 Separated SLG or MLG, afterward can be deposited on different substrates such as silicon wafers. Although a simple and low-cost method, mechanical exfoliation shows restrictions in the precise control of the size and quality of stacked graphene layers. The diameter of several microns, as well as the irregular shape and low-quality of produced graphene sheets, rise as a result of the residues left on the graphene surface after the Scotch tape removal.60 Consequently, the technology of graphene separation through chemical exfoliation was discovered as a good start for upcoming technologies/methods with fewer limitations. Epitaxial growth of graphene thin films on different substrates was found to be a smart alternative for graphene synthesis.61 Substantial progress in this direction has been achieved using silicon carbide (SiC) and close-packed metals as substrates. The mechanism was based on thermal decomposition of SiC in ultra-high vacuum and temperatures between 1000 and 1500 C, causing Si sublimation, and leaving a carbon-rich surface. The method provided successful production of wafer-scale graphene thin films with potential applications in the micro-electronics industry.62,63 In comparison to the epitaxial growth with the carbon precursor on the substrate surface, for CVD growth of graphene thin films, the carbon precursor was delivered into the chamber in a gaseous form using metal as a catalyst and/or substrate.64 The CVD method holds a unique advantage compared to other available methods in terms of the high-quality and uniformity of the large-scale synthesis of graphene thin films. The CVD enables the application of different substrates, such as nickel (Ni) or copper (Cu) heated at high temperature (usually around 1000 C), while hydrocarbons (methane mostly) were introduced as carbon sources for graphene growth. All of the mentioned qualities of the CVD method pushed it to the top of the scale in applied methods over the years. However, aside from many advantages, CVD also shows some disadvantages. The restrictions on the type of substrate or precursor used for graphene synthesis due to operations at elevated temperatures are one of them for sure. However, the demand for the carbon precursor to be volatile at room temperature, aside from being quite costly, toxic, explosive or corrosive, or even producing some hazardous by-products are the main setbacks in the application of the CVD method. The reduction of graphene oxide (GO) through thermal, chemical or electrochemical treatment is another method recognized for graphene sheet production.65 GO reduction by thermal treatment implies rapid heating, usually over 200 C min1, to exfoliate graphite oxide and to achieve graphene through the expansion of carbon monoxide (CO) or carbon dioxide (CO2) gases in-between graphene sheets. GO reduction by chemical reagents is based on chemical reactions at room temperature or by moderate heating. Finally, the electrochemical reduction of GO sheets or films was carried out in an electrochemical cell at room temperature by the electron exchange between GO and electrodes, without a special chemical agent. In addition to many benefits of graphene production from GO, such as ease of processing, versatility, cost-effectiveness and scalable mass production, the full reduction of GO to graphene is still hard to accomplish.66,67

Rising attention in the past 20 years over a plethora of detailed investigations of different methods for graphene synthesis pointed out several crucial issues preventing wider applications.68 Graphene synthesis efforts were made on resolving minimization issues of the synthetic route costs and simplicity, while enabling the production of large-scale materials with high purity and homogeneity.69 To do so, the focus on selected synthesis methods overwhelmed the literature data while several other known methods for graphene and graphene-based nanomaterials were neglected without exploration of their full potential.

Mechanism of graphene synthesis from electrochemical exfoliation

The electrochemical exfoliation process of graphene production implies the immersion of electrodes in an electrolyte solution with the application of electrical current between the electrodes.70 The process can be conducted by employing either two-71,72 or three-electrodes systems73,74 in both aqueous and non-aqueous electrolytes.75,76 In this kind of system, one of the electrodes has a role as a graphene source, typically consisting of a graphite rod,77,78 graphite foil,79,80 graphite slices,81 pencil core,82 graphitic paper,83,84 or highly-oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG).85 After completing the process, the obtained material has to be purified from the electrolyte residuals and large un-exfoliated graphite particles through several steps involving predominantly sonication, centrifugation, and/or filtration.

Graphene exfoliation takes place in two consecutive steps: intercalation and expansion. By applying the electrical current, the electrolyte ions intercalate between the graphene sheets in graphite, causing the sheets expansion and finally, exfoliation of the graphene layers.86 Depending on the charge at a working electrode that acts as a graphene source, the exfoliation can be either cathodic or anodic.87 Understanding the mechanism of graphene exfoliation paves the way for process optimization by defining the ideal conditions for both intercalation and the expansion step. According to Parvez et al. electrolysis of water gives hydroxyl ions, which act as strong nucleophiles88 attacking edges, defect sites, and exposed grain boundaries of graphene while introducing oxygen-containing functional groups in graphene structure. The introduction of functional groups, leads to the expansion of graphene layers, allowing the intercalation of electrolyte anions between layers.89 The anion reduction caused by the electrical current produced gas bubbles by applying force on the graphene layers. Consequently, the high demand for applied suitable reduction potential allows electrolyte anions to generate gas molecules under applied conditions. To fulfill this request, one of the most commonly used anions for electrochemical exfoliation experiments was sulfate ion (SO42). After the bubbles created enough force to exceed the weak van der Waals forces between layers, separation/exfoliation of the individual graphene layers were allowed. This mechanism was later confirmed by Huang et al.90 However, Rao et al. proposed the mechanism that implied the polymerization of hydrogen sulfate (HSO4) and SO42 anions into dimmers, trimmers, and clusters.91 The authors suggested that after the cluster reached a certain size, it was reduced to a neutral molecule, which eventually leads to the formation of large gas bubbles of sulfur dioxide (SO2). The evolution of these gas bubbles facilitated further exfoliation. Several papers reported the application of different electrode potentials for the intercalation and expansion processes. Hsieh et al. conducted electrochemical exfoliation under constant voltage and current conditions through the application of two-step direct current (DC) bias on the electrode.92 The processes of surface wetting and ionic intercalation occurred at low voltage or low current, while the exfoliation occurred at high voltage or high current. The separation of the overall process into two steps made it possible to produce high-quality graphene. Sahoo et al. initially applied negative potential at the working electrode, which was later switched to positive potential.93 A short period of cathodic treatment served for the intercalation of hydronium ions (H3O+) between graphene layers, which underwent electrochemical reactions and produced hydrogen gas bubbles. Although hydrogen gas bubbles encouraged the weakening of van der Waals forces, due to its small volume it was not sufficient to complete the exfoliation. In this phase, the surface of the electrode was cleaned from any physically adsorbed impurities. Switching to the anodic potential enabled the intercalation of electrolyte anions, which further lead to graphene exfoliation. During the anodic treatment, an introduction of various oxygen-containing groups to the graphene sheets was successfully achieved.

The electrochemical exfoliation of graphene was additionally performed in non-aqueous media. Liu et al. conducted the exfoliation in the solution of lithium perchlorate (LiClO4) in propylene carbonate initially applying negative potential followed by positive potential.84 At the first stage, after the application of the negative potential, Li+ ions along with propylene carbonate molecules intercalated graphite distorting layered structure. Once the electrode potential was switched to positive, perchlorate anions intercalated the distorted graphite with simultaneous de-intercalation of Li+ ions. The third stage pointed toward the immersion of the electrode in water for intercalated perchlorate anions to hydrolyze and release oxygen gas bubbles. At this stage, the formation of hydroxyl and carbonyl groups, in smaller amounts, on the graphene surface occurred additionally. Graphene was successfully electrochemically exfoliated in an ionic liquid electrolyte.94,95 Lei et al. performed the exfoliation of graphene from graphite paper in the mixture of 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride ([EMIm]Cl) and aluminum chloride (AlCl3) and proposed the mechanism for this approach.96 According to the authors, under constant voltage, anions tetrachloroaluminate (AlCl4) and heptachlorodialuminate (Al2Cl7) among others, adsorbed and intercalated into the graphite electrode, which resulted in the exfoliation of MLG nanoplates.

Quality of the exfoliated graphene

The quality of exfoliated graphene is generally characterized by the number of graphene layers in the final product, graphene oxidation degree (the C/O ratio), number of defects, or in some cases average lateral dimensions of graphene sheets. The oxidation or homolytic fusion of water molecules under an anodic exfoliation process generates hydroxyl and other oxygen radicals. The formed radical species react rapidly with graphene sheets introducing oxygen-containing functional groups into the graphene structure, making it partially oxidized.88 The level of graphene oxidation is usually monitored through analysis of the C/O ratio from elemental analysis techniques such as XPS. For certain applications of exfoliated graphene, such as the preparation of anode material for lithium-ion batteries,97,98 it is important to minimize the effect of radicals on the graphene structure. This can be achieved either during the process of the exfoliation or as a separate post-synthetic step. One way of radical effect improvement is the introduction of reducing agent additives. This way generated radical species preferably react with the additive molecule rather than graphene sheet. The introduction of the additives such as (2,2,6,6-tetramethyl piperidin-1-yl)oxyl (TEMPO),99 ethylene glycol or melamine,100 resulted in decrease in the oxygen content in the exfoliated graphene product. Some transition metal cations, such as cobalt (Co2+) and iron (Fe3+), as reported by Ejigu et al., showed similar effective nature in the protection of graphene surface and production of low oxygen content and defect density material.101 The usage of the Co2+ salt during exfoliation led to the formation of graphene with 80% less oxygen compared to graphene exfoliated in the absence of Co2+ salt. Likewise, the use of sodium chloride (NaCl) as an electrolyte additive prevented the oxidation of the graphene sheets and lead to an overall C/O ratio of approximately 0.020.03.102 One of the important factors for graphene formation with low oxygen content and low defect density is the electrolyte medium temperature. The exfoliation at lower temperatures lessens the number of generated hydroxyl radicals giving graphene with lower oxygen content103,104 providing graphene with better quality in terms of the decreased number of broken layers and fragments as well.105

The number of graphene layers produced remains an important parameter with a strong influence on the graphene electronic properties. This may be one of the most important disadvantages of the electrochemical exfoliation process since graphene obtained by this method is usually classified as an MLG (Fig. 5). The low-quality of produced graphene is frequently connected with the presence of overlapped layers, wrinkles, and/or folded edges. Modification of the electrochemical procedure, through the formation of an intermediate graphite intercalation compound, opens the possibility for the preparation of FLG106,107 or even BLG.108,109 However, SLG production by the electrochemical exfoliation method remains a research challenge.

Among various methods explored for the production of graphene, electrochemical exfoliation is considered one of the most efficient pathways. The increasing number of published papers on the electrochemical exfoliation of graphene reflects its prominence and high demand for countless applications from energy storage, sensors, catalysts, conducting films, anticorrosion and flame protection, hybrid membranes, to new composite materials. Compared to frequently employed methods, electrochemical exfoliation benefits in a practical sense as cost-effective, environmentally friendly, less time consuming, and graphene production scale-up. Despite significant progress in this area, there are remaining issues that need to be addressed, such as the production of preferably SLG, effective and tunable control of the oxidation level, and the production of electrochemically exfoliated graphene at a large industrial scale. All of the above-mentioned concerns still represent a challenge and motivation for future investigations in this field.

The mechanism of graphene synthesis by the RTA method

The difference between RTA and classical furnace lies in the time-consuming progression of the furnace-annealing process. Due to classical furnace capabilities and large size, occasionally a longer annealing time of 30 min to even several hours, is necessary for the furnace to reach the anticipated temperature and keep it constant during the annealing period.161 However, in the RTA process, the desired temperature can be reached in only a few minutes and maintained constant for the preferred time. Therefore, the RTA method offers possibilities for precise control of the whole annealing and cooling processes in a short time with additional advantages in setting temperature ramps.162 These advantages were found to be especially significant for the synthesis and modification of graphene-based materials as well as for the improvement of structural and consequently electrical properties of the fabricated film. Hence, the RTA process allows working with a fine combination of different parameters. Such reaction parameters of working in short periods, under low or high temperatures and different atmospheres while using different substrates and carbon precursors all together affect the final product's nature, size and quality.163 All of the above-mentioned benefits of the RTA method were found to be very useful to the carbon research community for the synthesis of graphene thin films on the desired substrates.

The effect of the substrate chosen for graphene growth is mostly dependent on the desired applications of the synthesized graphene thin films. Based on the literature findings, substrates, such as Si or/and SiO2 wafers,164,165 SiC,166 Cu foil20 or graphite18,19 are often used as supports during the RTA graphene synthesis, with or without the presence of metallic catalysts.164 Among different catalysts, Ni, Cu, Co and NiCu alloy167170 are typically used as catalysts for graphene synthesis by the RTA method for several reasons. One is the affordable price compared to other appropriate metals for the same purpose, while the other addresses the solubility of carbon in these metals. In fact, due to the high solubility of carbon in Ni, carbon atoms can segregate and diffuse through Ni during annealing at high temperatures and precipitate on its surface during cooling to form graphene.171,172 Oppositely, Cu demonstrates low carbon solubility and high thermal stability, which allows Cu foil to be used mostly as a support for graphene synthesis or as a boundary layer during graphene growth, preventing the diffusion of dissolved carbon back to the substrate. Therefore, besides Si or SiO2 wafers, Cu foil was found to be an interesting substrate in graphene synthesis by the RTA method as well. The graphene synthesis through the RTA method includes several basic steps. The first step is the pretreatment of the substrate surface to remove impurities and/or to decrease surface roughness, which will allow the growth of the fine graphene thin films avoiding defects in the form of folded layers or vacancies. The second step involves the deposition of carbon and metal sources on the substrate surface, which could be done through a chemical or electrochemical pathway or by metal sputtering and so forth. The final step implies the actual growth of graphene thin films under rapid thermal annealing and cooling.

The literature survey shows several research papers exploring the effect of different parameters on graphene thin film synthesis by the RTA method. For example, Zhang et al. investigated the possibility of graphene synthesis on the surface of different transition metals in the presence of pyrolytic carbon (99.99% purity). For this purpose, the carbon precursor was deposited on the surface of transition metals in the vacuum chamber using short-pulse laser-produced plasma deposition techniques. To explore the effect of annealing temperature on graphene thin film synthesis and quality, prepared samples were annealed at high temperatures such as 1100, 1200 and 1300 C.173 Another research investigated the effect of silicon wafer type on the synthesis of graphene layers. This research performed by Bleu et al. showed a comparison of the nature and quality of the obtained layers in the presence of Ni catalyst on crystalized (Si) and amorphous (SiO2) substrates. Prior to the deposition of carbon precursor, Si and SiO2 substrates were cleaned ultrasonically to remove any surface impurities after which the pulsed laser deposition was applied for the deposition of solid carbon source (high purity microcrystalline graphite) on defined substrates. Prepared samples were submitted to RTA under a low vacuum (104 Pa) at a temperature range of 600 C to 1000 C.174,175 The graphene synthesis from a solid precursor in the absence of metal catalysts leads to higher defect density compared to synthesis in the presence of a metal catalyst.176,177 Another similar experiment was performed on Si support in the presence of Ni as catalysts by annealing samples at 1000 C in a vacuum.178 The application of graphene quantum dots (GQDs), fullerol or amorphous carbon in this research as a carbon precursor showed that not only the support type and annealing conditions but additionally the carbon precursor type affected significantly the graphene synthesis. However, the amorphous carbon precursor due to a disordered structure gave no prospects in the synthesis of fine monoatomic graphene layers. Nevertheless, GQD and fullerol even at a very low concentration such as 0.06 mg ml1 resulted in the successful synthesis of nonhomogeneous, though SLG thin films.178

The effect of the origin of the carbon precursor, concentration and annealing temperature and time on the graphene growth by applying the RTA process is meaningful.1820,178 Aside from pulsed laser deposition, the electroless deposition of Ni thin film from batch water solution with dissolved carbon precursor is one of the most effective ways for the metal catalyst deposition on the desired substrate. Prekodravac et al. performed the synthesis of graphene thin layers on Cu foil by the RTA method under vacuum at 600 C.20 Prior to Cu surface plating, the authors performed pretreatment of Cu foil surface to avoid the presence of impurities, wrinkles or other types of defects introduced in the synthesized graphene layers (Fig. 8). The pre-treatment of Cu foil surface implied chemical treatment for the removal of the surface impurities (mostly oxides), after which the thermal treatment was performed to reduce the surface roughness and/or increase of the grain boundary size for the grown graphene.179181

The disadvantages of using metal catalysts are generally connected with the full possible application of graphene in functional devices requiring deposition of large-scale high-quality graphene sheets on dielectric supports. Therefore, the synthesis of graphene on Cu foil or in the presence of Ni catalyst would imply a necessary transfer of the synthesized graphene through a delamination process or etching of the metal support with strong acid which would substantially lead to the introduction of wrinkles, impurities or other defects into the graphene sheet. Due to its unique electronic properties, as mentioned before, graphene was recognized as potential material for electronic devices. However, acknowledging the methods for graphene synthesis as it was presented so far, it can introduce defects and impurities at the exfoliated/synthesized graphene layers with a significant reflection on its quality, and therefore on its properties. The arisen idea for applying the RTA method under low temperatures to remove impurities and improve graphene quality mostly resulted in the elimination of residual impurities with no significant effect on graphene quality improvement regarding defects.187189 Then, again, annealing under higher temperatures leads to amorphization of the formed graphene. One of the research investigations was focused on the removal of PMMA residue left on graphene surfaces after the transfer process.190 After synthesis of SLG through the CVD method on Cu foil, graphene samples were transferred on a SiO2 (300 nm) substrate by chemical etching of Cu foil using PMMA as a protection layer. The PMMA was then removed with acetone, leaving graphene/SiO2/Si with residual polymer on the surface. Samples were afterward annealed under diverse ambient conditions (Ar, H2, Ar/H2 and under vacuum) in the temperature range of 100600 C and temperature ramp 25 C min1 for 120 min. Variations in atomic percentage with RTA annealing temperature indicated that under different atmospheres there are no significant changes registered up to 400 C. However, above 400 C depolymerization of PMMA was followed by simultaneous destruction of pendant functional groups. These functional groups were almost or completely vanished under vacuum, H2 or Ar/H2 mixture, while polymer backbone bonds were still present in reduced percentages. Although the complete removal of the polymer was not achieved, the backbone bond-breaking was observed in the temperature range of 300 to 400 C, which led to the reduction of polymer residues on the graphene surface. Annealing ambiance under Ar was proven to not only be adequate for PMMA residue removal but also the prevention of amorphous carbon formation and preservation of quality graphene structure.191193

The electronic and chemical properties tuning is essential for diverse graphene applications, which can be accomplished by graphene chemical doping. The overall approach towards the synthesis of N-doped graphene, such as CVD performed in gas phases, requires transition metal catalysts, which could contaminate the products and affect their properties. Sheng et al. proposed a facile catalyst-free thermal annealing approach for the large-scale synthesis of N-doped graphene using low-cost materials such as melamine as the nitrogen source.194 As a starting material, they used GO obtained by Hummers' method and melamine in mass ratio 1:5. Under annealing from 700 to 1000 C in Ar atmosphere for 1 hour, melamine was adsorbed on the GO surface and transformed/condensed to graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4). At such a high temperature, the oxygen-containing groups of GO undergo decomposition, allowing the nitrogen atoms to attach to the formed active sites in the graphene framework. The incorporated nitrogen content measured by XPS in graphene layers was between 5.6 and 10.1%. Different nitrogen content (in at%) was achieved by controlling the mass ratio of GO and melamine, annealing temperature and time, with the maximum, reported to be 10.1 at% under 700 C with a 0.2 mass ratio of GO to melamine.

Quality of the RTA synthesized graphene

The quality of graphene thin films synthesized by the RTA method is usually monitored through several spectroscopic and microscopic techniques such as Raman spectroscopy, AFM, SEM and TEM among others. These techniques offer valuable information on graphene layer number and their defect level as well as the size of synthesized graphene thin films as one of the most important graphene properties for diverse possible applications.

Detailed analysis of synthesized graphene thin films from different carbon precursors, on different substrate types and reaction conditions, shows a significant effect of all the mentioned parameters on the size and quality of the obtained graphene thin layers. Zhang et al. confirmed the formation of highly defected nonhomogeneous MLG sheets from pyrolytic carbon, with an average length of graphene thin films of 1 to 3 m and a width of 200 to 350 nm. The obtained results revealed the high impact of annealing temperature on the quality of the synthesized graphene thin films.195 Through Raman spectroscopy and surface Raman mapping, Bleu et al. investigated homogeneity, crystallite size, defects and the number of layers in the synthesized graphene materials.175,196 After annealing at different temperatures ranging from 600 to 1000 C, the authors concluded that the type of substrate affects the quality and nature of the synthesized graphene layers significantly. The temperature increase induced the growth of defects in Si-supported samples with ID/IG from 0.297 to 0.468, resulting in a decrease in the mean crystallite size and consequently the formation of homogeneous multilayered graphene samples. However, graphene sheets grown on SiO2 supports were predominantly BLG and FLG with increased crystallite size and lower defect level (0.293 to 0.140) under the same annealing temperature. The synthesis of graphene thin layers on Si support in the presence of Ni as a catalyst at 1000 C in vacuum178 by Prekodravac et al. showed similar results for the GQD carbon precursor compared to high purity microcrystalline graphite. The defect levels for obtained graphene thin films were calculated to be 0.4 for the GQD precursor, while for the fullerol precursor the calculated ID/IG ratio was higher (0.9) as a result of different structures of used carbon precursors. The approximate sizes of the obtained graphene thin films were 900 nm and 600 nm, for the GQD and fullerol precursor, respectively. The fullerol source, due to the presence of five rings and buckyball shape, was generally harder to transform than GQD, which already possesses six ring honeycomb structures.178 The graphene thin films synthesis from GQD and fullerol as a precursor in a concentration range of 0.25 to 1 mg ml1, on Cu foil as a substrate at lower annealing temperature (600 C for 30 min) was also investigated through Raman analysis. Obtained results led to the conclusion that the concentration and type of precursor used affected the number of formed graphene layers, their quality and size. Through the I2D/IG ratio authors determined the growth of SLG at lower precursor concentrations, while at higher concentrations formation of BLG was observed. Additional bands in the Raman spectrum designed as D' (1620 cm1) and D + G (2940 cm1) confirmed the expected results showing that the quality of graphene layers decreased with the increase in NGL.20

The use of PMMA as a precursor for graphene growth, at higher concentrations, leads to the formation of a thicker PMMA layer (above 15 nm), which consequently, during annealing, resulted in the formation of graphene thin films only from the PMMA layers close or directly in contact with Ni. On the contrary, PMMA layers closer to the substrate (SiO2) led to the formation of lower quality graphene. Thus, the present research showed the vital influence of the PMMA concentration and the presence of the Ni layer on the quality of the synthesized graphene.186 RTA was proven to be a successful method for doping graphene layers with transition metals as well. One of these studies confirmed the formation of compact and stacked N-doped SLG thin films with highly defective structures formed upon exfoliation or N-doping.197

The application of the RTA synthesized graphene thin films

As it was mentioned earlier, one of the advantages of RTA-produced graphene is the ability to perform synthesis on different substrates as well as the possibility of performing delamination and transfer of synthesized graphene thin films on diverse substrates depending on the desired application. MLG thin films obtained through the RTA method were potentially investigated as an H2 gas sensor. Gas detection by graphene materials was generally based on the conductance changes upon the adsorption of sensing species. Several groups have studied the sensing performance of graphene with sensors capable of detecting a variety of gases such as NO2, NH3 and CO2. The performances of these sensors were influenced by several factors, such as temperature, rate of target gas and the length-to-width ratio of the graphene sheet.198

Growing markets for flexible transparent conductors such as touch screens, flexible displays, printable electronics and thin-film photovoltaics for renewable energy applications have established demands for exploring new transparent conductors for optoelectronic applications. For over four decades, indium tin oxide (ITO) has been the most widely used transparent conductor in optoelectronic applications. Nevertheless, material cost, exacerbation due to ion diffusion or brittleness limits the further utilization of ITO for future flexible devices. On the other hand, graphene is optically transparent, flexible and has high oxidation resistance, making it a candidate for transparent conducting films (TCFs). Chu et al. performed RTA synthesis and obtained graphene thin films with thickness of less than <2 nm. SLG thin films had low sheet resistance of approximately 0.9 k sq1, with optical transparency of nearly 94%, making them useful for applications as flexible transparent conductors.199 However, for wider applications, the conductance of graphene sheets remains below that of commercial ITO films.200

The high carrier mobility, optical transparency, mechanical strength and atomic thickness of graphene layers makes it particularly convenient for flexible field-effect transistor (FET) applications. The key prospects for graphene use in both high-performance and low-power applications stem from the atomic thinness of graphene-based devices, leading to almost perfect control of the channel potential. Graphene sheets processing into nanoribbons can open up a band gap large enough for a room-temperature transistor. Therefore, graphene with a width below 10 nm represents one of the most commonly used conducting materials for graphene field-effect transistor (GFETs). The GFET as an attractive replacement for silicon-based transistors consists of a thin graphene channel placed between the source and drain metal electrodes. In comparison to silicon-based devices, GFET allows equivalent conduction of electrons and holes. Due to ambipolar performance, the GFET device shows holes carrier conduction in the channel region under negative bias, while positive bias outcomes in electron carrier conduction. Superior electrical and thermal conductivities of graphene result in low resistance losses and better heat distribution compared to those in silicon, consequently, leading to the enhancement of device efficiency. Due to SLG thickness, the graphene channel located on the surface is in direct contact with the environment enabling high sensitivity operations at the terahertz frequencies. The precursor type and concentration used for the RTA production of the graphene thin film as well as the annealing temperature and time affect the diameter and number of obtained graphene layers, while the selection of the substrate for graphene thin film growth shows a strong effect on their quality. Subsequently, by the adjustment of all of the above-mentioned parameters, good quality SLG with the desired size can be produced, consequently improving the performance of the GFET device. Wang et al. demonstrated that the on/off ratio of GFET growth with the drop in the width of graphene nanoribbon at room temperature, with on/off ratio and carrier mobility of up to approximately 1 106 and 200 cm2 V1 s1, respectively.201 Metal or metal oxide as a conventional electrode for FETs expressed limited applications. The disadvantages such as large contact resistance between the metal electrode and the active layer of FETs, among others, limit their applications. Graphene as an electrode can effectively overcome several disadvantages of the metal electrode for FETs, which is why graphene transparent thin films were widely used as an electrode material for GFETs.202,203

Several recent reports demonstrated that N-doped graphene shows excellent electrocatalytic activity concerning the oxygen reduction reaction. These observations revealed the possibility of replacing expensive Pt-based catalysts with N-doped carbon nanomaterials inspiring the research community to search for highly efficient metal-free catalysts toward oxygen reduction reaction in fuel cells.204,205 Making the comparison on bare glassy carbon electrode (GCE) and graphene/GCE or N-doped graphene/GCE for reduction of O2 in 0.1 M potassium hydroxide (KOH) aqueous solution, several conclusions were made. First, graphene/GCE had a more positive onset and larger reduction current for the oxygen reduction reaction due to faster electron transfer kinetics for oxygen reduction reaction on graphene compared to the GCE alone. At the same time, N-doped graphene/GCE showed both the positive shift of onset potential and the enhanced reduction current for oxygen reduction reaction compared to graphene. Results demonstrate that the N-doped material possesses a much higher electrocatalytic activity toward the oxygen reduction reaction, probably due to fast reaction kinetics and a higher number of transferred electrons per oxygen molecule.

Among various bottom-up methods based on the literature search, the RTA method offers precise control of the reaction conditions for controllable graphene synthesis, enabling their synthesis on different substrates and from different carbon sources deposited through chemical, physical or electrochemical approaches. Control of the annealing temperature and time, as well as the selection of the carbon precursor type and concentration, provides the possibility of synthesizing graphene thin films with the desired number of layers. The quality of the RTA obtained films is closely connected to the substrate and precursor type or on some occasions with the transfer process conditions. Therefore, the RTA method can be considered as a cost-effective, environmentally friendly and less time-consuming efficient pathway for graphene production, enabling the production of SLG with low defect level. In addition to the many benefits that this method provides, one of the biggest disadvantages is a lack of scale-up synthesis since graphene thin films were predominantly from several hundred nanometers to few micrometers in diameter, which can restrict their wider application. All of the above-mentioned pros and cons still represent a challenge and motivation for future studies in this field.

The mechanism of graphene synthesis from biomass

The basic principle of biomass-derived graphene is based on the pyrolysis of different starting feedstocks as the carbon source under an inert atmosphere. The predominant biomass precursors, from the literature overview, for graphene-based nanomaterial production are natural sources such as plants or wood parts, agricultural waste, fruits, vegetables, biopolymers, or even insect parts, among many others (Fig. 11).

Depending on the experimental protocol and used chemicals, the most explored pyrolysis graphene synthetic processes are pyrolysis on Cu support, salt-based pyrolysis, template-based synthesis, and hydrothermal carbonization which is followed by pyrolysis. For salt-based pyrolysis, the selection of salt with a low melting point and high temperature of decomposition is beneficial due to the formation of a deep eutectic mixture. During the pyrolysis, salts melt, providing a liquid environment for chemical reactions to occur between carbon intermediaries to produce graphene. These carbon intermediaries are formed as a consequence of the non-carbon atom component loss in the form of volatile compounds under thermal treatment. Salt's ability to act as an activation agent enabled the induction of etching, pore formation and exfoliation process, with KOH as the often used activation agent. The K atoms from KOH, react with graphite microcrystals producing a charge transfer complex the K+[graphene-H] followed by the removal of H atoms and the formation of sp2 atoms.211 The reaction of KOH with carbon-based nanomaterials leads to the release of CO(g) and CO2(g) and consequently to graphene layer separation and formation of the interlayered species/pores.

The first biomass-derived high-quality graphene sample on one side of the Cu foil was obtained through a thermal treatment of different feedstocks, such as cookies, chocolate, grass, plastics, roaches, and dog feces. The key factor in obtaining high-quality graphene was the inability of all starting material components, besides carbon atoms, to dissolve in metal and migrate to the other side of the Cu foil.208 The experiment was performed by introducing a carbon source (10 mg) on Cu foil, followed by thermal treatment at 1050 C under low-pressure H2/Ar (flow rate 100 and 500 cm3 min1) for 15 min. Depending on the formed intermediates aside from the volatility and humidity of the precursor, using grass or dog feces, the moisture was removed by heating for 10 h under vacuum. Formed H2(g) acted as a reducing agent and as a carrier gas. Taking into consideration the starting material's high carbon percentage and thermodynamic stability of graphene, graphene itself was the singular material obtained on the Cu surface. Coconut coir dust, as a side product in the process of long coconut fiber fabrication, was another natural material successfully used for graphene synthesis. Experiments were performed under annealing for 2 h in the temperature range of 500 to 1500 C in N2 atmosphere.212 Very attractive biomolecules such as starch, abundant natural availability, low price and renewability are composed of linear amylose and branched amylopectin. These amylose layers were successfully used by Sayali et al. as a starting material for graphene synthesis.213 The agricultural waste, rice husk (RH), with the chemical composition of 15% carbon, 18% ash and 67% volatile matter as highly abundant material has a global annual production of approximately 120 million tons per year.214 So far, it was recycled only for low-value products and use, however recently, this natural material was successfully applied for graphene synthesis.215,216 The first suggestion for the RH bio-waste use as a starting material for value-added and high-demand materials such as graphene was made by Muramatsu et al.217 The study resulted in large-scale graphene production through heating at 850 C in the presence of KOH as the activation agent. The first step of graphene production involved the controlled combustion of dried RH to obtain ash/charcoal RH (RHA).218 The following step involved the chemical activation by mixing RHA (5 g) with KOH (25 g) under shield conditions to prevent oxidation against air at high temperatures. Finally, samples were washed thoroughly with water and filtered until neutral medium and dried at 100 C for 24 h. KOH activation was also used to produce graphene from natural reed membranes,219 bamboo (Phyllostachys viridis),220 cellulose pulp,221 spruce (Picea abies),222 Populus wood biomass (PWB),223 peat moss,224 peanut dregs225 and coal tar.226 Synthetic approaches from mentioned sources were based on mixing the cleaned and grounded starting material with KOH in different mass ratios followed by activation in the temperature range of 400 to 1400 C. In the final stage, the materials were washed with acid and water to remove the remaining activation agent. Even though 2D graphene-based nanomaterials have been proven favorable in many different electrochemical applications, proficient use of graphene's extraordinarily large specific surface area and physicalchemical properties remains a challenge. The integration of graphene into macroscopic 3D porous networks has attracted significant attention due to the derived unique properties as a result of a synergistic effect. An example of the synergistic action of the temperature and activation agent was the preparation of porous graphene-based nanomaterial from flower Plumeria rubra as precursor.235 The experiment was performed through gas exfoliation (H2, CO2, CO) in the presence of KOH as an activation agent, wherein the raw material-released gases caused the exfoliation. These events lead to the formation of both graphene and pores in the graphene-based nanostructure. Porous 3D graphene-based nanomaterial was also successfully formed from black liquor precursor,236 as a side product in the Kraft process during the cellulose conversion into paper, containing lignin, hemicellulose, and inorganic chemicals.237 Lignin is the toxic by-product of both the cellulose-ethanol industry and one of the most emerging pollutants of inland water in China.238 Lignin, isolated from alkaline pulp black liquor, was used for the synthesis of 3D graphene-based nanomaterials by rapid freeze-drying technique, where heat treatments led to carbonization and graphitization. The authors suggested that the decomposition of aromatic groups and alkaline fragments in lignin during pyrolysis. Benzene rings were involved in the building of graphene sheets, while free sp3 carbon atoms were chemically bonded to graphene sheets along with lignin polymeric chains. Due to the formation of these bonds, self-supported and self-constructed 3D graphene-based nanomaterial was successfully formed. Graphene with a 3D porous structure was also prepared from alga,239 through high-temperature metallic catalysis and chemical activation or directly from a plant Salvia splendens as a precursor240 using NaCl as a template. Various 3D porous graphene-based nanomaterials were formed from wood products, cellulose-based medium-density fiberboard (MDF),241Bougainvillea spectabilis flowers242 and vine shoots243 using different metal-salts catalysts. Pristine materials here were impregnated with metal salts and carbonized in an N2 atmosphere at a temperature range of 300 to 1000 C. A further approach for the fabrication of functional carbonaceous nanomaterials from biomass using pyrolytic gases and waste heat was reported recently.244 Biomass components, such as lignin, cellulose, wheat straw and sawdust when pyrolyzed in the CVD tube furnace at 800 C prompted biomass decomposition into volatiles and carbon residual material.

Heteroatom doped graphene-based nanomaterials offer advantages in the development of advanced catalysts. With this in mind, aside from porous 3D graphene-based structures, porous and hetero-doped 3D graphene/carbon-based nanomaterials obtained from biomass were successfully fabricated by several authors. One of the examples was the synthesis from soybeans milk precursor,245 which contained a high share of N and O atoms originating from the high protein content. The synthesis was achieved by using soluble salts as hard templates (NaCl, Na2CO3, ZnCl2 and Mg5(OH)2(CO3)4). Milk with added salts was quickly frozen with liquid N2 and dry-frizzed at 55 C under vacuum, followed by carbonization at 700 C and 950 C for 2 h in the N2 atmosphere. The S, N co-doped graphene-based structures were successfully synthesized from keratin as a starting material,246 with pre-carbonization of the biomass at 300 C for 30 min in N2 to encourage precursor decomposition to carbon, CO2 and H2O. The produced CO2 and H2O were removed under continuous N2 flow, while the residual carbon was mixed with KOH in the mass ratio of 1:2, and heated in the N2 atmosphere for 2 h at 600 C, followed by acid/H2O washing. The final step in graphene production involved the graphitization for 15 min at 1000 C with NH3. Graphene and N-doped or B-doped graphene was also successfully produced from chitosan through pyrolysis at 900 C in two stages.247,248 The first stage implied annealing with a sequence at 200 C for 2 h, followed by lowering to room temperature and consequently heating to 900 C. In the next stage, the obtained graphitic-based materials were exfoliated with an ultrasound source at 400 W (2 h). After 24 h of settling down, the dispersion was centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 30 min.249 The production of the 2D N-doped graphene nanomaterial from pine nutshell was performed at 600 C for 1 h in an inert atmosphere followed by mixing with KOH and melamine at different mass ratios (1:3:0, 1:3:0.5; 1:3:1; 1:3:2).250 The activation was achieved at 800 C for 2 h followed by KOH removal with HCl and water. N-Doped graphene was also produced from glucose, fructose, and 5-hydroxymethyl furfural as easily available and financially advantageous biomass sources by heating at 600 C and 900 C for 1 h under Ar atmosphere.251 Finally, powders were dispersed in water, sonicated for 1 h and dried at 60 C. Other bio-precursors with high potential as a carbon source for the production of N-doped graphene nanomaterial were ginger root and chitosan.252,253 Before synthesis, ginger was peeled and cut into small portions, washed with water and dried at 240 C. The carbonization was achieved at 600, 800, 900 C, for 1 h, under an Ar atmosphere. The N-doped graphene nanomaterial synthesis from chitosan was performed by dissolving chitosan in (NH4)3BO3 solution, filtration and deposition onto a quartz surface followed by pyrolysis at 900 C for 2 h under Ar flow. The soybean dregs, as a carbon source, also showed great potential in the production of N-doped carbon nanomaterial.254 For this purpose, dregs were first dried, grounded and mixed with asalt mixture (NaCl/ZnCl2, 80 to 20% ratios), after which the oxidizing agent NaNO3 in different amounts was added. The mixture was ball milled at 600 rpm for 6 h, followed by heating at 900 C under inert gas for 1 h. Purity of produced graphene-based materials

The Tour's group's pioneering results on the synthesized graphene nanomaterial through Raman spectroscopy showed the presence of G and 2D peaks with the I2D/IG ratio higher than 1.8 and FWHM values of 14.1 to 16.3 cm1 and 32.0 to 35.1 cm1, respectively.208 The absence of a disordered D peak from the sample and the ID/IG ratio below 0.1 pointed to the formation of high-quality graphene. These results undoubtedly prove the formation of high-quality SLG nanomaterial. Further analysis by XPS showed only a sharp band at 284.5 eV assigned to the sp2 graphitic band, while the UV-Vis analysis showed a band at 268 nm characteristic of * transitions of aromatics as well as the absorption at 550 nm from SLG. The measured graphene sheet resistivity was in the range from 1.5 to 3 k cm2.

Graphene obtained from coconut coir dust showed a highly disordered graphene structure,212 compared to graphene synthesized by Tour's group.208 Raman spectra showed changes in both D and G peaks shape and intensity with the temperature increase from 500 to 1500 C due to the formation of graphite crystallites. XRD analysis confirmed the presence of the band at 22 in the hydrothermally treated samples at 500 C originating from amorphous carbon, while samples treated at 1000 C showed a broad peak at around 43 assigned to the (001) plane of the graphite. Samples annealed at 1500 C showed a band around 26.5 assigned to the (002) plane of graphite and peaks at 43 and 45 designated to (100) and (001) planes, respectively, from graphite. These results prove that the graphene synthesis occurs only above 1000 C for hydrothermally treated samples. The measured distance between basal planes was 3.4 , while for the ideal graphite layerdistance is 3.3 . The application of graphene-based nanomaterials obtained from biomass and bio-waste

The produced graphene-based nanomaterials, among other possible applications, were mostly investigated for energy storage, as sorbents for water and air cleaning or as catalysts. As it was stated previously, the first graphene produced from biomass was obtained in the form of a one-atom-thick layer on Cu support, with graphene size limited by the size of the furnace tube and Cu support. Produced graphene as a defect-free material was transferred using PMMA on different supports and used in electronic devices or solar cells.208 Other graphene-based nanomaterials produced from biomass were mostly highly defective, with a large surface area, high porosity, often doped with N or other heteroatoms, which made them suitable for energy storage applications.256,272,273,282284,292,294296 For example, graphene produced from alga showed electrochemical capacitance of 90 F g1 at 1 mV s1, indicating a possible application as supercapacitors. Similarly, graphene flakes produced by annealing and carbonization of amylose had SBET of 1163 m2 g1 and capacitance of 261 F g1, which is comparable or even higher than that of nowadays materials used for electrodes in supercapacitors.272 N-Doped graphene-based nanomaterials from Pune nutshells showed outstanding supercapacitor electrochemical performances with a specific capacitance of 324 F g1 at 0.05 A g1.273 Graphene produced from cucumbers showed excellent supercapacitor performance for the produced material similar to graphene from potato starch and GO (capacitance of 301 F g1 at a current density of 0.5 A g1 with capability rate of 81% at 50 A g1, the energy density of 8.2 W h kg1 at a power density of 125 W kg1 in 6 M KOH electrolyte, or 62.5 W h kg1 in 1 M TEABF4/AN electrolyte).274,295 Graphene produced from spruce bark, reed membranes, and ginger root among others showed exceptional supercapacitor properties as well.282284,287,290,292,294,296

The porous graphene-like structure showed an efficient ability to store Li-ions due to the synergistic effects of both hierarchical porous structure and N, O co-doping.47 Another graphene-based nanomaterial produced from wood significantly increased the capacity of SIB to store Na+.297 Porous 3D graphene produced from black liquor, seaweed, and cellulose showed a high capacity as an adsorbent in applications for heavy metal ions in water purification, organic dye molecules, and organic solvents (benzene, xylene or toluene).259,271,278,298,299 Graphene produced from GO and sugarcane powder showed a great promise as a sorbent for the removal of both organic solvents and oil,300 with the capacity to absorb oils between 25 and 50 g of oil per 1 g of material. For indoor air cleaning, carbon-based nanomaterials produced from an olive stone could absorb NO2 gas (10.67 mg g1 after 90 min of gas loading) or CO2 (7.2 mmol g1).267,277 Porous graphene nanomaterials produce from the flower Plumeria rubra and functionalized with Cu nanoparticles showed remarkable photostability and photolytic activity.257 Graphene-based nanomaterials produced from biomass were also investigated in catalysis; as supports for noble free Fe/N/C catalysts289 with electron transfer numbers of 3.92 and the potential to replace the expensive Pt/C cathode; for the reaction of epoxidation of styrene,301 in the reaction of O2 reduction in alkaline media286 and the decomposition of methane gas.288 Excellent properties of the produced graphene materials were also applicable for the detection of catechin from green tea leaves in an electrochemical cell with a detection limit of 0.67 M and cancer biomarker 8-hydroxy-2-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG) with a sensitivity of approximately 1 nM.291,302

Graphene produced from biomass as natural sources open the possibility for graphene production in a cost-effective large-scale manner. Graphene-based nanomaterials produced from natural sources have a large free surface area and frequently a good specific capacitance. These graphene-based nanomaterials show great potential applications in energy storage, and sorbents, while other potential applications were not yet been fully explored. Considering that graphene surface often shows antibacterial activity, the produced graphene-based nanomaterials showed potential for this application as well.303305 The best quality graphene was produced by the pyrolysis of biomass in the atmosphere of H2 and Ar, otherwise, the produced graphene-based nanomaterials from natural sources were usually highly disordered. Considering issues with graphene transfer, the security issues and the high price of the safety equipment needed for additional detection systems or work with H2, the graphene mass production through this approach may not be largely exploited in the future. Although the quality of the produced graphene-based nanomaterials was low, these materials were easily produced on a large scale with potential applications in energy storage or as a sorbent due to its high specific surface area, conductivity, and a large number of active sites.

Summary and prospects

Based on the literature overview, among various methods for top-down productions of graphene nanomaterial, electrochemical exfoliation was considered one of the most efficient pathways. The increasing number of published papers on the electrochemical exfoliation of graphene in the past 5 years reflects its prominence and high demand for countless applications ranging from energy storage, sensors, catalysts, conducting films, anticorrosion and flame protection, hybrid membranes, to new composite materials. However, the inability of SLG production with controllable oxidation level yet remains a challenge and motivation for the research community, focusing on the wider range of applications. On the other hand, the RTA method of graphene synthesis as a bottom-up method represents a low-cost approach with diversity in the possible usage of substrates and precursors for graphene growth. The RTA method offers the ability of easy control of the reaction conditions including applied temperature, reaction time, type of substrate or carbon precursor type and concentration. However, the RTA graphene thin-film production showed limitations in producing good quality SLG on a large scale. Nevertheless, from the literature overview, the impression arises that the mentioned method was not explored to its full potential aside from the many advantages and challenges that it offers. Finally, research progress in graphene-based nanomaterial production from biomass provides the opportunity of exploring natural sources for eco-friendly transformation of bio-waste to graphene with emphasis on sustainability and reusability of organic matter in nature. So far, the explored natural sources and reaction conditions have resulted in the formation of highly disordered graphene-based nanomaterials with a large free surface area and good specific capacitance. These methods offer the possibility of converting low-price agricultural side products such as husk into high-quality devices like high-capacity Li-battery anodes. Most of these methods are economical, simple and appropriate for the transfer to large-scale production. Chemicals involved in the synthesis are inexpensive, non-reactive and low toxicity, such as KOH or Fe2O3. The only technological demand for graphene production is the use of the furnace at temperatures from 600 to 1000 C or higher, under an inert atmosphere. A few methods involved the use of H2 gas in the synthesis process. After the synthesis, the cleaning phase usually involved the use of diluted acids and water, while the number of produced graphene-based nanomaterials in one step was limited by the size of the furnace. Although graphene produced from biomass is a highly defective structure, it is appropriate for devices such as batteries or sorbents. Additionally, further development of the production approach will possibly lead to the improvement of graphene structure and expand its applications. Due to the risk and price of the safety equipment needed, such as additional detection systems to work with H2, it provides the research community an opportunity to further explore the possibilities for mass production. Even though graphene obtained by elaborate methods exhibits different properties, it has a broad spectrum of applications ranging from energy storage, sensors, catalysts, flexible electronics, anticorrosion and flame protection, hybrid membranes, sorbents for water and air purification, to new composite materials. The differences in properties of graphene prepared by different methods are mainly caused by the quality of the obtained material in terms of the number of layers, dimensions of sheets, and the presence of defects. These properties are strongly conditioned by the selected method for graphene production, synthesis conditions and the selected precursor type as the most important parameters among others. While the RTA method shows better controllability and allows the preparation of graphene with the desired number of layers, electrochemical exfoliation and graphene production from biomass mostly result in a few-layer highly defective graphene on a large scale.
