One dimensional nanostructure/nanoparticle composites as photoanodes for dye-sensitized solar cells

Abstract

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs) show potential as a low cost alternative to silicon solar cells. Power conversion efficiencies exceeding 12% have been achieved for DSCs. Typical DSCs are based on TiO2 nanoparticle photoanodes, which have numerous grain boundaries, surface defects and trap states as electrons transport from one particle to the other. Such defects and trap states increase back charge transfer (charge recombination) from the photoanode to electrolyte. One dimensional (1D) nanostructures such as nanofibers, nanorods, nanowires, and nanotubes can offer direct and fast electron transport to the electron collecting electrode. However, these 1D nanostructures have a major disadvantage of having insufficient surface area and inefficient dye attachment. To solve this challenge, mixtures of TiO2 nanoparticles and 1D nanostructures (e.g. nanofibers, nanorods, nanowires, and nanotubes) are used to take advantage of the large surface area of nanoparticles and efficient charge transport of 1D nanostructures. In this article, we review the recent developments in using mixtures of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles as photoanodes for efficient DSCs. Various randomly oriented and vertically aligned 1D nanostructures and their composites with nanoparticles are discussed. Future increase of efficiency in DSCs using 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composites will rely on the optimization of diameters of 1D nanostructures, control of ratios of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles, increase of crystallinity, and reduction of surface defects on the 1D nanostructures. This work will provide guidance for designing and growing appropriate 1D nanostructures, and combining them with nanoparticles at an optimal ratio for efficient DSCs.

1. Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs) show great potential as a cost effective alternative to Si solar cells due to their low material and fabrication cost.116 A normal DSC is composed of a photoanode and a counter electrode. A liquid electrolyte is filled into the space between the two electrodes.12,17 The photoanode is sensitized by a Ru-dye, porphyrin, organic dye, or inorganic quantum dot, which serves as a light harvester. After light illumination, the sensitizer injects electrons into the photoanode. The sensitizer will be reduced by taking electrons from the electrolyte. Recently the efficiency of DSCs has been increased to 12.3% under simulated AM 1.5 global sunlight.18 This new world record efficiency was achieved using a donor-bridgeacceptor zinc porphyrin (YD2-o-C8) to replace the commonly used Ru-based dyes as sensitizer and using the Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox to replace the conventional iodide/triiodide redox as electrolyte. The new YD2-o-C8 porphyrin can absorb broader spectrum light across the visible solar spectrum, leading to a larger photocurrent generation than typical Ru-dyes, while the Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox combined with YD2-o-C8 porphyrin significantly reduces the interfacial back electron transfer rate from TiO2 to the oxidized cobalt mediator, which leads to a strikingly high open circuit voltage (Voc) close to 1 V.

Although nanoparticle photoanodes exhibit high surface area for sufficient dye loading, structural disorders and trap defects at the contacts between nanoparticles lead to scattering of free electrons with reduced carrier mobility.1922 Enhanced electron transport was reported in various one dimensional (1D) nanostructures.2329 Specific 1D nanostructures, such as TiO2 nanofibers,30,31 TiO2 nanorods32 and nanowires,3335 TiO2 nanotubes,24,26,3639 1D hierarchical titanate,40 ZnO nanofibers,41 ZnO nanowires/nanorods,4249 ZnO nanotubes,24,50,51 and carbon nanofibers coated with TiO2,52 have been extensively used as an alternative to TiO2 nanoparticles to increase electron transport in the photoanode. Some of the 1D nanostructures are doped. For example, TiO2 nanofibers prepared by electrospinning were doped by silver nanoparticles and then used as a photoanode for DSCs;53 ZnO nanofibers prepared by electrospinning and ZnO nanorod arrays synthesized by a simple hydrothermal method were Al-doped for applications in DSCs.41,46 Titanium-decorated ZnO nanowires were also used as a photoanode in DSCs.54 However, the photoanode made of 1D nanostructure alone has constraints such as insufficient surface area and inefficient attachment of dye molecules. Another challenge is poor harvesting of solar light with wavelength longer than 600 nm due to the transmission of incident light through the nano-porous TiO2 photoanode.5 Also some 1D nanostructures including nanofibers or nanorods were used as a separate light scattering layer that was deposited on the TiO2 nanoparticle electrode to increase light absorption. To solve some of the above challenges, mixtures of TiO2 nanoparticles and 1D nanostructures (e.g. nanowires,55,56 nanofibers,22,57,58 nanorods,8,59 and nanofibers60) have been used to take advantage of both the large surface area of nanoparticles and efficient charge transport of 1D nanostructures. The incorporation of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticle films has an impact on the surface area, light absorption (light scattering), charge transport, and porosity.56 An additional advantage of using 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composites is improved light harvesting caused by light scattering. In addition to the widely used TiO2 and ZnO photoanodes, other metal oxides such as Nb2O5,61,62 Nb5+ doped TiO2,63 SnO2,64,65 SrTiO3,66,67 and Zn2SnO468,69 were also used as photoanodes in DSCs.

In this article, we review the recent developments in using mixtures of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles as photoanodes for efficient DSCs. Various randomly oriented and vertically aligned 1D nanostructures and their composites with nanoparticles are discussed. First, we discuss light harvesting improvement via scattering caused by the incorporation of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticles. Second, we describe the improvement in charge transport caused by the embedment of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticles. Third, we discuss the enlarged surface area by the incorporation of TiO2 nanoparticles into vertically aligned 1D nanostructures.

2. Incorporation of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticles for improved light harvesting via light scattering

Rayleigh scattering and Mie scattering are two main types of elastic light scattering that involves negligible energy transfer.

Raleigh scattering occurs when the particle size is <10% of the wavelength of incident light. This scattering depends strongly on the particle size and incident light wavelength, in which the scattered light intensity increases as the ratio of particle size to incident light wavelength gets larger. Another characteristic of Raleigh scattering is that it has identical scattered radiation in the forward and reverse directions. When the particle size is larger than 10% of the incident light wavelength, the Mie scattering model is used to describe the intensity of scattered radiation. Mie scattering is roughly independent of wavelength and it is larger in the forward direction than in the reverse direction. The greater the particle size, the higher the intensity of scattered light in the forward direction. Both large spherical particles and 1D nanostructures can be used to make scattering layers on top of the conventional TiO2 nanoparticle electrodes. Ito et al. used TiO2 powder with an average size of 160 nm as a second layer that functioned as a light scattering layer.70 Koo et al. reported that large TiO2 spherical particles with diameters in the range of 13 m used as a scattering layer can increase light harvesting for higher performance in DSCs.71 They also studied the particle size effects in the light scattering layer on DSC performance.72

1D nanostructures have also been used as a separate light scattering layer on top of a nanoparticle electrode to improve light harvesting.7376 Chuangchote et al. used electrospun TiO2 nanofibers with an average diameter of 250 nm as a separate light scattering layer that was directly deposited onto a thick nanoparticle electrode by electrospinning and solgel techniques.73 The formed anatase TiO2 nanofibers showed high crystallinity and an average diameter of 250 nm. DSC devices using N719 dye and the bilayer nanoparticle/nanofiber electrode achieved an incident photon to current conversion efficiency (IPCE) of 85% at the wavelength of 540 nm and a power conversion efficiency of 10.3% for a cell area of 0.052 cm2 under 1.5 AM illumination with a light intensity of 100 mW cm2. Zhu et al. compared DSC performance using rice grain-like TiO2 and nanofiber-shaped TiO2 nanostructures as an effective scattering layer on the nanoparticle electrode.74 The rice grain-like nanostructured scattering layer exhibited a 15.7% increase in efficiency, which is higher than that of the nanofiber scattering layer that showed 9.63% improvement. Yang and Leung reported a triple layer photoanode consisting of a first layer of TiO2 nanoparticles, a second layer of smaller-diameter electrospun nanofibers, and a third layer of bigger-diameter electrospun nanofibers.75 The smaller-diameter nanofiber layer that has a large surface area is used to attach dye to absorb light and transport electrons, while the bigger-diameter nanofiber layer can scatter and trap light to further increase light harvesting.

In addition to the above-discussed separated scattering layer, 1D nanostructures can also be incorporated into TiO2 nanoparticle films to increase light absorption due to light scattering. An additional advantage of blending 1D nanostructures with TiO2 nanoparticles also includes improved charge transport.56 Tan and Wu used a TiO2 nanowire/nanoparticle composite to fabricate dye sensitized solar cells with improved light harvesting due to light scattering.56 They also found that light harvesting enhancement depended on the concentration of the nanowires in the composite.

1D nanostructures can be randomly embedded and distributed in a nanocrystalline TiO2 nanoparticle matrix with respect to the direction of incident light. The composite of 1D nanostructures and nanocrystalline TiO2 nanoparticles can attach dye molecules and are filled with electrolyte as well. Depending on the size/diameters, 1D nanostructures can also effectively scatter the incident light, resulting in enhancement of light harvesting. 2.1 TiO2 nanofibers

Joshi et al. incorporated TiO2 nanofibers into a TiO2 nanoparticle film to make a composite photoanode to improve both light harvesting by scattering and charge transport with 1D nanostructures.57 This section will only discuss the improved light harvesting by scattering while the enhanced electron transport will be discussed in the next section. The above results provided a guide in optimizing TiO2 nanofiber diameters in the nanofiber/nanoparticle composites for higher DSC efficiency. Fig. 3 shows that the transmission spectrum in 300800 nm for a composite containing 15% nanofibers was lower than the TiO2 nanoparticle-only photoanode, suggesting an improvement in light scattering. The thickness is 7.5 m for both the composite and the pure nanoparticle photoanode. The low transmission in the composite in Fig. 3 suggested that 7.5 m is effective to absorb not only short visible (400600 nm) light, but also long wavelength (600800 nm) light. Light absorption becomes smaller as the wavelength (e.g. infrared regions) becomes longer in typical Ru-based dyes, leading to a larger light penetration depth ().10 To harvest sufficient light in long wavelength regions, the light penetration depth in these regions should be used as the photoanode thickness. The small percentage (<2%) of light transmission in the 600800 nm region suggested that 7.5 m was an effective thickness for efficient light harvesting.

2.2 ZrO2 nanofibers

Park et al. prepared a composite photoanode composed of TiO2 nanoparticles and ZrO2 nanofibers by blending TiO2 nanoparticles (Degussa, P-90) with polyethylene glycol, acetyl acetone, water, Triton X-100, HNO3, and ZrO2 nanofibers in which the concentration of ZrO2 ranged from 0, 3, 5, 7, to 9 wt%.60Fig. 4 represents the UV-vis-NIR absorption spectra of the composite photoanode made of 7% nanofibers. It shows that the absorption spectra were broadened to near infrared (NIR) regions with a higher absorption intensity. This indicated that photon absorption can be improved by adding ZrO2 nanofibers. According to an earlier report by Jing et al.,77 ZrO2 nanofibers with a diameter of 200 nm were synthesized by a solgel process, calcinations of the zirconium oxide/poly(vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP) composite nanofibers and the electrospinning technique. As discussed above, such diameters can induce Mie scattering and thus improve light harvesting.

2.3 ZnO nanowires

Tan and Wu studied the optical properties of the ZnO nanowire/TiO2 nanoparticle composite films with different concentrations of ZnO nanowires.56 At the same film thickness of 10 m, the films became more opaque as higher concentrations of ZnO nanowires were added into the composite film. The pure TiO2 nanoparticle film (0% ZnO nanowires) was almost transparent. When 5% of ZnO nanowires were added, the composite film became opaque and partially transparent. When 20% ZnO nanowires were embedded, the film became very opaque and the background on the other side of the film could not be seen. As the ZnO nanowire concentration increased to 77%, the film became completely white. As shown in Fig. 5, the diameters of the bundles of ZnO nanowires are up to 200 nm or even higher; this can cause strong light scattering as discussed previously. Therefore, with more ZnO nanowires added, the films exhibited stronger light scattering due to the incorporation of ZnO nanowires, which caused the film to become more and more opaque.

It is important to keep a balance between 1D nanostructure (nanorods, nanowires, or nanotubes) diameter and dye uptake in the composite. Typically, a larger diameter reduces the surface area and decreases the dye uptake. The surface area of nanofibers with diameters at hundreds of nanometres is about an order of magnitude smaller than that of nanoparticles with sizes at tens of nanometres.57

Similarly, Tan and Wu studied the reduction of dye attachment in the composite of single crystalline TiO2 nanowires and TiO2 nanoparticles.56 The same trend was observed when increasing the concentration of nanowires in the composite. Compared to the TiO2 nanoparticle alone, the 20 wt% TiO2 nanowire composite only had a 14% lower dye loading; however, dye loading was significantly reduced by 67% when the concentration increased to 77%. Fig. 5 shows cross-sectional SEM images of the nanoparticle only and of the composite film with nanowire concentrations of 5%, 20% and 77% by weight, respectively. It was shown that, at a nanowire concentration of 20 wt% or less, the individual nanowires or their bundles were dispersed in the nanoparticle matrix without perturbing the TiO2 nanocrystal packing. However, when the concentration increased to 77 wt%, the nanowires and their bundles constituted the majority of the composite with little nanoparticle packing present. The large unfilled space shown in Fig. 5d could lead to a substantial reduction of surface area in the composite, thereby causing a much less amount of dye loading for the 77 wt% nanowire composite.

3. Incorporation of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticles for improved charge transport

Electron transport in 1D nanostructures (e.g. nanofibers, nanowires, nanorods and nanotubes) has been studied by various groups.26,59,7880 Most results showed that such 1D nanostructures exhibited high electron diffusion coefficients and enhanced electron transport.2328,81

3.1 TiO2 nanofibers/nanorods

Joshi et al. blended electrospun TiO2 nanofibers with conventional TiO2 nanoparticles as an innovative photoanode and substantially improved light harvesting without substantially sacrificing dye attachment (uptake).57 In a later study on the same composite, Wang et al. reported transient photocurrent and photovoltage studies on charge transport in the composite of electrospun TiO2 nanofibers and conventional TiO2 nanoparticles for DSCs.58 They found that charge transport parameters such as charge recombination lifetime, electron transport lifetime, and electron diffusion length were improved by adding TiO2 nanofibers into composite photoanodes. Fig. 6a shows the photovoltage decay (V) and integrated photocurrent decay (Q) for DSC devices using a nanofiber/nanoparticle composite photoanode containing 15 wt% nanofibers under 8 mW cm2 bias light. By comparing Fig. 6b and c, it can be seen that the recombination lifetime (e) was much longer than the transport time (trans). Therefore the photocurrent decay represented the electron transport and collection time. Both e and trans depend on the illumination light intensity. As shown in Fig. 6b, e increases as the light intensity decreases in DSCs with different TiO2 nanofiber concentrations of 0 wt%, 15 wt%, 50 wt%, and 100 wt%. Also Fig. 6b shows that e of the nanofiber/nanoparticle composite photoanodes becomes closer to that of the nanofiber-only photoanode at high light intensity, but it turns out to be closer to that of the nanoparticle-only photoanode at low light intensity. This indicated that the recombination mechanism may be different at different light intensity after adding nanofibers into nanoparticles. Fig. 6b also shows that e became longer with larger amount of nanofibers in the composite, suggesting that back charge transfer from TiO2 to the I3 ions was reduced with more nanofibers. This might be caused by fewer electron trapping states and/or less confinement of I3 on nanofibers.58Fig. 6c shows that trans becomes lower as more nanofibers were added into the composite, indicating that the photoanodes containing more nanofibers had higher charge transport efficiency.

Electron transport efficiency of TiO2 nanofibers may be further improved by doping with Ag nanoparticles. Li et al. used Ag nanoparticle doped TiO2 nanofibers prepared by electrospinning as photoanodes for DSCs.53 They found that the Ag doped TiO2 nanofiber photoanode exhibited an improved electron collection efficiency as a result of faster electron transport than the undoped samples. In addition, post-treatment with TiCl4 was also conducted on the TiO2 nanofiber/nanoparticle composite to further improve electron transport. Kim et al. reported that the electron diffusion coefficient of the post-treated electrospun nanorods was 51% higher than that of the untreated ones, leading to 19% improvement of charge collection efficiency.78 Archana et al. found that the electron diffusion coefficient in the TiO2 nanowires, which were formed by sonication from electrospun nanofibers, was orders of magnitude larger than that of TiO2 nanoparticles.79 Saji and Pyo recently reported that the incorporation of 10% TiO2 nanorods in a nanoparticle matrix could lead to reduced charge transport resistance.59

3.3 ZnO nanorods/nanowires

Electron transport between pure TiO2 nanoparticle (NP) photoanode, pure single-crystalline ZnO nanowire (NW) array photoanode, and single-crystalline ZnO-NW array/NP composite photoanode was compared by Ku and Wu using impedance spectroscopy.82 Impedance spectroscopy is an effective tool to study electron transport and recombination at the photoanode (TiO2, ZnO, etc.).23,83 In the ZnO nanorod DSCs at forward bias, electrons are injected from FTO into ZnO nanorods, and then the ZnO nanorods are charged by electrons. Simultaneously, a portion of the injected electrons are lost by charge transfer from ZnO nanorods to I3 in the electrolyte. These processes can be illustrated by the transmission line model shown in Fig. 8a, which has been developed by Bisquert and others previously.23,81,83,84 If L is the ZnO nanorod length, the overall electron transport resistance, interfacial charge transfer resistance, and chemical capacitance in the ZnO nanorod/TiO2 nanoparticle film can be described as Rt (= rwL), Rct (= rk/L), and C (= cL), respectively.44Fig. 8b shows the Nyquist plots of DSC impedance spectra from mercurochrome-sensitized pure single crystalline ZnO NW (I), mercurochrome-sensitized ZnO NW array/NP composite (II), and N719-sensitized pure TiO2 NPs (III) based photoanodes. The reason is that TiO2 NPs used either partially mixed or fully as pure NPs have surface defects and trap states that cause electron scattering as electrons transport across these NPs. Multiple trapping/detrapping events still exist at the grain boundaries of the imbedded TiO2 nanoparticles during electron transport in the ZnO NW array/NP composite photoanode. Al-doping of ZnO nanorods/nanowires may help to increase electron transport along themselves. Yun et al. reported that A13+ ion doped into the ZnO lattice at the divalent Zn2+ site can increase electrical conductivity and support electrons to transport more efficiently in the Al-doped ZnO conduction band.46

In addition, electrolyte composition was found to affect electron transport and recombination on ZnO nanorod based DSCs. Electrolyte effects on electron transport and recombination at ZnO nanorods were studied using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy by Xie et al.44 Various electrolyte compositions were prepared by gradual addition of tert-butylpyridine (TBP) and guanidinium thiocyanate (GuSCN), and replacement of LiI by butyl, methyl imidazolium iodide (BMII). The incorporation of TBP and GuSCN, and the use of BMII to replace LiI retarded charge recombination at the ZnO nanorods/electrolyte interface, elongated electron lifetime (e), increased electron transport, and decreased collection time (d) on ZnO nanorods. The electron diffusion coefficient (Dn) and effective diffusion length (Ln) were also improved. The DSC devices using different electrolytes were fabricated and tested; the improvement of open circuit voltage (Voc), short circuit current density (Jsc), fill factor (FF) and IPCE was in good agreement with the findings from the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy results.44

However there is some debate concerning the use of randomly oriented 1D nanostructures such as nanowires, nanofibers, or nanorods to replace TiO2 nanoparticles to improve charge transport. Wu et al. reported that the effective electron diffusion coefficient in DSCs based on the nanowire-only photoanode and nanoparticle/nanowire composite photoanode with the nanowires randomly (non-vertically) oriented in both cases was smaller than that in the TiO2 nanoparticle-only DSCs.86 Mukherjee et al. studied electron transport in TiO2 nanofibers made by electrospinning and they found that electron diffusion lengths of TiO2 nanofibers were comparable to those of TiO2 nanoparticles but with a lower electron lifetime, which was probably caused by a large number of surface defects.87 Tan and Wu also reported that the nanowire/nanoparticle composite based photoanode with a small proportion of nanowire (< 20 wt%) did not contribute to an enhanced electron transport because there were no nanowire networks formed.56

4. Incorporation of TiO2 nanoparticles into vertically aligned 1D nanostructures for larger surface area

When vertically aligned 1D nanostructures are used to replace TiO2 nanoparticles, their surface area is not large enough to attach sufficient dyes. Therefore, nanoparticles are filled into 1D nanostructures to increase surface area and dye attachement.80,82,9597

4.1 Nanoparticles infiltration into ZnO nanowires or nanorods

Single-crystalline ZnO NW/ZnO NP composite electrodes were prepared by a simple wet-chemical method for applications in DSCs.82,98 Aligned ZnO NW arrays were synthesized on transparent conducting oxide (TCO) by aqueous chemical bath deposition (CBD). Then ZnO/Zn5(OH)8(CH3COO)22H2O [layered basic zinc acetate (LBZA)] nanoparticles were grown in the space between the ZnO NWs. Using the ZnO NW array/NP composite for DSCs, it was found that Jsc was increased due to a considerable enlarged surface area caused by the incorporation of ZnO NPs without sacrificing efficient electron transport of single-crystalline ZnO NWs. The electron density (n) in the ZnO NW/NP composite is larger than that in the ZnO NW array alone, which might be attributed to the larger surface area with more dye attachment and higher light harvesting.82 As discussed earlier, Deff in the ZnO NW/NP composite is lower than that in the ZnO NW array alone photoanode. However, the overall DSC efficiency using a ZnO NW/NP composite photoanode is much higher than the ZnO NW only devices. Therefore, such higher overall DSC performance is mainly caused by the larger Jsc and should be assigned to the larger area from the TiO2 NPs. It was also found that the NP occupying extent within the composite affects the DSC performance.98 An optimal ZnO NP growth period (i.e. NP occupying extent) needs to be found. Ku and Wu reported that at low NP occupying extent, for example in their reported case within an NP growth period shorter than 16 h, the surface area for dye attachment was increased substantially by forming more LBZA/ZnO NPs within the interstitial space in the ZnO NW arrays, leading to an increase in Jsc. However above 16 h, the ratio of the LBZA NPs was increased gradually in the composite and led to really large NPs as well as severe cracks that extended to the FTO substrate.98

ZnO NW/TiO2 NP composite photoanodes can also be prepared by the ultrasonic irradiation assisted dip-coating method reported by Gan et al.99 The ultrasonic irradiation was demonstrated as an effective method to enhance the infiltration of TiO2 NPs in the interstitial space between ZnO NWs. The ultrasonic irradiation assisted ZnO NW/TiO2 NP composite electrodes showed a deeper filling, leading to more dye attachment, stronger light absorption and higher overall conversion efficiency. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy measurements also suggested that the composite photoanode could couple improved electron transport along ZnO NWs and enlarged surface area by filled TiO2 NPs, which were responsible for overall cell performance improvement. It was also reported that Al-doping in ZnO nanorods can change the morphology and dye loading amount. Yun et al. reported that Al-doped ZnO nanorod array based DSCs exhibited a much higher efficiency than undoped devices.46 The authors attributed the performance improvement upon Al-doping to two factors: (1) Al-doped ZnO nanorod arrays have a large surface area for sufficient dye attachment and (2) A13+ ions doped into the ZnO lattice at the divalent Zn2+ site can increase electrical conductivity and support electrons to transport easily in the Al-doped ZnO conduction band. Therefore, Al-doped ZnO nanorods may offer additional advantages when mixed with TiO2 nanoparticles to make composite photoanodes.

As shown in Fig. 12, TiO2 nanoparticles were infiltrated into TiO2 nanotubes to combine the nanoparticles' large surface area for sufficient dye loading with nanotubes' high electron transport to increase the efficiency of DSCs.80,95 Alivov et al. used TiO2 nanotubes with three different diameters: 40 nm, 80 nm, and 160 nm. Different diameters led to different densities of nanotubes in the array, which provided different ratios of nanoparticles and nanotubes in the composite. The DSC efficiency was significantly increased at 17.4%, 55.6% and 131% for the devices with nanotube diameter at 40 nm, 80 nm, and 160 nm, respectively. The authors also used impedance spectroscopy to investigate the electron lifetime (e), first-order reaction rate constant for electrons (eff), electron diffusion length (Ln), and effective diffusion coefficient (Deff). 5. Device configuration

Traditional DSCs are based on the TiO2 nanoparticle-only photoanode. However, to achieve highly efficient DSCs, several technological challenges need to be overcome. One is the slow electron transport rate due to trapping defects and structural disorders at the surface contacts among adjacent nanoparticles that can lead to scattering of free electrons and reduction of carrier mobility.3 To improve electron mobility and electron transport rate, 1D nanostructures including TiO2 nanowires, nanorods, and nanotubes have been used to replace TiO2 nanoparticles.3,4 However, the surface area of 1D nanostructures appeared to be much smaller than the TiO2 nanoparticle only devices. This led to insufficient dye attachment. Then mixtures of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles were used to take advantage of both high electron transport (from 1D nanostructures) and large surface area to attach enough dyes (from nanoparticles). An additional advantage is that the incorporation of 1D nanostructures into nanoparticles can also increase light absorption by light scattering. Typical device structures based on mixtures of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles include random 1D nanostructures mixed with TiO2 nanoparticles57 and vertically aligned 1D nanostructures infiltrated with TiO2 nanoparticles.82,95Fig. 13a and b show representative device configuration of these two types of composites.

High performance DSCs depend on both high electron transport and large surface area. However, high electron transport requires more amount of 1D nanostructures in the composite, which then leads to less amount of nanoparticles in the composite. The inefficient amount of nanoparticles causes insufficient dye attachment and light absorption. Thus a balance between electron transport and light absorption, in other words, a balance must be achieved between the amount of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles. Various groups varied the ratios between 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles and achieved an optimal ratio for higher device efficiency.57,95

5.2 Vertically aligned 1D nanostructure infiltrated with nanoparticles

TiO2 or ZnO NPs were infiltrated into the interstitial space between the nanorods or both interstitial and inner space of nanotubes to increase the surface area for sufficient dye loading.80,82,9597 The example device structure is shown in Fig. 13b. Pan et al. formed TiO2 NPs with a size of 10 nm into the TiO2 nanotubes by a hydrothermal method in which they immersed TiO2 nanotubes into an aqueous TiCl4 solution in an enclosed autoclave having a Teflon vessel.80 Dye loading in the TiO2 NP filled nanotubes was found to increase by more than twice compared to that in the pure TiO2 nanotube based photoanode. Three different lengths of nanotubes including 8, 13, and 20 m were prepared and then infiltrated with TiO2 nanoparticles by the hydrothermal method. After filling with TiO2 nanoparticles, the DSC efficiencies were increased by 152%, 107% and 49% for 8, 13, and 20 m long TiO2 nanotubes, respectively. The largest increase in DSC efficiency found in the shortest nanotube (8 m) was attributed to the limited attachment of dye, which is the dominant limiting factor for low Jsc.80 As the nanotubes get longer, they themselves have larger surface area and can absorb more light; this is the reason that the efficiency improvement becomes less as the nanotube length increases. In addition, a ZnO nanowire arraynanoparticle (NWNP) composite was also used as the photoanode for DSCs by Yodyingyong et al.97 The NPs induced enriched surface area for more dye loading and the NWs caused higher charge transport, which worked together to increase the overall DSC performance. The overall DSC efficiency was 4.2% for the ZnO NWNP composite photoanode, which was much higher than 1.58% for ZnO NW only based DSCs and 1.31% for ZnO NP only based cells that are sensitized by N3 dye.

6. Summary and outlook

Currently, DSC devices with the highest efficiencies above 12% are still fabricated based on TiO2 nanoparticle only photoanodes.18 One obvious reason is that TiO2 nanoparticle photoanodes have a large surface area as well as exposed (1 0 1) planes that ideally chemisorb dyes.100 Although pure 1D nanostructures can probably increase electron transport, device performance improvement caused by increased electron transport cannot recover the reduction of final device efficiency due to their significant loss of surface area in the photoanode. To solve such a challenge, composites of nanoparticles and 1D nanostructures (e.g. nanowires, nanofibers, nanorods, and nanofibers) have been used to take advantage of the large surface area of nanoparticles and efficient charge transport of 1D nanostructures. Both randomly oriented and vertically aligned 1D nanostructures have been used to make a composite photoanode. In the randomly oriented 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composite, the 1D nanostructure may need to form a network or percolation pathway for electron transport.56 Vertically aligned 1D nanostructures filled with nanoparticles are typically considered as an ideal structure for a photoanode. However, it is quite a challenge to fully fill nanoparticles into the interstitial space of 1D nanostructures because the nanoparticle size may be large and/or aggregate into large particles. If particle aggregation can be prevented, reducing the nanoparticle size can help to fill more nanoparticles into the 1D nanostructure pores. However, the smaller nanoparticle sizes typically increase grain boundaries and defect sites that may reduce the charge transport between the nanoparticles before they arrive at 1D nanostructures.100 One promising approach that can be used to fully fill nanoparticles into 1D nanostructure pores is to in situ grow nanoparticles in the pores.80,82,98

It is still in debate whether a 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composite photoanode will reach a cell efficiency comparable to or higher than the world record efficiency from nanoparticle only devices. On the one hand, this is questionable because the world record efficiencies of DSCs, such as the recently reported 12.3% efficiency and previously reported 11%, were all achieved from the nanoparticles alone photoanode. However, on the other hand it is still very encouraging because it has been reported in many papers56,57,60 that 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composite photoanode based cells have exhibited higher efficiencies than nanoparticle only devices in the specific comparison between these two photoanodes. As discussed in the previous sections, 1D nanostructure/nanoparticle composite electrodes have been reported to have advantages including enhanced photon harvesting by light scattering and improved charge transport caused by continuous charge pathways. Further increase in DSC efficiency will need to consider the diameters of 1D nanostructures, ratios of 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles in the composite, crystallinity and surface defects in 1D nanostructures. The increase of diameters of 1D nanostructures (e.g., nanorods, nanofibers, nanowires) can enhance Mie scattering that can increase photon absorption; the decrease of ratios between 1D nanostructures and nanoparticles can minimize the reduction of surface area in the composite for dye loading; and the coating of 1D nanostructures with insulating or wide-bandgap semiconductors such as HfO2 and Al2O3 can passivate the surface defects.101,102

So low bandgap and wide absorption spectrum dyes, and efficient charge transport are needed to increase Jsc. Pushing the quasi-Fermi level of the photoanode materials closer to the dye LUMO but still having sufficient energy offset (e.g. 0.20.4 eV) for electron injection and moving the electrolyte redox potential closer to the dye HOMO but still having sufficient energy offset (e.g. 0.20.3 eV) for hole injection are intrinsically required to increase Voc.103 In addition, the back charge transfer from photoanode to electrolyte also needs to be suppressed to increase Voc and FF. Recently a new world record efficiency of 12.3% in DSCs under simulated AM 1.5 global sunlight has been achieved.18 This was accomplished by using a donor-bridgeacceptor zinc porphyrin to replace commonly used Ru-based dyes as sensitizer (YD2-o-C8) and using a Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox electrolyte to replace conventional iodide/triiodide redox. The new YD2-o-C8 porphyrin can absorb a broader spectrum of light across the visible region, leading to a larger photocurrent and Jsc generation than typical Ru-based dyes; while the Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox is more suitably matched with dye HOMO, resulting in a higher Voc. In addition, the combination of Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox and YD2-o-C8 porphyrin significantly suppressed the interfacial back electron transfer from TiO2 to the oxidized cobalt mediator, causing a further strikingly high Voc and FF. Based on these advantages of 1D nanostructurenanoparticle composites, we believe such a composite photoanode has a great potential to further increase its efficiencies by combining with broad absorption spectrum porphyrins and Co(II/III)tris(bipyridyl)-based redox electrolyte that have recently achieved a world record efficiency of 12.3% in nanoparticle only DSCs.18
