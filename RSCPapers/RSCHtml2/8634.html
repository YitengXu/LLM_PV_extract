Abstract

Through theoretical analysis, we study relevant properties of some molecular structures formed by oligothiophenes (T) and dioctylfluorenes (F) units, commonly employed in the fabrication of different kinds of optical and electronic devices. For so, we first consider Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules with different numbers of thiophene rings (n). Among other characteristics, we calculate the dipole moment change between the ground and excited state (Î”Î¼ge), a quantity that greatly influences the exciton dissociation and charge carrier mobility. We show that the planarity of the ground state geometry correlates Î”Î¼ge to the exciton binding energy (Eb), with higher Î”Î¼ge's corresponding to lower Eb's when n > 3. We also unveil a relevant dependence of Î”Î¼ge with the oddâ€“even parity of n and that Î”Î¼ge assumes higher values when the molecule is composed by bithiophene (instead of simple thiophenes) moieties in the syn-conformation (with the two heteroatoms pointing in the same direction). From molecules results, we then address larger systems, formed by different oligomers of Fâ€“T copolymers containing blocks of dioctylfluorenes and bithiophenes (T2). We systematic investigate their electronic and structural properties as function of the composition ratio between the T2 and F moieties. Similar to the molecules, we deduce that the magnitude of Î”Î¼ge is higher for the syn conformer of the T2 unit. Moreover, the highest values of Î”Î¼ge are achieved when the number of the T2 increases relative to a fixed number of the F units in the mer. Such behaviors are in agreement and actually can qualitative explain measurements in the literature on the quantum efficiency of charge carrier generation in Fâ€“T copolymers. The present findings can be helpful in designing novel materials with improved photoelectric responses.

1. Introduction

Conjugated polymers are essential components in a great variety of optoelectronic applications1â€“3 including organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), organic field effect transistors (OFETs) and organic photovoltaic devices (OPVs). A promising general strategy to enhance the performance of such broad class of devices is to use, as the active material, conjugated polymers formed by different sequences of donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) moieties along the chain, leading to the so-called copolymers. They represent a great advantage since the copolymer sequence (in the form of alternating, gradient and random blocks4â€“6) considerably impacts the electrical, optical, thermal and morphologic properties of the full system. For instance, variations in the copolymer sequence enables the fabrication of (i) color tunable emitting layers in OLEDs and of (ii) OPVs displaying band gap gradients which better matches the solar spectrum. But despite all the progress in the last decades, making use of copolymers in optoelectronic devices,7 in many contexts6 it is still not clear how rearrangements and manipulations of the structural sequences of chemical moieties along the chain could qualitatively improve a specific optoelectronic property of the resulting copolymer.

Archetypal copolymers are those formed by blocks containing different numbers of fluorene and bithiophene units, namely, the fluoreneâ€“thiophene (Fâ€“T) copolymers. Their great diversity of combinations bears an enormous potential for applications, being the subject of intense research.4,8,9 For instance, they have color tuning properties (thus relevant for (i) above) as a function of the Ï€-conjugation length. Also, Fâ€“T copolymers in diblock structures can be employed as active layers for chemical and biological sensors.10,11 The most studied Fâ€“T copolymer is the poly[(9,9-dioctylfluorenyl-2,7-diyl)-co-bithiophene], commonly known as F8T2. Its structure alternates segments of dioctylfluorene and bithiophene, producing a material which can serve either as donor layers in OPVs12,13 or as hole transport layers in OFETs14,15 and OLEDs.16,17 Although the optoelectronic properties of the F8T2 are well known, how these properties modify for variations in the relative number of fluorene and bithiophene units is still an open question.

In a recent work8 the intramolecular hole transport in Fâ€“T copolymers synthesized with different number of oligothiophene units have been investigated. It has been found that an even number of oligothiophene units (2 and 4) increases the hole's intramolecular conductivity when compared to chains with an odd number (1 and 3). The phenomenon has been related to a higher planarity of the chains with an even number of oligothiophenes units. Another study18 has reported that the field effect mobility in naphthalenediimideâ€“thiophene copolymers is also higher when the polymer has an even number of oligothiophenes (2 and 4). This also has been attributed to a higher degree of planarity of those chains.

In the same line, oddâ€“even distinctions appear in molecules that combine electron-deficient end groups (acceptor) and an electron rich central group (donor) to form an acceptorâ€“donorâ€“acceptor (Aâ€“Dâ€“A) structure.19,20 Using oligothiophene as the donor and 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)rhodanine as acceptor, it has been observed that molecules with an odd number of thiophenes (T) groups show higher power conversion efficiencies (PCE) in OPVs19 (reaching 10% for the molecule with 5 rings) compared to these with an even number of T moieties. This difference in the PCE is associated to a larger dipole moment change Î”Î¼ge = |Î¼e âˆ’ Î¼g| in the former case (for Î¼e (Î¼g) the dipole moment in the excited (ground) state). Indeed, a larger Î”Î¼ge is a signature of excited states with greater intramolecular charge separation. But this last feature tends to decrease the exciton Coulomb binding energy,21 hence leading to an improved quantum efficiency in carrier generation. The usage of copolymers or molecules with higher Î”Î¼ge's might constitute a relevant way to improve the performance of OPVs.22,23

As previously demonstrated in the literature,24,25 theoretical analysis is a valuable tool in exploring the main characteristics of thiophene based conjugated polymers and molecules. Thus, in trying to elucidate the effects described above, we perform quantum chemistry calculations of Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules formed by oligothiophenes and dioctylfluorenes. We address how the number of thiophene rings (n) influences the optoelectronic properties of the molecules. Among other characteristics, we calculate Î¼g, Î¼e and Î”Î¼ge for the Fâ€“Tâ€“F systems. We show that an enhancement of Î”Î¼ge is obtained when the bithiophene (instead of simple thiophenes) are in the syn-conformation (with the two heteroatoms pointing in the same direction). On the other hand, the anti-conformation of the bithiophene (with the two heteroatoms pointing in opposite directions) favors a decreasing of Î”Î¼ge. This is in agreement with a recent work,26 where it has been observed that the copolymer F8T2 with greater proportion of bithiophenes in the syn-conformation leads to solar cells with higher efficiency.

Using results for the molecules as benchmark, we consider different oligomers of fluoreneâ€“thiophene copolymers containing blocks of dioctylfluorenes (F) and bithiophenes (T2). We present a systematic investigation of the electronic and structural properties of the calculated copolymers as a function of the composition ratio between the F and T2 moieties. We find that the frontier orbitals are basically centered on the T2 units of the oligomers. On the other hand, the number of F moieties in the mer induces higher torsional angles that shorten the effective Ï€-conjugation length. Hence structures with lower F/T2 ratios tend to have lower energy gaps between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). Likewise the molecules, the Î”Î¼ge of the block structures are also higher for the syn conformer of the T2 unit. In this conformation, Î”Î¼ge increases when the number of T2 units increases for a fixed number of the F units in the mer. We mention that a careful analysis of the transient photoconductivity in Fâ€“T copolymer films using microwave conductivity (FP-TRMC) technique have show that the quantum efficiency of photocarrier generation (Ï†) tends to increase with increasing composition ratio of oligothiophene units.8 This is in agreement with the present findings since our calculations for the Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules suggests that higher values of Î”Î¼ge are related to excitons displaying a weaker bound between the electronâ€“hole pair. This effect would then improve Ï†.

As a final introductory comment, a series of conjugated copolymers derived from 9,9-dioctylfluorene (DOF) and 4,7-di-2-thienyl-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (DBT) have been synthesized with different feed ratios of DOF to DBT.27 Thus, with the continuous development of new routes to the fabrication of conjugated polymers of the type discussed here,28 our theoretical study might be an important guideline to the engineering of distinct Fâ€“T copolymers aimed to fabrication of efficient optoelectronic devices.

2. Model structures and computational methods

The model molecules and oligomers have been built following the same protocol described in ref. 26. We have carried out successive geometry optimizations (using the semi-empirical PM329 method) as new repetition units were added to the structures. To check for the accuracy of the method, we have compared the calculated total length of an unsubstituted oligothiophene (Î±4T) molecule with actual crystallographic experimental data taken from ref. 30 (see ESIâ€ ). The comparisons have demonstrated a good convergence of this procedure.

Regarding the oligomers simulations, first the sequence of dioctylfluorene units have been oriented in an alternate form, yielding the lower energy conformation (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ) also maximizing the interaction with a solvent. Second, the thiophene rings bounded to the dioctylfluorene have been oriented in way so to leave the sulfur atoms in the opposite side relative to the direction of the dioctyls chains linked to the fluorenes. This conformation stabilizes the resulting structure (Fig. S2, ESIâ€ ). And third, since the repetition units of the copolymers already contain many atoms (around 500 for the larger copolymers), for simplicity we have simulated the dimers of each copolymer.

For the copolymers optimized structures, we have performed single point simulations using time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT).31 We have tested distinct hybrid functionals and base functions. For each one of these sets, we have compared the obtained maximum absorption wavelength with experimental results of UV-vis measurements (Table S1, ESIâ€ ). We have found that in the best case, the difference between the calculated and experimental maximum absorption wavelength is around 10 nm. Then, among the methods leading to the above mentioned best agreement, we have chosen that with the lowest computational cost, which is just the B3LYP32/6-31G*. It is worth mentioning it is a very commonly employed method in the literature.33,34 Hence, with the B3LYP/6-31G* we have calculated the energy of the frontier orbitals, their spatial distribution and the electronic vertical transitions.

We also have implemented time-dependent PM3 (TD-PM3) calculations to optimize the geometry of the molecules and oligomers in the excited state. To ratify that the obtained structures indeed correspond to bonds lengths at the true energy minimum, we display the frequency analysis of Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules in the excited states. As it should be, we do not see any imaginary frequency (see ESIâ€ ). Then, we have used the resulting excited state geometry and performed a single-point TD-DFT calculation with B3LYP/6-31G* to find Î¼e. The change of the dipole moment between the excited and fundamental states is given by Î”Î¼ge = [(Î¼gx âˆ’ Î¼ex)2 + (Î¼gy âˆ’ Î¼ey)2 + (Î¼gz âˆ’ Î¼ez)2]1/2. We further have considered some distinct hybrid functionals and base functions, verifying that the same oddâ€“even effect is always present in all the analyzed cases (Fig. S3, ESIâ€ ). All simulations have been done with the Gaussian09 package.35

3. Results

3.1 Molecules

First, we study some key features of Aâ€“Dâ€“A molecules that will serve as guidelines to understand related properties in more complex structures (Section 3.2). Fig. 1a shows the ground state optimized geometry of dioctylfluoreneâ€“(1â€“8)thiopheneâ€“dioctylfluorene (Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F) molecules with a n number of thiophene (T) rings following an alternate orientation. In Fig. 1b we plot the respective values of |Î¼g| = Î¼g, |Î¼e| = Î¼e and Î”Î¼ge for each molecule. In the excited state the oligothiophenes are in their planar quinoidal backbone,36 characterized by a reduction of the bond lengths in the ring (Fig. S4 and S5, ESIâ€ ). The excited state quinoid structure increases the rigidity of the chain due to the limited rotation around the Câ€“C bonds between thiophene rings.20 There is an oddâ€“even effect for Î¼g and Î¼e when n varies from 1 to 8. But the oddâ€“even effect on Î”Î¼ge is only verified when the number of thiophenes varies from 3 to 8. This is similar to the behavior observed in Aâ€“Dâ€“A molecules composed by oligothiophenes with 2-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)rhodanine19 and is related to the system symmetry. Since the dipole moment of the thiophene ring is located on the ring's plane and oriented toward the sulfur atom, the dipole moments of adjacent T units oppositely oriented will tend to cancel out each other. Thus, the resulting dipole moment is smaller for an even number of T rings following an alternate sequence. Such effect is still more pronounced in the excited state (Fig. 1b) due to the higher planarity of the molecule which produces a nearly symmetric structure. In this case Î”Î¼ge is of the same order of Î¼g since Î¼e is close to zero.

On the opposite, there will always be an unpaired dipole moment when the chain is formed by an odd sequence of T rings. In this case Î¼g and Î¼e have approximately the same magnitude but different directions. This can be clearly seen in Fig. 1c displaying the variation of the angle between these vectors for an increasing number of T units in the molecule. Due to the higher torsional angles between the T rings in the ground state, Î¼g is not on the plane of the thiophene units. In contrast, the torsional angles are very small in the excited state so that the Î¼e is on the plane of the rings. The shift in the Î¼e direction relative to Î¼g gives higher values of Î”Î¼ge.

To further investigate the oddâ€“even effect produced by unpaired units of T rings, we repeat the above calculations for molecules formed by a sequence of alternate bithiophene (T2) units. We consider the dioctylfluoreneâ€“(1â€“4)bithiophene (syn)â€“dioctylfluorene (Fig. 2a) (Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F) molecule where a number n of T2 groups in the syn conformation are repeated along the chain with an alternate orientation. The optimized structure of these molecules are shown in Fig. 2a (the molecules with the same number of T rings but in the anti conformation are from 0.6 and 1.6 kcal molâˆ’1 more stable than the molecules with the syn conformation of Fig. 2a). The idea is to use such results as a benchmark to study the odd-effect in Fâ€“T block copolymers.

The previous oddâ€“even behavior for Î¼g, Î¼e and Î”Î¼ge in Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F is similarly observed in the Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F molecules (see Fig. 2b), however now with higher variations in such quantities values. Moreover, Fig. 2c indicates that increasing Î”Î¼ge are also linked to greater shifts in the directions of Î¼g and Î¼e. It has been observed in molecules19 and polymers22,23,37 that higher Î”Î¼ge's are correlated with higher power conversion efficiencies in organic solar cells. Then, Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F molecules formed by an odd number of T2 groups in the syn conformation would show better photovoltaic responses compared to the same kind of molecules with single thiophene units. These findings explain, e.g., the conclusions of a recent study26 using the F8T2 copolymer and the additive solvent DIO (1,8-diiodooctane): an enhanced solar cell power conversion efficiency is observed for systems with higher proportion of F8T2 in the syn-conformation compared to anti-conformation.

In order to check if the obtained results could be related to the lateral group of the dioctylfluorene, we repeat all the calculations replacing the octyl by methyl. The analysis shows that the oddâ€“even trend is actually related to the thiophenes (see ESIâ€ ). Although we are addressing Aâ€“Dâ€“A molecules with thiophene as donor and dioctylfluorene as acceptor, we believe that the present effect might take place for other molecules of different acceptor groups38 or derivatives of thiophene rings with substituent lateral chains.

As already mentioned, different works suggest a relationship between high values of Î”Î¼ge and a lower exciton binding energy (Eb). For instance, this connection has been proposed in a study19 involving Aâ€“Dâ€“A molecules containing oligothiophenes as the central donor unity. It has been verified a greater (lower) current density of photovoltaic devices with molecules containing 5 and 7 (4, 6 and 8) thiophenes. For our Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules, Eb can be estimated via DFT (but see also ref. 39) by subtracting the fundamental energy gap (Egap) from the optical gap (Eopt), as discussed in detail in ref. 40. The optical gap corresponds to the energy of the lowest electronic transition accessible via absorption of a single photon (obtained from TD-DFT calculations). The fundamental gap is defined as the energy difference between the ionization potential (IP) and electron affinity (EA). Actually, one must compare the molecules ground state (N â€“ electrons) with the anion (N + 1 electrons) and cation (N âˆ’ 1 electrons) states to determine, respectively, EA and IP. Then, IP = Etotal(cation) âˆ’ Etotal(neutral) and EA = Etotal(neutral) âˆ’ Etotal(anion).41 So, the cation and anion energies from this are derived in the ground state geometry of the neutral molecule, so that the vertical IP and EA are obtained. Here we should observe that recent42 simulations assuming different DFT functionals for a series of molecules have shown that a better agreement with experimental values of IP, EA and Eb is achieved when the DFT is implemented with long-range corrected hybrid GGA functionals, like the wB97XD.43

We therefore have followed the above prescription to calculate IP, EA and Eb for the Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules with n = 1â€“8. To reduce the computational time, we have replaced the octyl groups of the F moiety by smaller methyl groups. All calculations have been done using wB97XD/6-31+G** (for wB97XD/6-31G**, see ESI.â€  The use of diffuse functions for heavier atoms lead to a value for Eb differing in less than 1.7% of that for Eb calculated without those functions). We have not seen any particular trend in Eb for n = 1â€“3. On the other hand, for n = 4â€“8 (Fig. 4), Eb is smaller (higher) for an odd (even) number of thiophenes. Thus, is follows the variation of Î”Î¼ge, corroborating ref. 19 and others.22,23 Although we have only considered molecules in the gas phase, the oddâ€“even effect in Fig. 4 may also be present in the solid state. As mentioned in ref. 37, in this latter case eventual polarization of the surrounding molecules leads just to a generalized reduction in the gas phase value of Eb.

4. Discussion

In this section we shall briefly discuss our theoretical results in the light of some experimental data reported in the literature.47,48 In this respect, ref. 8 is specially useful since it involves a systematic experimental study of Fâ€“T copolymers synthesized with different number of T moieties. Such copolymers films have been measured8 through the FP-TRMC technique using a 355 nm laser as the excitation source. The results of special importance to us are those for the pFT4 polymers, which here correspond to the (1,2) oligomers. The maximum charge carrier generation efficiency at end-of-pulse (Ï†) â€“ estimated by DC techniques â€“ clearly tends to rise when the oligothiophene content is above 25% in the mentioned pFT4. It is important to note that such tendency is also followed by the other Fâ€“T structures in ref. 8. Our theoretical approach suggests an explanation to this behavior. It is clear from Fig. 9 that the magnitude of Î”Î¼ge increases with the number of T2 units when the amount of F groups in the mer is constant. The results for the Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules in Fig. 4 indicates that high dipole moment variations upon photoexcitation are linked to lower exciton binding energies (in Table S16, ESIâ€  we show the exciton binding energy for the (1,2)-syn and (1,3)-syn copolymers, such calculations confirm that Eb is always lower when Î”Î¼ge is higher). Generalizing this to the every copolymers, larger Î”Î¼ge's lead to easier charge separation, associated to a reduction in the electrostatic attraction between the in-chain electronâ€“hole pair due to a larger separation distance.21 This effect, therefore, would help the intramolecular exciton dissociation (and the consequent photogeneration upon laser illumination). In this way, one can boost Ï† with the addition of more T2 units in the mer. We mention it has also been pointed out that the recombination lifetime between an electron transferred to TiO2 and the hole at the thiophene units increases with n after the photoexcitation of fluorene-thiophene molecular wires.49

Another relevant point is the improvement of the higher power conversion efficiencies (PCE) in OPV devices, related (among other things) to achieving adequate charge separation.50 It has already been observed in some copolymers that there is a linear correlation between Î”Î¼ge and solar cell performance when other properties, such as charge carrier transport and film morphology, are comparable.23 The results for the Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules in Fig. 4 are in qualitative agreement with these observations. Our calculations indicate that large values of Î”Î¼ge are associated to a reduction of the exciton binding energy. Recalling that the fundamental step for an efficient charge carrier generation in OPVs is the photoinduced electron transfer from the donor to the acceptor molecule, the reduction of Eb facilitates the molecular ion generation. In turn, this reduces the energy cost for the electron transfer from the donor to the acceptor, auxiliating the dissociation process.23 Our findings thus shed some light on the intrinsic molecular mechanism underlying the interrelationship between Î”Î¼ge and the PCE.

Actually, the key result in our work is the dependence of Eb on Î”Î¼ge (evidenced in Fig. 4 and Table S5, ESIâ€ ), which can arise from the shift in the optical gap produced by the interaction between Î”Î¼ge51,52 and the dipole moment of the HOMO â†’ LUMO transition (Î¼ex). In the presence of an electric field Fex, the optical gap can undergo a Stark (St) shift,53 with Eopt = E0opt âˆ’ Î”Î¼geÂ·Fex, where E0opt is the optical gap for Î”Î¼ge = 0. Assuming that the direction and orientation of Î”Î¼ge is basically given by the dipole moment of the ground state, Table S7 (ESIâ€ ) shows the angle Î¸ between Î”Î¼ge and Î¼ex for the Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules. 5. Conclusions

In this work we have considered a two step approach to theoretically study (using quantum chemistry methods) molecular structures formed by oligothiophenes (T) and dioctylfluorenes (F). In a first step we have discussed Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F molecules with the number of thiophene rings (n) increasing from 1 to 8. For each molecule we have calculated the dipole moment in the ground state (Î¼g), the dipole moment in the excited state (Î¼e) and the variation of the dipole moment between ground and excited states (Î”Î¼ge). Î”Î¼ge is a key property that helps exciton dissociation and the consequent charge carrier generation in organic semiconductors. Our calculations indicate that there is an opposite trend for the dipole variation and the exciton biding energy (Eb), so that higher Î”Î¼ge corresponds to lower Eb when n > 3. We have found that Î”Î¼ge has higher (lower) magnitudes when n is odd (even). The enhancement of Î”Î¼ge for an odd number of thiophene units can be attributed to the lower symmetry of those molecules that necessarily have an unpaired dipole moment belonging to a single T ring.

Searching for instances further increasing Î”Î¼ge, we have considered molecules where the T units are replaced by bithiophene (T2) units. In these Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F structures, the number of T2 blocks (n) in the syn conformation increases from 1 to 4 (following a sequence of alternating orientations). The Î”Î¼ge of Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F molecules also shows an oddâ€“even effect with n (high values for n odd and lower values for n even), but with stronger magnitude variations compared to Fâ€“(T)nâ€“F systems.

Using these results as reference, we have discussed different oligomers of Fâ€“T copolymers containing blocks of dioctylfluorenes and bithiophenes. We have checked the influence of the composition ratio between the F and T2 moieties on the chain's planarity, HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap, main electronic transitions, and Î¼g, Î¼e and Î”Î¼ge values. We have observed that the planarity of the chain decreases when the mer are formed by blocks with rising number of F units. As a consequence, the effective conjugation length tends to shorten, so increasing the HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap of long chains. Finally, we have found that the oligomers with 3 syn-T2 units display higher dipole moments than the oligomers with 2 syn-T2 units. This is a signature of the oddâ€“even effect found in the Fâ€“(T2)nâ€“F molecules.

One important finding, with potential practical interest for the development of OPV devices, is that Î”Î¼ge tends to increase when the number of T2 units in the mer increases (for a fixed block of the F units). But a larger Î”Î¼ge seems to facilitate the intramolecular electronâ€“hole (exciton) separation. Hence, this suggests a higher charge carrier generation in copolymers with a greater content of T units relative to the F units in the mer. This result is in close agreement with measurements of the quantum efficiency of charge carrier generation in Fâ€“T copolymers.

Finally, we have demonstrated that subtle changes in the composition ratio of the copolymers mer can have huge impacts on the properties of the resulting system. Just to highlight a particular point, we find that the variation of one T2 ring group (keeping the same number of F units) in the mer can increase Î”Î¼ge by 120% (as it has been observed in the (3,2)-syn to (3,3)-syn oligomers). Such kind of information may be fundamental in designing new materials. For instance, it is well known that the addition of thiophene rings in the chain decreases the polymer solubility. Thus, our study can give some guidance on how to tune the ratio of F and T moieties in order to obtain optimal light harvesting and charge transport properties without severely compromising the use of standard fabrication techniques as solution-based deposition methods for the resulting materials.
