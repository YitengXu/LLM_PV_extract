Abstract

The CeNiO3 (CNO) supercapattery is an emerging energy-storage device. It brings together the high specific power of supercapacitors and the high specific energy of batteries. In general, the porous nature, composition and microstructure are the key factors that enhance the specific capacity and rate capability, and prolong the life cycle of an electrode material in an energy-storage device. In this study, porous CNO (P-CNO) is synthesized using a lemon-assisted solâ€“gel autocombustion method. Here we propose a lemon-assisted synthesis approach to obtain an enhanced electrochemical capacity along with a prolonged cycle life. The porous nature of the material improves the surface area and electrochemical performance by creating more electrochemical active sites. The P-CNO exhibits a specific capacity of 605 C gâˆ’1 and 191.5 C gâˆ’1 at a current density of 1 A gâˆ’1 in three-electrode and two-electrode systems, respectively. Then, to examine a real-life application, a device is fabricated with CNO as the anode and cathode. This symmetric cell configuration can deliver a high specific energy of 43.45 W h kgâˆ’1 at a specific power of 800 W kgâˆ’1. Furthermore, the device achieves a prolonged cycle life of over 50000 cycles whilst retaining 114.21% of its initial capacity and hence presenting a new material evolution for the energy-storage field.

Introduction

The current fast-paced lifestyle and the increase in the population have increased our fuel dependency tremendously. To meet this demand, it is fossil fuels that are mainly exploited, which results in the fast depletion of these fuel resources and climatic pollution.1â€“3 Hence, new energy-storage devices are being explored to meet the energy demands as and when required. This has prompted many researchers to develop better energy-storage devices over the last few decades, which has resulted in modern electrochemical energy-storage systems such as secondary batteries and supercapacitors.4,5 Commercial batteries can store much more energy than supercapacitors. However, supercapacitors can deliver a much higher power than secondary batteries. Hence, batteries are useful for applications that require a long-term energy supply;4,6,7 meanwhile, supercapacitors are useful for applications involving fast charge/discharge, where a large amount of energy is to be stored or delivered quickly.6 These include energy delivery in hybrid cars, power grids, regenerative breaking, and the opening of emergency doors in airplanes.

Supercapacitors usually exhibit two types of capacitance: electric double layer capacitance (EDLC), where charge storage takes place via a non-faradaic process; and pseudocapacitance, where charge storage takes place via a faradaic process.8â€“11 Carbonaceous materials such as activated charcoal and carbon nanotubes mostly exhibit EDLC behaviour, whereas pseudocapacitance dominates in materials such as polymers and metal oxides.2,3,5,12,13 Although advanced electrodes for supercapacitors have tremendously increased their power density, they cannot compete with the high energy density supplied by batteries. Hence, researchers focused on developing the characteristics of both the battery and the supercapacitor in one device, named supercapattery,1,2,4,14,15,17 which has greater energy capacity than a supercapacitor. The supercapattery device, whose performance is comparable to secondary batteries with a high power capability and long chargingâ€“discharging durability.4,16 The supercapattery usually uses a battery-type electrode and a supercapacitor-type electrode as the positive and negative electrodes, respectively.17 This is not a necessity, however, as the use of a battery-type electrode for both the anode and cathode still forms a supercapattery, which yields a high energy and power capacity without losing the long chargeâ€“discharge durability. The electrical polarities of the electrode determine whether the electrode is positive or negative. The distinction is that the positive electrode has a higher potential than the negative electrode.16,17

Recently, various transition metal oxides, metal sulfides, and conducting polymers have been reported to exhibit excellent supercapattery properties.1,8,15 These oxides and sulfides show different valence states, phases, and morphologies, thereby revealing their good electrochemical activity.16 Due to their high theoretical capacity, they have drawn much attention.11,18,19 Conducting polymers with a shorter cycle life, metal oxides with a low electrical conductivity, and transition metal dichalcogenides with a poor rate performance have hindered their widespread application in supercapatteries.18,20 To meet the needs of modern civilization and rising ecological concerns, flexible, lightweight, and conductive materials that are inexpensive, CO2 emission-free, and have environmentally acceptable energy-conversion and -storage systems are required.3,21 Perovskite oxides with the general formula ABO3 [where A-site elements are rare earth elements (La, Sr, Ce) and B-site elements are transition metal elements (Mn, Co, Ni, Fe)] are cheap, have a long cycle life due to their stable crystal structures, a high charge-storage capability due to rapid redox reactions, flexibility, environmental friendliness, and electrochemical and thermal stability. These properties have induced scientific researchers to choose perovskite oxides for wide application in potential fields, such as in solar cells, secondary batteries, electrochemical sensors, fuel cells, and supercapacitors.22â€“26 Moreover, oxygen-vacancy creation has a direct connection with the oxygen-ion-intercalation process, which facilitates the rapid redox reaction process of the perovskite structure.27,28 Hence the performance of perovskites can be improved through the creation of oxygen vacancies. This can be achieved through doping the perovskites with impurities;29,30 another way is to create a porous structure as it gives a shorter diffusion path, a large specific area, and the rapid transportation of ions.13,22,23,31 However, synthesizing porous perovskite oxides is tedious. Usually, the creation of the porous nature of a perovskite oxide is possible by mastering the possible advanced preparation strategies. However, different methods such as hydrothermal methods, electrospinning, molten salt-incorporated solid-state methods, and template-involved solâ€“gel synthesis, are primarily used to prepare the nanoporous structure.22,32 Sharma et al. and Y. Zhang et al. proved this by fabricating porous perovskite electrodes through the solâ€“gel method, which demonstrated excellent electrochemical properties.31,32 Careful synthesis routes of perovskites can help us to tailor their desirable properties (such as a high specific surface area and easy accessibility to the electroactive sites) to make an efficient electrode for supercapatteries as well as for other potential applications such as sensing, electrocatalysis, and photocatalysis.24,25,29,32,35 A facile green approach for producing such devices is the need of the hour. When these devices are fabricated in the nanoscale range, they achieve exciting properties, such as high surface areas and short transportation times, which help to enhance the energy density and power density of the energy-storage devices.

In this study, a facile, lemon-extract-assisted autocombustion method is used to synthesize porous CNO for the first time. Although CNO synthesized previously exhibits outstanding electrochemical properties, the morphology and texture of the as-prepared electrodes were different. Hence, we explored the idea of a green synthesis route to create porous CNO using various doses of lemon extract that contains citric acid, which acts as a fuel for autocombustion. Regardless of whether the electrode is operated in a faradaic or non-faradaic mode, the conductivity, porosity, and nanostructure are three crucial factors. A higher electrode conductivity helps the instantaneous polarization across the electrode surface, allowing more electrolyte ions to reach the electrode surface in a shorter amount of time as well as improving the conduction of transferred charges during the faradaic process. These can result in an improved energy and power capacity of the electrode. Hierarchically porous CNO perovskite oxides are highly porous with a high surface area and a large accessible space that is favourable for fast electron and ion transport, endowing the energy-storage devices with a superior performance. The electrode porosity benefits faradaic charge-transfer processes in two ways: (i) increased access to the charge-transfer active sites; and (ii) simpler ion conduction across the electrode. The nano-structuring improves the desirable qualities of energy-storage devices (such as secondary batteries and supercapacitors), which include the energy density, power density, and cyclic stability. An electrode material that has a smaller-sized nano-structure helps to improve the surface area, which increases the electrolyte-ion access, reduces the diffusion length, and increases the capacity value even under a high applied potential.21,32 In this study, we synthesize porous CNO nanoparticles with various doses of added lemon extracts to achieve an efficient pore size for improved electrochemical performance. All the samples with the added lemon extract exhibit a porous nature and fewer agglomerated nanoparticles due to the presence of citric acid as well as ascorbic acid. Furthermore, the studies show that an appropriate amount of lemon extract gives better surface area and hierarchically enriched pore creation. All electrochemical studies were carried out in aqueous 3 M KOH electrolyte using a three-electrode cell system to evaluate the best porous sample. The CNO3 sample shows a 2.5-fold enhanced capacity over the CNO1 sample (with no lemon extract added) due to the optimization of the porous structure, which provides more surface electrochemical active sites. All the porous samples show an excellent specific capacity with outstanding cycle stability owing to their high surface area and interconnected pore structure. The CNO1, CNO2, CNO3, and CNO4 samples exhibit a specific capacity of 242.54, 559.39, 605, and 591.25 C gâˆ’1, respectively, at a specific current of 1 A gâˆ’1. Among these, the CNO3 sample has an excellent charge storage capability of the active material, which enabled us to construct a symmetric device. The fabricated device delivered a specific energy of 43.45 W h kgâˆ’1 at a power density of 800 W kgâˆ’1. Moreover, the device achieved a potential of 1.6 V in 3 M KOH aqueous electrolyte and exhibited a specific capacity of 191.5 C gâˆ’1 at a specific current of 1 A gâˆ’1. Even though the device has an excellent specific capacity, it maintained a prolonged life of 50000 cycles with a capacity retention of 114.21% from its initial capacity at a specific current of 25 A gâˆ’1. The device achieves exceptional cycling stability, providing new insight into the field of perovskite oxide-based electrochemical energy-storage devices.

Synthesis of CeNiO3

In this typical synthesis, the lemon extract as the citric acid source, which was combined with nitrates and ethylene glycol, was associated using rapid, self-propagating combustion where nitrate acts as the oxidant and citrate acts as the organic fuel. The combustion can be triggered by the ignition of the sample at room temperature to form a porous material.36 Two nitrates, Ce(NO3)3Â·6H2O and Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O, were used as the starting material, and various amounts of lemon extract were added to optimize the porous material for our electrochemical purposes. 0.1 M Ce(NO3)3Â·6H2O and 0.1 M Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O were added to 75 mL of double-distilled sequentially after dilution of the first nitrate and again stirred continuously to obtain a well-dissolved solution. Again to the above solution, natural lemon extract, which was a pretreated and filtered solution, was added at different doses (0, 2, 4, and 6 mL) and magnetic stirring was continued for another 30 min. To control the combustion process, 2 mL of ethylene glycol was added to the above mixture and the stirring continued with the solution on a hot plate at 80 Â°C. The solution mixture, which was initially light green, started to evaporate and turned to a light brown gel after 2 h of stirring at 80 Â°C. Eventually, bubbling began and the gel suddenly underwent autocombustion. The photograph taken during the bubbling and combustion of the solâ€“gel is illustrated in Fig. S1(Aâ€“C) (ESIâ€ ). The obtained samples at different doses of lemon extract (0, 2, 4, and 6 mL) were labelled as CNO1, CNO2, CNO3, and CNO4, respectively. Electrode preparation

The working electrode was prepared by taking 80 wt% of the active material, 10 wt% of polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), and 10 wt% carbon black, all ground well using a pestle and mortar. The well-ground mixture was dispersed in N-methyl pyrrolidone (NMP) solvent, and the mixed slurry was drop-cast over a 1 cm2 area of a Ni-foam current collector. The coated sample was kept in a vacuum oven at 60 Â°C for 12 h to dry the working material. The electrode was compressed under 5 MPa pressure; the active mass loaded on the electrode was measured as 1.6 mg. To analyze and compare the electrochemical performance of the samples prepared with different doses of lemon extract, the active mass of CNO1, CNO2, CNO3, and CNO4 loaded on the Ni-form has to be constant and it was chosen to be 1.6 mg.

Crystal structure and phase identification

The crystallographic structure and crystalline nature of the CNO nanomaterials were analyzed using their powder XRD patterns. The crystalline phase of the CNO3 material was evaluated via XRD at different temperatures as shown in Fig. S2 (ESIâ€ ). Here, the as-prepared sample is less crystalline in nature. Moreover, many impurities from the precursors are present in the sample. During calcination the impurities of the samples disappear, and the crystallinity of the sample is improved. The CNO3 sample at 650 Â°C is less impure as well as highly crystalline in nature compared with the other samples. In addition, the samples heated at 650 Â°C have well-defined diffraction peaks, corresponding to an orthorhombic structure with the Pnma space group. Thus, heating of the sample after the combustion process is required for perovskite formation. The material is heated in a furnace to combust the organic matrix and form the perovskite product. All the XRD patterns for the various lemon-extract doses (0, 2, 4, and 6 mL) heated at 650 Â°C are shown in Fig. 1(a). The narrow and sharp peaks indicate the crystalline nature of the as-prepared samples under citrate-assisted preparation. The sample with the 0 mL dose does not undergo complete combustion due to the lack of citrate fuel. This results in the absence of diffraction planes (112) and (221) in the XRD pattern. The peak at 28.69Â° originates from the CeO2.35,39 The XRD patterns of all the samples correspond to the orthorhombic perovskite oxide structure of CNO with the Pnma space group, which is in good accordance with the Materials Project data and reported references.35,38

The morphology of the active materials greatly influences the electrochemical performance of the electrode. Here, a porous morphology was achieved through lemon-extract-assisted synthesis and was evaluated using FESEM. The FESEM images of all four samples are depicted in Fig. 2(aâ€“l). Among the four samples, for CNO1 (without the lemon extract, or 0 mL lemon extract) the particles are agglomerated and no porosity is visible in the FESEM images (Fig. 2(aâ€“c)). The remaining three samples (CNO2, CNO3, and CNO4) show a well defined porous nature, which is very clear in the FESEM images. The controlled self-combustion of the solâ€“gel lemon-assisted method turns the distorted sphere morphology of the samples into a porous structure and is a novel way to prepare porous perovskites. A high magnification image shows the mesoporous and microporous structure in the CNO2, CNO3, and CNO4 samples. Notably, the pore diameter gradually increases with an increase in the dose of lemon extract. The gradual increase of the lemon dose improves the porous nature of the sample and decreases the agglomeration of the samples. The porous CNO (PCNO) sample shows numerous irregular pores with different diameters on the surface, and was formed due to the optimum dose of lemon extract in the solution combustion process. The electronic properties, chemical reactivity, and surface interactions of the active materials are influenced by the morphological and nanoporous distribution in the material. Compared with the single pore size in the mesoporous structure, the appropriate combination of small and large pore sizes in the mesoporous structure provides the characteristics of a higher accessibility of ion transport, better electrolyte infiltration permeability, and a large space for volume expansivity.2,21,33 The hierarchically porous interconnected small and large mesopores in the structure work together to facilitate fast charge transfer through the electrode/electrolyte interface, minimize the ion-diffusion path length, and accommodate volume changes during cycling. The elemental mapping of CNO3 confirms the presence of Ce, Ni, and O atoms in the CNO structure, which are uniformly distributed throughout the structure, as shown in Fig. 2(mâ€“p).

Surprisingly the potential range of the electrode showed an enhancement of 10% (0â€“0.6 V), as presented in Fig. 4(e). The notably enhanced area and potential range in the CV of the electrode show the porous activation of the electrode material after long chargeâ€“discharge cycles.44 To investigate the best electrode among the four, CV was evaluated using the same scan rate of 20 mV sâˆ’1, as depicted in Fig. 4(f). The obtained result show that the CNO3 electrode has a larger CV area and a higher current response, which indicates the electrode's ability to store more charge and hence have a higher energy density. In addition, the contribution of charge storage from the bare Ni-foam is negligible. Interestingly, the electrode performance of the CNO3 electrode after 5000 chargeâ€“discharge cycles was obtained at a scan rate of 20 mV sâˆ’1 for comparison, as shown in Fig. 4(g). The improved electrode performance is due to the higher surface area as well as the mixed porosity with the large pore volume of the electrode.

The porous structure and the unique morphology enable the PCNO to achieve a high CV area via fast charge-transfer kinetics. The large pore volume and more surface-active sites facilitate a continuous diffusion reaction at a faster rate. According to various literature data, the charge-storage mechanism has a direct dependence on oxygen intercalation into the perovskite structure, as reported earlier.25â€“27,51 The perovskites have a lower ionic conductivity, which hinders a superior performance in the electrochemical field. The lattice structure of CNO contains many oxygen vacancies that can be tuned by substituting other elements for Ce and Ni atoms. The exposed oxygen vacancy on the surface of the CNO perovskite oxide readily absorbs oxygen. The absorbed OHâˆ’ ions from the alkaline electrolyte are converted to O2âˆ’ ions in the oxygen sites by releasing a proton. When the O2âˆ’ ion intercalates the oxygen vacancy of the perovskite surface, it diffuses along the octahedral lattice and the Ni atom at the B site is oxidized to maintain the charge neutrality.26,52,53 Thus, the oxidation of Ni2+ to Ni3+ will take place. In addition, more oxygen ions intercalate into the oxygen vacancies, resulting in the oxidation of Ce3+ to Ce4+ to maintain the charge neutrality. Thus, the electrode experiences an oxidationâ€“reduction reaction for the two cations in the CNO perovskite. At the same time, the protons created will transfer to the electrolyte medium and combine with OHâˆ’ ions yielding water as the product. Energy-storage properties of CeNiO3//CeNiO3

To further evaluate the practical application of the CNO electrode, a supercapattery device (CNO//CNO) was fabricated using CNO as the positive and negative electrodes, in a 3 M KOH aqueous electrolyte. The device was made using the CNO3 electrode, which was found to have the best surface area and pore volume and shows a better electrochemical performance in the three-electrode system. The operating-potential window of the device was obtained from the individual potentials of the electrode in the negative and positive potential regions of the three-electrode cell system. The operating potential in the negative and positive regions of the CNO3 electrode is depicted in Fig. S7(A) (ESIâ€ ). Furthermore, the potential of CV from 1 to 1.7 V at a scan rate of 100 mV sâˆ’1 and GCD from 1 to 1.6 V at a current density of 2.5 A gâˆ’1 are presented in Fig. S7(B and C) (ESIâ€ ), respectively. Whenever the potential window is increased the oxidation peaks become broadened, and two reduction peaks are observed at 0.7 V and 0.4 V. These broadened peaks at the higher potential window are due to the oxidation of Ce and Ni atoms to their higher valence states. Similarly, the two distinct peaks observed at 0.7 V and 0.4 V are due to reduction of Ce4+ to Ce3+ and Ni3+ to Ni2+, respectively.26 The CV configuration of the CNO//CNO supercapattery device is illustrated in Fig. 5(e), where we find that the charge-storage process is completed by redox reactions involving the OHâˆ’ ion at the positive electrode and K+ ions at the negative electrode. The typical currentâ€“voltage and potentialâ€“time curves ensure that the charge-storage mechanism is a combination of diffusion-controlled and surface-confined charge storage. The device achieves a voltage of 1.6 V in the currentâ€“potential curve and the potentialâ€“time curve. The well-obtained redox peaks of CV are characteristic of battery-type electrode behaviour.37,40 The broadened anodic peak area and the greater cathodic peak intensity are an indication of the efficient charge-storage possibility of the electrode through redox reactions. The plateau region in the GCD profile is consistent with the redox peaks of the CV profile. To investigate the electrical conductivity of the supercapattery device, EIS analysis was carried out at a perturbing potential of 10 mV in the frequency range of 100 kHz to 10 mHz. The Nyquist plot of the CNO//CNO device before and after cycling is demonstrated in Fig. 5(g) and its enlarged image is depicted in Fig. 5(h). This increase in the impedance values after cycling is because a long cycle run can cause the unfavourable diffusion of ions into the bulk of the electrode material. A lower value of resistance offers a higher electronic conductivity and ionic conductivity in the KOH medium for an electrode. The Bode phase-angle plot for the symmetric cell before and after cycling is shown in Fig. 5(i). The Bode plot demonstrates that the behaviour of the phase angle is changed slightly after a long cycle run, which is attributed to the activated porous site at intermediate frequency. At a lower frequency the phase angle has a lower value or a phase-angle shift towards a lower angle compared with before cycling, which is attributed to the lower capacitive behaviour of the electrode after cycling. EIS analysis further suggests that electron transfer inside the nanomaterial is higher before cycling due to the greater availability of the active pores. A long cyclic run results in the intercalation of electrolyte ions into the electrode pores, which may become trapped, thus decreasing the active pores of the electrode. This may halt the diffusion of ions into the bulk of the electrode material. However, surface pores remain the same for both electrolyte-ion intercalation deintercalation. Thus, the electrodes show capacitive or surface charge-storage behaviour.57,58

In a supercapattery, the life cycle of an electrode is a critical parameter for determining its potential performance. The device shows a notable cyclic stability of 114.21% of its initial capacity over 50000 cycles at 25 A gâˆ’1, as presented in Fig. 5(j). The higher surface area is favourable for the exposure of electrochemically active sites, which facilitates faster ionic diffusion through intercalation/deintercalation. Interestingly, the specific capacity value is increased until 12800 cycles. This drastic initial increase in capacity is attributed to the full activation of Ce and Ni atoms in the electrode species via effective contact between the electrode and the electrolyte. Electroactivation has been accomplished through the cycling-induced improvement of pore activation on the surface of the porous CNO3 electrode, which allows easy access of electrolytes to the electrode surface. This electrode activation has been reported previously in many research publications.44,46 After 12800 cycles, a gradual decrease in the capacity is observed and the cell stabilizes after 32200 cycles. Thereafter, at 50000 cycles the supercapattery device retained 114.21% of its initial capacity and 90.33% of its maximum specific capacity, which shows its practical evaluation to the scientific community. Even after long-term cycling, the faradaic efficiency of the electrode is maintained at 99.87%. The outstanding electrochemical characteristics of the porous CNO electrode are specified as follows:

(1) Nanoperovskite porous CNO gives rise to a porous structure with a high surface-to-volume ratio, which increases the active sites and generates more energy and power.

Conclusions

In summary, a series of porous CNO materials have been synthesized via a lemon-extract-assisted solâ€“gel autocombustion method. A porous structure with hierarchically interconnected pores with a larger pore volume can be achieved through the addition of an appropriate amount of the lemon extract. After comprehensive electrochemical testing, the best porous electrode material (CNO3) was determined to be that for which 4 mL of lemon extract was added. A porous structure with a very high surface area was achieved, which is favourable for the creation of electrochemically active sites. The CNO3 electrode achieved a larger specific capacity (605 C gâˆ’1at 1 A gâˆ’1) and a long-term cycle life (103% capacity maintained after 5000 cycles) using a three-electrode system. A symmetric supercapattery device was designed using the CNO3 electrode as both the positive and negative electrode, which provided a specific energy of 43.45 W h kgâˆ’1 at a specific power of 800 W kgâˆ’1. The device has an outstanding cycle life of over 50000 cycles with 114.21% retention of its initial capacity, which opening up a route for the synthesis of superior electrodes. In addition, the device was used to light up an arrangement of LEDs that showed â€˜NITTâ€™. Our simple and facile lemon-assisted synthesis route for the preparation of porous perovskite oxide with hierarchically connected pores could provide a favourable path for higher-level electrode materials for supercapacitors and batteries.
