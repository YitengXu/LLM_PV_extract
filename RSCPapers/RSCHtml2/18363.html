Optical molecular sensing with semiconductor quantum dots (QDs)

Abstract

Semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) exhibit unique optical and photophysical properties. These features are implemented to develop optical molecular sensor systems. The review addresses the methods to functionalize the QDs with chemical capping layers that enable the use of the resulting hybrid structures for sensing, and discusses the photophysical mechanisms being applied in the different sensor systems. Different methods to design the chemically-modified QDs hybrid structures for sensing low-molecular-weight substrates, metal ions, anions and gases are presented. These include the functionalization of the QDs with ligands that bind ions, the modification of the QDs with substrate-specific ligands or receptor units, and the chemical modification of the QDs upon sensing. Specific emphasis is directed to describe the cooperative catalytic functions of the QDs in the sensing processes, and to address the function of sensing with logic-gate operations.

1. Introduction

The synthesis and characterization of semiconductor nanoparticles, quantum dots (QDs), represent one of the major advances in material science in the last two decades. Photoexcitation of valence-band electrons into the conduction band of the semiconductor material yields electron–hole pairs that recombine and yield luminescence. The theoretical understanding of the parameters controlling the luminescence features of QDs was extensively reviewed,1–7 and experimental studies demonstrating the luminescence properties of numerous compositions of semiconductor materials exhibiting variable sizes, shapes and compositions were reported.8–13 These studies have established a new class of materials that find broad applications in energy conversion and storage,14–17 optoelectronic devices,18 sensor applications,19–21 photocatalysis22–25 and more.26 Ingenious synthetic routes for the preparation of size-controlled semiconductor nanoparticles were developed, and these included the kinetic-controlled growth of monolayer- or thin films-protected QDs27 or the synthesis of the QDs in nano reactor systems such as micelles,28 microemulsions,29,30 or mesoporous inorganic supports.31,32 Further versatility in the synthesis of QDs was demonstrated with the preparation of core–shell semiconductor materials6,33–39 or semiconductor materials exhibiting nanorod shapes40–43 and even branched nanorod structures.9,44–46 The unique functions of QDs are reflected by their photophysical and chemical properties: (i) the QDs exhibit high fluorescence quantum yields and they reveal a high stability against degradation and photobleaching.47 (ii) The QDs reveal size-controlled and shape-controlled absorption and luminescence features, due to the quantum confinement of the electrons in the particles. Thus, as the particles are smaller, the luminescence energies are blue-shifted to higher energies.1,3,48–50 Similarly, non-symmetrical particles, e.g. rods, exhibit longitudinal excitons controlled by the aspect-ratios of the nano-objects. As control over the sizes and shapes of QDs is achieved by the different synthetic and separation methods, particles of the same material revealing luminescence properties covering a broad spectral region are available. (iii) The fluorescence spectra of the QDs are usually characterized by narrow emission bands exhibiting a large Stokes shift. These properties are important for coupling the emission functions of the QDs to other sized QDs,51 or to organic fluorophores.52 (iv) Unlike molecular fluorophores, semiconductor QDs have broad absorbance bands. This allows the photoexcitation of different sized QDs by a single wavelength, while generating the size-controlled luminescence of the QDs. (v) Finally, the arsenal of synthetic methodologies for the preparation of QDs leads to monolayer- or thin-film protected nanoparticles with tailored functionalities. This enables the tethering of molecular units to the QDs,53 and allows the electronic coupling between the photoexcited QDs and the tethered molecules. The electron transfer (ET) quenching of photoexcited QDs is a versatile useful photophysical mechanism to follow spatially-restricted close interactions between electron donor–acceptor sites. The photoexcited QDs might participate in electron transfer via two general routes outlined in Fig. 1. The photoexcitation of the QDs yields an electron–hole pair in the conduction band and valence band of the QDs. In the presence of an electron acceptor (A), exhibiting a LUMO energy level more positive than the conduction band potential, electron transfer quenching of the conduction band electrons to the acceptor units occurs. Similarly, in the presence of an electron donor (D) exhibiting a HOMO level that is less positive than the valence band potential, electron transfer from the donor to the holes associated with the valence band proceeds.

Substantial progress in the application of QDs for optical sensing and biosensing was accomplished in the last decade, and several review articles summarized the advances in this field.57–60 Most of the reviews addressed, however, the use of the QDs for optical biosensing and intracellular imaging. The present review highlights recent advances in the use of QDs for sensing of low-molecular-weight substrates or ions using modified QDs as optical probes and addresses the application of chemically-modified quantum dots as optical switches or logic-gate devices.

2. Chemically-modified semiconductor quantum dots

Semiconductor quantum dots with superior photophysical features (i.e., high fluorescence quantum yields and stability against photobleaching) are usually synthesized from organometallic precursors, and are generally protected by a capping layer composed of organic ligands such as trioctylphosphine (TOP) or trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO).61–64 While QDs composed of a single semiconductor core material reveal, often, limited stability and low luminescence quantum yields, due to surface defects, composite core–shell QDs of two different, lattice-matching, semiconductors reveal enhanced stabilities and high luminescence yields of the core material.6,34,38,65–70 The resulting capped nanocrystals are hydrophobic, and therefore, soluble in organic solvents. One may replace, however, the TOP/TOPO ligands with other capping agents via ligand exchange reactions. Alternatively, modifications of the existing ligand using electrostatic, hydrophobic interactions or host–guest interactions can be performed.19,71–91 The selection of the ligands protecting the QDs depends on the desired applications of the QDs and the dispersion media. For application in aqueous media the organic capping ligands must be exchanged or further functionalized with hydrophilic capping agents or alternatively, aqueous synthetic methods for the production of QDs need to be implemented.

The introduction of the QDs into aqueous media is usually accompanied with a drastic decrease in the luminescence yields of the QDs. This effect presumably originates from the reaction of surface states with water, a process that yields surface traps for the conduction-band electrons.92 Different methods to stabilize the luminescence properties of semiconductor QDs in aqueous media have been reported, Fig. 4. These include the surface passivation with protective layers, such as proteins71,93 and the coating of the QDs with protecting silicon oxide films,94,95 polymer films,47,90,96 or alternatively, with amphiphilic polymers that have both a hydrophobic side chain that interacts with the organic capping layer of the QDs, and a hydrophilic component such as PEG backbone for water solubility.83,97 Such water-soluble QDs retained up to 55% of their quantum yields upon transfer from hydrophobic environments to aqueous media. While these methods successfully preserve the photophysical properties of the QDs, they produce QDs with thick stabilizing capping layers that hamper the photophysical functions of the QDs. For example, the thick passivation layers introduce barriers for the application of the QDs in fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) or electron-transfer quenching processes associated with sensing events. Thus, a delicate balance between the structure of the modifying capping layer associated with the QDs and its effect on the photophysical features of the particles must be maintained.

An additional method to produce organic or water soluble QDs relies on the exchange of native organic ligands linked to the QD, such as trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) or octadecylamine (ODA), with other ligands. The most common examples of binding groups are thiols, phosphines and amines. Polar and charged molecules provide good dispersibility in aqueous media, while hydrophobic ligands are soluble in nonpolar organic solvents. The most common thiolated molecules used to stabilize semiconductor QDs in aqueous media are thiolated aliphatic carboxylic acids, such as mercaptoacetic acid (MAA),52,71,98 mercaptopropionic acid (MPA), or mercaptoundecanoic acid (MUA), Fig. 4. To achieve higher stability, bidentate surface ligands composed of derivatives of dihydrolipoic acid (DHLA)93,99 or dithiothreitol74 were used for the preparation of water-soluble CdSe/ZnS QDs.100,101 These bidentate ligands provide stable interactions with the QD surfaces owing to the chelate effect of the dithiol groups. Also, lipoic acid units tethered to poly(ethylene glycol) spacers that were functionalized with linkable functionalities were used to modify the QDs. These modifiers eliminate nonspecific adsorption processes, and also provide anchoring sites for the covalent immobilization of the recognition ligands. Other thiol-containing materials, such as peptides with a polycysteine adhesive domains, were used to synthesize water soluble QDs.102,103 Additionally, QDs were stabilized by exchanging ODA with water-soluble dendrons to yield water-soluble QDs with quantum yields of 36%.104

Several methods to synthesize functionalized QDs directly in water were reported.57,105,106 As compared to the organic synthesis, aqueous synthetic approaches to prepare QDs are simpler, reproducible, and can be easily scaled up, yet the resulting QDs exhibit, often, lower crystallinity or quantum yields. Different aqueous-based synthetic routes to prepare QDs that retain high fluorescence yields have been developed. These methods are based on the reaction of metal salts (e.g. cadmium salts) and NaHTe or NaHSe in the presence of water-soluble molecular or macromolecular capping agents that kinetically protect the growth of the QDs by covalent or ligand–ion interactions to yield monolayer or thin-film capped particles.107 For example, nanocrystals, such as CdSe108,109 or HgTe,110 were successfully prepared in water using mercapto-ethanol and thioglycerol as stabilizers, and the resulting QDs possessed high quantum yields (up to 40%). Also, CdSe QDs with a narrow size distribution that reveal a high fluorescence quantum yield (25%) were synthesized by a single-step procedure in water using glutathione (GSH) as a stabilizing agent.111 Similarly, glutathione-capped CdTe (GSH-CdTe) QDs with tunable fluorescence features (500–650 nm) and quantum yields as high as 45% were synthesized in aqueous media and employed for the staining of fixed cells.112 The single-step synthesis of thiolated cyclodextrin-modified CdSe/CdS core–shell QDs resulted in water-soluble QDs with a quantum yield of 46% in water.113,114

Also, the aqueous synthesis of citrate-capped QDs115,116 and cysteine-modified QDs117 have been reported. Another aqueous synthesis of QDs is based on microwave irradiation under controlled temperature. This method allowed the preparation of a series of stable water-soluble MPA-capped CdTe QDs with tunable emission properties (505–733 nm) and high quantum yields (40–60%). This method was used also for the production of high quality alloyed QDs.118

The functionalization of the quantum dots, not only tunes their solubility properties, but also allows the introduction of functionalities for further coupling to other molecules or ligands, as depicted in Fig. 5. The most common method involves the coupling of amine-functionalized molecules to carboxylic acid residues associated with the encapsulating layer that modifies the nanoparticles. For example, amino-modified nucleic acids or amino acids may be covalently coupled to the QDs, Fig. 5, path (a) and (b). This method is, however, limited to mono-amine functionalized molecules since poly-functionalities may induce the aggregation of the QDs. The stepwise assembly of molecular functionalities on QDs can be achieved by the primary assembly of a thiolated capping layer that allows the covalent tethering of a ligand for the binding of the recognition element to the QDs. For example, the assembly of mercaptopropionic acid on the QDs provided the base capping layer for the covalent attachment of the p-aminophenyl boronic acid ligand. The subsequent binding of vicinal dihydroxy-modified host molecules enabled, then, the functionalization of the QDs with boronic ester molecular functionalization, Fig. 5, path(c). A further method to modify the QDs involves a layer-by-layer modification process using electrostatic interactions as a binding motif. For example, the deposition of positively charged polymers on carboxylate-monolayer-functionalized QDs enabled the construction of bilayers on the QDs, Fig. 5(d). By the appropriate functionalization of the positively charged polymer, further covalent modification of the QDs can be achieved.

As the different molecular sensing platforms involve ligand-functionalized QDs that, usually, bind the analyte by equilibrium, ligand–analyte, supramolecular complexes, the efficiency of the sensing process is controlled by the surface coverage of the ligands. Accordingly, it is important to characterize the surface coverage of the ligands on the modified QDs, and it is important to optimize the synthesis of the modified QDs to establish reliable sensing materials.

3. Sensing of metal ions with chemically-functionalized QDs

The functionalization of QDs with ligands that bind metal ions provides a general mean to develop functional QDs acting as ion sensors. The ability of many ions or metal–ligand complexes to quench the QDs provides an intrinsic feature for the operation of these sensors. While many of the sensors turn to be non-selective, the appropriate design of the ligands may, often, improve the selectivity of the systems. Different capping functionalities such as mercaptopropionic acid (MPA), mercaptoacetic acid, glutathione, mercaptosuccinic acid, thioglycolic acid or L-cysteine were used as ligands for the modification of QDs and for the association of metal ions.119–121 Electron transfer quenching of the luminescence of the QDs allows, then, the analysis of the ions. For example, mercaptoacetic acid-capped CdS QDs bind Hg2+ ions, resulting in a linear decrease in the fluorescence intensity of the QDs in the range 0.05 × 10−7 to 4.0 × 10−7 M of Hg2+. This enabled the analysis of the ions with a detection limit corresponding to 4.2 × 10−9 M.122 Also, mercaptopropionic acid-functionalized QDs were applied for the analysis of Cu2+ ions. The fluorescence of the QDs was quenched linearly in the range 0–256 ng × ml−1 of Cu2+ with a detection limit of 0.19 ng × ml−1.121 Calixarene-capped CdSe/ZnS QDs were, also, used for the selective sensing of mercury ions. Higher concentrations of Hg2+ resulted in lower intensities of the luminescence of the QDs.123 In addition, CdSe QDs were modified with triethanolamine (TEA) for the recognition of mercury and iodide ions. The iodide anion bridged the positively charged TEA-QDs thus, allowing the association of the Hg2+ ions and the formation of a stable QD–I−–Hg2+ complex. This resulted in the quenching of the luminescence of the QDs via an electron transfer path from the QDs to the surface-associated mercury ions.124 By designing capping layers consisting of peptides with tailored metal binding properties or Schiff-base ligands, enhanced sensitivities and selectivities were demonstrated. The pentapeptide Gly-His-Leu-Leu-Cys was immobilized on CdS QD, and the selective luminescence detection of Cu2+ or Ag+ was demonstrated.125 Similarly, CdS QDs modified with Schiff-base functionalities revealed specific sensing of Cu2+ and Fe3+ ions.119 These solution-based optical sensors were further improved by the assembly of the QDs on solid supports and the optical detection of ions in complex mixtures. For example, mercaptosuccinic acid-functionalized CdTe QDs were deposited on quartz surfaces by a layer-by-layer process that involved poly(dimethyldiallyl ammonium chloride) (PDDA) as electrostatic “glue”. The resulting surfaces were used for the optical detection of Hg2+.126

The Hg2+ ions quenched the fluorescence of the QDs by an electron transfer process. The fluorescence of the QDs multilayer films was examined in the presence of different concentrations of mercury ions, Fig. 6. As the concentration of the Hg2+ ions increased, the fluorescence of the QDs decreased. This enabled the sensing of mercury ions with a detection limit that corresponds to 0.01 μM.

While the interaction of metal ions with functionalized-QDs usually results in the quenching of the luminescence of the QDs, fluorescence enhancement can also be observed. Such a phenomenon is usually observed when the ligand associated with the QDs quenches the particles, yet the resulting metal ion–ligand complex lacks the possibility to quench the QDs. For example, CdSe/ZnS QDs modified with the azamacrocycle receptor units, cyclam (1), were used for the sensing of zinc ions (Fig. 7).127 The fluorescence of the QDs was quenched by the azamacrocycle units through electron transfer from the amine functionalities to the valence-band holes of the photoexcited QDs, thereby eliminating the radiative electron–hole recombination process, and the luminescence of the QDs. The binding of zinc ions to the azamacrocycle receptor eliminated the electron donor properties of the amine functionalities, resulting in an increase of the luminescence of the QDs, Fig. 7. This enabled the sensing of Zn2+ ions with a detection limit of ca. 2.2 μM.

Purine and pyrimidine bases incorporated in nucleic acids or in synthetic nucleic acid analogs bind specifically different metal ions. For example, thymine yields a thymine–Hg2+–thymine complex,128,129 and cytosine forms specifically a cytosine–Ag+–cytosine complex.130,131 These properties of the nucleic acids were utilized to develop QDs-based Hg2+-ions and Ag+-ions sensors, and different sized QDs were implemented for the multiplexed analysis of Hg2+ and Ag+.132 Two different sized CdSe/ZnS QDs were modified with nucleic acids exhibiting specific ion binding properties. The green-emitting QDs, λem = 560 nm, were functionalized with the T-rich nucleic acid, (2), that binds Hg2+-ions, and the red-emitting QDs, λem = 620 nm, were functionalized with the C-rich nucleic acid, (3), that associates the Ag+-ions, Fig. 8(A). The formation of the Hg2+-modified complexes on the (2)-modified QDs resulted in the quenching of the green-emitting QDs, Fig. 8(B), whereas the association of Ag+ to the (3)-modified QDs led to the quenching of the red-emitting QDs, Fig. 8(C). The respective QDs revealed selectivity and other metal ions did not affect the luminescence properties of the QDs. The QDs enabled the optical analysis of Hg2+ and Ag+ with detection limits that corresponded to 10 nM (2 ppb) and 1 μM (200 ppb), respectively. Also, multiplexed analysis of the two ions was accomplished by the different sized functionalized QDs, Fig. 8(D). Upon mixing of the two-sized (2)- and (3)-functionalized QDs the selective and simultaneous analysis of the Hg2+ and Ag+ ions was demonstrated by the quenching of the respective QDs.132

Synthetic nucleic acids exhibiting catalytic activities (DNAzymes) find growing interest as amplifying labels for sensing events.133–135 Among the many catalytic nucleic acids a series of metal ion-dependent DNAzymes are known. For example, DNAzymes that depend on Pb2+, Cu2+, Zn2+, Mg2+, UO22+ and more are known. The different catalytic nucleic acids differ in the loop-region composing the core for accommodating the respective metal ions, and the sequences for binding the substrates. The substrates usually include a ribonucleobase (r) as cleavage site, and the catalytic function of the DNAzyme is reflected by the hydrolytic scission of the substrate, and its separation into two subunits, Fig. 10(A). Accordingly, the Pb2+- and Cu2+-dependent DNAzymes and different-sized QDs were implemented for the analysis of Pb2+ and Cu2+ ions, as depicted in Fig. 10(B).136 The 5′ amino-functionalized substrates of the Pb2+- and Cu2+-dependent DNAzymes ((4) and (5), respectively) were covalently linked to different sized CdSe QDs functionalized with a carboxylated siloxane capping layer.

4. Ligand- and receptor-functionalized QDs for sensing

The competitive binding of vicinal diols to a boronic acid ligand allowed the development of quantum dots-based chemical sensors. 3-Aminophenyl boronic acid was covalently linked to CdSe/ZnS QDs. The ATTO-590-labeled-galactose (8) or ATTO-590-modified dopamine (9) were linked to the boronic acid ligand associated with the QDs, and this resulted in the FRET from the QDs to the dye. In the presence of monosaccharides, competitive binding to the QDs proceeded, and this resulted in higher intensities of the luminescence of the QDs and lower fluorescence intensities of the acceptor dye. Various molecular receptors, such as cyclodextrins, calixarenes and crown-ethers, were used for the optical analysis of different substrates. The luminescence of the receptor-modified QDs can be controlled by the binding of the analytes to the receptor sites using different affinity interactions. For example, β-cyclodextrin, β-CD, -modified CdSe/ZnS QDs have been used to develop selective sensing and chiroselective sensing platforms using a competitive FRET assay or a direct electron transfer quenching route.138 Cyclodextrins are cyclic polysaccharide consisting of glucose units linked by 1-4-β-glycoside bonds. They bind hydrophobic organic substrates to their hydrophobic cavities.139–143 The binding properties of the cyclodextrins are controlled by the relative sizes of the guest substrates and the dimensions of their hydrophobic cavities (α-, β-, and γ-cyclodextrines). Furthermore, cyclodextrins are chiral receptors, and hence, reveal chiroselective binding of chiral guest substrates.144–149 These properties of cyclodextrins were used to develop selective and chiroselective chromatographic matrices, sensors, and slow-release systems.139,141,144,146,149–154 The β-cyclodextrin (β-CD) receptor consists of seven glucose units linked through 1-4-β-glycoside bonds. β-CD was linked to CdSe/ZnS QDs modified by the boronic acid ligand, Fig. 12(A), to yield the receptor-functionalized QDs. The dye, Rhodamine B, (13) was incorporated into the receptor sites of β-CD, resulting in, upon excitation of the QDs, the FRET process from the QDs to the β-CD-entrapped dye. 5. Surface-modified QDs for sensing

While the previous QDs-based systems were based on the formation of complexes between the analytes and ligands associated with the QDs, other associative interaction between the modified QDs and the analytes, such as electrostatic or hydrophobic interactions, may also provide the driving interactions for the sensor systems. Different capping layers were used to passivate the QDs surface and to stabilize the QDs. These capping units may interact with different analytes, thus, affecting the luminescence properties of the semiconductor nanocrystals. Oleic acid-coated CdSe QDs were synthesized and dispersed in chloroform, and the nitroaromatic explosives: 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2.4-dinitrotoluene (DNT), nitrobenzene (NB) and 2,4-dinitrochlorobenzene were intercalated within the hydrophobic layer surrounding the CdSe QDs. The associated nitro-aromatic analytes quenched the luminescence of the nanocrystals via an electron transfer mechanism and this enabled the analysis of the different explosives.164

The adsorption of analytes on the capping layer stabilizing the QDs often affects the luminescence of the QDs. Electrostatic interactions facilitate the electrostatic association of charged substrates to QDs modified with an oppositely charged capping layer. For example, mercaptopropionic acid (MPA)-capped CdSe QDs adsorb edavarone by electrostatic interactions, resulting in the blocking of non-radiative electron–hole recombination paths and the intensified luminescence of the QDs.165 Similarly, the attraction of the negatively charged adenosine triphosphate by positively charged QDs affected the quenching of the QDs. The system enabled the selective optical analysis of ATP in the presence of other nucleotides.166 In addition, mercaptoethane sulfonate-modified CdSe QDs were used for the optical detection of cyanide ions via an electron transfer quenching of the QDs.167 Also, the fluorescence quenching of negatively charged MPA-functionalized CdSe/ZnS QDs by the positively charged herbicide, N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium, via an electron transfer mechanism was demonstrated. This sensor system was further developed by coupling the receptor cucurbituril that binds the bipyridinium salt to its cavity. The competitive dissociation of the bipyridinium salt from the QDs restored the luminescence of the QDs, thus providing a positive fluorescence signal for the sensing of the bipyridinium salt.168 In analogy, a boronic acid-modified bipyridinium salt associated with positively charged QDs was used as a label for the competitive analysis of glucose.169 While the adsorbed bipyridinium salt quenched the luminescence of the nanocrystals, the competitive formation of the glucose–boronate complex detached the bipyridinium units from the QDs leading to intensified luminescence. A similar approach was used for the detection of glutathione,170Fig. 17.

In the presence of glutathione, competitive binding to the QDs proceeded, and this resulted in higher intensities of the luminescence of the QDs.

QDs modified with carboxylic acid functionalities reveal pH-controlled luminescence properties. Although the origin for the effect of protonation/deprotonation of the carboxylic acid on the luminescence of the QDs is not fully understood, presumably, the neutralization of cationic surface-states by the carboxylate units removes electron traps, thus enhancing the fluorescence of the QDs. Acidification of the carboxylate units and the formation of carboxylic acids units regenerate the cationic surface traps, thus, decreasing the luminescence of the QDs. For example, CdTe QDs capped with thioglycolic acid have been used as a pH indicator for the pH range of 4–6.171 A QDs-based pH sensor of enhanced complexity was developed by the coupling of CdSe nanocrystals with the pH-sensitive Squarine dye, thus controlling the FRET process between the QDs and the dye units by means of pH, Fig. 18. The deprotonated Squaraine dye (a) exhibits the appropriate absorption band to stimulate a FRET process between the QDs and the dye acceptor, while the protonated squaraine state, (b), cannot act as a FRET acceptor. Thus, the pH-controlled ratio of (a)/(b) modulated the FRET intensity, and the ratio of the luminescence of the QD/squarine fluorescence provides a quantitative measure for the pH of the solution.59 In another example, the pH sensitive fluorescein isothiocyanate dye was conjugated to glutathione-modified QDs and the resulting conjugates were used as a ratiometric pH sensor within the pH range of 4–6.172 QDs functionalized with the [1,3] oxazine ligand (24) were implemented as pH sensors,173Fig. 19. The oxazine compound (a) undergoes structural isomerization to form the open phenolate state (b) (under basic conditions) or the open protonated phenol state (c) (under acidic conditions), Fig. 19(B). While the protonated phenol state (c) exhibits a slightly blue-shifted absorbance as compared to the parent [1,3] oxazine ligand, the phenolate open isomer (b) exhibits an intense absorbance band in the visible spectrum, λ = 585 nm, Fig. 19(C). The [1,3] oxazine ligand-functionalized QDs revealed a quenched luminescence as compared to the non-modified parent QDs, Fig. 19(D), curves (d) and (e), respectively, and this was attributed to the electron transfer quenching of the QDs by the indole, electron-donor, ring. While the protonated isomer (c) does not quench the luminescence of the QDs, Fig. 19(D), curve (g), the phenolate open isomer (b) quenched effectively the luminescence of the QDs, Fig. 19(D), curve (f). The quenching proceeds via a FRET mechanism due to the spectral overlap between the absorbance of (b) and the luminescence of the QDs. These functionalized QDs could probe pH changes in the range from 3 to 11. The pH-sensitive QDs were also used as a sensing device for different analytes. For example, the pH controlled luminescence of mercaptoacetic acid-capped CdTe QDs was used to detect the drug tiopronin (N-(2-mercaptopropionyl)glycine). Tiopronin acidifies the solution, thus, resulting in the quenching of the luminescence of the QDs.174

6. Chemically-modified QDs as reactive hybrids for sensing

While the sensor systems described in the previous sections included receptor or ligand sites for the selective association of the guest analyte, a variety of chemically-modified QDs use the capping layer as a chemically-reactive interface that actively participates in the sensing process. In these systems the analyte reacts with the capping layer, and this leads to changes in the luminescence properties of the QDs that enable the quantitative optical detection of analytes. Semiconductor QDs functionalized with redox-active dyes or with redox-active capping components may change their photophysical properties as a result of a color change of the dye by a redox process, or by the electronic coupling of the redox-active capping component with the photoexcited electron–hole pair generated in the QDs. Alternatively, the analyte might react with the inorganic shell layer of the QDs, and this may lead to changes in the luminescence features of the QDs. For example, Nile-blue-functionalized CdSe/ZnS quantum dots were prepared and used as a hybrid material for the optical sensing of the 1,4-dihydronicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (phosphate) cofactors, NAD(P)H.176 Water soluble CdSe/ZnS QDs were obtained by ligand exchange with 3-mercaptopropionic acid (MPA). The modified QDs were functionalized with bovine serum albumin (BSA), and then, oxidized Nile blue was covalently linked to the BSA layer. The fluorescence of the QDs was quenched by the oxidized Nile blue through a FRET quenching mechanism. The Nile blue capping layer is reduced by the NAD(P)H cofactors to the colorless dihydro Nile blue product that does not quench the luminescence of the QDs. The reduction of the Nile blue capping layer by the NAD(P)H cofactors activated the fluorescence of the QDs, Fig. 21(A). This enabled the quantitative optical detection of NADH, Fig. 21(B) and (C). The NADH-sensitive QDs were further used as luminescent probes to follow NAD+-dependent enzymes, and their substrates.176

In another example, phenyl boronic acid-functionalized CdSe/ZnS quantum dots (QDs) were synthesized and modified by nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) or 1,4-dihydronicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH), through the formation of boronic acid ester functionalities. The quenching of the luminescence of the QDs by the NAD+ cofactor, via an ET quenching route, was found to be substantially more efficient than the quenching of the QDs by the reduced cofactor, NADH. This difference in the quenching features of the QDs by NAD+ or NADH was used to optically sense the trinitrotriazine, RDX explosive.177 The NADH, coordinated to the boronic acid-functionalized CdSe/ZnS QDs, reduced the explosive while transforming the capping monolayer to the NAD+ state that effectively quenched the luminescence of the QDs, Fig. 21(D). The changes in the fluorescence of the QDs enabled the analysis of RDX with a detection limit corresponding to 1 × 10−10 M. Interestingly, besides the photophysical functions of the QDs in the detection of the RDX, cooperative catalytic functions of the QDs in the NADH-mediated reduction of RDX were discovered. It was found that Zn2+-ions present in the shell layer of the CdSe/ZnS QDs, coordinated as a Lewis acid to the nitro groups of RDX, thus polarizing the nitro group and enabling the hydride transfer from NADH, Fig. 21D. Such catalytic functions of appropriately shell-modified QDs are anticipated to act cooperatively with the luminescence properties of the QDs, and allow the development of new sensing platforms.

A nitric oxide (NO) QD-based sensor was developed by the NO-stimulated ligand substitution of a transition metal complex associated with CdSe/ZnS QDs.178 The red-colored tris(N-(dithiocarboxy)carcosine)iron(III), [Fe(DTCS)3]3−, was linked to ammonium-capped QDs by ionic interactions. The functionalized QDs were reacted with NO by an electron transfer process, followed by ligand substitution, to yield the colorless paramagnetic bis(dithiocarbamato)nitrosyl iron(I) complex as a capping layer. This process triggered-on the luminescence of the QDs, thus enabling the detection of NO, Fig. 22.

7. Chemically-modified QDs as optical switches

The modification of QDs with photoisomerizable chromophores was implemented to reversibly switch the luminescence of QDs. Specifically, the spectral overlap of one of the photoisomerizable states of the chromophore with the luminescence spectra of the QDs resulted in the FRET quenching of the luminescence of the QDs. Mercaptoalkyl nitrospiropyran photoisomerizable units were used to modify CdSe/ZnS core–shell QDs,181Fig. 25(A).

(B) Fluorescence intensity at λ = 546 nm upon subjecting the functionalized QDs to alternating cycles of UV and visible light. The nitrospiropyran units (SP) undergo, under UV irradiation, photoisomerization to the colored zwitterionic nitromerocyanine state (MR), exhibiting absorbance band at λ = 590 nm. The latter photoisomer undergoes reverse photoisomerization to the SP state upon irradiation with visible light. CdSe/ZnS QDs that exhibited a CdSe core diameter of 2.6 nm and a ZnS shell layer of 1.7 nm thickness revealed optimized optical properties where the luminescence of the QDs (λem = 578 nm) overlaps with the absorbance of the MR photoisomer state. This led to an effective FRET quenching of the luminescence of the QDs (ca. 95%). By the cyclic photoisomerization of the photoisomerizable units between the SP and MR states, the reversible “ON” and “OFF” switching of the luminescence was demonstrated, Fig. 25(B). The chemical nanoengineering of the QDs and their diameter was found to affect the FRET quenching efficiency. By substituting the thiolated linkage of the nitrospiropyran units to the QDs with a dithiolane linker, and upon the application of smaller sized QDs that emitted at λ = 554 nm, the quenching efficiency decreased to ca. 45%.182 This was attributed to the poor spectral overlap between the luminescence of the QDs and the absorbance of the MR state, and to the lower coverage of the photoisomerizable units on the QDs, due to the large footprint area of the dithiolane linker.

Similarly, the biotin-modified hexafluorinated dithiophene ethylene derivative, (25), was attached to avidin-functionalized QDs via avidin–biotin affinity interactions.183 The dithiophene compound exhibits photoisomerizable properties where the open isomer, (25a), that lacks absorbance in the visible region, undergoes photochemical electrocyclization upon UV irradiation to form the closed isomer state, (25b). The latter isomer (25b) absorbs in the visible spectral region that overlaps with the luminescence of the QDs, Fig. 26(A), and the FRET quenching of the luminescence of the QDs may proceed. The reverse photoisomerization of (25b) to (25a) occurs upon irradiation of the system with visible light. Indeed, upon photoisomerization of the functionalized QDs between the states (25a) and (25b), the cyclic “ON” and “OFF” switching of the luminescence of the QDs was demonstrated, Fig. 26(B). The quenching efficiency was only ca. 35%, due to the large distance that separated the acceptor dye (25b) from the QDs.

8. Conclusions and perspectives

Substantial progress was accomplished in the past few years in the synthesis of chemically-modified QDs and their application for optical sensing of molecular analytes, ions (cations or anions) and for monitoring pH changes. Different chemical methodologies were used to prepare the chemically-modified QDs conjugates, and a variety of means to bind the analytes to the functionalized QDs were developed. These included the functionalization of the QDs with monolayer or thin film coatings consisting of ligands, receptors, transition metal complexes, and pH-sensitive or redox-active units. The QDs conjugates were then implemented for developing different optical sensor systems using fluorescence, resonance energy transfer (FRET) and luminescence electron transfer (ET) quenching as readout signals. While the synthesis and sensing applications of chemically-modified QDs for molecular sensing is already a mature research area, several scientific challenges are still ahead of us: (i) the design of new molecular functionalities for conjugations to the QDs could allow the sensing of other analytes. For example, the use of donor–acceptor interactions, H-bonds, or the conjugation of new receptor sites to the QDs could reach this goal. (ii) Until now, most of the studies implemented, separately, the unique photophysical properties of the QDs and the electrical, optical, and binding properties of the chemical modifier layers. Nonetheless, many of the QDs consist of core–shell nanostructures, and, thus, the shell layer may cooperatively participate in the binding or catalytic activation of the analyte towards the interaction with the QDs. Similarly, one may introduce catalytic units into the capping layer that activate the substrate analyte toward interaction with the QDs. Such examples are scarce,177 yet the existing examples suggest that this is a viable promising approach. (iii) Most of the chemically-modified QDs were used to develop sensor systems.176 Nonetheless, other applications may be envisaged. For example, the electrical control of the luminescence properties of QDs that are modified by redox-active capping layers may lead to new optical switching systems. Similarly, the immobilization of chemically-functionalized QDs on electrode surfaces may lead to new electronic devices, and particularly, to improved solar cells. For example, the chemical modification of QDs with electropolymerizable groups might assist the ordered deposition on electrodes, and by tailoring of the molecular chemical coating layer, improved charge separation and effective charge transport may be accomplished. (iv) The successful construction of molecular-based, chemically-functionalized QDs for monitoring different sensing processes suggests that these hybrid nanostructures may be soon adapted for different practical applications. For example, the use of the QDs as in vivo imaging reagents, or the formulation of the functionalized QDs in the form of sprays for the detection of trace analytes on surfaces or skin, e.g., explosives may be interesting paths to follow.

With the rapid advances in the area of chemically-modified QDs one may expect a bright future for this class of materials.
