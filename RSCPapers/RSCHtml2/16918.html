Abstract

Fused-pyrrole spacer groups have been exploited for the first time computationally to improve the efficiency of dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs). Known dye 1 (TPA-TTAR-T-A) containing tetrathienoacene as a spacer group has achieved 10% efficiency, which is comparable to that of ruthenium complex dyes. The DFT study reveals that fused-pyrrole as a spacer in dye 3 can augment the efficiency of DSSCs and can perform better than dye 1. The pyrrole ring possesses lower resonance energy (21.0 kcal molâˆ’1) than the commonly used thiophene (29â€“34 kcal molâˆ’1) spacers. The higher conjugative effect of fused-pyrrole rings influence the short-circuit current density (Jsc), including driving force of electrons (Î”Ginjection) and singlet excited state lifetime (Ï„). Further, open-circuit photovoltage (Voc), which depends on dipole moments (Î¼normal) and number of electrons transferred from the dye to the TiO2 surface (Î”q), are also influenced with such conjugative effects. The calculated Î»max (539 nm) and Î”Ginjection (âˆ’1.82 eV) of dye 3 is much higher than the dye 1 (Î»max = 497 nm and Î”Ginjection = âˆ’1.28 eV). The calculated results show that the singlet excited state lifetime of dye 3 (2.2 ns) is higher than the dye 1 (1.7 ns). Dye 3 should have better Jsc value than 1 as the obtained Î”Ginjection, Ï„ and Î»max values are higher in the former case. The DFT calculations also suggest that the open-circuit photovoltage (Voc) of 3 would be higher than 1 and hence can augment the efficiency of the designed dye 3. The study was extended with tetrapyrrolobenzothiadiazole spacer groups (dyes 6 and 8) which are also shown to be promising candidates to improve the efficiencies of DSSCs. Importantly, the tendency of aggregation of dyes can also be prevented by introducing bulky groups in the fused-pyrrole ring which can also help to enhance the efficiency of designed dyes relative to reported dyes. The exploration of novel sensitizers with fused-pyrrole spacer groups would provide potential candidates for DSSCs.

Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSCs) technology has attracted considerable attention during the past two decades for new generation photovoltaic devices to provide electricity.1 The mostly used silicon-based cells, although efficient, are also very expensive.2 On the other hand, DSSCs can be made more affordable in comparison to those of conventional inorganic semiconductor photovoltaics.3 In DSSCs, the dyes play a critical role i.e., to determine the light harvesting properties, interaction with the semiconductor/redox couple and charge carriers.4â€“8 Various molecular sensitizers have been developed to achieve high power conversion efficiency.9â€“12 Sensitizers possessing ruthenium-complexes and Zn-porphyrin dyes have shown significantly higher efficiencies, which provide PCEs of 12.5â€“13.0% when employing a Co(II/III) redox shuttle as the electrolyte system.13â€“15 To broaden our understanding of DSSCs function and performance limitations, metal-free organic photosensitizers have attracted great attention owing to their modest cost, ease of synthesis and modification, large molar absorption coefficients, and also satisfactory stability.2,16,17 Nonetheless, the performance of DSSCs based on metal-free organic dyes has lagged behind those using traditional metal organic dyes due to their high recombination losses and lower open circuit voltages (Voc).18

The design of dyes demands consideration of multiple optoelectronic properties, such as band gap modulation, optical absorption coefficient, as well as dye aggregation, morphology and assembly on TiO2 photoanode.19 Organic dyes are commonly constructed with donorâ€“Ï€ bridgeâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“Ï€â€“A) frameworks because of their efficient intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) properties and other requirements.20 For further improving the photovoltaic efficiency it is necessary to the tailor the band gap energy, broaden the molecular absorption spectrum and increase the charge carrier mobility.21 Reports reveal that minor changes in the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A structure can change the photophysical, electrochemical properties of the dyes.22

Therefore, the design of molecular sensitizers depends on the appropriate selection of donor group and anchoring group to achieve better efficiency of solar cells. The choice of anchoring group also plays an important role in the efficiency of the DSSCs.23 The comparison of anchoring groups such as carboxylic groups and phosphonic acid has been documented where high symmetry of the anchoring group was highlighted to augment the efficiency of solar cells.24 Metal containing organic dyes have been examined with the modulation of anchoring groups, role of bridge dipole and redox active centers.25 Further, the role of the linker used in such metal containing dyes was also explored. Increases in the length in the Ï€-bridge hampered the efficiency of DSSCs.26 Efforts have been made to prepare Dâ€“Ï€â€“A dyes with various donors such as coumarin, carbazole, triphenylamine, diphenylamine, phenothiazine, indoline, azacyclazine and fluorenylamine.27â€“33 Recently, Wang et al. demonstrated that dyes containing an N-annulated indenoperylene electron donor achieved very high efficiency.34â€“38 We have also reported on the role of donor groups to improve the efficiency of DSSCs.39,40 It is also reported that the steric effect of the electron donor groups plays an important role in the optical, electrochemical and photovoltaic properties of the dyes.

For this class of dyes, the focus has been to modulate the Ï€-conjugated spacer units which help the charge transfer from the ground to excited state and to increase light absorption.41â€“44 Heterocycles and their derivatives are used as Ï€-bridges in the dyes due to their excellent conjugated structures.45,46 Thiophene systems such as oligothiophenes, thiophene-vinylenes and fused thiophenes have been exploited to achieve enhanced efficiencies.47,48 Moreover, a large number of conjugated groups such as carbazole, fluorene and spirobifluorene, have also been used as Ï€-bridges.49â€“55 Extended bridges induce advantages that lead to smaller band gaps for extended light absorption and better matching with the energetics of the Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox shuttle and thereby improve the DSSCs charge transfer characteristics. The thiophene group has been proved to be a good Ï€ conjugated spacer to increase light harvesting efficiency by inducing a bathochromic shift and strengthening the intensity of absorption, as well as being an excellent constitutional unit commonly used in high performance carrier transport materials. Recently, selenophene and tellurophene were also studied as Ï€ spacers in polymer solar cells.56 Thiophenes as Ï€-conjugated spacer units has been extensively used in DSSCs, however, we note that this five-membered heterocyclic ring is more aromatic than pyrrole; the resonance energy calculated for thiophene is 29â€“34 kcal molâˆ’1 and for pyrrole is 21.0 kcal molâˆ’1.57 The lower resonance energy of the latter suggests that the pyrrole unit would be more amenable to facilitate the charge transfer from the ground state to the excited state and to increase light absorption i.e., one of the important properties to achieve higher efficiency for DSSCs. This information prompted us to exploit fused-pyrroles as Ï€-conjugated spacer units to design Dâ€“Ï€â€“A dyes for DSSCs.

There are a few reports on the use of pyrrole as a Ï€-bridge in DSSCs.58â€“62 Diketopyrrolopyrrole has been used in polymer solar cells due to its high fluorescence quantum yield and high thermal stability.63 However, pyrrole as Ï€-spacer unit in DSSCs is less explored. In this article, we have exploited three heterocycles (tetrathienoacene, a fused thiopheneâ€“pyrrole containing ring system and tetrapyrroloacene) as Ï€-bridges in DSSCs. We have computationally examined the optical properties and photovoltaic performances of the dyes attached to such Ï€-spacer units. The tetrathienoacene and fused thiopheneâ€“pyrrole containing ring systems have been experimentally tested for their efficiency.64,65 The designed tetrapyrroloacene Ï€-bridges should have better efficiency in DSSCs than the tetrathienoacene and fused thiopheneâ€“pyrrole units with same donor groups and can be an alternative choice of Ï€-spacer.

Results and discussion

The Ï€-conjugated bridge is considered to be one of the key factors for the enhanced performance of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A structure DSSCs. The appropriate choice of Ï€-spacers between the donor and acceptor groups is essential for efficient light harvesting and to reduce the HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap. In order to screen the excellent Ï€-conjugated bridges of triphenylamine and phenothiazine derivative dyes, we have designed a series of metal-free organic dyes based on the reported dyes [TPA-TTAR-T-A (dye 1), DTP4 (dye 5) and DTP3 (dye 7)].64,65 The reported dye TPA-TTAR-T-A exhibited âˆ¼10% efficiency, which is a high value for a metal-free organic dyes. Tetrathienoacene is used as a Ï€-spacer in dye 1 (Fig. 1). Thiophene or thienothiophene bridges are commonly used in DSSCs because of their electron transfer ability and remarkable efficiencies.47 The highly efficient TPA-TTAR-T-A dye containing tetrathienoacene as spacer group can be used as an alternative spacer group to the commonly used thiophene or thienothiophene. We have replaced the X2 and X4 sulfur atoms in the tetrathienoacene spacer group with the NH group i.e. thiophene is replaced by pyrrole group (dye 2, Fig. 1). The sulfur, nitrogen-heteroacene is then studied as an alternative Ï€-spacer. Efforts are underway to prepare S,NH-heteroacenes that can be extended up to the decacene level.90 Furthermore, we have replaced all the thiophene units with pyrrole units as Ï€-spacers in this study (dye 3, Fig. 1). In the next series we have replaced the thiophene group of the reported spacer group dithienopyrrolobenzothiadiazole (DTPBT, dye 5) with pyrrole groups (dye 6, Fig. 1). The lower resonance energy for the pyrrole unit compared to the thiophene would help to augment the electron transfer and optical properties and hence can function as a better dye system for DSSCs. The advantage of the pyrrole moiety as a spacer in Dâ€“Ï€â€“A systems is that this moiety hinders the dye aggregation, which significantly influences the electrochemical and photovoltaic properties of dyes, which is not possible with thiophene Ï€-spacer groups.

Structure and frontier molecular orbitals

The geometries of the dye molecules (Fig. 1) calculated with B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level of theory showed some interesting trends in their geometrical properties. The Ï€-conjugated group serves as a bridge for the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) in the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A dye systems. The bonds between D/Ï€-spacer and A/Ï€-spacer give an account of the interactions within themselves. Shorter lengths of bridging bonds benefits the ICT in the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A molecules. The selective bond lengths d1, d2, d3 and d4 of the studied dye molecules (Scheme 1) are shown in Table 2. The calculated bond lengths of dyes 1â€“4 revealed that the d1 single bond length is relatively shortened with introduction of fused-pyrrole moiety in the spacer group (Table 2). The d1 bond length of dye 1 is longer than the dye 2 (Table 2). The d1 bond length shortened significantly in dye 3 in this series (Table 2). The presence of the fused-pyrrole moiety facilitates the conjugation of electrons and increases the â€“CCâ€“ double bond length (d3) and decreases the Câ€“C single bond lengths (d2, d4). The fused-pyrrole nitrogen atom not only participates in the conjugation but also donates its lone pair of electrons and helps in the conjugation.59 Enhanced conjugation is observed for dyes 6 and 8 relative to their corresponding dyes 5 and 7, respectively (Table 2). The degree of conjugation can favour the ICT process, which is an important factor to influence the performance of dye molecules in DSSCs.

The intramolecular charge transfer behaviour is governed by the energy gaps of frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs), which are responsible for the absorption spectrum for the studied systems.91,92 Typically, smaller energy gaps lead to longer absorption wavelength.93 As shown in Fig. 2, for the investigated dyes (1â€“8), the HOMO orbital is localized over the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A part whereas the LUMO orbital is localized over the Ï€â€“A part. The FMOs suggest that all the dyes have strong Dâ€“Ï€â€“A character. The energy levels of HOMOs and LUMOs were examined to evaluate the electron injection driving force in these cases. It has been shown that the HOMO levels of the studied dyes are sufficiently lower than the redox potential of Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ (âˆ’4.80 eV)94 which ensure the restoring of electrons from the electrolyte (Fig. 3). Further, all dyes have the ability of electron injection property to the conduction band of TiO2 as the LUMO levels are above the conduction band.94 For dyes 1â€“4, the calculated HOMO energy levels suggest that the dye 1 possesses a relatively higher energy level compared to the systems containing pyrrole units. The HOMO energy levels are in the order: dye 1 (âˆ’6.43 eV) > dye 2 (âˆ’6.11 eV) > dye 3 (âˆ’5.54 eV) < dye 4 (âˆ’5.75 eV). Therefore, restoring the electron from the electrolyte would be better facilitated with fused-pyrrole units as Ï€-spacer groups. The rise in the LUMO levels with the use of fused-pyrrole units in the Ï€-spacer group would facilitate the electron injection process. The LUMO energy levels are in the order: 1 (âˆ’2.11 eV) > dye 2 (âˆ’1.90 eV) > dye 3 (âˆ’1.42 eV) < dye 4 (âˆ’1.44 eV) (Fig. 3). The tripyrroloacene as Ï€-spacer unit with biphenyl amine as a donor group (dye 4) also lowered the HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap compared with dye 1. This result further infers that even a smaller number of fused-pyrrole units as Ï€-spacers can augment the efficiency of DSSCs. The dyes 5â€“8 containing dithienopyrrolobenzothiadiazole as spacer group also showed that the HOMO and LUMO energy gaps can be tuned appropriately using fused-pyrrole units to enhance the Î»max (Fig. 3).

Factors influencing Jsc and Voc

To seek out the relationship between the replacement of sulfur atoms with â€“NH groups and the efficiency, we have calculated the electron injection efficiency (Î”Ginjection), light harvesting efficiency (LHE), excited state lifetime (Ï„), dipole moment (Î¼normal) and number of electrons transferred from dyes to the TiO2 surface (Î”q). From eqn (2) (Computational section) it is clear that the Jsc value of a dye increases with increasing the LHE and Î”Ginjection value. The excited state lifetime (Ï„) of the excited state can be a useful factor to estimate the efficiency of electron injection. A dye with a longer lifetime in the excited state is expected to undergo charge transfer more easily and so can enhance the Jsc value. The excited state lifetimes of the dyes can be evaluated using the equation Ï„ = 1.499/fE2, where E is the excitation energy of the different electronic states (cmâˆ’1) and f is the oscillator strength of the electronic state.95 The results of all the examined dyes are presented in Table 4. Nonetheless, even the lower homologue of tetrapyrroloacene, i.e. tripyrroloacene spacer group, also provided a better Î”Ginjection value than the dye 1. The other studied dyes 5â€“8 also demonstrated the same trend as observed for dyes 1â€“4. Conclusions

The spacer groups in dye molecules can play a pivotal role to modulate the energy levels and absorption spectra to influence the efficiency of dye-sensitized solar cells. In this work, we have reported computationally a series of metal-free organic dyes based on a Dâ€“Ï€â€“A skeleton, which would improve the efficiency of DSSCs. The reported dye (TPA-TTAR-T-A, dye 1) containing tetrathienoacene as a spacer group already shows very high efficiency (10%).64 We have employed fused-pyrrole as spacer units in the designed dye molecules, which can narrow the HOMOâ€“LUMO energy gap compared to thiophene spacer groups as the resonance energy is lower in the former case. The calculated maximum absorption wavelength of dye 3 containing a fused-pyrrole moiety (539 nm) is much longer than for dye 1 (497 nm). Even with a lesser number of pyrrole units as spacer group, i.e. dye 4, can also exhibit a much longer maximum absorption wavelength (518 nm) than 1. The calculated parameters such as electron injection driving force (Î”Ginjection) and singlet excited state lifetime (Ï„) are closely connected with the short-circuit current density (Jsc). The DFT results show that the electron injection driving force (Î”Ginjection) is more favoured in the case of dyes 3 and 4 (âˆ’1.82 and âˆ’1.74 eV) than for 1 (âˆ’1.28 eV).
