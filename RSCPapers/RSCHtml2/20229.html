Abstract

Localized surface plasmon resonances (LSPRs) associated with metallic nanostructures offer unique possibilities for light concentration beyond the diffraction limit, which can lead to strong field confinement and enhancement in deep subwavelength regions. In recent years, many transformative plasmonic applications have emerged, taking advantage of the spectral and spatial tunability of LSPRs enabled by near-field coupling between constituent metallic nanostructures in a variety of plasmonic metastructures (dimers, metamolecules, metasurfaces, metamaterials, etc.). For example, the “hot spot” formed at the interstitial site (gap) between two coupled metallic nanostructures in a plasmonic dimer can be spectrally tuned via the gap size. Capitalizing on these capabilities, there have been significant advances in plasmon enhanced or enabled applications in light-based science and technology, including ultrahigh-sensitivity spectroscopies, light energy harvesting, photocatalysis, biomedical imaging and theranostics, optical sensing, nonlinear optics, ultrahigh-density data storage, as well as plasmonic metamaterials and metasurfaces exhibiting unusual linear and nonlinear optical properties. In this review, we present two complementary approaches for fabricating plasmonic metastructures. We discuss how meta-atoms can be assembled into unique plasmonic metastructures using a variety of nanomanipulation methods based on single- or multiple-probes in an atomic force microscope (AFM) or a scanning electron microscope (SEM), optical tweezers, and focused electron-beam nanomanipulation. We also provide a few examples of nanoparticle metamolecules with designed properties realized in such well-controlled plasmonic metastructures. For the spatial controllability on the mesoscopic and macroscopic scales, we show that controlled self-assembly is the method of choice to realize scalable two-dimensional, and three-dimensional plasmonic metastructures. In the section of applications, we discuss some key examples of plasmonic applications based on individual hot spots or ensembles of hot spots with high uniformity and improved controllability.

1. Introduction

Metallic nanostructures excited by photons or electrons under appropriate conditions exhibit strong resonant absorption and scattering properties. These fascinating phenomena have been understood to originate from collective oscillations of conduction electrons relative to the static ionic background,1–4 which are described as the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) modes in metallic nanostructures (Fig. 1a). Since ancient times, the unique optical properties associated with ultrasmall noble-metal nanoparticles (NPs) have been utilized to produce colorful glass art works, such as the famous Lycurgus cup displayed in the British Museum (a dichroic, ruby red (transmission)/green (reflection) glass cup made by the Romans in the 4th century AD) and antique stained glass windows of European cathedrals made with colloidal suspensions of gold and silver NPs (AuNPs, AgNPs) in the medieval period.5 Plasmonics,2 however, is a term coined recently to describe various approaches to manipulate light at subwavelength length scales using collective electron motions excited by light at the metal–dielectric interfaces (surface plasmon polaritons, or SPPs, as illustrated in Fig. 1b), as well as in individual or coupled metallic nanostructures. Interest in plasmonics is surging in recent years because of many important advances in related fields, including chemical synthesis methods for large-scale production of size- and shape-controlled metal NPs (spherical or quasi-spherical shape) and nanocrystals (NCs, polyhedral shape) with tunable plasmonic properties,6–12 advances in bottom-up self-assembly methods,13–19 top-down nanofabrication techniques based on electron and ion beams,20–22 and rapidly improving computation tools allowing the simulations of near-field and far-field electromagnetic fields for relative complex nanostructure geometries.23,24
Fig. 1 (a) Localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) modes of a silver nanosphere (diameter: 50 nm) and a silver nanorod (diameter: 30 nm, length: 230 nm), (b) illustration of electric field distribution (left panel) and dispersion relation (right panel) of propagating surface plasmon polaritons (SPPs) on a silver film.

The presence of localized plasmon resonances in metallic nanostructures makes it possible to concentrate the incident light beyond the diffraction limit, leading to optical field confinement and enhancement in deep subwavelength regions.25–27 The LSPR modes in metallic nanostructures are very efficient in capturing incident light due to a large effective cross-section, as illustrated in Fig. 2a.28 Under resonant illumination by a plane wave, the flow of field lines (i.e. the Poynting vector) is directed toward and into the metal NP. This phenomenon produces an antenna effect, resulting in light concentration from an area that is much larger than the physical size of NP or NC. In addition, plasmon-induced hot carriers29–31 enable efficient optical energy conversions in versatile ways, including photothermal,32–34 photochemical,31,35,36 photocatalytic,30,37,38 and photoelectric29–31,39 processes. To highlight just a few exciting applications, plasmon-enhanced ultrasensitive spectroscopy,40–44 photothermal cancer therapeutics,31,33,45 solar energy conversion and harvesting for photovoltaics and solar fuel generation,29–31,39,45 solar-driven water purification,46,47 plasmonic sensors,48–52 detectors,53,54 and heat-assisted ultrahigh-density data storage55,56 have been demonstrated in the past few years. Moreover, optical information processing based on nonlinear plasmonics,57–59 active plasmonics,60–64 magnetoplasmonics,65–68 and quantum plasmonics69 have recently been proposed and pursued.

Taking surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy or scattering (SERS) as a prime example in plasmonics40–44,70–72 we can immediately recognize the importance of plasmonic “hot spots” in metallic nanostructures for all of the plasmonic applications mentioned above. Fig. 2 shows log-scale color plots of the computed SERS enhancement factor (EF) for an elongated silver NP (spheroid, see Fig. 2b) exhibiting two hot spots at the tips, whereas a spatially more localized and electromagnetic “hotter” region can be obtained in a gap hot spot formed between two near-field-coupled silver nanospheres (plasmonic dimer, Fig. 2c73). Due to the strong dependence of the EF on NP shape (tip hot spot) and gap distance (gap hot spot), it is clear that uniform hot-spot properties across the SERS substrate are necessary for reproducible results. However, due to the stochastic nature of hot spots on conventional SERS substrates, comprised of randomly formed (e.g. nanoroughened noble-metal films) or lithographically patterned noble-metal nanostructures, only part of the analytes can be accounted for, making quantitative SERS measurements very challenging.74–77 To date, most of the plasmonic applications are related to the control and generation of plasmonic hot spots, which requires the design and fabrication of metallic nanostructure ensembles with tunable optical and plasmonic properties. In this review, the major themes are devoted toward this direction.

Several reviews have been written on various subjects of plasmonics.78–83 In the current review we focus on how to create plasmonic metastructures, such as clusters (zero-dimensional (0D) and one-dimensional (1D) metamolecules), two-dimensional (2D) metasurfaces, and three-dimensional (3D) metamaterials (or supercrystals) using colloidal metal NPs and NCs as the fundamental building blocks (plasmonic meta-atoms). Here, metastructures are defined as artificially structured media composed of subwavelength-sized meta-atoms with tunable optical (plasmonic) properties and interaction strength (plasmonic coupling). Importantly, the electromagnetic response of the entire metastructure arises from the collective responses of constituent meta-atoms. Recently, metamaterials84–88 and metasurfaces89–99 have enabled control over the flow of light, providing the possibility of realizing artificial optical media with unconventional indices of refraction ranging from the negative index,100 the near-zero index (i.e. near-zero effective permittivity or epsilon-near-zero)101–105 to the ultrahigh index.106 Ultimately, these novel artificial optical media could revolutionize the science and technology of photonics in the important areas of super-resolution imaging,100,107 nanolithography,107 integrated nanophotonics,45 nonlinear photonics,108–113 transformation optics,114,115 and so on. In this review, we aim to answer the following questions: What are the challenges one faces in nanomanipulation and large-scale self-assembly? And what are the new functionalities one can realize by taking advantage of the tunable coupling among the plasmonic meta-atoms?

This review is organized in the following way. In Section 2, we introduce the fundamental plasmonic properties of metal NPs, including basic mechanisms and important material issues. In Section 3, we review various chemical synthesis methods, as well as surface functionalization and passivation techniques, for size- and shape-controlled colloidal NPs and NCs (gold, silver, and related composite nanomaterials). Some recent developments for synthesizing large-scale, single-crystalline colloidal metal plates are also presented, which allow the fabrication of plasmonic structures by top-down nanofabrication techniques based on a high-quality material platform.113 Then, in Section 4 we introduce the theoretical concepts of plasmonic coupling and their relevance in controlling and engineering plasmonic hot spots. We then explain how experimentally we can control and observe near-field coupling among plasmonic NPs and NCs in order to create new properties and functionalities. In this review, we present both top-down and bottom-up approaches for fabricating plasmonic metastructures. In Section 5, we discuss how NP clusters can be assembled using a variety of manipulation methods, including mechanical manipulation by probe tips using an atomic force microscope (AFM) or in a scanning electron microscope (SEM), as well as using optical-beam- and electron-beam-based tweezers. We provide a few examples of NP clusters with designed properties realized in plasmonic NP clusters assembled using manipulation methods. Controlled self-assembly is the method of choice to realize scalable 1D, 2D, and 3D plasmonic metastructures with programmable plasmonic coupling. We devote Section 6 to various NP/NC self-assembly techniques and optical characterization techniques for these plasmonic metastructures. In Section 7, we highlight a few important applications enabled by plasmonic metastructures. Finally, we finish this review by offering a few remarks and perspectives on the remaining challenges and potential applications.

2. Meta-atom building block: fundamentals

The significant progress of colloidal chemistry in reproducible synthesis of the size-, shape-, and composition-controlled metal NPs and NCs, as well as the advances in ultrahigh-resolution lithographic techniques have contributed greatly to the rapid development of plasmonics in the past few decades. For example, it is now possible to synthesize size- and shaped-controlled gold, silver, and related heterogeneous NPs and NCs in large quantities using a variety of chemical methods.6–8 The precise control of their size, shape, and composition allows us to precisely tailor their plasmonic resonances.116 In addition, surface functionalization schemes of NPs and NCs make them promising candidates as building blocks for the bottom-up assembly of plasmonic metastructures. Alternatively, complicated plasmonic nanostructures and metastructures can also be designed and fabricated by top-down lithographic techniques using high-quality metal thin films117–120 or large-scale colloidal metal crystals.113,121–123 Considering practical applications, facile large-area fabrication of NP arrays and films is also very desirable. Recently, both laser124–126 and microwave plasma127 processing techniques have been developed to provide new possibilities for this purpose. In this section, we present first the theoretical background for understanding the LSPR properties of metal NPs (spherical or quasi-spherical, typically polycrystalline). Both the electrodynamic modeling of spherical metal particles by Mie theory and the quasi-static approximation for metal NPs are introduced for a general understanding of plasmon resonances in metal NPs. We then discuss some material issues for plasmonic applications, including intrinsic optical properties of fundamental plasmonic metals (gold, silver, copper, and aluminum) and the importance of material quality due to the differences in growth and synthesis techniques. In Section 3, we will review various colloidal chemistry approaches to synthesize gold and silver NPs and NCs in different sizes and shapes and with different surface functionalization techniques.

2.1. Mie theory and quasi-static approximation

In 1908, Gustav Mie published a seminal paper on the calculations of scattering and internal fields of a spherical particle in a homogenous medium.128 The importance of the Mie theory is that it can give analytic solutions of Maxwell's equations for spherical, ellipsoidal, and cylindrical scattering geometries in homogenous media.3,129,130 Moreover, it can be used to explain the peculiar optical phenomena observed by Michael Faraday for a suspension solution of gold colloids.131 Only in a few cases, particularly for NPs smaller than ∼2 nm (i.e. the nonlocality length5), quantum mechanical calculations are necessary to achieve more accurate results. In the Mie theory, the scattering and internal fields are expanded as infinite series of vector spherical harmonic functions. Therefore, the computation based on the Mie theory is complicated and a large number of terms (dipole, quadrupole, octupole, and other higher-order modes) is required for accurate calculations of particle properties with a large size parameter x = ka, where a is the particle radius and k = εdk0 is the wavenumber in the surrounding medium.129

Fortunately, in the case that the spherical particle size is much smaller than the wavelength of light in the surrounding medium (i.e. particle diameter D = 2a ≪ λ), the quasi-static approximation can be applied to understand the basic optical properties of metal NPs (D < 100 nm) in the visible and near infrared (NIR) regions.2,132,133 In particular, it is instructive to understand the origin of plasmon resonance and why it is sensitive to the refractive index of the surrounding medium. In this size regime, the phase of the harmonically oscillating electromagnetic field is practically constant over the whole NP volume, so that one can calculate the spatial electromagnetic field distribution by using the electrostatic equation. The harmonic time dependence (e−iωt) can then be added to the electrostatic solution.

Recognizing that εm′ < 0 and |εm′| ≫ εm′′ for good metals and assuming that εm′′(ω) does not change much in the vicinity of the resonance, the resonance condition means that a negative εm′ is necessary and εm′(ω) = −2εd, which corresponds to the resonance condition for the dipole LSPR mode. One important consequence of the resonance condition is the strong dependence on the dielectric environment, such that the resonance redshifts as εd is increased. As a result, metal NPs are the ideal platform for optical sensing of small changes in the refractive index of surrounding medium (LSPR sensors).

(ii) The magnitude of the cross-sections at resonance is limited by a non-vanishing imaginary part of ε(ω), i.e. Im[ε(ω)] = ε′′(ω) ≠ 0. For metals with very small ε′′ (e.g. silver), the plasmon resonance is strong with a large cross-section and a high resonance quality factor Q. In addition, material quality also plays an important role, which will be discussed in Section 2.2.

(iii) In comparison to the scattering cross-section, the absorption cross-section has a different size scaling relation such that σabs scales with a3, whereas σsca scales with a6. Consequently, for large metal NPs (roughly D > 130 nm and D > 60 nm for gold and silver NPs, respectively133) extinction is dominated by scattering, and for small metal particles absorption is more dominant. As a result, large metal particles (D > 100 nm) are preferred for the observation and manipulation of single metal particles using far-field optical techniques.134–136

Mie theory and quasi-static approximation give us useful insights into how metal NPs behave in terms of wave scattering and absorbing in an incident electromagnetic field. However, a precise quantitative analysis of plasmonic properties would require a detailed knowledge of comprising material. In the next section, we will discuss how intrinsic properties and growth/synthesis quality affect the plasmonic properties of metal NPs.

2.2. Material issues

2.2.1. Intrinsic properties. Here, we focus on the intrinsic optical properties of four fundamental plasmonic metals, gold (Au), silver (Ag), copper (Cu), and aluminum (Al), which have low plasmonic losses εm′′ related to their low resistivity (high electric conductivity). Fig. 3 shows dielectric functions for Au, Ag, Cu, and Al in the ultraviolet (UV), visible, and NIR spectral regions based on reported experimental data reported in the literature.119,137,138 As shown in Fig. 3b, Ag can be considered the best plasmonic metal since the imaginary part of its dielectric function is very small over the complete visible and NIR wavelength range. However, in the UV region at wavelengths below 300 nm, Al is a better plasmonic metal than Ag, and in the red and NIR regions both Au and Cu are reasonably good plasmonic metals. For long-wavelength applications, such as surface-enhanced infrared absorption and thermal imaging in the mid-infrared (mid-IR) spectral region, low-loss surface phonon polariton modes supported by polar dielectric crystals (e.g. silicon carbide) are interesting alternatives to the metal-based plasmonic systems.139

In Fig. 3, we can also find that interband transitions play an important role in the deviation of the dielectric functions from a simple Drude model.2 For noble metals, interband transitions have a threshold behavior. When the incident photons have a photon energy (wavelength) higher (shorter) than ∼2.5 eV (∼500 nm) for Au and ∼4.0 eV (∼300 nm) for Ag, they will predominantly excite electron–hole pairs.140–143 In Au, this phenomenon results from a transition between the 5d band (filled band) and the hybridized 6sp band (unfilled conduction band), starting from energies higher than ∼2.5 eV. This threshold behavior means that plasmon resonances can be observed only for incident photon energies lower than the interband transition energy (red and NIR for Au). In contrast, Al has a narrow-band behavior in its interband transition, such that it can exhibit surface plasmon resonances at wavelengths longer or shorter than the interband transition band at ∼1.5 eV (∼800 nm).144,145

To date, Au and Ag are two most used metals for plasmonic applications due to their low plasmonic losses in the optical frequency regime. However, due to the presence of interband transitions, Au and Ag cannot be used in the UV range, where Al exhibits both εm′ < 0 and a relatively low εm′′. This is why Al is such an appealing material for UV-plasmonics.145–148 Moreover, Al has other additional advantages, such as natural abundance, low cost, and excellent manufacturability. Recently, Al and Cu are becoming increasingly important for complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) compatible plasmonic applications.149,150 Besides Au, Ag, Cu, and Al, there is also increasing interest in alternative plasmonic materials based on transition metal nitrides (e.g. titanium nitride,151,152 zirconium nitride, chromium nitride, hafnium nitride), transparent conducting oxides (indium tin oxide,153,154 doped zinc oxide,155etc.), self-doped (p type) copper(I) sulphide (Cu2−xS) and copper(I) selenide (Cu2−xSe) semiconductor nanocrystals,156,157 and graphene.158,159 We would like to refer the reader to available reviews in the literature for more detailed information about these alternative plasmonic materials.160,161

Besides their intrinsic optical properties, there are also other important material considerations for plasmonic applications. We summarize relevant material information in the following:

Au: although Ag has a better plasmonic performance, Au is still the plasmonic material of choice due to its excellent chemical stability and biological compatibility. At present, a variety of chemical synthesis methods are available to synthesize size- and shape-controlled Au NPs and NCs in large quantities.6,7 Moreover, various versatile and facile surface functionalization schemes6,7 have been developed for Au NPs and NCs, making them the most popular building blocks for the bottom-up assembly of plasmonic metastructures. However, similar to Ag, Au suffers from high material cost and incompatibility with the CMOS processes. Also, the material quality of polycrystalline Au thin films is important in fabricating plasmonic nanostructures with high reproducibility using top-down lithographic techniques.

Ag: Ag has the best performance in the full visible spectral region. The imaginary part of the dielectric function of Ag is about one order of magnitude smaller that of Au and Cu in the visible frequency range. In addition, synthesis and functionalization of Ag nanocrystals with various sizes and shapes have been demonstrated by colloidal chemistry.8 It is well documented that polycrystalline Ag thin film growth is susceptible to dewetting and grain-boundary pinning issues.120 Moreover, Ag nanostructures and thin films are known to easily degrade in ambient air by sulfidation and/or oxidation.8 It has been demonstrated that this issue can be overcome by deposition of a surface passivation layer (e.g. conformal alumina (Al2O3) or silica (SiO2) coating by atomic layer deposition (ALD)) on top of the Ag surface.113

Cu: Cu has the second-best conductivity among metals (only next to Ag). Therefore, Cu is expected to exhibit promising plasmonic properties. Indeed, εm′′ of Cu is comparable to that of gold in the visible red and NIR regions. Considering the high material cost of Au and Ag, Cu could be a good candidate to replace Au and Ag as a plasmonic material if the performance of Cu is acceptable. In comparison to Ag, Cu has the advantage of weaker tendency to dewet, leading to smoother film morphology.120 For commercial applications, it is also important to realize that Cu is used extensively in CMOS devices. However, the complication is that fabricating plasmonic devices with Cu is challenging because it easily oxidizes under ambient conditions. Recently, it has been demonstrated that this issue can also be overcome by adding a passivation oxide layer (SiO2) on top of the Cu surface.150

Al: Al is a promising material for commercial plasmonic applications (especially in the UV region) despite its large εm′′ values in the visible region. Moreover, it has been reported that Al performs very well for applications in metal-enhanced fluorescence,162 deep-UV Raman scattering,163 nonlinear plasmonics,164–166 and CMOS-compatible color filters.149,167,168 Al is extremely reactive and highly sensitive to residual gases in the film deposition chamber.120 Published results showed that a native oxide layer is formed on a pristine Al surface within a few hours of air exposure, with a thickness stabilizing at 2.5–3.0 nm.146 The oxide layer can remain stable for a very long period of time, indicating that the native oxide acts as a passivation layer preventing further oxidation. No bulk oxidation is therefore observed, in striking contrast to the oxidation of Ag. Recently, the successful synthesis of high-quality Al nanocrystals has been demonstrated by McClain et al.169

It has been found that material quality strongly depends on synthesis, deposition, and fabrication processes.113,117–120,123 For example, although Au and Ag are the preferred material choices for low-loss plasmonics in the visible region, most of Au and Ag plasmonic nanostructures fabricated by lithographic methods are based on granular or polycrystalline films, where surface roughness and grain boundaries lead to strong scattering losses and short SPP propagation length. 3. Synthesis of size- and shape-controlled metal nanoparticles and nanocrystals

The unique plasmonic properties of colloidal metal NPs and NCs depend strongly on their size, shape, and composition.116,130,179 Furthermore, the bottom-up assembly techniques require building blocks with precisely controlled geometry, symmetry, and functionalities. Therefore, narrow size and shape distributions are necessary not only to control the particle plasmonic properties (e.g. resonance peak position and shape), but also to enable the bottom-up assembly of plasmonic metastructures using metal nanocrystals as meta-atoms. Otherwise, it would be very difficult to realize plasmonic metastructures with designable and reproducible properties since the plasmonic resonances and hot spots are very sensitive to detailed structural features of metal meta-atoms, such as facets, edges, vertices, and surface roughness, as well as their overall size and shape. In this section, we will focus on the size- and shape-controlled synthesis of Au and Ag NPs and NCs because of their excellent plasmonic properties and the availability of facile chemical synthesis methods. We will present two techniques for (1) spherical, polycrystalline metal NPs synthesized by the citrate–tannic method (Au NPs) and surface functionalized with alkanethiols by the Brust method (Au and Ag NPs) and (2) shape-controlled Au and Ag NCs synthesized by the polyol process. Additionally, we will give a simple overview of available surface functionalization schemes.

3.1. Citrate/tannic-acid and Brust methods for metal nanoparticles

The most widely used chemical routes for the synthesis of size-controlled, water-soluble AuNPs are as follows: (1) citrate method of Turkevich et al.,180–183 (2) citrate/tannic-acid method.184,185 In both methods, chloroauric acid (H[AuCl4]) is used as the gold precursor. The advantage of the second method is that the addition of tannic acid allows a precise NP size control by using a single parameter (i.e. the concentration of tannic acid). In the citrate/tannic-acid method, tetrachloroaurate ions ([AuCl4]−) are reduced in an aqueous solution by trisodium citrate and tannic acid, with both acting as the reducing agents of tetrachloroaurate ions and the stabilization agents preventing the AuNPs from aggregation. Using the citrate/tannic acid method and varying only the tannic acid concentration, the mean diameters of synthesized AuNPs in different aqueous solutions can be controlled from 3 to 20 nm with a typical size deviation less than 10%. The size monodispersity achieved by the citrate–tannic acid method is an important first step for the self-assembly of plasmonic metastructures using colloidal AuNPs.

The second important step to assemble NP metastructures is a reliable and versatile surface functionalization scheme for metal NPs. In 1994, Brust and Schiffrin186 introduced a two-phase (two immiscible phases) method to produce small (∼3–6 nm in diameter) AuNPs capped with alkanethiols (CH3(CH2)nSH) in an organic solvent (e.g. toluene). The NP products of the Brust method are very stable, which can be repeatedly dried and re-dispersed in organic solvents. In the conventional one-step, two-phase Brust method for the synthesis of AuNPs with a protection monolayer of alkanethiols, the tetrachloroaurate ions in the aqueous solution are transferred to the organic solution (toluene) using a phase transfer agent, tetraoctylammonium bromide ([CH3(CH2)7]4NBr, TOAB). The phase-transferred tetrachloroaurate ions are reduced by a strong reducing agent [sodium borohydride (NaBH4) in water] in the presence of alkanethiols. A strong reducing agent is used here because the reduction reaction occurs at the interface between two phases. The AuNPs are generated in the organic solution phase, with the particle surfaces coated with hydrophobic alkanethiolate molecules, which are used for particle stabilization and surface passivation.

In order to produce larger AuNPs and AgNPs with good size monodispersity, a modified two-step, two-phase method has also been developed for synthesizing thiol-capped AuNPs and AgNPs with a narrow size distribution by Tzeng et al.187,188 In this approach, AuNPs are first synthesized by trisodium citrate and tannic acid in an aqueous solution. The average particle diameter can be controlled from 3 to 20 nm with a size monodispersity <10%. In the second step, the aqueous AuNPs are transferred to toluene using TOAB as the transfer agent. Large AuNPs in water are difficult to be phase-transferred to a nonpolar organic solvent. In order to transfer larger AuNPs (>10 nm) into the toluene phase, it is necessary to heat the mixture at elevated temperatures, which requires special attention due to safety concern.

In comparison, for the synthesis of monodispersed, alkanethiol-capped AgNPs with an average NP size tunable from a few nanometers to ∼20 nm in diameter, the preparation method is similar to the case of AuNPs except that the first NP synthesis step is a polyol process. For the growth of AgNPs,189 a polyol solution of the silver precursor is prepared. Typical polyols used for this synthesis include ethylene glycol (EG) or diethylene glycol (DEG) and the silver precursor used for synthesizing AgNPs is silver nitrate (AgNO3). Colloidal AgNPs are created by mixing a silver nitrate precursor/DEG solution with a poly(acrylic acid) (PAA)/DEG solution at an elevated temperature (near the boiling temperature of DEG). Here, the PAA polymer is used as the stabilization agent. In the second step, an AgNP/DEG solution is transferred to the toluene solution using TOAB as the transfer agent and thiol as the final surface passivation agent.190

The alkanethiol-capped AuNPs and AgNPs are excellent building blocks for the self-assembly of NP films and metastructures because the size monodispersity and soft thiolate shells of Au and Ag NPs facilitate the large-scale (on the order of cm2) self-assembly of close-packed NP superlattices with uniform packing properties.190 Furthermore, the thiolate capping layers on AuNPs and AgNPs provide the needed chemical stability, reduced surface charge, and tunability of interparticle separation via the carbon chain length of alkanethiol molecules.191 In the case of citrate-reduced AuNPs, the original charge state is negatively charged because the citrate ions function as the surface stabilization agent for AuNP suspension in aqueous solutions. It has been confirmed that water-soluble AuNPs selectively adsorb onto a positively charged self-assembled monolayer (SAM) in a sparse random pattern because of strong electrostatic interactions of AuNPs with the SAM surface (attractive) and repulsive interactions between adsorbed AuNPs on the SAM surface.192 In contrast, in the case of the thiolate-capped AuNPs and AgNPs in toluene, the NP surfaces are close to charge neutralization, thereby allowing the self-assembly of close-packed NP monolayers.191

However, in the Brust method, the TOAB molecules turn out to be exceedingly difficult to be exchanged completely by thiol molecules. Therefore, the thiolate-capped AuNPs and AgNPs synthesized by the Brust method are slightly positively charged, as confirmed by the experimental result reported by Tzeng et al.187 It was conjectured that the remaining TOAB molecules on the thiolate-capped NPs result in the observed charge inversion. For the long-term stability of NP monolayers, these TOABs can cause some complications. Martin et al. removes the need for the TOAB transfer agent by generating “naked” AuNPs in water by reducing tetrachloroaurate ions with sodium borohydride without any stabilizer such as citrate.193 These AuNPs can then be phase transferred from a water and acetone mixture to hexane with alkanethiol molecules, which can bind covalently to the particle surfaces. However, using their method, the synthesized NP size is rather small, which can only be tuned from 3 to 5 nm.

The citrate/tannic-acid method is ideal for synthesizing size-monodispersed AuNPs of 3–20 nm in diameter. In the case of larger AuNPs produced by the citrate-based methods, size polydispersity and non-spherical particle shape (large eccentricity) become quite obvious when particle sizes are larger than 30 nm. In order to synthesize spherical AuNPs with narrow size distributions, seeded growth techniques offer some possibilities. For example, Perrault and Chan have reported a synthesis route using the seed-mediated growth technique, which can produced water-soluble AuNPs in the size range of 50–200 nm.194 However, precise shape control is challenging for large AuNPs produced by the citrate-based methods due to their polycrystalline nature. To produce large, single-crystalline noble-metal crystals, the combination of the polyol process and seeded growth techniques discussed below can offer versatile solutions for growing both size- and shape-controlled colloidal noble-metal crystals with large crystal sizes on the order of a few hundreds of nm.

3.2. Polyol synthesis method for metal nanocrystals

As discussed earlier in Section 2.1, Au and Ag particles on the order of 100 nm or larger are important for many far-field optical applications because the far-field scattering cross-section exceeds the absorption cross-section for large Au and Ag particles. In this size regime, both size and shape controls become major concerns for particle synthesis.

In terms of shape control, polyol reactions are arguably the most successful chemical synthesis routes for producing noble-metal colloids with controllable shapes.8,10,11,195–197 Typical polyol solutions include ethylene glycol, 1,2-propylene glycol, or 1,5-pentanediol and polyol reactions employ polymers such as poly(vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP) as a surface capping agent. The metal precursor for reduction (metal salt) is dissolved in a polyol liquid that is heated to near the solution reflux temperature because a high and precise reaction temperature is needed for the reduction reaction in polyol processes.

The role of the PVP polymer is to act as a stabilizing agent, preventing the aggregation of metal particles and maintaining a colloidal suspension. In addition, PVP is used as a shape-control agent, promoting metal reduction onto specific crystal facets. PVP has been shown to stabilize the low surface-energy facets of the face-center (fcc) crystal system. Therefore, it can enable the formation of nanocrystals bound by {111}, {100} and {110} facets. PVP has been demonstrated as an excellent shape control agent for Au and Ag, and their alloys. Synthetic procedures have been developed for a variety of crystal shapes,11,12 including rods, wires, hexagonal/triangular plates, cubes, octahedral, tetrahedral, icosahedra, and other polyhedral (see Fig. 7 for some examples195,198–205). These shapes are mostly terminated by {111} and {100} crystal facets. Polyhedral nanocrystals are almost exclusively single-crystalline, whereas one-dimensional rods and wires can be twinned nanostructures, consisting of multiple single-crystalline domains. Achieving monodispersity in size and shape requires the optimization of multiple reaction parameters and has been successfully demonstrated for both Au and Ag nanocrystals. Moreover, seed-mediated growth for large and uniformly sized/shaped Au and Ag nanocrystals has also been realized.206–209

However, spherical single-crystals are not stable under equilibrium conditions. Under or close to thermal equilibrium conditions, nanocrystals tend to adopt polyhedral morphologies to minimize their surface energy. 4. Plasmonic coupling: theoretical concepts and experimental techniques

The spectral features of LSPR resulting from a single metal NP are determined by its size, shape, material composition, and the surrounding dielectric environment. However, when multiple metal NPs are positioned in close proximity, individual LSPRs are coupled to one another due to electromagnetic interactions. The coupling effects are fundamentally important for applications since they can be used to control plasmonic resonances and to generate plasmonic hot spots with strong field localization and enhancement (see Fig. 2). Considering the simplest case of a NP dimer consisting of two spherical NPs, as illustrated in Fig. 11, one can already recognize the importance of interparticle coupling effect on the surface plasmon resonance.227–229 In a NP dimer, the coupling is manifested as far-field interference when the gap size is much greater than the wavelength. In the other limit of extremely small gap size (s) below ∼1 nm, electron quantum tunneling becomes important. Most recent studies, including the majority of those discussed in the current review, are concerned with the intermediate regime, where the plasmonic resonances strongly depend on the near-field coupling between neighboring NPs. In this intermediate regime, dipole–dipole interaction is a good approximation to describe the spectral shift of the dimer's plasmonic resonance as the interparticle distance reduces. In the longitudinal coupling geometry, where the E-field polarization of the incident light is along the axis of the dimer, a pronounced red shift of the dimer's plasmon resonance is observed with decreasing distance, as shown in Fig. 12.230 In the transverse coupling geometry, where the E-field polarization of the incident light is perpendicular to the axis of the dimer, a weaker but distinct blue resonance shift is observed.
Fig. 11 Schematic diagram for the gap-dependent plasmonic coupling in NP dimers. When the separation is reduced to the near-field coupling region, the dimer plasmons can form bonding and anti-bonding modes (hybridization of individual NP plasmons). A scaling relation has been reported for NP dimers in the near-coupling region, indicating that the NP separation (s) needs to be less than 20% of the NP diameter (D). A more general and powerful model for describing plasmon resonances for couple NPs is the plasmon hybridization model introduced by Nordlander et al.231–233 In the case of a NP with simple geometry (a sphere or a shell), it has been shown that both microscopic ab initio electronic structure calculations and macroscopic electromagnetic theory can be used to predict coupled plasmon resonances. The convergence of microscopic and macroscopic theories provides a rigorous foundation of the hybrization model. In this model, the interaction between NPs leads to the hybridization of electronic wavefunctions or plasmonic modes similar to the energy-level hybrizations in molecules. Again, we discuss the simplest case of two coupled NP spheres (denoted as α and β), as illustrated in Fig. 13.234,235 The single NP modes for the longitudinal and transverse polarizations (denoted by L and T) are degenerate. For two coupled identical NPs excited by longitudinal polarized light, a bonding mode with a lower energy and an anti-bonding mode with a higher energy are formed, where the subscripts “+” and “−” denote in-phase and out-phase coupling between the two modes. In the case of transverse excitation, the bonding mode is out-phase and the anti-bonding mode is in-phase . The plasmonic interaction for the transverse polarization is weaker than that in the longitudinal polarization, leading to the energy diagram shown in Fig. 13a. This diagram also illustrates that the two modes (ψL−, ψT−) are optically dark because the dipole oscillations are out of phase. In contrast, the two bright modes (ψL+, ψT+) lead to either a red or blue shift in plasmon resonances for longitudinal and transverse polarization excitations, respectively. The hybridization model is rather general for modeling different plasmonic coupling configurations. For example, the energy diagram shown in Fig. 13(b) based on the hybridization model can also describe the plasmon resonances in a dimer consisting of two different NPs (heterodimer).235

5. Nanomanipulation of plasmonic nanocrystals

A rather straightforward method for assembling small clusters of coupled plasmonic nanostructure is nanomanipulation. There are two classes of manipulation methods: (i) contact manipulation by pushing the NPs or NCs using AFM tips or SEM nanomanipulators and (ii) non-contact manipulation via optical tweezers or a focused electron beam. While these manipulation methods are quite time-consuming and may not be suitable for producing plasmonic clusters in large quantities or on large scales, they have some unique advantages in the assembly of simple model systems to demonstrate the controllable and designed properties.

5.1. Contact manipulation using a single scanning probe (AFM)

Since its invention in the eighties, scanning probe microscopy has been used to modify surfaces with atomic resolution in addition to simply imaging and characterizing surfaces.236–241 While the capability of manipulating individual atoms is impressive, manipulating objects with diameters ranging from a few nanometers to tens of nanometers in ambient environments poses its own challenges and has unique applications in nanotechnology.

AFM is particularly suitable for nanomanipulation tasks. Two AFM manipulation protocols are typically applied. The most commonly used “pushing-after-imaging” protocol (Fig. 17a) proceeds as follows.242 First, NPs are imaged with the feedback engaged, and a specific NP is selected to be manipulated. Next, a line is drawn that starts behind the chosen NP and ends at the target position using a commercial manipulation software. After positioning the tip behind the NP, the tip moves along the trajectory defined by the line on the screen and pushes the NP upon contact. The z feedback is disengaged in this pushing step. Once the tip reaches the target position, the AFM probe is pulled away and the feedback is re-engaged. After the pushing step, the sample is imaged again. This multi-step procedure is repeated if the NP is not at the targeted position. An early example of manipulating Au nanoclusters on an atomically smooth WSe2 substrate is shown in Fig. 17b.243

In the “pushing-after-imaging” method, the biggest inefficiency arises from the fact that the AFM is used either as imaging or as a manipulation tool, but not both simultaneously unless more than one AFM tip is used.244 Pushing small NPs over a relatively long distance often fails when the NP rolls off to the side of the tip. A second manipulation method, “pushing-while-imaging”, has been developed to overcome some shortcomings of the first method. This second method takes advantage of two empirical rules of friction.245,246 First, kinetic friction is lower than static friction.247 Secondly, the longer the two surfaces in contact, the stronger the friction becomes.248 This second effect is particularly pronounced at the nanoscale.

There are two steps in the “pushing-while-imaging” manipulation method. In the first step, one still has to apply the “pushing-after-imaging method” to kick a chosen NP in a random direction. The static friction experienced by this chosen NP is greatly reduced following this initial movement. In the second step, one can manipulate the NP controllably in the tapping mode with the feedback engaged. If the scanning rate exceeds a threshold value, the chosen NP starts to move in the direction perpendicular to the fast scanning axis, as illustrated in Fig. 17c. Once the target position is reached, the scanning rate is abruptly reduced well below the threshold value, and the NP stops moving. The other NPs will remain stationary because they experience higher friction. A ghost trace is often observed following the trajectory of the moving NP, making it easy to follow its position as illustrated in Fig. 17c. An example of moving small NPs over relatively large distances is presented in Fig. 17d, in which 8 NPs (∼10 nm in diameter) are arranged in a straight line with a few nanometer error in position.242

Beyond these basic nanomanipulation methods, automatic manipulation protocols have been implemented with some success.249,250 For example, in a method called “sequential parallel pushing”, one scan line perpendicular to the pushing direction is used to determine the lateral coordinate of the NP without fully locating the NP center. The longitudinal position of the NP is inferred from the position where the tip loses contact with the NP through analyzing the vibration amplitude of the cantilever in real time. This method can reduce the manipulation time by up to a factor of 4 compared to the target oriented pushing. An example of an image consisting of 170 NPs (∼50 nm in diameter) is shown in Fig. 18.

Much progress in AFM-based nanomanipulation has been made in the past two decades. In addition to using the AFM tip as a robotic arm, one can also functionalize the tip with a metallic sphere, rod, or nanotube.266–278 The functionalized tip is then used as part of a reconfigurable plasmonic nanostructure to investigate interaction with other plasmonic nanostructures or emitters. By identifying this regime experimentally, the researchers estimated a fundamental limit to plasmonic field confinement on the order of 10−8λ3 for visible light. This field confinement is approximately 6 orders of magnitude stronger than that achieved in photonic crystal cavities. 5.2. Contact manipulation using a multiple-probe nanomanipulator (SEM)

Complementary to AFM nanomanipulation, sophisticated nanomanipulators can be readily integrated with an optical microscope or inside a SEM chamber for the purpose of assembling plasmonic nanostructures as shown in Fig. 20. Such a manipulator typically consists of multiple probes with both coarse motion control in the range of a few millimeters and fine motion control with a few nanometers of accuracy.188,279–281 The key advantages of the SEM manipulation method include: (i) the simultaneous acquisition of an image for real-time feedback during the manipulation process, (ii) the high spatial resolution of SEM enabling potentially better accuracy in controlling the dimensions of the assembled structures, (iii) the capability of readily performing in situ electronic and optoelectronic measurements using conducting manipulator probes. The main disadvantage of the SEM manipulation is the possible damage to the metallic structures introduced by the high-energy electron beam used for the imaging purpose. This issue has been alleviated since recent SEM systems have been developed to have reasonable imaging resolution using a low-energy electron beam.

Multiple-probe nanomanipulators can be operated in different modes to assemble NPs. In addition to the method of pushing upon contact, one may use a pick-and-place method for small NPs. By applying appropriate voltage, it is possible to pick up a small NP via electrostatic force or by polarizing a NP in a non-uniform electric field. The challenge in the pick-and-place method is to reliably drop off a NP. When using a multi-probe manipulator, one probe can be used to pick up a NP and another probe can be used to slightly change the NP orientation or rub it off the first probe at the desired location.

5.3. Non-contact manipulation using an electron beam

An electron beam has been used to observe plasmonic modes in nanostructures and manipulate small NPs.208,283–287 In modern electron microscopes, electron beams focused down to the sub-nanometer scale are routinely used. Upon incidence on a metallic surface, the high-energy electrons (typically in the energy range of tens of keV and at a beam current of several nA) can excite surface plasmon modes. The incident electrons are directed to a precise location on a plasmonic nanostructure, and serve as broadband point dipole sources that couple to and interrogate the local plasmon modes. Two techniques are typically used to study extract information following this interaction: electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS)288,289 and cathodoluminescence (CL).290 In EELS, energy loss of an electron beam following transmission through a thin sample is measured and peaks in the transmission spectrum correspond to plasmonic modes excited in the sample. CL experiments are typically conducted in SEM chambers. Local surface plasmons can generate light emission to free space. Direct transition in metal following e-beam excitation can also decay via light emission. Both processes contribute to signals collected in CL experiments. Historically, the first evidence of plasmonic modes was reported in the 50s and 60s using electron-beam based experiments.291,292

Charged particles traveling near metallic NPs are known to exert a force on NPs (Fig. 23a). This force depends on the plasmonic modes excited by the incident e-beam, which, in turn, depends on the impact parameter b defined as transverse distance between the incident e-beam and the NP. This dependence can be described by the schematics shown in Fig. 23b.283 The ratio of the impact parameter b to the NP radius R determines the efficiency of exciting different modes. When the impact parameter is large, the NP size can be considered small, leading to predominantly a dipole mode. An attractive force is produced between the point charge and the induced dipole, moving the NP toward the incident e-beam. When the impact factor is small, higher-order plasmonic modes are excited. Surprisingly, a repulsive force is produced between the e-beam and the NP, pushing the NP away.

The simplest situation considered above can be extended to the case of an e-beam passing by a pair of NPs. When the electron beam passes through on one side of the NP pair and the impact factor is large, dipole modes are excited in both NPs, leading to an attractive force between them. In contrast, when the e-beam passes in between the NP pair at a small impact factor, the repulsive force between the beam and both NPs pushes the NPs apart. The force (or momentum transfer) between the e-beam and a NP (or a pair of NPs) is plotted in Fig. 23c as a function of the impact parameter.

The interaction between an e-beam and a NP pair has been used to controllably and systematically vary the gap size between two Ag NPs of 10 nm diameter from a few nanometers to the sub-nanometer scale until the two NPs eventually coalesce into a single NP,293 as shown in Fig. 23d. During the manipulation, EELS was used to probe the evolution of the surface plasmon modes. When the gap size is above 1 nm, the plasmonic modes can be well modeled by using the classical boundary element method. The experimental observation clearly deviates from the classical theory at a gap size of 0.5 nm. At this distance, the quantum tunneling effect becomes important and the local field enhancement between the two NPs saturates.294

5.4. Non-contact manipulation using optical tweezers

Optical tweezers use strongly focused light to exert force on an object. The optical force is typically decomposed into two components: a gradient force in the direction of light intensity gradient and a scattering force in the direction of light propagation as illustrated in Fig. 24a.286,295–312 In the seminal work by Ashkin et al. and many experiments that soon followed, optical tweezers were used to manipulate dielectric particles.313 Initially, metallic particles were considered difficult to use if not, impossible to trap. Because metallic particles are highly reflective, they tend to be pushed out of the focus. Svoboda and Block first trapped metallic NPs far from the plasmonic resonance.300 By choosing an infrared laser far from the plasmon resonance where a gold NP has a large refractive index, the gradient force on the gold NP is actually larger than that on a dielectric NP of the same size. It has been shown that metallic NPs as small as 5 nm can be trapped using optical tweezers.301

6. Controlled self-assembly of plasmonic nanoparticles and nanocrystals

The strong dependence of surface plasmon resonances and near-field enhancements on the details of gaps or void spaces (hot spots) between constituent building blocks in the plasmonic metastructures makes it an important task to accurately control the assembly of plasmonic building blocks, such as colloidal noble-metal NPs and shape-controlled NCs. However, the precise control of plasmonic hot spots is very challenging due to the demanding requirements for assembly precision and material quality. In the case of plasmonic building blocks synthesized by the most established colloidal chemistry methods, the scaling relation between the size of building blocks and the spatial range of the evanescent near fields (Fig. 11) requires the spatial resolution for gap control better than 1 nm for size-controlled AuNPs (AgNPs) synthesized using the combined citrate/tannic-acid (polyol) and Brust method (particle size 3–20 nm, gap size <5 nm) and better than 10 nm for the shape-controlled noble-metal NPs synthesized using the polyol process (particle size ∼100 nm, gap size is about a few tens of nm). These resolution requirements cannot be met reliably even by the state-of-the-art lithographic methods, such as electron-beam lithography (EBL), FIB milling, and scanning probe lithography. Moreover, it is time-consuming and cost-ineffective to make large metastructures one-by-one using the lithographic methods. Therefore, for most practical applications, self-assembly is the method of choice to realize uniform plasmonic metastructures with large structure size and small interparticle gap size. In particular, self-assembly can meet the requirements of efficiency, robustness, and reproducibility for practical applications.

Considering the breadth of the topic (self-assembly of nanomaterials), it would not be possible to provide an exhaustive review including all significant topics. Instead, we will focus here on some selected self-assembly methods, which can have immediate plasmonic applications. For example, we will give some working examples, which can be used for assembling plasmonic meta-atoms (complex nanostructures) into large-scale plasmonic metasurfaces and metamaterials. These metastructures can serve as plasmonic substrates in many fundamental studies and technological applications related to light-based science and technology, including ultrahigh-sensitivity spectroscopies, energy harvesting, nanoscale light sensing and optical signal transduction, integrated nano-optics, and super-resolution imaging. Some of these applications will be discussed in the next section.

6.1. Overview of controlled self-assembly techniques

In general, self-assembly refers to the spontaneous process by which discrete building blocks organize themselves into ordered macroscopic structures due to direct interactions between building blocks and/or indirectly through external forces and the environment.15,16,320–325 In order to exploit self-assembly for plasmonic applications and to ensure efficient scaling, a high-level control of these driving forces in self-assembly processes is needed. This is the reason why “controlled” is emphasized here for the self-assembly methods with programmability and scalability. In order to control the self-assembly process, it is important to modulate the internal interactions and external forces that drive the self-assembly process, by either the use of surface chemistry, templating, or by external physical forces (electrostatic, magnetic, thermal, or flow). Fig. 26 shows some examples of the controlled assembly of macroscale building blocks ranging from millimeter to the centimeter scale. Interestingly, many of these assembly concepts are also applicable to the controlled assembly of nanoscale building blocks.

(a) Controlled 3D assembly using anisotropic 0D building blocks (nanoblock™, http://www.diablock.co.jp/nanoblock/), (b) controlled 3D assembly using 1D chains of anisotropic 0D building blocks (magnetic spheres made of rare earth alloys), (c) controlled 3D assembly using 2D templates.

In this section, we will focus on the controlled self-assembly of colloidal NPs. The interactions between colloidal NPs in a solution or at a liquid/solid interface are very complex and involve various kinds of forces, such as van der Waals (vdW), electrostatic, solvation/depletion, frictional/lubrication, convective, and capillary forces.15 An effective way to prevent NP aggregation caused by strong attractive forces (e.g. vdW forces) between NPs is to coat them with charged ions (e.g. stabilizing citrate ions on AuNPs) or soft organic shells (e.g. alkanethiolates on AuNPs and AgNPs, PVP on AuNCs and AgNCs). In general, NPs coated with soft organic shells involve the following interactions: (i) attractive van der Waals forces between NPs and surface ligands; (ii) repulsive steric interactions between surface ligands; (iii) repulsive electrostatic forces between charged NP surfaces. These soft shells can be tailored to provide the appropriate balance (repulsive electrostatic or steric forces vs. attractive vdW forces) to direct the assembly of highly ordered NP structures.326 In addition, they can be functionalized to provide the chemical specificity for bonding or the capability of high-precision interparticle gap control (e.g. via the carbon chain length of alkanethiolates). Recently, DNA-based functionalization and assembly strategies have emerged as a promising approach for the programmable self-assembly of NP supercrystals. DNA has the distinct advantages of simple synthesis, wide availability, complementary base-pairing interactions, and programmable features. A number of novel plasmonic structures have been demonstrated via the programed hybridization of complementary DNA strands, ranging from dimers to 3D supercrystals.326–332 To control the plasmonic hot spots, DNA is also particularly useful because its length (number of bases) can be fine-tuned to the desired interparticle gaps using specific interactions by the DNA sequence. However, its structural flexibility also causes some issues of gap size rigidity. At present, DNA-programed self-assembly of novel NP structures is a rapidly evolving research frontier and we would like to refer the readers to several excellent reviews available in the literature.17,333,334

In terms of surface functionalization, the soft organic shells on colloidal NPs can be chemically modified so that molecular interactions can be tailored and used to create ordered assemblies of NPs. Recent advances in this research area have focused on designing particles with morphological (e.g. faceted nanocrystals335–338) or functional features that result in directional (anisotropic) NP interactions. Anisotropic interactions are typically created by modifying NP's surfaces with ligands localized on specific surface regions. Among them, Janus particles with two chemically or physically different surface regions14,339 and DNA-engineered NPs340,341 are two prime examples. In particular, the DNA technique has enabled the assembly of NPs with highly directional interactions, similar to the valence bonding of atoms due to hybridized atomic orbitals. As a result, complex or low-coordination NP structures are now possible in the colloidal domain.340

Other interesting possibilities based on chemical surface functionalization include photo-switchable molecules for reversible assembly and biomolecules (e.g. streptavidin–biotin, antibody–antigen) for biological functionalities.6 Alternatively, particles with specific physical properties can be designed to maximize their interactions with external directing fields e.g. the self-assembly of magnetite nanocubes into quasi-1D helical superstructures in an external magnetic field.342 Confined geometries, such as solid/liquid, air/liquid, and liquid/liquid interfaces, as well as patterned templates and micro flow channels are also very useful to achieve controlled assembly of ordered NP structures.

For plasmonic applications with an emphasis on plasmonic hot spot engineering, we will focus here on two capillary self-assembly techniques (dewetting and Langmuir–Blodgett techniques, see Fig. 27). Using both techniques, colloidal NPs are assembled at the air/liquid/solid interface driven by capillary and thermodynamic forces.190,191,343–348 The dewetting technique, involving drying-mediated (evaporation-induced) assembly of colloidal particles, is a very simple method of assembling 2D ordered particle arrays on solid surfaces. In this process, the relatively weak attractive forces between NPs in the solution become important when the solvent is evaporated. This self-assembly mechanism was interpreted by Denkov et al.349,350 to occur at the evaporation front passing over the surface at a controlled rate, where the particles dispersed in the solution are pulled toward the drying front and then deposited there as the drying front becomes very thin.322 The Langmuir–Blodgett technique is also a type of the interfacial assembly process. First, surface-passivated NPs (e.g. alkanethiolate-passivated AuNPs or AgNPs) are prepared under appropriate conditions to float over the liquid surface. A well-ordered NP monolayer can then spontaneously form at the air–liquid interface, which can be transferred using either horizontal or vertical lift-off to a solid substrate such as quartz. Large-area NP films of various core materials can also be prepared via the Langmuir–Blodgett technique.

6.2. Self-assembly of 2D and 3D colloidal NP metastructures

The dewetting method can be easily implemented for the large-area assembly of 2D ordered colloidal NP or NC arrays on solid substrates with very high precision in controlling gap sizes between NPs or NCs. Depending on the NP/NC and surfactant concentration, solvent evaporation rate, ionic strength (electrolyte solution), and other solution conditions, metal NPs or NCs can be assembled into well-ordered one-, two-, and three-dimensional plasmonic NP/NC metastructures. For example, a thiol-passivated AuNP and AgNP superlattice monolayer can be formed at the moving front of the evaporating droplet containing a suspension of colloidal NPs. The inter-particle gap sizes in these NP superlattices can be precisely regulated by the length of alkanethiolate ligands on the NP surfaces. Moreover, the shape anisotropy of metal NCs can be used to create special packing arrangements.

Fig. 28 shows a series of 2D hexagonal close-packed AuNP superlattices formed on quartz with an average Au core size of 10.5 nm. In this example, the heated solvent temperature controls the evaporation rate of the dewetting process and the kinetic process of superlattice formation.191 To control the interparticle gap size, the Au NP surfaces for different 2D superlattices are coated with various alkanethiolate ligands, ranging from twelve- (C12, dodecanethiolote), fourteen- (C14, tetradecanethiolate), and sixteen- (C16, hexadecanethiolate) to eighteen-carbon (C18, octadecanethiolate) chain length. Analyzed by high-resolution SEM images, the interparticle gap size can be controlled at an atomic resolution from 3.4 nm (C18) to 2.3 nm (C12), which cannot be achieved using any available lithographic techniques. The collective plasmon resonance changes from 575 nm (C18) to 607 nm (C12) accordingly, confirming a red-shift due to near field coupling between NPs. This study shows the distinct advantage of the controlled self-assembly method for fabricating designed plasmonic metastructures with an unprecedented spatial resolution in gap control. Moreover, this technique is scalable to centimeter-sized structures by using the Langmuir–Blodgett technique, which has better controllability than the dewetting technique. 6.3. Layer-by-layer self-assembly of 3D plasmonic metatructures

In order to realize large-scale 3D self-assembly of plasmonic metastructures, the layer-by-layer (LbL) assembly technique introduced by Decher354 in 1997 is a very versatile approach that can offer controllability, simplicity, flexibility, and scalability not possible using other self-assembly techniques. In the earlier implementation of the LbL assembly technique, electrostatic interactions were used to assemble alternatively deposited positively charged polymer layers (binder layers) and negatively charged NPs. This LbL approach has been applied to assemble a wide range of plasmonic nanomaterials into multilayered heterostructures.355–369 Moreover, using LbL assembly, various optical and electronic devices with engineered properties have been demonstrated. However, electrostatic assembly via charged polymer binders has its limitations in two aspects: (i) the thickness of the binder layer prevents near-field coupling between neighboring metal NP layers; and (ii) long-range ordering in the NP layer is not possible because of the strong electrostatic interactions.

For this reason, it would be better to develop an “epitaxial” growth method of NP films without the need for a binder layer. This “molecular beaker epitaxy” (“MBE”) is analogous to conventional molecular beam epitaxy for growing semiconductor and metal heterostructure films, but without the need for using expensive and complicated ultra-high vacuum systems. To meet this requirement, we have developed a simple and efficient method for synthesizing large-area (>1 cm2), 3D AuNP and AgNP supercrystal films.190 In this approach, Janus NP (top face solvent-phobic and bottom face solvent-philic) films with a large number of close-packed NP monolayers can be formed using LbL assembly from suspensions of thiolate-coated AuNPs or AgNPs (see Fig. 31). To create the Janus NP monolayers, Lin et al. applied a simple and scalable air plasma technique370 to modify (i.e. oxidation of the surface functional groups) the surfaces of closed-packed, thiolate-coated AuNP or AgNP monolayers after every LbL assembly step. It has been demonstrated that these NP films can act as 3D plasmonic metastructures showing transverse (intralayer) and longitudinal (interlayer) near-field coupling effects (Fig. 31).

In contrast to conventional polyelectrolyte-mediated LbL assembly processes, this approach allows for the formation of longitudinal plasmonic modes originating from the near-field coupling between NP monolayers because of the extremely small interlayer gap size. A clear spectral dependence on the number of NP monolayers has been confirmed. Moreover, a universal scaling relation was found between the spectral positions of the reflectance dips related to the longitudinal modes and the number of NP monolayers. This relation can be understood in terms of the formation of a plasmonic Fabry–Pérot nanocavity along the longitudinal direction that allows for the formation of standing plasmon waves under resonant conditions. The realization of 3D plasmonic coupling in the LbL-assembled Au/Ag NP supercrystals enables the broadband tuning of collective plasmon response over a wide spectral range (visible and near-IR), which is otherwise not possible using 2D Au or Ag NP superlattices in the ultrasmall size regime (<20 nm in NP diameter). Furthermore, according to our recent studies, the LbL-assembly technique can provide a unique pathway to fabricate conformal plasmonic metastructures on curved and artificially structured surfaces. The conformal possibility is very useful, for example, for super-resolution optical imaging using a hyperlens approach based on plasmonic metamaterials.371

7. Applications

In this section, we discuss a few selected applications of metamolecules assembled by using the nanomanipulation methods and metasurfaces created by using the controlled self-assembly methods.

7.1. Plasmon-enhanced photovoltaics

Most commercial solar cells based on crystalline Si include a ∼200 μm thick absorption layer because of the small absorption coefficient of Si as an indirect gap semiconductor. Reducing the Si layer thickness below the carrier diffusion length (i.e.several microns) not only reduces the cost but also increases charge collection efficiency. Incorporating plasmonic NPs or films improves photovoltaic devices by increasing light trapping efficiency.39,126,374–388 First, plasmonic NPs placed at the semiconductor interface increase preferential scattering into semiconductors, effectively increasing the optical interaction length. Second, the enhanced near-field of small NPs may increase light absorption of the surrounding semiconductor medium. Third, light trapping may also occur via the excitation of SPPs at the metal/semiconductor interfaces. Such SPPs effectively concentrate and guide light inside the semiconductor layer. Finally, hot electron generation in plasmonic NPs has been investigated recently as an alternative mechanism to improve photovoltaics.389–393

Hägglund and coworkers have demonstrated the impressively high absorption of visible light using an absorber/spacer/reflector structure as illustrated in Fig. 34a.379 The absorber layer consisting of dense, hexagonal Au dot arrays was produced using the undirected self-assembly of block copolymers as shown by the cross-sectional TEM view in Fig. 34b. The plasmonic resonance of the array is comparable or better than those produced using electron beam lithography. Subsequently, the arrays were coated with SnSx, ZnO, or Al2O3 using atomic layer deposition. A layer of ion-beam deposited SiO2 acted as the spacer layer between the absorber layer and the Al reflector. A maximum of >90% absorption in a volume-equivalent ∼2 nm thick gold was demonstrated as shown in Fig. 34c.

(c) >90% absorption was demonstrated at the plasmonic resonance by comparing absorption spectra from structures with/without the Au NP arrays (red and green curves). Using a plasmonic back reflector consisting of self-assembled AgNPs for light trapping, Tan and coworkers demonstrated a thin film Si solar cell with performance comparable to state-of-the-art random textures in amorphous silicon solar cells.380 A different design of the back plane reflector using self-assembled arrays of dielectric nanospheres in a continuous metal layer has also been shown to increase the photocurrent and the external quantum efficiency for thin film Si solar cells.377,381 By incorporating both Ag and Au NPs with different plasmonic resonances, Lu and coworkers showed a 20% enhancement in power conversion efficiency in polymer solar cells382 (Fig. 35a). More recently, ion-assisted aerosol lithography of metal NPs enabled controllable three-dimensional multiscale structures, leading to increased light scattering and absorption efficiency over a broad spectral range and improved power conversion efficiency of Si thin film solar cells385 (Fig. 35b).

Another mechanism for plasmonic NPs to enhance photovoltaic performance is via hot electron generation. Following the excitation of LSPR modes, the energy is dissipated via either radiative or non-radiative decay processes. In the non-radiative decay process, surface plasmons first decay to single-electron excited states, if the electron energy exceeds the work function of the materials, photoemission may follow. If the plasmonic nanostructure is placed in contact with a semiconductor, hot electrons with enough energy to overcome the Schottky barrier are injected into the conduction band of the semiconductor, leaving the metallic nanostructure positively charged. An electron-donor solution or a hole transporting material is required to transport the generated holes and to sustain an electric current. This hot electron injection mechanism offers a completely new scheme for solar energy conversion, thus, raising significant recent interest.

Early hints of surface-plasmon induced charge separation came from the observation of photocurrent generated in TiO2 with a layer containing Au or Ag NPs under visible light illumination. Because of the large band-gap (3.3 eV) of TiO2, photocurrents in TiO2 are usually only observable with UV-light illumination. Zhao and coworkers suggested LSPRs as a possible mechanism for the unexpected photocurrent.394 In another experiment, Ag/Au NPs absorbed onto porous TiO2 were placed in contact with an electron-donor solution in a photovoltaic cell. The measured incident photon-to-electron conversion efficiency varied as a function of wavelength and closely matched the extinction spectrum of the NP, establishing the critical role of LSPRs in photocurrent generation as shown in Fig. 36.37

Because the efficiency of hot electron generation is closely related to the plasmonic decay processes, this process depends on the shape of the NPs. For example, the photoelectron generation process is more efficient in small spherical NPs with a diameter of ∼15 nm than bigger NPs that exhibit more efficient radiative decay. Similarly, nanorods can lead to better hot electron generation efficiency due to modified decay dynamics and multiple plasmonic resonances leading to enhanced absorption over a broader spectral range.

7.2. Plasmonic rulers

The key idea incorporated in optical spectroscopic rulers is to transduce changes in spatial arrangement into spectral differences. The most widely applied spectroscopic ruler is fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET),395–398 which measures the distance between two labeling molecules (e.g. green fluorescent proteins), referred to as the donor and the acceptor by monitoring the fluorescence intensity from the acceptor molecule. FRET has widespread applications in biochemical science, e.g. as a quantitative sensor for ion concentration (e.g. Ca2+) in cells, organisms, and tissues. Because the energy transfer efficiency is proportional to the sixth power of the distance between the two molecules, FRET can only be used to estimate inter-chromophoric distances below a few nanometers.

The development of plasmonic rulers is mainly motivated by the desire to search for new optical labels that are more photochemically stable and to allow for longer observation periods and longer interaction distance (10–100 nm). Plasmonic rulers make use of plasmonic resonances in noble metal NPs. It has been successfully applied to investigate the kinetics of DNA hybridization, the dynamics of DNA bonding and cleavage, and the activity of enzymes. In the example shown in Fig. 37, two gold NPs were attached to a DNA to monitor its configurational changes.399 The plasmonic resonance exhibited a clear blue shift when the two NPs were pushed apart due to hybridization of the doubled stranded DNA upon addition of complementary DNAs.

(b) Spectral change in the scattering spectrum as a function time used to monitor the DNA configuration changes. Figures are adapted with permission from ref. 399. Copyright 2005 Macmillan Publishers Limited.

There has been a long-standing effort to extend the 1D nature of FRET to 3D by measuring the polarization of anisotropic fluorescent molecules.400–402 A previously demonstrated 3D plasmonic ruler was based on a large array of plasmonic elements, thus unsuitable for local orientation sensing at the single molecule level.403 By choosing two coupled NPs with distinct optical properties, one can determine the relative orientation between the two. Such an orientation sensor has been termed as a plasmonic protractor, as demonstrated in Fig. 38. A nanorod has a narrow “dark” resonance in the visible wavelength range, which corresponds to a quadrupole-like charge distribution. This quadrupole mode cannot be excited directly by the incident light. Instead, it can be excited effectively via near-field coupling to the nanosphere. The interference between the bright, dipole mode of the sphere and the dark, quadrupole mode of the rod leads to the Fano resonance. The strength of this Fano resonance, however, depends on the angle of the polarizer in the path of the incident light and the angle of the analyzer in the path of the collected scattered light as illustrated in Fig. 38.404 This polarization dependence allows us to determine the orientation of the Fano axis.

7.3. Plasmon-controlled fluorescence

One application of plasmonics relevant to quantum physics is controlling the emitter properties. The large photonic density of states as well as the ultrasmall, subwavelength optical mode volume associated with plasmonic nanostructures and cavities make them attractive alternatives to traditional dielectric cavities in exploring quantum electrodynamics (QED) phenomena.405 A plasmonic cavity influences both the absorption and subsequent decay dynamics of an emitter. The PL emission rate can be written as γPL = γexcY, where γexc is the excitation rate and is the quantum yield determined by the ratio of decay rates. κr is the radiative decay rate, and κnr is the nonradiative decay rate. By enhancing the E-field strength of the near field, a plasmonic NP or cavity increases the absorption rate of the emitter. The NP also enhances the radiative decay rate of the emitter via the Purcell effect and the non-radiative decay via energy transfer between the emitter and the NP.

A simple model system consisting of a dipole emitter near a spherical AuNP was examined.406 The properties of the dipole are taken to be typically of a colloidal semiconductor quantum dot, which has near unity quantum yield. We plot the modification factor η in the total decay rate ηt, the radiative decay rate ηr, and the non-radiative decay rate ηnr as a function of the distance d between the dipole and the NP of 35 nm diameter as shown in Fig. 39a. The modification factors are defined as the ratio between the rates for the coupled NP-dipole system and the dipole alone. As the distance decreases, all these decay rates increase. While the modification in the radiative decay ηr is moderate, the modification in the non-radiative decay is drastic, especially when the distance is reduced below 10 nm. This rapid increase in the non-radiative decay rate arises from the energy transfer between the dipole emitter and the NP. One can further calculate the modification in the PL intensity ηPL, which is the product of the modification in the excitation rate ηexc and quantum yield ηY, as a function of the NP and dipole distance as shown in Fig. 39b. Because of the rapid increase in the nonradiative decay rate at small distances, quantum yield ηY decreases monotonically as the NP approaches the dipole. The excitation rate, on the other hand, increases due to increasing local E-field strength. The PL intensity, calculated by the product of the quantum yield and excitation rate, displays a modest increase followed by a decrease at small distances.

7.4. Designed plasmonic metamolecules and circuits

Metamaterials represent man-made materials exhibiting novel properties that do not exist in natural materials.91,425–434 Metamolecules function as building blocks for metamaterials. Plasmonic NP clusters exhibit rich optical properties enabled by near-field coupling between NPs. One example is self-assembled NP clusters consisting of three or more NPs.435 As shown by Fan and coworkers, a trimer is one of the simplest geometries that supports interesting resonances. Such strong magnetic dipole resonances in the optical frequency range do not exist in natural materials mainly because the sizes of atoms and molecules are much smaller than the wavelength of light.436,437

Another example is a metamolecule consisting of four NPs assembled via AFM nanomanipulation, as shown in Fig. 43. Compared to other NP cluster geometries, a four-NP metamolecule has some unique properties. First, the four-NP ring geometry features a reduced radiative loss compared to odd-numbered nanorings due to cancellation of higher order multipolar terms.438 More importantly, the magnetic resonance may be tuned over a wide range and spectrally overlapped with the electric dipole resonance by simply adjusting the gap sizes between the NPs. Having spectrally overlapped electric and magnetic transitions allows for the manipulation of both permittivity and permeability of metamaterials consisting of such metamolecule building blocks. The spectrally narrow, dark magnetic resonance manifests itself as a Fano resonance via interference with the broad electronic dipole transition. 7.5. Plasmonic metasurfaces for nonlinear optics

Nonlinear optical processes play an important role in photonics, including frequency conversion, generation of ultrashort laser pulses, all-optical signal processing, and ultrafast switching.57,447,448 However, material nonlinearities are inherently weak. Thus, high-power lasers and special nonlinear crystals are typically required to observe the nonlinear optical effects. Therefore, plasmonic nanostructures are very useful in enhancing intrinsically weak nonlinear optical processes. Moreover, nonlinear optical processes, such as second-harmonic generation (SHG), third-harmonic generation (THG), four-wave mixing (FWM), and the Kerr effect, superlinearly depend on the electromagnetic field intensity and can be tremendously strengthened in appropriate material environments that provide surface plasmon resonances for large field enhancements.57–59

The unique advantage of plasmonic metasurfaces for nonlinear optics mainly lies in the possibility of engineering plasmonic resonances by controlling the size, shape, and composition of meta-atoms (e.g. asymmetric shapes,449,450) their geometric arrangements (e.g. chiral arrangements451–454), as well as their in-plane coupling and dielectric surroundings. Fig. 47 shows some of these examples in the literature for applications of plasmonics in nonlinear optics. Moreover, future on-chip nonlinear optical systems call for uniformly hot surfaces with precisely engineered plasmonic properties. The recent developments in plasmonic metasurfaces can fulfill these requirements. In general, plasmonic metasurfaces consist of arrays of 1D metallic nanostructures (e.g. nanoslits, nanogrooves, nanoribs) or 2D metallic nanostructures (i.e. subwavelength “meta-atoms”, such as nanoholes, nanoprotrusions, nanoantennas, split-rings, NPs) supported on solid substrates or embedded in optical films. Plasmonic metasurfaces exhibit collective and tunable resonance properties controlled by in-plane plasmonic coupling. In the past few years, plasmonic metasurfaces have been demonstrated to produce a range of unique linear and nonlinear optical properties unattainable with naturally occurring materials.89–92,95,96,98,109,111,190,191 In comparison to engineered linear properties, nonlinear optical applications are still not fully explored and we expect plenty of opportunities for this important emerging field.

Traditional nonlinear optics requires the phase matching conditions. The green laser pointer can be taken as an example58 for SHG applications. In a commercially available green laser pointer, a diode laser emitting at the near-infrared wavelength (1064 nm) is converted to 532 nm green light by a frequency-doubling crystal. However, this conversion process requires nonlinear crystals such that they must have the same refractive indices at 1064 nm (fundamental mode) and 532 nm (second-harmonic mode) to fulfill the phase matching conditions, i.e. the generated SHG light propagates in phase with the light at the fundamental frequency. Moreover, the SHG efficiency is very low, resulting in an expensive, electric power inefficient, and bulky green laser pointer on the market.

For plasmon-enhanced nonlinear optical processes by metallic nanostructures, strict phase-matching is no longer a prerequisite since the metallic nanostructures can facilitate the formation of subwavelength hot spots to enhance the nonlinear processes.57–59 In this case, mode-matching conditions are more important than phase-matching conditions. The design of multi-resonant nanostructures has been proven to be a very efficient approach to achieve plasmonic mode-matching.59,455–457 For SHG, the electromagnetic properties of a plasmonic system can be specifically optimized at the fundamental and second-harmonic wavelengths in order to reach an efficient excitation at the fundamental wavelength and a maximal emission at the second-harmonic wavelength. As an alternative approach to plasmonic mode-matching, optical zero-index metamaterials have recently been demonstrated for phase-mismatch-free nonlinear propagation.108

The plasmonic resonances on the metasurfaces can be designed to operate at a single wavelength, multiple wavelengths, or a wide spectral region at which the nonlinear response needs to be enhanced over a large sample area. In a recent work by Wang et al., using a precisely engineered plasmonic metasurface patterned with a V-shaped nanogroove array on giant (millimeter-scaled) colloidal silver single-crystalline plates,113 it has been demonstrated that double resonances at both fundamental and second-harmonic wavelengths can be designed and realized for strong plasmon-enhanced SHG at 532 nm (see Fig. 48). In the commonly used plasmonic metals (Au, Ag, Cu, and Al), the comprising atoms are organized in a face-centered cubic lattice, which is centrosymmetric. Therefore, SHG is forbidden in these bulk materials in the electric dipole approximation. To generate SHG, symmetry breaking originating from the metal surface,458,459 local geometry,460–464 or asymmetric meta-atom shape59,449,450 is necessary. In this aspect, the asymmetric geometry of the V-shaped nanogroove structure is an effective approach for plasmon-enhanced SHG. 7.6. Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy

One of the most important applications of plasmonics is SERS, which exploits the enormously large enhancement of the Raman scattering signal by placing Raman-active molecules (analytes) within the near-field region of noble-metal nanostructures. This enhancement is largely due to the excitations of LSPR modes at the noble-metal nanostructures, which are suspended in a solution (metal colloids) or supported on a SERS substrate consisting of a nanoscale roughened noble-metal surface or ensembles of specifically designed nanostructures.468–471 SERS has continuingly received extensive attention because of its great potential for ultrasensitive detection down to the single-molecular level (SM-SERS).73,472–478 In the SERS measurements, excitations of LSPR modes in noble-metal nanostructures significantly enhance and localize the incident electromagnetic field at nanoscale hot spots. The Raman scattering intensity of molecules, which are placed in close proximity to the hot spots of the SERS substrate, can be dramatically amplified with an enhancement factor frequently above 106. Until now, full control over the formation of plasmonic hot spots has remained as one of the major driving forces behind the design of highly sensitive, uniform, and robust SERS substrates with precisely tuned plasmonic properties.132,479

In many aspects, the most fundamental metric for SERS is the Raman enhancement factor (EF), which quantifies the amplification of the Raman signal for a specific molecular vibrational mode.480–482 The Raman EF associated with an analyte molecule in the vicinity of a plasmonic hot spot can be approximated to be on the order of |E|4. Therefore, the SERS signal is extremely sensitive to the local field enhancement, similar to the SHG case. In the gaps (hot spots) of closely positioned noble-metal NPs, the value of EF can be as large as 1010 at the plasmon resonance. In a comprehensive study by Le Ru and Etchegoin,480 they concluded that Raman EFs as low as 107, as opposed to the factor of 1014 initially claimed for the SM-SERS studies, are sufficient for the observation of single-molecule SERS signals. However, this EF value is for molecules with large Raman scattering cross-section, such as crystal violet (CV) and rhodamine-6G molecules, and the required minimum EF for SM-SERS is strongly analyte specific.

Although SERS was discovered more than forty years ago, quantitative SERS measurements remain still very challenging. Quantitative SERS at the single-molecule level is crucial for the precise determination of analyte concentration, which is very useful for biological and chemical sensing applications. For this reason, it is important to have all of the analyte molecules on the SERS substrates to be detected and accounted for. However, due to the stochastic nature of hot spots on conventional SERS substrates comprised of randomly formed or specifically fabricated noble-metal nanostructures (see Fig. 50 for some examples in the literature483–486), only a few experimental reports in the literature are focused on quantitative SERS measurements.74–77

Several major technical challenges need to be addressed to realize quantitative SM-SERS in an efficient and reliable way. First of all, uniform hot-spot properties across the SERS substrate is an important prerequisite for quantitative measurements. However, the required top-down nanofabrication precision to create metal nanostructure arrays with uniform hot spot properties is very difficult to achieve, if not totally impossible. The layer-by-layer assembled colloidal gold and silver NP films introduced in Section 6 appear to be a good solution for this challenge because they show a strong, uniform, and tunable plasmonic response.190,191 Moreover, this NP self-assembly method can also be scaled up to fabricate SERS substrates with a large detection area (>1 cm2). Recently, Chen et al. have adopted this approach to fabricate NP metasurfaces for quantitative SM-SERS detection and have confirmed that it is a simple, robust SERS method for quantitative measurements at the single-molecule level.77 In this experimental demonstration (Fig. 51 and 52), colloidal AgNPs are chosen as the meta-atom building blocks of plasmonic metasurfaces (see Fig. 51a and b) because of their superior plasmonic properties among all plasmonic metals.

The second technical challenge is to obtain a reproducible SERS signal. For this purpose, spectral matching of the hot spots on the SERS substrate with the laser excitation wavelength is necessary. For the NP array structures, this requirement involves the tunable interparticle separation across the SERS substrate. As discussed earlier, one of the main advantages of using alkanethiolate surface ligands for assembling NP superlattices, besides protecting the Ag NPs from degradation in ambient environment and regulating the formation of plasmonic hot spot arrays, is that the interparticle gap can be controlled with an atomic-scale precision by using different alkanethiolate carbon chain lengths.191 By changing the near-field coupling strength (i.e. interparticle separation) between adjacent NPs, one can vary the collective plasmon resonances of close-packed Ag NP films to match with the excitation laser wavelength used for SERS measurements. In the NP self-assembly method, the plasmonic peak position can be tuned precisely and uniformly across the SERS substrate by varying the carbon-chain length of alkanethiolate ligands and the resulting interparticle spacing. This plasmonic coupling effect can be readily visualized by the purple and green colors displayed in the transmission and reflection photographs, respectively, as shown in Fig. 51b.

Furthermore, control over the optimized separation between the analytes and the hot spots is necessary because the SERS signal can be dramatically changed when the location of the analyte relative to the hot spot is only slightly varied on the nanometer scale. In SERS measurements, the overall signal intensity is the sum of signals from all the analytes on the whole detection area covered by the excitation laser beam. In a study by Fang et al.,481 it has been shown that the local Raman EFs on Ag-film-on-nanosphere SERS substrates exhibit a broad distribution ranging from 104 to 1010. Notably, one-half of the overall SERS signal comes from the hot spots with enhancement factors greater than 108, which only account for less than 1% of total hot spots. These findings answer most of the questions about the inhomogeneous nature of the SERS substrates reported in the literature. Over the years, many research efforts have been devoted to SERS substrates providing the largest magnitude of SERS enhancement. In contrast, the strategy proposed by Chen et al. for reliable quantitative SERS measurement is to avoid anomalously hot detection sites (traditional hot spots) by using an ultrathin analyte-embedding dielectric layer, and position analytes in a uniform hot zone, which is several nanometers above the AgNP superlattice film (see Fig. 51c). Using this plasmonic metasurface approach, the achieved Raman enhancement factor is sufficiently high (>1.2 × 107) and spatially uniform (<5%) for single-molecule detection (see Fig. 52a–c).

The third technical barrier for quantitative SM-SERS concerns the measurement of absolute SERS signals. The Raman scattering intensity typically depends on various instrumental conditions, rendering it difficult to reproduce the signal even for the same sample. A solution for this problem is to establish internal standards, which can come from the SERS signals of independent and stable components included in the sample. Recently, it has been demonstrated that the SERS signal of passivating alkanethiolate ligands on the Ag NP surfaces can be used as a stable internal calibration standard for quantitative SM-SERS measurements.77 In their work, the capability of quantitative SERS detection at the SM level is demonstrated by measuring the areal density of crystal violet molecules embedded in an ultrathin (5 nm) spin-on-glass hot zone, which is a uniform plasmon-enhanced region with a well-controlled spatial separation above the Ag NP monolayer. The quantitative SERS measurement results show a linear response over a wide dynamic range of analyte concentrations (5–5000 molecules per μm2, see Fig. 52d).

8. Conclusions and outlook

One of the ultimate goals of plasmonics is the spectral and spatial control of plasmonic coupling and hot spot generation, which requires precise design, fabrication, and assembly of metallic meta-atoms into metastructures. For the spectral control, it is necessary to have microscopic control over plasmonic building blocks (meta-atoms) in terms of their size, shape, symmetry, composition, and inter-meta-atom coupling strength. For the spatial control, it is important to have macroscopic control over the spatial uniformity of hot spots in plasmonic metastructures formed by coupling of constituent meta-atoms. In this review, we have presented two complementary approaches based on the nanomanipulation and controlled self-assembly of metal nanocrystals synthesized by colloidal chemistry. The plasmonic meta-atoms are easy and widely available to produce, controllable in size and shape, of high crystalline quality, and give us flexibility in material choices (alloys, core–shell structures, surface functionalization schemes).

It is clear that controlled self-assembly is the method of choice to realize scalable two-dimensional and three-dimensional plasmonic metastructures. In this review, we have shown some simple, robust, and scalable self-assembly approaches for plasmonic metastructures composed of colloidal noble-metal NPs or NCs. Regarding the future perspective of self-assembly approaches, the layer-by-layer assembly technique of plasmonic NPs/NCs and other materials simultaneously can provide a unique pathway to fabricate conformal plasmonic heterostructures on curved and artificially structured surfaces with unique plasmonic metastructure properties. In addition to being used as building blocks, it is also a promising approach to utilize top-down lithographic techniques to fabricate plasmonic metastructures using large colloidal noble-metal crystals, synthesized by bottom-up chemistry methods with unprecedented material quality. This combined approach allows the design flexibility of meta-atoms and coupling mechanisms for plasmonic metastructures, and at the same time fulfills the stringent requirements for both material properties and quality.

Regarding the future perspective of nanomanipulation approaches, while there have been efforts to develop arrays of tips to be used in scanning-probe-based nanomanipulation applications, nanomanipulation is likely to remain as a unique tool for fundamental research on individual metamolecules and hybrid nanostructure assemblies. AFM-based nanomanipulation combines the advantages of top-down and bottom-up fabrication techniques. The individual components are synthesized using solution-based chemical methods. The typical assembly process relies on a single scanning probe. Although the single-probe assembly process is a serial, slow process, it provides the flexibility of selecting different types of NPs and varying the geometry of a cluster. Promising future technical developments in this area include manipulation of nano-objects with different shapes and rigidity, more refined and automated nanomanipulation protocols, exploring new probe tips with the capability of picking up and releasing NPs on demand, and simultaneous transport/optical measurements during manipulation processes. Nanomanipulation methods may be applied to address key scientific challenges in materials science in the mesoscopic regime. For example, while many previous studies have investigated both individual and ensembles of nanostructures, relatively little effort has been devoted to understand how collective excitations and properties emerge in nanomaterials consisting of 10–1000 NP or NC building blocks with designed coupling properties.
