Abstract

The surface wettability control of solid materials has been considered as an essential aspect of surface chemistry. In the past decade, superhydrophobic surfaces have revealed a cornucopia of novel structural and functional properties, exhibiting considerable importance in both fundamental research and practical applications. In this review, we summarize the recent developments of superhydrophobic surfaces with unique structural and functional properties. Both the fabricative methods and the working performance of superhydrophobic surfaces with multidisciplinary functionalities including self-cleaning, icephobicity, anti-corrosion, drag reduction, transparency, anti-reflection, structural color, droplet transportation, anisotropy, oilwater separation, water supporting force, superamphiphobicity and responsive switching, have been discussed briefly. Finally, the current challenges and future prospects of this dynamic field are discussed based on our own opinion.

1 Introduction

As an essential aspect of surface chemistry, the wettability control of a solid surface shows enormous value in both fundamental research and practical applications.14 Ever since the discovery of the lotus leaf effect,5,6 superhydrophobic surfaces with ultra high water contact angles (CAs, larger than 150) have earned rapidly increased interest as evidenced by the vast body of related publications.710 Particularly in the past decade, more and more multidisciplined researchers have participated in this dynamic field, and thus superhydrophobic surfaces have revealed a cornucopia of both novel functionalities and broad applications.24 On the basis of classical theories (i.e. the Wenzel and CassieBaxter models),11,12 there exist two crucial factors that govern the surface wetting behavior. One is the surface roughness represented by various periodically or randomly distributed micro/nanostructures, and the other is the surface chemical composition. According to this basic principle,13,14 various hydrophobic surfaces have been successfully prepared by combining hierarchical surface structures with low surface energy material coatings, such as fluoroalkylsilane (FAS).15,16 Generally, due to the diversity of the surface topography (e.g., hierarchical morphologies, periodic arrangements and orientations), the inherent multi-scale structures not only contribute to the universal water resistance, but also impart other structural and functional properties to the superhydrophobic surfaces.4,17 In recent years, beyond the research on the fabrication of superhydrophobic surfaces, more and more attention has been paid to the structural design and functional integration of superhydrophobic surfaces towards various scientific and practical applications.1822

In fact, the development of multifunctional superhydrophobic surfaces has been effectively motivated by mimicking nature.2330 In nature, various plants and animals exhibit promising structurally-defined functionalities in addition to the superhydrophobicity derived from special surface structure and chemical composition.13,3134 In order to endow man-made materials with surface properties or functionalities similar to or even better than that of natural materials, great efforts have been devoted to the fabrication of artificial materials with biomimetic morphology through various micronanofabrication techniques.3538Fig. 1 shows some successful bio-prototypes which have inspired researchers to develop such multifunctional superhydrophobic surfaces. For example, mimicking lotus leaves, superhydrophobic films with self-cleaning properties have been readily prepared; learning from butterfly wings, iridescent superhydrophobic surfaces with anisotropic wettability have been fabricated. Actually, superhydrophobic bio-prototypes are not limited to the well-known examples which have been reported elsewhere,29,39 with the rapid progress of interdisciplinary cooperation, newly discovered biomaterials or bio-behavior will continuously stimulate research interest in superhydrophobic surfaces with exceptional structural and functional properties. Besides learning from nature, the gradually increasing requirements on high-performance materials and smart surfaces will also push forward the rapid development of multifunctional superhydrophobic surfaces.

In this review, we will focus on the recent developments in superhydrophobic surfaces with novel structural and functional properties, especially, in the realms of anti-water functionalities derived from airsolid composite interfaces, optical properties, manipulation of droplets and other novel applications such as oilwater separation, water supporting forces and responsive switching. Recently developed methodologies for the fabrication of multifunctional superhydrophobic surfaces in combination with their excellent working performance in the above-mentioned applications have been discussed. The unique structural and functional properties of superhydrophobic surfaces have been summarized, and our personal opinion on the challenges and prospects within this topic is briefly discussed.

2 The unique structural and functional properties

2.1 Airsolid composite interfaces

When a water droplet contacts a superhydrophobic surface, typically, in a CassieBaxter state, a thin air layer is trapped between the water droplet and the solid substrate due to the presence of hierarchical structures, and thus waterairsolid composite interfaces are usually formed instead of simple watersolid interfaces.11,40 The presence of an air layer significantly reduces the contact area between the water droplet and the solid surface,41 which consequently brings various unique functional properties towards different applications. For example, with the help of the air layer, a water droplet can roll off easily when the superhydrophobic surface is tilted at a small angle, giving self-cleaning properties;13 the air layer also prevents contact with corrosive solutions and protects the substrate effectively, or acts as a lubricant to reduce the fluidic drag. In the following section, we will discuss the unique functional properties of superhydrophobic surfaces in the realms of self-cleaning properties,8,4245 icephobicity,4652 anti-corrosion5359 and fluidic drag reduction,40,6068 which are generally due to the presence of waterairsolid composite interfaces.
2.1.1 Self-cleaning properties. In nature, lotus leaves always take on a clean appearance despite growing in a mire environment, because water droplets can roll off immediately upon contact with the leaf, and take away the dirt adhered on its surface at the same time. The promising self-cleaning properties, the so-called lotus effect, have triggered research interest in the development of artificial superhydrophobic surfaces towards dirt-resistant applications. Based on experimental investigations on the surface of the lotus leaf, it has been found that the presence of hierarchical micro-nanostructures and hydrophobic epicuticular wax are critical for the formation of the superhydrophobicity.13 Therefore, artificial superhydrophobic surfaces with self-cleaning properties have been readily designed and prepared based on a principle of combining hierarchical roughness with low surface energy materials.

Fig. 2b shows the concept of the self-cleaning behavior of a superhydrophobic surface. As compared with a general surface, the water droplet on a superhydrophobic surface shows a quasi-spherical shape. When it is tilted to a certain angle, the water droplet rolls off the surface easily and picks up foreign dirt particles at the same time, this is considering the fact that most dust shows relatively strong adherence to water in comparison to a solid surface. In this way, superhydrophobic surfaces can keep their surfaces clean. Typically, when the sliding angle of a superhydrophobic surface is lower than 10, it means that the surface has self-cleaning properties.4

When both of the surfaces are tilted to a certain degree, denoted as , the water droplet just passes over the dust on the normal surface, whereas the round droplet on the superhydrophobic surface rolls off the surface with the dust.

Notably, superhydrophobic surfaces with self-cleaning properties show great potential in various realms, from windshield and architecture coatings to solar cells and anti-biofouling surfaces. For instance, in the solar cell,69,70 the superhydrophobic surface prevents efficiency degradation caused by dust or particles accumulated on the panels. Recently, Zhu et al.,69 fabricated nanodome solar cells by introducing close paced manocones into the glass or quartz substrates via LangmuirBlodgett assembly and reactive ion etching. By simply rolling a water droplet across the surface, the accumulated dust of different sizes and shapes on the device can be cleaned up, which effectively avoids the blocking of sunlight and the induced efficiency reduction. Besides, Yang and Jiang,71 created self-assembled macroporous films by roll-to-roll compatible doctor bladed coating technology and surface functionalization. After being submerged in a bacteria culture solution for 24 h, only less than 1% of the bacterial contaminant was left on the inclined sample at an angle of 5, showing the unique anti-biofouling property.

The self-cleaning surface is so promising that it could make all kinds of common surfaces clean and smart. However, for routine applications the stability and durability of the materials should be taken into account, as the tiny micro-nanostructures would be destroyed during repeated use. Moreover, the oleophobicity also plays an important role in the dirt-resistance property. For the development of self-cleaning surfaces, a robust superamphiphobic coating with high stability is highly desired.

2.1.2 Icephobicity. Water-repellent properties found in nature have continuously inspired researchers to develop anti-water materials or structures towards novel functionality. For instance, the compound eyes of mosquitoes show anti-fogging properties.72 Inspired by this phenomenon, superhydrophobic surfaces have been proposed to prevent ice accumulation, as droplets can bounce off from the superhydrophobic surface before ice nucleation takes place even in supercooled conditions. As compared with conventional thermal or chemical approaches, superhydrophobic surfaces provide a relatively simple but effective strategy to tackle the frost deposition problem, which frequently induces damaging occurrences in both daily life and aeronautics, such as slippery roads, frozen engines, attenuated signasl, and burst pipes. Triggered by the promising applications, the icephobicity of superhydrophobic surfaces has been investigated both theoretically and experimentally.

For example, Tourkine et al.,49 demonstrated delayed freezing on water repellent materials taking advantage of the presence of the airsolid composite interface; He et al.,48 pointed out that superhydrophobic films can retard frost formation; Cao et al.,51 reported direct experimental evidence that superhydrophobic surfaces prepared using nanoparticlepolymer composites are able to prevent ice formation upon impact with supercooled water both in laboratory conditions and in the natural environment. In the above mentioned studies, the work was mainly focused on the freezing process of static droplets resting on supercooled surfaces, however, ice accretion generally begins with dropletsubstrate collision followed by freezing, which is a dynamic process. Based on this argument, Aizenberg and coworkers,73 studied the dynamic droplet freezing experimentally and theoretically by analyzing a broad range of geometries, impact angles and droplet temperatures, as well as the physical mechanism. As shown in Fig. 3, through the comparison of the ice accumulation process on hydrophilic, hydrophobic, and superhydrophobic surfaces, the icephobicity of the superhydrophobic surface is quite obvious. The researchers also suggested that structures with close-cell geometries may be favorable in mechanical robust and pressure stability. Zheng et al.,52 fabricated impact icephobic carbon nanotube films by a straightforward and cost-effective acetone treatment, which has been proven to be promising for mass production. It is recognized that the entrapped air underneath the water surface could be responsible for the delayed droplet solidification. Triple phase line design is another important factor, as the heat transfer rate in this region is faster than that in other areas and solidification tends to be initiated from here.

Despite the successful examples of anti-icing surfaces based on superhydrophobic films, there are also opposite opinions.46 Poulikakos and coworkers, investigated water freezing delays on various surfaces ranging from hydrophilic to superhydrophobic and evaluated their icephobicity. As compared with superhydrophobic surfaces that possess larger hierarchical structures, the surfaces with nanometre roughness and higher wettability showed longer freezing delays, more than 1 order of magnitude. Their results directly bring in to question the emphasis on superhydrophobic surfaces for the retardation of ice formation, and suggest that the rational design of anti-icing surfaces must optimize the competing influences of both surface roughness and wettability.

2.1.3 Anti-corrosive coating. In the practical environment, many surfaces may gradually deteriorate due to corrosive reactions, especially in the cases of reactive metals widely employed in industrial applications. Despite methods including oil paint and treatment with chromium containing compounds as anti-corrosive layers, concerns regarding human health and environmental effects have significantly limited their broad application in both daily life and industry. Alternatively, superhydrophobic coating can be listed as a much more effective method to solve this problem.5359 When coated superhydrophobic surfaces are immersed in corrosive solutions, air trapped in the surface valleys between the rough structures and the liquid form natural isolations and prevent direct contact between the corrosive ions and the substrate. In this regard, superhydrophobic films coated on metal substrates could be used as effective corrosive inhibitors.

Typically, active metallic materials (e.g., copper, iron, magnesium and their alloys) do not resist the corrosive environment well, for instance, in the presence of aggressive chloride anions. Thus superhydrophobic coatings fabricated as anti-corrosive layers are generally applied to metallic substrates. Fig. 4 shows the preparation of a superhydrophobic and color-tuned magnesium alloy with anticorrosive properties and damping capacity through a simple chemical-free immersion process followed by surface modification with n-octadecyltrimethoxysilane.56 The corrosion resistance of the superhydrophobic magnesium alloy was investigated by using electrochemical potentiodynamic measurements. By mimicking the superhydrophobicity of plant leaves (Xanthosoma sagittifolium), Weng et al., reported a superhydrophobic electroactive epoxy coating applied as corrosion protection on cold rolled steel by direct replication of fresh plant leaves.57 The obtained material exhibited a synergistic effect of superhydrophobicity and redox catalytic capability, as well as remarkably enhanced corrosion protection. In addition, Xu et al.,74 generated a superhydrophobic surface by electroless galvanic deposition of a nano Ag film followed by the self-assembly of n-octadecanethiol. The prepared metal substrate is stable at different pH values from 1 to 13. Xu et al.,59 fabricated a superhydrophobic surface on Mg alloys via electrochemical machining using a relatively nonpoisonous neutral aqueous solution at room temperature, and was subsequently covered with a fluoroalkylsilane film. A high contact angle above 160 was still obtained after 24 h of immersion in 3.5 wt% NaCl aqueous, or 6 months of air exposure, indicating excellent anti-corrosive properties.

2.1.4 Drag reduction. On immersed superhydrophobic surfaces, there usually exist solidgas composite interfaces due to air filling.11 In this case, water tends to be stopped on the solid fraction while being locally accelerated on the waterair boundary in the flowing condition. This lubricating effect, known as the surface slip,7578 endows superhydrophobic surfaces with promising potential for underwater applications. Compared with the conventional bubble injunction method for drag reduction, for example with hovercraft,66 the volatile and energy-efficient superhydrophobic method is considered to be a superior alternative.

Over the past decade, the fluidic drag reduction effect of superhydrophobic surfaces has been investigated both theoretically and experimentally, and there are a lot of successful examples. For instance, Fukagata et al.,61 theoretically predicted that drag reduction could be achieved by superhydrophobic surfaces not only in laminar flow, but also in turbulent channel flow. In order to experimentally clarify the hypothesis that superhydrophobic surfaces can effectively decrease the fluidic drag, Zhang and coworkers,79 fabricated superhydrophobic coatings on gold threads, and compared the moving rate with the normally hydrophobic one. In their work, the gold threads were powered by the catalytic decomposition of H2O2 on the Pt deposited end. Experiment results confirm that a superhydrophobic coating can effectively reduce the fluidic drag for objects moving in water.

Recently, McHale and coworkers,66 created a uniform superhydrophobic nanoribbon layer on the inside of round copper tubes and experimentally tested the fluid flow behavior in both the superhydrophobic tubes and in as received tubes for comparison. In their experiments, a reservoir is used to feed the two tubes with open ends via a T-piece. As shown in Fig. 5, once the flow commences, the fluid flows down through the superhydrophobic tube preferentially, indicating the low frictional drag of the superhydrophobic tube wall. Typically, Newtonian liquids undergo laminar flow across solid surfaces, and it is commonly assumed that, in a circular solid channels, the velocity profile is a parabola, as shown in Fig. 5b. So the maximum flow rate is considered to be at the mid-point of the channel. However, in the boundary condition between a liquid and a gas, the maximum velocity is close to the liquidair interface due to the continuity of the shear stress (Fig. 5b). In this case, the frictional drag between the solid and liquid interface is replaced by a much lower frictional drag between liquid and air. Providing that the channel walls were coated with a superhydrophobic layer, the friction between fluids and channel walls is quite similar to that of the fluidair interface, because there exists a layer of air in the superhydrophobic walls, and therefore it presents a plug velocity profile (Fig. 5c).

Later, Carlborg and van der Wijngaart,80 introduced a novel self-regulating design for friction reduction in laminar flow systems with large channels and high pressure. An inevitable problem which limits the drag reduction by superhydrophobic surfaces to the laboratory scale is that the gas may diffuse away over time or the gas bubble would collapse under pressure. Lee and Kim,81 solved this problem by developing surface structures that allow the restoration of a gas layer under an immersed state and a self-controlled gas-generation mechanism that maintains the superhydrophobic condition under high liquid pressures, which therefore, removes a fundamental barrier against practical application.

2.2 Optical properties

2.2.1 Transparency. In addition to the typical self-cleaning properties, recently, superhydrophobic surfaces have also faced some specific applications which require optical transparency as well, such as windshields, window glass, display screens and even solar cells. Actually, the two properties (superhydrophobicity and transparency) are generally competitive.82 It is well known that a superhydrophobic substrate requires hierarchical structures to improve surface roughness, whereas rough surfaces constructed by various micro-nanostructures may be completely opaque due to the extensive light scattering effect. Consequently, to avoid Mie scattering of visible light, the feature size of the rough texture should be theoretically smaller than the wavelength of light, preferably less than 80 nm.83

To date, there exist some successful examples of achieving superhydrophobic surfaces with remarkable transparency.8295 A general approach to a transparent and superhydrophobic surface is the deposition of hydrophobic silica nanoparticles prepared by surface modification with low surface energy silane on a general glass substrate.88,89,95 For instance, He and coworkers,88 reported a transparent and superhydrophilic film prepared via the layer-by-layer (LbL) assembly of mesoporous silica nanoparticles, followed by calcination. After post treatment with a low surface energy material (fluoroalkyl silane), the surface became superhydrophobic (CA >150) with a very low sliding angle, while maintaining high transparency (>90%). In a similar manner, Yang and coworkers,87 reported a simple and versatile approach to a highly transparent superhydrophobic surface by the sequential dip coating of 3-aminopropyltrimethoxysilane (APTS)-functionalized silica nanoparticles of two different sizes (100 and 20 nm), followed by thermal annealing and hydrophobilization with fluoroalkyl silane. As shown in Fig. 6, when the hierarchical nanoparticle film was coated onto a glass substrate, the letters underneath were visible. The UV-vis spectra showed that it maintained a relative transmission larger than 99% in the visible wavelength region with respect to bare glass (Fig. 6b).

In addition to the silica nanoparticle coated films, nanoporous polymers which possess both hierarchical roughness and low surface energy frameworks have also been used for coating superhydrophobic surfaces with simultaneous optical transparency. For example, Frchet and coworkers,83 fabricated superhydrophobic nanoporous polymer layers through an in situ polymerization of divinylbenzene in the presence of a porogenic solvent. By simply tuning the composition of the mixture used in preparation, the average globule size of the porous polymers decreased from 486 nm to 45 nm, while the corresponding transparency gradually increased. Later, Zhang et al.,96 also achieved transparent superhydrophobicity by coating a transparent PDMS slice with a novel nanoporous polymer chalk prepared through solvothermal polymerization. Moreover the nanoporous polymer chalk can impart water-resistance to almost any nonplanar surface, such as paper, and even human skin, while retaining the transparent feature (Fig. 6c).

Lee and coworkers,86 introduced a facile method to fabricate transparent, conductive films from a mixture containing multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MCNTs) and a silane sol. By varying the silane compound used for the surface coatings (e.g., tetraethoxysilane, methyltrimethoxysilane, fluorinated silane, glycidoxypropyltrimethoxysilane, etc.), the wettability of the hybrid films can be controlled in a wide range, from superhydrophobicity to superhydrophilicity. In addition, both the transparency and the surface superhydrophobicity of the CNTsilane conductive films can be improved by introducing nanoparticles into the coating solution.

More recently, Vollmer and coworkers,97 designed an easily fabricated, transparent, and oil-rebounding superamphiphobic coating by using candle soot as a template. In their work, a porous deposit of candle soot was coated with a 25 nanometre-thick silica shell. The black coating became transparent after calcination at 600 C. After silanization, the coating was superamphiphobic and remained so even after its top layer was damaged by sand impingement. In combination with the optical transparent feature, superhydrophobic surfaces could find broad applications as window materials for either architectural glass or electronic display screens.

2.2.2 Anti-reflection. Anti-reflection is absolutely important to window materials widely used in optical devices such as solar cells, organic light-emitting diodes (OLED) and lasers. Inspired by moth-eyes,98,99 microstructured surfaces with sub-wavelength hexagonal honeycomb microstructures have been developed to efficiently reduce light reflection, and therefore gain high transmittance. The combination of anti-reflection and superhydrophobicity makes multifunctional films promising for developing novel optoelectronic devices. For example, in solar cells,70 efficient light management by reducing incident light reflection while enhancing optical absorption is important for performance improvement and cost reduction. Additionally, the superhydrophobicity may also contribute to the self-cleaning behavior. Taking advantage of the two merits, Cui and coworkers,69 reported novel nanodome solar cells, which have periodic nanoscale modulations for all layers from the bottom substrate, through the active absorber to the top transparent contact. Photographs of nanodomes and flat films show that the nanodome devices look black due to efficient anti-reflection and light trapping, whereas flat film devices are red colour because of inefficient light absorption at long wavelengths, indicating the anti-reflection property of the nanodome structure (Fig. 7). Additionally, the nanodome shape can also efficiently couple the incident light into the guided modes, contributing to the high transmittance. Interestingly, after surface treatment with hydrophobic molecules (perfluorooctyl trichlorosilane, PFOS), the nanodome surface shows hydrophobicity (CA of 141 2), due to the combination of nanodome morphology and the low surface energy coating layer. The hydrophobicity endows the nanodome surface with self-cleaning properties, which offers the capability for dust removal and therefore further improves light harvesting.

2.2.3 Structural color. Besides lotus leaves, some surfaces with beautiful colors in nature also possess unique superhydrophobicity. For instance, butterfly wings (e.g., Morpho) exhibit a brilliant blue color and anisotropy in the water sliding direction;32 red rose petals have superhydrophobicity with a high adhesive force to water droplets; peacock feathers show multiple colors and patterns, together with superhydrophobicity.34 Typically, the presence of these beautiful colors in the superhydrophobic surfaces arises from the periodically arranged photonic crystal structures. These microstructures define multi-colors, which are usually called structural colors or iridescence, are generally believed to originate from the interaction of light with these two-dimensional (2D) or three-dimensional (3D) periodic sub-microstructures.

Inspired by iridescent natural materials, artificial micro/nanostructured films have also been developed to replicate or partly replicate promising structural colors. In most cases, these iridescent films synchronously possess unique hydrophobicity, and some of them could reach a degree of superhydrophobicity. The simplest method for the reconstruction of these photonic crystal structures is direct replication. For example, by using the butterfly wing as a hard template, various metal oxide duplicates with structural colors could be obtained through novel approaches such as low-temperature atomic layer deposition (ALD) and the solgel approach.38,103 In addition to butterfly wings, the red rose is another example which possesses both iridescence and superhydrophobicity.33,35 Observation of the rose petal surface by either optical microscopy or SEM shows that there are micropapillae all over the surface and nanofolders exist on each papilla, which endow the petal with superhydrophobicity. Interestingly, on these nanofolders, periodically arranged nanorods with much tinier sizes were also observed, which contribute to the structural color. By directly duplicating the hierarchical structures on the rose petal, a polymeric duplicate further confirms the presence of structural color, since no pigment was introduced.

Mimicking the structural color of natural materials, colloidal photonic crystal films with both structural color and superhydrophobicity have been successfully prepared by the self-assembly of polystyrene spheres and silica nanoparticles.104 In addition to the self assembly approach, Sun and coworkers105 reported bio-inspired photonic surface structures consisting of periodic micro-needle arrays covered with nano-sized silver protrusions, which are prepared by the multi-beam laser interference patterning of photopolymers and subsequent electroless plating. The resultant films exhibit superhydrophobicity and synchronous iridescent colors.

Recently, inspired by butterfly wings and red rose petals, Zhang and coworkers106 reported the fabrication of superhydrophobic and iridescent graphene films by the two beam laser interference technique. As shown in Fig. 8, a periodic grating structure with an average period of 2 m was created through the laser ablation and removal of oxygen containing groups such as hydroxyl, epoxy and carboxyl groups. The origin of such a beautiful iridescence could be explained by the light diffraction of the grating. In addition to the 2D grating structure, on the edge of the grating patterns, a nanofolder structure was also formed due to the layered arrangement of the graphene oxide sheets. These hierarchical structures contribute to a high adhesive superhydrophobic property, similar to that observed on rose petals. Since the period of the grating on the graphene film could be tuned in a certain range, the optical properties could be manipulated in a controlled manner by changing the laser processing parameters. Recently, Kim and coworkers reported an electroactive subwavelength grating for color and intensity modulation. This conductive polymer grating changes its color intensity by the applied potential in wide spectral range.107 Therefore, it can be inferred that the graphene grating structure together with the unique surface wettability will attract intense interest in the development of graphene based optoelectronic devices.

2.3 Manipulation of water droplets

2.3.1 Droplet transportation. Generally, a water droplet on a superhydrophobic surface usually takes a quasi-spherical shape which dramatically reduces the contact area between the liquid and the solid, and accordingly avoids being contaminated by the surrounding medium. In particular, with the rapid development of superhydrophobic materials as well as novel wettability control techniques, water droplets could be pinned to the substrate or roll down freely from the surface without any residuals in a controlled fashion. In this regard, superhydrophobic surfaces become a valuable alternative in the mass-loss-free transportation of water droplets. Typical examples can be found in the work reported by Jiang and coworkers.108,109 In their work, a superhydrophobic surface constructed by aligned polystyrene (PS) nanotubes was successfully prepared by using a porous alumina membrane covering method. The unique nanotube topography endows the film surface with not only considerable roughness which contributes to the superhydrophobicity, but also to a strong adhesive effect on the water droplet, about 52 N, due to the capillary force and the capillarity-induced negative pressure. The PS nanotube surface could be used as a mechanical hand for the controllable and no-loss transportation of superparamagnetic microdroplets by manipulating the extra magnetic field. Later, inspired by the gecko's foot which can reversibly stick and unstick to various surfaces, a novel superhydrophobic iron surface was fabricated by the controlled acid corrosion of iron plates and subsequent fluoroalkylsilane modification.108 A tunable adhesive force to a superparamagnetic microdroplet could be achieved by simply switching between the magnetizing and demagnetizing state of the surface, due to the cooperation of the soft ferromagnetic property of iron plates and the microstructure. Such an intelligent surface holds great promise for a broad range of applications including biochemical separation, in situ analysis, and in particular the no-loss transport of microdroplets.

Recently, Lin et al.,110 reported the preparation of mechanically robust magnetic liquid marbles by simply coating a highly hydrophobic Fe3O4 nanoparticle layer on a water droplet. The magnetic liquid marbles are nonstick to all kinds of substrates such as flat glass plates, which provides the possibility for the manipulation of a single water droplet by magnetic field actuation. They found that, under the action of a magnetic field, the reversible opening and closing of the magnetic liquid marbles as well as the coalescing of two liquid marbles into a larger one could be easily realized, revealing a potential application as a single droplet microreactor for allowing non-loss transportation.

Besides the manipulation of magnetic droplets by using superhydrophobic substrates together with an external magnetic field, pure water droplets can also be transported in a non-loss manner providing the pinned and roll-down superhydrophobic states can be reversibly switched through a certain scheme. In our previous work,111 we reported the reversible tuning of the superhydrophobicity of PDMS micro-pillar arrays from the pinned to the roll-down state by adjusting the surface curvature. Both a schematic illustration and the video frames of the water droplet transportation are shown in Fig. 9. Typically, the PDMS micro-pillar array film, with a period of 2 m, shows a very high adhesion force to water droplets, represented by the pinned superhydrophobic state. When the surface curvature was increased to a certain range, both the adhesion force and the SA (sliding angle) decreased significantly (SA <5), which is a typical feature of the Cassie superhydrophobic state. The origination of the curvature induced switching between the pinned and the roll down states can be attributed to the difference in the contact area of the substrate and the droplet under different curvature. Based on this unique switching, the surfaces can be employed as a mechanical hand for water pickup at flat form and release under certain curvature.

Recently, Ras and coworkers112 reported the low-friction and guided transport of water droplets by using a superhydrophobic track. In their work, water droplets could be driven by gravity or electrostatic forces along designed tracks which have vertical walls as gravitational potential barriers. By tilting the platform slightly, for instance by a few degrees, the drops can move at a considerable speed up to 14 cm s1, even in highly curved trajectories, indicating both the low-friction and the guided feature of the superhydrophobic track.

Controlled manipulation of small volumes of liquid, for example droplets, is of great importance in various miniature systems for both chemical and biological applications, such as high-throughput analysis and synthesis. In addition to microfluidic devices, superhydrophobic surfaces also provide an alternative method to reach this end. Once superhydrophobic surfaces bearing tunable interactions with water have been successfully developed, water droplets could be manipulated in a controlled manner through magnetic field, electrostatic forces, or gravity. The technology for non-loss drop transport based on superhydrophobic surfaces could exhibit great potential for the development of microfluidic devices (e.g., low-cost microfluidic chips, channel-free microfluidic systems, surface microfluidic channels)113118 and smart droplet microreactors towards a wide range of applications including liquid transportation, biochemical separation, localized chemical synthesis and in situ detection.

2.3.2 Anisotropy. With regards to some of the superhydrophobic surfaces in nature, the surface textures are asymmetric. Due to this unique structural feature, a water droplet on them usually has a preferential sliding mode or direction, which therefore makes it possible for the guided transportation of water droplets. For instance, the wings of a butterfly Morpho aega not only show unique superhydrophobicity, but also exhibit a directional adhesive force to water droplets.32 A droplet can easily roll off the surface of the wings along the radial outward direction of the central axis of the body, whereas it is pinned tightly to the surface against the radial outward direction, indicating the interesting anisotropic wettability. The strider leg possesses numerous oriented micrometre-scale needle-shaped setae arranged at an inclined angle of about 20 with nanoscale grooves on the surface.31 The oriented arrangement of micro-needle arrays may not only impart superhydrophobicity to the leg, but also lead to anisotropic dewetting, which greatly influences the hydrodynamics and brings a super-repellent force to water, and thus allows the strider to stand effortlessly and walk quickly on water. These examples show that the unique anisotropic wettability is not only critical for the development of self-cleaning surfaces with oriented water rolling behavior, but is also potentially useful in the directional transportation of water and novel microfluidic devices.

In recent years, triggered by the promising properties and functionalities, anisotropic superhydrophobic surfaces have been investigated both experimentally and numerically.119130 Generally, a superhydrophobic surface with anisotropic dewetting properties can be achieved by creating micrometre-scale parallel grooves with considerable roughness. To date, various technologies including photolithography, electrospinning, multi-beam laser interference, and surface wrinkling have been adopted to fabricate the quasi-1D groove-like microstructures. For example, Takahara and coworkers123 reported a macroscopic wetting anisotropy on a line-patterned surface prepared by vacuum ultraviolet photolithography. Brennan and coworkers122 achieved anisotropic wetting surfaces with tunable static and dynamic contact angles through a soft lithography technique. Seven patterned topographies comprising elongated discontinuous microfeatures oriented along parallel paths and one comprising ridges were successfully fabricated, and the static contact angle, advancing/receding contact angle, contact angle hysteresis, and slip angle were carefully measured at three in-plane perspectives based on the feature orientation.

Brueck and Xia,126 observed strongly anisotropic wetting behavior on one-dimensional nanopatterned surfaces prepared by multi-beam-laser interference lithography. Anisotropic contact angles from 5 to 79 have been successfully produced by the precise design of the different groove arrays. Moreover, the surface wetting behavior could be tuned from hydrophobic to hydrophilic while retaining the structural wetting anisotropy through a simple coating with silica nanoparticles. Later, the anisotropic wetting properties of the 1D submicrometre-scale grooved surface were further tailored by using a simple plasma treatment with different gas compositions (e.g., CHF3, CF4, O2) and polymer deposition, due to a change in the surface chemical composition.

The above-mentioned examples of anisotropic wetting surfaces are generally based on quasi-1D microgrooved structures, which suffer from low contact angles, typically, in Wenzel's state. Therefore, the anisotropy was usually depicted by static measurements such as CA, advancing/receding CA, and CA hysteresis. For superhydrophobic surfaces with exceptional anisotropy, it is obviously insufficient to evaluate the anisotropic wetting only by CAs and water droplet morphology. In this regard, the sliding behaviour of the water droplets becomes an important parameter in the characterization of the anisotropy dewetting property. As inspired by rice leaves, which show strong anisotropic wettability represented by the oriented guiding of water droplets to roll along the leaf vein heading to the stem, Sun and coworkers125 proposed a three-level structure model containing macrogrooves, micropapillae and nanostructures to elucidate the directional sliding mechanism. As shown in Fig. 10, the hierarchically biomimetic surface was prepared by photolithography and subsequent nanoporous polymer coating. The relationship between the groove width (50200 m) and anisotropy with different groove heights (1060 m) was investigated. In their work, the oriental grooves are crucial for the anisotropy in that they can make the droplet run in the parallel direction but energy is blocked in the perpendicular direction. According to the model, they prepared a line of artificial rice leaves, and the exhibited sliding angle difference between the two directions reached 5, analogous to the natural performance.

In nature there is also a unique ratchet like phenomenon, that is, a droplet can be easily translated in one direction while being tightly pinned backwards, as observed on the wing of the Morpho aega butterfly mentioned above. Mimicking butterfly wings, Lyons and Barahman,131 fabricated surfaces composed of tilted posts by a type of 3D printing. By controlling the dispense parameters, the slope of the asymmetric posts could be varied from 0 to 50. When the surface was tilted opposite to the feature slope, the contact line was observed to increase once the droplet began to slip, and the increased interaction made a much larger slip angle compared to that in the case when the surfaces were tilted parallel to the slope.

Based on the thorough investigation of the anisotropic wetting behavior both experimentally and theoretically, functional hydrophobic surfaces with unusual anisotropy can be readily designed and fabricated for the controllable manipulation of water droplets. The development of hydrophobic surface anisotropic wettability holds great promise for novel devices concerning microfluids and single droplets.

2.4 Oilwater separation

In recent years, the frequently occurring leakage of organic pollutants into water, such as crude oil and toxic aromatics, continuously threatens human health and daily life. Therefore, enormous efforts have been devoted to the effective removal and collection of these organic pollutants from water by using advanced techniques and materials.132 In particular, for accident remediation, the effective separation of such organic compounds from water becomes of considerable importance, not only for the reduction of the economic loss but also due to the environmental concerns. However, the currently available methods for oil cleanup are generally based on common adsorptive materials such as zeolites, activated carbon, natural clays, straw, and wool fibers. These adsorbents usually suffer from their hydrophilic nature, resulting in a relatively poor selectivity for organic pollutants. In practical applications, they can be saturated by water and thereafter become disabled in terms of oil adsorption.

Considering the distinct difference in surface tension between water (72.8 mN m1) and oil (<30 mN m1), superhydrophobic surfaces which are commonly oleophilic or superoleophilic provide a desirable choice for the cleanup of oil spillages.133138 Taking advantage of the unique water repellency properties, organic compounds can be selectively removed or collected from water in a highly effective manner. To reach this end, various superhydrophobic surfaces or materials have been successfully developed. As shown in Fig. 11, Jiang and coworkers133 reported a novel poly(tetrafluoroethylene)-coated mesh prepared by a facile and inexpensive spray-and-dry method. In their work, the mesh film shows both superhydrophobicity and superoleophilicity, represented by the high water CA of 156 2.8 (inset of Fig. 11a), and the very small water SA (4), as well as an oil CA of 0, which makes the mesh an enabler for oilwater separation. When an oil droplet is dropped onto the superhydrophobic and superoleophilic mesh, it spreads out rapidly and then penetrates through the mesh in about 240 ms (Fig. 11b). It can be reasonably inferred that when an oilwater mixture is poured onto the mesh film, oil will penetrate through the film freely, whereas, water will remain on the superhydrophobic film, showing the feasibility for oilwater separation. Similarly, Zhang and Seeger136 fabricated polyester textiles from volatile, inexpensive and nontoxic materials by a simple chemical vapor deposition method. When a mixture of water and oil was poured onto the textile coated with silicone filaments, oil penetrated deep inside while water was left outside and was collected thereafter.

In addition to the superhydrophobic and superoleophilic mesh, Zhang et al.137 prepared a nanoporous polydivinylbenzene material by a simple solvothermal polymerization. The nanoporous polymers exhibited high surface areas, adjustable pore sized, large pore volumed, and exceptional swelling properties in organic solvents (Fig. 11c). Particularly, due to the hierarchical roughness caused by the nanoporous structure, and the organic framework, the resultant materials show unique superhydrophobicity and superoleophilicity. All of these features impart a high-capacity and selective adsorption of oil to the nanoporous polymers, which makes them excellent candidates for the effective remediation of oil spillages and chemical leakages. It is worth noting that, as compared with conventional porous adsorbents, for instance activated carbon, the superhydrophobicity of the nanoporous polymers determines the high selectivity for organic compounds. Latterly, Zhu et al.138 reported a superhydrophobic and superoleophilic sponge that could be used for the facile removal and collection of oils from water surfaces. By the electroless deposition of copper and subsequent modification with superhydrophobic and superoleophilic coatings, the as-prepared sponges could selectively absorb various oils with a capacity of 13 times the materials' weight while repelling water completely.

In fact, for the development of novel materials and films which could be used for oil separation from water, there exist a lot of key factors, for instance, high absorption capacity, low cost, scalable fabrication, high selectivity, and excellent recyclability, among which superhydrophobicity plays an important role for achieving high selectivity. Therefore, only if novel superhydrophobic surfaces have been prepared in a low cost and mass producible manner, can they be practically used for oil collection and accident treatment.

2.5 Other functional properties

2.5.1 Water-supporting force. Due to the excellent water repellency when objects which are wrapped with superhydrophobic coatings fall onto the water surface, the surface tension on the objectliquid interface will generate repulsive forces acting like vigorous supports and prevent the object from sinking even if the target object has a higher density than water.31,79,139 Larmour et al.140 obtained superhydrophobic particles through galvanic deposition and 1-decanethiol modification of commercially available copper powders. The powders can spontaneously move closer together and form a robust close-packed sheet capable of supporting external loads. Although the meniscus will deform downwards into the water, the enhanced buoyancy caused by the surface water-repellency will lift the external loads up vigorously.
2.5.2 Superamphiphobicity. As most contaminants and pollutants are oily and organic materials, superhydrophobic surfaces exhibiting extremely high oil repellency, named superamphiphobic surfaces, are expected to have wider practical applications than solo superhydrophobic surfaces. However, it is a technical challenge to achieve this superamphiphobicity in that many oils have lower surface energies than water and thus show high affinities toward most of natural and artificial surfaces. Recently, reports demonstrated that a unique bifunctional material can be realized by finely designing the surface structure.97,141151 The as-prepared re-entrant or overhang texture was able to pin the composite contact line and prevented deep penetration, indicating effective resistance with a variety of liquids.
2.5.3 Responsive switching. The development of stimuli-responsive materials (SRMs) has provided the possibility to control surface chemical properties or morphology under special environmental conditions (e.g., temperature, light,152 pH value,153 electric field, metal ions, solvent polarity). Therefore, the surface wettability of the resultant superhydrophobic surfaces based on the SRMs could be tuned in a controlled fashion, leading to responsive and reversible switching among diverse surface dewetting states, represented by significantly changed water contact angles or different superhydrophobic regimes.111,154160 Responsive switching would make the superhydrophobic surface smart, and contribute to various exceptional functions.

3 Conclusions and future perspectives

Learning from natures masterpieces, a great many artificial superhydrophobic surfaces with multiscale structures have been successfully fabricated; and the desired water-resistance has also been reproduced. Inherited from the core properties of water repellency, superhydrophobic surfaces have earned many extending capabilities effortlessly, for instance, self-cleaning, icephobicity, anti-corrosive, anti-fogging, anti-corrosive and drag reduction. In addition, the intricate structures themselves also bring about many inherent properties including transparency, anti-reflection and structural color, which show great potential for novel optical devices. Moreover, the combination of material or structure defined properties with superhydrophobicity may generate some new functions. For instance, superhydrophobic surfaces with certain oleophilicity could be used for oilwater separation, and anisotropic superhydrophobic surfaces provide liquid-guiding capabilities. However, it is worth pointing out that the functional application of superhydrophobic surfaces is not limited to the reported ones. It is anticipated that more and more properties and functions will be developed in the ongoing research. Considering the diverse characters of the materials used in superhydrophobic surfaces, there will be more interesting and new features, though this is not included in this review. In addition to the pursual of new functionalities derived from surface superhydrophobicity, it is also highly desirable to prepare superhydrophobic structures with multifunctional integrations. With the cooperation of diverse functionalities, superhydrophobic surfaces could exhibit fascinating properties and thus hold great promise for multidisciplinary applications.

For practical applications in both daily life and industry, the durability and stability of superhydrophobic surfaces, which directly relate to the performance of the functional surfaces in working condition, should be taken into consideration. After all, in real situations, there are various external disturbances, which may cause changes in the surface chemical composition or morphology, resulting in the degradation or even loss of functionality. With this in mind, many robust non-wetting surfaces are under investigation, including the mechanical tolerance, thermal resistance, performance under hydraulic pressure, impact patience, and so on. All in all, the perfect integration of novel structural and functional properties and long service life is an eternal goal for artificial superhydrophobic surfaces. With the continuous development of interdisciplinary cooperation, multifunctional superhydrophobic surfaces prepared from all kinds of materials may find broad applications in the future.
