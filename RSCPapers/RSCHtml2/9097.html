Silicon: toward eco-friendly reduction techniques for lithium-ion battery applications

Abstract

Silicon-based anode materials have been intensively studied over the past few decades owing to their exceptionally high specific capacity. However, silicon (Si) mainly exists as Si(IV) in the form of silica (SiO2) or silicates in nature. The current industrial production methods of Si mainly cater to solar energy and semiconductor industry applications, and they usually suffer from high energy consumption in order to produce ultra-high purity Si. Therefore, developing efficient reduction techniques for producing Si, especially nanostructured Si (Nano Si) with comparable cost and scalability, is fundamentally desirable for the commercialization of Si anodes. In recent years, several pioneering avenues have been established to explore the fabrication of Si and Nano Si in regard to anode materials. A few environmentally friendly and cost-effective reduction strategies such as magnesiothermic reduction, magnesio-milling reduction, molten salt reduction, and electrochemical reduction have been developed. In this review, we summarize the progress on current reduction approaches, including the detailed mechanism, reaction conditions, and the relationship between the diverse morphologies and electrochemical performance. The current synthetic challenges and future research directions based on these routes are summarized, which is of great significance to the fundamental research and industrial application of Si-based anode materials.

1. Introduction

Silicon (Si) is an extremely important foundational material with traditional applications in the metallurgical,1 solar energy,2–4 and semiconductor industries.5–7 Owing to its unique physical and chemical properties, nanostructured silicon (Nano Si) exhibits a variety of promising applications in nanoelectronics,8–10 optics,11–13 sensors,14,15 biocatalysts,16,17 and lithium-ion batteries (LIBs).18–23 The ever-growing momentum behind renewable energy storage calls for advanced anodes to substitute for commercial graphite anodes.24–27 Among the numerous anode materials, Si has been regarded as the most promising next-generation candidate anode material due to its highest known theoretical capacity and relatively low potential plateau.28–36 As the second most abundant element in the crust, Si makes up 27.7% of the Earth's crust. However, it is naturally found in all rocks as well as in soil, sand, and clays, combined either with oxygen as silica or other elements (e.g., aluminum, magnesium, or sodium) as metal silicates.37,38

Normally, industrial production methods of producing bulk Si mainly include carbothermic reduction which can be used to prepare metallurgical grade Si,39 the Siemens process which can produce both solar-grade (99.9999%)40 and electronic grade Si (>99.9999999%)41 and silane (SiH4) chemical vapor decomposition (CVD), which is the primary route for scalable synthesis of electronic grade Si.42 In specific, carbothermic reduction is typically performed in an electric furnace at high temperatures over 1700 °C with coke as the reducing agent, and has poor energy efficiency and generates significant CO2 emissions.43 Siemens and SiH4 CVD methods are both limited to tedious processing steps that involve preparation of expensive precursors (SiHCl3 and SiH4) and sophisticated equipment, suffering from high energy consumption.44

Over the past few decades, a wide spectrum of strategies has been developed for preparing Nano Si. The most frequently used approaches can be generally divided into reduction of silicon halides,45–47 decomposition of silane using plasma or heat,48–50 oxidation of metal silicides,51–54 metallothermic reduction,55–57 molten salt reduction,58–60 chemical etching,61–63 electrochemical etching64–66 or reduction,67–70 and ball-milling from bulk Si.71–73 With the sustainable demand in the LIB field,74,75 developing environmentally benign and cost-effective strategies for Nano Si or Si applicable to anode materials is highly desirable.76 In this regard, all reagents involved in the reaction should be low-cost, innocuous and easy to handle. Besides, all the reaction operation conditions should be simple and practicable, avoiding high temperature and/or high pressure. When combining these requirements with the available methods, there are only a couple of them that can cater to this demand.

So far, several classical and good reviews have been published to summarize the significant progress on Si-based anode materials from different perspectives such as surface and interface engineering,77 atomic-scale monitoring,78 chronicle scale,79 practical application-oriented development20 and dimensional design.80 These reviews have systematically outlined a historical map for the explosive progress on Si-based anode materials.81 However, there are few reviews that have focused on discussing the reduction techniques for producing Nano Si or Si in view of their potential application in LIBs.82 Past reviews on the preparation of Nano Si have focused on other application fields such as quantum dots,5,83 solar energy84 and optics.85 To date, all the current reviews addressing Si for use in LIBs discuss the surface modification and structural design toward high energy LIBs.

In this review, the progress of the fundamental research activities on fabrication strategies for Nano Si is reviewed. The most promising reduction techniques, which are environmentally friendly, price effective, and scalable, are highlighted. We start by discussing the selection of precursors and reducing agents in terms of stability, cost and environmental friendliness. Then, the structure (size, crystallinity, porosity and dispersity)–property relationship for Si-based anode materials is clarified. In Section 4, the most promising and eco-friendly synthetic routes such as the magnesiothermic reduction reaction (MRR), magnesio-milling reduction (MMR), molten salt reduction (MSR), and electrochemical reduction (ECR) are discussed in detail. The diverse morphologies of the produced Si-based materials based on these methods and the corresponding electrochemical performance are further summarized. Finally, we propose the current challenges and future directions of each promising reduction strategy toward commercial applications of Si-based anode materials.

2. General background

2.1 Precursors (stability and cost)

As the most abundant and fundamental element, Si has a wide spectrum of chemical compounds ranging from inorganic substances to organics. Up to now, diverse Si compounds have been explored as precursors to produce Nano Si. Owing to the variable chemical bonding environment, different precursors have different stabilities which substantially affect the reduction activity. It has been well demonstrated that the precursor stability depends mainly on the bond dissociation enthalpy and on the decomposition kinetics.85 The bond dissociation energy (standard-state enthalpy change) for Si–O, Si–Cl, Si–C, Si–Si, and Si–H is 798, 456, 435, 327 and 298.49 kJ mol−1, respectively. The relatively low bond dissociation energy endows silanes such as silane (SiH4) and disilane (Si2H6) with high reactivity, and they have been the most commonly used precursors to prepare Nano Si through chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or vapor–liquid–solid (VLS) growth triggered by laser ablation.86 However, the highly pyrophoric and toxic nature of these kinds of precursors and the expensive ultrahigh vacuum equipment hinder the commercialization of these synthesis approaches.87 In the development of wet-chemical synthesis of Nano Si, SiCl4 and RCl3 (R = H, cotyl) are the frequently used precursors and several reduction strategies have been demonstrated.45,88 Although in both cases Si can be well produced, it is crucial to carry out the synthesis in an inert atmosphere, owing to the extreme reactivity of silanes and chlorosilanes towards water and oxygen. It can be found that more reactive precursors are more dangerous, more difficult to handle and more expensive.89 Besides those compounds mentioned above, metal silicides such as Mg2Si and NaSi have also been explored as precursors to produce Si through the chemical dealloying process. Unfortunately, these methods generally require expensive reagents and the resulting Nano Si is partially oxidized. When considering issues of price and environment, as well as scalability, direct reduction of inexpensive silicas and natural silicates will be more welcomed.37,90

To make clear the relationship between upstream and downstream products in the whole Si industry, a flow chart of existing Nano Si synthesis routes including different precursors is summarized in Fig. 1. Fabrication of Nano Si from Si wafers through electrochemical anodization suffers from the highest energy consumption (route 1, red line). Similarly, both reduction from silane (route 2, orange line) and SiCl4 (route 3, purple line) are energy inefficient methods for scalable manufacturing of Nano Si. Alternatively, alloying Si with Mg followed by chemical dealloying (route 4, grey line) and acid leaching require major efforts to deal with huge amounts of waste discharge.91 Although silica (route 5, blue line) and other natural precursors such as earth, rock, plants and so on (route 6, green line) have stable Si–O bonds, and elimination of oxygen element from these well-defined structures requires high energy, researchers still spare no effort to develop effective reduction methods due to their potential environmentally friendly nature, cost-effectivity and large-scale production possibility.

2.2 Reducing agents

The strength of reductants has a significant influence on the nucleation rate and subsequent particle size and dispersity. Common chemical reducing agents include hydrides, such as sodium borohydride (NaBH4) and lithium aluminum hydride, metals, such as K, Na, Mg, Al and Zn, organic salts, such as sodium napthalenide, and H2 gas, with strength decreasing in the order hydrides > organic salts > metals > H2 gas. Powerful reductants such as hydrides have been largely used to synthesize Si quantum dots with a narrow size distribution and a relatively small size of no more than 10 nm.8 Sodium naphthalide92 and potassium napthalide93 have been employed to reduce SiCl4 to Nano Si at ambient temperature through a one-electron transfer mechanism. All these methods are based on organic solution, and can't be extended to the reduction of silica due to its insolubility in organic solvents.

One class of promising reductants is metals and their reduction ability can be evaluated from the Ellingham diagram (Fig. 2).94 Based on the thermodynamic principle, the metals with lower Gibbs free energy of oxidation on the diagram will reduce the oxides with higher Gibbs free energy of formation.95 In the case of SiO2, the potential reducing metals include Ti, Al, Mg and Ca.96 Because of the narrow gap between the two lines, Ti has low reducing effectiveness. Although Ca's line locates at the lowest position, rarely has headway been made in calcium reduction of silica. This should be attributed primarily to the difficult operation conditions considering issues of high exothermicity and violent reaction with moisture. In addition, NaK alloy has also been used as a reducing agent for the reduction of SiCl4.97,98 However, the aggressive nature (for example, explosion in contact with air) makes it an undesirable reducing agent. As Mg and Al are milder and safer than Ca or NaK, they have been investigated extensively for silica reduction through thermochemical and electrochemical processes.

3. Structure–property relationship in Si-based anode materials

Because of the various morphologies of Si precursors, changeable structures can be derived by using different reductants based on different mechanisms. Understanding the structure–property relationship can be conducive to developing impactful reduction routes. Traditionally, the structure–property relationship usually refers to the correlation between the structure and the intrinsic properties of materials.78 For electrode materials, enhancing the electrochemical performance requires deep understanding of the influence of the structure on the specific performance in terms of coulombic efficiency, stability, rate capability and so on. In this section, we will clarify the structure–property relationship for Si-based anode materials from size, crystallinity, porosity and dispersity.

3.1 Size

It has been well substantiated that reducing the size of Si to the nanoscale can mitigate the pulverization issue of the bulk Si anode.92 As theoretical evidence, Verbrugge and co-workers carefully analyzed diffusion-induced stresses in terms of surface tension and surface modulus in spherical nanoparticles.99 They pointed out that the significantly reduced tensile state of stress in magnitude with decreasing particle radius may be responsible for the resilience to fracture of nanoparticles. However, one of the basic questions remains indistinct: whether there exists a critical size below which the surface strain induced by lithiation can be accommodated in a Si electrode. Liu and co-workers discovered a strong size-dependent fracture behavior of Si nanoparticles during lithiation through in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM).100 There exists a critical particle size of ∼150 nm, that is, below which cracks and fractures would be inhibited, and above which surface cracks would be initiated followed by fractures after several cycles (Fig. 3a). Thus, tremendous efforts have been devoted to exploring different nanostructured Si-based anode materials, and fruitful achievements have been made.

Despite those impressive advances in Nano Si anode materials, the inevitable drawbacks such as high cost, poor CE, and low tapped density have hampered its widespread application.104 Engineering micrometer-sized Si as an anode has been resurrected.20 Some significant breakthroughs such as growth of conformal graphene cages on micrometer-sized Si particles105 and 3D structures with characteristics of Nano Si106 have been achieved. Accordingly, it is crucial to engineer nanosized Si as an anode to avoid structural pulverization. From the perspective of commercial application, it is more meaningful to explore micrometer-sized structures, with nanocharacteristics.107

3.2 Crystallinity

In addition to the size effects of Si on the electrochemical behavior, there have also been reports about the effect of crystallinity of Si on the performance. In earlier research, it was found that amorphous silicon (a-Si) had a superior cycling performance compared to crystalline silicon (c-Si).108 A subsequent study revealed that c-Si alloys with lithium at slightly lower potential (∼120 mV) than a-Si does (∼220 mV), which led to the idea of using a core (c-Si)–shell (a-Si) composite as the anode material through dedicatedly tailoring the limiting charging potential.109 Furthermore, the lithiation/delithiation behavior of a-Si nanospheres was observed through performing in situ TEM experiments (Fig. 3b).101 The first lithiation for both a-Si and c-Si was a two-phase reaction, in which c-Si underwent anisotropic lithiation while the lithiation of a-Si was isotropic (Fig. 3c). Additionally, a-Si has more favorable kinetics behavior when alloying with Li than does c-Si, making it beneficial for improving lithium ion transport. Consistent with this, Kim and co-workers demonstrated a fast-charging anode material via implanting an a-Si nanolayer in edge-site-activated graphite, in which the fast lithium diffusion rate was attributed to the a-Si nanolayer.110 It should be noticed that the c-Si will transform into a-Si after the first lithiation process and undergo the same lithiation process in the subsequent cycling. Although a-Si has superior kinetic properties compared to c-Si, preparation of a-Si always involves aggressive conditions such as large flows, high pressure or high temperature based on a CVD reaction, giving rise to few relevant studies.

3.3 Porosity

The contribution of a porous structure to the enhancement of electrochemical performance is mainly manifested in two aspects.65,102,111 First, the pre-formed pores can accommodate the large volume changes during the cycling process, which would help to stabilize the whole structure, maintaining cycling stability.112 Second, the high specific surface area can improve the accessibility of the electrolyte to the Si surface, shortening lithium ion diffusion lengths and thus increasing available capacity at high rates.

Both simulation and in situ TEM experiments have been utilized to study the effects of porosity (for example, pore size and void volume fraction) on the structural stability. Ge and co-workers established a mathematical model to the study the change of pore size and strain induced stress on porous doped Si nanowires.111 In specific, if the ratio of initial pore radius and the pore-to-pore distance (r/l) increases, the maximum stress would decrease. Therefore, high porosity with large pore size is beneficial for obtaining high electrochemical capacity and long cycling retention. Similarly, Liu and co-workers synthesized a large mesoporous Si sponge (MSS) as a model structure (Fig. 3d–f).65 The continuum medium mechanical calculations revealed that the overall volume expansion decreases with increasing the void volume fraction in the MSS particles. Combined with in situ TEM results, it was deduced that the volume expansion of MSS particles could be limited to ∼30% at full lithiation. Moreover, Shen and co-workers demonstrated a larger critical fracture diameter size of up to 1520 nm for porous Si particles than the previously reported 150 nm for c-Si particles.102 This remarkable difference could be due to the distinct lithiation behaviors in which porous Si adopted an end-to-end arrangement (Fig. 3g) while a surface-to-center arrangement was adopted by c-Si (Fig. 3h).

It is believed that the interconnected nanopores enable inward volume expansion without obvious particle-level outer expansion, which makes porous structures appealing candidates for LIBs.112 However, there are still several drawbacks to their further application, including poor electronic conductivity, increasing side reactions, and a large solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) because of the increased contact interface between the electrolyte and electrode.113

3.4 Dispersity

Monodispersity is generally considered as a critical factor that influences the homogeneity of performance. However, synthesis of monodisperse Si particles is a huge challenge based on current techniques.92,114 Thus, there exist few reports correlating the dispersity with the electrochemical performance. And yet, some trends can be summarized by a careful survey of the literature.

Zuo and co-workers fabricated a three-dimensional (3D) macro-/mesoporous Si hierarchical structure from zero-dimensional (0D) Stöber silica particles with a high mass loading density.115 A self-templating mechanism was proposed to explain the formation of a 3D structure. This assembling trend is probably caused by the good monodispersity of Stöber silica particles. Differently, Xu and co-workers designed watermelon-like Si/C microspheres via ball milling combined with spray drying.103 The optimized size distribution of the as-prepared Si/C microspheres contributes to achieving efficient occupation of void space and strengthening the supporting effect by filling the interspace (Fig. 3i and j), which leads to a high pressing density and a high areal capacity. Accordingly, dispersity mainly affects the areal capacity of an electrode via stacking patterns or assembling.

4. Progress on reduction strategies

Section 3 clearly elucidates the structure–property relationship in Si-based anode materials. No one has yet synthesized ideal Si-based anode materials that can be successfully used in commercial LIBs.20 As a bottleneck problem, developing cost-effective and environmentally benign reduction techniques from cheap and abundant precursors as discussed in Section 2 is of great significance. In this section, we discuss the most promising and eco-friendly synthetic routes towards Si-based anode materials: magnesiothermic reduction, magnesio-milling reduction, molten salt reduction, and electrochemical reduction. We will present the progress, mechanism, and unique structures obtained from each reduction approach.

4.1 Magnesiothermic reduction

4.1.1 Progress on this method. The magnesiothermic reduction reaction (MRR) is a popular reduction method in the metallurgical field due to its relatively low reaction temperature (500–900 °C) and ease of handling.43 The reduction process to obtain Si is through displacing oxygen from silica (SiO2) using magnesium (Mg) vapor, during which new oxide-bearing composites are formed, and subsequent selective magnesia (MgO) dissolution. This process can be described as follows:
 
SiO2(s) + 2Mg(g) → 2MgO(s) + Si(s)(4.1)

The standard Gibbs free energy change for this reaction is −327.5 kJ mol−1 at 650 °C, which indicates that this process is exothermic and thermodynamically spontaneous.14 The theoretical equilibrium partial pressure of Mg gas is calculated to be 4.1 × 10−7 Torr for reaction (4.1) at 650 °C, the value of which is far smaller than the equilibrium vapor pressure (3.1 Torr) of Mg vapor at this temperature.14 That is to say, the pressure of Mg vapor generated upon Mg evaporation at 650 °C is sufficient to drive reaction (4.1) to proceed from a thermodynamic perspective. However, it should be noted that the actual reaction process is controlled by both thermodynamics and kinetics, which makes it more complicated.

Besides, as the ratio between Mg and SiO2 varies, different chemical reactions are involved, as described by the following equations (eqn (4.2)–(4.4)).

SiO2(s) + 4Mg(g) → 2MgO(s) + Mg2Si(s)(4.4)

These potential reactions indicate that an appropriate ratio between Mg and SiO2 is of critical importance to acquire the target products MgO and Si.116 When the reaction stoichiometry of Mg and SiO2 is in excess of 2:1, Mg2Si can be the dominant product, while when this ratio is less than 1:1, Mg2SiO4 or Mg2SiO4 may inevitably be produced.117 The high reaction stoichiometry dependence feature, combined with the complex control of Mg vapor kinetics, gives rise to poor yield and repeatability of the MRR.

As a pioneering study, Bao and co-workers explicitly demonstrated the shape preserving feature and microporosity of the liberated Si product inevitably created when MgO is removed.118 They emphasized that a modest temperature (650 °C) is necessary to suppress the formation of volatile gas species such as SiO (g) and Si (g). Moreover, the formation of a continuous refractory MgO phase intertwined with the Si product effectively inhibited the coarsening and sintering of the Si product, so that an interconnected network of Si nanocrystals can be obtained after etching of MgO. Taking advantage of the MRR, a large number of assemblies with well-controlled morphologies were synthesized.11 For example, ordered mesoporous Si thin films were successfully fabricated through magnesiothermic reduction of mesoporous SiO2 films.119 Similarly, monodisperse Si ordered arrangements (opals) by reduction of silica opals were also fabricated.114

With the research boom in Si-based anodes, the MRR has been regarded as a robust strategy to synthesize Si-based anode materials.120 Yu and co-workers appear to be the first group to synthesize 3D macroporous Si-based anode materials with uniformly distributed macropores using the MRR.121 The rich and various silica templates with tailored geometries, along with the shape preserving feature of the MRR, enable fabrication of a wide range of Si-based anode materials as discussed in Section 4.1.3. In addition to silica, earth-abundant substances (such as sand,122 rice husk,123 reed leaf124 and so on125) have also attracted increasing attention owing to their low cost and sustainability. Moreover, silicon monoxide can also be converted into porous Si via the MRR at a low temperature of 500 °C.126

On account of the exothermic reaction nature, the local reaction temperature in the MRR may rise to a very high level, which would cause the pores to collapse and agglomerate. An effective way to solve this problem is through employing a heat scavenger such as table salt (NaCl).125 Fusion of NaCl during the MRR would consume a great deal of heat released, which effectively prevents the pore structure from collapsing and aggregating.127 Similarly and very recently, Pei and co-workers demonstrated that potassium iodide (KI) can also be used as a heat scavenger to suppress the formation of SiC, which provides an effective approach to extend the MRR to the composite of SiO2 and C.128

Further research on the MRR process demonstrated that an Mg2Si intermediate phase may be formed at the initial stage, which plays a critical role in tailoring the final morphology.129,130 Kim and co-workers synthesized Si nanosheets from natural sand, the formation mechanism of which was attributed to reactions between this intermediate Mg2Si phase and unreacted SiO2 which can be expressed as follows:129

 
SiO2(s) + Mg2Si(s) → 2MgO(s) + 2Si(s)(4.5)

Very recently, a hierarchical mesoporous/microporous Si-based anode was designed and constructed through adjusting the phase ratio between Mg2Si and MgO intermediate products.130 Besides, the air-oxidation demagnesiation of Mg2Si to obtain nanoporous Si has also been reported.131 Interestingly, the intermediate Mg2Si and MgO could also serve as both the template and catalyst for the conformal coating of graphene cages, giving rise to the formation of unique hollow graphene-encapsulated Si spheres.132

Despite intense research studies with respect to the MRR, the low conversion yield of this method, which may be governed by the reaction conditions, remains a critical issue.133 In Section 4.1.2, we will discuss the influence of different reaction conditions on the resulting products in terms of mixing and molar ratios, reaction temperature and time, ramp rate, and reaction pressure. As most research studies focus mainly on one specific precursor under the set reaction conditions, and the as-synthesized Si anode materials obtained from the same precursor can differ, these reduction conditions are not comparable between studies. Even then, some appealing conclusions can be drawn by a careful investigation of the literature.

4.1.2 Exploration of reaction conditions. Evenly mixing Mg and SiO2 precursors in an appropriate molar ratio is a prerequisite to obtaining a relatively high yield. In earlier research, the Mg and silica precursors were placed at separate locations in a steel boat, and Mg vapor diffuses to react with SiO2.118 The production based on this configuration usually contains different regions, and the extent of reaction differs with the distance from the Mg source, which limits the yield scalability. Accordingly, an even premixing method was adopted by subsequent researchers. There are several reactions involved in the reaction between Mg and SiO2 as discussed in Section 4.1.1. Insufficient or excessive Mg would lead to the formation of undesired by-products such as MgSiO4 or Mg2Si. It has been demonstrated that MgSiO4 was detected when decreasing the molar ratio of Mg and SiO2.55 Thus, a slight excess of Mg (the molar ratio of Mg/SiO2 is between 2 and 2.5) were preferred.134

Generally, the reaction temperature and time can modulate the rate and extent of chemical reactions. Bao and co-workers examined the influence of reaction temperature (650 °C/2.5 h and 900 °C/1.5 h) on the structure of the resulting Si replicas.16 They found that both the average size and specific surface area of the Si crystals significantly decreased after the 900 °C/1.5 h treatment compared to those after the 650 °C/2.5 h treatment, indicating enhanced reaction kinetics at higher temperature. Wong and co-workers demonstrated that the surface of soda lime glass can be converted into Si thin film through the MRR at an initial low temperature of 520 °C.136 Furthermore, the thin film thickness can be tuned by varying the reaction time from 1 min to 600 min, which confirms such extent of reaction control by reaction time.

The ramp rate can significantly influence the morphology of the Si product.137 Liu and co-workers found that the specific surface area of the Nano-Si obtained at 5 °C min−1 decreased dramatically compared with that of the 40 °C min−1 sample.138 The different characteristics were attributed to local heat accumulation on account of the large amount of heat released by the MRR. Therefore, a slow heating rate (less than 5 °C min−1) should be employed considering the heat transfer issues.

From the above analysis, it can be found that effective control of the Mg vapor diffusion kinetics remains a huge challenge. To address this issue, Yoo and co-workers reported an extremely high yield (>98%) MRR method through reducing the reaction pressure to mild vacuum (∼0.1 Torr).135 The homogeneity of the MRR can be remarkably improved owing to the homogeneous and fast transport of Mg under vacuum conditions (Fig. 4a). Ball-milled sands were selected as the starting materials, and Mg2Si is formed on the surface at the initial reaction stage. Then, the solid-state diffusion of Mg from Mg2Si will occur, resulting in reaction with the remaining SiO2, which will lead to the production of Nano-Si mixed with MgO, and the whole MRR process takes only ≈30 min. Fig. 4b shows the schematic structure of the partially reduced samples, which is consistent with the TEM analysis (Fig. 4c–e). Both the improvement of the yield and the acceleration of the kinetics were ascribed to the more efficient transport of Mg into the individual sand particles. The authors pointed out that the significantly amplified mean-free-path under mild vacuum ensures more rapid and uniform diffusion of Mg vapor (Fig. 4f–g).

4.1.3 Morphological variety. Owing to the various and abundant silica templates and derivations from natural resources, along with the morphology preserving nature of the MRR, diverse Si structures ranging from 0D, 1D, and 2D Nano-Si to 3D porous Si have been fabricated. For the 0D nanostructure, hollow porous Si (HP Si) has been intensively reported due to its negligible outward expansion and short diffusion path, which contributes to enhanced capacity retention and rate performance.139,141–145 The fabrication process of HP Si usually involves the preparation of HP SiO2 and the subsequent MRR.141 Differently, Xiao and co-workers reported a size-dependent chemical transformation strategy to fabricate HP Si nanoparticles, in which the MRR only occurred on the porous SiO2 shell, while the solid SiO2 core remained nearly intact (Fig. 5a–c).139 The final HP Si nanoparticles could be obtained after acid etching. Surprisingly, both mechanical analysis and in situ TEM results revealed that the HP Si nanoparticles featured inward lithium breathing, which is distinct from the outward volume change behavior of solid Si. However, the poor conductivity of the HP Si nanoparticles hindered their practical application. To tackle this issue, surface coating or binding strategies with conductive materials such as Ag,141 carbon,143 polypyrrole142 and so on146 were introduced. Very recently, more complicated Si nanoparticles (cp-Si) with canyon-like surfaces derived from wrinkled SiO2 nanoparticles (WSN) via the MRR were synthesized (Fig. 5d–j).140 Although this unique canyon-like structure with smaller Si dimensions exhibited enhanced electrochemical performance, a gradual capacity decrease remained obvious, indicating an unavoidable long-cycling electron transport issue.

Benefiting from the flexible nature and robust electrical contact between the collector and active materials, 1D Si structures were considered as one of the most promising candidates.86 Generally, two categories of 1D Si structures could be constructed via the MRR. One is direct conversion of 1D SiO2 templates to 1D porous Si,147 and the other is self-templating synthesis from natural sources.148 In the first case, a lotus-root-like mesoporous Si was fabricated from a rod-shaped mesoporous SiO2 template through the MRR (Fig. 6a–c).147 In addition, carbon nanotubes149 or electrospun organic nanowires150 were usually selected as templates for co-axial growth of SiO2, and 1D porous Si structures could be constructed by the subsequent MRR. Take sepiolite as an example: there is one octahedral (O) sheet and two tetrahedral (T) sheets in its crystal structure (Fig. 6d).148 Due to the discontinuous O sheet, a fibrous pattern with channels running parallel to it is naturally formed. This intrinsic crystal structure allows for the preservation of 1D structures during the MRR without any additional templates (Fig. 6e and f).

Similarly, 2D Si nanostructures can be fabricated either using graphene oxide (GO) as a sacrificial template or direct conversion of natural sources through the MRR. In specific, free-standing ultrathin Si nanosheets were synthesized from the SiO2 coated GO through the MRR.151 To improve the conductivity of the anode materials, 2D graphene/porous Si composites have attracted tremendous attention, giving rise to research studies on more complicated 2D structures based on the MRR.152–154 Interestingly, Kim and co-workers found that Si nanosheets can be synthesized from sand through the MRR, which is different from other research.129 They pointed out that an Mg2Si intermediate phase played a significant role in tailoring the ultimate morphology (Fig. 6g–i).

Despite the tremendous advantages of nanostructures, the inevitably low tapped/pressing density has hindered its commercial application. Hence, engineering micrometer-sized 3D hierarchical structures with nanosized features has been resurrected. Besides, 3D Si hierarchical anodes are of great interest because they can not only reap the benefits of nanostructures ranging from 0D to 2D, but can also extend these advantages to a 3D scale.157 Among the various strategies, the MRR has been regarded as one of the most facile and effective protocols to fabricate 3D hierarchical structures on account of the shape maintainability feature and intertwined nanopore structures possessed by the final products.158,159 Wu and co-workers designed a 3D macroporous Si by simple control of MRR reaction conditions (Fig. 7a–c).155 Furthermore, the 3D porous Si/GO architectures were also constructed based on the MRR.160 Significantly, the fabrication of 3D porous Si structures using natural sources as the skeleton templates becomes the unique superiority of the MRR. Liu and co-workers demonstrated that reed leaves can be transformed into 3D porous Si microstructures where the well-defined sheet-like hierarchical structures of reeds can be well inherited (Fig. 7d–g).124 The 3D hierarchical architecture with 2D highly porous nanonet/nanosheet units can accommodate the huge volume change and facilitate the diffusion of lithium ions into the electrode. Similarly, Praneetha and co-workers successfully extracted 3D porous Si architectures from earth-abundant agricultural residues such as bamboo culm, rice husk, and sugarcane bagasse based on the MRR, which further demonstrated its feasibility for synthesizing nanoporous 3D Si (Fig. 7h–j).156 Accordingly, this sustainable and low-cost approach based on the MRR has manifested its powerful validity in producing a highly porous 3D Si architecture from earth-abundant natural and biological resources.

4.2 Magnesio-milling reduction

The MRR usually experiences a vapor–solid process in which Mg atoms diffuse into the internal structure of SiO2 with a suitable Mg vapor pressure in the temperature range from 500 to 900 °C. Nonetheless, the yield of Si often varies because of the difficulty in precisely controlling the pressure. Besides, the heat-treatment process is time-consuming. For these reasons, the magnesio-milling reduction (MMR) strategy was proposed, and it can be expanded to a 5 L-scale.161 During the milling process, the SiO2 particles and Mg powder were repeatedly mixed, fractured, and rewelded, giving rise to a reduction in grain size. As the milling proceeds, increasing “active” interfaces and lattice defects triggered by continuously transferred kinetic energy from the milling balls will promote the increase of temperature inside the milling vial. When a certain critical point is reached, which can overcome the reaction activation barrier, spontaneous reduction will be initiated (Fig. 8a). Thereafter, the reaction will self-propagate without the aid of any external energy owing to its highly exothermic nature. The ignition time can be determined through testing the pressure in the milling vial. The Si yield based on the MMR method was determined to be as high as 90%. Furthermore, a variety of volume-dependent processes were investigated (Fig. 8b). Both the lab-scale planetary-ball mills and 5 L-scale attrition-mills can be successfully utilized as reactors of MMR. Recently, the validity of MMR has been further verified by reducing both the size of the SiO2 and Mg powder to the nanoscale.162 Liu and co-workers found that the reaction time can be reduced to 10 min with a high Si yield of 89% in MMR when using nanosized starting materials (Fig. 8c). The MMR process not only takes advantage of the traditional MRR but also shows high Si yield and outstanding scalability, making it an intriguing approach.

4.3 Molten salt reduction

4.3.1 An assortment of molten salts. Molten salt refers to salt which is in its solid phase at standard temperature (25 °C) and pressure (1 atm) but enters the liquid phase due to elevated temperature. Chloride salts such as alkali or alkali earth metal chlorides are the most commonly used molten salts due to the low temperature region of 200–900 °C, inert chemical character, and low cost.163 According to the Ellingham diagram (Fig. 2), SiO2 can be reduced to Si by most of the alkali (Na, K) or alkali earth (Ca, Mg) metals.56 These reducing agents will be solvated through an ionization process which produces ions and solvated electrons,58 contributing to the reduction of SiO2 by SiO2 + 4e− → Si + 2O2−. Single or binary molten salt systems have been investigated for the reduction of SiO2 to Si. In earlier research studies, LiCl–KCl and MgCl2–NaCl eutectic compositions have been chosen as the “solvent” and the Si yield increased as the reaction temperature was raised from 550 °C to 700 °C.58 Thereafter, Liang and co-workers found that mixed NaCl–KCl could also be used as a molten salt to obtain Si from SiO2 at 700 °C.164 To further reduce the reaction temperature, AlCl3 with low melting temperature (∼190 °C) was used as the molten salt to reduce both SiCl4 and SiO2 at temperatures as low as 200–300 °C.60,165 However, the likely sublimation properties of AlCl3 induce an exponential increase of the vapor pressure in the reactor, causing difficulties in practical operation. And thus, NaCl or ZnCl2 was added to form a mixture with lower eutectic melting temperature for easy handling.166,167
4.3.2 Investigation of the mechanism. Different from the vapor–solid chemical reaction of MMR, molten salt assisted reduction (MSR) is a liquid–solid process, where the reducing metal will be solvated. Liu and co-workers proposed an ionization mechanism in which metal ions and solvated electrons can be produced by M → M2+ + 2e−. These solvated electrons possess strong reducing power and can adsorb on the surface of SiO2 and break the Si–O bond, realizing complete reduction.58 Further study revealed that the molten salt does participate in the reduction process by forming AlOCl (Fig. 9a and b).60 Based on the XRD result of the crude products, the chemical reaction involved in the molten salt system can be described as follows:

Direct observation of the reaction process confirmed that the real reaction proceeds in a solution environment and the unreacted AlCl3 can be recycled (Fig. 9c). Recently, Song and co-workers elucidated the whole reaction pathway and revealed a salt-expedited reduction mechanism based on both theoretical simulation and experimental observation.168 At the initial stage of reaction, the Al-AlCl3 complex was the preferred species in situ formed at the surface of Al metal through the solvation effect. Subsequently, the as-formed complex adsorbed on the surface of SiO2, where activated AlCl* was formed through two pathways based on thermodynamic calculations (Fig. 9d). Path a can be regarded as a ligand dissociation mechanism in which ligand dissociation followed by two rounds of the Cl transfer process is involved. In path b, the Cl-sharing ligand was first formed and dissociated to AlCl2* which then underwent one Cl transfer process. Detailed calculation indicated that path b should be both the thermodynamically and kinetically favorable route (Fig. 9e). Finally, the activated AlCl* in situ formed on the surface of SiO2 can detach the O atoms, resulting in formation of the by-product AlOCl and Si, which is consistent with previous reports.60 Based on this mechanism, the formation of HP Si, which is the most frequently obtained structure, can be reasonably explained.169 As the reduction reaction proceeds, successively generated AlCl* species will dissociate more O atoms and the Si cluster will grow to a Si seed, which has relatively free motion in the salt solution. These concurrently generated Si seeds can recrystallize into a spherical shell, and inner seeds will dissolve away to minimize the surface energy, leading to the formation of HP Si. In this study, the step-by-step reduction mechanism of MSR was thoroughly investigated, which may provide vigorous theoretical evidence for molten salt assisted synthesis.170

Very recently, Ryu and co-workers found that an infinitesimal amount of sulfur can be doped into the Si crystal lattice during MSR through introducing MgSO4 into the reaction system.171 Because of the strong reducing power of the activated AlCl* in situ formed in the AlCl3 molten salt, the oxygen in MgSO4 can be dissociated, yielding metallic Mg and isolated sulfur clusters. Then spontaneous cogrowth of the sulfur seed and reduced Si contributes to the uniform doping of a small quantity of sulfur into the Si crystal in the form of an interior channel. Importantly, this kind of doping dramatically increases the electronic conductivity of the resulting product, resulting in the formation of quasi-metallic Si. This work revealed that the AlCl3 molten salt possesses unique and unexpected reduction ability, which deserves an in-depth study.

4.3.3 Diverse anode structures. Due to the different reduction mechanism, the morphology of precursors can't be preserved in MSR, causing reconstruction of the final morphology through the synergistic effect of minimization of surface energy and Kirkendall effect or Ostwald ripening.164,169 However, the above processes often fail to form a well dispersed HP Si owing to the absence of a robust support or stable interface.166 To tackle this issue, several strategies such as construction of an artificial interface167 or matrix encapsulation172 have been proposed.

In the first tactic, the SiO2 spheres were conformally coated with a thin porous carbon layer, which works as a stable interface in the MSR (Fig. 10a–d).167 The authors demonstrated that the coated carbon layer could influence the mass transfer kinetics through adjusting the interdiffusion of the reagent, facilitating the formation of the HP structure. Besides carbon materials, Ti3C2 MXene nanosheets can also be used as an effective interface and template. Very recently, Hui and co-workers fabricated a novel porous MXene Ti3C2/Si nanocomposite via a controllable hydrolysis of TEOS on the surface of treated Ti3C2 MXene nanosheets and subsequent low temperature MSR.175 Owing to the existence of a 2D interface, SiO2 can be reduced to ultra-small Si nanoparticles decorated homogeneously on the surface of Ti3C2 MXene nanosheets. Importantly, the relatively mild reaction conditions of MSR are beneficial for the preservation of the layered structure of MXene. As another strategy, Vrankovic and co-workers designed a novel structure in which carbon coated porous Si is embedded in a ceramic matrix (Fig. 10e).172 The irregular porous Si obtained by MSR could anchor strongly on the surface of a polymer-derived silicon oxycarbide matrix, forming a stable composite. Differently, Kai and co-workers demonstrated that a polymeric precursor derived from cross-linking divinyl benzene and polymethylhydrosiloxane can be reduced to a Si/SiOx@C composite through MSR and subsequent pyrolysis, indicating a wider range of selection of precursors.176

Despite the collapse of the original precursor structure, MSR still manifested its application, inheriting the primary skeleton template of some natural sources. Wan and co-workers fabricated 3D interconnected Si frameworks derived from natural halloysite clay by MSR (Fig. 10f).173 The tubular morphology of the natural halloysite clay could be well maintained after acid treatment, while the final product partially inherited the tubular characters due to the inevitable recrystallization process (Fig. 10g–i). Furthermore, a Si flake/nitrogen-doped graphene-like carbon composite was synthesized from organoclay through an in situ strategy, which involved carbonization followed by MSR (Fig. 10j).174 Specifically, the organoclay precursors can be fabricated by simply intercalating montmorillonite with crystal violet, simultaneously acting as the sources of carbon and nitrogen. Subsequent calcination induced the formation of a thin carbon layer, which served not only as a barrier to prevent Si seeds from agglomerating, but also as a template to induce the formation of a fake morphology (Fig. 10k and m). However, irregular agglomerated Si particles were formed without this pre-formed carbon layer, further indicating the importance of introducing a structural skeleton during MSR. Similarly, Huang and co-workers fabricated porous Si/C composite nanosheets from vermiculite, a kind of layered silicate, based on precoating carbon layers on expanded vermiculite. The authors also pointed out that the precoated carbon layer is necessary for the maintenance of the nanosheet morphology.

4.4 Electrochemical reduction

Electrochemical reduction (ECR) of SiO2 is a relatively new research field, mainly due to the insulating character of SiO2 at low temperature.177 Electrochemical deoxygenation of SiO2 is assumed not to occur as there is no pathway available for electron transfer.178 Inspired by the ECR of TiO2 in molten chlorides, Toshiyuki and co-workers first reported pinpoint and bulk ECR of quartz in molten CaCl2.67 This methodology involved a ‘contact electrode’ which can be realized by winding molybdenum (Mo) wire several times round a quartz plate. When the electrode potential was controlled to be lower than the SiO2 reduction potential, the O atom in the SiO2 near the three-phase interface would move to the conductor wire, where the O atom acquired two electrons to form O2−, diffusing into molten CaCl2 (Fig. 11a). The morphology and crystallization conversion during the electrolytic reduction were further investigated.179 The as-formed Si has hexagonal prism structures with many stacking faults along the 〈111〉 direction (Fig. 11b). It was found that these columns were perpendicular to the reaction interface, experiencing thermal transformation from amorphous Si to crystalline Si (Fig. 11c). Furthermore, the electrolytic reduction rate for dense SiO2 was found to be faster than that for a porous SiO2 pellet, which could be improved through Si doping.180 In addition, several fundamental studies, such as those on reduction rate,181 purity of the Si product,182 and the effect of different starting materials on the resulting morphology of Si,183 were performed in detail.

Recently, freestanding Si nanowire arrays were synthesized through ECR of SiO2 in molten CaCl2 at 850 °C.184 Typically, a SiO2 glass plate sandwiched by two pieces of nickel net was chosen as the working electrode, which was reduced to double-layered Si nanowire arrays as long as the reaction completed (Fig. 11d–f). However, the operating temperature in molten CaCl2 is still too high, and the Si yield is low.185 To address these two issues, Dong and co-workers developed a more sustainable approach to produce Si nanowires in bulk quantities via ECR of CaSiO3.186 Unlike previous reports, CaSiO3 would dissolve in the melt to generate Ca2+ and SiO32−, and then the SiO32− was reduced to Si at a certain potential. The whole process was controlled by the diffusion rate of O2−, which could be accelerated by adding CaO which acted as a supporting electrolyte (Fig. 11g and h). The authors further found that the electrolysis temperature could be reduced to as low as 650 °C when using a ternary molten salt system, CaCl2–MgCl2–NaCl. The resulting Si product has a 1D nanowire morphology and its highest yield reached 80 mg at an optimized combination (Fig. 11i). This research provided new insights into ECR, which opened up a new horizon for sustainable production of Si. However, the commonly used carbon anode in the above system is a consumable anode, the size of which will decrease as electrochemical reaction proceeds in the form of CO2. Differently and very recently, Ge and co-workers reported an alternative anode material (Ti4O7) which can be used to produce Si without CO2 emissions.187 What's more, different Si morphologies such as wires, films and particles can be prepared with this recyclable anode under different cathodic current densities. The development of this dimensionally stable anode can not only avoid the production of CO2 but also simplify the cell operation, which is less energy intensive.

Benefiting from the facile implantability of ECR, several Si-based composites have been fabricated. Very recently, a Si@C@Si hybrid was prepared via pyrolysis-cum-electrolysis of SiO2@polydopamine in a binary molten salt system, NaCl–CaCl2, at 800 °C.188 The authors pointed out that it is the synergetic effect between carbonization of polydopamine and electrodeoxidation of SiO2 in molten salts that results in the formation of the unique Si@C@Si structure. Carbon cloth can also be utilized as a freestanding carrier to achieve deposition of Si nanowires based on ECR of soluble SiO2 in the form of SiO32−.189 Intriguingly, it is found that the electrodeposited Si nanowires have much smaller diameters and thinner surface oxide coating layers, which can be attributed to the explosive nucleation process and sluggish oxidation with the role of carbon cloth. It can be seen that the ECR method is effective and facile for constructing various kinds of SI–C composites.

5. Electrochemical performance

The assessment of the electrochemical performance of the current Si anode material toward practical applications involves various performance metrics, such as specific capacity, rate performance, cyclability, mass loading, coulombic efficiency (CE), volumetric performance, and so on. Nonetheless, research studies in the current scientific literature have commonly focused on some of the parameters on account of optimization of one parameter at the expense of other parameters.190 In addition, due to the lack of uniform battery testing standards, there is no comparability between performance metrics reported in different reports. It seems that a large number of anode materials were reported to possess exceptional performance in academic research, which was often obtained through intentionally reducing the mass loading, or adding more conductive agent or binder. These overstated data further weakened the credibility of comparing the performance of anode materials with distinct structures. Typically, the ICE of different dimensional anode materials locates in the wide range of 50–89%. The low ICE is attributed to the irreversible capacity loss of forming a SEI and the consumption of Li by the amorphous carbon during the first lithiation.105 As shown from Table 1, the 0D hollow porous Si without compositing has a relatively low ICE of 52%, on account of the large surface area available to form a SEI.139 Interestingly, nano-porous Si anodes obtained from MMR have an ultra-high initial CE of over 80%, probably due to the aggregation of Si nanoparticles induced by the milling process, dramatically reducing the contact interface between electrolyte and Si anodes.72,162 When hybridizing Si with carbon or other substances, the ICE can be improved. For example, the ICE of silver coated HP Si is as high as 84%.141 Besides, the 3D hierarchical architectures derived from natural sources reveal distinct ICEs, which may be ascribed to the difference in grain size, crystallinity, and purity of the products.

The specific capacity retention for anode materials is normally related to the structural integrity and interface stability during deep cycling, which can be realized via compositing or building 3D assemblies. As shown in Table 1, the 0D hollow Si anode derived from SiO2@C through MSR demonstrated ultrahigh specific capacity which could reach about 800 mA h g−1 at a current density of 8 A g−1 after 600 cycles, corresponding to 80% capacity retention.167 This stable capacity revealed that through the introduction of a carbon layer, it was possible to preserve the structural integrity of the hollow Si during the lithiation/delithiation process. Very recently, an MXene/Si@C layer-by-layer structure was rationally fabricated through the MRR of MXene/SiO2.191 Such an intriguing superstructure delivered a stable specific capacity of 1547 mA g g−1 at a current density of 2.1 A g−1 after 200 cycles, suggesting its extraordinary stability. As a representative study on constructing 3D building blocks, a Si flower-like ensemble, which is composed of interconnected Si nanoplates with different spatial orientations, was derived from SiO2 fumes based on the MRR.192 The as-prepared 3D structure exhibits extraordinary gravimetric capacity and excellent cycling stability (1100 mA h g−1 at 2 A g−1 after 600 cycles). This building block revealed dual stabilized characters, in which the interconnected Si nanoplates in the respective spatial orientations can accommodate the volume change of Si, shorten the Li ion diffusion length, and further improve the interfacial stability due to the limited variable SEI area. Remarkably, the 3D porous hierarchical architecture derived from reed leaves via the MRR delivered ultra-stable cycling behavior (∼420 mA h g−1 at a current density of as high as 10C after 4000 cycles).124 These results indicate that 3D porous hierarchical architectures derived from natural sources can inhibit the original unique 3D hierarchical structures, and possess outstanding structural integrity and interface stability contributing to long cycle life.

A fast charging rate, which indicates the reaction kinetics of an electrode, is also a key element for practical application of LIBs.110 As we have discussed in Section 3.3, a porous structure can contribute to the enhancement of the Li ion and electron transfer rate, yielding excellent rate capacity, which is confirmed by the performance listed in Table 1. In specific, a 3D mesoporous Si/C nanocomposite derived from a mesoporous SiO2/C composite via the MRR shows a specific capacity of 1200 mA h g−1 at a current density of 10 A g−1.134 The lotus-root-like mesoporous Si can maintain a specific capacity of 568 mA h g−1 at a current density of 15C (1C = 4.2 A g−1).147 The porous Si@C microspheres synthesized from porous SiO2@C microspheres via MSR also demonstrated excellent rate performance (1290 mA h g−1 at 8 A g−1).166 Besides the porous structure, hollow spheres or hollow porous structures also can promote fast charging. Specifically, the 0D silver treated HP Si via MRR delivered a specific capacity of over 1290 mA h g−1 at a current density of 8 A g−1.141 The 0D hollow Si spheres obtained from MSR with and without carbon compositing showed a specific capacity of 974 mA h g−1 at a current density of 8 A g−1 and 936 mA h g−1 at a current density of 5 A g−1, respectively.167,169 The enhancement of reactivity with Li ions and electrons in porous or hollow structures originates from the largely exposed surface area available to the electrolyte which accelerates ion diffusion.

As we have discussed above, the reduction of SiO2 to Si-based anode materials has been attempted via several promising techniques. Each of them has their own merits and demerits toward ideal anode materials, and thus we summarized the characteristics of these methods as shown in Table 2. The MRR is one of the predominant reduction methods owing to its unique shape-preservation and generalization character. The as-prepared Si-based anode materials normally demonstrated excellent rate capacity and long cycle life, which may be applied to 3C (computer, communication, and consumer electronics) batteries, power batteries (e.g. electric vehicles) and energy storage batteries (e.g. grid storage). However, the product yield based on this method is always very low, and some byproducts like Mg2SiO4 and Mg2Si, which are caused by the discrepant atomic mobilities in the middle solid product are hard to control. What's more, the detailed kinetics during this process is not well understood which further hinders its scale-up. To overcome these issues, MMR was reported, which allowed homogeneous local reaction environments between Mg vapor atoms and SiO2. Nonetheless, the resulting Nano Si was liable to aggregate, giving rise to a gradual decay of the specific capacity. Besides, it is difficult to accurately control the ignition point when utilizing different types of SiO2 precursors. Furthermore, owing to the exothermic nature of this reduction reaction, the obtained product may experience fusion without a controllable microstructure. Significantly, low temperature MSR was a promising thermal reduction method owing to its facile operation and high yield. The limitations behind this method lie in the reconstruction of the morphology during reaction, suffering from the uncontrollable structure of products. Previously introducing structural skeleton materials such as a carbon coating layer not only could offer an interface for tailoring the interdiffusion of reagents, but also realized in situ composition with Si, giving rise to enhanced cycling stability and rate capacity. However, this pre-introduced structural framework may slow down the reduction reaction or even prevent it from happening. Therefore, longer reaction time may be required. Moreover, a large amount of molten salts may be used in this reaction, which gives rise to difficulties in post-processing. As a relatively novel approach, molten salt electrolysis reduction is a new sustainable method for producing Si from an inexpensive CaSiO3 precursor, which could be extended to the reduction of recycled glass waste or coal ash. However, the product morphology is difficult to control through changing the input parameters such as current density. Besides, the output based on this method is relatively low.

Although these reduction techniques are premature for commercialization, it is still useful to estimate the energy consumption of each method. It is well known that the traditional carbothermic reduction process suffers from high energy input (∼20 kW h kg−1-Si).186 This consumption of electrical energy will decrease to ∼13 kW h kg−1-Si when utilizing the ECR technique, in which the energy can be completely supplied with renewable energy.194 For the other reduction approaches, the energy consumption is expected be lower due to the lower reaction temperature. 6. Conclusions and outlook

In this review, a comprehensive discussion of synthetic techniques for Nano Si and Si has been presented. From the viewpoint of commercial application, it is reasonable to engineer the impactful reduction routes from low-cost precursors such as natural resources or wastes. The MRR is currently the most frequently used reduction method and has been extended to construct multiple dimensional structured Si-based anodes. Although a porous network is inevitably formed after acid treatment of by-product MgO, the evolution process and the detailed characterization of it has failed in current research work. Thus, an in situ method should be established to evaluate the detailed relationship between the pore structure and cycle life, which will give researchers more information necessary to design suitable porous structures. In addition, slow headway has been made with regard to the kinetic process mechanism during the MRR, which may play a critical role in regulating the final composition of products. Besides, the reactors used in the MRR are always homemade, leading to a distinct morphology even under the same reaction conditions. This may be ascribed to the different Mg vapor pressures caused by different reactor volumes. Thus, standard reaction equipment like hydrothermal synthesis reactors should also be developed.

MMR is an extension of the MRR method to some extent, as the reduction reactions involved in these two methods are the same. Differently, MMR is realized in a planetary ball mill, which can easily be extended to a larger attrition mill, and even a drum mill. However, the ignition point, a critical parameter indicating the initiation of reaction, may give rise to local high temperature, which poses a potential safety hazard. More efforts should be devoted to clarifying the influence of ignition point on the real reaction temperature. In addition, aggregation of the final products always inevitably happens during the milling process, giving rise to poor monodispersity which may be related to inferior cycling stability.

Low temperature MSR is a promising reduction method due to the facile operation and relatively high yield. The drawbacks of this strategy lie in the recrystallization of the final morphology during reaction. Accordingly, how the pre-introduced template or support skeleton influences the interdiffusion process should be further studied. Besides, an in situ technique should be applied to probe the morphology evolution process based on this method. Moreover, much deeper research should be done on the choice of different types of molten salts, which influence the optimized reaction temperature and product yield.

As a new reduction technique, the electrolysis process for reducing low-cost silicates is a sustainable approach. And more efforts should be made to test the electrochemical performance of Si-based anode materials based on this method. Besides, how to regulate the morphology of the products through changing the input parameters should also be explored. Furthermore, boosting the production efficiency based on electrolytic Si extraction is also of significant importance in promoting its commercialization.

In summary, we have surveyed the current eco-friendly reduction strategies for producing Si to address the fundamental issues involved in commercialization of Si-based anode materials. Approaches such as metallothermic reduction reactions (MRR and MSR), metal milling reduction (MMR), and electrochemical reduction (ECR) have been demonstrated to be powerful in reducing the Si precursors that have stable Si–O bonds. However, more intensive research should be devoted to developing new environmentally friendly strategies for the fabrication of Si from low-cost precursors, which has potential large-scale applications. Meanwhile, more efforts are still needed to explore the underlying mechanism of each reduction route, which is of critical importance to synthesize morphology controllable Si-based anodes. Besides, incorporating these reduction methods with the compositing strategy can significantly enhance the electrochemical performance of the electrodes. Consequently, how to effectively combine these approaches to construct an ideal hybrid anode that can achieve satisfactory performance remains a huge challenge.
