Abstract

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have been attracting great attention for application in electrolytes. Benefiting from the controllable chemical composition, tunable pore structure and surface functionality, MOFs offer great opportunities for synthesizing high-designability and high-performance electrolytes. This review emphasizes the state-of-the-art progress of MOF-derived electrolytes, including MOF-based solid-state electrolytes (SSEs, i.e., ionic liquid-based SSEs, polyoxyethylene (PEO)-based SSEs, single-ion conducting SSEs and others) and MOF-modified liquid electrolytes (i.e., self-assembling free-standing MOF separators, MOF-based composite separators and MOF membranes as coating interlayers on electrodes). A comprehensive discussion of fundamental chemistry including the structure characteristics, fabrication challenges and ion transfer mechanism is provided first. Next, the advances and challenges in various categories of MOF-derived electrolytes are summarized in detail. Finally, the opportunities and directions for future research of MOF-derived electrolytes are also proposed. This review will deepen the understanding about MOF-derived electrolytes and promote the development of high-performance batteries.

1. Introduction

Emerging demands for energy have driven the development of renewable energies (e.g., solar and wind energies) and the development of power-dense energy storage systems (ESSs).1 Particularly for ESSs, rechargeable batteries for electric vehicles and grid-scale energy storage have been regarded as the most promising candidates.2,3 Notably, electrolytes play an important and irreplaceable role in the successful operation of those rechargeable batteries.4â€“6 Nevertheless, most of the employed electrolytes, including traditional organic electrolytes,7 solid-state electrolytes (SSEs) (i.e., inorganic electrolytes and polymer electrolytes)8â€“10 and aqueous electrolytes,11â€“13 still face great challenges. Among them, the potential safety issues and poor electrochemical properties are urgent problems to be solved. For example, the catastrophic safety risks (including easy flammability, leakage and inevitable formation of dendrites) and limited energy density are the top two obstacles with regard to organic electrolyte-based metal-ion batteries.14,15 For solid-state batteries (SSBs), the low ionic conductivity and poor interfacial compatibilities between SSEs and electrodes always lead to unsatisfactory electrochemical performance, particularly at limited critical current density (the current density at which initial lithium dendrites form in a symmetrical cell).16,17 More seriously, as for aqueous electrolyte-based batteries, non-stable passive films between electrolytes and electrodes, attributed to undesirable side reactions,18 together with the narrow voltage window usually cause the limited rate performance and energy density,19â€“22 thus impeding their availability for practical application. To solve the above-mentioned issues, the optimization of the components of electrolytes can be effective, which provides great promise for developing high-performance rechargeable batteries.

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), also known as a new class of porous crystalline materials, have been widely applied in multiple fields, including gas adsorption,23 chemical catalysis,24 and molecular/ionic sieving/transport.25 In theory, due to the intrinsically ordered porous cavities, a crystalline MOF lattice is beneficial for hosting multiple guest liquid/gaseous species and thus achieving desirable molecular/ionic sieving/transport properties.26 More recently, MOF-based electrolytes have been developed as a novel emerging hotspot on the basis of their insulating character and intrinsic features. The superior characteristics of MOF materials, including controllable chemical compositions, tunable porous structures, and unique surface functionalities, endow them with great potential for producing high-designability and high-performance electrolytes.27 Thus, MOF-based electrolytes possess obvious merits compared to other electrolytes, including (a) uniform deposition without metal dendrites via homogenizing Li ion flux; (b) enhanced electrochemical stability by regulating the solvation structure; (c) high selectivity of ionic transport due to the unique structure and functional surface. Briefly speaking, the modular nature of MOFs with regard to composition, structure and internal environment is powerful to pursue high-performance electrolytes for current rechargeable batteries.27â€“29

In recent years, MOF-based electrolytes have inspired research in both electrochemistry and materials science communities. Benefitting from the significance and great opportunities in this novel field, there have been many research studies focused on MOF materials, particularly after 2018. This review aims to outline the state-of-the-art progress of MOF materials in electrolyte applications, including MOF-based SSEs (i.e., ionic liquid (IL)-based SSEs, polyoxyethylene (PEO)-based SSEs, single-ion conducting SSEs and others) and MOF-modified liquid electrolytes (LEs, i.e., self-assembling free-standing MOF separators, MOF-based composite separators and MOF membranes as coating interlayers on electrodes). Recent advances and challenges of MOF-derived electrolytes for various battery systems will be comprehensively reviewed. The perspectives and suggestions to address current dilemmas and future directions of MOF-derived electrolytes are also highlighted.

2. Fundamental chemistry of MOF-based electrolytes

2.1 Advantages and challenges

As discussed above, MOF-based electrolytes could deliver excellent performance on the basis of MOF materials, as shown in Fig. 1. Their advantages can be summarized as follows: (a) the versatile components of MOF materials enable various applications of electrolytes for different types of rechargeable batteries (e.g., metal-ion batteries,28,30,31 organic batteries32 or metalâ€“oxygen batteries33). (b) Ordered channels in MOF materials enable uniform plating processes of cation ions during the charging/discharging process, resulting in a stabilized interfacial layer and improved cycling stability. (c) Periodic crystalline structures with rich porosity provide homogeneously dispersed sites and well-defined pathways for ion diffusion, together with high surface area benefiting sufficient contact with other components, enabling the optimization of overall electrochemical properties (e.g., ion transference number and ion diffusion). (d) Tunable porous structures (particularly pore size and pore polarity) could serve as permselective channels for targeted ion/molecular sieves, suppressing electrode dissolution issues and solubilization of organic redox intermediates, as well as optimizing the solvation structure of electrolytes.26,34

Nevertheless, there are many challenges still existing in the synthesis of MOF materials for application in electrolytes.26,29 First, the high cost of tedious fabrication processes and expensive ligands are the top concerns. Thus, this seriously hinders the mass production and feasibility of MOF materials. Second, when incorporated into electrolytes, another concern in the application of MOF materials is the agglomeration of MOF nanoparticles due to the nano-effects, resulting in unsatisfactory electrochemical properties. Third, as for MOF-based membranes, it is still a huge challenge to obtain a perfect MOF-based membrane without any defects. This is because intrinsic material defects are harmful to ion diffusion, and unwanted grain boundaries could trigger the formation of dendrites. Last, the investigation of MOF-based electrolytes for rechargeable batteries is still in its infancy, as summarized in Table 1. As shown in Table 1, most of the studies mainly focus on the research of MOF components, synthesis methods and their structural characteristics. Note that MOFs possess different functions in various types of MOF-based electrolytes. Nevertheless, more attention should be paid on the battery performance, particularly on ion transport and the detailed operating mechanism. Therefore, it is of great significance to have a deep and systematic discussion for pursuing advanced MOF-based electrolytes.

2.2 Ionic conductivity

The ion transport mechanism of MOF-based electrolytes can be discussed from two aspects, as shown in Fig. 2. On the one hand, in MOF-based single-ion conductors, MOF materials without any ionic solvent intrinsically act as conductors to selectively conduct ions.50,51 Due to their inherent merits, such single-ion conductor-based membranes provide closely packed cation hopping sites along their ordered and periodic channels by storing a high density of charged species in a small geometric volume. Notably, a lot of coordinatively unsaturated cationic metal sites or functionalized cationic groups of MOFs without any solvent molecules can effectively anchor anions (Fig. 2a). For example, Park et al. reported that a Cu(II)â€“azolate MOF (MIT-20) with tubular pores and open metal sites effectively immobilized halide or pseudohalide anions, exhibiting free transmission of cations within the one-dimensional pores.52 Such a MOF-based electrolyte delivered a superior ionic conductivity of âˆ¼4.4 Ã— 10âˆ’4 S cmâˆ’1. Thus, the accessibility to MOF-based single-ion conductors provides new opportunities to develop novel electrolyte systems for rechargeable batteries. On the other hand, the ion-selectivity of MOF-based membranes with LEs or other conductors is based on the physical blocking effect.53

In detail, in these MOF-based electrolytes ionic transport channels are provided by organic LEs or other conductors, rather than by the MOFs themselves (Fig. 2b). Most of the MOF-based electrolytes are based on such an ion transport mechanism. 3. MOF-based SSEs

The application of MOFs in SSEs has become an emerging research hotspot in the energy storage field because of their intrinsic merits including (1) extraordinarily high surface and controllable surface polarity, optimizing the overall electrochemical properties of SSEs (e.g. ionic conductivity, electrochemical window and ion transference number).26 (2) Periodic crystalline structure with ordered channels, enabling well-defined pathways for ion transfer and uniform-cation plating/stripping processes.34 (3) Tunable porous structures (i.e. pore shape, pore size and pore polarity), enhancing cation diffusion and suppressing unsatisfactory side reactions. All the above super physicochemical properties elucidate the great opportunities for the fabrication of high-performance MOF-based SSEs. In this section, we discuss the significant milestones in detail and systematically summarize the recent progress in different SSE systems, including the IL-laded type, PEO-based type, single-ion conducting type and other types.

3.1 IL-laden SSEs

ILs have attracted considerable attention as novel green solvents in electrochemical devices (e.g., secondary batteries, capacitors and fuel cells). Their remarkable properties, including flame retardant ability, high ionic conductivity, and high electrochemical/thermal stability, are desirable for application in safe electrolytes.55 Recently, encapsulation of ILs into porous MOF materials has become an emerging research field for the development of new and environmentally friendly ionic conductors.56,57 Generally, MOFs behave as ideal outgoing reservoirs for ILs due to strong multiple interactions, including coulombic forces, van der Waals interactions, Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions, hydrogen-bonding, and coordination interaction, since their small micropores and tunable hostâ€“guest interactions benefit controlling the dynamics of ILs.58 Thus, based on the rich porosity and high surface-to-volume ratio of MOF materials, IL-laden MOFs (IL@MOFs) could not only retain all the above merits of ILs without the risk of leakage, but also enable boosted metal-ion transport within electrodes through effective interfacial contact. In addition, employing IL@MOFs as SSEs allows for the selective bonding of ions because of their unique interactions, inevitably affecting the intrinsic properties (i.e., ionic conductivity and transference number) of such electrolytes.59 Note that the structure information of prepared IL@MOFs, sizes of ILs, pore apertures, surface polarity, and coordination conditions of MOFs are greatly meaningful when they are employed as SSEs in batteries.

Early studies mainly focused on the effects of MOF materials on the phase change behavior of the confined ILs.60 The highly designable properties in terms of the surface polarity and pore structure dominate the overall properties of IL@MOF materials. In 2014, Kazuyuki et al. elucidated the pioneering example of an IL ((1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)amide), EMIM-TFSI) within the micropores of a MOF (Zn(2-methylimidazole)2, ZIF-8) by a simple physical mixing and a following heating method (Fig. 3a).60 Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) analysis indicated that EMI-TFSA maintains liquid phases by a nano-size effect in the micropores of ZIF-8, which was also witnessed in solid-state 19F static NMR measurements (Fig. 3b). However, the presence of fewer ion pairs of EMI-TFSA triggered a poor dynamic process of the ions. In their subsequent studies, they proposed that lithium ion (Li+) cations diffuse through the micropores via the exchange of the solvating TFSAâˆ’ anions, similar to the Grotthuss mechanism.59 Thus, a possible way of enhancing lithium diffusion is to employ a MOF with open metal sites as the host material for the IL, where the open metal sites could effectively anchor the counter-anions, accompanied by easier dissociation of lithium ions.5

3.2 PEO-based SSEs

SSEs have been widely studied because of their ability to overcome the leakage and combustibility problems of LEs.63â€“65 PEO-based SSEs have been industrialized due to their prominent advantages such as easy processability, excellent mechanical deformation ability, and satisfactory electrode|electrolyte compatibility compared to inorganic solid electrolytes (i.e., NaSICON type Li1.5Al0.5Ge1.5(PO4)3 (LAGP),17,66 garnet type LLZO,67,68 and sulfide type Li10GeP2S12 (LGPS),37,67,69). However, the low room temperature ionic conductivity (<âˆ¼10âˆ’5 S cmâˆ’1)70,71 and poor interface contact of PEO-based SSEs seriously hinder their practical application. There are many approaches to improve the ionic conductivity, one of which is to increase the current carrier concentration by selecting lithium salt with a high degree of dissociation, and the other is to reduce the crystallinity of the polymer matrix by adding a plasticizer72 or an inorganic filler.65,73 Intriguingly, MOFs are found to be effective fillers for PEO-based SSEs to enhance the ionic conductivity, compatibility, thermal stability, mechanical properties, and the lithium/electrolyte interfacial properties.26

Early studies mainly focused on the effects of MOF fillers on the ionic conductivity of PEO-based SSBs, but lacked research on their electrochemical properties. Yuan et al. pioneeringly proposed MOF nanoparticles as fillers in PEO-based SSEs to improve ionic conductivity.39 They prepared nano-sized (20â€“30 nm) MOF-5 (Zn4O(BDC)3, BDC = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate) by a solvothermal method and prepared a PEO/MOF-5 membrane by direct blending. This composite SSE delivered a high ionic conductivity of âˆ¼3.16 Ã— 10âˆ’5 S cmâˆ’1 at 25 Â°C, which was four times higher than that of the electrolyte without MOF-5 nanoparticles. The improved ionic conductivity was mainly due to the interaction between the Lewis acidic sites on MOF-5, the PEO chains and the lithium salt, which would improve the non-crystallization of PEO and increase the salt dissolution.39 Furthermore, porous MOF fillers could absorb the trace solvents and then form a facilitated passage for ion transport. These results verified the effectiveness of MOF-5 fillers in PEO-based SSEs; however, the battery performance needs to be further improved. Angulakshmi et al. also successfully synthesized PEO-based SSBs with UiO-66-NH2@SiO2 fillers.54 The PEO-based SSE with UiO-66-NH2@SiO2 fillers could effectively improve the ionic conductivity from âˆ¼7.6 Ã— 10âˆ’7 S cmâˆ’1 to âˆ¼8.1 Ã— 10âˆ’6 S cmâˆ’1 at 60 Â°C and effectively improve the Li+ transference number from âˆ¼0.2 to âˆ¼0.68 compared with the filler-free electrolyte (Fig. 4a). The exact performance depends on the intrinsic nature of MOF-fillers, including the morphologies, controlled porous structures, and designable components (including chemical compositions and surface polarities).38,70,74,75

3.3 Single-ion conducting SSEs

Single-ion conducting SSEs (denoted as SIC-SSEs) are a novel emerging hotspot due to their extremely small concentration polarization with the immobilized anions.78 The strategies for obtaining immobilized anions include (1) linking anions to the chain of polymers by covalent bonds; (2) linking anions to the inorganic main chain; and (3) adding a capturing agent to double-ion conductive SPEs.78â€“81 When most of the anions are immobilized, the cations are the only ones that can move and thus SIC-SSEs are obtained. Although MOFs are successfully used in SSEs to increase ion transfer number, enhance interfacial contact and stabilize cycling performance, there are still many problems to be solved. First, the influence of the physical properties (crystal size, orientation, aperture, morphology and structure types) of MOFs on SSEs remains to be determined which may play an important role in understanding the effects of the structure on performance. Second, the effect of chemical components of MOFs on SSEs should be further studied. Existing research studies on MOF-based SSEs are mainly focused on Zn-based, Mg-based, Zr-based and Cu-based MOF fillers; however, the role of the metal centrosomes has not been well studied. The reported MOF-based SSEs are all proved to be beneficial to ion transport due to the interactions between the MOF and ILs or metal salts or the polymer matrix, but the interaction mechanism of the complex system needs to be further clarified. Besides, the electrochemical stability of MOFs in MOF-based electrolytes during cycling is still unclear. Compared with SSEs (i.e., polymer SSEs and garnet-type SSEs), most of the MOF-based electrolytes may possess relatively low electrochemical stability. This is because most of the MOFs may undergo irreversible decomposition or conversion and structural collapse during the charge/discharge processes which has been demonstrated in cathode/anode applications.87,88 Meanwhile, the high porosity of MOFs will induce the increase of contact area between the electrode material and electrolyte, leading to a low volumetric energy density and low coulombic efficiency.27 Such behavior is detrimental to their practical applications. In addition, the stability between the ligands (i.e., carboxylate groups,41 imidazole rings,89 aromatic rings,90etc.) and the lithium metal has yet to be confirmed. It is important to make a reasonable choice of ligands, such as a F-doped ligand,91 trifluoromethanesulfonyl group,51etc. Last but not least, the metal centrosomes with higher reduction potential (vs. Li+) in theory may be reduced and deposited before lithium metal during cycling. This may also lead to poor cyclability of cells due to the structural collapse of MOFs. However, there is no symmetrical research in this field at present. In short summary, more efforts are required to achieve an in-depth but symmetrical understanding of the electrochemical stability of MOF-based electrolytes during cycling.

4. MOF-modified LEs

Note that when ester-based LEs are employed in LMBs, they trigger severe safety issues, including explosion and fire by thermal runaway, persistent side reactions, and non-uniform Li plating/stripping behavior with the formation of detrimental Li dendrites.17,92,93 Moreover, serious electrode dissolution issues and solubilization of their redox intermediates always lead to the obvious deterioration of electrochemical properties in some LMBs (i.e., lithiumâ€“organic batteries,45 lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries,48 or lithiumâ€“oxygen (Liâ€“O2) batteries33). The application of MOF materials probably improves the above issues by developing novel electrolyte systems for LE-based LMBs. Attributed to their intrinsically versatile properties, i.e., the plentiful cavity construction, MOFs with designed pore size could act as ionic sieves in their modified LEs, thus regulating the ion transport.53 In this case, MOF-based membranes are introduced into the current LEs, where the ion conductors are the organic/aqueous LEs rather than the MOF conductors themselves. Intriguingly, the open metal sites or the functional groups on MOFs enable the MOF-based LEs to selectively screen ions, thus improving the electrochemical properties of LMBs. In this section, we deeply discuss and symmetrically summarize the recent progress in LMBs based on three types of MOF-modified LEs, including self-assembling free-standing MOF separators, MOF-based composite separators and MOF membranes as coating interlayers on electrodes.

4.1 Self-assembling free-standing MOF separators

Despite the great promise of MOF materials, the fabrication of perfect MOF-based membranes faces a huge challenge due to a large number of interfacial voids and grain boundaries.94,95 The obtained MOF-based membranes with intrinsic defects cause inhomogeneous dispersions and large interfacial gaps, unavoidably resulting in the formation of a non-selective permeation pathway, thus further inducing dendrite formation in grain boundaries or deterioration of the electrochemical properties in full cells.18,53 Thus, it is of great significance to develop perfect free-standing MOF membranes as separators in LEs for high-performance LMBs.

Recently, free-standing MOF membranes were developed to optimize the Li+ solvation sheath for high-energy-density LMBs. 4.2 MOF-based composite separators

To date, the nature brittleness of MOF materials makes it hard to meet the current requirements of durability and stability in practical cell applications for free-standing MOF membranes. Constructing a MOF-based composite separator with both superior robustness and flexibility is greatly promising for their application in LMBs. Hence, various methods have been used to fabricate these MOF-based composite membranes, including vacuum filtration,48 electrospinning technique,97 and doctor-blading method.98 Nevertheless, it is urgent to develop facile, low cost and eco-friendly techniques for large-scale preparation of MOF-based composite membranes after taking industrial applications into consideration. 4.3 MOF membranes as coating interlayers on electrodes

Besides the application as MOF-based separators, MOF membranes could also regulate the cation solvation sheath and further control the transport and reaction process when acting as coating interlayers on electrodes.30,49,100,104 Such excellent coating interlayers could be seen as an effective artificial solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer, possessing both high ionic conductivity and cation transference number as well as electrochemical stability and ease of integration. In this section, we mainly summarize the operating mechanism and recent progress of MOF coating interlayers on electrodes in rechargeable batteries (including Li- and Zn-based batteries), based on the regulated solvation structure of electrolytes.104

For most of the MOF-based SSEs (including the IL-laded type, PEO-based type, and single-ion conducting type), the transference numbers and ionic conductivities can be improved by optimizing the structure of MOF fillers, functionalizing MOF materials to improve the surface properties, and optimizing the interaction relationship between components. Specifically, the IL-laded type SSEs have a wider working temperature window (âˆ’20â€“150 Â°C) due to the nonflammability and nonvolatility of ILs. However, the transference numbers of the IL-laded type SSEs still need to be optimized. The high manufacturing cost of IL-laded type SSEs is still a challenge for industry application. As for PEO-based SSEs, their high flexibility is beneficial for large-scale industrial production. The low oxidation potential and low ionic conductivity at room temperature need further improvement to achieve their application in high-voltage batteries, under shade and at room temperature. As for the single-ion conducting type SSEs, the high cation transference numbers and high mechanical strength are helpful to suppress dendrite formation. However, the theoretical basis needs to be further improved to guide the design of MOF structures and promote their practical application.

There are three-types of MOF-modified LEs, including self-assembling free-standing MOF separators, MOF-based composite separators and MOF membranes as coating interlayers on electrodes. The open metal sites or the functional groups on MOFs with matched pore sizes enable the MOF-based LEs to selectively screen ions and provide a uniform ion flux, thus improving the electrochemical properties of LMBs. Although there have been many studies on the application of MOF-based membranes in Liâ€“S batteries, Liâ€“O2 batteries and other electrochemical energy storage devices, their practical application presents great challenges. The low mechanical strength and low flexibility of self-assembling free-standing MOF separators may worsen life expectancy and safety.

The current synthetic strategies of MOFs mainly include template methods,105,106 modulation methods,107,108 microemulsion processes,109 chemical etching processes,110,111 spray-drying methods,112etc. However, the large-scale application of MOFs is impeded by their high cost, complex synthesis conditions and non-environmentally friendly nature. Specifically, the synthesis process of high-performance MOFs is usually conducted under complex conditions (i.e., high temperature, high pressure, templates, surfactants, catalysts or organic solvents), which are not only harmful to the environment but also cost higher than those of commercial electrolytes. Some MOFs with special structures require specific technology and equipment (i.e., modulators, spray pyrolysis furnace) which further impedes their mass production.107,112 In short summary, the preparation of MOF-based electrolytes requires higher raw material costs and higher manufacturing costs than that of commercial electrolytes. It is highly urgent to develop high-economic-efficiency manufacturing methods with environmental friendliness for large-scale industrial application.

For the future research of MOFs in electrolyte applications, multiple strategies to achieve high-performance MOF-derived electrolytes are also proposed as follows:

(1) The influence of physical properties (i.e., crystal size, orientation, aperture, morphology and structure types) of MOFs on battery performances, particularly ionic conductivity, remains to be determined, which plays an important role in understanding the effects of structure on performance and is of great significance to guide the synthesis of specific structures of MOFs. For example, 2D MOFs are more suitable for fabricating self-assembling free-standing MOF separators, while 3D MOFs are more suitable for SSEs. Widely accepted mechanisms are developed to illustrate the improved ionic conductivity: the porous structure and unsaturated metallic center sites of MOFs can directly bind anions to facilitate ion transport. Besides, the same MOFs used in different systems may play different roles.54,100 More aspects of the corresponding mechanisms need to be studied.

(2) The effect of chemical components of MOFs on SSEs should be further elucidated. The metallic center sites of MOFs are beneficial to the improvement of ionic conductivity. However, the roles of different metallic center sites and ligands are unclear, particularly, in terms of interaction with other components. Theoretical calculations may be helpful to understand the complicated systems.

(3) The mass production of MOFs with low cost, and controllable structure and composition is another great challenge. Particularly, novel and efficient methodologies for fabricating SSE membranes and separators with MOF fillers need to be explored. The existing preparation methods are not only low in efficiency, high in energy consumption and expensive, but also involve the use of a large number of organic reagents which are harmful to human health and the environment.

(4) Although MOFs can improve the ionic conductivity and induce uniform metal ion deposition in different systems, the detailed ion transport mechanisms in MOF-based membranes or at the interface need to be clarified deeply.

(5) It is necessary to improve the stability of MOFs for resisting electrochemical and chemical corrosion in batteries. Developing high-efficiency synthesis methods or modifying MOFs can improve the stability to meet the requirement of high-performance batteries. In addition, much attention should be given to the electrochemical properties of MOF-based batteries.
