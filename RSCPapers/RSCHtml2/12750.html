Abstract

In this study, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are used as a host for Ag species and the template of ZnO to fabricate Ag-embedded porous ZnO nanocomposites (Agâ€“ZnO NCs) with rich and stable interfaces, which is proven to be a perfect platform for constructing hybrid materials. Remarkably, Agâ€“ZnO NCs exhibit high catalytic activity for the thermocatalytic decomposition of ammonium perchlorate (AP) under the assistance of ultraviolet and visible light irradiation. The highly enhanced catalytic activity of Agâ€“ZnO NCs originates from the synergistic effects of thermal-driven thermocatalysis and solar light-driven photocatalysis. Especially, under the irradiation of visible light, the plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer (PRET) between the plasmonic metal Ag and the neighboring semiconductor ZnO enhances the charge carrier generation efficiency and suppresses the bulk recombination in the semiconductor, which are responsible for its outstanding catalytic performance.

Introduction

The development of catalysis techniques is still a significant objective due to their paramount importance for energy conversion. Photocatalysis and thermocatalysis have been studied for several decades, and substantial progress has been made in their corresponding research fields as two completely independent systems.1â€“6 The main advantage of photocatalysis is that it can utilize renewable solar energy to generate carriers (electrons and holes) and realize the initiation of redox reactions. However, compared to photocatalysis, thermocatalysis must be conducted at a higher temperature using an additional heater, which usually suffers from high energy consumption.7,8 Therefore, to overcome the current obstacles in thermocatalytic systems, it is inevitable to couple photocatalysis and thermocatalysis to drive reactions more efficiently. Recently, the synergetic effect of photo/thermocatalysis has been reported by several groups, where significant enhancement in catalytic activity was achieved for the oxidation of benzene and the CO hydrogenation reaction.8â€“11

The decomposition reaction of ammonium perchlorate (AP), as one of the representative thermocatalytic cases, has been the focus of the chemistry and material science communities for the last half-century.6,12,13 It is well-known that AP is the most widely used energetic oxidizer in solid rocket propellants, and its decomposition process can greatly influence the combustion performance of the propellant. In this thermocatalytic history, a variety of transition metal oxide materials have dominated. Especially, the use of zinc oxide (ZnO) as a catalyst has attracted much attention because of its low cost, nontoxicity, ease of availability, high electron mobility, and good physical and chemical stability.14â€“17 Meanwhile, ZnO is frequently studied as a promising photocatalyst both domestically and abroad.18â€“20 It is well acknowledged that the thermocatalytic mechanism of the decomposition of AP is very similar to the photocatalytic mechanism, consisting of an increase in the separation of electrons and holes.17 Therefore, the introduction of light into the thermocatalytic system, resulting in photo/thermocatalysis, holds tremendous promise to increase the catalytic activity of ZnO in this field for the AP decomposition reaction.

However, due to the wide bandgap (3.37 eV) of the ZnO semiconductor, the key step for determining the catalytic ability of ZnO specifically lies in the solar spectrum utilization efficiency and the charge-carrier separation.19,20 Recently, an approach involving coupling semiconductors with plasmonic metal nanoparticles (Ag,19,21 Au,22,23 Cu,24etc.) has attracted considerable attention as a means to extend the spectral response of ZnO toward visible light and improve its charge-carrier generation efficiency and suppression of bulk recombination. Among the plasmonic metal nanoparticles, Ag is regarded as one of the best materials because it possesses many unique properties such as bioaffinity, high chemical stability, and good charge storage capability.18 To achieve synergistic effects between the localized surface plasmon resonances (LSPRs) of Ag nanoparticles and band-gap excitation from the ZnO semiconductor, the formation of hybrid Ag/ZnO catalysts with good contact is the key process.

Several methods to anchor Ag nanoparticles on ZnO surface such as photochemical deposition,18,25,26 soft-chemical approach27 and the precipitationâ€“thermal decomposition method19,28 have been reported. In this study, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), as a relatively new class of porous materials synthesized from the self-assembly of organic linkers and metal joints into an infinite array with a high surface area, structural diversity, and tailorability, are used for the construction of the metal/semiconductor hybrid materials.29â€“31 One of the inherent superiorities of MOFs is their permanent porosity, which makes them attractive for confining guest species, particularly metal species, for the formation of metal ions@MOF hybrid materials with the metal precursor located in the MOF pores.32â€“34 Besides, due to their unique structures, MOFs have been used as novel templates to synthesize porous metal oxides with tailorable functionality, large surface areas and inter-connected pores.31 Thus, by pyrolysis of metal ions@MOF hybrids under an air atmosphere at a suitable temperature, the metal ion precursors and MOFs can be simultaneously transformed into metal nanoparticles and porous metal oxides, where the metal nanoparticles are highly embedded in the MOF-derived porous metal oxide network.

Herein, we report a novel method to fabricate Ag-embedded porous ZnO nanocomposites using zeolitic imidazolate framework-8 (ZIF-8), an important subclass of MOFs, as the host for Ag species and the template of ZnO. Ag NPs and ZnO NPs are successfully assembled into a porous rhombic dodecahedron structure to investigate the influence of Ag NPs on the thermocatalytic and photo-assisted thermocatalytic performance of AP decomposition. Agâ€“ZnO NCs result in enhanced catalytic activity for the thermal decomposition of AP, where the peak temperature decreases from 432 to 291 Â°C, and the activation energy decreases from 130.1 Â± 9.3 to 57.7 Â± 5.4 kJ molâˆ’1; these values are even lower than that of ZnO (309 Â°C and 85.4 Â± 9.1 kJ molâˆ’1, respectively). Under UV and visible light, Agâ€“ZnO NCs afford remarkable enhancement in the thermocatalytic AP decomposition performance due to the electron transfer from ZnO to Ag and the generation of additional electronâ€“hole pairs near the Agâ€“ZnO interface caused by the plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer (PRET) mechanism. Also, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that light is introduced in a thermocatalytic AP decomposition system.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All reagents and solvents were commercially available and used without further purification. Zinc nitrate hexahydrate (Zn(NO3)2Â·6H2O), 2-methylimidazole (MeIm) and silver nitrate (AgNO3) were used as received from Aladdin Industrial Inc. (Shanghai, China). Methanol (CH3OH) was purchased from Beijing Tong Guang Fine Chemicals Company.

ZIF-8 was synthesized using the method described in the literature.29 Typically, Zn(NO3)2Â·6H2O (810 mg, 40 mL) and MeIm (526 mg, 40 mL) were stirred in a methanolic solution at room temperature for 12 h. The as-synthesized ZIF-8 crystals were then immersed in a methanolic solution of AgNO3 under constant stirring for 12 h at room temperature. During this time, Ag+ ions were loaded within the micropores of ZIF-8 to obtain Ag+@ZIF-8. Finally, Ag+@ZIF-8 was pyrolyzed at 450 Â°C for 3 h under an air atmosphere at a heating rate of 3 Â°C minâˆ’1 to obtain the hierarchical porous catalyst Agâ€“ZnO NCs. By heating Ag+@ZIF-8 crystals in an air atmosphere, Agâ€“ZnO NCs were fabricated. Fig. 3a demonstrates the XRD pattern of the obtained Agâ€“ZnO NCs. As can be seen, the diffraction peaks perfectly corresponded to hexagonal wurtzite ZnO (JCPDS Card No. 36-1451) and cubic Ag (JCPDS Card No. 04-0783),38 indicating that the transformation of ZIF-8 to ZnO and the conversion of Ag+ ions to Ag occurred simultaneously and eventually yielded hybrid Agâ€“ZnO NCs during the pyrolysis process. This was also confirmed by the XPS results shown in Fig. 3b. All the signals in the survey spectrum of Agâ€“ZnO NCs could be ascribed to Zn, O, Ag and C. In the inset of Fig. 3b, the binding energies at around 373.8 and 367.8 eV with the splitting of 6 eV are indicative of the existence of metallic Ag, and peaks for other states are absent. It should be noted that the peak positions of Ag 3d shifted remarkably to lower binding energies compared with that of bulk metallic Ag (Ag 3d5/2, 368.2 eV; Ag 3d3/2, 374.2 eV), suggesting decrease in the electron density of Ag.18,25,28 This proposed electron transfer process from Ag to ZnO can be explained by the decrease in the electron density of Ag by the band theory, as shown in Fig. 3d. Due to the smaller work function of Ag (4.26 eV) than that of ZnO (5.2 eV), the Fermi level of Ag (Efm) is higher than that of ZnO (Efs). Hence, it is much easier for the electrons in Ag to transfer to adjacent ZnO during the formation of Agâ€“ZnO NCs, shifting Efs of ZnO in the composition to a higher value and Efm of Ag to a lower value; this finally results in an equivalent Fermi level (Ef).39,40 As a result, the equilibrated Fermi level electrons under visible light irradiation can be injected rapidly into the conduction band of ZnO via a surface phonon resonance (SPR) mechanism, and the photoexcited electrons on the conduction band of ZnO under UV irradiation can be more easily transferred to Ag NPs, enhancing the catalytic activity of Agâ€“ZnO NCs. On the other hand, the equilibrated Fermi level also indicates strong interaction between Ag NPs and ZnO NPs and further verifies the fabrication of Ag-embedded ZnO composites. Finally, the high loading content of Ag NPs in this composite (19.2 wt%) determined by the full peak fitting method using the Jade software is also beneficial for improved catalytic activity.

Thermocatalytic performance

The thermocatalytic effects of ZnO and Agâ€“ZnO NCs on the decomposition of AP were investigated via TG-DSC measurements. Fig. 5a and b show the DSC and TG curves of both pure AP and mixture of AP with ZnO and Agâ€“ZnO NCs at 3% mass basis under a heating rate of 10 K minâˆ’1. For pure AP, three thermal signals centered at 242 Â°C, 313 Â°C and 432 Â°C are observed, which correspond to the crystal transformation of AP from orthorhombic to cubic phase, low-temperature decomposition (LTD) and high-temperature decomposition (HTD). The LTD process has been proposed to be a heterogeneous process involving a series of chemical reactions, in which the decomposition process starts with the electron transfer in the AP subsurface to yield NH3 and HClO4 and ends on the unreacted NH3-covered surface due to the slow reaction rates for the decomposition of HClO4 and oxidation of NH3.15,16 The HTD process is assigned to the oxidation of NH3 by HClO4 in the gas phase.41

From the DSC curves (Fig. 5a), the endothermic peaks of AP without and with the catalysts are similar, indicating that the phase transformation of AP does not occur, whereas a significant difference in the exothermic processes suggests the disappearance of the HTD process. Catalytic mechanism of Agâ€“ZnO NCs

According to the mechanism for the thermal decomposition of pure AP proposed by Jacobs, the decomposition process starts with proton transfer in NH4+ClO4âˆ’ molecules to yield NH3 and HClO4 and finally ends with the decomposition of HClO4 and oxidation of NH3. When semiconductor materials (such as ZnO,16,42 g-C3N4,45,46 and CuO47) are applied in the decomposition of AP, one catalytic mechanism has been proposed as follows:
eâˆ’ + HClO4 â†’ HClOx (x = 0, 1, 2, 3) + Oâˆ’

Oâˆ’ + NH3 â†’ N2O + H2O + eâˆ’

h+ + NH3 â†’ N2O + H2O

In the above equations, HClO4 is adsorbed on the surface of the semiconductor nanoparticles and forms negative oxygen ions Oâˆ’ by binding to the thermally excited intrinsic electrons of the semiconductor materials. The thermogenerated Oâˆ’ and h+ exhibit powerful oxidation ability, which can further react with NH3 to form H2O and N2O. It should be noted that the decomposition of AP including the decomposition of HClO4 and oxidation of NH3 is accelerated by the semiconductor catalyst.

To understand the thermocatalytic and light-assisted thermocatalytic mechanisms of the Agâ€“ZnO NCs for the decomposition of AP, the PL spectra and UV-Vis diffuse reflectance spectra were measured, as shown in Fig. 8. PL spectroscopy has been proven to be an effective technique to determine the charge recombination and migration efficiency of semiconductor materials.

Fig. 8a shows the PL spectra of the as-prepared ZnO and Ag/ZnO NCs measured at an excitation wavelength of 325 nm. It can be observed that the individual curves show three emission bands including a near UV emission at around 380â€“400 nm, a strong blue emission at about 450â€“470 nm, and a weak and broad green emission at about 550 nm.40 Also, the emission intensity of the PL spectrum of Agâ€“ZnO NCs is lower than that of ZnO, indicating that Ag NPs act as electron sinks and thus hinder the recombination of electrons and holes.25,39 For the thermocatalytic performance of the Agâ€“ZnO NCs for the decomposition of AP, as shown in Fig. 9a, the valence-band (VB) electrons of ZnO can be excited by heating energy over 250 Â°C to move to the conduction-band (CB).16,42 Then, in the presence of Ag NPs, the electrons transfer to Ag, leading to corresponding increase in the separation efficiency of electrons and holes, which is favorable for enhancing the thermocatalytic activity of Agâ€“ZnO NCs. In addition, with the assistance of the UV light irradiation, as shown in Fig. 9b, more electrons in VB of ZnO can be excited, further accelerating the thermal decomposition of AP.

Ag is regarded as one of the best plasmonic materials, which possesses localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) in the visible wavelength region.48,49 Also, the optical properties of the obtained ZnO and Agâ€“ZnO NCs were measured via UV-Vis diffuse reflectance spectroscopy (DRS), as shown in Fig. 8b. As can be observed, pure ZnO shows a typical absorption edge at about 385 nm in the UV region. For the Ag NP-embedded porous ZnO composites, there is a wide absorption peak at around 440 nm, which is caused by the LSPR characteristics of silver.25,50 The extended absorbance of Agâ€“ZnO NCs in the visible region has considerable practical importance to enhance catalytic activity due to the efficient utilization of visible light. Also, the red-shifted plasmon absorption provides some evidence of the interaction between Ag and ZnO, which is also in accordance with the XPS results.51

Hence, when Agâ€“ZnO NCs are exposed to visible light, the outstanding SPR property of Ag NPs makes them excellent light-harvesting antennae. This is followed by the generation of strong local electric fields, the intensity of which is several orders of magnitude higher than that of the far-field incident light.52 For this SPR-induced strong local electromagnetic field, Ag NPs can effectively concentrate the incident photon energy and transfer it to the neighbouring ZnO semiconductor via the plasmon-induced resonance energy transfer (PRET) mechanism, thereby remarkably enhancing the concentration of the electronâ€“hole pairs in ZnO.53 As shown in Fig. 9c, under the irradiation of visible light and treatment of heat, SPR of the Ag NPs and the electronâ€“hole pairs in ZnO can be excited simultaneously. The plasmonic hot electrons from Ag NPs can be rapidly injected into the conduction band of ZnO, further increasing the number of electrons in ZnO. Meanwhile, the electrons and holes are captured to accelerate the decomposition of HClO4 and oxidation of NH3, respectively, to finally decompose AP. Besides, the specific Ag-embedded porous rhombic dodecahedron ZnO structure provides a rich and stable interface between the plasmonic metal and semiconductor, which is the main reason for the outstanding catalytic activity of Agâ€“ZnO NCs for the thermal decomposition of AP under visible light irradiation. On the one hand, the rich and stable interface between Ag NPs and ZnO can be expected to make the electron transfer within the materials easier and quicker, resulting in higher activity. On the other hand, the special mesoporous structure is good for efficient mass transfer, offering more opportunities for the diffusion and adsorption of product molecules during the catalytic decomposition reaction of AP. Based on the above discussion, the visible light-enhanced performance of Agâ€“ZnO NCs for the thermal decomposition of AP can be due to the synergistic effect of the plasmonic effect of Ag NPs under visible light irradiation as well as the specific porous structure of Agâ€“ZnO NCs with a rich and stable interface.

Conclusions

In summary, we have prepared Ag-embedded porous ZnO nanocomposites using ZIF-8, an important type of MOF, as the host for Ag species and the template for ZnO. Also, the Ag nanoparticles and ZnO nanoparticles are simultaneously assembled into a porous rhombic dodecahedron structure, providing a rich and stable interface between them. The obtained Agâ€“ZnO NCs exhibit intensive thermocatalytic effects on the decomposition of AP, as evidenced by the decreased peak temperature of 291 Â°C and decreased activation energy of 57.7 Â± 5.4 kJ molâˆ’1. Due to the similarity between the thermocatalytic mechanism and photocatalytic mechanism of Agâ€“ZnO NCs associated with the effective separation of electrons and holes, the remarkably enhanced catalytic activity of Agâ€“ZnO NCs is reasonable under the assistance of UV and visible light. Especially, the localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) of Ag nanoparticles within the visible wavelength region is responsible for the enhanced catalytic performance because it can improve the generation of charge carriers while suppressing the bulk recombination in the ZnO semiconductor.
