Abstract

Nature acts as a “Bio-Laboratory”, consisting of plants, algae, fungi, yeast, etc., which are composed of bio-molecules. These naturally occurring bio-molecules play a vital role in the synthesis of cuprous oxide (Cu2O) nanoparticles (NPs) and their nanocomposites (NCs), with distinct shapes and sizes, thereby acting as a driving force for designing safer, greener and sustainable routes using less hazardous chemicals. The present review focuses on the eco-friendly protocols for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs, techniques used for their characterisation and their various applications in organic catalysis, biosensing, environmental remediation and medicinal chemistry. The pivotal role of Cu2O NPs and their NCs in catalysing coupling (C–C and C–N), multicomponent, oxidation–reduction and heterocyclic reactions is highlighted. This emphasises their role in environmental sustainability and versatility in heterogenous catalysis. Furthermore, their use in biosensing (electrochemical detection) generates special interest in bioscience and clinical diagnosis. Their significance ensures a bridge between academic research and environmental safety.

Introduction

In the recent past, metal and metal oxide nanomaterials1,2 have received increasing attention and various synthetic routes demonstrated for their synthesis. The wide efficiency and versatility of these nano materials have further promoted their design and initiated the tailoring of suitable sizes and morphologies of metal and metal oxide NPs. The synthesis of nanoscale transition metal oxides3 such as tin oxide, iron oxide, titanium oxide, and copper oxide is of significant interest owing to their enhanced catalytic activity, high thermal conductivity, excellent antibacterial/antimicrobial activity, availability of economical and scalable synthetic routes, and non-toxicity. Among the various transition metal oxides, the production of Cu2O NPs and their NCs is of special interest in the scientific and industrial fields due to their various applications such as gas-sensing,4 solar cells,5 antibacterial agents,6 catalysis,7 and antifouling coatings.8 Recently, antifouling paints have been formulated with Cu2O NPs together with other chemicals to act as biocides,9 which are meant to impede the growth of algae and other marine organisms. The distinct properties of Cu2O NPs have been greatly harnessed for environmental remediation. Cu2O is also a p-type semiconductor with a small band gap of about 2 eV, which makes it an important material in electronics, magnetic storage, catalysis and solar energy conversion10 (shown in Fig. 1). Normally, Cu2O NPs and their NCs are obtained either via the oxidation of pure copper (thermal oxidation) or reduction of Cu2+. In the literature, there are several reports using different methods for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs such as electrochemical deposition,11 microwave synthesis,12 solvothermal methods,13 liquid phase synthesis,14–18 seed-mediated synthesis19 and microemulsions.20 All these were used to obtain Cu2O NPs with different sizes and morphologies and in certain methods even control these parameters. Among the methods, liquid phase synthesis should be highlighted due to its simplicity, no requirement of expensive lab gear and handling of hazard reagents, low cost and short reaction time. In this method, the synthesis generally starts with the addition of sodium hydroxide to a copper salt (e.g. copper chloride, copper acetate, copper nitrate, and copper sulphate),21 and then a reducing agent is added to obtain Cu2O NPs. Also, a surfactant can be added to control the particle size (e.g. Triton X-100).22–25 The major limitation of chemical methods for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs is the use of harsh and expensive chemicals, which damage the environment and are not bio-compatible.

The nano-version of Cu2O has further enhanced its interest from the scientific world and motivates researchers to develop newer routes for its synthesis together with safeguarding the environment using the least amount of harsh chemicals. In an era where every scientific activity directly or indirectly causes a great threat to the environment, the reproducible, facile, effortless, eco-friendly biological synthesis of stable and efficient Cu2O NPs and their NCs has recently envisaged a surge in designing green protocols using both biological extracts and biological wastes (waste to wealth approach) to synthesize a wide range of NPs. Extracts from plant leaves, roots, latex, seeds and stems have also been used for the synthesis of NPs since they act as both stabilizing/capping agents and reducing agents. All biological extracts contain organic compounds such as polyphenols, carbohydrates, and flavones, which act as reducing agents and stabilizing agents.

The main objective of this review is to present the various biogenic methods for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs and highlight the methods used for their characterization, vital applications in catalysis, bio-sensing, environment remediation and medicinal applications. In catalysis, the main focus is coupling (C–C and C–N), multicomponent, oxidation–reduction and heterocyclic reactions. The current findings entail the literature of the applications of Cu2O NPs and their NCs between 2006 and 2018. This is an important review showcasing green methods for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their applications, emphasising their role in organic catalysis and environmental sustainability, which will surely have significance due to the limited number of steps involved and utilization of natural extracts, making them highly cost effective in nature. The green methods for the synthesis of NPs and NCs together with their applications can be certainly mimicked at the bulk level to ensure major gains and productivity of the current research.

Biogenic synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs

Biogenic synthesis involves a very simple procedure. Several plants have been reported in the literature for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs (Tables 1 and 2). The synthesis of NPs is entirely dependent on the chemicals present in the precursor such as saponins, polyphenols, terpenoids, and carbohydrates.26–32 It was observed that the kinetic and thermodynamic modes for the growth of Cu2O NPs can be tailored through slight variations in experimental parameters, such as temperature, concentration and nature of the substrate/additive (ligand, polymer, surfactant, and ion), pH and reaction time.26–32 In all the protocols, the plant parts are collected from different sources, and thoroughly washed with tap and distilled water to remove unwanted materials. The parts are either dried or ground to form a powder and boiled in different solvents (water, ethanol, etc.) or used directly to obtain plant extract (PE). Mostly all extractions are carried out in aqueous medium since all the active ingredients are water soluble (concept of like dissolves like is applied). Different concentration of copper salts as metal precursor and PE can be used for the synthesis of NPs. Generally PE is mixed with copper salt solution and the bio chemicals present in the PE act as a reducing agent (bioreduction) for the synthesis of Cu2O NPs and their NCs and as a stabilizing agent (capping), particularly in the case of Cu2O NPs (as shown in Fig. 2). NPs/NCs are obtained via centrifugation at high speed and can be monitored by visual observation or using a UV-Visible spectrophotometer.

Size

The size of NPs is generally described as the diameter of spherical particles in the solid phase. There are different methods (electron microscopy, laser diffraction, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and X-ray diffraction) to measure the particles size of NPs, but TEM is most widely used. In the liquid phase, the size of NPs can be determined using dynamic light scattering.38

Crystallography

Powder X-ray diffraction may be used to determine the crystal structures of NPs. The diffraction peaks at 2θ values of 29.58, 36.5, 42.36, 61.64 and 73.62 correspond to the (110), (111), (200), (220) and (311), respectively, which clearly show the presence of pure crystalline Cu2O. The strong and sharp peaks of the prepared Cu2O NPs indicate the high crystallinity of the NPs.

Morphology

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)38 and scanning electron microscopy (SEM)39 are used to determine the morphology of NPs.

Zeta potential

The zeta potential of NPs dispersed in liquid is determined by using different electrophoretic techniques.26

Templates for the synthesis of Cu2O NCs

NPs can be stabilised or functionalised using templates to impart useful properties by controlling their size, morphology and protecting their surfaces, thereby preventing aggregation. Many commercial templates have been reported as functionalising agents for altering the shape, size and properties of NCs but these are difficult to remove and do not easily degrade. Thus, commercial templates are hazardous to the environment. The preparation of homogenous NCs using bio-molecules such as polysaccharides has recently gained interest due to their non-toxic nature and no harsh synthetic procedures. Polysaccharides have emerged as a renewable green alternative template for the fabrication of NCs.26,37 Polysaccharides are a class of polymeric carbohydrate molecules with repeating units of mono or disaccharides linked together by glycosidic linkages. They act as capping agents in the synthesis of NPs and as templates for the fabrication of NCs since they are low cost, hydrophilic, stable, safe, biodegradable and non-toxic. The synthesis is carried out in the presence of water as a solvent; thus, eliminating the use of toxic solvents. Carbohydrates, which have aldehyde groups, play a reducing and controlling role during the formation of Cu2O NPs in solution.34

Applications of Cu2O NPs and their NCs

Cu2O NPs and their NCs may have different applications depending on their various properties, which are highly influenced by their size, surface properties, optical and magnetic traits, with the synthesis method being important for controlling all these parameters, and thus, their biological properties. Some of these applications include doping materials in semiconductors, catalysts for different reactions, biosensors, remedial action, and antibacterial. We describe their advantageous applications in different subheads to emphasize the remarkable results as a guidance to new researchers for future prospects.

Catalytic applications

Coupling reactions. A coupling reaction in organic chemistry is the general term for a variety of reactions, where two hydrocarbon fragments are coupled with the aid of a metal catalyst. The most common catalyst is palladium, but an increasing number of reactions use nickel. Other catalysts include copper, platinum, iron and cobalt.44
C–C coupling reaction. Carbon–carbon coupling reactions are among the most useful and most widely studied synthetic transformations, in which two hydrocarbon fragments are coupled using a metal catalyst. In a coupling reaction having a common intermediate, the energy required is transformed from one side of the reaction to the other.45,46
Synthesis of amides by amidation of aryl halides with isocyanides using Cu2O NPs as a catalyst. Sarkar and Sen group developed an environmentally benign chemical process for the C–C coupling of aryl halides with isocyanides in aqueous media using Cu2O NPs as a reusable catalyst in the absence of additional stabilizing ligands or additives and organic solvents. Amides were obtained in good yields (74–88%) for a variety of aryl or aliphatic isocyanides and aryl halides (Scheme 1).47The scope and generality of the method were investigated using equimolar aryl or aliphatic isocyanides (2) and aryl halides (1) to give the desired product (3). It was found that the morphology of the catalyst remained unchanged even after three uses.

Sonogashira coupling reactions of aryl iodides with phenylacetylene catalysed by Cu2O@Cu NPs. Varma and co-workers tested the core/shell nanomaterial Cu2O@Cu for the Sonogashira coupling reaction.48 The Cu2O@Cu NPs exhibited the ability to catalyse the coupling reaction of aryl iodides (4) and phenylacetylene (5) to yield the corresponding diarylalkynes (6) in excellent yields (85–94%) in the absence of ligand or noble metal (Pd) (Scheme 2). When only Cu2O or Cu NPs were used, no target products were obtained. Thus, Cu2O@Cu NPs were used to promote the Sonogashira coupling reaction instead of the expensive Pd and other noble-metal catalysts. The Cu2O@Cu NCs recovered after the reaction retained their structure, and thus were stable. The catalyst could be recycled, and thus was compatible with the cost-effective and environmentally benign synthesis principle. The Sonogashira coupling reactions for the synthesis of ynones have garnered interest due to their potential for the development of valuable organic compounds/intermediates.49–51 Accordingly, the replacement of conventional Pd-based catalytic systems with earth-abundant metal-based catalysts still remains an area of active research.

Cycloaddition reaction via click chemistry. Click chemistry is an approach to develop a set of powerful, selective, and modular building blocks such as azide and alkyne, which work for both small and large scale synthesis.62 Synthetic methodologies facilitating the rapid construction of molecules have attracted the attention of many synthetic chemists. Among them, the 1,3 dipolar cycloaddition reaction, also known as Huisgen cycloaddition, between an azide and terminal alkynes giving rise to 1,2,3-trizole species is of particular interest. This reaction has far reaching applications since both alkyne and azide moieties are major components in many compounds used in the synthesis of medicinal compounds and can also be tagged into a wide range of substituents. The copper(I) catalysed version of this reaction gives only the 4-isomer and is a classic click reaction, which is catalysed at room temperature in 15 minutes to give 91% yield. Copper-catalysed click reactions work essentially on terminal alkynes, where compounds undergo metal insertion into the terminal alkynes.

Multicomponent reactions (MCRs). MCRs are a synthetic methodology also referred to as a multi-component assembly process, in which three or more starting materials react to form a single component in a single reaction vessel to form a new product, which contains portions of all the components. A multitude of MCRs exist, e.g. isocyanide MCRs,76 free-radical mediated MCRs,77 MCRs based on organoboron compounds and metal-catalysed MCRs.78 Among them, the isocyanide MCRs methodologies have applications in most stages of the drug discovery process. Here, we discuss the different MCRs catalysed by Cu(I) NPs and NCs.

Synthesis of propargylamines

Cu2O–ZnO NCs catalysed propargylamines synthesis. H. Sarvari et al. efficiently developed the three-component coupling of aldehydes (35) or ketones (36), terminal alkynes (38), and amines (37) to synthesize propargylamines (39) via C–H activation using Cu2O–ZnO NCs as a catalyst. Cu2O–ZnO showed the best catalytic activity at 100 °C in the absence of any additives or base under solvent-free conditions (Scheme 13).79 Aromatic, aliphatic and heteroaromatic aldehydes were efficiently converted into the corresponding propargylamines to achieve excellent yields. The reactivity of aryl aldehydes with electron-rich groups was faster, while substitution of electron-withdrawing groups on the benzene ring decreased the reactivity and required longer time for the completion of the reaction. However, 4-nitrobenzaldehyde did not give the product even after a long reaction time. To extend the scope of the reaction, various cyclic and acyclic secondary amines such as piperidine, morpholine, pyrrolidine, dibutylamine, and 2,2-(azanediylbis(ethane-2,1-diyl))bis(isoindoline-1,3-dione) were used, which gave good results. The best achievement is that this reaction was also applicable for aliphatic ketones, including cyclic ketones, but aromatic ketone, such as acetophenone and benzophenone, did not give the desired products.82 In the case of acyclic ketones, such as acetone, only a trace of the desired product was obtained after a long time period. Finally, the catalyst was easily recycled.

Biosensing applications

Biosensors are integrated receptor-transducer devices capable of providing selective quantitative or semi quantitative analytical information using a biological recognition element.107 These biological elements include enzymes, whole cell and tissues. They are generally highly selective and offer real-time measurements for bio-analysis and bioprocess control. Cu2O NP-based biosensors have been widely studied for the accurate electrochemical detection of many biomolecules, which is extremely vital to the development of biomedical instruments and clinical diagnosis. Electrochemical biosensors mainly are three types: amperometric-based, potentiometric-based and impedimetric-based, where the amperometric-based mode is the most widely used.

An ultrasensitive amperometric immunosensor based on Cu2O@CeO2-Au NCs108 was synthesized by Y. Dong et al. A novel label-free electrochemical immunosensor was developed for the quantitative detection of prostate-specific antigen (PSA). An Au–Cu2O/RGO NC-based electrochemical sensor for the selective and simultaneous detection of dopamine and uric acid was developed by R. Sivasubramanian and co-workers. Biological fluids such as human serum and urine were employed to validate the performance of the sensor for real-time applications.109 Cu2O NPs were successfully used for the fabrication of spray-coated and screen-printed working electrodes on alumina substrates for the electrochemical detection of purine bases. J. Chomoucka and co-workers used Cu2O NP-based amperometric-based biosensors for the simultaneous detection of adenine and guanine.110 R. Yuan et al. developed a novel route for the synthesis of Cu2O–SiO2 nanostructured particles via a solution-phase method, which were adopted for the construction of a label-free amperometric immunosensor. The porous Cu2O–SiO2 NCs exhibited good redox electrochemical activity, large surface-to-volume ratio, film-forming ability and high stability. The fabricated immunosensor allowed the low-cost, sensitive, and quantitative detection of ferritin, and has potential application in clinical immune assays.111 J. Yang and co-workers developed the size-controlled synthesis of Cu2O NPs on RGO sheets (Cu2O/RGO) and their application as non-enzymatic glucose sensor materials.112 The smallest size composite-based electrode showed excellent electrochemical activities for the electrocatalytic oxidation of glucose in alkaline medium. The proposed biosensor was applied for the quantification of glucose with high sensitivity and durability, wide linear range, low detection limit of 0.1 μM, and ease of construction, and thus is promising for the future development of non-enzymatic glucose sensors. Cu2O mesoporous nanospheres (Cu2O MPNS) with different pore size distributions were fabricated and applied as electrocatalysts for the detection of glucose113 by Y. Li et al. In the electrochemical measurements for glucose detection, the large pore size-Cu2O modified electrode exhibited better electrocatalytic activity compared with the small pore size Cu2O-modified electrode. The results showed that the internal diffusion rate plays a vital role in the performance of the sensor. The obtained large pore size Cu2O biosensor exhibited a low detection limit, good sensitivity, excellent selectivity, good stability, reproducibility, and a wide detection range (0.003 to 7.8 mM) due to its improved internal diffusion and electron transfer ability. G. W. Qin et al. developed an enzyme-free amperometric electrochemical sensor, which was fabricated by casting Nafion-impregnated Cu2O NPs onto a glassy carbon electrode (GCE). Electrochemical analysis of the particulate Cu2O for detecting H2O2 and glucose was described, showing remarkable sensitivity in both cases (Table 3).114

Applying semiconductor photocatalysts to remove organic contaminants in air and water provides a useful approach for solving environmental problems. Medicinal applications. Cu2O NPs and their NCs have wide application in the field of medicinal chemistry. In this section we discuss their antibacterial, antifungal, antimicrobial and anticancer activity. Although they have not been widely investigated as an efficient drug delivery system, Cu2O NPs have good biological properties including effective antimicrobial action against a wide range of pathogens and also drug resistant bacteria. These properties have led to the development of various approaches with direct applications to the biomedical field, such as tailored surfaces with antimicrobial effect. K. Giannousi et al. synthesized Cu2O and Cu/Cu2O NPs and their fungistatic and fungicidal activity were studied for in vitro activity against the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which constitutes a unicellular eukaryotic model microorganism in molecular and cell biology.137 Cu2O NPs/zeolite was synthesized by B. D. Du et al., which showed antibacterial activity against E. coli. Cu2O NPs/zeolite can be favourably produced on large scale for water treatment and agricultural application as an antimicrobial agent.138 S. Zhao and co-workers synthesized Cu2O NPs as a potential agent for uveal melanoma therapy. The synthesized Cu2O-NPs were able to adsorb serum proteins in the cell culture medium, which were then internalized by uveal melanoma cells mainly through lipid raft-mediated endocytosis. Furthermore, Cu2O NPs selectively inhibited cancer cell growth and impaired the ability of uveal melanoma cell migration, invasion and the cytoskeleton assembly.139 Shiyun Ai et al. used lignin (paper making waste liquid) to obtain Cu2O NPs. The synthesised Cu2O NPs exhibited efficient antibacterial activity and low cytotoxicity against E. coli and S. Aureus.33 Brown algae (Bifurcaria bifurcata) extract was also used to study the synthesis of Cu2O and CuO NPs. The synthesized NPs were found to exhibit high antibacterial activity against two different strains of bacteria, E. aerogenes (Gram negative) and S. aureus (Gram positive).39 The synthesis of hexagonal and cubic shaped Cu2O NPs using Tridax procumbens leaf extract was described by K. Gopalakrishnan et al. Conclusions

Currently, the most significant research is aimed at emphasising the synthesis and applications of Cu2O NPs. The control and tailoring of their architecture provide a comprehensive strategy for the formation of Cu2O NPs and their NCs, which have multifarious applications that are environmentally friendly. The current review highlighted the green protocols and sustainable applications of Cu2O NPs and their composites, which is expected to arouse special interest in innovating similar routes and applications with catalysis at the forefront. It will surely encourage new researchers and provide a meritorious platform for designing a wide range of Cu2O template architectures, which can eventually lead to environment friendly applications. It is also expected to motivate researchers at the industrial level to achieve scalability with economic advantages.
