Abstract

Thermoelectrics is a research area that focuses on the development of high-performance materials for direct thermal and electrical energy conversion in power generation and solid-state cooling. Ever since Ioffe's inference in 1950s that heavily doped semiconductors make the best thermoelectrics, inorganic semiconductors and semimetals have remained the cornerstone of high-performance thermoelectric materials. In this article, we review the recent advances in inorganic material thermoelectrics with special emphases on: (i) the complementary local-global view of material and the roles of configurational entropy and Fermi surface complexity in material screening; (ii) the emerging schemes of resonant bonding, dynamic disorder, “gap” engineering, pudding-mold-shaped bands in material performance enhancement; (iii) a list of promising bulk materials, such as host–guest structures, half-Heusler compounds, silicides, Zintl phases, and incommensurate structures; and (iv) state-of-the-art materials synthesis and fabrication techniques. These advances will facilitate the development of next-generation bulk thermoelectric materials.

1. Introduction

Balancing economic growth, environmental protection, and energy consumption in a sustainable manner has been a long-standing worldwide challenge, which makes the development of renewable energy technology an urgent need. In the energy spectrum, heat is ubiquitous but low quality: most of the energy we create and use ends up in the form of “waste” heat; by contrast, electricity has long been the most versatile form of energy, indeed since the second industrial revolution. Thermoelectricity is the simplest technology applicable for direct heat and electricity conversion.1–3 Heat from automotive exhausts, industrial sectors, radioactive materials, geothermal, solar, and even the human body can be directly converted into electricity by a thermoelectric (TE) device. A TE device can also work as a heat pump for spot-size distributed slid-state refrigeration. A conventional TE device consists of multiple n-type and p-type legs that are connected thermally in parallel and electrically in series. TE devices are all solid state without moving parts, feasible for miniaturization, and free of noises or greenhouse emissions, making them ready to work in tandem with other energy conversion technologies, such as photovoltaics.4–6

On a fundamental level, a TE device is no more than a heat engine using electrons (holes) as its working medium. Other than the Carnot term, the energy conversion efficiency of a TE device is largely determined by the performance of the device material, namely, the dimensionless figure of merit ZT = α2σT/κ, where σ is the electrical conductivity, α is the Seebeck coefficient (aka the thermopower), κ is the total thermal conductivity, and T is the absolute temperature. In the simplest case, κ = κl + κe, where κl and κe are the lattice and carrier thermal conductivities, respectively. For convenience, the power factor PF = α2σ gauges the electrical performance metric of a TE material. An ideal TE material should simultaneously possess a large α, as present in a semiconductor or semimetal, a high σ, as in a metal, and a low κ, as in glass. Accordingly, an ideal TE material is an electron-crystal phonon-glass (ECPG) semiconductor or semimetal.7 It is a challenge to realize all of these features in a single material because of their inherently adverse interdependency {σ, α, κ}.

In the early stage of TE materials research dating back to the 19th century, various metals, ceramics, and finally semiconductors,8–11 were investigated in an empirical across-the-board manner. It was not until the 1950s that the quantum-based electronic band theory of solids laid down the foundation of modern TE materials research and helped establish a set of selection criteria for bulk material screening.8–12 Since then, bulk inorganic semiconductors and semimetals have remained the cornerstone of modern TE materials. Although the expression of the bulk materials selection criteria slightly varies in the literature, the basic ideas can be summarized as follows:

(i) A carrier concentration n in the order of 1018–1020 cm−3 makes the best thermoelectrics because such n values balance the σ and α toward a high PF.

(ii) Semiconductors with a band gap Δ ∼ 6–10kBTo make good thermoelectrics, where kB is the Boltzmann constant, and To is the operating temperature. Such a band gap size is a compromise between the band effective mass m* and the detrimental bipolar effect.

(iii) High crystal symmetry and small electronegativity differences among constituent elements tend to yield high PF values because of the high band degeneracy and reduced charge carrier scattering by optical phonons.

(iv) Heavy elements, large number of atoms per unit cell, diverse chemical bonding, and strong anharmonicity tend to yield low κl values.

In the past two decades, we have witnessed rapid advances in inorganic TE materials research owing to many newly discovered materials and also new paradigms based on the classical and quantum size effect13–23 as well as advances in delicate band structure engineering.15,24–30Fig. 1 presents a number of representative bulk inorganic TE materials from the 1960s to the present. It is widely accepted that bulk inorganic materials will remain the cornerstone of next-generation TE materials.

2. Toward next-generation thermoelectric materials

Understanding the structure–property correlation is at the core of many diverse areas of functional materials research. Concerning the “structure” of a TE material, the solid-state chemistry perspective provides an intuitive local view of a material's real-space structure (e.g., lone pairs, orbital overlap, site symmetry, and resonant bonding) and microstructures, while the solid-state physics perspective tends to view a material in a global momentum–space picture (i.e., electronic band structure, phonon dispersion relations, and Fermi surface topology).31,32 These two distinct yet complementary perspectives, embodied in the aforementioned materials selection criteria, give insights into the development of next-generation TE materials. Specifically, next-generation TE materials will be able to exceed current TE materials through overcoming challenges in three major aspects: material performance (subsection 2.1), material screening (subsection 2.2), and material sustainability (subsection 2.3).

2.1 Enhancing material performance

In view of the adversely interdependency {σ, α, κ}, high material performance arises from a concerted compromise among and within pairs of various macroscopic and microscopic transport parameters:33 (i) the electrical conductivity σ versus the Seebeck coefficient α in relation to the carrier concentration n; (ii) the carrier mobility μ versus the effective mass m* in relation to the band structure and scattering mechanisms; (iii) the carrier mobility μ versus the lattice thermal conductivity κl, in that charge carriers and heat-carrying phonons are often scattered by the same type of scatterer. Some schemes have been devised. For instance, aiming at (i), the band convergence scheme optimizes σ and α at a given n, provided that the inter-valley scattering is negligible.34,35 In addition, the optimal n tends to have a positive correlation with the single-valley effective mass .36–41 Aiming at (iii), specific mechanisms are implemented to selectively scatter charge carriers and phonons.42–46 In the following subsections, 2.1.1–2.1.3, we narrow our focus to three key transport parameters {α, μ, κl}. Detailed theoretical discussions on the {α, μ, κl} can be found in the literature.30,39
2.1.1 Enhancing the Seebeck coefficient. The Seebeck coefficient (α) is the transport parameter of utmost significance in that it is the only squared term in the definition of ZT. On a fundamental level, the α measures the average entropy carried by a charge carrier at the limit of thermal equilibrium. The sign of α reflects the type of primary charge carriers: negative (n-type) for electrons and positive (p-type) for holes. The magnitude of α is determined by the electronic band structure and the scattering mechanism. Accordingly, α can be enhanced via: (i) resonant levels that resonantly scatter and conduct charges;47–49 (ii) energy filtering at grain boundaries;50–52 (iii) band convergence to increase the density of states effective mass;34,35,53,54 (iv) the Rashba effect to create low-dimensional-like DOS spikes;30,55 and (v) a broadening of the band gap,35,56,57 or a combination of these.
2.1.2 Retaining the carrier mobility. At the core of an “electron crystal”, the carrier mobility (μ) is susceptible to degradation. Taking solid solutions, often the case in TE study, as an example, the electron–phonon coupling and alloying scattering are two main channels for degradation at elevated temperatures. Modulation doping is a promising approach toward retaining the μ while increasing the n. In this approach, charge carriers are spatially separated from donors to minimize the ionized impurity scattering.58–60 In polycrystalline materials with a multitude of grain boundaries, band alignment is crucial to retaining the μ;15 nonetheless, there is a compromise between band alignment and energy filtering to balance μ and α.
2.1.3 Reducing the lattice thermal conductivity. Heat is carried by phonons with a wide range of frequencies and wavelengths. Toward a “phonon glass”, the lattice thermal conductivity (κl) can be reduced via shortening the phonon mean path by point defects,61–63 nanoinclusions,64,65 grain boundaries,66,67 and by adopting an “all-scale hierarchical architecture” approach.65 Also, κl can be suppressed via shortening the phonon life time by anharmonicity. Notably, anharmonicity can be enhanced by the presence of resonant bonding, which is a characteristic of phase-change materials.68 The study of phase-change materials is a current hotbed of TE materials research, especially in terms of the crystal structure, lattice dynamics, and thermal conductance. First-principles calculations by Lee et al. revealed that resonant bonding accounts for the low κl in many IV–VI, V2–VI3, and V materials, which are parent compounds of many state-of-the-art TE materials.69

2.2 Material screening

Next-generation TE materials are likely higher multinary bulk materials, most of which will be identified through advanced material screening approaches. One promising approach is high-throughput computational material screening, a key component of the Materials Genome Initiative.70–72 Concerning the indicators for high-performance TE materials, Gibbs et al. suggested a “Femi Surface Complexity Factor”, which depends on the number of pockets and the anisotropy of the Fermi surface.73 Liu et al. pointed toward the configurational entropy of a material as a useful indicator.74 The TE studies of PbSnTeSe,75 (BiSbTe1.5Se1.5)1−xAgx,76 and AlxCoCrFeNi77 were in line with high-entropy thermoelectrics. Other than these indicators, a webpage by Gaultois et al.78 provides useful information on many candidate materials, using the Herfindahl–Hirschman index as a merit metric.79 Despite long strides being taken, it is still a challenge to calculate the full set of TE properties of a given material from first principles.80 In this regard, machine learning techniques hold promise.81,82

2.3 Material sustainability

The sustainability (e.g., natural abundance, cost, and toxicity) of raw materials and the sustainability of material synthesis/fabrication are the key factors in the course of developing next-generation TE materials. To this end, the use of elements, such as Te, Pb, In, Hf, and Bi, should be minimized for large-scale applications, whereas materials made of earth-abundant (cf.Fig. 2) and nontoxic elements are commendable. Meanwhile, the sustainability of material synthesis and fabrication requires more energy and time-efficient techniques, which will be discussed in section 4.

3.1 Caged compounds

Binary Skutterudites with the reduced chemical formula MX3 (M = Co, Rh, or Ir; X = P, As, or Sb) form a family of benchmark intermediate-temperature TE materials. Adopting a space group Im, the unit cell possesses eight corner-shared MX6 octahedra and two large voids.90 Hence, filling these voids with guest atoms is the natural approach to optimizing the TE performance via suppressing the κl. The category of filler atoms ranges from alkali, to alkali earth, halogen, and rare earth elements.91–95 The efficacy of multiple filling has also been confirmed.94–98 In particular, economical didymium (DD) and mischmetals were used as cost-effective multiple fillers in Skutterudites.99 A ZT = 1.45 at 850 K was attained in the p-type DD0.7Fe2.7Co1.3Sb11.8Sn0.2 due to suppressed thermal conductivity.100 Values of ZT ∼ 1.7 at 850 K and 1.9 at 835 K were reported in Ba0.08La0.05Yb0.04Co4Sb12101 and Sr0.09Ba0.11Yb0.05Co4Sb12,102 respectively.

Despite intensive study in the past two decades, opportunities remain to understand the interplay (i) between the filler atoms and the host matrix and (ii) among fillers in the context of the rattlers scheme,103 resonant scattering scheme,104 and the avoided crossing scheme.105 In this regard, element-specific local probes, such as extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) and resonant inelastic scattering, could shed light on the interplay between the filler and its neighbor Sb4 ring. It is unusual that the second neighbor spring constants were found to be stiffer than the nearest neighbor counterpart,106 reminiscent of the characteristic of resonant bonding (cf. section 2.1.3).69 The solubility limit of the filler atom (aka the filling fraction limit (FFL)) imposes a restriction on the parameter space for performance optimization. One notable progress in this regard is the solubility design.107–109 It was found that the FFL of a Skutterudite profoundly depends on the details of the ternary phase diagram, the off-stoichiometry of the starting materials, and also the annealing temperatures. Caution needs to be taken to determine the FFL as there are spurious indications possible. Beyond thermoelectrics, Hu et al. reported a spin-polarized multi-pocket Fermi surface and quasi-Dirac bands in La-filled CoSb3.110

Not only guest atom filling but also nanocompositing holds promise in the TE study of Skutterudites, as showcased in the early work by Li et al. in InSb-InxCeyCo4Sb12.111 The observed performance enhancement could have been partially due to the off-stoichiometry of the host matrix caused by the formation of InSb. Rogl et al. incorporated Ta0.8Zr0.2B nanoparticles in p-type (Mm,Sm)yCo4Sb12112 and reported a ZT of 1.5 at 823 K along with a simultaneous improvement of the mechanical properties (hardness, elastic moduli, and fracture toughness). More evidence has also been presented in support of the efficacy of the nanocompositing approach in the TE study of Skutterudites.113

Similar to Skutterudites, clathrates adopt a caged crystal structure that invites guest atoms.114 Clathrates can be structurally categorized into various types, with the type-I structure more common and most studied. A ZT of 1.35 at 900 K was attained in single-crystalline n-type Ba8Ga16Ge30.115 Single-crystalline Ce-added Ba8Au5.3Ge40.7 prepared by the Bridgman method showed a very low κl and a ZT of 0.9 at 680 K due to a rattling-enhanced Kondo-type interaction.116 Takabatake's group reported progress in type-VIII clathrates: a ZT = 1.35 at 540 K and ZT = 0.97 at 500 K were attained in single-crystalline n-type Ba8Ga16−xCuxSn30117 and p-type Ba8.0Ga15.9ZnySn30.1,118 respectively. As for the polycrystalline clathrate materials, n-type Ba8Ga16−xGe30+x reached a ZT of 0.86 at 950 K at n ∼ 2 × 1020 cm−3.119 The Zintl-rule-derived framework substitution has been successfully extended to transition metals, yielding a ZT of 1.2 at 1000 K in polycrystalline Ba8Ni0.31Zn0.52Ga13.06Ge32.2.120 In applications, a wide temperature range of operation and high average ZT values matter more than the peak value of ZT. In this regard, the work by Falmbigl et al. in the n-type polycrystalline type-I clathrate Ba8NixZnyGe46−x−y−zSnz is noteworthy.121

3.2 Open framework structures

Mixed electronic and ionic conductors (MEICs) adopt open framework structures. The concept of a “Phonon-Liquid Electron-Crystal” (PLEC) was proposed to account for the exceptionally low κl, high PF, and promising ZT values in binary Cu and Ag chalcogenide superionic conductors122–127 and ternary compounds, such as Cu5FeS4,128 Cu7PSe6,129 Ag8SnSe6,130 Ag8SiSe6,131 and Ag9AlSe6.132 Zhao et al. reported that Cu2−ySe0.5S0.5 exhibited a complex hierarchical microstructure, good electrical properties, and an ultralow κl, yielding a ZT ∼ 2.3 at 1000 K.133 In these MEICs, the mobile nature of Ag and Cu on their sublattice accounted for the observed low κl, but the same led to a compositional gradient under charge current. Olvera et al. demonstrated improved chemical stability and a ZTave ∼ 1.5 over a broad temperature range in Cu2Se–CuInSe2 nanocomposites; a ZT ∼ 2.6 at 850 K was attained with 1 mol% In, due to the localization of mobile Cu ions by In on the Cu sublattice.134

In AgCrSe2, Ag ions are mobile between the CrSe6 octahedral slabs. The results of inelastic neutron scattering and theoretical calculations corroborated that the exceptionally low κl of AgCrSe2 was due to the diminishing transverse acoustic (TA) modes and disrupted longitudinal acoustic (LA) modes, both of which were attributed to the ultra-fast dynamic disorder of the Ag sublattice.135 The Ag ions underwent complex order–disorder (superionic) transition at the critical temperature Tc ∼ 450 K. The TA modes gradually softened and merged into diffusive scattering in the vicinity of Tc (Fig. 3). This mechanism can be employed in tandem with other κl reduction mechanisms (cf. section 2.1.3). Interestingly, the dynamic disorder of Cu ions in Cu2Se was not fast enough to disrupt the heat-carrying phonons,136 so the low κl of Cu2Se was mainly due to the static disorder of Cu ions.133,137

β-Zn4Sb3 is one of the best intermediate-temperature TE materials.138–140 It is metastable below ∼425 K but assumes its stability above 565 K.141 The crystal structure of Zn4Sb3 has three interstitial Zn sites,142 partly filled by highly mobile Zn ions.143,144 In light of its good electrical properties, the TE study of β-Zn4Sb3 has been mainly focused on reducing the κl. To this end, it was found that a trace amount of metal dopant at the Zn site remarkably reduces the κl,145–148 and dual doping at the Zn site was also tried with success.148,149 Toward a higher PF, the efficacy of band structure engineering was confirmed.150,151 A remarkably simultaneously increased σ and α led to a state-of-the-art ZT ∼ 1.4 at 700 K through introducing In interstitially.152

Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) form a class of van der Waals (vdW) materials. Here, the nomenclature “gap” refers not only to the band gap in the momentum-based space but also to the van der Waals gap in the real-space structure. Since substitutional doping tunes the band gap and intercalation tunes the van der Waals gap, the combined substitution–intercalation approach may be called “gap engineering”. In 2H-MQ2 (M = Mo, W; Q = S, Se, Te), the electronic structure near the band edge is sensitive to vdW interactions.153 Hippalgaonkar et al.154 and Wu et al.155 experimentally confirmed the high PF in single- and few-layer MoS2, respectively. Combined first-principles and Boltzmann transport theory calculations showed that the ZT was 0.11 at 500 K in single-layer MoS2,156 3.5 at room temperature in MoS2 armchair nanoribbons,157 and 2.4 in bilayer MoSe2.158 Lee et al. predicted a promising TE performance the mixed-layer compounds MS2/MTe2 (M = Mo, W).159

Holgate et al. demonstrated that Li intercalation in 1T-ZrSe2 single-crystals could lead to a ZT enhancement from 0.08 to 0.26 at room temperature.160 The possibility of doping merits the further study of intercalated TMDs. In light of the limited structural tunability of 1T-TiS2 and other 1T-TMDs, Guilmeau et al.161 devised a “block layers” approach: intercalating a whole MS layer (M = Pb, Sn, Bi) into the van der Waals gap of TiS2, thereby forming a misfit layered superlattice (MS)1+x(TiS2)2. For transport, the TiS2 layer acts as an electron path, while the intercalated MS layer acts as a phonon-block.162–164 As shown in the left panel of Fig. 4, the degree of disorder increased per the order: (PbS)1.18(TiS2)2, (SnS)1.2(TiS2)2, and (BiS)1.2(TiS2)2, as confirmed by the increasingly diffused electron diffraction pattern. All these misfit layer compounds showed fairly good TE properties and ZT values along the in-plane direction (right panel of Fig. 4). Compared to pristine TiS2, only (SnS)1.2(TiS2)2 showed a higher ZT value, while the other two compositions were subject to a degraded PF. Lowering the n may help further optimize the TE performance.

3.3 Zintl phases

Zintl phases are electronically located between the intermetallics and insulators, making them natural candidates for thermoelectrics. In a Zintl phase, the covalently bonded network of a complex anion or metalloids acts as an “electron crystal”, while the cations act as a “phonon glass”.165 Yb14MnSb11 is one of the most studied Zintl phases.166–169 Recently, the AB2X2 Zintl phase (A = Ca, Sr, Ba, Yb, Eu; B = Zn, Mn, Cd; X = Sb, Bi) has attracted intense interest due to its large DOS at the Fermi level EF,170 covalently bonded network of M–Sb, two-dimensional (M2Sb2)2− slabs sandwiched by A2+ cations (cf.Fig. 5), and the feasibility of performing chemical substitutions and structural modifications. After Gascoin et al., p-type Ca1−xYbxZn2Sb2 exhibited a promising ZT ∼ 0.56 at 773 K.171ZT above unity has been reported in YbCd1.6Zn0.4Sb2172 and EuZn1.8Cd0.2Sb2.173 Cooley et al. reported high α and low κ values in Yb2−xEuxCdSb2.174 Notably, the ZT values of Bi-based Zintl phases (Eu0.5Yb0.5)1−x CaxMg2Bi2, Ca0.5Yb0.5Mg2Bi2, Eu0.5Ca0.5Mg2Bi2, and Eu0.5Yb0.5Mg2Bi2 were found to be above unity.175,176 (Na, Zn) co-doped Mg3Sb2 yielded a peak ZT value ∼0.8 at 773 K owing to the Na dopants increasing the carrier concentration, while Zn dopants weakened the polar covalent bonding and ionized impurity scattering to preserve the carrier mobility.177 Zhang et al. demonstrated how InSb nanoinclusions simultaneously improved the electrical and thermal transport properties of p-type YbZn2Sb4.178

Progress has been made in n-type Zintl materials. A ZT ∼ 1.65 was reported at 725 K in an n-type Mg3Sb2-based Zintl phase.179 Mg3Sb2 is a special case of the aforementioned 1-2-2 Zintl phase, with Mg occupying two distinct cationic sites. Experimentally, excess Mg is required to make Mg3Sb2 n-type, pointing toward the presence and active role of interstitial Mg.180 This is reminiscent of the role of intrinsic point defects (vacancies and antisites) in V2VI3 compounds in which extrinsic point defects work in tandem with intrinsic point defects to optimize the carrier concentration.181 Concerning the low-cost Zintl phases, Zhang et al.182 reported a ZT ∼ 1.23 at 725 K in a Te-free n-type Mg3.07Sb1.5Bi0.48Se0.02, comparable with the ZT values of Mg3Sb1.48Bi0.48Te0.04.179 Another promising Zintl compound Zr3Ni3Sb4 is noteworthy because of its balanced n- and p-type performance.183 Specifically, Te doping on the Sb site as a donor significantly suppressed the bipolar effect in the nanostructured Zintl Zr3Ni3Sb4 through increasing the majority-carrier concentration and enlarging the band gap. As a result, a decent peak ZT of 0.6 was obtained in Zr3Ni3Sb3.95Te0.05 at 773 K. Ortiz et al. recently conducted a high-throughput search of 145 Zintl pnictides (arsenides, antimonides, and bismuthides) and confirmed the promise of n-type Zintl phases.184

3.4 Half-Heusler alloys

Half-Heuser (HH) compounds adopt a space group F3m and have a general chemical formula of XYZ, where X is a transition metal, a noble metal, or a rare earth element, Y is a transition metal or a noble metal, and Z is a main group element. As shown in the left panel of Fig. 6, the crystal structure of HH phase can be viewed as 3 interpenetrated FCC lattices with X, Y, and Z atoms occupying the Wyckoff positions 4b (1/2, 1/2, 1/2), 4c (1/4, 1/4, 1/4), and 4a (0, 0, 0). The 18-valence electron HH compounds are semiconductors with promising TE properties, a wide selection of elements, outstanding thermal stability, and robust mechanical properties.185 In particular, XNiSn and XCoSb (X = Ti, Zr, or Hf) form a family of benchmark mid- and high-temperature TE materials.186–201 The basic crystal chemistry and physical properties of HH compounds may be understood in the context of Zintl chemistry.202 We summarize the ZT values recently reported for a number of HH compounds in the right panel of Fig. 6.

Hf1−xZrxNiSn1−ySby alloys with good crystallinity have been successfully fabricated using a levitation melting-spark plasma sintering (SPS) procedure.201 Among promising HH alloys are also FeMSb-based (M = V, Nb) materials.195,196 Doping Ti on the V/Nb sites optimized the n in p-type FeV0.6Nb0.4Sb, yielding a ZT of 0.8 at 900 K.199 Chen et al. conducted a systematic study of the role of V, Nb, and Ta as prospective resonant dopants in Hf0.6Zr0.4NiSn0.995Sb0.005. The V doping was found to increase the α between 300 K and 1000 K, consistent with the resonant levels scheme. By contrast, Nb and Ta acted as normal n-type dopants. The presence of V resonant states and the reduced κl led to a ZT of 1.3 at 850 K in n-type (Hf0.6Zr0.4)0.99V0.01NiSn0.995Sb0.005.203 MCoSb-based (M = Hf, Zr) HH compounds are promising p-type materials, although the parent compound is a native n-type. He et al. investigated the TE properties of heavily Ni-substituted n-type MCoSb. The anomalous changes in n and κl at higher Ni doping ratio indicated the presence of native atomic disorder. Substituting Zr by Hf suppressed the κl and yielded a peak ZT exceeding 1 in Zr0.5Hf0.5Co0.9Ni0.1Sb at 1073 K.204 Makongo et al. achieved a simultaneous enhancement of α and σ through in situ-formed well-dispersed full-Heusler nanoinclusions.205 Notably, Ti0.5Zr0.25Hf0.25NiSn with a densification aid added in the sintering process206 showed a ZT of 1.5 at 825 K compared to a ZT of 1.5 at 700 K in Ti0.5Zr0.25Hf0.25NiSn0.998Sb0.002.207

Though the presence of Hf often simultaneously enhances the PF and suppresses the κl, the cost of Hf is an issue for large-scale use (cf. section 2.3). It is thus desired to reduce the amount of Hf used yet to retain the material's TE performance. To this end, He et al. reported that Hf0.19Zr0.76Ti0.05CoSb0.8Sn0.2 exhibited a ZT ∼ 1.0 at 973 K,208 comparable to a ZT ∼ 1.0 at 1073 K in Hf0.8Ti0.2CoSb0.8Sn0.2.209 A Hf-free, NbFeSb-based nanostructured HH compound synthesized by an arc melting-ball milling-hot pressing procedure yielded a ZT ∼ 1 at 973 K.192 In particular, Hf-doping helped boost the ZT of NbFeSb HH alloys up to ∼1.5 at 1200 K.41

Important progress has been made in nominal 19-electron HH compounds with intrinsic vacancies. NbCoSb with a nominal 19 valence electrons has been seldom studied and was thought to be metallic. Xia et al. recently demonstrated that NbCoSb is actually a composite of 18-electron Nb0.8+δCoSb (0 ≤ δ < 0.05) and some impurity phases. By removing the impurity phases and optimizing the n via controlling the Nb vacancies, a ZT ∼ 0.9 was attained at 1123 K in Nb0.83CoSb.210 MgAgSb-based HH materials exhibited ZT values exceeding unity near 500 K, comparable to those of state-of-the-art Bi2Te3-based TE materials.211 Through a combined study of first-principles calculations and X-ray photoelectric spectroscopy, Ni interstitials were demonstrated to play a key role in suppressing the κl of TiNiSn-based HH compounds.212 Ni interstitials were introduced by adding excess Ni in the starting materials.

3.5 Oxides

The history of oxides thermoelectrics is fairly short, partially because oxides are the outliers of the aforementioned materials selection criteria. Nonetheless, the chemical and thermal stability, chemical versatility, environmental friendliness, and low cost of the raw materials are some key merits of oxide TE materials. Oxides can tolerate a large temperature difference across the hot end and cold end, rendering a high Carnot efficiency that compensates for their relatively low ZT.213 Beyond ZT, the TE study of complex structured oxides is in line with the ECPG paradigm.7 A complex (“hybrid”) crystal structure can be regarded as being formed by various building modules, each functioning as a PG or an EC that can be tuned separately.214

Since the groundbreaking work in NaCo2O4, p-type Ca3Co4O9 and n-type SrTiO3 and CaMnO3 have been investigated.215 Dy-Doped CaMnO3 ceramics reached a ZT of 0.15 at 973 K, about four times higher than that of pristine CaMnO3.216 A ZT > 0.3 was attained at 1060 K in Nb-doped CaMnO3 synthesized by a “chimie douce” method.217 A ZT of 0.27 at 1073 K was reported in La-doped SrTiO3.218 Enhanced phonon scattering promoted by Al-induced grain refinement and the presence of ZnAl2O4 nanoprecipitates led to a low κl and a ZT of 0.44 at 1000 K in n-type ZnO.219 It should be noted that the electrical transport properties of oxides are closely dependent on the oxygen partial pressure,220 which provides an extra channel to tune the material performance.

Other than these ZT enhancements, the study of oxide TE materials is inspirational in physics. For example, a pudding-mold-shaped band221 that renders the coexistence of large σ and α in NaCoO2 was also discovered in SnSe.222 The pudding-mold-shaped band could be used as a useful indicator for material screening. In addition, 3d and 4f element-containing oxides are often featured by strong electronic correlation. As the present state-of-the-art TE materials are uncorrelated, it is imperative to know whether the electronic correlation effects help decouple the otherwise adversely interdependent {σ, α, κ}. Consequently, Tomczak recently reviewed the electronic correlation effects and their impacts on the thermoelectric properties of narrow gap semiconductors.223 While the explanation of {σ, α} of strongly correlated materials is often beyond the standard single-particle model, correlation-induced phenomena, such as heavy Fermion, a large thermopower at low temperatures, and the Kondo effect, are relevant to thermoelectricity, especially at low temperatures (cf. subsection 3.11).

3.6 Tetrahedrites

Tetrahedrites crystalized in the Cu12Sb4S13-type structure, a derivative of sphalerite structure, with a generic formula Cu10M2Sb4S13 (M = Cu, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Zn), encompass a wide array of sulfosalt minerals.224 Depending on the number of valence electrons and the stoichiometry, the conduction behavior of tetrahedrites varies from semiconducting to metallic.225 Containing earth-abundant environmentally friendly elements, and adopting a cubic lattice symmetry I3m and 58 atoms per unit cell, tetrahedrites are in line with the aforementioned bulk materials selection criteria.

Pristine Cu-based tetrahedrite tends to be an electron-deficient semiconductor. The Cu deficiency and Cu excess lead to a mixed valence state of Cu. In the crystal structure of tetrahedrites (Fig. 7), half of the Cu atoms occupy a three-fold coordinated distorted tetrahedral Wyckoff position 12d, while the other half occupy a three-fold coordinated Wyckoff position 12e.226 Accordingly, the crystal structure of Cu12Sb4S13-based tetrahedrites may be viewed as a host–guest structure in light of the Sb[CuS3]Sb cages. In each cage, a trigonal plane is made of one Cu (12e) and three S ions, with two Sb ions, one on each side of the plane. The lone-pair electrons of Sb render a strong out-of-plane anharmonic oscillation (rattling) of the Cu ion, which effectively scatter heat-carrying phonons. Meanwhile, the electrical conduction is ensured due to the weakened Cu–Sb bonding energy.227 To gain insights into the intriguing incoherent and coherent atomic dynamics underlying the intrinsically low thermal conductivity of tetrahedrites, Li et al. studied Cu12Sb4S13-based tetrahedrites by means of first-principle molecular dynamics simulations and inelastic neutron scattering measurements.228 They attributed the anomalous phonon-softening-upon-cooling phenomenon to the decreased anharmonic oscillation and lock-in of a Cu ion in the Sb[CuS3]Sb cage; in addition, the acoustic modes were found to be confined in a small dynamic scattering space, which led to minimum phonon mean free path.

There are some reports on the TE study of doped or dual-doped tetrahedrites, e.g., ZT ∼ 0.7 at 665 K in Cu10.5Ni1.5Sb4S13,229 ∼1.13 at 575 K in Cu11MnSb4S13,230 ∼0.98 at 673 K in Cu11.5Co0.5Sb4S13,231 ∼0.8 at 665 K in Cu12Sb3.2Te0.8S13,232 ∼0.8 at 673 K in Cu11.18Co0.82Sb3.59Te0.41S13.233 To synthesize Cu12Sb4S13 as a natural mineral, Sun et al. used mechanical alloying and spark plasma sintering; a ZT = 0.65 was attained at 723 K.234 Lu et al. showed that (Ni, Zn) dual-doped Cu12Sb4S13 exhibited a κl approaching the theoretical minimum values, which helped boost the ZT above 1.0 at 723 K.235

3.7 GeTe and SnTe

GeTe and SnTe have been extensively studied as intermediate-temperature TE materials.236,237 Gelbstein et al. reported that Ge0.87Pb0.13Te doped by 3 mol% Bi2Te3 exhibited a ZT ∼ 2 near 773 K via metallurgically controlled phase separations.238 Li et al. pointed out that in p-type group IV monotellurides increasing the cation size and decreasing the anion through isoelectron substitution was effective for reducing the κl. Due to the high solubility of isoelectron dopants, a high ZT ∼ 2.2 at 800 K was attained in GeTe–PbSe alloys (cf.Fig. 8).239 By slightly distorting the crystal lattice symmetry from cubic to rhombohedral through (Pb, Bi) co-doping, a record ZT ∼ 2.4 was attained at 600 K owing to a delicate balance between a convergence of the split low-symmetry bands and the alloying-induced low lattice thermal conductivity.240 Heterostructured composites of a host matrix (GeTe)17Sb2Te3 (GST) and CoGe2 precipitates synthesized by a solid-state reaction-annealing -quenching recipe exhibited unique “herringbone-like” microstructures and a ZT ∼ 1.9 at 773 K.241 Even when the GST host matrix underwent complex phase transitions, the high ZT values were reproducible. For SnTe-based materials, band engineering was utilized to improve the electrical properties and reduce the κl.242–244 Li et al. implemented band convergence and phonon scattering via interstitials, and attained a ZT ∼ 1.6 at 1173 K in the Sn0.89Mn0.14Te-(Cu2Te)0.05 composite.245 Bhat et al. reported a ZT ∼ 1.5 at 840 K in (Mg, In) dual-doped SnTe: in dopants induced resonant levels to increase the α at low temperatures while Mg dopants increased the band gap and facilitated the valence sub-bands convergence, which suppressed the detrimental bipolar effect and enhanced the α at elevated temperatures.246

3.8 AgSbTe2

AgSbTe2 is a promising lower-mid-temperature TE material. It adopts a rock salt crystal structure, with Ag and Te occupying one sublattice while Te occupies the other sublattice. It exhibits a glass-like κl due to the spontaneously formed nanostructures.247 Roychowdhury et al. reported a ZT ∼ 1.9 at 585 K in AgSb0.96Zn0.04Te2 due to simultaneously optimizing σ and κl.248 It was found that In doping simultaneously increased the effective mass and enhanced the phonon scattering, yielding a ZT ∼ 1.35 at 650 K in AgSbTe2.249 It would be interesting to study whether high-entropy thermoelectrics could be further developed in AgSbTe2.

3.9 In4Se3−δ

In4Se3−δ is unique among the state-of-the-art TE materials in that (i) it adopts a highly anisotropic crystal structure in which the In/Se chains weave into 2-dimensional sheets, and (ii) its electronic structure is susceptible to a charge density wave (CDW) instability. The feature (i) makes In4Se3−δ an outlier of the aforementioned material selection criteria (cf. Introduction), while feature (ii) attests the TE promise of sub-critical materials.2 It took a synergy of substitutional and interstitial dopants, Se vacancies, and nanostructures to attain a ZT ∼ 1.4 at 733 K in polycrystalline In4Se3−δ.250

3.10 Silicides

Higher manganese silicides (HMSs) form a family of green and robust TE materials promising in mid to high-temperature applications.251 HMSs are fundamentally interesting as they adopt an incommensurate Nowotny chimney-ladder structure,252 located between regular crystals and quasicrystals. HMSs generally consist of two tetragonal sublattices: the Mn sublattice exhibits less displacive modulation and the 3d states underlie the electrical conduction. By contrast, the Si sublattice tends to have more displacive modulation, which impedes the phonon propagation. The nomenclature “incommensurate” refers to the irrational value of the c-axis ratio of the two sublattices, whereby it is accepted that the c-axis constant of the Mn sublattice is fixed (cMn = 4.37 Å) while the c-axis constant of the Si sublattice varies with the detailed composition. The complex crystal structure of HMSs and the propensity of MnSi formation make the full determination of the atomic position and occupancy very difficult, which in turn makes the canonical structure (microstructure)–property correlation hard to establish. Nonetheless, these are challenges as well as opportunities. There is a spacious room for doping253 and nanostructuring254 HMSs. In general, semiconductors with an incommensurate crystal structure deserve more attention in future TE studies.

In addition to HMSs, magnesium silicides, e.g., Mg2Si1−xSnx solid solutions,25 have attracted interest due to their high TE performance, naturally abundant elements, and good mechanical and chemical strength. In particular, a band convergence was attained in Mg2Si1−xSnx when x = 0.65–0.70, yielding a high PF and a ZT of 1.3 near 700 K. Doping Mg2Si1−xSnx solid solutions with Sb255 or Bi256 further improved the TE performance. Meanwhile, the nanocompositing approach via incorporating extraneous SiC257 and TiO2 nanoparticles258 turned out to be effective. As for p-type Mg2Si1−xSnx materials, the hole concentration is governed by the dopant concentration and the Si:Sn ratio. Attaining the optimal hole concentration is thus a challenge. To date, the maximum ZT values of p-type Mg2Si1−xSnx materials are in the range of 0.3–0.5 via doping with Li,259 Ag,260 and Ga.261

3.11 Cryogenic temperature materials

Generally speaking, refrigeration is less cost-sensitive than power generation. Thermoelectrics especially suits the distributed spot-size solid-state refrigeration of microelectronics such as infrared sensors and laser diodes. In contrast to the strides made in high-temperature TE materials toward power generation, the progress in cryogenic temperature TE materials is limited. Small band gap multi-band semiconductors and semimetals are promising in this regard. In addition to BiSb solid solutions,262 CsBi4Te6,263 ZrTe5,264 Ca-doped Bi2Se3,265 and a phase-change material GeSb2Te5266 have been studied. The study of cryogenic temperature TE materials is a research area of rich physics: a quasi-ballistic phonon drag enhanced α in FeSb2;267 large PF in quasi-1-dimensional Ta4SiTe4;268 magnon drag enhanced α in ferromagnetic metals,269 and resonant levels49 are noted. The results of low-temperature measurements help pinpoint the materials’ ground state, which governs their excited state properties, including the ZT.

4. Synthesis and fabrication techniques

Time and energy efficient materials synthesis and fabrication techniques are crucial for developing next-generation TE materials. The discovery of new materials in a higher multinary phase space requires an extensive program of exploratory syntheses, where traditional synthesis techniques, such as growth-from-the-melt and solid-state reactions, fall short. Furthermore, the same composition synthesized by different techniques may have subtly different microstructures, which provides another channel for materials performance optimization.

4.1 Spark plasma sintering (SPS) technique

The spark plasma sintering (SPS) technique is one of the most effective techniques to sinter nanopowders in a short period of time and at a relatively low temperature. Owning many advantages over other traditional sintering processes, SPS utilizes a uniaxial force and pulsed direct electric current under a controlled atmosphere.270 SPS is not only a physical densification tool in general but also an electrochemical reaction tool on occasions. SPS is especially suitable for those cases in which the initial micromorphology of powders needs to be preserved through sintering. While the effectiveness of SPS is well established, the sintering mechanism at the microscopic scale is not completely known.271

4.2 In situ-formed nanocomposites

Nanocomposites are a new paradigm of TE materials research based on the classical and quantum size effect. In particular, in situ-formed nanoprecipitates play a key role in energy filtering and the scattering of mid to long wavelength phonons.272–275 Coherent or semi-coherent interfaces between the host matrix and the nanoprecipitates are generally favored as they tend to reduce the κl while retaining the μ.15,16 Kanatzidis’ group developed a series of in situ-formed PbTe-based nanocomposites through delicate control of the phase separation.276–278 There has been a wide variety of in situ-formed nanocomposites studied for their TE promise, such as (Ti,Zr,Hf)(Co,Ni)Sb-InSb279 and La0.8Ti0.1Ga0.1Fe3CoSb12-Fe3Si.280

4.3 Self-propagating high-temperature synthesis

Self-propagating high-temperature synthesis (SHS) is a technique used to synthesize refractory inorganic materials through self-heating and self-propagation. It has been successfully applied to a number of TE materials, e.g., Cu2Se,67 Mg2Si1−xSbx,281 BiCuSeO,282 Cu3SbSe3,283 and CoSb2.85Te0.15.284 In a SHS process, once the exothermal reaction reaches a critical temperature, the rapid propagation of the reaction wave and the subsequent exothermic reaction will sustain the entire combustion process. The equipment and operation of SHS are fairly simple and the reaction parameters can be readily controlled. Thus, the energy consumption is minimum and the procedure is feasible to scale up in commercial applications.285 However, the as-synthesized product needs to be densified, e.g., by on-site dynamical compaction, for subsequent characterization and device fabrication.

4.4 Mechanochemical synthesis technique

In mechanochemical synthesis, the stress between the grinding medium and powders induces a large number of lattice defects in the powders, which facilitate the chemical reaction without the need for external heating. The efficacy of mechanochemical synthesis has been proved in synthesizing nanopowders286 and refractory materials.287 Specifically, mechanochemical synthesis has been used to synthesize TE materials, such as Ca-doped La3Te4,288 β-FeSi2,289 and Mg3Sb2.290 Recently, the mechanochemical synthesis of MEIC TE materials Cu2X (X = S, Se) was reported by Yang et al.291 The ZT value of Cu2S reached ∼1.1 at 873 K, about 2 times as high as the typical ZT value of a grown-from-the-melt sample, while the ZT value of Cu2Se samples reached above 1.2 at 873 K, close to the state-of-the-art value.

4.5 Microwave-assisted synthesis technique

Microwave-assisted synthesis (MAS) is a chemical–physical method that utilizes microwave heating and a medium solvent to facilitate the reaction. The mechanism of MAS is that the polarity orientation of the medium is changed under a high-frequency electromagnetic field, causing molecular motion and internal friction. In the past few years, various TE materials have been synthesized by MAS, e.g., Bi2Te3,292,293 Mg2Si,294 Pb1−xYbxTe,295 and HH compounds.296,297 Nanostructured Sb2Te3 with rich micro-morphologies was fabricated by MAS, and the materials exhibited a higher α and a higher ZT than that of their bulk counterparts.298,299 Ultrathin Bi2Se3 nanosheets have been successfully synthesized by MAS under 1 kW microwave power in ethylene glycol for just 1 min,300 with a PF ∼ 157 μW m−1 K−2 at 523 K attributed to the surface states. In a recent report, Sb-doped TiNiSbxSn1−x bulk samples were fabricated by cold-pressing, microwave synthesis, and microwave sintering in evacuated quartz tubes.301

4.6 Three-dimensional printing technique

Three-dimensional (3D) printing or additive manufacturing is an advanced fabrication technique.302 3D printing can be realized by digital light processing, selective laser sintering, stereolithography apparatus, or fused deposition modeling.302–304 Li-ion microbattery,305 cell-laden tissue constructs,306 and TE materials307 have been fabricated by 3D printing. TE materials fabricated by 3D printing have suitable sizes and shapes better matched to the geometry of heat sources. 3D printing almost eliminates the material loss during the traditional dicing process and shortens the production time. One of the roadblocks in the 3D printing of TE materials is the organic binders used in the ink, which usually degrade the electrical properties of the as-printed material. Recently, Kim et al.308 reported an extrusion-based 3D printing of Bi2Te3-based TE materials with all-inorganic colloid inks as inorganic binders, yielding a ZT ∼ 0.9 at 398 K for a p-type material and ∼0.6 at 443 K for an n-type material (cf.Fig. 9).

5. Concluding remarks

Owing to tremendous experimental efforts in materials synthesis and characterization, and also the fast development of theory and computational capability, long strides have been taken in the field of inorganic TE materials research in the past two decades. High ZT values >2 have been reported constantly from innovatively tuned existing materials and also in novel materials. The field of inorganic TE materials research is at a crossroads, ready for the next transition. One such transition will be in the subfield of material selection and screening. First, with more outliers discovered, the traditional bulk materials selection criteria need to be refined. Furthermore, the higher multinary nature of next-generation TE materials entails high-throughput material screening and machine learning. Hence, inorganic TE materials research will transition from material driven to data driven.
