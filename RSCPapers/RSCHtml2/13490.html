Abstract

The remediation of environmental issues and solving energy crises are long-term challenges given the increasing world population and limited, unsustainable resources. Among the various possibilities, solar energy represents a clean, sustainable, and renewable technique for providing humans with power. Inspired by photoactive reactions in nature, such as photocatalysis in self-sustaining ecosystems, artificial photocatalysts based on metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are broadly designed for hydrogen evolution from water, value-added chemicals from carbon dioxide, nitrogen fixation, energy-saving organic transformations, and photodegradation/photocatalytic removal of pollutants in the air (e.g., aldehydes and aromatics) and pollutants in waste water (e.g., dyes, antibiotics, pesticides, herbicides, phenols, formaldehyde, and hexavalent chromium). Photovoltaic cells based on MOFs including perovskite solar cells (PSCs), organic solar cells (OSCs), dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), MOF-sensitized solar cells (MSSCs), and quantum dot-sensitized solar cells (QDSSCs) have been rapidly developing over the past few years. This review article covers recent progress in the aforementioned topics and discusses future directions.

1. Introduction

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) consist of organic linkers and metal salts or secondary building units (SBUs) that form open frameworks and predictable structures, especially when using SBUs and multidentate organic linkers.1,2 MOF-based materials can be used in many applications such as H2/CH4/C2H2 storage,3â€“5 CO2 capture/conversion,6â€“10 catalysis,11,12 photonic applications,13â€“15 and DNA delivery.16 The need to understand the mechanisms of crystallization of MOFs has stimulated deep investigations of transition states by in situ techniques17â€“22 and how parameters affect crystal growth in addition to the resultant behaviors. The impact of time on a dynamic process is important;23 for instance, MOF glasses were prepared by a melt-quenched method at low temperatures, and fracture toughness and short-range disorder were reported.24â€“26 The evolution of transition states of MOF glasses over time can be directly analyzed by X-ray diffraction, ultrahigh-field NMR, Raman spectroscopy, optical microscopy, etc.

As a class of multifunctional porous materials, MOF-based solar-driven water harvesting,27â€“30 solar-driven steam generation/desalination/water purification,31â€“36 photocatalysis,37â€“39 photovoltaics,40â€“42 and solar thermal energy storage43,44 have been widely studied because solar energy represents a sustainable, and green technology with good prospects. Among the abovementioned photoactive applications, photocatalysis and photovoltaics are particularly attractive.40,45â€“50 Consequently, intensive efforts have been made to prepare efficient, stable, eco-friendly, scalable, and cost-effective materials to fulfill the requirements, although challenging. One paramount factor involved in photoelectroactive MOFs for photocatalysis and photovoltaics is how to design and prepare semiconductive materials with appropriate band gaps suitable for solar-driven activities (Scheme 1). MOF-based photocatalytic reactions span from H2 evolution,51â€“55 CO2 reduction,56â€“60 N2 fixation61â€“63 to photodegradation of gaseous pollutants,64â€“68 photodegradation of aqueous pollutants,69â€“74 and various types of organic transformations,75â€“78etc. MOF-based photovoltaics have been studied with the integration of perovskites,79â€“82 dyes,83,84 organic polymers,85,86 and quantum dots.87,88

2. Strategies for designing photoelectroactive MOFs

Photocatalytic reactions occur upon light irradiation and provide a way to obtain chemical energy from solar energy. Photocatalysts that can maximally utilize the spectral wavelength range of sunlight, especially visible light, are highly needed. Therefore, semiconductive materials with the ability to generate high-density photogenerated charge carriers (i.e., photoelectrons and holes), good separation, slow recombination rate of electronâ€“hole pairs, quick migration speed, and suitable band gaps with tailorability to conduct efficient photocatalytic reactions for practical uses are vitally important.49,89â€“91 MOFs are promising due to their merits of large surface area, high porosity, and tunable light absorption ability with precise structural identification. These features of MOFs are also beneficial for MOF-based solar cells since photovoltaic cells convert solar irradiation into electrical energy, with the requirements of decent light harvesting ability, power conversion efficiency, and stability.40,92

Routine strategies for the design of MOF-based photoelectroactive materials include the following: (1) pristine MOFs; (2) hybrid MOFs or MOF composites; and (3) MOF derivatives. In general, in situ and ex situ methods can be utilized to construct four types of MOF materials for photocatalysis and photovoltaics: (I) pristine MOFs without loading guest species; (II) hybrid MOFs with guest species coated on MOF surfaces; (III) hybrid MOFs with guest species partially embedded in MOFs; and (IV) hybrid MOFs with guest species fully embedded or encapsulated in MOFs (Scheme 2).

To further enhance photoelectroactive performances, the integration with guest species to form hybrid MOFs was investigated, which helps to separate and suppress the recombination rates of photoelectrons and holes.57,109â€“112 MOF derivatives are obtained through the heat treatment of pristine MOFs or hybrid MOFs, which usually sacrifices organic linkers upon calcination but retains porosity and enhances electrical conductivity.

3.1 Photocatalytic H2 evolution

Hydrogen generation through photocatalytic reactions is quite attractive. 3.2 Photoreduction of N2

Inspired by N2 fixation by nitrogenase in nature, the efficient capture and conversion of N2 into NH3 in ambient atmospheres under mild conditions represent challenging work that provides sources for chemical fertilizers, plastics, pharmaceuticals, and energy storage media, etc.62,138 Pristine MOFs62,99,139â€“142 and hybrid MOFs such as Cr MOF/POM,61 Ni MOF/MXene,143 Zn MOF/perovskite,144 and Zr MOF/Au NPs/PTFE (PTFE = polytetrafluoroethylene)63 have been widely explored to photoreduce N2.

Furthermore, a heterovalent Fe MOF was constructed from mixed metal salts of FeCl3 and FeCl2 and H2BDC and was reported to exhibit a rate of 164 Î¼mol gâˆ’1 hâˆ’1 for NH3 generation in the presence of K2SO3 solution under visible light irradiation.142 Activation of N2 occurred at Fe2+ sites while Fe3+ was responsible for N2 adsorption. 5. Conclusions

The development of MOF-based photoelectroactive materials for applications in photocatalysis and photovoltaics is of significant interest, motivated by how to efficiently utilize solar energy to alleviate the energy crisis and protect the environment. The merits of MOFs include large surface areas, porosity, and the ability to tailor the pore size, light harvesting, and conductivity in addition to facile synthesis, etc. Moreover, the porosity of MOF derivatives, inherited from pristine MOFs by calcination, is beneficial when enhanced electrical conductivity and stability are needed. Besides, hybrid MOFs are also able to exhibit superior outcomes due to the virtues of the synergistic effect between the host MOFs and guest species. Pristine MOFs, hybrid MOFs, and MOF derivatives can be prepared stepwise. In principle, guest species with locations on the surface or outside of MOFs and partially or fully embedded in MOFs by in situ and ex situ methods can be adopted to construct four types of non-calcined MOF materials.

Photocatalysts based on MOFs are capable of boosting a series of reactions upon light irradiation. Hydrogen evolution from water using sunlight is appealing and promising for providing clean energy. Photocurrent density is paramount for improving hydrogen production efficiency, which is related to the bandgap, recombination rate of photoelectronâ€“hole pairs, catalytic active sites, stability of photocatalysts, etc. These common features also apply to the photoreduction of carbon dioxide and nitrogen, which require high activation bond energies. Accordingly, the enhancement of the electrical conductivity of MOF-based materials by using photoelectroactive organic linkers and multivalent metal ions is beneficial, besides the encapsulation of guest species into less conductive MOFs or the calcination of MOFs. The defect engineering strategy helps provide rich active sites due to more exposed unsaturated metal centers. Tetravalent titanium and zirconium ions prefer to form fairly stable carboxylic acids-supported MOFs because of the high Lewis acidity at high oxidation states. MOF-based photocatalysts are also applied in various organic transformations with high selectivity and efficiency under mild conditions. The photodegradation of pollutants (e.g., aldehydes, aromatics, dyes, antibiotics, pesticides, herbicides, phenols, and hexavalent chromium) to carbon dioxide, water, and less harmful or non-harmful species with high efficiency and good cycling life are observed as well. It is worth noting that durability, reusability, and cost-effectiveness are far from satisfied.

MOF-based photoelectroactive materials can be applied in photovoltaic cells. As additives to photoactive layers of perovskite solar cells, MOF materials can stabilize perovskites and enhance power conversion efficiency in addition to cycling life. Doping MOF materials in electron or hole transport layers can improve extraction and transport abilities. MOF materials can also be coated as blocking or interfacial layers for better performance. A similar strategy can be applied to organic solar cells with architectures that are analogous to perovskite solar cells. For dye-sensitized solar cells and quantum dot-sensitized solar cells, porosity and large surface area are significant for hosting and stabilizing sensitizers, which decrease the chance of sensitizers accumulating besides enhancing the loading mass and charge transfer to improve light harvesting and power conversion efficiency. MOF-sensitized solar cells work in another way, utilizing luminescent organic linkers for light harvesting. MOF materials play important roles as electrolytes, counter electrodes, and photoanodes in sensitized solar cells. Much effort is needed to improve the power conversion efficiency and cycling life of photovoltaic cells.
