Abstract

Robust, reproducible patterning over large areas is essential to the fabrication of miniaturized devices. When production and cost-efficiency are concerned, guided-assembly is a promising strategy for patterning that combines the advantages of both the top-down and bottom-up approaches. Most guided-assembly methods are enabled by controlling the instabilities of liquid solutions or polymer melts to be patterned. These instabilities can be observed in different ways according to the patterning strategies. This article reviews the strategies for micropatterning that are based on the manipulation of liquid instabilities, covering both physical principles and experimental demonstrations. Specifically, we discuss four types of liquid instabilities, which can be controlled for the reliable formation of micropatterns: (i) localization of the instability under an electric field, (ii) adjustment of the evaporation front line during solvent evaporation, (iii) template-directed selective dewetting, and (iv) hierarchical capillary instability for generating complex patterns. We also highlight future prospects of the instability-driven micropatterning techniques.

1. Introduction

Fabrication of miniaturized devices with multiple functions has been one of the major subjects of research in materials science and engineering. A key step to realize these devices is the patterning of functional materials at desired length scales. In general, the strategies for generating patterned microstructures can be divided into top-down and bottom-up approaches. Top-down approaches are processes from macroscale to micro-/nano-scale that create precise, small structures from macroscopic objects through various fabrication methods.14 Bottom-up approaches produce small systems/structures through assembly of atoms, molecules, particles, or other building blocks, which generally rely on self-assembly or self-organization.5,6 The terms self-assembly and self-organization are often used interchangeably in the soft-matter literature; in general, the former should refer to equilibrium situations, whereas the latter includes the non-equilibrium conditions.7 Synergistic combinations of both the top-down and bottom-up approaches compensate for their intrinsic disadvantages. One primary example of such combinations is guided-assembly with the assistance of lithographic patterns, which is a powerful method for assembling materials into useful patterns or structures. This guided-assembly strategy has attracted the attention of physicists, chemists and biologists in the last few decades because it can provide new opportunities for developing miniaturized multifunctional devices in a relatively simple and cheap way.814

Guided-assembly can be categorized into two approaches: field guidance and template guidance. In field-guided assembly, external fields such as pressure gradient and electric field lead to the assembly. In template-guided assembly, the existing surface patterns or nano-/micro-features are used as templates to confine the target materials. Most guided-assemblies take advantage of the instability of liquid solutions or polymer melts. This article reviews the recent progress in reproducible and robust patterning over large areas, specifically focusing on the liquid instability that is essential to the patterning processes. This review provides the physical concepts of the instabilities and the experimental demonstrations of each instability type. We first introduce the field-guided assembly by two robust instability controls: (i) localization of the instability under an electric field and (ii) adjustment of the evaporation front line during solvent evaporation. For the template-guided assembly, in the following section, we include (i) micropatterning by template-directed selective dewetting and (ii) capillary force lithography in confined geometries. The future prospects of the instability-controlled patterning are discussed in the final section.

2. Patterning by localized instability under an electric field

The instability of a non-conducting fluid on a conducting substrate under an external electric field, also known as electrohydrodynamic (EHD) instability, has been a subject of both theoretical and experimental studies.1316 Steiner and coworkers demonstrated that an external electric field could destabilize the interface between a thin film of polymer liquid and air, generating micro- and nano-sized features over a large area.17 This phenomenon has attracted great attention because of its potential use in patterning various materials into small structures in a quick and inexpensive fashion. As shown in Fig. 1a, patterning based on EHD instability is conceptually simple. When an external electric field is applied to a polymer liquid placed between two electrodes, the initially flat polymerair interface will be destabilized, generating a 2D array of pillar-like structures. The structures can be fixed by cooling the sample below the glass transition temperature (Tg) of the polymer while the electric field is still on. A dielectric spacer has to be included to maintain an air gap between the polymer liquid and the top electrode.

The formation of periodic structures can be explained using an EHD model.17,18 When an external electric field is applied, the difference in the dielectric constant () between the polymer and air will cause an electrostatic stress at their interface. Because of thermal fluctuations, a small capillary wave will be generated at the polymer surface. The minimum and maximum points of the interface will then experience different electric field strengths, generating a lateral pressure gradient proportional to the square of the field strength. If the electrostatic stress is larger than the capillary pressure generated by the curvature of the interface, the magnitude of the wave will increase to reduce the separation between the liquid and the top electrode. The capillary pressure also increases as the protrusion of the wave is accelerated. Therefore, a balance between the electrostatic stress and the capillary pressure determines a characteristic wavelength, , for the wavy structure on the liquid surface. When the top electrode has a flat surface, the resulting polymer pillars will be poorly packed into a hexagonal array. This can be attributed to the random fluctuation at the polymerair interface. The use of top electrodes with patterned surfaces, such as metallic masks with a variety of geometries, allows for spatial control of the electric field, leading to various types of highly ordered arrays for the pillars.20,21Fig. 1b shows a triangular array of polymer pillars fabricated using a top electrode with a triangular protrusion. Similarly, the use of a top electrode with a concentric ring protrusion led to the formation of polymer pillars in a concentric ring pattern.19 The polymer liquid close to the protrusion boundary of the top electrode is expected to experience a stronger electric field than other regions because of a smaller spacing between the top and bottom electrodes. Therefore, the pillars first grow at the boundary and then induce the growth of other pillars within the area of the protrusion. This technique can generate a highly ordered array of structures over a relatively small area as the packing in the central region is likely to lose order when a large area is involved. One solution to achieve ordered packing over a large area is to use a top electrode with a network of interconnected, narrow protrusions. The patterned electrode can be fabricated via two-step processes. In a typical process, spacers with a specific height are first formed on a Si wafer with a layer of thermally grown oxide. The spacers are prepared by etching the oxide layer with hydrofluoric acid. An ion-assisted etching process is then carried out to form narrow protrusions on the wafer with spacers.22 The growth of pillars is initially induced underneath the protrusion regions, generating an array of boundary pillars that conforms to the geometry of the protrusions. Then, highly ordered packing of the pillars develops in the regions enclosed by the boundary pillars. Fig. 1c shows an ordered array of triangular structures fabricated using a top electrode with a network of interconnected triangular features.22 The array extended over the entire patterned electrode (500 m across). However, the complex, time-consuming processes involved in the fabrication of such a patterned electrode limit the practical use of this strategy.

Recently, Li and coworkers demonstrated the use of optically induced EHD instability for rapidly generating a desired pillar array.23,24 A polymer is first deposited on top of a photosensitive component-coated bottom electrode, followed by combining with a top electrode. The assembled sample is then placed between a digital projector and an optical microscope as shown in Fig. 2a. Irradiation under visible light with a regular grid pattern stimulates the photosensitive layer to induce a heterogeneous spatial distribution of the electric field between the two electrodes. This heterogeneity in the electric field allows for controlled nucleation of the pillars, generating a well-ordered array of pillars over a large area (Fig. 2b and c). Hu and coworkers used a flat electrode over an initially pre-patterned polymer to generate a spatially modulated electric field, which led to the easy fabrication of a highly ordered array of pillars with a high aspect ratio.25

Many efforts have been devoted to the fabrication of nanoscale features by using this technique. Several strategies have been employed to achieve smaller feature sizes: reduction of the interfacial tension (), increase of the applied voltage bias (V), decrease of the separation between the two electrodes (H), increase of the electrostatic stress, and use of electrodes patterned with nanoscale features. Reduction of can be realized by replacing the air gap with another layer of fluid that is compatible with the underlying polymer film.26,27 However, the wavelength cannot be greatly reduced by this approach because of the decrease in dielectric constant () that can lower the effective electrostatic force at the interface. Nevertheless, the bilayer system is very attractive in that non-conventional, complex patterns can be obtained by taking advantage of the dewetting between the two polymer layers.28,29 When a strong electric field generated by an increase in V or a decrease in H is applied, structures small in size can be formed.17,18,30 However, dielectric breakdown of a polymer film and its bound fluid under a strong electric field limits the reduction of feature size.31 Russel and his coworker demonstrated the use of an ionic liquid to prevent the dielectric breakdown.32 They could reduce the size of features to the sub-microscale, as small as 351 78 nm by working with a polymerionic liquid bilayer. This result was theoretically confirmed by using a numerical model suggested by Bhattacharjee and coworkers.33

A higher electrostatic stress at the interface can be achieved by increasing the dielectric constant () of the polymer film. For example, incorporation of polystyrene (PS)-capped Au nanoparticles (NPs) in a PS film increased the effective dielectric constant from 2.6 (pristine PS) to 3.2, leading to a 20% reduction in .34 Doping polymer films with small organic ions and salts is also feasible for producing patterns with smaller feature sizes.35 The carbonyl groups of poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) or ether oxygen of poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) could complex with Li+ ions to increase the value.36,37

The use of a top electrode with a periodic array of sub-micron features is also a good strategy for generating nanoscale structures.17,38 The electric field is spatially modulated by the patterned electrode, inducing fluctuations of the polymer film with periodicities similar to those of the patterned electrode, ultimately replicating the pattern on the electrode. However, if the processing time is too long, the replicated polymer pattern will be driven by the secondary instabilities back to structures with the natural wavelength () corresponding to the flat electrode.19 Theoretically, the smallest feature size is one half to one third of Hd, where is the natural wavelength on the flat electrode and d is the depth of the protrusions on the electrode.19

Materials used in EHD instability patterning have been mostly polymers. This is because of their relevance to industrial applications, facile control over viscoelastic processing, convenience in preserving the pattern by quenching, and the diversity of polymer functionality through both chemical and surface modifications. However, EHD instability patterning is not a material-specific technique because any liquid can be destabilized at the interface. A monomer liquid with low viscosity has also been used to generate arrayed pillar structures within several minutes.39 A variety of inorganic materials have been applied to EHD instability patterning as well. Through a combination of EHD instability patterning and solgel processing, patterns of crystalline titanium oxide have been fabricated.40 Patterning based on the EHD instability has been applied to metals and semiconductors.41 This strategy involves the melting of metals using a pulsed laser for a short period of time (hundreds of nanoseconds) while applying an electric field to guide the flow of the molten metals into the desired geometry before solidification. In the case of Si, the fabricated line features had a width of 175 nm and a height of 90 nm. In addition, EHD instability patterning has allowed for the patterning of hybrid materials. In a demonstration, well-defined, hierarchical pillars consisting of CNTs and polymers were generated from their composite film.42 The aligning orientation of CNTs embedded within the structures could be tuned by adjusting the EHD parameters. The capability to simultaneously form composite structures and control the alignment of anisotropic components is potentially useful for integration of 1D nanomaterial-based composites into functional devices.

EHD instability patterning has been applied to biology-related studies. For example, arrays of polymeric posts have been used to mimic the nano-hairs on the pad of a gecko.43 The PS pillars have been used as templates to generate a porous polymeric separation membrane with high liquid permeability and size selectivity.44 Polymer patterns with microscale features have been utilized for studying the proliferation of cells.45 Recently, complex structures based on EHD instability have been applied to the fabrication of micro-electro-mechanical system (MEMS) devices, micro-fluidic mixers with high efficiency, and polymeric microlenses for optical devices.4648 Very recently, Oppenheimer and coworkers demonstrated the use of this technique for fabricating a liquid-ion gate field-effect transistor (FET) device (Fig. 3a).49 With appropriately chosen experimental parameters, in combination with an electrode having a suitable pattern on its surface, they fabricated a highly ordered array of electrically conductive polymer (polypyrrole, PPy) pillars with a width of sub-micron scale over a very large area (Fig. 3b), followed by fabricating a vertical FET that is potentially useful in 3D multilevel memory devices.49 The change in the drain current (IDS) as a function of drain voltage (VDS) and gate voltage (Vg) of the p-type device is shown in Fig. 3c and its inset. Along with the transistor, these conductive polymer structures also have great potential for diverse applications such as in supercapacitors, photovoltaic systems, sensors, and electronic devices.

3. Patterning by controlling the evaporative front

The assembly of nonvolatile solutes during solvent evaporation from a liquid droplet has gained considerable interest as a non-lithographic technique capable of producing a variety of unique patterns.5053 However, the spatial variation of evaporation and unexpected convection generally lead to the formation of irregular structures such as coffee rings, fingering patterns, and polygonal networks.5456 In recent years, many studies have been directed to the fabrication of ordered structures by precisely controlling a solvent evaporation process. The readers can refer to several recently published review articles.5759 This section will only focus on novel approaches that can provide reliable patterning over large areas.

A precise control of a three-phase contact line can facilitate the patterning of a solute material on a substrate. Dip-coating is capable of creating a straight, downward movement for the contact line. During this process, an outward capillary flow of the solvent takes place to compensate for the loss of solvent at the moving front. The flow carries the solutes to the three-phase contact line, resulting in a highly selective, horizontal deposition along the moving front.60 When a substrate is pulled out from a solution, the meniscus will be pinned by the uppermost line, dragged, and then stretched. The meniscus eventually breaks and moves until it is pinned at a new position on the substrate. This stickslip motion leads to the deposition of 1D arrays of the solute with regular spatial distributions. If the concentration of a solution is relatively low, periodic domains of the solute can be obtained in the direction perpendicular to the contact line, which is a result of finger instability arising from a transverse instability of the evaporative dewetting front.6165

Over the past two decades, great advancement has been made in the synthesis of nanoparticles. However, assembling them into ordered structures over a large area still remains a grand challenge. For a colloidal suspension of thiol-capped Au NPs, finger instabilities can develop when the mobility of the NPs is sufficiently low so that the NPs can be collected at the solvent front. The mobility is determined by parameters such as the length of the thiol molecule and the concentration of NPs.66,67 In a demonstration, highly ordered 1D arrays of NPs were fabricated without involving any lithographic process. A programmed dip-coating process, along with variation of the concentration of NPs and the adjustment of the wettability of a solution relative to a substrate, allows one to tune the orientation, width, and periodicity of the patterned array of NPs.60,61,65 Lenhert and coworkers discussed the mechanism of horizontal and vertical pattern formation based on the local curvature of the liquid meniscus at the three-phase contact line.68 During the dip-coating process, a thin solution layer is dragged across the substrate because of the difference in velocity between the withdrawing substrate and the movement of the three-phase line. At a relatively high surface pressure, the curvature of the meniscus is positive. The cylindrical liquid ridge breaks up into fingers due to Rayleigh instability, forming a vertical pattern. In the case of a relatively low surface pressure, the meniscus takes a negative curvature. The oscillation in the dynamic contact angle causes a stickslip motion, leading to the formation of horizontal stripes. This assembly has allowed for the generation of an array of well-aligned wires made of transition metal dichalcogenides.69 These wires were processed to fabricate heterostructured WS2/MoS2 as well as flexible, transparent devices in combination with graphene electrodes, paving the way to innovative optical and electrical devices.

The same outcome is expected even when the movement of the three-phase line is horizontal. A sandwich system, in which a solution droplet is put on a substrate and covered with another substrate, can generate three types of microscale patterns with controllable size: dots, stripes and ladders.70 In one example, the flow coating technique has accomplished the fabrication of micro-stripes comprising diverse materials including DNA, nanowires, polymers and metal nanoparticles.7173 Recently, Emrick and coworkers demonstrated the use of this technique for fabricating ribbons made of Au NPs.74 Stickslip motion of the NP solution and then drying of the solvent leaves the ribbons consisting of dodecanethiol-capped Au NPs assembled on a substrate (Fig. 4a). Topography of the ribbons is strongly dependent on the density of the ligand on the surfaces of the NPs. The lower ligand density led to the better packing among the NPs within a ribbon because the surface energy of Au NPs increased as the ligand density decreased.74 As shown in Fig. 4b, the use of Au NPs with the lowest ligand density (7.1 molecules per nm2) (Au NP-1) led to the generation of fine ribbons without any breakage or coffee rings. The dimension of the ribbons was in the range of 500700 nm and 3050 nm for width and height, respectively. The electrical conductivity of the pristine Au NP ribbons was significantly increased from 56 S m1 to 1.8 105 S m1 after a thermal annealing process. This result suggests that the flow coating technique has great potential in the fabrication of metallic electrodes for electronic devices. Very recently, Cho and coworkers have fabricated a grid electrode made of metal NPs by working with this technique.75,76 The metallic grid electrode was successfully applied to various organic electronic devices, such as organic field-effect transistors, organic light-emitting diodes, organic solar cells, and flexible piezoelectric nanogenerators.75,76

A precise control of the three-phase contact line can also be achieved with the use of restricted geometry. When a drop of solution is confined underneath a curved surface supported on a flat substrate (a curve-on-flat geometry), a pattern with high regularity is created after the evaporation of solvent.78,79Fig. 6a shows a schematic illustration of the formation of a pattern by a spherical lens-on-flat geometry. A drop of solution with a nonvolatile solute (mostly polymers) is trapped in the spherical lens-on-flat geometry. Because of the imposed geometrical restriction, the evaporation rate of solvent is the highest at the capillary edge, which will jam the solute into the edge, and thereby pin the contact line (stick). The initial contact angle of the capillary edge, i, gradually decreases due to the evaporative loss of solvent, to a critical angle, c, at which the capillary force (the de-pinning force) becomes larger than the pinning force. This change leads to a jump of the contact line to a new position (slip). The successive stickslip motion of the contact line generates patterned structures with high regularity.80Fig. 6b shows the polymer stripes formed with a wedge-on-flat geometry. The stripes are nanoscale in height and microscale in width, respectively. By changing the concentration of the polymer solution, the height (hd) and the width of the stripes can be manipulated. At low concentrations, the contact line is pinned for a short time, leading to the deposition of less solute (i.e., smaller values for both height and width). At the same time, the slip distance to the next position, CC, becomes short.81 The properties of used solvent have a direct impact on CC and hd, too. The use of a solvent with a lower boiling point leads to the generation of structures with larger hd and CC.81 This result is due to a great loss in volume for the solvent (V) as driven by its fast evaporation.

This gradient is a result of the competition between the pinning force and the capillary force.81 The pinning force (Fpin) is linearly proportional to the total length of the contact line, 2X, where X is an absolute position of the patterned structure away from the curve-on-flat center (CC = Xi Xi1). The imbalance between the linear Fpin and the nonlinear Fcap leads to a nonlinear evaporative loss in the volume of the solvent (V), and consequently, the formation of a gradient in spacing between the features.

These geometries direct the stickslip motion of the contact line. With this guidance, the solute is deposited in a way that conforms to the shapes of the upper surface. The interfacial interaction between the solute and the substrate has been controlled to generate non-conventional, highly ordered structures such as fingers, serpentines, jagged stripes, and punch hole arrays.79,83

Lin and coworkers demonstrated the use of curve-on-flat geometry for patterning three types of conjugated polymers: poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT), poly(3-butylthiophene) (P3BT) and P3BT-b-P3HT (P3BHT).84 During evaporation of the solvent, the polymer stripes were successfully generated. The gradient in spacing between the features was observed for all the polymers (Fig. 7ai). However, morphology of the features was dependent on the type of polymer. The stripes of P3BT were sharp and straight. This result is because of the difference in interfacial interaction between the polymer and a Si substrate (Fig. 7df). An increase in the length of alkyl side chain from butyl to hexyl decreases the surface energy of polymer and leads to unfavorable interfacial interaction between the polymers (P3HT and P3BHT) and the substrate, which results in finger instability of the polymers (Fig. 7ac and gi).84 The stripes could be formed directly on a patterned P++Si/SiO2/Au substrate for measuring their electrical conductivity as shown in Fig. 7j. The P3HT and P3BHT stripes became more electrically conductive after solvent annealing for 12 h, which was a result of the increase in crystallinity of the polymer chains (Fig. 7k). On the other hand, the annealing made the crystallinity of P3BT lower, which led to a decrease in the conductivity of the polymer.

4. Patterning by template-directed selective dewetting

Morphological development driven by the dewetting of a polymer thin film is a powerful means to achieve micro- and nanoscale structures over large areas. The dewetting of a thin film on a defect-free homogeneous substrate starts with the formation of holes.8994 These holes gradually grow and then coalesce, ultimately producing isotropic droplets. However, the random distribution of droplets in space limits the use of the dewetting phenomenon as a surface patterning technique. Tremendous efforts have been devoted to the achievement of dewetting in a controlled manner. One of the main approaches is to use physical or chemical templates.95100

Physical template-directed dewetting is a simple, powerful strategy for bringing the long-range order to the dewetted morphologies. When the temperature is higher than the glass transition temperature (Tg) of a polymer, the dewetting of the polymer film on a topological substrate will start from the edges of the topological protrusions. This is because the Laplace pressure of the polymer film sharply increases at the edges. The polymer chains located at the edges move to the recessed regions on the substrate for lowering the pressure.97 The same outcome can be observed when a polymer solution is spin-coated on a topological substrate.101105 Kargupta and Sharma have theoretically studied the dewetting phenomenon of a thin film on a physically templated, chemically homogeneous substrate.106 For demonstrating the dynamics of a thin film on a topological substrate, they derived a dimensionless equation from the NavierStokes equation using the lubrication approximation:106

More complex structures have been generated by using substrates with a variety of geometries.99,107 Theoretical studies have suggested experimental conditions for perfect templating of a liquid film by a topological substrate.106,107 The key parameter is a commensuration between the mean length scale of dewetting (D h2) and the periodicity of surface topography (P). The physical template-directed dewetting loses its effectiveness for a relatively thicker film on a substrate with small periodicity (i.e., D P).

In the case of chemical templates, there are two regions with different conjoining pressures. Dewetting of a liquid film on a chemically patterned substrate is driven by a potential gradient () that arises from the difference in wettability between the two regions. The liquid flows from the less wettable region (high pressure) to the more wettable region (low pressure) even when the spinodal stability condition (/ > 0) is satisfied over the entire liquid surface. Three conditions are required to perfectly replicate the chemical pattern on the substrate:106,108 (i) h < P < 2h, where h is the characteristic length scale of instability, (ii) Wc < Wless < Wt, where Wless, Wc, and Wt are the width of the less wettable region, the critical width, and the transition width, and (iii) the liquid film remains stable on the more wettable region. Bringing chemical heterogeneity to a topological substrate allows for the generation of non-conventional, dewetted structures.97,109,110 In one example, the use of a physicochemically-patterned substrate, prepared by covering the partial regions on a topological substrate with another material, demonstrated the generation of an asymmetric polymer pattern.109

Template-directed dewetting has been further developed to fabricate multiple polymer patterns because simultaneous, coupled deformation of polymerair and polymerpolymer interfaces offers a very complex motion of the polymer chains.111,112 Ding and coworkers studied the thermal dewetting of a bilayer with a corrugated interface.113115 They observed the formation of ordered domains aligned orthogonally to the direction of the initial stripes as the bottom layer underwent surface tension-induced flattening prior to capillary breakup of the top layer.113,114 They also created many hierarchical structures by adjusting the geometry and properties of the individual layers.115 Recently, Mukherjee and coworkers have systematically studied the dewetting of a PSPMMA bilayer on a grating patterned substrate.116,117 The bilayer was prepared by directly spin-coating each polymer solution in a mutually exclusive solvent on the patterned substrate in order. On a patterned substrate of a 2D array of square pillars with low surface energy (sub < PMMA and PS), three different morphologies for the bottom PMMA layer can be formed depending on the concentration of the polymer solution (CPMMA) used for the spin-coating process: (i) an array of PMMA droplets arranged along the substrate grooves at very low CPMMA (Fig. 8a), (ii) an interconnected network of PMMA threads filling the grooves of the substrate at intermediate CPMMA (Fig. 8b), and (iii) a continuous bottom layer at high CPMMA (Fig. 8c). Morphology of the top PS layer depends strongly on the morphology of the bottom PMMA layer as well as the concentration of the polymer solution (CPS). An ordered array of core (PMMA)shell (PS) droplets is generated by spin-coating the polymer solutions with very low CPMMA and CPS (Fig. 8d), which is a result of the minimization of surface energy. With an increase of CPS, the coreshell droplet structures change to an interconnected network of PS threads filling the grooves of the substrate with the pre-existing PMMA droplets covered (Fig. 8e). In the case of intermediate CPMMA and low/intermediate CPS, the as-cast morphology is always thread on thread. As CPS increases, the top PS layer becomes continuous, covering the interconnected network of PMMA threads. However, introduction of a post-thermal annealing process allows for the evolution of the morphologies, leading to the generation of non-conventional patterns consisting of multiple polymers such as submerged, embedded, and encapsulated structures. A continuous bilayer is formed for both high CPMMA and CPS. When thermally annealed, the thinnest part of the bottom PMMA layer on the top of the pillars ruptures first and retracts towards the pillar edges. The top PS film also ruptures over the pillars, eventually resulting in bilayer threads over the substrate grooves (Fig. 8f).

Similarly, Jeong and coworkers demonstrated the use of a microwrinkled substrate as a physical template for the fabrication of patterned structures comprising multiple polymers: PS, poly(-caprolactone) (PCL), and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) (Fig. 9).101 The key to success is to use the orientation change of a wrinkled topology in response to the direction of an external strain applied to the substrate. The use of post-treatment such as solvent and/or thermal annealing induces a secondary dewetting, generating non-conventional structures such as arrows, double bells, and encapsulated dots (Fig. 9c and d).

The use of functional materials for patterning increases the versatility of template-directed dewetting. In one example, a composite stripe consisting of a polymer and metal NPs, patterned into the trenches on a zig-zag shaped template, was demonstrated as a stretchable electrode.102 Ohzono and coworkers have demonstrated the use of polyimide (PI) microwrinkles supported on a poly(dimethyl siloxane) (PDMS) elastomer to form liquid filaments of various materials including organic solvents, liquid crystals, and ionic liquids.118122 After putting a small droplet of the materials, a bendable polyimide (PI) film is brought close to the surface of the microwrinkle for generating the liquid bridge between them, which leads to selective deposition of the materials in the grooves of the microwrinkles. The deposition of these materials depends strongly on topography of the microwrinkles. Among them, a spatially confined nematic liquid crystal (NLC) exhibits a new periodic molecular alignment with periodic domains of alternating twist deformations and a line defect of a zigzag form having equal lengths of zig (lR) and zag (lL) (Fig. 10a).122 This novel configuration arises from the intrinsic anisotropy in the elasticity of NLC molecules and the opposite boundary conditions: (i) the vertical alignment of NLC molecules at the flat NLCair interface and (ii) the planar alignment of NLC molecules at the curved NLCgroove interface. Injection of vapor molecules with chirality into the NLC phase results in the imbalance of zig and zag. When the chiral molecules with right handedness are introduced, the length of the right-handed domain (lR) with orange color will increase (Fig. 10b). This result is attributed to the fact that the right-handed twisted distortion of the NLC is strengthened by the injection of chiral molecules. The change in morphology of the line defect depends on the enantiomeric excess and concentration of vapor molecules, and is completed in a few seconds. In the case of carvone molecules, the minimum concentration to achieve the morphological imbalance was approximately 5 p.p.m. The high sensitivity as well as the rapid response and the simplicity of the setup make the system potentially useful as a versatile tool that can analyze the chirality of vapor molecules including their odor, environmental chemicals, and drugs. They also demonstrated the use of the zigzag line defect for fine control of colloidal assembly.121 Kink, a core of the line defect is more energetically unfavorable than other regions, which results in stronger binding of beads at the defect core (Fig. 10c). A further understanding of how the surface anchoring and the size of colloidal particles affect the trapping can provide potentially a new route for the manipulation of colloidal assembly.

However, despite the promising scientific and technological potential of template-directed dewetting, the generated features are still microscale in size. This limitation is attributed to the weak destabilizing force and high surface tension involved in the dewetting process. Recently, Sharma and coworkers have demonstrated the use of a solvent mixture consisting of good and poor solvents for a polymer to generate sub-microscale structures with high ordering and tunable shape.123125 The resultant structures have been employed as arrays of polymeric microlens for tuning the optical properties.125

5. Patterning by capillary instability in confined geometry

Placing a mold with confined geometry on the top of a polymer layer enables one to guide the dewetting of the polymer. One exemplary approach employing this concept is capillary force lithography (CFL), which allows for the generation of geometry-controllable, micro- and nano-structures over large areas without the use of any external force.126134 This approach involves the use of an elastomeric stamp, a prime element of soft lithography for molding a polymer melt. When a patterned PDMS mold placed on the top of a polymer layer is heated above the glass transition temperature (Tg) of the polymer, the capillary force will drive the polymer melt into the recessed regions on the mold, leading to the formation of a negative replica of the mold. The resultant structure is dependent on the thickness of the polymer film. A polymer layer thick enough to completely fill the recessed regions on the mold leaves a complete replica with a residual layer on the substrate (Fig. 11a). On the other hand, the use of a thin film leads to the formation of a concave replica without a residual layer (Fig. 11b). In the case of an extremely thin film, the concave structures go through additional dewetting in the recessed regions on the mold, as they are disconnected (Fig. 11c). The theoretical maximum height (hmax) that a polymer melt can rise to in the recessed regions on a mold is given as hmax = (2poly/aircos)/(gL), where poly/air is the surface tension at the polymerair interface, is the contact angle at the polymer/mold interface, is the density of the polymer, g is the gravitational constant, and L is the width of the recessed region on the mold, respectively.126 The time, t, required for complete molding of the polymer is defined as t = 2z2/(Rpoly/aircos), where is the viscosity of the polymer melt, z is the length of the recessed region to be filled, and R is the hydraulic radius (0.5L), which indicates that t is strongly affected by .127 For PMMA with a molecular weight (Mw) of 34000, it takes a long time (1 h) to fill the recessed regions at a temperature slightly above its Tg, which is attributed to high (9.61 106 Pa s at 105 C) given by high entanglement of the polymer chains.135 A higher temperature (Tg + 2040 C) is required to achieve quick generation of the polymer patterns. A crystalline polymer is more suitable for this process because of an abrupt drop in of the melt at its melting point (Tm). Recently, an advanced CFL technique has been introduced for producing complex patterns. Those strategies take advantage of various phenomena at the interface of the mold and the polymer, such as adhesion, temperature-directed polymer mobility, partial curing kinetics, and vacuum-assisted migration.136141 Among them, UV-assisted CFL involving photo-polymerization of a UV-curable resin is a very powerful strategy for the generation of striking, non-conventional polymer structures.142 This method involves two sequential molding steps. Under UV exposure, the use of a PDMS mold with a microscale pattern leads to the generation of partially cured microstructures. Partial curing is a result of the presence of oxygen in the recessed regions on the mold. Oxygen reacts with the radicals generated from the photoinitiator, hindering the UV curing of the resin.142 The partially cured microstructure is further molded with a polyurethane acrylate (PUA) mold having a nano-pattern for producing additional nanostructures on the top of the pre-formed microstructures. It is critical to optimize the UV exposure time because excess curing limits the capillary action of the resin. The hierarchical nanohairs fabricated using this UV-assisted CFL were highly adhesive even on a rough surface (roughness < 20 m) without any structural degradation.143 This adhesive property can offer great potential for mimicking multi-scale functional surfaces in nature such as lotus leaves and gecko foot hairs. The second molding at a low pressure (102 Pa) allows the partially cured resin to be driven into the channel on the nanoscale mold, leading to the formation of suspended bridge structures including lines, meshes, circles, and dots.142 The suspended polymer-bridge structures are of potential benefit for smart electronic devices, photonic devices and microfluidic systems.

Various applications of the CFL technique have been found in the fields of inorganic patterning, organic field-effect transistors (OFETs), and solar cells. Jeong and coworkers demonstrated the use of the CFL technique for micro-patterning of inorganic materials without vacuum deposition.135,144,145 A composite line pattern made of PEO and HAuCl4 is first fabricated using a PDMS mold.144 The line pattern is then cross-molded by working with the same mold. Complexation between protonated ether groups of the polymer and AuCl4 increases the surface tension of the composite melt, which leads to additional dewetting of the composite.144 As a result, an array of the composite dots is generated. Post-treatments including exposure to N2H4 vapor and sintering eventually allow for the formation of an array of Au nanodots that can be potentially useful in diverse optical applications based on surface plasmon resonance (SPR). Similarly, the micropatterning of metals and metal chalcogenides was also achieved by the same group.135 They used the viscoelastic flow of PCL features generated by the CFL technique for the fabrication of various inorganic patterns. An inorganic material to be patterned is randomly deposited on the surface of the PCL features, followed by thermal annealing (Fig. 12a). When heated above the Tm of the polymer, the viscoelastic polymer melt will spread, gathering the inorganic material in the recessed regions. The incompatibility of the polymer melt with the inorganic material induces phase separation between the two materials. Eventually, fine inorganic structures are produced. If an inorganic material has a high melting point (i.e., Ag NPs), porous inorganic patterns will be generated after thermal annealing (Fig. 12b). On the other hand, for an inorganic material with a low melting point (i.e., a-Se), solid lines with smooth surfaces are created after the same process (Fig. 12c). Embedding of Ag lines in a PDMS matrix enabled one to fabricate stretchable and conductive composite electrodes with high stretchability (up to = 0.4) and retained resistance (35 ) up to 300 stretching cycles (Fig. 12d and e).145

Gau and coworkers took advantage of the CFL technique to make nanosized gratings and nanoholes on the surface of ZnO and active layers of their solar cells.148 The generated structures led to the enhancement of light trapping within the P3HT/PCBM heterojunction solar cells and also made the pathway for charge transfer shorter, improving the efficiency of the solar cells. Rogers and coworkers demonstrated the use of the CFL technique for improving the performance of their device.149 They generated a SU-8 epoxy pattern by working with this technique, which was then removed by reactive ion etching for fabricating Si nanostructures. The resultant structures made the trapping of light more efficient. Similarly, Jung and coworkers reported that the greater absorption and electrical enhancement arising from Al cavities could significantly improve the power conversion efficiency (26.4% for the initial value).150

Further applicability of the CFL technique has been expanded to gas sensors. Iyer and coworkers reported a grafting to approach in combination with this technique for the fabrication of a linear array of Pd nanocubes.151 Poly(acrylic acid) (PAA) was selectively grafted onto the surface of poly(glycidylmethacrylate) (PGMA) on alternating PGMA and PS stripes made using the CFL technique, followed by attaching Pd nanocubes through electrostatic attraction. The array of Pd nanocubes with high density could be integrated into a circuit for the fabrication of a miniature hydrogen sensor. Hu and coworkers demonstrated the use of this technique for patterning reduced graphene oxide (rGO) and fabricating a wafer-scale thin film transistor and a gas sensor.152 The resultant rGO pattern exhibited high field-effect mobility (2.3 cm2 V1 s1 for holes and 0.84 cm2 V1 s1 for electrons) and electrical conductivity (1001000 S m1), as well as high sensitivity (R/R0 = 1.1) for NH3 gas.

Recently, CFL-based patterning has also found potential application in biomedical engineering. DeSimone and coworkers fabricated a microneedle patch for the delivery of vaccines using the CFL technique in combination with the traditional polymerization method.153 The use of a biodegradable, water soluble polymer, poly(vinylpyrrolidone) (PVP), in the fabrication process not only gave water soluble properties but also allowed for the quick fabrication of the patch. Another successful biomedical application of the CFL technique has been found in control of cell growth. Suh and coworkers guided the growth of cells on an array of biocompatible hydrogel nanopillars that was fabricated using UV-assisted CFL.154,155 The great organization of cells on the nanopillars is a result of strong cellcell interaction given by the surface topography. Furthermore, a systematic variation in local densities of the topography gave rise to the migration of cells toward a target area, facilitating the self-assembly of cells. These results suggest that cell organization by the control of local densities and anisotropy of surface topography is potentially useful for implantable biomedical devices and tissue scaffolds for regenerative or tissue engineering.

Even though the CFL technique has been rapidly developed as a versatile, effective and low-cost tool for large area patterning, some limitations still remain. High resolution of a pattern is essential to achieve high-performance of electronic devices. However, the CFL technique has difficulty in nanoscale patterning, which is attributed to the limited resolution of a primarily used elastomer mold. In addition, the thermal annealing process for the generation of patterns can degrade semiconducting polymers, hindering the direct use of this technique for the fabrication of organic electronic devices. In spite of its high potential for the constitution of multiscale and hierarchical building blocks, studies on the phase separation or self-organization of polymer blends during the CFL process have still not been conducted.128

6. Perspectives

Micro- and nano-patterning of materials has been a long endeavor in terms of both scientific understanding and industrial needs. Although many approaches have been developed to achieve the goal, only a few methods have been successful from the perspective of expense, reliability, and large-area capability. Guided-assembly, which combines the advantages of both the bottom-up self-assembly and the top-down lithographic approach, is a promising strategy.

Most of the guided-assemblies are enabled by controlling the instabilities of liquid solutions or polymer melts to be patterned. The instabilities are observed in different ways according to the patterning strategies. We have, in this review, introduced the strategies for patterning that are based on the manipulation of liquid instabilities: (i) patterning by localized instability under an electric field, (ii) patterning enabled by the controlled evaporative front, (iii) patterning by template-directed selective dewetting, and (iv) patterning by capillary instability in a confined geometry. In addition, the physical principles of liquid instabilities and the experimental demonstrations have been discussed. Approaches for patterning by other means such as excimer lasers, light, magnetic fields, pressure gradient, shear gradient, and focused ion beam have not been addressed here.

Very recently, the instability effects have been used to fabricate complicated features with the use of viscous materials and their instability. In an example, the use of reversible creasing instability for a swollen elastomer was demonstrated to selectively deposit water droplets. This strategy is potentially useful to form non-conventional polymer patterns on diverse substrates including curved surfaces.156 The formation of plastic liquid patterns by stretching an elastomer was demonstrated, the so-called ratcheting instability patterning,157 which can be a new, facile strategy for patterning soft materials governed by a model of elasticplastic deformation.

Currently, the utilization of guided assembly expands beyond its application to polymer patterning. The broad applicability to diverse materials, including metal oxides, metal nitrides, metals, superconductors, magnetic materials, dielectrics, and aligned biomaterials, enables the guided assembly to find a wide range of potential applications in photonics, electronics, optoelectronics, and bioengineering. For example, the CFL technique has been used for generating anisotropic metallic nanostructures that are potentially useful for optical and electromagnetic applications.141,158 Guided assembly is moving toward the fabrication of nano-patterns as well. Patterning of sub-10 nm Ge islands on Si(100) has been achieved by guided assembly.159 Amphiphilic regioregular polythiophenes underwent guided-assembled on the nanoscale.160 Regular assembly of block copolymers has been attained on a substrate with a 2D chemical pattern fabricated by electro-oxidation nanolithography.161 Entropy-driven segregation of nanoparticles in a multilayered composite polymer is also an example of the efforts of guided assembly toward nanoscience.162

Even though the guided assembly which is mainly based on the control of liquid instabilities has seen considerable advancement, it still has challenging issues in terms of reliability and complexity for the pattern design as compared with the conventional lithography techniques. Furthermore, the reproducibility is not yet enough to be applicable to industrial use. Patterning by controlled evaporative front and template-directed selective dewetting is quite sensitive to the environmental surroundings (humidity, temperature, and so on), which makes the formation of highly reproducible patterns over large areas difficult. EHD instability patterning exhibits the limitation of pattern resolution over large areas due to Ostwald ripening.163 In the case of the CFL technique, thermal expansion of an elastomeric mold causes the asymmetric liquid instabilities in a confined geometry, which leads to the poor uniformity in the shape and dimension of a pattern formed over a large area.129 Taken together, solving these issues will be a critical direction of future study.
