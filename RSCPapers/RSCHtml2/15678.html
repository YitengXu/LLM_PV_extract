Abstract

As one of the most promising alternatives for future energy systems, the rechargeable Znâ€“air battery (ZAB) has attracted extensive attention due to its extraordinarily high theoretical specific energy density. However, several obstacles restrict its practical application. One challenge is the sluggish kinetics of oxygen-reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen-evolution reaction (OER) in the discharging and charging processes of ZABs. In addition, when using unifunctional ORR or OER electrocatalysts as air electrodes, like noble metal catalysts (Pt/C or Ru/IrO2), there are the disadvantages of high cost and poor stability. Therefore, rational design of non-noble metal bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts with high activity and stability is essential for the development of ZABs. In this review, we discuss the latest developments of non-noble metal bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts for ZABs. Firstly, the related reaction mechanisms of ORR and OER are introduced. Then, the latest developments of bifunctional ORR/OER materials for ZABs are discussed in detail from three aspects: (i) MOF-based catalysts, including pristine MOFs and their derivatives; (ii) metal-free-based carbon catalysts, including heteroatom-doped carbon and defective carbon; (iii) metal-based catalysts, including metalâ€“nitrogenâ€“carbon materials (such as metals/alloys, single-atom) and metal compound materials. Finally, some challenges and outlooks for the optimal design of bifunctional air electrodes for rechargeable ZABs with high activity and ultra-long lifetime are put forward.

1. Introduction

Recently, with the growth of energy and environmental crises, great efforts have been devoted to develop alternative novel energy storage and conversion technologies.1 So far, the Li-ion battery (LIB) is currently the most successful energy storage technology since its first report in 1990.2â€“4 Unfortunately, its shortcomings have limited its further development and large-scale applications,5,6 such as rare lithium resources, low specific energy density (200â€“250 W h kgâˆ’1),7,8 and safety concerns. To avoid these problems, alternative inexpensive and electrically rechargeable battery technologies are attracting more and more attention.

The metalâ€“air battery is among the alternative cost-effective battery technologies with high theoretical energy density, low cost, and environmental friendliness, and which is composed of an air cathode and a metal anode (e.g., Li, Na, K, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe), generating electricity through the redox reaction between oxygen and the metal anode. A comparison of different metalâ€“air batteries is shown in Table 1. Among them, Liâ€“air and Znâ€“air batteries have received the most extensive attention. Currently, Liâ€“air batteries show ultra-high theoretical energy density of 5200 W h kgâˆ’1 and operating voltage of 2.96 V,9â€“11 but some shortcomings, such as the explosive reactivity of lithium with water or air and rare lithium resource, largely limit their large-scale practical application. The Znâ€“air battery (ZAB) is a very promising battery system due to its outstanding advantages. For example, the theoretical energy density of ZABs is 1086 W h kgâˆ’1, which is about 5 times higher than that of LIBs due to free oxygen supply from the atmosphere. In addition, the appropriate operating voltage (âˆ¼1.65 V) of ZABs is just practical, and it is not too high to induce the decomposition of water in electrolyte. Moreover, due to abundant Zn resources and the free supply of oxygen for the air cathode, ZABs can be potentially manufactured at a lower cost of <10$ per kW per h than of LIBs (400â€“800$ per kW per h).12 Overall, ZABs have been considered as promising for future energy application.

The first ZAB was originally designed by Maiche in 1878, and the first commercial product appeared in 1932. Recently, rechargeable ZABs have emerged and undergone rapid development by the introduction of new bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysts toward oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) of the air cathode during discharging/charging process.13â€“15 Nevertheless, rechargeable ZABs are still in their infancy and confronted with many challenges. On the one hand, the efficiency and power density of rechargeable ZABs are seriously restricted by the sluggish kinetics for the ORR and OER. Due to the high overpotential on the air cathode, the round-trip energy efficiency of rechargeable ZABs is usually less than 55â€“65%.16 On the other hand, the long-term cycle performance of ZABs is currently less than a quarter of that of commercial LIBs, which further limits their practical application.17 Based on the above considerations, developing high-efficiency and well-tolerated catalysts as bifunctional ORR/OER air electrodes is of great importance for rechargeable ZABs. Generally, the ideal catalysts should meet the following basic requirements:

(i) Activity

The activity of catalysts is closely related to each intrinsic catalytic active site and the number of active sites. Firstly, heteroatom doping (N, O, S, B, P, and metal atoms) and defect control (vacancy and edge) will generate charge redistribution and change the electronic structure, thereby reducing the adsorption energy of oxygen-containing species on them and improving the intrinsic catalytic activity. Secondly, control of the porous structure (such as micropores, mesopores, macropores) can also increase the exposed number/density of active sites. The macropores can provide more mass transfer pathways, while the meso-/micropores can provide larger specific surface areas to increase the accessibility of reactants to the active centers.

(ii) Conductivity

High conductivity is the key to electron transport in all electrochemical processes. For instance, Ï€-conjugated conductive ligands and unbound electrons dissociated from sp2 carbon structure can ensure the rapid charge transport in redox reactions, thus enhancing electrocatalytic activity.

(iii) Stability

The ideal catalyst must possess excellent chemical and structural stability in the harsh acid/alkali environment. Due to the increase of particle size, the agglomeration of particles, and the change of oxidation state on the surface of the electrocatalyst during the long-term operation of a battery, the catalyst will gradually age and partially inactivate, which will lead to the decay of the battery performance. Therefore, the durability of catalysts is an important factor affecting the commercialization of batteries.

(iv) Selectivity

The ideal electrocatalysts should have high selectivity towards specific processes. For instance, molecular oxygen (O2) can be electrochemically reduced to water (H2O) by 4eâˆ’, or transformed to hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) by 2eâˆ’. In order to maximize the energy conversion efficiency, the former is preferred in the fuel cell reaction, while the latter is a green synthesis method of H2O2. Thus, the selectivity of catalysts can effectively regulate the reaction pathway and products of ORR.

(v) Low cost and environmentally friendly

Noble metal (e.g., Pt, Ag, Ru, Ir, etc.) electrocatalysts possess high catalytic activity, but the shortcomings of high cost, low stability, and resource scarcity seriously restrict their practical application. Metalâ€“organic framework (MOF)-based catalysts, metal-free-based carbon catalysts or transition metal-based catalysts will be the focus of future electrocatalyst design due to intrinsic catalytic activity and low cost. Thus, the design and preparation of electrocatalysts should follow the principles of low cost, low energy consumption and no pollution.

In this review, we will systematically summarize the research progress of bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts in recent years and highlight their applications in rechargeable ZABs. Firstly, the configuration of rechargeable ZABs and the electrochemical reaction mechanism of the air cathode are briefly introduced. Secondly, as shown in Fig. 1, the recently reported bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts are classified as: (i) MOF-based catalysts, including pristine MOFs and their derivatives; (ii) metal-free-based carbon catalysts, including heteroatom-doped carbon and defective carbon; (iii) metal-based catalysts, including metalâ€“nitrogenâ€“carbon materials (such as single-atom, metals/alloys) and metal compound materials. Finally, the existing challenges and outlooks for the development of high-efficiency bifunctional oxygen catalysts for ZABs are discussed. This review will provide timely information of bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts and underline the importance of catalyst compositions and architectures, which will be helpful to guide researchers to design and manufacture high-performance rechargeable ZABs.

2. Battery configuration and working mechanism

2.1 Battery configuration

2.1.1 Composition of ZABs. As shown in Fig. 2, a ZAB is comprised of a metal Zn anode, an air cathode, a membrane separator, and an electrolyte.18

(a) Anode: Zn electrode. The Zn anode that determines the capacity of the battery is commonly in the form of pure Zn powder or Zn plate.19â€“21 Zn has a characteristic set of advantages, including low toxicity, abundance, electrochemical reversibility, constant discharge voltage, and high energy density, so it is the most stable and non-corrosive electropositive metal in aqueous and alkaline media.22 In general, the surface morphology of Zn is very important for obtaining a high reaction rate in an alkaline electrolyte. In order to improve battery performance under high currents, smaller particles, such as fibers23 and dendrites,24 are preferred in ZABs.
(b) Cathode: air electrode. The air cathode is usually composed of a catalyst layer and a gas-diffusion layer consisting of active carbon and polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE).25 As a wetting agent, superhydrophobic PTFE can not only allow air to pass through the gas-diffusion layer, but also prevent aqueous electrolyte leaking from the air cathode. In general, a high rate of oxygen reduction usually occurs close to the three-phase interface (catalyst, electrolyte, and oxygen).
(c) Separator. The separator that prevents physical contact between the anode and cathode should meet the basic requirements of high stability, high resistance, low electrical conductivity and high ionic conductivity.26â€“29 Separators used in ZABs are based on porous polymeric films composed of polyethylene, polypropylene, polyamide, polyetherimide, or polyacrylic acid,30â€“34 which can enable rapid OHâˆ’ transfer during discharging/charging processes. However, the pores of the separator are sometimes large, allowing Zn(OH)42âˆ’ anions to pass, resulting in the reduction of the battery capacity and cycling efficiency.35,36 Polysulfonium membrane is also an effective alternative material for ZAB separators to effectively prevent Zn ions permeating from anode to cathode.32,37
(d) Electrolyte. As the medium of ion migration during the discharging/charging process, the conductivity of the electrolyte is an important factor affecting the ohmic resistance of batteries. Zn reacts violently in acid solution, and anode corrosion is serious. Therefore, an alkaline electrolyte is often used instead of an acid electrolyte in most cases.38 6 M KOH aqueous solution is most usually used in primary ZAB electrolyte with an ionic conductivity of 620 mS cmâˆ’1. In some studies of rechargeable ZABs, ZnCl2 or Zn(Ac)2 with a low concentration (e.g., 0.2 M) is added to the 6 M KOH electrolyte to promote the reversible translation of Zn anode.39

Recent studies have shown that ionic liquids (ILs) can be used as electrolytes in ZABs because of their low volatility, no water leakage, intrinsic ionic conductivity, non-flammability, high electrochemical stability, and high thermal stability40 during the charging/discharging process.41,42 Kar et al. comprehensively analyzed ILs related to energy applications.43 Most ILs can wet PTFE and fill the electrode holes, despite their being usually too adherent to submerge the air electrode completely. However, the electrocatalytic activities of aprotic electrolytes are still inferior to that of KOH electrolyte due to their lower ion conductivity.44 In addition, the leakage of electrolyte is also a big problem that affects the safety and lifetime of batteries. In order to reduce water loss, gel electrolytes are a feasible solution due to their good safety and ionic conductivity. For instance, Mohamad et al. proved that KOH solution mixed with gel could be used as a new alkaline electrolyte for solid-state ZABs.45

2.1.2 Product form of ZABs. Rechargeable ZABs have several types of configurations, such as planar (liquid, solid), flexible (wearable, coaxial cable) and coin-type. As shown in Fig. 3a, the configuration of conventional planar rechargeable ZABs is easy to assemble, so it is the most widely used configuration in research.46 In addition, gel electrolytes can also be applied in planar rechargeable ZABs instead of liquid electrolytes due to their advantages of low reaction activity, low density, ease of film formation and good elasticity. As shown in Fig. 3b, a gel polymer electrolyte battery47 can be made into various shapes with the advantages of pressure resistance, impact resistance, low production cost and easy processing and utilization.

3. Oxygen reaction mechanism of air cathode

Compared with primary ZABs involving only the ORR discharging process, rechargeable ZABs require bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts as air cathodes in discharging/charging processes to accelerate the sluggish reaction rates and improve the round-trip efficiency. Since aqueous ZABs usually operate with alkaline electrolytes, herein, we only briefly introduce possible ORR and OER mechanisms in alkaline conditions.

4. Bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysts

To date, most unifunctional ORR or OER electrocatalysts have been developed with high catalytic performance, such as Pt- or Ru/IrO2-based noble metal catalysts.52,53 Despite that two unifunctional catalysts can be combined to achieve bifunctional catalysis, this combination increases the commercial cost and complexity of the preparation process. Thus a bifunctional non-noble metal ORR/OER electrocatalyst for the air electrode is regarded as the best choice and inevitably determines the performance of a rechargeable ZAB. Herein, we will systematically summarize recently developed bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts as air cathode for rechargeable ZABs from three aspects: (i) MOF-based catalysts, including MOFs and their derivatives; (ii) metal-free-based carbon catalysts, including heteroatom-doped carbon and defective carbon; (iii) metal-based catalysts, including metalâ€“nitrogenâ€“carbon materials (such as single-atom, metals/alloys) and metal compound materials.

4.1 MOF-based catalysts

MOFs,56â€“66 composed of metal ions or clusters coordinated with organic ligands, especially zeolite imidazole frameworks (ZIFs),67â€“71 have exhibited huge potential in catalysis due to their highly ordered crystalline structures, adjustable pore size, ultra-high specific surface areas and structural designability.

Compared with commercial non-porous noble metal catalysts, the MOF-based catalysts mostly possess the following unique advantages: (i) easy doping of highly dispersed heteroatoms (e.g., metal atoms, N, O, S, and P) can tailor the local electronic structure of catalysts, which can effectively reduce the adsorption energy of intermediate species, thereby improving the catalytic performance; (ii) the controllable size, morphology and structure of pristine MOFs are helpful for improving the catalytic performance; (iii) conductive ligands may endow MOFs with good electrical conductivity to achieve rapid electron transfer; (iv) the supermolecular feature of MOFs may provide good electrochemical stability and durability in electrocatalysis. Therefore, in order to achieve highly efficient MOF-based catalysts, it is vital to develop MOFs containing appropriate metal ions, organic ligands, architectures, and accessible voids. Herein, the design and synthesis strategy of MOF-based bifunctional oxygen catalysts will be generally divided into two types: pristine MOFs and their derivatives. 4.1.1 Pristine MOFs. As a new class of MOF material,72 two-dimensional (2D) conductive MOFs73,74 have attracted considerable attention due to their inherent structural porosity, chemical stability and high conductivity. To date, most 2D conductive MOFs have been constructed from the coordination between metal ions (Cu2+, Ni2+, Co2+etc.) and benzene- or triphenylene-derived ligands with imine (â€“NH2), phenol (â€“OH), or thiophenol (â€“SH) functional groups. Based on in-plane Ï€-conjugation, these 2D MOFs exhibit excellent electrical conductivity.

2,3,6,7,10,11-Hexaiminotriphenylene (HITP) as a classic conductive ligand has been applied to the synthesis of a new 2D conductive MOF, Ni3(HITP)2, by DincÄƒ and co-workers, as shown in Fig. 5a.75 In the MOF structure, the Ni coordinated by four amino groups is a conjugate coplanar structure, and the ligand has a large Ï€ bond, which could ensure electron transport and high conductivity. As a well-defined and highly ordered ORR electrocatalyst, 2D Ni3(HITP)2 exhibited high ORR activity and excellent electrical conductivity (Ïƒ = 40 S cmâˆ’1) without further treatment or modification (Fig. 5b).76 Subsequently, their group further revealed the real active site of O2 reduction with Ni3(HITP)2 by experimental and computational data.77 The kinetic and X-ray absorption spectroscopy data showed that the binding and activation of O2 in Ni3(HITP)2 did not occur on metal Ni, but on the ligand. As shown in Fig. 5c, density functional theory (DFT) calculations further identified that the most likely active site for catalysis was the Î²-C relative to the imine group. This is the first time that conductive MOFs with excellent electrical conductivity have successfully and effectively electrocatalyzed ORR, exhibiting potential applications for ZABs.

Previous studies on conductive MOFs only focused on unifunctional ORR or OER. Recently, researchers have been devoted to the study of the bifunctional properties of conductive MOFs. For instance, Peng et al. synthesized a series of conductive MOFs of M3HITP2 with various Ni/Co ratios using HITP as the ligand.79 Compared with Ni3HITP2, the non-preferred coordination of HITP ligands with Co2+, poor crystallinity and Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking of Co3HITP2 indicated a distorted quadrilateral structure with lack of coplanarity due to the unpaired electron of 3dz2, which had been confirmed by experimental results. As a result, Co3HITP2 demonstrated significant catalytic activities in both ORR (E1/2 = 0.80 V) (Fig. 5e) and OER (E10 = 1.6 V), indicating that the unpaired 3d electrons of the metal center can effectively promote ORR activity. Rechargeable ZABs using Co3HITP2 as an air cathode demonstrated excellent energy efficiency and long-term stability (over 80 h) (Fig. 5f). This is the first time that conductive MOFs with significant bifunctional ORR/OER activity, excellent conductivity, and good stability were used to fabricate rechargeable ZABs, which paves the way for the direct utilization of conductive MOFs without pyrolysis in the field of green energy.

In addition to the HITP series of conductive MOFs, Lee et al. also fabricated a novel 3D dual-linked hexaiminobenzene MOF (Mn/Fe-HIB-MOF) through isolated reactions of hexaiminobenzene ligands with ammonia solutions of Mn(II) and Fe(II) salts in air atmosphere (Fig. 5g), followed by a thermal process.80 The resultant Mn/Fe-HIB-MOF possessed quintet-shelled hollow spheres, a large surface area of 2298 m2 gâˆ’1, and effective M(II)â€“N4 moieties, exhibiting superior bifunctional oxygen catalytic activity (Î”E = 0.627 V) with a positive E1/2 of 0.883 V for ORR and a low E10 of 1.61 V for OER (Fig. 5h). Significantly, the Mn/Fe-HIB-MOF cathode showed a record-breaking cycle lifetime of over 1000 h (6000 cycles at 10 mA cmâˆ’2) and 600 h (3600 cycles at 25 mA cmâˆ’2) for liquid and all-solid-state ZABs, respectively (Fig. 5i). These promising results indicated these novel hexaiminobenzene MOFs with excellent bifunctional ORR/OER activity, good conductivity, and significant stability possess great potential in the industrial application of rechargeable ZABs.

Ultrathinning MOFs into 2D nanosheets with coordinated unsaturated metal sites is another effective strategy to obtain high-performance MOF-based electrocatalysts. For instance, Tang et al. synthesized NiCo bimetal MOF nanosheets (NiCo-UMOFNs) with a mixed solution of Ni2+, Co2+ and terephthalic acid (BDC).81 As electrocatalysts for OER, the NiCo-UMOFNs exhibited remarkable electrocatalytic activity with a low E10 of 1.48 V and long-term cycle stability (at least 200 h). DFT calculations indicated that the unsaturated metal sites were the main active centers for OER, and the electron coupling effect of Ni and Co further improved the OER activity. Thus, ultrathinning MOFs strategy is effective for preparation of high-efficiency OER electrocatalysts at the atomic level.

Furthermore, 2D MOF nanosheets modified with other MOF nanoparticles have also been considered as an effective strategy for the design of oxygen catalysts. For instance, Dou et al. synthesized a hybrid 2D dual MOF (denoted as Ni-MOF@Fe-MOF) for the first time by depositing inert Fe-MOF nanoparticles on active Ni-MOF nanosheets (Fig. 6a).82 Benefiting from the morphology of 2D nanosheets and the synergistic effect between Ni and Fe species, the catalytic performance of the hybrid 2D MOF was substantially improved. The optimal Ni-MOF@Fe-MOF with a Ni/Fe atom ratio of 3.5/1 possessed the lowest E10 of 1.495 V, which was lowered by 100 mV after hybridization (Fig. 6b). Similar performance enhancement had been also successfully confirmed in the NiCo-MOF nanosheets. In addition, it is found that the hybrid MOFs were in situ transformed into metal oxide nanoparticles during the OER process as real active centers. These findings provide new insights of excellent OER performance for the design of pristine MOFs in electrocatalysis.

For the synthesis of 2D MOF nanosheets, the conventional synthesis method of ultrasonic separation (top-down) and solvothermal (bottom-up) is very complex, time-consuming, and difficult to translate to mass production. Thus, it is very important to seek a simple and effective method to synthesize 2D MOF nanosheets. For instance, Zhu et al. reported a surfactant-free 2D oxide sacrifice approach (2dOSA) to prepare ultrathin FeCo MOF-74 nanosheets (denoted as FeCo-MNS) by using ultrathin iron-cobalt oxide nanosheets (FeCo-ONS, 1.2 nm) as self-sacrificial templates, as shown in Fig. 6c.83 The as-prepared FeCo-MNS possessed a thickness of 2.6 nm and coordinated unsaturated metal sites, exhibiting an excellent OER activity with a low E10 of 1.528 V (Fig. 6d). It is believed that this idea will provide a facile way to synthesize large-scale 2D MOF nanosheets with excellent OER activity for wide applications in catalysis.

The combination of 2D MOF and conductive material is also an effective way to improve the electrical conductivity and electrocatalytic performance. For instance, Huang et al. in situ hybridized a novel MXene-MOF composite (denoted as Ti3C2Tx-CoBDC) via an interdiffusion reaction strategy (Fig. 6e).84 The 2D CoBDC layer could provide high pore structure and large specific surface area, and the hydrophilic and conductive Ti3C2Tx nanosheets could enable charge and ion transfer rapidly across the interface of Ti3C2Tx-CoBDC and promote the electrolyte to enter the catalytic active surfaces of CoBDC. As shown in Fig. 6e, the hybrid material exhibited excellent OER catalytic performance (E10 = 1.64 V). More importantly, rechargeable ZABs with the hybrid nanosheets as an air cathode exhibited excellent cycle stability with no obvious potential loss after 25 cycles (for 15000 s) of charging/discharging (Fig. 6f). More importantly, a Ti3C2Tx-CoBDC-based ZAB could successfully power a light-emitting diode (Fig. 6g). Considering the versatility of composition of conductive materials, this work will provide a reference for preparing a series of MOF electrocatalysts with ideal OER catalytic activity and excellent conductivity by combining different conductive materials.

Despite some conductive MOFs having been successfully proven as emerging electrocatalysts for ORR and OER, the expensive conductive ligands and complex synthesis conditions restrict their practical applications in the field of catalysis. Besides, some pristine 2D MOF nanosheets exhibited excellent OER activities; however, their ORR performance needs to be further explored. Moreover, the structural stability and electrical conductivity of conductive MOFs need to be further improved. Therefore, the above disadvantages limit the further application of pristine MOFs in ZABs. Currently, it is more effective to develop MOFs as self-sacrificial templates to construct porous carbon encapsulated metal/metal compound electrocatalysts for ZABs.

MOF-derived transition metal compounds (such as transition metal oxides, metal chalcogenides, and metal phosphides) embedded in porous carbon matrix have also attracted wide attention due to the significantly enhanced catalytic performance and durability caused by a synergistic effect between the metal compounds and porous carbon materials.115â€“119

Transition metal oxides (TMOs) (M = Fe, Co, Ni, Mn, etc.) derived from MOFs have attracted wide attention as promising bifunctional electrocatalysts for ZABs. Firstly, the variable valences and rich structures of TMOs endow them with rich redox electrochemical properties. Secondly, the abundant organic ligands in MOFs enable the uniform dispersion of TMOs in porous carbon matrix during the controlled carbonization process, which can effectively enhance electrical conductivity and stability of catalysts.

Among cobalt oxide materials, Co-based MOFs derived from Co3O4 are deemed to be promising candidates for bifunctional electrode materials owing to their high electrocatalytic activity. For instance, Guan et al. synthesized a novel bifunctional catalyst (denoted as N-Co3O4/CC) composed of hollow Co3O4 nanospheres embedded in N-doped carbon nanowall arrays on carbon cloth by pyrolysis of Co-MOF (Fig. 10a).120 Carbon onion coating restrained the Kirkendall effect to facilitate the transformation of the Co nanoparticles into hollow Co3O4 nanospheres with fine-grained polycrystalline oxide structure, enhancing the bifunctional ORR/OER catalytic properties with a high E1/2 of 0.87 V for ORR (Fig. 10b). When assembled into a solid-state ZAB, it presented high OCP of 1.44 V and high specific capacity of 387.2 mA h gâˆ’1, outperforming Pt- and Ir-based devices. In addition, Jiang et al. also synthesized a hybrid (denoted as Co/Co3O4@PGS) comprised of metal Co and spinel Co3O4 nanoparticles embedded into porous graphitized carbon shells through ionic exchange reaction and redox reaction between Co2+ and 2D Zn MOF nanosheets.121 The hybrid catalyst possessed three different phases, i.e., metal, spinel oxide, and carbon, which were interconnected in the whole nanostructure. By constructing a triphase structure of atom trapping defect interfaces and porous graphitized carbon shell, charge transfer and utilization of active sites of the catalysts were significantly facilitated. Therefore, the Co/Co3O4@PGS catalyst exhibited significantly improved bifunctional catalytic activity with a low Î”E of 0.69 V (Fig. 10c). When integrating the hybrid catalyst into the air electrode of rechargeable ZABs, it showed exceptionally high cycle stability without voltage decay over 800 h at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 10d). Such unique interface engineering strategy will endow MOF-derived metal oxides with significantly improved bifunctional catalytic activity, excellent conductivity, and high cycle stability.

In addition to Co-MOF, other metal-based MOFs (such as those based on Mn) have also been selected to design carbon encapsulated metal oxide materials as effective bifunctional electrocatalysts. For instance, Lou et al. prepared a bimetalâ€“organic framework (Co/Mn-MIL-100) via a hydrothermal method for the first time.122 Due to the very large difference of reduction potential of cobalt and nickel, the Co/Mn-MIL-100-derived MnO/Co/PGC polyhedrons composed of MnO/Co and porous graphitic carbon possessed abundant MnO/Co heterointerfaces, rather than the conventional bimetal oxides (Fig. 10e). Owing to the formation of desired components and heterostructures, the resultant MnO/Co/PGC exhibited superior bifunctional oxygen catalytic activity with a small Î”E of 0.82 V and long-term durability (over 20000 s). Importantly, homemade ZABs with MnO/Co/PGC as air cathode exhibited excellent battery performance, including a high peak power density of 172 mW cmâˆ’2, a specific capacity of 872 mA h gâˆ’1 (Fig. 10f), and excellent cycling stability without obvious voltage decay (350 cycles for 116 h) (Fig. 10g), outperforming commercial Pt/C + RuO2-based ZAB. This work highlights the synergism of heterointerfaces in electrocatalysis of oxygen, thus providing a promising way for advanced Znâ€“air cathode catalysts.

Besides transition metal oxides, other metal compounds, such as metal chalcogenides and metal phosphides, derived from MOFs also exhibit remarkable bifunctional catalytic activity toward ORR and OER, and have emerged as attractive catalyst candidates for ZABs due to their rich catalytic active sites, high electrical conductivity, and good stability.

Transition metal chalcogenides are often formed by the interaction between a metal (such as Fe, Co, Ni) and chalcogen atoms (such as S, Se), forming a unique crystal structure, which is conducive to electron transfer and oxygen adsorption in the oxygen catalytic reaction. Recent studies have demonstrated that MOFs can be considered as promising precursors to synthesize porous carbon-based metal sulfide bifunctional catalysts. For instance, Wang et al. synthesized trimetallic CoNiFe sulfide mesoporous nanospheres (denoted as CoNiFe-S MNs) with uniform morphology by using a trimetallic MOF as precursor and a subsequent thioacetamide anion exchange method (Fig. 11a).123 Due to the synergistic effect of trimetallic ions, the CoNiFe-S MNs exhibited a low E10 of 1.429 V for OER and a low Î”E of 0.71 V between E10 and E1/2. A rechargeable ZAB based on CoNiFe-S MNs also exhibited a remarkable cycle stability (a low voltage gap of 0.76 V at 2 mA cmâˆ’2) (Fig. 11b) and high power density of 140 mW cmâˆ’2. This method can pave the way for the design of other MOF-derived metal sulfides with improved catalytic activity.

Besides sulfurization, selenization can also be applied to convert these MOF precursors into metal selenide embedded in carbon matrix. Transition metal phosphides coupled with conductive carbon matrix can be acquired using MOFs as templates through phosphorization processes. For instance, Manthiram et al. prepared 1D hybrid catalysts (denoted as CoP@mNSP-C) composed of ultra-small cobalt phosphide nanoparticles embedded into N-, S-, and P-doped carbon supports by combining the MOF-derived conversion approach with an in situ crosslinking method (Fig. 11f).125 In the in situ synthesis process, the 1D cobalt precursor reacted with 2-methylimidazole to form ultra-small ZIF-67 nanocrystals, and the introduced triethylamine was crosslinked with poly(cyclotriphosphazene-co-4,4â€²-sulfonyldiphenol) (PZS). Due to the high content of CoP in CoP@mNSP-C and very small nanoparticle size, the 1D hybrid catalyst exhibited remarkable bifunctional catalytic activity with a positive E1/2 of 0.90 V (Fig. 11g) for ORR and a low E10 of 1.64 V for OER. Furthermore, the rechargeable ZABs based on CoP@mNSP-C demonstrated a low potential gap of 0.86 V and excellent cyclic stability with negligible voltage change after 100 cycles over 80 h (Fig. 11h). This new method will offer an index for the design of metal phosphide catalysts with remarkable bifunctional catalytic activity and excellent cyclic stability.

To sum up, appropriate metal ions and organic ligands may coordinate with each other to construct crystalline porous MOFs with ordered crystalline porous structures, abundant accessible atomically dispersed metal active sites, and high surface area. Although these merits endow pristine MOFs with high catalytic activity, selectivity and rapid mass transfer, their structural instability in harsh acid/alkali environment and low electric conductivity and the use of high-cost conductive ligands largely limit their practical application in ZABs. To overcome this, controlled chemical/thermal treatment of MOFs has been adopted to prepare porous metal-doped carbon materials with tunable nanostructures and chemical compositions. Those MOF-derived materials combine the advantageous features of MOFs (e.g. porosity/high surface area) and metal-doped carbon (e.g. abundant active metal sites/desirable electrical conductivity), along with high catalytic activity, perfect selectivity, good conductivity, and excellent structural stability. Thus, MOF derivatives provide great opportunities as electrode catalysts for electrochemical energy applications in ZABs. However, high-cost MOF precursors and harsh synthesis conditions restrict the wide application of MOF derivatives in industrial production. In view of this, in order to develop the role of MOF-based materials as promising catalysts for ZABs in industry, it is important to develop more â€œstableâ€ and â€œlow-costâ€ MOFs in the future.

4.2.1 Heteroatom-doped carbon materials. Heteroatom-doped carbon materials have been developed as high-efficiency and low-cost catalysts for various reactions.129 This is because the introduced heteroatoms (e.g., N, S, O, B, P) can effectively adjust the charge/spin redistribution in sp2 conjugated carbon matrix, thereby enhancing the electrocatalytic activity.130â€“135 Herein, heteroatom-doped carbon materials are mainly divided into three categories: mono-atom doping, dual doping, and multi-doping.
(a) Mono-atom doping. Carbon materials doped with a single atom type (N, P, S, or B atoms) have been proven to be potential high-efficiency bifunctional electrocatalysts by generating plentiful active sites in the carbon skeleton and reducing the reaction energy barrier.136â€“140

N-doped carbon nanomaterials have been generally exploited as metal-free-based bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts. Most methods for foaming porous carbon electrodes from biomass are highly energy ineffective as they require the decomposition of the biomass and its successive reestablishment into powder-like carbon. Therefore, Loh et al. fabricated high-efficiency N-doped hierarchical carbon plates (denoted as N/E-HPC) from natural timber by an enzyme treatment and successive pyrolysis with NH4Cl (Fig. 12a).141 The carbon plate structure was mechanically strong and could support a weight over 650 times its own weight without damage to its pore construction (Fig. 12b). It also exhibited notable electrical conductivity in the perpendicular direction (Fig. 12c). At the best treatment temperature of 900 Â°C, both pyridinic N sites and graphitic N sites were introduced into the carbon framework with a high density, which endowed the catalyst with excellent ORR and OER performances. A reversible ZAB based on the catalyst also exhibited high peak power density of 192.7 mW cmâˆ’2, specific capacity of 801 mA h gâˆ’1, and remarkable cycle stability for 110 h (Fig. 12dâ€“f) without any evident performance attenuation. What is more, N/E-HPC-900 could be used immediately as an air cathode in ZABs (Fig. 12g). Hence, the direct conversion of raw wood into nanostructured catalysts is beneficial due to the ease of operation and the high cost-effectiveness of the process as well because the raw hierarchic structure can be easily utilized. Such ORR and OER performances of N/E-HPC-900 are comparable to those of commercial catalysts. Moreover, the N/E-HPC-900-based ZAB has good charging and discharging performance (gap of 0.77 V) and excellent stability.

N-doped carbon nanomaterials induced n-type carrier properties and exhibited hypersensitive electronic properties to the air; thus their catalytic mechanism is still controversial. The study of B-doped carbon nanomaterials has been more straightforward than that of N-doped ones due to the low doping concentration. Therefore, the design of B-doped carbon nanomaterials has been deemed as a valid tactic to enhance electrocatalytic activity.46,143â€“146 For instance, Choi et al. synthesized neoteric carbon-based composites made up of B-doped graphene quantum dots fastened to a graphene hydrogel (denoted as GH-BGQD) by adopting a hydrothermal route.147 The as-prepared GH-BGQD composites exhibited remarkable bifunctional electrocatalytic activity for ORR (E1/2 = 0.87 V) and OER (E10 = 1.6 V). The excellent bifunctional performance of GH-BGQD was due to the highly effective active sites of the BGQDs and the 3D construction of GH, which provided an easy approach to catalytic sites as well as high-speed charge and mass transport. A solid-state battery with GH-BGQD2 as air electrode showed an exceptional flexibility and cycling stability (over 15 h at 10 mA cmâˆ’2) even when bent at different angles. The introduction of B atoms can enhance the charge carriers due to their lower electronegativity than carbon, and can also promote an efficient chemisorption with the oxygen atoms.


(b) Dual doping. Dual doping in a carbon skeleton with two types of heteroatoms can produce a synergistic effect to facilitate catalytic activity, so it is regarded as an increasingly fashionable method to optimize electrocatalytic activity. Various doping foams, especially N connected with other heteroatoms like Nâ€“S,148â€“150 Nâ€“B,151 and Nâ€“P,152,153 are generally reported. A rechargeable ZAB using NPMC as air electrode also showed notable stability (600 cycles at 2 mA cmâˆ’2). Apparently, the N and P dual doping produced a synergistic effect to enhance the catalytic activity for both OER and ORR.

Above all, a rechargeable ZAB based on NOGB-800 displayed a small potential gap of 0.72 V and excellent cycle stability of more than 30 h (Fig. 13c and d). More attractively, two series-connected ZABs could light up three light-emitting diodes (working voltage: 3.0 V) (Fig. 13e), displaying the huge potential for practical application.

4.2.2 Defects in carbon materials. In carbon materials like graphene, the sp2 carbon atoms are arrayed in a hexagonal lattice. However, defects induced by heteroatom doping cannot be avoided in practice. Different constructions of the carbon atoms in the defects can change the spin or charge distribution of the sp2 carbon plane. Hence, the effective adjustment of defects in carbon materials is a valid way to adjust electrocatalytic properties.161,162 For instance, Li et al. proposed a unique gas-foaming method for the manufacture of nitrogen-doped carbon nanosheets (denoted as NCN-1000-5) via pyrolysis and introduced abundant defects and plentiful micropores into the NCNs.163 DFT calculations (Fig. 15a) indicated that the carbon atoms situated at the armchair edge acted as more active sites for the ORR and OER. The ORR performance of NCN-1000-5 is comparable to that of Pt/C, and its OER performance is close to that of RuO2. Due to the very good activity, a rechargeable ZAB (Fig. 15b) using NCN-1000-5 as the cathode catalyst displayed a low voltage gap of 0.77 V and exceptional durability (1000 cycles for 330 h at 10 mA cmâˆ’2). The activity and stability of the NCN-1000-5-based ZABs are some of the best for reported non-metallic catalysts. This work puts forward an original and general approach to synthesize progressive carbon materials with abundant defects.

But this process requires one to employ extra binding annexing agents, which is not only intricate, but also leads to high internal resistance. In order to solve this problem, Huang et al. successfully developed a defect-enriched carbon nanomaterial (denoted as DN-CP@G) with novel coreâ€“shell architecture by in situ stripping of graphene, followed by high-temperature treatment.164 The graphene shell had abundant defect sites, which primarily contributed to produce more high-efficiency active sites for catalytic reactions (Fig. 15c). In view of this, in order to achieve the best catalytic activity, it is highly advisable to generate plenty of defect sites.

Furthermore, increasing the number of oxygen-enriched vacancies could also accelerate the ORR process. For instance, Yan et al. designedly introduced oxygen vacancies into carbon material (denoted as O-NGM-800), and the resultant material possessed excellent oxygen reaction activity (Fig. 15e).165 In addition, for the first time, in situ XRD test was performed to give an immediate survey of the oxygen reaction catalyzed via vacancy-oxidized material during ZAB operation. The ORR performance of O-NGM-800 is similar to that of Pt/C, but the OER performance is poor. The significant change of the peak testified to oxygen vacancies in the catalyst promoting the adsorption of intermediates, thereby promoting the oxygen reaction. Rechargeable ZABs with the O-NGM-800 catalyst as air cathode exhibited high power density (186.3 mW cmâˆ’2) (Fig. 15f) and stable cyclic performance (500 cycles for 80 h at 25 mA cmâˆ’2), and reversibility was confirmed via in situ Raman spectroscopy (Fig. 15g).

Therefore, a lot of researches have shown that metal-free carbon materials usually have excellent ORR activity, conductivity, selectivity, environmental friendliness and low cost, but their bifunctional ORR/OER activity is relatively poor mainly because of inferior OER catalytic activity. The improvement of bifunctional ORR and OER activity of carbon materials is not only due to the doping of heteroatoms, but also to the influence of defects. In metal-free-based carbon materials, it is difficult to prepare catalysts with only one specific type of active center. In most cases, there are abundant active sites in one catalyst with high activity. Therefore, in order to obtain better bifunctional activity, it is necessary to adopt a reasonable design method to prepare bifunctional metal-free carbon catalysts with abundant and high-efficiency active centers. Moreover, another critical issue is carbon corrosion during charging that results in a gradual performance decay mainly because of the lack of stable metal active sites. Since catalyst activity and stability are the key factors to determine ZAB performance, how to improve the â€œactivityâ€ and â€œstabilityâ€ of metal-free carbon catalysts simultaneously is still an important challenge in the future.

Ternary thiospinels (AB2S4) have attracted increasing attention recently as bifunctional oxygen catalysts. Within the ternary thiospinels, two metal cations (A2+ and B3+) occupy the octahedral and tetrahedral positions of a close-packed S-anion lattice, respectively.225,226 For instance, Yu et al. designed a new bifunctional hybrid catalyst of urchin-like NiCo2S4 microspheres embedded in S-doped graphene nanosheets (denoted as S-GNS/NiCo2S4) by a sulfurization process.227 Due to the change of the spin density between carbon atom and adjacent heteroatom caused by the S doping, the resultant S-GNS/NiCo2S4 exhibited excellent bifunctional catalytic activity with a low Î”E of only 0.69 V (Fig. 20c). Additionally, rechargeable ZABs based on S-GNS/NiCo2S4 also showed a maximum power density of 216.3 mW cmâˆ’2 (Fig. 20d) and excellent stability without obvious decay after 100 h. Such design of urchin-like structure and S doping could drastically enhance the surface exposure of active sites, generating abundant mass and charge transport pathways, along with an increase of conductivity and cycling stability.


Metal phosphides. Transition metal phosphides (TMPs) are also considered as promising candidates for noble metal catalysts due to their excellent electrocatalytic properties. TMPs exhibit significant OER activity in alkaline media.228,229 In addition, by integrating TMPs with N-doped carbon material, TMPs/carbon-based electrocatalysts also exhibit high ORR activity.230,231 Thus, it is highly desirable to develop TMPs/carbon-based bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts as air cathodes in rechargeable ZABs. For instance, Song et al. reported defective carbon-CoP nanoparticle hybrids (denoted as CoP-DC) as bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysts obtained through a simple phosphorization reaction using a Co2+-containing polymer hydrogel (Fig. 20e).232 By combining experiments and theoretical DFT calculations, it was found that the interface charge redistribution occurred at the interface between CoP and defective carbon, while the holes gathered on the surface of CoP due to the strong interface coupling, which subsequently contributed to enhanced ORR activity for the DC and enhanced OER activity for the CoP. Notably, as shown in Fig. 20f, a low bifunctional oxygen electrode activity (Î”E = 0.76 V) of CoP-DC was obtained, indicating the key role of interfacial charge redistribution. This work systematically reveals the promoting factor of hybrids from the point of view of interfacial charge redistribution.

Besides cobalt phosphide, Shen et al. facilely prepared large-sized Fe2P/N-doped mesoporous carbon nanosheets (denoted as CNS-900).233 DFT calculations indicated that the adsorption energies of ORR intermediates on Feâ€“N could be boosted by coupling with Feâ€“P. Due to the co-existence of Feâ€“N active sites, Feâ€“P active sites, high N content, and the nanosheet structure, the as-prepared CNS-900 exhibited a superior ORR/OER performance with a low Î”E value of 0.79 V. Further to this, when assembled into a ZAB with CNS-900 as an air cathode, it exhibited good round-trip efficiency of 49% and excellent cycling stability (375 cycles for 125 h). Such P,N co-doped carbon matrix endowed the metal phosphide with abundant catalytic sites and a stable nanostructure, thereby improving the remarkable electrocatalytic performance and excellent cycling stability.


Metal hydroxides. Layered double hydroxides (LDHs), [M1âˆ’x2+Mx3+(OH)2][Anâˆ’]x/nÂ·zH2O, have been considered as effective OER catalytic materials owing to the good intrinsic catalytic activity.234,235 However, the poor ORR performance of LDH-based electrocatalysts limits their application as bifunctional catalysts in rechargeable ZABs.

To ensure LDH-based electrocatalysts maintain better ORR performance with a first-class OER performance, some research has been devoted to the integration of LDH with N-doped carbon materials, which can provide a promising way to further improve electrocatalytic performances.236 For instance, Chen et al. reported the growth of NiFe LDH nanodots (ca. 3 nm) with high loading (47 wt%) on N-doped 3D graphene-like macro-/mesoporous carbon (denoted as nNiFe LDH/3D MPC).237 Studies demonstrated the defect-rich mesopores on the 3D-MPC wall provided abundant adsorption sites for the instant nucleation of precursor cations, and as a size-restricted reactor, it played a key role in the high-load and size-limited growth of NiFe LDH. The results showed that the particle size of NiFe LDH can be adjusted by changing the number of carbon defects, and more defects could lead to a smaller particle size. Due to the intimate interface coupling of NiFe LDH with N-doped carbon, the nNiFe LDH/3D MPC catalyst resulted in an excellent bifunctional ORR/OER activity with a small Î”E of 0.71 V (Fig. 20g). More importantly, as shown in Fig. 20h, when assembled into two ZABs in a series connection with nNiFe LDH/3D MPC as air cathode, a group of LED indicators (â‰ˆ3 V) could be successfully powered, indicating promising application and feasibility.

Besides binary NiFe LDH, Duan et al. also prepared an array-like colloid composite of NiCoIIIFe-LDH/N-GO by an in situ growth route, which consists of ternary NiCoFe LDH (denoted as NiCoIIIFe-LDH) with Co3+ grafted on N-doped graphene oxide (N-GO).238 By exposing more Ni and Fe active sites and introducing high-valence Co3+, the as-prepared NiCoIIIFe-LDH/N-GO exhibited excellent bifunctional catalytic performance with a low Î”E of 0.769 V toward OER and ORR. Thus, this work once again proves that the coupling of LDH with N-doped carbon materials can significantly enhance the bifunctional electrocatalytic performance toward ORR and OER, which opens a new way for the design of bifunctional catalysts and the commercialization of ZABs.

Based on the above discussion, although metal-based catalysts (such as Mâ€“Nâ€“C, metal oxides, chalcogenides and phosphates, and LDHs) possess the advantages of abundant metal active sites, low-cost raw materials and environmental friendliness, their poor electrical conductivity and serious aggregation of nanoparticles seriously limit their catalytic activity. Therefore, in order to promote the role of metal-based materials as promising catalysts for ZABs, it is important to more efficiently utilize catalytic sites in the future. Thus, it is believed that metal-based catalysts have great potential to replace precious metals in rechargeable ZABs.

5. Conclusions and outlook

In conclusion, this review highlights recent research progress in the development of bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts as air cathodes for ZABs. The pros and cons (in terms of activity, stability, conductivity, selectivity, low cost and environmental friendliness) of the introduced pristine MOFs, MOF-derived catalysts, metal-free carbon catalysts, precious metal catalysts, and non-precious metal catalysts for ZABs are summarized in Fig. 21. The main goal of the research highlighted is the design of bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts with high activity, high selectivity, good conductivity, low cost and durability to replace noble metal catalysts for rechargeable ZAB applications to utilize energy in an economic and effective way. In this review, three types of non-noble metal bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts are introduced, namely MOF-based catalysts, metal-free-based carbon catalysts, and metal-based catalysts. In view of that, these three types of non-noble metal bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts have their own advantages and disadvantages.

As for MOF-based catalysts, they include pristine MOFs and their derivatives. Firstly, the construction of 2D Ï€-conjugated conductive MOFs with faster electron transfer and ultrathin 2D MOF nanosheets with coordinated unsaturated metal sites opens up a promising way for the direct utilization of pristine MOFs as pyrolysis-free catalysts. Secondly, compared with the traditional non-porous noble metal catalysts, MOF-derived 3D hierarchical porous carbon matrix possesses good conductivity to enable rapid electron and mass transfer, and atomically dispersed metal active sites in MOFs can lead to the homogeneous dispersion of single atoms, metals, or metal compounds in porous carbon matrix. Therefore, MOFs and their derivatives with numerous advantages of large specific surface areas, porosity, easy doping of heteroatoms, generous metal active sites, and controllable size/morphology/composition will be most promising candidates for precious metal catalysts in ORR and OER. Although MOF-based catalysts have the above advantages, the most pristine MOFs are limited by the disadvantages of high cost, complex synthesis conditions, low conductivity, and structural instability. Thus, the key of development of MOF-based catalysts should be committed to the design and construct of â€œstableâ€ and â€œlow-costâ€ MOF precursors.

In terms of metal-free-based carbon catalysts, they include heteroatom-doped carbon and defective carbon. Heteroatom doping (N, O, S, B, or P) and defect control (vacancy and edge) within the structure can effectively improve the catalytic activity by regulating the electronic structure. In addition, the hierarchical pores and large specific surface area of carbon-based materials can increase the exposure of active centers, and promote the diffusion of oxygen and reactants. Due to their high conductivity, the metal-free-based carbon materials could provide more charge transport channels during electrocatalytic oxygen reactions. Significantly, heteroatom doping can greatly boost the ORR activity leading to a favorable formation of OHâˆ’via the four-electron pathway, in particular, for various N-doped carbon materials. However, the dissatisfactory bifunctional ORR/OER property of carbon nanomaterials may restrict their practical application in ZABs, because the unavoidable carbon corrosion under the high oxidation potentials of the OER may cause catalyst agglomeration. Besides, carbon nanomaterials can also be easily fabricated into various nanostructures, and thus are generally considered ideal conductive supports for metals, metal oxides, and other metal compounds for efficient oxygen catalysis.

As for metal-based catalysts, they include metalâ€“nitrogenâ€“carbon materials and metal-based compounds, which are considered as effective candidates as alternatives to noble metal catalysts due to low cost, easy preparation, wide availability, and multiple valence states. Generally, their catalytic activity can be regulated by changing their morphology, structure, crystal phase, and valence state. However, the poor electrical conductivity and aggregation of nanoparticles seriously limit further practical application as bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts in ZABs. To overcome that, the effective combination of metal active species and low-cost carbon nanomaterials (e.g., CNT and graphene) has been proven to be a promising strategy to achieve the resultant hybrids with high electrocatalytic activity, rapid electron transfer rate, high selectivity and satisfactory stability. Moreover, in order to improve the utilization of active sites without agglomeration of nanoparticles, such strategies as coreâ€“shell design, space confinement and hierarchical structures can be adopted to uniformly load metal catalytic active sites on functionalized carbon supports with strong coupling synergetic effect, which may effectively solve the problems of low electrical conductivity and agglomeration of nanoparticles.

To sum up, practical high-efficiency catalysts should combine the advantages of MOF-based catalysts, metal-free carbon materials, and metal-based catalysts. However, the practical application of bifunctional ORR/OER catalysts in ZABs is heavily hampered by their disadvantages. Thus, we hope the following outlook will be helpful to the future design of bifunctional electrocatalysts for ZABs:

(i) Activity of electrocatalysts

Most of the catalysts have the disadvantages of low and unifunctional catalytic activity. To improve the bifunctional electrocatalytic activity, the following strategies can be adopted: (a) Morphology engineering: the engineering of 3D architectures (e.g. hollow structure, coreâ€“shell structure etc.) is the key to increasing mass transfer efficiency, because it not only exposes rich and easily accessed metal active sites, but also leads to more 3D channels for electron transfer. (b) Heteroatom doping and vacancy engineering: heteroatom doping (N, O, S, B, P and metal atoms) and defect control (vacancy and edge) within a carbon structure can effectively enhance the catalytic activity by modifying the local electronic structure. (c) Single-atom engineering: maximizing the atom-utilization efficiency by designing single-atom catalysts on conductive carbon matrix, thus further improving the catalytic activity of single-atom catalysts. d. Hybridization engineering strategies: the coupling of metal/metal compounds with conductive carbon nanomaterials (e.g., N-doped CNTs, graphene, and conductive carbon black) has been widely introduced to improve electrical conductivity, thereby increasing the electrocatalytic activity. Due to the synergistic effects between the two components, hybrid catalysts often exhibit higher catalytic activity and improved durability compared to the components used alone.

(ii) Durability of electrocatalysts

Another challenge for catalysts is to enhance the electrochemical durability and lifetime. Generally, under high oxidation potential during the charging process, the corrosion of catalysts is unavoidable, which will result in reduced performance and low stability. To solve this problem, the direct growth of highly active catalysts on highly corrosion-resistant conductive substrates (e.g., highly graphitized carbon materials, carbon cloth, nickel foam, etc.) can be considered as an efficient strategy to inhibit the cycle corrosion effect of high-efficiency air electrodes.

(iii) Large-scale production of electrocatalysts

The large-scale production of low-cost and high-efficiency catalyst is of great significance for practical applications or industrial production. However, the high cost of ligands, rigorous synthesis process, and the few synthetic methods have severely impeded the development process from basic research to commercialization. Thus, new synthetic methods, such as electrospinning, spray drying, and template sacrifice methods, are needed to achieve precise control and mass production of electrocatalysts.

(iv) In situ characterization

Further identification of the nature of the active sites is of great significance for the design of efficient and stable bifunctional electrocatalysts. However, due to the lack of abundant theoretical prediction and in situ characterizations, the mechanism of ORR and OER based on bifunctional electrocatalysts is still not very clear. Therefore, it is crucial that the development of in situ characterization techniques can provide real-time insights to monitor the complex reaction processes of oxygen catalysis. In addition, in combination with theoretical modelling and DFT computations, such characterization can help to reveal the reactivity of oxygen catalysts at the atomic level, guiding the rational design of high-performance electrocatalysts.

(v) Solid-state ZABs

In the near future, the development of portable/flexible ZABs which can be applied to wearable cell phones, human-like electronic skins and intelligent bracelets represents a very promising direction. However, the traditional designs of ZABs are flat, rigid and stack designs with aqueous alkaline electrolytes, which do not meet the requirements of portable/flexible applications. Therefore, in future research, the main challenge is how to fabricate solid-state or quasi-solid-state polymer-based electrolytes with high ionic conductivity and good mechanical strength.

Overall, the development of high-efficiency and durable bifunctional ORR/OER electrocatalysts as air cathodes is an important and challenging topic in the field of energy storage and conversion. We hope this review will offer some useful enlightenment for the design and construction of air electrodes for ZABs in the future. No doubt the rapid development of high-efficiency and low-cost bifunctional oxygen catalysts will greatly accelerate the commercialization of rechargeable ZABs.
