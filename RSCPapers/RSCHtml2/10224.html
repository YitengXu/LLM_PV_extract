1 Introduction

This review follows on from last years report1 and is a collection of the highlights from over 1000 research papers, reviews and books revealed by BIDS, Chemical Abstract and publisher website searches for fullerene and carbon nanotube references. Furthermore, it is intended that the papers reviewed reflect the current research trends in the field. Books reviewing aspects of fullerene nanotechnology,2 radical reactions of fullerenes,3 and carbon nanotubes (softback edition)4 have been published. The majority of the papers published in the journal dedicated to fullerene related papers, fullerene nanotubes and carbon nanostructures,5 are not individually referenced within this review. In addition, some journals have published special editions6 and others such as the new journal Nano Lett. frequently carry several fullerene related papers per edition.7 A draft document on fullerene nomenclature was made available on the worldwide web for public comment.8 Reviews of unimolecular gas phase ion chemistry,9 main group element chemistry,10 and the interplay between theory and experiment in solid state inorganic chemistry,11 each of which contain sections on fullerenes, have been published. In addition, the current status and prospects of the science and technology of nanomaterials have been reviewed.12

2 Synthesis, separation and properties of fullerenes

The presence of fullerenes in meteorite samples has been identified by mass spectroscopy.13 These results have, however, been disputed and the discussion as to whether fullerenes are genuinely to be found in meteorites continues.14 The formation of [60]fullerene by the cyclodehydrogenation of a polyarene containing all sixty carbon atoms has been shown to occur under mass spectroscopic conditions.15 A similar approach used by independent authors employing macrocyclic polyalkynes has led to the formation of [36]fullerene16 and [60]fullerene17 under gas phase conditions. Glow discharge reactors have been used to pyrolyse chloroform resulting in the formation of fullerenes and chlorinated carbon clusters.18 Moreover, the authors report that five isomers of [60]fullerene, identified using HPLC and mass spectrometry, are formed under these reaction conditions.19 The density-functional based tight-binding method has been used to show that pentagon adjacencies are stabilised by functionalisation in small fullerenes such as [36]fullerene.20 The Hückel bond orders of [60]fullerene isomers give a reliable graph-theory based illustration of the bond order pattern for the majority of isomers although only a small subset have bond orders that correspond to a Kekulé structure.21 A procedure for assigning Cahn–Ingold–Prelog descriptors to stereocentres in spheroalkanes, the fully hydrogenated form of a trivalent, polyhedral carbon framework, a class of molecules which includes the fullerenes, has been developed.22 The predicted nucleus-independent chemical shifts (NICS) of BN-doped fullerenes show that these species are more aromatic than [60]fullerene.23 Semi-empirical AM1 and MNDO methods have been used to examine the relative stabilities of several isomers of BN substituted fullerenes of the general formula C60−2x(BN)x.24

A review of the bonding present in fullerenes and fullerides has been published.25 Similarly, a comprehensive account of the spherical aromaticity of fullerenes has been published and the conclusion that there is delocalisation of π-electrons in fullerenes, which provides some energetic stabilisation, drawn.26 Previous calculations of the binding energy, Ea, of C60+ have been critically re-examined and the average value of Ea found to be 10.0 ± 0.2 eV.27 A comprehensive set of solubility data of [60]fullerene has been treated by multivariate stepwise linear regression and increasing molar volume and solvent polarity found to decrease solubility, whilst increasing electron pair donation ability and polarisability were found to enhance solubility.28 HPLC employing a chiral stationary phase, amylose tris(3,5-dimethylphenylcarbamate) bound to silica gel, has been used to resolve the enantiomers of [76]fullerene and CD spectra obtained.29 Density functional theory has been used to optimise the geometry of isolated pentagon rule (IPR) isomers of [82]fullerene and their 13C NMR spectra calculated, which were found to be in good agreement with the experimentally observed δ values for the C2 isomer.30 The same authors have applied the same technique to calculate the 13C NMR spectra of isomers of [84]fullerene.31 Arc produced soot obtained from dysprosium-doped graphite composite rods was found to contain a new isomer of C2 symmetric [84]fullerene isomer.32 Six isomers of [84]fullerene have been characterised by electrochemistry, electron spin resonance spectroscopy and molecular energy level calculations.33 Whilst the first electrochemical reductions of thin films of [84]fullerene on electrodes have been reported and found to result in the generation of multi-charged anions.34 The dimerisation of [60]fullerene has been subjected to symmetry considerations with the result that [60]fullerene is predicted to be in equilibrium with the known stable [2 + 2] dimer at moderate temperatures but this situation is not observed in the gas phase for thermodynamic reasons.35 Fluorescent and triplet absorption spectra of the [60]fullerene trimer, C180, are similar to those of 1,2-mono-adducts of [60]fullerene whilst other excited state properties of C180 have a shorter fluorescence lifetime due to non-radiative processes.36 High pressure and high temperature treatment of [60]fullerene thin films on steel transforms the fullerene layer to a superhard and superelastic material.37

3 Endohedral fullerenes

The EPR spectrum previously assigned to the endohedral [60]fullerene species, Cs@(COT)–C602− (COT = cyclooctatriene)38 has now been corrected by the authors and is not due to an endohedral fullerene derivative but to a reaction of a complex formed with Cs+ after reaction between [60]fullerene and the solvent, hexamethylphosphoramide, used for the investigation.39 Ba@C74 has been produced for the first time using a radiofrequency furnace.40 The single crystal X-ray structure of a trimetallic nitride unit encapsulated in [78]fullerene, Sc3N@C78, has been obtained.41 Similarly a scandium carbide endohedral metallofullerene, (Sc2C2)@C84, has been obtained and characterised using 13C NMR spectroscopy.42 A perfect pentagonal–dodecahedral charge density of La2+ has been observed in La2@C80 which is the result of a highly selective trajectory of the two La atoms hopping along the hexagonal rings of the [80]fullerene cage.43 Samarium-containing endohedral fullerenes have been prepared using an arc discharge methodology and soot enriched with these endohedral fullerenes obtained by successive extractions using carbon disulfide and pyridine.44 Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) has been used to show that Tb@C82 endohedral fullerenes have great mobility on a [60]fullerene film and form a close packed layer after aggregating along the edges of terraces of [60]fullerene films.45

4 Organic chemistry

There has once again been a considerable number of papers investigating aspects of the organic chemistry of fullerenes, only a fraction of which are reviewed here. The unequivocal assignment of all of the carbon atoms of 10% 13C enriched 1,2-methano[60]fullerene 61,61-dicarboxylate by 2D inadequate NMR spectroscopy has been achieved.46 Both thermal first-order and photochemical zero-order kinetics have been observed in the rearrangement of [6,5] open fulleroids to [6,6] closed fullerenes, which proceeds via a biradical intermediate under unimolecular conditions.47 Semi-empirical and density functional methods reveal that the addition of azides to [60]fullerene proceeds to give a stable triazoline intermediate which subsequently undergoes stepwise decomposition.48 A series of poly-adducts, hexakis, heptakis and octakis, of [60]fullerene have been prepared by exploitation of the bond specific reactivity of C2v-symmetrical pentakis-adducts of [60]fullerene using addition of diazomethane.49 In addition, high-level ab initio and density functional calculations are shown to provide strong evidence that a common mechanism, an eight-electron, orbital symmetry-controlled [2πs + 2πs + 2σa + 2σs] concerted process via an aromatic transition state, is responsible for the extrusion of nitrogen.49 Deuterium labeling of methoxy[(trimethylsilyl)ethoxy]carbene strongly suggests that a silacyclopropane carbocation is responsible for product formation when the carbene reacts with [60]fullerene.50 Deuterium labeling has also been used to show that [4 + 2] cycloadditions of [60]fullerene proceed by a concerted mechanism via a symmetrical transition state.513He NMR spectroscopy has been used to show that there are one mono-adduct, six bis-adducts, eleven tris-adducts and ten tetrakis-adducts in the equilibrium mixture formed after reaction of [60]fullerene with 9,10-dimethylanthracene.52 Mono-adducts of [60]fullerene from reaction with several anthracenes have been prepared and their thermolytic decomposition in the solid state found to produce a mixture of the parent [60]fullerene and, regioselectively, the antipodal bis-adduct.53 By contrast, eight bis-regioisomers resulting from the cycloaddition of benzyne and [60]fullerene have been isolated and characterised.54 Solid state reaction of [60]fullerene with di(2-pyridyl)-1,2,4,5-tetrazine results in the formation of a reactive mono-adduct which undergoes further reaction producing a dimeric fullerene incorporating a 2,3-diazobycyclo[2.2.2]oct-2-ene framework, further photochemical reaction of this product results in a [2 + 2] cycloaddition between the two [60]fullerene cages (Fig. 1).55 The same reactive intermediate has been prepared independently and found to undergo 1,4-hydrogenation and a rearrangement from the fused 4,5-dihydropyridazine into a 4,5-dihydropyrazole [60]fullerene adduct upon reaction with diprotic nucleophiles.56

A review of new fullerene derivatives and ring opened fullerene molecules has been published.57 The solid state reaction of [60]fullerene with phthalazine results in a [60]fullerene dimer, by contrast the liquid phase reaction produces a [4 + 2] mono-adduct which undergoes two further spontaneous rearrangements resulting finally in a ring opened [60]fullerene derivative having an eight-membered ring as part of the fullerene cage structure.58 The foregoing ring opened adduct undergoes an additional cleavage under photochemical conditions in the presence of oxygen affording a fullerene derivative with enol and ketone groups situated within a twelve-membered ring orifice.59 Similarly, 4,6-dimethyl-1,2,3-triazine undergoes a sequence of cycloadditions with [60]fullerene which, after additional cleavage under photochemical conditions in the presence of oxygen, affords a triketone with a twelve-membered ring orifice.60 A tetra-substituted bis[60]fulleroid has been shown to produce a diketone derivative with a twelve-membered ring embedded within the fullerene cage structure after photo-oxidation.61 A ring-opened [60]fullerene bislactam has been used to encapsulate either helium or molecular hydrogen and encapsulation confirmed by NMR studies.62

Dimethylsulfonium α-formylalkylides react with [60]fullerene producing formyl-substituted methanofullerenes and the formyl group shown to undergo typical reactions with, for example, aromatic amines.63 Similarly synthesis of methano[60]fullerenes has been achieved using the fluoride ion mediated reaction with silylated nucleophiles.64 Thiocarbonyl ylides undergo 1,3-dipolar cycloadditions with [60]fullerene resulting in tetrahydrothiophene-fused derivatives which can be oxidized to the sulfoxide using meta-chloroperbenzoic acid.65 Only the C2v isomer of two [78]fullerene isomers, C2v and D3, react with disilaranes producing a mono-adduct.66 The [60]fullerene dimer formed as the result of a [2 + 2] cycloaddition has been subjected to reaction under Bingel conditions resulting in the formation of five regioisomeric mono-adducts and several bis-adducts.67 Reductive electrolysis of mono-methano[60]fullerene adducts in THF produces good yields of bis-adducts with a unique regiochemistry.68 Tandem reductive ring opening of tethered bis-methano[60]fullerenes using sodium cyanoborohydride under protic solvent conditions leads to the formation of 1,2-dihydro[60]fullerylglycines.69

Fullerene-based amino acids and peptides have been reviewed.70 In addition, their roles in materials and medicinal chemistry have been highlighted and several new molecules synthesised from amino-functionalised [60]fulleropyrrolidines.71 A hybrid fullerene–trimethoxyindole–oligonucleotide, which is designed to have specific DNA binding activity, has been prepared using the same synthetic methodology.72 Malonodiserinolamide [60]fullerenes, C60[C(COSer)2]n where n = 4, 5 or 6 and Ser = 2-amino-1,3-propanediol, suitable for biological evaluation, having water solubility of >240 mg L−1, have been synthesised.73 Similarly α-D-mannosyl derivatives have been synthesised by azido addition to [60]fullerene and are shown to decrease the activity for both aggregating erythrocytes and in binding to β-D-galactopyranoside specific lectin (RCA120).74 Steroid containing [60]fullerene adducts have also been prepared by the Diels–Alder reaction of siloxydienes with [60]fullerene and one of these derivatives shown to inhibit the reconstituted sarcoplastic reticulum (SR) Ca2+–ATPase in soybean phospholipid liposomes.75 A new fullerene transfection agent bearing multiple functional groups has been produced by diastereoselective double cycloadditions of a biscyclopropenone acetal and shown to efficiently, comparable to that of a nor-analogue, transfer extra-cellular DNA into mammalian cells.76 The same authors have used atomic force microscopy (AFM) to show that a tetra-amino[60]fullerene transfection agent folds supercoiled DNA into a single molecule condensate through adhesion of DNA double strands.77 One of a series of methanophosphonate and methanophosphonic acid [60]fullerene derivatives, prepared using the Bingel methodology, has photo-induced cytotoxic properties, which the authors suggest implies that molecules of this type may be useful in photodynamic therapy.78 Similarly, polymeric fullerocyclodextrins, synthesised by reaction of diamines with [60]fullerene complexed with cyclodextrin, have efficient DNA-cleavage properties in the presence of visible light.79 Fullerene malonates have been shown to interact with nitric oxide synthase (NOS) distorting its structure, and in doing so, provide a unique mechanism for the isoform-selective inhibition of nNOS.80 Studies on other water soluble [60]fullerene malonates indicate that this class of molecule is capable of eliminating both superoxide anion and H2O2 and are effective inhibitors of lipid peroxidation.81 The photophysical properties of several bis-pyrrolidine derivatives of [60]fullerene have been shown to be similar to the parent fullerene and that aggregation is avoided, which may be a useful property in future biological evaluation of fullerene-based molecules, by incorporation of chains and charged units into the pyrrolidine unit.82 Water soluble hexa-sulfonated [60]fullerene has been subjected to several biological studies and shown to have neuro- and cardio-protective effects.83

The fluorination of fullerenes has been reviewed.84 A new fluorinated derivative, C60F20, which has a structure comprising of two dehydrocorannulene caps held together by a (CF)20 chain has been isolated.85 A facile migration of fluorine about the [60]fullerene cage has been identified during high temperature fluorination of [60]fullerene and this observation used to explain the formation of a limited number of regioisomers of C60F3686 and C60F48.86b An independent study has found that fluorination of [60]fullerene with binary metal fluorides is thermodynamically controlled and that C60F44 is formed by reaction with silver(II) fluoride.87 A mixture of two isomers of C60F48 has been characterised by single crystal X-ray diffraction studies.88 Several binary lead fluorides and their complexes with alkali and alkaline earth metal fluorides have been investigated as [60]fullerene fluorinating agents89a and seven bis(oxahomo)[60]fullerene derivatives of C60F18 have been isolated by HPLC from the reaction of [60]fullerene with K2PtF6.89b Perfluorinated [70]fullerene, C70F56, has been synthesised using fluorine gas and [70]fullerene.90 Anthracene is reported to add readily to C60F18 resulting in the formation of two major regioisomeric mono-adducts of C60F18 in which all of the fluorine atoms remain intact; one of the two isomers is unstable and decomposes to the more stable regioisomeric cycloadduct of C60F18 and the parent C60F18.91 By contrast, reaction of C60F18 under Bingel-like conditions results in the loss of three fluorine atoms from the fullerene cage and the formation of a [18]transannulene chromophore on the fullerene cage surface.92 Reaction of C60Cl6 with alcohols results in the formation of 1,4-dialkoxy[60]fullerene derivatives, whereas reaction with alcohols in the presence of sodium alkoxide results in the formation of C60(OR)5Cl (where R = alkyl group of the alcohol).93 Arylated [60]fullerenes, C60(Ph)5Cl, are found to undergo spontaneous oxidation94 whilst a cage-opened bisepoxide ketone is one of the products formed after reaction of C60Cl6 with methyllithium followed by hydrolysis.95 A series of arylated [70]fullerene products has been isolated from the reaction of C70Cl10 with benzene96 and the electrochemical96 and photophysical properties of the derivatives reported.97 In addition, two polyarylated [70]fullerene diols, C70Ph8(OH)2, have been characterised.98 The [5,6]-open oxide of [60]fullerene has been isolated by the photolysis of C60O3, a closed [6,6] ozone [60]fullerene adduct.99 Hydrolysis of the products from the reaction of [60]- or [70]-fullerenes with RuO4 results in the formation of 1,2-diols of [60]- and [70]-fullerenes.100

A review detailing the synthesis and characterisation of fullerene hydrides has been published.101 A novel gas phase chemical vapour modification (CVM) technique has been employed to produce the known C3v isomer of C60H18.102 C60H36 has been shown to start to decompose at about 200 °C and can reduce, for example, anthracene when nickel powder is used as a catalyst.103 The theoretical fullerene derivative C60H60 has been calculated by ab initio molecular orbital theory to be unstable, decomposing exothermically to C60H44.104 The chemically generated fulleride dianion, C602−, reacts with halo-containing derivatives to produce 1,2- and 1,4-adducts with long chain derivatives and 1,2-fused ring [60]fullerene derivatives with 1,3-dihaloalkanes.105 Highly stabilised carbocations, such as tri-tert-butylcyclopropenylium ion, did not react with fulleride anions, instead leading to the formation of a salt, whereas effective C–C bond formation between the fullerene and carbocation is observed with more reactive carbocationic species.106 A review of the key role played by C59N+ in azaheterofullerene chemistry has been published107 and pentakis-methano adducts of the azafullerene, C59N, having an octahedral Cs symmetrical addition pattern, have been synthesised.108

5 Materials chemistry

The formation of hexakis-adducts of [60]fullerene has been reviewed109 and several new adducts of this type, one of which was used to prepare a supramolecular [60]fullerene-based dimer, have been synthesised using the 9,10-dimethylanthracene template synthesis strategy.110 A highly symmetrical decakis-methano-adduct of the [2 + 2] [60]fullerene dimmer, C120, has been synthesised.111 The uses of fullerene-containing molecules in organic–inorganic nanocomposites,112a nanocomposites112b and in conjugated polymer containing solar cells has been reviewed.113 Several thiophene and oligothiophene containing [60]fullerene derivatives have been synthesised by independent research groups,114a,b,d their photophysical properties examined114a,b,c and their use in, for example, photodiodes investigated.114a In addition, self-assembled monolayers of oligothiophene–[60]fulleropyrrolidine dyads have been shown to be stable and electroactive.114d Several tetrathiafulvalene derivatives of [60]fullerene have also been synthesised using cycloaddition techniques.115 Unusual linear electrooptic effects (LEO) have been observed in some tetrathiafulvalene-containing [60]fullerenes.116 The same authors have also described an electrolytic electrospray ionisation mass spectrometry technique for the analysis and investigation of the gas phase reactivity of this type of molecule.117 A related [60]fullerene dimer, bridged by a cyanine dye unit, has been synthesised using the cycloaddition of an azomethine ylide and examined using STM.118 Poly(phenylenevinylene) (PPV) derivatives covalently linked to [60]fullerene have been synthesised as potential materials for use in solar cells and light emitting diodes and their photophysical properties examined.119 [60]Fullerene containing rigid dendrimeric meta-substituted phenylenevinylenes bearing a variety of electron donating groups have been synthesised and the competitive versus sequential energy and electron transfer events shown to vary between the derivatives.120 Similar derivatives have been synthesised independently and shown to have improved electron affinity with respect to the parent [60]fullerene.121 Methano[60]fullerenes which exhibit a photoinduced electron-transfer reaction have been used as the active layer of a photovoltaic cell.122 Oligophenyleneethynylene (OPE) derivatives of [60]fullerene have also been prepared and the molecules shown to have the characteristic properties associated with materials suitable for use in photovoltaic cells.123 Either the parent fullerene, [60]fullerene, or fullerene derivatives have been found to be suitable for incorporation, or used, in photovoltaic cells with conjugated polymers.124

Several new [60]fullerene dyads and triads containing a range of electron donor units such as, phytochlorin, and differing linking chains employing varying methodologies for covalent bonding to [60]fullerene have been synthesised and the photophysical properties of the molecules probed.125 Axial ligand coordination has been used to investigate “tail-on” and “tail-off” binding in porphyrin–[60]fullerene dyads.126 Similarly face-to-face and edge-to-face alignment of the π-electron systems of the donor and acceptor moieties in [60]fullerene dyads have been shown to play an important role in electron transfer.127 Self-assembled monolayers of fullerene dyads128 and triads129 have been prepared and the light harvesting and electron transfer properties of the films shown to mimic natural photosynthetic processes but, to date, have low incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE).129a In addition, simple complexing of diazabicyclooctane (DABCO) by [60]fullerene triads leads to rigid assemblies that display prolonged charge-separated states.129b

Tetraphenylporphyrins and metal(II) and iron(III) tetraphenylporphyrins form complexes with [60]- and [70]-fullerenes which do not show any charge transfer in the ground state.130 Similarly, chiral, effectively D4 symmetric, porphyrins have been employed to form chiral non-covalent, non-polar complexes with [60]fullerene.131 Short distances have been observed in the crystal structure of the complex formed between [60]fullerene and the C4 symmetrical cobalt tetrakis(di-tert-butylphenyl)porphyrin.132 Saddle-shaped tetraazaannulates also form complexes with [60]fullerene in which, as 13C NMR studies have shown, the [60]fullerene molecules undergo rapid pseudo-isotropic rotation at room temperature.133 Extremely high association constants (108 M−1) have been observed in rhodium(III) porphyrin cyclic dimers in which it is likely there is a rhodium to fullerene charge transfer interaction.134 A non-covalently linked meso-tetraphenylporphyrinatozinc(II) and [60]fullero-pyrrolidine complex has been studied using single crystal X-ray diffraction studies.135 An independent study shows that there is efficient charge separation, with lifetimes of about 10 µs in THF and several hundreds of microseconds in benzonitrile at room temperature in the same complex.136 Self-assembled molecular layers of hexagonally ordered [60]fullerene and quadratically ordered copper phthalocyanine molecules co-exist on Au(111).137 Large aggregates of [60]fullerene molecules and gold nano-particles capped with thiolated-cyclodextrin hosts are formed in water.138 Octanethiol derivatised [60]fulleropyrrolidine molecules also form complexes with gold clusters which have a narrow size distribution of 2 nm diameter.139

The transfer of Langmuir films of [60]fullerene onto iodine-modified Au(111) surfaces at an air-water interface followed by electrochemical removal and replacement of iodine adlayers with [60]fullerene adlayers in solution, the electrochemical replacement method, has been used to produce high quality [60]fullerene films.140 The interaction of [60]fullerene with porphyrins has been utilized to enhance the gelation properties of Zn(II) porphyrin appended cholesterol derivatives and gelation was found to depend on the odd or even number of methylene units in the porphyrin and cholesterol linking chain.141 A substituted resorcinarene has been used to incorporate [60]fullerene into Langmuir–Blodgett films in which, after introduction of poly(3-hexylthiophene), photoinduced charge transfer was observed.142 Similarly efficient photocurrent generation has been observed between the complex of [60]fullerene and cationic homocalix[3]arene after doping with an anionic porphyrin polymer.143 Naphthalene-based calixeranes, which are inherently chiral, have been used to complex [60]fullerene.144 Silver cation complexation results when a calix[5]arene substituted with bipyridine units has a sufficiently large cavity for complexing [60]- or [70]-fullerenes.145 A spectrophotomeric and thermodynamic study of [60]- and [70]-fullerenes with a number of calix[n]arenes has been performed.146 The formation of a stable inclusion complex of [60]fullerene with β-cyclodextrin has been reported for the first time.147 Electrodes chemically modified with the [60]fullerene–(γ-cyclodextrin)2 complex show two pairs of stable redox peaks and the first redox couple is capable of electrocatalysing dehalogenation of some organohalo acids.148 Pentakis-substituted corannulene derivatives have been synthesised and employed as concave hosts for [60]fullerene.149

A methano[60]fullerene substituted with triethylene glycol moieties terminated with decyl chains has been used to form a complex with silver ions which has a large optical limiting effect.150 [60]Fullerene derivatives bearing either α-, β- or γ-cyclodextrin units demonstrate an efficient quenching effect on the bound photo-luminescent guest rhodamine B.151 A [60]fulleropyrrolidine bearing a cone-shaped calix[4]arene has been synthesised and is able to perform mechanical-like movements of relatively large amplitudes.152 Liquid crystalline [60]fulleropyrrolidine153a and methano[60]fullerene153b,c with smectic A phases, have been synthesised.153 Cholesterol-containing polymethano[60]fullerene derivatives have been used to prepare Langmuir and Langmuir–Blodgett films.154 Langmuir and Langmuir–Blodgett films of a dendron containing five bis-methano[60]fullerene units have also been prepared and attached to a dendron functionalised with ethylene glycol chains.155 Bis-methano[70]fullerenes containing crown ether moieties form stable 1∶1 or stable 1∶2 host–guest complexes with alkali metal ions have been prepared and the complexation of the metal ion shown to have a significant influence on the first reduction potential of the fullerene moiety.156 A [60]fulleropyrrolidine amphiphile with a terminal ammonium group forms either fibrous or disk-like aggregates which the authors speculate may represent a biomembrane-like carbon nanosuperstructure.157 Another amphiphilic [60]fulleropyrrolidine derivative has a strong tendency to self-aggregate in binary or ternary solutions providing that the Hildebrand polarity index of the solvent is higher than about 15.158 [60]Fullerene has been covalently attached to a self-assembled monolayer of glutathione (GSH) formed on a gold (Au) electrode and changes in the pH result in opening and closing of pinholes in the [60]fullerene containing monolayer.159 Several [60]fulleropyrrolidine derivatives, such as dumbbells and star shaped tris-, tetrakis- and hexakis-[60]fullerene, have been synthesised and each shown to form stable Langmuir–Blodgett films.160 Larger aggregates of a [60]fullerene cyclodextrin conjugate exist in dilute solutions than in more concentrated ones.161 The electrophoretic behaviour of a water soluble methano[60]fullerene dendrimer has been examined.162 Radical triplet pairs, characterised by time resolved EPR spectroscopy, are formed by host-guest interaction of an excited methano[60]fullerene bearing a crown ether group and an ammonium aminoxyl radical.163 Quadruple H-bonded methano[60]fullerene164 and [60]fulleropyrrolidine165 dimers have been synthesised by independent research groups.

Stable colloidal dispersions with narrow size distributions of [60]fullerene have been prepared by mixing a solution of [60]- or [70]fullerenes in a good fullerene solvent with a polar organic solvent.166 Stable aqueous dispersions of [60]- or [70]-fullerene have been prepared by injecting a saturated solution of fullerene in tetrahydrofuran (THF) into water followed by removal of THF.167 Films of [60]fullerene clusters have been deposited on electrodes under the influence of a dc electric field.168 Two-dimensional [60]fullerene domains on a self-assembled monolayer of alkylthiols has been investigated by STM and are found to form an ideal two-dimensional system which has a second topological order originating from the orientational degrees of freedom.169 A lipid bilayer has been modified by saturation with [60]fullerene and used as an electrochemical sensor for detection of neutral odour molecules.170

6 Polymer chemistry

A review of advanced polymeric composite [60]fullerene and nanotubes-based materials has been published.171 Polystyrene radicals bearing 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinyloxy (TEMPO) end groups add to [60]fullerene forming a mixture of products containing one or two chains bound to one [60]fullerene molecule.172 Stable linear and star block copolymers of styrene and isoprene-containing [60]fullerene have been synthesised by anionic polymerisation followed by anionic addition to the fullerene.173 Similarly polymers with good photoconductivity containing [60]fullerene, styrene and acrylamide have been prepared.174 Soluble star-like polymers have also been prepared by the reaction of a [60]fullerene derivative with pendant N,N-diethyldithiocarbamate groups and methyl methacrylate or styrene.175 End-capping of poly(ethylene oxide) with [60]fullerene results in a dramatic reduction in the overall crystallization rates compared with those of the fullerene-free polymer.176 Films of poly(vinyl formamide-co-vinylamine) adsorbed on silica or titanium dioxide particles react with [60]fullerene producing novel hybrid materials with controllable versatile surface properties.177 A hydrophilic polymeric fullerene-containing material has been prepared by reacting Sephadex® G-200 with [60]fullerene and found to be a good candidate for use as a singlet-oxygen sensitiser.178 Thin films of [60]fullerene doped poly(methyl methacrylate) exhibit intense electroluminescence ranging from the visible to the near infrared.179 [60]Fullerene shows antioxidant properties similar to those of a standard antioxidant such as the sterically hindered phenol Irganox 1076.180

7 Organometallic chemistry

8 Charged fullerenes, magnetism and superconductivity

Two-dimensionally polymerised highly orientated rhombohedral [60]fullerene is a semiconductor if prepared below 923 K but exhibits metallic behaviour if prepared at higher temperatures.187 The same polymer of [60]fullerene has anisotropic electrical properties due to the layered structure of the material.188,189 Furthermore, the material has been shown to exhibit properties typical of ferromagnets and has temperature dependences of the saturation and remanent magnetization which indicate a Curie temperature (Tc) of about 500 K.189 The collection of a complete magnetisation data set near Tc of the ferromagnetic complex of [60]fullerene and tetrakis(dimethylamino)ethylene (TDAE) has allowed the susceptibility and magnetization critical exponents to be calculated.190 In addition, as the pressure on a sample of [60]fullerene–TDAE is increased the Tc decreases, reaching zero at a pressure of about 9 kbar.191

The lattice of [60]fullerene crystals has been expanded by intercalation with chloroform and bromoform and the latter becomes superconducting at a critical temperature (Tc) of 117 K after high densities of holes and electrons have been induced by gate doping in a field effect transistor.192 Superconductivity of hole and electron-doped [60]fullerenes has been subjected to a quantitative analysis and the high Tc for the former found to be due to an intrinsic correlation between two parameters κ (a reduced Fermi-vector length) and β (occurring in the theta functions for conduction states).193 Electric field-doped crystals of [70]fullerene exhibit superconductivity at 7 K after being doped with about four electrons.194

High resolution neutron powder diffraction of the polymer phase of AC60 (A = K or Rb) shows that there is significant distortion of the [60]fullerene molecule from its original icosahedral symmetry.195 Semiempirical paramatised (PM3) and density functional methods (B3LYP) have been used to analyse the rotational potential energy of C602− and (C59N)2 and each dimer shown to exist as equilibrium mixtures of anti- and gauche-conformers.196 A new tetragonal two-dimensional polymer phase of Li4C60 has been prepared at a temperature of 573 K.197 The first polymeric phase, which has a zig zag structure linked by [2 + 2] cycloadditions, of [70]fullerene has been synthesised by treatment of hexagonally packed [70]fullerene crystals at a pressure of 2 GPa and temperature of 573 K.198 An alumina template with a pore diameter of 100–300 nm has been used to produce ordered arrays of [70]fullerene from a colloidal suspension of C70−.199 A linear polymeric phase of C702− has been identified by single-crystal X-ray analysis of the barium salt, [Ba(NH3)9]C70·7NH3.20013C NMR studies shows that the hexa-anion of [70]fullerene, C706−, has unusual charge distribution.201 The absence of superconductivity has been reported in samples of K3+δC84 salts, for both D2 and D2d isomers, and has been attributed to the extensive anion orientational, and cation positional, disorder.202

The kinetics of the electron transfer reaction between C60− and aqueous Fe(CN)63− depend on the interfacial potential drop consistent with the Marcus inverted region behaviour for electron transfer.203 Electrochemical reduction of [60]fullerene molecules in the inclusion complex with cyclotroveratrylene results in the C60− ions generated being liberated from the inclusion complex.204 Anomalous upper-excited fluorescence is observable from [60]fullerene encapsulated in a Y zeolite.205 The anion of pentaphenyl[60]fullerene, C60Ph5−, is water soluble and forms large spherical bilayer vesicles.206 Formation of the radical cation of [60]fullerene, C60˙+, by photo-induced electron transfer from p-chloranil and p-benzoquinone is promoted by Sc3+.207

9 Carbon nanotubes and related materials

Single-walled (SWNTs) and double-walled (DWNTs) carbon nanotubes have been produced using a variety of chemical vapour deposition techniques.208 Multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWNTs) have been produced from amorphous carbon using a hydrothermal technique at 800 °C and 100 MPa.209 In addition solid-state metathesis between carbon halides and lithium acetylide catalyzed by cobalt dichloride enables the rapid synthesis of MWNTs.210 MWNTs have also been prepared from the reaction of supercritical CO2 with Mg in a closed cell at 1000 °C.211 A combinatorial approach has been developed for the identification of catalysts for the growth of MWNTs.212 An independent study of catalysts suitable for nanotube formation has revealed that there is an upper limit in the size of 8.5 nm for effective nanotube production.213 Aligned carbon nanotubes arrays have been prepared by chemical vapour deposition214 and electrophoretic215 techniques using a variety of catalysts. Nanotubes with different diameters have been joined216 and multibranched carbon nanotubes produced using a porous anodic aluminium oxide template.217 Porous silica films have also been employed to direct the growth of MWNTs.218 Thermolysis of [60]fullerene results in the self-assembly of SWNTs into crystals containing ordered arrays of tubes with identical diameters and chirality; weak forces such as applied magnetic fields are thought to play an important role in this self-assembly process.219 It has been shown that the early stages of carbon nanotube growth during CVD are dominated by superstructures of self-assembled nanotube domains.220 A detailed study of the conditions necessary for the synthesis of coiled carbon nanotubes by CVD of ferrocene has been published.221 Vertically aligned SWNTs are conveniently and easily used in the production of SWNT tips for scanning probe microscopes.222 Whilst MWNTs in pillar shaped columns have been grown by pyrolysis of iron(II) phthalocyanine.223 A new morphology of carbon nanotubes, in which an outer carbon nanotube is densely packed with small helicoidal carbon nanotubes, has been prepared under CVD conditions.224 Tetrahedral or other carbon ‘onions’ have been produced by either electron beam irradiation of amorphous carbon225 or graphite rod arc discharge under water, respectively.226

The mechanism of growth of MWNTs has been probed by use of 13C2H4 as a carbon source after initial use of 12C2H4 which results in distinct regions of the nanotubes being either 12C or 13C rich.227 Growth barrier technology has been used to produce direct nanowiring of carbon nanotubes at relatively low temperatures, 650–750 °C.228 Nanotubes can be soldered together, forming a graphitic junction, by irradiation with an electron beam in a scanning electron microscope (SEM).229 Transmisison electron microscopy (TEM) of the MWNTs results in breaking of the nanotubes with some covalent bond formation between the broken graphene layer and the inner layer observed.230 Heat treatment of SWNTs results in an increase in diameter of the nanotubes from 0.79 to 1.47 nm.231

Hydrogenation of SWNTs and MWNTs using liquid ammonia and sodium metal results in nanotubes with average composition of C11H.232 Several studies have examined the mechanism of hydrogen sorption on the walls of SWNTs.233 Alkali-metal doped nanotubes are calculated to possess a high hydrogen uptake, a fact which is predicted to be due to the charge transfer from the alkali metal to the nanotube, which in turn polarizes the hydrogen molecules.234 MWNTs have been used as efficient sorbents for dioxin removal.235

High dispersions of platinum236a,b,c and palladium, silver and gold nanoparticles236b on carbon nanotubes, useful as potential catalysts236a,b or for use in fuel cells,236c have been studied independently by several groups. Palladium modified SWNTs which exhibit electrical conductance modulation upon exposure to small concentrations of hydrogen gas in air have been used as molecular sensors for hydrogen.237 MWNTs have been doped with lithium238 and iron nanoparticles239 independently and are calculated to be at the surface of the tubes rather than in between the tube wall layers. Tungsten disulfide240 and niobium disulfide241 coated MWNTs have been produced by initial encapsulation of the MWNTs within metal oxide layers which are then converted to disulfide layers using hydrogen disulfide.

Methods for the purification of SWNTs by metal catalysed oxidation have been developed.242 Grinding of SWNTs with cyclodextrin cuts the nanotubes into shortened sections.243 The solubility of SWNTs in a number of organic solvents has been examined and 1,2-dichlorobenzene found to be a good solvent dissolving 95 mg L−1.244 Oxidatively shortened SWNTs have been assembled on silver245 and iron surfaces246 using the functional groups present at the ends of the nanotubes to complex with the metals. Carbon nanotube rings have been synthesised by linking the carboxylic acid and hydroxyl functional groups at the two ends of a nanotube using dicyclohexylcarbodiimide.247 Oxidatively shortened SWNTs form stable colloidal dispersions in electrolyte solutions.248 Full length SWNTs become soluble in common organic solvents once the carboxylic acid groups present have been ionically functionalised with reagents containing ammonium functional groups.249 Condensation of the carboxylic acid groups in SWNTs with 2-aminoethanesulfonic acid improves the aqueous solubility of the nanotubes considerably.250 SWNT and MWNT containing dendrimers251 and oligomeric poly(ethylene glycol) have been synthesised by functionalisation of the carboxylic acid and hydroxyl groups present in the nanotubes.252 Similarly, polymer containing nanotubes have been prepared by ether derivatisation of the carboxylic acid and hydroxyl groups present in the nanotubes,253 a process reversible by acid or base catalysed hydrolysis,254 or by reaction of the functional group free nanotube side-walls.255 Chromatographic purification of functionalised SWNTs using GPC256 and HPLC257 has been reported. Solutions of purified SWNTs and MWNTs are readily soluble in aniline and the solutions show strong fluorescence.258 Adsorption of several alkylamines with SWNTs produces either full or modulated chemical gating.259 Doping of SWNTs with amine-rich polyamines results in the formation of n-type field effect transistors.260 Composite materials containing polyaniline and MWNTs have been prepared and the product shown to have enhanced electronic properties.261 A radio frequency glow discharge plasma has been used to functionalise carbon nanotubes with acetaldehyde and the resultant material further reacted with amines to form nanotubes containing Schiff base groups situated along the tube walls.262 Diazonium compounds,263 nitrenes, carbenes and radicals264 also react with nanotube walls producing nanotubes with modified materials properties (Fig. 3). The sidewalls of SWNTs have also been functionalised non-covalently using the π stacking interaction of succinimidyl ester functionalised 1-pyrenebutanoic acid and the material produced used to immobilise proteins.265 A monoclonal antibody specific for fullerenes is now known to recognise and bind to SWNTs.266 Ultrasonication of SWNTs with poly(methyl methacrylate) results in covalent functionalisation of the nanotubes which contain many defects.267 Composite materials suitable for use in electrochemical devices have been prepared using MWNTs and sol–gels.268 Fluorinated SWNTs have also been prepared using F2/HF as the fluorinating agent and the resultant material used in the manufacture of lithium electrochemical cells.269 The energies, band structures270 and thermochemistry271 of fluorinated SWNTs have been investigated using, for example, density functional theory.
