Abstract

This chapter reviews the literature reported in 2010 on the homogeneous reactions of inorganic species in solution, excluding the reactions of p-block elements.

Highlights

A detailed theoretical study of the pathway for water oxidation in synthetic ruthenium-based catalysts has been presented. Structural and energetics data for the single-centre catalyst system were used as the basis for considering a representative Ru-dimer which was designed to form O2via coupling between the two centres. The acid-base mechanism presented suggests a new set of strategies for the rational design of multicentre catalysts.1

1 Introduction

While the main focus is on the varied mechanistic studies of redox, substitution, hydrolysis, solvent effects, atom transfers and oxidative additions/reductive eliminations of coordination complexes and organometallic complexes of transition and main group metals, those kinetic and mechanistic studies of inorganic and biologically relevant compounds have been included, as well as several interesting reports on techniques.

Mechanistic studies of heterogeneous or solid state processes are not covered in this review. Neither are electrochemical, fluxional, photochemical processes, nor redox reactions involving organic substrates or organic reactions of the p-block elements. Homogeneous catalysis of organic reactions are included, where the mechanistic role of the transition metal centre is highlighted. Computational methods continue to make a tremendous contribution to the field of inorganic reaction mechanisms. These studies have appeared along with experimental studies, or by themselves, and highlight the power of theoretical methods in both a predictive and confirmatory vein.

2 Redox reactions

Outer- and inner-sphere electron transfer

The kinetics of the hydrogen peroxide induced oxidative degradation of the azo dye Orange II in aqueous carbonate buffered solution were studied for the oxo-bridged [Mn2III/IV(-O)2(bpy)4](ClO4)3 complex and its mononuclear analogue [MnII(bpy)2Cl2] as catalysts. The key feature of both catalysts is a two-electron oxidation from a Mn(II) precursor to a high-valent MnIVO intermediate, which represents the potential oxidizing species.2

In weakly acidic media ([H+], 0.010.06 M), 2-mercaptoethanol, thioglycolic acid and L-cysteine reduce the superoxo ligand of {-amido--superoxo-bis-[tetra-amminecobalt(III)]}4+ to its corresponding hydroperoxo complex, {-amido--hydroperoxo-bis[tetraamminecobalt(III)]}3+. During this act, 2-mercaptoethanol and thioglycolic acid are quantitatively oxidized to their respective disulfides. The reactivity of the thiols decrease in the order: L-cysteine > thioglycolic acid > 2-mercaptoethanol.3

The oxidation of D-glucuronic acid by excess CrVI yields D-glucaric acid (Glucar) and CrIII as final products. The redox reaction involves the formation of intermediate CrIV and CrV species, with CrVI and CrV reacting with Glucur at comparable rates. The experimental results indicated that CrIV is a very reactive intermediate, and does not accumulate in the reaction. Kinetic studies show that the redox reaction between D-glucuronic acid and CrVI proceeds through a mechanism combining one- and two-electron pathways.4

The first example of a ruthenium complex, [RuIII(edta)(H2O)] (edta = ethylenediaminetetraacetate), that catalyzes the oxidation of hydroxyurea in the presence of H2O2 has been reported. The complex mimics the action of peroxidase or catalase and its action may have implications on the applicability of Ru anti-cancer drugs.5

The oxidation of [Co(edta)]2 by [IrCl6]2 proceeds by both inner-sphere and outer-sphere electron-transfer pathways. In the presence of added [Co(en)3]3+, the outer-sphere pathway is enhanced. When optically active [Co(en)3]3+ is used the [Co(edta)] formed is optically active reflecting a 1.5% selectivity. It is proposed that the selectivity arises from preferential formation and reactivity of the ion pair, {[Co(edta)]2,[Co(en)3]3+}.6

A sizeable H/D KIE of 3.7 was observed, highlighting the importance of proton transfer in the cleavage reaction. Iridium half-sandwich complexes Cp*Ir(NC)X, [Cp*Ir(NN)X]X, and [CpIr(NN)X]X (Cp = 5-C5H5; Cp* = 5-C5Me5; NC = 2-phenylpyridine-C,N; NN = 2,2-dipyridyl, 1,10-phenanthroline, 2,2-bipyrimidine, N,N,N,N-tetramethylethylenediamine) are highly active catalyst precursors for the homogeneous oxidation of water to dioxygen. Atom transfer

The self-exchange rate constant for hydrogen-atom transfer (HAT) for the couples CraqOO2+/CraqOOH2+ and L2(H2O)RhOO2+/L2(H2O)RhOOH2+ (L2=cyclam) was estimated to be 1011 M1 s1. The use of this value in the simplified Marcus equation for the CraqO2+/CraqOOH2+ cross reaction provided an upper limit kCrO,CrOH 10(21) M1 s1 for CraqO2+/CraqOH2+ self-exchange. Cross reactions with organic oxygen-centred radicals were fast and adopted a modified Russell mechanism.10 The CraqOO2+/(CH3)3C(O)OO reaction has a rate constant of greater than 108 M1 s1.

A monooxomolybdenum(VI) model complex for the oxidized active site in the DMSOR family of molybdoenzymes has been synthesized and structurally characterized. The compound was obtained from the desoxomolybdenum(IV) derivative by clean oxygen-atom transfer from an amine N-oxide in a manner similar to that observed in the enzyme. A combination of electronic absorption and resonance Raman spectroscopies, coupled with the results of bonding and excited-state calculations, has been used to provide strong support for a highly covalent Mo(dxy)S(dithiolene) *-bonding interaction in the molybdenum(VI) complex. It is proposed that the resulting MoS covalency facilitates electron-transfer regeneration of the catalytically competent DMSOR MoIV active site.11

The oxygen atom transfer reactivity of the dioxo-Mo(VI) complex, TpiPrMoO2(OPh) (TpiPr = hydrotris(3-isopropylpyrazolyl)borate), with a range of tertiary phosphines (PMe3, PMe2Ph, PEt3, PnBu3, PEt2Ph, PEtPh2, and PMePh2) has been investigated. The first step in all the reactions is a nucleophilic attack by the phosphine on an oxo ligand. The second step of the reaction, that is the exchange of the coordinated phosphine oxide by acetonitrile, is first order in starting complex and acetonitrile. The reaction occurs via a dissociative interchange (Id) or associative interchange (Ia) mechanism, depending on the nature of the phosphine oxide. The oxo-Mo(V) phosphoryl complex is more stable than the acetonitrile analogue, whereas the oxo-Mo(IV) acetonitrile complex is more stable than the phosphoryl analogue. The higher stability of the Mo(V) phosphoryl complex may explain the phosphate inhibition of sulfite oxidase. The reaction rate follows a linear correlation with the phosphine cone angle suggesting steric influences play a significant role in this system.12

Thermolysis of a green solution of (nacnac)V(N3)(Ntol2) (tol = toluene) in C6D6 at 100 C for 12 h caused transformation to a new diamagnetic and orange species assigned as (nacnac)V N(Ntol2). A negatively large S value suggests a very early transition state with respect to (nacnac)V N(Ntol2) and a highly ordered system in which the azide ligand plausibly forms a three- or four-membered ring with the V(III) centre, via chelation at the or positions. The reaction is 16 times slower in THF than in toluene.13

Several optically active Nb(salan) complexes were synthesized, and their oxidation catalysis was examined. A dimeric -oxo Nb(salan) complex (Fig. 1) that was prepared from Nb(OiPr)5 and a salan ligand was found to catalyze the asymmetric epoxidation of allylic alcohols using a urea-hydrogen peroxide adduct as an oxidant with good enantioselectivity. This is the first example of the highly enantioselective epoxidation of allylic alcohols using aqueous hydrogen peroxide as an oxidant. The -oxo dimer dissociates into a monomeric species prior to epoxidation.14

A series of molybdenum oxo imido and bisimido complexes of the type MoO(N-t-Bu)L2] and [Mo(N-t-Bu)2L2] that contain L = -etiminate ligands were synthesized. KL = KArNC(CH3)CHC(CH3)O (Ar = phenyl, 2-tolyl, 2,6-dimethylphenyl, 2,6-diisopropylphenyl). OAT properties to triphenylphosphine were investigated, and the findings show the steric effect of the t-Bu imido group to be dominating.15

The roles of the distinct electronic structures of the {Fe(NO)2}9 and {Fe(NO)2}10 dinitrosyliron complexes (DNICs) in modulating nitrite binding modes and nitrite activation pathways has been investigated via nitrosylation of [PPN]2[(ONO)2Fe(2-ONO)2] [PPN = bis(triphenylphosphoranylidene)ammonium] which yields the nitrite-containing {Fe(NO)}7 mononitrosyliron complex (MNIC) [PPN]2[(NO)Fe-(ONO)3(2-ONO)], which when reacted with 1 equiv of PPh3 triggers OAT of the chelating nitrito ligand under mild conditions to yield the {Fe(NO)2}9 dinitrosyliron complex (DNIC) [PPN][(ONO)2Fe(NO)2]. Both electronic structure [{FeIII(NO)}7, S = 3/2] and redox-active ligands are required for the transformation of {Fe(NO)}7 MNICs into {Fe(NO)2}9 DNICs. This result may signify that nitrite-containing DNICs may act as NO/NO2 storage/transport species to maintain cellular NO homeostasis in biological systems.16

Kinetic parameters for the reduction of copper(II) complexes in atom transfer radical addition (ATRA) in the presence of free-radical diazo initiator (AIBN) were determined using both experimental and kinetic modelling techniques. The rate constant of decomposition of AIBN (kdc) in various solvents was determined at 60 C using UV-vis spectroscopy. Rate constants of deactivation (kd,AIBN) of [CuII(L)Cl][Cl] (L = tris(2-pyridylmethyl)amine), tris[2-(N,N-dimethylamino)-ethyl]amine), N,N,N,N,N-pentamethyldiethylenetriamine) and [CuII(bpy)2Cl][Cl] (bpy = 2,2-bipyridine) complexes by radicals generated from the decomposition of AIBN were measured using the TEMPO-trapping method in a competitive clock reaction. The respective second order rate constants are 5.4, 53, 7.3 and 11 (107 M1 s1).18

CH bond activation by metal oxo complexes, which involve HAT have been reviewed.19 The importance of precursor and successor complex formation in the bimolecular proton-electron transfer reaction of TEMPOH to [CoIII(Hbim)-(H2bim)2]2+ (H2bim = 2,2-biimidazoline) has been reported. The energetics of formation of these encounter complexes are the dominant contributors to the overall energetics: G for the proton-electron transfer step is only 0.3 0.9 kcal mol1. Thus, formation of the precursor and successor complexes can be a significant component of the thermochemistry for intermolecular proton-electron transfer, particularly in the low-driving-force regime, and should be included in quantitative analyses.20

Reactions of the ruthenium(II) hydride complexes, Ru(terpy)(bpy)H+, Ru(terpy)(dmb)H+ and Ru(6-C6Me6)(bpy)(H)+ with the hydride acceptors, CO2, CO, CH2O and H3O+, have been examined in aqueous media at 25 C (terpy = 2,2;6,2-terpyridine, bpy = 2,2-bipyridine, dmb = 4,4-dimethyl-2,2-bipyridine). The reactions of two Ru(II) hydride complexes with CO2 in water are best described as insertion: formation of the CH bond of formate precedes formation of the RuO bond in the product formate complex.21

The electrophilic (salen)ruthenium(VI) nitrido species, [RuVI(N)(L)(MeOH)](PF6) [L = N,N-bis(salicylidene)-o-cyclohexyldiamine dianion], undergoes stepwise reactions with RSH to produce the first examples of sulfilamido and sulfilamine complexes of ruthenium. A review on the theory of coupled electron and proton transfer reactions includes concerted PCET reactions, sequential proton and electron transfer, concerted vs. sequential PCET, and some applications.23 PCET in cytochrome oxidase has also been reviewed.24

Complexes of the type CuII(L)X2 (L = H33m, D-H33m, p-NO2-H33m; X = CF3SO3, ClO4) have been employed to provide the mechanistic details of a mild aromatic CH activation effected by a copper(II) centre ligated in a triazamacrocylic ligand. Equimolar amounts of a CuIII-aryl species and CuI species are produced. At low temperatures, a three-centre, three electron CHCuII interaction, is identified by pulse electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy and supported by DFT calculations. Kinetic studies strongly implicate a rate-limiting PCET as the key CH activation step.25

Titanocene(III) aqua complexes have been used to efficiently reduce carbon-centred radicals of diverse nature. The success of this transformation is based on the excellent binding capabilities of water toward titanocene(III) complexes, and a low activation energy for the HAT step. The observed reactivity was explained in the framework of an unprecedented HAT reaction involving water.26

The synthesis of a niobium cyclopropyl complex, TpMe2NbMe(c-C3H5)(MeCCMe) has been reported. Thermal loss of methane from this compound generates an intermediate that is capable of activating both aliphatic and aromatic CH bonds. The active intermediate is an 2-cyclopropene complex formed via -hydrogen abstraction. CH activation chemistry of this type is favoured by the strain in the C3 ring which prevents the decomposition of the key intermediate via loss of cyclopropene. A detailed analysis of the reaction kinetics, along with a survey of the computed free energy surface, suggests that two unsaturated intermediates are unselective and react with aliphatic or aromatic CH bonds with approximately equal rates.27

It was revealed by quantum mechanical analysis that carbonyl reduction of aldehydes and ketones by the imine-based reductant cis-[OsIII(tpy)(Cl)(NHCHCH3) (NSAr)] (Ar = C6H5, 4-MeC6H4 and 3,5-Me2C6H3), which is accessible by reduction of the analogous nitrile, occurs by hydride-proton transfer (HPT) involving both the imine and sulfilimido ligands. Water or alcohol significantly lowers the barrier for proton shuttling between ligands, in carbonyl reduction. The N(H)SAr group activates the carbonyl group through hydrogen bonding while the NC(H)CH3 ligand delivers the hydride.28

Dipicolinate vanadium(V) alkoxide complexes (dipic)VV(O)(OR) (OR = isopropoxide, n-butanoxide, cyclobutanoxide and -tert-butylbenzylalkoxide) react with pyridine to afford vanadium(IV) and 0.5 equiv. of an aldehyde or ketone product. The evidence points to a bimolecular reaction with pyridine to generate a vanadium(III) intermediate. The involvement of base in the elementary alcohol oxidation step observed for the dipicolinate system is an unprecedented mechanism for vanadium-mediated alcohol oxidation and suggests new ways to tune reactivity and selectivity of vanadium catalysts.29

3 Solvent effects

The complex [Ru(Mebimpy)(bpy)(OH2)]2+ [Mebimpy = 2,6-bis(1-methyl benzimi-dazol-2-yl)pyridine, bpy = 2,2-bipyridine] and its 4,4-(PO3H2CH2)2bpy derivative on oxide electrodes are water oxidation catalysts in propylene carbonate and 2,2,2-trifluoroethanol (TFE) to which water has been added as a limiting reagent. The rate of water oxidation is greatly enhanced relative to that with water as the solvent and occurs by a pathway that is first-order in H2O; an additional pathway that is first-order in acetate appears when TFE is used as the solvent. The results demonstrate that catalytic water oxidation can occur in nonaqueous environments with greatly enhanced rates and by a new pathway involving direct O-atom addition to H2O. There may be important implications in these results for alternate solar-fuel strategies in nonaqueous environments.30

A new dinuclear nickel(II) complex, [Ni2(LH2)(H2O)2(OH)(NO3)](NO3)3, of an end-off compartmental ligand 2,6-bis(N-ethylpiperazine-iminomethyl)-4-methyl-phenolato, exhibits versatile catalytic activities of biological significance, viz. catecholase, phosphatase, and DNA cleavage activities. The catecholase activity of the complex observed is very dependent on the nature of the solvent. In acetonitrile medium, the complex is inactive to exhibit catecholase activity, but in methanol, it catalyzes not only the oxidation of 3,5-di-tert-butylcatechol but also tetrachlorocatechol, which is very difficult to oxidize under aerobic conditions.31

The interconversion of the closed- and open-tethered RuII6-arene complexes [RuII(6:1-C6H5(C6H4)NH2)(en)]Cl2 (1) and [RuII(6-C6H5(C6H4)NH2)Cl(en)]Cl (2), respectively, (en = 1,2-diaminoethane) (Fig. 1), has been investigated in different solvents. Complex 1 opens in dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) by solvent-induced dissociation of the NH2 group of the pendant arm. In methanol, however, equilibrium between 1 and 2 is reached after 12 h when both species coexist in solution in a ratio of 21 (open/closed). The kinetics of decomposition of the copper(II) complexes of a series of phosphate mono-, di-, and tri-esters with a common leaving group (2-(2-phenoxy)-1,10-phenanthroline), was studied in methanol at 25 C. 4 Hydrolysis/aquation

The ruthenium(II) dithiolate complex (bmmp-TASN)RuPPh3 (6) reacts with O2 under limiting conditions to yield isolable sulfur oxygenated derivatives as a function of reaction time. Rapid addition of O2 yields the thiolato/sulfinato complex (bmmp-O2-TASN)RuPPh3 (7). Steric interactions between the remaining thiolate and PPh3explains the slow, second oxygenation step, to give the sulfenato/sulfinato derivative (bmmp-O3-TASN)RuPPh3 (8). Further oxygenation to produce the bis-sulfinato complex (bmmp-O4-TASN)RuPPh3 (9) is further hindered by the PPh3 and occurs only on longer time scales. Stoichiometric hydrolysis of acetonitrile to acetamide by 7 and 8 is observed in mixed acetonitrile, methanol, PIPES buffer (pH = 7.0) mixtures. Complex 9 degrades in the presence of water.34

Two new isostructural mononuclear [Ln(L)(NO3)(H2O)3](NO3)2 complexes, with Ln = Tb and Eu, display high activity in the hydrolysis of the substrate 2,4-bis-(dinitrophenyl)phosphate. These complexes displayed catalytic behaviour similar to that of the mononuclear gadolinium complex [Gd(L)(NO3)(H2O)3](NO3)2 previously reported. The hydrolysis reaction occurs in two stages, with the second stage being independent of the complex concentration. Studies with a radical scavenger (dimethylsulfoxide) confirmed the hydrolytic action of the Tb and Eu complexes in the cleavage of DNA.35

New macrocyclic binuclear nickel(II) complexes synthesized from the bicompartmental mononuclear complex [3,3-((1E,7E)-3,6-dioxa-2,7-diazaocta-1,7-diene-1,8-diyl)bis(3-formyl-5-methyl-2-diolato)nickel(II)] and various diamines (1,2-bis(aminooxy)ethane, 1,2-diaminoethane, 1,3-diaminopropane, 1,4-diaminobutane, 1,2-diaminobenzene, and 1,8-diaminonaphthalene) hydrolyse 4-nitrophenyl phosphate, with observed first order rate constant values in the range from 8.69 103 to 1.85 102 s1. The complexes show good binding propensity to calf thymus DNA giving binding constant values in the range from 1.4 104 to 17.5 104 M1.36

Dimethylphosphate was used as a model in the benchmarking of density functional theory functionals for the hydrolysis of phosphodiester bonds. A total of 52 functionals were tested for four reaction paths, and a best basis set, based on time and accuracy, is proposed.37

5 Substitution

The spectroscopic and structural characteristics and the relative reactivity of several TpMsCu(olefin) (olefin = ethylene, 1-hexene, allyl ethyl ether (aee), cyclohexene and styrene) complexes bearing the bulky hydrotris(3-mesitylpyrazolyl)borate ligand have been examined. Kinetic studies of the exchange of TpMsCu(aee) with 1-hexene at 0 C in CD2Cl2 revealed the existence of a dissociative mechanism. The global equilibrium is shifted to the olefin with the less steric influence.38

The dissolution of [(CH3)N]2Na[MnN(CN)5]3H2O in water results in the dissociation of the labile trans-CN ligand to form [MnN(H2O)(CN)4]2, which reacts rapidly with a number of mono- and bi-dentate nucleophiles such as n-methylpyridines and pyridine-2-carboxylate and quinoline-2-carboxylate. The kinetics of substitution using pyridine-2-carboxylate and pyridine-2(x-di)carboxylate-type ligands showed that the first step in the two-step reaction was too fast to be observed. The second, ring-closing step, involving cyanide substitution, was observed, and has positive values for the activation entropy, supporting a dissociative activation.39

6 Isomerisation

The meso-dithioxamide H-((R)-1-(1-phenyl)ethyl)-NSC-CSN-((S)-1-(1-phenyl)-ethyl)-H (H2-mesoDTO) bonds [(6-p-cymene)chlorido-ruthenium(II)]+ or [(3-allyl)-palladium(II)]+ fragment and provides the Cs symmetrical complexes [(6-p-cymene)ClRu(H-mesoDTO -S,S Ru)] and [(3-allyl)palladium(H-mesoDTO -S,S Pd)]. These complexes are pseudochiral, and each of them exists as a mixture of two symmetrical meso forms. [(6-p-cymene)chlorido-ruthenium(II)]+ and [(3-allyl)-palladium(II)]+ fragments have been joined by means of the binucleating meso-dithiooxamidate ligand in a -S,S Ru -N,N Pd coordination mode. The resulting Cs-symmetrical complex [(6-p-cymene)ClRu(-mesoDTO -S,S Ru -N,N Pd)-Pd(3-allyl)] possesses two pseudochiral metal centres, and is a mixture of four isomeric meso forms. In a chloroform solution all of these isomers interconvert in that both palladium and ruthenium invert their configurations. A mechanism of epimerization for both palladium and ruthenium is proposed.43

The phosphinito-bridged Pt(I) complex [(PHCy2)Pt(-PCy2){2P,O--P(O)Cy2}-Pt(PHCy2)](PtPt) reversibly adds H2 under ambient conditions, giving cis-[(H)(PHCy2)Pt1(-PCy2)(-H)Pt2(PHCy2){PP(O)Cy2}](PtPt), which slowly isomerizes spontaneously into the corresponding more stable isomer trans-[(PHCy2)(H)Pt(-PCy2)(-H)Pt(PHCy2){P-P(O)Cy2}](PtPt). The nonclassical dihydrogen complex [(PHCy2)(2-H2)Pt(-PCy2)Pt(PHCy2){P-P(O)Cy2}](PtPt) is an intermediate in the dehydrogenation. The cis-trans isomerisation reaction occurs via an intramolecular mechanism which involves opening of the PtPt bond of the hydrogen bridge followed by the rotation of the coordination plane of the Pt centre with the terminal hydride ligand.44

9 Biologically-relevant systems

The range of reactions of peroxynitrite with heme proteins has been surveyed with particular attention to myoglobin and cytochrome c. These proteins are textbook paradigms for oxygen binding and electron transfer, respectively, and have been shown to have other important functions that involve NO and peroxynitrite.54 Also done, is an overview of the fundamental chemical properties of nitric oxide and nitrite ion in relation to the reactions with ferri- and ferro-heme models and proteins potentially relevant to the roles of these species in mammalian biology.55

9.1 DNA

The binding capacity, of a series of new cationic nickel and manganese metalloporphyrins, to bind in the minor groove of DNA was evaluated by binding competition experiments with manganese(III)-bis-aqua-meso-tetrakis(4-N-methyl-pyridiniumyl)porphyrin, Mn-TMPyP, a powerful artificial nuclease when associated with KHSO5. The four N-methylpyridiniumyl substituents on this porphyrin macrocycle are responsible for a strong binding affinity for the minor groove of AT-rich DNA. Data on the structural modifications that influence cationic porphyrin binding to DNA should be helpful in the design of new quadruplex selective agents based on porphyrins.56

The activation of DNA carbon-hydrogen bonds by metal complexes has been reviewed. Careful design of the ligand is necessary so that the metal complex is a good catalyst and a precise fit between the metal centre and DNA is achieved. The complex must interact with DNA in such a way that it is within reach of a CH bond and that it can be activated at the site of its interaction with DNA.57

Four vanadium(III) complexes of the general formula [V(maltol)2(NN)]ClO4, where NN is 2,2-bipyridine (bpy); 1,10-phenanthroline (phen); dipyrido[3,2-d:2,3-f]-quinoxaline (dpq), and dipyrido[3,2-a:2,3-c]phenazine (dppz), have been synthesized and characterized by IR, UV-visible, NMR spectroscopies, and electrospray ionization mass spectra (ESI-MS). The V(III) complexes of dpq and dppz ligands are more active than the bpy and phen complexes in cleaving plasmid DNA which also do not require any external reagents like ascorbate or hydrogen peroxide.58

The peptidyl antibiotic bacitracin (Bc) can bind divalent transition metal ions, including Mn(II), Co(II), Ni(II), Cu(II), and Zn(II). Metal-free Bc does not bind DNA. 9.2 Porphyrins

Reaction of hydrido[5,10,15,20-tetrakis(p-tolyl)porphyrinato]iridium(III) (Ir(ttp)H) with 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl (TEMPO) at room temperature in benzene gave a 90% yield of the unsupported iridium(II) porphyrin dimer, IrII2(ttp)2. Kinetic measurements revealed that the oxidation followed overall second-order kinetics: rate = k[Ir(ttp)H][TEMPO], k (25 C) = 6.65 104 M1 s1. The entropy of activation (S = 25.3 2.5 cal mol1 K1) and the kinetic isotope effect of 7.2 supported a bimolecular associative mechanism in the rate-determining HAT from Ir(ttp)H to TEMPO.62

The gas-phase oxygen-atom transfer chemistry of meso-tetrakis(pentafluoro-phenyl) porphyrin (TPFPP) chromium complexes, as well as some other tetradentate macrocyclic ligands, was explored in order to determine the structure of the species formed in solution. [(TPFPP)CrIII]+ forms adducts with a variety of neutral donors, but O-atom transfer is only observed for NO2. [(TPFPP)CrVO]+ species are much less reactive than the Fe and Mn analogues.63

The reaction of cysteine (CysSH) with aqueous nitrite and the water-soluble ferriheme models FeIII(TPPS) (TPPS = meso-tetra(4-sulfonatophenyl)porphyrinato) or FeIII(TMPS) (TMPS = meso-tetra-(sulfonatomesityl)porphyrinato) at pH 5.8 and 7.4 forms cysteine sulfenic acid CysS(O)H and the respective ferrous nitrosyl complex FeII(Por)(NO) (Por = TPPS or TMPS). Analogous oxygen atom transfers (OAT) were seen when glutathione (GSH) was used as the substrate. Preliminary kinetic studies are consistent with rate-limiting OAT from a ferric nitro complex FeIII(Por)(NO2) to CysSH.64

In coordinating solvent DMSO, the superoxide adduct of heme-FeII species, {(Porph)FeO2}, (H2Porph = 52-N-(4-aza-18-crown-6)methyl-54,104,154,204-tetra-t-butyl-56-methyl-5,10,15,20-tetraphenylporphyrin) exists in the isomeric forms, FeIIIperoxo and FeII-superoxo, which are not resonance structures, but two discrete isomers. 10 Organometallics

The ability of compounds CpCrII(nacnacAr,Ar) (nacnac = [ArNC(CH3)]2CH, Ar = Dipp, Xyl) to catalyze the atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) of vinyl acetate has been explored using methyl 2-chloropropionate as initiator. The general principles that interplay between atom transfer and metalcarbon bond formation for the radical trapping in controlled radical polymerization, thermodynamically favoured formation of the organometallic dormant species. The metalcarbon bond formation is greatly preferred from the kinetic standpoint.71

Quantum chemical calculations on the B3LYP level of theory were carried out to elucidate the initial step of the catalytic cycle of the aminohydroxylation of olefins with osmium reagents. Various reaction pathways of the model systems (O)2Os(NH)2 (Os1) and (O)2Os(NH)-cyclo-(NHCH2CH2HN) (Os2) and two of their isomers, (HO)(O)Os(NH)(N) (Os1) and (O)Os(OH)(N)-cyclo-(NHCH2CH2HN) (Os2), with ethylene have been considered. Os1, Os1, and Os2 prefer a [3+2] cycloaddition kinetically and thermodynamically, whereas Os2 is predicted to favour a [1+2] pathway due to kinetic reasons. Thermodynamically, Os2 prefers a consecutive rearrangement after the [1+2] addition. The isomer Os1 is lower in energy than Os1, but the kinetically and thermodynamically most favourable reaction is the [3+2]NH,NH ethylene addition to the latter isomer, which takes place without a barrier.72

In the interaction of an amido/bis(phosphine)-supported (PNP)Ir fragment with a series of linear and cyclic ethers, DFT investigation has revealed the factors dictating reaction direction toward either an iridium-supported carbene, which involves initial CH activation, followed by a cascading autocatalytic rearrangement, or a vinyl ether adduct, involving the same initial CH activation followed by -hydrogen elimination to form a weakly bound Ir(III) dihydrido vinyl ether. An interesting systematic error present in the DFT analysis of reactions where steric environment alters considerably within a reaction has been highlighted.73

A range of paramagnetic Cr(III) monohydrocarbyl complexes CpCr[(ArNCMe)2CH](R) (Ar = orthodisubstituted aryl; R = primary alkyl, trimethylsilylmethyl, benzyl, phenyl, alkenyl, or alkynyl) were synthesized to investigate how varying the steric and electronic properties of the R group affected their propensity for CrR bond homolysis. Modifying the neopentyl ligand from the previously reported CpCr[(XylNCMe)2CH](CH2CMe3) decreases its reactivity. Replacing one Me group with a H diminishes the rate of alkyl dissociation, while use of the CH2SiMe3 group effectively shuts down the thermal homolysis reaction at room temp. The homolysis rates increased when bulkier Dpp and Dep substituents were used on the -diketiminate ligands, consistent with the importance of steric repulsion in weakening the Cr(III)R bonds.74

11 Main group metal complexes

The complexes of composition fac-[(6-C6H6)Ru(L)][PF6][X] (X = PF6 or Cl), formed by reacting 2-MeSC6H4CHNCH2CH2E-C6H4-4R and 2-MeSC6H4CH2NHCH2CH2EC6H4-4R (where E = S or Se, R = H; E = Te, R = OMe) with [{(6-C6H6)RuCl(-Cl)}2] and NH4PF6, have been found to be efficient catalysts for catalytic oxidation of alcohols with N-methylmorpholine-N-oxide, tBuOOH, NaOCl, and NaIO4 and transfer hydrogenation reaction of ketones with 2-propanol. The oxidation appears to occur through Ru(IV)O-containing species and hydrogen transfer through a classical mechanism involving RuH formation. The catalytic efficiency for both processes follows the order Te > Se > S.78

Aluminium has been established as a highly active metal toward the hydro-amination/-hydrazination of carbodiimides. The commercially available [Al(NMe2)3]2 dimer is active in transformations that occur at temperatures above 75 C, whereas monomeric species such as the guanidinate-supported dimethyl Al show increased catalytic activity at low reaction temperatures. Al-aminoguanidinate proved to be catalytically active and is proposed to play an active role in the reaction mechanism.79

A systematic investigation into the Lewis base catalyzed, asymmetric, intramolecular selenoetherification of olefins has been described. These studies show that the 2-nitrophenyl group attached to the selenium atom significantly attenuates the racemisation of seleniranium ions, a critical challenge for the development of this process. A variety of achiral Lewis bases catalyze the intramolecular selenoetherification of alkenes using N-(2-nitrophenylselenenyl)-succinimide as the electrophile along with a Brnsted acid. Preliminary mechanistic studies suggest the intermediacy of ionic Lewis base-selenium(II) adducts.80
