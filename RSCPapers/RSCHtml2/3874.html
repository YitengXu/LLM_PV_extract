Donor–acceptor conjugated polymers based on multifused ladder-type arenes for organic solar cells

Abstract

Harvesting solar energy from sunlight to generate electricity is considered as one of the most important technologies to address the future sustainability of humans. Polymer solar cells (PSCs) have attracted tremendous interest and attention over the past two decades due to their potential advantage to be fabricated onto large area and light-weight flexible substrates by solution processing at a lower cost. PSCs based on the concept of bulk heterojunction (BHJ) configuration where an active layer comprises a composite of a p-type (donor) and an n-type (acceptor) material represents the most useful strategy to maximize the internal donor–acceptor interfacial area allowing for efficient charge separation. Fullerene derivatives such as [6,6]-phenyl-C61or71-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) are the ideal n-type materials ubiquitously used for BHJ solar cells. The major effort to develop photoactive materials is numerously focused on the p-type conjugated polymers which are generally synthesized by polymerization of electron-rich donor and electron-deficient acceptor monomers. Compared to the development of electron-deficient comonomers (acceptor segments), the development of electron-rich donor materials is considerably flourishing. Forced planarization by covalently fastening adjacent aromatic and heteroaromatic subunits leads to the formation of ladder-type conjugated structures which are capable of elongating effective conjugation, reducing the optical bandgap, promoting intermolecular π–π interactions and enhancing intrinsic charge mobility. In this review, we will summarize the recent progress on the development of various well-defined new ladder-type conjugated materials. These materials serve as the superb donor monomers to prepare a range of donor–acceptor semi-ladder copolymers with sufficient solution-processability for solar cell applications.

1. Introduction

Bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells based on fullerene–polymer composites have been developed over 15 years.1 The efficiency of polymer-based solar cells (PSCs) grew slowly from 3% to 5% for many years. However, this type of solar cell has made remarkable improvement in efficiency since 2009. Such a rapid progress is very significant, showing promise and brightness for future commercialization. Along with advanced device engineering and controlled active-layer morphology, the major breakthrough in this area comes from the development of superb conjugated polymers to achieve efficient light-harvesting and charge transport. Theoretical computation predicts that over 10% power conversion efficiency (PCE) of a BHJ solar cell is obtainable if an ideal p-type conjugated polymer is equipped with all required properties.2 Through tremendous research effort to create numerous novel conjugated polymers, encouraging device performances with PCEs up to 8–9% have been realized recently.3–14

Extensive research and accumulated experiences have provided the general guidelines for developing p-type conjugated polymers. The required intrinsic properties for an ideal p-type conjugated polymer at the molecular level include (1) sufficient solubility to guarantee solution processability and miscibility with n-type materials, (2) a low optical band gap (LBG) for the broad absorption spectrum to capture more solar energy, (3) high hole mobility for accelerating charge transport to reduce charge recombination and series resistance, and (4) suitable HOMO and LUMO energies to ensure a large Voc and a downhill energy offset for exciton dissociation. So far, the most useful method to prepare a LBG polymer is to connect electron-rich donor and electron-deficient acceptor segments along the conjugated polymer backbone, in either an alternating or a random fashion.15 Efficient intramolecular charge transfer will occur upon photoexcitation.

The most straightforward way to make an alternating donor–acceptor conjugated polymer is to independently synthesize a donor monomer and an acceptor monomer which are then polymerized via a transition-metal catalyzed cross-coupling reaction. In this regard, molecular design of new electron-rich donor monomers is central to the development p-type donor–acceptor polymers.16–24 Traditional linear conjugated polymers consist of continuous aromatic rings interconnected by carbon–carbon single bonds (Scheme 1a). Another important category of conjugated frameworks is ladder-type polymers which are comprised of linearly fused aromatic or heterocyclic subunits connected via sharing endocyclic carbon–carbon double bonds. Such double-strand conjugated structures feature high thermal, chemical and mechanical stabilities. More importantly, covalently forced planarization in a ladder structure strengthens the parallel p-orbital interactions to reduce the band gap by elongating the effective conjugation length and facilitating π-electron delocalization. Moreover, the rigidified coplanar structures can suppress the rotational disorder around interannular single bonds to lower the reorganization energy, which in turn enhances the intrinsic charge mobility.49

Although the physical and electronic properties of ladder-type polymers have attracted considerable interest in the area of nonlinear optics, light-emitting diodes and organic transistors, the highly rigid ladder-type polymers suffer from poor solubility for solution processability due to strong π–π interactions, greatly limiting their optoelectronic applications. To circumvent the infusibility of the ladder-type polymers/oligomers while maintaining the advantageous properties of structural planarization, it is envisaged that the well-defined ladder-type small molecules can function as donor monomers to polymerize with acceptor units, leading to a new class of donor–acceptor semi-ladder copolymers that can be suitably used for solution-processable polymer solar cells (Scheme 2). Therefore, molecular designs and synthesis have been devoted towards integrating electron-rich aromatic subunits into mutually ring-fused structures. Various accessible electron-rich aromatic and heterocyclic building blocks such as benzene, thiophene, furan, selenophene, pyrrole, silole, germole, cyclopentadienyl moieties can be chemically assembled to afford a range of fascinating ladder-type conjugated skeletons with tunable properties and functions. It should be emphasized that incorporation of aliphatic side chains into the conjugated backbone is necessary and side-chain engineering plays a critical role for such semi-ladder conjugated molecules.50–53


Scheme 2 Illustration of a semi-ladder donor–acceptor copolymer for photovoltaic applications.

The length and the branching position of an alkyl chain, and its attachment position on a polymer backbone would dramatically influence the solubility, crystallization, and molecular packing which alter the charge transportation and morphology of the composites, resulting in variations in device performance. Compared to non-substituted polyaromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and heteroaromatics, synthesis of new multifused conjugated entities with implantation of alkyl side chains is less explored and remains challenging. The increasing interest of these materials for organic photovoltaics thus opens up a new research field for synthetic and polymer chemists.

Several reviews have summarized the development of conjugated polymers for bulk-heterojunction solar cell applications.54–69 The target of this review will focus on the recent advance on the design and synthesis of new ladder-type conjugated small molecules toward the development semi-ladder donor–acceptor polymers. The order of content in this review will be systematically arranged according to the conjugated length of the multifused structures, including tricyclic, tetracyclic, pentacyclic, hexacyclic, heptacyclic, nonacyclic, and undecyclic arenes. The useful synthetic approaches for making these novel and important building blocks will be highlighted and presented. The principle of molecular design and structure–property relationships associated with device characteristics will be discussed.

2. Tricyclic system: tricyclic bithiophene-based D–A copolymers

Coplanar tricyclic bithiophene-based materials where the 3,3′-positions of the bithiophene are fastened by different bridging atoms have been demonstrated as superior building blocks to construct considerable solution-processable organic materials utilized in a spectrum of diverse optoelectronic applications. The molecular properties of these tricyclic skeletons could be modulated by changing the bridging moieties embedded in a central aromatic or heterocyclic ring. The carbon-bridged 4H-cyclopenta[2,1-b:3,4-b′]dithiophene (CPDT) was the early first monomer utilized for making donor–acceptor conjugated polymers for PSCs.70–73 The bridging carbon of the CPDT can be functionalized by two alkyl groups to increase the solubility of the corresponding copolymers. The EHOMO and ELUMO of P1 were estimated to be −5.30 and −3.55 eV, respectively. 3. Tricyclic system: benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (BDT)-based D–A copolymers

3.1 4,8-Bis(alkyl or alkoxy)-BDT-based copolymers

The electron-rich benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene (BDT) unit, a central benzene ring fused with two thiophenes, emerges as an attractive building block for conjugated polymers due to its symmetric, rigid and coplanar structure. These structural features are beneficial to induce intermolecular π–π interaction for efficient charge transport. Therefore, research efforts have been focused on the synthesis of BDT-based polymers for the applications of organic field-effect transistors (OFETs). The BDT-based polymer P10 (Scheme 9) has achieved a high hole mobility of 0.25 cm2 V−1 s−1.85 In 2008, Yang and co-workers reported a series of BDT-based donor–acceptor conjugated polymers used for the applications of organic photovoltaics (OPVs).86 The thieno[3,4-b]-thiophene (TT) unit is a unique thiophene-based building block for the construction of p-type conjugated polymers, because the TT moiety tends to adapt more quinoidal resonance structure along the conjugated backbone, resulting in smaller Eg (energy of bandgap).58,61,68 Alternating poly(benzodithiophene-alt-thieno[3,4-b]thiophene) (PBDTTT) derivatives prepared by polymerization of the BDT unit with the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene moiety have been the most successful polymers for OPV applications. The functional groups substituted on the 2-position of the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene unit play an important role in determining the molecular properties. Hou et al. reported that P11,87 having a stronger electron-withdrawing alkyl carbonyl group on the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene unit, showed lower-lying EHOMO (HOMO energy level) and ELUMO (LUMO energy level) (−5.12 and −3.35 eV, respectively) than P12 (−5.01 and −3.24 eV) using the ester group as the side chain; however, the Eg of P11 is approximately the same as that of P12 (Scheme 10).87 Furthermore, when the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene unit was further substituted with an electron-withdrawing fluorine atom, the resulting polymer P13 exhibited a lower EHOMO accordingly. The absorption spectra of these polymers in the solid state showed a similar absorption onset (∼770 nm) and thus similar Eg. The device based on the P12:PC71BM (1:1) blend exhibited a PCE of 5.15%, whereas the device based on the P11:PC71BM (1:1.5) blend exhibited a higher PCE of 6.58%. Because P13 possesses a relatively lower-lying EHOMO, the device based on the P4:PC71BM (1:1.5) blend showed a higher Voc of 0.76 V, leading to a highest PCE of 7.73% (Table 2).

A series of fluorinated BDT-based polymers P14–P17 were also reported by Yu et al.88 Compared to the absorption of P14 and P15 in the solid state, P16 and P17 showed sharper peaks and more blue-shifted absorption onsets due to the electron-withdrawing effect of the fluorine atoms. The blue-shifted absorption onset of P15 compared to P14 is attributed to the change of the alkyl side chain from n-octyl for P14 to 2-ethylhexyl for P15. The bulkier side chain causes longer polymer interchain distances, thus reducing interchain interaction. The absorption spectra of P16 and P17 in the solid state are very similar, suggesting that further substitution of fluorine to the thieno[3,4-b]thiophene unit in P17 does not change its optical properties. The cyclic voltammetry results indicated that both EHOMO and ELUMO can be lowered by the introduction of fluorine to the TT or the BDT units in the polymer backbones. Under the optimal device conditions, the devices based on P14:PC71BM (1:1), P15:PC71BM (1:1.5), P16:PC71BM (1:1.5) or P17:PC71BM (1:1.5) blends exhibited PCEs of 5.1, 7.2, 3.2 and 2.7%, respectively. Although P14 and P16 have similar molecular weight and backbone structure, the FF of P14 is around 50% higher than that of P16. Besides, the Jsc values of P14 and P15 are also higher than those of P16 and P17 by ca. 3–4 mA cm−2. The dramatic decrease of FF values observed in P16:PC71BM and P17:PC71BM blend devices are mostly due to the small mobilities and poor miscibility of the polymers with PC71BM. Very recently, Cao and coworkers introduced poly [(9,9-bis(3′-(N,N-dimethylamino)propyl)-2,7-fluorene)-alt-2,7-(9,9-dioctylfluorene)] (PFN) as a cathode interlayer, the device using the P15:PC71BM (1:1.5) blend as the active layer exhibited a Voc of 0.75 V, a Jsc of 17.46 mA cm−2, an FF of 0.7, leading to a very impressive PCE of 9.21%.13P15 is also commonly named as PTB7 in the literature.

Considering that introducing an electron-withdrawing group on the TT unit can lower EHOMO of the corresponding polymers to obtain greater Voc, BDT-based P18 having a strong electron-withdrawing sulfonyl group on the TT unit was reported by Li, Han and Hou et al.89 The absorption spectra of P18 were essentially unchanged in solution and solid states, and the optical Eg deduced from the onset of the polymer thin film absorption spectrum is 1.65 eV. The EHOMO and ELUMO of P18 were estimated to be −5.12 and −3.49 eV, respectively. The device based on the P18:PC71BM (1:1.5) blend exhibited a Jsc of 14.1 mA cm−2, a Voc of 0.76 V, and an FF of 0.58, leading to a high PCE of 6.22%, indicating that the sulfonyl group is also an effective substituent for BDT/TT-based polymers.

The thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (TPD) unit is a simple, compact, and planar structure, which can facilitate electron delocalization and strengthen interchain interactions in conjugated polymers. Furthermore, the TPD unit with electron-withdrawing ability can efficiently lower the EHOMO of the TPD-containing polymers to increase Voc. Leclerc and coworkers first prepared P19 containing BDT and TPD units in an alternating manner.90 The Eg of P19 obtained from the absorption onset is 1.8 eV. Interestingly, the absorption spectrum of P19 in solution is very similar to that in a thin film. The EHOMO and ELUMO of P19 were estimated to be −5.56 and −3.75 eV, respectively. The device based on the P19:PC71BM (1:2) blend exhibited a Jsc of 9.81 mA cm−2, a Voc of 0.85 V, and an FF of 0.66, leading to a promising PCE of 5.5%. At the same time, Jen et al. also reported an identical polymer P19 which resulted in a similar PCE of 4.2%.91

Later on, Fréchet et al. also reported BDT/TPD-based alternating copolymers P19, P20 and P21 with different alkyl groups on the TPD units.92 By replacing the 2-ethylhexyl chains in P20 with the less bulky alkyl chains, P19 and P21 showed broader and red-shifted absorption spectra. The Eg obtained from the absorption onset of the polymer films are around 1.7 eV for the three polymers. The estimated EHOMO levels are −5.4 eV for P19, −5.48 eV for P20, and −5.57 eV for P21. The device based on the P20:PC61BM (1:2) blend exhibited a PCE of 3.9%. Compared to P20, the device based on the P21:PC61BM (1:1.5) blend exhibited a higher PCE of 5.4% because of larger FF and Jsc. Furthermore, the device based on the P19:PC61BM (1:1.5) blend using 1 v% 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO) with higher boiling point as an additive to optimize the morphology of active layer showed the highest PCE of 6.6%. On the other hand, the Grazing Incidence X-ray Scattering (GIXS) analysis showed that the TPD-based polymers tend to adapt a face-on orientation of the polymer backbone and preserve a small π-stacking distance in the blend containing PC61BM.

A new BDT-based D–A alternating copolymer P22, using 6,7-difluoro-2,3-bis(3-hexyloxyphenyl)-5,8-dithiophen-2-yl-quinoxaline as an acceptor unit, was reported by Chou et al.10P22 showed more red-shifted absorption in the solid state than in the dilute solution state as a result of a higher degree of interchain π-stacking in the solid film. The EHOMO, ELUMO and Eg of P22 were estimated to be −5.52, −3.30 eV and 1.73 eV, respectively. By adding the processing additive (3 v% DIO), the device based on the P22:PC71BM (1:1) blend exhibited a Jsc of 17.9 mA cm−2, a Voc of 0.76 V, and an FF of 0.576, leading to an average PCE of 7.8%.

5. Multifused pentacyclic systems

5.1 Indacenodithiophene (IDT)-based D–A copolymers

The tricyclic 2,7-fluorene unit has shown to be an excellent building block to construct D–A polymers having deep-lying EHOMO levels to achieve high Voc values for PSCs.114–116 However, the benzene-based fluorene-containing polymers usually possess relatively large optical Eg (>2 eV) values thus resulting in limited light-harvesting ability and photocurrents. Tricyclic thiophene-based 4H-cyclopenta[2,1-b:3,4-b′]dithiophene (CPDT) is another useful electron-rich building block for D–A copolymers.74,76,117–120 Despite the fact that the CPDT-based D–A copolymers exhibited low optical Eg and high hole mobilities to achieve large Jsc, the Voc values of the devices are generally below 0.65 V.

“Integration of the benzene and thiophene units into a single fused molecular entity could be a rational molecular design to improve Jsc and Voc simultaneously.” The pentacyclic indacenodithiophene (IDT) unit was first reported by Wong et al. in 2006.121 The extended conjugation and coplanarity make IDT a good building block for preparing D–A copolymers. 5.2 Indacenodiselenophene (IDS)-based D–A copolymers

Donor–acceptor copolymers incorporating selenophene moieties have recently emerged as promising photoactive materials considering the ability of selenophene to improve light harvesting, photostability and charge mobility of the polymers.126–129 Incorporation of the selenophene moiety into a ladder-type framework is of great interest in material development but synthetically less explored. Recently, Cheng et al. developed a new indacenodiselenophene (IDS) unit, a selenophene-based analogue of indacenodithiophene (IDT).130 The distannyl IDS-OC8 and IDS-C6 monomers having 4-octyloxyphenyl and 4-hexylphenyl side chains were prepared and copolymerized with 77 and 78 acceptors, respectively, to yield P58–P61 (Scheme 29). Similar molecular weights of P58 (10.7 kDa) and P60 (10.8 kDa), as well as P59 (11.5 kDa) and P61 (9.3 kDa), provide fair comparison of the side-chain effects on the molecular properties. It was found that the octyloxy (OC8) and hexyl (C6) side chains have a negligible influence on the optical and electrochemical properties of the resulting polymers due to the fact that the side chains have no direct conjugation with the polymeric backbones. However, the solar cell performance is highly associated with the side chains of the polymers. Under similar device fabrication conditions, the P60:PC71BM (1:4) and P61:PC71BM (1:3)-based devices showed much improved efficiency than the corresponding P58:PC71BM (1:4), and P59:PC71BM (1:3)-based devices (2.6% and 1.9% vs. 3.8% and 3.9%). Consistently, P60 and P61 exhibited higher FET hole mobilities than the corresponding P58 and P59. These results revealed that the 4-hexylphenyl group is a better side chain than the 4-octyloxyphenyl group in the IDS system. Computational calculations showed that the octyloxy group in IDS-OC8 is nearly in-plane with the phenyl ring, whereas the hexyl group in IDS-C6 is out-of-plane with the phenyl ring. This structural variation might influence the molecular packing between the polymers. At the same time, Jen and coworkers also reported a similar alternating copolymer P61 using IDS-C6 as the donor and FBT as the acceptor segments.131 The effect of molecular weight on the properties of P61 was systematically examined. Higher molecular weight leads to higher mobilities as well as stronger absorptivity of polymer films. Consistently, the polymers with increasing molecular weights of 30, 46, and 62 kDa led to the enhanced solar cell efficiencies of 5.48%, 6.11% and 6.79%, respectively. These results demonstrate the importance of achieving high molecular weight and selenium-containing polymers are promising for high-performance organic photovoltaics.

5.10 Fused donor-acceptor-based copolymers

If neighboring electron-rich donor and electron-deficient acceptor units along a polymer backbone are covalently locked into a coplanar interfused D–A entity, the electronic interactions between the donor and the acceptor unit in a D–A copolymer might increase. Nevertheless, polymers containing D–A fused frameworks have not been well investigated. Benzothiadiazole (BT), and quinoxaline (QX) units with moderate electron affinity are the most widely used acceptors.86 The benzothiadiazole unit connected with two electron-rich thienyl rings to form a nonfused D–A–D type 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)benzothiadiazole (DTBT) moiety has been commonly incorporated in the D–A polymers.151 Cheng et al. developed a fused D–A–D benzothiadiazolodithienopyrrole (BTDTP) structure in which the 3 position of two outer thiophenes (D) are covalently linked to the 5, 6 positions of the central benzothiadiazole core (A) by the nitrogen bridges (Scheme 48).152 The structure of BTDTP is a useful precursor to synthesize the quinoxalinodithienopyrrole (QXDTP) unit. 9. Conclusions

Although ladder-type conjugated polymers are a unique class of organic materials that have received great interest over the past decades, complete rigidification and planarization of the conjugated backbones make the resulting materials insoluble, hindering their practical applications. To impart p-type conjugated polymers with broad and strong absorption ability for solar cell applications, incorporation of electron deficient units into the polymer backbone to form donor–acceptor copolymers is necessary. Structurally well-defined ladder-type small molecules emerge as promising conjugated frameworks that can suitably act as electron-rich donor monomers. Copolymerization of such monomers with acceptors leads to various semi-ladder donor–acceptor copolymers which possess not only solution-processability but also advantageous properties resulting from chemical planarization. Through molecular engineering to assemble various building blocks via well-designed synthetic methodologies, a series of multifused ladder-type small molecules flanked with aliphatic side chains has been created. Thiophene, selenophene and benzene units are usually the basic components of the conjugated backbones. Thiophene units are preferably installed at two ends of the molecules due to the facile functionalization (bromination or stannylation) at the α-carbons of thiophene for metal-catalyzed polymerization. Side chains can be directly functionalized on the sp2-carbons of benzene and/or β-carbons of the thiophene units. Five-membered rings such as cyclopentadiene, silole, germole, pyrrole units are also important components to be embedded into the ladder-type frameworks. The bridging carbon, silicon, germanium, nitrogen atoms have the additional valences to introduce alkyl chains for sufficient solubility. Meanwhile, these heteroatoms with different electronic and steric natures allows for fine-tuning the molecular properties and tailoring the molecular functions. The development of ladder-type structures associated with the concept of chemical rigidification/planarization has been demonstrated to be a powerful strategy to improve light harvesting, charge transportation and molecular packing, leading to the enhancement of solar cell device performance. Most of the D–A copolymers in this review were prepared by palladium-catalyzed Stille cross-coupling polymerization using difunctional stannyl aromatic monomers. The synthesis and purification of tin compounds can be tedious and costly. Furthermore, stannylated derivatives and the resulting stannyl-containing byproducts are environmentally toxic and hazardous. To improve the synthetic efficiency of polymerization, transition-metal-catalyzed direct arylation utilizing unsubstituted aryl monomers in combination with the corresponding dihalo aryl components could be the focus of future research.

In this review, we have summarized the recent development of the semi-ladder donor–acceptor conjugated polymers. These synthetic advances towards the preparation of the ladder-type conjugated frameworks have paved the important way for exploring high-performance solar cell materials.
