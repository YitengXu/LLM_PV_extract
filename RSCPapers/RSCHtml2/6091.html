Abstract

To date, remarkable progress has been achieved in the development of photocatalysts owing to their high activity, selectivity, and tunable light absorption in the visible light range. Recently, heterogeneous photocatalytic systems have emerged as potential candidates due to their beneficial attributes (e.g., high surface area, ease of functionalization and facile separation). Herein, we provide a concise overview of the rational design of heterogeneous photocatalysts by grafting photoactive complexes on heterogeneous support matrices via covalent grafting and their detailed characterization techniques, which have been followed by the landmark examples of their applications. Also, major challenges and opportunities in the forthcoming progress of these appealing areas are emphasised.

Introduction to photocatalysts

Sunlight is a clean, green, plentiful, inexhaustible, and widely distributed sustainable energy source, which has played an instrumental role since the evolution of life on Earth. Inspired by nature, artificial photosynthesis has been attracting immense attention from the scientific community as a promising candidate to tackle the current energy and environmental crisis by utilizing the vivid and renewable energy from sunlight.1–4 This untapped potential of sunlight has been well exploited in photocatalytic processes, wherein solar energy is used to drive thermodynamically uphill reactions to generate desired products. It is worth mentioning that visible-light-driven photocatalysis has proven its dominance and significance in various fields of chemistry such as organic transformations, dye degradation, H2 generation via water splitting, and CO2 reduction owing to its high proficiency, non-polluting nature, and mild conditions for substrate activation to solve various synthetic problems.5,6 The term “photocatalysis” is derived from Greek words, where “photo” stands for light and “catalysis” means a process to enhance the rate of reaction by the addition of a substance without itself getting consumed during the course of reaction. Thus, photocatalysis involves the concurrent arrangement of photochemistry and catalysis to accelerate a chemical reaction.

When a photocatalyst (PC) is irradiated with light of suitable wavelength, it gets photoexcited and transfers an electron from the ground state or the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of a molecule to its excited electronic state or the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). The photoexcited catalyst (PC*) accepts or donates a single electron through oxidative or reductive quenching cycles depending on the substrates and reagents present in the reaction mixture.7 Here, the photoexcited species acts as the reactive catalytic species and help in achieving cleavage of challenging bonds and the activation of organic species or the generation of radicals to promote a chemical transformation, which cannot be realized by classical organic chemistry techniques. Thus, “photocatalysis” has emerged as an environment friendly synthetic route where in the absence of acids, bases, and water-sensitive reagents such as Grignard reagents, the activation of innocuous starting reagents can be achieved facilely, which drastically reduces or limits the production of hazardous by-products during chemical synthesis. Lately, a major breakthrough has been achieved in this field with the discovery of energy efficient and economical irradiation systems for visible light-mediated organic transformations, which have garnered significant attention from scientists in photoinduced reactions to activate substrates, reagents, and catalytic intermediates under mild conditions by employing cost-effective, clean, and low-consumption irradiation setups.

Early breakthroughs in the field of photoredox catalysis

The utilization of photocatalysts (PC) has gained widespread recognition as a powerful tool for achieving the activation of organic motifs by using low-energy photons, paving the path towards the sustainable synthesis of chemicals by promoting the use of non-hazardous and environment-friendly reagents. However, the use of solar energy to initiate organic transformations is not a novel concept. This idea was originally proposed by Ciamician as early as 1912.8 Nevertheless, the potential of photocatalysts was identified by Fujishima and Honda in 1972 for water splitting using a titanium dioxide electrode and irradiating it with UV light and a weak external electric field of 0.5 V,9 which made photocatalysis one of the oldest explored techniques for transforming solar energy into chemical energy. In 1976, Carey and co-workers investigated the photocatalytic degradation of organic pollutants by utilizing TiO2 as a photocatalytic material. Three years later, Inoue et al. reported the photocatalytic reduction of CO2 to numerous organic moieties using semiconductor powders such as TiO2, ZnO, SiC, GaP, and CdS.10 Since then, the development of highly efficient photocatalysts has gained remarkable consideration and become a hot topic of research, as evident through the exponential growth in the number of publications (Fig. 1). Due to its versatility, this technique holds immense potential for use in a wide range of demanding applications such as environmental remediation, organic transformations, water splitting, and fuel generation. Besides, photocatalysts have also been successfully utilized (i) as components of dye-sensitized solar cells and organic light-emitting diodes, (ii) to initiate polymerization reactions, and (iii) in photo-dynamic therapy.11 In recent decades, the three pioneers of this area namely, Noble Laureate MacMillan, Yoon and Stephenson, have contributed immensely by bringing photoredox catalysts from laboratory curiosity to industrial mainstay.11–23 There are thousands of articles reporting photocatalytic synthesis relying on a small set of photocatalysts including homogeneous iridium and ruthenium polypyridyl complexes, a few organic dyes, and some semiconducting materials with appropriate redox potentials or triplet energies and long-lived excited states.7 In the past few decades, researchers have focused on two different classes of photocatalysts, namely, homogeneous and heterogeneous photocatalysts. Homogeneous photocatalysts are generally molecular complexes composed of well-defined coordination complexes of Ru(BPy)3 and Ir(PPy)3. Iridium(III) and ruthenium(II) complexes have endured as two of the most prevalent materials in the family of PCs. To date, several of these visible light-driven organometallic photocatalysts have been designed and fabricated to catalyse numerous organic/chemical transformations.24

Various fascinating properties of photocatalysts

Photocatalysis exhibits numerous advantages over conventional methodologies such as utilization of an eco-friendly and sustainable energy source, i.e., light, which is clean, abundant, renewable and does not generate any residue. Interestingly, highly efficient photocatalytic processes offer numerous advantages, which include working under mild reaction conditions and reduced possibility of the formation of side-products. The other fascinating features of photoredox catalysts are as follows: (1) absorption in the visible light range; (2) long-lived photoexcited states, which have sufficient lifetime for reacting with the organic substrates and catalysing transformations via single-electron transfer (SET) processes; (3) highly oxidizing or reducing species in either the ground or excited state compared to organic substrates; (4) sufficiently reversible electrochemical behaviour and photostability and (5) ease of tailoring the chemical structures and redox properties, rendering designability and tunability. Owing to the aforementioned versatile properties of photocatalysts, they are emerging as promising candidates to fulfil the goals of green and sustainable chemistry. Henceforth, the fabrication and application of visible-light-responsive photocatalysts is being immensely explored by researchers, wherein solar light has been utilized efficiently, which consists of a huge portion of visible light (ca. 43%).

Limitations and challenges in the field of photocatalysis

An all-encompassing search for reports on “photocatalysis” and “heterogeneous photocatalysis” on the Web of Science revealed that significant progress has been made in this field as the publication of articles related to visible light photoredox-catalyzed reactions have continuously increased since 1990.

Despite their remarkable potential to catalyse a wide range of distinct transformations (such as reaction type, versatility and mild reaction conditions), the use of photocatalysts has been mainly investigated in the homogeneous phase, where the recoverability and reusability of the catalysts are considered challenging. Additionally, the intrinsic high cost, limited availability on Earth, difficult isolation and poor recycling ability of homogeneous photocatalysts impede their practical use in large-scale industrial applications. Moreover, the inability to recycle the catalyst leads to heavy metal contamination of the organic products and increases the processing and waste disposal costs in large-scale reactions.5 Consequently, the wide-ranging expensive metal-based photoactive transition-metal complexes (Ru, Ir, Re, Pt etc.) with a high metal loading cannot meet large-scale application demands. Hence, the design and fabrication of highly effective and novel recyclable materials are desirable.

Similarly, the economical organic dye-based photocatalysts are effectively utilized to furnish numerous organic transformations. These dyes are known to have a notable negative impact on the environment due to their sluggish degradation, photobleaching and propensity to act as fluorescent pollutants,6 also exhibiting detrimental effects on photocatalytic systems. In fact, the low photostability of organic dyes, such as in the case of Rose Bengal (RB) is issue, which hinders their heterogenization over a support for accelerating reactions.25

To address the problems associated with catalyst retrieval and proceed towards designing robust and sustainable processes, numerous studies to develop new heterogeneous photocatalytic systems by the physicochemical deposition of photoactive sites on a diverse range of matrices such as inorganic semiconductors such as TiO2 or carbene nitride have been reported.26–32 In this review article, we describe the fabrication of catalysts based on surface science and solid-state chemistry, where the surface atoms serve as the active sites for reactions. However, these physicochemical methodologies have some limitations such as the uneven distribution and leaching of the catalyst, which are major drawbacks, ultimately leading to a reduction in the catalytic activity of the designed heterogeneous catalyst. Additionally, semiconductor-type photocatalysts such as TiO2 and ZnO typically require UV irradiation for excitation owing to their large band gaps and limited photo response, together with their inconvenient recovery and reuse, which restrict their commercial applications.33–38 The selectivity for the desired product also remains a significant concern due to the involvement of free radical intermediates in UV-induced chemical reactions. This issue becomes acute during the selective oxidation of organic compounds with molecular oxygen (O2), wherein O2 can be easily attached to free radical intermediates, thus leading to uncontrolled autooxidation products. UV-induced holes are also not only highly oxidative but can generate a variety of oxygen species to induce the formation of undesirable products and lead to lower selectivity for the desired product.39 Besides, the classical and hazardous UV radiation-catalysed reactions further demand massive and costly irradiation setups and often cause unselective reactions, which are problematic to predict and control.

The application of transition-metal based complexes as photocatalysts is restricted on the commercial scale due to their high cost and lack of sustainability because of their low reusability. Thus, to overcome these limitations, the immobilization of the photocatalytic active sites with functional efficiency and increased reproducibility has been found to be promising despite their high cost. Henceforth, the design and development of more robust, versatile and heterogeneous visible light-driven photocatalytic systems based on transition metals are urgent. In this review, we highlight the heterogenization of active homogeneous photocatalytic units for the synthesis of various organic scaffolds, which holds promise to use insolation (incident sunlight) to drive both thermodynamically and kinetically uphill conversions. The utilization of numerous suitable support materials for heterogenization is the focus of this review. Different materials can be chosen as the matrix or support system for grafting of the photoactive sites such as inert polymers and inorganic materials. The ideal carrier matrix should have the following properties: (i) low cost, (ii) inert nature, (iii) high stability, (iv) mechanical strength, (v) charge transfer ability, (vi) reusability, and (vii) ability to prevent nonspecific adsorption and inactivation.40 Herein, the numerous applications of heterogeneous photocatalysts ranging from organic transformations and dye degradation to water splitting are summarized.

Approach to overcome the above-mentioned limitations

Due to the high commercial value and increasing risk of exhaustion of the expensive transition metal-based photocatalysts, their recovery and reuse are highly desirable. Thus, to overcome the above-mentioned issues, scientists have started devoting their efforts on the development of novel, heterogeneous photocatalysts by employing top-down approaches and bottom-up approaches, which include co-precipitation, chemical reduction, chemical and physical vapour deposition, mechanical milling, chemical, sol–gel technique, laser pyrolysis, and solvothermal–hydrothermal method, which are also important from an environment friendly standpoint. Heterogeneous photocatalysts exhibit numerous advantages compared with their analogous homogeneous counterparts, for example, ease of catalyst separation, minimal product contamination, and remarkable reusability.41 Thus far, the scientific community has widely utilized semiconductor- and layered sheet-based photocatalysts for the effective removal of pollutants from water and air towards advancement of field of self-cleaning and anti-bacterial materials. However, although these conventional heterogeneous photocatalysts display high application value, they exhibit moderately inadequate photochemical activity in the visible-light range42 due to their fast photoelectron–hole recombination rate and slow transport together with sluggish interfacial activity and poisoning deactivation. Recently, scientists have made significant progress in the field by optimizing the energy bandgap of catalysts through surface modification, extending their light absorption in the visible light range. However, the photocatalysts synthesized by these approaches suffer from various obstacles including agglomeration of many metal-based species on the surface of the support during dehydration. The uniform distribution of active sites is essential to balance both the high catalytic activity and desired physical and chemical features. Hereafter, the quest to design facile and reusable heterogeneous photocatalytic systems that work in the visible light range has led to the development of various strategies such as immobilization on a heterogeneous core, tagging for phase separation, encapsulation (ship-in-bottle approach), adsorption, and electrospraying. One of these strategies is to covalently anchor the photoactive sites on robust materials to obtain well-defined active sites with enhanced interfacial charge transfer, which allows the facile separation and reuse of expensive and scarce metal-based photocatalysts. The fabrication of robust and visible light-responsive heterogeneous photocatalysts has triggered excitement in the research community as next-generation photocatalysts due to their facile synthesis, appealing electronic band structure, high physicochemical stability and reusability.43–49 This approach involves immobilizing the active species by chemical engineering or coordination chemistry on a heterogeneous support to achieve the goal of circular economy without hampering their catalytic properties. An amenable and potential material is required, which has high stability and does not self-degrade or leach. To date, investigators have employed various organic and inorganic supports such as silica, MOFs, POPs, COFs, metal oxides, nanoparticles, semiconductors, glass wool, carbon, graphene oxide, carbon nitride, boron nitride, biomaterial-based supports, amorphous aluminosilicates, zeolites, dendrimers, clay, Nafion and many more for the surface engineering of catalytic systems.50–53 Here, the covalent grafting of molecular photocatalytic motifs on suitable materials can directly resolve the pre-existing issues, and simultaneously meet the requirements of high activity and selectivity of homogeneous catalysts as well as the stability, reaction medium separation, and improved usage cycles of heterogeneous catalysts.

(ii) Numerous photochemical applications of heterogeneous photocatalysts.

(iii) Numerous physico-chemical techniques utilized to characterize catalysts.

(v) Future directions to overcome the accompanying challenges.

Engineering of photoactive complexes on various support matrices

In the past few years, there has been a surge in interest in the expansion of heterogeneous photocatalysts by taking advantage of the versatile platform of numerous cores. To date, several heterogeneous cores such as silica, magnetic nanoparticles, polymers and metal-organic frameworks have been employed for immobilization/anchoring photocatalytic active species by post-grafting, cross-linking or direct encapsulation to enable the easy separation and potential reusability of catalysts.54–57 These essential components of the emerging chemical methodologies will satisfy the need of recyclable and reusable systems and will meet the criteria of green and sustainable catalytic systems. As apparent from Fig. 1, the rapidly increasing number of reports in the literature on supported photocatalysts suggest that it is the right time to deliver a comprehensive and state-of-the-art review on various photoactive site anchored nanomaterials from a broad perspective considering the progress on this hot research topic. A large number of inspiring reviews has been reported on semiconductor-based heterogeneous photocatalyst covering their synthetic techniques, characteristic features, and promising applications.9,58–61 Nevertheless, the covalent grafting of photoactive complexes on a support matrix for numerous applications via visible light irradiation has not been thoroughly discussed thus far. This review covers all the reports encompassing the rational design and construction of photocatalysts from the perspective of engineering strategies by utilizing a diverse range of support materials such as silica, polymer, clay, and MOFs to achieve photoredox applications for solar energy conversion, environmental remediation, and transformations.

Mesoporous cellular silica foams (MCFs)

The captivating properties of mesoporous silica such as high specific surface area, uniform pore size, confinement effects inside its channels, controllable hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity, high stability and robust structural framework have been successfully accessed to develop numerous photocatalytic systems of interest.62–72
Depolymerization reaction. Considering the copious advantages of mesoporous materials, Zhang and group designed a mesoporous cellular silica-structured photoredox catalyst, (Ir(ppy)2(bpy)-MCFs).73

The fabricated photoredox catalyst is depicted in Fig. 2, wherein thiol-functionalized mesoporous cellular silica cores were grafted with a vinyl-tagged [Ir(ppy)2(bpy)]PF6 complex via thiol–ene click reaction. The authors utilized the synthesized Ir(ppy)2(bpy)-MCFs to carry out the highly selective and reactive reductive depolymerization of oxidized lignin β-O-4 model compounds under visible light irradiation. Moreover, the aerogel-like three-dimensional porous structure led to a significant reduction in the mass transfer resistance and photon propagation hindrance phenomenon. In addition, the developed catalyst exhibited recyclability for up to six runs.

Water splitting. Lately, the scientific community has shown profound interest in water splitting given that the abundant and clean sunlight can directly be employed in this process for the production of the valuable hydrogen energy. In past few decades, several photocatalysts have been employed to achieve efficient water splitting for hydrogen production with high efficiency.61,74–78 However, significant challenges need to be accomplished to enhance the performance of photocatalysts given that they suffer from the inherent disadvantages of low light utilization and rapid photogenerated electron–hole recombination in the semiconductor. Henceforth, the development of advanced and efficient photocatalytic systems is urgent. The report by Liu and group provides a potential answer for the current bottleneck of photocatalytic water splitting. The authors devoted their efforts to fabricating an active and durable silica-supported photocatalyst, which demonstrated practical utility in photocatalytic hydrogen evolution from formate and aldehyde.73 The [IrCp*Cl(μ-Cl)]2 (Cp* = η5-pentamethyl cyclopentadienyl) complex was anchored on bipyridine-based organosilica nanotubes (BPy-ENT) to achieve a promising organic-inorganic hybrid solid photocatalytic material (Ir-BPy-ENT) having numerous desirable properties such as stable nanotube structure, high surface area, and confinement effects inside its channels due to its large pore diameter, which reduced the diffusion restraint and facilitated the easy transport of substrates (Fig. 3). Furthermore, the synthesized composite also possessed controllable hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity and isolated active sites.
Fig. 3 Synthetic routes and photocatalysis of molecular heterogeneous catalysts based on organosilica nanotubes. Reproduced with permission from ref. 73. Copyright 2018, Elsevier B.V.

The fabricated heterogeneous iridium-grafted photocatalyst (Ir-BPy-ENT) could deliver high efficiency in the pH-dependent production of clean and highly pure H2 (TOF: 16.2 h−1 and quantum yield: ∼8.0%) from formate in a promising way. Furthermore, the conditions employed for the production of hydrogen were fairly green given that the reaction was carried out under mild conditions (visible light illumination (λ > 420 nm), weakly basic conditions and room temperature) in comparison to the analogous homogeneous catalyst. Furthermore, this group also designed a new approach to generate hydrogen from aldehyde–water solution on exposure to visible light, which was not studied or reported before. The recyclability of the catalyst was also investigated, which revealed its high reusability given that it showed high photocatalytic activity and stability for up to 6 cycles.

Likewise, Inagaki and co-workers reported the fabrication of periodic mesoporous organosilicas (PMOs) composed of large amounts of ruthenium(II) tris(bipyridine) complexes grafted in their pore walls as a solid photosensitizer (Fig. 4).79 Even after the complex was grafted in the pore walls, the properties of the homogeneous complex remained intact given that it exhibited strong absorption in the visible light region up to 600 nm due to its metal–ligand charge transfer band, and also showed redox behaviour at ∼0.8 V versus Ag/AgNO3 due to the one-electron oxidation. Afterwards, this group demonstrated the application of the developed catalyst for the production of hydrogen and water oxidation by loading platinum and iridium species on the pore surface of the Ru-PMO catalyst, respectively. Furthermore, the catalyst also showed good prospects for use in photocatalytic organic transformations.

Thus, researchers are keen to find a solution or effective technology that can readily reduce or completely eliminate the adverse effects arising from environmental pollution. Amongst the countless proposed methodologies, photocatalysis has gained extensive consideration given that it involves the direct utilization of incident solar light for generating reactive oxygen species to achieve the degradation/mineralization of various pollutants. In this regard, Balasubramanian and co-worker explored this field for effective dye degradation. Oxygen production. Recently, the scope of photocatalysts has also been explored for accomplishing the challenging water oxidation reaction.83 It is worth mentioning that the research group led by Inagaki et al. explored photocatalytic water splitting for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER). In this process, the anchoring approach was employed for the synthesis periodic mesoporous organosilica (PMO) grafted with a tris(2,2′-bipyridine)ruthenium sensitizer, which was further utilized for visible light-driven water oxidation.84 Here, the chelating ligand 2,2′-bipyridine (BPy) acquired a unique pore wall structure in the pores of the PMO, named BPy-PMO, which was responsible for the formation of photoactive metal complexes on the external surface of the material (Fig. 8). This group also reported interesting results, which indicated that in the presence of a sacrificial oxidant, the fine tuning of the photocatalyst could increase the turnover number by up to 20 times under visible light. The photochemical properties of the fabricated catalyst could be engineered precisely by varying the substituents on the BPy ligands such as X-BPy in the PMO framework and two X2bpy, denoted herein as Ru(X)-BPy-PMO, where X is H (2,2′-bipyridine), Me (4,4′-dimethyl-2,2′-bipyridine), t-Bu (4,4′-di-tert-butyl-2,2′-bipyridine) and CO2Me (4,4′-dimethoxycarbonyl-2,2′-bipyridine). Unfortunately, this group was unable to regenerate the catalyst for oxygen production after using it for 10–12 h, which was attributed to the decomposition of Ru complex on the surface, as evident in the case of the homogeneous photocatalytic system Ru(bpy)32+ and colloidal IrO2, where the amount of evolved oxygen levelled off within 1 h.

Organic transformations. To resolve the environmental problems associated with the rapidly increasing level of chemicals in our surroundings, it is imperative to design new methods that are devoid of the use of costly and harsh reagents. Currently, the intrinsic advantages associated with photocatalysis has been successfully utilized to develop ‘green’ methods for the formation of the C–C bond.4,39,85 Photochemical reactions can be performed under unparalleled mild conditions, where the activation of the substrates is attained simply via the absorption of a photon, which leaves no residues, whereas most catalytic methods involve the use of toxic/polluting reagents.86 In this regard, Francis and group described a facile route to synthesize a covalently grafted ruthenium photoredox catalyst by exploiting the carbodiimide-mediated coupling between a carboxylic acid-functionalized ruthenium complex and commercially available amine-functionalized silica beads.87 The highly versatile, stable and reusable silica-bound ruthenium chromophore was further employed to accomplish the intramolecular cyclization of 2-benzylidenehydrazine carbothioamides to afford 5-phenyl-1,3,4-thiadiazol-2-amines with good to excellent yields (Fig. 9). Interestingly, the catalyst exhibited high recyclability and reusability for up to eight times through a simple filtration process without showing any significant loss in catalytic activity.

Similarly, our group also participated in the quest to design and fabricate numerous magnetically retrievable heterogeneous photocatalysts by integrating a silica-coated Fe3O4 shell core and photocatalytic active sites, and further utilized them for various organic transformations (Fig. 11).56,57,89 The highly versatile, magnetic and robust iridium-based photoredox nanocatalyst Ir@PyBz@ASMNPs was employed to achieve sustainable and ambient oxidative C–H arylation and C–H cyanation under visible light having broad substrate adaptability and outstanding recyclability compared to analogous homogeneous catalysts. Polymers

To date, various research groups have effectively exploited the available energy resources for substantial developments in the field of catalysis to meet the current and future global energy demands. For instance, a diverse range of porous cross-linked polymers (PCPs) including crystalline covalent-organic frameworks (a new class of robust, nanoporous materials) due to their high affinity with reaction media have also been used as well-designed organic precursors to fulfil the requirement of heterogeneous photocatalytic systems.90–93 Additionally, the polymer unit as a support matrix offers a high surface area, which is not only beneficial for the mass transfer phenomenon but also provides easy access to active sites. Furthermore, the organic framework ensures the high affinity with organic reactants and their huge aromatic structure also tends to adsorb the sensitive visible light.94 Moreover, polymers possess permanent porosity and their compositions and properties can be easily tuned via copolymerization, which helps to introduce active sites more efficiently at the molecular level. PCPs are more efficient photocatalysts in comparison to MOFs given that the synthesis of MOFs requires unfilled d orbitals as connecting points, which have the tendency to quench the photoexcited states of the photocatalytic building units.5 The above-mentioned exciting properties make these cores a potential candidate for grafting various photocatalytically active moieties.
Organic transformations. Reduced graphene oxide (rGO)

Since its discovery in 2004, graphene has gained attention from researchers due to its superior physical, chemical, mechanical, optical and electrical features, enable an extensive series of applications ranging from electronics to nanoelectromechanical systems. Graphene, a single layer of sp2-bonded carbon atoms tightly packed into a two-dimensional honeycomb structure with high thermal conductivity, offers excellent charge carrier mobility at room temperature and exhibits an extremely high theoretical specific surface area. To date, there have been numerous reports discussing the sustainable synthesis of graphene, including micro-mechanical exfoliation, epitaxial growth, chemical and electrochemical reduction of graphite oxide and bottom-up organic synthesis.111–114 Due to the large number of bonds at the edge sites of graphene, it can be effectively utilized for the modification of π–π conjugation systems with highly reactive atoms or molecules, paving the way to alter its electron density distribution and physical and chemical properties. Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4)

Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) has emerged as a promising candidate as a heterogeneous support. The use of g-C3N4 composites has been widely reported due to their low cost, easy availability and eco-friendly nature. Polymeric graphitic carbon nitride consists of non-metal, earth-abundant elements such as carbon, nitrogen and hydrogen. It is considered to be the most stable allotrope in the CxNy series, owing to its analogous layered structure to graphene. Further, its 2-D structure retains some exclusive and intriguing physical and electronic features of graphene, which aid the photocatalytic activity of g-C3N4 in various applications. The graphite-like planer and π-conjugated systems of g-C3N4 possess unique structural and remarkable physicochemical properties with a band-gap energy of 2.7 eV, making it functionable at around 460 nm in the visible solar spectrum. Further, its high thermal and chemical stability under ambient conditions, biocompatibility, environment-friendly nature and resistance to acidic and alkaline conditions make it a suitable material as a heterogeneous core. Additionally, as a core, it can be easily synthesized in bulk through the thermal polycondensation of cheap nitrogen-containing carbon-based precursors such as dicyanamide, cyanamide, melamine, urea and thiourea. The catalytic materials based on C3N4 have demonstrated wide applications in various fields such as photochemical splitting of water and some reactions of organic oxidation and dehydrogenation.119,120

Clay

Catalyst supports play a significant role in the dispersion of the active species, and therefore they should be cheap and abundantly available to replace the costly supports, which are prepared by cumbersome and tedious procedures. Challenges and future directions in the field of photocatalysis

In the forthcoming years, photocatalysis can be considered a promising avenue for resolving environmental and energy issues, which are a great concern to researchers worldwide. Although, the latest developments in materials science and engineering have brought various advancements in this field, there is an urgent need to design highly efficient, economic and versatile photocatalysts with good stability and reusability for large-scale practical applications. Besides, this field requires further investigation to develop the understanding of photocatalytic reaction pathways and to unveil the complex processes and many significant aspects, which are vital to construct robust and novel photocatalytic materials. Hence, greater insight into the photocatalytic mechanism and structure–activity relationship is crucial for the search of new materials to achieve substantial breakthroughs for the practical use of photocatalysts for environmental and economical purposes. To date, the implementation of photocatalysts in industrial processes has been elusive due to their high cost and excessive consumption time. Thus far, there is a lack of ideal, viable and practical options for photocatalytic processes, which can be utilized on the commercial scale and completely replace the conventional catalysts. Additionally, there is room to design cost effective, reusable, non-toxic, sustainable photocatalysts, which are easy to operate, possess a broad spectrum of activity and employed on a large scale. Besides, the unidentified fate and noxiousness of heterogeneous photocatalysts, their inadequacy to perform on a large scale, confinement of mass transfer and durability issues are still a concern, which can cause the economic loss in full-scale applications of photocatalysis in practical and real environments. Furthermore, there is a research gap in the design of photocatalytic reactors that retain the photocatalyst in different conditions with optimized contact time and irradiation with white light/solar light instead of the harmful UV light without undergoing photocatalytic deactivation, and also assessing the environmental life-cycle.

Besides the above-mentioned issues, photocatalysis also faces some major difficulties, which require the consideration of practitioners in industrial and academic settings. In this regard, several key aspects and strategies can be helpful for material design to overcome the obstacles and limitations, as follows:

(i) The large-scale manufacturing of high-quality precisely controlled (involving anticipated composition, structure and function) photocatalysts is a noteworthy challenge. The research on fabricating ideal photocatalysts using the minimum number of steps is still progressing to fulfil the long-term requirements of the scientific community. Although numerous methodologies have already been established for the novel, sustainable and economical synthesis of heterogeneous photocatalysts with tuneable properties, computational simulation studies for designing advanced photocatalytic active moieties supported on heterogeneous matrices have been a comparatively less explored arena, but their integration with experimental studies during the fabrication process can open new avenues for the growth of the photocatalytic field. Henceforth, researchers should integrate experimental and computational approaches and employ various tools of chemistry and materials science to design and fabricate highly efficient photocatalytic systems on a large scale. Additionally, more attention should be paid to the structure–activity correlation to fully uncover the effect of various groups on the photocatalytic activity of the developed catalyst, study the photochemistry of the catalyst and reaction by employing the fundamental principles of photosynthesis and better understanding of the actual role of the active components.

(ii) The thermal, hydrothermal, and chemical stability of photocatalysts also raise serious concerns for their lifetime, which typically embodies a significant issue in their practical application. For instance, there are thousands of reports that shed light on various photocatalysts possessing excellent light-harvesting capabilities. Unfortunately, their photochemical stability is not attractive to make them as suitable candidates. Additionally, the photoactive motifs may undergo leaching, deterioration, aggregation, and agglomeration, which will severely affect the photophysical properties and photocatalytic proficiencies. Besides, the deactivation of photocatalysts by poisoning is another fatal downside for their industrial application given that they can be easily poisoned and may lose their activity owing to the surface adsorption of products or intermediates, which inhibit the absorption of photons and also impede the reactant-molecule diffusion to the active sites. Thus far, some of the other critical challenges in the field of photocatalysis include light absorption in a wide range and excitation to generate charge carriers, prevention of charge recombination, improvement in intrinsic activity, inhibition of poisoning deactivation, and expedition of the diffusion, adsorption, and desorption of molecules. Another significant problem that requires attention from the scientific community is mass transfer hindrance and aggregation-induced deactivation of the catalyst.148

(iii) The majority of photocatalysts can easily achieve fast (∼1–10 ps) intersystem crossing to form triplet excited states with microsecond lifetimes. However, the shortened excited-state lifetime of photocatalysts, which leads to a decrease in the photoluminescence quantum yield, limits their wide utilisation. Henceforth, attention should be devoted to developing strategies to achieve the goal of high relaxation time by improving the morphology of catalysts and developing methodologies for the restoration of the exhausted photocatalyst formed due to the quenching of the excited state.149

(iv) One of the major challenges faced by materials scientists is to achieve the efficient synthesis of photocatalysts on an industrial scale. Unfortunately, a huge gap persists between the academic level research results and the requirements for practical applications. Therefore, a comprehensive circular economy model that supports economic development, and simultaneously contributes harmoniously towards the benefit of businesses, society and the environment should be considered in comparison to the linear model of “take-make-waste” concept. Therefore, during the synthesis of the desired catalyst, a balance between green chemistry and profitable protocols should be maintained to increase the environmental and societal benefits together with health and economic gains.

(v) To date, the research in field of utilization of cost-effective, non-toxic and earth-abundant photocatalytic motifs and their heterogenization is still elusive. The earth-abundant metal complexes of Co, Cu, Mn and Ni act as potential candidates and need to be further explored in the near future.

(vi) The photocatalytic reaction processes and the structure–activity correspondence of photocatalysts are still elusive. Thus far, researchers are engrossed in half-reactions of either oxidation or reduction. Henceforth, there is room for improvement by selecting the most suitable pathway and intermediates with the help of modern physico-chemical characterization techniques and theoretical calculations to determine the mechanism at the atomic level. In the interim, the effect of the interface microstructure and electronic surroundings on photocatalytic activity should be further investigated.

(vii) Visible light-driven synthesis is usually accomplished by employing light-emitting diode (LED) lamps, which often have different specifications such as emission spectra and photon outputs. More accurate descriptions of the light sources and reactor arrangements are needed given that they have a significant effect on photochemical transformations. Additionally, special emphasis must be paid to the expansion of hybrid photocatalysts to drive various chemical transformations under sunlight.

(viii) The initiation of electronic transitions from the ground state to the excited state requires radiation of a particular wavelength, precisely monochromatic, whereas most light sources are polychromatic and emit a wide range of wavelengths, making them inappropriate for excitation. This decreases the energy efficiency and increases the costs; therefore, further research is required in this direction.

(ix) The thick quartz walls used in photoreactors are not only delicate but also expensive, thus making the set-up costs higher. Additionally, the problem of fouling in the photoreactors needs to be tackled efficiently.

Mostly, the promising tactics that have been developed to unravel the above-mentioned challenges and achieve improved photocatalysts can be grouped into three categories, i.e., structural control, compositional optimization, and combined effect of multiple catalysis. It is appealing to note that the morphologies and structural aspects of heterostructures play a pivotal role in their photocatalytic features. For example, hierarchical structure building and structure control, porous networks, high specific surface areas and improved facet exposure can boost the quantum efficiency by further assisting the diffusion of reactants and products, increasing the density of active sites, and enhancing the photon flux densities. Moreover, the photophysical properties can further be enhanced by evaluating the effect of composition (foreign element doping, heterostructure building, and surface chemistry modification) for optimizing the energy band structure, which can enhance the light absorption, enable interface electron transfer, reduce the overpotential, and improve the photocatalytic activity. Henceforth, highly efficient systems should be designed to tackle the above-mentioned problems and improve the photocatalytic properties, which can further provide a practical way to achieve suitable materials for designing of photocatalytic systems. It is sincerely anticipated that multiple collaborations in this rapidly evolving field can lead to major breakthroughs in terms of efficiency and activity for the commercialization and industrialization of photocatalysts. The interdisciplinary collaboration between chemists, environmentalists and engineers can help in designing photocatalytic systems where the solar irradiation time can be decoupled from the photocatalytic application to achieve high efficiency during the periods of no illumination or when the light is turned off and the reaction proceeds by stored solar energy.150 Additionally, it is anticipated that researchers will employ practical and theoretical tools to develop strategies to deal with the persisting challenges in this field by constructing efficient and viable heterogeneous photocatalysts with enhanced redox ability, ease of tunability and retrievability and will fully utilize the broad spectrum of sunlight give that only 43% of the solar energy reaching the Earth's surface consists of visible wavelength irradiation (400 to 700 nm), which is employed in the photocatalysis process. Besides, the proficiency of photocatalysts also depends on both the reaction thermodynamics and kinetics. Consequently, the rational design of the reactor and the assistance of an external field (thermal and electronic field), photo-electrons, photo-thermal, and plasmonic catalysis can support electron separation and improved transmission efficacy, and reduce the activation energy of the reactants. The insufficient details and information on the instructions and parameters of the photoreactor set-up such as light source, light uniformity and power, reactor geometry, agitation intensity (flow rate/stirring rate) and temperature control151 is a serious engineering-based problem that needs to be addressed separately.

Conclusions

During the last decade, scientists have gained immense interest in the growing field of designing and fabricating novel visible light-driven photocatalytic systems with adequate redox properties in their excited states and expanded reactivity window to achieve visible complex chemical reactions. In this review, we focused on the major goal of replacing the expensive homogeneous transition metal photoredox complexes by anchoring them on heterogeneous materials to retain and amplify their prolonged charge recombination and broader visible light absorption capabilities. A comprehensive analysis of different solid supports such as silica, polymers, and MOFs was carried out for the fabrication of cost-effective, highly selective and reusable photocatalysts via covalent grafting with materials possessing superior surface areas and remarkable photocatalytic efficiency. We also presented a summary of the intelligent strategies that have been anticipated based on the goals of improving the photocatalytic activity, attaining structural functionalization, and augmenting catalytic efficiency via the functionalization of photocatalytic structures.

The matrix-supported light active motifs have a bright future in the judicious catalytic synthesis of valuable organic motifs due to the ease of deliberately tailoring and tunability of their photophysical properties by modification of the ligands to suit a specific catalytic, purpose, which is of significance for artificial synthesis to mimic photosynthesis and conduct some photoreactions that are impossible to accomplish with semiconductor-based photocatalyst. Also, the well-defined reusable structure of photocatalysts at the atomic level allows the explicit assessment of their structure–activity relationships and application of the catalytic mechanism to a demanding reaction, and the uniform catalytic site distribution and isolation in three-dimensionality space make them readily available for substrates, which deliver countless benefits in terms of catalytic efficiency and economic perspective. According to the abundant examples reported on the use of heterogeneous photocatalysts under various conditions, it can be concluded that the supported photoactive moieties act as good candidates with high activity and selectivity to achieve a wide variety of chemical reactions, varying from degradation, water splitting, and CO2 reduction to C–C and C–X (X = halide) bond formation. Unfortunately, this field still faces enormous challenges for the fabrication of novel photocatalytic systems with superior efficiency in the photochemical transformations but it is envisioned that the above-mentioned limitations will be addressed in the near future. Moreover, the future trend of enhancing the performance of solar/visible light-driven materials by utilizing post-illumination catalytic memory is expected to use semiconductor coupling or dye sensitization rather than continuous exposure with light. We foresee that heterogeneous photocatalysts containing a diverse range of photoactive chromophores and even incompatible functionalities will be assembled to achieve and boost some new research topics and open new doors to the scientific community.
