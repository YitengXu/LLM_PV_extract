Photophysics of metal complexes

Paul I. P. Elliott *
Department of Chemical & Biological Sciences, University of Huddersfield, Queensgate, Huddersfield, HD1 3DH, UK. E-mail: p.i.elliott@hud.ac.uk

First published on 8th May 2012

Abstract

This review summarises the literature reported in 2011 on the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear supramolecular assemblies.

Highlights

Highlights in this year's literature include the synthesis and photophysical study of a conjugated hexakis(zincporphyrin) nano-ring assembly, near-infrared to visible up-conversion in a Cr2Er triple helicate complex and a record high efficiency dye-sensitised solar cell utilising a zinc porphyrin dye with cobalt diimine-based electrolyte.

Reviews

A number of reviews have appeared in the area of the photophysics of metal complexes including an entire issue of Coordination Chemistry Reviews (issue 21–22) featuring areas including luminescent platinum, iridium and rhenium complexes and dipyridylphenazene complexes.1 Reviews have also appeared regarding the tuning of ruthenium complexes for DSSC applications,2 luminescent chemosensing gold(I) complexes,3 the design of 2-phenylpyridine-based Ir(III) and Pt(II) OLED phosphors,4 metal-centred states of metal complexes,5 spin-orbit calculations for relativistic effects in the photophysics of heavy-metal complexes,6 heavy-metal complexes for bioimaging,7,8 Pt(II), Au(I) and Hg(II) acetylides as optical power limiting materials,9 d6, d8 and d10 luminescent metal complexes,10 fluorescent and colourimetric sensors for silver, gold and platinum ions,11 proton-coupled electron transfer from metal complex excited states,12 cyclometallated gold(III) complexes13 and luminescent lanthanide molecular thermometers.14

Complexes of main group elements

A conjugate of a N,N-bis(2-phenylthioethyl)amine binding domain and a 1,2-dihydroxyanthraquinone fluorophore is a selective and sensitive sensor for Al(III) ions through switching off of fluorescence-quenching PET processes on Al3+ binding.15 Al(III) complexes of a tetraiodinated corrole ligand exhibits deep red emission (612 to 700 nm) from a long-lived triplet excited state (τ = 92 μs in degassed toluene).16 The effects of the presence of both electron-donating and electron-withdrawing 8-hydroxyquinolate (q) ligand substituents on the photophysical properties of their Alq3 complexes has been investigated. High OLED efficiencies of up to 4.6% were observed using these fluorophores.17 The tris-homoleptic Al(III) complex of the fused-ring hydroxyquinolate ligand 11H-indolo[3,2-c]quinolin-4-ol exhibits deep blue luminescent emission and three times the fluorescent quantum yield compared to the parent Alq3.18 A series of stable indium bacteriochlorins have been prepared and show NIR absorption (741–782 nm).19 Fluorescent indium and gallium bis(thiosemicarbazonate) complexes have been investigated as confocal luminescent imaging agents.20 A series of pyrrolyldipyrrinato tin(IV) complexes have been prepared and have fluorescent quantum yields between 0.28 and 0.61.21 Cyclen-functionalised perylenediimides have been reported as selective and highly sensitive fluorescent probes for lead(II) ions and can be used for imaging of Pb(II) in living cells.22

Copper, silver and gold

Bright blue/white luminescent complexes of the form [Cu(pop)(N⁁N)] (pop = bis(2-(diphenylphosphanyl)phenyl)ether, N⁁N is a pyrazolylborate-based ligand) with quantum yields up to 90% have been reported. Room temperature emission is 1MLCT derived fluorescence in character with this emissive state being thermally populated from a lower lying 3MLCT state.23 [Cu(P⁁P)(N⁁N)] complexes (P⁁P is a chelating bisphosphine, N⁁N is a pyrrole- or indole-aldimine ligand) exhibit luminescent emission with from mixed 3IL and 3MLCT states with efficiencies up to 2.9%.24 The complex [(1,2-bis{di-o-tolylphosphino}benzene)CuBr] exhibits bright green luminescence and results in OLED devices with an external efficiency of 21.3%.25 Copper(I) dppb complexes of amido-1,2,3-triazolate ligands exhibit luminescent emission from yellow to orange-red in the solid state and have modest quantum yield in solution (up to 2.2%). The emissive states are assigned as mixtures of 3ILCT & 3MLCT or 3LLCT & 3MLCT.26 The complex [Ir(ppy)2(L)] (L is a dipicolylaminomethyl-appended 2-(benzothienyl)pyridine ligand) acts as a ratiometric sensor for Cu2+ under physiological conditions.27 DSSC devices prepared using Cu(I) complexes with bpy ligands functionalised at the 6-position with furyl, thienyl, methylpyrrolyl, phenyl and methyl substituents exhibit efficiencies approaching 1.5% (versus 4.5% for N719).28 Neutral Cu(I) complexes such as [Cu(qbm)(PPh3)2]BF4 exhibit blue-shifted and longer-lived phosphorescent emission than their cationic counterparts, such as [Cu(Hqbm)(PPh3)2] (Hbqm = 2-(2′-quinolyl)benzimidazole), attributed to additional ligand-centred ππ* transitions besides MLCT transitions.29 [Cu(N⁁N)(P⁁P)]PF6 complexes (N⁁N = bpy or phen, P⁁P = pop or dppb) yield light emitting electrochemical cells with efficiencies approaching those based on Ru(II) and Ir(III) complexes.30 Receptor I has been reported as a selective two-photon fluorescent probe for Cu(I) ions and has been used to image Cu(I) in live cells using two-photon fluorescence microscopy.31 A Cu(II) complex with a tridentate dansyl-functionalised ligand acts as a turn-on fluorescent sensor for nitric oxide.32 A sensor comprising a tricarbocyanine receptor and a 2,2′-azanediyl bis(N-hydroxyacetamide) NIR fluorophore gives a 10-fold enhancement in NIR emission in the presence of Cu2+ and has been applied in live cell imaging.33

An octadecanuclear Ag(I) cluster with the formula [Ag18(tttmb)12](NO3)18 (tttmp = 1,3,5-tris-(triazol-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene) has been reported which exhibits a large increase in luminescent intensity and lifetime with decreasing temperature. Emission is ascribed to a 3LMMCT state admixed with a 3LC state.34 Selective and sensitive sensors for Ag(I) ions have been developed based on a pyrene-functionalised rhodanineacetic acid35 and a di(phenylselenoethyl)amine-funcationalised coumarin.36 Films of a trinuclear bis(3,5-trifluoromethyl)pyrazolate Ag(I) complex exhibit selective vapochromic sensing of benzene.37

A cluster with the formula [Au6(C)Cu2(dppy)6]4+ (dppy = 2-(diphenylphosphino)pyridine) with an unprecedented trigonal prismatic Au6 core containing a hypervalent C-atom has been reported. The cluster exhibits bright red luminescent emission.38 A reported gold(III) complex of a tris(pentafluorophenyl)corrole ligand brominated at each β-pyrrole position exhibits rare emission in room temperature solution (λmax 769 nm, τ = 195 ps, ϕ = 0.003).39 A series of highly luminescent octanuclear Au(I)/Cu(I) clusters with the formula [Au6Cu2(CCR)6(P⁁P)2]2+ (P⁁P = 1,4-bis(diphenylphosphino)benzene) have been reported. When R = 1-cyclohexanolyl emission at 488 nm with a quantum yield of 94% is observed.40 Gold(I) complexes containing oligo(o- or m-phenyleneethynylene) ligands exhibit emission accounted for from short-lived prompt emission and long-lived delayed emission, the mechanism for the latter involving triplet–triplet annihilation.41 A series of Cu/Au systems of the form {[Au(C6X5)2][Cu(NCR)(μ2-C4H4N2)]}n (X = F, Cl; R = Me, Ph, –CHCHPh) are brightly luminescent when R is Me or Ph stemming from a pyrimidine-localised MLCT state whereas the styrenyl substituent results in non-radiative decay from a nitrile ligand based MLCT state.42 Cyclometallated gold(III) complexes of the type [(C⁁N)Au(CCR)2] have been reported (C⁁N = ppy, dfppy, 2-(thien-2-yl)pyridine, benzoquinoline, phenylisoquinoline; R = aryl, silyl groups). Emission occurs from metal perturbed C⁁N 3IL states allowing emission tuning on the basis of the identity of C⁁N.43,44 Organodiselone Au(I)-Tl(I) complexes such as II are luminescent in the solid state and at 77 K with lifetimes in the ns range. The emissive state is an admixture of 3MMLCT and 3LC states.45 Tetra- and octanuclear dendritic-like arylthiolategold(I) complexes around an oligophosphino core display mainly 3LMCT based emission with F-, MeO- and Me- 4-aryl substituents and 3LC ππ* based emission with –NO2 substituents.46 An 8-hydroxyquinoline-funcationalised rhodamine-based receptor acts as a reversible fluorescent sensor for gold(III) ions with high selectivity and has been used for imaging of gold(III) in living cells.47

Zinc, cadmium and mercury

As zinc is a biologically relevant element there has been significant interest in the development of luminescent sensors for Zn(II) ions. Examples that have appeared in the literature include a complex of the form [Ir(dfppy)2(phen)]+ with a pendant dipyridylamine receptor,48 a N,N-bis(2-pyridylmethyl)ethylenediamine chelator with a pendant 2-aminobenzamide group,49 a (1,2,3-triazolylmethyl)(8-quinolinyl)ether rececptor,50 a salicylaldehyde salicylhydrazone Schiff base derivative,51 di- and tripyrrin based receptors,52 and 2-(o-aminoophenyoxy)-N-(quinol-8-yl)acetamide-,53trans-(1R,2R)-N′,N′′-bis[(quinolin-2-yl)methyl]cyclohexane-1,2-diamine-,54 1-(benzimidazol-2-ylmethyl]-1,4,8,11-tetra-azacyclotetradecane-,55N,N′′-bis(6-methoxy-2-quinolyl-methyl)diethylenetriamine-based56 ligands. 2,2′-(piperazine-1,4-diyl)bis(N-(quinolin-8-yl)acetamide) acts a dual-mode fluorescent sensor for Zn(II) and Cu(II) ions, coordinating as the imidic acid tautomer with Zn(II) and as the amide-deprotonated form with Cu(II).57 As poisons and environmental pollutants, sensors for Cd(II) and Hg(II) ions are also of interest. The ligand 2,3,6,7,10,11-hexakis(2-pyridyl)dipyrazino[2,3-f:2′,3′-h]quinoxaline is a selective fluorescent sensor for Cd(II).58 Sensors for Hg(II) have included a bisdansyl-appended lysine receptor,59 a ratiometric pyrenesulfonimide derivative of methionene60 and a BODIPY dye with a benzo[2,1,3]thiadiazole bridge61 for example. The Hg(II) complex of an anthracene-thymine dyad acts as a selective turn-on fluorescent sensor for iodide ions with a detection limit of 126 nM.62 A coumarin-appended Zn(II) tpy complex acts as a luminescent sensor for biologically important organophosphates.63

A Zn2L2 complex (L = N,N′-bis(2-hydroxybenzilidene)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene-1,3-diamine) acts as an on–off–on sensor and undergoes luminescent quenching in the presence of Cu(II) and enhancement in the presence of Ag(I) ions.64 [M(II)Zn4Cl4L6] clusters (L = 5,6-dimethyl-1,2,3-benzotriazolate) have been prepared. When M = Zn a short-lived excited state is observed populated with high quantum yield but when M = Ru a much longer-lived excited state is formed by singlet to triplet state conversion.65 Zinc complexes of 2,3-bis[(4-dialkylamino-2-hydroxybenzylidene)amino]but-2-enedinitrile salen ligands have been developed as two-photon fluorescent sub-cellular imaging probes.66

Miscellaneous transition metal complexes

The rhodamine B based ligand precursor N-(3′,6′-Bis(diethylamino)-3-oxospiro[isoindoline-1,9′-xanthene]-2-yl)picolinamide is a turn-on fluorescent sensor for Fe(III) and intracellular fluorescent imaging agent.67 An organic assembly comprising BODIPY and cyanine fluorophores and a terpyridyl metal binding domain acts as a ratiometric sensor for Fe(II) ions. On iron binding, MLCT transitions quench cyanine emission whilst leaving BODIPY emission unchanged.68 Clusters with the formula [Mo6X8(n-C3F7CO2)6]2− where X is Br− or I− show extraordinarily bright long-lived red luminescence with ϕ = 0.23 and 0.36 respectively and τ = 370 and 303 μs respectively.69

Rhenium

[Re(bpy)(CO)3(L)]+ complexes (L is a monodentate 1,2,3-triazole ligand) have been shown to exhibit long luminescent lifetimes (∼0.5 μs) in aerated solutions at room temperature.70 The excited state dynamics of complexes of the form [Re(N⁁N)(CO)3L]n+ (N⁁N = bpy, phen, 4,7-dimethyl-phen; L = Cl, n = 0; L = imidazole, n = 1) have been studied by ultrafast spectroscopic methods. Complexes undergo rapid ISC (<150 fs) populating two long-lived triplet states.71 The cluster [Re6S8Cl5(4-ppy)]3− (4-ppy = 4-phenylpyridine) emits red light at 296 K in CH3CN. TA spectroscopy reveals the emissive state to be Re6-core to ppy charge transfer in nature.72 A series of Re(I) diimine carbonyl complexes containing isocyano ligands allow good tuning of emission properties from diimine-localised 3MLLCT excited states.73 [Re(N⁁N)(CO)3(5-aryltetrazolate)] complexes have been prepared and show MLLCT-based absorptions and excited state lifetimes from 102 to 910 ns with quantum yields from 0.012 to 0.099.74 [Re(CO)3(N⁁C)X] complexes (N⁁C = 3-butyl-1-(2-pyridyl)benzimidazolin-2-ylidene, X = Cl, Br) exhibit emission from a 3MLCT state partially mixed with a 3LLCT state.75 [Re(phen)(CO)3] complexes of 3-picolylamino-functionalised anthraquinone ligands show luminescent emission with short-lived anthraquinone emission superimposed on long-lived 3MLCT emission.76 Re(I) tricarbonyl complexes with pyrazolylpyridyl based chelate ligands are luminescent with quantum yields up to 5.1% and exhibit electrochemiluminescent emission properties.77

Ruthenium and osmium

Bithienyl amide-appended Ru(II) phen complexes have been shown to exhibit a very long-lived ligand localised charge separated state (3 to 7 μs) and storage of a large amount of energy (ΔGθ ∼2 eV).78 Radiative deactivation pathways in complexes of the form [Ru(bpy)2(PPh3)X]n+ (n = 1, X = Cl−, Br−, CN−, NO2−; n = 2, X = py, 4-aminopyridine, 4,4′-bpy, NH3, MeCN) via direct population of 1MC states from 1MLCT have been investigated.79 The complex [Ru(bpy)2(dppz)]2+ which is non-luminescent in aqueous solution or in the presence of amyloid-beta peptide monomers exhibit long-lived luminescence upon peptide aggregation and has been used in time-gated imaging studies.80 Exited state tuning of a series of supramolecular building blocks based on Ru(II) 2,2′:4,4′′:4′,4′′′-quaterpyridine (qtpy) complexes has been investigated. The excited states were shown to be qtpy based except where the ancillary ligands are dppz.81 The perylene-3,4:9,10-bis{dicarboximide}-appended (DPI) complex [(5,5-{CC-DPI}2-2,2′-bpy)RuCl2(CNtBu)2] has favourable energetics for singlet PET from Ru to DPI (ΔGCS ∼1 eV). TA spectroscopy reveals ultrafast PET (<150 fs) and fast charge recombination (τ = 63 ps).82 Charge-transfer in [Ru(phen)3]2+ complexes bearing a (dimesityl)boryldurylethynyl group have been investigated. Absorptions involve a mixture of Ru → phen-π* MLCT and phen-π → B charge transfer with emission lifetimes of up to 12 μs being observed.83 A [Ru(bpy)2(dppz)]2+ complex with a dppz-appended benzimidazolyl-coumarin group exhibits a non-emissive 3IL state but sensitises triplet–triplet annihilation up-conversion with DPA.84 [Ru(tpy)2]2+ complexes with ethnynyl- or methyleneoxyterthiophene groups appended at the tpy 4′-position have been prepared. The latter shows quenching of the 3MLCT by transfer to the thiophene T1 state whereas the former shows greatly increased excited state lifetime through an energy reservoir effect.85 The heteroleptic complex [Ru(tpy)(C⁁N⁁C)]2+ (C⁁N⁁C = 2,6-({3-methylimidazol-2-ylidene}methyl)pyridine) exhibits luminescent emission in contrast to the homoleptic carbene or bis-tpy complexes due to low lying 3MLCT and elevated 3MC states.86 The complex [Ru(bpy)(L1)(L2)]2+ (L1 = 4-[2-(4′-methyl-2,2′-bipyridinyl-4-yl)vinyl]benzene-1,2-diol), L2 = 4-(N,N-dimethylaminophenyl)-2,2′-bipyridine) with donor –NMe2 and catechol acceptor groups exhibits a low energy 3LLCT state that quenches 3MLCT emission.87 The homoleptic Ru(II) complex of ligand III has been prepared and shows NIR two-photon absorption power-limiting properties.88

A number of reports have appeared covering the photophysical properties of complexes with 1,2,3-triazole derived ligands. The mixed valence complex [(tpy)Ru(ttapyr)Ru(tpy)]3+ (ttapyr = 1,3,6,8-tetrakis(1-butyl-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)pyrene) exhibits intervalence charge transfer transitions.89 The complexes [Ru(tap)2(L)]2+ (L = 4-(pyrid-2-yl)-1,2,3-triazole or 4,4′-bi-1,2,3-triazolyl, tap = 1,4,5,8-tetraazaphenathrene) have been investigated as potential photooxidising agents.90 [Ru(tpy)(tripy)]2+ (tripy = 2,6-bis(1-mesityl-3-methyl-1,2,3-triazol-5-ylidene)pyridine) is luminescent (ϕ = 5.5%) with emission from a tripy-based 3MLCT state.91 Complexes containing [Ru(bpy)3]2+ and an amine-donor or naphthalenebisdiimide-acceptor group bridged by a 1,2,3-triazole linker show efficient electron transfer through the triazole moiety.92

Dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSCs) remain an active area for the application of ruthenium complexes. Several reports have appeared for the development of efficient cyclometallated thiocyanate-free complexes as new efficient dyes for DSSC applications. Complexes of the type IV93 and V94 for example, show efficiencies of ca. 8%. Remote substituent effects on DSSC performance have been investigated in complexes of the form [Ru(dcb)(4,4′-(p-C6H4X)2-2,2′-bpy)(NCS)2]. An efficiency of 8.3% was obtained in devices based on a dye where X = –OMe.95 [Ru(dcb)(4-{pyrid-2-yl}-1,2,3-triazole)(NCS)2] based complexes result in DSSC devices with high efficiency (7.8%) despite reduced optical absorption compared to established dyes such as N3.96

A series of [Os(N⁁N)2(P⁁P)] complexes (N⁁N = 5-(1-isoquinolyl)-1,2,4-triazolate) with relevance to OLEDs containing chelating diphosphine ligands P⁁P have been investigated by DFT and TDDFT methods. Results indicate that they are better electron than hole transporters.97

Iridium and rhodium

Iridium complexes continue to attract significant interest for applications in light emitting devices98,99 and as biological imaging probes.100,101 Emission wavelength tuning has been achieved through post-cyclometallation modification of [Ir(mppy)3] (mppy = 2-(4-methoxyphenyl)pyridine) at the aryl 5-position.102 Tuning has also been achieved through the use of substituted 2-phenylbenzothiazole,103,104 phenylimidazole105 and regioselective substitution in [Ir(tolylpyridine)3] based complexes.106 Complexes of the type [Ir(C⁁C⁁N)(C⁁N⁁N)] (C⁁C⁁N = pyridylbiphenylene, C⁁N⁁N = 6-phenyl-2,2′-bpy) have been prepared and have red-shifted emission spectra further in the NIR range than previously reported complexes of bicyclometallated diphenylpyridine ligands.107 The preparation and photophysics of novel a series of biscyclometallated complexes in which the cyclometallate ligands are different has been reported.108 The effect of the position of aryl substituents in [Ir(Arpz)2(bpy)]+ (ArpzH = 1-arylpyrazole) has been investigated. Substituents para to the metal have a greater effect on emission wavelength than those in the meta position.109 Enhanced luminescent emission is observed in complexes of the form [Ir(atz)2(tBu2bpy)]+ (atzH = 4-aryl-1,2,3-triazole, tBu2bpy = 4,4′-bis-tert-butyl-2,2′-bipyridyl) with quantum yields as high as 80%.110 Triazole-based cyclometallates bearing pyridyl-1,2,3-triazole derived ligands where the triazole ring is metallated at the 5-C position have also been prepared and show tuning of lifetime and emission wavelength based on the nature of the triazole substituents.111 Cationic [Ir(ppy)2] complexes with neutral pyridyl-1,2,3-triazole ancillary ligands have been shown to exhibit high quantum yields with long luminescent lifetimes and generate intense electrochemiluminescence.112 A carbazate complex [Ir(ppy)2(NH2–NH–CO2)] has been shown to exhibit long luminescent lifetime and high quantum yield (τ = 1.8 μs, ϕ = 0.45).113 Biscyclometallated iridium complexes with pyrene and perylene appended acetylacetonate ligands undergo quenched 3MLCT emission through triplet–triplet energy transfer processes leading to long-lived 3IL states.114 A complex containing two [Ir(ppy)2] moieties bridged by a 2,2′:6′,2′′-terpyridyl-6,6′′-dicarboxylate ligand shows intense luminescent emission with a quantum yield of 18%, only 11% less than the mononuclear picolinate analogue.115 [Ir(bt)2(acac)] (bt = 2-phenylbenzothiazole) acts a ratiometric luminescent sensor for Hg(II) ions resulting in blue-shifted absorption and emission.116 Iridium complexes with two cyclometallated 1-aryl-3-methylimidazol-2-ylidene ligands and a pyrazolylpyridine-based ancillary ligand have been investigated as efficient blue luminescent complexes for OLED applications.117 Introduction of phenyl substituents into the 6- and 6′-positions of the bpy ligand in [Ir(ppy)2(bpy)]+ complexes allows nonradiative 3MC states to become thermally accessible.118 [Ir(ppy)2(Me2SO)2]PF6 has been shown to be a nucleus-specific turn-on luminescent staining agent for living cells.119 Complex VI exhibits extremely long-lived coumarin-centred 3IL states (75.5 μs) and sensitises DPA-based triplet–triplet annihilation upconversion.120 Complex VII undergoes selective rhodamine spiro-ring opening in the presence of Hg(II) ions resulting in modulation of energy transfer to iridium and increased emission intensity.121 The complex [Ir(tBu2bpy)(L)(NCS)] where L is a tripodal biscyclometallated triphenylphosphite ligand is luminescent and affords green emitting OLED devices with efficiencies up to 14.1%.122 Ir(III) and Pt(II) cyclometallated complexes of oligothiophenylpyridines show a change over from 3LC/3MLCT phosphorescence to thiophene-based fluorescence on going from thiophenylpyridine to the bi- and trithiophenyl analogues.123 The tetranuclear complex in which two [Ir(ppy)2(CN)2]− anions are bridged by two [Ir(ppy)2]+ cations via the cyanide ligands retains the luminescent properties of the mononuclear components with a phosphorescent quantum yield of 0.66.124 An Ir(III)-based soft salt comprising [Ir(dfppy)2(NCS)2]− anions and a dinuclear cation with two [Ir(dfppy)2] units spaced by a bis(imidazophenanthroline)carbozole bridge yields OLED devices with an external efficiency of 0.44.125 A complex with an [Ir(ppy)2(acac)] core and phenylethynyl substituents in the pyridine 4-positions exhibits two-photon absorption (TPA) at 800 nm with a TPA cross-section of 44 GM and emission at 570 nm in toluene.126 The novel dinuclear Ir(II)–Ir(II) dinitrogen complex VIII has been prepared and exhibits luminescent emission at 582 nm in CH2Cl2 (ϕ = 2.5%).127

Platinum and palladium

Binuclear complexes with two [Pt(PEt3)2(CCPh)] moieties bridged by a pentacenyl-6,13-diacetylide unit show ligand fluorescence pushed into the NIR (710–726 nm).128 The complex [Pt(acac)(C⁁N)] (C⁁N is a cyclometallated 4-pyrazolylnaphthalimide ligand) yields a triplet–triplet annihilation up-conversion efficiency of 14.1% with DPA.129 The complex [Pt(N⁁C⁁N)Cl] (N⁁C⁁N = N-phenyl-N-(3-(pyridin-2-yl)phenyl)pyridin-2-amine) has improved square-planar geometry compared to the dipyridylbenzene analogue and is intensely luminescent (ϕ = 0.65).130 Increased electronic insulation between metal and photochromic dithienylethene moieties in a series of Pt(II) tpy complexes with dithienylethene-functionalised acetylide ligands has been shown to reduce the rate of MLCT sensitised ring closure and promote Pt(tpy)-based phosphorescent emission.131 Arylacetylide complexes of the form [Pt(C⁁C)(CCAr)2] where C⁁C is the chelating carbene 1,1′-dipentyl-3,3′-methylene-diimidazol-2,2′-diylidene display emission stemming from 3IL states with mixing of 3MLCT states.132 [Pt(tpy)Cl] complexes with 9,9-di(2-ethylhexyl)-7-diphenylaminofluoren-2-yl substituents in the tpy 4-position exhibit TPA Z-scan-derived cross sections of 600–2000 GM.133 The complex [{Pt(C⁁N⁁N)}2(μ-P-Fc-P)]2+ (C⁁N⁁N = 4-tolyl-6-phenyl-2,2′-bipyridine, P-Fc-P = 1,1′-bis(diphenylphosphino)ferrocene) undergoes PET from ferrocene to Pt at a rate of 1.9 × 106 s−1 to yield a charge separated state with a lifetime of 9.4 ms.134 Bisacetylide complexes with the structure IX with electron donating substituents have triplet excitons localised away from the Pt centre resulting in red-shifted spectra and elongated triplet state lifetime with the opposite true for inclusion of donating groups. The rate of ISC and the S1-T1 energy gap show a Marcus relation with a reorganisation energy of 0.83 eV.135

[Pt(bpy)(1,2-benzenedithiolate)] complexes with a BODIPY chromophore attached to either the bpy or dithiolate ligand show neither BODIPY-based fluorescence or Pt-based phosphorescent emission. TA spectroscopy reveals that BODIPY excitation to the 1ππ* state results in singlet energy transfer to the 1MLLCT state which then undergoes ISC to yield the 3MLLCT. Triplet energy transfer to the BODIPY 3ππ* then occurs. Both energy transfers proceed on the ps timescale and by a Dexter mechanism.136 A Pt(II) benzoporhyrin complex with four pendant BODIPY chromophores exhibits fast singlet and triplet bidirectional energy transfer processes between BODIPY and porphyrin units and is phosphorescent in the NIR (722 nm, ϕ = 0.26).137 Cyclometallated dipyridylbenzene Pt(II) chloride complexes with –CF3 pyridyl substituents are shown to result in red-shifted excimer emission extending into the NIR region.138 Cyclometallated pyridinechloropalladium(II) complexes of 4-aryl-2,1,3-benzothiadiazoles are luminescent in solution and exhibit two emission bands; a high energy ligand-based emission on excitation in the ∼300 nm 1IL band and a lower energy emission band on excitation in the 1MLCT band (∼450 nm).139 A bis(naphthalenediimideacetylide) complex of [Pt(tBu2bpy)] displays intense visible absorption and a long-lived naphthalenediimide localised 3IL state (τ = 22.3 ms). The complex sensitised triplet–triple annihilation up-conversion with perylene with an efficiency of 9.5%.140 [Pt(ppy)(O⁁O)] complexes with β-diketonate O⁁O ligands bearing methyl, phenyl or amine- or carbazole-functionalised aryl substituents have been prepared with emission colour tunable from yellow to green.141 A rhodamine/8-aminoquioline conjugate acts as an off-on fluorescent sensor for Pd2+ ions. Pd2+ induced spirolactam ring opening was confirmed by X-ray crystal structure determination of the Pd-bound sensor.142 Pt(II)acac complexes with benzothiazolyl-coumarin complexes with the coumarin moiety directly cycloplatinated give triplet–triplet annihilation quantum yields up to 15.4% with DPA.143 A Pt(II) chloride complex with a cyclometallated 2,6-bis(N-alkylbenzimidazol-2-yl)benzene ligand exhibits bright green luminescence (506–587 nm, τ = 5.3 ms, ϕ = 0.19) and OLED devices with an efficiency of 11.5%.144 [(tBu3tpy)Pt(CCtpy)PtCCtBu](OTf)2 exhibits unusually higher emission quantum yield in CH2Cl2 (ϕ = 0.43) than in MeCN (<0.1) attributed to the formation of an excited state “contact ion pair” in CH2Cl2 solutions.145 A [Pt(ppy)(acac)] complex with dimesitylboron electron-transporting group attached to pyridine and naphthanlenylphenylamine hole-transporting group attached to phenyl yield bright orange OLED devices with efficiencies up to 10.6%.146 Cyclometallated Pt(II) 2,6-bis(oxazol)phenyl complexes are moderately emissive with tuning of emission wavelength available through the oxazole moieties.147 Binuclear complexes [{Pt(Me)(L)}2(μ-dppm)] (L = ppy or benzo[h]quinoline, dppm = bis(diphenylphosphine)methane) show intense orange-red emission stemming from 3MMLCT excited states.148 1,3-bis(2-pyridylimino)isoindoline Pt(II) complexes with monodentate N-donors such as pyridine, phthalazine or phenanthridine show orange-red emission from 3IL states mixed with some 3MLCT character.149

Supramolecular assemblies

The hexafluorophosphate salt of the cationic dyad 5-{4-[Re(CO)3(picoline)(4-methyl-2,2′-bipyridyl-4′-carboxamidyl)]phenyl}-10,15,20-triphenylporphyrinato-palladium(II) photoreduces CO2 to CO. Time-resolved studies show Pd to porphyrin to Re energy transfer.150 Tetranuclear assemblies of the form [{(phen)2M(dpp)}2Ru{(dpp)PtCl2}]6+ (M = Ru, Os, dpp = dipyridylpyrazine) exhibit terminal metal to dpp 3MLCT transition. Emission from these states undergoes quenching by population of triplet charge transfer states centred on the dpp ligand bridging to Pt.151 Temperature-dependent excited state equilibria have been directly observed between 3MLCT states and 3LC states in dinuclear complexes containing [Ru(tpy)2] units spaced by conjugated electron-poor bridges.152 Dyads of the form [(bpy)2Ru(dpdpz)Pt(CCAr)2]2+ (dpdpz = 2,3-di(pyrid-2-yl)-5,6-diphenylpyrazine), in which the Pt(II) centre is cyclometallated to dpdpz, exhibit intense visible and NIR mixed Ru- and Pt-based MLCT absorption and NIR emission.153 Ru(bpy)2/Ru(bpy)2 and Ru(bpy)2/Os(bpy)2 bimetallic complexes bridged by 4,5-(benzimidazol-2-yl)imidazolate show quenched bridging ligand fluorescence and MLCT-based phosphorescence. Energy transfer from Ru to Os is observed in the heterobimetallic complex.154 Ir(III)/Pt(II) triad X displays red-shifted luminescent emission compared to both the mononuclear Ir(III) biscyclometallated and Pt(II) monocyclometallated component analogues such that the metallic units do not retain their individual photophysical properties.155 Multinuclear platinum(II) acetylide complexes containing triethynylbenzene or 1,4-bis(3,5-diethynylphenyl)buta-1,3-diyne cores show TPA induced luminescence and TPA cross sections from 6 to 191 GM upon excitation at 720 nm.156 A trinuclear triple helicate complex [CrErCr(L)3]9+ where L is ligand XI displays NIR to visible upconversion.157

Metalloporhyrin and –phthalocyanine complexes

A hexakis(zinc(II)porphyrin) nano-ring with butadiynyl inter-porphyrin linkers has been prepared. Light absorption generates an excited state delocalised over the whole π-system with a lifetime of 0.5 ps.158 A zinc porphyrin dye with a cobalt-based electrolyte has been used to prepare DSSC devices with the greatest efficiencies to date (up to 13.1%).159 Tetra-meso-arylporphyrinatozinc polyimide containing dendrons built on a dimethylxanthenebis(M(II)porphyrin) core (M = Cu, Zn) have been investigated. The dendrons can act at singlet and triplet energy acceptors or donors depending on the individual dendrimeric system.160 Cyclic bis-zincporphyrin forms host-guest inclusion complexes with C60 and C70 fullerenes. These exhibit an excited state involving electron transfer to fullerene which decays to the ground state over several hundred picoseconds.161 Picosecond TA spectroscopy reveals that excitation in the Soret band of tetraphenylporphyrinzinc(II) (ZnTPP) in the presence of excess C60 results in the formation of a relatively long-lived charge separated state.162 A diethylpyrrole-bridge dizinc bisporphyrin assembly complexes pyrene. Fluorescence studies show photoinduced singlet-singlet energy transfer from excited pyrene to Zn.163 Assemblies containing two zinc phthalocanines (ZnPc) bridged by an imidazole-functionalised perylenediimide unit displays charged separated states with lifetimes between 3 and 9.8 ns.164 Photoinduced energy transfer between non-covalent complexes of ZnPc and C60 pyrrolidine tris-acid ethyl ester (PyC60) occurs predominantly by energy transfer from a PyC60 triplet excited state to ZnPc.165 A distorted fused porphyrin-phthalocyanine conjugate ZnP–ZnPc has been reported. In the presence of pyridylfullerene, excitation results in energy transfer from ZnP to ZnPc followed by electron transfer to fullerene to generate ZnP–ZnPc˙+–C60˙− which deactivates to the ground state in about 1.5 ns.166 A conjugate, two C60 fullerenes appended to a ZnP moiety by 1,2,3-triazole linkers, exhibits PET and a long-lived charge-separated state (τ = 0.48 μs) in benzonitrile. In toluene, intramolecular energy transfer occurs resulting in C60 triplet state generation on ZnP excitation.167 Crown-BODIPY-ZnP assembly XII acts as a receptor for alkyl ammonium functionalised C60. Excitation of the BODIP moiety results in singlet energy transfer to ZnP occurring in 7ps. Following electron transfer processes result in the formation of the charge separated ion-pair C60˙−:crown-BODIPY-ZnP˙+ which has a lifetime of ∼100 μs, the longest reported for a system of this type.168

A supramolecular array comprising a tetrakispyridine substituted perylenebisdiimide acceptor coordinated to four Ru(II) carbonyl phthalocyanine (RuPc) donor moieties has been reported. Excitation of either the donor or acceptor groups results in formation of a radical ion pair state with a lifetime of several hundred ps in toluene.169 RuPc and Ru naphthalocyanines with dendronised axial pyridine ligands show optical power limiting properties.170 Tetrads containing a Sn(IV) porphyrin coordinated to a Ru(II) carbonyl porphyrin with two expanded thiaporphyrins coordinated axially to Sn have been reported. Photophysical studies show little coupling between components showing that they retain their individual features. Sn-based emission is significantly reduced, however, due to non-radiative decay pathways operating in these systems.171 In contrast to the non-luminescent Ni(II) complex, Pd(II) and Pt(II) complexes of 1,4,8,11,15,18,22,25-octabutoxyphthalocyanine exhibit deep red fluorescent and NIR phosphorescent emission from S1 and T1 ππ* states respectively.172 Water soluble Pt and Pd phthalocyanine complexes have been shown to sensitise singlet-oxygen formation with a quantum yield of 0.24.173 A multinuclear porphyrin (P) array constructed through triazole ‘click’ chemistry comprising a Au(III)P core surrounded by four free-base (fb) P units to which are attached a periphery of 12 ZnP units. Excitation of ZnP results in energy transfer to fbP and in turn charge transfer to Au(III)P. The resultant charge-shift state survives for microseconds in solution at room temperature.174 Mixed porphyrin/phthalocyanine ‘double-decker’ rare-earth complexes of Y(III), Sm(III), Eu(III), Tb(III), Dy(III), Ho(III) and Lu(III) have been developed as broad band light absorbers and electron donors in bulk heterojunction solar cells. Efficiencies of up to 0.82% have been achieved.175

Lanthanides and actinides

The mechanism of non-overlapping FRET (nFRET) (where energy transfer is observed between a spectrally non-overlapping donor emitter and an acceptor absorber) has been investigated using a europium chelate emitter and an organic fluorophore separated by an oligonucleotide. From distance and temperature dependent data the mechanism is proposed to proceed through thermal excitation from the lowest Eu emission state to a higher ionic or LMCT state.176 The sensitisation of lanthanide-based emission and energy transfer in mixed transition metal/lanthanide assemblies remains an active area with a number of reports appearing in the literature.177–182 Tb(III) complexes with octadentate bis(diaminethane) capped ligands containing four 2-hydroxyisonaphthalamide chelate groups have been investigated as new luminescent lanthanide standards and show high quantum yields and long lifetimes in water (ϕtotal≥ 50%, τ ≥ 2.45 ms).183 A water soluble Yb(III) rhodamine-appended porphyrin complex capped with a [CpCo{(MeO)2P = O}3]− anion exhibits TPA and impressive NIR emission (1060 nm, ϕ = 2.5%).184 The complex [Tb(deppa)3] (deppa = 6-(diethoxyphosphoryl)picolinate) exhibits emission at 545 nm (ϕ = 0.4) with a lifetime of 2.4 ms confirming no water ligands are present in the first coordination sphere.185 A Eu(III)/ferrocene dyad derived from copper catalysed cycloaddition of a progargyl-substituted EuDO3A complex and azidomethylferrocene exhibits reversible switching of Eu(III) luminescent emission.186 Luminescent [Ln(Tf2N)3] complexes in a [1-butyl-3-methylpyrrolidinium][Tf2N] ionic liquid (Tf2N = bis(trifluoromethyl-sulfonyl)amide) display remarkably long lifetimes compared to values quoted for Ln(III) complexes in solution due to weak vibrational quenching.187 Europium and terbium tris(hfac) (hfac = 1,1,1,5,5,5-hexafluoropentane-2,4-dione) complexes with chiral bis(oxazole)pyridine (pybox) ligands display circularly polarised luminescent (CPL) emission. Despite having the same chirality, phenyl-substituted pybox ligands result in opposite CPL spectra to those of Me- and iPr-substituted complexes.188 Dimeric lanthanide (Eu, Gd, Tb) DO3A complexes with bridging biphenyl or diphenylmethane bridges give enhanced luminescent emission for imaging in the presence of human serum albumin.189 Arylphosphonate-appended DO3A complexes of Tb(III) are emissive with quantum yields between 0.18 and 0.24 and lifetimes >3 ms.190 Nd(III), Yb(III) and Eu(III) complexes of DO3A ligands with appended 8-aminoquinoline and anthraquinone fluorophores display dual emission properties that are modulated in the presence of various metal ions.191 Complex XIII is brightly luminescent in water (ϕ = 0.13, τ = 1.8 ms) but displays poor TPA. Introduction of 4-arylacetylene substituents on the pendant pyridyl rings reduces the luminescent intensity but yield a TPA cross-section of 45 GM at 720 nm.192 Eu(III) complexes with fluorenyl-acac ligands with phenyl, 2-naphthyl or 4-biphenyl substituents allow tuning of the excitation wavelength. Replacement of phenyl by naphthyl or biphenyl significantly shifts excitation into the visible (ca. 500 nm) with quantum yields between 19 and 43% in the solid state, amongst the highest reported for a visible wavelength sensitised Eu(III) complex.193 Lanthanide complexes of a 2,6-bis(pyrazolyl)pyridine ligand functionalised with two iminobismethylenephosphate groups are stable in biological media and are luminescent with very long lifetimes (τ = 1.50 and 3.28 ms for Eu and Tb respectively).194 Lanthanide complexes based on bis(amide) derivatives of diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid with thiol functionalities allow access to heterobimetallic complexes through disulphide bridge formation. The Eu/Tb complex exhibits dual colour f–f emission from both Eu and Tb centres but with some energy transfer from Tb(III) to Eu(III).195 An 8-hydroxyquinoline ligand substituted with a BODIPY group at the 5-position has been used for the sensitisation of NIR emission from Yb(III), Er(III) and Nd(III). Nd(III) and Er(III) complexes emit weakly at 1060 and 1382 nm respectively whereas the Yb(III) complex emits strongly at 976 and 1003 nm.196 Tris(thenoyltrifluoroacetonato)Eu(III) complexes with 4,7-disubstituted phen ligands have been investigated by DFT/TDDFT methods. Calculations reveal that the luminescent 5D0 state can be populated for H, Me, OMe, Cl and Br substituents but not for π-conjugating CO2Et and C4H3OMe groups through tuning of the energy of the excited triplet state.197 A terpyridinetetraacetate Eu3+ complex connected to an ampicillin moiety covalently binds to β-lactamase. The long lifetime of the emissive Eu(III) label (>1.25 ms) has been exploited for time-gated and lifetime-based multicolour bioimaging applications.198 A related complex with a pendant 3,4-diaminophenoxy group acts as a turn-on luminescent time-gated imaging probe for NO which reacts with the diaminophenyl group to form a benzotriazole moiety.199

Divalent Eu(II) complex XIV exhibits luminescent emission at 528 nm which can be attributed to a 4f-5d transition and is a rare example of the photophysical study of a Eu(II) complex.200 Dinuclear complexes containing two Ln(hfac)3 moieties bridged by two μ-phenolic hydroxyquinoline ligands display fluoride ion induced luminescent enhancement through replacement of H2O ligands or formation of OH⋯F− hydrogen bonds that suppress non-radiative O–H oscillators.201 Lanthanide nitrate complexes of the oligoimidazole ligands bis- and tris{[2-{(imidazol-4-yl)methylidene}amino]ethyl}amine show enhancement of Ln(III) phosphorescence due to high ligand triplet state energies.202 Remarkable Nd(III) and Yb(III) containing clusters where the Ln(III) ions are surrounded by a complex arrangement of three metallacrown structures comprising 16 Zn(II) ions and 16 picolinehydroxamic acid ligands has been reported. These structures persist in solution and exhibit NIR emission.203 A Eu(III) cyclen-based complex with a 4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline-disulfonate (BPS) antenna ligand acts as a turn-off luminescent sensor for transition metal ions through BPS displacement. A detection limit of emission quenching by Fe(II) ions of ∼10 pM was observed.204 Luminescent emission from long-lived LMCT 3Πu excited states of uranyl(VI) ions in complexes with PO, PNH and AsO containing ligands has been used as a probe of the local coordination environment of the uranyl complexes in non-aqueous media.205 Eu(III) and Sm(III) trigonal dodecahedral complexes with chelating bis(phoshineoxide) ligands such as 4,5-bis(diphenylphosphoryl)-9,9-dimethylxanthene and hfac ligands display markedly high emission quantum yield (Eu, ϕ = 55–72%; Sm, ϕ = 2.4–5.0% in d6-acetone) due to enhancement of the electric dipole transition and suppression of vibrational relaxation.206
