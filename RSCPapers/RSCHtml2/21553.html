Abstract

Monodisperse Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ (NYF) hexagonal microplates with efficient near-infrared (NIR)-to-NIR up-conversion (UC) developed via a new ion-exchange modified hydrothermal method are reported. Ion exchange modification (IEM) not only significantly increases the UC intensity by up to 1000 times and prolongs the emission lifetimes of Tm3+ and Yb3+, but also enables the monodisperse morphology and size of hexagonal microplates to be maintained. A high UC quantum efficiency (QE) of 3.1% is obtained for IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,1%Tm3+ when excited with 980 nm light at a power density of 10 W cmâ2. The UC emission properties can be finely tailored by changing the NaF/NYF molar ratio (NYF represents lanthanide doped Î²-NaYF4) and the doping concentration of Tm3+. The two photon NIR UC emission centered at â¼803 nm (arising from Tm3+: 3H4 â 3H6) dominates the UC emission spectrum and high concentrations of Tm3+ favor NIR UC further. A proof-of-concept optical image of printed patterns is demonstrated to verify their applications in security. These results suggest the promising applications of the newly developed monodisperse Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ hexagonal microplates in security, luminescent labels, solid-state lasers, amplifiers, and biomedicine.

Introduction

Up-conversion (UC) refers to the phenomenon of converting low photon energy excitation, typically near-infrared (NIR) light, into high photon energy emission ranging from NIR to visible or even to ultraviolet (UV) wavelengths.1â3 Among them, NIR-to-NIR UC materials have wide applications in different fields of science and technology including security labels,4 biomedicine,5 solid-state lasers,6 solar cells,7 as well as emerging markets such as luminescent labels for plastic recycling.8 Upon excitation at 980 nm, Yb3+/Tm3+ co-doped UC materials show efficient NIR-to-NIR UC emission at â¼800 nm (within the first biological window, NIR I), arising from the transition of Tm3+: 3H4 â 3H6.9â15 For example, Yb3+/Tm3+ co-doped Î²-NaYF4 microcrystals have been proposed as one of the most efficient NIR-to-NIR UC emitters.8,16,17 In addition, Yb3+/Tm3+ co-doped UC materials also exhibit the most efficient NIR-to-blue UC emission, which further extends their applications in full color displays.4 Furthermore, they also demonstrate relatively weak NIR-to-UV UC emission allowing potential applications in UV lasers.14 For these different UC emission bands, their emission intensities greatly depend on the excitation power density â the higher the excitation power density, the stronger the shorter wavelength UC emission. Indeed, the adjustment of the excitation power density provides a unique route to manipulate the UC emission properties of UC phosphors. In particular, efficient UC emission at low excitation power density (<1 W cmâ2) is the one of the most important requirements for practical applications. In this case, more efficient NIR-to-NIR UC emission in Yb3+/Tm3+ co-doped microcrystals, such as Î²-NaYF4, and the exploration of their practical applications are in urgent need.

In the last decade, many strategies have been reported to enhance the UC emission efficiency of lanthanide doped nanoparticles such using a noble metal nanocrystal plasma,18 the photonic crystal effect,19 surface coating via a coreâshell structure,20etc. However, these methods may only work for nanoparticles.21 The exploration of novel methods to enhance the UC emission efficiency of Yb3+/Tm3+ co-doped Î²-NaYF4 microcrystals is urgently required. For the synthesis of Î²-NaYF4 microcrystals, one drawback of the traditional high-temperature solid-state reaction is that the morphology and size of these microcrystals cannot be controlled, which greatly limits their applications, such as in micro-optoelectronic devices.23â25 The morphology and size of Î²-NaYF4 microcrystals can be controlled via the traditional hydrothermal method, however the emission efficiency of lanthanides can decrease significantly due to the formation of lanthanide clusters and the high OHâ concentration.23,26 Recently, we developed a Na+ ion-exchange modified hydrothermal method for Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ to achieve efficient UC emission of Er3+. In contrast to the traditional post-high-temperature annealing method,22 the ion exchange method demonstrates unique advantages of not only greatly enhancing the UC emission efficiency but also maintaining the good microcrystal morphology.23,26,27 In this work, we report on efficient NIR-to-NIR UC emission at low excitation power density in microparticles with well controlled morphology and size. Different NaF/NYF molar ratios (NYF represents lanthanide doped Î²-NaYF4) and doping concentrations of Tm3+ provide effective routes to manipulate the UC emission properties of Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ microcrystals while maintaining the crystal structures and morphology. Using these NIR UC emission microcrystals, a proof-of-concept optical imaging experiment is carried out at a low excitation power density of 0.9 W cmâ2 to prove a possibility of their usage in security applications, where both the excitation light and the emitted light are invisible to the human eye.

Ion-exchange modification (IEM)

Pre-synthesized lanthanide doped Î²-NaYF4 (0.1200 g), NaF (0.1680 g) and NH4HF2 (0.3643 g) were mixed with deionized water (1.2 ml) and stirred for 10 minutes to conduct the Na+ ion-exchange reaction (Scheme 1). The NaF/NYF molar ratio (NYF indicates lanthanide doped Î²-NaYF4) was adjusted to 0.2, 1, 3, and 5 while maintaining the NaF/NH4HF2 molar ratio and H2O content. The mixed solution was transferred into a 25 ml Teflon bottle held in a stainless steel autoclave, sealed and maintained at 220 Â°C for 36 h. When the autoclave was cooled to 25 Â°C, the precipitates were separated by centrifugation, washed with deionized water and dried at 70 Â°C for 12 h.

NIRâNIR UC emission imaging

The Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ microcrystals after IEM with a NaF/NYF ratio = 5 were used to investigate the potential applications of NIR UC emission in security as sketched in Scheme 1. First, the Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ microcrystals (20 mg) were dispersed into H2O (1 ml) by ultrasonication to obtain luminescent inks. Then, as-prepared luminescent inks were printed on common white paper using hollow-out copper stencils to obtain different patterns as large as 5.7 Ã 8.0 mm. The images of these printed patterns upon 980 nm laser irradiation (0.9 W cmâ2) were taken with a help of an infrared viewer (FJW Optical Systems, Inc., USA) with a spectral sensitivity of 350â1550 nm. An 800 nm band-pass (FWHM = 40 nm, FBH800-40, Thorlabs) filter was placed in front of the camera to block 980 nm excitation light. The diameter of the excitation spot was 15 mm, larger than the size of the printed patterns.

For power dependent measurements, the power density of the laser beam (0.001â8 W cmâ2) was adjusted by the use of the controlled rotatable neutral density filter (Thorlabs). All characterization techniques were carried out at room temperature.

Up-conversion (UC) emission of Tm3+

IEM significantly improves the UC performance of hydrothermally synthesized Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+. Fig. 2a and d present the corresponding representative UC emission spectra of Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+ and Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ dependent on the NaF/NYF molar ratio (0.2, 1, 3 and 5), respectively. Upon excitation with a 976 nm LD (power density of 3 W cmâ2), Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ exhibits typical narrow line-type UC emissions of Tm3+, which are composed of seven individual bands varying from 330 to 850 nm. The NIR UC emission at â¼803 nm with a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of 18 nm (280 cmâ1) is by far the most intense UC emission peak observed within all samples. It is typical for a two photon UC process of Tm3+ and the peak is attributed to the 4f12 â 4f12 transition of Tm3+: 3H4 â 3H6. The UC emission in the blue region centered at â¼474 nm attributed to Tm3+: 1G4 â 3H6 shows the second strongest UC intensity. This peak is typical for a three-photon UC emission process involving Tm3+. Its intensity is >20 times weaker than that of the NIR band at 803 nm. Despite its weak intensity, the bright blue UC color can be observed by the naked eye under excitation with a 3 W cmâ2 980 nm LD after IEM, as exemplarily shown in the inset of Fig. 2a and d. The corresponding CIE-1931 chromaticity color coordinates are determined to be (0.17, 0.13) for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+ and (0.19, 0.14) for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ after ion-exchange with a NaF/NYF molar ratio = 5. According to our previous work, the faint UC emission in the AP sample is attributed to the formation of locally clustered lanthanide ions and the existence of a relatively high concentration of OHâ groups induced in the hydrothermal synthesis process.23,26 After IEM, the well maintained crystal phase, morphology and size of Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ microplates, almost unchanged chemical composition and significantly enhanced UC emission efficiency support that the majority of Yb3+ and Tm3+ ions are maintained in microplates rather than being dispersed in solution.23,26 The IEM process strongly disperses the locally clustered lanthanide ions. For the OHâ groups, Dyck et al. directly proved that the residual water does exist and strongly quenches the UC emission in the hydrothermally synthesized microcrystals.22 During the IEM process, a great amount of NaF and NH4HF2 are used, and these fluorides can easily reduce the OHâ groups via the fluoridation process.34

Thus, Fig. 2b and e illustrate the rise and decay behavior of the Tm3+ NIR UC emission at 803 nm for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+ and Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+, respectively, AP and after IEM with varying NaF/NYF molar ratios (0.2, 1, 3 and 5). The AP sample exhibits an extremely short rise lifetime of â¼0.07 ms and decay lifetime of â¼0.16 ms for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+. A higher Tm3+ concentration (0.5 mol%) results in an even shorter rise lifetime of â¼0.03 ms and decay lifetime of â¼0.12 ms for AP Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ (see Fig. 2e and f). After IEM, both the rise and decay lifetimes of Tm3+ are strongly prolonged. This is fully consistent with the enormously enhanced UC emission intensity after IEM. For example, the rise and decay lifetimes of Tm3+ in Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+ after IEM with a NaF/NYF molar ratio = 1 are strongly prolonged from â¼0.06 to 1.2 ms and from 0.16 to 1.75 ms, respectively (Fig. 2c). The rise lifetime remains almost constant (â¼1.2 ms) with increasing NaF/NYF molar ratio (Fig. 2c). The lifetime of Tm3+ slightly increased from 1.75 to 1.94 ms with increasing NaF/NYF molar ratio from 1 to 5 for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+. The long rise lifetime evidences the energy transfer UC mechanism of the Yb3+/Tm3+ pair in Î²-NaYF4.

A similar trend can be observed for Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+. The rise and decay lifetimes of Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.5%Tm3+ are slightly shorter than those of Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,0.1%Tm3+ with the same NaF/NYF molar ratio due to the pronounced back energy transfer (BET) from Tm3+ to Yb3+ at high Tm3+ concentration via the transition Tm3+: 3H4 + Yb3+: 2F7/2 â Tm3+: 3H6 + Yb3+: 2F5/2, which strongly depopulates the UC emitting level of Tm3+: 3H4 for NIR UC (Fig. 2e and f).

The decay lifetime reflects the competitive radiative and non-radiative processes of lanthanides (Yb3+ and Tm3+).35 In the present case, for the AP sample, the non-radiative process dominates the decay process due to the existence of a high concentration of quenching centers such as lanthanide clusters and OHâ groups.23,26 The high concentration of quenching centers leads to a fast non-radiative rate and fast energy transfer rate to these quenching centers resulting in a short abnormal lifetime in the AP sample. The rise time indicates the energy transfer rate from Yb3+ to Tm3+. The short rise time of the AP sample indicates the low energy transfer rate from Yb3+ to Tm3+, resulting in very faint UC emission. The existence of the quenching centers of Yb3+ and Tm3+ clusters and OHâ groups leads to a low energy transfer rate from Yb3+ to Tm3+ and thus a short rise time. IEM strongly reduces these quenching centers leading to a strongly prolonged lifetime and rise time, and significantly enhanced UC emission efficiency.

Fig. 3a shows the UC emission spectra of Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,x%Tm3+ (x = 0.1, 0.2, 0.5 and 1.0) after IEM with a fixed NaF/NYF molar ratio = 5 as a function of Tm3+ doping concentration. The UC emission intensity in the NIR region at 803 nm increases by â¼2.5-fold with increasing Tm3+ doping concentration from 0.1 to 1.0 mol%. In contrast, the UC emission intensity in blue and other visible bands strongly decreases. For example, a 6-fold decrease of UC emission intensity in the blue band (474 nm) can be observed. As a result, the intensity ratio of the two strongest emission bands, I803/I474, significantly increases from â¼24 to 380, as shown in Fig. 3b and c. With the Tm3+ doping concentration increasing even higher, up to 2.0 mol%, the visible UC emission is too weak to be detected and only faint NIR emission can be detected. It can be explained as the statistical ionic distance between Yb3+ and Tm3+ becomes shorter with increasing Tm3+ concentration. This strongly promotes cross-relaxation among Tm3+via the transition 1G4 + 3F4 â 3H4 + 3F3 and BET from Tm3+ to Yb3+via the transition Tm3+: 3H4 + Yb3+: 2F7/2 â Tm3+: 3H6 + Yb3+: 2F5/2. It strongly depopulates the blue emission level and facilitates the NIR UC emission level.

We measured the UC quantum efficiency (QE) of Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,x%Tm3+ (x = 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0) after IEM with a fixed NaF/NYF molar ratio = 5 as a function of Tm3+ doping concentration with a 10 W cmâ2 980 nm LD, as shown in Fig. 3d. The internal QE (with respect to absorbed photons) of Î²-NaYF4:20Yb3+,xTm3+ increases from 1.2 to 3.1% with increasing doping concentration of Tm3+ from 0.1 to 1.0 mol%. The highest Tm3+ doping concentration (2.0% mol%) leads to a sharp drop of internal QE from 3.1% to 0.4% and almost totally quenched visible UC emission. The QE value of 3.1% (10 W cmâ2) is among the highest reported values in the literature.38 The identical nominal doping concentration of Yb3+ (20 mol%) results in an almost equivalent absorbance of â¼20% (determined by the integrating sphere) for Î²-NaYF4:20Yb3+,xTm3+. Thus, the external QE (with respect to incident photons) exhibits the exactly same trend with increasing doping concentration of Tm3+. Here, the external QE increases from 0.2% to 0.6% with increasing doping concentration Tm3+ from 0.1 to 1.0 mol%. The external QE value is much lower than the internal QE due to the low absorbance â¼20%. Then, it strongly decreases from 0.6% to 0.09% with further increasing doping concentration of Tm3+ from 1.0 mol% to 2.0 mol%. Due to the non-linear process of UC emission, a higher QE value may be expected by applying an even higher excitation power density.16

UC emission mechanisms

Based on these results, the involved UC and PL mechanisms are proposed, as shown in Fig. 5a and b, respectively. In brief, Yb3+, the sensitizer, absorbs the 980 nm excitation and transfers the energy to Tm3+, the activator, which gives rise to the UC emission.12,42 Upon excitation at 980 nm, Yb3+ is pumped to 2F5/2. The successive transfer energy of two absorbed photons from Yb3+ to Tm3+ populates Tm3+: 3H5 and Tm3+: 3H4 which gives rise to the dominant NIR UC emission at â¼803 nm. The transfer of energy of the third photon from Yb3+ to Tm3+ can populate the Tm3+: 1G4 level leading to blue UC emission at â¼474 nm, and red UC emissions at 645 nm and 695 nm. The transfer of energy of the fourth photon will populate the Tm3+: 1D2 level leading to blue UC emission at â¼450 nm and UV UC at â¼360 nm. With the increase of Tm3+ concentration, the BET from Tm3+ to Yb3+ causes a decrease of the rise and decay lifetimes of Tm3+ at 803 nm. Moreover, the enhanced cross-relaxation among Tm3+via1G4 + 3F4 â 3H4 + 3F3 also leads to a decrease of UC emission from high-photon processes (â¥3) such as blue UC emission. Upon excitation at 808 nm in Fig. 5b, Tm3+ ions are pumped to the excited state Tm3+: 3H4 from the ground state Tm3+: 3H6. For Yb3+ PL upon excitation at 808 nm, it is realized via energy transfer from Tm3+: 3H4 to Yb3+: 2F5/2. After IEM, the promoted energy transfer between Tm3+ and Yb3+ results in much stronger Yb3+ PL emission at â¼1000 nm. The IEM disperses the Yb3+ and Tm3+ clusters and decreases the concentration of detrimental OHâ groups leading to strongly improved UC and PL emission from Tm3+ and Yb3+, respectively.

Conclusions

In summary, we report efficient NIR-to-NIR UC emission in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ with well controlled morphology and size of hexagonal microplates obtained via a new IEM hydrothermal method. IEM enormously improves the UC emission properties of NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+, while maintaining the monodisperse morphology and size of hexagonal thin plates. Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ shows dominant UC emission in the NIR region at â¼803 nm, arising from the transition of Tm3+: 3H4 â 3H6. IEM with varying NaF/NYF ratios leads to enormously enhanced UC emission intensity by up to three orders of magnitude and significantly prolonged lifetimes of energy levels. The UC emission properties of Tm3+ can be finely tuned by changing the NaF/NYF molar ratio and the doping concentration of Tm3+. The optimum NaF/NYF molar ratio and doping concentration of Tm3+ are determined to be 5 and 1 mol%, respectively. The maximum internal QE is measured to be 3.1% for IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,1%Tm3+. Similarity, IEM with a NaF/NYF molar ratio = 5 leads to significantly enhanced photoluminescence (PL) intensity of Yb3+ by â¼40-fold and a significantly prolonged lifetime. A proof-of-concept optical image of printed patterns is demonstrated to verify their possible applications in security. These results hint the potential applications of newly developed monodisperse Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ hexagonal microplates in security, luminescent labels, solid-state lasers, amplifiers, and biomedicine.
