Formation, growth and applications of femtoliter droplets on a microlens

Abstract

Formation and growth of femtoliter droplets on surface microstructures are important in many fundamental and practical interfacial processes, such as water collection, vapour condensation in cooling devices, drop self-removal on anti-icing surfaces and fabrication of droplet-templated functional microstructures. In this work, we experimentally and theoretically investigate the growth of femtoliter oil-like liquid on the microlens surrounded by a hydrophilic planar area. The droplets were produced by solvent exchange, a process where the droplets nucleate and grow from an oversaturation created by displacing a good solvent by a poor solvent of the droplet liquid. Our results showed that the droplet fully coats the lens surface and the contact angle of the droplet relative to the flat surface is finely tuned over a large range by the droplet volume. The growth of the droplet on a microlens is largely described by the constant contact radius model. To demonstrate the new opportunities provided by the controlled formation of the droplet situated on a microlens, we will show a simple and effective approach for production of arrays of composite microlenses consisting of two types of polymers with different refractive indices. A high curvature of the composite microlens results in desirable diffraction patterns with potential application for enhanced light harvesting. Moreover, we demonstrate that extraction of traces of a hydrophobic solute from the flow is much faster as the droplet is lifted up from the channel wall by the microlens, promising a time effective in situ detection process in narrow channels.

Introduction

Femtoliter droplets on a surface play an important role in many fundamental and practical interfacial processes.1–4 These surface droplets serve as simple precursors for fabrication of functional materials, such as microlens arrays to enhance light harvesting or microlamps to facilitate vapour condensation.5–8 Droplets also extract traces of analytes from solutions for in situ detection,9 accommodate catalytic nanoparticles for chemical reactions and synthesis, or accommodate active reagents for multiplex high throughput screening and analysis.10–12 Various approaches have been developed for producing femtoliter droplets with defined volume and morphology by controlled droplet evaporation on a nanopatterned substrate,13 thin polymer film dewetting,14–17 inkjet printing technique,18 or entrapment of emulsion droplets.19

Formation of femtoliter droplets through heterogeneous nucleation at the solid–liquid interface has gained great attention, due to simplicity and scalability of the bottom-up approach. However, the challenge is usually how to terminate the growth of the droplet and the final droplet size on the scale of femtoliter or even less. Among all possible approaches, solvent exchange has shown significant potential in controlled growth of surface droplets. In the process of solvent exchange,20,21 the droplets form on the substrate as a consequence of a pulse of oversaturation created by displacing a good solvent by a poor solvent of the droplet liquid in a controlled manner.21,22 The final size of the droplets produced by solvent exchange is influenced by several parameters, including flow rate, channel dimensions, solution composition and physical and chemical properties of the substrate.7,21,23 Both experimental and theoretical understanding of droplet formation by solvent exchange has been reported in the series of our papers published in the last 3 years. To be brief, the droplet volume per unit area increases with an increase in the flow rate of the poor solvent and also with an increase in the channel height.21,24 A ternary phase diagram of the liquids (good and poor solvents and droplet liquid) is the base for prediction of the droplet size when the solution composition is varied.23 The droplets may grow or dissolve in a constant contact radius (CR) mode, a constant contact angle mode or a mixed mode.25 On a substrate with highly ordered hydrophobic prepatterns, the droplet formation also follows the spatial arrangement of the domains.7,26 The solvent exchange is applicable to produce droplets of various types of liquids including extremely viscous ones that are challenging to jet.27 Clearly the solvent exchange possesses very attractive attributes for controlled formation of surface droplets with extremely small volume.

An interesting aspect that has not been explored much is the formation of femtoliter droplets on geometrical microstructures. In general, droplet behaviours on microstructures,28–34 in particular, spherical particles or bumps, are very important for controlling liquids on functional surfaces, as those microstructures are relevant to several most classic biomimetic substrates that are designed by following the inspiration from the back of desert beetles, lotus leaves or moth eyes for self-cleaning, antireflection and other functional surfaces.35–39 With our co-workers, we showed that droplets may form at the rim or on the top of the microlens or somewhere away from the microlens, depending on the wetting properties of the droplet liquid.40 A theoretical phase diagram was constructed to reveal the location for the droplet nucleation relative to the very thin microlens based on interfacial energy minimisation.40

In this work, we will focus on formation and applications of femtoliter droplets on a microlens. The contact angle of the droplet liquid is high on the surrounding area, but low on the microlens surface. With such wetting properties, the droplets are expected to form preferentially on the top of the microlens. Our results show that the formation of the droplet by solvent exchange is confined on the microlens surface, and the droplet volume on the microlens is finely tuned by solution concentrations or flow rate during solvent exchange. As a demonstration of the potential applications of femtoliter droplets on a microlens, we will show a simple approach for additive production of composite microlens arrays with desirable optical properties and droplet-based fast nanoextraction.

Results and discussion

1. Microlens arrays as the substrate

The substrates used in our experiments are prepatterned with highly ordered arrays of polymeric microlenses as shown in Fig. 1, which were prepared by following the protocol reported in the literature.7 The microlens was made of polymerised lauryl methacrylate (PLMA) and 1,6-hexanediol diacrylate (PHDODA). The small microlenses were 5 μm while the large ones were 10 μm in the lateral diameter. The spacings between two lenses in the arrays were 0.5–1 times of the lateral diameter.

The droplets of the oil-like polymerisable liquid lauryl methacrylate (LMA) were produced on the array of PLMA or PHDODA microlenses by solvent exchange and were also converted to PLMA by photopolymerization before morphological characterisation. The combinations of the microlens, liquid of the droplet, and contact angles of the droplet liquid on the lens material and the flat surrounding area of bare silicon are listed in Table 1. Both the advancing and the receding contact angles of LMA were much higher on the surrounding area than on the flat films of PLMA or PHDODA. For instance, when immersed in water the advancing angle of LMA was 103° on the flat area, but was 82° and 22° on a flat film of PHDODA and PLMA, respectively.

2. LMA droplet on PLMA microlens

LMA droplets were produced on an array of 5 μm PLMA microlenses, with a center-to-center spacing between two adjacent microlenses of 7.5 μm. Fig. 2 shows the representative optical images of composite microlens arrays after polymerisation of the droplets. The new microlenses exhibit high uniformity in their size, following the spatial arrangement and location of the original microlens array over a large surface area. The lateral diameter of the new microlens increases with the concentration of LMA in the first solution for solvent exchange. Meanwhile the spacing between the additive microlenses decreases obviously, and the distance between the neighbouring additive microlenses eventually became unresolvable for the optical resolution (∼1.5 μm) in our experiments. The results show that the microlenses in the array have provided uniform geometrical and chemical confinement for droplet formation.

The morphology of the new lens with a polymerised droplet on the top was further examined by atomic force microscopy (AFM) imaging. The cross-sectional profiles of the droplets were extracted from the images in Fig. 3(a)–(c), showing that the shape of the new microlens is a spherical cap. We note that the profile of the microlens with a high aspect ratio may likely be convoluted by the AFM tip. But the data are reliable for the microlens with a height less than half of the lateral diameter, i.e. the contact angle less than 90°. The above results demonstrate that it is possible to form femtoliter droplets selectively on top of microlenses. The height and volume of the polymerised droplets were extracted after subtraction of that of the underlying microlens, showing that the volume of the deposited liquid is of an order of femtoliter as shown in Fig. 3(d)–(g). The height increased from ∼2.5 μm to ∼3.5 μm, and correspondingly the contact angle of the new microlens was gradually built up from 70° to ≥90° by tuning the flow or solution conditions for solvent exchange.

3. LMA droplet on PHDODA microlens

It is also possible to form femtoliter droplets on a microlens made of other polymers. Here we performed solvent exchange to produce LMA droplets on the substrate with an array of PHDODA microlenses with the lateral diameter of 5 μm and 10 μm as shown in Fig. 1. After the process of solvent exchange-polymerisation, we obtained the composite microlens array shown in Fig. 4. To prove that the composite microlens consists of both PHDODA and PLMA, we performed infrared measurements on top of a pure PLMA lens, a pure PHDODA lens and a composite lens of PHDODA–PLMA. The measurements were performed by using an infrared microspectrometer and the obtained spectra are shown in Fig. 4. The infrared adsorption from all three types of polymeric microlens is located at a wavenumber of 1720 cm−1 from the stretching of a carbonyl group and at 2850 cm−1 and 2925 cm−1 from CH stretching of the α-hydrocarbon chain. The ratio of the peak intensity at a wavenumber of 1730 cm−1 and 2850 cm−1 is ∼9.2 on the pure PHDODA microlens, and is ∼0.6 for the pure PLMA microlens. The spectra from the composite microlens showed that the ratio of the intensity of these two peaks is ∼2.4, between the values from pure PHDODA (9.2) or from pure PLMA (0.8). The silicon substrate and the hydrophobic coating may have some interference with the spectrum of the supporting microlens (PHDODA) at a wavenumber around 1750 cm−1, leading to broadening of the peak. Although the interference makes it uncertain to determine the exact ratio of PHDODA and PLMA in the composite lens, it is sufficient to support our statement that the composite lens consists of both PLMA and PHDODA.

The droplets were also produced on microlenses of different heights, the representative AFM images of which are shown in Fig. 5(e)–(h). The height of the obtained droplet was from 1.57 μm to 3.16 μm under the same conditions of solvent exchange. The contact angle of the composite microlens is already above 90° for the two larger droplets. The slight increase in the lateral diameter from the droplet on the taller microlens may be partly related to the convulsion from AFM imaging. The droplet volume increased by 10–40 femtoliters after the solvent exchange. We compared the droplet volume on a substrate prepatterned with circular microdomains and on the microlenses under the same conditions of solvent exchange. The volume of the droplets produced on a planar substrate was from 6.9 fL to 19.5 fL for the domain diameter of 5 μm, and 21.1 fL to 96.5 fL for the domain diameter of 10 μm. Even though the conditions for solvent exchange were identical, the volume of the liquid varied with the size of the underlying lens: more liquid deposited on a taller lens, due to the difference in the growth dynamics of the droplets which will be discussed in the next section.

Furthermore, a new generation of droplets can be formed again on top of the composite microlens by performing the two-step process of droplet formation-polymerisation. In this way, it is possible to build up the microlens in a layer-by-layer manner. In our experiments, we repeated the process of droplet formation-polymerisation four times, and found discrete increment in the height of the composite microlens after each cycle while the lens remained in a highly ordered array all the time. For an array with a small spacing, a further increase in the droplet volume eventually led to contact and coalescence among droplets on adjacent microlenses, resulting in some spindle or worm-like microstructures.

4. Growth modes of the droplet on a microlens

We propose that there are two stages in the growth of droplets on a microlens. As sketched in Fig. 6(a), at an early stage, more than one small droplet form and grow on the microlens surface. There may be preferential nucleation of the droplets at the edge of the microlens, compared to the concave top surface.4 However there may be also droplets on the top surface as our lens was rather flat. These small droplets on the same microlens may grow in a mixed mode, switching between pinned and depinned modes. With time the droplets merge into one big droplet that fully covers the microlens surface and then grow upward in a constant contact radius (CR) mode. This two-stage growth may be similar to that observed for droplet formation in a smooth circular chemical pattern.26

5. Potential applications: additive production of composite microlens arrays and droplet-based faster nanoextraction

The controlled formation of femtoliter droplets on microlenses offers new opportunities for several droplet-based applications. As demonstration, we will show two very different cases: (1) additive production of composite microlens arrays with desirable optical properties, and (2) fast nanoextraction and in situ detection of traces of hydrophobic solutes from the flow of the solution.
(1) Optical properties of additive microlens arrays. Microlens arrays have been recently proposed to be a universal approach for improving the power conversion efficiency of organic-based photovoltaics.42,43 It is reported that in the coupling of incident light into the active layer of the device, a transparent polymer microlens array increases the light path in the active layer, resulting in an improvement of the overall cell efficiency by 15–60%.43 In a latest paper, the scheme based on a microlens array has been implemented in perovskite solar cells to enhance light harvesting.44 The arrays were attached on the glass side of the solar cell without disturbing the internal adsorbing layers. The reported absorption enhancement is ∼6% for the optimal period and height of microlenses due to their focusing effect.44 The microlens array is also reported to be beneficial for enhancing the efficiency of organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), providing a solution for reducing photon loss due to total internal refection at the substrate–air interface.44

Complementary to those elegant approaches for microlens fabrication,15,42,45–48 controlled formation of femtoliter droplets on a microlens provides a simple, flexible and solution-based approach for producing composite microlens arrays with tailored materials, curvature and spatial arrangement. The volume and hence the curvature of the composite microlens can be built up in an additive fashion. From the additive production, the curvature of the composite microlens is finely tuned by simple parameters for the solvent exchange. Another novel feature is the creation of a composite microlens that consists of different polymers of a desirable refractive index. Here we show the diffraction pattern from the array of composite microlenses embedded in a free-standing PDMS film prepared by following the protocol in our previous work.7

Fig. 7 shows the corresponding diffraction patterns from composite microlens arrays with PHDODA on the bottom layer and PLMA on the top layer. The lateral diameter of the composite microlens was ∼5 μm while the height was finely tuned from 0.94 μm to 2 μm during the formation of LMA droplets on the PHDODA microlens by solvent exchange. The results show that higher orders of diffraction patterns were obtained with the gradual increase in the height and hence the curvature of the composite lens. Clearly the additive production of microlens arrays offers great flexibility in terms of lens material, curvature and spatial arrangement.

(2) Fast nanoextraction and in situ detection. Surface droplets enrich traces of a hydrophobic solute, such as a fluorescent dye, from its aqueous solution, due to the high solubility of the solute in an oil phase than in water.9 We refer to this surface droplet based process as ‘nanoextraction’,49 in analogy to microextraction by emulsion droplets50,51 or a single hanging drop.52,53 In addition to the advantageous large specific surface area of surface droplets, the extracted solute may be also detected in situ as the solution of the analyte flows through the microchannel with the droplets on the wall. The droplet on the microlens is expected to be faster in extraction compared to the droplets located on the flat surface, due to the diffusion profile in the laminar flow.54,55 To demonstrate this aspect, we compare the time for a dye in the flow to be extracted by the femtoliter droplets and detected by using fluorescence microscopy as shown in Fig. 8.

In our experiments, the droplets were produced on microlens array substrates that were placed next to each other in the same microchannel with a height of ∼600 μm. The flow of the solution doped with the dye (Rhodamine 6G) reached the substrates at the same time. The droplets became red from the extracted dye, and the fluorescence intensity of the dye was uniform cross all the droplets in the array. The height of the droplet was varied by changing the height of the underlying microlens, or the oil concentration in the solutions for solvent exchange, and was characterised by AFM imaging of the polymerised droplets after the experiments of dye extraction. The plot in Fig. 8 shows the representative curves of the dye intensity in the droplets as a function of time. The results suggest that the dye was extracted to the droplets on taller microlenses much earlier.

To confirm that nanoextraction is earlier for the droplets lifted up from the wall, we tested nanoextraction by the droplet on a microlens sitting on the top of micropillars as sketched in Fig. 8(f). The micropillars capped with the microlens array were obtained by etching the substrate by following the reported protocol.8 The droplets were produced on the microlens on top of micropillars, the height of which was varied systematically. The measurements show that it took more than 100 s for the dye to be detected in the droplet with a distance of 430 nm from the substrate, but ∼50 s in the droplet with a distance of 3.92 μm. We obtained the time for dye detection in the fluorescent image as a function of droplet height in Fig. 8(g). Clearly placing the droplets on the microlens shortens the time required for nanoextraction by the droplets. The results demonstrate the potential of the droplet on the microlens for time effective extraction and separation.

Conclusions

In summary, this work reports controlled formation and potential applications of femtoliter liquid on a microlens. The selective formation of the droplet on top of the microlens was performed by solvent exchange on the microlens array with appropriate wetting properties. The droplet size is determined by diffusive growth, largely in a constant contact area mode with the lateral diameter similar to the original microlens. The contact angle of the coated microlens is finely tuned by the flow condition and the solution concentration for solvent exchange. This work shows the remarkable capability of solvent exchange in controlling droplet formation on surface microstructures, providing new opportunities for femtoliter droplet-based applications. As demonstration, we showed the additive production of the composite microlens array of the same or different polymers with diffraction patterns desirable for enhancement of light harvesting. We also showed that the process of nanoextraction of traces of hydrophobic solutes from the flow is much faster by using the droplet supported by a microlens, promising a simple approach for time effective detection in narrow fluidic channels.

Experimental section

(1) Preparation of microlens substrates

Highly ordered microlens arrays were prepared by polymerisation of droplets on a chemically patterned substrate by following the published protocol.7 In brief, polymerisable liquid droplets were produced by solvent exchange on the silicon substrate patterned with hydrophobic circular domains. The monomer was a mixture of 1,6-hexanediol diacrylate (HDODA) (80%, Sigma-Aldrich) and 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone (97%, Sigma-Aldrich). During the solvent exchange, the first solution was prepared by adding 4 mL of monomer in 100 mL of 50 vol% ethanol aqueous solution, while the monomer-saturated water was used as the second solution. The microlens arrays were obtained after the droplet arrays were photopolymerized by placing the fluid cell under a UV lamp (365 nm, 20 W) for about 15 min. We measured the shrinkage rate of a sessile LMA droplet a few micrometers in diameter upon curing, which was found to be less than 5% in our experiments.

(2) Formation and characterisation of droplet-on-microlens

Femtoliter droplets were formed on the lens-decorated surfaces by repeating the solvent exchange process, in which ethanol solution with LMA was replaced by water. In the process, the flow rates were controllable by a syringe pump. As the oil oversaturation and hence the droplet size produced by solvent exchange are strongly dependent on the flow rate, channel geometry and solution conditions,21,24 those conditions were well controlled in the experiments as specified. Solutions for the solvent exchange were prepared as follows: to prepare different concentrations of the monomer in solution A, lauryl methacrylate (LMA) with a volume of 0.2 mL (CL1), 0.3 mL (CL2), 0.4 mL (CL3) and 0.5 mL (CL4) was added into 10 mL of ethanol. When different solutions were used as solution A, the concentrations are specified in the figure captions. Water was used as solution B, due to the extremely low solubility of LMA.

Reflection mode optical microscopy (Huvitz HRM-300) was used to get the optical images of the microlens array and the newly formed composite microlens arrays. Atomic force microscopy (AFM, Dimension Icon-Bruker, ScanAsyst mode) was used to obtain the 3D topography of the polymerised droplet on the microlens. The images of the microlens were taken in the mode of ScanAsyst in air. The cantilevers for AFM imaging were purchased from Bruker (SCANASYST-AIR). We note that AFM images cannot reveal the real profile of the microlens with a contact angle >90°, due to the tip convolution.

The infrared spectra of the supporting microlens and the composite microlens were measured by using an imaging FTIR microscope (Perkin Elmer Spectrum Spotlight 400). The instrument was equipped with a diamond probe for attenuated total internal reflection mode (ATR). The configuration of ATR was used for the measurements of polymerized microlenses on patterned silicon substrates.

(3) Diffraction patterns of the microlens array and dye detection by nanoextraction

A thin layer of PDMS was cast on the array of composite microlenses and cured at elevated temperature. After being embedded into the film, the lens array was freed from the substrate by gently peeling off the PDMS film. The free standing film with the composite microlens array was illuminated normally with a white light source and the diffraction pattern was recorded using a colour camera.

Droplets were produced on the microlens array placed in a channel with a height (h) of 0.63 mm. The aqueous solution of Rhodamine 6G at a concentration of 5 ng mL−1 was injected into the fluid cell at a flow rate of Q = 300 μL min−1. The fluorescence intensity of the droplets was recorded by using top-view fluorescence microscopy. The recorded video was analyzed using self-written MATLAB codes.
