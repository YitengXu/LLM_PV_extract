Stable and efficient dye-sensitized solar cells: photophysical and electrical characterizations

Abstract

We investigate the lithium cation effects in high-performance dye-sensitized solar cells, which exhibit 10% efficiency and excellent thermal and light-soaking stability during 1000-h accelerated aging tests.


Broader context

Low-cost excitonic solar cells based upon exotic organic materials are receiving an ever-increasing amount of academic and industrial attention as potential alternatives to traditional inorganic photovoltaic devices. In this rapidly developing field, the dye-sensitized solar cell technology has achieved so far the highest validated efficiency of 11.1%. However the these champion cells employ volatile and toxic solvents such as acetonitrile in their electrolytes, which may be prohibitive for outdoor solar panels in view of the need for robust encapsulation. In this work, we formulated a new low-volatility electrolyte and demonstrated a 10% cell exhibiting a good stability under long-term thermal and light-soaking dual stress. Gelation of this high-efficiency practical electrolyte with silica nanoparticles could be applied to fabricate a quasi-solid-state cell, which also displays excellent performance.

Both scientific and industrial endeavors are continuously devoted to the research and development of the dye-sensitized solar cell (DSC) technology in view of its great potential to meet the demand for sustainable energy in the future low-carbon economy.1 The distinguishable feature of this photoelectrochemical cell mainly lies in an interpenetrating nanoscale network of electron-transporting inorganic nanocrystals and hole-transporting soft matter. Following light absorption by dye molecules grafted on a wide-bandgap titania film, exciton dissociation at the energy-offset dye/titania interface generates electronhole pairs possessing a sub-millisecond lifetime. The electron-hole lifetime is further prolonged to the millisecond domain by hole transfer to a redox electrolyte or a hole-transporter, allowing a high charge collection yield despite the low electron mobility in the nanocrystalline film.

Presently, a state-of-the-art DSC has already been validated with an impressive solar-to-electrical power conversion efficiency of 11.1%,2 albeit with a highly volatile acetonitrile-based electrolyte. To pursue a large-scale outdoor application of DSC, however, reducing electrolyte volatility is one of the critical issues concerning long-term device operation. To surmount this dilemma, over the past years innovative materials design and device engineering have powerfully impelled upward the efficiencies of cells with room-temperature ionic liquids and solid hole-transporting materials. In this context, an 8.5%-efficiency DSC exhibiting a fairly good thermal and light-soaking stability has been demonstrated by using a solvent-free ionic liquid electrolyte.3 While the power conversion efficiency has reached a certified 5% under AM1.5G full sunlight irradiation for solid-state DSCs with the organic hole transporter spiro-OMeTAD,4 the realization of long-term stable cells still remains a challenge so far.

3-Methoxypropinitrile (MPN) serves as a preferable compromise with regard to solvent volatility and materials cost as well as the cell efficiency. In combination with MPN-based electrolytes, dye-sensitized solar cells featuring moderate efficiencies have been disclosed with excellent thermal and light-soaking stability.3,5,6 In this work, we employ the high-absorptivity sensitizer C106 (Fig. S1 of the ESI) to construct stable DSCs exhibiting 10% efficiencies. Preliminary evaluation of C106 based on a traditional electrolyte formula3,5,6 without the lithium additive presents a limited cell efficiency with a relatively low photocurrent. Lithium iodide is purposefully introduced into the electrolyte, which results in an enhanced efficiency. Transient absorption and electrical impedance techniques are employed to scrutinize the physical origin of the variations in photovoltaic performance.

Fig. 1 presents the photocurrent action spectra of cells in conjunction with two MPN-based electrolytes E1 and E2. E1: 1.0 M 1,3-dimethylimidazolium iodide (DMII), 150 mM iodine (I2), 1.0 M 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP) and 0.1 M guanidinium thiocyanate (GNCS) in MPN; E2: 1.0 M DMII, 150 mM I2, 1.0 M TBP, 0.1 M GNCS and 50 mM LiI in MPN. The only difference of these two electrolytes lies in the lithium additive. Notice that almost no change of the electronic absorption (Fig. S2) was perceived for the C106-coated film immersed in electrolyte E1 compared to that in E2. Thereby, measurements on the incident photon-to-collected electron conversion efficiency (IPCE) can effectively evaluate charge generation and charge collection efficiencies in a testing cell. As shown in Fig. S3 of the ESI, while normalization of the IPCE subtracts any possible difference in the net charge separation efficiencies, the indistinguishable spectra indicate that the lithium cations in E2 do not noticeably influence the charge collection. Thereby, the notably higher IPCE maximum of cell with E2 (87%) than that with E1 (74%) could be attributed to the lithium-induced difference in net charge separation at the energy-offset dye/titania interface.


Fig. 1 Photocurrent action spectra of devices with electrolytes E1 and E2. Cells were tested using a metal mask with an aperture area of 0.158 cm2. An antireflection film was attached to the cells during measurement.

We resorted to the laser flash photolysis technique to examine the interfacial kinetic competition between dye regeneration and charge recombination of dye cations with electrons in the titania film,7,8 which is considered to make a remarkable contribution to the IPCE.9,10 On the basis of transient absorption measurements (Fig. S4), we selected a monochromatic light at 780 nm to probe the multichannel charge-transfer kinetics of dye cations. Excitation fluence was carefully controlled in our kinetic measurements to ensure that 3.70 1014 photons cm2 were irradiated onto the dye-coated titania film during every laser pulse. The absorption signal decays could be well fitted to a stretched exponential decay function (OD exp[(t/)]) to afford a half-reaction time t1/2. As shown in Fig. 2A, in the presence of inert electrolytes a and b with TFSI (bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide) anions, the absorption signal decays could be ascribed to the recombination of the dye cations with electrons in the titania film. The t1/2 with electrolyte a is 267 s, which decreases to 216 s upon the employment of electrolyte b with lithium cations. Active iodide anions in electrolytes c and d evidently speed up the absorption decays owing to the interception of dye cations by the electron-donating iodide anions (dye regeneration), giving the corresponding t1/2 of 2.9 and 1.7 s. Despite the fact that both interfacial charge recombination and dye regeneration are kinetically accelerated by lithium cations, their branching ratios are both over 92, indicating that almost 100% of the dye cations are intercepted with iodide. It thus can be soundly stated that the discrepancy in IPCE maxima is not attributable to the kinetic differences of dye regeneration and dye recombination with titania electrons.

We further investigated the initial amplitudes11,12 of dye cation absorption signals in inert electrolytes a and b, which are suitable for a comparative study of the electron injection yields. As shown in Fig. 2B, the initial amplitude of oxidized dye cation absorption with electrolyte b is apparently higher than that with electrolyte a, indicating that lithium cations in electrolyte b tend to improve the electron injection from excited-state dye molecules to the conduction band of titania, thus echoing well the observed IPCE height difference. We speculate that the difference in electron injection is probably related to the energy-offset variation at the dye/titania interface. Note that the invariable onset-wavelengths of IPCEs (Fig. 1) also indicate that the lithium cations exert no noticeable impact on the light absorption of C106 grafted on titania. JV characteristics of cells with electrolytes E1 and E2 measured under an irradiation of 100 mW cm2 AM1.5G sunlight are presented in Fig. 4, and photovoltaic parameters at different light intensities are collected in Table S1 of the ESI. The short-circuit photocurrent density (Jsc), open-circuit photovoltage (Voc), fill factor (FF), and overall power conversion efficiency () of cell with E1 are 14.97 mA cm2, 782 mV, 0.75 and 8.8%, respectively. In contrast, the device with E2 exhibits an incremented Jsc of 17.57 mA cm2, which is in good agreement with the photocurrent action spectra shown in Fig. 1. The Voc slightly decreases by 29 mV compared to that with E1, yielding an enhanced overall power conversion efficiency of 10.0%. Considering the fluidity of E2 which may cause inconvenience in the practical fabrication of flexible, compact and laminated DSC devices, silica nanoparticles were employed to construct a quasi-solid-state gel electrolyte18 (Fig. S1 in the ESI), which can be turned into liquid after 5 min sonication and remain liquid within 1 h for electrolyte injection. We note that the quasi-solid-state counterpart of E2 does not result in a noticeable variation in Jsc, while there is a small Voc diminishment of 12 mV compared to that with E2 and an almost invariable fill factor, contributing to an efficiency of 9.8%, as shown in Fig. S5.f

We submitted our high-efficiency cells covered with a UV absorbing polymer film to the 1000 h accelerated aging test at 60 C, under a solar simulator with a light intensity of 100 mW cm2. Note that the total contribution of UV photons to the standard AM1.5G solar emission is relatively small. UV light leads to direct band-edge excitation of titania nanocrystals, generating valence-band holes, which in turn may attack some organic materials in the device. This reaction can be partially suppressed by the hole capture with iodide. In our light-soaking experiments, UV light is removed with an antireflection coating. As presented in Fig. S6A, the cell with E1 has a relatively poor stability mainly due to the decrease of photocurrent. However, the device employing electrolyte E2 exhibits a fairly good stability (Fig. S6B) after 1000 h accelerated aging, keeping 92% of its initial efficiency. We also carried out stability tests of cells with the gel counterpart of E2 under thermal and light-soaking dual stress. As shown in Fig. 6, the quasi-solid-state cell presents an even better stability compared to that with the corresponding liquid electrolyte, retaining 95% of the initial efficiency after 1000 h accelerated aging.


Fig. 6 Photovoltaic parameters measured under the irradiance with 100 mW cm2 AM1.5G sunlight for a cell with the gel electrolyte during successive full sunlight soaking at 60 C.

In conclusion, by means of electrical impedance and transient absorption measurements, we scrutinize the origins of impacts of the additive lithium iodide in a low-volatility 3-methoxypropinitrile electrolyte upon the photovoltaic characteristics of dye-sensitized solar cells with a high-absorptivity ruthenium dye C106. Lithium cations significantly elevate the IPCE plateau by prompting a downward shift of the titania conduction band edge, which in turn brings on a more thermodynamically advantageous energy offset at the titania/dye interface for charge separation. This band edge movement is also found to play a prominent role in the adverse Voc shrinkage. The 3-methoxypropinitrile electrolyte with lithium cations and its quasi-solid-state counterpart have both been used to fabricate stable dye-sensitized solar cells exhibiting 10% power conversion efficiencies at the 100 mW cm2 AM1.5G conditions. This work should foster future DSC applications.
