Abstract

We are entering an era that emphasizes greenness and sustainability. Based on such a philosophy, it is critical to uncover novel and original sustainable reaction modes for future green chemical syntheses. The cross-dehydrogenative coupling (CDC) reaction has thus been widely developed as one of the most sustainable and efficient synthesis strategies for constructing Câ€“C bonds. This review summarizes the development of this field over the past 20 years, with a discussion on future trends and directions: from the original reaction model at the beginning and its development in the first decade, to extensive research in the second decade. The latest development sees the emergence of alternative forms of energy inputs (photoredox, mechano, microwave, electrochemical, continuous-flow and solar quantum dots) to facilitate CDC reactions, gradually replacing the classical form of thermal energy, which will inspire broader applications and innovations in the future.

1 Introduction

The construction of carbonâ€“carbon bonds â€“ central to organic chemistry â€“ is ubiquitous in all areas of scientific research.1 It has a fundamental impact on life sciences, the pharmaceutical industry, natural product syntheses, and materials science and technologies. Consequently, Câ€“C bond formation has been the subject of numerous studies by chemists around the world, with countless achievements. From the well-known classical nucleophilic substitutions, nucleophilic additions, electrophilic substitutions, electrophilic additions and pericyclic reactions,2 to the more modern transition-metal-catalyzed cross-couplings and radical chain reactions, and to the newer areas of NHC catalysis, nano-catalysis, photochemistry, electrochemistry and continuous flow chemistry, a wide range of reactions have been created for forming Câ€“C bonds.3

Coupling reactions, a powerful strategy for organic syntheses, are one of the most important topics under study in the field of organic synthesis.4 Different types of coupling reactions, such as the Kumada-coupling,5 Hiyama-coupling,6 Stille-coupling,7 Negishi-coupling,8 Suzuki-coupling9 and Sonogashira-coupling,10 and various coupling substrates have been developed over the past decades (Scheme 1).

All these protocols require two coupling groups: depending on the electronegativity of the leaving group, the carbon attached to a leaving group such as a halogen, usually bears a Î´ + charge, while the carbon attached to a leaving group such as a reactive metal carries a Î´ âˆ’ charge. When these two electronically different carbon coupling groups are placed in the transition-metal-catalyzed reaction system, they will undergo a favorable coupling reaction. However, the need to introduce these leaving groups adds extra-syntheses steps, leading to waste generation from reactions and purifications. Therefore, we can imagine that, if requirement for leaving groups is avoided and the cross-dehydrogenative coupling reaction takes place directly from the simplest Câ€“H bonds, it will greatly shorten the reaction steps and improve the reaction efficiency and atom utilization (Scheme 2). Thus, the concept of cross-dehydrogenative coupling (CDC) reactions was born and has gradually been developed and explored (Scheme 3).11 This review focuses on the historical development of this sustainable carbonâ€“carbon bond construction mode and its future trends.

2 Historical development of cross-dehydrogenative coupling

2.1 Pre-CDC era

The direct generation of Câ€“C bonds from only Câ€“H bonds can be traced back almost 150 years, when Glaser reported the homo oxidative dimerization of terminal alkyne Câ€“H bonds to form a conjugated diyne (Scheme 4a) in 1869, using a stoichiometric amount of Cu as a catalyst and oxidant.12 In the following decades, homo-dimerizations of Csp2â€“H bonds and Csp3â€“H bonds were also reported successively. Among them, Iataaki,13a Tanaka,13b and Yamaguchi's groups13c used palladium-catalyzed, rhodium/photocatalytic or thermal systems, respectively, to dimerize benzene to form biphenyl (Scheme 4b). In addition, Crabtree and co-workers14 reported in 1989 the use of the mercury/photocatalytic system to dehydrogenatively couple two Csp3â€“H bonds of small alkanes to form a mixture of larger aliphatic organic compounds (Scheme 4c). The analysis of the three classical reactions revealed that all three systems used stoichiometric transition metals or light sources as oxidants to enable the reactions to occur, but under harsh conditions with an extremely limited range of substrates, and generally homo-dimerizations. Such restrictions are due to the fact that, because carbon is partially negatively charged in the Câ€“H bond, due to its slightly greater electronegativity than hydrogen, there is an electronic mismatch when two negatively charged carbon atoms are coupled.
Scheme 4 Early examples of forming Câ€“C bonds from two Câ€“H bonds.

Therefore, an oxidizing agent must be added to the reaction system to selectively oxidize and dehydrogenate one of the Câ€“H bonds, so that the carbon is surrounded by the positive Î´+ and matches the other Câ€“H carbon Î´âˆ’ electronically for coupling. Importantly, this strategy will allow chemists to cross-couple any two different Câ€“H bonds readily. With this bold design and hypothesis, chemists have discovered through extensive exploration that this reaction mode generally produces the following three key intermediates in different catalytic reaction systems to make the cross-dehydrogenative coupling reaction successful: the first is a carbocation intermediate produced by the oxidative H-removal of Câ€“H by an oxidant; the second is a carbon radical intermediate produced by a single-electron oxidation of Câ€“H bonds by an oxidant or H-abstractor; and the third is a transition-metal complex intermediate formed by the activation of the Câ€“H bond during transition-metal catalysis. The subsequent reaction with the Câ€“H bond of another molecule results in the Câ€“C bonds being constructed (Scheme 5).

2.2.1.1 CDC-type aza-Henry reaction. The aza-Henry reaction was reported by Leonard and Leubner in 1949,17 by reacting phenylnitromethane with dihydroisoquinoline iodide, which was pre-generated by the oxidation of tetrahydroisoquinoline with iodine, eventually giving the corresponding coupling product. Li's group first reported the CDC-type aza-Henry reaction in 2005.18 Using different tertiary amines and different nitroalkanes as the two coupling partners, the authors found that the reaction under the catalysis of cuprous bromide with tert-butyl hydroperoxide as the oxidant gave the products in good yields, in which the Csp3â€“H bond at the Î±-position of the nitrogen atom dehydrocoupled with the Csp3â€“H at the Î±-position of the nitro group. Subsequently, the authors chose different tertiary amines (tetrahydroisoquinoline and N,N-dimethylaniline) for coupling with nitromethane and nitroethane for the reaction, to afford the expected products in 30â€“75% yields (Scheme 7).

As the Mannich reaction is an important method for synthesizing Î²-carbonyl amines, great efforts have been made to extend the CDC reaction to Mannich-type reactions.20

2.2.1.5 CDC-type alkynylation. The synthesis of alkynes has long been a focus of organic synthesis.30 To address this challenge through cross-dehydrogenative coupling, Li's group was the first to explore the coupling reaction of alkynes with tertiary amines in 2004.31 It was found that, when using CuBr as the catalyst and TBHP as the oxidant, the reaction could yield the tertiary amine alkynylated products in good yields at 100 Â°C, while still being able to react with less reactive aliphatic-terminated alkynes (Scheme 18).

2.2.1.9 Aldol-type reaction. The aldol reaction is an important strategy for the synthesis of Î±,Î²-unsaturated ketones and Î²-hydroxy or alkoxy ketones.50 In 2005, Li and co-workers used a CDC strategy to obtain aldol-type products. The reaction uses a copper/indium catalytic system to dehydrogenatively couple the Câ€“H bond adjacent to oxygen and the carbonyl group in the presence of DDQ or simply using DDQ as an oxidant without using a metal catalyst under heating.51,52 Mechanistically, with malonic acid ester as the reaction substrate, it formed a six-membered ring metal-complex with copper/indium coordination. For the ether substrate under the oxidation of DDQ, the hydrogen atom of the Câ€“H bond adjacent to oxygen was abstracted to form an oxonium ion, which then underwent nucleophilic addition with the metal-complex intermediate to form the aldol-type products. For metal-free catalysis, the authors proposed that DDQ, the two-electron oxidant, performed a secondary one-electron oxidation of the ether to give an oxonium ion intermediate. 3 Future perspectives of cross-dehydrogenative coupling

In the above sections, we discussed classical reaction types and research progress in the development and exploration of their CDC alternatives. These developments have led to the creation of a large volume of research works in the construction of Câ€“C bonds via CDC reactions and their applications in the synthesis of natural products, biological agents and organic materials. In recent years, CDC reaction systems have been further refined, for example, for the asymmetric synthesis of organic small molecules and metal-complexes. 4 Conclusion and outlook

This review summarized important research progress for the cross-dehydrogenation coupling reaction over the past 20 years. Guided by the concept of green and sustainable chemistry, the design and implementation of this concept, the initial development of this reaction in the first decade, and more recent developments and future trends are introduced in detail. The development of the CDC reaction has led to breakthrough advances in the field of carbonâ€“carbon bond formation. In particular, with the advance of a large number of science and technology tools, new forms of energy input, such as photoredox, electrochemical redox, microwave-assisted, continuous flow chemistry and quantum dot catalysis, are gradually replacing classical energy inputs in synthesis with their unique advantages. At the same time, the original design and intention of this reaction mode â€“ with only a single by-product of hydrogen â€“ has now been realized. Thus, in the future, CDC reactions will be widely developed not only for carbonâ€“carbon bond formation and the synthesis of important organic molecular skeletons, but also for energy storage while performing the above processes. Meanwhile, an untackled ideal of the CDC reaction is the production of long chain alkanes and hydrogen gas by a simple dehydrogenation of methane (a major greenhouse gas), which will also become one of the focuses of synthetic chemists in the near future. Indeed, this landmark organic synthesis protocol will have an increasingly meaningful role in and impact on various fields of science and technologies.
