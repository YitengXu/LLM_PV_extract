Abstract

Recently, intense pulsed light (IPL), also referred to as flash lamp annealing, has gained interest among materials scientists as a highly effective photonic technology for structural reformation and/or chemical modification of various nanomaterials. Split-second exposure of IPL on advanced materials of various compositions, including ceramics, metals, and carbon, and various structures, including nanoparticles, nanowires, and thin films, resulted in dramatic changes in the morphologies and chemical functional groups of the materials. Compared to conventional thermal heating and established photonic technologies such as lasers, IPL technologies have considerable advantages, such as facile equipment set-up, surface-selective treatment, large treatment area, short process times ranging from milliseconds to a few seconds, and roll-to-roll process compatibility. In this review, recent advances in the structural development and/or chemical modifications of various nanomaterials by IPL irradiation will be highlighted.

1. Introduction

Structural control of nanomaterials is of great importance in the electronic device industry, as the structure of nanomaterials determines the physical, optical, and electrical properties of future electronic devices. The initial micro- and nanostructures of the nanomaterials are natively determined during manufacturing processes such as deposition and printing. Furthermore, their ultimate structures can be reconstructed through post-treatments such as thermal annealing. In order to realize nanomaterial-based functional electronic devices with outstanding performances, the annealing process is almost necessary for modification or control of the nanostructures, which maximizes functionalities of the nanomaterials. The most commonly used method for annealing nanomaterials is thermal heating. However, thermal heating based on the heat transfer through a medium is a time- and energy-consuming process; thus, it cannot be appropriate for low cost and rapid manufacturing of the product. In particular, with an enormous demand for flexible and soft electronics based on plastic substrates, an annealing method that is not detrimental to the plastic substrate with a low glass transition temperature is required. In this case, if functional nanomaterials require temperatures that are higher than the deformable temperature of the plastic substrate, thermal damage of the substrate is unavoidable during the thermal heating process that influences the structural properties of the nanomaterials. To overcome this limitation, photonic technologies using various light sources such as ultra-violet light, infrared light, lasers, and microwaves have been applied as highly efficient methods for nanostructure control. Photonic technologies, in particular, allow material- and exposed-area selective treatment if they are chosen according to the following criteria: (i) use of a specific wavelength at which the target nanomaterial would selectively respond; (ii) manipulation of the irradiation condition; and (iii) utilization of photo-responsive chemical reagents.

Recently, intense pulsed light (IPL), also referred to as flash lamp annealing (FLA), has gained interest among materials scientists as a highly effective photonic technology for structural reformation and/or chemical modification of various nanomaterials. IPL is the non-laser high intensity light that is emitted from a xenon flash lamp with a broad wavelength output in the 400–1200 nm range. The most important advantage of IPL technology is its short processing time, which is on the order of milliseconds. The short processing time can reduce the thermal budget of a target material and enable one to achieve high temperatures in the near-surface region. Owing to its high speed processability, IPL has been utilized as a sub-second annealing process for silicon semiconductor technologies to overcome the limitation of the established rapid thermal annealing (RTA) process.1,2 From a flexible electronics standpoint, IPL is very promising because high temperature heating can be achieved only on the surface without heating the whole substrate.

IPL technologies have been widely utilized in the medical community for the treatment of many vascular and pigmented lesions, and the removal of unwanted hair.3–5 In dermatology, it is known that the working principle of the IPL rests on the selective photothermolysis of chromophores,6 in which thermally mediated radiation damage is confined to the epidermal and/or dermal pigmented targets at the cellular or tissue structural levels. In fact, as a photonic process, light amplification by stimulated emission of radiation (LASER) technology, which is based on a monochromatic light source, and its intense development predated IPL technologies. However, IPL has certain advantages over LASER: (1) The broad wavelength range discharged from an IPL device leads to the simultaneous emission of green, yellow, red, and infrared wavelengths, allowing various chromophores to be targeted concurrently, and (2) a relatively large light-spot size (over several square centimeters) minimizes the total number of treatments and provides swift treatment over a large area. These advantages are also quite fascinating when IPL is applied to nanomaterial processing. Most nanomaterials show spectral distributions during light absorption because the development of a monodisperse system of nanomaterials has been limited. Furthermore, hybridization of nanomaterials with various functional materials has been adopted to enhance their material properties. Considering these points, monochromaticity of the light source is not mandatory for photonic annealing of nanomaterials; rather, the broad emission spectrum of the IPL technology can effectively treat the multiple components of nanomaterials that have different light absorption properties.

Fig. 1a shows the schematic of an IPWL irradiation system. An IPWL device consists of a xenon flash lamp, a reflector, a power supply, capacitors, and a pulse controller. The pulse controller triggers the capacitor, which delivers electrical current to the lamp within milliseconds, causing consecutive light pulses to irradiate the substances. The fluence of irradiation can be controlled by varying the pulse parameters, including the pulse duration time (PDT), the free temporal period (FTP), the applied voltage, and the number of pulses, as shown in Fig. 1b. The pulse parameters are independently controllable in a millisecond range, and can be optimized according to the material properties. The radiation produced by the xenon flash lamp is primarily dependent on the density of the current.7 The emission spectrum of a xenon flash lamp measured at various applied voltages is presented in Fig. 1c. As shown in the figure, the range of the visible emission tends to be enhanced when a higher voltage is applied. The first demonstration of the effect of flash light on nanomaterials was reported in 2002 by Ajayan et al.8 They accidentally discovered that single walled carbon nanotubes (SWNTs) transformed into single layered conical tips reminiscent of nanohorns when exposed to conventional photographic flash light. This work sparked interest in the study of the influence of flash light on nanostructured materials. Another important demonstration of the application of the IPL technique to sinter copper nanoparticles printed on plastic substrates, reported in 2009 by Kim et al.,9 stimulated enormous interest in the research field of printed electronics. More recently, systematic studies on the structural development of various nanomaterials, including polymeric materials, metallic alloys, metal oxides, nanocarbons, metal nanoparticles, and nanowires, have been reported. In this review, we will highlight the recent advances in structural development and/or chemical modification of various nanomaterials by IPL irradiation. This review will cover the effect of IPL irradiation on various nanomaterials and the key features of each material. An attempt will be made to review the numerous publications in the literature and discussions will be presented regarding possible mechanisms for the structural development of nanomaterials.

2. Metallic nanoparticles and nanowires

One of the key components of printed electronic technologies is the conductive materials to make fine conducting patterns as an electrode.10–14 Although many studies have explored the use of conductive polymers such as polyacetylenes, polypyrroles, polythiophenes, polyanilines and poly(p-phenylene vinylene), their conductivity and durability are much lower compared with those of other metallic materials such as copper, gold, and silver.15,16 Intrinsically, metallic materials exhibit a low electrical resistivity of around 10−6 Ω cm and a high conductivity of around 106 S cm−1.17 In particular, metal nanoparticles are transformed effectively into conductive paths at a much lower temperature compared with bulk materials owing to their high surface area-to-volume ratio.18 Furthermore, as the size of the metal nanoparticles decreases by several tens of nanometers, nanoparticles can be more densely packed, which reduces the porosity created by features such as craters and cracks upon IPL irradiation, leading to a significant decrease in the resistivity of the sintered metallic films. This section will focus on metallic ink-based approaches that can provide robust and reliable conductive electrode patterns on the plastic substrates over large areas.

2.1. Copper based conductive inks

2.1.1. Copper nanoparticles. Most of the studies on conductive inks for IPL have been conducted with copper nanoparticles, which are cheaper than other metallic materials.19–21 Copper nanoparticles have been used widely as an IPL sinterable ink for the fabrication of printed electronic devices on flexible plastic substrates.22–25 The IPL method can rapidly sinter the nanoink in a few milliseconds, reducing the oxide shells of copper nanoparticles by any organic dispersants under ambient conditions at room temperature without any damage to the substrates. As a result, IPL sintering makes the non-conductive copper nanoinks conductive after they melt and then agglomerate by the photothermal effect.

In earlier studies that aimed to evaluate the effect of IPL irradiation, a commercial copper nanoink, which contains uniformly dispersed copper nanoparticles that are about 5 nm in diameter in a mixed solvent of ethylene glycol and 2-methoxyenthanol, was used in the fine printing of a plastic substrate.26 As shown in Fig. 2, the copper nanoparticles agglomerated at a light intensity greater than 40 J cm−2, while the surface morphologies exhibited large cracks without any particle aggregation at an intensity lower than 30 J cm−2. The sintered copper films developed a grainy structure owing to the formation of neck-like junctions between neighboring nanoparticles, and the resulting electrical resistivity was 5 µΩ cm, which is only 3 times as high as that of bulk copper. In spite of this, the resistivity of this IPL-sintered copper nanoink, which was 17.2 µΩ cm, was one third that of the thermally sintered copper nanoink. The lumped mass method was used to estimate the temperatures of the sintered copper patterns and polyethylene substrates. The results showed that the temperatures of the sintered copper patterns and polyethylene substrates increased by up to 297 and 57 °C, respectively, which was attributed to the absorption of heat transferred from IPL. IPL irradiation can suppress the thermal oxidation of the copper nanoparticles and any damage of the plastic substrates even when the sintering process is conducted under ambient conditions owing to the short exposure time of 2 ms. Therefore, this attribute of IPL sintering would prove beneficial for the development of next-generation electronic devices based on low-temperature flexible plastic substrates.

The fatal disadvantages of the copper nanoparticles are that they are prone to degrade and to readily oxidize after storage under ambient conditions, which affects their electrical properties as printed electrodes. Therefore, most commercially available copper nanoparticles have a core–shell structure, which is covered with thermodynamically stable oxide layers, as shown in Fig. 3.19 IPL irradiation can successfully remove the thin Cu2O oxide shells of the copper nanoparticle surfaces, making the oxidized copper particles conductive within a short period after exposure. In particular, poly(N-vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP)-functionalized copper nanoparticles exhibit outstanding oxide reduction performances owing to an intermediate acid created by ultraviolet light or hydroxyl end groups that act like long-chain alcohol reductants under IPL irradiation.19–23 The PVP-coated copper nanoparticle films show a high surface resistivity value of 5 µΩ cm, which corresponds to about three times that of bulk copper owing to the complete removal of Cu2O oxide by reactive sintering through a combination of IPL and PVP. PVP tends to be well adsorbed on the surface of the copper oxide nanoparticles by hydrogen bonding through an acid–base interaction between the metal surfaces and polymer segments. IPL irradiation with an energy of 50 J cm−2 leads to photo- and thermal-degradation of PVP that involves hydroxyl radical attacks caused by the absorption of high-intensity light energy and heat transport by the copper nanoparticles. As a result, the PVP chains are converted to primary alcohols, secondary alcohols, carboxyl acids, or acetic acid (Fig. 3). Therefore, these resulting intermediate species can completely reduce the copper oxide shells on the IPL-sintered copper nanoparticles.

Copper based ink systems are used primarily because they lower the production cost during mass production. Thus, the processing conditions must be well suitable for such rapid production systems. Most of the studies have concentrated on controlling IPL processing conditions rather than on making the printed copper inks highly conductive to realize highly reliable, durable, cost-effective, and productive conductive electrode patterns.27,28 A commercially available water-based copper oxide ink with 16 wt% solid content was inkjet-printed on one side of a primer-coated porous PET foil.20 The primer was a hydrophilic silica layer prepared by a sol–gel reaction, and it provided intimate contact with the dropping CuO inks that were used to print fine high quality patterns. The objective of this study was to improve the duration for the formation of the conductive layer to less than 1 s during a simultaneous ink drying and sintering process, as this would lead to high production of roll-to-roll based flexible electronics through an IPL process with less numbers of pulses and minimal fine-tuning of the IPL process parameters. The sheet resistance of the sintered copper layer was inversely proportional to the IPL energy dose. When a single-pulse of 3.98–5.48 J cm−2, double-pulses of 5.86–7.03 J cm−2, and triple-pulses of 7.48–9.98 J cm−2 were applied, the sheet resistance changed from 0.355 to 2.861 Ω sq−1, from 0.133 to 0.172 Ω sq−1, and from 0.121 to 0.599 Ω sq−1, respectively (Fig. 4). However, during IPL sintering, the printed electrode patterns were sometimes delaminated from the substrates owing to the fast evaporation of solvents and binders of the copper oxide inks by the strongly intensive pulse energy. These results indicate that the exposure to excessive energy for relatively short pulse durations causes a severe delamination of the sintered copper layers. As a result, the sheet resistance of the patterns that were exposed to a double-pulse irradiated energy of above 7.48 J cm−2 was increased to 4.57 MΩ sq−1. Therefore, fine control of the IPL processing conditions plays an important role in the revolution of mass-productive, cost-effective printed electronic devices.

2.1.2. Copper salts. Metallic salt solutions are more suitable for achieving conductive inks at low-temperature and without-damage processes for the polymer-based flexible substrates.29 Although copper nanoparticles offer highly conductive and cost-effective nanoinks for electronic devices, it is difficult to make nanoparticles homogeneous and to maintain a good dispersion of nanoparticles within solutions.30,31 A copper ion-based electronic ink that is free of any metallic particles can be more suitable for flexible substrates, as it enables sintering at relatively low temperatures.32 The copper ion ink was obtained by mixing copper(II) hydroxide and ammonium hydroxide in DI water. Then, formic acid and citric acid were added as complexing agents for the reduction and stabilization of the copper ions and to adjust the pH of the ink solution to 8. The carboxyl groups of the acids encourage copper ionization, which increases the solubility of the conductive ink solution without any formation of an oxide or hydroxide.33,34 After the copper ion-based ink patterns were irradiated with an energy of 40.6 J cm−2, the organic compounds combusted completely, and the copper particles became connected to each other by the nucleation of copper, after which they demonstrated excellent electrical resistance. The advantage of a metal ion ink system is that it does not use any types of organic dispersants to impede the performances of the conductive electrode patterns. As a result, following application in an electronic circuit, a spiral-type inkjet-printed micropattern with a line width of 160 µm exhibited an electrical resistivity of 5.27 µΩ cm at an IPL energy of 40.6 J cm−2. This type of ink could be more suitable for printing techniques such as roller-pen, inkjet printing, and microplotter, as it reduces the overall cost (Fig. 5).

Copper salt precursors, such as copper formate, copper acetate, and copper oleate, can be used as metallic sources for the formation of the printed electrode patterns after photonic sintering by IPL.35 The cupric ions are coordinated with carboxyl groups to form a metallic complex, and the light absorption characteristics of the copper salt-based inks, which are an important parameter for photonic sintering, are determined by their crystal field splitting.33,34,36 Carboxylated coppers with ligand molecules become slightly photo-sensitive with ligand splitting in the range of 650–850 nm as a function of the length of the carbon chains.26 Their crystal field splitting states are controlled by the carbon chain length of the ligands coordinated around the metal centers, depending on the light absorption ability of the precursor inks. As a result, the printed copper complex precursor films can be successfully converted to metallic copper patterns through photonic sintering under appropriate IPL intensity. The absorption peak of the carboxylated copper inks with short carbon chains is observed at about 700 nm, but the copper oleate ink with relatively long carbon chains displays a gradual absorption at around 400–600 nm. Such optical characteristics of the oleate ligands would be more beneficial for photonic sintering owing to the wide pulsed light absorption band of the IPL. Despite these significant advancements, the long carbon chains of the oleate ligands are converted to the carbide compounds owing to their high residual carbon content on the printed copper patterns after IPL sintering, which results in severe reduction in their electrical properties. The electrical resistivities obtained for the formate, acetate, and oleate ligands were 5.6 × 10−5 Ω cm, 2.1 × 10−2 Ω cm, and a value outside measurement range (OMR), respectively. These results were attributed to the increasing carbon chain length of the ligands contained in the inks. Furthermore, during IPL irradiation, the sintered copper patterns containing a large amount of carbon exhibited a crater-like defect morphology owing to the decomposition of the carbon chains and then evaporation of carbon oxide gases. This affected their electrical performances.

2.1.3. Copper nanowires. Metal-based nanowires have received increased attention as promising electrode materials for a wide range of next-generation electronic devices, such as flexible optoelectronics, wearable electronics, and E-skins.37–41 The metal nanowire-based ink patterns are well known to have highly conductive and stable operable characteristics under repetitive stretching–releasing cycles. For example, printed silver nanowire conductors with wavy structures display negligible resistance increase under a strain of up to 460%.39 However, the silver nanowires are expensive, and they easily melt and break, owing to electromigration even at relatively low temperature, which hinders their practical applications.42 In contrast, copper nanowires are cheaper than silver nanowires and have analogous electrical properties. Nevertheless, to develop highly stretchable nanowire-based electronic devices, complicated, time-consuming, and high temperature processes are essential if an improvement in the electrical conductivity and adhesion strength between nanowires and substrates is to be realized.

Interestingly, when the printed copper nanowire-based conductors were exposed to the fast photonic sintering technique, their electrical conductivity and adhesion to the substrates greatly increased (Fig. 6).43 The IPL can be irradiated on the spray-coated copper nanowires' percolating network on PU flexible substrates at room temperature in an environment without any protective atmosphere. The photonic energy can weld the copper nanowires at the contact point to each other and coincidently deoxidize the oxide layers on the surfaces of the copper nanowires, which results in the formation of a highly conductive metallic mesh structure.44,46 At the same time, the PU substrates are softened by IPL exposure, which allows the nanowires to embed into the polymer matrix, leading to the enhancement of the adhesion strength. As a result, the freshly prepared copper nanowire films exhibited a poor sheet resistance of 1 × 105 Ω sq−1 owing to the weak interconnection between nanowires and the oxide layers of the copper surfaces, while the IPL-irradiated conductive films showed an outstanding sheet resistance of several Ω sq−1 after photonic sintering with an energy of 272 mJ cm−2 for 20 µs under ambient conditions owing to the welding of nanowires at crossing points. A Scotch tape test showed that the buried copper nanowires did not easily detach from the PU surfaces. Furthermore, their sheet resistance remained almost constant without significant damages even after several cycles. Additionally, although the resistance of the copper nanowires embedded in the PU films gradually increased with applied strain, it recovered to 36 Ω, which was 4 times higher than the original value, as the strain was 0%, even after being stretched–released at 50% strain. This means that the copper nanowire embedded PU conductors exhibit good mechanical flexibility. Therefore, these types of transparent and robust copper nanowire-based conductors can be used to produce wearable strain sensors and heaters.

Furthermore, patterned flexible transparent electrodes can successfully be achieved by controlling the adhesion strength between the copper nanowires and the flexible polymer substrates, as shown in Fig. 7.47 A combination of IPL irradiation with a thick paper photomask and a successive roll-to-roll-based wiping technique can be used to selectively attach or detach the copper nanowire networks on the polycarbonate substrates depending on the difference in their adhesion strengths.47 The IPL-irradiated copper nanowires were not easily delaminated because of their strong adhesion that developed as a result of fusion of underlying polymer substrates with nanowires, while unexposed nanowires were easily removed from the substrates, enabling the copper nanowire-based flexible transparent conducting electrodes to be patterned. 2.2. Silver based conductive inks

2.2.1. Silver nanoparticles. Although copper nanoparticles have been mostly used as conductive inks for IPL-based electronics, because they are easily oxidized under ambient conditions, their use is limited in practice.48 Silver, as one of the alternatives, is adequate for IPL sintering when it has a particle size of less than 50 nm that can be sintered at temperatures lower than 200 °C.49–51 In general, silver nanoparticles have a relatively lower melting temperature and are also more resistant to air oxidation than copper.

Silver nanoparticle solutions were used to produce IPL-sintered conductive patterns on glass-fiber-reinforced polymer composite (GFRPC) laminates and polyimide films.52 The silver nanoparticle based conductive nanoinks were prepared by the dispersion of silver nanoparticles with a diameter of 20 nm to 40 nm and a melting temperature of 194.1 °C in diethylene glycol (DEG) and water.53,54 When the applied IPL energies were 20 J cm−2, 30 J cm−2, 40 J cm−2, and 50 J cm−2 on the inkjet-printed conductive line patterns using a single pulse mode, the silver nanoparticles began to agglomerate at an energy density of 40 J cm−2, and the sizes of the agglomerated particles were large enough to form a conductive path at an energy density of 50 J cm−2, as shown in Fig. 8. To more completely sinter the silver nanoink pattern, the total energy density was kept constant at 50 J cm−2, but the number of consecutive pulses was varied from one to three. The average resistivity of the IPL-sintered silver patterns with three consecutive pulses was 49 ± 3 nΩ m, which is low enough for printing electronics such as RFID tags, while that of thermally sintered silver patterns was 39 ± 2 nΩ m.26 The neck-like junctions between nanoparticles were observed on the sintered conductive silver patterns after three consecutive pulses at 50 J cm−2 in less than 30 ms, as shown in Fig. 8e. These results show that applying IPL with a high energy density for a longer period of time is more effective in fully sintering silver nanoink patterns. A lumped capacitance method showed that, although the silver conductive pattern was sintered at room temperature using IPL, its temperature increased by 500 °C upon irradiation with an IPL energy of 50 J cm−2.55,56

A camera flash-lamp was tested for photonic sintering of inkjet-printed silver tracks on polyimide, polyethylene terephthalate, and photographic paper at room temperature.57 Chemically synthesized silver nanoparticles were dispersed uniformly in ethanol with a concentration of 14 wt%, and printed on the silver patterns by a piezoelectric inkjet printer on various substrates. The resistivity of the silver line printed on the polyimide film decreased from a non-conductive state to 7.3 µΩ cm, which is about 4.6 times higher than that of bulk silver, upon the second flashing, and it exhibited a slight increase after the third and fourth flashing. These phenomena were attributed to the attenuated photothermal effect on the top layers of the printed silver nanoparticles, which causes oxidation of the surface of the silver patterns during the additional flash sintering.58 Furthermore, the resistivity of the silver pattern printed on PET substrates was the lowest, and the one printed on photographic paper exhibited the largest resistivity. These results are attributed to the considerable melting of the silver nanoparticles printed on the photographic paper. As the paper has poor heat conducting abilities, heat is dissipated to the substrates and the surroundings slowly, which increases the inner temperatures of the silver nanoparticles.

The advantage of photonic sintering is that it is more suitable for low Tg flexible polymer substrates compared with traditional thermal sintering, during which heating occurs at temperatures greater than 200 °C for several tens of minutes.26,59,60 High intensity light energy provided by IPL irradiation transforms printed silver nanoparticles into nanosilver films within a few tens of ms by instantly increasing the temperature of the nanoparticles to the melting point of silver.61 However, consecutive IPL irradiation results in remarkable morphological changes in the IPL-sintered metallic patterns that are due to the gas generated by the thermal decomposition of the organic additives, such as a binder and a dispersant, in the nanoink solution, as shown in Fig. 9.61 The surface morphology is affected by the intensity of the incident pulsed light and pattern dimensions. When three consecutive light pulses are applied to the ink-jet printed nanosilver patterns, the nanoparticles near the top surface primarily absorb the light energy of the first pulse, generating a thin metallic layer on the surface of the PI substrates. As the second and third IPL pulses are consecutively applied, the other unsintered nanoparticles beneath the as-formed metallic layer start to be sintered by heat transfer from the topmost surface. As this metallic layer formed on the surface prevents the expulsion of gas produced by the decomposition of the organic additives in the inner conductive pattern, the film slightly expands in the z-axis direction, resulting in the formation of a hollow microstructure inside the IPL-sintered patterns. The microstructure, owing to attributes such as delamination and high porosity, results in higher electrical resistance compared with the thermally sintered conductive patterns. This means that it is important to define a uniform microstructure of IPL-sintered surfaces in order to minimize the morphological changes and increase the electrical conductivity. When the IPL energy is increased, the grain growth behavior improves mechanical properties such as Young's modulus and hardness of silver nanoparticles. This diminishes the degree of swelling due to thickening and densification of the as-sintered metallic layers.

2.2.2. Silver nanowires. Compared with the copper nanowire-based nanoinks, silver nanowires can also be a promising candidate for mechanically robust and stretchable transparent electrodes.37,39,45,62–64 The silver nanowires synthesized via a polyol process were coated on the PET substrates, followed by sintering by IPL with varying levels of light intensity and pulse time.65,66 When exposed to an IPL energy of 1.14 J cm−2 for only 50 µs, the sheet resistance and transmittance of the sintered silver nanowire mesh film were 19 Ω sq−1 and 83% at 550 nm, respectively.45 Extreme local interface heating by IPL sintering provides the silver nanowire films with high conductivity and encourages strong adhesion between the nanowires and polymer substrates. Simulations using the software supplied by Novacentrix, USA, showed that the temperatures of the top layer of the silver nanowires, the interface between the substrate and nanowires, and the bottom of the PET substrate were 540 °C, 540 °C, and room temperature, respectively. At this time, an improvement in the adhesion strength is attributed to heat generated by silver after it absorbs irradiated energy that is immediately transferred to the interface between the sintered metallic films and substrates. The silver nanowire-coated films did not easily peel off even with multiple peeling tests owing to the formation of knurls on the surface of the silver nanowires and buried structures of the silver nanowires on the PET substrates after irradiating at an IPL energy of 1.14 J cm−2. These results show that the highly conductive and transparent silver nanowire mesh films with strong adhesion and flexibility on the PET substrates can be simply fabricated with short-duration IPL sintering through a one-step process, which is very appropriate for rapid printed manufacturing lines.

Interestingly, the IPL can be used to repair the damaged conductive electrode paths. A combination of the printed silver nanowires and polyurethane with Diels–Alder (DA) adducts as a crosslinker provides thermally amendable electrode patterns that enable effective repair of the damaged films by short-duration IPL irradiation of less than 1 ms.67 The DA reaction is a cycloaddition between a conjugated diene and a substituted alkene that forms a cyclohexene structure.68–70 The reaction is reversible under certain conditions, in which case it is known as the retro-DA reaction. The polyurethane incorporating furyl moieties reacts with bismaleimides to form thermally healable polymers through the retro-DA reaction at a temperature above 100 °C, but as the polymers cool, the DA reaction occurs and crosslinks are formed.71–73 To develop the IPL repairable transparent electrodes, the transferred silver nanowires were lightly buried at the surface of the DA-functionalized polyurethane films. After the scratched silver nanowires–polyurethane composite surfaces were irradiated with IPL of 0.66 J cm−2 light intensity for 500 µs, their resistances changed from 100 kΩ to 16 ± 1.8 Ω as a function of the number of pulses, as shown in Fig. 10. This result is attributed to the generated heat that the silver nanowires absorb from the IPL energy under the optimized irradiation conditions. The heat is efficiently transferred to the cut polyurethane substrates, resulting in significant repair to the damage sustained during the initial stage.74 Therefore, the IPL irradiation process can be considered as an alternative to conventional thermal heating for the development of healable electronic devices.

2.3. Hybrid-type conductive inks

In practice, the major components of printed electronics that form the conductive lines have to exhibit high conductivity and high oxidation resistance. Moreover, they need to be cheap. However, although copper is cheap, it is susceptible to oxidation. Silver and gold, which are resistant to oxidation, are expensive.75,76 Therefore, synergistic combinations of two-types of materials compensate for the material's intrinsic disadvantages, helping in realizing electronic devices that have a low price and high reliability. Therefore, the key to developing low-cost conductive inks is to prevent the thermal oxidation of copper having high electrical resistance and low cost.

The simultaneously co-located copper and silver hybrid nanoinks, obtained by a one-step wire explosion process, would be a promising candidate for IPL-based printed electronics.77 In order to prepare the copper and silver-based hybrid nanoinks, a wire explosion process was used to fabricate silver nanoparticles that were uniformly attached and surrounded by tiny copper nanoparticles.78,79 The wire explosion process generates metal nanoparticles with a non-uniform size, but the heterogeneous distribution of the bimodal-type particles exhibits better sintering characteristics than the uniform-sized particles.80 As the applied voltage of the wire explosion process was increased, the size of the Cu/Ag combined nanoparticles decreased, resulting in low electrical resistivity on the sintered metallic hybrid film. The sintered Cu/Ag combined nanoink films exhibited the lowest resistivity of 4.06 µΩ cm upon applying a flash light energy of 10 J cm−2 for 10 ms, this being even lower than that of the sintered pure silver films (5.3 µΩ cm) (Fig. 11). This result was attributed to the removal of the pores on the sintered Cu/Ag hybrid nanoink films and the increase in the sizes of Cu-attached Ag particles, which have double plasmonic peaks that accelerate the photothermal effect during IPL irradiation.81,82 Compared with the pure Cu film at a high temperature of 100 °C, the resistivity of the pure Cu film abruptly increased owing to oxidation after 2 h, while the sintered Cu/Ag hybrid nanoink film did not exhibit significant changes in its resistivity even after more than 15 h. This type of hybrid ink system can be widely used to generate printed electronics for low-cost, reliable, oxidation-proof applications.

The metal nanoparticle-based inks, which are composed of commercially available copper nanoparticles treated with formic acid, silver nitrate, poly(N-vinylpyrrolidone), and diethylene glycol, can be used to easily fabricate hybrid copper–silver conductive electrode patterns with an oxidation resistance by IPL sintering.47 Formic acid plays an important role in the removal of the native oxide layers.83,84 The copper nanoparticles are converted into copper formate in the presence of formic acid, and then, the copper formate is transformed into metallic copper after IPL irradiation.75,85,86 In general, the resistance of the copper films increases by 6 orders of magnitude following thermal oxidation at a high temperature of 220 °C compared with that of pure copper films, while the addition of silver nitrate leads to small resistance changes owing to the formation of the copper–silver hybrid films that suppress the oxidation of the copper. The silver nitrate is first absorbed on the surfaces of the copper nanoparticles, resulting in the formation of a silver outer shell on the surfaces through a galvanic replacement reaction. The IPL-sintered copper film without the silver nitrate exhibited an initial sheet resistance of 0.23 ± 0.02 Ω sq−1, but its resistance rapidly increased to 106 Ω sq−1 after 60 min at 180 °C, as shown in Fig. 12. However, the sintered copper–silver hybrid film, where the molar ratio of Ag to Cu was 0.22, did not exhibit significant changes in terms of the sheet resistance. In particular, its sheet resistance increased to only ∼50 Ω sq−1 even when it was heated at a temperature of 220 °C for 90 min. This result indicates that the oxidation of copper at high temperatures was successfully suppressed owing to the presence of silver. The copper–silver hybrid inks could effectively realize an electrical circuit with high oxidation resistance and cost effectiveness.

3. Nanocarbons

Nanocarbons are a broad range of carbon materials with nanoscale dimension. The most common nanocarbons are carbon nanotubes, fullerene, and graphene, which are well-known. Other types of nanocarbons such as nanofiber, coil, horns, and onions can be created; however, this is not common. Nanocarbons have been intensively studied in a wide range of research fields owing to their outstanding properties, which strongly depend on their nanoscale structure. Nanocarbons consist of sp2-bonded carbons, in which the hybridization of π-electrons determines their opto-electrical, mechanical, and thermal properties. The exceptionally large surface area of nanocarbons is also a promising attribute that indicates that nanocarbons can be used as surface-active catalysts or electrodes. This section will review the influence of IPL irradiation on the structural development and chemical modification of nanocarbon materials.

3.1. Nanocarbon nanotubes (CNTs)

In 2002, Ajayan et al. reported an astonishing observation that single wall carbon nanotubes (SWNTs) can be ignited in air, and by exposure to a camera flash at a close distance (several centimeters away from the sample) (Fig. 13a and b).8 The average light power needed to ignite SWNTs was found to be 100 mW cm−2 with a pulse rise time and decay time of 50 µs and 1.2 ms, respectively, for a sample with a density of about 0.2 g cm−3. As the density of the sample increased, more power was needed to ignite the SWNTs, because the heat generated by the photothermal conversion rapidly dissipated into the bulk owing to the high thermal conductivity of the SWNTs. Considering CNTs oxidize at above 600 °C, SWNTs might reach such a temperature at the light power threshold necessary for ignition. Heat confinement in flashing SWNTs resulted in many conical tips reminiscent of nanohorns. Interestingly, when SWNTs were flashed, a loud photoacoustic sound was observed, which was attributed to the expansion and contraction of trapped gases.

Brockrath et al. reported that the photoacoustic effect can be observed with other carbon materials, such as activated carbons and graphites, that particularly bear noble metal catalysts. They suggested that this unusual feature can arise from the combination of a well-dispersed metal catalyst in intimate contact with a high-surface area carbon.87 Although the mechanism of this ignition and reconstruction has not been fully understood, Ajayan et al. explained that the catalytic effect might not be the main cause, considering that the structural reconstruction of SWNTs occurred in the absence of ignition throughout the bulk sample, not just near the relatively sparse catalytic particles.

Inspired by the previous report, Guo et al. demonstrated the possibility of splitting SWNT-confined water under a visible light flash.88 When the sample chamber containing SWNT-confined water at a pressure under 10−1 Pa was exposed to photographic flash light (0.1–0.2 J cm−2, 8 ms), the chamber pressure jumped by a factor of 100, implying that some gases had been generated.89 Through mass spectral analysis, they found that about 79% of the flash generated gases was hydrogen (H2); however, other components like CO > He > CH4 > CO2 > H2O > C2H6 were also observed. It was suggested that the water molecules dissociated into hydrogen and oxygen atoms owing to the strong photothermal effect of flashing SWNTs, and the local explosion generated near nanotubes induced the liberation of some carbon atoms. Additionally, charge emission was also occasionally observed during the irradiation, implying that the generation of plasma could accompany the gas release process.

3.2. Reduced graphene oxides

There has been a growing interest in graphene-based materials owing to their well-known outstanding properties. Reduced graphene oxide (rGO), which is prepared by deoxygenation (i.e., reduction) of chemically exfoliated graphene oxide (GO), is considered a promising candidate for graphene applications owing to its potential use in solution processing. Because GO is inherently insulating, deoxygenation of GO is required in order to make it electrically conductive. Many studies have suggested various GO reduction methods, including chemical reduction, thermal treatments, and photonic irradiation using various light sources, such as microwaves, lasers, and flash light.90 Recently, photonic reduction of GO by IPL particularly has attracted much attention as a chemical-free, environmentally friendly, ultra-fast, and roll-to-roll processable method.

First, the flash reduction of GO applied by Cote et al. has kindled the study of the influence of IPL on the deoxygenation and nanostructure of GO.91 They found that photographic camera flash light instantaneously triggered the deoxygenation reaction of GO by photothermal heating. After the intensity of the flash reduced, the brown and transparent GO film seemingly turned black and opaque, indicating conversion of GO to deoxygenated graphitic carbon (Fig. 14a). This process was accompanied by a loud pop sound similar to that heard when SWNTs are exposed to flash light. The appropriate flash energies applied to reduce the GO film were around 0.1–2 J cm−2. One of the important advantages of the photonic process is that it can allow photo-patterning (Fig. 14b). They obtained rGO patterns such as interdigitated electrode arrays on paper substrates by simple IPL exposure through the photomask. The electrode fingers were 100 µm wide. Such electrode arrays were successfully demonstrated as ammonia vapor sensors. For flash reduction of GO, pulse parameters should be optimized, as the ability of multiple pulses of lower energy to improve the uniformity and contrast of the pattern was superior to those of other kinds of pulses. When exposed to relatively higher power flashes, the exposed area was ablated because of rapid degassing and air expansion. These results showed the scalable production of functional reduced-GO based devices using IPL.

The photoinitiated exothermic decomposition of GO can be a promising ignition promoter for fuels.92 SWNTs were initially considered as additives to rocket fuels to attain distributed fuel ignition because of the instantaneous temperature increase they experience when exposed to flash light, as mentioned above. However, the attempts conclusively failed because CNT-based ignition required an external oxygen supply, and it was difficult to uniformly disperse CNT in liquid fuels.92 Gilje et al. focused on the fact that GO carries its own oxygen supply and is highly dispersible in fuels. As a proof of concept, it was showed that when the GO foam sample was placed on a paper soaked with ethanol and exposed to flash light, the ethanol vapor readily combusted as a result of the GO ignition (Fig. 14c).

The large surface area of reduced GO microstructures induced by photographic flash light was successfully demonstrated as an anode for lithium-ion batteries by Mukherjee et al.93 Different from the reduced graphene oxide treated by a chemical reducing agent and thermally heated, flash-reduced GO in the graphene anode had microscale pores, cracks, and inter-sheet voids, which originated from rapid outgassing by photothermal deoxygenation (Fig. 15). These porous structures enhanced the lithium intercalation kinetics at a fast charge/discharge rate, and conclusively flash-reduced GO exhibited 3–6 fold higher discharge capacities compared to the chemically reduced GO without micropores.

Systematic studies on the influence of the pulse conditions of IPL on the reduction of GO have been reported in the last three years. Eom et al. employed the IPL treatment to rapidly reduce microstructured GO. After the reduction process, the GO self-assembled with a coffee-ring structure. This self-assembled GO can play a role in all graphene-based transistors.94 Unlike the previous demonstration, they investigated the IPL driven reduction of GO films that were a few nanometers thick. In their study, the IPL irradiation energy was varied from 65 to 98 J cm−2 by varying the pulse duration time from 1.5 to 5.0 ms. As the IPL irradiation energy increased, the C–O bonds gradually decreased, while the CC bonds increased (Fig. 16a). This showed that IPL irradiation effectively removed epoxy groups present on graphene flakes' basal planes and the degree of GO reduction could be controlled by IPWL irradiation energy. IPL-reduced GO sheets had folded and torn regions that were due to the rapid thermal expansion and liberation of oxygen-containing functional groups during the IPL reduction process (Fig. 16b).

The optimum conditions for GO reduction can be related to the thickness of the GO films and the type of substrates that support the GO films.95 Kim and Park studied the influence of flash light conditions on the photothermal reduction of free-standing 500 nm GO films prepared by the vacuum filtration method. They found that 40 J cm−2 light energy with 40 ms pulse duration time was the most appropriate to fully reduce the GO film. To study the effect of pulse number, they found that single pulse treatment was preferable, as the sheet resistance of the rGO film produced by single-pulse treatment was lower than that produced by multiple-pulse treatment. For GO thin films with a thickness below 50 nm deposited on a glass substrate, Al-Hamry et al. reported that light energy much higher than 200 J cm−2 was required to make the GO film conductive.96 When the glass substrate, which has a large thermal conductivity, was altered into a polymer substrate with a small thermal conductivity, applying a lower irradiation energy of about 0.5 J cm−2 was enough to reduce the GO thin films. By comparing the electrical and optical properties of the rGO films produced by IPL treatment and those produced by thermal annealing, it was concluded that exposing GO thin films to an IPL treatment for several seconds was as efficient as thermally annealing them at 400 °C for 30 min. When the GO thin films with different film thicknesses were exposed to the same IPL irradiation condition, the sheet resistance tended to decrease with increasing thickness, while the transmission of the thicker films remained relatively constant at around 50% (Fig. 17a). This converged transmittance was attributed to the formation of a porous structure in thicker films, which was the result of partial film ablation by IPL exposure (Fig. 17b).

4. Inorganic compounds and metallic alloys

Historically, the first flash lamp annealed sample was a piece of an implanted silicon wafer that had accidentally fallen into the laser mirror.97 In the semiconductor annealing community, IPL is a fascinating tool compared with laser annealing, as only a small percentage of light energy from the pump lamps is converted into laser pulse energy, while IPL can deliver much higher energy than the laser itself. Furthermore, unlike laser technology, one shot of IPL irradiation can be used to anneal a large area. Recently, the application of IPL has expanded from just the annealing of silicon to the annealing of various metallic and inorganic compounds in order to enhance their material properties. This section will review the IPL-driven morphological and structural development of inorganic compounds and metallic alloys.

4.1. Silicon nanowires and metal oxides

As an advanced material in silicon nanotechnologies, silicon nanowires have attracted considerable attention in recent years for their potential applications in mesoscopic research and electronic nanodevices. N. Wang et al. reported that when Si nanowires were exposed to photographic flash light with a light power of 0.1–0.2 J cm−2 and a pulse duration of 5 ms, they ignited and fiercely burned in air (Fig. 18).98 Si nanowires absorbed and confined the energy from the flash light, sufficiently increasing their temperature to a value higher than 1200 °C, which led to instantaneously fierce oxidation. Nanostructural reformation of flashing Si nanowires was affected by the environmental conditions. There was no ignition when the flashing experiment was performed in inert gases or a vacuum. When Si nanowires were flashed in inert gases, the Si nanoparticles became embedded in the silicon oxide nanowires, where the Si cores suddenly melted and segregated into crystalline nanoparticles. When flashing was done in a vacuum (∼10−3 Torr), long silica nanotubes were observed. This was attributed to the evaporation of molten Si cores or Si monoxide.

(d) Silicon oxide nanotubes formed by flashing a Si nanowire in a vacuum. Reproduced with permission, ref. 98, copyright 2003 American Chemical Society.

Transparent conducting or semiconducting oxides are vital materials for optoelectronic devices, such as light-emitting diodes and photovoltaic devices. With increasing demand for flexibility and deformability of such devices, development of transparent conducting oxides (TCOs) and transparent semiconducting oxides (TSOs), which, besides their transparency, also allow for low temperature processing and minimized processing costs, has been required. Interestingly, IPL has been applied as a low temperature sintering method for TCOs or TSOs, even though the visible light emitted from IPL mostly passes through these materials. Gebel et al. utilized IPL to prepare highly conductive Al doped ZnO (AZO).99 880 nm thick AZO film deposited by magnetron sputtering exhibited a resistance of 2.0 × 10−3 Ω cm. After exposure to a single flash with a energy density of 6.5–7.5 J cm−2, the resistance of AZO decreased down to 1.0 × 10−3 Ω cm. This improvement was due to the increase in the number of Al atoms replacing Zn in the ZnO lattice in the IPL irradiation process, which results in an increase in carrier concentration. More recently, highly conductive and transparent ZnO films were demonstrated by introducing the IPL technique.100 A highly resistive ZnO film prepared by chemical bath deposition turned into a conductive film through exposure to 50 pulses of IPL, in which the sheet resistance of the film decreased from 106 to 40 Ω □−1. The possible mechanism for the conductivity enhancement of ZnO by IPL was not clearly understood; however, a photodoping mechanism was considered as a main effect in that paper. Under UV light, photo-oxidized oxygen, water, and organic molecules can absorb on the ZnO surface, and photogenerated holes can be transferred to these absorbates. In this process, excess of electrons are generated in the conduction band, which increase the conductivity of ZnO.101,102

As a representative metal oxide photoelectrode for dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), TiO2 is prepared via high-temperature sintering at 500 °C after deposition of the TiO2 paste on fluorine-doped tin oxide glass. However, the plastic substrate cannot withstand a sintering process at a temperature above 150 °C. Jin et al. successfully demonstrated that IPL irradiation can be used to suitably prepare TiO2 photoelectrodes for flexible DSSCs.103 The application of 5 s of IPWL irradiation with a fluence of 21 J cm−2 to TiO2 nanopaste significantly improved the photocurrent density, which was attributed to an improvement in power conversion efficiencies by more than double, compared with the DSSCs without IPWL treatment (Fig. 19a and b). Although morphological changes following sintering of TiO2 nanoparticles were not shown, strong interconnections between TiO2 nanoparticles were supported by the improved charge collection efficiencies and reduced electron transport resistance obtained by photon-to-current conversion efficiency measurements and electrochemical impedance analysis, respectively.

Metal oxide semiconductors are of importance for next-generation transparent displays owing to their high charge carrier mobilities and high transparency in the visible light range. In particular, metal oxides prepared via solution based techniques have attracted special attention, as they guarantee the industrial demands for low cost and large scale production. Typically, the synthesis of metal oxide by using a sol–gel reaction requires high annealing temperatures over 450 °C to decompose the organic ligand groups and form the metal oxide structure. As a rapid annealing process, sub-second IPWL irradiation was successfully used to anneal solution-processed metal oxide semiconductors, including amorphous InGaZnO (a-IGZO), InZnO (IZO), and HfInZnO (HIZO).104 Upon irradiation of the precursor with IPL, the FT-IR peaks corresponding to organic ligands such as nitrate, carbonyl, and hydroxyl functional groups nearly disappeared or reduced in intensity, in accordance with the FT-IR spectrum of the IGZO film fabricated by the thermal annealing process (Fig. 19c). This indicates that the sub-second IPWL irradiation provides sufficient energy to decompose and eliminate the organic ligands of metal precursors and dehydroxylate the M–OH to a metal oxide. The electron carrier transport behavior in the IGZO film was significantly affected by the pulse fluence of IPL (Fig. 19d). Fluences higher than 83 J cm−2 were required to obtain good charge carrier transport properties in IPL treated IGZO films that corresponded to those of thermally annealed thin films used in conventional devices. To fully understand the mechanism that explains annealing of transparent metal oxides by IPL irradiation, further investigation is required.

4.2. Metal chalcogenides and synthetic perovskite

Instantaneous heating of materials by IPL can be particularly useful in the preparation of alloy materials containing chalcogenide elements, such as sulfur or selenium. Typically, chalcogenide elements can be relatively easily evaporated in thermal processes of their compounds, and additional selenization at high temperature has been utilized to ensure the stoichiometry of the chalcogenide alloy. However, sulfur or selenium vapor is toxic and seriously hazardous to humans. To solve this problem, S. Dhage et al. demonstrated that copper indium gallium diselenide (CIGS) alloy films can be prepared from Cu (In0.7Ga0.3) (CIG) and Se nanoparticles by intense pulsed light.105 CIGS shows promise as a solar light absorber for highly efficient photovoltaic devices. Exposure of a CIG and Se nanoparticle mixture to 2 ms of IPL with an appropriate light energy of 20 J cm−2 resulted in the formation of Cu (In0.7Ga0.3)Se2 with a tetragonal chalcopyrite structure. Although the mechanism of CIGS formation by IPL was not clear, IPL irradiation induced an instant temperature increase in the nanoparticle mixture that was sufficient to allow the melting of CIG (m.p 515) and Se (m.p 217) nanoparticles. Photonic curing of CuInSe2 nanocrystals, which are analogous to CIGS semiconductors, has been also demonstrated.106 Spray-deposited CuInSe2 nanocrystals were exposed to mild IPL conditions in a closed chamber with a quartz window using a single 160 µs light pulse with a flux of 2.2 J cm−2 (Fig. 20b and e). Nanocrystal films processed in this way were found to remove organic ligands and bring nanocrystals into better electrical contact without destroying their nanoscale dimensions. Exposure to IPL with a somewhat higher energy of 3 J cm−2 resulted in significantly larger grains and pores inside the films (Fig. 20c and f).

Consequently, the photovoltaic devices based on CuInSe2 nanocrystals treated by 2.2 J cm−2 IPL exhibited peak EQE exceeding 100%, indicating the possible occurrence of multiexciton generation and extraction from the device. In the case of the device made from 3 J cm−2 IPL sintering, poor performance was observed.

Synthetic perovskites have been identified as possible inexpensive base materials for next generation high-efficiency commercial photovoltaics. The perovskite structure is represented by the formula ABX3, where A refers to an organic group and B represents a metal, such as lead; X is a halide group. The challenges encountered when perovskites are used in solar cells involve producing larger perovskite crystals and improving the surface coverage of the film. To address this issue, a perovskite layer was thermally annealed. However, the optimization of heat treatment was fastidious owing to the instability of the perovskite structure. For example, in the case of the methylammonium lead iodide (CH3NH3PbI3), which has a perovskite structure, the annealing temperature was limited to below 150 °C because decomposition of the film and evaporation of the methylammonium iodide component occurred above 150 °C. Moreover, annealing at a high temperature reduced surface coverage, resulting in the formation of pores owing to agglomerization of perovskite islands. As short-duration IPL exposure can instantly increase the temperature of compounds with perovskite structures, IPL processing may be ideal for the manufacture of perovskite solar cells. Very recently, Lavery et al. successfully demonstrated the formation of a CH3NH3PbI3 perovskite structure using the IPL technique.107 Strong exposure of perovskite films to 2000 J per pulse IPL resulted in the transition of cubical particles into a densely sintered layer (Fig. 21). They suggested, based on a temperature profile simulation using finite element analysis, that films exposed to IPL irradiation with 2000 J were able to reach a temperature higher than 750 °C within 2 ms, and the temperature remains above 150 °C for almost 20 ms. The IPL processed perovskite solar cell exhibited improved power conversion efficiencies of over 11% compared to the perovskite solar cell that was annealed using conventional hot plate heating (8.2%), which was due to the improved perovskite morphology and pore filling.

5. Conjugated polymers

Polymer processing does not need the high temperatures that inorganic or metallic materials require, but interesting features have been reported in IPL processing of polymer materials. As conjugated polymers can absorb visible light if they contain chromophores, the occurrence of a photothermal conversion effect under IPL irradiation is expected.108 In fact, Huang et al. observed it for the first time when polyaniline nanofibres showing a deeply colored conjugated polymer were exposed to a camera flash; the nanofibres melted and became chemically crosslinked (Fig. 22a and b).109 This nanofibre welding appeared in either their doped or undoped form. Flash welding led to a marked change in optical reflectivity and solubility. Selective-area welding of nanofibre films was simply achieved by passing flash light through a photomask. Regarding the possible mechanism, it was suggested that the heat originating from photo-thermal conversion could accumulate within the polyaniline domain, leading to the welding of nanofibres, because polyaniline has very low luminescence efficiency and low thermal conductivity (∼10−1 W m−1 K−1). Additionally, they suggested that flash welding based on a conjugated polymer nanostructure can be utilized to prepare polymer–polymer blends by showing that the heat generated by photothermal conversion in polyaniline nanofibres can be transferred to other polymers, creating a fused blend structure (Fig. 22c).

IPL irradiation can efficiently eliminate thermally labile ester substituents from conjugated polymers. Helegen et al. exposed a conjugated copolymer based on dithienylthiazolo[5,4-d]thiazole (DZT) and silolodithiophene (SDT) with thermocleavable ester groups that permit solubility in organic solvents to IPL treatment.110 In order to eliminate the ester groups, a temperature increase of up to at least 200 °C was required. When this thermocleavable copolymer was exposed to IPL with a power density of approximately 6 kW cm−2 by means of 1–3 pulses, a distinct color change from orange to pale violet was observed and the resulting film did not dissolve in the organic solvent (Fig. 23). This indicated that thermally labile ester groups in the conjugated copolymer had been eliminated. IPL induced thermocleaving of conjugated polymers was successfully implemented in full roll-to-roll fabrication of large-area polymer solar cell modules.

An important feature of conjugated polymers is that their optical and electrical properties are closely related to the degree of crystallization of the polymer chains. Among the many potential applications of conjugated polymers, their use in solar cells has attracted special attention, as polymer solar cells are promising renewable energy sources with high practicality owing to their light weight, low fabrication costs, and high mechanical flexibility. In particular, it is known that the power conversion efficiencies of polymer solar cells are dictated by the morphologies and nanostructures of bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) photoactive layers consisting of a blend of conjugated polymer electron donor (D) and a fullerene electron acceptor (A). Typically, thermal annealing or solvent vapor annealing has been widely used to enhance the crystallization of polymer donors and control the nanostructure of BHJ photoactive layers. However, such treatment methods are time-consuming and problematic when used with flexible plastic substrates and in the R2R production of devices. Yang et al. demonstrated that IPL treatment can be applied for split-second nanostructure control of a BHJ photoactive layer.111 Two seconds of IPL irradiation (optimum condition, 35 J cm−2) on a blend of poly(3-hexylthiophene) and [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) (P3HT:PCBM) significantly enhanced power conversion efficiencies of the devices to values approaching those of typical devices treated with thermal annealing. It was shown that rod-like P3HT nanocrystals were developed, and the phase-separation of P3HT and PCBM was enhanced in IPL-treated P3HT:PCBM films (Fig. 24). Several reports on the relaxation dynamics of polymer chains support the hypothesis that polymer chains can crystallize within milliseconds; therefore this can explain why the polymer chain crystallized within such a short time.8,112 The pulse conditions of IPL need to be optimized, and the photobleaching phenomena were observed when the P3HT:PCBM film was exposed to high energy IPL.

6. Summary and outlook

As follows from the above overview, instant IPL irradiation on advanced materials with various chemical origins, including inorganic, metallic, and organic, and various structures, including nanoparticles, nanowires, and thin films, resulted in dramatic transformations in the morphology and chemical functional groups of the materials.

Compared with conventional thermal heating and established photonic technologies such as a laser exposure, IPL technologies have considerable advantages, such as facile equipment set-up, surface-selective treatment, large treatment area, short process times ranging from milliseconds to a few seconds, and roll-to-roll process compatibility. For metallic nanoparticles and nanowires, IPL treatment instantly sinters them under ambient conditions without damage to the low-temperature processable plastic substrate, which provides highly conductive electrode patterns. Low-temperature sintering of metallic nanomaterials is one of the important issues faced in the development of next-generation flexible electronics, and IPL can be a major breakthrough. When nanocarbons are exposed to IPL, a strong photothermal conversion effect is induced, which leads to significant structural deformations characterized by the dissociation and/or formation of chemical bonds. Graphene oxide that is photothermally reduced by IPL irradiation has unique microstructures with microscale pores and voids, which can provide high-performance electrochemical electrodes with a high surface-area for interacting with active ions. Ultra-fast temperature increase by IPL can be used to handle materials whose thermal treatment should be approached with care, such as metal chalcogenides and perovskites. IPL treatment of conjugated polymers can generate various physical and chemical transitions, such as welding, dissociation of chemical functional groups, and crystallization. Currently, IPL-driven nanotechnology is in the early stages of research and development. In order to apply IPL technologies to the industrial manufacturing process of nanomaterial-based products, several issues need to be addressed. First, reproducibility of results should be achieved. This can be related to the generation of highly reproducible pulsed light using a xenon flash lamp. In order to overcome this drawback, use of simmer circuits to control the flash lamp has been suggested, and additional efforts have been made to remove the residual current tail at the end of the light pulse duration. Second, fundamental studies that explain how exposure to milliseconds of IPL irradiation induces strong transition in nanomaterials are required. To date, it has been believed that photothermal conversion is a main cause of heat generation during IPL irradiation. However, temperature increase in IPL treated nanomaterials does not fully match this relation. Unexpectedly large temperature increase has often been observed when nanomaterials are exposed to IPL, and the energy confinement effect in nanomaterials can occur and contribute to heat conservation. To fully understand the photothermal effect of nanomaterials, fundamental studies including the investigation of thermal/optical parameters and photothermal conversion efficiencies of the nanomaterials, and heat transfer mechanisms in nanomaterials are needed. As metal oxides that are transparent in visible light can be sintered under IPL irradiation, a hitherto unknown phenomenon, besides photothermal conversion, may be possible. There is also a need to verify whether or not plasma is generated during IPL irradiation. A deeper understanding of the physical and chemical transition mechanism in nanomaterials following IPL irradiation has so far proved elusive for materials scientists.
