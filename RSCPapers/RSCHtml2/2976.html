Abstract

The interaction between ionic moieties and the electronic properties of organic and hybrid semiconductors can yield a variety of interesting and sometimes surprising effects. Ionic moieties can induce interface dipoles, support electrochemical doping and lead to band-bending. The dynamic motion of ions under electric fields and the induced changes in conductivity can make it difficult to de-convolute the various mechanisms at play. This review attempts to provide a perspective on different types of interactions between ions and semiconductors and goes into depth addressing the application of solution-processed polyelectrolyte and oligoelectrolyte materials to control the energy band structures and behaviour of organic and hybrid semiconducting optoelectronic devices. This review introduces and highlights different types of ionic materials (such as conjugated polyelectrolytes and molecular ionic dopants) that have been used to achieve different effects (such as creating interfacial dipoles, vacuum energy shifts, changes in Fermi energies and doping) in organic and hybrid semiconducting devices before going into greater depth with the emerging class of non-conjugated polyelectrolytes and oligoelectrolytes and provides a comprehensive summary of recent progress using these materials in semiconducting devices. This class of materials has been receiving increasing attention recently and provides insights into the fundamental effects of ionic functionalities on the energy band structures and functions in organic and hybrid semiconductors.

I. Introduction

Over the past several decades, efforts to understand electronic phenomena in π-conjugated organic materials and devices have prompted the evolution of an entirely new branch of semiconductor physics. Compared to silicon and inorganic semiconductors, organic semiconductors offer several advantages like solution processability, flexibility and low cost. These desirable attributes have spurred an intense, worldwide research effort, which has overcome many of the inherent challenges of these materials such as low charge mobilities, high exciton binding energies, amorphous structures and completely different processing requirements. After pioneering research with early organic semiconductors showed that thin-films and crystals of organic materials could yield measurable photo-voltages,1 electroluminescence2 and charge carrier mobilities,3 their potential to be used in semiconducting devices became apparent. Tang and VanSlyke4,5 demonstrated their application in the first practical organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) and the field of organic solar cells (OSCs) began to take off soon after. Since these landmark discoveries were made, academic and industrial interest in this field has grown rapidly.

The recently emerging class of lead-halide perovskites (LHPs) utilize many of the same device architectures that were initially developed for organic and dye-sensitized solar cells (SCs) and follow the same physics. Although LHPs have been known for more than 100 years,6 one of the reasons that they were not widely applied in optoelectronic devices until more recently7,8 may be that effective design principles and processing strategies used for OSCs and OLEDs did not exist. The emphasis of this review is on applications of polyelectrolytes in organic semiconductors, but it should be noted that many of the concepts and advances covered in this review may be applied to perovskite devices as well.

Almost every type of classic, inorganic semiconducting device, from diodes to SCs to transistors, relies on the formation of at least one P–N junction. P–N junctions can be formed by doping with donor and acceptor impurities in the case of inorganic crystalline materials (Fig. 1a). For example, when a group III element such as boron is added to a silicon crystal, it may remove an electron from the silicon lattice to form a negatively charged boron centre, which fits into the silicon lattice by forming four B–Si bonds; from a chemical perspective, this species can be considered a type of borate anion. The electron which was removed from the silicon lattice leaves an electronically delocalized hole (h+). This process moves the Fermi energy (EF) of the crystal closer to the valence band energy (VB). Likewise, adding a group V element such as phosphorus results in a tetravalent phosphonium ion while the excess valence electron of phosphorus is delocalized as an n-type charge carrier in the silicon lattice with a concomitant increase in EF.

Doping of organic materials has proven to require fundamentally different approaches than in that of silicon. In the early studies of conjugated polymers (CPs), it was found that their conductivities could be increased dramatically upon treatment with halogens (Cl2, Br2, I2) or alkali metals (Li, Na, K) – due to doping.9Fig. 1b schematically illustrates how treatment with elements Cl2 or K can cause doping in organic materials. When an organic semiconductor is treated with a halogen like Cl2, it may remove electrons from (oxidize) the highest occupied molecular orbitals (HOMOs) of the organic semiconductor to form radical cations and halide anions. The resulting holes (radical cations) are more localized on a single molecule (or repeat unit in the case of CPs) compared to silicon. Likewise, strongly reducing metal elements are able to transfer electrons to (reduce) the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) to form radical anions. These radical ions, which are coupled to spatial distortions in the geometry of the polymers or molecules (phonons), are referred to as polarons. Polarons can hop from one molecule (or repeat unit) to another and this hopping forms the basis for electronic conduction in organic semiconductors. Compared to crystalline inorganic semiconductors like silicon, where charge carriers are highly delocalized, radical ions in organic semiconductors are relatively more localized, however, often exhibit some degree of delocalization over several atoms or repeat units. For instance, if we consider the benzyl radical, the unpaired electron is stabilized by several resonance structures at ortho and para positions in the benzene ring. Likewise, in CPs, it is possible for radical ions to be delocalized between neighbouring repeat units as double bonds can be re-arranged to allow energetically equivalent resonance structures where the radical ion exists on either of two adjacent repeat units. Delocalization occurs in molecular semiconductors as well, where radical ion states may be spread out across several individual molecules that are stacked with overlapping π-orbitals. The extent of delocalization varies considerably between materials, however, delocalization correlates strongly with charge carrier mobility and tends to increase with crystallinity and decrease with reorganization (phonon) energy.10,11 For example, the well-known polymer P3HT and small molecule pentacene have been reported to have polarons delocalized over approximately 5 or 10 repeat units, respectively.12

It is important to note that in the case of silicon, the dopant atoms result in immobile borate or phosphonium ions which are covalently bound in the silicon crystal lattice, while in the case of halide or alkali metal doped organic semiconductors, the resulting anions or cations are not covalently bound and are prone to move under an electric field or by random diffusion, which makes it challenging to form stable P–N junctions. Stationary ions play a critical role in supporting stable, doped interfaces and this fundamental mechanism is one of the aspects which make polyelectrolytes useful.

Because doping with halogens or alkali metals is difficult to control and leads to unstable junctions, other approaches must be used to create P–N junctions in organic semiconductors. Two effective approaches to overcome this problem are developing strategies to achieve immobile dopant ions and employing heterojunctions between two semiconducting materials, which can function as P–N junctions without the need for doping. Heterojunctions can create energy barriers to electron and hole movement while promoting the conduction of the opposite type of carrier. A material with a deep HOMO energy band and low energy barrier for electron injection (ϕe) functions as an electron transport layer (ETL) or a hole blocking layer (HBL, Fig. 2a) while a material with a high-lying LUMO energy band and low energy barrier for hole injection (ϕh) functions as a hole transport layer (HTL) or electron blocking layer (EBL, Fig. 2b). While heterojunctions are very effective at controlling the motion of charge carriers, they often result in undesirable energy barriers or losses in electrical potential that can reduce the efficiency of devices. As seen in Fig. 2a, a drop in LUMO energy (ϕe) of an ETL creates a thermodynamic driving potential for electrons to move downward in energy toward the electrode with a work function (WF) value of Φ, however, in the case of SCs this may lower the maximum open circuit voltage (VOC) which may be achieved in a SC, and may create an energy barrier (ϕe) for electron injection in the case of light-emitting diodes (LEDs) and increase the operating voltage. Likewise, HTL heterojunctions (Fig. 2b) may provide a thermodynamic impetus for holes to move upward in energy toward the electrode, but also may reduce the VOC in SCs or increase the operating voltage in LEDs.

Doped interfaces have a strong advantage in this regard. Compared to a neutral semiconductor (Fig. 2c), n-doping (Fig. 2d) creates a band-bending effect where the energy bands slope downwards towards the doped interface, creating a potential energy well (VBB) which attracts electrons and repels holes. P-doping (Fig. 2e) creates an opposite band-bending effect, where VBB has the opposite sign and causes energy bands to slope upwards towards the interface, repelling electrons and attracting holes. These doped homojunctions do not introduce energy barriers (ϕe or ϕh) or offsets which cause potential energy losses that exist in heterojunctions. Band-bending effects in semiconductor devices have been studied extensively; a more detailed discussion of band bending effects can be found for example in the review by Zheng and Yates.13

The relatively low loss in potential is apparent when one compares the VOCs produced by doped homojunction solar cells like Si or GaAs, which are close to the band gap of the semiconductor, to the VOCs produced by heterojunction solar cells with energy barriers, which tend to be relatively lower compared to the band gaps of the light-absorbing layers. II. Theory and mechanisms of ionic interaction

Polyelectrolytes (and ionic materials) can function as effective ETLs and HTLs by two main mechanisms: changing the WF of an electrode24,25 and by compensating the charges of mobile carriers to dope, or promote electrochemical doping26,27 at an interface as illustrated in Fig. 2d and e. Polyelectrolytes are typically wide-bandgap, electrical insulators. It may seem counter-intuitive that an electrical insulator would improve current through a junction, however, it is important to note that polyelectrolytes are most effective when deposited as ultra-thin interfacial layers (IFLs), with thicknesses on the order of nanometers. Despite their wide band gaps, electrical conduction through the IFL is not blocked, while a thin layer of charged moieties is still able to influence the band structure of the adjacent semiconductor. An excellent example of thin layers of ionic insulators being used to improve the current through semiconductor junctions is the case of LiF,28 which is in practice one of the most commonly used materials to improve n-type contacts in solar cells and LED devices, despite being a wide band-gap insulator. Polyelectrolytes do not necessarily dope the interface when they are initially deposited, however, when an electric potential is applied to the interface, the presence of ions may support electrochemical doping29 in a manner that has been well documented in light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs).27

Perhaps the most useful technique to understand the effect of ionic materials on the electronic band structure is via ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS), which uses ultraviolet photons of a fixed energy (for example, He+ emission at 21.2 eV) to eject electrons from valence bands or HOMO levels present at the surface of a solid. The kinetic energy of electrons is related to the energy of incident photons by Einstein's photoelectric eqn (1)30 where Kmax is the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons, hf is the energy of the incident photon and Φ is the WF of the material:

Fig. 3a shows an example of the secondary edge region of a set of UPS spectra for a conjugated polyelectrolyte (CPE) on a gold substrate.32 The WF of the Au substrate, or minimum quantity of energy which is required to remove an electron from the surface, can be found by subtracting the onset of the secondary edge (∼16.6 eV in this example) from the incident photon energy (21.2 eV). This indicates a WF of 4.6 eV for the substrate. The WF changes as a CPE layer with increasing thickness is deposited on top. By convention, the EF is assumed to be constant through the Au/CPE stack, and the WF of the CPE layer matches that of the substrate. Thus, the shift in the secondary edge can be attributed to a change in the vacuum energy, rather than WF. This effect is referred to as a surface dipole19,31 or interfacial dipole (Δ).33 Surface dipoles can be generated when there is an anisotropic arrangement of polar molecules or ionic groups at the surface of a semiconductor or metal. For example, DeLongchamp and co-workers investigated the orientation of molecules when a thin CPE layer was deposited on an organic semiconductor and found that the ionic component of the CPEs migrated towards the top surface during spin-coating of the CPE while the hydrophobic backbone preferentially interacted with the organic semiconductor. This anisotropic arrangement of ionic functionalities led to a surface dipole, and the relative concentration of cations or anions at the top surface, and therefore the magnitude of this dipole, was dependent on which type of counter-ion was used.34

In the example shown in Fig. 3, the vacuum energy increasingly shifts downwards (VBB(Vac)), and Δ increases in magnitude as the CPE film thickness increases. Often, UPS and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) experiments are carried out at the same facilities using the same instruments with different light sources. Changes in vacuum energy (Δ and VBB(Vac)) are typically observed as shifts in both the secondary edge of UPS spectra as well as shifts in the binding energies of core electrons in XPS spectra, which can provide confirmation of vacuum level shifts and Δ.

The HOMO energy of the material can be quantified from the Fermi edge (Fig. 3b) by comparing the position of its onset relative to the onset of an Au or reference substrate. In this example, the onset occurs at increasingly higher binding energies compared to the Au reference as the film becomes thicker and indicates that the HOMO energy band bends downward away from the Au substrate (VBB(HOMO)). The LUMO energy band is typically calculated by adding the optical bandgap to the HOMO energy, and thus changes in the LUMO band follow changes in the HOMO band. The HOMO and LUMO energies relative to the vacuum energy are equivalent to the ionization potential (IP) and electron affinity (EA) of the solid CPE film. These different quantities are summarized as a band diagram as shown in Fig. 3c.

Organic materials deposited on metal interfaces are generally observed to reduce the WF of the metal,35 although it is possible to increase the WF depending on the orientation of the dipole moment of the IFL.36 Thus, there can be a competition between effects on the WF of a substrate for different ionic materials and IFLs.

III. Related ionic and interfacial materials

The focus of this review is on the recent application of non-conjugated polyelectrolytes (NPEs), oligoelectrolytes and small molecule electrolytes (SMEs) in optoelectronic devices, however, there are several classes of materials which perform related functions, including vacuum deposited dopants, electrochemically doped interfaces, polyethylenimine, ionic fullerene derivatives and CPEs. These topics have already been the subject of other review papers and are not covered in great detail in this review, however, in order to provide the reader with sufficient context to understand how polyelectrolytes relate to other ionic and interfacial materials, they are briefly summarized in the following sections.

One ionic material that deserves mention, which has been used ubiquitously in organic semiconducting devices, yet does not fit into other classifications covered in this review is LiF. Like polyelectrolytes, LiF is a wide-bandgap ionic solid which functions as an extremely effective ETL when it is deposited as an ultra-thin (∼1 nm) film, creating a Δ to effectively reduce the WF of electrodes.28 LiF has one of the largest band gaps (12.6 eV) of any solid material. Although it may seem counter-intuitive for an insulating layer to improve conductivity through a junction, LiF is one of the most widely used interlayers described in OLED and OSC literature and is highly effective in improving electron injection and extraction.

Vacuum deposited dopants

Doping with either alkali metals or halogens may lead to unstable homojunctions due to the reactivity of dopants and diffusion of ions. However, stable homojunctions can be prepared in a manner similar to inorganic materials by vapor-phase co-deposition of semiconductors and dopants, where the dopant molecules are fixed in the solid host semiconductor. In this approach, the distribution of dopants throughout the device can be controlled by varying the ratio of semiconductor to dopant during vapour deposition, and the relatively large size of the dopant molecules can prevent them from migrating through the device as smaller ions are prone to do. The resulting structure is similar to doped silicon, in the sense that charged dopant molecules are trapped in the organic semiconductor matrix and support mobile charge carriers with opposite charges.

Several groups have successfully developed doping mechanisms for both p-type and n-type doped semiconductors using vapour deposition. Effective dopants perform similar functions as alkali metals (n-doping) or halogens (p-doping) by either adding electrons to the LUMO level, or removing electrons from the HOMO level of the host semiconductor, resulting in positively charged dopants and mobile electrons in the former case, or negatively charged dopants and mobile holes in the latter case. The pioneering research of K. Leo37–39 and co-workers, for example, has demonstrated controlled doping of thin organic semiconductor films by co-sublimation of a matrix and dopant; subsequently there have been numerous efforts to control and understand doping in vacuum-deposited organic thin films for use in optoelectronic devices.40 The effect has been demonstrated for a variety of materials, for example, p doping of an amorphous aryl amines has been accomplished using very strong acceptors.41 Similarly controlled n-doping of organic semiconductors were also reported.42–45

Many organic semiconductors employed as thin films in OLEDs have been processed by vapour deposition techniques which yield smooth, homogenous films, with multi-component compositions including dopants.46–49 In some examples, vacuum deposition has been used in conjunction with solution-processed films. For example, poly(3-hexylthiophene-2,5-diyl) (P3HT) thin films have been doped by a small molecule 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ) using vapour phase infiltration which allowed the doped polymer to inherit higher electrical conductivity and applicability in thermoelectric power devices.38,50 Excellent reviews exist which cover the topic of dopants in greater detail.51,52

Electrochemical doping and LECs

Early in the development of CPs, it was realized that they could be doped by electrochemical oxidation or reduction in an electrolyte solution.53 Interestingly, when a combination of CP, ion-conducting polymer and electrolyte is deposited together as a thin film, the film may behave as a miniature electrochemical cell. Upon application of an electric field, the anode and cathode interfaces become electrochemically doped, effectively creating a homojunction and allowing efficient charge injection at the electrodes. If a fluorescent CP is used, charge recombination in the middle of the homojunction can result in light emission. This type of device has been termed the LEC. Because the ions are mobile, dynamic and reversible P–N junctions are formed in LECs depending on the applied external bias, and revert to an un-doped state when the bias is removed. One interesting aspect is that the electrode WFs do not strongly influence the LEC behaviour, since doping occurs at the electrodes and results in ohmic contacts regardless of the type of electrode. In one example,53 ionic liquids were used to create p-doping and n-doping effects in a luminescent polymer, resulting in up to 1.4% external quantum efficiency (EQE) at 10 V potential. Many of the details and mechanisms of the LEC device are still under investigation, however, the electrochemical doping and injection characteristics are supported by both electrochemical54–56 and electrodynamic57 models. Several review articles have been published on LECs very recently.58–60

Conjugated polyelectrolytes

Early CPs like polyacetylene, polythiophene and poly(p-phenylenevinylene) (PPV) possessed very poor solubility characteristics, which prompted the development of CPs with solubilizing groups. This led to some of the first CPs with practical applications of CPs in LED and SC devices. Efforts to produce water soluble CPs led to the development of the first ionic CPs–CPEs. The first CPEs based on polythiophene were reported by Wudl and coworkers in 1987; these were the first CPs with good solubility in water.61 These were prepared by polymerizing methyl 3-thiophene-β-ethanesulfonate or methyl 3-thiophene-δ-butanesulfonate followed by a conversion into their sodium salts. Since then, a wide range of research related to the application of CPE materials in optoelectronic devices has been carried out. For example, in 1996, Onitsuka et al.62 reported a thin-film LED based on a multi-bilayer combination of a PPV based CPE with different poly-anions which was fabricated using a layer-by-layer (LBL) processing technique. This material was found to dramatically improve the device luminescence and efficiency.

The solubility of CPEs in relatively polar solvents opened up the possibility to solution-process multilayer structures of CPEs by exploiting their “orthogonal solubility”. Attempting to solution process a second CP film on top of an underlying film generally tends to rinse away the underlying film if the polymers have similar solubility; however, by solution coating films from polymer solutions in alternately non-polar/polar solvents, multilayer structures are possible. Based on this finding, Heeger, Bazan and co-workers63 explored multi-layered solution processable polymer light-emitting diodes (PLED) and made the ground-breaking discovery that cationic CPEs functioned remarkably well as ETLs. Using CPEs resulted in low turn on voltages, better brightness and improved efficiencies in LEDs. Electrolytes containing conjugated backbones also found use in organic photovoltaic devices as reported by Baur et al.64 In 2005, Mwaura et al.65 showed that the performance of SCs is linearly related to the number of bilayers of an anionic CPE employed using the LBL method. Y. Cao reported66 an efficient approach to achieve large-area cathode interlayers using electrostatic layer-by-layer (eLBL) self-assembly of CPEs. In another example,67 a combination of side-chain functional groups on a conjugated polyfluorene backpone was studied as a cathode interlayer in polymer solar cells (PSCs) having PTB7:PC71BM as active layers, which showed great performance. Although CPEs are usually thought to function via an interface dipole effect and reduction in electrode WF, a recent report has confirmed that the cationic moieties in CPEs are able to support the self-doping of the CPEs if an electron withdrawing group is present.68 Several review articles have already been published regarding the topic of CPEs.69–71

Ionic fullerene derivatives

Since the invention of fullerene materials, amine-functionalized fullerenes72,73 have been widely studied in many fields especially in biomedical applications.74 Since their discovery, amino-functionalized fullerenes have made a mark in the photovoltaic field as effective ETLs. The photovoltaic performance of numerous devices has been improved by the use of a variety of amino-functionalized fullerene derivatives, especially as ultra-thin IFLs.75,76 A terminal amino group in the fullerene structure creates a dipole moment similar to amine functionalized CPEs and NPEs. The cation supporting amine groups allow the fullerene materials to self-dope, which, in combination with high charge mobilities, makes them very effective ETLs. These derivatives have already been demonstrated as effective cathode buffer layers77,78 in various PSCs and were also incorporable with high WF electrodes like gold, copper, and silver. In perovskite SCs, amine-functionalized fullerenes improve the molecular polarization and charge transport capabilities at the interface between perovskite and fullerenes, which results in improved photovoltaic performance and long-term stability of devices.79,80

IV. Polyelectrolytes, small molecule electrolytes and oligoelectrolytes

The focus of this review is on polyelectrolytes which do not contain extended π-conjugation (which have been covered in separate reviews) and the effects of ionic/polar materials on energy band structures. There is no clear boundary between materials that can be described as conjugated and non-conjugated. For the purposes of this review, we will consider polymers which contain two or fewer aromatic groups (such as viologen) continuously connected by sp2 carbons to be non-conjugated. Small molecules, low molecular weight oligomers and combinations of non-conjugated and conjugated materials have been included for thoroughness.

Another important distinction to make is in the case of alkyl amine moieties. Although their neutral structures imply that they are not ions, they are typically relatively strong Brønsted–Lowry and Lewis bases which are easily protonated or quaternized. Their amphoteric nature indicates that they may auto-ionize to a significant extent and are therefore considered to be classes of electrolytes in the context of this review.

Polyethyleneimine

The most widely applied class of NPE is based on the polyethylenimine (PEI) structure. Amine and ammonium functional CPEs were well known as effective ETLs before it was discovered that non-conjugated polyamines like ethoxylated polyethylenimine (PEIE)81 and PEI28 are able to reduce the WF of electrodes and function in a similar way. Since this discovery, many studies have confirmed the effectiveness of PEI derivatives as ETLs in SCs, LEDs and transistor devices.

For example, polyelectrolytes based on PEI and PEIE structures were found to be very effective in reducing WFs when blended with a bulk heterojunction composite.82 Here, self-assembly and vertical phase separation occurred, where the polyelectrolyte adsorbed to the bottom surface as an IFL and the photoactive layer migrated to the top. The effects of various counter anions such as Br−, I− and BIm4− counter anions83 were also investigated in PEI-based polyelectrolytes used as IFLs. Improved power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) were obtained from organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices using these materials.

In another study, a type of device referred to as a light emitting solar cell (LESC)84 was demonstrated using a methylammonium lead bromide (MAPbBr3) perovskite active layer, where polyelectrolytes were used as IFLs. Whereas using ETLS with stepped LUMO energies resulted in barriers to either electron injection (Fig. 4a) or electron extraction (Fig. 4b), NPEs were able exploit a band bending effect to achieve charge carrier injection and extraction, as shown in Fig. 4c and 4d. The ability of the device to function as both an LED and a SC was attributed to the formation of a homojunction by the polyelectrolyte. Non-conjugated polyelectrolytes

Polyelectrolytes can have polymer backbones which are either conjugated (semiconducting) or non-conjugated (insulating). Although NPEs are simpler than CPEs, CPEs were demonstrated as effective IFLs and widely studied in organic semiconducting devices before NPEs were. It may seem counter-intuitive at first to use an in electronic insulator to improve charge injection or extraction, which may be the reason that NPEs were not explored in optoelectronic devices earlier. Interestingly, despite being electronic insulators, bilayers of cationic and anionic polyelectrolytes are able to form current rectifying P–N junctions even when no semiconductor is present.85

Although the importance of ions in compensating charge carriers was realized early in the development of organic semiconductors, it was not until later that ion-bearing CPEs86 and NPEs87 became widely used as effective interfacial materials in semiconducting devices. CPEs have delocalized π-electrons throughout their backbones, which provide a level of electronic functionality, however, the conjugated backbones tend to be difficult and expensive to synthesize in contrast to NPEs, which are often derived from low cost commodity polymers. The backbones of NPEs lack conjugated π-bonds, which makes them electrically insulating. Thus, their impacts on the electronic band structure are almost entirely determined by their ionic groups. Some of the earliest examples of polyelectrolytes being used in conjunction with semiconductors did not involve them being used to influence the electronic band structure of semiconductors, but to modify the surface charges and direct self-assembly via electrostatic interactions. For example, in the 1990s, the LBL film deposition technique was first introduced,88,89 which later became used more extensively in semiconducting devices. Decher et al. demonstrated that cationic and anionic polyelectrolytes could self-assemble onto a charged substrate by alternately depositing one monolayer at a time. This proved to be a powerful technique to self-assemble polyelectrolyte films with well-defined thicknesses and to control the surface charge of substrates. For example, LBL deposition of polyallylamine (PAA) and polyvinylsulfonic acid (PVSA) was used with ZnS-capped CdSe quantum dots (QDs)90 to make hierarchical structures where QDs were stabilized by the NPEs. Similar studies were performed using another class of PEI NPEs, where indium tin oxide (ITO) surfaces were modified using the same LBL technique.91 The LBL technique has been used to fabricate various photovoltaic devices as well.92–96 Inspired by these results, the application of NPEs in SCs was reported28 as IFLs in inverted PSCs where the electrolytes were coated onto an ITO substrate using ionic self-assembly. Dipoles were formed between the cationic amine of the NPE and the anionic oxygen of ITO. The WF of ITO could be dramatically reduced from 4.8 to 4.0 eV.

Poly-viologen (PV) derivatives97,98 have been investigated as IFLs due to their unique electrochromic properties. The structure of PV contains aromatic bipyridine groups, however, the backbone is not fully conjugated. Derivatives of PV having p-toluene sulfonate ions (PV-OTs) resulted in surface dipoles when used as IFLs in PSCs. Wu et al. prepared an interesting composite by polymerizing a monomer consisting of zinc acrylate. The composite ETL was able to improve the PCE of inverted PTB7:PCBM solar cells from 6.67% to 7.37%.99 Another interfacial material study100 showed that an anionic NPE sodium poly(4-styrenesulfonate) (PSS-Na) could be employed in both PSC and PLED devices as a cathode interlayer. PSS-Na is commercially available and is soluble in protic solvents like water and water/alcohol mixtures. KPM revealed an interfacial dipole at the active layer/cathode interface, and a reduction in cathode WF which enhanced the performances of both devices. A mixed polyelectrolyte consisting of PSS and PEI was also investigated.101 Compared to PEI as an IFL, PEI:PSS resulted in a significant increase in photocurrent. Moreover, the device stability was improved when a bilayer of TiOx-PEI:PSS was used as an interlayer. In addition to tuning the cathode WF, the NPEs eliminated the necessity of post-UV treatment which is commonly required for devices containing TiO2 ETLs. PEIE has also been explored as an IFL for many PSC devices resulting in better device performance via a decrease in the electrode WFs. Inspired by these results a hybrid PEIE–Ag interfacial material was synthesized via ultraviolet irradiation of PEIE and silver nitrate solution. The generated silver nanoparticles were anchored to the PEIE polymer chains to form a conductive continuous interpenetrating network structure. As an IFL, PEIE–Ag leads to enhanced charge transport and improved light-harvesting ability (Fig. 6) largely due to the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) effect.102 Tiwari et al. used a variety of polyelectrolytes including polydiallyldimethylammonium chloride (PDADMAC), polyacrylic acid (PAA), PSS and polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) in combination with ZnO as ETLs. PDADMAC was found to offer the greatest benefit and improved the PCE of P3HT:PCBM based devices from 0.10% to 1.86%.103

Zwitterionic NPEs based on poly(vinylpyridine) were investigated as cathode buffer layers both in conventional and inverted PSCs. A comparative study between PEI and PEIE was reported106 using high performance inverted OSCs. PEI showed significantly improved performance compared to PEIE in terms of both stability and PCE when applied as a cathode interlayer in devices having the same configuration. In a recent study,115 alcohol-soluble polystyrene derivatives including poly[p-(N,N-dimethylamino)styrene] (PSN), poly[p-(N,N-dimethylamino)styrene]oxide (PSO), and poly[p-(N,N-dimethylamino)styrene] mesylate (PSM) were synthesized and studied as cathode interlayers in OSCs. This study focused on the interactions between pendent groups and acceptor molecules on the same non-conjugated backbones. Significant increases in VOC, Short circuit current (JSC) and fill factor (FF) were obtained under optimized conditions along with a PCE increase from 3.74% to as high as 10.11%. The effects of different counter anions (Br−, I− and BIm4−) were explored using the same non-conjugated backbone (PEI) (Fig. 9c) as a cathode IFL in OSCs and transistor devices based on indium zinc oxide (IZO).83 NPEs with different counter ions demonstrated the ability to form dipoles at the metal electrode interfaces and control the WFs of the electrode. As a result, the PCE of the OSCs could be improved from 1.05% to 6.77%; on/off ratios and charge carrier mobilities of transistor devices were also improved.

Li et al. carried out a direct comparison between a small-molecule, monomeric ionic liquid and its NPE ionic liquid analogue while using both as IFLs in OSC devices containing PTB7-Th:PC71BM as the active layer.116 This study showed that the polymeric ionic liquid (PIL) yielded a homogeneous film morphology and a higher PCE than that the monomeric counterpart. Ionic liquids were also used in a dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC), where they were added to a polyethylene glycol (PEG) electrolyte containing the I/I3− redox couple. The photovoltaic performance of the cell was enhanced by 38% and the ionic liquid containing cells showed improved long-term stability than the cells without IFLs.117 Rafiq et al. investigated a series of hyper-branched polyelectrolytes based on a 1,3,5-trispyridylbenzene core. These were used with non-fullerene acceptor active layers and improved the PCE from 5.80 to 9.87%. Interestingly, these polyelectrolytes were also shown to improve the performance of photo-catalytic hydrogen cells.118

Because NPEs are electrical insulators, their influence on organic semiconducting device characteristics is not due to their HOMO or LUMO energy band structures but can be attributed exclusively to their ionic components; thus, they offer insight into the effects of ions and dipoles on the band structure and characteristics of devices. Despite the counter-intuitive concept of improving electrical current using electrically insultating interlayers, polyelectrolytes have been demonstrated to improve the device characteristics in a wide number of studies and offer improved device performance and often improved stability as well. Several general review articles have been published in relation to interfacial materials in optoelectronic devices119–121 and show that simple polyelectrolytes offer benefits comparable to much more synthetically challenging or impractical materials. For example, the ability of polyelectrolytes to tune the WF of electrodes is comparable to alkali or alkaline earth metals, however, polyelectrolytes are much more stable than these reactive metals. In the past, the function of electrolyte containing interfacial materials has been primarily attributed to changes in the WF due to interfacial dipoles, however, increasing evidence is emerging showing that ionic materials like CPEs and NPEs are often involved in doping or electrochemical doping at interfaces, which offers the possibility to conveniently create ohmic contacts without introducing potential energy barriers as is the case with typical semiconducting ETLs and HTLs. In any case, these materials have the advantages of being minimalistic interfacial materials in the sense that they are structurally simple and economical to synthesize. As new studies are published and the field continues to mature, it is likely that these materials will be increasingly used as a strategy to create and control homojunctions in organic and hybrid semiconducting devices.

Small molecule electrolytes

Non-polymeric materials may perform the same function as polyelectrolytes; although as the size decreases, they may diffuse or drift through solid films over time, which may lead to uncontrolled doping (for example the tetrabutylammonium cation) and/or unstable junctions. Although we will refer to these materials as “small molecules”, we will consider any molecule that does not contain many repeating monomer units to be a small molecule, regardless of its size or molecular weight.

Compared to polymer interlayers, small molecules have intrinsic advantages of easier synthesis and purification procedures, well-defined structures, mono-dispersity, and good batch-to-batch reproducibility.122,123 Owing to these advantages, small molecule compounds can be applied in semiconducting devices such as OLEDs, OSCs,124 and organic field-effect transistors (OFETs).125 Several structures are listed in Fig. 10, which were some of the original small SMEs to be investigated as interlayer materials.

Some early OLEDs suffered from poor operating stability (less than 1000 h), a problem which could be mitigated by employing interlayers. Fang et al. synthesized a variety of novel zwitterionic small molecules as electron-injection layers in PLEDs. It was confirmed that the zwitterionic properties of the molecules were important for electron injection, while π-delocalized structures had less significant impacts on the device performance. A. Islam et al. have reviewed the recent progress and prospects of zwitterionic molecules recently.126 PLED devices using solid ionic liquids as ETLs exhibited improved performance and showed promise for general use as charge-injection layers in a variety of organic electronic devices.127 The discotic structure of pyrene derivatives, like 3,3′,3′′,3′′′-((pyrene-1,3,6,8-tetrayltetrakis(benzene-4,1-diyl))tetrakis(oxy))tetrakis(N,N-dimethylpropan-1-amine) (PBPA), makes it possible to be used as solution-processable ETLs in OSC devices. OSCs doped with PBPA reduced the interfacial resistance resulting in a FF increase from 55.1% to 63.6%, and a PCE increase from 7.2% to 8.3%.128

G. Feng et al. developed two hydrophilic small molecules consisting of a conjugated backbone with polar pendant groups and used them as cathode interlayers in inverted PSCs. These were found to be effective interface modifiers resulting in a PCE increase from less than 8.0% to 9.22%. As a result, it was demonstrated that the modification of the cathode with the electrolyte could increase not only VOC but also JSC and FF of inverted PSCs.129 Ouyang et al. introduced a zwitterionic small molecule layer and a LiF layer as a bilayer ETL between the cathode and active layer of perovskite solar cells (PeSCs). Atomic force microscopy (AFM) images of the double-interlayer devices revealed a smoother surface compared to the pristine perovskite layer, with decreased root-mean-square roughness (RRMS) after deposition of rhodamine 101. Compared to the single interlayer, double interlayers of devices composed of LiF and rhodamine 101 exhibited significantly improved VOC and FF values, which improved the PCE of the PeSCs from 11.0% to 13.2%.130

Electrolytes have been used in quantum dot solar cells as well. In a study by Kim et al., an ionic liquid (IL) was used to polarize the surface of PCBM and shift its band edge closer to the vacuum level when introduced as an interlayer between PbS QDs and PCBM. Impedance spectroscopy data showed that the IFL reduced the contact resistance at the interface of PCBM and PbS by improving the compatibility between the two layers. As a result, devices with the IFL exhibited enhanced performance in VOC, from 0.41 V to 0.49 V, FF, from 0.48 to 0.59, and a PCE increase from 1.62% to 2.21%.131

In another example, 4-chlorobenzoic acid (CBA), a small molecule surface modifier, was used as an interfacial material on an ITO electrode in order to produce a hydrophobic surface and high WF. Whereas most IFLs contain amine groups and lower the WF of substrates, this material contains an anionic carboxylate group and increases the WF. Devices using CBA as the interlayer showed improved performance, with PCE increasing from 5.67% to 8.48% due to increased absorption in the active layer and an ohmic contact formed at the interface between the active layer and ITO.132

Recent developments using small molecule and oligoelectrolytes as IFLs show that they have considerable impacts on the device performance. Compared to polyelectrolytes, small molecules and oligomers offer advantages in that they have well-defined structures, generally higher solubility than high MW polyelectrolytes and do not suffer from batch-to-batch variations or polydispersity issues that many polymers suffer from. Like polyelectrolytes, small molecule and oligoelectrolytes tend to have wider band gaps than adjacent semiconducting layers and function largely due to the influence of their ionic functionalities.

Small sized molecules, such as TBAI or TBAF, may diffuse into organic semiconducting layers and cause bulk doping effects throughout the film, which can be exploited to increase the bulk conductivity of semiconductor films, but their uncontrolled diffusion precludes their ability to create doped interfaces or stable junctions. Higher molecular weight molecules and oligomers can overcome this problem by forming separate, immiscible solid films which do support doped interfaces and stable junctions like polyelectrolytes.

Using conjugated small molecule and oligomer electrolytes provides the opportunity to exploit the electronic properties of the compounds as well, in order to achieve effects such as self-doping and cascading energy transfer. Future generations of these materials are likely to offer improved control over band structures and doping in organic and hybrid semiconductors and continue to have a remarkable impact on the field.

V. Conclusions

Ionic functionalities create localized electric fields and provide the ability to compensate charge carriers, aspects which have profound impacts on the properties, energy band structures and performance of organic and hybrid optoelectronic devices. Through these mechanisms, polyelectrolytes and oligoelectrolytes are able to modify the WF of metals and semiconductors by generating interfacial dipoles. It is also becoming apparent that they facilitate doping, compensating the charges of mobile carriers, offering control over the charge carrier density and allowing the creation of homojunctions. Although NPEs and oligoelectrolytes constitute simpler structures than CPEs, their development and an understanding of their role in optoelectronic devices has emerged more recently. Recent research has demonstrated that despite their wide band gaps, these materials are very effective at controlling the band structures of organic and hybrid semiconductors, even if they themselves are not conjugated and do not exhibit semiconducting behaviour by themselves.

Initially, these materials were assumed to influence device performance primarily by an interfacial dipole mechanism and by changing the WF of electrodes. In several recent studies where ESR spectra have been collected, there is evidence that the presence of ionic functionalities leads to radical ions and doping in the organic semiconductor. Thus, it appears that ionic polymers like NPEs and CPEs may provide a general strategy to create homojunctions in organic semiconducting devices. Although most studies using ionic materials regularly characterize interfacial dipoles and how the ionic materials affect the work functions of electrodes, relatively few studies have included experimental evidence showing doping, how ionic materials affect the population of charge carriers near the interface and how the band structure changes within the semiconducting layer next to the ionic material. The presence of free charge carriers can be detected by characteristic polaron absorption bands, ESR spectroscopy and through changes in the conductivity of the film. Changes in the band structure of the active layer can be measured via UPS by incrementally depositing thicker films of the active semiconductor material on top of the ionic material; however, this type of depth-dependent investigation of the band structure, so far, is uncommon. This kind of detailed characterization of charge carriers and band structure is needed to fully understand how interlayers affect the device function and constitutes an important potential area for growth and development in the context of organic and hybrid semiconductors.

The simple and cost-effective synthetic processes needed to prepare NPEs make them a convenient and economical tool to control the properties of organic semiconducting and hybrid devices in both laboratory and industrial scale applications. Their solution processability makes the fabrication of solar cells and other devices by methods like ink jet printing or roll-to-toll coating feasible on a large scale.

The ability to create and control homojunctions offers a route to overcome some of the fundamental energy losses encountered in organic and hybrid semiconductors by removing energy barriers. This can lead to higher VOCs in solar cells and lower operating voltages in light-emitting devices. Additionally, placing three junctions together opens the possibility of realizing organic bipolar junction transistors (BJTs), a type of transistor which has been used extensively in the context of silicon devices but has not yet been developed in the context of organic and hybrid electronics. The ability to control the population of charge carriers also offers the opportunity to improve the performance of field-effect transistors. Thus, there are many potential advances that can be made by studying the influence of ion containing materials, like polyelectrolytes, on the band structure of semiconducting devices.

As organic and hybrid semiconducting devices become more mature, it is likely that we will continue to see NPEs employed more often and realize their utility in creating homojunctions and controlling the energy band structure of organic semiconductors.
