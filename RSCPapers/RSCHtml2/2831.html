Rigid triarylamine-based D–A–π–A structural organic sensitizers for solar cells: the significant enhancement of open-circuit photovoltage with a long alkyl group†

Xiaohao Hu, Shengyun Cai, Guojian Tian, Xin Li, Jianhua Su and Jing Li*
Key Laboratory for Advanced Materials and Institute of Fine Chemicals, East China University of Science & Technology, 130 Meilong Road, Shanghai, 200237, P. R. China. E-mail: jli@ecust.edu.cn; Fax: +86-21-64252758; Tel: +86-21-64252758

Abstract

Five new organic D–A–π–A sensitizers DIA6–DIA10 containing benzothiadiazole (BTD) or benzotriazole (BTZ) as auxiliary electron withdrawing units have been synthesized. Their photophysical, electrochemical and photovoltaic properties have been studied. Consequently, although all of these dyes have long alkyl chains grafted on a π-bridge, DSSCs with dyes containing a BTZ unit displayed higher open circuit voltages (757–829 mV) than those containing a BTD unit (682–712 mV). This may be because the nitrogen-based heterocyclic group of BTZ can lift the conduction band edge and reduce the charge recombination that arises from the absence of the sulfur atom. Benefiting from this and the introduction of alkyl chains on the nitrogen atom, DSSCs with one dye containing BTZ and thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (TT) as the π-spacer (DIA7) showed the highest open circuit voltage (Voc = 829 mV). The addition of one more thiophene unit to DIA6 broadens the absorption spectra, leading the DIA8 device to produce the largest photocurrent. Among these sensitizers, the best photovoltaic performance was obtained by the DIA8 device, with a short circuit photocurrent density (Jsc) of 13.45 mA cm−2, an open-circuit photovoltage (Voc) of 757 mV, and a fill factor (FF) of 70%, corresponding to an overall conversion efficiency (η) of 7.15% which reached 93% of the reference dye N719-based cell under standard global AM 1.5 solar light conditions.

Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have been considered as a promising, sustainable and environmentally friendly energy device to meet the energy demands of mankind in the future.1,2 Up to now, a high photo-to-electricity conversion yield (η) exceeding 12.0% under standard AM 1.5 sunlight irradiation has been obtained by DSSCs based on Ru-complex dyes.3 Compared with metal complexes, metal-free sensitizers have also attracted considerable scientific attention, due to their low cost, facile structural modifications, easier preparation and purification.4 Generally, the donor–π–acceptor (D–π–A) structure is the most common character of organic dyes.5 Recently, Tian and co-workers have put forward a so called “D–A–π–A” structure, which introduces an auxiliary acceptor between the donor and π bridge to further optimize the HOMO and LUMO levels and broaden the absorption spectra.6

As is well-known, the short circuit photocurrent density (Jsc) and open circuit voltage (Voc) have become primary concerns in the design of dyes, because they directly influence the overall conversion efficiency. Dyes with high molar extinction coefficients (ε) and broad absorption spectra are advantageous to the light harvesting of DSSCs, and thus beneficial in getting high Jsc values.7 Additionally, high Voc values can be obtained by preventing the intermolecular π–π stacking among sensitizers and keeping I3− ions away from the TiO2 electrode surface in the DSSCs.8 Recently a series of sensitizers bearing semi-rigid9 or rigid10 planar11 triphenylamine donors were reported, the efficiencies of which are much better than simple triarylamine based dyes. In our previous study, we synthesized a rigid amine electron donor with a two-locking structure, which proved to be helpful to increase the ε of the corresponding dyes, as well as the lifetime of electrons injected into the TiO2 conduction band.12 The Jsc of these dyes based DSSCs can indeed display a good performance as the result of a good light-harvesting ability. However, the relatively low Voc became a drawback, due to dye aggregation and a high rate of charge recombination. Herein, we re-examine our concept by introducing long alkyl chains on the π-spacer. Based on that, we have designed and synthesized five new dyes DIA6–DIA10 (Scheme 1) with the D–A–π–A configuration using benzothiadiazole (BTD) or benzotriazole (BTZ) as auxiliary electron withdrawing units. All of these dyes were designed based on the following considerations: (1) the attached alkyl chains could keep sufficient solubility in common organic solvents. More importantly, the introduced alkyl chains can effectively inhibit the intermolecular aggregation functioning as DCA co-adsorbents and charge recombination for the purpose of reducing voltage losses;13 (2) like pyrimidine, and benzimidazole which have been tested as additives in the electrolyte for the sake of increasing Voc, the nitrogen-containing heterocyclic group of BTZ can be expected to improve the Voc.14 Compared with BTZ, BTD is a stronger electron-withdrawing unit and can improve the distribution of donor electrons and broaden the absorption spectra into the NIR region.15 Here we employ BTD and alkyl grafted thiophene as a π-spacer, which can be expected to obtain a desirable photo response ability and suppress dark current from TiO2 to the electrolyte. As the BTZ unit has some properties which are different to the BTD unit when replacing the sulfur atom by a nitrogen one, such as being more electron-rich, thus the effects posed by the two units on the DSSCs performances could be compared; (3) it's worth mentioning that besides general thiophene or n-hexyl-thiophene units, the thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (TT) unit has also been incorporated in the dyes due to its better π-conjugation and smaller geometric relaxation energy upon oxidation;16 (4) different numbers of thiophene or n-hexyl-thiophene units will influence the performance of the DSSCs. Above all, these dye based DSSCs are expected to have a broad response spectra and a suppressed charge recombination, thus leading to high performance. In order to further investigate the DSSCs performance, the stabilities of the dyes and devices were also measured.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

The synthetic route to the five new dyes DIA6–DIA10 is depicted in Scheme 2. The starting material 1 was synthesized according to our previous work.12 The intermediates 2, 3, B1, B2 and D1–D4 were constructed by common Suzuki coupling, formylation and bromination reaction (detailed synthesis procedures are described in the ESI†). Among them, the synthesis of B1 involved the protection and deprotection of the formyl group. The key π-spacer moieties B3, B4 were synthesized according to a previous report.17a The first final product DIA6 was obtained by the direct condensation of 3 with cyanoacetic acid in the presence of a catalytic amount of piperidine in THF. The other final products DIA7–DIA10 were obtained firstly through Suzuki coupling reaction between bromo-substituted materials 1 and the corresponding aldehyde precursor, followed by treatment with cyanoacetic acid in the presence of piperidine in THF. The purification of intermediates was performed by flash column chromatography and subsequent HPLC. All the intermediates and target dyes were characterized by standard spectroscopic methods, including 1H NMR, 13C NMR and HRMS.

Optical and electrochemical properties

The UV-Vis absorption spectra of DIA6–DIA10 in THF and the TiO2 films are shown in Fig. 1 and the corresponding data is summarized in Table 1. All of these compounds exhibit one major broad absorption band in the visible light region which is attributed to the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) between the donor and the acceptor. The absorption maxima in the ICT band for DIA6–DIA10 were located at 431, 444, 465, 478 and 510 nm, respectively. The corresponding molar extinction coefficients are 3.8 × 104, 5.6 × 104, 4.6 × 104, 2.9 × 104 and 3.1 × 104 M−1 cm−1. The order of these values DIA7 > DIA8 > DIA6 > DIA10 > DIA9 is perfectly consistent with that of the corresponding oscillator strengths (f), as determined by TDDFT (2.09, 2.07, 1.84, 1.35 and 1.28, respectively). By comparison, it was found that the absorption maximum of the ICT for DIA7 was more red-shifted than that of DIA6, in that TT could enhance the electron delocalization to a larger extent than the thiophene. Owing to the expansion of the conjugation of the systems, the bathochromic shift by 34 or 32 nm (DIA8 or DIA10) was observed when introducing one more thiophene unit into DIA6 or DIA9. It is also to be mentioned that DIA9 with a BTD unit exhibits a maximum absorption peak at 478 nm that is red shifted by 47 nm as compared with DIA6 containing a BTZ unit which has the same length of chain. The red-shift may arise from the stronger electron-withdrawing ability of the BTD unit, thus resulting in a stronger ICT effect on the dye molecule.16d

When adsorbed on TiO2, the dyes show mild changes (5–13 nm) with respect to those in solution. Two points could account for this phenomenon: (1) the alkyl chain plays an important role, reducing intermolecular aggregation when sensitizers are absorbed onto the TiO2 surface; (2) the incorporation of BTZ and BTD units is beneficial to counteract the deprotonation effect, which contributes to the observed small shift.17

The electrochemical behaviors of the dyes were measured by cyclic voltammetry (CV), and the results are given in the ESI.† As shown in Table 1, the first oxidation potentials, taken as the HOMO levels of DIA6–DIA10, are calculated to be 1.19, 1.25, 1.14, 1.15 and 1.10 V (vs. NHE, same below), respectively, which are more positive than the I3−/I− redox couples (0.4 V), indicating that the oxidized dyes formed after electron injection into the conduction band of TiO2 could thermodynamically accept electrons from I− ions. In particular, the lowest HOMO potential-energy level was found in DIA7, which implies that the device made with it may show the most efficient charge regeneration.18 The LUMO levels of the sensitizers were estimated by the values of Eox and the 0–0 band gaps (E0–0), whilst the latter values were calculated by the onset of absorption spectra in Fig. 1(b). Consequently, all the LUMO values of these dyes (−1.09, −0.98, −0.96, −0.93 and −0.83 V, respectively) are more negative than the conduction band (Ecb) of TiO2 (−0.5 V), indicating the electron injection from the LUMO orbital of these sensitizers to the conduction band of TiO2 was energetically permitted.

Computational analysis

To gain a better understanding of the geometrical configuration and electronic distribution of the frontier, as well as other close-lying orbitals, density functional theory (DFT) calculations were carried out. In the calculations, the long alkyl chains in these compounds were replaced by ethyl groups to save computational time, whilst preserving their steric effect. The hybrid B3LYP functional and the Ahlrichs split valence SVP basis set was employed to optimize the molecular geometries of the dyes and to compute their frontier molecular orbitals, using the ORCA program package.19–21 Subsequent time-dependent DFT (TDDFT) calculations were performed to gain information on the basis set implemented in Gaussian 09 program package.22

As is depicted in Fig. 2, in the ground state the geometry of the dyes is not planar. For DIA6–DIA8, only one major twist was observed in the molecular structure, which is located between the donor and neighbouring π-spacer and the corresponding dihedral angles are 25.4°, 26.0° and 16.4°, respectively. However, for DIA9 and DIA10, the molecules are mainly twisted at two positions: one is located between the donor and neighbouring π-spacer and the corresponding dihedral angles are 32.0° and 51.0°; the other is located between the acceptor and the neighbouring π-spacer and the corresponding dihedral angles are 31.2° and 31.8°. It is obvious that the structures of DIA9 and DIA10 are more twisted than the other dyes. This can be explained by the presence of the alkyl chain on the thienyl unit, which may cause steric hindrance, resulting in larger dihedral angles and a twisted conformation. This can be further used to explain that the loading amounts of DIA9 (5.5 × 10−8 mol cm−2) and DIA10 (4.8 × 10−8 mol cm−2) on the TiO2 surface were lower than that of DIA6–DAI7 (8.6 × 10−8, 7.6 × 10−8 and 7.5 × 10−8 mol cm−2, respectively), because a more twisted conformation could make the molecule occupy a larger surface area than a molecule of linear shape.23

DSSC performance

To investigate the light harvesting ability of DIA6–DIA10, the DSSCs based on dye-sensitized TiO2 (12 μm) films and the electrolyte in the experimental section were fabricated and the performances were measured. Fig. 3(a) shows the action spectra of the incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) for DSSCs with these dyes when THF was used as the solvent for dye loading. The IPCE onsets for DIA6–DIA10 are 620, 630, 680, 650, and 740 nm, respectively, which are broadened significantly with respect to the absorption spectra on sensitized films. This phenomenon could be ascribed to the scattering effect of the large particles in the film and the reflective effect of the Pt counter electrode. The IPCE values of these dyes reached a maximum of 85.1% at 440 nm, 88.2% at 490 nm, 86.2% at 500 nm, 72.6% at 490 nm and 63.2% at 520 nm respectively. The highest IPCE value was achieved by the DIA7 device, which may be a result of the high molar extinction coefficients. Although the IPCE value for the DIA8 device was almost the same as that for the DIA6 and DIA7 devices, DIA8 exhibited better performance with a broader spectra region, indicating that the DIA8 sensitized TiO2 electrode would generate a higher conversion yield compared to these dyes. The maximum IPCE values for the DIA9 and DIA10 devices were lower than that of the others, which may be attributed to the combined impacts of the electron injection yield from the excited dye to the CB of the TiO2 (related to the LUMO energy level), the absorption capacity and the amount of dye adsorbed on the TiO2 film.24

The generation of Voc is related to the charge recombination rate and electron injection efficiency in the DSSCs. Among all of the dyes, the DIA6–DIA8 based DSSCs (with the BTZ group) showed higher Voc values than the DIA9 and DIA10 based ones (with BTD). There are two possible reasons: (1) compared with the BTD-based sensitizers, the BTZ unit contains no sulfur element, which is attractive to iodine in the electrolyte. Therefore, the BTZ-based sensitizers can decrease the iodine concentration at the vicinity of the TiO2 and reduce the charge recombination;25 (2) the nitrogen-based heterocyclic group of BTZ may lift the conduction band edge of the TiO2 and improve the Voc.6c Obviously, the DIA7 based DSSCs exhibits the highest open-circuit voltage (829 mV). Two reasons could account for this phenomenon: (1) since the DIA7 dark-current onset potential shifted to a larger value than that of the other dyes (as shown in Fig. 3(b)), suggesting that DIA7 can inhibit the charge recombination between the injected electrons and I3− ions in the electrolyte more effectively; (2) DIA7 had the lowest HOMO potential-energy level which leads to a sufficient driving force for dye regeneration. It is noteworthy that the DIA7 based DSSCs exhibited higher values of Jsc, Voc and η than DIA6, suggesting that as an alternative to thiophene, the TT moiety can be advantageously incorporated into the dye sensitizer as a π-spacer and improve the cell efficiency. On the other hand, introducing an extra π-spacer between the donor and auxiliary acceptor seems to increase the Jsc of the DSSCs due to an expanded light response region (for example, DIA6 and DIA8). Such a regular phenomenon may be helpful to design new D–A–π–A dyes. The best performance was found in the DIA8 device, which shows a maximum Jsc of 13.45 mA cm−2, a Voc of 757 mV, a FF of 70% and an η of 7.15%. The high η was mainly attributed to the large Jsc, which may be derived from a good IPCE performance. Besides, the replacement of the sulfur element by nitrogen element may cause the higher Voc of the DIA8 device than that of the DIA9 and DIA10 devices.

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed to further elucidate the photovoltaic properties of these dyes. The Nyquist plot and Bode plot are shown in Fig. 4(a) and (b), respectively. The Nyquist plots for DIA6–DIA10 sensitized cells were measured under a forward bias of −0.70 V in the dark with a frequency range of 0.1 Hz to 100 kHz. Since the semicircle for Nernst diffusion within the electrolyte was overlapped by the intermediate-frequency large semicircle, only two semicircles were observed in the Nyquist plots. The smaller semicircle at a high frequency was assigned to the redox charge-transfer response at the Pt/electrolyte interface, while the larger one at the intermediate frequency represented the charge transfer resistance at the TiO2/dye/electrolyte interface (Rrec). This was related to the charge recombination rate, e.g., a larger Rrec indicates a slower charge recombination and therefore a smaller dark current (shown in Fig. 3(b)). The radius of the biggest semicircle Rrec values decreased in the order of DIA7 > DIA6 > DIA8 > DIA9 > DIA10. The detailed data analyzed by software (ZSimpWin) using an equivalent circuit shown in Fig. 4(c) can further demonstrate the results mentioned above. By fitting the EIS spectra to an electrochemical model,26 several important parameters, such as RS (the series resistance), Rrec and RCE (charge transfer resistance at the Pt/electrolyte interface) can be obtained. Among these parameters, RS and RCE are associated with the smaller semicircle. As listed in Table 3, the RS and RCE of these dye based DSSCs show almost the same value because the same electrolyte and electrode that the devices used. The Rrec of the DIA6–DIA10 dyes were 272.6, 543.9, 113.6, 34.4 and 28.4 Ω cm−2, respectively. The result appears to be consistent with the increase of Voc in the DSSCs with dyes. The smaller Rrec value means the electron recombination from the TiO2 to the electron acceptors in the electrolyte occurs more easily, thus resulting in a lower Voc. The electron lifetime measured by the relation τe = 1/(2πf) (f is the peak frequency) decreased in the order of DIA7 (152 ms) > DIA6 (136 ms) > DIA8 (113 ms) > DIA9 (86 ms) > DIA10 (82 ms), while the peak frequency in the Bode plot enhanced in the reverse. All of the data mentioned above could well support the order of the Voc of the DSSCs based on these dyes. It is reasonable that the DIA7 device has a high Voc value due to the long electron lifetime and slow charge recombination rate.

Stabilities of the dyes and devices

Photo-stability is one of the most important parameters for cell operation for durable application. According to the research by Katoh and coworkers,27 we measured the absorption spectra of the dye-loaded TiO2 film before and after 30 min of AM 1.5 simulated solar light irradiation, and observed the variation in the absorption curves. The details of DIA6–DIA10 are shown in Fig. S3.† It could be observed that the variations were huge before and after light irradiation without the use of a UV cutoff filter. However, the situation appeared entirely different when the UV cutoff filter at 400 nm was used, suggesting that these dyes were easily degraded by ultraviolet light. As shown in Fig.S3(b),† since no distinct absorption peak shifts were observed, the sensitizers DIA6–DIA10 were basically stable. From the comparison of DIA6 and DIA7, the introduction of TT instead of thiophene lead to less of a decrease in absorbance, implying that DIA7 had a higher photo-stability. On the other hand, extending the chain length of the oligothiophene moiety seems to be beneficial to stabilize the oxidized dyes upon light irradiation, such as DIA6 and DIA8.

To further evaluate the long term stability of the DIA7, DIA8-based DSSCs, a less volatile ionic liquid electrolyte (containing 0.6 M 1-propyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide, 0.15 M iodine, 0.1 M guanidinium thiocyanate and 0.5 M NMBI in 3-methoxypropionitrile) was employed. Table 4 and Fig. 5 shows the variations of the photovoltaic parameters during a long-term accelerated aging on DIA7 and DIA8 based solar cells, keeping a constant performance during a 500 h aging test under one sun at 60 °C. Upon a continuous 500 h of light soaking, the efficiencies of the DSSCs based on DIA7 and DIA8 showed a maximum change of 5.9% and 3.6%. These results suggested that the two dyes are promising candidates for practical application.

Conclusions

In summary, five rigid triarylamine based D–A–π–A structural dyes, with BTD or BTZ as auxiliary electron withdrawing units, were successfully synthesized and characterized. The result showed that the Voc values of DIA6–DIA8 with a BTZ moiety are higher than those of DIA9 and DIA10 with a BTD group. The TT-containing DIA7 based DSSCs exhibited the highest Voc of 829 mV due to its lowest HOMO potential-energy level, long electron lifetime and slow charge recombination rate. The additional thiophene or n-hexyl-thiophene in DIA8 or DIA10 lead to a significant redshift in the UV-Vis properties and photocurrent response by extending the conjugated system. However, owing to the strong steric hindrance caused by the n-hexyl-thiophene unit, a remarkable molecular twist exists in the spacer of DIA9 and DIA10, which decreases the dye-loading amount, as well as the IPCE values. Because of the broad absorption spectrum and efficient electron injection into the conduction band of TiO2, DIA8 with the large Jsc, exhibits the best overall efficiency performance of 7.15% among these dyes (Jsc of 13.45 mA cm−2, a Voc of 757 mV, and a FF of 70%). Based on the above findings, the insertion of an extra π-spacer between the donor and auxiliary acceptor and avoiding the long alkyl chain being close to the acceptor are good designs for D–A–π–A structured dyes.

Experimental

Materials

Optically transparent FTO conducting glass (fluorine doped SnO2, transmission >90% in the visible region, sheet resistance 15 Ω per square) was obtained from the Geo Science and Educational Co. Ltd. of China and cleaned by a standard procedure. Titania pastes of Ti-Nanoxide T/SP (13 nm) and Ti-Nanoxide 300 were also purchased from there. Tetra-n-butylammonium hexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6), 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP), 2.4 M BuLi solution in hexane and the reaction catalyst Pd(PPh3)4 were obtained from J&K. Lithium iodide, 1,2-dimethyl-3-propylimidazolium iodide (DMPII) were from Fluka and the iodine (99.999% purity) was from Alfa Aesar. Acetonitrile with HPLC purity was purchased from SK Chemicals. THF was pre-dried over sodium and distilled under an argon atmosphere from sodium benzophenone ketyl immediately prior to use. All the other chemicals were used as received without further purification. The starting materials 4,7-dibromo-2-octyl-2H-benzo[d][1,2,3]triazole, 4,7-dibromobenzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole and 3-bromo-8,8-diethyl-8H-indolo[3,2,1-de]acridine were commercially available.

Instruments and characterization

NMR spectra were obtained on a Brucker AM 400 spectrometer and HRMS were performed using a Waters LCT Premier XE spectrometer. Elemental analysis was performed on a German elementar vario EL III instrument. The absorption spectra of the sensitizers in solution and adsorbed on TiO2 films were measured with a Varian Cary 500 spectrophotometer. The cyclic voltammograms of the dyes were determined with a Versastat II electrochemical workstation (Princeton applied research) using a normal three-electrode cell with a Pt working electrode, a Pt wire counter electrode, and a regular calomel reference electrode in saturated KCl solution, 0.1 M tetrabutylammonium hexaflourophosphate (TBAPF6) was used as the supporting electrolyte in cyanoacetic acid. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements of all the DSSCs were performed using a Zahner IM6e Impedance Analyzer (ZAHNERElektrik GmbH & CoKG, Kronach, Germany). The spectra were scanned in a frequency range from 0.1 Hz to 100 kHz at room temperature with an applied potential set at open circuit. The alternate potential amplitude was set at 10 mV.

The DSSCs were evaluated by recording the J–V curves with a Keithley 2400 source meter under the illumination of an AM 1.5 solar simulator equipped with a 300 W xenon lamp (model no. 91160, Oriel). The power of the simulated light was calibrated to 100 mW cm−2 using a Newport Oriel PV reference cell system (model 91150V). A mask of 0.25 cm−2 was used to cover around the cell active area. The voltage step and delay time of the photocurrent were 10 mV and 40 ms, respectively. The photocurrent action spectra were measured with an IPCE test system consisting of a model SR830 DSP Lock-In Amplifier and a model SR540 Optical Chopper (Stanford Research Corporation, USA), a 7IL/PX150 xenon lamp and power supply, and a 7ISW301 spectrometer. The impedance spectra were recorded using a CHI-660c electrochemical station.

Fabrication of the DSSCs

The pretreatment of the transparent conducting oxide (FCO) glass and the fabrication of the solar cells were performed according to the published procedures,28 except that the coated TiO2 film consisted of a 8 μm thin TiO2 film and a 4 μm scattering layer (Ti-Nanoxide 300).The DSSCs were fabricated using DIA6–DIA10 as sensitizers with an effective working area of 0.25 cm2, and dye adsorbed TiO2 films pasted onto FCO glass as the working electrode.
