Abstract

The interactions between the electrolyte and electroactive species in redox active PProDOT derivatives can be enhanced with better-performing electrodes for electrochemical energy conversion and storage. Differently functionalized 3,4-propylenedioxythiophenes (ProDOTs) are electropolymerized from acetonitrile using Et4NBF4, Et4NPF6, or NaClO4 as an electrolyte to evaluate the electrochemical performance of the considered polymer derivatives, as well as under monomer-free conditions. We propose a new equation to assess the kinetics of the diffusion-limited adsorption process with two independent solved parameters: diffusion slope k and power exponent b. Electron-donating groups attached to the monomer stabilize radical cation and dication formation during electrogrowth. The ProDOT–Br structure has a low oxidation potential owing to the electron-rich group in the heterocyclic structure, which grants access to a radical cation intermediate and interaction with the ions of the electrolyte. Surface roughness and morphology are assessed by atomic force microscopy and scanning electron microscopy, respectively. The changes in the Fourier transform infrared spectrum are more pronounced with the selection of electrolyte type than the type of derivatives. Except the –OBz derivative, the largest specific capacitance calculated from the area under the final cyclic voltammetry curves is obtained for Et4NBF4, followed by Et4NPF6 and NaClO4. In all systems, the –OPhCH2OH derivative exhibited the lowest electrolyte diffusion. More pronounced diffusion is observed for the PProDOT derivatives measured in Et4NPF6 and Et4NBF4 electrolytes, whereas the lowest diffusion is obtained for the NaClO4 electrolyte. Findings in this study provide new insights into the factors requiring attention upon tailoring assemblies for organic electronic applications.


Design, System, Application

With this study, we introduce, for the first time, a comprehensive approach to determine the intrinsic properties of 3,4-propylenedioxythiophene (ProDOT) derivatives, which enables new application possibilities in energy, biomedical, and sensor material development. ProDOT derivatives bearing reactive functional groups like bromomethyl (ProDOT–Br), tolylsulfonyloxymethyl (ProDOT–OTs), benzyloxymethyl (ProDOT–OBz), and hydroxymethylphenoxymethyl (ProDOT–OPh) as precursors were electropolymerized and examined comparatively in three different electrolytes, namely tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (Et4NBF4), tetraethylammonium hexafluorophosphate (Et4NPF6) and sodium perchlorate (NaClO4). To evaluate the concentration and diffusion effects, we propose a new and a more practical equation which can be used when the adsorption of anions from the electrolyte is very rapid compared to diffusion. The largest specific capacitance in general is highest in Et4NBF4 electrolyte followed by Et4NPF6 and NaClO4. Different morphologies, roughness and characteristic absorption bands can be attained by changing the electrolyte which also influences the impedance and Mott–Schottky behavior. The investigated ProDOT derivatives can be used as polymeric interfaces in assembly systems to optimize charge carriers in organic electronic devices. This study involves chemistry, materials science and energy fields due to the in-depth investigation of electropolymerization, and electrochemistry, surface, spectroscopic and structural characterization of functionalized thiophene-based electroconductive polymers.

1. Introduction

The electrochemical polymerization of thiophene derivatives demands careful adjustment of a number of experimental parameters like solvent, monomer concentration, supporting electrolyte, electrode type, cell geometry and applied electrical conditions influencing structural and physicochemical properties of the electrodeposited polythiophene. A suitable solvent should possess a high dielectric constant to induce ionic conductivity combined with an adequate electrochemical resistance against decomposition. From aprotic media of low nucleophilicity like acetonitrile (ACN) and propylene carbonate (PC), polythiophenes of high conductivity can be deposited with reasonable current efficiencies.1 A supporting electrolyte is added to provide high ionic conductivity of the medium and doping of the deposited polymer. The choice of the salt depends upon its solubility, nucleophilicity, and degree of dissociation. Usually, polythiophenes are electrodeposited in the presence of anions like ClO4−, PF6−, BF4− and CF3SO3− associated with lithium, sodium or tetraalkylammonium cations. Also, an ionic liquid can be used as an electrolyte.2–4 Electrochemical behavior is strongly influenced by the nature of anion and cation of the electrolyte. The use of different electrolytes causes changes in the shape and position of the voltammetric waves of specific polythiophene.1

The effect of the supporting electrolyte on the electrochemical properties has been investigated for several structurally diverse polythiophenes. The electrodeposition of poly(3-methylthiophene) from ACN onto platinum (Pt) in the presence of PF6−, ClO4−, BF4− and CF3SO3− with Li+ or Bu4N+ as counter-ion gave cyclic voltammograms of similar shape for the first three anions, whereas CF3SO3− produced a broader anodic wave.5 Further experiments revealed that the anion controls the electrodeposition process and the formation of the polymer structure, whereas the cation influences the process of charging and discharging.6 Results independently obtained for this system demonstrated that films produced with lithium salts exhibit lower oxidation potentials than films electrogenerated with tetrabutylammonium salts and the electrolyte dependency of the charge transport rates.7 Poly(3-methylthiophene) synthesized on polyethylene terephthalate (PET)/indium-tin-oxide (ITO) from ACN using ClO4− and BF4− with Li+, Na+ and Bu4N+, Et4N+, respectively, exhibited the highest conductivity in the presence of NaClO4 as the electrolyte.8

The electrical properties of copolymers of 3-alkylthiophenes also were affected by the type of electrolyte (LiClO4/ACN or Et4NBF4/ACN) used in the electrodeposition onto Pt. Whereas polymer films of 3-methyl- and 3-hexylthiophene as well as polymer films of 3-methyl- and 3-octylthiophene exhibited lower resistivity when synthesized with LiClO4, the polymer films of 3-hexyl- and 3-octylthiophene showed the lowest charge resistance when obtained with Et4NBF4. The difference was explained by a high level of doping caused by attraction forces between ClO4− and BF4− anions and positively charged radical cation and dication species in the copolymer matrix.9

Poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT) is characterized by its low oxidation potential due to the electron-donating oxygen atoms, a moderate band gap, high number of π-conjugation because of the rigidity of the ethylene bridge, decent conductivity, high environmental stability and reversible switching between oxidized and reduced state. PEDOT has been commercialized as an aqueous polymeric dispersion using poly(styrenesulfonic acid) as dopant,10 leading to various applications in electrochromic devices,11,12 organic electronics,13,14 solar cells,15 sensors,16,17 actuators,18 biomedical engineering and bioelectronics,19,20 thermoelectric materials,21,22 and supercapacitors.23–25

Deposition of PEDOT onto Pt from ACN in the presence of ClO4−, PF6−, BF4− and CF3SO3− with lithium, tetraethyl- and tetrabutylammonium cation indicated a strong influence of the anion on the conductivity and morphology of the polymer film. Whereas ClO4− produced the highest conductivity values, negative shifts of the monomer oxidation potential were observed for PF6− and BF4−.26 Also the biocompatibility of electrosynthesized PEDOT films was influenced by the doping ion.27,28 The structure of PEDOT films electrogenerated onto ITO substrates using ACN with LiCF3SO3 contains aggregated grains connected by polymeric networks.29 PEDOT electrodeposited onto glassy carbon (GC) from PC using PF6−, BF4−, CF3SO3− with Li+ and Bu4N+ developed different morphologies: PF6− created fibril-like structures exhibiting a very homogeneous composition, but with BF4− and CF3SO3− less thick homogeneous films were obtained.30 Also, the solvent has a significant influence on the morphology of PEDOT. Polymer films made in PC have a smoother morphology than those generated from ACN. The differences between PC and ACN are based on the different solubilities of the EDOT oligomers. Small anions mediate the formation of a more open morphology and low conductivity.31 Therefore, differences in conductivity depending on the electrolyte were detected: at room temperature, a value of 200–300 S cm−1 for PF6− doping, and a value of 20–30 S cm−1 for BF4− and CF3SO3− doping was observed, respectively.32

Although poly(3,4-propylenedioxythiophene) (PProDOT) and its derivatives bearing an additional carbon atom in the alkylene ether bridge were initially developed for the improvement of the electrochromic properties of PEDOT,33,34 a multitude of non-functionalized and functionalized PProDOT derivatives have been later on synthesized for application in photovoltaics,35–44 sensors,45–49 biosensors,50–54 actuators,55 and supercapacitors.56–65 Electrodeposition of poly[3,4-(2,2-dimethylpropylenedioxy)thiophene] (PProDOT–Me2) onto carbon fiber microelectrode (CFME) from ACN revealed decreasing low-frequency capacitance values for different electrolytes in the order Et4NPF6 > Et4NBF4. Using PC or dimethylformamide (DMF) instead of ACN as the solvent led to reduced low-frequency capacitance values.66 The electrosynthesis of PProDOT–Me2 onto Au/Kapton was conducted from ACN or PC with Bu4NBF4 and LiCF3SO3 salts using cyclic voltammetry (CV) and potentiostatic method. Analysis of the variation of experimental parameters elucidated that the electrolyte concentration has a large effect on the areal capacitance due to increasing ionic conductivity and rapid counter-ion diffusion at high electrolyte concentrations. The nature of the electrolyte is influencing the areal capacitance to a lesser extent, wherein the smaller anion BF4− was more advantageous because of its higher mobility through the film. The electropolymerization method used was found to have a significant effect on the morphology of the polymer film due to specific polymerization mechanisms.67 Furthermore, electrochromic properties of PProDOT–Me2 depend on the type, amount and composition of the supporting electrolyte.68 Electrocoating of poly[3,4-(2,2-dibutylpropylenedioxy)thiophene] (PProDOT–Bu2) on CFME from ACN was carried out in the presence of ClO4− with Li+, Na+ and Et4N+ as well as BF4− and PF6− with Bu4N+ as the counter-ion, respectively. Peaks currents of the film growth showed a similar rising trend with the increase of low-frequency capacitance for all electrolytes, except Et4NClO4. The highest double-layer capacitance value was observed for NaClO4. In spite of different electrolytes, similar polymeric morphologies appeared with one exception: Bu4NBF4, LiClO4, and Bu4NPF6 produced homogeneous grainy coatings, whereas, with NaClO4, a more porous structure was obtained.69,70

Electrogeneration of poly[3,4-(2,2-dibenzylpropylenedioxy)thiophene] (PProDOT–Bz2) on CFME from ACN using Bu4NPF6, LiClO4, NaClO4, and Bu4NBF4 as electrolyte created polymer films of different thicknesses. The highest capacitance and formation of a nanofiber network with a highly porous structure were detected for Bu4NPF6. The perchlorate salts also exhibited porous structures but less than Bu4NPF6. The tetrafluoroborate salt showed less capacitive behavior and smooth morphology.71 During electropolymerization of 3,4-(2-benzylpropylenedioxy)thiophene (ProDOT–Bz) on CFME from ACN using the same electrolytes, different oxidation potentials of the monomer in the range of 1.20–1.36 V were observed, following the order NaClO4 < LiClO4 < Bu4NBF4 = Bu4NPF6. Nanofiber network structures obtained with LiClO4 showed the highest capacitance. Other types of morphologies with NaClO4 and Bu4NBF4 gave lower capacitances despite nearly the same film thickness.72

Recently, we have proposed several electropolymerized 3,4-propylenedioxythiophene derivatives bearing reactive functional groups like bromomethyl (ProDOT–Br), tolylsulfonyloxymethyl (ProDOT–OTs), benzyloxymethyl (ProDOT–OBz), and hydroxymethylphenoxymethyl (ProDOT–OPh) as precursors targeting for bioelectronics and supercapacitors.73,74 Developing robust polymer systems for these applications needs a deep understanding of the relationship between morphology and ionic and electronic conductivity.75–77 Interactions between the electrolyte and electroactive species in redox active PProDOT derivatives are critical for designing higher-performance electrodes for electrochemical energy storage and conversion. By the advantage of solubility and processability of PProDOTs which depend mainly upon the functional side chains, and the attached dopant ions during electropolymerization facilitate them with electrical and optical properties. The delocalization of π bonds plays an essential role in the generation of charge carriers like polarons and bipolarons, which are responsible for the transition from insulating to conducting state. Tuning charge carriers by functional groups and anion doping in PProDOTs help to improve the electrochemical properties in different electrolytes, and their versatile structural variability prior to electrodeposition investigated herein will serve as precursors and target materials in bioelectronics and supercapacitor applications.

Therefore, in the present work, we report on investigating the effect of various electrolytes on the electrochemical properties of differently functionalized PProDOTs. Electrodeposition of the polymers onto glassy carbon (GC) and ITO glass was conducted from ACN in the presence of Et4NBF4, Et4NPF6, and NaClO4 using CV for characterization by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), atomic force microscopy (AFM), Fourier transform infrared-attenuated total reflectance spectroscopy (FTIR-ATR), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), X-ray diffraction (XRD) and Mott–Schottky (M–S) analysis.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials

3,4-Propylenedioxythiophene (ProDOT, 97%) and tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (Et4NBF4, 99%), tetraethylammonium hexafluorophosphate (Et4NPF6, 99%) and sodium perchlorate (NaClO4, ≥98%) were received from Sigma Aldrich and used without further purification. Acetonitrile and dichloromethane (Sigma-Aldrich) were of analytical grade. ProDOT–Br, ProDOT–OTs, ProDOT–OBz, and ProDOT–OPh were synthesized as reported previously.73

2.2. Electropolymerization & characterization

Electropolymerizations were performed on GC (for monomer-free CV, EIS and M–S measurements) or ITO glass (for AFM, SEM, FTIR-ATR measurements) by CV between −0.5 V and 1.6 V for 10 cycles at a scan rate of 0.05 V s−1 using PARSTAT 4000A, AMETEK Scientific Instruments potentiostat/galvanostat equipped with a Versa Studio software package. GC or ITO glass was used as a working electrode, Ag/AgCl (3 M NaCl/Saturated AgCl) with a redox potential of +0.209 V vs. a standard hydrogen electrode at 25 °C as a reference electrode, and platinum wire as a counter electrode. Dissolved oxygen was minimized by argon gas purging for 10 min before each electrochemical analysis. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy measurements were conducted to monitor the electrical properties of each polymer coated on GC electrodes at room temperature using a conventional three-electrode cell at open circuit potential condition in 0.1 M electrolyte/ACN solution. The frequency range was from 1 MHz to 0.01 Hz, and the AC signal amplitude was 10 mV. The ZSimpWin V 3.10, an AC impedance data analysis software, was utilized to fit the appropriate equivalent circuit model to the impedance spectra. Structural analysis of the polymer-coated ITO glass electrodes was implemented using Fourier transform infrared-attenuated total reflectance spectroscopy (FTIR-ATR, Bruker Vertex 70 ATR) in the range of 400–4000 cm−1. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Gemini Leo Supra 35 VP) and energy dispersive X-ray analysis (EDX) were performed for polymers coated on ITO glass. AFM studies were carried out by a Nanoscope ezAFM+ with a cantilever tip radius of 10 nm. The images were retrieved using the non-contact mode of the AFM. Mott–Schottky (M–S) experiments were conducted to determine the semiconducting properties of polymer-coated electrodes using the PARSTAT 4000A potentiostat/galvanostat with a conventional cell arrangement. The capacitance measurements for Mott–Schottky plots were performed in 0.1 M electrolyte/ACN solution. M–S experiments were performed at potentials between −0.4 V and 1.5 V at a fixed frequency of 1 kHz with successive 20 mV steps.

3.2. Electrochemical polymerization of substituted ProDOTs

The electrodeposition of differently functionalized ProDOTs on GCE was performed in three electrolyte solutions (0.1 M Et4NBF4/ACN, Et4NPF6/ACN, and NaClO4/ACN) consisting of 10 mM ProDOT derivatives at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1 by 10 deposition cycles. Fig. 2 shows the influence of the used electrolyte for different ProDOT derivatives deposited onto GCE in various electrolyte/ACN solutions. The polymerization behavior of ProDOTs depends on both different electrolyte and substituent types. An enhancement in current density, that is, polymerization efficiency for –OPh, –OBz, and –OTs derivatives, follows the order of PF6− > BF4− > ClO4−, as was reported in electrochemical polymerization of thiophene.78 However, this increase in the current density with increasing cycle number for ProDOT–Br within BF4− is comparatively higher than those for PF6− and ClO4−, and also for other ProDOT counterparts. This is probably due to less steric hindrance of linear –CH2Br side chain than its counterparts, leading to the migration of small BF4− anion toward the ProDOT backbone (easier than that of PF6− anion), resulting in more efficient polymerization. This finding is consistent with the previous report that the growth rates of polyindole film in the ionic liquid BMImBF4/ACN are faster than that of polyindole growth in BMImPF6/ACN dependent on the nature of the ionic liquid and size of dopant ions, where the size of PF6− dopant ions are larger than BF4−.79

The potential window selected for each electrolyte or derivative indicates a suitable polymer film growth. As reported earlier, no polymer formation attains at a lower potential, or the polymerization rate is prolonged to reach desired film thickness. In comparison, at a higher potential, polymer film does not regularly grow in patterns due to over-oxidation of the generated polymeric film.80,81 For the NaClO4 case, more anodic potential to initiate the polymerization is required, so a low current density is observed compared to Et4NBF4 and Et4NPF6. It was reported that monomer charge density for polymerization of thiophene by ClO4−, BF4−, and PF6− anions decreased in the order of PF6− > BF4− > ClO4−.78 The higher Q value obtained with BF4− and PF6− anions indicates an enhancement of the oligomer formation and consequently a more efficient polymer deposition. In addition, the higher current density for PF6− anion than that of BF4− anion can be attributed to the higher alkaline character PF6− anion, which promotes the loss of H+ in the polymerization process.78 The linear increase in current for all ProDOTs indicates the electroactive film formation on the GCE surface. The anodic oxidation onset potentials for ProDOTs in different solvent–electrolyte systems estimated from the first CV cycle in cyclic voltammogram are shown in Table 2. Oxidation of ProDOT in 0.1 M Et4NBF4/ACN initiated at 1.49 V. However, in the case of PrODOT–Br and ProDOT–OTs, oxidation onset in 0.1 M Et4NBF4/ACN shifts to a higher value, which were 1.72 V and 1.75 V, respectively. Moreover, ProDOT derivatives were oxidized at a lower potential in 0.1 M Et4NPF6/ACN than in Et4NBF4/ACN and NaClO4/ACN. These results indicate that the oxidation potential of monomers in different electrolyte systems shows a noticeable difference. As shown in Fig. 2, multistep scanning potential of monomer ProDOT and ProDOT derivatives in various electrolyte systems result in a broad redox wave generally generated in the region from 0.2 to 0.8 V associating with redox behavior of electrodeposited PProDOTs, indicating the formation of conductive polymers.69,71,72 Cathodic polymer oxidation peak (Epc) in 0.1 M Et4NBF4/ACN shifted to a higher potential value for PProDOTs compared to that of PProDOT reference. The magnitude of observed, i.e. Epc and Epa, for PProDOTs increases in the order of NaClO4, Et4NBF4, and Et4NPF6 electrolyte systems. Generally, during the electrodeposition, electron-donating groups have higher π-electron density in the thiophene ring, making the electropolymerization more efficient, while the electron-withdrawing groups deactivate the ring towards electropolymerization by raising the oxidation potential.82,83 Here, substituted electron-donating or -withdrawing groups are not directly linked to the conjugated thiophene backbone, but they are attached to the 3,4-propylenedioxy bridge, where steric hindrance is probably of major importance rather than electronic effect. In addition, propylenedioxy substitution planarizes the polythiophene backbone by lowering the bandgap.84 The electron-rich groups mediate to proceed through the electro-oxidation step smoothly, but the intermediate radical cations are a little less reactive in the presence of relatively electron-poor groups, i.e. ProDOT–OPh, ProDOT–OBz, and ProDOT–OTs.85 –OPhCH2OH, –OBz, and –OTs are bulky substituents compared to linear chain –CH2Br leading to more steric hindrance than –CH2Br, where smaller current densities of oxidation and reduction peaks are obtained for these cases. The aforementioned bulky substituent causes a steric hindrance between two adjacent thiophene rings, which may perturb the conjugated system, and hence, directly affect the conductivity.86

If a redox process is solely influenced by diffusion, the peak potential should, in general, be independent of the scan rate. However, when the electrode kinetics plays a predominant role, the scan rate affects the entire voltammetric response, in particular the location of the peak potential.88 The CV waveform of reversible peaks is complex due to the combinatorial effects of polarization and diffusion. The change between the two peak potentials (Ep), ΔEp, is not small values and these results from the effects of substituted PProDOTs coating nature and diffusion rate of electrolyte ions.

The anodic and cathodic current density increases with the scan rate from 25 mV s−1 to 1000 mV s−1. The anodic peak shifts towards higher potentials with increasing scan rate. This concludes that anion doping takes place at high electrode potentials and low scan rates, demonstrating capacitive behavior at higher potentials. where k′ is the slope of the equation, which is determined according to the diffusion or adsorption of species by restricting the change of power ν. Anodic peak (ipa) and cathodic peak (ipc) currents are directly comparable to ν in the potential scan range 0–1000 mV s−1versus Ag/AgCl, and the peak forms are attributes of an adsorption-limited process (Fig. 3a). This means that the electrocoated PProDOTs on the working electrode during the redox cycles are stable, and thus, large capacitive currents are examined around the anodic potentials. This is because of the films' charging and discharging upon the oxidation and reduction and electroactivity of coating, and the slope of these lines depends on the functional groups of PProDOT due to the development of porosity when electrolyte anions are transferred in and out of the films.

The comparison of the slopes (k) indicates the effect of diffusion of electrolyte anions to the ring of polymers for different PProDOT derivatives in three different electrolytes (Table 3). For PProDOT–Br, the most prominent slope is obtained for the Et4NBF4 electrolyte, followed by –OTs, –OBz and –OPhCH2OH derivatives, respectively. Et4NPF6 tends to have lower values for –CH2Br and –OTs derivatives and the same values for –OBz and –OPhCH2OH derivatives. Due to the extremely thin coatings of NaClO4, we could not attain a clear peak signal from –CH2Br and –OPhCH2OH derivatives. –OTs and –OBz derivatives in NaClO4 have the smallest slopes, indicating very slow reaction kinetics in this electrolyte. The linear relationship between the redox peak current (ip) with the scan rate (ν) implies that the reaction of the PProDOTs with electrolytes was mainly a surface-controlled process. This result shows that the formation of PProDOTs on the carbon electrodes influenced the surface state. In the Et4NBF4/ACN electrolyte solution, the slope of peak current density vs. scan rate plot in Fig. 3b represented high ion storage (increased slope) in polymer-coated thin films due to pseudocapacitance contribution. EIS can also be employed to ascertain the potential, where the contribution of pseudocapacitive charge storage is dominant for polymer-coated GC electrodes (more explanations were given in section 3.5). EIS measurements were performed at different bias potentials in the range between 0–1.4 V with a 0.2 V potential step considering the oxidation range of polymer film. The real capacitance at a low frequency of 10 mHz from capacitance Bode plot versus applied bias potential and the cyclic voltammogram at 25 mV s−1 for PProDOT–Br are given in Fig. S1.† Two coupled redox peaks at 0.46/0.42 V and 0.73/0.67 V were observed for PProDOT–Br, indicating two unique electronic environments, which might correspond to the redox properties induced by the bromo substituent.89 The shape of the plot of Crevs. V in Fig. S1† is in good agreement with the shape of the CV of the PProDOT–Br, corroborating the pseudocapacitance contribution at selected potentials associated with two coupled redox peaks for PProDOT–Br. This reveals considerable capacitance increase with the increasing potential indicating Faradaic capacitance of PProDOT–Br. Since there is a peak to peak separation shift with scan rate (Fig. 3a), our case can be an electrochemically quasi-reversible system, and ions of electrolytes are freely diffusing species. It is mainly considered that deviation from linearity in plots of ipvs. ν1/2 suggest electron transfer may also be occurring additionally through surface adsorbed species, as well.

3.5. Impedance and Mott–Schottky analysis

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was used to investigate the capacitive behavior and study the effect of dopant anion on electrochemical properties of PProDOT coated electrodes. Fig. 8 shows the Nyquist plots of PProDOT derivatives in three different electrolytes. It can be seen that the shape of the plot strongly depends on the type of dopant anion as well as on the different substituents on PProDOT. However, the Nyquist plots of the PProDOTs are similarly revealed that depressed semicircles at high-frequency region corresponding to double-layer capacitance and charge transfer resistance of the polymer coated electrode, and a straight line towards low and medium frequency region indicating capacitive and mass transfer behavior. As shown in Fig. 8a and b, for all derivatives, the low-frequency straight lines are almost vertical except for PProDOT–OPh, indicating good capacitive behavior in Et4NBF4 and Et4NPF6. The deviation from vertical lines toward the imaginary axis means that electrodes were fully charged with a changing slope due to the fast diffusion of ions into the pores of electrodes during the charging process.109 The exception is the –OPhCH2OH derivative showing a slope of 4.4 (Fig. 8a). Furthermore, a relatively higher diffusion behavior is observed for the PProDOT derivatives measured in Et4NPF6 electrolyte compared to other electrolyte systems; the slope varies between 13.3 (–OPhCH2OH) up to 33.0 (–OBz) (Fig. 8b), indicating the lower Warburg region and faster electrolyte diffusion kinetics than that of Et4NBF4 and NaClO4.110,111 In the Et4NBF4 electrolytes, the increase in Zim is larger than Zre; thus, the low-frequency vertical line corresponding to higher diffusion kinetics with high slope values (between 14.4–17.1) indicates that most of the surface of the electrode is fully accessible for the BF4− anions than that of the ClO4− anion. Therefore, the smallest diffusion behavior is obtained for the NaClO4 electrolyte; it varies between 3.5 (–OPhCH2OH) and 7.9 (–CH2Br) (Fig. 8c). Thus, in all systems, the –OPhCH2OH derivative has the lowest electrolyte diffusion, whereas –CH2Br is the highest, which is in line with CV measurements. In NaClO4, it can be seen that the capacitive or diffusion/transport behavior at lower frequency domain is shifted to higher resistance along the x-axis of Nyquist plot and presents much higher resistance than those of Et4NBF4 and Et4NPF6, as well. This can be linked to NaClO4 electrolyte generating a polymer film with compact nanostructures, which is probably lesser the penetration of electrolyte ions into the polymer network leading to low diffusion of ions, low capacitance value and high resistance.112

4. Conclusions

In this study, the electrolyte effect on ring-substituted electropolymerized ProDOT derivatives is investigated in detail by electrochemical methods for the processability and functionalization of PProDOT, substituted functional groups on ProDOT is introduced and evaluated for the electrocoating performance of four derivatives, namely –CH2Br, –OPhCH2OH, –OTs, and –OBz, as well as for their monomer-free forms in three different electrolytes, i.e. Et4NBF4, Et4NPF6, and NaClO4. Electropolymerization of these monomers and monomer-free characterization are performed by cyclic voltammetry. Except for the –OBz derivative, the largest specific capacitance calculated from the area under the final CV curves is obtained for Et4NBF4, reaching Csp = 20.17 mF cm−2 and 13.56 mF cm−2 for the –CH2Br and –OTs derivatives, followed by Et4NPF6 and NaClO4. Our findings show that compared to the classical Randles–Sevcik equation, which depends on the concentration and diffusional properties of the electroactive species, the derived ip = kνb equation with k and a variable b exponent can be more suitable for the practical analyses of the samples where the adsorption of anions from the electrolyte is very rapid compared to diffusion.

Due to their π–π intermolecular interactions coming from their (semi)crystalline nature, the polar tosyl and weakly polar benzene groups induce π–π stacking with a displacement due to dipole–dipole attraction in the case of benzene ring containing ProDOTs and the possible formation of S⋯S interactions. The highest specific capacitance is obtained for the Et4NBF4 electrolyte, followed by Et4NPF6 and NaClO4, indicating that electrolyte type strongly affects the capacitance of the electrode. The ionic size of the anion decreases in the following order of PF6− > ClO4− > BF4−. In the used anions, the more coordinating the anion, it tends to exhibit fewer interactions with the cation, the solvent, or the analyte. ProDOT–Br has shown the maximum current density in the Et4NBF4 higher than Et4NPF6 solutions depending on coordinating nature and solubility. In all electrolyte systems, the –OPhCH2OH derivative has the lowest electrolyte diffusion, whereas –CH2Br is the highest which is found to be in line with monomer-free CV measurements.

The lowest roughness values are attained by AFM for the NaClO4 electrolyte. A highly porous, spiky ball-shaped, and relatively homogenous morphology is obtained from FESEM measurements for the Et4NBF4, Et4NPF6, and NaClO4 electrolytes, respectively. The changes in the chemical environment around ProDOT thiophene rings are induced by electrolyte anion doping, as confirmed by FTIR-ATR. From the EIS data the highest Q1 is always attained for the EtNPF6 electrolyte, which suggests that the PF6− ions accumulate more at the interface than the other ions. A Mott–Schottky analysis reveals that the largest and smallest NA values are observed for the –OTs and –OPhCH2OH derivatives in the NaClO4 electrolyte, respectively. From Mott–Schottky analysis, PProDOT–Br and PProDOT–OBz has the highest capacitance within Et4NBF4 and Et4NPF6 which is in line with the lowest charge transfer resistance confirmed by the EIS. The relaxation time constant of PProDOT–Br is found to be shorter for Et4NBF4, Et4NPF6, and NaClO4, than those of PProDOT–OBz as a representative of bulky substituents in the same electrolyte, and which also indicates that the full capacitance of the cell can be reached a better fast charging time within Et4NBF4 and Et4NPF6 rather than NaClO4 electrolyte systems.

The structural derivatives of ProDOTs in different electrolytes studied here can be used as a model for interfaces studies, while understanding the interfacial interactions is crucial to optimize the charge carriers and key parameters in organic electronic devices. This study also elucidates further on controlling the charge transport parameters by substituting functional groups and anion doping in polyalkylenedioxythiophene structures. Tuning charge carriers by substituting functional groups and anion doping in PPrODOTs by varying functionalization are shown to have a predominant impact on the electrochemical properties of investigated polymers, and their functionalities are reported to shape the energy level related properties of these polymers, with particular reference to n- and p-doping induced charged states.
