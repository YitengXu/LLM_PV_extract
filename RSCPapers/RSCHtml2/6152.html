Abstract

Polymer-based electronic packaging materials (such as epoxy resin, polydimethylsiloxane, and polyvinyl alcohol) are widely used in chips because they have the merits of light weight, corrosion resistance, electrical insulation and ease of processing. With the rapid improvement in the integration and power density of chips in the past decade, electronic packaging materials are required to have excellent thermal conductivity, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference compatibility (e.g., electromagnetic wave shielding and absorption). Unfortunately, most polymers have low thermal conductivity and are highly electromagnetic wave-transmittable and flammable. Thus, the development of polymer-based electronic packaging materials with high thermal conductivity, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference performance are highly desirable for next generation electronic devices. In this perspective, we presented the heat conduction mechanism of polymer-based composites and the emerging approaches to enhance the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites (such as constructing 3D thermally conductive networks). In addition, we also summarized the mechanism and recent progress in anti-electromagnetic interference and flame retardancy studies for these highly thermally conductive polymer-based composites, including compositing carbon materials with ceramics to achieve heat conduction and anti-electromagnetic interference or modifying ceramics with nitrogen and phosphorus compounds to improve heat conductivity and flame retardancy.

1. Introduction

With the advance of semiconductor and electronic technology (e.g., 3D stereo packaging technology), electronic devices in the 5G/6G era are moving toward miniaturization, high integration, light weight and high power.1 Thus, a significant amount of waste heat is generated and accumulated during the operation of electronic devices, exposing the electronic devices to thermal failure and spontaneous combustion risk.2 In general, heat can be released from electronic devices into the environment mainly through four processes: firstly, heat is transferred inside the devices; secondly, the heat is transferred between the devices and radiators, and then through the radiators; finally, the heat is transferred from the radiators to the ambient environment.3 Due to the narrow space and poor air circulation in electronic devices, it is difficult for waste heat to conduct or radiate to the outside of the equipment on time, leading to an increase in device temperature, degradation in performance, and an increase in the failure rate.4 For example, about half of all microelectronic packaged device failures or events of devices on fire are temperature-related. Therefore, heat dissipation and flame retardancy are quite urgent for electronics, such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs), ultrahigh-voltage electronic devices, integrated circuits, electric machineries, etc.,5 which has sparked widespread interest in materials with high thermal conductivity and flame retardancy.6 In addition, the widespread application of electronic devices with the continuous development of information technology and electronic communications makes electromagnetic pollution an unignorable issue that researchers both local and abroad must pay great attention to. Since the electromagnetic wave radiated by electronic devices not only affects the normal operation of the electronic devices, but also harms the human body, it is highly desirable to develop materials that exhibit excellent heat dissipation, flame retardancy and suppression of electromagnetic interference (e.g., electromagnetic wave shielding and absorption) performance.

In order to meet the demands of electronics, materials with high thermal conductivity are often required to have the characteristics of ease of processing, electrical insulation and good mechanical properties.5 In general, carbon7 and metal8 materials have high thermal conductivity and electromagnetic wave shielding performance, but they are electrically conductive. Insulated metal oxides9 and ceramics10 have relatively high thermal conductivity and flame retardancy, but they exhibit high density and high processing cost. Polymers (such as EP, polydimethylsiloxane, polyvinyl alcohol, etc.) which have the merits of low cost, light weight, corrosion resistance, electrical insulation and ease of processing11 are ideal materials for electronic device packaging.12 However, phonons are the dominant conduction carriers for most polymers, which causes polymers to exhibit poor thermal conductivity, because of their amorphous arrangement and vibration of molecular chains.13 Besides, polymers are also highly flammable and good electromagnetic wave-transmitting materials because there is no matter that can absorb or reflect electromagnetic waves. Thus, it is urgent to develop polymer-based composites with high thermal conductivity, anti-electromagnetic interference and flame retardancy.12

Recent studies concerning polymer-based composites have been widely reported, but they just focused on strategies for enhancing the thermal conductivity of polymers and did not report on the multifunctional design and strategies for the compatibility of heat conduction and anti-electromagnetic interference, heat conduction and flame retardancy, or even the compatibility of heat conduction, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference.14,15 Therefore, it is extremely significant to systematically review the progress and predict the prospect for polymer-based composites with concurrently high thermal conductivity, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference properties.1620 Herein, as presented in Fig. 1, we focused on the heat conduction mechanism of polymer-based composites, as well as methods for enhancing the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites, such as improving the crystallinity of polymers, adding thermally conductive fillers into the polymer matrix and constructing 3D thermally conductive networks. In addition, we also summarized the mechanism and recent progress in anti-electromagnetic interference and flame retardancy studies for these highly conductive polymer-based composites, including compositing carbon materials with ceramic materials to achieve thermal conductivity and anti-electromagnetic interference compatibility and modifying ceramic materials with nitrogen and phosphorus compounds to improve their thermal conductivity and flame retardancy. Finally, we pointed out the multifunctional design of polymer-based composites, such as the compatibility of heat conduction and anti-electromagnetic interference, heat conduction and flame retardancy, compatibility of heat conduction, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference should be the trend for polymer-based composites in the future.

2.3 Heat conduction mechanism of polymer-based composites

For most organic polymers, the heat transfer process mainly relies on lattice vibration, and phonons are the main heat-transferring carriers. However, it is extremely difficult for organic molecules to move and form perfect crystals due to the random entanglement of internal molecular chains.32 Adding high thermally conductive fillers into the organic polymer matrix is the most effective method at present to address the low thermal conductivity of polymers.33,34 Therefore, the improvement of the conductivity of polymers depends on thermally conductive fillers.3537 There are three theories to explain the thermal conduction mechanism of polymer-based composites: heat conduction path theory, percolation theory and thermal elasticity coefficient theory.
2.3.1 Heat conduction path theory. At present, the theory of heat conduction path is the mainstream.38 The thermal conductivity of thermally conductive polymer-based composites is determined by the synergistic effect between the polymer matrix and thermally conductive fillers. When the loading of thermally conductive fillers is low, they are dispersed and isolated in the matrix without contact (Fig. 2a), which makes it difficult to form thermally conductive paths and to improve the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites. With an increase in the loading of the thermally conductive fillers, thermally conductive fillers come into contact with each other and form heat conduction paths in the matrix, which significantly improve the thermal conductivity of the polymer-based composites (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, the continuous increase in the loading of the thermally conductive fillers will make the thermally conductive paths intertwined and twisted to form a thermally conductive network, which leads to further improvement and stabilization of the thermal conductivity of the composites (Fig. 2c).

2.3.2 Heat conduction percolation theory. Percolation theory39 shows that the electrical conductivity or thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites changes sharply near the percolation transition point, increasing hundreds or even thousands of times. The use of percolation theory to explain the electrically conductive behavior of polymer-based composites has been very successful, but using it to explain the thermally conductive behavior of polymer-based composites has encountered problems: for polymer-based composites, it is difficult to observe thermal percolation, and thermal percolation exists only in polymer-based composites filled with graphene nanosheets, carbon nanotubes or other particles. Researchers believe that this is mainly due to the electrical conductivity difference of 1091016 times between electrically conductive particles and the polymer matrix, while the difference in thermal conductivity between thermally conductive particles and the polymer matrix is only 10103 times. Such a small difference in thermal conductivity between thermally conductive particles and the polymer is not enough to produce thermal percolation. In order to produce thermal percolation, the thermal conductivity of thermally conductive particles needs to be high enough. Therefore, the theory of thermal percolation has always been controversial when explaining the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites.
2.3.3 Thermal elasticity coefficient theory. The variation law of the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites is extremely similar to the elastic coefficient and modulus in classical vibration and elasticity. Therefore, the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites can be regarded as the thermal elastic coefficient of the thermal vibration transmission process.40 The greater thermal conductivity of thermally conductive fillers leads to the greater thermal elastic coefficient and contributes to a higher transmission efficiency of thermal vibration. The thermal conductivity improvement of polymer-based composites can be regarded as the reinforcement effect between high thermally conductive fillers and the polymer matrix.

The thermal conductivity improvement of polymer-based composites by the filling of thermally conductive fillers follows the combined enhancement mechanism of the thermal elasticity coefficient of the polymer-based composites. The thermal conductivity of the polymer-based composites will gradually increase with no sudden growth, with the increase of the loading of thermally conductive fillers.

While the theories of heat conduction path and thermal elasticity coefficient can well explain the influence of the loading of thermally conductive fillers on the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites, it is difficult to explain the influence of the filler size, morphology and any other factors like the synergistic effect of the combination of different sizes and morphologies on the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites. Therefore, it is urgent to develop thermal conduction mechanism theories for polymer-based composites.

Therefore, polymers usually have low thermal conductivity, whose values are all below 0.5 W m1 K1 (Table 1). The choice of the polymer matrix will affect the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites.14 The polymer matrix is selected according to application requirements. The thermal conductivity of polymers is highly related to their microstructure. The thermal conductivity of polymers can be enhanced in two ways.74 The first way to improve the thermal conductivity of polymers is to optimize the structure of the polymers by introducing large conjugated bonds75 to their structures such as polyacetylene, polypyrrole, polyaniline and so on because this molecular structure can conduct heat through an electronic thermal conduction mechanism. Polymers with conjugated -bonds have higher thermal conductivity, because -bonds contain electrons, which can serve as heat carriers.76 However, there is a problem that this thermally conductive material is not insulated. The second method66 to enhance the thermal conductivity of polymers is to improve the phonon heat-conducting mechanism without changing the lattice vibration heat-transfer mechanism inside the polymer, by improving the crystallinity inside the polymers through the action of external forces. Polyphenylene sulfide (PPS) has higher thermal conductivity due to its rigid backbone, which can prohibit the rotation of polymer chains and improve the transmission of phonons.77 In order to further improve the thermal conductivity of polymers, it is critical to optimize the microstructure of the polymers to improve the crystallinity of the polymers and introduce conductive groups.78

3.2.2 Additive morphology. The morphology of thermally conductive fillers has an important effect on the improvement of the thermal conductivity of thermally conductive polymer-based composites. The effect of the thermally conductive filler morphology on the thermal conductivity of thermally conductive polymer-based composites has been widely studied.9599 Polymer-based composites with the combination of different morphologies of thermally conductive fillers can have a great influence on its thermal conductivity enhancement. The synergistic effect of hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN) with cubic boron nitride (c-BN) on the enhancement of the thermal conductivity of epoxy resin composites was studied.100 As shown in Fig. 4b, epoxy resin composites using h-BN as the thermally conductive filler achieved higher thermal conductivity than those with c-BN, which showed that two-dimensional materials could play a greater role in improving the thermal conductivity of epoxy resin composites compared with three-dimensional materials. It can also be seen from Fig. 4b that the combination of h-BN and c-BN is more beneficial to enhance the thermal conductivity of epoxy resin composites due to their synergistic effect on forming a thermally conductive network. When the filler type is determined, it is more effective to choose a combination of two or more different morphologies of thermally conductive fillers (Fig. 4a), such as one-dimensional and three-dimensional, one-dimensional and two-dimensional, two-dimensional and three-dimensional, or even one-dimensional, two-dimensional and three-dimensional thermally conductive fillers to improve the thermal conductivity of polymer composites. The synergistic effect of one-dimensional carbon fibers and two-dimensional graphite sheets on the improvement of the thermal conductivity of epoxy resin composites was also studied.101 The results showed that, after adding 17.48 vol% one-dimensional carbon fibers and only 6.34 vol% two-dimensional graphite sheets, the epoxy resin composite showed high cross-plane thermal conductivity up to 6.2 W (m K)1, which was attributed to the formation of a thermally conductive network composed of one-dimensional carbon fibers and two-dimensional graphite sheets. By adding 27.69 vol% GNPs and 0.57 vol% CNTs, the polymer composite could achieve a high thermal conductivity of 7.69 W m1 K1 through the synergistic effect of graphene nanoplatelets (GNPs) and carbon nanotubes (CNTs) on the enhancement of the thermal conductivity of the thermal interface material.102 Nevertheless, there was an optimum fraction of thermally conductive fillers to improve the thermal conductivity of polymer composites. Therefore, there was an optimum synergistic effect of two-dimensional GNPs and one-dimensional CNTs at the optimum filler loading. Natural, artificial and expanded graphite were used to improve the thermal conductivity of polysulfone.103 The results showed it was natural graphite that could improve the thermal conductivity of polysulfone better than artificial and expanded graphite. 3.2.4 Additive size. Thermally conductive filler size is another factor that must be considered in order to improve the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites. When the morphology and type are selected, choosing fillers with different sizes is better for improving the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites. By adding 50 wt% BA-functionalized BN at a 2:8 ratio of 2 m BN and 10 m BN, the thermal conductivity of the epoxy resin composite reached 2.71 W m1 K1. As can be seen, the combination of thermally conductive fillers of different sizes is an effective way to enhance the thermal conductivity of polymers.

3.3 Polymeradditive interface modification

The interface thermal resistance between the filler and polymer matrix is another important problem.124 Surface functional modification of fillers using surfactants, coupling agents and functional polymers can effectively improve the binding force between the fillers and polymers, as well as reduce the thermal resistance of the interfacial contact, improve the compatibility of the fillers and polymers and the dispersion of fillers in the polymer matrix and build an effective thermally conductive network under low loading of fillers, which is an effective way to improve the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites.125 The modification of fillers can be covalent or non-covalent.126 The former method is to introduce hydroxyl groups on the surface of fillers by ball milling, strong acid and alkali treatment, and high temperature treatment; then the organic long links are branched to the surface of the fillers through the hydrolysis of a silane coupling agent, titanate coupling agent, aluminate coupling agent, etc. The latter method is to introduce organic long links on the surface of fillers by electrostatic interactions, interactions, etc. such as polydopamine,127 benzoic acid108 and epicophenol and polymeryl acrylic.128 Recently, polydopamine (PDA) was adopted to modify BN, after which Ag was deposited on its surface (Fig. 7).129 PDA made the silicone rubber (SR) matrix and fillers tightly connected. Ag and BN can form better heat conduction paths to improve the thermal conductivity of the SR composite. By adding functionalized BN into the SR matrix, its thermal conductivity reached 0.75 W m1 K1 at a filler loading of 30 vol%. h-BN was modified by lignosulfonate to promote its dispersion in the polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) matrix and thus a PVA composite with a thermal conductivity of 1.22 W m1 K1 was attained.130

4 Multifunctional design of thermally conductive polymer-based composites

4.1 Thermally conductive and flame retardant polymer-based composites

The performance of flame retardancy is as important as the ability to conduct heat. Recently, researchers are working to develop flame-retardant polymers and polymer-based composites to enhance their flame retardancy, so as to reduce the risk of fire and mitigate the impact of related adverse consequences.156162
4.1.1 Flame retardant mechanism. When the polymer is exposed to fire, it will undergo degradation which includes a series of processes and steps.163166 It begins with physical degradation under the action of an external heat source. The temperature of the polymer will rise until it reaches the critical ignition temperature. In addition, the polymer loses its mechanical strength and begins to soften. Then, with the release of heat, chemical degradation occurs, and the low-energy bonds of polymer molecules break into small molecules and smoke and gas are generated, which are composed of a mixture of combustible and noncombustible gases, carbon and other particles. The decomposition temperature depends on the flame atmosphere and the chemical properties of the polymer. The essence of the flame retardancy of polymer-based composites is to inhibit the decomposition of polymers.

Metal hydroxide flame retardants are mainly Al(OH)3 and Mg(OH)2.167 For example, when the environmental temperature is 180200 C, Al(OH)3 begins to absorb heat, decomposes into A12O3 and also produces steam without combustion characteristics, which dilutes O2 and helps to take away the heat of systems.

Common phosphorus flame retardants include ammonium polyphosphate, aluminum hypophosphite, microencapsulated red phosphorus, etc.168170 They are well compatible with polymers and can catalyze the conversion of combustibles into carbon during combustion, which can isolate the transmission of heat and air, and protect the inner polymer from combustion.

Melamine, melamine polyphosphate and dicyandiamide are nitrogen flame retardants.168170 They have the advantages of low toxicity, low pollution and low price. Nitrogen flame retardants will release non-combustible gases such as N2 and NH3 during combustion, which can dilute the concentration of O2 on the surface of the polymer and absorb the heat generated by combustion. Therefore, nitrogen flame retardants are often combined with other flame retardants for intumescent flame retardants.

Carbon flame retardants are mainly expandable graphite (EG), graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), C60, etc. Carbon flame retardants can not only improve the carbon formation rate, but also protect the unburned polymer matrix by forming a continuous and dense carbon layer.171 CNTs and carbon black can absorb highly active free radicals and interrupt the chain reaction of the combustion process. EG is an inorganic intumescent flame retardant. When heated, it can expand and form a worm-like structural layer, which is covered on the surface of the material to block combustion. It is often compounded with other flame retardants to prepare intumescent flame retardants.

Intumescent flame retardants are mainly composed of phosphorus and nitrogen, including the acid source, carbon source and gas source.172,173 They can form a porous expanded carbon layer during combustion, which acts as a barrier and protects the internal polymer. The decomposition of the acid source (phosphate, borate and phosphate ester) after heating promotes the dehydration reaction between the carbon source and the polymer matrix to form a carbonization layer. The carbon source is generally a compound with high carbon content, such as pentaerythritol, phenolic resin, etc. The gas source (urea, melamine, cyanurate) is the non-combustible gas released during combustion, which expands the carbon layer and forms a closed and fluffy structure. Compounding can improve the flame-retardant performance, heat stability and mechanical properties of the composites at the same time, according to the advantages of different flame retardants.174177

4.2 Thermally conductive and anti-electromagnetic-interference polymer-based composites

Fabricating multifunctional polymer-based composites of high thermally conductive properties and excellent anti-electromagnetic-interference performance is highly urgent to meet the requirements of consumers and a sustainable society.181 The design of materials with high anti-electromagnetic-interference performance has in the recent years become a research hotspot.182 According to the structural design, the materials can be classified into four categories: uniform structure, isolation structure, multi-layer structure and foam structure. Composites with a uniform structure can be directly made by blending a conductor filler and an insulating resin and then processing and molding by opening mixing, internal mixing, injection molding and so on. Electrical conductors such as Fe, Cu, Au, and Ag are good anti-electromagnetic-interference and thermally conductive materials, and their thermal conductivity is determined by their electrons, phonons and electronphonon coupling,183185 while their anti-electromagnetic-interference performance is determined by the reflection and absorption of electromagnetic waves caused by electronelectron interactions (i.e., Coulomb interactions) and electronphonon interactions.186,187 Therefore, these materials with high thermal and electrical conductivity show great application potential in anti-electromagnetic interference and thermal management fields.
4.2.1 Mechanism of anti-electromagnetic interference. The anti-electromagnetic-interference mechanism183 is presented in Fig. 19. When an electromagnetic wave radiates to the surface of the shielding material, part of the incident wave is reflected and dissipated at the interface of the shielding layer and returns to the outside world; the other part passes through the surface of the shielding layer and goes inside the anti-electromagnetic-interference material, which is dissipated after multiple reflections on the inner surface of the material. The remaining electromagnetic wave will be transmitted through the material and return to the free space outside. Therefore, anti-electromagnetic interference can be realized by electromagnetic wave shielding and electromagnetic wave absorption.

The electrical conductivity, permeability and thickness of materials are three basic factors affecting the shielding effectiveness.183 The electromagnetic shielding and heat dissipation that exist in highly integrated electronic devices can be well addressed by conductive polymer-based composites (CPCs).208 MXenes with excellent metallic conductivity and interactions with polymers are favorable for electromagnetic wave shielding, which are called two-dimensional transition metal carbides and/or nitrides,182,209212 such as Ti3C2Tx.213216 Metallic materials (such as Cu, Ag, etc.) and carbon materials (such as CF, graphene, CNT, etc.) are also good electromagnetic shielding materials.56 Cellulose/MWCNT composite films with high thermal conductivity by filtration and drying were fabricated, whose best electromagnetic interference shielding effectiveness was 49.2 dB only with a thickness of 0.3 mm.181

5 Conclusions and outlooks

The development of polymer-based composites with high thermal conductivity, flame retardancy and anti-electromagnetic interference performance is highly desirable for next generation electronic devices. However, there are still some problems to be addressed for thermally conductive polymer-based composites for thermal management in the electronic packaging field: (1) more models should be developed to explain the synergistic effect of different sizes of thermally conductive fillers on the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites; (2) more models should be developed to explain the synergistic effect of different morphologies of thermally conductive fillers on the thermal conductivity of polymer-based composites; (3) new filler modification methods such as non-covalent modification to substitute covalent modification which is very complicated should be developed to address the dispersion of fillers in the polymer matrix and interaction with the polymer matrix; (4) environment-friendly, non-toxic and harmless flame retardants with a good flame retardant effect and low dosage such as BN modified by nitrogen and phosphorus groups to substitute halogenated compounds should be developed; (5) structural design to realize integrated development of heat conduction, anti-electromagnetic interference and flame retardancy should be developed.
