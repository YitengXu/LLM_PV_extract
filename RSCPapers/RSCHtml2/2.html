Abstract

The metal–organic complexes of Sc, Y, La and lanthanides, which were tested as luminescent materials in organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs), are collected. The performances of the devices are given. Advantages and drawbacks of organic derivatives of rare earth metals as emissive materials are considered.

Introduction

Complexes of rare earth metals with organic ligands present an astounding class of emissive materials for OLEDs due to peculiarities of their luminescence properties.1–7 As defined by IUPAC, rare earth metals are a collection of seventeen chemical elements in the periodic table, namely scandium, yttrium, and the fifteen lanthanides.8 Most of the studies on these electroluminescent complexes have been limited to lanthanide compounds thanks to their unique ability to emit narrow line-like emission bands in the visible and near-infrared spectral regions. These properties of Ln3+ ions are stipulated by the existence of 4f electrons which are efficiently shielded from external influences by the overlaying 5s2 and 5p6 orbitals. It is well known that the ligands in the first and second coordination sphere perturb the electronic configurations of the lanthanide ions only to a limited extent. The f–f transitions are parity and, sometimes, spin forbidden and consist mainly of weak magnetic dipole and induced electric dipole parts. The intensities of the first type are practically not influenced by the coordinating surroundings of the ion, whereas those of the second one are quite sensitive to it.9 Therefore selection of organic ligands for luminescent lanthanide complexes is especially important. In most cases this role is played by O,O- or O,N-chelate groups with extended conjugated systems. In such compounds the luminescence of lanthanide ions is explained commonly by the ligand-to-metal energy transfer from the lowest triplet level of the ligands to a resonance level of the lanthanide ion.10,11 Recently, efforts also have been started in the utilization of other rare earth metals that have no f electrons (Sc, Y, La) or a completely filled f shell (Lu). The study of these electroluminescent complexes is not only of fundamental interest, but has a high potential for applications in OLEDs.

The simplified diagram in Fig. 1 shows the energy migration paths in rare earth complexes. The metallo-complex may undergo a radiative transition from the singlet state S1 to the ground state S0 (molecular fluorescence). The excited S1 state can be deactivated nonradiatively to the triplet T1 and then radiatively to the ground state S0 (molecular phosphorescence). The complex may undergo nonradiative transitions from the singlet or triplet states to an excited state of the metal ion (in the case of lanthanide). Additionally, recent theoretical computations indicate that the ligand-to-metal energy transfers also include the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).12 Due to the competition of radiative and nonradiative transitions, it is possible to observe some combination of ligand- and metal-centered emissions.

In general, three main types of electronic transitions are considered for rare earth compounds. The first, the metal-centered f–f transitions of the lanthanide ions, have been partially described above. The block of these metals can be divided on two parts: fluorescent (e.g. Pr(III), Nd(III), Ho(III), Er(III) and Yb(III) emitting in the NIR region), and phosphorescent (e.g. orange Sm(III), red Eu(III), ultraviolet Gd(III), green Tb(III), yellow Dy(III) and blue Tm(III)).13 Note that according to the IUPAC rules the term “fluorescence” refers to processes that occur without spin change (e.g. for Yb (2F5/2→2F7/2)), and “phosphorescence” refers to transitions involving a change in spin (e.g. for Eu (5D4→7FJ)).8

The second, also metal-centered, f–d transition, involves the promotion of a 4f electron into the 5d subshell meaning all transitions become spin-allowed. Generally, they occur at energies which are much higher than those of f–f transitions. Their energy depends largely upon the metal environment because the 5d orbitals are external and interact directly with the ligand orbitals.13 In addition, it has been pointed out (for Ce3+ complexes) that the metal-centered f–d transition can be viewed also as a metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) transition.14

Finally, ligand-centered or charge-transfer transitions represent the third type of electronic conversions. As a general rule, LMCT states are considered for the complexes of lanthanide ions, having high electron affinity, with organic ligands, which have low oxidation potentials. Recently it has been established that LMCT states also play an important role in the luminescence of Sc complexes. These transitions are allowed and as the π* ligand orbital is mainly delocalized over the acceptor ligand, the charge-transfer states may cause minimum structural distortion and facilitate the energy transfer efficiency from ligands to the metal ion.

An intermixture of various transitions in the complexes with complicated molecular structures is common and can play a crucial role for decent luminescence efficiency. The relative ordering of these transitions may be changed in several ways: by selection of the metal, choice of the ligand, modification of the ligand or by changing the geometry of the complex. Generally speaking, to achieve efficient emission a rare earth ion needs a carefully tailored environment consisting of organic ligands that simultaneously provide a rigid and protective coordination shell for minimizing non-radiative deactivation and ensuring effective population the metal ion excited states through the energy transfer.

However, the luminescent efficiency of metallo-complexes is the only mandatory part, but it is not enough for the design of organic light-emitting devices. The main differentiation between electroluminescence and photoluminescence is the excitation method: an emission by recombination of electrons and holes under the influence of an electric field versus an emission after excitation by irradiation with electromagnetic radiation. The basic and simplified structure of an OLED device (Fig. 2) consists of a stack of thin organic layers. It typically contains a hole-transporting layer, an emitting layer and an electron-transporting layer sandwiched between an anode (such as ITO) and a metallic cathode. In some cases, in state-of-the-art devices, additional hole- and exciton-blocking layers are used.


Fig. 2 The basic structure of organic light emitting diodes.

Under the action of a driving voltage, the electrons are injected from a metal cathode with a low work function into the electronic state corresponding to the LUMO of the neighboring layer material. Still further the electrons hop via the LUMO states of molecules towards the anode. In the mean time the holes are injected from an anode into the HOMO of the neighboring layer. And still further the holes hop via the HOMO states of molecules towards the cathode. In the emissive layer the electrons and the holes recombine and form excitons. In suitable cases, this leads to a population of excited states of the emitter which subsequently emits light.15 Therefore, for the design of efficient electroluminescent devices it is necessary to estimate such requirements as a good adjustment of work functions of the electrodes relative to the HOMO/LUMO of the neighboring organic layers, a good alignment of HOMO and LUMO levels of the different organic layers relative to each others, a high and balanced electron and hole transport mobility, etc.

The aim of this review is to describe the known classes of trivalent rare earth metal coordination complexes possessing electroluminescence. As a detailed consideration in a short review of all known electroluminescent complexes with organic ligands seems to be an impossible task we have included in the paper only the most efficient of them. The less efficient compounds, as well as polymeric and dendrimeric lanthanide luminophores, remain beyond the scope of this article. The general molecular architectures of rare earth metal complexes are sketched in the corresponding sections, each of which is devoted to one metal. Ancillary neutral ligands in the lanthanide complexes are classified by capital letters as depicted in Fig. 3. Scandium

Recently it was found that the performances of OLED devices based on scandium 8-oxyquinolinate 1(Sc) were significantly enhanced compared with those of analogous device with an Alq3 (q = 8-oxyquinolinate) emitting layer, which is commonly used as a standard.16 Both the current–voltage and luminance–voltage characteristics in the case of 1(Sc) shift to lower driving voltage.16 The turn-on voltage (3.8 V) for the Sc-containing diode is also lower than the value for the devices with Alq3 (4.8 V).16 The current efficiency and power efficiency increase more than double on going from Al to Sc.16 Such improved performances were explained by enhanced charge mobility of 1(Sc). In addition, the good performance of 1(Sc)-based device was obtained for utilizing Yb metal (with low work-function 2.59 eV) as a cathode instead of Al (4.31 eV).17,18 Thereby, it was confirmed that lowering the energy barrier between the Fermi level of the cathode metal and LUMO of the emitting layer facilitates efficient electron injection and improves device performance. Another noticeable difference to the standard device includes the new synthetic method of preparation of rare earth 8-oxyquinolinate complexes using the chemical reaction of rare earth silylamides with 8-hydroxyquinoline.19 This method affords neutral mononuclear water- and acid residue-free compounds in high yields. The rare earth 8-oxyquinolinates synthesized by the traditional water–alcohol method have an essentially more complicated structure than the simple Lnq3.19,20 As a rule these substances contain coordinated water or acid residues, which greatly impair their luminescent properties and make them inapplicable for the design of OLEDs.

Particular interest has been paid to the influence of the substituents at the C2 position of 8-oxyquinolate ligand on the electroluminescent properties of corresponding complexes 2(Sc), 3(Sc), and 4(Sc).21 The performances of the device with methyl-substituted 2(Sc) are very similar to those of 1(Sc)-based analogue. The complex with nitrile-substituted ligands 3(Sc) exhibits a red-shift (λmax = 615 nm), while amino-substituted derivative 4(Sc) reveals a blue-shift (λmax = 495 nm) and both display significantly lower electroluminescent efficiency than 1(Sc) and 2(Sc).21

As will be presented below, the majority of electroluminescent rare earth metal complexes consists of the β-diketonate species and its analogues. Recently we have found a new type of luminescent compound of rare earth elements containing heterocyclic ligands.22 One of these complexes is 5(Sc) in which three flat 2-mercaptobenzothiazolate ligands surround the metal center in η2-fashion via N and exocyclic S atoms. On the basis of DFT calculations it was found that the three HOMOs of the complex are ligand-localized while the LUMOs contain a significant metal d contribution. In relation to the emission properties, the electroluminescent peak at 550 nm can be assigned to a LMCT transition.23 Lately, prominent success has been achieved with the exploitation of heterocyclic ligands 2-(2-benzoimidazol-2-yl)phenolate, 2-(2-benzoxyazol-2-yl)phenolate, 2-(2-benzothiazol-2-yl)phenolate to synthesize a series of new air-stable volatile scandium complexes 6(Sc), 7(Sc), and 8(Sc), suitable for preparation of OLEDs.24 In contrast to the most frequently applied dual or multiple dopants in multilayer white OLED devices, all simpler devices with the configuration of ITO/TPD/neat Scandium complex/Yb exhibit near-white light with a blue hue (CIEx,y = 0.2147; 0.2379) in the case of 6(Sc), cyan hue (0.2702; 0.3524) for 7(Sc) and yellowish hue (0.3468; 0.4284) for 8(Sc). X-Ray investigation of 6(Sc) and 7(Sc) revealed that the Sc atom in these compounds is coordinated by three bidentate ligands via N and exocyclic O atoms and located in distorted octahedral geometry. The intermolecular π–π stacking interactions with the distances varied between 3.412 Å for 6(Sc) and 3.357 Å for 7(Sc), which approaches the 3.35 Å between the layers of graphite. This has led to the good carrier transport properties.24 Among the tested scandium complexes, 7(Sc) has shown the highest performance (Table 1).

Yttrium

Yttrium complexes as electroluminophores have very seldom been studied. Only two known compounds 1(Y)16 and 5(Y)25 tested as emitting materials for OLED design. The 8-quinolinolate complex 1(Y) has shown moderate electroluminescent characteristics comparable to that of the Alq3-based analogue and nearly half those of 1(Sc).16 The mercaptobenzothiazolate 5(Y), similar to 5(Sc), turned out to be not sufficiently bright but compared with 5(Sc) it exhibits a red shift emission at 600 nm.25

Another example of Y used in an OLED device is a mixture of Y and Eu β-diketonates, in which the Y component plays the role of a sensitizer. It was hypothesized that the presence of the Y3+ ion promotes energy transfer from the triplet level of the ligand to the europium ion, resulting in an increase in the emission intensity.26 Noticeable enhancement of luminance was also found when Y was added to Tb carboxylates.27

Lanthanum

Data on application of La complexes in OLED technology is even poorer than those on Y compounds. An attempt to utilize 8-oxyquinolinate complex 1(La) in a simple two-layer device ITO/TPD/1(La)/Yb revealed that this compound is luminescently silent.28

Cerium, praseodymium, and neodymium

Based on the energy level schemes one can assume that there are only three trivalent lanthanides (Ce3+, Pr3+, Nd3+) for which allowed f–d emission can be observed. For all other lanthanides fast relaxation from the lowest f–d state to the 4fn states is expected, resulting in sharp line emission.9 In fact, this trio can exhibit a broad emission band in the UV-vis region, but really only Ce3+ is characterized by strong f–d transitions. This indicates that the overlap between the 4fn−1 core and the delocalized 5d electron decreases with n. Generally, the emission of Ce3+ ion occurs in the UV and/or in the blue spectral region and can be red-shifted depending on the ligand environment.29 Moreover, in some cases the organic ligands in cerium complexes quench the luminescence of metal ion. That is why the design of efficient electroluminescent cerium complexes is a very complicated task and examples of such compounds are limited. In the case of praseodymium, two single electrons in the 4f orbital of metal ion give rise to a series of divided electronic states and as a result a luminescence covering the ultraviolet-visible-NIR wavelength range.9 It is considered to be that neodymium is a unique element as an emission center for the construction of a laser system because the four-level energy structure of neodymium is ideal for the laser transition.30 The performances of OLED devices based on emitting Ce, Pr, and Nd complexes are given in Table 2.

Cerium

Up till now there are only two reports about UV emission OLED with the electroluminescent layer composed of the cerium complexes 9(Ce)31 and 10(Ce).32 These compounds are based on a cationic cerium part balanced by a negative counterion. The molecular structure of the 9(Ce) established by X-ray analysis confirms that the central Ce3+ ion is hosted in the cavity formed by two tripodal ligands arranged face-to-face. The CF3SO3− counteranions and solvated molecules have no direct interactions with the central Ce3+ ion. Note that ultraviolet emission at 376 nm has been shown only by 10(Ce) in which the Ce3+ ion is coordinated by six oxygen atoms of crown ether molecule.32 The 9(Ce) complex exhibits deep blue luminescence (CIE 0.18, 0.21) with moderate efficiency.

Praseodymium

The β-diketonate complex 11(Pr)LO33 demonstrates a first successful application of praseodymium complexes in an OLED. The device exhibits a maximum luminance of 50 cd m−2 at 18 V and presents a set of bands in visible and near IR range at 608 nm (1D2→3H6), 890 nm (1D2→3F2), 1015 nm (1D2→3F3), 1065 nm (1D2→3F4) and 1550 nm (1D2→1G4).

Neodymium

The 8-oxyquinolinate 1(Nd) exhibits infrared luminescence centered at 900, 1060, and 1320 nm, which corresponds to the 4F3/2→4I9/2, 4F3/2→4I11/2, 4F3/2→4I13/2 transitions.34 A structurally-pure neodymium tetrakis complex 12(Nd)35 yields devices that show a NIR irradiance of 2.0 nW mm−2 at 25 mA mm−2. X-Ray crystallographic analysis of this complex indicated a nonplanar molecular structure of a trivalent neodymium ion surrounded by four bidentate 5,7-dichloro-8-hydroxyquinoline ligands. No solvent molecules are present in the coordination sphere.35 One of the most efficient Nd emitters was found to be the complex 13(Nd)36 It has been demonstrated that the employment an aromatic ketone as a ligand sensitizer of 13(Nd) lead to NIR emission with a maximum irradiance of 8.5 nW mm−2 and an external quantum efficiency of 0.007%. The diketonate complex 11(Nd)LO37 exhibits the electroluminescence at 890, 1070 and 1350 nm. Note that the relative intensities of these peaks were different from those observed in the photoluminescence; the intensity of the transitions at the long wavelength in the electroluminescent spectrum was lower than those in the photoluminescent spectrum.37

Promethium

It is well known that promethium does not occur naturally on the earth, and must be handled with great care because of its high radioactivity.38 Due to this high radioactivity promethium salts shine in the dark with a pale blue or greenish glow. The beta particles emitted by the decay of promethium would be converted into electricity by a device similar to a solar cell and this type of battery could provide power for five years. It is not surprising that publications about OLED based on Pm compounds are absent.

Most of samarium derivatives used in OLEDs belongs to a class of β-diketonates. Usually they reveal orange emission consisting of three main peaks near 563, 598 and 643 nm corresponding to the 4G5/2→6H5/2, 4G5/2→6H7/2 and 4G5/2→6H9/2 transitions, respectively. Note that a lot of europium complexes has been synthesized during the last decade with the aim of exploiting their electroluminescent properties. For this review we have tried to select the most efficient and promising examples. It is well known that the simple homoleptic β-diketonate europium complexes show a highly efficient photoluminescence but due to a poor carrier-transport ability they do not satisfy the requirements to OLED materials. Thus 11(Eu),4814(Eu),49 and 15(Eu)50 compounds exhibit luminance below 10 cd m−2. To improve these properties, two ways of modification have been developed: (i) introduction of charge-transport substituents to the basic ligands and (ii) introduction to a complex of ancillary ligand enhancing an emission of a metal atom, for instance, derivatives of 1,10-phenanthroline. With the example of complex 11(Eu),51–73 it has been shown that among the ancillary ligands (A, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, O, P, T, R, U, V, and W) the best results (luminance around 1000 cd m−2) were achieved with D, O, P, R, U, and V. For 15(Eu),52,67,74–81 among the ancillary ligands (B, C, E, H, K, L, M, N, P, S, W, and X) the best results were achieved with P, S, and X. Note that the modification of triphenylphosphine oxide W (rather the application of the chelate phosphine oxide X instead of monodentate W) significantly improves the electroluminescent properties: maximal brightness enhances from 10 cd m−2 for 15(Eu)LW279 to 634 cd m−2 for 15(Eu)LX.81 The high efficiency can arise from the rigid structure of X, which facilitates the triplet energy transport from ligands to the metal ion.81 On the other hand, the comparison of four different europium complexes 11(Eu)LC, 14(Eu)LC, 15(Eu)LC, and 25(Eu)LC with bipyridine as an ancillary ligand, has shown that 11(Eu)LC containing dibenzoylmethane as the anionic ligands possesses the highest efficiency while the naphthyl derivative 25(Eu)LC has the lowest one.52 The complexes 26(Eu)LK,8327(Eu)LK,8728(Eu)LK,87 and 29(Eu)LK88 with the longest conjugative ligands also result in a less efficient device. Whereas the polyfluoration on the alkyl group of the ligand and the introduction of the conjugate naphthyl group into the ligand in 18(Eu)LK led to a very efficient device based on europium complexes. This device exhibits the maximum brightness of 957 cd m−2, current efficiency of 4.14 cd A−1, and power efficiency of 2.28 lm W−1 with a pure red Eu3+ ion emission.41 The best performance has been reached for OLEDs based on 15(Eu)LS78 (with luminance of 1670 cd m−2), which consist of 2-thenoyltrifluoroacetonate as anionic ligands and dipyridophenazine as a neutral ligand. Recently the first successful syntheses of Eu complexes with pyrazolonate ligands have been realized. However, the devices based on obtained compounds 31(Eu)LK90 and 32(Eu)LK91 possess only a middling performance. 33(Eu)LW2,40 which contains an inorganic nitrate ligand, exhibits strong photoluminescent and very weak electroluminescent emission. The devices based on the complexes 34(Eu)LK,9235(Eu)LK,93 and 36(Eu)LK94 with aromatic carboxylate ligands also have weak luminance efficiency.

Most of the studies of electroluminescent terbium compounds belong to a class of β-diketonates. It is remarkable that the first reported electroluminescent lanthanide complex was 20(Tb).1 Later 20(Tb), 20(Tb)LK, 38(Tb), 38(Tb)LK were synthesized and tested in OLEDs for comparison.97 It was established that the triplet state energy of the acetylacetonate ligand decreases when protons are replaced by fluorine atoms within the ligand. Thus the level becomes more suitable for energy transfer towards the 5D4 state of Tb3+, resulting in an improvement of the emission intensity of 38(Tb).97 It is interesting to note that there is no information about electroluminescence of the comparable 38(Eu). Similarly, while 20(Tb)LK98 exhibits the luminance of 150 cd m−2, the electroluminescence of 20(Eu)LK83 is very weak. On the other hand, from considering the efficient europium complexes such as 11(Eu)L(A-W), 15(Eu)L(B-X) and 18(Eu)LK it can be summarized that these useful β-diketonates for Eu are not good ligands for design of highly efficient electroluminescent complexes of Tb. In attempt to improve the electroluminescent properties of β-diketonate terbium complexes, the authors100,101 synthesized some new β-diketones with oxadiazole and azaindol derivatives and prepared on their base the complexes 39(Tb)100 and 40(Tb).101 The highest performance (with a brightness 550 cd m−2) of devices based on 39(Tb) the authors explain as a significant improvement of the charge transport properties of 39(Tb) due to oxadiazolyl substituents.100 In the second case, it has been demonstrated an important role of promoting energy transfer from the ligand to the metal center in 40(Tb).101 Carboxylate compounds 34(Tb),10234(Tb)LK,10334(Tb)LW,104 and 35(Tb)LK105 like their Eu analogues exhibit very weak emission. The design of a new suitable carboxylate ligand has led to improvement of electroluminescent properties of 44(Tb)LK,10645(Tb)LK,10746(Tb),10847(Tb),10848(Tb),10949(Tb),11050(Tb),111 and 51(Tb)LK112 complexes. The best characteristics (the maximum luminance of 230 cd m−2 at 20 V and the maximum efficiency of 0.62 cd A−1) have been obtained for 45(Tb)LK-based OLED due to the introduction of hole-transporting diphenylamino-group into carboxylate ligand, representing the best result among Tb carboxylate complexes.107

The fascinating results were achieved for terbium (not for europium) compounds based on pyrazolonate ligands 37(Tb),11337(Tb)LW2,11341(Tb)LW,114–11642(Tb)LW,113,11743(Tb),11843(Tb)LW,11843(Tb)LK.118 Modification of the ligand with the aim of adjusting the carrier-transport properties of these complexes, and optimization of device configuration had led to the best record of performances, which reached for 42(Tb)LW the brightness up to 20000 cd m−2 at 20 V and efficiency up to 11 lm W−1 at 7 V.117 This is the highest characteristics for organolanthanide electroluminophores. Note, the ancillary ligands also strongly affect the electroluminescence performance; the best ligand for these complexes was found to be W. It is very significant that the complexes 37(Tb),11337(Tb)LW2,11341(Tb)LW,115,11642(Tb)LW,113 exhibit strong brightness in the single-layer devices without additional hole-transporting or electron-transporting layers. This indicates that these complexes possess not only excellent emitting but also good charge-transporting properties.

Recently, as an alternative to known electroluminescent lanthanide complexes based on β-diketonate, 8-oxyquinolinate, pyrazolonate and carboxylate ligands the imidodiphosphinate complexes were suggested.119,120 The much bulkier imidodiphosphinato groups were used as the inorganic analogues of the β-diketonate ligands. It was found that only Tb complex 52(Tb) exhibits enough efficient (0.7 cd A−1) green emission. The imidodiphosphinates of the other metals 52(Ce), 52(Nd), 52(Ho) turned out luminescent silence. These compounds revealed good hole-blocking and electron-transporting ability as well as a tendency for exciplex formation between complexes and TPD.120

The new complex 5(Tb) with heterocyclic ligands in a simple non-doped two-layer device exhibits pure sharp metal-centered emission of Tb3+ with current efficiency of 0.16 cd A−1.25 Remarkably, the analogue complexes of samarium 5(Sm) and europium 5(Eu) have not found electroluminescent properties though their current–voltage characteristics are quite similar to that of 5(Tb). The data of X-ray single crystal structure analysis have revealed that 5(Tb) and 5(Eu) are isostructural.25

Holmium

The Ho3+ ion can generate five emission bands: at 580 nm and 660 nm in visible region and 980 nm, 1200 nm, and 1500 nm in near-infrared region, which are attributed respectively to the transitions (5S2, 5F4) →5I8, 5F5→5I8, 5F5→5I7, 5I6→5I8, and 5F5→5I6.9 The electroluminescence of 8-oxyquinolinate 1(Ho) was observed in visible region at 665 nm.28 The β-diketonate complex 11(Ho)LO in the similar two-layer device exhibits three peaks at 980 nm, 1200 nm, and 1500 nm in NIR region.123 Efficiency of emission in both cases is weak. Nevertheless it is worth noting that the band at 1500 nm with a considerable bandwidth corresponding to the 5F5→5I6 transition suggests the potential application of this complex for the optical communication systems.123

Erbium

The Er3+ ion is able to exhibit visible green emission at 545 nm and infrared emission at 1.54 and 2.94 μm.9 The transition around 1540 nm, which coincides with 4I13/2→4I15/2, represents a high interest for optical communications. The first report of near-infrared electroluminescence of 8-oxyquinolinate 1(Er) has triggered off investigations in the field of lanthanide NIR OLEDs.30 The β-diketonate compounds 11(Er)LO124 and 20(Er)LK125 also have been used for fabrication devices, which showed the typical Er3+ near-infrared emission at 1.54 μm. Recently one of the best reported NIR devices was developed with the pyrazolonate complex 37(Er)LW2.126 It has displayed efficient NIR irradiance (0.21 μW cm−2) and full width at half maximum of the emission spectrum (70 nm) at 1.54 μm, which indicate the best potential for optical amplification applications.126

Thulium

In spite of multiple energy levels of Tm3+ ion, the visible emissions at 480 nm assigned to 1G4→3H6 transition and the NIR electroluminescent emissions at 0.8 and 1.4 μm assigned to 3F4→3H6 and 3F4→3H4 transitions have been described.25,28,127,128 The complexes with heterocyclic 8-oxyquinolate and 2-mercaptobenzothiazolate ligands 1(Tm)28 and 5(Tm)25 exhibit single sharp emission band at 795 nm due 3F4→3H6 transition. The devices based on β-diketonate complexes 11(Tm)LK127 and 11(Tm)LO127 exhibit a sharp peak at 803 nm and a broad-band emission of 1.4 μm, whereas 20(Tm)LK127,128 and 20(Tm)LO127 exhibit also a weak near-infrared 0.8 μm and a blue emission at 480 nm. It was found that the anionic ligand plays a more important role for Tm3+ ion emissions rather than the ancillary one.

The 4f shell of Yb3+ consists of an inverted 2F level resulting in two levels with an energy separation of nearly 1.27 eV.9 Consequently characteristic infra-red emission was obtained at 0.98 μm corresponding to 2F5/2→2F7/2 transitions.9 The additional types of electroluminescent Yb complexes are depicted below.

Lutetium

Lu3+ has a filled 4f shell, and consequently no metal-centered luminescence can be observed for the complexes of these ions. In other words, lutetium complexes belong to the ligand-emission group, as with Sc, Y, and La compounds. It should be noted as well that lutetium is one of the most expensive metals, which interferes with broad study of its complexes including electroluminescent materials. Recently, the pyrazolonate complexes containing various ancillary ligands 37(Lu)LC, 37(Lu)LK, 37(Lu)LW2 were synthesized and their EL was investigated.133 Although the PL intensity of 37(Lu)LW2 is about 100 times higher than that of complexes 37(Lu)LC and 37(Lu)LK, the latter display a better electroluminescent performance due to formation of the exciplexes with a high efficiency. Conclusions

The data quoted above do not cover all known organolanthanide electroluminophores, but only the most efficient of them. Nevertheless they show that during last decade this direction in the field of emissive materials for OLEDs developed greatly. Enough bright, stable and efficient materials, the luminescent characteristics of which are not worse than those of the majority of organic and d-metal–organic electroluminophores, have been found. Efficient lanthanide emitters for all regions of the visible and NIR spectra have been demonstrated. At the same time the obtained results testify that the abilities of the rare earth metal complexes as emissive materials for optoelectronic devices are far from being exhausted. A search of compounds with high intrinsic quantum yield of metal-centered emission remains the most challenging task. Since this characteristic depends on the coordination surrounding a lanthanide atom the synthesis of new ligands which would provide efficient transfer of excitation energy to the metal centre is a key problem in this field.
