Abstract

Hydrogen fuel is among the cleanest renewable resources and is the best alternative to fossil fuels for the future. Hydrogen can be best produced by means of electrolysis or photoelectrolysis of water among the various routes available for hydrogen production. So far, Pt has been recognized as the best electrode material for electrochemical hydrogen production. However, the cost of the catalyst, activity, and durability make Pt-catalyzed hydrogen production unsuitable on a commercial scale. It has hence become imperative to explore low-cost, highly active and durable HER catalysts to replace platinum as a catalyst. This perspective provides key concepts and the current status of the research on the properties of nanocatalysts that influence the hydrogen evolution reaction. Important structural features controlling the surface chemistry (i.e. facets, defects, dopants), nature of supports (graphene, CNTs, black phosphorus), role of heteroatoms, media and morphology are the key points of discussion in this perspective.

Introduction

As global energy requirements are increasing, keeping pace with traditional power generation may lead to environmental deterioration. The development of sustainable and environment friendly energy production is a mandatory challenge for future generations.1–5 Environmental issues caused by fossil fuels have attracted wide attention and countless efforts are currently underway to find a cheap, carbon-less and abundant energy replacement.6–8 As we all know, hydrogen is one of the most striking molecular fuels and has the highest energy density which makes it an excellent energy-carrier.9–13 Hydrogen is being actively pursued as a clean and green energy source for the replacement of conventional fossil fuels due to its recyclability and pollution-free by-products.11,14,15 Molecular hydrogen gas can be produced through the electrochemical hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in the presence of an inexpensive catalyst using renewable energy sources like solar energy, wind, geothermal heat, tides, biomass, etc. It is the most viable way for hydrogen production economically and efficiently in high purity and large quantities.16 Like any chemical reaction, there is an energy barrier in electrochemical processes to produce hydrogen from water which is called the overpotential. A suitable catalyst is required to minimize the activation energy by reducing the overpotential of the system for efficient hydrogen production. At present, noble metals like Pt-based catalysts and their derivatives are the most active HER catalysts17 due to their optimum hydrogen binding (ΔGH*) and low activation energy for H desorption from the surface of Pt.18 Pt catalyzed HER requires ∼zero onset overpotential and leads to high cathodic current density in acid solution. However, bulk application of these catalysts is restricted due to their high cost.18,19 Thus, the development of earth-abundant electrocatalysts exhibiting high catalytic efficiency and high electrochemical stability is essential to replace Pt and other expensive metals like Pd, Ir, Rh, etc. 3d-Transition metal-based compounds (TMCs) are probably a good replacement of these noble metal-based catalysts. Identification of electrocatalysts composed of earth abundant materials is crucial and of great importance to maintain the demand for hydrogen energy in solar water-splitting devices, fuel cells and metal air batteries. The literature shows that electrocatalytic studies have been carried out on several hundreds of compositions for HER. The hydrogen evolution reaction is characterized based on overpotential, exchange current density, Tafel slope, turn over frequency (TOFs) and Gibbs free energy. There are several reviews that have been written from different perspectives on the hydrogen evolution reaction including synthesis, characterization, catalyst surfaces, physical, chemical and catalytic properties, atomic and electronic structures and applications.20,21 In this perspective, we have discussed key characteristics of nanoparticles/nanocrystals that influence significantly the hydrogen evolution and water splitting processes. The fundamental concepts, materials and mechanism of hydrogen evolution reactions have been discussed in the light of recent advancements in this area.

Hydrogen evolution reaction (HER)

The electrochemical hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) is the reduction of protons or water to hydrogen molecules (H2) on applying external potential or electrochemical energy. The overall HER can be written as

In acidic medium,

2H+ + 2e− → H2

In alkaline medium,

2H2O + 2e− → H2 + 2OH−

Hydrogen can be generated electrochemically from water splitting,22 NH3 splitting,23 hydrazine-borane reduction24 and amine-borane reduction.24,25 However, the easiest and cheapest way to produce hydrogen is by the splitting of water.

Impact of hydrogen evolution reactions on the energy sector

The energy sector is facing significant challenges in moving towards sustainable energy and a clean environment and in preventing depletion of natural resources, volatile international oil prices, intense pressure on energy security and deteriorating air quality.26 Decisive measures are needed to bring about low-carbon energy options. Hydrogen is emerging as a notable future alternative for replacing oil and natural gas delivering high-quality and clean energy for all sectors across the energy spectrum.26,27 The reason for hydrogen to emerge as a suitable energy carrier is because it can be generated from a variety of primary energy sources, renewable and non-renewable, and it is a clean, renewable, light, nonpolluting, and abundant source of energy with high calorific value compared to other sources of energy.26,28,29 An increasing number of reports show that hydrogen can have a role in almost every possible energy generation system—including electricity and transportation. Though hydrogen as a fuel has significant potential in the energy sector, there are also some challenges due to which the technology cannot be commercialized easily everywhere. There are challenges in production, storage, transportation and distribution.27 Currently, it is stored in high pressure tanks in the form of compressed gas which is highly expensive, requires large volume and also has safety issues.30 The drawback of storing hydrogen in liquid form is that it requires a low temperature and high pressure, which can be dangerous. New technologies or additional safety measures added to the current procedures are required to turn H2 into a consumer-friendly fuel which can be utilized in a safe and responsible manner.31 Recently, several materials have been developed for hydrogen storage due to their high hydrogen adsorption–desorption reversibility and stability. However, the production of hydrogen locally will be much more accepted with respect to the safety and cost of transportation. Current hydrogen economy is more focused on local and low-cost production and transportation. In this context, electrochemical hydrogen production via water splitting is one of the best ways to produce H2 locally.

Mechanism of the HER process and kinetics

The mechanism of the HER processes on the catalyst's surface follows two steps:34 (i) electrochemical adsorption process (Volmer reaction) where a proton binds to the catalyst's surface and (ii) electrochemical discharge process (Heyrovsky reaction) where one proton combines with the adsorbed proton and then is desorbed from the surface or (iii) chemical desorption process (Tafel reaction) where two adsorbed protons are combined together and released from the surface.

In the Volmer reaction:

H+ + e− + M(active site) ⇄ MHads (acidic media)
or
H2O + e− + M(active site) ⇄ MHads + OH− (alkaline media)
In the Heyrovsky reaction:
H+ + e− + MHads ⇄ M + H2 (acidic media)
or
H2O + e− + MHads ⇄ M + OH− + H2 (alkaline media)
And in the Tafel reaction:
2MHads ⇄ 2M + H2

HER with transition metals as catalysts: theoretical aspects

Generally, metals and alloys are used for electrochemical water splitting (electrolysis) because of their lower electronegativity compared to that of hydrogen. Metals have lower hydrogen binding energy compared to semiconductors and insulators which favors the chemisorption process as well as desorption process in HER. It has been shown that HER activity of the transition metals also depends on the work-function (Φ) of the metals and M–H bond strength.36
log(j0) = 6.7Φ − 36.6

Generally, it is easier to extract an electron from metals having lower work-function (Φ). This could lead to the higher HER activity of the metals having lower work-function. But experimentally, the reverse effect has been observed.36 There are two factors as mentioned below which are additive factors to the HER activity of the metals.

(a) Direct strong interaction of the proton with the metal surface.

(b) Electrical double layer effect: at equilibrium, the potential at the outer Helmholtz layer is more positive for metals having lower Φ. This would lead to a decrease in the proton concentration at the interface (metal/electrolyte) with decreasing Φ, resulting in lower HER activity.

This correlation with work-function suggests that all the metals fall in a single group and their HER activity can be explained by a single theory. Therefore, metal–hydrogen binding energy has been considered for their HER activity which divides the metals into groups. Due to the variation of hydrogen binding energies of metals, the HER activity differs from metal to metal. This has led to the evolution of the well-known Volcano plot.37 However, the platinum group metals and their alloys are among the most effective catalysts for the hydrogen evolution reactions because of their optimum hydrogen binding energy (−5.8 kJ mol−1)38 and higher work-function (Φ).

Transition metal-based nanostructures as catalysts for HER: experimental studies

Nanostructures of metals, metal chalcogenides, nitrides, carbides, phosphides, and borides have been investigated extensively for hydrogen evolution studies. The properties of these nanocrystals vary significantly based on their surface structure, elemental environment, medium of catalysis and supporting templates which are discussed below.

The surface chemistry of CoxNi4−xS4 (220) facets in HER processes has been explored recently. When the Co/Ni ratio changes from 3 to 1, then there is a shift in the X-ray diffraction peak of the (220) plane which suggests that the surface chemical environment of the (220) facet has changed.43 In Co2Ni2S4, the Gibbs free energy for H-binding43 at Co sites is 548.7 kJ mol−1, whereas at the Ni site it is 506 kJ mol−1. The subtle variation in the surface chemical state influences the hydrogen adsorption properties of CoxNi4−xS4. Layered structures of transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) have important features that make them excellent candidates for electrocatalysis. Vertically oriented MoS2 and MoSe2 layers exhibit remarkable HER activity due to their exposed edge sites.44 The basal plane of MoS2 is electrocatalytically inactive45 due to its unfavorable ΔGH of 2 eV. Jaramillo et al. showed that the electrocatalytic activity of MoS2 is linearly proportional to the number of layers.46 The exposed edges of layered TMDs have been shown to exhibit remarkable HER activity. For example in MoS2 having ∼5 layers, the current density follows the terminal sulphur content (STerminal), where the ratio of STerminal/STotal is very crucial for HER activity.47 It has been observed that the maximum terminal sulphide and disulphide content results in high current density (Fig. 3b). The edge orientation of MoS2 is also very important for its HER activity. The density of the MoS2 edges of particular length is highly influential in enhancing the HER activity and the current density increases linearly with the edge length density (Fig. 3c).48

When the edge orientation changes and results in interlayer expansion, the current density enhances significantly.48 Stepped edges of MoS2 were developed in a vertical array of MoS2 which exhibited 10 mA cm−2 current density for HER at 104 mV of overpotential with an exchange current density of 0.2 mA cm−2. These stepped edges showed almost thermo-neutral H-adsorption facilitating HER processes.45 The stepped edge surface terminated MoS2 and flat edge surface terminated MoS2 were developed by using microwave hydrothermal treatment for 2 h and 50 min respectively (Fig. 4a and d). Stepped edges and flat edges were confirmed from electron microscopic images (Fig. 4b, c and e). The HBE calculations suggest that stepped edge terminated MoS2 has lower free energy compared to that of flat edge terminated MoS2 (Fig. 4f). There is medium range to long range ordered p–π resonance due to aligned disulphide (S22−) ligands through interlayers in flat edge terminated MoS2 whereas long range ordered p–π resonance is absent in stepped edge terminated MoS2 (Fig. 4g). The absence of long range ordered p–π resonance enhances electron density at the edge sites which facilitates the hydrogen binding and results in high HER activity.

Hinnemann et al.49 theoretically proved that unstable edges of layered MoS2 nanocrystals are the catalytically active sites for HER. Since then researchers have focused on finding ways to increase the number of active edge sites of MoS2 to make excellent electrocatalysts. Jaramillo et al. have carried out experimental investigations with varying density of edge sites present on MoS2 nanoplates for HER to prove the above phenomenon,46 where the exchange current density of MoS2 is directly proportional to the length of the edge. Xie et al.50 also proved experimentally that defect-rich ultrathin nanosheets of MoS2 display the largest cathodic current density with a small overpotential compared to either defect-rich MoS2 or calcined MoS2 nanosheets. This is due to the more unique active edge sites available in defect rich MoS2 nanosheets compared to others.

In general, MoS2 is known to be an active HER catalyst because of its active edge sites. Nørskov et al.51 first reported the activation and optimization of the basal plane for the enhancement of HER activity by creating sulfur vacancies and strain on the basal plane of MoS2. Theoretical and experimental studies also support the fact that by creating pores and strain, new catalytic sites can be generated in the basal plane of MoS2, where hydrogen can bind directly to the exposed Mo atoms. Theoretical calculations were carried out to demonstrate the S-vacancy engineered hydrogen binding on MoS2, where the free energy of hydrogen binding becomes negative with increasing S-vacancies (Fig. 5a). Again strain-engineered electrocatalytic activity of m-MS2 (m = metal terminated) (M = Mo and Nb) in both 1H and 1T phases52 was demonstrated. However, strained MoS2 having S-vacancies exhibits much higher HER activity compared to only strained MoS2 or MoS2 having S-vacancies (Fig. 5b). The stable bulk structure of MoS2 has a trigonal prismatic structure and this phase is known as 2H-MoS2 where two stoichiometric S–Mo–S layers with a hexagonal atomic arrangement are present in the structure. This 2H-MoS2 phase can be further exfoliated into 1H-MoS2 phase which contains only one layer of S–Mo–S.53 These 1H and 2H phases are semiconducting where the 1H phase is a direct band gap semiconductor and the 2H phase is an indirect band gap semiconductor. The single layer of MoS2 with an octahedral 1T atomic arrangement is known as the 1T-MoS2 phase. The distorted version of 1T-MoS2 is known as the 1T′-MoS2 phase.53 The 1T-MoS2 phase is metallic in nature but 1T′-MoS2 is non-metallic with a band gap of 0.1–0.2 eV.53 It has been found that 1H-MoS2 and 1T-MoS2 phases are not as active as the metastable 1T′-MoS2 phase.53 Strain induces more active centers on the inert basal plane of TMDs due to stretching of the plane. It has been shown that tensile strain enhances the HER activity of MS2, whereas compressive strain decreases the HER activity. Again, in tensile strain, biaxial tensile strain influences the HER activity more significantly compared to uniaxial tensile strain.52 The role of defects in MoS2 in HER performance can be best understood by the studies carried out by Ajayan and co-workers.54 The electrochemical activity of the MoS2 monolayer before and after plasma treatment suggests that MoS2 on plasma exposure for 20 s results in a larger cathodic current density with a small onset potential compared to that without plasma treatment (Fig. 5c), indicating that more defects are created with plasma exposure. More defects create more exposed active sites in MoS2 and hence plasma treatment is expected to improve hydrogen adsorption and catalysis. Similarly, defects can also be generated using annealing methods to improve the catalytic properties of MoS2. The annealing temperature affects the defects in MoS2 and hence the HER activity (Fig. 5d). The lowest onset overpotential for HER was observed for MoS2 annealed under 500 °C, whereas MoS2 annealed at 400 °C (∼470 mV) and 600 °C (∼450 mV) results in higher onset overpotential.54

Oxygen plasma treatment was used to introduce high density atomic scale pores in TaS2 nanosheets where it increases the defect density and controls the HER activity.55 Higher defects enhance the HER activity of TaS2, lowering the Tafel slopes.55 However, the HER activity depends on an optimal defect concentration and hence on the plasma treatment (10 min). Guo et al. have synthesized defect- and S-rich MoS2 embedded on N-doped carbon nanofibers where more edges (active sites) were exposed and hence led to enhancement of the HER activity.56

The morphology of the nanostructures defines the exposed facets and surfaces of the crystals and hence surface|electrolyte interfaces which would have an impact on their overall electrocatalytic HER activity. The array of nanostructures (nanowires, particles and sheets) were shown to exhibit much better electrocatalytic activity compared to the corresponding nanostructures. Wang et al.57 have demonstrated that the vertical array of VS2 nanoplates exhibit high HER activity with a η10 of 42 mV with a Tafel slope of 36 mV dec−1 and an exchange current density of 0.955 mA cm−2. Our group have shown that variation in the morphology of Mo2N nanostructures controls the HER activity. Mo2N nanostructures with varying morphologies of hexagons, triangles, and nanowires were investigated for HER activity in acidic media where the highest apparent electrocatalytic activity was found in Mo2N having hexagonal morphology.58 Mo2N with hexagonal morphology having a high surface area and a large number of particle–particle voids exhibits better contacts between the surface and electrolytes which is responsible for the high HER activity. Our group has reported the synthesis of MoP particles using two different complexation routes using citric acid and aniline as the complexing agents.59 Citric acid complexation leads to the formation of MoP microparticles whereas the aniline complexation route results in interconnected nanoparticles. It has been shown that interconnected MoP nanoparticles exhibit good HER activity compared to the MoP microparticles due to exposed surfaces and surface area accessible to the electrolyte.59

Role of heteroatoms

The atomic environment around the active centers is one of the crucial factors that controls the electronic properties of the active center and hence the catalytic activity. The hydrogen evolution reactions catalyzed by metals and alloys are restricted for widespread applications due to several factors like high cost, availability from natural resources, poor stability in electrolytes and lower electrocatalytic activity. Some of the non-precious transition metal based compounds like borides,60 carbides,61–64 nitrides,65–67 chalcogenides39,68–72 and phosphides have shown high stability, corrosion resistance, high mechanical strength and high melting point. Recently, these transition metal-based compounds have been shown to be promising catalysts for the replacement of Pt for hydrogen production from water. The HER processes with transition metal compounds (where the active center is the metal ion) are highly influenced by the anions (heteroatoms) which is quite interesting.

Understanding the electronic structure of these transition metal compounds is a key factor in designing catalysts for HER activity. Both the mechanisms (Volmer–Heyrovsky and Volmer–Tafel) involve first the hydrogen adsorption (Volmer reaction) which depends on the hydrogen binding energy (HBE) of the compounds or metals. The Volcano plot for the metals suggests that superior HER activity can be possible if the catalyst exhibits an optimum HBE (neither too strong nor too weak). Therefore, the HBE is the key parameter of the electrocatalyst for exhibiting high HER activity and depends on the electronic structure of the materials. Non-metallic atoms like C, N, P, S or Se in transition metal-based compounds modify the electronic structure of the compounds which directly affect the HER activity of the metals. In transition metals, the d orbitals have significant contribution to the binding of adsorbates on the metal surface. These metal d orbitals are hybridized with the s and p orbitals of the heteroatoms (C, N, S, P, etc.) which broadens the d band structure. This affects the position of the d-band center due to charge transfer from the metal to the heteroatom center which alters the hydrogen binding energy. Generally, the downward shift of the d-band center of the metal relative to its Fermi level decreases the HBE. This favors the desorption process in HER and enhances the catalytic activity.

The primary principle of the d-band center theory is based on the variation in the coupling between adsorbate levels and transition metal d-bands. There are two contributions in adsorbate–metal surface interactions.73

ΔE = ΔEsp + ΔEhyb-d

In the case of hydrogen binding, the first one is the interaction of the hydrogen 1s electron with the electrons on the metals, which results in a filled, low-lying bonding molecular orbital (σ) and an empty anti-bonding MO (σ*). This interaction does not vary significantly among the transition metals implying similar contribution in HER from different metals. In Fig. 6a, the projected densities of states (DOS) are shown for the adsorbate and metal centers. Now in hydrogen binding, another contribution is from a metal's d-electrons. The metal d-band hybridizes with the adsorbate (σ) orbital and forms (d–σ) bonding and (d–σ)* antibonding states as shown in Fig. 6b. Now the d–σ bonding orbitals are filled with electrons whereas the filling of the antibonding state depends on the surface electronic structure of the metals. The metal–adsorbate bonding becomes weak with the increase in the filling of the antibonding state. Now the d-band center plays a crucial role in the filling of the antibonding state.

The upward shift of the d-band center with respect to the Fermi level shifts the antibonding state to higher energy which decreases the occupancy of the state. This implies that the metal–adsorbate bond strength becomes stronger. The modification in the d-bandwidth significantly influences the d-band center as the bandwidth (w) is related to band filling (fd).74

As shown in Fig. 6c, the d-band center shifts upward with respect to the Fermi level as the bandwidth decreases. A linear relationship is shown in Fig. 6d for Pt–Sn alloys. Now the d-band center plays a key role in hydrogen binding on the metal surface. It is evident that the d-band centers of the (111) surface of Cu and Au are far apart from the Fermi level whereas Pt and Ni have the d-band center close to the Fermi level (Fig. 7a). It also shows that the antibonding orbitals of the M–H bond are occupied in the case of Cu and Au. This implies that Cu and Au do not form a M–H bond whereas Pt and Ni form a M–H bond.76 This appears to be the main reason for the known HER inactivity of Cu and Au.

Several doped transition metal based compounds (TMCs) were developed where significant effects of the dopant were found to control the electronic properties of metal centers. Mo2C has strong hydrogen adsorption (strong Mo–H bonding) which decreases the HER activity. However, cobalt doping in Mo2C structures increases the electron density around the Fermi level of Mo which reduces the Mo–H bond strength.81 An increase in the electron density around the Fermi level (EF) results in electron transfer into anti-bonding orbitals of the Mo–H bond. A decrease in the electron density around EF reduces electrons in the bonding orbitals. Therefore, in both cases, the Mo–H bond strength decreases which is favorable for the desorption step. In metal phosphides, P atoms act as proton and hydride acceptors along with the metal centers.

The Gibbs free energy of hydrogen binding on MoS2 is 0.08 eV (Mo edges) and 0.18 eV (S edges), whereas it is 0.22 eV for both W and S edges in WS2. When a Co atom is introduced into the MS2 system (M = Mo, W), the ΔGH (S-edges) is drastically lowered to 0.07 eV in WS2 (Fig. 9a). Therefore, in MS2 systems, the HER activity of MoS2 is higher than that of WS2, but after Co addition (Co–MS2 systems) the HER activity of Co-doped WS2 is much better than that of Co-doped MoS2 systems (Fig. 9b).82 Cobalt incorporation in 2D WS2 as CoxW1−xS2 lowers the overpotential for 10 mA cm−2 from 236 mV to 121 mV and nearly a 10 times decrease in the charge transfer resistance which is quite significant in HER activity.83

TMDs with different anions, e.g. NiX2 (X = S, Se, Te), have been shown to exhibit variation in the HER activity in the order of NiSe2 > NiS2 > NiTe2. The Gibbs free energy of hydrogen binding at the Ni sites is lower than that of S and Se sites in NiS2 and NiSe2 respectively, whereas it is higher than that of Te sites in NiTe2. The hydrogen binding at Ni sites is influenced significantly by anions and surface structures.86 The crystal phases (2H and 1T phases) of TMDs are very crucial for their HER activity where the 2H phase is semiconducting due to the complete filling of d-orbitals whereas in the 1T phase, incomplete d-orbital filling leads to metallic nature in TMDs. The 1T phase of MoS2 has a ΔGH of 0.12 eV and the 2H phase has a ΔGH of 1.96 eV. Jiang et al.87 have shown that the hydrogen adsorption energies of various TMDs with varying chalcogens are dependent on the crystal phases (Fig. 10a). Various dopants were also introduced in MoS2 systems and it was observed that B, N, and P doping significantly lowers the HBE of the neighboring S atoms (Fig. 10b). S-Vacancy dependency on the HBE in MoS2 was also shown and it suggested that higher vacancy lowers the HBE (Fig. 10c).

2D-TMDs, e.g. MX2 (M = V, Nb, Ta and X = S, Se, Te), were theoretically shown to be good proton adsorbers for hydrogen production.88 Pan et al.88 predicted that MS2 is a better HER catalyst than MSe2 and MTe2 and among the metal disulphides, VS2 is the best HER catalyst compared to NbS2 and TaS2. However, experimentally,89 it has been shown that VTe2 is the best HER electrocatalyst and the catalytic activity follows the order of VS2 < VSe2 < VTe2 whereas in metal ditellurides (MTe2), the HER activity follows the order of VTe2 > NbTe2 > TaTe2.

Transition metal phosphides (TMPs) were recently explored as the star catalytic materials for electrochemical hydrogen evolution reactions. Nickel phosphide (Ni2P) nanocrystals were synthesized from the hot-injection method and were demonstrated as excellent HER catalysts in acidic media almost similar to Pt.90 The role of the phosphorus atom in nickel phosphides demonstrated that Ni5P4 is the most active for HER compared to Ni12P5, Ni2P and Ni.91 With increasing P:Ni ratio, the Ni–Ni bond distance increases which contributes to the electronic structure and bonding of the active metal centers.91 It weakens the M–H adsorption processes. Again, the weak adsorption of the hydrogen atom on P-sites in metal phosphides also contributes to the higher HER activity. Theoretical investigations suggest that Ni5P4 has abundant P3-hollow sites and that in Ni2P, the P-rich surfaces are less stable.92 These TMPs exhibited high exchange current density like Pt. Co doping on Ni2P also weakens the H adsorption, facilitating the H2 desorption from the surface leading to high HER activity.93,94 NiCoP nanostructures supported on Ni foam exhibited HER activity with 10 mA cm−2 at an overpotential of 32 mV in alkaline media.94 Nest like nickel cobalt phosphide on carbon cloth (NiCoP/CC) exhibited 10 mA cm−2 current density for HER at overpotentials of 44 mV and 62 mV in acidic media and alkaline media respectively.95 Bimetallic metal phosphides/sulphides (NiFeSP) were explored for HER with an overpotential of 91 mV at 10 mA cm−2 in alkaline media.96 CoP and Co2P were also explored and were found to be remarkable electrocatalysts for hydrogen evolution. Cobalt phosphide exhibits higher exchange current density compared to nickel phosphide as shown in the review.91 Iron phosphide (FeP) nanoparticles coated with a carbon shell exhibited high stability during HER in acidic media with an overpotential of 71 mV to drive 10 mA cm−2 current density.97 A rugae-like FeP nanocrystal assembly on a carbon cloth was shown to exhibit HER activity with 10 mA cm−2 at an overpotential as low as 34 mV.98 However, other metal phosphides like MoP and WP exhibit HER activity at much higher overpotentials. The Volcano plot based on the TOF of various metal phosphides suggests that Fe0.5Co0.5P exhibits thermo-neutral H-binding and the highest TOF of HER activity. Ni2P, Co2P and FeP exhibit high HER activity due to weak H-adsorption energies leading to higher H2 desorption.78 Again, MoP, WP, CoP, Fe2P, etc. having stronger H-adsorption exhibit less HER activity.78 Metal phosphides exhibit extraordinary HER activity in both acidic and alkaline media. Metal phosphides have the following key features which are beneficial for HER activity.

(i) Contribution of P atoms to the metal's electronic structure and H-binding, i.e. P atoms weaken M–M bonding which in turn weakens M–H bonding leading to low adsorption energies and fast H2 desorption processes.

(ii) P atoms are also involved in bonding with H atoms. Weak P–H bonding facilitates H2 desorption processes leading to higher HER activity.

(iii) Extraordinary electrochemical stability in both acidic and alkaline media.

(iv) Very high exchange current density and high TOF in HER activity.

The role of non-metallic atoms in metal phosphides, nitrides, carbides and sulfides is not clearly evident. Theoretical investigations suggested the modification of the metal d-band center in these compounds which significantly affected the electrocatalytic performance. However, doping with hetero anions like N in carbide,99 S in phosphide,100 S in carbide101 and N in sulphides significantly enhances the electrocatalytic performance. For example, cobalt phosphosulphides102 and molybdenum phosphosulphide100 exhibit remarkable HER activity compared to the bare CoS2 and MoP respectively. In mixed anion catalysts like molybdenum phosphosulphides, the synergistic effects between S and P result in increased number of electrochemically active sites.100 Cobalt phosphosulphides exhibit 10 mA cm−2 at an overpotential of 48 mV with an exchange current density of 1.14 mA cm−2 which is in the same order as that of Pt.102 Co|P|S is a distinct ternary pyrite type phase featuring P–S bonds without any S–S bonds as observed in CoS2 (pyrites).103 In CoS2 pyrite structures, some of the S atoms were substituted by P atoms randomly which does not change the lattice parameters.103 There is a positive correlation between P content and electrocatalytic activity in iron phosphosulphides (Fe|P|S) where higher HER activity was observed at the Fe atoms coordinated with P atoms.104 It is evident that P substitution in FeS drastically enhances the HER activity, while S substitution in FeP slightly deteriorates the activity.104 Ni|P|S nanocrystals exhibit 10 mA cm−2 at an overpotential of 52 mV in alkaline media which is quite impressive.105 The high HER activity of Ni|P|S is because of the presence of electronegative P–S species and theoretically it has been shown that S incorporation in the NiP system shifts the ΔGH value to the thermo-neutral position balancing the H-adsorption and desorption.105 In a mixed metal sulphide like NiCoSx, the surface S atoms are easy to oxidize where P atom doping decreases this oxidation possibility. Nickel and phosphorus co-doping in CoS2 nanowires exhibits good HER activity and durability and exhibits lower charge transfer resistance.106 P being less electronegative enhances the metal-anion covalence which enhances the electrochemical stability of the materials.106 P doping in Mo2C increases the electron density around the Fermi level of Mo which weakens the Mo–H bonds and promotes the HER kinetics.107 Thermo-neutral hydrogen binding (ΔGH ∼ 0) was observed with optimal doping with P (2.9%).107 Similarly, in tungsten carbonitride, N-bound W sites are the more positively charged centers and behave as the active sites for HER. The d-band centers of N-bound W atoms are shifted downwards which facilitates the H-adsorption.108 Introducing heteroatoms can tune the electron donating/withdrawing capability which makes M–N–C like materials promising electrocatalysts. Ni-Doped Mo|C|N exhibits 10 mA cm−2 current density at an overpotential of 110 mV where the synergistic interaction of Mo, Ni, C and N reduces the M–H bond strength.109 Ni–C|N composites were derived from polyoxometalates where Ni sites are the active sites as confirmed from thiocyanate110 and the catalysts exhibit a η10 of 61 mV and a remarkable TOF of 6.67 s−1 at 200 mV. C and N hybrid coordination in the Co–C–N complex leads to charge distribution and enhances the electron transport.111 These result in higher HER activity of Co|C|N catalysts. In molybdenum carbide nitride composites, the synergistic effect of Mo2C and Mo2N influences the charge transfer leading to better HER activity of the composites.99,112 An amorphous structure, a disordered lattice, and edges having unsaturated coordination are some characteristics which lead to the efficiency of an electrocatalyst. These hetero anions not only contribute to the electronic structure of the metals, but also contribute significantly to hydrogen binding on the surfaces.

Demonstration of two-electrode water splitting

On a laboratory scale, the overall water splitting was investigated using the electrocatalysts as cathode and anode in a two-electrode glass cell assembly. When these electrocatalysts are bifunctional, i.e. active for both HER and OER, then these catalysts were assembled as both cathode and anode. Otherwise, an active OER catalyst can be integrated with these HER catalysts in a two-electrode configuration. These extraordinary electrocatalysts like TMPs are generally active for both HER and OER, i.e. bifunctional electrocatalysts. NiCoP/CC was demonstrated for practical application in a two electrode assembly for overall water splitting95 where NiCoP/CC acts as a bi-functional electrocatalyst for both cathode and anode. It generates a cell voltage of 1.52 V at 10 mA cm−2. NiCoP hybrids with reduced graphene oxide (rGO)93 were used to fabricate a two-electrode assembly for overall water splitting which yielded 10 mA cm−2 at 1.59 V in 1.0M KOH and was stable over 75 h with a Faradic efficiency of ≈100%. NiCoP nanostructures on Ni foam were used in overall water splitting in a two-electrode configuration94 which exhibited a cell voltage of 1.58 V at 10 mA cm−2 in alkaline media. However, Cu3P on Ni foam113 exhibited a higher cell voltage of 1.67 V in overall water splitting at 10 mA cm−2. Fe and O doped Co2P grown on Ni foam exhibited 10 mA cm−2 at a cell voltage as low as 1.56 V in overall water splitting.114 Bi-functional electrocatalysts like nickel iron phosphide/sulphide (NiFeSP) were integrated with photoactive materials like photocathode Cu2O and photoanode BiVO4 to get overall water splitting in two electrode configuration using solar light.96 It exhibited a cell voltage of 1.58 V at a current density of 10 mA cm−2.

Role of conducting supports

Conducting substrates play a very crucial role in supporting the electrocatalysts in the hydrogen evolution activity in terms of enhancing the electrochemical stability, active surface area and facile charge transport. Carbon based materials have unique advantages due to their high electron mobility, tunable molecular structures, abundance and strong tolerance to acidic/alkaline environments.115 Various non-metallic heteroatom (N, B, O, S, P, F) doped graphene were coupled with HER catalysts and their efficiencies were investigated. The orbitals of C atoms at the active sites overlap with the adsorbed H* to form bonding and antibonding levels.115 Heteroatoms having higher electronegativity decrease the charge density on active centers which reduces the ΔGH and enhances HER. It was predicted that N,P co-doped graphene surfaces can have low ΔGH* and high HER activity.115 N,S co-doped graphene surfaces were synthesized and investigated for HER activity (Fig. 11).116 These N,S co-doped graphene surfaces were synthesized using pyridine and thiophene in a H2/Ar atmosphere (Fig. 11a) and were confirmed using elemental mapping (Fig. 11c). The HBE of the N, S co-doped graphene is similar to that of Pt and is reflected in HER activity (Fig. 11d). It exhibits the lowest charge transfer resistance during HER activity which also corroborates with the HER activity (Fig. 11e and f).

Therefore, the role of graphene in the composites/hybrids with electrocatalysts can be understood from various studies like graphene–Mo2C,117–119 graphene–MoP,59,120 graphene–MoS2,121etc. The following have been found as the key role of graphene/other carbon materials.

(1) Heteroatom doped graphene/other carbon materials are involved as a co-catalyst in the composites and contribute directly to the HER performance of the composites/hybrids.

(2) It provides good support to the primary electrocatalysts.

(3) It enhances the air-stability as well as electrochemical stability to the electrocatalysts.

(4) In hybrids, the interfaces between graphene and electrocatalysts provide a better electronic environment to the metal centers at the interfaces which facilitates the HER activity.

(5) It provides a high surface area and more number of catalysts|electrolyte interfaces which lead to better HER performances.

(6) It lowers the cost of the catalysts and enhances the durability of the catalysts.

Our group has developed rGO–Mo2C and rGO–MoP composites (Fig. 12a) and has clearly indicated the role of rGO in the composites.59,117 In rGO–Mo2C composites, Mo2C rods were coupled with rGO where the ratio of rGO/Mo2C contributes significantly to the HER activity and stability of Mo2C (Fig. 12b). Higher rGO content in the composite reduces the HER activity due to low content of the primary catalyst (Mo2C). The graphene–MoP composite exhibits good HER activity in both acidic and alkaline media and it has been confirmed that the presence of rGO in the composite enhances the current density drastically (Fig. 12c and d). This indicates the significant contribution of rGO in the composite to HER activity.

MoS2 particles/sheets normally aggregate when synthesized using wet chemical methods. Graphene acts as an important substrate/support for the nucleation and subsequent growth of highly dispersed MoS2 without agglomeration. The strong chemical and electronic coupling between the graphene sheets and MoS2 leads to good HER activity121 with a small overpotential of ∼0.1 V, a large cathodic current, and a small Tafel slope of 41 mV decade−1. The interconnected conducting network of graphene sheets facilitates electron transport from the active catalyst (MoS2 nanoparticles) to the electrodes.121 The extremely low conductivity and aggregation in MoSx materials is an inherent problem for HER applications.122 However, introducing conducting supports like graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) and other mesoporous carbon materials to MoSx nanostructures by making composites, heterojunctions, core–shell, etc. dramatically improves the electrocatalytic activity. MoS2/rGO hybrids121 have been developed where small MoS2 crystals were embedded on the conducting reduced graphene oxide. The rGO support significantly enhanced the electrocatalytic activity of MoS2 with a small onset overpotential of 100 mV and a low Tafel slope of 41 mV dec−1. This is attributed to the increase in the number of edges (active sites) due to the reduced sizes of MoS2 crystals as well as the enhanced electrical conductivity.122 Since rGO and MoS2/rGO become agglomerated due to restacking of the layers during the hydrothermal process, a ternary composite of MoS2/WC-rGO was developed where WC acts as the conductive dispersant to prevent the restacking of layers.123,124 This results in higher exposed surfaces leading to the excellent HER activity. The presence of WC contributes to the dispersion of MoS2 as well as generates a synergistic effect between WC/rGO and MoS2 layers in the form of conductivity and porosity. Jaramillo et al.125 reported the growth of core–shell MoO3/MoS2 nanowire catalysts where the sub-stoichiometric MoO3 (MoO3−x) serves as a conducting core and the MoS2 shell acts as both a HER catalyst and a protective layer. To further improve the electrocatalytic HER efficiency, research on three dimensional (3D) electrodes was carried out where mesoporous graphene foam (MGF) supports MoS2 nanocrystals.126 This 3D-MGF provides a very high specific surface area to grow MoS2 nanocrystals in a well dispersed manner with rich edge sites where conducting and mesoporous graphene allows the diffusion of electrons and ions.126 This in turn results in the high HER activity of the MoS2/MGF nanocomposite with a low onset overpotential of 100 mV and a small Tafel slope of 42 mV dec−1. Li et al.127 have realized the formation of MoSx (x ≥ 2) on graphene-protected nickel foam (MoSx/graphene/3D Ni) where excellent catalytic activity was observed due to the conductivity with the use of a 3D graphene/Ni foam support and the presence of bridging S22− or apical S2−. Banerjee et al.128 used interface engineering to enhance the HER performance of low-dimensional materials. The 3D array of MoS2 nanosheets interfaced with nC60 was proven to be an excellent catalyst compared to the bulk or nanostructured MoS2 in terms of HER performance. Density functional theory calculations of C60/MoS2 also agreed with the experimental results. Hexagonal rings of C60 were placed above the S sites of MoS2 basal planes which allowed buckminsterfullerene to rotate freely on the surface.129 This helps in the charge transfer process from C60 to MoS2 upon interfacing and increases the electron density on MoS2 and hence reduces the resistance of the hybrid materials. Electrons can easily be donated from C60 to MoS2 making the interfacial layer conductive as well as polarize the Mo–S bonds which helps to enhance the catalytic activity.

The hybrids of transition metal phosphides (TMPs) with carbonaceous materials like carbon nanotubes (CNTs), mesoporous carbon, graphite, graphene, etc. can not only increase the dispersion of the active components due to their high surface area and the conductivity of the hybrid catalysts, but also modulate the electron density and the distribution of electronic potential in the hybrid materials which significantly enhances their HER activity and stability.130,131

Role of media

Hydrogen evolution reactions are highly dependent on the type of electrolytes. NiCo2Px exhibits a current density of 1 mA cm−2 and 10 mA cm−2 due to HER at an overpotential of 11 mV and 58 mV respectively in KOH media, whereas Pt exhibits 1 mA cm−2 at 23 mV overpotential. In 1 M phosphate buffer (PBS) (pH = 7.0) and 0.5 M H2SO4 media, NiCo2Px requires a higher overpotential of 63 mV and 104 mV respectively to realize a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in HER.139 If we look at the Tafel slopes in various media, it is found that the HER process follows the Volmer–Tafel mechanism in KOH media, whereas the Volmer–Heyrovsky mechanism is indicated in PBS and H2SO4. In acidic media, the Volmer reaction in the HER process was effectively accelerated.139

In the mechanism of the water splitting processes139 using NiCo2Px as a catalyst (i) first, H2O molecules near the electrochemical double layer at the surface are affected. H–OH bond weakening occurs by coordination to the metal centers (Mδ+) with O atoms. The interactions between P atoms (Pδ−) having dangling bonds and hydrogen atoms are very crucial for metal phosphide based electrocatalysts. (ii) H–OH bond breaking with the aid of free electrons. (iii) OH− is adsorbed on Mδ+ sites while the hydrogen atom is adsorbed on the nearby vacant Mδ+ site. This whole process is kinetically controlled. (iv) OH− desorption from the metal sites. Now Niδ+ is more effective for OH− desorption in alkaline solution compared to Coδ+ which has higher activity in Heyrovsky and Tafel steps compared to Niδ+. In NiCo2Px, the surface Niδ+ sites assist in the dissociation of water while the surface Coδ+ sites facilitate H2 generation.139

The HER processes in neutral media are not so effective for most of the electrocatalysts. However, activated Ni3S2@NPC exhibited remarkable HER activity in neutral media.140 This is an excellent pH-universal electrocatalyst. It required an overpotential of 91.6 mV to drive a current density of 10 mA cm−2 with a Tafel slope of 63.5 mV dec−1.140 WP2 showed excellent HER activity in a wide range of pH.141 The hydrogen binding energy is proportional to the pH value.142 Yan et al.143 have shown the polarization curves for Pt in buffer solutions of varying pH values and compared the overpotential at a fixed current density of 1 mA cm−2. It was found that with increasing pH of the media, the HER activity decreases. The overpotential value changes drastically from 3 mV to 20 mV to reach a current density of 1 mA cm−2 when the pH changes from 0 to 13 (Fig. 14a). It was found that the hydrogen binding energy of Pt increases with the increase in the pH of the electrolyte (Fig. 14b). This results in stronger hydrogen binding on the Pt surface which leads to slow desorption of hydrogen. However, the role of the anions present in various electrolytes was not discussed in the hydrogen binding energies (HBE) of the Pt electrode. This suggests that the site poisoning of the Pt electrode which might result in higher overpotential was neglected.

Role of wettability of the catalyst

Interface engineering gives us a new direction to control the catalyst|electrolyte interfaces which remarkably influences the electrocatalytic activity. Generation of gas bubbles at the flat surface of the catalyst is a big issue for most of the electrocatalysts causing an ohmic drop and lowering the current density by blocking the diffusion of the electrolyte.

Jiang et al.144 have reported that MoS2 nanostructures with a superaerophobic surface show excellent HER performance compared to their flat MoS2 counterpart. The HER activity was even better than expected from Pt/C (20 wt%) at high overpotentials. This superaerophobic architecture does not allow gas bubbles to adhere on the MoS2 surface during the hydrogen evolution reaction.144,145 In contrast, the flat MoS2 shows poor HER activity due to the accumulation of bubbles on its surface. In Fig. 15a and b, the schematic representations of flat MoS2 and textured MoS2 and their corresponding SEM images are shown. This lowers the adhesion force to a gas bubble compared to that of hydrophobic electrodes. Therefore, in case of hydrophilic surfaces, more protons can interact with the active sites, and the lower adhesion force leads to the quick release of H2 bubbles from the electrode surfaces148 which maintains the contact between the catalysts and electrolyte. Summary

In this perspective, the importance of hydrogen evolution and its fundamental aspects were discussed in the light of advancement in this specific area. The fundamental electrochemistry behind the HER processes was covered. Most importantly, the properties of the nanostructures/nanocrystals which play a crucial role in controlling the HER activity of the nanocrystals were discussed. These properties include surface chemistry of the nanostructures (facets, surface energy, doping, edges, etc.), the role of heteroatoms (doping with hetero metal ions, anions), the role of supports, the role of media (electrolytes, pH) and the role of surface wettability. Each of these properties significantly contributes to the HER activity of the nanocatalysts and were discussed in detail with examples. It has been shown that surface energies, defects and exposed facets/edges are very important to control the HER activity. To design better electrocatalysts, the incorporation of heteroatoms and providing conducting supports are very important. It has been shown that the number of layers of graphene (an important substrate) plays a very crucial role. Most importantly, the surface wettability is very crucial to get better electrocatalysts.
