Abstract

Plexcitonics is a rapidly developing interdisciplinary field that holds immense potential for the creation of innovative optical technologies and devices. This field focuses on investigating the interactions between plasmons and excitons in hybrid systems. In this review, we provide an overview of the fundamental principles of plasmonics and plexcitonics and discuss the latest advancements in plexcitonics. Specifically, we highlight the ability to manipulate plasmonâ€“exciton interactions, the emerging field of tip-enhanced spectroscopy, and advancements in optical chirality and nonlinearity. These recent developments have spurred further research in the field of plexcitonics and offer inspiration for the design of advanced materials and devices with enhanced optical properties and functionalities.

1. Introduction

The plexcitonics has become a rapidly growing interdisciplinary field in the past few decades, involving the study of hybrid quasiparticles that are formed when plasmons (collective oscillation of electrons in the metallic nanostructure) coherently couple with excitons (bound states of an electron and a hole in the semiconductor).1 These hybrid quasiparticles known as plasmonâ€“exciton polaritons or plexcitons,2 can be exhibited in hybrid nanostructures, including metalâ€“semiconductor nanocomposites,3 metalâ€“semiconductor hybrid nanostructures,4 and metallic nanostructures embedded in semiconducting matrices.5,6 The interactions between plasmons and excitons in these hybrid nanostructures results in the formation of hybrid excitation modes, which exhibit properties that are distinct from those of plasmons and excitons alone. Moreover, plexcitons have properties that are intermediate between plasmons and excitons, which enables them to exhibit the best features of both. For example, plexcitons have the high energy-density of plasmons and the long coherence length of excitons, allowing them to reach distances of around 20 Î¼m.7 Additionally, it is crucial to consider that the advantages of plexcitons are dependent on the particular system and intended application.8,9 The inherent properties of the material, as well as the configuration and environment in which plexcitons, plasmons, and excitons are generated and interact, can significantly influence their observed values.10â€“12 Therefore, it is essential to perform a meticulous evaluation of the performance metrics relevant to the specific application and to assess the feasibility of incorporating plexcitons into practical devices involved.

Plexciton is essentially the polariton formed by coupling plasmon and exciton, which can undergo Boseâ€“Einstein condensation,13 leading to the formation of a macroscopically occupied polariton state. This effect promotes polariton-mediated interactions between light and matter. A conspicuous expression is enhanced lightâ€“matter interactions, resulting from the strong local electromagnetic field enhancement near metallic nanostructures.14,15 The strong response to light makes plexcitonic systems ideal for energy-related applications, such as solar cells,16,17 LEDs,18,19 and light-emitting devices.20 The hybrid nature of plexciton results in unique molecular spectroscopy, such as Fano resonances, which can be used for sensors.21â€“23 The interactions between plasmon and exciton can result in the transfer of energy, momentum, and charge between these quasiparticles,24,25 leading to a range of interesting optical and electronic properties. For example, plasmons can transfer energy to excitons, resulting in increased exciton lifetime and improved photoluminescence efficiency.26,27 Furthermore, the properties of plexcitons can be easily tuned by varying the geometry of the plasmonic structure or the composition of the material, which makes them highly versatile and suitable for a wide range of applications. The high optical nonlinearity of plexcitonic system is also worth emphasizing. In nonlinear optics, the response of the medium to light is not directly proportional to the light intensity, but instead depends on the higher-order nonlinear susceptibility which describes the relationship between the incident electric field and the induced polarization in the medium, leading to inherently weak optical nonlinearity.28 However, the strong interaction between light and plexciton polaritons in metallic nanostructures can lead to enhanced lightâ€“matter interaction and reduced light losses, and the resulting strong electromagnetic field allows weak nonlinear processes to be significantly enhanced. This makes plexcitonics highly attractive in nonlinear optics, and can be used to control and manipulate light in new and interesting ways.

According to the above mentioned, plexcitonics has attracted significant attention in the field of optoelectronics due to its potential for creating new and more efficient devices for energy conversion, light generation and detection, and information processing.29 It is important to acknowledge that the study of plexcitonics is still in the nascent stage, and significant ongoing research efforts are directed towards addressing prominent problems and uncovering the distinctive properties. The exact mechanism of plexciton formation, the role of plasmonâ€“exciton coupling in plexciton properties, and the effects of interactions between plexcitons remain incompletely understood. Moreover, the lack of stability and reproducibility of the plexcitonic materials and devices, as well as the challenge of maintaining the quantum coherence of the plexcitons over long distances and timescales, significantly limit their potential for commercial applications.30 As a result, it is imperative to develop scalable fabrication methods capable of producing uniform and reproducible plexcitonic structures, and to integrate plexcitonic devices with established technologies for realizing their diverse applications.

In this review, we aim to provide an overview of the fundamental principles of plasmonics and plexcitonics and discuss the latest advancements in plexcitonic hybrid systems. The relevant concepts of plasmons are introduced, followed by the mechanisms of plasmonâ€“exciton coupling in hybrid plexcitonic systems. More importantly, the recent outstanding breakthrough in plexcitonics is highlighted, focusing on the ability to manipulate plasmonâ€“exciton, the advancements in tip-enhanced spectroscopy, and the emerging fields of the plexcitonic optical chirality and nonlinearity. These excellent performance offers unprecedented avenue for high spatial resolution measurements and ultrafast optical switching, and endows deeper insights into the complex coupling dynamics. Overall, the recent developments in plexcitonics have led to exciting new possibilities in the design of advanced materials and devices with enhanced optical properties and functionalities. We believe that this review will provide readers with a comprehensive understanding of the latest advancements in the field of plexcitonics and inspire further research in this exciting area of study.

2. Background and physical principles

2.1. Plasmon photonics

The plasmon photonics has been developed for more than a century. During this period, a proliferation of literature with inconsistent and convoluted explanations of key conceptual terms frequently confuses novices, or even misleads them. In view of this, clear explanations of the conceptual terms involved are necessary. First of all, what is the plasma and plasmon? A specifical definition of plasma is as follows: the fourth state of matter in which electrons are separated from their nuclei, characterized by the presence of a significant portion of charged particles in any combination of electrons or ions. In other words, plasma is a description of ionized gas that can conduct electricity and respond to electromagnetic fields,31 as if metal ions are steeped in the electron cloud according to Drude model.32 Under this framework, massive metal ions are relatively static, while electrons exhibit coherently collective oscillation around metal ions, and â€œplasmonâ€ is introduced to quantify this plasma oscillation. Plasmon is a quantum quasiparticle, meaning it can exhibit the collective excitation that behaves like a particle, but is actually a phenomenon arising from the interactions of many individual particles. There are different types of plasmons, including volume plasmon, surface plasmon, and dark plasmon.
2.1.1. Volume plasmon. Volume plasmons are collective oscillations of electrons with a longitudinal wave or compressional wave, which can be induced by electron beams or charged particles, but can not be excited by electromagnetic wave in the form of transverse wave, such as light.33 The bounded electrons experience displacement and a resulting change in charge density due to the Coulomb force of the incident electron beam, as shown in Fig. 1(a). This gives rise to collective oscillations of the electrons with the plasma frequency. While volume plasmons are originally associated with free electrons, they have also been observed in semiconductors and insulators as a collective oscillation of the entire valence electron. 2.1.2. Surface plasmon. Surface plasmons (SPs) are collective oscillations of electrons that occur at the interface between a metal and a dielectric material.35 In fact, SPs share a similar nature to volume plasmons, both of which are electron oscillations waves. However, while volume plasmons can only be induced by charged particles, surface plasmons can be excited by both electron beams and light. This is because of the creating energy exchange channel between the metal and dielectric, allowing electrons in the metal to move freely and couple with the electromagnetic fields in the dielectric material. When the frequency of ambient electromagnetic field, typically photons, matches the natural frequency of the surface plasmon, the electrons on the metal surface can be excited into a collective oscillation. This phenomenon is known as surface plasmon resonance (SPR), and it can induce a new electromagnetic surface mode called surface plasmon polaritons (SPPs).36 Strictly speaking, SPs and SPPs are different types of collective oscillations of electrons. SPs are electromagnetic waves at the metalâ€“dielectric interface, while SPPs are coupled SPs with photons that form a new mode. However, plasmons can exist as either pure plasmons or mixed plasmonâ€“photon modes (plasmon polaritons), and â€œpureâ€ SP modes are only an approximation of SPPs, where the photon contribution is small or negligible, such as in the electrostatic approximation.37 Consequently, most literature conventionally considers the terms SP and SPP to be interchangeable, but in reality, they are fundamentally different.

LSPR produce internal polarization inside NPs, and this perturbation creates a scattered electromagnetic field that is radiated away from the NPs, producing detectable scattered light. Therefore, LSPPs are responsible for enhanced absorption and scattering cross-sections, as well as strongly enhanced electromagnetic fields in close vicinity of the NPs surface. 2.1.3. Dark plasmon. Plasmon modes can be classified as either â€œbrightâ€ or â€œdarkâ€, depending on whether they are radiative or non-radiative. The distinction between bright and dark plasmons applies specifically to LSPRs in metal nanostructures, where bright plasmons are radiative, meaning that they can absorb and scatter photons, and produce an electromagnetic field that can be detected outside of the nanostructures, while dark plasmons refer to plasmonic modes that can not couple to far-field radiation and are therefore â€œinvisibleâ€ to conventional optical spectroscopy techniques.50 Dark plasmons are pure near-field modes that occur in a variety of metal NPs, and arising from the plasmonic hybridization in a set of interacting NPs.51 When the electric field of one plasmon mode destructively interferes with that of another plasmon mode, the possible plasmonic hybridization is the cancellation of the oppositely oriented dipole moments, exhibiting the non-radiative nature of dark plasmons. Scheme of the different plasmonic hybridization models is shown in Fig. 1f. Therefore, experimentally probing dark plasmons is more difficult than their radiative counterparts, such as LSPs, and their properties are still not well understood in many cases. However, dark plasmons can be indirectly excited through several strategies such as electron tunneling, nonradiative energy transfer, and can interact strongly with nearby molecules or other nanoscale objects, leading to changes in their optical properties that can be detected.52 Interestingly, dark plasmons fare better in optical applications due to their longer lifetimes, because they do not couple efficiently with far-field radiation, which means they have a relatively low radiative decay rate and more time for interactions with their environment.53 Additionally, dark plasmons can contribute to energy transfer processes in metal nanostructures, which can be useful for applications such as plasmon-enhanced catalysis or sensing.

2.2. Plexciton photonics

We have discussed the metallic plasmon systems in the previous section. Next, we will introduce the background and principles of molecular exciton systems and the molecular excitonâ€“metallic plasmon systems, focusing on the plasmonâ€“exciton interactions.
2.2.1. Molecular exciton system. Excitons are quasiparticles that arise from the interaction between an electron and a hole in a semiconductor or an insulator. When an electron is excited from its ground state to a higher energy level, it leaves behind a positively charged hole. The electron and the hole interact through the Coulomb force, forming a bound state known as the exciton. This bound state behaves like a quasiparticle with its own characteristic energy, momentum, and lifetime. Excitons can be divided into two typical categories: Frenkel excitons and Wannier excitons, as shown in Fig. 2a. Frenkel excitons are formed when an electron is excited, resulting in an electronâ€“hole pair that is localized within a single molecule. Wannier excitons, on the other hand, are formed when an electron is excited from the ground state to a higher energy state, resulting in an electronâ€“hole pair that is delocalized over several molecules. Excitons have a finite lifetime, as they eventually dissociate into free electrons and holes. 3. Recent breakthrough of plexcitonics

In this section, we delve into the recent breakthrough in the plexcitonic hybrid systems, including the control of plasmonâ€“exciton coupling in plexcitonic lattices, plexcitonic tip-enhanced spectroscopy, plexcitonic optical chirality, and nonlinear plexcitonics. Collectively, this section provides a comprehensive overview of the recent progress in plexcitonics and demonstrate its potential for newly-developing research and practical applications.

3.1. Control of plasmonâ€“exciton coupling in plexcitonic lattices

The ability to control the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling is of great significance for designing and optimizing plexcitonic systems for various applications. Researchers have explored various methods to manipulate the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling, including changing the size and shape of metallic nanoparticles,66â€“69 adjusting the distance between the excitonic and plasmonic components, modifying the dielectric environment,70â€“72 and altering the energy levels of excitons. For instance, controlling the exciton energy levels can be accomplished by using different materials, such as quantum dots or organic molecules with different bandgap energies, or by modifying the electronic structure of the materials. Additionally, the use of hybrid materials, anisotropic materials, and applying external stimuli like magnetic fields, can also be used to achieve better control over the coupling strength. Recently, progress has been made in developing ultrasharp surface lattice resonances (SLRs) in plexcitonic lattices.73,74 SLRs are characterized by ultra-narrow linewidths, high quality factors, strong field enhancements, and polarization sensitivity, and the collective modes creates unlimited potential for tunable coupling properties. While SLRs are commonly observed in metallic nanostructure arrays,75 studies have also demonstrated the advantages of SLRs in other types of periodic nanostructures integrated with carbon nanotubes,76 2D transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs),77 organic dyes,78â€“80 and photosensitive complexes.81,82

3.2. Plexcitonic tip-enhanced spectroscopy

The strong lightâ€“matter interactions in plexcitonic structures can exhibit remarkably enhanced spectroscopic signals at the nanoscale. By virtue of the LSPRs, plasmonic nanostructures behave as optical nanocavities capable of confining light to the nanoscale, and result in orders of magnitude enhancements of electromagnetic fields, which especially work in surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS),84,85 and surface-enhanced fluorescence (SEF),86,87etc.88 It is known that the excitation probabilities of the molecule incorporated with nanocavities generally depend on the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling, for the plasmonic and excitonic resonances are spectrally matched. Moreover, the plexciton states can be used to enhance the sensitivity and spatial resolution of spectroscopy even at the single-molecule level, which opens up new avenues for probing fundamental photophysical processes, such as energy transfer and exciton dynamics. Recently, tip-enhanced spectroscopy (TES) technique that combines scanning probe microscopy with optical spectroscopy, has provided unprecedented sensitivity and sub-nanometer spatial resolution,89 enabling researchers to explore new frontiers in single-molecule spectroscopy and imaging.90 For instance, Zhang and co-workers independently developed a â€œscanning Raman picoscopyâ€ technique combining TES and scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM), which can be utilized to intuitively construct the chemical structure of a single molecule.91 Furthermore, their team achieved precise control over the coherent coupling between a single molecule and a plasmonic nanocavity by monitoring the evolution of Fano line shapes and photonic Lamb shifts at a sub-nanometer scale.92 By utilizing STM combined with optical spectroscopy, they were able to position a single molecule within the nanocavity with sub-nanometer accuracy, allowing them to manipulate the interactions between the molecule and the confined light. This manipulation led to the formation of hybrid plexciton polaritons, which exhibited strong coupling and tunable energy properties. These findings have profound implications for advanced technique capable of detecting single molecule.

Kim's group also made outstanding contributions in TES for the detection and analysis of individual molecule.93â€“95 In a Raman spectroscopy study on the isolated CuNc molecule,93 they used STM with tip-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (TERS) to unveil the critical role of perpendicular electromagnetic field components under resonance excitation conditions. Considering both the electromagnetic and chemical enhancement components pose limitations to the applicability of TERS, designing a new TES model is necessary. For electromagnetic enhancement, due to the parallel enhanced field to the tip stronger than that perpendicular to the tip, small and flat molecules must adsorb perpendicular or tilted to the substrate, and larger molecules must have vibrational modes with perpendicular components to the surface to benefit from electromagnetic enhancement in TES.96 On the other hand, for chemical enhancement, such as charge transfer (CT) states, molecules must be adsorbed on the metal surface, which causes significant perturbations to the electronic structures of the molecules, making it difficult to analyze their intrinsic vibrational characteristics. To solve that, Kim and his colleagues employed an ultrathin NaCl insulating film to electronically decouple the molecule from the metal surface, as illustrated in Fig. 4a. The STMâ€“TERS spectra of isolated CuNc molecule for different tip locations are shown in Fig. 4b and c, where the insets are STM images, and the Raman spectra with different colors correspond to the tip locations indicated by the corresponding colors in the insets. The distinct Raman spectral features with three kinds of vibrational symmetries in Fig. 4b allow to explore the correlation between the plasmonic nanocavity and the molecule. Note that the Raman signals exhibit minimal enhancement at the center of the molecule, which can be attributed to the fact that the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling efficiency decreases to zero when the symmetric center of plasmonic field is located directly above the center of the molecule.97 Additionally, the difference of Raman spectra shown in Fig. 4c implies that the CuNc molecule retains the intrinsic electronic structure when adsorbed on NaCl instead of directly on the Ag substrate. The TES technique based on plexcitons can not only be used to amplify Raman spectral signals, but also provides opportunities to microscopically understand the individual electronic states. For example, applying narrow-line tunable laser combined with STMâ€“TES can achieve PL spectra of single-molecule electronic and vibrational states with micro-electron volt energy resolution.94 Experiments have shown that electroluminescence (EL) spectroscopy can feasibly provide information on excitation dynamics by combining the analysis of photon signals with STMâ€“TES.98â€“100 However, EL suffers from low versatility and state-selectivity that can be overcome by PL spectroscopy. Fig. 4d uses EL-TES as a reference, and shows PL-TES spectra of H2Pc on the NaCl insulating film acquired at different tip locations, see the STM image in the inset. When the tip is away from the H2Pc molecule, the PL-TES spectrum shows almost no photon signal. When the tip approaches the molecule gradually, the photon signal rapidly increases, and the PL spectrum exhibits vibrational peaks that correspond to those in the EL-TES spectrum. The PL-TES investigation reveals that the PL process can be strongly enhanced by the plasmonâ€“exciton interactions. Additionally, the peaks in the PL-TES spectra are sharper than those in the corresponding EL-TES spectra, and the PL excitations need lower photon energies and are solely induced without charge injection than EL.

3.3. Plexcitonic optical chirality

Optical chirality is the lack of mirror symmetry in the response of materials or molecules to circularly polarized light, arising from their chiral nature. A primary tool for studying optical chirality is the circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, by characterizing the differential absorption of left-handed and right-handed circularly polarized light, providing a means to determine their stereochemistry and the underlying mechanisms of chiral optical activity. Plexcitonic optical chirality is a specific field of chiral optics that studies the strong coupling between plasmons and excitons in chiral nanostructures, as well as hybrid systems consisting of achiral nanostructures and chiral materials, resulting in enhanced chiral optical responses.104â€“106 More and more interesting experimental and theoretical phenomena resulting from the interactions between hybrid complexes of chiral molecules and plasmonic nanostructures have attracted great attention and extensive research.107â€“109 Research progress has focused on developing new chiral plexcitonic nanostructures with enhanced optical chirality and coupling,110,111 and have solved several important issues, including the selective detection of chiral molecules and observation of circularly polarized emission using plexcitonic systems.112,113 Lan et al. devoted DNA-templated strategies to manipulating chiral self-assembly of achiral chromophores on Au NP surfaces with tuned exciton transitions, achieving strongly enhanced molecular CD signal of chromophores and an induced plasmonic CD signals at visible wavelengths.114 Wu et al. experimentally investigated the optical chirality of plexcitonic system consisting of composite metal NPs and chiral J-aggregates, and developed a quasistatic theory to understand the chiral optical responses of the hybrid system.115 Chaitanya Indukuri et al. demonstrated the successful use of negative-index metamaterial (NIM) cavities as plexcitonic nanocavities to enhance fluorescence detection CD sensing, achieving background-free chiral sensing with globally enhanced volume super chiral fields, and enabling the detection of few chiral molecules at the zeptomole level.116 Gong et al. studied chirality inversion in self-assembled nanocomposites directed by curvature-mediated interactions, showing that the assembly process is highly sensitive to size of the NPs and the overall curvature of the composite structure.117 These studies have proved the potential of plexcitonic optical chirality in various fields. Nevertheless, plexcitonic optical chirality still faces lots of challenges, such as achieving greater control over plexcitonic systems, developing new experimental and theoretical models, and finding ways to effectively integrate plexcitonic systems into practical applications.

Rational theoretical frameworks facilitate unraveling the mechanisms of chirality evolution. Govorov et al.118 have made significant theoretical contributions in the field of plexcitonic chirality. They have proposed two mechanisms by which nanocrystals can influence the CD effect, where the first involves the modification of the internal electromagnetic field of chiral molecules induced by plasmonâ€“exciton interactions, and the second is the optical absorption of the nanocavityâ€“molecule complex due to the chiral currents inside the metal nanocavity induced by the dipole of the chiral molecule. Ultimately, it is confirmed that an achiral nanocrystal can dramatically alter the CD of a chiral molecule, when the nanocrystal and molecule form a complex and coherently couple through dipole and multipole Coulomb interactions. Yuan and co-workers proposed a theoretical model to interpret multi-scale chiral interactions based on high-order chiroplasmonic superstructures assembled using DNA-bound chromophores (K21) and Au@Ag NPs.119 As depicted in Fig. 5a, the complex chirality of the one-dimensional (1D) hybrid chain involves plasmon coupling (type 1), exciton coupling (type 2), and plasmonâ€“exciton coupling (type 3), and based on this model, the simulation results can be well matched with the experimental findings. The absorption spectra of CavAu@Ag series with K21 are shown in Fig. 5b, which reveal the formation of K21 J-aggregates on the CavAu@Ag NPs. The Au@Ag NPs surfaces assembled with DNA matrix are expected to dramatically influence the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling and the resultant CD line shape of the chiral hybrids. Specifically, CavAu@Ag-3 sample (Fig. 5c) shows a double-peak J-band CD spectrum with a dip, changing from the original peak-dip bisignate line shape, demonstrating the existence of plexcitonic optical chirality. The chiroptical activities of single Au@Ag NPs and 1D NP chain interacting with K21 molecules are shown in Fig. 5d and e, respectively. It is suggested that the chiroptical activities arises from both local chiral properties of the NPs and overall chirality of the DNA scaffold, further demonstrating that the chiral response can be modulated by the order of assembly of the NPs on the DNA scaffold. The CD signals of single NP hybrids (Fig. 5d) are weaker than those of corresponding 1D NP chain (Fig. 5e), attributed to the 1D chain's global structural chirality resulting from the dynamic deformation or curvature of the DNA origami tube, known as metastable twists. Therefore, it can be concluded that the complex chiral properties are the synergistic effect of multiple excitation modes couplings. Wang et al. recently carried out an investigation combined experimental and theoretical approaches referring to transferring the optical asymmetry from chiral Ï€-conjugated molecules to the plasmonic Ag NP assemblies though CHâ€“Ï€ interaction.120 They found that the absorption of 1,3,5-triphenylbenzenetricarboxamides (TPBA) in the UV-vis region would not interfere the plasmonic bands of Ag NPs, indicating that the intense CD signals over 500 mdeg shown in Fig. 5f are attributed to the plasmonic chirality from Ag NP assemblies, which demonstrates the possible transfer of the chirality from chiral molecular systems to achiral Ag NP assemblies. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations notarize the mechanisms of chirality transfer, as shown in Fig. 5g. At t = 0 ns, no contact can be observed between the aliphatic (red) and polystyrene (blue) chains, while after t = 20 ns, the aliphatic chains are penetrated into the layer of polystyrene functionalized Ag NP, suggesting a strong interaction between them probably due to the CHâ€“Ï€ interaction. Furthermore, controlling the plexcitonic optical chirality remains an important and challenging task. Interestingly, in certain plasmonâ€“exciton systems, such as coherently coupled 2D TMD-metal systems,121 optical chirality exhibits a strong magneto-optical response, which offers a viable approach. The normalized extinction spectrum inspected in Fig. 5h shows respective Fano interference line shape with a narrow dip, which provides fundamental criteria of subsequent plexcitonic optical chirality in the weak coupling regime. Fig. 5i and j illustrate that the magneto-induced CD becomes increasingly pronounced with rising magnetic field, and the CD spectra feature reversal signs at magnetic fields with reversed directions. As such, magneto-chiroptical effects could be exploited to manipulate nonreciprocal phenomena with magnetic fields, which enables the unidirectional circularly polarized photons to flow as required for information transmission in quantum networks.122

3.4. Nonlinear plexcitonics

Optical nonlinearity has emerged as a critical area of research in recent years, owing to its potential to enable new applications in ultrafast optical switches, frequency conversion, optical signal processing, etc.123 However, the complexity of nonlinear optical processes involved a variety of mechanisms and interactions between multiple particles, presents significant difficulties for understanding and controlling these phenomena. Studies of polariton nonlinearities are receiving increased attention as a promising avenue for overcoming some of the limitations.124,125 These limitations refer to challenges or drawbacks encountered in traditional nonlinear optical systems or approaches. While the specific limitations can vary depending on the system and application, some common limitations in the field of nonlinear optics and polaritonics are encountered, such as high threshold power, limited tunability, nonlinear losses, slow response time, material compatibility, etc. In particular, the recent emergence of plexcitonic systems has opened up new possibilities for achieving enhanced nonlinear effects in novel ways.126â€“129 When excitons are brought into close proximity, their Coulomb interaction can lead to modifications in the plexciton properties. The strength of the Coulomb interaction between excitons affects the energy and lifetime of these plexcitonic systems, thereby influencing their optical and electronic characteristics. Specifically, the Coulomb interaction can induce a strong coupling between the electronic excitations of the molecule and the confined photons in the plasmonic nanocavity, stimulating the formation of hybrid lightâ€“matter states known as plexcitons. The Pauli exclusion principled plays a crucial role in the strong nonlinearity observed in plexcitonic systems. It leads to a limited number of available electronic states within the molecule or material, causing confined photons in the plasmonic nanocavity to interact with specific electronic states. This restricted availability of electronic states results in enhanced optical and electronic response, leading to strong nonlinearity. The Pauli exclusion principle facilitates efficient exchange of energy and momentum between confined photons and electronic excitations, enabling the observed strong nonlinear behavior in plexcitonic systems. Furthermore, in comparison to exciton polaritons in microcavities, plasmonic nanocavities play a distinct role in determining the nonlinearity of the system. Plasmonic nanocavities exhibit a low Q-factor, indicating high efficiency in trapping and confining light. This efficiency enables rapid energy exchange between confined photons and the material, resulting in enhanced nonlinearity. Moreover, the small mode volume of plasmonic nanocavities confines the interaction to an extremely compact spatial region, intensifying the interaction and yielding stronger nonlinearity compared to microcavities. The combination of a low Q-factor and small mode volume in plasmonic nanocavities facilitates efficient energy transfer and robust lightâ€“matter coupling, thereby inducing notable nonlinear effects in the system.

The investigation of nonlinear plexcitonics is expected to yield valuable insights into lightâ€“matter interactions, such as giant Lamb shift and single-photon nonlinearity,130,131 and to inspire new approaches for developing practical applications.132 Artuso et al.133 theoretically investigated the optical response of a semiconductor QD coupled with a metal NP, and demonstrated nonlinear Fano effect due to the significant self-interaction of the semiconductor QD via metal NP feedback. Fofang et al.134 studied the ultrafast optical dynamics of nanoshell-J-aggregate complexes where plasmons and excitons interacted strongly, and ultrafast characteristics indicated that these hybrid nanostructures could possess enhanced, tunable, and on/off-resonance nonlinear optical properties. Nan et al.135 investigated the ultrafast one-photon nonlinear responses of strongly coupled hybrid plexcitonic Fano systems, revealing that the severely suppressed linear absorption around the Fano dip significantly enhanced the unidirectional energy transfer from the plasmons to the excitons and further allowed one-photon nonlinearity to be drastically and reversibly tuned. Even so, it can be said that the development of nonlinear plexcitonics is highly challenging and tortuous. One of the main bottlenecks is the lack of suitable nonlinear materials with strong plexcitonic interactions and high stability. Many of the materials used in current research are limited by their low quantum yield, poor stability, or difficulty in fabrication. Additionally, the development of techniques for controlling the nonlinear response of plexcitonic systems remains a challenge task. Furthermore, the optimization of nonlinear plexcitonic manifestation demands a comprehensive understanding of the intricate interplay between a multitude of parameters that govern the behavior of the plexcitonic systems. These parameters can include, but are not limited to, the coupling strength of plasmonâ€“exciton hybrid modes, the phonon-stimulated regime, and the pump power. These bottlenecks necessitate the interdisciplinary collaborations and the development of new materials, along with the advanced theoretical and experimental support.

Second-order nonlinear process in hybrid systems play a crucial role in the study of nonlinear plexcitonics. However, the conversion efficiency of second-harmonic generation (SHG) is typically low due to the centrosymmetry of bulk noble metals. To enhance SHG, precise control of the shape, size, or arrangement of plasmonic structures is necessary to optimize the momentum, spatial, and spectral matching between the fundamental light and second harmonics (SH). Chervy et al. reported the second-order nonlinear susceptibility of c-porphyrin nanofiber crystals by hybridization with plasmonic microcavities in the strong coupling regime.136 An intense SHG peak at 420 nm can be observed outside the cavity, as shown in Fig. 6a, which is the direct demonstration of the plasmon enhanced SHG effect. Fig. 6b is the wavelength-dependent SHG measurement under the same conditions for a c-porphyrin nanofiber inside the cavity. Instead of one maximum peak at 420 nm, the SHG intensity in the cavity has two peaks at 390 and 460 nm corresponding to the enhanced SHG from polaritonic states. Moreover, the polarization-dependent SHG signal (Fig. 6c) follows a cosine fourth curve as expected for resonantly enhanced SHG. Fig. 6d shows the SHG intensity map, and it clearly outlines the coupled fiber-cavity structure, giving a much higher SHG signal than the nearby non-coupled Ag-coated fibers, revealing that the SHG efficiency is strongly enhanced by the plasmonâ€“exciton coupling. This proof of principle opens the door for the enhanced SHG by hybrid plexciton states. Zhang et al. engineered a nonlinear plexcitonic system with an efficient and tunable second-order susceptibility.137 More than 3000-fold enhancement of SHG can be achieved at the plexciton states in a nanoparticle-on-mirror (NPOM) configuration embedded with multilayer WSe2 compared with that in the bare exciton material on the film. All the scattering spectra in Fig. 6e show two obvious peaks with anti-crossing behavior for the upper plexciton branch and lower plexciton branch, indicating the appearance of the strong plasmonâ€“exciton coupling. The SHG spectrum of the hybrid NPOM system with 11-layer WSe2 is shown in Fig. 6f. In this case, maximum enhancement factor (EF) values are achieved at 725 and 800 nm, as shown in Fig. 6h. Noting that the SHG response of the bare WSe2 on film shows a single Lorentz peak (Fig. 6h), the double-peak feature of the SHG enhancement indicates the effective tailoring of the second-order susceptibility by the strong plasmonâ€“exciton coupling. The similar SHG measurements involving the even layer (8-layer WSe2) can be seen in Fig. 6g and i. The qualitative double peaks feature of SHG spectrum implies that the SH response of the hybrid system inherits the plexciton characteristics. Moreover, the comparison of the SHG response of the two cases can best elucidate the interplay of plasmon or exciton in nonlinear conversion, that is, the spectral profile of the SHG response of the hybrid system is independent on whether the exciton has a strong SH response or not. However, the total nonlinear conversion efficiency does depend on the SH response of both the plasmon and exciton, resulting from the cooperative effort of the increased local field from the plasmon component, the second-order susceptibility from the exciton component, and their coherent coupling.

The adjustable ultrafast nonlinear properties verify nonlinear plexcitonics a promising platform for nonlinear applications, meeting the fundamental requirements for ultrafast optical modulation and optical neuromorphic computing.

4. Conclusions and perspectives

In summary, this review has shown how plexcitons, which arise from the strong coupling between surface plasmons and excitons, exhibit unique optical properties that have stimulated extensive investigations in nanophotonics and materials science. Furthermore, notable advances in plexcitonics have been highlighted, including the ability to manipulate plasmonâ€“exciton coupling in plexcitonic lattices, the use of plexcitonic tip-enhanced spectroscopy for single-molecule probing, the demonstration of plexcitonic optical chirality, and the emergence of nonlinear plexcitonic effects. These advances not only expand our fundamental understanding of lightâ€“matter interactions but also hold great promise for the development of new optical devices and technologies. Looking ahead, there are several key directions that could further advance the field of plexcitonics. Firstly, researchers could explore more complex plexcitonic systems that involve multiple plasmonic and excitonic resonances or incorporating other materials to reveal new phenomena and enhance functionality. Secondly, efforts could be made to integrate plexcitonic structures into practical devices, such as sensors, LEDs, and photovoltaic cells, to demonstrate their real-world applications. Thirdly, the development of new fabrication techniques, such as bottom-up assembly or lithography-based approaches, could enable the creation of large-scale plexcitonic structures with precise control over their properties. Finally, exploring the fundamental physics of plexcitons in extreme conditions, such as ultrafast timescales or strong magnetic fields, could reveal novel quantum effects and pave the way for new types of quantum technologies. Despite the challenging and demanding nature of scientific progress, it is believed that continued research on plexcitonic systems and their underlying physics will result in further breakthroughs and discoveries in the coming years.
