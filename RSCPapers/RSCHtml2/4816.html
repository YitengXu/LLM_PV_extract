Inner transition metal-modulated metal organic frameworks (IT-MOFs) and their derived nanomaterials: a strategic approach towards stupendous photocatalysis

Abstract

Photocatalysis, as an amenable and effective process, can be adopted for pollution remediation and to alleviate the ongoing energy crisis. In this case, recently, metal organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted increasing attention in the field of photocatalysis owning to their unique characteristics including large specific surface area, tuneable pore architecture, mouldable framework composition, tuneable band structure, and exceptional photon absorption tendency complimented with superior anti-recombination of excitons. Among the plethora of frameworks, inner transition metal based-MOFs (IT-MOFs) have started to garner significant traction as photocatalysts due to their distinct characteristics compared to conventional transition metal-based frameworks. Typically, IT-MOFs have the tendency to generate high nuclearity clusters and possess abundant Lewis acidic sites, together with mixed valency, which aids in easily converting redox couples, thereby making them a suitable candidate for various photocatalytic reactions. Therefore, in this contribution, we aim to summarise the excellent photocatalytic performance of IT-MOFs and their composites accompanied by a thorough discussion of their topological changes with a variation in the structure of the metal cluster, fabrication routes, morphological features, and physico-chemical properties together with a brief discussion of computational findings. Moreover, we attempt to explore the scientific understanding of the functionalities of IT-MOFs and their composites with detailed mechanistic pathways for in-depth clarity towards photocatalysis. Furthermore, we present a comprehensive analysis of IT-MOFs for various crucial photocatalytic applications such as H2/O2 evolution, organic pollutant degradation, organic transformation, and N2 and CO2 reduction. In addition, we discuss the measures employed to enhance their performance with some future directions to address the challenges with IT-MOF-based nanomaterials.

1. Introduction

With the rapid development of the field of photocatalysis, many researchers have devoted their efforts to the design and fabrication of novel nanomaterials. Semiconducting materials rich in active sites, desirable band structure, and superior exciton anti-recombination remain the perquisites for excellent photocatalytic outputs. However, the traditional inorganic catalysts cannot satisfy all the above-mentioned criteria. Thus, the introduction of hybrid organicinorganic materials has revolutionized the field of photocatalysis. Typically, the realization of permanent porosity in these materials has further propelled their role as light active catalysts. Among them, metal organic frameworks have become very popular. Moreover, metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are not restricted to a single application, rapidly demonstrating an astounding impact in various fields. MOFs can be described as hybrids porous frameworks containing metal ions/metal ion clusters coordinated through organic ligands. They are extremely porous because of the combination of an infinite number of potential building blocks to form hybrid crystalline materials with flexible1 structural geometries extended over 3D frameworks.1,2 Owing to the synergistic effect of both their metal nodes and organic linkers, MOFs and their derivatives are suitable candidates for a wide range of solar light-illuminated catalysis.35 In fact, in the last several years, the increase in reports on MOFs in the literature has provided diverse framework structures, which are fabricated using various metals as metal nodes and diverse categories of polytopic linkers such as cationic, anionic, and neutral linkers as another building block of the framework.6,7

Additionally, MOFs possess a designable pore structure, high surface area (7800 m2 g1), free volume (90%), and low density (0.124 g cm3), making them superior to related materials such as conventional nanomaterials, zeolites, and 1D-coordination polymers.8 Based on properties, MOFs can act as excellent photocatalysts for diverse applications, as reported by various research groups.9 Basically, a photocatalyst should have some suitable features, as follows: (i) high light absorption ability followed by electronhole separation at their corresponding conduction band and valence band position, (ii) high exciton (electronhole) life time and low recombination rate, (iii) high surface area to adsorb reactant molecules to enable redox reactions, (iv) suitable band edge potential and (v) thermal and chemical stability, to act as a potent material for various applications. In this context, besides achieving all the above-mentioned criteria, the remarkable surface area, ultrahigh porosity, easy chemical tunability, low synthesis cost, and environmentally friendly nature of MOFs make them robust candidates towards photocatalysis.1,10 Additionally, an efficient photocatalyst should possess a bandgap (Eg) energy of less than 3 eV, enabling light absorption to occur in the abundantly available visible range to maximize solar power utilization. Therefore, the structural engineering of MOFs can be realized through pre- or post-synthetic approaches, in which metals and/or ligands can be post-synthetically exchanged or additional molecules grafted to the backbone of the ligand to achieve the above-mentioned target.9 Further, recent research revealed the construction of an MOF with an even more complex structure, which consists of a variety of organic ligands as well as hetero-metallic ions having different functionalities but the same connectivity with the metal SBUs, which are referred to as multivariate MOFs, showing a noticeable performance enhancement compared to pristine MOFs.

In this context, the use of potential semiconductors as the metallic node in MOFs can be beneficial to diminish their charge carrier recombination probability and enhance their light absorption capacity. Among the various types of MOFs, inner transition metal-based MOFs are gaining significant attention as photocatalysts because of their variable valence states in metallic oxo-clusters, thereby promoting flexible bonding with a plethora of organic linkers and enabling the fabrication of rigid complex structures capable of performing specific applications. Therefore, combining or inserting a metal from the inner transition series in the framework structure can overcome many potential barriers observed for the pristine d-block metal organic frameworks. Additionally, one advantageous property of inner-transition metals is their luminescent nature, which combined with other transition metals, semiconductors, or MOF-derived templates can enhance the photocatalytic efficiency of composite materials. Diverse IT-metal-based MOFs (IT-MOFs) have been reported to date by various research groups and their historical development is outlined in Scheme 1. The basic advantages of IT-metals are their high electropositive character and diverse coordination numbers (6, 12, etc.), which make them good Lewis acids and feature high nuclearity clusters. This leads to varying complex framework geometries, which facilitate the creation of multiple structures that are not possible by conventional d-block metals.6

Moreover, the presence of oxygen vacancies in inner transition elements plays a crucial role in trapping the photoexcited electrons, which causes enhanced lifetime of photoexcitons and delays the recombination process. Additionally, the empty low lying 4f-orbitals of IT-MOFs are capable of trapping the photoexcited electrons at the catalyst surface or the between VB and CB position of IT-MOFs, which cannot be achieved in traditional d-block metal-based MOFs.11,12 These two important factors are the advantages of IT-MOFs to trap the photoexcited electrons and delay the recombination of excitons, resulting in superior photocatalytic activity.1317 In this regard, Zhang et al. reported that the low-lying unoccupied 4f orbitals of Ce4+ in UiO-66(Ce) have longer-lived excited states and facile electronhole separation, with attractive catalytic performance.18 Additionally, Qiu et al. explained the contribution of the low-lying empty 4f orbitals of Ce towards the extension of the light absorption range to the visible region, which leads to the efficient visible-light oxidation of benzyl alcohol.12 Because of these important properties, MOFs such as Ce-MOFs, Nd-MOFs, Eu-MOFs, Tb-MOFs, Ho-MOFs, Yb-MOFs, U-MOFs, Pr-MOFs, and Sm-MOFs have been formulated by several research groups.

Additionally, IT-metals are capable of forming labile coordination complexes. Subsequently, the ligand exchange phenomenon becomes facile, which promotes the effortless post-synthetic modification (PSM) of IT-MOFs. All lanthanoids (except La3+ and Lu3+) show luminescence nature with high specificity, sensitivity, transient time, feasibility, and repeatability.1921 Moreover, in the actinide series, because of the relatively vast natural abundance, low specific activity, and intermediate chemical toxicity of uranium, U-MOFs have realized the most visible development. IT-metals have a broad range of coordination numbers and geometries, with minor energy variations between them, and their geometry is governed mostly by ligand steric hindrance, making them a lucrative option towards photocatalysis. Although this complicates the structural prediction of MOFs, it simultaneously offers the possibility of uncovering a large variety of novel structures with unique features such as high aqueous stability, high coordination number, presence of low-lying vacant f-orbitals, easily convertible redox couples, and potent Lewis acidic characteristics. Owing to these superiorities, there has been significant traction in the development of IT-MOFs over the past two decades, as depicted in the Web of Science publication database and illustrated in Scheme 2. Additionally, the presence of chromophoric (-conjugated systems) linkers, which have the ability to trap the photon of required wavelength, and linkers containing functional groups (auxochromes), modifying both the wavelength and intensity of absorption, have a significant impact on the IT-MOF band structure. They change the position of the corresponding highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the aromatic moiety and enhance the light-trapping ability of the framework. In addition, linkers with rigid structural backbones are generally preferred, given that the rigidity makes it easier to interpret the network geometry in advance of synthesis. Moreover, the rigidity also helps to withstand the open pore structure even after the removal of entrapped solvent.9 Given that MOFs are considered as molecules assembled to form a crystalline structure, their orbitals are denoted as HOMO and LUMO according to the frontier molecular orbital (FMO) theory instead of the VB and CB, respectively. The photocatalytic reaction takes place only when the energy of a photon equal to or greater than the HOMOLUMO energy gap of the IT-MOF falls on the semiconductor material. Then, the electrons get excited to the LUMO via the charge transfer mechanism mostly through the ligand to metal charge transfer (LMCT) process. Similar to semiconductor-based photocatalysis, the segregation and transportation of charge carriers to the active surface sites are crucial to carry out various redox reactions. The ELMCT of IT-MOFs plays a pivotal role in the anti-recombination of excitons and effective photocatalysis. Thus, the detailed study of IT-MOFs and related nanomaterials for photocatalysis has significant implications.

In this review, we mainly focus on inner-transition metal-based MOFs and their composites (pristine f-MOFs, df multi-variate MOFs, f-MOF-derived products, and their nano-composites) as photocatalysts. They are considered as a class of progressing MOF-based materials useful in the direction of photocatalytic performance, controllable hierarchy in improved surface characteristics, and multi functionalities. The importance of diverse types of IT-metal nodes and the corresponding linkers towards the morphological and physico-chemical variation is discussed thoroughly. In addition, the involvement of f-block metals in MOFs as a metal node enhances their catalytic and photocatalytic activity because of the metalligand synergistic effect and the details are discussed in the required section. Although many reviews have been published on inner transition MOFs, the importance of f-block metals and their junction with various other nanomaterials, derived products, and multivariate inner transition MOFs with d-elements has not been specifically discussed thus far with a focus on photocatalysis.6,22,23 Therefore, in this review, we present the prominence of IT-MOFs, their multitrack synthesis protocols, characterization, and potential applications relevant to photocatalytic energy and environmental usage. 2. Structural features of IT-MOFs

The structural variations of MOFs are basically influenced by the choice and nature of their metal ions/clusters and linkers used in the framework design. Typically, the inner transition elements with variable coordination numbers generate multitudinous secondary building units (SBUs) with polydentate linkers, thereby generating numerous 3D frameworks. Moreover, to analyse the structural properties of IT-MOFs, a thorough examination of their fundamental building blocks is adopted. Accordingly, the detailed explanation is categorized into two categories, i.e., (i) influence of metal nodes and (ii) influence of linker.

2.1. Influence of metal nodes

Metal nodes are one of the integral parts of the MOF structure, which influence their morphological and physiochemical characteristics. Among the metallic elements, the d-block and f-block elements are widely accepted choices for the construction of the framework because of their high coordination numbers and diverse chemical properties. Typically, some of the d and f block elements employed in the fabrication of pristine and multivariate IT-MOF-based materials are listed in Scheme 4. Here, specifically, the underlying science of inner transition elements toward the formation of multinuclear MOF clusters is further elaborated. In this regard, the metal nodes are categorized into three sections, as follows: (i) metal ions, (ii) metal chains and (iii) metal clusters, which are subsequently discussed in detail with various reported examples.

2.1.3. Metal clusters as nodes. The most prominent category of metal SBUs for the formation of frameworks is metal clusters. Typically, these clusters are oxo- and hydroxo-bonded polymetallic clusters. These SBUs of IT-MOFs as reported by various groups can be segregated into multiple categories as a dinuclear metal cluster, trinuclear metal cluster, tetranuclear metal cluster, hexanuclear metal cluster, heptanuclear metal cluster, and nonanuclear metal cluster depending on the structural complexity and nuclearity. The metal cluster is named according to the number of metal ions present in the cluster together with the oxo-hydroxo bonding involved. The metal cluster can be regarded as the most stable form of the metal node compared to the metal ion and metal chains because of their multiple bridging units, restorable sacrificial hydroxyl moieties, and bonding rigidity. Various research groups have reported diverse MOFs by using these metal clusters. Some of the predominant IT-MOF clusters formed are discussed as follows.
2.1.3.1. Dinuclear clusters. Dinuclear clusters are formed by the combination of two metal ions by MM bonding having oxo and hydroxo linkages as auxiliary support. The dinuclear metal clusters of lanthanide and actinide series are widely used with ligand moieties to form diverse metalorganic hybrids. In this aspect, Yaghi et al. reported the preparation of MOF-9 having rectangular channels and pcu topology by using dinuclear Tb(III) as cluster nodes, as shown in Fig. 1(I) and azobenzene-4,4-dicarboxylic acid as the linker. The crystal structure of Tb2C6O12 SBUs represents that every Tb ion is connected to five ADB linkers, where four are coordinated as di-monodentate and one as bidentate linker bridging between the Tb ions. Two DMSO molecules are also bonded to each Tb atom with Tb-OS(CH3)2 = 2.40 (2) to give eight coordinated Tb centers, producing an octahedral topology of C6 units that are joined by long linkers to provide a 3D structure.6,27 Similarly, Gao et al., Stylianou et al., and Tasiopoulos et al. reported the preparation of diverse MOFs using trivalent inner transition ions such as Gd(III), Nd(III), and Eu(III) as dinuclear metal clusters taking various linkers such as S,S-dioxodibenzothiophen-3,7-dicarboxylic acid, tris(p-carboxylic acid) tridurylborane and 5-(4-carboxybenzylideneamino) isophthalic acid, respectively.3032 Gao et al. reported that the crystal structure of the dinuclear cluster, as shown in Fig. 1(III), contains two Eu(III) ions, three DBTDC-O2 ligands, and four solvent (DMF) molecules with eight oxygen atoms coordinated to each Eu ion to produce a distorted square antiprismatic geometry. The above-mentioned Eu-cluster is assembled with the linker having a non-linear geometry to produce an MOF with sxb topology, which is different to the traditional pcu of similar MOFs. Alternatively, Stylianou et al. explained the structure of binuclear Eu(III)2 clusters, as shown in Fig. 1(IV), with two Eu(III) atoms joined by two 2-bridging H2O molecules and two tctb3 ligands. The metal ions in the cluster are separated from each other with a distance of 3.9679(11) and the interconnection between the dinuclear cluster together with linker leads to the generation of a polymeric framework extending in 3-D having small, zig-zag-shaped channels in SION-105. Alternatively, Tasiopoulos et al. shown that the 6-coordinated nodes with the formula Nd2(COO)6(H2O)1.2(DMF)2.8, where each SBU consists of two, nine-coordinated Nd3+ ions and six carboxylate ions from six different CIP3 ligands linked through tri-coordinated linkers, give rise to a flu topology with the average cavity diameter of 3 to 4 and connected by smaller pores of around 2 .6,32
2.1.3.2. Trinuclear clusters. Because of the relatively higher stability of trinuclear clusters, it has been observed that their formation tendency is also very high. Therefore, they are formed under a wide variety of conditions and their triangular M3, skeleton remains intact in ligand exchange reactions, making them well-suited for post-synthetic modifications. Although these clusters are very common for d-block series, few examples exist using IT-elements because of the empty f orbitals and the higher coordination capacity of inner transition metals. In this case, Cheng et al. engineered an MOF with the formula IT3(ATPT)2(HATPT)4, in which each SBU was formed by the linking of two Ln3 nodes through two ATPT anions as a double bridge in a triangular form, which is then joined with non-coordinated ATPT ligand to create a pcu topology, as shown in Fig. 1(VI).6,33 An isostructural MOF series with Dy(III), Yb(III), and Eu(III) metal sources and BTC linker was fabricated by Zhao et al., which was reported to have 1D channels with a pore aperture in the range of 3.286.55 and calculated void occupancy of 39.6%. Herein, each SBU consisted of three building blocks, i.e., two separate Eu3+ ions and one Eu3-cluster. The Eu3 cluster has an eight-coordinated distorted square antiprism geometry, which is attained by six oxygen atoms from the carboxylic group of the linkers and two H2O molecules. Moreover, three distinct building blocks are joined by a BTB3 linker to form a 3D anionic framework.6,34 Additionally, Liu et al. prepared JXNU-3, which consisted of both nonanuclear and trinuclear IT(III)-cluster (IT = Er[III] Gd[III], and Tb[III]) using 2-fluorobenzoic acid as a modulator, where the trinuclear cluster [Ln3(O2C)6-(HCO2)3(3-O)(H2O)3] exhibits six-fold symmetry, with six carboxylate ions from BTB ligands capping the cluster and a 3-O in the center.35
2.1.3.3. Tetranuclear clusters. Although tetranuclear clusters are not as common as other clusters some IT-clusters with a square planar, rectangular, cubane tetranuclear geometry have been reported. In this regard, Gao et al. synthesized Gd(III) and Dy(III)-MOFs in 2000, which are made up of cubane-like tetranuclear IT(III) clusters, as shown in Fig. 1(VIII), i.e., IT4O4 nodes, with L-proline or L-aspartic acid linkers connecting them. The highly porous structure having formula Dy4(asp)3(3-OH)4(H2O)8 with an average pore aperture 12 in diameter was obtained even without removing the water and perchlorate solvent molecules from the framework.6 Furthermore, a mixed metal MOF was synthesized by Yang et al. using nicotinic acid and 2-pyrazinecarboxylic acid as the linkers with two different metal nodes of Cu(I) and Er(III), or Yb(III). The chair-like Cu(I) cluster node combined with cubane-like Er(III) cluster node produces a highly porous framework.36 Also, Petoud et al. and Rosi et al. explained a family of isostructural IT-MOFs with pcu topology formed by cubane-like tetranuclear Gd(III)-clusters, i.e., [Gd4 (COO)6(3-OH)4(H2O)8x (1-OH)x](2x)+, as shown in Fig. 1(IX), linked by the linear aminophenylene linkers to design the 3-D framework.37 The four cubane-like structures have the chemical formula Gd4(3-OH)4 with tetrahedral symmetry located at the vertices of the tetrahedron and bridged by four 3-OH groups. Alternatively, Zhou et al. discovered PCN-17, a series of IT-MOFs made of unique square planar tetranuclear clusters of Er(III), Dy(III), or Yb(III) metal nodes, as shown in Fig. 1(X), and 4,4,4-striazine-2,4,6-triyl-tribenzoate linker having trigonal planar shape. Here, the Ln4 (4-H2O) SBU consists of four Ln atoms at the vertices of a square with 4-H2O residing at the center. All the Ln atoms in the SBU are in the same plane and coordinated to seven oxygen atoms, i.e., four from carboxylate ions and four from different TATB ligands. The materials were activated despite their tiny pore diameters of around 3.5 under dynamic vacuum at 250 C and their BET surface area was calculated to be 600820 m2 g1 by the CO2 adsorption process. Lastly, a rectangular tetranuclear IT-cluster (IT = Tb(III), Dy(III), Ho(III), Er(III) or Yb(III)) was reported for the preparation of IT-csq-MOF-1 using 2-fluorobenzoic acid acting as a modulator to prevent the formation of metal chains by Trikalitis et al. The IT-csq-MOF-1 features a csq topology, with large hexagonal channels nearly 16 in diameter, surrounded by six smaller triangular channels of approximately 6 .38
2.1.3.4. Hexanuclear clusters. Metal clusters containing six metal atoms have been known for their stability and diversity for many years. This is due to the framework flexibility, which is crucial to be realized due to the synergistic and cooperative effect between different types and strengths of interactions, including intra- and intermolecular forces of interaction, coordination bonds, and hostguest interactions.39 To date, many hexanuclear clusters containing IT-elements have been reported with octahedral, hexagonal prism, cuboctahedron, and triangular cupola building units. One of the potential IT-metal-based SBUs as a building block for the synthesis of MOFs was discovered by Yao et al. in 2008, which was formed by the repetition of an octahedral unit having the formula [Er6 (3-OH)8 (6-O)]8+ in a unidirectional manner. This resulted in the formation of a rod-shaped structure, which is a hybrid of an IT(III) chain and IT(III) cluster node, as shown in Fig. 1(XIII).40 Another interesting example by Almeida Paz, Rocha et al. involves the connection of a hexanuclear cluster, i.e., [Pr6(3-O)2(3-OH)6]8+, with another cluster through one Pr(III) ion to form a hybrid of metal chain-cluster node, as shown in Fig. 1(XIV).41 Moreover, in 2010, Monge et al. composed an octahedral IT(III)-hexanuclear cluster as nodes, as shown in Fig. 1(XII) with p-sulfonatocalix[4]arene as linkers to prepare an MOF, namely, RPF-9. Here, each ligand is coupled to four clusters, and the cluster with 6-OH is located in the middle of an oh-face having six OH group joined to eight sulfonate groups from the linkers, resulting in a framework with voids of 11.87 (height) between calixarene molecules and 8.32 (width) among clusters.42 The preparation of another MOF was reported by Lang et al. using an IT(III) hexanuclear cluster, i.e., [Eu6(3-OH)8(6-O)(H2O)14]8, linked by TPPS linkers, yielding an open framework having pores ranging from 4 to 5 . MOFs were generated in both examples by adjusting the pH of the reaction solution with triethylamine (TEA) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH), respectively.43 A series of Tb-sph-MOFs was reported to be comprised of Tb(III) hexanuclear cluster nodes having a cuboctahedron geometry with 12 coordinating sites bridged by a tridentate and hexadentate linker to form the sph topology. The Tb-sph-MOFs have two tetrahedral cages with the pore length of 6.522 and 2.59.5 , respectively, and BET surface areas ranging from 10202170 m2 g1. Eddaoudi et al. and Li et al. showed different topologies, i.e., fcu and ftw, of IT-MOFs using 2-fluorobenzoic acid as a modulator to control the formation of a hexanuclear cluster, i.e., [IT6 (N4C)6(3-OH)8 (O2C)6]2, and bridging these through di and tetratopic linkers, respectively.44,45 Additionally, Zhou et al. prepared the [(CH3)2NH2]2[Eu6(3-OH)8(TCPP)1.5(DCDPS)3](solvent)x MOF using the mixed linker concept and concluded that it possessed the novel tam topology. For the synthesis of PCN-900(IT) (IT = Tb(III) Yb(III), Eu(III), and Dy(III)), they used a hexanuclear IT(III)-cluster, where each Eu6 cluster was connected with six TCPP linkers to form the scaffold structure, which is isostructural to PCN-224 having twelve coordinating sites together with square planar tetradentate and bent bidentate linkers. The bent 4,4-dicarboxydiphenyl sulfone (DCDPS) linker in this MOF series can be substituted via a post-synthetic procedure with linear ditopic linkers such as BPDC to produce a broad class of MOFs with various chemical functions, as shown in Fig. 1(XVII). The surface areas of the PCN-900 (Eu) series vary in the range of 17302525 m2 g1, which were made up of several ditopic linkers.46

In one example, Gao et al. used a dicubane IT(III) node, i.e., [Yb7 (3-OH)8]13+, in which the 3-OH groups are also considered as vertices of the polyhedron, choosing Yb(III) and Ho(III) as the metal source linked through naphthalene-based dicarboxylic acid to prepare an MOF.47 In the second example, Zhang et al. prepared an MOF with the same heptanuclear IT(III)-cluster choosing Sm(III), Tb(III), or Eu(III) as the metal and a linear bidentate ligand having an hxl topology.48 Also, a trigonal prismatic heptanuclear IT(III)-node was used to prepare an MOF by Yang et al., as shown in Fig. 1(XVIII).49

Using a nonanuclear IT(III)-cluster, i.e., [IT9(2-OH)3 (O2C)18(3-OH)8] (IT = Y Tb, Er, and Eu), with 18 binding sites as a molecular building unit, Eddaoudi et al. in 2014 revealed two different topologies, i.e., gea and gez. gea-MOF-1 has a complicated architecture with three types of voids, constructed with triangular tritopic BTB linkers having a surface area of 1490 m2 g1.50 It was reported by this group that the use of tritopic linkers, i.e., biphenyl-3,4,5-tricarboxylic acid and 5-(4-carboxybenzyloxy) isophthalic acid, led to the formation of nonanuclear clusters with 18 binding sites, as shown in Fig. 1(XIX). 2.2. Influence of linker towards the construction of MOFs

MOFs are constructed by the coordination of metal ions or metal clusters (SBUs) with organic struts, which are mostly carboxylic acid- or hetero atom (such as nitrogen)-containing ligands, as shown in Fig. 1(df).53,54 The rational design and development of new functionalized linkers are highly desirable for the successful formation of MOFs. Moreover, linkers and their functional groups further assist in pore aperture control, guest moiety selectivity and anchoring sites for PSM, thereby making them indispensable entities in modulating the framework functionality. Based on charge accumulation and ion formation, the organic linkers can be typically divided into three groups, as follows: (i) anionic linkers, (ii) cationic linkers, and (iii) neutral linkers, as shown in Fig. 1(df), respectively. Among these linkers, the anionic and neutral linkers are considered suitable for the formation of MOFs given that the metallic clusters are mostly electropositive in nature. Secondly, the favorable metal-linker binding can be determined in accordance with Pearson's HSAB (HardSoft Acid Base) principle. Particularly, the linkers found in the structures of IT-MOFs are diverse, ranging from bidentate to multitopic connectivity, including sulfonate, nitroso, pyridine-based, phosphate, and carboxylate functional groups. Because IT-metal-based clusters have a high coordination number, the complexity of IT-MOFs is regulated by utilizing lengthy, complex linkers (i.e., multitopic rather than ditopic ligands).55 Due to the adequate stability of the resulting frameworks, the isoreticular synthesis technique can be regarded as a desirable method for the amplification of pore volume from the microporous to mesoporous range (250 nm). By methodically lengthening the ligand structure, an isoreticular succession of IT-MOF structures can be produced, and similar to the dicarboxylate IT-MOF series, the structural diversity by multi-carboxylate linkers is quite difficult to form straightforward isoreticular expansion of frameworks given that slight modifications to the linker can have significant implications on the structure of the framework.8 The types of diverse linkers to form IT-MOFs and isoreticular IT-MOFs are shown in Fig. 1(cf).

3. MOF fabrication routes

The vacant orbitals of metal ions indirectly define the coordination number of metals, which helps to determine the number of ligands that can bind to them, thereby modulating the desired features and functionalities of the resulting framework.57 The employment of diverse metal ions and ligands, as well as varied reaction conditions such as solvents, duration, temperature, and pressure always result in the formation of unique MOFs. The affinity between the metal ions and linkers is one of these variables that play a crucial role in the nucleation, construction, and final morphology of nanomaterials. Additionally, the temperature and solubility of the reactants in the solvent system have an impact on the formation of reaction sites during nucleation. Considering all these parameters, the fundamental principle behind the chemical interplay between metal ions and linkers in the construction of MOFs is the Pearson HSAB principle, which demonstrates that the greater chemical stability in the MOF framework is due to the polarizability and bonding characteristics developed between the strongly charged metal ions (hard acid) and carboxylate linkers (hard base) or vice versa.58 Similarly, the IT-MOFs synthesized with a controlled morphology, composition, structure, and size have a significant impact on their photocatalytic applications. Moreover, selectivity and band gap tuning can be achieved by choosing the appropriate combination of metal nodes and linkers. To date, numerous methods such as solvothermal/hydrothermal method, microwave-assisted method, reflux method, sonochemical method, ion exchange deposition method, and DBD plasma method have been reported by many research groups for the synthesis of IT-MOFs. Furthermore, post-synthetic changes can also be used to synthesize modified IT-MOFs as well as IT-MOF derived materials, which are particularly useful for improving their features by replacing, altering, or eliminating linkers or nodes from the framework. In this section, the possible methods for the synthesis of IT-MOF-based nanomaterials are discussed as follows, and moreover, these fabrication routes are illustrated in Scheme 5.

3.1. Hydrothermal/solvothermal method

The hydro/solvothermal synthesis method mainly takes place at elevated conditions of temperature and autogenous pressure above the boiling point of the solvent to cause chemical integration (by combination and/or decomposition of the precursor components), improving the solubility of salts, which is a conducive condition to shape the desired output directly from a solution. The term solvothermal refers to the involvement of a solvent during the synthesis of MOFs, where if water is used as the solvent, then the term hydrothermal is preferred. In this case, the reaction is carried out in a specific closed chemical container (stain-less steel autoclave or sealed apparatus having the capability to withstand high pressure). This method is very suitable to form stable MOF grids and produces high yielding products together with the added benefit that the reaction temperature, precursors, solvents, pH and reaction time can be flexibly adjusted to obtain the desired IT-MOF morphology. Various IT-MOFs such as Eu-MOF, Ce-UiO-66, Ce/Eu-HKUST-1, and Gd-MOF have been described by various research groups.5963 Lina et al. synthesized isostructural IT-MOFs having La, Nd, Sm, Ho, and Er metal nodes with 9,9-spirobi[9H-fluorene]-2,2-dicarboxylic acid (H2sfdc) linker via the solvothermal route and heating at 200 C for 72 h in water. The fabricated MOFs displayed stability up to 420 C as well as ribbon-of-rings topology. The light gathering antennas and active sites for the catalytic performance of the above-mentioned materials are beneficial for the oxidative reaction of amine to imine using molecular oxygen from the air the oxidizing agent. In addition, Xia et al. reported the preparation of Ce, Tb and Dy coupled with an anthracene-derived dicarboxylate linker to form a broad band visible-active MOF by solvothermal treatment (85 C for 3 days in DMF), which showed the calculated band gap energy (Eg) of 2.11, 2.20, and 2.15 eV, respectively.59 Similarly, Choi et al. used the hydrothermal technique to prepare an Eu-BTB MOF having a BET surface are 1001 m2 g1, with an average pore volume of 0.50 cm3 g1 and a pore dimension of 2 nm.60

3.2. Microwave-assisted method

Microwaves are a type of electromagnetic radiation (frequency/ = 30030000 MHz), which are exploited for synthesis, involving the rapid interaction of electrical components of microwave and mobile electric charges of the polar solvent and reactant molecules to obtain the product in a much shorter time compared to the conventional hydrothermal method. Solvents that are capable of transforming electromagnetic energy into heat by absorbing microwave energy through molecular vibrations and rotations are generally used and the selection of solvents was experimented by di-electric loss tangent as the effectiveness of solvent increases with greater dielectric loss. Moreover, they have greater energy conversion efficiency, fast crystallization, controlled morphology, good particle size distribution, and phase-selectivity. Various IT-MOFs such as Ce-TCPP, CeO2/N-doped carbon/Ce-TCPP, Tb-TCPP, and Eu-TCPP have been fabricated using this method by various research groups.64 Jiang et al. synthesized a 2D Tb-TCPP nano-sheet MOF via the microwave synthesis method and its bandgaps were found to be 1.65 eV, 1.68 eV, 1.70 eV, and 1.72 eV for Tb-TCPP (14), respectively, for various thicknesses.65 Lin et al. reported the preparation of Ce-MOF [Ce(BTC)(H2O)]DMF via the microwave-assisted synthesis method and its BET surface area was found to be 62 m2 g1.66 Zhao et al. used the microwave approach to prepare Ce-MOF in 2 h at 100 C. Then, the pyrolysis of the Ce-TCPP sample was carried out for 2 h under an N2 flow at a temperature ranging from 300 C to 550 C to synthesize heterogeneous CeO2/N-doped C/Ce-TCPP catalysts. Ce-TCPP has a BET surface area of 36.4 m2 g1, and its BJH pore size distribution verified its mesopore structure.67

3.3. Reflux method

The reflux method can be considered one of the facile synthesis techniques in which the framework fabrication process is carried out at a consistently elevated temperature for a given interval. However, although this method is one of the most useful methods towards the synthesis of IT-MOFs, the obtained products are relatively less stable, possess non-uniform particle size distribution, aggregated particles, and demonstrate indiscernible morphology compared to that from the hydrothermal synthesis method.68 These setbacks tend to make the framework susceptible to diminished crystallinity with relatively poor textural features. Although various MOFs such as Ce/Zr-UiO-66 and U-MOF have been reported thus far, Khandan et al. synthesized a spherical uranyl-curcumin MOF by the reflux method using ethanol-water as the solvent at 50 C for 4 h with a low BET surface area of 42.62 m2 g1, together with an average pore aperture of 2.74 nm.70

3.4. Sonochemical method

The sonochemical synthesis approach is considered one of the fastest, smoothest, and environmentally benign methods for the synthesis of nano MOFs using 20 kHz to 10 MHz ultrasound radiation. This reaction is milder and faster than the solvothermal approach, and it can happen in a short time period, making it lucrative for bulk production. Furthermore, acoustic cavitation in solvent happens when the resultant precursor mixture interacts with ultrasonic vibrations, resulting in the creation of a huge number of tiny bubbles with varying pressures. The moment the bubbles collide and collapse, they create a massive quantity of energy, which promotes the framework self-assembly reaction.66,67 Importantly, an increase in temperature and pressure hinders particle aggregation, which favors smaller framework nucleation with narrower particle size distributions. Additionally, the bond breakage in large polymer molecules becomes facile together with the expulsion of the solvation shells and adsorbates from the surface of the material, which is beneficial for the generation of new active sites. Owning to these synthetic benefits, IT-MOF composites such as the g-C3N4@Ce-MOF-based heterojunction have been fabricated. Karimia et al. reported the synthesis of Ce-UiO-66 by the ultrasonic method in 1 h. The produced Ce-UiO-66 had a polyhedron structure, with micro particles ranging in size from 0.1 to 2 m, together with a high specific BET surface area of 1089.09 m2 g1 (Ce-MOF).61

3.5. Ion exchange deposition method

Ion exchange is a stoichiometric and reversible procedure, where one ionic species replaces another on the exchanger. The ionic functional groups and metal ions from the cluster are exchanged through sites having the capability of creating an electrostatic connection with an oppositely charged ion. The movable counter ions are substituted by other ions with the same charge from the solution. This process needs to be repeated several times for better results but this leads to multiple disadvantages including a complicated synthesis route, long reaction time, and solvent-consuming process.68,70 The preparation of MOFs such as Ce-doped Fe-MIL-101-NH2 sensitized with Ag3PO4 has been reported using this post-synthetic modification technique. Zhao et al. reported the synthesis of an Fe-MIL-101-NH2 MOF doped with Ce through an in situ ion-exchange deposition approach, which was further sensitized by Ag3PO4. The SEM images revealed that the Ag3PO4 nanoparticles were unevenly distributed on the spherical Ce/Fe-MIL-101-NH2. The composite, Ce/Fe-MIL-101-NH2/Ag3PO4, showed greater light absorbance than the pristine Ag3PO4, which was examined by UV-Vis DRS spectra, and the Ce3+/4+ interconvertible redox couple promoted the migration of photo-induced excitons. Moreover, an increase in the content of Ce dopant in the framework enhanced the efficacy of electronhole pair separation, thereby boosting the photocatalytic output.70

3.6. Dielectric barrier discharge (DBD) plasma method

The DBD method typically employs a high-voltage alternating current at frequencies ranging from lower radio frequency to the microwave region. Plasma is created in configurations with an insulating (dielectric) substance between the electrodes, which is responsible for the self-pulsing operation, popularly known as dielectric barrier discharge (DBD).71 This method is less suitable towards the formation of stable MOF in comparison with the hydrothermal method. IT-MOFs such as Ce-MOFs were prepared using this method, and further used as a template for the fabrication of MOF-derived CeO2. Tao et al. synthesized Ce-MOFs using the DBD plasma-assisted technique, where Ce-MOFs were produced by non-thermal plasma discharging with an applied voltage of 5.2 kV for 2 h. Initially for a short discharge time (up to 1 h), agglomerated large particles with poor dispersibility were formed, while on prolonging discharging period (2 h), uniform rod-like crystals were produced. Additionally, the synthesized MOF templates were further calcined at 200 C and 250 C to get morphologically unaltered CeO2 having abundant oxygen vacancies with a bandgap of 2.35 and 2.6 eV, respectively.63

3.7. Thermal treatment

Thermal treatment entails exposing the MOF template to extremely high temperatures and can be categorized into three types, as follows: (i) inert atmosphere (N2, Ar, etc.)-based thermal treatment, (ii) oxidizing environment (O2, air, etc.)-based thermal treatment and (iii) reducing atmosphere (H2S and NH3)-based thermal treatment.68 Using this method, MOF-derived porous products are obtained having a lower surface area in comparison to the pristine MOF. However, this technique is widely used to form stable metal oxides, sulfides, and other MOF-derived products, which have a significantly higher surface area, distinct topology, porosity, vacancies, and dopants than their traditional nanomaterial counterparts. The most important fact is C, N-based metal oxides and sulfides can be prepared using this technique without using any additional carbon and nitrogen doping sources and show comparatively good performances compared to the traditionally synthesized metal oxides, sulfides, and phosphides towards photocatalysis by reducing the bandgap energy and making the framework more light responsive. IT-MOF-derived products such as CeO2/NiO, CeO2 have been synthesized and described by various research groups. Pengfei Li et al. reported a one-pot synthesis method that involves hydrothermal mixed metal MOF synthesis followed by calcination for the preparation of an MOF-derived NiO/CeO2 photocatalyst. The spherical multi-variate Ni/Ce BTC MOF was transformed into an irregular porous spherical NiO/CeO2 heterojunction. It was observed that the BET surface area increased from 9.18 m2 g1 to 43.49 m2 g1 with an increase in the Ce content together with a decrease in the average pore size (from 25.3 nm to 11.0 nm) monotonically with an increase in the CeO2 content.69

It is noteworthy that not all these synthesis processes will yield the same physico-chemical product even if a similar reaction precursor is used for the synthesis. Among the various changes in the synthesized MOF, defects are one of the important types of characteristic features that may arise either because of stoichiometry or reaction condition variation. Basically, spots where missing or displaced atoms or ions disturb the normal orderly arrangement of ions of the rigid crystalline framework, are termed as defects. Defects are categorized into three types on a geometric scale, i.e., point, line, and planar defects. In the structure of MOFs, missing linker/cluster defects are the two most common types of point defects. After an organic linker is removed from a structure, it leaves coordination vacancies on two nearby metal clusters, whereas missing-cluster defects occur when a cluster and its whole set of organic linkers are removed, leaving one coordination vacancy on each of the neighbouring clusters. Many research groups have reported the development of defects in MOFs and their importance towards the enhancement of their characteristic physico-chemical features. Defective MOFs such as Ce-BTC, and Ce/Zr-UiO-66 have been reported by various research groups.14,61

4. Characterization of IT-MOF-based nanomaterials

The comprehensive characterization of IT-MOF-based nanomaterials is crucial to study and understand their physico-chemical properties as well as the relative variation between diverse semiconductors. Moreover, the role of f-orbitals, electronic transitions, redox couples, and bulk IT-cluster together with their interaction with functionalized linkers remains key in understanding their exceptional photocatalytic performance. Physico-chemical, textural, optical, electrochemical, and morphological aspects can be measured with the aid of several sophisticated instrumental techniques. Some of these crucial properties of reported IT-MOFs are explained with suitable examples.

4.1. Physico-chemical characterization

X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis represents the interpretation of the scattered X-ray intensity from the crystal planes. It provides preliminary information about the crystallinity, crystallite size (100 nm), and lattice strain of a material, which helps to calculate the crystallinity and structural defects in the material from the full width half-maximum values. In this regard, Tripathy et al. explained the lower angle peak shifting of a UNH (Ce/Zr) bimetallic MOF, as shown in Fig. 2(a and b) compared to pristine UNH (Zr) because of its expanded unit cell and the larger lattice parameter of Ce ion. Also, the decrease in peak intensity and broadening of the peak can be expected because of the insertion of the Ce ion, and eventually decrease in Zr oxy cluster content.14 Alternatively, More et al. explained the XRD pattern of CeO2 derived from Ce-MOF, as shown in Fig. 2(c), which depicts a slightly lower intensity of the diffraction peaks for the MOF-derived photocatalyst, suggesting the presence of some carbon residue from the BTC framework in the derived CeO2.72 With an increase in the calcination temperature, it was expected that the carbon residue of the Ce-MOF was removed, and thus the peak intensity increased.

To predict the textural properties of a photocatalyst, the determination of its BET surface area and BJH pore volume is indispensable. Generally, the N2 gas adsorptiondesorption measurement technique is very helpful to estimate these features of a photocatalyst. To obtain the isotherm, it is necessary to verify the volume of adsorbed N2 gas by the catalyst by observing the pressure difference in the system. The volume of the pore of a photocatalyst can be determined by the BJH model, according to which there are two separate zones present in the pores of the photocatalyst, as follows: (i) the pore walls containing an adsorbed layer and (ii) condensed amount in the residual pore channels. In this regard, More et al. synthesized a Ce-BTC MOF, which displayed a type-II isotherm, as shown in Fig. 2(d), accompanied by no notable hysteresis and the BJH pore-size distribution indicates the weak and broad class of pores with a size in the range of 0 to 50 nm.72 Alternatively, the BJH pore size distribution in the range of 235 nm of the derived CeO2 shows a type-IV isotherm with an H hysteresis loop, from which the broad mesoporous nature of the material was concluded. Also, Lammert et al. performed N2 sorption measurements for bimetallic Ce/Zr MOF at 77 K, resulting in type I(a) isotherms, which are typical for microporous materials. It was reported that the relatively high surface areas of around 1363 to 1443 m2 g1 for the mixed-metal compounds compared to pristine Zr and Ce-UiO-66 results from the missing linker molecules in the framework, which was also confirmed in the TGA study.73 Moreover, Zhao et al. reported the BET surface area and pore nature measured by the BJH method of Ce-TCPP to be 36.4 m2 g1 and its mesoporous nature, respectively. Interestingly, it was observed that the surface area decreased sharply with an increase in the calcining temperature for the composite in contrast to the average pore diameters, which exhibited an increasing pattern from 10.433.6 nm.59 Also, Karimia et al. showed the decrease in the BET specific surface area of Ce-UiO-66 with g-C3N4 composite to 331.35 m2 g1 compared to the pristine sample, indicating the integration of constituents and occupation of a section of pores by g-C3N4 nanoparticles. Besides, the BJH calculation provided information about the reduction in pore volume for the composite with the reported values for g-C3N4, Ce-UiO-66, and CN@CeMOF-3 of 0.31, 0.48, and 0.29 cm3 g1, respectively.61

However, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) can be used to measure the oxidation state, composition, and role crystal defects on the physico-chemical and photocatalytic performance of a fabricated catalyst. The elemental composition at the surface of a photocatalyst also can be examined by using the XPS technique, which confirms the presence of elements by using semi-quantitative databases of XPS or by comparing them with reference materials for quantitative information. Alternatively, electron spin resonance (ESR) spectroscopy is very useful for locating active free radicals, color centers, cationic point defects, and other things. 4.3. Optical-electrochemical insights

Using the fundamental optical and electrochemical techniques, the influence of two parameters, namely, bandgap and band edge potential, on the inherent features and functions of a photocatalyst is well understood. These parameters of a photocatalyst, as well as the flat band potential, are computed using two important analytical tools, i.e., UVVis DRS and MottSchottky. In the electrochemical analysis for evaluating many aspects of a photocatalyst such as nature of the material such as p-, n- or pn type, flat band potential, and charge current density, they are obtained via MottSchottky studies. This is complementary with UVVis DRS given that both techniques evaluate the optical bandgap and band edge potential of a photocatalyst using eqn (1) and (2), respectively, as follows:
Tauc equation: h = A(h Eg)n/2(1)
Vfb[vs. SHE] = Vfb[vs. ref] + Vref[vs. SHE] + 0.0591 pH(2)
where Vref = reference electrode potential concerning SHE, Vfb = flat band electrode potential, pH = log[H+], h = Planck's constant, = proportionality constant, = incident light frequency, A = absorption coefficient and Eg = optical band gap. Furthermore, n represents the plausible electronic transitions for n = 1, 3, 4, and 6 related to the allowed direct transition, forbidden direct transition, allowed indirect transition and forbidden indirect transitions, respectively.

Additionally, the lifetime of the excitons is an important parameter of any photocatalyst for its practical utility. Characterisation techniques such as electrochemical study, PL analysis and TRPL analysis are very helpful to determine the comparative photo-generated exciton lifetime because of the migration of exciton towards the donor/acceptor main interface of photocatalysts.77,78 Basically, electrochemical analysis techniques such as electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and transient photocurrent analysis are helpful to provide adequate ideas regarding the charge migration at the donoracceptor interface.10,13 Mostly, composite nano-hybrid semiconductors show enhanced exciton lifetimes because of the charge migration at the donoracceptor interface, as reported by various research groups based on various charge transfer mechanisms such as Z-scheme, S-scheme, double charge transfer, and co-catalyst loading.7982 In both Z-scheme and S-scheme charge dynamics, the electrons of one semiconductor get fused with the holes of the coupled semiconductor to make their corresponding holes and electrons available, which practically cannot fuse further, thus enhancing their lifetime for optimum suitable redox reaction. Similarly, in the double-charge transfer mechanism, the electrons and holes of opposite semiconductors are transferred to their corresponding CB and VB, respectively, depending up their Fermi level position. Consequently, the density of electrons and holes increases in the CB and VB, respectively, of the opposite semiconducting material, thus increasing their comparative lifetime. Besides, employing the co-catalyst loading method, the electrons of the semiconductors are trapped by the co-catalyst, which hinders electronhole recombination, and hence enhances their lifetime to show optimum productivity towards suitable photocatalytic reactions. In this regard, Yan et al. prepared a bimetallic IT-MOF using a metalloligand, i.e., Ru(phen)3-derived ligand, as the linker to achieve enhanced photocatalytic performance, which was verified by UV-Vis DRS spectra, as shown in Fig. 4(a).83 It was observed that the strong absorption band at 300350 nm is due to the * transition in the conjugated phen group and relatively weaker absorption peaks at 420480 nm are due to the characteristic MLCT transition. 4.4. Computational insights

Electronic structures correlating to the properties and reactivity of photocatalytic materials are readily predicted by using computational techniques based on quantum mechanics. Among the appropriate computational techniques, KohnSham density functional theory (KS-DFT) has attracted attention due to its combined advantages towards economically friendly and excellent precision. For solids, the KS-DFT band gaps can be determined as the difference in energy of the orbitals in the ground state, in particular, as LUCO HOCO, where signifies the orbital energy, LUCO represents the lowest unoccupied crystal orbital, and HOCO represents the highest occupied crystal orbital. The electronic exchangecorrelation energy density at a location builds up on the local factors with local functionals such as the electronic kinetic energy densities, electronic spin densities, and their gradients. Due to the HartreeFock exchange present in hybrid functionals, the exchangecorrelation energy of these systems is non-local and includes an integral over all space. In contrast, for the molecules and linkers of IT-MOFs, one generally uses time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT), in the linear response approximation. In addition to the very low energy because of the van der Waal interactions that exist among the molecules, DFT can also offer a plausible description of the geometry, dissociation energy, and kinetics of chemical reactions.2,4

The primitive cell of the UiO-66 framework, as shown in Fig. 5(a), was used to calculate DFT using the Vienna ab initio simulation package for the purpose of reducing the computational cost by Wu et al. Also, it was discussed how both metal alteration and linker modifications impact the framework characteristics, as shown in Fig. 5(b), which affects the photocatalytic applications in UiO-66-based series of IT-MOF using KS-DFT. Actually, a desirable photocatalyst must have an unoccupied metal orbital with lower energy than the orbital of donor species, which leads to negative ELMCT and facile ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT). Here, in this case, it was observed that UiO-66(Zr) was located at 2 eV above the LUCO of the ligand orbital, which is not a convenient case for effective LMCT. However, the replacement of Zr with IT-metal elements such as Th, U, Ce, Hf, and Ti in the UiO-66 series resulted in some variations with positive stimulus towards photocatalytic reactions. Due to the lower energy of 3d-orbitals of Ti4+, which encourages good mixing with the *-orbital of the linkers, UiO-66(Ti) shows greater facile LMCT than that of other d0-metal ions. Also, recent research revealed that Ti+4-MOF has a longer exciton separation lifetime than that of isostructural Zr+4 and Hf+4 MOFs, as shown in Fig. 5(c). Moreover, in the case of UiO-66(Ce), TD-DFT was performed to determine the place where excitations mainly occur based on three major parameters, as follows (i) node-localized, (ii) ligand-to-metal, and (iii) linker-localized excitations. In the case of the first two types of excitations, 300 excited states are obtained, which are weak in nature because of their smaller oscillator strength with energy ranging from 3.003.47 eV. It was expected that the linker localised excitations were more likely to occur than the node-localised and LMCT excitation because of the strong to * transitions in the case of the linker, which is responsible for negative value of ELMCT, allowed LMCT, greater charge separation with long-lived excited states and enhanced photocatalytic performance.

5. Photocatalytic mechanism insights

The photogeneration of exciton pairs and their proper transportation are a challenging task for the prediction of the mechanistic pathway of charge species for any photocatalytic reaction.55 The supremacy of IT-MOFs in contrast to other conventional transition metal-based MOFs towards photocatalysis can be explained through their extraordinary luminescence characteristics. It is the radiation emitted by an atom or a molecule following the absorption of photons and excitation into a higher energy triplet state. Because lanthanide metals except for La and Lu show excellent luminescence due to their ff transition, their incorporation in the framework reduces the width of the bandgap energy level between the VB and CB, which permits the photocatalyst to be active under visible light. Additionally, in these types of materials, the adsorbed radiation is re-emitted at a slower intensity and can emit radiations in the absence of light for a small duration, enabling a longer lifetime of photo-generated excitons. In the case of doping using IT-metals such as Ce, Sm, and Eu, the energy levels of metals can be inserted into the band gap of the pristine material owing to the lower energy separation between the VB and CB, suppressing e/h+ recombination and leading to the increment in their photocatalytic performance. The linker can also exhibit luminescence because of the extended -conjugated system but sometimes ligand luminescence is not seen because of LMCT. To target the luminescence in MOFs with the least amount of energy transfer, it is crucial to choose suitable linkers with appropriate energy levels.

Besides, up-conversion luminescence is a distinctive process where high wavelength photons can be converted to low wavelength photons, which is contrary to the conventional luminescence. It was reported that lanthanides are capable of exhibiting upconversion luminescence because of the availability of many states in the IR and visible region with longer lifetimes. Upconversion agents are introduced in photocatalysis to overcome the theoretical efficiency by harvesting photons with energy lower than their Eg which has a great advantage over other transition metals. Jingsan Xu et al. reported that when g-C3N4 was doped with Er3+, Er3+ triggered upconversion either by excited-state absorption (ESA) or by energy transfer (ET). Combining both process, the emitted energy of Er3+ after being relaxed to the ground state transferred to the pristine material to produce electronhole pairs. Hence, this effect can be adopted for the improvement of the photocatalytic process by doping lanthanide series metals.

Furthermore, Xu et al. and Tripathy et al. suggested that metals with mixed valency states and easily inter-convertible redox couples (e.g., Eu3+/2+ and Ce4+/3+) have an empty 4f-orbital and can be reduced to their lower oxidation state easily, which is also a thermodynamically feasible reaction that promotes superior photocatalytic performance in IT-MOF-based nanomaterials. Thus, the reductant on the surface of the catalyst can trap electrons, thereby inhibiting electronhole recombination, and eventually the quantum efficiency increases.14,72

5.1. Heterojunction-based charge dynamics in IT-MOFs

Circumventing the shortcomings of a single component photocatalytic system, type-II heterojunction nanocomposites were introduced through the combinatorial impact of two semiconductors (I and II) with the view to upgrade the photocatalytic performance by increasing the lifespan of excitons, as shown in Scheme 6(a). In this case, the CB and VB levels of semiconductor-I are higher than the corresponding levels of semiconductor-II. Thus, the photogenerated holes will migrate to the semiconductor-I and electrons will migrate to the semiconductor-II under irradiation, resulting in efficient charge carrier separation and migration in comparison with the type-1 and type-III mechanisms.55 Zhao et al. explained the plausible mechanism through a heterojunction-mediated process for doped Ce-MIL-101-NH2/Ag3PO4 composites towards dye (TC and MB) degradation.62 Another case of a type-II heterojunction-based pathway involves photocatalytic pollutant degradation, as reported by Kuila et al.86 Pengfei et al. predicted the heterojunction-mediated mechanism for the NiO/CeO2 material used for photocatalytic pollutant degradation and hydrogen evolution.69 Baize et al. also explained the same mechanism for the MIL-101/UCNP heterostructure towards photocatalytic rhodamine-B degradation.87 Mansingh et al. followed the pn heterojunction mechanism to explain the photocatalytic nitrogen reduction ability of the CeO2/Ce-MOF material.13

5.2. Z-Scheme-based charge dynamics in IT-MOFs

In this type of mechanism, both the semiconductors get excited by absorbing photons resembling our natural photosynthesis process as photosystem(I) and photosystem(II) with varying band gaps. The principle involves the combination of photo-excited electrons in the CB of one semiconductor with the holes in the VB of another semiconductor because of the transmission at the composite interface phase. For the solid- and liquid-phase Z-scheme process, a mediator and charge trapping solid conductor are required to connect the two photosystems indirectly. However, comparing them, the direct Z-scheme shown in Scheme 6(b) is more beneficial and we can understand the principle based on the metals present in the framework of IT-MOF for studying various photocatalytic oxidation and reduction reactions. Classifying, the semiconductors into three categories including metal node (M1) and intercalated metal cluster (M2) or metal of organometallic linker (M2), or another metal (M2) of the same metal cluster (or, maybe same as M1 with dissimilar oxidation state), it was explained that with UV-Vis light illumination followed by the generation of photoexcitons, the e/h+ recombination occurs between the CB of M1 and VB of M2. The CB hole in M1 and CB electrons in M2 are available for redox reactions.88 In this regard, Zhao et al. explained the Z-scheme CeO2/N-doped C/Ce-TCPP system, which exhibited an excellent photocatalytic performance towards organic pollutant degradation.67 Karimia et al. also predicted the Z-scheme pathway for the g-C3N4@Ce-MOF composite, which is useful for aerobic oxidative Hantzsch pyridine synthesis of styrene.61

5.3. Co-catalyst-based charge dynamics in IT-MOFs

One more route is the use of a metal co-catalyst on the semiconductor surface, as shown in Scheme 6(c), to trap the electrons by promoting e/h+ anti-recombination and enhancing the lifetime of excitons. In general, noble metals, transition metals, and their oxides, sulfides, and phosphides are used as co-catalysts to capture the photo-generated electrons from the active surface of the host catalyst to intensify the photocatalytic reactions. A co-catalyst can have multiple key roles such as lowering the activation energy, promoting separation and migration of electronhole pairs, high selectivity towards a particular reaction, obstructing side, and back reactions, which are beneficial for the activity, selectivity, and stability of the semiconductor photocatalyst. In this case, the photo-excited electrons on the conduction band are transferred to the co-catalyst to cause reduction reactions.67

5.4. Vacancy defect-based charge dynamics in IT-MOFs

Framework defect engineering is the most popular and advanced method to enhance electronhole pair separation, which is crucial for understanding the correlation between their structure and photocatalytic properties.74 The geometric electrical assembly and chemical characteristics of semiconductor photocatalysts are typically altered by defects at the surface/interface or in the bulk.8,9,74 In general, defects can be categorised in to three parts, as follows: (i) bulk defect, (ii) surface defect, and (iii) interface defect. In case of bulk defect, the atoms may be completely missing from their position or occupy an interstitial site to create vacancies. This is the most common type of defect exhibited by IT-MOF, which includes oxygen vacancies and metal ion vacancies responsible for narrowing the Eg and separating the photogenerated electronhole pairs, as shown in Scheme 6(d). In this regard, Tripathy et al. describe the oxygen vacancy-mediated charge transfer process for enhanced photocatalytic reactions in a Ce/Zr bimetallic MOF.14 The surface of a nanomaterial possesses various unique features due to its high surface area and surface energy resulting from O, N, and S surface vacancy defects, which were reported in metal oxides, nitrides, and sulfides in the past. The formation of the most common oxide vacancy defect can modulate the coordination environment and electronic structure of adsorbates via surface bonds and localized electrons, which in turn help to narrow the Eg and trap the charge carriers, enhancing the photocatalytic activity.8,54,5760,64,74,8992 The interfacial defect is the most complex one, which is developed at the interface where both the surface and bulk coexist. In this case, the separation of photogenerated electronhole pairs in various defects, transfer of excitons, their stabilization, and the effective coupling of different crystal faces, collectively decide the occurrence of a photocatalytic reaction. In addition, interface defects increase the electrical conductivity and reduce the energy barrier, which favor photocatalytic reactions.74,82,9398

5.5. Photosensitizer-based charge dynamics in IT-MOFs

In addition to the above-mentioned charge dynamics mechanisms, another pathway has been explored by various research groups, i.e., photosensitizer-based charge dynamics. Photosensitizers are compelling materials because of their high visible spectrum photon trapping ability and utility. However, their stable functionality and activity can be well improved by suitable interaction with another robust photocatalytic material. Given that IT-MOFs have well-proven capabilities to provide variable vacancies, interconvertible redox couples, and high surface area towards photocatalysis, they tend to act as a suitable material for interaction with photosensitizers, which can further improve the light sensitivity feature of the framework and its photo-reactivity, as shown in Scheme 6(e). Basically, dyes such as thionine, Nile Blue A, methylene blue, eosin Y, and rhodamine B are used as photosensitizers, which contain light harvesting antennas in the form of extensive benzene rings and the presence of multiple functional groups (OH, COOH, etc.), enabling strong stacking and H-bonding, respectively. Mechanistically, dye molecules in the ground state absorb photons and transformed them into their excited states (dye*). As long as the potential of dye* is more negative than the LUMO position of IT-MOFs, electron transformation from dye* to the LUMO of MOFs will occur. Finally, the electrons can be transported to different acceptors in the reaction medium (O2, H+, or CO2) to form O2, H2, or HCOO, respectively. It is noteworthy that for a photocatalyst, its excitation wavelength can be extended to the visible light region with the aid of the desired photosensitizer. In this regard, Thakare and Ramteke studied the PSM of MOF-5 using 8-hydroxyquinoline (HOQ) dye, which was reported to have enhanced phenol degradation from 5% to around 90% in 80 min.

6.1. Organic transformation reactions (OTRs)

Humans cannot survive without organic molecules such as drugs, reagents, polymers, agrochemicals, and cosmetics. In this case, the synthesis of natural products and their synthetic analogues, molecular libraries of biologically relevant entities, and industrially viable compounds are at the heart of the modern chemical industry. Consequently, the evolution of new synthetic methods and the modernisation of existing ones are at the forefront of current organic chemistry research trends.99 Arduous segregation of synthetic products through traditional homogenous organic catalysis and photocatalysis has led to the development of heterogeneous photocatalysis. The role of porous coordination polymers (PCPs) in high-yielding organic photo-transformation has been recently prioritized due to their abundant active sites, high surface area, functionalized guest or linker moieties, and coordinatively unsaturated metal sites. Moreover, the mouldable pore architecture in PCPs significantly favors exemplary product selectivity. Also, the light harvesting and transforming sites present in the framework promote photon-assisted OTRs, mitigating the need for complex organic procedures, high temperature, hazardous organic reagents and solvents, paving the path for a superior atom economy. Especially, the subclass of MOFs containing inner transition metal clusters is being extensively explored compared to their transition metal counterparts due to their high aqueous stability, high coordination number, empty f-orbitals and potent Lewis acidic characteristics, making them stable and reusable candidates towards OTRs. A few of these IT-MOF-based nanomaterials are explained in detail regarding some essential photocatalytic OTRs.

In this regard, Karimia et al. synthesized a composite of cerium-based MOFs (Ce-UiO-66) modified with graphitic carbon nitride nanosheets (g-C3N4). To attain the highest efficacy, a series of different composites with varying mass ratios of both pristine materials was prepared, and it was observed that CN@CeMOF-3 (composite g-C3N4@Ce-UiO-66 in 58/42 wt%) showed the maximum conversion efficiency. Indeed, the reaction began to yield more products abruptly as the temperature was increased from RT to 40 C, but with an increase in temperature, even with an increase in catalyst loading, had the reverse effect on the conversion of styrene, which could be due to the partial solubility of O2 in the reaction medium.62 In the presence of various quenchers, TEMPO showed the maximum quenching ability, in which the conversion plummeted to around 5%, as shown in Fig. 6(a). The ESR spectrum in Fig. 6(b and c) confirmed the low concentration of OH and significant detection of O2, respectively. Moreover, the cascade mechanism involving aerobic oxidation of styrene was propagated through Z-scheme-mediated O2 radical generation, as shown in Fig. 6(d). Also, Campanelli et al. performed organic transformation reactions such as oxidation of 4-methoxybenzyl alcohol (MeOBA) by using Ce-UiO-66 as a photocatalyst. Additionally, they investigated the reaction-efficiency by altering the substituents of the BDC linker, such as Br, NO2, and F, and discovered that Ce-UiO-66-Br performed the best, reaching the extreme rate of product yield (94%) in 80 min. The reaction mechanism involves the absorption of light by the linker of the MOF to form photogenerated excitons through LMCT, and then reaction with alcohol to form the radical cation by the transfer of an electron from the aromatic nucleus to the photogenerated hole in the MOF. Subsequently, deprotonation of the radical intermediate occurs to form the 4-methoxy--hydroxy benzyl radical with the removal of O2 to form substituted benzaldehyde as the product.75 Moreover, Lina M. et al., also worked on organic transformations involving amine to imine as well as on cross-condensation reactions of amines and observed the excellent attainment of the inner transition polymeric frameworks, namely, RPF-30-Er, RPF-32-Ho, RPF-31-Nd, and RPF-31-Sm, towards the above-mentioned activity. The use of RPF materials as catalysts (10% mol) resulted in great selectivity and a good conversion range of up to 6076% of the condensation of benzyl amine. In the oxidation of the benzylamine process, the RPF-30, RPF-31, and RPF-32 materials showed good activity (about 70%) and exceptional selectivity (>99%). They discovered that when using RPF-31-Nd as a photocatalyst in the presence of both air and molecular oxygen, the selectivity remained >99%, but the conversion declined significantly from 60% to barely 30% after 48 h.90 It is quite clearly seen that only a limited number of IT-MOFs has been used thus far in organic transformation reactions, and further research needs to be carried out on a variety of organic transformation reactions to get a thorough understanding of the underlying mechanism.

6.2. Carbon dioxide reduction

The burning of fossil fuels at an alarming rate, with large emissions of CO2, has sparked a catastrophic change in climate and global energy shortage. These challenges prompted researchers to look into sustainable techniques to reduce CO2 emissions or even capture CO2 from the atmosphere. Given that CO2 photo-reduction can be done under lower pressure and temperature conditions, it has the potential to be a more environmentally friendly alternative. The reduction of CO2 to minor quantities of HCOOH, HCHO, CH3OH, CO, and CH4, consuming different number of electrons and protons, has attracted significant attention for semiconductor-based photocatalytic CO2 reduction to chemical fuels.82 Photo-catalytic semiconductors such as MOFs are the utmost utilized materials for this concept, and their effectiveness is typically determined by their optimum bandgap (Eg), charge separation, and transfer. However, conventional d-block MOFs display relatively lower efficiency, selectivity, and poor stability. These drawbacks of traditional MOFs can be easily overcome by using IT-MOF-based nanomaterials. Strategically armed IT-MOFs tend to overcome the shortcomings with product selective CO2 reduction by promoting boosted charge separation/migration, electron availability to the surface adsorbed CO2, and tuning of the conduction band (CB) potential of semiconductor photocatalysts owing to the stupendous yield accompanied with definite product selectivity for CO2 photoreduction along and suppressed back reactions.100

In this regard, Yan et al. successfully fabricated an Eu-Ru(phen)3-MOF using dinuclear Eu(III)2 clusters as nodes and Ru(phen)3-derivative as linkers to test its photocatalytic application towards CO2 reduction. The reaction was studied under visible-light irradiation (420800 nm) utilizing Eu-Ru(phen)3-MOF as a heterogeneous photocatalyst and triethanolamine as a sacrificial agent. In the liquid phase, the concentration of formate product (HCOO) was determined using ion chromatography, as shown in Fig. 7(a). Electron injection into the Eu nodes is triggered by photo-excitation of the metalloligands, resulting in excited dinuclear Eu(II)2 active sites, which selectively decrease CO2 to formate in a two-electron process at a rate of 321.9 mol h1, as shown in the mechanism image in Fig. 7(e). The greater photocatalytic activity in this system can be explained by the presence of the effective light-harvesting Ru(phen)3 moiety, as well as the absence of side products, indicating strong selectivity for the reduction of CO2 to formate. Recycling tests of photocatalytic reduction of CO2 in MeCN/TEOA (v:v = 20:1) were carried out to assess the photocatalytic stability of the material, as shown in Fig. 7(c and d). The HCOO level was around 47 mol after 10 h of illumination, with no apparent deterioration after three reactions. Meanwhile, the PXRD patterns after photocatalytic reactions match that of the as-prepared sample, and the ICP-MS results showed 0.1% metal leaching to the solution after a 10 h photocatalytic reaction, indicating that the Eu-Ru(phen)3-MOF was stable following photocatalytic usage.83 Basically, photocatalytic CO2 reduction, depends up the absorbed CO2 on the active surface area, surface defects and surface atomic construction of the photocatalyst and requires an intense reorganizational energy to convert linear CO2 to bent radical anion of CO2, followed by the formation of a variety of products such as HCOOH (0.81 V), HCHO (0.48 V), CH3OH (0.38 V), CO (0.53 V), and CH4 (0.24 V) by consuming different numbers of electrons and protons.82,101 In this regard, product selectivity, which basically depends on factors such as photon energy, CB position, intensity of light, surface catalytic active sites, separation of photo-generated charge carriers, and adsorption/desorption of reactant/intermediates, is an important criteria to determine the target product. Many photocatalytic materials including IT-MOFs have been reported by various researchers to get the selective target product from CO2 photoreduction. In this regard, Payra et al. reported the selective formation of HCOOH (requires 2e/2H+) as the reduction product using Ce-BDC, while CH3OH (requires 6e/6H+) and HCOOH (requires 2e/2H+) as reduction products using Ce-BDC-NH2.100 Similarly, Yan et al. reported the selective formation of HCOOH from CO2 reduction by using an IT MOF, i.e., Eu-MOF.83 Additionally, Stawowy et al. reported high selectivity for CH3OH as the CO2 photo-reduced product using Cu-doped (Ce/Zr)-MOF.102 All these reports represent the specific tendencies of IT-MOFs towards product-selective CO2 reduction.

6.3. Photocatalytic water splitting

Overall photocatalytic water splitting and scavenger-prompted half-reactions to generate oxygen and hydrogen as solar-mediated fuels have attracted substantial attention as a potential source of renewable energy with no reliance on fossil fuels and display zero carbon footprints. Basically, this reaction with change in Gibbs free energy G = +237.13 kJ mol1 consists of two half-cell reactions, i.e., photocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction (PHER) and photocatalytic oxygen evolution reaction (POER). For the purpose of water splitting, the band gap of a semiconductor photocatalyst should be greater than 1.23 eV and considering thermodynamics, its CB should be more negative than the reduction potential of H+/H2 (0 V vs. NHE at pH 0) and VB should be more negative than the oxidation potential of O2/H2O (1.23 V vs. NHE at pH 0). After the resourceful work of Fujishima and Honda in 1972, tremendous efforts have been contributed to fabricating more efficient photocatalysts for hydrogen evolution with multiple aspects such as photo-absorption proficiency, copacetic bandgap, proper band edge position, active surface sites, and exciton separation with transfer efficacy. TO date, various MOF-based photocatalysts have been reported with excellent performances for water splitting but IT-MOFs exhibit superiority compared to traditional MOFs with respect to their high nuclearity cluster making ability, Lewis acidic nature, aqueous stability, and redox couple forming tendency. However, two basic mechanistic ways have been explored to understand the mechanism of photocatalytic water splitting reactions involving IT-MOFs. The first is to use a single photocatalyst with sufficient band edge potential, a narrow Eg for harvesting visible photons, and significant photo-corrosion resistance. The second is the Z-scheme, a two-step strategy inspired by natural photosynthesis in green plants. Comparing both methods, the latter is found to be more advantageous because of the lower value of G for each of the photocatalysts and the easy separation of the evolved H2 and O2.103

Tripathy et al. previously reported the photon-induced H2 evolution reaction, as shown in Fig. 8, to evaluate and correlate the efficiency of a bimetallic photocatalyst over its monometallic counterparts. It was reported that the neat UNH (Ce) and UNH (Zr) exhibited slow evolution of hydrogen due to the quick recombination of excitons and inadequate LMCT aspect, while the bimetallic MOF displayed a significantly enhanced evolution rate, i.e., 468.30 mol h1, which was around four-fold greater than that of the pristine Zr-MOF. This can be attributed to the slow recombination rate of charge particles, large active surface area, and appropriate bandgap. The photo-stability cycle test result exhibited minute changes in the photo evolution output, which in turn manifests the excellent stability of the UNH (Ce/Zr 1:1).14 In another case, Wu et al. performed the photocatalytic water splitting reaction of Ce-based MOFs with two important screening criteria of the prepared sample, as follows: (i) Eabs 3.0 eV and (ii) Eg 1.48 eV to screen visible-response photocatalysts for driving water splitting reaction. Under the optimal conditions such as temperature (298.15 K) and pH = 7, the PHER and POER band levels are positioned at 3.87 and 5.09 eV, respectively, when nine Ce-based MOFs were detected for visible light-responsive water splitting photocatalysts UiO-66(Ce6)-I, UiO-66(Ce2Zr4)-L, where L = 2,5-I, I, OH, 2,5-Br, and UiO-66(Ce2Ti4)-L, where L = I, 2,5-NO2, OH, 2,5-Br. Amongst the six promising photocatalytic bimetallic MOFs, UiO-66(Ce2Zr4)-2,5-I and UiO-66(Ce2Ti4)-I are the most favorable given that they have a low Eabs of 2.48 and 2.55 eV, respectively, and consequently have great ability to harvest light.104 Moreover, a monometallic IT-MOF-derived system (CeO2@N,S-C) was recognised for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution by Hao et al. In this case N,S-co-doped C-coated CeO2 with a 3D hinge-like structure assembled by 2D corrugated porous nanosheets based on MOF-808 (Ce) as a hard template was synthesised. The large proportion of surface active sites located on the hierarchical hinge-like structure, and the coating of N,S-doped C layer on CeO2 as the adsorber are two main driving features for improved photocatalytic H2 evolution reaction (HER). The synergetic effect of the N,S-doped C layer and hinge-like porous structure resulted in the superior photoactivity and cycle stability of the composite.105

6.4. Organic pollutant degradation

Pollution of the environment constituents (air, water, and soil) is a global concern that poses a major threat to the health of all species on Earth. Thus, efforts have been devoted to developing effective, ecologically friendly, and inexpensive technologies for eliminating organic dyes, antibiotics, microplastics, aromatic compounds, pesticides, and other persistent organic contaminants from water. The majority of all industrial wastewater impurities are organic dyes or persistent organic pollutants containing azo (NN) groups and benzene or naphthalene or heterocyclic rings.106 Photocatalysis using semiconductor catalysts, notably using IT-MOFs has shown to be effective in decomposing and mineralizing a number of bulk organic moieties in addition to inorganic NO3, CO2, PO43, H2O, and halide ions into biodegradable or less hazardous organic molecules.107

In this regard, Zhao et al. studied the photocatalytic activity of AFC-x and M-AFC01 composites under LED light for the degradation of MB dye. After 120 min, it was seen that AFC01 had a degradation rate of 86%, while that of M-AFC01 was 38% for MB removal, as shown in Fig. 9. 6.5. Artificial nitrogen fixation

Ammonia (NH3) is an indispensable component in the production of an ample range of chemicals, together with fertilizers and HNO3 in modern society. Moreover, ammonia also acts as a significant clean energy carrier and fuel by acting as an ideal hydrogen storage material with large hydrogen energy release efficiency. Currently, ammonia is produced by utilizing the well-known HaberBosch method, which uses H2 and N2 under excessively high pressure and temperatures, often over an iron-based catalyst for a faster chemical reaction. The wide use of the HaberBosch process results in massive amounts of energy consumption and CO2 emissions, putting immense pressure on global energy demand and promoting environmental deterioration.108 As a green alternative, the energy carrier ammonia can also be obtained from N2 by the photocatalytic reduction process. However, splitting the ultra-stable NN bond remains a challenge, accompanied by high bond dissociation energy (941 kJ mol1), high ionization potential (15 eV), high electronegativity (1.9 eV), large HOMOLUMO energy gap along with zero dipole moment. These factors contribute to rigorous reaction conditions for ammonia formation. However, with the conscientious choice of photocatalyst, these setbacks can easily be overcome. Typically, the process of photocatalytic N2 reduction occurs by surface chemisorption, followed by association or dissociation of nitrogen molecules and reductive attachment of hydrogen atoms. In this regard, the functionalized high surface area MOFs and related nanomaterials are observed to show significantly superior photocatalytic nitrogen to ammonia conversion efficiency. Especially, the MOFs built with IT-metal nodes are an excellent choice for photocatalytic ammonia production.

7. Stability of IT-MOFs

The stability of any photocatalytic material is a significant property for its practical utility. In this regard, basically two types of stability can be considered, i.e., chemical stability and thermal stability.

For practical usage, the chemical stability of IT-MOFs is essential. Chemical stability can be defined as the resistance of IT-MOFs to chemical treatments (such as exposure to water, acid, alkali, and salt solutions) to maintain their framework integrity and permanent porosity. Various elements corresponding to the nature of coordination bonds, their surrounding environments, and other structural factors, affect the robustness of the metalligand bond upon chemical treatments. Alternatively, the effect of water molecules on building blocks of IT-MOF, i.e., the metal ions and organic linkers, determines the aqueous stability of the frameworks. Generally, in a humid environment, ligand exchange reaction or hydrolysis with metal ions plays a pivotal role in determining the degree of aqueous stability of IT-MOFs. Thus, as the thermodynamic basis of the stability of IT-MOFs, a strong coordination bond with high resistance to various entities results into the formation of highly stable IT-MOFs, which follows Pearson's HardSoft AcidBase (HSAB) principle, as explained in section 3. Based on the above-mentioned guidelines, various IT-MOF-based materials have been reported with enhanced chemical stability according to the crucial choice of metals and linkers to form stable frameworks. In this regard, Zhang et al. reported some IT-MOFs (IT = Ce, Tb, Er, Nd, Dy, and Gd) showing excellent chemical, aqueous, thermal, and solvent stability for various cascade reactions.109 Moreover, Payra et al. also reported the high chemical stability of Ce-BDC-NH2, which is probably due to the stronger interactions between the metal and coordinated linkers and the aqueous stability also tested exposing the MOF in water for up to 6 days with same XRD pattern as the initially synthesised one.100 Additionally, Lia et al. prepared an Eu-based MOF (CTGU-19) and reported that it has remarkable water stability and can retain its crystalline integrity under ambient conditions. Also, further evaluation was carried out by immersing CTGU-19 crystals in solutions with different pH values (410) for 24 h at room temperature, and the unaltered PXRD pattern signifies the good crystallinity of the material.110

Secondly, multiple factors have been recognised to influence the thermal stability of IT-MOFs. In particular, the nature of coordinated solvent, hardness of the metal, nature and position of functional groups significantly affect the stability of the frameworks. With the help of thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), the thermal stability of IT-MOFs can be determined, as explained in section 4.1. In this regard, various IT-MOFs were reported to have excellent thermal stability up to around 350500 C because of their flexible coordination sphere and coordinated unsaturated metal centres to promote more complex structures. Huang et al. reported a U-based MOF (IHEP-9) with framework stability of up to 400 C, maintaining the intact crystalline state in the pH range of 211.111 Moreover,112 Li et al. reported the thermal behavior of an Eu-MOF (CTGU-19) by TGA experiment in air, which showed a gradual reduction in mass (12.5%) before 72.8 C, corresponding to the loss of solvent molecules in its channels, and then the loss of coordination water, which accounts for 3.4%, between 72.8 C and 193.1 C, until the framework begins to collapse at 370 C.112 However, the thermal stability of IT-MOFs can further be improved by preparing mixed-metal IT-MOFs, through ligand functionalization or using complex planar ligands to maintain the extended conjugation, making framework rigid and more stable.

8. Summary and future perspective

Inner transition metalorganic frameworks (IT-MOFs) have become a competent candidate for photocatalysis, which are being utilized in ongoing research to develop, characterize, and implement robust nano-materials in many domains for environmentally friendly and cost-effective catalytic usage. In this review, we presented several aspects of IT-MOFs, starting from their fabrication routes, structures, characterization, and photocatalytic applications. Among the different inner transition MOFs, actinoid-based MOFs are the least explored because of their radioactive nature and low Earth abundance. Despite these setbacks, MOFs based on Th and U are popularly explored from the actinide series because of their tendency for high reactivity, variable oxidation state (+4, +5, +6, and +7), and complex framework-constructing nature. Unlike the widely studied d-block MOFs, these inner transition elements show irreplaceable nature towards coordination chemistry, where their empty f-orbitals, high coordination number of their inner transition metal clusters and ligand steric factors decide the coordination dynamics, geometries, and desired characteristics of the formed IT-MOFs. Moreover, their synthesis protocols have a remarkable influence on their photocatalytic activity through the optimization of crystallinity, physicochemical, and microstructural properties. Traditionally, solvothermal treatment was the most sought-after route towards IT-MOFs and multi-variate IT-MOF-based nanomaterial fabrication. However, recently, microwave-assisted solvothermal method and sono-chemical process have attracted more attention because they consume minimal duration and produce higher yields of crystalline frameworks. Moreover, the spatial orientation of the building blocks of IT-MOFs considerably influences the topologies of the generated frameworks, which is a function of the activities of the resultant MOFs. With the diversified nuclearity of metal clusters/nodes of the IT-elements, a large number of new topologies was reported, which can be incorporated to alter the position together with the orientation of the constituents present in MOFs to tune their photocatalytic active sites. In addition, designing the desired topology by the selection of appropriate starting materials favors the achievement of photocatalytic reactions involving an energetically suitable transition state through the concerted binding of reactants on the catalytic surface. Nevertheless, the photocatalytic performance of IT-MOFs can be further upgraded by mixed metal framework, nano-composite, metal nanoparticle composite, and MOF-derived products. These IT-MOF-based nanomaterials have been thoroughly studied considering their physio-chemical, morphological, and optical-electrochemical characterizations to contemplate the cooperative effect of textual and microstructural properties and charge carrier separation followed by migration and bathochromic shift in terms of absorption range for which the composite photocatalysts exhibit excellent performance towards diversified applications. Mostly, IT-MOF-based materials have been vividly explored for emerging photocatalytic applications involving organic transformations, water splitting, organic pollutant degradation, and CO2 and N2 reduction. Typically, for these applications, IT-MOF-based nanomaterials follow four mechanistic pathways, as follows: (i) heterojunction-based type-II charge transfer, (ii) Z-scheme-based charge transfer, (iii) co-catalyst-based charge transfer and (iv) photosensitizer-based charge transfer. Moreover, the photocatalytic performance may further be enhanced owning to their criteria such as small bandgap, suitable band-edge position, suppression of e/h+ pair recombination, and lattice vacancies present in IT-MOFs.

IT-MOF-based nanomaterials have displayed promising potential to promote future research in the development of efficient photocatalysis. However, the research on IT-MOFs is in its infancy and yet to make a significant impact as photocatalysts. Commonly, some of the pivotal and pressing attributes for the development of these materials may be helpful to lead researchers in the right direction in the future. Typically, high throughput synthesis (HTS) remains amiss for IT-MOFs and can be game changing for developing diverse morphologically and physio-chemically distinct frameworks in a short time. Moreover, the composition range for inner transition metals and available linkers is vast. Therefore, IT-MOFs with desirable features can be scrutinized with aid of combinatorial HTS and machine learning (ML) platforms for thorough optimization of synthetic parameters. Among the various IT-MOFs, Ce, Eu, and Tb-based MOFs are the dominant ones, and hence more attention should be paid to other inner transition frameworks. Moreover, in photocatalytic application, IT-MOFs with their composites are mostly explored for organic transformations and organic pollutant degradation, whereas scarce studies are have been performed on carbon dioxide reduction, water splitting, and artificial nitrogen fixation. Additionally, assisted photocatalytic techniques such as sono-photocatalytic, photo-thermal catalytic, and piezo-photocatalytic effects of IT-MOFs should be explored significantly, given that there no such studies in the literature. Although IT-MOFs basically show superior photocatalytic efficiency in comparison to other group metal node-containing MOFs, their quantum efficiency needs further improvement to realize their practical and industrial utility.
