Abstract

The rise of organic thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) materials as NIR emitters and hosts have engrossed wide consideration as they can demonstrate 100% internal quantum efficiency. These materials have exceeded the phosphorescent noble-metal complexes due to cost-efficiency without compromising the OLED performance. Numerous applications of the NIR spectrum, e.g., in telecommunication, night-vision display, biomedical applications, and chemical sensors, render the NIR-TADF emitters vulnerable to evolution. This review highlights the various strategies adopted in the recent past (2015â2022) to judiciously design purely organic NIR-TADF emitters (Î»maxPL/EL > 700 nm) with in-depth discussions of photophysical and device-electroluminescence studies. Approaches such as the incorporation of extended Ï-conjugated acceptors, diazoles of group-16 elements, âBF2â-acceptor, non-conjugated donorâacceptor dendrons, exciplex formation, and TADF sensitization provide effective pathways to construct high-efficiency NIR-TADF-OLEDs. Thus, this review aims to provide a new perspective for the next generation researchers to implement strategies for the thoughtful design of efficient NIR-TADF materials.

1. Introduction

The mass application of NIR light (700 nm < Î» < 2500 nm) has enriched the rapid development of research and technology of organic semiconductors that find diverse applications. For example, Apple's patent for a device in which NIR light could be used for fingerprint detection as a security application,1 but this personal identification technique could be extended to capillary vein mapping to diminish identification errors.2 Other applications include photodynamic therapy causing lower damage to the nearby living tissues with longer penetration depths,3,4 biosensing (e.g., blood oxymetry),5,6 short-range (last meter or so) through space communication (TSC) systems,7 and organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs). Realistically, OLEDs are smart alternatives to traditional liquid crystalline displays (LCDs) as they offer high contrast over a wider view-angle, faster response time, energy efficiency, and fabrication flexibility while being thinner and lighter at the same time. Other advantages of NIR-OLEDs include (a) high bandwidth optical Li-Fi (light-fidelity) networking systems superior to wireless (Wi-Fi) systems8 and (b) optogenetics (in vivo regulation of cell behavior by light, particularly on neurons).9 Since the revolutionary work of Tang and Van Slyke based on a double layer fluorescent green OLED device with tris(8-hydroxyquinoline)aluminum (Alq3) as the electron-transporting and emitting layers (ETL and EML) that showed an external quantum efficiency (EQE) of â¼1% with a driving-voltage of <10 V, tremendous research and commercialization have been witnessed on OLEDs, particularly for display applications.10 However, fluorescent OLEDs, such as OLED based on Alq3, can give rise to only 25% of maximum internal quantum efficiency (IQE) according to spin statistics11 where the exciton formation through charge (electron and hole) recombination can give rise to singlet:triplet excitons in the ratio of 25%:75%. The maximum EQE of a fluorescent OLED could rise up to 5% (20% of 25% singlet exciton), assuming a light out-coupling efficiency (Î·out) of 20% in Lambertian emission.12 OLEDs based on phosphorescent emitters are promising alternatives as they offer 100% IQE, thanks to the access to the triplet states via intersystem crossing (ISC), which is triggered by the strong spinâorbit coupling (SOC) of the heavy metal center.11,13â17 The seminal work on phosphorescent OLED includes the use of 2,3,7,8,12,13,17,18-octaethyl-21H,23H-porphyrine platinum(II) complex (PtOEP) in combination with a fluorescent host Alq3, where an enhanced IQE and EQE of 23% and 4%, respectively, were reported by the efficient energy transfer to the emitter from the host.11 Numerous other RGB OLEDs based on phosphorescent materials have then been developed with nearly 100% IQE and moderate to good EQEs (up to â¼33%).18â21 However, the low natural abundance and the environmental contamination associated with the often-used heavy metals in phosphorescent OLEDs enhance the overall cost of commercialized materials. Other drawbacks include the degradation of the device due to the annihilation of the high energy polarons (singlet or triplet)22 and also the dissociation of the ligand by forming unstable radical cation in the complex.23,24

The third-generation emitter that operates by thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) mechanism is the most promising alternative to conventional 1st and 2nd generation fluorescent and phosphorescent emitters. TADF emitters are generally recognized with a small singlet (S1) and triplet (T1) energy gap (ÎEST usually <0.1 eV) where the triplet excitons can be thermally upconverted to the singlet excitons by reverse intersystem crossing (RISC), thereby offering 100% of IQE (Fig. 1a). Thus, TADF emitters typically exhibit a prompt fluorescence lifetime (transition of singlet excitons from S1 to the ground state) and a delayed fluorescence lifetime (transition of upconverted triplet excitons to the ground state from the S1 state after spin-flip). Since the seminal report of a series of purely-organic TADF emitters with an established TADF mechanism by Adachi and co-workers in 2012,25 the field of TADF-OLEDs has witnessed a complete resurgence where highly emissive RGB TADF emitters (Î»PL/EL â¼ 430â700 nm) with astonishingly high EQE values have been reported.26 However, less reported are the NIR-TADF emitters and fabrication of their OLED devices, which are useful in a plethora of applications; such as biomedical applications (in vivo imaging, photothermal and anticancer therapies), biosensors, surveillance, and the night vision to name a few.27â29 Other plausible ways for effective use of NIR light comprise NIR quantum dots (NIR-QDs) and NIR emitting perovskites. NIR semiconductor QDs with their 3D quantum confinement effect and distinct optoelectronic properties (narrow and symmetrical emissions in NIR-I and NIR-II) hold great promise, e.g., a protease-activatable NIR-II QD composed of PbS/CdS/ZnS (core/shell/shell, Î»em = 1200 nm) by Jeong et al. showed selective tumor imaging via a colon cancer mouse model.30 In another example, dual-emissive fluorescent nanohybrid (DEFN) as far-red gold nanoclusters with NIR PbS QDs (AuNCsPbS-QDs, Î»em = 640 nm and 813 nm) exhibited target-responsive detectability of ascorbic acid with excellent photostability, rapid response, and high selectivity.31 On the other hand, inorganic and/or inorganic-organic hybrid perovskites, for example, CsPbI3, FAPbI3 (FA = formamidinium) and CH3NH3PbI332 with a band gap of 400â850 nm show promising application in NIR-OLEDs (the electroluminescence (EL) peak typically being centered around 700â800 nm), NIR-LEDs solar cells,33,34 photodetectors,35 X-ray detection,36â38 night vision devices,39,40 and biolabeling.41 Although intriguing, the NIR perovskite OLEDs typically suffer from trap mediated non-radiative recombination42 with a Î»EL limited up to 800 nm. A further attempt to red shift the Î»EL with Sn-based perovskites results in unbalanced charge injection and oxidation of Sn2+ to Sn4+.32 Also, the use of Pb and Cd is environment non-friendly and high temperatures with prolonged duration is required for the synthesis of NIR-QDs.43 The NIR-TADFs are advantageous over the NIR-QDs and NIR perovskites (a) by providing 100% exciton harvesting facility,26 (b) mechanical flexibility and conformability, typically being purely organic in nature and (c) the associated non-toxicity makes them biocompatible and suitable for wearable ones.44,45

According to the energy-gap law, with a red/NIR shift of the emission maximum, the rate of the non-radiative transitions (knr) increases rapidly due to the mixing of the vibrational levels of the excited and ground electronic states, which directly impacts the efficiency of the NIR-OLED device.47 Another challenge is that (3) the organic layer materials, inclusive of the emitter, should possess high thermal (for vacuum deposited devices) and morphological (for solution processed devices) stabilities to compensate for the Joule heat produced during the device operation.26 With the progress in the field of NIR-TADF emitters and their OLEDs, several strategies have been adopted to design and NIR-shift the emission of a TADF emitter, e.g., (a) optimization of the donor (D) and acceptor (A) orbital overlap to afford a degree of communication between the DâA (or D-spacer-A) covalently bonded unit to facilitate the faster radiative rate without largening the ÎEST. (b) Introduction of rigidity and planar extended Ï-conjugation in the A-moiety with strong accepting electron withdrawing groups (EWGs) to induce intermolecular interactions48 and (c) TADF assisted fluorescence (TAF) where the emission energy of the TADF is transferred to an external fluorophore by virtue of FÃ¶rster Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET), and the final NIR emission is originated from the fluorophore.49 Approaches (a) and (b) of incorporating strong and suitable DâA units with rigid and extended Ï-structures help to decrease the gaps of HOMOâLUMO and ÎEST and increase PLQY (and thus EQE of the device) by prohibiting the non-radiative decay through vibrational and rotational deactivations.50 On the other hand, strategy (c) helps to narrow-bandwidth the NIR emission.49,51 (4) Aggregation induced quenching (ACQ) is one of the limitations in designing NIR-TADF emitters.52 In comparison to organic semiconductors with larger energy gap, well-conjugated NIR-TADF emitters show a higher degree of molecular planarity, thus favoring a substantial formation of poorly emissive (H-type) aggregates.53 Such a problem can be tackled by (1) judicious molecular designing54 or by tethering the emitter into cyclodextrin rings resulting in conjugated polyrotaxanes,55 (2) dilution in a solid-state matrix (acting as quenching inhibitor and a charge transport agent) either through solution or vacuum processing,56 (3) copolymerization with a polymer host with a wider bandgap, thus in turn leading to reduced phase segregation.57

The EQE could further be enhanced by the effective horizontal orientation of the emitting transition dipole moments (TDMs) and recombination and balanced charge transport in the EML. It has also been observed that a highly twisted DâA structure is not crucial for NIR-shifting the emission; in some cases, unprecedented NIR-shift is also observed with a compensatory through-space intermolecular interaction effect between adjacent molecules.58 Since the donor has a limited role in designing a NIR-TADF emitter,47,59 this review comprehensively focuses on various designs of acceptor moieties that help NIR-shift the emission maximum without compromising the PLQY and thus help achieve efficient NIR-TADF OLEDs with high EQEs. 2. Conjugated/fused Ï-acceptor (pyrazine) with strong EWGs

Acene molecules containing nitrogen atoms, such as dicyanoquinoxaline/dicyanopyrazinoquinoxaline derivatives were found to be useful in various fields of applications, such as n-type semiconductors in field-effect transistors,61 photocatalysts,62 pharmacological activities,63etc. These types of derivatives enjoy the following advantages, (1) the presence of cyano and pyrazinopyrazine groups induces strong electron withdrawing ability, (2) an effective intermolecular interaction due to the extended Ï-conjugated arrangement, (3) easy tuning of the HOMOâLUMO gap by introducing substituents at the phenyl that would lead to highly polarized DâA structure and consequently elevated molecular packing. However, these advantageous properties have never been explored as NIR emitters until the first organic NIR-TADF was reported by Wang et al. as TPA-DCPP (Î»PL (CHCl3) = 720 nm, Î»PL (neat film) = 708 nm), 1, having a V-shaped DâÏâAâÏâD structure, which was applied for fabricating highly efficient deep-red/NIR-OLEDs.64 In this molecule 2,3-dicyanopyrazino phenanthrene (DCPP) was used as the acceptor due to the presence of large and rigid Ï-conjugation and strong electron-withdrawing capability, whereas diphenylamine (DPA) was used as an electron donor due to its excellent hole-transporting ability and to mitigate the effect of aggregation-induced quenching. The DFT calculations of the molecule were also performed to study the molecular orbital distribution from which the dihedral angle between DCPP and the phenyl-conjugated bridge was found to be 35Â° (Fig. 4a). The small dihedral angle suggested a near-planar arrangement and a reasonable orbital overlap, enhancing the fluorescence rate (kF = 9.0 Ã 10â7 sâ1). From the time-independent DFT (TD-DFT) calculation, the ÎEST value was found to be 0.20 eV. Thus, the molecular design proved effective in affording a small ÎEST and large kF value. Molecule 1 was synthesized by the Suzuki coupling of substituted DPA and DCPP moieties (Scheme 1). The photoluminescence (PL) spectra of this molecule in different solvents, e.g., chloroform (CHCl3), tetrahydrofuran (THF), and dichloromethane (DCM) were studied, and they exhibited red-shifted TADF emission. 6. Non-conjugated quinone-acceptor with dendron donors

Dendrimer-based OLEDs are attractive alternatives to traditional fluorescent, phosphorescent or TADF OLEDs as the intermolecular interactions and ACQs between the emissive cores are efficiently reduced, thus ensuring the stable operation of a solution-processed device. In this context and to prevent the ACQ of emission in a TADF molecule, Sun, Jiang, and coworkers reported for the first time two NIR DâÏâAâÏâD-type TADF dendrimers, MPPA-Cz, 29 and MPPA-3Cz, 30 with non-conjugated carbazole/tricarbazole as peripheral dendrons (Fig. 13a and b).101 The dendrons were connected with the bromo substituted anthraquinone moiety by Suzuki coupling to afford the molecules 29 and 30 in a moderate yield of 40â55%, where the presence of peripheral carbazole moieties and the long alkyl chains enhanced the donating nature of the dendrons. To compare the photophysical properties of molecules 29 and 30, a simple donorâacceptor moiety 2,6-bis(4-(bis(4-methoxyphenyl)amino)phenyl)anthracene-9,10-dione (MPPA) was also synthesized (Fig. 13c). The DFT calculation suggested a ÎEST value of 0.18 eV for MPPA where the HOMO orbital was mostly confined over the methoxy triphenylamine units, and the LUMO was distributed on the anthraquinone-acceptor and partially on the phenyl substituent. The ÎEST and the HOMOâLUMO distribution of 29 and 30 were nearly similar to the emissive core MPPA and matched well with the experimentally observed ÎEST ranging from 0.16â0.17 eV. Neat films of 29 and 30 exhibited Î»PL at 715 nm and 708 nm, respectively, whereas the same of MPPA was not detected. This observation suggested that the introduction of non-conjugated aliphatic chain carbazole dendrons would enhance the emission by decreasing the ACQ, thus inducing the Î¦PL value of 6% (for 29) and 8% (for 30) compared to the non-emitting MPPA. To confirm the TADF character of these compounds, the transient PL decay of MPPA and 29 and 30 in thin films were studied. The ÏF and ÏTADF values of MPPA were found to be 10.8 ns and 0.36 Î¼s, respectively. Molecules 29 and 30 showed delayed fluorescence decays at 0.56 Î¼s and 0.93 Î¼s, respectively, representing that the introduction of the dendrons in the TADF core enhanced the rate of RISC. To investigate the EL performance, host-free solution processed OLED devices were fabricated as ITO/PEDOT:PSS (HIL, 40 nm)/(29 or 30) (EML, 40 nm)/TPBI (ETL, 40 nm)/Cs2O3 (2 nm)/Al (100 nm). Molecules 29 and 30 showed Î»EL at 728 nm and 715 nm, respectively, with CIE coordinates of x = 0.70, y = 0.29 (for 29) and x = 0.69, y = 0.30 (for 30). It was also observed that at a range of voltage from 7â16 V, device 29 showed very high color stability, whereas device 30 showed a weak blue shift (Fig. 13d). The non-doped OLED device of 30 showed an EQE value of 0.254% at 135 cd mâ2, whereas the device of 29 showed an EQE value of 0.064% at 24 cd mâ2 (Fig. 13e). This suggested that there needs to be an optimized number of carbazole-ended alkyl chains to fine-balance the effect of ACQ and EQE. At a high current density of up to 400 mA cmâ2, the non-doped 29 and 30 devices showed negligible change in the efficiency roll-off. This study suggested the scope of flexible alkyl chain TADF dendrimers that could be improved by optimizing the device structures.

7. Conclusion and future perspective

This review emphasizes the various strategies developed over the recent past for the effective NIR-shift of Î»PL/EL of purely organic TADF emitters with enhanced OLED device performance. Since the first example of NIR-TADF emitter, TPA-DCPP (Î»EL = 710 nm, EQE = 2.1%),64 careful design of the acceptor moiety with extended Ï-conjugated structure has led to evolution of some champion emitters, e.g., TPA-CN-N4-Py (Î»EL = 712 nm, EQE = 21.9%),48 TPA-PZTCN (Î»EL = 734 nm, EQE = 1.1%),49 and TPAAP (Î»EL = 765 nm, EQE = 5.1%).77 The strategies of exciplex emission and resonance energy transfer also proved to be effective in enhancing the exciton utilization and improving the device performance; examples include POZ-DBPHz/m-MTDATA exciplex (Î»EL = 741 nm, EQE = 5.0%),83 TPA-PZTCN (sensitizer) + BBT-TPA (NIR fluorophore) (Î»EL = 901 nm, EQE = 1.1%, TAF).49 The approach of using a conjugated benzothiadiazole acceptor (TPACNBZ, Î»EL = 712 nm, EQE = 6.57%) becomes more effectual when an analogous acceptor is used as a fluorophore in a TAF-OLED (TPA-THQ (NIR fluorophore) + TPA-DCPP (sensitizer) Î»EL â¼ 780 nm, EQE = 3.3%). Solution processable OLED with simple structured emitter as dendron donor + quinone acceptor, MPPACz, also shows promising EQE of 0.25% at Î»EL = 728 nm through dipole induced ICT.101 The curcuminoid BF2 derivative, TPABF2 affords an impressive solution-processable NIR-OLED with an EQE of â¼10% and negligible efficiency roll-off at a high current density of 750 mA cmâ2 with a record high radiance due to the quenching of STA and TTA.50

Although the past decade has witnessed a wide resurgence, there is a large scope for further development in the field of NIR-TADF-OLED. (1) Improving the color purity without the use of a NIR optical filter could be of more practical use.60 The inherent ICT induced broadening of FWHM of a typical NIR-TADF could be well-addressed with multi-resonance TADF emitters where a short-range reorganization of the electron density from S1 to S0 is observed in a fused polycyclic aromatic compound with a donor and acceptor (typically N and B atoms para to each other), maintaining a high degree of electronâhole overlap.108,109 (2) It has been observed that an increase in doping ratio often red-shifts the emission maxima albeit with a stark sacrifice in both the PLQY and EQE due to ACQ.101,102 The effect of aggregation induced emission enhancement (AIE) could be introduced with extended Ï-surface, e.g., conjugated thiadiazole moiety, to counter-balance the adverse effect of ACQ.110,111 (3) Typically a rational design of twisted DâA or D-spacer-A leads to effective FMO overlap. This results in a bright fluorescence with a fast RISC process and effective triplet harvesting while avoiding the TTA effect. In this respect, since the NIR luminescence of a TADF is typically associated with the 1CT transition, the detrimental 3LE state should be well separated from the 1CT state.112 (4) In some cases where the FMO integral is poor, through-space electronic interactions between adjacent TADF molecules could also serve as a fruitful pathway for effective lowering of ÎEST and thus faster RISC.58 (5) To better commercialize with lower fabrication cost, the NIR-TADF OLEDs should be comprised of minimal sandwiched layers preferably with finely tuned and rationally designed HOMOâLUMO barriers including small organic or dendrimeric organic emitting layers.26
