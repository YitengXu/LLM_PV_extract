Abstract

Multifunctional electrocatalyst enabled electrochemical hydrogen/oxygen redox plays pivotal roles in variable energy conversion/storage devices and some coupling devices. The daunting challenge in developing multifunctional electrocatalysts at present is to effectively incorporate multiple active sites into one material. Herein, we presented a general protocol by a controllable pyrolysis/vapor reforming process, which allows for reconstitution to form CoNC nano-units, while preserving Co and Co oxides simultaneously. The material by co-intercalation of these active units into graphene generates outstanding trifunctional activities. The overpotentials for hydrogen and oxygen evolution reactions are 205 and 360 mV (at 10 mA cmâˆ’2), respectively, and the half-wave potential for the oxygen reduction reaction is 0.81 V, outperforming most of the state-of-the-art trifunctional electrocatalysts. A maximum power density of 23 mW cmâˆ’2 and 1000 stable cycles were realized in the as-prepared material equipped Znâ€“air battery. This battery further drove overall water splitting for 24 hours, at a faradaic efficiency of ca. 100% and gas production rate of 0.035 and 0.017 mL minâˆ’1 for hydrogen and oxygen, respectively. Thus this work offers a general approach to explore other efficient multifunctional electrocatalysts for application in renewable energy technologies.

Introduction

The growing problems associated with energy security and environmental pollution have stimulated extensive efforts worldwide for the development of green and renewable energy technologies, including metalâ€“air batteries, fuel cells, electrolysis cells, and solar synthetic reactors.1â€“7 The hydrogen evolution/oxidation reaction (HER/HOR) and the oxygen reduction/evolution reaction (ORR/OER) are the most crucial electrochemical processes and usually coexist to realize energy storage and conversion in these technologies. Typical examples are the metalâ€“air battery involving ORR and OER, and the water splitting process combining the HER and OER. The development of multifunctional electrocatalysts with low overpotentials and high activities is central for simplifying the design of the electrodes for fuel cells, metalâ€“air batteries and overall water splitting setups.8,9 Noble metals such as RuO2 and IrO2 capable of executing the OER with high catalytic activities still suffer from the limitation of single function.10 Pt-based catalysts, although function well in the ORR, HER and HOR, underperform in the OER.10 Further, the critical drawback of scarcity and high cost render these noble metals less ideal materials for incorporation into bulk production scale devices. Since Pt is still irreplaceable in the HOR,10 the exploitation of low-cost, efficient trifunctional electrocatalysts for the HER, ORR and OER still remains a big challenging issue.

Among the non-noble metal alternatives, cobalt-based hybrids have harvested extensive interest, because they are effective in the OER or HER based on the low energy barrier for H adsorption or favorable electrostatic affinity for water dissociation.7,11â€“15 These monofunctional alternatives were further extended to bifunctional application towards both the HER and OER by manipulation in the electronic structure.7,16 To take a further step towards trifunctional application (HER/OER/ORR), the unsatisfactory ORR activity of cobalt-based materials needs to be improved upon. In this context, previously reported carbon-based electrocatalysts such as nitrogen-doped graphene have shown excellent electrocatalytic activity for the ORR.17â€“19 Of particular interest, heteroatom doped graphitic carbon and defective graphene have been revealed to be trifunctionally active, although most of which focused on fast ORR/OER kinetics.4,20,21 However, a mechanical combination of cobalt-based materials and N-doped graphene may generate harmonious trifunctional electrocatalysis. Nevertheless, the best performance reported to date22,23 is still not unified in one system. In particular, it is not in one electrocatalytic material and under the same pH environment for an overall highly efficient trifunctional electrocatalysis. One of the reasons is due to the lack of efficient structure control for fully utilizing the active sites of the material. Moreover, there is a discrepancy in the synthetic method and reaction mechanism of monofunctional units. A mandatory intercalation of such active units into one system likely causes a mutual inhibition of their respective performance. Therefore, design for the simultaneous optimization of monofunctional active units without mutual inhibition is highly desirable and feasible for efficient trifunctional electrocatalysis. Coupling N-doped graphene (NG) with Co-based materials has two merits: one is improvement of the HER/OER electrocatalytic activities of cobalt species via increasing the number of active sites and enhancing the interfacial charge transfer; the other is reinforcement of the ORR activity via electron density modulation of cobalt (cobaltâ€“nitrogen-doped carbon, CoNC).24â€“29 Besides, the electrocatalytic performance is strongly governed by components, structures and morphologies.30 Inspired by these analyses, only exquisite multiform cobalt species/CoNC nano-units with abundant accessible active sites via unified synthetic processes are technically desirable to derive advanced trifunctional hybrid electrocatalysts.

Herein, we employed a precursor with hydrogen bonding interactions. A controllable nitrogenâ€“carbon pyrolysis/vapor reforming method was demonstrated, for reconstitution to form CoNC nano-units, preserving Co and Co oxides simultaneously. The material by co-intercalation of these active units into highly conductive graphene (D-Co@CNG) generates synergetic trifunctional activities. It displayed only 59, 320 and 230 mV of onset overpotentials for the HER, ORR and OER, with corresponding Tafel slopes of 95, 83 and 130 mV decâˆ’1, respectively, outperforming most of the state-of-the-art trifunctional electrocatalysts. More importantly, excellent stability was revealed for the D-Co@CNG electrode relative to noble metal benchmarks. The robust trifunctional performance further enables the long-term operation of Znâ€“air batteries (over 1000 cycles per week) and water electrolysis (about 24 hours). An alkaline electrolyzer was fabricated by using D-Co@CNG as both the cathode and anode. This electrolyzer was powered by two series-connected Znâ€“air batteries based on an air electrode made from the same electrocatalyst. This water-splitting unit can produce stable H2 and O2 with 0.035 and 0.017 mL minâˆ’1 gas production rate, respectively. Based on the aforesaid points, it is fair to say that this work puts forth a general framework for the design of robust multifunctional catalysts for zincâ€“air batteries and overall water splitting setups.

As illustrated in Fig. 1 and ESI Fig. 1,â€  cobalt ions are first adsorbed on the surface of graphene oxide (GO) by electrostatic interaction in the mixed solution. The anchoring effect of 2-MIM can be attributed to the hydrogen bonding between â€“OH groups on GO and N moieties on 2-MIM. To illuminate the origin of robust trifunctional performance on D-Co@CNG, we have connected the physical structure and component of the catalyst to its HER, OER and ORR activities. Firstly, more promotion in the HER and OER mainly comes from Co doping than N doping, as demonstrated by comparison of CoG and NG. It was also verified that metallic cobalt and CoxOy contribute more to the high HER via different roles with synergistic effects:50 metallic cobalt possesses a suitable binding energy close to Pt for the H atom;51â€“53 while cobalt oxides can be hydroxylated to dissociate water based on a strong electrostatic affinity to the locally positively charged Co(II/III)/species.54,55 The D-Co@CNG hybrid contains metallic cobalt and Co oxides (as confirmed by XRD and XPS analysis). Thus, abundant OHâˆ’ will be generated from H2O decomposition and preferentially attach to the Co oxide surface, and the decomposed H tends to adsorb on the nearby cobalt site. This synergistic effect then probably facilitates the HER through the Volmerâ€“Heyrovsky pathway based on the case that its Tafel slope (95 mV per decade) is comparable to that for NiO/Ni (ca. 51 mV decâˆ’1).56 NG without cobalt doping also exhibits lower activity relative to CoG for the OER, again revealing the pivotal role of Co in the OER. It has been reported that Co(II) can yield better catalytic ability while Co(III) contributed less to the OER.50 In our case, deconvolution of Co(II) is difficult to conduct in the XPS spectrum because of the complex cobalt components in D-Co@CNG. However, continuous oxygen generation at high potential may induce partial transformation of Co(II) to Co(III), thus resulting in the decrease of catalytic efficiency in the OER test.50

Although CN plays a less important role in the HER and OER, its contribution to the ORR is exclusively prominent. Comparison of Co@CNG, NG, CoG and G in the ORR (ESI Fig. 19bâ€ ) obviously demonstrated the critical effect of N on onset potentials. Meanwhile, the effect arising from cobalt still can't be overlooked as it influenced the limiting current. D-Co@CNG, D-Co@CN and Co@CNG which contain abundant highly active pyridinic CoNC units outperform CN in ORR activity. This result suggests that CoNC sites contributed more to the activity in comparison to Câ€“N sites. It has been reported Coâ€“Nâ€“C species hold a strong interaction with HO2âˆ’,32 thereby facilitating an approximate four electron reduction process for the ORR. Another notable advantage of D-Co@CNG in comparison to D-Co@CN is the incorporation with highly conductive graphene. This incorporation yields a high ion diffusion rate and evidently lower charge transfer resistance (ESI Fig. 22â€ ), which imparts preferable ORR, HER and OER activities. Although incorporation of graphene improved the integral conductivity of the materials, the local charge transfer between different active moieties still cannot be ignored.57 In XPS analysis, we concluded that N incorporation induced the positive charge of adjacent carbon atoms, which are considered to be the active sites for the ORR.43,58 Meanwhile, Co incorporation may also slightly modulate the electron configuration of C at a molecular level, towards more favorable ORR processes. Vice versa, the electronic configuration of Co was changed by C and N. Due to the co-existence of diversified Co species in the material, however, precise structural analysis associated with the HER and OER activities is difficult to conduct. The more complex model and mechanism require further investigation. The surface area is not a dominant factor, but it may still play some degree of electrocatalytic role in D-Co@CNG relative to other materials, except D-Co@CN. All the above results indicate that the remarkable multifunctional electrocatalytic performance of D-Co@CNG could be attributed to the synergistic effect of co-intercalated metallic cobalt, Co oxide and CoNC active units in graphene, together with relatively high conductivity and large surface area.32,50

As a proof of concept for the multifunctional electrocatalytic system, we further constructed an integrated water-splitting setup. Faradaic efficiency approaching 100% was obtained during the long-term electrolysis (see the ESI â€ ). Conclusions

In summary, we have demonstrated a controllable pyrolysis/vapor reforming process for reconstitution to form CoNC nano-units, while preserving Co and Co oxides simultaneously. The co-intercalation of these active monofunctional units into graphene generates three positive effects: (1) the generated highly active Co/CoxOy nano-units promote the HER and OER; (2) incorporation of NC species, especially, the highly active pyridinic Coâ€“Nâ€“C imparted high ORR activity; (3) the hybrid with high conductivity and relatively large active surface area offered fast electron transfer and abundant active sites for trifunctional reactions. The newly developed trifunctional electrocatalyst enables a rechargeable Znâ€“air battery to deliver a maximum power density of around 23 mW cmâˆ’2 and excellent charge/discharge performance for over 1000 cycles (one week). The Znâ€“air batteries can power a water-splitting setup for approximately 24 hours with faradaic efficiency close to 100%, and a gas production rate of 0.035 and 0.017 mL minâˆ’1 for hydrogen and oxygen, respectively. Thus, this work puts forth a general strategy for developing multifunctional electrocatalysts towards application in renewable energy technologies.

Synthesis of GO

Graphite oxide (GO) was prepared according to the previous literature by Hummers from graphite powder (Aldrich, powder, <20 micron, synthetic).59,60

Synthesis of C3N4

In a typical synthesis, 5 g melamine was first heated at 600 Â°C in an Ar atmosphere for 4 h with a heating rate of 5 Â°C minâˆ’1. The resultant product was fully ground to get C3N4 powder.

The Coâ€“C3N4â€“GO powder was then placed in the centre of a quartz tube furnace (Î¦ = 10 cm, OTF-1200X-100). Electrochemical measurements

Three-electrode measurements were performed with a CHI660e electrochemical working station (CH Instrument Inc.) in a three-electrode cell at room temperature. The Pt net (1 cm2) and the Ag/AgCl saturated with KCl were used as the counter and reference electrodes, respectively. A glassy carbon (GC) electrode covered by the as-prepared catalyst was used as the working electrode. For the preparation of the working electrode, a specific amount of the catalytic material, commercial 20 wt% Pt/C catalyst or 20 wt% Ir/C catalyst was dispersed in a suspension containing 950 Î¼L of isopropyl alcohol and 50 Î¼L of 20 wt% Nafion solution (DuPont, USA) under ultrasonic stirring. The slurry was pipetted with a micro-syringe and spread on the polished GC electrode surface and the electrode was dried under an infrared lamp (catalyst loading 0.2 mg cmâˆ’2). 1 M KOH and 0.1 M KOH aqueous solutions saturated with nitrogen/oxygen were employed as the electrolyte for the HER/OER and ORR, respectively. The potentials, measured against an Ag/AgCl electrode, were converted to the potentials versus the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) by RHE calibration. The details of the calculation of the number of electrons transferred and the percentage of HO2âˆ’ generated during the oxygen reduction, and RHE calibration are given in the following sections. All measurements with assessment of activity are IR-drop corrected.

For Znâ€“air battery testing, the ORR air electrode was prepared by uniformly coating the as-prepared catalyst ink or Pt/Câ€“Ir/C (1:1 in mass ratio) on a carbon paper electrode (catalyst loading 0.5 mg cmâˆ’2). A Zn plate was used as the anode. 6 M KOH + 0.2 M Zn(Ac)2 was used as an electrolyte of the Znâ€“air battery.

For the preparation of the water splitting electrodes, the nickel foam was pre-treated in 3 M hydrochloric acid solution for 30 min at room temperature in order to remove oxides on the surface. The nickel foam was rolled up along with the end of a copper wire. The superfluous copper end was coated with epoxy resin to resist the electrolyte. The catalyst was loaded by adsorbing a catalyst ink (10 mg mLâˆ’1) on the pre-treated nickel foam, followed by drying at 80 Â°C. The catalyst loading is about 1 mg cmâˆ’2. For the calculation of H2 and O2 production volumes during the water splitting, a calibration was conducted by deducting the corresponding volumes of Cu wire.
