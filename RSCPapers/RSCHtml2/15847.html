Abstract

We review photoinduced charge transfer in organic solar cells without and with an external electric field and then we introduce the visualization methods of the transition density, charge difference density and transition density matrix for the analysis of the photoinduced charge transfer in a neutral system and a charged system excited by one-photon and two-photon absorption. This review will not only promote a deeper understanding of the available theories and methods of PICT, but also lead to further developments in this field.

1. Introduction

With the increasing development of our society, searching for alternative and effective energy supplies has attracted immense attention from both industrial and academic communities, mostly due to the increasing demand for energy sources and the depletion of traditional fossil energies.1 It is reported that the annual energy input of solar irradiation on Earth (5% UV, 43% visible, 52% IR) actually exceeds the annual energy consumption of the world by several orders of magnitude.2 As we all know, solar energy possesses the characteristics of inexhaustibility and no pollution, and is regarded as the most promising renewable energy of the future.3

Recently, organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices have become highly attractive, due to the fact that they have numerous potential advantages over traditional silicon-based solar cells,4,5 such as low cost, flexibility, large-area capability, easy processing and so on.6 The working principle of organic solar cells relies on the conversion of solar emissions into electrical charges, which are collected in an external circuit.7 The main steps of operation are as follows. (i) Light absorption: light is absorbed in the organic layer and generates electron–hole pairs (excitons); the absorption characteristics of the organic layer should match the solar spectrum as closely as possible. (ii) Exciton dissociation: excitons then have to be dissociated, that is, charge separated; for the charge-separation process to be efficient, the reverse process, which is called charge recombination, must be limited properly. (iii) Charge transport: the charges that remain separated (and hence do not recombine after exciton dissociation) have to drift in the organic layer under the influence of the electric field, which is generated by the equalization of the Fermi energies of the two electrodes. (iv) Charge collection: the charges ultimately have to be collected at the electrodes.1 In other words, the key process set forth above is the dissociation of the photogenerated excitons into separated charges.8

The exciton dissociation and charge recombination both correspond to the charge-transfer reaction and their corresponding rates can be estimated using the weak coupling limit of Marcus theory and extensions thereof.9 What's more, Marcus theory has been widely employed to describe the dynamics of charge transfer and charge recombination in any donor–acceptor system, including organic solar cells.10 The theory is valid, based on the assumption that the system has to reach the transition state for the transfer to occur, though it neglects tunneling effects that can assist the transfer, especially at low temperatures.11 As a development of the Marcus theory, the Marcus–Levich–Jortner energy transfer rate expression emphasizes the vibrationally quantized energy transfer reaction.12 This formula introduces the population of the vibrational state density in the initial reactant and the final product, i.e., the Franck–Condon and intramolecular charge transfer electronic states.13 In the following review, the above discussed formulas both have two forms for the situation without an external electric field and with an external electric field, respectively. It is surprising that one recent paper noted that energy transfer coupled with electron transfer is a convenient approach to mimic photosynthesis in light energy conversion.13

It has been reported that fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) can significantly enhance the light harvesting and photocurrent generation in solid-state dye sensitized solar cells,14,15 because of both exciton energy transfer and nanoscale charge generation in the blend via the formation of an excited state complex with charge transfer character.16

The tools and techniques of visualization have become extremely attractive to understand and explore the optoelectronic properties of solar cells. There are several elaborate theoretical approaches that have been used to inspect visually all molecular orbitals contributing to the excitation for large conjugated carotenoids and polymers.17 One is the two-dimensional (2D) real-space analysis of the transition density matrix, representing the electronic transition between the ground state and the electronically excited state,18–25 which is used to analyze the electron–hole coherence and the excitation delocalization of conjugated molecules.17 Another one is the three-dimensional (3D) real-space analysis of the transition density (TD) and charge difference density (CDD),18,21 which has been used to analyze the charge and energy transfers in several conjugated polymers. The combination of 2D and 3D real-space analysis methods has been widely used to study the excited-state properties of polymers.18,21,26,27

In this review, we review the available methods of photoinduced charge transfer and the visualization methods of several systems including one-photon, two-photon and charged systems, respectively. This review will not only promote a deeper understanding of the available theories and methods of photoinduced charge transfer, but also advance further developments in this field.

The above discussion is on the 2D and 3D methods in neutral systems; in fact, these methods are also suitable for a charged system.

Sun and coworkers studied the electronic structure and charge transfer of ppp cation radicals theoretically. It was found that most extra positive charges move to two outer units upon excitation (see Fig. 32), which were mostly localized on the two central units in the ground state.61 Therefore, direct visual evidence for quinoidal charge delocalization in the PPP cation radical can be clearly derived. From Fig. 33, the exciton for S1 is within two adjacent units (AB units and CD units, see |Q|2), while the electron–hole oscillation is between AB units and CD units, so S1 is a strong charge transfer (between AB and CD) excited state (see |P|2). Note that the exciton of S8 is not strongly localized within two adjacent units (AB units and CD units) but rather is very delocalized (see Fig. 33) and the ability of charge transfer between the units of S8 is much weaker than that of S1 (see Fig. 32). The real space transition densities for S1 show significantly different characteristic features from that of S8 (see Fig. 34). Therefore, the transition dipole moment of S1 represents mesoscopic dipole antennae, while the transition dipole moment of S8 should rather be represented by a series of small transition dipoles, one per monomeric unit.62

4. Conclusions and perspectives

We have already reviewed photon induced charge transfer by one and two phonon absorption in organic solar cells in detail, including the theoretical methods of PICT and two-dimensional and three-dimensional visualization methods in one-photon, two-photon and charged systems. However, there are still many challenges ahead of us that should be overcome in order to improve the photoelectric conversion efficiency of organic solar cells, because the main factor that impedes the development of organic solar cells is still their low photoelectric conversion efficiency. Therefore, in the future, it will be necessary for us to do more experimental and theoretical research so as to achieve the full potential of enhancing PICT in various ways. For example, designing more rational FRET systems to make full use of their advantages for promoting PICT and searching for new effective means to promote the process of PICT. More convenient theoretical calculation methods should also be used, since a perfect theoretical calculation is half of the battle. What's more, more intuitive and precise visualization methods should be applied to the presentation and analysis of experimental image results, which would be of benefit to understand the experimental phenomena intuitively.
