Abstract

Since the first discovery of Ti3C2 in 2011, two-dimensional (2D) transition-metal carbides, carbonitrides and nitrides, known as MXenes, have attracted significant attention. Due to their outstanding electronic, optical, mechanical, and thermal properties, versatile structures and surface chemistries, Ti3C2 MXenes have emerged as new candidates with great potential for applications in optoelectronic devices, such as photovoltaics, photodetectors and photoelectrochemical devices. The excellent metallic conductivity, high anisotropic carrier mobility, good structural and chemical stabilities, high optical transmittance, excellent mechanical strength, tunable work functions, and wide range of optical absorption properties of Ti3C2 MXene nanostructures are the key to their success in a number of electronic and photonic device applications. Herein, we summarize the fundamental properties and preparation of pure Ti3C2 MXenes, functionalized Ti3C2 MXenes and their hybrid nanocomposites, as well as their optoelectronic applications. In the end, the perspective and current challenges of Ti3C2 MXenes toward the development of advanced MXene-based nanostructures are briefly discussed for future optoelectronic applications.

1. Introduction

Since the successful exfoliation of graphene from graphite using Scotch tape by Novoselov, Geim and their co-workers in 2004,1 two-dimensional (2D) materials have been extensively studied in the past decade due to their excellent electrical, optical, thermal, and mechanical properties.26 Undoubtedly, 2D graphene nanomaterials possess one of the great potential applications in optoelectronics, rechargeable lithium-ion batteries, catalysis, environmental protection and biomedicine.712 Hence, other graphene-like ultrathin 2D nanomaterials have also been explored and extensively studied.1318 Due to their similar structural features but different compositions from graphene, 2D metalorganic frame-works (MOFs),1923 transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs),2428 graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4),2931 black phosphorus (BP),3235 hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN),3639 layered metal oxides,4043 and layered double hydroxides (LDHs)4446 are considered typical graphene-like ultrathin 2D nanomaterials that exhibit versatile properties.47,48 Recently, a new family of novel 2D transition metal carbides, nitrides, and carbonitrides, MXenes, was discovered by Naguib et al. and has attracted extensive attention.4952

2D MXenes are generally synthesized by selectively etching away the atoms from their parent ternary MAX phases.49 The synthesized 2D MXenes with a hexagonal lattice symmetry have the general formula: Mn+1XnTx, where M is an early transition metal (Sc, Ti, V, Cr, Zr, Nb, Mo, Hf, Ta, or W), A is a group IIIA or IVA element (Al, Ga, Si, etc.), X is carbon and/or nitrogen, and Tx describes the surface-terminated functional groups (O, OH, F, etc.).53 Since their discovery in 2011, about 30 different stoichiometric MXenes have been synthesized and the structures and properties of dozens more have been theoretically predicted.5457 Owing to their promising physicochemical properties, 2D MXenes pioneered by Gogotsi and co-workers are considered as promising alternatives to graphene and other carbon nanomaterials,58 and have been developed and predominantly used in transparent conductors, catalysis, electronics, biomedicine, energy conversion and storage applications.5971

The high surface area and rich surface termination of MXene nanostructures endowed their impressive optical properties, tunable electronic properties, hydrophilic surfaces and large number of active sites with efficient loading of active substances and strong redox reactivity.50,61,7274 Additionally, MXenes have excellent metallic conductivity, high anisotropic carrier mobility, good structural and chemical stabilities, high optical transmittance, excellent mechanical strength, tuneable work function (WF) and broadband absorption.54,58,7580 Among them, 2D Ti3C2Tx nanostructures in the MXene groups were first discovered and investigated, which exhibit an electronic conductivity in the range of 600015000 S cm1,81,82 an impressively high Young's modulus in the range of 0.33 0.03 TPa measured in the single layer,83 and a broadband absorption from the ultraviolet (UV) to near-infrared (NIR) region.58,8486 Moreover, the large range (1.6 to 5.8 eV) of adjustable WF enables Ti3C2Tx MXenes combined with other semiconductors or metals to form Ohmic contacts or Schottky junctions at will.8789 Thus, these provide unique and new opportunities for MXene applications in optoelectronics. In addition, some initial studies have reported their application in solar cells, photodetectors (PDs) and photoelectrochemical (PEC) devices.9098 Therefore, there is a great need to underline their optoelectronic mechanism and compile their applications in MXene optoelectronic devices.

In this review, we systematically and comprehensively summarize the recent advances in MXene photoelectronic applications based on Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures. First, we summarize the synthetic methods for the preparation of pure MXene nanostructures, functionalized MXene nanostructures and their hybrid nanocomposites. Then we provide highlights of the theoretical studies about the effects of surface modification on their electronic and optical properties, and explore experiments to further expand their electronic, optical and mechanical properties. Afterwards, we give a demonstration of the preliminary achievements in their various optoelectronic applications, such as photovoltaics, photodetectors and photoelectrochemical devices (Fig. 1). Finally, we present a perspective on the current challenges and future development opportunities of MXenes for optoelectronic applications, aiming to inspire a broader interest in the near future.

2. Synthesis of Ti3C2Tx MXenes and nanostructures

Similar to the MAX phases, MXenes are also hexagonal crystals with a P63/mmc space group.99 As shown in Fig. 2a, the M atoms are arranged in a compact hexagonal structure with X element atoms at the octahedral interstitial sites.100 The model structures for 2D MXene systems can be constructed by removing the A element from the bulk MAX phases. In the MAX phases, the in-plane MX bond has a lower chemical activity than the metallic MAl bond, making it possible to selectively etch the Al element layer, as discussed in the previous section.54 When hydrofluoric acid (HF) or a fluorine salt selectively etches the A layer from the MAX phases, the MA bond is gradually replaced by surface terminals such as OH, F, and O, which results in the good hydrophilicity and surface structure tunability of MXenes. Chen et al. used a modified model based on the HF etching method to investigate the surface structure of Ti3C2Txvia density functional theory (DFT) calculations.100Fig. 2a displays the energetically most favorable configuration of F and OH terminations, where the OH and F terminations are orderly adsorbed on the surface of the Ti3C2 monolayer and prefer to stay on the topmost sites of the Ti atoms. The different surface terminations have dramatic effects on the structural, electronic, optical and mechanical properties of Ti3C2 nanostructures, which has been demonstrated using theoretical and experimental research studies.85,86,99,101108 In the following section, we will summarize the synthesis of MXene nanostructures, interlayer engineering and the surface modification of MXenes.

2.1. Synthesis of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures

To date, a growing number of synthesis methods have been developed to produce high-quality MXene nanosheets in high yields by selectively etching the A atoms from their parent MAX phases.65,113117 A schematic of the synthesis of MXenes from MAX phases is illustrated in Fig. 2b. The most commonly used etchant for the MXene phase, Ti3AlC2, is aqueous HF.118 However, mainly due to the safety concerns associated with the storage and handling of HF, there is a fast shift toward its in situ generation using hydrochloric acid (HCl) and lithium fluoride (LiF), ammonium hydrogen difluoride (NH4HF2), or ammonium fluoride (NH4F).50,119122 Meanwhile, some fluoride-free etching methods, such as hydrothermal method in alkali,123125 electrochemical etching method,126 and Lewis acid treatment,111,127,128 have been developed and some novel fabrication routes have been explored. Then, the Ti3C2Tx MXenes were intercalated and delaminated by the intercalation of large organic molecules116,128133 and cations,134136 which were usually used for expanding the interlayer distances of multilayered MXenes, followed by sonication treatment to produce monolayer MXenes.

On this basis, we focus on the synthesis techniques of Ti3C2Tx MXenes as well as their respective pros and cons, as listed in Table 1. We summarize the recent advances in Ti3C2Tx MXene synthesis and elucidate the relationship between the synthesis techniques and structural properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes. The detailed characteristics of the synthesis techniques as well as the properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes will be discussed in the following sections.

2.1.1. HF and fluoride salt etching. In 2011, Gogotsi et al. first fabricated Ti3C2Tx nanosheets using a 50% HF solution to selectively etch aluminum (Al) from Ti3AlC2 powders at room temperature (RT).49 Subsequently, this HF etching method has been extensively explored for preparing Ti3C2Tx MXenes.99,137141 This facile and simple method is beneficial for the high yield and easily scalable production of MXenes. The scanning electron micrograph (SEM) images of Ti3AlC2 and multilayer Ti3C2Tx are shown in Fig. 2c and d. The etching reaction based on HF etching can be described by the following equations:
Ti3AlC2 + 3HF = AlF3 + 3/2H2 + Ti3C2(1)
Ti3C2 + 2H2O = Ti3C2(OH)2 + H2(2)
Ti3C2 + 2HF = Ti3C2F2 + H2(3)
The weak bonding energy of metallic TiAl bonds induced the selective etching of the Al atoms by HF, and the exposed Ti atoms per unit formula in the exfoliated Ti3C2 layers need dangling bond passivation.63 Both DFT-based geometry optimizations and experiments demonstrated that the surfaces of Ti3C2Tx were terminated with abundant OH and F surface groups.53,86,101,105,142 There are some modified methods through changing the etching temperature, HF concentration (1050%) and reaction time to obtain flakes of different sizes, excellent conductivities and other properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes.99,112,143149 However, the difficulties associated with the high toxic nature and the dangers in handling, probably resulting in plenty of surface defects on the obtained MXenes.

Considering the disadvantages of the HF etching method, a mild etchant using LiF/HCl, NH4HF2, and NH4F was proposed for the in situ formation of HF with similar etching behavior.50,82,122,150152 After the etching of Ti3AlC2, apart from the F and OH terminations on the surface of Ti3C2Tx, there were many more O terminations in the etched samples, evidenced by the nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) experiments.153 Compared to the HF etching material, the obtained Ti3C2Tx MXenes have high quality, clay-like plasticity, large lateral size, high electrical conductivity and weak interlayer interactions.154 Cation (Li+) intercalation could promote the delamination of Ti3C2Tx MXenes with a large interlayer spacing, and is beneficial for further structural regulation.134,155 Another merit of this mild etching method is that it causes little damage to the MXenes, which reduces the defects on the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets.122

2.1.2. Fluoride-free etching. Although the HF and fluoride salt-based etching methods could effectively etch the Al from Ti3AlC2, the processes are harmful to the environment and decrease the electrochemical performance due to the F-terminal groups in Ti3C2Tx.51,74,156 Thus, some fluoride-free methods were implemented to prepare the Ti3C2Tx. Recently, due to its strong binding ability with the amphoteric element Al, alkali has been selected as an etching agent to synthesize Ti3C2Tx from Ti3AlC2.123,131,157,158 For example, Geng et al. used an organic etchant, tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAOH) for the etching and delamination of Ti3AlC2 to obtain Ti3C2 nanosheets terminated with Al(OH)4.131 Li and his co-workers reported potassium hydroxide (KOH) mixed with a little water as an etching agent to prepare Ti3C2(OH)2 nanosheets by the hydrothermal method at 180 C.158 In addition, Zhang et al. employed a 27.5 M sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution to selectively etch away Al atoms from Ti3AlC2 at 270 C and successfully obtained high-purity (92 wt%) Ti3C2Tx terminated with O and OH groups.123 The Al atoms in Ti3AlC2 can be oxidized to soluble Al(OH)41 by NaOH. The reaction temperature and the concentration of NaOH play a key role in the quality of Ti3C2Tx involving the following reactions:
Ti3AlC2 + OH + 5H2O = Ti3C2(OH)2 + Al(OH)4 + 5/2H2(4)
Ti3AlC2 + OH + 5H2O = Ti3C2O2 + Al(OH)4 + 7/2H2(5)
Although high-purity Ti3C2Tx MXenes could be obtained by the above alkali-assisted hydrothermal etching method, the preparation methods generally require high temperature, pressure and alkali concentration to dissolve Al (oxide) hydroxides. Feng and co-workers prepared Ti3C2Tx by an electrochemical etching method in a binary aqueous electrolyte.126 The aqueous electrolyte consisted of 1.0 M ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) and 0.2 M TMAOH with a pH value >9. By tailoring the composition of electrolyte, the selective removal of Al atoms followed by the replacement with hydroxide groups enable the formation of single or bilayer sheets with high yield (>90%) and large average dimension.
2.1.3. Lewis acidic melts. Very recently, another method was proposed to synthesize halide-terminated MXenes by employing Zn-based MAX as precursors and molten ZnCl2 salts as etchants in 2019.127 Firstly, Zn-based MAX was synthesized by using Lewis acids (molten ZnCl2 salts) to replace the A element in traditional MAX phases at 550 C, as the molten ZnCl2 salts could generate strong electron-accepting ligands, which thermodynamically reacted with the A element in the MAX phases. Then, Ti3ZnC2 could be further transformed into Ti3C2Cl2 MXene, as Zn dissolved well in molten ZnCl2 due to the redox reaction between Zn and Zn2+. Huang and his co-workers further proposed to directly etch MAX phases by redox coupling between the A element and the cation of CuCl2 molten salt at 750 C.111 The synthesis involves the following reactions:
Ti3SiC2 + 2CuCl2 = Ti3C2 + SiCl4(g) + 2Cu(6)
Ti3C2 + CuCl2 = Ti3C2Cl2 + Cu(7)
Lately, Talapin et al. obtained Ti3C2Cl2 and Ti3C2Br2 MXenes using CdCl2 and CdBr2 molten salts at 610 C.128 Due to the labile surface bonding of Cl- and Br-terminated MXenes, MXenes with O, NH, S, Cl, Se, Br, and Te surface terminations, as well as bare MXenes (no surface termination) were successfully prepared by the substitution/elimination reactions. The method yielded MXenes with Cl and Br, which are more stable than MXenes with F. Moreover, the reaction time is also efficiently shortened compared to that of HF etching and the reaction is environmentally friendly, which are beneficial for the large-scale and even commercial applications of MXenes. But the disadvantages of the etching process are the high synthetic temperature and energy consumption.
2.1.4. Intercalation and delamination. In order to obtain ultrathin MXene nanosheets and small-sized nanoflakes from multilayered MXenes (Fig. 2e) typical intercalation and delamination processes followed by mechanical forces are required.52 Intercalants are adopted to enlarge the interlayer spacing of MXenes through weakening the interlayer interactions within multilayered MXenes. A variety of organic molecules, such as N-methyl formamide (NMF),128 TMAOH,131 tetrabutylammonium hydroxide (TBAOH),116,155 tetrapropylammonium hydroxide (TPAOH),130,133 polypyrrole (PPy),159 dodecyltrimethylammonium bromide (DTAB), tetradecyltrimethylammonium bromide (TTAB), hexadecyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), stearyltrimethylammonium bromide (STAB), dioctadecyldimethylammonium chloride (DDAC),143 hydrazine monohydrate,160,161N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and urea,129,162 have been applied to swell the multilayered MXenes. For example, Gogotsi and his co-workers attempted to intercalate DMSO and urea into Ti3C2 at RT, and the c-lattice parameter of Ti3C2 increased from 19.5 to 35.04 and 25.0 , respectively.129 Interestingly, TMAOH not only served as an effective etchant for Al to exfoliate MXenes, but also weakened the interactions within adjacent Ti3C2 nanosheets and expanded the interlayer spaces.126,131

Apart from organic molecules, metal cations, including Li+, Na+, K+, NH4+, Mg2+, Al3+, Rb+, Cs+ and Ca2+, in aqueous solutions can also be used to intercalate multilayered MXenes for delamination, due to the intrinsic negative charges on MXenes.134,136,140,141,145,150,163168 The intercalated ions could enlarge the interlayer spacing of MXenes and weaken the van der Waals force among the layers. For instance, Gogotsi and his co-workers incorporated a variety of cations (Na+, K+, NH4+, Mg2+, and Al3+) of various charges and sizes to multilayer exfoliated Ti3C2Tx and obtained few layers of Ti3C2Tx.134 This phenomenon depends on the pH and nature of the cations. Consequently, the intercalated multilayer MXenes can easily be delaminated by mechanical force such as magnetic stirring, sonication or manual shaking to form a stable colloidal solution of monolayer nanosheets.

Recently, some groups applied aqueous KOH solution to synthesize ultrathin Ti3C2 nanoribbons by continuous shaking treatment.166,169,170 The as-prepared Ti3C2 exhibited increased interlayer d-spacing, oxygen-terminated surface, narrow widths of nanoribbons, and 3D interconnected porous frameworks. Wu and Huang et al. demonstrated a simplified and effective approach to exfoliate multilayer Ti3C2Tx flakes via the gentle water freezing-and-thawing (FAT) method.171 The intercalated water molecules could lead to the volume expansion of Ti3C2Tx flakes and promote the exfoliation of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets. After four cycles of the FAT process, the yield of large Ti3C2Tx flakes with special wrinkles could be up to 39%. However, on further treatment with the sonication process, the yield of small-sized Ti3C2Tx flakes can reach to a record high value of 81.4%. This work could greatly improve and simplify the aforementioned laborious intercalation procedures, and permit large-scale fabrication of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures.

2.2. Synthesis of Ti3C2Tx MXene quantum dots

Ti3C2Tx MXene quantum dots (MQDs), fragments of single- or few-layer Ti3C2Tx nanosheets with generally very small lateral dimensions, have been explored for several years due to their novel structures and various unique electrical and optoelectronic properties, such as a high surface area, hydrophilic surfaces, and photoluminescence (PL) properties due to quantum confinement effects, excellent electrical conductivities and high anisotropic carrier mobility.172 The electronic and optical properties of MQDs can be tuned by changing the surface termination, morphology and composition of the QDs.173 The above properties make them much more desirable for many applications in optoelectronics, biological, catalysis, sensing, energy etc.96,112,174179

Until recently, the strategy for the fabrication of MQDs was mainly focused on the top-down method.112,172,175,180182 In 2017, Zhi and his coworkers firstly reported Ti3C2 MQDs using a facile hydrothermal method.112 As shown in Fig. 2f and g, the as-prepared MQDs exhibit excitation-dependent PL properties with a quantum yield of 9.9%. At the same time, our group synthesized functionalized MQDs (10.2 nm) via a solvothermal method with Ti3C2 nanosheets as the starting material and polyethylenimine (PEI) as the surface passivation agent.110 The functionalized MQDs exhibited bright excitation-dependent blue PL with an absolute quantum yield of 7.13%, and a pH dependent luminescence behavior. Besides the hydrothermal/solvothermal method,173,176,183185 many other methods including ultrasonic treatment,180182,186,187 reflux method,188 ball-milling189 and microexplosion175 have also been proposed to synthesize MQDs. Recently, Tao et al. developed a self-designed microexplosion method to prepare nonoxidized MXene-Ti3C2Tx QDs.175 The average lateral particle size is 7.23 nm with a thickness of 5 nm. This method could be completed in a few minutes, and exhibits the advantages of safety, nontoxicity, simplicity and low-cost, and is suitable for large-scale preparation.

Based on the above statements, though various synthetic methods could efficiently provide MQDs, some obstacles should be solved, such as time consumption, low production yield, and low repeatability. Combined with the excellent electronic and optical properties of MQDs, it is desirable to explore more efficient strategies with the advantages of controllable, low-cost manufacturing process, large-scale preparation and so on.

2.3. Surface engineering

Due to the high proportion of surface exposed Ti atoms and the TiOH/F/O binding nature, Ti3C2Tx MXenes have high surface activity and vulnerable resistance to oxidization, which offers abundant opportunities to prepare functional Ti3C2Tx MXenes and Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites. Therefore, surface modification based on MXene nanostructures retains the original 2D morphology but expands the properties and applications of the MXene materials, in pursuit of modified physiochemical properties.65,190194 In this section, four surface modification methods of Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanostructures are summarized and discussed, including controlling oxidation, ion-exchange reactions, Ti3C2Tx MXene-based hybrid nanocomposites and self-assembled hierarchical structures, which will provide insight on the surface modification of Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites.
2.3.1. Controlling oxidation. Surface functional group oxidation is able to tune the surface structure and dielectric properties, and increase the electronic conductivity of Ti3C2Tx MXenes.155,195 During delamination and storage in ambient air environment, the delaminated-Ti3C2Tx monolayers are not stable toward oxidation in the colloidal solution, and spontaneously transform into titanium oxide (TiO2) nanocrystals for the formation of Ti3C2Tx MXeneTiO2 composites.196198 Subsequently, a few calcination methods have been implemented to in situ oxidize Ti3C2Tx MXenes while maintaining the morphology of layered structure.140,144,146,155,165,199211 Annealing under ambient conditions (Ar, vacuum, CO2, and air), reaction temperature and reaction time plays a crucial role in controlling the degree of oxidation.107,212 After annealing at 1200 C under Ar atmosphere, the OH and F groups on the surface of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets can be eliminated without damaging the layered structure and the hexagonal crystal structure of Ti3C2Tx at the stable phase.146

Taheri et al. synthetically investigated the effect of vacuum annealing temperature on the de-intercalation and surface de-functionalization of Ti3C2Tx MXenes using in situ electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) and ex-situ thermogravimetric analysis with mass spectroscopy (TGA-MS).155 They demonstrated that the de-functionalization of OH and F termination species at high temperatures (300 to 775 C) could increase MXene conductivity. Han and his co-workers prepared localized sandwich structures of Ti3C2Tx MXenes decorated with TiO2 nanoparticles and amorphous carbon by the simple annealing treatment (Fig. 3a).195 The bilateral Ti layers were oxidized by surface oxygen groups, and amorphous carbon was formed due to the outward migration of the inner Ti layer. The localized sandwich structure retained the original layered morphology.

Recently, some groups proposed alkaline oxidative reagents (NaOH and KOH) for the in situ synthesis of Ti3C2-based nanocomposites at RT.166,213,214 Yang and his co-workers prepared novel sandwich-like Na0.23TiO2/TiO2 composites through a facile, mild and scalable strategy.213 Interwoven amorphous Na0.23TiO2 nanobelts in situ grew on layered Ti3C2 nanosheets though the decomposition of Ti3C2 layers, which were oxidized by dissolved O2 in the NaOH solution. The content of Na0.23TiO2 nanobelts and Ti3C2 nanosheets can be simply altered by controlling the reaction time. However, hydrothermal method was also used to fabricate TiO2/Ti3C2Tx nanocomposites by oxidizing the MXene surface.215224 Other research studies indicated MXenes can be also oxidized by Ar sputtering and plasma treatment for the modification of terminal groups.225,226 This could quantify the distribution of certain functional groups on the surface of Ti3C2Tx MXenes, which is a significant key to design Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites for improving optoelectronic properties and lead to the realization of semiconducting, magnetic, and topologically insulating MXenes for potential applications.

2.3.2. Ion-exchange reactions. The ion-exchange in Ti3C2Tx MXenes is evidenced as an effective method to tune the surface functional groups for distinctive structural, optical and electronic properties. As mentioned above, 2D MXene materials generally have rich surfaces with varied terminations (OH, F, and O) after exfoliation. The cation substitution of OH groups can provide some ion-exchange sites to enhance the optical, electrochemical and other physiochemical properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes. For example, Peng and his co-workers demonstrated functional 2D-MXene materials with alkalizationintercalation treatment, that exhibited excellent lead (Pb) uptake performance.227 The adsorption behavior of Pb(II) is related to the ion-exchange reaction on the cation substitution of OH groups in activated Ti sites. Inspired by the Pb(II) uptake behavior of alkalized Ti3C2Tx, the Sn4+ ions in the polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)/SnCl4 solution were anchored on the surface and entered the interlamination of Ti3C2, constituting PVPSn(IV)@Ti3C2 nanocomposites through ion-exchange and electrostatic interaction (Fig. 3b).141 Subsequently, CTAB was used to intercalate the Ti3C2 interlayers and replaced the H+ in [TiO]H+ to form [TiO]CTA+, providing some ion-exchange sites on the cation substitution of OH groups.143 Then Sn4+ was intercalated into the CTAB@Ti3C2 matrix by ion-exchange interaction with the CTA+ group, forming CTABSn(IV)@Ti3C2 nanocomposites. With the aid of the OH groups, the Ti3C2Tx surface was functionalized by organofunctional siloxanes with NH2, COOR, C6H6, and C12H26 groups.228 Additive Al3+ ions were added during the etching process of MAX phases, which could be converted into Al oxoanion functional groups Al(OH)4 in the presence of excessive TMAOH.229

A similar cation substitution of F groups is observed for Ti3C2Tx when treated with KOH, which is explained by a replacement reaction between KOH and F termination.140,156,230 Another effective ion-exchange method based on the etching of MAX phases in Lewis acidic melts was implemented by the substitution/elimination on the surface terminations of Ti3C2Cl2 and Ti3C2Br2.128 Due to the labile surface bonding of Cl- and Br-terminated MXenes, Ti3C2Tx with O, NH, S, Cl, Se, Br, and Te surface terminations, as well as bare MXenes (no surface termination) were obtained.

2.3.3. Ti3C2Tx MXene-based hybrid nanocomposites. In order to expand the photoelectronic, electrochemical and other physicochemical performances of Ti3C2Tx MXenes, many research studies were focused on Ti3C2Tx MXene-based hybrid nanocomposites,231 which included carbon-based nanomaterials such as carbon nanotubes,232235 carbon nanoplating,236 nano-carbon sphere,237 graphene, reduced graphene oxide;238,239 simple substance such as Si,93 Ni,240 Pt,241,242 Ag, Rh, Au, Pd,243 Cu,220 Co,96 and Ru;244 compounds such as TiO2,200 C3N4,177,245,246 Cu2O,174 Co3O4,247 BP,248 Fe2O3,249 Fe3O4,250 Bi2WO6,251 MoS2 and CdS;223 and polymers such as poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA) and polyvinyl alcohol (PVA),252 PPy,139,159 polyaniline (PANI),250 polystyrene (PS),253 epoxy,254 natural rubber,255 polyacrylamide (PAAm),256 sodium alginate (SA),257 poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythio-phene)poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS),258 and poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO).259 These nanocomposites have been extensively applied in photocatalysis, electrochemical energy conversion and storage, electrocatalysis, electromagnetic interference shielding, photoelectrochemical devices and so on. In this section, we mainly discuss the synthesis of the related nanocomposites with photoelectronic properties.

Recently, Yin and his co-workers prepared a brand new Janus-structured Co-Ti3C2 QD Schottky catalyst through a facile thermal-anchoring hydrothermal method (Fig. 3c).96 Co nanoparticles can be obtained via the reduction of Co(NO3)26H2O under the effect of sodium citrate. As the thermal-anchoring reaction temperature of the initial precursor cobalt increases from 45 to 65 and 85 C, the amount of Co nanoparticles loaded on the surface of Ti3C2Tx MXenes can be efficiently improved. Zhang et al. synthesized the Ti3C2/Cu2O nanocomposite via a simple oil bath heating reaction.260 Cu2O nanoparticles were hybridized with Ti3C2 by the reaction of Cu(CH3COO)2H2O and Ti3C2 in C6H12O6 solution at 90 C for 6 h. The large surface area and rich surface termination of Ti3C2 nanostructures are beneficial to the growth of Cu2O. Subsequently, TiO2/Ti3C2Tx/Cu2O composites were prepared using the hydrothermal reaction between TiO2/Ti3C2Tx and the precursor of Cu2O.261 TiO2/Ti3C2Tx was synthesized in an oven at 120 C for 16 h, and then reacted with Cu(CH3COO)2H2O and C6H12O6 as the reducing agents. The Cu(CH3COO)2H2O/C6H12O6 ratio could tune the photocurrent signal of the TiO2/Ti3C2Tx/Cu2O heterojunction.

In the work by Gao and his co-workers, Ti3C2Tx MXenes were cut into nanoparticles and integrated on the surface of CdS via a hydrothermal strategy.73 It is worth to note that the surface F on Ti3C2Tx nanoparticles can be replaced by O and OH groups under hydrothermal conditions. Meanwhile, nucleation of CdS nanoparticles on Ti3C2Tx nanoparticles occurred. The heterogeneous nucleation can be ascribed to the adsorption of Cd2+ cations on these oxygen-containing groups. Further growth and aggregation of CdS nanoparticles led to the cauliflower-structured CdS@Ti3C2 sub-microspheres, on which the Ti3C2Tx nanoparticles were intimately coupled. Then, various heterojunctions including Cu/TiO2@Ti3C2Tx,220 Ti3C2/MoS2/TiO2 nanosheets,222 Ti3C2(TiO2)@CdS/MoS2,223 Ceria/Ti3C2,262 CdS nanorod/Ti3C2,263 Zn2In2S5/Ti3C2,264 Ag3PO4/Ti3C2,265 CsPbBr3/Ti3C2,266 C3N4/Ti3C2Tx,267 and Ti3C2/Bi2WO6,251,268,269 were investigated for photocatalytic application.

2.3.4. Self-assembled hierarchical structures. The above mentioned surface modification methods are all based on chemical reaction approaches, and the size, morphology, and distribution of the nanocomposites are difficult to be controlled. However, Ti3C2TxMXenes would be oxidized by oxygen dissolved in aqueous solutions, even along with the damage of Ti3C2TxMXene skeleton during the high temperature reaction. Relying on the electrostatic interactions, the hydrogen bond and van der Waals interactions of Ti3C2Tx MXenes with the abundant surface functional terminals, a self-assembly method with mild preparation conditions and simple process can be utilized to obtain Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites.

Recently, Chen et al. used an electrostatic self-assembly method to construct the hierarchical Ti3C2Tx QDs/Cu2O nanowires (NWs)/Cu heterostructures, as illustrated in Fig. 3d.174 Firstly, Ti3C2Tx QDs were synthesized through hydrothermally cutting the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets and passivated by PEI, and the surface charge of the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets was transformed from negative to positive. Then, a Cu2O NWs/Cu mesh was fabricated through calcination at 550 C in a N2 atmosphere, and further treated with poly(sodium 4-styrenesulfonate) (PSS) to obtain negatively charged surface on Cu2O NWs. Finally, Ti3C2Tx QDs modified with positively charged PEI are self-assembled onto the Cu2O NWs through electrostatic interaction, forming the hierarchical Ti3C2Tx QDs/Cu2O NWs/Cu heterostructure. The PSS and PEI on the Ti3C2Tx QDs/Cu2O NWs/Cu heterostructure are completely eliminated via the calcination treatment in an inert atmosphere. Yin et al. proposed a facile solution-phase co-feeding and electrostatic attraction self-assembly approach based on negatively charged Ti3C2Tx nanosheets and N-rich porous carbon nanosheets modified with positively charged PDDA.235 The PDDA/Ti3C2Tx nanocomposites, with a porous interconnected conductive network structure and high specific surface area, can ensure Ti3C2Tx nanosheets and the NPCNs in intimate contact, which could combine the advantages of both components and promote more accessible active sites. Similarly, Yu and his co-workers prepared MXene@polystyrene nanocomposites based on the negative MXene nanosheets and the positive PS microspheres via the electrostatic assembly approach.253 Wang and his co-workers fabricated molecular-level Ti3C2/NiCoAlLDH heterostructures by a similar method through the assembly of NiCoAlLDH and Ti3C2Tx MXenes.270 The difference is that the electrostatic attraction of the Ti3C2/NiCoAlLDH heterostructure is owing to the assembly of negatively charged Ti3C2Tx and positively charged NiCoAlLDH nanosheets.

Hydrogen bond and van der Waals interactions also could contribute to the self-assembly process.271 Recently, Guo et al. reported an iron phthalocyanine (FePc)/Ti3C2Tx hybrid based on FePc and Ti3C2Tx MXenes in DMF solution by the self-assembly approach.272 The self-assembly process was driven by the strong interactions between FePc and MXene through intermolecular van der Waals forces or hydrogen bonding. Xu et al. prepared transition metal oxide (TMO)/MXene heterostructures via the one-step assembly method with van der Waals interactions.273 The self-assembly process occurs in the tetrahydrofuran (THF) solution, where the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets have a huge surface energy and TMO nanostructures (TiO2 nanorods and SnO2 NWs), in the presence of an organic (oleylamine/oleic acid) layer on the surfaces, have a relatively low surface energy. For minimizing the surface energy of the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets, the TMO nanostructures could be simply and efficiently assembled on the surfaces of the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets through van der Waals interactions. Yan et al. presented transition metal layered double hydroxide (TMLDH) decorated on the surface of MXene nanosheets by a facile self-assemble process.274 The self-assembly process can be attributed to the fabrication of OH-F hydrogen bonds between the OH groups on the surface of TMLDHs and the F groups on HF-etched Ti3C2Tx MXene nanosheets.

Recently, capillary-forced assembling strategy was adopted to synthesize hierarchical 3D Ti3C2Tx architectures with excellent electronic and mechanical properties.275277 A similar method was used to prepare Ti3C2-based nonocomposites. Quan et al. obtained the g-C3N4/Ti3C2Tx nanocomposite by an evaporation-induced self-assembly method.278 The g-C3N4 nanosheets and Ti3C2Tx nanosheets were mixed and stirred in a 60 C water bath for 8 h until the solvent evaporated completely. Our group reported the TiO2 inverse opal photonic crystals (IOPCs)/Ti3C2Tx QD heterojunction prepared via the evaporation-induced self-assembly method.97 These exciting works open a new horizon in the nano-engineering of metal/chalcogenide and metal/ternary oxide semiconductor heterointerfaces for potential device applications.

Based on the discussions above, the synthetic methods of pure Ti3C2Tx MXenes and Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites have been investigated. Intuitively, the synthesis route adopted would influence the surface chemistry of Ti3C2Tx MXenes. In fact, different synthesis methods will affect their electronic structure, conductivity, WF and optical properties. The outstanding electrochemical and optoelectronic properties due to the morphology, composition, and stability of Ti3C2Tx MXenes make them attractive as supports for photoelectric devices, electromagnetic interference shielding, energy storage, catalysts, field effect transistors and biomedicine. Therefore, the most attractive characteristics of Ti3C2Tx, including their electronic, optoelectronic and stability properties as well as their mechanical properties, will be discussed in the following sections.

3. Properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes

3.1. Electronic band structures

DFT calculations were performed to determine the electronic band structures and the total and projected densities of states (DOS) of the Ti3C2 monolayer.279 As shown in Fig. 4a and e, the non-terminated Ti3C2 monolayer presents a metallic character with the continuous electronic states crossing the Fermi level in both the spin-up (red lines) and spin-down (blue lines) channels.101 The DOS of Ti3C2 near the Fermi surface is mainly composed of the outer layer Ti 3d electrons, which could be modified by surface functionalization.53,106,280 Therefore, various surface terminations that bond with the outer layer Ti atoms, could significantly modify the electronic band structures through electron transfer from the transition metal to electronegative surface terminations.281 Due to the acceptance of a single electron, the identical oxidation states of F and OH groups present similar influence on the electronic structures of Ti3C2Tx MXenes. However, O groups display different behaviors because they accept two electrons in the equilibrium state.61,72,282 With hydroxylation of the 3d orbitals of the Ti sites and the p orbitals of X atoms,63 the electronic structures of Ti3C2(OH)2 and Ti3C2F2 present a semiconducting state with observable narrow band gaps of around 0.05 and 0.04 eV, respectively, as shown in Fig. 4b, c and f, g,101 while the band structure of the Ti3C2O2 monolayer displays metallic behavior (Fig. 4d and h),283 which has been demonstrated by previous calculations and experiments.53,73,142,284286 Chen and his co-workers calculated the total and projected DOS of O-terminated Ti3C2 QDs by DFT simulation, as shown in Fig. 4i.174 It can be seen that numerous electronic states cross the Fermi level, indicating that the O-terminated Ti3C2 QDs possess excellent conductivity. Li et al. demonstrated that both pristine Ti3C2O2 QDs and N-doped Ti3C2O2 QDs presented metallic behavior through DOS calculations.173

WF was also obtained from the first-principles calculations based on DFT, which is critical for photoelectronic device applications. WF is defined as the energy difference between the Fermi energy and the vacuum potential, highly dependent on their surface functional groups (Fig. 4j). It can be observed that the WFs of bare, F, OH, and O terminated Ti3C2Tx MXenes are distributed in the ranges of 3.34.8, 3.15.8, 1.62.8, and 3.36.7 eV, respectively, which are independent of the thickness (n).89 Functionalized Ti3C2Tx MXenes with O and F surface terminations induce the Fermi level to shift lower, resulting in increasing the WFs. However, OH-terminated Ti3C2Tx MXenes have a lower WF than bare Ti3C2Tx MXenes. This suggests that the WFs of F- or O-terminated MXenes can be tuned by the following factors: the induced dipole moments by charge transfers between F/O and the substrate, and the changes in the total surface dipole moments caused by surface relaxation upon functionalization. Besides these two factors, the intrinsic dipole moment of the OH groups in the OH-terminated Ti3C2Tx MXenes plays the most important role in determining the total dipole moment, which consequently justifies the ultralow WF.

Recently, Koch and co-workers reported that different surface terminations affected the WF and electronic properties of Ti3C2Tx by different annealing temperature treatments.88,287 Through comparing the experimental and the calculated WF, they found that the interactions between the different surface moieties and their local dipoles were crucial. The WF of Ti3C2Tx increased from 3.9 to 4.8 eV after heating at 380 C in vacuum, mainly owing to the desorption of water, carbon-dominated contaminations, and OH species. With desorption of F at higher temperatures from 500 to 750 C, the WF of Ti3C2Tx decreases again to 4.1 eV. Considering the surface dipoles together with the weak Fermi level pinning effect,55 Ti3C2Tx MXenes can be used to construct Schottky-barrier-free contacts with semiconductors or metals through van der Waals junctions, in which the well-matched WFs between the Ti3C2Tx MXenes and semiconductors or metals will lead to a suitable barrier height for photoelectric current generation and transport.288 Thus, these pave a way for potential applications in field emission,289,290 catalysis,291 and photoelectric devices.87

However, compared to multilayered Ti3C2Tx nanosheets, Ti3C2Tx QDs exhibit excellent optical properties, attributed to their quantum confinement effects and surface defects.112,174,185,188 Moreover, Ti3C2Tx QDs have numerous hydrophilic functional groups, large specific surface area and excellent electrical conductivities, endowing them with tunable properties, by changing their composition and terminal functional groups.96,172175 Therefore, Ti3C2Tx QDs could be extensively applied in optoelectronics, catalysis, biomedicine, and energy areas. Generally, HF-MXenes mainly contain F ends, while those etched by LiF/HCl contain more O terminations. A significant improvement in conductivity was achieved when a mixture of LiF/HCl was used as an etchant. Moreover, heat treatment of MXenes in an inert environment could also lead to a significant increase in conductivity due to the removal of intercalated molecules and surface terminations.

The degradation of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets in water solution usually starts at the edges with a single-exponential decay model.154,196 Barsoum et al. proposed a simple method to suppress the oxidation of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets in aqueous colloidal solutions via capping the edges of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets with low-cost and green polyanions (polyphosphates, polyborates, and polysilicates).319 Due to the negatively charged surface and positively charged edges of the Ti3C2Tx nanosheets,322 the negatively charged polyanions are selectively decorated on the edges of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets. Recently, aqueous Ti3C2Tx MXene flakes were stored at low temperature to substantially reduce the oxidation degree.316,317 Surprisingly, even after 650 days of freeze storage, the Ti3C2Tx MXene flakes could also retain the initial flake morphology and physical properties.

The effect of ambient atmosphere on the electrical and optical properties of Ti3C2Tx films was also studied, as illustrated in the sheet conductance of three films with different transmittance values in the sequence of wetting and drying (Fig. 5g).58 The colors blue, red, purple, orange, green, and yellow represent the following storage conditions, respectively: as prepared, dry N2 overnight, continuous 2 days with H2O-saturated N2, dry N2 for 1 day, open atmosphere for 2 days, and finally dry N2 for 1 day. The change in sheet conductance through dehydrating/hydrating Ti3C2Tx films is reversible. The short-term effect of O2 was compared in the open atmosphere for 2 days (green) and under dry N2 for 1 day (yellow). Similar to wet N2, hydrating films in the open atmosphere decrease sheet conductance. Then the subsequent dehydration using dry N2 increases sheet conductance. Interestingly, the transmittance of Ti3C2Tx films shows no change after storage under atmosphere for 30 days. Thus, the air stability, which induces the change in the electrical conductivity and concomitant optical properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes, should be given extensive attentions for practical optoelectronic applications.

Thermal stability is another vital index of Ti3C2Tx that depends on their chemical composition and the environment. For example, Ti3C2Tx (F and OH groups) nanosheets are stable at 200800 C in Ar atmosphere, but existence of some F or OH groups and small amount of formed TiO2and AlF3is attached on the surface of Ti3C2Txnanosheets after heated to 500 C.146 Furthermore, the hexagonal crystal structure of Ti3C2Tx can be preserved even at 1200 C and the defects were eliminated. But there are some non-layered TiC particles. The Ti3C2Tx nanosheets with in situ vacuum annealing were investigated using the rotationally averaged (RA)-selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern.155 After final annealing at 775 C, the Ti3C2Tx samples largely retained their structure with small amounts of anatase. At vacuum annealing temperatures above 800 C, a phase transformation would occur and cubic TiC and TiO2 were formed with CO/CO2 gas evolution.160,211 On the contrary, while Ti3C2Tx were annealed in oxygen atmosphere, they could be oxidized into anatase TiO2 nanocrystals on Ti3C2Tx layers at 200 C, and then completely converted to rutile TiO2 at 1000 C.149 By controlling the heating rate, annealing temperature and exposure time, the Ti3C2Tx MXenes could be transformed into carbon sheet-supported TiO2 with different crystal structures, particle sizes and morphologies, which have been observed in the in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images.212 Therefore, these attractive properties could be a viable support or template for preparing functional Ti3C2Tx MXenes and Ti3C2Tx MXene-based nanocomposites.

3.4. Mechanical properties

Apart from the excellent optical, electrical and thermal properties, the Ti3C2Tx films also exhibit outstanding mechanical properties for potential applications in flexible photovoltaic, optoelectronic and transparent conductive electrode devices. DFT and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were carried out to investigate the mechanical properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes.78,279,301,323325Fig. 5h shows the calculated strainstress curves. Young's modulus can be calculated from the initial regions (1%) of the strainstress curves by a linear fit, about 502 GPa, which is insensitive to the strain rate in the range of 0.00020.001 ps1.301 Subsequently, Sinitskii and his co-workers measured the Young's modulus of Ti3C2Tx monolayers and bilayers through nanoindentation with the tip of an atomic force microscope (AFM).83 The effective Young's modulus of monolayer Ti3C2Tx was determined to be 0.33 TPa, which is higher than those of other solution-processed 2D materials.

Similar to other properties, the mechanical properties of Ti3C2Tx also strongly depend on the surface terminated groups, due to the presence of van der Waals and hydrogen bonds between the sheets.78,279 The gap distance between two OH terminated Ti3C2Tx layers is only 0.57 , which is much narrower than that that of O and F terminated Ti3C2Tx (2.5 ), resulting in a higher normal-to-the-plane elastic modulus for Ti3C2Tx decorated with OH group.78 According to the contact resonance AFM (CR-AFM) techniques, the corresponding elastic moduli of flat Ti3C2Tx flakes in air and water are found to range from 12 to 75 GPa and from 7 to 70 GPa, respectively. More importantly, the mechanical properties of Ti3C2Tx films could be enhanced though combining with polymeric materials,193 such as PVA,252,326 polyethylene,327 chitosan,328 PEI and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS).329 However, some research studies reveal that the Ti3C2Tx films also exhibit good mechanical properties when coated on flexible substrates.75,299 As shown in Fig. 5i, the resistance of Ti3C2Tx films coated on flexible substrates changes with the bending radius in the range of 13.5 to 3.8 mm.299 It can be seen that the films exhibit excellent electromechanical stability as the bending radius is up to 8 mm, and then Rs increases about 15% at a minimal radius of 3.8 mm during the first bending cycle. Subsequently, the resilience of the films is measured after 10 bending cycles to be r = 3.8 mm, as shown in the inset of Fig. 5i. Rs exhibits a variation of 14% during the first cycle and fluctuates around 25% during subsequent cycles. 4. Optoelectronic applications of Ti3C2Tx MXenes

Based on their outstanding structural, electronic, optical, thermal and mechanical properties, Ti3C2Tx nanostructures have great potential as a new class of conducting materials in many applications including photoelectric devices, supercapacitors, catalysis, biomedicine, electromagnetic interference shielding and so on. For many applications that have already been predicted or verified, the tunable chemistry and controllable structure of Ti3C2Tx should be optimized to highlight the desired electrical, optical, and other physicochemical properties. Here, we will give a brief summary on the preliminary development and achievements of optoelectronic device applications of Ti3C2Tx nanostructures, mainly focusing on the photovoltaic, photodetection, and photoelectrochemical devices.

According to previous discussions, excellent electrodes based on Ti3C2Tx nanosheets should have a high transmittance and low Rs. Thus, the authors balanced the transparency and conductivity of Ti3C2Tx thin films by tuning the concentration of Ti3C2Tx colloidal solution. Fig. 13c shows the JSC and VOC of Ti3C2Tx/n-Si device with different concentrations of Ti3C2Tx colloidal solution. It can be observed that as the concentration of Ti3C2Tx colloidal solution increases, the VOC only increases a little, while the JSC of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si device exhibits a gradual increase as the concentration increases from 0.2 to 2.0 mg mL1. Over a critical concentration value of 2.0 mg mL1, the JSC decreases. This result is mainly attributed to the following two reasons. On the one hand, the Ti3C2Tx thin films have a good transmittance, which allows more photons to pass through it and generates more carriers in n-Si. On the other hand, the Ti3C2Tx thin films have high series resistance consisting of the contact resistance with n-Si and Rs, and form a weak built-in electric field with n-Si. Thus the photon-generated pairs of electrons and holes could not be separated, generating a negative effect on VOC and JSC of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si device.

In order to investigate the carrier separation and transport processes, the energy band diagram of Ti3C2Tx/n-Si Schottky junction is shown in Fig. 13d. The WFs of n-Si and Ti3C2Tx are calculated by the UPS to be 3.71 and 4.35 eV, respectively. Thus, the EF difference between n-Si and Ti3C2Tx is calculated to be 0.64 eV, which leads to the band bending of n-Si and generates a built-in potential between n-Si and Ti3C2Tx with a field pointing from n-Si to Ti3C2Tx. The Schottky barrier height (B) between n-Si and Ti3C2Tx is 0.74 eV. While the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si heterostructure is illuminated, the photon- generated carriers in or near the space-charge region could be separated by a strong built-in electric field. The holes could transport to Ti3C2Tx, and the electrons transport to the metal back electrode through n-Si, generating electrical signals in the external circuit.

Subsequently, the authors investigated the effect of temperature on the electronic properties of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si Schottky junction, as shown in Fig. 13e, and the ideal factor of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si van der Waals heterostructure device was studied. The ideal factor is determined by the IV curves of the Schottky junction diode, which is a vital parameter to evaluate the performance of Ti3C2Tx/n-Si Schottky junction. The closer the ideal factor is to 1, the better the Schottky junction performance of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si device. By comparing the IV characteristics of Ti3C2Tx/n-Si Schottky junction before and after vacuum annealing at 200 C, the ideal factors and B of the Ti3C2Tx/n-Si device could be calculated to be 3.10 and 0.48 eV, and 2.53 and 0.53 eV, respectively. It can be observed that after annealing, the current and ideal factor both decrease, which is attributed to the elimination of the interface defect between the n-Si and Ti3C2Tx films. However, annealing Ti3C2Tx in Ar could remove the OH and F groups from the Ti3C2Tx surface, which could increase the WF of Ti3C2Tx.287 Thus, the B of Ti3C2Tx/n-Si after annealing treatment is higher than that without annealing, which suggests that B between n-Si and Ti3C2Tx could be modulated by adjusting the surface functional groups of Ti3C2Tx.

This work shows that Ti3C2Tx MXenes, as a novel transparent and conductive material with wide range adjustable WF, could form a good Schottky junction with Si by van der Waals force. 4.3. Photoelectrochemical

Ti3C2Tx nanosheets not only have excellent optical and electronic properties, but also possess large surface area and rich hydrophilic functional groups (OH, F and O) on their surface, enabling them to easily and strongly connect with a variety of semiconductors as well as photoactivator supporters. In addition, Ti3C2Tx nanosheets have much stronger redox properties than other 2D carbon materials, due to the exposed terminal Ti atoms. Thus, MXenes will be promising materials to be employed in PEC devices.353,354 In 2019, Zhang and co-workers proposed a PEC non-enzymatic sensor based on Ti3C2Tx/Cu2O heterostructure for the detection of glucose, in which the Cu2O acts as a photoactive material and Ti3C2Tx as the supporting material.260 A schematic illustration regarding the principles of the glucose PEC sensing is shown in Fig. 14a. Under visible light irradiation, the electrons are excited from the VB of Cu2O to the conduct band (CB) and the holes are retained in the VB. Owing to the low WF of Ti3C2Tx, the holes could transfer from the VB of the p-type Cu2O to Ti3C2Tx and the electrons are accepted by the dissolved O2. Then after adding glucose into the electrolyte, the partial reduced species (O2, H2O2) of O2 are consumed by glucose, which causes the inhibition of electron transfer and the decrease in photocurrent.

The UV-visible absorption spectra of multilayered Ti3C2Tx, Cu2O and Ti3C2Tx/Cu2O nanocomposite are displayed in Fig. 14b. The Ti3C2Tx shows a broadband absorption from 200 to 800 nm without an obvious absorption edge. Cu2O has an absorption edge at 650 nm, exhibiting a strong visible light absorption capacity. The light absorption capacity of Ti3C2Tx/Cu2O nanocomposite is stronger than that of pristine Cu2O. As shown in Fig. 14c, with the increase of glucose concentration from 0.5 nM to 0.5 mM, the photocurrent gradually decreases. The relationship between photocurrent and the logarithm value of glucose concentration is linear. The detection limit is about 0.17 nM. This research demonstrates that the Ti3C2Tx/semiconductor based heterostructure can be successfully applied to the PEC non-enzymatic sensor and has enormous potential in the PEC fields.

In 2019, our group prepared TiO2 IOPCs/Ti3C2 QD composite films by the self-assembly method for the PEC sensing of glutathione (GSH), as shown in Fig. 14df.97 The PEC process with the TiO2/Ti3C2 electrode based PEC cells could be proposed as schematically shown in Fig. 14d. It can be observed that the incorporation of Ti3C2 and TiO2 results in a uniquely matched Type-II heterojunction. For TiO2 IOPCs/Ti3C2-based devices under light illumination, the heterojunction enables the entire collection of cold electrons from Ti3C2 interband transitions and the hot electrons for Ti3C2 intraband interactions. The photogenerated electrons from Ti3C2 can be directly injected into the CB of TiO2 IOPCs, with the photogenerated holes oppositely injected into the CB of Ti3C2, leading to an enhanced carrier separation performance for PEC detection.

Fig. 14e displays the photocurrent responses of TiO2/Ti3C2/Nafion electrode with the concentration of GSH under a Xenon light excitation (150 W). The photocurrent of TiO2/Ti3C2/Nafion gradually increased as the GSH concentration increased from 0.1 to 1000 M. The inset of Fig. 14e displays the linear calibration curves, which have a good linearity with a detection limit of 9.0 nM. We also investigated the selectivity of the PEC sensor for GSH. As shown in Fig. 14f, after adding GSH (100 M), the photocurrent increased. While the ascorbic acid (AA), glucose (GLU), uric acid (UA), and Fe3+ (100 M) were added, the photocurrent changed a little. This implies that the PEC device has an excellent selectivity for GSH detection, and also indicates a good anti-interference capacity of the TiO2/Ti3C2/Nafion electrode.

To investigate the photoelectronic properties of Janus-structured Co-MQD, the UV-visible absorption spectra were obtained and are shown in Fig. 15c. It can be seen that Ti3C2 MQD has quite a strong absorption intensity within 200600 nm, which is attributed to the quantum confinement effect. After modifying the MQD with Co nanoparticle, the absorption intensity increases with the Co/Ti molar ratio in the visible light region. Notably, there is an obvious additional absorption edge in 380520 nm, which results from the surface plasmon effect of Co nanoparticle. Note that the surface plasmon effects could improve the visible light harvesting capability and generate more photo-generated excitons. Then, the PEC water oxidation performances of MQD and Co-MQD were investigated, as shown in Fig. 15d. From the linear sweep voltammogram (LSV) curves of MQD and Co-MQD, the Co-MQD-48 photoanode was found to have the highest photocurrent density of 2.99 mA cm2 at 1.23 V (versus RHE), which is almost two times larger than that of pristine MQD (1.54 mA cm2). This drastically enhanced PEC performance might be attributed to the following three factors. The first is that the surface plasmon effect of Co nanoparticle enhances the light harvesting capacity. Second, the carrier transport between the MQD and Co nanoparticle could be efficiently improved. Third, the exposed Co sites could lead to much stronger redox reactivity and strongly boost the surface water oxidation kinetics. While, for the Co-MQD-65 photoanode, the photocurrent density sharply reduces, which is the reason that the excessive Co terminals lead to severe quantum efficiency loss.

In Fig. 15e, the incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) of MQD and Co-MQD is measured to evaluate the light harvesting efficiency and electron injection efficiency for PEC water oxidation. For the pristine MQD sample, the optimal IPCE intensity could be up to 45% at 225 nm, and rapidly decreases to almost zero around 600 nm, which is related to light harvesting efficiency. After modifying MQD with Co nanoparticles, the IPCE intensity significantly increases. In contrast, the Co-MQD-48 photoanode has the largest IPCE intensity of about 90%. Moreover, it can be obviously seen that after modifying the Co terminals, an additional IPCE peak was found in the wavelength range of 380520 nm, assigned to the surface plasmon effect of Co nanoparticles. Moreover, the IPCE intensity of Co-MQD-x increases in the visible light range. Thus, the coupling of the MQD and Co nanoparticles could efficiently promote the PEC water oxidation performance of Janus-structured Co-MQD. Then, they investigated the carrier transport in the Co-MQD Schottky junction through quantitatively calculating the charge separation (chargeseparation) and charge injection (chargeinjection) efficiency of pristine MQD and Co-MQD-x, as shown in Fig. 15f and g. The Co-MQD-48 sample has the largest chargeseparation efficiency of about 95.6% (1.23 V versus RHE), which is almost 146% higher than that of pristine MQD. Co-MQD-48 sample also achieves the highest chargeinjection efficiency of about 91.9% (1.23 V versus RHE), which is almost 162% larger than that of pristine MQD. The charge migration efficiency, determined to be chargeseparation chargeinjection, is up to 87.56%, which has increased by 236% comparing with that of pristine MQD.

To verify the PEC water oxidation kinetics, the reaction schematic illustration of Janus-structured Co-MQD-48 Schottky catalyst is displayed in Fig. 15h. It can be observed that the enhancement of PEC water oxidation performance is mainly due to the surface plasmon effect introduced by the Co nanoparticle. However, owing to the energy band bending effect induced by the unique Schottky junction at the Co nanoparticle and MQD interfaces, the photo-generated carriers could be effectively separated. The photoinduced electrons transform to the MQD, and the trapped holes from MQD could be rapidly consumed by the modified Co terminal to oxidize OH from water into oxygen. Obviously, this process would boost the electrode/electrolyte interfacial charge injection and further improve the carrier separation within the Co-MQD structures. Therefore, the Janus-structured Co-MQD Schottky junction could be efficiently applied to the PEC water oxidation. Based on the discussions and analysis above, the application of Ti3C2Tx MXenes in the PEC devices is mainly due to their adjustable WF, high conductivity, strong redox properties, large surface specific area and abundant functional groups. Firstly, the reaction mechanism of PEC device should be expounded with the theoretical prediction and experiments. Secondly, it is necessary to develop simple, low cost and efficient synthetic methods to prepare Ti3C2Tx MXenes with controllable functional groups. Finally, optimizing the structure and enhancing the light absorption ability could further improve the performance of PEC devices.

5. Conclusions and perspectives

This review summarizes the recent progress of photoelectric applications based on Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures from the following aspects: (1) the various synthesis strategies for Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures, which contain three major synthesis routes, including HF and HCl/LiF etching, alkali etching, and Lewis acidic melts. (2) The surface modification of MXene-based nanostructures, including controlling oxidation, ion-exchange reactions, MXene-based hybrid nanocomposites and self-assembled hierarchical structures, has been discussed to obtain functionalized MXene nanostructures and MXene-based hybrid nanocomposites. (3) Theoretical studies and experimental research are discussed to explore the effects of surface modification on electronic, optical and mechanical properties. (4) The development and achievements of potential photoelectric device applications based on Ti3C2Tx nanostructures have been summarized and discussed in three categories, including photovoltaics, photodetection, and photoelectrochemical devices.

The Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures possess excellent and attractive electronic, optical and mechanical properties for various photoelectric devices applications with high performances. However, some challenges exist that have to be faced and addressed for future study. First, the instability of aqueous colloidal MXene solutions is still one of the main drawbacks, which restricts their further practical applications. Aqueous colloidal solutions of MXenes decay fast without any protective measures, due to the attack from dissolved oxygen and water molecules on the defects/edges of the nanostructures. Therefore, there is an urgently need to extend the shelf-life of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures and endow them with high stability. Second, the large-scale fabrication of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures via a simple method still remains to be a great challenge. Currently, the Ti3C2Tx MXenes are mainly prepared through top-down strategies, in which controlling their surface terminations is difficult. Thus, synthesizing Ti3C2Tx MXenes with a particular surface termination is highly needed but challenging for experimentalists. In particular, bare MXenes with no surface termination is difficult to obtain, despite DFT predictions. Hence, it is desirable to search for more high-yielding, low-cost, environmentally friendly approaches that can be explored to prepare MXenes with controllable morphology, structures and surface terminations. Third, apart from the excellent electrical properties, light absorption based on the metallic thin layered Ti3C2Tx MXenes is limited, which greatly impedes the quantum efficiency of the potential photoelectric application. Therefore, strong optical response behavior of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures is quite essential. Ti3C2 QDs possess attractive properties of luminescence and would be promising candidates for photoanode materials for high-performance photoelectric devices. However, the synthesis of Ti3C2 QDs is quite challenging, due to the broad size distributions, uncontrollable morphology, and less reproducibility. Thus, searching for efficient synthesis methods of Ti3C2 QDs is highly desirable. Fourth, from the perspective of optimizing Ti3C2Tx MXene based photoelectric properties, combining the Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures with other semiconductors or metals to form Ohmic contacts or Schottky junctions with tuneable Schottky barrier heights is beneficial to improving light adsorption and significantly increasing the charge transfer efficiency. Moreover, the combination of Ti3C2Tx MXene nanostructures with other types of functional materials to form hybrid materials will combine the advantages of both materials and make it possible for multifunctional photoelectric applications.

We expect that this comprehensive review will serve as a guide to those exploring the electronic, optical and mechanical properties of Ti3C2Tx MXenes for photovoltaic, photodetection, and photoelectrochemical applications. Overall, we predict that Ti3C2Tx MXene-based photoelectric application will continue its fast progress made in less than half a decade in the future with growing speed. Combining theoretical calculations and experimental research studies, further investigations should be devoted to increasing the fundamental understanding and widespread utilization of the Ti3C2Tx MXenes in practical photoelectric applications. Thus, we hope this review could provide a broad insight into the current challenges and future opportunities for optoelectronic applications, aiming to inspire a broader interest in the near future.
