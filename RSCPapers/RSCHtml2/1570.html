Abstract

Thiopheneâ€“pyrazineâ€“thiophene building blocks with carboxamide or carbamate substituents were synthesized and their crystal structures were compared. The carboxamide substituted building block formed intermolecular hydrogen bonds. It showed a twisted conformation and a herringbone packing in a single crystal. The carbamate substituted building block formed intramolecular resonance-assisted hydrogen bonds (RAHBs) and the hydrogen bonds enabled a co-planar structure, an extended Ï€-conjugation, and a tight lamellar arrangement in a single crystal. Conjugated polymers based on these two building blocks were also synthesized. The hydrogen bonding caused significant differences in the optical, redox, and photovoltaic properties of the polymers. Solar cells based on the polymers as electron donors and Y6 as the acceptor were studied. The devices based on PC-BDT2 which had the intramolecular hydrogen bonds showed a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 7.9%, while the devices based on PI-BDT which possessed the intermolecular hydrogen bonds gave a PCE of 0.1%.

Introduction

Semiconducting polymers exhibit many advantages, such as flexibility, solution processability, low cost and light weight, over conventional inorganic semiconductors.1â€“3 They are promising materials for high-throughput manufacturing of emerging wearable and flexible electronic products. Significant effort has been made in the search for high-performance semiconducting polymers in the past decade.4â€“6 One of the effective approaches for designing high-performance semiconductor polymers is constructing a highly planar polymer backbone with extended Ï€-electron delocalization, thus reducing the energy band gap and facilitating inter-chain Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions. The early strategy used covalent bonds to connect adjacent aromatic rings for eliminating the rotation of single bonds between them.7 Recently, noncovalent intramolecular interactions were employed to increase the torsional barrier, thereby enhancing coplanarity.8â€“11 The binding energies of the noncovalent interactions such as Sâ‹¯X and Câ€“Hâ‹¯X (X = O, N, F) are usually small (in the range of 0.44â€“2.20 kcal molâˆ’1).7 However, their existence significantly improves the semiconducting properties of many conjugated polymers.11â€“13 Hydrogen bonding is an important noncovalent interaction that plays crucial roles in diverse areas, such as supramolecular chemistry, biochemical processes, and crystal engineering.14â€“19 H-bonds are usually classified as strong (>15 kcal molâˆ’1), moderately strong (4â€“15 kcal molâˆ’1), and weak (<4 kcal molâˆ’1) in terms of stabilization energy.20 Strong H-bonds are often resonance-assisted H-bonds, or Ï€-cooperative H-bonds, where a hydrogen donor and an acceptor are connected by a Ï€-conjugated system of alternating single and double bonds (Fig. 1).21,22

The interaction between a H-bond and a Ï€-conjugated system can significantly enhance the H-bond itself because of the extra stabilization associated with the delocalization of the Ï€-electrons through the entire conjugated system.23â€“25 Meanwhile, the quasi-ring formed by the hydrogen bridge can partly act as an aromatic ring to form dynamic ring-fused structures. This usually leads to a stable conformation in heterocyclic systems. Recently, the importance of including RAHBs and other intramolecular hydrogen bonds in design strategies for semiconducting polymers has been demonstrated.13,26,27 The intra-molecular H-bonds between carbamate hydrogens and nitrogen containing heteroaromatics can significantly improve the coplanarity and effective Ï€-conjugation in polymers. In order to further understand the influence of H-bonds on Ï€-conjugated polymers, we herein present new building blocks, namely thiopheneâ€“pyrazineâ€“thiophene with carboxamide or carbamate substituents. Both carboxamide and carbamate groups have protons that can form H-bonds with lone pair electrons of heteroatoms. The carboxamide substituted building block formed intermolecular H-bonds. It showed a twisted conformation and a classical herringbone packing in a single crystal. The carbamate substituted building block formed intramolecular RAHBs. It displayed a co-planar structure, an extended Ï€-conjugation and a tight lamellar structure in a single crystal. Polymers based on these two building blocks were also synthesized. The hydrogen bonding caused significant differences in the optical, redox, and photovoltaic properties of the polymers. Solar cells based on the polymers as electron donors and Y6 as the acceptor were studied. The devices based on PC-BDT2 which had intramolecular hydrogen bonds showed a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 7.9%, while the devices based on PI-BDT which possessed intermolecular hydrogen bonds gave a PCE of 0.1%.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

The syntheses of monomers and polymers are shown in Scheme 1. Ethyl 2-(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolan-2-yl)-thiophene-3-carboxylate (1) was prepared according to literature methods.28 2,2â€²-(Pyrazine-2,5-diyl)-bis(thiophene-3-carboxylate) (2) was synthesized by the Suzuki coupling reaction of 1 and 2,5-dibromopyrazine. Ester 2 was hydrolyzed to give 2,2â€²-(pyrazine-2,5-diyl)-bis(thiophene-3-carboxylic acid) (3). Carboxamide (4) was synthesized by an amidation reaction with 2-octyldodecan-1-amine.29 Carbamate (5) was synthesized by one-pot reactions of acyl azide formation and Curtius rearrangement in 2-octyldodecan-1-ol. 4 and 5 were brominated to give 6 and 7, respectively. Compounds 5a and 4a with the ethylhexyl side chain instead of the 2-octyldodecyl side chain were prepared in a similar manner for the single-crystal X-ray diffraction study. Two known BDT-type monomers were chosen to copolymerize with the new monomers. The polymers were synthesized by Stille cross-coupling reactions. The number-average molecular weights (Mn) of PI-BDT1, PC-BDT1 and PC-BDT2 were 45.5, 131.9 and 22.6 kDa, and the polydispersity indexes (PDIs) of PI-BDT1, PC-BDT1 and PC-BDT2 were 2.8, 2.2 and 2.6, respectively.

The decomposition temperatures (5% weight loss) of PI-BDT1, PC-BDT1 and PC-BDT2 were 316, 274 and 275 Â°C, respectively (Fig. S23â€ ). Conclusion

In summary, thiopheneâ€“pyrazineâ€“thiophene building blocks with carboxamide or carbamate substituents were synthesized and their crystal structures were compared. The carboxamide substituted building block formed intermolecular hydrogen bonds. It showed a twisted conformation and a herringbone packing in a single crystal. The carbamate substituted building block formed intramolecular resonance-assisted hydrogen bonds (RAHBs) and the hydrogen bonds enabled a co-planar structure, an extended Ï€-conjugation, and a tight lamellar arrangement in a single crystal. The Ï€-conjugated polymers based on the two building blocks were also synthesized. The solar cells based on the polymers as electron donors and Y6 as the acceptor were studied. The devices based on PC-BDT2 which had intramolecular hydrogen bonds showed a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 7.9%. In contrast, the devices based on PI-BDT which possessed intermolecular hydrogen bonds gave a PCE of 0.1%. Our investigations demonstrated that the RAHBs caused significant differences in the optical, redox, and photovoltaic properties of the polymers. The RAHBs are dynamic, directional, and relatively strong intramolecular interactions. The synergistic effect between hydrogen bonding and Ï€-conjugation can be used for designing semiconducting polymers with improved device performances.
