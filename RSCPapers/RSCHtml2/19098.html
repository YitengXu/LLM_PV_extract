Abstract

Solâ€“gel metal oxide films are an important type of functional materials for energy technologies and beyond owing to their versatile properties and ease of processing, but their applications are limited by the typically required high sintering temperatures. This study reports a novel phenomenon where the sintering temperatures of solâ€“gel metal oxide films were substantially lowered when a metal phase was embedded within the solâ€“gel precursor films. Morphological and compositional analyses revealed that the reduction in sintering temperatures was enabled by a mechanism similar to the metal-induced crystallization (MIC) process, with a distinction that catalysis of solâ€“gel reactions occurred alongside the induction of crystallization. We observed this catalytic MIC (c-MIC) mechanism in a variety of solâ€“gel material systems including NixO with embedded Au or Ag; TiOx with embedded Ni, Au, or Pt; and SnOx with embedded Ni. Based upon this concept, we demonstrated highly efficient flexible and rigid organic/inorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells (PSCs) using a low-temperature-sintered NixO film embedded with Au nanoislands as the hole-transporting layer (HTL). The c-MIC mechanism reduced the sintering temperature of the Au-embedded NixO HTL by 100 Â°C, making the NixO process compatible with plastic substrates while allowing the NixO HTL to retain more NiOOH surface groups, which enhanced hole collection and improved the quality of the perovskite layer. Thanks to the advantages of the c-MIC NixO film, the flexible and rigid PSCs achieved high power conversion efficiencies of up to 15.9 and 19.0%, respectively, which were sustained for >1200 h at 65 Â°C and 65% relative humidity. Our findings provide a practical route for low-temperature fabrication of high-quality oxide functional films by solution-based solâ€“gel processes, which will be valuable to a wide variety of applications in addition to thin film solar cells.

Introduction

Thanks to their versatile yet excellent properties, metal oxide functional materials are indispensable in almost all types of existing and upcoming energy applications, from energy generation technologies such as solar cells1â€“3 and thermoelectric generators,4â€“6 energy storage technologies such as batteries6â€“9 and supercapacitors,6,10,11 to energy conservation technologies such as light-emitting diodes12,13 and transistors/sensors14,15 used in smart appliances. Additionally, many metal oxides can be cast into films via solution-based solâ€“gel processes, which greatly enhance their manufacturability over the commonly used vacuum deposition techniques. However, the solâ€“gel process of metal oxide films still poses significant challenges that limit their practical application. Firstly, the solâ€“gel process typically requires a high-temperature sintering step to thoroughly remove the organic contents contained in the reaction chemistry, but the random and explosive escaping patterns of the organic contents at such high temperatures often lead to non-uniform, defect-laden films. Secondly, the high sintering temperatures, also required to obtain adequate crystallinity from crystalline metal oxides, render many solâ€“gel chemistries incompatible with sensitive materials such as organic semiconductors and plastic substrates. Moreover, the energy- and cost-intensiveness of the high-temperature process undermines the merits of solâ€“gel metal oxides in energy-related applications. Recent research has demonstrated low-temperature fabrication of metal oxide films by casting from metal-oxide nano-crystal suspension solutions16 or by combustion solâ€“gel reactions.14,17,18 Such methods, however, still face limitations in that casting from a nanocrystal solution tends to leave copious discontinuities in the resultant film, and the organic fuels required in the combustion method can produce large amounts of escaping side products, forming voids and defects in the resultant film. Consequently, a solâ€“gel method that can achieve thorough chemical conversion, adequate crystallization, and good film quality in metal oxide functional materials without the need of a high-temperature sintering step will be highly valuable.

In this study, we discovered a novel phenomenon related to the metal-induced crystallization (MIC) mechanism,19â€“24 where the sintering temperatures of solâ€“gel metal oxide films were greatly reduced when a metal phase was embedded within the solâ€“gel precursors. The discovery was serendipitous during an attempt to incorporate a NixO hole-transporting layer (HTL) into hybrid organicâ€“inorganic perovskite solar cells (PSCs) to take advantage of the excellent electron-blocking/hole-transporting function and the environmental stability of NixO.25â€“31 Upon observing that the optimal sintering temperature of the NixO HTLâ€”in terms of the PSC device performanceâ€”was lowered by 100 Â°C when an Au phase (in the form of nanoislands or a film) was embedded in it, we conducted a series of microstructural and compositional analyses on the NixO films, with which we determined that the reduction in the sintering temperature was via a MIC-like mechanism. We verified such a mechanism, which we termed catalytic MIC (c-MIC), occurred in a variety of other solâ€“gel material systems, including TiOx with embedded Ni, Au, or Pt; SnOx with embedded Ni; and NiOx with embedded Ag instead of Au. Finally, utilizing the low-temperature sintering process of the Au-embedded NixO HTL, we demonstrated high-efficiency flexible and rigid PSC devices with excellent environmental stability as an example of the utility of the c-MIC mechanism in energy applications.

With the regular NixO HTL (Table 1 and Fig. 1a), a 180 Â°C sintering temperature resulted in poor PSC performance, where the high series resistance (Rs), low short-circuit current density (Jsc) and low open-circuit voltage (Voc) pointed to low hole mobility and the presence of impurity in the NixO HTL due to incomplete sintering.32 Moreover, the PSC devices exhibited severe Jâ€“V hysteresis, as shown by the pronounced difference between the forward-scan (FS) and reverse-scan (RS) Jâ€“V curves, indicating the abundance of hysteresis-inducing charge-trapping defects in the NixO HTL.33,34 Incrementally raising the sintering temperature gradually improved the PSC device performance and Jâ€“V hysteresis until 280 Â°C, above which the performance started to decline due to degradation in the conductivity of the ITO electrode. The highest average power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the PSC devices, 16.1%, was obtained with a sintering temperature of 280 Â°C, where the Jâ€“V hysteresis was also eliminated (i.e. identical FS and RS Jâ€“V curves). Analysis with grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (GIXRD) revealed that the crystallinity evolution of the NixO HTL at various sintering temperatures correlated well with the PSC performance results. As shown in Fig. S4 (ESIâ€ ), the 180 Â°C-sintered NixO films were essentially amorphous, showing no observable diffraction peak. The NiO characteristic diffraction peaksâ€”at 35.7Â°, 43.1Â°, and 62.7Â°, corresponding to the (111), (200), and (220) planes, respectivelyâ€”started to appear as the sintering temperature increased to 200 Â°C, and they gradually intensified with higher sintering temperatures until 280 Â°C, above which the peak intensities remained unchanged. Both the PSC device and GIXRD results indicated that for the regular NixO HTL to be fully sintered, the sintering temperature should be at least 280 Â°C.

With the Au-embedded NixO HTL, in contrast, the sintering temperature required for the PSC devices to achieve optimal performance was only 180 Â°C. While the Au-embedded NixO HTL appeared to be incompletely sintered at 160 Â°C, as evidenced by the high Rs, low Jsc, low Voc, and severe Jâ€“V hysteresis of the corresponding PSC devices, sintering was apparently complete at 180 Â°C, where all of the device characteristics were significantly improved, leading to the optimal average PCE of 17.9% (19.0% champion) and elimination of Jâ€“V hysteresis. Further increasing the sintering temperature to 200 Â°C did not further improve the PSC device performance but caused slight deterioration (discussed later). The accuracy of the measured device metrics of the optimal with- (180 Â°C-annealed) and without-Au (280 Â°C-annealed) PSC devices was verified with incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) measurements, as shown in Fig. 1c, where the integrated photo-current density of the two devices across the incident light spectrum agreed well with their Jsc values measured with the solar simulator.

Microstructural analyses also revealed that the 180 Â°C-sintering of the Au-embedded NixO HTL resulted in extensive crystallinity. We first conducted GIXRD analysis, which showed strong diffraction peaks at 35.7Â°, 43.1Â°, and 62.7Â° in the 180 Â°C-sintered Au-embedded NixO HTL, as shown in Fig. S5 (ESIâ€ ). However, this was not adequate evidence of the crystallinity of the NixO HTLs, as the three diffraction peaks correspond to both the NiO (JCPDS 47-1049) and the Au (JCPDS 04-0784) crystals, and therefore the origin of the peaks could not be discerned. As an alternative to XRD, we used high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) to observe the crystalline phase of the Au-embedded NixO HTLs, and the results are shown in Fig. 2. The top-view HRTEM image in Fig. 2a showed clear lattice fringes occurring throughout the Au nanoislands and the surrounding NixO region, whose spacing of 0.211 nm corresponded to the (200) planes of the NiO crystal, indicating that the NixO phase was indeed crystalline after the 180 Â°C sintering. To more closely examine the crystallinity of the NixO film under HRTEM, we prepared a cross-section sample with the structure of Si wafer/50 nm Au film/50 nm NixO, sintered at 180 Â°C and cross-sectioned with a focused ion beam (FIB). Fig. 2c and d show the cross-sectional HRTEM images, and Fig. 2e presents a selected-area diffraction pattern collected in the NixO region. The cross-sectional HRTEM images showed continuous NiO lattice fringes extending from the Au film/NixO interface to the NixO top surface, confirming that the NixO film was thoroughly crystallized. Additionally, the electron diffraction pattern of the NixO film showed three diffraction rings. The d-spacings corresponding to the diffraction rings are 0.25 nm, 0.21 nm, and 0.15 nm, respectively, which in turn correspond to the (111), (200), and (220) planes of NiO. It is worth noting that both forms of embedded Au examined here, nanoislands and flat films, showed the same low-temperature crystallization effect.

One curious fact regarding the PSC characteristics was that the optimal PCE from the Au-embedded NixO HTL (180 Â°C-sintered) was higher, with higher FF and lower series resistance, than from the regular NixO HTL (280 Â°C-sintered), even though the LSPR absorption of the Au-embedded NixO centered at âˆ¼550 nm (Fig. S3, ESIâ€ ) should have diminished the intensity of the incident light to the perovskite active layer. IPCE measurements (Fig. 1c) revealed that the IPCE of the Au-embedded devices was indeed lower than that of the regular devices in the âˆ¼550 nm range, but the difference was small. Moreover, the IPCE of the Au-embedded devices was higher across the rest of the spectrum, where the Au-embedded NixO had identical or slightly lower optical transmission than the regular NixO. This pointed to the presence of certain enhancement mechanism with the Au-embedded NixO. Closer examination with XPS analysis revealed that the chemical composition at the NixO surface varied with its annealing temperature, as shown in Fig. 4. Specifically, the O 1s spectra contained a shoulder corresponding to the NiOOH group beside the NiO peak, and the intensity of the NiOOH shoulder intensified by lowering the annealing temperature. This was consistent with the results from water contact angle measurements (Fig. S8, ESIâ€ ), where the 180 Â°C-annealed NixO layer showed a lower contact angle than the 280 Â°C-annealed NixO. The NiOOH group has been shown to enhance the performance of the NixO HTL in organic solar cells, where its strong dipole facilitates the collection of holes, reducing the series resistance and improving FF.35 Additionally, the increased hydrophilicity of the low-temperature-annealed NixO surface improved the quality of the over-coated perovskite layer, as revealed by SEM in Fig. S9 (ESIâ€ ), where the perovskite layer formed on the 180 Â°C-annealed NixO showed larger crystal grains than on the 280 Â°C-annealed one. Therefore, the enhancement mechanism of the Au-embedded NixO was attributable to its low sintering temperatures, which allowed the NixO HTL to retain a higher concentration of surface NiOOH groups than did the high-temperature-sintered regular NixO, resulting in better HTL function and better film quality of the perovskite layer. This may also account for the slight deterioration of device performance of the 200 Â°C-sintered Au-embedded devices compared with the 180 Â°C-sintered ones.

As to the mechanism that enabled the Au-embedded NixO film to be fully sintered at low temperatures, we speculated that it followed a similar route to the metal-induced crystallization (MIC) process. The MIC process is widely used to transform amorphous functional materials to crystalline ones at low temperatures, by physically contacting a metal component (usually a film) with an amorphous material to be transformed.36â€“38 The general MIC mechanism involves three steps: (1) an interface layer composed of a monolayer of the metal and the amorphous material is formed at the metal/amorphous material interface; (2) bonding within the amorphous monolayer in the interface layer is weakened by its coordinating interactions with the metal monolayer, allowing the amorphous monolayer to crystallize; (3) the metal monolayer migrates to the front with the amorphous phase to form a new interface layer, and the above steps repeat through the entirety of the amorphous material.24 To verify whether a similar mechanism occurred in the Au/NixO system, we used time-of-flight secondary-ion mass spectrometry (ToF-SIMS), whose high sensitivity enabled accurate determination of trace amounts of elements, to map the Au depth profile starting from the surface of the 180 Â°C-sintered Si wafer/50 nm Au film/50 nm NixO sample. Our rationale was that if a MIC-like mechanism was indeed involved, an Au monolayer would be present at the top surface of the NixO film, having migrated there from the NixO/Au interface during the MIC process. Fig. 5 presents the Au as well as Ni and O depth profiles determined with ToF-SIMS. It can be clearly seen from the depth profile that the thin layer at the topmost portion of the NixO film was Au-rich, indicating that an Au monolayer was indeed present at the top surface. It should be noted that the relative yields of Au, Ni, and O are not directly equal to the ratios of their atomic contents, as the ionization efficiencies of the elements were different in the ToF-SIMS measurements.


Fig. 5 ToF-SIMS depth profiles of Ni, O, and Au obtained from the 180 Â°C-sintered Si wafer/50 nm Au film/50 nm NixO sample.

Although a MIC-like mechanism appeared to be at play, there was one unique aspect to our observed phenomenon: the Au component not only induced amorphous-to-crystalline physical transformation of the NixO phase, as occurs in the typical MIC process, but it also catalyzed the chemical reaction of the solâ€“gel precursors. This phenomenon has not been reported before, and the exact mechanism requires more work to elucidate; but it is conceivable that the bond-weakening effect in a metal/amorphous material interface layer may also occur in a metal/solâ€“gel precursor interface layer, facilitating the removal of the organic ligands in the precursors and thus freeing the Ni and O atoms to crystallize. A schematic illustration of the above-described catalytic MIC (c-MIC) process is shown in Fig. 6.


Fig. 6 Schematic illustration of the proposed mechanism of catalytic metal-induced crystallization: (a) weakened bonding (black dashed lines) within the solâ€“gel precursor at the Au/precursor interface facilitates the removal of organic ligands and the condensation reaction of the precursor; (b) an interface layer consisting of an Au monolayer and an amorphous NixO monolayer forms; (c) the amorphous NixO monolayer, also with weakened bonding according to the MIC mechanism, crystallizes while the Au monolayer migrates to the new front with the solâ€“gel phase; (d) an new Au/precursor interface layer forms, repeating the cycle of (a) through (d).

Conclusions

In summary, a new phenomenon related to the metal-induced crystallization mechanism was discovered, where a solâ€“gel precursor film in contact with a metal phase exhibited greatly reduced sintering temperature via the catalytic action of the metal/precursor interface layer. Specifically, a NixO solâ€“gel precursor film embedded with Au nanoislands underwent a complete reaction and extensive crystallization at 180 Â°C, as opposed to the 280 Â°C required to fully sinter a NixO solâ€“gel precursor film without the embedded Au phase. Depth-profiling elemental analysis revealed that an Au-containing interface layer migrated through the entirety of the NixO film during sintering, similar to the MIC process. The low sintering temperatures of the Au-embedded NixO film permitted its incorporation into flexible as well as rigid perovskite solar cell devices as a hole-transporting layer, achieving PCEs of up to 15.9 and 19.0% from the flexible and rigid devices, respectively, with excellent stability under accelerated aging conditions. Moreover, the low sintering temperature of the Au-embedded NixO HTL allowed it to retain a higher concentration of surface NiOOH groups, which enhanced hole-collection at the NixO surface and improved the quality of the over-coated perovskite film, resulting in superior PSC performance. More importantly, the generality of the c-MIC mechanism was demonstrated with several solâ€“gel material systems, including TiOx with embedded Ni, Au, or Pt; SnOx with embedded Ni; and NiOx with embedded Ag instead of Au. Our findings provide a practical route for low-temperature fabrication of high-quality oxide functional films from solution-based solâ€“gel processes, which will be valuable to a wide variety of energy applications in addition to thin film solar cells.
