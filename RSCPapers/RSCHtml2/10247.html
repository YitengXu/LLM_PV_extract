Recent progress in organic photovoltaics: device architecture and optical design

Weiran Cao and Jiangeng Xue *
Department of Materials Science and Engineering, University of Florida, Gainesville, FL 32611, USA. E-mail: jxue@mse.ufl.edu

First published on 17th March 2014

Abstract

Research on organic photovoltaic (OPV) materials and devices has flourished in recent years due to their potential for offering low-cost solar energy conversion. With a deepened understanding on the fundamental photovoltaic processes in organic electronic materials and the development of tailored materials and device architectures, we have seen a rapid increase in the efficiency of OPV devices to over 10%, which attracts tremendous commercial interests for further development and manufacturing. Here, we review recent progress in the field of organic photovoltaics, particularly on various innovative device architectures and optical designs to maximize the power conversion efficiency of OPV cells for a given set of photoactive donor and acceptor materials. Following an introduction of the basic device operation of organic photovoltaic cells and the advances in active materials, we firstly present different device architectures that have been used to optimize the charge generation and collection characteristics within the OPV devices. We then discuss various methods to manage and manipulate the light wave propagation in OPV devices for more complete absorption of the incident light, an important area that has been underexplored so far.

Broader context

Organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices based on -conjugated organic molecules or polymers offer great opportunities for low-cost solar energy conversion, due to several technological advantages of organic semiconductors. These advantages include low material cost, easy and high-throughput manufacturing processes, compatibility with flexible substrates and tunable material properties via chemical structure modification. In recent years, the performance of OPV devices, including the efficiency and stability, has steadily improved, resulting from the development of new active materials and material processing/treatment methods, novel device architectures and internal/external optical structures to manage the light. The state-of-the-art power conversion efficiency of laboratory cells has reached over 10%, attracting tremendous commercialization interests.

1 Introduction

Finding clean and renewable energy sources has become one of the greatest challenges for our modern society, due to the rapidly increasing energy demand and the limited reserve of traditional fossil fuel sources (coal, oil and natural gas).14 Technologies that harvest the clean, renewable and abundant solar power are among the most attractive methods to solve this energy challenge. Among those technologies, photovoltaic (PV) cells, or solar cells, which directly convert solar energy to electricity, have emerged as a promising candidate. Since the invention of the first modern silicon solar cell in the 1950's,5 we have seen tremendous advances in solar cell research and development. With the use of improved and new materials and the introduction of new device architectures,6 the power conversion efficiency (PCE, P), arguably the most important scientific figure of merit for solar cells, has steadily increased over the years from about 6% to 44%.7 On the commercialization side, the PV industry has also grown tremendously, especially in the last two decades. The worldwide PV cell/module production capacity steadily increased by 3540% per year from 1996 to 2003.8 Since 2006, the global annual PV installation has risen at an even faster pace of an approximately 70% increase per year,9 although there is some volatility in the growth in the last few years as influenced by the overall economic and financial environment in the world. The annual PV installation in the world first exceeded 1 GW in 2003, and by the end of 2012 the world's cumulative installed PV capacity has surpassed 100 GW.9 PV now covers 2.6% of the overall electricity demand (and 5.2% of the instantaneous electricity demand when the PV generation reaches peak at mid-day) in Europe, which represents approximately 70% of the world's cumulative PV capacity.9

Existing commercial PV devices are mostly based on crystalline silicon wafers or ribbons.8 Other inorganic semiconductors such as amorphous silicon, CdTe, and Cu(In,Ga)Se2 have been used to fabricate thin-film solar cells to reduce material consumption and manufacturing cost; however, generally these thin-film cells have lower efficiency than crystalline Si cells.10 GaAs and other IIIV compound semiconductors have been used to fabricate the highest efficiency solar cells, in both single-junction and multi-junction architectures.7 While the refinement of processes has led to the decrease of PV module prices to $1 per W today,11 the price of solar electricity is still several times higher than the electricity generated from coal,12 which has been the limiting factor for a larger scale adoption of the PV technologies.8,10,13 Despite the tremendous growth, PV still only accounts for less than 1% of the world's energy supply. Costs associated with various materials used in the modules and the cell/module manufacturing processes are the major contributors to the relatively high costs of solar electricity.11

Organic photovoltaic devices, based on -conjugated small molecules or polymers, offer great opportunities for low-cost solar energy conversion, due to several technological advantages of organic materials.1320 The -conjugation in these organic molecules and polymers results in typical energy gaps of 1 to 3 eV between the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) and the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO), leading to semiconducting behavior and strong interactions with visible and near-infrared light. The maximum absorption coefficients of typical organic semiconductors are in the order of 105 cm1, which suggests that the use of 100 nm thick films can absorb most of the incident light. Compared to their inorganic counterparts, these organic electronic materials generally have significantly lower material costs and can be made into thin films using inexpensive room-temperature processes.2124 Organic materials are intrinsically compatible with flexible substrates and high-throughput, roll-to-roll manufacturing processes; hence they are ideally suited for the fabrication of large-area electronic and optoelectronic devices with low cost.13,17,23,25,26 Moreover, the electronic and optical properties of organic semiconductors can be tuned to a large extent with appropriate structure modifications during synthesis, leading to tailored materials properties for specific applications.17 With low-cost materials and processes, some statistical studies have shown that PV modules based on organic semiconductors are expected to have a much lower energy payback time and better environmental sustainability with less greenhouse gas (CO2) emission than inorganic PV technologies.23,2729 In addition to PV applications, organic semiconductors have been used to produce organic light-emitting devices for lighting and displays,3033 field-effect transistors,3436 photodetectors,3741 and memory devices,42,43 some of which have already been commercialized.17

While the organic donoracceptor heterojunction (HJ) structure reported by Tang44 in 1986 has been widely considered as the foundation for all efficient OPV devices, studies on the photovoltaic behavior of organic solids can be traced back to the 1950's, around the years when the first Si solar cell was demonstrated.4547 As early as in 1967, Golubovi predicted that organic photoconductors may prove practical for solar cells.48 However, the early OPV devices employed an organic light absorber sandwiched between two electrodes,49,50 thus relying on the Schottky contact effect to achieve the separation of electrons and holes,51 which as we know now is rather inefficient. Hence, the power conversion efficiencies of these early OPV devices were generally significantly below 0.1%.52 In Tang's report, a two-layer OPV cell containing a layer of copper phthalocyanine (CuPc) serving as an electron donor and a layer of 3,4,9,10-perylenetetracarboxylic bis-benzimidazole (PTCBI) as an electron acceptor was demonstrated.44 This CuPc/PTCBI donoracceptor heterojunction serves to efficiently separate electronhole pairs through a rapid electron transfer process at the interface, and for the first time, a nearly 1% efficient OPV cell was demonstrated. Since then, the performance of OPV devices, including the efficiency and stability, has steadily improved with new materials and/or novel device architectures,5361 with P now exceeding 10% for both polymer and small molecule based OPV devices,7,24,62 which has really driven the strong research and development interests of this technology. The rapid expansion of research in the OPV field can be illustrated by the exponential growth of the annual totals of peer-reviewed publications and citations (Fig. 1), which have an annual growth rate of 3540% in the last 15 years. In 2012 alone, there were more than 3000 peer-reviewed publications and more than 90000 citations on organic photovoltaics.

In this review article, we present the recent progresses in OPV research through the application of new device architectures and the design of optical structures within the cells/modules. In Section 2, we briefly discuss the basic principles and characterization of PV devices, introduce the two donoracceptor heterojunction structures (planar and bulk/mixed heterojunctions) that have been widely used in OPV cells, and present some representative small molecule and polymer donor and acceptor materials with high voltage output and good solar spectra coverage. More complete reviews on the active OPV materials can be found elsewhere.18,24,37,63,64 This review focuses on device architectures and optical designs that can maximize the photovoltaic performance of a given set of active materials. In Section 3, new donoracceptor heterojunctions, including planar-mixed and ordered HJ structures, are reviewed, which can mitigate the exciton diffusion/dissociation and charge transport/collection bottlenecks in planar and mixed HJs. Other structures with different layer-stacking sequences for achieving high device efficiency, e.g. inverted and tandem device architectures, are also discussed. Instead of studying the internal components of OPV devices, the optical management within OPV cells as optical systems is another critical factor that influences the photovoltaic performance. In Section 4, we discuss the optical field distribution in both single and tandem devices and review the methods for maximizing light absorption by tuning the layer thicknesses in the devices. Applying optic designs is another promising, yet underexplored approach to increase the device efficiency. This section also covers the use of external optical designs for either changing the optical intensity distribution or introducing light trapping within devices. Finally, we present conclusions and outlook in Section 5.

2 Organic photovoltaic cells: overview

Organic semiconductor based PV cells share certain basic device operation principles with inorganic semiconductor based devices, but also present some important differences due to the different physical properties of the semiconductors. Upon the absorption of an incident photon that has a higher energy than the optical gap of the organic active material, an electron in the HOMO will be excited to the LUMO, leaving a hole in the HOMO. Such photo-generated electronhole pairs are localized on the same molecule or on neighboring molecules, due to the weak intermolecular interactions between organic molecules, which then quickly relax to Frenkel or charge-transfer excitons with binding energies in the range of 0.1 eV to 2 eV.65,66 A dissociation of the charge-neutral excitons into separated electrons and holes before they recombine, either radiatively or non-radiatively, is necessary for the photovoltaic device to function. For an efficient electron/hole separation after photoexcitation (light absorption), nowadays essentially all OPV devices use the so-called donoracceptor heterojunction structure. With a type II (staggered) heterojunction energy level alignment at the donoracceptor interface (Fig. 2(a)), a photogenerated exciton near this interface may dissociate through a rapid electron transfer process,14 resulting in separated holes in the donor HOMO level and electrons in the acceptor LUMO level. To allow the electron transfer process to be energetically favorable, the LUMO (or HOMO) level offset at the interface needs to be greater than the exciton binding energy. After the exciton dissociation/charge separation process at the donoracceptor interface, the separated electrons and holes move towards their respective electrodes, driven by either the built-in electric field or the charge carrier concentration gradient, leading to a photocurrent or photovoltage. Hence, the overall external quantum efficiency (EQE), or the ratio of the number of electronhole pairs collected at the electrodes to the number of incident photons can be written as: EQE = AEDCTCC = AIQE, where A, ED, CT, and CC are the efficiencies of light absorption (exciton generation), exciton diffusion, charge transfer (exciton dissociation), and charge collection, respectively, and IQE is the internal quantum efficiency, or the ratio of the number of electronhole pairs collected at the electrodes to the number of absorbed photons.37,67,68

Since the first demonstration by Tang, the bilayer donoracceptor heterojunction structure with sequentially deposited neat donor and acceptor layers (Fig. 2(b)) has been widely used for small molecule based OPV cells. However, most organic semiconductors have exciton diffusion lengths on the order of 10 nm, which is considerably shorter than the optical absorption length (typically on the order of 50100 nm).6971 To allow the photogenerated excitons reaching the donoracceptor interface with high probabilities, the donoracceptor layers need to be thin, which then limits the light absorption efficiency. Thus, this fundamental trade-off between optical absorption and exciton diffusion strongly limits the performance of planar HJ devices. Another type of HJ structure, mixed or bulk heterojunction (BHJ), by intermixing or blending the donor and acceptor materials, has therefore been developed to overcome this exciton-diffusing bottleneck.15,72,73 For thermal vacuum-deposited small molecules, the mixed HJ structure can be realized through the co-evaporation of donor and acceptor materials as first shown by Hiramoto et al. in 1992.72 In 1995, the groups of Heeger and Friend independently reported solution-processed bulk HJ structure using polymerfullerene and polymerpolymer blends, respectively, which has now become the predominant structure for solution-processed OPV devices.15,73 As illustrated in Fig. 2(c), the high interfacial area between the donor and acceptor materials facilitates the exciton diffusion process, even for active layers significantly thicker than the exciton diffusion lengths. A fundamental challenge for the bulk HJ structure is to control its three-dimensional morphological structure to achieve the optimal spatially distributed exciton dissociation interfaces and charge transport channels.54,7477 Considering the importance of the morphology on the photovoltaic characteristics of bulk HJs, researchers have employed different processing conditions7883 and applied post-deposition treatments58,8486 to influence the phase separation process. For solution-processed bulk HJs, phase separation and donoracceptor network formation are strongly influenced by the solvent's physical properties, such as the vapor pressure and molecular polarity.87 Moreover, processing the donoracceptor blend with appropriate solvent additives has also become a useful strategy to increase the degree of separation, local structure ordering and charge transport.54,8890 Post-deposition treatment, such as thermal85,91 and solvent annealing,86,92 can also induce changes in the bulk HJ morphology. For many vacuum deposited mixed HJs, the donor and acceptor materials are intimately mixed and the domain features are difficult to distinguish before any post-deposition treatments. This fine intermixing of the materials inevitably leads to disruption in continuous charge transport paths for photo-generated electrons and/or holes, and in turn leads to low carrier mobilities and a low fill factor, FF, for the OPV cells.93 After thermal or solvent annealing, the donoracceptor networks will become more apparent with a typical feature size of 10 nm, as a result of the increased crystallinity of either donors or acceptors, which can benefit the charge transport in the active layers and hence improve the power conversion efficiencies.85,91

The design of both the donor and acceptor active materials is obviously essential for the OPV performance. The structures of some widely used donor and acceptor materials are shown in Fig. 3. Copper and zinc phthalocyanines (CuPc and ZnPc, respectively) are arguably the two most common small molecule donors used in OPV cells,37,44,53,94 whereas poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) is the most widely used polymer donor material.84,92 In the past 15 years or so, many more efficient donor materials have been explored, which yield a higher voltage output and a better solar spectral coverage.64,95 For example, Mutolo et al. reported the use of boron subphthalocyanine chloride (SubPc) as the donor material for small molecule based OPV devices, which shows a maximized open-circuit voltage, Voc, of 1 V (much higher than Voc 0.5 V for the CuPc/C60 cells) due to the deeper HOMO energy level of SubPc.96,97 Lead phthalocyanine (PbPc) was explored as the donor material to extend the spectral coverage to more than 1000 nm.41,98 Most recently, some aromatic molecules, such as tetraphenyldibenzoperiflanthene (DBP)99,100 and diindenoperylene (DIP),101 have also been used as donor materials, which show the optimized efficiency of more than 5% with simple planar HJ structures.

Similarly, polymer donor materials with deeper HOMO levels have been demonstrated by many groups.24,64,102 To achieve low-gap polymers and improve the solar spectrum coverage, the design of an alternating donoracceptor structure is one of the most successful methods, in which the covalently connected electron-rich units (donor) and electron-poor units (acceptor) lead to new hybridized HOMO/LUMO orbitals and a narrower optical gap than either the donor or acceptor unit.103,104 For example, the low-gap donoracceptor polymer, poly[2,6-(4,4-bis-(2-ethylhexyl)-4H-cyclopenta[2,1-b;3,4-b]-dithiophene)-alt-4,7-(2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)] (PCPDTBT), combines a dialkyl-cyclopentabithiophene donor unit and a benzothiadiazole acceptor unit, and has an optical bandgap of about 1.4 eV, corresponding to a long-wavelength absorption edge of up to 900 nm.54 Electron-withdrawing functional groups, such as fluorene and carbazole, are added to the electron-rich polymer chain to make it less electron-rich and result in a lower HOMO level.104,105 In addition to narrowing the optical gap, some other parameters of polymers, such as carrier mobility, crystallinity and HOMO level, should also be taken into consideration to further improve device performance. Chen et al. replaced the bridging carbon atoms in PCPDTBT molecule with a Si atom (referred as PSBTBT), which has a slight change on absorption but improves the hole transport and material crystallinity, leading to a higher short-circuit current density, Jsc, for devices based on PSBTBT.106 Using a different side chain, a similar donor, alternating the copolymer of 4,4-bis(2-ethylhexyl)-dithieno[3,2-b:2,3-d]silole and N-octylthieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (PDTSTPD) was reported by Chu et al. with a deeper HOMO level of 5.57 eV, which resulted in a maximum power conversion efficiency of 7.3% in OPV devices.107 Last but not least, Amb and co-workers used an even larger atom, germanium, as the bridge, leading to even stronger -stacking and a certified efficiency of 7.4%.108,109

So far, fullerenes and their derivatives have been found to be the best acceptor candidates, owing to their high charge carrier mobility and electron affinity.15,37,84,110,111 For example, C60 is the common acceptor material for most small molecule based PV devices, the unique properties of which offers special packing ability in donoracceptor blends to form an efficient charge transport channel.53,94,112 To make these fullerene materials compatible with solution processing methods, functional side groups are anchored onto the fullerene molecules, which not only improve the solubility in common organic solvents, but also yield tunable miscibility with the donor components. [6,6]-Phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM)113115 and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM)104,109 are the most widely used acceptors for solution-processed OPV devices. Additionally, some n-type polymers with a high optical absorption have been developed as acceptors.73,116,117 However, the efficiency of the polymerpolymer systems is typically lower than those based on fullerene derivatives, resulting from the low electron mobility and limited charge transport of such polymer acceptors.57,118 Inorganic semiconductor nanocrystals with a high electron mobility and electron affinity, such as CdSe119121 and ZnO,122,123 have also been used as alternative acceptor materials. Research on such polymernanocrystal hybrid PV cells received great attention after Huynh et al. demonstrated nearly 2% efficient P3HT:CdSe nanorod cells in 2002,121 and there appears to be a renaissance of this type of device in the last few years, with better interfacial engineering and a deepened understanding of the device physics.124 Most recently, our group demonstrated polymerCdSe nanorods hybrid PV cells with a maximum efficiency over 5% using an ethanedithiol treatment.125

3 Advanced device architectures

Along with the efforts on developing new materials and new material processing methods, there have also been significant research efforts on developing suitable device structures to maximize the photovoltaic function of the active (donor and acceptor) materials. Heterojunction structures other than the basic planar or bulk HJ structures were reported to further control the exciton and carrier dynamics in the photoactive region. Devices with the polarity of the top and bottom electrodes inverted enabled the incorporation of some electrode modification layers to improve charge injection from the electrode. Finally, the tandem cell architecture has been applied to OPV devices to achieve the highest efficiencies.

3.1 Double heterojunctions and use of electrode interfacial layers

The basic OPV device consists of a donoracceptor HJ (planar or bulk) sandwiching between a transparent electrode (typically indium-tin oxide, or ITO) and a reflective metal (Ag or Al) electrode. Besides the focus on a basic planar or bulk HJ structure for achieving efficient OPV devices, different studies have shown that a double or even multiple HJ structure with additional functional layer(s) inserted between electrodes and photoactive layers significantly enhances device performance.126 Such additional layer(s) can engineer the electrodeorganic interfaces for improving electrical (i.e. blocking misdirected carriers) and physical (i.e. preventing damage from electrode deposition) properties and affect the light intensity distribution within the active layers (see below).

Peumans et al. reported the incorporation of a 10 nm thick bathocuproine (BCP) layer inserted between the active layers and the Ag cathode as an exciton-blocking layer (EBL) in a CuPc/PTCBI device to form a double heterojunction (CuPc/PTCBI/BCP).127 With the thin BCP layer, the EQE of the device was slightly increased as the CuPc and PTCBI active layer thickness was reduced, whereas it was dramatically reduced for the thinnest device if the BCP layer was omitted. This suggests that the BCP layer can eliminate excitons diffusing towards and quenching at the acceptormetal interface, hence improving the device quantum efficiency. Some other small molecule materials with lower LUMO levels (higher electron mobility) than BCP, such as bathophenanthroline (BPhene),128 tris(acetylacetonato)ruthenium(III) (Ru(acac)3)129 and tris-8-hydroxy-quinolinato aluminum (Alq3),130,131 have also been used as EBLs in small molecule based OPV devices to reduce or eliminate the electron transport barrier from the acceptor. Transparent metal oxides (such as ZnO (ref. 120, 132 and 133) and TiOx (ref. 126, 134 and 135)) are another type of materials for use at the organiccathode interface, especially for solution-processed bulk HJ devices. The wide bandgap of these oxides provides good exciton-blocking characteristics, and their deep valence band blocks the unwanted transport of photogenerated holes towards the cathode.

On the anode side, suitable interfacial layer(s) have similarly been inserted to prevent exciton quenching and/or block photogenerated electrons from reaching the anode.136 Poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) is widely utilized as an anodic buffer layer in OPV devices, which planarizes the anode (ITO) surface and reduces the leakage current. However, the PEDOT:PSS layer is not a good EBL and sometimes an addition layer is inserted between the PEDOT:PSSdonor interface to further reduce exciton quenching and improve the device performance. For example, Hirade et al. introduced a tris[4-(5-phenylthiophen-2-yl)phenyl]amine (TPTPA) layer in a DBP/C60 device, and achieved an increase of Jsc from 5.61 mA cm2 to 7.15 mA cm2 and an increase of P from 3.9% to 5.3%.100 Besides serving as an EBL, some anode interfacial materials (i.e. pentacene,98 tetracene,137 and CuI138,139) have also been employed to affect the growth of organic molecules and enhance the total optical absorption.140 Metal oxides, such as V2O5,141 MoO3,142144 WO3,145,146 and NiO,147149 are another types of anode modification materials. Irwin et al. demonstrated a P3HT:PCBM device with a p-type NiO layer, which indicated an enhanced device performance of Voc = 0.638 V, Jsc = 11.3 mA cm2, FF = 0.69, and P = 5.2% compared to that of a PEDOT:PSS based control device (Voc = 0.624 V, Jsc = 9.54 mA cm2, FF = 0.40 and P = 2.4%).150

3.2 Planar-mixed heterojunction

As discussed above, exciton diffusion limits the performance of a planar donoracceptor HJ device, since organic semiconductors usually have the exciton diffusion length (10 nm), considerably shorter than the active layer thickness for efficient light absorption. A mixed or bulk HJ device is designed to achieve high exciton diffusion efficiency while maintaining good absorption. However, the low charge transport/collection efficiency, as a result of the intermixing of the active materials, often limits the performance of a mixed HJ device.151 A hybrid planar-mixed HJ (PM-HJ) structure consisting of a mixed donoracceptor layer sandwiched between a neat donor layer and a neat acceptor layer has therefore been developed to mitigate the limitations of planar and mixed HJs while preserving their advantages.152154 Exciton dissociation and charge separation in a planar-mixed HJ occurs at the donormixed and mixedacceptor layer interfaces as well as throughout the entire mixed layer. This broadened charge generation zone allows the use of somewhat thinner neat and mixed layers compared to those in optimized planar HJ or mixed HJ devices, mitigating the exciton diffusion or charge transport bottlenecks in the latter devices.

Xue et al. compared the performance of an optimized CuPc:C60 planar-mixed HJ cell with those of the optimized planar and mixed devices.152,153 As shown in Fig. 4, with a 10 nm thick mixed layer sandwiched between a 15 nm thick donor layer and a 35 nm thick acceptor layer, the planar-mixed HJ cell showed 25% higher Jsc than the planar HJ device (20 nm thick CuPc/40 nm thick C60), and nearly the same as that of the mixed HJ cell (with a 33 nm thick mixed layer). Moreover, the planar-mixed HJ cell processed the same high FF 0.6 as the planar HJ device at 1 sun illumination, which is much higher than that of the mixed HJ cell (FF = 0.46). Combined, the high Jsc and FF of the hybrid PM-HJ cell lead to a 40% improvement in P over the planar or mixed HJ devices.153 Most recently, Xiao et al. demonstrated a high efficiency DBP:C70 device using such a planar-mixed HJ structure.155 The planar-mixed HJ device showed an internal quantum efficiency higher than 90% between the wavelengths of 450 nm and 550 nm, 10% higher than the mixed HJ device, and thus an increase of P from 5.7% to 6.4%.155

It is also noted that the design of the planar-mixed HJ does not require the donor material in the mixed layer to be the same as that in the neat donor layer, and similarly for the acceptor materials in the mixed layer and in the neat acceptor layer. Hence, it is possible to use this device structure to incorporate two different donors (or acceptors) to extend the overall spectral coverage of the OPV cell.124 For example, Yuen et al. reported an OPV cell using the active layer structure of ZnPc/ClInPc:C60/C60. The somewhat complementary absorption of the two donor materials (ZnPc and ClInPc) contributed to an enhanced Jsc of 6.75 mA cm2, considerably higher than that from the ZnPc/C60 bilayer cell (Jsc = 4.47 mA cm2) or from the ClInPc/C60 bilayer (Jsc = 3.95 mA cm2).156

3.3 Controlled bulk heterojunctions

Despite the significant advances in controlling the morphology of the bulk heterojunctions using appropriate processing methods and optimized conditions, the random nature of the phase separation process in the donoracceptor blend possess a fundamental limitation. The degree of phase segregation strongly depends on the intermolecular interactions and the processing conditions. Morphological islands, bottlenecks, or cul-de-sacs in the bulk HJs may be formed during phase separation, which can cause charge trapping within the unfavorable structures and limit charge transport and collection. Seemingly minor changes to the molecular structures of the active materials could sometimes lead to dramatic changes in the bulk HJ morphology and require significantly different optimal processing conditions to achieve maximum OPV performance. This present a strong challenge to the further development of new active materials, as one may need to survey a wide range of processing methods and conditions to explore the full potential of a new OPV active material.

Due to the lack of a theoretical foundation nowadays to accurately predict the BHJ morphology given a set of active materials and processing conditions, it is desirable to explore alternative ways to achieve a desired bulk HJ morphology using bottom-up or top-down techniques. The interdigitated donoracceptor structure is commonly conceptualized as an ideal structure for bulk HJs, which has the nano-scale donor or acceptor phases that secure efficient exciton diffusion and the pure donor and acceptor phases that are respectively connected with the two electrodes leading to superior charge transport over that in a mixed film.157161 The realization of such a structure, however, is very challenging, and there have been only moderate success so far.

In one example, Yang et al. used the organic vapor phase deposition (OVPD) method to grow CuPc nanostructures as the foundation for an interdigitated bulk HJ structure.162 Different from the vacuum thermal evaporation (VTE) method commonly used for deposition of small molecules, the OVPD method employs an inert carrier gas to carry small molecule vapors to the substrate.163 A wide range of CuPc film morphologies were achieved by controlling the growth conditions in OPVD, which include source and substrate temperatures, carrier-gas flow rate, and chamber pressure.164 With optimized process conditions, CuPc films with densely distributed short needle features that have an average diameter of 30 nm and peak-to-valley height of 35 nm were successfully obtained. The interdigitated bulk HJ active structure was then created by filling the acceptor molecules, PTCBI, into the spacing between the CuPc protrusions using OVPD. The resulted CuPc/PTCBI device showed much improved Jsc and P (11 mA cm2 and 2.7%, respectively), approximately doubling those of the planar HJ devices fabricated using the VTE method (Jsc = 6 mA cm2 and P = 1.0%).

Zheng et al. also reported the synthesis of vertically oriented molecular nanorods using the oblique angle deposition (OAD) method, in which the molecular beam reaches the substrate surface at a large angle from the substrate normal, instead of perpendicular to the substrate, as in conventional VTE processes.165,166 Due to the ballistic shadowing effect of the nuclei formed on the substrate during the initial deposition in OAD or other non-planar surface features, the molecules subsequently deposited on the substrate grow on top of the nuclei, forming nanorods slanted in the general direction of the incoming molecular beam (but at a smaller angle from the substrate normal). As shown in Fig. 5(a), CuPc nanorods with a diameter of 20 to 50 nm and a length up to 200 nm were successfully grown. Rotating the substrate during the OAD growth led to mostly vertically aligned nanorods (Fig. 5(b)). To create an interdigitated bulk HJ active layer, a chlorobenzene solution of PCBM was spin-coated onto the nanorod array, resulting in relatively smooth top surface as PCBM molecules infiltrate into the gaps between nanorods (Fig. 5(c)). Based on such interdigitated HJ structure, the CuPc nanorods/PCBM device shows a maximum current density of Jsc = 5.6 mA cm2 and efficiency of P = 1.8% (Fig. 5(de)), approximately twice that of the planar HJ devices (Jsc = 3.4 mA cm2 and P = 0.85%), due to the increased exciton dissociation/charge collection efficiency. A further reduction in nanorod size is likely needed to improve the overall device efficiency using this method.

For solution-processed devices, several different methods, such as using inorganic templates and developing polymer nanowires or nanoparticles, have been employed to fabricate the nanostructured devices.167 Among those methods, nanoimprint lithography (NIL), which directly transfers the nanopattern from a template to the active layer, is an emerging technique to form polymer nanostructures with predesigned structures.168170 With careful operation and proper surface treatment, imprinted P3HT nanopillars and nanoholes with 15 nm diameter (aspect ratio 2.5) were reported by Park et al. A large number of grain boundaries, defects and trapping states at the donoracceptor interface result from the contactrelease processing of NIL, which causes excitons/carriers to recombine and lowers device performance. Hence, further optimization of this technique or some alternative methods should be developed to further improve the donoracceptor interfaces.

3.4 Inverted device architectures

Conventionally, OPV devices are fabricated with the organic active layer first deposited on the top of a transparent high work function anode (e.g., ITO) on a transparent substrate (e.g., glass) followed by the deposition of a low work function metal cathode (e.g., Al or Ca). A PEDOT:PSS hole injection layer is also commonly deposited on ITO to improve the anode work function. However, there are several concerns about this conventional architecture in terms of device stability and fabrication cost. The low work function metals used as top electrodes may be easily oxidized in the ambient environment, which allows the diffusion of oxygen into the active layer and causes device degradation. Also, a high vacuum is required for the deposition of these metal electrodes, limiting their compatibility with large-scale roll-to-roll processing. Inverted OPV devices that have similar stacks as the conventional devices, but have reversed charge movement, have been developed to remedy these problems. Due to the reversed polarity of charge collection, high work function metals, like Au, Ag and Cu, can be utilized as the top electrode to minimize the device degradation.180,181 Two types of inverted OPV devices have been demonstrated recently: bottom and top illuminated devices based on the direction of the incident light. Bottom illuminated inverted devices use a transparent or semi-transparent bottom electrode to collect electrons and a top reflective anode to collect holes, whereas the top illuminated devices have a bottom reflective cathode and a top transparent anode.

The bottom illuminated structure has been widely studied and utilized since its geometry is similar to conventional devices with a bottom transparent ITO electrode and a top reflective metal electrode. One essential requirement for this geometry is to engineer the ITO interface for effective electron collection.182 Several highly transparent interfacial materials have been developed to modify the ITO layer for efficient electron collection, such as Ca,183 ZnO,184,185 TiO2,186,187 Cs2CO3,188,189 and some polymer materials including polyethylenimine ethoxylated (PEIE).190 In addition, a hole-transporting and electron-blocking layer (e.g. transition metal oxides, MoO3,143,191 WO3,146,192 V2O5,193 and PEDOT:PSS194,195) is inserted between the top electrode and active layer to improve hole selectivity and avoid possible damage to the active layer during electrode deposition.196198 With the design of new small bandgap materials, efficiencies of the inverted OPV devices have been steadily improved, from P 3.0% (P3HT:PC61BM)184,187,188 and P = 6.1% (poly[N-9-hepta-decanyl-2,7-carbazole-alt-5,5-(4,7-di-2-thienyl-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)] (PCDTBT):PC71BM)199 to P = 8.5% (polydithienogermole-thienopyrrolodione (PDTG-TPD):PC71BM).109 Based on the inverted device architecture, You et al. recently built an inverted tandem OPV device that has a record efficiency of P = 10.6% for polymer PVs.102 Furthermore, such inverted OPV devices show relatively higher stability compared to conventional devices. As compared by Hau et al., a 20% degradation in device efficiency was observed for the un-encapsulated P3HT:PC61BM inverted device after stored in air for 40 days, whereas the normal reference device was very unstable and was totally degraded after 4 days.200

The top illuminated inverted device geometry requires the deposition of a transparent or semi-transparent conductive electrode on top of the active layer(s). Glatthaar et al. used a PEDOT:PSS/Au grid layer as the top semi-transparent electrode and an Al/Ti bottom cathode and demonstrated an inverted P3HT:PCBM device with a maximum efficiency of P = 1.4%.201 Hsiao et al. reported a similar inverted device with the highly conductive PEDOT:PSS as the top electrode to increase the efficiency to 2.4%.197 Cao et al. used an oxide/metal/oxide trilayer structure as a good ITO substitute for OPV devices, the transparency and conductivity of which are tunable by varying the choice of metal and layer thicknesses in the trilayer stack.202 With this semi-transparent electrode, a maximum efficiency of up to 2.5% was achieved for an OPV device based on P3HT:PCBM blends.202 Moreover, such top illuminated devices can be fabricated on different types of surfaces, either transparent (glass and plastics) or opaque (stainless steel foils) substrates. For example, a top illuminated P3HT:PCBM device was fabricated on a stainless-steel substrate with sputtered ITO and Al grids as the top transparent electrode showing the efficiency of up to 3.0%.203

However, searching for top transparent electrodes is still one of the biggest challenge for the top illumination devices. Besides the similar requirements as that of the bottom transparent electrode, such as the high transparency, low resistivity and good mechanical properties, the deposition processes for the top transparent should also be take into consideration to minimize the possible damage to the underlying active layer(s). There have been several successful materials developed for the top transparent electrodes, e.g. thin metal films,202 metal grids,204 and conductive polymers,205 however, the relatively poor electrical and optical properties limit the overall device performances. Solution-processed metal nanowires,206208 carbon nanotubes,209,210 and graphenes211,212 have also emerged as attractive transparent electrodes for large-area OPV cells, although significant efforts are still needed to develop better processing techniques for large-scale deposition of these transparent electrodes on top of organic active layers while maintaining good film properties.

3.5 Tandem devices

Although many low bandgap materials have been developed to harvest more infrared photons, it is often offset by the reduced absorption of the shorter wavelength visible photons due to the relatively narrow absorption spectral range of those materials, typically about 200 nm in wavelength. Tandem photovoltaic cells consisting of two or more sub-cells with complementary absorption stacked together have been designed to overcome this absorption limitation. The most common tandem cells are based on a two terminal monolithic geometry, in which two or more sub-cells are connected in series by a charge recombination zone (CRZ) where photo-generated electrons from one sub-cell recombine with holes arriving from the sub-cell on the opposite side of the CRZ. Therefore, the current is continuous throughout all sub-cells (I = I1 = I2 = ), whereas the voltage of the tandem device is the sum of all sub-cell voltages (V = V1 + V2 +), where the subscripts, 1, 2, , denote the individual sub-cells.213

To build an efficient tandem cell, the charge recombination zone connecting the sub-cells needs to be transparent to allow light to reach the sub-cell(s) further from the light source, while allowing the effective recombination of electrons and holes transported from the opposite sides of the CRZ.214218 The first two-terminal tandem cell was demonstrated by Hiramoto et al., where two identical sub-cells were connected by a thin Au layer.219 The poor transparency of the CRZ in that device did not lead to higher efficiency than a single cell. Later, Yakimov et al. demonstrated that using an ultrathin (<1 nm thick) Ag layer, in the form of distributed Ag nanoclusters, was sufficient for efficient holeelectron recombination and voltage addition, without attenuating the light passing through.220 The plasmonic effect of the Ag nanoclusters also led to an enhanced optical intensity in the surrounding region, resulting in an enhanced absorption and more than doubling the efficiency in a two-cell tandem device.221 However, for bulk-heterojunction sub-cells, the thin metal interlayer, which is connected with both the donor and acceptor domains in the mixed active layers, yields poor selective extraction of holes or electrons. This is solved by combining carrier/exciton blocking layers with the thin metal layer as the CRZ interlayer, which not only provides sufficient recombination sites but also prevents the losses of excitons and opposite charge carriers. As reported by Xue et al., a CRZ layer comprised of Ag nanoclusters sandwiched between a 4,4,4-tris(3-methyl-phenyl-phenyl-amino)triphenylamine (m-MTDATA) hole-transport layer and a PTCBI electron-transport layer was used in a CuPc/C60-based tandem device, showing a P 5.7%.214 Additionally, highly doped organic layers have been developed as the CRZ contact for small molecular tandem devices in order to avoid the utilization of metal layers or clusters that may interrupt the organic evaporation. Also, the high carrier mobility of those highly doped layers enables the possibility of using a rather thick interlayer in the tandem cell that can improve the optical management.216,222 This will be discussed in more detail in the next section. With an optimized interlayer and active layer thicknesses, a certified efficiency of up to 6.1% was achieved by Riede et al. for a tandem device with a 2.0 cm2 active area.223

The advantages of vacuum deposition make it rather straightforward to fabricate small molecule based tandem devices. However, it is more challenging to fabricate tandem cells using solution process as the integrity of the underlying layers may be affected by the solvent used for the deposition of overlayers. Prior to all solution-processed tandem cells, researchers had been focusing on so-called hybrid tandem cells with one solution-processed polymer-based device and one vacuum-deposited small molecule-based device that would have a broad coverage of the solar spectrum.224,225 The first demonstration of a tandem cell with two identical solution-processed sub-cells was conducted by Kawano et al. using sputtered ITO as the connection layer with relatively low device efficiency.226 An important breakthrough in this area was the demonstration of a transparent TiOx/PEDOT:PSS interlayer by Kim et al.,227 which not only serves as an efficient CRZ in tandem cells but physically protect the underlying sub-cell active materials from potential damage during the deposition of the second sub-cell active materials. Based on this interlayer, a maximum efficiency of up to 6.5% was achieved with the low band-gap PCPDTBT:PC61BM and large band-gap P3HT:PC71BM cells as the two sub-cells.227 Similar to a TiOx/PEDOT:PSS interlayer, a solution-processed ZnO nanoparticles/PEDOT:PSS layer was also developed as a good charge recombination interlayer.228 Here, the ZnO nanoparticles were dispersed in alcohol based solvents, which do not affect the bottom polymer cell. The aqueous PEDOT:PSS was also pH neutralized to avoid reaction with the ZnO. With the proper selection of new active materials that have a higher Voc and a better coverage of the solar spectrum and the optimization of device architecture that balances the current of the two individual cells, the efficiency of the solution-processed polymer tandem PV devices have been rapidly increased.228 A tandem device with diketopyrrolopyrroleoligothiophene copolymer (PDPP5T)/PC61BM and poly[[9-(1-octylnonyl)-9H-carbazole-2,7-diyl]-2,5-thiophenediyl-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole-4,7-diyl-2,5-thiophenediyl] (PCDTBT)/PC71BM as the sub-cells was demonstrated by Gevaerts et al., showing a Voc = 1.44 V, Jsc = 8.8 mA cm2 and P = 7.5% under 1 sun illumination.217

Furthermore, inverted architectures reviewed in the previous section have also been combined with the tandem structure.215,226 Vacuum deposited MoO3/Ag/Al/Ca was first reported by Sun and co-workers which connected two P3HT:PC61BM inverted sub-cells leading to an efficiency of P = 2.8%.229 Following this, different types of hybrid (needing both the vacuum and solution processes) recombination interlayers, such as MoO3/Al/ZnO and V2O5/Ag nanoparticles/ZnO were demonstrated for inverted tandem devices, which show a minor loss in the Voc addition.230 Most recently, a fully solution-processed inverted PEDOT:PSS/ZnO interlayer was demonstrated by several groups for tandem cells and further improved the device performance.231,232 Using a P3HT:indene-C60 bisadduct (ICBA) front cell and a poly[2,7-(5,5-bis-(3,7-dimethyl octyl)-5H-dithieno[3,2-b:2,3-d]pyran)-alt-4,7-(5,6-difluoro-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole)] (PDTP-DFBT):PCBM back cell, You et al. demonstrated an inverted tandem cell with a certificated P of 10.6% (see Fig. 8).102

So far, tandem structures that combine two sub-cells for broader solar spectrum coverage have been the most successful design for OPV devices to achieve high power conversion efficiencies, which are likely needed to increase the efficiency to meet practical application requirements (laboratory P > 15% and module P > 10%).24 Meanwhile, tandem cells with more than two sub-cells potentially can further increase the spectral coverage and lead to high efficiencies, although it certainly increases the device complexity and manufacturing cost.

4 Optical designs in organic photovoltaic devices

Much of the work in the field of organic photovoltaics to date has been focused on understanding the fundamental photovoltaic mechanisms and in developing new materials and device structures to maximize the light absorption, charge separation and collection and voltage output. While we have some basic understanding of the OPV devices as an optical system, there have been limited attempts to exploit the optical designs to manage the light in-coupling and propagation as means to enhance the overall device performance.

4.1 Optical management in planar, non-textured devices

Typically, an OPV device is fabricated on a flat substrate with no surface texture, and the multilayer stack consisting of the two electrodes, one or more organic active layer(s), and other additional interfacial layers are only patterned to define the overall device area or for electrical connection and packaging purposes. The overall thickness of the multilayer stack is in the order of 100 nm, shorter than or approaching the incident light wavelength. Hence, there is interference between the incident light and the light reflected from the metal electrode within the multilayer stack, leading to a strong intensity variation within the OPV device in the normal direction. Consequently, it is important to optimize the active device structure, including the layer thicknesses and placement of the absorbing materials, to maximize their total light absorption.
4.1.1 Optical spacer in single-junction cells. For conventional OPV devices, the incident light first passes through the transparent electrode and is absorbed by the active layer, and the unabsorbed light is then reflected by the highly reflective metal electrode with a phase shift of 180. The reflected light is coupled with the incident light, leading to the formation of a standing wave and strong oscillation of the optical field within the multilayer structure. With near-zero electric field of the light wave at the metal surface, the optical intensity peaks are located at approximately (2m + 1)/n from the metal electrode surface, where is the wavelength of the light, n is the index of refraction of the active materials, and m is a non-negative integer (m = 0, 1, 2, ) (note that the light wave has a small penetration depth into the metal electrode). The intensity peaks progress further away from the metal electrode with the increase of wavelength. Therefore, it is important to position the active layer of the OPV device in the region with high optical intensity to maximize the total light absorption. Peumans et al. illustrated that a favorable optical intensity profile exists in the CuPc/C60 cells: the C60 acceptor layer, which predominately absorbs in shorter wavelengths (400550 nm), is located closer to the metal cathode, whereas the longer wavelength (550750 nm) absorbing CuPc layer is located further away from the metal cathode.37

The incorporation of a transparent layer between the absorber and the refractive electrode to function as an optical spacer layer has now become one of the most common methods to manage the optical field distribution in OPV devices.37,126,233 As the optical field near the reflecting metal cathode is very weak, any light-absorbing active materials in that region would have minimal contribution to the overall light absorption. On the other hand, inserting a transparent spacer makes it possible to displace the active materials to the region with the highest optical field to maximize light absorption, especially when the active layer thickness is limited due to either exciton diffusion (such as in planar HJs) or charge transport (such as in bulk HJs). Furthermore, the spacer layer could also positively impact the carrier and exciton dynamics in the OPV device and function as a buffer layer to alleviate potential damages to the active materials from the electrodes during deposition or device operation.

Peumans et al. first used a 10 nm thick BCP layer in small molecule OPV devices, which acts as an optical spacer and an exciton-blocking layer, to improve the device efficiency.127 However, the low conductivity and very high LUMO level of such wide gap materials limit the BCP layer thickness to 10 nm. Using Ru(acac)3, whose LUMO level is more aligned with the LUMO of the C60 acceptor, Rand et al. showed that this exciton-blocking layer can be more than 30 nm without negatively affecting the collection of photo-generated electrons by the cathode, thus providing a greater opportunity for optical management optimization.129 Doped layers are also used as the optical spacer to achieve high electrical conductivity, which can be more than 100 nm thick with negligible loss in electron transport. The doped materials are widely utilized in small molecule-based tandem devices, which not only serve as good carrier transporting layers but also provide the high possibility to optimize the absorption of the two individual cells.222,234236

Solution-processed metal oxides, such as TiOx and ZnO, have also been used as optical spacers in solution-processed OPV devices, which provide good electron contact and are sufficiently transparent in the visible and near infrared spectral regions.132,133,186 As an example, Gilot et al. simulated the optical electric field distribution in a P3HT:PC61BM devices with a ZnO layer as the optical spacer (Fig. 9(a)), and calculated the corresponding device current assuming an 100% internal quantum efficiency.233 As shown in Fig. 9(b), the effect of ZnO layer on device absorption is related to the thickness of the photoactive layer. For active layers of 70 to 130 nm thick, the insertion of the ZnO spacer shifts the active layer away from the maximum of the optical field, resulting in a decrease in Jsc. However, for active layers thinner than 50 nm, the inclusion of the ZnO spacer could lead to a strong increase in light absorption and Jsc. For polymernanocrystal hybrid photovoltaic devices, it was also shown that the use of a ZnO nanoparticle layer between the photoactive region and the metal cathode not only improves the device efficiency due to the optical spacer effect and carrier/exciton blocking effect, but also leads to drastically improved device lifetime.120,125,132,133

4.1.2 Optical optimization for tandem cells. The tandem cell structure has proven to be a critical solution to achieve high efficiency for both organic and inorganic semiconductor based PV devices.237 Stacking two or more sub-cells enables a broad coverage of the solar spectrum and more complete absorption of the incident photons. One important difference for organic and inorganic tandem solar cells is the arrangement of absorbers with different bandgaps in the light path. For inorganic tandem cells, the absorber bandgap decreases along the light path; i.e. the front sub-cell (or the sub-cell closest to the light source) has the highest bandgap and the back sub-cell (or the sub-cell furthest from the light source) has the lowest bandgap. In this way, the front sub-cell harvests short-wavelength photons but remain transparent to longer wavelength photons, allowing them to reach the subsequent sub-cells and be harvested. For organic tandem cells, such a bandgap arrangement requirement is not necessarily needed due to the relatively narrow absorption spectral range for typical organic semiconductors; i.e., a low-gap organic semiconductor, while absorbing long-wavelength photons, may possess a reasonably broad window for the transmission of short-wavelength photons. Due to the optical interference effect (see above), the longer-wavelength light has peak intensities at locations further away from the reflecting metal cathode. Hence, it may be more desirable to position the longer-wavelength absorbing (or lower-gap) active materials in the front sub-cell, in complete opposite to their position in an inorganic tandem cell.

Early organic tandem cells were actually built with same absorbers in the sub-cells. As described earlier, the short exciton diffusion length in organic electronic materials limits the layer thickness in planar and bulk HJ cells, leading to a trade-off between using a thin layer to achieve high internal quantum efficiency and using a thick layer to absorb the incident light more completely. Stacking multiple thin cells in the tandem cell architecture is therefore an effective way to circumvent this trade-off. Yakimov et al. demonstrated more than doubling of efficiency when two or more very thin CuPc/PTCBI planar HJ cells (each <20 nm thick) were stacked in tandem cells.220 For the more efficient CuPc/C60 planar-mixed HJ structures, stacking two such sub-cells still led to an increase of the efficiency from 5% to 5.7%,214 although the relative increase is much lower as the same donoracceptor material combinations were used in both sub-cells. Nevertheless, the complementary absorption spectra of CuPc and C60 was exploited in the planar-mixed HJ structures to create an asymmetric tandem cell to maximize light absorption, in which the front sub-cell has a thicker CuPc layer to achieve more longer-wavelength absorption whereas the back sub-cell has more C60 for more shorter-wavelength absorption.214

The full potential of the tandem cell will have to be realized with different donoracceptor material pairs in the sub-cells, achieving complementary light absorption in different spectral regions. Regardless, with a large number of different layers in the tandem structure, optimizing the thicknesses of all layers is essential to maximize the optical absorption and to control the electrical coupling among the sub-cells. Similar to that of the planar single-junction cells, the reflected light from the reflective metal electrode coupled with the incident light results in an optical field redistribution within the multi-layer stack. Therefore, optical modeling of the tandem cells allows optimizing the active layer thicknesses of the two sub-cells to determine the obtainable device current.217,228 Gevaerts et al. demonstrated a solution processed polymer tandem solar cell using small bandgap polymer PDPP5T:PC61BM and wide bandgap polymer PCDTBT:PC71BM as the two sub-cells.217 Using an optical model, together with the thickness dependent JV and IQE of the single devices, the power conversion efficiency of such a tandem device was calculated as the function of the layer thickness of both cells. Assuming no losses in the charge recombination layer, a maximum power conversion efficiency of up to P = 7.1% was found, when the front PCDTBT:PC71BM device and back PDPP5T:PC61BM device have the active layer thickness of 170 and 120 nm, respectively, which is consistent with the experimental device with ZnO and pH-neutral PEDOT:PSS as the intermediate connection layer.217

Furthermore, the intermediate connection layers, the charge recombination zone, for the sub-cells in a tandem device may also play an important role to tune the optical field distribution within the sub-cells, provided that they are highly transparent and conductive. The thicknesses of such connection layers lead to a significant change in the short circuit current and the corresponding device efficiency for the tandem device due to the strong optical interference within the whole structure. As schematically illustrated in Fig. 10(a), a combination of p-doped N,N-diphenyl-N,N-bis(4-(N,N-bis(naphth1yl)-amino)-biphenyl-4-yl)-benzidine (p-DiNPB) and n-doped C60 (n-C60) layers was employed by Schueppel et al. as the charge recombination zone for small molecule tandem devices, the thickness of which could be more than 300 nm without compromising the open-circuit voltage and fill factor of the devices appreciably.236 A maximum short-circuit current of up to Jsc = 5.0 mA cm2 and a power conversion efficiency of P = 5.6% were achieved when a 165 nm thick spacer was used between the two sub-cells (Fig. 10(b)), which displaces the front sub-cell to the second interference maximum and maximizes the overall photocurrent. As a comparison, a similar tandem device with a 60 nm thick spacer only generates a photocurrent of Jsc of 3.0 mA cm2.223

4.2 Light trapping configurations

As mentioned in Section 2, the external quantum efficiency of an OPV cell is determined by the efficiencies associated with light absorption, exciton diffusion, charge transfer, and charge collection processes. There is a fundamental trade-off between the light absorption efficiency (A) and the internal quantum efficiency (IQE). According to the BeerLambert law, a thicker active layer leads to more complete absorption of the incident photons, whereas a thinner active layer generally improves IQE, either by increasing the probability of an exciton diffusing to a DA interface for dissociation (i.e. ED, such as in a planar HJ cell) or by increasing the probability for charge carriers to travel to an appropriate electrode and be collected (i.e. CC, such as in a bulk HJ cell), or both. As reviewed in the previous sections, there have been several approaches to ameliorate this trade-off and improve the device efficiency, including the synthesis of new active materials with enhanced carrier mobilities and the control of favorable active layer morphologies with high exciton diffusion and/or charge collection efficiency.

An alternative method is to involve new optical designs to increase the optical path lengths of the incident light inside the OPV cell (i.e. the optical thickness) without increasing the physical thickness of the active materials. For normal incident light on a typical OPV cell with a reflecting electrode, the optical path length is twice the active layer thickness. The unabsorbed light escapes the device in the opposite direction of light incidence. Using appropriately designed optical structures to re-direct or even trap the incident light inside the device, it is possible to increase the optical path length to beyond twice the film thickness. Here, we present a few recent demonstrations of such optical management approaches for OPV devices.

4.2.1 Folded device architectures. A V-shape tandem device with two individual cells combined in a folded geometry was proposed by Tvingstedt and collaborators in 2007.238240 As shown in Fig. 11(a), the unabsorbed light from the first cell will be reflected towards and absorbed by the next cell. Due to the multiple reflection and absorption in this V geometry, the total optical path length within the active layers of the two individual cells is highly enhanced. At small entrance angles, almost all the light can be trapped within the structure and eventually absorbed by the active layers. Also, similar to the conventional tandem device, active materials with different absorption range can be used in this geometry to have a broader coverage of the solar spectrum. The two individual devices in this tandem structure can be either series or parallel connected, both of which exhibit about two-fold power conversion efficiency at a small entrance angle of 20, compared to the planar reference cells. Rim et al. calculated the absorption by the active layer and the corresponding device current as a function of the V-fold opening angles.241 The current of the devices is highly enhanced with narrowing the opening, which quantitatively agrees with the above experimental result. They also demonstrated the use of such a V-shaped geometry in devices with many different active materials, including small molecule CuPc/PTCBI cell, polymer P3HT:PCBM cell and thin film amorphous (a-Si) and microcrystalline silicon (c-Si) solar cells. According to the optical simulation, about 30% to 300% increases in the short circuit current are obtained for devices with the above active materials, depending on the absorption coefficient and the layer thickness of the active layers (Fig. 11(bd)).

After annealing, the stamp is then separated from the device, leaving behind the textured active layer. With periodic Al reflectors or gratings, incident light could be diffracted backwards according to the diffraction equation: m = npsind, where is the wavelength of incident light, n is the refractive index of the active layers, p is the grating period, d is the diffraction angles, and m is the diffraction order (0, 1, 2, ). Experimentally, Na et al. presented the P3HT:PC61BM layer with such soft lithographic method with both one- and two-dimensional structures (p = 500 nm).243 Compared to P = 3.6% for the planar reference cell, the textured devices show an optimized efficiency of P = 4.3% due to the increased light absorption.

4.2.3 Light trapping in planar devices. The optical management techniques described above are built upon non-planar substrates or active films, which may affect the electrical characterization of the device and limit their compatibility with large-area, high throughput, and inexpensive processing techniques. Light trapping systems that have the optical elements separated from the thin-film devices have been proposed to enhance the light absorption without affecting the internal optoelectronic properties of the devices.

An array of microlenses combined with a self-aligned array of apertures in a metal film was applied to OPV device to induce the light trapping within the active layer.244 The incident light is focused by the microlenses, which passes through the apertures, and is subsequently confined between the metal film (the top mirror) and the bottom reflective metal electrode of the OPV device, resulting in multiple optical passes through the active layer and thereby increasing the total light absorption. As reported by Tvingstedt et al., both the spherical and cylindrical lens were assembled with an alternating polyfluorene copolymer (APFO Green 9)/PC71BM device, which show a 25% increase in the short circuit current compared to the reference cell without the light trap.238 A drawback for this approach is that it only works for normal incident light. For titled incident light, the focal point could be outside the apertures of the top mirror, resulting in significantly reduced or no light through the apertures. This is not suitable for PV modules without a solar tracking system. In addition, the complicated lithographic processes used increase the device fabrication cost and limit the compatibility with large area manufacture.

A rather simple optical design was demonstrated by Myers et al., by directly molding a transparent polymer microlens array on the device substrate, which can also remedy the angle dependent limitation.245 When it strikes the microlenses, the incident light will be deflected by the curved microlens surface, resulting in an increased path length within the active layer and thereby an enhanced device performance, as shown in Fig. 13(a). The close-packed, hemispherical microlens array (see Fig. 13(b)) was fabricated using a low-cost soft lithographic method that can be easily scaled up for large-area devices.246248 Myers et al. demonstrated that applying this microlens array method to several different material systems (small molecule, polymerfullerene, polymernanocrystal) and device platforms (planar HJ, bulk HJ) led to a 15%50% relative enhancement in the PV cell efficiencies.245 For example, the power conversion efficiency under 1 sun 1.5G solar illumination of a small molecule-based SubPc/C60 device was increased from P = 3.1% to P = 3.7% with the microlens array attached, a 19% relative enhancement (Fig. 13(c)), whereas the efficiency of the poly(benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b]dithiophene)-(5,6-difluoro-4,7-dithien-2-yl-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole) (PBnDT-DTffBT):PCBM cell was increased from P = 6.2% to P = 7.0%. Moreover, such efficiency enhancement for devices with the microlens arrays was present regardless of the light illumination angles, due to the reduction of reflection at high-incident angles, suggesting their great application in organic solar cell systems.245

Another light trapping design was demonstrated by Cao et al., in which rear pyramidal reflectors were integrated on semi-transparent thin-film OPV devices (Fig. 14(a and b)).55 With the pyramidal reflectors, the incident light passes through the active layer due to the total internal reflection at the front deviceair interface. However, the non-ideal surfaces of the pyramids and the possible absorption in the transparent electrode limit the overall enhancement. These above examples, which are limited in number and have varying levels of success in efficiency improvement, do indeed open up a new direction for the further improvement of OPV device performance. Besides lenses and pyramids, there are other possible optical structures that can be utilized to change the incident light travel directions and hence enhance the total optical path length. Furthermore, a robust simulation method should be employed to predict the possible enhancement for devices with external optical structures as well as the angle-dependent enhancement. To find a suitable material for these optical structure is another challenge for this technique. Materials used for fabricating such optical structures need to be fully transparent to minimize the optical loss when light is travelling through the structure and to be robust to sustain any damage during the device fabrication process and normal operation periods. The refractive index of optical structure properties should also be taken into consideration, since it determines the light travel direction and hence, influences the optical path length enhancement. Last, but not least, more work is needed to develop suitable methods to scale up the production of the external elements on different substrates, which should also be compatible with the high-throughput roll-to-roll OPV fabrication lines.

4.3 Plasmonics for OPV devices

Another method for achieving light trapping in thin-film PV devices is to utilize surface plasmon resonance (SPR) in metallic nanostructures. Surface plasmons (SPs) are electron oscillations excited by either photons or electrons at the metaldielectric interfaces, associated with well-confined surface electromagnetic waves.249 With proper engineering of the metal structures, such confinement can lead to an enhanced electromagnetic field at the metaldielectric interfaces, which has been utilized in many applications, such as SP optical antennas,250 surface enhanced Raman scattering,251 and plasmonic lasers.252 For the PV applications, incident light can be concentrated around metallic nano-clusters that can improve the active layer absorption, and thereby increase the device performance.253,254

Metal nanoparticles can be embedded in different layers of the OPV devices, either in carrier transport layers or organic active layers.255258 As reported by Lee et al., Au nanoparticles were doped in the PEDOT:PSS layer of the P3HT:PCBM device to induce the plasmonic enhancement for the active layer.80 The power conversion efficiency for the device with a low concentration of Au nanoparticles was improved from P = 3.04% to P = 3.65%, although only slightly enhancement in Jsc of the device was observed. Most of the P enhancement came from a higher FF, which was attributed to the efficient charge dissociation in devices with metal nanoparticles due to the strong coupling between the plamons and excitons in the P3HT:PCBM device.80

Instead of being embedded in the carrier transport layer, the metal nanoparticles have also been mixed into the organic active materials, which scatter the incident light and increase the total optical path length within the active layer (Fig. 15(a)).255 As demonstrated by Wang et al., doping 40 nm size Ag nanoparticles into a PCDTBT:PC71BM active layer with a concentration of 1 wt% led to an up to 10% enhancement in Jsc (from 10.79 mA cm2 to 11.61 mA cm2) and P (from 6.3% to 7.1%) (Fig. 15(b)).256 Kim et al. also demonstrated well-dispersed Ag nanowires with a diameter of 110 nm and length of 5.08.0 m in P3HT:PC61BM blends to introduce the SPR enhancement.259 With the Ag nanowires, the optical absorption by the P3HT:PC61BM layer was increased by approximately 20% in the spectra range of 330 nm to 650 nm, leading to an increase of Jsc from 8.25 mA cm2 to 9.28 mA cm2.259

In summary, metallic nanostructures, either randomly dispersed or periodically built in OPV cells, may improve the photoactive layer absorption due to the plasmonic effects. Generally, there are three different ways to utilize the plasmonics.249,261 Such nanostructures can be applied at the front surface of devices as the scattering elements to improve the optical path length in active layer(s). They can also be embedded in transport/active layer(s) to generate localized surface plasmons and improve the absorption by the active material. The third position is the back metal/active layer interface, which will couple the photons to surface plasmon polaritons and propagate into the active layers. Although there have been a few experimental and computational evidences for these approaches, many fundamental physical mechanisms are not systematically understood. The size, shape, concentration and material properties of the metallic nanostructures are key factors for the plasmonic enhancement in OPV cells. Moreover, these nanostructures are incorporated either in the transport/active layer(s) or at the related interfaces of OPV cells, which can affect the charge carrier movements within the layers. Thus, it is also important to minimize the possible loss mechanism, such as the exciton quenching and charge carrier recombination in cells. Tandem cells are the most efficient organic based PV devices; however, there are few examples showing the incorporation of the plasmonic geometries to improve the device efficiency. With proper structure design and material selection, the plamonic-enhanced tandem OPV cells can be further improved.

Conclusions & outlook

In the last two decades, the field of organic photovoltaic materials and devices has expanded enormously and attracted researchers from many disciplines such as materials science, chemistry, physics and electrical engineering. With the rapid development in this field, the community now has a better understanding of the key factors limiting the OPV cell performance. The development of new materials, in terms of enhancing voltage output, solar spectrum coverage and morphology understanding, etc, has been the subject of several other reviews. This review article focuses on advances in device architectures and optical designs that impact OPV device performance.

The three-dimensional spatial distribution of the donoracceptor heterojunction strongly affects the transport and dynamics of photogenerated excitons and charge carrier in an OPV device. Aside from the basic planar and bulk HJ structures, other novel device architectures have been demonstrated, including double (planar) HJs, planar-mixed HJs, and controlled bulk HJs, all of which target an improved balance between potentially conflicting mechanisms. OPV devices based on inverted structure that reverses polarity of charge collection can use the high work function metals as the top electrodes, which can not only minimize the device degradation but also reduce the fabrication costs and complexity. Furthermore, tandem devices that stack two or more individual cells together to obtain a broader coverage of the solar spectrum have been widely explored to achieve the highest power conversion efficiency.

In addition to the optimization of the internal factors that affect the OPV device performance, managing the light wave propagation within the multi-layer stacks through internal and external modifications has also shown some success in improving OPV device performance. The interference-induced strong intensity variation inside the multilayer stack can be exploited to maximize light absorption, especially with strategically located transparent spacers. External optical designs, such as V-shaped geometries and molded microlens arrays and pyramids, have been applied to OPV devices to manipulate the light propagation and/or induce light trapping within the active layers, resulting in prolonged optical path lengths and more complete absorption of the incident light by the active layer without physically increasing the layer thicknesses. By embedding metal nanostructures into the active layers, the optical absorption may also be increased as a result of the strong plasmonic effect at the metalorganic interfaces.

With the above optimization strategies, the maximum power conversion efficiency of OPV devices has exceeded 10% in laboratory cells24,62,102,237 and 78.5% in modules.237 Besides the design of novel organic semiconductors to have a broader solar spectrum coverage for high Jsc and well-aligned energy levels for high Voc, controlling the phase separation of the donoracceptor blends for efficient exciton dissociation and charge transport should be further explored. The complex interplay between molecular properties and processing conditions makes it difficult to predict the bulk heterojunction morphology. Often minor modifications to the molecular structures and/or changes in the processing conditions lead to significant changes in film morphology and device performance. This presents significant challenges for quickly assessing newly developed materials, but also may result in quality control issues during the manufacturing process. It could be very challenging to find active materials that simultaneously have optimal optoelectronic properties (energy gap, ionization potential/electron affinity, charge mobility, etc.) and can yield optimal morphological structures in thin films (especially in bulk heterojunctions). Thus, it may be desirable to design self-assembly material platforms to control the film-forming characteristics of the active materials and make them less dependent on the necessary changes in molecular structure for the optimization of the optoelectronic behavior.

Furthermore, the external optical components need be integrated with devices with efficient materials and optimized structures to obtain higher efficiencies. A useful optical approach should be compatible with large-scale, roll-to-roll manufacturing of OPV devices/modules without incurring a significant additional cost. Both microscopic geometric structures and plasmonic nanostructures have shown promise for inducing light trapping and enhancing light absorption. When evaluating the effectiveness of a particular approach, we need to consider the possible variation in the light incident direction. If a certain method only works well with normal or near-normal incident light, but not all incident angles, the usage of such method will be limited to OPV devices with a sun-tracking system, which more likely will not be the case in real-world applications of large area OPV modules.

Last, but not least, the stability of OPV devices is a critical hurdle that should be tackled before their commercialization. Although OPV devices with a lifetime over seven years have been demonstrated,261 a more robust encapsulation technology, as well as more detailed study about the degradation mechanism, is required to improve the device stability. In particular, the phase separated bulk heterojunction structure generally is not at equilibrium; thus its morphological stability during operation may be a concern that needs to be better studied.
