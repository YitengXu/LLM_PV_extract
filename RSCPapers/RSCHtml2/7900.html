Abstract

Metal-catalyzed chemical vapor deposition (CVD) growth of graphene is one of the most important techniques to produce high quality and large area graphene films. Apart from the outstanding electronic and optoelectronic properties of the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene, its wetting behavior also possesses great potential that can be exploited for various applications such as water harvesting, micro–nano fluidics, triboelectricity generation, electrowetting, oil–water separation, biomimetics, etc. In this review article, we discuss the most recent advancements in the wetting behavior based applications of the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene. The focus is mainly on the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene as it offers better control over the growth of graphene films as compared to other techniques. Moreover, only those wetting behavior based applications are discussed where liquid droplet motion and actuation, and its stable hydrophobic/superhydrophobic state on graphene's surface are exploited. The article starts with a brief introduction of graphene, its metal-catalyzed CVD growth, and surface energy. Thereafter, it is mainly divided into two sections, viz., (1) important concepts and results related to liquid wetting behavior on the surface of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene and (2) liquid droplet wetting behavior based applications such as omniphobic/hydrophobic/superhydrophobic 3D graphene foam based filters for oil–water–gas separation, biomimetic surfaces for anti-corrosion and droplet transfer applications, water harvesting and defogging, triboelectric nanogenerators, electrowetting-based liquid lenses, and electro-active flow control devices. Finally, conclusions and outlook of this emerging research field are presented. This article aims to provide up-to-date information about the rapidly emerging research area of wetting behavior based applications of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene.

1. Introduction

The discovery of graphene led to remarkable progress in the field of two-dimensional (2D) materials and its wide variety of applications in the era of nanotechnology. Graphene is a thin mono-atomic sheet of sp2-bonded carbon atoms arranged into a 2D honeycomb lattice.1–3 It is a basic building material of all graphitic forms such as zero-dimensional (0D) fullerenes, one-dimensional (1D) carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and three-dimensional (3D) graphite, as shown in Fig. 1a.1 Graphene is also known as a single layer of graphite and its electronic structure rapidly evolves with the number of layers and reaches the 3D limit of graphite at 10 layers.1–3 The aggregates consisting of 2, 3 to <10, and between 2 to about 10 well-defined, countable, stacked graphene layers of extended lateral dimension are called bi-layer graphene (BLG), few-layer graphene (FLG), and multi-layer graphene (MLG), respectively.1–3 Graphene is well known for its application in new electronic devices owing to its unique structure and extraordinary properties such as high charge-carrier mobility.1,4 To date, various techniques have been adopted to produce graphene such as mechanical exfoliation, chemical exfoliation, chemical synthesis, chemical vapor deposition (CVD), etc.1,3–12 However, for the large-scale production of high quality graphene, the metal-catalyzed thermal CVD method is superior amongst them.10 The CVD technique has been used to produce not only a bulk amount of polycrystalline graphene films on transition metals,9–11 but also single crystalline graphene films.7–9 Generally, Cu and Ni are used to catalyze graphene growth in a CVD system, but Cu is considered as the ideal candidate for producing high quality single layer graphene (SLG) on its surface, which is attributed to its low carbon solubility as compared to Ni.13,14 Besides Cu and Ni, other transition metals such as Mo, Pt, Co, Au, Rh, Pd, Ti, W, and Ta15–25 have also been used for the CVD growth of graphene. Moreover, a variety of metal alloys such as Cu–Zn, Ni–Ti, Cu–Ni, Pt–Rh, Co–Cu, Ni–Mo, Cu–In,26–32etc., have also been used to achieve graphene growth via CVD. It was observed that the properties of the as-grown graphene films such as purity, quality and the number of layers were dependent on the catalyst used. Growing metal-catalyzed graphene via CVD is a promising approach and facilitates several unique advantages including controllability, scalability and feasibility for fundamental research and practical applications.33,34 However, difficulties still exist in achieving the growth of graphene on industrial scale. Apart from its excellent opto-electronic and thermal properties, the water–graphene interactions have also been studied both theoretically and experimentally.35–41 The water–graphene interaction is an important research area which needs to be explored further if graphene is to be used in conformal coatings.

The interaction of a liquid with a solid surface is described by the contact angle (CA), θC. The ability of a liquid to wet the solid surface depends on the interfacial tensions between the solid–liquid–gas phases. The behavior of a liquid droplet on a chemically homogeneous, rigid and ideally flat solid surface, as shown in Fig. 2a and b, is defined by its intrinsic CA which is also a measure of the wettability of the solid surfaces. In the equilibrium state, the intrinsic or apparent CA of a liquid droplet can be expressed in terms of interfacial tensions given by Thomas Young in 180542 by balancing the interfacial forces at the three phase contact line:

 
γSG = γSL + γLGcosθC(1)
where θC is the intrinsic or apparent CA and γSG, γSL, γLG are the solid–gas, solid–liquid, and liquid–gas interfacial tensions. When γSG = γSL + γLG, complete wetting of the surface will be observed, whereas for γSG < γSL + γLG, a finite CA will be observed. So, on the basis of θC values, the surfaces are defined as hydrophilic (θC < 90°, γSG > γSL) and hydrophobic (θC > 90°, γSG < γSL) as shown in Fig. 2a and b, respectively. In reality, ideal surfaces as mentioned above do not exist and chemical heterogeneities and roughness are always present on the surface. For these kinds of real or rough and chemically heterogeneous surfaces, two models were proposed by Wenzel43 and Cassie–Baxter44 by modifying the original Young's equation as given in eqn (2) and (3), respectively:
 
cosθw = rcosθC(2)
 
cosθCB = φs(cosθC + 1) − 1(3)
where, in eqn (2), θw is the apparent CA in the Wenzel state, θC is the Young's CA on the same but smooth surface, and r is the roughness factor, the ratio between the actual surface area and the apparent surface area of a rough surface. In eqn (3), θCB is the apparent CA in the Cassie–Baxter state, φs is the fraction of the solid substrate in contact with the liquid droplet. The Wenzel model suggests that the liquid droplet fills the gaps on the rough surface and is completely pinned (shown in Fig. 2c), whereas the Cassie–Baxter model suggests that on a rough and chemically heterogeneous surface, a liquid droplet partially sits on the top of solid asperities and the trapped air underneath as shown in Fig. 2d. The Cassie–Baxter model seems to be more appropriate to understand the wetting behavior of micro/nanostructured surfaces as such surfaces can trap large amounts of air and form continuous micro/nano air pockets. On such surfaces, the apparent CA exceeds 150° which is known as superhydrophobicity, as originally exhibited by lotus leaves. It is generally achieved by creating highly rough fractal surfaces and multiscale hierarchical micro/nano structures and further reducing the surface energy by chemical modifications.

Similarly, the water–graphene interactions could also be studied in terms of water CA (WCA) and surface energy.45–48 Wang et al. determined the surface energy of graphene sheets produced through chemical exfoliation of natural graphite flake and hydrazine conversion.45 At ambient conditions, the graphene sheet was hydrophobic in nature with a surface energy of 46.7 mJ m−2. Similarly, at ambient conditions, Shin et al. also observed that a single layer epitaxial graphene (EG) on SiC substrate was hydrophobic in nature with a WCA of ∼ 92.5° as shown in Fig. 3a.46 On the other hand, Kozbial et al. observed the hydrophilic behavior of a freshly prepared CVD grown SLG on a Cu substrate.47 However, as the ambient air exposure time increased, its WCA also increased (as shown in Fig. 3b), which was attributed to the adsorption of airborne hydrocarbons on the SLG surface. The initial high surface energy of the freshly prepared SLG continuously decreased as the adsorbed airborne hydrocarbons shielded the polar surface sites. In contrast, very high surface energies of γ = 115 ± 4 mJ m−2 and γ = 119 ± 3 mJ m−2 were found for Cu-catalyzed CVD grown SLG and FLG, respectively, when directly measured in dry nitrogen with the help of graphene–graphene pull-apart experiment where pristine graphene-coated surfaces with a smooth topography brought into contact, and the force required to separate them was measured (Fig. 3c and d).48 Hence, irrespective of the graphene film preparation method and due to the contamination of graphene surface by airborne hydrocarbons, the initial high surface energy of graphene would always decrease with ambient air exposure time, and consequently its WCA would increase.47,49,50

Since pristine graphene is a gapless and semi-metallic material, its interaction with water is dominated by van der Waals interaction.51–53 Graphene remains non-invasive to the substrate/water interface and transparent to the substrate wetting behavior as van der Waals force controls the wetting on surfaces of metals such as Cu, Au, etc.52 This effect is known as the wetting transparency of graphene. However, when graphene sits on a substrate, the water contact angle (WCA) can be influenced by the number of graphene layers, graphene–substrate interaction, and the nature of the underlying substrate.51–53 Graphene is intrinsically hydrophilic with a WCA of ∼45°.54,55 Moreover, it is also hydrophilic in water as CAs of liquids having different polarities allow the transparency towards polar and dispersive interactions.56 On the other hand, the pristine SLG film behaves as a flexible impermeable membrane to water, standard gases, and other liquids.57–60 But the unavoidable defects present in the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene, such as nanometer-sized holes and cracks primarily concentrated on graphene wrinkles, cause water permeability.58–60 Furthermore, creating randomly etched nanoscale apertures and pores in the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene could help in the permeation of water molecules and could be used for water desalination and purification applications.58,59 However, this is difficult to achieve because the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene exhibits hydrophobic behavior at ambient conditions as the hydro-carbonaceous adsorbates of ambient origin impart hydrophobicity to it by creating local hydrophobic spots sufficient to mask its intrinsic hydrophilicity.47,49,50,61 Therefore, it is more convenient to exploit the hydrophobic or superhydrophobic behavior of the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene for practical applications. As the underlying substrate can play a major role in the wettability of graphene, graphene-based surface-coatings can be employed for micro/nano fluidics, water transport, water harvesting, triboelectricity generation, and electrowetting applications where liquid/droplet motion or actuation is required on its surface.62–67 Similarly, wetting behavior of the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene coupled with its excellent thermal and opto-electronic properties has been exploited for fabricating defoggers and liquid lenses, where it is used as a transparent conducting electrode.68,69 Moreover, it has also been exploited for applications such as fabrication of 3D graphene foam based filters for oil–water separation and biomimetics.70,71

Here, in this review article, we discuss the most recent advancements in the wetting behaviors and applications of the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene. This research area is still developing and less explored so far. Graphene research area is very broad and a number of good reviews are already available on the water–graphene interactions and applications.39–41,72–78 However, these review articles are mainly focused on theoretical and experimental results related to wettability and fabrication of hydrophobic/superhydrophobic surfaces based on graphene and graphene/carbon-related materials such as graphene oxide (GO), reduced graphene oxide (rGO), CNTs, carbon nanofibers (CNFs), etc., prepared by different methods.39–41,72–78 Moreover, some of them are also focused on the applications of graphene and graphene/carbon-related materials but mainly on water desalination, water purification, and oil/water separation applications.72–78 On the other hand, our focus is mainly on the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene as it offers better control over the growth of graphene films as compared to other techniques. Moreover, only those wetting behavior based applications are discussed where liquid droplet motion and actuation, and its stable hydrophobic/superhydrophobic state on graphene's surface are exploited. The article starts with a brief introduction of graphene, its metal-catalyzed CVD growth, and significant discussions about the surface energy of different graphene films and their interactions with water. Thereafter, it is mainly divided into two sections, viz., (1) important concepts and results related to liquid wetting behavior on the surface of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene and (2) liquid droplet wetting behavior based applications such as omniphobic/hydrophobic/superhydrophobic 3D graphene foams for oil–water–gas separation, biomimetic surfaces for anti-corrosion and droplet transfer, water harvesting and defogging, triboelectric nanogenerators, electrowetting based liquid lenses, and electro-active flow control devices. Finally, conclusions and outlook of this emerging research field are presented.

2. Wetting behavior of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene

Rafiee et al. in 2012 studied the wetting behavior of transferred graphene on different substrates such as Cu, Au, Si, and glass.52 It was observed that the intrinsic wetting behavior of surfaces did not vary significantly by using graphene coatings because surface–water interactions were dominated by van der Waals forces. Moreover, SLG was observed to be wetting transparent on Cu, Au and Si. SLG films were synthesized via CVD on Cu foils using hexane as the carbon precursor, and then transferred onto Si and Au substrates, respectively, followed by static WCA measurements. Fig. 4a shows the WCA on Si and on an ∼300 nm thick Au film on Si, and found to be ∼32.6° and ∼77.4°, respectively, for uncoated SLG. However, SLG coating resulted in only ∼1–2% increase in the WCAs. Fig. 4b shows the wetting experiment results on Cu substrates with a varying number of SLG layers. WCA increased by ∼0.7% and found to be 86.2° for SLG with the WCA of baseline Cu being ∼85.9°. WCA increased to ∼86.6° for 2–3-layer graphene, and beyond four graphene layers, the WCA increased sharply to ∼88.3°. Finally, after six graphene layers the WCA saturated at ∼90.6° (bulk graphite value). These results confirmed the wetting transparency of the underlying substrate for SLG coatings. The wetting transparency of graphene on Cu was explained in terms of how the graphene layer affected the adsorption energy of water on the Cu substrate. A water molecule adsorbed on the Cu substrate interacted with neighboring Cu atoms within the interaction range. When a SLG was inserted between the Cu substrate and water molecule, only the uppermost layer of Cu was replaced by carbon while essentially keeping the neighboring Cu atoms the same (Fig. 4c). With a varying number of graphene layers, the adsorption energy for Cu substrates only differed by ∼0.4% with or without the SLG coating (Fig. 4d). As the number of graphene layers increased, the adsorption energy decreased monotonically and saturated at approximately four layers.

The hypothesis of wetting transparency of graphene breaks down at extreme cases of underlying substrate wettabilities as proposed by Shih et al. (in 2012) while explaining the complexities associated with the wettability of graphene.53 It was predicted and experimentally demonstrated further that the metal-catalyzed CVD grown SLG film was only partially transparent to wetting, and the highest attainable WCA on a SLG-coated surface was 96°. The deviation of WCA on SLG coated substrates as compared to that of bare substrates was observed to be more significant on superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic substrates, and the wetting transparency could be decreased for MLG by further screening the effect of the underlying substrate. In this way, the wetting transparency of graphene could break down significantly on superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic substrates. Furthermore, Raj et al. in 2013 studied the effect of the underlying substrate and the number of graphene layers on wettability by performing dynamic CA measurements unlike in previous cases.51 It was observed that the unavoidable defects present in CVD grown transferred graphene affected the receding CA on different substrates, which resulted in a significant CA hysteresis (16–37°). However, the advancing CA remained unaffected irrespective of the number of graphene layers. CVD grown SLG films on Cu foils were transferred onto glass and ∼300 nm thick thermally grown oxides on Si to obtain mono-, bi-, and tri-layered graphene sheets.

Raman spectra confirmed that as the number of graphene layers increased, the ratio of I2D/Ig increased (Fig. 5a). During the liquid addition phase, the CA measured on MLG on a thermally grown SiO2 surface was found to be 93 ± 2° (Fig. 5b). Due to evaporation, the CA decreased once the liquid addition was stopped (t = 26 s), whereas between t = 26 and 78 s, the contact line remained pinned by the negligible decrease in the droplet base radius. The receding CA on the MLG surface was found to be 60 ± 3° after t > 78 s, indicating a significant CA hysteresis (Δθ > 30°). Similarly, for the bare SiO2 substrate, the advancing and the receding CAs were observed to be 41 ± 1° and 10 ± 2°, respectively, as shown in Fig. 5c. For all nine graphene samples (mono-, bi-, and tri-layered graphene on Cu, silica, and glass), three bare substrates and highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG), the dynamic CA measurements are summarized in Fig. 5d–f. As shown in Fig. 5d, the advancing CAs reached the value on the bulk HOPG with the addition of just one layer of graphene on Cu, silica and glass substrates in spite of significant differences between the wettability of these bare substrates (θA,Cu = 0° and θA,SiO2/glass = 41°). The number of layers of graphene sheets and the underlying substrate strongly affected the receding CA as observed from Fig. 5e. It was higher for graphene on the Cu substrate and consistently increased with the number of graphene layers on glass and SiO2 substrates. Hence, it was observed that the wettability of 3D bulk graphite was matched by that of Cu, glass and silica substrates coated with graphene, while only static WCA measurements for studying wetting behavior on these kinds of surfaces are not sufficient.

In 2016, Hong et al. proposed and demonstrated further that chemical doping (such as fluorination) could also tune the wetting behavior of graphene by shifting its Fermi level.79 A DC voltage bias was used to establish this as electrical voltage doping is more controlled and similar to chemical doping. SLG films were grown on Cu foils via CVD, and transferred onto SiO2/Si substrates. All the SLG/SiO2/Si samples were annealed in a 10% H2/Ar atmosphere at 500 °C for 1 h to eliminate polymeric residue and thereafter stored at ambient conditions for 1 week to saturate the SLG surfaces with carbon species. A Cu wire was connected on the SLG surface using silver paste to form the top electrode, and the Si substrate acted as the bottom electrode. At 0 V, the static WCA on the SLG surface was found to be 88° which confirmed the saturation of SLG surface with adsorbed hydrocarbons (Fig. 6). The Fermi level moved upward with respect to the Dirac point at −100 V, which was equivalent to n-doping of SLG. This led to a decrease in the WCA which was found to be 78° (Fig. 6). Similarly, at +100 V, the Fermi level moved downward with respect to the Dirac point, which was similar to p-doping of SLG, and the WCA was found to decrease further to 60° (Fig. 6). The WCA values remained constant when the applied negative and positive bias voltages were off. It was inferred that the intrinsic hydrophilicity of SLG is actually related to its Fermi level state and should not be attributed to charge trapping at the interface between the adsorbed hydrocarbons on SLG and water.

Furthermore, Ashraf et al. demonstrated that substrate doping-induced charge carrier density modulation could lead to the tunable wetting behavior of graphene.80 The WCA could vary up to 13° when the work function (WF) was modulated by 300 meV. Graphene on Cu foil was synthesized via CVD using methane (CH4) and then transferred onto a polyelectrolyte-coated SiO2/Si surface by the modified graphene transfer technique as shown in Fig. 7a. A peristaltic pump injected and replaced the etchant solution with deionized (DI) water to obtain free-standing graphene floating on DI water. After injecting the polyelectrolyte into the water, the same pump was used to lower and place the graphene on top of the SiO2/Si substrate, trapping polyelectrolyte in between. The WCA was measured on the micrometer scale by environmental (E)-SEM image analysis, and due to the thermally conductive nature of graphene, the chance of electron beam heating-induced droplet shape alteration was reduced significantly (Fig. 7b). The E-SEM and scanning Kelvin probe microscopy (SKPM) results demonstrated that chemically doped graphene exhibited a smaller WCA than nominally un-doped graphene on the same SiO2 substrate (Fig. 7d). Compared to that resulting from polyelectrolyte-induced doping, doping by bare SiO2 substrate was found to be negligible. Depending on the polyelectrolyte type (electron donor or acceptor), graphene's Fermi level shifts could result in either p- or n-type doping, which influenced the WCA (Fig. 7c). Both the n-doped graphene [by high molecular weight (HMW) poly(allylaminehydrochloride) (PAH) and poly-L-lysine (PLL)] and the p-doped graphene [by HMW poly(sodium 4-styrenesulfonate) (PSS) and poly(acrylic acid) (PAA)] exhibited lower WCA by up to 13° (Fig. 7d) as compared to un-doped graphene on a SiO2 substrate. Moreover, a lower WCA was observed at higher doping levels. On the other hand, surface wettability of Cu-catalyzed CVD grown SLG can be controlled by treating it under tetrafluoromethane (CF4) plasma as reported by Lim et al.81 The CF4-plasma treated graphene for 20 s exhibited hydrophobic behavior (CA ∼ 104.9°) as compared to hydrophilic pristine graphene (CA ∼ 66.7°). Chemisorption of fluorine on graphene surface after CF4-plasma treatment resulted in durable and stable hydrophobic properties.

Additionally, the wetting behavior of graphene can also be changed by directly growing or transferring it onto rough or textured substrates. Zhao et al. studied the wetting behavior of Cu catalyzed CVD grown MLG by transferring it onto a micron-scale hole array (MSHA) SiO2/Si textured substrate.82 The transferred graphene on a MSHA substrate was hydrophobic (CA ∼ 93.1°), whereas it exhibited hydrophilic behavior (CA ∼ 83.0°) on a planar SiO2/Si substrate. This was attributed to the fact that the MSHA substrate had a regulation effect on the wettability of graphene only when it completely complied with the morphology of the MSHA substrate surface. This technique can pave the way for designing graphene coated surfaces with controlled wettability. Xia et al. developed an optical visualization approach for SLG with the help of water vapor on a wide variety of substrates [Cu, Si, HF-treated Si, SiO2/Si, quartz, glass, polyethylene terephthalate (PET), polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), and micropatterned PDMS] by combining the wetting properties of SLG and water condensation.83 It was observed that the edge of SLG acted as a confining wall which blocked the outside water droplets, while enabling the inside water droplets to move out. Thus, a graphene domain looked like an island which enabled the optical visualization of graphene. Seamless SLG and BLG or MLG domains were grown on Cu foils in an atmospheric pressure CVD (APCVD) system by using different flow rates of CH4 for different times.

A schematic of the continuous condensation process of water droplets and its merging on and around SLG/Cu is shown in Fig. 8a, and its corresponding optical images are shown in Fig. 8b–i which clearly depicted the evolution of water droplets. The merging of water droplets inside a graphene domain is shown in Fig. 8d and e, and the merging of a water droplet on the graphene into a nearby droplet on the surrounding substrate is shown in Fig. 8f–i. As the condensation of water vapors increased, an obvious difference between graphene domains and the bare substrate area were observed both in terms of color contrast and in terms of the morphology of water droplets. Due to the difference in wetting properties of graphene and the underlying substrate, optical visualization of graphene domains with water became possible. HF-treated Si exhibited an absence of graphene domains even with the assistance of water vapor as shown in Fig. 9a and b. But graphene could be observed for a naturally formed oxide layer on pristine Si with the assistance of water vapor as shown in Fig. 9d and e. The WCA of the HF-treated Si substrate (∼81.7°) closely matched the WCA of the SLG coated substrate (∼82.2°), which led to a morphology similar to that of water droplets on graphene and bare substrates. The WCA of the pristine Si substrate was 42.2°, whereas the WCA of the SLG coated substrate was 79.7°. The dispersion and morphology of water droplets on such surfaces are schematically depicted in Fig. 9c and f, respectively. Fig. 9g shows the WCAs on various substrates with and without SLG coating, whereas HF-treated Si and Au substrates were not applicable for the water-assisted optical visualization approach. 3. Wetting behavior based applications of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene

3.1. 3D-graphene foams for oil–water separation

In order to fabricate an adsorption material for oil–water separation, the adsorbent must be hydrophobic/superhydrophobic and oleophilic in nature such that it can adsorb oil but repel water at the same time. The adsorption capacity (C) is one of the most important performance criteria for adsorbent and defined as
 
C = Mf − Mi/Mi(4)
where Mi and Mf are the weights of the adsorbent before and after adsorption, respectively. The adsorption capacity directly depends upon the specific surface area, porosity and density of the adsorbent. Generally, 3D carbon based foams, sponges and aerogels have interconnected pores which help to increase the specific surface area, and can provide a large number of adsorption sites and paths for oils and organic solvents. So far, the sponge and aerogel filters based on GO have mainly been used for oil–water separation.75,77,78,84,85 However, graphene foam (GF) based filters fabricated with the help of metal-catalyzed CVD process are more controllable in terms of porosity, surface roughness and wettability, and can also be used as filters for separating oil and water as well as gas–oil–water.70,86,87 Hydrophobic/superhydrophobic GFs are mainly prepared via CVD by using 3D Ni foam as a template.70,86–89 To date, very few reports are available on GF based filters for oil–water separation and the important results are discussed in this section. For example, in 2014, Singh et al. fabricated a novel macroscopic graphene structure composed of an integrated foam like network of graphene sheets with well controlled microscale roughness and porosity.70 The graphene foam was first synthesized by using a sacrificial Ni foam via template directed APCVD with CH4 as the precursor at 1000 °C, and thereafter the Ni foam was removed by chemical etching.88 A continuous 3D network of graphene was formed over the entire Ni foam scaffold with thin interconnected sheets of graphene having no physical breaks or interfaces (Fig. 10a and b). The walls of the foam were composed of monolayer to FLG sheets with strongly suppressed defects related D-band which confirmed high quality of graphene foam as revealed by Raman analysis (Fig. 10c).

These 3D GFs had an extremely low density of about 5 mg cm−3, a very high porosity of about 99.7% which was close to that of the lightest aerogel (2–3mg cm−3), and a very high specific surface area of about 850 m2 g−1.70,88 Thus, they fulfill all the important criteria to be an excellent adsorption material. In order to increase the hydrophobicity, the graphene foam was coated with a ∼200 nm thick layer of Teflon. The Teflon coated graphene foam exhibited a WCA of ∼163.01° (Fig. 10d) for advancing fronts and ∼143.78° for receding fronts (Fig. 10e). On the other hand, the advancing and receding WCA on the uncoated graphene foam was found to be ∼134.86° and ∼110.72°, respectively (Fig. 10f and g). The Teflon coated foam was much more hydrophobic and the difference of ∼30° between advancing and receding WCA (CA hysteresis) was due to the high surface roughness and micro-scale porosity, which could cause pinning of the liquid front particularly during the receding of the droplet. Hence, 3D GFs modified with low surface energy coatings can exhibit outstanding water repellency, and thus possess great potential for use in oil–water separation applications.

Similarly, in 2015, Bong et al. also adopted the same approach to fabricate 3D GFs as discussed above, and practically used them as filters for separating not only oil and water but also gas.86Fig. 11a show a schematic diagram, photograph (diameter = 25.4 mm; thickness = 2 mm) and field emission (FE)-SEM image of a 3D GF. A 3D structure with pores of 150–200 μm diameters was observed, and the graphene-coated area (Fig. 11a inset) exhibited a perfectly laminated and rough surface. They prepared three different types of GFs with the help of surface modification. The omniphilic GF was obtained by surface O2-plasma treatment by creating defects on the surface (Fig. 11b). The pristine 3D GF was hydrophobic in nature (Fig. 11c), but it became omniphobic (Fig. 11d) after coating with self-assembled (heptadecafluoro-1,1,2,2-tetrahydrodecyl)trichlorosilane (HDF-S). The –OH groups on the graphene surface and the active group (trichlorosilane-SiCl3) of HDF-S formed covalent bonds and resulted in a strong siloxane network build-up. These three different types of 3D structural GF based filters were successfully tested for selective filtering of gas, water and oils.

3.2. Biomimetics

There are several examples from nature which possess almost perfect micro–nano structures such as lotus leaves which exhibit superhydrophobicity and self-cleaning effect and butterfly wings and bird feathers that exhibit unique hydrophobic properties and beautiful structural colors.90 However, these kinds of biomimetic surfaces can also be fabricated by using high quality continuous graphene. These graphene coated biomimetic surfaces are mainly used to enhance the chemical stability of metal or alloy surfaces.63,91–93 Metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene coatings either directly grown or transferred onto textured metal/alloy substrates can easily mimic these examples from nature (lotus leaves, rose petals, butterfly wings, etc.), and can also be used for anti-corrosion and micro-droplet transfer applications.63,71,92,93 For example, Jiang et al. fabricated hierarchical structured FLG films directly on laser textured Cu foils via template directed CVD by using CH4 at 1020 °C.71 They used femtosecond laser pulsing for fabricating multi-scale hierarchical micro–nano structures on Cu foils, which mimicked butterfly wings (Fig. 14a–d). The iridescence of the FLG film transferred onto a cover glass confirmed the presence of periodic nanostructures (Fig. 14d). Due to the high surface energy of Cu and Cu oxide, the polished Cu surface exhibited a hydrophilic property; however, the laser-textured Cu surfaces exhibited a WCA of ∼90°. The WCA values were found to be in the range of 106–152°, which confirmed the hydrophobicity/superhydrophobicity of all the hierarchical-structured FLG films on textured Cu foils (Fig. 14e). One sample exhibited the highest WCA of 152° when the laser intensity was as high as 200 mW. A structural color was observed when light irradiated the biomimetic FLG films, like the butterfly wings.94Fig. 14f shows iridescence of FLG on Cu foil without corrosion and Fig. 14g shows camera captured iridescence of FLG on glass substrates.

3.4. Triboelectric nanogenerators (TENGs)

Electricity can be produced when ions-containing water or droplets of ionic liquid move over graphene/SiO2/Si or graphene/polymer heterostructures.113–116 The liquid flow enabled electricity generation is based on an electro-kinetic phenomenon, where an electric potential is created by moving ionic liquid on a thin-film electrode. This is also known as liquid flow enabled triboelectricity generation. Moreover, creating an electric potential irrespective of electrode material is based on the formation of the electric double layer (EDL) boundary at the electron/ionic liquid interface, where the electron migration takes place during the rapid electrode insertion into or its pulling-out of the ionic liquid. Based on this, Guo and co-researchers demonstrated the generation of a few millivolts of voltage by moving a liquid droplet of sea water or ionic solution on the SLG/polyester terephthalate (PET) heterostructure.114 They obtained high quality Cu-catalyzed LPCVD grown SLG films by using CH4 as the carbon precursor. The SLG film in the form of a strip (∼5 × 110 mm2) was transferred onto a PET substrate, and metal wires were connected to the terminals of the SLG strip to form an ohmic contact (Fig. 18a). Thereafter, a droplet of simulated sea water (0.6 M NaCl aqueous solution) was sandwiched between the SLG and a 5 × 5 mm2 SiO2/Si wafer. The SiO2/Si substrate was placed 1 mm above the SLG and moved along a track at fixed separation. The droplet motion on the SLG with advancing and receding CAs (θA and θR) of ∼91.9° and ∼60.2°, respectively, could be controlled by moving the SiO2/Si substrate (Fig. 18a, inset). Fig. 18b shows that a voltage of ∼0.15 mV was generated by moving (from the left to right end) a 43 mL droplet of 0.6 M NaCl solution on the SLG strip with a constant velocity of 2.25 cm s−1. The generated voltage sharply dropped to zero as the droplet stopped, and the same magnitude voltage with reversed sign was generated as the droplet moved back with the same velocity. It was observed that the generated voltage increased linearly as the velocity of the droplet increased, and its magnitude could be enhanced by increasing the size of the droplet. The voltage generation mechanism was explained with the help of density functional theory (DFT) calculations. A hydrated Na+ ion could be adsorbed on the SLG with an adsorption energy of over 2 eV, and a hydrated Cl− anion was repulsive due to its negative adsorption energy as shown in Fig. 18c. The surrounding water molecules impeded the direct interaction between the SLG and ions, and the adsorbed hydrated Na+ ions drew image electrons from the SLG. This contributed to a significant electron accumulation on the upper surface of the SLG as depicted by the distribution of the differential charge (Fig. 18c). A thin electron accumulation layer extended along the upper surface of the SLG and formed a pseudo-capacitor with the adsorbed Na+ layer as the number of adsorbed hydrated Na+ increased. The formation of pseudo-capacitance was significant, which was attributed to the very small interlayer distance (only ∼0.4 nm) between the positive hydrated Na+ layer and the negative electron layer. Fig. 18d shows that for a static NaCl droplet, there was no potential difference between its left and right sides as the charge redistributed symmetrically on both sides of the droplet on the SLG. However, for a moving droplet, the pseudo-capacitor advanced forward and drew electrons in the SLG as ions were adsorbed at the front end. At the same time, the pseudo-capacitor discharged and released the electrons to the SLG as ions were being desorbed at the rear end of the moving droplet (Fig. 18e). This resulted in an increase/decrease in electron density behind/ahead of the moving droplet, and led to a higher potential at the front end than at the rear end (Fig. 18e). On the other hand, no detectable potential was obtained for deionized water droplets even at a velocity of 8.55 cm s−1, as the pseudo-capacitance could not be formed due to the insufficient ions. Moreover, other ionic solutions were also tested and it was found that the voltage generated by three droplets of NH3·H2O and MgCl2 was ∼2.6 and ∼1.5 times, respectively, as compared to the same number of NaCl droplets, which confirmed that these droplets could draw larger amounts of image electrons. Furthermore, the generated voltage was reduced as the number of graphene layers increased because they provide additional channels for electrons to flow back from the rear end to the front end.

The voltage of the signal output could be reversed to ∼0.45 V (right) by reversing the moving direction of the droplet. The typical current output was ∼4.5 μA (left) and ∼4.8 μA (right) according to the direction in which the droplet was moving (Fig. 19c). Fig. 19b shows the load resistance dependence of both the voltage output and the current output with the resistance ranging from 10 kJ to 1 GJ using a single droplet. The output voltage was increased and the output current was decreased by increasing resistance, and thus the maximum output power was evaluated to be about 1.9 μW at 50 MJ (Fig. 19d). The output voltage for BLG and tri-layer graphene (TLG) was also studied and corresponded to 1.3 mV and 1.2 mV, respectively (Fig. 19e). It increased linearly with an increase in the size of the droplet (Fig. 19f). However, once the droplet achieved constant movement in one direction, the cations inside the droplet accumulated at one side ahead of the droplet. The uniform balance of surface charges in the SLG was broken due to the alteration of cations, and this started to generate the relative potential difference at both ends of the droplet. The potential difference of the droplet was maintained until it reached the end of the SLG, and electrons were constantly drawn when the droplet had a fixed moving speed (Fig. 19i). A continuous voltage output (∼0.25 V) was generated by rain droplets due to the continuous contact between the inclined SLG/PTFE hetero-structure and the sliding water droplets. Furthermore, three individual SLG/PTFE hetero-structures were connected in series and parallel to enhance the output voltage and output current of 1.1 V and 8 μA, respectively. In this way, a huge amount of electric power can be generated by using appropriate series and parallel connections of several SLG/PTFE hetero-structure based devices.

In addition to this, in 2017, Lin's group used polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) substrate to generate triboelectricity from a moving liquid droplet on SLG/PVDF hetero-structure.116 In this case, they demonstrated the generation of output voltage even by using de-ionized water droplets unlike previous cases where only ionic aqueous solutions were used.64,113–115 The graphene growth and transfer processes were the same as mentioned above.64,114 A SLG strip (2 × 6 cm2) was transferred onto a 30 μm thick PVDF piezoelectric film, and the fabricated triboelectric nanogenerator was fixed on a slope inclined at 30° (Fig. 20a). Driven by its own gravity, a 0.2 mL droplet of DI water, tap water, and 0.1 M NaCl aqueous solution, respectively, could easily move over the SLG surface. During the droplet motion, the generated voltage increased with time, and when the droplet reached the bottom end of the SLG strip, it decreased sharply to its initial value (Fig. 20b). The peak voltages of around 1.6 mV, 7.5 mV, and 15.5 mV could be generated by using droplets of DI water, tap water, and 0.1 M NaCl aqueous solution, respectively (Fig. 20b). The possible mechanism of voltage generation based on triboelectric effect for DI water or polar liquids was discussed. Fig. 20c shows that electrical charges appeared on the surface of PVDF film due to the pressure exerted by the water droplet when dropped onto the SLG/PVDF substrate. At the same time, opposite charges in the SLG was attracted to the water-contacted area. Due to the very short screening length of SLG (∼0.6 nm), the corresponding ions in the droplet were also dragged and accumulated at the water–SLG interface to screen the piezoelectric charges. There was a symmetrical charge distribution when the droplet was static, but there was no potential difference. For a moving water droplet, piezoelectric charges were generated at the front end and the opposite charges in the SLG along with corresponding ions in the droplet were drawn to the front end to balance the piezoelectric charges. As the motion of ions or water molecules is much slower as compared to SLG, more carriers in the SLG were drawn at the front end of the water-contacted area to temporarily screen the piezoelectric charges. On the other hand, piezoelectric charges disappeared at the rear end and released the carriers and ions back, but the motion of ions was much slower which led to a relative accumulation of ions at the rear end. This resulted in repelling of the induced charges away from the rear end of the droplet contact area (Fig. 20d). Hence, the carriers attracted to the front end of the droplet were released towards the rear end which flowed across the SLG under the droplet, and thus created a potential difference between the two ends of the droplet (Fig. 20e). In order to minimize electrolysis of a liquid droplet, a dielectric layer is introduced between the substrate and the hydrophobic/superhydrophobic coating.69,131,134 This is known as EW on dielectric (EWOD), and the concept can be used for practical applications such as liquid lenses, micro/nano-fluidics, lab-on-a-chip devices, etc.69,131,134 In 2012, Tan et al. performed EWOD experiments using transparent, flexible, and stretchable metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene.65 Growth of graphene was carried out using CH4 on a Cu foil substrate. The graphene was then transferred onto PET films, and coated with a thin Teflon layer. The PET film served as the transparent, flexible, curved, and 3D substrate which helped to demonstrate EWOD. A voltage was applied between the droplet and the Teflon coated graphene electrode, and the CA at 0 V was found to be 109.5° ± 1°. The CA was decreased to 70.2° ± 1° as the voltage increased to 90 V, and beyond that no further decrease was observed. This was attributed to the instability of the applied electric field as the high density of the charge carriers was injected into the insulator which led to the CA saturation.135,136 Furthermore, reversible EWOD on graphene was also demonstrated on flat and curved substrates by applying both DC and AC (10 Hz to 1 kHz) voltages.

Similarly, for the first time, Shahini et al. from the same research group fabricated very high resolution (∼456 lp mm−1) tunable ionic liquid lenses based on the EWOD concept using graphene as a transparent electrode.69,134 Cu catalyzed CVD grown SLG was transferred onto a flexible parylene/PDMS substrate. The patterned SLG was used as a transparent and flexible electrode material which allowed a large lens aperture (diameter = 2.4–2.74 mm). The substrate was deformed with a 3 mm radius of curvature and the CA modulations of a 3 μL KCl droplet to ac voltages (0–120 V) was studied. In addition to this, some research groups have also performed EW experiments on the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene. In 2016, Lomax et al. demonstrated an ultra-low voltage EW directly on HOPG conducting substrates by using a variety of aqueous liquid electrolytes.130 Following this, Ounnunkad et al. from the same group performed EW experiments directly on CVD grown graphene transferred onto SiO2/Si substrates.144 As compared to the results obtained for HOPG, no significant wetting response was seen for CVD graphene (Fig. 22a). This was attributed to the presence of polymeric residues from the growth substrate and the presence of grain boundaries due to the high polycrystalline nature of CVD graphene (AFM Fig. 22b), which ultimately resulted in pinning of liquid electrolyte droplets. Recently, Park et al. demonstrated a novel scalable direct patterning method of graphene by simply using water and electricity without vacuum.66 Graphene was synthesized via the typical CVD method on a 35 μm thick Cu foil using CH4 as precursor, and then transferred onto SiO2/Si substrates. An aqueous electrolyte solution (1 M KCl) was used for CA measurements and anodic configuration, and the electrolyte droplets (5 μL per drop) were directly dropped onto graphene using a micropipette (Fig. 22c). The applied bias voltages were scanned from 0 V to 3 V with a 0.2 V step and from 0 V to −4 V with a −0.2 V step. Initially, the CA was in the range of ∼70° to ∼80° at zero applied voltage. The CAs of the electrolyte droplets on graphene decreased, and the radius increased as the applied bias voltage increased (Fig. 22d). The CA variation was relatively small and symmetric for the bias voltage less than 2 V. However, the CA was dramatically changed for the bias voltage higher than 2 V. At the room temperature of 24 °C and relative humidity of 44%, the CA was reduced by the evaporation of the liquid droplet (Fig. 22e). Unexpectedly, without any change in the in-plane radius, the CA of the droplet on graphene reduced (Fig. 22e). Pinning occurred at the edge of the droplet due to the relatively high surface tension at the triple point of graphene–liquid–air.145 On the other hand, when the graphene acted as a cathode, the variation rate of the CA (per voltage) was relatively high compared to that of graphene anodic configuration. It was also observed that the CA variation was not reversible. Although these kinds of behaviors have already been observed on HOPG and CVD graphene,130,144 it clearly suggests that at high voltages, the CA variation does not follow the Young–Lippmann's relationship.130,132,133,146 At the triple point of solid–liquid–air interface, the liquid droplet spread on the graphene surface and permeated between the graphene and the substrate. As the bias voltage increased with time, the permeation of small liquid droplets increased. Due to this, a significant stress developed at the graphene blisters which caused the fracture of the graphene at the weakest bonding sites most probably at pentagon–hexagon bonding sites or impurity sites. In this way, by applying a bias voltage, the intercalation of water under graphene at the triple point could be controlled effectively. Also, a physical or chemical modification (oxidation) may take place depending on the bias polarity. Based on these observations, three different graphene patterning techniques were realized (Fig. 22f–h). A ring shaped patterning and functionalization of graphene could be achieved by controlling the droplet size and bias polarity (Fig. 22f). Similarly, pattern transfer on graphene using a pre-patterned photoresist (PR) pattern and direct patterning or writing of graphene by simply dragging a droplet on graphene using an electrically charged tip could be achieved (Fig. 22g and h).

Furthermore, for the first time, the potential of direct EW on metal-catalyzed CVD graphene coatings was exploited for application in electroactive flow control devices by Tabassian et al.147 They exploited two antagonistic functions, hydrophobic repellency versus liquid permeability, for electroactive flow control with the help of EW on graphene-coated metal meshes. Here, they used electrical stimuli to control the shapes of liquid droplets and the degree of water penetration through the graphene-coated meshes. Additionally, two active flow devices demonstrating the dynamic locomotion of water droplets and electroactive flow switching were developed. FLG growth was carried out by heating Ni wire mesh substrates at 1000 °C and flowing CH4 gas (Fig. 23a). Also, an etching process was employed to obtain free-standing and hollow FLG meshes (Fig. 23a). The fabricated graphene-coated nickel mesh (GCNM) was comprised of a unique rough surface with periodic topographical feature, and a water droplet exhibited a Cassie state when placed on it.44,148 The water droplet did not penetrate through the GCNM because of the negative capillary effect created from the liquid–air interface in the GCNM openings. The electrical stimulation of the water droplet was realized by using the fabricated 10 mm × 20 mm GCNM as the negative electrode and using a Cu tape as the positive electrode. The control of the hydrophobic repellency and permeability of liquid droplets on the GCNM were manipulated by electrical stimuli as shown in Fig. 23b.

Firstly, the dynamic movement of water droplets through multi-stage GCNM electrodes was realized by an arrangement of parallel GCNM electrodes in series which were aligned in the vertical direction and having water droplets placed on each mesh (Fig. 24a). The water droplet between the first and second GCNM electrodes was actuated upward and suddenly pulled down the droplet placed on the upper electrode when an electrical field was applied to the first electrode. In this way, sequentially switching the location of the applied voltage between the two GCNM electrodes caused the movement of water droplets from the top GCNM to the bottom GCNM electrodes (Fig. 24a). Eventually, all combined droplets could be accumulated at the bottom GCNM electrode. Secondly, an electroactive flow switcher was realized by functionally utilizing antagonistic control between hydrophobic repellency and liquid permeability (Fig. 24b). A rectangular flow channel standing vertically was used in which a GCNM electrode and a Cu electrode were fixed. Due to the hydrophobic repellency of the GCNM, water was contained above the GCNM electrode in the absence of an electrical field. However, the contained water penetrated through the GCNM on application of an electrical field beyond a critical value. These novel EW and EWOD experiments using metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene as electrode material will prove to be beneficial for various applications such as flexible displays and droplet manipulation in micro/nano fluidics.

4. Conclusions and outlook

In this review article, we have discussed the most recent advancements in the wetting behavior based applications of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene. The process of metal-catalyzed CVD growth of graphene is more controlled as compared to other techniques. Important concepts and results related to liquid wetting behavior on the surface of metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene are presented. A pristine SLG is intrinsically hydrophilic in nature. However, the metal-catalyzed CVD grown graphene films are generally hydrophobic in nature due to the presence of physiosorbed airborne organic contaminants. Hence, they are more suited for a variety of applications based on the hydrophobic or superhydrophobic behavior of liquid droplets. Graphene's stable hydrophobic/superhydrophobic behavior and motion of liquid droplets as well as actuation of liquid droplets on its surface have been exploited for a variety of applications such as omniphobic/hydrophobic/superhydrophobic 3D graphene filters for oil–water–gas separation, biomimetic surfaces for anti-corrosion and micro-droplet transfer, water harvesting and defogging, energy harvesting (triboelectricity generation), electrowetting based liquid lenses and electro-active flow control devices. Oil–water or chemical–water separation has been achieved by using FLG coated hydrophobic or superhydrophobic porous Ni foams. Moreover, robust graphene coatings (SLG/BLG/FLG) either directly grown or transferred onto textured metallic substrates have been exploited for corrosion protection, liquid droplet transfer, and water harvesting applications. Generally, Cu-catalyzed CVD grown SLG films are transferred onto polymeric substrates to fabricate TENGs and liquid lenses where the motion and actuation of ionic droplets are exploited. The graphene/PTFE structure could be a very promising platform to produce large power output using a variety of ionic liquid droplets. The FLG coated metal meshes could be exploited for electroactive flow control device application where electrical stimuli control the shapes of liquid droplets and the degree of water penetration through the meshes. High transparency of SLG coupled with its conductivity enabled its use as transparent conducting electrodes in liquid lenses. Similarly, its high thermal conductivity coupled with high transparency enabled the fabrication of defoggers which could be useful in making smart windows, defogging mirrors, and windscreens. There is a need to further study the performance dependence on the number of graphene layers and the durability of the above-mentioned devices. Furthermore, attempts could be made toward growing metal-catalyst free, high quality and large area graphene films directly on PET, glass, quartz, and sapphire substrates by exploring new carbon precursors and by designing novel CVD systems. This will be helpful in fabricating hydrophobic, transparent, conducting electrodes which can be used for micro–nano fluidics applications. This new emerging research area of graphene possesses a great potential and is attracting many researchers to exploit graphene's wetting behavior and develop novel concepts for device making and applications.
