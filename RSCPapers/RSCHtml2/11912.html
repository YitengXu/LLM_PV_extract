Abstract

Carbon dot (CD) is the youngest member of the carbon based nanomaterials family but it has emerged as the brightest in recent times, as now it is one of the most intensive research topics in the domain of materials science. The popularity is due to its excellent aqueous dispersibility, functionality, biocompatibility and ease of preparation from different carbon based resources. All such favorable attributes help CDs to find applications in the fields of optoelectronic, energy conversion, bio-imaging, drug delivery, sensors, catalysis, etc. Most recently, CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids have been extensively employed for several photochemical and electrochemical applications due to their unique and interesting up-conversion photoluminescence and photo-induced electron/charge transfer properties. Therefore, the recent progress of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids in such applications needs to be compiled in the form of a review for highlighting their benefits to researchers in this field. In the present review, hence, fabrication methods, structural characterizations, properties and applications of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids are highlighted in a concise but systematic way to cover the desired information. Different photochemical and electrochemical applications like photocatalysis, solar cell or photovoltaic, energy storage, and photochemical and electrochemical sensors of the CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids are focused in this article. This review thus tries to provide a decent and updated coverage on the topic.

1. Introduction

Over the last two decades carbon based nanomaterials are gaining significant interest both in academic and industrial scenarios. A number of studies have been reported in the literature on different aspects of carbon based nanomaterials including fullerene as zero-dimensional, multi-walled/single-walled carbon nanotubes, and carbon nanofibers as one-dimensional, and graphene, graphene oxide, and reduced graphene oxide as two-dimensional materials. Recently, carbon quantum dots (also known as carbon dots, C-dots, CDs or CQDs) have emerged as a new class of attractive zero-dimensional nanomaterial in the carbon family. It is one of the youngest but the brightest nanomaterials in the carbon family. CD was first noticed accidentally by Xu et al. during the purification of single-walled carbon nanotubes in 2004.1 It was discovered by them while separating single-walled carbon nanotubes using gel electrophoresis from carbon soot produced by arc discharge. At that time it was named fluorescent carbon. Subsequently, Sun et al. synthesized similar fluorescent carbon nanoparticles with diameter less than 10 nm and the product was named as carbon dot (CD).2 The size range of this oxygenous carbon nanoparticle quantum dot lies within 10 nm and was popularized as carbon or carbogenic or carbon quantum/nanodot in the literature.3â€“8 It is superior to other semiconductor based inorganic quantum dots in terms of its easy and fast aqueous dispersibility, polar functionality, low toxicity, production simplicity, etc.3,4 It possesses fascinating physical, chemical, biological and optical properties, which make it an attractive material in the fields of biomedical science, sensors, optoelectronics, photocatalysis, energy devices and so on.3â€“6,9,10 However, subsequent research clearly demonstrated that the photoluminescence (PL) characteristic of CDs can be quenched efficiently either by electron acceptor or by electron donor molecules in solution. This result clearly indicates that photoexcited CDs can act as an excellent electron donor as well as an electron acceptor. This interesting photoinduced electron transfer ability of CDs offers exciting opportunities for them in light energy conversion devices and in photocatalysis.10 Current research further indicated that CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids can dominate the fields of photochemical and electrochemical devices due to their interesting up- and down-conversion PL characteristics as well as electron or charge transport properties.11â€“18 These hybrid nanomaterials thus find potential applications in the fields of photochemistry and electrochemistry including photocatalysis,9â€“11 solar cells or photovoltaics,10,14â€“16 sensors,9 and energy storage.17,18 Most interestingly, over the last few years although several reviews have been published on the synthesis, characterization, properties and applications (mainly biological and photocatalysis) of CDs, such reviews have not discussed CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids for photochemical and electrochemical applications.3â€“6,8,19,20 The present article, therefore, mainly reviews the preparation, characterization, properties and applications confined to the photochemical and electrochemical fields of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids to provide an updated coverage on the topic to researchers in the field.

2. Preparation of CDs and their metal based nanohybrids

2.1. Preparation of CDs

CDs are prepared by using both top-down and bottom-up approaches. In the top-down method, CDs are prepared by breaking large carbon clusters through chemical oxidation or by physical cleavage. On the other hand, in the bottom-up approach CDs are prepared by using different carbon based precursors through a series of chemical reactions to form nanoscale fluorescent particles.21 Various preparative techniques of CDs by top-down and bottom-up approaches are schematically shown in Fig. 1.

2.1.1. Preparation of CDs by the top-down approach. The top-down approach for the preparation of CDs mainly includes arc-discharge, laser ablation, electrochemical or strong acid oxidation.4 The first synthesis process used for the preparation of CDs was arc discharge. CDs were produced during the purification of single walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) obtained from carbon soot by the arc discharge process.1 Carbon soot is oxidized with nitric acid (to improve its hydrophilicity) followed by separation of SWCNTs by the electrophoretic method to produce CDs. Actually, in this arc discharge process, two new classes of nanomaterials are produced from the crude soot. One of them is the short, tubular carbon nanotubes, while the other one is a mixture of fluorescent carbon nanoparticles or CDs. CDs are also prepared by laser ablation of a carbon target in the presence of water vapor with argon as the carrier gas followed by surface passivation by attaching simple organic species.2 This carbon target is prepared by hot-pressing of a mixture of graphite powder and cement, followed by stepwise baking, curing, and finally annealing under an argon atmosphere. However, researchers used different carbon based materials such as graphite, nano-carbons, etc. to prepare CDs by the laser ablation method.22,23 The electrochemical oxidation method is also used to prepare CDs using graphite as the working electrode along with platinum (Pt) as the counter electrode and Ag/AgCl as the reference electrode in different aqueous electrolytes, like NaH2PO4, phosphate buffer, 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium tetrafluoroborate, etc.24â€“26 Researchers also used a strong acid based oxidation method, where different types of carbon soots obtained from burning candles, combustion chambers, etc. were oxidized by strong nitric acid to produce CDs.27â€“29 In all these processes CDs of different sizes are commonly separated by a high-speed centrifugation technique using a waterâ€“ethanolâ€“chloroform solvent mixture. However, these top-down methods suffer from several drawbacks including the necessity for a series of experiments, need for strong acids or electrolytes, and surface passivating agents for the improvement of fluorescence properties.
2.1.2. Preparation of CDs by the bottom-up approach. To address the drawbacks of top-down methods, researchers attempted to prepare CDs through different bottom-up approaches. These approaches are mainly established for polymerization reactions followed by carbonization of small organic molecules for the formation of quantum size CDs. Hydrothermal,30â€“32 microwave-assisted pyrolysis,33â€“35 ultrasonic,36,37 pyrolysis,38,39 and dehydration/direct heating40â€“43 methods are included in this approach. Carbon containing molecules such as glucose, fructose, sucrose, ascorbic acid, chitosan, glycerol, ethylene diamine tetraacetic acid, etc. are used as carbon based precursors for the preparation of CDs by the above methods. Some inexpensive bio-precursors are also reported for the preparation of biocompatible water soluble fluorescent CDs by this approach.44 Hydrothermal treatment is the most facile and self-passivation based one pot method for the preparation of water soluble fluorescent CDs containing a large number of oxygenous groups. In this method, carbonization of small organic molecules takes place at elevated temperature (150â€“200 Â°C) and pressure in the presence of water.30â€“32 In this vein, a microwave method has also been developed to prepare CDs using carbohydrates as a carbon source and PEG200 as both the solvent and coating agent.33 The reaction medium is irradiated using a 500 W microwave power for 2â€“10 min and the reaction medium gradually transforms to dark brown from a colorless solution. Similarly, an ultrasonic method has also been developed for the preparation of CDs. It is a simple approach for the large-scale synthesis of water-soluble green carbon nanodots from different kinds of food waste-derived carbon resources.37 Again, Lai et al. synthesized highly emissive CDs by the pyrolysis of glycerol under reflux (âˆ¼230 Â°C, 1 atmosphere air pressure) conditions for 30 min.38 In this work, the researchers also prepared PEG-modified CDs using the same procedure. Amphiphilic CDs have also been prepared by Wang et al. using low-cost, natural, edible chicken eggs by a plasma-induced pyrolytic technique.39 These CDs are highly soluble in a broad range of solvents from aqueous to organic, and also resistant to acids and bases. However, the recent trend to prepare CDs is mainly based on bio-precursors, like orange juice,45 banana juice,40 strawberry juice,46 sugarcane juice,47 chicken egg,39 oatmeal,48 silkworm,49 waste paper,50 waste food,37etc. In this milieu, we have also developed a simple synthesis protocol to produce water soluble fluorescent CDs by direct heating of commercially available relatively low cost bio-based raw materials like banana juice, Colocasia esculenta extract, glycerol, citric acid, etc. in aqueous medium.40â€“42

It is pertinent to mention here that in all these bottom-up approaches, the basic mechanism of formation of CDs is the same. It follows hydrolysis, dehydration and decomposition of different carbon precursors under suitable conditions and results in soluble compounds like furfural aldehydes, ketones and several organic acids like acetic, levulinic, formic, etc. The aromatization and carbonization of these products take place through polymerization, condensation and cycloaddition reactions in the subsequent steps. In the final step, CDs are formed from the aromatic clusters at a critical concentration of super-saturation point.40,45

However, due to the presence of poor interactions between the metal nanoparticles and CDs in the nanohybrids obtained by the physical mixing technique, currently, researchers are using chemical reduction and hydrothermal techniques to prepare various kinds of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids as alternative methods.

2.2.2. Preparation of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids by the chemical reduction technique. In this technique metal based nanoparticles are prepared using CDs as the reducing agent. Actually, a large number of peripheral oxygen containing groups like hydroxyl, carbonyl, carboxylic acid and epoxy groups of CDs are capable of reducing metal ions to metals by transferring electrons. The important advantage of this reduction technique is that CDs can also act as a stabilizing agent during the formation of metal based nanoparticles due to the presence of these oxygenous polar functional groups on their surface. Therefore, in most of the cases the growth of metal nanoparticles starts on the surface of CDs to form the nanohybrid. In 2011, Mitra et al. utilized CDs for the first time as the reducing agent to prepare Au and Ag nanoparticles from their corresponding salts in the presence of PEG under microwave irradiation.59 Similarly, carbon-dot-supported silver nanoparticles are prepared by using CDs as the reducing agent for the reduction of silver ions under ultraviolet irradiation.60 In this reduction reaction, electrons are transferred to Ag+ ions from photo-excited CDs under UV light and the color of the solution is changed from yellow to dark brown, which indicates the conversion of Ag+ ions to silver nanoparticles, as shown in Fig. 2b. Shen et al. utilized CDs for the direct reduction of Ag+ to elemental silver (Ag0) by incubating Ag+ with CDs for 5 min in a water bath at 50 Â°C.61 Ag-nanoparticles are simultaneously formed with an average size of 3.1 Â± 1.5 nm and decorated on the surface of CDs. In this process, the oxidation of amine or phenolic hydroxyl groups on the aromatic ring of CDs takes place and at the same time CDs protect and stabilize the Ag-nanoparticles from aggregation in aqueous medium, as shown in Fig. 2c. The prepared Ag-nanoparticles are stable at least for 45 days in aqueous medium. The researchers also prepared Au-nanoparticles by heating aqueous solution of CDs and HAuCl4 at 70 Â°C in a water bath for 10 min.61 Here also Au-nanoparticles are formed on the surface of CDs. Dey et al. reported CD reduced palladium nanoparticles, where CDs acted as a reducing as well as stabilizing agent for the synthesis of Pd nanoparticles.62 The surface hydroxyl, carboxylic and epoxy groups of CDs acted as the nucleation centers for the growth of Pd nanoparticles and CDs formed a thin shielding layer around the surface of Pd nanoparticles. In our previous report, a CD/Cu2O nanohybrid was prepared by simple reduction of CuSO4 solution by CDs at 70 Â°C in the presence of ammonia.63 In this reduction process also CDs acted as a reducing as well as capping agent, and mainly the peripheral polar groups such as hydroxyl and aldehyde of CDs helped to reduce Cu2+ to Cu+. The hydroxyl groups reduced Cu2+ by a polyphenolic mechanism and the aldehyde groups reduced Cu2+ by a Benedict reaction mechanism (the color of the solution changes to reddish brown after addition of the Cu2+ salt due to the formation of Cu2O). On the other hand, Gao et al. employed poly(amido amine) dendrimers capped-CDs as the reducing and capping agents for the preparation of a CD/Au nanohybrid and the reaction was performed at 100 Â°C for 2 h under constant stirring.64 Recently, Zhuo et al. utilized reduced-CDs as the reducing agent for the preparation of metal clusters.65 It is observed that reduced CDs exhibited excellent electron-donating capability thereby facilitating the fast reduction of metal ions to the corresponding metal nanoclusters or nanoparticles. Subsequently, other researchers also utilized CDs as the reducing agent for the preparation of various metal based nanohybrids.66â€“68

In this technique, nanohybrids are prepared by the mixing of aqueous solution of the metals salts with either CDs or carbon precursors of CDs before performing the required hydro/solvothermal treatment. This technique is mainly used to achieve controlled size and shape of the nanohybrids as well as to establish strong interactions between the metal nanoparticles and CDs. 3.2. Properties

The most fascinating features of CDs are excitation wavelength dependent PL behavior, overall biocompatibility as well as water solubility (nanoscale stable dispersibility). PL is the classic signature of CDs. In spite of the diversity in structure, all types of CDs prepared by different approaches from various carbon precursors, exhibit a similar type of PL behavior. Generally, PL spectra of different types of CDs show the highest intense emissive peak in the visible region (400â€“500 nm) at an excitation wavelength of 340â€“380 nm.4,40,45 The most interesting feature in the PL of CDs is the emission wavelength-dependent excitation behavior. Thus the PL emission peak position and its intensity are always related to the excitation wavelength. A large number of studies demonstrated that this type of PL behavior of CDs is mainly controlled by the size of the graphitic core and the functional groups present on the surface, though the exact mechanism is still not very conclusive and hence needs more understanding and clarification.4,84 The quantum size graphitic core controls the PL behavior by the quantum confinement effect through the variation of band gap with the size of the quantum carbon dots.40,45 On the other hand, surface functional groups have different energy levels, which may result in a series of emissive traps. It is assumed that upon excitation of a certain wavelength of light, a surface state emissive trap dominates the emission, while upon excitation of some other wavelengths, other corresponding surface state emissive traps become dominant.4,40,45,84

However, CDs exhibit a broader PL profile and lower quantum yield compared to semiconductor quantum dots. But still they display more fascinating PL behavior in the application point of view due to their photo-induced electron and charge transfer ability, and up-conversion luminescence properties. As the photo-excited CDs can act both as an excellent electron donor and an electron acceptor, the PL intensity of CDs could be quenched efficiently either by electron acceptor or electron donor molecules.4 Yu et al. investigated the transfer efficiency of photo-generated electrons in the CD/TiO2 nanosheets (CD/TNS) nanohybrid by transient photocurrent response measurements,57 as shown in Fig. 9a. The figure shows the photocurrent responses of three types of materials, namely pure TiO2 nanosheets (TNS) and the nanohybrids CD/P25 and CD/TiO2 nanosheets (CD/TNS) and the results are compared. It is observed that the CD/TNS nanohybrid exhibited stronger transient photocurrent response, which is about 3 times larger than that of the CD/P25 nanohybrid, while pure TiO2 did not show any transient photocurrent response. These results clearly indicate that the CD/TiO2 nanosheets nanohybrid exhibited the highest transfer efficiency of photogenerated electrons. Di et al. also carried out the transient photocurrent response analysis for the CD/Bi2MoO6 nanohybrid to study its photo-induced electron transfer process.82 It is noticed that the photocurrent of the CD/Bi2MoO6 nanohybrid is almost three times higher than that of pure Bi2MoO6. This significant improvement of photocurrent response of the nanohybrid revealed that the photogenerated electrons in the conductance band (CB) of Bi2MoO6 are transferred to the CDs.

On the other hand, up-conversion PL is another most interesting optical property of CDs, which makes them a promising material for photocatalytic application, particularly after combining with different types of metal based nanomaterials. Up-conversion luminescence is a process in which the sequential absorption of two or more photons leads to the emission of light at a shorter wavelength than the excitation wavelength. Thus the emission of higher-energy photons occurs upon excitation with lower-energy photons. Cao et al. first introduced the two-photon emission of CDs under irradiation of an argon-ion laser in the visible region (458 nm) or a femtosecond pulsed laser in the near-infrared range (800 nm).85 Later, several researchers also observed up-conversion fluorescence emissions of CDs prepared by different synthesis approaches.42,51,86â€“88Fig. 9b shows the typical PL spectra of CDs excited by long wavelengths of light at 700, 750, 800, 850, 900, 950 and 1000 nm, where the emissions are located at shorter wavelengths of 300â€“650 nm, as reported by Zhang et al.89 The detailed emission energy distribution located in the range of 300â€“530 nm after up-conversion is also compared, as shown in Fig. 9c. It is found that upon excitation in the wavelength range of 700â€“1000 nm, a large distribution of shorter emission wavelengths in the area 300â€“530 nm results, which is much more useful. This up-conversion PL of CDs is very important for different photochemical applications based on metal semiconductor nanohybrids. The up-conversion PL of CDs is employed to transfer lower energy photons into higher energy ones, which can excite the conduction band electrons of the semiconductor materials according to their band gaps.4,51,52 Thus the CD metal based nanohybrids showed excellent photochemical properties compared to the pure semiconductor metal nanoparticles or even pristine CDs.

4.1. Photocatalysis

Photocatalysis is one of the greenest techniques to prepare various substances as well as to degrade different types of anthropogenic organic chemicals. This is also useful for environmental remedy as well as production of H2 and O2 using solar energy. Generally, metal based semiconductor materials are widely used for the same purpose. However, efficiency, cost-performance ratio, toxicity, etc. are the major concerns for such catalysts. Again, the wide band gap (in the UV region) of the semiconductor metals also inhibits the total utilization of the solar energy (UV is only 3â€“4% of sunlight), because visible and infrared parts account for the majority in photocatalysis. Therefore, recently researchers have designed CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids to increase the photocatalytic efficiency using the full spectrum of solar light. This enhanced photocatalytic effect is mainly due to the photo-induced electron transfer and up-conversion PL properties of CDs.

Li et al. designed a TiO2/CD and SiO2/CD nanohybrids based photocatalyst to harness the use of the full spectrum of sunlight, based on the up-conversion luminescence properties of CDs.51 These nanohybrids are used for the degradation reaction of methylene blue (MB) under visible light. It is observed that use of pure TiO2 or SiO2 without CDs as photocatalysts resulted in no or only a very limited amount (<5% for TiO2, <10% for SiO2) of degradation of MB. Thus it is proved that CDs are necessary for the efficient photo-degradation of MB under visible light. In contrast, under the same conditions, it is found that degradation of MB is nearly 0% when pure CDs are used as the catalyst. The results indicate that the excellent photocatalytic activities of TiO2/CD and SiO2/CD nanohybrids might be attributed to the interactions between CDs and TiO2 or SiO2 in the catalyst system. The mechanism of this photocatalytic reaction can be put forward as follows. When the nanohybrid based photocatalysts are illuminated by solar light, CDs absorb visible light and emit shorter wavelength of light (325â€“425 nm) by up-conversion PL, which in turn excites the electrons of TiO2 or SiO2 to generate electron/hole (eâˆ’/h+) pairs. These eâˆ’/h+ pairs react with the adsorbed oxidant/reductant (usually O2/OHâˆ’) to produce active oxygen radicals (e.g. Ë™O2âˆ’, Ë™OH), which subsequently cause degradation of the MB dye. Again, in the nanohybrids as CDs are situated on the surface of TiO2 or SiO2, the relative position of the CD band edge permits the transfer of electrons from the TiO2/SiO2 surface, which allows charge separation, stabilization, as well as hindered recombination. The electrons can shuttle freely with the conducting network of CDs, thus the longer-lived holes on the TiO2 or SiO2 account for the higher activity of the nanohybrid based photocatalysts.

4.3. Energy storage

Energy storage is one of the most promising and attractive electrochemical applications of carbon based nanomaterials. Among these applications, supercapacitors are one of the most promising devices to store energy for the next-generation wearable electronics due to the transport of high power within a very short period. Generally, carbon based materials store energy by the electrical double layer capacitance (EDLC) mechanism, and thus they have low energy density. Therefore, recently nanohybrid materials have gained more interest in electrochemical energy storage applications. 5. Summary and outlook

This article reviewed the recent progress in CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids and their photochemical and electrochemical applications. The intentional research on CDs started in 2006, though it was discovered accidentally in the year 2004. Now it is widely entering in almost all aspects of materials science. The interesting up-conversion photoluminescence, photo-induced electron/hole or charge transfer properties of CDs make it a highly efficient and tunable nanomaterial for photocatalysis, solar cell or photoelectric, photodetector, energy storage or harvesting, photochemical and electrochemical sensor applications along with the metal based nanoparticles. A large number of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids have been prepared through different approaches and their application spectrum has also been widened. However, still the progress of research on CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids remains in its early stage as compared to graphene based metal nanohybrids. Still many issues are waiting to be resolved for further development of CDs as well as their nanohybrids. The photoluminescence spectrum of CDs is generally too wide due to the large heterogeneity of the synthesized products. Thus, a genuine need to develop a better understanding of the tunable photoluminescence properties, controllable and scalable synthesis and surface engineering is very much expected from researchers in the field. The mechanisms of photoluminescence, up-conversion photoluminescence, electron and charge transfer demand for more studies and clarification. The electrical conductivity and crystallinity of CD based nanomaterials still remain as shortcomings for advanced electrochemical applications. More research needs to be performed on CD based nanohybrids in energy storage and harvesting applications like graphene based nanohybrid materials. However, early research clearly demonstrated the enormous potential of CDâ€“metal based nanohybrids in the photochemical and electrochemical fields. Therefore, this review article tried to establish the recent progress in the field and also urged researchers to go further in the development and practical applications of CD based metal nanohybrids. In a nut-shell, a strong and collaborative interdisciplinary platform is very much essential for genuine exploitation of this novel material in various fields.
