Building next-generation supercapacitors with battery type Ni(OH)2

Abstract

Electrochemical energy-storage technologies remain the only key to solve the increasing demands of global energy supply, zero-emission transportation, and grid storage in a greener way. Accordingly, novel strategies and the new promising designs of electrodes attempt to conquer the high energy density of supercapacitors without decreasing their power density and cycle life. In supercapacitors, asymmetric supercapacitors outshine the symmetric supercapacitors (<1.2 V) via the benefit of great energy density through high operating voltage (>2.0 V) in a safer way using aqueous electrolytes. Especially, emerging high energy supercapacitors perfected by suitable low cost-electrode materials are now the crown of energy storage research. Employing battery type Ni(OH)2 as a positive electrode has become a promising approach to building next-generation high energy supercapacitors besides its abundance, cost efficiency, environment friendliness, and high theoretical capacity. This review exclusively elaborates the unnoticed vision into the design, fabrication, mechanism, and investigation of such fascinating Ni(OH)2 based supercapacitors in an asymmetric fashion. Further, this review eventually mitigates the issues associated with the Ni(OH)2 component toward the goal of building next-generation supercapacitors for a sustainable environment.

1. Introduction

In recent years, intense research efforts have been undertaken to seek clean energy technologies in favor of the environment and natural resources as the Annual Greenhouse Gas Index has increased by more than 40% since 1990, also triggered by the hasty potential reduction of fossil fuels on Earth. The existing alternative renewable sources such as photovoltaic cells, wind turbines, and biomass plants, are inconsistent in electricity production due to the unpredictable climate conditions. Certainly, the resultant smaller electricity production compared to traditional power plants from these technologies cannot fulfill the huge global energy demand, which is rising by ∼3.6% every year.1 Thus, the promising candidates for electrochemical energy storage devices such as batteries and supercapacitors play crucial roles in overcoming these shortcomings with their superior characteristics. At present, batteries are considered as a good technology with great efficiency, among which lithium-ion battery (LIB) has high energy (100–240 W h kg−1) to store a large amount of charge among various batteries, including Pb–acid, redox-flow, Zn–air, and Na-batteries.2–6 However, the high processing cost of LIBs and the safety-related issues that arise from heat generation and dendrite formation while operating at high power hampers their full success in the energy storage market, which can be perceived from the fire accidents in Tesla cars and Dreamliner airplanes.7,8

Alternatively, supercapacitors or electrochemical capacitors have received spectacular attention owing to their secure supply of high power and have the ability of fast charging (1–10 s) with exceptional super-long cycle lifetimes (500000–1 million cycles) over batteries, resulting in more applications in heavy-duty vehicles, light rail, solar energy warning lights, beacon light, small current backup power supply, small charging products, wind power generation, load-leveling systems, storing and releasing the regenerative braking energy of electric vehicles (EV) and lessening the energy usage in the industrial equipment effectively. However, the lower energy density (∼5–10 W h kg−1) of supercapacitors than batteries (e.g., LIB) overcomes the challenges and extends their range of possible applications, including electric vehicles and grid storage.9–12 For instance, China exploits the supercapacitor as the only power source to operate the Capabus since 2006 for the public transport network, and a company in the UK identified the prototypes of supercapacitor that could store more energy per volume in mobile phones and electric cars than batteries.13,14 In addition, researchers have set the platform to employ supercapacitors as a 3D printed form in the future and intend to develop next-generation lightweight, flexible, and wearable electronics and optoelectronic devices.15 Certainly, the breakthrough would happen in the future effectually not only in electric vehicles but also in portable device technology if the efforts continue in this rapid way.

Thus, the exploration of the high energy density of supercapacitors toward battery performances without dropping the power density and cycle life make it an attractive topic in the energy storage field, which predicts the possibility of replacement of batteries in the market very soon. To rectify the low energy density issue, the development of asymmetric supercapacitors (ASC) is the main objective, including an extensive range of device configurations. Based on the charge-storage mechanism, supercapacitors can be classified into electric double-layer capacitors (EDLC), pseudocapacitors, and “asymmetric supercapacitors (ASCs)”.16–20 Generally, a supercapacitor comprises a positive electrode and a negative electrode dipped in an electrolyte (liquid, solid-state, or redox-active) and a separator that allows the transfer of ions. The charge is stored physically for EDLCs, capacitance is created at the electrode–electrolyte interface efficiently from charge separation, and no charge transfers in the electrode–electrolyte interface occurs. Only the electrostatic attraction involved between the positive electrodes of EDLC and the anions of the electrolytes during the charging process forms the two effective charged layers. In contrast, fast and reversible redox reactions (i.e., faradaic process) transpire at or near the surface of the electrodes, facilitating charge storage in pseudocapacitive materials.19,21–24

Carbon, as an independent material, covers graphite, activated carbon (AC), template porous carbons, carbon aerogels, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and graphene, which are being employed to fabricate the EDLCs typically and bring about a capacitance of ∼100–500 F g−1 based on the high surface area, electrical conductivity, fast charge/discharge rates, electrochemical stability, electrolyte medium, and porosity of the carbon materials.25,26 Pseudocapacitors widely exploit metal oxides and conducting polymers due to their fast reversible redox reactions, displaying great capacitance compared to EDLC devices using the transferred additional charges. Still, pseudocapacitors have trouble in the acquirement of exceptional cycle life due to active material degradation as a consequence of faradaic reactions. The energy density is the major obstacle for both EDLC and pseudocapacitors in their further commercialization in more applications even though they have great advantages for high-power density and respectable cycle life. However, the commercially accessible supercapacitors in the market are all organic-based electrolytes, reaching 2.5–2.85 V with an energy density of ∼5–10 W h kg−1 by consuming organic-based electrolytes but it is not environmentally beneficial. Thus, due to the failure of symmetric supercapacitors containing carbon electrodes to manage the working voltage up to 1.0 V, low-energy-density is obtained in aqueous electrolytes.

Hence, ASCs have recently achieved high working voltage using aqueous electrolytes with the common goal of higher energy and power density, containing one battery-type faradaic electrode (energy source) and another one as a capacitive-type electrode (power source), for example, the integration of carbon-based electrodes with pseudocapacitive electrodes to meet the market demands.27 In addition, another ASC can be structured as redox//redox type to succeed in practical high-energy-density applications. Having the benefits of two dissimilar electrodes in the ASC, which possess different potential windows, permits to expand the operating voltage of the full device beyond 2.0 V.28–31 The higher operating voltage is the deciding factor here for achieving high energy density in the ASC. A huge diversity of methods to prepare all types of electrode materials for an ASC in an aqueous electrolyte is likely to substitute the organic electrolyte-based symmetric supercapacitors in the market owing to their low cost. In a capacitive ASC, RuO2 and MnO2 based capacitance materials are grabbing more attention on pairing with negative carbonaceous materials such as graphene in an aqueous electrolyte, which attains a maximum output voltage up to 2.0 V, in addition to high energy and power density.33 Based on faradaic materials based aqueous hybrid ASC, metal oxides, hydroxides, mixed transition metal-based electrodes, and composite-based faradaic materials/carbon electrodes have revealed excellent electrochemical properties due to combination with negative carbon electrodes.34 However, the dependency of these electrodes is typically related to the Ni and Co-hydroxides and oxides. Due to the high cost and toxicity of Co, the researcher's approval is turning toward Ni-based materials.35 The recent trends in synthesizing Ni(OH)2-based electrode materials have increased sharply due to the high theoretical capacity, lower cost, high chemical stability, definite redox transitions, undisputed high-rate performance, and environmental friendliness.35–37 Apart from their contribution to the traditional primary and secondary alkaline batteries or Ni–MH and Ni–Cd batteries, the current electrochemical capacitor technologies also mostly rely on this battery-type material, increasing the dependency on the energy storage market massively.38–41 In recent years, considerable focus has gradually augmented to build the Ni(OH)2-based cathode electrodes due to their excellent performance with negative carbon electrodes in aqueous electrolytes. In this context, it is mandatory to review the recent progress on Ni(OH)2-based ASCs for further development toward the EV perspective.42,43

In the review, our main focus is only on the battery-type Ni(OH)2 ASC systems to understand their performances that may afford ideas/solutions/concepts/designs to further improve the capacitance, cycle life, energy, and power density of the next-generation devices. First, we briefly summarize the role of the current collector and the impact of the electrolytes in the ASC device in the first section. Further, Section 2 describes the crystal structure, reaction mechanism, and electrochemical properties of Ni(OH)2. Then, the synthetic methods of pristine, mixed Ni(OH)2, a combination of materials including carbon, metal oxides, sulfides, selenides, and phosphides with Ni(OH)2, the resultant materials morphology, synthesis mechanism, design/fabrication of ASC along with their electrochemical performance are elaborately discussed. Further, the same is constructively given for polymers with Ni(OH)2, a combination of polymer–carbon and polymer–metal oxides with Ni(OH)2, and the redox system of Ni(OH)2 (Section 8). Finally, we point out the concerns in the future perspectives based on the reported results for Ni(OH)2-based ASCs to realize the correct combination of electrode materials and high-performance devices for a sustainable earth.

1.1. Role of the current collector

Two important interfaces, such as electrode–electrolyte and the electrode-current collector interfaces, play a vital role in determining the energy density in all kinds of charge storage devices. In any energy storage system, the electrode–electrolyte interface has many choices; thus, it can be easily controlled by selecting structurally controlled electrode active materials and a suitable electrolyte solution.44 At the same time, a limited number of current collectors have been explored in energy storage devices according to the experimental conditions. To enhance the reaction kinetics at the electrolyte–electrode interface, a good current collector should be used. Importantly, the current collector should be electrochemically stable when employed in electrochemical energy storage devices. The better current collector should facilitate a shorter pathway for enhancing the transport of charge carriers (preferably electrons). Thus, the electrical resistance of the current collector should be very less to promote the abovementioned transport behavior. Recent studies have demonstrated that nanostructured current collectors with practical design have shown significant improvement in charge carrier transport.45–47 Generally, the choice of a current collector mainly depends on the medium of the electrolyte used, and most of the current collectors showing reactivity (either mild or severe) with electrolyte influence the cycle life. For instance, Cu and Al have been used as better current collectors in many applications, especially in LIBs; however, their corrosive nature restrains their applications, particularly in acid and alkaline electrolyte medium. An ideal current collector should be chemically robust and stable and should have good electrical conductivity. In such a scenario, carbon-based materials will be the best-suited regardless of the electrolytes and, of course, energy storage devices. High purity stainless steel will also be a good choice; however, it is a little expensive compared to current carbonaceous collectors. Also, the weight of the current collector is an important factor here. These dead masses unnecessarily dilute the net energy density of the system. Therefore, carbonaceous materials would be the manufacturer's choice, as always.

Meanwhile, Cu and Al have also been used in some commercial configurations, in which the electrolyte does not have any direct contact with the current collectors. Since, in the case of three-dimensional foam, the active materials are loaded inside three-dimensional matrixes of the current collectors (CC), the electron transfer will be very facile, which will favor the operation of the device at a very high current rate, i.e., tends to deliver high power capacity. In general, several factors influence the selection of a suitable CC. A better CC should be highly conductive and has an inert nature in a given electrolyte medium to avoid overestimating the charge storage. Thus, three-dimensional Ni foam is well established and preferred by many researchers due to its desirable characteristics such as high conductivity than carbon materials, larger contact area for high mass loading, shorter electron transfer length, and importantly, lightweight. Specifically, the Ni-based current collector has been extensively used, particularly in aqueous supercapacitor applications; simultaneously, Ni foam may not be used for high voltage devices and acid electrolyte medium due to its poor electrochemical stability and corrosive behavior. This can be a potential CC only in alkaline and neutral electrolyte medium; therefore, it is preferred mostly in the case of Ni(OH)2-based active materials.48 As we know, the equivalent series resistance (ESR) is a very important factor in determining the power and rate capability of supercapacitors. Thus, it is very important to have better charge transport efficiency without any loss. However, this can be affected by the resistance stretching at the electrode materials and the current collector interface. Suddenly, this will form an interface between the two electrical layers, and hence, the contact spot is reduced in the actual devices.49,50 To increase the contact spot and avoid such an interface formation, the resistance (lowering the ESR) of the current collector is decreased further by coating graphene on the conventional current collectors. Such modifications have been demonstrated successfully by improving the power and rate capability of the devices.51 Since the present review focuses only on the Ni(OH)2 electrode's electrochemical activity in asymmetric supercapacitor configuration, little discussion is given for current collectors. Amongst current carbon-based collectors, Ni-foam in pristine form or encapsulated with carbon or graphene is the better-suited substrate toward efficient charge transportation, especially Ni(OH)2-based electrodes.

1.2. Influence of the electrolyte

The electrolyte is one of the most important and integral parts of the electrochemical supercapacitors (SCs). Generally, the electrolytes will be prepared by dissolving the salt into the solvent/solute. This solution maintains charge neutrality and provides necessary ionic transportation during the charge–discharge processes. Various electrolytes have been employed in SCs, such as aqueous, organic, and solid-state ceramic electrolytes. Among them, organic electrolytes are used in commercial supercapacitors owing to the wider operating potential (2.5 to 3 V), which translates to an enhanced energy density of the system. Mostly, acetonitrile (ACN)-based solutions are employed in such systems. Though the aqueous electrolytes have a confined voltage window (up to 1.23 V), they are less toxic and cost-effective, and safer than the organic configurations. In addition, the mobility is much higher for the case of aqueous-based configuration, irrespective of the salts used in the aqueous electrolyte compared to organic. In other words, such mobility is capable of accelerating the ions at faster rates than that of the ions in the organic electrolytes. Also, the high ionic conductivity is an added advantage for the aqueous-based system. Thus, the ESR will be very low for the aqueous electrolyte-based systems, particularly for alkaline electrolytes. As mentioned, one of the major disadvantages of using the aqueous solution is its limited working potential. Thus, the energy density of the system will be less compared with the organic-based one. Recently, many efforts are being devoted to improving the potential window of the aqueous systems such as water-in-salt and incorporation of additives. The water-in-salt-based electrolyte is one of the successful approaches to widen the working potential window up to 2.3 V, and of course, has been demonstrated successfully.52–54 However, many factors need to be optimized for improving the SC performances. Though the electrolyte has a wide potential window, the electrodes should also have stable redox potential within the limit. Electrolytes, particularly the counter ions (both anion and cation), have a major role in determining the power density and rate capability though these parameters mainly depend on the electrodes' nature. It is worth mentioning here that the electrolytes play a crucial role in determining many characteristics such as internal resistance, ESR, operating temperature range, cycling lifetime, and self-discharge. In general, aqueous electrolytes can be classified into acid, alkaline, and neutral solutions in which H2SO4, KOH, and Na2SO4 are representatives and the most frequently used electrolytes. The highly concentrated acid-based solutions accelerate the dissolution of the most electro-active materials but it remains one of the best choices as an electrolyte for carbon-based electrode assemblies. At the concentration of 1.0 M H2SO4, the maximum ionic conductivity is realized at 25 °C. At the same time, the current tolerance is very less for the acid electrolyte due to the poor stability of the sulfate ions (SO42−) present in the electrolytes. On the other hand, the neutral electrolyte, Na2SO4 or the Li analog have been used in many cases due to the ion size, which helps to improve the cycle life. However, in such neutral electrolytes, poor ionic conductivity is a prime issue that provides higher values of ESR.56–58 Thus, the neutral electrolyte is not an appropriate option though it offers an extended potential window. KOH is the most preferred choice among alkaline solutions due to its higher conductivity (∼0.6 S cm−1 for 6 M concentration at 25 °C), charge carrier tolerance even at high current, high stability at wide temperature range, etc. A recent report revealed that the Ni-based electro-active materials showed better performance in 2 M KOH electrolyte than the other basic electrolytes such as 2 M LiOH and 2 M NaOH solutions. This is mainly due to the differences in the hydrated radius between the K+ (3.31 Å), Na+ (3.58 Å), and Li+ (3.82 Å) ions59 and also due to the ionic conductivity of K+ ions (73 cm2 Ω−1 mol−1), which is much larger than those of Na+ (50 cm2 Ω−1 mol−1) and Li+ ions (38 cm2 Ω−1 mol−1) at room temperature. Thus, the smaller hydrated radius of the K+ ions helps to promote fast ion transport, larger ionic mobility, and interaction with the active electrode materials.59–61

Therefore, in Ni-based oxides and hydroxides, KOH is the most preferred choice and has been extensively studied because of the reasons stated above. Its the ionic conductivity is directly related to the salt dissolution concentration, an optimum concentration is required to keep the ESR values lower, decent specific capacitance, and controlled oxygen evolution reactions.55,62,63 For instance, in Ni(OH)2 and its composite, 2 M KOH, is predominantly used as the electrolyte solution. Ni(OH)2 is a prominent electrode material used in Ni–Cd and Ni–MH secondary batteries in the presence of KOH as the electrolyte.3,4 Concentration is one of the important factors but unfortunately, different research groups have adopted different concentrations. Therefore, it is very difficult for us to pinpoint the electrochemical activity of Ni(OH)2. Nevertheless, the specific surface area, morphological features, and degree of crystallinity also play a vital role in influencing the electrolyte concentration. Stoyanova et al.64 studied the effect of mixed alkaline ion behavior for the α-Ni(OH)@AC composite. The IR-drop and the discharge capacitances were significantly improved for mixed LiOH–KOH electrolytes compared to KOH. Interestingly, the electrode roughness factor is drastically reduced for the mixed electrolytes, and this eventually enhanced the more homogeneous contact with the electrolyte. As a result, the system delivers a higher specific capacitance. Also, the mixed ion electrolyte encouraged the intercalation of Li-ions, which consequently improved the stability of the layered structure of α-Ni(OH)2 upon cycling. Although the mixed ion electrolyte exhibited better performance, the leakage current, power density, and rate capability must be significantly improved.

2. Crystal structure and reaction mechanism

Generally, the hexagonal-layered structure of Ni(OH)2 allows their existence as two pseudopolymorphs, i.e., α and β phases, which were first branded by Bode et al.65 in 1966. Both the α and β phases have the same hexagonal layered structure; however, they have shown difference in their chemistry between the brucite layers. The β phase with trigonal symmetry is isostructural with brucite Mg(OH)2 and naturally occurs as a theophrastite mineral. Moreover, the brucite layers in β-Ni(OH)2 are impeccably stacked to form a hexagonal structure, while those observed randomly changed the α-Ni(OH)2 structure into a turbostratic order with intercalated water molecules and anionic species. Also, it can be said that the α-Ni(OH)2 hydrotalcite-like structure is wrapped up in a large number of positively charged layers of Ni(OH)2 in the stacked mode, and also intercalated with H2O molecules and anions (carbonate, nitrate, chloride, and sulfonate), creating a wider interlayer spacing (7.2 Å) in its organization, which endows greater electrochemical activity than β-Ni(OH)2. In addition, Ni has an oxidation state of +4 in α-Ni(OH)2 with an average oxidation state of 3.3–3.7, which leads to exhibit superior electrochemical properties compared to β-Ni(OH)2.

The brucite-like structure of β-Ni(OH)2 has an ordered stacking layers of Ni(OH)2 in a neutral composition, deprived of intercalated species.36 The reason behind the highly stacked layers in these Ni(OH)2 is the strong ionic bond among the Ni2+, H+, and O2− ions in every sheet and the weak interactions between the adjacent layers. However, both the phases are shaped by the Ni layers in the hexagonal arrangement through the edge-shared octahedra where the OH− ions are along the c axis.66 It is well-known that the hydrotalcite structure could be obtained from the brucite structure through the partial substitution of Mg/Al and the associated introduction of the interlayer anions.67 Nevertheless, the metastable phase of α-Ni(OH)2 could change to β-Ni(OH)2 rapidly during synthesis or particularly at a low discharge current densities in the course of the charge–discharge process, resulting in substantial capacity loss and poor performance of the supercapacitor.67,68 On the other hand, Ni(OH)2 undergoes phase transformation during the charge–discharge process, as illustrated in Fig. 2, based on the Bode's diagram. In the redox process, both the α-Ni(OH)2 and β-Ni(OH)2 phases transform into γ-NiOOH and β-NiOOH, respectively, with oxidation states of 3.3–3.7 and 2.9 for Ni, which would increase the specific capacitance of electrode material considerably.69,70 Further charging the β-Ni(OH)2 yields γ-NiOOH, comprising water molecules and potassium available from the alkaline electrolyte; at the same time, it can be discharged back to reform Ni(OH)2. Also, when γ-NiOOH is being discharged, the formation of α-Ni(OH)2 occurs in consort with other molecules including CO32− and water either obtained from air or electrolyte, also converting the γ-phase to β-Ni(OH)2via direct discharge after crossing the α-phase.66,71 Also, the electrochemical redox reaction of β-Ni(OH)2/β-NiOOH could not acquire one-electron transfer per Ni atom, whereas the α-NiOOH)/γ-NiOOH pair creates the possibility to involve 1.6 to 1.67 electron transfer per Ni atom72,73 owing to the existence of cations (Li+, Na+, K+, etc.) and anions (CO32−, NO32−, SO42−, Cl−, etc.) in the water layers of their respective phases, which creates non-integral oxidation states for both the phases.66,74,75 This makes the α-NiOOH)/γ-NiOOH pair present superior electrochemical performance compared to the β-Ni(OH)2/β-NiOOH couple. Accordingly, low theoretical capacity (289 mA h g−1) is observed for β-Ni(OH)2/β-NiOOH as compared to the theoretical capacity (462–480 mA h g−1) for α-γ conversion.73 However, the high theoretical capacity and greater electrochemical properties of Ni(OH)2 have compelled researchers to find suitable methods for their preparation with appropriate properties.

3. Pristine Ni(OH)2

In 2006, Iwakura and co-workers76 made a hybrid electrochemical capacitor using commercially available Ni(OH)2 and AC as a positive and negative electrode, respectively, with the prepared polymer hydrogel electrolyte using potassium poly(acrylate) PAAK and KOH. The analysis indicated that this hybrid capacitor succeeded in the large voltage range, and the capacitance was ca. 2.4 times higher than that of the EDLC cell, which consists of two AC electrodes. At a current density of 10 mA cm−2 for 20000 cycles, the capacitor lost 10% of its initial capacitance. Due to the poor performance of amorphous Ni(OH)2, the researcher's attention is engaged by crystalline Ni(OH)2 because of its electrochemical properties and tractability to form complex three-dimensional structures. Nevertheless, the amorphous phase of Ni(OH)2 provides a distinct electrochemical behavior, which can be applied to specific device applications. Wang and co-workers77 successfully synthesized the amorphous Ni(OH)2 nanospheres with a size of ∼400 nm using a typical electrochemical method. For this asymmetric supercapacitor (ASC), a maximum specific capacitance of 153 F g−1 at a scan rate of 5 mV s−1 was obtained and 81% of the initial capacitance was retained after 10000 cycles (in 1 M KOH solution). Besides, it delivered a specific energy density of 35.7 W h kg−1 at a power density of 490 W kg−1. Parallelly, Su et al.78 proposed a simple and convenient approach for preparing amorphous Ni(OH)2 on 3D Ni particles via an electrodeposition technique. The assembled Ni(OH)2@3D Ni/AC-based ASC with 1 M KOH electrolyte showed a specific capacitance of 92.8 F g−1 and specific energy density of 21.8 W h kg−1 with a loss of only 4% after 3000 cycles. To attain high capacitance and good rate performance, Ni(OH)2 nanostructured materials with different morphology have been prepared in an efficient route (Fig. 3).

Sun et al.79 reported the synthesis of a few layered Ni(OH)2 through the solvothermal method, thereby enhancing the ion diffusion efficiency and charge transfer kinetics. The as-synthesized α-Ni(OH)2 nanosheets comprised of the graphene-sheet-like morphology with the thickness in the range of 4–5 nm also had a high specific area of 114 m2 g−1. Electrochemical studies have shown that the assembled ASC exhibited an excellent performance of 248 F g−1 in 6 M KOH with an energy density of 22 W h kg−1. The capacitance remained 85.7% after 4000 cycles, indicating the good long-term stability of the ASC device. Three types of hierarchical α-Ni(OH)2 microspheres, such as pompon-like, flower-like, and microflower-like microspheres, were prepared via a solvothermal method using solvents such as water, ethanol, and their mixtures, respectively. Among these, mesoporous α-Ni(OH)2 microflower-like microspheres unveiled a high surface area of 101.3 m2 g−1 with in the size range of 2–10 nm. After showing the best capacitance in the three-electrode systems, it was evaluated for practical application with the negative AC electrode. It was reported that the Ni(OH)2/AC supercapacitor can reach a cell voltage of 1.6 V in 6 M KOH and established outstanding electrochemical performances with an energy density of 32.9 W h kg−1.80 Another attempt of fabricating Ni(OH)2/AC obtained a specific energy density and specific power density of 25.8 W h kg−1 and 31.5 kW kg−1, respectively, at 1.6 V.81

Yang and co-workers82 developed a solution-based solvo-thermal-precipitation approach to synthesize Ni(OH)2 and prepared the hierarchically porous carbon in a one-step sintering technique. They found that the as-prepared porous Ni(OH)2 comprises the co-existence of α and β phases with a high specific area of 79.4 m2 g−1. The fabricated ASC can deliver a maximum specific energy density of 40.9 W h kg−1. Tian et al.83 successfully synthesized nano-Ni(OH)2 powder with the mixture of α and β phases via a simple precipitation method under ultrasonication to find the influence of single electrodes in Ni(OH)2/AC-based ASC. Interestingly, the authors assembled two series of a supercapacitor, in which the mass of AC altered, whereas the Ni(OH)2 mass was fixed and excessive, and on the contrary, the mass of Ni(OH)2 was varied while the AC mass was fixed and excessive. Thus, the obtained supercapacitors revealed the highest specific capacitance (189 F g−1) and the poor cycling stability for the lowest mass of Ni(OH)2 (0.40:1). In contrast, the highest mass of Ni(OH)2 (0.71:1) exhibited the lowest capacitance (176 F g−1) and finest cycling stability. Wang and co-workers84 directly grew β-Ni(OH)2 on Ni foam (NF) through a template free-growth process and made an ASC with AC as the negative electrode with 6 M KOH electrolyte. This β-Ni(OH)2–NF/AC might be cycled reversibly between 0 and 1.6 V and exposed the capacitance of 105.8 F g−1. Remarkably, the fabricated supercapacitor could retain 92% of its initial capacitance after 1000 cycles, with a high energy density of 36.2 W h kg−1.

Similarly, in another study,85 Ni(OH)2 was deposited on Ni foam (NHNF) by applying the hydrothermal method to preclude the use of a binder, which diminishes the electrochemical performance. Initially, four different concentrations of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O were prepared, then the cleaned NF was immersed into the metal solutions separately. The as-synthesized β-Ni(OH)2 on NF with 0.12 M concentration after presenting excellent electrochemical performance (2727 F g−1) in a three-electrode cell was then fabricated as an ASC using the commercial AC as a negative electrode in 6 M KOH electrolyte. This ASC device disclosed 31 mA h g−1 capacity with an energy density of 39.2 W h kg−1 with a potential window of 1.6 V. In addition, capacity retention of 84.3% was observed after 3000 charge–discharge cycles. Another effort aimed at the synthesis of β-Ni(OH)2 was described in the literature.86 In the beginning, the β-Ni(OH)2 plates were prepared by the reflux method. Dong et al.91 reported another type of ASC, which was wire-shaped and had flexibility, by combining an Ni(OH)2 fiber electrode and an ordered mesoporous carbon (OMC) jointly with the PVA–KOH gel electrolyte. The cycling profile was validated by its initial capacitance retention of 64.9% after 10000 cycles. In another new approach, Brouse and co-workers93 found that the grafting of 9,10-phenanthrenequinone molecules on the porous carbon surface significantly enhanced the Ni(OH)2-carbon's electrochemical performance compared to the pure carbon material. By assembling the Ni(OH)2 as the positive electrode, they have examined the performance of the modified BP and the unmodified BP for the negative electrode to fabricate the ASC device. The further increase in the PQ molecules on the BP decreased the capacitance retention; however, the device maintained higher than 70% capacitance retention even after 10000 cycles and showed their impact on carbon's grafting. Correspondingly, the PQ-grafting reinforced the device to make the capacitance 1.8 times higher than the unmodified BP and also showed a specific energy 1.5 times higher than that of the unmodified BP after 10000 cycles because it is well-known that the quinone-based molecules could perform as a good negative electrode in an alkaline medium, which has the benefit of exchanging more than one electron on the surface of the carbon.

4. Mixed Ni(OH)2

In recent years, layered double hydroxides (LDH) are known to be two-dimensional metal hydroxides, whose structure screening brucite-layer structure, as mentioned for β-Ni(OH)2 earlier, received greater attention as gifted supercapacitor electrodes on account of their inherent aspects such as large specific surface area, great anion exchange capacity, revocable chemical composition, high chemical stability, and rich redox activity. Nevertheless, the attained capacities could not conquer their theoretical capacity mostly due to the sluggish ion transfer rates and poor electronic conductivity.95 On the other hand, the Ni(OH)2 capacitance is still enriched even though it offered higher capacitance. Then, researchers have come up with an idea to form the complex hydroxides/oxides, fulfilling the goal of high faradaic capacitance due to the redox reactions and electronic conductivities, which could be greater than their hydroxides/oxides. This kind of complex hydroxides/oxides could provide a higher surface area to improve the electroactive sites for faradic reactions with more double-layer charges than individual hydroxides/oxides. Also, the integration of oxides will greatly enhance the electronic conductivity to favor the faradic reactions. This strategy of constructing hybrid assemblies with the aid of the battery-type Ni(OH)2 material would greatly contribute to the asymmetric device's total capacitance capably.

This approach has been attempted by Hui et al. recently. CoAl–LDH@Ni(OH)2, as a binderless electrode, was prepared in a two-step process.94 The inclusion of Co into the LDH is a technique to enhance the electrical conductivity of the electrodes, thereby achieving good capacitance because CoOOH will be formed when Co2+ is oxidized in the redox process, which has a conductivity of 12.8 S cm−1.96–98 Keeping this idea in mind, Al and Co hydroxides were prepared using the hydrothermal method, and Ni(OH)2 was grown on CoAl–LDH (over Ni-foam) using the electrodeposition process for various deposition cycles to ensure the enhanced performance of the composites (Fig. 6). CoAl–LDH@Ni(OH)2 coated up to 6 cycles showed a maximum specific capacitance of 1811 F g−1. Thus, the nanoflake structure improved the interfacial contact between the active material and the electrolyte, leading to more efficient charge transportation. An ASC cell was fabricated using MWCNT as the negative electrode and the PVA–KOH gel served as the electrolyte with the above specific properties. The ASC cell delivered a maximum power density of 16.7 W h kg−1. The cycle life was not provided for the ASC, whereas the cycle life of the individual electrode material showed a decent cycle life (3000 cycles with 76% retention) but it may not be the best profile reported where the Ni(OH)2 composite is concerned.

Later, Jun and co-workers99 conquered this methodology by replacing CoAl–LDH with MnCo–LDH on Ni(OH)2. Here, hydrothermal treatment was used to deposit the mixed hydroxides on NF first, then the flower-like core–shell structure of MnCo–LDH@Ni(OH)2 was obtained in the second time hydrothermal reaction of immersing the resultant NF on Ni solution under the optimized conditions. The dissociated Mn2+ and Co2+ ions in the solution responded to the OH− ions, which were available from urea hydrolysis and produced the [MnCo2(OH)6] precursor in the course of the hydrothermal treatment to perform as a source of the seed crystals after having to cling on the NF. Later, the adjoining nanoparticles led to aggregation as nanowires (Fig. 7a and b) through specific crystal orientation growth caused by surface energy minimization. Afterward, the as-prepared LDH was represented as a platform to exchange the Ni2+ ions with OH− by offering more nucleation sites and finally bloomed the flower-like structure with the continuous growth of the nuclei. The deposition of several Ni(OH)2 nanosheet arrays on the conductive substrate NF can be seen from Fig. 7c and d.

This core–shell nanostructure presented many active sites for more redox reactions to attain a high specific capacitance of 2320 F g−1 in 3 M KOH, using AC as the negative electrode. Also, this device could show a good capacitance of 1308 F g−1 under harsh conditions (30 A g−1) in the operating voltage of 1.5 V. The good cycling performance was also revealed with only ∼9% loss after 5000 cycles. Prominently, this device proved to be an excellent one for practical applications by delivering a high energy density of 47.9 W h kg−1 and showed its retention up to 9.8 W h kg−1 at a high power density of 5.21 kW kg−1. The high electrochemical performance of this device is due to the introduction of manganese, which improved the electrochemical activity of the whole device effectively with its various oxidation states.100,101

Thus, the fabrication of electroactive LDHs with non-electroactive materials such as Al–OH or Zn–OH presented a worthy improvement in the ASC performances. Despite this, the cycling stability of the LDHs possessed less than 90% of its original retention typically after 5000 cycles, probably because of the instability of the dopants (e.g., Al and Zn) in the alkaline medium, which led to inferior cycling stability. Until now, it remains a contest to build a hybrid Ni(OH)2 with stable structure/morphology and exceptional pseudocapacitance stability through a facile method. However, one of the promising approaches has been revealed by Guo and co-workers102 very recently via an in situ ion-exchange method to develop hybrid Ni(OH)2 on NF by avoiding additional alkali sources, and heat treatment for OH− production-such as the common procedures, followed in the synthesis of Ni(OH)2 and LDHs. As a sacrificial substrate, Mg(OH)2 was first coated on NF, then immersed in the Ni(NO3)2 solution at room temperature to proceed with an ion-exchange reaction effectively. Thus, the obtained Mg–Ni(OH)2 would be stable in a strong alkaline medium because of Mg(OH)2 and stabilizes the morphology/structure of Ni(OH)2. Accordingly, the BET specific surface area of Mg–Ni(OH)2 was calculated to be 220 m2 g−1, higher than that of dopant-free Ni(OH)2 (42 m2 g−1). As a result of pairing the mixed hydroxide with the negative AC electrode in 6 M KOH, the maximum specific capacitance of 167 F g−1 was attained, and Mg-free Ni(OH)2 displayed a maximum of only 98 F g−1 capacitance. Also, the ASC could maintain a high energy density of 49.6 W h kg−1 at a standard power density of 4.0 kW kg−1 than the fabricated device, containing Mg-free Ni(OH)2 (26.9 W h kg−1). In addition, Mg–Ni(OH)2 outperformed the Mg-free Ni(OH)2 device in the super-long cycling behavior, showing up to 91% retention after 10000 cycles. At the same time, Mg-free Ni(OH)2 faced 52% loss of capacitance only after 3000 cycles at a high current density of 10 A g−1, possibly due to the stable morphology/structure of the large surface area of Mg–Ni(OH)2 on NF, which supplied huge electrochemically active surface area for redox reactions.

5. Ni(OH)2–carbon composites

From the above constructive reports, we can conclude that Ni(OH)2 is the most attractive and active pseudocapacitive electrode in the midst of various transition metal hydroxides/oxides for supercapacitors by way of their electrochemical performance in ASC. Though the poor electrical conductivity reduces its competency, it thereby becomes unsuccessful in the achievement of its theoretical capacity, and also the low cycling life regulated their practical applications in the energy storage market.19,25,103–112 To overcome these obstacles, researchers have introduced carbonaceous materials/conducting additives into the pseudocapacitive Ni(OH)2 material to form nanocomposites so as to succeed in the high electrochemical performance of ASC, preferably extending the calendar life and realizing high power capability. Different kinds of carbon materials with Ni(OH)2 for the ASC are discussed in the forthcoming sections.

5.1 Ni(OH)2–graphene

Revolutionary nanostructured graphene, also termed sp2 bonded carbon material, has π electrons, making it highly electrically conductive and a “hot” material in the energy storage field.113–115 In addition, the excellent properties such as high theoretical specific surface area (∼2630 m2 g−1), high chemical stability, low resistivity (∼10−6 Ω cm), and the excellent energy density of 600–800 W h kg−1 inspired the formulation of promising composites with Ni(OH)2 and showed remarkable results as compared to pristine Ni(OH)2.111,116 Yan et al.117 reported a microwave heating process to obtain a composite, in which flower-like Ni(OH)2 were decorated on graphene sheets. The high surface area (1654 m2 g−1) porous graphene was synthesized via a template chemical vapor deposition (CVD) to fabricate an ASC in a 6 M KOH electrolyte. At a higher voltage window of 1.6 V, this ASC claimed the maximum specific capacitance of 218.4 F g−1 with an energy density of 77.8 W h kg−1. In addition, the Ni(OH)2–graphene/porous graphene-based ASC device suffered long cycling life of 3000 cycles only by the forfeiture of 5.7% of its initial capacitance.

Similarly, NF-supported vertically-oriented graphene or horizontally-oriented graphene nanosheets were used as a substrate for depositing Ni(OH)2, which has the advantages of 0.1–10 mm lateral and vertical dimensions along with few nm thicknesses (sometimes, <1 nm) in each individual graphene nanosheets for improving the specific capacitance by way of their vast surface-to-volume ratio, exclusive orientation, and non-stacking morphology.118,119 By pairing up the β-Ni(OH)2-vertically oriented graphene nanosheets (v-GNs) on NF as the negative electrode and AC as the counter electrode, Zheng and co-workers120 developed a 1.4 V ASC in 6 M KOH electrolyte. First, v-GNs were grown on chemically pre-cleaned NF through plasma-enhanced CVD, and electrodeposition was carried out to grow Ni(OH)2 on the v-GNs in a three-electrode system at slightly elevated temperature conditions (45 °C). The SEM image displayed the growth of Ni(OH)2 nanoflakes vertically on the supported v-GNs-NF, which had a length of typically 50–150 nm, whereas the average thickness of v-GNs was only about 15 nm. The ASC delivered a specific capacitance of 80 F g−1 with a maximum energy density of 11.11 W h kg−1.

Xia and co-workers121 first reported the ASC by introducing the combination of Ni(OH)2 and ordered mesoporous carbon (CMK-5) to serve as the positive and negative electrodes, respectively. A simple hydrothermal method was used to grow the α-Ni(OH)2 nanowires on the layered graphene, whereas CMK-5 was prepared with a high specific area of 1530 m2 g−1via a template method to build an ASC. During the charging process, the Ni(OH)2 nanowires gave away the protons and cations present in the aqueous electrolyte, which were also concurrently absorbed in the pore structure of CMK-5, which had a pore size of 3.5 nm (Fig. 8). Furthermore, the fabricated ASC with a decent mass loading could achieve a maximum specific capacitance of 157 F g−1. Furthermore, it maintained over 90% of initial capacitance after 5000 charge–discharge cycles at a high current density of 4.5 A g−1. In addition, this ASC device exhibited a maximum energy density of 41.3 W h kg−1 and possessed 17.3 W h kg−1 even at a high power density of 4.08 kW kg−1 in 6 M KOH electrolyte.

Zhang and co-workers124 developed transparent Ni(OH)2–graphene and FeOOH–graphene micro-structured films through a gas–liquid diffusion method. The Ni and Fe salts were first dissolved by an appropriate amount of NH3 separately, and then the film was grown at room temperature with the benefit of flowing air in the fume hood for Fe; however, Ni required various temperatures (50–90 °C) after sealing in an evaporating dish. The Ni film was finally grown in 4 min through a constant ventilation mode once the cover was removed after the reaction temperature turned out to be stable. Both the Ni(OH)2 and FeOOH films were shifted to a transparent ITO–PET substrate individually. Further, the pre-synthesized graphene film was deposited on the respective metal hydroxides to evaluate their electrochemical performance. Then, the electrodes were dipped into the pre-prepared KOH–PVA gel electrolyte. Thus, the fabricated ASC had a total thickness of 111 μm, contributing to a positive electrode of 450 nm, an anode electrode of 550 nm, electrolyte 10 μm, and 0.1 mm of two ITO–PET substrates. The areal capacity of the ASC remarkably increased from 3.3 mF cm−2 (29.5 F g−1) to 17.42 mF cm−2 (155.9 F g−1) while the current density decreased. With the mass loading of 45.2 μg of the positive electrode and 66.5 μg of the negative electrode, this transparent and flexible ASC device exhibited a high volumetric energy density of 0.67 mW h cm−3 with an operating voltage of 1.8 V. Moreover, 85.1% of its original capacitance was retained after 20000 cycles, which prove their cycling stability for real applications.

In general, graphene sheets do not allow the electrolyte ions to utilize their surface area and active sites fully as they are impacted by irreversible sheet-to-sheet restacking, triggered via strong van der Waals force attraction during the drying and heat-treatment process. Consequently, researchers hosted interlayer spacers such as nanoparticles, nanosheets, and nanowires to reduce the agglomeration of reduced graphene oxide (rGO) agglomeration. This strategy has been used by Liu et al.125 recently. With the use of hydrazine as the reducing agent, graphene oxide (GO) was reduced in the chemical reduction method. Further, the enhanced rGO sheets were produced in support of the Ni(OH)2 precursor solution via the hydrothermal reaction, followed by freeze-drying and heat treatment under an Ar atmosphere. Finally, the as-prepared composite was eroded with HCl to remove the ultra-small Ni(OH)2 sacrificial spacers, attaining a high specific area of 443 m2 g−1. The positive electrode, Ni(OH)2–rGO composite, was prepared in the two-step process, which involves the preparation of the Ni–citrate complex and subsequent hydrothermal treatment, followed by freeze-drying, which resulted in a high surface area of 152 m2 g−1 compared to that of pure Ni(OH)2 (109 m2 g−1). Moreover, the poor crystallinity of β-Ni(OH)2 in the obtained composite was expected to enhance the electrochemical performance. Subsequently, Ni(OH)2–rGO/rGO was assembled in a 1.6 V voltage window using the aqueous 2 M KOH electrolyte, which possessed a superb cycling stability of 10000 cycles and retained 89% of the initial capacitance. Furthermore, this device exhibited the remarkable energy density of 75 W h kg−1.

A similar ASC was fabricated and reported by assembling the α-Ni(OH)2–rGO composite as the positive electrode and AC as the negative electrode in 6 M KOH as the electrolyte. In the beginning, the exfoliation of α-Ni(OH)2 was done using sodium dodecyl sulfate in formamide at 25 °C for 5 days after the successful preparation of α-Ni(OH)2 in the hydrothermal reaction. This type of layered composite was prepared to prevent the re-stacking and deposition of the exfoliated α-Ni(OH)2 nanosheets due to the unstable nature when the solvents were removed. Moreover, it was assumed that the exfoliation process helps α-Ni(OH)2 become more hydrated, thereby improving the capacitance through the easy transport of OH− ions. Furthermore, the better electron transport channel rGO will help to improve the electrical conductivity and capacitance of the composite. In this study, α-Ni(OH)2 and GO were mixed first to flocculate and were permitted to stand overnight to complete the aggregation process. Thus, the obtained composite via electrostatic attraction was further subjected to chemical reduction with the backing of hydrazine to acquire the α-Ni(OH)2–rGO composite. The CV curves with the redox peaks measured the maximum capacitance of 168.8 F g−1 for the ASC α-Ni(OH)2–rGO/AC device and still managed to obtain 114.5 F g−1 at higher rates. The synergic contribution from α-Ni(OH)2 and rGO and the large surface area of these materials cemented the superior electrochemical performance of this ASC device; for example, this device divulged only 92.4% of its original capacitance after 2000 cycles, showing a significant cycling life and rate performance. Furthermore, this ASC device grasped an apparent energy density of 76 W h kg−1 and maintained an energy density of 51.5 W h kg−1 even at a high power density of 8.13 kW kg−1, which is recommended for highly promising energy storage applications.126

The flexible solid-state ASC has been fabricated by sandwiching the polymer PVA–KOH electrolyte in between the Ni(OH)2–rGO–Ni wire and rGO–Ni wire fiber electrodes without any binder-conducting additives. The materials' preparation is described as follows: the 3D rGO aerogel was first coated on Ni wire and used as the negative electrode material. The same rGO–Ni wire was dipped into the Ni(NO3)2 solution, heated, and shifted into the mixture of KCl and KOH solution, leading to the deposition of Ni(OH)2 on the rGO–Ni wire. The achieved Ni(OH)2–rGO–Ni composite was further freeze-dried after washing, which performed as the positive electrode. To validate the stable voltage windows of this ASC device, it was examined up to 1.6 V in the CV; there was no distortion observed in the curves; further, the extension leads to the distortion of the CV traces due to the decomposition of the electrolyte. The positive and negative electrode materials had a mass ratio of 2:5 and supplied a high specific capacitance of 69 F g−1 with typical triangular curves. The flexibility studies of the ASC device were proved through CV measurements at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1, where no significant changes were observed in the CV curves under different bending conditions, establishing good flexibility and outstanding mechanical stability. The specific volume capacitance of the ASC was also calculated to be 2320 mF cm−3 with a maximum volumetric energy density of 0.83 mW h cm−3. Remarkably, the assembled ASC retained 83% of its initial capacitance after 6000 cycles at a high current density of 10 A g−1.127

In the same fashion, 3D Ni(OH)2–rGO hydrogel was prepared to be used as a binder-free electrode material for ASC by way of merging the as-synthesized 3D rGO hydrogel in 6 M KOH electrolyte. The cylinder-shaped hydrogel of Ni(OH)2–rGO was attained in a two-step approach; at first, the 3D rGO hydrogel was synthesized through the in situ reduction of GO nanosheets using vitamin-C in NaOH solution. Later, Ni(OH)2 was doped into the as-synthesized rGO hydrogel via hydrothermal treatment. The symmetric supercapacitors Ni(OH)2–rGO/Ni(OH)2–rGO and rGO/rGO were assembled for comparison along with the Ni(OH)2–rGO/rGO ASC in a two-electrode system. The ASC rendered the specific capacitance of 94.9 F g−1, which is much higher than that of the symmetric supercapacitors. After 10000 cycles, 90% of the capacitance could be maintained, presenting its good cycling stability with a high energy density of 21.71 W h kg−1.128

Having the benefits of high abundance sources, low cost, environment friendliness, and particularly high theoretical capacitance, manganese-based oxides or hydroxides have been focused on more for a long time as electrode materials in the energy storage field. Besides, these pseudocapacitive materials face trouble in attaining high capacitance due to their low specific surface area, unsuitable crystallinity, shorter pathway for electrons transport, or wettability issues with the electrolyte. Therefore, designing the structure and composition of materials is becoming a key strategy to overcome these problems and achieve higher specific capacitance. Recently, Wu and co-workers129 handled the synthesis of hybrid spheres of Ni(OH)2–MnO2–rGO in a one-step methodology through the hydrothermal reaction, which offers good electrochemical performance by coupling the porous freeze-dried rGO as the negative electrode. Scheme 2 clearly illustrates the possible formation mechanism of this composite. Initially, the Ni2+ and Mn2+ ions were situated on the GO surface because of the electrostatic attraction, and further, the metal ions reacted with the OH− ions of the methanol to yield primary particles, paving the way for deposition on the GO surfaces partly. This continuation process made the aggregation of the amorphous core primary particles on their surface crystallize and produced the primary nanosheets progressively. Moreover, the dissolved oxygen transformed Mn2+ into Mn4+ during this process, and at high temperatures, the GO turns out to be rGO, due to the reaction with methanol. The primary nanosheets of MnO2 might be disorderly stacked with Ni(OH)2 due to the hydrogen bonding interactions and tend to form the hybrid nanosheets and rGO. Further, these hybrid nanosheets triggered the construction of the spherical structures through the self-assembly process. Thus, the obtained material had the composition of α-Ni(OH)2, γ-MnO2, and the graphene layers and the high BET surface area of 147 m2 g−1, while Ni(OH)2–MnO2 only reached 76 m2 g−1. The Ni(OH)2–MnO2–rGO/porous freeze-dried rGO ASC demonstrated good capacitive behavior using 1 M KOH in the range of 0–1.6 V, which also had impressively high energy density of 32.6 W h kg−1. Also, 75% of the original capacitance remained after 2000 cycles, displaying the cycle life of the ASC device.

Meanwhile, Shen et al.130 described the simple and effective method for the synthesis of Ni(OH)2–rGO nanohybrids in a formamide medium. At the start, Ni(OH)2 nanosheets were prepared through a facile hydrothermal treatment with the intercalation of dodecyl sulfate ions, and exfoliation was done in a formamide medium. Also, GO was prepared by the modified Hummers' method, and the exfoliation obtained the nanosheets in water. Then, the exfoliated nanosheets were re-dispersed in formamide when freeze-drying was completed and reduced via chemical reduction using hydrazine after re-dispersion in water. Finally, the resultant Ni(OH)2–rGO nanohybrids were developed via freeze-drying, possessing the porous flower-like structure with a specific surface area of 77.7 m2 g−1 and an average pore size of 9.2 nm. The optimized Ni(OH)2–rGO nanohybrids/AC-based ASC showed high specific capacitance of 102.3 F g−1 in 6 M KOH and provided a high energy density of 36.7 W h kg−1. The high electrochemical performance was mainly due to each nanosheet being present in the nanohybrid that would have been contacted with the electrolyte. Besides, graphene in the nanohybrid could have enhanced the electron conduction channels, thereby reducing the inner resistivity. Furthermore, the microstructural characteristics of the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets also contributed to the high performance as their sheets are broken harshly and get distorted during the exfoliation/re-organization process, which is facilitated by reducing their size.

Similarly, Zhang and co-workers131 successfully developed the Ni(OH)2–graphene covered carbon fiber paper (CFG) without the assistance of hard templates or any surfactants using a one-pot hydrothermal reaction. The nanostructured CFG was obtained by the addition of GO on the surface of the carbon fiber paper, followed by reduction in the presence of hydrazine hydrate and the freeze-drying process. Subsequently, CFG was added to the Ni salt solution to acquire the vertical and controllable conducting Ni(OH)2 network. The α-Ni(OH)2 phase was determined for the pure Ni(OH)2 and without the peaks, except for carbon, showed the poor crystallinity of the composite. However, the existence of graphene layers led to the high BET surface area of 425.9 m2 g−1 of the composite than that of pure Ni(OH)2 (366.9 m2 g−1) and CFG (184.8 m2 g−1), mostly by enlarging the Ni(OH)2 nucleation sites and also setting a larger electrochemical surface area. The as-prepared composite was assessed as the positive electrode to built the ASC with AC as the counter electrode in a 2 M NaOH solution. To evaluate the stable voltage window, the ASC was analyzed under different potential windows from 0–1.6 V at 10 mV s−1 (Fig. 11). The maximum gravimetric specific energy density of 10.4 W h kg−1 was achieved for this Ni(OH)2–CFG/AC-based ASC. Most importantly, there was no capacitance decay up to 5000 cycles, identifying the stable cycling performance of this ASC.

5.2 Ni(OH)2–graphite or expanded graphite (EG)

Very recently, Ni(OH)2-ultra-thin graphite foam (UGF) has been used as the positive electrode material for ASC. The reasons behind the usage of Ni(OH)2–UGF as the positive electrode material were believed to be as follows. (1) The three-dimensional network of high conductive UGF had the prospect of improving the transportation of electrons to the injected Ni(OH)2 than the other metal current collectors. (2) It was assumed that the porous Ni(OH)2 could shorten the ion-diffusion pathway, thereby enabling the easy movement of electrolyte ions even at higher current densities. (3) The problem of increasing contact resistance between the electrode material and the current metal collectors during the electrochemical process can be avoided by using lightweight UGF foam and supporting the minimization of the total mass of the electrode. Typically, the UGF comprising of ultra-thin graphite struts were prepared through CVD, replacing old-fashioned current collectors such as carbon paper or Ni foam. Further, the homogeneous solution containing Ni-ions and urea was injected into the UGF placed in the filter flask under vacuum. Then, the wetted UGF with all the solutions was subjected to hydrothermal treatment. The resultant composite was merged with the pre-pasted negative electrode material of the activated microwave exfoliated graphite oxide to fabricate the ASC. This ASC displayed a specific capacitance of 119 F g−1. Interestingly, the potential window of this ASC could be extended up to 1.8 V compared to the normally tested window of 1.6 V. Unfortunately, the capacitance loss was found to be 36.8% after 10000 cycles, mainly due to the variations in the volume of the active materials during the cycling process.132

Due to the high-cost of graphene, EG has been explored more in energy storage applications; also, this graphene-like cheap material having good electrical conductivity, chemical stability, and a high surface area compared to graphite inspires researchers to develop low-cost supercapacitors with excellent electrochemical performance. Qu et al.133 made this kind of ASC using Ni(OH)2–EG as the positive electrode and the commercial AC as the negative electrode in 6 M KOH solution. Ni(OH)2–EG was synthesized through the facile microwave-assisted method using ethylene glycol as the solvent in the preparation process. XRD confirmed the structure of the as-synthesized composite; the diffraction peaks were slightly shifted from the standard spectrum of α-Ni(OH)2 because of the substitution of the α-Ni(OH)2 interlayer by the ethylene glycol molecules during the precipitation process; also, the composite further displayed the additional peaks for EG. The ASC was constructed with different mass ratios of AC to α-Ni(OH)2 to conclude the optimal ratio. Among these, the high specific capacitance of 86.4 F g−1 was achieved with a mass ratio of 2.0. This ASC device suffered a loss of 19.9% specific capacitance after 1000 cycles, which is attributed to the increased charge transfer resistance and diffusion resistance of the electrolyte ions in the cycling process. Moreover, the ASC gave a maximum energy density of 37.7 W h kg−1 at a power density of 490.9 W kg−1.

Yuan et al.134 also developed a Ni(OH)2–EG composite and analyzed its electrochemical performance as the positive electrode in ASC. For instance, they developed a combination of electrochemical and chemical processes to deposit Ni(OH)2 on a conductive substrate containing EG and then placed its as the positive electrode material in an ASC. During the electrodeposition process, OH− ions were formed on the cathode through the electrochemical reduction of NO3− anions grabbed from the electrolyte of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O. The homogeneous distribution of Ni(OH)2 on the EG afforded the layered structure and aided in the expansion of the interlayer spacing for the easy penetration of the electrolyte ions. As expected, the sandwich-like structure of the ASC (Fig. 12) demonstrated a typical rectangular-shaped CV curves, which is accountable for both EDLC and pseudocapacitance behavior. Correspondingly, the asymmetrical triangular charge–discharge patterns attributed to the valuable capacitive behavior also favored the high coulombic efficiency (Fig. 12c). This ASC assembly rendered a specific capacitance of 64.6 F g−1 but the cycling stability of the ASC suffered as it can achieve an energy density of 32.3 W h kg−1.

5.3 Ni(OH)2–carbon nanotube (CNT)

Researchers have paid great attention to CNTs (also known as sp2 bonded carbon) for their use as an electrode material for excellent electrochemical performance when graphene was not isolated. The cylindrical structure of CNT has a sheet of graphene along with captivating properties of the high surface area, high stability, and low resistance. Besides, the hollow interior space of the CNT supplies more reaction sites through their conducting channels, consequently bringing about more reports on the development of metal hydroxide or oxide–CNT composites for energy storage applications.25,135–137 Feng and co-workers138 introduced the CNTs matrix into Ni(OH)2 to improve the electrochemical activity of the layered hydroxides. They produced CNTs with a diameter of 15–50 nm via catalytic pyrolysis using the reagent-based Ni and ethylene gas, also having a high surface area of 500 m2 g−1. The spherical-shaped Ni(OH)2 was prepared through a chemical precipitation process with a diameter of about 1–20 μm. Both the electrodes Ni(OH)2 and Ni(OH)2–CNT had shown faradaic redox behavior, as evidenced through the CV results. The specific capacitances of the Ni(OH)2 and Ni(OH)2–CNT were 311 and 137 F g−1, respectively, in a single electrode configuration, which is mainly attributed to the increase in the surface area and active sites of the Ni(OH)2 in the composite. The support of commercial AC as the negative electrode showed 68 F g−1 in the ASC with the composite in 6 M KOH and an energy density of 25.8 W h kg−1.

Salunkhe et al.139 synthesized the composite using a low-cost, one-step, and simple chemical process to implement the growth of a thin layer of Ni(OH)2 coaxially onto oxidized CNT surface (o-CNT), then fabricated the ASC by preparing rGO as the negative electrode. In the precipitation process, the composite Ni(OH)2–o-CNT was formed by the adsorption of positively charged Ni(OH)2 on the negatively charged oxygen functional groups, encompassing o-CNT under the optimized conditions. The uniform distribution of the α and the predominant β phases of the Ni(OH)2 on the o-CNT surface was witnessed. After optimizing the mass ratio and the potential window, the fabricated ASC could deliver a relatively high working voltage of 1.8 V in an aqueous electrolyte. The recorded charge–discharge curves were linear at low current densities and the specific capacitance was estimated to be 78 F g−1. Further, the ASC presented a maximum energy density of 35 W h kg−1; even under harsh conditions (27 kW kg−1), the cell retained a value of 26.24 W h kg−1.

Another effective synthesis of Ni(OH)2–CNT on NF for ASC has been reported in two steps by Gong and co-workers.140 A thermal CVD method was applied to grow the CNTs directly on the NFs in a furnace with the flow of C2H4 gas. The as-grown CNTs–NF was immersed in the mixture of NH3 containing nickel sulfate and potassium persulfate solution to deposit the Ni(OH)2 nanostructures further at room temperature. A 1.8 V ASC was efficiently fabricated by pairing the Ni(OH)2–CNT–NF with a commercial AC to act as the positive and negative electrodes. Moreover, a very rare unseen rectangular CV curve was obtained for this ASC, offering a typical capacitor behavior. Further to the confirmation of the capacitor-like behavior through the discharge curves, the specific capacitance was calculated to be 112.5 F g−1. In an aqueous electrolyte, the superior energy density of 32.5 W h kg−1 was gained due to its high capacitance and higher working potential. In addition, the ASC device was rewarded with a retention of 83% after 3000 cycles.

Stimulated by the advantages of binary composites on CNTs, Yan and co-workers141 developed the 3D Ni(OH)2–MnO2 on the CNT substrate by growing the CNT first on a chemically pre-cleaned NF through catalytic thermal CVD, then the Ni(OH)2–MnO2 was deposited on the prepared template by the solvothermal process using their respective salts in cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) and methanol. Generally, the doping of Mn in Ni(OH)2 could be beneficial for the increase in the conductivity, offering a stable crystalline phase in alkaline medium, and the availability of wealthier redox reactions than the individual ones. Also, the CNT could support the high gravimetric and volumetric capacitance of the ASC by upgrading the mass loading of active materials without the damage to its supercapacitor performance. With these benefits, further intending to improve the ASC's performance, AC was prepared with N-doping as the negative electrode via polymerization, carbonization, and KOH activation to design this N-rich AC from polyaniline. The resultant ASC was constructed by pairing this carbon electrode with Ni(OH)2–MnO2@CNT-NF as the positive electrode material. A slight hump owing to the oxygen evolution was observed in the range of 1.7–1.75 V in 1 M KOH. Thus, 1.7 V was selected as the working voltage for this assembled ASC. At high mass loading, the ASC device revealed a high specific capacitance of 315 F g−1 with a typical capacitive behavior. The cycling test was demonstrated and a capacitance retention of 78.2% was detected after 3000 cycles. In this work, the ASC device produced a tremendous gravimetric energy density of 126.4 W h kg−1 and a corresponding volumetric energy density of 10.9 mW h cm−3 based on the device volume, fulfilling the demand requirement of the supercapacitor device.

A simple chemical bath deposition (CBD) process was employed to grow the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets uniformly on the surface of pre-oxidized CNTs to set as the positive electrode toward the fabrication of ASC by Yi et al.142 The mixture of CNTs and urea in metal solution was heated, followed by ageing, resulting in the core–shell nanostructure of the composite. The decomposition of urea during the deposition headed the way to release CO2 and ammonia, which reacted with water and Ni ions to grow the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets on the CNT surface, which directed the formation of the core–shell nanostructure as well. Further, the 3D porous graphene hydrogel (3DGN) was prepared to prevent the restacking of the graphene sheets, thus improving the ASC's capacitance as the negative electrode material with a high surface area of 134.7 m2 g−1. The two as-synthesized electrodes were successfully combined and separated using the polypropylene separator in 1 M KOH (Fig. 13) with an optimized mass ratio of 0.36. The assembled CNT@Ni(OH)2/3DGN-based ASC showed the broad peaks of the CV curves in the potential voltage window of 0.0–1.6 V, indicating the pseudocapacitive nature. Further, the maximum specific capacitance of the ASC was calculated to be 124 F g−1 based on the total mass of both the active materials. The high capacitance was mainly attributed to the porous Ni(OH)2 nanosheets, which offered a high surface area and more active sites for electrolyte transportation. Moreover, 3DGN also contributed to shortening the transport distance between the electrode and the electrolyte to attain a high rate of energy storage. Further, the Ragone chart indicates that the device presented a high energy density of 44.0 W h kg−1 with good cycling stability and 83% capacitance retention after 8000 cycles.

By in situ loading the nanosized Ni(OH)2 on multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNT), Xia and co-workers144 prepared the Ni(OH)2–MWCNT composites in an alkaline medium to construct the high power ASC. Because the rate capability of Ni(OH)2 mainly depends on the diffusion of protons in the Ni(OH)2 framework, the researchers were compelled to develop nanosized Ni(OH)2 and disseminated Ni(OH)2 on the net-like MWCNT. Wei et al.145 manually mixed the CNT with 3D interspersed flower-like Ni(OH)2 to improve the electrochemical performance of the ASC. 5.4 Ni(OH)2–carbon fibers

Carbon fibers are also generally employed as current collectors and substrates because of their praiseworthy properties such as excellent conductivity, good chemical stability, and high mechanical strength. Importantly, the intrinsic flexibility of the fibers has empowered to change the fabricated fiber-based electrodes into flexible supercapacitors.25,146 Recently, Shi et al.147 reported the coating of the Ni(OH)2 nanosheet ink on the commercial carbon fiber yarns (CFY) to make the ASC wearable. The fast co-precipitation method was adopted to synthesize the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets and the Nafion (Nf)–Ni(OH)2 ink, prepared using ethanol, allowed to impregnate the CFY to design the CFY@Nf–Ni(OH)2, known to be an impregnation-dyeing method. In a thermal plastic tube, fabrication was done using the as-prepared PVA–KOH gel electrolyte to support a non-woven fabric separator. The fabricated ASC could achieve the wide operating voltage of 1.5 V and achieved a volumetric capacitance of 36.3 F cm−3. 5.5 Ni(OH)2–carbon nanosheets

As said earlier, the pseudocapacitance functionalities of Ni(OH)2 can be fully exploited by coating the nanostructured Ni(OH)2 on the surface of carbon in an appropriate way. Recently, Xie et al.149 grew Ni(OH)2 on the carbon nanosheets by introducing a simple methodology using Mg(OH)2 as the template and phenolic resin as the precursor. In general, it is not easy to grow the Ni(OH)2 coating on the carbon material directly due to their surface incompatibility. Therefore, researchers have sorted out this issue by oxidative treatment before the coating, which facilitates the growth of metal hydroxides on the surface of carbon materials. In this study, the Ni(OH)2–carbon nanosheets were prepared without any oxidation process for the carbon material for Ni(OH)2 growth. The Mg(OH)2–resin composite was prepared in the impregnation method, carbonized, and immersed in the Ni solution to facilitate the ion-exchange reactions. Thus, the obtained composite had the α-phase of Ni(OH)2 and exposed the mesoporous structure with a larger specific surface area of 278 m2 g−1. To build an efficient ASC, the high surface area (1999 m2 g−1) of commercial AC was chosen as the negative electrode. The ASC disclosed a maximum specific capacitance of 230 F g−1 in 6 M KOH, which is mainly due to the coated 2D structure on the carbon material, which shapes the pathways for electrolyte diffusion and rapid electron transport. Further, the cycling stability for this ASC was assessed with the retention of ∼93% after 10000 cycles. The reason for the good cycling performance would be primarily attributed to the microstructure of the composite as obtained from the doped Mg(OH)2 and the carbon material.

5.6 Ni(OH)2–activated carbon (AC)

While facing issues in achieving specific capacitance and overall cycle stability for ASC due to some limitations, frequent modifications to the synthesized materials have been reported to prevail these struggles. For instance, the fabrication of Ni(OH)2 with the addition of carbon-based materials such as AC and CNT would increase the specific capacitance of the electrode material to accomplish the overall electrochemical performance of the ASC. Among the porous carbon materials, ACs bestows a high specific surface area of 3000 m2 g−1 along with pore sizes ranging from micropores to macropores.150 The low cost, easy accessibility, and most widely employed material has also been combined with Ni(OH)2 to evaluate the electrochemical performance. Recently, Sui et al.151 reported the synthesis of Ni(OH)2–CNT–AC through the facile microwave-assisted heating method in the ethylene glycol medium.

As a consequence of pairing the Ni(OH)2–CNT–AC as the positive electrode and the commercial AC as the negative electrode, it is capable of working up to 1.6 V. The CV curves exposed the broad redox peaks and were not changed when the scan rate increased from 5 to 60 mV s−1, which indicated the virtuous capacitive behavior of the device. 6. Ni(OH)2–metal oxides, sulphides, selenides, and phosphides

The greatest electrochemical properties for the ASC could be conquered by picking appropriate nanostructured electrode materials with suitable electrolytes. In this rivalry, different metal oxides, sulfides, selenides, and phosphides with their distinct properties have been tested to form the composite with the battery-type Ni(OH)2 electrode to augment the performance in the ASC. For instance, the substitution of O and S by the lower electronegativity benefit of Se in the transition metal compounds evades the decay of the structure through elongation between the layers and facilitates the electronic transportation in the structure conveniently, leading to the attainment of high electrochemical performance.160 On the other hand, the transition metal oxides and hydroxides also acquire a good capacitance through a rich reversible faradaic reaction with their multiple valence states contributing to the overall electrochemical performance of the ASC.161 As a competitor, transition metal phosphides are also in the row to substitute the conventional electrode materials with their n-type semiconductor structure and provide pseudocapacitance as a faradaic type electrode and an electrically conductive additive in consort with the remarkable properties of superior electrical conductivity and metalloid characteristics.162,163 These different interesting performance outcomes obtained from the reports for positive electrode Ni(OH)2 composite-based ASCs have been well-constructed and discussed extensively in the following sub-sections.

6.1 Ni(OH)2–metal oxides

For the past few years, hydroxides, particularly Ni(OH)2, have received much attention due to their higher specific capacity in an alkaline aqueous medium. The high capacity of such a material is due to the redox reaction associated with the layered structure, in which the metal hydroxide shows a larger interlayer distance. Though it has low redox potential, the high capacity of Ni(OH)2 highly improves the energy density, which is still higher than the metal oxide and carbonaceous electrode materials. In general, metal hydroxides have less surface-dependent activity than metal oxides; thus, the power capability will be straightaway affected. This limits their performance in the SCs in terms of the power. Another critical challenge with this Ni(OH)2 is its poor durability. Since it has a layered structure, the micro-stress linked with the layered structure leads to poor performance upon cycling at a high current response. Thus, the research activities are focused on improving the electrochemical performance such as cycle stability, power capability, and further increasing the Ni(OH)2 using various approaches.164–167 Among the approaches, the formulation of the composite with either metal oxide/sulfide to circumvent the above issues is efficient.

Recently, MnCo2O4@Ni(OH)2 belt-based core–shell nanostructure-based composite was reported to construct high-performance SCs.168 It was observed that the core–shell structure showed much higher specific capacitance (2154 F g−1) than the bare MnCo2O4 and Ni(OH)2. The same trend was observed at higher rates as well. However, Ni(OH)2 into the core–shell structure leads to a marginal increase in the ESR. This implied that the nanosheet morphology of the Ni(OH)2 in the MnCo2O4 compound largely enhanced the electrical contact with the Ni-foam current collector; as a result, the active material was highly utilized. Further, it helps shorten the path lengths for both the electrolyte ions and electrons, promoting the diffusion and migration of electrolyte ions during the rapid charge–discharge process. Although the high specific capacitance was achieved, only 6.5% of the capacitance is extracted when they were explored in ASC with AC. As a result, the energy and power densities of the configuration were limited to 48 W h kg−1 and 14.9 kW kg−1, respectively. Unfortunately, the cycle life of the ASC is limited to 2500 cycles (Fig. 17),168 which clearly showed that the formulation of the composite with MnCo2O4 is not effective for supercapacitors.

6.2. Ni(OH)2–metal sulphides

Researchers have broadly explored the transition metal hydroxides and oxides for high-performance pseudocapacitors due to their great benefits of low cost, less toxicity, and excellent flexibility in the morphology and structure. Nevertheless, significant methodologies have been developed for three-dimensional conductive nanostructures such as carbonaceous materials to deposit these pseudocapacitive materials, which improved the rate capability and shortened the diffusion pathways for ions and electrons; however, there are a lot of issues that disturb the selection of these backbone materials. For instance, the fabrication of carbon nanomaterials such as graphene could limit their practical applications. Also, the performance of the transition metal oxides such as CoOX in the hybrid materials would hastily decrease with more cycles because of the poor electronic conductivity even though they possess high pseudocapacitance. Similarly, inorganic materials, such as porous Au183 and H–TiO2,184 do not hold a specific capacitance. Therefore, developing the structural hierarchy of these backbone materials with high specific capacitance and good electrical conductivity has been interesting, with high ionic penetrability and specific surface area. Recently, metal sulfides as scaffolds are widely used with pseudocapacitive materials, which could perform as a highly conductive substrate, permit the fast electron/ion transfer effectively, and initiate the active sites rapidly for a higher rate capability.185 Also, apart from the good electrochemical properties, metal sulfides have higher conductivity than their compatible metal hydroxides or oxides due to their narrowband gap.186,187

In a recent study, Zhu and co-workers188 studied the performance of hexagonal prism-such as Co9S8@Ni(OH)2 core–shell nanotubes grown on carbon fibers in ASC configuration. Here, the one-dimensional Co9S8 nanotubes served as an ideal backbone to improve the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets' electrical conductivity and provide a large number of active sites that helped improve the charge storage capacity of the Ni(OH)2. First, a typical CBD process was employed to prepare Co9S8 on the pre-treated carbon fibers, followed by a hydrothermal treatment for sulfide precipitation to attain the nanotube arrays. In the second step, the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets were coated on Co9S8 through the electro-deposition process. This formation of Co9S8@Ni(OH)2 core–shell nanotube arrays structure design involves three steps. Briefly, the starting material Co(CO3)0.35·Cl0.20(OH)110·1.74H2O nanoneedle arrays were formed on the carbon fibers when the CoCl2 solution was used in the CBD, and this precursor was transformed into sulfide via an anion exchange reaction using S2− ions in the hydrothermal treatment. Then, the obtained material after the hydrothermal reaction was subjected to calcination under an inert atmosphere, aiding the deposition of the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets on its conductive scaffold nanotube arrays. It was noted that the optimized composition of the Co9S8@Ni(OH)2 electrode showed a high specific capacity of 149 mA h g−1 in 6 M KOH. Impressively, this device can be operated in a high working voltage of 1.8 V, and a further increase in the voltage instigates the significant polarization. Moreover, the specific capacity of 75 mA h g−1 can be achieved for this core–shell electrode of Co9S8@Ni(OH)2. The full-cell with AC showed a maximum energy density of 31.35 W h kg−1 and 97.3% retention of its initial capacity after 5000 cycles. 6.3. Ni(OH)2–metal selenides

It is imperative to find out the innovative positive and negative electrodes with substantial electrochemical performance to elicit a worthy ASC device. However, the electrode materials being used in the supercapacitors currently, such as carbonaceous materials, conducting polymers, especially transition metal compounds having nanostructures, are receiving prodigious attention because they can store the charge as well afford rich faradaic reactions. These transition metal compounds, including hydroxides, sulfides, and oxides, possess discrimination in good conductivity by increasing the electrodes' charge transfer resistance and sheet resistance. Therefore, discovering a higher conductivity of the ideal electrode materials has become an inevitable issue in the goal of achieving high-performance ASC. Also, it should be noted that the same group elements O, S, and Se in the periodic table express similar electrochemical properties. In particular, Se displays a higher conductivity of 1 × 10−3 S m−1 when S only exhibits a conductivity of 5 × 10−28 S m−1, revealing the greater electroactivity and speedier electrochemical reaction of Se.189,190 Consequently, transition metal selenides have become a great choice for supercapacitors as a faradaic battery-type electrode in recent years due to their intrinsic physicochemical properties such as superior electrical conductivity, various oxidation states, and distinct geometric structures.

Very recently, Kirubasankar et al.191 synthesized a two-dimensional MoSe2–Ni(OH)2 using a one-step hydrothermal process. Here, the authors planned to select β-Ni(OH)2 because its stability in an alkaline medium would help Ni in the redox process with the electrolyte ions to improve the performance. Similarly, among the transition metal diselenides, MoSe2 possesses a layered sandwich structure along with other astonishing features, for instance, larger interlayer spacing (0.65 nm), multiple oxidation states (+2 to +6), small bandgap (1.33–1.72 eV), greater mechanical strength, and most importantly, the electrochemically active unsaturated edges make them shaped as an extra pseudocapacitive behavior electrode. The nanosheet-like morphology was found for this nanohybrid with an average thickness of ∼17 nm, where the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets presented only ∼11.5 nm. However, the MoSe2–Ni(OH)2 exhibited a high specific surface area of 121 m2 g−1 compared to the surface area of the Ni(OH)2 nanosheets (72 m2 g−1). The hybrid exhibits a lower capacitance than the metal oxide composites but renders decent performance in the ASC configuration with AC in 6 M KOH. The maximum specific capacitance of 124 F g−1 is observed. The ASC cell showed the maximum energy and power densities of 43 W h kg−1 and 0.817 kW kg, respectively. The cell can work up to 1.6 V with good stability of over 1600 cycles; however, the capacity loss was observed due to the slight increase in the internal resistance. Here, the loss in the capacitance is related to the structure and 85% of the initial capacitance is retained after 5000 cycles. However, this kind of metal diselenides–Ni(OH)2 composite would pave the way of obtaining a high-performance ASC device by pairing with the suitable negative material in different media.

6.4 Ni(OH)2–metal phosphide

In the hybrid-storage materials, transition metal phosphides (MaPb, where M = Ti, V, Mn, Fe, Ni, Co, Cu, and Zn; a = 1 or 2; b = 1, 2, 3, or 4) are anticipated to play major portions as a new interesting class of electrode materials in the improvement of high-performance supercapacitors because of their remarkable properties such as high theoretical specific capacitance, good electrical conductivity, environmentally benign nature, high chemical stability, and great abundance in nature. Remarkably, metal phosphides offer pseudocapacitance as electrode materials, which also act as an electrical conductivity additive, motivating researchers to study the electrochemical properties of these materials by developing the composites, especially with the higher capacitance of transition metal hydroxides as carbonaceous materials such as graphene fail to provide pseudocapacitance to the electrodes. Among these phosphides, cobalt phosphide has a different combination of Co in the structure, in which covalently bonded Co2+ in CoP acquires high capacitance via faradaic reactions after storing the energy, and Co joined by metallic bonds greatly develops the electrical conductivity by providing free electrons that benefit from accomplishing the good rate capability of supercapacitors.163,192

More recently, Fang and co-workers193 successfully synthesized CoP on the carbon cloth first via electrodeposition, followed by vapor phase phosphidation process using sodium hypophosphite at 350 °C for 2 h. Again, the electrodeposition process was carried out to grow Ni(OH)2 on the surface of the CoP nanosheet arrays in a three-electrode system, thereby preparing the porous CoP@Ni(OH)2, thus evading the usage of additives. The two different kinds of combinations of Co in the CoP material have been confirmed through the XPS analysis. As expected, the porous composite exhibited high capacitance (1989 F g−1) compared to CoP (645 F g−1) and Ni(OH)2 (1659 F g−1), which affords upgrading the electrical conductivity and high capacitance of the supercapacitors when the composite is fabricated with AC in an aqueous electrolyte. Unsurprisingly, the ASC extended the working voltage up to 1.8 V with a very good capacitance of 220 and 162.9 F g−1 at current densities of 1 and 20 A g−1, respectively. In addition, the device demonstrated great cycling behavior with 89% of the original capacitance after 4000 cycles at 6 A g−1, which can also be endorsed to the high theoretical capacity of Ni(OH)2 on the CoP that increased the contact with the electrolyte. Also, the energy density can be enhanced to 89.6 W h kg−1 at 0.91 kW kg−1, with a high coulombic efficiency.

6.5 Ni(OH)2–metal doping

Recently, serpent-cactus-like Co-doped Ni(OH)2–Ni3S2 hierarchical structure composed of ultrathin nanosheets were explored for supercapacitor applications.194 It is noted that the half-cell test showed a CV curve of the electrode materials in which the CV curve was recorded from −0.2 to 0.6 V but there was no information about the reference and counter electrodes. Recently, the preparation of P-doped Ni(OH)2 over Ni-foams was reported by Li et al.195 From the observation, the P-doped sample showed better areal capacitance, which is mainly attributed to P doping because P doping on Ni(OH)2 enhances the active sites and also improves the conductivity. Hence, ASC was assembled with Au–Ni(OH)2 as the positive electrode and an AC counter electrode with 1 M KOH as the electrolyte. Further, the cell Au–Ni(OH)2/AC-based ASC retained 84% of its initial specific capacity after 10000 cycles with a maximum energy of 51.2 W h kg−1.

7. Ni(OH)2–polymers

In the various strategies of preparing a Ni(OH)2 composite electrode for ASC, the development of hybrid materials holding high electrical conductivity with Ni(OH)2 and employing high chemically stable conducting substrates have become an advisable choice to improve the performance as a result of the low electrical conductivity of the Ni(OH)2, displaying only ∼10−15 Ω−1 m−1.198 Accordingly, Ni(OH)2 donates only a small surface for the redox reactions observed, and does not provide accessibility to the electrolyte ions in its more available sites.199 Several attempts have inquired about resolving these downsides affected by the low electrical conductivity, comprising a synthesis of the thin films, porous materials, nanoparticles, and nanosheets to prepare the Ni(OH)2 surface sites for more electrolyte ions transportable.166,200 Besides, the formation of composites with conducting polymer hybrids or carbon materials or the combination of both with Ni(OH)2 could enhance the electrical conductivity by incorporating the effective conductive network, which would be anticipated to provide long cycle life of the ASC. This kind of results has been debated in this section elaborately.

Recently, g-C3N4@Ni(OH)2 honeycomb nanostructure was developed, and its electrochemical supercapacitor performances were studied in 6 M KOH aqueous alkaline electrolyte.201 A class of 1.3 V g-C3N4@Ni(OH)2/rGO-based ASC on Ni-foam was fabricated using the optimized characteristics from the three-electrode system configuration. Here, g-C3N4, a soft polymer having good chemical stability and well-crystallized materials with lamellar structure favor charge transfer along the horizontal direction. This further supports perpendicular charge-transporting direction in the charge–discharge process, facilitating electron transportation along that direction.202–204 An ASC exhibited a maximum cell capacitance of 51 F g−1 and with capacitance retention of 72% after 8000 cycles. Moreover, the high energy density of 43.1 W h kg−1 was realized, which clearly reveals that the inclusion of g-C3N4 is one of the effective approaches to build high-performance supercapacitors.

Here, the PVdF–AB seed layer played a crucial role in the high quantity growth of the hybrid nanostructures. Also, it was understood that the combination of Ni and PVdF–AB could increase the binding force between the MnO2–Ni(OH)2 nanosheets and the low contact resistance of NF so that the hybrid materials might utilize the 3D NF surface to grow the MnO2-based active material by delivering more active sites. Interestingly, the device could retain 86% capacitance after 10000 cycles even in 1 M KOH, which revealed long cycling performance with a high energy density of 37.2 W h kg−1 (Fig. 23).205

8. Full-redox system

The design of the alternate combination of positive and negative electrodes may possibly explore the high energy density ASC by means of increasing their capacity, along with extending the operating potential window. As discussed above, most studies reviewed the ASC using metal oxides or polymers as the battery-type faradaic positive electrode and high surface area carbonaceous materials as the capacitor-type negative electrode mostly in the configuration to increase the capacity energy density of the full device. However, the outcome of low capacitance from carbon materials restricts the high energy density of a device that makes them inadequate in meeting the demand of high energy and high power ASC. To motivate, the energy density limit of the ASC, new types of negative electrodes are needed in the combination of high capacitance and good conductivity to replace the low capacitance of the EDLC electrodes.33 Keeping this in mind, the higher energy density of the new negative electrodes such as Fe2O3,218 MoO3−x,219 and VN,220 which can work in a negative potential, has been examined with other positive electrodes Ni(OH)2, MnO2,221 and Co3O4.222 In this combination, both electrodes undergo redox reactions which offer more fast surface rich redox reactions to store the charge and represent a positive approach to improve the total energy density of the ASC, which is similar to that of conventional rechargeable batteries charge-storage mechanism.223,224

8.2 Ni(OH)2vs. γ-Fe2O3

Generally, Fe2O3 exists as two polymorphs, viz., maghemite, which occurs naturally in soils, and the α-Fe2O3 ore termed as hematite. The cubic γ-Fe2O3 named maghemite has an inverse spinel structure with 21 Fe3+ ions, 32 O2− ions, and 2 1/3 vacancies in each unit cell and several oxidation states certify the evaluation with Ni(OH)2 to exhibit the capacitance completely.237–240 As a result, the combination using hierarchical β-Ni(OH)2 nanostructures as the positive electrode and the commercial γ-Fe2O3 nano-hexagons effectively resulted in an ASC device with an aqueous KOH electrolyte. The obtained β-Ni(OH)2 from the chemical precipitation process showed nanorod/sheet-like structures in the length of ∼20 nm and width of ∼5 nm. The sole performance of β-Ni(OH)2 has been tested and found to be 2178 F g−1 at 11 mA cm−2 thanks to the high specific surface area (181.95 m2 g−1). Correspondingly, the commercial γ-Fe2O3 unveiled a superb specific capacitance of 1800 F g−1 at a high current density of 13 mA cm−2. Also, its admirable capacitive performance was confirmed by 279.25 F g−1 at a current density of 2 mA cm−2. The high energy density of this ASC was recorded to be 99.28 W h kg−1 with retention up to 89.3% of its initial capacitance at a stable current density of 15 mA cm−2 after 5000 cycles.241 This outstanding electrochemical property might be related to the fast and proficient ion pathways resulting from the great surface area of β-Ni(OH)2 and acquired favorable reaction kinetics.

8.3 Ni(OH)2vs. RuO2

RuO2 has marked its name as the first electrode material to exhibit pseudocapacitive behavior, introducing a new type of electrochemical capacitor in 1971 by involving faradaic processes. Since its discovery, RuO2 have been employed as a positive electrode for an ASC on account of its high theoretical capacity (1358 F g−1), low internal resistance, and high electrical conductivity (3 × 102 Ω−1 cm−1). However, the high cost of RuO2 hampers its large-scale applications even after its approval as a perfect pseudocapacitive material for an ASC.242–246 Hence, researchers have endeavored to produce RuO2 hybrids with other pseudocapacitive materials or carbon that decrease the quantity of high-cost RuO2 and uphold high capacitance simultaneously. Dai and co-workers247 coupled the pseudocapacitive materials Ni(OH)2 and RuO2 to assemble the ASC device in a 1.5 V window. Chemically exfoliated graphene assisted in adorning the single-crystalline hexagonal Ni(OH)2 nano-plates on their nanosheets first via a two-step solution-phase method, which also aided to control the nano-crystalline morphology of Ni(OH)2 that brought up good stability during the cycling process. Further, RuO2–graphene was prepared using the same approach where RuO2 nanoparticles were decorated in <10 nm on graphene nanosheets. The combination of Ni(OH)2–graphene and RuO2–graphene resulted in a high specific capacitance of ∼160 F g−1 in 1 M KOH. Even though a high current density (20 A g−1) was obtained, a handy ∼97 F g−1 was gained mainly due to the discrete properties of electrode materials that contain high theoretical capacitance. Still, the assembled ASC device showed capacitance retention of ∼92% after ∼5000 cycles. Most importantly, this ASC device demonstrated a high energy density of ∼48 W h kg−1 at a power of ∼0.23 kW kg−1.

9. Outlook

For the past few years, Ni(OH)2 has fascinated much more attention due to its proficient high storage capacity and electrochemical catalytic activity toward ORR and OER applications. In this review, the performance of Ni(OH)2 as an energy storage material in the aqueous asymmetric capacitor was examined. A large number of studies focused on this material as a positive electrode due to its high theoretical capacity. Most of the studies are mainly dedicated to aqueous battery application as a positive electrode due to the active potential window of Ni(OH)2. There are various phase structures such as α, β, and γ-Ni(OH)2 phases, which present different characteristics. At the same time, various morphologies such as nanoflower, nanosheets, nanospheres, nanowires, nanoparticles, etc., of α and β-Ni(OH)2-based materials were synthesized and characterized. All the reported morphologies exhibited excellent electrochemical storage behavior than the other metal hydroxides. Since it has a layered structure, the accumulation of charge carriers showed a potential plateau region at a potential window of 0.3 V with high experimental capacitance.

We have comprehensively analyzed the combination of Ni(OH)2 with various materials, including carbon-based nanomaterials, metal oxides, conductive polymers, phosphides, sulfides, and selenides. Primary and binary metal oxides such as Mn2O3, Co3O4, ZnCo2O4, and MnCo2O4 have presented reasonable capacitance as the positive electrode when combined with Ni(OH)2 as the poor electrical conductivity despoils their power density and cycle life. Conductive polymers hold extra advantages of greater conductivity, flexibility, and ease of handling but the composite undergoes volume expansion/shrinking in the course of the charge–discharge processes, which curtails their cycle stability. Carbonaceous materials such as graphene, CNTs, and biomass-derived carbons offer outstanding opportunities due to their distinct properties and structures. The unusual integrated combination in graphene-based materials, viz., electrical, mechanical, and thermal properties, creates more expectations to offer excellent benefits from this nanostructured material, forcing the CNTs in more applications. CNTs have also enticed more with Ni(OH)2 owing to their high electrical conductivity and rapid ion-transport advantages; however, the high processing cost of purification impedes their commercial applications. Both sp2 bonded carbon materials graphene and CNTs face stacking/aggregation issues but can be rectified. Besides, the easy availability, low cost, flexibility in synthesis, and environmental friendliness of biomass-derived carbon captivates their combination with Ni(OH)2, and it has to be developed and explored well. Furthermore, oxides and sulfides have increased the charge transfer resistance while phosphides and selenides present good capacitance and energy density when combined with Ni(OH)2 separately.

Although it exhibits battery-type behavior, most studies have reported the specific capacitance value in the F g−1 unit. Since capacitance is voltage-dependent, it may not be advisable to estimate and report the specific capacitances for such faradaic-type materials. Instead of calculating the specific capacitances, it is much more meaningful and reasonable to estimate the value as “capacity” for the Ni(OH)2 based systems, particularly in three-electrode systems.248 The performance concluded that Ni(OH)2 possesses battery-type faradaic behavior from the CV and galvanostatic studies. However, the charge-storage process in pseudocapacitance occurs from the faradaic mechanism for the electrode, in which the electrochemical behavior reveals a capacitive nature. It is well-understood that charge storage is linearly dependent on the charging potential, which implies the definition of capacitance, also known by dQ/dV.

Consequently, Ni(OH)2 should not be considered as a pseudocapacitive electrode, and the quantity of charge-storage capacities of Ni(OH)2 has to be evaluated in terms of mAh g−1 or C g−1 unit as their electrochemical behavior does not exhibit the capacitance characteristics because of the faradaic charge-storage mechanism and the phase transitions. Fortunately, none of the reports has focused on this point; we forced this here because the layered structural materials may collapse when tested at high current rate. Since the electrochemical capacitors are power-dependent devices, this may not be suitable for high-power applications in practice due to their poor rate capability. This can be improved by preparing a composite with metal oxides and carbonaceous materials before using the high current rate. At the same time, it will be a potential candidate as a positive electrode for higher energy applications due to its high energy density.

On the other hand, poor stability is another prime issue though it delivers high energy to the full device. Many of the studies presented only a limited number of cycles, indicating the poor cycle life of these materials at the high current rate. The cycle life may be increased without altering the structure if it is rated at a low current. Few attempts assist in improving the cycle life of Ni(OH)2; in particular, metal ion-doped composites exhibited better stability. Among these, limited metal dopants such as Ti, Al, and Si are specifically encouraged to use as dopants to enhance the Ni(OH)2 performance. Although several attempts have been made, still the performance of the Ni(OH)2-based ASC is far away from practical applications in terms of cyclability and energy density. There are many reasons such as high ESR, poor rate capability, and cycle life, which restricts their utilization the commercial level. Undoubtedly, one can achieve or overcome these issues; Ni(OH)2 will be a potential positive electrode in electrochemical energy storage applications, particularly in hybrid devices. Thus, we conclude that Ni(OH)2 can be used as a positive electrode in any hybrid device (specifically aqueous) for extracting more energy rather than power.

Out of all the specific factors related to Ni(OH)2, the overall supercapacitor performance is based not only on the specific electrode but also on the fabrication devices and other materials related to the cell performance. For example, most of the lab-scale techniques could not be adopted during the scaling-up process, such as using a binder in the electrode preparation will increase the ESR, and the active materials will easily peel from the substrate upon the degradation of polymer binders. Hence, electrode preparation will play a crucial role in the device's performance. As mentioned, the choice of the electrolyte is also a critical part; this should have high ionic conductivity, wide thermal and electrochemical stability, and inherent safety. In this view, aqueous electrolytes will be the best choice, including the reasons behind the high toxicity of organic electrolytes thought they guarantee a wider operating voltage but also incompatibility of some pseudocapacitive materials with ionic liquids or organic electrolytes. On the other hand, the electrolyte with a pH closer to neutral or neutral will strictly increase the ESR due to the poor conductivity of the electrolyte. Thus, from our observation, Ni(OH)2 exhibits compatibility with an alkaline medium, particularly KOH.

Ni(OH)2 has also been synthesized using electrochemical methods and used as a binder-free electrode in supercapacitor applications. Ni foam is mostly used as a growth substrate for good adhesion and used as a current collector to increase the electrical conductivity; this may be a good option for the time being; however, stability and scalability are questionable while running the cell for a long time. In addition, the high cost of the Ni foam restricts their practical applications; other low-cost substrates such as Al or stainless steel might be a replacement for the fabrication of supercapacitors. Although the recent development of cable/fiber-type energy storage is attractive in terms of the lightweight and good flexibility, they are expected to accommodate more in modern flexible/miniaturized and wearable applications such as electronic skins, flexible mobile phones textiles/fabrics, and on-body sensors, which could pave the way to avoid the metal substrates that tend to increase the weight of the device and show limited flexibility. Hence, electrochemically synthesized electrode materials may not be the right choice for high-end high power applications, and achieving a large capacitance value is also practically impossible due to the experimental limitations.

As discussed above, in diverse applications ranging from electric vehicles to consumer appliances, supercapacitors can influence in providing the demand energy and power density for these applications. At present, supercapacitors having a lower energy density (5–10 W h kg−1) perform a supportive role in some of these applications. However, the recent research progress affords high energy supercapacitors in the range of ∼40–100 W h kg−1, which encouragingly builds the platform for the next choice in energy-storage systems. Though it is especially important to note that the above results are contradictory to each other, investigation is the main drawback here, and still, a huge challenge to conclude the performance of devices as some of the studies have conveyed the capacitance based on the total mass of the active materials and remaining calculated from the total cell of the device. We strongly recommend here to follow the consistency in the calculation of specific capacitance that supports the comparison of the performance of various studies, faciliates the easy categorization based on the devices, and helps to design new electrode materials for an ASC. By considering all these arguments mentioned above in mind, the new ideas for the proper combination of electrode materials have to be constructed to improve the energy density and overall electrochemical performance. In addition, more research activities also must be kept ongoing for the possibility of rolling them out in the industry with high device performance as well. Thus, developing a next-generation supercapacitor with a suitable combination would efficiently store the energy and supply uninterruptible power for a sustainable earth.
