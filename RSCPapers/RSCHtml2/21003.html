Synthesis, photophysical and electrochemical properties and DSSC application of triphenylaminochalcone cored dendrimers with anthracene and pyrene as surface groups and triazolylcholanoate as the bridging unit

Abstract

Triphenylaminochalcone-cored dendrimers decorated with anthracene and pyrene at the periphery have been synthesized by a convergent methodology using a click chemistry approach and characterized from spectral data and elemental analyses. All the dendrimers show excellent optical and electrochemical properties. Higher generation dendrimers exhibit better power conversion efficiency than lower-generation dendrimers when used as additives in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs).

Introduction

Conjugated organic supramolecules have attracted considerable research interest in recent years, owing to their potential applications in fluorescence imaging,1 chemo sensors2 and as optical power limiting materials3 and active gain media for light-emitting devices.4 Anthracene is one of the most common fluorophores, possessing good aggregation behaviour due to its unique chemical and electron-rich structure, and a low electronic band gap and strong blue fluorescence which lead to blue emission in OLEDs.5 Anthracene-containing polymers have attracted much attention owing to their interesting photophysical and optoelectronic properties and possible applications for photovoltaics and organic field-effect transistors.612

Among the polycyclic aromatic units such as fluorene,1315 anthracene16,17 and pyrene,18,19 pyrene has the best charge carrying mobility, a high photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY), and excellent thermal stability. Pyrene and its derivatives tend to aggregate, resulting in red-shifted emission and low PLQYs in the solid state, and this can be suppressed by introducing bulky groups and star-shaped structures. The pyrene moiety with appropriate structural modification has been widely studied in various areas of chemical biology as a fluorophore.

Organic molecular assemblies can function as alternate potential low-cost additives for solar energy conversion and have attracted considerable attention due to their potential applications in flat panel displays due to outstanding advantages such as self-emission and operating at low voltage with high brightness. Triphenylamine and its derivatives have an important place in DSSC research as molecules with multiple functions, most notably electron donation when linked with a sensitized dye and hole transport.

In recent years a new class of macromolecules known as dendrimers has received tremendous interest in various areas such as solar cells,20 light harvesting systems,21 catalysis,22 molecular encapsulation23 and also in medical fields.24 We report herein the synthesis of triphenylaminochalcone cored demdrimers 110 decorated with pyrene and anthracene surface units by a convergent strategy via click chemistry. The photophysical, electrochemical and Dye Sensitized Solar Cell (DSSC) properties of the synthesized dendrimers 110 have been also focused on in the present investigation (Fig. 1).

Dye-sensitized solar cell studies

In an attempt to improve the solar cell performance, studies were carried out to examine the role of the electrolytic solution in dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs).27 In general, various nitrogen containing heterocycles such as aminopyridine, pyrimidine, pyrrolidinopyridine, and pyrrolidinoethylpyridine have been used as additives to drastically increase the open-circuit voltage (Voc) due to the electron donating properties of the nitrogen lone pair.28 Hence, we have designed and synthesized a new series of electron rich anthracene and pyrene based dendrimers 110 and employed them as additives for DSSCs. The photovoltaic performance data of the short-circuit current (Jsc), the open-circuit voltage (Voc), the fill factor and the energy conversion efficiency () are summarized in Tables 4 and 5. As seen in Fig. 5 (IV curves of DSSCs), the device performance, such as the short circuit current density (Jsc) and open circuit voltage (Voc), is enhanced with anthracene and pyrene units and also with the triphenylamine core unit. While comparing the efficiency of fabricated DSSCs using anthraceno and pyrenodendrimers the efficiency and Voc increase relatively due to the introduction of pyrene groups, in particular in the second generation dendrimer 10. Among the tested dendrimers, second-generation dendrimer 10 displayed the largest Voc (900 mV) and Jsc (12.6 mA cm2) values and the highest efficiency is 8.5% (Table 5 and Fig. 5b). From the results it is clear that as the dendritic generation increases the power conversion efficiency () also increases.

In conclusion, dendrimers with anthracene and pyrene as the surface and triazole as the bridging unit were synthesized in good yields by a convergent synthetic strategy using a click chemistry approach. UV studies of the synthesized dendrimers indicate that as the number of anthracene and pyrene units increases, the absorption coefficient also increases, and hence the light absorbed by the dendritic antennae increases with an increase in the dendrimer generation. In the cyclic voltammogram studies, as the dendritic generation increases from zero to second generation, the cathodic peak potential is shifted to the less positive potential side which might be due to the presence of more anthracene and pyrene units. Further, the DSSC performance also increases as the number of anthracene and pyrene units increases with the increase in the generation of dendrimers. Dendrimers 9 and 10 exhibited better power conversion efficiency () values of 8.0 and 8.5%, respectively, when used as additives in DSSCs.

Experimental section

General information

All chemicals and solvents were purchased commercially and used as such without further purification. All melting points were determined using a Toshniwal melting point apparatus by the open capillary tube method and are uncorrected. ESI-MS spectra were measured on a JEOL DX-303 mass spectrometer. Elemental analyses were carried out using a Perkin-Elmer CHNS 2400 instrument. Column chromatography was performed on silica gel (ACME, 100200 mesh). 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on Bruker 300 MHz instruments. Chemical shifts are given in parts per million, and J values are in hertz. UV-vis absorption spectra were measured with a Perkin-Elmer Lambda 35 UV-vis spectrometer. Steady-state fluorescence spectra were recorded using a Fluoromax 4 (Horiba JobinYvon) spectrofluorimeter equipped with a Xe-150 W lamp.
General procedure for synthesis of monobromoacetyl derivatives (procedure A). To a solution of propargyl ester (1 mmol) in dry CHCl3 (20 mL), anhydrous K2CO3 was added. To this mixture, bromoacetyl bromide (1 mmol) was added dropwise at 0 C and the reaction mixture was stirred for 2 h. After the completion of the reaction, water (5 mL) was added and it was extracted with CHCl3 (2 75 mL). The chloroform layer was separated and washed with saturated NaHCO3 (5 mL) and brine (100 mL). The chloroform layer was dried over anhydrous Na2SO4. The chloroform layer was filtered and evaporated completely. The crude product was purified by column chromatography with 20% ethyl acetate in hexane as the eluent to give the corresponding monobromoacetyl derivative.
General procedure for azidation of dendritic bromides/chlorides (procedure B). To the solution of the corresponding bromide/chloride (1 equiv.) in dry DMF (10 mL), sodium azide (1.5 equiv.) was added and stirring was continued at 60 C for 12 h. The reaction mixture was evaporated and extracted with CHCl3 (3 100 mL). The organic layer was washed with water (100 mL) and saturated NaCl (aq.) (3 100 mL), dried over Na2SO4. Solvent was evaporated under reduced pressure to afford the crude product, which was purified by column chromatography (SiO2), using the eluent as mentioned under each compound.
General procedure for Cu(I)-catalyzed azidealkyne cycloaddition (procedure C). A mixture of a polypropargylated derivative (1.0 equiv.), an azide functionalized compound (1.2 equiv. per azide functional unit), CuSO45H2O (0.3 equiv.), and sodium L-ascorbate (0.3 equiv.) was stirred at room temperature for 18 h in a 3:1 mixture of THF/water. After confirming the completion of the reaction with TLC, chloroform (100 mL) was added to the reaction mixture and it was washed with water (10 mL), and brine (10 mL). The organic layer was separated, dried over Na2SO4 and evaporated to obtain the crude product, which was purified by column chromatography (SiO2), using CHCl3MeOH as the eluent to give the corresponding dendrimers.
