Abstract

Stability of polymer-based bulk-heterojunction solar cells (PSCs) is an even more crucial factor than power conversion efficiency for commercialization. Here, the thermal stability of PSCs is studied using a thiopheneâ€“thiazolothiazole semiconducting polymer system. Interestingly, it was found that the cells that used polymers with a less regular side chain placement (iPTzBT) showed higher thermal stability than the cells that used polymers with a more regular placement (PTzBT). The difference can be correlated with the change in the polymer structural order upon thermally annealing the polymer/fullerene blend films. Whereas the structural order of iPTzBT is unchanged after annealing, that of PTzBT is decreased, although the crystallinity is lower in iPTzBT than in PTzBT. These results clearly demonstrate that careful design of the side chain placement in the semiconducting polymers is an important factor for improving the thermal stability of PSCs.

Introduction

A number of studies on polymer-based bulk-heterojunction solar cells (PSCs) have been carried out in the last two decades because of the great interest for flexible, light-weight, and large-area renewable energy sources that can be produced by low-cost and low-energy consumption solution-processes.1â€“3 In PSCs, typically a semiconducting polymer and a fullerene derivative, such as [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM) or [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM), are used as a p-type (electron-donor) and a n-type (electron acceptor), respectively, in the bulk-heterojunction photoactive layer. Much attention has been focused on developing new semiconducting polymers, optimizing the processing methods and structures of PSCs toward the improvement of the power conversion efficiency (PCE). These intensive studies have led to the PCE in excess of 10%.4â€“12 In addition, more recently, the development of non-fullerene n-type materials and/or the use of ternary blend bulk-heterojunction have raised the PCE over 12%.13â€“23 On the other hand, the stability (life time) of PSCs is equally or even more important than PCE for the practical applications. However, the studies on the stability improvement are very limited as compared to the studies on the PCE improvement.

Important environmental factors that are believed to cause degradation of PSCs are oxygen, water (moisture), sunlight (UV light), and heat,24 as is the case for other organic devices. Hence, the use of materials with high durability to those factors is necessary. It has been reported that the optimization of buffer layers (hole or electron transporting layers), including the use of inverted architectures, has successfully improved the stability under ambient conditions.10,25â€“27 Moreover, it should be noted that encapsulation can prevent penetration of oxygen and moisture into the cell28â€“32 and the use of UV-protection films can cut off the UV light,33 both of which are effective for increasing the stability. However, heat that is induced to the cell by continuous exposure to sunlight is directly conducted to the active and buffer layers, and thus cannot be avoided even with those protections of the cell. Therefore, the thermal stability of the materials is a necessary condition and one of the most important issues for the PSC stability. Recently, the group of Chambon34 and Osaka35 independently reported that the use of WOx as the hole transporting layer (HTL) instead of familiar MoOx in the inverted cell increased the thermal stability. Maes and co-workers demonstrated that modification of the side chain of P3HT (regioregular poly(3-hexylthiophene)) and PCPDTBT (poly{4,4-dialkyl-4H-cyclopenta[2,1-b;3,4-bâ€²]dithiophene-2,6-diyl-alt-2,1,3,-benzothiadiazole-4,7-diyl}) systems, such as introducing the hydroxyl and ester groups at the end of the alkyl group, can increase the thermal stability of their cells.36

Recently, we have reported that PSCs with an inverted architecture, based on a semiconducting polymer system consisting of thiophene, thiazolothiazole (TzTz), and naphthobisthiadiazole (NTz), PTzNTzs (Fig. 1), showed surprisingly high thermal stability in addition to relatively high PCEs of âˆ¼9%.35 PTzNTz cells that used MoOx as the HTL showed only ca. 15% drop from their initial PCE after 500 hour-storage at 85 Â°C under a N2 atmosphere (in glove box), regardless of the alkyl side chain length and the use of the solvent additive (1,8-diiodoctane: DIO) at the time of fabrication. Furthermore, when WOx was used as the HTL, PTzNTz cells showed almost negligible thermal degradation under the same conditions. In contrast, the cells using a thiopheneâ€“thiazolothiazole polymer, PTzBT (Fig. 1), even with WOx as the HTL, showed a significant drop at the initial â€œburn-inâ€ stage and resulted in the 30% drop of the PCE after 500 hours. This large difference between PTzNTz and PTzBT prompts us to further investigate the thermal stability of PSCs. In the viewpoint of the molecular structure, there are two major differences between these two polymers, that are the backbone building unit and the side chain placement (Fig. 1). As for the backbone, while PTzNTz incorporates TzTz and NTz along with alkylthiophenes, PTzBT incorporates only TzTz along with alkylthiophenes. With respect to the side chain, in PTzNTz, R1s heads â€œtowardâ€ the TzTz moiety, whereas R2s heads â€œoutwardâ€ the NTz moiety. In PTzBT, on the other hand, all the alkyl groups head â€œtowardâ€ the neighboring TzTz moieties. We hypothesized that these structural differences may impact the thermal stability of their PSCs.


Fig. 1 Chemical structure of PTzBT, PTzNTz, and iPTzBT.

In this work, we focus on the effect of the side chain placement on the PSC thermal stability. We therefore synthesized new polymers, iPTzBTs (Fig. 1), which possess the same backbone as PTzBT, but different side chain placement. In iPTzBTs, one pair of alkyl groups (R1s) heads toward the TzTz moiety, whereas the other pair (R2s) heads outward the TzTz moiety, and thus the placement is less regular than in PTzBTs (Fig. 1). We found that there was a clear difference in the thermal stability between the PSCs of PTzBTs and iPTzBTs; iPTzBTs showed higher thermal stability. The difference will be discussed by correlating the structural change in the bulk-heterojunction films upon thermal annealing.

Thermal stability of the iPTzBT and PTzBT cells

Thermal stability of the solar cells that used iPTzBTs and PTzBTs was tested according to a procedure defined by the consortia of the international summit on solar cell stability (ISOS).38,39 The cells were heated at 85 Â°C in the dark under a N2 atmosphere (glovebox), which refers to category ISOS-D-3,38 for 1000 hours. The Jâ€“V characteristics were measured at room temperature (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ). Fig. 4a shows the relative change of PCE as a function of storage time (annealing time). Interestingly, thermal stability of the cells was greatly improved in iPTzBTs than in PTzBTs. The PCE of the iPTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD cells gradually decreased by ca. 10% in 200 hours, and then plateaued thereafter. As a result, losses of the PCE after 1000 hours were 11% and 8% for iPTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD, respectively. In sharp contrast, as reported previously, the PCE of the PTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD cells decreased immediately by more than 20% within the initial 24 hours. Then the PCEs almost plateaued after 200 hours, resulting in losses of 24% and 30% after 1000 hours for PTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD, respectively. In addition, it is interesting to note that iPTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD cells fabricated with DIO as a solvent additive were also thermally stable, as losses of the PCE in the DIO-aided cells that used iPTzBT-BOBO and -BOHD after 1000 hours were 14% and 8%, respectively (Fig. S4câ€“f, ESIâ€ ).

Fig. 4bâ€“d show the relative change of JSC, VOC, and FF, respectively, as a function of storage time. In all parameters, the iPTzBT cells showed higher stability than the PTzBT cells. In particular, the loss of FF was clearly different. Whereas losses were 6â€“7% for the iPTzBTs cells, those were 14â€“23% for PTzBTs cells.

As the degradation of the FF was the main difference for the thermal stability between the iPTzBT and PTzBT cells, we investigated the change in the charge carrier mobility of the blend films after annealing at 85 Â°C. Hole-only devices (ITO/PEDOT:PSS)/(polymer/PC71BM/WOx/Ag) were fabricated, and the mobility (Î¼SCLC) was calculated using the space-charge-limited-current (SCLC) model. As the â€˜â€˜burn-inâ€™â€™ stage dominated the degradation of the solar cells, we evaluated the change in Î¼SCLC for the initial 24 hours (Fig. 5 and Fig. S6, ESIâ€ ). Ordering structure

In order to correlate the morphology and structural order of the polymer/PC71BM blends in the thin film with charge carrier transport and the photovoltaic properties, we first performed the atomic force micrography (AFM) of the blend films before and after annealing. The AFM images of all the polymer/PC71BM films became slightly rougher after annealing at 85 Â°C (Fig. S7 and S8, ESIâ€ ). However, there is no specific difference among these polymers.

We then carried out the grazing incident X-ray diffraction (GIXD) measurements. Fig. 6aâ€“d depict the two-dimensional (2D) GIXD patterns of the as-spun polymer/PC71BM blend films. The polymers were found to form a predominant face-on orientation, as the diffraction corresponding to the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking appeared in the qz axis. It is noted that, in all cases, the structural order of the polymers observed in the blend film was almost the same as in the polymer neat film (Fig. S9, ESIâ€ ). The lamellar distance (dl) of iPTzBTs was about 1 Ã… larger than that of PTzBTs (Fig. 6e, annealing time = 0), probably due to the weakened side chain interdigitation originating in the different side chain placement. Accordingly, the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance (dÏ€) of iPTzBTs was also larger than that of PTzBTs by about 0.1 Ã… (Fig. 6f, annealing time = 0); 3.75 Ã… for iPTzBTs and 3.60 Ã… for PTzBTs. We also investigated the crystallinity of the polymers in the blend film by calculating the coherence length (LC) of the lamella (Fig. 6g) and Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking (Fig. 6h) structures in the face-on crystallite using a modified Scherrer's equation (LC = 2Ï€/FWHM, where FWHM is the full width at half-maximum of the diffraction peak).40,41 The LC values of both the lamellar and Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking structures in the as-spun films were higher for PTzBTs than for iPTzBTs, indicating that the crystallinity was higher for PTzBTs. We note that, however, the enlarged intermolecular distances and decreased crystallinity in iPTzBT did not critically affect the initial performance of the cells, as described above.

By annealing the blend films at 85 Â°C, we observed a clear difference in the change of the structural order between iPTzBTs and PTzBTs. Fig. 6e and 6f show changes in dl and dÏ€ of the polymers, respectively. In iPTzBTs, both dl and dÏ€ did not change by annealing the blend films. In contrast, those increased in PTzBTs though the changes were moderate; dl increased to that of iPTzBT with the same side chains, and dÏ€ increased to 3.65 Ã…. Similarly, while LC values of the lamellar and Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking structure of iPTzBTs almost unchanged after annealing at 85 Â°C for 24 hours, those of PTzBTs decreased (Fig. 6g and h). We note that the structural order of PC71BM, in its aggregation that is detectable in 2D GIXD, did not change upon annealing (Fig. S13, ESIâ€ ). Therefore, the difference in the structural changes of the polymers upon annealing the blend films correlates well with the difference in the changes in Î¼SCLC and thus the difference in the solar cell degradation between iPTzBTs and PTzBTs. It is also interesting to note that, in the case of the neat films, only a marginal change was observed for dl, dÏ€, and LC in all the polymers after thermal annealing (Fig. S16, ESIâ€ ). Thus, the structural change by thermal annealing only occurred in the blend films of PTzBTs. We speculate that in iPTzBTs there should be larger regions where the polymer and PC71BM are well mixed than in PTzBTs, due to the lower crystallinity. This state is probably thermodynamically stable. In the meantime, in PTzBTs the polymer and PC71BM domains are well phase-separated due to the higher crystallinity of the polymers. However, some diffusion of PC71BM molecules into the polymer domains likely to occur by thermal annealing, as often seen in P3HT/PCBM bilayer films.42â€“44 This may cause the structural change in the polymers, such as the increase in intermolecular distances or the decrease in crystallinity, giving rise to the degradation of the charge carrier mobility and thus the photovoltaic performances.

Conclusions

New thiopheneâ€“thiazolothiazole semiconducting polymers, iPTzBTs, which possess the same backbone as PTzBT, but different side chain placement were synthesized in order to study the effect of side chain placement on the thermal stability of the polymer-based solar cell. iPTzBTs had a similar electronic structure to PTzBTs, but showed slightly lower crystallinity than PTzBTs. Although the solar cell performance of the cells that used iPTzBTs and PTzBTs were similar, interestingly, the thermal stability was higher in the cells with iPTzBT than in those with PTzBTs. The thermal degradation of the solar cell was consistent with the change in the charge carrier mobility of the polymers in the blend films upon thermal annealing. 2D GIXD measurements revealed that the structural order of iPTzBTs almost unchanged after annealing their blend film at 85 Â°C, whereas that of PTzBTs decreased. The structural change agreed well with the degradation of the solar cell as well as the mobility. These results clearly demonstrated that the side chain placement of the semiconducting polymers is an important factor that impacts the thermal stability of PSCs. We believe that this work provides an important guideline for developing new semiconducting polymers that afford highly efficient and highly thermally stable PSCs.

Experimental

Solar cell fabrication and measurement

ITO substrates were first pre-cleaned sequentially by sonicating in a detergent bath with de-ionized water, acetone, and isopropanol at rt, and in a boiled isopropanol bath each for 10 min, and then baked at 120 Â°C for 10 min in air. The substrates were then subjected to an UV/ozone treatment at rt for 20 min. ZnO layers were prepared by spin-coating (at 5000 rpm) the precursor solution prepared from 0.4 M zinc acetate dihydrate in 0.3 M monoethanolamine and 2-methoxyethanol. ZnO substrates were immediately baked in air at 200 Â°C for 30 min, and then rinsed with acetone, isopropanol, and finally were washed in a boiled isopropanol bath for 10 min. A solution of the polymer/PC71BM blend was prepared by mixing them (weight ratio of 1:2) in a CB with a concentration of 5 g Lâˆ’1 based on the polymer. The photoactive layer was deposited, in a glove box, by spin coating the solution at 600 rpm for 20 s, in which the solution was kept heated at 100 Â°C. The thin films were transferred into a vacuum evaporator connected to the glove box, and the WOx layer (15 nm) and the Ag layer (100 nm) were deposited sequentially through a shadow mask. The active area of the cells was 0.16 cm2 (4 mm Ã— 4 mm).

Jâ€“V characteristics were measured using a Keithley 2400 source-measure unit under 1 Sun (AM1.5G) conditions using a solar simulator (SAN-EI Electric, XES-40S1) in a glovebox. The light intensity for the Jâ€“V measurements was calibrated with a reference PV cell (KONICA MINOLTA AK-100 certified at National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology, Japan). EQE spectra were measured with a Spectral Response Measuring System (SOMA OPTICS, LTD., S-9241). Thermal stability test of the cells was carried out in the glovebox, where the cells were heated at 85 Â°C on a hot plate for 1000 hours. During the annealing, the cells were subjected to the Jâ€“V measurements as described above at rt in the glovebox.
