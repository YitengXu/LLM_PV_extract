Engineering the electronic and optoelectronic properties of InX (X = S, Se, Te) monolayers via strain

Hao Jin a, Jianwei Li a, Ying Dai b and Yadong Wei *a
aCollege of Physics and Energy, Shenzhen University, Shenzhen 518060, People's Republic of China. E-mail: ywei@szu.edu.cn
bSchool of Physics, State Key Laboratory of Crystal Materials, Jinan, 250100, People's Republic of China

Received 29th November 2016 , Accepted 9th January 2017

First published on 9th January 2017

Abstract

In this paper, we present a comprehensive study on the electronic and optoelectronic properties of indium monochalcogenide (InX with X = S, Se, Te) monolayers with and without strains. Our results show that InX monolayers are indirect semiconductors. Upon the application of strain, the band structures can be modulated and an indirect-to-direct bandgap transition is observed in an InSe monolayer. The electron mobility of up to 2.0 103 cm2 (V s)1 is quantitatively determined in the framework of deformation potential theory. Though the mobility of holes is relatively small, it can be greatly improved by introducing compressive strain, with a value up to 2.8 103 cm2 (V s)1. In addition, the performance of the photoresponse of InX monolayers is evaluated based on first-principles calculations. Under illumination, the InX based systems exhibit high photoresponsivity (Rph = 0.18 A W1) and external quantum efficiency (EQE = 62.5%), which can be further enhanced via strain. Owing to such excellent electronic and optoelectronic merits, InX monolayers will become promising candidates for next-generation ultrathin and flexible electronic and optoelectronic devices.

1 Introduction

Since the successful synthesis of graphene, there has been growing interest in atomically two-dimensional (2D) materials for applications in electronics and optoelectronics, including optical sensors, solar cells, and light-emitting diodes.13 A large number of 2D materials, such as transition metal chalcogenides (TMDs)46 and black phosphorus (BP),7,8 have been experimentally fabricated or theoretically predicted. As a class of layered semiconducting materials, indium monochalcogenides (InX with X = S, Se, Te) have attracted intense interest due to their exotic electronic structures and optoelectronic properties.911 For example, Sucharitakul and co-workers11 demonstrated that the intrinsic electron mobility of InSe obtained by mechanical exfoliation exceeds 103 cm2 (V s)1, which is well above that of many other 2D semiconductors.6,12,13 In recent experiments, few-layered GaS, GaSe, and InSe have been proven to be promising candidates in fields such as optoelectronics, field-effect transistors (FETs), and solar energy conversion.10,1416 Despite this recent explosion of experimental results, theoretical investigations on the optoelectronic properties of the InX (X = S, Se, and Te) monolayers are still in their early stages. Additionally, little is known about how strains influence the photoresponse or photovoltaic performance of 2D material based nano-devices. Thus, new findings in strain-engineered modulation on the electronic and optoelectronic properties of InX monolayers are highly desirable.

In this work, to figure out the strain-induced electronic and optoelectronic properties of InX systems, a comprehensive study is presented in the framework of density functional theory (DFT). Our results show that the band gaps of InX monolayers are tunable over a relatively large range. A transition between indirect and direct band-gaps is observed for the InSe monolayer under compressive strain, which favors the solar energy conversion. In addition, the hole mobility can be greatly improved by introducing compressive strain, with the value up to 2.8 103 cm2 (V s)1, which is nearly two orders of magnitude higher than the unstrained case. For the first time, the performance of the photoresponse of InX monolayers is evaluated based on the first-principles calculations. Upon illumination, InX monolayers exhibit high photoresponsivity (1.8 101 A W1), which is about 1 order of magnitude higher than that of MoS2 based phototransistors (1.6 102 A W1) and 3 orders of magnitude higher than that of graphene based photodetectors (5 104 A W1).17,18 The performance of the InX monolayers can be further enhanced by the strains. These results demonstrate that InX monolayers are a new category of 2D semiconductors with high potential for novel applications in electronic and optoelectronic devices.

2 Computational methods

2.1 DFT calculations

Spin-polarized DFT calculations are performed using the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP).19,20 The projector augmented wave (PAW) method21 is used from the standard VASP library with the PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE) generalized gradient approximation (GGA) exchange correlation functional.22 Ground state structures are found by allowing all systems to fully relax with respect to both the atomic positions and the supercell shape and size using a conjugate-gradient method with an energy convergence of 106 eV and a force convergence of 103 eV 1. The cutoff energy for the basis set is chosen to be 500 eV, and the Brillouin zone is sampled with a MonkhorstPack grid of 18 18 1.23 In this work, periodic boundary conditions are applied and a vacuum space of 20 along the z direction is set to avoid the interactions between two slabs in the nearest-neighbor unit cells, which gives well-converged results.

The state-of-the-art hybrid functional (HSE06) is used for all calculations,24,25 in which the hybrid functional is mixed with 25% exact HartreeFock (HF) exchange. A damped vdW correction based on Grimme's scheme is also incorporated to better describe the nonbonding interaction.26

The strain-induced bandgap engineering has recently been demonstrated to be a powerful technique for semiconductors, in particular for 2D materials due to their flexible features. In experiments, the 2D materials can be deformed using an atomic force microscope (AFM) or by a two-point bending method.34,35 It is predicted and confirmed that appropriate strains can exert an important influence on the electronic structures and optical properties, and trigger internal changes of the system.3537 Thus, it is of significance to investigate how the strains influence the electronic properties of InX monolayers. We probe this question by introducing 3% to 3% biaxial strains on InX monolayers and keep the crystal symmetries. The isotropic strain is defined as = a/a0, where a0 is the equilibrium lattice constant and a is the deformation of a0. As shown in Fig. 2d, when the compressive strain is applied on the single-layer InSe, the transition between indirect and direct band gaps is observed, in which the CBM and the VBM are located at the -point. The direct-band-gap InSe monolayer is expected to have higher solar energy conversion efficiency compared to those of indirect-band-gap monolayers. By contrast, an indirect band gap is observed for the InSe monolayer when the tensile strain is applied as shown in Fig. 2f.

In Fig. 3, we plot the band gap evolution of InX monolayers as a function of the strains. The results show that for InS and InSe monolayers, the band gaps reach the maximum values when the compressive strain ( = 1%) is applied, and decrease gradually with the increase of the compressive strain. In the case of tensile strain, the band gaps decrease approximately linearly, and reduce by 20% and 19% for InS and InSe monolayers under 3% tensile strains, respectively. For the InTe monolayer, the bandgap peaks with a maximum value Eg = 2.32 eV at = 1% tensile strain, and decreases to 1.96 eV when the compressive strain ( = 3%) is applied. In addition, as the chalcogen species changes from S to Te, the band gaps decrease, which can be attributed to the decrease in ionicity.9,38

To obtain a deep insight into the strain-engineered modulation on the electronic structures of the InX monolayers, we analyze the physical origin of the atomic orbitals at the band edge positions. The InSe monolayer is taken as an example. In Fig. 4a and b, the band-decomposed charge densities of the CBM and the VBM at the point are plotted. The results indicate that at the point, the CBM is dominated by the Se 4pz and In 5s orbitals, while the valence band 3 is mainly composed of the Se 4px and 4py states. When the compressive strain () is applied, the position of the valence band 1+ goes down, and eventually the VBM occurs at the point (see Fig. 2d). This can be understood in terms of the chemical bonding. The bond length dInSe decreases with the increase of , giving rise to a larger overlapping of the charge density between In and Se atoms. The stronger bonding between In and Se atoms indicates that the valence band 1+, which is hybridized by the In 5s, 5pz, and Se 4pz states, becomes more stable for the electrons and thus moves to the lower energy region. When is large enough, i.e. 3% in our case, the energy level of 3 is higher than that of 1+, and the indirect-to-direct band-gap transition occurs. On the other hand, when the tensile strain is applied, the bond length dInSe increases. The InSe bonds are weakened due to the smaller electron cloud overlap. As a result, the valence band 1+ is lifted up (see Fig. 2f). Similar results are also found for InS and InTe monolayers. However, when the systems undergo compressive strains, the CBM changes from the point to the M point (see Fig. 2a and g), and the systems are still indirect semiconductors.

We now turn on the light and investigate the photoinduced current in InX monolayers. As shown in Fig. 5a, a two-probe model is employed, which consists of a central scattering region sandwiched by two semi-infinite InX sheets as electrodes. An external bias voltage Vds = 0.2 V is applied across the scattering region. The incident light power density is 0.1 W mm2 in our simulations. The predicated photocurrents of the InX monolayers as a function of photon energy are plotted in Fig. 5b. The results indicate that under illumination, the electronhole pairs are generated in InX monolayers via the interband transitions. These electrons and holes were swept to electrodes in different directions guided by the drainsource electric field, leading to the photoinduced current.

To investigate the influence of the strains on the photoresponse characteristics, we calculated the photocurrent of the InSe monolayer as a function of photon energy under different strains (). As shown in Fig. 6, when the compressive strain is applied, the photocurrent is enhanced due to the indirect-to-direct bandgap transition at the point. The corresponding Rph and EQE are 0.21 A W1 and 71.9%, respectively, which are about 15% higher than those of the unstrained InSe monolayer. On the other hand, when the system undergoes a tensile strain, it offers a broader spectral response in the visible-ultraviolet (UV) regions. The corresponding Rph and EQE are 0.19 A W1 and 63.5%, respectively. Thus, one can expect that appropriate strain is always favorable, which can improve the performance of InX monolayers in fields such as optoelectronics, FETs, and solar energy conversion.

4 Conclusions

In summary, we have systematically investigated the electronic structures and optoelectronic properties of InX (X = S, Se, and Te) monolayers. Our results show that upon the application of strain, the band gaps of InX monolayers can be modified and tuned over a relatively large range. A transition between indirect and direct band gaps is observed in the InSe monolayer under the compressive strain, which is favorable for the adsorption of the light. In addition, the predicted electron mobility exceeds 103 cm2 (V s)1, which is consistent with previous experimental results. Though the mobility of holes is relatively slow, it can be greatly improved by applying the compressive strain.

For the first time, the performance of the photoresponse of InX monolayers is evaluated based on the NEGF-DFT calculations. Under illumination, the InX based systems exhibit high photoresponsivity and EQE, with the values up to 0.18 A W1 and 62.5%, respectively. Such values can be further enhanced by the strains. Owing to such excellent electronic and optoelectronic merits, InX monolayers will become not only promising candidates for fabricating advanced appliances of photodetectors and low power consumption FETs, but also essential components for configuring 2D heterostructure devices.
