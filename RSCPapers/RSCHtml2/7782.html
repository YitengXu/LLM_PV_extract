Manipulating open-circuit voltage in an organic photovoltaic device via a phenylalkyl side chain†

Swee Ling Lam a, Xiaoqian Liu a, Fangli Zhao a, Chi-Lik Ken Lee *a and Wei Lek Kwan *b
aCentre of Biomedical and Life Sciences, Singapore Polytechnic, 500 Dover Road, Singapore 139651, Singapore. E-mail: kenlee@sp.edu.sg
bEngineering Product Development, Singapore University of Technology and Design, 20 Dover Drive, Singapore 138682, Singapore. E-mail: KwanWL@sutd.edu.sg

Received 17th January 2013 , Accepted 28th March 2013

First published on 2nd April 2013

Abstract

We report the use of a phenylalkyl side chain modified donor molecule to increase the open-circuit voltage (Voc) of an organic photovoltaic device. The bulky side chain is able to alter the donor/acceptor interface without interfering in the molecular packing. As a result, both Voc and device performance are improved.

Organic photovoltaic devices have gained wide interest due to their simplicity and low fabrication cost. Improvements in molecular design,1 device structure,2 and fabrication techniques3 have led to organic solar cells with power conversion efficiencies reaching 9%.4 A key factor in improving the efficiency of an organic solar cell lies in optimizing the open circuit voltage (Voc). The difference between the optical band gap and Voc remains high in organic solar cells as compared to other solar cell technologies. One reason is that the Voc of an organic solar cell is limited by the difference between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the donor and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the acceptor (herein ΔEDA), and has little correlation with the band gap of the absorber material.5 Therefore, a common strategy for increasing the Voc is to raise the LUMO level of the acceptor6 or to lower the HOMO level of the donor.7 However, in doing so, the optical bandgap of the material will be inevitably increased, therefore limiting its absorption of the solar spectrum. Besides ΔEDA, Voc can also be further limited by other factors including charge transfer (CT) states,8 reverse saturation current,9 shunt resistance,10 and charge recombination.11 It is also known that the donor/acceptor (D/A) interface can affect the Voc.12 By modifying the donor molecule with a bulky substituent, a larger D/A spatial separation can be formed,13 thereby increasing the Voc. However, these bulky side groups often alter the electronic configuration and molecular packing and may have other unexpected effect on the performance of the solar cell.12c,14 We report here the use of a phenylalkyl side chain to control the D/A interface while retaining the material properties of the molecule. We found that the phenylalkyl substituent induces steric effects which lead to higher Voc, without changing the molecular packing or the HOMO level of the donor.

A previously reported squaraine dye15 is modified and utilized as the donor molecule in this study. Squaraine is a promising donor material due to its strong and broad absorption in the near infrared region16 and thus is suitable for applications in transparent solar cells17 and tandem devices.18 The molecular structures of the squaraine dyes utilized in this experiment are shown in Fig. 1. Details of the synthetic routes are available in the ESI.†Table 1 summarizes the Voc of various squaraine:Phenyl-C71-Butyric-Acid-Methyl Ester (PC70BM) devices under AM1.5G irradiation at 100 mW cm−2. Table S2 in the ESI† shows the device performances at different D:A ratios. As shown in the two tables, the phenylalkyl derivative devices (SQ-P1 and SQ-P2) consistently exhibited higher Voc (0.63–0.64 V) as compared to the alkyl derivative devices (SQ1–3) (0.57–0.58 V). As all the devices have similar fill factors and short circuit currents, the efficiencies of the phenylalkyl derivative devices are much improved. The efficiencies of the SQ-P2 devices improve to 1.52–1.57% from 1.21–1.49% of the SQ1–3 devices. (SQ-P1 shows lower efficiency than the rest due to its lower solubility which results in thinner active layers in the devices.)

Single crystal X-ray structures were obtained for SQ2 and SQ-P2 to study the spatial arrangement of the side chains and molecular packing. The results are shown in Fig. S2 (ESI†). SQ2 shows very similar molecular conformation to those of the previously reported squaraine dyes with alkyl derivatives.15 On the other hand, the phenyl rings of SQ-P2 are twisted out of plane and make an angle of 105° with respect to the planar backbone. This could result in a greater separation between the phenylalkyl substituted squaraines and PCBM (Fig. 2). We reckon that the increase in separation between the donor and acceptor molecules results in the improvement in Voc of the phenylalkyl substituted devices. Transient photovoltage experiments were conducted to confirm this and will be elaborated below. The crystal structures of SQ2 and SQ-P2 show remarkable similarities despite the out-of-plane extension of the phenylalkyl side chain. In contrast to a previously reported alkenyl substituted squaraine, where the interplanar distance was changed,15 the phenylalkyl substituted squaraine (SQ-P2) has similar interplanar and interstack distances to those of the alkyl substituted squaraine (SQ2) (∼3.4 Å and ∼7.0 Å, respectively, Fig. S2, ESI†). Since the phenyl ring is attached to the end of an alkyl chain, the extended side chain is able to reside between the squaraine molecules without adversely affecting the packing arrangement. The torsion angles between the pyrrole and the nearly coplanar phenyl ring are both less than 10° for SQ2 and SQ-P2, unlike for the alkenyl substituted squaraine, in which the angle is 25°.15

As a result of the similarities in molecular packing, the material properties of the phenylalkyl substituted squaraines remain very similar to those of the alkyl substituted squaraines. All five squaraine dyes exhibited almost the same absorption spectra (Fig. S4, ESI†) and HOMO and LUMO levels (Table S1, ESI†). This shows that the optical bandgaps did not change. This is expected since the phenyl rings of SQ-P1 and SQ-P2 are connected to the main squaraine core via a non-conjugated alkyl chain and do not affect the conjugation of the core. λmaxs are ∼730 nm and ∼790 nm for both solution and thin films respectively. In the case of thin films, all spectra are red-shifted and broaden up to 900 nm. This further verifies that phenylalkyl substituted squaraines have very similar packing to those of the alkyl substituted ones. Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) and Grazing Incidence X-Ray Diffraction (GID) of the thin films also show similar characteristics (Fig. S7 and S8, ESI†). Hole mobilities (Table S1, ESI†) are also comparable among the five squaraine dyes and are in the order of 10−5 cm2 V−1 s−1. Therefore, the differences in Voc cannot be explained by the molecular packing19 or the variation in the ΔEDA values.5

As mentioned earlier, the introduction of a bulky side group can influence the D/A interface through steric repulsion.13a,20,21 In the case of the phenylalkyl substituted squaraines, the extended side chain of the phenylalkyl could force the PCBM acceptor to stay further away from the squaraine core. The effect of the phenylalkyl side chain is probed using the combined Shockley and charge recombination analysis described below.

Using transient photovoltage measurements, as described in ref. 11a and 23, of the devices at various light biases, we found that Jreco is indeed at least an order of magnitude lower for phenylalkyl substituted squaraines as compared to the alkyl substituted squaraines (Fig. S6 (ESI†) and Table 1). We note that the charge density, n, and the recombination rate constant, Krec, did not vary much under the 1 sun condition (Table S3, ESI†), resulting in a parallel shift in n of the phenylalkyl substituted devices when plotted against Voc (Fig. S6a, ESI†). This suggests that the improvement in Voc is unlikely due to the reduction in charge recombination.

All five squaraines exhibited similar ΔEDA, therefore the difference in Jreco is attributed to the variance in JBGreco. The calculated JBGreco values (Table 1) for the phenylalkyl substituents (103 mA cm−2) are of one order of magnitude lower than those for the alkyl substituents (104 mA cm−2). This shows that the phenylalkyl group effectively modifies the D/A interface. In addition, the calculated Voc (Table 1) from eqn (1) is comparable to the measured Voc, confirming the validity of our analysis.

In conclusion, we had demonstrated that the phenylalkyl side chain could be utilized to increase Voc by manipulating the D/A interface. The steric effect introduced by the bulky phenyl ring increases the separation between donor and acceptor molecules. This is achieved without changing the optical bandgap and the molecular packing, thereby retaining desirable properties of the molecule. Our approach demonstrates the potential of phenylalkyl derivatives as functional side chains to control the D/A interface and Voc.
