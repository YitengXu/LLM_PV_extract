Abstract

In recent years, self-assembly has emerged as a powerful tool for the construction of functional nanostructures. Myriad applications of these nanoscale architectures, especially the supramolecular gels derived from low molecular mass compounds, in fields such as optoelectronics, light harvesting, organic–inorganic hybrid materials, tissue engineering and regenerative medicine are being envisaged. This review attempts to present a succinct overview of the current state of research on functional nano-scale systems—the design, synthesis and applications of self-assembled nanomaterials “engineered” to carry out precise functions, with an emphasis on supramolecular gel phase materials.

Introduction

Hierarchical growth of self-assembled structures is ubiquitous in nature. Whether it is the “double strand” of DNA or the triple helix of the structural protein collagen, the globular to fibrillar transition of the muscle protein actin or the intriguing structural features embedded in the tobacco mosaic virus, molecular recognition through self-complementary functions leading to complex superstructures has profound implications in life. The ease and efficiency with which nature constructs complex self-assembled architectures to carry out various biological processes has continuously provided inspiration towards the design and realization of engineered “materials” based on simple molecular building blocks.

In the present review, due to space limitations, we do not intend to discuss all aspects of self-assembled functional materials. Instead, we shall concentrate on the importance and scope of molecular gels for applications. In this context, we shall specifically deal with self-assembled gels based on low molecular mass compounds. The “solid phase” in these gels consists of a three dimensional fibrous network which is generated from the self-assembly of the gelator molecules through non-covalent interactions. The bulk phase, i.e., the solvent, is immobilized within this network. The advantage of these gels over their polymeric counterparts stems from the reversibility of these interactions, so that the gels can be broken, generally, by the application of “heat”, and are re-formed upon cooling the sol.

The newly “rediscovered” area of supramolecular gels has witnessed an enormous surge of activities in the past decade, owing to a growing interest in constructing nanomaterials that have potential applications in fields as diverse as optoelectronics, biomedicines, tissue engineering and light harvesting. Be it in wound healing, antimicrobial functions, as a matrix for cell proliferation, as a medium to carry out organic reactions with high specificity and selectivity, or as a sink to tap and funnel light energy, molecular self-assembly is being extensively utilized in “bottom up” nanofabrication to obtain smart materials that can perform the desired functions. A number of comprehensive reviews and a book on molecular gels have appeared in the literature, which have outlined the evolution of this field in terms of the fundamental understanding of the gelation processes, the design of new gelators,1 and towards their possible applications as functional materials.2 This article highlights some of the recent advances in the field of molecular gels in terms of the myriad functions that they can be made to execute.

Design and synthesis of self-assembling molecules leading to functional systems

To generate a nanostructure with a desired function, it is of paramount importance that the constituent molecular blocks be designed so as to contain all the required structural features. The subsequent self-assembly should then lead to the expression of the required functions. In other words, we need to have a programmed self-assembly process to achieve the material properties. The concept is schematically represented in Fig. 1. Several examples will be discussed in this review which will illustrate this principle.
Fig. 1 A schematic representation of programmed self-assembly. The building block leads to pre-aggregates, and eventually to self-assembled fibrous network, exposing the functional motif on the surface of the nanofibers.

One such example of self-assembling molecules is a peptide amphiphile (PA). There are several features that can be built in such a PA. For example, specific peptide sequences can be introduced to carry out specific biological functions after the self-assembly has occurred. The shape and the hydrophobic–hydrophilic balance in the molecule is another parameter that should influence the shape of the supramolecular assembly and consequently their functions. How these key parameters can be manipulated will be showcased in the following sections with prototype examples of the construction of bone-like composites and spinal cord repair materials through self-assembly of peptide nanofibers.

Another example demonstrating the principle of pre-programmed synthesis leading to various functions is the utilization of chiral diaminocyclohexane-based organogelators to produce chiral silica by the transcription of the gel fibers. This chiral silica was further exploited as a chiral template in asymmetric synthesis. These two examples highlight the point that the proper programming of the structure of the molecular building blocks is a vital aspect for the conception and realization of a wide variety of self-assembled materials with useful functions. From the next section onwards this principle will be further elaborated through a thorough discussion of various kinds of functional supramolecular gels.

1. Nanofibers of supramolecular gels as templates for the synthesis of inorganic nanostructures

The self-assembly of organic building blocks can generate a variety of supramolecular structures having diversity in size, shape, chemical composition and function as found in many natural and artificial systems. The transcription of these structures can lead to the development of novel inorganic materials. In the past different kinds of amphiphilic molecules have been utilized to create inorganic materials of varying morphologies.3 During the past decade, the use of SAFIN's of supramolecular gels as templates for the transcription of nanofibrous materials has also become one of the productive research fields. In this section, a few representative examples of gelators which have been used for this purpose are presented.

An early report of using an organogel as a template to make hollow silica was by Ono et al.4 Compound 1a was found to form gels in a number of organic solvents including tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS). But when the polycondensation of TEOS was attempted in the presence of 1a, it did not result in the transcription of the organogel structure to the silica. So another cholesterol based gelator 1b (Chart 1), which resembled the structure of the conventional cationic surfactants generally used for such polycondensation reaction, was employed. The sol–gel polymerization of TEOS was carried out in an acetic acid gel of 1b. After removal of the solvent, the material was calcined to remove the organic template and SEM of the resulting material clearly showed the presence of fibrous silica with the tube edges containing cavities. TEM also revealed that the fibers had a tubular structure (Fig. 2) with inner diameter 10–200 nm. The electrostatic interacation between the oligomeric anionic silica and the cationic gel fibers was believed to be the reason for the successful transcription with 1b.

The previous example illustrated that the presence of cationic charge on the gel fibers is a prerequisite for transcription but at the same time, the modification of the gelator structure by incorporating cationic groups led to reduced gelation ability in several instances. To circumvent this problem, Shinkai's group later designed another cholesterol based gelator (2, Chart 2) having a crown ether moiety capable of binding to metal ions.5 Compound 2 could be used for transcription in the presence of only K+ ions, but not with other cations (Li+, Na+, Rb+, Cs+). Following this report, many other examples appeared in the literature utilizing cholesterol/crown ether based organogelators for transcription.6

Jung et al. have demonstrated the formation of right- and left-handed chiral silica structures by transcription of organogel fibers of chiral diaminocyclohexane-based gelators.8 For the transcription process, mixtures of 4 and 6 (or 5 and 7, Chart 4) were used to maintain an adequate amount of cationic charge on the gel fibers. After sol–gel polymerization of TEOS, it was found that the mixture of 4/6 produced silica with left-handed helical structures whereas right-handed helical structures were obtained by using a mixture of 5/7 (Fig. 3).

Shinkai's group reported the creation of lotus shaped hollow silica fibers using the organogels of sugar derivatives 8 and 10 (Chart 5).9 These two derivatives did not possess any cationic charge, but the amino group present in both of these derivatives played a crucial role by binding to TEOS and other intermediate species through hydrogen bonding during the polycondensation. The importance of the amino group was further supported by the fact that the sugar derivative 9, containing a nitro group gave rise to granular silica under the same polycondensation conditions.

A novel TiO2 material with macaroni like hollow-fiber structure was synthesized by Kobayashi et al. using the organogel fibers of compound 11 (Chart 6) as template.10 The sol–gel polymerization of titanium isopropoxide was carried out in the presence of 11 using either a base or an acid as the catalyst. The SEM of the dried samples showed the presence of fibrous aggregates of similar diameters (150–600 nm) in both cases (Fig. 4). However when the dried samples were calcined to remove the gelator, it was found that the fibrous structure was preserved in the sample made under basic conditions whereas it was lost in the sample prepared with an acid catalyst.

Cationic gemini surfactants (12 and 13, Chart 7) having chiral tartarate counter ions were reported to form gels in chlorinated and aromatic organic solvents.11 The gelators 12 and 13 were used to make helical silica fibrils by transcription in 1:1 pyridine–water. Interestingly, the helical shape and the handedness of the transcribed silica fibers could be controlled by varying the ee. For example, with a gel of pure 12 (100% ee), right handed helical fibrils were obtained (SEM) whereas with lower ee (50% or below), the formation of double stranded helical structures was observed.


Chart 7

Zhan et. al have reported the generation of both right- and left- handed silver nanohelices using the organogel fibers of a racemic gelator 2-acrylamide-dodecane-1-sulfonic acid (ADSA).12 The reduction of Ag(I) cations adsorbed on the gel of ADSA in chloroform was achieved with sodium borohydride and TEM images of the reultant material showed that opposite handed helices with widths of 100–200 nm were present. More interestingly, a single nanohelix with both handedness was also observed.

Sanchez and co-workers reported the preparation of double porous silica with pore sizes of two different length scales (nm and μm) using an organogelator 2,3-di-n-decyloxyanthracene (14, Chart 8).13 The transcription was most successful when tetramethyl orthosilicate (TMOS) was first hydrolyzed by an acid catalyst in MeOH–water, followed by the addition of DDOA to gelify the system and finally the addition of NH4OH to increase the pH to 12.6. The same molecule was also utilized later to make alumina-based porous materials having both meso and macroscale porosity.14


Chart 8

A templated synthesis of CuS nanofibers using the organogel of a dicholesterol derivative (15, Chart 9) possessing two binding sites for Cu2+ was reported by Lu's group.15 The interesting aspect of this templated synthesis was that depending on the sulfur source, CuS nanofibers of different morphologies could be obtained. Straight and bent helical fibers (with helical pitch of 100–200 nm) were obtained from butyl acetate and benzene–butanol gels, respectively by using H2S as the sulfur source whereas the use of thioacetamide gave rise to bent helical CuS nanofibers (with helical pitch of 50 nm) from gels in both the solvents (Fig. 5).

One limitation of using organogels as templates is that the necessary precursors that can be employed are restricted to metal alkoxides and this actually limits the number of metal oxides that can be synthesized by this template method. Metal salts can be used as precursors if hydrogels are employed as templates. Gundiah et al. have utilized this advantage of using the hydrogel fibers of a tripodal cholamide 16 (Chart 10) as templates to synthesize nanotubes of various metal oxides like SiO2, TiO2, ZrO2, ZnO, WO3 and also metal sulfates like ZnSO4 and BaSO4.16 Compound 16 forms gel in 20% (v/v) AcOH–water at low concentrations.17 So, the metal salt precursors were either added directly to the hot sol of gelator 16 in AcOH–water or pre-made solutions/gels of the precursors in some suitable solvents were added to the sol of 16 in AcOH–water. The TEM of the native gel showed that the fibers had diameters 8–10 nm and after calcination the nanotubes were also found to have diameters in this range (Fig. 6). This was also the first instance of synthesis of zirconia nanotubes using gel fibers as templates.

A similar strategy with gelator 16 was again utilized for the preparation of nanotubes of CdS, ZnS and CuS from the corresponding metal acetates and Na2S.18 The nanotubes were polycrystalline in nature with inner diameters of ∼5 nm. Depending on the concentration of Zn(OAc)2, both ZnS nanotubes and nanorods were obtained (Fig. 7) whereas the formation of nanowires was also observed in case of CuS.

Stupp and co-workers demonstrated the self-assembly and mineralization of a peptide amphiphile (PA) to create a nanostructured composite material that resembles the structural orientation of collagen and hydroxyapatite (HA) observed in bone.19 Five key structural features (Fig. 8) were introduced in the peptide amphiphile such as a long hydrophobic alkyl tail (region 1), four cysteine residues (region 2) which can form disulfide bonds if oxidized, three glycine residues (region 3) as flexible linkers between the hydrophilic head group and more rigid cross-linked region, a phosphorylated serine residue (region 4) for interacting with calcium ions to direct the mineralization of hydroxyapatite and the cell adhesion ligand RGD (region 5). This peptide formed gels below pH 4 (>2.5 mg mL−1). The mineralization property of the PA nanofibers was investigated by directly coating the TEM grid with the PA solution, allowing it to self-assemble, oxidizing the cysteines and diffusing CaCl2 and Na2HPO4 into the film from opposite sides. Preferential alignment of crystallographic c axis of HA with PA long fiber axis was observed. So, the PA fibers were able to nucleate hydroxyapatite on their surfaces. This could be of interest in the design of materials for mineralized tissue repair.

Stupp's group later reported the mineralization of CdS using the helical nanoribbons of a “dendron rodcoils” molecule.20 The TEM image of gels of this molecule in solvents like ethyl methacrylate (EMA) and 2-ethylhexyl methacrylate (EHMA) revealed the presence of twisted ribbons. The mineralization was done by adding a solution of cadmium nitrate in THF to the gels of this molecule in EMA/EHMA and exposing to hydrogen sulfide gas. The hydrophilic hydroxy region of this dendron acted initially as the binding site of the Cd2+ ions and also as a nucleation centre for the growth of CdS crystals after exposure to H2S gas.

Nakano's group has described the templated synthesis of fluorocarbon functionalized silica nanotubes by carrying out the sol–gel condensation of a mixture of TEOS and fluorocarbon functionalized triethoxysilanes (18a–d, Chart 11) in the presence of a semi-fluorinated organogelator (17).21 The presence of functionalized triethoxysilane was essential as the polycondensation of only TEOS resulted in the formation of granular silica. The fluoroalkyl chain length of the functionalized trialkoxysilane also played a role in transcription. Tubular silica structures were obtained with F6-TEOS, F8-TEOS and F10-TEOS whereas granular silica was obtained with F4-TEOS.


Chart 11

In this section, through several examples, it has been demonstrated that the synthesis of nanofibrous inorganic materials employing the fibrillar networks of supramolecular gels as templates is an efficient way to make different classes of materials (mainly oxide based but also of metals and chalcogenides) having a wide variety of morphologies (fibers, tubes, ribbons, helices, rods, etc.). However, to achieve a better control on the size and the morphologies of the nanomaterials, a deeper knowledge of the various complex interactions governing the transcription processes needs to be developed. We also hope that the transcribed inorganic structures will find use in nanotechnology in the coming years.

2. Gel–nanoparticle hybrid materials

The design of inorganic–organic hybrid materials using the matrix of supramolecular gels is another area closely related to templated synthesis. The tremendous interest in this type of materials in recent times stems from the fact that they are promising materials for potential applications in optics, electronics, ionics, mechanics, biology, fuel and solar cells, catalyst, sensors, etc.22 The basic criterion to develop materials for such applications is to organize the nanoparticles in two and three dimensional architectures. The supramolecular gel matrix has been used to synthesize and immobilize nanoparticles by modulating the gel structure with appropriated functionality. This section will highlight some examples of this kind of functionalized gels.

Simmons et al. have described the incorporation of nanoparticles into an organogel formed by the addition of 4-chlorophenol to a solution containing the reverse micellar aggregates of the well-known surfactant bis(2-ethylhexyl)sulfosuccinate (AOT, Chart 12).23 Superparamagnetic ferrite and semiconducting CdS nanoparticles were synthesized in AOT water-in-oil microemulsions, dried to remove the water and isooctane was added to reconstitute reverse micelles. Upon the addition of 1 equivalent of 4-chlorophenol (Chart 12) the system transformed into a gel, whose AFM images revealed the incorporation of nanoparticles into the fibers of the gel network.


Chart 12

Kimura et al. demonstrated the self-organization of gold nanoparticles into a network structure using organogelator 19a (Chart 13) containing two functional groups, a trans-1,2-bis (alkylamide)cyclohexane unit and two thiol groups.24 Octanethiol stabilized gold nanoparticles (having average diameter of 1.7 nm) were prepared separately and these octanethiol residues were then replaced by the thiol groups of the gelator by site-exchange reactions. The resulting gel–NPs hybrid was stable for more than a month. An interlocked 3D structure of fibrous aggregates consisting of individual Au NPs with diameters of 10–30 nm was observed from the TEM (Fig. 9). The fiber diameters are comparable to that of the organic fibers made from only 19a. The importance of the thiol groups in the stabilization of the nanoparticles was demonstrated by the fact that no fibrous aggregates were observed from the TEM image from a gel of structurally similar 19b lacking the thiol groups.

A dendritic organogelator 20 (Chart 14), containing a disulfide bridge, was utilized by Love et al. for the synthesis and stabilization of gold nanoparticles.25 For the preparation of the hybrid material, a solution of HAuCl4/n-(C8H17)4NBr in toluene was layered on a toluene gel of 20. After a few days the gel assumed a uniform yellow color and the excess of toluene was removed from the top of the gel. When this gel was irradiated with a 100 W mercury vapor lamp, the color of the gel initially changed from orange–yellow to colorless and then after a few hours from colorless to purple. This is consistent with the initial reduction of Au(III) to Au(I) and then to Au(0). The process was monitored with absorption spectroscopy and the final gel sample appeared to have a purple band running through the gel. TEM images revealed that the synthesized Au NPs had an average diameter of 13 nm. It was observed that when the gel was melted to form the sol, the purple nanoparticles separated from the medium to form a black precipitate. This indicated that the gel medium played a crucial role to prevent the aggregation of GNPs.


Chart 14

Banerjee and co-workers reported in situ formation and stabilization of gold and silver nanoparticles (GNPs and SNPs) within supramolecular gel networks of tripeptide based organogelators 21a, 21b and 21c (Chart 15).26 These tripeptides contained tyrosine residues which were utilized to reduce Ag(I) and Au(III) salts to generate SNPs and GNPs in the gel phase. Gelator 21a or 21b was added to HAuCl4 solution in toluene and heated above 100 °C. After cooling, a gel was obtained which retained the yellow color for several days. When triethylamine was added to this gel and heated to make a clear solution, the yellow color initially disappeared. But within a few minutes the solution turned violet and after 1 h it transformed into a gel. This indicates the formation of GNPs via the oxidation of the tyrosine residues. The authors also demonstrated the in situ formation of GNPs in a gel of peptide 21c in 1:1 MeOH–water.


Chart 15

A similar strategy has been employed recently by Mitra et al. to synthesize gold nanoparticles of different shapes in hydrogels of tryptophan containing peptide amphiphiles.27

The influence of capping agents to tune the morphological features and the viscoelastic properties of gel–Au nanoparticles composites has been demonstrated by Bhattacharya and co-workers.28 A fatty acid amide of L-alanine 22 (Chart 16) which formed stable gels in both aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons was used as the gel component to make the composites. Different sets of capping agents were used to investigate the gel–NP interaction. The Au NPs were based on n-alkanethiols (AuCm+2, m = 4, 6 and 10), a cholesterol based thiol (AuChol) and p-thiocresol (AuPhMe) and they had average diameter of 3–6 nm depending on the nature of the capping agent used. The incorporation of the NPs in the gel had a profound impact on the morphological features of the resulting composite as observed by SEM (Fig. 10). The native gel had fibrous assemblies whereas the incorporation of AuC12 resulted in the coalescing of the fibers. On the other hand “rolled-tubular” type and platelet-like aggregates were obtained in the case of AuChol and AuPhMe. The rheological characterization of composites revealed interesting viscoelastic properties (Fig. 11). The incorporation of alkanethiol and cholesterol thiol capped NPs improved the rigidity of the gel as revealed from the higher yield stress values of the gel composites. However incorporation of thiocresol based NPs in the gel network slightly decreased the rigidity of the resulting composite. So, the n-alkyl chain and the cholesteryl groups interdigitated better with the gel fibers compared to the thiocresol group and this led to mechanically more-rigid composites in those cases.

Gelator 22 has recently been exploited to make gel–CNT composites by incorporating SWNTs, MWNTs and SWNTs functionalized with amides of different chain lengths (in order to achieve more solubility in organic solvents) such as hexadecyl, dodecyl, octyl and benzyl in the gel phase.29 Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) study of the nanocomposites showed that the incorporation of pristine SWNTs did not change the thermal stability of the nanocomposites but functionalized SWNTs especially those with longer chains caused depression in the thermal stability of the composites compared to that of the native gel indicating a better chain interdigitation of the pendant functions of the nanotubes with the organogel fibers. That the functionalized SWNTs were more efficiently dispersed in the gel matrix than the pristine SWNTs was also evidenced by SEM of the nanocomposites (thickening of fibers in the former case and formation of clusters for the latter) and from the rheological experiments performed on the nanocomposites (higher rigidity in the former case compared to that of the latter). The nanocomposites were NIR responsive due to the characteristic van Hove transitions of the SWNTs in the NIR region and hence their gel–sol transition could be induced by NIR irradiation. The gel–nanocomposites in toluene upon NIR laser irradiation (1064 nm) at 20 °C underwent a gel-to-sol transition whereas the native gel of 22 did not show this melting behavior even after irradiating for a longer time. The functionalized SWNTs, being more homogeneously dispersed in the gel, showed faster gel-to-sol transition than the pristine SWNTs for the gel–CNT composites. This kind of NIR- responsive supramolecular gels may have important ramifications in the design of “smart” drug delivery systems, since, unlike UV, NIR radiation has no harmful effect on biological systems.

Another recent report of hybrid CNT–gel came from Srinivasan et al.30 The hybrid materials were made by dispersing CNTs (both SWNTs and MWNTs) in oligo(p-phenylenevinylene) (OPV) gels in nonpolar solvents. Interestingly, the gelation of the OPV derivatives (23a, 23b and 23c, Chart 17) in toluene was induced below their normal critical gelation concentration when the CNTs were dispersed. The hybrid gels were also found to be thermally more stable than the native gels. A similar trend was observed for their mechanical properties. From the rheological studies, it was found that the ratio of the storage modulus (G′) to the loss modulus (G″) was higher for the hybrid gels compared to the native gels, indicating the formation of a stronger solidlike network in the hybrid gels. These observations can be attributed to the strong π–π stacking interactions between the OPV moieties of the gelators and the carbon nanotubes.


Chart 17

In the context of these examples on CNT–gel hybrid materials, we must mention that SWNT dispersed gels of ionic liquids have emerged as interesting soft functional materials in recent years, largely due to the work of Aida and co-workers. This area will be covered in some detail when we discuss the gels of ionic liquids in general towards the end of this review (section 7.2.).

Maitra's group has utilized the already discussed tripodal cholamide 16 to make gel–nanoparticle hybrid material employing the structurally similar bile acid derived thiols (24a, 24b and 24c, Chart 18) as capping agents.31 The gold NPs were prepared by the reduction of HAuCl4 in MeOH by NaBH4 in the presence of steroidal thiols. The average sizes of 24a, 24b and 24c capped nanoparticles were found to be 3.5, 2.5 and 1.5 nm, respectively (TEM images of 24a-Au NP are shown in Fig. 12). There was a noticeable increase in stability of GNP dispersion in the gel compared to that in only 20% AcOH–water. For example, the GNP dispersions (>0.05 mg mL−1) in 20% AcOH–H2O were stable only for 5 h and then separated out from the medium whereas the composite was found to be stable for several months even with higher amount of doping (>2 mg mL−1) of GNPs, which underscores the importance of the gel network in obtaining a stable dispersion of nanoparticles. The thermal stability of the hybrid material was similar to that of the native gel although the melting of the gel resulted in the precipitation of the NPs from the medium.

Vemula et al. have demonstrated the in situ preparation and stabilization of gold nanoparticles in gels formed by monourea derivatives 25a–c (Chart 19).32 These derivatives formed gels in water and also in various organic solvents. The amino group of the urea moiety in the gelators was utilized to perform the twin role of the reducing as well as the capping agent. The GNP-embedded gels were pink in color and showed the appearance of surface plasmon band at 553 nm. The hybrid materials showed similar morphological features in SEM and TEM as observed for the native gel and were stable for several months.

The same group later described the in situ synthesis and stabilization of gold nanoparticles using gels of self-assembling systems based on vitamin C (ascorbic acid, Asc-A, known to reduce Au(III) to Au(0) to form gold nanoparticles) amphiphiles without using any external reducing/capping agents.33 They utilized the gels of three amphiphiles (Asc8, Asc12 and Asc18, Chart 20), which were synthesized using enzymatic catalysis.

Schenning's group has reported a composite material where gold nanoparticles were organized on π-conjugated oligo(p-phenylenevinylene) (OPV) tapes.34 Hybrid 26a/26b (Chart 21) was synthesized by mixing 26a and 26b–Au in toluene, heating the system above the gelation temperature of 26a and then cooling. From the TEM images of the hybrid materials, pairs of parallel rows of gold nanoparticles were visible on the tapes. The electronic communication between the 26a tapes and the bound 26b–Au particles was demonstrated by the observation that in the hybrid material the fluorescence of 26a was considerably quenched with a concomitant decrease in its lifetime.


Chart 21

The mineralization of silver nanoparticles in organogels of L-valine based oligopeptides (27a and 27b, Chart 22) was recently reported by Taubert and co-workers.35 Both oligopeptides (individually/mixtures of variable compositions) formed gels in n-butanol. The mineralization of silver nanoparticles was done by using DMF as the reducing agent. The shape and the size of the SNPs could be tuned by varying the amount of sulfur containing peptide 27b in the gel. 3. Functional hydrogels as biomaterials

3.1. Enzyme triggered hydrogelation of small molecules

The use of enzymes to initiate the self-assembly of small molecules leading to hydrogelation has important implications in developing novel biomaterials. The integration of self-assembly in water with intracellular enzymatic reactions opens up the possibility for the use of these hydrogels, for example, in drug delivery systems and screening of enzyme inhibitors. Application as biomaterials for wound healing can also be envisaged, since the supramolecular hydrogels, especially those crafted from amino acid derivatives, can be made biodegradable.

The general principle of enzymatic hydrogelation requires an enzyme to convert a precursor molecule to a hydrogelator (by catalysing a bond forming or a bond cleavage reaction). One of the first reports of enzyme catalysed supramolecular hydrogelation came from Xu's group, using commercially available Fmoc-tyrosine phosphate (28) as the precursor.36 When alkaline phosphatase was added to a weakly alkaline solution of 28, the phosphate group was cleaved converting 28 to more hydrophobic 29 (Scheme 1), which gelled water. This design integrated enzymatic dephosphorylation, an important biological reaction observed in many organisms, with hydrogelation.


Scheme 1 Structure of the precursor (28) and the gelator (29) for phosphatase catalyzed hydrogelation

This strategy was further utilized as a visual assay to screen inhibitors for the phosphatase enzyme.36 The inhibitor competes with the precursor 28 for binding to the active site of the enzyme, and thus prevents the hydrolysis of the phosphate, thereby preventing hydrogelation. From the visual detection of the sol–gel transition, the minimum inhibition concentration can be determined. In spite of being a semi-quantitative assay allowing only an approximate determination of the IC50 values, this is a useful protocol for a rapid and inexpensive initial screening of enzyme inhibitors. Since the phosphate ions cleaved from the precursor molecule 28 remain in the matrix of the hydrogel, this strategy (phosphatase catalysed hydrogelation) was further used by Schnepp et al. to utilize the hydrogel as a template for biomineralization (shown as a schematic in Fig. 13).37 When the phosphate containing hydrogel (from 29, which was derived from the precursor 28) was soaked in CaCl2 solution, the Ca2+ ions diffused into the gel and formed calcium phosphate, leading to a gel with well-defined mineralized fibers (Fig. 14). The association of calcium and phosphate with organic fibers was confirmed by energy-dispersive X-ray analysis. The mineralized hydrogel showed increased thermal stability, stiffness and yield stress, indicating that this protocol could be used to produce hybrid materials with improved thermal and mechanical properties.

An enzymatic hydrogelation catalysed by β-lactamase has been reported by Yang et al.38 β-Lactamase catalyses the opening up of the β-lactam ring in precursor 30, releasing hydrogelator 31, which self-assembles to form nanofibers leading to the gelation of water (Scheme 2). Importantly, 30 also responded to β-lactamases (CTX-M13, CTX-M14, SHV-1 and TEM-1) present in the lysates of E. coli, which were able to convert 30 to 31, leading to hydrogelation. Nitrocefin, a cephalosporin, is widely used in assaying the activity of β-lactamases. However, due to its low reporting threshold, systems having significant background activity may show a false positive on nitrocefin assay. In contrast, the gelation based assay reported here is less sensitive, and hence can be a reliable reporter for systems having significant background activity, which show a false positive on nitrocefin assay.

The examples discussed so far used enzyme catalysed bond cleavage to reveal a hydrogelator. An alternative approach is to reverse the reaction, i.e., to form a hydrogelator through formation of a covalent bond. Toledano et al. used thermolysin to covalently link compounds 32 and 33 to form 34 (Scheme 3), which subsequently formed self-assembled nanofibers leading to hydrogelation.39

Wang et al. have recently shown that an acid phosphatase (AP) can be self-immobilized in a supramolecular hydrogel derived from enzymatic hydrogelation, leading to a considerable enhancement of its catalytic activity.40 Precursors (28 and 35) were converted to their corresponding hydrogelators (29 and 36) using AP (Scheme 4). During the formation of the hydrogel network, the enzyme was entrapped into the hydrogel. TEM and AFM images indicated that the enzyme molecules were located on the surface or inside the fibers, suggesting a strong interaction between the enzyme and the nanofibers. CD analysis of the entrapped enzyme showed that the secondary structure was preserved in the gel network. This hydrogel immobilized enzyme displayed markedly improved catalytic activity (for the hydrolysis of A to B) in organic solvents compared to the free enzyme in water (Fig. 15). For example, the activity increased 100 fold in CHCl3. The amphiphilic gel nanofibers facilitated rapid mass transfer of the reactant and the product between the hydrogel and the organic environment. So, this work is significant in the context of carrying out efficient biotransformation in organic solvents. One more example of the enhanced catalytic activity of an enzyme entrapped in hydrogel will be taken up when we discuss gels as reaction media.

Yang et al. extended the concept of enzymatic hydrogelation to encompass intracellular construction of artificial nanostructures.41 A soluble precursor esterase substrate 37 was employed, which diffused into the mammalian cells and got converted to the hydrogelator 38 by the endogenous esterase enzymes (Scheme 5). Compound 38 self-assembled inside the cells to form a hydrogel, leading to cell death. Interestingly, although NIH3T3 cells did not display any significant decrease in cell proliferation with increasing concentration of 37 even after 3 days, the MTT assay of HeLa cells showed severe decrease in cell growth under the same experimental condition. This observation was related to a higher level of expression of esterases in HeLa cells compared to NIH3T3 cells, leading to a quick build up of 38 in HeLa cells. This indicates that the formation kinetics of these intracellular nanofibers is greatly influenced by even a small difference in the expression of enzymes in different cell lines. So, this approach appears to have great potential in countering antimicrobial drug resistance and to cause cell death in a controlled way with good specificity.

The examples discussed above illustrate that coupling the supramolecular hydrogelation process with various biological enzymatic processes has opened up new opportunities to regulate cell functions. Advancement in this field is likely to have major implications in the design of reliable assays for enzyme inhibitors, designing strategies to overcome antimicrobial drug resistance, in the context of biomineralization, or to carry out enzymatic reactions in organic media with high efficiency. The fabrication of self-assembled fibers inside cells to probe various cellular processes is complicated by the fact that the stimulus required (e.g., heat, pH change etc.) can affect the very processes that one wants to monitor. Enzymatic hydrogelation offers an easy solution to this problem.

3.2. Hydrogels in biomedicines

3.2.1. Hydrogels in drug delivery. Amphiphilic oligopeptide and sugar-based supramolecular hydrogels have considerable potential as smart drug delivery systems. They are biocompatible and biodegradable. Moreover, due to the reversible nature of the interactions holding the gel structure together, they can be designed to respond to various stimuli, like a change in pH, ionic strength, or the action of an enzyme to break the hydrogel structure and release the drug at specific targets.

van Bommel et al. have described a model two-stage drug delivery system based on a hydrogelator–drug conjugate.42 They used a cyclohexane trisamide gelling scaffold, to which a drug mimic was attached through a α-chymotrypsin labile linker to design hydrogelator 39 (Chart 23). In the hydrogel of 39, the enzyme susceptible linker is protected from the enzyme. However, when the gel is dissociated by a stimulus (pH or temperature), the enzyme can access and cleave the linker, resulting in the release of the “model drug”.

Bhuniya et al. designed (S)-(+)-ibuprofen based hydrogelator 40 (Chart 24) containing a dipeptide linkage.43 When carboxypeptidase Y was added to a colloidal buffered solution of 40, it was able to hydrolyse the gelator molecule, releasing the anti-inflammatory drug (S)-(+)-ibuprofen. The enzyme was also found capable of breaking the hydrogel of 40, demonstrating a general principle of drug release by triggering the collapse of a hydrogel through enzyme response.

Seo et al. have explored a biotin based hydrogel to entrap an ethynylpyrene labeled molecular beacon (S1), and have achieved its controlled release in response to a specific stimulus, e.g., pH change of the environment (Fig. 16).44 A molecular beacon is a fluorescently tagged oligonucleotide which can detect specific sequence in nucleic acids through a change in fluorescence. The oligonucleotide probe S1 was prepared by incorporating two pyrene-labelled phosphoramidite building blocks into the stem region of a hairpin oligonucleotide. The hairpin state, after the introduction of the fluorescent tag, showed a green fluorescence due to the stacking of the pyrene units. However, when this π-stacking interaction was disrupted by trapping the probe in a hydrogel of 41, the emission was blue shifted resulting in blue fluorescence. When the dissociation of the gel was triggered by a basic pH or through the enzymatic hydrolysis of the peptidic linkage, S1 was released into solution, leading to the restoration of the hairpin state with concomitant change of fluorescence from blue to green. Upon addition of the target complementary oligonucleotide (S2) to this solution, the hairpin state of S1 changed into a duplex due to binding with S2, and the emission again changed from green to blue due to the de-stacking of the pyrene units. This work introduces biotin based hydrogels as potential materials to carry oligonucleotide probes and related drugs to biological targets, whose release may be accomplished by physiological pH or enzyme trigger and visually monitored by a color change.

Vemula et al. have used the hydrophobic pockets inside the hydrogels derived from amygdaline based amphiphiles 42–44 to encapsulate curcumin (Scheme 6).45 There was no release of curcumin from the gel into water, but its release was effected by breaking the gel using a hydrolase enzyme at physiological temperature. The rate of curcumin release could be controlled by changing the enzyme concentration or temperature. It should be noted that these hydrogelators were themselves synthesized through enzyme catalysis from amygdaline, a naturally occurring glycoside found in many food plants.

Zhou et al. have described pH responsive reversible volume shrinkage and swelling of a hydrogel based on N-acetylgalactosamine appended glutamate ester 45 by mixing with amphiphilic carboxylic acids 46a or 46b.46 An equimolar mixture of 45 and 46a/b produced a swelled hydrogel at neutral pH. When the pH was lowered by exposing the gel to HCl vapor, the gel excluded water and shrank to almost half of its original volume, and the originally transparent hydrogel turned opaque (Fig. 17a and 17b). The carboxylate ion in 46 probably causes inter-fiber repulsion, leading to swelling of the gel at neutral pH, whereas, the decreased osmotic pressure due to the release of the counterion and absence of the electrostatic repulsion at acidic pH results in gel shrinkage. Water soluble vitamins (for example, vitamins B1, B6 and B12) entrapped in the mixed hydrogel of 45 and 46b (1:1) could be released from the gel by lowering the pH of the medium. The process was quantitatively monitored as a function of time by absorption spectroscopy (Fig. 17c). However, when the same release experiments were performed with hydrophobic flavone derivatives like myricetin and quercetin, the efficiency of release was lower. This is because hydrophobic guests residing predominantly in the hydrophobic domains of the gel are not easily released into expelled water. This supramolecular copolymerization protocol has the potential to control drug release by two possible means: (a) by a pH trigger, and (b) by tailoring the amphiphilicity of the gel.

Panda et al. have recently reported50 on a self-assembled hydrogel based on a dipeptide 51 (Chart 26) that contains an α,β-dehydrophenylalanine residue. Due to their biocompatibility and the tunability of the drug release from the gel matrix, these hydrogels are potential candidates as functional biomaterials.

3.2.2. Hydrogels in regenerative medicine and tissue engineering. Self-assembled nanostructures can mimic an excellular matrix and support cell proliferation and differentiation, crucial for healing damaged tissues. Supramolecular hydrogels consisting of nanofibers from biocompatible molecules are attractive for such applications as they are biodegradable and non-cyctotoxic, the two vital requirements for applications in vivo. Hence, they are likely to play an important role in the field of regenerative medicines. In this section, a few representative examples are discussed to highlight the enormous progress this field has witnessed.

Holmes et al. have reported RAD16-I and RAD16-II (see Fig. 21) self-assembled peptide (sapeptide) scaffolds that support neurite outgrowth and synapse formation.51 These scaffolds consist of β-sheet oligopeptides containing periodic repeats of alternating ionic hydrophilic and hydrophobic amino acid residues (arginine-alanine-aspartate sequence), which spontaneously self-assemble under physiological conditions, leading to the hydrogel scaffold. The scaffold consists of intertwined fibers, 10–20 nm in diameter (as revealed by high resolution SEM). This scaffold not only acts as a substrate for the neuronal cell attachment and differentiation, but also supports extensive neurite outgrowth and functional synapse formation between the attached cells. Scanning laser confocal microscopy images showed that the neurite growth closely follows the contours of the scaffold (Fig. 21a). A fluorescent lipophilic probe FM1-43 was used to selectively label the location of the active synapses. FM1-43 has a lipophilic tail that anchors the molecule into the cell membrane, and a positively charged ammonium head group that prevents the molecule from permeating the cell. Once the samples were stained with FM1-43, positions of the bright fluorescent spots indicated the positions of the individual synapses (Fig. 21b).

Ulijn and co-workers have used supramolecular hydrogels derived from Fmoc based dipeptides as scaffolds for 3D-cell culture.52 Peptide 52 gelled water at a pH less than 4, whereas 53 formed hydrogel at a higher pH (< 8) (52 and 53 are shown in Chart 27). However, a 1:1 mixture of 52 and 53 was found to gel water at pH 7 (gel 54). Also, a stable hydrogel (gel 55) at neutral pH could be obtained by mixing 53 with Fmoc-Lys in 1:1 molar ratio. Gels 53, 54 and 55 were tested as scaffolds for retention and proliferation of phenotype bovine chondrocytes. In a typical experiment, the cells were incorporated into the gel by thorough mixing with the gel. Two photon fluorescence microscopy of the gel samples stained with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) revealed the cells were embedded in the gel matrix, confirming that the gel retained the cells. An MTT assay with the peptide gels over a seven day culture period showed a definite increase in the number of cells when measured on day 7 compared to day 1, indicating that the gel scaffold supported cell growth. Unlike gels 53 and 54, which showed similar cell growth profiles, gel 55 showed higher rate of cell proliferation and hence highest number of cells after 7 days, suggesting a possible role of the cationic Fmoc-Lys residues in chondrocyte cell proliferation.

Stupp and co-workers constructed self-assembled nanostructures that display heparin to proteins for cell signalling directed to angiogenesis.53 Peptide amphiphile 56 (Chart 28), which remains predominantly in the α-helical form in aqueous solution, adopts a β-sheet conformation in the presence of heparin, leading to cylindrical nanostructure formation and subsequent gelation of water. The negatively charged heparin screens the positive charge of 56 and triggers its self-aggregation. Because of its charge, the heparin is excluded from the hydrophobic interior of the gel and is presented on the surface of the nanofibers. When the nanofibers were doped with a PA containing a donor fluorophore that is exposed on the surface of the nanofibers and fluorescein-tagged heparin was used as an acceptor, Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET) with high efficiency was observed, indicating that heparin is indeed present on the exterior of the nanofiber surface. A hydrogel formed by mixing a dilute aqueous solution of 56 with an aqueous solution of heparin in the presence of the angiogenic growth factors FGF-2 and VEGF promoted significant neovascularisation response in a rat cornea angiogenesis assay, whereas the growth factors alone did not induce any detectable angiogenesis. The response was significantly higher than what was achieved with collagen gels in the presence of heparin and the growth factors. This was attributed to the rigidity of the self-assembled nanostructure over the polymer scaffold, which minimizes the Brownian motion opposing cell signalling and also reduces the entropic cost in the ligand–receptor binding.

In a clever exploitation of the interactions between peptide amphiphiles (PA) and biopolymers, Stupp's group has shown54 that when an aqueous solution of a peptide amphiphile (for example, 57) was brought in contact with an aqueous solution of hyaluronic acid (HA), a biopolymer present in human joints and cartilages, a solid membrane immediately formed at the interface of the two liquids, leading to flexible self-assembled sacs. Spinal cord injury (SCI) is a serious neurodegenerative condition which often leads to paralysis. This can be attributed to the formation of glial scars around the site of injury, which prevents the elongation of axons across the injury region, necessary to repair the damage. Therefore, the prevention of glial scar formation must be an essential component of any neuroregenerative technique that is used to treat injuries to the central nervous system. Mattiace et al. have chosen a negatively charged peptide amphiphile (PA) that can self-assemble into cylindrical nanofibers in the extracellular matrix of the injured spinal cord and present the laminine epitope IKVAV (isoleucine-lysine-valine-alanine-valine) to the cells in the spinal cord. Using a clip compression model of SCI (initial impact followed by persistent compression), they have shown that the injection of an aqueous solution of this peptide can significantly inhibit glial scar formation, reduce cell apoptosis and promote axon elongation in the event of a SCI in a mouse model.55,56 This work demonstrates the immense potential of self-assembled fibers displaying biological epitopes on the surface for controlling various cellular processes.

The durability of orthopaedic implants is limited by the stability and the sustainability of the cement or screw fixation. The design of implants that can promote bone ingrowth leading to osteointegration can help circumvent problems due to cement degradation or screw loosening. This nanofiber matrix was shown to assist mineralization of calcium phosphate phases with a Ca:P ratio of 1.71 ± 0.18 that corresponds to that of hydroxyapatite. An in vivo experiment conducted using a bone plug model in the diaphysis of the hind femurs of a Sprague Dawley rat revealed bone formation and neovascularisation around the implant, showing ample evidence of osteoconduction. The absence of neutrophils ruled out any cytotoxic response.

The examples discussed in this section clearly demonstrate the power of self-assembly and the increasingly important role it is poised to play in biomedicine. The recent burst of activities in this front is a testimony to that. 4. Sensing and responsive gels

Supramolecular gels are thermoreversible and this inherent property makes them thermoresponsive. Additionally, the incorporation of a receptor site or a photoresponsive group in the gelator molecule imparts sensitivity towards external stimuli such as chemicals or light. Azobenzene and anthracene units have been extensively used as the photoresponsive units whereas pH-responsiveness has been achieved by the introduction of acidic/basic sites in the gelator structure. The basic criterion however is that the supramolecular organization of the gelators must be influenced by the recognition events. The following examples will illustrate this point.

Shinkai and co-workers have designed several stimuli responsive gels by coupling chromophores to cholesterol based gelators.59 In one of these examples, the gel–sol transition of the gelator 59 (Chart 30) could be induced reversibly by irradiating lights of different wavelengths. The trans form of 59 gels n-butanol while the cis form does not. Thus, the irradiation of the gel of 59 at 330–380 nm results in gel-to-sol transition. The sol could be reverted back to the gel by irradiating with visible light (>460 nm) which induces the cis-to-trans isomerization.

The photoinduced dimerization of anthracene was exploited to trigger the gel–sol transition of a gel of n-decylammonium anthracene-9-carboxylate (60, Chart 31).60 The gel of this compound in cyclohexane could be transformed into a sol at 15 °C by irradiating with a mercury lamp as a result of the formation of dimerized product which was a non-gelator. Warming the sol (in the dark) to 30 °C dissociated the dimer and the gelator was regenerated as a precipitate. The gel could be reformed by heating the precipitate to sol and subsequently cooling the sol.

Pozzo et al. reported pH-sensitive organogels based on 2,3-di-n-alkoxyphenazines.61 The protonation of the basic sites of the phenazine moiety in these derivatives was exploited to make their gels pH-responsive. It was observed that the thermal stability of a gel of 61 (Chart 32) in acetonitrile increased till the addition of one equivalent of TFA but further addition of acid decreased the thermal stability. This was attributed to the fact that the hydrogen bonding between the nitrogen atoms and the ammonium centres in the monoprotonated species (in presence of one equivalent of acid) strengthened the gel but further protonation led to less soluble species. The reversibility of the process was demonstrated by the recovery of the initial thermal stability of the gel when ammonia was bubbled through the system.

Yoshimura et al. demonstrated the immobilization of artificial receptors in a hydrogel matrix of the glycosylated amino acid derivative 45 (Chart 33) for both sensing and discriminating phosphate derivatives such as phosphate ion, phosphate esters, ATP, etc.62 A PET type fluorescent chemosensor bis-Zn/Dpa-anthracene 62 in the hydrogel matrix showed fluorescence enhancement only upon the addition of phosphate derivatives whereas other ions such as sulfate, nitrate, acetate, azide, etc., did not exhibit any change. To distinguish between different phosphate derivatives, another chemosensor 63 having an environmentally sensitive dansyl chromophore was employed. It was found that upon addition of different phosphate derivatives, 63 got redistributed between the aqueous microcavity and hydrophobic fibers of the gel network depending on the nature of the phosphate derivatives. For example, hydrophilic ATP caused a red shift in the emission of 63 with reduced intensity whereas a blue shift with increased intensity was observed for hydrophobic phenyl phosphate, thus allowing the discrimination between different phosphate derivatives from both the wavelength shift and intensity change. This dynamic redistribution of the receptor upon guest binding was further utilized to design a FRET type molecular recognition chip by immobilizing a coumarin appended receptor 64a (FRET donor) and a hydrophobic styryl dye 64b (FRET acceptor) in the hydrogel matrix. Before addition of the phosphate derivatives, the receptor 64a was mainly localized in aqueous phase whereas 64b was fixed on the hydrophobic nanofibers. But upon addition of hydrophobic phosphate derivatives, the complexed receptor moved to a more hydrophobic domain which led to a shorter average distance between the donor and the acceptor and hence there was an increase in FRET. 5. Reactions in gel media

The prospect of carrying out reactions in the gel medium is attractive and has considerable potential. For one, doing reactions in a constrained environment can provide the advantage of specific interactions between the reactants, leading to better selectivity. Secondly, if the supramolecular gel network is chiral and the functions in the gelator molecule are tuned to facilitate specific reversible interactions with the chiral reactants, the gel network may act as a chiral template to carry out asymmetric reactions with high ee and de. This chiral gel network may also be used to template inorganic nanostructures that have potentials as heterogeneous asymmetric catalysts.

Koshima and co-workers reported possibly the first example of a photoreaction of the gelator molecules in the gel state.66 Benzophenone derivatives 68a–68c (Chart 34) containing alkylamides of different chain lengths gelled H-atom donor solvents like 2-propanol and 1,3,5-triisopropylbenzene. Irradiation of a 2-propanol gel of 68a afforded the corresponding pinacol as the main product. The reaction was much slower in the gel state (55 h) when compared to a control (solution of 68d in 2-propanol, 2 h). The restricted molecular mobility in the gel state was responsible for the slow kinetics of the reaction. The opaqueness of the gel leading to diffuse reflection of light could be another contributing factor. The scope of the photoreaction was also tested in a 1:1 gel of 68c with 69, a hydrogen donor gelator, in benzene. SEM image of this gel showed the coexistence of spherical aggregates of 68c and fine fibrous domain of 69, the respective microstructures observed in their individual gels. When this gel was subjected to photoreaction and the resulting precipitate was analyzed by SEM, no fine fibers could be observed, indicating the consumption of 69.

Triethylamine and 4-butylaniline were used as quenchers. The Stern–Volmer analysis of the emission (both steady state and time resolved) showed that the quenching constants (kq) were slightly higher in gel state than that in the solution state (in the absence of the gelator). The authors attributed this to an increased local concentration of the quencher due to “volume exclusion effect” of the gel network.

Miravet et al. used a reactive supramolecular organogel as a scaffold to create new gels with superior thermal stability.68 Valine derived gelator 71 contained reactive 4-nitrophenyl carbamoyl groups, which could easily be substituted through facile nucleophilic reaction with primary amines (72a–72d) in its acetonitrile gel to provide bisurea gels of 73a–73d (Scheme 9). However, for amine 72e, as the reaction proceeded, the gel progressively broke down. This was explained by considering that the basic secondary amino group of the piperidine moiety captured the proton released in the reaction, resulting in charged species that made the gel unstable due to electrostatic repulsion.

In an extension of this work, they tested the scope of the acetonitrile gel of 71 as a template for a macrocyclization reaction.69 When a mixture of the diamine 74 and Et3N in acetonitrile was placed on the top of the gel and the system was allowed to stand overnight, the gel underwent complete disintegration. Hence, this supramolecular gel acted as a “controlled release system” of the reactive species in the solution.

Shinkai and his group reported the construction of helical inorganic silica using a chiral organogel network (based on 1,2-diaminocyclohexane derivatives) as template.8 This example has been covered when we discussed the use of supramolecular gels as templates for inorganic nanostructure synthesis. Both right and left handed silica could be obtained by this process. They have further explored the utility of this chiral silica in asymmetric synthesis.70 When the addition of diisopropylzinc to aldehyde 76 was carried out in the presence of this chiral silica, high enantioselectivity (upto 97%) was achieved (Scheme 11). Specifically, the use of left handed helical silica led to the preferential formation of the R-enantiomer. The opposite enantiomer could be obtained when right handed silica was used. This work demonstrated the scope of chiral inorganic materials (whose chirality is derived from a chiral organic gel template) in asymmetric catalysis.

Recently, Diaz et al. reported a copper catalyzed azide–alkyne [3 + 2] cycloaddition reaction (CuAAC) as a means to stabilize the supramolecular organogel network,71 the first report of “click chemistry” in a supramolecular network. Organogelator 77, first reported by Hanabusa et al., was functionalized with alkyne and azide groups at the end of the hydrophobic side chain to give compounds 78 and 79. When the CuAAC reaction was performed in 19:1 MeCN:2,6-lutidine gels made from an equimolar mixture of 77 and 78 (or 79), in the presence of both dialkyne (82, 85) and diazide (80, 83) cross-linkers, gels with markedly increased thermal stability (by approximately 30 °C) were obtained (Scheme 12). However, when monofunctional “caps” (81, 84 and 86) were used, no such improvement of gel stability was observed. The strengthening of the gel structure was attributed to the covalent cross-linking of the non-covalent supramolecular network through triazole formation, which was confirmed from 1H NMR and MALDI-MS analysis of the cross-linked gels. Although several accounts of in situ stabilization of supramolecular gel networks had previously been reported, mostly post-polymerization of the gel fibers (containing polymerizable groups), these processes generate covalent polymers. The use of click chemistry in the present work shows a way to enhance the gel stability without sacrificing the thermoreversibility and preserving the overall structure of the supramolecular network, which underscores its utility.

Guler et al. have reported74 an example of supramolecular catalysis of ester hydrolysis on the surface of nanofibers formed by aggregation of the peptide amphiphile 90 (Chart 36). This section ends with an example where the use of supramolecular hydrogels as matrix to immobilize and improve the catalytic activity of enzymes has been explored. Enzymes often display substantially reduced activity in organic media compared to water, because of factors like substrate desolvation, reduced conformational flexibility etc. To circumvent this problem, Wang et al. immobilized methemoglobin (Hb) in the supramolecular hydrogel formed by the self-assembly of two amino acid derivatives 94 and 95.75 The similarity of the absorption and CD spectra obtained for the molecular gel bound Hb (Hb(I)), free Hb in water (Hb(U)) and a polymeric hydrogel bound Hb (Hb(III)) indicated that there is no structural difference between the gel bound enzyme and the free enzyme in water (Fig. 27a and 27c). The oxidation of pyrogallol by H2O2 was used as the model reaction. The activity of Hb(I) in toluene was found to be eight times that of Hb(U) in bulk water. Also, the initial rate of oxidation was much higher for Hb(I) compared to both Hb(U) and the polymeric gel immobilized enzyme Hb(III) (Fig. 27b). The observed superactivity of the supramolecular gel immobilized enzyme was attributed to the amphiphilicity of the nanofibers, which allows hydrophilic pyrogallol molecules to diffuse into the hydrogel and approach the enzyme. Also, the presence of diffuse nanoporous channels allows the products to smoothly leave the gel matrix, unlike in the polymeric gel matrix which consists of a randomly cross-linked network.

The examples shown above are by no means exhaustive. A certain level of success has been achieved in carrying out chemical reactions in gel media with superior selectivity and specificity. However, for these self-assembled gels, loading of the reactants is a critical issue. Not only do we need to achieve cleaner and selective reactions, we also need to enhance the loading capacity of these gels so that they can qualify as practical substitutes and the media of choice over the reactions in solutions. On the other hand, relatively less effort has been directed towards performing asymmetric reactions in chiral gel media. This area is expected to receive increasing attention in the coming years.

6. Organogelators as light harvesting systems

The creation of artificial architectures to mimic photosynthesis, the natural light harvesting process, has been an active area of research. The understanding of the detailed mechanism of the energy transfer processes in a simple synthetic system is likely to lead to the development of organic based electronic and photonic devices. One of the key requirements for the energy transfer is the organization of the donor and acceptor chromophores and this can be achieved by using the gel matrix of a supramolecular system as the scaffold. Additionally, the energy transfer in the supramolecular gels can be controlled by changing the temperature. Hence, in recent years there have been many reports in the literature on excitation energy transfer in chromophore-containing gelators. We will discuss a few selected examples. For a more elaborate discussion, the readers are referred to a recent review by Ajayaghosh et al.76

One of the first reports of energy transfer in organogel medium was reported from Sagawa et al.77 Pyrene functionalized glutamate derivative 96 formed gels in benzene and cyclohexane whereas the porphyrin functionalized derivative 97 (Chart 37) did not form gel. In a gel of 96 containing 97, singlet-singlet energy migration from pyrene excimers to porphyrin was observed.

Nakashima et al. reported an efficient energy transfer from naphthalene chromophores to anthracene chromophores in hydrogels of cationic L-glutamate derivatives 100 and 101 (Chart 38).78 These glutamate derivatives formed hydrogels upon addition of equimolar amount of an anionic naphthalene derivative 98 to their aqueous dispersions. When the hydrogels were doped with a small amount of an anionic anthracene derivative 99, quenching of the naphthalene fluorescence was observed along with a concomitant increase of anthracene emission.


Chart 38

Shinkai and co-workers reported an interesting energy transfer phenomenon in an organogel of a 1,10-phenanthroline based gelator 102 (Chart 39) containing two cholesteryl units.79 The gel of this compound in 1-propanol was purple under UV light but upon the addition of TFA the color changed to greenish-yellow. On melting the gel, a blue colored sol was obtained. The observed variation in color on going from the gel-to-sol state is because of the efficient energy transfer operating selectively in the gel state. The unprotonated and the protonated forms of 102 emit at 396 nm and 530 nm, respectively and the fluorescence spectrum of the unprotonated form overlapped with the absorption spectrum of the protonated form. So, in the gel state, energy transfer occurred from the free gelator to the protonated form leading to the enhancement of the emission from the protonated form and hence the greenish-yellow color was observed. This energy transfer was not operative in the sol state and so it was blue under UV light.

The spectral overlap between 103a and other derivatives decreases on going from 103b to 103d. So in binary gels, the energy transfer efficiency was maximum between 103a and 103b whereas 103a and 103d shows the least efficient energy transfer. However, the energy transfer was not observed in the solution state, which suggests the importance of chromophore organization in the gel state.

These systems mimic natural light harvesting processes and are also promising for the design of electronic devices. However many practical problems, including long-term stability, will have to be addressed before real applications are envisaged.

7. Organic optoelectronics

The supramolecular approach towards creating organic nanostructures for optoelectronic applications has attracted much attention in recent years. In section 7.1, with some selected examples, we shall demonstrate how a suitable execution of this approach leads to the construction of 1D ordered assembly of electroactive molecules through non-covalent interactions to give conducting supramolecular materials. Apart from their favorable electronic and optical properties, a proper spatial positioning of these molecules is the key to having efficient electron mobility, which may find use in solar cells, light-emitting diodes, field-effect transistors, etc. The supramolecular polymers based on these semiconducting, π-conjugated molecules not only offer flexibility in terms of reversibility of the interactions associated with the formation of the assembly, but also have precise long range arrangements of the monomer units, which sometimes are missing in conducting polymers due to the defects incorporated during the polymerization process.

The role of ionic liquid based physical gels will be discussed in section 7.2, with an emphasis on the SWNT dispersed gels of ionic liquids, showcasing the utility of gel–nanotube hybrid materials in optoelectronic applications. In these gels, the electron conduction pathway is provided by the ionic liquid. The concluding section will discuss the scope of the supramolecular gels of liquid crystalline materials, where the templating effect of the liquid crystal leads to an ordered arrangement of the growing gel fibers, having wide implications in the design of electroactive materials.

7.1. Supramolecular gels based on electroactive molecules

There are several reports of the use of π-conjugated molecules like tetrathiafulvalene (TTF), thiophene and phthalocyanine which form electroactive nanofibers through self-assembly. When the air dried gel was doped with I2, the self-assembled nanowires showed a near infrared (NIR) absorption band around 1750 nm (Fig. 31c), indicating the generation of a mixed valence-state. The creation of such mixed valence states has important implications in the construction of conducting nanofibers.

Aida and co-workers have recently succeeded in achieving the formation of single handed nanocoils from the coassembly of hexabenzocoronene (HBC) derivatives 118 and 119.88 Racemic 118 has the propensity to form nanocoils (a mixture of right and left handed) from its CH2Cl2 solution on vapor diffusion of Et2O into the solution, through π-stacking of its HBC units. This difference in color was attributed to the difference in the ECL of these polymerized gels (10 mer for 120 and 25 mer for 121). This example demonstrates a useful protocol that makes use of the supramolecular interactions to obtain highly ordered nanostructures, and then follows it up by polymerization of the nanofibers to obtain conducting polymers. Würthner and co-workers have reported on a perylene bisimide (PBI) based organogelator 122, which can gel several aliphatic and aromatic solvents at significantly low concentrations (< 0.2 wt%).90 The aggregation is driven by π–π stacking of the perylene chromophores reinforced by the intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions between the amide functions. AFM investigation of the gels showed the presence of well-defined helical fibrous aggregates (Fig. 36a). Slow cooling of an isotropic melt of compound 122 resulted in the formation of bundled fibers, with diameters in the micron range, which were visible under a polarizing optical microscope (Fig. 36b). Pulse-radiolysis time-resolved microwave conductivity (PR-TRMC) experiment showed significantly higher charge-carrier mobility (0.052 cm2 V−1 s−1) than the liquid crystalline mesophases of the structurally related PBI compounds. The results suggest that this molecule is an efficient n-type semiconductor, and the highly ordered structures created from its self-assembly can find application in solar cells in the context of creating bulk p–n-heterojunctions.

Shinkai and co-workers have recently reported orthogonal self-assembly of an oligothiophene based organogelator (123 or 124) and a perylene bisimide based organogelator (103) to form separate p-type and n-type stacks in a mixed organogel of the two compounds (Scheme 15).91 Both the compounds individually formed gels in chlorobenzene (the oligothiophenes 123 and 124 formed p-type stacks and the perylene bisimide 103 formed n-type stacks), the self-assembly being governed by the π–π stacking and hydrogen bonding interactions. The absorption and CD spectra of a mixed gel (123 and 103) formed in chlorobenzene were the summation of the individual spectra of the gelators obtained from their respective gels, indicating that the two compounds formed independent assemblies in the mixed gel. SEM investigation of these gels showed that the fiber diameters are similar (∼20 nm) in the mixed organogel when compared to the gels made from the individual gelators. This indicated the formation of separate p-type and n-type fibers, with p–n heterojunctions created at the nodal points of the fibers. Fluorescence studies of a mixed gel cast on an ITO electrode indicated energy transfer between the oligothiophene and perylene bisimide chromophores. Photoelectric current could be generated by irradiating this film with visible light, and the current could be reversibly turned on and off. Although Meijer and co-workers have recently been able to create p–n hereterojunctions through a supramolecular approach,92 this is the first example where the concept of orthogonal self-assembly has been successfully employed in supramolecular organogels.

Shirakawa et al. have shown that metallogels have potentials as field emission materials.93 They reported 3,4,5-tris(n-dodecyloxy)benzoylamide appended 8-quinolinol/metal(II)-chelate derivatives 125, 126 and 127 (Chart 45), which act as versatile gelators of both polar and non-polar organic solvents due to a synergistic operation of π–π stacking, hydrogen bonding and van der Waals interactions. The gels cast on ITO electrodes showed different threshold electric fields (90, 125 and 40 V μm−1 for 125, 126 and 127 gels respectively). These metallogels provided an easy handle to control the turn-on field values by changing the central metal atom, which would otherwise be difficult to achieve through synthetic manipulation.

7.2. Gels of ionic liquids

Physical gelation of ionic liquids by low molecular mass organic compounds is of current interest because of the many favorable properties of the ionic liquids. This makes these quasi solidified ionic liquids (physical gels) attractive replacements for organic electrolytes, which are generally volatile and flammable. Hanabusa et al. reported94 the gelation of a large number of ionic liquids by cyclodipeptides 128 and 129 (Chart 46). These gels showed high ionic conductivity and high thermal stability, with the melting point above 140 °C at a gelling concentration of 7% (w/v). The ionic conductivities of the gels from ionic liquids 130 and 131 formed by the gelator 128 were 4.75 and 3.61 mS cm−1, respectively. These values are quite similar to the conductivities of the neat ionic liquids, indicating that the presence of the gelator did not affect the ionic mobility of the ions present in the ionic liquid. Moreover, cyclic voltammetry of these gels showed that the gels are electrochemically stable over a wide potential range, underscoring their utility for electrochemical applications.

Aida and co-workers have recently observed that grinding a suspension of single walled carbon nanotubes (SWNT) with imidazolium based ionic liquids led to the formation of physical gels, the so called “bucky gels”.95 These gels have good potential for electrochemical applications since they simultaneously contain dispersed nanotubes (which are π-conjugated materials) and an electrolyte (the ionic liquid). These gels have been used in electric double layer capacitors,96 where the carbon nanotubes act as nanoelectrodes and the ionic liquid serves as an electrolyte. Aida's group reported the construction of a bucky gel based plastic actuator that works in air.97 The actuator was prepared by a layer by layer casting of the electrode (SWNT) and electrolyte (ionic liquid) components in a gelatinous mixture of 4-methyl-2-pentanone and a vinylidene difluoride hexafluoropropylene copolymer (PVdF(HFP)). On application of an electric potential, the actuator showed a bending motion towards the anode. Apart from these examples, facile chemical and electrochemical functionalization of SWNTs dispersed in the bucky gels have also been reported.98 Electroconductive polymers obtained from the bucky gels of polymerizable ionic liquids display large electrical conductivity.99

The examples discussed in the preceding paragraph give a brief overview of the extensive use of SWNT dispersed gels of ionic liquids as soft functional materials, particularly in the field of electrochemistry. For a comprehensive account of this subject, the readers are referred to a recent review by Fukushima and Aida.100

7.3. Liquid crystalline physical gels

Physical gels of liquid crystals represent an unusual anisotropic phase separated materials (containing liquid crystalline mesophase and the fibrous network of the gelator) and is an important class of compounds for electro-optic applications. A gelled liquid crystalline material can display two independent thermoreversible phase transitions: sol-to-gel transition, and isotropic-anisotropic phase transition of the liquid crystal. When the isotropic–anisotropic phase transition temperature (Tiso–lc) is higher than the sol–gel transition temperature (Tsol–gel), the LC phase can act as a template for the oriented growth of the gel fibers. Such ordered arrangement of fibers in the anisotropic phase leads to improved electro-optic response. For example, Kato and co-workers demonstrated that the nematic LC physical gels (having Tiso–lc > Tsol–gel) of 133 with 0.4 wt% of 132 (Chart 47) showed faster electro-optic response compared to the parent liquid crystals in twisted nematic cells.101 Kitamura et al. have reported the self-assembly of TTF based gelators in aromatic liquid crystals.102 These self-assembled fibers, after doping with I2, showed conductivity due to the formation of a mixed-valence state. Zhao and co-workers have reported on a fluorescent LC gel (0.5–2.0 wt% of the gelator 134 used to gel BL006, a nematic LC), whose fluorescence can be controlled by applying an electric field.103 In the field-off state, the LC domains in the gel are randomly oriented, leading to an inhomogeneous system with low optical transmittance, where the incident light can have many encounters with the gel fibers and can be harvested, leading to an intense photoluminescence. However, when an electric field is applied, the LC molecules align along the direction of the field, leading to the homogenization of the system. As a result, the optical transmittance of the medium increases, along with a decrease in photoluminescence intensity. The simple principle of electrical switching of photoluminescence discussed in this example may have important ramifications in display applications. In an extension of this work,104 they showed that the photoluminescence of quantum dots dispersed in LC gels can be controlled with an electric field in the manner described above (high intensity at field-off state and low intensity at field-on state). The reverse control could also be achieved by preparing a LC gel using a covalent polymer instead of a self-assembling gelator.

Conclusions

Despite a lack of complete understanding of the self-assembly process, gelators and gels have been manipulated in recent years in a variety of ways to design useful functional systems. The initial development took place in the templated synthesis of inorganic nanomaterials. After successfully employing different classes of supramolecular gels for this purpose, this knowledge was subsequently extended to make hybrid materials. Hybrid materials in which metal nanoparticles are stabilized/immobilized in the gel matrix by incorporating appropriate functionalities in the gel structure, are novel composites with interesting properties. The reversible gel–sol transition property of supramolecular gels has been utilized to make multiresponsive materials. In chromophore-containing gelators, the ordered arrangement of donor–acceptor pairs has been exploited to mimic natural light harvesting processes and this knowledge is likely to be useful for the development of nanodevices. In the area of supramolecular electronics the integration of electroactive motifs in the gelator structure has afforded materials suitable for optoelectronic applications. On the other hand, the use of biocompatible and biodegradable building blocks such as peptides and sugars in hydrogels has led to functional biomaterials with applications in areas such as drug delivery, screening of enzyme inhibitors and tissue engineering. These gels have been successfully utilized as scaffolds for cell growth and proliferation, opening new vistas in the field of regenerative medicine. Major advances in this field have reflected the practical utility of supramolecular gels and self-assembled nanostructures in general. For example, self-assembled sacs discussed earlier have been shown to encapsulate and support the growth of stem cells. This can be visualized as a miniaturized cell machinery with exciting prospects in cell therapy. Despite such encouraging advances, many practical challenges such as the stability of these systems in prolonged use remain to be addressed. For everyday application of hydrogels as drug delivery vehicles, key issues such as the response of these gels to a multienzyme environment encountered in human cells need deeper understanding. In order to achieve practically useful and functional materials, it is also important that the constituent molecules are constructed easily. The gel–nanoparticle hybrids have not yet graduated for real life applications, except for the exploration of ionic liquid–SWNT hybrid gels in electrochemical devices. The research exploring the use of supramolecular gels as reaction media is still in its infancy. We are still far from achieving the kind of selectivity and specificity in reactions such as photochemical and asymmetric transformations (using a chiral gel network as template) that will make these gels compete with reactions in isotropic media. Another challenge is to enhance the loading capacity of reactants in the gel matrix. We are certain, however, that this field will witness significant progress in the coming years leading to more practical applications.
