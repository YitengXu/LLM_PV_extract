Abstract

Emerging perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have evolved rapidly in recent years, and their efficiency has been substantially boosted from the initial 3.8% to certified 25.73% now, showing great application potential. Therefore, they have received extensive attention from both industry and academia. However, there is still a certain gap between this value and its theoretical efficiency. In addition, long-term stability is a major challenge hindering its commercial applications. The modification of the heterojunction interface in PSCs, especially buried interface passivation, is crucial to further improve device efficiency and stability. In this review, the impacts of the morphology, composition, microstructure and other characteristics at the buried interface on PSC performance are systematically investigated. Moreover, proposed strategies and corresponding mechanisms for effective buried interface passivation are summarized, looking forward to further promoting the device performance by providing reference and guidance for designing and developing more effective passivation strategies.

1. Introduction

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have achieved dramatic improvements in efficiency in just over a decade,1–6 showing broad application prospects.7 This is mainly attributed to the excellent optoelectronic properties of perovskite materials, such as tunable band gap,8 high optical absorption coefficient,9,10 small exciton binding energy,11 long carrier diffusion length,12 high carrier mobility,13 relatively balanced electron and hole transfer,14 and easy fabrication. Although power conversion efficiency (PCE) has been boosted to certified 25.73%15 in single-junction devices, there is still a certain gap compared with its theoretical limit.16 Additionally, the device long-term stability has not completely succeeded17 and definitively hinders its commercialization.

When the device short-circuit current density (JSC) closes to the Shockley–Queisser (S–Q) limit, the further increase in PCE mainly depends on the improvement in open circuit voltage (VOC) and fill factor (FF),18 which requires effective inhibition of non-radiative recombination in the device.19 The interface of PSCs is involved in all processes of carrier separation, transport, collection, and recombination. Hence, the high trap density at the interface inevitably leads to severe non-radiative recombination and VOC loss.20 Moreover, the perovskite degradation induced by the defects at the interface is the principal reason for the device deterioration.21 Therefore, proper interface regulation between various functional layers in PSCs is vital to further ameliorate the device performance.22

Perovskite morphology and crystallinity significantly influence the device performance.23 Tremendous progress has been achieved through composition engineering and the optimization of the perovskite growth process. Regulating the interface between the perovskite and carrier transporting layer (CTL) is becoming important to further improve the device performance.24 As illustrated in Fig. 1a and b, Jiang et al. employed a phenethylammonium iodide (PEAI) solution with an appropriate concentration to reprocess the top surface of the perovskite.5 This decreases the surface roughness from 32 to 24 nm and obviously heals the iodine vacancies on the surface and grain boundaries (GBs), thereby improving the photoluminescence (PL) intensity and carrier lifetime. Accordingly, planar PSCs with a VOC up to 1.18 V and a certified PCE of 23.32% were achieved. Moreover, this strategy effectively alleviates perovskite degradation. Therefore, interface passivation is a simple and effective measure to promote device performance and has become the mainstream for the optimization of PSCs.

Piperazinium iodide (PI) can modulate the terminal groups and chemical environment on the perovskite top surface,25 as shown in Fig. 1c, which effectively heals defects and improves the device performance. PI-treated perovskite shows intensive PL with an intensity about five times greater than that of the reference film, and the carrier lifetime is also significantly enhanced (Fig. 1d), indicating that the energy loss arising from the interfacial non-radiative recombination is significantly inhibited by the effective passivation of lead clusters, uncoordinated halogens, and Pb–I antisites. Compared with the PI molecular one, piperazinium diiodide (PDI) containing only the R2NH2+ group shows an inferior passivation effect, indicating the superiority of multifunctional passivation molecules.

Cyclohexylethylammonium iodide (CEAI) can form a two-dimensional (2D) perovskite intermediate layer that comprises CEA2PbI4 on the perovskite surface, as illustrated in Fig. 1e. The 2D layer effectively passivates the defects at the surface and GBs of the 3D perovskite, resulting in a significant reduction in surface roughness and non-radiative recombination without affecting charge transport.26 Compared with PEAI, the interaction between the cyclohexanes in CEAI is weaker and hence avoids molecular stacking. This is more conducive to forming a uniform film and reducing recombination loss. Moreover, attributed to the intrinsic properties of the 2D perovskites and cyclohexylethyl groups, the device exhibited excellent long-term stability.

As described above, previous studies have primarily concentrated on the modification and passivation of the perovskite upper surface.27–29 However, deep-level traps, such as excessive lead halide, aggregate at the perovskite bottom interface, which causes severe non-radiative recombination.30 As the PCE of PSCs gradually approaches the theoretical limit, it becomes the primary obstacle to further ameliorating the device performance.

This paper discloses the formation mechanism of defects at the buried interface and their influence on device performance. It conducts a comprehensive review of effective buried interface passivation strategies and the specific effects on defect density, perovskite quality, energy band alignment, and charge transfer. It is anticipated that this review can provide useful information and constructive guidelines for exploring more effective passivation strategies, thus further promoting the device performance.

2. Impact of a buried interface on device performance

One of the highlighted advantages of PSCs is that they can be easily prepared using a simple and low-cost solution process at a low temperature.31 This implies that the formation energy of perovskites is low. Thus, associated with the annealing process and rapid crystallization during film fabrication,32 combined with the influence of environmental factors, such as temperature and humidity, numerous defects33 related to FA or I vacancy,34 Pb–I antisite, uncoordinated Pb2+ and lead cluster are introduced at GBs and interfaces of polycrystalline perovskite films. This verifies that the defect density of polycrystalline perovskite is two to three orders of magnitude greater than that of a high-quality monocrystalline film. Moreover, the defect density at the interface is one to two orders of magnitude higher than that of the interior,35 as depicted in Fig. 2a–d. Therefore, healing defects through effective interface passivation is essential to improve the device performance. In addition, by measuring the spatial trap distribution in PSCs with the structure of ITO/PTAA/perovskite/C60/BCP/Cu utilizing the drive-level capacitance profiling (DLCP) method, it is found that the defect density near the PTAA/perovskite interface is greater than that around the C60/perovskite interface (Fig. 2b). This surprisingly indicates that the defect density near the bottom interface is significantly higher than that of the top interface. Hence, a comprehensive study on the basic properties of buried interfaces in PSCs is necessary to further improve device performance.

However, as the buried interface is sandwiched between functional layers, it brings technical difficulties to characterize directly with existing technology.36 Yang et al. proposed a simple lift-off process,37 in which a PTAA transporting layer dissolvable in chlorobenzene (CB) was coated on the ITO substrate as a sacrificial layer for lift-off. Then, the perovskite film was fabricated above, followed by a copper capping layer to ensure the toughness of the polycrystalline film during the lift-off process. Perovskite/copper film could separate from the ITO substrate after soaking in CB for 20 minutes at room temperature, which was then transferred onto the substrate and exposed to the bottom interface, as illustrated in Fig. 2e. Subsequently, the relationship between the microstructure of the buried interface and device performance is established based on systematic characterization and evaluation. It is found that the morphology and surface potential of the perovskite top and bottom interfaces distinctly differed. The top surface is uniform with smaller lead halide particles at GBs, whereas large flaky lead halide ones are identified on the buried interface, as shown in Fig. 2f and g. In addition, A-site cations are unevenly distributed, and the ([I] + [Br])/[Pb] ratio is 2.32 on the buried interface, which obviously deviates from the stoichiometric ratio compared with 2.81 of the top surface. Multiscale inhomogeneity ultimately leads to more defects and severe non-radiative recombination at the buried interface. Therefore, submicroscopic-scale extended defects and lead halide inhomogeneity at the buried interface are the main reasons for the device degradation. Introducing moderate PbI2 to adjust the stoichiometric ratio on a buried interface can improve the interface quality to a certain extent.38 However, excess PbI2 conversely deteriorates device performance. Therefore, a more effective strategy for buried interface passivation is preferred.

Holes with ten to hundreds of nanometers are detected on the buried interface of both MAPbI3 and MA–FA hybrid perovskite films after peeling off from the substrate,39 as shown in Fig. 3a. This causes charge accumulation and recombination and triggers various perovskite decomposition processes, thereby accelerating the device deterioration under illumination. Introducing high-boiling point dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) into the precursor solution can regulate the perovskite crystallization process, resulting in the improved film morphology and quality commonly employed in the fabrication process of almost all high-efficiency perovskite solar cells. The solvent evaporates and escapes from the film surface during the fabrication; therefore, a solid shell is rapidly formed,40 which in turn temporarily confines about 10% of DMSO at the film bottom. Remnant DMSO eventually escaped from the film, especially after annealing, causing the collapse of film volume and introducing holes at the buried interface, as shown in Fig. 3b. Accordingly, solid carbohydrazide (CBH) was employed as an additive to partially replace DMSO, which can form stable coordination with Pb cations through CO bonds and remain in perovskite film without evaporation during the later annealing process. Therefore, CBH can avoid volume shrinkage and hole formation at the buried interface. It functions as a reducing agent to inhibit the generation of harmful I2 in perovskites. Finally, PSCs with a PCE of up to 23.6% were achieved combined with superior process repeatability and stability.

Moreover, buried CTLs significantly affect device performance. TiO2 (ref. 42) and SnO2 (ref. 6) are typical electron transporting materials (ETMs) adopted in normal structured PSCs, and the corresponding PCE has exceeded 25%. However, devices based on TiO2 show obvious J–V hysteresis and relatively poor ultraviolet (UV) stability because of its photocatalytic activity.43 The high-temperature sintering involved in TiO2 preparation hinders the achievement of flexible devices and elevates costs.44

In contrast, SnO2 is recognized as a more promising ETM45 with high light transmittance46 originating from the wide bandgap of ∼3.6 eV47 and electron mobility as high as ∼240 cm2 V−1 s−1.48 Additionally, it exhibits superior chemical stability even when prepared using the solution method at low temperatures.49 However, numerous defects are introduced during the preparation.50 As shown in Fig. 3c, SnO2 surface defects mainly include dangling bonds and two kinds of hydroxyl groups.41 Dangling bonds can easily absorb O2 and H2O in the atmosphere, and the absorbed oxygen captures electrons from the SnO2 conduction band and converts them into O−, O2− or O2−, forming a potential barrier and hindering electron transport.51 The hydroxyl groups include alkaline hydroxyl connected with Sn and acid one bridged on two metal sites. These surface defects lead to severe non-radiative recombination, disturb the interfacial interactions, and affect the perovskite quality. SnO2 internal defects are mainly caused by Sni, VO and Oi, VSn,52 and external defects are related to the doping of impurity elements. These defects easily cause carrier recombination, impede electron extraction and collection, and affect the device stability.53 Furthermore, the energy level mismatch between SnO2 and perovskite also hinders the efficient extraction and transfer of photogenerated electrons.54 Therefore, it is essential to optimize the buried CTL to further improve device performance.

3. Passivation strategies for the buried interface

As mentioned above, a high density of defects, especially deep-level defects, can induce severe carrier recombination at the buried interface,55 which also accelerates perovskite degradation, particularly when light is injected from the buried interface.56 In addition, appreciable strain appears at the interface if physical properties,57 such as thermal expansion coefficient,58 lattice constant59 and temperature gradient, between the CTL and perovskite differ,60 promoting the formation of interface defects, carrier transporting barriers, and ion migration, which, in turn, results in the device degradation.61 Moreover, energy level alignment at the interface influences the carrier extraction;62 a large energy level mismatch provides a sufficient potential gradient for carrier extraction but with a drastic energy loss through thermal relaxation and vice versa.63 Therefore, improving the interface performance and healing the defects through systematic modulation of the buried interface with effective strategies are judicious selections to further enhance the device performance.

3.1 Optimization of the buried carrier transporting layer

Ideal buried CTL possesses a suitable energy level matching well with the perovskite,54 guaranteeing sufficient light transmission64 and promoting the generation and extraction of photogenerated carriers.65 Additionally, high carrier mobility and conductivity are indispensable for facilitating carrier transport and suppressing charge accumulation at the interface.66 Moreover, a hydrophilic surface is expected to form good contact with perovskite, which is essential to improve perovskite quality and prevent non-radiative recombination.

However, defects are unavoidable in CTL, which reduces conductivity and causes additional recombination loss. The wettability and roughness of the CTL surface significantly influence the defect density and perovskite crystallinity.67 In addition, energy level mismatch between the CTL and perovskite results in charge accumulation and increased defects,68 inducing J–V hysteresis69 and degradation of device performance.70 Accordingly, numerous strategies have been developed, including surface passivation,71 additives introduction,72 and interfacial treatment, as presented in Table 1.

3.2 Passivation strategies at buried interfaces through ion diffusion

The crystalline quality of perovskite determines the carrier mobility and diffusion length, so a uniform and dense film with large grains is preferred to mitigate energy loss. Additionally, numerous defects parasitic in the interior, surface, and grain boundaries of perovskite,79 especially prepared with the solution process, can introduce states in the bandgap of perovskite,80 which reduces carrier extraction efficiency and accelerates film degradation assisted with oxygen and moisture.81 Moreover, energy barriers introduced between mismatched functional layers impede carrier transport, resulting in large VOC and energy loss.82

It has been verified recently that specific ions, such as halogen ions Cl−, F−,83 alkali ions Na+, K+,84 and Cs+ as well as other ions Eu3+, SO42−, CO32−, and S2−,85 introduced at the buried interface diffuse into the buried CTL and perovskite, respectively. This exhibited great potential to inhibit ion migration, heal defects, enhance perovskite crystallization, and optimize the energy band alignment of the device. This, in turn, alleviates the energy loss in the device and eliminates J–V hysteresis. A bifunctional approach was also proposed by introducing Eu3+ in SnO2,88 as shown in Fig. 6c. Most Eu3+ ions aggregate at the SnO2/perovskite interface, while some can diffuse from SnO2 into the perovskite interior owing to the guidable transfer of Eu3+, as illustrated in Fig. 6d and e. With the synergetic passivation effect of Eu3+ collected at the SnO2/perovskite interface and dispersed in SnO2, the electron mobility of SnO2 is considerably improved by eight times, associated with enhanced charge transfer and suppressed interfacial loss. Moreover, Eu3+ diffused in perovskite combined with this at the interface heals the lead- and iodine-related defects at the perovskite GBs, thereby improving the device FF. Furthermore, doped Eu3+ can elevate the quasi-Fermi level of electrons. The resulting larger quasi-Fermi level splitting enhances the built-in potential, thereby providing a sufficient driving force for carrier separation and inhibiting recombination. PSCs with an efficiency of 20.14% and enhanced VOC were achieved. In addition, it can retain 87% of the initial efficiency after 84 day storage in the atmosphere probably because Eu3+ aggregated at GBs and interface can stabilize the perovskite by preventing moisture penetration.

CsI incorporated in SnO2 can change the electron density around Sn atoms and reduce the work function of the SnO2 surface by possibly forming an Sn–O–Cs dipole,94 as shown in Fig. 6f, thereby decreasing the energy barrier in the device. It is demonstrated through extensive characterization that Cs can promote vertical growth and improve the crystallinity of perovskite by modulating nucleation. Consequently, photovoltage decay time increased obviously from 29.4 to 92.2 μs, and the defect density in perovskite decreased from 9.67 × 1015 to 4.83 × 1015 cm−3, as illustrated in Fig. 6g, indicating that CsI doping prolongs the carrier lifetime and reduces the non-radiative recombination loss significantly. Simultaneously, the gradient distribution of Cs+ around the buried interface provides a more matching energy level arrangement (Fig. 6h) and passivates the interface defects, thereby facilitating charge transfer. As mentioned above, the SnO2/perovskite buried interface, with more defects than the upper side, suffers from severer degradation under stronger light stress and threatens the stability of PSCs.97 As indicated in Fig. 6h, this strategy enables Cs+ to diffuse into the perovskite bottom, thereby playing a key role as a stabilizer to adjust the lattice strain in FAPbI3 and effectively mitigating ion migration and light-induced perovskite degradation. Accordingly, device stability against UV radiation is improved considerably, which preserves 88% of the initial PCE after 500 hours of UV irradiation. PSCs with an efficiency of 23.33% and negligible J–V hysteresis were finally achieved, and the unpackaged device retained 90% of the initial PCE after prolonged illumination at the MPP for more than 500 hours, as shown in Fig. 6i. This study provides a versatile and effective method for efficient and UV-resistant PSCs.

Xiong et al. fabricated an NdCl3 layer at the SnO2/perovskite interface by vacuum deposition,91 as depicted in Fig. 7a. Chloride ions interact strongly with perovskite and compensate for iodine vacancies, thereby passivating defects and suppressing non-radiative recombination. Moreover, NdCl3 can reinforce the ion migration activation energy in perovskite from 118.2 to 214.0 meV, as shown in Fig. 7b, thereby effectively suppressing iodine migration. In turn, both vacancy-related defects at the perovskite GBs and the interface trap between the CTL and perovskite can be effectively annihilated, as illustrated in Fig. 7c. NdCl3-treated perovskite film exhibits obviously annihilated PL and quick PL decay when excited from the ETL side, implying faster carrier collection and suppressed traps at the buried interface. Finally, a PSC with negligible J–V hysteresis, a VOC as high as 1.138 V and an efficiency of 22.13% was achieved. Moreover, it retained 83% of the initial value after 100 hours of MPP tracking, showing excellent stability.

Lithium salts with various anion configurations (CO32−, C2O42−, and HCOO−) were employed to modify the FAPbI3/SnO2 interface, as shown in Fig. 7d. Compared with C2O42− and HCOO−, CO32− has more negative adsorption energy with FA+, indicating that the hydrogen bond formed between the CO of this anion and the N–H of FA+ is stronger, thus resulting in a higher formation energy of the FA vacancy (Fig. 7e).95 Therefore, it passivates FA+ vacancies more efficiently. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) results show only when CO and two C–O bonds are in a triangular configuration, just as in CO32−, the C–O interacts strongly with uncoordinated Sn4+ and binds more tightly with SnO2, thus effectively improving the SnO2 morphology. The (002) diffraction peak of perovskite film modified with Li2CO3 shows a larger shift to a small angle, and the slope of the 2θ–sin2Ψ curve is also smaller in the grazing incidence mode, implying that the internal strain in perovskite film is relieved, as illustrated in Fig. 7f. Therefore, Li2CO3 modification can efficiently release residual strain98 and promote the crystallization and stability of perovskite.61 Moreover, the PL intensity and carrier lifetime of Li2CO3-modified perovskite are obviously reduced, demonstrating that Li2CO3 modification leads to faster charge extraction and transport. In addition, Li+ diffused around the grain boundary attracts electrons but repulses holes, thereby inhibiting non-radiative recombination. The diffusion of Li+ to SnO2 is also beneficial for enhancing conductivity. Furthermore, Li2CO3 modification increases the contact potential difference at FAPbI3/SnO2 interface, yielding an enhanced built-in potential and depletion region and providing a stronger driving force for carrier separation and extraction. Devices with an efficiency of 23.5% were achieved, as shown in Fig. 7g, which maintained 90% of their initial efficiency under steady illumination for 1200 hours, demonstrating improved light stability.

Volatile ammonium formate (HCOONH4) pre-embedded in SnO2 ETL evaporates and percolates into perovskite from the bottom to the top during annealing, achieving the in situ integral modification of ETL, perovskite and the interface,93 as shown in Fig. 7h. HCOONH4 treatment reduces oxygen vacancies on SnO2 surface and suppresses carrier recombination through enhanced electron extraction and transport, which diffused to the SnO2/perovskite interface can enhance the interfacial contact and improve the lattice uniformity of perovskite by alleviating strain, as illustrated in Fig. 7i. Moreover, it can retard crystallization through the exchange of halogen anions with HCOO− percolated in perovskite, thereby reducing lattice defects, remarkably removing the surface wrinkles of perovskite grains, and improving film quality. The bottom-up modification significantly releases the residual strain and micro-strain in perovskite, passivates defects in perovskite, interface and GBs,99 and prolongs the device operation stability, as shown in Fig. 7j. Consequently, a flexible perovskite solar cell (F-PSC) with a laboratory PCE of 22.37% (a certified value of 21.9%) was achieved, which is the highest PCE reported to date. The resulting device retained more than 90% of the initial efficiency after 4000 cycles of bending without obvious changes in perovskite surface morphology (Fig. 7k), showing excellent mechanical durability. This study provides scientific guidance on residual strain mitigation at the buried interface.

Potassium sulphate (K2SO4) introduced at the SnO2/perovskite interface can passivate defects in perovskite and buried interface89 through the synergistic effect of anions and cations,100 as shown in Fig. 8a. K+ can diffuse into perovskite, as verified in Fig. 8b, thereby passivating defects in perovskite and prolonging the carrier lifetime. Moreover, it can inhibit ion migration by suppressing halide Frenkel defects, thereby minishing the device J–V hysteresis. Moreover, SO42− can form strong coordination with oxygen vacancies on the SnO2 surface and Pb2+ on the perovskite bottom interface (Fig. 8c and d), thereby passivating buried interface defects and inhibiting non-radiative recombination. In addition, K2SO4 facilitates electron transport by enhancing the SnO2 conductivity and promoting the growth of perovskite grains (increased from 259 to 279 nm). Moreover, K2SO4 treatment decreases the SnO2 conduction band from −3.90 to −3.95 eV, matching well with triple cation perovskite (CsFAMA) (−3.92 eV), which increases the device built-in potential from 0.94 to 0.99 V and facilitates carrier separation and transfer owing to the increased driving force and reduced potential barrier, as shown in Fig. 8e. Consequently, the VOC of the device was improved by limiting the voltage loss to 0.53 V, and the PCE was substantially elevated from 19.45% to 21.18%.101 The corresponding device depicts promoted stability, showing only a 15% PCE decline even after storage at 25% relative humidity for 1000 hours.

SnO2 conductivity can be enhanced with this ionic liquid treatment, thereby facilitating electron transport. Improved surface wettability of SnO2 and the introduction of Cl promote perovskite crystallinity. Thiophene-based molecules exhibit a polarized charge distribution (Fig. 14g), which can improve the perovskite crystallinity and tune the device band alignment through a simple solution process,30 thus reducing the energy loss, as illustrated in Fig. 14e. The –COOH group can passivate the –OH dangling bonds on SnO2 surface. The thiophene ring donates electrons to the carboxylic acid side, improving the electrical conductivity of SnO2 and inhibiting non-radiative recombination by tuning the electronic state of the SnO2 surface, as illustrated in Fig. 14f. In particular, the thiophene-3-acetic acid molecule (TA 2) with a larger dipole moment (4.03) can reduce the SnO2 work function from 4.65 to 4.36 eV, tuning the Fermi level (EF) closer to the perovskite conduction band, thus enhancing charge collection. The perovskite band bends upward without treatment, as shown in Fig. 14h, which results in electron accumulation. In contrast, it bends downward because of the interfacial dipole with the TA 2 treatment, as illustrated in Fig. 14i. The energy gap between the EF of SnO2 and the conduction band of perovskite decreases, while the gap between SnO2EF and the valence band of perovskite increases, thereby suppressing interfacial carrier recombination and enhancing device VOC by promoting electron extraction and impeding hole reverse transfer. Moreover, TA 2 treatment reduces nucleation density by improving the SnO2 surface morphology. In addition, S atoms on the thiophene ring with an electron lone pair can interact with uncoordinated Pb2+ in perovskite, thus delivering polycrystalline perovskite film with greater grain size and lower defect density. MAPbI3-based PSCs with promoted VOC from 1.072 V to 1.117 V and PCE of 20.61% were achieved eventually, which also exhibits good thermal and humidity stability.

Both the top and bottom interfaces of Sn–Pb hybrid perovskite are modified by introducing glycine hydrochloride (GlyHCl) into the perovskite precursor, and post-treatment on perovskite with ethylenediammonium diiodide (EDAI2),128 which considerably improves device performance, as illustrated in Fig. 15a–c. The EDA2+ cations introduced by post-treatment are mostly accumulated on the perovskite surface. One of its methylamine groups connects to perovskite grains and greatly inhibits surface defects; the other one points away from perovskite and induces the surface dipole. The potential difference generated by the dipole promotes the extraction and collection of electrons from the perovskite to the ETL. The methylamine groups of GlyH+ cations aggregated at the buried interface can act as nucleation centers during perovskite growth, consequently enhancing the film crystallinity and passivating the recombination centers simultaneously. The electronegative –COOH groups point to the HTL; accordingly, an electric field directed to the HTL is introduced by the dipole, promoting hole extraction. Consequently, Sn–Pb hybrid PSCs with a PCE as high as 23.6% were achieved. The unencapsulated device also maintained over 80% of the initial PCE after 200 hours of illumination in an inert environment.

Electron density gradually decreases from the negative charge center (–SO2−) to the positive charge center (–C(NH2)2+) in the zwitterion of formamidine sulfinic acid (FSA), thereby introducing a strong dipole moment at the buried interface,129 as demonstrated in Fig. 15d and e. This optimizes the energy level alignment between SnO2 and perovskite, facilitates electron extraction and blocks hole transport (Fig. 15f). In addition, Sn atoms on SnO2 accept electrons from FSA molecules through Sn–O bonds, resulting in a remarkable increase in delocalized electrons on Sn atoms and the charge redistribution on the ETL surface, thus enhancing ETL conductivity. FSA treatment can improve interfacial contact by flattening the SnO2 surface and hence promoting the vertical growth of high-quality perovskite grains. Moreover, the stacking direction of residual PbI2 in film can be modulated in the vertical direction, which regularly distributes at the perovskite GBs after FSA treatment, adequately inhibiting the side effects on the perovskite structure (Fig. 15g). Furthermore, the strong interaction between C(NH2)2+ groups in the FSA and [PbI6]4− structures of perovskite can effectively suppress the Pb–I antisites in perovskite (Fig. 15h). Consequently, this obviously reduces carrier recombination and promotes interfacial charge extraction and transport, delivering FAPbI3 PSCs with efficiency as high as 24.1%, which is one of the highest value for FAPbI3-based PSCs fabricated using the two-step method. The unpackaged device remained at 85% of the initial PCE after 1000 hours of continuous illumination in a nitrogen atmosphere, showing excellent long-term operating stability.

Therefore, dipoles induced through directional aggregation at the interface demonstrated an excellent ability to improve the device performance. Comprehensive characterization and systematic study of the formation and function mechanisms further promote their vast applications in solar cells.

4. Conclusions and outlook

In this review, we systematically inspect the morphology, composition and microstructure of buried interface, and we then summarize the effective passivation strategies and corresponding mechanisms from the perspectives of buried carrier transporting layer optimization, ion diffusion, chemical bridge, 2D perovskite, and electrical dipole effect.

It is recognized that, as the PCE of PSCs approaches the theoretical limit, buried interface engineering is becoming critical for improving device performance. Although great progress has been made, there are still some challenges that need to be addressed. For example, a comprehensive study through systematic in situ characterization of buried interfaces is relatively difficult and needs further exploration. In addition, novel materials with lower cost, multiple functions and excellent environmental stability should be proposed. However, it is believed that as an effective strategy, buried interface passivation further promotes PSC performance.
