Abstract

As a newly-rising member in the perovskite family of solar cell absorbers, the lead-free halide double perovskites Cs2M+M3+X6 (X = Cl, Br, I), with suitable bandgaps in the visible-light region and intrinsic thermodynamic stability, pave a new way in designing environmentally friendly perovskite solar cell devices. Here, for the first time, we optimize 24 kinds of layered Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) type n = 1â€“3 Csn+1Mn/2+Mn/23+X3n+1 (M+: In+, Cu+, Ag+, Au+; M3+: Bi3+, Sb3+) and investigate their electronic, optical and transport properties based on powerful first-principles and GW-BSE calculations. Surprisingly, we find that Cs4In3/2Sb3/2I10 (n = 3) has a direct bandgap of 1.29 eV, a very suitable value for visible-light absorption and emission. In order to modulate the bandgap and charge distribution, we further construct 7 two-dimensional halide double perovskite heterostructures with direct bandgaps and type-II band alignment, leading to a remarkable spatial separation of photo-generated carriers and a noticeable reduction of the carrier recombination rate, which is beneficial for photovoltaic devices. Typically, the Cs4In3/2Sb3/2Cl10/Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4 heterostructure has a direct bandgap of 1.65 eV with a great electronâ€“hole spatial separation. Both the different layered CsInSbI perovskites and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl-based heterostructures have an ultrahigh absorption coefficient of âˆ¼105 cmâˆ’1 in the visible-light region, which is comparable with bulk MAPbI3. Moreover, their electron and hole mobilities are three/four orders of magnitude larger than MAPbI3 up to 102â€“103 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. The novel properties of the suitable bandgap, spatial separation of carriers, excellent absorption coefficient and extremely high carrier mobility suggest that the 2D layered RP-type CsInSbI- and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl-based perovskites have great potential as promising lead-free solar cell absorbers and solid-state-lighting materials.

Introduction

Hybrid organicâ€“inorganic halide perovskite semiconductors have attracted widespread attention recently due to their remarkable optoelectronic properties for device applications, such as photovoltaic and solid-state-lighting devices, especially the lead (Pb)-based perovskite materials MAPbX3 and FAPbX3 [MA = CH3NH3+; FA = HC(NH2)2+; X = Clâˆ’, Brâˆ’, Iâˆ’].1â€“5 The Pb-based halide perovskites have revolutionized the field of solution processable solar cells, achieving a certified record power conversion efficiency (PCE) up to 23.3% quite recently, which has surpassed multicrystalline (22.3%) and thin-film silicon photovoltaic devices (21.2%).6 Lead halide perovskites have many advanced properties, such as a low cost fabrication process,7 remarkable light absorption in the visible-light region,8 and long diffusion length of the photo-generated carriers,9 as well as the ability to recycle the photons emitted upon radiative recombination.10 However, despite these attractive merits, lead hailde perovskite solar cells are still facing severe challenges, i.e., the presence of toxic element Pb and the poor stability upon long-time exposure to light, humidity and increased temperature.11â€“15 Therefore, looking for non-toxic, environmentally friendly and more stable perovskite solar cell absorber materials has attracted much attention and become a current research hotspot.

Recently, the emerging two-dimensional (2D) perovskites are attractive due to their long carrier lifetime, high photoluminescence (PL) quantum efficiency, widely tunable bandgap, high carrier mobility and great defect tolerance.16â€“18 More importantly, 2D perovskites have greater structural stability than their 3D counterparts due to the moisture resistance imposed by the hydrophobic organic molecules at the surface, making them more promising for large-scale industrial implementation.19â€“21 The 2D halide perovskites can be easily obtained by mechanical exfoliation,22 solution-processing synthesis23 or a colloidal engineering method.24 The first solar cell device with n = 3 perovskite (C6H5(CH2)2NH3)2(CH3NH3)2Pb3I10 showed a PCE of 4.73% under ambient conditions without encapsulation and the perovskite structure can keep well for 40 days exposed to 52% relative humidity.21 Li et al. adopted the colloidal strategy to prepare a 2D MAPbI3 film and achieved a high PCE of 19.14%.24 Tsai et al. synthesized a Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) type n = 4 (BA)2(MA)3Pb4I13 perovskite with an increased PCE of 12.5%.25 Liao et al. found that unencapsulated 2D Sn-based perovskite solar cells showed no appreciable decay in efficiency (âˆ¼5.94%) over 100 hours.19 Quite recently, 2D RP-type lead-free and highly stable Cu- and Ge-based perovskites, i.e., (C6H5CH2NH3)2CuBr426 and (C6H5C2H4NH3)2GeI4,27 have also been successfully synthesized in experiments. The layer thickness provides a direct way to optimize the performance of perovskite absorbers. Large n means better sunlight absorption and higher carrier mobility, while small n provides better moisture resistance and higher quality films. Moreover, 2D perovskites are also promising in solid-state-lighting fields, such as light-emitting diode (LED) devices, due to their excellent light absorption and emission and widely-tunable bandgaps. LEDs based on 2D halide perovskites have been fabricated recently with energy conversion efficiency âˆ¼7%.28

Although lots of advantages exist in 2D perovskites as solar cell absorber materials, there is still an urgent issue to be solved, i.e., the toxicity of Pb. In order to remove the toxic element, researchers have proposed to replace Pb2+ by the same family elements, such as Sn2+ and Ge2+. The strategy of using Sn and Ge has so far been unsuccessful due to the tendency of Sn and Ge to oxidize in the +4 state rather than the desired 2+ state, which directly leads to the unstable structure of Sn- and Ge-based perovskites, resulting in relatively low device performance and reproducibility.29,30 Quite recently, four independent groups have reported simultaneously the successful replacement of Pb via heterovalent substitution with Bi and Ag,31â€“35 so as to form a halide double perovskite. The computational design of a new halide double perovskite family with bulk phase Cs2M+M3+X6 (M+ = In+, Cu+, Ag+, Au+; M3+ = Sb3+, Bi3+ and X = Cl, Br, I) has been proposed.31,34 Zhao et al. have confirmed the good phase and structural stability against decomposition of these perovskite materials through theoretical calculations. The bandgaps, optical properties and carrier effective masses can also be highly tunable according to the diversified combination of M+, M3+ and X.31 Recently, three members of the lead-free double perovskites, i.e., Cs2AgBiCl6, Cs2AgBiBr6 and Cs2AgInCl6, have been successfully synthesized experimentally.33â€“35 The devices based on Cs2AgBiBr6 film present a PCE of 1.44% and have high stability under ambient air and light without encapsulation.36 Besides the fully-inorganic double perovskites, the hybrid organicâ€“inorganic halide double perovskite (CH3NH3)2BiKCl6 was also synthesized,37 which further expands the potential for material design of lead-free double perovskites. It is interesting to note that all theoretical and experimental investigations are focused on bulk perovskites, while 2D double perovskites are still absent up to now.

In this work, we adopt first-principles calculations within density functional theory (DFT) and advanced many-body perturbation theory to explore the electronic, optical and transport properties of 2D layered RP-type lead-free halide double perovskites Csn+1Mn/2+Mn/23+X3n+1 (M+: In+, Cu+, Ag+, Au+; M3+: Bi3+, Sb3+; X: Cl, Br and I) and their heterostructures for the first time. Surprisingly, we find that the layered Csn+1Inn/2Sbn/2I3n+1 perovskites are direct bandgap semiconductors with bandgaps of 1.55, 1.37 and 1.29 eV for n = 1â€“3, which are very suitable for visible-light absorption and smaller than the bandgap of MAPbI3 (âˆ¼1.50â€“1.61 eV).2,4,38 The type-II band alignment of the Cs4In3/2Sb3/2Cl10/Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4 heterostructure, with a direct bandgap of 1.65 eV, leads to the significant spatial separation of photo-generated carriers, which results in a low carrier recombination rate. The energy gap can be further reduced by increasing the layer thickness. Both CsInSbI and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl have an ultrahigh absorption coefficient (âˆ¼105 cmâˆ’1), as high as MAPbI3. The exciton binding energy of âˆ¼0.30 eV is suitable and favourable for LED devices and can be greatly reduced to several meV by a high-k dielectric transport layer in solar cell devices. Both the electron and hole mobility are three/four orders of magnitude larger than MAPbI3 up to 102â€“103 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 for all layered and heterostructure perovskites. Our results of 2D RP-type lead-free halide double perovskites offer useful guidance for selectively utilizing these stable and environmentally friendly halide perovskites as promising solar absorbers and solid-state-lighting materials. The layered CsInSbI and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl heterostructures are thus favourable optoelectronic candidates.

Computational methods

DFT and GW calculations

Our first-principles calculations are carried out within the framework of DFT using the projector augmented wave (PAW)39 method as implemented in the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) code.40 The generalized gradient approximation (GGA) formulated by Perdewâ€“Burkeâ€“Ernzerhof (PBE)41 is used as the exchange correlation functional. An energy cutoff of 500 eV is set for the plane-wave expansion. All the cubic double perovskites are relaxed by total energy minimization by the conjugated gradient algorithm and the convergence tolerances of energy and residual atomic forces are smaller than 10âˆ’5 eV and 10âˆ’3 eV Ã…âˆ’1. The k-point mesh is sampled by 13 Ã— 13 Ã— 1 for structure optimization and 15 Ã— 15 Ã— 1 for self-consistent calculation in the first Brillouin zone. The vacuum spacing of 20 Ã… is chosen along the c-axis to avoid spurious interaction between the periodically repeated units. Considering the severe underestimation of semiconductor bandgaps in common DFT-PBE calculations, we adopted the GGA-1/2 scheme42,43 to revise the bandgap to an accurate value compared with experiments and time-consuming GW calculations with a comparable computational cost to the ordinary GGA-PBE calculations. Moreover, we used the BerkeleyGW package44 to perform the GW calculations of electronic structure and to solve the Betheâ€“Salpeter equation (BSE) for the absorption spectrum. For the quasiparticle calculations, the ground-state Kohnâ€“Sham wave functions and eigenvalues are obtained using the Troullierâ€“Martins norm-conserving pseudopotenetials,45,46 as implemented in QUANTUM ESPRESSO code.47 The kinetic energy cutoff of 60 Ry is used for the wave function and a 13 Ã— 13 Ã— 1 k-point mesh is chosen during the GW calculations. To perform the BSE calculations, we interpolate the electronâ€“hole interaction kernel on a 21 Ã— 21 Ã— 1 k-point mesh. This choice of parameters yields well-converged exciton eigenenergies and sufficient accuracy in our calculations.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure and stability of 2D RP-type lead-free halide double perovskites

In order to completely eliminate the toxicity from the Pb element, Zhao et al. proposed the transmutation of two divalent Pb2+ ions into a monovalent metal ion M+ and a trivalent metal ion M3+, i.e., 2Pb2+ â†’ M+ + M3+, to build bulk lead-free double perovskites with a chemical formula of A2M+M3+X6.31,60 It forms alternative M+X3 and M3+X3 octahedrons to keep the total number of valence electrons unchanged from the 2Pb2+X3 octahedron. Here, we consider the 2D layered RP-type lead-free halide double perovskites, composed by different layer thicknesses of octahedrons, as shown in Fig. 1. In order to maintain the structural stability of the octahedron, the available choices of M+ and M3+ are size-suitable elements, which have a similar ionic radius to Pb2+. Compatible with lead, suitable M+ can be chosen as In+, Ag+, Cu+, Au+, and TI+ and M3+ can be Bi3+ and Sb3+. Considering the severer toxicity of TI+ than Pb2+, we have removed TI+ directly without further consideration. The large radius inorganic cation Cs+ can be adopted at the A site to stabilize the perovskite structure. Based on this transmutation, we construct and optimize 24 kinds of n = 1 Cs2M1/2+M1/23+X4 perovskites. Their fully-relaxed lattice parameters are summarized in Table S3 (ESIâ€ ).

It is well-known that indirect bandgap semiconductors have a low light absorption efficiency and thus a thick layer is needed to absorb sufficient solar energy for photovoltaic applications. On the contrary, direct bandgap semiconductors, although with good optical absorption, have a disadvantage of their fast carrier recombination rate and thus short carrier lifetime. Moreover, we know from the above discussions that Cl/Br-based perovskites have a larger bandgap and more stable structure than I-based ones. In order to balance the benefits of direct and indirect bandgap semiconductors and broaden the choice of Cl/Br-based double perovskites, we further construct 7 heterostructures by combining several different perovskite layers based on the principles of lattice match, direct bandgap and type-II band alignment, in which the different 2D perovskite layers are directly stacked together with a shift of b/2 along the b-axis just as the bulk RP-type perovskite (see Fig. S4, ESIâ€ ). Optical absorption and excitonic effect

In order to investigate the important interband optical transition and excitonic effect, in Fig. 6 and Fig. S6 (ESIâ€ ), we present the imaginary part Îµ2 of the dielectric function based on the powerful and time-consuming GW-BSE calculations. As shown in Fig. 6a and b, the GW electronic (optical) bandgap is 1.29 (1.01) and 1.65 (1.31) eV for freestanding Cs4In3/2Sb3/2I10 and Cs4In3/2Sb3/2Cl10/Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4, in which the exciton binding energies, given by the difference between the electronic and optical bandgaps, are found to be 0.28 and 0.34 eV, respectively. The values of exciton binding energy in these 2D lead-free halide double perovskites are much larger than that in their bulk phase (âˆ¼2â€“20 meV for MAPbI3).9 This is because of the strong 2D quantum confinement and the incomplete dielectric screening due to their ultrathin thickness. The exciton binding energies are comparable with those in other familiar 2D semiconductor materials, such as phosphorene (0.8â€“0.9 eV70,71), MoS2 (âˆ¼1.0 eV72,73) and graphdiyne (âˆ¼0.55 eV74). Moreover, we can see from Fig. S6 (ESIâ€ ) that the exciton binding energy of the heterostructure is much smaller than the isolated Cs2In1/2Sb1/2Cl4 (0.94 eV) and Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4 (1.02 eV) due to increasing of the thickness.

It is worthwhile to note that, compared with the 3D counterparts, the relatively large exciton binding energy in these 2D perovskites is just derived in the freestanding case with the vacuum dielectric environment (Îµr = 1). However, considering the actual structure of a solar cell device, shown in Fig. 6c, we note that a high-k electron transport layer is usually used. For example, the widely-used electron transport layer TiO2 has a large dielectric constant of about 75â€“80.75,76 Furthermore, the electrode is always a metal, like Au. Both the high-k transport layer and metal electrode will significantly enhance the environmental dielectric constant and dielectric screening effect. Hence, the exciton binding energy of 2D perovskite-based photovoltaic devices is still small enough (âˆ¼several meV) and the photo-generated carriers can be separated easily. Both electrons and holes can thus be regarded as free carriers in 2D perovskite-based photovoltaic devices. On the contrary, the large exciton binding energy (âˆ¼0.3 eV) in freestanding 2D perovskites is also extremely useful for solid-state-lighting devices as shown in Fig. 6d, which can enhance the PL efficiency due to their large exciton binding energy. Moreover, the carrier spatial separation in all heterostructures with type-II band alignment is also beneficial to reduce the photo-generated carrier recombination in photovoltaic devices. The good optical properties greatly broaden the potential of 2D lead-free halide double perovskites both in solar cell and LED devices.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we have systematically investigated the electronic, optical and transport properties of 24 RP-type 2D lead-free halide double perovskites Csn+1Mn/2+Mn/23+X3n+1 (M+: In+, Cu+, Ag+, Au+; M3+: Bi3+, Sb3+) and their 7 heterostructures based on powerful first-principles and advanced GW-BSE calculations for the first time. Through comprehensive comparison and analysis, we find that 2D Csn+1Inn/2Sbn/2I3n+1 is a promising material with a direct bandgap of 1.55, 1.37 and 1.29 eV for n = 1 to 3 and has a large optical absorption coefficient âˆ¼105 cmâˆ’1. The energy bandgap and optical absorption are quite suitable for solar cell absorbers and comparable with the widely-used MAPbI3. The Cs2In1/2Sb1/2Cl4/Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4 heterostructure with type-II band alignment has a direct bandgap of 2.45 eV with spatial separation of photo-generated carriers, which directly leads to a low carrier recombination rate for photovoltaic devices. Moreover, the energy gap can be further reduced by increasing the layer thickness, for example, 1.65 eV for the Cs4In3/2Sb3/2Cl10/Cs2Cu1/2Bi1/2Cl4 heterostructure. The exciton binding energy âˆ¼0.30 eV in Cs4In3/2Sb3/2I10 is beneficial to enhance PL quantum efficiency for solid-state-lighting devices. On the contrary, the exciton binding energy will be significantly reduced to several meV by the high-k dielectric transport layer and metal-electrode for solar cell devices. The absorption coefficient (âˆ¼105 cmâˆ’1) of heterostructures is obviously larger than that of the isolated building-blocks. Surprisingly, the carrier mobilities of 2D CsInSbI and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl are three/four orders of magnitude larger than those of MAPbI3 up to 102â€“103 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. Our calculations for 2D halide double perovskites offer useful guidance for designing Pb-free halide perovskites as solar cell absorbers and suggest that the layered CsInSbI and CsInSbCl/CsCuBiCl-based heterostructures are promising materials with great potential as the best candidate among 2D halide double perovskites for future solar cell and solid-state-lighting device applications.
