Comparative study of light- and thermal-induced degradation for both fullerene and non-fullerene-based organic solar cells†

Leiping Duan *, Haimang Yi , Yu Zhang , Faiazul Haque , Cheng Xu and Ashraf Uddin *
School of Photovoltaic and Renewable Energy Engineering, University of New South Wales, Sydney, NSW 2052, Australia. E-mail: Leiping.duan@unsw.edu.au; a.uddin@unsw.edu.au

Received 21st November 2018 , Accepted 8th January 2019

First published on 9th January 2019

Abstract

Solution-processed organic solar cells (OSCs) have become a promising renewable energy technology in recent years. The fast initial degradation, which causes performance reduction at the initial stage for OSCs, needs to be understood and investigated thoroughly. In this work, for the first time, we have conducted a comparative study of light- and thermal-induced degradation during the burn-in process. As revealed in the estimation of Urbach energy, both light and thermal degradation created an increased energetic disorder. However, characterisation by steady-state photoluminescence (PL), time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) and capacitance–voltage (C–V) indicates that the mechanisms behind these two types of degradation are very different. The loss in light degradation was found to be more related to its degraded energy transfer ability, exciton dissociation ability and increased non-radiative recombination; whereas, the loss in thermal degradation was found to be more related to the reduced current extraction ability and increased leakage current.

1. Introduction

Bulk heterojunction (BHJ) organic solar cells (OSCs), possessing the advantages of simple and cheap fabrication, light-weight, semi-transparency and flexibility, have attracted significant research attention over the past few years.1–4 Recently, non-fullerene acceptor-based organic solar cells have become a research hot spot.5 Compared to conventional fullerene acceptors, non-fullerene acceptors have advantages such as stronger light absorbing ability, tunable energy levels and variable molecular structures.6–8 Poly[4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophen-2-yl)benzo[1,2-b;4,5-b′]dithiophene-2,6-diyl-alt-(4-(2-ethylhexyl)-3-fluorothieno[3,4-b]thiophene-)-2-carboxylate-2-6-diyl] (PTB7-Th) is a widely applied low bandgap polymer donor material that has demonstrated good compatibility with both classic fullerene and non-fullerene acceptor materials, namely, [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM) and 3,9-bis(2-methylene-(3-(1,1-dicyanomethylene)-indanone))-5,5,11,11-tetrakis(4-hexylphenyl)-dithieno[2,3-d:2′,3′-d′]-s-indaceno[1,2b:5,6-b′]dithiophene (ITIC).9 With the development of novel organic materials synthesis and device processing technology, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of OSCs have surpassed 14% for single junction devices and 17% for tandem devices to date.10–14

OSCs emerged as a promising renewable energy technology for commercialisation. Nevertheless, besides reaching the high PCE, stability is a critical barrier to commercialisation. It was found that OSCs usually showed dramatic performance degradation at the initial stage of the device operation.15–18 This rapid reduction is defined as the “fast initial degradation”, which is responsible for a significant loss in OSCs.19 Commonly, a burn-in test is required for most photovoltaic devices to ensure their reliability.20 For future applications, the fast initial degradation of organic solar cells also needs to be well understood and investigated. There are many factors contributing to the degradation of OSCs, including the chemical instability, oxidation, interfacial degradation, thermally induced changes, light-induced instability and so on.21–24 Different factors may contribute to various failure mechanisms. To date, many studies have investigated the mechanism behind the light-induced fast initial degradation, also called burn-in photodegradation.16,18 Upama et al. reported that the formation of sub-bandgap trap states is the main reason for the burn-in photodegradation in PTB7:PC71BM fullerene-based organic solar cells.18 Similarly, the trap-mediated charge recombination was found to be the dominate burn-in photodegradation mechanism in non-fullerene-based PBDB-T:ITIC devices.17 Nevertheless, researchers have rarely studied and performed comparative studies on the fast initial degradation that results from other factors. Many stability studies only investigated a single factor (such as thermal or air) over the long term, and they lacked detailed mechanistic analysis.25–28 As such, more detailed and comparative stability studies are needed in this field. In addition to light-induced degradation, thermal-induced degradation is a non-negligible part of the fast initial degradation. To fully investigate the mechanism behind the fast initial degradation, a comparative analysis of the fast initial photodegradation and fast initial thermal degradation for organic solar cells is inevitable.

Herein, we have evaluated the fast initial photodegradation and fast initial thermal degradation of both PTB7-Th:PC71BM-based fullerene and PTB7-Th:ITIC-based non-fullerene OSCs and compared these two types of degradation. Similar PCE reduction was observed for both photo-degraded devices and thermal-degraded devices. However, photo-degraded devices showed a higher open circuit voltage (Voc) loss, while thermal degraded devices showed a higher short circuit current (Jsc) loss. To investigate the root cause of the difference, characterisations such as external quantum efficiency (EQE), photoluminescence (PL), capacitance–voltage (C–V) and atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements were conducted. We determined the Urbach energy from the tail region of the EQE spectrum and estimated the carrier recombination resistance from the diode ideality factor. As a result, the degradation mechanism was found to be different between the fast initial photo-degradation and thermal degradation. An increase in the Urbach energy was observed for both photo-degraded and thermal-degraded devices to a similar extent. This indicates a larger energetic disorder in the device. The loss in fast initial photodegradation was found to be related to its degraded energy transfer ability, exciton dissociation ability and increased non-radiative recombination. On the other hand, the loss in fast initial thermal degradation was found to be more related to its decreased current extraction ability and increased leakage current.

2. Experimental details

2.1 Material preparation

The pre-patterned ITO glasses with an area of 12 mm × 12 mm were purchased from Lumtec. PTB7-Th and ITIC were purchased from Solarmer. PC71BM was purchased from 1-Material. Zinc oxide nanoparticles, reagent alcohol (anhydrous, <0.003% water), chlorobenzene (99.8%), 1,8-diiodooctane, and MoO3 were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.

2.2 Device fabrication

We used an inverted device structure of ITO Glass/ZnO/PTB7-Th:ITIC or PTB7-Th:PC71BM/MoO3/Ag. The ITO glass substrate was cleaned with soapy deionised (DI) water, pure DI water, acetone and isopropanol and ultrasonicated for 10 min in sequence. The ZnO nanoparticle solution (3%) was made by adding 50 μL of zinc dispersion into 1622 μL of reagent alcohol. The ZnO nanoparticles solution was stirred overnight in the N2 filled glove box and then spin-coated on the top of the cleaned ITO glass with a spin rate of 5000 rpm for 60 s. After coating with the ZnO solution, all the samples were annealed at 120 °C on a hot plate inside the N2 filled glove box for 10 min. The PTB7-Th:PC71BM active layer solutions were made with 10 mg PTB7-Th and 15 mg PC71BM in a 1:1.5 wt ratio in a 25 mg mL−1 chlorobenzene solution with 3 vol% DIO. The PTB7-Th:ITIC active layer solutions were made with 10 mg PTB7-Th, 15 mg ITIC in a 1:1.5 wt ratio in 25 mg mL−1 chlorobenzene without DIO addition. The active layer solution was stirred overnight inside an N2-filled glove-box and then spin-coated at a rate of 1800 rpm for 2 min onto the samples to reach 100 nm thickness. After that, the coated samples were put into a vacuum chamber at a pressure of 10−5 Pa. The 10 nm thick film of MoO3 and 100 nm thick film of silver were deposited on the sample surface through a shadow mask by thermal evaporation. The device area fabricated was 0.045 cm2.

2.3 Device characterisation

Fresh samples were stored in a N2-filled glove box to avoid degradation before characterisation. Photo-degraded samples were exposed to one-sun test conditions under ambient conditions for 5 hours. Thermal-degraded samples were put on an 85 °C hot plate for 5 hours in ambient conditions. The current density–voltage (J–V) measurements were conducted by using a solar cell I–V testing system from PV Measurements, Inc. (using a Keithley 2400 source meter) under illumination power of 100 mW cm−2 by an AM 1.5G solar simulator. The device temperature was measured by a GM1350 50:1 LCD infrared thermometer digital gun and maintained at around 25 °C. An Autolab PGSTAT-30 was used for the capacitance–voltage (C–V) determinations and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). This was conducted inside the glove box with a frequency range of 106 to 100 Hz by using a frequency analyser module. A QEX10 spectral response system from PV measurements was used as a characterisation machine for external quantum efficiency (EQE) measurements. Optical characterisation was conducted using a UV-VIS-NIR spectrometer (Perkin Elmer-Lambda 950). A Bruker Dimension ICON SPM with a scan size of “5 μm × 5 μm” at the scan rate of 0.512 Hz was used as the atomic force microscopy (AFM) to characterise the film surface morphology. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were taken using a Nano SEM 230 system. The micro-photoluminescence (μPL) characterisation was conducted on a self-developed μPL system. The sample was excited by a pulsed OPO laser at 532 nm. For steady state spectral PL, the signal was detected by a Glacier X TE Cooled CCD Spectrometer with a detection range of 200 nm to 1050 nm. For time-resolved PL (TRPL), the signal was detected by an id110 VIS 100 MHz Photon Detector operated in free running mode.

The organic solar cells that were characterized immediately after fabrication are called “fresh” devices. These devices were then divided into two groups for fast initial degradation analysis. To evaluate the fast initial photodegradation in ambient conditions, fresh devices were exposed to continuous one-sun test conditions for 5 hours in ambient conditions to become the so-called “photo-degraded” devices.17 To evaluate the fast initial thermal degradation, fresh devices were put on a hot plate at 85 °C under dark conditions for 5 hours in ambient conditions to become the so-called “thermal-degraded” devices.15 The photovoltaic parameters, including Voc, Jsc, fill factor (FF), PCE, series resistance (Rs) and shunt resistance (Rsh), of those devices were evaluated at a regular interval of 1 hour during the degradation process. From the photodegradation perspective, non-fullerene-based devices showed a typical burn-in degradation, where the PCE showed a significant drop in the first hour followed by a stabilization regime. For fullerene-based devices in the burn-in degradation regime, it was not apparent that the PCE decreased continuously over 5 hours. From the thermal degradation perspective, both fullerene and non-fullerene-based devices showed the burn-in process with a significant PCE drop within the initial 1 hour. On the other hand, the fullerene-based devices showed a PCE recovering trend for the following 4 hours. Similar recovering trends were also observed in the degradation curve of the fill factor, shunt resistance and series resistance for fullerene-based devices; the recovering of these three factors can be the main reason for the PCE recovering trend. Since all devices used the same device structure and the same donor material in this study, the difference in degradation between the fullerene and non-fullerene-based devices mainly results from the degradation in the active layer, which is related to the morphology change and the molecular structure of the acceptor. Compared to fullerene acceptor PC71BM, non-fullerene acceptor ITIC has four 4-hexylphenyl groups that can restrict molecular planarity, aggregation and large phase separation.9 Thermal treatment was proven to cause aggregation and larger domain size, leading to higher carrier mobility for PTB7-Th:PC71BM devices, which may be the root reason for the observed PCE recovery.29

From Fig. 3(a)–(d), it is easy to observe that the degradation of Voc and Jsc is different in the fast initial photodegradation and thermal degradation, where photo-degraded devices have a higher Voc loss and thermal-degraded devices have a higher Jsc loss. From Fig. 3(i)–(l), it can be seen that the degradation of the internal resistance is also different in photodegradation and thermal degradation. After 5 hours of degradation, thermal-degraded devices showed a higher shunt resistance loss while photo-degradation devices showed a higher series resistance increase. Thus, the degradation mechanism of fast initial photodegradation and thermal degradation can be very different.

Dark J–V characterisation was then conducted to further analyze the current loss in both fast initial photo- and thermal-degradation. The obtained dark J–V curves are shown in Fig. 5(a) and (b) for fullerene and non-fullerene-based devices, respectively. It was clear that the leakage current of the photo-degraded and thermal-degraded devices increased for both types of devices under the reverse bias conditions as compared to fresh devices. Moreover, the thermal-degraded devices showed a higher increase than the photo-degraded devices. The high leakage current usually indicates a high shunt possibility, which is responsible for the low Jsc in the device, and a low shunt resistance is responsible for the low FF in OSCs.38 Herein, the observation from the leakage current explained the higher Jsc loss in thermal-degraded devices and is consistent with the observed higher shunt resistance loss from J–V characterisation. The results from dark J–V measurements proved that the fast initial thermal degradation is responsible for the increased shunt resistance and higher shunt probability in OSCs.

On the other hand, a saturation current density (Jsat) with charge carrier extraction possibility (P) analysis was performed for both fullerene and non-fullerene-based fresh, photo-degraded and thermal-degraded devices. The plots of the photocurrent density (Jph) vs. the effective voltage (Veff) are shown in Fig. 6. The Jph and Veff were calculated by using the equations below:39

 
Jph = JL − JD(2)
 
Veff = Voc − Vbias(3)
where JL is the light current density under one-sun testing conditions, JD is the dark current density under the dark testing conditions, and Vbias is the externally applied voltage bias. When the Veff is higher than 1.0 V, the assumption that all the generated excitons in the active layer are dissociated and collected can be made. In the meantime, the photocurrent density (Jph) can be assumed to be equal to the saturation current density (Jsat), which is only restricted by the absorption of incident photons. The charge carrier extraction possibility (P) was then obtained by calculating the ratio of Jsc to Jsat, which can reasonably explain the current loss during the current extraction process.40Table 3 presents the Jsat and estimated P value for all devices. It was found that for both fullerene and non-fullerene based devices, the thermal-degraded devices showed a higher reduction in Jsat and P values than the photo-degraded devices when compared to fresh devices, which revealed that fast initial thermal degradation had a more negative effect on the current extraction process in OSCs as compared to fast initial photodegradation. The charge extraction process in OSCs is commonly related to the charge collection at the interface between charge transport layers and active layers.41,42 The low charge collection at the interface usually results from the poor surface contact with the shunt conditions and indicates low shunt resistance.43 Thus, the results from the Jph–P analysis were also found to be consistent with the observed higher shunt resistance loss in J–V characterisation.

Steady-state photoluminescence (PL) was performed for all devices in order to gain deeper insights into the degradation mechanism. The normalized PL spectra are shown in Fig. 8. It is obvious that for both fullerene and non-fullerene-based devices, the photo-degraded devices showed a dramatically increased PL intensity, while the PL intensity of the thermal-degraded devices is almost unchanged when compared to fresh devices. The PL intensity from the steady-state PL is usually related to the number of excitons in organic solar cells. The high PL intensity indicates that a large number of excitons exist in the active layer.47,48 The low PL intensity indicates that the excitons are efficiently dissociated into charge carriers.49,50 Thus, a significantly increased PL intensity implies a largely decreased exciton dissociation ability for photo-degraded devices, which is considered one of the main reasons for the fast initial photodegradation. Herein, the observation is consistent with the results obtained from C–V characterisation. On the other hand, time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) was conducted to investigate the dynamics of fast initial photodegradation and thermal degradation for the fresh, photo-degraded and thermal-degraded fullerene and non-fullerene-based devices. Nevertheless, both fullerene and non-fullerene-based photo-degraded devices showed a much larger increase in fast and long excited lifetimes than thermal-degraded devices. Excited lifetimes can indicate the energy and carrier transfer process inside the active layer, which corresponded with the potential of the heterojunction.52 Since the Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET) offers an alternative non-radiative pathway between the donor and acceptor, the excited state lifetime can be used to evaluate the energy transfer process in the active layer.53 The higher excited state lifetime usually represents a more inefficient energy transfer. Herein, fast initial photodegradation was found to have a larger negative impact on the energy transfer process as compared to fast initial thermal degradation. This is in accord with the higher Voc loss observed in photo-degraded devices. The degraded energy transfer ability was identified as one of the main reasons for the fast initial photodegradation but is less related to the fast initial thermal degradation.

AFM and SEM characterisations were conducted to investigate the morphology changes during the fast initial degradation. AFM images are shown in Fig. 11 and SEM images are shown in Fig. S1† for both fullerene and non-fullerene-based fresh, photo-degraded and thermal-degraded devices. In addition, the estimated root mean square roughness (RMS) values are shown in each corresponding image. It was found that the RMS value for both photo-degraded and thermal-degraded devices was larger as compared to the value of the fresh device. For fullerene-based devices, the RMS value increased from 3.65 nm to 5.35 nm and 5.00 nm after 5 hours of the fast initial photo- and thermal-degradation, respectively. Similarly, for non-fullerene-based devices, the RMS value increased from 4.24 nm to 4.98 nm and 4.34 nm after the fast initial photo- and thermal-degradation. Apparently, the fast initial photodegradation was found to induce a greater extent of roughness than the fast initial thermal degradation. The high RMS value with rougher surface morphology implies strong aggregation behaviour inside the active layer, which is related to large phase-separated domains with more traps. It can negatively affect the charge dissociation process and increase the non-radiative recombination in the device.60,61 Hence, the decreased charge dissociation and increased non-radiative recombination were found as two major degradation reasons for the fast initial photodegradation. Herein, the result is consistent with the steady-state PL results, the diode ideality factor analysis and carrier recombination resistance analysis.

Overall, although a similar device PCE drop was observed from the fast initial photo- and thermal degradation for both types of devices, the mechanism behind these two types of degradation was found to be different. Urbach energy estimation pointed out that both photo- and thermal degradation can cause an increased energetic disorder with higher trap-mediated charge recombination. Meanwhile, steady-state PL, TRPL and C–V measurements indicate that the fast initial photodegradation is more dominated by degraded charge dissociation ability and energy transfer ability as compared to fast initial thermal degradation. Moreover, carrier recombination resistance estimation and AFM characterisation found that photodegradation caused a relatively stronger negative impact on the non-radiative recombination in the fast initial process. However, the dark J–V measurement and the Jsat with P analysis showed that the fast initial thermal degradation is more related to the increased leakage potential and degraded current extraction process as compared to fast initial photodegradation.

4. Conclusion

We have studied and compared the fast initial photodegradation and fast initial thermal degradation of both PTB7-Th:PC71BM fullerene-based and PTB7-Th:ITIC non-fullerene-based devices. The determined Urbach energy indicates that both photo- and thermal degradation during the fast initial process can cause energy disorder. However, the fast initial photo and thermal degradation were identified to have very different mechanisms. Compared to the fast initial thermal degradation, the fast initial photodegradation was found to have more negative effects on the energy transfer process and exciton dissociation process and caused a higher increase in non-radiative recombination. On the other hand, the fast initial thermal degradation was found to be more responsible for the degradation in the current extraction process and can induce a higher current leakage potential. As the organic solar cell emerges as one of the promising energy technologies, it becomes essential to address its stability issues. Understanding the mechanism behind the fast initial degradation process is thus a crucial step towards further development and commercialization.
