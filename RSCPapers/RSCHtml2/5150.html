Abstract

Optical nonlinearity of two-dimensional (2D) materials with a permanent dipole is a subject of intense research in the fields ranging from all-optical signal processing to laser nanotechnology due to the potential outstanding performance of 2D materials in nonlinear optical processes, and the feasibility for extensive integration into optoelectronic devices. In the present work, the group-VA binary monolayers are predicted to have giant and broadband second harmonic generation (SHG) properties superior to some of the most popular NLO materials by first-principles calculations. The SHG susceptibility of the Î±-PAs reaches up to 8.0 Ã— 106 pm2 Vâˆ’1, which is nearly 1â€“2 orders of magnitude higher than that of honeycomb monolayer MoS2 and h-BN, and twice larger than the record-high SHG susceptibility of a 2D ferroelectric GeSe monolayer. This intriguing peak is formed by intraband two photon transition ascribed to the relatively small bandgap and large transition matrix elements between valence and conduction band edges. A high-performance nonlinear optical device can be developed using those 2D group-VA monolayer materials with puckled structure and in-plane polarizabilities for multifunctional and integrated optoelectronic applications.

1 Introduction

There continues to be considerable interest in materials with novel nonlinear optical (NLO) properties due to their important applications in optoelectronic technologies, such as optical parametric oscillators, difference frequency generation, laser frequency conversion, and signal communication.1,2 Nonlinear optical properties are highly dependent on the symmetry of materials. In the past few years, a renewed research interest in NLO materials from organic molecules to inorganic NLO crystals with broken symmetry has been ignited in both experiment and theory.3,4 Recently, non-centrosymmetric 2D materials have emerged as an effective candidate with intriguing NLO effects and numerous applications. For example, h-BN5,6 and MoS2 monolayers with broken in-plane inversion symmetry were reported to have a remarkable second harmonic generation (SHG) with potential applications in nonlinear optical devices. van der Waals heterostructure,4,7 2D alloy compounds,8,9 and Janus 2D materials with out-of-plane mirror asymmetry10â€“12 guarantee the generation of even-order optical nonlinearities in their bulk or multilayer structures. Especially, ferroelectric (FE) 2D materials such as group IV monochalcogenides have good NLO properties which motivate us to explore these 2D ferroelectric materials as NLO materials.

2D group-VA materials and 2D group-IVA monochalcogenides are isoelectronic materials with similar puckled structures.13 Black phosphorus has been explored widely as a layered semiconductor material with in-plane anisotropy, tunable bandgap, the high carrier mobility at room temperature and strong infrared responsivity which is promising for high-performance optoelectronic applications.14 Recently, its highly anisotropic and layer dependent third order nonlinear optical effects were also reported.15 However, two critical shortcomings limit its application as a second order NLO material. First, chemical decomposition influences the lifetime of optoelectronic devices, especially under strong light irradiation.16â€“18 Second, the centrosymmetric D2h point group of the crystal structure eliminates the even order NLO responses. Group-VA binary materials provide the solutions to these two problems. In group-VA binary materials, the interplay between the crystal symmetry and spinâ€“orbit coupling may lead to multiple applications including optoelectronics,19â€“24 magnetics25 and solar cells.26 For example, black PAs monolayer (Î±-PAs) has great prospects for photovoltaic devices because of the favorable direct bandgap, extremely high charge mobility of nearly 14000 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, and 22.1% power conversion efficiency.26â€“28 The thin film of Î±-PAs has been fabricated with molecule beam deposition29 or exfoliation30 with high stability.31 The Î±-NP has high stability31 and can sustain under 1500 K for several picoseconds as verified with ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulation.32

Earlier theoretical works33â€“35 revealed that the monolayer structures with puckled shape often possess a large SHG response.36,37 The two-band tight binding model interpreted the giant second harmonic generation and shift current of group-IVA monochalcogenides.38,39 The upper limit of shift current conductivity and SHG susceptibility were predicted through the Riceâ€“Mele model.40 This limit can also be understood in the extended Kuzyk model41 based on the Wannier interpolation.42 In the framework of tight binding models, the underlying correlation mechanisms between second order nonlinear optical phenomena and in-plane polarization are determined by the puckled structure and polar covalent bonds in the armchair direction.36 There is intriguing dependency between in-plane dipole and the second order nonlinear optical susceptibility.38,42 Thus, it is desirable to study the nonlinear optical properties of Î±-phase 2D group-VA binary materials.

In this work, the SHG properties of a puckled Î±-PAs monolayer and Î±-NP monolayer are explored with first principles calculations. The SHG responses of monolayer Î±-PAs reach nearly twice the largest value of group-IVA monochalcogenides. The anisotropic behaviors of NLO properties are studied through the angle-resolved diagram of the SHG responses in different incident and polarization angles. The critical role of bandgap is identified through analyzing the contributions of different transition components and processes. The permanent dipole plays an important role on the SHG behaviors of Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers. Both bandgap and permanent dipole can be modified by structural asymmetry and the polarity of bonds between different elements.

Due to the different electronegativities, phosphorus is the electron donor in the Î±-NP monolayer, and it is the electron acceptor in the Î±-PAs monolayer. The stronger polarity of the Pâ€“N bond than the Pâ€“As bond leads to an indirect bandgap and the absence of parabolic band nature of band dispersion along the Î“â€“Y direction in the Î±-NP monolayer (Fig. 2b). The asymmetric wavefunction of the VBM and CBM along the z axis is observed in these two monolayers, especially with larger distortion in the Î±-NP monolayer. In puckled structures, the dipole moment in each unit cell is along the same direction, thus forming a macroscopic permanent dipole in these 2D monolayers. Two effects of dipole moment on the electronic properties of the Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers can be observed. First, as shown in Fig. 2, the Î±-NP monolayer has large dipole moment, 0.0244 cmâˆ’2, which is 1.62 times as large as that of the Î±-PAs monolayer, 0.0150 cmâˆ’2. This leads to the large modification of band dispersion of the Î±-NP monolayer in contrast to that of black phosphorus. Second, in the Î±-NP monolayer, the local transition from the top valence band to the lowest conduction band is more likely to occur, because these bands consist of orbitals from the same atoms. Those features have significant effect on SHG susceptibilities.

GW-BSE calculations of the imaginary part of the dielectric function are carried out for the Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers. The quasiparticle shift, which is taken as the difference between the energy of the most stable exciton and the electronic bandgap, is introduced in the GW-BSE calculations. Like that of phosphorene, the linear dielectric responses of the Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers exhibit an anisotropic feature: the onset of the Î±-PAs monolayer along the armchair direction (y) is below 1.14 eV, corresponding to the first exciton state composed of electrons and holes at the Î“ point. The absorption onset along the zigzag direction (x) appears at 1.85 eV, composed of the first exciton state dominated by the transitions at the point (1/6, 0, 0) in the first Brillouin zone. For the Î±-NP monolayer, the onsets of the first peak are 2.7 eV and 3.49 eV for Îµyy and Îµxx respectively. Their intensities are significantly stronger than the other transitions, showing the highly localized character of the exciton state. The linear responses amended by the scissor operator have similar features to that obtained from GW-BSE calculations, as shown in Fig. 3. Though there still exists controversy about the role of exciton in the prediction of the second-order nonlinear optical properties,46â€“49 it is valuable to explore the mechanism of the nonlinear optical response by the prediction based on independent particle assumption, because the role of intrinsic dipole can be certainly observed.

3.3 Discussion

First, we focused on the intriguing in-plane component Ï‡(2)yyy of the Î±-PAs monolayer. To better understand the SHG susceptibility, the imaginary part of the double-frequency linear optical absorption spectra (Im[Îµyy(2Ï‰)]) is predicted (Fig. 6a). This is to approximate the two-photon processes with identical energy related to the double-photon resonance. The profile of the double resonant spectra resembles that of the SHG spectra. It illustrates that, at a low energy region, the characteristic peak of Ï‡(2)yyy comes from the double photon resonance. This agrees with the trend in the real and imaginary part of the SHG response, which are shown in Fig. 6c and e respectively.59,60 The contribution from single-photon transition and double-photon transition in the opposite direction largely diminishes the response at the photon energy of 3.5 eV. Explicitly, the marked A, B, C, and D peaks in the spectrum (Fig. 4b) are generated from transitions shown with corresponding shadowed rectangles in Fig. 2a. The peak A corresponds to the resonant energy at the Î“ point in the band structure. The peak B at 1.06 eV originates from the double photon transition from the highest valence band to the second lowest conduction band with 1.96 eV transition energy at Î“ point. The peak C at 1.28 eV arises from the double photon transitions at the Î“ point from the third highest valence band to the lowest conduction band with a 2.39 eV transition energy. The peak D at 1.39 eV originates from the double photon transitions at the band edge of the Y point with a 2.61 eV transition energy.

The SHG tensor Ï‡(2)yyy of both the Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers can also be decomposed into the interband term (eqn (1)) and intraband term (Fig. 7). The intraband term consists of the pure intraband term (eqn (2)) and band modification term due to intraband transitions (eqn (3)). Both the Î±-PAs and Î±-NP monolayers show the same behavior. In the low energy range, the intraband term dominates the in-plane SHG responses, and this indicates that the group velocity difference term Î”mn of the band structure dominates the SHG response of these materials. In the high energy range above the bandgap, there are comparable but opposite contributions from the intraband term and interband term.60 The role of the interband term in the Î±-NP monolayer is more important than it is in the Î±-PAs monolayer. These differences between the Î±-NP monolayer and the Î±-PAs monolayer come from two aspects. First, the expansion of bandgap of the Î±-NP monolayer reduces the overall SHG response. This semi-Dirac cone leads to an opposite group velocity distribution on both sides. As a result, the region with large velocity matrix is established. With the narrowing of the bandgap, it enables the contribution of Tabc to the SHG susceptibilities. Atomic orbital decomposition reveals that py orbitals dominate this semi-Dirac zone with significant contribution to the enhancement of Ï‡(2)yyy, indicating the important role of the permanent dipole along the y direction. For the Î±-NP monolayer, there is a similar large contribution along the Î“â€“X direction due to the parabolic band dispersion at the CBM near the X point. That additional contribution leads to the enhancement of the SHG response along the x polarization direction (Fig. 4fâ€“h and kâ€“m). The permanent dipole leads to the generation of second order nonlinear optical response along the armchair direction. It is valuable to note that although the magnitude is small, the Î±-NP monolayer has more nonzero SHG responses related to the out-of-plane direction, which can utilize incident light in various directions in practical applications.

4 Conclusions

The SHG properties of group-VA binary materials: the Î±-NP and Î±-PAs monolayers have been investigated systematically with first-principles calculations. The low symmetry in group-VA binary monolayers produce unique NLO properties. The permanent dipole plays an important role in the electron transition process in three aspects: breaking the symmetry, modifying the band dispersion, and altering the optical matrix elements of those materials. The distortion along the armchair direction and the relatively small bandgap grant the Î±-PAs monolayer with record high in-plane SHG susceptibility. Because of the large and highly anisotropic SHG susceptibility in visible wavelengths, the 2D group-VA binary materials with Î±-phase is expected to be star NLO materials and an alternative to ferroelectric group-IVA monochalcogenide. This work provides useful information for developing nonlinear optical materials for information processing and photovoltaic devices with multi-functions and high efficiency.
