Recent advancements in synthesis and property control of graphene quantum dots for biomedical and optoelectronic applications

Abstract

Being a novel and interesting zero-dimensional material of the carbon family, graphene quantum dots (GQDs) have been studied extensively in the last few years due to their exceptional and desirable optical, chemical, physical, electrical and biological characteristics. Such unique size-dependent properties exhibited by them are a reflection of combined structural characteristics of small graphene sheets, furnished with quantum confinement and edge effects, and help them to demonstrate versatile applications in biomedical and optoelectronic domains. In this review, we summarize various top-down and bottom-up synthetic procedures to prepare GQDs with ease. Following this, several strategies adopted by researchers such as heteroatom doping, functionalization and controlling the reaction parameters to regulate their structure and chemical behavior are also discussed. A concise summary of several recent advances in the areas of biomedical (bio-sensing, bio-imaging, drug delivery, anti-bacterial activity and photo-thermal/dynamic therapy) and optoelectronic (light emitting diodes and solar cells) applications of GQDs has also been presented. Finally, a brief discussion on existing challenges and future prospective of this exciting material has also been included.

1. Introduction

Graphene, a sheet made of several sp2–sp2 carbon bonds, has a unique set of properties including high electronic conductivity, high mechanical stiffness, large surface area, high thermal stability and strong elasticity.1–5 Owing to these exceptional properties, application of graphene in several domains has extensively increased in the last decade. However, since graphene is a zero-bandgap material with low solubility in aqueous media, this could be tailored by controlling its size, surface functional groups and also by introducing heteroatoms as defects into the graphene lattice. Therefore, in order to bridge this gap, a new quasi zero-dimensional nanomaterial of the graphene family (i.e. Graphene Quantum Dots, GQDs) has gained more attention in recent years. In comparison to graphene, GQDs exhibit some additional virtues including tunable bandgap, excellent aqueous solubility, good biocompatibility, high fluorescence quantum yield and presence of several active functional groups, thus offering unprecedented opportunities in the field of nanodevices. For instance, owing to their size dependent properties, GQDs can be used in light emitting diodes, molecular switches, single-electron transistors, and photoelectric detector devices.6,7 The multiple carrier collection phenomena of GQDs used in silicon-based solar cells have been proven to enhance the Shockley–Queisser efficiency limit.8 Moreover, the ability to tailor the desired physical and chemical properties of GQDs with ease during the synthetic process makes them more attractive and versatile as compared to other allotropes of carbon such as fullerene, graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) etc. Among the several attractive properties GQDs possess, photoluminescence (PL) has fascinated the researchers worldwide in the last few years. Although GQDs retain several attractive characteristics, their comparatively low quantum yield limits their commercial application in various fields. To address this issue, heteroatom doping has appeared as a more appropriate strategy to tailor the optical and electronic properties of GQDs.9–11 For example, Chen et al. investigated the influence of nitrogen (N) functionalities present in N doped GQDs on the transition of photogenerated electrons and their quantum yield.12

The synthesis of GQDs can be accomplished by various top-down and bottom-up synthetic processes.8,13 GQDs obtained using bottom-up techniques including microwave,14 hydrothermal,15 and pyrolysis16,17 methods have been found to be superior to bare and doped GQDs. As the modification of GQDs plays a key role in altering their properties, several metal and non-metal dopants have been studied earlier by different groups.16,18 Due to toxic effects caused by metal atom dopants, non-metal or heteroatom dopants have been preferred to tailor their physical and chemical properties. Among various heteroatoms including N, boron (B), phosphorus (P) and sulfur (S), N and S dopants have been found to be very advantageous not only to enhance their optical and electrical properties with concomitant benefits for device application but also due to the ease of doping, even at room temperature using special strategies.19 In comparison to the size and shape-dependent optical and other properties of other traditional semiconductor QDs, heteroatom doped GQDs exhibit superior optoelectronic properties mainly as a function of the layer numbers.16,20 Since the desired optimal wavelength light may be harnessed by selective doping of GQDs during synthesis, these doped GQDs with tunable PL have huge potential to be used in bio-imaging and bio-sensing applications. Moreover, the existing functional groups (i.e. carboxyl and hydroxyl groups etc.) on GQD edges grown during synthesis endow them with excellent aqueous solubility and support biological applications via improved cyto-compatibility, stability, etc.21 Additionally, these functionalized edges enable them for the easy external surface modification of required biomolecules including cancer targeting receptors or chemotherapeutic agents for use in targeted drug delivery.22,23 Unlike other core–shell nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems where the covering of the core restricts several binding sites available to conjugate the drugs, GQDs provide a large surface area to volume ratio and free π-orbitals to load a large amount of drug through π-stacking for enhanced drug delivery applications.24 In the last few years, several studies have been reported on novel synthetic strategies of GQDs using widely available precursors with enhanced PL properties. These reports have brought GQDs to the next level in the world of nanoparticle research with enticing advancements. However, several gaps exist ranging from variability issues during large-scale synthesis, accurate layer and size control, structural and electronic properties, and regulation of the ratio of arm-chair to zig-zag state edges to more reproducible in vivo toxicity investigations.

This review fills many such missing gaps systematically in various sections to provide the readers with a comprehensive knowledge about several aspects of doped and undoped GQDs spanning many interesting applications, as shown in Scheme 1. The first section focuses on various latest and classic top-down and bottom-up synthetic procedures to prepare GQDs with ease in keeping size (i.e. number of layers) as the primary control parameter. The next section will concentrate the spotlight on several strategies including heteroatom doping, functionalization and controlling the reaction parameters to regulate the structure and chemical behavior of GQDs so as to enhance their optical, electronic and biological properties. In addition, the versatility and ability of graphene-like structures to accommodate different types of defects in their framework are also highlighted in the same section. Subsequent sections will provide a concise summary of emerging advances in the areas of biomedical and optoelectronic applications of GQDs. Critical insights into the existing challenges and future scope related to GQDs are also discussed in the last section. Hence, this comprehensive review is expected to help readers in quickly understanding the recent advances in the field of GQDs that make these quantum dots indispensable future materials.

2. Synthesis of GQDs

The synthetic procedures of GQDs mainly rely on either break down of large graphite pieces or even graphene sheets (top-down) or fusion of small molecules (bottom-up). Many of the synthetic strategies under these two divisions are simple and inexpensive and could use easily available precursor materials. It has been reported that the properties of GQDs depend on both the synthetic procedure as well as the raw materials used.25,26 The procedures include physical grinding, reductive cutting, oxidative cutting, elevated temperature conditions, solvothermal and hydrothermal processes, pyrolysis and incomplete carbonization. Top-down approaches use inexpensive bulk carbon or graphitic material as a precursor but comparatively take a longer reaction time and have low production yields, whereas bottom-up approaches offer the convenience of doping heteroatoms during preparation and exhibit relatively higher yields.27,28 We describe the research and application progress in these two approaches in forthcoming sub-sections.

2.1 Top-down methods

Top-down methods use bulk precursors including carbon black,29 graphene,30,31 graphite,32 carbon fiber,33 CNTs34,35 and graphene oxide36 to cleave them into nano-size graphitic sheets via physical or chemical oxidation processes using strong acids. For instance, Dong et al. prepared single- and multi-layered GQDs through chemical oxidation of CX-72 carbon black (Fig. 1a).29 Next, Ye et al. reported that it is relatively easier to synthesize GQDs from coal, as it contains weakly connected nano-size graphitic domains which can be easily cleaved via an oxidation process.37 Further, Luo et al. have synthesized white fluorescent GQDs by cleaving graphite through sonication in acidic medium (Fig. 1b).32 Although the precursor used in the above methods is abundant, the removal of strong oxidants used in the synthesis makes them expensive. In continuation, Shin et al. reported an acid-free, eco-friendly ozone oxidant-assisted solvothermal preparation of GQDs by using multiwalled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs), graphite, charcoal and carbon fiber as raw precursors (Fig. 1c).38 Similarly, Liu et al. developed a new acid-free approach to synthesize GQDs with high yield (more than 80 wt%) from coal tar pitch using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) as the only oxidant under mild conditions (Fig. 1d).39 Further, Zhu et al. also synthesized GQDs by cutting graphene oxide through acid-free hydroxyl radical oxidants attained by decomposition of H2O2 (Fig. 1e).40 On the other hand, Arundithi et al. have reported electrochemically exfoliated GQDs (Fig. 1f) using graphene sheets (grown by chemical vapor deposition (CVD)) as a raw material and claimed that this method is advantageous to produce size and shape controlled GQDs.31 Subsequently, many efforts have been made by different groups to prepare size and shape controlled GQDs with different precursors. For example, Zhou et al. introduced the photo-Fenton reaction of GO under UV radiation for the mass production of carboxylic group enriched GQDs by simply controlling the Fenton reaction parameters.41 The DNA cleavage experiment of as-prepared GQDs revealed their promising potential to be used in several biological and medical applications. Next, Lin and Zhang developed a potent strategy to disintegrate and exfoliate MWCNTs and graphite flakes for the preparation of highly fluorescent GQDs with an average diameter of ∼20 nm and desirably high quantum yield.42 Parallelly, Yan et al. synthesized uniform-sized black GQDs through a solution-processed synthetic method and used them for solar cells.43 In line with this, the same group used an oxidative condensation reaction introduced by Mullen and colleagues along with their own solubilization strategy for the preparation of stable colloidal GQDs with tunable size (1–3 nm).44 Apart from these, Shinde and Pillai synthesized size controlled GQDs via electrochemical oxidation of MWCNTs by using LiClO4 in propylene carbonate.45 Hung et al. prepared 3 to 8 nm size GQDs from graphene paper by using a weak electrolyte-based electrochemical approach (Fig. 1g).46 However, CNTs offer high efficiency and yield despite high cost. Parallelly, Zhang et al. used graphite rods (relatively low-cost precursors) as an alternative to synthesize highly fluorescent size controlled GQDs via an electrochemical approach.47

Similar to oxidative cleavage of carbonaceous materials, reductive agents have also been used to cut the C–O bond in epoxy groups of oxidized carbon precursors. For example, Gone and Giri reported the formation mechanism of GQDs synthesized using oxidized graphene oxide as a precursor via a hydrothermal process.49 Next, Tayyebi et al. prepared tunable-sized and water-dispersible GQDs by reducing and fragmenting graphene oxide through supercritical water.50 Further, Tetsuka et al. prepared bright color fluorescent amino-functionalized GQDs from oxidized graphene sheets with tunable optical properties.51 Similarly, many efforts have been devoted by different groups but various constraints, such as low production yields, high-cost equipment and precursors, complicated synthetic protocols, extreme conditions and high cost, preclude their large-scale industrial applications. In this regard, Santiago et al. introduced pulsed laser ablation (PLA) to successfully prepare a series of GQDs from carboxyl-functionalized MWCNTs by controlling the PLA time (Fig. 1h).48

2.2 Bottom-up methods

The bottom-up approaches refer to gradual enlargement or fusion of small carbon containing molecules (preferably aromatic) or atoms including citric acid, aspartic acid, glucose, folic acid, etc. These methods mainly include hydrothermal processes, cage opening, thermal pyrolysis, incomplete carbonization and microwave techniques. For instance, Dong et al. have synthesized photoluminescent GQDs by tuning the carbonization degree of citric acid (Fig. 2a).17 Lu et al. reported the transformation of C60 (raw precursor) into fluorescent GQDs through a ruthenium-catalyzed cage-opening technique.52 Chua et al. showed that size controlled GQDs can be synthesized through the treatment of C60 with a mixture of oxidants and acids.53 The authors further reported that tunable optical properties can be achieved by changing the oxidants or acids. Zhang et al. synthesized blue fluorescent GQDs through one-step pyrolysis of an aspartic acid and NH4HCO3 mixture and employed them for bio-imaging and ion sensing applications.54 Qu et al. prepared N-GQDs and S, N co-doped GQDs via a hydrothermal route using citric acid and urea (or thiourea) as precursors.55 The as-prepared GQDs exhibited excellent photocatalytic activity and multi-color emission (Fig. 2b). Jeon et al. reported a new approach for the synthesis of N-GQDs and N, S co-doped GQDs through a solvothermal reaction of a single precursor (norepinephrine) by simply varying the solvents under microwave irradiation (Fig. 2c).56

Zhang et al. reported a one-pot pyrolysis method for the preparation of N, S co-doped GQDs using L-cystine and citric acid as precursors.60 Khodadadei et al. developed blue fluorescent N-GQDs through hydrothermal pyrolysis of two precursors (citric acid and urea) and used them as nanocarriers to deliver an anticancer drug (Fig. 2d).57 Kadian et al. synthesized bright blue fluorescent S-GQDs by simply pyrolyzing citric acid and 3-mercaptopropionic acid.16 Zhu et al. utilized salicylic acid as a precursor to synthesize GQDs through a free-radical polymerization technique and irradiation using ultraviolet (UV) rays (Fig. 2e).58 The thermal pyrolysis and hydro/solvothermal methods discussed above generally require hot plates, heating mantles and hot air ovens. These methods certainly result in some sort of thermal gradient within the reactor which leads to discrepancies in size and structure of the synthesized product. In this regard, a microwave-assisted synthetic process has been reported as a substitute to traditional methods.

In this process, the heat generated by microwave irradiation can be evenly distributed throughout the reactor, which results in a more precise and uniform synthesis of GQDs. Moreover, this process helps in the preparation of functionalized GQDs by pre-mixing various functional groups contributing precursors. In an initial attempt to employ a microwave-assisted method, Tang et al. irradiated an aqueous solution of glucose in a simple microwave oven in the presence of ammonia (Fig. 2f).21 It was also reported that the microwave power and reaction time led to changes in the optical characteristics of synthesized GQDs. Next, Kumawat et al. synthesized size-controlled red-fluorescent GQDs through one-step microwave-assisted synthesis using the ethanolic extract of mango (Mangifera indica) leaves as a raw precursor (Fig. 2g).59 Further, Hasan et al. used glucosamine and thiourea as raw materials to synthesize N-GQDs and N, S co-doped GQDs through a facile microwave-assisted synthetic method. The as-prepared GQDs exhibited varying optical properties in both NIR and visible range.61

Conclusively, both the top-down and bottom-up approaches have been extensively used for the preparation of GQDs. As each coin has two sides, the above discussed methods also have some advantages and disadvantages as listed in Table 1. The key advantages of the top-down procedure are the sufficient accessibility to the raw material used, simplicity of the process and economics of scale, while a longer reaction time, use of strong acids during synthesis and low production yield limit large-scale production in industrial applications. Comparatively, the solvothermal and hydrothermal methods have been widely used among several top-down approaches. Next, the bottom-up techniques require selective carbon containing precursors such as aromatic structural molecules but are helpful in synthesizing size controlled, doped and functionalized GQDs with precision in a shorter duration of time. Among the above discussed bottom-up approaches, microwave-assisted synthesis is reported to be a simple, easy, rapid and environmentally friendly method of GQD preparation.

3. Regulation, property control and structural defects of GQDs

In recent years, GQDs have fascinated and garnered marvelous attention of researchers because of their exceptionally good physical, chemical, electrical, biological and optical properties.62–64 Moreover, the ease of regulation or modification of these properties according to the chosen field of application makes them more attractive and indispensable materials. In this section, we will discuss the strategies used for the regulation and property control of GQDs during and post-synthesis.

3.1 Size and intrinsic property control

It has been reported by many research groups that the size of GQDs may be changed by just varying the reaction temperature, time, stabilizing agent and concentration of reactants, as demonstrated in Table 2.31,45,50,65–67 For example, Kwon et al. synthesized size controlled (varying from 2 to 10 nm) GQDs via amidative cutting of tattered graphite by simply regulating the concentration of amine (Fig. 3a).67 Next, during an investigation of the quantum confinement effect of GQDs, Yeh et al. have also synthesized different sized GQDs by using ultrafiltration through varying pore size membranes.66 It has been observed that the luminescence of the as-prepared GQDs varies from orange to blue as the size is reduced from 8 nm to 1 nm, which leads to a change in the sp2-domain size and hence in the π*–π gap. This confirms that the bandgap of GQDs depends on their size and emission (Fig. 3b). Parallelly, Ye et al. also arrived at the same conclusion and introduced size-controlled GQDs from anthracite coal by varying the temperature of the reaction, and hence, tailored its level of functionality and bandgaps.68 Kim and Suh synthesized GQDs through a low-cost thermal plasma jet process with controlled size distribution by varying the length of CNTs.69 Recently, Gan et al. reported that the top-down approaches usually give rise to GQDs with varying size distributions which leads to the occurrence of excitation-dependent emission spectra.70

Apart from size control, modification of other intrinsic properties such as number of layers, shape and edge states has also been studied. For instance, Hassan et al. prepared activated GQDs embellished with both bound and free edges via an ultrasonic method in the presence of KOH (Fig. 3c).71 It has been observed that the as-synthesized activated GQDs exhibited enhanced electrochemical capacitance and photoluminescence intensity as compared to bare GQDs and graphene sheets. Further, the richness of oxygenated groups could be controlled by varying the strength of the oxidant, reductant and time of oxidative cutting, while the functionalization of oxygen containing groups depends on the specificity of the oxidant and reductant used in the synthesis.73,74 Recently, Zhou et al. discussed the dependence of photo-induced reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation and cytotoxicity capability of GQDs on the presence/absence of ketonic carbonyl, carboxylic and hydroxyl groups and reported that the ROS generation ability of GQDs is highly dependent on the existing ketonic carbonyl groups (and pH) as shown in (Fig. 3d).72 Also, the removal of oxygen containing groups from GQDs could desirably reduce the photo-induced cytotoxicity.

3.2 Structural defects

The characteristics of graphitic materials are vastly dependent on the structural defects present in their structure.75 In this section, we focus on the versatility and ability of graphene-like structures to accommodate different types of defects in their framework. These defects mainly change the physical, chemical and structural properties of graphitic moieties. For example, a graphitic cluster of twelve pentagons can construct a fullerene, and hence, such a spherical assembly shows an electronegative surface with excellent chemistry.75,76 Similarly, Pedersen et al. discussed the role of vacancies or defects in the graphene-like structure by preparing an antidot graphitic lattice.77 Generally, there are five different types of structural defects which can arise in graphitic materials as discussed below.
Structural defects. These defects mainly appear due to significant distortion in the structure caused by the occurrence of heptagons, pentagons and octagons within the hexagonal rings. However, the reactivity of such non-hexagonal rings with acceptor or donor atoms has not yet been systematically (theoretically and experimentally) investigated.75
Grain boundaries, bond rotation or topological defects. These defects can be Stone–Thrower–Wales type or 5-7-7-5 pairs rooted in the hexagonal network of the graphene-like structure that can be generated by a 90° rotation of CC bonds, hence resulting in the transformation of two heptagons and two pentagons.78
High-strain folding defects. These defects mainly deal with the existing non-hexagonal rings and are induced due to the high temperature annealing of the graphitic structure creating localized strain in the structure.79 However, their effects on the electronic and chemical properties of graphitic materials have not yet been explored systematically.
Non-sp2 carbon defects. These defects arise due to the occurrence of highly reactive carbon including carbon chains, vacancies, edges, adatoms, interstitials, etc. The establishment of these defects can encourage the formation of nanotube junctions.80
Doping-induced defects. These defects appear due to substitutional heteroatoms embedded in the framework of graphene sheets. These defects could tailor the electronic, chemical and physical characteristics of graphene-like structures,81 as discussed in the next section (Section 3.3).

3.3 Heteroatom doping

Since the first attempt of N doped GQDs reported by Li et al. in 2012, doping of heteroatoms has become an efficacious approach for regulating the characteristics of GQDs and other nanomaterials.82 Through this doping process, some of the C atoms in the GQD framework are substituted with other elements (but not limited to) such as B, N, S, F, P, Cl, Se, Si etc. which plays a crucial role in enhancing the fluorescence quantum yield, catalytic properties and conductivity of GQDs.36,55,60,83–85 The introduction of heteroatoms could be realized through in situ (during synthesis) and ex situ (after synthesis) procedures. For example, Yeh et al. synthesized intact N doped graphene oxide QDs (iNGO-QDs) through a thermal treatment of graphene oxide QDs in the presence of ammonia (Fig. 4a).86 The in situ doping of N repaired the vacancy-type defects and introduced n-type conductivity to balance the charge on p-type GO, and hence the coexistence of p- and n-type domains avoided the leakages of photogenerated charges and helped in charge separation. Similarly, Chen et al. also prepared N doped graphene oxide QDs by thermally treating the graphene oxide sheet in the presence of ammonia followed by ultrasonic exfoliation of the sheet in nitric acid.12 Further, the as-prepared N-GQDs were subjected to a hydrothermal treatment in the presence of ammonia to convert pyridinic/pyrrolic-N into amino/amide groups (Fig. 4b). It has been observed that the N functionalities of amino/amide groups influenced the transition of photogenerated electrons due to the non-planarity of N atoms. Next, Kundu et al. synthesized N, F and S co-doped GQDs by a simple microwave treatment of MWCNTs (Fig. 4c) in ionic liquid medium, and they exhibited excellent PL quantum yield (70%).19

Among several heteroatoms, nitrogen is the most widely used element of the periodic table in heteroatom doping of GQDs. This is not only due to the higher electronegativity of N atoms (3.04) as compared to C atoms (2.55) but because of the size compatibility (lower lattice strain) also.87,88 Various methods have been used to synthesize N-GQDs that include microwave-assisted hydrothermal, pyrolysis, solvothermal, electrochemical and other methods. For instance, Hou et al. synthesized strong green-fluorescent multi-layer N-GQDs through a microwave-assisted hydrothermal process using urea and glucose as precursors.89 Li et al. used citric acid and urea to produce “on–off” switchable fluorescent N-GQDs via a hydrothermal approach.82 Recently, Fu et al. synthesized multicolor (red, green and blue) fluorescent N-GQDs with an average diameter of 20 nm through electrochemical exfoliation of graphite rods and used them for ferric ion sensing and bioimaging applications.93 Further, since B and C atoms have similar atomic radius, the scientific community is quite devotedly interested in investigating the characteristics of B-GQDs. In this direction, Zhang et al. prepared B-GQDs by cutting boron-doped graphene via a hydrothermal approach and observed that the B atom introduced a new kind of surface state, which increased the optical PL properties after treatment with glucose.94 In 2017, Tam et al. used glucose and boric acid as raw precursors to develop B-GQDs through a hydrothermal treatment (Fig. 4d) and reported that the presence of the B dopant enhanced the electrocatalytic activity of GQDs by generating multiple active sites.85

In addition to N and B atoms, some other dopants, such as S, P, F etc., have also been studied for modulating the properties of GQDs. In line with this, Gong et al. synthesized fluorinated GQDs (F-GQDs) by using a gradient fluorine-sacrificing strategy (Fig. 4e) with tunable F content and controlled size from bulk fluorinated graphite.90 The as-prepared F-GQDs exhibited stable fluorescence in both alkali and acidic conditions with increased paramagnetic properties in addition to regulating surface hydrophobicity. Li et al. fabricated P-GQDs via an electrochemical approach by using sodium phytate as an electrolyte for availing both an electrolysis environment and a source of P-dopants (Fig. 4f).91 The obtained P-GQDs showed excellent free radical scavenging activity. Next, Li et al. synthesized blue-green fluorescence emitting S-GQDs with enhanced electronic and surface chemical properties through simple electrolysis of graphite in sodium p-toluenesulfonate solution and used them for Fe3+ ion sensing (Fig. 4g).92 Kharangarh et al. investigated the influence of several S atom containing compounds in improving the quantum yield of S-GQDs.95 Recently, Kadian et al. systematically investigated the effect of S dopants (by varying the amount of dopant, time and temperature of reaction) on the fluorescence and quantum yield of GQDs, and reported that the presence of S atoms significantly enhances their PL and quantum yield (Fig. 4h).16 These reports proved that the properties of GQDs depend on the type of dopant (especially the size and charge) used and these dopants can regulate the surface properties and energy band gap of GQDs, resulting in their improved quantum yield and photoelectric properties.92

3.4 Surface or edge functionalization

The surface functionalization technique has also played a key role in modulating the optical, electrical, biological and catalytic properties through facilitating electron-accepting/donating, carboxy, amine, hydroxyl and other biological receptor target groups on their surface as well as on the edge, and thus, expanded the area of research of GQDs. Functionalization may occur during the preparation of GQDs by using selected raw precursors (containing desired functional groups) or post-synthesis through molecular attachment. In an experimental and theoretical investigation of surface chemistry of functionalized GQDs (Fig. 5a) with a series of small organic molecules, Qian et al. reported that diamines have the strongest capability to regulate the optical properties or quantum yield of GQDs, owing to their exceptional protonation phenomenon.96 Functionalization of primary amines also influences the electronic and optical properties of graphene. Tetsuka et al. prepared amino-functionalized GQDs (AF-GQDs) through a hydrothermal treatment (Fig. 5b) of a mixture of graphene oxide and ammonia solution.51 The primary amine moieties on the edge of AF-GQDs modified the electronic structure, and thereby, tailored the optical properties of GQDs. In some cases, functionalization with alkylamine (e.g. polyethylene glycol-amine (PEG-amine) and poly-ethylenimine (PEI)) and amino groups leads to a red and blue-shift in the PL of GQDs, respectively. For instance, Jin et al. investigated experimentally as well as theoretically that the charge transfer from the surface functional group (PEG-amine) to GQDs narrows the band gap of GQDs due to increased electron density and results in red-shifted PL (Fig. 5c).97 Sun et al. reported that green-yellow fluorescent GQDs with low quantum yield (2.5%) could be converted into blue fluorescent amino-functionalized GQDs with high quantum yield (16.4%) through a facile hydrothermal treatment in the presence of ammonia.98 The change in the PL emission could be attributed to the abundant sp2 domain carbon atoms after amination and may also be due to the strong electron donating capability of amino groups in comparison to hydroxyl groups.

Apart from this, PEG and PEI have been proved to be beneficial passivating agents, which not only regulate the PL of GQDs but also avail several binding sites for other modifications. For instance, Wang et al. synthesized green fluorescent PEG-GQDs (Fig. 5d) with high PL quantum yield and used them as drug carriers by loading the drug via binding sites facilitated by PEG on the GQDs.99 Likewise, Shen et al. prepared PEG-GQDs (Fig. 5e) by reducing PEG passivated GO through hydrazine hydrate.100 The as-synthesized PEG-GQDs showed blue fluorescence (originated from band-gap transition) and green up-conversion fluorescence (due to anti-Stokes PL) under excitation of 980 nm.100 Later, the same group improved the quantum yield from 7.4 to 28.0% through cutting of GO sheets into GQDs and simultaneously passivating the obtained GQDs.104 A systematic (theoretical and experimental) investigation by Tetsuka et al. demonstrated that N-functionalized GQDs can regulate the PL and energy levels of GQDs (by narrowing the energy gap), and hence, improve the performance of optoelectronic devices due to regulated optical and electronic properties of GQDs.105

Apart from amine, PEG and PEI, aryl has also been used as a passivating agent to tailor the chemical and optical properties of GQDs. Luo et al. reported a versatile diazonium process to implant aryl groups 5-sulfonaphthyl, phenyl, 4-sulfophenyl and 4-carboxyphenyl through the Gomberg–Bachmann reaction as shown in Fig. 5f.101 The quantum yield of aryl grafted GQDs was enhanced up to 6 times and PL bands were tuned between 418 nm and 447 nm. Next, Li et al. prepared sulfonated GQDs by reacting GQDs and chloro-sulfonic acid in a nitrogen environment at 70 °C for 24 hours.102 The obtained S-GQDs exhibited excellent catalytic activity and were capable of degrading carbohydrates (such as cellulose, glucose and fructose) into 5-hydroxymethylfurfural, as shown in Fig. 5g. Additionally, functionalization of targeting groups has been used to increase the internalization of GQDs loaded with anti-cancerous drugs through an endocytosis process. Bioreceptor functionalized GQDs can target the tumor cells that are overexpressed with a specific receptor. For example, Li et al. have synthesized folic acid functionalized GQDs (FA-GQDs) via thermal pyrolysis of folic acid and maleic acid with tunable fluorescence and used for cancer cell imaging (Fig. 5h).103 Abdullah-Al-Nahain et al. prepared hyaluronic acid functionalized GQDs (HA-GQDs) through hydrothermal cutting of carbon fiber followed by conjugation of HA on GQDs. The obtained HA-GQDs were used for targeted drug delivery and cancer cell imaging.23 Further, Zheng et al. synthesized insulin-conjugated GQDs for dynamic tracking of insulin receptors in 3T3-L1 adipocytes.106

4. Applications of GQDs

Due to exceptional features including large surface area, favorable biocompatibility, capability to facilitate charge transfer, ease of functionalization, abundance of active sites, tunable bandgap and other desirable optical properties, GQDs have shown great potential for several applications. A massive number of reports published on the synthesis and application of GQDs show that GQDs may become highly desired and essential materials for the upcoming advanced technologies. GQDs have already been used in a variety of applications including biosensing, bioimaging, drug delivery, photodynamic therapy, antibacterial action, photovoltaics, light emitting diodes, photodetectors, photocatalysis, supercapacitors, thermoelectric devices, rechargeable batteries and electrocatalysis. This section will discuss the ongoing and possible future applications of GQDs with focus on biomedical and optoelectronics fields.

4.1 Biomedical applications

Owing to the good biocompatibility, availability of multiple binding sites, ease of surface functionalization and desirable low cytotoxicity, GQDs have been deployed widely for several biomedical applications. In many cases GQDs have indeed shown some outstanding performances, which allow them to replace the traditional nanomaterials used in this area. Due to exciting and promising outcomes, researchers and scientists are continuously exploring the potential of GQDs for other novel medical applications. 4.1.1 Biosensing. Due to beneficial aspects of an intrinsic defect-induced structure, tunable PL properties, ease of functionalization, high quantum yield, abundance of active sites, excellent electrochemical and optical properties, GQDs have evolved as innovative nanomaterials for the development of next-generation biosensing kits and point-of-care devices. The interaction between GQDs and several inorganic ions and small organic and biomolecular substances via π–π conjugation, electrostatic interactions or electron transfer can quench their fluorescent intensity.31,107–112 Following this mechanism, several chemo- and bio-sensors have been designed with enhanced sensitivity and selectivity.7,113,114 Several inorganic cations and anions present in the environment are essential for living species and play a key role in their biological activity. Nevertheless, the unnecessary aggregation of these inorganic ions will cause a serious health issue to living species. Therefore, a precise detection of these ions with high selectivity and sensitivity is one of the most crucial challenges for biosensing devices.

To address this issue, several investigators have explored various strategies of functionalization and doping during the last few years, making use of the subtle changes in the band gap effecting tunable changes in structural, optical, and opto-electronic properties. For example, Tam et al. used N-GQDs as a fluorescent probe for the detection of Fe3+ ions, with a detection limit of 1 mM.108 It has been reported that N-GQDs were more selective and sensitive to Fe3+ ions as compared to other metal ions. The interaction among hydroxyl groups of N-GQDs and Fe3+ ions culminated in metal hydroxide formation and initiated charge transfer and restrained exciton recombination which encouraged the fluorescence quenching process of GQDs.108 Similarly, Wang et al. also used graphene oxide derived luminescent GQDs as a nanoprobe for Fe3+ ion detection.115 Following the same aggregation induced PL quenching mechanism, Shen et al. used highly fluorescent N, S co-doped GQDs for the simultaneous detection of Ag+, Fe3+ and Cu2+ ions (Fig. 6a) with high sensitivity and a low limit of detection of 50 nM, 250 nM and 8 nM, respectively.116 Ananthanarayanan et al. demonstrated the interaction of Fe3+ ions and the imidazole ring present on the surface of 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium hexafluorophosphate functionalized GQDs for the detection of Fe3+ ions. The fluorescent probe showed sensitivity and selectivity towards ferric ions with a limit of detection of 7.22 μM.31

In addition to Fe3+, Sun et al. reported a new amino functionalized GQD (AF-GQD) based highly luminescent nanoprobe for the determination of Cu2+ ions in living cells through high binding affinity and fast chelating kinetics with existing O and N on the surface of AF-GQDs.98 Chen et al. prepared amine and phosphate group modified GQDs (PiNGQDs) for the parallel detection of various heavy metal ions including Cu2+, Zn2+, Fe3+ and Cr3+ (Fig. 6b) via a PL turn-on and turn-off mechanism caused by the interaction of ions and existing amine and phosphate groups on the surface of PiNGQDs.117 Qian et al. developed an optical turn-on nanoprobe for the detection of Pb2+ using an aptamer-GQD and graphene oxide based biocompatible platform with the lowest detection limit of 0.6 nM.118 It has been reported that graphene oxide easily quenches the fluorescence of aptamer-GQDs through efficient photo induced electron transfer while the subsequent turn-on process of PL was noticed with the addition of lead(II) ions ascribed to the formation of a G-quadraplex aptamer–Pb2+ complex.118 More recently, Wang et al. reported yellow light emitting N-GQDs for rapid and selective detection of Fe3+ ion in natural water samples.119 After favorable performance obtained for heavy metal cation sensing, GQDs have also been used for the detection of non-metallic anions including sulfides (S2−),120,121 nitrites (NO2−),122 pyrophosphates (P2O74−)123 and chloride ions (Cl−).124 Liu et al. demonstrated that nitrites can effectively quench the fluorescence of N, P co-doped GQDs (N,P-GQDs) (Fig. 6c), due to the electron transfer of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital levels of N,P-GQDs to NO2− and strong binding affinity among existing –NH2 groups of N,P-GQDs and NO2−.122 The developed biocompatible fluorescent probe exhibited high sensitivity in the living cells with a detection limit of 2.4 nM. Yu et al. developed an on–off–on system for the detection of S2− ions using GQDs as a fluorescent probe and copper(II) ions as a quencher.120 It has been reported that Cu2+ ions can interact with the oxygen-containing groups of GQDs, and thus, quench the fluorescence of GQDs via an electron or energy transfer process from the GQDs to copper(II) ions. However, S2− ions have a higher binding affinity for the copper(II) ions than oxygen-containing groups, leading to the turn-on process of fluorescence of GQDs through separation of the aggregated GQDs.

Similar to inorganic ions, various small organic biomolecules including vitamins, amino acids, bioflavonoids, carbohydrates, bio-thiols and other metabolites also participate in several physiological activities; hence an accurate detection of these organic molecules is of great importance. In this direction, many research groups have used the PL down- and up-conversion phenomena of GQDs to detect these molecules with high sensitivity and selectivity.125–128 For example, Zhang et al. demonstrated the PL down- and up-conversion phenomena of GQDs by using Eu3+ as a trigger to quench the fluorescence of GQDs that could be recovered through introduction of two amino acids, aspartic acid (Asp) and glutamic acid (Glu).127 The sensitive and specific quantification of amino acids (Asp and Glu) can be achieved by monitoring the changes in PL of GQDs. Achadu and Nyokong reported a maleimide-derivatized GQD (M-GQD) and zinc phthalocyanine (2) based fluorescent sensing platform for the detection of bio-thiols (homocysteine, glutathione and cysteine) via a rapid Michael addition reaction between the maleimide-derivatized probe and bio-thiols.125 Recently, Kadian and Manik designed a highly sensitive and selective fluorescent probe (SGQDs) for the detection of quercetin (the most common bioflavonoid) through an inner filter effect mechanism as shown in Fig. 6d.129 It has been reported that the fluorescence of SGQDs was significantly quenched with the addition of quercetin due to the formation of a non-fluorescent ground state complex between the quencher and fluorophore. This probe has been used to monitor the quercetin content in real-time red wine samples with satisfactory recovery results. Further, Liu et al. developed a fluorescence on–off–on system for the fast detection of H2O2 and ascorbic acid using GQDs, horseradish peroxidase, catechol and H2O2.131 In this, catechol gets oxidized in the presence of H2O2 and converts into o-benzoquinone, and thus, quenches the PL of present GQDs whereas ascorbic acid opposes the formation of o-benzoquinone, and hence, leads to the fluorescence recovery of GQDs. A satisfactory fluorescence recovery was obtained when this developed probe was applied to detect ascorbic acid in human serum samples.

Similar to inorganic ions and organic molecules, several biomarkers and bio-macromolecules have also been quantified through an optical and electrochemical bio-sensing platform using GQDs.130,132–134 For instance, Qian et al. developed an ultrasensitive nanoprobe for DNA quantification through fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) between GQDs and CNTs.132 In this, the single-strand DNA functionalized GQDs (ssDNA-GQDs) when allowed to interact with CNTs resulted in their PL quenching due to the occurrence of FRET between ssDNA-GQDs and CNTs while the addition of target DNA recovers the PL by liberating double-stranded DNA-GQDs. The nanoprobe displayed a lower detection limit of 0.4 nM. Likewise, Bhatnagar et al. developed an immunosensor for the early detection of cardiac arrest or heart attack by monitoring the antigen Troponin I (cTnI) through the FRET mechanism (Fig. 6e) between amine functionalized GQDs conjugated with antibody anti-cardiac Troponin I (anti-cTnI) and graphene.130 The immunosensor exhibited a detection limit of 0.192 pg mL−1. Further, Tran and Doong demonstrated the selective and sensitive detection of hemoglobin (Hb) using S-GQDs as a fluorescent sensing probe.135 They reported that the fluorescence of S-GQDs could be significantly quenched by Hb due to strong hydrophobic interactions among S-GQDs and Hb. In addition to use as optical probes, GQDs have also been used to develop electrochemical biosensors for the detection of dopamine,136,137 glutathione,138 pesticides,139 metal ions,137 alkaline phosphatase140etc., owing to their excellent charge transfer and catalytic activity. For example, Lu et al. used nanochannel-confined GQDs as a signal amplifier and recognition element to develop an electrochemical biosensing probe for ultrasensitive detection of Cd2+, Hg2+, Cu2+ and dopamine with detection limits of 4.3 nM, 9.8 pM, 8.3 pM, and 120 nM, respectively.137

4.1.2 Bioimaging. Bioimaging is a considerably advanced technique in the field of biomedical research that uses several parts of electromagnetic spectra to monitor biological events such as cellular uptake, targeted delivery, cell tracking and bio-distribution of therapeutics in a detailed manner.23,141 Due to photobleaching issues, traditional organic dyes or fluorophores have limited use in long-term imaging applications. To address this problem, semiconductor QDs have been extensively used as an alternative to organic dyes. Nevertheless, due to their large size in comparison to a biomolecule, these QDs may change the functions or process of the target and can create an artificial cluster of multiple targets. Moreover, semiconductor QDs have high possibilities of being toxic due to the use of heavy metal ions in their synthetic process. In contrast, owing to an exceptional combination of numerous key virtues of GQDs, including controlled molecular size, good photo-stability, tunable PL properties, biocompatibility, good aqueous solubility, ease of functionalization, and chemical stability, they have emerged as a potent substitute for bio-imaging applications.142–145

Since the initial utilization of single-layered GQDs by Dong et al. for cell nucleus imaging of human breast cancer cells (MCF-7),29 there are numerous reports worthy to be spotlighted. For example, Zheng et al. utilized insulin-conjugated GQDs for dynamic tracking and targeted imaging of insulin receptors in 3T3-L1 adipocytes (shown in Fig. 7a).106 In this study, the internalization, distribution, and recycling of insulin receptors were monitored through trafficking and it was reported that the dynamics of insulin receptors in adipocytes were eased by apelin and opposed by a pro-inflammatory factor. Further, Zhang et al. used uniform size yellow fluorescence GQDs for stem cell imaging in three different types of cells including cardiac progenitor cells (CPCs), neuro sphere cells (NSCs) and pancreas progenitor cells (PPCs).47 The results illustrated that the GQDs are promising fluorophores for biological imaging. However, the trivial cytotoxic effect of GQDs was recorded at a concentration of 100 mg mL−1 after 3 days of incubation time. Peng et al. used green luminescent GQDs prepared from carbon fibers for application of cell imaging of nucleus stained human breast cancer cell lines T47D (Fig. 7b).33 The results clearly demonstrated that the cell's nucleus indicated a blue color as stained by DAPI and agglomerated high-contrast green due to the presence of GQDs around the nucleus. Tan et al. utilized uniform size (3 nm) red fluorescent GQDs (RF-GQDs) derived from graphite through electrochemical exfoliation by using K2S2O8 solution for cellular imaging of HeLa cells (Fig. 7c).146 In this report, the imaging results showed red fluorescence under 488 nm excitation in the cell membrane as well as cytoplasm of the model cell. The cytotoxicity assay of RF-GQDs demonstrated favorable biocompatibility with HeLa cells and proved that they can be used for biological labeling. Next, Li et al. developed a redox-sensitive fluorescence on–off–on probe (Fig. 7d) using desferrioxamine-functionalized GQDs (DFOB-GQDs) to monitor dynamic cellular redox homeostasis.147 Later, the same group developed a (2,4-dinitrophenoxy)tyrosine-functionalized GQD (DNPTYR-GQD) based fluorescent probe for real-time monitoring of intracellular H2S level in MCF-7 cells.142 In this work, DNPTYR contains electron withdrawing groups (dinitrophenoxyl) that reduce the fluorescence of GQDs through PET while later H2S potentially cleaved the dinitrophenoxyl group leading to the recovery of GQD fluorescence. Inspired by these reports, Chen et al. synthesized, for the first time, sweet GQDs by functionalizing them with two sugar moieties (galactose (Gal-GQDs) and mannose (Mal-GQDs)) for real-time trafficking dynamics of carbohydrate receptors in live cells.148

4.1.3 Drug delivery. Due to exciting biological and physicochemical properties, GQDs have gained enormous attention for drug delivery applications.63 The presence of several functional groups on the edge of GQDs offers active binding sites to conjugate the drugs, therapeutics and targeting ligands. Moreover, the occurrence of sp2 carbon in the structure of GQDs allows chemotherapeutic drug molecules, which usually carry aromatic rings, to be attached onto the surface of GQDs leading to an increase in drug loading capacity. For example, chemotherapeutic drugs such as methotrexate (MTX) and doxorubicin (DOX) are effective for cancer treatment but cannot be delivered proficiently to a specific tissue in the body due to their poor aqueous solubility. The large surface area-to-volume ratio of GQDs offers them several binding sites for attachment via π-stacking onto the basal plane and the drugs are delivered through a hydrophilic carrier. Initially, Abdullah et al. demonstrated an efficient targeted delivery of DOX through HA-functionalized GQD (HA-GQD) as a drug carrier, as shown in Fig. 8a.23 In this, the as-prepared conjugate was applied to CD44 receptor overexpressed A549 cell lines and the loading and release kinetics of DOX (hydrophobic drug) under mild acidic conditions were monitored. Next, Wang et al. reported that GQDs can efficiently deliver DOX to nuclei without any pre-modification in their structure as shown in Fig. 8b.155 Also, the as-prepared GQD/DOX conjugate could significantly improve nuclear uptake and the DNA cleavage activity of DOX. Further, Wang et al. demonstrated the suitability of green fluorescent polyethylene glycol (PEG) passivated GQDs (PEG-GQDs) as a nano drug carrier.99 It has been reported that the presence of PEG on the surface of GQDs not only enhanced the PL but also improved the drug loading capacity by offering free hydrogen binding sites. Lv et al. developed a highly fluorescent GQDs–chitosan (CS) hybrid (CS–GQDs) xerogel for in vivo bioimaging and a pH-dependent drug delivery system through a protonation/deprotonation process of the –NH2 groups of CS chains.156 Further, Iannazzo et al. reported a biotin (BTN) conjugated GQD (GQD–BTN) based cell traceable drug delivery system (Fig. 8c).24 In this, DOX was simply mixed with the as-prepared nanoconjugate and the drug loading and release kinetics were tested on (A549) human lung epithelial cancer cell lines. Similarly, Khodadadei et al. developed MTX loaded nitrogen-doped GQD (MTX-(N-GQD)) nanocarriers as a potent drug delivery system as shown in Fig. 8d.57 The release of MTX was assessed through dialysis of MTX-(N-GQDs) in PBS buffer of pH 7.4. It was observed that only 60% MTX was released in the initial 9 h and a slow liberation of drug was continued up to 48 h ensuring a controlled prolonged release of drug. Recently, Javanbakht et al. designed a hydrogel nanocomposite thin film with chemotherapeutic properties via incorporating GQDs and DOX into a carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) hydrogel.157 The hydrogel nanocomposite exhibited low cytotoxicity, high loading and prolonged pH-dependent release for DOX as compared to bare CMC. More recently, Nasrollahi et al. developed a novel nanocarrier through the incorporation of GQDs, iron, and DOX into ferritin nanocages for bimodal imaging and drug delivery.158

In addition to chemotherapeutic drugs, GQDs have been used for gene therapy applications to deliver RNA or DNA that can replace a mutated gene, express new gene products or knockout an inappropriately functioning gene to combat disease.159,160 In this way, Ghafary et al. designed a new nanoconjugate composed of GQDs, MPG-2H1 chimeric peptide and plasmid DNA (pDNA) with dual functionalities of real-time tracking and gene delivery.161 The nanoconjugate was designed via noncovalent interactions of all components. The obtained transfection efficiency of the optimized complex was nearly eightfold as compared to that of the control peptide–pDNA complex. The results obtained in this study suggest that GQDs could be a potent transfection vector for gene delivery application. Owing to the presence of excessive sp2 domain in the framework and possible π-stacking, GQDs provide higher drug loading as compared to other nanoparticle-based drug carrier systems. However, the active sites on the edge of GQDs for conjugation of a cationic polymer for DNA loading appear to be restricted to functional groups, which leads to a low possibility of their use in gene delivery application, as these biomolecules need to be covalently bound onto the edge groups. Though the ability to track gene delivery through fluorescence of GQDs is beneficial, state-of-the-art investigations on using the basal plane for gene delivery application would propel GQDs as a feasible transfection vector for gene therapy in future.

4.1.4 Antimicrobial/antibacterial agents. GQDs are innovative multipurpose biocompatible nanomaterials of the carbon family, which offer several binding sites to bind other biological moieties and can easily form conjugates with other nanomaterials, and hence, can be used in several potential biomedical applications. In this section, we will discuss the antibacterial or antimicrobial properties of GQDs and their nanocomposites. For any antibacterial agent it is quite essential that it should have selective toxicity towards only bacterial or microbial cells without harming the healthy cells.162 After the confirmation of their low cytotoxicity towards healthy cells, various research groups devotedly started the investigation of antibacterial properties of GQDs. For instance, Habiba et al. synthesized biocompatible nanocomposites of PEGylated silver nanoparticles and graphene quantum dots (Ag–GQDs) through a pulsed laser method and examined their antibacterial activity against Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) and Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) (Fig. 9a).163 The results exhibited significant improvement in the antibacterial activity of as-prepared nanocomposite Ag–GQDs as compared to bare AgNPs and GQDs, due to the existing synergy between AgNPs and GQDs. Further, Hui et al. examined the antibacterial activity of GQDs prepared by rupturing the C60 cage (C60-GQD) and found that these GQDs could effectively kill S. aureus, but were unable to kill Escherichia coli (E. coli), Bacillus subtilis (B. subtilis) or P. aeruginosa (Fig. 9b).164 Inspired by this study, Biswas et al. used MWCNTs as raw precursors to prepare GQDs and investigated their antimicrobial activity against E. coli (NCIM 2931), B. subtilis (NCIM 2063), P. aeruginosa (NCIM 5029), and S. aureus (NCIM 5021) through microtiter plate assay.162 The results showed that the as-prepared GQDs have selective toxicity towards microbial cells with a minimum inhibitory concentration of ∼512 μg mL−1 for P. aeruginosa and S. aureus and ∼256 μg mL−1 for E. coli and B. subtilis.

However, many of these studies need independent validation protocols before commercial applications.

4.1.5 Photodynamic and photothermal therapy. Early diagnosis and therapy of cancer or tumor cells are of great significance. Owing to their several unique merits including broad light absorption, excellent dispersibility, good photostability, favorable biocompatibility, ease of degradation by enzymes, fast body clearance, and ability to generate radicals, GQDs have also been used as a therapeutic agent in photodynamic therapy (PDT) and photothermal therapy (PTT) for cancer treatment. In PDT, tumor cells are destroyed using ROS generated by photosensitizing chemical substances under irradiation. The ROS generation capability of GQDs under irradiation offers an opportunity to use them as a PDT agent to kill cancer cells. In contrast to traditional therapies, such as radio- and chemo-therapy, PDT is a non-invasive technique to destroy tumor cells with fewer side effects. Initially, Ge et al. introduced GQDs as a potential PDT and imaging agent with an excellent 1O2 quantum yield of ∼1.3.151 In the investigations, GQDs exhibited superior photostability and low toxicity as compared to CdTe (a semiconducting QD) and Ppix (a clinically approved PDT agent). These investigations suggest that GQDs have played a critical role in PDT and PTT application by reducing the size of cancer cells as well as by destroying the tumor. Moreover, traditional PDT agents need UV-Vis region light to irradiate them, which limits their tissue penetration. In contrast to this, GQDs could be irradiated with NIR light, and thus, they open a new window for cancer treatment as more efficient PDT agents.

4.2 Optoelectronic applications

Since the discovery of graphene by Novoselov et al.,2 its high electric conductivity, linear dispersion band structure and good thermal stability have made it a potential candidate for optoelectronic applications.171 Nevertheless, due to its zero band gap and poor aqueous solubility, graphene will have several restrictions in future applications. In contrast, due to the exceptional optical and electronic properties of zero-dimensional GQDs, including tunable PL, good photostability and aqueous solubility, large surface area, abundant active sites, ability to integrate with other nanomaterials, and capability to tailor the band gap and to facilitate charge transfer caused by quantum confinement and edge effects, they have attracted considerable attention for optoelectronic applications such as in light-emitting diodes (LEDs),67,172 photodetection,173,174 solar cells or photovoltaics,175–179 plasmonics,180,181 supercapacitors,182,183 and rechargeable batteries,184,185 as shown in Table 4. Several research groups have devotedly engaged in investigating the potential of GQDs for technology transfer and to deliver next-generation low-cost optoelectronics devices. 4.2.1 Light emitting diodes. The luminescence of GQDs basically relies on the phenomenon “where an external energy excites the carriers followed by emission of light due to immediate recombination”.186 In the development of LEDs, GQDs can play several roles including that of a gap tuner, dopant, electroluminescent phosphor, and color converter. For example, initially Gupta et al. prepared a mixture of different concentrations of methylene blue functionalized GQDs (MB-GQDs) and poly(2-methoxy-5-(2-ethylhexyloxy)-1,4-phenylenevinylene) (MEH-PPV) and used it as a light emitting layer for the development of organic LEDs (OLEDs).175 The obtained results showed that the MB-GQDs dispersed in MEH-PPV provided easy electrical transport pathways that enhanced the charge injection and the carrier density, and hence, offered an enhanced performance. Further, Tang et al. reported another exciting application of GQDs as a white light converter through their coating on commercially available blue color LEDs with a significant shift in the Commission Internationale de l’Eclairage (CIE) coordinates (Fig. 11a) from (0.242, 0.156) to (0.282, 0.373).21 Sekiya et al. explored the white light emission feature of edge functionalized GQDs to be used for the fabrication of LEDs and other optoelectronic devices.187 Further, Kim and Kim developed solution-processed and highly-efficient LEDs using octadecylamine (ODA)-GQDs (Fig. 11b).188 It was observed that the occurrence of nucleophilic substitution among epoxy and amine functional groups reduced the amount of oxygen in ODA-GQDs as compared to bare GQDs. The current efficiency (6.51 cd A−1) of ODA-GQDs was measured to be the highest among previously reported (3.47 cd A−1 by Kwon et al.189 and 0.65 cd A−1 Song et al.172) GQD based LEDs. Later on, the same group developed ultrahigh-luminosity white LEDs (WLEDs) using edge functionalized GQDs (EF-GQDs) synthesized from a simple solution approach.190 The CIE coordinates and maximum luminance for the WLEDs prepared from EF-GQDs were found to be (0.27, 0.29) and 464 cd m−2, respectively (Fig. 11c), which was superior to those of other GQD based WLEDs (10 cd m−2 by Kwon et al.67 and 200 cd m−2 by Luo et al.32).

Recently, Dong et al. explored the effect of chemical modification of GQDs by using a reduction protocol.192 The results demonstrated that the reduction of carboxyl, epoxy or five- and six-membered-ring lactol group containing GQDs into aromatic carboxylic acid and hydroxyl group enriched GQDs effectively affected the solid-state PL of GQDs and resulted in white-light emitting GQDs (W-GQDs) having the CIE coordinates (x = 0.30, 0.36), as shown in Fig. 11d. Furthermore, Lin et al. investigated the impact of GQDs on optical and electrical properties of GaN-based UV LEDs.194 The obtained results displaced that incorporation of GQDs enhanced the electroluminescence and decreased the series resistance of LEDs, due to photon recycling and the carrier transfer between the active layer and GQDs. Recently, Kim et al. produced white-light emitting polymer films from blue-luminescent GQDs by incorporating a europium(III) complex, due to resonance energy transfer between GQDs and the europium(III) complex.195 More recently, Chen et al. prepared a transparent photo-luminescent film composed of poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA), highly efficient blue-green emission GQDs and orange color emission GQDs (D-GQDs) obtained from fullerene with preferable white light emission as shown in Fig. 11e.193

4.2.2 Solar cells. Photovoltaic (PV) solar cells are an essential source of energy, which can convert sunlight into electricity, and thus, provide clean and renewable energy to the society which has a shortage of resources to produce electricity through traditional methods. Due to their unique optoelectronic characteristics and band gap tunability, incorporation of GQDs with traditional charge carriers for the development of solar cells exhibits superior efficiency. Because of the large specific surface area, photon down-conversion ability and high electron mobility characteristics, GQDs are extensively used as an electron/hole transport layer, a sensitizer, or an active layer additive for several PV solar cell devices including dye-sensitized solar cells, organic and inorganic hybrid solar cells and perovskite solar cells.196,197 For example, Gupta et al. demonstrated that dispersion of GQDs in conjugated polymers can significantly enhance the performance of organic PV devices due to their excellent optical and electronic characteristics.175 Dutta et al. investigated the effect of GQDs on the solid-state solar cell device by preparing a composite of ZnO nanowires and GQDs.198 Such a GQD incorporated solid-state solar cell device displayed a high open circuit voltage of 0.8 V. Further, Kundu et al. introduced an exciting approach by modifying the photoanode of dye-sensitized solar cells through NFS-GQDs into the TiO2 meso-porous network to increase the light harvesting efficiency with an appealing power conversion efficiency (PCE) value of 11.7%.199

Next, He et al. employed GQDs as a photon down-conversion material into n-type silicon heterojunction (SHJ) solar cells (Fig. 12a) and achieved a high efficiency of 16.55%.200 Through photon down-conversion phenomena, GQDs absorb photons in UV regions and emit photons in visible regions. At an optimal amount of GQDs (0.3%), a significant increase in the fill factor (from 70.29% to 72.51%) and short circuit current (from 35.31 to 37.47 mA cm−2) was recorded. At the same time, He et al. incorporated GQDs with PEDOT:PSS and developed Si/PEDOT:PSS hybrid solar cells with 13.22% efficiency.179 When the amount of GQDs was 0.5%, the fill factor and short circuit current were increased from 62.85% to 63.87% and 32.11 to 36.26 mA cm−2, respectively. On the basis of these results, they concluded that incorporation of GQDs into commercially available PV devices holds great promise for fabricating next-generation PV devices with ultra-high efficiency. Further, Ryu et al. explored the effect of incorporation of GQDs on the performance of planar-type perovskite solar cells.201 In this, GQDs were introduced into a blocking TiO2 layer (Fig. 12b) through an O2 plasma treatment process which reduced the contact resistance at the perovskite/TiO2 interface, and hence, the series resistance reduced and the compound resistance increased which enhanced the fill factor and open circuit voltage of the device. The obtained results demonstrated that the appropriately sized GQDs displayed an average of 10% improvement as compared to cells without GQDs and attained 19.11% as the best PCE. Recently, Bian et al. deposited highly stable N-GQDs on the top of γ-CsPbI3 as a photon down-converter material (Fig. 12c) to harvest light from the UV region of <350 nm.202 The results obtained from final inorganic perovskite solar cells having GQDs on the top displayed a significant increment in the PCE from 15.53% to 16.02%. Further, Xie et al. introduced a small amount of GQDs into thin films of SnO2 to improve the electronic properties and PCE of SnO2 based perovskite solar cells as shown in Fig. 12d.196 In this, GQDs fill the electron traps of SnO2 through high electron transport ability, and hence, enhance the conductivity of SnO2, which is advantageous for improving the electron extraction efficiency and plummeting the recombination at the SnO2:GQDs/perovskite interface. The device developed with SnO2:GQDs exhibited very little hysteresis and the highest steady-state PCE of 20.23%. In summary, the incorporation of GQDs into dye-sensitized solar cells, organic and inorganic hybrid solar cells, perovskite solar cells and perovskite thin films not only effectively removes electron traps and reduces charge recombination but also encourages electron transfer across the layers.

5. Summary and future prospects

In this review, we summarized the recent progress in the synthesis and property tuning of GQDs through different approaches with focus on their applications in the biomedical and optoelectronic fields. We have discussed both the top-down and bottom-up strategies of GQD synthesis and conclude that both approaches have their own advantages and disadvantages. It can also be mentioned that heterogeneity in size, composition, and shape of GQDs cause several changes in their physical, chemical, biological, optical and electrical properties. Hence, the GQDs synthesized for a particular application may not efficiently work in another field of application. Consequently, a simple and universal method to prepare GQDs with well-defined characteristics or to precisely tailor GQD properties is highly desired so that a more systematic understanding and characterization of GQDs with real-time applications can be attained. Further, several methods developed for property control and regulation of GQDs through heteroatom doping, functionalization and controlling the reaction parameters have been discussed. The tremendous progress in the synthesis and property modification approaches have inspired researchers to investigate several applications of doped and functionalized GQDs. More accuracy and precision in the synthesis of size and shape controlled GQDs with high quantum yield and active edge functional groups is required to achieve new milestones in several real-world biomedical and optoelectronic applications.

The initial research and the revealed exceptional characteristics of GQDs have instigated great interest in various applications, including biosensors, bioimaging, drug delivery, antimicrobial/antibacterial agents, photodynamic therapy, photovoltaic solar cells, LEDs, photodetectors, photocatalysis, supercapacitors, thermoelectric devices, rechargeable batteries and electrocatalysis. Some of the current applications have been discussed in this review article. For example, the incidence of a specific biomarker or molecule can be monitored in real samples through quenching or recovery of fluorescence of GQDs, based on the design of the sensing platform, and thus, can act as a suitable biosensing probe. Similarly, GQDs have been used as a potent fluorophore to explore the in vitro and in vivo targeted bioimaging and real-time trafficking of cellular dynamics. The presence of several edge functional groups and excessive sp2 domains in the framework enables them for high drug loading, and hence, helps in drug delivery application. Next, the ability to produce ROS through irradiation allows them to serve as photosensitizers for PDT and PTT applications. Good biocompatibility and ability to strongly interact with other antibacterial nanomaterials make them potential candidates for antibacterial applications. Beyond this, the capability of band gap tuner and color converter characteristics enables them to work as an effective material for LED development. Further, the photon downconversion phenomenon and good electron transport facility allow them to serve in the fabrication of PV solar cells. Therefore, this review suggests that GQDs have made great progress in several domains in the last few years, but still there are some issues to be addressed and milestones to be achieved in the near future. In general, the lack of size definition and control hampers their use in a wide range of real-time applications due to significant dependence of chemical and physical properties on their size. Ensuring a low-cost, green and large-scale synthesis of GQDs is another serious problem, which also influences their practical applications. Although the green synthesis of GQDs with low toxicity has gained more attention, still there is scope for further advancements in these approaches using interconnected GQDs. Next, a detailed explanation of the PL mechanism of GQDs is also lacking, and hence, more theoretical and experimental investigations are required for a better understanding of their PL mechanism. This is particularly important since some of the GQDs reveal excitation independent PL while similar sized GQDs prepared using different methods display excitation-dependent PL thus necessitating the need for understanding the role of surface states and traps. Further, though most of the reported GQDs exhibit green and blue color fluorescence, red or NIR emission is needed for in vivo bio-imaging application posing a challenge to the synthesis of GQDs with red color emission. Additionally, a limited number of reports are available regarding the interplay between existing electron donating and withdrawing chemical groups as well as between coexistence of multiple heteroatom dopants. Therefore, more detailed investigations on these aspects will definitely help to successfully understand the electronic properties of GQDs which may bring more opportunities in optoelectronic applications. Conclusively, GQD research is still at an infant stage and has great potential to become the most attractive material of the graphene family if current issues can be resolved and the exceptional combination of several virtues of GQDs can be properly operated.
