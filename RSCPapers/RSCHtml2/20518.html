Abstract

Hydrogen production from electrochemical water splitting represents a highly promising technology for sustainable energy storage, but its widespread implementation heavily relies on the development of high-performance and cost-effective hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) electrocatalysts. Metal oxides, an important family of functional materials with diverse compositions and structures, were traditionally believed inactive towards HER. Encouragingly, the continuous breakthroughs and significant progress in recent years (mainly from 2015 onwards) make engineered metal oxides emerge as promising candidates for HER electrocatalysis. In this article, we present a comprehensive review of recent advances in metal oxide-based electrocatalysts for HER. We start with a brief description of some key fundamental concepts of HER, such as mechanisms, computational activity descriptors, and experimental parameters used to evaluate catalytic performance. This is followed by a overview of various types of metal oxide-based HER electrocatalysts reported so far, including single transition metal oxides, spinel oxides, perovskite oxides, metal (oxy)hydroxides, specially-structured metal oxides and oxide-containing hybrids, with special emphasis on designed strategies for promoting performance and property–activity correlation. Finally, some concluding remarks and perspectives about future opportunities of this exciting field are provided.

Broader context

Hydrogen (H2) is a clean and renewable energy carrier for powering future transportation and other industrial applications. Water electrolysis is a highly promising technology for sustainable hydrogen production from renewable resources (e.g., wind and solar power). A key issue to realize a high-efficiency water splitting process is to develop highly active, stable, and low-cost hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) electrocatalysts. Due to the clear superiority of structural and compositional flexibilities, metal oxide-based materials have emerged as promising candidates for catalyzing HER in recent years. In this review, we present an overview of recent advances in the development of metal oxide-based HER electrocatalysts together with some representative examples, especially from 2015 to 2020. Various designed strategies and property–activity correlation are highlighted. In addition, we conclude existing challenges and provide some perspectives about future opportunities of this exciting field. We believe that such a comprehensive review not only promotes a profound understanding of metal oxides for HER electrocatalysis but also offers some guidelines for advanced catalysts design.

1. Introduction

In the 21st century, energy is regarded as one of the most important issues for sustainable societal development. Due to an ever-increasing global energy demand and associated environmental pollution, developing clean and sustainable energy systems is a matter of utmost urgency.1,2 During the past decades, renewable energy resources (e.g., solar, wind, and hydraulic power) have been explored as sustainable alternatives to traditional fossil fuels; however, their utilization is usually limited by the spatial and temporal intermittencies.3,4 Hydrogen (H2), an ideal carbon-free energy carrier with the highest energy density, has been recognized as a promising next-generation clean energy source.5–7 To this end, producing hydrogen via an efficient and eco-friendly route is crucial for the hydrogen economy and clean energy future. Hitherto, industrial hydrogen production is largely dominated by steam methane reforming and coal gasification which generally involves the use of fossil fuels and high temperature, bringing about intensive energy consumption, high costs, low-purity of hydrogen products and greenhouse gas CO2 emissions.8,9 Alternatively, water electrolysis (or electrochemical water splitting) for hydrogen production coupled with renewable energy resources is a promising approach, which has many advantages such as abundant water resources, stable output, high H2 purity (>99%) and feasibility of large-scale application.8–13 Fujishima and Honda conducted a pioneering work on the use of a semiconductor electrode for electrochemical photolysis of water in the 1970s.14 However, the rate of the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) at the cathode of water splitting is sluggish, thus necessitating efficient electrocatalysts to expedite the reaction rate. Currently, noble platinum group metals (e.g., Pt) are identified as the benchmark HER electrocatalysts with high activity, but the prohibitive costs and scarcity of these noble metals have seriously impeded their widespread commercial application. Therefore, extensive research have been conducted to the development of efficient Pt-free electrocatalysts for HER, e.g., metals/alloys,15–18 metal phosphides/sulfides/selenides/carbides/nitrides,19–23 single atoms,24–26 functional carbon materials,27,28 and various synergistic hybrids.29,30 These materials have been extensively studied and show high potential for use in the HER electrocatalysis.

As a large class of inorganic solids, metal oxides composed of low-cost and earth-abundant elements have been an important family of functional materials.31–35 Compared with other types of metal compounds, a clear superiority of metal oxides lies in the compositional and structural diversity, which offers an electronic and crystal structure flexibility with various desirable physical/chemical properties. Due to the advantages of compositional/structural diversity, flexible tunability, low cost, earth-abundance, easy synthesis and environmental friendliness (Fig. 1a), metal oxide-based materials have received considerable interest for various applications.31–35 Nevertheless, pure metal oxides, especially the bulk materials, were generally believed inactive towards HER in the past because of poor electrical conductivity, inappropriate hydrogen adsorption ability and limited catalytic-active site, although many of them display high activity for the anodic oxygen evolution reaction (OER).19,33–38 To address this issue, in the last few years, researchers have been devoting substantial efforts with great enthusiasm to develop advanced metal oxide-based materials that can serve as efficient HER electrocatalysts. Several recent important advances make metal oxides undergo a renaissance as HER electrocatalysts, which motivates the drive for reviewing this research field. To date, several reviews relevant to HER have been published, with a focus on Pt-free electrocatalysts (e.g., ruthenium-, nickel-, cobalt-, carbon-, phosphide- and transition-metal-based materials)8,19,20,27,37,39–45 or HER fundamentals.9,46–48 However, a comprehensive review on HER electrocatalysis based on metal oxides is timely but has never been published.

2. Fundamental concepts of HER

2.1. General HER mechanism

The HER is a two-step electron transfer reaction occurring at the catalyst surface, and generally proceeds via either Volmer–Heyrovsky or Volmer–Tafel mechanisms7,9,46–48 HER in acidic medium associated with the adsorption/desorption of a hydrogen intermediate (H*) involves the following steps: Volmer step: H+ + e− → H*; Heyrovsky step: H* + H+ + e− → H2; Tafel step: H* + H* → H2, where * denotes the active site of catalyst surface. Similarly, the HER in alkaline or a neutral medium also follows the Volmer–Heyrovsky or Volmer–Tafel pathway as shown below: Volmer step: H2O + e− → H* + OH−; Heyrovsky step: H2O + H* + e− → H2 + OH−; Tafel step: H* + H* → H2. Notably, in alkaline or neutral HER it is necessary to cleave the strong covalent H–O–H bond prior to H* adsorption, in contrast to the supply of H* from the readily available hydronium cations (H3O+) in acidic solution. As a result, compared with acidic media, the kinetics of HER in alkaline media is always slower due to the extra water dissociation in the Volmer step. For example, the experimentally observed HER rate for noble Pt in alkaline medium is two or three orders of magnitude lower than that under acidic condition due to the unfavorable water dissociation kinetics on Pt.9,37,38 Therefore, ways to boost the initial water dissociation process of alkaline HER have been a point of focus. The HER mechanism can be indicated by the calculated values of the Tafel slope in addition to the insightful theoretical calculations. Specifically, ∼120, ∼40, and ∼30 mV dec−1 values of Tafel slope indicate the rate-determining step (RDS) of the Volmer, Heyrovsky, and Tafel, respectively.7,9,49 Tafel slope can be consequently considered as a convenient indicator of the reaction pathway and RDS for HER.

2.2. HER energetics and computational activity descriptors

Owing to the rapid development of computational power and techniques, density functional theory (DFT) calculations have become a powerful tool to provide in-depth information on electrochemical processes at the atomistic level.46,48,50 The calculated energetics based on DFT calculations for both kinetic and thermodynamic aspects can identify possible active sites of a reaction, and also act as intuitional descriptors for predicting the activity. Regardless of the pathway by which the HER proceeds, H* is always involved in the HER. Thus, the free energy of hydrogen adsorption (ΔGH*) is widely accepted to be a key descriptor for evaluating the HER activity, which can reflect the binding strength of H* on the catalyst surface.19,28,40,48 An optimal HER catalyst in acidic media generally have an ideal zero value of ΔGH* (ΔGH* ≈ 0) in accordance with the Sabatier principle, which reflects the optimal balance between absorption and desorption of H* on the catalyst surface.19,28,40,48 For example, ΔGH* is about zero for Pt, and correspondingly Pt is among the most active catalyst toward acidic HER.28,48 Nevertheless, the HER activity in alkaline media cannot be merely described by ΔGH*, because the kinetic barrier of water dissociation and hydroxyl adsorption may also affect the overall reaction rate.9,19,36,38,40,46–48 From a microscopic view based on HER mechanism, the apparent alkaline HER activity is governed by three important factors: (i) the H* adsorption on the surface of catalyst; (ii) the kinetic barrier of water dissociation; (iii) the prevention from hydroxyl adsorption (OH−) referred as poisoning of active sites.9,36,51 As a result, three corresponding computational energetics, i.e., the kinetic barrier of water dissociation (Eb), the free energy of hydrogen adsorption (ΔGH*) and the free energy of hydroxyl desorption (ΔGOH*), are jointly employed as the alkaline HER activity descriptors. Taken together, ΔGH* alone can act as a single descriptor for assessing the acidic HER activity; however, in alkaline media, descriptors of Eb, ΔGH* and ΔGOH* collectively (instead of alone) determine the overall HER activity.

2.3. Experimental parameters for performance evaluation

To evaluate the various aspects of HER catalytic performance of a given electrocatalyst, some important parameters are required to be calculated or measured, such as overpotential, Tafel slope, exchange current density, mass and specific activities, turnover frequency, faradaic efficiency as well as stability. A comprehensive understanding of these parameters is beneficial to design an efficient catalyst. In this section, the physicochemical meaning and interpretation of these parameters are briefly introduced and discussed, and more detailed contents about these parameters can refer to some previous reviews.7,8,19,27 (1) Overpotential. HER can start at 0 V (vs. reversible hydrogen electrode, RHE) at standard conditions, and the extra potential relative to 0 V is called overpotential (η), a key and main parameter to evaluate the activity of catalysts. Three special η values at the current densities of 1 (η1), 10 (η10), and 100 (η100) mA cm−2 are usually calculated, and a smaller η value suggests a high activity. The η1 is often defined as the onset overpotential, which indicates the starting point of HER. The η10 is a metric related to solar fuel synthesis,52 which is always used for activity comparison. The η100 is another crucial criterion for the catalyst evaluation in practical applications. (2) Tafel slope and exchange current density. Tafel slope and exchange current density are two significant indicators to the kinetics behavior of HER, which can be gained from the Tafel equation (η = blog(j/j0), where b and j0 are the Tafel slope and exchange current density). Also, the Tafel slope is indicative of the reaction pathway and RDS, as stated above. Low b and high j0 values imply better HER activity. (3) Mass and specific activities. The mass activity (MA, normalized by the catalyst mass loading) and specific activity (SA, normalized by Brunauer–Emmett–Teller or electrochemical surface area) are two quantitative parameters to describe the catalytic reactivity of a catalyst. Considering that MA is important for practical applications and SA implies the catalyst's intrinsic activity, it is meaningful to compare the MA and SA. (4) Turnover frequency. The turnover frequency (TOF) represents the catalytic activity of individual active site, which is defined as the total number of molecules transformed into a desired product per catalytic site per time unit. Thus, TOF is another important parameter that reflects the intrinsic activity of an electrocatalyst, which is not dependent of the surface area. The relatively precise calculation of TOF values can be based on a well-known method reported by the Jaramillo group.53,54 (5) Faradaic efficiency. In HER, faradaic efficiency (FE) is defined as the ratio of the experimental to theoretical amount of hydrogen production. The theoretical amount of hydrogen generation can be calculated from galvanostatic or potentiostatic electrolysis, and the practical hydrogen generation can be detected by gas chromatography or a water displacement method. The FE of HER is generally reported to be close to 100%, suggesting the good selectivity for the HER catalysis. (6) Stability. Besides the activity, the stability is also a critical criterion to evaluate the practical value of an electrocatalyst. Two popular methods were always adopted to explore the long-term stability of catalysts, i.e., chronopotentiometry/chronoamperometry test and continuous cyclic voltammetry scan. Because these parameters are easily affected by some experimental conditions (e.g., substrate, mass loading, electrolyte, ink preparation), different catalysts in one study should be kept under the same experimental conditions for fair comparison. As a successful example, McCrory et al. proposed a benchmarking methodology for HER catalyst performance, which is of great significance for the development of standard electrochemical tests and evaluation protocols.55

3. Overview of metal oxide-based electrocatalysts for HER

As mentioned, metal oxides with diverse structures and compositions have been widely used in various fields including OER electrocatalysis. In view of the considerable OER performance of metal oxides, it is of great demand and significance to develop metal oxide-based HER catalysts to form efficient bifunctional catalysts for practical applications in overall water splitting. Especially in the last five years, substantial endeavors have been made to improve the HER catalytic performance of metal oxides by adopting multiple strategies, such as doping, oxygen-vacancy engineering, phase-structure engineering, strain engineering, morphology engineering, crystallinity engineering, valence regulation, composition optimization and hybridization. Some existing progress in catalytic activity and mechanism exploration has been achieved, and the developed metal oxide-based HER catalysts have shown to be promising alternatives to noble metal-based materials. According to structural features, the metal oxide-based HER electrocatalysts ever reported can be roughly classified into the six categories: single transition metal oxides, spinel oxides, perovskite oxides, metal (oxy)hydroxides, specially-structured metal oxides and oxide-containing hybrids. In the following section, the recent advances of these electrocatalysts are comprehensively reviewed and some illustrative studies are emphatically presented. In consideration of fair comparison and testing media difference, the HER electrocatalysts based on metal oxides under acidic and alkaline conditions will be discussed separately in each part.

3.1. Simple transition metal oxides

Simple transition metal oxides composed of earth-abundant elements (Ni, Co, Ti, Mo, W, Mn, Ce, etc.) are the most common among the family of metal oxides, such as NiO, CoO, TiO2, Ti2O3, MoO3, MoO2, WO3, WO2, MnO2, CeO2, which have been intensively studied as good candidates for HER electrocatalysis by various approaches. Before summarizing the recent development on simple transition metal oxides, here we list out the strategies adopted for enhancing their HER activities in both acidic and alkaline media. Therefore, this part will be elaborated according to five main strategies, i.e., oxygen-vacancy control, phase-structure engineering, non-metal doping, metal doping and morphology engineering.
3.1.1. Oxygen-vacancy control. To date, the most studied simple transition metal oxides for HER in acids are based on group IV and VI transition metals (e.g., Mo, W and Ti) since these metal oxides have good thermodynamical stability in acidic solutions. Specific examples include MoO2, MoO3, WO3 and TiO2, etc., but these pristine stoichiometric oxides usually show unfavorable acidic HER activity. The creation of oxygen vacancies (OVs) in stoichiometric oxides has been regarded as one of the most commonly used and efficient ways to boost intrinsic catalytic activity by modulating the electronic structure, conductivity, and the hydrogen adsorption energies.34,56–58 In this regard, Y. Li et al. prepared oxygen-defect WO2.9 as an efficient HER electrocatalyst in acidic media through a facile thermal treatment (Fig. 2a).59 The WO2.9 with tailored structure exhibits significantly enhanced acidic HER activity (η10 of 70 mV and Tafel slope of 50 mV dec−1) than pristine WO3 (Fig. 2b and c). Further DFT calculations indicate that the origin of the enhanced activity could be attributed to the optimal hydrogen adsorption energy (close to thermodynamic neutral, ΔGH* ≈ 0 eV) (Fig. 2d). On this basis, Zheng et al. also used a liquid exfoliation method to prepare a conductive WO3 nanosheets (NSs) with rich OVs, realizing better HER activity in acidic media (η10 of 38 mV and Tafel slope of 38 mV dec−1).60 First-principles calculations demonstrate that the gap states introduced by OVs make WO3 act as a degenerate semiconductor with high conductivity and desirable hydrogen adsorption free energy (Fig. 2e and f). Likewise, TiO2 can be activated towards acidic HER by oxygen vacancy engineering.61 Swaminathan et al. presented a electrochemical cathodization method to prepare defect-rich conducting oxide TiO1.23 with plentiful active sites for HER, which shows excellent HER catalytic performance in acidic media in terms of onset potential, Tafel slope and stability.61

Besides the acidic condition, engineering the OVs of simple transition metal oxides could also improve the catalytic HER performance in alkaline media. For example, Qiao's group used a surface strain engineering strategy to enhance the alkaline HER activity of CoO nanorods (NRs).62 Based on theoretical and experimental results, they demonstrated that the atomic and electronic structure properties can be optimized through the tensile strain with the creation of large quantities of OVs, and these OVs can facilitate water dissociation and weaken the hydrogen adsorption towards the optimum region (Fig. 3a–c). As a result, the surface-strained CoO NRs with rich OVs shows excellent HER activity in alkaline solution, close to the benchmark Pt/C catalyst (Fig. 3d and e). Later, the same group also synthesized NiO NRs with abundant OVs via a cation exchange method (Fig. 3f).63 On the basis of experimental studies and DFT calculations, the chemical and electronic property of NiO NRs is successfully optimized through oxygen-vacancy engineering; the OVs on the surface of NiO NRs remarkably enhance their electronic conduction and promote HER reaction kinetics simultaneously. The engineered NiO NRs exhibit efficient alkaline HER catalytic activity with a low η10 of 110 mV and Tafel slope of 100 mV dec−1 (Fig. 3g and h). Similar tactics with introducing OVs have been also employed to reform the TiO2, MoO3 and MnO2 oxides for enhanced alkaline HER activity.64–66 The successful synthesis of these metal oxide nanomaterials with abundant OVs paves a highly promising way for rationally fabricating highly efficient HER electrocatalysts.

3.1.2. Phase-structure engineering. In addition to the creation of oxygen vacancy, phase-structure engineering in the metal oxide itself can also boost the catalytic activity, which is reflected in a recent pioneering study by Li et al.67 By recrystallization on the substrates during epitaxial growth, three different Ti2O3 polymorphs, trigonal (denoted as α-Ti2O3), orthorhombic (denoted as o-Ti2O3), and cubic (denoted as γ-Ti2O3), were obtained (Fig. 4a and b). Electrochemical measurement reveals distinct activity difference, and γ-Ti2O3 exhibits the highest HER activity with smallest overpotential of 271 mV at 10 mA cm−2 in 0.5 M H2SO4 (Fig. 4c). Importantly, it was found that the HER activity of the Ti2O3 polymorphs is strongly correlated to their electronic structures. As shown in Fig. 4d, the overpotential of HER decreases with smaller charge-transfer energy (Δ) together with stronger Ti–O hybridization, suggesting the HER activity is highly enhanced by the electronic reconstructions. Thus, it is identified that the smallest charge-transfer energy in γ-Ti2O3 gives rise to the strongest hybridization of Ti 3d and O 2p orbitals, which results in the highest HER activity among Ti2O3 polymorphs. This work highlights the significance of the electronic structures on the acidic HER activity of oxide electrocatalysts, and also provides a strategy to achieve efficient oxide-based HER electrocatalysts by proper phase-structure engineering.

3.1.3. Non-metal doping. As stated in Section 3.1.1, Mo and W-based metal oxides with rich OVs has been developed as efficient HER electrocatalysts in acidic media via various fabrication techniques. Beyond that, non-metal doping (e.g., P and S) could also induce the generation of OVs for activity enhancement.68,69 For instance, Li et al. reported a facile and controllable two-step intercalation method for the synthesis of P doped MoO3−x NSs, which displays superior HER activity due to synergistic effect between the P dopants and the OVs.68 The relative low electronegativity of P element can help the adsorption and desorption of protons and then enhance the activity of the catalyst for acidic HER. Besides, Geng et al. reported that the acidic HER activity of MoO2 is much enhanced by S doping.69 The experimental results show that doping the S atoms into MoO2 could produce the OVs, induce the lattice defect, regulate charge transfer, and subsequently improve the HER.

Introducing foreign non-metal heteroatoms (e.g., N, F, S) could also improve the alkaline HER performance via adjusting the electronic structures.70–72 For example, Zhang and co-workers used N atoms to modulate electrochemical adsorption sites on the surface of NiO to achieve enhanced alkaline HER activity.70 Based on theoretical and experimental results, the surface charge redistribution after N modification can simultaneously facilitate the initial water dissociation, the Had on low coordinated oxygen (LCO) sites and the desorption of OHad on nickel sites, thus accelerating the overall hydrogen evolution process (Fig. 5a and b). In another recent study, Zhong et al. reported the halogen fluorine (F) anion doping strategy to enhance the alkaline HER activity of CoO.71 F-anion doping at O2− sites induces the charge redistribution of neighboring Co sites, facilitates water dissociation and weakens hydrogen adsorption, enabling the optimal states for the HER (Fig. 5c). In addition to the electronic effect, the hetero non-metal doping can tune the crystallinity of metal oxides. In this respect, Yu et al. found that incorporation of electronegative S into crystalline cobalt oxide can lead to the formation of disordered structure (Fig. 5d).72 Compared with its crystalline form, the disorder in S–CoOx catalyst increases low oxygen coordination and rich defect sites, endowing S–CoOx with a superior catalytic activity for both HER and OER in alkaline media.

In short, hetero non-metal atoms (e.g., N, F, P, S) doping is a facile and efficient approach to boost the HER performance of transition metal oxides in both acidic and alkaline media. The intrinsic reasons and beneficial factors for selecting non-metal atom doping can be summarized as follows: (1) facile operation without complicated fabrication process; (2) generating OVs as HER active sites; (3) enhancing electronic conductivity for promoted charge transfer; (4) optimizing the electronic structure and modulating the adsorption and dissociation of reactants. In acidic condition, the modulated electronic structure of metal oxides can optimize the ΔGH* for HER. However, in alkaline media, the surface charge redistribution after non-metal atom modulation not only facilitates ΔGH* but also promotes the initial water dissociation and ΔGOH*, synergistically accelerating the overall alkaline HER process. These considerations will be of great importance for the development of low-cost and highly active HER electrocatalysts in the future.

3.1.4. Metal doping. In some cases, metal oxides consisting of a suitable combination of transition metals (e.g., binary/ternary metal oxides) can possess superior catalytic performance to single metal counterparts.73,74 Metal doping is an effective and easy way to introduce the second metals. For acidic HER, a recent systematic study carried out by Zhong et al. on binary metal oxides showed that Mo doping can significantly boost the HER activity of W18O49.75 Based on DFT calculations, the remarkably enhanced acidic HER activity of W18O49 after Mo doping is because of the ability of Mo dopant to increase the number of active sites, consequently resulting in optimal hydrogen adsorption on the active sites due to electronic and geometric modulation (Fig. 6a and b). Likewise, Zeng et al. recently reported an efficient strategy to modulate the electron density of MoO2 by Ni doping to accelerate HER in acid.76 DFT calculations confirmed that Ni doping can lead to electron-deficiency on neighboring O sites, which is induced to adsorb hydrogen and then increase the surface hydrogen coverage (Fig. 6c–e). Increasing the surface hydrogen coverage suggests a larger number of effective active sites and a higher probability for HER. The strategy of adjusting the electron density and hydrogen adsorption of the catalytic sites could provide valuable insights for the rational design of highly efficient catalysts for HER.

Apart from Mo/W-based metal oxides, other transition metal oxides can be designed as efficient alkaline HER electrocatalysts via proper metal doing. Very recently, Jiang et al. reported Co dopant into CeO2 NSs can promote HER, due to the optimized hydrogen binding/water dissociation and increased active sites.77 Ternary metal oxides manifest great potential for the HER as well. As one representative study, the Qiao group utilized a dual-elemental (Ni,Zn) doping strategy to change the inert CoO oxide into a highly active alkaline HER electrocatalyst (Fig. 7a and b).78 Experimental and theoretical results demonstrated that the high activity is ascribed to Ni,Zn dual doping-induced modulation of surface and bulk electronic structure; that is, the Ni dopant provides an ideal surface electronic environment for hydrogen intermediate binding (Fig. 7c), while Zn dopants modulate the bulk electronic structure to boost electrical conduction (Fig. 7d). Besides, few other multi-metal simple oxides, such as Ru1/TiO2,79 NiFeOx,80 CoIIFe-ONC,81 Ir0.8Ru0.2Oy,82 Ni2Fe1–Mo,83 CoFeNi–O84 were also developed as efficient HER electrocatalysts in alkaline media.

3.1.5. Morphology engineering. Other than the above strategies for tuning the intrinsic activity, engineering the morphology of the metal oxides (e.g., nano-structure or pore-structure) by producing more active sites is another effective strategy for performance enhancement.66,85–89 One typical study is from the Zhang group, who synthesized ultrathin δ-MnO2 NSs with only two monolayer thickness on a nickel foam (NF) using an in situ hydrothermal method (Fig. 8a).66 The MnO2 NSs have good electrocatalytic activity toward HER in alkaline, which is attributed to its nanosheet nature containing abundant O vacancies as well as a large number of Mn3+ active sites. Likewise, the Cui group also grew ultrathin porous MoO2 NSs directly on a NF via a wet-chemical route (Fig. 8b), exhibiting much higher alkaline HER activity than conventional compact MoO2.85 In electrocatalysis, the porous structure is beneficial because not only does it offer more catalytic active sites, but it also provides efficient pathways for reactant transport, electrolyte transfer, and gas diffusion.34,86,87 Later, Luo et al. synthesized mesoporous MoO3−x with large accessible pores (20–40 nm) by an inverse micelle-template method (Fig. 8c), which are able to facilitate mass transport and charge transfer during HER.88 As a result, the mesoporous MoO3−x displays significantly enhanced HER activity in alkaline solution relative to the commercial samples. Besides the meso-porosity, Haque et al. later prepared crystalline 2D MoO3 with ordered hexagonal pores (Crys-AMO) as efficient HER catalyst with only 138 mV at 10 mA cm−2 in 0.1 M KOH.89 These ordered intracrystalline pores acting like 1D hexagonal tunnels in Crys-AMO are particularly useful, allowing easy migration of water molecules and gaseous species (Fig. 8d).

3.2. Spinel oxides

Spinel-type metal oxides can be depicted with the general formula of AB2O4, where A is briefly a divalent transition metal ion at a tetrahedral site and B is a trivalent transition metal ion occupying an octahedral site (Fig. 9a).73 In the spine structure, A and B sites can be occupied by the same elements (e.g., Co3O4) or different elements (e.g., NiCo2O4). Owing to easy synthesis, low cost and tunable composition, spinel oxides have attracted considerable interest for various applications in oxygen electrocatalysis,90 photocatalysis,91 lithium batteries,92 supercapacitors,93etc. In the alkaline HER electrocatalysis, some spinel oxides with desirable performance were also designed and synthesized, which will be described in the following subsections according to two spinel compositions of Co3O4 and AB2O4 (A ≠ B).

3.2.1. Co3O4 spinel. Co3O4 is the most common among the spinel oxides, and a few efforts have been made to achieve desirable Co3O4-based HER electrocatalysts in alkaline media. Recently, Zhu et al. fabricated hollow Co3O4 microtube arrays (Co3O4-MTA) with hierarchical porosity (Fig. 9b) through an electrochemical sacrificial-template method.94 Featuring hollow interior and porous shells, Co3O4-MTA exhibits superb alkaline activity for HER with low onset overpotentials and high current densities (Fig. 9c). In another example reported recently, Xiao et al. used a plasma-assisted approach (Fig. 9d) to fill the in situ-generated oxygen vacancies of Co3O4 with P atoms to enhance the HER activity.95 Theoretical calculations proved that the improved electrical conductivity and intermediate binding by P filling contributed to the significant activity enhancement (Fig. 9e and f). These two studies suggest that the HER activity of Co3O4 in alkaline media can be improved by both intrinsic and extrinsic strategies.
3.2.2. AB2O4 spinels. Besides Co3O4, other AB2O4 spinel oxides with different A and B elements (e.g., NiCo2O4) were also intensively studied in HER electrocatalysis.96–99 For example, Gao et al. prepared hierarchical NiCo2O4 hollow microcuboids constructed by 1D porous nanowire subunits (Fig. 10a) via a thermally driven conversion process.96 Benefiting from these unique 3D hierarchical structures, the HER kinetics of NiCo2O4 is significantly promoted. Later, Liu et al. found that the concentration of OVs can greatly affect the HER activity of NiCo2O4.97 Based on theoretical results, the increased OVs reduce the adsorption energy and dissociation energy barrier of H2O molecules on the catalyst surface, thus leading to the activity enhancement of NiCo2O4 toward alkaline HER. Combining the benefits of morphology and OVs, Peng et al. developed an “adsorption–calcination–reduction” strategy to synthesize a series of spinel oxides (NiCo2O4, CoMn2O4 and NiMn2O4) with unique necklace-like multishelled hollow structure and rich OVs (Fig. 10b and c).98 Compared with the pristine NiCo2O4, the reduced sample shows better HER activity with η10 of 135 mV and Tafel slope of 52 mV dec−1 in 1 M KOH. In addition, the HER catalytic ability of spinel oxides can be also boosted by a conventional doping strategy. Very recently, the Wang group fabricated zirconium (Zr) regulated CoFe2O4 (CoFeZr oxides) nanosheets on 3D NF as highly efficient HER electrocatalysts through one-step solvothermal method (Fig. 10d and e).99 They found that the incorporation of Zr into CoFe2O4 can adjust the nanosheet morphology by generating more active sites and electronic structure for enhancing the intrinsic activity of active sites. Moreover, several other types of spinel oxides with different compositions, e.g., CuCo2O4 NSs,100 2D ZnCo2O4,101 Ce–MnCo2O4,102 were also reported to show encouraging HER catalytic performance in alkaline solutions.

3.3. Perovskite oxides

Perovskite oxides are a family of functional metal oxides with a general formula of ABO3, where larger A-site cations are commonly rare-earth or alkaline earth metals with 12-fold oxygen coordination, and the smaller B-site cations are commonly transition metals with 6-fold oxygen coordination (Fig. 11a). More than 90% of the elements in the Periodic Table can be doped into the A, B or O sites, leading to a large family of oxides with highly diversified properties. In addition to the primary crystal structure with ideal cubic symmetry, other multiple structures can be also formed in perovskite oxides. By tailoring the compositions and structures of the perovskite oxides, a variety of interesting physical properties, e.g., ferroelectric, pyroelectric, piezoelectric, electrical, optical, magnetic, catalytic, photovoltaic, mixed conducting and superconducting properties, can be created, which result in a wide range of applications that were explored in the last few decades.32–34,86,103–106 However, in the field of HER electrocatalysis, perovskite oxides were rarely studied in the past. Although tentative research on the use of A-site deficient Sr1−xNbO3−δ to catalyze HER in strong acids in the 1990s were reported, the HER activity remained very low.107,108 More recently (especially in the past four years), substantial efforts were devoted to improving the HER catalytic performance of perovskite oxides via multi-strategy design, and some significant advancements have been achieved. In this section, the representative development of HER electrocatalysts under alkaline conditions based on three types of perovskite oxides (i.e., simple perovskites, double perovskites and Ruddlesden–Popper-type oxides) will be reviewed, and designed strategies are highlighted.

3.3.1. Simple perovskites. One pioneering study by Xu et al. reported an A-site praseodymium (Pr)-doped Pr0.5(Ba0.5Sr0.5)0.5Co0.8Fe0.2O3−δ (Pr0.5BSCF) perovskite with cubic structure as an active and stable electrocatalyst for HER in the alkaline media.109 Because of multiple beneficial factors induced by Pr-doping, Pr0.5BSCF shows a significant enhancement of HER catalytic activity with a low overpotential of 237 mV to approach 10 mA cm−2 as compared to the undoped Ba0.5Sr0.5Co0.8Fe0.2O3−δ (BSCF) perovskite (Fig. 11b). Besides the traditional cation doping, anion-doping approach could also promote catalytic performance. For example, a trace of fluorine (F) dopants in La0.5Ba0.25Sr0.25CoO3 helps upshift the O 2p-band center and activate the redox capability of the lattice O, and thereby improve the alkaline HER activity.110 Perovskite oxides usually have a low specific surface area, which is due to high calcinations temperature required for the phase formation, resulting in low mass activity for electrocatalysis.34,86,111 Thus, the preparation of nanostructured perovskites by increasing the number of active sites could be an effective way to boost the performance of the catalysts. In our previous work, we prepared a SrNb0.1Co0.7Fe0.2O3−δ perovskite nanorod (SNCF-NR) by a facile electrospinning method, displaying excellent HER activity and stability in the base electrolyte (Fig. 11c and d).112 The same method was also used to synthesize La0.5(Ba0.4Sr0.4Ca0.2)0.5Co0.8Fe0.2O3−δ (LBSCCF) perovskite for HER catalysis.113 In another example reported recently by our group, a LaFeO3 perovskite with 3D ordered macroporous (3DOM) structure (3DOM-LF) was fabricated through a colloidal crystal template method, exhibiting approximately fourfold activity enhancement for alkaline HER compared to its bulk LF counterpart (Fig. 11e and f).114 Such 3DOM structure generates increased number of active sites and facilitates mass transfer; consequently, the activity and durability are improved.
3.3.2. Double perovskites. Double perovskite oxides with a formula of AA′B2O5+δ have a layered structure with alternating layers of |AOδ|BO2|A′O|BO2, where generally A is a large-radii alkaline-earth metal element (e.g., Ba), A′ is a small-radii lanthanide element (e.g., La, Pr, Nd, Gd), B is transition metal element, and the oxygen vacancies (OVs) are located in the AO layer.115,116 Double perovskites feature some unique electronic structures that are not usually found in single perovskites,115,116 which may generate several benefits to the HER electrocatalysis. In this respect, Guan et al. carried out a systematic study on A-site ordered RBaCo2O5.5+δ (R = lanthanides) double perovskites for alkaline HER electrocatalysis.117 It was observed that the OVs in the lanthanide plane of RBaCo2O5.5+δ are ordered for δ = 0 and δ = 0.25 while no vacancy ordering exists for δ = 0.37 and δ = 0.5 (Fig. 12a). Based on computational results, two active sites on RBaCo2O5.5+δ surfaces, namely, the oxygen vacancies at Co3+pyr sites and O 2p ligand holes of oxygen atoms from the Co4+oct clusters, can be active sites for H2O adsorption and H2 desorption, respectively (Fig. 12b and c). Benefiting from such synergy of the two anionic active sites, the experimentally synthesized (Gd0.5La0.5)BaCo2O5.75 (Gd0.5; δ = 0.25) exhibits the highest HER activity among the RBaCo2O5.5+δ system. Later, it was also proposed that the A-site ionic electronegativity (AIE) can serve as an efficient unifying descriptor to predict the HER activities of 13 cobalt-based perovskites, and found (Gd0.5La0.5)BaCo2O5.5+δ with an AIE value of ∼2.33 possesses the highest activity (Fig. 12d).118 The peak HER activity at a moderate AIE value of ∼2.33 can be associated with the optimal electronic states of active B-sites via an inductive effect in perovskite structure (Fig. 12e). In addition, the Ciucci group reported another promising double perovskite oxide, i.e., NdBaMn2O5.5, as a highly efficient HER electrocatalyst, which can be understood based on multiple beneficial factors associated with electronic structures.119

3.3.3. Ruddlesden–Popper-type oxides. Layered Ruddlesden–Popper (RP) type oxides (formula of An+1BnO3n+1 or equivalently [AO](ABO3±δ)n), wherein n ABO3 perovskite layers are sandwiched between two AO rock-salt layers, have received considerable attention due to their chemical flexibility, layered structure, wide element accommodation, and labile lattice.34,120–122 However, the HER electrocatalysts based on RP oxides were never studied previously until a most recent report by our group.123 In this representative work, RP Sr2RuO4 with a space group of I4/mmm was prepared by a facile and mature solid-phase reaction method (Fig. 13a). Sr2RuO4 oxide presents an ultrahigh HER activity in alkaline media with an extremely low overpotential of 61 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and Tafel slope of 51 mV dec−1 (Fig. 13b and c). By theoretical calculations, the excellent HER activity of Sr2RuO4 was mainly attributed to an unusual synergistic effect in the layered structure, whereby the (001) SrO-terminated surface cleaved in rock-salt layer facilitates a barrier-free water dissociation while the active apical oxygen site in the perovskite layer promotes favorable hydrogen adsorption and evolution (Fig. 13d and e). This seminal study opens a new avenue for the development of high-performance HER electrocatalysts through constructing synergistic active sites in unique single-phase metal oxides. One direction of future research is to develop RP-type oxides composed of non-noble transition metals as potential catalysts for the HER, since Ru is a noble metal that can impose limitations on practical applications.

3.4. Metal (oxy)hydroxides

Transition metal (oxy)hydroxides, including the special class of layered double hydroxides (LDHs), are layered metal oxides, which are featured with low cost, high specific surface area, good alkaline stability and distinctive electron distribution.19,73,124 Pristine metal (oxy)hydroxides are generally deemed as favorable OER electrocatalysts, but usually have low activity for HER because of inappropriate hydrogen adsorption energies.38,47,62,125 Considering that metal (oxy)hydroxides can effectively dissociate water, hybridizing them with other electroactive materials is an efficient approach to achieve high-performance alkaline HER electrocatalysts, which will be discussed in Section 3.6.2 below. Although studies on pure metal (oxy)hydroxides for HER are very limited, few metal (oxy)hydroxides with promising alkaline HER activity were still reported. Very recently, Fe-substituted hierarchical VOOH hollow spheres assembled by 2D flake with different doping ratio were synthesized through a one-pot hydrothermal process, and the optimized VOOH–3Fe shows a low overpotential of 93 mV to afford 10 mA cm−2 in alkaline media (Fig. 14a).125 According to DFT calculations, the Fe site of the Fe-doped VOOH generates optimal binding energies compared to that of V sites before and after Fe doping (Fig. 14b), which accounts for the enhanced activity. Chen et al. demonstrated a strategy to accelerate the hydrogen evolution kinetics of the NiFe-LDH by partially substituting Fe atoms with Ru.126 In 1 M KOH solution, the as-synthesized Ru-doped NiFe-LDH (NiFeRu-LDH) nanosheets exhibit excellent HER activity with an overpotential of 29 mV at 10 mA cm−2, close to the benchmark Pt/C catalyst (Fig. 14c). Theoretical results reveal that the dopant of Ru atoms into NiFe-LDH can efficiently reduce energy barrier of the Volmer step, eventually accelerating its HER kinetics (Fig. 14d). Likewise, Fanet et al. reported a bifunctional catalyst by doping atomic Ir into the NiCo LDH using a facile atmospheric solvothermal method and subsequently spontaneous galvanic displacement.127 The as-prepared Ir–NiCo LDH displays excellent catalytic properties with a HER overpotential of 21 mV at 10 mA cm−2 in 1 M KOH electrolyte. Li et al. also used the anionic S doping to boost the HER activity of CoOOH, resulting from the modified t2g orbital state, enhanced electrical conductivity and reduced reaction barriers.128 Moreover, the in situ formed new components during the electrochemical process could further improve the HER performance of metal (oxy)hydroxides. For instance, the Edvinsson group reported the dynamically self-optimized NiFe layered double hydroxide (DSO NiFeLDH) nanosheets as highly active HER electrocatalysts in alkaline media.129 By in situ Raman spectra (Fig. 14e and f), the dynamically formed species were identified and the real HER mechanism of DSO NiFe LDH was uncovered: the synergetic effect of NiFe compounds serves to optimize the reaction route by promoting water dissociation on the formed FeOOH and enhancing the bond association with Had–NiO (Fig. 14g). These studies suggest that the inert transition metal (oxy)hydroxides can be activated for HER by proper approaches, e.g., doping and electrochemistry-induced surface reconstruction.

3.5. Specially-structured oxides

In addition to the common metal oxides stated above, some other types of metal oxides with special crystal structures were also reported to show desirable catalytic activity towards HER in both acidic and alkaline media. Very recently, the Li group firstly studied the acidic HER performance of a Pt-containing polyoxometalate (PtW6O24), which is constructed by a {PtO6} octahedron connected to six {WO6} octahedra in an edge-sharing mode (Fig. 15a).130 The PtW6O24 shows an ultrahigh HER activity in acidic media with a very low overpotential of 22 mV at 10 mA cm−2, better than that of commercial 20% Pt/C (Fig. 15b). The in situ synchrotron radiation experiments (Fig. 15c) and DFT calculations (Fig. 15d and e) demonstrated that the elongated Pt–O bond acts as the active site during the HER process, which accelerates the coupling of proton and electron and the rapid release of H2. Not limited to this example, some oxides with special structures, such as layered misfit Ca3Co4O9,131 and layered V10O24·nH2O nanofiber,132 were investigated as promising candidates for HER in acid as well.

Besides acidic HER, other types of special oxides, like crystalline cobalt phosphate Co3(OH)2(HPO4)2,133 nickel phosphate Ni11(HPO3)8(OH)6,134 helical cobalt borophosphate LiCo(H2O)2[BP2O8]·H2O,135 monoclinic MnMoO4136 and β-CoMoO4 nanoplate137 were studied as efficient alkaline HER electrocatalysts after adopting some effective strategies. For example, crystalline Co3(OH)2(HPO4)2 was explored as a precursor for alkaline HER, exhibiting remarkable catalytic activity due to the formation of in situ amorphous CoOx(OH) phase under electrochemical conditions.133 Electrochemically-inert MnMoO4 was successfully transformed into highly active alkaline HER electroctalyst through hydrogenation, which is ascribed to the formation of amorphous phase and enlarged electrochemically active surface area.136 Moreover, Zhao et al. reported P doped monoclinic β-CoMoO4 nanoplate as an efficient and stable HER electrocatalyst in alkaline electrolytes, and found both (P–O1−) and Co3+–OH− are prospective surface active sites for the HER via combined computational and in situ spectroscopic techniques.137 We note that the exploitation of specially-structured oxides as the HER electrocatalysts is still in its early stage, but the above limited studies highlight the key roles of unique structure design and proper strategy adoption for boosting the HER performance.

3.6. Oxide-containing hybrids

3.6.1. Mixed-metal-oxide composites. Some mixed metal oxides containing two or more oxide components were also reported for HER electrocatalysis in view of the expectancy of advantageous properties from multiple components.138–142 One typical example is that of Liu et al. who adopted a simple wet chemistry method to prepare a hybrid porous nanosheet arrays composed of strongly coupled RuO2 and NiO nanoparticles (Fig. 16a).138 The resulting RuO2/NiO exhibits exceptional alkaline HER activity, comparable to the benchmark Pt catalyst (Fig. 16b). They found that the excellent HER activity is closely related to the potential-induced interfacial synergy between the RuO2 and NiO: NiO promotes water dissociation and RuO2-derived Ru is active for hydrogen adsorption and evolution (Fig. 16c). A similar phenomenon in mixed TiO2/Co2P4O12 metal oxides was also reported by Lv et al.139 During the alkaline HER process, TiO2 was found to accelerate the surface reduction of Co2P4O12 to metallic Co, and enhanced performance is associated with the synergetic effect between Co, Co2P4O12, and TiO2. Some other catalyst systems composed of mixed metal oxides for alkaline HER were also available in recent reports, e.g., Co–Cu–W NW,140 CuCoO NW,141 MoO2/Fe1.89Mo4.11O7,142 NiO/Co3O4,143 CoO/Fe3O4,144 TiO2/NiCo2O4145 and Ni3(VO4)2/NiCo2O4.146

3.6.2. Hybridizing with other electroactive materials. In recent years, heterostructure materials have become a prominent family of electrocatalysts due to their unique physicochemical properties.19,29 The construction of heterostructure is considered as an important approach to promote the advancement of catalysts with defined functions. For the HER process, the unique features of heterostructure catalysts can lead to improved or completely novel catalytic performance. Thus, constructing heterostructures by hybridizing metal (hydr)oxides with other electroactive materials (e.g. metals/alloys and metal phosphides/sulfides/selenides/carbides/nitrides) is an advisable and effective way to enhance the HER catalytic performance of bare metal (hydr)oxides.7–10,19,27,29,36–38 In this section, we will discuss three beneficial effects of heterostructure catalysts in promoting HER process, including synergistic effect, electronic effect and support effect. A few representative examples are described in detail.
Synergistic effect. It has been demonstrated that metal (hydr)oxides can serve as effective promoters for water dissociation.19,29 Therefore, metal (hydr)oxides can effectively facilitate the Volmer step (i.e., water dissociation) during alkaline HER process. Hybridizing metal (hydr)oxides with those conventional HER electrocatalysts is an efficient way to obtain high-performance electrocatalysts for the alkaline HER. Successful application of such synergistic hybrids has been extensively reported. In this regard, one pioneering study by the Markovic group designed a synergistic hybrid catalyst for alkaline HER via coupling Ni(OH)2 with Pt.38 More specifically, they electrodeposited a water dissociation promoter, namely Li+-intercalated Ni(OH)2, onto Pt to boost the initial water dissociation in alkaline media and the production of hydrogen intermediates, which are then adsorbed on the Pt surface to recombine into molecular hydrogen (Fig. 17a). As a result, the as-obtained hybrid catalyst can increase the Pt activity by about eight folds through this synergy (Fig. 17b). Following this early work, various noble metal/metal (hydr)oxide systems, e.g., Pt NWs/SL/Ni(OH)2,147 Pt/Co(OH)2,148 Ru/(Fe,Ni)(OH)2,149 Ru/CoO,150 Ru/SnO2,151 Ag/Cu2O,152 have been studied as excellent catalysts for the alkaline HER. Such hybrids can take advantage of the high catalytic activities of noble metals and favorable water dissociation ability of metal (hydr)oxides.

Hybridizing metal (hydr)oxides with non-noble metal compounds (e.g., transition metal sulfides) can produce efficient and low-cost alkaline HER electrocatalysts. As a typical example, Hu et al. assembled a MoS2/NiCo-LDH hybrid as a highly efficient electrocatalysts for HER in alkaline electrolytes.153 The MoS2/NiCo-LDH hybrid exhibits high intrinsic activity than bare MoS2, suggesting the promoting role of NiCo-LDH (Fig. 17c). Benefiting from the desirable heterostructure, the MoS2/NiCo-LDH interface synergistically favor the chemisorption of H (on MoS2) and OH (on LDH), and can thus effectively accelerate the water dissociation step and the overall HER catalysis (Fig. 17d and e). Apart from the transition metal sulfides, other noble metal-free electroactive materials can also realize promoted HER activities in the alkaline environment when coupling with metal (hydr)oxides, such as Ni-Laser (Ni/NiO),154 Ni/Mn3O4,155 Ni/LixNiO,156 e-LSTN,157 Co NSNTs/TiO2 NDs,158 MoNi4/MoO2,159 NiCu/NiFeOx,160 Co9S8/TiO2,161 NiS2/Ni(OH)2,162 Co0.85Se/NiFe-LDH,163 and Cu3N/CuO.164 The combination may vary from one system to another, but the essence of the synergistic effect for alkaline HER is similar. To be specific, metal (hydr)oxides facilitate water dissociation, and electroactive material serves as the catalysts for H* adsorption and H2 evolution.


Electronic effect. The presence of heterointerfaces in the hybrid catalysts can tailor the electronic structure of active catalysts, consequently offering a moderate binding energy of hydrogen for improved HER catalytic performance in acid.29 Very recently, Xie et al. prepared a Pt–WO3 hybrid catalyst with excellent acidic HER activity close to that of commercial Pt/C (Fig. 18a).165 By in situ Raman spectroscopy and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy characterization, the electrochemically driven in situ phase transition from WO3 to HxWO3 was identified and the promoting mechanism of Pt/WO3 was uncovered. As schematically shown in Fig. 18b, in situ formed HxWO3 promotes electron transfer during HER process and accelerate HER kinetics by providing the hydrogen transfer pathway from HxWO3 onto Pt. Jiang et al. also reported a Pt/MoO2 heterostructure that possesses Pt-like HER activity in acidic solution with an overpotential of 20 mV at 10 mA cm−2.166 In addition to Pt metal, other materials, such as polyaniline, metal sulfides, etc., have been applied in constructing heterostructure catalysts for the acidic HER.167,168 For instance, Wang's group found that the proper d–π interfacial electronic coupling of WO3 with polyaniline (PANI) optimizes the electronic structure of the PANI surface.167 Theoretical calculations demonstrated that the antibonding states move lower with a higher occupancy, which leads to a weaker interaction between adsorbate hydrogen and catalyst surface and thereby triggers the HER activity on the inert PANI surface (Fig. 18c–f). As a result, the WO3/PANI shows excellent electrocatalytic HER activity with low overpotential of 74 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and small Tafel slope of 46 mV dec−1 in 0.5 M H2SO4. Besides, an efficient self-standing HER catalyst in acid has been developed by coating CuS/NiCo2O4 on a carbon fiber paper (CFP).168 Remarkably, the as-obtained CuS/NiCo2O4/CFP hybrid maintains excellent durability with at least 50 h operation under acidic conditions. The great stability of CuS/NiCo2O4/CFP may be ascribed to the heterostructure with strong electronic coupling, which ameliorate the negative effect of the irreversible dissolution for spinel NiCo2O4 oxide under the corrosive acidic conditions.

In another recent work, a porous nanospindle composed of carbon-encapsulated MoO2–FeP heterojunction (FeP/MoO2C) is proposed as a robust HER electrocatalyst in alkaline media.170 By combined X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy analysis and theoretical calculations, the electron transfer from MoO2 to FeP at the interfaces results in electron accumulation on FeP, which favors the optimization of H2O and H* absorption energies for alkaline HER (Fig. 19e–g). Support effect. Constructing hybrid can not only tune the electron structure by electron transfer on the hetero-interface, but also bring about some enhanced properties (e.g., more exposed active sites, higher electronic conductivity and faster mass transfer), which are beneficial for the HER electrocatalysis. Such an effect can be called as “support effect”. Functional carbon materials usually act as good conductive supports for well-dispersed nanostructures to prevent their aggregation and provide more exposed surface area. Thus, the construction of metal oxide/functional carbon hybrid to expose more active sites has been an effective approach for boosting HER. For example, Wu et al. synthesized metallic WO2–carbon mesoporous nanowires with high concentration OVs (MWCMNs) via calcination of inorganic/organic WO3–ethylenediamine hybrid precursors (Fig. 20a).179 The MWCMNs are endowed with three characteristics: metallic nature (Fig. 20b), mesoporous structure (Fig. 20c) and the in situ-formed carbon as rigid substrate and electron mobility promoter. Experimental and theoretical data reveal that the unusual electronic structure and great number of active sites induced by the mesoporous carbon are key factors for excellent activity of MWCMNs in acid (Fig. 20d). Likewise, other hybrid catalysts with functional carbon component have been also reported, such as WO3−x/CNFs,180 WO3/NPRGO,181 WO2/C3N4182 and NiCo2O4/rGO.183 In addition to functional carbon, some other materials with high surface area (e.g., metal–organic framework (MOF)) can be hybridized with metal oxides as promising HER electrocatalysts. Recently, Zeng et al. used a one-pot hydrothermal method for the preparation of Co3O4@Co-MOF as an efficient electrocatalyst for overall water splitting (Fig. 20e).184 The uniformly distributed Co3O4 nanocubes supported on a high-surface-area Co-MOF (Fig. 20f) enable the full use of active sites of electrocatalyst, which results in high catalytic activity of Co3O4@Co-MOF hybrid.

3.7. Summary

In summary, various types of metal oxides for HER in both acidic and alkaline media as reported in the literature have been reviewed in this section. These oxides have their own advantages and disadvantages. Among the single-phase oxides, the simple metal oxides are the most studied materials and extensively explored as highly active electrocatalysts for both acidic and alkaline HER by various strategies. However, from the viewpoint of material design, simple metal oxides have disadvantages of relatively poor tunability in the composition and structure. Acidic HER electrocatalysts are mainly based on Mo and W elements, and alkaline HER electrocatalysts are basically composed of Co, Ni, Fe, Mn elements. By virtue of the compositional and structural flexibility, quite a few of spinel and perovskite oxides with desirable properties have been designed and synthesized as efficient HER electrocatalysts in alkaline media. Nonetheless, most spinel and perovskite oxides are not stable in acidic solutions, consequently there is nearly no report about spinel and perovskite oxides for acidic HER electrocatalysis. Metal (oxy)hydroxides also suffer from the poor stability in acidic environment. In addition, some pure metal (oxy)hydroxides and specially-structured oxides with unique properties show great potential in HER electrocatalysis, but so far the study on this topic is very limited. With regards to oxide-containing hybrids, various heterostructure materials display their distinct HER catalytic characteristics, which comes from the sum of the interaction contributed by individual components, leading to higher activity and better stability. There is no doubt that developing rational hybrid catalysts is an effective approach to improve the HER performance of single-phase oxides. However, the fabrication processes for these hybrids are usually complicated. Therefore, in terms of the industrial large-scale application, it is expected to realize the facile synthesis of metal oxide-based catalysts with high catalytic activity and good stability.

4. Conclusion and perspectives

The development of high-performance and cost-effective electrocatalysts for HER is crucial for the sustainable production of hydrogen as an alternative clean fuel and energy carrier. Over the past few years, continuous breakthroughs have been achieved in developing metal oxide-based electrocatalysts towards efficient electrochemical HER, reviving interest and challenging the traditional view of HER-inactive metal oxides by turning them into promising HER electrocatalysts. We hope that this timely and comprehensive overview of metal oxide-based electrocatalysts for HER will stimulate further research interest in this area. In this review, we have presented recent advances and understanding in the design of efficient HER electrocatalysts in both acidic and alkaline media based on various types of metal oxides such as single transition metal oxides, spinel oxides, perovskite oxides, metal (oxy)hydroxides and specially-structured oxides, as exhaustively listed in Tables 1 and 2, respectively. In addition, hybrids comprising metal oxides and other electroactive materials (X) have been widely reported as a family of high-performance HER electrocatalysts due to synergistic effect, electronic effect and support effect. Tables 3 and 4 lists nearly all “metal oxide + X” hybrid catalysts for HER in acidic and alkaline media where “X” refers to metal, alloy, metal phosphides, metal sulfide, metal selenide, metal nitride, metal carbide, metal (hydr)oxide, functional carbon, metal–organic framework (MOF), conductive polymer and metal phosphate. Among these reported electrocatalysts, some show close activity to the benchmark Pt catalyst. Specifically, strategies to boost the HER electrocatalytic performance of metal oxides by enhancing electronic conductivity, optimizing the electronic structure and increasing catalytically active sites, are highlighted. The designed effective strategies introduced in this review include doping, oxygen-vacancy control, phase-structure engineering, strain engineering, morphology engineering, crystallinity engineering, valence regulation, composition optimization, hybridization, etc.

(1) Exploiting new materials

Requirements for ideal catalysts. Despite the great promise of metal oxides for HER, the catalytic activity is still not satisfactory and very few of them can possess comparable activity to noble-metal Pt. Therefore, the exploration of new metal oxide-based electrocatalysts with better performance remains at the heart of future research. Besides developing new catalysts with high performance (including activity and stability), the cost of the catalysts should be kept in mind as well, because high cost will be prohibitive for wide-scale fabrication and will increase the overall price of hydrogen products. Catalysts composed of non-noble-metals prepared by the facile synthetic methods are desired. In addition, good electrical conductivity of the metal oxides themselves is also vital for high-efficiency catalysis considering that HER is an electrocatalytic process which requires an efficient flow of electrons through the electrode to produce high currents.86,288,289 In short, an ideal metal oxide-based HER electrocatalyst should meet several requirements: (a) high intrinsic electrical conductivity; (b) low-cost compositions and facile fabrication process; (c) HER activity comparable or superior to benchmark Pt; (d) robust stability during prolonged HER operation; (e) scalability for the mass commercialization. However, up to now, none of the reported metal oxide-based HER electrocatalysts possess all the above criteria; more efforts are hence required in the future.
Stability issues. Stability is quite a critical criterion to evaluate the practical value of an electrocatalyst in a real-life water splitting device. In the laboratorial studies, the short-term stability of a catalyst is commonly explored by chronoamperometric/chronopotentiometric experiments or accelerated durability tests. Then, the activity profile of the catalyst is indicative of its stability. However, the stable activity obtained in this way does not necessarily represent the real-world stability of the catalysts in consideration of possible compositional and structural change on the surface of catalysts while maintaining the same level of activity. For metal oxides, they are usually unstable due to serious metal ions dissolution in acidic solution. The mass variation of metal oxides during electrocatalysis is a significant piece of information associated with their stability, and the electrochemical quartz crystal microbalance (eQCM with a detection limit of 1 ng) is a powerful tool to detect in situ the possible mass change in the electrocatalysis process.290,291 Besides, the elemental analysis by inductively coupled plasma (ICP) spectrometry is also reliable for detecting the mass change of the catalysts although it is normally not adaptable for in situ monitoring.292 In short, the study on the stability of metal oxide-based HER catalysts for future research can be summarized as follows: (a) improving the stability of metal oxides in acidic media via effective approaches (e.g., engineering special structures and heterostructures); (b) enabling prolonged stability test from hours to weeks or months or longer. (c) establishing a comprehensive stability profile by the combination of several independent methods (e.g., eQCM and ICP).

(2) Utilizing synergy conception

Generally, the overall catalytic performance for one electrocatalyst depends on multiple factors, such as the composition, structure, size, morphology and conductivity. In view of this fact, various effective strategies have been adopted to enhance the catalytic performance. However, each design strategy applied alone always leads to limited performance enhancement. To address this issue, it is more compelling to maximize the catalytic performance by integrating the synergistic advantages from multiple design strategies. For example, combining ion doping (aimed at enhancing the intrinsic activity) and morphology engineering (designed to generate more active sites) have been demonstrated as an effective way to boost the overall catalytic performance of metal oxides.68,69,75,77,78,83 Apart from adopting multiple strategies, creating multiple active sites is also very critical for the rational design of superior electrocatalysts. Based on the alkaline HER mechanism, an optimal alkaline HER electrocatalyst should have multiple active sites for favorable H2O dissociation, OH* desorption and H* adsorption simultaneously. For instance, to achieve multiple active sites for synergistic alkaline HER electrocatalysis, constructing multiple elements by doping and multiple components by hybridization can be deemed as two effective approaches. In particular, creating atomic-scale synergistic active sites in single-phase systems is highly desirable, because it can overcome the main disadvantage of long reaction paths which induce large reaction resistance in the hybrid catalyst systems. On basis of this principle, we have designed few single-phase perovskite-based oxides with synergistic active sites (such as (Gd0.5La0.5)BaCo2O5.75117 and Sr2RuO4123) as highly active alkaline HER electrocatalysts. In addition, some single-atom metals supported on metal oxides (e.g., Ru1/TiO2,79 Rh SAC/CuO,293 Pt SA/WO3−x294 and Pt SA/α-MoOx295) also show highly efficient activity for HER. Unfortunately, such studies are still very limited and future research should focus more on this topic. Overall, synergy conception can enable harnessing merits from multiple strategies, elements, components and active sites, which is worth considering when designing advanced electrocatalysts.

(3) Advancing computational and experimental tools

With the fast development of computer science, it is now possible to investigate electrocatalysts by machine learning and DFT calculations.32 Key developments for machine learning, such as identifying key descriptors, can predict catalytic activity and accelerate new catalyst discovery, which is more effective than the conventional trial-and-error methods. Besides, DFT calculations have been regarded as a powerful tool to explain experimental results at the atomic level. In spite of extensive use of DFT calculations, it is worth noting that more accurate and reasonable models describing the real catalyst's surface/interface under real HER conditions should be built. Besides, some factors affecting the computational energetics (e.g., pH, potentials, solvent effects and water arrangements), which are usually ignored before, should be also considered afterwards. Meanwhile, some advanced characterization techniques such as scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM), scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) can greatly benefit the research into the metal oxides, as they are capable of providing very useful information about the crystal and electronic structures. Moreover, in situ/operando techniques, which can allow for real-time observation of the HER process and help to uncover the real catalytic active sites, are strongly recommended in future research. For instance, in situ Raman and XAS can track the active species in HER, and in situ TEM can detect structural and morphological variation during HER. Some prior studies have demonstrated that metal oxides can be reduced to the metals under HER conditions.138,139 Is this phenomenon universal for all metal oxides? Are there different reconstruction phenomena in different metal oxides? To unravel these questions, employment of in situ/operando analysis deserves to be done. At present, the surface structure of metal oxides during HER are seldom explored and future in-depth investigation could shed more light on this aspect. Therefore, combination of rigorous theoretical computations and advanced analytic techniques may be the best way to fundamentally understand catalytic mechanisms and develop new electrocatalysts.

(4) Dedicating practical applications

In light of the practical water electrolysis application, the ideal HER electrocatalyst is anticipated to work well in basic media for alkaline electrolyzers, neutral media for microbial electrolysis cells, and acidic media for proton-exchange membrane-based water electrolyzers. Notably, electrolyzers operating in neutral media can potentially enable the direct use of saltwater, the most abundant water source, which increases the feasibility of water-splitting for hydrogen production.51 To date, metal oxide-based materials have been extensively studied in alkaline and acidic media for HER, but there is almost no report on HER in neutral media. It is thus imperative but still a big challenge to develop efficient metal oxide-based HER electrocatalysts for neutral electrolyte. Exploiting pH-universal HER electrocatalysts based on metal oxide can be one of the future focuses. Aside from the electrolyte, electrodes for practical use should sustain large current density for long-time HER operation, e.g., ≥500 mA cm−2 for thousands of hours. Furthermore, coupling HER devices to other renewable power resource, such as solar energy, wind energy or tidal energy, will greatly accelerate the industrial application of water electrolysis. We believe it would be a significant advance if metal oxide-based materials can catalyze HER efficiently, which shows great promise as bifunctional electrocatalysts for practical water electrolysis application.

In summary, recent work has revealed the great promise of metal oxide-based materials for electrochemical hydrogen evolution. Despite several challenges ahead, continuing research in this area will contribute to the development of low-cost, highly efficient and robust HER electrocatalysts that enable renewable hydrogen technology for sustainable future.
