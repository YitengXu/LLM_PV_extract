Photophysical properties of metal complexes

Paul I. P. Elliott *
Department of Chemical & Biological Sciences, University of Huddersfield, Queensgate, Huddersfield, HD1 3DH, UK. E-mail: p.i.elliott@hud.ac.uk

First published on 3rd June 2011

Abstract

This review summarises the literature reported in 2010 on the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear supramolecular assemblies.

Highlights

Highlights include new thiocyanate-free bis(pyridylpyrazolate) ruthenium complexes as efficient sensitisers for dye-sensitised solar cells and the photophysics of ion-paired soft-salts comprising cationic and anionic cyclometalated iridium complexes. The use of transition metal complexes as phosphors in organic light emitting diodes, as luminescent sensors and as phosphorescent biological imaging agents have continued to be areas of significant activity and interest.

Reviews

A number of reviews have appeared, including a special issue of Coord. Chem. Rev. (volume 254, issue 21–22), detailing developments in various fields including heavy-metal phosphorescent chemosensors,1 design of luminescent d6 metal complexes as biological probes,2,3 cyclometalated transition metal phosphors,4 atomistic modelling of organometallic complexes with optoelectronic applications,5 photophysical properties of Ru(bpy)2 pyridylimidazole complexes,6Ru(II) complexes with expanded bite-angle tridentate ligand for improved photophysical properties,7 charge-transfer transitions and reactive centres in diimine dithiolate complexes,8 metal complexes with photochromic ligands9 and the sensitisation of lanthanide luminescence.10–12

Copper, silver and gold complexes

A number of molecular sensors for the detection of copper ions have been developed including those based on rhodaminespirolactam,13diethylaminocoumarin-appended benzyldihydrazone,14merocyanine,15tris(picolyl)amine-appended flourescien16 and 2,2′-bipyridyl-3,3′-diol17 frameworks. Photophysical studies show that photoinduced electron transfer (PET) from Cu(I) to fluorescent probes based on thiazacrown receptors tethered to a 1,3,5-triarylpyrazoline fluorophore does not compete with radiative deactivation and that Cu(I) induced fluorescence switching is kinetically controlled.18[Cu(phen)2]+ complexes (phen = 1,10-phenanthroline) with bulky 2,9-substituents show general increased luminescent quantum yield and lifetime with increasing steric bulk. Poorer luminescence is observed for oxygen containing substituents due to internal exciplex quenching.19 Trigonal trinuclear complexes of the formula [Cu3(μ-dppm)3(μ3-CC–Ar)]+ (dppm = bis(diphenylphosphino)methane) are highly luminescent (ϕ = 0.574 when Ar = 3,5-(CF3)2C6H3–) from metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT) states.20[(L)Cu(POP)]+ (L = diphenyl(2-pyridylmethyl)phosphine oxide, POP = 2,2′-bis(diphenylphosphino)diphenylether) exhibits intense MLCT-based blue-green emission (ϕ = 0.72) with a long lifetime (τ = 18.6 μs) in polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) films.21[Cu(POP)(dcb)]+ (dcb = 2,2′-bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid) has been applied as a dye-sensitised solar cell (DSSC) dye yielding a conversion efficiency of 0.053% (3.05% for N719).22 The dinuclear complex [Cu2(L)(OH)(H2O)]2+ (L = 2,6-bis-((2,2-dimethyl)hydroxyethyliminomethyl)-4-methylphenolate) is a selective turn-on luminescent sensor for azide anions.23 Three-coordinate Cu(I) phen and 2-(2-pyridyl)benzimidazolate complexes with a bulky N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligand undergo significant enhancement of luminescent intensity in rigid media due to inhibition of Y- to T-shaped excited state distortion-based quenching.24 Variation of the phosphine and substituents of the N-donor in a series of three-coordinate diphosphine Cu(I) complexes with diarylamido or carbazolate ligands allow emission tuning across the blue to yellow region with quantum yields of 0.11 to 0.24 in fluid at room temperature.25 Tetranuclear Cu(I) iodide complexes with chelating 1,2-bis(1,2,3-triazole)phenylene ligands are brightly luminescent at 77 K in PMMA films (λmax = 495–524 nm) with lifetimes of 90–140 μs.26 Crystalline [Cu(xantphos)(phen)]+ complexes (xantphos = 4,5-bis(diphenylphosphino)-9,9-dimethylxanthene) are highly efficient oxygen sensors through luminescence quenching.27 The copper(II) complex of a N-(1,2,3-triazolylmethyl)-N,N-dipicolylamine ligand with a pendant borondipyrromethene reporter group is a selective turn-on luminescent sensor for the direct detection of nitroxyl (HNO).28 A highly emissive dinuclear Cu(I) bis(phosphine)diarylamido complex shows E-type delayed fluorescence. Organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs) doped with the complex exhibit an external quantum efficiency of 16.1% demonstrating efficient harvesting of triplet excitons.29

A thiazacrown receptor appended to a dimethylanilinylfuroquinoline fluorophore acts as a selective ratiometric sensor for Ag+ ions.30 The complex [Au(C⁁N⁁C)(CC–C6H4NPh2)] (C⁁N⁁C = 2,6-diphenyl-4-(2,5-difluorophenyl)-pyridine) exhibits green phosphorescence and organic light-emitting diode (OLED) devices with 11.5% external efficiency.31 Zero-field splitting for the lowest triplet state of complexes of the form [Au((PPh2)2B9H10C2)(PPh3)] of 29 to 47 cm−1 suggest both metal and considerable ligand triplet state character.32 A bis(dipicolyl)NHC Au(I) complex binding two Cu(I)(NCMe)2+ moieties exhibits vapochromic luminescent colour changes due to “on–off” modulation of Cu⋯Au interactions. Exposure to MeOH vapour cause a shift in emission from 462 to 543 nm.33Tetragoldethynyl-complexes with ethene and benzene cores are not phosphorescent, showing no “heavy atom effect”. Calculations show that electronic structure of central ligands and symmetry of molecular orbitals involved in spin-orbit coupling are highly important in intersystem crossing (ISC).34 Several reports have appeared detailing the synthesis and spectroscopic properties of luminescent mixed group 11 metal clusters35–39 and those with platinum.40

Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexes

A range of π-extended 2,2′-alkoxycarbonyldibenzo[2,3]dipyrrin and corresponding dinaphtho[2,3]dipyrrin complexes of a range of metal salts show bright luminescent emission tunable over the red/NIR region. Bis-coordination of these ligands results in near total switching off of emission.41 A pyrene-tagged zinc–cyclen complex acts as a receptor for nucleotides. Two complexes bind to the phosphate and base ends of the nucleotide and result in pyrene-based excimer emission.42

The preparation of new ligands as fluorescent sensors for zinc ions continues to be an active area of interest. 2-(hydroxymethyl)-4-methyl-6-((quinolinyl-8-imino)methyl)phenol acts as a highly selective sensor with a 14-fold enhancement in emission on Zn2+ binding.43 A 2,5-diphenyl[1,3,4]oxadiazole-based ligand with a –CH2NH(CH2CH2NH)2CH2CH2NHCH2– chain connecting the ortho-positions of the phenyl rings acts a turn-on luminescent sensor for Zn2+ under physiological conditions even in the presence of Cd2+ and Pb2+.44 A rhodamine-based receptor with a bis(pyridin-2-yl)amine binding domain acts as a ratiometric sensor for Zn2+.45Rhodaminespirolactam I undergoes 220-fold enhancement in emission through Zn2+ binding induced ring opening of the spirolactam, is highly Zn2+ selective and can be used as a probe to monitor intracellular Zn2+ level changes.46 A similar phenylurea rhodaminespirolactam conjugate has been shown to be a highly sensitive and selective turn on fluorescent sensor for Pb2+ and Hg2+ ions.47Naphthalenediimide-based fluorophores with similar pyridin-2-ylmethylamino binding domains have been developed as sensors for Zn2+ with emission extending into the NIR.48 A naphthalimide-based chemosensor with an amide linked bispicolylamine receptor group is a highly selective, cell permeable sensor for Zn2+ ions undergoing Zn2+ induced amide tautomerism; its Cd2+-bound form allows ratiometric sensing of Zn2+ ions.49 2-p-tolyl-1H-imidazo[4,5-f][1,10]phenanthrolinium hydroselenite also acts as a highly selective OFF–ON fluorescent sensor for Zn2+ in living cells.50Fluorene-based probe II shows a high sensitivity for Zn2+ over a good dynamic range (10−4 to 106 M) and exhibits efficient two-photon absorption characteristics.51Styryl Bopdipy derivatives with dipicolylamine and dithiazacrown receptor groups undergo modulation of Bodipy luminescence on Zn2+ and Hg2+ binding and act as molecular AND logic gates through selective manipulation of intramolecular charge-transfer (ICT) and PET processes.52Anthryl-4-dipicolylaminomethyl-1,2,3-triazole exhibits turn on fluorescence on Zn2+ binding via cut-off of quenching PET processes.53 Heteroditopic receptor III exhibits three distinct fluorescence states over a wide Zn2+ concentration range through chelation enhanced fluorescence involving termination of quenching PET processes and enhanced ICT-enabled FRET resulting in fluorescence enhancement followed by a bathochromic shift.54 The macrocyclic ligand 1,9(4,7)-diphenthroline-3,7,11,15-tetraazacyclohexadecaphane exhibits Zn2+ induced chelation enhanced luminescent quenching.55

A 2,8-dithia-5-aza-2,6-pyridinophane with a 5-chloro-8-hydroxyquinolin-7-ylmethyl pendant arm has been shown to be a selective sensor for Cd2+ ions in living cells.56 A 2-(benzimidazol-2-yl)-6-dipicolylaminomethylpyridine-based receptor is a highly selective and picomolar sensitive ratiometric fluorescent probe for Cd2+ in living cells.57Terfluorene receptors have been developed that exhibit a highly sensitive and selective ratiometric turn-on fluorescent response in the presence of Hg2+ ions.58 A naphthalimide-appended thioether crown receptor shows 5-fold luminescent enhancement on Hg2+ binding. Fluorescence is then quenched in the presence of Ag+ ions.59 A coumarin-based fluorophore with an azacrown ether receptor linked by a thiocarbamate bridge is a fluorescent turn-on sensor for Hg2+ in aqueous solution.60 A thiazacrown ether receptor with a pendant naphthalene-based fluorophore has been developed as a two-photon fluorescent probe for Hg2+ ions and has been used for the detection of Hg2+ acculumation in fish organs.61

Miscellaneous transition metal complexes

Homoleptic complexes Sc(O⁁N)3 (where “O⁁N” represents 2-(2-benzoimidazol-2-yl-, 2-(2-benzothiazol-2-yl- and 2-(2-benzoxyazol-2-yl-phenolate ligands) are luminescent (λmax = 430–455 nm) and yield near-white emissive OLED devices.62Trans-[Mo(O)(Cl)(CNtBu)4]BPh4 and trans-[Mo(O)(F)(pyridine)4]BPh4 show pressure dependent luminescence with +12 cm−1kbar−1 blue-shifted emission for the former and −8 cm−1 kbar−1 red-shifted emission for the latter.63 Complexes of the form [M(L)n] (L = N-ferrocenylmethyl-N-benzyldithiocarbamate; n = 2, M = Ni, Cu, Cd, Hg, Pd, Pt, Pb; n = 3, M = Co, UO2) have been prepared and assessed as potential DSSC sensitisers showing efficiencies of η = 0.31–0.63% (η = 1.0% for N719).64

Rhenium and technetium complexes

The excited state dynamics of [Re(CO)3(Me4phen)(L)]+ (Me4phen = 3,4,7,8-tetramethyl-1,10-phenthroline, L = cis-4-styrylpyridine or cis-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethylene) have been investigated and reveal a complex equilibrium between three excited states. UV irradiation results in conversion of L to the trans isomer (ϕ = 0.15) which competes with radiative decay from phen-based 3MLCT and intraligand 3IL states.65[Re(CO)3(diimine)(X)]+ complexes where X is a N,N-functionalised 4-aminopyridine ligand show highly responsive luminescent lifetime modulation in the presence of Cu(II), Zn(II) and Hg(II) ions.66Solvent effects on the spectroscopic properties of [Re(CO)3Cl(glyoxime)] complexes have been studied by DFT methods and show red shifted absorption and emission with decreased solvent polarity.67 [Re2(CO)6(μ–Cl)2(μ-4,5-(Me3Si)2-pyridazine)] crystallises in two highly luminescent polymorphs (ϕ > 0.5) with greater than 10 times luminescent emission than that in solution due to hindered rotation of the Me3Si groups in the solid state.68

The Re(V) complex [Re(O)(Cl)2(L)] (L = bis(3,5-diphenylpyrazolyl)-methanecarboxylate) exhibits one and two photon emission at 335 nm upon irradiation at 290 and 580 nm respectively.69 Complexes of the form [Re(dmpe)3−n(depe)n]2+ (dmpe = bis(dimethylphosphino)ethane, depe = bis(diethylphosphino)ethane) exhibit rare examples of luminescent ligand to metal charge transfer (LMCT) states (ϕ = ∼0.07) for d5 transition metal complexes in solution at room temperature.70 The complex [Re2(dppm)2(p-O2CC4H6NO2)2Cl2] (dppm = bis(diphenylphosphino)methane) has a 1MLCT absorption at much higher energy to similar quadruply bonded Mo2 and W2 complexes, and holds less promise for panchromatic photovoltaic applications.71 The first emission studies of Tc2X82− (X = Cl, Br) have been carried out and show luminescence from the 1δ–δ* state with shorter lifetimes than their rhenium analogues.72

Ruthenium and osmium complexes

The low energy limits of MLCT states for complexes of the form [Ru(bpy)2L2]n+ (L2 = (NH3)2, (CH3CN)2, CO32−, malonate, acac, oxalate) have been probed by emission spectroscopy at 77 K and approach a limit at about 14000 cm−1.73 The series of complexes [Ru(bpy)3−n(dab)n]2+ (dab = 1,4-diaza-1,3-butadiene) have been investigated by DFT methods. Introduction of the dab ligands results in strongly red-shifted absorption maxima and lowering of the lowest 3MLCT state such that the dab containing complexes may show NIR luminescence.74 Complexes in the series [Ru(bpy)3−n(dppp2)n]2+ (dppp2 = pyirdo-[2′,3′:5,6]-pyrazino-[2,3-f][1,10]phenanthroline) show highly solvent dependent luminescence. Where n = 1, emission is blue shifted by 99 nm on going from acetonitrile to CH2Cl2 with a 19-fold enhancement in intensity attributed to a localisation of the 3MLCT state involved to a position distal to Ru on the dppp2 ligand.75 This excited state is initially populated from a 3MLCT state proximal to Ru and the rate of interconversion can be tuned by an order of magnitude depending on choice of solvent.76Ru(II) bpy complexes with a phenylacetylide and a 1,3,7-trithiocyclononane ligand are weakly luminescent in acetonitrile (660–680 nm) which results from a 3MLCT/3ILCT state.77 A series of cyclometalated Ru(II) complexes with improved octahedral geometry have been prepared. [Ru(dqp)(dqb)]+ (dqp = 2,6-diquinolin-8-pyridine, dqb = 1,3- diquinolin-8-benzene) is non emissive at room temperature but emits at 824 nm (τ = 16 ns) at 77 K.78 A Ru(tpy) complex with an electron donating N,N′-dimethyl-N,N′-dipyridin-2-ylpyridine-2,6-diamine (ddpd) ligand exhibits intense 3MLCT emission at 729 nm with a long lifetime of 722 ns. The favourable photophysical performance is attributed to the strong field ddpd ligand raising the energy of the emission-deactivating 3MC state.79 The complexes [M(fpbpyk)2] (M = Ru, Os, fpbpyk = [5-(trifluoromethyl)pyrazol-3-yl](2,2′-bipyrid-6-yl)ketone) exhibit solid-state NIR phosphorescence at 810 nm (Ru) and 994 nm (Os).80 A [Ru(phen)(bpy)2]2+ complex with an ethynylpyrene substituent in the 3-position of the phen ligand displays a drastically increased luminescent lifetime over the unsubstituted parent complex (τ = 58.4 μs versus 0.4 μs) and a 77-fold increase in luminescent O2 sensing sensitivity.81 The complex [Ru(bpy)(dpb)(dppn)]2+ (dpb = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)benzoquinoxaline, dppn = 4,5,9,16-tetraazadibenzo-[a,c]naphthacene) shows high singlet O2 quantum yield (0.43) with absorption at long wavelength (548 nm).82 Complexes of the form [Ru(4,4′-Me2bpy)2(L11)]2+ show emission behaviour largely dictated by the nature of the remote ligand substituent. Electron acceptor character when X = −NO2 results in much stronger emission than when X = −OC6H13 and the ligand has donor character.83[Ru(dppzp)(bpy)(CN)]+ (dppzp = 6′-(2′′-pyridyl)dipyrido[3,2-a:2′,3′-c]phenazene) undergoes switch on luminescent emission from a dppzp localised MLCT state in the presence of DNA.84 The excited-state dynamics of the complex [Ru(bpy)2(L22)]4+ have been studied by fs transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy. Photoexcitation leads to a charge separated state where the excited electron is delocalised over the extended π-system of L22 whereas it is localised on the bpy unit adjacent to Ru in the non-bisquaternised analogue.85Bisterpyridyl ruthenium complexes with π-conjugated triphenylamine substituents have been shown to possess strongly coupled electronic states that result in strongly excitation wavelength dependent luminescent behaviour caused by mixing of different electronic states.86 The effect of aryl substituents in cyclometalated complexes of the form [Ru(dcb)2(ppy)]+ (ppy = 2-phenylpyridine) have been investigated and luminescence is quenched when nitro substituents are sited meta to the Ru–C bond.87 The bimetallic complex IV displays panchromatic absorption characteristics superior to those of the most efficient DSSC sensitisers with absorption at 578 nm (ε = 9 × 103 M−1 cm−1) and a shoulder at 640 nm and a long tail extending beyond 700 nm.88

Sequential replacement of bpy in the series [Ru(bpy)3–n(dpb)]2+ (dpb = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)benzoquinoxaline) leads to an unexpected significant enhancement of excited state lifetime, 1O2 quantum yield and binding affinity for dsDNA. The complexes where n = 2 and 3 show efficient DNA cleavage under irradiation at ≥ 600 nm.89 The osmium complex [Os(bpy)2(dppn)]2+ (dppn = benzo[i]dipyrido[3,2-a:2,3-c]phenazine) has a 1O2 quantum yield of 0.42 and shows efficient DNA cleavage upon irradiation λ ≥ 645 nm.90 The complex [Ru(dmcb)(dppz)]2+ (dmcb = 4,4′-dimethoxycarbonyl-bpy, dppz = dipyridophenazene) shows unexpected luminescence in aqueous solution in contrast to similar dppz complexes suggesting fundamentally different excited state relaxation pathways on incorporation of the ester substituents.91Tris(4-methyl-2,2′-bipyridyl-4′-carboxaldehyde)ruthenium(II) acts as a highly sensitive and selective luminescent probe for cysteine and homocysteine.92

Ruthenium complexes continue to attract significant interest due to their applicability as absorbing chromophores in DSSC devices. Charge-neutral thiocyanate-free complexes such as V have been developed and show superior conversion efficiencies up to η = 9.54%.93 Interfacial electron transfer processes of complexes of the form [M(bpy)(L)]2+ (M = Ru, Os; L = 4-(2,2′-bipyridin-4-yl)benzene-1,2-diol) have been studied by fs TA spectroscopy. When M = Ru, single-exponential electron injection takes place from nonthermalised MLCT states whereas for Os, biexponential injection from both thermalised and nonthermalised states takes place.94 The complex [Ru(dcb)2(L)] (L = 6,7-bis(methylthio)tetrathiafulvalene dithiolate) exhibits a long-lived charge separated stated on excitation when bound to TiO2 (20 ms). DSSC containing the dye show very poor efficiencies as the long-lived charge-separated state persists even in the presence of the I−/I3− electrolyte and thus the dye is poorly regenerated.95 The dye [Ru(dcb)(L)(NCS)2] (L = 1-benzyl-2-(pyridin-2-yl)benzoimidazole) gives a high DSSC conversion efficiency of 7.7% (η = 7.8% for N719) attributed to good light harvesting characteristics and slow charge recombination kinetics.96 A series of Ru complexes with 2,6-bis(imidazolyl-2-idene)pyridine and tpy based ligands show poor photovoltaic efficiencies (0.1–0.5% versus 6.34% for N719) primarily due to small short circuit photocurrent density values resulting in small IPCE values.97 The complex [Ru(dcb)(L)(NCS)2]− (L = 4-(1H-imidazo[4,5-f][1,10]phenanthrolin-2-yl)-N,N-diphenylaniline), where the dcb ligand is mono-deprotonated, yields DSSC devices with an efficiency of η = 6.85% (7.4% for N3).98 The complex K2[Ru(CN)4(L)] (L = 4-[2-(4′-methyl-2,2′-bipyridin-4-yl)vinyl]benzene-1,2-diol) shows a longer wavelength absorption band tail when bound to TiO2. TA spectroscopy shows the complex to undergo single exponential electron injection (< 100 fs) with multiexponential back electron transfer kinetics (3.5 ps (37.5%), 70 ps (23.9%) and >1 ns (38.6%)).99 Photophysical pulsed-laser excitation studies of [Ru(dcb)(dtb)2]2+ and [Ru(dcb)(dtb)(NCS)2] (dtb = 4,4-tBu2-bpy) anchored to TiO2 nanoparticles reveal rapid excited state injection while MLCT absorption spectra of the dyes differed from that which was initially excited indicating an underlying Stark effect.100,101 The dinuclear complex [(4,4′,4′′-(CO2Me)3-tpy)Ru(N⁁C⁁N-tpy)Ru(tpy)]3+ (N⁁C⁁N-tpy = 4-(3,5-di(4-tert-butylpyridin-2-ylphenyl)-tpy) shows metal to metal charge-transfer (MMCT) upon one-electron oxidation (1034 nm) and points the way toward two-step upconversion in DSSC devices for the utilisation of low energy photons.102

Iridium complexes

Cyclometalated iridium(III) complexes continue to attract significant interest due to the efficiency of phosphorescent emission and the tuning of emission wavelength throughout the visible spectrum achievable through ligand modification. Complexes of this type, therefore, have potential applications in OLED devices and as sensitive biological imaging probes. The emitting triplet state of fac-[Ir(ppy)3] has been studied by high resolution optical spectroscopy at cryogenic temperatures. Three 0–0 transitions for the triplet substates I, II and III were identified with large zero field splitting of 170 cm−1 and individual substate lifetimes of 116 μs, 6.4 μs and 200 ns respectively.103 The excited state relaxation dynamics of [Ir(ppy)2(bpy)]+ have been investigated by time-resolved spectroscopy. The complex exhibits triple phosphorescent blue, green and orange emission bands resulting from 3LLCT, 3MLCT (ppy) and 3MLCT (bpy) states respectively with lifetimes for the 3MLCT states of 0.13–4.8 and 425–617 ns respectively.104 Emission wavelength has been tuned through the use of cyclometalated benzyldiphenylphosphine ligands,1052-(4-fluorophenyl)-3-methylquinoxaline106 and phenylbenzimidazolinato107 ligands and bis-NHC,108 and 2-(1,2,3-triazol-5-yl)pyridine109 ancillary ligands. The complex [Ir(dfppz)(tp-pyim)]+ (dfppz = 1-(2,4-difluorophenyl)-1H-pyrazole, tp-pyim = 2-(1-(4-tritylphenyl)-1H-imidazol-2-yl)pyridine) exhibits highly efficient blue-green luminescence in both solution and the solid state due to the significantly suppressed intermolecular interactions in solution and excited state self-quenching in the solid state due to the bulky tritylphenyl group.110 The caged complex VI is brightly luminescent and shows an 80% decrease in oxygen quenching in comparison to the archetypal complex [Ir(ppy)3].111 A series of homoleptic complexes of the form [Ir(N⁁N)3] where N⁁N is 5-(2-pyridyl)pyrazolate or 5-(2-isoquinolyl)pyrazolate have been prepared. OLED devices using [Ir(3-tert-butyl-5-(2-isoquinolyl)pyrazolate)3] yield bright orange emission with external efficiencies of up to 14.6%.112 A series of complexes have been prepared with tridentate 6-(5-trifluoromethyl)pyrazol-3-yl)-2-2′-bipyridine (fpbpy) ligands of the form [Ir(fpbpy)(L)]n+ which are luminescent in acetonitrile at room temperature. Emission is markedly red-shifted when L is biscyclometalated 2,6-diphenylpyridine and n = 0 relative to the homoleptic complex where L = fpbpy and n = 1.113 Red-emitting biscyclometalated 1-phenylisoquinoline complexes with carbazole appended acac ligands have been shown to yield enhanced efficiency in polymer LED devices.114 Ion-paired soft salts such as [Ir(tolpy)(4,4-tBu2bpy)][Ir(dfppy)(CN)2] (tolpy = 2-p-tolylpyridine) show energy transfer between constituent ions in solution and takes place at diffusion controlled rates.115 The boron dipyrromethane (Bodipy) appended complex [Ir(ppy)2(bpy-CC-Bodipy)]+ (bpy-CC-Bodipy = 5-ethynyl-2,2′-bipyridine-8-phenyl-1,3,5,7-tetramethyl-4,4′-bis(2,5-dioxaoct-7-ynyl)-4-bora-3a,4a-diaza-s-indacene) exhibits Bodipy-based phosphorescent emission at 730 nm (τ = 25 μs) at 77 K due to energy transfer through quenching of the 3MLCT state.116Bisterpyridyl Ir(III) and Ru(II) complexes with 4-(PhCHCH–C6H4–) ligand substituents exhibit two-photon luminescence when irradiated in the NIR at 740–820 nm.117

Biscyclometalated pyridylbenzaldehyde complexes of rhodium and iridium with biotin tagged bpy ligands have been investigated as luminescent biological imaging agents. The complexes and their bioconjugates with bovine serum albumin have been isolated and photophysically characterised.118 Complex VII shows outstanding switch-on luminescence on the selective binding of Mg2+ ions to the azacrown ether groups.119 The complex VIII containing both triarylboron and carbazole moieties acts as a ratiometric probe for fluoride ions through switching from triplet to singlet-based emission by quenching of orange metal-based phosphorescence and enhancement of blue ancillary ligand fluorescence on fluoride binding.120

Platinum and palladium complexes

Cyclometalated platinum complexes have also been the subject of intense interest for their application in light emitting electroluminescent devices. Complexes have been prepared and tuning of photophysical properties achieved utilising diaryl ketimine,121triphenylamine-capped ppy,122 aryl-boron, -silicon, -germanium, -phosphine oxide capped ppy,123N,N-di(6-phenylpyridin-2-yl)anilines124 and aryl lepidine125 cyclometalated ligands for example. Pt(ppy) complexes of β-diketonate ligands have been investigated as highly active second-order non-linear optical (NLO) chromophores.126Platinum acetylide complexes have also continued to attract attention due to their NLO and optical power limiting properties.127–133 The complex (C⁁N*N)Pt–CCPh] (C⁁N*N = N-(2-phenylpyridin-6-yl)-N-pyridin-2-ylaniline) exhibits significantly improved phosphorescence (ϕ = 56%) compared to its 6-phenyl-2,2′-bpy analogue (ϕ = 4%) due to an improved square planar geometry.134 Cyclometalated 6-phenyl-2,2′-bpy complexes of Pt with pyridyl-acetylide ligands and their derivatives have been reported and show selective luminescent sensing of lead ions.135[(C⁁N⁁C)PtCl]Cl where C⁁N⁁C = 2,6-bis(1-butylimidazol-2-ylidenyl)pyridine display distinct aquachromic photoluminescence in the solid state.136 Complexes of the form [(4,4′,4′′-R3-tpy)Pt(CC–R′)] (R = tBu, Cl, R′ = alkyl, aryl) show emission derived from mixed 3MLCT and ligand to ligand charge-transfer 3LLCT to tpy when R′ is alkyl but only 3LLCT for aryl analogues. In the π-extended complexes [(tBu3tpy)Pt(CC(C6H4CC)n−1Ar)], absorption maxima are red shifted with increasing n when Ar = Ph but blue shifted when Ar = p-C6H4NMe2 due to the fundamentally different natures of the HOMO in each case.137

[n-Bu4N][Pt(dfppy)(CN)2] exhibits intense monomer-based sky-blue phosphorescence in the solid state (ϕ = 60%, τ = 19 μs) from a 3LC state which is largely quenched in solution attributed to energy stabilisation of dd* states due to molecular distortions and/or bond elongations.138 [Pt(ppy)(acac)] type complexes with π-conjugated naphthalimide- and napthalene-based pendant groups on the ppy ligand have been shown to be sensitive O2 sensors with high quenching constants of up to 0.231 Torr−1.139 [(C⁁N⁁N)PtCl] where C⁁N⁁N is 6-phenyl-1,10-phenathroline has been used as a switch-on luminescent probe in G-quadruplex DNA-based selective detection of Ag+ ions at nanomolar concentrations.140 Homoleptic Pt complexes with bis(1,2,4-triazol-5-ylidene)methane ligands give blue phosphorescence (∼433 nm, ϕ = 0.23–0.31) in polyvinylalcohol and PMMA.141 Mononuclear Pt(II) complexes of 6-(5-trifluoromethyl-pyrazol-3-yl)-2,2′-bpy with a monodentate pyrazolate ligand yield emission from LLCT and ILCT states. In dinuclear complexes where the pyrazolate bridges the Pt centres, dual phosphorescence is observed from ILCT and ligand to metal to metal charge-transfer LMMCT excimer states.142Naphthalimide-based phosphorescence has been observed in the complex [Pt(4,4′-tBu4-2,2′-bpy)(CC–NI)2] (NI =4 -(N-butylnaphthalimide)) through promotion of intersystem crossing (ISC) by the platinum acetylide moiety.143 The cyclometalated pyridylpyrene (C⁁N) complex [Pt(C⁁N)(acac)] along with ppy analogues with a pyrene containing substituent exhibit deep red/NIR phosphorescence attributed to pyrene-localised 3LC states.144 The excited states of pyrazolate-bridged [Pt2(μ-pz)2(bpym)2] (bpym = 2,2′-bipyrimidine) and [Pt2(μ-pyt)2(ppy)] (pyt = pyridine-2-thiolate) have been studied by DFT. Supramolecular transition metal assemblies

Dinuclear complexes of the form [(bpy)2M(pzbzim)M′(bpy)2]3+ (M & M′ = Ru and/or Os; pzbzim = pyrazole-3,5-bis(benzimidazole)) show MLCT bands which are replaced by LMCT bands on stepwise oxidation of the metal centres. In the case of the RuIIRuIII system, an intervalence charge transfer band is observed at 910 nm.152 The complexes [{Ir(dfppy)2}L] and [(dpffy)2IrLPt(ppy)] where L = 1,3-biz(3-phenyl-3-oxopropanoyl)benzene exhibit weak emission in solution from a 3LC state centred on L. Intense orange aggregation induced emission is observed in the solid state arising from a metal to ligand to ligand 3MLLCT state.153 Heterodinuclear complex IX has been shown to undergo a series of PET processes proceeding through 1L → 1Os ET followed by ISC to 3Os and a similar route to the generation of 3Ru which proceed by a Förster mechanism followed by 3Ru → 3Os ET proceeding by a long range through bond mediated Dexter mechanism.154 When this complex is functionalised with a (tpy)Pt–CC– moiety at the truxene 12-position, truxene-based phosphorescence is observed attributed to the closeness of the Pt 3CT and truxene triplet states.155 A Ru–Pt–Ru triad consisting of two [Ru(bpy)2L]+ moieties (L = 3-CC-phen) forming a bisacetylide complex with a bridging Pt(PBu3)2 unit undergoes efficient ET from the bisacetylide to Ru followed by charge injection from Ru to the extended π-system of the ethynylphenanthroline.156 A Ru(bpy)2/Os(tpy) dyad bridged by a phen-tpy ligand (connected by the 3- and 5-positions of the phen and tpy domains respectively) shows PET from the phen to tpy domain but is relatively slow resulting in dual emission.157 The complexes [(bpy)2M(bpt)Ru(tpy)Cl]2+ (bpt = 3,5-bis(pyridin-2-yl)-1,2,4-triazolate) exhibit intervalence charge transfer transitions in the mixed valent state at 7090 and 5990 cm−1 where M = Ru and Os respectively. Both complexes show identical wavelength emission despite excited state localisation at Ru(tpy) and at Os when M = Ru and Os respectively indicating efficient interaction between the two metal centres.158 A number of polynuclear cyclic and cage platinum complexes including bismetalated anthracene containing rectangles and bismetalated phenanthrene containing triangles have been investigated by femtosecond upconversion and TA spectroscopy. Results reveal ultrafast charge transfer processes yielding charge separated states that undergo recombination to give triplet excitons. Such materials have been proposed as candidates for non-linear optical and optical limiting materials.159

Metallo-porphyrins and -phthalocyanines

Zinc porphyrin IX 2,4-bis(ethyleneglycol) has been assembled into light-harvesting antennae using the M13 virus as a template with chemical linkage.160 A zinc porphyrin (ZnP) has been used as a FRET donor to study unfolding in cytochrome c in which an Alexa 660 dye had been inserted into different positions along the amino acid chain.161 Triplet–triplet annihilation supported upconversion of visible to partly UV light has been achieved by encapsulation of a Pt octaethylporphyrin sensitiser and anthracene emitter in a dendrimer.162 PET from zinc tetrasulphonatophenylporphyrin to methylviologen has been studied by ultrafast methods. The S2 and S1 states undergo forward and back electron transfer with the same time constants (180 and 700 fs respectively) but the resultant ground state is vibrationally excited for the former showing that the initial excess excitation energy survives the two-step electron transfer.163 Coordination of pyrazine or 4,4′-bpy to ZnTTP results in ultrafast population of an intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) state from the S2 state which then undergoes near quantitative back electron conversion to the S1 state.164

A dinuclear zinc naphthalocyanines bridged by a shared benzene ring has been reported. The extended π-system and dimerisation results in an absorption band in the NIR at 965 nm.165 A multiporphyrin array of the form (ZnP2)2·(TPyP) composed of a central free-base meso-tetrakis(4-pyridyl)porphyrin (TPyP) connected to two zinc bisporphyrins containing phenanthroline bridging units (ZnP2) does not undergo expected sensitisation of TPyP fluorescence but rather quenching. Excitation of the TPyP unit at 77 K results in an unusual HOMO–HOMO electron transfer from the peripheral ZnP2 to produce a charge separated state with a lifetime of 11 ns.166Porphyrin boxes constructed from four meso-pyridine-appended ZnP dimers with di- or tetraalkynylene bridges have been studied by fs-TA methods. Exciton–exciton annihilation is well described by a Förster-type incoherent energy hopping model.167 A series of heterometallic porphyrin arrays consisting of peripheral ruthenium tetraaryl porphyrins and a central [Sn(TPyP)] complex have been investigated and show quenching of localised Sn singlet and Ru triplet states by electron transfer to yield oxidised Ru/reduced Sn charge separated states.168 A tripodal tris-zinc(porphyrin) (TPZn3) containing a central bridging tristriazolylmethylamine linker can encapsulate [Au(TPyP)]+ through coordination to Zn and exhibits TPZn3 singlet state quenching by PET processes.169 A series of tetrapyrazoporhyrazines with peripheral N,N-diethylamino substituents show competitive relaxation to fluorescence and ISC pathways through mixing of the S1 state of the macrocycle and a nearby ICT state.170 A series of carboxylic acid functionalised Zn porphyrins with electron donating meso-diarylamine substituents show broadened Soret and red-shifted Q-bands and yield DSSCs with up to 6.6% power conversion efficiency.171Porphyrin DSSC sensitisers with long –(CC–C6H6)n–CO2H anchors suffer from diminished electron injection efficiencies due to porphyrin aggregation-induced energy transfer processes.172ZnP based DSSC dyes with enthynyl-acene–ethynyl-benzene spacers between the porphyrin and carboxylate anchor (acene = benzene to pentacene) have been investigated. The greatest efficiency is achieved with an anthracene bridging moiety (η = 5.44%, 6.73% for N719).173

The bisporphyrin assembly X has been shown to act as a sensitiser for triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA) based near-IR to visible upconversion in the presence of N,N-bis(ethylpropyl)perylene-3,4,9,10-tetracarboxylicdiimide. Irradiation at 780 nm results in emission at 541 nm, a record anti-Stokes shift of 0.86 eV for sensitised TTA.174 Oligomers of the form Cl–[Pt(PBu3)2–CC–ZnP]n–CC–Pt(PBu3)2Cl exhibit reduced fluorescent lifetimes and quantum yields compared to those of the monomer HCC–ZnP–CCH.175 A ZnTPP/tungsten alkylidyne dyad of the form ZnTTP–CC–C6H4–CW(dppe)2(Cl)2 (dppe = bis(diphenylphosphino)ethane, TPP = tetraphenylporphyrin) exhibits a triplet–triplet equilibrium where the ZnTPP unit acts as a lifetime reservoir resulting in a 1300-fold enhancement of phosphorescent lifetime.176 Femtosecond TA studies of zinc porphyrin/polypyridyl platinum acetylide dyads show that PET occurs considerably faster when the porphyrin is connected via the meso-position rather than the β-position.177 A mitochondria-specific ZnP-(phen)Ru(bpy)2 dyad with an acetylene linker between the meso-porphyrin and 4-phen positions sensitises singlet oxygen formation (ϕ = 0.93) under visible and NIR excitation and causes significant cell death suggesting its possible use in photodynamic therapy.178Assembly XI undergoes efficient photoinduced energy transfer (PEnT) from the Bodipy moiety to ZnP followed by fast PET from ZnP to C60. When immobilised on an indium-tin-oxide electrode in the presence of diazabicyclooctane and the sacrificial electron donor ascorbic acid, the resultant photochemical cell exhibits 29% quantum yield.179

A number of reports have appeared detailing the photophysics of donor–acceptor porphyrin/fullerene assemblies. A L-glutamide substituted zinc porphyrin undergoes J-type self assembly to which pyridylated fullerenes coordinate to zinc resulting in a novel bicontinuous donor–acceptor system with an unusually high efficiency of fluorescence quenching.180 A panchromatic supramolecular conjugate of a zinc phthalocyanine-Bodipy dyad with Zn-coordinated pyridylfulleropyrrolidine forms a charge separated state of the form Bodip-ZnP˙+–C60˙− on visible light irradiation with an extremely long lifetime in toluene of 39.9 ns.181 A [2]catenane constructed through Cu(I) phenanthroline-templation and ‘click’ chemistry with ZnP and C60 termini produces a charge separated state of the form ZnP˙+–(Cu(phen)2)–C60˙− which decays with a lifetime in the microsecond range.182 Charge separated radical ion pairs have been observed in self assembled donor–acceptor conjugates consisting of a ZnPc with four 18-crown-6 moieties and alkylammonium-functionalised fullerenes and have longer lifetimes than porphyrin analogues.183 A series of double-decker lanthanide bisphthalocyanine complexes with a fullerene moiety appended to phthalocyanine show no evidence of ground state interactions between the LnPc2 and C60 groups. Only after excitation at 387 nm, which excites both groups, does electron transfer from LnPc2 to C60 occur.184

A series of π-extended benzo- and naphthoporphyrin palladium and platinum complexes have been prepared and show NIR absorption (628–691 nm) and room temperature emission (815–882 nm) and NIR oxygen sensing.185Acetylene-bridged palladium and platinum porphyrin oligomers have been studied by TA spectroscopy and show fast S1 → T1ISC (τ = 5.2–49.2 ps) and long triplet lifetimes (5–50 μs) and have potential NIR optical limiting and NLO applications.186 A doubly N-confused hexaphyrin with two octaarginine arms gives enhanced NIR luminescence at 1050 nm in the presence of zinc ions in aqueous solution.187Ir(III) corroles have been reported exhibiting room-temperature NIR phosphorescence at wavelengths longer than 800 nm.188
