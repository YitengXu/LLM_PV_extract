Photophysical properties of metal complexes

Paul I. P. Elliott *
Department of Chemical & Biological Science, University of Huddersfield, Queensgate, Huddersfield, HD1 3DH, UK. E-mail: p.i.elliott@hud.ac.uk

First published on 18th May 2010

Abstract

This review summarises the literature reported in 2009 on the photophysical properties of metal complexes and their polynuclear supramolecular assemblies.

Highlights

Highlights include a variety of novel highly luminescent polynuclear heterometallic silver/gold clusters, and covalent and non-covalent fullerene–porphyrin conjugates as light harvesting photosynthetic mimics. The use of transition metal complexes as chromophores in dye-sensitised solar cells, as phosphors in organic light emitting diode devices, as luminescent sensors and phosphorescent biological imaging agents have been areas of significant activity and interest.

Review articles

A number of reviews have appeared detailing the photophysics and applications of luminescent metal complexes. These include the development of organometallic electrophosphorescent iridium1 and platinum complexes,2,3 the use of metallo-dendrimers for organic light-emitting diodes,4 photonic interfacial transition metal supramolecular assemblies,5 the luminescent properties of phosphine gold(I) halide and thiolate complexes,6 metalloporphyrins as potential sensitisers for efficient dye-sensitised solar cells,7 low-melting liquid crystalline metal complexes,8 discrete cyclic porphyrin arrays,9 supramolecular porphyrin/phthalocyanine-fullerene/nanotube donor–acceptor hybrids,10 lanthanide-based luminescent molecular probes,11–13 sensitised lanthanide luminescence in d-f hybrid complexes,14 luminescent di- and poly-nuclear lanthanide complexes with polyazine ligands,15 non-linear optical properties of lanthanide complexes,16 small molecular fluorescent sensors for investigating zinc metalloneurochemistry17 and calixarene-based fluorescent molecular sensors for toxic metals.18

Main group compounds

Trivalent metal 8-hydroxyquinolate complexes (Mq3) are of interest for their applications in analytical chemistry and for the fabrication of OLED devices. The effects of high hydrostatic pressure on the photophysical properties of Alq3, Gaq3 and Inq3 have been investigated. For the meridional isomers, increasing pressure results in red-shifted luminescent maxima due to enhancement of intermolecular π–π interactions. Abrupt changes are observed due to phase changes including amorphisation at ∼6 GPa in all cases. The facial isomer of Alq3 in contrast does not show significant pressure dependent changes in luminescent properties.19 A series of blue emissive tris(8-hydroxynaphthyridinato) (ND) Al, Ga and In complexes have been investigated as green luminescent Mq3 analogues (Alq3 λmax = 524 nm; Al(ND)3 λmax = 433 nm). The origin of the wide band gap in these materials stems from a significant stabilisation of the HOMO relative to that of Alq3. The Al complexes show high electron mobility and yield efficient non-doped OLED devices.20 A series of complexes with the formula [{(R5)2-salen(3-tBu)2}Al(OC6H4-p-Ph)] (I) are highly tunable luminophores with quantum efficiencies as high as 40%. Modulation of the HOMO by changing the R5 substituents allows tuning of the emission maxima throughout the entire visible spectrum (λmax = 438 nm R5 = –NMe3; 487 nm R5 = –Ph; 599 nm R5 = –NMe3+).21 Dipyrrin complexes II are luminescent and show significant enhancement in quantum yield and blue-shifted emission on binding of ZnCl2 or ZnBr2 to the phenolate oxygen atoms.22

PET and PEnT in triads consisting of a silicon phthalocyanine with two phenolate-substituted boron dipyrromethene axial ligands have been investigated. In toluene PET supercedes PEnT whereas in dimethylformamide PEnt dominates with formation of a charge separated state which undergoes fast recombination with lifetimes of ∼35 ps.23 2-(2′,4′-dihydroxybenzylidene)-aminobenzeneboronic acid acts as a selective sensor for hydroxylated organotins in aqueous solutions. Addition of BuSn(OH)2Cl results in a 13-fold enhancement of fluorescence.24 A divalent tin dipyrromethene complex (III) undergoes a 10-fold increase in fluorescent quantum yield on dechlorination by AgOTf which is reversible on addition of Bu4NCl.25 Ethynyl- and butadiynyl-bridged lead hexa(butoxy)phthalocyanine complexes show non-linear absorption with reduced transmission through the sample at high light intensities. Comcomitant irreversible formation of a photoproduct however minimises the applicability of these dimers as optical limiting materials.26 L1 acts as a highly selective turn-on fluorescent sensor for Pb2+ ions over a wide pH range (4 to 10) at 10 ppb levels even in the presence of other metal ions.27

Copper, silver and gold complexes

High-energy emissive Cu(I) complexes of the formula [Cu(N⁁N)(POP)]+ (POP = bis(2-(diphenylphosphino)phenyl)ether) have been designed through use of diimine ligands with high energy π* LUMOs and utilised in OLED devices. Emission can be tuned between blue and green where N⁁N is 2,2′-bisimidazolyl (λem = 470 nm, τ = 2.1 µs, ϕ = 0.08) and 1-ethyl-2-thiazol-4-yl-1H-benzimidazole (λem = 525, τ = 16.1 µs, ϕ = 0.34).28 The complex [Cu(N⁁P)(POP)]+ (N⁁P = 8-(diphenylphosphino)-quinoline) is luminescent at room temperature in CH2Cl2 solution (λem = 642 nm, τ = 0.13 µs, ϕ = 0.0013). Introduction of a methyl substituent at the 2-quinoline position blue-shifts the emission and increases excited state lifetime and quantum yield (λem = 608 nm, τ = 0.7 µs, ϕ = 0.012) attributed to steric inhibition of an excited state conformational change from tetrahedral to a flattened structure that has a reduced energy MLCT state with faster non-radiative decay.29 Complexes of the formula [Cu(N⁁N)(POP)]+ where N⁁N is an Ar-functionalised imidazo-[4,5-f][1,10]phenanthroline display solid state luminscent intensities tunable on the basis of the N⁁N substituents. With Ar = Ph, coplanarity with the imidazo-phen moiety extends intraligand electron delocalisation and minimises excited state distortion whereas naphthyl and anthracyl aryl substituents significantly deviate from coplanarity resulting in weaker emission.30 The dinuclear complex [Cu2mbim2](PF6)2 (mbim = μ2-bis(3-methylimidazol-2-ylidene-1-yl)methane) exhibits dual emission phosphorescence in the solid state at room temperature from 3MC and 3MLCT mixed excited states.31 [Cu(dtbp)2]+ (dtbp = 2,9-di-tert-butyl-1,10-phen) exhibits significantly blue-shifted and extended lifetime emission (λem = 599 nm, τ = 3.26 µs) compared to a series of complexes with less sterically demanding substituents due to sterically induced conformational rigidity. Excitation in MeCN, however, results in facile ligand substitution.32 The cluster [(bzq)2Pt2Cu6(CC-2-Py)6(I)2]2+ is luminescent at room temperature (λem = 638 nm in CH2Cl2) and is tentatively attributed to having substantial PyCC → Cu 3LMCT character.33 The Cu(I) clusters [Cu6(btt)6] and [Cu4(btt)4] have been prepared (btt = 2-benzothiazolethiolate). [Cu6(btt)6] exhibits unusual near-IR emission (861 nm) whereas [Cu4(btt)4] shows strong red luminescence (621 nm).34

Ratiometric fluorescent sensors for Cu2+ ions including a dansyl-naphthalimide dyad,35 coumarin-36 and rhodamine-based receptors37,38 have been reported. The two-coordinate copper complex with a 1,3-bis-(3,5-dimethylpyrazol-1-ylmethyl)-2-phenyl-2,3-dihydro-1H-pyrimidine ligand acts as an iodide-controlled fluorescent switch.39 The copper(II) complex of 4-(dipicolylaminemethyl)-7-hydroxycoumarin is a selective luminescent sensor for pyrophosphate anions.40 Fluorescein derivatives with morpholine and thiomorpholine groups are highly selective sensors for silver(I) ions.41 The trinuclear complex [Ag3(3,5-bis(trifluoromethyl)pyrazolate] forms a π-stacked adduct with naphthalene in the solid state which exhibits sensitised naphthalene phosphorescence due to the spin–orbit coupling of the heavy metal atoms.42 The Ag4S4 metallacyclic complexes [Ag(LPEt)4] and [Ag2(LPEt)(LPEtH)2].2X (LPEtH = 2-(2-(diphenylphosphino)-benzylidene)-N-ethylthiosemicarbazone, X = NO3−, AcO−) are strongly luminescent with the position of emission maxima strongly affected by complex charge and the nature of the counterion.43

The biscyclometallated complex [Au(C⁁N⁁C)(CCC6H4NPh2)] (C⁁N⁁C = 2,6-diphenylpyridine) exhibits electrogenerated chemiluminescence, the first reported example for a discrete gold complex.44 A series of related NHC complexes, [Au(C⁁N⁁C)(NHC)]+, exhibit luminescence in solution at room temperature arising from π → π* intra-cyclometallate ligand excited state.45 Selenolate complexes [Au(SeC6H4-2-(CH2NMe2))(PR3)] (PR3 = PPh3, PPh2py) are luminescent as solids and in frozen solutions at 77 K but not at room temperature. Emission is proposed to arise from the formation of Au⋯Au interactions through molecular aggregation and derives from mixed 3MC and 3LMMCT excited states.46 Complexes of the formula [Au(dppb)2]X (dppb = 1,2-bis(diphenylphosphino)-benzene) exhibit dramatically altered solid state luminescent properties on changing the counterion X−. When X− is Cl− and BF4−, the complex is intensely blue phosphorescent (481 nm, ϕ = 0.86 and 490 nm, ϕ = 0.90 respectively) but distortion from a symmetrical tetrahedral geometry in the solid state for the PF6− salt results in drastically red-shifted luminescence with low quantum yield (596 nm, ϕ = 0.04).47 Dinuclear complexes with bridging ortho-, meta- and para-dicarborane ligands of the formula [(C2B10H10)(AuPPh3)2]2 exhibit luminescence from MLCT excited states at 500–555 nm.48 Isonitrile complexes of the formula [(p-tosyl-CH2NC)AuX] (X = Cl−, Br− and I−) are non-luminescent even at 77 K but display blue-green emission at 30 K with red-shifting of emission with increasing softness of the halide ligand.49 A trinuclear complex of the formula [MeC{CH2P(Ph2)AuCC–C6H4O(CH2)2O(CH2)2Me}3] (λem = 454 nm in CH2Cl2) acts as a selective probe for Mg2+ ions. Binding of a Mg2+ ion to the three oligoether arms, results in emission at 675 nm due to formation of Au⋯Au interactions and a reduced HOMO–LUMO gap.50

A gold–silver cluster with a central hypercoordinated central carbon atom of the formula [Au6Ag2(C)(PPh2py)6](BF4)4 is intensely luminescent in solution at room temperature tentatively assigned as arising from a cluster-based MC excited state.51 A series of gold–copper clusters with a cyclic [Au3(R2PC6H4PR2)3]3+ belt encapsulating a [Au3Cu2(CCAr)]− core display intense luminescence that is only weakly quenched by oxygen.52 These clusters display two-photon induced luminescence when irradiated at 840 nm.53 A cluster with a silver-gold-bisacetylide core with the formula [{Au8Ag10(CCPh)16}{(PhCCAu)2(Ph2P(C6H4)3PPh2)}2] displays similarly intense phosphorescence at 568 nm with a quantum yield of 0.70 under air.54 Series of gold and silver chain complexes with bridging linear tetraphosphine ligands show similarly intense luminescence in the presence of non-coordinating anions originating from triplet ndσ* → (n + 1)pσ excited states.55,56

Zinc, cadmium and mercury complexes

Significant efforts have been made toward the development of chelators for the selective turn-on fluorescent sensing of Zn2+ ions. A variety of sensors have been developed including a 1,2,3-triazolylisobenzoquinoline-1,3-dione substituted cyclam,57 polyamine-bpy or –phen macrocycles,58 tris(quinolylmethyl)amine,59 bis(quinolylmethyl)-ethylenediamine,60 tris(6-phenyl-2-pyridylmethyl)amine,61 polyaminophenols,62 benzoxadiazolylbidpicolylamine63 and bis(2-picolyl-4-picolylamine)fluorescein derivatives.64 Ratiometric Zn2+ fluorescent sensors involving 2-(pyridin-2-yl)benzimidazole-,65 coumarin-66 and furoquinoline-substituted67 bispicolylamine frameworks have also been investigated. The receptor (7-alkoxyquinolin-2-ylmethyl)(bispicolyl)amine exhibit Zn2+ responsive two-photon induced luminescence when irradiated at 800 nm.68 The heteroditopic ligand L2 gives Zn2+ responsive luminescence over a concentration range of six orders of magnitude through successive Zn2+ binding, first to the tripicolyamineethylenediamine domain (10−14 to 10−11 M, λem = ∼409 nm) and then to the bpy domain (10−11 to 10−8 M, λem = ∼450 nm).69

[Zn2(L)2(NO3)2] (L = 6-amino-2-[(2-hydroxybenzylidene)amino]hexanionc acid) acts as a luminescent sensor for a variety of anions, in particular phosphates, and as a selective Cu2+ sensor through Zn2+ displacement.70,71 An amphiphilic bis(salicylaldiminato)zinc(II) complex with pendant hydrophobic ponytails forms defined aggregates in non-coordinating solvents such as CH2Cl2 (λem = 597 nm, ϕ = 0.07) but switches to the monomer in THF with dramatically enhanced fluorescence (λem = 593 nm, ϕ = 0.24) thereby acting as a fluorescent probe for supramolecular aggregation.72 The sulfur complex [Zn(L)(S)2] (L = N-hexyl-3-{2-[4-(terpy-4′-yl)phenyl]ethenyl}carbazole) has been prepared by a facile solvothermal process from ZnS and exhibits two-photon induced luminescence when irradiated at 800 nm.73 Binding of zinc to L3 leads to quenching of pyrene based luminescence. Weak luminescence for the complex [Zn(L3)2] is attributed to a pyrene to terpy charge transfer excited state.74 The ditopic coumarin-based ligand L4 forms dinuclear helicate complexes with dicationic metal ions. Luminescence suffers quenching on binding Cd2+ and Hg2+ but undergoes 20% enhancement on binding Zn2+.75 Complex IV has been developed as a selective fluorescent probe for the detection of neurofibrillary tangles of hyperphosphorylated tau proteins that are indicative of Alzhiemer's Disease.76 Binding of thymidine nucleotides to complex V leads to quenching of monomeric pyrene luminescence and enhanced excimer emission as the pyrene groups are brought into face-to-face contact as the pendant arms hydrogen-bond with the nucleotide.77

The ligand N-(2-((bis(pyridin-2-ylmethyl)amino)methyl)-4-isobutoxyquinolin-8-yl)acetamide acts as a selective fluorescent sensor for Cd2+ ions with a 40-fold enhancement in emission and picomolar sensitivity.78 The related ligand with a 8-methoxy group and a BOC-protected amine in the 7-position is highly fluorescent with both zinc and cadmium with the zinc complex acting a sensor for Cd2+ ions through ratiometric displacement.79 A number of reports have appeared detailing selective fluorescent sensors for Hg2+ ions in solution including receptors based on thiophene-bridged rhodamine,80 polyoxyethylene macrocycle-terphenylbisimine conjugates,81 polyoxyethylene-bridged pyrene containing triazoles,82 quinolinol-substituted tetrathiadiazacyclo-octadecane,83 rhodamine-cryptand conjugates,84 dansyltryptophan methyl ester85 and ferrocene-azaquinoxaline86 architectures and hydroxyquinoline87 derivatives. The complex [Pt(C⁁N⁁N)(L)]+ (C⁁N⁁N = 6-phenyl-bpy, L = 2-amino-4phenylamino-6-(4-pyridinyl)-1,3,5-triazine) acts as an indirect nanomolar turn-on fluorescent probe for Hg2+ in the presence of single-stranded DNA. Hg2+ induced DNA folding into a double hairpin allows intercalation of the complex thereby shielding it from solvent interactions, suppressing non-radiative decay and turning on 3MLCT-based emission.88 A series of pyridyl bisimine complexes of the formula [Hg(2,6-{ArNCMe}2C5H3N)(Cl)2] exhibit blue luminescence with emission maxima in CH2Cl2 at ∼ 395–427 nm attributed to ligand centred π* → π transitions.89

Miscellaneous transition metal complexes

A series of pyrazine-, 2,3-pyridine- and 3,4-pyridine-fused tetraazachlorin (TAC) and tetraazabacteriochlorin (TABC) nickel complexes have been investigated by UV-vis absorption spectroscopy and show a large splitting of the Q-band whereas aazaisobacteriochlorin (TAiBC) complexes show a single absorption band whose position shifts strongly between isomers.90 A cobalt(II) nitrate complex with a 1,6-bis(thiazolin-2-yl)-1,6-dithiahex-3-ene ligand is a selective switch-on fluorescence sensor for fluoride ions with quantum yield enhanced from 0.11 to 0.38 on addition of 5 equivalents of F−.91 The photoexcited state properties of MM quadruple bonded dinuclear complexes of the formula [M2(μ2-2,4,6-iPr3C6H2CO2)2(μ2-ThCOS)2] (ThCOS = 2-thienylthiocarboxylato, M = Mo, W) have been investigated. Both complexes exhibit intense 1MLCT absorption bands and are emissive in the near-IR at 77 K with emission maxima at ∼ 1100 nm. Emission from the Mo2 complex is assigned as arising from a triplet MoMo δ → δ* excited state whereas emission from the W2 complex is derived from a 3MLCT state. Femtosecond transient absorption studies show transients with short lifetimes from 400 fs to 4 ps assigned to decay of the 1MLCT S1 state which do not decay fully indicative of intersystem crossing to longer lived triplet states. Nanosecond TA measurements yield MoMo δδ* and 3WLCT lifetimes of 51 µs and 3–10 ns respectively.92

Rhenium complexes

Neutral dinuclear complexes with bridging diazine ligands of the form [Re2(CO)3(μ2-Cl)2(μ2-diazine)] suitable for OLED applications have been shown to have the highest luminescence quantum yields for neutral Re tricarbonyls (8 to 22%).93 The excited state relaxation dynamics of [Re(CO)3X(phen-macrocycle)] complexes (X = Cl, Etpy) have been investigated by picosecond TRIR where a bulky polyether macrocyclic chain restricts solvent access to the Re(CO)3 moiety.94 The unique solvent dependent photophysical properties of the complex [Re(bpy)(CO)3(3-(C(O)C12H25)pyridine)]+ have been investigated. MLCT emission is blue shifted with enhanced intensity and extended lifetime going from acetonitrile to aqueous solutions, which is attributed to a hydrophobically driven fold-back conformational change where the alkyl chain restricts access of solvent molecules to the bpy ligand.95 Re-based rectangles with 4,4′-bpy and electron rich quinone bridging ligands have been prepared and shown to exhibit multiple 3IL, 3MLCT and 3LLCT emission.96 The luminescent complex [Re(4,4′-(CO2H)2-bpy)(CO)3(4,4′-bpy)]+ with three protonation sites shows a wide dynamic pH sensing range with possible applications as an on-off-on-type molecular switch.97 A series of rhenium tricarbonyl bisimine complexes containing a triarylboron moiety have been prepared and show reduced luminescence on fluoride ion binding.98 The fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) properties of a range of rhenium bpy complexes with oligoether-appended coumarin groups have been studied along with their metal ion binding behaviours. Binding of Mg2+ results in conformational changes that reduce coumarin to Re FRET efficiency resulting in reduced luminescent intensity for Re and increased luminescence for the coumarin moiety.99,100 Complexes of the type [Re(phen)(CO)3(HT)]+ where HT is a hole transporting ligand (4-(CH2CH2NMe2)py, 4-(CHCHNMe2)py or 4-(CCNMe2)py) show diminished PLQY as conjugation in HT increases, deactivating MLCT emission by overlap with non-emissive ILHT states.101 Bis-biotin complexes [Re(N⁁N)(CO)3py]+ (N⁁N = e.g. 4,4′-bis{[2-(biotinamido)ethyl]aminocarbonyl}-2,2′-bipyridine) show increased emission intensity and prolonged lifetime on binding with avidin.102 A rhenium(I) complex containing a dipicolylamine derivatised pyridine ligand exhibits enhanced emission and lifetime elongation in the presence of Zn(II) and Cd(II) ions.103 Rhenium(I) Re(CO)3Br complexes with second generation carbazole dendron-appended phenanthroline ligands show diminshed emission compared to their monocarbazole appended parent complexes. This has tentatively been ascribed to carbazole centred HOMO–LUMO transitions disrupting emissive MLCT states.104 The photophysical properties of fluorinated [Re(N⁁N)(CO)3Cl] bpy complexes have also been studied in supercritical CO2 and are potential photodriven CO2 reduction catalysts.105

Ruthenium and osmium complexes

[Ru(bpy)3]2+ has been shown to be able to act as a molecular encoder through the compression of 4 photon excitation and electrochemical input bits to 2 output bits.106 The excited states of [Ru(terpy)(bpy)(H2O)], [Ru(terpy)(bpy)Cl]+ and [Ru(terpy)2]2+ have been investigated. DFT calculations demonstrate that the complexes have closely spaced 3MC and 3MLCT states and that the 3MLCT state is stabilised in polar solvents.107 The heteroleptic complex [Ru(terpy)(tripy)]2+ (tripy = 2,6-bis(1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)pyridine) displays a luminescent maximum at 77 K that is blue-shifted by 20 nm relative to [Ru(terpy)2]2+ and is attributed to the presence of a relatively low lying π* orbital that allows more effective metal 4d to ligand back bonding.108 Resonance Raman spectroscopy has been used to determine the localisation of the 1MLCT excited state in complexes of the formula [Ru(4,4′-tBu2bpy)2(3,8-(R)2-1,10-phen)]2+ (R = H, Br, 3,5-(CF3)2C6H3 and –CC–Ph). For R = –CC–Ph resonant Raman signals show significant localisation of the 1MLCT states on the substituted phen ligand rather than the bpy-based ligands.109 Deep-red emitting Ru terpy complexes with a 2-phenyl-4,6-dipyridin-2-yl)-1,2,3-triazine ligand with excited state lifetimes of 12–15 ns have been prepared and show good stability in their LEEC devices.110

The complex [Ru(L5)2]2+ has been prepared and exhibits 3MLCT emission with high quantum yield (13%) and long excited state lifetime (1.36 µs) in aerated solvent at room temperature.111 A series of ruthenium complexes with a benzodipyridophenazine (L7) have been prepared and are efficient singlet oxygen sensitisers. In contrast to known dipyridophenazine (L6) analogues which display a MLCT-based lowest lying excited state, all benzodipyridophenazine complexes exhibited a triplet π–π* lowest lying excited state.112 The linear and non-linear optical properties of a series of complexes with pendant 4-N,N-dimethylaminostyryl)pyridinium groups have been investigated. Luminescence quenching is observed in these complexes but even the terpy-based complexes give detectable room temperature emission.113 DFT studies of the complex [Ru(bpy)2L]2+ (L = 2-(tetrazol-5-yl)pyrazine) have been carried out. Calculations suggest that the observed increase in quantum yield and excited state lifetime on methylation of the tetrazole ring is due to buffering of the complex from solvent interactions and enhanced MLCT participation in the excited state.114 Ordered ultra-thin films of the complex [Ru(4,7-(SO3-C6H4)2phen)3]4− display well defined polarised red light luminescence.115 The photophysical effects of N⁁C⁁N versus C⁁N⁁N (dipyridylbenzene/phenyl-bpy) cyclometallation have been investigated. The MLCT state of [Ru(N⁁C⁁N)(terpy)]2+ is associated primarily with the terpy ligand whereas that of the C⁁N⁁N isomer is associated with both ligands.116 The light absorption properties of a series of cyclometallated ruthenium complexes have been investigated through variation of ligand substituents117 and their viability for use as DSSC chromophores has been assessed.118

A series of isocyanide complexes of the formula [Ru(q)2(CNR)] (q = 8-quinolate) show Ru(dπ) → q(π*) MLCT absorptions between 400–492 nm and yield orange-red luminescent emission in solution at room temperature (645–680 nm).119 Saturation behaviour of the non-linear optical properties of alkynyl complexes of the formula [Ru(-[CCC6H4]x-[CC-2,5-(OEt)2C6H2]y-CCC6H4-NO2)(dppm)2Cl] (x = 0, 1, 2, y = 1, 2) have been investigated. Quadratic non-linearity and two-photon absorption cross-section reach maximal values for the triphenyleneethynylene ligand.120 A series of complexes of the formula [Ru(N⁁N)(L)(X)]n+ (N⁁N = bpy or phen, L = κ3-trithiacyclononane, X = RCN, RNC: (n = 2) and RCC− (n = 1)) have been prepared. The complexes are luminescent at 77 K and show tuning of emission based on the nature of the monodentate ligand X.121,122

PET has been investigated in Ru and Os terpy-based donor–accept dyads VI using femtosecond pump-probe absorption spectroscopy. Ultra-fast electron transfer rates of up to ∼2 × 1011 s−1 were measured for osmium-based systems which show efficient quenching of 3MLCT emission.123 PET has also been investigated in a series of ruthenium tris-bpy complexes with pendant naphthalenebisimide electron acceptor groups (e.g.VII). TA spectroscopy indicates that the MLCT excited states in these complexes decay primarily via electron transfer to form a charge separated state.124 A homoleptic tris-bpy complex with a pendant dithienylcyclopentene group on each ligand undergoes sensitised dithienyl cyclisation upon irradiation of the MLCT band of the complex.125

Ruthenium(II) polypyridyl complexes continue to be an active area of study for their application as sensitising chromophores in DSSC applications. Electron injection dynamics and quantum yields of DSSC sensitised by the [Ru(dcb)2(NCS)2] dye have been studied by time-resolved single photon counting to examine the key parameters influencing the efficiency of electron injection in DSSC devices.126 The complex [Ru(L8)(dcb)(NCS)2] with an electron-rich hexylthio-substituted ligand has been prepared and gives an excellent DSSC power conversion efficiency of 11.5%.127 A similar complex where the ligand is appended with 5-octyl-2,2′-bis(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) groups is shown to have an extremely high extinction coefficient of 2.74 × 104 M−1 cm−1 at 521 nm and yields DSSC of 10.7% efficiency.128 [Ru(L9)(dcb)(NCS)2] exhibits a low energy MLCT band (547 nm) with high molar absorption coefficient (1.93 × 104 M−1 cm−1). Thin film cells using this sensitiser have good conversion efficiencies of ∼8%.129 Complexes of the type [Ru(dcb)2(L)] (L = S2CN–CN, S2C–OEt) have been investigated for DSSC sensitisation. An efficiency of 1.2% was obtained for devices with dyes where L is S2C-OEt.130 [Ru(L10)(dcb)(NCS)2] acts as a dual pH induced on-off-on/on-off-on-off-type emission switch and yields DSSC with an overall efficiency of 1.5%.131 [Ru(L11)(dcb)(NCS)2] has been shown to give DSSC IPCE values in excess of 60% over the spectral range 420 to 610 nm with an overall efficiency of ∼7.8% for these devices compared to 7.9% for devices fabricated using the N719 dye.132

The interfacial electron transfer dynamics of [Ru(L)(bpy)(NCS)2] (L = 4-[2-(4′-methyl-2,2′bipyridin-4-yl)vinyl]benzene-1,2-diol) bound to TiO2 have been investigated by TA spectroscopy with electron injection occurring in <100 fs.133 [Ru(otip)(dcb)(NCS)2] (otip = 2-(4-octylphenyl)-1H-imidazo[4,5-f][1,10]phenanthroline) exhibits broad MLCT bands (ε = 9.9 × 103 M−1 cm−1 at 519 nm) and its TiO2-based DSSC devices have high IPCE values across the entire visible spectrum (81.6% at 530 nm) and an overall efficiency 8.3%.134 [Ru(L)(dcb)(NCS)2] where L = 4,4′-di(5-hexylthieno(3,2-b)thiophen-2-yl)-2,2′-dipyridylhexylamine exhibits a low energy MLCT band with high extinction coefficient (543 nm, 1.56 × 104 M−1 cm−1). Fabricated DSSC show high efficiencies of 9.03% compared to 8.88% for the N3 dye under the same conditions.135 Polypyridyl pyrazolate complexes VIII and IX have high molar extinction coefficient MLCT bands (ε = 16,600 M−1 cm−1 at 424 nm and 19800 M−1 cm−1 at 550 nm respectively) and yield highly efficient DSSC devices (10.05% and 8.07% respectively).136,137

[Os(bpy)3]2+ has been tethered to zinc/cadmium/selenium-based nanocrystals. Nanocrystal luminescence is invariant on the presence of oxygen whereas that of Os is strongly quenched allowing for two-photon absorbing ratiometric oxygen sensing.138 A dyad consisting of [Os(bpy)3]2+ and a boron subphthalocyanine (SubPc) with a 1,4-bisethynylbenzene bridge shows highly quenched SubPc S1 luminescence on excitation of the Q-band by singlet to triplet energy transfer to Os. Triplet to triplet energy transfer back to the SubPc moiety then takes place with a rate of 4 × 108 s−1 which results in a doubling of the singlet oxygen sensitisation quantum yield compared to that for the non-metallated p-iodophenylethynyl-substituted SubPc.139 Neutral bis(5-(isoquinol-1-yl)-1,2,4-triazolate osmium(II) complexes with dimethylphenylphosphine, 1,2-bis(diphenylphosphino)benzene and 1,2-bis(dimethylphosphino)benzene ligands are near-IR emitters (λem 692 to 805 nm) with quantum yields reaching 15% and have been used in the preparation of near-IR OLED devices.140 The [OsII/III(bpy)2Cl(PyCO2H)]+/2+ redox couple acts as a luminescent probe for a series of enzymatic reactions where luminescent changes occur on production of oxidising or reducing enzymatic reaction products.141 [Os(pytpy)2]2+ (pytpy = 4′-(pyridin-4-yl)terpy) acts as a proton dependent luminescent switch. The diprotonated form is weakly luminescent at 850 nm whereas the deprotonated form is luminescent at 755 nm and is a singlet oxygen sensitiser. Switching between states can be achieved by photolysis of a photoacid and is reversible on application of heat.142

Iridium complexes

Cyclometallated iridium complexes have continued to attract wide interest for their application as OLED phosphors,143–146 DSSC chromophores,147 molecular sensors148,149 and as luminescent biological probes and imaging agents.150,151 Cyclometallated iridium complexes have also been investigated as photosensitisers in water-reduction catalysis.152,153 The emission wavelength has been tuned by introduction of sulfonyl- and fluorine-substituted ppy ligands,154N,C4′-2′,6′-difluoro-2,3′-bipyridinato,155 1,2-biarylimidazole,156 phenyl-thiophene and -furan,157 phenylbenzoquinoline158 and 5-phenyl-1,2,3-triazole159 based bidentate cyclometallated ligands. The complex [Ir(L)(fppy)(CN)] (L = 2,3-difluoro-5-(1-methylimidazol-2-yl)-1-(pyridin-2-yl)-benzene, fppy = 2-(4-fluorophenyl)pyridine) exhibits blue luminescence (493 & 468 nm, τ = 1.4 µs) with high quantum yield (0.63).160 Heteroleptic complexes with N,N′-diisopropylbenzamidinate ancillary ligands show highly efficient red luminescence and yield OLED devices with low driving voltages.161,162 Emission wavelength tuning has also been achieved through use of phosphine-substituted imidazolate, bisimidazole,163 cyclometallated fluorinated benzylphosphine164,165 and NHC,166 1-pyridylimididazo[1,5-α]pyridine167 and acac-based168 ancillary ligands. Iridium ppy complexes with carbazole-substituted diketonate acac analogues show good luminescence efficiences of up to 36% from red to green in polystrene blends.169 The complex [Ir(mpi)2(acac)] (mpi = N,C2′-1-methyl-2-phenylimidazole) exhibits single molecule white light phosphoresence with a maximum intensity at 570 nm, ϕ = 0.015 and τ = 1.96 µs over the range 440 to 800 nm.170 Complexes of the formula [Ir(ppy)2(pytz)]+ (pytz = 2-(1-R-1,2,3-triazol-4-yl)pyridine, R = e.g. adamantyl) show long luminescent lifetime and high quantum yields (τ = 2.8 µs, ϕ = 0.23) most likely due to the rigidity of the pytz ligand and shielding of MLCT state from quenching by solvent and oxygen molecules.171

Heteroleptic biscyclometallated complexes with ancillary ferrocene-172 and tetrathiofulvalene-substituted173 ligands display reversible redox luminescent switching with enhanced luminescence upon oxidation of the ferrocene and fulvalene moieties. The inclusion of phosphorescent iridium complex cores within dendrimers has been investigated and yield increased luminescent efficiency through protection of the luminescent excited state through inhibition of intermolcular triplet–triplet annihilation.174–177 Femtosecond luminescence and TA spectroscopy have been used to demonstrate ultrafast intersystem crossing in the complex [Ir(piq)3] (piq = 1-phenylisoquinoline).178 A cationic complex containing a triarylamine donor moiety and a phenanthroline acceptor ligand has been shown to exhibit an unusually long-lived charged separated excited state (0.04 and 1.7 µs) due to inverted Marcus region effects and spin selection rules.179

Platinum complexes

Cycloplatinated complexes have also been shown to have applications as phosphors for OLED applications. The wavelength of emission of platinum(II) complexes has been tuned by use of cyclometallated substituted thienylpyridine ligands.180 [Pt(L)(acac)] complexes (where L is a ppy ligand with -GePh3 and -OPh substituents in the 4-phenyl position) have been used as phosphors in WOLED devices and show the best quality white light emission yet reported.181 Excimer formation by [Pt(dfppy)(acac)] has been investigated by DFT methods. Calculations show that Pt–Pt interactions are crucial for excited state dimer formation and that π–π interactions play a significant role in determining the optimum excimer emission geometry.182 The role of weak arene-based Lewis bases in exciplex quenching has been investigated with the complex [Pt(N⁁C)(acac)] (N⁁C = 5-nitro-2-phenylpyridine).183 The luminescent properties of ppy and benzoquinolinate platinum(II) nitrile complexes have been compared to their isocyanide analogues. Isocyanide complexes show significantly blue-shifted emission with longer luminescent lifetimes assigned to mixed 3LC/3MLCT phosphorescence, and in concentrated glassy solutions to excimeric 3ππ* or 3MMLCT transitions.184 The thermochromic luminescence of the dinuclear complex [Pt(ppy)(μ-Ph2pz)]2 (Ph2pz = 3,5-diphenylpyrazolate) has been investigated and involves intramolecular dz2 interactions. Cooling from RT to 77 K results in a blue-shift of emission in contrast to known intermolecular Pt–Pt interactions.185

The excited state properties of the highly luminescent N⁁C⁁N ligated [Pt(dpyb)Cl] (dpyb = 1,3-di(2-pyridyl)benzene) have been investigated by high resolution spectroscopy at cryogenic temperatures. Huang-Rhys parameters of S ≈ 0.1 were determined which are smaller than those for ppy based complexes (S ≈ 0.2) and are indicative of the smaller S0 to T1 structural changes on excitation due to the greater rigidity of the Pt(N⁁C⁁N) system.186 Incorporation of electron rich anisoles, aryl amines, oxocrown and azacrown groups into the 5-aryl position yields strongly luminescent complexes that are efficient singlet oxygen sensitisers. Binding of metal ions to the crown-appended complexes results in pronounced blue-shifting of emission.187 Platinum(II) complexes with electron rich chloride or aryl acetylide ligands and an electron deficient terpy or N⁁C⁁N cyclometallated dpyb ligand appended with strongly electron withdrawing groups have been investigated for their second order non-linear optical properties.188 DFT calculations have rationalised the weaker luminescent behaviour of the C⁁N⁁N analogue with a cyclometallated 6-phenylbipyridine ligand. The complex undergoes large structural distortion in the excited state which results in fast non-radiative decay.189 Introduction of alkoxy substituents on the phenyl ring of C⁁N⁁N cyclometallated phenylbipyridine complexes results in increased excited state lifetimes (460–570 ns) and emission quantum yields (0.15–0.21).190 A C⁁N⁁N cycloplatinated complex with a deprotonated phenylcarboxylate group undergoes a 26-fold enhancement of phosphorescent emission on addition of bovine serum albumin and acts as a switch on probe for protein staining and cell imaging.191 Substitution of two C⁁N⁁N complexes onto a xanthene linker results in excimer emission across the visible spectrum and shifted into the near-IR.192 A cyclometallated C⁁N⁁N 2-(5-pyrazolyl)-6-phenylpyridine complex has been investigated for its use in two-photon induced luminescent live cell imaging.193

Platinum acetylides have been the subject of considerable interest due to their photophysical and non-linear optical properties. Femtosecond time-resolved fluorescence and TA spectroscopic studies have been carried out on complexes of the form [Pt(PBu3)2(–CC–{C6H4–CC}n-Ph)2] (n = 0 to 2). Ultrafast ISC was observed with time constants ranging from 40 fs (n = 0) to 2.1 ps (n = 2).194 Di- and tri-metallic acetylide complexes with [Pt(PEt3)2(CCC6H4R)] moieties (R = e.g. N-carbazole, NPh2, Me, methylbenz-imidazol-2-yl) attached to 2,5-diphenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazole- and truxeneacetylide cores have long-lived emission in solution at room temperature and exhibit two-photon absorbance and two-photon induced emission.195 A Pt(II) terpy based complex with a bipyridylacetylide ligand (L12) is luminescent at room temperature with a lifetime of 5.8 µs. Binding of Zn2+ ions to the bpy sub-unit results in the formation of a higher energy emissive state with increased lifetime (26 µs).196 The bisacetylide complex [Pt(L12)2(L13)] shows unusual solid state vapochromic luminescent changes on exposure to a variety of solvent vapours. Under acetone vapour the complex is emissive at 562 nm and displays a dramatic vapoluminescent response red-shift of 185 nm on exposure to THF vapour (λem = 747 nm).197 The complex [Pt(meBIAN)(tda)] (X) has been shown to be a near-IR emitter (λem = 832 nm) and efficient singlet-O2 sensitiser.198

The excited state dynamics of a Pt(II) terpyridine/C60 dyad (XI) have been investigated. No charge separated excited state was detected but TA measurements reveal 3Pt–C60 → Pt–1C60 energy transfer.199 Population of the triplet manifold is shown to sensitise the cyclisation of dithienylethene units appended to acetylide ligands in a bisacetylide Pt(PBu3)2 complex at longer wavelength light (<415 nm) than is possible for the isolated ligand (<340 nm).200 A platinum terpy complex with an appended naphthalene diimide electron acceptor group forms a long-lived charge separated excited state with a very slow back electron transfer rate of 8 × 104 s−1 at room temperature in MeCN.201 Complexes with dimesitylboryl-substituted bpy ligands show turn-on luminescence in the presence of fluoride ions.202 Cycloplatinated pyridyl azaindolyl complexes with pendant boryl groups also show bright fluoride switchable phosphorescence.203 Dipyridophenazine complexes with deprotonated carboxylate groups act as probes for G-quadruplex DNA resulting in 293-fold luminescence enhancement.204 Pt(II) bis((diimidazolylidene)methane) complexes have been shown to be near-UV/deep blue emitters.205 Bis(Pt{salphen}) complexes with rigid xanthene and dibenzofuran backbones have been prepared and show blue shifted emission profiles on addition of Pb2+ ions with quenching of emission with Cu2+ and Hg2+.206

Supramolecular transition-metal assemblies

Supramolecular assemblies containing photo- and redox-active metal centres are an area of intense interest due to their potential applications in molecular electronics, artificial photosynthesis and solar energy harvesting and conversion. The extent of intermetallic communication in a series of homo- and hetero-dinuclear rutheniun and osmium complexes has been investigated. The complexes [(bpy)2Ru(pytr-bpy)Os(bpy)2]3+ and [(bpy)2Os(bpbt)Os(bpy)2]2+ (pytr-bpy = 3-(2,2′-bpyridin-6-yl)-5-(pyrdin-2-yl)-1,2,4-triazolato; bpbt = 5,5′-bis-(pyridin-2″-yl)-3,3′-bis-1,2,4-triazolato) exhibit intervalence charge transfer bands on partial oxidation in the near-IR at ∼6000 and 8000 cm−1 respectively which disappear on full oxidation.207 The Ru/Os dinculear complex XII with a non-conjugated spiropyran bridge exhibits Os-based emission from a 3MLCT excited state with quenched Ru derived emission due to energy transfer. The rate of energy transfer, kET, was determined to be 6.6 × 107 s−1 indicating that the spiropyran bridge is a fairly good insulator of energy transfer.208 The metallomacrocyclic complex [{(bpy)2Ru(qtpy)}2{Re(CO)3Cl}2]4+ (qtpy = 2,2′∶4,4″∶4′,4‴-quaterpyridyl) displays a DNA binding affinity two to three orders of magnitude greater than those of its component complex [(bpy)2Ru(qtpy)]2+ and results in a 33% reduction in luminescence intensity with a blue-shift from 662 to 655 nm. Binding of the isolated Ru component complex results in little change in luminescence suggesting non-intercalative binding of the macrocycle.209 A dyad containing bis(4,4′-diethylamido-bpy)2Ru (Rubpyam) and Ru(CN)4 moieties bridged by 1,2-bis(5′-methyl-2,2′-bpyridin-5-yl)ethane shows solvent induced reversal of the direction of photoinduced energy transfer. Time-resolved IR studies show that RuCN4 → Rubpyam PEnT occurs due to the higher energy 3MLCT state of the Ru(CN)4 moiety compared to that of the Rubpyam fragment in D2O whereas the Ru(CN)43MLCT state lies at lower energy in acetonitrile solutions.210 A series of di- and tri-nuclear Ru(II) and Ir(III) phenylene bridged complexes with a benzene core have been investigated. The bis-Ru(terpy) adduct of XIII shows quenching of Ir-based luminescence through energy transfer to the Ru-based chromophores.211 The dinuclear complex [(terpy)Ru(bdppb)Ru(terpy)]4+ (bdppb = 1,4-bis[(2,6-dipyrazol-1-yl)pyridin-4-yl]benzene) is luminescent below 100 K at 619 nm, occurring at the same wavelength as [Ru(terpy)2]2+ indicating that emission arises from a terpy-centred 3MLCT state.212

The asymmetric linear dinuclear 1,4-bis(2,2′∶6′,2″-terpyridin-4′-yl)benzene-bridged complex [(p-BrC6H4-terpy)Ru(terpy-C6H4-terpy)Ru(L)]4+ (L = 4′-p-bromophenyl-2,6-bis(pyridin-2-yl)-1,3,5-triazine) exhibits luminescence at 720 nm from a 3MLCT excited state associated with the triazine-based peripheral ligand. TA spectroscopy reveals fast and efficient energy transfer from the terpy-based chromophore to the triazine-based ruthenium centre.213 Bisterdentate cyclometallated iridium complexes have been used for the preparation of oligometallic supramolecular complexes. The heterotrinuclear complex XIV displays luminescence characteristic of Ru(bpy) 3MLCT-based emission with efficient energy transfer from the Ir terminus to Ru.214 The dinuclear cyclometallated complexes [(terpy)Ru(tpp)Ru(N⁁C⁁N)]3+ and [(N⁁C⁁N)Ru(tpp)Ru-(N⁁C⁁N)]2+ (tpp = 2,3,5,6-tetrakis(pyridin-2-yl)pyrazine, N⁁C⁁N = 1,3-bis(pyridin-2-yl)benzene) have red-shifted absorptions relative to the bis-terpy analogue which tail into the near-IR. One electron oxidation leads to near-IR absorptions at 1050 and 1661 nm respectively indicative of intervalence charge transfer.215 The tetranuclear complex [Ru{(μ-dpp)Ru(bpy)2}3]8+ (dpp = 2,3-bis(pyridin-2-yl)pyrazine) has been investigated as a photosensitiser for IrO2 mediated water oxidation and shows superior performance over the mononuclear sensitiser [Ru(bpy)3]2+.216 Homo- and hetero-trinuclear Ru(bpy)2 and Re(CO)3Cl complexes of the tritopic ligand tris[(4′-methyl-2,2′-biyridin-4-yl)methyl]carbinol have been prepared. In the Ru2Re and RuRe2 complexes, rhenium based luminescence is essentially quenched with enhancement of Ru derived emission with an energy transfer rate for the latter of 1.7 × 108 s−1. Both heterometallic complexes act as photodriven CO2 reduction catalysts.217 The complex [Cl2Rh{(μ-dpp)Ru(bpy)2}2]5+ is a hydrogen evolving photocatalyst. The complex possesses a Rh localised LUMO where photoinitiated electron collection occurs via 3MLCT and/or 3MMCT excitation.218 The photophysics of the Ru–Pd hydrogen evolving photocatalyst XV have been investigated by resonance Raman and ultrafast absorption spectroscopy. PET from Ru to Pd proceeds within nanoseconds through sequential formation of a photoexcited Ru centre, electron transfer to the bridging ligand and subsequent transfer to the Pd centre. The rate of transfer is faster in polar solvents through solvation stabilisation of the charge-separated state.219 The dinuclear Ru bpy-phthalocyanine dyad XVI has been applied as a DSSC chromophoric sensitiser. It is a more efficient sensitiser on a per molecule basis but has lower overall efficiency than the N719 dye due to lower density of TiO2 surface coverage.220

Metallo-porphyrins and -phthalocyanines

Copper(II) and nickel(II) porphycene complexes with a [Cp*Ru]+ moiety π-bonded to the macrocycle exhibit intense Q-type bands around 870 nm. Femto- and nano-second TA spectroscopy reveal PET from ruthenium to the macrocycle with a rate of 3.1 × 1010 s−1.221 8-Coordinate complexes [Yb(TTP)(κ2-CH3CO2)(N⁁N)] (TTP = 5,10,15,20-tetraphenylporphyrinate) with phenthroline-derivative ligands are near-IR emissive in the range 980–1003 nm and are enhanced and have longer lifetimes in toluene solutions than the 7-coordinate precursor complex [Yb(TTP)(κ1-CH3CO2)(MeOH)2].222 [Pt(TPBP)] (TPBP = meso-tetraphenyltetrabenzoporphyrinate) sensitised blue-green upconverted emission from 2-chloro-5,10-bisphenylethynylanthracene occurs at 490 nm after selective excitation of [Pt(TPBP)] at 635 nm via anthracene triplet–triple annihilation (TTA).223 Similar results are obtained with [Zn(TTP)] with perylene or coumarin 343 (C343). Here, a triplet C343-[Pt(TTP)] exciplex is formed which then undergoes TTA with a second [Zn(TTP)] triplet resulting in blue light emission.224 A series of Pt(II) and Pd(II) tetrafluoroaryl-substituted tetrabenzoporphyrin complexes give efficient red-luminesence (773–814 nm) with quantum yields >20% with good oxygen partial-pressure dependent quenching.225

Photoinduced energy transfer pathways in the bisporphyrin photodyad XVII have been established and quantified. Absorption by the metallated porphyrin is followed by efficient PEnT via dipole–dipole Förster interactions with a rate of 6.99 × 1010 s−1 and subsequent emission from the free-base porphyrin moiety.226 The green zinc porphyrin complex XVIII shows good photovoltaic properties with DSSC power conversion efficiencies (6.0%) approaching that (6.1%) for the ruthenium-based dye N3 in similar devices.227,228 A series of unsymmetrical tris-tBu-substituted phthalocyanine zinc complexes with carboxylic anchoring groups have been investigated for DSSC applications. Good IPCE values are obtained for dyes with acroylylphenyl spacer (65%) and no spacer (85%) between the phthalocyanine and carboxylic anchor group.229

5-(1′-pyrenyl)-10,15,20-trisarylporphyrinatozinc(II) (aryl = p-MeOC6H4, C6F5) undergoes anomalous solvent polarity dependent quenching of S2 fluorescence through an intramolecular charge transfer state which relaxes to the S1 state with high quantum yield.230 A dyad consisting of 5-(p-phenolate)-10,15,20-tris(4-diphenylaminophenyl)porphyrinatozinc(II) and a fluorinated boron subphthalocyanine forms a long-lived charge separated state generated on a ∼1012 s−1 timescale with a lifetime of 370 µs.231 PET from the porphyrin S2 state to Re has been monitored in an amide linked zinc porphyrin-Re(bpy)(CO)3Cl dyad. TA measurements allowed the lifetime of the charge separated state to be estimated at 90 ± 10 ps.232

A number of reports have appeared detailing the singlet oxygen sensitisation by zinc phthalocyanine derivatives and oligomers.233–235 A series of zinc di-α-polyether-substituted phthalocyanines show high solubility and low aggregation evidenced by a sharp and red-shifted Q-band (689–701 nm) and are shown to be efficient singlet oxygen sensitisers with high photocytotoxicity.236 Ruthenium tetrakis(tert-butyl)phthalocyanine complexes with dendritic axial oligothiophene ligands with a pyridin-4-yl core have been utilised in bulk heterojunction solar cells. The dendritic ligands act to give absorption over the range 380–550 nm where ruthenium phthalocyanine absorption is weak. This improves overall light absorption and device performance with efficiencies of 1.6% being obtained, by far the best for a phthalocyanine-based bulk heterojunction solar cell.237

Energy and electron/hole transfer studies on a number of directly linked and hydrocarbon bridged diporphyrin complexes have been carried out.238–241 Light excitation induces very fast quantitative charge separation in a zinc porphyrin/gold porphyrin dyad bridged by a bisethynyl quaterthiophene spacer which occurs through a superexchange mechanism. The charge separated lifetime is 3 ns in toluene and recombines to the ground state.242 A bis(zinc porphyrin) dyad bridged by a bis(ethynyl)perylenebis(dicarboximide) derivative forms a charge separated excited state with a lifetime of 290 ps. The TA spectrum of this species matches the summed spectra of the chemically generated Zn porphyrin (ZnP) radical cation and perylene derivative radical anion.243 ZnP oligomers with [–(C6H4CC)Pt–(PBu3)2(CCC6H4)–] spacers exhibit T1 triplet energy transfer from the Pt spacer to ZnP domains with kET rates varying from 2.4 × 104 to 1.3 × 106 s−1. Increase in oligomer length leads to increased kET interpreted by an excitonic process involving delocalisation of the energy along the array.244 Zinc and palladium porphyrin dimers and trimers bridged by similar Pt-based spacers show Pd → Zn S1 and T1 energy transfers with rates of 2 × 109 (S1) and 0.15 × 103 & 4.3 × 103 s−1 (T1).245

Excitation energy migration in light harvesting cyclic ZnP arrays (consisting of meso-linked porphyrin dimers linked by 1,3-phenylene spacers) has been studied by transient absorption anisotropy decay measurements. For Zn12 to Zn18 rings the number of excitation electron hopping sites matches that expected but large discrepancies occur for Zn10, Zn24 and Zn32 rings due to structural distortions imposed by the phenylene spacer geometry.246Anti- and syn- isomers of meso, β-doubly linked ZnP trimers exhibit very different photophysical properties. The syn-trimer has an absorption spectrum with extinction coefficients much smaller than that of the anti-isomer and exhibits unusually low energy IR fluorescence (1048 nm) whereas luminescence from the anti-isomer is hardly detectable.247 10,10′-bis(5-azaindol-2-yl)-appended meso,meso’-linked ZnP dimers self assemble to form a fluorescent trimeric hexanuclear assembly with a trigonal prismatic orientation of zinc centres.248 A trimer with three zinc porphyrins, amide coupled to a benzene core (P), forms a self assembled co-facial hexanuclear structure with three bispyridyl-derivatised perylene(biscarboximide) linker molecules (L) with the formula P2L3. Porphyrin luminescence is quenched through intramolecular electron transfer to the perylene bridges with a rate of charge separation formation of ∼1010 s−1 and a lifetime of 110 ps in dichloromethane and 6 ns in toluene.249 The effect of ligand coordination to zinc on charge transfer has been investigated in Zn/Au porphyrin triad and tetrad complexes in which the terminal porphryrins are Zn and Au metallated with free-base internal porhyrins. Ligation of Cl− allows the observation of a charge separated excited state in toluene solutions with a lifetime of 277 ns.250Meso-meso directly linked ZnP dimers, doubly β-functionalised with carboxylic anchoring groups have been utilised as sensitisers in TiO2-based DSSCs. IPCE values of 47% were obtained at the Soret band with overall efficiencies reaching 4.2%.251Meso,β-linked dimers with a –CHCH2C6H4– bridge show ultrafast and efficient electron injection into TiO2, absorbed photon to photocurrent efficiencies of 70% and overall device efficiencies of ∼3%.252

A number of reports have appeared involving porphyrin/phthalocyanine-fullerene donor–acceptor assemblies. Mn(III), Fe(III), Ni(II) and Cu(II) dendronised porphyrin complexes with a C60 electron acceptor doubly tethered at opposing sides of the porhyrin ring form charge separated radical ion pair states with lifetimes of 15 to >3000 ps observed dependent largely on solvent and the coordinated metal.253 Dendritic ZnP-fullerene conjugates of the form C60–(H2P)–(ZnP)3 (H2P = free-base porphyrin) mediate efficient light harvesting and charge transfer processes. Absorption initially excites the periphary, forming C60–(H2P)–*(ZnP)3, and is followed by singlet PEnT to yield C60–*(H2P)–(ZnP)3. The free-base porphyrin reduces the C60 to yield C60−˙–(H2P+˙)–(ZnP)3 which then undergoes charge-shift to generate C60−˙–(H2P)–{(ZnP)3+˙} for which lifetimes of 460 ns have been found.254 Steady-state fluorescence, lifetime and TA studies show that a supramolecular porphyrin/fullerene assembly (schematically represented by structure XIX) undergoes PEnT from the nonazinc macrocyclic ring to a tenth zinc porphyrin moiety appended to the tripodal ligand. Electron transfer to the C60 moiety then occurs to yield a charge separated state with 94% efficiency.255 PEnT in ruthenium phthalocyanine complexes, Ru(Pc), with axially coordinated N-(4-pyridyl)-3,4-fulleropyrrolidine ligands (C60Py) has been investigated. The complex [Ru(Pc)(C60Py)(CO)] forms a charge separated excited state with a lifetime of 170 ns.256 A multiantenna array naphthalene and borondipyrrolmethene antennae along with two adjacent ZnP groups appended to a hexaphenylbenzene core has been prepared. In the presence of a dipyridylmethylene-C60 acceptor, excitation results in energy transfer from the antennae to the ZnP groups followed by electron transfer from ZnP to the fullerene with a time constant of 3 ps. A resultant charge separated state lifetime of 290 ps was measured.257 Zinc phthalocyanine and porphyrin complexes with macrocyclic oligoether crown receptor groups form non-covalent conjugates with alkylammonium-C60 derivatives. Excitation yields charge separated states with lifetimes in the 4–23 µs range.258,259 Zinc porphyrins tethered to single walled carbon nanotubes via 1,2,3-triazole linkers undergo rapid excited state deactivation via electron transfer to the nanotube. Charge separated states have lifetimes from 200 to 820 ps.260

Lanthanide complexes

Luminescent lanthanide complexes continue to attract considerable interest stemming from their narrow emission spectra with tuning over the visible and near-IR ranges with long excited state lifetimes. Laporte forbidden and therefore weak f–f transitions can be overcome through ligand assisted light harvesting sensitisation and population by energy transfer of lanthanide centred excited states. Homoleptic benzimidazole-substituted pyridine-2-carboxylate Eu complexes [Eu(L14)3] display 2D0 → 7F2 based emission with high quantum yields (56–61%) and long luminescent lifetimes (2.1–2.6 ms).261,262 The Pr3+, Nd3+, Er3+ and Yb3+ complexes [Ln(L15)] show ligand sensitised near-IR luminescence in aqueous solution with a lifetime for the latter of 3 µs.263 6,6″-bis(tetrazol-5-ato)terpy and 6,6′-bis(tetrazol-5-ato)bpy-based ligands are efficient sensitisers of near-IR emission in their Eu3+, Tb3+ and Nd3+ complexes with quantum yields of 5 to 53, 6 to 35 and 0.1 to 0.3% respectively dependent on ligand triplet energies. These are among the highest reported for Nd complexes.264 2,2′-bipyrimidine (bpm) complexes [Ln(bpm)2(NO3)3] (Ln = Sm3+, Tb3+, Dy3+, Eu3+ and Yb3+) show ligand sensitised luminescence with quantum yield for Tb3+ and Eu3+ of 80 and 70% respectively. The uranyl complex [UO2(bpm)(NO3)2] exhibits ligand sensitised luminescence between 465 and 575 nm.265 Europium complexes of chelating trisphosphineoxide ligands, for example [Eu(L16)(hfa)3] (hfa = hexafluoroacetylacetonato), exhibit efficient luminescence with quantum yields of ∼60% and lifetimes of ∼1.2 ms.266

The pentafluoro(fluoren-2-yl)pentanedionate (pffpd) complex [Eu(pffpd)3(L⁁L)] where (L⁁L is a bis(diphenylphosphino)xanthene oxide ligand) has twice the emission quantum yield (48%) compared to that for the bis(diphenylphosphinophenyl)ether analogue (28%). This may be due to the xanthene based ligand having a T1 state intermediate between the S1 and T1 states of the fluorene-based ligand consequently improving overall energy transfer sensitisation of Eu3+ luminescence.267 The complex [Eu(L)3(phen)] (HL = N-(5-phenyl-1,3,4-oxadiazol-2-yl)-benzamide) gives sharp red luminescence at 612 nm (ϕ = 37%). The complex was used as a phosphor in an electroluminescent device with a maximum power efficiency of 0.16 lm W−1.268 A sulfonated tris([hydroxyquinolate]-methyl)triazacyclononane ligand forms stable lanthanide complexes and gives some of the highest reported quantum yields for Yb3+ complexes in aqueous solutions (0.14% at pH 7.4 and 0.55% in D2O).269

A heterometallic complex [Yb(L)2(μ-L)2Na] with 2-(benoxazol-2-yl)-8-hydroxyquinolate-based ligands exhibits surprisingly bright near-IR luminescence at 925–1075 nm. Quantum yields of up to 2.6% in CH2Cl2 solution with a short lifetime of 20 µs were determined.270 Complexes of the formula [Al3(Mq)4(HMq)(μ3-OH)2(μ2-OH)2{Ln(hfa)2}] (Mq = 2-methyl-8-hydroxyquinolate, Ln = Nd, Eu and Yb) exhibit enhanced lanthanide-based luminescent intensities and lifetimes on fluoride ion binding through weakening of O–H bond vibrations by H-bond formation thereby minimising non-radiative decay processes. The Eu-based complex displays broadband emission from both Eu(hfa)2 and Al(Mq)2 based domains whereas Al(Mq)2 → Ln energy transfer results in efficient quenching of Al(Mq)2 derived luminescence for Nd and Yb.271

Sensitisation of lanthanide luminescence by energy transfer in d–f hybrid complexes remains a popular area. Several reports have emerged on the preparation and properties of mixed lanthanide–zinc complexes containing salen type ligands. These complexes show Ln-based luminescent spectra with quenched Zn(salen) emission indicating efficient energy transfer to Ln.272,273 Complexes of the formula [Ln2Cd2(p-toluylate)10(phen)2] (Ln = Nd, Pr, Sm) show emission spectra with evidence of ligand to Ln and Cd to Ln energy transfer sensitisation.274 The metalloligand receptor L17 binds Eu3+ ions resulting in typical Eu3+ luminescent spectra on irradiation of the Pt-based 1MLCT band. L17 luminescence is suppressed confirming energy transfer sensitisation of the lanthanide centre. In CH2CL2/TFA mixtures, Eu3+ binding results in a broad emission at 684 nm tentatively assigned to a charge transfer state involving Eu reduction to Eu2+.275[M(bpy)3]2+ complexes (M = Ru, Os) with pendant aza-18-crown-6 macrocycles are receptors for lanthanide ions. For Ru d → f energy transfer is not observed for Yb(III) but sensitised near-IR luminescence occurs with Nd(III) whereas Os luminescence is quenched by both Yb(III) and Nd(III). The unexpectedly high efficiency of Os → Yb PEnT is explained by an initial formation of a PET Os(III)/Yb(II) charge separated state with subsequent back transfer providing the energy to leave Yb(III) in an emissive state.276

Cyclen-based DO3A ligand (L18) frameworks remain popular lanthanide coordination motifs. A europium coordinated tris(dimethylcarbamoyl) derivative with a pendant phenanthroline exhibits phen sensitised luminescence that is quenched on binding of Cu(II), Fe(II) and Co(II) ions.277 The Ru(phen)2 adduct of the Yb complex of the same ligand displays sensitised near-IR 2F5/2 → 2F7/2 based emission upon Ru 3MLCT excitation.
