Abstract

Over the last ten years, the molecular architectures of p-type donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) conjugated polymers designed for bulk heterojunction (BHJ) photovoltaics, when mixed with fullerenes or n-type polymers, have progressed substantially from one-dimensional (1-D) to branched to two-dimensional (2-D) Dâ€“A conjugated structures. In the 1-D structures, alternating D and A units allow internal charge transfer along the conjugated backbone and increase the effective resonance length, as a result of facilitated Ï€-electron delocalization. Upon progressing from 1-D structures to branched Dâ€“A conjugated polymers (comprising repeating donor units in the main chain with electron-withdrawing side chain units) to 2-D conjugated polymers (having Dâ€“A repeating units on their backbones as well as perpendicular electron-donating groups on their D units), the solubility, effective conjugation length, and photophysical and BHJ photovoltaic properties have all been altered dramatically. The ideal p-type 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymer for use in BHJ photovoltaic devices should possess a low band gap (to broaden the absorption range), excellent packing characteristics (particularly along the out-of-plane direction, ensuring good carrier transport), and suitable energy levels for efficient electron transfer (to fullerene moieties or n-type polymers). In this review, we discuss the effects of the structural characteristics and optical properties of these conjugated polymers as well as their packing characteristics on the device performances.

1. Introduction: donorâ€“acceptor conjugated polymers

Organic photovoltaics (OPVs) is a promising technology for the production of renewable energy, with the attraction of allowing the formation of large-area devices on flexible substrates through simple solution processing. In the current field of OPVs, the most prevalent and efficient active layer design is the bulk heterojunction (BHJ) structure,1â€“6 comprising nanometer-scale-phase-separated blends of p- and n-type materials (defined in terms of their relative differences in electron affinity; e.g., polymers and fullerenes, respectively) that generate large interfacial areas. The fundamental mechanism of photon-to-electron conversion in a BHJ OPV device involves four sequential steps, each with its own conversion efficiency; the overall OPV efficiency is effectively the product of all these four independent step-efficiencies together. First, upon the absorption of a photon, an electron in the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the polymer undergoes photoinduced excitation, moving to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the polymer, forming a Frenkel exciton comprising a coulombically bound electron and hole. Second, some of the generated excitons can diffuse to the pâ€“n (e.g., polymerâ€“fullerene) interfaces within the diffusion length. Third, an exciton at the pâ€“n interface that is subjected to the built-in internal field undergoes charge transfer (CT) at an ultrafast pace (ca. 100 fs)7 to form a CT exciton, where the hole and electron remain in the p- and n-type materials, respectively, held together through coulombic attraction. Fourth, the CT exciton dissociates into a free hole and electron, which are then transported through the p- and n-type materials, respectively, in the BHJ layer, to the anode and cathode, respectively, within a period of time ranging from nano- to microseconds. Because CT at the pâ€“n interface is highly efficient, the most critical aspects affected by the polymer structure (p-type material) are the band gap (photoinduced excitation) of the polymer and the packing of the polymer (carrier transport) in the active layer. In this review, we focus on the effects of the molecular architectures of polymers on their band gaps and packing orientations.

The band gap of the polymer in the active layer largely determines the energy and amount of photons that the active layer can absorb (we note, however, that some fullerene derivatives also absorb a minor portion of the solar spectrum). Therefore, designing suitable polymer structures that absorb the maximum amount of photons remains a major approach toward the production of highly efficient OPVs. An effective way for tuning the band gap of the polymer is to give it a molecular structure in which an electron-rich donor (D) unit is conjugated with an electron-withdrawing acceptor (A) unitâ€”herein termed a â€œDâ€“A conjugated polymerâ€; such a Dâ€“A conjugated structure induces intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) between the D and A units, with the strength of ICT depending on the relative electron affinities of the two units. Because of the rigid nature of conjugated structures, Dâ€“A polymers typically pack into crystalline or ordered structures. The packing orientations of conjugated polymer molecules are, however, affected by their molecular architectures, which also affect their carrier (hole) transport, thereby influencing the photocurrents and efficiencies of the resulting OPV devices.

An ideal Dâ€“A conjugated polymer for BHJ photovoltaics8â€“11 would ideally exhibit four characteristics: (i) a suitable band gap to broaden the range of the absorbed solar spectrum; (ii) a molecular packing orientation that can conform to the direction of the electrodes to ensure good carrier transport; (iii) a low HOMO energy level to enhance the open-circuit voltage (Voc); and (iv) a suitable LUMO energy level for efficient electron transfer to fullerene molecules. The backbone Dâ€“A repeating units of a conjugated polymer largely determine its HOMO and LUMO energy levels and, therefore, its band gap, although the side chains can also have influence to some extent. The molecular weight of the Dâ€“A polymer also has some influence on the band gap when it is below a threshold value (ca. Mn = 20000). Hence, a weak electron-donating unit conjugated to a strong electron-withdrawing unit is necessary to simultaneously decrease the HOMO energy level and band gap of a Dâ€“A conjugated polymer that results in simultaneously increasing the value of Voc and the short-circuit current density (Jsc) of the resulting device when blending the polymer with fullerenes. A relatively low photocurrent arises in an OPV device mainly from a relatively large optical gap of a molecule that absorbs a relatively small portion of the solar spectrum, typically 1.5â€“2 eV (cf. 1.1 eV for silicon). Therefore, it is necessary to develop Dâ€“A conjugated polymers with suitable band gaps to extend the absorption of light from the visible and into the infrared.12

The redistribution of electrons from their original orbitals to the newly hybridized orbitals results in a higher lying hybrid HOMO and a lower lying hybrid LUMO, relative to that of the individual D and A units. Alternating D and A units allows an internal CT process along the conjugated chain and, therefore, increases the effective conjugation area of the Ï€-electrons, leading to tunable band gaps as a result of facilitated Ï€-electron delocalization (i.e., through planarization).13

Because there are not only structural variations in conjugated D, spacer, and A units that constitute Dâ€“A conjugated polymers but also torsion angles between these units, the effective conjugated band gaps are largely determined by the energies of five structural factors. Fig. 2 presents the five energy structural factors that can contribute to the band gap energy (Eg) of a Dâ€“A conjugated polymer.14 The bond length alternation energy factor (EÎ´) is defined as the energy difference between the average lengths of the nominally single and double bonds along the linear Ï€-conjugated backbone.15 The resonance energy factor (Eres) is the energy required to switch from the aromatic form to the quinoid form through delocalization of moving Ï€-electrons along the conjugated chainâ€”converting double bonds into single bonds and synchronously transforming single bonds into double bonds. The value of Eg decreases upon increasing the quinoid character and decreasing the value of EÎ´. The energy factor for the rotational disorder around inter-angular single bonds (EÎ¸) represents the effect of the mean dihedral angle Î¸ between consecutive units contributing to delocalization of Ï€-electrons along the conjugated backbone. The energy factor Esub is related to modulating the HOMO and LUMO energy levels with electron-donating or -withdrawing substituents. The energy factor Eint, which represents the intermolecular interactions of multiple individual polymer chains, has a considerable impact on the magnitude of the band gap energy. Since 2006, linear low bandgap one-dimensional (1-D) conjugated Dâ€“A polymers have been developed widely for BHJ OPVs.16 In 2007, branched conjugated Dâ€“A polymers, comprising main-chain D units and side-chain A units, were also developed for OPVs; this particular molecular architecture can facilitate exciton dissociation when a strong electron-withdrawing group is incorporated in the side chain, with the dissociated electrons sequentially transferring to the side chain and then to the fullerenes. The first two dimensional (2-D) conjugated polymerâ€”polythiophenes with conjugated bi(thienylenevinylene) side chainsâ€”for the application in polymer solar cells were first developed in 2006.17 But not until 2010 and later, a variety of structural modifications were further performed on the D units of 1-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers to evolve into 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers, which were designed to not only have tunable solubility and band gaps but also a tendency toward out-of-plane packing, as we will explain later. Recently, photo-crosslinking of p-type polymers has also been developed to stabilize the active layer morphology to achieve OPVs with longer lifetimes.18Fig. 3 provides schematic representations of the molecular architectures of conjugated Dâ€“A polymers. A 1-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymer having D and A units alternating on the backbone allows delocalization of Ï€-electrons along the backbone or through an ICT processâ€”the overlapped Ï€-electron wave functions between adjacent units allow intramolecular carrier transport. On the other hand, a branched conjugated Dâ€“A polymer incorporating A units on the side chains and attached to the D units constituting the main chain can improve the electron-withdrawing ability and, thus, the dissociation of excitons. The 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymer structure is based on the concept of introducing additional D units, albeit short, normal to the original D units in the 1-D Dâ€“A structure, creating another direction of conjugation that is normal to the direction of the polymer main chain.19 In 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers, the Ï€-electrons can further delocalize along the conjugated chains in the normal direction (even over a short conjugation length) in addition to the main chain direction, increasing the co-planarity and conjugated area and ensuring more effective Ï€-overlap between neighboring polymer chains than that in 1-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers.

In this review, we discuss these three types of molecular architectures: 1-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers (characterized by conjugated alternating D and A units that from a linear main chain); branched conjugated Dâ€“A polymers (characterized by conjugated D units in a linear main chain and side-chain A units conjugated to the D units); and 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers [characterized by additional D1 units linearly conjugated to the backbone D units (D1â€“D conjugation), with normal alignment of Dâ€“A and D1â€“D conjugation]. We also focus on the packing orientations of these different types of polymer architectures.

2. Polymer packing orientation

Because of the rigid nature of conjugated Dâ€“A polymer structures, Dâ€“A polymers readily pack into crystalline or ordered structures with different orientations. If we consider a single conjugated polymer chain as a lamella, there are three possible types of lamellar orientations based on the geometry of the lamella: edge-on, face-on, and end-on. Here, we use the molecular packing of poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) as an example to illustrate edge-on, face-on, and end-on orientations of a polymer that is used in various organic electronic devices (e.g., organic field-effect transistors and OPVs), as demonstrated later. Fig. 4(a)â€“(c) display edge-on, face-on, and end-on packing structures of P3HT, as well as their 2-D (qzvs. qy) grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) patterns. Most reports adopted the notation of the qxy axis rather than the qy axis in their representation owing to the fact that spin-cast samples contained crystalline domains distributed in the xy plane, and the incident X-ray beam could align with either the x- or y-axis. The reason why 2-D GIWAXS analysis20 is required to resolve the various molecular packing directions within an active layer is that the typical active layer is rather thin, usually having a thickness between 100 and 200 nm. Fig. 4(a) reveals the location of the (100) reflection in the out-of-plane (z) direction, qz, and the (010) reflection in the in-plane (y) direction, qy, in the 2-D GIWAXS pattern that can be used to determine the chain-to-chain (lamellar stacking) and conjugated plane-to-conjugated plane (Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking) distances, respectively. The d-spacings of the chain-to-chain stacking and the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking can be calculated using the equations d(100) = 2Ï€/q and d(010) = 2Ï€/q, respectively, where q is the wavenumber of the (100) and (010) reflections in the out-of-plane (z) and in-plane (y) directions, respectively. Fig. 4(b) reveals that (100) and (010) reflections appear in qy and qz, respectively,21 for face-on packing of the conjugated polymer. For end-on packing [Fig. 4(c)], the absence of a reflection in qz and the presence of (100) and (010) reflections in qy indicate that both the edges and the faces of the polymer lamellae are oriented parallel to the substrate.22 These oriented crystalline structures provide anisotropic carrier transport; carrier transport occurs more efficiently in the main chain direction (ICT) than in the Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking direction (heavily overlapped orbitals of Ï€-electrons) than in the chain-to-chain packing direction (the alkyl chains surrounding the conjugated planes).23 As a result, the ICT and Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking direction of the polymers or fullerenesâ€”which possess higher densities of Ï€-electronsâ€”more strongly dictates the optimal carriers' transport direction in the active layer24,25 than the chain-to-chain packing direction. 7. Conclusion and future perspectives

The band gaps and molecular packing orientations of 1-D, branched and 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers are determined primarily by the effective conjugation areas of the polymer structures. The band gaps of Dâ€“A conjugated polymers having 2-D structures are slightly lower than those of 1-D structures having similar chemical units, but the molecular packing can differ significantly in terms of either the packing orientation or the intensity of packing, both of which influence the photocurrent and, thus, the PCE of the device. For example, for 1-D Dâ€“A conjugated polymers, the edge-on orientation is favored when the side chains are on the same side of the CPDT (D) unit, but the face-on orientation is preferred when the CPDT donor is copolymerized with an A unit having greater molecular co-planarity and a larger effective conjugated area (e.g., BTI). For conjugated polymers with BDT as D units, two side chains located symmetrically on each side of the D unit result in preferential face-on-oriented packing. Further evolution from branched to 2-D structures led to BDT-based conjugated polymers having extended conjugated side chains that increased the effective conjugated areas and resulted in symmetrical molecular structures for preferred face-on packing. Compared with BHJ OPV devices based on 1-D and branched conjugated Dâ€“A polymers, devices incorporating 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers had higher short-circuit current densities and open-circuit voltages, resulting in larger PCEs as a result of better charge carrier transport to the respective electrodes. Except for OPV devices based on BDT-TPD, the PCEs of the best devices based on the 2-D structures were higher than those of the devices based on 1-D structures. The average PCEs of the devices incorporating the 2-D Dâ€“A polymers were always higher than those of the devices incorporating the 1-D Dâ€“A polymers, indicative of greater stability. In future studies, 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers should be prepared not only to ensure face-on packing but also to have additional functional groups that can be involved in crosslinking reactions to enhance the stability of their OPV devices. Hence, 2-D conjugated Dâ€“A polymers will remain in the mainstream of molecule design for improving the performance of BHJ OPV devices.
