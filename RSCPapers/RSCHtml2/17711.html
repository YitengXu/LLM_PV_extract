Abstract

The performance of Ru(II) dye-sensitized nanocrystalline TiO2 solar cells (DSSCs) is described with newly synthesized multivalent macromolecules, containing excellent optoelectronic acridinedione linked with 1,2,3-triazole units, via a novel and convenient Cu(I)-catalyzed alkyne and azide 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction. These unique macromolecules have proved to be highly suitable for utilizing additives in I−/I3− redox couple electrolytes. Among the various substitutions, the maximum photo-current conversion efficiency of about 6.4% with a Voc of 810 mV was obtained with electron rich substitutions in the nitrogen heteroatom.

Introduction

The largest challenge for our global society is to find ways to replace the slowly but inevitably vanishing fossil fuel supplies by renewable resources and simultaneously avoid negative effects from the current energy system on climate, environment and health. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have attracted much attention in recent years due to the rapidly increasing energy demand worldwide and also their attractive, and environmentally friendly nature, offering high conversion performance. However, capturing solar energy and converting it into electricity or chemical fuels such as hydrogen at low cost and using abundantly available raw materials remain a huge challenge.

Chemistry is expected to make essential contributions to identify environmentally friendly solutions to this energy problem. An area of great promise in this regard is that of solar converters, generally referred to as “organic photovoltaic cells” (OPVs), that employ organic constituents for light harvesting or charge carrier transport. DSSCs have been well developed by Grätzel and Regan and they reported high power conversion efficiency.1a,b A typical DSSC is composed of a photosensitizing dye adsorbed on a mesoporous film of nanocrystalline TiO2 as a photoelectrode, an electrolyte containing triiodide/iodide redox couple, and a platinized counter electrode. The triiodide/iodide redox couple dissolved in an organic solvent is an excellent mediator for the electron regeneration process in DSSCs.

The DSSC using this type of organic liquid electrolyte achieved a quite high power conversion efficiency,2 although there is an inherent drawback of the cell due to the volatility and possible leakage problem of liquid electrolytes during the long-term out-door operation. In order to improve the performance of the DSSCs, extensive research has been carried out using various non-metallic organic dyes,3a,b semiconductor nano-crystalline TiO2 electrodes,4a–c supporting electrolytes5a–c and counter electrodes.6a,b Considering the mechanism of light to electricity conversion, it has been demonstrated that the physical characteristics of solvents in the electrolyte including the donor number, dielectric constants, viscosity, electron diffusion coefficient, etc. become vital. In particular, the injection of electron from the photo-excited state of the sensitized dye into the conduction band of TiO2 is an essential part in the photoelectric performance of DSSCs. By focusing on the investigation of the influence of electron diffusion coefficient in the multilayer absorption on the TiO2 surface, it can be enhanced to stabilize the conductivity of I−/I3− electrolytes.7 This can be achieved by the optimization of I−/I3− concentration by adding a small amount of electric additives. Grätzel et al.1a,b reported the study on such an I−/I3− electrolyte with TBP as an additive8a and also the additives are still showing their novel performance in modern organic photovoltaic devices.8b They found that TBP adsorbs onto the dye-free nanoparticle surface upon addition, and drastically increases the Voc, the fill factor and the solar energy conversion efficiency without altering Jsc, thus playing important roles in determining the photovoltaic performance of DSSCs.

In this context, organic molecules play a significant role in photovoltaic and light harvesting systems due to their excellent optical and electrical properties.9–11 In particular, N-containing organic molecules are commonly used as additives in electrolytic solutions to improve the performance of DSSCs.12 N-containing heterocycles other than TBP, such as pyrazole, imidazole, 1,2,4-triazole, pyridine, pyrimidine and pyrazine, have been recently used as additives in DSSCs.13a,b One of the novel N-heterocyclic organic molecules, acridinedione and its derivatives, finds remarkable application in optoelectronic aspects,14a–d with high stability under thermal and light.14b Though there are few reports only in material studies, it is easier to find considerable biological applications because of their resemblance to 1,4-dihydropyridines which are analogs of many of the biologically important co-enzymes, NADH and NADPH.15a,b

To the best of our knowledge, the acridinedione derivatives have never been used for DSSC applications. On these lines, highlighting our ongoing research aimed at the synthesis and potential applications of cycloaddition chemistry,16a–e we are gaining interest for the application of click chemistry methodology that offers the following advantages, namely, novelty based on 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition chemistry, high yields, selectivity and versatility.17 In broader terms, despite the availability of numerous click-chemistry based macromolecular studies on 1,2,3-triazole linked Ru(II) complexes, ligands,18a,b porphyrins and fullerene triazoles,19a,b and 1,2,3-triazole dendrimers,20a,b currently there is no report available in the literature for the synthesis of macromolecules having acridinediones with 1,2,3-triazole moieties. To bridge this gap, in the present study, we successfully report for the first time the synthesis and utilities of acridinedione linked 1,2,3-triazole macromolecules for DSSCs.

An interesting future perspective along the aforementioned concept is to extend it for the use of aliphatic and aromatic moieties as core units, thus helping in efficient synthesis protocols. In the current study, mesitylene and pentaerythritol derivatives are used as core units, which allow the attachment of three and four groups that can be either identical or different. Hence, we chose mesitylene and pentaerythritol as core units for the synthesis of acridinedione-based macromolecules using a recently described copper(I)-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction,17 giving high yields and complete regioselectivity.

Dye-sensitized solar cell studies

According to Grätzel's hypothesis,26 light to electricity conversion is a kind of complex mechanism in the DSCCs. As one of the basic components, the electrolyte plays an important role in improving the solar cell performance, in the process of light to electricity conversion in DSSCs. The electrolyte must have long term stability, including chemical, thermal, optical, electrochemical and interfacial stability, which does not cause desorption and degradation of the dye from the oxide surface and helps to maintain the high enough I3− diffusion coefficient, which are the key prerequisites for high DSSC performance. The electron donor–acceptor reaction in organic solvents and redox couple triiodide27 can be explained by considering the following reaction equilibrium in the electrolyte solution, eqn (1).
 
Solvents + I3− → Solvents–I3− ↔ Solvents–I2 + I−(1)

The higher donor number of molecules present in solvents leads to the inclination of reaction equilibrium to the right,28α–c which results in a decrease in the sublimation of iodine and also an increase in Voc according to .

 
(2)

In general, the amine derivatives, used as additives, drastically increase the open-circuit voltage (Voc) due to the electron donating properties of the nitrogen lone pair.12,13a,b In the present investigation, the synthesized 1,2,3-triazole linked acridinedione derived macromolecules as additives in I−/I3− electrolyte solution influences ruthenium dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) performance, and the Voc values are considerably altered. Typically, cells with macromolecules display higher Voc values and the chemical reaction of charge-transfer complexation in the electrolyte solution is given in eqn (3).

 
Macromolecule + I3− → Macromolecule–I2 + I−(3)

Macromolecules bearing triazole and acridinedione effectively form charge transfer complexes with iodine in the redox complexation reaction which results in a decrease in the sublimation of iodine and also reflects the high performance of DSSCs. The DSSCs, with the synthesized molecules as additives, remain highly stable even until a period of 48 hours with an efficiency deviation of only ±0.01%. Conclusions

We have presented an efficient and systematic route towards the syntheses of a series of luminescent tri- and tetra-acridinedione macromolecules built on mesitylene and pentaerythritol cores by using regioselective Huisgen 1,3-dipolar cycloadditions of alkynes to azides. The synthesized macromolecules were successfully demonstrated as additives of standard, cis-dithiocyanato-N,N-bis(2,2-bipyridyl 4,4-dicarboxylic acid) ruthenium(II) (N3 dye), DSCCs. The synthesized macromolecule containing electrolyte has an appreciable impact on short-circuit photocurrent (Jsc), open-circuit voltage (Voc) and fill factor (FF) under identical conditions. A maximum current conversion efficiency (η) of about 6.4% was obtained for electron rich substituted macromolecules and the overall conversion efficiency of DSSCs enhances from 1.37 to 6.40% which is relatively quite high. This improvement in the efficiency is attributed to the lower volatility of the 1,2,3-triazole linked acridinedione macromolecule added electrolytic solution. The higher FF is related to the facile charge transfer at the electrode/electrolyte interface. The enhancement of Voc afforded by the decreased back electron transfer from the TiO2 conduction band to the I3− ions in the electrolyte is supported by strong π–π* intramolecular charge transfer of N-aryl electron rich substitutions. The results motivate us to achieve highly efficient DSSCs by further tuning the molecular structure by increasing the number of acridinedione and triazole units.

Experimental

Materials and methods

Chemicals and solvents (AR quality) were used as received without any further purification. All melting points are uncorrected. IR spectra were recorded on a BRUKER TENSOR-27 series FT IR instrument. 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a BRUKER 300 MHz instrument in deuterated chloroform (CDCl3) as a solvent. Tetramethylsilane (TMS) was used as an internal standard. Matric-assisted laser-desorption ionization time-of-light (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry was performed on a Voyager De-Pro mass spectrometer. Elemental analyses were carried out using a Perkin-Elmer CHNS 2400B instrument. Column chromatography was performed on silica gel (ACME, 100–200 mesh). Routine monitoring of the reaction was made using thin layer chromatography developed on glass plates coated with silica gel-G (ACME) of 25 mm thickness and visualized with iodine. The UV-Vis spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu 260 spectrophotometer.

Spectral studies

The solvents used for the spectral studies were of HPLC grade. All the absorption and fluorescence experiments were carried out at room temperature. Absorption spectra were recorded using an Agilent 8453 diode array spectrophotometer. Emission spectra were recorded on a HORIBA JOBIN YVON Fluoromax 4P spectrophotometer. The fluorescence quantum yield was determined by exciting the sample at 366 nm with the use of quinine sulfate as the standard (ϕF) 0.546 in 0.1 N H2SO4.

Electrochemical studies

Cyclic voltammetry measurements were performed in a conventional three electrode system on a CHI model 1100A series electrochemical analyzer (CH Instrument, USA). A glassy carbon electrode (GCE) was used as the working electrode with Pt foil (large surface area) and a silver–silver chloride (Ag/AgCl) as counter and reference electrodes, respectively. Prior to each electrochemical experiment, this GCE was mechanically polished with 0.05 micron alumina powder and cleaned in an acetone:ethanol (1:1) mixture in an ultrasonic bath to remove impurities, rinsed with water and then dried in air. Then the electrode was cleaned by cycling between the potentials of 0.0 to −2.7 V versus AgCl in 0.1 M of tetrabutylammoniumhexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6) in DMF at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1 for approximately 30 min until reproducible scans were recorded. All the electrochemical experiments were performed in a quiescent solution at room temperature (25 ± 1 °C).

General procedure for preparation of dye-sensitized solar cell and studies

The dye-sensitized solar cells consist of nanostructured TiO2 film photoelectrodes covered with a photosensitizing dye such as cis-dithiocyanato-N,N-bis(2,2-bipyridyl-4,4-dicarboxylic acid) ruthenium(II) (N3 dye), a redox electrolyte solution (I−/I3−) and a Pt counter electrode on fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) coated conducting glass. The TiO2 photoelectrode was prepared as described earlier.26 The TiO2 electrodes were immersed in a 5 × 10−5 M solution of the sensitized dye cis-dithiocynato-N,N-bis(2,2′-bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid) ruthenium(II) (N3 dye) in absolute ethanol for one day. The photoelectrode was washed, dried and immediately used for the measurement of solar cell performance. A sandwich type photoelectrochemical cell was composed of a dye-coated TiO2 photoanode. The counter electrode was a platinum coated flourinated tin oxide (FTO) conducting glass. The electrolyte solution was injected into the space between two electrodes. The electrolyte solution composed of KI is 2.2 × 10−5 M; I2 is 3 × 10−6 M, and acridinedione macromolecules (13a–g and 14a–g) 8.5 × 10−6 M additives in 10 ml DMF solvent. The photoelectrochemical properties were measured under simulated solar light at 40 mW cm−2 and 100 mW cm−2. The photocurrent–photovoltage (J–V curve) was measured using a BAS 100A Electrochemical analyser. The apparent cell area of the TiO2 photoelectrode was 1 cm2 (1.0 cm × 1.0 cm).
