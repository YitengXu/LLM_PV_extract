Abstract

A novel method has been developed for the preparation of nanostructured haematite (Î±-Fe2O3) films for use in photoelectrocatalytic (PEC) water splitting and the degradation of organic pollutants. The method has two stages, the electrodeposition of Fe films in alkalescent aqueous electrolyte with ferrous sulphate and ammonia, and the in situ thermal oxidation of the Fe films to Î±-Fe2O3. The thickness and crystallinity of the Î±-Fe2O3 films can be precisely controlled by adjusting the duration and the annealing conditions of the electrodeposition, respectively, avoiding the microstructural defects arising from the traditional electrodeposition of FeOOH films and the unwanted phases of FeO and Fe3O4 produced by thermal oxidation of Fe foils. This facilitates the generation, transportation and collection of photogenerated charges on the Î±-Fe2O3 film. The optimized Î±-Fe2O3 film, obtained from a Fe film deposited for 30 s and then annealed at 500 Â°C for 2 h, showed a stable PEC water oxidation current around 1.35 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. a reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) under AM 1.5 irradiation. This was the highest current so far obtained using undoped Î±-Fe2O3 films produced by electrodeposition. When further coated with a cobalt phosphate (CoâPi) co-catalyst, the optimized CoâPi/Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode showed an incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) above 18% at 400 nm and a stable photocurrent of 1.89 mA cmâ2. The Î±-Fe2O3 film also showed excellent stability and degradation efficiency (rate constant 0.9372 hâ1) in the PEC degradation of methylene blue (MB) in neutral aqueous solution under a positive bias potential.

1. Introduction

The extensive use of cheap and plentiful energy in the form of fossil fuels has produced a revolution, both in human life and in the natural world. However, fossil fuels are a limited resource, and in addition burning them has a negative impact on the environment. In order to reduce fossil fuel consumption and at the same time address the associated environmental problems, the need to increase the utilization of clean energy, such as solar energy, has been widely acknowledged. It is inspiring that a photoelectrocatalytic (PEC) technique has been developed to solve these problems using sunlight to split water and degrade organic pollutants, following the pioneering work of Fujishima and Honda.1â3

Considerable attention has been devoted to developing an ideal photoelectrode, which is the key to the PEC conversion of solar energy to chemical energy, for example in PEC water splitting and waste water treatment.4â9 Among the various semiconductor materials so far studied (e.g. TiO2, ZnO and WO3),10â14 haematite (Î±-Fe2O3) is one of the promising due to its small band gap (2.1 eV), which allows it to harvest the energy of about 40% of the solar spectrum, up to a wavelength around 600 nm; it also has excellent stability under alkaline conditions, and it is environmentally benign, naturally abundant and low in cost.15â19 However, the PEC activity of Î±-Fe2O3 is limited by a number of factors, including the short lifetime of its photogenerated charge carriers (<10 ps), short hole diffusion length (â¼2â4 nm), and the poor kinetics of its oxygen evolution reaction.20â24 To address these limitations, a number of strategies have been developed for improving the PEC properties of Î±-Fe2O3, including modifying its electronic structure by elemental doping,25â27 synthesizing nanostructured Î±-Fe2O3 in order to shorten the pathway that photoexcited electrons and holes are required to travel,28,29 integrating Î±-Fe2O3-based composite photoanode with a better conducting material,30,31 and decorating the surface of Î±-Fe2O3 with a co-catalyst such as cobalt phosphate (CoâPi), to facilitate the oxygen evolution reaction.32,33

Numerous methods have been reported for preparing the Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode, including hydrothermal methods,34,35 spray pyrolysis,36,37 DC magnetron sputtering,38 electrodeposition,25,28,39 atmospheric pressure chemical vapour deposition,33,40 atomic layer deposition41 and anodization.42 Among these, electrodeposition is both convenient and cost-effective.

The reported electrodeposition method for preparing Î±-Fe2O3 films involves two steps, the first being the electrodeposition of a FeOOH film and the second being calcination. The electrodeposition may be either cathodic or anodic, depending on the nature of the electrolyte. Cathodic electrodeposition involves the electrochemical reduction of H2O2 in a solution containing Fe3+ to form OHâ, and the subsequent precipitation of Fe3+ by increased pH in the locality of the cathode to form FeOOH films.25,39 Anodic electrodeposition on the other hand involves the oxidation of Fe2+ to Fe3+, followed by precipitation of Fe3+ and the development of FeOOH films near the anode.28 The FeOOH films prepared in this way can be converted to Î±-Fe2O3 films by calcination.

In summary, the processes for FeOOH film formation by both cathodic and anodic electrodeposition arise from the direct transformation of Fe2+ or Fe3+ to FeOOH films, the reduction of H2O2 to OHâ, the oxidation of Fe2+ to Fe3+, precipitation of Fe3+ to form FeOOH, and annealing of the FeOOH film to form an Î±-Fe2O3 film. In this complex process a variety of compounds of Fe2+ and Fe3+ are formed in aqueous solution, including polyhydroxy compounds, polynuclear complexes and bridging ligands.43â46 These occur at the working electrode, causing unavoidable microstructural defects in the FeOOH film deposited, and these are also then present in the final Î±-Fe2O3 structure.47 Neither cathodic nor anodic electrodeposition gives an orderly arranged FeOOH film.

A number of studies have described the preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 films by the direct thermal oxidation of Fe foils, which promises a simple way of preparing an Î±-Fe2O3 photoelectrode.48â51 A variety of iron oxide structures can be formed directly on the Fe foil by varying the annealing conditions, and simple aerial oxidation of iron provides a convenient route to haematite nanowires.50 The thickness and crystallinity of the oxide film is difficult to control, however, due to the rapid oxidation of metallic iron during thermal oxidation of the Fe foil. In addition, the presence of FeO and/or Fe3O4 at the interface of metallic iron and Fe2O3 is unavoidable, and as a result the photoresponse of the Î±-Fe2O3 photoelectrode is somewhat poor.

Based on the above background, we have been able to propose a novel method for the preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 films, combining the advantages of electrodeposition and thermal oxidation, and at the same time avoiding their defects. The method involves direct electrodeposition of Fe films on a conductive substrate, such as F-doped tin oxide (FTO), the Î±-Fe2O3 film being obtained under alkaline conditions on coated glass by adding an excess of ammonia in ferrous sulphate solution, followed by the in situ thermal oxidation of the Fe film to form the Î±-Fe2O3 film. This method has a number of advantages:

(i) it ensures the uniformity of the deposited Fe film and the resulting Î±-Fe2O3 film, since the alkaline conditions inhibit the corrosion of the Fe deposited;

(ii) it facilitates the deposition of the Fe film in an orderly manner, since the existence of ferrous hydroxide compounds modifies the reduction of Fe2+ to Fe, and prevents the generation of microstructural defects in the deposited film, which distinguishes it from traditional strategies such as the direct electrodeposition of FeOOH films from Fe2+ or Fe3+;

(iii) it prevents the presence of the undesired phases of FeO and/or Fe3O4 formed during the traditional thermal oxidation of Fe foils, since the uniform and tunable Fe film on the FTO substrate is completely transformed into Î±-Fe2O3;

(iv) it facilitates the generation and transport or collection of photo-generated charges in the Î±-Fe2O3 film due to its tunable thickness, phase purity and absence of microstructural defects.

In the present study, the feasibility of the convenient method described above for the preparation of highly photoactive Î±-Fe2O3 films for effective PEC water splitting and organic pollutant degradation has been investigated. The results indicate that the Fe film deposited can be completely converted into Î±-Fe2O3, and the thickness and the crystallinity of the resulting Î±-Fe2O3 film are tunable by adjusting either the duration of the deposition of the Fe film or the annealing conditions.

The effect of annealing temperature and time on the PEC water-splitting performance of Î±-Fe2O3 films was studied in detail. Î±-Fe2O3 films decorated with CoâPi were also prepared by photo-assisted electrodeposition in order to increase the efficiency of oxygen evolution at decreased over-potential. Finally, the effect of PEC organic pollutant degradation with the Î±-Fe2O3 films has been examined.

2. Experimental

2.1 Synthesis of Î±-Fe2O3 film

The Fe film was first electrodeposited on the F-doped tin oxide (FTO) coated glass substrate (13 Î© cmâ1) in a two-electrode configuration with the FTO glass forming the cathode and a platinum foil the anode (Fig. 1 and ESI Fig. S1â ). The distance between the cathode and the anode was 3 cm. The electrolyte was prepared by dissolving 5 g of FeSO4Â·7H2O and 30 mL of ammonia (27%) in 150 mL of deionized water, giving pH 10.8 at 25 Â°C. Electrodeposition was carried out at 2 V for 15, 30, 45 and 60 s with vigorous stirring. The Fe film deposited was rinsed with deionized water for 5 min and dried at 50 Â°C in a gentle stream of nitrogen over 1 h, followed by thermal oxidation in air. The deposition of CoâPi on the surface of the Î±-Fe2O3 film was carried out in a three-electrode system with the Î±-Fe2O3 film forming the working electrode, platinum foil as the counter-electrode and a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) as reference, by a photo-assisted electrodeposition (PED) method. 0.1 M sodium phosphate buffer solution at pH 7 containing 5 mM Co(NO3)2 was used as electrolyte. The PED was carried out at 0.1 V under illumination provided by an artificial solar light. After PED, the Î±-Fe2O3 film modified with CoâPi was rinsed with deionized water for 5 min and dried at 50 Â°C for 1 h.

2.2 Characterization

The morphology and microstructure of the samples were investigated by FE-SEM (Sirion 200, Philips, Netherlands) equipped with an energy-dispersive X-ray detector (EDX). XPS spectra were assessed using an AXIS Ultra DLD (Kratos, Shimadzu) using non-chromatic Al KÎ± radiation at 12 kV and 25 mA. The crystal phase of the samples was determined by X-ray diffractometry (XRD; AXS-8 Advance, Bruker, Germany). Raman spectra were recorded on a Senterra R200 system (Bruker, Germany) with laser excitation at 532 nm. The UV-visible absorption spectra of the samples were recorded on a UV-vis photospectrometer (TU-1901, Beijing Purkinje General Instrument Co.).

2.3 Photoelectrocatalytic measurements

PEC water-splitting experiments were carried out in a 1 M KOH electrolyte using a three-electrode system with a SCE as reference, a platinum foil as the auxiliary electrode, and the prepared films as working electrode. The PEC tests were controlled from an electrochemical workstation (CHI 660c, CH Instruments Inc., USA). A 350 W xenon lamp (Shanghai Hualun Bulb Factory) with AM 1.5 filter was used for light illumination (light density, 100 mW cmâ2). Incident photon-to-charge conversion efficiency (IPCE) was measured using a monochromator (Zolix, China), a 500 W xenon arc lamp, calibrated silicon photodetector and a power meter.

The PEC degradation of MB was conducted at pH 7 under vigorous stirring, AM 1.5 irradiation, electric bias 0.6 V (vs. SCE), and with 0.01 mol Lâ1 sodium sulphate supporting electrolyte. The photocatalytic (PC) degradation was performed without application of an external potential to the working electrodes. The initial concentration of the MB solution was 5 mg Lâ1 and 25 mL of reaction solution was used. At different time intervals, the reaction solution was analyzed using a UV-vis spectrophotometer (UV2102 PCS, UNICO, Shanghai).

Intensity-modulated photocurrent spectroscopy (IMPS) was carried out using an electrochemical workstation (Zennium; effect-Elektrik, Germany) equipped with a controlled intensity-modulated photospectroscopy set-up (CIMPS, PP211), following a three-electrode configuration, with the prepared Î±-Fe2O3 film as the working electrode, a platinum foil as the counter-electrode, and a SCE as reference electrode with a 1 M KOH electrolyte. A white-light lamp (WLC02; Zahner-Elektrik) was used as light source. Modulated light in the frequency range 0.1â10 KHz superimposed on a steady DC light intensity of 80 mW cmâ2 was applied. The bias potential applied at the working electrode was 0.23 V vs. SCE.

3.1 Preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 film

In the present study we have developed an alternative technique for the preparation of Fe films, using electrodeposition in ammonia. In order to understand the effect of ammonia on the electrolyte, a variety of electrolytes were used for electrodepositing the Fe films. The stability of the photoanode in PEC water splitting is crucial in both fundamental and practical studies. The PEC activity of the optimized Î±-Fe2O3 film was also investigated experimentally by organic compound degradation. The PEC (at a bias potential of 0.6 V vs. SCE), photocatalytic (PC; PEC without bias potential), and direct photolytic (DP) degradation of MB in neutral aqueous solution were respectively performed under the conditions indicated. 4. Conclusions

A convenient method has been developed for the preparation of photocorrosion-stable and highly photoactive Î±-Fe2O3 films. Ammonia in the electrolyte was crucial for obtaining a uniform Fe film by electrodeposition, and the Fe film was completely transformed to Î±-Fe2O3 film by in situ thermal oxidation. The optimized Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode showed a stable PEC water oxidation current of 1.35 mA cmâ2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE under AM 1.5 irradiation. After decoration with a CoâPi co-catalyst, the optimized CoâPi/Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode showed IPCE above 18% at 400 nm with a stable photocurrent of 1.89 mA cmâ2 for PEC water splitting. The Î±-Fe2O3 photoanode also demonstrated excellent stability and degradation efficiency (rate constant 0.9372 hâ1) for the PEC degradation of MB in neutral aqueous solution. This study provides a useful insight into the design and preparation of Î±-Fe2O3 photoanodes for cost-effective and highly effective PEC applications.
