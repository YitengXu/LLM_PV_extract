Enhanced solar-to-hydrogen efficiency for photocatalytic water splitting based on a polarized heterostructure: the role of intrinsic dipoles in heterostructures

Abstract

Inspired by natural photosynthesis, direct Z-scheme heterostructures are considered as promising photocatalysts for solar-driven water splitting and attract ever-growing interest. To date, it is still a challenge to achieve a high efficiency based on direct Z-scheme photocatalysts for overall water splitting, because suitable band gaps and overpotentials for both half-reactions and spatially separated catalytic sites should be fulfilled simultaneously in a photocatalytic system. These challenges can be solved by taking advantage of the intrinsic dipole effect for polarized materials. Here, we propose a new strategy to achieve this goal by constructing van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures based on two-dimensional (2D) polarized materials. Using density functional theory calculations, we predict a promising photocatalyst In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure, with the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) taking place separately on the SnP3 and In2Se3 layers. It is found that the intrinsic dipole of the In2Se3 monolayer effectively enhances the redox abilities for both the HER and OER. Moreover, the intrinsic dipole can promote the spatial separation of photogenerated carriers, and also contributes to a high solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency of 19.26%, which is quite promising for commercial applications. This work opens up an avenue for the design of highly efficient Z-scheme photocatalysts for overall water splitting.

Introduction

Photocatalytic water splitting utilizes sustainable solar energy to generate O2 and H2 without polluting the environment.1,2 Its efficiency is primarily determined by three processes: (1) light harvesting; (2) the photogenerated electronhole pair separation and migration to the surface of semiconductors (active/reaction site); (3) the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) on the surface of photocatalysts.35 For single-component photocatalysts, the charge separation efficiency is quiet low due to the recombination of electronhole pairs induced by the strong coulombic force.3,5 Type-II photocatalysts assembled from two semiconductors with a staggered band structure configuration can promote the spatial separation and suppress the recombination of charge carriers.6,7 However, the formation of type-II heterojunctions reduces the redox ability of the system, and thereby the driving force for photocatalytic reactions.4,5,8 Moreover, it is difficult for conventional type-II heterojunctions to simultaneously possess a wide light absorption range and strong redox ability. This is understandable since the light-absorption range requires a small band gap, but the strong redox ability needs to widen the band gap.3,5 Furthermore, type-II heterojunction photocatalysts mainly focus on the half reaction by utilizing one type of charge carrier. Consequently, the remaining photogenerated carriers need to be consumed by sacrificial reagents to suppress photocorrosion; this may cause the secondary pollution and consumption of natural resources.3,9

Inspired by natural photosynthesis in green plants, direct Z-scheme photocatalytic systems were proposed to overcome these drawbacks for overall water splitting based on type-II heterojunctions.5,8,10,11 Although direct Z-scheme photocatalysts possess many merits, such as increased light harvesting, spatially separated reductive and oxidative active sites, and well-preserved strong redox ability, the solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency of current Z-scheme systems is still far from that required for economically feasible applications for their commercialization.1113 The maximum STH efficiency of Z-scheme systems for theoretical stimulations is predicted to be 10.5% in a MoSe2/SnSe2 heterostructure.14 One of the challenges is that the formation of a direct Z-scheme is highly dependent on the nature of the semiconductor, which limits the flexibility in material selection.5,12 In theory, the difference (1.23 eV) between the redox potentials of H+/H2 and H2O/O2 further limits the applications of semiconductors with a narrow band gap in Z-scheme photocatalysts, because kinetic overpotentials are demanded to drive catalytic reactions. Therefore, highly efficient direct Z-scheme photocatalysts based on semiconductors with a narrow band gap (<1.2 eV) and strong redox ability to achieve solar-driven spontaneous overall water splitting have rarely been reported.14,15

Two dimensional (2D) materials show excellent performances for photocatalytic water splitting, due to their large surface area, high carrier mobility, short carrier migration distance and tunable electronic structures.1618 Recently, 2D polarized materials with an intrinsic dipole have been proposed as promising photocatalysts for water splitting.1925 First, the intrinsic dipole can efficiently separate photogenerated carriers. Second, the intrinsic dipole induces a shift of the redox potentials of H+/H2 and H2O/O2 at the two surfaces of materials, and thus breaks the conventional limitation of 1.23 eV for the difference between the redox potentials of H+/H2 and H2O/O2. This makes it possible to achieve solar-driven spontaneous overall water splitting using photocatalysts with a narrow band gap, leading to enhanced light absorption.9,20

Although some 2D polarized materials have been theoretically proved to be potential photocatalysts for water splitting, such as III2VI3 materials,21 Li-IIIVI compounds20 and Janus transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs),25 Z-scheme photocatalysts based on 2D polarized materials and the effects of the intrinsic dipole in these structures have not yet been explored. More recently, by stacking Janus materials, the intrinsic vertical electric field leads to a rigid shift of the conduction band minimum (CBM) and valence band maximum (VBM) resulting in a large band offset and a small interlayer band gap.19,2629 Inspired by these studies, we assumed that one can make use of the shift of the CBM and VBM due to the intrinsic diploe for polarized layers to construct a Z-scheme junction based on the polarized layers. In this case, the shift of the CBM and VBM would enhance redox ability and the reduced interlayer band gap would favor the recombination of photogenerated carries with lower redox ability. Therefore, to construct a highly efficient Z-scheme heterostructure for photocatalytic water splitting, a narrow band-gap is first needed to achieve a large light absorption. Second, a suitable intrinsic dipole for polarized materials is preferred to improve the separation of photogenerated carriers and enhance the redox abilities for heterostructures. Moreover, a high carrier mobility is required which helps the fast migration of carriers to the surface of heterostructures, and a type-II band alignment is expected which is a prerequisite to achieve the Z-scheme system mode.

In this contribution, we report an In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure as a potential highly efficient direct Z-scheme photocatalyst for overall water splitting. Using the density functional theory (DFT) method, we systematically investigated a series of Janus Group-III chalcogenides (M2XY, M = Ga, In; X = S, Se; Y = Se, Te; X Y) and III2VI3 groups (M2X3, M = Ga, In; X = S, Se, Te). We found that the M2XY and M2X3 layers can effectively separate photogenerated carries, and the intrinsic diploe for these polarized materials leads to a rigid shift for redox potentials at the two surfaces of materials, resulting in the enhancement of the redox abilities.

To further investigate the role of the intrinsic dipole for polarized materials in heterostructures, we constructed a series of heterostructures by stacking two different polarized monolayers together. Our calculation results show that, due to the intrinsic dipole of polarized materials, different contact surfaces will lead to different charge transfer modes (Z-scheme or type-II heterojunction). More importantly, we found that for Z-scheme heterostructures, to enhance the redox abilities, the intrinsic dipole of the polarized layer should possess the same direction as the interfacial electric field of the heterostructure and they will be reduced conversely. Based on these findings, we construct an In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure, which is a Z-scheme photocatalyst for overall water splitting with large overpotentials for both the HER (0.58 eV) and OER (0.89 eV). Moreover, due to the strong intrinsic dipole of the polarized In2Se3 layer, the photogenerated electrons of In2Se3 and holes of SnP3 accumulate at the interface of the heterostructure and would recombine with each other rapidly under the interfacial electric field, while the photogenerated holes of In2Se3 and electrons of SnP3 remained on the two surface sides of heterostructures participating in the HER and OER. Finally, a high solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency of 19.26% was achieved in the direct Z-scheme In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure for photocatalytic water splitting, which is larger than that of conventional Z-scheme photocatalysts.

Results and discussion

The role of the intrinsic dipole in polarized materials

We first investigated a series of 2D polarized materials, which includes Janus Group-III chalcogenides (M2XY, M = Ga, In; X = S, Se; Y = Se, Te; X Y) and III2VI3 groups (M2X3, M = Ga, In; X = S, Se, Te), and the representative geometric structures are shown in Fig. 1(a). Janus Group-III chalcogenides consist of four atomic layers with the two M layers sandwiched by different X and Y atoms on the two sides and the stable 2D M2X3 monolayers show quintuple layer structures of XMXMX. The calculated lattice parameters, bond lengths and band gap of Janus M2XY monolayers and M2X3 monolayers (listed in Table S1) agree well with previous reports.21,30,31 The asymmetric structure for 2D polarized materials breaks the centrosymmetry of the 2D plane, giving rise to an intrinsic dipole along the z direction which induces a vertical electric field (E) and the direction of E is plotted as shown in Fig. 1(a).

Due to the built-in electric dipole in 2D polarized materials, the two vacuum levels for their two surfaces are different from the conventional 2D semiconductors. In the latter case, there is only one vacuum level, and thus the redox potentials of materials are the same on the two surfaces. However, for 2D polarized materials the vacuum level difference in the perpendicular direction between the top and bottom atomic layers can result in rigid shift of the redox potentials of H+/H2 and H2O/O2 between the two surfaces, as shown in Fig. 1(d). The shift of redox potentials at the two surfaces would increase the overpotentials of the HER (H2) and OER (O2) for polarized materials, which represent the redox abilities of photogenerated carriers and thus are important criteria for evaluating the performance of photocatalysts during the water splitting process.

Geometric structures and band structures of heterostructures

To construct highly efficient Z-scheme photocatalysts for overall water splitting, as shown in Fig. 2, we built a series of heterostructures based on the above 2D polarized materials: In2STe/Ga2STe, In2STe/In2SeTe, In2Se3/In2Te3, In2Se3/Ga2Te3 and In2Te3/Ga2Te3 systems. Owing to their close lattice constants, the heterostructures are built by their unit cells containing 8 or 10 atoms in total with the energetically most stable configuration being the AA model (see Fig. S2 and Table S2 in detail). In2STe/InS systems, containing one polarized material layer and one non-polarized material layer, are also constructed for comparison, as shown in Fig. 2(a). To investigate the role of the intrinsic dipole for polarized materials in heterostructures, we constructed two types of polarized heterostructures for each system according to different atomic stacking orders. As shown in Fig. 2(b) and (c), the intrinsic dipole for two polarized layers in heterostructures are in the same direction. The band structures of these heterostructures show that the majority of these heterostructures formed a type-II band alignment, see Fig. 2(d), which is a prerequisite to achieve the Z-scheme system mode. Due to the strong built-in electric field, the charge transfer can affect the band alignment of heterostructures. The superposition of strong intrinsic dipoles for the two polarized monolayers in heterostructures with the same direction would cause a shift of the band edges of each monolayer leading to a large band offset and small interlayer band gap, much smaller than those of the monolayers which is beneficial for the recombination of photogenerated carriers with lower redox ability.19 Especially, In2Te3In2Se3 and Ga2Te3In2Se3 have relatively larger charge transfer at the interfaces, resulting in a high internal electric field Ein of 0.58 and 0.65 eV. Therefore, the band edges shift considerably due to the strong interaction between the two layers, which turns In2Te3In2Se3 and Ga2Te3In2Se3 into type-III heterostructures. The SGa2TeSIn2Te and In2Se3In2Te3 systems are type-I heterostructures, whose VBM and CBM are contributed by the same layer. The charge transfer Q, the interlayer distance dl, the binding energy Eb, and the internal electric field Ein of vertical M2XY and M2X3 heterostructures in different types of stacking are listed in Tables S2 and S3.

The role of the intrinsic dipole in heterostructures

The direction of the internal electric field Ein determines the type of charge transfer mode (Z-scheme or type-II heterojunction) under light irradiation, which is depended on the charge transfer at the interface of the heterostructures. For conventional vdW heterostructures, the direction of charge transfer at the interface is determined by the work function of the two layers. However, due to the intrinsic dipole of polarized materials, the work functions for the top and bottom surfaces of the polarized layers are different, and thus the two monolayers expose different work functions at the interface when they form heterostructures with different stacking configurations. Therefore, different stacking orders may lead to an opposite charge transfer direction at the interface, resulting in distinct charge transfer mode (type-II or Z-scheme) under light irradiation. The work functions for those polarized monolayers and InS monolayer are listed in Table S4. Taking In2STe/Ga2STe systems for example, for SIn2TeSGa2Te, the charge density difference and average Hartree difference potential in Fig. 3(a) show that the electrons prefer to accumulate on the side of the Ga2STe layer, rather than that of the In2STe layer due to the larger work function (5.72 eV) for the S-surface of the Ga2STe layer than that (4.64 eV) for the Te-surface of In2STe. The for the SIn2TeSGa2Te heterostructure is lower than the sum value of those for the individual Janus layers, meaning that there is an Ein opposite to EJanus pointing to the Ga2STe layer from the In2STe layer. However, for SGa2TeSIn2Te, the In2STe layer would obtain electrons from the Ga2STe layer due to the large work function (6.02 eV) for the S-surface of the In2STe layer than that (4.96 eV) for the Te-surface of Ga2STe, resulting an opposite Ein pointing to the In2STe layer from the Ga2STe layer. For the In2STe/InS system, the electrons always transfer to the InS layer from the In2STe layer forming an interfacial electric field pointing to the InS monolayer with different atomic stacking configurations Fig. 3(c) and (d). This is because the InS monolayer has a relatively high work function (6.22 eV) which is larger than those of both the S-surface (6.02 eV) and Te-surface (4.64 eV) of the In2STe monolayer.

According to the direction of charge transfer at the interface and interfacial electric field Ein, the Z-scheme systems are shown in Fig. 4(a)(c). In the Z-scheme systems, the internal electric field would promote recombination between photogenerated carriers with a lower redox ability and hinder the transfer of photogenerated electrons and holes with a strong redox ability, and thus the photogenerated carriers with a strong redox ability are preserved and spatially separated to participate into the HER and OER. In contrast, for conventional type-II heterojunctions, the opposite internal electric field leads to a different charge transfer mode under light irradiation due to the opposite direction of charge transfer at the interfaces as shown in Fig. S3.

Considering the shift of the potentials for H+/H2 and O2/H2O due to the intrinsic diploe of polarized materials, we found that for Z-scheme heterostructures the shift direction of potentials for H+/H2 and O2/H2O is important, which determines the increase or decrease of overpotentials for the HER and OER. As shown in Fig. 4(d), for Z-scheme heterostructures, to effectively improve the redox abilities for the HER and OER, the layer L1 with a higher CBM and VBM should possess a surface with a lower vacuum level while the layer L2 with a lower CBM and VBM should possess a surface with a higher vacuum level. As the interfacial electric field for Z-scheme heterostructures is pointed to the layer L2 with a lower CBM and VBM from the layer L1 with a higher CBM and VBM, only the direction of the polarized layer is the same as that of the interfacial electric field, and the overpotentials for the HER and OER can be increased, and conversely they will be reduced. Therefore, the heterostructures should fulfil the following requirements: surf1 in1 < in2 surf2, where surf1 and surf2 are the work functions of L1 and L2 at the two surfaces of the heterostructure, respectively, while in1 and in2 are the work functions of L1 and L2 at the interface of the heterostructure, respectively. Most importantly, the CBM (VBM) must be more positive (negative) than the reduction (oxidation) potential of H+/H2 (O2/H2O) to achieve water splitting on the heterostructure. However, for heterostructures containing two polarized layers, the exposed work functions at the interface always lead to an interfacial electric field opposite to the intrinsic dipole for individual polarized layers as shown in Fig. 2(a) and (b). For the InSSIn2Te heterostructure, the large work function of the InS layer leads to an interfacial electric field with the same direction of the intrinsic dipole for the In2STe layer.

Based on the above discussions, only InSSIn2Te formed a Z-scheme heterostructure with increased (H2) (0.64 eV) and (O2) (0.92 eV), containing only one polarized material layer, which is a promising candidate for overall water splitting Z-scheme photocatalysts. We further calculated the solar-to-hydrogen efficiency (STH) of InSSIn2Te heterostructure which is a key factor in determining the catalytic properties of photocatalysts and the corresponding formulas details can be seen in the Methods. Assuming 100% efficiency of the catalytic reaction, a solar-to-hydrogen (STH) of 12.71% and corrected of 8.52% for the InSSIn2Te heterostructure are obtained with the efficiencies of light absorption abs of 49.28% and carrier utilization (cu) of 25.81%.

Overall water splitting on the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure

The low efficiency of InSSIn2Te is originated from the large band gap of InS and In2STe layers, which determines the light absorption abs and energy of photons that can be used for water splitting E. Therefore, searching for an unpolarized semiconductor with a narrow band-gap and suitable work function to contact with the polarized layers is the key to further improve STH. To date, 2D semiconductors with a narrow band gap in the range of 0.71.2 eV are rarely known. Recently, several 2D triphosphate monolayers have been discovered, such as InP3, BP3, CaP3, GeP3 and SnP3,3236 and the reported band gaps range from 0.55 eV to 1.15 eV along with relatively high carrier mobility (over 103 cm2 V1 s1). The SnP3 monolayer is an unpolarized semiconductor with a narrow indirect gap (0.80 eV) as shown in Fig. 5(a). We constructed a heterostructure by stacking SnP3 and In2Se3 layers together, and the supercell contains a 1 1 unit cell of SnP3 and 1 3 rectangle unit cells of In2Se3. It is noted that the SnP3 monolayer with a work function of 4.68 eV comes into contact with the surface with a lower work function (5.02 eV) of In2Se3 as shown in Fig. 5(b), resulting in charge transfer from the SnP3 layer to the In2Se3 layer at the interface and thus making the internal electric field keep the same direction as the intrinsic dipole of the In2Se3 layer. The charge density difference in Fig. S4 demonstrated that there is an internal electric field pointing to the In2Se3 layer from the SnP3 layer.

The projected density of states (PDODS) in Fig. 5(c) shows that the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure forms a type-II band alignment and the spatial distributions of the VBM and CBM for the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure indicate efficient electronhole separation. The spatially separated reductive and oxidative active sites are beneficial for decreasing the recombination probability of photogenerated carriers participating in redox reactions. The intrinsic electric field of the polarized layer can further help the spatial separation of photogenerated electrons and holes. According to the band alignment in Fig. 5(d), the internal electric field would promote the transfer of photogenerated carriers with lower redox ability at the interface and hinders the transfer of photogenerated electrons and holes with strong redox ability. Also, the strong dipole induces a large shift of the band edges of individual layers, resulting in a tiny interlayer band gap of the heterostructure. For the intrinsic Z-scheme, the tiny band gap increases the probability of electron hopping between potential energy surfaces, which brings about fast eh recombination with lower redox abilities at the interface. Therefore, photogenerated carriers with strong redox ability are preserved and spatially separated to participate in the HER and OER. All these processes can enhance the lifetime of photogenerated carriers participating in reactions, which can well increase the efficiency of carrier utilization and finally improve the STH efficiency of the system. The intrinsic dipole of In2Se3 with the same direction as the internal electric field layer leads to the shift of the reduction potential (H+/H2) and the water oxidation potential (O2/H2O), which increases redox abilities for the HER and OER. More importantly, as shown in Fig. 5(d), based on our design, under light irradiation the photogenerated electrons for the In2Se3 layer and holes for the SnP3 layer with lower redox abilities would accumulate at the interface of the heterostructure, which would recombine fast with each other due to the strong interfacial internal electric field. On the other hand, the photogenerated electrons for the SnP3 layer and holes for the In2Se3 layer with strong redox abilities are preserved and spatially separated on the two surface sides of the heterostructure participating in redox reactions to generate H2 and O2.

The fast migration of carriers is beneficial for the separation of photogenerated electronhole pairs. Due to the van der Waals interaction between In2Se3 and SnP3 layers, the high carrier mobility can be retained in the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure. The calculated result shows that the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure exhibits both high hole and electron mobilities of 1.02 103 and 1.05 103 cm2 V1 s1, respectively, which would contribute to fast carrier transfer. We then explore the energy conversion efficiency of the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure for photocatalytic water splitting. The light absorption efficiency is strongly dependent on the band gap. The quite small band gaps (0.94 eV) enable the large efficiencies of light absorption abs (88.60%) and the efficiency of carrier utilization cu is 35.35% with large (H2) (0.58 eV) and (O2) (0.89 eV). Finally, a high STH efficiency of 31.32% is obtained and the corrected STH efficiency is 19.26%, which are higher than those predicted for other conventional Z-scheme photocatalysts by using the same theoretical strategy, such as MoSe2/SnSe2 (10.5%) and WSe2/SnSe2 (9.53%).14

Conclusions

We have proposed a direct Z-scheme photocatalyst by constructing an In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure based on polarized materials. Density functional theory calculations are performed to reveal the role of the intrinsic diploe for polarized layers in Z-scheme heterostructures. By investigating a series of heterostructures based on polarized materials, we found that the different stacking orders determine the different charge transfer modes (type-II or Z-scheme) for the different work functions at the two surfaces for polarized layers due to the intrinsic diploe. Moreover, the same direction of the intrinsic dipole as the internal electric field of heterostructures can enhance the redox abilities by increasing the overpotentials for the HER and OER. Based on this, we constructed an In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure, which is a direct Z-scheme photocatalyst for overall water splitting with strong redox abilities and effective separation of photogenerated carriers. As a consequence, we achieve a high STH efficiency of 19.26% in the In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure. Our theoretical computations offer not only a promising photocatalyst for high efficiency overall water splitting but also a general strategy for the design of Z-scheme photocatalysts by making the best use of the intrinsic dipole effects. Notably, the defects usually play a significant role in the photocatalyst. Previous studies have demonstrated that surface chalcogen vacancies can create more active sites and improve the intrinsic catalytic activity.37,38 Furthermore, S vacancy induced trap states in monolayer MoS2 will accelerate eh recombination, which indicates that defects may effectively engineer the competitive relationship between charge separation and eh recombination, and even switch the charge transfer path from type-II to Z-scheme.39,40 The effects of defects at the interfaces will be studied in our further work.

Methods and computational details

Structural optimization based on density-functional theory (DFT) was carried out with the plane-wave basis set Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) code,41,42 along with the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) of PerdewBurkeErnzerhof (PBE) for the exchange and correlation functional.43,44 The vdW correction model45 within the PBE functional was involved to describe the long-range interlayer interaction. A kinetic energy cutoff of 450 eV was used in the simulations. For geometry relaxation calculations, the Brillouin zone sampling used was 9 9 1 in the MonkhorstPack grid.46 Geometry optimization was continued until the maximal residual force per atom is less than 0.02 eV 1 and the energy difference is less than 105 eV. The vacuum thickness was set to 20 . Because the GGA-PBE exchange correlation functional tends to underestimate the band gap of semiconductors, the screened hybrid HSE06 functional,47 which was implemented in the Fritz Haber Institute ab initio molecular simulation (FHI-aims) code,48 was utilized to determine the electronic band structures of all the systems. The Hartree difference potential (VH) is calculated as follows: VH = Vtotal Vatom, where Vtotal and Vatom represent the Hartree potentials of the system and each atom. The dipole correction has been considered in all the calculations of work functions. We carried out Bader charge analysis49 to investigate the electron transfer at the interface of the heterostructures.

The optimized lattice constants of rectangle SnP3 are a = 7.38 and b = 12.78 , while the lattice constants of the rectangle In2Se3 unit cell are a = 7.12 and b = 4.11 . These results are consistent with reported values.36 The overall induced mismatch between the optimized SnP3 and In2Se3 monolayer lattice parameters is <3%.

The carrier mobility of the of In2Se3/SnP3 heterostructure was calculated according to the following equation:50,51 = 2e3C/(3kBTm*2D2), where e, , kB, and T denote the electron charge, reduced Planck constant, Boltzmann constant, and room temperature, respectively.
