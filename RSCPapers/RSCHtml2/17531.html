Abstract

A [70]fullereneâ€“benzodifuranone acceptor dyad synthesized by a Ag+-mediated coupling reaction was used to construct a thin-film organic solar cell. The fullerene and the benzodifuranone dye in the dyad have close-lying LUMO levels in the range of 3.7â€“3.9 eV, so that energy transfer from the dye to the fullerene can take place. A pâ€“n heterojunction photovoltaic device consisting of a tetrabenzoporphyrin and a [70]fullereneâ€“benzodifuranone dyad showed a weak but discernible contribution from light absorption of the dyad to the photocurrent under both a positive and a negative effective bias. These results indicate that the benzodifuranone moiety attached to the acceptor contributes to light-harvesting by energy transfer.

Introduction

Thin-film organic photovoltaic (OPV) devices1 often consist of a light-harvesting donor and an electron-transporting fullerene derivative,2 but suffer from the common problem of a limited range of light absorption by the donor. A high-performance printable small molecule OPV device that we recently reported also suffers from this limitation because the tetrabenzoporphyrin (BP) donor absorbs only at its Q and Soret bands and little around 500 nm.3 To remedy this issue, one can employ an additional dye that absorbs at 500 nm through covalent attachment of the dye to an acceptor molecule or in the form of an additional layer.4,5 We report here a new dyad molecule 3 consisting of a [70]fullerene molecule and a benzodifuranone moiety that absorbs at 500 nm (Scheme 1 and Fig. 1). Photoillumination of a pâ€“n device consisting of BP and 3 showed a weak but discernible contribution of light absorption of 3 under both a positive and a negative effective bias, indicating that energy transfer from the benzodifuranone moiety to the fullerene contributed to photocurrent generation. This finding encourages a further search for dyes that show broader and stronger absorption at wavelengths not covered by the electron donor.

Results and discussion

The [70]fullereneâ€“benzodifuranone dyad 3 was synthesized by a Ag+-mediated coupling of C70 bromide 1 with benzodifuranone dye 2, and was examined as an acceptor material in a pâ€“n device with BP as the donor material. Benzodifuranones are widely used in the textile industry,6 but not as yet in OPV research. The dyad was designed to have close-lying LUMO levels (spectral overlap proximity) for both the fullerene and the dye in order to permit energy transfer from the dye to the fullerene.

Synthesis and characterization of dyad 3

The bromide 1 was synthesized efficiently from C70via a three-fold addition reaction reported previously,7 followed by deprotonation/bromination and then coupled with the alkylhydroxy-substituted benzodifuranone6 precursor 2 in the presence of silver tosylate in THF/1,2-dichlorobenzene (2:1) to obtain the dyad 3 and a regioisomer.8Purification on a silica chromatography column and preparative HPLC gave a racemic mixture of pure 3 as a single regioisomer. 1H NMR, UV/Vis, and high-resolution mass spectra are consistent with the structure of 3.

The dyad 3 was further characterized by cyclic voltammetry, photoluminescence, and DSC measurements. Four quasi-reversible reduction waves were observed down to âˆ’1.8 V vs.Fc/Fc+ (Fig. 2 and Table 1). Based on a comparison with benzodifuranone and fullerene reference compounds9 (see ESIâ€ ), the first and third reduction waves were assigned to the benzodifuranone group and the second and fourth reduction waves were assigned to the fullerene core. The difference between the first reduction potentials of the benzodifuranone and fullerene constituents is 140 mV. Therefore, as desired, the fullerene and dye units in the dyad have close-lying LUMO levels in the range of âˆ’3.7 to âˆ’3.9 eV that match well with that of BP (LUMO: âˆ’3.2 eV), which we used as a donor material.

The UV/Vis spectrum of 3 in CH2Cl2 showed characteristic absorption bands that can be interpreted as a superposition of the absorption bands of the dye and the fullerene units (Fig. 1, black line). The absorption maximum and the corresponding extinction coefficient (501 nm, 49000 L molâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) were similar to those of monomeric benzodifuranone dyes.6a

The photoluminescence spectrum of the dyad 3 (Fig. 1, gray line) exhibits overlapping peaks between 550 nm and 750 nm that are assigned mainly to fullerene fluorescence (broad peak at Î»max = 635 nm) and partly to benzodifuranone fluorescence (Î»max = 595 nm), as judged by a comparison with known compounds11 (see ESIâ€ ). Fluorescence quenching of the dye occurred with an efficiency of >99% (Î»ex = 470 nm). This efficiency is similar to that of [70]fullereneâ€“porphyrin dyads.12 Because absorption and emission for the fullerene and dye units overlap with each other, a definitive determination of the quenching mechanism is not obvious. However, based on several studies of [60]fullereneâ€“perylene and other dyads with similar energy levels and architecture,12â€“14 it appears likely that energy transfer from the dye to the fullerene is predominantly responsible for the fluorescence quenching in dyad 3 in environments with low dielectric constants; however, a contribution from electron transfer processes cannot be ruled out. The dyad 3 exhibits no phase transition or decomposition up to 200 Â°C, according to DSC measurements (see ESIâ€ ).

Device fabrication and measurements

An ITO/PEDOT:PSS/BP/dyad 3/NBPhen/Al pâ€“n heterojunction device was fabricated and tested under AM1.5G (100 mW cmâˆ’2) illumination. The dyad 3 exhibited a power conversion efficiency (PCE) value of 0.46% (short circuit current density (JSC) = 2.68 mA cmâˆ’2, open circuit voltage (VOC) = 0.59 V, fill factor (FF) = 0.29) (Fig. 3a), which was lower than that of an ITO/PEDOT:PSS/BP/PCBNB/BCP/Al pâ€“n device (PCBNB: phenyl C61-butyric acid n-butyl ester) that we previously reported (PCE = 2.2%),15 but comparable to those of other dyad devices with multicomponent active layers.4,5 The incident photon to current conversion efficiency (IPCE) spectrum (Fig. 3b, solid line) under zero bias voltage mainly originated from the absorption of BP; there is no obvious peak around 500 nm because of the smaller absorption coefficient of the benzodifuranone dye (Fig. 1) compared with that of BP (Î»max = 440 and 693 nm, Îµ = 309000 and 229000 cmâˆ’1, respectively, in the thin film).

Nonetheless, we noticed that the maximum IPCE value of 12.4% at 460 nm was red-shifted by 23 nm with respect to the absorption maximum of BP (Fig. 3b, dotted line). This shift is due to the photocurrent from the light-harvesting dyad, which is understandably small because of four to six times weaker absorption of the dye (vide supra). Theoretically calculated IPCE spectra that are based on the number of photons absorbed within a photoactive layer (see ESIâ€ ) gave a calculated IPCE value of 12.3% at the peak wavelength of 462 nm, which matches very well the experimental value of 12.4% at 460 nm. Therefore, we also consider that the red-shift occurred because of an optical filtering effect by the BP layer that absorbs light before the light reaches compound 3 in the active layer.16

To study the dependence of the photocurrent on the external electric field, we used the absolute photocurrent Jph (= JL âˆ’ JD) as a function of effective applied voltage V0 âˆ’ V, where V0 is the compensation voltage at which Jph = 0 (Fig. 4a).17 The IPCE spectra changed dramatically upon application of a âˆ’0.72 or âˆ’0.22 negative effective bias voltage (Fig. 4b, top), which resulted in a photocurrent in the forward direction (JPh = 1.7 mA cmâˆ’2 at V0 âˆ’ V = âˆ’0.72 V, Fig. 4a) that is highest between 500 and 550 nm (Fig. 4b, top). We consider that the photocurrent generation at this wavelength (cf.Fig. 1) is a signature of the benzodifuranone dye contribution for photocurrent generation, owing to excitation of the fullerene component by energy transfer. These measurements also suggest that two separate charge carrier generation processes exist in this photovoltaic device. That is, a realignment of energy levels upon application of the bias voltage occurred and changed the relative driving force of the two charge-separation pathways. (1) Under a 1.78 or 0.28 V positive effective bias (Fig. 4a and 4b (bottom)), electron transfer from the LUMO of BP to the LUMO of the dyad components (dye, fullerene) was normally favored (Fig. 5a). (2) Under a positive effective bias, the LUMO level of BP is lowered and the LUMO of the dyad components is increased (Fig. 5b). Therefore, charge carrier generation viabenzodifuranone excitons becomes predominant (Fig. 4b, top) and the photocurrent flows in the reverse direction.18,19

Conclusions

We synthesized a new light-absorbing [70]fullereneâ€“benzodifuranone dyad to harvest light around the 500 nm wavelength region. By introducing the benzodifuranone dye unit to a [70]fullerene, we have shown electric-field-dependent photocurrent generation from BP- or dyad-excitons in a BP/dyad OPV device. The latter results in the generation of a forward photocurrent by light absorption of the benzodifuranone dye. However, this was achieved effectively only under negative effective applied bias. Under reverse photocurrent generation with zero bias voltage, the contribution of the dye was discernible but weak, because of overwhelming light absorption by BP. With the principle of a donorâ€“acceptor dual OPV system in hand, we expect that proper selection of a dye will allow us to increase the range of wavelengths available for thin-film OPV devices.

Device fabrication

The organic photovoltaic devices were fabricated on a poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrenesulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) (Baytron AI4083) coated indium tin oxide (ITO) substrate. The insoluble p-layer BP was deposited via a solution procedure as described elsewhere.3[70]Fullereneâ€“benzodifuranone dyad 3 (0.7 wt%) was solubilized overnight in THF and filtered through a 0.45 Î¼m poly(tetrafluoroethylene) (PTFE) filter prior to use in device fabrication. The solution was spin-coated at 3000 rpm for 30 s and thermally annealed at 120 Â°C for 5 min in an inert-atmosphere N2 glove box. The devices were completed by deposition of a 7 nm 2,9-bis(naphthalen-2-yl)-4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline (NBphen) layer and an 80 nm Al electrode.

Performance evaluation of the OPV devices

The encapsulated organic photovoltaic cells were subjected to Jâ€“V measurements under both dark and irradiated conditions. Currentâ€“voltage sweeps were acquired using a Keithley 2400 source measurement unit controlled by a computer. For the power conversion efficiency measurement, a solar simulator system (Sumitomo Heavy Industries Advanced Machinery), AM1.5G, with an intensity of 100 mW cmâˆ’2 was used as the light source. For the incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) measurement, a constant power mode was employed using monochromatized photons from halogen or xenon lamps.
