Abstract

The presolvated Li2S cathodes can be matched with lithium metal-free electrodes to overcome the safety problems of lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries, which can contribute to promising devices for next-generation energy storage fields and enhance the performance of batteries by tailoring the solidâ€“liquid reaction interface. Most importantly, this facile loading method of liquid Li2S can provide a new opportunity to construct a commercially viable assembly for industrial batteries. However, few reviews have concentrated on the challenges and opportunities of preliquid Li2S-based batteries. Hence, we provide an overview of recent results on preliquid Li2S cathodes for high-performance batteries. First, the synthetic methods of liquid Li2S are summarized with their features. This is then followed by an up-to-date account of different functional materials to reduce the overpotential of preliquid Li2S and improve the electrochemical properties by engineering the structure, tailoring the interface, modifying catalytic sites, and adding functional materials. Finally, a short outlook is proposed about the potential future developments and commercialization processes in preliquid Li2S-based batteries.

1 Introduction

With the growth of the economy and the improvement of lifestyle, global energy demand is increasing rapidly.1,2 Compared with traditional energy (e.g., fuels and coals), renewable sources are adopted in alternative energy technologies, including solar, wind, and tidal energy.2â€“15 To maximize the utilization of clean and renewable energies, many initiatives have been taken toward energy storage technologies.2,13,14,16â€“18 Among them, lithium-ion batteries serve as the most efficient way of applying these clean energy sources to daily consumption, which can decrease the usage of fossil fuels and can be beneficial to the environment.2,4,5,12â€“14,19â€“23 Meanwhile, this battery technology has promising prospects, especially in the fields of notebook computers and vehicles, because of its 350 W h kgâˆ’1 energy density and suitable operating voltage range. As a case in point, the Tesla company offers a Li-ion-based plug-in electric car (Model 3) with an all-electric range of 400â€“500 km and an energy density of 60â€“80 kW h. According to the lithium-ion battery factory assessment launched in 2021, the recent global cell manufacturing for electrical vehicles target is set to grow to 2492 G W h in 2025.24,25 However, current commercial electrode materials (lithium iron phosphate, lithium nickel manganese cobalt oxides, etc.) are reaching theoretical limitations of capacity and are still far from the increased energy density demand.26â€“30

Researchers are increasingly giving more importance to sulfur (S8) cathode-based lithium-ion (Liâ€“S) batteries due to their high capacity (1672 mA h gâˆ’1), suitable energy density (2600 W h kgâˆ’1), and better potential range (1.5â€“3.0 V versus lithium) for high-capacity electrode materials.31â€“34 In addition, the environmentally friendly sulfur element has rich resources on Earth, leading to a relatively lower cost (âˆ¼150 USD per ton) than raw cathode materials (âˆ¼3300 USD per ton of cobalt) in Li-ion batteries.15,24,29,35,36 Unfortunately, Li-ion batteries use a pure S8 cathode and a highly hazardous lithium piece as the anode electrode. In manufacturing processes, the continuous plating and stripping behavior of pure lithium metal can cause formation of the dendritic and mossy Li surface and further puncture separators of the cell, generating serious safety issues.17,37â€“43

To solve this problem,13 the Li2S material represents a better candidate for using as a promising lithium-containing cathode electrode in lithium-ion batteries, where Li-ions are already incorporated into the Li2S structure. Li2S can pair with a lithium-free anode (such as silicon, graphene, copper, and graphite) to assemble a full battery with high performance,14 which will avoid lithium dendrite issues. More crucially, Li2S has a high electrochemical capacity (1166 mA h gâˆ’1), which is greater than the theoretical electrochemical capacity of existing commercial cathode materials.20,33,44,45 Furthermore, the Li2S cathode can minimize volume expansion (it has a lower density of 1.66 g cmâˆ’3 than sulfur, which has a density of 2.07 g cmâˆ’3), resulting in a more stable electrode structure. Meanwhile, Li2S has a greater melting point (938 Â°C) than S8 (115 Â°C), which might broaden the application field of Liâ€“S batteries, including robotics in firefighting, spacecraft, and electronic equipment in high-temperature environments. Benefiting from these excellent characteristics, Li2S materials will be deemed to be an alternative electrode for Liâ€“S batteries (Fig. 1).4â€“6,46,47

Unfortunately, Li2S cathodes also have certain application problems, such as LiS being mainly an electrochemically inactive material and requiring a high activated potential barrier in the first-time cell charging. More specifically, Li2S is an inorganic deliquescent ionic compound with a face-centered cubic (fcc) anti-fluorite (anti-CaF2) crystal structure and space group Fmm.3 In this structure, each Li+ cation is surrounded by four S2âˆ’ anions inside the structure, and the corresponding S2âˆ’ anion has a coordination number of eight with Li+ cations, resulting in enormous voids in the crystal structure and high solubility in polar solvents.

As a result, the bulk Li2S material shows mostly inert electrochemical reactions, and it requires activation at a high charging potential.3,20,48â€“53 In 2012, the Yang team reported that a high charging potential of 4.0 V was used to overcome the voltage barrier of the Li2S cathode, achieving an attractive capacity of 800 mA h gâˆ’1, whereas the normal cutoff charge potential of Liâ€“S batteries (3.0 V) cannot make the Li2S-based cell work.39 Worse still, electrolyte decomposition-based side reactions can cause poor cycle stability and low energy efficiency after charging at 4.0 V.54â€“57 For the high activation voltage, the Yang team also revealed that the activation process of Li2S implies a two-phase reaction (solidâ€“liquid) during the first charging behavior, and the high activation barrier is related to phase nucleation.39 More specifically, polysulfides do not nucleate until the voltage reaches the top of the activation barrier because of the higher formation enthalpy of Li2S. In addition, Kang et al.40 showed that increasing the contact area between soluble polysulfides and solid Li2S can effectively improve battery performance. Therefore, directly applying preliquid Li2S as cathodes can enhance the performance of batteries, which greatly increases the contact areas between Li2S and the functional materials. Therefore, the electrons and Li ions become more easily transported, making it easier to convert Li2S to polysulfides, thereby reducing the barrier to Li2S activation. Subsequently, Chiang et al.4 proposed that the morphology and particle size can also affect the activation process of Li2S particles. Decreasing the particle size of Li2S can lead to a reduced activation barrier, better utilization of Li2S, and improved performance. For example, high-energy ball milling is applied to lower the activation energy barrier and boost battery performance.58,59 More specifically, Kang et al. showed that dissolved long-chain lithium polysulfides can improve the charge transfer for solid Li2S, resulting in the removal of the large potential barrier after surficial solid Li2S electrochemically oxidizes into liquid and soluble lithium polysulfides.40 They proposed that increasing the contact area between soluble polysulfides and solid Li2S can significantly boost the performance of Li2S-based cells. Moreover, the Chiang team showed that regulating the morphology of electrodeposited Li2S by controlling the nucleation density and rates can minimize the overpotential.4 Benefitting from the regulated size of Li2S particles,58 some research groups aim to reduce the initial activation potential by the ultrahigh-speed ball milling approach, which increases the contact surface of each electrode material, shortens Li+ diffusion, and enhances Li2S reactivity.60â€“62 However, the ultrahigh-speed ball milling approach not only brings about security problems, a low output rate, and high cost, but also the produced reaction interface of the electrode remains in solidâ€“solid contact. As a better choice, the presolvation approach of Li2S has received much interest since it is a simple way to change the contact interface of solidâ€“solid into a better contacting area of the solidâ€“liquid state. Most importantly, this facile loading method of liquid Li2S can provide a new opportunity to construct a commercially viable assembly for industrial batteries. However, Li2S cathodes share a similar set of drawbacks as the S8 material, including the â€œshuttleâ€ effect and low electric/ionic conductivity. The challenge of the â€œshuttleâ€ effect is the notorious self-diffusion behavior of polysulfides. In cell running, intermediate products of polysulfides can get away from the cathode to the anode by the concentration gradient in the liquid electrolytes, resulting in the loss of active materials and poor cycling stability. Currently, people are interested in lowering the activation potential of Li2S and restraining the â€œshuttleâ€ effect of polysulfides.

Hence, we review the emerging advances in tailoring of the reaction interface by the presolvation of bulk Li2S to enhance the electrochemical performance of devices. Meanwhile, this facile loading method of Li2S solution will be a critical step in commercializing Liâ€“S cell applications. First, we start with a discussion about the presolvation of Li2S with their outstanding advantages based on the high performance of batteries. Then, we overview different types of main kinds of functional materials (carbon materials, metal sulfides, oxides, phosphides, and organic compounds) with emphasis on their interface properties in preliquid Li2S batteries (Fig. 2a). Finally, we summarize existing opportunities and provide a brief perspective about the presolvation strategies of Li2S in potential commercial applications.

2 The presolvation of Li2S

Generally, random accumulations (Fig. 2b) of Li2S particles have limited contact to generate a solidâ€“solid interface with the functional material with slower reaction kinetics, resulting in polysulfide migration. Worse still, partial Li2S cannot come into contact with the electrolyte or/and electrode, which is an intermediate location of the cathode, making the electrochemical process difficult to complete. Following the presolvation treatment, the bulk Li2S particles are transformed into liquid Li2S, allowing the solidâ€“solid contact to be transformed into the solidâ€“liquid contact. As a result, the reaction kinetics and electron/ion transfer are considerably enhanced, and the redox overpotential is reduced, which also lowers the barrier in Li2S activation and polysulfide conversion processing.

The tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) solvent and phosphorus pentasulfide (P2S5) materials are used to solvate the bulk Li2S (Fig. 3a). Liang's team reported that the P2S5-TEGDME system can accelerate the dissolution of bulk Li2S,63 promote the reaction kinetics of Li2S, and optimize the lithium metal surface to suppress the shuttle effect of polysulfides. A P2S5-based Li2S cell demonstrates a better reversible capacity (approximately 1300 mA h gâˆ’1), retaining the stabilized performance (900 mA h gâˆ’1) of the Liâ€“S cell with 40 cycles (approximately 98% coulombic efficiency) at 0.1C (Fig. 3b and c). Zu et al. further demonstrated that this method can help reduce the activation potential to 2.4 V.64

3 Functional materials in the presolvation of Li2S cells

The strong chemical interaction between functional materials, Li2S, and polysulfides can improve the capacity of Li2S-based batteries,6,41,71 which facilitates the free migration of polysulfides in the electrolyte by physical confinement and/or chemical adsorption to enhance the redox conversion efficiency of polysulfides by various catalytic materials. By modifying the carbon surface with heteroatoms (e.g., oxygen, nitrogen, and boron), nonpolar carbon materials can be converted to polar hosts.72 These heteroatoms can chemically anchor polysulfides by forming polar interactions. Apart from heteroatom-doped carbon materials, metal oxides/sulfides/phosphides have been proven capable of confining polysulfides via chemical interactions such as â€œlithium bondingâ€ and electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged active sites in polysulfides and host materials.3 For example, metal oxides have been widely reported to confine polysulfides by polarâ€“polar interactions. The strong polarity allows them to form metalâ€“sulfur and/or oxygenâ€“lithium bonds. Additionally, the complex multistep reactions and sluggish redox kinetics enhance the polysulfide migration. Metal sulfides can significantly decrease the activation energy barrier by forming stable reaction intermediates. Metal sulfides also show catalytic properties in the conversion of soluble polysulfides and oxidation of Li2S during charging and discharging. In general, functional materials have positive effects for improved Li2S-based battery performance.46,73

3.1 Carbon materials

Generally, the preliquid Li2S cathode can reduce the initial activation barrier to avoid the decomposition of the electrolyte, enabling more stable battery performance with a longer cycle life.14 Nevertheless, the intrinsically nonconductive nature of Li2S and polysulfide shuttling behavior are still not negligible.49 Therefore, functional materials tend to be applied to enhance the conductivity and impede the â€œshuttleâ€ effect. Among them, the carbon material is an efficient and inexpensive option due to its strong conductivity (approximately 5000 S cmâˆ’1 for carbon nanotubes), flexible fabrication processes, and rich resources in nature.38,39,49,74â€“79

In what follows, the hierarchical electrodes of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) and reduced graphene oxide (rGO) are suitable options for manufacturing composites with preliquid Li2S to restrict the migration of polysulfides.75,80,81 Furthermore, the combination of the two carbon materials can constitute a three-dimensional (3D) construction of the cathode with enough inner space for the electrochemical reactions in polysulfides during Liâ€“S cell cycling. To date, researchers have developed diverse ways to prepare preliquid Li2Sâ€“carbon composite cathodes (after electrode drying, Li2S materials are uniformly covered with the carbon material).44,49,74,81,82 The facile approach is directly dropping Li2S solution onto carbon materials, which can be extremely easy and convenient. Another method is to encapsulate Li2S nanoparticles by combining Li2S solution with precursors for further carbonization, resulting in a more stable structure at a lower cost. In 2015, the Li and Chen team developed a flexible rGO-based Li2S cathode, which is constituted by the facile dropping of the Li2S solution (Fig. 4a).83 This flexible Li2S-based rGO cathode will be utilized directly as the cathode in conjunction with â€œlithium metal-freeâ€ anodes, which include nano silicon/copper materials, oxides, graphite, graphene, and so on. Under this free-standing cathode design (cutoff charge potential of 3.6 V), the cycling capacity of the Li2S/rGO cell can remain 462 mA h gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles at 5C current. The as-prepared Li2S/rGO cell also exhibits an outstanding rate capability, with capacities of approximately 1000, 900, 750, 700, and 640 mA h gâˆ’1 under 0.05, 0.1, 1, 3, and 5C, respectively. At a high current of 7C, this cell still maintains a stable reversible performance of 597 mA gâˆ’1 (Fig. 4b). To optimize the structure of carbon materials, the He group fabricated freestanding cathodes of 3D CNT-graphene-Li2S,33 exhibiting reversible performance of Liâ€“S cells (approximately 1100 mA h gâˆ’1) and a low fading capacity (0.02% per cycle). Additionally, this cell exhibits approximately 500 mA h gâˆ’1 at 4C at 3.6 V charge potential. Owing to such tailoring of microstructures, the 3D CNT-graphene-Li2S cathode shows freestanding and binder-free features. In conclusion, the optimization of the mesopore structure comes from one-dimensional (1D) CNTs. In addition, the enhanced redox electron transfer and improved interface of the electrolyte/electrode come from the 3D framework of cathodes, which enhance both the electrochemical activity and utilization rate of Li2S.33,75,84â€“91

Borrowing from the idea of doping of a single atom, multiatom codoping may be conducive to better performance of Liâ€“S cells, which will offer sufficient active sites for the stable adsorption of polysulfides and more doped atom characteristics.33,44,75,83 For instance, He et al. reported cobalt and nitrogen codoped stabilized graphitic carbon (Câ€“Coâ€“N), which was obtained by carbonization of metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs, named ZIF-67).92 The prepared Câ€“Coâ€“N materials serve as an effective carbon substrate for hosting Li2S solution by the liquid infiltrationâ€“evaporation technique (Fig. 4e). Although doping strategies of carbon can increase the performance of Liâ€“S batteries, they also increase the cost of material synthesis.

3.2 Metal compounds

Compared to the atomic doping of carbon materials, functional materials (metal sulfides, metal oxides, and so on) couple directly with the cathode, which further enhances the better properties of Li2S-based cells.41,99,100 Nanostructured functional materials can afford more physical/chemical trap sites for polysulfides and shorten the ion/electron diffusion length, thus promoting redox-electrochemistry with enhanced electrochemical reaction kinetics.22,37,41,77,99â€“102
3.2.1 Metal sulfides. Among the various metal compounds, metal sulfides have abundant resources on Earth, which has attracted researchers' interest in different fields, especially in energy storage systems.41,103 Metal sulfides have some advantages, as described below. The first is the strong anchoring property for the polysulfide-containing species between the Li atoms of lithium polysulfide (LiPS) and the S atoms of metal sulfides.54 Second, metal sulfides are generally metallic or semimetallic with better electrical conductivity.37 Lastly, the interfacial active sites of metal sulfides can promote the conversion of polysulfides by the absorption effect on the substrate, thus restraining polysulfide migration.10,41,99,104 The Y. Cui team discovered that the electrochemical reaction process of Li2S is critical for obtaining the high performance and better cycling properties of Liâ€“S batteries, where Li2S can be formed by electrochemically depositing polysulfides and/or chemically disproportionately depositing polysulfides (Fig. 5a).41 According to the first-principles calculations, six metal sulfides (SnS2, CoS2, FeS, TiS2, VS2, and Ni3S2) are selected as model systems to analyze the interaction of Li2S conversion and polysulfide adsorption. During the initial charging process, the SnS2-Li2S, Ni3S2-Li2S, FeS-Li2S, CoS2-Li2S, VS2-Li2S, and TiS2-Li2S cathodes (Fig. 5b) demonstrate various potential barriers (3.53, 3.47, 3.25, 3.01, 2.91, and 2.88 V, respectively). Compared to pure carbon materials and Ni3S2-, SnS2-, and FeS-based cathodes, the VS2-, TiS2-, and CoS2-decorated electrodes show better cell performance, higher specific capacity, and lower overpotential, which is consistent with the DFT calculations.41

The initial voltage to activate Li2S can be decreased by accelerated redox interactions, which results in the formation of soluble intermediates that are simpler to oxidize.116 Meini et al. for the first time introduced the application of redox mediators (RM) to lower the activation potential of Li2S (Fig. 7d).117 Five different mediators are investigated with different redox potentials, where lithium iodide, ferrocene, and decamethylferrocene all show a higher voltage than the reaction voltage of Li2S, thereby effectively affecting the redox potential. 4 Outlook and summary

For the sake of a high energy density energy storage system, rechargeable Liâ€“S batteries that utilize sulfur as the cathode could be an available option. However, if researchers can provide sufficient measures to address the poor irreversibility and security risks of lithium, the commercial applications of Liâ€“S batteries will be further promoted. Following modern development trends, replacing sulfur with lithium sulfide (Li2S) as the lithium-containing cathode represents an alternative solution to avoid reactive lithium metal issues. Despite the combination with the lithium-free anode, there is also considerable room for improvements in fully lithiated Li2S, which suffers from a high charging overpotential, low electrical conductivity, and poor ion diffusivity. Given these results, significant progress has been made for Li2S cathodes by fabricating electrodes of Li2S cathodes with better performance and adding electrolyte additives to provide feasibility in commercializing Li2S-based batteries. Meanwhile, the activation energy of Li2S is affected by a highly stable ionic crystal, which is an antifluorite structure with the opposite arrangement. The corresponding anions show coordination number 8 in the face-centered cubic array, and cations have coordination number 4 in the tetrahedral holes. Due to this structure, the stronger bond energy of Li2S shows a higher standard enthalpy of formation. Additionally, the actual structure (i.e., amorphous), morphology, particle size, catalyst varieties, and area of reaction contact affect the activation process of Li2S during the first charge cycle. Ergo, the optimization strategies based on the above factors can contribute to a decreased activation barrier, better utilization of Li2S, and improved performance, which includes electrode design and material synthesis. Compared to solid Li2S, Li2S solution can expand the contact area between the activated materials and hosts and further lead to more nucleation sites. Moreover, the fast diffusion rate of solvating Li2S can promote charge transfer and reduce the ion transfer path, thereby enhancing the kinetics of the reaction interface and oxidation process of Li2S.

Hence, this review attempts to comprehensively summarize the presolvation strategies of Li2S for the development of Liâ€“S cells. Although many methods have been proposed and developed (Table 2), research on Li2S is still lacking. For example, with regard to the fundamental electrochemical study and engineering of cathodes, considerable work needs to be done. First, in situ analytical techniques may offer valuable references (such as Raman spectroscopy, electron spin resonance spectroscopy, and X-ray spectroscopy) to better understand the electrochemical reaction process. The obtained results could provide significant improvements in the rational design of solvated Li2S cathodes.

Second, the obtained Li2S-based batteries would have to reduce the high initial charging barrier but also suppress the â€œshuttleâ€ effect. The shuttling of polysulfides is as important as Li2S activation, which can be fully explained in most sulfur-based batteries. Therefore, the structural design of Li2S cathode materials should be considered as an integral strategy that combines electrocatalysts with physical material modification. Due to Li2S being sensitive to moisture and prone to hydrolyze in air, developing reasonable synthetic strategies to keep Li2S stable is an interesting direction as well.

Third, the development of Li-free, high-capacity anodes and electrolytes can be of great importance in Li2S-based batteries. To date, some researchers have chosen graphite, silicon, or tin as the anode to match with Li2S cathodes. However, their low electronic conductivity and large volume change during charging/discharging should be settled. Adding novel electrolyte strategies is an effective way to overcome the above issues.

In the end, the presolvation approach of Li2S has received much interest since it is a simple way to change the contact interface of solidâ€“solid into the greater contacting area of the solidâ€“liquid state. After solvent evaporation from the Li2S solution, low surface tension solvents can lead to fast homogenous nucleation rates and facilitate the uniform distribution of Li2S particles, decreasing the activation barrier. However, the presolvation strategy of solvents need to be prepurified operation, additional cost, and assembly step, which both should be further improved to develop presolvation technology.
