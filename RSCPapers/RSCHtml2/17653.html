Abstract

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are an interesting class of porous crystalline solids assembled from organic ligands and metal clusters (or simply metal cations), which have shown a rich variety of promising applications. In recent years, these interesting materials have become one of the most actively studied areas in the field of new materials development. One of the most interesting aspects of MOFs is their optical properties and porosity, which may be smartly coupled with the aim of creating multifunctional materials. This work gives an overview of the most significant achievements on this particular theme by bringing an up-to-date collection of work focused on tuning the luminescent emission of MOFs, according to structural changes derived from their porous nature.

1. Introduction

Today, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) do not need to be introduced in all areas related to chemistry and materials science, since they hold a central place in the development of porous functional materials.1 This particular class of coordination polymers has awakened a special interest due to its topological diversity of open structures that obey a well established rational design,2 in addition to a wide set of potential properties originating from their hybrid nature.3 As a consequence, these materials are bringing an important contribution to be applied in some highlighted areas, such as luminescence, gas adsorption, magnetism, drug delivery, and optical storage.4 The above-mentioned design of MOFs is successfully achieved by deconstructing the structures into nodes (metal ions or clusters) and linkers (organic ligands), following the strategy of reticular chemistry.5 In this regard, synthetic conditions often play a significant role in the construction of MOFs, since they direct the binding between the initial constituents into secondary and molecular building blocks (SBUs and MBBs).1b,2a,6 Although they are generally prepared using solvothermal methods, conventional or even novel and less-explored routes are also starting to be employed.7 Hereafter, these SBUs and MBBs govern self-assembling process in a highly ordered and crystalline manner, such that structures contain a uniformly distributed network of voids. Therefore, the selection of linkers plays a main role in directing the topology of the structures. Accordingly, synthetic chemists have devoted much effort to designing novel organic ligands for the preparation of new MOF materials.8 The choice of metal centres is also of importance, since these afford most of the previously mentioned properties and characteristics. Specifically, coordination polymers containing d10 metals, such as Cd2+ or Zn2+, have attracted extensive interest in recent decades, given their ability to render fascinating structures.4a,9 This fact is a consequence of the absence of ligand field constraints, associated with the d10 configuration of these ions, which provides flexible coordination environments that can be adapted to a wide variety of geometries. Hence, they allow fine tuning of the structures and/or topologies. Moreover, the closed-shell configuration also possesses some additional advantages regarding the photoluminescence properties.10 On the one hand, the absence of potential quenching processes derived from dâ€“d transitions permits efficient luminescent emission. On the other hand, the resulting polymers contain excited states of a ligand-centred (LC) and/or a ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) nature, a coexistence that may give access to interesting luminescent emissions.

As we will explain in greater detail during the following sections, two major approaches can be distinguished to tune the luminescence performance of Zn/Cd-based MOFs. The first concerns a change in the luminescence of the neat material when subjected to variations that are inherent to the crystal structure. To start with, it deals with the emission capacity of MOFs in terms of their luminescence lifetime, discerning between the fluorescent and phosphorescent components present in the few examples that report afterglow or long-lasting phosphorescence. Then, it continues with an analysis of how the emission intensity may be promoted or inhibited according to structural features. In particular, varying the excitation wavelength, changing the interpenetration degree, and doping the host materials with lanthanide luminophores are described in detail. Moreover, these parameters may also be coupled with the response of the material according to temperature. The second approach focuses on the influence of external species upon the luminescence behaviour, taking advantage of their porous nature. The next sections are devoted to modifying the colour of the emission by encapsulating lanthanide ions or other luminescent complexes. Selected recognition of ions, solvents, or molecules is developed during the remaining sections, which opens up a route to the construction of multifunctional materials.

Therefore, bearing the above considerations in mind, in this article we perform a detailed discussion of the possibilities offered by Zn/Cd MOFs to be used as luminescent devices for a broad set of applications in the near future. While we will provide the reader with a number of recent studies, we would like to refer to reviews by Allendorf,11 Binnemans,12 Chen,3a Rocha,13 and MÃ¼ller-Buschbaum14et al. for a comprehensive and more general view on this topic.

2. Structure related inherent tuning

2.1. Fluorescence and phosphorescence behaviour

Afterglow, long-lasting phosphorescence (LLP) and persistent luminescence are highly desired phenomena because they imply luminescence processes with decay times ranging from seconds to hours, after the removal of the excitation source. Thus, materials possessing this property may have an immediate impact in many commercial applications in the fields of traffic signs, light-emitting diodes, flat panel displays, and similar devices,15 as well as in developing chemical sensors, optical recording devices, biological imaging and security systems.16 Luminescent MOFs containing noble metals or lanthanides usually present photoemission lifetimes in the range of hundreds of microseconds (Î¼s) to a few milliseconds (ms), far from the persistent luminescence that requires emission decays of tens of ms. However, a few MOFs with LLP behaviour are starting to emerge. In this line, materials with formulae {[Zn(tipa)Cl]NO3Â·2DMF}n and {[Cd2(tipa)2Cl4]Â·6DMF}n (tipa = tri(4-imidazolylphenyl)amine), reported by Yuan et al.,17 are good examples of metalâ€“organic long-lived phosphors. Although both compounds exhibit open architectures, the crystal structures are somewhat different. The first consists of a 3D twofold interpenetrated utp framework, whereas the second originates from the non-interpenetrated packing of 2D kgd networks (Fig. 1). This fact is a consequence of the different secondary building units established in each case, given the tetrahedral and octahedral coordination environments established around the metal centres, probably owing to the size of the metal ions. Despite their different structural features, both compounds show the capacity to entrap iodine molecules (as I3âˆ’ anions) into their pores. This capacity proceeds via single-crystal to single-crystal transformations that undergo a simple anion-exchange (for the cationic zinc framework) or a more complicated process involving breakage/formation of Mâ€“Cl bonds (for the neutral cadmium structure).

Another example of phosphorescent behaviour in MOFs is constituted by two zinc-terephthalate (TPA)-based compounds. Both [Zn(Î¼4-TPA)(DMF)]n, and the widely explored MOF-5, show an obvious afterglow emission at room temperature,19 a fact that clearly facilitates their applicability. The [Zn(Î¼4-TPA)(DMF)]n is formed from the linkage of paddle-wheel [Zn2(COO)4] dinuclear SBUs, giving rise to 2D layers that are stacked on each other in an offset fashion and in an ABCABC sequence. The resulting architecture shows a 2D â†’ 3D polythreading that preserves 1D channels of 8.7 Ã— 8.7 Ã…2. The well known MOF-5 was the first robust MOF prepared and is possibly one of the most explored MOFs for many different purposes.20 This presents a 3D cubic architecture built from [Zn4O] clusters arranged into octahedral subunits that are joined by TPA ligands, leading to a microchannel network enclosing a BET surface area of 3900 m2 gâˆ’1. Both [Zn(Î¼4-TPA)(DMF)]n and MOF-5 present dual-emission fluorescent/phosphorescent behaviour. Fluorescent emissions at 326 and 434 nm are observed with very different quantum yields of 2.25 and 46.27%, respectively. Phosphorescent emissions at 503 and 513 nm (associated lifetimes of ca. 472 and 153 ms) possess much lower quantum yields, according to the inherent weaker intensity at these wavelengths. As a result, green emissions are observed by the naked eye after the removal of UV light (Fig. 3). The distinct photoluminescence features may be ascribed to the different stacking fashions of the ligands and metal clusters present in the MOFs. These results are somewhat surprising, considering the very weak phosphorescence (of ca. 0.5 ms) shown by pure TPA,21 particularly for [Zn(Î¼4-TPA)(DMF)]n, with a lifetime enhanced by nearly three orders of magnitude. Such an achievement prompted the authors to analyse the electronic structure of [Zn(Î¼4-TPA)(DMF)]n by periodic density functional theory (PDFT). According to the electron-density distribution of the HOMO and LUMO (highest occupied and lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals) upon the [Zn2(COO)4] cluster and the TPA ligand, respectively, it is concluded that the photoluminescence performance occurs via MLCT (metal to ligand charge transfer). Furthermore, it is also deduced that the long afterglow observed possesses a dual origin: (i) the participation of the zinc ion in the frontier orbital distribution promotes spinâ€“orbit coupling that improves the electronâ€“hole separation time during the charge transfer process; (ii) the non-radiative losses are minimised by the coordination of the TPA ligand, which may increase the intersystem crossing between the singlet and triplet excited states.

2.2. Influence of the excitation wavelength

The hybrid nature of MOFs, which facilitates a rational design through an ever increasing number of synthetic procedures, stands as a clear advantage over other materials with respect to exhibiting a second harmonic generation (SHG). This is a property of practical importance in many areas, such as the laser industry and optoelectronic technologies.23 Bearing in mind that a SHG requires an acentric structure, the most direct way to obtain SHG-active MOFs lies in the use of asymmetrical or even chiral ligands. Among others, V-shaped ligands (in which the two benzene rings are not coplanar) may induce helicity or a flexuous nature in the polymeric chains and therefore the homochiral MOFs. This is the case with the homochiral {[Zn(L1)(oba)]Â·H2O}n compound [L1 = 1,1â€²-oxybis(3,5-dipyridine-benzene), oba = 4,4â€²-oxybis(benzoate)],24 which is based on double-stranded 65 helical chains established from the coordination of carboxylate groups of the oba ligands and the pyridine rings of the L1 ligand. The overall 3D architecture, grown by further connection of each helix to six neighbouring ones through the oba anions, can be regarded as an open qtz topological network, which crystallizes as a triply interpenetrated framework that still leaves helical channels. Preliminary studies on the powdered sample indicate that it is SHG-active, with an efficiency of approximately 0.8 times that of urea. Moreover, as measured by solid-state circular dichroism, the compound exhibits an obvious positive Cotton effect, which suggests a same-handed conformation for the entire bulk sample. Under excitation at 380 nm, this compound exhibits an emission band peaking at 460 nm, which is remarkably red-shifted with respect to the intraligand fluorescent emission (of Ï€*â€“Ï€ nature) of L1.25

An alternative way to achieve tuneable luminescence in terms of structural design, although very uncommonly achieved, deals with the coordination of metal centres with symmetrical ligands in a non-centrosymmetric manner to render acentric MOFs. 2.3. Dependence of the interpenetration degree

Although a direct relationship between the interpenetration phenomenon and the luminescence properties could not be established, mainly owing to the difficulty in controlling the associated structural feature, it has been observed that both phenomena affect each other. Thus, provided that the parameters governing the interpenetration dominate, modulating the emission of the materials would be allowed. In this sense, it must be pointed out that several strategies have already been reported, though success is limited to a few systems. On the one hand, factors such as the concentration of reactants, synthesis temperature, the use of non-conventional synthetic procedures, or the presence of a templating agent in the reaction mixture, proved to be valid.30 Two 2D non-interpenetrated or interpenetrated structures, {[Zn2(L3)(btmbb)(H2O)4]Â·2H2O}n and [Zn2(L3)(btmbb)2]n [where H4L3 = 5,5â€²-(1,4-phenylenebis(methylene))bis(oxy)diisophthalic acid and btmbb = 4,4â€²-bis((1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)methyl)biphenyl], can be obtained by subtly modifying the temperature of the solvothermal reaction.31 It is shown that the variation in non-interpenetrated/interpenetrated structures is due to the conformations of the bis(triazole) ligands and the different spatial orientations of the tetracarboxylate ligands as a consequence of the presence/absence of the two coordination water molecules. This fact imposes different dihedral angles between the side and central aromatic rings of the L3 ligand as well as anti or syn conformations in the bis(triazole) ligands. Accordingly, this leads to a variable aperture in the network that governs the overall packing. The emission maxima of these compounds are observed at 384 and 412 nm, and are broader and red-shifted with regard to the H4L3 (366 with Î»exc = 280 nm) and btmbb (352 with Î»exc = 280 nm) ligands (Fig. 10). Therefore, it can be concluded that the LLCT responsible for the emission of these compounds appears to be substantially influenced by the conformation and the accumulation of changes in the ligands.

2.4. Doping MOFs with lanthanides

An alternative strategy to endow the light emitted across the entire visible spectrum with high colour purity is to perform a judicious doping of an appropriate host material with luminous lanthanide ions occupying the metal position in the structure. Thereby, the combination of red (Eu3+, Pr3+, Sm3+), green (Tb3+, Er3+), and blue (Tm3+, Ce3+, Dy3+) emissive ions into isostructural ligand-based luminescent MOFs gives access to white-light emission.34 This approach is successfully employed for the heterometallic {[LnOH(H2O)6][Zn2Ln4(4-Htbca)2(4-tbca)8(H2O)12]}nÂ·6nH2O [where LnIII = Eu and Tb] MOF prepared by Liu et al.,35 with bifunctional 4-(1H-tetrazol-5-yl)biphenyl-3-carboxylic acid (4-H2tbca). As inferred from its chemical formula, this compound consists of an anionic backbone, in which ZnN4 and LnO8 chromophores are linked by means of 4-Htbca and 4-tbca linkers, showing four different coordination modes. The resulting 2D layers, simplified as (3,6)-connected kgd networks, form octagonal motifs with quadrangle-like cavities, in which cationic [LnOH(H2O)6]2+ complexes and lattice molecules are sited. The solid-state emission spectra of the Eu and Tb counterparts show the characteristic intra-ionic transitions, although a broad band centred at 400 nm (blue emission), attributed to the Ï€* â†’ Ï€ ligand, is also observed in the terbium counterpart. Therefore, it was found that the overall colour of the Tb host doped with an Eu3+-based complex could be tuned from green to red, crossing the white light region, simply by varying the ratio of the doping ion from 0.4â€“1.5 mol%. Fortunately, a material was achieved (0.5% of Eu3+@Tb) whose emission falls within the CIE coordinates (0.330, 0.328), with a quantum yield of 11.4%, which can be considered as a very promising result. The main reason for such a broad variation in the emission colour seems to lie with a Tb3+-to-Eu3+ energy transfer, confirmed by luminescence decay measurements.

3. Tuning by external modification

3.1. Encapsulation of lanthanide(III) ions in MOFs

When tuning the luminescence properties of a poorly emissive MOF material in order to achieve an increase in its emission brightness or a change in its colour, a very useful strategy is to encapsulate luminescent molecules or complexes within the structure. Taking into account that this approach relies on the availability of empty pores in MOF structures, the stability of these materials is crucial, especially their stability in air and water, to accomplish post-synthetic modifications.36 In this sense, the encapsulation of lanthanide(III) ions is a very recurrent strategy that occupies a main position in the area of molecular photonics. Their wide employment comes from the inherent photoluminescence properties of these ions, as well as the ease with which they are combined to tune multiband emissions, just by adjusting the stoichiometry of the hetero-lanthanide cocktail.37 This reaction is particularly advantageous for obtaining white-light,38 given the demand for more environmentally friendly, safer and energy-efficient white phosphor materials to be used as white-light-emitting diodes (WLEDs).39 JUC-113 is an appropriate host material that has proven its capacity to foster lanthanide ions.40 Its crystal structure originates from the joining of centrosymmetric [Zn3(COO)6] units by flexible TCPB3âˆ’ ligands (TCPB3âˆ’ = 1,3,5-tri(4-carboxyphenoxy)benzene) into double-(6,3)-layers. The packing of these layers by aromatic Ï€â‹¯Ï€ interactions leads to a 3D framework with channels of about 4.0 Ã— 4.0 Ã…2. The as-synthesized JUC-113 yields a blue emission (owing to the band ranging from 400 to 500 nm), which is attributed to the emission of the organic linker. While filling the MOF materials with lanthanide ions at various concentrations has been consolidated as a good approach for developing white light, as shown in the previous examples, their low quantum yield remains a major drawback that hinders their use as efficient WLEDs.49 Nonetheless, apart from the strategy of combining trichromatic emitters, i.e. red, green and blue lights, other plausible methods to generate phosphor-based white light focus on dichromatic emitters that blend blue and yellow light. The ordered and crystalline porous structure in MOFs is a key feature for targeting a wide range of functional properties, typically in guest species recognition.59 Taking into account that this review focuses on luminescence responses with regard to structural features, compounds showing a modulation of the luminescence in response to an external stimuli, for instance following an induced reversible structural transformation, are of particular interest. 3.4. Molecular recognition

The selective luminescent response of MOFs is not only limited to charged ions, but is also extended to a wide diversity of neutral molecules. Unlike previous cases, in which ions are captured, molecular recognition of neutral molecules in MOFs originates necessarily from the flexibility of the framework to adapt towards the guests by means of weak interactions. In this context, it can be pointed out that low-dimensional materials, in which porosity is introduced by supramolecular interactions, may afford a promising dynamism over high-dimensional counterparts formed by metal-linker coordination bonds.71 Moreover, when these flexible nanospaces are decorated by chromophoric organic linkers, a rapid fluorescence readout may be obtained when appropriate guests are accommodated. For instance, in addition to the above-mentioned wavelength shifts and increased or decreased emission intensity, new emission bands can also be generated owing to charge transfer or exciplex/excimer formation.72 In a successful attempt to seek luminescence tuning by aromatic amine confinement, the work by Haldar and co-workers was given a substantial boost, given the capacity of the flexible {[Zn(ndc)(o-phen)2]Â·DMF}n MOF (ndc = 2,6-napthalenedicarboxylate, o-phen = 1,10-phenanthroline) to recognize different aromatic amines.73 The importance of simple devices for detecting aromatic amines comes from their relevance in drug synthesis and from their carcinogenic effects in humans.74 The good performance of this material originates from its crystal structure, which consists of zigzag chains established by the sequential coordination of ndc2âˆ’ and o-phen ligands to the zinc(II) ion, and these are further assembled into a 3D porous supramolecular structure through Ï€â‹¯Ï€ and Câ€“Hâ‹¯Ï€ interactions. 3.5. Detection of explosives

Notwithstanding the above-mentioned properties derived from the encapsulation of solvent molecules into MOFs, they also provide a good opportunity to obtain probe materials for the detection of small hazardous molecules, as proven with the NENU-522 MOF.75 Specifically, much effort is being devoted to the fast, efficient, and reliable detection of explosives, given its profound implications on both security and environmental issues.76 The single-crystal X-ray structure of this MOF, [Zn4O(L8)3(H2O)2]3[Zn4O(L)3]Â·xDMF (where L8 = 9H-carbazole-3,6-dicarboxylate), consists of two kinds of [Zn4(Î¼4-O)] clusters, distinguished by the presence of coordinated water molecules in one of them. The Zn4O SBUs are organised into truncated cages delimited by 30 ligands and 14 clusters, linked together by the L8 ligands to give rise to a 3D microporous framework with pores of about 1 nm in diameter (Fig. 24). These pores are suitable for accommodating up to 1.28 Ln3+ ions (Ln3+ = Sm3+, Eu3+, Tb3+, Dy3+), which affords composite materials with a clearly different luminescent emission. Among them, Sm3+@NENU-522 and Dy3+@NENU-522 materials are of high interest, since they approach the coordinates of white light, i.e. (0.3233, 0.2606) and (0.3301, 0.3742), respectively. This is also the case with Eu3+/Tb3+@NENU-522 (0.2917, 0.3211).

4. Conclusions

In this highlight, some of the most representative examples of metalâ€“organic frameworks exhibiting unusual and/or tuneable luminescence properties are shown. In particular, the selected materials are based on closed-shell d10 metal atoms (ZnII or CdII), since they are well-suited to the construction of fascinating open architectures, given their large coordination plasticity, while they also afford interesting photoluminescence properties. On the one hand, the intrinsic hybrid nature of these materials gives them the opportunity to experience different charge transfer processes that enable various emission phenomena, from the habitual fluorescence to the less frequent phosphorescence. Moreover, taking into account that these processes are strongly connected to some intrinsic features of the structure, the photoluminescent emission may show a dependence on subtle changes, such as the excitation wavelength and the interpenetration degree. On the other hand, the porous nature of MOFs supports the uptake of many different guests, which may also influence the emission in several useful ways. Among others, two major aims can be derived from the latter performance: tuning the emission colour and/or the selective detection of particular ions or molecules. The first takes advantage of the emissive properties of the encapsulated or doped species to generate new composite or solid-solution-like materials, in which their luminescence is enhanced or modulated. The second, developed under many sections of this highlight, deals with the changes observed in the emission spectra of the compounds (through an increase/decrease in the intensity or a shift of the maxima), depending on the incorporation of specific cations or anions. Therefore, this work shows a selection of MOFs that make an important contribution to many top-rated topics, such as long-lived emission, ions and explosives detection, white-light emission, luminescent second-harmonic generators, and materials for dye-sensitized solar cells, all of which could signify a further step towards the quest for enhanced luminescent devices.
