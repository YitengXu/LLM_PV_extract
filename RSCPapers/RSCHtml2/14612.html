Abstract

We have engineered, synthesized, and characterized a series of porphyrin sensitizers that contain either one or three methoxy groups on the phenyl ring of the triphenyl imidazole donor moiety and either 3-(5-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4-yl)thiophene-2-yl)-2-cyanoacrylic acid (LG24 and LG26) or 2-(5-(benzo[c] [1,2,5]thiadiazole-4-yl)thiophene-2-yl)methelene-4-oxo-2-thiathiazolidin-3-yl-acetic acid (LG25 and LG27) acceptor groups, adopting a donor-Ï€-acceptor structure. All four sensitizers are characterized using various spectroscopic techniques and electrochemical methods. The Q bands of all four sensitizers are redshifted when compared to the previously detailed LG18 sensitizer, and the absorption onset is extended to 800 nm. Density functional theory calculations suggest that the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels are delocalized on the triphenylimidazole donor and porphyrin macrocycle, while the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels reside on auxiliary acceptors and anchoring groups, facilitating intramolecular interactions. Finally, we have evaluated the photovoltaic performances of these sensitizers in dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) using a liquid Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox electrolyte, and the performances were compared with the previously reported LG18 sensitizer. We observed lower efficiencies from the porphyrin dyes having rhodanine-3-acetic acid anchoring groups (LG25 and LG27) than from the porphyrin dyes having cyanoacrylic acid anchoring groups (LG24 and LG26). Both the LG24 and LG26 dyes with conjugated electron acceptors can not only maintain a broad absorption spectrum but they can also enhance the electronic structure, considerably improving electron injection and lowering charge recombination compared to our previous reports. On the other hand, the LG18 dye showed better photovoltaic performance than the LG24 and LG26 dyes, probably due to its more negative LUMO, and as a result its Voc was enhanced. The power conversion efficiency (PCE) order was LG26 â‰¥ LG24 > LG27 â‰¥ LG25. Furthermore, we fabricated a co-sensitized DSSC using the UV-absorbing dye Y1. The LG26 + Y1-based DSSC showed the highest PCE of 10.45%. To understand the reasons for the photovoltaic performance trends, we have carried out intensity-modulated photovoltage spectroscopy and nanosecond transient absorption studies on the present sensitizers and compared them with the previously reported sensitizer LG18.

Introduction

In recent years, focus on dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) has intensified after G24 released its first commercial products.1 Although commercialization has started, attention must still be paid to the design of low-cost efficient materials to further improve efficiency and, in particular, durability. Oâ€™Regan and GrÃ¤tzel first reported DSSC technology in 1991 with a device efficiency of 7.1%, using a Ru(II) polypyridyl complex as a sensitizer.2 Over a period of a couple of decades, certified power conversion efficiency (PCE) values have reached 11.9% using Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes as sensitizers and PCEs of up to 14.7% have been obtained using metal-free organic dyes.3,4 Despite these high efficiencies, the search for low-cost efficient materials and new fabrication methods is ongoing. Among the diverse components of DSSCs, the sensitizer plays an important role in achieving high PCEs. However, both ruthenium-based dyes and metal-free organic dyes have their own merits and drawbacks.5â€“7 As a result, porphyrins are found to be the best alternatives for use in DSSC technology, based on a concept inspired by natural photosynthesis. Porphyrins are widely present in biological systems and have shown impressive performance for many optoelectronic applications, such as organic light-emitting diodes, hole-transporting materials for perovskite solar cells, sensitizers for photodynamic therapy, and DSSCs.8â€“14

Several porphyrin-based dyes have been designed and used in DSSCs, using either meso-phenyl or Î²-pyrrole designs. However, their PCEs remained at âˆ¼7%, perhaps due to the recombination of oxidized (whole) porphyrin with electrons present in the TiO2 conduction band.15â€“20 For this reason, the design strategy has been changed to a donor-Ï€-acceptor (D-Ï€-A) concept, using an organic moiety as a donor, a porphyrin as a Ï€-spacer, and a heteroaromatic moiety as an acceptor.21â€“30 In this design strategy, the hole in the porphyrin after the injection of an electron into the TiO2 conduction band migrated to the donor, minimizing recombination. GrÃ¤tzel and several other groups, including our group, have adopted the D-Ï€-A approach and a PCE of 13% was reported using a Co(III/II) redox couple.28 Further, device efficiencies using porphyrin sensitizers have been enhanced to 14.2% using either co-sensitization with an organic dye or using a tandem concept.31,32 These design strategies have provided a highly flexible platform in which the donor is generally an organic moiety having absorption in the UV region and connected to the meso-phenyl position of the porphyrin through an ethynyl bridge, porphyrin acts as a Ï€-spacer, and an anchoring group, either cyanoacrylic acid or rhodanine-3-carboxylic acid, is present in an auxiliary acceptor heteroaromatic structure based on benzothiadiazole (BTD), thiophene, etc. In addition to this, the molecular structure also has long alkoxy chains at the ortho position of the meso-phenyl ring which are necessary to enhance the solubility of the dye in common organic solvents, minimize the aggregation of porphyrin dye on the TiO2 surface, and diminish the unnecessary recombination of electrons at the TiO2 conduction band with oxidized sensitizers. GrÃ¤tzel et al. adopted a D-Ï€-A design strategy and reported the porphyrin sensitizer SM315, utilizing a bulky bis(2â€²,4â€²-bis(hexyloxy)-[1,1â€²-biphenyl]-4-yl)amine donor and 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole auxiliary acceptor, obtaining a record PCE of 13% using a Co(III/II) redox mediator.28 We introduced a phenothiazine donor through an ethynyl bridge to the meso position of a porphyrin with a thiophene-2-cyanoacrylic acid acceptor (LG5), obtaining a PCE of 10.20% using an Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox couple.26 Recently, we substituted the donor phenothiazine for a non-planar triphenyl imidazole and used a 4-(thiophen-2-yl)benzothiadiazole auxiliary acceptor with cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group (LG18), observing a PCE of 10.51%.25 In an extension of our work aimed at the development of the efficient D-Ï€-A approach using porphyrin sensitizers, we understand that the incorporation of electron-releasing groups into the donor triphenyl imidazole moiety may influence the photovoltaic performance. For this reason, we designed, synthesized, and characterized a series of porphyrin sensitizers having either one or three methoxy groups on the phenyl ring of the triphenyl imidazole moiety and using either 3-(5-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4-yl)thiophene-2-yl)-2-cyanoacrylic acid (LG24 and LG26) or 2-(5-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4-yl)thiophene-2-yl)methelene-4-oxo-2-thiathiazolidin-3-yl-acetic acid (LG25 and LG27) (Chart 1). All four sensitizers are characterized via various spectroscopic techniques and electrochemical methods, and we evaluated the photovoltaic performances of these dyes in DSSCs using a liquid Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ redox electrolyte, comparing the performance with the previously reported sensitizer LG18. Additionally, we used the best performing LG26 dye with the UV-dye Y1 in a co-sensitization system, showing a PCE of 10.45%.

In order to understand the stability of the sensitizers in an oxidized state, which is one of the essential factors determining the durability of a dye, we have performed spectroelectrochemical studies of the newly designed sensitizers. Fig. 2b demonstrates the representative example of the LG26 sensitizer under controlled potential oxidation at +0.88 V vs. SCE. Fig. 2b indicates that the absorption of the Q band at 700 nm steadily diminishes in intensity, the Soret band at 449 nm decreases in intensity with a bathochromic shift, and a new band is formed at 440 nm with an increase in intensity. In contrast, the absorption of the donor imidazole moiety undergoes a hypsochromic shift during controlled potential oxidation. Throughout this process, clear isosbestic points were observed at 445 nm, 560 nm, 640 nm, and 760 nm, which obviously indicate the oxidation forms of a single product. These observed transformations signify the formation of the porphyrin cation radical.39 The porphyrin cationic form does not fully recover to its neutral form when the applied potential is changed to +0.2 V. This might be due to the slight degradation of porphyrin due to the prolonged timescale of the spectroelectrochemical study, as was the case for other porphyrin sensitizers. However, under real-world conditions, the sensitizer stays in an oxidized form for a much shorter time and LG26 certainly shows greater oxidative stability during controlled potential oxidation than well-known Ru(II) polypyridyl complex based sensitizers.40 Similar spectral changes are observed for the LG24, LG25, and LG27 sensitizers (see Fig. S20, ESIâ€ ) and even the LG18 sensitizer.25

Quantum mechanical calculations

In order to gain a fundamental understanding of the structural, electronic, and optical properties of the LG24â€“LG27 dyes and compare their properties with the earlier reported LG18 sensitizer, we have conducted theoretical optimization studies, encompassing DFT and time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) with a basis functional set at the B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level. Fig. 3a illustrates the ground-state optimized structures of all four newly designed sensitizers, which comprise donor and acceptor moieties separated by a porphyrin macrocycle through an ethynyl Ï€-bridge. The phenyl rings of the donor triphenylimidazole with one or three methoxy substituents are present perpendicular to the porphyrin macrocycle, resulting in non-planarity being shown by the newly designed sensitizers, as suggested by the optimization studies. Additionally, the long alkyl dodecyloxy chains at the meso-phenyl positions of the porphyrin macrocycle cover the space above and below the porphyrin plane, minimizing the recombination of electrons in the TiO2 conduction band. Additionally, in all these sensitizers, the heteroaromatic thiophene group present between the BDT moiety and either cyanocarboxylic acid (LG24 and LG26) or rhodanine-3-carboxylic acid (LG25 and LG27) further results in non-planarity.

In Fig. 3b, the frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) were approximated using the B3LYP/6-31(d,p) method in THF solvent to investigate the HOMO and LUMO positions and other electronic properties of the sensitizers. These studies will highlight the effects of both the methoxy groups of the donor moiety and the Ï€-conjugated auxiliary acceptors on the HOMO and LUMO positions. In the case of the LG18 dye and the new dyes, the HOMO levels are delocalized on the triphenylimidazole donor and zinc porphyrin, while the LUMO levels are present on the auxiliary acceptors and anchoring groups, making intramolecular interactions easier. On the other hand, HOMOâˆ’1 was delocalized mainly on the donor part and partially on the porphyrin in the cases of LG24 and LG25, whereas in the cases of LG26 and LG27, the electron cloud was mainly distributed over the porphyrin macrocycle. The location of the electron cloud of LUMO+1 is mainly on the porphyrin and partially on the auxiliary acceptor groups in all these sensitizers. In the case of HOMOâˆ’2, this is delocalized mainly on the porphyrin for the LG24 and LG25 dyes and on the donor moiety in the cases of the LG26 and LG27 dyes; LUMO+2 is distributed mainly on the auxiliary acceptors and anchoring groups in the LG24 and LG26 sensitizers (Fig. S21, ESIâ€ ). The HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps of the present sensitizers in comparison with the LG18 sensitizer were not much affected either upon the introduction of methoxy groups to the triphenylimidazole donor moiety or upon a change in the auxiliary acceptor from cyanoacrylic acid to rhodanine-3-carboxylic acid. Fig. 3a shows the molecular electrostatic potential (MEP) maps of the sensitizers, and these suggest the existence of positive electrostatic potential at the donor and porphyrin macrocycle, whereas negative potential was generated at the auxiliary acceptor and anchoring group. In addition to this, we also executed TD-DFT studies in order to understand the excited-state transitions, as illustrated in Fig. S19 (ESIâ€ ). The acquired results were found to be in harmony with the experimental results (Table S1, ESIâ€ ).

Effects of TBP on efficiency

In addition to this, we have also studied the effects of the TBP concentration in the liquid redox electrolyte on the efficiency of the device. It is well documented in the literature that upon a change in the concentration of TBP in the redox couple, there is an adjustment in the conduction band edge of TiO2, which can affect the Voc of the overall DSSC.22,23 Fig. S22 (ESIâ€ ) demonstrates the IPCE spectra of all four sensitizers using 0.1 M, 0.3 M, and 0.5 M concentrations of TBP, and the accompanying data are accessible in Table 3. From Table 3, it is seen that the IPCE decreases for all four sensitizers as the concentration of TBP is increased. Similar negative effects from TBP are also seen in the Jâ€“V curves of all four sensitizers (Fig. S23, ESIâ€ ), and the data are summarized in Table 3. As the concentration of TBP increases from 0.1 M to 0.5 M, Voc is marginally enhanced while JSC is drastically reduced for all four sensitizers. Therefore, the most favorable concentration of TBP for the present porphyrin sensitizers is 0.1 M.

Although the LG26-based DSSC showed the highest PCE, there is still an absorption dip in the IPCE spectrum in the 320â€“400 nm region. The electron lifetime (Ï„) in a DSSC has been highlighted as a powerful tool for understanding the dynamics of charge recombination, which are associated with the open circuit voltage (Voc) of a DSSC.44â€“46 To comprehend the discrepancies in the Voc values observed from DSSCs based on LG24, LG25, LG26, LG27, Y1, and LG26 + Y1 dyes, the comparative conduction band positions and electron lifetimes of the respective DSSCs has been evaluated. Because of the very poor output signals, we have used a high laser intensity to record adequate transient absorbance signals. As reported by the GrÃ¤tzel group,48 this should not result in a considerable change in the lifetime decay. Effects of methoxy groups

The introduction of either one or three methoxy groups to the donor triphenylimidazole moiety enhances the HOMO orbital distributions on the donor moiety when compared to the LG18 sensitizer. Thus, charge recombination (Ï„w.o) may be suppressed in the sensitizers LG24 (4.74 Î¼s) and LG25 (3.07 Î¼s) containing three methoxy groups compared with the sensitizers LG26 (3.45 Î¼s) and LG27 (1.86 Î¼s) containing one methoxy group. Further, dye regeneration (Ï„w) was also influenced by the introduction of methoxy groups. Dye regeneration for all the presented sensitizers, LG24â€“LG27, is slow when compared to LG18 (0.28 Î¼s). On the whole, the introduction of methoxy groups to the donor moiety not only suppresses charge recombination but also the dye regeneration process.

Conclusions

We have introduced either one or three electron-releasing methoxy groups to the donor triphenyl imidazole moiety in D-Ï€-A-based porphyrin sensitizers to adjust the HOMOâ€“LUMO levels of the sensitizers for DSSC applications. The incorporation of the auxiliary acceptor rhodanine-3-acetic acid redshifted the Q band absorption, and the onset of absorption was increased to 800 nm compared with a corresponding thiophene auxiliary acceptor. DFT studies suggest that the HOMO extends over the donor and porphyrin groups while the LUMO spreads over the porphyrin and acceptor groups, which is favourable for DSSC applications. Device studies suggested that donors having one methoxy group showed better efficiency than the donors with three methoxy groups due to greater stabilization of the LUMO. Similarly, the sensitizer having a thiophene cyanoacrylic acid acceptor group (LG26) showed better device performance (PCE: 9.87%) than the corresponding sensitizer having a rhodanine-3-acetic acid acceptor group (LG27; PCE: 5.94%) because of the non-conjugated structure between the rhodanine ring and the carboxylic anchoring group. Under similar test cell conditions, LG18 showed a PCE of 10.51%. Additionally, the LG26 sensitizer was co-sensitized with a metal-free organic dye (Y1) having absorption in the UV region, and the resulting device had complementary absorption; this was reflected in the IPCE spectrum being particularly enhanced in the UV region and, as a result, the Jsc, Voc, and PCE values were improved to 21.25 mA cmâˆ’2, 698 mV, and 10.45% in the LG26 + Y1 device, with a PCE greater than individual devices made using LG26 (9.87%) and Y1 (3.44%). Intensity modulated photovoltage spectroscopy and nanosecond transient spectroscopy were used to account for the low efficiencies of the devices compared to a device based on the LG18 dye. Finally, thermal studies suggested that these sensitizers are stable up to 200 Â°C and have potential for roof-top applications.
