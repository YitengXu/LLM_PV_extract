Abstract

Zincâ€“polyiodide redox flow battery (RFB) systems with highly soluble triiodide/iodide (I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’) couples have demonstrated significantly enhanced energy densities. However, their low power density has limited their broad application. In this work, MoS2 nanoplates were in situ grafted onto graphite felt (GF) surfaces to accelerate the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ redox reaction. The hexagonal phase MoS2 with enlarged interlayer spacing can facilitate the diffusion of the electrolyte, and the defects on the MoS2 nanoplates can serve as very effective catalytic active sites. Cyclic voltammetry testing revealed the high catalytic performance of MoS2 in the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ redox reaction. A non-flow cell with the MoS2-modified GF serving as a positive electrode showed a higher energy efficiency than that of pristine GF; increases of about 10.2% and 10.9% at current densities of 30 mA cmâˆ’2 and 40 mA cmâˆ’2 were obtained with the optimized electrode, respectively.

1. Introduction

With the growing worries about environmental pollution caused by the combustion of finite fossil fuels, the development of environmentally benign energy sources is gaining more and more attention.1,2 However, renewable energy sources such as wind and solar are inherently intermittent and generally dispersed from each other, which make it difficult for their extensive penetration into the grid.3 Large-scale electrochemical energy storage (EES) devices can play a significant role in improving the stability and power quality of the grid by smoothing fluctuating electricity patterns (load leveling and peak shaving).4 Among various EES devices, redox flow batteries (RFBs) have long been considered as a promising candidate for electrochemical energy storage devices because of their excellent safety, long cycle life and flexibility in energy and power design.5â€“7

Since it was first introduced in the 1980s by Skyllasâ€“Kazacos et al., the aqueous all-vanadium flow battery (VRB, pure sulfuric acid based) has been the most investigated flow battery so far.8 However, the state-of-the-art energy density of the aqueous VRB has reached only 25 W h Lâˆ’1Catholyte+Anolyte, which is only 1/10 of that of Li-ion batteries.9 Such low energy density as well as corrosivity of the strong acid increase the system cost, preventing its further penetration into the market. Depending on solvent choice, non-aqueous RFBs can have a wide electrochemical stable window and operating temperature range. Numerous high-energy-density non-aqueous RFBs have been demonstrated.10â€“12 Nevertheless, the poor power densities, high cost of the electrolyte and complex packaging process of non-aqueous RFBs are still the main obstacles for their practical applications in the future. Due to the low potential (âˆ’0.762 V versus the standard hydrogen electrode), high abundance, low toxicity and intrinsic safety character, metallic zinc (Zn) is regarded as an ideal anode material for aqueous hybrid flow batteries.13 Various redox couples from inorganic to organic were explored as cathode materials for zinc hybrid flow batteries.14â€“18 Since it was first proposed in 2015, the zincâ€“polyiodide redox flow battery (ZIB) with a high energy density and high safety has been widely investigated.19â€“23 However, the relatively low energy efficiency at high current density means that a larger cell stack is required for identical energy output, which eventually results in increased cost. High electrode polarization resistance is believed to be one cause of the low energy efficiency. Such resistance is primarily due to the poor kinetic reversibility of the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ redox reaction on GF surfaces. In 2016, Li et al. reported two nanoporous metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs), MIL-125-NH2 and UiO-66-CH3, as electrocatalysts to accelerate the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ redox reaction.20 The MIL-125-NH2-modified GF electrode showed a higher energy efficiency of 6.4% than the pristine GF at the current density of 30 mA cmâˆ’2, but suffered from poor chemical stability.

In fact, similar research on iodide/triiodide dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) has been extensively reported. Various metal sulfides, oxides, carbides, and nitrides and carbon-based materials have been investigated as alternative catalysts to replace the expensive Pt.24 Because of its special structures and electronic properties, molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) and MoS2-nanostructured carbon composites with different morphologies have been investigated and proven to produce equivalent or even higher energy conversion efficiencies than that of Pt.25,26 It is believed that the catalytic activity of MoS2 is highly dependent on the exposed edges and unsaturated sulfur atoms on the surface of MoS2 materials. Herein, MoS2 nanoplates were synthesized and in situ grafted onto the GF surface through a solvothermal method.27 The as prepared MoS2 nanoplates have an enlarged interlayer spacing and a low crystallinity. Such characters offer several advantages. Firstly, interlayer spacing is beneficial for the diffusion of the electrolyte. Secondly, the relatively low crystallinity leads to more defects on the MoS2 nanoplates, providing catalytic active sites. Finally, the direct growth of the MoS2 nanoplates on the GF surface is very facile and uniform. First-principles calculations were also carried out in this work to reveal the effective catalytic active sites in the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ reaction.

To further verify the catalytic effects of MoS2 on the electrochemical performance of the ZIB, a non-flow cell was assembled identically with Nafion 115 as a membrane separator and titanium wire as a current collector, and the charge/discharge cycling performance was tested in 2.0 M ZnI2 electrolyte. Because this work focuses on the study of electrodes used in ZIBs, a zinc plate was used as the negative electrode to eliminate the influence of zinc deposition and extraction. Fig. 3a shows the voltage profiles of the GF and three MoS2@GF electrodes at a current rate of 30 mA cmâˆ’2. Compared to the GF electrode, the MoS2@GF electrodes show a remarkably decreased overpotential in both the charge and discharge processes, which could be ascribed to the effect of MoS2 with many active sites for the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ couple, resulting in a lower charge and higher discharge voltage. All the cells exhibited a high CE of âˆ¼99% during cycling (Fig. S12â€ ). The UV-Vis spectra of the anode and cathode electrolyte samples after charging also show that I3âˆ’ only appeared on the cathode side (Fig. S13â€ ), which suggests minor crossover of the active species. As the concentration of (NH4)2MoS4 in the precursor solutions increases, the amount of catalyst increases accordingly on the GF electrode, and the energy efficiency (EE) value first increases and then decreases (Fig. 3b), with the highest EE value of 74.3% at 20 mg of (NH4)2MoS4 in the precursor solutions, which is around 10.2% higher than the one without catalyst. Even at a high current density of 40 mA cmâˆ’2, the MoS2-modified GF electrodes show a remarkably decreased overpotential of about 77.8 and 111.6 mV in both the charge and discharge processes (Fig. 3c), which has never been reported in such ZIB systems.19â€“21Fig. 3d shows the cycling performance of a ZIB with a 20 mg MoS2@GF electrode at a current density of 40 mA cmâˆ’2. Nevertheless, long term cycling performance was restricted by the unstable deposition/abstraction of the zinc electrode. Further study can be focused on the optimization of the zinc anode.

Quantum chemistry calculations were also carried out to verify the origin of the electrocatalytic activity of the MoS2 catalyst in the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ reaction. According to previous research,30 the adsorption energy of the I atom (EIad) on the surface of the catalyst is a key parameter affecting the catalytic performance. A small EIad cannot dissociate I2 molecules into I atoms but a large EIad means that the desorption of the I atom into Iâˆ’ (sol) would be thermodynamically difficult to proceed, and thus the overall activity is restricted. So, a suitable EIad should lie within the range of 0.33â€“1.20 eV.31 In this article, three possible catalytic sites, namely a sulfur vacancy (S vacancy), sulfur edge (S edge) and molybdenum edge (Mo edge), were chosen to calculate the EIad. Fig. 4 shows the quantum chemistry calculation results of the selected sites. One can see from the adsorption configuration that I2 molecules can dissociate directly and form I atoms at the three interfaces. The EIad values at the S vacancy, S edge and Mo edge were calculated to be 0.79 eV, 2.54 eV and 4.97 eV, respectively. Based on the value of EIad, the S vacancy seems to be a very effective catalytic active site for the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ reaction. Combined with the TEM and XRD analysis results, the catalytic performance of the MoS2 nanoplates is due to the following reasons: (i) defects (such as the S vacancy) on the MoS2 nanoplates are very effective catalytic active sites; (ii) the active S edge and S ligand can be introduced into the plate-like MoS2 structure, providing more active sites than simple MoS2; and (iii) the vertically aligned MoS2 nanoplates on the GF surface can facilitate electrolyte diffusion and provide more effective catalytic sites for the I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ reaction.

3. Conclusions

In summary, MoS2 nanoplates, which were synthesized by a solvothermal method and in situ grafted onto GF surfaces, can act as a powerful electrocatalyst for I3âˆ’/Iâˆ’ redox couples to enhance the electrochemical activity of the GF electrode in ZIB systems. The improved performance of the ZIB is ascribed to the enhanced electrochemical activity after MoS2 modification. The rate constants of both the oxidation and reduction reactions of the triiodide/iodide couples are increased. With regard to the non-flow cell, the MoS2-modified GF electrode demonstrates excellent performance and stability, and the 20 mg MoS2@GF electrode exhibited the highest energy efficiency. At 30 mA cmâˆ’2, the EE of the ZIB with the 20 mg MoS2@GF electrode is stable at 74.3%, which is 10.2% higher than that using GF. Moreover, even at an especially high current density of 40 mA cmâˆ’2, the EE of the ZIB with the 20 mg MoS2@GF electrode can still achieve 67.9%. These results suggest that the MoS2 nanoplate modified GF can offer great promise as a high performance electrode for ZIB applications.
