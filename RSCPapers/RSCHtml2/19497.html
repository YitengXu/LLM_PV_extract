Chemical formation of soft metal electrodes for flexible and wearable electronics

Abstract

Flexible and wearable electronics is one major technology after smartphones. It shows remarkable application potential in displays and informatics, robotics, sports, energy harvesting and storage, and medicine. As an indispensable part and the cornerstone of these devices, soft metal electrodes (SMEs) are of great significance. Compared with conventional physical processes such as vacuum thermal deposition and sputtering, chemical approaches for preparing SMEs show significant advantages in terms of scalability, low-cost, and compatibility with the soft materials and substrates used for the devices. This review article provides a detailed overview on how to chemically fabricate SMEs, including the material preparation, fabrication technologies, methods to characterize their key properties, and representative studies on different wearable applications.

1. Introduction

Both the industrial and academic communities have shown enormous enthusiasm for flexible and wearable electronics in the past decade.112 Indeed, nowadays any new proof-of-concept report of prototype flexible/wearable devices, such as deformable smartphones and wearable sensors, can always attract immediate attention in the social media. As shown in Fig. 1, academic publications with regard to flexible and wearable electronics are also undergoing exponential growth. In contrast to their rigid counterparts, electronic circuits and devices constructed on flexible substrates provide substantial advantages in terms of light weight, mechanical flexibility and durability to withstand multiple deformations (stretching, compression, bending, twisting). The carefully designed flexible electronics also offer specific benefits of being conformable to non-planar structures, comfortable to wear, and easy to integrate with other systems and objects. Without a doubt, the emerging flexible wearable electronics will redefine the paradigms of electronic products in the near future.
Fig. 1 Number of publications sorted by year with the key words wearable electronics and flexible electronics indexed in the Web of Science in Dec. 2017.

On the way toward practical applications of these exciting devices and systems, one of the most significant obstacles is the lack of high-performance flexible electrodes, which are indispensable for all electronic devices. Ideal electrodes for flexible and wearable electronics require good integration of electrical and mechanical properties, and sometimes optical properties. Electrically conductive materials, including metals, carbon materials, and conducting polymers, have been widely examined as candidates to fabricate flexible electrodes.13 After decades of research, it is getting clear that metal-based electrodes are a more reasonable choice owing to their intrinsically high electrical conductivity, stability, cost-effectiveness and better understanding of device optimization. However, the relatively high-density and rigid characteristics of bulk metals determine that thick metal foils cannot be used as ideal flexible electrodes. Therefore, metal electrodes, in which conductive metal thin films are physically or chemically deposited on flexible plastic or elastomer substrates in specific structures, are advocated.1416

To achieve metal electrodes with superior flexibility, many structural designs and optimizations have been established over the past decade. Common metals can only sustain limited tensile strains (<1%). However, ultrathin metal can withstand harsh bending conditions without deformation because the bending strain proportionally decreases with thickness.17 For example, bending a 100 nm thin film with a radius of curvature of 1 cm only yields a tensile strain of 0.1% on the materials, which is far below the fracture limit of metals. In light of this, highly flexible and stretchable soft metal electrodes (SMEs) can be readily obtained by combining suitable bendable structures with particular layouts that enable stress absorption. The efficient layouts include wavy structures, buckling, serpentine patterns, coiled springs and others.4,14 These SMEs have led to the development of a wide variety of flexible and wearable electronic devices ranging from flexible circuit boards, sensors and actuators, to displays and energy conversion/storage devices.

Early works on SMEs are based on conventional microfabrication which consists of evaporation of metal layers, resist deposition, lithography and pattern development, and post-etching. This is mostly due to the direct migration from Si technology that has been long-used in the industry. However, such a hard fabrication not only suffers from a high production cost per area due to the use of expensive equipment and sophisticated treatment procedures, but also may damage the soft substrate due to the use of strong solvents, high temperature or high vacumm.3,1821 In contrast, solution-based wet chemical methods including a variety of printing and plating techniques allow rapid, low-temperature and cost-effective fabrication of SMEs over large areas.

This review aims at providing a broad overview of the recent progress in the chemical formation of SMEs toward advanced flexible and wearable electronics. In order to give a better understanding of the desirable properties of SMEs, an overview of the characteristics of the often-used metal and substrate materials is firstly discussed. The methods to measure these properties are also briefly provided. We then review the chemical approaches to fabricate SMEs in detail, including the chemical mechanisms to prepare the materials, the processing strategies including coating, printing and spinning technologies to fabricate the patterns and thin films, and the post-treatments necessary to achieve good electrode properties. Subsequently, some representative examples of the applications of chemically formed SMEs in wearable electronic circuits and devices are presented. Finally, a summary of the progress and a perspective comment on the future development of the field are provided.

2. Characteristics of soft metal electrodes

2.1 Choice of metal

In the literature, the most frequently reported metal materials for chemical formation of soft electrodes include gold (Au), silver (Ag), copper (Cu) and Nickel (Ni). An ideal metal electrode should possess high electrical conductivity () and should be low-cost and easy to synthesize. In terms of direct current (dc) electrical conductivity (dc), bulk Ag exhibits the highest value of 6.30 107 S m1, followed by Cu (5.96 107 S m1), and Au (4.10 107 S m1). However, by taking the cost into account, noble metals (Au, Ag, Pt, and Pd) show extremely low conductivity/price ratios (as shown in Table 1). Cu, a typical transition metal, shows a considerably high dc which is only 6% lower than that of Ag but possesses an excellent conductivity/cost ratio. However, the spontaneous surface oxidation of Cu in air introduces significant challenges for its practical application. Ni, another important transition metal, shows a similar conductivity/price ratio but much better resistance to oxidation than Cu. Therefore, Ni is emerging as a very promising metal for SME fabrications, especially for electrochemical energy storage devices.22,23 Currently, Ag is the dominant material that has been utilized to fabricate soft metal electrodes, though its cost performance is only ca. 1% of that of Cu. Other noble metals, carbon materials, and conducting polymers are prohibitively expensive, and are not suitable for mass production of flexible electrodes.

2.2 Choice of substrate

Many polymer thin films have been developed as substrates of soft electrodes and electronic devices because they are low-cost and readily processable. To date, the widely utilized polymer films in bendable electronics include polyethylene terephthalate (PET), polyethylene naphthalate (PEN), polyimide (PI), and polycarbonate (PC). Elastomers such as polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) and polyurethane (PU) can serve as suitable substrates for stretchable electronics. Currently the flexible electronic devices are often fabricated on substrates of PI, PET, or PEN with a typical thickness of 100200 m. Some PI substrates with a low thickness of 251 or 12.53 m have also been employed to endow the devices with better flexibility (smaller bending radii). Polymer foils with extremely low thicknesses of 13 m were also reported to fabricate ultrathin, ultraflexible devices.18,24 The key physical properties of typical polymer substrates for fabricating SMEs are summarized in Table 2. It should be noted that, with specific consideration of the compatibility between these physical properties of the polymer substrates and the practical requirements in different SME manufacturing operations, there are actually several key challenges to be overcome. Though the developments of polymer substrates for advanced flexible electronics are beyond the scope of this review, we would like to highlight these key challenges as they are fundamental guidelines in designing the chemical approaches for SMEs.

First, the relatively low thermal stability of polymers severely limits the processing temperature of the electrode fabrication. For example, PET, the most common substrate for flexible electronics, allows a maximum processing temperature of only 140 C25 above which the polymer chains begin to slip and the film becomes mechanically unstable. PI can withstand a processing temperature as high as 350 C; however, its yellow color partially reduces this advantage for optoelectronic applications. In addition, polymers exhibit a higher coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) than metals and inorganic materials. When polymers undergo thermal stresses, the mismatch between the CTE of the polymer substrate and the functional materials deposited on top of it will result in cracks or delamination.26

Second, the high permeability of polymer substrates gives rise to substantial difficulty for the long-term stability of flexible electronics, especially for those containing oxygen or moisture sensitive materials. For example, organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) require the permeation rate for oxygen and water vapor to be less than 105 mL m2 day1 and 106 mL m2 day1, respectively. The requirement of electronics with extremely low penetration rates poses a serious challenge to the polymer substrates which generally exhibit a gas barrier performance in the range of 100102 mL m2 day1.27 Therefore, additional encapsulation or surface treatment such as atomic layer deposition (ALD) may be needed.

Third, the surface quality of the polymer substrates can be hardly compared to Si wafers (0.32 nm RMS) commonly used in the electronic industry. The relatively high surface roughness will cause pinholes of materials deposited on it, and will lead to failing devices and low yield. Further planarization of the polymer substrates is very much required for ultrathin electronic devices such as thin-film transistors (TFTs).3 Furthermore, it is also necessary that the polymeric substrates should be chemically inert to solvents used in the solution-based processing so that the surface will not be damaged.

2.3.2 Mechanical flexibility and robustness. Apart from the electrical properties, it is quite necessary to quantitatively measure the mechanical flexibility and robustness of the SMEs. To date, there is still no measurement standard to evaluate such performance in this area. Generally, bending tests are used to characterize the bendability of SMEs by monitoring the change of Rs or bulk resistance during repeated bending cycles. Similarly, the stretchability/compressibility of SMEs are characterized by testing the change of normalized resistance under different tensile/compression states (Fig. 2c). Apparently, an intrinsically low Rs along with stable Rs under considerable mechanical strains and a long lifespan are highly useful in practical applications of SMEs. In the literature, the mechanical robustness of SMEs is commonly examined by cyclic deformation tests for thousands of cycles. Some reports also demonstrated the mechanical robustness of SMEs in a simulated wearable environment which couples the multiple dimensional mechanical deformations with changes in temperature and moisture. Nevertheless, the excellent mechanical flexibility and robustness of SMEs rely on the interaction between the polymeric substrate and the metal layer atop. Thus, the characterization toward the metal/substrate interfacial adhesion is also important to fully understand the mechanical properties of SMEs. To this end, standard peeling strength tests are often utilized to characterize the adhesion energy of the chemically formed metals on substrates. The most typical peeling test in a laboratory uses 3 M tape (namely the taping method) to repetitively tape and peel away from the metal electrodes (Fig. 2d). 3. Chemical approaches to soft metal electrodes

SMEs play an important role in a wide variety of flexible and wearable electronic devices. However, the technical requirements for the SMEs for every specific electronic device are different from each other. For different end uses, the considerations of the fabrication processes and optimization methods are quite diverse. Therefore, it is a challenging task to elaborate all the aspects in the chemical formation of SMEs. Instead, in this section we mainly focus on the chemical strategy for the chemical formation of SMEs. According to different end uses, the same chemical strategy can be applied, together with additional lithography or processing, to yield different electrode properties. For example, the same nanomaterials can be used to fabricate opaque or transparent electrodes with tunable optical transmittance and electrical resistance based on the applied amount and pattern structure etc. Strategies for the fabrication of SMEs via chemical approaches can be classified into two major categories (as shown in Fig. 3): (1) additive manufacturing of metal patterns on polymeric substrates by using metallic building blocks, and (2) in situ growth of metal patterns through surface chemical reactions. This section will discuss the various chemical approaches in detail.

3.1 Additive chemical manufacturing on polymeric substrates

In this strategy, the metallic building blocks including metal particles and metal precursors are synthesized first. These building blocks are then deposited by coating, printing, or spinning onto the flexible substrates. A post-treatment to reduce and to sinter the materials is necessary to enable the electrical conductivity of the electrode. These three major steps are discussed below in detail.
3.1.1 Metallic building blocks.
Zero-dimensional (0D) materials. Central to the additive manufacturing of SMEs is the design of metallic building blocks in which metal-based materials are mixed with solvent, stabilizers, and other additives. Most common types of metallic building blocks are metal micro and nanoparticles and metal precursors. Traditionally, commercial metal (Ag, Au) pastes composed of metal particles and adhesive polymers have been widely employed to fabricate metal electrodes through printing.1,4246 Recently, solution-phase synthesized metal nanoparticles (NPs) with tunable size, morphology, and other physical properties have been widely explored as starting materials to prepare SMEs. For example as shown in Fig. 4a, AgNPs can be synthesized by reducing inorganic Ag-containing salt AgNO3 with various reduction agents including NaBH4,47 triethanolamine,48L-ascorbic acid,49 and D-glucose.50 Ag NPs with a diameter under 100 nm and a narrow size distribution can be well synthesized by controlling the metal salt concentrations, temperature, and surface stabilizing molecules. Other metal NPs including AuNPs, and CuNPs can also be prepared in a similar way by reducing metal salts with external reducing agents. There are several comprehensive reviews concerning the synthesis details, control parameters, and formation mechanisms of AgNPs,51,52 AuNPs,53,54 and CuNPs.55 The percolating networks of these 0D metal particles have been proved to be highly electrically conductive and mechanically flexible.56

One-dimensional (1D) materials. SMEs constructed with one-dimensional 1D metallic building blocks have emerged as more promising materials in recent years (Fig. 4be).2,8,57,58 According to the percolation theory, 1D nanomaterials such as nanorods (NDs) and nanowires (NWs) can form percolating networks with much lower thresholds than 0D nanoparticles.59,60 That is, utilizing 1D metal nanomaterials allows one to prepare conductive electrodes with less amount of metal. Therefore, particular interest has been paid to developing Ag or Cu NWs as building blocks for the chemical formation of SMEs.2,57,61 The metal NWs can be synthesized through two mainstream approaches: a hard template assisted synthesis and a soft template assisted synthesis. For the hard template assisted method, porous materials including anodic aluminum oxide (AAO), mesoporous silica, and carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have been widely used as the template to prepare metal NWs in the pores.62,63 However, the complex procedures and the limited production yield severely limit this method. In contrast, the wet chemical synthesis of metal NWs using soft templates such as surface stabilizing molecules and polymers facilitates a scalable production. Perhaps the most successful soft template method for AgNW synthesis, until now, is the polyol method using poly(vinyl pyrrolidone) (PVP) as the capping agent.64,65 In a typical synthesis, ethylene glycol (EG) is utilized as both solvent and reducing agent, AgNO3 is used as the Ag ion precursor, and PVP acts as the soft template to direct the anisotropic assembly of Ag atoms into NWs. By carefully controlling the quantity of reagents, temperature, and reaction time, gram-scale AgNWs with a diameter in the range of 20100 nm and an aspect ratio of 1002000 can be easily obtained.63 To further improve the aspect ratio of AgNWs, Lee and coworkers recently developed a successive multistep polyol method, in which previous generation AgNWs were repeatedly concentrated and introduced into fresh EG solutions containing a suitable amount of AgNO3 and PVP.66,67 By carefully tuning the conditions, the average length of AgNWs can be increased step-by-step. Ultralong AgNWs with a length over 500 m (aspect ratio >3000) can be achieved after 7 generations of growth. Some groups have also been devoted to developing SMEs by using ultrathin AuNWs, with diameters as low as 2 nm, as the building blocks.6870 The AuNWs are mainly prepared via solution chemistries, in which complexes of HAuCl4 are highly efficiently reduced in the presence of hexadecyl-trimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) or oleylamine as the capping agent.7174 The as-prepared ultrathin AuNWs show high aspect ratios (>1000) that are very promising for construting percolative pathways with low thresholds (Fig. 5a and b).

Recently, CuNWs have come under increased attention as a potential substituent for expensive AuNWs and AgNWs due to their extremely low cost (100 times less expensive than Ag) and relatively high conductivity (only 4% less conductive than Ag). Scalable and low-cost solution synthesis approaches have also been invented to prepare CuNWs. The general chemistry is similar to that of AgNW synthesis, in which Cu ions/complexes are reduced by efficient reducing agents such as hydrazine,7678 glucose,7981 silanes,82 or benzoin radicals,83 or by catalyst-assisted methods28,84,85 in the presence of suitable capping molecules. Alkyl amines including ethylenediamine,76,86 hexadecylamine79 and oleylamine84 have been demonstrated as suitable capping agents to produce CuNWs with diameters of 1560 nm and a length in the range of 10100 m. However, the well-known intrinsic weakness of Cu, rapid surface oxidation under ambient conditions, brings a subtaintial challenge for the practical application of CuNWs. Passivating the surface of CuNWs by introducing dense and conductive graphene,8789 metal oxide,80 Au,90 or Ni28 shells makes them more stable when exposed to air. However, such passivation strategies increase the operation procedures and the production cost, deteriorating their advantages in SME fabrications.

Apart from the wet chemical synthesis, there are also some innovative methods to prepare high quality 1D metal materials. For example, Wu's group proposed a blown spinning approach to prepare Ag nanofibers (NFs) with aspect ratios higher than 10000 (Fig. 5c and d).91,92 The AgNFs were fabricated through a gas-assistant blown spinning of AgNO3/PVP precursor fibers and subsequent calcination91 or UV irradiation.92 During the blown spinning, the solution was quickly solidified into fibers by the shearing force when passing the nozzle. Then in situ reduction of AgNO3 into Ag metal and decomposition of PVP were simultaneously carried out under thermal annealing at 280 C or UV irradiation, resulting in highly conductive AgNF networks with a diameter of 2001000 nm and a length over 2 mm.

AgNFs with high aspect ratios can also be synthesized through electrostatic spinning.93 Very recently, Ji et al. developed a novel fabrication strategy to prepare metallic nanofibers (NFs) with controllable aspect ratios (Fig. 5eg).94 They electrospun a AgNP suspension on a PVP-coated PDMS substrate and heated the resultant materials at 180 C in air to sinter the AgNPs and form highly aligned, ultralong AgNFs with an average diameter of 300 nm. Then AgNFs with aspect ratios ranging from 167 to 700 were achieved by uniaxially stretching the PDMS substrate under various strain rates. The cracking of the brittle PVP film during stretching caused the fracture of the ultralong AgNFs into shorter ones. The resultant AgNFs can be redispersed to form inks by soaking the samples in water and dissolving the PVP. Remarkably, the 1D AgNFs fabricated through this method possess much lower length distribution (deviation 10%) than those prepared by wet chemical systhesis methods.94


Metal precursors. Apart from the direct construction of SMEs based on metallic 0D and 1D nanoparticles, there are also some reports using metal-containing precursor materials as the building block to achieve metal electrodes through a post-fabrication transistion process. This approach is also known as metal organic decomposition (MOD), in which solutions of metal organic compounds patterned on substrates are decomposed into metal under suitable conditions. Recently, tremendous interest has been evoked to develop Ag and Cu-based MOD solutions mainly due to the more controllable properties and easier operation of these solutions than the dispersions of metal particles. Ag-Based solutions such as silver carboxylate in xylene,95 silver acetate in aqueous ammonium hydroxide and formic acid,96 and silver neodecanoate in aqueous ethyl cellulose,97 have been examined as efficient precursors to offer SMEs with high dc even equal to the bulk Ag after treatment by heating96,98 or light exposure.97 Similarly, Cu-based MOD solutions composed of Cu complexes and solvents have also been widely investigated to prepare SMEs through thermal/optical post-treaments.99104 However, the as-fabricted Cu electrodes commonly showed a dc nearly one order of magnitude lower than that of bulk Cu, which might be caused by the rapid surface oxidation of Cu.
3.1.2 Chemical fabrication processes of SMEs. With the advancement of various solution-based fabrication technologies, it is very convenient to construct SMEs on flexible substrates with solution-processable metal nanomaterials or metal-containing precursors. According to the working principle, the fabrication strategies can be roughly divided into three categories, namely, printing, coating, and spinning. The printing technique is able to make a predesigned pattern directly, while coating and spinning often should be combined with other processing approaches to achieve patterned electrodes. In the following, we will introduce these three categories in the aspects of working principle, application scenario and advantage/disadvantage in processing SMEs specifically by using chemically synthesized metal micro/nanomaterials as building blocks. For conciseness, fabrication of metal electrodes on hard substrates and the fabrication of other components of electronic or optoelectronic devices using these will not be considered.
Printing-based processing. Printing is a sort of low-temperature processing technique, which allows for the deposition of patterned films on diverse substrates (Fig. 6). Specifically, such a solution-based and low-temperature technique meets the demand for fast development of soft electronics. The printing techniques that have been applied in the fabrication of SMEs mainly include inkjet printing,105107 screen printing108110 and nanoimprinting lithography (NIL).111113 In a typical printing process of SMEs, metal pastes or inks are deposited on soft substrates, and patterned by a stencil (stencil printing and screen printing), nozzle movement controlled by software (inkjet printing), or pre-processed nanopatterns on the substrate (NIL). The pastes and inks used in a printing process can be both suspensions of metal or metal oxide micro/nanomaterials and solutions of metal salts. The metal micro/nanomaterials include Ag/Cu/Au/Ni or alloy micro/nano-particles114118 and metal nanowires.119 The metal oxide nanoparticles for printing include CuO nanoparticles and NiO nanoparticles.117,120 For printed metal electrodes using a solution of metal salt, only noble metals have been reported, mainly because of their highly reducible nature.121123 It was reported that the addition of heterogeneous metal or metal oxide particles, such as Zn particles and ZnO particles, in a metal paste or ink can significantly improve the performance of the final devices.108,124 The substrate used for printing processing of soft metal electrodes can be a polymer film,125127 fabric,128130 or paper,131133etc. Polymer films are usually used for the fabrication of photoelectric devices, such as solar cells and OLEDs, which require optical transparency and a smooth surface. Fabric and paper can be used in the fabrication of wearable electric circuits and sensors, etc.

Screen printing is a well industrialized technique. In a typical manual screen printing process, a mesh is used to transfer ink onto a substrate, except in areas impermeable to the ink by a blocking stencil. A blade or squeegee is moved across the screen to fill the open mesh apertures with ink, and a reverse stroke then causes the screen to touch the substrate momentarily along a line of contact (Fig. 6). This causes the ink to wet the substrate and the desired pattern will be transferred onto the substrate. Screen printing is characterized by high processing efficiency, low cost, and suitability for continuous roll-to-roll (R2R) fabrication. The metal inks used in screen printing should have relatively high viscosity, in order to avoid uncontrolled leakage of inks from the screen. Various kinds of commercial metal inks are available for screen printing, of which the silver inks predominate overwhelmingly. The resolution of the patterns processed by screen printing usually drops in the range of tens of micrometers, which is only slightly better than those fabricated through direct writing approaches (as shown in Fig. 7eg), making this technique not suitable for processing high-resolution patterns. Because of the high viscosity of the inks, screen printed metal electrodes usually have relatively big thickness, which is not beneficial for flexible devices. Soft metal electrodes prepared by screen printing usually aimed at the applications of solar cells (as a back contact),134138 lighting sources,139141 thin film transistors,142146 flexible or stretchable antenna,147151 soft sensors,119,152,153 and electric circuits,129,154,155etc.Ref. 155 and 156 give a more comprehensive review of the applications of screen printed electrodes. Recently, a stretchable metal electrode processed by screen printing has been reported.139

Compared with screen printing, inkjet printing is not so efficient, but its processing resolution can be dramatically enhanced by diminishing the size of the printhead, optimizing the jetting mode to reduce the drop size, and modifying the surface nature of the substrates to inhibit the diffusion of the ink.157 The smallest feature size of the reported SMEs with inkjet printing technology has reached 510 m. Inkjet printing is a non-contact, drop-on-demand type of printing technique, which outweighs screen printing in avoiding the contamination to any pre-made pattern and the substrate and is very material-saving. Inkjet printing requires careful control of the rheological property, drying speed, and wetting property of the metal inks to the substrate to avoid the clogging of the printhead and improve the resolution of the patterns.158 Metal inks with low viscosity, high fluidity, high colloidal stability, and small size of the metal particles are beneficial for practical use. The inks used by inkjet printing can be a suspension of metal nanoparticles,114116,124,159163 metal nanowires,121,164,165 metal oxide nanoparticles,120,166 organic/inorganic metal precursors122,167170 and mixtures of metal nanoparticles and metal precursors.121 For example, Fig. 7d shows an inkjet-printed Au pattern with a feature size of 62 m by using an AuNP solution as the ink. The reduction of the metal particle size can significantly bring down the postsintering temperature of the metal patterns.114,159 Organic ligands are added to the ink to stabilize the metal nanoparticles and tune the rheological property of the inks. Various kinds of metal electrodes, such as silver,107,122,171 copper,116,166,172 gold,115,161,173 nickel117 and alloys118,124,163 have been prepared by inkjet printing on various kinds of flexible or stretchable substrates, such as PET,120,160,164 PEN,174176 PC,177,178 PI179181 and rubber.171,182,183 Very recently, a surface-embedded stretchable metal electrode has been fabricated by direct inkjet printing.182

NIL is a technique for fabrication of patterns with nanometer-scale feature size. It creates patterns by mechanical deformation of the imprint resist and subsequent processes. The imprint resist is typically a monomer or polymer formulation that is cured by heat or UV light during the imprinting. Adhesion between the resist and the template is controlled to allow proper release. For preparation of metal patterns using NIL, usually post metal deposition on patterned substrates is employed. The metal deposition methods include direct metal evaporation,184186 and site-selective casting of the metal nanoparticle ink and post sintering.70,112,187 When metal inks with proper rheological and drying properties are used, metal patterns of nanosized features can also be processed directly without post metal deposition.188 The fast development of soft photoelectric devices, for example flexible solar cells, flexible photo detectors, and flexible lighting sources, has boosted the demand for high-performance transparent electrodes with high transmittance, high flexibility and low Rs. The NIL has been established as a promising technique for processing such high-quality transparent electrodes (Fig. 7b).189 Some recent works have demonstrated the feasibility of using metal ink in NIL processing of large-area flexible transparent electrodes for industrial applications.190192 In a typical process, an interconnected nano-trench is firstly patterned on the polymer substrate by nanoimprinting. Then the ink of metal nanoparticles is pasted over the substrate, and surplus ink is scraped away to make a clean surface with the trenches filled by metal ink. After post treating, for example annealing and wet etching, the transparent electrode can be obtained. The flexible transparent electrode with the best performance prepared by NIL demonstrated a Rs of 8.7 sq1 at the transmittance of 88%.187 In addition, metal grids by NIL can be embedded in a flexible substrate, thus rendering a smooth surface and improved adhesion to the substrate.41,112 Such property is very useful for improving the performance of many devices, especially for solar cells and OLEDs.112,185 Nano-imprinting provides the highest pattern resolution of the metal electrode, and various high-performance soft metal electrodes have been successfully prepared on different soft substrates.70,111,184 However, NIL lacks high enough processing efficiency and is difficult to manipulate. This is the bottleneck that hampers the industrialization of NIL for fabrication of soft metal electrodes.


Coating-based processing. Coating processing techniques include spin coating,193195 spray coating,196198 doctor-blade or Meyer-rod coating,199201 dip coating,202,203etc. (Fig. 8). Spin coating is a frequently used lab-scale technique enabling the preparation of metal films with a thickness down to several nanometers. Scalable deposition of large-area metal films using spin coating is not feasible. In addition, it requires high surface smoothness and a certain degree of stiffness of the substrate, which may limit its application for soft electrodes. Spray coating can be conducted on various substrates for the continuous and scalable fabrication of large-area thin films. It is less dependent on the surface morphology of the substrate, and also can be conveniently integrated with R2R systems. Besides, spary coating can deposit patterned structures by using shadow masks. However, like inkjet printing, spray coating also requires careful control of the rheological property, drying speed, and wetting property (to the substrate). Doctor-blade coating is also suitable for scalable and large-area applications. It can precisely control the thickness and uniformity of the metal film by jointly tuning the concentration of the metal suspension or solution, and the gap between the edge of the blade and the surface of the substrate. The relatively low coating speed is the biggest obstacle toward its industrialization. Dip coating is the simplest among all the coating methods. It's easy to operate, and can be applied to various materials and substrates. Nevertheless, the thickness uniformity of the film deposited by dip coating usually is poor, and both sides of the substrate will be coated by the target material.

When making SMEs, typically, suspensions of metal nanomaterials or solutions of metal precursors will be first cast on soft substrates by coating to form a thin film. Then the patterning process will be performed through site-selective sintering by the means of a laser,204 focused ion beam,205etc., after which the residual non-sintered metal nanomaterials or precursors will be removed by washing. Spinning-based processing. Very similar to the working principle of spray coating, the spinning technique also relies on ejection, smashing, deposition and drying of the solution on the substrate. But the ejected and smashed solution jets will be elongated during flying to the substrate, due to the spinnability of the solution. Finally, a fibrous film can be deposited on the substrate. The driving force in a spinning process can be centrifugation (centrifugation spinning), high-pressure air flow, and electrical field (electrospinning), or their combination. The spinning technique is suitable for synthesis of various kinds of fibers with size down to tens of nanometers, including polymer fibers, inorganic non-metallic fibers, and metallic fibers. For fabricating inorganic fibers, usually post thermal annealing is required. Among the various spinning processes, electrospinning (demonstrated in Fig. 8) has drawn the most research interest, because of its ability in fabricating ultra small fibers (Fig. 7c), and its versatility in controlling the assembly of fibers. Electrospinning has great potential for industrial fabrication of large area fibrous films. In addition, it's also easy to be integrated with R2R processes for continuous fabrication.

Electrospinning processing is particularly suitable for making soft transparent metal electrodes. Transparent electrodes composed of high aspect-ratio metal nanowires synthesized through wet chemical reaction, or metal grids prepared based on lithography and deposition/plating have demonstrated their bright future in replacing the commercial ITO transparent electrode.41,211 However, the limited length of the chemically synthesized metal wire network and the capping agent on the surface of the metal wires ultimately lead to relatively low conductivity of the electrode, while metal grids require high-cost lithography and are difficult to be scaled up. Therefore, researchers began to investigate electrospun metal nanofibrous networks as transparent electrodes. The pioneering work was carried out by H. Wu and Y. Cui, et al.34 By electrospinning PVA aqueous solution containing copper acetate, and post calcination, they prepared a Cu nanofibrous network with a fiber diameter down to 100 nm. Due to the ultra small diameter, and perfect welding of the inter-fiber junctions, the Cu fiber network demonstrated Rs as low as 50 sq1 at a transmittance as high as 90%. By applying a pair of parallelly placed metal plates as a fiber collector, metal grids can be obtained due to oriented assembly of the nanofibers. To inhibit the oxidation induced conductivity degradation of the electrospun copper nanofibrous electrode, P. Hsu, and Y. Cui, et al.212 performed an atomic layer deposition process to coat the copper nanofibers with a passivation layer of aluminum-doped zinc oxide (AZO) and aluminum oxide, thus remarkably enhancing the chemical stability of the transparent electrode in ambient environment. They also demonstrated that mesoscale metal wires derived by near-field electrospinning could substantially improve the performance of transparent electrodes composed of chemically synthesized metal nanowires, oxides, or conductive polymers.213 The performance of the transparent electrode based on electrospun metal nanofibers was further enhanced by the same group by employing an electrospun nanofiber templated deposition of metal nanotroughs, which exhibited a very low resistance of 2 sq1 at a high transmittance of 90%. Meanwhile, the metal nanotroughs demonstrated very high flexibility, making them promising for flexible devices.19 Many other improvements have been made in this area in the aspects of further improving the performance of electrospun nanofiber electrodes,214217 fabricating other metal-based nanofiber electrodes,215,218,219 and developing other approaches to room-temperature fabrication processes.220,221 Very recently, S. Lin and H. Wu et al.92 proposed a strategy by combining R2R blow spinning with simultaneous UV reduction for large-scale and continuous and room-temperature fabrication of flexible transparent electrode with Rs of 12 sq1 at a transmittance as high as 95%, paving the way for the industrialization of the spinning technique for fabrication of metal-based transparent electrodes.

3.1.3 Post-treatments. As mentioned previously, the synthesized metallic building blocks are commonly capped with organic or polymeric stabilizers. After additive manufacturing and drying, the presence of remaining capping agents at the junction sites of metallic building blocks prevents their close contact, resulting in increased tunneling distance of charge carriers and low Rs of the SMEs. As demonstrated by Hu et al., networks of single AgNWs with an electrical resistance of only several hundreds of ohms exhibited huge NWNW junction resistance higher than 108 after preparation and simple drying.201 Therefore, post-treatment, which is also known as sintering or welding, is necessary to efficiently remove the capping agents and ensure an improved Rs for practical applications. Until now, a variety of sintering processes including thermal sintering,1,42,225227 optical sintering,44,66,228231 microwave sintering,232 electrical sintering,233 and chemical sintering,234237 have been reported to treat the SMEs. The mechanism underlying the sintering is the fusion of metallic building blocks when exposed to high energy which could cause vibration of electrons and the corresponding localized temperature rise. It should be mentioned that the sintering process is also constructive to improve the adhesion strength between the metal layer and polymer substrates, which affords SMEs with better mechanical flexibility.1

Thermal sintering is the most commonly used method to treat the metal electrodes, in which the capping agents surrounding the metallic particles are decomposed by simple heating of the samples for a certain time. Thermal sintering depends greatly on the diameter of the metallic NPs and the volatilization or decomposition temperatures of the capping agents. Generally, improving the sintering temperature and extending the sintering time are beneficial to enhance the conductivity of the SMEs. For example, printed AgNP lines exhibited lower resistivity after annealing at higher temperature for shorter time intervals along with morphology evolution (Fig. 9a). Wiley's group also confirmed that AgNW films sintered at 70 C are more conductive (1.8 105 cm) than AgNP films annealed at 300 C.225 However, too high a sintering temperature should be avoided to prevent damage to the underlying flexible polymeric substrates of SMEs whose thermal stability are much lower than that of metals.

Another efficient sintering method is illuminating the metal electrode with lasers, flash lamps, and other light sources. When exposed to suitable photons, the surface plasmon resonance of metal nanomaterials will lead to a localized temperature rise to melt the adjacent metal nanomaterials. Such a photonic sintering is advantageous due to the tailorable illumination conditions including the power of the light source and the illuminating time and area. Therefore, a rapid and selective sintering with a predesigned pattern and controllable heat generation can be achieved. For example, Ko et al. demonstrated an ink-jet printed OFET by using a photonic sintered SME.44 They used a focused Ar ion laser with the wavelength of 514.5 nm to sinter the printed AuNPs into patterns and washed away the unsintered NPs to form patterns. Lee et al. carefully characterized the structure of optically sintered AgNWs.66Fig. 9b shows the SEM and HRTEM pictures of sintered AgNWs after laser irradiation. At the junction site, the top and bottom AgNW pairs fused together with good crystalline characteristics. Such perfect structure ensures both the good electrical conductivity and mechanical flexibility of the SMEs. However, the irradiation energy of the light beam should be carefully limited, otherwise the flexible substrates or the metal networks will be destroyed with overdose of photons. Oh and Lee have demonstrated that AgNW transparent electrodes converted into insulating electrodes by irradiating with a nanosecond-pulse ultraviolet laser (wavelength = 355 nm, power = 5 W) for seconds,238 because pristine AgNWs were cut into shorter ones by the laser and the percolating network collapsed. A low-energy photonic sintering has been proposed by using flash lamps with lower energy than lasers.228,239,240 The metal electrodes can also be optically sintered at an air/water interface first, and then transferred onto polymer surfaces, to avoid the photo-induced damage of flexible substrates.235 Usually, SMEs treated by optical sintering processes show relatively lower performance than that of thermally annealed ones.97 In addition, mechanical pressing is also an effective post-treatment to obtain low resistance (8.6 sq1) and smooth AgNW transparent electrodes (Fig. 9d and e).

Better contact of metallic building blocks in SMEs can also be easily and cost-efficiently realized by wet chemical treatments. For example, the contact resistance of AgNW electrodes can be dramatically reduced by immersing into a boiling HAuCl4 solution,201 immersing in NaCl, KCl, or CaCl2 solutions,241 or coating with PEDOT/PSS conducting polymers (Fig. 9f).234 Recently, Liu and coworkers reported a capillary force sintering process to achieve highly conductive AgNW electrodes (Fig. 9c). They found that the Rs of the AgNW electrode can be reduced from over 6.3 104 to 37 sq1 by exposing to moisture for seconds.236 They proposed that the water droplets at junction sites induce capillary force upon drying which can cause the cold-welding of AgNWs. This simple, rapid, and low-temperature sintering method is highly desirable for fabrication of SMEs.

3.2 In situ growth of metal electrodes on polymeric substrates

In this strategy, metal is directly grown on the substrate through in situ reduction of metal salts in a plating solution. The flexible substrate is firstly modified with suitable surface properties such as immobilization of catalytic moieties or a conductive layer to allow the chemical or electrochemical plating process. It is superior to the first strategy in that post-treatment is usually not necessary and the entire fabrication is often carried out at a low temperature, which is favorable for the polymeric substrates. The conductivity, surface smoothness and substrate adhesion are also much better than those made by the first strategy. However, since it requires immersing the entire substrate in a liquid bath with a complex mixture of metal salts, this strategy may not be used to fabricate SMEs on substrates pre-decorated with other functional materials.

Electroless plating bath. The electroless plating bath usually consists of precursor solution A and reductive solution B. Solution A is a mixed solution of metal salt, complexing agent and stabilizer (if needed), while solution B contains a reducing agent that is more electronegative comparing with the metal ion. Table 3 lists some typical examples of electroless metal plating systems. The combinations of metal salts, complexing agents and reducing agents are diverse for different plating systems. The metal source is always a metal salt that is not only soluble but also stable in water. The complexing agent has multiple functions that are important for the efficient and stable deposition of the metal. First, the complexing agents stabilize the metal ions to avoid unwanted reactions in the solution. Second, they work as a buffer to prevent dramatic change in pH, which may result in degradation of the solution. Third, the complexing agents coordinate with metal ions to reduce the concentration of free metal ions in the solution, thus preventing the direct reaction between the metal ions and reducing agents in the solution rather than on the catalytic surface. For example, ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) is a commonly used complexing agent for electroless Cu plating in alkaline solution.250,251 An alkaline environment is preferred for the deposition of Cu by the Cu2+/HCHO system because the redox potential for HCHO is lower at high pH. However, the formation of Cu(OH)2 precipitates at high pH will stop the Cu deposition. Hence, EDTA is applied as a stabilizer for the Cu plating solution by functioning as both pH buffer and ligand. The reducing agent is a water-soluble mild reductant such as formaldehyde (HCHO), dimethylamine borane (DMAB), hydrazine (N2H4) and sodium hypophosphite (NaH2PO2).252254 This reducing agent should be neither too strong nor too weak for successful electroless metal plating. If the reducing agent is too strong, the reduction of metal ions will happen in the solution rather than prior to the deposition on the substrate. If the reducing agent is too weak, the plating cannot happen spontaneously under ambient conditions.

Substrate pre-treatment for electroless deposition. Apart from the plating bath, the substrate is also very important for electroless metal plating. As mentioned above, the electroless metal plating normally takes place in the presence of a catalyst. Hence, the surface of the substrate should be either catalytic itself or modified with the catalyst. The simplest method is using catalytic metals or alloys such as Cu and steel as substrates.266,267 Since commonly used flexible substrates do not have catalytic activity, a pre-modification with catalytic moieties before metal plating is needed. Generally, there are two strategies for the modification of catalysts on flexible substrates. One is direct coating/printing of the catalyst/catalytic precursor on the substrates, and the other is absorbing the catalyst/catalytic precursor with polymer-modified substrates. Fig. 10a demonstrates a simple method to pattern a catalyst on flexible PET and PI substrates by direct printing of AgNO3 ink.268 The viscosity of the ink is adjusted by ethylene glycol to fulfil the requirement of inkjet-printing and direct writing.

The direct printing method is simple and effective, but the adhesion between metal and substrate is often unsatisfactory. To address this issue, employing a layer of polymer as an interfacial layer between the substrate and the metal is an effective approach. For example, polydopamine is a widely used adhesive material, and the ease of self-polymerization makes it convenient for surface modification.269,270Fig. 10b illustrates a simple strategy to deposit patterned metal thin films with the aid of a polydopamine adhesive layer,271 which is found to be a versatile modifier on noble metals, oxides, polymers, etc. However, such a method requires a photolithography process to pattern the substrate, which limits the scalability and processing speed. Instead, Gracia et al. developed a method that can deposit patterned metal by either photolithography or direct printing of the resist. Poly(acrylic acid) (PAA) is grafted on PET and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) as the ligand for Cu2+, which is further reduced to Cu0 seeds for electroless plating of Cu (Fig. 10c).272 The chemical bonding between PAA and the substrate provided firm adhesion between the Cu film and the substrate. As a result, the Cu on PET passed the industrial adhesion test that the Cu film could not be peeled-off from the PET substrate using Scotch tape. Apart from PAA, poly[2-(methacryloyloxy)ethyl-trimethylammonium chloride] (PMETAC) is another functional polymer that is often used for catalyst immobilization. The quaternary ammonium groups in PMETAC show strong affinity with PdCl42 ions, which are versatile catalytic precursors for the electroless deposition of multiple metals.273 Guo et al. and Yu et al. developed multiple surface coating methods to graft PMETAC on different substrates, such as surface-initiated atom transfer radical polymerization (SI-ATRP), free-radical polymerization, and crosslinking between the substrate and poly[2-(methacryloyloxy)ethyl trimethylammonium chloride-co-3-(trimethoxysilyl)propyl methacrylate] [P(METAC-co-MPTS)] (Fig. 10d).29,274,275 This polymer-based electroless plating method, namely, polymer-assisted metal deposition (PAMD) is suitable for the deposition of both patterned and unpatterned metal thin films. For the unpatterned fabrication, the polymer/copolymer modified substrates are dipped in an aqueous solution of (NH4)2PdCl4, and then immersed in the metal plating bath. For the patterned fabrications, a sticky paste consisting of (NH4)2PdCl4 and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) is printed on the polymer/copolymer modified substrates to avoid the diffusion of the catalytic precursor. The polymer/copolymer is uniformly modified on these substrates; the acquired metal thin film shows very low surface roughness on flat PET substrates, which is desirable for flexible thin film electronics.

A common disadvantage of all these polymer-grafting techniques is that the grafting process is usually time consuming. To address that, Yu et al. recently reported photoreactive copolymer poly(4-methacryloyl benzophenone-co-2-methacryloyloxy ethyltrimethylammonium chloride) [P(MBP-co-METAC)] that can be grafted on multiple organic substrates by UV-induced crosslinking (Fig. 10e).276 The copolymer shows good solubility in methanol, ethanol, 2-methoxyethanol and some other solvents, so it is easy to dissolve it as an ink for inkjet printing even by a household inkjet printer. The UV-induced crosslinking process only takes several minutes, and the obtained metal electrodes show good conductivity and mechanical robustness. Such printed metal electrodes are demonstrated to be eligible for high performance OSCs, OFETs, etc.276

Electrodeposition of metals is widely used in various industries for coating objects with a layer of metal with some desired properties. The universality of electrodeposition in industry is mainly because of the following advantages: thickness and morphology can be precisely controlled by adjusting the electrochemical parameters (such as current density, deposition time, electrolyte composition and concentration, current type (direct current and pulse current), etc.); higher deposition rates are possible; and the low-cost solution process requires inexpensive equipment, when compared with physical vapor deposition (PVD, such as thermal evaporation and sputtering) in terms of simplicity and cost effective use of expensive equipment and metal materials.


Direct electrodeposition on pretreated flexible substrates. It should be pointed out that the object used in electrodeposition needs to be electrically conductive. However, unlike metals, most reported flexible or wearable substrates are naturally non-conductive, and require special pre-treatment before the electrochemical deposition process (Fig. 11). The conductive carbon nanofibers, which were processed using a heat treatment (carbonization), provided bi-functionality: they formed conductive core channels and artificial supports of the metal structures (Fig. 11c). Han et al.294 introduced multilayer graphene to support metal electrodeposition. A polyurethane sponge fully coated with multilayer graphene was formed, which was subsequently coated with Ni metal via an electrodeposition process (Fig. 11d).


Electrodeposition on conductive rigid substrates and subsequent transfer to flexible substrates. Since the electroplating of metals on conventional conductive substrates is already very mature, some research groups employed the transfer technique to transfer electroplated metal layers to flexible substrates. For example, Su et al. firstly fabricated a uniform and continuous Ni film by electroplating metallic Ni on fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) and then transferred it onto a flexible PET substrate via hot-pressing.296 Li's group developed embedded metal-mesh transparent electrodes by an approach combining lithography, electrodeposition, and imprint transfer.40 FTO glass patterned with photoresist is obtained through a lithography technique. In the following electrodeposition step, the selected metal is deposited inside the lithographically defined trenches and fills the trenches with a uniform metal mesh. Finally, the metal mesh is transferred and embedded in the plastic film. Liu et al. reported a similar study where a patterned photoresist was obtained through laser direct writing and the electroplating substrate was Ni metal.297 Furthermore, they promoted this approach to develop a freestanding transparent Ni network.298

4.1 Wearable interconnects

A simple and direct application of SMEs in electronics is use as a flexible conductive interconnect to connect the soft/rigid functional microelectronic units and construct an intact flexible circuit. The main function of SMEs in such a scenario is to (1) transport electrons and (2) dissipate the mechanical stresses applied on the flexible circuits. Thus, a SME with a Rs of as low as possible and excellent mechanical robustness would be highly welcome. During the past decades, great progress has been achieved in the search and development of flexible interconnects by using the chemically formed SMEs. Up until now, developing large-scale and low-cost manufacturing processes that can readily integrate with well-established electronic industries has become the central issue in this area. Printing is highly desirable for flexible interconnect fabrication due to its programmability, scalability, and cost-effectiveness.15,307309 Recently, a hybrid 3D printing technique, which combines the direct writing of metal inks with automated pick-and-place processes of electronic components, has been reported to fabricate wearable electronic circuitry (Fig. 12).310 In this approach, insulating substrates and conductive interconnects with arbitrary patterned layouts can be efficiently fabricated by carefully tuning the ink recipes and printing parameters.1,96,131,311313 Rigid electrical components such as resistors, capacitors, microprocessors, and LEDs can be further integrated into the flexible circuit by using a second vacuum-controlled nozzle to pick-and-place in the desired location (Fig. 12a). Fig. 12b illustrates a flexible circuit composed of five distinct components with lateral dimensions ranging from 0.8 mm 1.6 mm (LED) to 9 mm 9 mm (microprocessor) and printed Ag interconnects fabricated through this hybrid 3D printing technique. The hybrid 3D printed LED circuit can work well as a stretchable device withstanding a max tensile strain of 30% (Fig. 12c and d).

4.2 Wearable resisters

SMEs can also be directly integrated into wearable electronic devices as passive components such as resistors to generate heat or detect mechanical stress/strain. A wearable heater is a kind of device for tuning the body temperature, in order to keep warm or for thermotherapy. For such applications, the heaters should simultaneously provide high wearing comfort and breathability, high controllability over the temperature, high safety to the human body, and acceptable cost. In addition, for a wearable device, the voltage of the power supply cannot be too high, while the power (P) is determined by the equation: P = V2/R, wherein V is the voltage, and R is the resistance. Therefore, to pursue maximized temperature, the R of the resistor should be minimized.316 The SMEs have made the design and realization of high-performance wearable heaters highly possible.209,210,214,215,306,317324 Among various SMEs, those prepared based on AgNWs are predominant for the fabrication of wearable heaters due to their high electrical conductivity, colorless characteristic, excellent biocompatibility, and good environmental stability. For example, Fig. 13a and b demonstrate a large-area, transparent, conductive film composed of AgNW networks on PI with superior properties (Rs of 1.3 sq1 with an optical transmittance of 90% in the visible regime). A wearable heater based on it exhibited a maximum temperature of 250 C when connected to an applied voltage of 4.5 V (Fig. 13ce).214 The transparent and wearable heater can be further integrated with a wireless operation system, realizing temperature control using a smartphone via Bluetooth (Fig. 13fh). CuNW-based SMEs are also attractive for fabricating low-cost wearable heaters; however, their relatively low conductance stability in the ambient environment is the main challenge.215,306,318,325

Another important application of wearable resistors is in pressure/strain sensors for detecting human physiological movements. Flexible and lightweight pressure and strain sensing devices have been well-known for their importance in a wide variety of applications such as electronic skin,326,327 soft robotics,328 and energy harvesting.240,301 These devices are mainly classified into four categories: piezoresistive, capacitive, iontronic, and piezoelectric.329 Compared to the others, the piezoresistive-type pressure sensors hold the advantage of simplicity in device configuration and fabrication. The change in resistance of conductive materials when subjected to external mechanical stress/deformation, which is also known as the piezoresistive effect, provides the possibility to measure the stress/strain. The sensitivity of a piezoresistive sensor is typically characterized by using the gauge factor (GF). The GF is determined by R/R0/, where R is the change in resistance, R0 is the unstrained resistance, and is the strain. SMEs with optimized electrical and mechanical properties can be used as a piezoresistive sensor directly.56,69,173,223,237,330333 For example, a conductive paper dip-coated with ultrathin AuNWs is a high-performance piezoresistive sensor, showing a low energy consumption (<30 W), a high detection sensitivity of 13 Pa, a fast response time (<17 ms), and a high gauge factor of 7.38 (Fig. 14a).69 The device life is also good, showing high stability over 50000 loadingunloading cycles, thanks to the inert chemical properties of Au and polymer substrates. This highly sensitive wearable AuNW sensor can be used to monitor blood pressure, voice vibrations, and the sport positions of humans by pasting it onto the wrist, throat, and joints. The piezoresistive GF of SMEs can be tailored by optimizing the stacking morphology of metal nanomaterials which is determined by the morphology of the metallic building blocks, the properties of the polymer matrix, and the metal/polymer interfacial interactions. Such an AuNW film based piezoresistive device was also found to be compatible with multiple stretchable substrates, and thus can be integrated on latex rubber to serve as a stretchable strain sensor (Fig. 14b). Very recently, a wearable strain sensor with a large GF of 13.3 was reported using a printed Ag-TPU as the soft electrode (Fig. 14c).310

4.3 Wearable optoelectronic devices

Optoelectronic devices contain electrical-to-optical or optical-to-electrical transducers that can generate, harvest, or detect light. The crucial requirements for transparent electrodes in optoelectronics are high conductivity and high transparency. Vacuum-deposited transparent conducting oxides (TCOs) and metal thin films are widely used as electrodes for thin film optoelectronic devices such as organic solar cells (OSCs), OLEDs, dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs), perovskite solar cells (PSCs), etc.334342 However, the intrinsic unbendable and brittle nature of vacuum-deposited TCOs has become the bottleneck for the realization of flexible and wearable optoelectronics.343,344 On the contrary, chemically formed SMEs are very promising due to their balanced electrical, mechanical, and optical performance. Nowadays, SMEs with Rs lower than 10 sq1 and transparency higher than 90% can easily be chemically fabricated in high-throughput R2R processes,92,137,345 and have become a competitive alternative of TCOs. Over the years, Ag-based transparent electrodes printed from AgNPs through multiple techniques such as flexographic, inkjet, and screen printing have been proposed for solution-processed TCO-free flexible OSCs and OLEDs. Typically, Ag electrodes are printed on top of the solar cells instead of vacuum-deposited metal electrodes, and the printed Ag grids are combined with transparent conducting polymer PEDOT:PSS to replace TCOs.136,346348 However, the printed SMEs always show relatively high thickness and surface roughness, which lead to poor device performance or short circuit failures.349,350 To overcome this problem, Chen et al. have developed an ultra-smooth Ag/Ni hybrid transparent electrode with printed metal meshes embedded in the substrate.289 The surface roughness of the electrode can be reduced to 0.17 nm after polishing with aqueous silica particle solutions, ensuring a flexible quantum dot LED with an efficiency of 10.4 cd A1 and 3.2 lm W1 at 1000 cd m2 (Fig. 15a). Kang and coworkers introduced a capillary printing method to prepare high quality AgNW-based transparent electrodes.351 By carefully modulating the alignment of AgNWs, SMEs with high optical transmittance (96.7%), low Rs (19.5 sq1), and low surface root-mean-square roughness (15.6 nm) were achieved. The OLEDs constructed on such SMEs show a luminance efficiency as high as 14.25 cd A1 (Fig. 15b). A PSC using such aligned AgNWs electrodes also exhibited a high power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 8.57% (Fig. 15b).

Besides printing, the SMEs fabricated through the in situ chemical deposition approach are also promising for flexible optoelectronics. In contrast, electroless deposition consumes no additional electricity, and the substrates do not need to be conductive. Highly flexible full-solution-processed OSCs are obtained by using Cu bottom electrodes fabricated through electroless deposition. Thin (<100 nm-thick) and smooth (root-mean-square roughness <5 nm) Cu electrodes are obtained through this method, and the mechanical durability of such electrodes is far better than that of reference Cu electrodes fabricated by vacuum deposition (Fig. 15c).357 However, both electrodeposition and electroless deposition require the immersion of the entire substrate in the plating solution, which makes them not suitable for the fabrication of top electrodes in these photoelectronic devices, because most of the active materials are sensitive to water and other solvents.358,359

4.4 Wearable electrochemical energy storage devices

Lightweight and high-energy-density electrochemical energy storage devices are essential components to power wearable electronics. Over the past few years, research on wearable supercapacitors and rechargeable batteries has exhibited rapid growth.360362 Metals are generally not electrochemically reactive and cannot be used as active materials. Instead, chemically formed SMEs have been recognized as promising flexible current collectors for constructing wearable energy storage devices. Ag- and Cu-based SMEs are rarely used in supercapacitors, mainly due to their weak tolerance to the corrosion of electrolyte solutions. Ni-coated cotton yarns and fabrics are very promising current collectors to fabricate wearable supercapacitors with high-energy-density and high-power-density. For example, Zheng's group demonstrated a yarn type all solid-state supercapacitor with an energy density and a power density of 6.1 mW h cm3 and 1400 mW cm3, respectively.23 They used Ni-coated cotton yarns made by electroless deposition of Ni on pristine cotton as the current collector and electrochemically deposited graphene nanosheets as the active material (Fig. 16a).23 They also demonstrated fabric-type supercapacitors, by integrating more amount of carbon nanotubes and/or reduced graphene oxide on Ni-coated cotton fabrics. The as-made wearable supercapacitors demonstrated an ultrahigh areal capacitance up to 6.2 F cm2 and good capacity retention over 10000 chargingdischarging cycles.363,364 All these supercapacitors can be integrated with electronic components into textile-forms (Fig. 16b).

5. Summary and outlook

In summary, SMEs have achieved rapid development over the past two decades to meet the rapidly increasing demand for flexible and wearable electronics. In contrast to conventional microfabrication processes migrated from silicon technology, chemical approaches for making SMEs afford significant advantages of simplicity, cost effectiveness, and R2R compatibility to the mass production of SMs. It is also found that in some studies, SMEs made with chemical approaches show better adhesion with the underlying substrates, which provides better fatigue resistance during repetitive mechanical bending, stretching, and twisting. Chemical preparation of SMEs can either go through (i) additive construction on flexible polymers using metallic building blocks; or (ii) in situ growth from flexible substrates. An increasing amount of work on the development of new materials, structural designs and fabrication technologies have emerged in recent years to prepare SMEs with better electrical conductivity and mechanical robustness, simpler and faster processes, and controllable optical and electronic properties related to end applications. Indeed, some effective chemical processes toward SMEs have been transferred to industrial sectors.

In spite of the great progress made in the past decades, several materials and technological challenges are still waiting to be addressed. First of all, the resolution of metal patterns prepared via chemical approaches is rather limited. General inkjet printing and screen printing can only achieve a resolution with a low limit of 20 m. Therefore, the resultant SMEs can hardly be used for the manufacture of microelectronics with miniature size. In addition, most of the SMEs prepared by printing or plating exhibit unacceptable surface quality for thin-film optoelectronic devices, which results in deteriorated device performance. New and improved fabrication technologies that can overcome these shortcomings are highly desired.

Secondly, the intrinsic nature of metals, such as the relatively large density, the fatigue issue, and the spontaneous surface oxidation of Ag and Cu, restricts the SMEs from practical applications in flexible electronics. To achieve ideal flexible electrodes, conductive materials with improved electrical and mechanical properties are still needed. The promising solutions to modify the metals, at least, include (i) alloying with lightweight and/or chemically inert elements such as titanium and carbon, (ii) developing highly efficient surface protecting/passivation materials and technologies, (iii) developing new chemistries to prepare metals with new structures such as amorphous or new crystal forms, and (iv) optimizing the interfacial structure between the metal and the underlying polymer substrates.

Thirdly, the safety issue of the SMEs and the corresponding flexible electronic devices should be thoroughly considered before they enter our daily life. This is especially true for the wearable, skin-contact, and implantation applications. All the materials and processes to fabricate the SMEs and electronic devices should be cautiously examined to eliminate any safety risk to the health of humans. Developing SMEs and electronics fabricated from bio-compatible materials are highly necessary. For example, wearable electronics constructed on cotton textiles and cellulose papers by using bio-compatible metallic inks are more meaningful.

Finally, a chemical process with low energy consumption, and low CO2 and pollutant emissions is important for the mass-production of SMEs. Those chemistries using a large amount of toxic materials and/or producing much waste should be avoided, and new green chemistry should be developed in the future. We believe the collaboration from a variety of communities of the chemistry, physics, materials, electronics, and textiles industry will effectively promote this multidisciplinary field forward.
