Abstract

Phenothiazine dyes have attracted significant attention as metal-free sensitizers for dye-sensitized solar cells since their introduction in 2007. Since then, over 430 phenothiazine dyes have been prepared and investigated. Despite the ever-increasing molecular variation, the overall improvement in the power conversion efficiency has been slow. In this review, we identify the traits of successful phenothiazine dyes and shed light on inherent molecular limitations. We include all phenothiazine dyes reported to date, and the big dataset is used to look for trends in photophysical, electrochemical and photovoltaic performance. The dataset used in this study is curated to contain only photovoltaic performance data that is validated using a novel credibility assessment. We compare the distinct sub-geometries of phenothiazine dyes, and investigate the effects of auxiliary donors, the use of Ï€-spacers, DSSC electrolyte compatibility, N-alkyl vs. N-aryl substituents and mono- vs. multianchored vs. tethered dyes. Lastly, the phenothiazine dyes are compared to their closest dye analogue, the phenoxazines. Based on the findings in this review, we believe phenothiazine dyes are unlikely to be the main sensitizers of high-efficiency DSSCs in the future, due to low oxidation potentials, low extinction coefficients and inherent limitations restricting the further optimization of their photophysical properties.

Introduction

After 30 years of intense research, the beauty and versatility of dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) continue to fascinate researchers. The ever-evolving number of discipline-spanning applications utilizing this technology is testament to its popularity. Recent developments in the field have led to photovoltaic devices of tuneable transparency either by employing near-infrared (NIR) absorbing dyes or photochromic dyes.1,2 Advancements such as these means DSSCs now can be employed in greenhouse windows, while the beautiful colours of conventional DSSCs makes these attractive as decorative building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV).3â€“5 The possibility to tailor the absorption properties to the light source or environment means DSSC devices are now powering an increasing amount of IoT devices and wearable consumer electronics, such as sensors, watches and headphones.6,7 The versatility and robustness of the DSSC architecture and components have no doubt contributed to the development of other closely related technologies such as photo-rechargeable batteries,8 solar fuel synthesis9 and photo redox catalysis.10,11

In the general field of dye-sensitized solar cells, there have been several notable achievements in recent years.12 Kakiage et al. published a co-sensitized carbazole-/triarylamine-based system delivering an impressive power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 14.3% under 1 sun illumination.13 Recent advances in dye and electrolyte engineering have pushed the NREL certified DSSC record to 13%, and the efficiency of indoor DSSCs has reached 34.5% under 1000 lux, following several major improvements over the last years.14â€“17 An attractive aspect of the DSSC technology is the inherent low toxicity of its components and prospects of using recycled materials.18 In the pursuit of environmental friendliness and low toxicity, aqueous electrolytes and alternative device components have gained momentum in later years.19â€“21 The development of new dye molecules has always been central to DSSC research, and the search for capable metal-free dyes have become increasingly important in the recent years. The tunability of fully organic sensitizers is crucial when designing dyes for novel applications, e.g. dyes with energy levels that match the novel electrolytes, display photochromic behaviour, or dyes that exibit NIR-absorption. Among the metal-free chromophores used in DSSCs, phenothiazine was one of the first scaffolds, and with over 430 different dyes reported to date, still receives considerable attention in the dye community.

The first record of a phenothiazine-based compound was the successful synthesis and characterization of the dye and malaria drug now known as methylene blue. Reported by Heinrich Caro as part of a patent in 1877 (German Patent No. DE-18771866), phenothiazine has since been employed in an array of fields predominantly related to pharmaceuticals, synthetic dyes and organic electronic materials. The unsubstituted 10H-phenothiazine itself was first prepared in 1883 by A. Bernthsen.22

Phenothiazine is a heterocyclic compound comprising three six-membered rings, linked as a structural analogue of anthracene. The central ring accommodates the nitrogen and sulphur atoms, bridging the two outer benzene rings. The numbering of phenothiazine starts at the carbon atom adjacent to the nitrogen and continues around the outer ring, giving sulphur number 5 and nitrogen 10, see Fig. 1.

Owing to its structure, phenothiazine has a dihedral angle between the two benzene rings, usually reported being around 150Â°, depending on the substituent on the nitrogen atom.23,24 This is often referred to as a butterfly structure. The radical cation of phenothiazine is reported to be planar.25 In an electrochemical application where the phenothiazine dye is oxidized in cycles, a flapping motion is thus expected as the dyes alternate between a flat and a bent configuration. In dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) literature, this is commonly claimed to be beneficial as it prevents dye aggregation.26â€“29 Another consequence of the flapping motion not frequently mentioned is the reorganizational energy cost associated with this inversion.30â€“32 Energy estimates range from 0.1 eV33 to 0.3 eV34 for this motion. For DSSC, this has the implication of lowered injection efficiency, limiting the JSC and ultimately the PCE.

The low oxidation potential of phenothiazine means it has been much studied electrochemically and used in many electrochemical applications.35,36 For instance, in early DSSC research, phenothiazine was tethered to a ruthenium complex sensitizer.37 Due to the high local concentration and electron-donating abilities of phenothiazine, the overall result was rapid intramolecular charge transfer, locating the hole further away from the TiO2 surface increasing the VOC. Another application in DSSCs before being used as a chromophore itself was the use of 10H-phenothiazine in the electrolyte in conjunction with cobalt.38 As the early cobalt electrolytes were plagued by slow dye reduction and rapid recombination, phenothiazine was introduced as a co-mediator alongside Co(II), in an attempt to remedy this issue. The Co(II)/PTZ system outperformed both the Co(II)/Co(III) and the Co(II)/Fc systems. Phenothiazine has also been used in organic field-effect transistors, where polythiophene functionalized with phenothiazineâ€“vinylene side chains has been reported as a promising system.39 Phenothiazine also features in polymers for OLEDs,40â€“42 small-molecules for sensory applications,43â€“45 and hydrogen production.46â€“48

From an organic synthesist's point of view, the phenothiazine scaffold is a forgiving scaffold to work with. The lone pair of nitrogen stabilizes arenium ions formed on position 3 and 7 during the useful electrophilic aromatic substitution. This allows for selective halogenation reactions, where tuning of reaction conditions can result in high yields of both mono- and dihalogenated phenothiazine intermediates following convenient and scalable purifications.49,50 These intermediates can further undergo an array of metal-catalysed coupling reactions, most commonly the Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross-coupling. Owing to a plethora of coupling partners, this allows unlimited possible extensions to the phenothiazine scaffold. The facile and selective halogenation of phenothiazine derivatives is key for the high tunability of this class of sensitizers and is in the authorsâ€™ opinion one of the inherent strengths of 10H-phenothiazine.

Phenothiazine in DSSC history

In 2007, the first example of a phenothiazine-based dye for DSSC was reported by Haining Tian et al.51 as one of the early metal-free dyes scaffolds. The DSSC field had at that point gained great momentum, 16 years after the seminal paper by Brian Oâ€™Regan and Michael GrÃ¤tzel.52 At the time, DSSC research efforts were aimed at developing new metal-complex dyes in addition to optimizing other key device components, such as the mesoporous semiconducting oxide and the electrolyte containing the redox shuttle. However, over time the focus was turned towards metal-free, fully organic dyes based on chromophores such as triarylamines,53â€“57 coumarins,58,59 carbazoles,60,61 indolines62,63 and phenothiazines.64â€“66 Compared to metal-complexed dyes, the metal-free dyes commonly display higher extinction coefficients and are also compatible with a wider range of organic reactions and purification procedures. Furthermore, metal-free dyes do not rely on costly, low abundance and often toxic precious metals. However, achieving the same wide light absorption properties can be challenging, and the number of synthetic steps and complexity of the dye synthesis are often also increased for the metal-free dyes.

The number of annual publications in the entire DSSC field has been declining since the peak in 2015, a trend commonly explained by the rapid increase in the perovskite solar cell (PSC) research activity since the introduction in 200967 with major advancements reported in 2012.68,69Fig. 2 shows the number of patents and research papers per year for phenothiazine-based DSSCs, as well as the development of the entire DSSC and PSC fields. A rise in patents in 2018 and research papers in 2019 could be signalling a resurgence of the phenothiazine dye class. Therefore, it is a matter of urgency to collect all the reports on these sensitizers for researchers to make well-illuminated decisions on the direction of the sensitizer class. For the phenothiazine dye class, two review papers were published in 2016 by Luo et al.70 and Huang et al.71 Both reviews cover largely the same dyes, describing the findings of the individual papers thoroughly. Two other review papers focus on the optoelectronic properties of phenothiazine-based molecules. In 2019, Al-Busaidi et al. includes 29 phenothiazine dyes72 and in 2020 Devadiga et al. covered 121 phenothiazine dyes.29 Recently, Al-Ghamdi et al. reviewed the synthetic strategies for phenothiazine and phenoxazine dyes.73 While all these reviews are thorough in the data collection and the description of the individual findings, they all lack the overview analysis of the entire dye class. The conclusions are mostly based on the most recent, and the highest performance accounts.

The success of any dye class is most crudely judged by its highest-performing dyes, and in a field where power conversion efficiency is the ultimate measure of quality and performance, reports with questionable performance claims can quickly be accepted as the state of the art. For the phenothiazine field, several reports of the most efficient dyes are overly optimistic about the performance of the devices fabricated with these dyes. Therefore, we have included a control calculation for the photovoltaic data in this work, so only credible data will be used for the conclusions. A description of the assessment method and thresholds is given in the section below called Data credibility assessment.

When looking into the development of the average efficiencies of phenothiazine dyes, we were surprised to find only a marginal improvement despite intense efforts through 14 years of research, over 150 research papers and several highly cited breakthroughs in device efficiency. This led us to formulate two hypotheses: (i) phenothiazine as a dye scaffold is inherently limited in some way, thus resisting further efficiency improvements. (ii) It is also possible that insufficient overview means the science could be progressing, but the communal learning is not, thus hampering overall development. While phenothiazine is not the most common dye motif in the DSSC field, it is one of the earlier scaffolds used in metal-free dyes for DSSC. As a steady stream of papers investigating phenothiazine dyes are published every year, we believe it is due time to investigate the entirety of the field and attempt to draw some conclusions following 14 years of research and over 430 published dyes.

Phenoxazine is the closest analogue of phenothiazine, only differing in position 5, where oxygen takes the place of sulphur. Dyes based on this scaffold were first introduced by Tian et al. in 2009.83 Forty different phenoxazine dye molecules have been published, with overall good performances in DSSC. The scarcity of these dyes is most likely linked to availability and cost of commercial building blocks for synthesis. While similar in structure to phenothiazines, the smaller size of oxygen vs. sulphur changes the neutral structure of the scaffold. This is likely to affect the molecular properties and DSSC performance. Hence, by including the complete class of phenoxazine DSSC dyes into this review, we hope to further illuminate the workings of both dye scaffolds.

DSSC operating principles and characterization

The dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) is a photoelectrochemical cell. A conventional n-type DSSC consists of three main components, see Fig. 4. (1) A working electrode commonly fabricated by a thin layer of mesoporous TiO2 nanoparticles deposited on a transparent glass slide coated with a conductive layer of fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO). The TiO2 of the working electrode is sensitized with the dye, by immersion in a dye bath. (2) The material of choice for the counter electrode is commonly FTO glass. A layer of platinum is usually deposited on the surface to increase the catalytic activity. Alternatives, such as electropolymerization-deposition of organic semiconducting polymers such as poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT), have increased in popularity with the introduction of novel electrolytes based on copper and cobalt.84 (3) While the external circuit connects the working and counter electrodes outside the cell, the task of shuttling the electrons back to the dye is performed by the electrolyte containing the redox shuttle species. The iodideâ€“triiodide redox couple has been a favourite in the DSSC field since the very beginning.85 For phenothiazine dyes, only a handful of reports do not employ a variety of an Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ electrolyte.61,86â€“90
Fig. 4 Schematic of the working principles of a DSSC.

Under DSSC operation, dye molecules are excited by incident sunlight, injecting an electron into the conduction band of the TiO2 before the electron completes the circuit as described above. While the current of the device is simply the rate of electrons passing through the external circuit, the voltage from the device is dictated by the difference between the Fermi level of the TiO2 and the redox potential of the redox shuttle.

Correctly characterizing the performance of a DSSC should be of the highest priority. As comprehensive and fundamental work has been done to cover the entire topic thoroughly,91 only a short introduction to the most important techniques will be mentioned here. First, the pure dyes are commonly characterized by UV-vis spectroscopy, fluorescence spectroscopy and the electrochemical properties are measured by cyclic voltammetry or other electrochemical techniques.

Currentâ€“voltage characteristics measurements under 1 Sun illumination (100 mW cmâˆ’2) is arguably the most important measurement for dye-sensitized solar cells, as the all-important power conversion efficiency value can be obtained. Often called Jâ€“V sweeps, it is performed by stepwise altering the potential across the device under illumination while measuring the current. When no external potential is applied, the current may flow unrestricted and this yields the short-circuit current density (JSC) value. Furthermore, as the external potential is increased, at a certain point the external potential is equal to the device potential and no current will flow. This point is called the open-circuit voltage, the VOC. The result from the Jâ€“V measurements is known as a Jâ€“V curve, and the JSC, VOC and fill factor (FF) may be obtained from this curve. Another useful technique is incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE). Here, the devices are stepwise illuminated by monochromatic light with wavelengths spanning at least the part of the electromagnetic spectrum the dye is absorbing. The wavelength of incident illumination is changed stepwise and the JSC is measured for each step. The current value is converted to the number of electrons, which is compared to the flux of the monochromatic light at that wavelength, i.e. the number of photons. While the resulting efficiency spectrum does not give an overall efficiency value, it is still extremely useful. By integrating the IPCE spectrum over the 1 Sun AM 1.5G solar spectrum, an estimate for the short-circuit current density under full illumination can be calculated. Thus, an IPCE spectrum can be used as a control measurement to check the validity of the JSC value obtained from the Jâ€“V sweep.92 The JSC value is the most easily manipulated parameter in Jâ€“V sweeps, and thus the one that should be closest inspected when performing a control.

Method

Data collection and treatment

This review aims to look at the complete set of phenothiazine dyes and use the big dataset to learn about the strengths and weaknesses of these dyes with statistics as the main tool. Certain assumptions and limitations have been set to obtain the most reliable results. (i) Only dyes with the cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group have been included. As just a few accounts include phenothiazine dyes with other anchors, we do not consider this a large limitation of the data set.93â€“95 There are two possible configurations of the cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group, E and Z. Hong et al. investigated the difference and found the E isomer to be the predominant product in the Knoevenagel condensation, and fortunately the most efficient configuration in DSSCs.96 (ii) Only DSSC devices fabricated with Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ electrolytes have been included. (iii) Dyes where the phenothiazine scaffold is an auxiliary moiety of another major chromophore or other structural variations of phenothiazine are reported separately as other phenothiazine dyes.26,97â€“112

Having set the limitations of the scope, a total of 154 papers were found to report original experimental photovoltaic data of phenothiazine sensitizers. Where photovoltaic performance values are duplicated in different papers for the same dyes, only one set of values has been included in the main dataset.

Data credibility assessment

The importance of reliable photovoltaic data cannot be emphasized strongly enough, so it is in the interest of the authors to filter the data to improve the quality of the data set. The more credible the data set, the higher accuracy can be obtained in the later stages of data comparison and observing the trends in the data. In addition to deliberate manipulation of photovoltaic data and calculation errors, other ways of inadvertently altering the photovoltaic data are possible. The intensity of the incident light may be altered, drift or fluctuate. Omitting the use of a shadow mask may also affect the photovoltaic performance significantly by allowing channelling of light or inaccuracies in the device area.91

Regardless of the motives or reasons behind erroneous data being reported, knowing you can trust the data is of utmost importance. In a review like this, it is the relationship between the molecular structures of the dyes and the photovoltaic performance which is all-important. Errors in the structure of the dyes are highly unlikely, but as the devices are fabricated by different scientists using different tools and materials, the performance characteristics are associated with higher inherent uncertainty. Consequently, minimizing this inaccuracy is of interest, and this has been done by looking at the short-circuit current densities (JSC). Establishing the maximum attainable JSC,max value from an optical material under a known illumination and spectral distribution is a useful way of assessing the credibility of reported photovoltaic performance parameters. A single incident photon may ultimately give rise to a maximum of one electron, which again may contribute to the short-circuit current density of the device if collected efficiently. Therefore, the number of available incident photons dictates the maximum attainable JSC value of any photovoltaic device. Accurate solar irradiance spectra data is available for free online (https://rredc.nrel.gov/solar//spectra/am1.5/). For the 1 Sun AM 1.5G spectrum, integrating the irradiance over the part of the solar spectrum relevant for solar cells yields a convenient graph, as shown in Fig. 6. Here, the absorption onset can be related to the maximum attainable JSC value. The optical bandgap is found from the absorption onset: E0â€“0 = 1240/Î»abs.onset. It is then a simple task of comparing the theoretical maximum values to the reported values for the devices. For silicon solar cells, with a typical bandgap of 1.1 eV,248 the maximum attainable JSC is approximately 44 mA cmâˆ’2.

Arguably, the accuracy in measuring the absorption onsets is likely to be the least reliable variable of this analysis. Dissolved dyes display somewhat different absorption spectra depending on the solvent, and the absorption properties can vary further when anchored on a TiO2 film.49,188,222 In liquid electrolyte DSSC devices, the dyes are deposited on a TiO2 film while immersed in the solvent-based electrolyte. This solvent is most commonly acetonitrile, but other pure solvents and mixtures have also been reported from time to time.85,249 To get the most accurate estimate for the actual absorption onset of the dyes under DSSC operation conditions, we have based our calculations on the absorption onset of the IPCE spectra. Owing to the transmission properties of FTO glass, IPCE measurements rarely exceed 85%. As this method assumes an IPCE of 100% and the average maximum IPCE for the dataset is 70%, a sufficiently large margin has been added not to erroneously exclude valid measurements.

The reported short-circuit current density values have been compared to two calculated values. Method A: the reported bandgap (E0â€“0) or absorption onset in solution is used to calculate the JSC,max. Method B: the IPCE onset is used to calculate the JSC,max. If the reported value is found to exceed both calculated values, the dye properties reported in the particular paper will not be included in the statistical basis for this review. One may also include the maximum IPCE value in the calculation, or even integrate the entire IPCE curve of all the dyes to get an accurate estimate of the IPCE-based JSC. However, as the IPCE and Jâ€“V measurements are conceptually different measurements, errors in the IPCE intensity should also be expected. The IPCE onset, on the other hand, should not be affected by errors in intensity, and we believe this to be a more trustworthy measure for assessing the validity of photovoltaic efficiency claims across a wide range of scientific accounts.

Fourteen of the papers have individual dyes failing both methods A and B, and consequently all 39 dyes reported in these papers have been excluded from the statistical basis for this review paper.74,75,81,82,105,111,116,130,146â€“148,203,204,207 Among the top 17 highest PCE phenothiazine dyes, ten dyes have been removed. These include the dyes in the 2015 paper from Lin et al.74 and 2017 paper from Nagarajan et al.75 This is especially worrying as these papers report two of the most efficient dyes to date for the phenothiazine dye class.

Results and discussion

Development of phenothiazine dyes for DSSC

The average photovoltaic performance for phenothiazine dyes has barely developed since the first report by Tian et al. in 2007, as visualized by the red dataset in Fig. 7. In this period, the average PCE has increased from 5.15% to 5.44% in 2020. It should be noted that the best efficiencies have improved at a higher rate than the averages. Looking at the top 1/3 performing dyes from each year, the blue dataset in Fig. 7 shows an increase to 8.04% PCE, and the yellow dataset are the highest PCE reports from each year. In comparison, the NREL DSSC efficiency record has increased from 11.1%250 in 2006 to 13.0% in 2020. A portion of the development is likely attributed to general improvements in materials and fabrication techniques. However, to distinguish this from the overall development of the phenothiazine dyes is difficult. Fig. 7 further suggests that, from 2007 until 2015, the performance of phenothiazine dyes was increasing, and since then no reports of higher efficiency devices have been verified, and the average and top 1/3 graphs reflect this stagnating development. This observation further spurred our desire to answer the main questions motivating this review.

When reviewing a field like this, a range of notations and researcher-specific preferences, units and standards are encountered, likely a natural consequence of the variety of backgrounds for researchers in the DSSC field. This introduces some challenges, especially when reviewing the electrochemical properties of dye molecules, as there are so many different reference electrodes, internal standards and solvent systems. Furthermore, electrochemical data are also reported differently depending on the field of research, and for DSSC research all manners of electrochemical scales are being used. The standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) is the preferred reference electrode by IUPAC, but the normal hydrogen electrode (NHE), standard calomel electrode (SCE) and eV vs. vacuum are still in use. When trying to compare the electrochemical data across different notations, one immediately runs into difficulties. In the data collection stages of this review, any electrochemical data reported vs. SHE, NHE or SCE were left as is, while data reported vs. vacuum was converted to V vs. NHE with a correction factor of âˆ’4.44 eV corresponding to 0.0 V.251 The differences in the other standard electrodes are small and thus assumed to average out over the data set.

With regards to the photophysical properties, an inherent uncertainty is always associated with the comparison of dyes measured in different solvents. It is well known that the solvent has a profound effect on the photophysical properties of metal-free dyes.188 In contrast to the electrochemical data, correction factors are of little use when it comes to these properties and consequently, the data has to be used as reported in the original papers.

The same issue of uncertainty arises when looking into the photovoltaic properties of the dyes. Apart from works published from the same research groups, the photovoltaic performance is in general impossible to directly compare due to differences in fabrication methods and materials. Another important point to address is the sample size of individual studies. On average, only three dyes are investigated per paper in the field of phenothiazine dyes for DSSCs. Paired with the large variations from paper to paper in photovoltaic performance, it is a tough task navigating the field and looking for overall trends. Hence, albeit with a significant uncertainty due to differences in reporting data, the review of an entire class of dyes is the only meaningful way to look for the performance trends related to molecular traits in DSSC dyes.

Photophysical properties

The overall average value of the absorption maxima (Î»max) in solution was 465 nm, while the average on TiO2 films was 453 nm, suggesting the anchoring of phenothiazine dyes on TiO2 is generally associated with a blueshift in absorption maxima. Recently, Venkatraman et al. came to the same conclusion for a smaller set of phenothiazine dyes.252 However, Fig. 9(a) also suggests the shifting in absorption varies greatly depending on the geometry of the dyes. Bathochromic and hypsochromic shifts upon anchoring on TiO2 are generally associated with J- and H-aggregates, respectively.253 As the most notable exception to the general trend, the 10H-PTZ dyes display a significant redshift in absorption maxima of 37 nm upon anchoring on TiO2. A possible explanation of this phenomenon could be the fact that when the 10H-PTZ dyes are anchored on the metal oxide surface, due to the T-shape of the dyes, the phenothiazine moieties are still free to rotate and arrange linearly end-to-end, allowing J-aggregates to form. Meanwhile, for the other geometries, with the assumption of a perfect monomolecular dye layer, only H-aggregates may form on the TiO2 surface.

Electrochemical properties

The average oxidation potential of phenothiazine dyes is 0.92 V vs. reference. The geometry Dâ€“Ï€â€“A (0.86 V vs. ref.) has the lowest and Dâ€“A (1.02 V vs. ref.) the highest. A range of reference electrodes have been included in the average, but the normal hydrogen electrode (NHE) is the most frequently used. Comparing the average Eox to the redox potential of the iodide/triiodide electrolyte (0.40 V vs. NHE)254 gives an average driving force for dye regeneration of 520 mV, which is more than enough.

The tuneable ligand-dependent redox potentials of copper electrolytes means a straight comparison is challenging, but redox potentials can at least range from 0.87 to 0.97 V vs. SHE for copper electrolytes with different ligands.255 This would give driving forces of 50 mV through to a negative driving force of âˆ’50 mV. Negative driving forces will not work, but even driving forces as low as 50 mV is too low for efficient regeneration, making copper electrolytes predominantly incompatible with phenothiazine dyes in DSSCs. The authors have not found any examples of phenothiazine-based DSSC with copper electrolytes in DSSC.

Cobalt electrolytes have less positive redox potentials compared to copper. One report of a range of ligand systems by Feldt et al. lists redox potentials from 0.43 to 0.85 V vs. NHE, and simultaneously identifying a driving force of 390 mV to be sufficient to achieve 80% IPCE.256 Assuming a similar driving force is necessary for phenothiazine dyes with Co electrolytes, only the complexes with the lowest oxidation potential will be compatible. Compared to the iodide/triiodide electrolyte, this will yield slightly elevated VOC and possibly higher PCE. A few accounts of cobalt electrolytes with phenothiazine-based dyes exist.61,86â€“90 However, due to the comparatively low rate of diffusion of cobalt electrolytes, thinner TiO2 films are needed, and extinction coefficients could become a limiting factor. Compared to state-of-art sensitizers such as Y123257 (49000 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) and R7258 (85500 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1), the average extinction coefficient of phenothiazine dyes of 29600 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1 is not competitive.

Effect of auxiliary donors

Auxiliary donors, an additional donor moiety on the non-anchoring side of the phenothiazine scaffold, are responsible for modest improvements in the PCE. This is the case for the comparisons of both the Dâ€“A to Dâ€“Dâ€“A and Dâ€“Ï€â€“A to Dâ€“Dâ€“Ï€â€“A geometries. This is an interesting find, as the phenothiazine scaffold is bent and contains a partial conjugation break along the Sâ€“N axis. An explanation of this observation could be related to the inductive nature of the auxiliary donor moieties, which is increasing the electron density of the phenothiazine scaffold. In the papers where the effect of auxiliary donors has been measured directly against the equivalent non-auxiliary donor dye, the findings are in general agreement with the findings reported here.129,133,200,263,264 By looking at the average oxidation potentials of the Dâ€“A and Dâ€“Dâ€“A geometries, we observe the auxiliary donor lowers the oxidation potential. This suggests that auxiliary donors increase the electron density of the phenothiazine scaffold.265

To investigate the effect of different auxiliary donors, we have compared the donors from the top and bottom 10% performing dyes from the Dâ€“Dâ€“A geometry, see Fig. 11. In both groups, we find 5- and 6-membered aromatic rings, heterocycles and various anti-aggregation alkyl chains. For the top dyes, all auxiliary donors feature alkyl chains of six or more carbon atoms, while these chains only feature in half of the lowest-performing dyes. As the introduction of anti-aggregation moieties is expected to decrease recombination, this could explain the observed difference in VOC between the Dâ€“A and Dâ€“Dâ€“A geometries. The same reasoning can be applied to the auxiliary donors from the top and bottom 10% of the Dâ€“Dâ€“A dyes. The most efficient sensitizers feature a higher number of longer alkyl chains.

Another striking difference in the two groups is found by comparing the average oxidation potentials. The top 10% performing dyes have oxidation potentials of 0.73 V vs. reference and the average of the lowest 10% was 1.03 V vs. reference. Effect of Ï€-spacers

Another interesting observation is the effect that Ï€-spacers have on the VOC of the devices. In the cases of Dâ€“A vs. Dâ€“Ï€â€“A and Dâ€“Dâ€“A vs. Dâ€“Dâ€“Ï€â€“A, a noticeable drop in the average VOC is observed. According to Table 1, the average loss in open-circuit voltage is 36 mV and 7 mV for the two comparisons, respectively. There are to date twelve reports in the literature where this phenomenon is possible to observe directly from the dyes studied.49,51,123,126,131,133,135,137,142,151,157,159 From these twelve papers, it is possible to extract 23 individual comparisons of dye pairs (with and without Ï€-spacers). Apart from two exceptions (dyes SH-6135 and PTZ-3126) the dyes with Ï€-spacers display significantly lowered VOC compared to the dyes without Ï€-spacers. Fig. 12 shows the distributions as violin- and boxplots, with corresponding average VOC of 684 and 726 mV with and without Ï€-spacers, respectively. The accompanying average power conversion efficiency values further highlight the disadvantage of the Ï€-spacers, as these dyes have an average PCE of 4.82% while the non-Ï€-spacer dyes have an average PCE of 5.35%.
Fig. 12 Violin plots of the cases where direct comparisons of dyes with and without Ï€-spacers are possible. Boxplots are overlaid with averages of 726 mV and 684 mV, respectively.

The VOC observations are very clear, however few of the papers point out the phenomenon and only one paper, from our group, determined a shift in conduction band caused the shift in VOC.151 To explain this phenomenon, we start by determining the ICT transition in phenothiazine dyes is an n to Ï€* transition,266 and it is natural to assign the origin of this transition to the electron lone pair on the phenothiazine scaffold, as this is usually the only lone pair in conjugation with the anchoring group. By comparing two simpler systems, the pKa of acetic acid is 4.75, while that of cyanoacetic acid is 2.45. This is due to the inductive effect from the nitrile group increasing the acidity of the carboxylic acid. Any electron-donating groups will, following the same reasoning, reduce the acidity of the carboxylic acid. When transferring this scenario to phenothiazine dyes, we have the nitrile group attached to the anchoring group providing a strong electron-withdrawing effect along with the carboxylic acid. The donor group, i.e. the 10H-nitrogen of phenothiazine, is providing the opposite effect through the conjugated system. As Ï€-spacers are introduced between the donor and acceptor moieties, the distance is increased and the effect from the donor group is decreased. Consequently, a lowering of the pKa must be expected when introducing a Ï€-spacer with the direct effect of the dye becoming a stronger acid. As increased protonation is known to lower the conduction band edge of the TiO2,267 we consider this a highly plausible explanation of the trends here highlighted for the VOC. RÃ¼hle and co-workers investigated a similar phenomenon observed for metal-complex dyes, basing the explanation on differences in the dipole moments of the dye complexes.268 We see no reason why this phenomenon should be exclusive to phenothiazine-based dyes, and we hope this discussion may give further insight into how to design dye molecules with a wide absorption while maintaining a high VOC. A possible solution is to approach dye structures incorporating donor moieties closer to the anchoring group, keeping the acidity of the anchoring group controlled.

The prevalence of sulphur-containing heterocycles used in Ï€-spacers can also contribute to the lower VOC observed. Quantum calculations have revealed that thiophenes in Ï€-spacers form sulphurâ€“iodine complexes, and these are responsible for increasing iodine concentration near the surface of TiO2.269 This interaction increases in strength with increasing numbers of sulphur atoms in the Ï€-spacer, and facilitates recombination, which lowers the VOC of the devices.91 This claim is supported experimentally where sulphur-rich sensitizers demonstrate inferior VOC compared to sensitizers with lower sulphur-content in Iâˆ’/I3âˆ’ electrolyte, but superior VOC in Co2+/Co3+ electrolyte.269

A similar direct comparison of the Ï€-spacer effect is not possible for the multianchored dyes due to insufficient data, but two publications allow the comparison of one vs. two thiophene units in the Ï€-spacers of the multianchored dyes.219,229 Here, the two findings conflict as to the effect of enlarged Ï€-spacer on the VOC. However, the larger Ï€-spacers generally give higher PCE values by a narrow margin.

Effect of N-aryl vs. N-alkyl substituents

The 10H-position of the phenothiazine scaffold is always substituted by either alkyl chains or an aromatic substituent. Of the dye reports in the geometries Dâ€“A, Dâ€“Ï€â€“A, Dâ€“Dâ€“A, Dâ€“Dâ€“Ï€â€“A and multianchored dyes, 58 featured N-aryl substituents, while 263 had N-alkyl substituents. The average photovoltaic parameters are for N-alkyl (JSC = 10.75 mA cmâˆ’2, VOC = 699 mV, PCE = 5.15%) and for N-aryl (JSC = 12.78 mA cmâˆ’2, VOC = 720 mV, PCE = 6.10%). The more common alkyl-substituted dyes are outperformed in every aspect by the aryl-substituted dyes, as it can be seen from the density plot in Fig. 13(a). The biggest difference is found for the JSC, resulting in a performance gap of nearly 1% PCE. The aryl-substituted dyes also deliver the superior Î»max (473 nm vs. 463 nm), extinction coefficients (30100 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1vs. 26300 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1), IPCE onsets (672 nm vs. 660 nm) and average maximum IPCE value (75% vs. 70%). The effects highlighted for Î»max, extinction coefficients and IPCE onsets all suggest the aryl dyes are the better light-harvesting molecules, resulting in higher JSC and average maximum IPCE values. The effect in VOC can be a result of the aryl moiety providing a more efficient blocking effect of the surface, reducing undesirable recombination of electrons in the TiO2 to oxidized electrolyte species. It could also be a result of the radical cation situated on the nitrogen atom is more stabilized by three aromatic substituents compared to two for alkyl-substituted dyes. As the earliest aryl-substituted dyes were reported in 2012, the overall development in DSSC fabrication materials and techniques may favour these dyes over the alkyl dyes that were introduced five years earlier. As the photophysical properties of the N-aryl dyes are also superior, we feel confident recommending the use of aryl over alkyl substituents.

The data from the N-alkyl dyes have been further extended to look at the effect of chain length, see Fig. 13(b). Six atoms in the longest chain is by far the most frequent with 172 occurrences. The most efficient dyes are also found employing chains of this length, and a noticeable drop in PCE is found for dyes with chains of eight carbons. The alkyl-substituted dyes vastly outnumber the aryl dyes. Many factors are likely contributing to this being the case. Firstly, alkyl chains are extremely common for the synthesis of dyes for DSSCs in general, used for their anti-aggregation properties. Choosing alkyl chains over aryl moieties may come down to avoiding undesirable stacking of aromatic units, which could increase the interactions between adjacent dye molecules. Consequently, many of the dyes here investigated employ alkyl-substituted phenyl substituents in the 10H-position on the phenothiazine scaffold. Secondly, the synthesis of the alkyl-based dyes is considerably less complex. Simple SN2 reactions of phenothiazines with inexpensive alkyl halides are usually sufficient to attach the anti-aggregation moiety in N-alkyl dyes. Meanwhile, for the N-aryl dyes the copper-catalysed Ullmann coupling, palladium-catalysed Buchwald coupling, or a reagent-limited electrophilic aromatic substitution are the most common methods for the introduction of the 10H-moiety.35,71,113,134

Multianchored dyes

The symmetric nature of the phenothiazine scaffold allows easy access to dyes with multiple anchoring groups, and numerous studies have investigated the photovoltaic effects of such dyes. From Table 1 it is clear the multianchored dyes are outperforming all the monoanchored dye geometries. Compared to the most efficient geometry of monoanchored dyes, multianchored dyes have 7.5% higher relative PCE values. Being comparable in every property apart from extinction coefficient, we attribute the higher JSC, and consequently PCE, to the increased extinction coefficient of the multianchored dyes.

To the best of our knowledge, the higher extinction coefficients of multianchored phenothiazine dyes have not been identified nor investigated before. Assuming there is a partial break in the electronic communication along the Sâ€“N axis of the dye in the ground state, the contribution from the donor side of the dye scaffold has to be restricted to some extent. Conversely, if this side of the scaffold is in conjugation with another anchoring group, the resulting dianchored molecule will contain two separate electronic systems, both originating from the phenothiazine nitrogen and both contributing to the ICT transition. This suggestion is supported by the fact that the multianchored dyes do not show vastly red-shifted absorption maxima or IPCE onsets compared to the monoanchored dyes. An unsymmetrical dianchored dye with two distinct ICT transitions can confirm this theory.

In the dataset we have been able to find six papers where meaningful direct comparisons of mono- and dianchored sensitizers could be extracted.114,117,170,174,195,229 In no cases were the monoanchoring dyes found to outperform the dianchoring analogues, which delivered on average 16% higher relative PCE values. While the multianchoring dyes are more efficient, they are also somewhat limited in terms of further modification. As the only reactive handle left on the scaffold is the nitrogen atom, all modification apart from tuning of Ï€-spacers will have to be introduced at this position.

Tethered dyes

The dyes comprising the tethered dyes in this study all consist of separate dye chromophores containing donor and/or anchor groups tethered by flexible linkers. This approach has the desirable effect of significantly increasing the extinction coefficient. However, as the tethers are linking separate electronic systems, the increase in extinction coefficient is only a result of increasing the number of electronic systems per molecule, not improving the light-harvesting properties of the electronic systems. The increased extinction coefficient does not translate into improved JSC, and the efficiencies obtained with these dyes are comparable to the monoanchored geometries.

The use of tethers has previously been used to increase the local concentration of a reducing agent, increasing the rate of dye regeneration and moving the cation further away from the TiO2 surface.37 The authors have successfully used a tethered approach to control the molar ratio of triarylamine dyes and anti-aggregation additive (chenodeoxycholic acid).270 In our opinion, the use of tethers is a wonderful tool to ensure proximity and control the stoichiometric ratios of additives or co-sensitizers, but ineffective in increasing the light harvesting properties of DSSC dyes.

Phenoxazine dyes

Phenoxazine is the structurally closest analogue of phenothiazine, where the sulphur atom of the scaffold is replaced by an oxygen atom. The smaller size of the oxygen atom (0.6 Ã… vs. 1.0 Ã… atomic radii for sulphur)271 means the dihedral angle of phenoxazine is expected to be smaller. While no crystallographic data was found for phenoxazine, an early study on dipole moments by Cullinane and Rees concluded that if there was any folding of the scaffold, it had to be small.272 As the phenoxazine structure is less bent than that of phenothiazine, it is expected that it should have more red-shifted absorption and lower reorganizational energy upon excitation. The first phenoxazine dyes for DSSC were reported in 2009 by Tian et al.83 Since then, nineteen papers reporting 46 phenoxazine dyes were found in the literature when applying the same limitations used for the phenothiazine data set. The scarcity of these dyes is most likely due to the lower availability of low-cost starting materials. Considering the conventional monoanchoring geometries, the Dâ€“Dâ€“A geometry is superior to the others and the 10H-PTZ geometry is the least efficient by far. As Dâ€“Dâ€“A dyes outperform the Dâ€“Ï€â€“A dyes, we conclude the performance enhancement from auxiliary donors is greater than that of Ï€-spacers. This is a bold interpretation, as Ï€-spacers are frequently attributed with the virtues of improving the absorption properties and increasing the efficiency of the devices.

Another repeatedly mentioned feature of phenothiazines is their claimed anti-aggregation properties, linked to the flapping motion of the scaffold. We have no evidence opposing this, but we do want to highlight the reorganizational energy cost associated with this motion. The required energy is subtracted from the exciton energy and is thus an inherent limitation for the phenothiazine scaffold, although we do not believe this phenomenon to be exclusive to phenothiazine dyes.

While reviewing the properties of phenothiazine dyes, we have presented several inherent limitations for the phenothiazine scaffold. The most prominent is the incompatibility of the oxidation potentials with novel electrolytes. This effectively excludes phenothiazines as a main scaffold for low-light and high-efficiency (>10% PCE) sensitizers. However, the strong electron donating abilities of this moiety can be a great asset if used as an auxiliary donor on other chromophores. Also, we firmly believe that phenothiazine in the future can play a role as main energy harvesters in other applications where the molecular properties of phenothiazine are favourable.

Conclusion

In this review, we have collected all photophysical, electrochemical and photovoltaic data from 372 phenothiazine dyes (431 reports) and 40 phenoxazine dyes (46 reports). The dyes were grouped according to their basic geometry, and the credibility of the photovoltaic data was assessed by comparing every dye report to the calculated theoretical maximum short-circuit current density expected for each dye. This resulted in the removal of 39 dye reports from the dataset.

Using the large dataset, we have looked for answers to questions asked by researchers in the field of phenothiazine dyes for DSSCs, such as the effect of Ï€-spacers, auxiliary donors, the best overall dye geometry, alkyl vs. aryl substituents, anti-aggregation chain lengths and molecular weights. A brief summary of findings based on our review of the complete class of phenothiazine dyes is:

â€¢ The most efficient geometry is multianchored dyes.

â€¢ N-aryl substituents are more efficient than N-alkyl.

â€¢ The most efficient anti-aggregation chain length has six atoms in the longest chain.

â€¢ Auxiliary donors contribute more than Ï€-spacers.

â€¢ Molecular weight ideally below 1000 g molâˆ’1.

â€¢ Iodide/triiodide is the most suitable redox couple.

â€¢ Phenoxazine scaffold looks more promising than phenothiazine.

To accelerate the learning and improve the credibility and reliability of the photovoltaic data in the DSSC field, we encourage the following guidelines of best practice with regards to experimental design and reporting. (i) Include IPCE measurements and validate the photocurrent density based on the integration of said spectra. (ii) Base conclusions on photovoltaic data gathered within the study in question. For complex systems with many variables, a design of experiment (DoE) approach is encouraged. (iii) Include known and easily available reference sensitizers as benchmarks. Even though it is not required for the conclusion of the paper in question, it may still be of use to other researchersâ€™ future comparisons.

Among the reports excluded from the data set were several previous record dyes. The consequence of removing these is a less optimistic, albeit more realistic view of phenothiazine compared to other dye scaffolds. We feel the honest research is brought back into the light, although phenothiazine as a chromophore may appear less attractive. The most efficient phenothiazine dye is currently YL4, a multianchored dye from Li et al. with an impressive PCE of 9.8%.219 Among the more common monoanchoring geometries, the most efficient dye is 6d from Wang et al. with a PCE of 8.9%.179 Phenothiazine, when used as an auxiliary donor in a ruthenium sensitizer, has reached a PCE of 10.4%, for the dye SCZ-1 reported by She et al.101

Many of the latest achievements in DSSC performance are related to increases in VOC by employing copper and cobalt electrolytes. We have here established that the oxidation potentials of phenothiazine dyes are generally not compatible with novel copper-based electrolytes, and the comparatively low extinction coefficients do not favour the slow diffusing cobalt-based electrolytes. The reorganizational energy cost associated with the butterfly flapping motion is a further inherent limitation for phenothiazine dyes.

There is great science being performed in the field of phenothiazine dyes, and we hope this review can be of aid to future researchers navigating this field. It is the opinion of the authors that, although the phenothiazine scaffold is easily modified and the dyes are stable and reliable, it is a tricky system to understand and difficult to improve. While phenoxazine appears to be a slightly more efficient scaffold, we believe the two heterocycles are so similar that they are likely to share weaknesses and limitations as well.

In summary, phenothiazine dyes appear to have significant limitations in terms of absorption, extinction coefficients and DSSC electrolyte compatibility. We do however see no reason why the donating powers of phenothiazine cannot be of great use as auxiliary donors in other chromophores. As the focus of the DSSC field is currently shifting towards powering applications for the internet of things in indoor ambient illumination, and exciting new research fields continuously spring up around DSSCs, we have no doubt phenothiazine in some shape or form will be part of this exciting development in the future.
