High pressure CO2 photoreduction using Au/TiO2: unravelling the effect of co-catalysts and of titania polymorphs

Abstract

A series of Au/TiO2 based catalysts with low gold loading (0.1–0.5 wt%) were prepared by a modified deposition–precipitation method and their activity was tested for CO2 photoreduction in the liquid phase at high pressure (7 bar). Methanol, formic acid and formaldehyde were detected as major products in the liquid phase, whilst gas phase products, CO, CH4 and H2, were produced at different concentrations depending on the catalyst formulation. The deposition of small Au nanoparticles (3–5 nm) (NPs) onto TiO2 was found to quantitatively influence the product distribution and the productivities of various products. Characterization techniques, such as DRIFTS, XRD and TEM/HAADF, were used to correlate the photocatalyst features with the photoactivity obtained. Methanol productivities of 1360 and 1350 mmol kgcat−1 h−1 were attained for 0.2 wt% Au/TiO2 and 0.5 wt% Au/TiO2, respectively, which are impressive results with respect to the literature. However, the highest loaded sample revealed simultaneously the highest H2 and CH4 productivities. By contrast, lower activity was obtained using anatase and rutile pure phases, with respect to the mixed phase P25 commercial sample, confirming the key role of interfacial defects between borders of different crystalline phases.

1 Introduction

The Earth's surface temperature has continuously risen in the past century, with particularly significant warming trends over the past two decades.1 CO2 emissions from fossil sources are believed to be the primary anthropogenic contributor to this phenomenon. The reduction of CO2 emissions can be achieved by three approaches: i) efficient use of carbon-based energy sources (energy efficiency concept); ii) use of alternative or carbon-free energy sources (e.g. hydrogen obtained by wind/solar energy); iii) post-treatment processes. The last option involves the carbon-capture and storage technologies (CCS) and the further CO2 conversion to chemicals or fuels.2 CO2 utilisation can be a turning point, able to change the image of carbon dioxide from a critical waste with a cost of disposal, to a resource and a business opportunity, with also benefits in terms of positive image for companies. The post-treatment conversion of carbon dioxide can be performed on high purity gas streams from current and planned CSS plants or directly at the outlet of the emission source, especially where the CSS option is unsuitable (due to the distance from safe sequestration sites or diluted concentration of CO2 in the emitting gas or small–medium size sources).

CO2 is a relatively inert and stable compound, therefore its reduction is very challenging. In particular, the CO2 reduction by H2O to form hydrocarbons is an uphill, strongly endothermic process, requiring a considerable amount of energy:3

CO2 + 2H2O → CH3OH + 3/2O2 (ΔG0 = + 702.2 kJ mol−1)

CO2 + 2H2O → CH4 + 2O2 (ΔG0 = + 818.3 kJ mol−1)

The recent innovations in photocatalytic technologies have introduced CO2 photo-conversion to fuels and chemicals as a tangible future alternative.4–6 Direct conversion of CO2 under light irradiation is possible only using artificial light because of the absorption below 200 nm in the so-called deep UV region.7 However, using a photo-catalyst, UV and solar sources can be used under milder conditions, as recently reviewed.8,9 The photo-catalyst plays a crucial role because of the generation of active charged species (electrons and holes) upon light absorption.10 These species are very active and can convert the chemical compounds adsorbed on the material or lying around it. Among the various semiconductors used as photocatalysts, TiO2 is widely used because of its low toxicity, low cost and photo-corrosion resistance.

Titania has three polymorph forms: anatase, rutile and brookite. Among these, anatase is the most photocatalytically active form, though not for all applications.11 It also has a slightly larger band gap compared to rutile and brookite (3.2, 3.0 and 3.1 eV, respectively). However, there is no univocal interpretation on the activity of the different crystal phases. The role of the TiO2 structure in photocatalysis has been recently reviewed12,13 highlighting the effect of crystal phases and co-catalysts.

The drawbacks of bare TiO2 are the high electron–hole recombination rate and the limited absorption in the visible region. An attractive method to improve these limitations is the modification of TiO2 by means of non-metal elements or deposition of surface metallic nanoparticles.14 The influence of the promoter or co-catalyst is very different in these two cases. Doping involves the incorporation of atoms or ions in the crystalline oxide lattice, although the real location of the hetero-atoms is not always clear.15 For instance, Olivo et al. designed several N doped CuO–TiO2 photocatalysts studying the co-promotion effect on the photoreduction process.16 C-Doping and the development of heterojunctions is another important strategy.17–21

On the other hand, deposition of metallic nanoparticles, such as Pt,22 Pd,14 Ag,23 Au,24 and Co,25 onto the TiO2 surface could enhance the photocatalytic activity by suppressing significantly the electron–hole recombination due to the formation of a Schottky barrier at the interface between the metal and support. Since the TiO2 conduction band is essentially formed by the overlapping of Ti 3d orbitals, the presence of the metal introduces an empty d orbital below the conduction band edge causing the effective band gap reduction and acting as the charge carrier trap.

In our previous investigations, we have described a new photoreactor concept which can operate at up to 20 bar.26–28 We have demonstrated that the photoreduction of CO2 in the gas phase is less productive than that in three-phase gas/liquid/solid configuration16,29 and that the increase of CO2 pressure can increase its solubility and adsorption over the catalyst, also allowing a considerable increase of the working temperature.27,28,30 The role of pH has been also addressed, leading to two different reaction pathways, summarized in the following.30

In this work, a modified deposition–precipitation method was selected for the preparation of photocatalysts for the reduction of CO2. The method involves the use of urea as the basic agent and NaBH4 as the chemical reductant in contrast to the traditional high-temperature reduction step. In this way, highly dispersed gold nanoparticles can be obtained at very low metal loading, in a metallic form with a partial negative charge.31 Low metal loading (0.1–0.5 wt%) was chosen to avoid excessive surface coverage and relative inhibition of substrate adsorption, but maintaining visible light absorption properties and enhanced electron–hole separation after the photo-generation.

The materials were fully characterized32 and here tested for the CO2 photo-reduction in the liquid phase at high pressure (7 bar or higher). This represents a new, unconventional approach to the reaction, with a substantial improvement of the productivity towards both liquid and gas phase products with respect to conventional ambient conditions. We have previously demonstrated that pressure higher than ambient conditions allows a substantial improvement of CO2 solubility and permits the operation at higher temperature than conventional one.29,33–35 Much higher productivity has been reported by even using a bare TiO2 photocatalyst for this application, which may represent an important step towards the commercialization of this technology.36–38

This high pressure photoreactor was successfully tested and the CO2 photoreaction mechanism was determined over the un-promoted TiO2 P25 catalyst. The present results shed light on the beneficial effect of Au nanoparticles in boosting the productivity to methanol and other regenerated fuels and chemicals. Indeed, Au nanoparticles are already considered as very active co-catalysts for this application. We here determined their application under unconventional reaction conditions to boost the productivity of all the detected products.

2 Experimental

2.1 Preparation of Au/TiO2

Gold nanoparticles were prepared by a modified deposition–precipitation method using urea and a chemical reductant (DP-UC). 1 g of commercial TiO2 (Evonik P25, 50 m2 g−1) was dispersed in distilled water (100 ml) with 5 g of urea (Aldrich, >99%). NaAuCl4·2H2O solution (Aldrich, 99.99%) was added to the support and left under vigorous stirring for 4 h at 353 K. The catalyst was filtered and washed several times with water. The material was then suspended in distilled water and a freshly prepared solution of 0.1 M NaBH4 (Fluka, >96%) was added (NaBH4/Au = 4 mol/mol) under vigorous stirring at room temperature. The sample was filtered, washed and dried at 373 K for 4 h. The actual Au loading was checked by atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) analysis of the filtrate, on a Perkin Elmer 3100 instrument. As expected, using this method,39 all the gold present in the solution was deposited on the support. The nominal composition was also confirmed by EDX analysis. Au/P25 catalysts with the following composition were prepared: 0.1 wt%, 0.2 wt%, and 0.5 wt% Au/P25. Bare P25 was used as the comparative reference. Using the same methodology, 0.5 wt% Au/rutile (rutile, Aldrich, 99.99% purity) and 0.5 wt%/anatase (anatase, Aldrich, 99.80% purity) were prepared.

2.2 Characterization

DRIFTS studies were carried out with a Bruker Tensor 27 spectrometer fitted with a HgCdTe (MCT) detector and a Harrick Praying Mantis HVC-DRP-4 cell equipped with two ZnSe windows, and operated with OPUS software. The DRIFTS cell included gas inlet and outlet ports with capabilities for heating and cooling. The required gas flow, 40 cm3 min−1 of a 5% CO/N2 gas mixture, was controlled by mass-flow controllers. The data are reported in the form of absorbance. Each spectrum represents an average of 64 scans collected with a spectral resolution of 2 cm−1. The ZnSe windows in use cut off the spectrum below 650 cm−1 therefore this region was not included in the discussion. In the reported spectra, a positive increase of peak intensity indicates an increase of population of that species, whereas a negative deflection shows loss of moieties associated with that particular mode. All samples were ground before the analysis. Background subtraction and normalization of the spectra were performed by subtracting (deducing) the spectra recorded under N2 flow (40 cm3 min−1) after heating the sample at 393 K for 30 min, to eliminate physisorbed and chemisorbed moisture.

Specific surface area and pore size distribution were evaluated through the collection of N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms at 77 K on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 instrument. Surface area was calculated on the basis of the Brunauer, Emmett and Teller equation (BET), whereas the pore size distribution was determined by the BJH method, applied to the N2 desorption branch of the isotherm. Prior to the analysis, the samples were outgassed at 573 K for 24 hours.

The specimens for transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were prepared by dispersing the catalyst powder on TEM grids coated with a holey carbon film. They were examined by means of an FEI Titan 80–300 electron microscope equipped with a CEOS image spherical aberration corrector and Fischione model 3000 high angle annular dark field (HAADF) scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) detector.

X-ray diffraction (XRD) experiments were performed on a Rigaku D III-MAX horizontal-scan powder diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation, equipped with a graphite monochromator on the diffracted beam. The crystallite size was estimated from the peak half width by using the Scherrer equation with corrections for instrumental line broadening (β = 0.9).

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was carried out with a Kratos (Manchester, M17 1GP, UK) Axis Ultra-DLD instrument with a monochromatic Al Kα X-ray source (144 W, 12 mA, 12 kV). High resolution and survey scans were performed at pass energies of 40 and 160 eV, respectively. Spectra were calibrated to the C (1s) signal at 284.8 eV and quantified using CasaXPS and a modified Wagner sensitivity factors supplied by the manufacturer.

2.3 Photo-catalytic testing: CO2 reduction

The innovative photoreactor used for activity testing has been described in detail elsewhere.33,34 Briefly, the photo-reactor is made of AISI 316 stainless steel. The lamp is introduced vertically in the reactor axis and a magnetic stirrer ensures proper liquid mixing. The internal capacity is ca. 1.3 L, filled with ca. 1.2 L solution. The temperature is kept constant through a double-walled thermostatic system.

As the source of radiation, we used a medium-pressure Hg vapor lamp with a range of emission wavelengths from 254 nm to 364 nm (this latter was the main peak). The emitted power was periodically measured by means of a photo-radiometer (Delta OHM HD2102.2) at 315–400 nm. At the bottom of the quartz candle, where most of the suspension is located, the power was 45 W m−2 at 13.5 cm from the side of the lamp and 77 W m−2 at 6 cm under the bulb. The harmful overheat of the lamp bulb was avoided thanks to an air circulation system. The irradiation power depends on the flow rate of the cooling pressurized air, therefore a compromise between the high irradiation power and high lamp lifetime was chosen and corresponded to 1000 L h−1.

The catalyst, ca. 0.6 g, has been suspended in demineralized and outgassed water (ca. 1.2 L). Catalyst concentration was chosen in accordance with our previous studies,33,34 in order to avoid diffusional limitation and scattering related to an excessive solid amount. The suspension has been saturated with CO2 at 7 bar pressure overnight before starting irradiation. The pH was neutral, unless specified differently. Testing was carried out over 24 h of continuous irradiation at 7 bar pressure and 80 °C (conditions previously optimized33,34). The temperature was controlled thanks to a thermosetting bath with circulating fluid and approximately the same under dark conditions.

Na2SO3 (ca. 0.85 g L−1) has been used as the hole scavenger and its consumption at the end of the reaction (24 h) was evaluated by standard iodometric titration. Sulphite conversion ranged from 78 to 94% depending on the test. Negligible productivity both in liquid and gas phases has been observed without sulphite addition. We have underlined in a previous work that the hole scavenger allows the accumulation of organic products in the liquid phase, whereas after its complete consumption, gas phase products (CO and H2) start forming by photoreforming of the formed organic species.34

After the reaction, the liquid mixture has been analysed by means of a HPLC (Agilent 1220 Infinity, with a column Alltech OA-10308, 300 mm × 7.8 mm), equipped with both UV and refractive index (Agilent 1260 Infinity) detectors. Aqueous H3PO4 solution (0.1 wt%) was used as the eluent. This analysis allowed HCOOH, HCHO and CH3OH to be quantified. HCHO was also quantified in parallel by UV-vis spectrophotometry through the Nash reactant procedure. The gas phase accumulated in the headspace of the photoreactor has been analysed using a gas chromatograph (Agilent 7890) equipped with HP Plot Q and MS columns and a TCD detector. The set-up was suitable for the quantification of H2, CH4 and polar/non polar light gases. Samples of 20 cm3 of gas were collected through a gas syringe and injected in the sampling loop of the gas chromatograph.

Appropriate blank tests have been carried out to ensure the absence of any significant productivity i) under dark conditions, ii) in the absence of the catalyst, and iii) in the absence of the hole scavenger. A specific blank test in the absence of CO2 will be extensively discussed in the following to demonstrate the productivity of organic species by CO2 rather than from possible organic species contaminating the catalyst or the reaction environment, as often criticized. The same pressure of 7 bar was set with N2 instead of CO2 after careful outgassing, and the other reaction conditions such as the catalyst, hole scavenger, pH and irradiation were the same.

3 Results and discussion

3.1 Morphological, structural and textural characterisation of Au/TiO2 photocatalysts

Au catalysts have been prepared by a deposition precipitation method with urea and using NaBH4 as the reducing agent40 using TiO2 as the support (P25, rutile and anatase). For P25, Au/TiO2 samples with different loadings (0.1, 0.2, and 0.5 wt%) have been prepared, whereas for rutile and anatase, a 0.5 wt% Au loading has been considered only. Fig. 1 shows the different polymorphs of TiO2 in the 0.5 wt% Au samples. Au reflections have never been observed due to the very low Au loading and the small gold nanoparticle size. 0.5 wt% Au/rutile was predominantly composed of rutile (96% rutile, 4% anatase), 0.5 wt% Au/anatase of anatase (97% anatase, 3% rutile) and 0.5 wt% Au/P25 of a mixture of anatase (78%) and rutile (22%). Crystallite size was calculated with XRD using the Scherrer equation. P25 TiO2 was characterized by its small crystal size (∼15 nm), much smaller than rutile (∼83 nm) and anatase (∼92 nm). These results are in good agreement with the values previously reported for 0.1 wt% Au/TiO2.33

3.2 XPS and DRIFTS

The charge of gold plays an important role in the process, affecting the charge trapping efficiency, migration, recombination and interfacial charge transfer.41 The oxidation state of gold has been studied by XPS, which confirmed the surface composition of the samples and the presence of metallic Au both in the fresh and the spent catalysts.32 CO-DRIFTS adsorption analyses were also carried out to further elucidate this point (Fig. 4). This technique is very powerful for the detection of metal dispersion and oxidation state.42,43 For all the Au/P25 fresh samples the adsorption of CO gave rise to one or two IR bands in the CO carbonyl spectral region (1950–2150 cm−1). The highest frequency IR band (2106 cm−1) was attributed to linearly adsorbed CO on metallic gold and is strictly dependent on the gold nanoparticle diameter. Therefore, with increasing the wt% of Au loading on P25, the intensity of this peak increased (Fig. 4).44 The broad and asymmetric band at lower frequencies with the maximum located at 2040–2072 cm−1 was attributed to the linearly, multisite and bridged CO on Auδ−.44 The observation of this band is mainly due to the electron transfer from the reducible titania support to small gold nanoparticles or clusters.44
Fig. 4 DRIFTS spectra of the fresh samples using CO as the probe molecule.

The intensity of both bands increased with Au loading. Anatase and, most of all, rutile materials revealed a markedly higher amount of partially negative gold particles, with respect to 0.5 wt% Au/TiO2 P25, for which the intensity of the two bands was similar. This could be correlated with the larger titania crystal size characterizing the rutile and anatase samples, as detected in XRD analysis.

Furthermore, XPS analysis is in line with these data. At first, it confirms partially negative charge on Au particles (binding energy ca. 82.9 eV for the present samples with respect to standard values of 84 eV for Au0). Furthermore, Fig. 5 shows a lower surface exposure of Au (per unit surface) for 0.5 wt% Au/TiO2 P25 with respect to the other two TiO2 samples. This again agrees with the higher amount of negatively charged Au detected by CO-DRIFTS analysis.


Fig. 5 XPS spectra of the fresh samples with 0.5 wt% Au over different TiO2.

CO2 adsorption on P25 gives rise to several IR bands in the range of 1800–1000 cm−1.32 Three main peaks at 1312, 1416 and 1577 cm−1, with a shoulder at higher frequency, respectively, assigned to CO2 bent, bicarbonate HCO3− and bidentate carbonate (b-CO32−) species were detected. The nature of the carbonate form present is related to the titania crystalline form. The role of water has been studied by performing the experiment under dry and wet conditions. In the presence of water vapour, a marked increase of the relative intensity of the band at 1312 and 1675 cm−1 was detected (i.e. peak height subtracted by the baseline of the spectra) suggesting that H2O promotes the formation of the CO2 radical anion, with a synergistic effect for CO2 photoreduction. For the gold sample, a further band at 1199 cm−1, associated with a particular bicarbonate, was present. Moreover, the greater surface area of P25 with respect to anatase and rutile should be considered. In fact, over P25, Au particles are more dispersed and isolated thus promoting the linear adsorption of CO over the small gold particles. In contrast, over the other samples, with a lower surface area, the gold nanoparticles are less dispersed favouring the formation of bridged CO adsorbed species.

3.3 Structure–activity correlation for CO2 photo-reduction

Preliminary blank tests were carried out to enhance the effect of:

i) Irradiation. A test under dark conditions allowed non-photoinduced effects to be excluded, since the productivity was below the detection limits for all the products.

ii) Photocatalyst. A blank test without the photocatalyst, under irradiation, returned negligible productivity for all products.

iii) Hole scavenger. Insignificant amount of products (3 orders of magnitude lower productivities) was observed in the absence of the hole scavenger.

iv) Possible organic impurities. In order to compare the reactivity towards hydrogen production through direct water splitting and CO2 photoreduction, a test at 7 bar of nitrogen was compared with the ones in a CO2 atmosphere. In the presence of nitrogen, the main use of the photo-generated electrons in the CB, besides recombination, is the reduction of H+ to H2. The test with nitrogen confirmed this result, with an unexpectedly high productivity of ca. 350 mmol H2 kgcat−1 h−1 at 7 bar pressure, but no productivity to HCOOH, HCHO and CH3OH by using the 0.1 wt% Au/TiO2 catalyst. H2 production comes from the water splitting, aided by the addition of the inorganic hole scavenger and is, as it will be evident in the following, much more effective than in the presence of CO2 due to competitive reaction of the promoted electron for the reduction of H+ and CO2 when the latter is present. This test definitely demonstrates that the organic products are due to the photoreduction of CO2 and have no correlation with possible organic impurities present on the catalyst (e.g. organic residua from the preparation) or in the apparatus.

3.3.1 Effect of the gold-loading. Temperature, pressure and pH are very critical parameters for the CO2 photo-reduction processes.1 A comprehensive study of the high pressure CO2 photo-conversion was carried out in one of our previous papers in order to shed light on the complex reaction pathways towards both liquid and gas phase photo-reduction products.34 In this case, TiO2-P25 was chosen as the reference material and product distribution was compared at different reaction times and under pH conditions. The formation of formaldehyde was independent from that of formic acid and constituted the major product during tests at shorter reaction times (3–16 h). By contrast, at longer reaction times (24–72 h), only gas-phase products were present (H2 and CO) due to the consecutive step of photo-reforming (Fig. 7).
Fig. 7 Evolution of the various products vs. reaction time for TiO2 P25 at 7 bar, 80 °C. Productivity expressed as mmol kgcat−1 h−1 for HCOOH and HCHO (left axis) and for H2 (right axis). The conversion (%) of the hole scavenger (Na2SO3) is also reported (right axis).34

No methanol or methane was detected in the case of bare TiO2. Two parallel reaction pathways were observed. The first one involved the photoreduction of molecular carbon dioxide to formic acid, which may reduce further to formaldehyde and methanol in a strictly consecutive path or evolve to gas phase products by photoreforming, i.e. the formation of H2 + CO/CO2 by oxidation of the accumulated organic molecules (HCOOH, HCHO, and CH3OH) through the photogenerated holes. This latter route has been already demonstrated by us30 observing the H2/CO formation only after all the sulphite HS has been consumed, with a simultaneous decrease of the organics concentration in the liquid phase. The latter reaction occurs only when the hole scavenger is fully consumed, so that the valence band holes oxidise the organic species accumulated in the liquid phase, while conduction band electrons are used to reduce protons to molecular hydrogen. The second mechanism implies the reduction of carbonates to give formaldehyde and possibly methanol in a further consecutive step. Also in this case, photoreforming, leading to the production of H2 and CO at the expense of the organic products accumulated in the liquid, is a consecutive step after consumption of the hole scavenger. The results led to the conclusion that at basic pH (12–14), the mechanism is tightly related to the conversion of CO2 in carbonate while at neutral pH, two hypotheses can be formulated: a) the mechanism is the 2nd one, but running at a lower rate (low carbonate concentration); b) the first path is preferred.

The addition of a metal to TiO2 decreases the band gap of this material and improves visible light absorption and the lifetime of the photogenerated charges. For instance, the comparison of the DR-UV spectra of a sample of TiO2 P25 and a 0.2 wt% Au loaded sample revealed that the absorbance curve for the Au containing sample was shifted towards larger wavelengths and the corresponding Tauc plot resulted in a band gap calculation decreasing from 3.36 to 3.17 eV after gold loading.45 Furthermore, Au addition modifies the potential of electrons,33,34 thus possibly modifying the product distribution. Based on our previous work,33,45 the use of various TiO2 polymorphs and Au addition effectively tuned the band gap of this material from 3.36 eV (bare P25 TiO2) to 3.08, 3.24 and 3.02 eV for Au/P25, Au/anatase and Au/rutile, respectively.

Testing was here carried out at neutral pH, which is more interesting from an applicative point of view. The results of activity testing at 7 bar, 80 °C, and 24 h reaction time with Au/TiO2 P25 at variable metal loading are shown in Fig. 8.


Fig. 8 Liquid phase productivities at variable Au loading (T = 80 °C, p = 7 bar, pH = 7).

Methanol was the main product in the liquid phase, demonstrating the higher reactivity of the present Au-loaded samples with respect to the bare semiconductor. It should be also interestingly noticed that methanol is not formed after 24 h at pH = 14. By looking at the relative product amounts, the intermediate concentration of formaldehyde confirms the validity of the reaction scheme proposed in ref. 34. The highest methanol productivity was achieved with a Au loading of 0.2 with a slightly higher yield of the less desirable products (formic acid and formaldehyde).

The samples showed promising activity also for the formation of high valued gas phase products, i.e. H2, CO and methane. Methane is produced by direct photoreduction of CO2. It was never observed for bare titania photocatalysts, whereas Au doping allowed its formation. The productivity of methane increased abruptly when doubling the Au amount from 0.1 to 0.2 wt%, and then very slightly upon reaching 0.5 wt%. This can be correlated to the increasing surface concentration of Au nanoparticles and confirms the role of the surface area of the exposed Au nanoparticles in this reaction pathway.

As for CO, it can be either obtained by i) direct photoreduction, ii) as a product of photoreforming of the organic compounds obtained in the liquid phase by CO2 photoreduction, iii) by decomposition of HCOOH, or even iv) by catalytic reduction of CO2 by using the photogenerated H2. The productivity trend of CO and H2 is visibly shown in Fig. 9 and are very similar. This suggests that both species are produced by photoreforming of the primary organic products of photoreduction accumulated in the liquid phase.


Fig. 9 Gas phase productivities varying with Au loading (T = 80 °C, p = 7 bar, pH = 7).

A methane productivity of 0.30 and 0.32 mmol kgcat−1 h−1 was obtained for 0.2 and 0.5% Au/TiO2-P25, respectively. By contrast, no traces were detected in gas effluents after tests using bare P25 and 0.1 wt% Au/TiO2-P25. This critical point deserves further discussion due to the implications associated.

Collado et al. performed several CO2-photoreduction tests using gas-phase configuration and gold/titania catalysts prepared by the DP method using NaOH.46 The highest CH4 production was obtained with the 1.0 wt% Au/TiO2-anatase sample with a maximum productivity of ca. 8.9 mmol kgcat−1 h−1, using UV light (365 nm) and an average intensity of 71.7 W m−2, similar to the present conditions. Hydrogen productivity was maximum 9.5 mmol kgcat−1 h−1, and was attributed to the competitive reaction of H2O reduction by photo-generated electrons. Although only the anatase crystalline form was investigated, a stronger enhancement of carbon monoxide productivity was obtained decreasing the metal loading, combined with a decrease of methane selectivity.

Matějová and co-workers studied several gold-oxide photocatalysts, obtaining a maximum hydrogen and methane productivity of 160 and 6 mmol kgcat−1 h−1 respectively,53 but no liquid phase products were reported.

In our case at 7 bar, the highest loading of Au revealed the highest methane and hydrogen productivities. Thus, it can be considered the most promising among this series, although leading to a slightly lower formation of methanol (1350 mmol h−1 kgcat−1) with respect to sample 0.2 wt% Au (1360 mmol h−1 kgcat−1).

The good performance of very low gold loading on titania was confirmed recently by Tahir et al.54 In particular, they investigated the incorporation of gold nanoparticles at low loading into TiO2 nanowires as suitable materials for the CO2 photoreduction in the gas phase under visible light. The optimum loading of Au for methane productivity was obtained using the 0.5 wt% sample, however the 0.2 wt% and 0.7 wt% analogues revealed lower activity.

3.3.2 Effect of the titania polymorph phase. The crystal phase of the titania photocatalyst was varied as well. This aspect is important because many publications discuss the effect of titania polymorphs in the oxidative photocatalytic applications, but only a few of them are related to reductive photocatalysis. The highest metal loading was chosen (0.5 wt%) for all the samples because of the slightly enhanced total productivity and due to the much stronger plasmonic band associated. For this reason, this material is very interesting also for the next process implementation using visible radiation under high pressure, because of its higher visible light harvesting ability.

In particular, the commercial Evonik P25 titania was used as the mixed titania phase, and pure anatase and rutile as single titania phase supports.

Methanol productivity (Fig. 10) was very high for all the samples and promising with respect to the most representative papers reported in the literature (Table 2). Much higher methanol productivity was achieved for the P25-based sample, at the expense of the more oxidized intermediates. On the other hand, the complete absence of formaldehyde for the anatase-based sample and its highest productivity for the rutile base sample suggest the promotion of the carbonate-based reaction path by rutile. This hypothesis is supported by the low productivity of formaldehyde of P25, which is a mixture of anatase and rutile with a ratio of 78:22. These results support the validity of the approach to fix CO2 through a contemporary storage of solar energy.36


Fig. 10 Liquid phase productivities with different titania crystalline phases (T = 80 °C, p = 7 bar, pH = 7).

Methane was obtained only for the mixed phase P25 sample (Fig. 11). The direct photoreduction of CO2 to methane is indeed a complex multielectron pathway. The greatest activity usually exhibited by the titania P25 mixed phase is ascribed to the electron transfer from one phase to the other, which acts as the electron sink, thus inhibiting the electron–hole recombination. Therefore, only by increasing the lifetime of the photogenerated electron, can the direct CO2 photoreduction safely proceed to methane.


Fig. 11 Gas phase productivities with varying titania crystalline phases.

Also, the photocatalytic activity towards H2 and CO production was higher in the case of the P25 mixed phase than for the pure anatase and rutile phases. As highlighted before, their productivity trend was similar, confirming their origin from the photoreforming of organic compounds rather than by direct reduction.

Delavari et al. explored the gas phase reaction using immobilized TiO2 nanoparticles on a stainless steel mesh.55 Considering the crystalline phase, an increase in CO2 and CH4 conversions was detected due to the increase of the anatase/rutile ratio in TiO2, in such a case directly correlated with the enhancement of surface area. Mixed-phase titania nanomaterials were studied by Chen et al., for gas phase CO2 photoreduction involving the DC magnetron sputtering as the preparation method.56 All the sputtered samples constituted by a mixed-phase displayed greater reactivity compared to the single phase ones. The results were correlated to the high density of interfacial sites.

The synergistic effect between rutile and anatase for photo-catalytic applications is not new in the literature, but the deep explanation of the phenomena is still under debate.15 The more consistent argumentation considers the low electron–hole recombination as a consequence of the spatial separation caused by the interconnection between rutile and anatase particles. Li et al. investigated several TiO2 polymorphs as a photo-catalyst for CO2 reduction under UV light illumination.57 They found a very different methane production varying the crystalline phase of the titania. The synergistic effect was apparently demonstrated because higher photocatalytic activity was obtained using a mixed-phase TiO2. In contrast, Ohtani et al. supported a contrasting explanation, considering the lack of information about the direct evidence of the inter-particle charge migrations and the expected lower level of activity of pure anatase or rutile particles alone.15 For this reason in another work,58 they prepared a reconstructed P25 (mixture of pure anatase, rutile and amorphous titania) which demonstrated a less probable synergistic effect on the oxidative decomposition of acetic acid, acetaldehyde and methanol dehydrogenation. Recently Kafizas et al.59 employed transient absorption spectroscopy to study the hole transfer process across the anatase–rutile heterojunction in films as a function of phase composition, revealing a real difference between the electron hole recombination time using different titania forms. In addition Kullgren et al.60 investigated the phenomena by photoelectron spectroscopy and flat-band potential measurements in the case of mixed-phase nanoparticles in a humid environment. Although the explanation is still far from an unmistakable solution, the higher activity of mixed rutile–anatase TiO2 was still reported for CO2 photo-reduction in the literature, and our results confirmed this trend also for the present unconventional high pressure conditions.

Similar CO productivity rates were obtained for all the samples, ascribable to two main reasons: i) the CO formation occurred through a reaction without any contribution of the photo-catalyst, such as the CO2 photolysis; ii) the CO formation occurred independently from the metal loading; iii) photoreforming. The direct photolysis of CO2 is the less probable option in our conditions. The CO2 splitting occurs in the so-called deep UV region (wavelengths below 200 nm requiring pure quartz or metal halides and vacuum):7

CO2 + hν(<200 nm) → CO + 1/2O2 (ΔH = + 257 kJ mol−1)

The blank test without the catalyst revealed negligible CO productivity and ruled out definitely the first hypothesis. The independence from metal loading in the studied range can be explained considering the detailed work of Lin et al.61 A direct correlation between Ti centers and CO2 reduction to CO by water in the presence of UV irradiation was obtained using mesoporous framework substituted Ti silicalites. The proposed mechanism involved the formation of transient Ti3+ and a hole on a framework oxygen (Ti3+–O−) after the excitation of the Ti centers (Ti4+–O2−). Therefore, the effective role of Au, as the electron sink, is not useful in this context.

The hydroxylation of the surface depending on the support was evaluated examining the characteristic Ti–OH bonds, and correlated with the photo-activity of the catalysts.62 Indeed, hydroxyl groups have been recognized to play an important role in the photo-catalytic processes through their interactions with photo-generated holes63,64 and the preparation method directly affects the hydroxylation state of the catalyst surface. The experimental DRIFTS spectra indicated also in the present case the different OH species depending on the titania used (Fig. 12). Generally, hydroxyl groups can be divided into two main types considering the TiO2 surface: isolated hydroxyls (3600–3700 cm−1) and H-bonded hydroxyls (3400–3450 cm−1).63–65 The band at 3691 cm−1 relative to the stretching vibration of the H-bound OH group was present for all the samples but in different intensities.64 P25 revealed also a peak at 3629 cm−1 corresponding to physically adsorbed H2O (ref. 66) while rutile a peak at 3410 cm−1 is often ascribed to symmetric and antisymmetric OH modes of molecular water coordinated to Ti4+ cations.66 All these hydroxyl species are more intense in the case of P25, whilst decreasing with anatase and rutile. Although no significant correlation can be found considering the liquid-phase product obtained by the photoreduction process, the results are in line with the hydrogen productivity, as reported in Fig. 13. This is in accordance with the study of Maira et al. that reported the correlation between isolated hydroxyl groups and adsorption/photooxidation activity.64

Fig. 13 H2 productivity versus OH peak intensities for the different titania samples.

In order to compare the reactivity towards hydrogen production through direct water splitting and CO2 photoreduction, a test at 7 bar of nitrogen was compared with the ones in a CO2 atmosphere. In the presence of nitrogen, the main use of the photo-generated electrons in the CB, besides recombination, is the reduction of H+ to H2. The test with nitrogen confirmed this result, with an unexpectedly high productivity of ca. 350 mmol H2 kgcat−1 h−1 with respect to 17.9 mmol H2 kgcat−1 h−1 obtained under a CO2 atmosphere. The fundamental role of the photocatalyst was finally confirmed by the blank test without any catalyst, where no hydrogen was detected.

There are few mechanistic details associated with single electron transfer events. CO2− is believed by some groups to be a key surface intermediate in photoreduction on TiO2, implying direct electron attachment to an adsorbed CO2-containing species.12 The transition from this species to those possessing C–H bonds is still unclear. We interpret this increase in productivity of all the reduced organic products with respect to the literature due to the increase of CO2 pressure. This leads to an increase of the dissolved CO2 fraction (even if operated at high temperature, as predicted by thermodynamics and extensively described in ref. 27). Indeed, operating at 80 °C, the CO2 molar fraction at saturation is calculated to be ca. 0.01 at 1 bar, ca. 10% at 7 bar and ca. 35% at 20 bar. This in turn increases the amount of reactant adsorbed on the catalyst surface, overcoming one of the limits of this reaction in the liquid phase.

Also, the increase of adsorbed CO2 can influence the reaction rate of the different consecutive and parallel steps shown in Fig. 6, thus leading to a different product distribution. At higher pressure, the formation of products in the liquid phase is favored over the consecutive photoreforming path to H2 (Fig. 14). Depending on pH, HCOOH was favored over methanol, which is the final consecutive reaction product. Indeed, the abundant CO2 consumes the available electrons through the first reaction step. When the amount of dissolved and adsorbed CO2 is lower, the further reduction of the intermediates (HCHO and CH3OH) is instead favored. The pH can also strongly influence the product distribution, as already discussed elsewhere, and neutral pH favours the consecutive path to methanol with respect to the accumulation of HCOOH, which is the main product at pH = 14. Thus, we compared the effect of pressure at this latter pH in order to simplify the product distribution and have an overall check on the reaction pathways (Fig. 14). From this comparison, it is clearly seen that an increase of pressure favors the accumulation of HCOOH at the expense of the consecutive photoreforming path.

Conclusions

The photocatalytic reduction of CO2 in water was studied at high pressure under optimized conditions, investigating the role of Au loading and the TiO2 crystalline phase. In particular, a modified deposition/precipitation method was adopted for the synthesis on 0.1 wt%, 0.2 wt% and 0.5 wt% Au/TiO2. Commercial P25, pure anatase and pure rutile were used as semiconductors. Methanol productivities of 1359 and 1349 mmol kgcat−1 h−1 were detected for 0.2 wt% Au/TiO2 and 0.5 wt% Au/TiO2, respectively, showing the promising potential use of supported gold catalysts with low Au loading. The highest loaded sample led also to the highest H2 and CH4 productivities (17.9 and 0.32 mmol kgcat−1 h−1). By contrast, lower activity was achieved using anatase and rutile pure phases, confirming the key role of interfacial defects for enhancing the lifetime of the photo-generated charges between borders of different crystalline phases.

High pressure tests allowed the conclusion that increasing the pressure favors the accumulation of organic reduced products in liquid phase, whereas gas phase productivity is favored at intermediate pressure. Au was needed to produce methanol and methane and the former did not form under basic pH conditions.

These processes can be applied for CO2 conversion to high value products such as methane, methanol, formaldehyde or formic acid. Very high methanol productivity was achieved with the present catalysts, incomparably higher than the best results reported in the literature (ca. 1.4 mol kgcat−1 h−1).
