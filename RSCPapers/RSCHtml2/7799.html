Abstract

A highly ordered nanoporous structure based MIS type photo-detector is a promising device for next-generation optoelectronic applications due to its excellent light absorption, better mechanical strength, low density with a larger diffusion coefficient and charge accommodation ability. Herein, we have fabricated a highly sensitive MIS Schottky barrier diode by sandwiching nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3 films as the interfacial layer prepared by a jet nebulizer spray pyrolysis technique. XRD patterns confirmed the polycrystalline nature with monoclinic and orthorhombic phases of Snâ€“WO3 films, whose crystallite size gradually increased with the Sn concentration. The FE-SEM images confirmed that the Snâ€“WO3 composite films with 12 wt% of Sn exhibited unique surface morphology of nanoplates, nanowires and nanoporous-like structures. The optical band gap energy improved from 3.2 to 3.6 eV with increased Sn concentration. By establishing the nanoporous structure of Snâ€“WO3, we are the first to report on the photo-diode properties of Cu/nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3/p-Si diodes which recorded a positive photo-response with a higher reverse saturation current under illumination. This is supported by the enhanced detectivity of the interface layer with an increased Sn concentration. We have achieved an ultra-high responsivity of 5083.5 mA Wâˆ’1 for the diode fabricated with 12 wt% of Sn, which is 154 times higher than that of pure WO3. The presence of a nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3 layer in the MIS diode helped in recording âˆ¼60% quantum efficiency thereby making it ideal for ultra-violet photo-detector applications.

1. Introduction

Currently, the fabrication of metalâ€“insulatorâ€“semiconductor (MIS) layer based electronic devices namely Schottky-diodes, photo-diodes and UV photo-detectors is becoming more popular, when compared with other types of layered devices. They are the essential components in the area of semiconductor devices and play an active role in discrete applications like radio-frequency generators, optical communication systems, smoke detectors, etc.1â€“3 In contrast, the MIS structure has a great impact on the Fermi level depinning, improved carrier mobilities at the interface, limiting the charge leakages and large enhancement of the device tunneling/current. Deep-rooted formation of the depletion region in the semiconductor of a MIS diode can provide an electric field for the separation of photo-generated charge carriers (electronâ€“hole).4 The insertion of an ultra-thin interlayer in the MIS structure reduces the Schottky barrier height by inducing an interfacial dipole effect thereby reducing the specific contact resistivity of the device.5

Thin films with a unique surface morphology have received wide attention from the research community in the fabrication of advanced optoelectronic based applications.7,8 Indeed, the presence of novel surface structures like nano-sized porous, rods, belts, plates, flowers, wires, tubes and honeycombs arranged in-between the metalâ€“semiconductor interface enhances the reverse saturation current, photo-responsivity and quantum efficiency of the diode.9â€“12 This behaviour is attributed to its superior surface to volume ratio, longer lifetime of the photo-generated charge carriers, better electrical conduction at the M/S junction and quick light absorption. Particularly, the honeycomb/nonporous-like morphology is highly significant and unique as it provides better mechanical strength, accommodates more charge and has low density with a larger diffusion coefficient.6â€“8,11â€“14 Based on the synthesis fabrication technique, the hallmark features of the honeycomb-structure can be modified as it plays a major role in various modern technological applications such as solar cells, organic memory devices, light-emitting diodes and electrochromic devices.15â€“17 A honeycomb-like structure with a very well ordered film induces an interface dipole effect for homogeneous charge carrier extraction as well as separation with high optical absorbance.7,12,15

Certain morphologies of metal oxide can provide spacious channels in films and can facilitate the movement of charge or ions, which can improve the performance of the electronic device. Indeed, transition metal oxides (V2O5, WO3, Ta2O5, etc.) offer a number of diverse surface structures depending on their preparation procedures.18â€“20 Among them, tungsten trioxide (WO3) is a prospective material which can yield a variety of surface morphologies especially honeycomb.19 The TiO2/WO3-nanohoneycomb structure prepared by the solâ€“gel method can strongly enhance the photo-electrochemical behavior of both water splitting and hydrogen production due to its novel properties like lesser energy gap, huge surface area and improved charge separation.21 Honeycomb structures can control the ion insertion kinetics which will improve the charge transfer behavior and the porosity can enhance the gas sensor properties of the Schottky diode by intercalating the adsorption and desorption of gas molecules.22,23

It is worth mentioning that the oxygen vacancies could act as doubly ionized donors and also contribute at least a maximum of two electrons for the conductivity. Mukherjee et al.24 confirmed this behavior by adding Sn ions into the WO3 lattice effectively, which reduced the resistivity and improved the film conductivity owing to the increase in the oxygen vacancy and carrier concentration. Thus controlling the surface morphology of the materials is a big challenge in thin film technology as it is confined to the deposition process and methods. Moreover, the physical and chemical properties of the films like thickness, doping concentration, deposition-temperature/time and pressure do have a significant role to play in device formation. Thus the scientific world have probed different approaches to fabricate a nanoporous/honeycomb-like structure, such as spin coating, spray pyrolysis, sputtering, ete.,13,14,21 Among these methods, spray pyrolysis enables us to produce good quality films with a novel surface morphology by forming uniform-sized spray droplets. Specifically, the jet nebulizer spray pyrolysis technique (JNSP) has several advantages, which include less deposition time, uniform coating with large area coverage and low-cost.

In this study, we have incorporated Sn ions in a WO3 matrix. Through the JNSP technique, nanoporous and nanowire-like surface morphologies of Snâ€“WO3 films were formed. The films were analyzed for their structural, morphological, optical, and electrical properties based on the Snâ€“WO3 composite films that were formed on the glass substrate with different concentrations of Sn (0, 4, 8 and 12 wt%). We fabricated Cu/nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3/p-Si diodes, with the nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3 structure, by sandwiching them between the Cu and p-Si layers to enhance the performance of the MIS type Schottky diode. More importantly, the photo-detector parameters which include the ideality factor (n), barrier height (Î¦B), saturation current (I0), photo-sensitivity (PS), responsivity (R), external quantum efficiency (EQE) and specific detectivity (D*) of the MIS diodes were calculated and interpreted based on the influence of nanoporous structures on the device performance. By establishing the nanoporous structure of Snâ€“WO3, we are the first to report on the photo-diode properties of Cu/nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3/p-Si structured Schottky barrier diodes.

2. Experimental details

2.1 Pure WO3 and nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3 films preparation

Thin films of pure WO3 and Snâ€“WO3 were chemically deposited on a heated glass substrate using aqueous solutions of tungsten hexachloride and tin dichloride through a JNSP technique according to our previous work.25 The homogeneous spray solution was prepared with 0.2 mole of tungsten hexachloride and different wt% of tin dichloride (0, 4, 8, and 12) which is dissolved in 5 ml of ethanol separately and stirred continuously at room temperature for 1 hour using a magnetic stirrer.

Finally, the prepared homogeneous solution was effectively sprayed on the ultrasonically cleaned glass substrates (2 cm Ã— 2 cm) at an optimized temperature of 400 Â°C with air as the carrier gas. This particular mole and temperature range have been chosen from the optimized results.26 The substrate temperature is controlled by using a PID thermometer which is also connected to a heater. 2.2 Cu/nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3/p-Si Schottky barrier diode fabrication

The p-type silicon (p-Si) substrate with 279 Î¼m thickness (Â±25 Î¼m), (1 0 0) orientation and 60 Î© cm resistivity was employed as a semiconductor in the fabrication of MIS structured SBDs. The p-Si substrates with extra impurities like grease, metallic impurities, dust, and organic residues were cleaned using different acids. The key process of silicon substrate cleaning involves the following steps.27

(i) All the Si wafers were degreased for 10â€“15 min in boiling acetone and ethanol.

(ii) The organic residues were removed from the substrates using piranha solution by mixing sulfuric acid and hydrogen peroxide (H2SO4 + H2O2) in the ratio of 3:1.

(iii) Finally, the native oxide layer on the Si wafers was removed with the help of hydrofluoric (HF) acid diluted with water (H2O) in the ratio of 1:10.

(iv) Notably, all the wafers were completely rinsed with de-ionized water after each step.

The prepared WO3 and Snâ€“WO3 precursor solutions of 3 ml were deposited separately on the cleaned p-Si substrates (1 cm Ã— 1 cm) by the JNSP technique with different concentrations of Sn (0, 4, 8 and 12 wt%) at an optimized substrate temperature of 400 Â°C. After deposition, the copper (Cu) metal contact was coated on the Snâ€“WO3/p-Si using dc sputtering (Model Name: HIND high vacuum-PS 2000) with 4 mm diameter and 500 nm thickness. The instrument details and deposition conditions of the metal contacts have been mentioned previously.25,26 Better contact is established with the silver paste, which is applied on both sides of the Cu/Snâ€“WO3/p-Si diode and was dried for 6 hours under ambient conditions.

Pure WO3 and nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3 composite thin films were prepared with different Sn concentrations through the JNSP technique and were used as an interfacial layer in the MIS diode. The structural, morphological, optical and electrical properties of the coated films were analyzed by XRD, FE-SEM, UV-Vis and Iâ€“V characterization. 3.1 Structural properties

Fig. 2a shows the XRD pattern of pure WO3 and Snâ€“WO3 composite thin films with different concentrations (0, 4, 8 and 12 wt%) of Sn. Notably, polycrystalline nature with dual-phases of monoclinic and orthorhombic was observed for pure WO3 and Snâ€“WO3 thin films. A well-defined triplet peak was recorded for the pure WO3 film at diffraction angles of 2Î¸ (Â°) = 23.34, 23.85 and 24.56 and their corresponding h k l values are (0 0 2), (0 2 0) and (2 0 0). This indicates the monoclinic (Î³-WO3) phase and is in accordance with standard JCPDS card no. 83-0950.28 The growth of the (2 0 0) plane was much better relative to that of (0 0 2) and (0 2 0) planes as shown in for the pure film. Moreover, the triplet peak intensities (002), (020) and (200) of pure WO3 are suppressed without any change in its phase due to the addition of higher wt% of Sn. This is supported by the emergence of additional peaks in the directions of (133), (351), (410) and (081) indicating the formation of a new crystalline phase of SnWO4 (Orthorhombic). Interestingly, the Snâ€“WO3 composite film coated with 8 wt% of Sn exhibits a peak shift along the lower angle side. This may be attributed to the difference in the atomic radius of the guest and host atoms along with the variations of lattice parameters of the films.28

The most preferential growth occurred along the (1 3 3) and (0 8 2) directions when increasing the Sn concentration from 0 to 12 wt% (JCPDS card no. 29-1354). Besides, the growth of the (3 5 1) and (4 1 0) planes was found to increase gradually with Sn concentration. These results indicate that Sn atoms are strongly present in the WO3 lattice. Thus, the Snâ€“WO3 composite films are polycrystalline in nature and consist of the orthorhombic crystal structure (Î±-SnWO4). Furthermore, some additional peaks (SnO and SnO2) are spotted in Snâ€“WO3 films, which are marked with an @ symbol (Fig. 2a) and they are in good agreement with JCPDS cards No's. 72-2324 and 78-1063. The crystallite size (D) of the films was calculated by the Debyeâ€“Scherrer equation.29 The average crystallite size (DA) was found to increase linearly, whose corresponding FWHM dwindled with increasing Sn concentration. The Snâ€“WO3 films deposited with 12 wt% of Sn recorded a maximum average crystallite size (DA) of 50.8 nm. Apparently, the incorporation of Sn atoms in WO3 improved the crystallite size of the films, which could enhance the electrical conductivity of the films and charge transport properties of the MIS SBDs.

The texture coefficient (TC) is an essential parameter to analyze the diffraction peaks of the films. The TC values indicate the structural information, development of the peak and orientation of the plane. TC > 1 suggests the preferential growth of the films and the 0 < TC < 1 condition implies the lack of grain orientation in a particular direction. From Fig. 5 it is evident that the calculated TC values are higher for the (1 3 3) plane and are varied from 4.46 to 3.40 with Sn concentrations as shown in Fig. 2b. Our XRD results demonstrated that the incorporation of Sn atoms has a stronger influence on the structural parameters of Snâ€“WO3 composite films.

The vertically porous-like structures will act as good light harvesters, allowing light photons to penetrate deeply into the thin layers by the scattering effect.31 This might enhance the quantum efficiency of the detector. Moreover, a porous structure with a large surface area will provide higher charge transport/separation, which will enhance the power conversion efficiency of the device. Interestingly, the 12 wt% of Snâ€“WO3 composite films show different structures with surface morphologies like nanoplates, nanowires and also nanoporous. We believe that the presence of tin dichloride and the impact of spherical droplets on the heated substrate might have facilitated the unique morphology. The nanoporous structure can have a higher charge accommodation ability, which will increase the density of the charge carriers and diode performance.13

This is mainly due to the increase in the photo-generated charge carriers (eâˆ’âˆ’h+) along with the improved conversion efficiency of the semiconductor layer and absorption of interfacial layer.32 However, the 4 wt% MIS diode shows a higher ideality factor of n = 4.03 under light conditions compared to that under dark conditions. This may be ascribed to the generation of a smaller amount of eâˆ’âˆ’h+ pairs and their recombination. The MIS SBDs fabricated with 12 wt% of Sn obtained a lower ideality factor (n = 2.35) in particular under light conditions. Generally, the deviation of the ideality factor from the ideal value is because of the presence of a large number of trapping states. Also, a higher n value is sometimes observed in the hetero-structures of two different materials due to the large lattice mismatch.49â€“52

In fact, the potential variation across the thin interfacial layer and the effect of interface states with applied voltage are the main reasons for the changes in the barrier height (Î¦B) of the diode, which has been analyzed by Turut et al.53 A Schottky diode with lower barrier height will improve the quantum efficiency of the photo-detector as per studies by Joshua Duran et al.54 The maximum quantum efficiency was recorded for a lower barrier height. We demonstrated that all diodes are very sensitive to both light and impurity concentration. The photosensitivity of the diode is mainly due to the intensity of incident light and the absorption rate of the interfacial layer, which is in accordance with our optical parameters (Table 1). The ratio of the generated photocurrent and incident optical power is measured in terms of photoresponsivity (R) of the diode. We observed an expeditious variation in R of the diode when the interfacial layer is varied between 0 and 12 wt% Sn concentrations. Most importantly, the 12 wt% of Sn based MIS diode recorded a very high responsivity of Rs = 5083.5 mA Wâˆ’1 which is due to the deep penetration of light in the nanoporous channels facilitated by the effect of scattering. The study of quantum efficiency (QE) and specific detectivity (D*) of the MIS diode is very useful for connecting the present Cu/Snâ€“WO3/p-Si structured diode with an ultra-violet (UV) photo-detector. Compared to lower concentrations, the MIS diode fabricated with 8 and 12 wt% of Sn disclosed better quantum efficiency and higher detectivity. Especially, 12 wt% of Sn recorded a very high QE and D* of 1971.1% and 12.089 Ã— 1010 Jones, respectively. Interestingly, we have achieved a higher detectivity value than that reported by Ouyang et al. (3.7 Ã— 107 Jones) and Lim et al. Compared to other types of structures (MS, MOS and MIM), the MIS structure has a depletion region very closer to the surface and is one of the reasons for exhibiting higher quantum efficiency. The larger detectivity values imply a less noise level of the fabricated diodes.59 From the Cu/Snâ€“WO3/p-Si diode results, we observed that the presence of nanoporous-like structured Snâ€“WO3 films in between the metal and semiconductor interface strongly enhanced the photo-diode parameters, particularly, the MIS diode fabricated with 12 wt%, which suggests that the nanoporous structure has promising potential for MIS type diode applications.

The dual-phase (monoclinic and orthorhombic) nature of the Snâ€“WO3 films was confirmed from the XRD patterns. The composite film deposited with 12 wt% of Sn recorded a maximum average crystallite size (DA) of 50.8 nm and exhibited a variety of surface structures like nanoplates, nanowires and nanoporous. A maximum optical Eg of 3.6 eV was obtained for a higher concentration of Sn. The measured electrical conductivity was found to be improved from 10âˆ’15 to 10âˆ’13 S cmâˆ’1. Also, the Snâ€“WO3 film with 12 wt% of Sn showed a minimum activation energy Ea of 0.041 eV and a resistivity of Ï = 9.7 Ã— 1012 Î© cm. Remarkably, a lower ideality factor of n = 2.35 was obtained for the Cu/nanoporous:Snâ€“WO3/p-Si diode measured under light conditions.
