Abstract

Solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) have emerged as a promising technology for efficient hydrogen production and syngas conversion to low-carbon fuels. Among various SOEC configurations, symmetrical solid oxide electrolysis cells (S-SOECs) offer unique advantages due to their simplicity, reduced operational steps, and reversible operation. The symmetric electrodes enable the production of hydrogen and oxygen during electrolysis, while the generated gases can be harnessed for electricity generation during fuel cell operation. This reversible operation not only minimizes the number of components necessary for the system but also decreases the overall cost of the technology. This review provides a comprehensive overview of the advancements in S-SOEC research, with a primary focus on materials development. We discuss the current challenges associated with material limitations and explore the potential for utilizing syngas products as a resource. Finally, we propose future research directions to further improve the performance and durability of S-SOECs, paving the way for widespread implementation of this versatile technology.

1. Introduction

The increasing consumption of energy, primarily from fossil fuels, has led to a rise in atmospheric pollution and exacerbated global warming.1 To tackle these challenges, it is crucial to develop economically viable technologies for carbon dioxide conversion into valuable products and transition towards clean, renewable energy sources such as solar, tidal, and wind energy.13 However, the intermittency and temporal dependence of renewable energy necessitate the implementation of energy storage and conversion technologies.1,4 Electrochemical energy conversion and storage technologies, including fuel cells, electrolysis cells, secondary cells, and supercapacitors, are expected to play a pivotal role in addressing these issues.

Solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) have garnered considerable attention as they can electrolyze water and carbon dioxide using excess electricity and heat. This technology not only mitigates excessive CO2 emissions but also converts electrical energy into chemical energy for storage. The generated syngas can be stored and utilized in various industrial production processes, with valuable substances synthesized through the FischerTropsch (FT) reaction and other technologies.5

Each SOEC component, including the anode, cathode, and electrolyte, plays a critical role in the electrochemical reaction. Modifying or replacing any of these components can impact the efficiency and effectiveness of the entire electrolysis process. Symmetrical solid oxide electrolysis cells (S-SOECs) are a solution to the challenges posed by the thermal expansion coefficient between the two electrodes and the electrolyte. Using the same materials for the anode and cathode reduces sintering steps and improves actual operability.6

In recent years, researchers have increasingly focused on S-SOECs due to their potential advantages. This review paper aims to summarize the latest achievements in S-SOEC research to promote their practical application and to explore the integration of low-carbon technologies to increase the utilization of syngas products.

2. Co-electrolysis of CO2/H2O by SOECs

2.1. Principle of solid oxide electrolysis cells

SOECs are divided into two types: oxygen ion-conducting type (O-SOEC) and proton-conducting type (H-SOEC). Both types consist of three components: anode, electrolyte and cathode as illustrated in Fig. 1. In O-SOEC, reduction reactions occur at the cathode, which is filled with water, carbon dioxide and other gases. The generated oxygen ion O2 is transferred to the anode through the electrolyte and oxidized to produce oxygen at the air electrode. Currently, research on O-SOEC is mainly focused on high temperature (>600 C) electrolysis. This method is economically more attractive than low-temperature electrolysis because it requires less electricity and can utilize a wider range of catalytic materials under high-temperature conditions.7 However, traditional materials are prone to degradation during high-temperature electrolysis, and long-term durability is poor under high current density. Additionally, coke deposition is common under high concentrations of CO.8,9 To overcome these problems, traditional materials and reaction mechanisms are being studied and developed. H-SOEC is also being investigated because it can operate at lower temperatures and reduce the activation energy required for the reaction.10 Fig. 1b illustrates the schematic diagram of H-SOEC. In this type, water is transported from the anode to decompose into H+ and O2. H+ enters the cathode through an electrolyte that can conduct protons to produce H2. On the same side, CO2 also undergoes a reduction reaction to produce CO. The proton-conducting type SOEC helps to obtain dry H2 directly and saves the drying process. However, the electrolytic efficiency of H-SOEC is lower than that of O-SOEC.

2.3. Kinetics

As thermodynamics is discussed on the basis of the ideal state, practical short-circuiting can occur. Hence, it is necessary to consider the open-circuit voltage (Voc), as its chemical formula is shown in eqn (8).14 When the cell is in a reversible state, the open circuit voltage is theoretically the cell's theoretical electromotive force. However, in practice, the open circuit voltage of the cell is always less than the theoretical electromotive force due to the various resistance. When the applied voltage is higher than the open-circuit voltage, the entire cell system will pass through the current. At this point, the electrochemical potential of the electrode will be deviated, resulting in electrode chemical reactions. Co-electrolysis reactions produce three polarization phenomena in the whole cell system: Ohmic polarization, activation polarization and concentration polarization.

2.4. 3D modeling

The previous discussion mainly focused on microscopic models for SOECs, but there are also other types of modeling approaches available, such as three-dimensional (3D) models. 3D modeling can investigate the dynamic behavior of SOECs under various working conditions and suggest beneficial control strategies. The 3D model is typically simulated in the form of an SOEC stack (Fig. 3a),16 which is often calculated using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). CFD is based on the conservation equations of mass, energy, and momentum as well as reaction dynamics.17 The model employs the modified ButlerVolmer equation to describe the electrochemical kinetics of water and carbon dioxide at the three-phase boundary (TPB) and the related thermal catalysis kinetics. 3. Research progress of S-SOECs electrode

The material and structure of the electrode play a critical role in the electrochemical reaction as the main site of the reaction. Currently, various types of perovskites are commonly used as anode and cathode materials due to their mixed ion and electronic conductivity (MIEC) and excellent oxidation-reduction stability.19 When studying the impact of electrode material and structure on cell performance, the focus is mainly on factors such as ion conductivity, current density, and other factors that reflect the electrocatalytic effect. To enhance the catalytic effect, continuous optimization of electrode material and structure is necessary.

3.1. Material optimization

3.1.1. Perovskite material. Perovskites are commonly used as electrodes in SOECs, and they can be classified into three types: single perovskite, double perovskite, and RuddlesdenPopper (RP) perovskite. Single perovskite oxide has the general formula ABO3, where the A and B sites are cations. The A-site cations have a larger radius and coordinate with 12 oxygen ions, while the B-site cations with a smaller radius coordinate with 6 oxygen ions, which can form BO6 octahedra in space (Fig. 4a).20 When low-valent cations replace the A and B sites, oxygen vacancies are generated in the perovskite structure to maintain electrical neutrality, resulting in oxygen ion transport properties.

Double perovskite oxide has the general formula AAB2O5+ or A2BBO6 (Fig. 4b).21 The double perovskite structure can accommodate elements that cannot exist in the single perovskite structure and has higher chemical stability and ion-electron conductivity, making it a research hotspot.22 RuddlesdenPopper (RP) perovskite is a layered perovskite derivative composed of alternating layers of perovskite (ABO3) and rock salt (AO). Its chemical formula can be written as An+1BnO3n+1 (Fig. 4c).21 The RP perovskite structure can provide abundant oxygen vacancies and interstitial oxygen, which can positively impact the diffusion of oxygen ions.

The electrode material and structure are continuously optimized to improve the catalytic effect. In H-SOEC, the specific conduction mechanism has not been clearly studied, but due to the possibility of proton defects in RP perovskite and the fact that proton conduction mainly occurs through hopping and rotating, RP perovskite can be used in proton-conducting type electrolysis cells.23

Compared to an asymmetric cell, a symmetrical cell structure uses the same material for both the anode and cathode. This reduces the number of cell modules, making assembly more convenient, and helps ensure stability and chemical compatibility at the electrode/electrolyte interface. However, since the anode and cathode have different electrochemical reactions, the electrode material used must meet the requirements of stable phase structure and acceptable conductivity.24 Currently, the most commonly used electrode materials for symmetrical cells are single perovskite LSF (LaxSr1xFeO3) and double perovskite SFM (Sr2Fe1.5Mo0.5O6) (Table 1).5,6,2531 These two types of perovskite have excellent electrochemical performance as symmetrical cell electrodes, and the Faraday efficiency of H2 and CO produced by decomposing H2O and CO2 exceeds 50%.

3.1.2. Material optimization methods.
3.1.2.1. Doping method. The process of electrolytic H2O and CO2 involves the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), which are crucial reaction rates in the overall electrochemical process. In the OER reaction, the reactant must first be adsorbed onto the perovskite surface. Currently, the oxygen vacancy site is considered the active site for adsorption of carbon dioxide and water during electrolysis.7 The existence of a large number of oxygen vacancies is believed to increase the adsorption sites and improve electrocatalytic performance. Additionally, the ionic radius of the A and B sites not only affects the structure of perovskite, but also influences the electrocatalytic reaction. ORR involves the conversion of oxygen species on the surface of the catalyst, and the BO bond is closely related to ORR performance. As such, research into modifying the ionic radius of the A and B sites remains ongoing.

Doping refers to the intentional introduction of foreign atoms into a lattice, which can induce electron or hole transfer, thereby altering the electronic properties of the material.34 Successful doping should not alter the crystal structure or microscopic characteristics, which makes it a useful strategy for modifying perovskite materials.35 Perovskite's crystal structure at A and B sites is highly flexible, making it amenable to doping. Transition metal elements are commonly used for doping. By substituting some A sites with transition metals, covalent bonds can be formed, which can prevent the precipitation of alkaline earth metals. The introduction of low-valence metal ions can create additional oxygen vacancies, which can improve the OER and ORR activity.36 Doping at the A site can have a significant impact on the transition metal elements at the B site, particularly if the A site dopants have high valence states. This can inhibit the ionic valence at the B site, reducing its valence state or leading to an unstable oxidation state. For instance, Xu et al. found that Pr doping at the A site introduced tetravalent Pr, which suppressed the high valence of Fe ions and resulted in more divalent iron ions, as observed by XPS.37 The metal cations at the B site also play a crucial role in the electrocatalysis process, and doping at the B site can lead to more oxygen vacancies in the perovskite lattice, affecting the BO covalent bond and ORR performance.7,38 Sundell et al. used BaZrO3 perovskite oxide as an example and combined with DFT calculations to explore the energy and charge changes after cation doping (Fig. 5a, b).39 When the abundance of elements at the A and B sites differs, the position of the ions doped into the site also differs. For example, in this article, most dopants entered the A site when Zr was abundant. The abundance of oxygen vacancies formed by ion doping varies due to the different radii of each ion and the size of the interaction between the ions. LSF perovskite is an excellent catalytic material for symmetrical electrolysis cells. Bi doping at the A site significantly improves the catalytic activity of fuel cells, reduces the polarization resistance between interfaces, and has good stability in air and reducing atmosphere, as reported by Wan et al.40 In this regard, Bi element was applied to symmetrical electrolytic CO2 by Wan et al., who synthesized La0.75xBixSr0.25Cr0.5Fe0.5O3 (x = 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4) with different doping amounts.41 The results were ideal as the performance of electrolytic CO2 was significantly improved. At 1.5 V and 800 C, when x = 0.05, its current density increased by about 30% compared to undoped Bi, and the current density continued to increase by about 10% with an increase in Bi doping. However, when the content of Bi is too high, it can be unstable in the reducing atmosphere. Therefore, the proportion of metal element doping should be carefully considered. B-site cations also play a vital role in the catalytic performance of perovskite materials. LCFN, with good OER and ORR performance, was confirmed by Tian et al., who doped Sc at the B site to explore its electrocatalytic activity.42 At elevated temperatures ranging from 750850 C, perovskite materials exhibit significantly reduced polarization resistance and do not exhibit carbon deposition during CO2 electrolysis, leading to enhanced cell performance. Research has shown that RP perovskite, due to its excellent oxygen transport properties and thermal expansion coefficient, is a popular focus for developing SOEC electrodes. Crystal modifications, including A and B cation doping, have been adopted to enhance electrochemical performance. While partially replacing La with Sr failed to yield significant performance improvements, doping Mn at the B site in La1.5Sr0.5Ni1xMnxO4+ led to increased oxygen vacancy and decreased polarization resistance, according to Zheng et al.43,44 Other elements like Cu and Bi have also been successfully doped at the B site.4547 Pr2NiO4+ is another commonly used RP perovskite due to its high anion diffusion and conductivity that rivals La2NiO4+. However, despite its higher electrochemical performance, Pr2NiO4+ is less stable. To overcome this limitation, researchers have experimented with doping and improving its structure, such as A-doping with Ba48 and Sr,32 as well as B-doping with Cu.49

Compared to the well-studied oxygen ion-conducting type (O-SOEC), proton-conducting (H-SOEC) systems have received less attention. The proton conduction mechanism in H-SOEC is mainly attributed to hopping and rotating pathways, and the doping of cations affects the energy required for these two mechanisms. Doping ions with smaller radii can reduce the hopping energy, while the rotating path will generally be reduced due to the shorter inter-ion spacing and the increase in Coulomb force. However, the rotating energy may increase due to these factors, and therefore, the total energy required for proton conduction must be determined experimentally.37,50 Fig. 5c shows a possible proton conduction pathway (represented by a green dot).51 Currently, the doping of H-SOEC is mainly focused on ordinary perovskite materials doped with Dy, K, and Ba metal elements, or on improving the proton conductivity of BaZrO3, a material with high proton conductivity.5255 RP perovskites such as La2NiO4+ and Pr2NiO4+, which exhibit excellent performance in O-SOEC, have conductivity and electrocatalytic performance that can continue to improve in H-SOEC. However, BaLaInO4 has been receiving attention due to its potential for exhibiting pure proton conductivity under certain conditions.56 The doping of this material is mainly concentrated on the A and B positions. The chemical formula can be expressed as BaLa0.9M0.1InO3.95 (M = Ca, Sr, Ba) or BaLaIn0.9M0.1O4+d (M = Ti, Zr, Nb), and the conductivity after doping is higher by more than one order of magnitude.57

Besides the doping of metal ions at A and B sites, the doping of non-metallic ions at O sites has been proposed to optimize the electronic structure, crystal structure, and ion/proton mobility of perovskite catalysts. Currently, the O position can be partially replaced by F, N3, Cl, S2, Br, and other ions.5865 Fig. 5d presented proposed reaction mechanism of the F-doping catalyst for CO2 electrocatalysis.58 It is noteworthy that F has a slightly greater electronegativity (v = 3.98) than O (v = 3.44), but its ionic radius and valence electron density are both less than O2. Consequently, F doping reduces the bond strength between metal ions and oxygen ions, particularly the BO bond. This reduces the energy required for the formation of oxygen vacancies and increases the mobility of oxygen ions, thereby enhancing the activity of perovskite catalysts.65,66 Additionally, after F doping, the alkalinity between metal ions and fluorine ions is less than that between metal ions and oxygen ions, resulting in superior stability during CO2 electrolysis.58 However, research on anion doping in RP perovskite is limited, and F doping remains the most common choice of O-site doping to enhance sulfur resistance.67


3.1.2.2. Defecting method. Doping is a method that introduces other elements into perovskite without changing its stoichiometric ratio. However, it may cause some side effects due to the interaction between elements. To avoid this, the non-stoichiometric ratio of perovskite, known as ionic defect, has been proposed. In this case, the ratio of A and B cations of ABO3 perovskite is not equal to 1, and the degree of cation defects at A and B sites will affect the perovskite structure. Within a certain range, the crystal structure can still be maintained under the condition of cation defects.68 Cationic defects are more likely to appear at A than B because the energy required for the formation of cationic defects at B is stronger than that at A.68 A-site cation defects generate more oxygen vacancies, providing space for electrochemical reaction.69 The advantages of A-site cation defects are also evident in their impact on OER, ORR, and oxygen ion migration.70,71 For example, Liu et al. analyzed the XRD diagram of (La0.3Sr0.7)1Ti0.3Fe0.7O3 ( = 3%, 5%, 7%, 9%), and observed that the diffraction peak shifts to low angle, the lattice expands, and the oxygen vacancy increases.70 Similarly, during CO2 electrolysis, Zhu et al. successfully increased oxygen vacancies in La2xCuO4- (x = 0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3) using A-site defects. Even when there are too many defects, CuO/RP mixture is formed, which has a positive effect on CO2 adsorption and activation.72 However, Su et al. have suggested that La defects are not suitable for RP perovskite.68 Therefore, further research is needed to fully understand the application of defect chemistry to RP perovskite.

Apart from the in situ metal exsolution method for enhancing the surface properties of perovskite materials using nano-metal particles, topological ion exchange is another approach for surface engineering. For example, a study on Fe-infiltrated Cu and Cu-infiltrated Fe samples (Fig. 9c and d)101 suggests that topological exchange only occurs when Fe permeates Cu. SEM analysis reveals that the size of precipitated nanoparticles is larger in this case, which is more consistent with the properties of all precipitates. 4. Research progress of S-SOECs electrolyte

Electrolyte selection is critical for ion conduction in SOECs. The chosen electrolyte should meet the following requirements: high ionic conductivity to achieve high conversion efficiency, good compactness to prevent the contact of reaction gases on both sides, stability to enable long-term operation, and a thermal expansion coefficient that matches the electrode to prevent component detachment and cracking. Consequently, a considerable amount of research has been conducted to develop high-conductivity electrolytes. The perovskite system materials have emerged as a promising option due to their significant derivatives.103 Among them, the research on oxygen ion-conducting electrolytes has been the most extensive. In recent years, research on proton-conducting electrolytes has also gained momentum, and studies on electrolyte materials for simultaneous oxygen ion and proton conduction have commenced.

The conductivity of solid electrolytes is influenced by various factors, such as grain size, grain arrangement structure, grain boundaries, ambient temperature, element distribution, and element segregation. Xue et al. have utilized the brick layer model to investigate the impact of grain size and element segregation on the conductivity of different parts, including grain boundary conductivity, specific grain boundary conductivity, and volume conductivity (as illustrated in Fig. 10).104 Element segregation can vary based on the sintering temperature employed. The research findings indicate that larger grain size, greater crystallinity, and smaller grain boundaries lead to increased grain boundary and volume conductivity. However, due to element segregation, oxygen vacancy near the grain boundary also increases, causing the specific conductivity to decline. Moreover, the optimal operating temperature varies for different ions conducted by the electrolyte, and therefore, requires analysis for different electrolyte types.

4.1. In O-SOEC

Solid electrolytes that conduct oxygen ions include zirconia-based (ZrO2), ceria-based (CeO2), bismuth-based (Bi2O3), and lanthanum gallate (LaGaO3) electrolytes. Among these, ZrO2 and CeO2 have been studied extensively.105 To enhance the ionic conductivity of ZrO2-based electrolytes, low-valence cations such as Y3+, Er3+, Gd3+, Eu3+, Dy3+, Sc3+, Ca2+, Yb3+, and Mg2+ are doped.90 Y-doped yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) is a relatively efficient electrolyte that can sustain electrolysis for a long time.106 However, its high cost and tendency to degrade over time and under high current densities limit its practical use.106 To address this, nickel is doped on the basis of YSZ to form Ni-YSZ electrolyte, which has better stability and lower polarization impedance.107 Scandium is also used to synthesize scandium-stabilized zirconia (ScSZ), which has a significantly higher ionic conductivity than YSZ, but it is prone to phase change at low temperatures.105 Co-doping with Ce3+ and Gd3+ enhances its structural stability.108

Ceria-based electrolytes have a higher ionic conductivity within the temperature range of 500800 C than YSZ. However, its stability is reduced due to the reduction of its high valence cation Ce4+ to Ce3+.108 To improve its performance, Sm3+, Gd3+, and Dy3+ are doped.109 Sm3+ and Gd3+ doped CeO2 electrolyte (SDC, GDC) show significantly improved conductivity. GDC and SDC are often combined with YSZ to make use of their advantages. For example, a YSZ-SDC double-layer electrolyte is prepared, with YSZ acting as a barrier layer of SDC to reduce current loss and increase the stability of the cell structure.110

Bismuth-based (Bi2O3) electrolytes have a ionic conductivity one to two orders of magnitude higher than YSZ at the same temperature. However, they are prone to degradation under low oxygen partial pressure, and therefore need to be combined with other electrolytes to enhance their practical application.111 For example, P Temluxame doped Bi2O3 into gadolinium-stabilized zirconia (SCGZ), which helps to reduce unnecessary phases and increase the densification of electrolyte particles, maintaining the high ionic conductivity of the electrolyte.112

Lanthanum gallate (LaGaO3) is a perovskite oxide that exhibits high ionic conductivity (>0.01 S cm1 at 600 C) and good stability.113 Sr and Mg-doped La1xSrxGa1yMgyO3 (LSGM) is an excellent oxygen ion conductor, with negligible electronic conductivity. However, when LSGM is used as an electrolyte, it reacts with the traditional Ni-YSZ electrode to form a heterophase that affects the conductivity.114 Therefore, it can be combined with other electrolyte materials, such as GDC and SDC, to improve its performance. There are two main methods for combining LSGM and SDC/GDC. On the one hand, LSGM and SDC/GDC are mixed proportionally as electrolyte materials, and on the other hand, SDC/GDC is used as a barrier layer to prevent the reaction between the electrolyte and the electrode. Recently, researchers have found that SDC can be grown in situ on electrolyte sheets through water bath heating, which better addresses the shortcomings of traditional screen printing methods.

4.2. In H-SOEC

The high operating temperature required for oxygen ion-conducting type solid oxide electrolysis cells (O-SOEC) can negatively impact the long-term performance of electrode materials, resulting in decreased electrolytic performance. Therefore, there is a need to reduce the operating temperature of O-SOEC. However, reducing the temperature also reduces the performance of O-SOEC, leading to increased interest in proton-conducting type solid oxide electrolysis cells (H-SOEC) that can operate at intermediate temperatures.

Perovskite-type oxides are commonly used as electrolyte materials in H-SOEC, and among them, BaCeO3 and BaZrO3 have been extensively studied. BaCeO3 and its derivatives exhibit high proton conductivity at 700 C but are prone to react with acidic gases such as CO2. 4.3. Hybrid-SOEC

The oxygen ion-conducting type SOEC is often limited by high operating temperatures, which can lead to degradation of electrode materials and decreased electrolytic performance. On the other hand, the proton-conducting type SOEC can operate at intermediate temperatures, but has lower electrolytic performance compared to O-SOEC. Thus, efforts in O-SOEC research are focused on reducing operating temperature while maintaining conductivity, while in H-SOEC research, efforts are aimed at improving conductivity. Some researchers have attempted to combine the two types of electrolytes, resulting in the concept of Hybrid Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell (Hybrid-SOEC). In 2018, Kim et al.122 proposed a Hybrid-SOEC that utilized NdBa0.5Sr0.5Co1.5Fe0.5O5+ (NBSCF) as the electrode material and BaZr0.1Ce0.7Y0.1Yb0.1O3-(BZCYYb) as the electrolyte, achieving mixed conduction of oxygen ions and protons and improving electrolytic performance (Fig. 11a). However, research in this area has stagnated, and BZCYYb remains the most suitable electrolyte material for mixed conduction. 6. Summary and prospect

After multiple optimizations of electrode and electrolyte materials, the performance of SOEC electrolysis of water and carbon dioxide has been greatly improved. However, there are still some technical and economic challenges that need to be addressed. Most of the technical challenges are due to the high temperature environment, which can cause changes in the material and structure of the cell module and degrade its electrolytic performance. Therefore, the materials used in SOEC design should meet strict requirements, including good chemical and thermal stability at high temperature and a matching coefficient of thermal expansion between components. Additionally, attention should be paid to factors such as air tightness, smooth startup, and suitability for large-scale production. Economic challenges stem from high input costs and the lack of a complete SOEC raw material supply system. The power cost required by SOEC electrolysis is currently higher, and the loss of materials and devices during operation increases operational costs. As a result, the entire SOEC industry is still in the process of continuous optimization and improvement.

In the future, research directions will focus on reducing the reaction temperature to the intermediate temperature range (500750 C), exploring materials and structures with good performance at lower temperatures, and analyzing the RWGS reaction mechanism in co-electrolysis reactions. In addition, research on H-SOEC operating at lower temperatures will continue, with a focus on exploring more electrode and electrolyte materials suitable for proton conduction and analyzing the proton conduction mechanism. Furthermore, the newly discovered Hybrid-SOEC can not only electrolyze CO2 but also realize the co-electrolysis of water and carbon dioxide, and explore electrolyte materials other than BCZYYb. Additionally, more gases such as CH4 and C2H6 can be electrolyzed to realize the diversification of resource utilization. Along with exploring the cell itself and the incoming gas, the final syngas products can also be utilized. Currently, the commonly used resource technology is the FT process, which adds catalysts in different proportions of H2/CO syngas to synthesize various valuable fuels under appropriate conditions. Additionally, H2 produced by SOEC can be combined with CO2 discharged from industry. Under high-temperature conditions, CO2 can be hydrogenated into a variety of hydrocarbons, such as methanol, high alcohol, and liquid fuel. Similarly, machine learning is currently a research hotspot, and combining machine learning with SOEC is also a promising direction. In conclusion, the exploration in the field of SOEC is worth continuing.
