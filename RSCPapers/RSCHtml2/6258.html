Abstract

MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials with tunable structure, high surface area, and adjustable pore size are attracting a great amount of research interest in many fields. This review focuses on the recent progress in the fabrication and utilization of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials for energy-related fields. A variety of mixed transition metal nanomaterials, including mixed transition metal oxides/chalcogenides/phosphides/carbides/hydroxides, single-atom catalysts (SACs) with dual metal sites, alloys and their hybrids are systematically discussed related to the synthetic routes. Afterwards, the applications of these materials in energy storage and conversion, including lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), supercapacitors, hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) are summarized. Finally, several challenges for the future development of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials are proposed.

Introduction

The global energy crisis and environmental issues have occurred as a result of unprecedented population growth and fossil fuel depletion.1â€“4 There is a pressing need to develop various types of efficient, green and sustainable energy sources, as well as new technologies related to energy storage and conversion. In recent years, renewable energy such as wind, tide and solar have emerged as eco-friendly substitutions that provide promising strategies to address the ever-increasing energy demand.5 However, these renewable energy sources are intermittent and highly depend on the season and region, thus hampering their widespread applications. Among these new energy storage and conversion technologies, including supercapacitors, batteries and electrochemical water splitting have drawn considerable attention because of their high efficiency, energy security and environmental benignancy.6,7 It is worth noting that the efficiency of these technologies is largely impeded by the sluggish kinetics of several key electrochemical reactions, such as the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR).8â€“10 The search for next-generation electrode materials and electrocatalysts featuring high activity to expedite these reactions has attracted particular interest from both fundamental research and industry.11â€“13 Presently, noble metal-based materials (e.g., IrO2, RuO2, Pt, Au and Ag) are considered the most efficient electrocatalysts for a variety of electrochemical reactions but their high cost, scarcity and insufficient durability represent major obstacles in real industrial applications.14â€“16 Therefore, exploring the alternative nanomaterials with efficient, robust and low-cost features is highly desired for the successful realization of energy storage and conversion.

Earth-abundant transition metal (e.g., Fe, Co, Mn, Ni, Cu, Mo, etc.)-based materials have been identified as excellent candidates for the electrochemical field owing to their large reserves and economical prices. For example, iron element accounts for 5.1% of the crust quality. The average content of nickel in the crust is abundant at 0.018% and cobalt reserves are 0.001% (mass) in the crust.17 By virtue of favorable electronic conductivity and rational electronic configuration, recent years have witnessed great progress in the elaborate design and widespread applications of transition-metal based nanomaterials, some of which have even exhibited comparable performance to precious-metal materials.18 Notably, mixed transition metal nanomaterials have better activities in catalytic reactions as compared to their single-element counterparts, benefiting from the complementary and synergetic effect of different metal components.19â€“21 These materials with multimetallic active sites are demonstrated to promote catalytic properties and selectivity in a variety of chemical transformations.22â€“24 Conventional synthetic methods using wet impregnation, co-precipitation and combustion have been reported and implemented on commercial scales, whereas, there is difficulty in precisely controlling their elemental composition due to the varied reaction kinetics and redox potentials of different elements.25 Thus, there is growing interest in seeking advanced nanomaterials with controlled structures and chemical compositions.

Metalâ€“organic frameworks, commonly called MOFs, which are self-assembled by metal nodes and organic ligands through chemical coordination bonds, have attracted significant attention in the scientific community due to their atomic-level structural uniformity, high surface area, tunable porosity and uniform pore structures.26â€“29 The metal center in MOFs usually are transition metal ions and lanthanide ions, and the organic linkers generally contain functional groups such as carboxylates, polyamines, pyridyl and cyano groups. To date, over 20000 different MOFs have been fabricated and characterized, as well as utilized in various applications, such as gas storage and separation, environmental remediation, sensing, and electrochemical energy storage and conversion.30,31 In addition to direct applications, MOFs have been widely developed as promising sacrificial templates/precursors to fabricate nanomaterials with high porosity, high surface area and distinctive morphology.32,33 Thanks to the flexibility of MOFs in structure and chemical composition, transition-metal-based materials can be purposely designed by selecting versatile metal centers and ligands. Using MOFs to construct mixed transition metal nanomaterials with high efficiency has many advantages: (1) tailored structure, size and composition can be achieved based on the targeted reaction by adjusting the synthesis parameters; (2) the interconnected carbon matrix derived from organic ligands is conducive to promoting the conductivity; (3) different metals can be integrated into one single structure without changing the original topology; (4) the coupling effect among diverse metals ensures their good performance in electrocatalysis.

Although several excellent reviews have been published on MOF-derived nanomaterials in energy storage and conversion, these articles mainly focus on the single metal-based materials and structure design, such as hollow and/or coreâ€“shell structure.34â€“36 A comprehensive and systemic summary on MOF-derived multi-metal materials has been neglected. In this review, the synthesis and electrochemical applications of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials are emphasized. Recent progress on the mixed transition metal nanomaterials based on MOFs in terms of synthetic routes is summarized and classified into versatile types, including mixed transition metal oxides, mixed transition metal chalcogenides, mixed transition metal phosphides, mixed transition metal carbides, mixed transition metal hydroxides, single-atom catalysts (SACs) with dual metal sites, alloys and their hybrids. The applications of these nanomaterials in energy storage and conversion are discussed, especially for lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), supercapacitors, the HER, OER and ORR. This review aims to provide new thoughts and a deeper understanding of the elaborate synthesis and important role of mixed transition metal nanomaterials in energy-related fields.

Synthesis of mixed transition metal nanomaterials

Over the years, the explosive development of MOF derivatives has been witnessed by the scientific community. Specifically, MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials have been extensively studied due to their enormous potential applications in catalysis, sensors, energy storage and conversion, environmental cleaning, and so on. Compared to mono-metallic systems, mixing two or more metals into an integrated framework benefits the improvement of performance through the regulation or reinforcement of each component. It should be mentioned that pristine metal-based nanomaterials have poor electric conductivity, and the inevitable aggregation during high-temperature calcination greatly decreases the exposed surfaces of active sites, resulting in inferior performance in many applications. Notably, the fabrication of nanostructured mixed transition metal components/carbon composites has been proved as one of the most effective and straightforward routes to achieve high performance.37â€“39 The carbons could be obtained directly from organics in MOF frameworks without the need for other carbon sources, which provides a facile and economical method for preparing metal and carbon composites. In particular, N-doped carbon-based catalysts with active N sites have been confirmed as promising alternatives to noble-metal-based catalysts.40 In addition, the utilization of supportive substrates such as porous carbon, amorphous carbon, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and graphene (GO) has been reported to achieve the orderly stacking of heterogeneous catalysts and maintain their porous structures after high-temperature carbonization, which is beneficial for improving the electrocatalytic activity and structural robustness.41â€“43

Currently, various novel synthesis methods have been reported for the fabrication of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials, which could be classified as follows: (i) pyrolysis treatment, (ii) solidâ€“liquid reaction and (iii) electrochemical synthesis method. Broadly, the pyrolysis treatment could be further categorized into three types: (i) self-pyrolysis in an inert atmosphere such as Ar or N2; (ii) thermal treatment in an oxidation/reduction atmosphere such as O2, air, H2S, NH3 and PH3; (iii) pyrolysis of MOF precursors with secondary reactants, including S powder, Se powder, NaH2PO2, NaSeO3, etc. Typically, heating MOFs in inert gas could produce alloys, single-atom catalysts and so on, while pyrolysis of MOFs with air/O2 can produce metal oxides. On the other hand, metal-containing nanomaterials like phosphides, chalcogenides, and carbides could be created by pyrolysis of MOFs with a reactive vapor or a secondary reactant. Solidâ€“liquid reactions involve ion exchange, chemical etching, self-assembly, etc., which usually proceed by reacting MOF precursors with chemical regents under hydrothermal/solvothermal conditions. Through such methods, metal chalcogenides and metal hydroxides are often synthesized. The electrochemical synthesis approach is another attractive route towards the fabrication of MOF-derived metal nanomaterials (metal oxides, chalcogenides, phosphides and their corresponding carbon composites) with favourable properties, which could effectively control the rate and extent of MOF conversion via adjusting the reaction parameters. Mixed transition metal oxides. MOFs have been widely used as sacrificial templates/precursors to fabricate nanoarchitectured mixed transition metal oxides through solid-state thermolysis.44 Thanks to the abundance of oxygen on the Earth, mixed transition metal oxides are more popular as compared with other transition-metal-based materials because of their facile and economical synthetic processes. With the carbonization of MOFs in oxidative atmospheres (i.e., air), metal atoms anchored in MOF scaffolds can be transformed into the corresponding materials, and other elements (e.g., C, N, H) are separately oxidized to gas. During heat treatment, these generated gaseous molecules are released from MOFs and promote the formation of hierarchical porous structures with interconnected pores. By calcination in inert gas, the composites of metal oxides-loaded porous carbon can be obtained. It is worth noting that metal ions in MOFs with a reduction potential below âˆ’0.27 V, such as Zn2+, Mn2+ and Mg2+, are inclined to combine with oxygen in organic ligands to produce metal oxide nanoparticles (NPs) during thermolysis in nitrogen flow.45

Considering the toxicity of cobalt ions, other transition metals with environmentally benign species have been investigated. As highlighted in the above discussion, metal oxides from MOFs have been extensively synthesized; however, most are single-component materials. Multicomponent-active metal oxides (metal oxide/metal oxide or metal oxide@metal oxide) have been reported to exhibit improved functional performance compared to single-component counterparts owing to the intimate contact and created interfacial effect between different components, thus bringing appealing possibilities for improving the activity, selectivity and structural robustness.63,64 As an example, Li et al. reported yolkâ€“shell structured ZnO/NiO microspheres using Znâ€“Ni MOFs as precursors.65 The nanorods grew uniformly on the surface of the microspheres and were interweaved with each other to assemble into a structure like that of urchins. In their study, the co-existence of Zn2+ and Ni2+ ions was very important for the formation of yolkâ€“shell Znâ€“Ni MOFs. The distinctive structure and the synergistic effect from ZnO and NiO of ZnO/NiO microspheres made them high-performance anode materials for lithium-ion batteries. Based on a similar synthetic procedure, a typical ball-in-ball structured nanocomposite of NiO/ZnO was synthesized by the thermolysis of bi-MOF in air.66 Such composites showed superior electrochemical performance when used as supercapacitor electrodes due to the unique double-shelled hollow architectures.

Zhan et al. conceived a novel synthetic protocol to prepare binary compositions of multi-shelled hollow CuO/ZnO through a rapid thermal process.67 In this composite, ZnO as the primary phase was directly derived from amorphous (am) ZIF-90, and CuO as the secondary phase came from the Cu NPs preloaded on amZIF-90. Specifically, special amZIF-90 with random network topology was first produced in the presence of cetyltrimethylammonium chloride (CTAC) surfactant. Such a sample showed a dandelion-like configuration with a large number of radially distributed nanorods, which may have resulted from the surface pressurization effect on the nanoscale. In the next step, Cu ions were introduced into amZIF-90 and then reduced in situ to Cu NPs by a reducing agent, which existed not only on the outer layer of amZIF-90 but also in the whole porous structure of amZIF-90. After pyrolysis of amZIF-90/Cu in air, binary oxide CuO/ZnO spheres with quadruple-shelled hollow structures were yielded. Notably, the number of shells in the hollow structure could be controlled by adjusting the calcining temperature or heating ramp rate. It was observed that the formation of the unique structure relied on the Cu NPs-aided oxidative degradation of spherical amZIF-90. Calcining amZIF-90 alone or amZIF-90/Au only produced nonhollow samples.

Mixed transition metal chalcogenides. Metal chalcogenides have a general chemical formula MX2, where M represents a transition metal (e.g. Co, Zn, Ni, Fe, Mo, etc) and X stands for a chalcogen, commonly S, Se or Te.83 Mixed transition metal chalcogenides, by the incorporation of hetero-metal elements, possess excellent catalytic activity and superior electrochemical performance due to the abundant active sites, large exposed surface area and good redox properties.84,85 When applied as electrodes for lithium storage, these transition-metal-based chalcogenides can present almost two or three times higher capacities than commercial electrode materials.86 In recent years, MOFs have been considered as attractive precursors for the preparation of mixed transition metal chalcogenides, particularly for metal sulfides and metal selenides, with the help of S and Se-containing chemicals.
Mixed transition metal sulfides. Mixed transition metal sulfides could bring down the kinetic energy barriers of the electrochemical reactions by facilitating charge transfer between diverse ions as well as manipulating electronic structures.87,88 The phase boundaries at the heterointerfaces of mixed metal sulfides can create numerous lattice distortions, mismatches, as well as defects, significantly impacting the charge carrier transport behavior via adjusting the reaction kinetics.89 Compared to metal oxides and hydroxides, metal sulfides have a lower bandgap energy and thus a higher specific capacity. Besides, they generally show improved electrical conductivity and faster reaction kinetics in light of the easier breakage of the Mâ€“S bond in metal sulfides than the Mâ€“O in metal oxides. Taking NiCo2S4 as an example, its conductivity is two-fold that of NiCo2O4 and almost 200 times that of NixOy or CoxOy.90 The considerable enhancement of the electrochemical properties makes them appealing in energy storage and conversion. To date, direct calcination methods and liquid-phase methods have been widely utilized for the preparation of mixed metal sulfides. The former typically involves the direct pyrolysis of MOF precursors with S powder, while the latter, including the hydrothermal method and solvothermal method, often takes place using Na2S and thioacetamide (TAA) as S sources.

Applications in energy storage and conversion

In the design of MOF-derived metal nanomaterials for specific applications, many pivotal properties that limit the activity of an electrochemical reaction need to be considered, including efficiency, the number of exposed active sites, conductivity, porosity, surface area, stability, structural robustness, durability, reserves and cost. Several strategies have been applied in the design of catalysts with high performance, including constructing hollow/coreâ€“shell structures, fabricating binary or ternary alloys, growing on stable substrates and introducing heteroatoms (e.g., N, S, P, etc.). Interestingly, MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials with tailorable compositions and structure create opportunities for designing promising catalysts that meet all these needs to the greatest extent possible. Besides, the precise control of crystal facets is important to the in-depth understanding of the structureâ€“performance relationship. Molecular simulations have shown that vertex and edge atoms are more active than face atoms in catalytic reactions, and bulk atoms in metal-based catalysts are completely ineffective.209 In this regard, morphology regulation and textural optimization of catalysts need to be considered. Most catalysts are developed under laboratory conditions with given ideal parameters but these catalysts usually suffer from severe challenges under actual operating conditions. Close collaborations between scientists and industrial partners are therefore important for purposefully fabricating desirable catalysts.
Lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors. MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials are favourable for energy storage applications in LIBs and supercapacitors because of their large pore volume, high surface area and abundant redox reactions between active metals.210â€“212 These features are beneficial for mass transfer and buffer the volume variation in the chargeâ€“discharge process. Mixed metal oxides and mixed metal sulfides stand out as promising electrode materials for energy storage owing to their rich redox reactions, high electronic conductivity and high theoretical capacity. Supercapacitors, as a new energy storage device between traditional capacitors and rechargeable batteries, are intensively employed in transportation, military and other fields. Electrocatalysis for the HER, OER, and ORR. Electrochemical water splitting, rechargeable metalâ€“air batteries and fuel cells are important technologies in energy conversion. The water splitting directly depends on the HER and OER, and metalâ€“air batteries are mainly involved in the OER and ORR. Also, the ORR is regarded as one of the fundamental electrochemical reactions for fuel cells. Electrocatalysts play a crucial role in these processes that can accelerate reaction kinetics, thus resulting in excellent electrochemical activity. In order to replace the costly noble-metal-based catalysts (e.g., RuO2 and IrO2 for OER and Pt for ORR), many cheap materials have been widely studied. Large surface areas and numerous accessible active sites enable MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials to have significant merits in the energy conversion process.225,226 Specifically, mixed metal oxides with superior electrical conductivity, sufficient electrode reactions and decent electrochemical reversibility exhibit excellent performance in electrocatalysis. Compared with transition metal sulfides, the corresponding metal selenides display superior electrocatalytic activity because of their higher intrinsic electrical conductivity.227 Metal phosphides possess a large number of unsaturated surface atoms, thus resulting in increased intrinsic catalytic activity.228 Benefiting from the electronic structures being similar to that of Pt, and remarkable chemical stability under acidic and alkaline conditions, transition metal carbides are usually utilized in electrocatalytic applications.229 Layered hydroxides are confirmed as promising electrocatalysts owing to their stability under alkaline conditions.230 SACs are of particular interest as electrocatalysts due to their high atom utilization and unique low coordination and unsaturation. Summary and outlook

MOFs as a novel class of porous crystalline materials have attracted much attention because of their high surface areas, ordered network structure, and permanent porosity. Considering the high flexibility in structure and composition, MOFs have been demonstrated as versatile sacrificial templates and precursors for the preparation of various functional materials. Their successful utilization provides great opportunities in many fields, including catalysis, sensors, energy storage and conversion, and so on. As highlighted in this review, mixed transition metal nanomaterials with well-defined structures are promising candidates in energy-related applications. The presence of a secondary metal enables the strong synergistic effect between diverse metals, giving them superior characteristics as compared to their monometallic counterparts with respect to their properties and applications. Owing to their various advantageous features, such as more exposed metal sites, excellent stability and conductivity, these resulting nanomaterials could adapt to more challenging reactions under harsh conditions. Herein, the recent progress on MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials related to the synthetic routes is comprehensively discussed, including mixed transition metal oxides, mixed transition metal chalcogenides, mixed transition metal phosphides, mixed transition metal carbides, mixed transition metal hydroxides, single-atom catalysts with dual metal sites, alloys and their hybrids. Their applications in the electrochemical area, especially for LIBs, supercapacitors, HER, OER and ORR, are summarized.

Though great progress has been achieved in the synthesis and electrochemical application of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials, this research field is still in its infancy, and substantial efforts should be dedicated to realizing the practical usage of MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials. In view of the published literature, there are certain challenges to be addressed in future development:

(1) Cost-efficient and environment-friendly synthesis paths should be considered. At present, sustainability-oriented synthesis research concentrates on limiting the formation of undesirable contaminants. Some transition metals with sufficient resources and less toxicity such as Fe3+, Cu2+, Mn2+ and Zn2+ could reduce the release of secondary pollutants into the environment, and are favorable for practical utilization. In addition to the metal nodes, some simple and green ligands such as fumaric acid and citric acid are considered as good alternatives to reduce environmental risks. Besides, it is expected that more MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials pyrolyzed at lower temperatures, exhibiting excellent performance, will be developed. The less-energy-consuming processes can better meet the requirements of future applications.

(2) Other types of MOF-derived multi-metallic nanomaterials should be explored. Among these reported transition-metal-based materials, oxide- and sulfide-based composites are in the majority, while there are fewer reports on other derivatives, such as selenide-, carbide-, phosphide-based materials and SACs with dual metal sites. Meanwhile, iron-, cobalt- and nickel-based materials are the most frequently used in catalysis. The investigations on other metal-based catalysts such as V, Mo and W are still insufficient, although they have proven to be efficient. Novel design strategies based on the high flexibility of MOF superstructures are needed to realize the diversity of mixed transition metal materials. Besides, â€œquasi-MOFâ€ is emerging to extend the family of MOF derivatives, which not only can expose more active sites but also retain the porosity. The unique characteristics of quasi-MOFs need to be further explored, which will create new opportunities for the development of next-generation nanomaterials.

(3) The conductivity of most MOF-derived mixed transition metal nanomaterials is poor. Even though the organic ligands in MOFs could be transformed into carbons after pyrolysis, there is still far from enough for usage. Combining highly conductive substrates such as graphene oxide, carbon nanotubes and amorphous carbon, as well as introducing additive carbon sources are favorable approaches to solving this problem. Composites not only possess synergistic effects between different components, but also conquer the defects of individual components.

(4) More specific efforts are required to unravel the relationship between structure composition and performance. During oxidation or reduction procedures, phase transformation and structural self-reconstruction may occur on the surface of most MOF-based catalysts. However, the mechanism for this unique phenomenon has not been figured out thoroughly. In particular, some advanced technologies, especially in situ characterization methodologies, such as in situ Raman spectroscopy and in situ X-ray absorption spectroscopy, are effective technologies for capturing the dynamic structural evolution under electrochemical processes.

(5) Serious side reactions with electrolytes may occur during cycling. This would cause a low initial coulombic efficiency, leading to significant capacity decay of the anodes. Based on the strategies used in silicon anodes, surface coating and pre-lithiation may provide a good opportunity to address this problem. Moreover, the electrochemical devices usually involve more than one process in practice, while the majority of mixed transition metal electrocatalysts based on MOFs are single-function. In the future, developing multifunctional MOF-derived electrocatalysts with high activity and stability for the HER, OER and ORR is extremely desirable.

Overall, mixed transition metal nanomaterials based on MOFs are promising candidates for electrochemical applications due to their unique structures and performance advantages. However, there is still a long way to go before they are widely employed on the industrial scale, and continuous efforts should be devoted to this highly exciting research field.
