Abstract

Employing a double overlapping wave band strategy based on DFT, five X-shaped anisotropic low energy gap donor compounds (D1â€“D5) have been designed for solar cell applications. A series of new PDI acceptor molecules are built to match each designed donor in terms of frontier molecular orbital energy levels by tuning substituents at P1 and P2 positions of PDI1. The designed donors consist of a central electron donor fragment benzodithiophene (DF), electron accepting fragments (A1 to A5) and terfuran and ethynyl-terfuran bridges (B1 and B2 respectively). The absorption bands of the designed donors based on TD-DFT not only cover the visible region but also extend to the infrared region of the spectrum. The donor fragment DF and multibranched spacers B1 and B2 are responsible for absorption in short and middle wavelength regions while the acceptor fragments contribute to the middle and long wavelength regions of the spectrum. In addition, PDIs also exhibit complementary absorptions in the visible range of the solar spectrum. Among designed donors, D1 exhibits ideal lowest band gap, FMO energy levels and exclusive broadest absorption because of the strongest electron withdrawing fragment. The lower Î»e values as compared to Î»h illustrate that these five donors would be favorable for electron transfer. The carrier mobility of D1 in the crystalline state has been predicted using the P21/c space group. D1 displays higher carrier mobility for Î¼e = 2.00 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and Î¼h = 1.7 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. The calculated Voc of D1 is 1.02 V. The designed donors and PDI acceptors are suitable and recommended for high performance solar cell devices.

Introduction

Solar energy is the most promising alternative above all renewable energy resources through the development of efficient and economical photovoltaic devices.1â€“3 The main focus of organic solar cell research is on polymers and small molecules, because of their attractive features like low-cost, light-weight and large area for fabrication on flexible substrates.4,5 In particular, the potential advantages of small molecules over conjugated polymer counterparts in terms of defined molecular structure, simpler purification, definite molecular weight and superior batch-to-batch reproducibility are more attractive for organic solar cells (OSCs).6,7 Among the small molecular materials for OSCs, a novel class of X8,9 or star10,11 shaped donor Ï€-conjugated bridge-acceptor molecules because of their high purity and chemically well-defined structures are receiving more attention. Previous studies have shown device open circuit voltage (Voc) scales with the difference between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy of the donor and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy of the acceptor.12 The power conversion efficiency (PCE or Î·) of solar cell devices is simply the ratio of power output (Pout) vs. power input (Pin), the latter is dependent upon the incident light flux (l0) and the former indicates the properties of the device itself (eqn (1)); namely the short-circuit current (Jsc), Voc and fill-factor (FF).13

In order to improve the efficiency of organic solar cells, an ideal donor must possess a lower HOMO to increase Voc, since the difference of energy between the HOMO of the donor and the lowest LUMO of the acceptor is directly related to Voc. Further, it must have a lower HOMOâ€“LUMO gap to harvest more sunlight, which also results in higher short circuit current density (Jsc). Moreover, strong and broad absorption encompassing both the visible and the near-infrared (NIR) regions of the solar spectrum and high charge carrier mobility is also necessary.14,15

Previously our group reported a strategy9 to design novel donor molecules, using double overlapping wave band theory for broad and intensive absorption in the visible and NIR regions of the solar spectrum. For this strategy, the basic building blocks required are: the electron-rich multibranched donor fragment16 (DF), the electron-excessive Ï€-bridges17 (B1 and B2) and the electron-deficient fragment18 (A). To this end, the overlapping absorption spectra of designed donors are the combination of Ï€â€“Ï€* absorption bands which include the short and middle (SM) wavelength regions and pushâ€“pull anisotropic intramolecular charge transfer (ICT)19 absorption bands between donor and acceptor fragments which cover the middle and long (ML) wavelength spectral range. This can be obtained by building an anisotropic planner compound. Hence, in the present paper, new anisotropic X-shaped donor compounds with planar conformations are designed by linking three basic units i.e., (A-B1)2â€“DFâ€“(B2-A)2 as shown in Scheme 1, where DF is benzo[1,2,-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene (BDT), the donor fragment (central core) that exhibits outstanding photovoltaic properties for applications in OSCs like narrow HOMOâ€“LUMO band gap, planar structure, charge carrier mobility, and broad absorption. In particular, the fused benzene associated with the dithiophene rings provides multiple-Ï€-conjugated branches.20,21 B1 and B2 are different electron-excessive Ï€-spacer chains (terfuran and ethynyl-terfuran respectively), which can serve both as a part of the light absorbing chromophore and a pathway for transporting charges. Furthermore, the use of furan reduces the use of long alkyl chain substituents for solubility of donor molecules in common organic solvents as it also contributes to overall solubility of the resultant final molecules.22 Actually furan incorporation helps in solubility by getting approach to that parts of structure otherwise which are not soluble.23 It might be the difference of the atomic radius of oxygen and its electronegativity as compared to sulphur of thiophene.24 Finally the optical and electronic properties of furan and thiophene containing conjugated molecules are almost the same.25 Similarly more electronegative oxygen atoms of furan help to lower the HOMO energy level of the donor compound, which in turn increases Voc.14,26,27 A triple bond insertion assists in the fine-tuning of molecular planar conformations by lowering the steric hindrance between the arms and the core and broadening the absorption spectra.28 End groups A act as acceptor fragments and have more electron deficient units and therefore they are able to reduce the energy gap (Eg) of final donors compounds.29 The multibranched Ï€-conjugated B12â€“DFâ€“B22 is considered as the donor fragment of the molecule. B12â€“DFâ€“B22 itself is responsible for the strong and broad absorptions in the SM wavelength region of the solar spectrum, while the anisotropic ICT between the Ï€-conjugated B12â€“DFâ€“B22 and the A fragments in the ML region of spectrum.

Further, suitable acceptor molecules i.e., perylenediimide (PDI)30,31 derivatives have been reported here for newly designed solar cell donors in terms of matched energy levels. New derivatives of PDIs (PDI3, PDI4, PDI5 and PDI7) are built by substituting electron donating and withdrawing groups at P1 and P2 positions of PDI1 (Scheme 2). We believe that our newly designed donor and acceptor molecules for solar cells seem to possess the following advantageous features. Firstly, use of different acceptor fragments resulted in absorption over a sufficiently broad wavelength range. Secondly, use of highly conjugated systems can lead to attain small Eg and broader absorption over the entire visible and NIR regions of the solar spectrum. Thirdly, the planarity of a conjugated system is pivotal to achieve higher ICT. Fourthly, geometrical-adjustment ability of selected building units is a good approach towards lowering the reorganization energies values of corresponding final compounds. Finally, new PDI derivatives are also liable for future practical applications.

Theoretical methodology

The ground state geometries including all neutral, cation, and anion molecules and properties of all organic donor molecules were obtained by utilizing density functional theory (DFT) calculations with B3LYP functional.32,33 All calculations have been carried out by Gaussian 09 program package.34 The 6-31G (d) split valence polarized basis set was used for all calculations, as it has produced the most suitable results for geometries and electronic properties.35,36 In addition, to ensure uniformity in the calculation, we performed DFT and time-dependent DFT (TD-DFT) calculations, also using other hybrid functionals such as PBE0 and B3P8637 and long range functionals WB97XD and CAM-B3LYP38 to compare with available experimental data (ESIâ€ ). Most approximate results were obtained for both geometrical parameters and the maximum absorption wavelength with B3LYP functional. It is worth mentioning that long range functionals are underestimated especially in terms of absorption properties. Frequency calculations, using the same methods as those for geometry optimizations, were performed for each of the obtained structures. All real frequencies have confirmed the presence of a local minimum. The density of states (DOS) was computed by the PyMOlyze program. The UVâ€“Vis spectra of all the compounds were investigated with TD-B3LYP/6-31G (d), based on the optimized geometries obtained by the B3LYP/6-31G (d) method in the gas phase. GaussSum 2.2.0 was applied to yield simulated spectra. To analyze MO analysis results for the charge transfer in the excited transition states we performed natural transition orbital (NBO) analysis using B3LYP functional and 6-31G (d) basis set. Although long range functionals (CAM-B3LYP, Wb97xd, LC-PBE, LC-BLYP, etc.) are well known to study electron transfer from donor to acceptor these methods are also more useful for large systems.39 We employed B3LYP functional also for NTO analysis as a long range functional underestimated especially in terms of absorption properties (as described in the ESIâ€ ). The NTO analysis has been carried out by a method as described by Martin.40

The B3LYP/6-31G (d) method was also used to optimize and calculate charge transport properties of the PDI derivatives. In order to save calculation time, the molecular structures were simplified by replacing the dodecyloxy group with a methoxyl group and cyclohexyl groups with methyl groups. Results and discussion

Selection of building blocks

The schematic design and chemical structural sketches of the building blocks of our newly designed X-shaped donors are shown in Schemes 1 and 2 respectively. Here, DF and Ï€-bridges B1 and B2 act together as the donor fragment B12â€“DFâ€“B22 of the designed donor compounds, while A1 to A5 are acceptor fragments. To check the reliability of these building blocks for the proposed strategy, we first conducted quantum calculations on DF, B12â€“DFâ€“B22 and seven A fragments to determine their energy levels.

Fig. 1 shows the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of these building blocks providing a supreme match of donorâ€“acceptor fragments for designed organic solar cell donor candidates. Addition of B1 and B2 into DF results in a B12â€“DFâ€“B22 fragment. This B12â€“DFâ€“B22 fragment has lowered HOMO, LUMO and Eg than DF due to higher conjugation. Hence, it argues convincingly that B12â€“DFâ€“B22 acts as a donor fragment due to higher HOMO and LUMO levels as compared to those of A fragments. As is well known that electron withdrawing strength is inversely proportional to the LUMO energy level, so it can be clearly understood by observing Fig. 1 which demonstrates that LUMO levels consecutively increase in energy from A1 to A7 i.e. A1 < A2 < A3 < A4 < A5 < A6 < A7, while electron withdrawing strength decreases from A1 to A7 i.e. A1 > A2 > A3 > A4 > A5 > A6 > A7. In this respect, benzene insertion into pyrazine (in the case of A6) causes the HOMO energy level to move up by 0.13 eV, but LUMO to move down by 0.51 eV as compared to A7. In addition, replacement of the six membered pyrazine ring (A6) with a five membered oxadiazole ring (A5) has enhanced the electron withdrawing strength on the one hand and lowered both HOMO and LUMO levels on the other hand. These findings are in accordance with previous experimental results.50 Also, the oxadiazole acceptor fragment and the terfuran Ï€-spacer have produced improved results than thiadiazole and thiophene analogues (Table S5â€ ).9 Furthermore, NO2 group substitution leads to a tremendous increase in electron withdrawing capability of acceptor fragments, which results in small Eg molecules. On the basis of these initial quantum calculations, it is found that A1, A2, A3, A4, and A5 have both lower HOMO and LUMO levels than the donor fragment B12â€“DFâ€“B22. It was therefore intended to constitute five X-shaped donor compounds for organic solar cells. These donors were abbreviated as D1, D2, D3, D4, and D5. Once the energy levels of model compounds deemed appropriate for OSC applications, the donors were built to investigate their electronic, absorption and charge transport properties.

Donor HOMO and LUMO energy levels relative to their fragments

Herein we report on a detailed analysis which explains the dependence of HOMO and LUMO and Eg of the designed donors relative to their building blocks (donor and acceptor fragments). Moreover, the purpose is to figure out some new fascinating facts by comparing these energy levels of donor compounds (D1â€“D5) with a donor fragment B12â€“DFâ€“B22 and acceptors fragments (A1â€“A5). Fig. 2 presents HOMO and LUMO energies of five donor molecules and their donorâ€“acceptor fragments. From this physical appearance it is clear that HOMO energy levels of the donor compounds are closer in energy to the HOMO of the donor fragment than those of acceptor fragments whereas LUMO energy levels of the donor compounds are closer in energy to LUMOs of acceptor fragments than that of the donor fragment. Hence, it supports the fact that usually HOMO of the donor compound is determined by the donor fragment while LUMO by the acceptor fragment. Further, the detailed comparative analysis showed that there is no more reliable non-linear fit equation relationship between HOMO, LUMO and Eg of all donor molecules and their building units (Fig. S4â€ ).

Frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs)

The molecular structures of five X-shaped donors in their ground state were fully optimized. The optimized geometries of five donors and B12â€“DFâ€“B22 fragment with planar conformations are shown in Fig. 3 (Fig. S1aâ€ ). The optimized structures also confirm their planar geometries, which facilitate the electrons to be delocalized within the molecule systems via conjugation to increase carrier mobility. The energy level diagrams of HOMO and LUMO of the donor compounds show that HOMO energy levels range from âˆ’5.41 to âˆ’4.85 eV while LUMO energies range from âˆ’3.86 to âˆ’2.76 eV (Fig. S2bâ€ ). The values for HOMO and LUMO energy levels increase from D1 to D5 except D4, as the electron withdrawing strength of acceptor fragments decreases. D1 and D2 have the lowest HOMO and LUMO energy levels due to the substitution of a NO2 group in A1 and A2. Additionally, HOMO and LUMO energy levels of D1 are lower than D2 as A1 has a five membered oxadiazole ring which is more electron withdrawing than the six membered pyrazine ring of A2. Similar behavior is observed for D3 and D4 due to nitrogen substitution in A3 and A4. The exceptional behavior of D4 as compared to D5 having higher HOMO and LUMO levels can be ascribed by the presence of the pyrazine ring in A4 while an oxadiazole ring in A5. Finally, the order of Eg for the designed donors is: D1 < D2 < D3 < D4 < D5, as with an increase in electron withdrawing strength of their acceptor fragments, the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of the final donor compounds are becoming lower which result in smaller Eg. Thus, it shows that substitutions of more electron withdrawing components result in lower HOMO, LUMO energies and smaller Eg of the final compounds.

The calculations have also been carried out for comparison purpose of D1 with one of the previously studied compound (X1)9 containing two thiophene analogues in the Ï€-spacer chain, thiadiazole as a part of the acceptor fragment and CN as the electron withdrawing group. We made three changes in D1 forming D1a to compare the effect of furan with the thiophene analogue X1 i.e. (I) use of two furan rings instead of three, (II) CN group substitution with NO2 and (III) use of thiadiazole as a part of A1 instead of oxadiazole. D1a has almost the same Eg and absorption properties like X1. Match of optoelectronic properties between donors and acceptors

For practical application, we present a scheme for an exact match between newly designed donors with suitable acceptor molecules i.e., PDIs, which are potential acceptors for photovoltaic devices. As expected electron withdrawing groups lower the HOMO and LUMO energy levels while electron donating makes them higher (Table S8â€ ). The calculated results for PDI derivatives are comparable with previous experimental studies.30 The LUMO energy level of the donor must be positioned above the LUMO energy level of the acceptor by at least 0.2â€“0.3 eV to ensure efficient electron transfer from the donor to the acceptor (Ed in Scheme 1). Stability

Molecular electrostatic potential surface (MESP) results from the computation of the electrostatic potential on a defined set of points surrounding the molecule. The surfaces are described in terms of total electronic density and produce a color map of the electrostatic potential. This color-coded description of the molecular morphology is very informative for the reactive potential of a molecule. In Fig. 11, electron density maps of all donors are represented by nature and intensity of color. The blue color acts as a region of low charge density (positive charge) and the red color acts as a region of high charge density (negative charge). The green color corresponds to the neutral regions. For D1 and D2, more partial positive charges are located on the carbon atoms over whole molecules, except the end groups of Bi, which are more negatively charged due to higher electron withdrawing A fragments. Stronger electron withdrawing A1 and A2 fragments impart more positive charge on the carbon atoms which render the oxidation difficult and enhance stability. It is convincing that higher stability of D1 and D2 is likely due to the more electron-withdrawing A1 and A2, which is according to previous results.61 In the case of D3, D4 and D5 because of the lack of â€“NO2 groups more negatively charges are located at the oxygen atoms of furan rings on Bi chains and nitrogen of oxadiazole and pyrazine ring of acceptor fragments. Moreover A3â€“A5 fragments limit the negative charge on their oxygen and nitrogen atoms and render carbon atoms less positive than those of D1 and D2 due to their less electron withdrawing fragments. Hence, we conclude that D1 and D2 are more stable than D3, D4 and D5. As electron withdrawing strength decreases from A1â€“A5, it influences the stability of the donors in the same order (D1â€“D5). Thus it is the strong electron withdrawing strength of oxygen and nitrogen substituents which enhance the stability of designed donors as compared to those carrying less electronegative sulfur and thiophene molecules. Consequently, the electron withdrawing strength of acceptor fragments and the pullâ€“push between donorâ€“acceptor fragments have a pronounced effect on the optical, electronic and stability properties of the designed donors.

Conclusions

In summary, we have designed five X-shaped anisotropic donor molecules D1â€“D5 for organic solar cell devices, following the double overlap band strategy. Also, in order to obtain a closer match, derivatives of PDI (PDI3, PDI4, PDI5 and PDI7) have been built by substituting electron donating and withdrawing groups at P1 and P2 positions of PDI1. These newly designed donors showed lower HOMO, LUMO levels, smaller Eg and higher carrier mobility. The significant constructing units i.e., central donor fragment benzo[2,1-b:3,4-bâ€²]dithiophene, anisotropic multi-branched Ï€-spacers terfuran and ethynyl terfuran and the end group acceptor fragments have enhanced the optical and electronic properties of the resulting donors. Because of their suitable FMOs and absorption properties, the designed PDI acceptors are preferable for designed donors. The optical investigations reveal that the central anisotropic multibranched donor B12â€“DFâ€“B22 fragment is responsible for Ï€â€“Ï€* absorption in short and middle wavelength regions, while A fragments are responsible in the ML wavelength region of the solar spectrum. With an increase in electron withdrawing strength of acceptor fragments from A1 to A5, there was a consistent decrease in the LUMO energy level of the corresponding donor compounds. There is no non-linear fit relationship between FMOs of the donor molecules and their building blocks. The absorption spectra of D1, D2 and D3 are extended over the NIR region in addition to the visible region of the solar spectrum. Among them D1 shows the broadest absorption wavelength (898 nm). Besides, the comparison of D1 with the previously studied thiophene analogue has shown that the use of furan instead of thiophene as a Ï€-spacer exhibits the same electronic and optical properties. In addition, the significant red shift in absorptions of D1â€“D3 and smaller Eg than previously studied sulfur and thiophene analogues is likely due to more electron withdrawing oxygen and NO2 group substitutions. Particularly the use of longer Ï€-spacer chains has helped to lower the LUMO energy levels. Therefore, our designed compounds especially D1 to D3 show more red shifts over the NIR region of the spectrum than previously studied compounds following this strategy. NTO analysis is also in agreement with the results of electronic excitations based on MO analysis. The combined analysis of reorganization energy and drift mobility has verified that these five donors would be better electron transporters for solar cell devices. Additionally, Voc of D1 has been calculated to be 1.02 V. All designed donors also retained a planar configuration in the ionic form with slight changes in the Câ€“C, Câ€“N, Câ€“S, and Nâ€“S bond lengths, which indicates their best geometry adjustment ability. The MESP results show that electron withdrawing strength of acceptor fragments and the pullâ€“push between donorâ€“acceptor fragments has pronounced effects on the stability properties of the designed donors. The stability decreases from D1 to D5 as the optical gap increases. Consequently, theoretical calculations confirm that our designed donors and acceptors would be suitable for organic photovoltaic devices.
