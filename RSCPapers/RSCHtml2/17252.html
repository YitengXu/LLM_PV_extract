Abstract

We report the colloidal synthesis of quaternary kesterite CZTSâ€“CZTSe heterostructures via anion exchange reactions on a kesterite CZTS template. The crystal phase selectivity during the synthesis (kesterite vs. wurtzite) is due to the initial nucleation of cubic Cu9S5 seeds, followed by incorporation of Zn and Sn. Upon injection of Se-precursor, which triggered simultaneous anion exchange and overgrowth of the pristine CZTS template, sandwich CZTSâ€“CZTSe (core-tip) nanoheterostructures were obtained. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and optical band gap measurement results suggest a change of intrinsic electronic structure of CZTS by Se-treatment. Our study not only provides insight into mechanisms of formation of kesterite CZTS nanocrystals (NCs) and subsequent anion exchange reactions, but also opens doors to access novel CZTSSe nanostructures for potential applications.

Introduction

Copper-zinc-tin-sulphide-selenide (Cu2ZnSnSxSe4âˆ’x, CZTSSe) has drawn attention as a promising, earth-abundant, non-toxic alternative to CuInxGa1âˆ’xSe2 (CIGS) and CdTe as a light absorbing material for thin-film solar cells.1â€“6 CZTSSe is a direct band gap semiconductor, with a band gap of 1.0â€“1.5 eV, an optimal range for use as an absorber in a single-junction photovoltaic device.7,8 Photovoltaic devices using CZTSSe directly deposited from hydrazine have achieved a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 12.6%, which is becoming competitive with other thin film solar cells.9â€“13 CZTSSe photovoltaics produced from copper zinc tin sulphide (CZTS) nanoparticle inks with post-treatment using Se vapor have reached a PCE of 9.0%.14â€“19 Direct use of CZTSSe nanoparticle inks could, along with other developments in processing, eliminate the separate selenization step and reduce cost and environmental impacts of production.

Synthesis of CZTSSe NCs were recently reported by injection of Sâ€“Se precursors into metalâ€“organo complexes.20,21 Compared to other polymorphs (stannite and wurtzite), kesterite is the best candidate as an absorber in thin film solar cells, because of its optimal band gap, maximum absorption and high electronâ€“hole-pair generation.22,23 In a study by Yang et al., the NC products were in the wurtzite phase, but underwent a phase transformation to kesterite after sintering. These devices had lower PCE compared to devices formed from kesterite NPs.24 However, direct synthesis of pure kesterite CZTSSe nanoparticles is challenging because of the minimal energy barrier and energy difference between the polymorphs.23 Moreover, the mechanism of phase-selectivity to kesterite is unclear. Therefore, a clear mechanism for robust synthesis of kesterite CZTSSe is needed.

In this work, we synthesized kesterite CZTSâ€“CZTSe nanoheterostructures by a hot-injection colloidal method. This is, to the best of our knowledge, the first report of kesterite CZTSSe NCs using an anion exchange approach. The template kesterite CZTS NCs were formed by incorporating Zn and Sn into Cu9S5, followed by Seâ€“anion exchange reaction from both tips of the overgrown CZTS. Optical absorbance (optical band gap) measurements and XPS results suggest that the intrinsic electronic structure was affected by Se-treatment. We believe this approach can enable creation of novel CZTSSe-based heterostructures for photovoltaics and other applications.

Separation of nucleation and growth phases is much more difficult in the quaternary compounds than in the binary ones.25 Most metal chalcogenides can crystallize in both wurtzite- and zinc blende-type polymorphs, and the energy difference and barrier between them are usually low.25 The overlapping nucleation window (e.g., similar nucleation temperature) leads to simultaneous production of both polymorphs, separated by stacking faults and grain boundaries.26â€“29 Moreover, the hexagonal packing of the atoms in (112) planes of the zinc-blend structure allows many kinds of stacking faults or polytype domains, which may be exacerbated by the added strain due to the imperfect stoichiometry of the structure.26,29 Multiple tools have been applied to understand the formation, atomic arrangement, and generation of defects in these structures.25,30 As the current synthesis exclusively produces kesterite (zinc blende-type) phase, understanding of its formation mechanism can be highly instructive.

To reveal the growth mechanism of CZTS NCs and understand the phase selectivity toward kesterite, we studied the reaction intermediates by extracting aliquots at different reaction times. As indicated by X-ray Powder Diffraction (XRD) patterns in Fig. S5 and S6,â€  we detected metallic Cu, SnO2 and ZnO NCs as solid products in these aliquots. Because metallic Sn and Zn NCs are very easily oxidized in air during sample handling, additional analysis is needed to understand the initial form of these NCs in the solution. The reduction potential of these three metal ions ranks as Zn2+ â†’ Zn (âˆ’0.76 V) < Sn2+ â†’ Sn (âˆ’0.14 V) < Cu+ â†’ Cu (+0.52 V).31 This indicates Cu+ ions were reduced to metallic Cu prior to injection of sulphur precursors, while Zn2+ ions could maintain their oxidation states. Experimentally, we also found Sn2+ was oxidized to Sn4+ (SnO2). These observations are supported by the previous report that Sn2+ and oleylamine (OAm) could behave as mild reducing agents.32,33 Moreover, the reducing potential of OAm should be weaker than Sn2+ (âˆ’0.14 V). Lattice spacings observed in HRTEM images in Fig. S6bâ€“dâ€  further suggest the formation of isolated metal/metal oxide NCs and not bimetallic (e.g., Cuâ€“Sn or Cuâ€“Zn) structures, consistent with the XRD pattern. In contrast, if the system initially existed as metallic Sn or Zn and then oxidized to SnO2 and ZnO, we should be able to track some metal/metal oxide interfaces in core@shell or dimer heterostructures,34â€“37 rather than homogeneous and defect-free metal oxide NCs (Fig. S6â€ ). Therefore, the reaction mixture at elevated temperature most likely contained Cu, ZnO, and SnO2.

Upon injection of S-precursor, a mixture of kesterite CZTS, cubic digenite Cu9S5, SnO2, and ZnO were quickly formed, as revealed by the XRD patterns in Fig. 2a. Extending the reaction time led to the conversion of other phases into CZTS NCs, indicated by reduced peak intensities for those phases. These ex situ measurements do not allow us to capture the initial nucleation stage of the reaction, but the formation of Cu9S5, which is the only metal sulphide observed (other than CZTS), illustrates the strong affinity of Cuâ€“S bonding and suggests that cubic Cu9S5 could act as the template for uptake of Sn4+ and Zn2+ ions to yield kesterite CZTS.

We can gain insight into the mechanism of formation of kesterite CZTS NCs using HRTEM imaging and structural models. The intermediate extracted 30 s after S-precursor injection shows that the reaction initially yielded three types of products: sub-5 nm nanospheres, âˆ¼10 nm nanoplates, and nanorods. Based on the TEM imaging in Fig. 2bâ€“e (30 s) and Fig. S7â€  (2, 5, and 10 min), the three products are identified as CZTS, SnO2, and ZnO NCs, respectively. Although it distinguishing CZTS and Cu9S5 phases is difficult because of their similarity in lattice spacings, we believe the initially formed Cu9S5 NCs should exist as nanospheres and act as the template for accepting Sn4+ and Zn2+ ions, as the lattice spacing and atomic arrangement for NCs with other shapes are exclusively assigned to SnO2 and ZnO. As the reaction proceeded, nanospheres grew at the expense of other products (nanoplates and nanorods), producing more uniform and phase-pure CZTS NCs (Fig. S8â€ ).

All the cations in both Cu9S5 and CZTS unit cells are tetrahedrally coordinated. The sulphur atoms in both structures are in a square array, as viewed along a- and a-/c-axis, respectively. The distance between neighbouring sulphur atoms are 2.72 Ã… in CZTS, which is only 2.3% smaller than the corresponding distance in Cu9S5 (2.78 Ã…). Moreover, the lattice constants for kesterite CZTS are akest = bkest = 5.43 Ã…; ckest = 10.85 Ã…, which corresponds to two Cu9S5 unit cells (adig = 5.56 Ã…), overlaid along the adig-axis. These features allow transformation from Cu9S5 to CZTS with minimal changes in the sulphur framework, which not only facilitates uptake of Zn and Sn but also helps to preserve the NC morphology.38,39 As we did not detect any ZnS or SnS/SnS2 in either HRTEM imaging or XRD patterns, we conclude that CZTS NCs are formed by incorporation of Zn and Sn into Cu9S5 templates. The observation of sequential nucleation and growth also explains the formation of linear heterostructures.

Pristine CZTS NCs served as a template for subsequent anion exchange. The TEM image in Fig. 3a reveals the ellipsoidal morphology of Se-treated NCs, whose overall size is larger than that of the pristine CZTS NCs. The ellipsoidal product NCs have average dimensions of 15.2 Â± 2.0 and 23.6 Â± 2.7 nm along short and long axes, respectively (Fig. S1â€ ). As shown in Fig. 3b and S9,â€  HR-STEM images reveal that the NCs are sandwich-like heterostructures. Based on the lattice spacings of (200) planes for the center region (2.7 Ã…) and tip regions (2.8 Ã…), the nanoheterostructures have a CZTS core and two CZTSe caps. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of nanoscale colloidal CZTSâ€“CZTSe heterostructures. The formation of heterodomains was also supported by the FFT filter analysis provided in Fig. 3d and S10.â€  During the anion exchange process, S atoms in the NCs were gradually replaced by Se atoms, and the larger size of Se atoms expanded the lattice, producing the observed shift of the diffraction peaks to smaller angle (Fig. 3e, e.g., (112), (220), and (312)). Although the peaks for (200) planes, whose lattice spacings were observed in the HR-STEM image, were relatively weak, they did not shift to lower angle, and even the line shape was unchanged. This reflects the fact that the CZTSe domain grew from the CZTS domain through anion exchange reaction along (200) planes, where the atomic rearrangement is minimal.

STEM-EDS mapping further reveals that the S signals are concentrated at the center of NCs while Se signals are located at both tips (Fig. 3c and S11â€ ). Cu and Sn are distributed throughout the entire NCs, while Zn signals are relatively weak on both tips, suggesting these CZTSe domain could be Zn-deficient. In contrast to an immiscible crystal boundary, which is commonly observed in metal chalcogenide heterostructures,38,40â€“42 we observe a diffusion layer between the CZTS and CZTSe domains. The lattice spacings and imaging contrast in the diffusion layer, which has a considerable amount of both anions (Fig. 3c and line scan profiles in S12â€ ), are different from those in core and tip domains. This observation shows that Se ions can coexist with S ions in the kesterite structure, producing an intermediate in the conversion from CZTS to CZTSe. Strictly speaking, the NCs can be considered CZTSâ€“CZTSSeâ€“CZTSe nanoheterostructures. Such a structure containing quaternary and quinary phases with partial anion exchange from S to Se, can potentially provide an electronic structure that promotes electronâ€“hole separation in solar cell devices.13,43,44 Further studies aimed at tuning the anion exchange ratio (CZTS:CZTSe) and corresponding solar cell device fabrications and testing are currently ongoing to gain deeper understanding of the relationship between the electronic structure and device performance for this novel nanomaterial.

Based upon TEM images of reaction intermediates like those shown in Fig. S13,â€  the monodispersed CZTS NCs tended to merge into larger NCs quickly after the Se-precursor was added (30 s). Prior to anion exchange, the incoming Se2âˆ’ can adsorb on the surface of CZTS NCs, and then partially displace the pristine OAm ligands.45 Such ligand replacement can trigger aggregation of NCs, consistent with previous reports.46,47 In addition, we observed the reaction yielded some small particles, whose population increased as the larger NCs grew. During this process, active Cu-, Zn- and Sn-species could form by partial decomposition/dissolution of the CZTS NCs. Consequently, the larger NCs grew into ellipsoidal NCs by consuming most of the small particles as the reaction proceeded. This Ostwald ripening process is driven by the difference in surface energy between large and small particles.48 Meanwhile, Se atoms started replacing S atoms from the tip of the NCs. The observation is consistent with observations for other metal chalcogenide systems in which ion exchange occurs preferentially on high curvature regions, low index facets and where surface atoms have low coordination (e.g., edges), as these sites intrinsically have lower energy barriers for the exchange reaction.49â€“51 The regio-selectivity of ion exchange reactions has been used as a robust method for creation of programmable semiconductor heterostructures.38,40â€“42 SEM-EDS results show that there were only 1.9 and 3.1 at% of Se incorporated into the particles after 30 s and 2 min of Se-injection, respectively (Table S1 and Fig. S13â€ ). This also supports our proposed mechanism that the overall reaction involved coalescence of CZTS NCs and a subsequent Se exchange process. Ellipsoidal CZTSSe alloy NCs have been observed elsewhere, though they contained a cubic CZTSSe core and two wurtzite CZTSSe tips.20,21 In that case, the CZTSSe system preferentially adopted an ellipsoidal morphology due to its intrinsic crystal structure, although the formation mechanism differs from the present study.

XPS spectra of the NCs before and after anion exchange were used to further understand the unique heterostructures. As shown in Fig. 4a, Cu 2p spectra reveal that copper before and after Se-treatments was present solely as Cu(I); no Cu(II) satellites were observed.52 After anion exchange, the binding energies of the Cu 2p peaks shifted to slightly lower binding energy (âˆ¼0.3 eV). This is expected, as S ions in the pristine CZTS lattice were replaced by Se ions with lower electronegativity.6,53,54 Similar shifting (0.3â€“0.4 eV towards lower BE) of XPS peaks was observed in Sn 3d and Zn 2p spectra. As shown in Fig. 4d, most sulphur was present as S2âˆ’ prior to Se-exchange, but di- or poly sulphide impurities are also likely to be present. We note that deconvoluting the S 2p spectrum for post-treated NC is difficult because of the interference from Se signals (âˆ¼160.0 and âˆ¼166.0 eV), while the line shape and downshifting of the spectrum are consistent with the observations for cations. Fig. S14â€  presents the XPS spectrum in the Se 3d region, indicating dominance of Se2âˆ’ with very minor di- or poly selenides. In all, the XPS analyses suggest the electronic structure of CZTS was altered by the anion exchange reaction.

The optical bandgap of the CZTSSe NCs can be estimated from a Tauc plot of (Î±hÎ½)2versus hÎ½ (where Î± is absorption coefficient and hÎ½ is the photon energy).55,56 For CZTS NCs, as shown Fig. S15â€  and Fig. 4e, the NCs obtained after 30 s and 2 min show plasmonic absorbance near 1600 nm and 1300 nm, and optical band gaps of 2.42 and 2.33 eV, respectively. The plasmonic feature also confirms the fact that p-type Cu-deficient Cu9S5 NCs were formed at the initial stage of the reaction, rather than stoichiometric Cu2S, which would not exhibit this feature. The as-synthesized CZTS NCs have a band gap of âˆ¼2.0 eV, which monotonically decreased with increasing reaction time after Se-injection, except that a small increase in band gap (2.04 eV to 2.10 eV) was observed immediately following Se-precursor injection, which could be related to a change in the size or surface charge of the NCs. The final CZTS-CZTSe heterostructures show the narrowest band gap of 1.67 eV. This value is slightly larger than pure CZTSe NCs (1.0â€“1.5 eV), because of coexistence with more CZTS in the composite (âˆ¼75% based on EDS results) with wider band gap. The Se-treatment-induced reduction of band gap is consistent with prior experimental and theoretical reports.57â€“60

Conclusions

In summary, we have produced a previously unavailable CZTSâ€“CZTSe heterostructure via anion exchange. Initially, we produced CZTS in the kesterite via nucleation of a cubic digenite Cu9S5 template followed by Zn and Sn incorporation. Subsequent Se treatment triggered both overgrowth and anion exchange processes on the CZTS template, producing CZTSâ€“CZTSe sandwich structures. We showed that the incorporation of Se can influence the intrinsic electronic structure by continuously narrowing the band gap. These observations and discussion can inspire new designs for novel CZTSSe nanomaterials for photovoltaics and related applications.

Experimental

Chemicals

Copper(I) chloride (CuCl, 99.995%), oleylamine (OAm, 70%), oleic acid (OA, 90%), trioctylphosphine (TOP, 90%), sulphur powder (S, 99.98%), and selenium powder (Se, 99.99%), were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Zinc acetylacetonate (Zn(acac)2, 99.995%), and tin chloride (SnCl2, reagent grade 98%), were purchased from Fisher Scientific. All chemicals were used as received.

Preparation of OAm/TOPâ€“Se precursor

As previously reported,61,62 we first dissolved 2 mmol of Se powder in 1 mL of TOP by sonication and stirring at room temperature under nitrogen until a clear solution formed. Then, to prepare TOP/OAmâ€“Se precursor, 1 mL OAm was added into the TOPâ€“Se solution and the mixture was stirred for 10 minutes.

Preparation of OAmâ€“S precursor

The OAmâ€“S precursor was prepared in a manner similar to our previous reports.61,62 Briefly, 2 mmol of S powder was added into 2 mL of OAm, heated to 130 Â°C, and held for 3â€“5 minutes to form a clear red OAmâ€“S solution.

Synthesis of CZTS NCs

In a typical synthesis, 1 mmol CuCl, 0.5 mmol Zn(acac)2, and 0.5 mmol SnCl2 powder were mixed with 10 ml OAm. The mixture was held under vacuum at 110 Â°C for 30 min and then heated to 200 Â°C under nitrogen. The colour of the mixture turned from transparent to golden, indicating formation of organoâ€“metal precursor complexes. The temperature was further increased to 225 Â°C, followed by injection of 2 mL of OAmâ€“S precursor. After injection, the temperature was held for 30 minutes. To collect the NCs, 10 mL of ethanol was added, followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 2 min. To purify the NCs, the NCs were redispersed in hexane, then ethanol was added, followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 2 min.

Synthesis of CZTSâ€“CZTSe NCs through anion exchange

For synthesis of CZTSâ€“CZTSe heterostructures, after the crude reaction mixture for synthesis of CZTS NPs was held for 30 min, 2 ml of OAm/TOPâ€“Se precursor was injected in the CZTS NP reaction mixture at 225 Â°C. The temperature was held at 225 Â°C for another 30 min. To collect the NCs, 10 mL of ethanol was added, followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 2 min. To purify the NCs, the NCs were redispersed in hexane, then ethanol was added, followed by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 2 min. The NCs were dispersed in hexane at a concentration of 10 mg mLâˆ’1 for subsequent characterization.

Characterization

TEM images were obtained using a JEOL JEM-2010 at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. HRTEM images were obtained using a FEI Tecnai G2 F30 at an accelerating voltage of 300 kV. High-angle annular dark-field scanning TEM (HAADF-STEM) images, HR-STEM images, and STEM energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (STEM-EDS) maps were collected using a JEOL JEM-ARM200F STEM equipped with spherical aberration correctors on the image at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. TEM grids were prepared by dropping a dilute NC dispersion onto a carbon-coated copper or gold TEM grid and allowing the sample to dry under air. Size distributions were obtained from TEM images by measuring at least 200 NCs from multiple TEM images from different areas of the grid, using Nanomeasurer ver. 1.2. XRD measurements were carried out using a Rigaku Ultima IV diffractometer with a Cu KÎ± X-ray source. Samples were prepared by drop-casting concentrated NC dispersions onto glass slides. Elemental analysis of NCs by energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was obtained using a Hitachi S-4800 within a Zeiss Auriga scanning electron microscope (SEM). XPS were acquired on an ESCALAB 250Xi with an Al KÎ± X-ray source (1486.6 eV) with nominal spot diameter of 400 Î¼m. The pressure in the analysis chamber was maintained below 5 Ã— 10âˆ’8 mbar. Optical extinction of NC dispersions was measured using a Shimadzu 3600 UVâ€“visibleâ€“NIR spectrophotometer.
