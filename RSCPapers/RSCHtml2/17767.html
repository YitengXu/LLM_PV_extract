Sensitizer-free photon up conversion in (HQ)2ZnCl4 and HQCl crystals: systems involving resonant energy transfer and triplet–triplet annihilation†

Amira Samet *a, Sebastien Pillet b and Younes Abid *a
aLaboratoire de Physique Appliquée, Université de Sfax, BP 1171, 3018 Sfax, Tunisia. E-mail: younes.abid@fss.rnu.tn
bUniversite de Lorraine, BP 239, 54506 Vandœuvre-les-Nancy, France

Received 10th November 2019 , Accepted 9th December 2019

First published on 9th December 2019

Abstract

This work deals with normal luminescence and up-conversion luminescence involving charge transfer and triplet–triplet annihilation in the lead free hybrid materials (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and HQCl salt; HQ is the hydroxyquinolate cation (HQ+ = C9H8NO+). The crystal structures were determined by X-ray diffraction and the optical properties were investigated by optical absorption and photoluminescence measurements and electronic band structure calculations. Under UV excitation, the normal luminescence is associated with π–π* transitions within the organic cation and involves energy and charge transfer between the inorganic ion and organic cation. Moreover, photoluminescence measurements under various excitation wavelengths performed on the hybrid (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and the salt HQCl have shown efficient up conversion of light from the near infrared (855 nm) to the visible region at 471 nm and 490 nm respectively. This behavior is described as sensitizer-free up-conversion luminescence based on the triplet–triplet annihilation process (TTA-UCL). These compounds are believed to be the first sensitizer-free TTA up-converting materials found in the organic metal halide family. Compared with the conventional TTA-UC solid systems based on precious and heavy metal organic complexes, the title compounds exhibit efficient photon up-conversion. Furthermore, they have extended the NIR conversion photons to a wide spectral interval of about 300 nm centred around 850 nm.

1. Introduction

Processes combining low energy photons to convert them into higher energy photons are known as photon up-conversion (UC). In recent years these processes have proved to be of great interest for various applications, especially to improve the efficiency of photovoltaic cells by extending their spectral operating bands to ranges below the gap (red and near infrared: NIR). Among the mechanisms known hitherto, such as two-photon absorption (TPA),1–4 or up conversion based on energy transfer in lanthanide doped nanocrystals,5,6 the triplet–triplet annihilation-based up-conversion process (TTA-UC) has attracted great attention as it may occur at lower excitation intensities than other mechanisms,7–16 such as solar light (a few mW cm−2 is sufficient, unfocused terrestrial solar irradiance is 100 mW cm−2). In a typical TTA-UC mechanism (Fig. 1), a sensitizer molecule absorbs a low energy photon (long wavelength), creating a singlet excited state that undergoes intersystem crossing (ISC) to the triplet excited state, this excitation is then transferred to the triplet state of an acceptor molecule or ion that acts as an emitter, and then two triplet emitters collide and two excitations fuse into a higher energy singlet excitation, which leads to the emission of a photon of higher energy via the TTA mechanism (Fig. 1). So far, the best efficiencies were obtained for TTA-UC systems in solution because of molecular diffusion, which favors collisions.17–19 Moreover, recent studies have been conducted in solid state systems demonstrating that the use of ionic interactions may be a strategy for obtaining competitive efficiencies.20
Fig. 1 Schematic representation of the triplet–triplet annihilation up-conversion mechanism (TTA-UC); the triplet levels may occur with a sensitizer via Inter System Crossing (ISC) or without a sensitizer via Spin Orbit Coupling (SOC) or via singlet–triplet Fission (SF) within the emitter.

Recently, many pioneering studies have been reported on new sensitizer free up-conversion systems that do not include heavy or precious metals. In such systems the triplet levels are not populated via the ISC mechanism, but rather via spin orbit coupling as for the case of anthracene bromide21 or via a singlet–triplet fission mechanism like that described for many organic crystalline systems such as pentacene,22–25 tetracene22,23 or solid thiophene based organic polymers.24

Recall that the Singlet-Fission mechanism (SF) is described as a bimolecular process occurring between two identical or different organic molecules with aromatic rings. Under IR irradiation the strong interaction between these two chromophores can generate fission of the singlet level into two triplet levels according to the following equation:

S0 + S1 (fission) → (T1, T1)
Then two triplet levels can again fuse into one level 2T to give the emission of a double energy photon via the triplet–triplet annihilation mechanism.
(T1, T1) (TTA) → 2T1.
In this work we will focus on the study of hydroxyl quinolinate zinc chloride: (HQ)2[ZnCl4] material, belonging to the vast family of organic metal halides, which contains the famous class of inorganic organic perovskites known for their potential interest in optoelectronic and photovoltaic devices.26–28 Given the paucity of literature concerning the TTA process in hybrid crystals in general and those based on hydroxyquinoline in particular and in order to investigate the contribution of organic and inorganic entities in this process, we have conducted a comparative study of the hybrid (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and HQCl salt. In both compounds, the electronic structure and the stacking of the organic HQ molecules into dimers are typically in favor of an upconversion mechanism involving the singlet fission process followed by triplet annihilation (SF-TTA-UC).

These compounds will be investigated by optical spectroscopy measurements and by theoretical band structure calculations. We will demonstrate below that these materials may operate as normal and up conversion luminescence systems involving resonant energy transfer (RET) and the TTA-UCL mechanism respectively. This compound would be the first material of hybrid organic metal halides endowed with the property of TTA-UC luminescence. These hitherto unrecognized properties turn this compound and similar hybrid materials into prospective candidates for potential applications as up converting materials in photovoltaic conversion, optoelectronic devices or biological imaging.

2. Materials and methods

2.1. Crystal synthesis and structure determination

Single crystals of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] were synthesized by slow solvent evaporation at room temperature. Firstly, the hydroxyl quinoline chlorine salt (C9H8NO·Cl), abbreviated as (HQ·Cl), was obtained by dissolving (C9H7NO) in an aqueous HCl (37%) solution. The (C9H8NO·Cl) salt appears some days later following the remove of residual solvent. Then, 1 mmol of the resulting (C9H7NO·Cl) and 1 mmol of ZnCl2 were dissolved in HCl solution (37%) and stirred well at ambient temperature. One week later, yellow crystals of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] were formed.

Good quality single crystals of the hybrid (HQ)2[ZnCl4] of 0.30 × 0.35 × 0.01 mm3 were selected for X-ray diffraction. Data collection was carried out at ambient temperature on a KCCD diffractometer using MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The crystal structure was solved by direct methods and refined by the full matrix least-squares technique using the SHELX-97 crystallographic software package.29 In a separate experiment a single crystal of HQ·Cl salt was selected. The crystal structure was determined at 100 K using a Bruker D8 diffractometer equipped with a CMOS PHOTON 100 detector, and using MoKα radiation. The unit cell determination and data reduction were performed using the APEX2 program suite on the full data set. An empirical absorption correction was applied. The structure was solved by direct methods and successive Fourier difference synthesis, and was refined by the full matrix least squares method using the SHELX-2014 crystallographic software package. All non-H atoms were refined anisotropically. H atoms were located in difference Fourier maps and treated using a rinding model, constraining the isotropic displacement parameters to 1.2Ueq of the parent C atom.

2.2. Optical measurements

Optical measurements were performed on thin films of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and (HQ·Cl) deposited by the spin coating technique on glass substrates. Photoluminescence (PL) as well as excitation photoluminescence (PLE) measurements were performed on a Fluoromax-4 spectro-fluorimeter equipped with a xenon lamp as an excitation source. In order to take into account the variation of the excitation intensity over all the UV-visible wavelength range all PL data have been multiplied by a calibration ratio, so that all PL spectra seem to be measured under the same excitation power. Optical absorption (OA) measurements were performed, on the same samples, using a conventional UV-vis spectrophotometer (HITACHI, U-3300).

2.3. Electronic band structure calculations

The calculations were achieved with the modified Becke and Johnson (mBJ)30 exchange potential in the framework of the full-potential linearized augmented plane wave method, as implemented in the Wien2k package.31 The radius of Muffin-Tin spheres (RMTs) was chosen in such a way that there was no charge wastage from the core, and therefore total energy convergence was ensured. For a wave function inside the interstitial region, a plane wave cutoff value of Kmax = 3/RMT was taken. Self-consistency was achieved since the total energy difference between successive iterations was less than 10−5 Ryd per formula unit.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structures

The crystal structure of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] has been published elsewhere.32 Briefly, (HQ)2[ZnCl4] crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with a primitive unit cell of dimensions: a = 15.2108(17) Å, b = 8.1418(8) Å, and c = 16.7204(19) Å and four formula units (Z = 4). The projection of the crystal structure is shown in Fig. 2. It consists of discrete [ZnCl4]2− tetrahedra surrounded by (HQ+) organic cations. (HQ+) cations are associated in interacting dimers through π–π stacking with centroid distances of 3.657 Å and interacting with [ZnCl4]2− ions through N–H⋯Cl (dH⋯Cl = 2.344 Å) and O–H⋯Cl (dH⋯Cl = 2.284 Å) hydrogen bonds. The HQ·Cl salt crystallizes in the triclinic P space group with a primitive unit cell of dimensions: a = 7.2651(8) Å, b = 8.2751(9) Å, and c = 8.4760(9) Å, and angles α = 78.281(3)°, β = 86.099(3)°, and γ = 66.537(3)°. It consists of (HQ+) organic cations arranged in dimers exhibiting π–π stacking interactions with centroid to centroid distances of 3.554 Å (Fig. 3). The cations are further connected by a network of three types of hydrogen bonds: N–H⋯Cl (dH⋯Cl = 2.36(1) Å), C–H⋯Cl (dH⋯Cl = 2.74(1) Å) and O–H⋯OH2 (dH⋯O = 1.70(3) Å). By comparing the two crystal structures, it is interesting to note that the organic cations [C9H8NO]+ are arranged in dimers in both cases, nevertheless, the intermolecular interactions in both crystals show two remarkable differences:
Fig. 2 (a) Crystal structure packing of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] projected along the [110] direction and (b) structural arrangement within the organic cation. Black arrows depict the intra π–π stacking interactions, while N–H⋯Cl and O⋯Cl hydrogen bonds are shown as dotted lines.

Fig. 3 (a) Crystal packing of the HQ·Cl salt projected along the [001] direction, and (b) structural arrangement within organic cation dimers. Black arrows depict the intra-dimer π–π stacking interactions, while N–H⋯Cl, C–H⋯Cl and O–H⋯OH2 hydrogen bonds are shown as dotted lines.

(i) in the hybrid material each quinoline molecule is linked to two tetrahedra ZnCl4via ionic bonds CH⋯Cl and OH⋯Cl, whereas in the salt each molecule is bound to a chlorine ion via a CH⋯Cl hydrogen bond on one hand and via a van der Waals CH–O bond with a neutral water molecule on the other hand (this indicates that the charge transfer is more favored in the hybrid than in the salt), and

(ii) the aromatic cycles are closer in the salt than in the hybrid.

This specific structural topology induces strong overlap between the molecular orbitals of the cations, and may be related to the observed optoelectronic properties, such as the blue shift of the PL signal of the hybrid and its enhancement with respect to that of the salt (see below).

3.2. Density of states and band structure calculations

As for the case of all the hybrid materials containing several molecules per unit cell and involving interactions of different natures (covalent, ionic, van der Waals…), the detailed and rigorous interpretation of the absorption and emission spectra is not so obvious.

It has been found that in addition to comparisons with peer systems, the calculation of band structures and electronic density of states (DOS) provides an effective tool for accurate and rigorous optoelectronic interpretation. In this context we performed a simulation of the DOS in terms of periodic DFT calculations (Fig. 4). Let us recall that for conventional IV or III–V semiconductors whose cohesion is ensured by strong covalent bonds, the valence bands and the conduction bands are generally characterized by large dispersion and by a relatively continuous density of states. For our hybrid material, as for the case of homologous materials,33,34 since it is a crystal formed by organic and inorganic molecular entities interacting with weak bonds of the van der Waals or hydrogen type, the dispersion curves are fairly flat, and the band structure is formed of several discrete sub bands. It is then more appropriate to use the HOMO–LUMO frontier orbital notion for each type of molecule, rather than valence band and conduction band.


Fig. 4 Calculated electronic band structures and electronic density of states of the hybrid (HQ)2[ZnCl4].

In Fig. 4 is illustrated the calculated electronic band structure along the high symmetry points of the Brillouin zone of the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] hybrid compound. Partial densities of states of the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] hybrid compound are also plotted in Fig. 4. The same calculations were performed for the (HQ·Cl·H2O) salt (see the ESI†). As we can see in the same figure, the optical band gap is estimated at 2.63 eV (471 nm). It is also obvious that the valence band maximum is composed mainly of O(2p) and C(2p) organic atomic orbitals, with a little contribution of Cl1(3p) and Cl2(3p) inorganic ones. Whereas, the lowest levels of the conduction band, rather the sub band sweeping the energy range from 2.65 eV to 3 eV, correspond closely to the orbitals of carbon C(2p) and nitrogen N(2p) atoms of the organic molecule with a slight contribution of Zn(s,p) and Cl(p) orbitals. These results may indicate that in this material the conjugated electrons are strongly involved in electronic transitions and charge transfer, contrary to similar compounds based on non-conjugated organic ligands in which the inorganic part mainly contributes to the valence band. Moreover, the alignment of the energy levels of the quinoline π-conjugated cation with those of the inorganic [ZnCl4]2− anion may be at the origin of an enhancement of carrier mobility in the hybrid structure. We will see below that the results obtained by the semi-local mBJ method are in perfect agreement with those of the experimental data. Indeed, the mBJ exchange potential allows the calculation of band gaps with a precision similar to that with the very expensive Green function calculations,35 and therefore has the capability to overcome the well-known shortcoming of DFT-based methods in predicting the band gaps.

4. Optical properties

4.1. Normal photoluminescence

As we have mentioned, because of the existence of two luminescent entities in the same crystal, the organic cation (HQ+) on the one hand and the inorganic [ZnCl4]2− ion on the other hand, and in order to distinguish and analyze the contribution of each ion in the process of emission, we have systematically performed optical absorption (OA), photoluminescence (PL), and photoluminescence excitation (PLE) measurements on the (HQ·Cl) salt as well as on the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] crystals. As seen in Fig. 5, the optical absorption spectra of both the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] compound and (HQ·Cl) salt show the same shape consisting of two wide absorption bands with maxima around 315 nm (3.93 eV) and 370 nm (3.35 eV) associated with deep levels of the organic cation.
Fig. 5 Optical absorption, PL and PLE spectra of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and the salt HQCl. For comparison the PL spectrum of Cs2ZnCl4 is drawn according to ref. 36. PL measurements were performed under 375 nm excitation.

Whereas, the PLE measurements show a very broad excitation band, covering the optical absorption bands entirely and leading to an optical gap of 2.63 eV (471 nm). Many recent studies were carried out on hybrid compounds based on ZnCl4 and containing optically inactive molecules such as (DMA)2ZnCl4,36 (APC)2ZnCl437 and [C7H16N2][ZnCl4];38 these studies reveal that the ZnCl4 ion is characterized by a broad absorption band between 300 nm and 400 nm corresponding to a HOMO–LUMO gap around 3.1 eV, whereas the photoluminescence was measured only for Cs2ZnCl4 and shows a PL band around 450 nm (2.75 eV).39 The obtained results are in good agreement with the calculated band structure, confirming the alignment of the electronic levels associated with the (HQ+) organic cation and the [ZnCl4]2− anion as predicted by the DFT simulation. Moreover, as seen in Fig. 5, the photoluminescence spectrum recorded under 375 nm excitation is characterized by an extremely intense line at 471 nm fairly coinciding with the PLE band limit; compared with the PL spectrum of the (HQ·Cl) salt, this line originates from the π–π* transitions within the organic molecule. The blue shift from 490 nm in the salt to 471 nm in the crystal is due to the stacking of the aromatic rings of the (HQ+) cation and their interaction with the [ZnCl4]2− anion.

The results we have at this stage lead to an energy diagram for the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] crystal described by a wide HOMO–LUMO of 3.1 eV associated with the inorganic anion [ZnCl4]2− with an inorganic excitonic level at 2.81 eV which is resonant with electronic levels of the organic (HQ+) cation. This diagram (Fig. 6) suggests an interaction between the inorganic and the organic ions involving resonance energy transfer (RET).40,41 One must recall that RET is a dipole interaction which describes at the molecular scale the transfer of energy (or charges) between two chromophores (molecules or ions): a donor (D) entity having a high HOMO–LUMO band gap and an acceptor entity (A) with a smaller HOMO–LUMO band gap. This interaction is conditioned by mainly two criteria: (i) the distance between the acceptor and donor entities must not exceed a few nanometers, and (ii) the donor emission spectrum must overlap significantly the absorption spectrum of the acceptor. In the case of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] both criteria are quite satisfied. In fact as seen above in Fig. 5(b), the emission band of [ZnCl4]2− (donor) has significant overlap with the excitation spectra of the (HQ) cation (acceptor). In addition, from the X-ray diffraction measurement, it is noticeable that ZnCl4 tetrahedra are linked with (HQ) organic cations through N–H⋯Cl and O–H⋯Cl bonds of 2.344 Å and 2.285 Å length respectively, which is in favor of Dexter charge transfer.42 Furthermore, as described above, the DFT density-of-state calculation demonstrates a coincidence of the electronic levels of the hybrid ZnCl bond with those of the organic cation, a fact which is in favor of energy and charge transfer between the anionic [ZnCl4]2− tetrahedra and the (HQ+) cation. Thereby, the intense luminescence of the title compound results from radiative recombination occurring within the HQ+ organic cation. As illustrated in Fig. 6, under the 375 nm incident laser, ZnCl4 inorganic excited carriers will be transferred to the π* levels of the organic cation via the Dexter charge transfer process, leading to the enhancement of organic photoluminescence. The blue shift of the organic emission from 2.5 eV in the salt to 2.63 eV in the hybrid stems from the intermolecular interactions between the aromatic rings of the organic cations (Fig. 2 and 3). Furthermore, as seen in Fig. 5a, we notice an enhancement by up to 3 orders of magnitude of the emission intensity in (HQ)2[ZnCl4] compared to that of the salt HQCl. This can be explained by the fact that in the hybrid compound crystalline cohesion is ensured by ionic interactions, which are in favour of energy and charge transfer, whereas in the salt van der Waals interactions are added with neutral water molecules, which are less favourable for these transfer mechanisms.


Fig. 6 Energy diagram of the electronic structure of the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] crystal. The relevant energy levels include the HOMO and LUMO levels for the [ZnCl4]2− anion and (HQ+) cation. The red dashed lines correspond to the nonradiative transitions outlining the conversion of inorganic excitation to organic excitation.

4.2. Up conversion photoluminescence

So far, to the best of our knowledge, no up-conversion studies have been performed on ZnCl-based hybrid crystals or on crystals containing the hydroxyl quinoline entity. In the present work, in order to characterize the resonant energy transfer, we carried out a systematic study of photoluminescence under variable excitation ranging from UV to IR (250 nm to 900 nm); surprisingly, we found in addition to the normal luminescence described above photoluminescence under IR excitation. In Fig. 7 we present the variation of the photoluminescence band at 471 nm as a function of the excitation wavelength.
Fig. 7 (a) PL spectra of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] at different excitation wavelengths. (b) Intensity of PL spectra of the (HQ)2[ZnCl4] compound and the (HQ·Cl) salt as a function of excitation wavelength. (c) Comparison of the PL spectra of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and (HQ·Cl) under 400 nm (normal PL) and (d) comparison of the PL spectra of (HQ)2[ZnCl4] and (HQ·Cl) under 855 nm (up-conversion luminescence). In (a) and (b) all data were multiplied by a calibration ratio to take into account the variation of the excitation power with the excitation wavelength.

As we can see, for the UV-blue excitation, intense luminescence is observed for wavelengths between 250 nm and 500 nm and the intensity reaches a maximum around 350 nm, and then the intensity drops off steeply for excitation between 500 and 700 nm. For wavelengths between 700 and 1000 nm the photoluminescence signal reappears and reaches its maximum under excitation around 855 nm. For a reliable investigation of the up-conversion mechanism and in order to determine the role of each of the organic and inorganic components, we have carried out, in the same conditions, photoluminescence measurements under different excitations on the hybrid material as well as on the organic cation in the salt. The results are illustrated in Fig. 7. We note that for both the hybrid and salt materials for the same infrared excitation range around 850 nm we obtain the same emission spectrum in the visible region around 470 nm and 490 nm respectively. In addition, the PL spectra show the same profile and differ only in intensity. This result indicates that in both the normal and up-conversion regimes, the emission processes involve radiative recombination occurring between the same initial and final electronic states. These comparative measurements lead to the following deductions:

(i) in both the up and normal regimes the emission originates mainly from the organic cation;

(ii) the TTA-UC process occurs without a sensitizer, a fact which is expected as the Cl− and [ZnCl4]2− ions are not endowed with singlet or triplet levels that can absorb in the IR and NIR regions.

Among the possible sensitizer free mechanisms, it seems that the singlet-fission process followed by triplet–triplet annihilation perfectly describes this up-conversion process from the NIR to the visible region. As we have already pointed out, many recent studies have been carried out on up-conversion systems involving singlet fission (SF) processes such as pentacene,22–25 tetracene22,23 or solid thiophene based organic polymers.24

In these studies it has been established that SF is conditioned mainly by the following criteria:

(i) SF is a bimolecular process occurring between two chromophore molecules containing at least two aromatic rings and the energy of the singlet level is almost equal to twice the energy of the triplet level;

(ii) in the solid state, the π stacking of aromatic rings and their interactions favour and facilitate the SF process;

(iii) the triplet exciton diffuses from one molecule to another via Dexter transfer, which consists of electron exchange at short distances not exceeding 1 nm.

For both materials studied herein, all these criteria are perfectly satisfied indeed:

(i) The quinoline (HQ+) cation has two aromatic rings and has singlet energy levels (1.45 eV, 413 nm) greater than double the triplet energy level (2.91 eV, 426 nm).

(ii) The structural study by XRD showed that the HQ+ cations are associated in pairs and their interaction favours the SF process.

(iii) The XRD measurements have also shown that the interaction distance π–π between two cations is of the order of 3.5 and 3.6 A, which allows the overlap of wave functions between neighbouring molecules to facilitate Dexter transfer.

Consequently, as we have described in Fig. 8, the TTA-UC process may be tentatively explained as follows: under infrared excitation, the strong interaction between organic HQ+ cations favours the fission of singlet excitonic levels into two triplet levels; once the triplet levels of two neighbouring HQ+ cations are populated, energy transfer occurs in such a manner that two triplet levels are fused into a double energy excitation (2T), which then gives rise to emission of a photon of higher energy via TTA.43,44 Furthermore, as seen in Fig. 7, we observe an enhancement by up to 5 orders of magnitude of the up converted emission intensity in the hybrid material compared to that of the salt. This can be explained by the fact that in the hybrid compound crystalline cohesion is ensured by ionic interactions, which are in favour of energy migration and charge transfer, whereas in the salt van der Waals interactions are added with neutral water molecules, which are less favourable for these transfer mechanisms. Accordingly, as we mentioned above, in this work we report the first result that describes a sensitizer-free process. We notice that, compared to the conventional two-component solid state TTA-UC systems, sensitizer-free TTA-UC ones are pertinent to compensate for energy losses, following the intersystem crossing of the sensitizers and the triplet energy migration (TEM) from the sensitizer to the emitter. It is also worthy of note that organic up conversion systems based on TTA-UC have been extensively used for improvement of solar cells efficiencies. The best up converting material has reached a wavelength limit of 790 nm,45 with up conversion efficiencies up to 28%.46 Nevertheless, it has proved challenging to find long-lived organic chromophores that allow up-conversion systems to operate beyond 800 nm. The up-converter material reported here is believed to be of great potential interest, as it could extend the NIR conversion photons at wavelengths as long as 855 nm. This near IR spectral domain is of particular interest because the conventional absorber semiconductors in solar cells, like silicon (Eg = 1.1 eV) and CdTe (Eg = 1.5 eV), as well as the absorber perovskites (Eg = 1.6 eV),47 are unable to collect low energy photons whose wavelengths exceed 1200, 800 and 765 nm respectively. Moreover, previous studies, relating to TTA converters, show that the infrared spectral intervals converted to visible do not exceed a few tens of nanometers.48,49 Whereas for the studied compound, the width of the converted interval is remarkably larger and reaches 300 nm, allowing one to harness a greater fraction of the solar spectrum, a fact which further increases the interest of these materials for improving the efficiency of photovoltaic cells.


Fig. 8 Schematic representation of the sensitizer free TTA-UC mechanism: the fission of two singlet states into two triplet states occurring in two interacting HQ+ cations is followed by emission via triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA).

5. Conclusion

In this work, we have studied the optical properties of the inorganic organic hybrid crystal (HQ)2[ZnCl4] in which the two components HQ+ and [ZnCl4]2− are two chromophores having quite close HOMO–LUMO gaps. The study of the luminescence as a function of the excitation wavelength showed an intense emission signal around 470 cm−1 that can be observed under UV excitation or under excitation in the near infrared (up conversion). The comparative study showed that the normal photoluminescence involves a resonant energy transfer mechanism in which ZnCl42− acts as a donor and HQ+ acts as an acceptor, whereas the up-conversion luminescence is governed by sensitizer-free triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA). In addition, (HQ)2[ZnCl4] is the first TTA up-conversion material discovered in the family of organic metal halide crystals, which is promising for the improvement of solar cell efficiency as it has extended the conversion of infrared light to a wider spectral range. Given the band gap tunability depending on the nature of the organic molecule, this opens a new pathway for improving solar cell performance. Moreover, considering that zinc is the least harmful metal for the human body, such up-conversion materials can be good candidates for biological imaging applications. Therefore, we believe that by the discovery of the first TTA up conversion material in the hybrid perovskite family and their derivatives, this work will enrich this class of materials by arousing renewed research on low cost, eco-friendly raw materials, with facile synthesis and excellent optical properties at room temperature.
