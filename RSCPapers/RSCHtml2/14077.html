Abstract

In recent years, key questions about the interaction of 2D MXene nanomaterials in electrochemical and biomedical applications have been raised. Most research has focused on clarifying the exclusive properties of the materials; however, only limited reports have described the biomedical applications of 2D nanomaterials. 2D MXenes are monolayer atomic nanosheets resulting from MAX phase ceramics. The hydrophilic properties, metallic conductivity, stability, and exclusive physiochemical performances make them promising materials for electrochemical and biomedical applications, including CO2 reduction, H2 evolution, energy conversion and storage, supercapacitors, stimuli-responsive drug delivery systems, regenerative medicine, and photothermal cancer therapy. In this review paper, we have provided facile approaches to synthesising MXene and characterization of chemical and physical properties. Their potential applications in medicine, ranging from antibacterial agents to targeted drug delivery, cancer photo/chemotherapy, tissue engineering, and electrochemical applications, have not been comprehensively reviewed and discussedwhich encouraged us to come up with this work. We also discuss the most common challenges of utilizing MXene-related materials and areas that can be further developed in the future, with possible struggles and limitations one may face.


Environmental significance

MXenes are always considered toxic materials with significant optoelectronic and physical properties. However, they are not suitable for biomedical applications because of their highly toxic behavior. On the other hand, electrochemical and biomedical applications of MXene, including CO2 reduction, H2 evolution, and photothermal cancer therapy, have not been comprehensively reviewed and discussed in literature, which encouraged us to come up with this study. For the first time, we provide a full and comprehensive future perspective and applicable routes for using MXenes in biomedicine. We also discuss the most common restrictions of utilizing MXene-related materials and areas that can be further developed in the future, with possible struggles and limitations one may be faced with. Also, this review article deals with a wide range of methods and procedures for reducing toxicity, increasing bioavailability/biocompatibility, and changing the physicochemical properties of highly toxic and hazardous materials, including MXene, for potential and promising biomedical applications.

1 Introduction

With the introduction of graphene in 2004, the unearthing of mono-layer graphene's exceptional physical and chemical properties, two-dimensional (2D) materials have attracted considerable attention.1 Great interests in 2D materials could be associated with their astonishing electronic, mechanical, optical, physical, and chemical behaviour, superior to bulk materials.2,3

The recent interest in 2D materials led to the discovery of another new type of material with electrifying behaviour, which placed them into real use. The newly investigated 2D materials are as follows: 2/3D graphene,47 carbon nanotubes,810 graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4),11,12 transition metal di-chalcogenides,13 boron nitride,14 and numerous other 2D materials.

The attractive behaviour of 2D materials could be associated with the electrons being bound in the atomic region in two dimensions, providing outstanding electronic performance. Besides, 2D materials present high specific surface area, and excellent freestanding flexible ultrathin layers with tunable thickness can be created from a wide range of processes.15 Furthermore, the surface functional groups of 2D materials can be changed when other materials are added. As a result of these fascinating features, 2D materials reveal a wide range of potential applications.16

One of the most outstanding 2D materials that have received special attention in previous years is graphene, and many different applications in many fields have been reported.17,18 However, other innovative materials have also received significant attention as a result of their fascinating structures and new properties such as conductivity, stability, and compatibility.19,20 Among these materials, new 2D transition-metal carbides, nitrides, and carbonitrides (named as MXenes, the first type of which have been fabricated at Drexel University) have emerged and are at the cutting edge of 2D material investigations and are predicted as new scientific and technological materials. Furthermore, it is well-documented that 2D graphene and MXene structures can be synthesized via exfoliation using strong reagent chemicals by utilizing their original bulk material.21,22

Nowadays, MXenes are singular, and speedily growing 2D transition metal nitride and carbides have lately been made by etching MAX phases.23,24 It is noteworthy to point out that MAX phases are associated with a well-documented hexagonal crystalline structure, which can be considered as part of ceramic-based materials with a simple chemical formula of Mn+1AXn (n = 13); in this equation, M, A, and X demonstrate transition metals, an element from groups 1316 in the periodic table, and carbon and/for nitrogen, correspondingly (as shown in Fig. 1). M and A are layers of MAX phases that have been exfoliated into layers or sheets. The X atoms are positioned in the octahedral spots made by M components.25

More than 70 compounds of MAX phases have been fabricated experimentally until now. MAX phases generally show the favourable features of ceramics, like robust structural strength and stiffness; besides these, MAX phases also demonstrate favourable metallic properties such as thermal and electrical conductivity.26,27 In addition to explaining briefly about MAX phases, it is worth explaining MXenes properties. It has been shown from MAX structures that the A layer can be taken out selectively to create a new family of 2D materials named MXene. Since the first report on the MXene structure, MXenes have become recognized as a new and interesting family of 2D materials with significant potential for diversification of their composition and modification of the material properties.

MXenes resemble the MAX phase equation with some differences in terms of chemical formula. In this chemical formula, M represents layers of transition metals (M = Zr, Mo, V, Ti, Ta, or Nb), and X denotes carbon or nitrogen atoms.28 It is worth mentioning that the Tx in the formula denotes the surface terminations (such as O, OH, F, and/or Cl), which these groups can be composed of, depending on the type of solution, a mixture of F, O, or OH groups as surface terminations. MXenes have been formed by selective wet-chemical etching in hydrofluoric compounds (e.g., HF), sonication, and intercalation of Ti3AlC2 (Ti3AlC2(layered MAX) Ti3C2Tx (2D MXene)).

The unique structure of MXenes with metallic part provides high conductivity and several surface functional groups, which are formed during the etching and exfoliation process, providing excellent capabilities for tuning structural and chemical properties,29 and offering an inherent active material for many fields of interest like wireless communications,30 energy storage and conversion,3133 electromagnets interference shielding,34 sensors,35 imaging and therapy,36 water treatment,36 gas sensing, pervaporation, desalination, photo/electrocatalysis for water splitting, and CO2 electrochemical reduction.3740

Besides the abovementioned discussions, the uninvestigated family of MXenes and their exclusive combination of attributes have made an ample range of practical applications, and the new opportunities and new structures and compositions have attracted researchers and have made them believe that there are several ways to be paved in MXene research and numerous thrilling innovations are to come.41 Over the last years, many advancements have been made in the synthesis and applications of MXenes materials to accomplish the specific desires of researchers. Some reviews on the synthesis and application of MXenes have been reported.4244

However, we have provided this review paper in terms of MXene's potential applications and challenges in improving their potential usage and contains a discussion of the inherent benefits and astonishing opportunities of MXene in an effort to inspire scholars to translate current understanding in the direction of new MXene structural engineering and designs. The goal of this outlook is not to afford an inclusive review on a specific topic in terms of application or MXene development. As a substitute, the purpose is to deliver a forward-looking viewpoint to motivate and offer direction for scholars' investigation fields.

A considerable number of articles have been published regarding the different applications of Mxene.39,45,46 Nevertheless, we classify essential attributes for understanding the underlying chemistry, processes, and phenomena in MXenes. Moreover, advancing catalysts with high performance, selectivity, and energy efficiency toward hydrogen and oxygen production, CO2 conversion, and supercapacitors is still a wide-open question. Developments in fundamental understanding in recent years have led to new nanomaterial designs that considerably enhance the energy field. Inspired by such works, in this work, we summarize the basics of MXene 2D materials, from the synthesis process to application aspects. Our aim is to highlight techniques that permit enhancing material performance. Such a school of thought is crucial to moving the state of MXene-based technologies forward to commercial practicality.

2 Synthesis of Mxene and parameters affecting its structure

Commonly, 2D MXenes are synthesized through etching the A layer from the MAX phases with hydrofluoric (HF) acid.47 Generally, the synthesis of MXene occurs in three phases, including the preparation of the Ti3AlC2 precursor MAX phase, etching of the Al layer, and intercalation and exfoliation.48 In fact, the first synthesis of MXene involved the etching of metal layers from the MAX phases, layered transition metal carbides, and carbonitrides using HF. Various synthesis methods have been established, such as etching in a mixture of fluoride salts and different acids, halogens, non-aqueous etchants, and molten salts, permitting the synthesis of novel MXenes with good control of their properties (Fig. 1).

Presently, about 150 types of MAX phases have been identified, and 30 various MXenes with good properties have been synthesized.49 For example, Ti3C2Tx MXene is a commonly studied 2D Mxene.48 Researchers developed a good Ti3C2Tx with outstanding electrical properties using HF, which showed a similar layer-stacked structure to graphene.50 In fact, this assay can provide the door to synthesizing intercalation of 2D MXenes. Although, one of the biggest challenges with this method is the use of the hazardous chemical HF. In fact, common etching methods include the application of H2O2-assisted synthesis of Ti2CTx. This method can provide improved properties compared to the usual exfoliation employing HF. Additionally, to escape the use of hazardous HF, the modified etching assay with a mixture of hydrochloric (HCl) acid and lithium Fluoride (LiF) was approved to substitute HF.51

LiF/HCl can be suggested as a mild reagent, and the product displays good mechanical stability. In this method, by using LiF/HCl, a clay-like intermediate Ti3C2Tx is made, which this hydrophilic compound outcome in a volume expansion as an aftercoming of the lithium intercalation.52 For this purpose, Ghidiu et al. demonstrated that after Ti3AlC2 was absorbed in a LiF/HCl solution, the creation of HF not only changed the MAX phase to MXene, but the obtained product presented similar rheological properties due to the hydration of intercalated cationsLi+.53 In fact, in many laboratories where the use of HF is prohibited, etching in LiF/HCl is licensed to produce MXenes.

MXenes can be attained through etching without applying acid. For example, the Ti3C2 MXene can be synthesized through anodic etching in an alkaline aqueous electrolyte comprising ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) and tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAOH), where Ti3AlC2 sticks were applied as electrodes (synthesis methods and their interrelationships with the chemical structures are mentioned in the ESI).54 The fluoride termination-free MXenes have been discovered for use in biomedical fields.55 Although traditional wet acid etching methods have been used to synthesize carbon nitride MXene, the first 2D transition metal nitride, Ti4N3, was attained through molten salt etching.50,56 The reasons for the strict synthesis conditions of nitride MXenes may initiate from the higher formation energies of nitrides than their carbide counterparts.

With the progress of MXenes, different methods, such as chemical vapour deposition (CVD), hydrothermal, UV-induced, electrochemical, chemical vapour deposition, spray drying, and thermal reduction, were utilized effectively to produce 2D MXenes without applying hazardous etchants.57,58 Although different types of MXene synthesis methods are based on the MAX phase, along with their details given in the ESI, synthesis procedure (S1) contains synthesis of MXene (S1.1), synthesis of MXene from MA phase (S1.2), synthesis of MXene via the wet chemical method (S1.3), synthesis of MXene via freeze-drying technique, synthesis of MXene via spray drying (S1.4) and also the synthesis of MXene via sacrificial hard-template technique (S1.5).

2.1. Influence of synthesis strategies on MXenes structure

Since the development of 2D MXenes, there have been many attempts to tune and optimize the fabrication process to scale up MXene material production. The general process of synthesis MXene is a selective etching of MAX phases in which mostly HF and LiF/HCl etchant solvents are used. However, several parameters affect the resultant MXene's physical and chemical properties.5961 For example, the etchants moiety and concentration, time of etching procedure, the used surfactant, sonication time and temperature, stirring, etching temperature, and original particle size of MAX precursors have been demonstrated to affect the resultant MXene.57,62

Thereby, there is a great demand to optimize the fabrication process, and the optimization requires a deep insight into how these factors affect the nanostructure of the obtained MXene architecture. The current review paper will discuss the progress from recent reports on the influence of the fabrication process on porosity, structure, surface terminations, vacancies, and defects in resultant MXene-based materials. One of the significant parameters that the synthesis process affects is crystalline structure. For example, a higher weight percentage of HF in the solvent with a longer etching duration and temperature will result in a bigger c-lattice constant of the MXene phase. It should be highlighted that the lattice constant is a good parameter to evaluate a sufficient etching process.

Besides water molecules, the surface functional group also affects the final MXene crystal structure. For example, the water molecules can increase the distance between MXene layers so that the interlayer spacing can alter the crystalline structure. For example, Ying and co-workers have shown that a 10 wt% of HF in the etching solvent will result in a higher c-lattice constant due to the higher water percentage. Furthermore, the prepared material showed higher Cr(VI) uptake, which is valuable for eliminating this species that is highly toxic and water-soluble, besides being a strong oxidant.63,64 Thereby, rationally tuning the etching solvent, which leads to controlling water percentage in the solvent, can help control the lattice constant, resulting in tuning the crystalline structure.

Another effect of the synthesis process is on defects and vacancies. For example, etching can remove the A atom layers, but the etchant can remove the layers of carbide by several chemical reactions, resulting in defects in the MXene structure. A regular type of defect observed is nonporous on the atomic scale. In fact, the resultant nanoporous material can influence ion transport behaviour, which is important for electrochemical applications. Naguib and co-workers investigated pores on a nanoscale at atomic resolution in the Ta4C3Tx MXene phase.63

They first observed nonporous defects when using 50 wt% of HF as etchant and treatment for 72 h. They have reported average nonporous size of fewer than 3 nm in that work. According to their results, defect formation resulted from an excessive etching by employing an etchant solution with elevated HF concentration (50 wt%). Halim and co-workers have reported a good result on the effect of the fabrication process on the average size of nonporous or nano-defects. They systematically examined different etching times, exfoliation approaches, and sonication durations using the Mo2CTx MXenes phase.

The pore size rises from about 10 nm to hundreds of nanometers as the etching duration is ranged from 616 days.65 They have also discovered that applying a post-etching treatment remarkably enhances the nonporous defect number with a minor effect on the pore magnitude. Furthermore, the sonication process after exfoliation increases the pore size. Consequently, such investigations directly indicate the presence of defects in MXenes structures from the etching process and specify that defects can be organized by regulating important factors during the fabrication process. Also, more information on the synthesis methods of these types of MXenes and their characterizations is mentioned in the ESI, Fig. S1.

Functional groups (mostly F, O, OH) are an undeniable part of MXene structures that form during MXene fabrications by etching solvents. Therefore, these functional groups play an important role in MXene features. Besides, MXene phases have inhomogeneity and a highly complex structure, and gaining structural information from MXene structure is not possible by XRD since this analytical technique does not have enough sensitivity to detect light elements such as hydrogen and oxygen. Therefore, this lack of sensitivity makes detecting OH and hydrogen groups impossible.

Thus, comprehending the functional groups' function at the MXene layers' surface is crucial for tuning MXene properties. It should be highlighted that surface functional groups from the moment A layers are etched from MAX phases. The A thin elemental layer is broken when the chemical bonds first join the MAX layers, forming new chemical bonds between MXene and the etchant species. For example, when HF is used as an etchant solvent in a synthetic process, HF ionizes into F and H+ ions. After the ionization, the F species are strongly attracted to M and A species. On the other hand, H+ ions are preferably reduced to H2 rather than reacting with A or M elements. The MA bonds finally break by enhancing the HF concentration, and AlF3 is formed.

In this sense, A elements are removed from MAX layers, and the increase of MXene phases starts. When A species are eliminated from MAX layers, the freed M species will contact F and H+, resulting in an MXene material with F and H end groups. It should be highlighted that between these two possible bondings, F-terminated configurations are preferable to H-terminated and H atoms prefer to produce H2 gas as a by-product. Therefore, it was suggested that the surface terminations initiated from the etching practice from the reactions:

Ti3AlC2 + 3HF AlF3 + 1.5H2 + Ti3C2(1)
Ti3C2 + 2H2O Ti3C2(OH)2 + H2(2)
Ti3C2 + 2HF Ti3C2F2 + H2(3)

As seen in the abovementioned equations, the prepared MXenes are terminated by F species; however, some DFT studies have shown that the OH ending group is the preferable ending group rather than F.

Therefore, OH and F species were suggested for possible termination on MXene surfaces. Moreover, other reports have also demonstrated that the O species are among the probable species that can terminate the MXene surface.66,67 This exclusive result was proposed to occur when the OH termination on the surface recombined with water molecules; as a result of that, O-terminations form. Therefore, the possible chemical reaction was proposed to be as follows:

Ti3C2(OH)2 Ti3C2O + H2O(4)

In addition, in the prepared MXene phases, TiAl bonds are replaced by TiF, TiOH, and/or TiO bonds. Until now, most of the reports on MXene demonstrated these terminations. However, theoretical studies have shown that O, F, and OH terminations have higher stability when compared to pure MXene phases.25,67,68 Furthermore, some experimental and theoretical reports propose that the O functional group has higher stability than F and OH terminations, and F surface termination is more stable than OH termination. The lower stability of the OH termination is because hydrogen atoms can substitute OH terminates or convert into O terminated at high temperatures, which all involve low stability of OH terminations.69,70 Therefore, as can be seen, it is very remarkable to control the ending group of the MXene surface to acquire high-quality MXene with a specific surface area and feasible for being utilized in certain applications.

2.2. Synthesize of MXene from non-MAX phase

It is clear that most researchers have been devoted to Max-based phases to synthesize MXene structures showing a wide range of applications. However, some research has recently been on non-MAX phases to synthesize MXene structures. Generally, despite the conventional method to synthesize MXene by selective etching of Al-containing MAX structures, in order to synthesize MXene from non-MAX phases, one should selectively etch non-MAX phases such as Al2C3 from Zr3Al3C5 to gain a Zr3C2Tx phase, in this process Al3C3 phases are centrifuged out instead of Al atoms through the etching process.71 For the first time, Zhou and co-workers have done a detailed study on non-MAX phases to construct MXene. They have shown that 2D Zr3C2Tx, synthesized from the selective etching of Zr3Al3C5 materials instead of using the conventional MAX phase, has higher structural stability and relatively better durability under high-temperature conditions compared to Ti-based MXene structures. Furthermore, Zhou and co-workers conducted experimental and theoretical processes and demonstrated that the following chemical reactions are highly likely to occur during the etching process, resulting in non-MAX MXene phases.
Zr3Al3C5 + HF AlF3 + CH4 + Zr3C2(5)
Zr3C2 + H2O Zr3C2(OH)2 + H2(6)
Zr3C2 + HF Zr3C2F2 + H2(7)

The abovementioned chemical reactions are similar to the proposed reaction for conventional MAX-based MXenes. Further evaluations by TEM have shown that sonicated Ti3C2Tx MXene phase has lower stability due to its partial distribution. However, after treatments, TEM images of Zr3C2Tx show that it has maintained its original shape. Besides, the SAED studies confirmed the initial hexagonal structures. Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that Zr3C2Tx shows better structural integrity and maintains its original 2D stricture.

3 Overview of MXenes electrochemical applications

Currently, there are several reports on the applications of 2D MXene materials in the literature. Energy conversion and storage are of great importance among electrochemical applications than others. From an energy aspect, it has been a long time since humanity has dealt with the energy crisis and global warming due to the ferocious consumption of natural resources (fossil fuels, oil, gas, etc.). In this regard, many efforts have been associated with this area to improve or overcome this problem. MXene-based structures are one of the responses to this end. Graphene/graphene-based nanocomposites may possibly serve as next-generation energy devices. Still, MXenes are also very attractive in this context, being very safe, exhibiting large interlayer spacing, environmental flexibility, and outstanding biocompatibility. In addition, such electrodes can not only store large quantities of charge per unit volume but also rapidly discharge.72 The specific characteristics of MXenes include high surface area, presence of hydrophilic functional groups, high atomic numbers (for certain transition metals), and paramagnetic behaviour. The functional groups on MXenes also contribute to their rigidness and flexibility, which is essential in thin film formation as a part of bio-electronic devices. MXene nanosheets exhibit high surface area; hence they are suitable for drug loading and delivery for theranostics applications and synergistic disease treatment.

MXenes exist as stacked 2D sheets, held together by weak van der Waals (vdW) forces and/or strong hydrogen-bonding interactions between the surface functional groups.7376

In the following sections, we will delve into some cogent discussion and overview of the most recent developments on MXene applications in the energy field, including electrochemical supercapacitors, CO2 electrocatalytic reduction, and other biomedical applications.

3.1. MXenes as electrode materials for supercapacitor applications

The electronic feature of MXenes is among the most attractive properties that could be adjusted by functional group modification through stoichiometry or solid solution approaches. Furthermore, it has been noted that MXene conductivity is similar to graphene but higher than carbon nanotubes and reduced graphene oxide.77,78 These excellent electrical properties of MXene have made them a conceivable material as catalysts for energy storage or energy conversion applications such as supercapacitors, hydrogen production through water splitting, batteries, fuel cells, etc.79 It should be noted that M atoms in MXene structures are the reason for their excellent electronic properties. In addition, pristine MXenes are known to have metallic conductivity like Tin+1Xn.

Supercapacitors are electrochemical capacitors storing energy through a double-layer mechanism. Supercapacitors adsorb charge carrier ions on their surface porosity, known as electrical double-layer capacitance. The other type is pseudocapacitive activity, which stores charges on electrode surfaces or near redox reactions. As a result, a high-power density can be stored and released through electrochemical supercapacitors compared to conventional capacitors. Therefore, electrochemical supercapacitors have received considerable attention and could be an excellent candidate for future high-power storage systems.

Numerous other questions are to be answered for MXene-based nanomaterials. The 2D family of MXene-structures requires to be deployed to investigate several Mn+1XnTx with distinct elements (M) and functions of T and x. Tuning and changing MXenes' surfaces would help obtain MXenes with suitable assets. For instance, MXenes with terminals on the surface could have low conductivity. On the other hand, fabricating MXene-based nanostructures without terminals would lead to tremendous electrochemical activity.

Additionally, it is hard to make large-scale one-layered MXene structures.19,80,81 Porous electrode materials demonstrate excellent electrochemical surface area and higher capacitance to store charge. However, most conventional electrode materials for supercapacitors suffer from high costs, toxicity, poor conductivity, and durability during the electrochemical process. Therefore, enhancing high-performance electrode materials with high conductivity, high surface area, and cost-effectiveness are crucially required.15,8284 In this regard, MXene-based materials have been employed as supercapacitor electrode materials due to their high electrical conductivity, stable 2D structures composed of layers that can provide high surface area, and accessible sites for solution ions. Since discovering MXene materials, they have been used as supercapacitor electrode materials.85

The prepared MXene showed nano-layer structures with O and F terminations proved by EDX and XPS evaluations. In that work, a high-performance capacitance of 560 F g1, which is similar to the most carbon-based supercapacitor, was obtained. Furthermore, the CV results demonstrated general electrochemical behaviour with rectangular CV curves (Fig. 2B). As shown, the high performance of the provided electrode material was associated with the high surface area of MXene, high stability, and high conductivity, which resulted from the metallic part of the MXene structure.87

Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were used to further evaluate the charge storage mechanism. Zhang and co-workers employed DFT to study the charge storing mechanism when MXene was used as supercapacitor electrode materials by utilizing the ion solvation model and Gibbs free energy assessments.88 Their evaluations suggested the following reactions as a possible mechanism for the charge storing phenomenon in MXene-based electrodes.

Ti3C2O2q + 2H+(aq) Ti3C2O22x(OH)2xq + 2(1 x)H+(aq)(8)

In the mentioned reaction, x stands for surface hydrogen coverage, and q is the total charge on the electrode material. Furthermore, it is noted that redox reaction on the electrode surface has been involved in the capacitance performance of a supercapacitor electrode. As we have discussed above, terminal species type influence the chemical and physical properties of MXenes; their effect also has been exhibited on the electrochemical performance of MXene electrode materials for supercapacitors. Surface terminations that form during the etching process have a great influence on the electrical and electrochemical behaviour of MXene-based electrodes, which also was confirmed by DFT studies by many other researchers;89,90 for instance, the electron localization in the MXene-nanostructures and nanomembranes have a direct effect on the non-lithium anode battery applications; therefore, prediction of this effect is of great importance.

Several attempts have investigated the electron localization on O-terminated MXene (Fig. 3), which revealed that K+ and Na+ have moderate capacities on the mentioned MXene. Also, they could have acceptable rate capabilities compared to other cations.91 Dall'Agnese and co-workers showed this effect by using Ti3C2Tx as electrode material for supercapacitors in acidic solution.92 They showed that O terminations have a greater influence on electrochemical behaviour than F terminations. In fact, replacing F terminates with O terminates on the electrode surface by chemical treatment in basic solution displayed a four times increase in the capacitance of the electrode performance. They noted that the main reason for this increase is that F groups are mostly inactive while replacing them with O groups can increase the reversibility of bonding and debonding of hydronium ions, which increases the electrochemical capacitance performance of the prepared electrodes. Further studies also confirmed that the O terminations perform better than F terminations.9395

3.2. Electrocatalytic application of MXene

With the rising global energy crisis, great demands for new types of nanomaterials have been raised to address energy issues. Fossil fuels as a natural energy source for humankind are decreasing fast. Therefore, replacing natural energy sources with other reversible and renewable energy sources is among the most crucial demands for humankind.15,118 In this regard, electrochemical water splitting, either through electrocatalysts or photo-electrocatalysts, is a more favourable way to produce hydrogen, which can be utilized as a promising energy source to replace natural sources.

In this regard, several pieces of research have shown that MXene-based electrocatalysts provide excellent conditions to produce hydrogen through the electrochemical water splitting process.119121 The theoretical evaluations have shown that MXenes with O and OH ending moieties provide excellent metallic behaviour that boosts the charge transfer. Furthermore, O terminate on the MXenes surface offers active sites for electrocatalytic activity, providing excellent conditions for H*-weak bonding for hydrogen release. Besides, DFT evaluations indicated that the hydrogen adsorption energy depends on the number of metallic layers in the MXene architecture because the layer's thickness in 2D MXenes encourages the whole structure's activity toward hydrogen evolution.

Most recently, through theoretical and experimental evaluations, Li and co-workers (in 2018) have shown an excellent capability of Ti2CTx toward electrocatalytic performance. They modelled a F terminate rich MXene structure with controlled atomic layers put forward to assess the catalytic activity. They found that F end group rich MXene structures with ultrathin layers decreased hydrogen adsorption energy on the electrode surface, encouraging hydrogen evolution kinetic by more proton adsorption. Furthermore, as they claimed, the presence of F terminates enhanced charge transfer performance because the F terminate groups on MXene surfaces reduce charge transfer resistance. This is due to their assessment through DFT evaluations and temperature-dependent resistance calculations, which exhibited that the prepared rich F terminated Ti2CTx nano-layers are inherent and show metallic behaviour, contributing to conductivity and reducing charge transfer resistance. At the same time, the controlled atomic layers increased accessible catalytic active sites. The synergetic effect of F terminations and atomic layer control in Ti2CTx have resulted in high HER performance.103

Most recently, other MXene-based materials have received attention among researchers in electrocatalytic activity. In this regard, significant attention has been devoted to improving Mo2CTx, and Ti2CTx MXene structure's electrocatalytic performance,122124 structural modification of MXene by nitrogen,125,126 phosphor doped-MXene,127 single-atom assisted MXenes,128,129 vacancy tuning (vacancy tuning is an important topic for understanding the exact chemical/physical mechanisms to improve different applications), and in case of electrocatalytic applications, the carbon vacancies inside the MXene nanostructures should be investigated.

In this manner, scientists have been focused on the formation energies of these carbon vacancies inside the MXene nanostructures (Fig. 4) and found that it is an energetically favourable reaction; therefore, avoiding these vacancies for advanced electrocatalytic applications is needed,130,131 and hybrid MXene with other electroactive materials such as platinum,132134 other transition metals,47,120,135,136 graphene,103etc. for instance, Cheng and co-workers have shown through DFT calculation that Cr2CTx MXene with O terminate groups, and carbon vacancies offered lower Gibbs free energy or reaction for H adsorption on the surface.

Another attractive research on MXene is being devoted to single metal atoms combined with MXenes structure. As a realization of the single metal idea in order to demonstrate its feasibility, single metal atoms can considerably increase the catalytic achievement due to quantum size influence, rapid charge transfer between single metal atoms and substrates due to the strong interactions between them, strong contact with metalsupport speeding up chemical interactions, and the low coordinated metal atom-induced high surface free energy.139

Liu and co-workers have reported a high-performance single atom combined with MXene electrocatalyst toward HER as proof of this principle. A coordination-assisted approach introduced isolated single-atom ruthenium (Ru) into a nitrogen-doped Ti3C2Tx MXene structure. They have shown that Ru atoms become stable on the MXene structure by establishing RuN bonds with N atoms, significantly improving the catalytic activity towards the HER at a wide range of pH media. DFT evaluations exhibited that the coordinative interaction of Ru encourages charge redistribution in the interfacial area of the Ti3C2Tx MXene structure to attain a zero Gibbs free energy (GH*) for hydrogen adsorption, further accelerating the HER kinetics. The greater electrocatalytic activity of the ruthenium atom-supported MXene electrocatalyst highlights the significance of the atomic-level engineering approach to modify and optimize the activity by the atomic-scale intention.6,132

Ramalingam and co-workers have worked on synthesizing a single atom composed of nitrogen and sulfur-doped Ti3C2Tx MXene.129 They synthesized single ruthenium atoms (RuSA) in that work and combined them with N and S-doped MXene. They showed that RuSA on the MXene surface demonstrated higher stability due to the formation of RuS and RuN chemical bonds, eradicating the phenomena of agglomeration. Furthermore, the formed chemical bonds between Ru and N and S increased the stability of the provided electrode, restricted agglomeration, and improved the interaction between metalsupport overall structure. Therefore, the provided electrode (which was denoted as RuSANSTi3C2Tx) showed impressive HER performance, with a low overpotential of 76 mV to reach a current density of 10 mA cm2.

Furthermore, the RuSANSTi3C2Tx electrode showed a low Tafel slope of 90 mV. dec1, which demonstrates electrochemical reaction rates concerning overpotential. The higher HER performance was associated with the combination of RuSA and active Ti3C2Tix MXene. Computational evaluations also confirmed this high performance.

The Gibbs energy of hydrogen adsorption over RuASNSTi3C2Tx near zero values showed the outstanding nature of this material that is favourable for hydrogen gas evolution. 3.3. Electrochemical CO2 reduction

The diminution of greenhouse gas releases is a main global concern to diminish their role in changes in global climate. Therefore, it is necessary to decrease carbon dioxide discharges by changing from fossil fuels (petrol, charcoal, etc.) to clean energy sources.131,155,156 Thus, the electrochemical reduction of CO2 into environmentally friendly chemical compounds is one of the best ways to resolve global warming.157 During this electrochemical process, CO2 is reduced at the cathode and catalyses this gas in the electrochemical cell, forming other chemical species.

The efficiency of this electrochemical process greatly rests on the activity and capability of the catalyst and the overall structure and chemical composition of the electrode. The cathode, where the electrochemical reduction takes place, determines the overall catalyst accomplishment for the electrochemical reduction of CO2. Therefore, the catalyst could derive a high current density at lower overpotential and elevated stability and selectivity for forming a certain product, resulting from CO2 electrochemical reduction. In this process, it is possible to evaluate the catalyst's efficiency for the electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide. That being said, excellent potentials (high stability, electronegativity, hydrophilicity, and electron conductivity) from the unique structure of MXene, and surface terminals (F, OH, and O,) make it an impressive material for electrocatalytic reduction of CO2.

It has been shown through DFT evaluations that O surface terminations on MXene surfaces display elevated selectivity for CO2 reduction into HCOOH species at low overpotential values. This reaction mechanism results from the O termination groups, which help stabilize the intermediate species by coordinating with H moieties.158

Titanium (Ti) based MXenes have attracted much interest in the electrocatalytic reduction of carbon dioxide due to their high conductivity and stability. The computational results demonstrated that Ti3C2O2 MXenes with O terminate groups demonstrated higher carbon dioxide electrocatalytic reduction performance with high selectivity. Recently, Li and co-workers have worked on very important aspects of the MXene-based electrodes for carbon dioxide reduction.159 They did a computational simulation on Cr3C2 and Mo3C2 MXene toward selective conversion of carbon dioxide to methane. Their DFT results demonstrated that the O terminates and OH terminates on the MXene surface are advantageous for the selective reduction of CO2 into CH4. This is due to the lower overall reaction-free energy than bare materials. To be sure, Fig. 5(A and B) signifies the minimum energy pathway for the electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide to methane using the OH and O termination of Mo3C2. The energy for the first step (hydrogenation) is 0.92 eV, which is lower when compared to bare Mo3C2 (0.64 eV). This step represents the fundamental contribution of OH moieties to carbon dioxide hydrogenation. The following steps are included by the energy discharge contributing to a greater accomplishment of Mo3C2(OH)2 than pristine Mo3C2. Also, it can be detected for Mo2C2O2 in which input energies require the formation of radicals (e.g., *CH3OH, *CH2O, and *COOH) since the remaining steps are related to exothermic reactions.

We have focused on electrochemical applications of MXene materials, which are highly important for human beings. This section will focus on the medical application and mechanism of MXene materials, which is also of great importance for humankind, and more importantly, there are few reports on this issue. 2D layers of MXenes, as a new type of 2D functional materials, have been widely studied for a wide range of applications (Fig. 6). However, the drug-delivery performance and applications of 2D MXene have not been extensively considered and require more research to understand its capabilities in this area. It has been shown that Ti3C2-based MXenes hold excellent drug-loading potentials and show both pH-responsive and near-infrared laser-triggered on-demand drug release. The benefit of MXenes in target drug delivery is their hydrophilicity, which enhances the bioavailability of drugs for organs and tissues, high mechanical strength, high encapsulation, and high drug loading capacity. Mxenes can transport in the body more easily than particles with large sizes and need lower drug loading than other drug delivery systems. They could allow specific targeting by binding to drugs or encapsulating them in a polymer matrix. Though some Mxenes, such as Ti3C2Tx-based nano vehicles, lack enough controllability and suffer from low drug-loading capacity, with blood circulation, the drug-carrying nanocarriers are constantly removed from the body and cause hurt to normal tissues.181183 Therefore, we need to develop smart Mxene-based nanocarriers for drug delivery systems. The development of controllable drug delivery systems based on MXene can be improved by adding pH-sensitive linkers, natural polymers, or magnetic nanomaterials. By applying a magnetic field, the drug carrier is controlled and confined to the target cells.184 Besides, by combining drug-loaded MXene with polymers such as PLGA or PCL, the intramuscular injection can be facilitated for the long-term release of highly effective but toxic drugs into cancerous tissues for cancer treatment.185

In a study, Rabiee et al. established alginate and chitosan-coated Mxene for co-delivery of DOX/pCRISPR. The synthesized nano platform was coated with alginate and chitosan to enhance the surface potential (+4 to +6 mV), which is suitable for pH-sensitive drugs and gene delivery 184. Here, the MOF-5 and MXene were synthesized through a high-gravity technique, and a vacuum-related filtering procedure was used for the synthesis of 3D MXene/MOF-5 nanocomposite. This platform helped to achieve a novel nanocomposite through the combination of MOF-5 and MXene, thereby showing the beneficial features of the layer nanostructures of MXenes and the 3D porous structure of MOF-5 for drug delivery. The drug release can occur through the protonation of amino groups of chitosan in an acidic condition, which produces repulsive forces and aids in dissociating the chitosan layer and releasing the drug. By adding natural polymeric layers, the cell viability was higher on HEK-293, HeLa, PC12, and HepG2 and showed non-toxicity of nanocarriers. The results exhibited 46% of drug payload efficiency in the MXene/MOF-5 nanostructures, and the adsorption of the drug onto and into the nanostructures was performed with high efficiency, as well as the GFP positive results documented up to 25.8 and 21.3% efficiency with respect to the chitosan-and alginate based nanocarriers, respectively. In the presence of polymeric layers, the populations of the cells enhanced significantly, which could be because of the strong interactions among the polymeric layers and the cellular membranes that caused better internalizations.184

Their higher photothermal-conversion potential has been discovered for effective tumour eradication by synergistic photothermal ablation and chemotherapy. Ti3C2-based MXenes have also been exhibited as an appropriate distinction agent for photoacoustic imaging, presenting the possibility for diagnostic-imaging help and observation during therapy. Ti3C2-based MXene has been shown to possess high in vivo histocompatibility and facile evacuation out of the body, and this property has provided them with excellent biocompatibility and biosafety for additional medical and clinical translation. It should be highlighted that MXenes in nanomedicine applications can be employed in combating cancer, which has been a big issue in this era and has claimed several human lives.

It is well-known that many researchers have been attracted from the beginning and used nanomaterials in a vast majority of different applications such as medical, electronic, mechanical, energy, etc. However, the new type of recently explored 2D material, MXene, has attracted the interest of many researchers because of its singular structure that provides elevated specific surface area, good mechanical and thermomechanical behaviour, and quantum effect in its electronic structure. These properties have made MXenes outstanding nanomaterials more than their counterpart materials.186 Furthermore, since MXene 2D materials have great biocompatibility for medical applications, many researchers have used them in cancer therapy.

Moreover, MXene materials have good photothermal properties in near-infrared radiation (NIR). The photothermal effect is generated by the photoexcitation of MXene, yielding in the thermal energy (heat) zone production. Thus, MXenes materials can absorb light and transform it into heat that is used to treat cancerous tissues. In transforming light into heat phenomenon, the absorbant materials must present elevated light-absorbing potential in the NIR region, elevated efficiency in converting light to heat as an energy source, and sufficient biocompatibility. Furthermore, NIR light should penetrate the cancer tissues, scatter them, and absorb them at a low amount to perform efficiently with the penetration of light. As a result, the self-heating up of tissues will be diminished, and conversely, the efficiency of conversion of photothermal energy will be augmented. Other medical applications of MXene have been reported in the literature.187

In a study, Han et al. showed the high drug-loading capacity of Ti3C2 MXenes (211.8%) with pH-responsive and near-infrared laser-triggered on-demand drug release. The researcher showed the Ti3C2 MXenes capability in efficient tumour eradication through photothermal ablation and chemotherapy both in vitro and in vivo. Ultrathin Ti3C2 MXene nanosheets were formed through a two-step exfoliation methodology. In order to increase the stability and biocompatibility of Ti3C2 MXene nanosheets, their surface was modified by soybean phospholipid (SP), which could promise great dispersity and easy transport of nanosheets inside the blood vessel. As a result, they have a large surface area, and the drug could be loaded onto their surface. The drug-releasing rate at a pH of 7 was about 14%. However, the acidic environment considerably accelerates the DOX release from the nano platform to 58.0% at a pH of 4.5. In addition, the electrostatic interaction between the drug and Ti3C2-SP could be affected through H+, contributing to faster drug release. On the other hand, with 808 nm laser irradiation, the temperature of the tumour increases significantly, and then the temperature can reach 68.5 C, which is enough to destroy the tumour. The great in vivo histocompatibility of Ti3C2 on the 4 T1 tumour-bearing mice and its easy excretion out of the body has been assessed and showed high biosafety.188

4 Conclusion

This paper comprehensively reviews the recent synthesis methods of Mxene materials. When synthesized through the traditional Max phases, H2O and functional groups greatly impact the structure of MXene. High-quality Mxene can be prepared by controlling the types of functional groups. MXene structure synthesized by the non-Max phase has good controllability. In addition, more environmentally friendly methods such as chemical vapour deposition (CVD), hydrothermal, UV induction, electrochemistry, etc., are also effectively used to produce 2D MXenes.

Moreover, the applications of Mxenes in electrochemistry, biomedicine, and other fields are comprehensively reviewed and discussed, as well as the existing limitations. Making nanocomposites of MXene with other materials could be a promising way to improve the performance of fabricated MXene-based composites due to their synergistic effect, enhanced conductivities, and reduced interfacial resistance. Another effective way to enhance MXenes' performance when employed as electrode materials in electrochemical or biomedical applications is to introduce ultrathin nanoparticles as spacers between MXene layers, such as carbon nanotubes or metal oxide nanoparticles, which can enhance the conductivity of the overall structure. Mass and charge transfer and easy electrolyte ions diffusion inhibit agglomeration of singular MXene layers. It is also noted that due to water molecules' presence during MXene fabrication, oxidation of MXene is inevitable. Few studies have been reported in the literature, requiring focused research to eliminate oxidation phenomena during MXene synthesis.

Most importantly, the economic aspects of MXene fabrication are also important because providing MAX phase precursor, etching process, exfoliation, and product/device built-up and designing costs are extremely high, which is a big concern in this area. Therefore, we believe this review will serve as a useful platform for further improvement in the fabrication of MXene-based materials.

5 Challenges, current limitations, and future perspective

Despite numerous kinds of research on graphene-based materials for a wide range of applications, recently, MXene-based materials have attracted many researchers' interests due to their excellent nano-layer structure and tunability of functional groups. MXenes have offered excellent capability for further improvement, superior catalytic and metallic properties, and photothermal properties for medical and other environmental applications. In addition, MXene materials provide excellent stability due to their highly crystalline features. The high stability of MXene enables it as the best candidate for applications that require high stability, such as electrochemical hydrogen evolution reaction, electrochemical CO2 reduction, medical therapy, etc.

Several open doors will be investigated since there are many reports on MXene-based materials. For instance, medical applications of the various type of MXene is necessary due to their importance in being well-understood. Furthermore, electrochemical biosensors are among the important fields that need to be discovered by MXene-based materials. Although there are some reports on titanium carbides, there are still many opportunities with other types of MXene to be explored. In taking into account the importance of medical and energy-based application materials, the recent situation requires broad scientific efforts to discover other transition metal-based MXenes for medical, energy conversion/storage, and electrochemical industries. Although some reports on the above-mentioned area for MXene-based materials are available now in the literature, there is still a tremendous demand for further improvement of other remarkable platforms for energy, medical, and electrochemical-based applications.

In fact, excellent structural stability, surface tunability through functional groups, high surface area, and high biocompatibility have derived several interests in developing engineered nano-hybrids composed of MXene-based systems with highly efficient materials for CO2 conversion, cancer therapy, energy storage, and or electrochemical sensors. Such developments in the engineering of MXene-based materials in the aforementioned areas can provide excellent state-of-the-art materials and technology for a wide range of applications, particularly mentioned in this review.

In the case of medical applications, particularly cancer therapy through the photothermal mechanism, the advancement of new and novel MXenes is highly required to accomplish the universal picture. Besides, structural modifications through functional groups of MXenes are crucial to their development in activity, storage capability, photothermal therapy, and electrochemical surface area, which are interesting aspects of MXenes and have attracted many researchers' focus.

This review also emphasizes the hybridized capability of MXene to produce high-performance materials for energy and medical applications. Such configurations are idealistic candidates for high efficiencies and stabilities. Besides the abovementioned potentials of MXenes, MXene-based systems also are emerging nanomaterials as photocatalysts to reduce hydrogen or CO2, wireless communications between electronic devices, sensors, piezoelectric, and many other electrical and mechanical applications. It must be highlighted that more efforts are required to be devoted to these research fields in the future.

In addition to MXenes' potential applications, there are some disadvantages to address to properly empower their highest potential in practical applications. It is obvious that till now, theoretical calculations predicted several MXenes, while only a few of them have been experimentally discovered in recent years, and the remaining have not been successfully synthesized or used in practical applications. Besides, although MXenes have been used as special materials for a wide range of fields, it suffers from a high toxicity synthesis procedure and harsh experimental environment. Thus, new approaches to eliminate the effect of utilizing toxic precursors are required. Other than that, producing an MXene structure with fine microstructure and without surface oxidation, which results in surface agglomeration, is highly challenging.

Furthermore, like other 2D materials such as graphene, the conversion of MXene nanoflakes into thick film leads to layers over-stacking the initial nanoflakes, decreasing available electroactive centres. Also, for electrocatalytic activity, materials are required to be highly stable with long-time charge storage ability. Unfortunately, MXene materials suffer from long-time charge storage and electrocatalytic stability. Therefore it requires several other efforts to make significant developments to improve it. In order to address these issues, several procedures have been reported. Among them, fabricating 3D nanostructures would improve the restacking problems, and hybridization with other materials such as carbon nanostructures, metal nanostructures, and polymers would help improve surface agglomeration and oxidation issues.207,208

On the other hand, innovative methods must be developed to fabricate MXenes with novel features. The novel etching process and obtained MXenes are being advanced continuously. Hence, it is expected that new and novel MXene compositions will be added to the new family of 2D materials soon. Until now, selective etching of A layers from MAX phases to produce MXenes is one of the most accepted and well-organized processes to fabricate MXene. Recently, in order to reduce the environmental impacts of HF etchants, there has been some progress in introducing other types of etchants to reduce destructive and harmful effects of HF etching, such as toxicity and carcinogenic effects, which were alleviated by using LiF/HCl, molten ZnCl2, and electrochemical methods. Despite many signs of progress, methods other than the HF etching process have many concerns, such as low productivity, long processes, and being expensive. Besides these problems, the fabrication of an MXene with a specific termination is highly challenging. Even though HF etchant-based processes do not confidently produce functionalized MXene with a specific termination, the production of MXene with specific termination is a challenge. It must be highlighted that synthesizing specifically functionalized MXene is highly important because some of the areas require specific termination to be applicable. For example, in order to apply MXene in the biomedical field, as reports have shown, it is better to synthesize MXene with O or OH terminations rather than F termination and thus, introducing a suitable route for elimination of such functional groups or new process to synthesize MXene without fluorine termination could be a promising solution.

Hence, it is clear that to scale up MXenes production, there should be a more environmentally friendly and safer method, which requires further studies to achieve this goal. Moreover, the exfoliation route through ultra-sonication requires a long-time process and produces MXenes with many structural defects and low productivity; thus, a different influential route must be designed and developed. Furthermore, most of the MXenes reported in the literature are terminated with functional groups such as O, OH, and F groups. Therefore, it will be highly interesting to synthesize MXenes without any functional groups, especially for electrochemical applications. Furthermore, as shown in the above section about the structure of MXene and their effects on properties, it will be highly efficient to synthesize MXenes with a uniform surface termination distribution ability to tune MXene morphology and their lateral sizes, which will help enhance their potential applications and performance. As a result, there is an urgent need to explore efficient methods such as the bottom-up route, which permits superior control over morphology, nano-size of layers, and surface termination distributions.
