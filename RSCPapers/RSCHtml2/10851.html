Low power, non-coherent sensitized photon up-conversion: modelling and perspectives

Abstract

In the last few years, non-coherent sensitized photon up-conversion (SUC) in multi-component systems has been developed to achieve significantly high quantum yields for various chromophore combinations at low excitation powers, spanning from the ultraviolet (UV) to near infrared (NIR) spectrum. This promising photon energy management technique became indeed suitable for wide applications in lighting technology and especially in photovoltaics, being able to recover the sub-bandgap photons lost by current devices. A full and general description of the SUC photophysics will be presented, with the analysis of the parameter affecting the photon conversion quantum yield and the quantities which define the optimal working range of any SUC system, namely the threshold and saturation excitation intensity. It will be shown how these quantities depend on intrinsic photophysical properties of the moieties involved and on the SUC solid host matrix. The model proposed represents a powerful tool for evaluation of a newly proposed system, and its reliability will be discussed in respect to an optimized system with SUC yield of 0.26 0.02. The results obtained will outline the research guidelines which must be pursued to optimize the SUC efficiency for its perspective technological applications.

1. Introduction

In the last five years, a brand new strategy for obtaining photon up-conversion with low-power non-coherent radiation sources has been proposed and developed. This approach is based on the annihilation of metastable triplet states of organic oligomers which are indirectly excited via energy-transfer (ET) from a suitable sensitizer. In this way, S. Baluschev at the Max-Planck-Institute of Mainz (Germany), and the group led by F. N. Castellano at the Bowling Green State University (U.S.A.) obtained sensitized up-conversion (SUC) with yields sufficiently high to allow for bright up-converted emission to be detected by naked eyes at excitation power densities comparable with the solar irradiance and using non-coherent excitation sources.19

Following this approach, it is possible to obtain SUC quantum efficiencies (QYSUC) higher than 10% by using excitation power densities as low as a few mW cm2, instead of MW cm2 or GW cm2 that are typically required for conventional second harmonic generation or two-photon absorption techniques. For this reason, SUC materials are currently considered to be promising candidates for improving the performances of photovoltaic (PV) devices and photocatalytic hydrogen production (PC) systems, as recently reported by Castellano's group.10 In fact, the efficiency of both PV and PC systems is mainly limited by the incomplete overlap between the absorption spectrum of the active medium and the solar spectrum, which leaves a large portion of the solar emission unutilized. Current efforts for overcoming such limitation are focused on strategies for adapting the working spectral range of PV and PC devices to the solar emission profile, either by using new active materials exhibiting wide absorption spectra, or by modifying the device architecture.11,12 In this regard, the SUC approach operates in a reversed logic as it provides a mean to tune the spectral distribution of the solar irradiation matching the wavelength range in which a PV or PC device works with the highest yield,1318 being therefore fully implementable to the existing device concepts. Potential applications of SUC also include the fabrication of flexible, transparent and multicolour displays, and lighting devices.1921

To date, however, the applicability of SUC to real technologies is still greatly limited by several issues. Specifically: (i) SUC materials work efficiently in the visible spectral range, while absorption in the near infrared and emission in the UV are far from being obtained; (ii) high SUC efficiencies are commonly observed in solutions, while SUC efficiency larger than 1% in solid materials has only recently been demonstrated;22 (iii) an exhaustive theoretical model able to establish the optimal dye concentration for maximizing the SUC efficiency has not yet been realized.

In this paper, after a brief description of the SUC photophysics, we present a general model in which the main parameters affecting QYSUC, threshold (Ith) and saturation (Isat) excitation fluences are accounted for. The obtained findings and the ability of the model to predict real SUC behaviours are confirmed by experimental investigation of a case system. This model is expected to be the starting point for assessing the validity of novel SUC materials for PVs and other applications. In the last section, we provide a review of the most recent SUC-dye pairs and strategies for SUC in the solid-state.

2. SUC Parametrization and quantum yield

Standard multi-photon processes such as two-photon absorption (TPA) and second harmonic generation (SHG) require excitation power densities of MW cm2 or GW cm2 to occur efficiently.23 This is not the case of SUC, in which photon conversion occurs through four distinct photophysical steps (Fig. 1, red arrows): (i) linear (one-photon) absorption of excitation radiation by a donor molecule producing singlet excited states, SD, (ii) population of the triplet states, TD, of the donor via intersystem crossing (ISC) from SD states, (iii) energy-transfer (ET) from the excited TD states to metastable triplet states, TA, of an acceptor molecule, and finally (iv) triplettriplet annihilation (TTA) yielding high-energy singlet excited states SA of the acceptor moiety from which the up-converted emission takes place. By efficient TA sensitization, this mechanism permits UC at excitation fluencies as low as the solar irradiance (100 mW cm2, under AM1.5 conditions, for the whole solar spectrum). Fig. 2 shows the absorption and photoluminescence (PL) spectra of some of the most common donor/acceptor SUC systems: for light conversion from green to blue (Fig. 2A, 9,10-diphenylanthracene, DPA, and Pt(II) octaethylporphyrin, PtOEP), red to blue (Fig. 2B, perylene, PERYL, and Pd(II) meso-tetraphenyltetrabenzoporphyrin, PdPh4TBP) and red to green (Fig. 2C, 9,10-bis(phenylethynyl) anthracene, BPEA, and Pd(II) meso-tetraphenyltetrabenzoporphyrin, PdPh4TBP). The molecular structures of the compounds are reported in the ESI (Fig. S1). Up-converted PL can be obtained in solution at excitation power densities lower than 1 mW cm2. However, to evaluate the realistic technological applicability for each SUC system it is crucial to know (i) the maximum QYSUC, (ii) the excitation intensity threshold Ith (namely the incident fluence required to maximize the conversion yield), and (iii) the saturation intensity Isat, which indicates the excitation fluence for which TA becomes strong enough to negatively affect the efficiency of the bimolecular processes necessary for SUC (ET, TTA).

Eqn (1) indicates that QYSUC is determined by intrinsic photophysical properties of the donor and acceptor moieties (such as f, A, ISC), by quantities depending on the molecular concentration (ET) and by a parameter (TTA) which depends on both intrinsic and extrinsic factors. As a consequence, SUC optimization requires a careful selection of donor/acceptor pairs as well as a suitable choice of their respective concentrations. The factor 1/2 is required since two low-energy photons are needed to produce a high-energy one. The parameter f is the statistical probability for obtaining a singlet excited state after annihilation of two triplet states. The annihilation is mediated by the formation of a collisional complex (TT) of singlet, triplet or quintet multiplicity (S, T, Q in Fig. 1) originating from the tensor product of the initial spin states. The weighted statistical probability of formation of these states is 1:3:5.3235 Generally, the pure statistical factor f = 1/9 representing the potential fraction of generated singlets can be enlarged to 1/4, since quintet complexes are not energetically accessible. The TT complex then produces a ground state moiety, through quenching of a triplet plus a higher-energy Tn,A state which decays back to TA. Therefore, for each four TT encounter complexes (3 with triplet character and 1 with singlet) five TA are lost for one generated up-converted photon, thus giving the expected upper limit for aromatic compounds f = 2/5. The latter value implies that 40% of TT collisions generate an excited singlet, as it is indeed observed in anthracene.3638 In some cases this limit can be further exceeded. If T2,A TA > kBT the collisional complex can decay back to TA with no energy loss, or T2,A states can undergo reverse intersystem crossing into S1, which leads to fluorescence from the T2,A channel.39 In this scenario, f values approaching unity can in principle be reached for suitably selected acceptors, as shown by Cheng et al. who recently demonstrated f values up to 0.66 between rubrene triplets (i.e. QYSUC = 0.33).40

As concerns the other parameters in eqn (1), the quantum yield of acceptor PL, A, is given by the ratio krad/ktot, where ktot is the total decay rate of the excited state and krad is the radiative decay rate of the underpinning electronic transition. In general, A depends on dye concentration, excitation power density and on the local environment surrounding the dye, which determines the non-radiative deactivation channels.41,42 A good acceptor should have A 1, and its emission efficiency should be preserved in the working conditions of the targeted SUC application and ideally in the solid state.

These results allow for tracing further guidelines for the realization of SUC based devices. Specifically, the analysis of back-ET and Isat highlights the importance of careful tuning of the concentration of photoactive moieties in order to find a convenient equilibrium between technological and photophysical requirements for optimized SUC. For example, large CD-values ensure high optical density of a potential SUC coating, but concomitantly promote strong back-ET with detrimental effects on QYSUC. As a consequence, very large CAs must be used to push Fw as close to unity as possible, which ultimately renders the device performances independent of back-ET. The proposed kinetic analysis is strongly dependent on the electronic properties of the employed donor/acceptor pairs, which determine the bimolecular interaction radii and consequently Fw, Bk and TT.

It is noteworthy that the proposed model maintains its validity as long as the chemical compounds are stable under high intensity excitation and in high concentration regimes as in the case of DPA and PtOEP. It has been demonstrated that photoinduced processes such as dimerization by cycloaddition chemistry and photooxidation could affect QYSUC.62,63 Therefore, the extent of possible photoinduced chemical reactions must be estimated to avoid quenching of QYSUC due to molecular degradation (see Fig. S2, ESI).

3. SUC in the solid state

To effectively develop technological applications of SUC, it is necessary to extend the high QYSUC at low excitation observed in solution to the solid state. At this aim, some fundamental aspects should be considered for the rational design of efficient up-converting solid materials. (i) As previously demonstrated, the matrix must ensure reasonably high diffusion of molecules and/or molecular excitons, which is strictly necessary for SUC operation at low excitation photon densities.65,66 (ii) The hosts must protect the dyes from molecular oxygen (O2) which typically quenches the triplet states involved in SUC.29,60 (iii) The materials should be inexpensive and easily processable for industrial fabrication techniques.

In order to fulfil the first requirement and ensure a large exciton mobility, we have grown a DPA single crystal doped with PtOEP (Cryst-UC).67 Indeed, in molecular single crystals triplet excitons have a diffusion length similar to that of dyes in low viscosity solvents.6871 The cryst-UC (0.4 0.5 mm2) luminescence spectrum under 532 nm excitation is reported in Fig. 5. Besides the intense emission from PtOEP at 660 nm, a weak blue light at 435 nm is emitted from DPA, with intensity increasing with the square of the incident power, thus confirming its SUC origin. To understand the reasons for such low QYSUC, we investigated the sample morphology. Fig. 6A is a picture of Cryst-UC, grown with the procedure described in ref. 66. Fig. 6B reports a Cryst-UC image taken by a confocal microscope under 532 nm CW excitation. Despite the presence of uniform areas, several structures emitting red light are observed. Fig. 6C reports the same image filtered to selectively detect the red emission. The bright red emitting needles in the left high corner on the crystal surface are composed of aggregated PtOEP, which crystallize separately from DPA.72 The segregated pure donor crystals cannot experience the ET necessary to SUC and no blue emission is detected from these areas (Fig. 6D). All in all, single crystals obtained by co-crystallization of acceptor/donor tend to form segregated crystal structures, thus preventing the intimate contact between donors and an acceptor at the molecular level which is needed for the efficient ET to occur. Novel strategies to avoid segregation upon co-crystallization should be therefore envisaged. Following the suggestion of Islangulov et al., we turn our attention on dye doped amorphous polymeric films.7 These authors incorporated Pd(II) octaethylporphyrin (PdOEP) and DPA into a copolymer of ethyleneoxide and epichlorohydrin (EO-EPI). By using this host, they successfully obtained SUC from 544 nm to 435 nm by low power non-coherent excitation thanks to the molecular mobility of the dye within the rubber polymer which allows ET and TTA. Up-converted light is indeed completely suppressed at 77K when the system is frozen losing its rubber properties. We and others have been able to observe SUC by using more rigid polymers such as cellulose acetate or poly-methylmethacrylate (PMMA). Cellulose acetate is an inert and easy to handle host matrix. It is transparent in the spectral range of optical activity of the selected chromophores (PdPh4TBP and BPEA) which successfully yield red-to-green SUC. We have directly compared the QYSUC to that of the corresponding THF solution under low power excitation, and have observed a reduction of three orders of magnitude. Even with Fw 1, the molecular mobility of the acceptor and its exciton diffusivity in cellulose acetate are too low to ensure a good TTA.73 SUC was observed also in rigid PMMA films. To overcome the problem of low molecular mobility Merkel and Dinnocenzo used very high concentrations of the emitter molecule thus allowing for exciton migration within the acceptor population. Despite this strategy a disappointingly low QYSUC value of 2 104 has been measured under solar excitation power density of 0.9 mW cm2 (estimated by indirect methods based on the comparison between directly excited acceptor fluorescence and donor phosphorescence).74

A general principle for selecting host polymers for SUC could be deduced from these results, as proposed by T. N. Singh-Rachford et al.75 The best approach is to use host materials with low glass transition temperature (TG).76 When T < TG, the amorphous regions of a polymer solid are in the glassy state and polymer chains are frozen in their position. The chains are incapable of segmental motion which allows the dye molecules to wiggle. Under these conditions the excitation diffusion is determined by the hopping between the nearby acceptor centres. Since energy migration is due to short range interactions, the total excitation diffusion is strongly limited. As shown above, Fw can be manipulated by using large acceptor/donor concentration. However, the portion of excited acceptors in glassy hosts is limited to a small sphere of volume determined by the ET radius.24,29,65,77 Due to its low excitation mobility, the density of TA states is enough to allow TTA only in the limited region around donor centres. Polymers with low TG are instead in the partially fluid rubber state even at room temperature, which translates in chromophores with sufficient mobility for SUC to take place.75,78

At last, the coupling of the chromophores to an amorphous matrix should be carefully checked, since it could increase the decay rate of metastable TA states by activating additional non-radiative decay channels with detrimental effects on TTA.41,55,79

To date, several groups reported on solid state SUC, showing a variety of potential polymeric hosts. For example, the acceptor moiety can be eliminated by using a properly selected emitting host matrix. This method has been demonstrated in poly-9,9-bis(2-ethyl-hexyl)fluorene (PF2/6) films doped with PdOEP and in a blue emitting poly-spirobifluoreneanthracene (PSFA) co-polymer doped with low concentration of PtOEP.31,80 Upon 532 nm excitation of PdOEP the characteristic blue emission of PF2/6 was observed together with the expected phosphorescence of the PdOEP component.31 In this particular case, the authors proposed different mechanisms responsible for up-converted PF2/6 emission. Beyond the SUC described in Fig. 1, sequential multiphoton absorption and/or TTA on the porphyrin followed by ET to the higher energy singlets of the host result in blue up-converted emission. It is possible to discriminate between these mechanisms by considering the excitation power density regime, which should in principle be significantly higher in the case of multiphoton absorption compared to SUC. At any rate, co-polymers such as PSFA open the way to additional tuning methods of the SUC host matrix. Laquai et al. demonstrated a net increase in the up-converted fluorescence compared with the anthracene-free spirobifluorene copolymer (PSF) and polyfluorene (PF) thin films. This increase is ascribed to the higher ET towards anthracene arising from a better energy resonance with respect to PSF and PF triplet states. It is concluded that tuning the position of the polymer triplet level and proper choice of the dopant in samples of optimized compositions can lead to a further increase of solid state QYSUC.80

An interesting example of converting solid system has been very recently tested by J. Lissau et al. The authors observed low power photon SUC on the surface of nanostructured metal oxides, the same materials currently used to build dye sensitized solar cells. Unfortunately, they recorded an extremely low QYSUC of 6 104, given that photoactive dyes on oxide surfaces are completely exposed to oxygen.81 We found a possible solution to the O2 contamination problem by incorporating a SUC bicomponent system into polymeric nanoparticles. The confinement of all of the involved photophysical processes in a nanometre-scale volume makes each nanoparticle a single and isolated high-efficiency up-converting unit. As a consequence, these dual dye-loaded nanoparticles can be used to produce drop-cast films, as well as dopants for polymeric matrices, while still preserving the performances of the moieties in solution.22

5. Display and lighting applications of SUC

Systems capable of generating short-wavelength radiation from long-wavelength light sources are very desirable because of their wide application potential, which is not limited to energy conversion technologies, but instead extends to coloured displays and lighting applications. Specifically, given current global energy concerns, increasing efforts are being focused in the development of materials that emit white-light in the pursuit of highly efficient lighting devices, thereby minimizing electrical energy waste in daily applications.82,83 With this perspective, solid state SUC systems should potentially provide a remarkable progress in design and fabrication of advanced light-emitting devices.

The principle on which most up-conversion based displays operate is multiphoton absorption followed by ET in rare-earth ion doped solids.84 However these systems require moderate (tens of kW cm2) to high (MW cm2) excitation intensities, which are usually above the damage threshold of most optical materials.85 Another disadvantage of ion-doped UC displays is the complicated technology to obtain large area devices. Indeed, in the case of heavy metal fluoride glasses doped with rare earth ions, samples with acceptable optical quality have a relatively small size (of the order of 102 m), while dispersing doped glassy UC powders into a polymer matrix results in highly scattering materials.84 T. Miteva et al. overcame these problems by proposing robust all-organic, flexible and transparent displays based SUC, which can be fabricated on large surface areas.8,19 The displays have good brightness and show stable operation for more than 100 hours. The optical density of the screens can be easily tuned, in order to obtain the required transmittance for the excitation beam depending on the application, and the response time of the device can be adjusted allowing for kHz-refreshing rate of the displayed information. Moreover, the remarkable robustness of the materials allows efficient manufacturing techniques, such as printing and roll-to-roll processing, to be used.

Also white light emission can be realized by exploiting SUC. T. N. Singh-Rachford et al. successfully obtained broad band white emission by combining up-conversion and down-conversion phenomena in acceptor species which form emitting excimers in bicomponent systems. An emission spectrum can be tuned by varying the acceptor concentration CA, on which depend both up-converted emission intensity and excimers formation. In this way, by mixing the acceptor singlet emission with lower energy excimer luminescence, a well balanced white light can be obtained starting from single frequency laser excitation.20 Chen et al. obtained a bright white emission by SUC by exploiting a different approach.21 They employed 2,4,5,7-tetraiodo-6-hydroxy-3-fluorone (TIHF) and DPA as donor/acceptor pair. The sensitizer exhibits a phosphorescent emission in the yellow region of the spectrum in an energy range far from DPA lowest triplet state. As a consequence, the ET to DPA is not complete, but still efficient to achieve blue up-converted light to be mixed with the yellow one. By varying CA and Iexc (which below Ith controls QYSUC), the authors obtained a good quality white light (X = 0.33, Y = 0.40 in the CIE convention) by using an excitation power density one order of magnitude lower than the one necessary in Tm3+/Yb3+/Er3+-doped nanocrystals.86

The explored approaches are based on the exploitation of dye pairs. We are currently working on an alternative technique to obtain white or tunable PL by introducing a third component. Since the acceptor up-converted PL can be shifted in energy of more than 0.5 eV with respect to the absorption band of the light harvester, we introduced an additional molecular species able to emit light after being excited by the acceptor up-converted light. In this way, it is possible to achieve multi-peak luminescence in a wide spectral region and by varying dye concentrations, to obtain white light emission upon single low-frequency excitation.87

6. Materials for SUC

Commonly used SUC dyes present a good solubility in a variety of solvents, and chemical as well as thermal stability. Both commercial and custom synthesized polyacenes-like acceptors and metalloorganic donors fulfil the requirements previously illustrated to allow SUC with reasonably high yield. Fig. 7 shows the spectral properties of a commonly used acceptor/donor pair which gives rise to red-to-green conversion. Incident red photons are absorbed in the Q-band of PdPh4TBP around 630 nm (blue circles). Donor PL (blue triangles) is resonant with BPEA triplets at 810 nm (red arrow), which are quickly populated by ET and experience TTA with a consequent singlet emission in the green region (green solid line). The large energy gap between Soret and Q absorption bands allows to keep high BPEA A thus avoiding re-absorption by the donor.8790 Furthermore, the large acceptor SATA singlet/triplet splitting permits acceptor sensitization even by using different donors, with an UC energy gain as large as 0.65 eV.48,91,92
Fig. 7 Solid line is the PL spectrum of a BPEA in 104 M THF solution. It falls in spectral window between Soret and Q absorption bands of PdPh4TBP (circles). PdPh4TBP phosphorescence (triangles) is quenched by BPEA triplets (solid arrow), whose energy is enough to match BPEA singlet manifold absorption (dotted line) after TTA thus generating PL.

A system composed of aromatic hydrocarbons as acceptors paired to metalloorganic complexes as donors shows unbeaten performances up to now.1 However, the choice of producing SUC by a limited family of compounds reduces the wavelength range which can be up-converted. For example, it is still difficult to synthesize metalloporphyrins to up-convert solar photons possessing energy below silicon based PV bandgap solar photons.93 Moreover, the highest energy blue shift so far obtained by these materials is 0.8 eV.10,80

The design of new acceptors and donors is essential for successful future application to real devices. T. N. Singh-Rachford et al. reported a new acceptor molecule belonging to the family of BODIPY chromophores.9497 BODIPY fluorophores possess high A and high resistance to photo-bleaching and are therefore suitable materials to be used under continuous excitation.98,99 The same group successively proposed a dye pair to obtain visible-to-UV up-conversion by the use of a small metal-free organic molecule as sensitizer, namely the 2,3-butanedione (BIACETYL). In deareated benzene solution with a commercial laser dye 2,5-diphenyloxazole (PPO) as acceptor, they obtained UC up-converted dye emission at 360 nm by 442 nm excitation. Unfortunately, the intrinsically low A keeps QYSUC at a low level.1,80 Recently, clever supramolecular architectures (porphinatozinc (II) and metal(II)polypyridyl (M) units connected via an ethynyl linker) as donors have been proposed, with the purpose of expanding the spectral window covered by SUC systems and finely tune the electronic properties of known moieties to improve the ET processes even at the solid state.99 Most importantly, these supramolecular sensitizers allow for the use of new acceptors which can produce relevant wavelength shifting from the NIR to the visible spectral region.

Another new approach is to consider novel donor/acceptor pairs as proposed by Cheng et al., who observed that SUC is subject to several enthalpic losses that limit the up-conversion margin. They address one of these losses, namely the donor/acceptor ET, by selecting a fluorophore whose triplet state energy slightly exceeds that of the sensitizer. By choosing suitable concentrations for the sensitizer and emitter molecules, it is possible to use the entropy (instead of the energy) as the driving force for the migration of triplet excitation from the sensitizer to the emitter, thus obtaining a large energy shift by SUC of 0.94 eV.100

7. Conclusions

A complete description of the SUC process has been presented based on the general principles that control the SUC efficiency independently from the specific molecules adopted. SUC has been fully parameterized to quantitatively predict the overall efficiency of every donor/acceptor pair. The proposed analysis allows for tracing important guidelines for the rational design of up-converting solid materials, and to improve the existing systems. Furthermore, the analysis of SUC kinetics, improved with the introduction of the back-ET effect and Isat, predicts the optimal excitation range to fully exploit SUC materials.

A maximum QYSUC = 0.26 2 with Ith = 1.7 1015 ph cm2 s1 has been reported for a model SUC dye-pair in solution, which emphasizes the potential of this process as a powerful tool to effectively manipulate photon energy. The striking feature of the SUC mechanism is that it works with low excitation power densities, in contrast to standard UC processes. This feature could be exploited for the fabrication of inexpensive, low-consumption light sources and wavelength-shifting devices for lighting technologies. Most importantly, it opens the way to SUC-PV devices where the low-energy tail of a solar spectrum, which is usually lost because of the low light-harvesting cross section of most PV materials in the red and infrared spectral ranges (Fig. 8), is efficiently recovered.101


Fig. 8 Outline of a model PV cell coupled with a SUC device. Incoming photons are partially absorbed (blue and green arrows) while sub-bandgap photons (red arrows) reach the SUC layer. Here, they are up-converted and emitted, thus higher energy photons re-absorbed by the cell.

In order to be employed in PVs, it is imperative that SUC operates at maximum efficiency under the solar irradiance. Thanks to the fast energy diffusion in solution, which enhances all short range bimolecular processes, even at low dye concentrations, Ith is comparable to the solar intensity under AM1.5 conditions.102 Indeed, samples presented in this paper can approach their maximum QYSUC even under solar excitation as, for example DPA2 which shows QYSUC (AM1.5) = 0.13. However, the reported yields in solid state materials are still significantly lower than in solution. For amorphous flexible polymeric matrixes, mobility of both molecules and excitons is strongly reduced which represents the main bottleneck for efficient SUC in these materials. For crystalline solids, the tendency of phase separation seriously reduces the necessary intimate contact between donor and acceptor moieties necessary for ET. To date, the best host matrix has been identified with disordered rubbery polymers and co-polymers with low TG, able to grant an even low molecular mobility and a good mixing of the active moieties.1,103

In summary, several research lines should be pursued to optimize the SUC efficiency for technological applications: (i) the design and synthesis of new donor/acceptor pairs with the necessary photophysical requirements, namely strong one-photon absorption in the NIR region and fast ISC for the donor. For the acceptor, long-lived triplet states resonant with the triplets of the donor moiety are needed. Electronic structures suitable for the singlet generation by TTA and high A are also critical. The latter aspect is necessary to cover all the spectral ranges interesting for applications and also to obtain photon energy conversion with reasonable yields. (ii) In the solid state, the overall QYSUC is still too low. While the net ET efficiency ET is tuneable to its maximum value by varying CA in each matrix, the second order rate constant TT (which is determined by energy diffusivity) represents the real limitation for achieving low power high QYSUC. It is in fact difficult to obtain the dye mobility of a solution in a solid material. Single crystals could in principle grant large diffusion lengths for the excitation energy, but their applicability is hampered by the high costs and the cumbersome procedure for the fabrication of suitable bi-component crystals. Room for possible improvements is represented by reducing of acceptors spontaneous decay rate . Such a goal could be achieved by using a matrix that shields the dye from the interactions with the environment. In such a way, even with an intrinsic low diffusion coefficient, the long triplet lifetime should permit a sufficient diffusion length to allow high TTA.

However, the easiest and most direct approach to reach maximum QYSUC in solids seems to modify the absorption properties of the light harvesting dye. In fact, large absorption bandwidths (E) = a(E)dE of 5060 nm versus 15 nm currently used could be achieved by the introduction of proper donor mixtures, with a distribution of absorption bands while keeping the necessary energy matching condition between acceptor and donor triplet states. In such a way, the enhanced harvesting of low power solar light combined with high ET can ensure large TA densities needed to have TTA close to one, therefore easily reaching the maximum efficiency regime.
