Abstract

Colloidal quantum dots (CQDs) exhibit extraordinary features due to their bandgap tunability and solution processing. Instead of the ZnO layer usually used as the electron transport layer (ETL) in CQD heterojunction devices, we developed, for the first time, tin dioxide (SnO2) as the ETL in colloidal quantum dot solar cells (QDSCs). Its wider bandgap and higher electron mobility, as well as appropriate band alignment with PbS QDs, could favor light absorption and photocarrier extraction. Our low-temperature processed SnO2 film could retain chlorine atoms (SnO2â€“Cl) to achieve interface passivation in QDSCs. Utilizing 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide (EMII) as the absorber ligand, our superior device obtained a power conversion efficiency of 9.37%, which was 44% higher than that of a control device. Physical characterizations revealed that this remarkable improvement could be ascribed to the chlorine passivation of the SnO2/QD interface contact and to the EMII ligand passivation effect on the QD surface. Our newly developed ETL, along with an efficient interface passivation technique, is expected to enhance the performance of full solution-processed colloidal QDSCs.

Introduction

Lead sulfide (PbS) colloidal quantum dots (CQDs) have gained considerable attention in the innovative technology of optoelectronics such as solar cells,1,2 light-emitting diodes,3,4 photodetectors5,6 and lasers,7 due to their size-tunable characteristics, simple synthetic route,8â€“11 solution processing and efficient multiple exciton generation.9,12 Their broad absorption (up to 2 Î¼m) properties boost the potential to harvest low-energy photons, which is difficult for dye-sensitized and organic solar cells.13 Their band gap (Eg) could be optimally tuned to âˆ¼1.3 eV for harvesting visible-near infrared radiations.13 Also, PbS quantum dot solar cells (QDSCs) have been involved in ligand passivation in recent years.14â€“16

In planar PbS QDSCs, metal oxides such as ZnO and TiO2 have commonly been utilized as the electron transport layer (ETL) in colloidal QDSCs, due to their high dielectric constant (Îµ = 7) for exciton dissociation17â€“19 and suitable energy levels. Moreover, the wider bandgap of ETLs is expected to transmit more high-energy photons for photocurrent generation.20 In this regard, doped ETL material such as Mgâ€“ZnO has been explored to enlarge the bandgap.21 In addition, most synthesized ZnO layers using the solâ€“gel method have led to carbon residuals with deteriorated carrier transport.22 Based on the limitations mentioned above, tin dioxide (SnO2) has emerged as a new ETL because of its unique properties, such as wider band gap (âˆ¼3.6 eV), high mobility, good anti-reflection nature, good low-temperature processing,20,23 and compatibility with CQD device fabrication techniques. These novel properties promise superior performances in perovskite solar cells.20,24 In addition, theoretical and experimental results have demonstrated that interfacial incorporation of chlorine atoms could greatly suppress the oxygenic defect which is commonly introduced in halide ligands in QDs.17,25 Utilizing this chlorine-based contact passivation technique, Tan et al. obtained efficient and stable TiO2â€“Cl/perovskite solar cells.26 Therefore, in the present work, we first combined the novelties of tin dioxide with chlorine (SnO2â€“Cl) for efficient PbS QDSCs.

In addition to interface passivation, bulk defect passivation of PbS QD absorbers also played a key part in the efficiency of energy conversion. Various organic and inorganic ligands were investigated to alleviate geminate recombination originating from midgap trap states in QDs band structures.27,28 Halide ligands, especially 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide (EMII), strongly favor halide binding and excellent removal of hydroxide moieties during ligand exchange in PbS QDs.29

Herein, we introduce SnO2â€“Cl ETL in PbS QDSCs with a device structure of FTO/SnO2â€“Cl/PbS-EMII/PbS-EDT (dithioglycol)/Au (PbS-EMII, PbS QDs are passivated by EMII ligands). Jsc and Voc for the newly developed device were improved with SnO2â€“Cl ETL. Systematic physical characterization showed that the improvement mechanism was attributed to retention of the Cl atom for interfacial passivation. In addition, compared with traditional TBAI ligands, EMII ligand exchange could further remove the deep defect state of hydroxyl ions to obtain a higher FF. By integrating the unique features of SnO2â€“Cl with PbS-EMII QDs, the superior device reached 9.37%, with a Voc of 0.57 V, a Jsc of 25.63 mA cmâˆ’2 and a FF of 0.64. The present work demonstrated that SnO2â€“Cl ETL has good potential for low-cost, large-area and highly efficient colloidal QDSCs.

Compared with the reported efficiencies of PbS QDSCs, the main deficit of SnO2â€“Cl/QD-TBAI devices came from the low FF which may be induced from the band tails during the TBAI solid ligand exchange process.28,29 A recent report demonstrated that EMII salt may facilitate stronger halide binding and more efficient passivation on QDs surfaces compared with TBAI ligands.29Fig. 5a shows the molecular structure diagram for the two ligands: EMII has fewer steric structures and smaller molecule size. Thus, the EMII ligand was expected to more efficiently passivate the QD surface so as to shrink the band tails. To confirm the passivation effect, we collected the PCEs of two batch devices using TBAI and EMII as ligands (60 devices). The device performance distribution is summarized in Fig. 5b, which indicated better reproducibility and higher average efficiency (âˆ¼8.8%) based on SnO2â€“Cl/PbS-EMII devices. EQE values of SnO2â€“Cl/PbS-EMII devices approached 90% at 450â€“630 nm spectra, whereas the values for the reference only reached 70%. From the demonstration above, the improved performances of PbS QDSCs were highly reliant on the optimized SnO2â€“Cl ETLs. Thus, SnO2 film was characterized in detail to probe its origins. First, the ETL thin-film products from different annealing temperatures were measured by XPS to investigate the chemical component effect. The Sn 3d and O 1s characteristic peaks of the product film are shown in Fig. 6aâ€“d and Fig. S3,â€  respectively. The Sn 3d spectrum can be fitted well by two oxidation states (Sn2+ and Sn4+). For annealing treatment at 100 Â°C, it was composed of SnO and SnO2. The molar ratio of SnO/SnO2 was about 0.6:1, which illustrated that the product was non-stoichiometric and contained heterogeneous components. The 100 Â°C-annealed ETL films showed low conductivity with an amorphous state (data not shown) which may lead to deteriorated device performance (Fig. 6e). When the annealing temperature reached 200 Â°C, there were obvious shifts for Sn 3d and O 1s peaks (Fig. 6a, b, S3b and câ€ ). The film composition was evolved into SnO2 and the ratio of SnO/SnO2 was 0.05:1. When the annealing temperature was increased further to 400 Â°C, the composition changed slightly (Fig. 6c and S3dâ€ ) with a SnO/SnO2 ratio of âˆ¼0.04:1. Simultaneously, as shown in Fig. 6d, the chlorine component gradually decreased in the tin-oxide product with increasing treatment temperature. When it reached 400 Â°C, the chlorine content was almost removed. To explore the effect of SnO2 composition on device performance, we fabricated QDSCs devices based on different annealing temperatures for tin-oxide ETLs. In Fig. 6e and Table S1,â€  the 100 Â°C-annealed ETL devices had low performance (PCE <2%). However, the 200 Â°C-annealed ETL (SnO2â€“Cl) device achieved the best efficiency (PCE >9%) and the 400 Â°C-annealed SnO2 devices reached a PCE of just âˆ¼3%. Such distinct performances confirmed that the low temperature (200 Â°C)-processed films with Cl incorporation obtained higher PCE than Cl-free devices (400 Â°C treatment). To further confirm the key role of chlorine, a control experiment utilizing a non-chlorine tin precursor (SnSO4, tin sulfate) instead of SnCl2 was carried out and the annealing temperature was kept at 200 Â°C. The power conversion efficiency was only âˆ¼5% (data not shown), much lower than our innovative devices. Halide ions and halide molecule ligands have been traditionally used in PbX (X = S, Se) QDs to suppress oxidation and passivate surface trap states.25,37 Thus, we reasoned that interfacial Cl capping in SnO2 films could passivate the interface trap states. The dark Jâ€“V curves (Fig. 6f) revealed the poorest rectification in 100 Â°C-annealed devices, whereas the reverse saturation current of 200 Â°C-annealed devices was highly suppressed. To gain further insight into the passivation results from SnO2â€“Cl, we characterized the charge-transfer kinetics and charge recombination in SnO2â€“ and SnO2â€“Cl-based devices. The charge transport and recombination times (Ï„t and Ï„r) were extracted from transient photocurrent and photovoltage decay, respectively. This similarity indicated that charge transfer was not the main reason for performance enhancement. However, the charge-recombination lifetime of SnO2â€“Cl-based devices (Ï„r = 140 Î¼s) was obviously longer than that of Cl-free SnO2 devices (Ï„r = 60 Î¼s) (Fig. 7b), a result that agreed well with the alleviated charge recombination at the SnO2â€“Cl/QDs interface. Similar results were also reported in recent pioneering work on chlorine contact passivation in TiO2/perovskite high-efficiency solar cells.26 From these results, we confirmed the interfacial Cl capping effect for SnO2 films, which could suppress the trap states and reduce interfacial recombination.

Therefore, we conclude that our SnO2â€“Cl ETL by a simple solâ€“gel method can achieve high-quality contact passivation and efficient carrier extraction. The newly developed efficient ETL combined with EMII ligand passivation holds great potential for high-performance and flexible QDSCs.

Conclusion

We developed SnO2â€“Cl ETL in PbS QDSCs with a device structure of FTO/SnO2â€“Cl/PbS-EMII/PbS-EDT/Au. Compared with typical FTO/ZnO/PbS-TBAI/PbS-EDT/Au devices, Voc, Jsc and FF values were all improved for the newly developed devices. Systematic physical characterization revealed that the improvement was mainly attributed to a SnO2â€“Cl ETL and EMII ligand passivation effect. In addition to the aligned energy levels and superior optoelectronic properties of SnO2â€“Cl ETL, the incorporation of chlorine played a key part in heterojunction passivation. Also, the EMII ligand could further suppress the introduction of deep defects so as to obtain a higher FF. By combining SnO2â€“Cl ETL with PbS-EMII QDs, the champion device reached 9.37% efficiency with a Voc of 0.57 V, a Jsc of 25.63 mA cmâˆ’2 and a FF of 0.64. The newly developed SnO2â€“Cl ETL demonstrated superior coupling capability with PbS QDs. In cooperation with new, efficient ligands, the development of PbS QDSCs holds great potential.
