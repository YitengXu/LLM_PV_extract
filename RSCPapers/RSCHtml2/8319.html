Effect of graphene between photoanode and sensitizer on the intramolecular and intermolecular electron transfer process

Abstract

The main goal of this work is to investigate the effects of a graphene layer between the photosensitive layer and semiconductor substrates on the electron transport performance of dye-sensitized solar cells from the perspective of intramolecular arrangement and interfaces. The benzothiadiazole sensitizer YKP-88 is used as the photosensitive layer and the influence of the graphene layer on the short-circuit current density (Jsc) and open-circuit voltage (Voc) is also discussed by exploring the frontier molecular orbitals, intramolecular charge transfer, weak interaction, interfacial electron dynamic propagations and other microscopic parameters after the anchoring of the graphene layer. The results demonstrate that the graphene layer can accelerate the electron injection of dye molecules into the semiconductor substrate, which not only has a qualitative reduction in injection time, but also has a qualitative change in the increase of the injection amount. In addition, it is also found that the graphene layer increases the stereoscopic effect, the absorption of long wavelength (>700 nm) photon flu and the amount of electron injection into the photoanode, which benefits the intramolecular charge transfer and increases the Jsc and Voc of solar cells. The combination of intermolecular and interfacial perspectives indicates that the appropriate configuration of graphene layers can effectively improve the photoelectric conversion efficiency of dye-sensitized solar cells.

1. Introduction

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are widely researched as a result of their simple synthesis, environmental friendliness and compatibility with rigid and flexible semiconductors.1 A typical DSSC has four important components, namely, a semiconductor photoelectrode, a photosensitizer, a redox couple (I/I3) and a counter electrode.24 The sensitizer is adsorbed on the surface of the photoelectrode by the acceptor group. When exposed to visible light, the excited photosensitizer will inject electrons into the photoanode. The electrons are collected by the photoanode and transmitted to the counter electrode. The oxidized sensitizer obtains electrons from the electrolyte to complete a photoelectric cycle process.57 The sensitizer plays an important role in photoelectric conversion by injecting free electrons from the photoactive layer into the photoelectrode. Among the reported sensitizer materials, organic dyes have aroused significant attention due to their various types and easy molecular design. Specifically, dyes with donor-bridgeacceptor (DA) structures are potentially near-infrared sensitizers for DSSCs and have been widely researched and developed due to their excellent charge transmission performance.8,9

There have been numerous works on the application of high-performance sensitizers with DA structures in DSSCs. Based on this type, a series of novel structures have been gradually researched, such as the ADA and DADA structures. Di and co-workers10 researched a -substituted porphyry in sensitizer and metallic zinc was introduced into the donor part of the molecule, which allowed the photoelectric conversion efficiency (PCE) of the sensitizer to reach 13%. Nagarajan and co-workers11 synthesized six types of sensitizer molecules with DA structure. Donor parts of the six types of sensitizer were non-traditional ethynyl-pyrene structures, with the PCE of one sensitizer reaching 12%. Maragani and collaborators12 used Knoevenagel condensation reactions to synthesize two sensitizers with (DA)2DA structures and revealed the donoracceptor interaction through theoretical calculations. Both the experimental and theoretical results showed the ultra-high PCE of the sensitizer.

Quantum chemical computation has become a powerful means to study the photophysical and photochemical properties of sensitizers.1319 By adjusting the spatial configuration between graphene and the semiconductor photoanode, Xi et al.20 found that the CTi single bond connection between graphene and the semiconductor could accelerate electron injection, thereby providing an effective way for future theoretical research on graphene materials. Li and his collaborators21 used quantum chemical calculations to study the structure-performance relationship by changing the -bridge, showing that the -conjugation section, including electron-deficient and electron-rich portions, can improve the ability of light absorption and the electron delocalization can be effectively promoted by reducing the aromaticity of the -bridge and destroying the coherence of the system. By means of quantum mechanical simulation, Lu and co-workers22 studied the transferred electron of a new pyridinium in the sensitizer/photoanode interface. Experimental data show that pyridinium ylide-based anchors are beneficial to absorbing light and improving the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) performance. In addition, the edge of the photoanode conduction band moves up, further increasing the Jsc and Voc.

Based on the research of Xu et al.,23 the photophysical and photochemical characteristics of the dye molecule YKP-88 are simulated using density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent DFT(TDDFT). In addition, the graphene layer (GR) between the photosensitive and semiconductor layers is also been studied theoretically. It has to be said that titanium dioxide/graphene composites have been preliminarily studied in other battery fields, but have not been reported in the field of DSSCs.24,25 Therefore, we establish a theoretical model to explore the feasibility of this scheme. The main purpose of this research is to understand the role of the GR in DSSCs and to explain the role of the GR in cells from the microscopic perspective of electron transfer. We also theoretically establish the global scan methods to consider intramolecular weak interaction, Jsc parameter, electron transfer dynamic and Voc parameter changes after the GR is plated between the sensitizer and the semiconductor to explain the effect of graphene.

2. Computational methods

The ground-state optimal structures of sensitizers were simulated using the B3LYP/6-31G(d) basis set.2628 Frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) were also obtained with this basis set and processed by the Multiwfn29 and VMD30 programs. In order to make the final simulation results comparable with the experimental values, we also used the experimental solvent (CH2Cl2) in the process of simulation, so as to make the final simulation results more credible. In addition, we analyzed the charge-transfer excitation during the electron transition from a three-dimensional perspective in order to explore the influence of the addition of GR on electron transfer. In addition, the Coulomb interaction energy was also calculated to explore the effect of GR on the electron transfer. Due to the steric hindrance effects of the ring structure,36 the weak interaction with GR has also been investigated based on the non-covalent interactions method. Moreover, the special location of the GR is particularly important for the investigation of electron transfer at the interface.

We use a (TiO2)16 cluster as the photoanode substrate37 to balance the solid properties and time consumption, and the semi-empirical extended Hckel theory to calculate the dynamic process of interfacial electron transfer (IET). Moreover, the (TiO2)16 cluster was simulated using the LANL2TZ pseudopotential basis set and periodic boundary conditions were used to calculate the dye@(TiO2)16 system. The spectra of the density of states (DOS) was simulated by the GaussSum program and all the calculation data were obtained from the Gaussian 09 program suite using DFT and TDDFT.38,39

3.2 FMO analysis

The efficient injection of electrons from sensitizers to semiconductors and the regeneration of dyes in electrolytes play fundamental roles in the working mechanism of DSSCs. Therefore, the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels must meet the energy standard for electron transfer in dyes.

The HOMO level of the sensitizer is lower than the redox electrolyte of I3/I (4.8 eV vs. vacuum), which makes it easy for the dye to gain electrons from I3/I.45 The LUMO level of the sensitizer should be higher than the semiconductor band level of TiO2 (4.0 eV vs. vacuum), which ensures that the sensitizer can inject its own electrons into the semiconductor.46 The HOMOs, LUMOs and FMOs of the sensitizer and its graphene complex are shown in Fig. 2(a). As illustrated in Fig. 2(a), the HOMO for YKP-88 is 4.90 eV and the electrons are distributed among the donor and -conjugation parts, with most of them concentrated on triphenylamine (TPA). The LUMO energy level is 2.94 eV and the electrons are distributed among the -conjugation and acceptor moieties, with most of it concentrated on cyanoacrylic acid (CAA). When the sensitizer was anchored to the GR, there was no change in the HOMO level, but the LUMO energy level decreased to 2.99 eV, which reduced the energy gap of the sensitizer by 0.05 eV.

In order to further understand the tendency of electron transfer within the photoactive sensitizer, the FMO diagram and the projected density of state (PDOS) analysis are presented in Fig. S1 and S2(a and b) (ESI), respectively. By assessing Fig. S1 (ESI) and comparing the values of Fig. S2 (ESI), we can make reasonable judgments regarding the electron transfer. Compared with the LUMO, LUMO+1 and LUMO+2 of YKP-88/GR, there is only a small concentration of electron density in the electron-withdrawing groups CAA of isolated sensitizers, which indicates that the GR can improve the ability of electron transfer to the electron-withdrawing groups for excited dyes.

3.4 IP, EA and reorganization energy

In general, excellent electron transfer and hole transport capabilities, as well as the relative balance between electron and hole transport, are the necessary conditions for the outstanding performance of DSSC devices.4951 The smaller vertical ionization energy (IP) will facilitate the electron transfer ability of the sensitizer and the higher vertical electron affinity potential (EA) will facilitate the electron transfer from the sensitizer into the semiconductor. In addition, the reorganization energy can evaluate the equilibrium performance of sensitizers.

The IPs of the sensitizers YKP-88 and YKP-88/GR are 4.780 and 4.776 eV, respectively, which means that the hole transfer capacity of the two sensitizers is approximately equal. Meanwhile, the EAs of YKP-88 and the complex are 3.084 and 3.148 eV, respectively. This can be interpreted by the fact that the sensitizer anchored to graphene possesses a better electron-withdrawing capability. In addition, the intramolecular reorganization energy includes the hole reorganization energy (h) and the electron reorganization energy (e), and the transport performance of the sensitizer is improved with the decrease of the reorganization energy. Here, e and h represent the electron and hole transporting capacity, respectively:

e = (E0 E) + (E0 E0)(3)
h = (E+0 E+) + (E0+ E0)(4)
where E+0(E0) represents the energy of the cation (anion) state based on the neutral optimized state, E+(E) represents the energy of the cation (anion) based on the optimized state, E0+(E0) is the energy of the neutral state based on the optimized state of the cation (anion) and E0 is the energy of the neutral optimized state. The hole reorganization energies of the sensitizers YKP-88 and YKP-88/GR are 0.189 and 0.198 eV. The electron reorganization energies of the sensitizers YKP-88 and YKP-88/GR are 0.277 and 0.272 eV, respectively. The simulation results show that the ability of electron transport can be improved by anchoring the sensitizers on the GR.

3.5 Intramolecular weak interaction analysis

Steric hindrance not only affects the charge transfer within molecules but also the charge transfer between molecules, thus affecting the photoelectric overall performance of the DSSC. When the steric hindrance increases, it will cause some groups of the dye to deviate from the normal dihedral angle and cause tension within the molecule, which will produce a stereoscopic effect, so as to effectively inhibit the dye aggregation and improve the efficiency of light harvesting.52

We use a reduced density gradient (RDG) isosurface with color filling to distinguish the types of weak interaction within molecules and the results are shown in Fig. 5(a). Here, the x axis represents the sign of the second largest eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix of the electron density (2) multiplied by the electron density (). According to Bader's AIM theory, the electron density is concentrated on the left side of Fig. 5(a), which has an attractive interaction. Meanwhile, the steric effect indicates that the electron density is depleted at the right side of the figure. In Fig. 5(a), we can see that after the sensitizer is anchored on graphene, the more critical point appeared in the steric hindering area (red), with the most prominent part circled. Therefore, it can be concluded from Fig. 5(a) that the sensitizer has enhanced the stereoscopic effect after it anchored to the graphene, thus better inhibiting the dye aggregation. Although the sensitizer slightly increases the steric resistance after anchoring graphene, there are many reasons for this phenomenon. It may be due to the fact that the anchoring of graphene changed the structure of the sensitizer, which increased the steric hindrance. It is also a possible reason that the steric hindrance at a certain point in the complex is too large to inhibit the accumulation of dyes.

3.7 Electron transfer dynamics

The IET is defined as the survival probability of excited electrons from excited electronic states to the semiconductor by the time-evolved electronic wavefunction. The IET from the photosensitive layer to the semiconductor layer is affected by many factors, such as the type of semiconductor layer and the geometry of the sensitizer.50 Here, we focus on the effect of adding the GR between the photosensitive layer and a semiconductor layer on the dynamic process of IET. In addition, the (TiO2)16 cluster was calculated as a semiconductor layer to make the simulation results more reliable. The IET simulation is to expand the excited electronic states at time t|(t) (the probability of photo-excited electrons remaining in the dye) into a linear combination of the adsorbate atomic orbitals |Kj, and make it evolve with time by analyzing and integrating the time-dependent Schrdinger equation, as follows: . The and Ek are eigenstate and eigenvalue, which is obtained by solving the extended-Hckel (EH) eigenvalue problem based on Sij = Ki|Kj and atomic orbitals |Kj. Based on the EH Hamiltonian, both and orbitals contained in S, P and D atomic orbitals can be calculated.5557

When the system is excited, the electrons remain in the adsorbent before being injected into the semiconductor. Therefore, it is necessary to simulate the LUMO and above of the sensitizer to determine which orbital should be used as the starting point to calculate the IET. As can be seen from Fig. 3, YKP-88 calculates the IET from the LUMO+1 and YKP-88/GR calculates the IET from the LUMO. By observing the FMO, it can be clearly found that adding the GR to DSSC makes it easier for electrons to transition from the sensitizer to semiconductor. In Fig. 8, the TiO2 DOS and TiO2 PDOS represent pure (TiO2)16 clusters and the contribution of (TiO2)16 clusters to the total dye@(TiO2)16 system, respectively. The ECB value of the YKP-88 sensitizer is 0.09 eV. When the graphene sheet was added to the semiconductor surface, the value of ECB was 0.29 eV. This suggests that the GR can easily affect the conduction band edge of the semiconductor and these superior ECB values are conducive to improving the Voc of the DSSC. In addition, the quantity of electric charge transferred was calculated by the inter-fragment charge transfer method, where YKP-88 is 0.034C and YKP-88/GR is up to 0.997C. The nc could be deduced according to the charge of the elementary charge, so the nc of YKP-88 and YKP-88/GR was 2.31 1017 and 6.23 1018, respectively, which directly reflect the outstanding contribution of graphene layer to electron injection. Based on the above data, we can deduce the Voc of DSSC through eqn (9) and (10). The Voc of YKP-88 and YKP-88/GR is 683 and 968 mV, respectively. By comparing the experimental value (680 20 mV) of YKP-88 with the theoretical value, it can be seen that the theoretical value has a high credibility. Therefore, it can be concluded that the GR is conducive to the Voc of DSSC when it resides between the photoanode and photosensitive layer.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a DFT global scan was used to in-depth reveal the influence of adding GR on intramolecular charge transfer and photovoltaic performance in solar cells. The impacts of a GR on the DSSC are summarized as follows: (a) the graphene layer can improve the ability of electron transfer to electron-withdrawing groups in excited dyes; (b) through RDG analysis, the GR can increase the stereoscopic effect, which is beneficial to improving ICT characteristics; (c) the graphene layer can improve the absorption of photon flu in the long-wavelength region of the dye, thus contributing to the improvement of Jsc; (d) the GR can effectively reduce the electron injection time and increase the electron injection, thereby increasing the Voc of solar cells. Therefore, it can be hoped that the use of an appropriate GR is an effective approach to improve the PCE of the DSSC.
