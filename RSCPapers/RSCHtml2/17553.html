Abstract

In the past few years, bioinspired eco-friendly superhydrophobic materials (EFSMs) have made great breakthroughs, especially in the fields of environment, energy and biology, which have made remarkable contributions to the sustainable development of the natural environment. However, some potential challenges still exist, which urgently need to be systematically summarized to guide the future development of this field. Herein, in this review, initially, we discuss the five typical superhydrophobic models, namely, the Wenzel, Cassie, Wenzel–Cassie, “lotus”, and “gecko” models. Then, the existence of superhydrophobic creatures in nature and artificial EFSMs are summarized. Then, we focus on the applications of EFSMs in the fields of environment (self-cleaning, wastewater purification, and membrane distillation), energy (solar evaporation, heat accumulation, and batteries), and biology (biosensors, biomedicine, antibacterial, and food packaging). Finally, the challenges and developments of eco-friendly superhydrophobic materials are highlighted.

1. Introduction

Superhydrophobic organisms,1–3 such as lotus leaves,4–6 water strider,7–9 rose petals,10,11 butterfly wings,12,13 rice leaves,14 and taro leaves,15 among others,16–19 exist in nature. Benefitting from their strong water-repellency, these natural creatures survive perfectly in the surrounding environment. Thus, to illustrate the basic principles of superhydrophobicity, scientists conducted a series of investigations in the last three decades. In 1997, Barthlott's group explained that the self-cleaning superhydrophobicity on lotus leaves is attributed to their hierarchical structures and epicuticular wax.20 Jiang et al.7 demonstrated the non-wetting of the strider leg based on an air layer occupied on needle-like microstructures in 2004, thus contributing to their free sliding on the surface of water. In 2008, their group also found that rose petals are high-adhesion superhydrophobic, and thus water drops adhere to their surface, even upside down.21 In general, these results indicate that superhydrophobicity is dependent on the microstructure of the surface and chemical composition of the material, that is, surface energy.22–25 High roughness and low surface energy are more conducive to obtaining superhydrophobic surfaces, which also guide the design, preparation and applications of artificial superhydrophobic materials.26–34

To obtain a lower surface energy, fluorides,35–37 sulfhydryl compounds,38–40 long carbon chain substances,41–43 and other reagents are commonly used to construct or modify surfaces to achieve superhydrophobicity. However, these chemical modifiers are toxic and harmful to human beings and the environment, which is not conducive to health, safety, and sustainability requirements of current practice development and greatly restricts the popularization of superhydrophobic materials.44–47 Therefore, researchers are focused on designing and preparing eco-friendly superhydrophobic materials (EFSMs). Through continuous efforts in recent years, a series of breakthroughs has been made in this field. EFSMs have begun to play a crucial role in various application fields and are expected to be more widely used in the fields of environment, energy, biology, etc.48–51 By searching the topic of “superhydrophobic materials” and “EFSMs” in the ISI (Institute of Scientific Information) Web of Science, we observed that the number of papers and citation frequency of these two types of materials both show an increasing trend annually (Fig. 1a and d). Moreover, as can be seen from the data in the statistical chart, EFSMs, as an emerging field, have received increasing attention. However, this research field has entered a critical period after early development, where there are challenges and defects together with the great development trend. Thus, a timely summary and review of this progress and further clarification of the future development of this field are significant.

In this review, we summarize the recent developments regarding environmental, energy and biological applications based on EFSMs. Firstly, we discuss the typical wetting models including the Young model, Wenzel model, Cassie model, Wenzel–Cassie model, “lotus” model, and “gecko” model, and the relation among wetting, surface tension and surface energy. Then, superhydrophobic natural creatures, i.e., lotus leaves, rose petals, rape flowers, Osmanthus flowers, sunflower grains, and taro leave were summarized, followed by an introduction of artificial EFSMs, including carbon-based, silicon-based, and polymer-based raw materials, and other materials. Subsequently, we detail the progress of EFSMs in recent years, which are categorized as environmental (self-cleaning, wastewater purification and membrane distillation), energy (solar evaporation, heat accumulation, and batteries), biological (biosensor, biomedicine, antibacterial, and food packaging) applications. Finally, the challenges and prospects of EFSMs are presented.

2. Wetting models

2.1 Definition of wettability

Wettability can be used to characterize the ability of a liquid to spread out on a solid surface (CA). At the gas–liquid–solid three-phase contact line, the angle formed between the gas–liquid interface and tangent line of the solid–liquid interface is called the CA (θ). Taking water as the research object, surfaces can be defined as superhydrophilic (θ < 10°), hydrophilic (10° < CA < 90°), hydrophobic (90° < CA < 150°), and superhydrophobic (>150°).52–56

2.2 Surface free energy

Surface free energy (SFE) is the embodiment of the intermolecular force on the surface of an object and is closely related to the wettability of a surface. The SFE is calculated using the CA of the material, the surface tension of liquid and the interfacial tension between the solid and liquid. Surface tension is a manifestation of molecular forces, which occurs at the boundary of liquid and gas contact.57 Surface energy and surface tension per unit area are numerically the same, but have different physical meanings. Specifically, one is energy, whereas the other is force.58 As shown in Fig. 2a, if 0 J m−2 < SFE < 28 J m−2, the surface displays (super) hydrophobicity and is (super) oleophobic; 28 J m−2 < SFE < 72 J m−2, the surface displays (super) hydrophobicity and is (super) oleophilic; and SFE >72 J m−2, the surface displays (super) hydrophilicity and is (super) oleophilic.59

2.3 Wetting models

2.3.1 Young's equation. Young's equation represents the wettability of droplet on a perfectly smooth solid surface (Fig. 2b),60 as follows:
 
cosθ = (γSV − γSL)/γLV(1)
where γSV is the surface tension between the solid–vapor interface, γSL is the surface tension between the solid–liquid interface, γLV is the surface tension between the liquid–vapor interface, and θ is the CA of the droplet on a smooth solid surface. Young's equation expressed the relation between CA and surface energy can for the first time. However, it only applies to solid surfaces in an ideal state.
2.3.2 Wenzel model. It is impossible to have a completely smooth surface. Thus, considering the influence of roughness, Wenzel modified Young's equation, and the CA of a rough surface can be expressed, as follows:61
 
cosθ* = rcosθ(2)
where θ* is the apparent contact angle of the droplet on the rough surface under the Wenzel state and r is the roughness factor, which is the ratio between actual contact area of solid and liquid and area under its geometric projection, and in general, r > 1.

As shown in Fig. 2c, when water droplets are placed on a rough surface, they fill the gaps in the rough surface.62 The Wenzel model indicates that the roughness of a solid surface can enhance the wettability of a solid surface. When θ < 90°, the solid surface will have stronger hydrophilicity with an increase in roughness, whereas when θ > 90°, the solid surface will have stronger hydrophobicity with an increase in roughness. It is worth mentioning that the Wenzel equation only applies to stable thermodynamic equilibrium states.

2.3.3 Cassie model. Cassie further refined the wetting equation and believed that droplets have no contact with the grooves on a solid surface but form a gas–liquid–solid composite contact surface on an uneven, rough surface. The Cassie model is as follows:63
 
cosθ* = fcosθ1 + fLVcosθ2(3)
where θ* is the intrinsic contact angle of the surface and θ1 and θ2 are the apparent CA of a droplet on a rough surface, respectively. fSL represents the percentage of contact surface between the droplets and solid in total area and fLV represents the percentage of contact surface between the droplets and air in total area.

The Cassie model also applies to porous surfaces because droplets do not fill the gaps between rough surfaces and air fills the gaps and below the droplets (Fig. 2d).

2.3.4 Wenzel–Cassie model. The Wenzel–Cassie model is an intermediate state between the Wenzel model and Cassie model.64 As shown in Fig. 2e, the droplets moisten part of the groove, trapping air at the bottom. Droplets can be transferred from the Wenzel state to the Cassie state by external stimuli such as pressure and vibration.65–67
2.3.5 “Lotus” model. The lotus model is a special form of the Cassie model with a composite binary structure on the micro- and nanometer scale.68 The surface of the lotus leaf has a fine microscopic rough structure and epidermal wax structure. These structures reduce the contact area between the solid surface and liquid, and thus the liquid is in a semi-suspended state (Fig. 2f), enabling pollutants to be carried away by the falling water droplets, and ultimately play the role of self-cleaning.
2.3.6 “Gecko” model. The surface in the “gecko” state is highly repellent and adherent to water (Fig. 2g). At the contact point between a water droplet and the surface, air is directly connected with the atmosphere and enclosed air trapped in nanotubes, respectively. Due to the change in the volume of air in the nanotubes, the negative pressure in the nanotubes increases, thus generating a high CA and making then have high adhesion to water.69

3.1 Lotus leaf

Lotus leaves are typically superhydrophobic surfaces.70–72 In 1997, Barthlott and Neinhuis demonstrated that the self-cleaning effect of lotus leaves is ascribed to the combination of micropapillae and low surface energy waxes.20 Subsequently, Jiang et al.73 found dendritic nanostructures on the surface of lotus leaves (Fig. 3a). Their dual-scale roughness provides superhydrophobicity, reducing the contact among droplets with the material surface, thus keeping themselves clean by rolling contaminants away with the droplets.

3.2 Rose petal

Unlike lotus leaves, superhydrophobic rose petals exhibit high adhesion to water droplets.74 The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image in Fig. 3b shows neat micropapillae on the surface of rose petals, with nanoscale fold structures at the top. These nanostructures trap air in their folds, effectively preventing water from the wetting surface and making the surface superhydrophobic and highly adherent to water.21

3.3 Rape flower

Fresh rape flowers exhibit similar properties to lotus leaves. The surface of their petals is distributed with “intestine-like” microstructures with a and of about 0.3 μm and their cross-linked microstructures ensure roughness. Their petals contain carotenoids, which are water-insoluble pigments. Thus, the synergistic effect between their microstructure and surface composition ensures that rape flowers have superhydrophobicity with a water contact angle (WCA) of 155° (Fig. 3c).75

3.4 Osmanthus flower

Osmanthus flowers also exhibit interesting wetting behavior in nature, which are composed of long carbon chains. Uniform micro fold structures were observed on the surface of Osmanthus flowers (Fig. 3d). The synergistic effect of low surface energy substances and micro structure make their surface hydrophobic, with a WCA of 128.6 ± 3.5°.76

3.5 Sunflower pollen grains

Sunflower pollen grains have microcapsule structures and spiny structures. Researchers used freeze-drying and thermal annealing to produce pollen sponges with three-dimensional porous structures, and then transformed them into hydrophobic pollen sponges by adding stearic acid (SA) (Fig. 3e). With a change in the SA concentration, the maximum contact angle of sponge pollen can reach 145°.77,78

3.6 Taro leaf

Taro leaves exhibit superior water repellency, with water droplets remaining spherical on their surfaces. SEM was used to study the surface morphology of taro leaves. As observed in Fig. 3f, a series of micropapillary structures is distributed on the surface of taro leaves and there are vertical nanosheets on the micropapillae, which further improve their surface roughness. Their superhydrophobicity is attributed to the micropapillae and nanosheet structures.15

3.7 Water strider

Water striders can swim freely on water without getting wet or sinking. Jiang et al.7 found that water strider legs are superhydrophobic, with their surface covered with tiny hairs in a nano-fluted structure. This unique layered micro- and nanostructures ensure roughness, making them waterproof (Fig. 3g).

3.8 Butterfly wing

When butterflies fly in wet conditions, their wings remain clean. Butterfly wings are covered by layered microscale scales, which form a superhydrophobic and self-cleaning surface with a WCA of 152° ± 2°.79 In addition, the superhydrophobic butterfly has directional bonding properties, as observed in the nano-stripe of its laminated sheet, in which droplets easily roll off radially outward but are firmly anchored in the opposite direction (Fig. 3h).80,81

4 Artificial EFSMs beyond nature

4.1 Materials for the preparation of EFSMs

Eco-friendly materials refer to materials that are natural and non-toxic themselves, or after artificial processing and synthesis, their toxicity is harmless to the human body and pollution-free to the environment within the scope of national regulations. Eco-friendly materials can be divided into two categories, as follows: (1) natural materials, such as wood, sand, and gypsum.82,83 (2) Artificial materials prepared by processing and synthesis technology, such as cotton fabric, metal pipes, plastic and metal composite pipes.84,85 The EFSMs described herein represent those in which the sources of their ingredients, derived substances, and preparation process are eco-friendly.

4.2 Substances for the preparation of EFSMs

The use of perfluoroalkyl substances or polyfluoroalkyl substances has shown great advantages in the preparation of superhydrophobic materials, which have a WCA of more than 150°, strong mechanical properties and chemical environmental tolerance.86,87 However, these substances cause widespread pollution in the environment and are a persistent form of pollution. In addition, long-term exposure to these substances can have many negative effects on human health. Thus, it is the best choice to replace perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances with eco-friendly substances. 4.2.1 Carbon-based materials. Carbon-based materials are often used to prepare superhydrophobic materials because of their low surface energy, outstanding chemical stability, low cost and environmental protection,88,89 such as candle soot,90,91 carbon nanotubes (CNT),92,93 multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs),94–96 graphene,97–99 and nanodiamond.100,101

The layered carbon nanostructure of candle soot provides roughness, which makes it appear superhydrophobic with a WCA greater than 160°. Its surface is easily destroyed because the carbon particles in candle soot are connected by the synergy of weak van der Waals forces and weak network structures. The mechanical stability can be enhanced by covering the surface with an SiO2 layer by vapor deposition (Fig. 4a).90,91 The addition of carbon nanomaterials (CNT and graphene) to porous materials can alter their microstructure and significantly improve their hydrophobicity. Liu et al.92 successfully produced a superhydrophobic strain sensor via simple laser-irradiation. The laser-irradiated polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS)/CNT composite formed an irregular raised structure, which provided superhydrophobicity (WCA is 155°). Shen et al.102 proposed the use of a superhydrophobic porous monolithic material for efficient oil adsorption. They used a green thermally impacted nonsolvent-induced phase separation method to prepare porous cellulose acetate/carboxylated MWCNT monolithic material. In addition, the two-dimensional structure and superior electrical conductivity of graphene make it an ideal material for the preparation of multifunctional surfaces. Han et al.97 produced superhydrophobic graphene films via laser scanning and demonstrated that solvents such as ethanol could change the slip angle of the graphene films, causing the water droplets on the surface to change from pinning to rolling. They can be used for surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) analysis of analytes and anti-icing. Similarly, nanodiamond is a candidate material for the preparation of superhydrophobic surfaces due to its outstanding hardness and electronic properties.

4.2.2 Silicon-based materials. Silicon-based materials play a critical role in the preparation of superhydrophobic materials,103–105 among which SiO2 has been proven to be superhydrophobic.106 Lei et al.107 modified colloidal silica with octyl chlorosilane, and then mixed it with resin to prepare a fluorine-free superhydrophobic material by the thermal curing method, which successfully replaced the superhydrophobic materials based on fluorine-modified colloidal silica. Pedraza et al.100 selected metals such as germanium, copper, and steel as substrates, and after coating with methyltrimethoxysilane (MTES), these metal substrates exhibited outstanding self-cleaning properties, acid resistance, and corrosion resistance. PDMS, as an eco-friendly fluorine-free polymer, is often used in the construction of superhydrophobic materials. Lai et al.109 prepared self-repairing cotton fabrics using a “PDMS-in-water” emulsion method, with a WCA of up to 160° (Fig. 4b). Miljkovic et al.110 cross-linked PDMS with dynamic borate esters to construct a durable self-healing ultra-thin and super-hydrophobic material. Chen et al.111 prepared superhydrophobic thin films on copper wafers via the hydrolysis of vinyl trimethoxysilane (VTES). Tetramethylsilane (TMS) was cleaved in radio-frequency glow discharge plasma to form a superhydrophobic surface via a simple coating method.112 Xiong et al.113 prepared transparent superhydrophobic films using trimethylethoxysilane (TMES) as the precursor system, exhibiting different adhesion properties and superior transmittance. Si et al.114 designed a fluorine-free self-healing superhydrophobic gel nanocoating by using a simple trimethyl silicyl modification process. In particular, the coating could be applied to a variety of substrates by spraying.

4.2.3 Polymer-based materials

Some common polymers are among the ideal materials for the construction of superhydrophobic nanocoatings because of their low surface energy, flexibility, and low cost. Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) is a typical polymer, which is considered an ideal material for the preparation of superhydrophobic materials.115–117 Alawajji et al.118 chose glass as a substrate and prepared a highly transparent pure PTFE superhydrophobic film via a simple pulsed laser deposition method without any chemical modification, and the CA of PTFE film was 151.6° ± 1° (Fig. 4c). Qu et al.119 introduced silicon particles to prepare superhydrophobic materials to improve the stability and reusability of pure PTFE films.

Polycaprolactone (PCL) and poly(lactide) (PLA) have become new candidates for the preparation of superhydrophobic materials due to their non-toxic nature, strong mechanical properties and good biodegradability.120–124 Based on previous studies, Li et al.125 designed bionic superhydrophobic PCL composite membranes with hierarchical microspheres. The membranes were fabricated by electrostatic spinning and electrospray. SiO2 nanoparticles (NPs) mimicked hydrophobic biological wax on the surface of the lotus leaf and the PCL porous microspheres imitate the nanoprotuberance of the lotus leaf structure, which together gave the membrane superhydrophobicity with a WCA of 162.0° ± 1.5°. The membrane exhibited outstanding separation efficiency, reusability and corrosion resistance. Thus, this may provide a new way to design polymer materials for use as environmentally friendly membranes. Gore et al.126 developed Janus fabrics with superhydrophobicity/superlipophilicity and low ice adhesion through the simple electrospinning technique. The biodegradable PLA polymer-produced nanofibers were deposited on one side of cotton fabric, producing superhydrophobic properties where the loaded nano-clay provided spinnable viscosity. Due to their layered texture, the Janus fabrics demonstrated excellent oil–water separation efficiency and mechanical durability. Importantly, the authors claimed that the prepared Janus fabrics exhibit microbial and hydrolytic biodegradation.

4.2.4 Others. In addition to the common materials described above, paraffins, metal,127,128 metal oxides, metal salts129–132 and alkyl mercaptan can also be used to prepare superhydrophobic materials. Paraffin is a common material with low surface energy. There are numerous works in the literature on the preparation of superhydrophobic surface using paraffin. Zhao et al.133 synthesized a self-repairing superhydrophobic polyester textile. They placed raw textiles in a mixture of PDMS and paraffin wax (PWT) at 120 °C and obtained superhydrophobic textiles with a WCA of 156.7° (Fig. 4d). The textile displayed an excellent self-cleaning performance, chemical stability and mechanical stability. In the presence of strong acid, strong alkali and other harsh environments, the chemical structure and properties of the coating almost remained unchanged. Upadhyay et al.134 built micro/nano structures on the surface of copper using hydrogen sulfide as a green oxidant, and then a copper surface with a WCA of 150° was obtained by modification with SA. Yin et al.135 obtained superhydrophobic cellulose-based membranes (CBMs) through solution impregnation (Fig. 4e). Here, the synergistic effect of the CuO nanostructure coating and SA modification provided superhydrophobicity. This study indicated that the membrane has superior self-cleaning performance photocatalytic degradation ability.

4.3 Safe-by-design concept of EFSMs

Besides the precursor sources and derived materials, the safe design of eco-friendly superhydrophobic materials is also significant. Thus, to satisfy the sustainable development of functional materials, the development of simple, non-toxic and low-cost preparation methods is highly necessary. It is known that textured structures and low surface energy are crucial for surface superhydrophobicity. Now, instead of the construction and chemical modification of structures, coating technologies, such as spray coating,136,137 brushing coating,138 and dipping coating,139 are popular to achieve superhydrophobic materials based on diverse substrates. Meanwhile, the template method with the pre-design of desirable structures is also beneficial for the preparation of eco-friendly superhydrophobic materials.140 Solvents are used in most of the preparation methods to prepare functional materials. Thus, the eco-friendly nature of the solvent is also a key point that decides whether a material is an EFSM. Furthermore, solvent-free methods for the preparation of superhydrophobic coatings have been developed. Therefore, the concept of safe-by-design is also of high importance for the preparation of EFSMs.141,142

5. Applications of EFSMs in the fields of environment, energy and biology

The rapid development of FESMs has not only greatly reduced the harm of superhydrophobic materials to the human body and the environment, but also broken the restrictions on the application of superhydrophobic materials in many fields, especially in the three fields of environment, energy, and biology. A host of new and interesting applications, such as self-cleaning, oil–water separation, biological detection, biomedicine, antibacterial, food safety, solar evaporation, heat accumulation, and membrane distillation, has experienced rapid change within a short time. In this important section, we systematically summarize the representative and latest research on EFSMs in these three fields.

5.1 Environmental applications

In daily life, most surfaces are easily polluted by dirt, and thus self-cleaning superhydrophobic materials can fundamentally solve this problem. Because of their water repellency, superhydrophobic materials have broad application prospects in the fields of sewage purification and seawater desalination. The pursuit of more efficient and faster oil–water separation methods has always been the focus of researchers, where the introduction of superhydrophobic surfaces can overcome the challenges of improving the oil–water separation efficiency. However, the mechanical stability of superhydrophobic materials has always been a difficult problem to solve, which makes it impossible to employ them in practical applications on a large scale. Membrane distillation technology can convert seawater into fresh water to alleviate water shortages; however, performance degradation due to membrane scaling is a key challenge associated with this technology. Therefore, the preparation of membranes with high permeability and superhydrophobicity is an effective strategy for successful desalination.
5.1.1 Self-cleaning. Inspired by nature, researchers have endowed various substrates with superhydrophobicity, which reduces the adhesion of water droplets to their surface and can remove pollutants through rolling water droplets to achieve a self-cleaning performance. Lai et al.143 deposited PDMS NPs on a glass substrate by heat treatment, and then sprayed a mixed solution of titanium dioxide (P25) NPs and PDMS microparticles to obtain a superhydrophobic glass material (Fig. 5a). The prepared material had a good self-cleaning property and high transparency (Fig. 5b and c). Eshaghi et al.144 prepared a self-cleaning superhydrophobic glass material with a CA of 155° by depositing a mixture of PTFE and SiO2 NPS on a glass substrate using a simple impregnation method. Metal oxide can build a coarser surface structure and improve hydrophobicity. Saffar et al.145 deposited a ZnO solution on a copper sheet to obtain a superhydrophobic copper material with a WCA of 165°.

The degradation of organic pollutants on the surface of materials is another manifestation of self-cleaning phenomenon. At present, the introduction of photocatalytic NPs is an important means to realize superhydrophobic photocatalytic self-cleaning. Under the irradiation of light, organic matter is degraded into CO2, H2O, and other inorganic molecules. TiO2 is an ideal material for the preparation of multifunctional superhydrophobic materials due to its excellent photocatalytic properties.146,147 Our group used P25 NPs and PDMS to prepare superhydrophobic copper mesh (COM) with a WCA of 155.5° (Fig. 5d).148 Due to the presence of TiO2 NPs, a Nile red solution was degraded under UV irradiation (Fig. 5e), while the superhydrophobic COM maintained good superhydrophobicity even after 30 h of UV irradiation (Fig. 5f). Thus, the superhydrophobic COM realized dual self-cleaning of removing dirt particles and degrading organic pollutants (Fig. 5g). Similarly, our team constructed a superhydrophobic–superhydrophilic pattered bionic surface, which could reduce the concentration of pesticides in sewage, where candle soot and PDMS were mixed to form a superhydrophobic material, and P25 NPs were used to construct a superhydrophilic region. To improve the visible light absorption capacity of TiO2, Liu et al.149 prepared a WO3–TiO2 nanorod/PDMS superhydrophobic material, which had self-cleaning property and could degrade methyl red alcohol solution and oleic acid solution under visible light and ultraviolet light irradiation. It is worth mentioning that the material could also be used for the degradation of NO and be sprayed on the exterior walls of buildings and other places. Simultaneously, superhydrophobic materials based on polypropylene and graphite carbon nitride also displayed superior photocatalytic self-cleaning performance.150

5.1.2 Wastewater purification. With the occurrence of oil leakage and disordered discharge of industrial oily wastewater, the problem of water pollution becomes increasingly serious. Accordingly, superhydrophobic surfaces are considered a favorable choice for wastewater purification because of the significant difference in surface tension between oil and water. Oil can easily penetrate or be absorbed from superhydrophobic-superoleophilic materials and mesh surfaces, leaving water on the other side. Starting from nature, researchers have made promising progress in oil–water separation.

The lotus leaf floats on the water because its upper and lower surfaces exhibit opposite superwettability. Jiang's team151 made use of the difference in wettability of lotus leaves to prepare perforated lotus leaves to demonstrate the oil–water separation process (Fig. 6a). Water droplets could only one-way pass through the holes from the superhydrophobic surface to superhydrophobic surface. On the contrary, oil could pass through the porous lotus leaves from the superhydrophobic surface to superhydrophobic surface. Thus, the separation of light oil or heavy oil from water could be realized by flipping the lotus leaves (Fig. 6b). More than 99.9% separation efficiency could be achieved at different holes spacings of the lotus leaf. Furthermore, increasing the hole spacing could reduce the water flux (Fig. 6c). The perforated leaf exhibited outstanding durability and its separation efficiency was still higher than 99.9% after 25 cycles of separation experiment (Fig. 6d). In all types of bionic superhydrophobic materials, filter membranes and mesh superhydrophobic membranes are dominant. Zhang et al.152 reported a simple and environmentally friendly superhydrophobic filter paper with a separation efficiency of over 98%. Lim et al.153 prepared a superhydrophobic aluminum mesh material, which exhibited perfect separation efficiency for both oil and organic solvents. Li et al.154 sprayed a solution containing aluminum phosphate and TiO2 NPs on stainless steel mesh, and then modified it with 1H,1H,2H,2H-perfluoro-decyltriethoxysilane to form a superhydrophobic film, which could effectively remove water in lubricating oil to restore its lubrication ability. The superhydrophobic copper mesh (COM) prepared by our team could achieve more complex oil–water separation.148 When there were different components of oil in the oil–water mixture, the superhydrophobic COM could initially achieve the separation of heavy oil and water. After treating COM with oxygen plasma, the separation of light oil and water could be achieved, with a separation efficiency higher than 99.4%. Three-dimensional (3D) porous oil absorbent materials such as sponge and foam exhibit higher adsorption capacity because they have a greater exposed adsorption area. Wang et al.155 developed a superhydrophobic 3D PU sponge using a simple impregnation method. Guo et al.156 prepared superhydrophobic PU foam, which could efficiently separate oil and water mixtures by the uniform dispersion of carbon nanofibers on the PDMS foam skeleton through ultrasonic treatment.

5.1.3. Membrane distillation. Membrane distillation (MD), as new technology, has been widely studied to deal with seawater desalination and saline wastewater because of its low cost and easy automation. Commonly, (super) hydrophobic membranes with microporous structures are used, where wastewater is repelled, and the water vapor generated by a temperature difference penetrates through the membrane. Non-wetting interfaces play decisive roles.159,160 At present, hydrophobic PTFE,161 polypropylene (PP)162 and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF)163–165 have been extensively studied to produce MD membranes.166 Liao et al.167 reported the preparation of super-hydrophobic PTFE films with high porosity via electrostatic spinning. In the MD process, water vapor can easily pass through a highly porous superhydrophobic membrane and condense on the permeable side (Fig. 8a). The incorporation of vinyl-grafted polyhedron polysiloxane (Vinyl-POSS) improved the roughness of the PTFE nanofiber and increased the WCA of the film (Fig. 8b). The salt retention rates of both PTFE and PTFE/POSS nanofiber membranes developed were above 99.99%. Among them, the #POSS-2 membrane had the best performance, with a high permeability of 40 ± 2 LMH (Fig. 8c). The properties of the #POSS-2 membrane remained unchanged during the continuous distillation process of up to 200 h, providing long-term durability (Fig. 8d). Loading CNTs on PTFE and PP hydrophobic membranes can improve the desalination rate and water flux of the membranes, which provides a new strategy for the preparation of high-performance MD films.168,169 Compared with the two above-mentioned membranes, PVDF is superior for the preparation of superhydrophobic materials due to its unique chemical properties and easy modification. Azeem et al.170 reported a method for designing superhydrophobic PVDF membranes by casting sandpaper textures and performing phase conversion on PDMS templates. The superhydrophobic PVDF membrane had superior scale-prevention and desalination properties. Thus, PVDF membranes with special textures provide a promising alternative for the treatment of high saline water.

5.2 Energy applications

Fresh water resources and world energy resources are still in shortage. Thus, obtaining fresh water resources and developing and utilizing clean new energy becomes an urgent demand. As a type of renewable clean energy, solar energy can be directly converted into heat energy through photothermal conversion materials and applied in various fields. At present, interface evaporation solar desalination is an effective and green new method to solve the shortage of fresh water. In practical applications, due to the inevitable pollution and salt accumulation in the process of operation, the evaporation performance of the membrane is reduced, whereas superhydrophobic-modified membranes can maintain a stable high evaporation rate. Similarly, as emerging applications, heat storage coatings, supercapacitors and batteries based on superhydrophobic materials also display superior energy storage capacity and durability.
5.2.1. Solar evaporation. In recent years, solar-powered interfacial water evaporation has shown great potential in seawater desalination. In practice, due to the unavoidable pollution and salt accumulation, the evaporation efficiency cannot be maintained for a long time. Superhydrophobic materials are reliable for efficient and sustainable seawater desalination and exhibit great potential for sustainable water purification.

Zhang et al.171 sprayed multi-walled carbon nanotubes on the surface of superhydrophobic organosilicate sponge, and then modified the surface with oxygen plasma to be superhydrophilic, producing a yolk@shell superhydrophobic/superhydrophilic solar evaporator (Fig. 9a), where MWCNTs were uniformly covered on the top surface of the evaporator (Fig. 9b). In a seawater desalination experiment with 200 h of sunlight exposure, no salting-out was found on the top surface of the evaporator (Fig. 9c), and the generated steam condensed into water drops in the device (Fig. 9d). The evaporation rate increased rapidly to 1.72 kg m−2 h−1 within 5 h and remained almost constant (Fig. 9e). Furthermore, it exerted outstanding evaporation performance in a hostile environment. Zhang et al.172 reported a novel strategy to promote the rapid atmospheric deposition of dopamine (PDA) and Ag NPs on fabrics under UV irradiation and was modified by dodecylmercaptan (DT) to obtain superhydrophobicity. PDA and Ag NPs exhibit outstanding photothermal properties, which enabled the fabric to collect solar energy to promote the local evaporation of seawater. The evaporation efficiency was 1.66 kg m−2 h−1 and the solar energy conversion efficiency was up to 91%. To achieve high energy conversion efficiency and stable desalination rate, Chen et al.173 produced a superhydrophobic fabric with air permeability and photothermal conversion properties through a completely wet process, which remained stable after 12 h long-term evaporation.

5.2.2 Heat accumulation. Thermal energy storage as a renewable source of energy storage and reduced energy consumption technology has received widespread attention. Therefore, phase-change materials (PCMs) with a stable shape have been favored by researchers due to their excellent heat storage ability. Among the phase-change materials, the development of organic solid–liquid PCMS is the focus of attention. However, this material is prone to fluid leakage, where the introduction of a porous skeleton and the construction of PCMS with a stable shape can effectively solve this problem. In addition, the introduction of a superhydrophobic material can further prevent fluid leakage and improve its heat storage capacity.174,175

Guo et al.176 successfully obtained a stable shape superhydrophobic composite organic phase-change material via the impregnation of paraffin (PA) in a polymethylsilsesquioxane aerogel. Zhang et al.177 developed a superhydrophobic thermal storage material based on polydivinylbenzene nanotubes to improve their mechanical durability. Yang et al.178 impregnated 1-tetradecanol (TD) into DW to obtain a TD/DW composite phase change material, and then sprayed a superhydrophobic material on the surface of the material to prepare a superhydrophobic TD/DW composite phase-change material with a WCA of 155° (Fig. 10a and b). In addition, the PCM had strong thermal stability (less than 110 °C) and chemical stability (Fig. 10c), and thus it has promising application in the utilization of solar energy resources.

5.2.4 Battery. Long-term exposure to air will accelerate the passivation of batteries, resulting in reduced performance and safety concerns.183 Thus, the design of superhydrophobic materials have proven to be an effective way to protect batteries. The protective layer of hierarchical porous carbon designed by Wang et al.184 effectively improve the usability and cycling stability of a battery. Zhang et al.185 generated silicon nanofilaments (SNFs) on the surface of a polypropylene separator (Celgard 2400) through the hydrolysis condensation reaction of trichloromethyl silane and prepared an SNF-Celgard separator to improve the performance of the battery (Fig. 12a). When activated by O2-plasma, the surface produced a large amount of hydroxyl groups, and the separator exhibited excellent superhydrophobicity (Fig. 12b and c), reducing the water absorption and achieving a hygroscopic capacity of 0% at 92% humidity. As can be seen in Fig. 12d, the battery with SNF-Celgard separator was more stable than that with a pure Celgard separator, where the battery capacity remained basically unchanged. At 200 °C, the thermal shrinkage of SNF-Celgard120ppm and SNF-Celgard220ppm was not obvious (Fig. 12f and g, respectively). Here, the SNF nanostructure provided superhydrophobicity and inhibited the reaction on the lithium anode, effectively alleviating the growth of dendrites. In addition, the SNF-Celgard separator is suitable for common lithium batteries.

5.3 Biological applications

Superhydrophobic materials have improved the problems of traditional technologies in biology because of their good repulsion to water, bacteria, blood, urine, cola and milk. It is difficult to detect ultra-low concentrations of biomolecules with early technology. Alternatively, detection based on superhydrophobic materials provides a new strategy for the ultra-sensitive detection of biomolecules, where the analyte on the superhydrophobic surface finally condenses into small points for detection. Air trapped on rough surfaces not only can reduce bacterial adhesion, but also disrupt their growth on the surface. The use of superhydrophobic materials instead of conventional packaging can reduce food waste to some extent. In recent years, the selective release of drugs has been one of the concerns of researchers, where as a new drug carrier, superhydrophobic materials have the advantages of high drug release rate and good controllability.
5.3.1 Biological detection. SERS technology can enhance the intensity of Raman signals and can better analyze chemical and structural information of molecules.186 In recent years, SERS technology has been widely used for the high sensitivity detection of a low concentration analytes. Considering the limitations of traditional bio-photonic devices for biological detection, Gentile et al.187 combined SERS and superhydrophobicity based on silicon microcolumns and obtained a new device, which could detect trace amounts of analyte. The analyte droplet was highly concentrated at a small point on the superhydrophobic surface after the solvent evaporated. In this work, the device could selectively detect very low concentrations of rhodamine (R6G) molecules. To obtain a more convenient and low-cost process, Xu et al.188 proposed the use of natural hydrophobic materials as SERS substrates, such as rose petals. Inspired by this, Chen et al.189 used rose petals as an SERS substrate and enriched Ag NPs on the upper epidermis (UE) and lower epidermis (LE) (Fig. 13a and b), and then condensed to a small point by using their superhydrophobicity (Fig. 13c), thus preparing an efficient and highly sensitive SERS substrate. However, due to the inhomogeneity of the nanostructure on the surface of the rose petals, the substrate could not achieve the reusability of SERS signals. Thus, it is necessary to find a type of natural hydrophobic material with a uniform nanostructure for the fabrication of SERS substrates. Taro leaves have a layered micro-nano structure, which exhibit superhydrophobicity similar to lotus leaves.

5.3.2 Biomedicine. Superhydrophobic surfaces are commonly used in clinical diagnosis and biomedical drug delivery and can also be used for the quantitative detection of biological proteins.192,193 The superhydrophobic liquid–solid contact triboelectric nanogenerators (TENG) developed by Zhao's team194 could be used as a droplet sensor for clinical diagnosis. Its structure diagram is shown in Fig. 14a, where as a superhydrophobic layer, gas-phase SiO2 NPs uniformly covered the double-sided adhesive, forming a superhydrophobic TENG surface with a copper electrode. In addition, a TENG surface had a strong sense of blood and could be applied in the drainage bottle, which reflected the flow rate of fluid in real time to finish the operation, where the wound will not cause secondary injury, affecting the wound healing speed (Fig. 14d). In terms of drug delivery, Mahmoodi et al.195 used PTFE NPs to modify the surface of PDMS microchips, which made the original microchannel surface superhydrophobic and improved the synthesis efficiency of dexamethasone-coated poly(lactic-glycolic acid) nanoparticles, thus achieving a greater drug load. The material also had good resistance to organic solvents, which could effectively prevent the adhesion of solvents on a channel. Superhydrophobic materials can also address post-operative thrombosis and hemolysis by reducing the flow resistance to reduce blood damage in medical devices. Marlena et al.196 prepared superhydrophobic materials with durable drag reduction via the simple modification of porous PTFE foam. It exhibited high blood compatibility and protein adsorption and could be used in medical devices in contact with blood.

5.3.4 Food packaging. In daily life, it can be observed that milk, honey and other sticky liquid foods leave a residue on the packaging box wall after drinking, resulting in a large amount of food waste. This problem can be solved by introducing superhydrophobic surfaces. To obtain a superhydrophobic material that can be used for food packaging, the material under preparation must be safe and edible. Beeswax has been proven to be a naturally hydrophobic material. In their work, Zhang et al.200 combined beeswax with coffee lignin to produce a heat-resistant and edible superhydrophobic material (WCA > 150°) by spraying (Fig. 16a). Various liquid foods appeared spherical on the surface and could roll freely (Fig. 16b). A contrast method was used to detect the low adhesion of the material to honey. As shown in Fig. 16c, a large amount of honey remained in the blank cup, while there was almost no honey in the cup covered with the superhydrophobic material. The preparation of this material is simple and inexpensive, and thus it has a great development prospect in the packaging of liquid food with adhesion. Guo et al.201 demonstrated that the cuticle of the skin of tomatoes protects their pulp, and they used beeswax to modify a cuticle-pectin mixture to produce an edible superhydrophobic film. Subsequently, Wang et al.202 developed a double-layer cellulose-based superhydrophobic material, where the heat resistance and mechanical stability of the material were improved remarkably by introducing a precipitated calcium carbonate layer. In addition to beeswax, carnauba wax, candelilla wax, rice bran wax and soy wax are also widely used to prepare superhydrophobic materials. Gupta et al.203 developed non-fluorinated superhydrophobic composites using naturally hydrophobic carnauba wax. Xue et al.204 reported for the first time an edible superhydrophobic material prepared on polypropylene (pp) material via a one-step spraying method using candelilla wax and rice bran wax as the main materials. The sprayed pp surface exhibited superhydrophobicity to various viscous liquids, and it was found that the material could resist sandpaper friction and immersion in hot water at 70 °C. The authors claimed that this material can be widely used on the inner surface of food packaging to effectively reduce the amount of liquid food residue.

6. Conclusion

Herein, we summarized the recent development and application of EFSMs. Initially, the basic wetting theory and typical superwetting models were introduced, such as the Wenzel model, Cassie model, Wenzel–Cassie model, Lotus model and Gecko model. Secondly, the superhydrophobic behavior of lotus leaves, rose petals, rape flowers, Osmanthus flowers, sunflower pollen, taro leaves and other natural organisms were described. Subsequently, biomimetic superhydrophobic materials based on carbon-based and silicon-based substances, PTFE and paraffin were introduced. Finally, the recent progress in the application of superhydrophobic materials in the environment, energy and biology was summarized. Although the research on EFSMs has attracted wide attention, there are still some challenges in the related development research. In our view, the following four points are worth noting (Fig. 17).

1. Under the premise of ensuring superhydrophobicity, how to correctly select cheap and green materials is the key step to prepare superhydrophobic materials. Most of the superhydrophobic materials currently available use perfluorinated compounds or organosilane-based chemicals to reduce the surface energy of liquids, both of which are typically toxic, expensive, and bio-cumulative in nature. Therefore, removing the content of alkyl fluoride compounds in materials without sacrificing their surface superhydrophobic properties is the most important thing to achieve green chemistry. The mentioned compounds such as silica, silicates, carbon and wax can be widely used as fillers in the preparation of superhydrophobic materials. In addition, hydrogels, 3-D-printed biological materials and polymers such as PTFE, PVDF, PVDF, PCL and PLA have also been used in the successful preparation of superhydrophobic materials.

2. How to keep superhydrophobic stability is of great significance. The stability of superhydrophobic materials needs to be further strengthened, and excellent stability can enable the wide use of superhydrophobic materials. At present, the stability of most non-toxic superhydrophobic materials cannot be guaranteed. For example, wax-based materials tend to lose their wettability under friction behavior. Therefore, for their practical production and wide application, the stability of superhydrophobic materials needs to be higher. Here, the stability should include mechanical stability, temperature resistance and chemical stability. Therefore, in the construction of superhydrophobic materials, the stability of materials can be improved by introducing a bonding layer, mixing high adhesion substances and constructing an organic skeleton.

3. Developing ways to ensure that the design, preparation, and application of superhydrophobic materials are environmentally friendly is worth exploring. At present, environmental protection materials can be used completely in the preparation process, and the focus should be on the development of new applications of environmental protection superhydrophobic materials. Using environmentally friendly principles through environmentally friendly preparation technology for application in the field of environmental protection is the best choice to realize environment-friendly superhydrophobic material.

4. Realizing the industrial large-scale production of EFSMs. At present, the preparation technology of small-scale environment friendly superhydrophobic materials in the laboratory is basically mature.219,220 Inspired by these studies, through chemical stability and robust roughness, a scientific team from the Chinese Academy of Sciences utilized a series of super-hydrophobic materials to develop a new intelligent oil-spill emergency device, where crude oil, heavy oil, light oil, diesel oil, gasoline, organic chemical liquid and underwater organic chemical liquids could be purified with high adsorption or recovery efficiencies. Meanwhile, coating companies mainly focus on a series of products including high-temperature-resistant coatings, high hardness ceramic coatings, special antifouling coatings, and special superhydrophobic self-cleaning coatings. These products are now widely used in various electrical appliances, lighting, medical equipment, metal processing industry and building exterior walls and other fields. However, in regard to the widespread practical applications of EFSMs in various fields, it is still challenging. Firstly, the depth of cooperation between the scientific research community and industry is not deep enough, and many problems need to be solved by the cooperation of science and technology. Another reason is that environmental factors have a huge impact on product quality, which directly leads to the failure of applications. Furthermore, how to balance the relations between surface energy arising from chemical stability, roughness from textured structures and eco-friendly property is a challenging but urgent task for the real use of EFSMs in the future.

The most challenging aspect is creating a durable superhydrophobic material without compromising its wettability. We believe that this comprehensive review can provide new ideas for the development and application of superhydrophobic materials. The research on super hydrophobic environmental protection materials is developing and innovating, which will become a hot development direction in the next few years.
