Abstract

Î³-CsPbI3 solar cells have achieved promising efficiencies, yet the quantitative understanding of their defect properties is limited due to the severe computational challenges when using hybrid functionals. We have discovered an algorithm to improve the convergence speed through a combination of structural relaxation with a strongly constrained and appropriately normed (SCAN) Meta-generalized-gradient approximation (Meta-GGA) functional and further ionic and electronic calculations with the HeydâScuseriaâErnzerhof (HSE) hybrid functional. The static HSE calculations with SCAN results as inputs are qualitatively reliable in defect calculations, different from one-ionic step HSE calculations based on GGA inputs. Contradictory to previous GGA defect results, a suppressed bipolar conductivity by p-type VCs and VPb, and n-type CsI is found. Additionally, stable bipolar defects Iint and CsPb, with features of strong bond orbital coupling or structural deformation, detrimentally serve as carrier-traps. This strengthened bond orbital coupling in Î³-CsPbI3 causes more defect charge states than organic perovskites with larger lattice constants.

Introduction

Organic lead halide perovskites (LHPs) are regarded as promising candidates of renewable energy materials in applications of solar cells, light emitting diodes and sensors.1â5 They are furnished with advantages including moderate bandgaps, good defect tolerance, low cost of raw materials and facile synthesis.6â9 Unfortunately, state-of-the-art organic LHP fabrication lacks long-term stability.10 Thermal and operational decomposition of monovalent organic cations (i.e., methylammonium and formamidinium) causes detrimental fragment migration and crystal degradation, which hinder their commercialization.11,12

An inorganic element of cesium has been proposed to replace organic cations in order to enhance the colloidal stability of LHPs.13,14 Totally-inorganic LHPs are thermally and chemically stable with a prolonged device lifetime. For example, Î²-CsPbI3 solar cells reach a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 18.4% and only lose 5% efficiency after continuous operation.15 However, compared with hybrid LHPs, devices based on inorganic LHPs are still inferior in terms of efficiency,16 probably due to the different electronic profiles of intrinsic defects that play significant roles in device performance,17,18 yet are missing a direct physical understanding. Therefore, an improved investigation of intrinsic defects is a key to efficiency enhancement of inorganic LHPs. Still, reliable theoretical calculations are needed because defect formation at an atomistic level is difficult to characterize experimentally. Moreover, the density functional theory method has been proven to be one of the most efficient and accurate methods in the calculations of perovskite structures.19â23

So far, limited defect research in Î±-, Î³- and Î´-CsPbI3 based on first-principles calculations has been carried out using generalized-gradient approximation (GGA) functionals.24â27 It was found that energetically favorable defects are shallow while deep-level defects are less likely to form. However, the PerdewâBurkeâEnzerhof (PBE) GGA functional applied in these studies is not quantitatively accurate enough to predict defect levels due to a bandgap underestimation and an over-delocalized nature of charge density.28,29 Alternatively, the hybrid functional can be highly attractive because of an incorporation of exact HartreeâFock approximation in the exchange term of GGAs.30,31 Van de Walle's group applied the HeydâScuseriaâErnzerhof (HSE) screened Coulomb hybrid functional to calculate PbI and IPb defects in Î³-CsPbI3.32 Meanwhile, iodine-related and energetically favorable defects are investigated by the PBESOL0 and HSE hybrid functional in Î±-CsPbI3, respectively.33â35 However, the complete understanding of electronic and optical properties calls for a systematic study of native point defects by hybrid functionals, which are still lacking in Î³-CsPbI3, probably due to the challenging nature of hybrid calculations.

Supercell calculations by hybrid functionals are restricted by prohibitive computational costs and oversized matrix diagonalization that are about two orders of magnitude larger than GGAs.36 Early attempts based on static HSE calculations with structural relaxation by PBE (defined as PHSE) are usually inaccurate because of large structural deviations.37 Recently, the strongly constrained and appropriately normed (SCAN) meta-GGA functional has been constructed, with computational costs similar to GGAs and improved accuracy.38,39 Still, whether SCAN can help improve accuracy in defect calculations is unknown.

In this work, we carry out a point defect study of the energetically favorable Î³-CsPbI3 which is the most stable crystal phase among the âblackâ phases at low temperature.40,41 All the point defects are considered here, including vacancies (i.e., VCs, VPb and VI), interstitial defects (i.e., Csint, Pbint and Iint) and antisite defects (i.e., CsPb, PbCs, CsI, PbI, ICs and IPb), with the charge states labelled using a superscript. We propose to conduct structural relaxation by HSE with SCAN results as inputs that largely improve the convergence efficiency, while static HSE calculations with structural relaxation by SCAN (defined as âSHSEâ) can serve as a quick check that yields semi-quantitatively similar results to HSE, verified by both GaN and Î³-CsPbI3. Next, in our defect calculations using the HSE functional, we discover a bipolar conductivity profile, with both p-type and n-type conductivity possible under moderate chemical potential conditions,42 different from the previous unipolar results using the GGA functional.24 However, the conductivity is restricted by a high defect formation energy (DFE) and a multiple transition processes of p-type VCs and VPb, and n-type CsI. Moreover, as indicated by the results of Iint and CsPb, defects with both electron- and hole-captured processes,43 defined as bipolar defects, tend to couple strongly with the surrounding electronic environment through the bond orbital coupling mechanism44 and may introduce large reconstructions, respectively, serving as non-radiative recombination centers. This enhanced bond orbital coupling in Î³-CsPbI3 leads to more detrimental gap states than in their organic counterparts.

Methods

The first-principles calculations are based on density functional theory45,46 and the projector augmented wave approach,47 using Vienna ab initio simulation package code.48

For Î³-CsPbI3 unit cell calculations, we apply PBE, SCAN, SHSE and HSE functionals to calculate the lattice constants and bandgaps. The plane-wave cutoff energies in PBE and SCAN calculations are set as 400 eV with a Î-centered Brillouin zone meshing. Meanwhile, the plane-wave cutoff energies in SHSE and HSE calculations are set as 300 eV. A default mixing ratio of 0.25 in HSE yields a bandgap of 1.47 eV. To fit the experimental bandgap, which is a standard practice in a few other semiconductor systems,32,49 we tune the ratio to be 0.41. The same setting of mixing ratio is applied in the defect calculation of Î³-CsPbI3. In all the Î³-CsPbI3 calculations, we consider the spinâorbit coupling effect, which splits Pb p state degeneracy.50

For Î³-CsPbI3 defect calculations, a 2 Ã 2 Ã 1 supercell containing 80 atoms and a 1 Ã 1 Ã 2 k-point Brillouin zone meshing are used. Relaxation of the supercell is performed by SCAN functional with a 400 eV plane-wave cutoff energy. Subsequent relaxation and electronic calculations are done using the HSE functional with a 300 eV energy cutoff. The range separation parameter Î¼ is set as 0.2 and the mixing ratio is adjusted to 0.41 for the HSE functional. The accuracy in supercell energy is set to 10â5 eV per atom, and relaxation will consistently perform until the forces are less than 10â3 eV Ãâ1. The FreysoldtâNeugebauerâVan de Walle (FNV) finite-size correction is introduced to reach the single dilute limit.51,52 We strictly follow the correction algorithm listed in these two papers. At last, a rigid shift of 0.68 eV, which is a typical practice in defect calculations with GGA functionals,24 is added to the CBM of the SCAN band edge in order to match the HSE bandgap.

Defect transition levels calculated by SCAN are shown in Fig. 4b. Generally, the SCAN results show the stable bipolar nature of Iint and CsPb, with the deep (1+/1â) transition levels of Iint at 0.73 eV above the VBM, and the (1+/0) and (0/1â) transition levels of CsPb at 1.71 eV below the CBM and 0.75 eV above the VBM, respectively. These results are qualitatively consistent with the HSE conclusions. However, the positions of transition levels in several defects deviate from the HSE results. Firstly, the (1+/0) transition levels of VI, Pbint, Csint and PbCs are in the bandgap, but they are above the CBM in the HSE results. Secondly, the (3+/2+) transition levels of CsI, (2+/1+) transition levels of CsPb and (1+/0) transition levels of ICs are below the VBM, but they are in the bandgap in the HSE results. Thirdly, the (3+/1+), (1+/0) and (0/1â) transition levels of PbI are totally different from the HSE results of the (3+/2+) and (2+/1+) ones. Some of the differences are probably a result of the erroneous estimation of band edges by SCAN.39 Apparently, downward shifts of the band edges in both the CBM and VBM are observed in the SCAN results compared with the HSE ones. Therefore, a rigid shift of the CBM in the SCAN results, which offsets the bandgap underestimation,60 is insufficient to correct the erroneous coupling between the band edge and defect states. This problem can be particularly severe for semiconductors with small bandgaps,61,62 because calculations performed using semi-local functionals may yield very small or zero bandgaps. Moreover, we find different defect displacements of PbI, PbCs, Pbint and CsI in the SCAN results from the HSE ones, because the SCAN results misestimate the bond orbital coupling between the defect states and the electronic environment that leads to wrong bonds and neighbors. This error further leads to transition level discrepancy of PbI, PbCs and CsI, compared with the HSE results. See Note S4 (ESIâ ) for details. Both the band edge problem and structural discrepancy of the defects are probably due to the incorrect correlation term of the SCAN functional that is especially severe for states with local electronic redistribution surrounding the defects. Nevertheless, the SCAN results can still provide reasonable starting points for further HSE relaxations that lead to correct configurations.

Analysis of bipolar defects

Since both the SCAN and HSE results show the detrimental bipolar nature of Iint and CsPb, we conduct structural analyses to understand the physical origin of them. Iint is stable in 2+, 1+ and 1â charge states. Iint2+ and Iint1+ couple with two nearby I atoms and form a trimer with average distances of 2.87 and 2.88 Ã, respectively, which are significantly shorter than the unstable I0int of 3.33 Ã. The partial charge density contour in Fig. 5a of Iint2+ and Iint1+ reveals localized Ï-antibonding states with a p orbital nature that connect the trimer, indicating a relatively strong covalent bond. The formation of the trimer leads to stabilization of the mid-gap states, with the external pressure for Iint1+ supercell reduced to â0.46 kB, which is smaller than 2.07 kB of the I0int one. The reduced pressure is due to an electronic-environment-induced volume contraction from I0int to Iint1+ when the charge state changes.55 Meanwhile, Iint1â deforms the localized trimer and forms a delocalized defect-centered network, introducing extra bond orbital coupling among the inter-iodine pâp and iodineâlead sâp bonding or antibonding states, as shown in Fig. 5a. Therefore, the interesting (1+/1â) transition in Iint, that effectively suppresses the neutral state, can be understood as a combined effect by the stable local defect reconstruction of Iint1+ and extra coupling among the surrounding bond orbitals of Iint1â. By the way, according to our HSE calculations, the localized iodine trimers also exist in I0int, which can be similar to the results of Î²-CsPbI3.63 However, I0int is unstable as mentioned above. Therefore, the stability of the localized states should be related to the stability of different charge states.

We find that some defects in Î³-CsPbI3, i.e., Iint, ICs, Pbint and VPb, yield more defect charge states and more deep transition levels than the organic ones. Moreover, the organic results show more negative-U centers than inorganic ones. Still, VMA, VI, PbMA and MAint in MAPbI3 have similar transitions compared with Î³-CsPbI3. (1) Iint in MAPbI3 only shows a (1+/1â) transition level at 0.57 eV above the VBM,43 however, Iint in Î³-CsPbI3 shows a deeper (1+/1â) transition level at 0.83 eV above the VBM and an extra (2+/1+) transition at 1.43 eV below the CBM. (2) IMA in MAPbI3 only shows a (0/2â) transition level at 0.54 eV above the VBM,43 however, ICs in Î³-CsPbI3 exhibits a bipolar nature with a (1+/0) transition at 1.62 eV below the CBM and deeper (0/1â) and (1â/2â) transitions at 0.65 and 0.73 eV above the VBM. (3) Pbint in MAPbI3 is treated as a resonance peak,43 however, Pbint in Î³-CsPbI3 has an extra (2+/1+) transition at 0.80 eV below the CBM. (4) VPb in MAPbI3 only shows a (0/2â) transition with negative-U behavior at 0.49 eV above the VBM,58 however, VPb in Î³-CsPbI3 shows (0/1â) and (1â/2â) transitions at 0.23 and 0.51 eV above the VBM, respectively. The complex defect profiles and extra transition levels possibly originate from the strengthened bond orbital coupling due to relatively small lattice constants in Î³-CsPbI3.

Conclusion

In summary, the SCAN structures as inputs can improve the convergence speed of HSE, while the SHSE is qualitatively reliable in defect calculations. Nevertheless, SCAN still causes deviations from the HSE results. The intrinsic defects in Î³-CsPbI3 show a bipolar conductivity, restricted by deep levels and high DFE of the major carrier providers. Moreover, the enhanced bond orbital coupling and structural morphing result in the detrimental bipolar defects, which can cause detrimental defect complexity in Î³-CsPbI3 compared with the organic ones. Based on our calculations, several possible directions for further enhancement of device efficiency can be proposed. Stable extrinsic dopants of p-type and n-type can be essential to increase the carrier concentration. Meanwhile, to reduce the deep states, it could be important to passivate stable bipolar defects Iint and CsPb by intrinsic or extrinsic co-doping strategies.
