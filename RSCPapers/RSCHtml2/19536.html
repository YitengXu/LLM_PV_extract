Abstract

Carbon dioxide (CO2) reduction using solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) has attracted great attention because of the high efficiency and fast kinetics enabled by high operating temperatures. Electrode materials with high catalytic activity for CO2 reduction and good stability over long-term operation, nevertheless, are still to be developed. In this work, layered-perovskite oxide electrodes with in situ exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles are developed for efficient CO2 electrolysis to produce carbon monoxide (CO). Using double perovskite oxide Sr2Ti0.8Co0.2FeO6âˆ’Î´ as a solid precursor, a Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper phase oxide matrix with exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles uniformly distributed on the surface (Coâ€“Feâ€“STCF) is synthesized by thermal reduction. The cell with a mixture of Coâ€“Feâ€“STCF and Sm0.2Ce0.8O2âˆ’Î´ (SDC) as the fuel electrode exhibits outstanding performance for CO2 electrolysis, with a polarization resistance (Rp) as low as 0.22 Î© cm2 at 800 Â°C. A current density of 1.26 A cmâˆ’2 is acquired at a bias of 1.6 V at 800 Â°C, and the CO production rate reached 8.75 mL minâˆ’1 cmâˆ’2 with a high value of Faraday efficiency (âˆ¼100%). Moreover, the Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC electrode shows good stability for long-term (100 h) operation with no carbon deposition on the surface. Such high performance of the Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC electrode is attributed to abundant oxygen vacancies in the oxide matrix and the high catalytic activity of the exsolved metal nanoparticles. The result of this work can guide the design of highly active and stable (electro)catalysts for high-temperature energy and environmental devices.

Introduction

The use of fossil fuels has released large amounts of carbon dioxide (CO2) over the past few decades, which poses great threat to the environment. Great efforts have been devoted to developing an efficient approach to reduce CO2 emissions.1,2 Electrochemical conversion of CO2 into valuable products using electricity from renewable energy has attracted much attention because it not only provides an effective way for energy storage, but also reduces the environmental effects of CO2. Among various electrochemical devices, solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) which function at high temperature (600â€“1000 Â°C) exhibit great advantages such as outstanding conversion efficiency, high Faraday efficiency and fast kinetics.3â€“6 The performance of SOECs for CO2 electrolysis, nevertheless, is still strongly limited by the lack of high-performance fuel electrode materials. Ni-based composites are the most widely investigated fuel electrodes for SOECs.7,8 Despite its high catalytic activity, metal Ni gets easily oxidized to insulating NiO and faces serious carbon deposition issues when exposed to an atmosphere with a high concentration of CO2.9,10 Developing new electrode materials with high activity toward CO2 reduction and good stability over long-term operation at high temperature is of great significance for the practical application of SOECs for CO2 electrolysis.

Perovskite-based oxides, such as SrFeO3âˆ’Î´, doped-SrTiO3âˆ’Î´ and La0.75Sr0.25Cr0.5Mn0.5O3âˆ’Î´, have been widely studied as alternative fuel electrode materials for solid oxide fuel/electrolysis cells (SOFCs/SOECs) because of their good stability.11â€“16 Their catalytic activity, nevertheless, is not sufficient for practical applications. It was reported that active B-site cations, such as Ni, Co, and Fe, could exsolve from the perovskite-based oxide matrix to form metal nanoparticles on the surface upon thermal reduction or applying negative bias.17â€“26 Because the metal nanoparticles were exsolved from the inside of the oxide matrix to the surface, the metal particles were normally embedded in oxide matrices with robust heterointerfaces.25,26 As a result, the exsolved metal nanoparticles and oxide matrix normally exhibited strong interfacial interaction, which facilitated electron-ionic transport across the metalâ€“oxide interface and restricted the aggregation of metal nanoparticles.25â€“27 Exsolution materials were reported to exhibit comparable performance as Ni-based fuel electrodes for high-temperature CO2 electrolysis.18 However, auxiliary gases, such as CO and H2, were normally required to be added to the fuel electrode surface to prevent the electrode from being oxidized and improve the electrolysis performance.28â€“31 The addition of auxiliary gas diluted the reaction gas and made the research system more complex. Only very limited studies showed that pure CO2 can be directly reduced to CO by SOECs with appropriate exsolution materials as the fuel electrode.32â€“35 Nevertheless, the production rate of CO was still quite low, and the stability of the electrode materials required further improvement.

In this work, we demonstrated layered-perovskite oxides with in situ exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles as a highly efficient and stable fuel electrode for high-temperature electrolysis of pure CO2 to produce CO. Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (Râ€“P) phase oxides with exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles uniformly distributed on the surface (Coâ€“Feâ€“STCF) were prepared by reducing stoichiometric double perovskite oxide Sr2Ti0.8Co0.2FeO6âˆ’Î´ in a H2 atmosphere at high temperature. The cell with the obtained exsolution materials as the fuel electrode (Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC) exhibited outstanding activity toward CO2 reduction, with an electrolysis current density of 1.26 A cmâˆ’2, CO production rate of 8.75 mL minâˆ’1 cmâˆ’2 and a Faraday efficiency close to 100% at 1.6 V at 800 Â°C. The Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC electrode also showed good stability for operation at 800 Â°C under 1.2 V for 100 h in pure CO2 environment. Such high performance was attributed to oxygen vacancies in the oxide matrix and the exsolved metal nanoparticles. This work demonstrates exsolution materials as promising high-performance catalysts for high-temperature energy and environmental devices.

Experimental procedures

Materials preparation

Double perovskite oxide Sr2Ti0.8Co0.2FeO6âˆ’Î´ (STCF) powders were synthesized by solid-state reactions. SrCO3, TiO2, Co3O4, and Fe2O3 powders (Macklin, China) with a stoichiometric ratio and appropriate amount of ethanol were mixed and ball milled for 24 h. The obtained mixtures were dried in a muffle furnace and then calcined at 1200 Â°C for 8 h in air to form stoichiometric STCF powders. The as-prepared STCF powders were heated in a tubular furnace at different temperatures (800 Â°C and 900 Â°C) and times (1, 2, 4, and 8 h) in a H2 atmosphere. After hydrogen reduction, Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles with different population densities and diameters exsolved from the oxide matrix to the surface.

Oxygen electrode materials, La0.7Sr0.3CoO3âˆ’Î´ (LSC), were synthesized using a similar method to the one used for STCF, with La2O3, SrCO3, and Co3O4 powders (Macklin, China) as the precursors and ethanol as the solvent. The mixtures were calcined at 1000 Â°C for 6 h to form the stoichiometric LSC phase. Electrolyte materials, La0.8Sr0.2Ga0.83Mg0.17O3âˆ’Î´ (LSGM), were prepared by solid-state reactions with the detailed procedure described in the Experimental section of the ESI.â€ 

Electrolysis cell fabrication

CO2 electrolysis was carried out using an electrolyte-supported single cell with STCFâ€“SDC as the fuel electrode, LSGM as the electrolyte and LSCâ€“SDCâ€“PrOx as the oxygen electrode (STCFâ€“SDC/LSGM/LSCâ€“SDCâ€“PrOx). Sm0.2Ce0.8O2âˆ’Î´ (SDC) (Ningbo SOFCMAN Energy Technology Co.) was mixed with STCF to enhance the adhesion between the fuel electrode and the electrolyte (LSGM). When the pure STCF electrode material was prepared at high temperature, the electrode suffered from sintering effects, which resulted in the reduction of the specific surface area (Fig. S1â€ ). Adding high temperature resistant cerium-based oxide to the fuel electrode can also effectively prevent such sintering of the electrode structure. Moreover, the presence of cerium-based oxide with high oxygen ion conductivity in the medium temperature region36,37 can improve the ionic conductivity of the electrode. The fuel electrode slurry was prepared by mixing STCF, SDC and graphite. The oxygen electrode slurry composed of LSC and SDC was prepared using a similar approach to that of the fuel electrode, and a PrOx decoration layer was impregnated on the LSCâ€“SDC oxygen electrode by using Pr(NO3)3Â·6H2O as the precursor. A Ag grid was painted on both sides of the electrode surface to serve as current collectors. The effective area of the cell was 0.2 cmâˆ’2.

Electrochemical measurement

The obtained cell was tested in both SOFC and SOEC modes using an electrochemical workstation (IM6, Zahner, Germany). In SOFC mode, dry H2 was fed to the fuel electrode side with a flow rate of 50 mL minâˆ’1, while the oxygen electrode was directly exposed to air. In SOEC mode, the fuel electrode side was fed with pure CO2 at a flow rate of 50 mL minâˆ’1, and pure Ar was fed to the oxygen electrode side with a flow rate of 50 mL minâˆ’1. Before the electrochemical test, the STCFâ€“SDC fuel electrode was reduced in pure H2 gas at 900 Â°C for 1 hour to obtain Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles on the surface of the oxide matrix (denoted as Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC). The production of CO was analysed quantitatively using gas chromatography (GC9790II, Fuli, Zhejiang, China).

Materials characterization

The crystal structure of the materials was analysed by X-ray diffraction (XRD, Bruker D8 Advance, Germany) with Cu-KÎ± radiation. The topography was investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, SU8010, Japan). The microstructure and local composition were probed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) which was coupled with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis (FEI Tecnai G2 F20) operating at 200 kV. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (ESCALAB250Xi, Thermo Scientific) with an Al KÎ± X-ray source was used to analyse the surface composition and chemical environment of different elements. The binding energies of all the XPS spectra were calibrated by adventitious carbon (C 1s) at 284.6 eV. The XPS spectra were fitted using a Shirley-type background and peaks with 80% Gaussian and 20% Lorentzian functions.38,39 Raman spectra (LabRAM HR 800) were collected to characterize the possible carbon deposition on the fuel electrode after the stability test. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA SDT Q600) was performed from 50 to 800 Â°C in air for STCF before and after reduction. Temperature-programmed desorption of CO2 (CO2-TPD) was conducted to probe the CO2 adsorption characteristics on the sample surface. We also observed that exsolution occurred more easily at higher reduction temperature. Increasing the thermal reduction temperature is likely to facilitate cation migration from the bulk to the surface during exsolution.18,40,41 More importantly, the thermodynamic driving force for exsolution was also impacted by the reduction temperature. The crystal structure of the as-prepared and thermally reduced STCF samples was probed by XRD measurement.17,44 As shown in Fig. 1j, the as-prepared STCF and the one subjected to thermal reduction treatment at 800 Â°C for 1 h had a double perovskite structure and showed no other impurity, which was consistent with the absence of exsolved nanoparticles in the SEM images (Fig. 1a and S2â€ ). All the other STCF samples that were reduced at higher temperature or longer time exhibited characteristic XRD peaks of the Coâ€“Fe alloy. As shown in Fig. 1j, the two peaks marked with â€œ*â€ were assigned to the (110) and (200) peaks of the Co3Fe7 alloy (PDF no. 48-1817).45 With the increase of the annealing time and temperature, the intensity of Coâ€“Fe alloy peaks showed noticeable enhancement, suggesting the increase of Coâ€“Fe alloy contents. The peak position of the alloy did not show a noticeable shift, suggesting a similar composition of the alloy nanoparticles.33 This result is consistent with the increased amount of exsolved nanoparticles on the surface, as observed in SEM measurement. Accompanied by the presence of a Coâ€“Fe alloy peak, a new diffraction peak, which was assigned to Râ€“P phases, appeared in the XRD pattern. Due to the lack of polarity of linear molecules, the adsorption and activation of CO2 on the fuel electrode surface are normally quite poor, which led to the starvation of CO2 during operation and low electrochemical performance.58 Therefore, the adsorption of CO2 on the surface plays a critical role in determining the activity of the fuel electrode toward CO2 reduction.65 To investigate the CO2 adsorption characteristics, we carried out CO2-TPD measurement of the STCFâ€“SDC and Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC electrode materials. Conclusions

In this work, we developed highly active and stable fuel electrode materials, which consist of a porous Râ€“P phase oxide matrix and exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles, for electrochemical reduction of CO2 to produce CO at high temperature. Stoichiometric double perovskite oxide Sr2Ti0.8Co0.2FeO6âˆ’Î´ (STCF) was thermally reduced in a H2 atmosphere at high temperature. The size and population density of the exsolved Coâ€“Fe alloy nanoparticles were found to depend closely on the reduction temperature and time. The sample subjected to annealing at 900 Â°C for 1 h in H2 (Coâ€“Feâ€“STCF) exhibited the optimal microstructure with a small particle size and high particle population density. The combination of SEM, XRD and TEM characterization suggested that the Coâ€“Fe alloy metal particles were embedded in the oxide matrix with a robust interface. Moreover, XPS and TG analysis revealed that abundant oxygen vacancies were generated in the oxide matrix after the exsolution of metal particles. The cell with Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC as the fuel electrode exhibited outstanding CO2 electrolysis performance, with the current density, CO production rate and faradaic efficiency as high as 1.26 A cmâˆ’2, 8.75 mL minâˆ’1 cmâˆ’2 and âˆ¼100%, respectively, at 1.6 V and 800 Â°C. Such high activity was attributed to the promoted adsorption of CO2 molecules on the surface of Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC as revealed by CO2-TPD measurement. Furthermore, the Coâ€“Feâ€“STCFâ€“SDC electrode exhibited excellent stability with no carbon deposition after the 100 h test of CO2 electrolysis at 1.2 V at 800 Â°C. These results demonstrated cation exsolution as an effective approach to construct high performance electrode materials for high-temperature CO2 electrolysis. The methodology and finding in this work can be applied to other high-temperature energy and environmental devices, such as solar thermal water splitting and volatile organic compound (VOC) removal.
