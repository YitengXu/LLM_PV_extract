Theoretical studies on the effect of a bithiophene bridge with different substituent groups (R = H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) in donor–π–acceptor copolymers for organic solar cell applications†

Zhiyong Fu , Wei Shen , Rongxing He , Xiaorui Liu , Huili Sun , Wanqiang Yin and Ming Li *
College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Southwest University, Chongqing 400715, China. E-mail: liming@swu.edu.cn

Received 12th September 2014 , Accepted 25th November 2014

First published on 26th November 2014

Abstract

An effective way to enhance the efficiency of bulk heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells is to insert suitable bridges (π) between donor units (D) and acceptor units (A) in D–π–A copolymers. This work is devoted to uncovering how the characteristics of a HT (the substituent groups via head- to-tail (HT) connection) bithiophene bridge with different substituent groups (R = H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) affect the ground state structure, electronic, optical and charge transport properties of D–π–A copolymers for improving the photovoltaic performance. Based on the D–π–A copolymer PPBzT2-CEHβ (P1) with a HT bridge of 3,4′-diethylhexyl-2,2′-bithiophene (π1), we designed six new copolymers (P2–P6′) by introducing six kinds of HT bridges. From the calculated results, the introduction of different substituent groups into the bithiophene-bridge can markedly affect the HOMO and LUMO levels, band gaps, light-absorbing efficiency and hole transport ability of the copolymers. In particular, the copolymer P6 combining the cyano and methoxyl groups into the bridge has remarkable electronic and optical properties and hole transport ability among all the copolymers P1–P6′, and it can be a candidate for donor materials of organic solar cells. We hope that the present results could provide a theoretical guidance for designing efficient donors in organic solar cells.

1. Introduction

Over the past few years, bulk heterojunction (BHJ) polymer solar cells (PSCs) offer great opportunities as renewable energy sources due to their lightweight, low-cost production, large-area and flexible photovoltaic devices.1,2 In general, the active layers of BHJ polymer solar cells are comprised of a phase-separated donor–acceptor blend layer with a p-type conjugated polymer as the electron donor, and an n-type fullerene derivative (PC61BM or PC71BM) as the electron acceptor.3,4 The working mechanism of donor–acceptor heterojunction solar cells includes four main steps:5,6 (1) the photo-excitation of the donor generates an exciton (a Coulomb-correlate electron–hole pair), (2) the exciton diffuses to the donor–acceptor interface, (3) the bound exciton dissociates at the donor–acceptor interface to form free charge (electron and hole), (4) free charge transportation in the active layer and collection by the electrodes.

As everyone knows, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of an OSC (organic solar cell) is closely connected with the open circuit voltage (Voc), the short-circuit current density (Jsc) and the fill factor (FF). Therefore, the most straightforward way to improve the PCE is to enhance Voc, Jsc and FF. In BHJ solar cells, Voc is in a relationship with the charge carrier mobility, light intensity as well as dissociation and decay rates of photo-induced electron–hole pairs.7 Moreover, the Voc is linearly dependent on the difference between the HOMO level of a donor and the LUMO level of an acceptor.8Jsc is affected by the light-absorbing efficiency of the active layer, exciton transportation and dissociation at the D/A interface, and charge transportation in the photoactive layer as well as charge collection on the electrodes.9,10 FF is associated with two elements of the devices: the series resistances (Rs) and shunt resistances (Rsh), lower series resistance and higher shunt resistance lead to higher FF values.10–12 Besides, the morphology of the photovoltaic material also markedly influences the FF in the manufacturing process of the BHJ solar cell device.9 Therefore, an ideal donor material would possess suitable HOMO and LUMO energy levels, a broad and strong absorption band in visible and near infrared regions to match the solar spectrum, high charge carrier mobility and high air-stability.

In the search for promising D–A donor copolymers for PSCs, much effort has been focused on the design and synthesis of new D and A building blocks.13,14 In comparison with inorganic semiconductors, a unique advantage of the conjugated copolymers is that their optoelectronic properties can be easily tuned by attaching functional substituents on the polymer main chains. For instance, Berson and co-workers15 studied the effect of carbonitrile and hexyloxy substituents on the performances of the alternated copolymer polythiophene in photovoltaic cells. Bredas and Heeger16 paid much attention to the effect of the functional substituents (electron-donating and electron-drawing substituents) on the electronic energy levels of poly(p-phenylene vinylene) (PPV). Huang and co-workers17 investigated how the alkoxy and carbonitrile groups affect the photoelectric properties of the PPV-based electroluminescent polymers. Those reports show that the electron-withdrawing groups will downshift the energy levels with the LUMO level more than the HOMO level, while the electron-donating groups will upshift the energy levels with the HOMO level more than the LUMO level.

There is already considerable research13,14 on the design and synthesis of new D or A building blocks, however, the studies focusing on the bridges between D and A units are insufficient, which provide a new idea to improve the performance of organic solar cells. For example, Wang and co-workers18 synthesized a series of conjugated D–π–A copolymers to investigate the effect of π-conjugated bridges on photovoltaic properties. They found that the π-conjugated bridges can markedly influence the molecular geometries and optoelectronic properties of these copolymers, and consequently directly affected the photovoltaic performances. Furthermore, Biniek and co-workers19 studied a series of copolymers with different numbers of thiophene bridges and found that PPBzT2-CEHβ (3,4′-diethylhexyl-2,2′-bithiophene as π-bridge) possesses larger Voc and Jsc, higher hole mobility and PCE than that of PTBzT2-CEHβ (3-ethylhexylthiophene as the π-bridge). However, up to now, few studies have concentrated on the effect of the π-bridges with different substituent groups (electron-donating groups or electron-withdrawing groups) between D and A segments on the ground state structure, electronic, optical and charge transport properties of D–π–A copolymers. Compared with the thiophene-bridge, the bithiophene-bridge has more substitution positions to facilitate introducing different substituents, which can adjust the frontier molecular orbital energy levels and energy gaps (Eg) of the copolymers.20–22 Herein, we focus on investigating the effect of a bithiophene-bridge with different substituent groups (R = H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) on the ground state geometry, optoelectronic and charge transport properties of D–π–A copolymers.

A series of questions will be put forward when a bithiophene-bridge with different substituent groups (R = H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) is inserted between D and A units in the polymer backbone. (1) What is the effect of the substituent groups on the ground state geometry, electronic, optical and charge transport properties of these copolymers as well as the photovoltaic performance? (2) Is it true whether the more the electron-donating groups or electron-withdrawing groups introduced into the bithiophene-bridge, the more it is beneficial to improve the photovoltaic performance of organic solar cells? It should be focused on. (3) How to make the copolymers have deep-lying HOMO energy levels and narrow band gaps, and high hole mobilities by introducing the substituents into the bithiophene-bridge? We introduce a series of different π-bridges, such as 2,2′-bithiophene (π2), 3,4′-dialkoxy-2,2′-bithiophene (π3), 2,2′-bithiophene-3,4′-dicarbonitrile (π4), 4′-alkyl-2,2′-bithiophene-3-carbonitrile (π5), 4′-alkoxy-2,2′-bithiophene-3-carbonitrile (π6) and 3′-alkoxy-2,2′-bithiophene-4-carbonitrile (π6′) to replace π1 (3,4′-diethylhexyl-2,2′-bithiophene) in PPBzT2-CEHβ (P1) for expounding the above-mentioned problems. Herein, our calculations are an attempt to provide a theoretical model for analyzing how a bithiophene bridge with different substituent groups between the donor unit and the acceptor unit influences the photovoltaic properties of PSCs.

2. Computational details

In recent years, quantum chemical calculations provide a competitive alternative for the interpretation of experimental data and even offer predictability on the performances of novel materials, therefore, it can reduce the development cost of the new materials. Nowadays, the density functional theory (DFT) method23,24 has attracted much attention because this method can consider the electronic correlation very well and be applied to the organic macromolecular systems.

In this work, the ground state geometries of the oligomers (monomer and dimer) were fully optimized by DFT with B3LYP,25 PBE0, BHandHLYP26 and B1B95 functionals at the 6-31G** and 6-311G** basis sets. In order to simplify the calculations and reduce the computational costs, all the alkyl and alkoxy branched chains (in Fig. 1) were replaced by methyl and methoxyl groups, respectively, with little effect on the frontier molecular orbitals and optoelectronic properties,27,28 and the terminal groups of the repetitive units were saturated with hydrogen atoms.28 As shown in Table 1, the calculated HOMO energy level (dimer model) at the B1B95/6-311G** level (−5.05 eV for P1) agrees well with the experimental value of P1 (−5.04 eV). By contrast, the calculated HOMO energy levels with a smaller basis set (6-31G**) are listed in Table S1 (ESI†). Based on the optimized ground state structure of the dimer model at the B1B95/6-311G** level, the electronic absorption spectra of the copolymer (P1) were calculated with TDDFT at the B1B95/6-311G** level in agreement with the experimental value. Therefore, the computations at the B1B95/6-311G** level are more appropriate for this system. The calculated vibrational spectrum in each molecule has no imaginary frequency, which implies that all the optimized structures are located at the minimum point of the potential surface. The LUMO level was taken from the sum of the HOMO level plus the TDDFT transition energy (S0 → S1, ELUMO = EHOMO + Eg,TD) rather than from the unreliable KS (Kohn–Sham) LUMO eigenvalue.28–30 The cationic and anionic geometries of the oligomers were also optimized at the B1B95/6-311G** level. The intermolecular distance between two adjacent molecular segments was scanned using the M06-2X functional at the 6-31G** basis set.31 The correlative calculations of the transfer integral were also performed at the M06-2X/6-31G** level. In this paper, the spin-restricted DFT was adopted for the ground state geometries, whereas the spin-unrestricted DFT was used for the ion-state geometries. All the calculations were carried out with the aid of the Gaussian 09 package in the gas phase.32

3. Results and discussion

3.1. The ground state structural properties

In order to investigate the effect of a HT bithiophene-bridge with different substituent groups (R = H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) on the ground state structure properties of the corresponding copolymers (P1–P6′), we deduced the ground state structure properties of P1–P6′ by investigating that of the corresponding monomers (M1–M6′). The main optimized geometry parameters of M1–M6′ are shown in Table 2. The optimized geometries of all monomers are listed in Fig. S1 (ESI†). As shown in Table 2, the calculated dihedral angles (C1–C2–C3–S1/S1–C4–C5–C6/C7–C8–C9–C10/C11–C12–C13–C14/C15–C16–C17–S2) of M3 are less than the corresponding dihedral angles of M1, M2 and M4, meaning that the molecular backbone of M3 has better planarity than that of M1, M2 and M4. The possible reason is that the interactions of sulphur–oxygen (S–O) could result in the steric hindrance of the methoxyl group smaller than that of other substituents (H, CH3 and CN). The introduction of the methoxyl into M3 leads to a stronger conjugation effect and shorter bond lengths (C2–C3/C4–C5/C8–C9/C12–C13/C16–C17) in comparison to M1, M2 and M4. For M6 and M6′, the values of the bond length and the dihedral angle are different, although the substituent groups are the same in the bithiophene-bridge. This suggests that the substituent positions have a significant effect on molecular planarity.

3.2. Frontier molecular orbital energies

The analysis of the molecular orbitals of the copolymers is very important because the relative order of the occupied and virtual orbitals offers a reasonable qualitative indication of excitation properties and the ability of electron or hole transport.33 Herein, we focus on analyzing the HOMO and LUMO levels of the copolymers in the next section.

We calculated the HOMO/LUMO energy levels of all the copolymers on a dimer model by DFT at the B1B95/6-311G** level. From Fig. 2a and Table 3, for the copolymers of P1–P4, one can see that the HOMO levels are in the order P3 > P1 > P2 > P4, and their LUMO levels are in the sequence P1 > P3 > P2 > P4. Moreover, the energy gaps (S0 → S1) of those copolymers are in the order P3 < P1 < P2 < P4. These results clearly show that the introduction of the double CN groups into the bithiophene-bridge of P4 can significantly lower the HOMO/LUMO levels, and the HOMO level down-shifted more than the LUMO level in comparison with P2. This is not consistent with previous reports10,15,16 that the electron-withdrawing groups make the LUMO level down-shifted more than the HOMO level. As we can see from the electron density plots of P4 in Fig. 3, the CN groups involved in the HOMO orbital while it hardly participated in the LUMO orbital. This may be the reason of the above-mentioned result (for P4, the HOMO level reduced more than the LUMO level). However, compared with that of P2, the copolymers P1 and P3 in which the dual electron-donating groups (CH3/OCH3) were introduced into the bithiophene-bridge can observably increase the HOMO/LUMO levels, and reduce the energy gap, especially methoxyl. This result is consistent with previous reports that the electron-donating groups induce the HOMO levels up-shifted more than the LUMO levels.

It is interesting to note that the incorporation of cyano-group and methoxyl into the same bithiophene-bridge has a narrow energy gap (Eg) and lower-lying HOMO level. The values of HOMO/LUMO and energy gaps for P6/P6′ are in between P3 (methoxyl as substituted group) and P4 (CN as substituted group). Similarly, the values of HOMO/LUMO and energy gap for P5 are in between P1 (methyl as substituted group) and P4. Moreover, for P6/P6′, the HOMO/LUMO levels and the energy gaps are different, although the substituent groups are the same in the bithiophene-bridge. This result reveals that the substituent positions in the bithiophene-bridge greatly influence the electronic properties.

In order to investigate whether the π-bridge fragments of D–π–A donor copolymers make the above-mentioned changes (such as the variation of HOMO and LUMO levels), we separately calculated the energy levels (HOMO/LUMO) and energy gaps (see ESI,† Table S2) of bridge fragments. As shown in Fig. 2b, for the four π-conjugated bridges (π1, π2, π3 and π4), the HOMO energy levels are in the order π3 > π1 > π2 > π4, and the LUMO values are in the sequence π1 > π3 > π2 > π4. We found that the variation of HOMO and LUMO energy levels of P1–P4 is in accordance with that of the four π-conjugated bridges. These results are also in accord with the previous reports10,15,34 that an electron-withdrawing group generally results in the decreasing of the HOMO/LUMO energy levels, while an electron-donating group usually leads to the HOMO/LUMO energy levels increase. Interestingly, the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of π5 are in between the values of π1 and the values of π4 in accord with the above-mentioned results (the HOMO and LUMO values of P5 in between P1 and P4). Likewise, the HOMO and LUMO values of π6/π6′ are also in between π3 and π4. It shows that the π-bridges directly affect the electronic properties of the corresponding D–π–A copolymers. Moreover, the HOMO/LUMO energy levels of the copolymers can be controllably adjusted by introducing the electron-donating groups and electron-withdrawing groups into the bithiophene-bridge.

For P1, P2, P4, P5 and P6′, from the molecular orbital diagrams (see Fig. 3), the HOMOs are mainly delocalized on the electron-rich TT units and bridge units as well as the benzene ring of the inner electron-deficient units. For P3 and P6, the densities of HOMO are mainly delocalized in the middle of molecular chains (electron-rich TT units and bridge units). This result suggests that the introduction of different substituent groups into the bithiophene-bridge has a significant effect on the distributions of HOMO electron density. Moreover, for those copolymers (P1–P6′), the electron densities of LUMOs are mainly located on the electron-deficient units (BT). The electronic transitions (HOMO → LUMO) involving these orbitals are intramolecular charge transfer, where electrons should be moved from the electron-rich unit to the electron-deficient unit.

3.3. Theoretical characterization of three parameters (Voc, Jsc and FF) for organic solar cells (OSCs)

As is known to all, the PCE of an OSC is determined by three important factors: open circuit voltage (Voc), short-circuit current (Jsc) and fill factor (FF), and it can be written as PCE = (Voc × Jsc × FF)/Pin, where Pin is the light power incident on the device. Therefore, it is very meaningful to analyze the above-mentioned parameters for the improvement of the PCE of an OSC.

Generally, Voc is proportional to the difference between the HOMO level of a donor and the LUMO level of an acceptor. What is the effect of the substituent groups on the optical properties of the copolymers? The calculated results show that the introduction of two strong electron-withdrawing groups (CN) into a bithiophene-bridge in P4 sharply increases the energy gap and leads to the significant blue shift of the maximum absorption peak in comparison with P2 (H as the substituent group). The reason for this result may be due to the strong electron-withdrawing groups leading to the energy levels downward with the HOMO level down-shifted more than the LUMO level. In contrast, the introduction of two strong electron-donating groups (–OCH3) into the bithiophene-bridge in P3 significantly decreases the energy gap in comparison with P2, which is responsible for the markedly bathochromic shift of the maximum absorption peak. That may be due to the strong electron-donating groups inducing the HOMO level to rise more than the LUMO level. However, the energy gap of P1, with the weak electron-donating CH3 as the substituent group, is almost equal to that of P2. Therefore, the results demonstrate that: (1) the strong electron-withdrawing group can increase the energy gaps of the copolymers to some extent, (2) the strong electron-donating group reduces the energy gap of the copolymers, and the weak electron-donating group has a little effect on the energy gap. Compared with the values of P6 and P6′, the positions of the substituents in the bithiophene-bridge have great influence on the optical properties. This may be due to the fact that the substituent positions of the bithiophene-bridge are able to affect the molecular backbone coplanarity to a large extent.

3.4. Charge transfer properties

In the BHJPSC, a high hole mobility for the donor contributes to the enhancement of the charge transport efficiency and the FF of the solar cell.37 In general, the incoherent hopping model and coherent band model are two typical types of carrier (hole–electron) motion in materials, which appear under different conditions. For instance, the latter is currently appropriate for rigid inorganic semiconductors which through the strong valence bonds interact with two different sites. However, the coherent band model may be invalidated by the dynamic structure disorder at a high temperature, implying that the former may be the predominant mechanism at room temperature. In this work we adopt a hopping model38 to describe the charge transport properties along the directions of the interchains in the donor polymer system. 3.4.1. Reorganization energy. Generally, the total reorganization energy consists of the inner reorganization (λint) and external reorganization energy (λext). Firstly, the rapid change in molecular structure determines the λint, as a charge is removed or added from a molecule. And then, the polarization effects induce variations that λext denotes in the surrounding medium.5 The calculated values of the λext in the medium (such as film) are much smaller than their internal counterparts (λint).43,44 In this case, the λext could be ignored. Therefore, we would mainly discuss about the λint in this paper. Moreover, a clear correlation between λint and the charge transfer rate has been reported in the previous papers.45,46 The inner hole reorganization energy (λh) and electron reorganization energy (λe) values can be calculated using eqn (10) and (11):40,45
 
λh = [E+0 − E+] + [E0+ − E0](10)
 
λe = [E−0 − E−] + [E0− − E0](11)
where E0 is the energy of neutral geometry, E+ (E−) denotes the energy of the cation (anion) in cationic (anionic) geometry, E0+ (E0−) represents the energy of the neutral structure at the cationic (anionic) state, and E+0 (E−0) stands for the energy of cation (anion) with neutral geometry, respectively. The calculated λh and λe values are listed in Table 4. The λh values of the investigated copolymers on a dimer model (0.185–0.278 eV) are smaller than that of the typical hole transport material N,N′-diphenyl-N,N′-bis(3-methlphenyl)-(1,10-biphenyl)-4,4′-diamine (TPD) (λh = 0.290 eV),47 which implies that these copolymers may have higher hole transfer rates. Similarly, the λe values (0.097–0.141 eV) are smaller than that of tris(8-hydroxyquinolinato)aluminum(III) (Alq3) (λe = 0.276 eV), a typical electron transport material,48 which denotes that the investigated copolymers may have higher electron transfer rates.

For the copolymers (P1–P6′), their values of λh are 0.278, 0.221, 0.231, 0.193, 0.251, 0.185 and 0.204 eV, respectively, and their λe values are 0.141, 0.137, 0.103, 0.129, 0.121, 0.097 and 0.112 eV, respectively. These results suggest that the introduction of different substituent groups (H, CH3, OCH3 and CN) into the bithiophene-bridge has a large effect on λe and λh of those copolymers. For the copolymers of P1–P6′, their λh values decrease in the following order: P1 > P5 > P3 > P2 > P6′ > P4 > P6, and their λe values are in the sequence: P1 > P2 > P4 > P5 > P6′ > P3 > P6. This shows that P6 has the best charge transfer property among all the copolymers. Moreover, the difference between λh and λe values of P6 is smaller than that of P1–P3, P5 and P6′. This reveals that P6 is more conducive to equilibrium transport for hole and electron in comparison with these copolymers. Therefore, the combination of the cyano-group and methoxyl in the bithiophene-bridge of the copolymer can significantly improve the charge transport ability.

3.5. Solar cell performances

Generally speaking, the polymer solar cells are based on the BHJ structure of a blend of donors and acceptors (fullerene derivative). Now, PC61BM/PC71BM (PC71BM, [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester)8,52,53 is extensively used as the acceptor in organic solar cells because of the advantages of high electron affinity, high electron mobility and good solubility in common organic solvents. In this study, PCBM (PC60BM) was selected as the electron acceptor with the HOMO and LUMO energy levels of −6.0 eV and −4.2 eV, respectively. As shown in Fig. 5, the differences of LUMO energy levels between those donors (P1–P6) and the acceptor of PC61BM are corresponding to 1.07, 0.99, 1.03, 0.19, 0.69 and 0.62 eV, respectively. All of them (except P4) are located above 0.3 eV, which can be beneficial to the electron transfer from the donor to the acceptor.8,14

For the sake of qualitative evaluation the performance of the designed copolymers, Scharber proposed a model8 that the LUMO level and the band gap of the donor copolymer can be used to predict the PCE of the BHJ solar cell. Lately, Ku28 has employed the Scharber diagram to predict the PCE of the solar cell made by donor and PC61BM. The results show that the predicted PCEs of the solar cells are in agreement with the reported experimental data. Next, we predict the PCE (∼3.3%) of P1 (using the calculated results of Eg,TD and ELUMO = EHOMO + Eg,TD) by the Scharber diagram, which agrees well with the experimental data (3.2%).19 As shown in Fig. 6, the predicted PCEs of the newly designed copolymers (P2, P3, P5 and P6) are ∼3.7% (P2), ∼0.6% (P3), ∼5.8% (P5) and ∼6.8% (P6), respectively. The PCE of P3 is so low due to its very small band gap. Among these copolymers, P6 possesses the narrow band gap and the deepest LUMO energy level, which exhibits the highest predicted PCE close to ∼7% when combined with the PCBM acceptor in organic solar cells. The result shows that our idea can be used to improve the performance of the D–π–A copolymers in the application of solar cells.

4. Conclusion

In conclusion, a vast number of calculations and parallel comparisons are used to elaborate the role of the π-bridge with different substituent groups in the performance of PSCs. According to the calculated results, the strong electron-donating group leads to the HOMO level up-shifted more than the LUMO level, while the electron-withdrawing group induces the HOMO level down-shifted more than the LUMO level. Furthermore, the weak electron-donating group (methyl) shifts the HOMO and LUMO levels upward to a similar extent. The calculated results also show that the substituent positions in the bithiophene-bridge have a significant effect on the ground state structure, electronic, optical properties and hole mobility of D–π–A copolymers. Interestingly, we found that the introduction of the electron-donating groups and electron-withdrawing groups into the same bithiophene-bridge can not only easily adjust the energy levels and energy gaps of the copolymers, but can also improve the charge transport properties and the photovoltaic performances. Therefore, the introduction of strong electron-withdrawing groups and strong electron-donating groups via a HT connection in the same bithiophene-bridge is an effective way to improve electronic, optical and charge transport properties as well as the photovoltaic performance of PSCs. In our designed compounds, the copolymer (P6) has a deep-lying HOMO level, strong and wide light absorption, high light-absorption efficiency, the smallest hole reorganization energy and the largest hole mobility among all the copolymers. Especially the predicted PCE of the newly designed copolymer (P6) attains ∼6.8%. This study presents us with a design guideline for improving the photovoltaic performance of organic solar cells.
