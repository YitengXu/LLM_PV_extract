Abstract

Design and engineering of crystalline advanced photocatalysts with specific facets is one of the most challenging tasks to enhance the photocatalytic performance. The surface energy of different facets is different in a crystal which leads to a corresponding change in their photocatalytic behaviour. The present study provides an experimental as well as theoretical understanding of the role of different facets of NaNbO3 in cubic and orthorhombic phases with crystals showing cubic and cuboctahedron morphologies in enhancing the photocatalytic activity of NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures. Herein, we discuss the importance of the approach of facet-selective synthesis and trace the origin of enhanced photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting and photocatalytic dye degradation activity for calculated surface energies of the {100} family of facets of the cubic phase and the (110) and (114) facets of the orthorhombic phase of NaNbO3. We propose that different mechanisms contribute to the enhancement of catalytic activity in these two phases. In the prepared core/shell heterostructures containing NaNbO3 as the core material, the presence of highly reactive facets of the cubic phase contributes to higher photocatalytic activity as compared to the orthorhombic phase which has a spatial charge separation assisted inter-facet charge transfer mechanism.

1. Introduction

The ever-increasing demand for energy and rapid depletion of fossil fuels triggered massive research efforts oriented towards finding alternative methods for obtaining abundant and sustainable energy.1,2 Photocatalysis has been developed as a suitable technique that can help in generating renewable and environmentally friendly energy. There have been numerous studies aimed towards enhancement of photocatalytic activity of the commonly used materials by using various approaches.3–5 These include investigations into the mechanism of photocatalysis on the nanoscale,6,7 engineering the energy-band structure of the materials,8,9 optimization of the crystal structure of the material10–13 and crucially control of the size and shape of the crystals, etc.14–17 Although such studies have been carried out on a wide variety of materials ranging from oxides to chalcogenides such as TiO2, BiVO4, WO3, Fe2O3, Ag3PO4, CdS, Ag2S, CdSe, In2S3etc.,18–25 there are only a few reports on interesting perovskite materials such as NaNbO3 and even fewer on core/shell heterostructures based on NaNbO3.26–30 Nevertheless, facet engineering of binary oxides as well as perovskites has been a challenging problem studied by a number of groups in the recent past. Nanocrystals of these materials with various morphologies ranging from nanorods to cubes, cuboctahedra and octahedra etc. have been synthesized. Asymmetric binary nanocrystals comprising two sections involving c-axis elongated anatase TiO2 and spherical γ-Fe2O3 attached to each other have been reported.31 Recently, facet dependent disorder in pristine Li(LiyNix−yMnxCo1−2x)O2 has been observed wherein a strong segregation tendency of defects to prefer open facets rather than closed facets is reported.32 New insights into the role of binary oxides such as MnO2 and perovskites such as LaMnO3 have been gained and these insights can help in identifying key parameters that influence catalysis in oxygen reduction reactions (ORR). Further, such studies will help in developing design guides for highly active ORR catalysts and gain new knowledge about their molecular mechanism as well.33 On site-selective substitution in perovskite oxides such as Ba0.5Sr0.5Co0.8Fe0.2O3−d (BSCF), it has been shown that structural optimization is possible following molecular orbital principles.34 For overall water splitting, the synthesis of (111)-layered perovskite nanofibers of Ba5Ta4O15, Ba5Ta2Nb2O15 and Ba5Nb4O15 has been achieved.35 In perovskite solar cell materials based on organometal halide light absorbers, power conversion efficiency (PCE) has been increasing steadily due to structural optimization through facet engineering approaches.36 The rapid strides taken in facet engineering of some of these perovskites have shown us not only the energy harvesting potential of these materials but also further possibilities of exploring materials such as NaNbO3 in the present study.

NaNbO3 has been found to be a highly efficient photocatalyst in the UV region which facilitates water splitting leading to H2 and O2 evolution.37–40 Structurally, NaNbO3 is one of the most complex materials as it is known to exhibit a series of structural phase transitions from a rhombohedral structure at low temperatures (∼27 °C) to a cubic structure at high temperatures (above 630 °C).41,42 Between these two temperatures, NaNbO3 exists in a variety of orthorhombic phases as well as in the tetragonal phase.43,44 The structural complexities become all the more intriguing in NaNbO3 at the nanocrystalline level.45 In this size regime, it is possible, albeit not facile, to synthesize and stabilize the cubic perovskite phase of NaNbO3 only at high temperatures above 600 °C whereas the orthorhombic phase forms at lower temperatures. Since the past decade, many researchers are working on the surface chemistry of nanocrystals and the mechanism of surfactant ligand modification for the growth of the crystal.46 Zhang et al. also successfully reported facet activity of triangular pyramidal and cubic morphologies of NaNbO3 in photocatalytic reactions for 2,4-dichlorophene and rhodamine B degradation under full arc irradiation of a Xe lamp.47,48

The stable morphology of a crystal depends on the surface energies and growth rates of different competing facets arising out of the inherent anisotropy in such materials. Generally, in the synthesis of nanocrystals, the commonly used surfactants, e.g. cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) and polyacrylic acid (PAA), tend to modify the energy of specific facets through preferential adsorption, which influences the relative growth rate of different facets, thereby resulting in different morphologies of the crystal. Three of the most important facets that determine the crystal's geometric shape having Miller indices (100), (110) and (111) are low index facets. For a given volume of the crystal, its shape or morphology depends on the surface energy and is determined by the construction arrived using Wulff's theorem.49–51 For most lattice types, the increasing order of surface energies (γ) of low index planes follow the order γ(100) > γ(111) > γ(110).52,53 The surface energy of a given facet increases with increasing number of dangling bonds and the magnitude of bond energies of these bonds.54,55 Therefore, the fundamental requirement is to understand the role of various facets of the core material NaNbO3 in such heterostructures and trace the origin of efficient photocatalysis to the surface energy of various facets responsible for enhanced catalytic activity.

In the present work, we aim to understand the structure–morphology correlations of NaNbO3 to design a core/shell heterostructure with CdS for efficient facet-dependent photocatalytic activity under the visible region. The morphology of the crystals is controlled by a set of synthetic conditions such as temperature, processing duration and type of surfactant used in each case. Different surfactants show different adsorption on to various crystal facets during the growth are depending on both the surfactant as well as of certain facets and suppression of others thereby enabling control of the morphology of the crystal. In the present case, the CTAB surfactant has been found to show preferential adsorption onto the {100} facets of the cubic phase, whereas PAA shows preferential adsorption onto the (110) and (114) facets of the orthorhombic phase of NaNbO3 crystals. These results are helpful in the development of cubic and cuboctahedron morphologies of crystal growth in the two synthesis procedures followed in this study as shown in Scheme 1. We have stabilized the cubic NaNbO3 with a well-defined cubic morphology at a much lower temperature than previously reported.56 Gu et al. also reported a modified solvothermal strategy where the inherent temperature fluctuations of the autoclaving process was utilized for crystal growth of NaNbO3 cubic phase nanowires.57 Herein, we were able to design NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, based on the mechanism of crystallization of NaNbO3, with stable cubic and cuboctahedron morphologies driven by surface energy and surfactant ligand dynamics. Hence, these semiconductor core/shell heterostructures show enhanced photocatalytic efficiency based on their morphologies.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Synthesis of NaNbO3 crystals

All chemicals used in the synthesis of NaNbO3 are of analytical grade and used without further purification. NaNbO3 cubes and cuboctahedra were synthesized via a hydrothermal method. In materials synthesis, 2 g of niobium pentoxide (Nb2O5) (≥99.90%, Aldrich) was dispersed in double distilled water. The solution was mixed with 10 N NaOH (98%, Fisher Scientific) solution with continuous stirring for 2 h at room temperature, and then 500 mg of CTAB was added and the solution was stirred further for 4 h. Finally, the reaction mixture was then transferred to a 100 mL Teflon autoclave with the heating temperature maintained at 170 °C for 48 h (forming a cubic morphology); further, the same experimental conditions are used where 500 mg of PAA and 2 g of Nb2O5 were mixed at 120 °C for 60 h (forming a cuboctahedron morphology). The final product was collected by centrifugation and washed with double distilled water and absolute ethanol several times, and finally, the samples were dried at 80 °C for 6 h.

2.2 Synthesis of CdS nanoparticles

The hydrothermal method is used for the synthesis of CdS nanoparticles as well, using cadmium nitrate tetrahydrate (Cd(NO3)2·4H2O, ≥98%, Aldrich) and sodium sulphide nonahydrate (Na2S·9H2O, ≥98%, Aldrich) as precursors. In the reaction method, Cd(NO3)2·4H2O and Na2S·9H2O were dissolved separately in 60 mL of ethylene glycol. Afterwards, both solutions were mixed properly and loaded in a 100 mL Teflon autoclave. Reaction temperature was maintained at 175 °C for 2 h, and then, the autoclave was naturally cooled down. Finally, the samples was separated and collected by centrifugation and washed with double distilled water and absolute ethanol repeatedly.

2.3 Synthesis of NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures

For the fabrication of NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures by the growth of a CdS shell onto NaNbO3 cubes and cuboctahedra, we used 3-mercaptopropionic acid (MPA) as the surface functionalizing agent. 25 μL of MPA was added to the bare NaNbO3 (cube and cuboctahedron) nanoparticles dispersed in double distilled water, and the reaction mixture was stirred for 6 h at room temperature for functionalization of the surface of the NaNbO3 crystals. Subsequently, 25 mL of 0.1 M cadmium nitrate tetrahydrate (Cd(NO3)2·4H2O) solution was added slowly to the above reaction mixture using a burette with constant magnetic stirring at room temperature. In the second step, 25 mL of 0.1 M sodium sulphide nonahydrate (Na2S·9H2O) solution was added gradually to the reaction mixture and stirred constantly. The final yellow colored product was extracted by centrifugation and washed continually with double distilled water and absolute alcohol and finally dried overnight at 50 °C.

2.4 Materials characterization

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was carried out to characterize the phase composition and crystalline nature and also revealed the purity of the resulting synthesized products formed during the experimental process. An Advance Bruker D8 diffractometer equipped with Ni-filtered Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) in the 2θ range of 10−70° at a scan rate of 0.02° per second is used for PXRD analysis. Field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM-FEI QUANTA 3D FEG) is carried out to determine the surface morphologies of the samples. Instrument equipped with an energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) detector for elemental mapping operated at an accelerating voltage of 5 kV was also used. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and HRTEM studies were carried out to gain detailed insights into the morphology and interface between core and shell materials, by using a TEM (FEI Technai G2 20) operated at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) system equipped with a TEM instrument performing point EDS of core/shell heterostructures can individually from the edge and the centre of the materials to further validate the core/shell morphology establishment in NaNbO3/CdS heterostructures. Dynamic light scattering (DLS) measurements were performed using a Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZS-90 (Malvern Instruments) instrument. The band gaps of the synthesized samples are determined by UV–vis diffuse reflectance spectroscopy recorded on a Shimadzu UV-2450 spectrometer, employing BaSO4 as a reflectance standard and the measurements are carried out over a wavelength range of 200–800 nm. A Quanta chrome (nova 2000e) surface area analyzer was used to obtain the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas of the as-synthesized samples. Nitrogen adsorption–desorption measurements were performed at the temperature of liquid nitrogen (∼77 K).

2.5 Photoelectrochemical and photocatalytic studies

For photoelectrochemical (PEC) measurements, a NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructure electrode was prepared by mixing 20 mg of photocatalyst in powder form with 80 μL of solution comprising anhydrous isopropanol used as a dispersant and Nafion in a 3:1 ratio. The prepared suspension was spread on a 1.5 × 1.5 cm2 ITO glass (SnO2:In) substrate with an active area of about 1 × 1 cm2 by the doctor-blade method using scotch tape as the spacer. Silver paste and copper wire are used for the undeposited area of the conducting glass substrate for fabrication of ohmic electrical contacts. Non-transparent and non-conducting epoxy resin was used to cover the undeposited area of the electrode. Three-electrode photoelectrochemical cell was used to obtain the current–voltage (I–V) characteristics, electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) and Mott–Schottky plots. CIMPS-2 (Controlled Intensity Modulated Photospectroscopy) was carried out using a photoelectrochemical system consisting of a Zennium electrochemical workstation (X-Pot Potentiostat). Linear sweep voltammetry scans were executed under dark and visible light irradiation using a xenon lamp (150 W) fitted with a filter that cuts light with wavelengths ≥380 nm with an output illumination intensity of 100 mW cm−2. Mott–Schottky measurements were performed under dark conditions in the potential range of −1.0 to +1.0 V versus Ag/AgCl at a scan rate of 20 mV s−1 in a 0.5 M Na2SO4 electrolyte (pH = 7). The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were also carried out under open bias conditions (frequency range: 100 kHz–100 MHz) at an AC signal amplitude of 10 mV. The improvement in photoelectrochemical response in the PEC cell for NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures was investigated at 0.62 V vs. Ag/AgCl (1.23 V vs. RHE) via incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) measurements in the wavelength range of 350–550 nm. Chronoamperometric measurements were scrutinized for 3 h in 0.5 M Na2SO4 under visible light irradiation at an applied potential of 1.0 V versus Ag/AgCl to optimize the stability of the material.

To evaluate the degradation efficiency of methylene blue (MB) dye, photocatalytic measurements were carried out. MB was utilized as a representative organic pollutant. The whole measurement was carried out under simulated visible irradiation induced by a 500 W xenon arc lamp equipped with an ultraviolet 420 nm cutoff filter used as the light source.

During visible-light photocatalytic experiments, 40 mg of catalyst was dispersed in a 100 mL aqueous solution of 1 × 10−5 M MB dye and the resulting solution was further stirred for 2 h under dark conditions to ensure adsorption/desorption equilibrium between the organic dye and the catalyst. The catalyst suspension was subsequently irradiated to initiate the photocatalytic reaction using a xenon arc lamp as the light source. Aliquots (2 mL) of the suspension were taken out at an interval of 5 min and centrifuged. To ensure that the degradation of the dye is solely driven by a photocatalytic process, control experiments were carried out in the absence of a catalyst and under dark conditions. The photodegradation efficiency was optimized by the decrease in intensity of the characteristic absorbance of MB (664 nm) using a Shimadzu UV-2450 spectrometer. A plausible mechanism for photocatalytic dye degradation using c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures is proposed from the analysis of the active species involved in photocatalysis such as electrons, hydroxyl radicals, holes and superoxide radical anions (O2˙−) during the photodegradation process utilizing various active species scavengers.

The optical properties of the NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures were explored by UV–visible diffuse reflectance spectroscopy (DRS) as shown in Fig. 3. The band gaps of the bare NaNbO3 samples and core/shell heterostructures were also calculated using the DRS spectra. The materials band gap can be calculated by a variant of the Tauc equation as given below:

 
(hν·F(R∞))l/n = A(hν − Eg)(1)
where h and ν are the Planck constant and the frequency of vibration, respectively, A is the proportionality constant, F(R∞) is the Kubelka–Munk function and Eg is the band gap of the synthesized photocatalytic materials. The exponent n = 2 for an indirect allowed transition and n = 1/2 for a direct allowed transition; therefore, the Tauc plot (inset of Fig. 3a) could be utilized to determine the band gap of the semiconductor materials.

The estimated band gap from the DRS spectra of bare NaNbO3 clearly shows that the observed band gap of c-NaNbO3 (cubic) is slightly higher (Eg = 3.46 eV) than that of the o-NaNbO3 (orthorhombic) phase (Eg = 3.43 eV) as shown in the inset of Fig. 3a. The difference in band gap of the material between the two phases can be attributed mainly to the difference in their crystal structure, size, and morphology. In NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, the DRS spectra validate the generation of a new energy level, compared with the bare NaNbO3 where an obvious red shift in the absorption edge was observed (Fig. 3b and c). The band gap of the core/shell heterostructures is found to shift towards the visible region of the spectrum which is advantageous for visible light photocatalysis. In both cubic and orthorhombic core/shell heterostructures, two energy bands are observed, one represents CdS and the other represent NaNbO3 which clearly evidences the formation of core/shell composite heterostructures. The characteristic absorption spectrum with the fundamental absorption stopping edge at ∼505 nm lies within the visible light region. The calculated band gap energies of c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures from the DRS spectra corresponding to NaNbO3 and CdS are 2.81 and 2.40 eV, respectively, and the corresponding values for the o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures are 2.95 and 2.42 eV as shown in Fig. 3b and c, respectively. Thus, the DRS results mirror the visible light photoactive behaviour of core/shell heterostructures.

Similarly, the Na1s peak is also shifted by 0.3 eV towards a higher binding energy in the o-NaNbO3/CdS cuboctahedral sample. The shift of the corresponding Nb3d and Na1s peaks toward higher binding energies is probably due to the change in the morphology and structure of the NaNbO3. Importantly, the Cd3d5/2 peak and S2p peak positions in both samples are the same and not affected due to the inner core structure (Fig. S6d and e‡).

3.1 Photoelectrochemical activity

To study the photoelectrochemical (PEC) performances of the bare c-NaNbO3, o-NaNbO3 crystals, and c-NaNbO3/CdS and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, the prepared photoelectrodes were used in the PEC cell and linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) measurements were performed in a 0.5 M Na2SO4 electrolyte under dark and visible light illumination conditions with an output intensity of 100 mW cm−2 in a three-electrode PEC system with the NaNbO3 based photoelectrode as the working electrode, Ag/AgCl as the reference electrode and Pt mesh as the counterelectrode (Fig. 4(a)). The PEC activities of the different phases of NaNbO3 crystals were measured to evaluate the facet dependent performance. In c-NaNbO3, o-NaNbO3 and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, the dark current from 0 V to 1.0 V (versus Ag/AgCl) was almost zero. However, in the case of c-NaNbO3/CdS heterostructures, the dark current onset potential shifts to 0.3 V vs. Ag/AgCl and the current density increased up to 0.4 mA cm−2 (inset of Fig. 4a). The photocurrent onset for all the samples was obtained at around −0.5 V versus Ag/AgCl. The obtained photocurrent density [(current under light − current under dark)/electrode area] for the o-NaNbO3 cuboctahedron is very low, ∼0.13 mA cm−2 at a potential of 1.0 V versus Ag/AgCl. The photocurrent of c-NaNbO3/CdS cubic and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell is significantly enhanced when compared with the bare NaNbO3 samples. The c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell photoanode exhibited superior PEC performance in terms of an increased photocurrent density of ∼2.05 ± 0.05 mA cm−2 at a potential of 1.0 V versus Ag/AgCl and a decreased onset potential from 0.3 to −0.5 V versus Ag/AgCl under visible light illumination. Further, the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell photoanode also showed much higher photocurrent than the bare NaNbO3 which suggests that the CdS shell promotes light harvesting efficiency and encourages photogenerated charge carrier separation. The result reveals for the first time that the catalytic properties of NaNbO3 crystals are highly facet dependent. The {100} facet with a higher surface energy of the c-NaNbO3 with a cubic morphology has played a very important role in enhancing the photocatalytic efficiency of c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures compared to the (110) and (114) facets in o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures. Additionally, the solar-to-hydrogen (STH) conversion efficiency of the samples was calculated for water splitting reaction under visible light irradiation of 150 mW cm−2 by employing standardized protocols and following the equation η = Jp[(1.23 − Vapp)/Io] × 100,58 where Jp is the photocurrent density at the measurement applied bias, Vapp is the applied bias (vs. RHE), and Io is the incident light intensity of 100 mW cm−2. In a 0.5 M Na2SO4 solution, the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) potential can be converted from the Ag/AgCl reference electrode as RHE = V (Ag/AgCl) + 0.61 V and this potential was used to calculate the STH conversion efficiency. The STH efficiency is plotted as a function of applied potential (V versus Ag/AgCl) and is shown in Fig. S8.‡ The c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell photoanode exhibits an optimal conversion efficiency of ∼0.06% at 0 V versus Ag/AgCl, whereas the o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell photoanode exhibits a low conversion efficiency of ∼0.01% at a low bias of 0 V versus Ag/AgCl.

To understand the underlying mechanism for the electrical conduction properties in the NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterojunctions, Mott–Schottky (M–S) plots and electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) were obtained. The Mott–Schottky curves (1/C2versus electrode potential) obtained for c-NaNbO3, o-NaNbO3, c-NaNbO3/CdS and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures under dark conditions are shown in Fig. 4(b). To estimate the flat band potential (VFB), the following equation was used:

 
1/c2 = (2/qε0εNd)(Vapp − VFB − kT/q)(2)
where ε, ε0, Nd, Vapp, and VFB represent the dielectric constant of the semiconductor, the permittivity of the vacuum, the donor density, the applied potential and the flat band potential, respectively. kT/q indicates the temperature-dependent term in the Mott–Schottky equation. The value of the intercept of the linear plot at 1/C2 = 0 gives the flat band potential. A positive slope in the Mott–Schottky plots was observed for the c-NaNbO3 and o-NaNbO3 samples at a flat band potential of −0.6 V, which indicates that c-NaNbO3 and o-NaNbO3 are n-type semiconductors with electrons as the majority carriers. The prepared c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures clearly displayed an apparent reversed “V-shaped” p–n heterojunction characteristic with co-existence of positive and negative slopes corresponding to the positive and negative charge carriers with two flat band potential values (Fig. 4(b)).59 A slightly higher slope in Mott–Schottky curves for c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures than bare NaNbO3 (Fig. 4(b)) indicates low donor density. This implies that CdS exhibits p-type characteristics in the PEC cell which is also confirmed by the slope of the Mott–Schottky curve as it has an inverse relationship with the electron density of n-type c-NaNbO3. The c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructure electrode shifts the conduction band edge to a more negative value, −0.92 V versus Ag/AgCl as compared to −0.57 V versus Ag/AgCl for the o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell. This indicates that deposition of CdS on c-NaNbO3 offers better kinetics for water oxidation. In the case of o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, although the thickness of the CdS shell was the same as it was for the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, co-existence of positive and negative slopes for the p–n junction was not clearly visible (Fig. 4(b)). This p–n junction characteristic in the Mott–Schottky plot of c-NaNbO3/CdS helps in the separation of photogenerated charge carriers. Holes are extracted out of NaNbO3 which oxidizes water to oxygen. This is due to the fact that the electrons and holes were trapped in the depletion layer of the p–n junction under dark conditions.

We also propose that the existence of CdS coating over NaNbO3 decreases the reaction barriers for water oxidation and facilitates the transfer of holes to the electrode/electrolyte interface accounting for the greatly improved PEC performance. 3.2 Photocatalytic activity

Visible-light induced photocatalytic degradation of the aqueous MB dye solution led to a significant reduction in the MB concentration when the NaNbO3 based core/shell was used as a photocatalyst. Fig. 5a shows the comparative degradation study of c-NaNbO3/CdS and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures under visible light irradiation. For a better comparison of the photocatalytic efficiency of the synthesised photocatalysts, kinetic analysis of the degradation of the MB dye under visible light irradiation was carried out. The rate constant for dye degradation efficiency with respect to irradiation time is plotted and given in Fig. 5b. The apparent rate constants (Kapp, min−1), determined from regression curves of ln(C0/C) versus irradiation time, are 2.12 × 10−1 and 1.57 × 10−1 min−1 for c-NaNbO3/CdS and o-NaNbO3/CdScore/shell heterostructures, respectively (Fig. 5b). The photodegradation efficiency curve (Fig. S10‡) revealed the degradation of 98.5% of the MB dye in the presence of c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, whereas around 86.4% degradation was observed in the presence of o-NaNbO3/CdS, after 25 min. The above results corroborate that the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell exhibits higher photocatalytic activity than o-NaNbO3/CdS under visible light irradiation. We observe that {100} facets of c-NaNbO3 have been shown to be more photocatalytically active than the (110) and (114) facets of o-NaNbO3 due to their higher surface energy of c-NaNbO3 crystals. Charge separation at the core/shell interface and the large BET surface area (Fig. S11‡) obtained in c-NaNbO3/CdS are also accountable for enhanced catalytic activity. We briefly discuss below the photocatalytic mechanism of the NaNbO3/CdS interface in the core/shell heterostructures. We also observed the role of active species in photocatalytic dye degradation using various scavengers (Fig. S12‡).

The mechanism following the enhanced photoelectrochemical water splitting and photocatalytic dye degradation activity of the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell compared with bare c-NaNbO3, o-NaNbO3 and o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures can be explained by looking at the energy band edge positions at the heterojunction. In the NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures, the conduction band (CB) edge of the CdS sensitizer lies above the CB of NaNbO3. When NaNbO3 and CdS come into mutual contact, the p–n heterojunction forms at the interface in the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell leading to a rise in the Fermi level of CdS. This shifts the whole energy band of CdS up and that of NaNbO3 down until a new stable equilibrium is achieved. In the modified structure, the conduction band (CB) edge of CdS exceeds that of NaNbO3. Hence, upon visible light irradiation, the p–n heterojunction causes effective charge separation and quick transportation of the photogenerated charge carriers,65 in c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures as illustrated in Fig. 6. Under visible light irradiation, photons of higher energy than the band gap energy resulted in the photoexcitation of electrons from the VB to the CB of CdS and left holes in the VB. Due to the lower potential energy of NaNbO3, electrons transfer from the CB of CdS to the CB of NaNbO3 and, conversely, hole transfer from the VB of NaNbO3 to the VB of CdS. Consequently, the photogenerated charge carriers are separated at the NaNbO3/CdS core/shell interfaces, which reduces their recombination probability and allows them to migrate effectively to the surfaces of NaNbO3 and CdS. These photogenerated charge carriers (e− and h+) are directly involved in the redox reactions and result in the formation of active species, which participate in the photocatalytic activity. However, in the o-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell, an n–n type junction is formed and efficient charge separation is not favourable as compared to the c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell hetero-interface. The formation of an n–n type junction between the o-NaNbO3 core and the CdS shell interface was revealed by the Mott–Schottky plot shown in Fig. 4b. Thus, the above mechanism involving extended light absorption in the visible region and further higher surface energy of the facets and large surface area taken together result in enhanced photoelectrochemical and photocatalytic activity of c-NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures.

3.3 Calculation of the surface energy of the facets

To trace the origin of enhanced photocatalytic and photoelectrochemical performance of the NaNbO3/CdS photocatalysts, we have calculated the surface energy of different facets that contribute to catalytic activity by taking into consideration the structural differences between the cubic and orthorhombic phases of NaNbO3, the atomic arrangement in the corresponding unit cells, the coordination geometry of different atoms in the respective facets and the density of dangling bonds at each of these facets. The surface energy of different facets depends on various factors such as crystal structure, type of atoms present, surface atom density, dangling bonds present at the surface and the bond energies of the broken bonds. From this dependence of surface energy on the density of dangling bonds, it becomes obvious that it is difficult to achieve faceting of {hkl} on the fcc cubic metal oxide crystal where {hkl} represents high index planes with at least one Miller index larger than 1. Using a combination of techniques such as X-ray diffraction, high resolution TEM and TEM-SAED, we have been able to investigate the facets responsible for efficient catalysis in the cubic and orthorhombic phases of NaNbO3. We find that in the cubic phase of NaNbO3 leading to a cubic morphology of the crystal, all the six equivalent facets result from the {100} planes, whereas for the cuboctahedron morphology, the (114) and (110) planes of orthorhombic NaNbO3 contribute to the faceting of the crystal as shown in Fig. 7.

Cubic and orthorhombic phases of NaNbO3 show different catalytic activities depending on the surface energies of the different facets involved in these two structures. The cubic and the orthorhombic phases of NaNbO3 have different atomic arrangements in their unit cells. Here, it is important to consider the crystallization of NaNbO3 into crystals having cubic and cuboctahedron morphologies arising from the fundamental difference in the crystal structure with cubic and orthorhombic symmetries.

We could trace the origin of enhanced photocatalytic activity in the c-NaNbO3 core/shell heterostructure to the highly reactive facets of the c-NaNbO3. Catalytic reactions on the free surface of the crystal are initiated on illumination with photon flux during photocatalysis. The catalytic activity depends on the surface energy of various exposed facets of the crystal.69–71 Usually, highly reactive facets contribute to higher photocatalytic activity which results from the atomic arrangement on the surface and the density of the dangling bonds. But, the real problem stems from the fact that during the crystallization process, the growth of highly reactive facets is suppressed owing to the principle of minimization of energy.72,73 Commonly used surfactant ligands tend to cling onto these highly reactive surfaces thereby impeding their rapid growth resulting in crystals with more of low energy facets. Various approaches have been adopted earlier in order to achieve crystals with favourable facets to achieve high photocatalytic activity such as optimizing synthesis conditions such as temperature or pressure, using chemical agents to control the morphology, using ligands that adhere to specific atoms on the surface thereby inhibiting growth of selective facets while allowing stabilization of the crystal with desired facets, etc.74–79

From the calculated surface energies of the facets, we understand that among the three facets considered in the present work, the {100} family of facets of the cubic NaNbO3 possesses the highest surface energy, and hence, it is the most reactive of the three facets. Since all the six facets in the cube are equivalent, all of them would show the same reactivity and there is no scope for internal charge transfer among the facets. However, in the case of orthorhombic NaNbO3, the wide gap between the surface energies of the (110) and (114) facets results in spatial electron–hole charge separation and internal charge transfer occurring among the two facets.81,82 Such a charge transfer creates suitable oxidation and reduction sites on the facets which would assist in enhanced photocatalytic activity in the orthorhombic phase. However, for the cubic phase, the presence of six equivalent highly energetic facets results in substantial enhancement of photoelectrochemical and photocatalytic dye degradation activity over the orthorhombic phase.

4. Conclusions

We have demonstrated the possibility of facet engineering of crystals leading to higher photocatalytic activity in core/shell heterostructures based on the surface energy of different facets. We have shown that it is possible to achieve enhanced and stable current density over long duration in photoelectrochemical water splitting and efficient photocatalytic dye degradation by facet-selective synthesis of the NaNbO3 core material in NaNbO3/CdS core/shell heterostructures where the contribution of different facets between the cubic and orthorhombic phases of NaNbO3 has been studied for such a selection. The results suggest that the highly reactive {001} facets of the cubic phase contribute better towards improved catalytic activity when compared to the (110) and (114) facets of the orthorhombic phase where the spatial charge separation assisted inter-facet charge transfer mechanism plays a crucial role. This study on facet-selective synthesis based on the surface energy of facets brings out the importance of stabilizing the core material with suitable structure and morphology to achieve significant improvement in its activity. The findings of the involvement of highly reactive facets in the cubic phase and efficient charge transfer between different facets in the orthorhombic phase are significant advancements in the understanding of the mechanism of photocatalytic reactions and design of highly efficient photocatalysts that operate in the visible region.
