Abstract

The quest for multifunctional, low-power and environment friendly electronics has brought research on materials to the forefront. For instance, as the emerging field of transparent flexible electronics is set to greatly impact our daily lives, more stringent requirements are being imposed on functional materials. Inherently flexible polymers and metal foil templates have yielded limited success due to their incompatible high-temperature growth and non-transparency, respectively. Although the epitaxial-transfer strategy has shown promising results, it suffers from tedious and complicated lift-off-transfer processes. The advent of graphene, in particular, and 2D layered materials, in general, with ultrathin scalability has revolutionized this field. Herein, we review the direct growth of epitaxial functional oxides on flexible transparent mica substrates via van der Waals heteroepitaxy, which mitigates misfit strain and substrate clamping for soft transparent electronics applications. Recent advances in practical applications of flexible and transparent electronic elements are discussed. Finally, several important directions, challenges and perspectives for commercialization are also outlined. We anticipate that this promising strategy to build transparent flexible optoelectronic devices and improve their performance will open up new avenues for researchers to explore.

1. Introduction

Transparent flexible electronics (TFE) have attracted significant attention from academia and industry alike due to their numerous emerging electronics application prospects in wearables, transparent logic, conformable biomedical sensors, etc. Research discoveries over the past decade have led to the emergence of advanced electronic and optoelectronic systems that can bend, twist, fold, stretch, and wrap conformally onto arbitrary curved surfaces without significantly changing device characteristics. Fully transparent and flexible electronic devices demand transparent active layers, in addition to the transparent electrodes being flexible at the same time. The design and development of transparent flexible (TF) electronic elements necessiates the integration of intended electronic, mechanical, and optical properties. This multi-property coupling requirement is not straightforward, as these properties are often mutually exclusive. Moreso, additional impositions, such as longevity, thermal and chemical stabilities, call for careful consideration. Eventually, research on TFE led to the development of wide variety of new materials, novel structural engineering, and smart fabrication processes that allowed the broad field of soft electronics to make scientific and technological progress in TF electronic devices, including TF conductors, heaters, sensors, field-effect transistors, memory devices, optoelectronic components, nanogenerators, and supercapacitors, beyond the scope of devices built on conventional rigid and inflexible templates. The key challenge is to identify and optimize systems that can simultaneously provide transparency, high performance, high thermal stability and soft compliant mechanics (Fig. 1). TFE devices must be capable of maintaining their functional properties and electronic performance parameters even under mechanical deformation. Correspondingly, devices that measure strain (sensors) or harvest energy from strain (piezoelectric generators) should have consistent response to strain throughout the device lifetime.1
Fig. 1 Schematic of the combined functionalities required in the design and development of transparent flexible electronics applications.

Oxide materials are ubiquitous in modern technology, and their inorganic nature result in environmentally stable devices with long lifetime. Complex oxides are attractive due to the interplay of lattice, charge, orbital, and spin degrees of freedom, which generate a rich spectrum of competing phases and physical properties. The design, synthesis and processing of novel oxide materials with suitable electronic, mechanical and optical properties allow large number of material combinations for oxide electronics. Fully transparent and flexible substrates incorporating transparent oxides are very critical to realize next generation electronic devices with sensing, display, storage, self-powering and portable functionalities. For instance, exploiting transparent conducting oxides for electrical conduction, ferroelectric oxides for memory, piezoelectric oxides for sensing and energy harvesting, and semiconducting or dielectric oxides for high-performance transistors and logic can be envisaged in the context of invisible-flexible electronics. This integration will eventually lead to high-performing, powerful and practical transparent flexible electronics only if single crystalline forms are used. However, a generic approach to build flexible electronics based on epitaxial functional oxides is yet to be developed. In fact, the realization of freestanding single crystalline films would provide an additional degree of freedom to functional oxides (Fig. 2) in addition to the charge, spin, orbital and lattice degrees of freedom.

In this review, devices with the capability of withstanding a level of mechanical strain less than 3% and optical transparency above 60% are mostly discussed. We focus on the current methodologies, technologies, and strategies for the development of TF electronic devices and on the desired performance. First, we introduce the materials, underscoring the importance of two-dimensional materials, followed by fabrication strategies and mechanics relevant to transparent flexible electronics. Second, some of the most recent developments in this emerging field based on van der Waals epitaxy on mica, with some emphasis on the work from our group, are highlighted. Challenges, conclusions and future prospects in the field of transparent and flexible electronics are outlined in the final section.

2. Materials systems

Advances in materials science research arise in the pursuit of new materials and structures with emergent properties and subsequently lead to application-inspired basic research. The choice of materials and fabrication strategies, including systems architectures and processes with suitable optical, electrical and mechanical attributes, will enable future TFE technologies.

2.1. van der Waals oxides

Interest in two-dimensional (2D) materials is increasing daily across the scientific community as an important class of materials for next generation electronics. The discovery of graphene in 20042 led to a resurgence of research activities in 2D layered materials, leading to explosive progress in various aspects, including new material discovery, preparation, property exploration and device applications.2,3 2D materials or van der Waals (vdW) materials refer to structures which present anisotropic bonds, strong intra-layer chemical bonds and weak inter-layer vdW bonds. Based on the chemical element location in the periodic table, the 2D materials library2,4 encompasses group IV (graphene, silicene, germanene), group V (black and blue phosphorene), III–V compounds (h-BN), transition metal dichalcogenides (MoS2, WSe2, MoTe2), III–VI compounds (In2Se3, GaS, GaTe), IV–VI compounds (GeS, SnSe, SnS2), V–VI compounds (Bi2Se3, Bi2Te3), IV–VII compounds (HgI2, PbI2), metal oxides, hydroxides (V2O5, Ni(OH)2) and others. They cover a complete set of metallic, semimetallic, semiconducting, topological insulating and insulating properties with atomic scalability, transparency and mechanical compliance, which can revolutionize applications in electronics, optoelectronics, energy harvesting, catalysis and quantum fields.5 Despite their unique features, 2D oxides have received relatively little attention compared to other members of the 2D material family. Unlike layered van der Waals materials, which have weak inter-layer bonds, layered oxides consist of charged sheets with charged species intercalated between them. They can be further categorized into binary oxides (MoO3, WO3, Ga2O3, and V2O5), clay-minerals (Mica, Kaolinite, Montmorillonite), ABO2-type (LiCoO2), layered oxide perovskites (Ruddlesden–Popper, Dion–Jacobson and Aurivillius phases), Brownmillerite Ca2Fe2O5, etc.6 These 2D nanosheets/membranes find potential applications in supercapacitors, photocatalysts, batteries, sensors and even as templates for epitaxial growth.

2.2. Transparent and flexible substrates

The venture into fabricating transparent flexible electronic device elements must begin with the choice of a proper substrate material, as it plays a pivotal role in terms of meeting cost, performance, reliability, and manufacturing goals for the emergence of TFE as a competitive product. This material must be transparent, flexible, chemically inert, thermally conducting, and electrically insulating, and it must possess high thermal stability.7,8 Currently, flexible device elements can be made directly on metal foil, ultra-thin glass, or a variety of plastic substrates. Thin metal foils are good choices for flexible electronics because they offer superior chemical resistance in a number of environments, act as good barriers and are compatible with high temperature processing. However, they undergo plastic deformations at very small bending strains, restricting their flexibility range; this can be improved by reducing their thicknesses. Moreover, they are opaque and prone to oxidation; pre-treatments such as polishing or planarization to reduce surface roughness and, in some cases, electrical passivation to reduce capacitive coupling between the devices and the conductive metal foil surface are necessary. Ultra-thin glass (50–200 μm) displays properties such as flexibility, diffusion barrier, transparency, high surface quality, chemical and thermal durability; however, its cost, fragility and handling difficulty in manufacturing are liabilities. Polymer substrates exhibit excellent flexibility and have light weights compared to glass substrates; however, their sensitivity to heat treatment and chemicals, susceptibility to gas permeation and large thermal expansion coefficients are major drawbacks.9 Buffer layers are used to avoid moisture permeation and develop smooth surfaces.10 Most polymer substrates suffer from structural instability and melt at 300 °C, restricting the growth processes to low temperatures.11 The non-availability of an inorganic flexible substrate paved the way for electronics built using organic materials on polymer/plastic substrates with excellent mechanical attributes to prosper in the past; however, the high performance applications of these materials were limited due to their intrinsic thermal instabilities and low mobilities. Moreover, crack formation during repeated flexing, ingress of moisture, etc., and electrical reliability issues related to polymer degradation, continued phase segregation, surface passivation, contact diffusion, and low-efficiency are important challenges faced by organic electronics9. 2D mica overcomes the shortcomings of the aforementioned transparent flexible substrates. Mica qualifies as an ideal substrate for transparent-flexible electronic device applications due to its 2D structure with atomically flat surface as well as its transparent, elastic, flexible, chemically inert, non-toxic, thermally conductive, and electrically insulating properties. Also, it is nonmagnetic, lightweight, biocompatible, inexpensive and compatible with all modern thin film deposition techniques.12 Mica unanimously stands out as the best flexible substrate to date for realizing high performance flexible electronics applications, and it will be shown in following sections that the direct growth of functional oxides on mica,12–105 currently serves as the best possible strategy. 3. Fabrication strategies

Soft technology platforms using flexible and stretchable electronics are built via complementary strategies of using structures or materials that can flex or stretch.108 One strategy involves new structural layout designs in conventional materials, while the other relies on the design of new materials in conventional layouts. Therefore, the fabrication of soft transparent electronic devices employs either geometric engineering of transparent and flexible materials on TF substrates or direct deposition of intrinsically transparent and flexible materials on TF substrates. The former approach of structural engineering exhibits high stability but limited flexibility, while the latter approach of intrinsic TF electronic devices shows limited stability but high flexibility. Therefore, combining smart structural engineering of more compliant TF materials to fabricate TF electronic devices may be the next candidate for improvements in the mechanical durability and electrical stability of TF electronic devices.

3.1. Direct growth

First, the direct growth involves the deposition of functional elements on already available flexible substrates via conventional and van der Waals epitaxies. Epitaxial growth is a method of depositing a film of specific crystalline orientation on a crystalline substrate. It includes liquid phase epitaxy (LPE), chemical vapour deposition (CVD), pulsed laser deposition (PLD), sputtering, atomic layer deposition (ALD) and molecular beam epitaxy (MBE). Amongst transparent-flexible substrates, 2D mica has emerged as an effective template to realize TFE device elements. A number of growth methods to grow functional oxides on mica are being explored. To date, PLD, MBE, sputtering, and CVD methods have emerged as popular means to achieve van der Waals oxide heteroepitaxy on mica, as indicated in Table 3. In the recent past, the field of MICAtronics12–16 has evolved as an effective approach to realize single crystalline functional elements on mica substrates via van der Waals epitaxy to exploit their best performance metrics and flexibility at the same time. Mica, the most abundant subgroup of phyllosilicates with 2D layered structures, is commonly used as an insulating material, a dielectric in capacitors and an unmatched substrate for the study of 2D ion diffusion, crystal nucleation, surface conductivity phenomena and surface force measurements. Recently, ultrathin mica sheets were used as insulators for large area organic thin film transistor arrays with promising implications in organic and inorganic electronics to minimize coupling capacitances.8 Muscovite [KAl2(Si3Al)O10(OH)2], biotite [K(Mg,Fe)3(AlSi3O10)(F,OH)2], and phlogopite [KMg3(AlSi3O10)(OH)2] are common mica variants which exhibit monoclinic structures with a tendency towards the pseudo-hexagonal structure on the basal plane. Each structural unit of muscovite (∼1 nm) contains an aluminium octahedral (AlO4(OH)2) layer sandwiched between two silicate (SiO4) tetrahedral sheets wherein Si ions are partially replaced by Al ions (Al/Si = 1/3), resulting in a net negative charge. These tetrahedral–octahedral–tetrahedral layers with strong covalent bonding within them are weakly held together by interlayer (K+) cations, maintaining charge neutrality and separating each unit by the van der Waals gap (Fig. 4a). Atomically flat surfaces with equal but randomly distributed K+ ions are obtained by cleaving mica along this vdW gap (Fig. 4b).

MICAtronics, or vdW epitaxy on mica, is likely to have a significant impact on high-performance flexible optoelectronics, sensors, memories, energy-harvesting devices, etc. In particular, 2D materials or mica are promising candidates for miniaturized and high-performance electronic and optoelectronic devices due to their atomically flat, ultrathin natures and lack of dangling bonds. This lack of surface bonds results in a new form of epitaxy, namely van der Waals epitaxy,109–115 which offers several benefits over conventional epitaxy. Normally, van der Waals epitaxy is typified by a consistent over layer-substrate orientation relationship with sharp, incoherent and incommensurate interfaces. These interfaces show no excessive strain induced by the epitaxy; also, this epitaxy persists even for systems with large lattice mismatches (as large as 60%)116,117 and different symmetries, allowing material combinations that are inaccessible to conventional epitaxy. vdW epitaxy can be achieved in 2D materials which naturally exhibit no surface dangling bonds or in 3D materials whose surfaces are treated to passivate surface dangling bonds. The conventional epitaxy characterized by strong interfacial chemical bonds (covalent, ionic and metallic) of the constituents presents larger substrate clamping effect than the van der Waals epitaxy characterized by weaker interfacial van der Waals bonds (Fig. 4c and d). Generally, the nucleation and growth in conventional epitaxy (3D/3D) involves chemisorption, while that in van der Waals epitaxy (2D/2D) involves a physisorption process. In quasi-vdW epitaxy (3D/2D and 2D/3D), a strong interfacial layer is initially formed via directional bonds on which the film further grows homoepitaxially. The vdW bonds present weak inter-molecular/ionic coulombic interactions wherein there is no electron transfer or sharing between ions or molecules participating in the bond.118 Therefore, vdW epitaxy as an alternative strategy towards the successful heteroepitaxial growth of planar and nonplanar geometries (nanowire arrays, tripods, tetrapods and complex nanostructures) will broaden the domain of thin film heteroepitaxy. It also provides an opportunity to investigate the fundamental mechanisms that govern nucleation and growth in view of the inherent anisotropic interactions (strong intra-planar and weak inter-planar) of the 2D materials. In short, vdWE is set to expand the functionalities of existing heterostructures for potential applications while enabling the investigation of emergent phenomena originating due to the proximity of 2D materials with 2D materials, 3D materials, and combinations thereof.119 A summary of soft transparent yet epitaxial elements realized via van der Waals oxide heteroepitaxy on mica is presented in Table 3.

3.2. Growth thinning

Second, the growth-thinning technique employs the growth of functional elements on a rigid support and consequent thinning down to render them flexible. Thinning down the substrate post-device fabrication via ion-etching,120 polishing, grinding and thinning-by-dicing121 tends to damage the on-chip devices, reducing the yield and increasing the cost. Alternately, flexible substrates obtained from rigid substrates by exfoliation or spalling processes122,123 suffer from limited bendability, opaqueness of the released portion and use of costly substrates. Similarly, anodic etching,124 which creates a double layer of Si and introduces porosity in the bottom layer, involves unfamiliar procedures; thus, integration with modern fabrication processes is difficult. Another approach of combining the mechanical and electrical properties of silicon with flexible polymer substrates125 has made significant progress.

3.3. Growth-transfer

Third, in the growth-transfer technique, removal of the epitaxial functional element from the rigid support post-growth process is achieved via laser, physical and chemical lift-off/release methods, and the element is ultimately transferred onto a flexible template. The epitaxial growth-transfer technique relies on the lift-off of freestanding single crystalline films by selectively etching the buffer layer using acid,126,127 dissolving the substrate128 or sacrificial buffer layer129,130 using water, or melting the film-substrate interface using laser131 and ion implantations132 to transfer them onto any desired flexible substrates. Despite successful prototype demonstrations and the advantage of reusable substrates, the epitaxial-transfer process (a) uses sophisticated and expensive equipment, rendering it costly, (b) has inherent unpredictability of the transfer process due to the limited options for complete removal of the finished devices, and (c) is applicable to a limited set of materials.

However, both growth-thinning and growth-transfer strategies are multistep, tedious and difficult to generalize to a wide range of oxides. 2D materials with atomic flat surfaces without dangling bonds or charge traps and ultra-thin thicknesses offer novel mechanics and architecture designs as well as endless material combinations2,5,108,133–136 to achieve stable device performance under stretching, flexing or twisting. In addition to (Q)vdW epitaxy based on 2D mica for epitaxial flexible electronic applications, remote epitaxy based on popular 2D graphene has recently emerged as a promising strategy. In remote epitaxy, epitaxy of functional oxides occurs through remote atomic interactions between the oxide overlayer and substrate via graphene. The inability of the weak vdW potential of graphene to screen the strong potential fields of many substrates allows the epitaxial growth. Eventually, the functional layer on graphene is mechanically exfoliated and transferred onto a flexible substrate of interest, which offers cost saving advantage in terms of the reusable graphene-coated substrate.119,137,138 However, the quality of graphene on the substrate determines the crystalline quality of the epilayer and exfoliation area. Also, mastering of nucleation and growth is still required to obtain perfectly planar films via this method.119 Many interesting oxide materials as well as the effects of nanoscale defects in graphene on the functional layer properties remain unexplored in this 2D material-based remote epitaxy. Among the strategies discussed above, simple direct growth of epitaxial functional elements on flexible mica with pristine properties offers new research avenues for the design and development of next-generation smart electronic devices.

4. Applications

A typical soft electronic system will comprise the following elements: (i) human–machine interface (display, sensor, actuator and functional system); (ii) signal control, processing and storage (logic circuits and memory); (iii) communication (radiofrequency and microwave circuits); and (iv) power management (battery, solar cells, supercapacitors and energy harvestors). All these elements are integrated on a soft template.

4.1. Conductors

Transparent electrodes are essential elements in information (monitors) and energy (window glass, solar cells and photovoltaics) technologies that meet the requirements of the transport of electrons and photons.139 The attributes of flexibility, transparency, and electrical conduction are the defining features of flexible transparent conducting oxides (TCO). Therefore, the choice of materials for the development of TCO is critical, as they are the primary elements which influence the optical, electrical, and mechanical performance of flexible optoelectronic devices. Flexible TCOs-indium tin oxide (ITO) and Al-doped zinc oxide (AZO) fabricated on mica25 reveal only ITO(111) and AZO(001) orientations on mica, as shown in Fig. 5a, and 6-fold symmetry in the phi-scan measurement for the ITO{440} and AZO{101} reflections (Fig. 5b), implying epitaxial growth. The high-resolution cross-sectional transmission electron microscopy study showed semi-coherent, defect-free and clear interfaces. Average visible transmittances of 68%–97% for ITO films and 75%–90% for AZO films on mica with optical band gaps of 4.1(2) eV for AZO and 3.7(2) eV for ITO were obtained. The photographs of 200 nm ITO/mica and AZO/mica in the inset of Fig. 5(b) demonstrate the high optical transparency of these heterostructures. The mobility of flexible TCO/mica as a function of carrier concentration was on par with that of epitaxial TCO counterparts grown on rigid single crystalline substrates. Comparable figure of merit values (ΦTC (Ω−1) = T10/Rs, where T is the average optical transmittance and Rs is the sheet resistance) with those of their rigid counterparts assert the superior optoelectronic performance brought about by the improved structural quality. The sheet resistance remains constant down to 4 mm (7 mm) and 6 mm (9 mm) bending radii during both the compressive and tensile deformations, respectively, for ITO(AZO). It is evident from Fig. 5(c) that the critical bending radius of curvature decreases with the TCO thickness. It is noteworthy that the critical bending strains for TCO under compressive strain (0.75% for ITO and 0.80% for AZO) are higher than the tensile strains (0.41% for ITO and 0.46% for AZO), which is comparable with reported values. Furthermore, the AZO/mica exhibited very stable sheet resistance even after 2000 bending cycles under a radius of curvature of 8 mm, while ITO/mica exhibited an 10% increase in its resistance beyond 100 cycles. The long-term optoelectronic stability of TCO under mechanical flexing was marked by stable conductivity retention at room temperature in open atmosphere for longer durations of time. Moreover, these heterostructures maintain inherent metallic character under bending even at elevated temperatures (∼350 °C) due to the higher melting point of the underlying mica, unlike conventional polymer substrates, which curl11 at temperatures above 150 °C and eventually lead to device failure. They also exhibit robust conductivity against various chemicals, such as acetone, ethanol, xylene, toluene and chlorobenzene, for at least 10 min. The aforementioned mechanical, thermal and chemical stabilities of these heterostructures open up new avenues for transparent functional devices and, hence, their commercialization. Relying on the high thermal conductivity and low specific heat capacity of mica, an AZO/mica TF heater (Fig. 5d) with an ultra-high heating rate (200 °C s−1), 5–50 times faster than that of conventional flexible heaters, was fabricated.57 TF heaters57,64 can be employed in defogging/de-icing transparent windows and as potential heating sources for automobiles, displays, sensors, reaction cells and microchips. Semiconducting perovskite Ba0.96La0.04SnO3 on flexible mica with a BaTiO3 buffer layer also displayed promising transparent conducting oxide properties.56

These transitions are sensitive to thermal, electrical, optical and strain stimuli and have potential applications in smart windows, thermal switches, ultrafast switching and thermo-optical modulators. 4.3. Sensors

Modern resistive flexible sensors based on polymeric matrices and conductive materials (graphene, CNTs, metal nanoparticles, etc.) suffer from sensitivity, repeatability, reproducibility and lifetime issues. A transparent flexible ZnO/mica65 sensor was demonstrated based on the inherent piezoresistive effect of ZnO. The heterostructure exhibits neither strain nor the substrate clamping effect, with the sharp interface expected of a van der Waals heteroepitaxy. The electromechanical performance in Fig. 7a–c presents the following salient features: (i) very large resistivity changes of −80% and 240% in flex-in and flex-out modes, respectively, for a 1 μm-thick film. (ii) The heterostructure maintains (a) a retention of >105 s and (b) a durability of 104 bending cycles for a bending radius of 5 mm. This very large resistivity change is attributed to changes in the carrier concentration (mobility), which increases (decreases) and decreases in the flex-in and flex-out modes, respectively. Reversible piezopotential developed along the mechanical strain direction results in the accumulation of depletion of carriers at the ZnO surface, acting like gating. This conjuncture was further supported by XPS analysis and bending model simulations using the finite element method. A gauge factor of >1000 was achieved, which is the best amongst oxide systems. This demonstration marks an important milestone in the field of TF piezotronics in terms of both science and technology. Another prototype strain sensor52 based on polycrystalline BaNb0.5Ti0.5O3/mica (Fig. 7d) robust over a wide temperature range (20–773 K) for potential high-temperature applications in interstellar probes, polar exploration, petrochemical and metal smelting; stable over 5000 bending cycles, low-power consumption (0.96 μW cm−2) and a gauge factor of 27.5 (best among flexible strain sensors) was demonstrated. Typical sensing applications, including human body joint motion monitoring and multimodal sensing in the presence of light and mechanical bending stimuli, were performed successfully (Fig. 7e).

4.4. Memories

Flexible memory is an integral part of flexible electronic systems for data processing, information storage, and communication. The room temperature structural characterization revealed the epitaxial nature of the Pb1−xLax(ZryTi1−y)O3 (PLZT)/ITO/ZnO/mica heterostructure.21 This transparent FE capacitor had a saturation polarization (Psat) of 84 μC cm−2, remanent polarization (Pr) of 41 μC cm−2 and coercive field (EC) of 290 kV cm−1 at 25 °C, which remained constant up to 200 °C, as shown in Fig. 8a; this underscores its potential for high-temperature TFE applications. These transparent capacitors are robust against mechanical strain, wherein no noticeable changes were observed in the values of Psat, Pr and Ec when both compressive and tensile bending strains down to 5 mm were applied. Neither the bending cycles in both compressive and tensile modes (Fig. 8b) nor the radiation dosage affected the properties. The heterostructure retained its optical transmittance of ∼80% over a wide range in the ultraviolet-visible-infrared regime before (inset of Fig. 8b) and after the tests and displayed no visible damage or cracks, emphasizing the importance of vdWE in mitigating the mechanical strain effect. In addition, the transparent ferroelectric PLZT/ITO/ZnO/mica heterostructure generated an open circuit voltage of 0.3 V and short-circuit current of 4 nA under bending, suggesting its potential application in transparent antiradiative piezoelectric devices, self-powered sensors and nanogenerators. In another study,59 a TF ferroelectric Pb(Zr0.1Ti0.9)O3/ITO/YSZ/mica heterostructure with 73% transmittance, Psat = 71 μC cm−2, Pr = 39 μC cm−2 and EC = 220 kV cm−1 retaining stable FE metrics up to 150 °C was demonstrated. Recently, vdWE of ferroelectric ε-Ga2O3 on mica143via chemical vapor deposition method attractive for flexible deep-ultra violet optoelectronic devices such as solar blind detectors was demonstrated. A polycrystalline ITO/Bi3.25La0.75Ti3O12(BLT)/Ag-ITO/mica transparent ferroelectric memory with data writing/erasing speed <50 ns and 108 cycles that are stable up to 150 °C was shown. An additional functionality of nondestructive optical reading of ±P states by photovoltaic current was displayed, unlike the destructive reading by applying bias and detecting polarization switching current with a further rewrite step to recover initial information in conventional FeRAM devices.142 When an external bias of 8 V induces the −P state, the ISC increases to −0.43 nA. The +P state induced by a −8 V bias changes the ISC to 0.35 nA. The −P and +P states can be identified by detecting the corresponding ISC under illumination (Fig. 8c and d).

Memristors or resistive switching memories can store and process information by recording changes in resistance states under an external electric field. Therefore, they have been widely investigated as one of the most promising candidates for next-generation logical, computing, synaptic, and non-volatile memory devices due to their simple structures, small sizes, high speeds, low power consumption, good endurance, long retention and cost-effectiveness. A transparent flexible memristor based on van der Waals heteroepitaxial growth composed of an AZO/NiO/AZO (ANA) sandwich structure on a muscovite substrate was fabricated19 owing to the wide band gap (3.6–4.0 eV), high-temperature stability and reliable memory characteristics of NiO. The XRD results revealed the presence of three sets of structural domains of NiO, with a small out-of-plane epitaxial compression of 0.33% strain. The high-resolution TEM images revealed defect-free and coherent NiO/AZO and AZO/muscovite interfaces.

The different thermal activation energies for HRS (0.66 eV) confirm that different carrier conduction mechanisms are prevalent in these states. More so, the thermal evolution of the resistive switching behavior of a transparent ANA/mica memristor (Fig. 9a) at different temperatures ranging from 25 °C to 185 °C reveals that the VThreshold (Vth) and VHold (Vh) resistances in the high-resistance state (RHRS) and low-resistance state (RLRS) decrease gradually with temperature, such that the resistance ratio RHRS/RLRS ∼105 at 25 °C decreases to 103 at 185 °C. The optical transmittance of the ANA/muscovite heterostructure is 80% in the visible region, and the inset shows a photograph of the ANA/muscovite memristor. Not only were stable endurance (up to 103 cycles) and retention (∼105 s) of the memristor maintained under compressive and tensile bent states down to 5 mm radius of bending (Fig. 9b), but these properties were also retained even after undergoing as many as 1000 mechanical bending cycles (r = 6.5 mm). Therefore, the TF ANA/mica memristor with reliable memory characteristics (resistance ON/OFF ratio >105, endurance of 105 cycles and long relation time of 105 s) under thermal and mechanical constraints is set to offer new pathways in the development of next generation memory, logical and computing devices. Transparent resistive random access memory (ReRAM)58 based on a polycrystalline ITO/Ag/BaTi0.95Co0.05O3−δ/Ag-ITO/mica heterostructure exhibiting uniaxial resistive-switching behavior with fast response speed (50 ns), long retention time (>106 s) and low operation voltage (<2.8 V) was reported. The dependence of RHRS and RLRS on the retention time and writing/erasing cycles showed a RHRS/RLRS of ∼5000 even for the dynamic bending process with a bending radius of curvature of 2.2 mm (Fig. 9c). Moreover, the I–V curves of the memory cell remained stable even after 14400 bending cycles (Fig. 9d).

4.5. Fuel cells

Fuel cells convert the chemical energy of fuel into electrical energy and have higher efficiency than conventional power generators. Solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) employing oxide ion conductor-based electrolytes allow oxygen to directly permeate into the fuel electrode and achieve direct oxidation of various fuels. Oxide ion conductors show better stability than proton-conducting polymers under the same operating conditions. Corrosion-resistant and chemically stable YSZ is a popular ionic conductor with potential applications in the development of solid oxide fuel cells, membranes, salteries and electrochromic windows. XRD studies confirmed the growth of high-quality epitaxial YSZ with a multi-domain feature. The cross-sectional TEM image in Fig. 10a–c reveals a sharp and defect-free YSZ/mica32 interface. The ionic conductivity of the YSZ/mica heterostructure in Fig. 10(d) is higher than the bulk single crystalline counterpart due to the redistribution of ionic defects at the hetero-interface, which breaks the crystalline symmetry. It is also clear that there are two activation energies in the low and high temperature regions which have contributions from the bulk and interface, respectively. Moreover, the high and stable values of ionic conductivity are maintained even under compressive and tensile bending strains up to a radius of bending of ∼10 nm. The low Ea (∼0.75 eV) and high Ea (∼0.125 eV) energies obtained using the thermally activated Arrhenius law remain constant. Furthermore, the ionic conductivity and activation energies remain within 10% of their unbent state values even after 800 bending cycles, as shown in Fig. 10(e), and maintain their retention times of 105 s, implying their long-term stability. The inset shows an actual image of the YSZ/mica heterostructure, which has transmittance as high as 90%. This heterostructure has also shown promising results as a gas sensor.

4.6. Energy harvesting and storage systems

Ever-increasing demand for energy consumption and depletion of fossil fuels has necessitated the development of clean and renewable energy sources. Conversion of solar power to chemical energy based on solar-driven photo-electrochemical (PEC) water splitting has emerged as an eco-friendly alternative. A semi-transparent (T ∼70%) but epitaxial γ-Fe2O3/ZnO/mica photoelectrode-based PEC cell displayed photocurrent three times higher than that of pure ZnO and Fe2O3 electrodes under visible-light irradiation.144 The change of photocurrent collected at 1.0 V vs. Ag/AgCl under simulated sunlight irradiation (Fig. 11a) shows retention of stable photocurrent down to a smallest bending radius of 3.5 mm in both bending modes. The heteroepitaxial photoelectrodes retain their photocurrent after continuous bending cycles (>3000 cycles; Fig. 11b) after being bent to a 5 mm radius. The photoelectrodes also exhibited long-term PEC stability (9 h) in bent conditions and robust photocurrent against prolonged light irradiation. The field of electrochemical energy storage with long life, high energy/power density, and ecofriendliness has emerged as another promising alternative for energy-storage conversion for portable electronic devices. A TF supercapacitor using NiO-coated AZO/mica nanorod array66-based electrodes with enhanced energy storage performance was demonstrated. The geometry of the nanorods offers the advantages of large surface area, short carrier diffusion length and freedom of volume expansion. A combination of SEM and TEM with EDs confirmed the epitaxial nature and the chemical composition of the NiO-coated AZO nanorods. The as-prepared epitaxial NiO-coated AZO nanorods on muscovite (Fig. 11c) with 80% transmittance showed stable conductivity under bent conditions down to a 5 mm radius of bending. The cyclic voltammetry investigations of the TF supercapacitor with Pt, Ag/AgCl and NiO-AZO/muscovite electrodes in KOH, LiClO4, Na2SO4, H2SO4 and Zn2SO4 electrolytes displayed ideal charge–discharge cycles under flat and bent conditions with a large areal specific capacitance of 3.4 mF cm−2 and a long life of 1000 cycles (Fig. 11d). In addition, they serve as promising electrodes in hybrid Zn-ion batteries with interesting electrochemical characteristics even under mechanical constraints, reaching 92% coulombic efficiency. Dielectric mica displayed an excellent energy density of around 11.27 J cm−3 in the temperature range of 50 °C–200 °C with a charge–discharge efficiency of 94.3% at the breakdown strength of 580 MV m−1, which is best among known conventional TF energy storage systems at elevated temperatures; therefore, it is a sought-after material for high-temperature energy storage.145

4.7. Photo-luminescent systems

Electric field-controlled optical properties of functional ferroelectrics are attractive for tunable light sources, photodetectors and biological imaging. Reversible manipulation of photoluminescence (PL) properties by an electric field via strain mediation in ferroelectric photoluminescent films has significant implications in tunable transparent memory, sensors and displays. Purely mechanical strain-controlled PL response in Eu-doped 0.94Bi0.5Na0.5TiO3-0.06BaTiO3 (BNTBT:Eu) transparent ferroelectric films on mica has provided an additional degree of freedom to realize these advanced applications.54 A transparent BNTBT:Eu/mica heterostructure had a bulk lattice constant (strain-free, εi = 0) atypical of vdWE. The PL emission peaks centred at 585, 624, 654 and 690 nm due to the 5D0 → 7F1, 5D0 → 7F2, 5D0 → 7F3 and 5D0 → 7F4 transitions of Eu3+, respectively, increase in intensity and split into two peaks as the bending radius of curvature approaches ±2 mm (Fig. 12a).

5. Challenges

As novel applications that exploit the functional properties of oxides on 2D templates continue to grow out of fundamental research, many technologically important multifunctional oxides and their composites remain unexplored. The nature and strength of the (Q)vdW interfacial bonds in a vdW heteroepitaxy play crucial roles in determining the electromechanical coupling and, hence, the device performance. However, a theoretical understanding beyond vdW interactions on the interfacial characteristics of (Q)vdW is lacking. Therefore, detailed investigations on the effects of inherent anisotropic interactions (strong intra-planar and weak inter-planar) in 2D materials and the fundamental mechanisms that govern the nucleation and growth of overlayers (2D or 3D) are necessary to exploit the full potential of vdW heteroepitaxy. Also, more experimental and theoretical studies are needed to unravel the physiochemical-mechanical interactions that lead to device failure and to develop suitable strategies. Therefore, in situ characterization tools, control of interfacial properties and effective computational methods to devise more (Q)vdW heterostructures along with scalable synthetic routes and understanding of their structure–property correlations will be indispensable in translating their potential into practical high-performance soft technology. Although a large number of prototypical soft electronic devices have been demonstrated in research laboratories, it remains challenging to understand the role of defects, disentangle and model the complex physics of cooperative quantum phases, and identify and translate their most compelling industrial applications. Development of large-scale synthesis and assembly techniques for high-quality films will not only outperform conventional electronics in performance and function but also open up new markets for the electronic industry.

6. Conclusions and outlook

The vision of TFE applications lies beyond the reach of current optoelectronic technologies, and their commercial viability is difficult to assess. Over the past few years, both organic and inorganic van der Waals heterostructures, owing to the multifunctional platforms they provide due to their artificial stacking characteristics, have significantly contributed to developing new strategies for high-performance TFE. The opportunities in the design and development of high-performance and low-power applications stems from the natural thickness scalability of 2D material-based flexible electronics. Combining 2D materials with traditional materials may be the best option to realize practical applications, and the range of material combinations within the scope of van der Waals heteroepitaxy on mica is very broad. New concepts in materials science based on vdW epitaxy on mica, such as those illustrated in previous sections, can rapidly open up new possibilities in device engineering with potential implications in areas of healthcare, such as epidermal and implantable functional membranes to monitor physiological status, as well as in lighting and photovoltaic systems. For instance, invisible sensing and processing electronic systems on the human body to harvest energy from motion can be envisioned. Mica can also serve as a template to build high-performance transient electronics. In all cases, fundamental developments in existing and new materials, including composite designs, growth techniques, processing strategies, integration schemes, and mechanical-electrical-optical properties, will determine the rate of progress. Undiscovered phenomena, design of innovative functionalities, and commercialization are expected to emerge in the near future, leading to scientific contributions and novel applications for next-generation electronics. The ultimate goal of realizing soft transparent smart electronics with excellent chemical and thermal stabilities appears to be not much further away. By summarizing the recent progress made in this field, we anticipate that this field of MICAtronics will flourish, with significant contributions extending from scientific value to practical applications in the foreseeable future.
