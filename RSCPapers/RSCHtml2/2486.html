Abstract

Producing green hydrogen fuel from water is one of the most promising strategies for sustainable and clean energy supply in the future. Seeking for highly efficient, noble-metal-free and stable catalysts for typical electrochemical catalytic (EC), photoelectrochemical (PEC) and photocatalytic (PC) hydrogen production has attracted much attention. In this work, for the first time, we rationally designed and fabricated an innovative class of CdS@Ni3S2 coreâ€“shell nanorod arrays (CSNS) on Ni foam. Thanks to the synergistic effect and catalytic role transformation between CdS nanorods and Ni3S2 nanosheets, CSNS was determined to be a new type of multifunctional noble-metal-free catalyst, which exhibited highly stable and efficient H2 production properties in EC, PEC and PC hydrogen production systems. Specifically, with an optimal Ni3S2 shell thickness, CSNS achieved its highest PEC activity with an applied bias photon-to-current conversion efficiency (ABPE) of 7.9% and an extremely high H2 production rate of 112.5 Î¼mol cmâˆ’2 minâˆ’1. Moreover, a series of inconsistent catalytic behaviors were found during the EC, PEC and PC hydrogen production procedures. By establishing three corresponding charge separation and transfer models, the underlying catalytic mechanisms and the dominant role transformations between CdS and Ni3S2 in these three catalytic systems were systematically investigated. This study provides a new strategy and guidelines for searching for suitable photocatalysts/electrocatalysts and coreâ€“shell multifunctional catalysts which can be used in an optional H2 production system.

1. Introduction

To meet the increasing global energy demand and address the environmental pollution issues caused by the consumption of fossil fuels, exploring alternative energy production and conversion systems for renewable and clean energy sources (such as hydrogen energy) has sparked tremendous research attention.1â€“3 Generating green hydrogen fuel from water has been considered as one of the most promising strategies for sustainable and clean energy supply in the future.4â€“6 To realize this goal, there are three typical approaches including electrochemical catalytic (EC), photoelectrochemical (PEC) and photocatalytic (PC) water splitting strategies.7â€“10

Incorporating the cathodic hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) with the anodic oxygen evolution reaction (OER), EC water splitting provides a simple method for mass production of hydrogen fuel.11,12 The key technology in this field is the development of low cost, efficient and stable electrocatalysts that can achieve a high current density at a moderate HER or OER overpotential.13â€“16 In recent years, in order to reduce the utilization of high cost precious metal electrocatalysts, such as Pt and RuO2, earth-abundant non-noble Ni- and Ni-alloy based electrocatalysts, which are commonly directly grown on nickel foam, have been intensively investigated and exhibited improved EC catalytic performance.17 For example, a number of Ni-based metal phosphides,18â€“21 sulfides22,23 and selenides24 have been investigated as HER or OER electrocatalysts. In particular, Ni3S2, which can be used as both a HER and an OER electrocatalyst in alkaline electrolysis devices, is extremely attractive, because such bifunctional electrocatalysts can not only simplify the EC water splitting systems, but are also feasible for the industrial application of water splitting technology.25â€“27 Recently, Feng et al. synthesized Ni3S2 nanosheet arrays on nickel foam, and they proved that these Ni3S2 nanosheets with exposed high-index {210} facets had a large number of reactive sites and could provide enhanced catalytic activity toward both the HER and OER in alkaline electrolyte.12,28 Although significant progress has been made for these Ni3S2 and other Ni-based electrocatalysts, it is very hard to further increase the EC activity due to the limited reactive sites caused by the fixed surface area on Ni foam.29 Therefore, in pursuit of an ideal EC activity, it is highly desirable to fabricate novel nanostructured Ni foam supported electrocatalysts with sufficient active surfaces and fast charge transfer capacity.

Superior to the traditional EC water splitting technology, the power-saving PEC and PC hydrogen generation strategies combined with the inexhaustible supply of solar energy have stood at the forefront in the field of energy conversion.29â€“32 However, up to now, the solar to hydrogen (STH) efficiency and stability of these PEC and/or PC systems are still far from meeting the practical demand of hydrogen production on a large scale. To realize a high STH efficiency, the employment of appropriate semiconductors with suitable band gaps and optoelectronic properties is extremely important. Cadmium sulfide (CdS), especially one dimensional (1D) ordered CdS nanoarray materials, is an excellent candidate,33â€“35 because it can not only remarkably enhance the light absorption and scattering efficiency, but can also provide a fast electron transport pathway for the photogenerated electrons.36,37 Unfortunately, the serious self-photocorrosion of CdS largely hinders its photoactivity, photo-stability and practical application.38â€“40 Moreover, like most other photocatalysts, pure CdS material is kinetically incapable of producing hydrogen from water. It is widely accepted now that CdS-based photocatalysts or photoelectrodes should be integrated with hydrogen or oxygen evolution cocatalysts to achieve efficient solar-driven water splitting.41â€“44 In recent years, it has been found that many of the HER or OER electrocatalysts can be used as highly efficient cocatalysts to enhance the photocatalytic properties of CdS.45â€“47 Especially, Ni-based metal sulfides can significantly improve the PC hydrogen generation properties of CdS.48â€“51 Despite these exciting advancements, most of the reported nickel sulfide/CdS composites always exhibit irregular alignment and/or serious aggregation, resulting in small specific surface area and insufficient catalytically active sites.52â€“54 Thus, it is quite desirable to engineer well-confined nickel-sulfide/CdS nanoarray catalysts with sufficient active sites and fast charge transport abilities to achieve a stable and high PEC or PC water splitting performance.

Based on the above considerations, herein, for the first time, we rationally designed and fabricated an innovative class of Ni3S2 {210} high-index facet exposed and shell thickness controllable CdS@Ni3S2 coreâ€“shell nanorod arrays on Ni foam (CSNS) by a very simple one-pot hydrothermal treatment. The unique CSNS possesses two main advantages in enhancing both the EC performance of Ni3S2 and the PEC/PC activity of CdS: (i) in the EC water splitting system, the well-aligned 1D CdS nanorod array cores can serve as supports, thus enhancing the electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) of the Ni3S2 nanosheets; (ii) during the PEC or PC water splitting process, the outer layers of Ni3S2 can act as cocatalysts, which effectively suppress the photocorrosion of CdS and significantly enhance the photoactivity and stability of CSNS. Thanks to the advantageous heterostructural features and judicious integration between Ni3S2 and CdS functional components, the as-prepared CSNS samples can be employed as multifunctional catalysts in EC, PEC and PC hydrogen production systems, and they exhibit highly efficient and stable hydrogen production properties. Interestingly, it was found that the shell thickness of Ni3S2 had a prominent effect on the final catalytic performance of the as-prepared samples in the three different hydrogen production systems. The underlying catalytic mechanisms were systematically investigated, and three corresponding models were established for further understanding the interfacial charge separation and transfer properties during the catalytic process.

2.1 Preparation of CdS@Ni3S2 nanorod arrays on Ni foam (CSNS)

The CdS@Ni3S2 nanorod arrays were in situ grown on commercial Ni foam by a facile hydrothermal synthesis strategy. Firstly, a piece of Ni foam (2.5 Ã— 3 cm2) was sequentially cleaned using acetone, deionized water, 0.1 M HCl and alcohol for 5 min to remove the surface oxide layer. Then the cleaned Ni foam was submerged into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave containing 30 mL of 20 mM Cd(NO3)2Â·4H2O, 20 mM thiourea and 17.5 mM glutathione solution. The autoclave was sealed and maintained at 200 Â°C for 3 h. After the autoclave was cooled to room temperature, the samples were washed with water and ethanol and dried in air.

3.1 Morphology and composition

The morphology characterization of the obtained CSNS sample was first investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM). 3.2 Formation mechanism of coreâ€“shell CdS@Ni3S2 nanorod arrays

According to the preparation process and the characterization results of CdS@Ni3S2 nanorod arrays, the formation mechanism of the sample can be explained as follows (Scheme 1). (i) At the early stage of the hydrothermal treatment, thiourea was firstly decomposed to release NH3, H2S and CO2 (eqn (4)). Simultaneously, under such alkaline conditions, the Ni0 atoms on the surface of Ni foam were oxidized to Ni2+ in the hydrothermal process (eqn (5)). (ii) Then, as the solubility product constant (Ksp) of CdS is much smaller than that of Ni3S2,65,66 the active S2+ species preferentially reacts with the Cd2+ ions to cause CdS nucleation, and with the presence of a glutathione capping agent, the CdS nanorods are aligned along the (002) direction and grown on the Ni foam substrate (eqn (6)). 3.3 Electrochemical catalytic (EC) activity

Both the HER and OER electrocatalytic performance of the CSNS-x samples was investigated by linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) in alkaline solution (1 M NaOH). For comparison, the catalytic activity of pure Ni3S2, CdS, Pt/C (20% wt) and RuO2 was also measured. Fig. 4A shows the OER spectra of CSNS-x samples and RuO2 catalysts. It can be seen that all of the CSNS-x samples have pre-oxidation peaks before their onset potential, and the peak intensities increased with the Ni3S2 shell thickness; these phenomena are commonly observed with most Ni-based electrocatalysts and can be attributed to the oxidation of Ni(II) to Ni(III).69,70 The OER onset potential of CSNS-x is gradually decreased from 1.56 eV (CSNS-1) to 1.49 eV (CSNS-5), and then increased again to 1.51 eV for the CSNS-3 sample. Typically, the OER electrocatalysts are benchmarked by comparing their overpotential (Î·) at a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2, which is expected from a 10% efficient solar water splitting device. In this case, since the large oxidation peak for the CSNS-x samples masked the overpotential at 10 mA cmâˆ’2, here we compared the Î· value at 20 mA cmâˆ’2 to verify the OER properties of the obtained samples. It can be found that the CSNS-5 catalyst exhibits the best OER activity with a Î· of 310 mV, which is much lower than that of pure Ni3S2 on Ni foam (Î· = 420 mV) and other CSNS-x samples. In addition to the OER catalytic activities, all the CSNS-x samples exhibited good HER properties in the same alkaline solution. As shown in Fig. 4B, CSNS-5 also has the best HER performance achieving a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 at an overpotential of 142 mV. Although this HER activity is still lower than that of Pt/C (Î· = 102 mV), it is actually much better than that of the pure Ni3S2 in this work (186 mV) and the previously reported high-index {210} facet exposed Ni3S2 (âˆ¼170 mV) in the same electrolyte.28 The kinetic parameters corresponding to the OER and HER activities were further calculated from the measured Tafel slope collected near the onset potential (Fig. 4C and D). The observed OER and HER Tafel slopes of CSNS-5 were about 176.8 and 138.6 mV decâˆ’1, which are respectively smaller than those of pure Ni3S2 catalysts. The smaller Tafel slope suggests that CSNS-5 has faster reaction kinetics than pristine Ni3S2. According to a previous report, the turnover frequency (TOF) is another important parameter that can be used to evaluate the electrocatalyst in the EC catalysis procedure.71 In this work, the H2 TOFs of CSNS-5, CSNS-10 and pure Ni3S2 samples are calculated at various potentials for HER electrocatalysis (Fig. S5â€ ). The TOF value of CSNS-5 is higher than that of CSNS-10 and pure Ni3S2; this result confirmed that the CdS nanorods play a critical role in dictating the HER activities and can further boost the EC catalytic performance of the active Ni3S2 nanosheets. In order to gain further insights into the intrinsic activity of Ni3S2 for hydrogen electrocatalysis, the exchange current density for the hydrogen evolution/oxidation reactions (HER/OER) was calculated from the Tafel plots and fitted with the simplified Butlerâ€“Volmer equation.72,73 As shown in Fig. S6 and Table S1,â€  CSNS-5 exhibited the highest exchange current density; this result suggests that CSNS-5 has the fastest reaction kinetics compared to the other samples. Besides, we also tested the long-term stability performance of the CSNS-5 electrode in 1.0 M KOH. As can be seen in Fig. 4E and F, when the applied current density is set at 20 mA cmâˆ’2 and âˆ’10 mA cmâˆ’2 for the OER and HER measurements respectively, a constant OER potential of 1.58 V (vs. RHE) and HER potential of âˆ’0.28 V (vs. RHE) can be well maintained for at least 10 h without obvious decay. These results indicate a good level of stability for CSNS-5 in both OER and HER electrocatalytic reactions.

3.6 Discussion of the EC, PEC and PC catalysis mechanisms

Based on the above results, the possible EC, PEC and PC H2 evolution catalysis mechanisms of CSNS-x samples are presented, and the three corresponding charge separation/transfer models are displayed in Scheme 2: (i) in the EC water splitting system, the HER or OER mainly takes place on the surface of the Ni3S2 nanosheets and the opposite reaction occurs on the counter electrode, the electrons flow direction is shown in Scheme 2 (left). In this case, the CdS nanorods mainly play a supporting role to increase the exposed surfaces of the Ni3S2 nanosheets. CSNS-5 with suitable Ni3S2 exposed surfaces and conductivity exhibits the highest HER and OER properties in the alkaline solution. (ii) For the PC H2 production system as shown in Scheme 2 (middle), under visible light irradiation, the photogenerated electrons on the CB of CdS nanorods transmit to the surface of Ni3S2 nanosheets and take part in the H+ reduction reaction. In this process, the Ni3S2 nanosheets mainly act as cocatalysts, and an incompletely covered structure of the CdS nanorods has also provided advantages for the donor oxide reaction which takes place on the partially exposed CdS nanorod surface. As a result, with a balance between the appropriate synergetic band gap and light absorption capacity, CSNS-3 received the highest PC H2 production performance. (iii) Combined with the visible light irradiation and the external electric field, the photogenerated electrons from CdS nanorods transfer to the counter electrode through the Ni foam substrate, as shown in Scheme 2 (right). In this PEC H2 production system, the thickness of the Ni3S2 layers remarkably affects the light absorption performance of the CdS core. Therefore, with the assistance of external bias, the CSNS-2 sample exhibits the highest photon to current conversion efficiency.

4. Conclusion

In summary, a class of versatile, binder-free, non-noble metal catalysts based on Ni3S2 {210} high-index facet exposed and shell thickness controllable CdS@Ni3S2 coreâ€“shell nanorod arrays has been successfully synthesized on Ni foam. The Ni3S2 shell thickness can be easily controlled from 1 to 10 nm by adjusting the Cd2+/S2âˆ’ molar ratio in the precursors. Moreover, it was demonstrated that the as-synthesized CSNS samples have trifunctional catalytic properties in EC, PEC and PC water splitting processes. The excellent catalytic performance in the PC, PEC or EC systems was primarily attributed to the controllable shell thickness of the Ni3S2 nanosheets, and the synergistic catalytic role transformation effect between Ni3S2 and CdS. The results show that (i) the CSNS-5 sample exhibited a good level of activity and stability in both OER and HER electrocatalytic reactions; the lowest OER and HER overpotential is 310 mV (masked with a current of 20 mA cmâˆ’2) and 142 mV (masked with a current of 10 mA cmâˆ’2), respectively. (ii) The CSNS-2 sample achieved the highest PEC activity with an ABPE value of 7.9% and a high average PEC H2 production rate of 112.5 Î¼mol cmâˆ’2 minâˆ’1. (iii) The CSNS-3 sample realized an AQE value of 1.36% under 475 nm monochromatic light irradiation, and the PC H2 production rate reaches 8.2 Î¼mol cmâˆ’2 hâˆ’1. These advantageous properties as well as the durability and cost-effectiveness of CSNS can make it a promising material to replace noble metal-based catalysts for water splitting. This study provides new insights into the design and development of high-performance electrocatalysts/photocatalysts and coreâ€“shell catalysts for selective application of H2 production systems.
