Abstract

Single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) have attracted significant attention due to their outstanding mechanical, chemical and optoelectronic properties, which makes them promising candidates for use in a wide range of applications. However, as-produced SWCNTs have a wide distribution of various chiral species with different properties (i.e. electronic structures). In order to take full advantage of SWCNT properties, highly purified and well-separated SWCNTs are of great importance. Recent advances have focused on developing new strategies to effectively separate nanotubes into single-chirality and/or semiconducting/metallic species and integrating them into different applications. This review highlights recent progress in this cutting-edge research area alongside the enormous development of their identification and structural characterization techniques. A comprehensive review of advances in both controlled synthesis and post-synthesis separation methods of SWCNTs are presented. The relationship between the unique structure of SWCNTs and their intrinsic properties is also discussed. Finally, important future directions for the development of sorting and purification protocols for SWCNTs are provided.

1. Introduction

In the past two decades, we have witnessed tremendous advances in developing new materials with tailored characteristics for advanced technologies. A vast amount of effort has focussed on research into one-dimensional (1D) nanomaterials due to their unique structure and excellent properties. One such revolutionary material is carbon nanotubes (CNTs). Since their discovery in 1991,1 CNTs, especially single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs), have attracted significant attention from both scientific and industrial communities, due to their excellent mechanical, electrical, thermal and optical properties.2 A typical cylindrically shaped 1D SWCNT can be regarded as a rolled-up graphene-sheet, while rolling up multiple sheets of graphene results in the structure of multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs). Their unique properties means they have been integrated in a variety of applications3 such as photovoltaics,4 sensing,5 drug delivery,6 and biological and medical applications.7,8 In addition, the remarkably improved performance of devices after the successful incorporation of SWCNTs could inspire the potential use of this promising material in other fields. However, in general, SWCNTs are a random mixture of different species, such as metallic (m-SWCNTs) and semiconducting (s-SWCNTs) types, with different chiralities and diameter distributions, which limits their successful use in some specific applications. Furthermore, the bandgaps of the s-SWCNTs are found to be inversely proportional to the tube diameter.9 Given well-defined structure and properties are crucial for various applications, the separation of the mixture into individual species with highly controllable properties is of great interest. A simple and effective method for separating SWCNTs with high purity and reasonable yield has long been a challenging task for scientists and researchers. Promisingly, in recent years, excellent progress has been made in the separation process and the sorted materials have been used in a comprehensive range of fields. Therefore it is necessary to highlight the recent advances that have been made in this rapidly booming research area.

This review is split into two main sections. In the first section, we explore the structures of the SWCNTs and highlight the factors that strongly influence their properties all of which are inextricably linked to their morphology, structure and further functionalization. In the second section, we provide an in-depth review of recent advances that have focused on both preparation of SWCNTs with specific properties via preferential growth and post-synthesis purification techniques. At the end of this review, some important future directions in the separation of nanotubes are also provided.

2. Structure and properties

SWCNTs have a unique structure and possess many excellent chemical, physical, optical and electronic properties. In the following section, we provide a detailed description of the SWCNTs and compare the properties of the well-defined nanotubes with both the as-produced form and other nanomaterials. Moreover, the related characterization methods and their roles in evaluating the purity and yield of CNTs are discussed.

Hence, SWCNTs can be categorized into armchair (n = m), zigzag (m = 0), or chiral (n ≠ m ≠ 0) (see Fig. 1b). The electrical properties of the resulting nanotubes vary considerably depending on the chirality and diameter. SWCNTs exhibit metallic behaviour if |n − m| = 3i, whereas if |n − m| = 3i ± 1 they exhibit semiconducting behaviour. Recently, it has been realized that in addition to the classification by chirality, SWCNTs can also be categorized by the helical structures into left- and right-handedness (known as enantiomers or optical isomers).12 The non-chiral armchair and zigzag conformations have no optical isomers, while chiral SWCNTs have a pair of left- and right-handed spiral alignments of the hexagonal carbon rings along the nanotube axis (Fig. 1c and d).13 It is worth mentioning that chiral s-SWCNTs are classified into type-I and type-II depending on whether mod (2n + m,3) = 1 or 2 respectively.14 Both types exhibit opposite signs of circular dichroism (CD) values at the optical transition S22 which defines the left-handed nanotubes (L-SWCNTs) and right-handed nanotubes (R-SWCNTs). For type-I, the negative and positive CD peaks are associated with L- and R-SWCNTs respectively and vice versa for type-II.13

2.2. Fundamental properties of separated SWCNTs

Despite the fact that SWCNTs exhibit excellent properties, the existence of both metallic and semiconducting nanotubes in the as-produced samples limits their potential use in many advanced applications. The reported sorting methods have allowed the selection of nanotubes with desirable properties for each specific application. The electrical, optical, thermal and mechanical properties have been found to be dependent on the specific species present. These properties are described in detail in the following sub-sections.
2.2.1. Electrical properties. The variation of the nanotube types results in inconsistent electrical characteristics which play significantly important roles in many electronic and optoelectronic devices. Several studies have investigated the electronic properties (conductivity and resistivity) of individual SWCNTs species.16–19 For instance, Kataura et al.20 have studied the influence of varying metallic nanotube content on the resistance of thin films. Two thin films based on m-SWCNTs and a mixture of m- and s-SWCNTs (1:2 ratio) (denoted as a reference film) were fabricated. The film made with only m-SWCNTs showed extremely low resistance (∼1.03 kΩ sq−1) as compared to the reference film which exhibited a sheet resistance as high as 19.6 kΩ sq−1, despite the fact that the reference film was 1.4 times thicker than the m-SWCNTs film. Importantly, the films made of pure m-SWCNTs possess extremely high sheet resistance stability in comparison to the mixed SWCNT films upon exposure to air, chemical doping and annealing processes.21 Such a high sheet resistance stability is believed to be associated with the constant DOS near the Fermi level of m-SWCNTs due to the lack states close to the Fermi level making the m-SWCNTs less sensitive to the surrounding environment. On the contrary, in s-SWCNTs there are many states much closer to the Fermi level and hence the DOS shift has more pronounced effect leading to a variation in the carrier concentration and thus the resistivity upon exposure to ambient environment/chemical doping. The stability of the resistance is a crucial factor for practical applications particularly in transparent electrodes. Clearly, m-SWCNTs are promising candidates and expected to play a critical role in the next generation flexible devices.

It is worth mentioning that the reported resistance values of SWCNTs are higher than the theoretical resistance predicated to be 6.5 kΩ. This can be ascribed to the high contact ohmic resistance.22 The various bandgaps of the semiconducting nanotubes can lead to inhomogeneous contact between the Fermi level of the metal and conduction/valence band of the nanotubes.23 However, due to the absence of energy gaps between the conduction and valance bands in the m-SWCNTs, the devices based on this type of nanotubes have been reported with low resistance values of ∼7 kΩ.24 Blackburn and co-workers,25 for instance, investigated the optical and electrical properties of a number of films based on different ratios of highly pure m- and s-SWCNTs networks. The film resistivities were measured as a function of m-SWCNTs loadings. It has been observed that at low m-SWCNTs ratios, the resistance starts to decrease, however, ratios above 30% lead to an unexpected resistance increase. This was attributed to the high tube–tube junction resistances. It has been established that the resistance at the tube–tube junctions in SWCNTs networks is a significant source of the overall network resistance26 leading to poor film conductivities. Tunnelling barriers between the similar types of tubes (i.e. metallic–metallic nanotubes) have been reported, while Schottky barriers are confirmed between metallic-semiconducting junctions which have a significant impact on the electrical and optoelectronic properties.27 This was further investigated by Yanagi et al.28 who studied the transport mechanisms in the SWCNT networks composed of different m-, s-SWCNTs ratios. Increasing s-SWCNTs content causes transition of the conduction mechanism from the weak to strong localization regime. The strong localization is associated with the disordered nanotubes, which can be due to the Schottky barriers between metallic-semiconducting nanotubes or the different diameter distribution of s-SWCNTs and thus the large band-gap differences. The authors concluded that different conduction mechanisms were observed; in particular quantum transport and Coulomb-gap-type in highly pure m- and s-SWCNTs, respectively. The conduction in the mixed networks of different ratios of m- and s-SWCNTs has further been studied by Topinka and co-workers29 revealing the effects of the Schottky barrier on conductance. As can be seen in Fig. 2, by varying the ratio of s-SWCNTs in the simulation, the current flow through the nanotube films ranges from high and uniform current observed in the films containing only m- or s-SWCNTs (a and e) while combination of both types blocks the current (b–d).

Energy band gap is one of the most important properties in the s-SWCNTs and is highly desirable in many applications. However, their high resistance (or low conductivity) as compared to the m-SWCNTs is an obstacle. Promisingly, this issue can be improved and/or addressed by treating the films using HNO3 or SOCl2 to induce doping effect.394 Chemical doping is a very promising strategy to enhance the electrical properties and has been well documented.25,31,32 The enhancement observed in the electrical conductivity of the s-SWCNTs after chemical doping is higher than that of the m-SWCNTs.19,33 Despite the fact that the conductivities can be largely enhanced, this approach suffers from several drawbacks such as thermal/chemical stability, complexity and reproducibility.34 In addition to the resistances at the inter-tube junctions, there are other factors that play some roles in influencing the films conductivities such as surface defects.35

In general, semiconducting nanotubes have been found to exhibit many unique properties including excellent field effect mobility (79000 cm2 V−1 s−1), the highest estimated intrinsic carrier mobility (>100000 cm2 V−1 s−1) among other available semiconducting materials at room temperature36 high hole motility of 20000 cm2 V−1 s−1,37 long free path exceeding ∼0.7 μm38 and high on/off ratios.39 On the other hand, metallic nanotubes have been reported to possess equal or even higher conductivities and current densities as compared to other metals,38 long free path ranging from hundred nm to several μm (ballistic transport)40 which make them an ideal candidate for high-performance interconnects.41 In addition, they have a Fermi velocity of νF = 8 × 105 m s−1 which is comparable to that of the traditional metals, and show room-temperature resistivity of ∼ρ = 106 Ω cm.42

2.2.2. Optical properties. The optical properties of the SWCNTs are also structure-dependent and can be tuned to obtain the desirable properties. Due to their quasi-1D structure, the optical absorption spectrum of SWCNTs show sharp peaks which correspond to the optical transitions resulting from so-called van Hove singularities (VHSs) in the density of states (DOS). The energy bandgap of the s-SWCNTs is inversely proportional to the tube diameter43 and both can be identified by the chiral vector (n,m). The energy bandgap can be described as
Eg = 2γ0aC–C/dt = 0.9 eV/dt
where, γ0 is the nearest neighbour overlap energy, aC–C is the carbon–carbon bond length and dt is the nanotube diameter (Eg increasing with decreasing the diameter). It can be seen from Fig. 3a that the energy gap between the van Hove singularities S11 and S22 of the s-SWCNTs with small diameter ((6,5)) are larger than that of the species with larger diameters (10,5)). However, due to their zero band gap, the first optical transition M11 in the metallic species occurs at widely spaced VHS, and thus only M11 can be observed. Therefore by varying nanotube diameters, distinct colours can be observed (see Fig. 3b).

High electrical conductivity and low optical absorption of the materials are highly desired in numerous applications such as photovoltaics, liquid crystal displays (LCDs) and organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs). As such, SWCNTs with their capability to meet these requirements have been employed to fabricate optically transparent and electrically conductive thin films.45–49 However, the heterogeneous mixture of nanotubes is regarded as a real obstacle considering the strong and broad absorption of s-SWCNTs species in the critical positions of the absorption spectrum limiting their effectiveness. To this end, the optical properties of the thin films fabricated based on highly enriched nanotubes fractions have been investigated.33,50 Yang et al.51 studied the effect of unsorted, m- and s-SWCNTs on the transmittance (400–700 nm wavelength range) and sheet resistance of films coated on polyethylene terephthalate (PET) substrates, as shown in Table 1. 2.2.3. Thermal and thermoelectric properties. SWCNTs exhibit excellent thermal properties with a predicted thermal conductivity of 6600 W m−1 K−1 at room temperature.54 However, the thermal conductivity of the films has been shown to be severely compromized by the high inter-tube junction thermal resistance,55,56 resulting in low thermal conductivity. In contrast, individual nanotubes have been reported to have thermal conductivity as high as ∼3500 W m−1 K−1 at room temperature.57,58 Several theoretical studies confirmed the chirality-dependence of thermal conductivity.59,60 Pop et al.61 were able to experimentally measure the thermal conductivities of various SWCNT solution processed films which were fabricated based on highly pure samples of m-, s-, purified and as-produced SWCNTs. Thermal conductivities ranging from 80 to 370 W m−1 K−1 were recorded at near room temperature. Fig. 4a shows the comparison between the measured thermal conductivity values in this study and thermal conductivities reported in the literature for different types of CNTs. It has been shown that the m-SWCNTs based films exhibit higher electrical conductivity as compared to the s-SWCNTs films, however, the thermal conductivity of the film made with m-SWCNTs is typically lower due to the shorter tube lengths. Importantly, no correlation between the chirality-sorted SWCNT films and thermal conductivities was observed. Instead, the morphology of the networks (i.e. lengths, overall junction and mass density of the SWCNTs) was found to play an essential role in controlling the thermal conductivities.

Thermoelectric materials (TE) have attracted a great deal of attention as promising candidates for use in several applications, such as cooling/heating systems and electrical power generation.62 The frequently used thermoelectric materials (Bi2Te3 and its alloys) generally suffer from several disadvantages such as toxicity, the use of heavy rare earth elements, poor processability and high cost.63,64 The performance of thermoelectric devices, defined as devices that can directly convert thermal energy from heat sources into electricity,65 can be evaluated by a figure of merit (ZT),

ZT = (S2σ/k)T,
where S is the Seebeck coefficient (also called the thermoelectric power (TEP) or thermopower), σ is the electrical conductivity, k is the thermal conductivity and T is the absolute temperature.66 Materials with high thermoelectric power coupled with high electrical and low thermal conductivities are highly desirable and but challenging to fabricate.67 The real difficulty lies in improving all parameters simultaneously since altering one parameter has a considerable impact on the rest of the parameters.68 It has been demonstrated that low dimensional thermoelectric materials exhibit better performance than bulk materials.69 To this end, SWCNTs with their excellent thermoelectric properties and low dimension are ideal thermoelectric materials and hence have been intensively investigated.70–76 However, the as-produced SWCNTs resulted in unsatisfying performance with a TEP ranging from 11 to 65 μV K−1,77 which is relatively low as compared to the required S values for good thermoelectric performance (believed to be at 150–250 μV K−1).78 Therefore, the effect of SWCNTs’ electrical type and chirality on the thermoelectric performance has been studied theoretically and experimentally. In one of the earlier theoretical studies, Hung et al.79 calculated the thermoelectric power of s-SWCNTs as a function diameter (0.5–1.5 nm) using the Boltzmann transport formalism combined with an extended tight-binding model. It was found that decreasing SWCNT diameter, which increases the band gap, increases the thermopower values to 2000 μV K−1 at room temperature for SWCNTs such as (5,3) and (6,1) with a diameter of less than 0.6 nm (see Fig. 4b). Another recent theoretical and experimental study was reported by Ferguson and co-workers80 to get insight into the influence of controlled chirality nanotubes on the thermoelectric power. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations have been performed to study the influence of SWCNT diameter, bandgap and carrier density on thermopowers using five SWCNTs species. It was revealed that the peak of the intrinsic thermopower of (7,5) and (10,0) (both are s-SWCNTs) is nearly an order of magnitude higher than that of the m-SWCNTs species (9,9) (Fig. 4c). The results indicate that the thermopower increases with increasing nanotube bandgap, which is in agreement with the finding reported by Hung et al.79 Interestingly, it was found that a small change in the Fermi level ΔEf (60 meV) either up or down reflects positively on the S value, highlighting the useful role of controlled doped s-SWCNTs. Based on this promising predication, highly enriched s-SWCNTs films were fabricated and high thermopower values exceeding 100 μV K−1 were obtained.

In addition to the chirality separated nanotubes, a few groups have also focused on the effect of pristine, m- and s-SWCNTS networks on the thermoelectric properties. It is worth noting that individual nanotubes possess high electrical conductivity which is advantageous in thermoelectric applications, but their high thermal conductivities limit their effective use. Therefore, nanotubes sheets which showed lower thermal conductivity are more attractive. Kim et al.81 systematically compared TEP of pure m-, s- and mixed SWCNTs thin films prepared using vacuum filtration-based method. The authors showed that the s-SWCNTs films possess higher TEP (88 μV K−1) than that of m-SWCNTs based films which exhibited only 13 μV K−1. Furthermore, the result suggests that increasing s-SWCNTs content in the mixture improves TEP values (Fig. 4d). The effect of Schottky junction between m- and s-SWCNTs on the thermoelectric properties has also been investigated theoretically. It was found that inter-tube junctions in the SWCNTs networks play a significant role in the overall TEP which was found in a previous study to represent 97–99% of the total thermal resistance.82 A very large Seebeck coefficient of 170 μV K−1 at 300 K was reported by Maniwa et al.63 who used sorted s-SWCNT film (Fig. 4d). Despite the large Seebeck coefficient, the electrical conductivity was relatively low. This issue has been addressed by using various types of chemical doping approaches.25,83 Maniwa et al.84 studied the doping effect on the thermoelectric properties. The as-produced SWCNTs and enriched s-SWCNTs films were p-doped using HNO3, and compared them as a function of the electrical resistivity or alternatively electrical conductivity. The result suggests that decreasing the electrical resistivity (by doping) increases S value due to the increase in the charge carrier concentration. However, this behaviour was much more evident for the enriched s-SWCNTs films as compared to the mixed SWCNTs film. This can be attributed to the presence of m-SWCNTs content in the mixed film. Increasing charge carrier concentration results in Fermi energy level (EF) shifts toward the valance or conduction bands and hence changes the density of states (DOS). Considering the presence of VHSs in the DOS near EF for s-SWCNTs, low amounts of carrier doping can move EF to the van Hove singularity and thus a significant change is observed as compared to the larger spacing between the van Hove singularities in m-SWCNTs.63 The influence of nanotube electronic type on the thermoelectric properties has also been investigated by Wang and co-workers.85 SWCNTs/polyaniline nanocomposite films were fabricated based on m- and s-SWCNTs with different ratios. Similar to previous observations, s-SWCNTs/PANI achieved an S value three times larger than that of m-SWCNTs/PANI films (Fig. 4e). These studies clearly indicate the high potential of the separated SWCNTs, particularly s-SWCNTs, to be used effectively as emerging material in thermoelectric devices.

2.2.4. Mechanical properties. One of the most promising features of SWCNTs is their remarkable mechanical properties (i.e. stiffness and bending strength) due to the sp2 carbon–carbon bonding between the individual carbon atoms, which makes them potential candidates as reinforcements in nanocomposite materials. This has been experimentally confirmed by Qian et al.86 who reported a significant enhancement of up to 42% and 25% in the elastic stiffness and tensile strength of the CNTs–polystyrene composites, respectively after the addition of low content of CNTs (1 wt%). Tremendous efforts have been made to understand and evaluate the mechanical properties of CNTs and hence there are several theoretical and experimental studies available. A variety of techniques have been employed to experimentally measure the mechanical properties of SWCNTs. In early experiments, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM) have been utilized by Treacy et al.87 and Wong et al.88 reporting the first experimental measurements for MWCNTs, and high Young's modulus values of 1.8 and 1.28 TPa, respectively were obtained. Krishnan et al.89 investigated Young's modulus using TEM on 27 isolated SWCNTs with diameter ranging from 1 to 1.5 nm yielding an average Young's modulus of 1.25 TPa. Raman spectroscopy was also employed and values as high as 2.8–3.6 TPa for SWCNTs were obtained.90 Other experimental studies on the mechanical properties of SWCNTs have also revealed high Young's modulus values of 0.8191 and 1.20 TPa.92 Tensile strength measurements have also shown similar variations in the reported values. Tensile strength of 11–63 GPa395 for MWCNTs was reported, while the tensile strength of SWCNTs has been found to be 13–52 GPa (mean: 30 GPa)396 and 45 ± 7 GPa93 in different reports. Despite the success of the previous experiments, there is a large discrepancy in the reported values reflecting the difficulty and complexity of characterizing CNTs at the atomic scale. Besides, the high cost associated with experimental studies makes computational and theoretical methods more favourable and straightforward tools to estimate Young's modulus values. Numerous theoretical and numerical studies concerning the mechanical properties of CNTs have been conducted.94–96 In addition, the effects of structural details on the mechanical properties of SWCNT have also been investigated. Hernández et al.97 applied a tight-binding approach to investigate the effect of CNT diameter and chirality on Young's modulus reporting values ranging from 1.22 TPa to 1.26 TPa. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were employed by WenXing et al.98 to calculate the Young's moduli of different chiralities and values of 0.904 to 0.939 TPa were reported. The authors concluded that tube chirality and radius weakly affect the Young's moduli of SWCNTs, and that is in an agreement with Nakayama et al.'s99 observation. However, in Nakayama's investigation, the critical tensile strain for breaking has shown a chiral dependence. Meo and Rossi100 observed a significant influence of chirality on the mechanical properties of SWCNTs using a Finite Element Model based on molecular mechanics theory. Li and Chou101 reported that Young's moduli of armchair and zigzag SWCNTs increases with increasing tube diameter. This increase can be attributed to the effect of nanotube curvature as decreasing tube diameter results in higher curvature and therefore more significant distortion of C–C bonds. This finding was also reported by Tserpes and Papanikos.102 In addition, the Young's modulus of chiral nanotubes has been found to be larger than that of armchair and zigzag. Conversely, Ávila and Lacerda103 have reported that Young's modulus of zigzag is larger than that of armchair and chiral (Fig. 4f-black). In 2012, Lu and Hu104 developed an improved 3D finite element (FE) model to study the effect of chirality on the mechanical properties of SWCNTs. They revealed that the Young's modulus of zigzag and chiral increases with increasing CNT diameter with no significant changes for armchair (Fig. 4f-blue). Furthermore, the largest Young's modulus was found for zigzag followed by chiral and armchair respectively. It was also found that Young's modulus increases as the chiral angle decreases. 2.3. Evaluation and characterization

The optimization of CNT properties is crucial for many applications. Therefore, the quality, diameter distribution, purity, length, electronic type and chirality of the obtained nanotubes should be precisely evaluated. Due to the rapid development in the controlled synthesis and post-purification protocols of SWCNTs, various effective characterization techniques have been developed and utilized in order to investigate the morphological, structural characteristics and properties of the nanotubes either during the synthesis or after the production/purification processes. The following sections discuss the range of techniques that have been adapted and used for characterizing the chirality and electronic properties of the sorted SWCNTs.
2.3.1. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM). SEM is one of the most frequently used tools to visualize various nanomaterials including CNTs providing a detailed insight into the sample morphology. However, only SWCNT bundles can be imaged which limits the use of SEM to characterize the separated individual nanotubes. Interestingly, by taking advantage of the contrast differences arising from the variation of conductivities between m- and s-SWCNTs, Jiang et al.108 reported a successful identification method of m- and s-SWCNTs via SEM, demonstrating the high potential of SEM to provide a rapid and large-scale approach to discriminate between m- and s-SWCNTs. Despite these advantages, this method exhibits several drawbacks which can be described as follows: (i) the identification process is only valid if both SWCNTs types appear in the same image, otherwise no contrast difference can be distinguished, and thus the individual SWCNTs type appearing will be unknown. (ii) The nanotubes should be prepared in well-dispersed form, as the bundle contains both s- and m-SWCNTs, resulting in no difference in the contrast.108

Future studies on the contrast differences particularly between s-SWCNTs species are expected to bring a significant step forward for the realization of a powerful and direct characterization tool for chirality assignment.

2.3.2. Transmission electronic microscopy (TEM). TEM is a powerful tool to visualize SWCNTs using a high energy electron beam based on electron transmission. The SWCNT characteristics such as lengths, diameters,109 purity and m-, s-SWCNTs ratios and chirality can be revealed by TEM. For example, using an aberration-corrected TEM, Sato et al.110 reported a successful determination of the chirality population in an enriched sample that was obtained via the density-gradient ultracentrifugation method. For better resolution, high resolution TEM (HR-TEM) can be used which allows the imaging of the atomic structure of the SWCNTs. HR-TEM and electron diffraction patterns (EDP) were employed to visualize the atomic structure of the individual nanotubes.111–114 For example, Tian et al.115 utilized both HR-TEM and EDP simultaneously in order to validate the absorption spectrum measurements of the chirality population in SWCNTs sample. Beside its role in characterizing CNTs after the growth, TEM has also been used in situ to monitor the nucleation and growth processes and the catalyst particles formation which is of paramount importance to obtain chirally pure SWCNTs (see section 3.1).
2.3.3. Scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM). STM is employed to characterize SWCNTs at the individual nanotube level. The atomic resolution enables probing of the SWCNT surface structure and hence the determination of CNT chirality is realized. The proposed strategy is to identify (n,m) indices using STM by measuring the chiral angle and diameter of a specific nanotube. The chiral angle can easily be determined from the angle (ϕ) between the zigzag tube axis direction and the tube axis direction.116 The successful determination of various chiralities utilizing STM have been reported by several groups.116–119 However, the complicated sample preparation and high conductivity requirements may restrict its effective use.
2.3.4. Resonance Raman spectroscopy (RRS). Resonance Raman spectroscopy is a versatile method for assigning the chirality distribution for a given SWCNTs sample. This technique is widely used for the characterization of individual SWCNTs due to its simplicity, non-destructive nature, availability, short experimental time and easy sample preparation process.120,121 The assignment of each (n,m) can be carried out by combining RRS experimental measurements with the calculated electronic transition energies for all (n,m) species (known as Kataura plot).122 Due to the unique transition patterns of each species between VHS in the very large DOS, a well-defined expected spectrum of each individual SWCNT is known. Experimentally, there are two main features which can be utilized to identify the chirality/electrical type of SWCNTs: tangential mode (G band) defined from graphite (occurs at ∼1560 cm−1) and the radial breathing mode (RBM) associated with the vibration of the carbon atoms in radial direction (observed at 100–300 cm−1) (Fig. 5a). The tangential G mode in SWCNTs usually splits into two intense peaks due to quantum confinement and the resulting submodes are commonly labelled as G− and G+. Compared to s-SWCNTs, the shape of G− peak of m-SWCNTs is broadened and this is mainly due to the presence of high fractions of metallic nanotubes which possess high number of free electrons123 (Fig. 5b). The positions as well as the intensities of G− and G+ peaks have been found to be a useful tool to determine the diameter and chiral indices of SWCNTs. Telg, Doorn et al.124 conducted resonant Raman studies on 14 highly purified single chirality samples obtained using a selective polymer wrapping technique. A strong correlation was observed between (i) the diameter and chiral angle, and (ii) the position and frequency of the G mode features. This allowed the successful chirality assignment of the purified nanotubes having a diameter of <1.1 nm. Such a technique is useful for (n,m) assignment considering the high signal of G mode as compared to the RBM. Therefore, no high excitation laser energies are required which implies that Raman with a fixed energy source accessible to a wide variety of laboratories can easily be used to accomplish such task. However, the disadvantage of this method is the fact that only small diameter nanotubes can be chirally assigned as significant peak overlap occurs with the large diameter nanotubes leading to difficult and inaccurate chirality assignment.

It is worth mentioning that despite the many advantages of Raman spectroscopy, this technique has some limitations such as the difficulty in obtaining quantitative chirality assessment of each individual nanotube presented in the sample and the extremely time-consuming mapping of large sample areas. In addition, the validity of Raman spectroscopy for quantitative evaluation the metallic fractions in a given sample has recently been questioned.134 As such, further development of this technique is needed for more accurate and reliable chiral SWCNTs characterization.

Raman spectroscopy has also emerged as a powerful tool for the discrimination of empty and water-filled SWCNTs.135,136 Determining whether SWCNTs are empty (closed) or water filled (opened) is crucial since it affects the density of the dispersed CNTs, hence the separation process.137,138 The RBM peak features (position, width and intensity) can be used to identify whether the CNTs is empty or filled. Fagan et al.,138 for instance, showed that (15,2) and (16,4) – water filled SWCNTs were red-shifted with a broader peaks as compared to that of the empty (15,2) and (16,4)-SWCNTs.

Raman optical activity (ROA) is one of the latest techniques that has emerged for chirality characterization of SWCNTs. To date, only a limited number of theoretical studies on the SWCNTs resonance ROA (RROA) have been published.139,140 In addition, to the best of our knowledge, the first experimental work in this field was reported in 2015 by Bürgi et al.141 who were able to measure the first ROA spectra of SWCNTs. The samples enantiomerically enriched with (6,5) SWCNTs were prepared using a nonlinear density gradient ultracentrifugation (DGU) sorting method. An enhancement of the ROA intensity was realized which is believed to be due to the near-resonance of Raman-scattered photons with the second electronic transition of the (6,5) SWCNTs. As such, the measurement of ROA spectra was possible despite the low concentration. Although this technique is a promising tool to investigate the handedness of the separated samples, further development is still required.

2.3.5. UV–Vis–NIR absorption spectroscopy. UV–vis–NIR absorption spectroscopy is one of the most widely used and easily accessible techniques to determine the concentration, purity and chirality of enriched SWCNT samples.145 On the basis of the transitions between the first and second pairs of VHSs in the DOS (explained earlier), several sharp peaks are expected to appear in the optical absorption spectrum of a particular SWCNT species.146 Here, the optical absorption spectrum can be used to determine the chirality of each individual (n,m) SWCNT as they possess different sets of inter-band transitions between VHSs. For s-SWCNTs species, four inter-band transitions peaks (S11, S22, S33, and S44) can be discerned in the UV–Vis–NIR spectrum. Whereas, only one inter-band transition for m-SWCNTs can be detected (M11) in the UV–Vis–NIR with other transitions outside this range. It is worth noting that S33 and S44 are regarded as one group since they are often heavily overlapped. The variations in the peak intensities arise from the different abundance and absorption cross-sections of individual nanotubes. The latter is an important optical parameter allowing the determination of the sample concentration through the absorption spectroscopy.147 However, a quantitative evaluation of the concentration for a sample containing different SWCNTs species with unknown abundance has long been a real challenge. Owing to the recent advances in the separation methods, a number of studies on measuring the absorption cross sections for different (n,m) species have been reported.147,148
2.3.6. Photoluminescence spectroscopy (PL). PL has become an indispensable tool for reliable (n,m) assignments, especially for s-SWCNTs. Highly structured PL in the NIR was observed for the first time by Weisman et al. in 2002.149 Over 30 different s-SWCNTs species have shown distinct electronic absorption and emission transitions. Since then, this technique has been adapted to provide a rapid determination and the detailed composition of bulk SWCNT samples. The PL process involves; (i) an induced absorption of light at ES22, (ii) relaxation from ES22 to ES11 by an electron–phonon interaction, and (iii) spontaneous emission at ES11.150 Since ES11 and ES22 of each chirality are unique, the structure of individual SWCNTs can be rapidly identified.149 The fluorescence intensity of each (n,m) is directly proportional to their concentration.149 Each bright spot corresponds to a specific chirality. Hence, PL has been used to evaluate the separation efficiency and the population of each nanotubes species in the separated samples. It is worth noting that de-bundling of nanotubes is required as big bundles do not emit. This is due to the PL quenching caused by the m-SWCNTs when present in the bundles.151 In addition, the PL intensity is also correlated with the degree of isolation of s-SWCNTs species.152 A reduced nanotube aggregation yields brighter PL emission.

In a recent study, NIR PL spectroscopy was used to investigate two enantiomers; (+) (6,5) and (−) (6,5) -SWCNTs.153 From the E11 fluorescence emission peak, it was observed that (−) (6,5) enantiomer exhibited 1.7 times higher brightness than that of the (+) (6,5) enantiomer. In addition, the peak was blue-shifted by 5 nm and narrower by 6 nm. This phenomenon was observed with enantiomeric samples prepared by either DNA or coated by surfactants (sodium cholate hydrate (SC) and sodium deoxycholate (SDS)) indicating the possibility to identify and distinguish between a pair of enantiomers using PL. However, when sodium dodecylbenzenesulfonate (SDBS) was used to prepare (6,5) enantiomers, the emission spectra were identical and no differences were observed. This implies that surface coating of SWCNTs plays an important role in not only controlling the helicity of nanotubes but also in their recognition process.

2.3.7. Circular dichroism (CD). Circular dichroism (CD) is an effective tool to distinguish between the right and left-handedness of the nanotubes.154–156 CD is a technique used to implement a direct measurement of the differential absorption of right- and left-handed circularly polarized light as a function of wavelength.13Several early studies have been conducted in which CD was used to characterise the preferentially extracted R- and L-SWCNTs. However, due to the presence of a number of chirality species in the sample, the assignment of CD peaks was difficult.13,157 Considering the fact that the intensity of CD is proportional to the differential ratios between left- and right-handed SWCNT,158 highly pure single chirality with high enantiomeric purity are of the great importance to generate an improved CD signal which can facilitate the analysis of the CD spectrum. Recently, Wei et al.159 were able to separate different single-chirality enantiomers with high purity which did not only allow for a reliable analysis of the CD spectra, but also the assignment of Sii and Sij optical transitions.
2.3.8. Other novel techniques. In addition to the conventional characterization methods, some other novel techniques have been developed to characterize the structure and properties of sorted nanotubes. Wang et al.160 introduced polarization-based microscopy combined with supercontinuum laser illumination. When a SWCNT is illuminated with a laser light on a substrate, an interference occurs between the nanotube-scattered electric field and the substrate-reflected electric field at the detector which derives the optical contrast of a nanotube in a reflection configuration. By manipulating the polarization, the optical contrast can be significantly enhanced. This strategy enabled high-throughput real-time optical imaging and broadband in situ spectroscopy of individual CNTs on various substrates and in FET devices. As such, complete chirality profiling of hundreds of individual CNTs including semiconducting and metallic once on a growth substrate was obtained. In addition, unlike other techniques, this technique has shown great promise for improving SWCNTs growth as it is capable of reliably and accurately determining both SWCNTs chirality and abundance simultaneously which can contribute to a systematic optimization of SWCNTs growth conditions.

CNTs under white light illumination should appear colored due to resonance Rayleigh scattering which in turn could facilitate the chirality assignment of individual SWCNTs. However, due to the extremely low scattering intensity of SWCNTs and strong substrate scattering, true-colour imaging of SWCNTs on substrates is difficult to achieve. Recently, Jiang et al.161 reported that considerably enhanced Rayleigh scattering can be achieved by an interface dipole enhancement effect. This means SWCNTs can be colourful on substrates and hence be directly imaged under an optical microscope by wide field supercontinuum laser illumination. The colour differences in SWCNTs are the indication of diverse chiral indices. As such, this technique allows for high throughput chirality characterization of SWCNTs quickly and easily. In this approach, a number of interesting observations were revealed: (1) supercontinuum white laser with its higher intensity was used to illuminate individual SWCNTs, (2) water serving as the refractive index matching medium was found to play important roles in improving the image quality as it greatly reduces the scattering from the substrate, and (3) SWCNTs appear colored as a result of the resonance Rayleigh scattering peaks in the visible region.

3. Synthesis and purification

SWCNTs enriched with a specific chirality/electrical type has long been considered an emerging functional material due to the growing demand for pre-defined CNTs structures and properties in various applications. Excellent research efforts have been devoted to develop effective and viable methods to produce specific CNTs. This section covers the various mainstream approaches to control the structure of nanotubes. In general, the separation methods of SWCNTs can be classified into two main categories, namely “preferential growth”, which is achieved during the synthesis, and “post-synthesis purification”. This section further details and compares the approaches that have widely been used to purify the nanotubes, and highlights their strengths and weaknesses. Some alternative strategies with improved controllability over the purity and yield of the nanotubes are also proposed.

3.1. Preferential growth

Over the past few years, progress has been made toward achieving the ultimate goal of chirality/electrical type-specific growth of SWCNTs. Currently, there are three main approaches for controlling the chirality/electrical type of SWCNTs: (i) SWCNT growth from segment seeds or organic precursors as templates; (ii) design of catalyst systems with high activity and selectivity; and (iii) optimization of preferential synthesis conditions.
3.1.1. CNT growth from segment seeds or organic precursors as templates. The growth of SWCNTs from pre-defined segments as seeds is regarded as one of the successful strategies to control the chirality of SWCNTs. This approach is known as seeded growth, or nanotube cloning. In this approach, long SWCNTs are cut into short segments with open ends where catalysts can be docked. This short segment is then employed as a seed to grow longer SWCNTs preserving identical chirality and diameter. One noticeable advantage of this approach is that chemical vapor deposition (CVD) production is initiated from the pre-defined CNT structure contrary to the conventional catalyst-based CVD growth technique.162 This approach was first reported by Smalley et al.163 who demonstrated the continued growth of SWCNTs from an array of open-ended segments docked with metal nanoparticles as a catalyst. The regrown SWCNTs exhibited a diameter distribution and chirality similar to that of the original SWCNTs. This was confirmed via the closely matched positions and frequencies of RBMs in the Raman spectrum obtained before and after the growth using excitation lasers with 514.5, 633, and 780 nm wavelengths to cover a wide range of (n,m) SWCNTs. Motivated by this result, they further investigated the validity of this approach on individual SWCNTs in another study.164 A short SWCNT-docked-Fe seed as a template for vapor liquid solid (VLS) amplification was used to grow an individual long SWCNT. The short SWCNT seed was polymer-wrapped with Pluronic F87. As can be clearly seen from Fig. 6a and c the growth in nanotube length at the same location after the regrowth process indicates the successful amplification of the original tube. By measuring the height profiles using AFM, the identical diameter of the grown section to the original seed SWCNT was confirmed (Fig. 6a–e). Despite the increase in the nanotube's length to several micrometres, the yield of seeded growth nanotubes was estimated to be as low as ∼3% which can be increased by carefully controlling the catalyst concentration and size. In addition, further investigation to detect any chirality changes before and after the amplification process are needed.164

To avoid a sophisticated purification process and prevent introduction of contamination and defects upon the removal of metal catalyst residue,165 a metal-catalyst-free SWCNT cloning strategy was proposed. In this promising strategy, open-end SWCNTs as seeds can be duplicated without involving any metal catalyst. Zhang, Liu and co-workers reported the successful growth of SWCNTs with controlled chirality using this concept.165 Ultralong SWCNTs were cut into short segments via electron beam lithography (EBL) which were subsequently used as seeds for regrowth (Fig. 6f). Among 600 short seeds, around 9% were successfully regrown. However, a considerable improvement in the regrowth yield reaching 40% was achieved when the growth substrate was changed from SiO2/Si to quartz but the reason for this improvement was not clear. The diameter and chirality of the duplicated SWCNTs were confirmed to be nearly identical to the seed tubes as indicated by the AFM height profiles (Fig. 6g), and the same RBM was obtained from the Raman spectra. A significant improvement was later reported by Zhou et al. in two subsequent studies using the catalyst-free vapor-phase epitaxy (VPE) growth approach.166,397 Their strategy was as follows; firstly, different (n,m) species were sorted to provide high purity (∼90%) samples using a DNA-based chromatographic separation technique. Secondly, the obtained pre-selected chirality was then used as seeds for metal catalyst-free growth (Fig. 6h). In their first study, (7,6), (6,5) s-SWCNTs and (7,7) m-SWCNT were obtained and then employed as growth templates.166 Methane and ethanol were used as the carbon sources and both were found to be suitable for achieving chirality-controlled growth from the seeds used. The characterization of the extended portions of nanotubes were carried out via AFM, SEM, and Raman spectroscopy confirming that the length of the nanotubes significantly increased to 34.5 μm after cloning as compared to the average length of the as-purified nanotubes seeds at 0.34 μm and they exhibit the same chirality as seeds employed. It was also revealed that the yield of the cloning process was dependent on the air and water vapor annealing process. These annealing processes can alter the functional groups on nanotubes ends resulting in an activation of the CNT ends. In this way, CNTs are capable of incorporating incoming carbon atoms for further growth. In their second study and by using a VPE-based cloning platform, the group systematically investigated the chirality-dependent growth kinetics and termination behaviour of seven single-chirality nanotubes with similar diameters and different chiral angles including semiconducting nanotubes species (9,1), (6,5), (8,3), (7,6), (10,2), and metallic nanotubes species (6,6) and (7,7).397 It was found that the growth rates of nanotubes increase with the chiral angle, while the active lifetimes of the growth have a decreasing trend. With the aim of improving the carbon yield, Wang and co-workers further investigated the influence of the seed pre-treatment in air and water, growth substrate and carbon sources on the efficiency of the re-grown nanotubes.398 Short SWCNTs seeds were re-grown to longer nanotubes utilizing cold-wall and hot-wall acetylene–ethanol CVD. The SWCNT seeds pre-treatment including air oxidation and water treatment were found to activate the nanotube edges resulting in high yields of long re-grown nanotubes under optimized growth conditions. Raman analysis suggested that the chiralities of the seeds were preserved in the re-grown SWCNT. Despite the reported success in increasing the length of the nanotubes and confirming the chirality preservation of the elongated nanotubes, the yield obtained with the metal catalyst-free approach is still very poor. One of the critical obstacles that hinders the growth efficiency improvement of this approach is the activation of seed edge for the re-growth of the nanotubes. Nevertheless, an enhancement in terms of the growth yield can be observed after the pre-treatment of the nanotubes seeds. Despite this, more effective strategies need to be developed to further increase the total yield.

Very recently, Lin et al.167 reported a new strategy that is based on a combination of microwave treatment and CVD growth process. SWCNTs connected with carbon fragments were prepared using an air plasma exposure treatment from a long SWCNT directly grown on SiO2/Si substrate. By exposing the SiO2/Si substrate to microwave radiation under argon, an extremely high temperature can rapidly be achieved around the substrate. As a consequence, oxygen functional groups bonded to the edges of carbon fragment can be effectively removed and resulted in active edges. The as-obtained CNTs can then serve as the template for the regeneration growth. SEM and AFM confirmed that the efficiency of microwave-assisted regeneration can reach 100%. Raman spectroscopy confirmed that the chirality of the regenerated part of the nanotube has the same chirality as the template used (Fig. 6i–k).

It is well known that the structure of SWCNTs is determined by the structure of the “cap” formed during the nucleation stage.168 Hence, it is possible to control the chirality of SWCNTs during their growth by controlling the cap formation precisely.169 However, due to the use of metal nanoparticles as the catalyst in the CVD growth process, the carbon caps formed on the particle surface is stochastic due to the unavoidable thermal vibrations which makes the control of the SWCNT structure a real challenge.169 In recent years, much progress has been made to explore other nanocarbons systems as uniform templets for SWCNTs growth. Wang et al.170 reported the efficient growth of SWCNTs from fullerenes but no attention was paid to the nanotubes’ structure. In 2010, Liu et al.169 demonstrated that fullerendione can be opened by thermal oxidation to yield hemispherical caps which can be subsequently used to initiate SWCNT growth (Fig. 7a). The structure of the fullerene cap can be engineered by changing the temperature of thermal oxidation. At higher temperature (450 °C in air), the cage is largely opened and small diameter SWCNTs (<0.7 nm) are produced, while at lower temperature (350 °C in air), larger diameter SWCNTs (>1.4 nm) can be grown. The diameter distribution was measured using AFM on 100 SWCNTs of each sample, which was further confirmed using the RBM feature in Raman spectra. The diameter distribution of SWCNTs grown from the fullerene caps showed step-like features. Two drawbacks are apparent; the carbon caps are difficult to obtain with size uniformity by the thermal oxidation process, and the growth efficiency is low when the opened fullerene is used as the growth seed. Rümmeli et al.171 systematically investigated the growth of catalyst-free grown SWCNTs nucleated from C60. The role of the solvent used to disperse the fullerenes was found to be crucial in affecting the size of the C60 cluster. The larger the cluster size, the higher the SWCNTs yield. This was attributed to the fact that higher number of fullerenes can be provided from large clusters and hence more fullerenes can serve as seeds. Itami et al.172 identified cyclopar-aphenylenes (CPPs), acene-inserted CPPs, and cyclacenes as the shortened sidewall segments of armchair, chiral, and zigzag SWCNTs respectively (Fig. 7b). Motivated by these results, the same group later demonstrated the use of carbon nanorings namely cycloparaphenylenes (CPPS) as templets for the growth of SWCNTs using bottom-up organic chemistry approach.173 Despite the fact that the average diameters of the as-grown SWCNTs was close to that of CPP nanorings used as templates, poor controllability of the diameter and chirality of the grown CNTs was evident. Very recently, carbon nanobelts were synthesized with the great potential to be used as a seed for the growth of well-defined SWCNTs structures.174

Zhou et al.175 reported another promising strategy to obtain chirality-specific SWCNTs from seeds which was initially proposed by Scott et al.176 Corannulene (C20H10) was used as a starting material to synthesize the caps of (5,5) SWCNTs using bottom-up organic chemistry synthesis approach, aiming for chirality-controlled growth of SWCNTs via VPE growth technique. High-temperature air treatment, followed by water vapor treatment was found to be a very effective strategy to activate C50H10 for the SWCNTs growth. In particular, the highest nanotube yield was achieved with air oxidation at 500 °C followed by water vapor treatment at 900 °C. SEM confirmed the growth of SWCNTs from the pre-treated C50H10 molecules. However, Raman characterization revealed that the structure of as-grown SWCNTs and the molecular end-cap are not identical. The authors attributed the structure changes to the seed pre-treatments by oxygen and water. Raman and electrical measurements revealed that the as-grown nanotubes were >97% pure s-SWCNTs.

The use of an intramolecular cyclodehydrogenation (CDH) reaction on metal surfaces is another exciting approach to obtain well-defined SWCNT caps for single-chirality SWCNT synthesis. The precursors for the short CNTs and the large-diameter hemispherical buckybowls could be used for CNT growth initiation of single-chirality SWCNT on metal surfaces.177 In several studies, Mueller et al.177,178 reported the synthesis of a variety of precursor molecules to be used for the synthesis of isomerically pure armchair, zigzag, and chiral SWCNTs. Fasel et al.179 reported the use of a planar metal surface combined with the use of an end-cap precursor to synthesize single-chirality SWCNTs. A C96H54 precursor was initially synthesized and subsequently deposited on a Pt (111) surface (Fig. 7c). After a proper annealing under ultrahigh vacuum to allow surface-catalyzed CDH, (6,6) SWCNTs seeds were formed. The ultra-short (6,6) SWCNTs seeds were then epitaxially elongated by incorporating carbon atoms from the surface-catalyzed decomposition of ethylene or ethanol into CNTs. STM demonstrated that the structure of the elongated nanotubes were consistent with a (6,6) SWCNT. Raman spectroscopy was also employed to confirm the orientation of the SWCNTs revealing single RBM peaks at 295 cm−1 assigned to (6,6) SWCNTs which further proved the synthesis of single type SWCNTs. However, the authors pointed out that the laser excitation wavelength (532 nm) is not in resonance with the optical transition of (6,6) nanotubes. This implies that the single RBM peaks at 295 cm−1 might correspond to other species that have good resonance with the 532 nm laser wavelength and exhibit RBM peaks close to 295 cm−1 particularly (9,2) and (10,0) SWCNTs. Furthermore, the splitting of the G band into two peaks is not in agreement with the observed single G peak for armchair metallic SWCNTs in other studies.180,181 Further investigations are needed to confirm the growth of pure (6,6) SWCNTs.182 Despite this success, this approach has two practical limitations that may hinder its scalability; the growth yield of CNTs is low under high vacuum growth conditions, and the clean Pt surfaces required in the process.183

The same group later reported the successful conversion of two precursors into potentially singly capped (9,0) zigzag and (6,6) armchair SWCNT using surface-assisted cyclodehydrogenation on the Rh (111) surface.30 They found high conversion efficiency reaching nearly 100%, and more importantly, showed high thermal stability of CNT-caps on the Rh surface (∼500 °C) which is high enough for the required growth temperature for CNT synthesis. They have demonstrated that Rh (111) is a suitable catalyst for the controllable synthesis of SWCNTs by the surface assisted bottom-up approach. They recently reported a powerful strategy for preparing precursors that can be utilized for the growth of large-diameter chirality-pure SWCNTs including (6,6), (8,8), (10,10), and (12,12).30 The molecules showed a rigid and fully fused structure. This implies that highly efficient conversion to seeds as well as high yield of the chirality-pure SWCNT are expected. Due to the considerable progress in the state-of-the-art organic synthesis methods witnessed in recent years, it is expected that more controllable, large-scale production of chirality-specific SWCNTs via growth methods might be available in the near future.

3.1.2. Design of catalyst systems with high activity and selectivity. Catalyst nanoparticles are usually used as an essential component to initiate SWCNT growth. Various metal nanoparticles (NPs) have been explored as catalysts for SWCNT growth, including Fe, Co, Ni, Pt, Pd, Cu, Au, Ag, Mo, Mn, etc.184–187 It is well known that the nature and size of the metal NPs used to synthesize SWCNTs have a direct effect on the structures/chiralities of the SWCNTs product. Therefore, in order to achieve chirality-controlled growth of SWCNTs when using metal NPs catalysts, some major drawbacks need to be solved. Firstly, metallic NPs with homogenous composition, size and structure distribution are difficult to produce. In addition, the small diameter differences between the different SWCNT structures represent a real challenge to obtain a specific-chirality SWCNT by controlling the catalyst size. Furthermore, at high temperatures, catalyst NPs tend to aggregate which can significantly increase the primary size of the particles. Moreover, the considerable fluctuation of the NPs orientations leads to a great variability during the early stages of SWCNT nucleation and growth.188 Based on the afore-mentioned factors, the key factor for achieving high chiral selectivity in SWCNT growth is to effectively control the size and structure of those catalytic NPs in order to produce a uniform dispersion on the substrate. Therefore, intensive studies on the catalyst composition/size have been performed to control the structure of SWCNTs.

Resasco et al.189 reported the selective growth of (6,5) and (7,5) SWCNTs using a silica-supported Co–Mo (CoMoCAT) bimetallic catalyst. Using PL, the chiral enhancement was estimated to be 38% of the as-prepared sample (Fig. 8a). The high selectivity was ascribed to the uniform size of Co NPs resulting from the interaction between Co and Mo NPs which could stabilize the Co NPs against aggregation at high-temperature. The selectivity of (6,5) SWCNTs was further enhanced to 55% by optimizing the CVD process parameters including the gaseous feed composition (CO), synthesis temperature (800 °C), and the catalyst support (SiO2).190 Owing to its high selectivity, high yield, easy preparation method and low cost, this well-developed catalyst was an important step to the commercialization of the highly enriched (6,5) SWCNTs.399

Kauppinen et al.192 developed Fe–Cu bimetallic catalysts supported on a porous MgO that can grow predominantly (6,5) SWCNTs at 600 °C using CO as the carbon source. The role of Cu was found to promote the reducibility of Fe and inhibit the aggregation of Fe clusters. The large abundance of (6,5) SWCNTs in the prepared sample was confirmed via the absorption spectra and PL spectra. The selective growth of Co–Rh catalyst towards (7,5) and (7,6) SWCNTs, and Co–Mo catalysts towards (7,6) and (8,6) have also been reported.193 Fe–Ru catalyst for high selective growth of SWCNTs was demonstrated by Dai et al.194 using methane CVD. The preferential growth of (6,5) and (7,5) SWCNTs was observed at 600 °C, while (7,5), (7,6), and (8,4) SWCNTs becomes dominant at higher temperature (850 °C). It is worth mentioning that CNTs produced at such low growth temperatures to control the chirality distribution in the above studies usually suffers from low yield and poor quality since such temperature affects the SWCNTs growth efficiency.195 In addition, CNTs defect-healing during growth requires high temperature.196 Ren et al.197 reported the development of bimetallic Co–Pt catalysts which showed high selectivity toward (6,5) SWCNTs at high growth temperatures of 800 °C. The high-melting-point Co–Pt alloy is believed to provide an increased catalyst stability responsible for selective growth of SWCNTs with a narrow chirality distribution at such high temperature. Other catalysts with high chirality selectivity in SWCNT synthesis were also reported including Co–Mn,198 Co–Cr,199 FeMn–MgO200 and Fe–Cr.201

Alongside the use of porous substrates, the chirality-controlled growth of SWCNTs by catalysts supported on flat substrates such as Co–Mo on sapphire202 and Au/Al2Ox on SiO2/Si400 has also been considered. Fouquet et al.203 thermally evaporated an ultrathin layer of Co on SiO2-coated Si wafers. After annealing at 600 °C or 700 °C in vacuum, the nanotubes were grown in pure acetylene (C2H2). At 600 °C, a narrower chirality distribution of (6,5), (6,6) and (7,4) SWCNTs with the ratios of 27%, 19% and 19% respectively can be confirmed by Raman spectra. He et al.204 demonstrated the growth of SWCNTs with a narrow chirality distribution and high abundance of (6,5) SWCNTs using Co catalysts supported on SiO2. Later the same group developed a monometallic CoxMg1−xO catalyst to grow SWCNTs with a strong chiral selectivity by impregnating MgO with a Co(NO3)2 aqueous solution.188 Then, the calcination in air at high temperature (1000 °C) leads to the formation of the CoxMg1−xO solid solution. The CO reduction then took place to activate the catalyst which in turn resulted in an epitaxial formation of Co NPs on the surface of the MgO support. Consequently, Co NPs can catalyze the growth of small diameter SWCNTs with a narrow chirality distribution. The catalyst structure and the growth of SWCNTs in situ was monitored using a high-resolution TEM. On the basis of PL and electron diffraction analysis, 90% of the nanotubes in the as-prepared sample were s-SWCNTs with high chiral selectivity of (6,5) reaching up to 53% under the optimum growth temperature of 500 °C, while high enrichment in (7,6) and (9,4) was obtained at lower temperatures. The lattice-mismatched epitaxial relationship between Co and MgO, shown in Fig. 8b, is believed to be responsible for the structural uniformity and stability of the catalysts during the growth which led to the high selectivity growth of SWCNTs. These results shed some light on the high potential of catalyst NPs formed through lattice-mismatched epitaxy to be used as catalysts for efficient and controlled synthesis of single-chirality CNTs.

The catalysts formed through lattice-mismatched epitaxy can only facilitate the growth of small diameter nanotubes such as (6,5) and (7,3). However, for many potential applications, large diameter nanotubes with small bandgaps are needed. A series of Co catalysts supported on mesoporous silicates like including Co–TUD-1 and Co–SiO2 have been developed by Chen et al. to selectively grow large diameter (9,8) SWCNTs. In their primary work, a cobalt incorporated TUD-1 catalyst (Co–TUD-1) was designed that showed highly selective growth of (9,8) SWCNTs.205 The excellent selectivity towards (9,8) SWCNTs was ascribed to the strong interaction between metal NPs (Co) and support (TUD-1) which can stabilize Co clusters leading to the growth of (9,8) SWCNTs. In a subsequent study, the same group developed CoSO4/SiO2 catalyst for the growth of (9,8) SWCNTs reporting high abundance of the targeted species compared to other species.206 The UV–vis–NIR spectra showed that the abundance of (9,8) SWCNTs in the as-prepared sample was 33.5% over all nanotube species. A narrow chirality distribution was achieved after the catalyst was reduced in H2 at 540 °C. This clearly indicates the importance of H2 reduction in controlling the chirality selectivity. The catalyst's reduction at 540 °C resulted in the formation of Co NPs with a narrow size distribution that matches with the diameter of (9,8) SWCNTs. Moreover, there are two potential roles of the sulfur content in the catalyst which is decreased with increasing reduction temperature. Firstly, it limits the aggregation of Co atoms, and secondly, it forms Co–S compounds on Co NPs which enables the chirality selectivity of (9,8) SWCNTs. The group further studied the effect of catalyst calcination temperature on chirality selectivity.207 Large diameter (9,8) SWCNTs were produced after the catalyst is calcined in air at 400 °C. However, by calcination at 800 °C, selectivity towards small-diameter nanotubes were observed (Fig. 8c). The authors attributed this shift to the formation of surface cobalt oxides and silicates resulting from the removal of sulfur at higher temperature. The abundance of (9,8) (50.52% among all s-SWCNTs) was confirmed from PL spectra. Very recently, Wang et al.208 investigated the influence of three metal sulfate-based catalysts; MSO4/SiO2, M = Fe, Ni, or Co on the chirality distribution of the SWCNT growth. The results suggested that small diameter nanotubes particularly (6,5) SWCNTs were produced with Fe2(SO4)3/SiO2 catalyst, while medium and large diameter nanotubes dominated by (8,7) and (9,8) were grown on NiSO4/SiO2 and CoSO4/SiO2 catalysts respectively (Fig. 8d and e). The thermal decomposition of the three metal sulfates into different compounds has a considerable impact on the resulting catalytic activity. Sulfur maintained on the catalysts influences the selectivity toward relatively large diameter SWCNTs. These studies indicated the potential of developing metal sulfates with proper decomposition temperatures in order to control the growth of SWCNTs with a specific structure. An exciting approach called “smart poisoning” has been recently proposed by Chen et al.209 In this approach, sulfidation of Co/SiO2 catalysts was firstly carried out in a mixture of H2S and H2 to form Co9S8 NPs which were subsequently used to grow SWCNTs by CVD using CO as the carbon precursor. The chirality selectivity toward (9,8) nanotubes was demonstrated to be ∼38% among the s-SWCNTs species by PL peak intensities. Importantly, the carbon yield of sulfided catalyst (2.5 wt%) was found to be comparable to that of the non-sulfided one (2.7 wt%). The successful chirality-selective synthesis of (9,8) alongside the high carbon yield reported here provides a distinct advantage over other studies that showed a trade-off between the chiral selectivity and the growth yield. This promising method opens a new avenue to designing chirality-selective catalysts with high activity for scalable synthesis.

Solid state catalysts NPs are promising for chirality-selective synthesis since they maintain their morphology during the SWCNTs growth. This implies that the structure of SWCNTs can be effectively well-defined by epitaxial growth. Sankaran et al.210 compared the chirality distribution of the grown SWCNTs after solely tuning the composition of NixFe1−x NPs. The catalyst size and other process parameters were kept constant to decouple their influence. Consequently, a narrow chirality distribution dominated by (8,4) SWCNTs was observed with the catalyst Ni0.27Fe0.73. The shifts in the chirality distribution were attributed to the effect of altering NPs composition, which changes their crystal structure, on the lattice match of the catalyst with certain chiralities. Zhang et al.211 developed a straightforward and effective method to selectively synthesize small-diameter nanotubes arrays with narrow chirality distribution. The SWCNTs were grown on quartz substrates utilizing solid Mo2C catalysts at a proper growth parameters including low carbon feeding rate (20 sccm) for short-time growth (2 min) in hydrogen-free CVD environment. Under these conditions, only small-size Mo NPs are carbonized into stable Mo2C and can efficiently catalyze the growth of small-diameter SWCNTs arrays with a narrow chirality distribution and a density of 0.3–0.5 tubes per μm. Meanwhile, larger catalysts are inactive due to underfeeding. As a result, three major chiralities were obtained including (8,4), (8,5) and (7,6) with estimated abdundances of 46.6%, 17.5% and 8.9% respectively. Interestingly, the density of SWCNTs was considerably improved to ∼15 tubes per μm while preserving the small diameter and narrow chirality distribution when the catalyst was loaded on the sapphire substrate followed by annealing process. Later, by controlling the crystal symmetry of the catalyst surface to match with the desired chiralities, the same group was able to grow horizontal semiconducting (8,4) and metallic (12,6) SWCNTs arrays using uniform size WC and Mo2C solid catalysts respectively.212Fig. 9a shows the two steps that were proposed by the authors (a thermodynamically controlled step and a kinetically controlled step) to tune the chirality of the as-grown SWCNTs. To form uniform catalysts, a two-step process was carried out: (i) the catalyst precursors were dispersed on the pre-annealed sapphire substrates and annealed at 1100 °C for 8 h in air to transfer the aggregate precursors into monodispersed MoO3 and WO3 (ii) after reducing temperatures starting at 450 °C for MoO3 and 850 °C for WO3 were used, uniform-sized catalysts NPs of 1.36 ± 0.23 nm and 1.09 ± 0.46 nm were obtained respectively. As a comparison, when the initial temperature of MoO3 was increased to 800 °C, a broader particle size distribution of 1.59 ± 0.65 nm was observed. The average density and the abundance of (8,4) in the as-grown sample were estimated to be more than 10 tubes per micrometer and 80% respectively while the surface densities of (12,6) were found to be more than 20 tubes per micrometer with the chiral purities of 90%. This work clearly demonstrated that selective growth of SWCNTs from symmetry matching between the CNTs and the solid catalysts provide potential control over the chirality of as-grown SWCNTs, with the highest reported purity of single-chirality s-SWCNTs in a horizontal array. Further control of the catalyst size and the reducing temperature is expected to result in an increase in the purity.

Maintaining the crystal structure of the catalysts used to grow SWCNTs is very critical to realize the preferential growth of a specific chirality. Achieving this is not feasible if the catalysts with low melting point are used as high temperature process is required during the CVD process to synthesize high-quality nanotubes. Using high melting point tungsten-based bimetallic alloy as catalysts, Li et al.213 were able to produce (12,6) SWCNTs with an impressive abundance of higher than 92% (Fig. 9b), which was verified by micro-Raman measurements, UV–vis–NIR absorption spectra and the electron diffraction measurements. Due to the high melting point of W6Co7 NPs (∼2400 °C), their crystal structure and solid state were successfully maintained during the heating in vacuum to 1100 °C, as revealed by in situ TEM (Fig. 9c). HR-TEM revealed that the axis of (12,6) SWCNTs is perpendicular to the (0 0 12) planes of W6Co7 NPs. The density functional theory simulation (DFT) analysis showed the structural matching between (0 0 12) plane and (12,6) tube and poor matching with other nanotubes species which therefore resulted in the predominant growth of (12,6) SWCNTs. In a follow up study, the same group reported the selective growth of (16,0) SWCNTs in the proportion of ∼80% using intermetallic W6Co7 catalysts with high abundance of (116) planes under the optimized growth conditions (1050 °C, 150 cm3 min−1 H2 flow rate, and 200 cm3 min−1 Ar through an ethanol bubbler as carbon feeding stock).191 They attributed the highly selective growth of zigzag (16,0) SWCNTs to their structural match with the atomic arrangements of the (116) planes in W6Co7 which therefore can act as the structure templates for (16,0) SWCNTs growth (Fig. 9d). The same group introduced H2O vapor during the reduction and crystallization process of W6Co7 nanocrystals.214 This resulted in a structural modulation of W6Co7 with a large presence of (1 0 10) planes which were subsequently used as structural templates to grow (14,4) SWCNTs recording a noticeably high purity of ∼97% (Fig. 9e and f). Very recently, the effect of the W/Co ratio on the selective growth of SWCNTs has been systematically investigated by the same group.215 The W–Co bimetallic catalysts were prepared by mixing two available salts that contain tungsten and cobalt namely (NH4)6W7O24·6H2O and Co (CH3COO)2·4H2O as the catalyst precursors. X-ray diffraction (XRD) and TEM measurements confirmed the formation of W6Co7 catalysts in the W–Co bimetallic catalyst. The as-prepared catalysts were subsequently used to perform CVD of SWCNTs. The W–Co ratio has been found to be critical in controlling the selective growth of SWCNTs. By optimizing the W/Co ratio and CVD conditions, the group was able to obtain s-SWCNTs with a purity of ∼96% in which (14,4)-chirality enriched represented 62% of the content (Fig. 9g).


3.1.2.1 Controlled growth of semiconducting and metallic SWCNTs. In recent years, great effort has focussed on developing large scale, direct selective growth of pure s-SWCNTs and m-SWCNTs for their potential use in various applications. This seems to be less challenging than the single-chirality selective synthesis. The separation of SWCNTs based on their electrical type has been reported through both controlled synthesis and post-synthesis approaches. However, since the post-synthesis approach involves sophisticated physical and chemical treatments, and the use of chemicals to disperse/separate SWCNTs (e.g. surfactants), the separated nanotubes from this approach suffer from several drawbacks including low yields, contamination, defects and length shortening which in turn can negatively affect the device performance. Therefore, direct growth of single electrical type is a more favourable approach since no additional treatments and chemicals are involved.

Generally, two strategies to directly synthesize pure s-SWCNTs have been developed, namely in situ growth selective etching and catalyst engineering. The selective removal of m-SWCNTs via in situ growth selective etching is a successful strategy based on the higher chemical reactivity of m-SWCNTs as compared to the s-SWCNTs when they have identical diameters.216 Several etchants have been reported including oxygen,292 water vapor,131,217,218 hydrogen,219,220 and hydrogen peroxide. For instance, Cheng et al.221 reported the preferential etching of m-SWCNTs by introducing oxygen during the synthesis process through the floating catalyst CVD. The growth parameters involved in the synthesis were a H2 flow of 500 sccm, CH4 flow of 3 sccm, and growth temperature of 1100 °C. The oxygen flows were varied from 0 to 0.4 sccm. The optimal oxygen flow rate was found to be 0.2 sccm. The oxidation process is crucial as introducing a proper amount of oxygen can remove the m-SWCNTs mainly due to their smaller ionization potential. However, too much oxidation can result in etching and removing s-SWCNTs which may affect the selective synthesis growth. After optimizing the growth conditions, a sample with ∼90% s-SWCNTs was obtained. Liu et al.222 introduced an appropriate amount of water in the CVD growth process to remove the m-SWCNTs obtaining >97% s-SWCNTs in the as-grown sample. Although highly selective horizontally aligned s-SWCNTs arrays can be achieved using this etching approach, the diameter distribution of the as-grown SWCNTs must be very narrow to obtain highly pure s-SWCNTs samples. In a follow up study, Liu et al.217 grew s-SWCNTs by firstly controlling the diameter of the SWCNTs using uniform and stable Fe–W nanoclusters as the catalyst precursors followed by the introduction of water vapor as the etchant. As a result, a population of ∼95% s-SWCNTs was realized. To understand the etching mechanism, several reports investigating the SWCNTs etching process have been published employing various in situ techniques such as in situ Raman spectroscopy,223–225in situ environmental TEM,226 and environmental SEM.227 Zhang et al.,228 for instance, investigated the dynamic of SWCNTs etching process in real time by combining polarized optical microscope with hot-wall chemical reactor. The former was used for in situ real-time imaging tracking of the process as well as chirality identification. The latter was employed to initiate the etching process by providing high temperature (1300 K). It has been revealed that the etching sites, etching termination sites, and nanotubes structure dependence exhibited a random behaviour in air. Later, systematic research in which three chemicals (O2, H2O, and CO2) were used as the etchants was performed by the same group to study the nature of dynamic process.229 The optimum etching temperatures, etching times and flow rates were found to be 610–690 °C, 10 min in air for O2, 750–850 °C, 60 min, 200 sccm for H2O, and 830–870 °C, 30 min, 200 sccm for CO2. The chirality assignment was carried out using optical spectroscopy. The highest s-SWCNTs selectivity was achieved when the nanotubes were etched with H2O followed by CO2 and O2 respectively. This was further evidenced from the field effect transistors (FETs) fabricated based on the etched CNTs. The DOS of SWCNTs at the Fermi level has been found to play a major role in identifying the CNTs reactivity behaviour with three possible scenarios. s-SWCNTs are more reactive than the counterpart m-SWCNTs in H2O2 due to their higher DOS near the Fermi level as compared to the m-SWCNTs.230 On the contrary, m-SWCNTs become more reactive in CO2 and H2O than s-SWCNTs and thus are easily removed owing to the doping effect which leads to an up-shifting of EF position to the top of the valence band of m-SWCNTs. As such, the DOS of m-SWCNTs is higher than that of s-SWCNTs at the Fermi level. Lastly, poor selectivity is achieved in O2 as a consequence of the DOS of s-, and m-SWCNTs sitting at the same energy resulting in the strong doping effect.

Catalyst engineering is another approach to selectively synthesize s-SWCNTs since they play a critical role in the nucleation and growth of SWCNTs. Sankaran et al.231 synthesized Ni0.27Fe0.73 catalysts with precise size and composition which subsequently were used to catalyze SWCNT growth with a predominant growth of s-SWCNTs (90%). Using ceria with its ability to store oxygen that can provide an oxidative environment as a catalyst support, Li et al.232 were able to eliminate m-SWCNTs during SWCNTs growth process producing s-SWCNTs with high purity reaching an average of 98% as compared with only 65% for samples produced with silica supported catalysts. Oxygen-deficient TiO2 NPs as catalysts were also reported for enrichment of s-SWCNTs with a purity of greater than 95%.233 The selective growth of s-SWCNTs was attributed to the formation energy between s-SWCNT and TiO2 NPs which is lower than that of m-SWCNT. This formation energy can be achieved at a suitable concentration of oxygen vacancies in TiO2 NPs which in turn can be controlled by optimizing the flow rate of H2 and carbon source during the growth process. In a recent study, Cheng et al.234 reported the synthesis of s-SWCNTs with a noticeably high purity. An acorn-like, partially carbon-coated cobalt nanoparticle catalyst was designed and prepared with a uniform size (mean diameter of 3.1 nm). SWCNTs grown from these catalysts possessed a very narrow diameter distribution and small bandgap difference of ∼0.08 eV. The introduction of H2 as an etchant during the CVD growth further resulted in preferential removal of m-SWCNTs. Consequently, a s-SWCNT purity of greater than 95% was obtained. Recently, metal-free s- and m-SWCNTs were selectively synthesized using SiOx nanoparticles as a catalyst with various oxygen content.235 The oxygen deficiency in the SiOx catalyst can be controlled by altering the catalyst preparation method, heat treatment process and the ambient atmosphere during the CNTs growth. The nanoparticle size and oxygen content in the SiOx have been found to play a critical role in the selective growth of m- and s-SWCNTs. Under the optimized conditions, samples with purity of 80% and 91% m- and s-SWCNTs were obtained respectively. The purity was estimated from RBM Raman peaks using two laser excitation wavelengths (785 and 633 nm). In another study, an electro-renucleation (ERN) approach has been developed by Jiang et al.236 for the production of s-SWCNT arrays. By switching the direction of an applied electric field during the growth, m-SWCNTs can be twisted into s-SWCNTs which resulted in the production of highly pure s-SWCNT arrays with less than 0.1% residual m-SWCNTs as estimated from SEM images. Fig. 10a and b show the steps involved in the twisting process of m-SWCNTs into s-SWCNTs. According to the authors, the amount of m-SWCNTs can be further reduced to be less than 1 ppm by tuning the nanotube diameters to ∼1.3 nm. These results indicate the promising potential of this approach for practical applications of CNT electronics in which such extraordinary purity is indeed required. Cheng et al.237 reported the selective growth of s-SWCNTs from SiC NPs as a catalyst on SiO2/Si substrate using ethanol CVD. After the Si atoms at the surface of the SiC NPs were depleted during the thermal treatment (900 °C), carbon nanocaps were formed on the SiC NPs. In order to selectively etch the m-SWCNTs caps, 200 sccm H2 was introduced. The remaining s-SWCNTs caps were then grown by CVD using ethanol. Fig. 10c–f show a schematic illustrating the nucleation and growth stages for growing s-SWCNTs. On the basis of the Raman spectroscopy (with wavelengths of 532, 633, and 785 nm) and the absorption spectroscopy, s-SWCNTs contents were calculated to be 95% and 97% respectively. The high purity s-SWCNTs obtained were further used to fabricate high performance thin film FETs.

To date, very few studies on the selective growth of m-SWCNTs have been reported which is possibly due to the limited number of applications of this electronic type of nanotubes as compared to the s-SWCNTs. Harutyunyan et al.238 reported that the ratio of m- and s-SWCNTs was sensitive to the noble gas ambient used during the thermal annealing of the catalyst. The presence of H2O in He ambient favoured the growth of m-SWCNTs while the same amount of H2O in Ar ambient promoted the growth of s-SWCNTs. After the growth conditions were optimized, the content of m-SWCNTs was characterized to be 91% by Raman spectroscopy. By injecting sulfur containing-compounds (thiophene or carbon disulphide) with ferrocene into a CVD reactor, Windle et al.239 successfully achieved the direct synthesis of m-SWCNT with 95% of the tubes having a diameter of 1.1–2.0 nm. It was observed that the presence of sulfur was responsible for the retardation of the growth of Fe catalyst NPs at ∼1.6 nm enabling the selective growth of m-SWCNTs species particularly (9,9) and (12,12). By controlling the nature of substrate, Wei et al.240 successfully synthesized short aligned (<1 μm), metallic-rich SWCNTs using perpendicular Mo-containing FeMgAl layered double hydroxide (LDH) films and CH4 as the carbon source within a short growth time (15 min). The diameter of the grown nanotubes was found to be in the range of 2.0–3.0 nm which was closely matched with the mean size of the catalysts NPs at 2.7 nm. Large diameter m-SWCNTs (3.2 ± 1 nm) were produced by Liu et al. using a sulfur-containing compound (thiophene).241 As a comparison, with the absence of thiophene and under the same growth conditions, SWCNTs with an average diameter of 1.43 ± 034 nm were obtained. The selectivity towards m-SWCNTs was ascribed to the reverse relationship between the sensitivity of m-SWCNTs to oxidant and the diameter due to the lower strain. In addition, large diameter s-SWCNTs with their small band gaps behave as metals at room temperature. The electronic properties of the as-synthesized SWCNTs were evaluated using the electrical measurements of the fabricated FETs. It was demonstrated that 95% of the as-synthesized CNTs have low on/off ratios (<100) indicating the high conductivity and hence the metallic behaviour of the obtained CNTs. Using hydrogen as an etchant gas, Cheng et al.242 reported the selective removal of small-diameter s-SWCNTs from large-diameter m-SWCNTs. On the basis of absorption spectroscopy characterization, the as-grown sample contained 88% m-SWCNTs. Zhang et al.243 reported the use of SO3 as a gas-phase etchant at a temperature of 400 °C to eliminate s-SWCNTs. This method allows for large-quantity etching of s-SWCNTs (>5 mg) in a relatively shorty time (2 h). The growth of m-SWCNTs using a solid supported catalyst such as FePt NPs on a MgO substrate and FePt–Au catalyst on a MgO substrate has also been reported.244,245 Despite all these studies, the mechanism behind the preferential growth of m-SWCNTs still remains unclear. The preparation of directly grown SWCNTs with a uniform electrical type and high purity and yield has been one of the main aims of CNT research in recent years.

3.1.3. Optimization of preferential synthesis conditions. In addition to controlling the chirality of SWCNTs through the size/chemical composition of catalytic NPs, the parameters involved in CNTs growth have been found to be very important as they can significantly influence the chirality distribution. To date, the influence of several growth parameters such as feed carbon, growth temperatures and system pressure on the structure of the grown CNTs have been investigated.190 For instance, Chen et al.246 was able to compare the effect of four different carbon precursors including CO, C2H5OH, CH3OH, and C2H2 on the chirality distribution when grown on Co–Mo catalysts. When CO was used, the narrowest chirality distribution dominated by (7,6), (7,5), and (8,4) was obtained. On the other hand, C2H5OH and CH3OH produced more larger diameter tubes including (8,6), (9,5), and (8,7). This demonstrates the considerable variations of (n,m) distribution when using different carbon precursors. High growth selectivity of 95% aligned s-SWCNTs has been reported by Liu et al.247 when methanol was introduced to the growth process. This was due to the OH radical from methanol which can selectively etch m-SWCNTs because of their relatively smaller ionization potential as compared to s-SWCNTs counterpart. Kauppinen et al.195 studied the effect of introducing a small amount of ammonia (500 ppm, NH3) as an additive into floating catalyst CVD process (FC-CVD) at 880 °C. A narrow chirality distribution dominated by (13,12) SWCNTs was realized with over 90% of as-grown nanotubes having a chiral angle in the range of 20°–30° and ∼50% in the range of 27°–29°. The introduction of NH3 is believed to result in etching away of small chiral angles SWCNTs due to their high reactivity and lower stability. Very recently, the same group achieved a direct growth of SWCNTs using CO as a carbon source and introducing various amount of CO2 in FC-CVD process.248 SWCNTs films exhibiting tunable narrow chirality distribution and for the first time various colours were produced. The flow rate of CO2 introduced was adjusted to 0.0, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0 and 5.0 sccm. This corresponded to CO2 concentration of 0, 0.25, 0.37, 0.50, and 1.23 vol%, respectively. At a concentration of 0.25 vol%, a green colour thin film was obtained, while a brown colour was displayed at 0.37 vol% indicating the changes in the diameter and chirality distribution. Upon increasing the CO2 concentration, S11 and S22 peaks were red-shifted, the nanotubes diameters increased from 1.1 to 1.9 nm, and the percentage of metallic nanotubes increased from 29.8 to 46.3%. The narrowest diameter (1.0–1.5 nm) and chirality distribution (evident from the green colour of the film) were obtained at 0.25 vol% CO2. The chirality distribution became much narrower when the temperature was increased from 850 to 880 °C leading to the growth of CNTs with chirality distribution centred around (11,9) (∼17%) and narrow diameter of 1.2–1.5 nm (>98%). The role played by CO2 in the process is not clear and cannot be ascribed to the CO2 preferential etching of small diameter CNTs as proposed by other studies since the ratio of metallic nanotubes increased after the introduction of CO2. It is well known that metallic nanotubes are more reactive than semiconducting nanotubes, therefore one should expect the ratio of metallic nanotubes to decrease when etched with CO2 instead of increasing as observed here. As such, further investigation is required to understand the role of CO2 which in turns might further tailor the chirality distribution.

In addition to the carbon source and additives, the flow rate has also been found to affect the diameter249,250 and the selective enrichment of SWCNTs.251 To explain the relationship between carbon flow rates and the diameter distribution, a hypothesis has been proposed. At a fixed carbon source flow, there is an optimal diameter of catalyst NPs to nucleate SWCNTs. The larger the size variation between this optimal size and the catalyst diameter, the less likely such NPs will catalyze the growth of SWCNTs. Smaller NPs are poisoned due to “overfeeding” and larger NPs are inactive due to “underfeeding”.249 Therefore, it is critical to adjust the carbon flow rate in order to match the optimum diameter of NPs.

The pressure of the carbon source has also been reported to influence the growth of SWCNTs. Chen et al.252 designed a special CVD reactor that allows an increase of the pressure from 1 bar to 19 bar in less than 3 s resulting in identifying pressure-induced chiral selectivity. On the basis of the analysis of PL and absorption spectra, (6,5) SWCNTs had the highest abundance under 18 bar CO (48% among other s-SWCNTs species). However, when the CO pressure decreased from 18 to 2 bar, the (6,5) abundance decreased to less than 2%. In contrast, (7,6) SWCNTs were present in an abundance of 26% under 2 bar CO and decreased to 7% with increasing CO pressure, whereas the greatest abundance of (7,5) SWCNTs was found to be 28% at around 12 bar CO pressure (Fig. 11a). An increase of CO pressure increases the striking coefficient of the CO molecules on the Co NPs. Therefore, under higher CO pressure, SWCNT growth may be initiated faster due to the higher concentration of active carbon atoms available, which results in the growth of smaller diameter CNTs. On the other hand, when CO pressure is lower, the formation of a stable carbon cap is slower and catalyst aggregation during SWCNT growth is increased. This results in the growth of larger-diameter SWCNTs.

The growth temperature is another obvious parameter that was found to affect the diameter and chirality distribution of the as-produced SWCNT.190,192,197,203,253 This is due to the strong impact of the growth temperature on the size of the catalyst NPs and the decomposition of the carbon source. On the basis of these studies, it was observed that an increase in the growth temperature results in broadening of chirality distribution in the as-grown sample. For example, Resasco et al.190 systematically investigated the effect of changing synthesis temperature, catalyst support, and the composition of the gaseous feed. Considerable changes in the dominating chirality species grown with CoMo–SiO2 were observed as a function of growth temperature. At low synthesis temperature (700 °C), (6,5) SWCNTs were the dominant species with a purity of 54%, while this selectivity shifted towards larger diameter species particularly (7,6) and (8,7) SWCNTs at higher growth temperature (850 °C) with an estimated population of 15% and 13% respectively. Therefore, it can be concluded that changing the growth temperature led to an increase in CNT diameter, while no change in the chiral angle was observed. In contrast, it turned out that changing the catalyst support from SiO2 to MgO resulted in nanotubes with different chiral angles but comparable diameters. Moreover, different chirality distribution can be realized by varying the composition of the gaseous feed. Kauppinen et al.192 reported high selectivity toward (6,5) SWCNTs when the synthesis temperature was 600 °C.192 However, when the temperature increased to 750 °C, (7,5) SWCNTs became the predominant species (Fig. 11b). More recently, a new CVD approach, named “tandem plate CVD” has been developed by Zhang et al.254 By varying the temperature of growth periodically, the enrichment of SWCNTs towards small chiral angle (<10°) nanotubes, which are anticipated to have a lower interfacial formation energy than larger chiral angle tubes, was increased to up to ∼72% (Fig. 11c). DFT calculations of the interfacial formation energy between SWCNTs (with identical diameter and different chiral angles) and different catalysts (Fe55, Co55, or Ni55) are in excellent agreement with the experimental results, confirming the considerably lower interfacial formation energy of the small angle nanotubes (Fig. 11d). This method opens a new way to achieve direct growth of chirality-selected SWCNTs by designing a catalyst with low SWCNT-catalyst interfacial formation energy. Thus from the growth parameters, it is feasible that optimization of the growth conditions is the first step towards synthesizing SWCNTs in a controlled manner.

3.2. Post-synthesis purification

As mentioned in the section 3.1, direct growth is an effective way to preferentially synthesize SWCNTs enriched with semiconducting (and metallic) species of a purity above 95%222 (and 85%)242 as well as single chiral species (12,6) with a purity higher than 90%.213 Nevertheless, the impurities appear to be unavoidable in the products from selective synthesis. Given that, it has been shown that the purity of SWCNTs can be further improved via the combination of controlled synthesis with post-synthesis separation, such as ion exchange chromatography (IEX).194 Over the last two decades, a large number of efforts have been made on the post-synthesis separation techniques to prepare SWCNT species with supreme purity.255 In the following sections, the details of each individual mainstream purification approach will be presented, and their advantages and disadvantages will also be discussed.
3.2.1 Preferential destruction. After the synthesis of a mixed SWCNTs soot, SWCNTs with a certain type of electronic properties can be achieved either by liquid-processed approaches,256–261 (refer to sections from 3.2.2 to 3.2.7) or selective destruction. The latter is simpler to carry out due to the nature of a dry process. There are several well-developed techniques available, including electrical breakdown,262 gas-phase etching263 and others. However, the processed SWCNTs generally have low purity because of the limited etching precision. In most cases, these methods are appropriate in selectively destroying either metallic or semiconducting SWCNTs.

The application of the gate electrode, as shown in Fig. 12a and b, in most cases, requires the utilization of traditional substrates (for example, an oxide layer coated silicon wafer). In 2015, a junction-based electrical breakdown approach was developed and designed with the amplification of the different resistance of SWCNT pathway formed by various combinations of semiconducting and/or metallic SWCNTs (process shown in Fig. 12c).273 In detail, a voltage across the source and drain is applied and maintained between electrodes E1 and E2 in order to selectively breakdown SWCNTs stepwise. Obviously, charge carriers are required to cross at least one junction to travel from E1 to E2. The current density of pathways consisting of two metallic SWCNTs is much higher than that of those formed with at least one semiconducting SWCNT.28 As a result, metallic SWCNTs tend to be damaged more easily than semiconducting species. Improved selectivity of such breakdown is realized by applying the voltage (1 V) across electrodes E2 and E3, E3 and E4, and E1 and E4 electrodes repetitively. The transistor fabricated with this approach has a superb switching behaviour with an on/off ratio of about 1.4 × 106, which indicates the elimination of metallic SWCNTs.

Based on the previous examples, it is worth noting that the major disadvantages of the traditional selective electrical breakdown is that the broken metallic SWCNTs cannot be removed completely because the Joule heating process stops right after the breakdown of the metallic SWCNTs at their highest-temperature.276 Recently, Chiashi and colleagues designed several strategies to tackle this problem. Their first report published in 2014 involved the application of a polymer layer (α,α,α′-tris(4-hydroxyphenyl)-1-ethyl-4-isopropylbenzene and poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA)) covering the aligned SWCNT arrays.271 The polymer layer had an exothermic oxidation during the electrical breakdown, and the generated heat resulted in the propagation of the oxidation and long-length removal metallic SWCNTs up to 16.4 μm rather than a simple breakdown gap (about 100 nm in length).271 The self-sustained oxidation rate of the metallic SWCNTs was further increased to about 1000 m s−1 (estimated by simulation) by the introduction of oxygen gas saturated with water vapor to the system, in which the water droplets adsorbed/encapsulated on/in the SWCNTs act as oxidizers in the water and polymer assisted (WPA) burning.275,277

Given that the position of the breakdown points is formed randomly and the oxidation during WPA process only propagates along one direction,275 additional efforts were made by the same group to overcome the shortcomings and realize full-length removal of metallic SWCNTs in an array.274 As shown in Fig. 12d, an additional Au electrode was placed between the top and bottom ones by thermal evaporation. A small gap (about 1.5 μm) was thus formed between the middle and bottom electrodes, where the conventional electrode breakdown was performed in air at first. As a result, some gaps (about 100 nm in length) in the metallic SWCNTs were created close to the bottom electrode. The middle contact was then removed by iodine-based etchant. After the application of PMMA layer and introduction of water vapor-saturated oxygen atmosphere, the full-length removal of metallic SWCNTs was achieved consistently from the initial breakdown points with the help of WPA approach. Multiple transistors were established with the removal of polymer residue and deposition of several parallel electrodes along the array containing purely semiconducting SWCNTs.274

Recent progress in this field reveals several advantages of electrical breakdown, including the easy nature of the process in the fabrication of transistors, limited level of impurities in the purified product (the process is generally performed without dispersing agents), and the ability to prepare high-density arrays of semiconducting nanotubes.278 However, there are still some shortcomings. Firstly, full-length removal of metallic SWCNTs is difficult to realize without additional measures, which complicates the purification. Secondly, the limited choices of substrates (for the application of gate voltage especially in the case of full-length destruction) might hinder its practical application. Thirdly, destruction of semiconducting SWCNTs is challenging due to their less reactive nature. Fourthly, semiconducting SWCNTs might be sacrificed when they are bundled with/close to the “burning” metallic counterparts due to the local excessive heat produced by the oxidation. Fifthly, the process is not efficient in the fabrication of many transistors since each one has to be treated individually.


3.2.1.2 Laser-assisted selective destruction. Apart from electrical breakdown, selective destruction of SWCNTs can also be achieved by laser irradiation under appropriate conditions, as shown in Table 4. One of the earliest reports was published by Iijima et al. in 2003, in which laser irradiation (488 and 515 nm) was introduced during the oxidation of SWCNTs in 25.5 wt% H2O2.279 The analysis of the Raman spectra showed that semiconducting SWCNTs with specific diameters were selectively removed when the wavelength of incident light is close to the gap energy. For example, when a 515 nm laser was used for 5 min to irradiate the SWCNT/H2O2 suspension, the BWF feature (at the lower wavenumber region of G band) became more distinct as the intensity of the RBM peak at 184 cm−1 decreased. This suggested that the metallic content in the SWCNT mixture was increased by the removal of the semiconducting SWCNTs with a diameter of 1.33 nm (the S33 of such SWCNTs is close to 2.41 eV (∼515 nm)).280 One year later, a similar technique (Laser Resonance Chirality Selection, LARCS) was developed based on the identical principle (resonant effect: absorption of the energy from the incident beam is dramatically enhanced for certain type of SWCNTs when the electronic transitions are matched with the exciting photon energy) to selectively remove SWCNTs with specific chiral index via intense laser irradiation.281

Apart from preferential removal of semiconducting SWCNTs, it has been found that the metallic SWCNTs are more sensitive to the incident laser when they are selectively functionalized with 4-brominebenzenediazonium.282 Briefly, a SWCNT dispersion was prepared by a typical sonication/centrifugation processes with the presence of dispersing agent (Triton X-405), and then the mixture was treated with 4-brominebenzenediazonium which selectively binds to metallic SWCNTs because of the presence of electrons near the Fermi level.287 The treated SWCNTs were deposited on a substrate before laser irradiation. The intensity of the metallic species in RBM region, (12,0), (8,5) and (9,3), decreased significantly (by 85% after 80 min) while the signal for (16,0) remained constant during the irradiation period. In addition, the BWF feature (due to the electron–phonon interaction of metallic species) at the tangential region (G band) of Raman spectra became less obvious, which indicates a preferential removal of metallic SWCNTs as well. This result suggested that the functionalized metallic species are more reactive than semiconducting counterparts. The removal of metallic species was also achieved without the addition of small molecules by the application of xenon-lamp irradiation.283 It was observed that smaller diameter (d < 1 nm) semiconducting and metallic SWCNTs were removed after 60 min irradiation (500 W) and the final purity of the semiconducting SWCNTs approached 95%.

The relationship between the laser intensity and the reaction time was first investigated by Zandian et al. in 2009.284 It was revealed that rapid burnout of the SWCNTs can occur with high laser intensity (4 × 107–9 × 107 W cm−2) within 2 minutes while a relatively gentle laser (8 × 106 W cm−2) requires tens of minutes to remove the SWCNTs. Given the higher energy photons of lower wavelengths, they tend to damage both semiconducting and metallic SWCNTs. In order to overcome this problem, a laser with a single wavelength of 2 μm was applied to perform irradiation on a mixture of SWCNTs on silicon substrates.285 When exposed to such low-energy photons (0.62 eV) for 20 s, metallic SWCNTs were completely damaged during laser irradiation. The authors attributed the removal of metallic SWCNTs to the different electronic properties of metallic and semiconducting SWCNTs, which results in the movement of free electrons with metallic species responding swiftly to the electromagnetic field oscillation while semiconducting counterparts are damaged only when the photon energy fits their band energy.285 However, a conflicting view on the relationship between the wavelength of the laser and the type of the removed SWCNTs was published recently,286 where 473 and 632 nm laser showed evidence of the removal of metallic SWCNTs while 785 and 1064 nm laser removed semiconducting species. The authors proposed that fitting of gap energy M11 (S11 and S22) to the incident photon energy enables the destruction of metallic (semiconducting) SWCNTs.286 Further efforts, including both experimental and the theoretical studies288 are required to confirm and reveal the science behind laser irradiation.

The major benefits of laser irradiation may involve the potential in performing large area purification, and the compatibility in both liquid and solid systems.279 However, the main disadvantages of this approach include the unclear working mechanism and high energy consumption.

In addition to the selective removal of m-SWCNTs, the s-SWCNTs can also be etched by choosing a proper oxidant (NO2).293 In this work, pristine SWCNTs were stored in a U-type quartz tube under a NO2 atmosphere and the tube was then placed in an oil bath for the etching reaction (16 h at 220 °C), as shown in Fig. 13b. Under such reaction conditions, the semiconducting SWCNTs were dissociated into gaseous products, NO and CO and metallic species remained. The colour of the SWCNTs solid (dispersion) changed from black to gold (red) after the oxidation, which implies the enrichment of m-SWCNTs in the product, as shown in Fig. 13c. The authors proposed the selective absorption of NO2 on the walls of semiconducting SWCNTs. However, this is contradictory with previous theoretical calculations, which showed that NO2 can selectively be adsorbed on specific types of SWCNTs and may result in a severe structural deformation and dissociation of SWCNT walls.297,298 Further theoretical and experimental evidence is still required to determine the exact mechanism of this approach.

Recently, Zhao et al. developed an igniter-assisted gas-phase etching approach, by which both SWCNTs enriched with semiconducting or metallic species were prepared by choosing appropriate “igniter”.263 Briefly, after the initial growth of SWCNTs on quartz, the substrate was immersed in an “igniter” solution (1 wt%), sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) or 2,3,9,10-tetra-(5-hexyl-thiophen-2-yl)-1,4,6,8,11,13-hexaaza-pentacene (4HP), for 12 h at room temperature during which SDS and 4HP selectively adsorb on the metallic and semiconducting SWCNTs, respectively. After the immersion, as shown in Fig. 13d and e, the diameter of the m-SWCNT on the right side increased more than 0.7 nm while there was no obvious change in the diameter of s-SWCNT on the left. This implied that SDS molecules (igniter) selectively adsorbed on the surface of m-SWCNTs. As a result, the m-SWCNTs covered by SDS tended to be oxidized and removed after the gas-phase etching, as shown in Fig. 13f. During the process, since these small molecules have a lower oxidation temperature than that of the SWCNTs, they acted as an “igniter” to accelerate the oxidation of the wrapped SWCNTs via providing additional heat.263 In contrast, the bare SWCNTs with different electronic properties remained under the etching conditions.

It is worth mentioning that the chemical reactivity differences between SWCNTs with different chiralities were investigated. Kataura et al.299 compared the reactivity of SWCNTs during oxidation in air and hydrogen peroxide. PL and Raman spectra were recorded to monitor the chirality distribution before and after the oxidation process. Under air oxidation conditions, rapid decomposition of the nanotubes that had a higher chiral angle and smaller diameter was observed. This result suggests that small chiral angle SWCNT enrichment is possible by preferential combustion of large chiral-angle SWCNTs in air. Using individual suspended SWCNTs, Liu et al.300 further studied the relationship between nanotubes chirality and their chemical reactivity through TEM. The nanotubes chirality assignment was performed on the basis of nanobeam electron diffraction. The chemical stability of an individual nanotube was subsequently evaluated by TEM after an oxidation treatment in air at different temperatures (400, 450, 470, 490 °C) took place. The results suggest that diameter, metallicity and chirality of SWCNTs play an obvious role in their reactivity to O2. Unlike Kataura's observation, large diameter and higher chiral angle s-SWCNTs in this study exhibited more stability against the air treatment, suggesting that large diameter and high chiral angle s-SWCNTs can be selectively obtained.

The gas-phase etching provides several advantages, including the ability to purify both metallic and semiconducting SWCNTs and the potential for large-scale production. However, relatively lower purity compared to that of liquid processing techniques and the lack of purification of single chiral SWCNTs are the major shortcomings.

Microwave has also been applied with solution based separation technique to purify the as-synthesized SWCNTs by Shen et al. in 2014.308 In detail, the SWCNTs were dispersed with a solution of nitronium tetrafluoroborate (NO2BF4, NTFB) by ultrasonication (10 min), which was followed by the exposure to microwave irradiation for 2 h at 60 °C. A final thermal treatment was performed at 1000 °C in Ar atmosphere for 30 min to remove the adsorbates on the SWCNTs. The selective removal of metallic SWCNTs results from the abundance of charge density at Fermi level, which makes them tend to donate electrons to an electron acceptor (NO2+) to form covalent bonds (as a result, the dispersibility of SWCNTs was enhanced and they could be easily removed as the supernatant by centrifuge) as well as have higher reactivity with microwave compared to semiconducting species.

High throughput and low power consumption are the most significant advantages of the microwave-assisted purification. Nevertheless, this method so far has only been applied to remove metallic and leave semiconducting SWCNTs, which makes it less competitive than other techniques. In addition, the selectivity (the purity is generally below 90%) is not as high as most of the solution-based processes.

Recently, a direct current injection was developed by the same group, in which interdigitated electrodes were used to selectively inject current into metallic individuals in an array of SWCNTs.314 The process is composed of four major steps: (1) the growth of SWCNTs via CVD, (2) fabrication of interdigitated electrodes, (3) application of pulsed voltages to cause Joule heating selectively through metallic SWCNTs, and (4) reactive ion etching to remove exposed metallic SWCNTs and dissolving the residue of thermocapillary resist by acetone. Among the entire process, the choice of low work-function electrode material is crucial because it is important to obtain sufficiently high Schottky barrier between these metals and s-SWCNTs to block the current flow effectively and thus eliminate the dramatic heating in these SWCNTs.313 Typical materials can be used as electrodes include Ti, Al, and Mo.314

The most obvious advantage of thermocapillary-assisted purification is the ultrahigh purity of the sorted s-SWCNTs, which has the great potential to satisfy the technical requirements for low-power digital circuits (>99.9999%).316 However, there are several disadvantages as well, including the multistep fabrication, the possibility of residues and slow scanning speed of the light source (0.4 μm s−1).311

An obvious reduction in the signal intensity of S22 and S33 was observed after dielectrophoresis process, which indicates the effective purification of metallic SWCNTs. However, the peak intensity (at about 740 nm) of the metallic SWCNTs-enriched dispersion is low implying that the concentration of the purified SWCNTs is low though the exact concentration of the sorted species was not provided.

An approach based on free electrophoresis with a simple vertical column was developed by Ihara et al.,328 which has the potential to improve the yield significantly (to a few hundred micrograms from a single operation). As shown in Fig. 16e, platinum wires were connected to the top and bottom side of the tube which was used as the separation chamber. A constant voltage (30 V) was applied across the chamber. The homogeneously dispersed SWCNTs then began to accumulate at the top and bottom region of the chamber, as shown in Fig. 16f, and two layers of SWCNTs were separated by an intermediate layer. The optical spectra of cathode- and anode-fraction and reference SWCNTs are shown in Fig. 16g. The intensity of M11 (S11 and S22) of the cathode-fraction SWCNTs increases (decreases) after the electrophoresis. In contrast, the intensity of S11 and S22 (M11) of the anode-fraction SWCNTs increases (decreases), as shown in Fig. 16g. Though the exact nature of the process is not fully understood, the authors suggested that there may be a change in the specific gravity of metallic and semiconducting SWCNTs under the influence of an electric field and the semiconducting-enriched SWCNT layer has a larger specific gravity than that of metallic-enriched layer.

The advantage of the matrix-free dielectrophoresis is its simple procedure especially when a constant voltage is applied. In addition, it shows the ability to achieve both metallic and semiconducting enriched fractions. However, the experimental purity of these separated fractions is not high enough to be practically applied though the theoretical purity of the metallic species is 99%.323 Furthermore, it, so far, has not been shown that this technique is appropriate for targeting single-chiral species.

The first work applying gel-based electrophoresis in sorting SWCNTs into different electronic properties was reported by Tanaka et al.321 in 2008. As shown in Fig. 16h, the SWCNT dispersion was first mixed with melted gel in a buffer solution (tris(hydroxymethyl)aminomethane) and the mixture was then added to a glass tube filled with agarose gel. Gel-based (di)electrophoresis has a higher resolution for the separation of SWCNTs by electronic properties compared with that of matrix-free condition. In contrast, the tedious and nontrivial post removal of the gel is its main issue.

In detail, it could change the structure of the surfactant coating layer by affecting the electron-transfer between redox molecules and SWCNTs. In terms of PFO related polymers, the reducing condition could enhance the dispersion yield with a lowering of selectivity, whereas oxidizing conditions do the opposite.

It is well known that the s-SWCNTs with small diameter and large bandgap are appropriate for photovoltaic devices. As reported by Wang et al.352 in 2014, poly(3-alkylthiophene) was used to disperse and purify SWCNTs in order to fulfil this requirement. The polythiophene-assisted separation was carried out using a probe sonication (for 30 min), followed by a centrifuge step (17000 rpm, 1 h). In addition, the yield of the SWCNTs was improved with the length of the alkyl side chain (from 4 to 31%) with no obvious loss in selectivity, which was mainly due to the flexible backbone, side chains and their regioregularity, and those factors together resulted in an increase in the contact area between the chain and the surface of SWCNTs.352

In addition to the purification associated with electronic properties and diameters, promising results of length sorting of SWCNTs using polymers have recently been reported by Gui et al.,353 where two different polymers, namely polymethacrylate (PMAA) and polystyrene sulfonate (PSS), were found to be effective in separating deoxycholate or cholate suspended SWCNTs aqueous dispersion. As shown in Fig. 17d, PMAA or PSS was added to the sodium deoxycholate SWCNT dispersion to a final concentration of 1 wt%, followed by incubation for 4 h at room temperature. Then, a mild centrifugation (5 min at 943 rad s−1) was applied to partially precipitate SWCNTs from the dispersion. The pellet was redispersed by sodium cholate (2 wt%) and labelled as the 1% sample. The same process was repeated several times to prepare more fractions. At each step, a certain amount of polymer was added to the supernatant to adjust the concentration to be 2, 4 and 6%. As shown in Fig. 17e, 4% PMAA sample was abundant in short SWCNTs (<0.4 μm) while the majority part of 1% sample was long SWCNTs. Statistically, the length of the SWCNTs in each fraction decreased from 650 to 250 nm with the PMAA concentration (Fig. 17f). The clear trend in the length sorting was attributed to phase separation (SWCNT enriched pellet phase versus aqueous polymer supernatant phase) that smaller SWCNTs contributed more entropy to the free energy of the pellet phase than longer ones with identical mass, and this resulted in an easier phase separation for the latter.353

The versatility in sorting SWCNTs (via electronic property, chirality, diameter or length) is the major advantage of the polymer/small molecule-assisted purification. However, as with many other additives, post removal of these polymers/small molecules is required for certain applications. In addition, it can be difficult to control the properties of the synthesized polymers, including the purity and the polydispersity.

In addition to the separation by electronic properties and enantiomers, DGU is shown to be effective in separating SWCNTs into empty and filled individuals.137 Briefly, arc-discharge SWCNTs were dispersed into D2O with SC via gentle sonication and centrifuge to remove bundles but leave both empty and filled individuals. It can be concluded based on these studies that DGU is a powerful technique in separating SWCNTs by diameter, electronic property, chirality, enantiomer as well as empty/filled state. However, this technique has several issues to be addressed before commercial application, including time and the energy intensive process of high-speed ultracentrifugation, difficulties in the preparation of density gradient media and low yield of CNTs obtained.

In 2011, single surfactant multicolumn gel chromatography (SS-MUGEC) was developed by Liu et al.,44 where 13 different chiral species were separated. As shown in Fig. 19a, the SWCNT dispersion is loaded at the top of a series of vertically placed columns. SWCNTs with the strongest interaction with the gel gradually replace others with increasing the loading of the dispersion. High-concentration surfactant eluent (5% SDS) is used to collect SWCNTs bound in each individual column. In addition to the exceptional ability of GC to sort SWCNTs by chirality, this technique was further improved and managed to isolate enantiomers for 9 different (n,m) species.365 Briefly, each collected semiconducting fractions in SS-MUGEC was collected and separated repeatedly by the identical process based on overloading of the dispersion. Because of the achiral nature of SDS, the SDS coatings on two enantiomers for one certain (n,m) species are regarded the same. Thus, the interaction difference between different enantiomers and the gel bed is not from the different SDS coverage. Simulations revealed that the difference in the adsorption energy of two (6,5) enantiomers on the gel is 0.519 kcal mol−1, which is attributed to the different nature of adsorption sites on SWCNT/SDS hybrids with different enantiomers.365

The most frequently used stationary phase in the GC sorting is Sephacryl gel. However, this is expensive and limits the commercialization of this technique. In order to tackle this problem and reduce the economic cost of the process, Wang et al.376 recently synthesized a new type of gel from guar gum powder, glutaraldehyde (cross-linker) and HCl (catalyst) with a facile, low cost method, which showed the potential to replace the expensive Sephacryl gel. Although a new method is required to realize the separation of SWCNTs by chirality or even handedness, there are many excellent results in sorting SWCNTs into fractions with different electronic properties. The m-SWCNTs have the lowest hydrophobic interaction with the gel bed and eluted out of the column while semiconducting species, especially large diameter counterparts, stay in the gel bed and can be collected by using 1 wt% DOC as the eluent.

There is no doubt that GC is a strong and powerful technique in separating SWCNTs in a preferential way. However, the current GC procedures still do not provide single metallic chiral species with high purity with the exception of (10,4). Additionally, the cost of the gel will need to be addressed without losing the effectiveness in purification.

The selective partition of SWCNTs in an aqueous two-phase system was initially observed by Khripin et al. in 2013.385 PEG/DX was the first polymer two-phase system used in this field. It was observed that the partition behavior of SWCNTs is highly related to the diameter. When small-diameter CoMoCAT SWCNTs (∼0.8 nm in diameter) were subjected to the PEG/DX system, SWCNTs with smaller-diameter tended to move to the more hydrophilic PEG-enriched top phase while larger-diameter SWCNTs were observed in the more hydrophobic DX-enriched bottom phase. However, separation based on electronic properties was observed when large-diameter arc discharge SWCNTs (∼1.35 nm in diameter) was used as the parent material. In this case, metallic/semiconducting SWCNTs partitioned into more hydrophilic/hydrophobic phase enriched with DX/PEG. Though there was no chirality separation observed at that time, it built the foundation of later advances in this technique.

Just one year later, several papers reported the successful sorting of SWCNTs into highly pure chiral species, including both metallic (such as (5,5) and (6,6)) and semiconducting species (such as (6,5), (7,5) and several others).386,387 With the assistance of counter-current chromatography, fractions enriched with enantiomers ((+)(7,5) and (−)(7,5)) were also achieved388 In addition, some larger diameter metallic species ((10,10), (9,9) and (8,8)) were obtained by Fagan et al. though multistep ATPE.389

In addition to the surfactants, ssDNA sequences have also been used as dispersing agents in the separation of SWCNTs by ATPE.392 As shown in Fig. 20b, 23 types of (n,m) species were isolated with the aid of ssDNA, including armchair, zigzag and chiral SWCNTs. In addition, CD spectra shows that the application of ssDNA in ATPE has the ability to isolate SWCNTs by handedness. It is worth noting that this technique achieved the wildest coverage in chirality as well as enantiomers with exceptional level of purity (see Table 14).153 Recently, Streit et al.393 exchanged strong binding bile salt on SWCNTs with ssDNA in a mixture of methanol and water, followed by precipitation with isopropanol. The methods take only 10 minutes with above 90% conversion in the replacement of dispersing agent. It was shown that subsequent ATPE on such dispersant-exchanged dispersion maintained the sorting ability of ssDNA in terms of chirality and handedness. It is anticipated that such an exchange process paves the way for the commercial application of ssDNA-assisted ATPE since the original bile salt-dispersed SWCNTs dispersion has very high concentration (10 mg mL−1).

ATPE is regarded as one of the potential techniques appropriate for future commercial applications because of high selectivity in chirality and handedness, broad coverage of diameter separation, and facile process. However, the removal of the polymers as well as other additives used in the process has been proven to be nontrivial.393

Among all of the post-synthesis techniques in purification of SWCNTs, GC and ATPE seem to have the highest selectivity and greatest potential to be applied on an industrial scale. Overall, liquid processing-based techniques generally provide high yield of separated SWCNTs as compared to the selective breakdown. However, the removal of the introduced additives could be time consuming and complicated. In addition, the preparation of SWCNTs normally include labour-intensive sonication and centrifugation processes. Such processes usually reduce the length of pristine SWCNTs, which is known to show adverse influence on the properties of the materials.

4. Conclusion and perspectives

Single electronic type/chirality pure nanotubes are of paramount importance for their wide-ranging advanced applications. Great efforts have been devoted to obtain samples with highly enriched nanotubes species through both direct controlled synthesis and post-synthesis separation approaches. To this end, several critical parameters have been identified to play critical roles in controlling the chirality selection and recognition during the growth/separation process. This includes CVD conditions (i.e. growth temperature, carbon source feeding, system pressure), substrates, size, chemical nature and structure of the catalysts, polymer/surfactant type, centrifugation parameters, etc. Significant progress has been made in understanding the relationship between those parameters and the chirality selectivity in SWCNTs which has undoubtedly resulted in considerable improvements toward the realization of nanotubes with well-defined properties. Up-to-now, a variety of seeds and templets such as fullerenes for the structure-controlled growth of SWCNTs have been intensively explored. In addition, several approaches have also been proposed and implemented such as cloning and organic synthesis routes (i.e. cap formation and carbon nanorings). Moreover, a number of strategies have been developed in which high selectivity towards specific type/chirality has been achieved such as growth selective etching and catalyst engineering.

On the other hand, intensive studies have been performed to develop a simple, rapid and effective post purification methods to achieve high-throughput SWCNTs separation with reasonable purity and yield. Indeed, these efforts have seen the development of various promising separation techniques such as those based on solution processed approaches (e.g. ion exchange chromatography and density gradient ultracentrifugation) and selective destruction (e.g. electrical breakdown and laser irradiation).

Despite these achievements, many challenges still remain. First, the low yield obtained from seeded growth methods is a clear limitation that can be solved by optimizing the growth and pre-treatments conditions to effectively activate ends of seeds. Second, catalysts have been found to be very crucial for controlled growth of SWCNTs. The selectivity of catalysts relies on several factors including diameter, composition, morphology and properties (i.e. melting point). Therefore, it is important to design catalysts with controlled diameter and structure that match with the desired nanotube species and most importantly with high melting point to maintain their structure fixed at high temperatures during the epitaxial growth of chirality-pure SWCNTs. Tungsten-based bimetallic compounds (e.g. W6Co7) used as catalysts are suitable candidates for chirality-controlled growth of nanotubes, since they possess high melting points and unique atomic arrangements. Nevertheless, while these catalysts showed excellent selectivity towards large diameter nanotubes, other solid-state catalysts with high melting points and various structures are needed to grow various chiral species required for many applications. Third, the CVD conditions need also to be taken into consideration. Optimization of CVD parameters is critical to obtain the proper environment that is suitable for the nucleation and growth. The required properties of the catalysts for selective should also be explored further. Fourth, in spite of the current characterization techniques used to determine the abundance of chiral species in the resulting samples such as Raman spectroscopy, TEM, absorption spectroscopy, PL, and electron diffraction, there is a real necessity for developing other techniques that are more reliable, accurate, convenient and fast, particularly for large diameter species. Fifth, it is noted that recent efforts have focused on the s-SWCNTs fractions for their wide range of applications, however, m-SWCNTs are of great importance for other applications such as interconnects. Sixth, despite the considerable achievements and progress in separating SWCNTs based on their electrical type, diameter, length, chirality and even handedness using the various post-synthesis purification techniques, the underlying mechanisms behind the selectivity of the nanotubes targeted and the parameters involved in the process are not fully understood. Further investigations with profound insights into the mechanism of these techniques are required to simplify the separation protocols, enhance the purity and yield of the separated nanotubes and to extend the functionality of these methods to chirally separate the nanotubes instead of only being able to separate them on the basis of their electrical type (e.g. gas phase etching and microwave-assisted purification). Seventh, despite the exceptional purity and large-scale capability of several post-synthesis methods such as gel chromatography and polymer-wrapping, the high cost associated with their chemicals and their relatively low yield (well below the industrial level: 1 mg mL−1) make them scientifically and commercially inapplicable. As such, scalable and high yield methods are still need to be developed.

While SWCNTs-based applications have undoubtedly experienced dramatic advances in recent years, it is likely to see further improvements in the near future as a result of the increased availability of single type/chirality nanotubes with high purity, yield and low-cost.
