Abstract

As an emerging class of luminescent nanomaterials, carbon quantum dots (CQDs) have recently shown enormous potential for optoelectronic applications on account of their characteristic broad emission, tunable fluorescence emission, high thermal stability, and low cytotoxicity. In this review, we will update the latest research progress achieved in CQDs, including their synthesis, optical properties, luminescence mechanism, and applications in optoelectronics. Mainly reviewed here are their room temperature phosphorescence, delayed fluorescence properties, as well as their optoelectronic applications including light-emitting diodes, lasing, solar cells, and photodetectors. Finally, current problems and challenges of CQD-based optoelectronics applications are discussed with an eye on future development. We hope that this review will provide critical insights to inspire new exciting discoveries in the area of CQDs from both fundamental and practical standpoints so that the realization of their potential in the optoelectronic areas can be facilitated.

1. Introduction

Carbon quantum dots (CQDs) are typically referred to as a class of zero-dimensional (0D) carbon nanoparticles with sizes below 10 nm, whose electronic bandgap structures are largely influenced by the remarkable quantum confinement effect.1 Since their first discovery in 2006, CQDs have attracted intensive attention due to their unique advantages compared with fluorescent dye molecules and conventional semiconductor quantum dots, such as high luminescence, biocompatibility, water solubility, excellent photostability, and low toxicity.2–4 Generally, CQDs prepared from solution are indigenously functionalized with abundant surface groups, especially oxygen related functional groups, such as carboxyl and hydroxyl, which impart excellent water solubility and suitable chemically reactive groups for surface passivation and functionalization.5 More importantly, the optical properties such as tunable photoluminescence ranging from deep ultraviolet to near-infrared (NIR) and exceptionally efficient multiphoton up-conversion of CQDs can be tuned by their size, shape, surface functional groups and heteroatom doping.6 These intriguing properties afford CQDs a variety of potential biological applications such as cell imaging, cancer therapy, and drug delivery.7 In 2010, Pan et al. successfully cut graphene sheets into blue luminescent CQDs via a hydrothermal route, expanding the application of graphene-based materials to optoelectronics such as photovoltaic devices, light emitting diodes, photodetectors, and photocatalysis.8 In 2013, a facile and high-output method for the fabrication of CQDs with a quantum yield (QY) as high as ca. 80% was developed, marking a significant advance in this area.9 However, most luminescent CQDs show obvious excitation-dependent fluorescence originating from surface-defects serving as capture centers for excitons, which results in fundamental limitations to the effectiveness of carrier injection for optoelectronic applications.10,11 Notably, we have first demonstrated monochrome electroluminescent light-emitting diodes (LEDs) based on bright multicolor bandgap fluorescent CQDs directly as an active emission layer.12 Furthermore, we have demonstrated multicolored narrow bandwidth emission (full width at half maximum of 30 nm) from triangular CQDs for high color-purity full-color LEDs, opening up the great prospects of CQDs for the next-generation display technology.13 In addition, CQD-based random lasers14 and wide-color gamut backlight displays15 have been first realized by our group as well.

Triplet-excited-state-involved materials have attracted tremendous attention for their higher electroluminescence efficiency than their fluorescent counterparts owing to the allowable utilization of the three quarters of the electrically generated excitons for light emission.16–18 Surprisingly, novel room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) and thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) properties have been discovered by embedding CQDs into various matrices, including zeolites,19 polyvinyl alcohol,20,21 polyurethane,22 potassium aluminum sulfate,23 and recrystallized urea/biuret.24 Moreover, CQDs with intrinsic RTP properties without the need for additional matrix compositing have also been reported recently, demonstrating promising alternative RTP materials based on the CQDs for highly efficient optoelectronic devices.

There have been already a number of excellent reviews focusing on different aspects of CQDs, such as their synthesis, surface functionalization, photoluminescence properties and biological applications.25–28 In this review, we will primarily update the latest significant developments in the synthesis, intriguing luminescence properties and mechanisms such as RTP and TADF, and the applications of CQDs in optoelectronic devices, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Finally, key challenges, feasible improvements and perspectives of CQD-based optoelectronics for future developments are highlighted.

2. Synthesis

Synthetic approaches for CQDs are generally classified into two categories – “top-down” and “bottom-up”. The former involves breaking down or cleaving larger carbon structures, such as graphite,2 carbon nanotubes,29 carbon soot,30 activated carbon31 and graphite oxide,32 to smaller ones by methods like arc discharge,1 laser ablation2 and electrochemical oxidation.33,34 Such methods can produce monodispersed CQDs with precisely defined molecular structure and size. However, they are difficult, tedious, expensive, and of low production yield. And the resulting CQDs are prone to π–π stacking due to the lack of functional groups. We here focus on the bottom-up methods that are realized by the fusion of organic molecules under thermal35/hydrothermal36/solvothermal37 conditions or carbonization of organic precursors. In general, bottom-up methods offer relatively high yield and the convenience to introduce multiple heteroatom doping by readily inheriting from the organic precursors during synthesis. The precursors chosen often determine the physicochemical properties of the resulting CQDs such as the size, crystallinity, oxygen/nitrogen content, fluorescence characteristics including QY, colloidal stability, and compatibility. Over the past few years, our laboratory has developed highly efficient red emissive CQDs (R-CQDs) with a QY up to 53% by solvothermally treating 1,3-dihydroxynaphthalene and KIO4 in ethanol solution (Fig. 2a).37 More importantly, we recently have prepared high color-purity, narrow bandwidth (FWHM of 29 to 30 nm) and multicolored (from blue to red) emission triangular CQDs (T-CQDs) with a QY up to 54 to 72%. The synthesis is conducted by choosing a three-fold symmetric phloroglucinol (PG) as the reagent (a triangulogen) together with a tri-molecular reaction route designed into the neighboring active –OH and –H groups for 6-membered ring cyclization, propagating to the target high color-purity narrow bandwidth emission T-CQDs (NBE-T-CQDs) (Fig. 2b–f).

3. Optical properties

Photoluminescence (PL) is one of the most important characteristics of CQDs and the basis of their various applications. To date, CQDs of various sizes and different PL emission wavelengths ranging from ultraviolet (UV) to near-infrared (NIR) have been prepared.38 In addition, intriguing room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) and thermally activated delayed fluorescence (TADF) properties of CQDs have been discovered recently.

3.1 UV-vis absorption of CQDs

CQDs typically show obvious absorption in the UV region (230–320 nm) with a tail extending into the visible region due to the π-conjugated electrons in the sp2 atomic framework and the chemical groups connected at the edge. The broad peak at ∼230 nm is ascribed to the π–π* transition of aromatic CC bonds, whereas the shoulder at 300 nm is attributed to the n–π* transition of CO bonds or other connected groups.28,34 Furthermore, the size of π-domains, shape, composition or structure of hybridization derivatives such as functional groups and surface passivation can also contribute to the absorption characteristics of CQDs. The bandgap fluorescent CQDs generally show strong excitonic absorption bands in the UV-vis absorption spectra due to the quantum confinement effect (QCE) (Fig. 3a).37 Furthermore, the red-shifted excitonic absorption bands from blue to red fluorescence are consistent with the gradually increasing size of CQDs. In addition, various functional groups (C–OH, CO, O–CO, CN, etc.) introduced during the growth of CQDs could form surface states with energy levels between π and π* states of CC,9,47 and induce absorption bands due to the electron transitions within one or several of these groups. Usually, the CQDs are dominated by surface-defect or molecular states, leading to a weak emission within only UV region (Fig. 3b).39 And the absorption wavelength may increase with the emission wavelength red shift after doping and surface passivation, which is a common phenomenon for multi-emission CQDs. For example, Sun synthesized S,N codoped CQDs, with two absorption bands at 550 and 595 nm which can be attributed to the π–π* and n–π* of CS and SO, respectively, and were related to the doping of sulfur.40

3.2 Fluorescence properties of CQDs

CQDs are a new class of nanomaterials that have attracted significant attention in the past decade. Two popular models have been proposed to explain the fluorescence mechanism of CQDs even though the exact origin of their fluorescence emission remains debatable and more research is needed in order to paint a clearer picture of the mechanism of their fluorescence emission. One is based on the bandgap emission in the conjugated π-domains, while the other is related to surface defect-states, which primarily manifest the edge effect.
3.2.1 Fluorescence emission from bandgap transitions of conjugated π-domains. The quantum confinement effect (QCE) is the major feature of CQDs, which occurs when CQDs are smaller than their exciton Bohr radius. For CQDs with an intact carbon core and fewer surface chemical groups, the bandgap transition of conjugated π-domains is thought to be the major intrinsic PL center.28 Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations have clearly demonstrated that the bandgaps of CQDs decrease approximately from 7 eV for benzene to 2 eV for CQDs consisting of 20 aromatic rings (Fig. 4a).41 Using DFT and time-dependent DFT calculations, Sk et al. also found out that the PL of CQDs originates from the QCE of π-conjugated electrons in the sp2 atomic framework and can be tuned by their size, edge configuration, and shape.42 Typically, the localized states at zig-zag edged CQDs lower the energy of the conduction band and thus reduce the bandgap, which is distinctive compared with the arm-chair edged CQDs. Therefore, it is expected that the similarly sized CQDs with a zig-zag edge would narrow the bandgap and consequently red-shift the fluorescence emission.

Our group successfully synthesized red fluorescent CQDs with no chemical modification by direct electrochemical exfoliation of graphene in a K2S2O8 solution and identified isolated sp2 domains with a diameter of 3 nm (Fig. 4b and c).33 This is the first report on the direct observation of “molecular” sp2 domains with a diameter of about 3 nm that are directly responsible for the red emission. Furthermore, the size-dependent QCE of CQDs has been observed by our group. We report the first demonstration of bright multicolor bandgap fluorescent CQDs (MCBF-CQDs) from blue to red with a QY up to 75% for blue fluorescence (Fig. 5a).12 The MCBF-CQDs show strong excitonic absorption bands in the UV-vis absorption spectra (Fig. 5b) centered at about 350, 390, 415, 480, and 500 nm for B-, G-, Y-, O-, and R-BF-CQDs, respectively, which is similar to the absorption characteristics of quantum-confined semiconductor QDs but quite different from that of previously reported CDs within only UV region.33,34,50 The fluorescence peaks of MCBF-CQDs are centered at about 430 (B-), 513 (G-), 535 (Y-), 565 (O-), and 604 nm (R-BF-CQDs) (Fig. 5c). The red-shifted fluorescence colors from blue to red are well consistent with the red-shifted excitonic absorption bands, manifesting the band edge nature of the optical transitions in the MCBF-CQDs. Another piece of evidence for the bandgap emission properties was obtained by time-resolved PL analysis (Fig. 5d), which revealed a monoexponential decay of about 14.2, 12.2, 11.3, 8.8 and 6.8 ns for B-, G-, Y-, O-, and R-BF-CQDs, respectively. The monoexponential decay characteristics indicate the band edge exciton-state decay rather than the defect-state decay within the MCBF-CQDs, which is conducive to efficient fluorescence emission and visibly different from that reported for CDs with multi-exponential decay.6,47 Meanwhile, the decreased highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels from 5.72 to 5.27 eV determined by means of ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS) and the increased lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels from 2.70 to 3.15 eV (Fig. 5e) directly reveal the bandgap transitions in MCBF-CQDs.5–7 More recently, we have reported the synthesis of multicolored, high color-purity, and narrow bandwidth (FWHM of 29–30 nm) emission triangular CQDs (NBE-T-CQDs) with a quantum yield (QY) up to 54–72% (Fig. 6).13 For the first time we overturn the belief that CQDs can only give broad emission and inferior color-purity with the full width at half maximum (FWHM) commonly exceeding 80 nm, and achieve an unprecedented narrow bandwidth of 29 nm. The typical aberration-corrected high-angle annular dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) images of the NBE-T-CQDs (Fig. 2c–f) clearly demonstrate the almost defect-free graphene crystalline structure with an obvious triangular shape. Other detailed structural characterization methods demonstrated that the as-prepared NBE-T-CQDs are highly crystalline and have a unique triangular structure functionalized with pure electron-donating hydroxyl groups at the edge sites. More importantly, the femtosecond transient absorption spectroscopy and temperature-dependent emission narrowing were conducted to reveal the emission mechanism of NBE-T-CQDs, which demonstrated the dramatically reduced electron–phonon coupling due to triangular structural rigidity as the origin of the high color-purity excitonic emission. In addition, the DFT calculations revealed that the unique triangular CQDs showed highly delocalized charges and high structural stability, which subsequently resulted in dramatically reduced electron–phonon coupling and further led to the high color-purity bandgap emission (Fig. 7).

3.2.2 Fluorescence emission of surface defect-derived origin. The second kind of the fluorescence mechanism of CQDs arises from surface-related defect states. Both sp3 and sp2 hybridized carbons and other surface defects of CQDs, such as oxygen containing functional groups, can serve as capture centers for excitons, thus giving rise to surface-related defect state fluorescence.43–45 With support from DFT calculations, it has been demonstrated that the carboxyl groups on the sp2-hybridized carbons could induce significant local distortions and consequently narrow the energy gap.46,47 And the optical properties may be completely changed such as radically different fluorescence emission bands and intensity distributions after the reduction of these oxygen containing functional groups (Fig. 8).48 Hu et al. prepared a series of CQDs by varying the reagents and reaction conditions and concluded that the surface epoxides or hydroxyls were predominantly responsible for the resulting PL red shift.49 The fluorescence intensity and even the peak position of CQDs originating from surface-related defect states can be changed significantly with the pH of their solution, which may be ascribed to the protonation or deprotonation of the oxygen containing functional groups and thus shift the Femi level of the CQDs. Our group has reported novel multicolor fluorescent CQDs which are responsive to all-pH from 1 to 14 and can be observed even with the naked eye.50 A novel quinone structure in the CQDs, which was for the first time transformed from the lactone structure under strong alkaline conditions, is responsible for the red emission under strong alkaline conditions. The pH-dependent PL is very important for exploring the fluorescence mechanism of CQDs, the understanding of which is still at an early stage and much deeper research is still required to solve this issue. In addition to the oxygen-containing functional groups, amine-containing groups also contribute to the surface defect-states in CQDs, which is attributed to their unpaired electrons as electron donors, and improve the fluorescence properties of CQDs.50,52 Furthermore, the surface defect-state emission can be due to surface modification of CQDs. For instance, after the passivation of –COOH and epoxy of CQDs into –CONHR and –CNHR with alkylamines, the intrinsic blue emission was greatly enhanced while the surface defect-state green emission disappeared.47 It should be emphasized that the obvious excitation-dependent fluorescence of the above reported CQDs dominated by surface defects severely results in fundamental limitations to the effectiveness of carrier injection for optoelectronic applications.

3.2.3 Up-conversion fluorescence. In addition to the conventional down-conversion fluorescence, certain CQDs show upconversion fluorescence emission (anti-Stokes emission), which is orders of magnitude higher in efficiency compared to conventional fluorophores.51,53 Up-conversion fluorescence is particularly attractive for in vivo imaging due to the deep-tissue penetration ability of long excitation wavelength especially in the NIR region.54–57 Our group has developed the first facile and large-scale synthesis of nitrogen-rich CQDs (NRCQDs) with bright two-photon fluorescence (TPF).36 And we further demonstrated that a large imaging depth of up to 440 μm for live rat liver tissues could be achieved by NRCQD-based TPF imaging, which indicates that CQDs with TPF possess a further application future.

Although great progress regarding the fluorescence properties of CQDs has been achieved, there are still some issues which are imperative to be solved, such as the still unclear fluorescence mechanisms and their lower QYs in the long-wavelength region, especially in the NIR region. Furthermore, both in-depth experimental verifications and theoretical calculations are still urgently needed to provide convincing explanations for the fluorescence mechanism. One of the most important challenges to demonstrate the fluorescence mechanism of CQDs is to develop an efficient and controllable method for producing high quality CQDs with a well-defined structure. Most CQDs reported to date usually show different sizes and different crystallinity, numerous but uncertain surface defects as well as functional groups, making it difficult to distinguish the key factors that influence the fluorescence emission. Once the structures of CQDs are well controlled by suitable methods, it will be possible to make the fluorescence mechanisms clear.

3.3 RTP properties of CQDs

The quantum mechanically allowed transition of singlet excitons (S1) with antisymmetric spins and a total spin quantum number of zero (S = 0) to the ground state (S0) results in fluorescence within nanoseconds. Conversely, the quantum mechanically forbidden transition of the triplet state (T1) with even symmetry and S = 1 to the singlet ground state results in phosphorescence with lifetimes in the microseconds to seconds regime. Because of the corresponding multiplicities of the angular momentum states (i.e., mS = 0 for S = 0 and mS = −1, 0, 1 for S = 1) and the random nature of spin production in electroluminescent devices, exciton formation under electrical excitation typically results in 25% singlet excitons and 75% triplet excitons. In general, the theoretical maximum external quantum efficiency (ηEQE) for electroluminescent LEDs as a key parameter can be estimated by eqn (1).
 
ηEQE = ηint × ηout = (γ × ηγ × ϕPL) × ηout(1)
where ηint is the internal quantum efficiency and ηout is the light-outcoupling efficiency (typically ηout ≈ 0.2); γ is the charge balance of injected holes and electrons (ideally γ = 1); ηγ is the efficiency of radiative exciton production (ηγ = 0.25 for conventional fluorescent emitters); and ϕPL is the photoluminescence (PL) quantum yield of the emitter material. For fluorescence materials, 75% of the electrically generated energy is dissipated as heat by triplet excitons owing to the spin-forbidden nature of triplet state transitions, leading to the theoretically highest external quantum efficiency of 5% after considering a light outcoupling efficiency of ∼20% in the device. In contrast, the phosphorescent materials, due to their ability to harvest light from both triplet and singlet excitons, allow the internal quantum efficiency of the device to reach nearly 100% (Fig. 9), and have thus aroused considerable interest in these materials for optoelectronic applications. Generally, short lifetimes (dozens of microseconds), in addition to high phosphorescence quantum efficiencies, are required for phosphorescent materials to realize high performance electroluminescent devices. This is because the long lifetime of triplet states will inevitably result in a severe efficiency roll-off at high current densities in electroluminescence due to triplet–triplet exciton annihilation (TTA) or triplet–polaron annihilation (TPA).

So far, luminogens with phosphorescence features have been typically limited to inorganics or organometallic complexes, and they often suffered from poor processability, high cost, and heavy-metal toxicity.57–61 In contrast, recently, metal-free RTP materials, especially pure organic RTP phosphors, have attracted much attention as advanced phosphorescent luminogens on account of their attractive characteristics such as good processability, low cost, versatile molecular design, and facile functionalization.62–64 However, metal-free RTP materials are rare and generally exhibit dim phosphorescence under ambient conditions because of inefficient spin–orbit coupling, the long-lived sensitive triplet excitons and quenching by impurity with long lifetimes.65–67 To achieve metal-free RTP luminogens, first we need to introduce heavy atoms and heteroatoms (N, O, S, P, and so on) into the luminescent skeletons to facilitate the effective intersystem crossing (ISC) and meanwhile to modulate the aggregation behaviors to suppress the nonradiative dissipation by polymer aggregation, hydrogen bonds, or supramolecular assembly. Huang et al. proposed that H-aggregation stabilized the triplet excitons by enhancing the intersystem crossing process, in pursuit of ultralong RTP.68 Tang et al. suggested that the crystallization-induced phosphorescence mechanism might be mainly responsible for the RTP character because of its effective inhibition of non-radiative decay.69,70 Chi and colleagues identified the key role of the intermolecular electronic coupling in achieving efficient persistent RTP.71 However, most organic RTP materials often require ordered crystal structures or stringent conditions associated with inert atmospheres to suppress the nonradiative relaxation process of the triplet states, which inevitably results in quenching of phosphorescence in the amorphous form and fundamentally limits its practical application.72

As an emerging class of luminescent nanomaterials, novel RTP properties of CQDs have been expected, which offers an intriguing prospect for utilizing triplet states for breaking through the 5% limitation of the traditional fluorescence devices. However, the search for CQD-based RTP materials remains a challenging research area because of the weak spin–orbit coupling of singlet-to-triplet. In 2013, Zhao's group first reported pure organic RTP by dispersing CQDs in the polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) matrix.73 This RTP emission is proposed to originate from triplet excitons of aromatic carbonyls on the surface of CQDs and the PVA molecules which effectively protect their energy from rotational or vibrational loss by rigidifying the groups with hydrogen bonds. Since then, further studies have shown that the layered double hydroxides,74 cyanuric acid75 and inorganic crystalline nanocomposites76 are also applicable as host materials for CQDs to exhibit remarkable RTP upon excitation by UV light, which indicates the potential of intersystem crossing and lays a solid foundation for the progress of promising alternative metal-free RTP materials based on the CQDs. However, this strategy using CQD composites to achieve RTP is more vulnerable to phase segregation, poor thermal stability and conductivity, which severely impedes their practical applications. Therefore, single-component CQDs with intrinsic RTP properties are highly desired. Blue-green RTP CQDs without the need for additional matrix compositing were developed through hydrothermal treatment of polyacrylic acid and ethylenediamine (Fig. 10a–c). This study indicated that the covalent crosslinking occurring in the interior of CQDs can generate luminescence centers while simultaneously restricting their vibration and rotation, thus providing favorable conditions for effective intersystem crossing.77 Before long, other examples including a gram-scale method for the preparation of ultralong RTP CQDs (1.46 s) via microwave assisted heating of ethanolamine and phosphoric acid aqueous,78 and blue-green self-protective RTP emission based on fluorine and nitrogen codoped carbon dots (FNCDs) synthesized from glucose and Et3N·3HF,79 further demonstrate the intrinsic ability of intersystem crossing in CQDs. However, the reported RTP emission wavelengths of those CQDs reported above were constrained to the blue or green region below 540 nm, and color-tunable RTP emission is of great necessity and significance for optoelectronic applications. CQD-based color-tunable fluorescence and RTP over the broad spectral range of 500–600 nm were revealed by the seeded growth method (Fig. 10d–f).80 The origin of RTP is believed to be related to the surface nitrogen containing groups of the CQDs, with a contribution from hydrogen bonds to protect the triplet states from quenching, and the additional stabilizing action of the PVP polymer chains.

3.4 TADF properties of CQDs

When T1 and S1 are close in energy, i.e., the singlet–triplet energy splitting (ΔEST) is small, the endothermic reverse intersystem crossing (RISC) process can be overcome at high temperature. As a result, the nonradiative triplet excitons, due to a spin-forbidden T1 → S0 transition, are transformed to singlet excitons via RISC, leading to TADF emission (S1 → S0) with lifetimes in the range of hundreds of nanoseconds to dozens of milliseconds (Fig. 9). Thus, the TADF materials also can employ the triplet state energy for improving the energy conversion efficiency.81,82 Recently, Yu et al. reported a facile and general “dots-in-zeolites” strategy to in situ confine CQDs in zeolitic matrices during hydrothermal/solvothermal crystallization to generate high-efficient TADF. The resultant CQDs@zeolite composites exhibit high quantum yields up to 52.14% and ultralong lifetimes up to 350 ms at ambient temperature and atmosphere (Fig. 11),19 which introduces a new perspective to develop CQD-based TADF materials for advanced optoelectronic device applications.

Despite the intensive work on the RTP and TADF properties of CQDs, the research on CQD-based triplet state modulation is still at an early stage with extremely low phosphorescence quantum efficiencies and long lifetime (hundreds of milliseconds) compared with well developed transition metal complexes. Besides, the color range of CQD-based RTP is quite narrow, leaving long wavelength emission (particularly orange or red emission) unexplored. In addition, the luminescence mechanism of the CQD-based RTP is not fully understood until now due to the complicated and unclear structural features of CQDs. Therefore, to obtain efficient CQD-based RTP, first we need to populate triplet excitons by introducing heteroatoms to enhance intersystem crossing and meanwhile suppress the nonradiative dissipation for achieving its application in electroluminescence. Furthermore, more efforts should be devoted to advanced and high-resolution characterization techniques as well as to computational simulations, which may increase our understanding of structure–property relationships and the fundamental phosphorescence mechanisms. Related works along this line are underway in our laboratory, and will be reported in due course.

4. Optoelectronic applications

Benefiting from the high specific surface areas, unique optoelectronic properties such as high electron mobility, long hot-electron lifetimes, ultrafast electron extraction, tunable bandgaps and strong stable fluorescence, the emerging CQDs have shown huge prospects in optoelectronic applications including light-emitting diodes, lasing, solar cells, and photodetectors.

4.1 Light-emitting diodes

Light emitting diodes (LEDs) are the enabling elements of the rapidly growing solid-state lighting and full-color display industry.83 As a new kind of fluorescent QD system, the CQDs appear to be a promising alternative to rare earth-based expensive phosphors and toxic metal-based semiconductor QDs in LEDs due to their tunable stable fluorescence emission, low cost and environmental friendliness. In particular, the characteristic broad emission of CQDs makes them ideal candidates for fabricating white LEDs (WLEDs), which have been regarded as the next-generation lighting devices due to their superior merits such as energy-saving, long-lifetime and compact size. Therefore, tremendous efforts have been made to realize the great potential applications of CQDs in LEDs.

Generally, the applications of CQDs in LEDs can be divided into two categories, i.e., phosphor-converted LEDs (pc-LEDs) and electroluminescent LEDs.84 In pc-LEDs, UV- or blue-LED chips are usually employed as the primary light source to optically pump CQD-based phosphors. In contrast, electroluminescent LEDs, where electrons and holes are injected into the active CQD emission layer by an external bias voltage and undergo radiative recombination, possess potentially higher efficiency. Thus, it is of great significance to develop electroluminescent LEDs.

4.1.1 Electroluminescent LEDs. As a direct benefit of the bandgap emission, the first example of monochrome electroluminescent LEDs with stable emission color from blue to red based on MCBF-CQDs directly used as an active emission layer was realized by our group. It should be emphasized that no hole transport layer (HTL) was used in the simple device structure to reveal that electrons and holes are injected into the active MCBF-CQD emission layer to give rise to electroluminescence by radiative recombination. The LEDs show very stable and voltage-independent emission color, which is quite different from that of previously reported CQDs with voltage-dependent emission.85 The maximum luminance (Lmax) reached 136 cd m−2 for blue LEDs, which represents the best performance for CQD-based monochrome electroluminescent LEDs (Fig. 12). Moreover, WLEDs with CIE coordinates at (0.30, 0.33) are also fabricated by using BF-CQD blended poly(N-vinyl carbazole) (PVK) as an emissive layer. The Lmax and current efficiency (ηc) reached about 2050 cd m−2 and 1.1 cd A−1, respectively, which are comparable to those of semiconductor QD-based LEDs. This work does serve the purpose of providing significant opportunities for further improvements of CQD-based electroluminescent LEDs. In addition, some efforts have been made to explore the potential applications of CQDs in electroluminescent LEDs.86,87 White electroluminescent LEDs associated with the energy transfer in the CQDs were achieved, and the maximum EQE at a current density of 5 mA cm−2 reached about 0.083%.89

Compared with the high performance LEDs fabricated by using the high color-purity and narrow bandwidth semiconductor QDs which possess extremely high QY as the active emission layer,91,92 the efficiencies of the CQD-based electroluminescent LEDs are still relatively low. There are two key challenges facing the high color-purity and high-performance electroluminescence of CQDs: new strategies for synthesizing narrow bandwidth CQDs with higher QY, as well as the carrier (holes and electrons) transport property of CQDs with a conjugated surface and optimization of the charge injection with better controlled device fabrication through energy level matching by adding and tuning the thickness of the hole transport layer or the electron transport layer. In addition, efficient shorter or longer-wavelength (e.g., blue or near infrared) emissive CQDs with narrow bandwidth are highly desired for high color-purity LEDs.

4.1.2 Phosphor-converted LEDs. Compared with the studies on electroluminescent LEDs, research about applications of CQDs in phosphor-converted LEDs is relatively extensive. Since the first example of CQDs as an excellent phosphor was demonstrated by coating the CQD phosphor onto a commercial blue LED centered at 410 nm,93 great efforts have been made to prepare efficient blue, green, and yellow emissive CQD-based phosphors for application in WLEDs.94–98 For indoor lighting, warm WLEDs with a high color rendering index (CRI > 80) and a low correlated color temperature (CCT < 4000 K) are strongly desired due to their comfortable ambient lighting that can stave off eye fatigue. However, it is a major challenge to realize warm WLEDs due to the lack of red emissive CQDs with high QYs.99,100 The absence of efficient red light in the emission spectral region of the CQD-based WLEDs previously reported resulted in a cool white light with a low CRI.101 Our laboratory developed R-CQDs with a QY up to 53% as the red emitting phosphor,37 and using this in combination with our blue emissive CQD (B-CQD) and green emissive CQD (G-CQD) phosphors, we have realized a UV-pumped CQD phosphor-based warm WLED with the CIE coordinates, CCT and CRI of (0.3924, 0.3912), 3875 K and 97, demonstrating good color chromatics stability with increasing applied current from 20 to 90 mA and high device stability after working for 72 h (Fig. 14). The luminous efficiency of the optimized warm WLED was as high as 31.3 lm W−1, which is comparable to that of semiconductor QD- and rare-earth phosphor-based WLEDs.

Ideally, single component white-emitting CQD phosphors covering the entire visible spectral window from 400 to 700 nm are highly desired.102–104 By constructing novel dual-fluorescence morphologies (i.e., monomers around aggregates), white-light emissive CQDs were synthesized in organic media (Fig. 15a and b).105 And WLEDs with the CIE coordinate (0.277, 0.362) were fabricated by combining white-emitting CQDs dispersed in epoxy resin and a UV chip (370 nm), which approached the coordinate (0.33, 0.33) of pure white light. However, the existing white emission arising from modulating aggregation state of CQDs in aqueous solutions is sensitive to their concentration and the solution pH, which limits their practical application for LED lighting. The aggregated CQDs were fixed in the melamine-formaldehyde (MF) polymer matrix with their emission extended from blue to the full visible range, presenting bright white luminescence under ultraviolet excitation.102 By dispersing the CQD-MF microspheres in a cross-linked polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) matrix, a remote planar WLED was fabricated with an average luminous efficacy of 31 ± 4 lm W−1 under a working current of 700 mA (Fig. 15c and d).

In fact, the current efficiencies of CQD-based warm WLEDs are still relatively low compared with those of the well developed QD-WLEDs with high luminous efficiency and CRI. And it is still a huge challenge to obtain warm WLEDs with high CRI and high luminous efficiency due to the limited QY of red emissive CQDs. In addition, the best approach to realizing high performance WLEDs is to achieve single component WLEDs based on intrinsic white emissive CQDs covering the entire visible spectral window from 400 to 700 nm, which has so far remained a major challenge. Therefore, new strategies for preparing broadband white light emitting CQDs with high QY and for broadening the emission peaks to the red spectral region need to be developed for the highly efficient single component WLEDs.

4.2 Random lasing

Developing low-threshold random lasers with high brightness and high monochromaticity has been the subject of intense academic research for years, in anticipation for widespread applications spanning from military weapon devices, biomedical therapy and materials processing to energy savings.106–108 And we have demonstrated full-color random lasing with thresholds in the range of 2.1–5.8 mJ cm−2 in blue, green, red and white colors based on bright multicolor fluorescent bandgap emission CQDs.109 However, the large FWHM (>80 nm) and the large Stokes shifts (>80 nm) of the bandgap emission CQDs inevitably prevent sustaining population inversion and the dissipation of exciton energy into heat during the pumping process, thus fundamentally limiting the further development.110 The bright and unprecedented narrow bandwidth (FWHM of 29–30 nm) emission of the NBE-T-CQDs prompted us to exploit their potential application in highly efficient random lasers (Fig. 16a–c).14 As a benefit of the very small FWHM of NBE-T-CQDs, which facilitates the ultrafast build-up of population at a low pump energy density, and the subsequent optical amplification at a low pump energy density, the blue, green, and red random lasers show a remarkably narrow FWHM of 0.9, 0.37, and 0.82 nm and low pump thresholds of 0.087, 0.052 and 0.82 mJ cm−2, even superior to the state-of-the-art perovskites and semiconductor QD-based random lasers (Fig. 16d–f). Although some progress has been achieved, the CQD-based random lasing remains underexplored. Further research on lasers operating in other modes, such as the Fabry–Pérot mode and the whispering-gallery mode, should be carried out, which will expedite developing the next-generation of ultrastable and low-threshold NBE-T-CQD-based laser technology.

4.3 Solar cells

CQDs usually possess a wide range of light absorption tailing into the visible region, which is beneficial for their application in solar cells (SCs). Up to now, CQDs have been used in many kinds of SCs, such as dye-sensitized SCs,111 organic SCs112,113 and silicon based SCs. Our group inserted a sub-monolayer layer of CQDs between the perovskite and the mesoporous titanium dioxide layers in a typical perovskite SC (Fig. 17a and b).114 The results of the photocurrent–voltage curve, photocurrent quantum efficiency and transient absorption measurements showed that the CQDs could serve as a superfast bridge to facilitate the electron injection from the halide perovskite into the titanium dioxide, leading to a significantly enhanced photocurrent and power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the corresponding SCs (from 8.81% to 10.15%). This work indicated the possibility of collecting hot electrons from the photo-excited perovskite due to the rather slow cooling of the hot electrons. In addition, we introduced CQDs as an additive for the stabilization of MAPbI3via passivation of the grain boundaries of the perovskite to improve the PCE (from 17.59% to 18.81%) and increased the stability of perovskite SCs (Fig. 17c and d).115 The carboxylic groups, hydroxyl groups and amino groups on the surface and edge of CQDs could form strong and stable interactions with the uncoordinated Pb in MAPbI3, inducing a lower trap-state density and better optoelectronic properties. Moreover, the bonding between CQDs and MAPbI3 formed a protective layer to prevent the perovskite from coming in contact with water, thereby enhancing the stability of SCs. Although the current research on CQDs in the field of solar cells is rare, let alone the efficiency of CQD-based devices, it is expected that CQDs are promising alternatives to the toxic and unstable inorganic halide perovskites in solar cells116,117 due to their superior merits such as hot-electron lifetime, high electron mobility, broad optical absorption, and solution-processability.

4.4 Photodetectors

As a reverse process of electroluminescence, photo-generated carriers can be collected and used in photodetectors. The response band and responsivity are the basic parameters for photodetectors. Commonly, owing to the absorption peak transition at ≈250 nm derived from the π–π*, deep-UV photoluminescence and photodetection are possible for CQDs.118–123 A nitrogen-doped CQD-based broadband photodetector with responsivity as high as 325 V W−1 is demonstrated by coating N-CQDs onto interdigital gold electrodes (Fig. 18a and b).124 Interestingly, the CQD based photodetectors exhibit negative photocurrent when irradiated with different light, which can be explained as follows. Because of the surface passivation of CQDs, photogenerated electron–hole pairs cannot move freely. The bound excitons may trap carriers when exposed to light, forming photoinduced charge traps. And then, carriers will be trapped when they pass through the CQDs, leading to the negative photoresponse. The photodetectors with high detectivity (>1011 cm Hz1/2 W−1), high responsivity (0.2–0.5 A W−1) and a broad spectral response range from UV to near-infrared were realized by embedding CQDs in p-type graphene sheets.125 Due to the tunneling of charge carriers through the energy states in CQDs, the photocurrent of the device can be significantly increased under different voltages. Deep UV CQD based photodetectors with asymmetric electrodes combined with Au and Ag were fabricated (Fig. 18c–f).126 Ag serves as the cathode while Au serves as the anode. Thus, photogenerated carriers can drift freely toward electrodes under forward bias and carrier recombination can be suppressed because electrons and holes can be separated readily. The as-prepared device possesses a high on/off ratio even under a weak light of 8 μW cm−2 and the rise time and decay time are reduced to 64 and 43 ms, respectively.

5. Conclusions

In summary, we have reviewed the recent research progress achieved in CQDs, including their synthesis, optical properties, luminescence mechanisms, and applications in optoelectronics. Mainly discussed are their optical properties, as well as applications in optoelectronic areas including LEDs, lasing, and solar cells. There is no doubt that the development of CQDs for optoelectronic applications has seen great progress within a short time. However, the research still faces a set of challenges in terms of fundamental understanding and practical applications as stipulated below.

First, one of the most controversial fundamental issues of CQDs is the fluorescence mechanism. Further new structural characterization methods and theoretical calculations are badly desired to provide a clearer coherent picture. Moreover, CQDs with a high QY comparable to traditional semiconductor QDs at longer wavelengths beyond the blue light region are yet to be synthesized on a large-scale through facile methods.

Secondly, triplet-excited-state-involved materials have attracted considerable attention as a promising and efficient approach to harvest triplet excitons for electroluminescence applications. However, CQD-based triplet-state emission, especially the RTP properties of CQDs, is rare and generally exhibits extremely dim RTP with long lifetime due to the weak spin–orbit coupling of singlet-to-triplet. Furthermore, the CQD-based RTP with long wavelength emission (orange or red) remains underexplored. Thus the practical applications of CQDs with RTP properties are only limited to anti-counterfeiting, optical recording, sensors, and security protection, and there remains a long way to go though potential applications in electroluminescence. Therefore, new strategies for production of CQD-based triplet-state emission with high quantum efficiencies and short lifetime need to be urgently developed.

Thirdly, with continual achievements of the multicolor PL and high QY, CQDs will become more and more promising for optoelectronic applications. For phosphor based WLEDs, developing high performance single component warm WLEDs based on intrinsically broadband white emissive CQDs covering the entire visible spectral window from 400 to 700 nm is highly desired for solid-state lighting, which has so far remained a huge challenge.

Fourthly, for electroluminescence, the successful synthesis of unprecedented narrow bandwidth emission triangular CQDs will set the stage for developing next-generation CQD-based display technology. However, some problems still should be settled for further development of full-color displays. For example, new strategies for preparing narrow bandwidth CQDs ranging from blue to near-infrared with high QY need to be developed. Furthermore, efficient RTP or TADF CQDs as well as optimization of the charge injection with better controlled device fabrication are highly demanded for improving the performance of CQD-based electroluminescent LEDs. In addition, facile methods for preparing CQDs with low costs and on a large-scale need to be developed for practical applications.

Finally, the research on CQDs in the field of laser, solar cells, and photodetectors is just the beginning, and there is a lot of room for further development. With the continued advances of experimental and theoretical studies, we can expect to achieve more ground-breaking advances in both fundamental research and optoelectronic applications of CQDs in the future.
