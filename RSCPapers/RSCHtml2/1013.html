Abstract

A hydrogen bonded pyridine-thieno[3,2-b]thiophene-pyridine type building block TTPY has been synthesized for Ï€-conjugated polymers. The single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that resonance-assisted hydrogen bonds (RAHBs) formed in the building block. The RAHBs and weak intramolecular noncovalent interactions together enabled a rigid co-planar structure, extended Ï€-conjugated and a tight lamella arrangement in the solid state. Two conjugated polymers based on TTPY have also been synthesized. The PTTPY-DPP-based devices exhibited a p-type field-effect mobility of 0.17 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and the PTTPY-BDT:Y6-based polymer solar cells showed a power conversion efficiency of 10.8%. The results demonstrated that pyridines and other azines, due to their combined electron deficiency, less steric hindrance, and especially their ability to form RAHBs, can be useful building blocks for organic semiconducting materials.

Introduction

The prospective applications of Ï€-conjugated polymers in many emerging technologies, such as wearable and flexible electronics, have led to a surge of interest in recent years.1â€“3 Molecular design plays an important role in creating high-performance polymers with specific electronic and optical properties.4â€“8 Recently, the strategy of embedding nitrogen-based aromatic heterocycles as alternatives to conventional arenes and thiophenes has attracted much attention.5,9 Pyridine is the parent of a huge class of Ï€-electron deficient heteroaromatics which are referred to as azines. The hexatomic, nitrogen-containing aromatic heterocycles have three major advantages as building blocks for conjugated polymers. (1) The electron-withdrawing property of pyridine is beneficial to the construction of semiconducting materials with low frontier molecular orbital levels.10â€“12 (2) The nitrogen atom of pyridine is sterically less demanding compared with the Câ€“H bond in benzene. So, the torsion angles between the repeating units of the conjugated backbone are reduced.11â€“15 Furthermore, the imine nitrogen of pyridine can form weak intramolecular noncovalent interactions with hydrogen or sulfur atoms in the adjacent repeating unit, and therefore improve coplanarity.16â€“22 (3) The pyridine nitrogen atom with an unshared electron pair, like tertiary amines, can be metal ion coordinated, N-protonated, N-oxidated, and quaternized.23â€“28 When a pyridine reacts as a base or a nucleophile, it forms a pyridinium cation in which the aromatic sextet is retained and the nitrogen acquires a formal positive charge. These reactions at nitrogen can significantly alter the optical and electrical properties of the polymers.23,24,29,30 Despite these advantages, the pyridines as building blocks of high-performance semiconducting polymers have been restricted to a few examples so far, especially, focusing on the pyridine-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole (PyDPP) type polymers.10,12,31â€“35

Polymers with high charge carrier mobility in organic electronic devices usually have planar structures and extended Ï€-electron delocalization.5,36â€“38 Recently, dynamic noncovalent intramolecular interactions, such as Oâ‹¯S, Nâ‹¯S, and Xâ‹¯S (where X is the halogen), have become an effective strategy for designing high-performance organic semiconductors.8 They contribute to planarizing the conjugated backbone and favor charge delocalization and transport. Hydrogen bonding (H-bond) is the most common dynamic noncovalent interaction, which occurs through various functional groups within and between molecules.39â€“43 H-bonds are relatively strong directional interaction and can lead to stable supramolecular structures. In many organic and biological compounds, the H-bonds are decisive factors governing conformation, molecular recognition, and packing.42,44 Recently, we have developed a strategy for high performance organic semiconducting polymers by incorporating intramolecular resonance-assisted hydrogen bonds (RAHBs) into polymer main chains.45,46 The RAHBs are special type of strong hydrogen bonds with a bond energy of 20â€“30 kcal molâˆ’1 which is about 3â€“5 times ordinary hydrogen bonds. The RAHBs often occur in a six- or five-membered ring, and the hydrogen donor and the acceptor are connected by a Ï€-conjugated molecular framework, an example is the enol form of Î²-diketone.42,47â€“49 The large bonding energy of RAHBs was suggested as a result of the increase in the donor and acceptor strengths through a charge flow in the polarizable Ï€-bond systems.42 Combining Ï€-electrons delocalization effect and strong hydrogen bonding, the formation of RAHBs promotes planarity and polarization and thus enhances Ï€-electron delocalization.50,51 Herein, we present a new pyridine based building block TTPY (Fig. 1) with RAHB interactions, the carbamate functionalized thieno[3,2-b]thiophene (TT) flanked by two pyridines. In TTPY, the imine nitrogen atoms of pyridines are hydrogen acceptors and the carbamate hydrogens are hydrogen donors. The single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of TTPYa revealed that medium strength RAHBs formed in the building blocks. The analysis also revealed the weak non-covalent interactions between the carbonyl oxygen of the carbamate and the thienyl sulfur atom of TT. The RAHBs and weak non-covalent interactions act simultaneously in the long and short molecular axis of TTPYa, improving the rigidity and planarity of a molecular skeleton. The two polymers PTTPY-DPP and PTTPY-BDT based on TTPY also have been synthesized. Both polymers showed strong aggregation behavior in their dilute solution. The organic field-effect transistor (OFET) devices based on PTTPY-DPP offered the highest/average hole mobility of 0.17/0.16 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. Solar cells fabricated with Y652 as a small molecular non-fullerene acceptor (SM-NFA) and PTTPY-BDT as a donor afforded a power conversion efficiency of 10.8%.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

The synthesis route and detailed procedure for the preparation of monomers and polymers are presented in Scheme 1 and the Supporting Information. The new compounds were characterized by 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy, mass spectrometry, and elemental analysis. Thieno[3,2-b]thiophene-3,6-dicarboxylic acid was prepared by following a literature procedure.53 It was converted to bis-carbamate (1a) in a one-pot reaction of acyl azide formation, Curtius rearrangement, and then 2-octyl-1-dodecanol attacking the intermediate isocyanates.541a was brominated with NBS to give compound 2a. The Stille coupling reaction of 2a and 2-(tributylstannyl)pyridine provided TTPY. The chemical shift of the carbamate protons in 1a and TTPY appeared at 7.37 and 12.17 ppm, respectively. It shifted significantly to the down-field due to the formation of RAHBs. TTPY reacts with bromine in chloroform to produce monomer TTPY-Br2 in 40% yield. The bromination happened exclusively on the 5,5â€²-positions of pyridines. The regio-chemistry of the bromination reaction was confirmed by analyzing the splitting patterns and the coupling constants of the 1H NMR spectrum. The detailed analysis was provided in the Supporting Information. TTPYa-Br2, which had the same backbone but ethylhexyl side chains, was synthesized by the same method for the cultivation of a single crystal. The structure of TTPYa-Br2 was directly determined by X-ray single crystal analysis. Dibromo compounds have been practically used as universal intermediates in the preparation of extended Ï€-conjugated structures. Prior to this work, 1,4-bis(5-bromopyridin-2-yl) flanked building blocks were usually synthesized from 5-brominated pyridine derivatives and the connections to the central ring(s) were made either by ring-closure reaction (S1) or by cross-coupling reactions (S2 and S3) (Scheme S1, ESIâ€ ). Here, we demonstrated an alternative way of making this type of building blocks. The bis(pyridin-2-yl) could be dibrominated selectively on 5, 5â€² positions of the pyridines after the preparation of the conjugated core.

The polymers PTTPY-DPP and PTTPY-BDT were prepared via Stille cross-coupling reactions of TTPY-Br2 and distannyl monomers. At room temperature, both polymers were soluble in chloroform and formed a viscous solution. These two polymers were dissolved well in hot chloroform, chlorobenzene and o-dichlorobenzene. Gel permeation chromatography (GPC) measurements were conducted at a high temperature (150 Â°C) with 1,2,4-trichloroenzene as the eluent. PTTPY-DPP and PTTPY-BDT showed the number average molecular weight (Mn) of 153.5 and 86.6 kDa and polydispersity indexes (PDIs) of 3.5 and 4.0, respectively. The relatively large PDIs could result from strong aggregation in a polymer solution.25,55,56

Single-crystal structure analysis

The single crystals of TTPYa and TTPYa-Br2 were cultivated by the slow diffusion of ethanol into their solutions in chloroform. The molecular conformation, intramolecular and intermolecular interactions, and solid-state packing were analyzed. The bond lengths and torsion angles in the two structures are summarized in Table S3 and S4 (ESIâ€ ). The TTPYa molecules were stacked in a one-dimensional lamellar packing with a slipping angle of 24.4Â°, and the interplanar distance was 3.17 Ã…. This close packing motif increased the overlapping area of Ï€ orbital between molecules. The distances of Nâ€“H, Nâ‹¯N, and Hâ‹¯N in TTPYa were about 0.88, 2.71 and 2.08 Ã…, respectively. For the TTPYa-Br2 single crystal, the distance of Nâ€“H, Nâ‹¯N, and Hâ‹¯N was about 0.79â€“0.96 Ã…, 2.70â€“2.73 Ã…, and 1.96â€“2.15 Ã…, respectively. The short Ï€â€“Ï€ distance and large Ï€â€“Ï€ orbital overlap in TTPYa might be beneficial to the charge transport in the conjugated polymer based on it. The carbamate side chains not only provided solubility but also enhanced intramolecular conjugation and tight intermolecular packing.

Theoretical calculations

Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations were carried out to investigate the molecular geometries and frontier molecular orbitals using Gaussian at the B3LYP/6-31G(d) level. The calculations were performed on three repeat units for each polymer, and the alkyl chains were replaced by methyl groups. As shown in Fig. S2 (ESIâ€ ), the dihedral angle between the pyridine unit and carbamate-functionalized thieno[3,2-b]thiophene was about 9Â° and 10Â° for trimers of TTPY-DPP and TTPY-BDT, respectively. The dihedral angles were close to those obtained from the single crystal structure analysis of TTPYa.

This suggested RAHBs and non-covalent interactions improved the coplanarity. In contrast, when there were no RAHBs between the pyridine and the adjacent thiophene, the dihedral angles were about 20â€“24Â°. The computational results suggested that the RAHBs significantly reduced the twists in the conjugated units. The lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)/highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) levels were calculated to be âˆ’3.00/âˆ’4.90 eV for PTTPY-DPP and âˆ’2.44/âˆ’4.94 eV for PTTPY-BDT.

Thermal properties

The thermal properties of the polymers were studied by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). The TGA and DSC curves are presented in Fig. S3 and S4 (ESIâ€ ), respectively. The decomposition temperatures (5% weight loss) of PTTPY-DPP and PTTPY-BDT were at 262 and 250 Â°C, respectively. Photovoltaic properties of PTTPY-BDT

The photovoltaic property of PTTPY-BDT was studied in solar cell devices with the structure of indium tin oxide (ITO)/ZnO/blend film/MoO3/Ag. Devices were optimized in terms of the donor/acceptor weight ratio and the amount of additives. The performances of solar cells were evaluated under AM1.5G illumination (100 mW cmâˆ’2). The current densityâ€“voltage (Jâ€“V) characteristics and the external quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra are shown in Fig. 6. The device metric acquired under various fabrication conditions is present in Table S5 and S6 (ESIâ€ ). The highest power conversion efficiencies were obtained from the devices with a D/A ratio of 1:1.2, an active layer thickness of 95 nm, and chloronaphthalene (CN) as the additive (0.5 vol%). The solar cell device exhibited a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 10.8%, with an open circuit voltage (Voc) of 0.79 V, a short circuit current density (Jsc) of 22.2 mA cmâˆ’2, and a fill factor (FF) of 0.62. The EQE curve of the solar cell showed a broad response range. The Jsc value which was calculated from the integration of the EQE spectrum was consistent with the result acquired from the Jâ€“V measurements. The polymer PTTPY-BDT has a large absorption extinction coefficient (1.37 Ã— 105 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) at its maximum absorption (545 nm), and Y6 has strong absorption in the near infrared region. The devices showed a high photoelectric response in visible and near-infrared regions. A small depression between 540 and 650 nm on the EQE spectrum could result from the mismatch between the absorption spectra of the donor and the acceptor. PTTPY-BDT possessed a relatively high HOMO level for donors and led to moderate Voc. Some high performance polymer donors, such as PM6,52 PM7,65 PBQ-1066 and P2F-EHp,67 had optical bandgaps in the range of 1.80â€“1.92 eV, and they were significantly smaller than that of PTTPY-BDT (Eg = 2.12 eV). Microstructure characterization

The surface topographic features of the as-spun and thermal annealed thin films of PTTPY-DPP were investigated by atomic force microscopy (AFM). The film morphology did not change much with thermal annealing processes. The as-spun and thermal annealed thin films displayed fibrous features. The 120 Â°C thermal annealed film showed the lowest root-mean-square (RMS) roughness of 0.65 nm (Fig. 7). The morphology might reduce charge traps and lead to efficient charge transport. The OFET devices also showed the highest hole mobility after annealing at the temperature. After thermal annealing at 160 Â°C, the films displayed a slightly larger RMS value, which might be responsible for the deterioration of device performances.71 The microstructures of PTTPY-BDT:Y6 thin films were also studied via AFM. The height image of the PTTPY-BDT blend film showed uniform, smooth and appropriately phase separation with the RMS of 2.30 nm, and the morphology was beneficial for charge generation and transport (Fig. 8a).

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have synthesized a new hydrogen bonded pyridine-thieno[3,2-b]thiophene-pyridine type building block TTPY for Ï€-conjugated polymers. The single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of the monomer revealed that the RAHBs formed between carbamate hydrogens on TT and the nitrogen atoms of pyridines, and weak intramolecular noncovalent interactions existed between thienyl sulfur atoms and carbonyl oxygens. They together enabled a rigid co-planar structure, extended Ï€-conjugated and a tight lamella arrangement in the solid state. Two conjugated polymers based on the TTPY have been also synthesized. The PTTPY-DPP based devices exhibited the p-type field-effect mobility of 0.17 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and the PTTPY-BDT:Y6-based polymer solar cells showed a PCE of 10.8%. Our investigations demonstrated that hexatomic, nitrogen-containing aromatic heterocycles can be used as hydrogen acceptors for the construction of RAHBs in conjugated structures. Due to their combined electron deficiency, less steric hindrance, and especially their ability to form RAHBs, pyridines and other azines can be useful building blocks for organic semiconducting materials.
