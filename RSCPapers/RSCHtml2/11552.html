Recent highlights and future prospects on mixed-metal MOFs as emerging supercapacitor candidates

Abstract

Mixed-metal metal–organic frameworks (M-MOFs) consist of at least two different metal ions as nodes in the same framework. The incorporation of a second or more metal ions provides structural/compositional diversity, multi-functionality and stability to the framework. Moreover, the periodical array of different metal ions in the framework may alter the physical/chemical properties of M-MOFs and result in fascinating applications. M-MOFs with exciting structural features offer superior supercapacitor performances compared to single metal MOFs due to the synergic effect of different metal ions. In this review, we summarize several synthetic methods to construct M-MOFs by employing various organic ligands or metalloligands. Further, we discuss the electrochemical performance of several M-MOFs and their derived composite materials for supercapacitor applications.

1. Introduction

Together with the modernization of human civilization, the need for energy has been a driving force for innovation and the development of society. However, the continuous depletion of natural resources has amplified the demand for sustainable clean and green energy.1 Accordingly, to solve this problem, various classes of cleaner energy sources such as wind energy, solar power, and sea tides have been explored for the development of energy conversion and storage devices.2a Undoubtedly, solar energy has been exploited vastly due to the efficient conversion of solar energy into electricity. However, in the efficient storage of electricity derived from solar energy, it still remains a challenge to maintain a balance between the demand and supply of energy.2b,c Furthermore, the intermittent nature of solar energy restricts its practical applications to combat the high energy demand.3 Thus, the use of large systems (e.g. transportation systems) remains difficult to supply energy efficiently from the consumption of solar energy. Therefore, reliable technologies for energy storage such as supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries have gained tremendous attention from the scientific community.4,5 Together with batteries and fuel cells, supercapacitors are also important energy storage devices owing to their promising features such as rapid charge–discharge cycle, high power density and long cycle life.6 Based on the mechanism their of charge storage behavior, supercapacitors are classified into two categories, i.e. electrical double-layered capacitors (EDLCs) and pseudocapacitors.7a EDLCs consist of various carbon-based materials such as graphene, activated carbon, and carbon nanotubes (CNTs), where electric charge is stored in the electric double layers produced at the boundary between the electrode and the electrolyte.7b–d In contrast, redox-active materials such as metal oxides (MOs) and conducting polymers fall into the category of pseudocapacitors. The materials used to fabricate high-performance supercapacitors must possess some crucial properties such as temperature stability, large specific surface area, high resistance against corrosion, optimized size with a regular distribution of pores, cost effectiveness, and suitable processing.8–12 In the early 1990s, Yaghi et al. defined a new class of porous materials, i.e. metal–organic frameworks (MOFs). MOFs are well known for their porous crystalline architectures with periodic networks comprised of rich metal centers. Their stable architectures display a large surface area with well-regulated pores, which endow MOFs with vast potential applications.17–19 This new class of materials outperforms all related materials in various applications such as catalysis, gas separation and storage, drug delivery, ion sensing, optoelectronics and charge storage.13–16 Furthermore, MOFs and their derivatives exhibit superior specific surface area, structural flexibility, and penetrability to guest molecules, and therefore, can be considered as potential candidates for charge storage applications.20–23 For the electrochemical studies, MOFs were explored in the early 2010s and delivered encouraging performances.24,25 According to recent research trends, MOFs and their derived nanostructures have been widely recognized for their great application possibilities in solar cells, electrochemical sensors, fuel cells, supercapacitors, and Li-ion batteries.19,26–28 Additionally, the development of new multi-functional MOFs is a very important area of research, where the incorporation of mixed ligands/metals is the most common approach to alter the properties of MOFs.

Mixed metallic metal–organic frameworks (M-MOFs) are advanced materials with enhanced framework complexity to achieve improved performances compared to single metal MOFs.29,30 M-MOFs consist of two or more different metal nodes in the MOF unit. It is also possible to have immobilized metal ions, metal complex/nanoparticles and organometallic units anywhere in the MOF unit with or without interactions and their structure provides fascinating multi-functionality/complexity and tunable properties for specific applications.29–33

2. Synthetic strategies

M-MOFs can be synthesized by employing multiple metal salts as the metal precursor in various synthetic techniques such as the slow diffusion, hydro/solvothermal, slow evaporation sonication and mechanochemical methods.34–37 The above synthetic approaches for the construction of M-MOFs proceed via either one-pot synthesis or post-synthetic modification methods. Conventionally, O- and N-donor ligands (strong Lewis base) are used to generate strong bonds with highly charged metal ions (strong Lewis acid). Moreover, the synthetic methodology of M-MOFs depends on the following factors of metal ions: (i) similar chemical behavior, (ii) similar ionic radius, and (iii) same coulombic charge to increase the homogeneous co-incorporation of various metal ions in the same structure;38,39 otherwise, the resultant MOF may preferentially contain only one metal in its nodal position, and another metal somewhere in the amorphous part/free substantial portion of the solid material without being involved in the crystallinity of the MOF unit.40,41 Additionally, the size of the metal cations also plays an essential role, which is related to their excellent coordination with specific anionic moieties. Thus, ionic radius/size-based deliberations are useful in envisioning the strength of crystalline phases.42–44 Moreover, a large difference in the size (ionic radius) of metal cations can obstruct the homogeneous mixing in the molecular unit of M-MOFs.43 However, the incorporation of mixed multitopic ligands can generate M-MOFs using the different hardness–softness of metal ions, which is difficult to construct utilizing the common multitopic ligands. Thus, linkers carrying distinct donor atoms can bind with different Lewis acidic metal nodes to preferably prepare M-MOFs.42,44,45 M-MOFs can be thermodynamically/kinetically controlled based on the nature and ratio of their different metal precursors and synthesis parameters. These conditions also influence the contribution and position of their metal nodes. To characterize the obtained structure of M-MOFs, various advanced techniques such as extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) and X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) are applied. These high-resolution techniques give information on the metal environment and geometrical information/average coordination number as well as average bond lengths together with other data. Data from the EXAFS technique can lead to the more desirable characterization of M-MOFs to provide evidence of the co-incorporation of different metal nodes in a homogeneous manner. However, the utilization of high-resolution techniques is not sufficient to predict the oxidation states of different metals in M-MOFs, especially transition metal ions.

2.1 One-pot synthesis or direct synthesis approach

M-MOFs can be constructed using an appropriate mixture of two or more different metal precursors during the direct synthesis approach, as shown in Fig. 1. The key point of the one-pot synthesis is its simplicity and reduction of synthetic steps.44,46,47 In this strategy, it is also important to produce a controllable distribution of metal nodes in the formed framework.
Fig. 1 Schematic representation of the one-pot synthesis of M-MOFs using different metal nodes.

However, the construction of M-MOFs via a one-step approach is very challenging because the incorporation of additional metal ions into MOFs often produces inconsistent topologies and breakable networks.29,30,48 Recently, many researchers have made considerable progress in this area. Serre et al.49 reported a direct synthetic approach to prepare a bimetallic MIL-53(Cr/Fe) framework. The M-MOF was synthesized via the hydrothermal method using a stoichiometric ratio of chromium nitrate, iron powder, terephthalic acid and hydrofluoric acid in water. The authors observed breathing behavior in the M-MOF since it could undergo reversible expansion and contraction in its structure under external stimuli such as temperature/pressure and involvement of guest particles. Another strategy to ensure a highly controlled ratio of metal ions in M-MOFs was also explored by Lah's50 group. They demonstrated a series of isostructural bimetallic NixM1−x-ITHDs [M = Zn(II) and Co(II)] with varying molar ratios of Ni(II), Zn(II) and Co(II) metals, which exhibited enhanced porosity and surface area compared to that of the pristine MOF. Ranjbar et al. prepared Ni/Co-MOF using a direct synthetic approach through the hydrothermal method by adding an equimolar ratio of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and Co(NO3)2·6H2O solution into an aqueous mixture of terephthalic acid and pyrazine ligands, resulting in a 3D framework.51 Subsequently, He and coworkers also reported the fabrication of Ni/Co-MOF through a simple hydrothermal method using Ni(NO3)2·6H2O, Co(NO3)2·6H2O, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (BTC) and polyvinylpyrrolidone K-30 (PVP).52,53 Young et al. also prepared M-MOFs of NiCo-MOF-74 using NiCl2, CoCl2 and 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid in the presence of PVP through the solvothermal method, which exhibited a constant spherical morphology with a size of 500 nm.54 Iqbal et al. demonstrated a series of bimetallic Co/Fe-MOFs through a one-pot synthetic approach using 2-aminoterephthalic acid. They varied the stoichiometric ratio of Co and Fe metal salts, and the resulting structure consisted of M3(μ3-O) trimeric clusters, as depicted in Fig. 2a–c.55 The construction of Co/Fe-MOFs took place via the replacement of irons from the Fe3O cluster (a) of MIL-88 to create a new Fe2Co(μ3-O) cluster in the framework, and subsequently, a Co3O (b) cluster was incorporated in the MOF unit. The above phenomena depended on the hard/soft acid-base theory, where Fe3+ coordinates to carboxylate and Co2+ can bind with N–H (c).56,57

M-MOFs containing the same metallic species in mixed-valence states can also be synthesized through the direct synthesis approach.48,58,59 Zhang et al. reported the synthesis of a mixed-valence redox-active cobalt(II/III) MOF using CoCl2·6H2O benzene-1,4-dicarboxylate and 2,4,6-tri(4-pyridinyl)-1,3,5-triazine via a solvothermal method.58 Fischer's group demonstrated the synthesis of mixed-valence Ru(II)/Ru(III)-based M-MOFs with 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate through the solvothermal method.59 Generally, the contribution of two different metal nodes in one molecular unit creates M-MOFs. However, Yaghi and coworkers established multivariate MOFs (MTV-MOFs), which contained more than two divalent transition/alkaline metal ions in one SBU to generate advanced M-MOF materials, where various metal ions were distinctly arranged inside the solid.60 They demonstrated a series of 23 nanocrystalline nMOF-74s. The nanocrystalline nMOF-74 framework was prepared via a microwave-assisted solvothermal reaction using 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid and acetate salts of Co, Mg, Ni, Zn, Mn, Fe, and Cd in N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (Fig. 3). These MOFs were also explored for electrochemical applications because of their structural versatility, different functionality/geometry, presence of multi-metallic nodes, varying pore size and nanocrystalline nature.60 Subsequently, Zhang et al. reported a multivariate MOF-177 through the solvothermal technique using a triangular 1,3,5-benzenetribenzoate (BTB) linker and octahedral Zn4O(–COO)6 node, which exhibited a 3D framework with the qom topology.61 They demonstrated that other essential factors such as pH, coordination sphere, reactivity and solubility play an important role in defining the ratio of metal nodes present in the formation of MTV-MOFs.60,61 Considering the various possibilities in the synthetic method, suitable screening of diverse conditions determined the impact of different parameters in the M-MOFs.

2.2 Post-synthetic modification

The post-synthetic method is a useful strategy for the formation of targeted heterometallic MOF units, which are unattainable or difficult to achieve using direct synthesis or a one-pot synthesis approach. Generally, the post-synthetic modification approach is employed for ligand exchange, which recently has been incorporated to construct mixed metallic frameworks due to the distinct reactivity of their metal components.33,62–64 The typical post-synthetic modification method for the formation of M-MOFs involves new metal insertion, exchange of metal nodes and trans-metalation by immersing MOFs in a different metal salt solution (Fig. 4). A controllable stoichiometric ratio of metal nodes in M-MOFs can also be achieved using the post-synthetic approach,64 which can tune the physical and chemical properties of M-MOFs for a specific application and is crucial to construct di- and trivalent redox-active M-MOFs for energy storage.
Fig. 4 Schematic synthesis of M-MOFs by post-synthetic approach.

The process metal ion exchange in post-synthetic modification is kinetically controlled.65a–d Generally, metal ion exchange is achieved by stirring pristine MOF with a suitable solution of other metal ions, which can partially replace the metal nodes of the pristine MOF, followed by controlling the reaction time, concentration and temperature.65d,e,66 Generally, the metal ion exchange process is mainly conducted on “labile”, weakly stable MOFs, which contain weak metal–ligand bonds. However, it is also explored for the highly stable UiO, ZIF, and MIL inert MOFs. Fei et al.67 first established the post-synthetic/metal exchange on thermally/chemically intact zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF)-based MOF structures in 2013. They introduced the redox-active Mn2+ ions in ZIF materials without deforming the framework of ZIF-8 and ZIF-71. The metal exchange process of ZIF-71 and ZIF-8 was reversible, where 12% and 10% of the Zn2+ ion centers were substituted with Mn2+ ions for ZIF-71 and ZIF-8, respectively. Kerr et al.68 reported three copper and uranium-based M-MOFs through the post-synthetic method by utilizing pre-synthesized Cu-complexes and treatment with UO22+. Generally, the metal ion exchange method has been investigated via a solvent-assisted approach, although another metal ion exchange approach for post-synthetic modification was also explored by Li's group.69 They demonstrated a series of bimetallic PCN-6′ (M) through a vapor-assisted solid-phase method by mixing PCN-6′ and solid CoCl2, FeCl2, MnCl2, and ZnCl2 using a grinding process. The proportion of exchanged second metal was 18.4% for Mn, 19.5% for Fe, 11.7% for Co, and 16.8% for Zn. Dinca et al.63 demonstrated a post-synthetic ion exchange method using transition metal nodes such as V2+, Ti3+, Cr2+/Cr3+, Mn2+, and Fe2+ in MOF-5 as a host material to assist in the synthesis of redox-active M-MOFs (Fig. 5). The synthesis of M-MOFs was achieved by immersing the crystals of MOF-5 in concentrated DMF solution of Fe(BF4)2·6H2O, MnCl2, CrCl2 or VCl2(pyridine)4 for 7 days to obtain M-MOF-5, while CrCl3·3THF, VCl3·3THF and TiCl3·3THF salts were employed to introduce Cr3+, V3+ and Ti3+ metal ions. It is well known that the metal exchange technique to construct M-MOFs is influenced by the reversible nature of the metal and ligand coordination bonds of highly crystalline MOFs.70,71

Demetalation/metal insertion is another alternative post-synthetic strategy for the synthesis of M-MOFs.32,33,71–73 In a typical method, vacant nodes can be created in pristine MOFs through elimination, and the simultaneous refilling of vacancies by another metal node generates M-MOFs.71 Metal insertion in various MOFs is also an advantageous technique to obtain M-MOFs. It is carried out by sacking different metal complexes in suitable pristine MOFs with the chelating functional groups, which leads to the formation of M-MOFs.72–74

MOFs containing bifunctional ligands can subsequently connect with other metal nodes and generate M-MOFs. Post-synthetic modification is the common two-step process used for the preparation of M-MOFs that are difficult to construct through the direct synthesis method.74–76 However, it is challenging to control the position of the metal node and stoichiometric composition in the final M-MOFs. Kim et al.77 reported a stepwise synthetic route for the synthesis of stoichiometric controllable NiZn-MOF-74 and MgZn-MOF-74. Moreover, stoichiometric controlled M-MOFs were also published by employing a two-step mechanosynthesis procedure. This strategy exhibited substantial enhancement in controlling the 1:1 stoichiometric composition of two discrete metal ions in an M-MOF, which was facilitated by RT mechanochemical milling reactions (Fig. 6a–c).34,78 Recently, Ayoub et al. demonstrated a simple two-step mechanochemical strategy for the preparation of twelve microporous MM′-MOF-74 in a controlled stoichiometric ratio (1:1) of divalent transition metal and main group metal ions, where M = Zn, Mg, Ni, Cu, Co; M′ = Zn, Co, Ca, Mg, and Cu,34 where they observed noticeable color changes in the reaction mixture (Fig. 6).

2.3 Metalloligands

Incorporating metalloligands is a useful approach to construct mixed metallic MOFs. In this synthetic approach, metal-containing ligands are employed with compatible metal salts to prepare M-MOFs. Another method involves the immobilization of metal complexes to construct varying structural motifs of M-MOFs via direct synthesis or ligand exchange approaches.79–81 The important factors to utilize metalloligands are their specific coordination with one type of metal ion and rational immobilization of various metal sites to generate M-MOFs. This method used a pre-functionalized metallocomplex as the ligand or ligand exchange strategies to develop new types of M-MOFs (Fig. 7).79–81
Fig. 7 Synthesis of M-MOFs using metalloligands and via ligand exchange approaches.

An appropriate example of the construction of M-MOFs based on metalloligands is the incorporation of the 1,1′-ferrocene dicarboxylic acid (FcDCA) ligand. Du et al. reported a series of M-MOFs by employing the FcDCA ligand together with other metal ions through the solvothermal method.82 In our previous works, we used the 1,1′-ferrocene dicarboxylic acid metalloligand to construct M-MOFs (Fig. 8a and b).16,83 These M-MOFs were obtained through the slow diffusion method at room temperature (RT). Moreover, a specific metalloligand coordinates with metal ions in different binding modes to generate M-MOFs with fascinating structural diversity.79–82


Fig. 8 (a and b) Synthesis of M-MOFs using metalloligands. Reproduced from ref. 16 with permission from the American Chemistry Society, Copyright 2019, and ref. 83 with permission from the Royal Society of Chemistry, Copyright 2017, respectively.

This phenomenon is related to the incorporation of preformed metallocomplexes as ligands to achieve an advanced class of M-MOFs. MTV-MOFs were also obtained by employing two different oxamide-based metalloligands. The framework consisted of a hexagonal channel with two flexible and functional “arms” (–CH2OH and –CH2CH2SCH3).81

In the aforementioned section, we discussed different synthetic strategies for the synthesis of M-MOFs. Although it is challenging to incorporate various metal ions in a single structure, it has been reported that a similar size, hardness–softness of metallic species, charge density, and synthetic conditions are crucial parameters for the successful synthesis of M-MOFs. However, the current review focuses on the potential of M-MOFs and their derivatives to develop advanced materials for charge storage applications.

3. Mixed-metal MOFs in supercapacitors

3.1 M-MOFs as supercapacitor electrode materials

Recently, M-MOFs have attracted great attention in supercapacitor applications.83,84 Supercapacitors are emerging energy storage technologies that can supply efficient power to electronic devices and also exhibit high power density, superior cyclic stability and higher charge–discharge ability.19,85–87 The electrochemical performance of MOFs generally relies on several factors such as structural stability, functionality, conductivity and morphology.88,89 There are several reports on single metal MOFs as suitable supercapacitor materials.90,91 Very recently, the paradigm shifted to the incorporation of M-MOFs that deliver superior framework stability and tunable breathing behavior owing to their high oxidation state and synergic effect of their mixed metal component, resulting in higher capacitance performance and excellent cyclic stability compared to that of single metal MOFs.35,92,93 Subsequently, the development of electrochemically active M-MOFs for direct usage in supercapacitors is one of the essential tasks. Some reports have presented the direct consumption of M-MOFs for supercapacitor application.85,94,95

Ranjbar's51 group first reported a bimetallic MOF for supercapacitor application. The Ni/Co-MOF was prepared via the direct synthesis approach and the resultant M-MOF exhibited a 3D framework. The structural analysis suggested that the Ni2+ and Co2+ metal ions were equally distributed in the Ni/Co-MOF framework, where the occupation of each metal ion was 50%, which was also verified by the ICP technique.51 In the 2D frameworks, the terephthalic acid linker was linked with two different metal nodes via bidentate and bridged coordination modes, as shown in Fig. 9a. Further, the resultant infinite 2D layers were pillared with pyrazine molecules and generated a 3D framework (Fig. 9b). Owing to the high surface area of Ni/Co-MOF, it exhibited a high specific capacitance (1049 F g−1 at of 1 A g−1) with remarkable rate ability (642 F g−1 at 10 A g−1) in 3 M KOH electrolyte solution. The higher specific capacitance of Ni/Co-MOF was attributed to the involvement of both metal components in the redox reactions. The MOF showed excellent stability and retained 97.4% of its original capacitance after 5000 cycles, signifying its capacity as a promising pseudocapacitive material.51


Fig. 9 (a) Infinite 2D layer of Ni/Co-MOF along the a-axis and (b) 3D framework of Ni/Co-MOF along the b-axis. Reproduced from ref. 51 with permission from the Royal Society of Chemistry, Copyright 2016.

Xia et al.96 prepared hollow structured Ni-MOF nanoflakes and Ni/Co-MOF nanoflakes through the solvothermal method, which were employed as supercapacitor materials. In the synthesis of the Ni-MOF nanoflakes and Ni/Co-MOF nanoflakes, as-synthesized ZIF-67 was dispersed in methanol by adding an equimolar ratio of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O/Co(NO3)2·6H2O in methanol solution, respectively. The Ni/Co-MOF nanoflakes exhibited a superior capacitance performance (530.4 F g−1 at 0.5 A g−1) in 1 M LiOH electrolytic solution, which was higher than that of Ni-MOF (306.8 F g−1 at 0.5 A g−1). The Ni/Co-MOF nanoflakes demonstrated good rate capacity and superior cyclic performance with a negligible change in capacitance.96 The optimized capacitance corresponded to the 2D nanoflake structure of Ni/Co-MOF, which accelerated the charge and electron transfer during the charge–discharge process. Gao et al.52 also prepared a dandelion-like hollow structure Ni/Co-MOF via the hydrothermal method, which showed an excellent specific capacitance (758 F g−1 at 1 A g−1) in 2 M KOH electrolyte. Ni/Co-MOF retained 75% of its primary capacitance after 5000 cycles with excellent rate ability of 89% at 10 A g−1.52 This high capacitance can be ascribed to the small size of the Co2+ ions that partially substitute the Ni2+ ions, resulting in the generation of more defects and free holes in the MOF framework, which enhanced the electric conductivity.53 Zhao et al. constructed an Ni/Co-MOF via the solvothermal method using NiCl2·6H2O, BTC and a different ratio of CoCl2·6H2O in DMF:H2O solution.97 The resultant network can be demonstrated as a layered structure with small pores on its surface, which may enhance the contact between the MOF and electrolyte in the supercapacitor. Ni/Co-MOF exhibited an excellent specific capacitance of 1067 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and stability up to 2500 cycles and retained 68.4% of its initial capacitance. Wang et al.98 constructed an NiCo-MOF nanomaterial on nickel foam (NF) via a simple one-step hydrothermal reaction using Ni(NO3)2·6H2O, Co(NO3)2·6H2O, terephthalic acid and nickel foam in water and ethanol. The optimized molar ratio of Ni/Co for the obtained triangle-like NiCo-MOF/NF was 3:2, which demonstrated a high specific capacitance of 2230 F g−1 at 1 A g−1, which is higher than that of other reported M-MOFs (Fig. 10). The outstanding supercapacitor performance was ascribed to the 3D well-aligned nanosheet structure of the NiCo-MOF/NF arrays, which boosted the electroactive area, and the presence of conductive NF substrate, enhancing the charge transfer from the electrode to the conductive material.98

Owing to the fascinating properties of MOFs, intricate frameworks such as polycatatened entangled MOFs have also been studied for supercapacitor applications. Polycatenation frameworks have high structural complexity/dimensionality and they can tune the breathing behavior of MOFs, providing a high supercapacitor performance. Gong et al.99 reported the first example of a supercapacitor based on polycatenated entangled frameworks of Cd- and Zn-MOFs with the very low supercapacitance performance of 22 F g−1 and 23 F g−1, respectively.99

In our previous work, we reported the use of a Co-based M-MOF as a supercapacitor electrode material.83 Fe/Co-MOF was synthesized via the slow diffusion technique by adding an equimolar ratio of Co(NO3)2·6H2O and mixed ligands of 1,1′-ferrocene dicarboxylic acid (FcDCA) and 4,4′-bipyridyl (bpy) in aqueous methanolic solution. The structural analysis revealed the 2D + 2D → 3D inclined polycatenation framework of the Fe/Co-MOF. Due to the presence of 11 lattice and 6 coordinated water molecules, the formed 3D framework was stabilized by electrostatic interactions such as π⋯π stacking and hydrogen bonding interactions, which provided a conducting pathway for charge transfer (Fig. 11a). From a topological point of view, the whole network was classified as a 2D + 2D → 3D inclined polycatenation class with a Doc1/1 class (Fig. 11b). The presence of FcDCA resulted in an enhanced surface area for Fe/Co-MOF (41.56 m2 g−1) compared to Co-MOF (10.30 m2 g−1).83 Fe/Co-MOF exhibited a high specific capacitance (446.8 F g−1 at 1.2 A g−1) in 1 M KOH aqueous solution without the use of any binder (Fig. 11c–f), which was much higher than that of Co-MOF (58.72 F g−1 at 1.2 A g−1). Thus, the above results indicate that the existence of FcDCA together with bpy provides structural flexibility, which was highly suitable to boost the supercapacitance performance of Fe/Co-MOF. This MOF exhibited an excellent rate performance and retained 81% of its primary capacitance at 10 A g−1. It also showed excellent cyclic stability up to 800 cycles with 88.37% retention of its initial capacitance, suggesting its capacity as a promising pseudocapacitive material. This observation gives new prospects to construct progressive M-MOFs with superior supercapacitor performances.83

Subsequently, according to recent reports, it has been observed that the presence of Na metal ions and redox-active linkers enhances the framework stability of electrode materials during the charge/discharge cycles, resulting in enhanced cycling stability.35,93 In our recently published work, we synthesized a sodium–cobalt-based mixed metallic MOF using a mixed ligand strategy. 3.2 M-MOF-derived metal oxides, metal sulfides, and metal phosphides as supercapacitor materials

By varying the coordinating sites and size of linkers in MOFs, the size and volume of their pores can be tuned easily. Besides their tunable sizes and pore volumes, their high surface area and advantage of preserving these properties upon combustion even at high temperature make MOFs an excellent sacrifice surface to obtain MOs. The resulting MOs exhibit an exceptionally large surface area with a rich morphology, controllable structure, and active surface area.101 The redox-active nature of MOs and their cost-effective production have brought them into the limelight as materials for the fabrication of electrodes for supercapacitor devices. The rich morphological surface area with desirable volume and size of pores of MOs are their key features utilized is electrochemical charge storage devices. Accordingly, to facilitate more functionalized and morphologically richer MOs, various techniques have been applied, such as hydrothermal synthesis, microwave sonication approach, sonochemical synthetic approach, and electro-deposition. Recently, several energy storage-oriented publications based on MOFs and derivative materials have been published worldwide. Among them, the MOF-derived MOs were shown to be superior. For example, solvothermally grown ZIF-67 (Co-ZIF) was synthesized, which possessed a I3m space group of a cubic system containing Co(NO3)2·6H2O as an inorganic salt and 2-methylimidazole as an organic linker.14 Saraf et al. further calcined this Co-ZIF to obtain Co3O4 nanoparticles and deposited them on the surface of an electrode to conduct the charge storage experiment. At a higher charge density of 5 A g−1, a specific capacitance 190 F g−1 of was recorded with a decent cycling stability of 71.42% even after 5000 cycles.

Li et al. synthesized porous microflowers of Co3O4 MOs via the simple calcination of a flower-shaped Co-MOF.102 This Co-MOF was synthesized through a solvothermal process. This group synthesized Co-MOFs using nicotinic acid as the organic linker together with Co(NO3)2·6H2O as the inorganic salt, and NaOH in alcohol to produce a pink color precipitate. The flower-like morphology was maintained in the combustion process and the MOF-derived MOs were used to fabricate supercapacitor electrodes. These MOs electrodes exhibited a specific capacitance of 240.2 F g−1 at a current density of 0.625 A g−1 and retained 96.3% capacitance even in the 2000th charge–discharge cycle. The specific capacitance of these microflowers was measured to be in the range of 240.02 to 202.1 F g−1 for a charge density ranging from 0.625 to 6.25 A g−1, demonstrating the retention of 84.1% of their initial capacity. This indicated the good rate capability of these as-fabricated micro-flower-based electrodes. Further, to enhance the charge storage capabilities of cobalt metal oxide nanostructures, Liu et al. improved their surface activity.103 Specifically, NaBH4 was used as a reducing agent to generate the affordable R-Co3O4 from ZIF-67. This resulted in an enhancement in specific capacitance up to 329 F g−1 on 1 A g−1 rather than only 116 F g−1 at the same current density. However, although this new class of charge storage is exciting, and from the above-mentioned reports, it is clear that MOF-derived MOs result in a significant improvement in specific capacitance, these reported values are much lower than the theoretical values, for example, the reported value for Co3O4 is 3560 F g−1.104 Additionally, for nickel oxide, its theoretical value is 2584 F g−1, and that for Mn2O3 is 1109 F g−1.105,106 This discrepancy may be due to the pulverization of the material during the charge–discharge process, together with the poor electrical conductivity of mono metal oxides. With structural rigidity, this problem can be overcome by preparing mixed metal MOs for further improvement.

To achieve this goal, a variety of mixed metal oxides (MMOs) have been prepared from M-MOFs such as scheelite-like, spinel, and CaFe2O4-type crystal structures.107,108 These MMOs exhibit richer redox chemistry than single MOs because of their relatively low electron transfer activation energy. They always contain at least one transition metal and one electrochemical active or inactive ion, or sometimes more. Based on their greater structural stability, relatively high reversible capacity, and electrical conductance, these bimetallic metal oxides (BMOs) have been widely studied to modify and prepare novel electrode materials for supercapacitors. Owing to their diverse morphological variety and configuration, there are several reports on the use of BMOs to functionalize electrode materials and electrodes for supercapacitors. Jiang et al. used Pluronic P123 and H2C2O4 dissolved in a mixture of polyethylene glycol:ethanol (4:1) with Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and Co(NO3)2·6H2O was stirred for 10 min.109 After filtration and washing with water–ethanol several times, the precipitate was collected and dried for 6 h at 60 °C. Followed by air combustion, the dried precipitate turned into porous 1D NiCo2O4 nanowires. These MMOs were further processed at RT via a polyethylene glycol-directed facile technique to remove impurities. The modified electrode material exhibited a reasonably high specific capacitance of 743 F g−1 at 1 A g−1. It also displayed improved cyclic stability and retained 93.8% of its initial capacitance at the same current density. Wei et al. reported spinel 2D sheet-like nanostructures of NiMn2O4, which were fabricated through a one-pot hydrothermal synthesis method.108 Co(NH2)2, Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and NH4F were stirred in distilled water with KMnO4 for 12 h at 160 °C. After filtration and washing with water–ethanol several times, the mixture was dried for 12 h at 60 °C followed by combustion at 350 °C for 2 h. Further, on investigating it as a binder-free material, it exhibited much higher specific capacitance compared to Mn3O4. Also, in the cyclic stability test (1000 cycles), it retained up to 96% of its initial capacitance as 662.5 and 370.5 F g−1 in two different electrolytes at a current density of 1 A g−1. To optimize the dimensionality factor, a 3D flower-shaped NiCo2O4 was prepared by An et al.110 This nanostructure was prepared via the solvothermal process followed by annealing. Metal glycolate and surfactant played a crucial role in the morphological enhancement in these flower-shaped BMOs. The Ni–Co mixed metal oxide displayed a remarkably high specific capacitance of 755.2 and 1191.2 F g−1 at current densities of 10 A g−1 and 1 A g−1, respectively. Its larger surface area of 212.6 m2 g−1 was the reason for its higher specific capacitance. Still, the common issue with these methods is achieving a precise metal ratio and molecular level mixing by generating a homogeneous phase in MMOs. A well-defined stoichiometric ratio and even atomic-level precision in homogenous mixing were found in mixed metallic MOFs, which can be easily transformed into MMOs.

A general strategy widely investigated by various scientific groups is to take advantage of M-MOFs as a precursor for MMOs. These M-MOFs are generally synthesized via the typical method, i.e. one-pot synthesis (Fig. 15). The as-synthesized M-MOFs are dried at a significantly high temperature to remove solvent molecules from their cavity and impurities followed by high-temperature combustion of the obtained precursor under various environments.112–115

Taking advantage of the tunable nature of M-MOFs, Chen et al. reported the first mixed MOs (ZnCo2O4) derived from a Zn–Co-MOF (JUC-155) and utilized it as an electrode material in supercapacitors.111 This group used ZnCl2 with CoCl2·6H2O salts, and the organic counterpart was BTC in a mixture of DMF and water, which were reacted for 6 h at 80 °C. Block-shaped purple-red shaded JUC-155 was collected upon cooling to RT, which was an I4cm space group tetragonal system. After calcination at three different temperatures (400 °C, 450 °C, and 500 °C), JUC-155 produced three forms of Zn–Co-MMOs, which were further denoted as compound 1, compound 2, and compound 3, respectively (Fig. 16a–f). JUC-155 was calcined in air for 2 h at 400 °C and transformed into porous spinel phase ZnCoO4 nanoparticles (NPs). These NPs when utilized as electrode materials exhibited a specific capacitance of 450 F g−1 at a very low scan rate of 5 mV s−1, and maintained 97.9% of their initial capacitance even after 1500 cycles. After introducing the concept of MMOs from M-MOFs, this research group explored a series of Ni–Co-MMOs, which were isomorphic to the Co3O4 crystals derived from the MOF-74 topological phase.112 They prepared a series of isostructural MOFs of MOF-74 in a mixture prepared with the V/V/V combination of water–ethanol–DMF (1:1:1) using 2,5-hydroxyterephthalic acid as an organic linker with Co(NO3)2·6H2O and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O as inorganic salts in various combinations to generate five MOFs in a solvothermal process at 100 °C for 24 h.

All these synthesized MOFs were submerged in methanol to facilitate the formation of pores by removing the impurities trapped in them. This further improved and maintained the desired stoichiometric ratio of both metals up to atomic level precision in the MMO samples upon calcination. After calcination of these M-MOFs, a series of NixCo3−xO4 material were derived from MOF-74-NiCo4, MOF-74-NiCo2, MOF-74-NiCo1, MOF-74-Co, and MOF-74-Ni showing the composition ratio of Co:Ni = 4:1, 2:1, 1:1, 1:0, and 0:1, respectively (Fig. 17). This series was examined to determine the electrochemical relation with the intentional doping of one metal in another metal oxide. The authors described the direct impact of a higher concentration of Ni3+ ions on the charge storage capacity of the NixCo3−xO4 series. NiCo1-MMOs exhibited the highest surface area among the NixCo3−xO4 series, which was prepared via the calcination of MOF-74-NiCo and provided a larger surface with more Ni3+, resulting in its superior performance compared to the individual Ni-MOs and Co-MOs. Moreover, in the NixCo3−xO4 series, NiCo1-MMOs exhibited higher charge storage capability compared to the Co3O4 MOs NPs. NiCo1-MMOs displayed a specific capacitance value of 782 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 and retained up to 75% of its initial capacity at a current density of 20 A g−1. Jayakumar et al. further explored the effect from the altering amount of Ni-metal in NixCo3−xO4 MMOs, where the authors optimized the ratios of Ni:Co = 0.5:1, 1:1, and 1.5:1 to generate hollow Ni–Co MMOs from ZIF-67.113

ZIF-67 was prepared via the standard process of crystallization at RT, in which both starting materials were added to two immiscible solvents separately and left undisturbed for the crystallization process. The reaction mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O as the inorganic salt with 2-methyl-imidazole as an organic linker was left undisturbed for a period of 24 h to obtain crystalline ZIF-67. The obtained crystals of ZIF-67 were processed in methanol and dried at 60 °C under vacuum overnight to remove the impurities from the cavity sites of ZIF-67. Nickel was introduced as a dopant from a Ni(NO3)2·6H2O salt to the processed ZIF-67 at 90 °C for a period of 1 h. These samples were again washed with ethanol and calcined in air at 350 °C for 1 h, followed by natural cooling, further producing Ni–Co MMOs. The prepared Ni–Co MMOs possessed a rhomboid dodecahedron shape similar to ZIF-67, but no well-defined structural representation of pure Co3O4 was obtained. Introducing Ni3+ into Co3O4 resulted in a relatively more structured and regular morphology. NiCo2O4 exhibited a flawless clear surface as its modified morphology, which is the result of an increase in the amount of Ni in the sample. Hollower and thinner layered surface nanostructures appeared to form at a 1:1 ratio of Co:Ni. A further increment in Ni-metal concentration provided a dendron–like thick surface formed by the additional nickel oxide layer on the surface. The novel morphology formed using a ratio of 1:1 from Co:Ni was superior to the other combinations in capacitance by delivering a specific capacitance of 1931 F g−1 at the current density of 1 A g−1, also showing 69.5% capacitance retention even after 5000 repeated charge–discharge cycles.

An Mn–Co MMO combination was explored by Dong et al., where they synthesized MMOs from Mn–Co-ZIFs through a calcination process.114 A mixed metallic ZIF was produced by using Mn(NO3)2·4H2O and Co(NO3)2·4H2O as the source of the inorganic part and 2-methylimidazole as the organic counterpart in a hydrothermal process for 12 h at 80 °C. The As-prepared mixed metallic ZIF was processed by washing several times in water–ethanol and dried for 12 h to remove the captured solvent and impurities from the cavity sites of Mn–Co-ZIF. This process was followed by calcination in air at 400 °C for 2 h to produce Mn–Co-MMOs. Dang and coworkers synthesized nano-caged MnCo2O4 MMOs and compared their electrochemical performance with the Co3O4 fabricated electrode obtained from Co-ZIFs by the same preparation process. MnCo2O4 outperformed Co3O4 with a better specific capacitance of 1763 F g−1vs. 1209 F g−1 at a current density of 1 A g−1 together with capacitance retention of 95.0% vs. 81.2%, respectively. With this unique morphology, it is believed that the hollow structured nanocages have an appropriate channel size, which provided better interaction to charge carriers through the interconnected NPs.

Lan et al. synthesized a greenish bimetallic MOF using Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and Mn(NO3)2·4H2O as the source of inorganic part and benzenedicarboxylic acid as the organic counterpart in ethanol as the solvent in an autoclave at 120 °C for 4 h.115 After washing and drying, the as-prepared bimetallic MOFs were further annealed at 450 °C in air, developing a flower-shaped MnNi2O4 mixed metal oxide. The M-MOF-derived MMOs exhibited an excellent specific capacitance equivalent to 2848 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 with promising capacitance retention stability of 93.25% at 10 A g−1 after 5000 cycles. Further, this group assembled an MnNi2O4vs. active carbon for positive vs. negative electrode and observed a power density of 800 W kg−1vs. energy density of 142.8 W h kg−1 together with excellent cyclic stability for up to 2000 cycles.

To improve the properties of MMOs derived from M-MOFs for charge storage applications, Zardkhoshoui et al. utilized graphene sheets to wrap MMOs and further used the graphene-wrapped multi-shelled/yolk-shell NiM2O4 (M = Ga and Fe) hollow spheres as electrodes in a supercapacitor device.116 Because of its highly porous nature and hollow spherical shape, NiM2O4 was used as an electrode material. Multi-shelled NiGa2O4 fabricated using graphene sheets worked as a positive electrode, and its counterpart yolk-shell NiFe2O4 functionalized as a negative electrode. M-MOFs were produced via the typical one-pot synthesis method. For Ni–Ga MOFs, Zardkhoshoui et al. used Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and Ga(NO3)3·xH2O salts as inorganic precursors, while isophthalic acid was used as an organic linker in a mixture of acetone and DMF for 4 h at 160 °C to produce a crystalline product. Ni–Fe MOFs were also synthesized via a hydrothermal process at 120 °C for 6 h using 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid, Fe(NO3)3·9H2O and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O as constructing units in a DMF–acetone mixture. The as-synthesized Ni–Ga MOFs and Ni–Fe MOFs were calcined at 350 °C to 500 °C, and 400 °C, respectively, under an argon environment to yield MMOs. Further, they were treated with isopropyl alcohol and triethoxysilane overnight to activate their surfaces. The obtained products were stirred for 6 h with graphene oxide (GO) in water to achieve graphene-wrapped Ni–Ga MMOs (GW-MSNGOHS) and graphene-wrapped Ni–Fe MMOs (GW-YS-NFOHS). After drying under an argon environment for a period of 4 h at 500 °C, these graphene wrapped-MMOs were used as an electrode in a supercapacitor device. The GW-YS-NFOHS- and GW-MSNGOHS-modified electrodes provided a rapid pathway for charge carriers, which was reflected in their capacities (254.25 mA h g−1 and 411.25 mA h g−1 at 1 A g−1) and exceptional cyclability (98.9% and 90.9%), respectively. The advantages of the engineered positive and negative electrodes in the fabrication of an asymmetric device (GW-MSNGOHS//GW-YS-NFOHS) resulted in an exceptionally good energy density vs. power density (118.97 W h kg−1vs. 1702 W kg−1), maintaining a high capacity of 140.2 mA g−1 at 1 A g−1. Even after 10000 cycles, a remarkable capacity retention rate of 92.1% was observed.

Among them, the NiCoP/C material displayed a specific capacity of 775.1 C g−1 at 1 A g−1 with a retention as high as 75.1% even at the high current density of 20 A g−1. Even after the significant progress achieved, it should be noted that there is an urgent need for simple and cost-effective techniques to produce functional MMOs from M-MOFs, which demonstrate high supercapacitor performances and can also be suitable for various other applications.108,109,113,115 By developing novel and efficient strategies and without compromising the fascinating properties of these materials, the focus should be on lowering their cost together with enhancing the possibility of the large-scale production of MMOs. 3.3 M-MOF composites as supercapacitor materials

3.3.1 M-MOF@GO, rGO, CNTs. Graphene and its derivatives provide huge advantages for supercapacitor applications since they possess a huge surface area, incredible electrical conductivity, and excellent thermal/chemical stabilities, and hence emerged as the most popular materials recently introduced as EDLC supercapacitors.121,122 Supercapacitors employing graphene exhibit a high power density but low specific capacitance.123 Hence, the desire for high energy density and cycle life has not been fulfilled.124 There are numerous reports on the association of MOF with graphene structures, which propose that these composites are effective alternatives to resolve the above problems.122 However, their cyclic stability and retention capacitance remain a problem. Therefore, the integration of mixed metal MOFs with graphene materials has been explored, which revealed very high specific capacitance together with excellent cyclic stability and complete retention of their initial capacitance.

Hong et al. proposed a series of hybrids by growing Ni–Co MOF on sheets of graphene oxide (GO) by varying its concentration to obtain Ni–Co-MOF/GO 1 (0.1 g of GO), Ni–Co-MOF/GO 2 (0.2 g of GO) and Ni–Co-MOF/GO 3 (0.3 g of GO). These hybrids were prepared via a solvothermal process in a one-pot reaction by mixing Co(NO3)2·6H2O and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O as metal precursors and 2-methylimidazole as the ligand in a dispersion of GO (Fig. 20a–e).125a The Ni–Co-MOF/GO hybrids maintained their 3D structure. Ni–Co-MOF 2 crystals with a chestnut-like shape were embedded on GO sheets, which provided a greater contact area between the electrolyte and electrode, causing a reduced path for the diffusion of ions. Ni–Co-MOF/GO 1, Ni–Co-MOF/GO 2, and Ni–Co-MOF/GO 3 exhibited specific capacitances of 230.9, 447.2, and 341.8 F g−1, respectively, at a current density of 1 A g−1. Ni–Co-MOF/GO 2 achieved the highest specific capacitance with remarkable cyclic stability of 99.6% after undergoing 300 cycles. However, the specific capacitance observed for Ni–Co-MOF was 120.0 F g−1 at 1 A g−1 with a retention of 81% after 300 cycles. The excellent electrochemical performance and capacitive features of the hybrid electrode were due to the synergistic effects and redox chemistry of Ni and Co present as binary metals in M-MOF and GO. Further, its fabrication with GO created 3D nanostructures, resulting in admirable energy storage properties. Its high mechanical strength is credited to the large number of oxygen atoms from the functional groups of GO, which are well-coordinated with Ni and Co ions from the solvent and organic ligands, also resulting in an enhancement in the cycle performance of the electrode. The bimetallic system present inside the composite offers a system in which the original metal ion can be replaced by a second metal ion, providing a new functionality.125b

3.3.7 M-MOF-derived oxides/metal nanocomposites. The well-regulated structures of MOFs with tunable pore sizes enable the fast interfacial transport of ions, acting as a template for the preparation of porous metal oxides. These hybrid materials showing remarkable electrochemical properties contained graphitic carbon and binary mixed metals, where the synergy between them enhanced the electrical conductivity of the composites. The easy diffusion of electrolyte was facilitated by the presence of mesopores having a large surface area and pore volume.

In a recent report, a bimetallic Co/Ni-MOF [(NixCo3−x)3O(BTC)2(H2O)-(DMF)]n was used as a template to generate Cox/Ni1−x(OH)2, a layered structure, via an alkali hydrolysis approach (Fig. 31a), which exhibited an outstanding specific capacitance of 1956 F g−1 at a current density of 5 A g−1.137c This capacitance value was 1.59 times and 15.37 times that of the MOF-derived Ni(OH)2 and Co(OH)2, respectively. However, the cycling performance was quite low, possessing 48% capacitance retention after 2000 cycles, which was further improved by forming the NiCo2O4/β-NixCo1−x(OH)2/α-NixCo1−x(OH)2 ternary composite (Ni/Co-TC) via the selective oxidation of Cox/Ni1−x(OH)2. The as-synthesized ternary composite with a structure of accordion-like nanosheets displayed 90.7% capacitance retention after 10000 cycles, which is quite high in comparison to its precursors. An asymmetric all-solid-state hybrid supercapacitor was also established by the ternary composite, named Ni/Co-TC//AC, which exhibited a high energy density of 36.98 W h kg−1 at a power density of 801.49 W kg−1 (Fig. 31b–e). The above work presented an approach of hydrolysis-oxidation to assemble multi-component electrode materials through a bimetallic MOF template, resulting in precise morphologies and outstanding electrochemical properties.

4. Future scope

M-MOFs are a promising class of MOF materials that offers numerous benefits over single metal MOFs. The incorporation of different metal ions significantly increases the complexity and stability of MOFs, which leads to promising synergic properties. M-MOFs demonstrating structural stability and multi-functionality can offer superior performances to single metal MOFs.16,48,100 Generally, the applications of M-MOFs depend on the reactivity of their metal ions.33,48 Although the synthesis of M-MOFs appears to be feasible and simple, there are several substantial tasks: (a) incorporating various metal ions in the same structure and find their nodal positions, (b) preparation of a controllable stoichiometric composition of metal ions in one structure, and (c) predicting/controlling the functionally and topologies of M-MOFs. Recently, M-MOFs have attracted significant consideration in the area of supercapacitors due to the increased complexity/stability, redox-active metal ions and breathing behavior of their frameworks.35,51,83,95 The strategy to enhance framework complexity can be easily applied in M-MOFs to boost their supercapacitive performance. M-MOFs have emerged as a popular sacrificial precursor to obtain mixed-metal oxides/sulphide/phosphide and their easy preparation method together with exciting properties such as tunable pore size, large surface area, redox-activity and reticular nature. Furthermore, the activated surface of M-MOF-derived materials is considered to be crucial to ensure high stability and activity of supercapacitors. M-MOF-derived mixed-metal oxides/sulphide/phosphide or further modified materials provide a rapid charge transfer pathway by shorting the mechanistic diffusion pathway, which results in a notable improvement in reaction kinetics. MMOs prepared from M-MOFs offer superior charge storage capabilities compared to MMOs prepared by other methods due to their rigid structure and precise mixing of atomic-level constituent elements.9,111,117–120 Selecting sulfur or phosphorus atoms instead of the oxygen atom further enhances their charge storage capability.113,117,118

However, M-MOF-derived materials have only started receiving attention recently, and thus need more exposure for their development. Furthermore, their commercial viability remains a prime challenge and has been a persistent issue like other many fascinating materials. During industrial-level production, a cost-effective and simple synthetic process of these materials, and subsequently electrodes, is necessary for their mass production. The durability of the fabricating material is one of the most critical factors of supercapacitors for long-term stability during charge–discharge in practical application.115,120 The improvement in the conductivity of these materials is very important for enhancing the rate performance and capacitance in supercapacitors. Thus, considering all these issues, it is crucial to develop innovative synthetic pathways to achieve the highly scalable, cost-effective production of M-MOFs.

Bimetallic MOFs offer huge possibilities for designing and modifying the properties of MOFs.138 The intrinsic properties of MOFs such as bandgap and thermal stability are largely influenced by the incorporation of a second metal cation. Single metallic MOF derivatives enhance the properties of electrode materials in a limited mode, whereas massive constructive changes in properties and functionalities are observed when mixed bi-metallic or tri-metallic MOFs are used as precursors to derive these nano-porous materials.14,113,116 There are many bimetallic MOFs reported up to date, most of which are Ni/Co MOFs and their derived oxides, sulfides, or composites with carbon, sulfur or nitrogen.33,34,36,37,54,131c,135,136 The presence of two metals in bi-metallic MOFs works in a synergistic way to increase the redox reactions and result in the enhanced movement of ions in the electrolyte, which consequently improves their electrochemical performance. However, still there is much more to explore since the synthesis of M-MOFs is limited to some transition metals only. Also, the applications of M-MOFs will find more possibilities in solid-state flexible supercapacitors, and in lithium-ion batteries, which still needs further exploration. Bimetallic MOF-based nanofibers exhibit brilliant electrochemical applications in flexible supercapacitors and can further be utilized in wearable electronics and devices due to their high stability, light weight, large surface area, flexibility and good conductivity. These wearable electronics can be worn near to the skin surface and transmit information after the detection of signals. Hence, the emerging world of M-MOFs is full of exciting opportunities and possibilities.

5. Conclusions

Herein, we mentioned several synthetic methods such as one-pot synthesis, post-synthetic modification, and use of metalloligands for the preparation of M-MOFs. It has been explored that a similar size, hardness–softness of metallic species, charge density, and synthetic conditions are crucial parameters for the successful synthesis of M-MOFs. The design of M-MOFs with high framework stability and tunable breathing characteristics may lead towards the synthesis of M-MOFs containing various metal centers to achieve a new class of advanced materials for supercapacitor applications. Also, the fabrication of hybrid nanostructured material derived from M-MOFs can certainly expand the family of energy storage materials. However, M-MOFs and their derived materials have not been fully explored to date, and the number of reported synthetic routes is limited. Nevertheless, it is possible in the future that we may see several advanced materials that will exploit the fascinating properties of M-MOFs in building high-performing supercapacitor devices for industrial purposes.
