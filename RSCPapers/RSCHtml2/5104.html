Abstract

Due to their fabulous performance, porphyrins are considered to be emerging materials for dye-sensitized solar cell applications (DSSCs). Herein, we have designed and synthesised four novel porphyrin sensitizers tethered with a triphenylimidazole group as the donor, a porphyrin macrocycle as the -spacer and 4-ethynyl phenyl (LG15), 5-ethynylthiophene (LG16), 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BTD)-phenyl (LG17), and BTD-thiophene (LG18) as auxiliary acceptors and either a carboxylic acid or cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group, which resembles the structure of a donor-bridgeacceptor (DA). The absorption spectra of LG17 and LG18 are red-shifted, when compared to LG15 and LG16, due to the presence of extended -conjugation of the BDT group in its molecular structure. The red-shifted absorption spectra of LG17 and LG18 result in highest efficiency of 10.11% and 10.51%, respectively. Optical and electrochemical properties, and Density Functional Theory calculations of LG15LG18, suggested that LG18 exhibits strong absorption and optimized lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) aids the injection of electrons very effectively from the excited state of the dye into the TiO2 conduction band. Both LG17 and LG18 exhibit broad incident to photon conversion efficiency (IPCE) and the onset extends up to 950 nm, with >80% at 700 nm. Current densityvoltage (JV) curves of LG15LG18 revealed that LG17 and LG18 exhibit highest short-circuit current density of 20.64 and 21.43 mA cm2 resulting in power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 10.11% and 10.51%, respectively, in a liquid I/I3 redox couple electrolyte. Bulky donor groups, -spacer length and anchoring groups have significantly contributed to achieving high efficiency in a liquid redox shuttle that can be helpful to make efficient DSSCs for future generations. We have adopted intensity modulated photovoltage spectroscopy and nanosecond laser flash photolysis spectroscopy to explain the PCE and molecular structure relationship of the LG15LG18 sensitizers.

Introduction

Porphyrins are ubiquitous in nature and have found many applications such as donoracceptor systems for bio-mimicking of natural photosynthesis, catalysis of organic transformations, electrocatalytic water splitting, sensitizers for photodynamic therapy, dyes for dye-sensitized solar cells, etc.18 Of these, we are particularly interested in porphyrin-based dyes for dye-sensitized solar cell applications (DSSCs). DSSCs are fascinating because of their beneficial capability of converting solar energy into electricity with comfortable fabrication which leads to economically viable production.9 In DSSCs, the sensitizer is one of the vital components because it harvests the light, i.e., absorption of light by the sensitizer followed by ejection of an electron, then injection into the TiO2 conduction band, the passage of the respective electron to the corresponding electrode, and yielding of a photocurrent in the external circuit. The extensively used sensitizers are Ru(II) polypyridyl complexes with certified efficiency of >11%.1014 However, Ru dyes have their own limitations, i.e., the scarcity of Ru metal in the earth's crust, effort in synthesis and purification, and a lack of absorption at the near-IR region of the absorption spectrum. For this reason, researchers have continuously rendered their efforts towards the development of novel sensitizers to further improve the Photon Conversion Efficiency (PCE). The essential requirements for the development of efficient dyes are: (a) firstly, the dye should adhere to the TiO2 conduction band, (b) LUMO (Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbitals) should be higher than the TiO2 conduction band and HOMO (Highest Occupied Molecular Orbitals) lower than that of the redox electrolyte for the regeneration of the oxidised dye, and (c) finally, the dye must have broad absorption from visible to the near-IR region for harvesting the maximum light from the sun.15

A great variety of metal-free organic dyes have been designed based on triarylamine,16 coumarin,17 perylene,18 indoline,19 cyanine20 hemocyanin,21 tetrathiafulvalene,22 carbazoles,23 and diketopyrrolopyrrole24 scaffolds using donoracceptor (DA) architecture. But these dyes are also unable to show broader absorption. In this regard, porphyrins are found to be alternative sensitizers for DSSC applications based on their optical, thermal and electrochemical properties.25 Moreover, the optical and electrochemical properties can be tuned by introducing substituents either at the meso-phenyl position or at the -pyrrole position. The most common porphyrin sensitizers used for DSSCs have meso-substituted benzoic acid linking groups. In 2000, 5,10,15,20-tetrakis-(4-carboxyphenyl)porphyrin was reported to have a PCE of 3.5% under low light intensity.26 Later, Grtzel and co-workers changed the design strategy by functionalizing the anchoring group at the -pyrrole position with Zn metallation and reported a PCE of 7.1%.27 To achieve the highest efficiency a continuous effort has been made on structural modifications at the meso positions of porphyrin macrocycles with a DA approach.2830 By this approach, the porphyrin sensitizers have crossed 10% PCE. But, stacking between the porphyrins causes aggregation on the TiO2 surface, which may affect further improvement of the PCE. To smooth the way for the enhanced conversion of sunlight into energy, the dye must have less aggregation. For this reason, Grtzel and co-workers have developed octyloxy-enveloped constructions at the meso phenyl position of a DA porphyrin, employing a benzothiadiazole (BTD) as an auxiliary acceptor allied between a spacer and a carboxylic acid (SM315), reporting a top score efficiency of 13% using a Co(II/III) redox couple.29 With the inclusion of bulky donors and long octyloxy chains, porphyrins have improved in solubility, reducing the stacking of the dyes as well as a reduction in the unfavorable charge recombination between injected electrons in the TiO2 and the redox couple in the electrolyte, which impacts on the PCE of the dye.

However, in the DA approach the -spacer and acceptor groups explore not only the light harvesting ability but also enhance the rate of electron injection and binding capacity on the TiO2 surface.3134 In this view, we have constructed a series of DA based porphyrin sensitizers, in which we have incorporated butterfly-conformation 3-ethynyl-10-octyl-10H-phenothiazine as a donor, with various acceptor moieties, and achieved a PCE of 10.20%.35 Recently, we further re-designed porphyrin sensitizers by introducing a more bulky phenanthroimidazole moiety as a donor with efficient changes on its substituents, which exhibited a PCE of 7.45% and co-sensitization with an organic dye has shown a PCE of 8.27%.36 In order to promote the efficiency of the device, there will be an efficient donor with non-planarity with integration of auxiliary acceptors between the porphyrin macrocycle and -spacers necessary for the DA approach. Thus, the porphyrin dyes fill the absorption gap between the Soret and Q bands, which avoids the co-sensitization approach.32

In the present work, we have designed and synthesised novel non-planar triphenylimidazole donor-based porphyrin sensitizers, which are linked to different acceptor groups (Chart 1). By this rational approach of synthesis, there is systematic improvement in the open circuit voltage (VOC) and short circuit current (JSC), thus pursuing high efficiency for DSSC applications. As shown in Chart 1, the porphyrin moiety was protected by octyloxy groups; these may diminish the unwanted charge recombination process between the iodine/triiodide redox shuttle and the oxidized dye. Triphenylimidazole has non-planarity in its structure, and it is a good electron donor linked to the meso-position of the porphyrin through an ethnyl bridge in all sensitizers (LG15LG18). The donor also participates in the suppression of aggregation due to its non-planar nature. All of the four porphyrins were differentiated only by the acceptor groups attached between the porphyrin macrocycle and anchoring group. To understand the effect of -spacers in the DSSCs, we have introduced phenyl, (LG15) and thiophene (LG16) moieties tethered as -spacers, and carboxylic acid as an anchoring group. To gain the long wavelength response, LG17 and LG18 have the BDT moiety as the auxiliary acceptor between the porphyrin core and the -spacer (phenyl) carboxylic acid (LG17), and -spacer (thiophene) cyanoacrylic acid (LG18).

Experimental section

Materials

We procured all commercially available chemicals and reagents from Sigma-Aldrich and Merck and they were used without any further purification. All the reactions were done with analytical reagent (AR) grade solvents and laboratory reagent (LR) grade solvents were used for purifications and column chromatography. Dichloromethane, chloroform, and acetonitrile were dried in the presence of calcium hydride under nitrogen atmosphere followed by distillation. Hexane, toluene, and tetrahydrofuran were purified by stirring overnight with Na metal added to benzophenone, then distilling under a vacuum and storing over 4 molecular sieves. Triethylamine was distilled over NaOH pellets. Column chromatography was carried out using ACME silica gel (60120 mesh) and thin layer chromatography was performed on Merck-precoated silica gel 60-F254 plates. Either gravity or flash chromatography was performed for purification of all compounds. All the reactions were carried out under nitrogen or argon atmosphere using dry and degassed solvents.

Methods and instrumentation

1H NMR spectra were obtained at 300 MHz using a Bruker 300 Avance NMR spectrometer running X-WIN NMR software and the chemical shift is relative to the tetramethylsilane (TMS), CDCl3, and pyridine D5 used as the internal references. The elemental analyses were performed on an Elementar, Vario MICRO CUBE analyzer. Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry was performed on a Shimadzu Biotech Axima Performance 2.9.3.20110624: Mode Reflectron-HiRes, power: 85. Major fragmentations are given as percentages relative to the base peak intensity. Differential pulse and cyclic voltammetric measurements were performed on a system controlled CH instruments model CHI 620C electrochemical analyzer. Cyclic voltammetric experiments were performed on a 1 mM sample solution in tetrahydrofuran solvent at a scan rate of 100 mV s1 using 0.1 M tetrabutylammoniumhexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6) as a supporting electrolyte. The working electrode was glassy carbon, a standard calomel electrode (SCE) was used as the reference electrode, and a platinum wire was the counter electrode. After a cyclic voltammogram (CV) was recorded, ferrocene was added, and the other voltammogram was measured. Using the same instrument, a CH instruments model CHI 620C electrochemical analyser, the spectroelectrochemical studies were measured using a three-electrode configuration of a thin layer quartz spectroelectrochemical cell at 25 C. A Pt gauge was the working electrode and a Pt wire was the counter electrode. The SCE reference electrode separated from the bulk of the solution by a double bridge was used. A Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA 851e instrument was used for the thermogravimetric measurements at a heating rate of 10 C min1 with 10 mg of sample.
Absorption steady state and time resolved fluorescence measurements. The absorption spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu (Model UV-3600) spectrophotometer at concentrations of 1 106 M for Soret band and 1 105 M for Q band absorption. A Spex model Fluorolog-3 was used for steady state fluorescence spectra for solutions having an optical density at the wavelength of excitation (ex) 0.15. Time-resolved fluorescence measurements were carried out using a HORIBA Jobin Yvon spectrofluorometer. Briefly, the samples were excited at 650 nm and the emission was monitored at 700 nm. The count rates employed were typically 103 to 104 s1. DAS-6 software was used for the deconvolution of the data, which was carried out by the method of iterative reconvolution of the instrument response function and the assumed decay function. Standard statistical tests were employed to judge the goodness of fit of the experimental data to the assumed decay function.
Theoretical calculations. All of the optimized geometries for LG15LG18 were stable in their conformation by means of global minimum energy, which were carried out using the B3LYP hybrid functional39 and 6-31(d,p) basis set40 as the input. Further calculations were done using Gaussian 09 (RevisionB.01) ab initio quantum chemical software41 on a personal computer. Ground state properties like energy-minimized structures and frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) were implemented using Density Functional Theory (DFT) in the gas phase. Excited state properties like percentage of molecular contribution, oscillatory strength, and singlet transition energy were executed using Time Dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) in tetrahydrofuran solvent. TDDFT calculations to describe the solvation of the dyes in tetrahydrofuran were carried out using the integral equation formalism polarizable continuum model (PCM) within the self-consistent reaction field (SCRF) theory.42,43 Simulation of the major portions of the absorption spectra and interpretation of the nature of transactions were carried out by employing the software Gauss Sum 2.2.5.44,45 The contributing percentages of individual units present in the dyes to the respective molecular orbitals were calculated.
DSSC cell fabrication. Generally the DSSC devices were fabricated using a TiO2 nanoparticle-based working electrode and Pt-coated counter electrode. The procedures for the synthesis of the TiO2 electrode and fabrication of the solar cells for the photovoltaic calculations were espoused from previous procedures.35,36 For the working electrode, the paste composed of a transparent TiO2 nanoparticle layer (20 nm diameter) was screen printed onto a fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) conducting glass substrate (transmission >85% in the visible, sheet resistance 10 cm2). The required thickness (8 m) and cell area (0.25 cm2) were obtained by repetitive screen printing. Subsequently, an additional scattering layer made of anatase TiO2 particles of 5 m thickness (400 nm in diameter) was screen printed onto the active transparent layer to improve the device performance. Then the screen printed double layer was sintered at 500 C for 1 h in the presence of oxygen. A Surfcom 1400A surface profiler (Tokyo Seimitsu Co. Ltd) was used to measure the thickness of the films. To coat the TiO2 film, the dye was dissolved in 1:1 (v/v) acetonitrile/tert-butyl alcohol at 0.2 mM concentration. To these dye dissolved solutions, the prepared TiO2 films were immersed and kept at 25 C for 15 h, so the dye was loaded onto the TiO2 films. The presence of water in the pores of TiO2 causes reduced injection efficiency of the dye. Therefore, to avoid rehydration of the TiO2 surface or capillary condensation of water vapours from ambient air inside the film, the dye was coated immediately onto the TiO2 after the high-temperature annealing. The electrode was dipped into the dye solution while it was still hot (80 C). After complete adsorption of the dye on the TiO2 surface, the photoelectrode was removed from the solution, and the non-adsorbed dye was removed by washing with acetonitrile under a stream of dry air or argon. The Pt counter electrode was prepared by spin coating of H2PtCl6 solution onto the FTO conducting glass substrate and heating to 385 C for 15 min. The dye-coated TiO2 film and thermally platinised FTO conducting glass counter electrode were separated by a Surlyn spacer (40 m thick) and sealed by heating the polymer frame at 100 C. Finally, the DSSC cells were assembled using the electrolyte solution consisting of a mixture of 0.6 M dimethylpropyl-imidazolium iodide, 0.05 M I2, 0.1 M LiI, and 0.10.5 M tert-butylpyridine in acetonitrile. A black metal mask with an area of 0.25 cm2 under AM 1.5 sunlight (100 mW cm2, WXS-155S-10: Wacom Denso Co. Japan) was used to measure the currentvoltage characteristics. The incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE) spectra were measured with a monochromatic incident light of 1 1016 photons per cm2 in direct current mode (CEP-2000BX, Bunko-Keiki).
CEM and IMVS measurements. The photovoltaic response induced by the modulated light was studied using intensity-modulated photo voltage spectroscopy (IMVS). A potentiostat (Solartron1287) equipped with a frequency response analyzer (Solartron1255B) at an open-circuit condition, based on a monochromatic illumination (420 nm) controlled by Lab view system, was used to measure the IMVS. The modulated light was driven with a 10% AC perturbation current superimposed on a DC current in a frequency range from 0.1 to 106 Hz. The charge extraction method (CEM) was performed with the same monochromatic light source. The solar cell was illuminated at an open-circuit condition for 5 s to attain a steady state and then the light source was switched off when the device simultaneously switched to a short-circuit condition to extract the charges generated at that light intensity.
Transient absorption spectroscopy. A laser flash photolysis spectrometer (model LP920, Edinburgh) was used. It was associated with a continuum Nd-YAG laser (Surelite; 10 Hz repetition rate; FWHM 5 ns). A Surelite optical parametric oscillator permits conversion of the second harmonic to a visible spectrum of 400820 nm. The respective dyes were excited at 445 nm. The excited state decay of the dye and the recovery of its fundamental state were monitored by the change in the absorbed continuous wave light. Satisfactory signal-to-noise ratio was obtained by averaging absorption spectra for 100200 laser shots.

Optical properties

The new porphyrins exhibit an intense Soret band at around 460 nm and less intense Q bands (550750 nm). The Soret band is an a1u()/eg(*) electronic transition, allocated to the second excited state (S2), and less intense Q bands coined from the a2u()/eg(*) electronic transition, attributed to the first excited state (S1). In LG15 and LG16 the absorption maxima of the Soret bands are found be at 459 and 457 nm, and the Q bands are found at 665 and 667 nm, respectively. The change of the auxiliary -spacer phenyl group (LG15) to the thiophene group (LG16) has not much effect on the absorption maxima. Whereas in the case of LG17 and LG18, more redshift in both the Soret and Q band was observed due to incorporation of the benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole (BDT) moiety as the -spacer between the porphyrin ring and acceptor group, which explains the panchromatic behaviour of the BDT moiety. Electrochemical and spectroelectrochemical studies

In order to evaluate the HOMOLUMO levels of the present sensitizers, we measured redox potentials by adopting a cyclic voltammetry technique in THF solvent using a 0.1 M solution of TBAPF6 as the supporting electrolyte with an Fc/Fc+ couple as the internal reference. Fig. 3 illustrates the cyclic voltammograms of all four sensitizers and the corresponding redox values are presented in Table 1. Each sensitizer undergoes two reversible one electron oxidations and one quasi-reversible reduction. From Fig. 3, it is suggested that all four sensitizers have an oxidation potential at 0.66 V vs. SCE irrespective of the auxiliary acceptor. The oxidation of these sensitizers are more positive than the I/I3 redox couple, which demonstrates effective regeneration of the sensitizer by the reduced redox couple. The excited state oxidation potential (EOX*) of the newly designed porphyrins was calculated using the equation EOX* = EOX E00, and was found to be more negative than the TiO2 conduction band (0.57 V vs. NHE), which demonstrates that the efficient injection of an electron from the excited dye to TiO2 is viable (Table 1).52 The active dye regeneration and electron injection on TiO2 in dye-sensitized systems are energetically favourable for the present dyes, which is evidently explained by an energy level diagram of the LG15LG18 dyes (Fig. 4). The favourable band gap of LG17 and LG18 may efficiently inject an electron into the TiO2 conduction band and dye regeneration by taking an electron from the redox couple, which may favour high efficiency. The reduction potential of these porphyrin sensitizers was found to be 0.95 V vs. NHE (see ESI).

Theoretical studies

The structural, electronic, and optical properties of the dyes LG15LG18 were explained by Density Functional Theory (DFT) and Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) calculations and were performed with a basic functional set at the B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level. The ground state optimized structures of LG15LG18 including donor and acceptor moieties separated with a porphyrin ring through an ethynyl--bridge are presented in Fig. S18 (ESI). From these optimized structures, the three phenyl rings in the donor moiety give non-planarity to all of the porphyrin dyes. This leads to minimization of aggregation of the macrocycle in solution. Except for LG15, all of the dyes are non-planar in nature, due to incorporation of the heteroatomic thiophene moiety (LG16), and tethering of the BDT moiety between the porphyrin ring and phenyl carboxylic acid (LG17), and heteroatomiccyanoarylic acid (LG18).

The investigation of the HOMO and LUMO position and the electronic properties of the four porphyrin dyes were explained by using the B3LYP/6-31(d,p) method in THF solvent. Fig. 6 shows frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) of the LG15LG18 dyes. The position of HOMO and LUMO in the present dyes explains the intramolecular charge separation by the electron transfer from donor to acceptor through the porphyrin framework. In LG15 and LG16, the electron population distribution of HOMO and LUMO is occupied partially on the donor, porphyrin and acceptor moieties and in LG17 and LG18 HOMO is occupied partially on the donor, porphyrin and acceptor moieties, and LUMO is occupied in the porphyrin macrocycle and acceptor moiety. For HOMO1 and LUMO+1 the electron density is mainly on the porphyrin macrocycle in the case of LG15 and LG16, and in LG17 HOMO1 it is present on the porphyrin ring only, but for LUMO+1 it is partially present on the porphyrin ring and more in the auxiliary acceptor. In HOMO2 it is distributed mainly on the donor moiety, and in LUMO+2 it is located mainly on the acceptor group in the case of LG15 and LG16, but in LG17 it is fully present on the porphyrin ring, and in LG18 it is partially on the porphyrin and acceptor moieties. It is theoretically feasible that the electron transfer processes from the donor to acceptor group through the porphyrin macrocycle followed by the electron injection into the conduction band of TiO2. LG15 > LG16 > LG17 > LG18 is the order for the HOMOLUMO gaps of these sensitizers. In addition to this, the TDDFT studies of these dyes were calculated by using B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) for better understanding of the excited-state transitions with the framework of the polarizable continuum model (PCM) in tetrahydrofuran (THF) solvent with the M06-2X function. The experimental values are in good agreement with the obtained theoretical values. Singlet excited state properties of the dyes, like excitation energies, oscillator strengths, and theoretical absorbance, are listed in Table S2 (ESI).

Device performance

All of the devices were fabricated by using standard mesoporous 8 m thick TiO2 films and an acetonitrile based redox electrolyte (composed of 0.6 M 1,2-dimethyl-3-propylimidazolium iodide (DMPII), 0.05 M I2, 0.1 M LiI, and 0.10.5 M 4-tert-butylpyridine (TBP)) (Fig. S20 and S21, ESI). The meticulous fabrication method was described in our earlier studies.22,35,36Fig. 7a depicts the photocurrent action spectra for LG15LG18. The wavelengths of LG15 and LG16 showed two absorption maxima at 460 nm and 660 nm, which indicates the Soret and Q band absorbances. The wavelength at 460 nm showed an IPCE of 81% in LG15 and 68% in LG16 (Table 2). In the case of LG17 and LG18, the photocurrent action spectra were found to be at wavelengths of 500 nm and 700 nm with broad spectral response and both the Soret and Q bands were found to be merged. This is due to the integration of a BDT auxiliary group between the porphyrin ring and the anchoring group in LG17 and LG18 and the IPCE is at 80% and 82%, respectively (Table 2). These broad photocurrent action spectra may lead to a substantial increase in PCE. The onset wavelengths were red-shifted and extended up to 950 nm in the case of LG17 and LG18 and those of LG15 and LG16 were extended up to 800 nm. Fig. 7b describes the photocurrentvoltage (JV) curves of LG15LG18 based DSSCs measured at simulated AM 1.5G irradiation (100 mW cm2) in combination with a liquid electrolyte. The cell performance parameters such as open-circuit potential (VOC), short-circuit current density (JSC), fill factor (FF), and energy conversion efficiency () are summarized in Table 2. Among all the fabricated DSSCs, the phenyl -spaced dye (LG15, JSC = 12.35 mA cm2) showed enhanced JSC when compared to the thiophene -spaced dye (LG16, JSC = 8.40 mA cm2) and it may lead to the improved PCE in LG15 ( = 6.25%) compared to LG16 ( = 4.21%). This might be due to poorer electron injection from the excited state of LG16 than LG15 to the TiO2 conduction band, as this is evident in the IPCE spectra as well as in recombination. On the other hand, in the case of LG17 and LG18, the introduction of the 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BDT) group between the porphyrin macrocycle and anchoring group resulted in a PCE of 10.11% in LG17 and 10.51% in LG18. The greater efficiency in LG18 compared to LG17 is due to the incorporation of the powerful electron withdrawing cyanoacrylic acid group, which leads to improved cell performance parameters in LG18 (JSC = 21.43 mA cm2, VOC = 0.68 V, = 10.51%). Thus, the order of power conversion efficiencies (PCE) is found to be LG18 > LG17 > LG15 > LG16. From these results one can deduce that efficient porphyrin sensitizers can be designed by combining highly effective non-planar donors with -conjugators having heteroatoms through a significant auxiliary acceptor coupling with a cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group.

Charge extraction studies and IMVS studies

In order to further understand the effect of VOC with divergence in molecular structure of the newly designed porphyrin dye-based DSSCs, we adopted a charge extraction method (CEM) first to measure the relative conduction band position of TiO2. Fig. 9 exemplifies VOC as a function of electron density for DSSCs fabricated with the newly designed porphyrin sensitizers. Fig. 9 suggests that VOC linearly increases as a function of electron density for all DSSC devices. This result indicates that the dyes contribution to the band edge shift is similar as the plots for all dyes overlap with each other, in spite of having various auxiliary acceptors between the porphyrin macrocycle and anchoring group. Consequently, the VOC is more likely dependent on charge recombination reactions rather than the molecular structures of the sensitizers. Accordingly, VOC for all DSSCs having LG15LG18 dyes should be attributed to the extent of charge recombination, which is ultimately related to the electron lifetime () in TiO2. We adopted intensity-modulated photovoltage spectroscopy to measure . Fig. 10 shows the electron lifetime () as a function of VOC. At a certain electron density, follows the following order: LG15 > LG16 > LG17 > LG18, which is in agreement with the order of decreasing VOC observed in the DSSCs. It can be clearly seen that LG15 and LG16 with small -spacers showed a longer electron lifetime than LG17 and LG18 with larger -spacers. The longer lifetimes observed for the LG15 and LG16 cells compared to the lifetimes observed for those of LG17 and LG18 suggest that both LG15 and LG16 may form a compact dye layer on the TiO2 surface, which reduces the concentration of I3 in the vicinity of the TiO2 surface, and thus suppresses the recombination of electrons in the TiO2 film with I3.

Transient absorption study

DSSC interfacial kinetics, including charge injection from the * orbitals of the anchoring group to the titanium 3d-orbital dye regeneration, etc., is one of the crucial steps for the DSSC performances.5557 For this, a good overlap of these orbitals is mandatory. We adopted nanosecond laser flash photolysis spectroscopy to investigate and compare the transient kinetics between dye regeneration kinetics by the electrolyte, among the dyes on one side and their respective DSSC performance parameters on another side. Nanosecond laser flash photolysis, considered as an ideal tool for investigating DSSC interfacial kinetics in a nano-to-microsecond time range, is seen as a complementary tool with the IMVS method (used above) which measures the decay lifetime of injected electron recombination with I3 in a millisecond time range and above. Conclusions

We have designed and synthesised four novel pushpull porphyrin dyes with triphenylimidazole as the donor, a porphyrin macrocycle as the -spacer and either carboxylic acid or cyanoacrylic acid as the anchoring group. They were systematically investigated as sensitizers for DSSCs. The tuning of the HOMOLUMO band gap leads to suitable absorption and electrochemical properties by varying the auxiliary acceptor groups. The incorporation of the 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BTD) moiety between the porphyrin core and anchoring group (LG17 and LG18) showed extended -conjugation which alters the electronic properties. As a result the valley present between the Soret and Q band is filled in the IPCE and the onset of wavelength extended to 950 nm, which eliminates the use of co-sensitization. The introduction of an auxiliary acceptor with 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BTD), thiophene moiety and cyanoacrylic acid results in great device performance, i.e., IPCE of 82% at both 492 and 700 nm, open-circuit voltage VOC of 0.68 V, short-circuit current density JSC of 21.43 mA cm2, fill factor of 0.71 and a power conversion efficiency of 10.51% under one sun illumination (AM 1.5G, 100 mW cm2). The thermal studies indicate that these dyes were stable up to 150 C and suitable for rooftop applications. Finally, these results provide a fruitful base for further investigation, to achieve a PCE greater than 13% without using a co-sensitizer.
