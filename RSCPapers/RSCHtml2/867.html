Abstract

Considerable interest is raised by organic materials owing to their exceptional performance in electronic and optoelectronic applications. Among these, electrochromic materials (EC) that can be switched between a distinct color and a bleached state exhibiting high contrast, multicolor and improved long-term stability are attractive in the fabrication of electrochromic devices (ECDs). Ionic materials, in particular, have received persistent attention owing to their tunable optical and electronic properties. 4,4′-Bipyridinium salts, commonly called viologens (V2+), are a well-recognized class of electrochromic materials that exhibit three reversible redox states, namely, V2+ (dication, pale yellow colored/colorless) ↔ V+˙ (radical cation, violet/blue/green) ↔ V0 (neutral, colorless). The electrochromic properties of these materials can be modulated by varying the nitrogen substituents on the pyridyl ‘N’; also, besides this, varying the counter ions with specific functionalities has been shown to enhance the electrochromic behavior, such as switching time, cycling stability and device performance. Although ECDs based on viologens are well regarded for their low operational voltages, they exhibit certain disadvantages such as low cycle life and poor efficiency of the device in the long term. Extensive efforts have been made to fine tune the EC properties of viologens, either through alteration or by adding suitable electrochromic counter electrode materials, which includes the incorporation of conducting polymers in the device set-up and/or the addition of complementary redox species. Optimization of the device parameters has shown that the addition of such external agents has a positive effect on the overall device performance. This review describes recent developments in the synthesis of viologen-based electrochromes with co-redox species and their ECD performance.

1. Introduction

Materials that tend to change their color reversibly upon electrochemical stimulus are called electrochromic materials and this phenomenon is called electrochromism.1,2 These materials can be utilized in electrochromic devices (ECDs) that act as energy-saving smart windows, anti-glare rear-view mirrors or low power consumption displays.3,4 Electrochromic (EC) materials and their related applications have been investigated extensively.5–7 Very recently, EC materials have been employed for electrochromic supercapacitors (ECSs) and ECSs are emerging applications of ECDs.8,9 These materials can be broadly classified into two main categories, namely, inorganic and organic materials. Inorganic materials include transition metals and metal oxides, Prussian blue systems, and lanthanide coordination complexes, etc.4,10–12 Organic materials include viologen derivatives (1,1′-dialkyl 4,4′-bipyridinium dications),13–17 TTF (tetrathiafulvalene) derivatives,18,19 TCNQ (tetracyanoquinodimethane) derivatives,20,21 quinones,22 conducting polymers such as polythiophene (PTh),23,24 polyaniline (PANI),25,26 polypyrrole (PPy),27,28etc.29,30 The most important property of these materials is that they should be chemically/electrochemically stable under applied electrical potential and display color changes between their oxidized/reduced states at much lower potentials.14,31 Furthermore, the design of the chemical structure of EC materials plays a crucial role in determining their light absorption ability at different wavelengths, making them good candidates for low power chromic applications.15 Among these classes of material, the electrochromic properties of viologen have been studied extensively owing to their low driving voltage for visual color changes (exhibiting three primary colors), high contrast, switchable electrochromic property and easy cell construction.16 The characteristic ionic states of viologen salts can be classified as the dication (V2+), the radical cation (V+˙) and the neutral state (V0).32–34 The one electron-reduced state of viologen (V+˙) imparts an intensely blue color and exhibits intramolecular optical charge-transfer complexation with high molar absorption coefficient values.35 Moreover, the spectral and electrochemical response of viologen36 mainly depends on the type of organic groups (alkyl, benzyl, and aryl)35 at the pyridyl nitrogen, which has led to a plethora of synthetic efforts towards the synthesis of new viologens with different functional groups.37 The success of any electrochromic material in an ECD largely relies on its long-term stability, i.e., the optical intensity should be stable over a long period of time, which is usually measured as cycling stability (the color of the EC material should not fade over time during on and off cycles). However, some of the side reactions/processes during cycling, such as comproportionation,38–40 dimerization,41,42 and recrystallization would destroy the reversibility of viologens, giving rise to poor stability of the viologen-based ECDs.

Materials such as transparent conducting oxides (TCOs), indium-doped tin oxide (ITO), and fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) are the most significant substrate materials that are frequently employed in ECDs. In particular, ITO conducting glasses are commonly used as electrodes in ECDs. However, certain drawbacks of ITO, such as being expensive, fragile, sensitive to corrosion, and having a high refractive index, restricts their ECD applications. These drawbacks pave the way to choose alternative conductive materials like TCOs, FTO, etc., and these materials are in the form of conducting polymers, nanomaterials and/or thin coatings of metals. Recently, high-performance, transparent, spray-coated flexible electrodes based on Ag nanowire and poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) were fabricated and successfully applied to viologen-based ECDs.43 In recent times, a wide range of such materials have been investigated as alternate electrode materials in the field of electrochromic applications.44 This review article will focus on the latest developments on viologen-based ECDs. A detailed discussion concerning the synthesis of appropriate viologen electrochromes and fabrication of viologen electrochromic gels for ECDs is discussed. To provide a better understanding, the discussions are divided into several sub-sections, i.e., synthesis of EC materials and discussion of the ECDs based on the nature of the materials (organic, inorganic, polymers, ionic liquids, and carbon materials (graphene/graphene oxide)) used for the construction.

3. Electrochromic device fabrication

An ECD entails an electrochromic material that undergoes visual color changes in a persistent but reversible manner when subjected to a redox reaction.58 The color intensity of EC materials is mainly determined by the passage of charge through the electrode.59 Further, the reversible redox process enables the molecule to switch between different colored states based on the redox states of the EC material. A few important resources are needed for ECDs construction. They are: (i) redox-active material; (ii) conductive electrolyte; (iii) conducting electrodes; and (iv) separator or spacer. The conductive electrolyte is typically separated by an electrochromic film/gel and a conductive electrode (ITO, FTO). The contact between the redox-active material and the electrode must be perfect in order to initiate and effective electron-transfer process. The electrochromic performance of any device is mainly centred on the selection of the electrolyte and device fabrication methods. The general operating modes which could be employed in ECDs are transmittance and reflectance. In transmittance mode, conductive electrodes are transparent and allow the light to permeate through the device. In addition, transparent and reflective counter electrodes are employed in reflectance mode, in which reflected light enters into the device, and the smart window is an example of a transparent ECD. 4. Viologen-based organic composite

Organic electrochromic materials are highly conjugated systems with lower band gap values (0.5 to 3.0 eV). These materials possess numerous advantages compared to inorganic materials, such as multiple colorations, reasonable stability, etc. However, these rigid aromatic systems exhibit reduced solubility in most solvents, which reduces their processability. These drawbacks can be avoided by the proper selection of suitable precursors or by incorporation of solubilizing substituents into the aromatic scaffolds.

Reus et al. reported the physical and electrochromic properties of π-extended phosphaviologens.55N,N′-Diarylated phosphaviologens are appropriate electron acceptor materials owing to their lower lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels compared with the parent dialkyl viologens (0.3 eV) and phosphaviologens (Scheme 7). The lowest energy gap of thienyl-substituted phosphaviologen displayed excellent electrochromism by switching between yellow and purple colors (Fig. 2). The thienyl-substituted phosphaviologen showed excellent electrochromic behavior in the visible wavelength at an operative potential range of 0.1–0.4 V. Moreover, the fully conjugated planar structure and the presence of donor moieties on the terminus enhanced the electrochromic behavior of the thienyl-phosphaviologen derivatives.

Viologen (1-(9-hexyl-9H-carbazole)-1′-(propylphosphonic acid)-4,4′-bipyridinium dichloride) and triphenylamine ((4-(diphenylamino)phenyl)methylphosphonic acid)-based electrochromic materials were synthesized and used as cathodic and anodic electrochromic materials, respectively.60 Both these materials were anchored onto TiO2 electrodes using phosphonate linkers via chemisorption. Propylene carbonate, ethylene carbonate, and LiPF6 mixture were used as the electrolyte. An ECD constructed using these materials showed excellent electrochromic switching with high speed (<1 s), good electrochromic memory (15 h), and high transmittance change (63% at 670 nm) with a long lifetime of over 100000 switching cycles.

The viologen-based EC material was prepared by covalently linking viologen with a triphenylamine scaffold (TPA-Vio),56 where the viologen moiety acts as a charge-trapping layer which significantly reduces the driving voltage and switching time of the ECD compared with an ECD with TPA alone that was investigated separately. The viologen–TPA scaffold was used as an ambipolar EC material. Combination of the redox potentials of the anodic (TPA) and cathodic (viologen) part were shown to complement each other, resulting in an improved switching response, and enhanced the overall electrochemical stability as well as electrochromic performance of the device. Moreover, the ECD showed color transition from colorless to deep blue color as a result of the combination of TPA and viologen.

5. Viologen-based inorganic composite

Viologen–inorganic composites are often synthesized as the hybrid EC material, which imparts better coloration and exhibits long-term stability. Commonly used inorganic electrochromic materials are WO3, NiO, TiO2, V2O5 and Prussian blue. In particular, coloration/bleaching of these materials is mainly based on insertion/extraction of ions into or out of the EC materials.61 Furthermore, these hybrid materials improve the performance of viologen-based ECDs. In this segment, electrochromic properties of recently developed viologen–inorganic composites are discussed in detail.

Fan et al. used uniformly dispersed Prussian blue (wPB) in water as an electrochromic ink and spray-coated the ink on an ITO surface.62 This electrode was used as an anodic coloring electrode, while the cathodic coloring electrode was composed of a poly(butyl viologen) (PBV) thin film. Succinonitrile with 0.1 M potassium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide and SiO2 nanoparticles were used as the solid electrolyte. The resultant thin film exhibits high color contrast from blue violet to transparent. Good electrochromic performance of the device was achieved with high coloration efficiency, short switching time (10 s for bleaching and darkening), and long-term stability (up to 1000 cycles). Furthermore, they developed another ECD consisting of vinylbenzyl viologen (VBV) as a cathodic coloring material complementing the Prussian blue anodic coloring material.63 The device used ferrocene (Fc) as a redox mediator which provides an alternate path to electron transfer; as a result the ECD made of VBV–Fc displayed multicolor, in particular a turquoise color. Excellent electrochromic performance of the device was achieved with very short switching time (1.32 and 2.13 s) and good long-term stability (up to 10000 cycles). Sydam et al. employed a layer-by-layer technique to prepare a PBV and poly(peroxotungstate) assembly as a cathodic coloring material (Fig. 3).64 The anodic coloring unit contained ruthenium purple thin film, which was prepared by electrodeposition. The ECD showed good performance with short switching time (∼1 s) at a remarkably low operational voltage. In addition, the ECD showed high optical contrast (Fig. 4).

A methyl viologen-modified ZnO nanotube electrode was prepared for ECD fabrication by Wei and co-workers (Fig. 4).65 The electrode consists of an array of nanotubes (NTs)/nanorods (NRs) with large surface area, which allows the electrochromic methyl viologen molecules to absorb prominently.65 As a result of the high surface area, more viologen molecules are absorbed inside the ZnO nanotubes, resulting in a deep blue colour during switching. In addition, the porous structure of the ZnO nano arrays facilitated rapid electron transport, resulting in a fast EC response time and good durability.

A high-performance ECD was fabricated using slime-type EC system comprising of PVA (polyvinyl alcohol) and borax, with viologen as an EC material (Fig. 5).66 This ECD exhibits excellent wettability, transparency, high optical contrast, fast switching times (<5 s), and long-term stability (∼10000 cycles). Furthermore, the response time of the ECD was simply modulated by varying the concentrations of viologen and potassium ferro/ferricyanide salts.

The highly durable and long-term stable solution phase ECD consisting of ethyl viologen and Ti-doped V2O5 electrochromic materials was reported by Li et al.68 Electrochromic performance of the device showed good optical properties in the UV-visible region, with transmittance of 70% and 8% between bleached and colored states at a wavelength of 600 nm. Remarkable stability of the ECD was experienced up to 250000 cycles at 60 °C. Excellent stability of the device was achieved by proper selection of matching state and durable Ti–V2O5 film, where it was shown that Ti provided excellent stability to the distorted V2O5 structure. Besides this. The optical performance of the device, such as color change and switching speed, was modulated by varying the viologen concentration.

6. Viologen-based polymer composite

Polymers/conducting polymers, including polyaniline (PANI) and polythiophene (PTh), are widely explored for their electrochromic behavior. Several ECDs have been fabricated using PANI and PTh-based materials owing to their ease of processability and cost-effective factors.69 Besides these, selection of appropriate complementary redox species with conducting polymeric material plays an important role in the enhancement of the optical properties of EC materials like viologens. Several composites consisting of polymer and viologen were prepared and their EC behaviors evaluated. In this section, recently developed viologen–polymer composite-based ECDs will be discussed in detail.

To overcome the shortage of liquid electrolytes in ECDs, Ho and co-workers proposed the use of succinonitrile-based solid polymer electrolyte composed of HV (heptyl viologen, electrochromic material), TMPD (N,N,N′,N′-tetramethyl-p-phenylenediamine), PVdF-HFP (poly(vinylidene fluoride-co-hexafluoropropylene)), and ionic liquid (IL).70 The ionic conductivity of the solid polymer electrolyte varied with the concentration of ionic liquid, which served as a limiting factor for the redox reactions of the EC molecule. Best redox behavior of HV and TMPD was achieved with 5 wt% IL. The ECD exhibited a coloration efficiency value of 120.8 cm2 C−1 and excellent cycling stability over 2000 cycles of switching. A simple neutral-grey color ECD was fabricated in the combination of glass/TCO/EC gel/TCO/glass.50 The EC materials used were 1-ethyl-1′-(p-cyanophenyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium dibromide (Et-pCNVio) and 1-benzyl-1′-(p-cyanophenyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium dibromide (Bn-pCNVio) asymmetric viologens. The EC performance of asymmetric viologens and their symmetric counterparts were evaluated. Among these, (Et-pCNVio) offered excellent electrochromic performance with a grey color in very short switching time (≤15 s) and high cycling stability (∼15000 cycles). Better performance of the device was achieved by using a gel electrolyte, which is composed of 4% PVA, aqueous solution of viologen and a 1:1 mixture of potassium ferrocyanide and potassium ferricyanide and 4% borax solution.

An absorption-complementary method was used to fabricate an ECD consisting of electroactive polyamide-functionalized copolymers and heptyl viologen (HV).71 Electroactive polyamides with N,N,N′,N′,-tetraphenyl-p-phenylenediamine (TPPA) and tetraphenylbenzidine substituents were used. Use of heptyl viologen as a charge-trapping layer significantly reduced the working voltage and improved the overall performance of the ECD. Interestingly, the ECD displayed three different colors (green, blue, and red) while tuning the redox states and these colors combined into a black color.

In an independent study, Beneduci and co-workers evaluated the role of oxygen impurities on the electrochromic response of viologen-based thermoplastic polyacrylate matrix.72 The response time of these plastic ECDs was dependent on the oxygen content, which is shown to affect the electrochromic kinetics in a complex way. The electron-transfer process on the electrochromic film is controlled by the diffusion of reactive species, including viologen and oxygen. The oxygen in the inflexible, stiffer electrochromic film enhances the rate of optical responses, but a contrary response was observed in the flexible film. Reports described in the study by Beneduci and co-workers could improve the performance of viologen-based ECDs, provided the factors addressed in the study are crucially considered. Frisbie and co-workers used ion gels comprising polystyrene-block-poly(methylmethacrylate)-block-polystyrene, 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide, viologen, and ferrocene molecules.73 The EC gel was coated between two ITO glass slides, or over flexible plastic sheets, to fabricate an ECD.

PVA–borax-based electrolyte systems were used to develop easy-to-make viologen-based multichromic devices (Fig. 7).74 Moreover, combination of two different viologen electrochromes with different redox potentials offered five well-defined colorations, including colorless, green, pink-violet, orange, and purple, in a single EC layer sandwiched between two electrodes. In addition, the potential of this simple multi-ECD reached a low transmission in most of the visible wavelength range, which could effectively block the whole visible radiation in smart windows, paving the way for applications in the fields of display technologies, smart windows, climate-adaptive building shells, etc.

Thermal-cured ECDs were assembled using VBV (vinylbenzyl viologen) by Kao et al.15 The presence of bulky substituents of VBV/PMMA (poly(methylmethacrylate)) in the cross-linked polymer reduces the possibility of agglomeration of viologens and viologen dimers, which is known to limit the cycling stability of ECDs. Furthermore, the dual role of VBV as an electrochromic material as well as a cross-linker offered excellent optical performances in ECDs, with easier fabrication steps. The combined VBV/PV (phenyl viologen)-based ECD also exhibited high transmittance change (ΔT) with good cycling stability. Ho and co-workers prepared UV-crosslinked ethoxylated trimethylolpropane triacrylate (ETPTA) and immobilized heptyl viologen (HV) and phenyl viologen (PV) onto it.75 The studies showed that PV exhibited attractive electrochromism; however, rapid aggregation occurred in liquid electrolyte owing to strong π–π stacking, which was subsequently overcome by employing a solid electrolyte. The two-cell configuration consisted of an ITO electrode, multicolor EC material (PV + HV), solid electrolyte (succinonitrile)-mediator (15 wt% ETPTA/FcMeOH), and ITO electrode. The ECD showed green, marine and grey-blue from transparent, with a fast switching time. Combination of two different viologen salts with different redox potentials and colors opens up a new avenue for color-tuning.

6.1. Viologen-based ionic liquids (ILs)/polyionic liquids (PILs)

Ionic liquids (ILs) play a dual role as ions and solvents, with exceptional properties such as high boiling point, wide operating potential window, non-flammability, good ionic conductivity, and compatibility with organic solvents.76,77 In addition, polymeric ionic liquids (PILs) consist of the properties of both polymer and ionic liquids.78 Moreover, the presence of PILs affords additional ions to the electrolyte, which enriches the ionic conductivity, and the PIL can immobilize electroactive species like viologen in the electrolyte solution, which slows down both the surface and recombination reactions.37 These properties may improve the electrochromic performance of PIL–viologen-based ECDs. Some of the recently published work utilizing this concept will be discussed in detail in this section.

Viologen-based ionic salts comprising mono- and di-alkyl bipyridinium cations, with anions such as I−, Br−, NTf2− were synthesized and used as stand-in electrochromic ionic liquids (ILs).35 Thermal and physico-electrochemical properties of these ILs were modified by varying the substituents so as to improve their optoelectronic performance. Among various alkyl-substituted viologens used in the study, 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide [EMIM][NTf2], was found to exhibit stable and efficient electrochromic properties. Although no ECD was fabricated, electrochromic performance of the viologen IL was briefly evaluated in this study. Similarly, a prototype ECD cell was constructed using bipolar viologen–ferrocene ([FcC11VC1][TFSI]2) redox-active ionic liquid (RAIL),79 where the non-volatile RAIL exhibited high durability up to 10000 potential cycles without degradation. In addition, use of a RAIL avoids the use of other additives such as solvents, supporting electrolytes, and/or any sacrificial agents displaying the multiple role of such ILs. Perhaps, such RAIL-based ILs will be interesting for EC applications provided they exhibit high durability, long-term stability, and high contrast at low operational voltages. Oxo-bipyridinium salts possessing symmetric and non-symmetric disubstituents on bipyridinium, with different anionic counterparts (I−, [NTf2]− and [AOT]− (docusate anion)), were synthesized for electrochromic applications.13 The existence of ether linkages in the bipyridinium scaffolds fine-tunes their physical properties, specifically, lowering the melting point and Tg (glass transition temperature). Thus, these salts are considered as ionic liquids and these electrochromic materials exhibit pale yellow and blue color at a potential cycle of −2.0 to 0.0 V. As a result, ECDs were fabricated using these IL-based electrochromic materials between two PET (polyethylene terephthalate)–ITO layers to yield an efficient and reversible ECD.

Ho and co-workers investigated the EC properties of heptyl, octyl and nonyl viologens (as BF4 salts).37 An ECD was fabricated using Fc/V with UV-cured polymer electrolyte consisting of polymer ionic liquid (poly(1-allyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide)) and ETPTA (ethoxylated trimethylolpropane triacrylate). Addition of a PIL substantially decreased the operating potential and improved the long-term stability, with increased coloration efficiency. Among various concentrations, 20 wt% PIL addition was shown to display better EC performance.

An ECD with self-bleaching ability comprising electroactive species such as ethyl viologen and ferrocene-based IL was reported by Rochefort and co-workers (Fig. 8).80 It has been demonstrated that O2 is least soluble in [BMIM][NTf2] (BMIM, 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium) and hence the use of this IL for ECD fabrication offers stability towards atmospheric oxygen and water, even during electrochemical oxidation and reduction. As a result, excellent optical properties and high stability in open atmosphere were obtained when an ECD was fabricated using this system. The ionic liquid electrolyte ([BMIM][NTf2]) makes the electroactive species insensible to atmosphere because of the low solubility of O2 in this solvent system.81 On the other hand, the high midpoint potential (1.06 V) difference between the two redox couples, such as [Fc(II)NTf]/[Fc(III)NTf] and [EV(II)/EV(I)] vs. Fc/Fc+ in the IL electrolyte enhances the homogenous electron transfer, which is crucial for the self-bleaching process.

Ion gel-based ECDs were fabricated by mixing appropriate viologen electrochrome in the gel consisting of P(VDF-co-HFP) and [BMI][NTf2] using the “cut-and-stick” approach (Fig. 9).82 The optoelectronic properties of EC gels were then modulated either by controlling the chemical equilibrium between the viologen radical cation and radical dimer (V+˙ ↔ (V+˙)2) or by modifying the chemical structure of the EC viologens with suitable substituents, such as –CN, –CF3 and heptyl. As a result, a patterned, multicolored, flexible ECD on plastic was fabricated that is operational at lower potentials.

Dual-responsive (thermo and electro) polyionic liquid (PIL) was synthesized by the co-polymerization of N-isopropylacrylamide (NIPAM) with diallyl viologen (DAV).54DAV was used both as a cross-linking agent and EC material and the PIL was used as an electrolyte. A smart window was designed using polyionic liquid (PIL) by Chen et al. The low critical solution temperature of the electrolytes (PIL) can be tuned by changing the weight ratio of IL/H2O or molar ratio of monomeric substances such as N-isopropylacrylamide (NIPAM) and 3-butyl-1-vinyl-imidazolium bromide ([BVIm][Br]). The dual-responsive diallyl viologen (DAV) can perform simultaneously at thermal and electrical stimulus, which is incorporated into the polymer chain. The performance of the device was evaluated for only 100 cycles; however, the design of a dual-responsive ECD based on thermal and electrical stimulus seems very interesting for the areas of smart windows, temperature sensors, etc., provided these materials render excellent performance and long-term stability.

6.2. Various electrolytes and their impact in viologen-based ECDs

In a different approach, Cruz et al. utilized deep eutectic solvents (DESs) as green electrolytes (choline or lithium chloride with ethylene glycol and glycerol) for electrochromic applications, and tested the efficiency of these low-cost electrolytes with two different viologen derivatives, namely, 1,1′-(dimethyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium dichloride and 1,1′-[di-(methoxyethoxyethane)]-4,4′-bipyridinium diiodide as electrochromes.33 However, a major setback in this system is the application of high operational voltage. Apart from this, DESs proved to be a green and low-cost electrolyte with reduced viscosity compared with IL-based electrolytes showing excellent electrochromic switching between the colored to bleached states, and vice versa.

Ion gel-based ECDs were assembled by Moon and co-workers (Fig. 10).83 Ion gel patterns were printed using electrostatic-force-assisted dispensing printing, which has potential applications in printed electronics. Favorable electrostatic interactions between EC gel [(dimethyl ferrocene) comprising of copolymers (poly(vinylidene fluoride-co-hexafluoropropylene)) and RTIL (1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide)] and substrate (diethyl viologen) induce the performance of the ECD, together with high coloration efficiency and transmittance contrast (82%) between the bleached to colored states. Furthermore, ECDs based on these ion gels were used to test the efficiency of diheptyl viologen (DHV) and monoheptyl viologen (MHV) as electrochromes.84 MHV displayed a magenta color upon application of voltage, whereas DHV displayed a blue color, while a conventional dialkyl viologen/monoalkyl viologen typically shows a blue/violet colored state during cycling. In contrast, when both MHV and DHV are combined, the device displayed different optical properties by displaying yellow and blue to maroon color with respect to the applied potentials.

Very recently, Moon and co-workers fabricated an ECD based on ion gels using the same viologen electrochromes and device fabrication approach, but instead of RTIL, EMI-Tf2 (1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide) IL was used.85 A systematic investigation showed that, the blue-colored state was obtained at low concentrations of the DHV electrochrome; however, at high concentration, a sequential transition from blue to magenta occurred. Irrespective of the concentration, MHV showed only a magenta-colored state. In relation to DHV, MHV showed high transmittance contrast, high color, and good cycling stability.

Chen et al. improved the repetitive stability of electrochromic cells comprising viologen derivatives as electrochromes by adding ferrocene, an electron donor, to the electrolyte solution.16 Ferrocene acted as a mediator and gave better switching properties. In the case of a multiviologen EC cell, with an increase in cathodic and anodic potential, the transmittance decreased and increased, respectively. These results are ascribed to the sharing of electrons between viologen molecules in the mixed viologen cell, which limits their electrochromic properties. This problem was tackled by preparing various EC cells by controlling viologens with 0.09 M of Fc and modifying the viologen molecules. Studies involving the long-term stability of viologen ECDs with ferrocene mediators would provide a better insight into such a system, as viologen-based ECDs suffer from internal charge-transfer complex formation (quenching), which limits their ECD performance.

In a typical study, gel-switchable ECDs comprising viologen electrochromes (alkylbipyridinium and oxo-bipyridinium) with iodide and NTf2 salts were developed.86 It is noteworthy that the anion has some influence on the color contrast and electrochromic reversibility, in addition to the substituent on the viologen. Three switching colors (yellow, blue, and red [alkylbipyridinium]/violet[oxo-bipyridinium]) were obtained for the viologen salt with iodide as a counter anion, whereas the same viologen salt with NTf2 as a counter anion gave only two reversible colors (transparent and blue), which is attributed to dimer formation in the case of the iodide salt. The NTf2 salt showed better optical performance in short transition time compared with the iodide salt, which suffers from intermolecular charge-transfer complexation (dimer formation). An eco-friendly gelatin-based electrolyte containing iodide/triiodide redox mediator was fabricated by Rougier and co-workers.87 The gel electrolyte was composed of LiI in 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide (BMII) IL and biodegradable gelatin. A nano TiO2–viologen-based ECD was fabricated. Fast switching times with high cycling stability of up to 20000 cycles were achieved. Moreover, the gel electrolyte exhibited high ionic conductivity, transmittance, and electrochemical stability. Oh et al. synthesized cyanophenyl viologen (CNPhV2+) and 3-fluoro-4-(trifluoromethyl)phenyl viologen (TFMFPhV2+) derivatives and compared their EC performance in an ion gel consisting of PVDF-co-HFP and 1-ethyl-3-methylimidazolium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide (EMI-TFSI) (Fig. 11).88 TFMFPhV2+ showed excellent transmittance contrast, coloration efficiency, cycling stability, and faster switching times compared with CNPhV2+. Despite the difference in their performance, both these compounds exhibited low operational potential compared with other viologen-based ECDs.

Su et al. reported the fabrication of a sustainable and pro-environmental ECD made up of a composite film comprising of cellulose nanocrystals (CNCs) as biological matrix, viologen as electrochromic material, and KCl.89 Alkyl- and benzyl-substituted viologens were used as EC materials. Among these, benzyl-substituted viologen-based ECDs showed good electrochromic performances at low operational voltage, whereas methyl viologen showed better switching in a short time compared with the ethyl viologen-based ECDs. Very recently, Alesanco et al. reported the fabrication of an ECD containing alkyl- and aryl-substituted symmetric viologens with phosphonate tails that were anchored onto nano-TiO2.90 The device was constructed using a PVA–borax gel electrolyte, which enhanced the neutral coloration. In an another study, the authors utilized 1-alkyl-1′-aryl asymmetric viologens with phosphonate tails as EC materials with a similar set-up and achieved a competitive device performance with excellent stability over 10000 cycles.91

7. Viologen-based graphene/graphene oxide composite

Graphene/graphene oxide work as an active material in EC applications due to their unusual physical properties, such as optical transparency, large theoretical specific surface area, and high charge carrier mobility at room temperature. Thus, they are employed as an electrode substrate due to their low sheet resistance, high transparency, and flexibility. Composites of graphene/graphene oxide were made with chromophores like viologens to enhance the electrochromic properties of viologens. This part of the review will focus on recent developments on graphene/graphene oxide–viologen composites and their ECD applications.

Hwang et al. assembled a prototype electrolyte-free ECD using methyl viologen–graphene quantum dot (MV2+–GQD) nanocomposites.92 The nanocomposite-based ECD exhibited good thermal stability, high durability and displayed a very stable switching performance. Strong electrostatic interactions between MV2+ and GQD were used for the construction of the ECD, in addition to electrostatic interactions; π–π and cation–π interactions also stabilized the nanocomposite, rendering a stable ECD performance (EC stability >3200 s) under mechanical and thermal stresses. This flexible device displayed a continuous reversible color change from colorless to purple at an applied potential range of −2.8 to 0.0 V (Fig. 12).

In a different approach, Ruiz and co-workers electrodeposited reduced graphene oxide (RGO) from graphene oxide (GO) solution on ITO-coated polyethylene terephthalate (ITO–PET) and used the flexible transparent electrode to fabricate an ECD using ethyl viologen as the EC material.44 Later, the ECD performance of the ITO–PET electrode and the RGO-coated ITO–PET electrodes were compared. In the case of the RGO-coated ITO–PET electrode, it was observed that, upon continuous cycling at large voltages, the RGO film is oxidized and dispersed in the EC mixture. As a result the device contained a mixture of GO and RGO. Consequently, the redox couple GO/RGO in the electrochromic solution played the role of electrocatalyst and assisted the color switching process of the ECDs, with high color contrast at relatively lower potentials compared to the electrode without the RGO coating. Gadgil et al. reported the synthesis of a polyviologen–reduced graphene oxide (PV–RGO) composite film via electrocodeposition method.93 During the electropolymerization of the CNP monomer, GO was concurrently reduced. Furthermore, it was shown that the insulating PV redox matrix was non-covalently wrapped by RGO sheets, which amplified the redox activity of PV, affording better conducting properties and improved EC performance. Invention of novel EC materials has of profound significance in the development of new electronic devices. Interestingly, these materials show reversible colors with respect to an external stimulus which could be applied potential or thermal energy, etc. Often, such materials has been applied in various fields such as smart windows, sensors, glare attenuation in automobile rear-view mirrors, and solar cells.79 Viologens are inevitable cathodic EC materials in the field of electro-optics and they impart different colors depending on their oxidation states and substituents at the “N” atoms.31

8. Conclusions and future prospects

In summary, simple and accessible methods for the preparation of different viologen electrochromes were discussed in detail. Although considerable efforts have been made to achieve high-performance viologen-based EC materials, ECD performance still faces some limitations owing to the agglomeration of viologen radical cations on the electrode surface, which enhances radical–radical dimerization and/or aerial oxidation of the radical cation species. Viologen-based ECDs are attractive, as viologens exhibit different redox states and each redox state displays a different color. In addition, the redox switching of viologens occurs at very low potentials, making them ideal candidates to act as EC materials. Viologens also show photoelectrochromic behavior. The selection of suitable electrochromic materials, such as viologen, with complementary redox species and conducting substrates/polymeric materials is essential to produce multicolored ECDs, which remains a challenging task. These additional components might enhance the performance of the ECD or impart additional colors in addition to the characteristic blue/violet/green of viologens. Several attempts have been made to synthesize multichromic viologens, however it was very difficult to achieve a wide range of colors just by varying the ‘N’ substituent alone. To improve device performance, hybrid organic/inorganic or polymer composites have been synthesized. To overcome the shortages of liquid electrolyte, gel/IL-based electrolytes have been successfully employed. Even polymer-based ILs have been synthesized and successfully employed as electrolytes in ECDs. Although IL-based electrolytes (ILs and PILs) have been successfully employed, their intrinsic viscosity, air/moisture sensitivity and high cost hamper them from a commercialization viewpoint. In some cases, even viologen-based ILs have been synthesized and used as EC materials and electrolytes, showing better ECD performance. Although such studies are preliminary, previous studies have shown that in such systems, viologen would suffer from aerial oxidation and dimer formation. Such problems were recently addressed by Oh et al. and Alesanco et al. through the installation of higher electronegative substituents and bulkier functional groups, respectively.88,91 Hence, in these cases, optimization of fabrication conditions would deliver an ideal ECD with excellent long-term stability. New materials, rational design, and advanced synthetic approaches will offer solutions to these challenges, such as multichromic materials with better switching capability and overall long-term stability at low operational potentials.
