Impact of various dopant elements on the properties of kesterite compounds for solar cell applications: a status review

Abstract

Earth-abundant and nontoxic kesterite compounds, such as Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS), Cu2ZnSnSe4 (CZTSe), and Cu2ZnSn(S,Se)4 (CZTSSe), have made tremendous progress, becoming a hot topic in optoelectronic materials research on power generation through solar energy capture. These compounds can be considered possible alternatives to conventional, toxic, and expensive absorber layers, such as crystalline silicon (c-Si), Cu(In,Ga)Se2 (CIGS), and CdTe. The p-type semiconductor kesterites (CZTS, CZTSe CZTSSe) are the most promising light absorbers for solar cell applications, reaching record efficiencies of up to 12.7% owing to their unique optical properties. These properties include a variable band gap energy ranging from 1.0 eV (Cu2ZnSnSe4) to 1.5 eV (Cu2ZnSnS4) and a large optical absorption coefficient of 104 cm−1, which make kesterite a feasible option for photovoltaic technologies scalable to terawatt levels. Furthermore, as kesterite absorbers do not contain any rare metals or toxic materials, one can expect their application to low-cost solar cells in the near future. This review aims to present an overview of the most recent research activity on state-of-the-art kesterite-based absorbers, including a brief introduction to kesterite compounds, deposition methods, and improved device performance through doping with alkali, germanium, and other metal elements either during kesterite synthesis or after growth as a post-deposition treatment. As the key component for improving the power conversion efficiency of the kesterite absorber, doping with alkali, germanium, and other metal elements has attracted much interest and is vital for delivering promising efficiencies. Furthermore, current knowledge on doping, its impact on structural, optical, and electrical properties, and the solar cell performance of kesterite compounds are summarized. Finally, important achievements in this field to date and further directions for improving the solar cell efficiency of kesterite-based absorbers have been summarized.

1. Introduction

The increasing global crises regarding energy shortages/fossil fuel depletion and carbon dioxide emissions are the two major challenges of the 21st century.1 These require researchers to develop clean, cheap, safe, and sustainable renewable energy technologies to provide long-term solutions to environmental problems for the development of human society. Presently, the total global power requirement is about 15 TW, with worldwide energy demand estimated to be 30 TW by 2050 and 45 TW by 2100, along with a predicted global temperature increase of 5 °C.2,3 To meet these demands, renewable energy resources are expected to play a critical role. Of available potential renewable energy resources, energy harvesting using solar photovoltaics (PV), which can directly transform sunlight into usable electricity, has attracted significant attention and is on the forefront among available resources.4–6 In 2014, 38.7 GW of PV was installed globally, which increased to 42.8 GW in 2015.7 The commercially available and technologically most important solar cell modules developed to date are based on silicon (Si), which has more than 80% PV share market8 and a record efficiency of 26.7% for crystalline silicon (c-Si).9 However, constructing highly efficient Si solar modules requires perfect single crystals, which contributes to an expensive manufacturing cost, especially regarding the cost of silicon wafers. In contrast, CIGS solar cell-based PV technology, comprising chalcogenide thin films, has achieved a 22.9% power conversion efficiency.10–14 However, such PV technology uses either rare elements, such as gallium, indium, and tellurium, or toxic elements, such as cadmium, which can result in costly and unsafe solar cells. Therefore, to compete with costly c-Si, CIGS, and toxic CdTe based solar cells, and harvest solar light effectively for the commercial production of high-performance PV technology, the fabrication of low-cost solar cell absorbers with high power conversion efficiencies is essential. As a replacement for expensive CIGS and CdTe, cheap and earth-abundant binary compounds, such as Fe2S, FeS2,15,16 Cu2SnS3,17,18 and Cu2O,19,20 have been proposed for solar cells application. However, the record efficiency of these compounds is below 10%, which is not satisfactory for practical usage.

In recent years, attention has turned toward earth-abundant kesterite (CZTS, CZTSe, and mixed chalcogenide CZTSSe) solar cells as low-cost and non-toxic PV technology, and is considered a possible replacement for expensive and conventional absorber layers, such as c-Si, CIGS, and CdTe solar cells.21–23 The p-type conductive CZTS or CZTSSe absorbers are similar materials to well-established GIGS solar cells.24–27 Therefore, a large amount of CIGS knowledge and experience is expected to be directly transferable to CZTSSe technology, which contains earth-abundant constituents, such as zinc and tin, rather than the rare and expensive gallium and indium in CIGS, making their potential commercialization cheaper and faster. Furthermore, the optical properties of CZTSSe, such as a band gap range of 1–1.5 eV, absorption coefficients greater than 104 cm−1,28,29 and excellent theoretical conversion efficiencies of up to 31%,30 suggest the possibility of large-area solar cell fabrication without significant module prices.31,32 Therefore, we can expect cheap thin-film solar cells (TFSC) in the near future. In comparison to first-generation solar cells (Si solar cells), second-generation TFSCs have advantages of low cost, easy processing methods, and better performance under diffused light conditions. Furthermore, the required absorber layer thickness in TFSCs to absorb all incident sunlight (1–2 μm) is much lower than that of Si solar cells (100–200 μm). Therefore, TFSCs have been effectively applied to photovoltaic technology.

Recently, several strategies have been developed to synthesize CZTS, CZTSe, and CZTSSe absorbers, including both vacuum33–35 and non-vacuum24,36–40 techniques, resulting in promising solar cell performance. Since the first report of CZTS (kesterite-type crystal structure) solar cells with a power conversion efficiency of 0.66%41,42 in thermally evaporated devices in 1996, materials growth optimization and hydrazine-processed CZTSSe solar cells have resulted in significant improvements and yielded a world-record light-to-electricity conversion efficiency of 12.7% using a CdS/In2S3 emitter.32 However, hazardous hydrazine has a toxic and explosive nature that might prevent its use in industrial applications, with more classical deposition methods required. As an alternative to highly toxic hydrazine, dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) is a solvent of choice for synthesizing CZTS absorber with a maximum efficiency of 11.2%.43–47 However, the record efficiencies of 12.7% reported by Mitzi and coworkers in 2014 for CZTSSe using the hydrazine approach32 and 13.80% reported by the KIER group in 2016 for a small-area (0.181 cm2) CZTSSe device prepared using a sputtering method48 are much lower than those of their CIGS (22.9%),13 c-Si (26.7%),9 and CdTe (21%)49 solar cell counterparts. The key limitation to the high efficiency of kesterite solar cells remains a deficit in the open circuit voltage, often expressed as VOC-deficit, which is often linked to structural disorder, which manifests as band tails and deep trap states, to limit device performance. Important strategies to reduce the high VOC-deficit of the kesterite absorber layer include the post-deposition or doping of alkali and other related metals, such as Na, K, Cs, Rb, Li, Mg, Ge, Sb, and Ag.50–66 This can aid crystallization and decrease elemental disorder, resulting in an improved PCE.

An enhanced VOC and fill factor has been observed in Na-doped CZTSe owing to an increase in the hole density and shift in the acceptor level close to the conduction band.67 Furthermore, Na incorporation into CZTSSe absorber significantly modified its electronic properties, with the possibility of tuning the doping levels and gradients to maximize the collection of photogenerated carriers in thin film CZTSSe solar cells.68 Repins et al. showed that a NaF layer is needed in addition to Na from the glass substrate to improve the photovoltaic parameters without affecting grain growth.69 Na doping lowers the thermal activation energy and pulls the Fermi level towards the valence band, resulting in a build-up of potential (VOC) in the structure.70 Furthermore, Na doping enhances the carrier concentration and grain size. Other alkali metals, such as Li, Rb, and K, have shown effective performance in CZTS TFSCs.58 Li-doping reduces deep defects and improves the VOC,61 Rb-doping improves the grain size and increases VOC, and K-doping suppresses Sn loss and enhances the JSC.53 Therefore, alkalis and other metals can help promote grain growth and increase the hole concentration, grain boundaries, and defect passivation, thereby enhancing the PCE of CZTSSe solar cells.

From this perspective, this review aims to describe current progress in state-of-the-art kesterite thin-film solar cells from the perspective of material properties and synthetic procedures (vacuum and non-vacuum). We have also reviewed changes in the electrical, optical, and structural properties of kesterite compounds after doping/post-deposition treatment with various light and heavy alkalis, and other metals, and the corresponding improvements in solar cell performance. Furthermore, key issues associated with kesterite thin-film solar cells have been identified, and potential solutions and future directions provided. By increasing the power conversion efficiency above 15% and maintaining a low processing cost using a non-hazardous synthesis approach, kesterite compounds could make promising solar cell absorbers for practical applications, not only by converting intermittent renewable solar energy, but also triggering PV power systems in the near future.

2. Architecture of kesterite solar cell devices

CZTS solar cells consist of stacked thin-film layers deposited on conductive substrates using the substrate/absorber/buffer/widow concept, as shown in Fig. 1. The main component of the solar cell is CZTS, a semiconductor material with a suitable bandgap, that harvests and converts solar light directly into usable electricity. Generally, CZTS absorbers are grown on metallic molybdenum (Mo) grown on soda lime glass substrates, which serve as a good back contact and exhibit high temperature stability, good adhesion to the CZTS absorber layer, and resistance to alloy formation with copper (Cu).71 Other conducting substrates, such as fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) or indium-doped tin oxide (ITO) can be used. The thickness of the p-type absorber CZTS layer is in the range of 1–1.5 μm. The absorber layer is deposited either by physical or chemical deposition to obtain maximum light absorption. The deposited absorber layer is annealed under a sulfur and selenium atmosphere to improve the crystallinity. KCN etching is then performed on the sulfurized/selenized absorber compound to remove any formed secondary phase (CuxS). Two n-type semiconductors comprising a chemical bath-deposited CdS layer (50 nm) followed by a sputter-deposited intrinsic i-ZnO layer (80–100 nm) are obtained on the CZTS layer. As a possible substitute for CdS, a more eco-friendly ZnS buffer layer can be applied. The thickness of the CdS layer is kept at a minimum (40–60 nm) to decrease series resistance (RS) in the device. For electrical front contact purposes, a layer of a transparent conductive oxide (TCO), such as Al–ZnO (AZO) or ITO, is deposited. The TCO should exhibit high transmittance and high electrical conductivity. Finally, for charge extraction, ohmic Ni/Al contacts are deposited on the TCO. Usually, metal electrodes exhibiting a higher metallic work function than the semiconductor (Φm > Φs) are used.

3. Overview and defect formation in kesterite compounds

Kesterites are sulfide and/or selenide compounds with formulas Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS), Cu2ZnSnSe4 (CZTSe), and Cu2ZnSn(S,Se)4 (CZTSSe). The first report on thermally evaporated functional CZTS solar cells by Katagiri et al. achieved a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 0.66%.41,42 However, in recent years, Se has been incorporated into CZTS to form a CZTSSe alloy with a suitable band gap of 1.13, which is a potential candidate for large-area thin-film solar cells and has provided a record PCE of 12.7%.32 The crystal structure of CZTS belongs to the I2–II–IV–VI4 quaternary material system, which can be achieved from II–VI semiconductors72 by satisfying the octet rule with the total charge remaining neutral. For example, kesterite CZTS is notionally derived from chalcopyrite CuInS2 (CIS2) by substituting two In atoms for one Zn and one Sn atom, which is further derived from sphalerite ZnS, as shown in Fig. 2a. As shown in Fig. 2b, the basic crystal structure of kesterite compounds is debated.73 The oxidation states of Cu (red), Sn (blue), Zn (green), and S and/or Se (yellow) atoms are 1+, 4+, 2+ and 2−, respectively, in stoichiometric Cu2ZnSn(Sx, Se1−x)4 compounds.74 To fabricate functional high-efficiency solar cell devices based on Cu2ZnSn(Sx, Se1−x)4 compounds, non-stoichiometric Cu-poor (Cu/(Zn + Sn) < 2) and Zn-rich (Zn/Sn > 1) films that still maintain the kesterite structure are required.

3.1 Defect formation in kesterite absorbers

The optical properties of CZTS/Se compounds depends upon the amount of chalcogen incorporated. Furthermore, chalcogens have a very specific role in regulating the band gap of the compound. Sulfur incorporation widens the bandgap, while selenium incorporation narrows the bandgap. Therefore, the optical properties of the compound can be varied through precise control of the sulfur/selenium ratio. The optical properties are also enhanced by annealing treatments due to the improved crystallinity. However, internal structural defects and secondary phase formation are also prominent. Elemental losses (SnS, SnS2, SnSe, SnSe2) significantly alter the composition of the compound, which in turn affects the optical, microstructural, and electrical properties.75 Therefore, close control of annealing conditions (temperature and time) is important to maintain the optical and structural properties of the compound. In addition to the optical and structural properties of the CZTS compound, other factors, such as composition, crystal structure, and defects, are important. To employ CZTS compounds in TFSCs, the composition needs to be Cu poor and Zn rich. A Cu-rich composition imparts higher electrical conductivity to the CZTS compound, resulting in shunt losses that eventually lead to reduced solar cell performance. Therefore, precise composition control is very important. Furthermore, intrinsic defects in the compound can lead to charge trapping and recombination losses. The intrinsic structural defects are important because they significantly affect the solar cell performance. Kesterite CZTS is a p-type semiconductor with major copper vacancies (Vcu) and CuZn antisite defects. The origin of these defects depends upon the preparative growth conditions and compositional inhomogeneities. These defects are present in shallow or deep positions in the band gap (between the valence and conduction bands) and their concentration is dependent on the formation energy. The CuZn antisite defect is located at approximately 0.10 eV above the valence band maximum (VBM), whereas the Vcu is located 0.02 eV above the VBM. The latter generates a deep donor level in the band gap, causing a significant reduction in the band gap energy, which explains why CZTS must be Cu-poor and Zn-rich. The Vcu passivates the detrimental Cu2Se phase and the other deep level phase, which act as charge recombination centers to improve the solar cell efficiency. As the intrinsic structural defects significantly affect the optical and electrical properties of CZTS, defect passivation is an important aspect for consideration in further research on improving solar cell performance.

4. Deposition techniques for kesterite absorbers

Different vacuum and non-vacuum-based synthesis routes have been used to prepare kesterite absorber materials, as shown in Fig. 4. Vacuum techniques, including sputtering, coevaporation, and pulsed laser deposition (PLD), and non-vacuum techniques, including hydrazine-based methods, electrodeposition, inkjet printing, and hot injection, have been used to synthesize high-quality kesterite solar cell absorbers, as discussed below.

4.1 Vacuum techniques

Techniques based on vacuum technology, such as sputtering,76,77 coevaporation,78,79 and pulsed laser deposition (PLD),80,81 are commonly used to synthesize CZTSSe thin films. Sputtering is a well-developed and commonly used technique for the deposition of CZTSSe thin films.76,77 In this method, Cu, Zn, and Sn metal films are sputtered at or near room temperature and reacted in a S and/or Se environment at high temperature, resulting in 10.4% efficiency. Clean and uniform deposition processes with high throughput of the absorber can be easily achieved using the sputtering technique. In thermal coevaporation, highly polycrystalline CZTSe thin films are directly evaporated on the Mo substrate, yielding 11.6%.78,79 Single-phase CZTSe films were formed through selenization for 30 min at temperatures in the range of 480–540 °C to achieve a high-quality device. PLD is regarded as a high-precision vacuum technique that allows detailed control of CZTS film formation, wherein high-power laser (Nd:YAG laser) pulses are focused onto a solid state target (CZTS) placed in a vacuum chamber.80,81

4.2 Non-vacuum techniques

Non-vacuum-based techniques, such as hydrazine-based,32,82 electrodeposition,38,83 inkjet printing,84,85 and hot-injection methods,39,86 have been developed. As these processes are low-cost, high-output techniques, non-vacuum-based deposition methods are being discovered as substitutes to costly vacuum-based processing techniques. As shown in Fig. 5, the current record efficiency of 12.7% for CZTSSe was achieved with a solution approach using hydrazine as solvent32,82 by optimizing the degree of In diffusion into the CdS/CZTSSe films. However, hydrazine is unsafe and hard to recycle.32 More recently, an inkjet printing technique has been used to deposit a CZTS precursor ink to demonstrate its ability to achieve good quality thin film layers, which supports the potential use of this technique for roll-to-roll solar module fabrication.84,85 Inkjet printing is a promising route for thin film deposition owing to its low material wastage and potential for high-throughput production. Lin et al.84 deposited a CZTSSe thin film absorber using inkjet printing and studied the influence of sodium on device performance. Furthermore, Colina et al.85 attempted to optimize the parameters, achieving an efficiency of 6.55% for inkjet-printed CZTSSe solar cells. The hot-injection method involves the synthesis of CZTSSe nanocrystal-based ink and has yielded outstanding PV performances beyond 9% PCE,39,86–88 which is comparable to those produced using vacuum-based techniques. 4.3 Present drawbacks of kesterite-based solar cells

Various factors affect the performance of kesterite-based thin-film solar cells. These factors include the kesterite microstructural, compositional, structural, optical, and electrical properties. These factors are interrelated and, as such, any small change in a single property can cause major changes in the overall compound properties. Controlling the kesterite composition is vital. Ideally, for efficient performance, the composition should be copper-poor and zinc-rich. If the composition is copper-rich, the compound conductivity is increased, resulting in an increased shunt loss. The microstructure of the CZTS compound should be smooth and compact. If the microstructure is porous, it enhances the probability of charge recombination at the grain boundaries, eventually resulting in reduced solar-cell performance. The existence of a multi-element system and the smaller single-phase region is responsible for the formation of secondary phases in the CZTS compound. Therefore, the formation of a single-phase CZTS compound is difficult. In addition to the narrow phase stability region, CZTS compounds exhibit an additional drawback of lower thermal stability. Above some specific temperature (400 °C), CZTS becomes unstable due to forming binary (SnS, SnS2, CuS, Cu2S) and ternary (Cu2SnS3, Cu3SnS4 and Cu4SnS4) secondary phases. These secondary phases hamper the morphological, structural, and electrical properties. The secondary phases either increase or decrease the carrier concentration, which affects the electrical properties and, therefore, the performance of CZTS solar cells. The deleterious effect of secondary phases is also demonstrated by the formation of deep and shallow structural defects, resulting in charge recombination losses. Therefore, the performance of CZTS-based solar cells is strongly influenced by secondary phases. Furthermore, back contact interface problems are also important. The formation of a MoS2 layer at the absorber/back contact interface restricts the charge carriers, resulting in increased series resistance in the solar cell device. Therefore, controlling the MoS2 layer at the absorber/back contact interface is important. Furthermore, the performance of the kesterite CZTS compound is influenced by electrically activated defects, such as voltage deficit (VOC deficit). This VOC deficit is caused by interface recombination and/or band tailing due to electrostatic fluctuations, which eventually results in a lower minority carrier lifetime. Therefore, the aforementioned factors clearly influence the performance of CZTS solar cells. Simultaneous control of these factors is challenging and considered a drawback of CZTS-based solar cells that is deserving of further research to achieve performance comparable to Si and CIGS solar cells. An important future research strategy for overcoming these drawbacks is to focus on understanding the phase stability, the relationship between processing parameters and device performance, and the formation of single-phase compounds. Another possible strategy to overcome these drawbacks is element doping into kesterite compounds. Various elements (Ge, Sb, Ag) including alkali elements (Li, Na, and K) can be doped into kesterite compounds. These elements enhance the optical, electrical, structural, and morphological properties of CZTS compounds. Furthermore, using optimal amounts of dopant elements and combinations of various dopant elements in kesterite compounds can be used to enhance the electrical/microstructural and defect passivation properties to obtain improved solar cell performance.

5. Overview of alkali doping in kesterite compounds

Na-doping into absorber compounds has resulted in beneficial and harmful effects on the solar electrical performance. For example, Na-doping into chalcopyrite Cu(In,Ga)Se2 (CIGSe) improved the solar cell performance, while Na-doping into CdTe-based thin film solar cells resulted in deteriorated performance.89 The reduced performance of CdTe solar cells was attributed to the disrupted morphological structure of the absorber compound and changes in the properties of the transparent conducting oxide (TCO). Therefore, Na-doping in CdTe-based thin film solar cells was concluded to have a negative impact and should be avoided. However, in the case of CZTS-based solar cells, Na-doping enhanced the electrical conductivity and grain size, and provided solar cell performance similar to that of CuInSe2 films.90–93 Therefore, Na-doping in CZTS-based thin film solar cells has received significant research attention. Multiple strategies for sodium incorporation into absorber compounds, such as Na diffusion from the glass substrate and employing Na-based precursors, have been adopted.94–96 Usually, Mo-deposited soda lime glass (SLG) substrate is used as the back contact in thin-film solar cells. During the sulfurization process (500–600 °C), Na from the SLG becomes mobile and diffuses into the absorber compound through the Mo layer. The exact mechanism of Na diffusion in CZTS remains unclear. However, based on literature reports of Na-doped CIGS solar cells, it was concluded that Na diffusion from the SLG proceeded through Na+ ions, along with the counter diffusion of positively charged ions to maintain charge balance,97 as shown in Fig. 6. The mechanism responsible for diffusion was a H+/Na+ ion-exchange process.98,99 The Na+ ion source was Na2O present in the glass, while the H+ ion source was the Mo layer incorporated during air exposure. As the annealing treatment proceeded, Na from the SLG diffused through the Mo layer via oxygenated grain boundaries. Notably, other elements (Si, Ca, Mg, K, Al, Fe, S) present in the SLG can also diffuse along with Na, which may or may not collectively positively impact the absorber properties. Therefore, several reports have reported inconsistent results regarding the positive impact of Na doping.80–83,100–103 Although SLG has low cost, exhibits higher thermal stability, and is widely employed as a Na source, it still has some limitations. It remains unclear how the amount of Na doping can be controlled and reproduced. Furthermore, the Mo layer over SLG also affects the Na diffusion process. The Mo oxidation state and its physical properties are known to regulate the diffusion process.89 Therefore, Mo properties should also be considered in studies focusing on Na doping through diffusion from SLG.

Another strategy for incorporating Na diffusion into absorber compounds involves simply placing an SLG substrate over the absorber (CZTS) film during the annealing treatment. In the study conducted by Yang et al.104 for Na doping in CZTS, a similar strategy was adopted. Na successfully diffused in the CZTS from the top SLG and as well as from the bottom Mo/SLG substrate. Such a simple and cost-effective strategy can be employed for an efficient Na doping. Another effective strategy is to employ MoNa as a back contact layer. This approach was first tested by Granath et al.105 for CIGS solar cells in which the Na was ion-implanted into the Mo layer. This resulted in effective diffusion of Na into the absorber layer. A similar approach can be employed in CZTS solar cells. The Na concentration in the MoNa layer is important for realizing incremental effects. Studies based on Na concentrations varying from 2 to 15 at% in MoNa were conducted, with 10 at% Na found to be optimal. Furthermore, the results of Blösch et al.106,107 indicated that Na diffuses through the internal intermediate grain boundaries between MoNa layers and, therefore, that the MoNa microstructure plays a key role in influencing the diffusion process. Employing multiple stack layers of MoNa can enhance the diffusion process into the absorber compound. Therefore, the approach of Na diffusion from Mo/SLG or MoNa layers into the absorber compound is effective, but still suffers from diffusion concentration control problems.

Another strategy through which alkali metal (Na or K) can be incorporated into the absorber compound is achieved by employing Na/K compound-based precursors.108–111 Usually, during absorber layer growth, elemental Na, Na2S, Na2S, NaBr, NaCl, NaI, NaF, KF, and KCl have been used. The precise control of alkaline precursors is important. When employing NaF precursor in CIGS solar cells, a thickness of 10 nm was found to be optimal. Below a thickness of 10 nm, the fabricated solar cell had a low VOC, while above a thickness of 20 nm, the cell performance degraded due to peeling of the absorber layer during CdS deposition. Furthermore, according to reported studies,112,113 alkaline metal diffusion is influenced by processing conditions (annealing temperature and time). Therefore, a stable precursor source must be used to efficiently control diffusion. Despite the improvements realized by alkali doping in thin-film solar cells, the fundamental mechanism responsible for improved effects is uncertain. Therefore, further detailed studies are required to uncover the underlying chemistry.

6. Impact of alkali doping on CZTS properties

Clearly, alkali metal doping into CZTS had a significant impact on the electrical, optical, structural, and microstructural properties. In work conducted by Laghfour et al.,114 the impact of Na content on the structural and optical properties of sol–gel-processed CZTS films was performed. NaCl was used as the Na source in CZTS solution and using contents of 0%, 10%, 20%, and 30% (NaCl/CZTS molar ratio). The film was then sulfurized at 500 °C for 1 h to obtain the desired crystallinity. As shown in the XRD spectra in Fig. 7, the prominent (112) plane peak was observed in all cases, except for the film prepared using a Na concentration of 10%, which exhibited a higher peak intensity indicating enhanced crystallinity. For higher Na concentrations above 10%, the intensity was reduced, indicating reduced film crystallinity due to detrimental effects emerging from higher Na concentrations. Therefore, the optimum concentration of Na dopant in the CZTS film was concluded to enhance the film crystallinity. Furthermore, the optical bandgap was reduced from 1.45 eV for undoped CZTS film to 1.34 eV for Na-doped (30%) CZTS film. The decrease in bandgap with increasing Na concentration was attributed to Na-induced defects present in the CZTS crystal structure. Therefore, Na doping into the CZTS thin film at optimum concentration enhanced the crystallinity and reduced the bandgap, which was beneficial for realizing high-efficiency solar cells.

In addition to structural and optical properties, Na doping also influenced the microstructure of the CZTS film. The effect of varying Na concentration on the microstructural properties of CZTS film was studied by Sun et al.115 In the study, Cu, Zn, and Sn precursors were sputter-deposited on stainless steel substrate followed by sputtering with a NaF layer. Different NaF layer thicknesses of 0, 10, and 20 nm were used, and their effect on the microstructure and solar cell performance was evaluated. As shown in the FESEM images in Fig. 8, the undoped CZTS film exhibited a non-uniform and smaller grain-sized morphology. For films deposited with NaF, significant changes in the microstructure were observed. The film deposited with 10 nm-thick NaF exhibited a relatively smooth, compact, and large grain-sized morphology. However, for the film deposited with a higher NaF thickness (20 nm), the morphology deteriorated, with the grain-sized decreasing and the morphology becoming porous, resulting in larger grain boundaries (higher charge recombination). A similarly reduced grain size was observed with excess Na doping, as reported by Wen et al.116 The fabricated solar cell with 10 nm-thick Na-doped CZTS film exhibited an improved efficiency of 4.10% compared with 3.07% for that using the undoped film. Meanwhile, 20 nm-thick Na-doped CZTS film showed a reduced efficiency of 2.78% owing to microstructure deterioration originating from the higher Na doping. Therefore, optimum Na doping clearly enhanced the microstructure regarding improved grain size, enhanced film compactness, and smooth texture. Furthermore, Na forms a desirable ‘spike-like’ structure at grain boundaries, which effectively repels holes and attracts electrons to promote efficient charge transfer. Several studies have reported that Na doping increases the carrier concentration suitable for enhancing the efficiency. However, increased carrier concentration beyond a certain limit reduces the mobility, which affects solar cell performance. Therefore, effectively controlling the carrier concentration through regulated Na concentration is vital. Therefore, Na doping in CZTS is beneficial for improving the optical, structural, electrical, and microstructural properties.

Formation of the MoS2 layer at the absorber/MoSLG interface during sulfurization is inherent and difficult to control. A MoS2 layer of up to 100–150 nm is acceptable because it forms an ohmic contact. However, if the MoS2 layer thickness increases above 150 nm, it introduces series resistance in the solar cell and reduces efficiency. Therefore, controlling the thickness of the MoS2 layer is important. As a possible strategy to reduce the MoS2 layer thickness, Yang et al.117 annealed the Mo/SLG substrate under a N2 atmosphere in the temperature range of 180–550 °C for 10 min. This resulted in the diffusion of Na from SLG into the Mo layer and the formation of molybdenum oxide due to the presence of compounds such as SiO2, Na2O, CaO, and Al2O3 in SLG as rich oxygen sources. The diffused Na and formed molybdenum oxide suppressed the MoS2 layer and reduced the defect density in the absorber compound. X-ray diffraction was conducted to analyze the formation of MoS2117 due to the reaction between Mo and S (produced by sulfurization), as shown in Fig. 9. The MoS2 intensity was found to have an inverse relation with the Mo annealing temperature. Therefore, Na doping also proved to be beneficial for controlling MoS2 layer formation and defect passivation, reflecting its diverse role in CZTS compounds.

Similar to Na doping, K doping has had positive effects on CZTS thin-film solar cells due to enhancing carrier collection and passivating recombination at the front interface. Various reports have shown the impact of K doping on CZTS film in terms of enhanced grain size, improved film crystallinity, increased carrier concentration, and suppressed secondary phases.21,53 However, as literature on K doping in CZTS is very limited, the exact role of K in enhancing solar cell performance remains quite unclear. In a recent study conducted by Li et al.,56 the effect of K doping in CZTSSe solar cells was investigated. CZTSSe films were prepared using the sol–gel technique, and K doping was accomplished by simply mixing KCl solution into the absorber precursor solution, followed by annealing in selenium atmosphere at 560 °C for 10 min. The K concentration was varied from 0–1.5 mol% to evaluate its effect on the CZTSSe properties and the corresponding solar cell performance. Fig. 10 shows FESEM images of the CZTSSe film obtained from varying the K concentration. Undoped CZTSSe film exhibited a smooth and large grain-sized morphology with sizes in the range of 0.5–2 μm, while K-doped CZTSSe films exhibited faceted and smaller grain-sized morphologies (0.5–1 μm). The difference in grain size was attributed to the reaction kinetics of the alkali-Se compound during annealing. Furthermore, the bilayer structure of the film (top layer of larger grains and bottom layer of sparsely packed smaller grains), as seen in the cross-sectional images, was attributed to the carbon residue from the solvent.

7. Impact of Ge doping/alloying on kesterite films

The enhanced open circuit voltage (VOC) and improved charge collection and transport properties achieved by fine-tuning the Ga/[Ga + In] ratio resulted in a maximum attainable efficiency of 21.7% for CIGSe TFSC.118 The partial replacement of In with Ga reduced the unit cell size, which widened the bandgap and eventually resulted in an enhanced VOC. Such a similar approach of cationic incorporation was adopted in CZTS/Se solar cells to improve the power conversion efficiency. In particular, Ge element alloying in CZTS/Se was found to be beneficial because it effectively replaced cationic tin (Sn) and reduced recombination losses.62 Multivalent Sn undergoes oxidation from Sn2+ to Sn4+ state, which reduces the device stability/performance.119,120 In contrast, the Ge4+ state is more stable for restoring solar cell performance, and Ge has a similar crustal abundance to that of Sn. Furthermore, Ge-doped CZTSe exhibits a smaller interaction parameter (5.6 mV per atom) than pristine CZTSSe (5.6 mV per atom) suitable for forming a stable alloy compound.63 Therefore, Ge doping into CZTS/Se-based solar cells has proven to be a judicious choice in terms of incurred cost, compatibility, and improved performance.

Ge doping improves the solar cell performance by enhancing the optical and microstructural properties of the compound. The bandgap can be varied (1.2–2.5 eV) by adjusting the Ge/[Ge + Sn] compositional ratio.121 When Ge is doped into CZTS/Se compounds, the Ge-4s atoms displace Sn-5s atoms, which leads to increased s–s and s–p orbital repulsion between Ge–S and Sn–S. The repulsion of Ge–S bonding was higher than that of Sn–S bonding, which increased the conduction band to a higher energy state from the Fermi level, eventually resulting in the compound having an increased band gap. Therefore, close control of the bandgap through optimizing the Ge/[Ge + Sn] compositional ratio was vital. Lokhande et al.122 reported a reduction in the bandgap of Ge-doped CZTS/Se film from 1.25–1.01 eV by reducing Ge/[Ge + Sn] compositional ratio from 0.132 to 0.078 (Fig. 11). A similarly reduced bandgap with decreasing Ge/[Ge + Sn] compositional ratio has been observed in several reports.119,121 Notably, Ge loss during annealing treatment was significant due to the vapor pressure of Ge being relatively low. Therefore, fluctuations in the composition of Ge-doped CZTS/Se films were induced, which might affect their power conversion efficiency.122 Therefore, various reports have suggested using GeS2 or GeSe2 pellets during annealing treatment to compensate for Ge loss.63,64

The morphology is another factor that is directly influenced by Ge doping. Several reports have concluded that Ge doping improves the morphology of CZTS/Se films by producing a highly crystalline and compact morphology.62,118–120,123 Ge doping favors liquid-phase assisted growth. During annealing treatment (500–550 °C), the GeS/Se2 liquid phase was highly dominant in the CZTS/Se film. This liquid phase served as a flux for high-density atomic transport, resulting in the formation of a large grain-sized morphology with a smooth texture (Fig. 12).64,122 Furthermore, in the study conducted by Saucedo et al.,124 it was concluded that Ge formed Ge-oxide nano-inclusions in the CZTSe film, which promoted large grain formation suitable for fabricating high-efficiency thin film solar cells. Therefore, the Ge doping approach has proved beneficial in CZTS/Se films.

Various chemical and physical techniques have been employed to fabricate Ge-doped CZTS/Se solar cells.63,118,120,122,124,125 Chemical technique-based nanoparticle and spray-deposition methods have obtained efficiencies of up to 9.4%120 and 11%,123 respectively, while physical techniques such as pulsed laser deposition (PLD), sputtering, and co-evaporation have also been employed. Saucedo et al. introduced a superficial 10 nm-thick Ge layer over CZTSe film through co-evaporation and obtained a power conversion efficiency of 10.1%.124 The highest obtained efficiency was 12.3%, along with a JSC of 32.2 mA cm−2 and VOC of 0.527 V, using Ge-doped CZTSe films using a co-evaporation method,63 which was quite close to the record reported CZTSe solar cell efficiency (12.6%). In most reported studies, the improved performance of the Ge-doped kesterite solar cells was mainly attributed to the enhanced VOC originating from the large grain-sized morphology formed. The literature shows that a large grain-sized morphology accompanied with smooth texture resulted in reduced charged-recombination losses and improved charge-separation capabilities. Furthermore, the enlarged grain size significantly improved the carrier diffusion length and charge lifetime, which were beneficial for delivering improved power conversion efficiencies.

8. Impact of alkali and other metal dopants on device performance

Electronic and optical properties can be tuned by impurities in Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS). By tuning the electronic and optical properties, the efficiency of the photovoltaic device may decrease. To investigate the effect of foreign dopants when pairing atoms and lattices, the phonon dispersion susceptibility can be used as a criterion.127 Sarswat et al. used a bath containing ions of interest (Cu2+, Zn2+, Sn2+, and Sb2+) to grow thin antimony-doped CZTS films electrochemically on a fluorinated tin oxide (FTO). A Gamry Instruments reference 600 potentiostat operated by virtual front panel software with a three-electrode cell has been used to perform co-electrodeposition. A 50 mL electrolyte solution containing copper sulfate pentahydrate, tin chloride, zinc sulfate, and antimony trichloride has been used to synthesize a zinc–copper–tin–antimony alloy. Potassium sodium tartrate has been used as a complexing or chelating agent to keep the electrode potentials of different metal ions similar to each other for controlling the preferential electroplating. The potential used for conducting electrodeposition was −1.6 V and the time was limited to 160–180 s to obtain a 1–1.5 μm thick as-deposited film. Electrodeposited films were cured in a tube furnace at 550 °C for 2 h under an evaporated sulfur environment for sulfurization. Ag and Fe-doped CZTS films were deposited using the same procedure. Experiments were also conducted with CZTS films without Sb doping.126 Atomic force microscopy (AFM) analysis, as shown in Fig. 13, confirmed that the grain boundaries of Sb-doped CZTS film were more conductive than those of CZTS thin films. Sb-doping has also improved the uniformity and size of CZTS grain boundaries.126

9. Conclusions and outlook

In this review, we have summarized the current state-of-the-art in kesterite technology and summarized the impact of alkali and other metal dopants on crystal structure, optical, electrical, and microstructural properties, and solar cell performance. As shown throughout, alkali and other metal dopants significantly affect the basic electrical and optical properties of kesterite thin-film-based solar cells. Doping with alkali and other metals can passivate lattice defects without changing the kesterite structure, and can reduce the diode quality factor and reverse the saturation current to improve device performance. Kesterite devices showed increased open circuit potentials (VOC), fill factor (FF) values, and short-circuit current densities (JSC) through post-deposition of alkali and related metals, with some properties that were strongly dependent on the type of alkali metal species. The maximum efficiency improvement in kesterite solar cells was obtained for Na-doped CZTSSe absorber. Preparative procedures for alkali and other metal doping in kesterite and a brief description of characterization were also presented. Among alkali metals, Na seems to be the most promising dopant for kesterite-based absorbers owing to the resulting optoelectronic properties. The improvements in VOC and PCE after doping with metals such as Ge, Sb, and Ag, helps to promote grain growth, increase the hole concentration, grain boundaries, and defect passivation, and, therefore, broaden the surface bandgap and lower the valence band maximum of the kesterite surface to enhance the PCE of CZTSSe solar cells. For Ge-doped CZTSSe, the device conversion efficiency reached up to 12.3% with a VOC deficit of 527 mV. Furthermore, the most important factor to be considered is the concentration of the dopant element in the parent compound. A very high dopant concentration can have detrimental effects on the structural, optical, and electrical properties of the compound, resulting in poor device efficiency. A very low dopant concentration is also undesirable because the positive effects of the dopant cannot be imparted. Therefore, controlling the optimum dopant concentration is important. Table 5 shows the PCEs of reported kesterite solar cells with alkali and other metal dopants. Various dopant elements contribute differently to improving the solar cell performance. Na and Ag doping increases the carrier concentration, thereby improving the current density (JSC) in the solar cells. Ge doping enhances the microstructure and widens the bandgap, eventually resulting in an improved voltage (VOC) in the solar cells. Therefore, if a systematic combination of these dopant elements is utilized in kesterite compounds, the synergistic effects of these dopants might further improve the solar cell performance.

Despite the remarkable PCE performance achieved by kesterite solar cells, they remain at too early a stage of development to make an impact similar to those of c-Si and CIGS technology in commercialization. Therefore, much effort should be devoted to the continued development, and significant challenges must be addressed. Firstly, the development of cost-effective and stable technologies is needed to prepare kesterite devices with high PCEs. Secondly, research should focus on achieving efficient PCEs for practical applications. Thirdly, further effort should be devoted to achieving in-depth insight into the doping structure–PCE relationship and the dynamics of charge carriers, which are important for rationalizing the design of future PV technology. Finally, strategies for improving the crystal quality to avoid secondary phase formation during synthesis should be explored to obtained increased VOC values. From the above assessment, we predict that the combination of alkali and other metals could be a subject of interest to improve the VOC deficit and PCE performance of kesterite solar cells. This unprecedented and unparalleled progress will generate new avenues for exploring and developing novel dopant elements for efficient kesterite solar cells.
