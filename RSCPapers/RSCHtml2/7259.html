Abstract

The conversion of the [60]fullerene-fused lactones to ketones with triflic anhydride as an unusual reductant under aerobic conditions has been achieved in excellent yields. The present thermal retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction from lactones to ketones has no precedents. In addition, the unique ring-opened peroxy [60]fullerene derivatives can be obtained by the electrochemical reactions of the synthesized [60]fullerene-fused ketones.

The transformation of ketones into esters or that of cyclic ketones into lactones, namely the Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction,1 is one of the most important and fundamental reactions in organic synthesis.2 However, retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction is still extremely challenging. Because of their significance in materials science and biological science, a variety of fullerene derivatives have been synthesized over the last 30 years.3 Among them, the carbonyl derivatives of [60]fullerene (C60) have received extensive attention from scientists.4,5 Indeed, once the fullerenes are converted into fullerene derivatives by acylation, these compounds have strong electron-accepting ability4b,i and exhibit a high electron-transport mobility.4i Moreover, the presence of the acyl groups provides a powerful tool for the further transformation of fullerene derivatives. In 2019, we reported the intriguing reaction of the C60-fused lactone dianions with acetic acid (AcOH). It was found that treatment of the C60-fused lactone dianions with AcOH efficiently afforded the C60-fused ketones.5 This was the first report on retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction by electrochemical reduction followed by acid quenching (Scheme 1). Subsequently, we investigated the reaction of the C60-fused lactone dianions with benzyl bromide (PhCH2Br), and ring-opened benzyl adducts were unexpectedly obtained.6 In addition, the ring-opened products could be used as the efficient electron-transport materials (ETMs) in perovskite solar cells (PSCs), and a slightly higher power conversion efficiency (PCE) than that of [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) was obtained.

Although the electrochemical retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction of the C60-fused lactones to ketones was very efficient, it was still limited by some conditions, such as the requirement for an electrochemical workstation and an inert atmosphere. Therefore, conventional thermal methods for the retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reactions are highly desirable. Herein, we are delighted to disclose that the C60-fused lactones can be efficiently converted to the corresponding C60-fused ketones with triflic anhydride (Tf2O) as an unusual reductant under heating and aerobic conditions. This is the first time that the retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction has been achieved under thermal reaction conditions (Scheme 1). Furthermore, we also explore the electrochemical functionalization of a representative C60-fused ketone to provide unique ring-opened peroxide products.

Organic acids and anhydrides are often used as effective reagents for the transformations of fullerene derivatives.7 Therefore, we initially treated the C60-fused lactone 1a8 (0.015 mmol) with AcOH (0.090 mmol) in 1,2-dichlorobenzene (1,2-C6H4Cl2) (3 mL) at 120 Â°C for 10 h under an air atmosphere and no reaction took place (Table 1, entry 1). Other organic acids such as trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) or p-toluenesulfonic acid monohydrate (p-TSA) could not induce the reaction either (Table 1, entries 2 and 3). To our delight, when using triflic acid (TfOH) as the acid, the desired product 2a was obtained in 8% yield (Table 1, entry 4). Subsequently, the corresponding anhydrides, such as acetic anhydride (Ac2O), trifluoroacetic anhydride (TFAA), p-toluenesulfonic anhydride (p-TSAA) and Tf2O, were also investigated (Table 1, entries 5â€“8). To our satisfaction, when 3 equiv. of Tf2O was used, the yield was significantly improved to 87% (Table 1, entry 8). When reducing the amount of Tf2O to 2 equiv., 2a was delivered in 62% yield (Table 1, entry 9). Continuing to increase the amount of Tf2O to 4 equiv., a slightly lower yield of 86% was obtained (Table 1, entry 10). Further altering the reaction temperature or time, a better yield could not be obtained (Table 1, entries 11â€“14). In addition, the reaction under an argon atmosphere was examined, and the yield was equivalent to that under aerobic conditions, indicating that O2 had no effect on the reaction (87% vs. 87%, Table 1, entries 8 vs. 15) and an inert atmosphere was not required in the current reaction. Thus, the optimal reaction conditions were a molar ratio of 1:3 for 1a and Tf2O at 120 Â°C for 10 h under aerobic conditions.

Since the retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction obtained by a thermal approach has not been reported before, and the reaction intermediates are difficult to obtain, the exact pathway leading to the C60-fused ketone 2 is not currently clear. Based on the previous literature,9â€“13 we propose a possible reaction mechanism (Scheme 2). Firstly, under the action of Tf2O, the C60-fused lactone 1 is transformed to the trifloxycarbenium cation I,9,10 which exists with its resonance structure, that is, oxonium ion II.10 Similar iminium/imino triflates can also be formed from amides and Tf2O.11 Subsequently, cationic triflic carboxylic anhydride III is produced by ring opening with the Câ€“O heterolytic cleavage. Fullerene cations have been widely proposed as the reaction intermediates in the literature.14 Finally, the cationic species III undergoes an intramolecular cyclization with the aid of TfOâˆ’ to give the C60-fused ketone 2 along with TfOOTf.12 Similar attack of TfOâˆ’ on the oxygen atom of a triflate intermediate to generate TfOOTf was previously proposed.12 Because TfOOTf is extremely unstable, all efforts towards its separation and detection failed.12,13 Tf2O has been extensively used in organic synthesis, and has oxidative properties.11a Nevertheless, the deoxygenation of sulfoxides to sulfides by Tf2O has been documented in the literature.12,15 However, thermal deoxygenation of lactones to give ketones by Tf2O or other reagents has never been reported before. The current protocol presents the first example of thermal retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction.

Additionally, the electrochemical reactions of the C60-fused ketone 2 were investigated. Product 2a was selected as a representative substrate for further electrochemical functionalization (Scheme 3). First, the dianionic 2a2âˆ’ was obtained under controlled potential electrolysis (CPE) at âˆ’1.30 V vs. saturated calomel electrode (SCE). It is worth noting that the solution color of 2a2âˆ’ was dark-green, which was the typical color for C60 anionic species bonded with only one addend.16 Thus, we surmised that after 2a accepted two electrons, the C60â€“C bond cleavage occurred to generate ring-opened dianionic 2a2âˆ’. The Câ€“C bond was difficult to break, and there were few reports on the electrochemical cleavage of the Câ€“C bond of the fullerene-fused carbocycles.17 Then, we applied 2a2âˆ’ to the benzylation reaction. In summary, we have developed a straightforward thermal retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction and realized the conversion of the C60-fused lactones to ketones with Tf2O as an unusual reductant. This is the first retro Baeyerâ€“Villiger reaction using traditional thermal methods. The reaction performed under aerobic conditions makes the experimental operations much more simple and convenient. In addition, we have studied the electrochemical reactions of the C60-fused ketone 2a and obtained ring-opened peroxyester or peroxyacid compounds, which may be utilized in further functionalizations and solar cell devices.
