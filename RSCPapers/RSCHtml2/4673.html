Abstract

Green synthesis of Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) and Cu2ZnSn(S1âˆ’xSex)4 (CZTSSe) nanocrystals is highly desirable for low-cost and high-efficiency solar energy conversion devices. In this work, scalable synthesis of multinary CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals at room temperature has been achieved by a simple metal complex solution mixing (Metcomix) process. In the Metcomix process, CZTS or CZTSSe nanocrystals are formed by simply mixing aqueous solutions of copper thiourea complex ([Cu(TU)4]2+), zinc ammonium complex ([Zn(NH3)4]2+) and tin chalcogen complex ([Sn2S6]4âˆ’) or tin double chalcogen complex ([Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’) at room temperature. The Metcomix process features low-energy-consuming, low-cost, environmentally friendly, high-purity, and scalable-production. The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals have a small size of 4â€“10 nm and exhibit remarkable room-temperature photoluminescence and optical absorption properties. The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals are also deposited onto ZnO nanorod arrays and demonstrated as efficient photoanodes for photoelectrochemical water splitting. The ZnO/CZTSSe photoanode exhibits a photocurrent density of 9.06 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V (vs. the NHE) and an optimal applied bias photon-to-current efficiency (ABPE) of âˆ¼3.43% at a bias of 0.60 V. The present work demonstrates a new approach for synthesizing eco-friendly multinary chalcogenide nanocrystals at room temperature and their promising applications in solar energy conversion devices.

Introduction

Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) as a multinary I2â€“IIâ€“IVâ€“VI4 compound is a promising light-harvesting material for solar energy conversion applications due to its merits of suitable bandgap (âˆ¼1.5 eV), high absorption coefficient (>104 cmâˆ’1), earth-abundance of the constituting elements, low toxicity, good photostability, and tunable absorption via Se incorporation to form Cu2ZnSn(S1âˆ’xSex)4 (CZTSSe).1â€“10 Synthesis of CZTS or CZTSSe colloidal nanocrystals via solution processing has attracted much recent attention.8â€“22 The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals are usually synthesized by colloidal solution methods, including both hot-injection and non-injection strategies.8â€“22 Such synthesis typically requires a high-temperature (usually >200 Â°C) heating process and is still energy-consuming. It also involves long-chain organic solvents such as oleylamine, oleic acid, trimethylamine, trioctylphosphine oxide, octadecene, and dodecanethiol, which are expensive and hazardous to the environment. Moreover, the attached organic ligands on the surface of the CZTSSe nanocrystals can form insulating barriers, suppress charge separation, and consequently deteriorate their solar energy conversion performance. Therefore, exploration of new chemistry for preparing CZTSSe nanocrystals featuring room temperature reaction, green aqueous solution, no toxicity and large-scale synthesis is highly desirable. However, to the best of our knowledge, there have been no reports on synthesis of CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals at room temperature.

There are two critical challenges for synthesizing CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals at room temperature. On one hand, there are large differences in solubility product constants (Ksp) of the component binary sulfides: CuS (6.3 Ã— 10âˆ’36), ZnS (2.5 Ã— 10âˆ’22), and SnS2 (1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’25). This suggests that it is much easier to form CuS because of its small Ksp instead of the quaternary alloyed CZTS. Once binary copper sulfide is formed, high temperature or pressure is required for its conversion to alloyed CZTS.23â€“25 On the other hand, there is a lack of suitable aqueous precursor solutions containing both S and Se anions that are chemically bonded together as a group for preparing pentabasic alloyed CZTSSe nanocrystals. As a result, mixtures of CZTS and CZTSe can be easily formed instead of CZTSSe.

To address these challenges, we report for the first time a simple metal complex mixing (Metcomix) process for preparing high purity CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals in aqueous solution at room temperature. In the Metcomix process, formation of binary sulfides is suppressed by employing the reaction between metal complexes instead of direct reactions between Cu2+, Zn2+, Sn4+, S2âˆ’ and Se2âˆ’ ions. Furthermore, formation of high purity CZTSSe is enabled by developing an aqueous [Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’ precursor solution in which complex ions containing simultaneously S and Se are achieved. The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals exhibit a kesterite phase with an average size of 4â€“10 nm. They show outstanding room-temperature PL properties and photoresponse properties. Importantly, CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystal sensitized ZnO nanorod arrays are prepared and demonstrated as efficient photoanodes for photoelectrochemical water splitting.

Experimental

Preparation of the complex solutions

Copper thiourea complex ([Cu(TU)4]2+) solution was prepared by dissolving a Cu(NO3)2 aqueous solution (3.0 mL, 3.3 M) in a thiourea (TU) aqueous solution (105 mL, 1.0 M) under continuous magnetic stirring. Zinc ammonium complex ([Zn(NH3)4]2+) solution was prepared by dissolving a Zn(NO3)2 aqueous solution (1.5 mL, 3.3 M) in an ammonium solution (6 mL, 25â€“28%) under continuous magnetic stirring. Tin chalcogen complex ([Sn2S6]4âˆ’) solution was made by dissolving the S powder (10 mmol) and Sn powder (5 mmol) in a (NH4)2S aqueous solution (9 mL, 20%) under continuous magnetic stirring. Tin double chalcogen complex ([Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’) solution was prepared by dissolving the Se powder (9 mmol) and Sn powder (5 mmol) in a (NH4)2S aqueous solution (9.0 mL, 20%) under continuous magnetic stirring.

Synthesis of CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals

For the synthesis of CZTS nanocrystals, the [Zn(NH3)4]2+ and the [Sn2S6]4âˆ’ complex solutions were added into the [Cu(TU)4]2+ complex solution under vigorous magnetic stirring. The mixed solution was kept for 2 h at room temperature. The synthesis of CZTSSe nanocrystals was performed by the same procedure except that the [Sn2S6]4âˆ’ complex solution was replaced with the [Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’ complex solution. Black precipitation products were collected and washed with distilled water and ethanol several times. The product yield in one pot is larger than 2 g.

Preparation of ZnO/CZTSSe and ZnO/CZTS photoanodes

Arrays of ZnO nanorods on FTO glass substrates were synthesized by a hydrothermal method as described in our previous reports.26,27 The CZTSSe (or CZTS) nanocrystals were deposited onto the surface of the ZnO nanorods by spin-coating the nanocrystal inks. Finally, the ZnO/CZTSSe and ZnO/CZTS photoanodes were annealed at 400 Â°C for 30 min.

Characterization

The electrospray ionization-mass spectroscopy (ESI-MS) spectrum of the [Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’ complex solution was measured on a liquid chromatography time-of-flight mass spectrometer (ACQUITY UPLC LCT Premier XE). Crystallographic structures of the CZTS and the CZTSSe samples were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD, Rigaku D/Max 2500v diffractometer, Cu KÎ± radiation). Morphologies, sizes, crystal structures, and compositions were studied by transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Philips CM 200 FEG, 200 kV, or JEOL JEM-2100F, 200 kV), energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX), and scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Philips XL30 FEG). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed on a Thermo ESCALAB 250Xi XPS spectrometer system with a monochromatic Al KÎ± X-ray source (1486.6 eV). Raman spectra were measured with a Lab RAM HR Evolution system with a 532 nm excitation source. Absorption spectra were recorded with a Cary 5000 UV-Vis-NIR spectrophotometer. PL spectra were carried out with a Hitachi F-4600 fluorescence spectrophotometer with an excitation of 550 nm.

Photoelectrochemical water splitting measurement

A standard three-electrode configuration in a quartz cell was assembled to carry out photoelectrochemical measurement using an electrochemical workstation (CHI 660E). A Pt wire and a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) were used as the counter electrode and the reference electrode, respectively. The arrays of ZnO/CZTSSe, ZnO/CZTS, and bare ZnO on FTO glass substrates with an active area of 0.2 cm2 were used as the working electrodes for electrochemical water splitting. A Na2SO4 aqueous solution (0.5 M) with a pH = 6.78 was used as the electrolyte, which was bubbled with N2 for 30 min prior to measurements. The simulated sunlight (CEAULIGHT, CEL-S500) under AM 1.5G illumination at a power density of 100 mW cmâˆ’2 was employed as the light source.

The reactions to synthesize CZTS and CZTSSe might take place as follows:

(1) Synthesis of CZTS nanocrystals,

In the above two equations, redox reactions occur. Reduction of Cu2+ to Cu+ is accompanied by oxidation of S2âˆ’ and/or Se2âˆ’ to form the by-product of elemental S and/or Se. The by-product of S can be further dissolved in the mixed solution containing excess (NH4)2S to form soluble poly-sulfide ions (Sn2âˆ’). For the formation of CZTSSe nanocrystals, the by-product of Se is formed and well demonstrated by the XRD and SEM results as shown in Fig. S3, ESI.â€ 

The absorption and photoluminescence (PL) properties of the freshly prepared CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals were investigated respectively by UV-vis spectroscopy and fluorescence spectroscopy. Fig. 5a shows UV-vis spectra of the two nanocrystals, revealing that the absorption edge red shifts slightly after incorporation of Se into CZTS to form CZTSSe. Recently, it has been reported that wurtzite CZTSSe nanocrystals have PL properties at low temperature of 20 K.10 Room-temperature PL properties are seldom reported.5,35,36 In our work, both the CZTS and the CZTSSe nanocrystals show remarkable PL at room temperature. The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals exhibit prominent emission peaks at respectively 832 and 838 nm (Fig. 5b). The bandgap is estimated to be 1.49 eV for the CZTS nanocrystals and 1.48 eV for the CZTSSe nanocrystal. While it is difficult to tune the bandgap by a quantum confinement effect owing to the small Bohr radius (2.2â€“4.3 nm) of CZTS,37 the ability to control the composition allows for versatile tuning of optical properties. Therefore, the as-synthesized nanocrystals, containing no toxic metal, can serve as promising candidates for replacing the widely studied cadmium or lead chalcogenide quantum dots in biological tagging, quantum-dot solar cells, photoelectrochemical water splitting, or as components for optoelectronic devices.

To demonstrate potential applications of the CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals in photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting, the as-prepared nanocrystals are coated onto ZnO nanorod arrays to construct photoanodes of PEC cells (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ). As depicted in Fig. 6a, upon illumination, electronâ€“hole pairs are generated at the photoanode. When the holes move to the interface of photoanode/electrolyte and oxidize OHâˆ’ ions to produce O2, the photoelectrons transport through an external circuit to the Pt counter electrode and reduce H+ ions to generate H2.38â€“41 The cross-sectional SEM images of the bare ZnO nanorods and the ZnO/CZTSSe photoanodes are presented in Fig. 6b. The arrays of the nanorods have a length of 5â€“6 Î¼m. The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves of the different photoanodes under illumination are presented in Fig. 6c. The photoanode of the bare ZnO nanorod array delivers a photocurrent density of 0.48 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. the NHE. After coating the CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals, the photoanodes show significantly enhanced photoelectrochemical performance. Photocurrent density is increased to 6.38 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. the NHE for the ZnO/CZTS photoanode and further to 9.06 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. the NHE for the ZnO/CZTSSe photoanode. The increase in photocurrent density is attributed to the expanded light harvesting ability of the photoanodes after being sensitized with CZTSSe nanocrystals. The pristine ZnO photoanode exhibits an optimal ABPE of only 0.16%. Sensitization with CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals substantially enhances the ABPE by improving the photocurrent. Furthermore, the photoresponse of the photoanodes is investigated by measuring the LSV under chopped light illumination (Fig. 6e). It is observed that both ZnO/CZTS and ZnO/CZTSSe exhibit a rapid and reproducible photocurrent response with respect to the on/off cycles. When light illumination is turned off, the current density of the PEC device is negligible, indicating the formation of a space charge region in the photoanodes. When the light is turned on, high current densities are measured, owing to the collection of photogenerated carriers. The vertical rising and falling photocurrent densities of the transient photoresponse indicate fast charge transport in these PEC devices.

Conclusions

In summary, CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystal inks have been successfully synthesized at room temperature for the first time, featuring zero energy-consumption, simple synthesis, large-scale production, and low-cost and environmentally friendly water solvent. While a [Sn2S6]4âˆ’ complex is used for growing CZTS nanocrystals, a novel [Sn2S4Se2]4âˆ’ complex is designed for synthesizing CZTSSe nanocrystals. Utilization of the complexes balances the reaction rates between metal cations and chalcogen anions in aqueous solution, and consequently overcomes the big challenges of easy formation of binary impurities. The as-prepared CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals possess a kesterite structure with sizes of 4â€“10 nm. They show remarkable room-temperature PL properties, and yield a shift of an emission peak from 832 nm for CZTS to 838 nm for CZTSSe owing to the Se incorporation. The CZTS and CZTSSe nanocrystals have been further employed as promising light-harvesting materials for PEC water splitting. The ZnO/CZTS and ZnO/CZTSSe photoanodes exhibit optimal ABPEs of 2.80% and 3.43%, respectively. This synthetic strategy in current work could be extended to prepare other multinary chalcogenide nanocrystals at room temperature, which opens up a new door for synthesizing colloidal nanocrystals and fabricating low-cost and high-efficiency solar energy conversion devices.
