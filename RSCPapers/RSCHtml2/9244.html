Recent development on the synthesis, properties and applications of luminescent oxidized phenothiazine derivatives

Abstract

Oxidized phenothiazine chromophores have received significant attention in various technological applications including organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), photovoltaic devices, data storage, sensors and bioimaging due to their excellent photoluminescence properties, low toxicity, easy synthetic modification, non-planar structure and environmentally friendly behavior. The oxidized phenothiazine chromophores exhibit high phosphorescence quantum yields, better photochemical and photothermal stabilities, and long-lived emission lifetimes in the solid state. The oxidized phenothiazine derivatives possess room temperature phosphorescence properties which enhance the importance of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units and provide a new pathway for their multi-purpose applications like digital security, optical recording devices, bioimaging, sensors, organic light-emitting diodes, data storage, low-cost anti-counterfeiting technologies and temperature monitoring. In this review, we have explained the synthesis, photophysical, photoluminescence, electrochemical, theoretical and room temperature phosphorescence properties of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide derivatives. In the current situation, the outlook of this review provides a better understanding of the significance of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units for the development of novel materials for optoelectronic and biological applications.

1. Introduction

In the past few years, pushpull chromophores have received tremendous attention owing to their applications in solar cells, light-emitting diodes, sensors and data storage.114 The incorporation of heteroarene units in the pushpull chromophores further helps in tuning the frontier energy levels thereby improving their luminescence properties.1520 Phenothiazine is a tricyclic heteroarene with a unique butterfly shape and is widely used as a strong electron donor unit (p-type electron donor) due to the presence of electron-rich sulfur and nitrogen atoms.2125 A significant electron density in the phenothiazine unit is mainly localized over the sulfur atom, which makes the phenothiazine unit highly sensitive towards oxidizing agents.2530 The sulphur atom in the phenothiazine unit exists in the sulfide state (+2 oxidation) and can be easily oxidized to sulfoxide (+4 oxidation) and sulfone (+6 oxidation) as present in the phenothiazine 5-oxide unit and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide unit, respectively (Chart 1).3038 The change in oxidation state from sulfide (+2) to sulfoxide (+4) and sulfone (+6) in the phenothiazine unit results in a drastic change in the electronic properties of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units. The S atom in the sulfide state renders the phenothiazine molecule as a strong electron donor, whereas the S atom in the oxidized sulfoxide (+4) and sulfone (+6) states results in a strong electron-withdrawing behavior.37,38 Due to the increase in the oxidation state from sulfide (+2) to sulfoxide (+4) and sulfone (+6), the polarity of these derivatives was improved compared to that of the phenothiazine unit. When the sulfur atom of the phenothiazine unit was oxidized to sulfoxide and sulfone states, the dihedral angle (the angle between the planes of the annulated benzo groups) slightly decreases but the structural architecture remains the same.29

Over the past few years, oxidized phenothiazine units (phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide) have emerged as an important class of luminophores due to their favorable photoluminescence, phosphorescence and room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) properties.3941 There are three active sides (3-, 7- and 10-positions) in both phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units (Fig. 1), which can be easily functionalized by synthetic modification and the incorporation of strong pushpull units to extend the -conjugation and tune the photonic properties.42,43

In recent years, phosphorescent materials have attracted considerable research interest in various applications such as phosphorescent organic light-emitting diodes, displays, molecular sensing, anti-counterfeiting and bio-imaging due to their flexible structure, light weight and low-cost production.5760 The phosphorescence properties were first identified in inorganic materials and some specific metal complexes; however, these inorganic materials are toxic in nature, insoluble in aqueous solutions and are costly.6164 In comparison to these inorganic materials, organic materials with phosphorescence properties are safer to the environment and inexpensive.6575 Phenothiazine oxide is used as an active material for organic phosphorescent materials, due to its high luminescence properties. Phenothiazine oxide derivatives also exhibit ultra-long organic phosphorescence (UOP) and persistent room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) properties.3943 This review provides a detailed analysis of the structural modification of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide luminogens by using various donor and acceptor units to modulate the photonic and photoluminescence properties. This review also explains the challenging research field, room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) shown by phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units, and their applications as hole transporting materials, OLEDs and biological materials.

2. Properties

2.1. Luminescence properties

The organic luminogens having high fluorescence and phosphorescence properties are considered as best emitter materials for OLEDs owing to the following properties: low-cost fabrication, less toxicity, high quantum yields, long excited-state lifetimes up to milliseconds and easy synthetic modification as compared to inorganic materials. Hetero atoms (B, N, S, P, etc.) as well as halo atoms (Cl, Br, I) based luminogens have been utilized as emitting materials for LEDs. These luminogens are used in optoelectronic devices, biosensors and many other applications. The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide substituted chromophores are considered as efficient materials for OLEDs due to their high quantum yields and long excited-state lifetimes. 2.2. Ultralong organic phosphorescence and room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) properties

In recent years, luminescent materials with efficient solid-state emissions are considered as active building blocks for the development of advanced materials for optoelectronic applications.81 The phosphorescence properties were normally identified in the solution state under inert conditions and cryogenic temperatures. However, in the last few years, room temperature phosphorescence properties have been identified in limited organic materials in the solid state. In 1978, Bilen et al. reported pure organic materials for the first time, which showed RTP properties.82 In the literature, few organic chromophores with features like hydrogen bonding, halo atom interactions and intermolecular stacking interactions showed phosphorescence properties to a greater extent.70 The inorganic materials and transition-metal complexes showed strong phosphorescence behavior with long lifetimes up to milliseconds due to the presence of the dp bond; however, it would be a real challenge to incorporate the dp bond in organic chromophores to explore the phosphorescence properties.83 To confirm this postulate, the organic chromophores with phenothiazine units (shown in Fig. 3) were preferred as ideal models to develop several luminogen materials.

Li and co-workers have designed and synthesized a series of phenothiazine-5,5-dioxide-based luminogens which showed room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) properties.85 They investigated in detail the molecular structures of these luminogens and explained the persistent RTP properties through strong interactions in the solid state. The phenothiazine-5,5-dioxide-based luminogens 2834 were synthesized by the CN coupling reaction followed by the oxidation reaction in the presence of hydrogen peroxide which is shown in Scheme 11. The photophysical and PL studies explained that the luminogens 2834 showed three absorption bands around 275 nm, 300 nm, and 330 nm and two emission peaks at 390 nm and 405 nm, respectively. In the luminogens 2834, the substitution of strong electron-withdrawing substituents (Br, Cl, and F atoms) at the 10-position in the phenyl ring would reduce the electron density of the phenyl ring via inductive effects and stabilize the T1* state by enhancing the interactions in-between the neighboring units while other luminogens (28, 29 and 30) with electron-donating substituents (CH3, H and CH3O) exhibited more repulsion. The RTP effect of luminogens 2834 reveals that the luminogens of 31, 32 and 33 showed longest RTP lifetimes of 268 ms, 256 ms and 410 ms, whereas luminogens 28 and 29 with electron-donating substituents exhibited the shortest RTP lifetimes of 88 ms and 96 ms, as shown in Fig. 5. The luminogen 34 showed no RTP effect in the single crystal, although it contains the trifluoromethyl unit as the stronger electron-withdrawing unit attached to the 10-phenyl ring.

Li and co-workers have also reported five organic luminogens 3540 and explored their RTP properties.86 The luminogens 3540 (Chart 3) were synthesized by the CN coupling reaction, followed by the oxidation reaction in the presence of hydrogen peroxide as shown in Scheme 12. The RTP studies of all these derivatives explained that the odd alkyl substituent derivatives 35 (245 ms), 37 (327 ms) and 39 (205 ms) exhibited longer RTP lifetimes compared to those of even alkyl substituent derivatives 36 (92 ms) and 38 (181 ms) shown in Fig. 6. Their photophysical studies demonstrated that each luminogen exhibited similar absorption spectra in THF solution with three major bands at 270 nm, 295 nm and 335 nm due to the similar electronic structure. Furthermore, there were two major emission peaks observed around 390 nm and 405 nm for each luminogen in the phosphorescence studies. The RTP studies explained that each luminogen showed a small variation, in the range of 246273 ms, in the solution state. However, in the case of solid state, the RTP emission bands and the corresponding lifetimes were completely different from each other. The experimental results explained that the molecular packing in the solid state is a key factor to alter the RTP properties and excluded the role of various substituent effects on electronic properties. The single-crystal analysis provided more details about the importance of alkyl substituents on molecular packing and how it alters the RTP behavior through the oddeven effect. In the single-crystal analysis, it was revealed that the interactions, distances and overlapping are the major factors in molecular packing. The odd alkyl-substituted chromophores 35 and 37 showed better molecular arrangements with strong interactions between the two neighboring phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide groups and the distances between the neighboring units were as short as 3.59 and 3.70 , which confirmed their longest RTP lifetimes of 245 ms and 327 ms. Due to this close molecular packing in 35 and 37, their triplet exciton may be stabilized in the ultralong RTP effect. In the case of even alkyl substituents 36 (92 ms) and 38 (181 ms), disordered molecular arrangements were observed compared to odd alkyl substituents 35 and 37. The interactions are the important factor to tune the ultralong RTP properties. Luminogen 37 was used in in vitro and in vivo studies due to its stronger RTP emission compared to others and clearly visible in living mice with a low background noise, which is a great plus point of organic luminogens used in in vivo imaging due to their higher ultralong lifetimes. These RTP properties explained that without introducing any donor and acceptor units in the -conjugated system of luminophores, the RTP lifetimes can be easily altered by varying the alkyl units on the N-position.

2.3. Perovskite solar cells

The organicinorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells have evolved as renewable and alternative energy sources. In the past few years, perovskite solar cells have gained major attention due to the increase in power conversion efficiencies from 3.8% to 24.2%.89 The power conversion efficiencies were enhanced by introducing new hole transporting materials in the perovskite solar cells. Recently, in the perovskite solar cells, Spiro-OMeTAD and poly[bis(4-phenyl)(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)amine] (PTAA) are most commonly used as active materials due to their high hole transporting properties.90,91 However, the large-scale production of both derivatives, Spiro-OMeTAD and PTAA, is not cost-effective. To overcome this limitation, it is necessary to develop a new derivative which is highly efficient compared to Spiro-OMeTAD and PTAA and is of low cost. More recently, phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide was used as an n-type building block in the donoracceptor systems due to its electron-withdrawing properties. Phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide unit is an electron-deficient tricyclic heteroarene with a non-planar structure due to the presence of powerful sulfone heteroatom. In the literature, 3-,7- and N-positions of the phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide can be easily functionalized by substituting the various donor and acceptor units to tune the chemical and photophysical properties and the HOMOLUMO energy levels. Phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide can be easily synthesized by the one-step oxidation of phenothiazine (PTZ) (6$ per kg) with hydrogen peroxide.

2.4. OLEDs and TADF OLEDs

In 1987, Tang et al. reported the organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) by utilizing the metal complex of 8-hydroxyquinoline aluminum (Alq3) as a fluorescent material.92 Due to the presence of fluorescent emission properties in Alq3 materials, 75% of the triplet excitons could not be utilized for organic light-emitting diodes and only 25% of the singlet excitons can be used for fluorescent OLEDs which means that the theoretically calculated maximum internal quantum efficiency (IQE) of a fluorescent OLED is 25%. To overcome these drawbacks, phosphorescent metal complexes have been introduced as novel luminescent materials for organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs) due to their 100% internal quantum efficiency (IQE) which is four times better than that of fluorescent materials (25%). Phosphorescent heavy metal complexes Ru(II), Ir(III), Os(II), and Pt(II) were used as promising materials in PhOLEDs due to their highly emissive properties, better stability, excellent electroluminescence efficiency and a good phosphorescence quantum yield (p).93 However, these heavy metals have the lowest abundance on earth and are expensive and toxic. Therefore, in recent years, pure organic phosphorescent materials are introduced as new alternatives for OLEDs and TADF OLEDs due to their important benefits compared to fluorescent materials.94

Important benefits of RTP materials:

(1) Easy synthetic modification.

(2) Higher phosphorescence quantum yields.

(3) Easy tuning of the photonic and electronic properties by using the pushpull systems in the -conjugated system.

(4) RTP materials show a longer decay lifetime up to milliseconds to seconds compared to fluorescent materials (micro or nanoseconds).

(5) Phosphorescence spectra show a red-shift compared to fluorescence spectra because T1 showed a lower energy level compared to S1.

(6) RTP materials show a broad Stokes shift in phosphorescence spectra as compared to fluorescence spectra due to the intersystem crossing between two different spin states (S1 and T1).

(7) RTP materials showed excellent phosphorescence properties with a longer decay lifetime in the solid state at room temperature, while no phosphorescence was observed in the solution state.

The phenothiazine oxides (sulfoxides and sulfones) are recently used as active luminescent materials in OLEDs due to their high quantum yields, reverse intersystem crossing (RISC) and better singlettriplet exciton annihilation (STA) and triplettriplet exciton annihilation (TTA). Su and co-workers have designed and synthesized two bipolar isomeric chromophores 44 and 45, in which phenanthro[9,10-d]imidazole was used as the electron-withdrawing unit and phenothiazine dioxide was used as the electron-donating unit (Chart 5). In the solid state, 44 and 45 exhibited three absorption bands in the UV-vis region at 270 nm, 353 nm, 370 nm and 259 nm, 339 nm, 368 nm, respectively. The PL spectra of both derivatives 44 and 45 exhibited single emission at 510 nm and 508 nm in the solution state, respectively. The isomeric chromophores show quite similar emission spectra. The optical band gaps estimated for 44 and 45 are 2.98 eV and 2.93 eV, respectively.

2.5. Biosensors

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are present in mitochondria, which play a crucial role in the elimination of unwanted xenobiotics. However, excess amount of ROS is hazardous to mitochondria due to the undesirable oxidation of biomolecules. In the ROS, the hydroxyl radical (OH) and hypochlorite ion ClO behave as highly reactive species.98,99 They cause serious damage to many biomolecules such as proteins, nucleic acids and lipids due to their strong oxidant properties. The excessive amount of OH and ClO can damage the cell and lead to various diseases. Due to this reason, reactive oxygen species are considered a serious concern owing to their high reactivity. Thus, the detection of OH and ClO species is highly essential to avoid the serious damage of cells. Many organic derivatives are used to identify the OH and ClO species through sensitive and selective methods. Phenothiazine-substituted chromophores are mostly used for the identification of OH and ClO species because phenothiazine is a heteroatom-based donor unit which contains sulfur and nitrogen atoms, which are electron-rich in nature. Due to this reason, the maximum electron cloud density of the phenothiazine unit is mainly concentrated over the sulfur atom. The sulphur atom in the thiazine ring is highly sensitive towards the OH and ClO species. Xu and coworkers have synthesized a phenothiazine-substituted cyanine chromophore 55,100 which was used for the detection of the mitochondrial OH in live cells. Cyanine derivative 55 was used as a ratiometric absorbant as well as a fluorescent turn-on sensor for the identification of OH species (Scheme 20). The photophysical studies explained that the cyanine derivative 55 exhibited two absorption bands at 380 and 550 nm in water.

3. Conclusion and outlook

The phenothiazine oxide derivatives, namely, phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide derivatives, have emerged as an important class of luminogens in organic electronics due to their vast array of structural modifications. The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide derivatives have major reactive sites of 3-, 7- and N-positions which allow several synthetic modifications to develop phenothiazine oxide-based pushpull systems which can be employed in a wide range of optoelectronic devices. The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide derivatives serve as suitable candidates for various optoelectronic applications due to their desirable optical properties such as broad absorption with high molar extinction coefficients, high phosphorescence quantum yields with long lifetimes up to milliseconds, and better photochemical and photothermal stabilities. In this review, we have successfully addressed the advancements in the design, synthesis, luminescence properties and applications of phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide chromophores from 2010 to date. The achievements of oxidized phenothiazine derivatives in various applications are as follows:

The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units exhibit high quantum yields in the solution and solid states and long excited-state lifetimes which make them suitable candidates for OLEDs.

The excellent phosphorescence properties of phenothiazine dioxide chromophores enable further development of PhOLEDs, which is beneficial in increasing the IQE above 25%. The phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide derivatives show better electroluminescence in terms of lifetime, brightness and efficiency for blue, green and yellow thermally activated delayed fluorescent (TADF) OLEDs, due to their outstanding electron transporting ability and high phosphorescence quantum yields in the solid state.

The unique non-planar shape of the phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide can lock the intramolecular motions thereby suppressing the non-radiative decay and render the materials with AIDF properties which can further improve the external quantum efficiency of OLEDs.

The electron-deficient nature of the sulfone unit in phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide exhibits efficient charge carrier mobility and its nonplanar structure makes it an excellent hole transporting material for perovskite solar cells.

The electron-rich hetero atom in the -conjugated backbone of the thiazine ring possesses a unique character which allows the detection of OH and ClO species thereby protecting the body cells from serious damages and various diseases.

The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units exhibit room temperature phosphorescence (RTP) which allows the researchers to develop novel derivatives for the new generation-based advanced technological applications including digital security, optical recording devices, bioimaging, sensors, organic light-emitting diodes, data storage, low-cost anti-counterfeiting technologies and temperature monitoring. The interactions, molecular packing in the solid state and oddeven effect of alkyl substituents are important factors to tune the ultralong RTP properties.

The phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units also exhibit some unique properties such as photoinduced room temperature phosphorescence and mechanochromic behavior observed in copper metal amide complexes.

Despite several progress in various applications, the phenothiazine 5-oxide and phenothiazine 5,5-dioxide units face various challenges, which could be addressed by continuous research by the scientific community. Oxidized phenothiazine derivatives show excellent performance as OLEDs, NLOs, sensors, HTMs, and PSCs; however, only limited derivatives have been reported to date. The N-positions of oxidized phenothiazine derivatives have been mainly functionalized by mild acceptor units like haloarenes and carbonyl-substituted derivatives; however 3-, 7- and N-positions have not been much explored. The incorporation of strong electron-withdrawing units like benzothiadiazole (BTD), 1,8-napthalimide (NPI), perylene diimide (PDI), diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) and benzothiazole at the 3-, 7- and N-positions of oxidized phenothiazine derivatives may further enhance the RTP properties and provide a new pathway for the design and synthesis of materials for NLOs, sensors, PSCs, as well as chromophores for ultrafast charge transfer. The oxidized phenothiazine derivatives reported in the literature are highly polar in nature and mostly soluble in high polar solvents such as ethyl acetate or DMSO which makes their purification a tedious process.24 The solubility of oxidized phenothiazine derivatives can be improved by using long or branched alkyl chains at N-positions. The incorporation of different alkyl chains further affects the molecular packing which can help in modulating the solid-state emission properties. The easy synthetic modification, excellent electron-withdrawing properties, high quantum yields and RTP properties of phenothiazine oxide make it a potential core for the development of various pushpull systems and open up new avenues for the development of various optoelectronic devices. In summary, the excellent performances in various applications will offer a great platform for the development of new derivatives.
