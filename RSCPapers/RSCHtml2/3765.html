Abstract

Green synthesis approaches are gaining significance as promising routes for the sustainable preparation of nanoparticles, offering reduced toxicity towards living organisms and the environment. Nanomaterials produced by green synthesis approaches can offer additional benefits, including reduced energy inputs and lower production costs than traditional synthesis, which bodes well for commercial-scale production. The biomolecules and phytochemicals extracted from microbes and plants, respectively, are active compounds that function as reducing and stabilizing agents for the green synthesis of nanoparticles. Microorganisms, such as bacteria, yeasts, fungi, and algae, have been used in nanomaterials’ biological synthesis for some time. Furthermore, the use of plants or plant extracts for metal and metal-based hybrid nanoparticle synthesis represents a novel green synthesis approach that has attracted significant research interest. This review discusses various biosynthesis approaches via microbes and plants for the green preparation of metal and metal oxide nanoparticles and provides insights into the molecular aspects of the synthesis mechanisms and biomedical applications. The use of agriculture waste as a potential bioresource for nanoparticle synthesis and biomedical applications of biosynthesized nanoparticles is also discussed.

Introduction

Nanomaterials and nanoparticles possess unique properties that can be tailored to obtain new and modified functionalities compared to their bulk counterparts. Nanoparticles exhibit novel and improved properties based on their nanoscale dimensions, molecular distribution, and morphology. Their smaller size and unusual characteristics gained attention for use in chip technology, and the capability to fine-tune the nanomaterial's properties offers broad applications in optoelectronics, photovoltaic, biomedical, waste water treatment,25 and thermo-electric fields.36,42 Furthermore, semiconductor quantum dots showcase the impressive progress of nanomaterials in the field of electronics.54 These nanomaterials are intermediaries between molecules and particulates with an extensive range of elemental and material compositions, such as organics, inorganics, and hybrid structures, along with excellent surface functionality. Furthermore, nanomaterials are synthesized with relatively less raw materials, lower cost, and less energy than micro-sized or bulk materials.65

Researchers are widely focusing on preparing noble metal nanoparticles, as they can exhibit improved physical and chemical properties due to their reduced size, extensive surface area, quantum size effect, and electronic configuration.78 Common noble metals, such as silver, gold, platinum, and palladium, retain characteristic non-interaction features with other elements, and their modification to nanosized improves their properties. Moreover, the properties of nanosized metal particles can be fine-tuned by controlling parameters such as size, shape, composition, and surface functionalization. Alterations in the nanoparticles’ size will lead to distinct optical, chemical, magnetic, and electrical properties that are beneficial for several applications.87 It is noteworthy that an atom at the surface of a nanoparticle exhibits improved properties compared with the properties of an atom on the surface of the bulk material. The unique properties shown by the atoms at different crystal locations in a nanoparticle are due to their surface or quantum effects.94 Thus, several novel nanomaterials are being developed by researchers with new properties and applications.

In general, nanoparticles and nanomaterials are synthesized via smart techniques that utilize hazardous solvents and toxic chemicals, such as pyrolysis, the sol–gel technique, chemical vapor deposition, etching, and the supercritical fluid approach.101 Even though nanoparticles are extensively beneficial for several applications, high energy consumption, the huge cost of production, the use of toxic chemicals, and the formation of undesirable by-products are some of the limitations, while considering a physical or chemical approach for nanoparticle synthesis.65 Hence, a synthesis approach that can diminish hazardous reaction and waste generation is inevitable to convert nanoparticles from the laboratory to commercial products. Numerous methodologies have been developed for the natural and cost-effective synthesis of nanomaterials without the use of harmful chemical solvents.101 Recently, green protocols have gained significance as a novel route to the preparation of nanoparticles to reduce synthesis approach-mediated toxicity towards living organisms and the environment. Green synthesis approaches for nanomaterial preparation are highly promising as they promote the use of non-toxic and renewable materials and efficient and reduced energy consumption compared with traditional synthesis approaches that rely on harmful chemicals and solvents, such as sodium borohydride or hydrazine.116 In green synthesis methods, water is mostly used as the solvent along with renewable and eco-friendly materials as reducing and stabilizing agents.120 The biomolecules and phytochemicals extracted from microbes and plants, respectively, are extensively utilized biological reducing and stabilizing agents for the green synthesis of nanoparticles.124 Extensive use of microorganisms, such as bacteria, yeasts, fungi, and algae, are involved in the biological synthesis of nanomaterials with minimum contamination. Furthermore, the use of plants or plant extracts for metal and metal based hybrid nanoparticle synthesis is currently a brand of new research focus that has gained wide acceptance.134,135 Thus, the aim of this article is to provide an overview of several biosynthesis approaches via microbes and plants for the green preparation of metal and metal oxide nanoparticles as well as to provide an insight into the synthesis mechanism. In addition, the use of agricultural waste as a source of biomass for nanoparticle synthesis and potential applications and opportunities are discussed.

Biosynthesis of metal and metal oxide nanoparticles

In general, the term ‘biosynthesis’ refers to the synthesis of a particle via biological organisms (either dead or alive) or their extracts as reducing, capping, and stabilizing agents.141 The biosynthesis of metal and metal oxide nanoparticles is widely employed as a green synthesis and bottom-up approach, as the demand for these nanoparticles is high and offers an environmentally-friendly synthesis approach. Biosynthesis as a bottom-up approach leads to the formation of nanoparticles by utilizing biomolecules as reducing and stabilizing agents to convert the precursor into ions. Later, the ions are transformed into atoms, followed by nucleation and growth processes to form nanoparticles.147 For instance, gold nanoparticles, which are commonly synthesized using toxic chemicals, such as sodium borohydride151 and trialanine phosphine152 as reducing and capping agents, can yield smaller sized nanoparticles with toxic by-products.155 The by-products can cause environmental toxicity during the synthesis and purification of gold nanoparticles.159 Furthermore, it can be noted that gold nanoparticles that are synthesized via conventional and biosynthesis approaches are toxic in nature at a threshold concentration or dose, as they can induce the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in live cells and inhibit their growth.162 Hence, physically and chemically synthesized nanoparticles possess toxic chemicals on their surface, whereas biosynthesized nanoparticles possess functional capping biomolecules. Thus, nanoparticles synthesized via conventional approaches must be purified to reduce the potentially toxic and hazardous functional groups on their surface, especially for biomedical applications,165 a challenging, time-consuming, and costly process. Once the biosynthesized nanoparticles possess biomolecules as surface functional groups, they also possess certain biological properties that reduce their critically toxic nature.167 Furthermore, the production cost is lower for the biomolecule-mediated green synthesis of nanoparticles due to the ease of availability of raw materials (especially plants) and non-requirement of additional purification processes, compared with conventional approaches.168

In several cases, the biosynthesis approach has been proven to improve the biological properties of metal and metal oxide nanoparticles, in addition to reducing toxicity.169 The bioavailability; biocompatibility (compatible with biological systems – humans, microbes, animals, and plants); and cell internalization properties, which helps to cross the blood–brain barrier, of metallic nanoparticles can also be improved via the biosynthesis approach.170

The biosynthesis of metallic nanoparticles is broadly classified as microbe and plant-mediated (phyto-) synthesis methods.172 In both these methods, live and dead organisms can be utilized for nanoparticle synthesis. Microbes and plants possess the enhanced potential to reduce complex bulk materials (metal precursors) to simpler forms by reducing their sizes (top-down approach) or assemble metal ions in solution (bottom-up approach) and utilize them as nutrients.173 This characteristic of live microbes and plants is highly beneficial for the synthesis of nanoparticles. However, in most cases, this strategy is not recommended as the nanoparticles will be formed within the organism (intracellular) and must be isolated via several tedious and expensive processes.174 Furthermore, bioaccumulation of nanoparticles during this process may lead to toxicity in the organism, cause an unfavorable reaction, and inhibit their growth175 or even result in death. Again, the growth of microbes will be inhibited for the extraction of specific metabolites, which will be utilized for nanoparticle preparation.176 Likewise, plants from agricultural waste were also considered potential biomolecule sources for nanoparticle biosynthesis.24 However, the biomolecules will not be stable after the organism's death and start to degrade immediately, which will lead to the improper formation of nanoparticles.177 Thus, a mixture of biomolecules, such as enzymes, proteins, and peptides extracted from microbes and phytochemicals from plants, is widely used to synthesize metallic nanoparticles with distinct morphologies,178 as shown in Fig. 1. In addition, this approach also proved to be beneficial for the synthesis of metallic nanoparticles with distinct morphologies in recent times.179 It has recently been identified that the process parameters, such as temperature, pH, mixing ratio, incubation period, and aeration in the biosynthesis approach, can facilitate the formation of nanoparticles with distinct morphologies.9,180

Metal nanoparticles

Gold, silver, copper, and platinum are the most common nanosized metallic nanoparticles that are widely synthesized via microbial approaches.183 In addition, the possibility of forming other nanoparticles, such as iron, zinc, silicon, and rare earth metals, via microbes was also demonstrated.184 It is noteworthy that spherically shaped metallic nanoparticles are yielded via extracellular microbial synthesis, in most cases, as the biomolecules reduce the metals, engulf them to stabilize them and prevent agglomeration.185 Later, thermal, acid hydrolysis or solvent mediated centrifugation treatments will be used to eliminate surface bound biomolecules and yield pure nanoparticles.186

Gold nanoparticles

Gold is the most commonly biosynthesized metal nanoparticles due to its wide range of biological applications. However, bacterial synthesis of gold nanoparticles is not recommended due to the tedious purification processes required as the binding affinity of the bacterial metabolites is high towards gold ions. Thus, few attempts to synthesize smaller gold nanoparticles for specific biological applications were made in recent times. Patil et al. (2019) prepared ∼21 nm sized gold particles via extracellular, cell-free supernatant extracted from a marine bacteria named Paracoccus haeundaensis sp. nov. BC74171T.187 The study revealed that extracellular synthesis yielded polydisperse spherical gold nanoparticles with primary amine, amide, nitro compounds, aromatics, and alcohol or carboxylic acids as their surface functional groups. These results indicated that the gold nanoparticles formed due to the synergistic effect of enzymes, proteins, and metabolites in the supernatant. Furthermore, these biosynthesized gold nanoparticles are reported to possess antioxidant activity with an antiproliferative property against several human cancer cell lines.30 Similarly, sulfite reductase enzyme with a molecular weight of 116 kDa was purified from Gram-negative Escherichia coli (E. coli) bacteria and utilized to synthesize spherical ∼10 nm sized gold particles with high stability. These nanoparticles exhibited substantial antifungal properties against a wide range of fungal strains without any cytotoxicity towards the kidney epithelial Vero cell line from the African green monkey (Chlorocebus sp.) and human epithelial type-2 (Hep-2) cell lines at a concentration of 0.31 to 10%.44

Fungi and algae are common microbes utilized to prepare a wide variety of gold nanoparticles. Fungal extract mediated biosynthesis of metallic nanoparticles is considered as the most successful approach due to the smaller nanoparticles yielded in a short reaction time. The fungal extracts of Aspergillus japonica,188Basidiomycete lentinus edodes,189 and Hypocrea lixii and intracellular phenol-oxidizing enzymes, such as manganese peroxidases, laccases, and tyrosinases, are widely used for the biosynthesis of gold nanoparticles.190 Recently, Molnár et al. (2018) demonstrated that nanosized gold particles could be synthesized using the autolysates and extracellular as well as intracellular fractions extracted from 29 thermophilic fungi. The study showed that the size of the nanoparticles was in the range of 6–40 nm with 30–70% size distribution, depending on the synthesis parameters and fungal strain. Furthermore, it has been identified that the biological reducing agent responsible for the formation of gold nanoparticles is less than 3 kDa, which may be a monosaccharide, cofactors, antibiotics, amino acids and other molecules like melanin, whereas the stabilizing agent is above 3 kDa, which can be a biopolymer or protein.16 Similarly, Qu et al. (2020) synthesized novel rod-shaped gold nanoparticles with the help of filamentous Aspergillus species WL-Au fungi that were induced by lead (Pb+) ions. The study stated that the rod-shaped gold nanoparticles were in the minimum size range of 24–31 nm (length) and 5.1–7.3 nm (width) and maximum of above 100 nm (length) and 20.5–49.4 nm (width). Apart from lead ion induction, the presence of aliphatic acids, phenols, and primary and secondary amines, especially the amine II group, has been identified as being responsible for the synthesis of rod-shaped gold nanoparticles in this study.37

In recent times, algal extracts were also extensively employed for the biosynthesis of gold nanoparticles. Khan et al. (2019) listed the benefits of algae and blue–green algae in the synthesis of gold nanoparticles for specific biomedical applications. The article emphasized that the presence of fucoidans (a type of sulphated polysaccharide) in marine Cladosiphon okamuranus and Kjellamaniella crassifolia algae, along with optimized synthesis parameters, were responsible for the formation of gold nanoparticles. The presence of exclusive biomolecules in algae has been identified to yield spherical, hexagonal, triangular, octahedral, diamond-shaped, and polycrystalline gold nanoparticles.13 Furthermore, the cyanobacteria Anabaena sphaerica was recently used for the biosynthesis of polydisperse nanoparticles of 1–50 nm in size with shapes, such as hexagonal, spherical, triangular, and irregular. The study confirmed that the chloroplast and thylakoids present in the cyanobacteria were responsible for forming nanosized particles.191 Moreover, Lyngbya majuscule cyanobacteria has been utilized as a factory to prepare ∼42 nm sized gold particles. In this study, the presence of protein shells as the surface functional group around the resulting gold nanoparticles proved to be responsible for nanoparticle formation.28

Silver nanoparticles

Silver nanoparticles are widely synthesized using biosynthesis approaches due to their excellent antimicrobial properties. Several recent studies showed that, with bacterial cell-free supernatant, intracellular and extracellular approaches using both Gram-positive and negative bacterial strains were beneficial for silver nanoparticle preparation. Saravanan et al. (2018) recently prepared 41–68 nm sized, spherical, silver nanoparticles using the cell-free extract of Gram-positive, spore-forming, aerobic Bacillus brevis (NCIM 2533) bacteria. The study showed that the bacterial extract proteins were responsible for nanoparticle formation by acting as reducing, capping, and stabilizing agents.57 Likewise, Javaid et al. (2018) listed several bacterial strains used to synthesize silver nanoparticles. The prepared nanoparticles are in the size range of 2–100 nm with morphologies such as spherical, cuboid, fiber, oval, hexagonal, cubic, triangular, rod, and disc shaped. The article emphasized that the proteins in the bacterial cell wall or soluble enzymes were responsible for the formation of silver nanoparticles via the bacterial extracellular synthesis approach. Also, the silver ions are internalized into the bacterial cell, depending on the medium and growth factors, facilitated by membrane proteins and this leads to the formation of nanosized silver particles, which can later be recovered by bacterial cell lysis and ultra-sonication.79

Like bacteria, fungi and algae are extensively utilized to manufacture biogenic silver nanoparticles to be beneficial for biomedical applications, especially as an effective and non-toxic antimicrobial agent. Mistry et al. (2020) recently synthesized spherical, polydisperse and 6–16 nm sized silver nanoparticles via cell-free filtrate obtained from marine Aspergillus brunneoviolaceus fungi. The specific biomolecules in the fungal extract with functional groups such as nitro or sulfur compounds, alcohols, phenols, alkenes, amines, aliphatic amines, and alkynes serve as capping or stabilizing agents for nanoparticle synthesis.49 Furthermore, Casagrande et al. (2019) listed several distinct silver nanoparticle types prepared via a fungi-mediated biosynthesis approach. In this article, the author emphasized that enzymes, such as nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) and NADH-dependent nitrate reductase, and biomolecules, namely, quinones and anthraquinones, were significant as reducing, capping, and stabilizing agents in the extracellular fungal extract mediated biosynthesis of silver nanoparticles.192

Algal mediated biogenic silver nanoparticle synthesis is considered to be the most environmentally friendly approach, as it is simple and similar to plant-mediated nanoparticle synthesis. Arya et al. (2019) synthesized 40–90 nm sized silver nanoparticles with mixed morphologies of spheres, cubes, and truncated triangles using aqueous, green, Botryococcus braunnii algal extract. The study demonstrated via FTIR spectra that the formation of silver nanoparticles was due to the presence of long-chain fatty acids, amides, proteins, and polysaccharides.38 Similarly, Fatima et al. (2020) synthesized 70–75 nm sized, spherical silver nanoparticles via extracts from the powdered red alga Portieria hornemanni collected from Rameshwaram (Gulf of Mannar), India.53 The study revealed that proteins, carbohydrates and amino acids present in the extract were responsible for algal-mediated silver nanoparticle biosynthesis. In addition, the extracts of cyanobacteria, such as Desertifilum species (isolated from soil, Egypt) and Microchaete NCCU-342 (cultured in India), were also recently used for the synthesis of 4.5–26 nm and 60–80 nm sized spherical silver nanoparticles, respectively. Both studies demonstrated that the formation of silver nanoparticles was due to the proteins and polysaccharides that existed in the extract and highly dependent on the synthesis parameters.55,56

Other metal nanoparticles

Apart from gold and silver, nanosized metal particles, such as palladium, copper, and iron, were also widely prepared via microbes. However, the specific biomedical applications of these nanoparticles are still under research and are not widely used for commercial applications compared with gold and silver nanoparticles. Recently, dendritic shaped, 20–40 nm sized palladium nanoparticles were synthesized by using the bacterial biomass (intracellular) of Cupriavidus metallidurans strain CH34 after fixing them with formate or glutaraldehyde. The synthesized nanoparticles were identified in the cytoplasm, where the metabolites, such as pyruvate, formate, and lactate, act as electron donors for nanoparticle formation.85 Similarly, Citrobacter bacterial species extracted from wastewater sludge (via intracellular approach) were used to prepare palladium nanoparticles with 11–16 nm crystallite size. The nanoparticles were formed at 30 °C, pH 6, and under anaerobic conditions with sodium formate as an electron donor. This study emphasized that the complex enzymes in the bacterial cells were beneficial for the formation of nanoparticles.93 Likewise, Arya et al. (2020) synthesized platinum nanoparticles using aqueous Botryococcus braunii green alga extract, collected from a lake, with an average particle size of 86.96 nm and monodispersity in nature. The FTIR results showed that the synergistic effect of polysaccharides, proteins, long-chain fatty acids, and amides present in the algal extract was responsible for the formation of monodisperse, nanosized platinum particles.69

Another exclusive type of nanoparticle comprises copper, which has been widely synthesized in recent times via microbial extracts for several biomedical applications. Lv et al. (2018) utilized Gram-negative, marine Shewanella loihica PV-4, which was isolated from the Loihi Seamount hydrothermal vent in the Pacific Ocean, to synthesize 10–16 nm sized, polycrystalline and spherical copper nanoparticles. The study demonstrated that electron transfer via cytochrome c with a metal reductase-containing locus for heavy metal reduction, the existence of organic acids, vitamins, and electron-donating NADH/NADPH via soluble proteins were responsible for copper nanoparticle formation.102 Furthermore, endophytic actinomycetes isolated from seaweeds were used recently to synthesize furry clustered copper nanoparticles below 50 nm in size. This study emphasized that the presence of metal-resistant genes, peptides, proteins, organic materials, and reducing cofactors was responsible for reducing copper ions and nanosized copper formation.89 Moreover, magnetotactic bacteria are widely used for the synthesis of iron nanoparticles known as magnetosomes. These Gram-negative motile bacteria usually grow near iron ores or soils with high iron content and possess the ability to synthesize magnetic iron nanoparticles (iron oxides) via an intracellular approach. Thus, it is considered a natural process of iron nanoparticle formation, where environmental conditions such as iron geocycling, geographic localization, polyphyletic diversity, and magnetotaxis of the bacteria play a crucial role. In addition, recent studies suggested that magnetosome-associated membrane (MAM) proteins encoded by the magnetosome gene island (MAI) were responsible for the formation of iron nanoparticles in magnetosomes.193 In addition, the biomolecular extracts of fungal species, such as Trichoderma species and Rhizopus stolonifera, were used recently to form iron nanoparticles below 100 nm in size. The presence of carboxyl, alkene, phenol, sulfhydryl, and mercaptan (alcohol and phenol) groups in the fungal extract as capping, stabilizing, and reducing agents has been identified as being beneficial for the formation of iron nanoparticles.74,75 Besides, several types of algae, such as macro-algae (Dictyota dicotoma) and microalgae (Chloroccum and Chlorella species), are widely used for the formation of smaller (20–80 nm) sized iron nanoparticles. It has been revealed in several studies that the amine and carbonyl groups of glycoproteins and carbohydrates of algal extracts possess the ability to reduce iron ions into zero valent iron nanoparticles.98

Metal oxide nanoparticles

Like metals, metal oxides, such as zinc, copper, iron, magnesium, and cerium oxides, have been widely synthesized via microbial extracts. It is noteworthy that the synthesis of metal oxide nanoparticles via microbial extracts is easier compared with metal nanoparticles, as the biomolecules in the extract can act as an oxygen donor,194 along with their capping, stabilizing, and metal-reducing properties.195 Hence, metal oxide nanoparticles are more extensively synthesized via a microbial approach than nanosized metals for biomedical applications.

Zinc oxide

Zinc oxide is an exclusive metal oxide nanoparticle that can be prepared by microbial extracts, such as bacteria, fungi, and algae, as shown in Fig. 2 for specific biomedical applications. Yusof et al. (2020) utilized Lactobacillus plantarum strain TA4, which was isolated from local fermented food to form monodisperse zinc oxide (ZnO) nanoparticles. The study showed that biomolecular components with carboxyl and amide groups secreted by the bacterial cell wall were responsible for facilitating nanoparticle formation.107 Likewise, the broth culture of Gram-negative, rod-shaped Pseudomonas putida soil bacteria has been utilized recently to synthesize spherical, 25–45 nm sized (diameter) ZnO nanoparticles. The study showed that nanoparticle formation was facilitated by amino acids and carbohydrates present in the bacterial broth culture as stabilizing agents.112 Similarly, Sumanth et al. (2020) recently synthesized zinc oxide nanoparticles via Xylaria acuta, which is an endophytic fungi isolated from the leaves of Millingtonia hortensis. The resulting nanoparticles were identified to be 40–55 nm in size with a hexagonal shape; enhanced antibacterial activity against Gram-negative bacteria, such as E. coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa, as well as Gram-positive bacteria, namely, Staphylococcus aureus and Bacillus cereus; antifungal activity against Cladosporium cladosporioides, Aspergillus flavus, Fusarium oxysporum, and Phomopsis species; and anticancer activity against human MDA-MB 134 mammary gland carcinoma cell lines. The study revealed that nanoparticle formation was due to the presence of hydroxyl groups and polyphenols.77 Furthermore, Ganesan et al. (2020) demonstrated a novel sol–gel-based synthesis approach to produce zinc oxide nanoparticles via extracts of an endophytic fungi named Periconium species. The fungal extract led to the formation of 16–78 nm sized, quasi-spherical shaped nanoparticles with a high negative surface charge of −88.6 mV, which indicates their enhanced stability. The FTIR spectra revealed that the synergistic effect of alcohols, aldehydes, carboxylic acids, esters, hydroxyl primary amines and amide I from proteins present in the fungal extract were responsible for the formation of gel and nanoparticles.96

Algal extracts, which include both micro- and macroalgae, have also been used to synthesize zinc oxide nanoparticles. Hasan et al. (2021) revealed that zinc oxide nanoflowers could be synthesized with the help of Withania coagulans algal extract. The resulting nanoflowers were 360–550 nm in size with a hexagonal Wurtzite crystal structure, where the nanosized flowers were identified to be uniformly arranged as nano-petals that radiated out from the spherical pedicel to which they were adhered. FTIR spectral analysis showed that the presence of phenolic molecular groups in the extract promoted the reduction of zinc precursor to form nanoflowers, whereas the amide linkages and amino acids in the algal extract served as a stabilizing agent for nanoflower formation.111 Besides, Hamouda et al. (2020) recently utilized the green alga named Ulva fasciata to synthesize biogenic zinc oxide nanoparticles and compared their efficacy to inhibit infectious bacterial growth with the same nanoparticles synthesized via a wet chemical approach. The results showed that both synthesis approaches yielded flake-shaped particles, the algal extract yielded rod-shaped nanoparticles, and the chemical approach led to the formation of 10–14 nm sized spherically shaped nanoparticles. The FTIR spectra showed that nanoparticles from both synthesis methods possessed N–H, CC, CO, and NO2 functional groups. The study also confirmed that the zinc oxide nanoparticles synthesized via a biological approach were less toxic to albino rats and were proposed as a potential nanomedicine due to the presence of natural surface functional groups compared to chemically synthesized nanoparticles.117

Copper oxide

Similar to zinc, copper oxide nanoparticles were also extensively prepared via microbes, such as bacteria, fungi, and algae. It can be noted that there are only a few recent publications available that report on the synthesis of copper oxide nanoparticles via bacterial extracts. Kouhkan et al. (2020) recently synthesized copper oxide nanoparticles using nutrient broth containing a probiotic bacteria named Lactobacillus casei, subspecies casei. The results showed that synthesized nanoparticles were spherical in shape and 30–75 nm in diameter with high monodispersity. FTIR analysis revealed that the amine and amide linkages present in the membrane of bacteria and carbonyl groups from the peptides and amino acids from bacteria bound to the metal ions to reduce and stabilize them, leading to the formation of nanoparticles.118 Furthermore, Saravanakumar et al. (2019) demonstrated the biosynthesis of copper oxide nanoparticles via an aqueous cell-free extract of an indigenous fungus named Trichoderma asperellum. The existence of amide and aromatic groups in the secondary metabolites as reducing or encapsulating agents has been identified as being responsible for forming cubic face-centered, 10–190 nm sized and spherical nanoparticles.103 Similarly, El-Batal et al. (2020) utilized fungal filtrate solutions, Penicillium chrysogenum, along with gamma rays for the mycogenic synthesis of monodisperse, spherical copper oxide nanoparticles with a mean diameter of 9.70 nm. The study showed that hydrogen, N–H bonds and amide groups in the fungal filtrate helped aid the formation of copper oxide nanoparticles.108 Recently, Bhattacharya et al. (2019) utilized the aqueous algal extract of Anabena cylindrica for the synthesis of copper oxide nanoparticles. The resulting nanoparticles were identified to be of monoclinic crystalline phase, with rod-like morphology and 40–60 nm in size. The study also showed that the algal extract contained vibrational groups corresponding to polyols, such as polysaccharides, flavones, and terpenoids, responsible for nanoparticle formation.123

Similar to other metal oxide nanoparticles, magnetic iron oxide nanoparticles were also synthesized by several microbes. Fatemi et al. (2018) prepared novel superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles via extracellular metabolites (supernatant) of the extracts obtained from Bacillus cereus strain HMH1. The nanoparticles had an average size of 29.3 nm and spherical shape. FTIR analysis emphasized that the existence of carboxylic, primary amine, carbonyl, ester, and ether or ester covalent bonds as functional groups in the bacterial extract were responsible for nanoparticle formation.138 Furthermore, Chatterjee et al. (2020) demonstrated the synthesis of superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles using extracellular metabolite extracts of the manglicolous fungus named Aspergillus niger BSC-1 strain. The resulting nanoparticles were 20–40 nm in size with an elongated needle or flake-like morphology. FTIR spectra revealed the presence of hydrocarbons; carboxyl, amide II, and amide I bonds; and NADH-dependent hydrolase enzyme in the fungal extract as a reducing agent helped aid the formation of magnetic nanoparticles.114 Furthermore, Mahanty et al. (2019) isolated three filamentous fungi from the Sundarban mangrove ecosystem of India, namely, Phialemoniopsis ocularis, Trichoderma asperellum and Fusarium incarnatum, and utilized their cell filtrate with metabolites for the synthesis of magnetic iron oxide nanoparticles. The resulting nanoparticles were 25, 13.13, and 30.56 nm in size, obtained via the cell filtrate of T. asperellum, P. ocularis and F. incarnatum, with spherical morphology. FTIR analysis emphasized that the cell filtrate contained amide I and II bonds corresponding to the extracellular proteins as reducing agents for the formation of magnetic nanoparticles.119 Moreover, Salem et al. (2019) utilized aqueous brown and red seaweed (algae) extracts, such as Colpomenia sinuosa and Pterocladia capillacea, for the preparation of 11.24–33.71 and 16.85–22.47 nm sized magnetic iron oxide (Fe3O4) nanospheres, respectively. The FTIR results showed that the polysaccharides and hydroxy or sulfate groups present in the algal extract participated in the reduction of iron precursor and the formation of iron oxide nanoparticles.136

Other metal oxide nanoparticles

Apart from these more common metal oxide nanoparticles, other metal oxides, such as magnesium, titanium, cerium, and calcium, were recently synthesized using microbial extracts.

Magnesium oxide

Abdel-Aziz et al. (2020) recently utilized the cell filtrate of the endobacterium Burkholderia rinojensis for the biosynthesis of 26.7 nm sized magnesium oxide nanoparticles with spherical granular morphology. FTIR spectra showed that the bacterial cell filtrate contained proteins with methylene groups and aliphatic amino acid that acted as potential reducing and stabilizing agents for the formation of nanoparticles.144 Likewise, El-Sayyad et al. (2018) synthesized magnesium oxide nanoparticles using melanin pigment extracted from Penicillium chrysogenum fungi along with gamma-ray irradiation. The resulting nanoparticles were 5–12.8 nm in size with spherical morphology. FTIR analysis revealed that the fungal melanin contained functional groups corresponding to indolic, carboxylic, pyrrolic, and methane moieties and aromatic hydrogen–carbon bonds responsible for nanoparticle formation.127 In another example, Pugazhendhi et al. (2019) prepared magnesium oxide nanoparticles using aqueous extracts obtained from a marine brown alga named Sargassum wighitii. The resulting nanoparticles had an average size of 68.06 nm, with a zeta potential of 19.8 mV, indicating high stability with agglomerated and flower-shaped morphology. As expected, the FTIR spectra revealed the presence in the algal extracts of typical reducing agents, such as carbohydrates, saturated primary alcohols, aromatic groups, chlorophyll molecules, and polysaccharides, that helped with nanoparticle formation.143

Titanium dioxide

Agceli et al. (2020) prepared titanium dioxide nanoparticles using soil bacteria named Streptomyces species HC1. Titanium hydroxide was added as a precursor to the bacterial growth medium, where the metabolites of the bacteria reduced the titanium precursor to form spherically shaped, 43–67 nm sized titanium dioxide nanoparticles.149 Similarly, Taran et al. (2018) utilized the metabolite extracts of proteobacteria, named the Halomonas elongata IBRC-M 10214 strain, for the biosynthesis of titanium dioxide nanoparticles. The resulting nanoparticles were spherically shaped and 104.63 nm in size, where the FITR spectra showed that the presence of alkyne, alcohol, alkyl halide, and alkene groups in the extract were responsible for nanoparticle formation.156 Furthermore, Rajakumar et al. (2012) synthesized novel 62–74 nm sized, rutile phase, spherical and oval-shaped titanium dioxide nanoparticles via the mycelium of Aspergillus flavus fungi. The fungal metabolites, detected by FTIR, containing peroxo, proteins with peptide linkages, maleic anhydride, carbonyl, amide I, and amide II groups, are responsible for reducing and stabilizing agents for the formation of nanoparticles.133 Surprisingly, algal extracts were not widely used to synthesize titanium dioxide nanoparticles, which will be a potential area of research in the future.

Cerium and other oxide nanoparticles

In addition, spherically shaped cerium oxide nanoparticles were widely synthesized via fungal extracts in recent times.196 Venkatesh et al. (2016) used the aqueous extract of plant pathogenic fungi named Fusarium solani to form spherical, 20–30 nm sized ceria nanoparticles. The FTIR spectra demonstrated that nanoparticle formation was due to the presence of proteins, amines, and heterocyclic compounds in the extract as reducing and stabilizing agents.145 Moreover, Gopinath et al. (2015) utilized the cell culture filtrate of Aspergillus niger for the preparation of 5–20 nm sized, cubic structure and spherically shaped ceria nanoparticles. The FTIR spectra exclusively revealed that the hydroxyl molecules present in the fungal extract served as a potential reducing and stabilizing agent for nanoparticle formation.150 Likewise, extracts from bacterial strains such as Desulfosporosinus species (1.5–2.5 nm sized, spherical)158 and Shewanella oneidensis were used for the synthesis of uranium dioxide (uraninite) nanoparticles,161 whereas manganese oxide nanoparticles (amorphous spherical) were synthesized via Shewanella loihica.164 Similarly, fungal extract from Fusarium oxysporum was used to effectively synthesize bismuth oxide and barium titanate (BaTiO3) nanoparticles, while antimony oxide (Sb2O3) nanoparticles were synthesized using Saccharomyces cerevisae extracts.154

Novel metal and metal oxide nanoparticles

Recently, numerous nanocomposites were synthesized using microbes as novel nanoparticles for various biomedical and environmental applications. Tuo et al. (2015) prepared palladium–iron oxide (Fe3O4), gold–iron oxide, and palladium–gold–iron oxide magnetic nanocomposites using bacterial cells of the Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 strain. The palladium–iron oxide sample was identified to be both spherical and rod shaped with an average particle diameter of 5.5 nm (spherical), 100–200 nm in length, and 7–17 nm in width (rod). Contrarily, gold–iron oxide and palladium–gold–iron oxide magnetic nanocomposites contain spherically shaped nanoparticles of 15.4 nm and 8.3 nm, whereas the nanorods possess lengths of 140–190 nm and 200–300 nm as well as widths of 10–18 nm and 8–18 nm, respectively. The FTIR spectra demonstrated that the bacterial extract contained amides II and III, carboxyl groups, fatty acids, and carbohydrates as functional groups, which acted as reducing, capping, and stabilizing agents for nanocomposite formation.166 Similarly, Araujo et al. (2018) synthesized copper oxide nanocomposite via bacterial cellulose fibrils using a hydrothermal approach. The cellulose fibrils were extracted from Gluconacetobacter hansenii, and the nanocomposites were nanometers in diameter with interwoven fiber networks. The FTIR spectra are a useful tool for analyzing the bond vibrations characteristic of the functional groups present. The FTIR results showed that the alkanes, asymmetrical hydrocarbons, and alcoholic groups present in the bacterial cellulose were responsible for forming the bio-nanocomposite.171 Likewise, Sharma et al. (2018) prepared titanium dioxide nanoparticles using the aqueous green algal extract of Chlorella pyrenoidosa and deposited them on the surface of graphene oxide sheets to form novel nanocomposites. The biosynthesized titanium dioxide nanoparticles were 50 nm in size and spherical in shape with pure tetragonal anatase crystalline phase.148 Moreover, Samuel et al. (2018) demonstrated the synthesis of a novel bio-nanocomposite by integrating graphene oxide and fungal hyphae. The fungal hyphae were developed from the MSR3 strain of Neurospora discreta and TEM analysis showed the irregular, non-homogeneous, and rough surface of the nanocomposite. FTIR analysis emphasized that the polysaccharides, amides of protein and chitin, and carbonyl, epoxy and alkoxy functional groups were responsible for nanocomposite formation.160 All these studies revealed that the specific biomolecules from the microbes were beneficial for the formation of metallic nanoparticles.

Mechanism of microbe mediated nanoparticle synthesis

The most significant aspect of microbe-mediated nanoparticle synthesis is the reduction in their toxicity and increment in their biological activity, such as bioavailability, biocompatibility, and bioreactivity.197 The surface functional groups in the microbial extracts reduce and stabilize the precursor as an encapsulating agent to form nanoparticles, which help them to internalize into cells with high biocompatibility and bioavailability.165 In specific microbes, bacteria possess the ability to precipitate metals to form nanoparticles and can be beneficial for large-scale nanoparticle production with minimal expensive/hazardous chemical usage.184,198 Furthermore, fungal extracts were identified to possess enhanced intracellular metal uptake capacity, with the existence of several specific enzymes and fungal cultivation over the inorganic substrate surface to form nanoparticles with distinct shapes and sizes.198–200 Furthermore, algae are beneficial for reducing toxic by-products and yield colloidal biocompatible nanoparticles.201 However, these microbes possess limitations that deter their use in the commercial or large-scale industrial production of nanoparticles. In general, bacteria-mediated nanoparticle synthesis is time-consuming and is highly tedious to control the resulting nanoparticles’ size, crystallinity, and morphology.202 Likewise, fungal synthesis also requires a long reaction time and a purification step to yield pure nanoparticles. Additionally, there are difficulties in the down-stream process that limit its use as a potential alternative to conventional synthesis approaches.203–205 In addition, algal extracts are used to synthesize only a few metallic nanoparticles as they do not possess specific biomolecules to reduce or stabilize metallic precursors for nanosized particle formation.206 Thus, it is necessary to focus on overcoming these limitations to utilize the microbe mediated biosynthesis approach as a potential alternative to conventional nanoparticle synthesis methods in the future.

The specific mechanism of nanoparticle formation by microbial extracts is the most crucial unanswered question in the biosynthesis approach. It is noteworthy that identifying specific biomolecules present in the microbes that are responsible for nanoparticle formation may help to improve the synthesis method, and thus, extracting and producing them on a large scale will eventually reduce the reaction time207 and price. However, Tables 1–3 show us that almost all the nanoparticles are formed by the synergistic effect of several biomolecules or metabolites present in the microbial extracts.208 It can be noted that proteins, enzymes, and certain electron donors, such as NADH, present in the bacterial extracts are responsible for nanoparticle formation.209 Likewise, complex functional groups as well as proteins, amino acids, and enzymes in the fungal extract enable nanoparticle formation,210 whereas polysaccharides, pigments and certain functional groups in algal extracts can lead to nanoparticle production.211 These biomolecules initially reduce the metallic precursor to form ions of metals to form nanosized particles. Later, the metabolites/biomolecules degrade due to synthesis parameters such as temperature, pH or time, and bind to the nanoparticle surface to control their size as stabilizing or capping agents with regards to metal nanoparticles.173,194 In metal oxide nanoparticles, the degradation of biomolecules donates oxygen to the metal particles, reduces their size to the nano dimension, and stabilizes them to prevent agglomeration.212 However, the exact mechanism of action is still unclear as there is no specific equipment to analyze the in situ biosynthesis of nanoparticles, a topic that deserves more attention in the near future.

Plant extract mediated synthesis

It can be noted that the microbe-based synthesis protocols started to be reported in 1989 or earlier. In contrast, the plant-mediated biological synthesis of nanoparticles began only in the year 2002.213 However, the major drawbacks of microbe-mediated nanoparticle synthesis are the obligatory constraints of aseptic conditions and complex procedures, such as microbial isolation, growth optimization, and maintenance, that require trained staff and longer reaction times, thus raising the scaling-up cost.214 On the other hand, plant-mediated nanoparticle synthesis can be completed within a few minutes or hours, depending on the types of plant used and the concentration of phytochemicals.213 Moreover, plant sources are easily available in nature and they have simple, swift and straightforward reactions. Interestingly, the inherent safety of plant-mediated synthesis makes it a preferable scale-up production method for biocompatible nanoparticles at the industrial level.10,35,214,215

Gold nanoparticles

Recently, gold nanoparticles have been intensively explored in the fields of drug delivery, photothermal therapy, imaging, sensing, catalysis, and antimicrobials.215,216 Chloroauric acid (HAuCl4) is the common precursor used to synthesize AuNPs, where the phytochemicals reduce HAuCl4 to form zero-valent gold atoms, which later undergo nucleation to form nanosized particles.15 In recent times, Mimosa tenuiflora bark extract, which was rich in polyphenolic molecules, was used to synthesize gold nanoparticles. In this study, zerovalent metal atoms (Au0) are formed when the hydroxyl groups present in the polyphenolic molecules are deprotonated and donate electrons to Au3+ ions.27 The synthesized AuNPs were spherical in shape with sizes ranging from 40 to 150 nm. Furthermore, it can be noted that a higher gold precursor concentration can lead to the formation of larger-sized AuNPs. Furthermore, AuNPs with a size of less than 30 nm exhibited a visible ruby-red color due to the localized surface plasmon resonance.15 Moreover, the plant extract-mediated synthesis of AuNPs can be used in food packaging and as a coating material in fabrics due to their active antibacterial and antioxidant activity.215 Interestingly, a study reported that the prepared AuNPs were harmless to bacteria and fungi irrespective of their sizes. It has been suggested that, instead of AuNPs, it could be gold ion residue remaining in the resultant AuNPs that exhibits antimicrobial and antifungal activity in other studies.15 Therefore, purification could be a crucial step to determine the future application of the prepared nanoparticles.

Silver nanoparticles

Silver (Ag) and silver salts have long been recognized as essential agents for hygiene and the healing of wounds due to their prominent antibacterial effect without a cytotoxic effect.10,46,213,215 Also, AgNPs are widely employed in the areas of pharmaceutics, agriculture, water detoxification, air filtration, the textile industries, and oxidation catalysis.213Aloe vera, Salvia spinosa, Ocinum sanctum (Tulsi), Impatiens balsamina and Lantana camara are the plants that are extensively used to synthesize AgNPs.35,46,59,68 It was reported that polyphenolic components, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, could reduce the Ag ions and stabilize the resulting AgNPs.68 Besides, the sizes of the produced AgNPs were in the range of 10 to 190 nm. Furthermore, the morphology of the AgNPs can be in various forms, such as a sphere, trapezoid, prism, and rod, depending on the reaction conditions.213 Furthermore, AgNPs have a yellowish brown color and absorb visible light at a wavelength of 400–500 nm, as shown in Fig. 3.10 Moreover, AgNPs at a low concentration of 10 μg mL−1 had similar antibacterial effects to 10% of DMSO, a well-known antimicrobial agent.88 In addition, Aritonang et al. (2019) demonstrated that the smaller-sized Ag nanoparticles could exhibit antibacterial effects due to their ability to internalize into the bacterial membrane with ease, destroying the respiratory chain and, thus, inhibiting cell respiration to cause bacterial cell death.68

Other metal nanoparticles

Among metallic nanoparticles, the palladium nanoparticle (PdNP) is an efficient catalyst in fuel cells and organic reactions. Phytochemical extracts from Pimpinella tirupatiensis and Chrysophyllum caimito, which are rich in polyphenols, were used to synthesize PdNPs.95,100 The resulting PdNPs were in the range of 12 to 170 nm with an absorbance value of 260 nm. Interestingly, a recent study identified that the size of PdNPs could be reduced by 2.85-fold when the concentration of plant extract was increased by 2-fold.95 It was also reported that the surface of PdNPs was negatively charged, which induced a repulsive interaction to maintain a stable colloidal suspension of NPs.100 Furthermore, PdNPs were identified to possess an efficient nano-catalytic ability by shuttling electrons from donor to acceptor due to its high surface area, providing abundant active sites for interaction.100 The resulting PdNPs were successfully used as a catalyst in Suzuki and Heck coupling reactions and involved in converting 3- and 4-nitrophenols to the safer corresponding aminophenol.95 Similarly, the resulting PdNPs also possess an excellent catalytic property to degrade a highly toxic organic pollutant, namely, Congo red dye, within 11 min.100

Copper nanoparticles (CuNPs) have diverse applications, including catalysis, water treatment, information storage, solar cells, and disinfection. It is noteworthy that copper is 6 times cheaper than silver and 24 times cheaper than gold.215 The relatively low cost of copper has intrigued scientists to investigate significant ways to reduce its toxicity, which would be beneficial for pharmaceutical, health care, and environmental health areas.110 The common precursor salts used for the biosynthesis of CuNPs are copper sulphates and copper nitrate.215 Although copper metal possesses efficient antimicrobial properties, it is susceptible to oxygen, making it difficult to handle in air.110 Thus, the plant mediated synthesis of CuNPs can help to mask them from oxidation and improve their biocompatibility at the same time.215 The phytochemicals extracted from Cassia occidentalis linn, Tilia, Ocinum sanctum and Nerium oleander were recently used to synthesize CuNPs.88,110,217,218 The phytosynthesized CuNPs are proved to absorb visible light at wavelengths of 535 to 563 nm to form 5 to 77 nm sized particles. Moreover, it has been reported that CuNPs can also absorb in the ultraviolet region (range 325–270 nm), depending on their size and functional groups.88,218

In addition, nano-sized iron nanoparticles (FeNPs) have promising advantages that can combat environmental pollution due to their magnetic properties as well as the large surface area to volume ratio to offer abundant reactive sites for interaction. Recently, FeNPs were investigated for their antibacterial effect, which suggested that they exhibited an antimicrobial property via the Fenton reaction. In the presence of FeNPs, hydrogen peroxide, one of microorganism's metabolic products, is converted into highly toxic hydroxyl free radicals and singlet oxygen, which can cause oxidative stress and inhibit bacterial growth. Besides, it was reported that high concentrations of the phytochemical extract and iron precursor would result in larger nanoparticles with low colloidal stability.115

Metal oxide nanoparticles

The most common metal oxide nanoparticles that are synthesized via plant extracts are zinc, copper, iron, cerium, calcium, magnesium, and titanium dioxide. These metal oxide nanoparticles possess significant biomedical properties that plant extracts can enhance with reduced toxicity.

Zinc oxide

Zinc oxide nanoparticles (ZnONPs) have several distinct applications, such as semiconductors, UV absorbers, electroluminescent and antibacterial materials. In recent times, ZnONPs have been extensively synthesized using the leaf extracts of Aloe barbadensis, Abutilon indicum, Melia azedarach, and Indigofera tinctorial. Likewise, ZnONPs were recently biosynthesized via extracts of Matricaria chamomilla L. (chamomile flower), Olea europaea (olive leaves), Lycopersicon esculentum M. (red tomato fruits). It has been reported that the biosynthesis of ZnONPs was effective due to the presence of ascorbic acid in the tomato extract as well as the presence of flavonoids and glycoside in olive leaves and chamomile flower extracts, which acted as reducing and capping agents in the formation of nanosized particles, compared with chemical synthesis approaches. The resulting ZnONPs were about 50 nm in size and absorbed electromagnetic radiation at wavelengths of 380–386 nm. It was found that the resulting ZnONPs exhibited an excellent antibacterial effect by generating reactive oxygen species, such as singlet oxygen (1O2), superoxide (˙O2−), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), and the hydroxyl radical (˙OH), which could damage bacterial DNA and severely disrupt the bacterial membrane to cause cytoplasm leakage and bacterial death. Moreover, the physicochemical properties of ZnONPs, such as size, shape, surface charge, the surface to volume ratio, and the number of oxygen vacancy sites, can also affect their antibacterial property.121 For instance, ZnONPs with a small size (below 30 nm) and positive surface charge are proved to exhibit higher antibacterial effects.215

Copper oxide

Copper oxide nanoparticles (CuONPs) have been widely used as batteries, heterogeneous catalysts, gas sensors, high-temperature superconductors, and tools for solar energy conversion. Also, they are used as drug delivery and imaging agents in the biomedicine field.126 However, CuONPs have been reported to be more toxic than other metal oxide nanoparticles. Thus, functionalizing their surfaces with biomolecules extracted from plants is proposed to reduce their cytotoxicity. The main precursors for plant-mediated synthesis of CuONPs are copper sulfate and copper nitrate trihydrate.215 Currently, CuONPs are biosynthesized using walnut leaf and Enicostemma axillare.126,130 Walnut leaf was reported to contain numerous bioactive compounds, such as malic acid, 3-O-caffeoylquinic acids, quercetin O-pentoside, sucrose, disaccharide, α-tocopherol and tocopherol isomer, and phenolic compounds, which were proved to possess the ability to reduce and stabilize copper precursors to form nanosized particles. The sizes of the resulting CuONPs were in the range of 30 to 80 nm, with absorbance at 226–234 nm. The synthesized CuONPs are an economical antimicrobial agent and have a longer shelf life than other organic antimicrobial agents.126

Iron oxide

There are three forms of iron oxide, namely, magnetite (Fe3O4), hematite or iron(III) oxide (Fe2O3) and iron(II) oxide (FeO), where each of them has its own beneficial functionality. Fe3O4 NPs are known as superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles and are widely used in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), drug delivery, and hyperthermia therapy due to their biocompatibility and magnetic properties.137,215 Similarly, Fe2O3 NPs are mainly used in wastewater treatment and biomedical applications, as they possess exclusive magnetic properties. Likewise, FeONPs are mainly used as a photocatalyst in environmental remediation as well as biomedical fields.215 It can be noted that ferric chloride and iron sulphate are the essential precursors for plant-mediated iron oxide nanoparticle synthesis. Plant extracts of Glycosmis mauritiana, Punica granatum, and Phyllanthus niruri have been extensively used in recent times for the formation of iron oxide nanoparticles.137,142,146 All these iron oxide nanoparticles are in the size range of 10 to 50 nm with an absorbance value in the range of 330 to 400 nm. Furthermore, Phyllanthus niruri was reported to contain active phytochemicals, such as flavonoids, alkaloids, terpenoids, lignans, polyphenols, tannins, coumarins, and saponins, that could act as both reducing and capping agents in plant extract mediated synthesis.146 Furthermore, Fe3O4 NPs are considered to be a promising magnetic antimicrobial agent to generate reactive oxygen species to inhibit pathogens.215

Other metal oxide nanoparticles

Magnesium oxide (MgO) has various applications, such as an adsorbent for toxic chemicals, a photocatalyst, refractory materials, paints and superconductors.153,215 It is currently used in the medicinal field to treat various diseases, including as an antacid for heartburn and indigestion.153 The most common precursor for the phytosynthesis of MgONPs is magnesium nitrate.215 Recently, plant extracts of Costus pitus D. Don and rosemary were used to synthesize MgONPs between 20 and 50 nm in size with an enhanced antimicrobial property against Gram positive S. aureus bacteria as well as anticancer activities.153,157,215 In our previous studies, we have also prepared spherical and hexagonal shaped, 18–80 nm sized MgO nanoparticles using crude phytochemicals extracted from Amaranthus tricolor, Andrographis paniculata and Amaranthus blitum.219 Furthermore, we have also prepared MgO nanorods using aqueous phytochemicals extracted from the leaves of Eucalyptus globus.220

Likewise, cerium oxide (CeO2) or nanoceria is amongst the most utilized rare earth compound due to its multiple oxidation states. It has oxidation states that can switch between (+3) and (+4) to form Ce2O3 and CeO2, respectively. Besides, CeO2 is widely used in various areas, including fuel cells, optical devices, gas sensors, catalysis, ultraviolet absorbers, hydrogen storage materials, polishing materials, and biomedical applications.215,221 Although CeO2 might be considered a toxic substance, biosynthesized CeO2NPs showed less or nontoxicity towards normal cells in in vitro analysis.215 It can be noted that cerium(III) chloride (CeCl3) and cerium(III) nitrate hexahydrate (Ce(NO3)3·6H2O) are the most common precursors that are used to synthesize CeO2NPs. Recently, CeO2NPs were synthesized using phytochemicals extracted from different plants, such as Gloriosa superba, Acalypha indica, Aloe vera and Lycopersicon esculentum.80,222 The resulting CeO2NPs are 18.71 nm in size and absorb at a wavelength of 298 nm.80 Furthermore, the synthesized CeO2NPs exhibited an antibacterial effect222 with prominent antioxidant properties.80 The antioxidant potential of CeO2NPs is due to their multiple oxidation states that can lead to an excellent reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging ability. Furthermore, cerium possesses two different oxidation states, which include Ce3+ (Ce2O3) and Ce4+ (CeO2). Therefore, nanoceria is bestowed with redox potential and, thus, can tolerate free radicals. Hence, CeO2NPs possess the exclusive ability to promote organogenesis of nodal and leaf explants with 91% of callogenesis in tissues compared with the control group (N6 medium without NPs), leading to 39% of callogenesis.80 Additionally, calcium oxide (CaO) nanoparticles are significantly used in the fields of disease diagnosis, therapeutics, catalysis, microelectronics, and antimicrobial applications.163 It can be noted that very few studies are available on the phytosynthesis of CaO nanoparticles that have utilized phytochemical extracts of Cissus quadrangularis and calcium chloride as the precursor.163,215 The resulting CaONPs were about 58 nm in size with cubic and hexagonal morphology, exhibiting surface plasmon resonance peaks between 400 and 450 nm.163 The synthesized CaONPs demonstrated excellent potential in the medical field, such as swift healing of wounds, and were proposed for future incorporation into fabrics as an antibacterial protecting coating.163,215

Mechanism of plant extract mediated nanoparticle synthesis

Plant extract mediated nanoparticle synthesis has been explored for more than a decade; however, the exact mechanism of nanoparticle formation is still yet to be understood. Naturally, plants possess the ability to actively take up and reduce metal ions in soils during the detoxification process.213 The same principle has been identified to be applicable in plant extract mediate nanoparticle synthesis. According to previous studies,59,212,215 the mechanism of nanoparticle formation generally involves four stages: reduction of metal ions, clustering or nucleation, growth of the nucleus via an Ostwald ripening process, and the stabilization stage that defines the final shape of the nanoparticles. Various studies have reported that phytochemicals, such as methylglyoxal (NADH)-dependent reductase, terpenoids, terpene, sugars, alkaloids, flavonoids (flavone), phenols, saponins, tannins, ketone, aldehydes, carboxylic acids (e.g. ascorbic acid), amides, carbohydrates, and proteins, play a dual role by acting as both reducing and stabilizing agents.46,121,212,215 Furthermore, several studies have indicated that polyphenols, flavonoids, tannins, ascorbic acids, and sugars in plant extracts serve as reducing agents.10,110,126,213 Hydroxyl (–OH), carbonyl (CO), amino (–NH2), and methoxide (CH3O−) groups in the plant extracts are the stabilizing agents and functional groups responsible for nanoparticle formation in plant extract mediated nanoparticle synthesis.215 For instance, flavonoids with hydroxyl groups can reduce metal ions via the tautomeric transformation from enol-form to keto-form, releasing the reactive hydrogen atom and transferring electrons to the metal ions for metal nanoparticle formation.59,212 It was suggested that the catechol moiety of flavonoids participated in metal ion reduction as lower energy is required to dissociate the O–H bonds in the catechol moiety, compared with other –OH groups present in the flavonoids.59 Furthermore, variations in the reaction conditions, such as types or concentrations of precursor and extract, temperature, and pH, can lead to alterations in the rate and yield of production as well as the characteristics of the end products.215 For example, glucose mediated synthesis was reported to yield metallic nanoparticles with different morphologies, whereas fructose could result in the monodisperse form of gold and silver nanoparticles.212 Moreover, it can be noted that smaller nanosized particles with uniform size distribution are formed at higher temperatures, where the phytochemicals will degrade into simpler forms.59 Besides, numerous studies suggested that the pH could alter the electrical Antimicrobial agents

Antimicrobial activities are obtained from a wide range of green synthesized metallic NPs, such as silver (Ag), gold (Au), zinc (Zn), and iron (Fe), and metal oxide NPs, such as MgO, CaO and CuO. Predominantly, silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) are used to mitigate microbes, as shown in several reports.229–231 For example, different plant- or microbial-based synthesis routes, have been used to synthesize AgNPs having different shapes and sizes for the treatment of microorganisms, such as E. coli, P. aeruginosa, S. aureus and B. sublitis.229 Bindhu et al. (2015) demonstrated the synthesis of ∼15 nm AgNPs using beetroot extracts, to inhibit the growth of both Gram positive (G+) and Gram negative (G−) bacteria, including S. aureus, and E. coli, respectively.8 Also, the fungus Trichoderma viride was used to synthesize AgNPs (5–40 nm), which were synergetically used with different antibiotics to mitigate bacteria.32 Typically, the antimicrobial efficacy of the AgNO depends on its size, pH, ionic strength, and capping agent. For example, smaller particles having a higher SSA penetrate easily into cell membranes, facilitating their disruptive ability.232 Moreover, the amount of Ag ions (Ag+), their affinity to cellular membranes (whether G+ or G−),32,233 and their time of release (e.g. slow release in silver sulfadiazine),234 determine their potency to act as inhibitory agents. For G− microbes, such as E. coli, and P. aeruginosa, strong interactions between Ag+ and the microbial cell wall facilitate the antimicrobial properties.233

Similar to AgNPs, AuNPs have a strong affinity for the cell walls of G− microbes due to the release of positively charged gold ions (Au3+), promoting their antimicrobial ability. For example, AuNPs synthesized from pineapple extracts showed a higher zone of inhibition for G− P. aeruginosa than G+ S. aureus.52 Upon contact with moisture, metal ions from NPs are released, attaching to cell walls via strong electrostatic attractions. The released ions and the NPs themselves accumulate on the outer layers of the cells and subsequently penetrate inside, causing damage to the cellular walls and membranes. Inside the cells, metallic ions bind to enzymes, form complexes with nucleic acids of DNA, and cause the denaturing of proteins, disrupting the overall cellular activity and causing cell death.235,236 The various interactions of NPs on cells to facilitate their degeneration is illustrated in Fig. 6.

Due to weak electrostatic interactions between microbes and metallic ions of the same surface charge, alternative mechanisms are needed to facilitate antimicrobial activity in this scenario. Another limiting factor is thicker bacterial cell walls, such as in the G+ bacteria, due to the high buildup of peptidoglycan molecules. This aspect retards the invasion of metallic ions.235 Other mechanisms, such as transmembrane electron-transport of AgNPs or zinc nanoparticles (ZnNPs), oxidative stress with Ag2O, membrane potential modification with AuNPs, the generation of hydrogen peroxides with ZnO, high alkalinity and the production of active oxygen species with CaO and MgO, and the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), are alternative means by which metallic NPs can facilitate cell damage irrespective of the cell's surface charge.3,237

Metallic oxides such as CaO and MgO are, in general, efficient antimicrobial agents against both G+ and G− bacteria due to the generation of superoxides on the surface of particles and an increase in pH based on hydration. Such is the case for MgO, which, upon interaction with cells, damages the cell membranes and causes leakage of the intracellular contents, leading to apoptosis.238 Using Coctus pictus D. Don leaf extracts, Suresh et al. (2018) demonstrated the synthesis of ∼50 nm cubic MgO NPs with proven antimicrobial efficacy against both G− and G+ bacteria (e.g. S. aureus and B. subtilis), as well as certain fungal strains (e.g. A. niger and C. albicans).153 It is important to note that unlike metallic NPs where electrostatic interactions are required for antimicrobial activity, metallic oxides such as MgO are versatile antimicrobial agents based on ROS formation. Nanoparticles synthesized via an extracellular approach generate ROS, leading to the destruction of cell walls and membranes, causing leakage of the intracellular contents.3,239 Additionally, strong electrostatic interactions of metal oxides with cell membranes lead to oxidative stresses, which contribute to overall cell death.153 Other metal oxides like CuO NPs cross the bacterial cell membrane and damage the vital enzymes of the bacteria; these oxides are effective against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.3 This is demonstrated by using certain leaf extracts84,240 and algal sources33,90 to mitigate bacterial growth. Drug delivery system

Green-synthesized NPs used in several drug delivery applications, owing to their ecofriendly and nontoxic nature, are presented in this section. For example, biocompatible antimicrobial agents on metal-based NPs are incorporated into textiles for external wound dressing245 or in situ medical systems to deliver drug molecules to target sites.246 The design of efficient drug-delivery systems implies that NP drug carriers must precisely navigate through blood–tissue interfaces, blood–brain barriers, and tight epithelial skin junctions to safely deliver, distribute and accumulate therapeutic agents to target cells. Due to their small sizes, high SSA, and stimuli-responsiveness, NPs are highly desired for such functions. Moreover, NP-hosts improve the stability and solubility of different drug molecules, significantly boosting drug performance compared with the pristine molecules without particles.

Based on their magnetic susceptibility and biocompatibility, magnetic nanoparticles (MNPs) like iron oxides are also widely used for drug delivery. For example, magnetosomes crystallized from microorganisms1,247 are biocompatible and non-toxic with biological tissues. These magnetosomes having functional groups on their surfaces enable the conjugation of anticancer drugs, such as doxorubicin (DOX), for cancer treatment.248 An example is what Sun et al. (2007) demonstrated using magnetic bio-nanoparticles to treat liver cancer.249 They demonstrated a low-cell mortality rate (∼20%), low cardiac toxicity, and increased tumor suppression (∼10.4%), showing significantly improved performance compared with the pristine chemical (DOX) alone. In a similar scenario, anti-tumor activity was increased by 10% by loading DOX onto magnetosomes instead of using the drugs alone.250 Magnetic manipulation of these systems can be potentially used to direct, accumulate, and execute efficient therapeutic responses to targeted diseased cells. Bioimaging applications

The development of luminescent NPs with multifunctional properties such as optical and magnetic modulations to visualize complex cellular structures (tissues and organs), receptors, and tumors are in high demand.259,260 Unlike their bulk counterparts, NPs such as silicon emit photons in the visible–near infrared range and are adaptable to surface functionalization and bioconjugation, making them ideal for in vivo bioimaging applications.261,262 Ag- and AuNPs have tunable geometries and exhibit significantly higher absorption and scattering cross-sections. These properties make them efficient contrast agents for the optical imaging of tumors.263,264 Magnetic NPs such as iron oxides have superparamagnetic properties and are efficient magnetic labels for nuclear magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).

MNPs conjugated with biological molecules exhibited chemiluminescence in enzymes by antibody immobilization onto bacterial magnetic nanoparticles.265,266 The competitive reaction of different xenoestrogens detectable by the immobilized antibodies facilitated the process of bioimaging. Lu et al. (2020) demonstrated the use of bacteria in inducing aggregation of AuNPs, giving rise to a photoacoustic signal for pathogenic bioimaging.267 Bao et al. also demonstrated the use of the bacteria E. coli, to synthesize biocompatible cadmium telluride (CdTe) quantum dots (QDs), with tenability for fluorescent emission in the range 488–551 nm. The presence of a protein capping layer enhanced cell viability (∼93%), irrespective of high QD concentrations (2 μM). Surface functionalization of the QDs with folic acid facilitated the imaging of cervical cancer cells in vitro, presenting an economical approach to make highly fluorescent biocompatible CdTe QDs for bio-imaging and bio-labeling.7

In another study, Zinjarde et al. synthesized ∼10 nm CdTe QDs using two marine bacteria, Bacillus pumilus and Serratia marcescens. Their fluorescent properties were confirmed via photoluminescence studies, further affirming their utilization for bioimaging and biolabeling applications.19 CdTe QDs with photoluminescence emission within the range 488 to 551 nm were conjugated with folic acid and used in vitro to image cancerous cells with good biocompatibility and cytotoxicity. In another scenario, the fungus fusarium oxysporum was used to synthesize highly fluorescent CdTe QDs under ambient conditions, providing an economical green approach for the synthesis of highly fluorescent NPs for bioimaging.31 By using a fungal filtrate from Trichoderma viride, Fayaz et al. Metal nanoclusters (NCs), made up of nanoparticle aggregates, are new fluorescent probes for sensors and bioimaging applications. The sizes of NCs are near the Fermi wavelength of electrons, between metal atoms and NPs. This results in molecular properties such as discrete energy levels, size-dependent fluorescence, enhanced photostability and biocompatibility.268 Wang et al. Interestingly, gold nanoparticles form by Au(III) reduction inside the cytoplasm of the cells and these NPs eventually concentrate around the nucleoli. However, this does not happen in normal cells, differentiating tumor cells for specific in vivo bioimaging. The efficacy of this method was tested in certain xenograft tumors, which yielded the effective biosynthesis of Au particle clusters with fluorescence, but without spreading to neighboring normal tissues, and specifically biomarking the tumors.269

Diagnostic applications

Biosynthesized NPs are desirable for biosensor applications due to their biocompatibility, physiological stability, and optical properties. Typically, biosensors have receptor units that respond to specific analytes, which are mostly of biological origin like bacterial or viral DNA, proteins from antibodies or antigens, glucose molecules, or pollutants. NPs also improve transduction (signal capture), showing higher electrochemical, magnetic, and optical signals. Furthermore, efficient immobilization of bioreceptor units on particles in the form of AuNPs, ZnONPs, PtNPs, and AgNPs helps to increase biosensor sensitivities and lowers detection times.270

Particularly, AuNPs have widespread applications in biosensor applications.271 Kumar et al. (2011) demonstrated the biosensor potential of AuNPs (5–15 nm) synthesized with Zingiber officinale extracts.271 The NPs had low protein adsorption, and non-aggregation in red blood cells, making them biologically compatible. By combining properties such as high SSA, anisotropic nature, and structural tenability, the modifications of nano-shell configurations confer desirable biosensor properties to the AuNPs. These properties facilitate the detection of AuNPs through a signal in the near-infrared (NIR) region for improved performance. Kuppusamy et al. (2014) demonstrated the use of AuNPs synthesized from C. nudiflora plant extract to detect the hCG hormone in pregnant women's urine. The particles turned pink for pregnancy-positive urine, confirming pregnancy, whereas gray coloration indicated no pregnancy.272

By reporting the use of yeast cells to biosynthesize polygonal Au–Ag alloy NPs, Zheng et al. (2010) demonstrated their use as an electrochemical vanillin sensor. The Au–Ag alloy NPs were immobilized on a glassy carbon electrode (GCE) surface using a binder (Fig. 8a). This composite electrode exhibited a high sensitivity to electrochemical response for the oxidation of vanillin, as shown in Fig. 8b, with low detection limits (up to 40 nM), compared with 16 μM and 0.4 μM for pristine GCE and graphite electrodes, respectively.11 Deng et al. (2018) also used Plectranthus amboinicus leaf extract to synthesize ZnO NPs and used them to coat a 3.0 mm-diameter GCE electrode. The composite ZnONP/GCE electrode showed high electro-catalytic activity, selectivity, ultra-sensitivity, and stability towards hydrazine determination.273

Other novel biomedical applications

Apart from the above-listed biomedical applications of green-synthesized metal-based NPs, other potential uses such as tissue repair, diabetes treatment, DNA modeling, optical coatings, nanoelectronics, separation and detection, and dental applications are being developed. In the case of magnetic NPs, for example, their ability to perform catalytic functions and at the same time, be effectively dispersed in biological systems is a key advantage. Shan et al. made use of microbial cells of Pseudomonas delafieldii with magnetic Fe3O4 NPs (10–15 nm) for the desulfurization of dibenzothiophene. The particles demonstrated strong adsorption on cells, owing to their high surface energies, generating improved performance. These particles could be used several times, showing greater desulfurizing activity and operational ability.274 Furthermore, magnetic particles conjugated with biological molecules are desirable for magnetic separation and detection. Antibodies conjugated to bacterial magnetic particles have demonstrated chemiluminescence to aid in the rapid and sensitive detection of hormones.265

Moreover, the ability to manipulate biosynthesized magnetic nanoparticles and use them as solid-phase adsorbents make them suitable for DNA extraction.195 Besides the typical antimicrobial uses of biosynthesized NPs for parasite, viral or fungal treatment, they can find useful applications against various disease-causing insects. Using Nelumbo nucifera leaf extracts, AgNPs were synthesized and used for mosquito larvicidal activity.275 Similar malaria mitigation strategies have been devised based on AgNPs synthesized from different leaf extracts and are elaborated in a recent review.276 Certain metallic NPs have biosorption properties to remove micro-pollutants such as pharmaceuticals ingredients (e.g. ibuprofen and diclofenac). This was demonstrated by Forrez et al. (2011), who used Pseudomonas putida to synthesize palladium (Pd) NPs.277 Biocompatible modes of these NPs could facilitate the purification of human blood in the case of drug overload. To demonstrate the treatment of diabetes, Basha et al. (2010) used Psidium guajava to synthesize AuNPs, showing inhibition for protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP 1B).311 Research on using biosynthesized metal-based NPs for biomedical applications continue to grow, showing great promise for widespread clinical implementation.

Innovative opportunities in green nanoscience and nanotechnology

Green synthesis or chemistry produces a material using less energy without causing any hazardous effects or harm to the environment or living organisms.36 The biosynthesis approach helps to reduce environmental toxicity by eliminating toxic byproducts and potential toxicity to living organisms, such as humans, animals, and plants, compared with conventional chemically and physically synthesized nanoparticles.278 The surface functional groups, size, and shape of nanoparticles play key roles in their eco-friendly nature.170,279 It should be noted that the biochemicals and biomolecules present on the surface of biosynthesized nanoparticles are less/nontoxic as they are biogenic compared with physically/chemically synthesized nanoparticles.280 Furthermore, the final product from conventional (chemical and physical) approaches can be modified with functional groups as an additional process step, whereas biosynthesized nanoparticles possess biochemicals and biomolecules as functional groups, eventually reducing their potential toxicity.165Table 11 compares the morphological properties of physical/chemical and biosynthesized metallic nanoparticles. Centrifugation, dialysis, and chromatography are used for the purification of nanoparticles.281–283 Purified nanoparticles synthesized by conventional and biosynthesis approaches can possess similar properties and differ on the basis of their size and shape.284 However, there are certain cases where the nanoparticles, even certain biosynthesized nanoparticles, exhibit toxicity towards humans due to the internalization of nanosized particles into the cells of organisms, which affects the osmotic pressure of the cells and toxic or highly reactive functional groups from the chemical precursor.285,286 Moreover, high energy, such as heat; bioreactors using electric current;287,288 and pH alteration using chemicals180 also call into question the claim of biosynthesis as a green approach. Thus, green technology has been incorporated into the biosynthesis approach in recent times to produce nontoxic and environmentally sustainable nanoparticles.170 Recently, solar energy has been used as a potential alternative for the heating process to yield biosynthesized nanoparticles.289 Likewise, light sources, such as ultraviolet, microwave,290 gamma291 and visible light,292 have been introduced as an effective nanocatalyst to form nanoparticles rapidly, instead of a chemical catalyst. Besides, efforts have been taken to identify the specific biomolecule, enzyme or phytochemical responsible for nanoparticle formation via chromatographic or mass spectroscopic techniques.293,294 These specific biomolecules were recently isolated and utilized as reducing or stabilizing agents to improve nanoparticle formation efficiency295,296 and enhance their stability through additional processes such as pH alteration or biotemplate incorporation.297,298 It can be noted that genetically engineered microbes will be highly beneficial for producing these specific biomolecules and will help in the large-scale industrial and commercial production of nanoparticles, compared with the chemical synthesis approach.299 Similarly, plant tissue culture along with a genetic engineering approach to produce transgenic plants will be useful to yield specific phytochemicals that can be extracted for the large-scale production of nanoparticles.300,301 Besides, these lab-based green biosynthesis techniques will not affect the natural ecosystem and will help to overcome issues related to bioprospecting and biopiracy.302

Challenges in green synthesis and future perspectives

Controlling the size and shape of the synthesized nanoparticles is always a great challenge for the green synthesis approach, as these physical properties are predetermined mainly by the phytochemical compounds present in the plant extract.215 Uncontrolled size and shape will degrade the market value of the nanoparticles as commercial nanoparticles are usually finely tuned. Also, plant extracts contain various active biomolecules, which will bind to or coat the nanoparticles. This coating might improve the biocompatibility of the nanoparticles for medical applications and assist in preventing aggregation or agglomeration of the nanoparticles. Otherwise, the coating might conceal or mask the intrinsic properties of the nanoparticle. This will pose a myriad of challenges for the isolation and purification of nanoparticles from the plant extract.303 Furthermore, biomass required for the formation of nanoparticles (conversion factor) and the cost factor cannot be standardized in the biosynthesis approach, as it depends on the biomolecules in each organism, which eventually depends on the nutrient level, soil/culture medium quality, seasons, geography, and various other factors.304 Thus, the conversion and cost factor can be calculated for specific cases, considering these parameters and the type of nanoparticle to be synthesized. Also, these parameters affect the reproducibility of the biosynthesized nanoparticles, compared to conventionally synthesized nanoparticles.305 For instance, MgO nanoparticles were synthesized via three different leaf extracts, namely, Amaranthus tricolor, A. blitum and Andrographis paniculata, in our previous work. In that study, 1 g of leaves were used as precursor biomass, which led to a yield of ∼10 mg ml−1 of colloidal nanoparticles; the yield differs depending on the phytochemicals present in each plant.219 Contrarily, MgO nanoparticles synthesized via the sol–gel approach led to a yield of 200 mg for 1 g of magnesium precursor.109 It can be noted that the chemical method seems to be better than the phytosynthesis approach in terms of conversion (yield). Furthermore, 1 M and 0.001 M concentrations are required to form MgO nanoparticles via chemical and phytosynthesis approaches, respectively, which shows that the production cost will be less for the biosynthesis approach. However, this lab-scale analysis does not reflect large-scale production economics, and it depends on the type of nanoparticle and various experimental as well as external factors. Besides, the stability and yield of biosynthesized nanoparticles are not comparable with the nanoparticles prepared via conventional approaches, as they have poor stability due to degradation of the biomolecules.306 More metal precursor can be added to increase the yield. However, it will lead to agglomeration and an increase in the size of nanoparticles.307 It can also be noted that the nanoparticles resulting from most of the biosynthesized approaches are polydisperse, which is a major challenge while utilizing them for biomedical applications.290 In addition, the lack of clear design guidelines for biosynthesis, the requirement for extensive fundamental research and coordination between the industrial and research communities, the lack of toxicity analysis protocols, higher regulatory barriers compared with nanoparticles synthesized via conventional approaches, and unclear end-market demands are the other challenges that must be addressed in the future to utilize biosynthesis as a potential synthesis approach for large-scale nanoparticle production.308–310

Conclusion

The development of biosynthesized nanoparticles with green chemistry approaches has catalyzed the development of environmentally friendly, non-toxic, and biocompatible nanosized particles that are useful in various applications. The increasing popularity of green approaches and the demand for nanoparticles in biomedical applications have triggered novel nanoparticle preparation methods from various sources, such as microbes and plants, in large-scale production. Green biosynthesis methods are recognized as a more sustainable approach for the commercial production of nanoparticles with reduced impact on the environment. However, more research is required to address some of the major challenges relating to the synthesis and application of biosynthesized nanoparticles, including the standardization of procedures to evaluate toxicity, regulatory protocols for green biosynthesis, and identifying relevant mechanisms of nanoparticle formation. These advancements will be useful in supporting commercial scale synthesis and the widespread application of biosynthesized nanoparticles.
