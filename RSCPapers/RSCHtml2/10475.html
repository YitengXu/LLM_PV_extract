Single-chirality of single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) through chromatography and its potential biological applications

Abstract

The separation of highly pure single-chirality single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) is challenging and also in demand due to their intrinsic physical, optical, and electronic properties. The use of single-chirality and their performance characteristics makes them a selective candidate for multifunctional applications and opens a new front in nanotube development. It has previously been reported that SWCNTs can be separated in various ways by employing direct control and post-synthesis approaches. Herein, we review the separation of single-chiralities of SWCNTs on account of simplicity and time/cost effectiveness by using gel chromatography. The most recent progress in the controlled synthesis of SWCNTs is comprehensively reviewed in terms of selective-diameter, single-chirality, and specific geometric shape. The method to achieve the single-chirality of SWCNTs is also highlighted. Besides addressing COVID-19 characteristics, epidemiology, and pathology, we also review the most recent developments in nano-biosensors for the rapid and early detection of COVID-19. Furthermore, the photothermal/bioimaging response of single-chirality is reviewed in order to enhance the cytotoxicity of drugs against cancer cells over simple carbon nanotubes (CNTs). The single-chirality allows for precise imaging (due to efficient absorption and emission) of tumors/blood vessels up to ∼10-fold higher by injecting a low dose. We hope this review stimulates further study on single-chirality controlled SWCNTs for practical applications.

1 Introduction

Carbon is a fascinating element because of its essential role in numerous aspects of daily life and its ability to exist in a range of allotropic forms, which is rare in other elements.1 CNTs with varying diameters were first made public in 1991 by Iijima.2,3 Furthermore, nanomaterials have unique physical, electrical, and optical properties, opening up new possibilities for potential applications.4 CNTs (species of carbon material) have been extensively used in scientific and industrial nanotechnology research and development. Since their discovery, CNTs, which are rolled-up graphene sheet cylinders that are seamless, have caught the curiosity of scientists due to their outstanding properties.3,5,6 According to the number of graph layers from which a nanotube is composed, CNTs are classified as SWCNTs, double-walled carbon nanotubes (DWCNTs), and multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs), as illustrated in Fig. 1.7 Thus, the structure of a SWCNT can be described by its chirality (n,m), diameter (d), and chiral angle (θ), which is further explained in Section 2. Notably, SWCNTs can be metallic (m-) and semiconducting (sc-) depending on their chirality and diameter, whereas MWCNTs and DWCNTs are predominantly metallic (m-). For instance, if n − m is a multiple of 3, SWCNTs exhibit metallic (m-) or quasi-metallic (when n − m = 3q, where q is an integer) behavior due to bands crossing at the Fermi level, while the remaining two-thirds are semiconducting (sc-) (when n − m ≠ q) with a finite bandgap.8–10

SWCNTs have received significant attention and exploration, as evidenced by the number of articles published. SWCNTs have been used in a wide range of applications, indicating their expanding significance. SWCNTs are compatible with field effect transistor (FET) designs and high-K dielectrics, which are well-known industrial processes and commercial products.11–14 Furthermore, it is utilized in lithium-ion batteries, biosensors, medical equipment, and drug delivery systems.15–17 Unfortunately, the heterogeneity of the as-synthesized SWCNTs (metallicity (metal/semiconductor), chirality, and diameter) has hindered the use of SWCNTs in the aforementioned applications. Thus, to ensure the desired performance in all conceivable applications, the population of SWCNTs should be monodisperse in terms of the selective-diameter and single-chirality.18–21

SWCNTs may be metallic or semiconducting, while MWCNTs are mostly metallic, depending on the chirality. Metallic types should be avoided since they have a negative impact on the electrical performance of the devices and circuits. Unfortunately, there are no direct means to create electronic-pure-grade sc-SWCNTs by regulating the growth. However, high purity sc-SWCNTs can be obtained by sorting the SWCNTs raw materials. In general, there are three main approaches to acquire a certain type of SWCNTs: post-synthesis sorting/separation, preferential synthesis, and selective destruction, and each method is briefly discussed in Table 1. Post-synthesis sorting is a long and time-consuming process, but it has the advantages of large-scale production, ultra-high purity, and high resolution, which make them both scientifically and commercially valuable.

Generally, before sorting the SWCNTs, the nanotubes must be dispersed in a liquid form like water or an organic solvent. The misfortune is that there are some restraints that prevent separation in an aqueous dispersion. The main hurdle in the sorting process is the interaction (van der Waals force) between the nanotubes. Thus, numerous techniques have been reported for sorting the mixed species of SWCNTs (dispersed in an aqueous solution). Various post-growth procedures for sorting SWCNTs have been developed, including selective chemistry, dielectrophoresis, selective oxidation, ultracentrifugation, DNA wrapping, aqueous two-phase extraction (ATPE), polymer sorting, and gel chromatography.27–32 However, low yields and exorbitant costs plague these potent nanotube sorting technologies. Notably, to use nanotubes in a wide range of applications, it is necessary to have tight control over their structural and physical properties, such as selective-diameter, single-chirality, and electrical properties. To regulate single-chirality is the most difficult one. So, we need to explore the m- and sc-separation of SWCNTs on account of the simplicity and cost/time effectiveness.33

Gel column chromatography is a feasible and scalable approach for sorting SWCNTs based on their single-chirality. In 2008, Tanka et al. reported on the large-scale separation of m-SWCNTs and sc-SWCNTs using agarose gel electrophoresis.34 Later, they developed the gel squeezing method based on selective adsorption onto an agarose gel that yielded up to ∼70% m-SWCNTs and ∼90% sc-SWCNTs.35,36 On the basis of these methods, Moshammer et al. and Tanaka et al. developed a simple chromatographic method for sorting the m-SWCNTs and sc-SWCNTs.37,38 Generally, the SWCNTs were dispersed in sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) solution before being applied to a column filled with agarose gel beads. Firstly, the parent suspension was applied onto the gel to adsorb the suspension. The sc-SWCNTs were trapped by the gel beads, while the m-SWCNTs passed through the column because they had not been absorbed with the gel. A low concentration of the eluting solution (such as sodium deoxycholate (DOC) or sodium cholate (SC) solution) can be used to extract any leftover m-SWCNTs in the gel column due to the weak attraction/adsorption with gel beads. Furthermore, the solution was applied to release the trapped sc-SWCNTs, and the column could be used for repeated separation, further explained in Section 3. This novel approach has the potential to drastically cut the cost (via recycling) and considerably increase the separation efficacy of multiple chiral species. It uses a cheap SDS surfactant and disperses the eluent for SWCNTs so that the gel medium can be used again. In addition, gel column and ATPE techniques were examined for prospective applications (bioimaging, biosensor).

The different adsorption strengths of the gel and surfactant wrapped SWCNTs assemblies are because of the variable quantities of surfactants linked to the different m-/sc-SWCNTs. A decrease in the adsorption strength occurs when the surface area of a surfactant increases, resulting in an increase in the SWCNTs’ adhesion to the gel beads. Thus, the selective adsorption of the surfactant-wrapped SWCNTs that were assembled onto the gel provides the basis for separating the SWCNTs using gel chromatography. Usually, the SWCNTs adsorption decreased for the m-SWCNTs because it was coated with a greater amount of surfactant than sc-SWCNTs. Surfactant molecules on sc-SWCNTs are typically aligned parallel to the SWCNTs side walls, whereas on m-SWCNTs, they are stacked vertically, as shown in Fig. 2.

It was confirmed experimentally in 1998 through STM research on randomly selected SWCNTs that the single-chirality electronic structure was one of the most important aspects of such chirality-dependent properties.40 Liu et al. correlated the transport characteristics of DWCNTs with the chirality of both outer and inner tubes using diffraction and transport analyses.41 Furthermore, by using electron diffraction, Kauppinen et al. demonstrated that m-SWCNTs were more reactive than sc-SWCNTs when their diameters were identical, focusing attention on the mechanism of the selective growth of sc-SWCNTs in an oxidative environment.42 According to Dai et al., they fabricated FETs employing enriched (10,5) large-diameter (∼1.01 nm) SWCNTs, which had a higher on-state current as compared to the small-diameter SWCNTs.43

Additionally, our group recently synthesized m-SWCNTs (95%) and sc-SWCNTs (99%), employing temperature-controlled gel chromatography.44 The temperature influenced the interaction of the nanotubes with gel, determining the orientation and covering density of the surfactant on the nanotubes. A repeatable separation approach was carried out using the same co-surfactant system by observing the nanotube retention and release in the hydrogel at various temperatures, as shown in Fig. 3. A further study evaluated the variation in the surfactant coverage among several (n,m) SWCNT species, which resulted in different adsorption strengths for each single-chirality nanotube. The curvature of the nanotubes affected the surfactant coverage, and this was mainly true for SDS. The adsorption of the nanotubes was carried out at elevated temperatures (30, 35, 40 °C) and eluted at a lower temperature (12 °C) without altering the eluent solution. As the temperature rose, the SDS micelles shrank in size and became more loosely packed in solution. However, the release of SWCNTs led us to believe that the retention forces in the gel were weaker when the temperature was low (10 °C), which was consistent with this hypothesis (Fig. 3). Yanhya et al. suggested that the temperature variation was due to changes in the structure and density of the surfactant micelles. At low temperatures, the SDS micelles around the nanotube undergo structural rearrangement, resulting in an increase in the surfactant covering density.

The single-chirality and selective-diameter of the SWCNTs influence their electrical and electronic properties, while modifying the geometric shapes of the SWCNTs also affects their intrinsic properties. Theoretically, many SWCNTs based on geometric shapes have been observed, but less experimental work has been done. The shape of the SWCNTs was determined by the periodic arrangement of the pentagon-heptagon atomic tori/coil within the hexagonal carbon network. The most typically experienced geometric shape variations are coil/tori, waved, Y-shape, and beads, as shown in Fig. 4.45–48 Tori/coil is one of the geometric shapes that has garnered a lot of attention among scientists.49 Numerous studies have shown that the coil/tori SWCNTs, are more stable under high pressure than their straight ones, showing excellent mechanical strength, electrical conductivity, and persistent current (Ipc).50–57 However, the controlled structuring of SWCNTs that have always developed as long strings into a tori-like topology may open up new application possibilities.58–60 Recently, the Pickering emulsion approach has been used to synthesize a toroidal carbon nanotube (TCN) by using the pure m- and sc-SWCNTs collected from gel chromatography.61–65 The facile and simple approach in the fabrication of m- and sc-TCN should provide insight towards the practical uses of TCN and tori-based quantum interference devices. Furthermore, the tori/coil nanotubes exhibit the negative magnetoresistance characteristics of weak one-dimensional localization at low temperatures. The intriguing electronic behavior is explained by quantum interference, which is enabled by the tori structure. An electron wave entering the tori can propagate in a clockwise or counter-clockwise manner around the ring and the two waves interefere constructively at the injection point due to the identical lengths of the paths. The constructive interference is destroyed by applying a magnetic field perpendicular to the tori/coil where the conjugate electron's wave acquires opposite phases.62,65 Here, the highly enriched m- and sc-SWCNTs are separated and coiled into a form of nanotori via oil-in-water and water-in-oil emulsions in a controlled manner. Notably, a m-TCN sheath (Ipc) shows the same behavior as it results in TCN, while a sc-TCN sheath (Ipc) shows the same behavior as their primary tori/coil.53 Whenever, the single-chirality shifts to self-coiling geometry, the electrical properties of the material alter in a noticeable way. Overall, this phenomenon is a new way in electronics and biomedical applications at large-scale production.

Nanomaterials with a one-dimensional (1D) structure, such as SWCNTs, have numerous applications in bioimaging, phototherapy, and drug delivery. SWCNTs can absorb light in the visible-to-NIR range, which make them ideal for phototherapy. Light scattering across biological tissues is related to light wavelengths. The longer the light wavelength is, the larger the penetration depth into biological tissues, resulting in better internal organ images. Thus, only a small portion of SWCNTs emit light in the biological environment, and the remaining amount does not contribute due to the mixed chiralities. This means that single-chiralities (sc-SWCNT with narrow bandgap) are excellent candidates for phototherapy and bioimaging. Single-chiralities of SWCNT enable low doses in biological systems and improve detection sensitivity. It is essential to sort the different SWCNT species because the electrical and optical characteristics of SWCNTs are influenced by their chirality (with high purity). These chiralities have very similar E11 (∼802 nm for (12,1) chirality and ∼798 nm for (11,3) chirality) and E22 (∼1171 nm for (12,1) and ∼1197 nm for (11,3) chirality) energies. Thus, they can be stimulated by a single wavelength laser (808 nm), and both have good photoluminescence (PL) at ∼1200 nm.20,66 The sorted chiralities that have ∼5 times stronger PL and a 6-fold lower injection dose is therefore required to achieve good biological and photothermal imaging.67 In addition, the irradiation of (6,5) SWCNTs under a 980 nm laser allowed for a higher temperature as compared to the unsorted SWCNTs.

In this review, single-chiralities have been studied through the gel column chromatography separation approach, and the incorporation of multistage and temperature control, to enhance the M/S separation purity. Despite that, there is still room for improvement and bottlenecks for future development. Explicitly, the small-diameter SWCNTs should be separated into high purity (n,m) subpopulations. These small-diameter nanotubes are practically useful for therapeutic and sensor applications due to their large bandgap. The large bandgap is favorable in sensing applications for better gating effect and Schottky barrier build-up. The early diagnosis of infectious diseases (like COVID-19) using these sensing technologies is still highly beneficial in reducing the disease impact. Thus, compared to traditional methods, single-chirality SWCNTs provide a high signal-to-noise ratio, quick, selective and label-free detection. The sc-SWCNT FET detection test technique (for COVID-19) provides the ability for multiplex detection of not only viral antigens, but also antibodies that recognize these antigens. The exceptional properties of the SWCNTs are also extensively related to their geometric structure. Several methods have been found for forming SWCNTs with annular geometry (ring/tori) by using a simple and general emulsion process. Here, we review the formation of nanotori SWCNTs for various applications, such as drug delivery and bioimaging. Due to the strong plasmonic interaction between the AuNPs and the CNT nanoring (CNTR), CNTR@AuNP has stronger Raman, photoacoustic (PA), and photothermal therapy (PTT) signals than straight CNT@AuNP. Furthermore, the photothermal responses of the simple SWCNTs and separated SWCNTs (single-chirality) under NIR irradiations are compared. There is a clear trade-off between the power, density/concentration, and time. Having efficient light-heat conversion is always favorable in photothermal therapy to alleviate toxicity concerns and optical hazards. Therefore, sc-SWCNTs with the strongest light-heat conversion are the most appropriate choice for photothermal therapy and bioimaging. Herein, we review the latest progress in sorting the SWCNT species and their applications based on single-chirality, selective-diameter, and specific geometric shapes.

2 Characteristic of SWCNTs

The manner in which graphene is rolled onto a cylinder determines the properties of SWCNTs.7 To illustrate the two directions and unit vectors of the hexagonal lattice, a pair of indices (n,m) or different chiralities are utilized, as shown in Fig. 5. Along the nanotube axis, the SWCNT is a periodic 1D structure.68 Those nanotubes (n, 0) are named “zigzag,” while those (n,n) are named “armchair”, and the rest of the nanotubes are referred to as helical. When n − m is a multiple of 3, SWCNTs display metallic or quasi-metallic behavior; otherwise, they are semiconducting with a finite bandgap.

Importantly, the vector (connecting the origin to the lattice point defines the nanotube index) determines the helicity and diameter of the tube. All of the (n, 0) nanotubes are called zigzag nanotubes, (n, n) nanotubes are the armchair nanotubes, whereas the others are chiral nanotubes.69 Copyright RCS, 2009.

Armchair tubes, also known as (n,n), are truly metallic, with band crossings at k = ±2/3 of the 1D Brillouin zone. The graphene electronic structure can be described as a zero-gap semiconductor, wherein the bonding and antibonding π-bands are degenerate at the K-point (zero corner) of the hexagonal 2D Brillouin zone.68 Metallic conduction occurs when one of these permissible wave vectors crosses the K-point of the hexagonal 2D Brillouin zone. The valence and conduction bands are degenerate because of the peculiar symmetry of the 2D graphene lattice. More wave vectors become allowed along the circumferential direction when the nanotube diameter increases, forging more 2D nanotubes where the semiconducting bandgap vanishes. In the case, n − m ≠ 3l (where l is an integer), the bandgap (Egap) depends on the diameter.70

2.1 UV-vis-NIR spectrum

SWCNTs exhibit distinctive optical absorption in the UV, visible, and NIR regions. Through Kataura's plot, UV-vis-NIR spectroscopy is increasingly utilized to determine the purity and concentration of single-chirality. The electrical properties of the SWCNTs are determined by the diameter and chirality because the density of states (DOS) of SWCNTs has a series of strong interband transitions or Van Hove maxima at energies indicated by E11E22, etc. (depending on tube diameter). SWCNTs with pure subpopulations (diameter and chirality) have spectrally narrow interband transitions. Although each index has a unique set of interband transitions between the Van Hove singularities (VHs), the optical absorption spectrum makes it simple to detect the SWCNT single-chirality. Absorption peaks in the range of 950–1350 nm, 650–950 nm, and 300–450 nm correspond to the first, second, and third optical bands of the sc-SWCNTs and are denoted as S11, S22, and S33, respectively. The absorption peak between 450–650 nm is denoted as the optical transition of m-SWCNTs (M11). The SWCNTs (dt > 1.0 nm) are denoted at wavelength ranges from 1400–1900 nm, 750–1150 nm, 420–580 nm, and 600–750 nm as S11, S22, S33, and M11, respectively.

2.2 Raman spectrum

Even though UV-vis spectrum analysis has a limited resolution, it is inexpensive and fast. Thus, Raman spectrum analysis is a powerful characterization approach for carbon-based nanomaterials. The single-chirality (n,m) can be calculated by comparing the observed Raman spectrum with the Kataura plot shown in Fig. 7. According to a previous study, nanotube species with optical transition energies in resonance with the laser energy can exist in the spectrum.72 As a result, Raman spectra with numerous laser lines can be used to determine the diameter distribution of all nanotubes in a sample. Arujo et al. measured 125 radial breathing mode (RBM) frequencies with laser energies between 1.26 to 2.71 eV, and plotted them on a two-dimensional graph.73 Raman spectrum are typically used to identify the metallicity and chirality of SWCNTs. The modes defined from graphite include the high-frequency tangential mode and the RBM, which are associated with the vibratory motions of carbon molecules. The tangential G mode in SWCNTs (around 1500–1600 cm−1) breaks into two submodes, G+ for atomic displacement along the tube axis and G-mode in a circumferential direction, because of the quantum confinement effect. Due to the huge number of free electrons in m-SWCNTs, the G-peak has a broadened shape compared to the Lorentzian-like line shape of sc-SWCNTs.

Hence, the (n,m) assignment is possible through the analysis of the ωRBM and IRBM. The relationship between the tube diameter and ωRBM is monotonic. The SWCNTs RBM are unique and occur in the range of 120–250 cm−1 for SWCNTs of diameter in the range of 0.7 nm < dt < 2 nm.

3 Sorting of the carbon nanotubes

The peculiar properties of SWCNTs only manifest upon well-separated single-chirality and selective-diameter conditions. This section will discuss the metal/semiconductor (M/S) separation and its mechanism, together with the incorporation of multistage and temperature control, to enhance the M/S separation quality. The M/S separation is a low-cost, rapid, and straightforward process. This enables the separation of selective-diameter SWCNTs via gel chromatography. Moreover, the temperature effect modulates the SDS and SC micelle's structure, as well as their coverage on the nanotube. We also have a very brief overview of the ATPE method employed for biomedical applications (based on single-chirality).

Many approaches for the separation of single-chirality have been used over the last few decades. In 2001, the selective destruction methods enabled the first separation of SWCNTs via electrical destruction.75 However, the publication on post-synthesis separation increased significantly since 2003, which probably stimulated by the individualization of nanotubes in aqueous solution and the advancement in spectroscopic analysis of SWCNTs by Smalley and Weisman.76–78 These efforts provide a more accurate preliminary and post-separation appraisal of the SWCNTs diameter and structures (n,m). In 2003, the wrapping of SWCNTs with single-stranded DNA enabled the separation of the nanotubes by tube diameter, single-chirality, and electronic properties.27 In 2004, Zheng et al. adequately reported the (6,5) enriched SWCNTs via ion exchange chromatography.79 Furthermore, DGU separation was reported by Hersam and co-workers in 2005 and 2006.28,29 The DUG method is an established and widely adopted method for biochemical separation. They found that DUG performed on SC and DOC dispersed the SWCNTs, allowing the SWCNTs to be separated by electronic structure and diameter. Fluorene-based polymers and aromatic polymers were used to isolate specific species in 2007.29,30 By this method, ∼10 semiconducting single-chiralities were sorted by applying size exclusion chromatography, which first reduced the nanotube length distribution. This was followed by ion-exchange chromatography to separate the (6,5), (6,4), and (9,1) nanotubes.80 Subsequently, the 12 primary semiconducting single-chirality sequences were identified by screening the hundreds of single-standard DNA sequences.81 The Nicolas group found that polyfluorenes (PFO) selectively dispersed sc-SWCNTs in the organic solvent.30 His finding was considered a landmark achievement in SWCNTs separation.

A chromatography-based separation approach was devised in 2009, where a polysaccharide gel was employed as a separating agent for the single-chirality purification of surfactant-dispersed SWCNTs.36,37,82,83 Like ion-exchange chromatography, the polysaccharide gel is widely used in biochemical separation. Tanaka et al. and Moshammer et al. found an unexpected phenomenon when they exposed SDS-dispersed SWCNTs to a gel medium. Adsorption of the surfactant-dispersed SWCNTs with the gel was significant, as was the surfactant employed to separate the metallic (m-) and semiconducting (sc-) SWCNTs. Temperature also had a significant impact on adsorption. The semiconducting species were mainly trapped or drawn to the gel. 3.5 Advantages and disadvantages of both separation methods

Here, we look at the advantages and disadvantages of both methods based on how they work and what they can be used for.

To sort the SWCNTs, gel chromatography is a viable technique. By this method, both small-diameter and large-diameter single-chiralities are achieved. To be more specific, the small-diameter SWCNTs should be sorted into high purity subpopulations. The bandgap of these small-diameter SWCNTs makes them valuable in therapeutic and sensing applications. Because their bandgap decreases with increasing diameter, large-diameter SWCNTs are extremely susceptible to oxidation, making separation difficult. Therefore, more work needs to be put into developing thin-film transistors and transparent conductive thin films using sorted SWCNTs (based on selective-diameter and high purity). Gel chromatography for SWCNT separation has the disadvantage of being very expensive due to the high cost of the gels that are used, the low separation power, and time-consuming process. Furthermore, the mass of the SWCNTs is also limited to application on the gel column. Thus, researchers need to recycle the gel to overcome this hurdle. Gel columns also have advantages such as suitability, simplicity, and straightforward process.109,110

ATPE has been used to separate animal and plant cells, bacteria, viruses, and cellular organelles, such as chloroplasts, mitochondria, membrane vesicles, proteins, and nucleic acids. ATPE is made by adding two chemically different water-soluble polymers or polyethylene glycol and salt to water, forming two immiscible phases. With their high-water content, aqueous systems are suitable for the purification and separation of biomolecules. ATPE provides a favorable separation environment for biomolecules, where polymers stabilize their structure and biological activities. These systems are versatile enough to be utilized with both streams of tiny particulate matter and huge quantities moving continuously with short contact periods. This method is environmentally friendly, low-cost, continuous, scalable, and efficient for many studies, especially for biomolecule concentration and purification.108 Biomolecule partitioning in ATPE is notoriously difficult to predict, especially for bigger molecules, which is one of the major disadvantages. Biological products may be harmed by the process conditions and organic solvents used in other liquid–liquid extraction (LLE) procedures.111

4 Selective-diameter of sorted SWCNTs

SWCNTs are a complex mixture of multi-species described by the chiral index distribution (n,m). SWCNTs have demonstrated their potential to achieving the next generation of electronics, optoelectronics, and biological applications based on their single-chirality and diameter selectiveness.112–114 Although there is tremendous potential for improving the effectiveness of the current procedures, a mixture of different types of chirality is routinely used, even after selective synthesis methods have been published.115,116 The separation of single-chirality and selective-diameter is thus a critical and application-oriented goal.117–119

By loading unbound nanotubes into a gel column, the sc-SWCNTs can be gradually and selectively separated by diameter on the basis of their magnitude of interaction strength with the gel. Based on the strength of their interaction with the gel, nanotubes with different structures were classified into different columns/environments. As in the multi-gel-column method, every col was enriched with certain chiralities. By using this method, almost ∼13 single-chiralities were separated. The separation in diameter and selection was caused by the different grades of SDS coverage on the nanotubes, in which the coverage scales the interaction strength or affinity of each species (van der Waals or hydrophobic) with gel. The coverage and morphology of SDS on the nanotubes are dependent on the diameter, chiral angle, and the smallest bond curvature radius.18,95,102,120 The nanotubes with the smallest bond curvature are less likely to be covered with SDS coating. They have the highest affinity or strong interaction with the gel matrix due to their low aggregation number, causing them to appear in the first column.

Large-diameter sc-SWCNTs (dt > 1.0 nm) are preferred over smaller-diameter sc-SWCNTs in thin film transistors, solar cells, and infrared detectors due to their lower exciton binding energy and high carrier mobility.121–123 The enormous carrier mobility scales quadratically with the diameter of the nanotube, but the exciton binding energy scales inversely with the diameter of the nanotube.124,125 However, the large-diameter has decreased the difference in the nanotube properties species, as a large radius of curvature decreases the differentiation in the electronic structure and likely the difference in the surfactant shell. In comparison with smaller-diameter SWCNTs, the large-diameter SWCNTs have a greater number of species. There are roughly ∼150 species present within the 1–2 nm diameter range, whereas 32 species are found within the 0.6–1 nm diameter range.126–128 As the diameter increases, the difference in bandgap between the metallic and semiconducting subspecies decreases. The difference in bond strain and diameter among nearby species reduces the size of these subpopulations. The reduction in the chemical difference between species, in combination with the decreased mass of specific species, by mass minimizes the structural differences in the surfactant shell around each (n,m) species, which impedes separation based on SWCNTs wrapped with the surfactant.129–131 In 2013, Zhang et al. examined the transistor performance with various diameters of sc-SWCNTs. The thin film transistor has the highest mobility with the large-diameter SWCNTs.132,133

Moreover, large-diameter SWCNTs are covered with more surfactant or heavier surfactant shells due to the stronger polarization by smaller bandgaps. We may expect a strong screening effect from the heavy surfactant shell, which leads to weak retention forces in the gel matrix, and inevitably complexing the separation. Thus far, the primary goal remains focused on the extraction of large-diameter SWCNTs by metallicity in high purity and large throughput, yet achievable with a simple protocol.

5 Washing techniques

The use of an amphiphilic surfactant allowed the usage of accessible SWCNTs to explore their significant physical and electrical properties for various potential applications. The SWCNTs’ poor water-solubility makes them challenging to work with. Therefore, a stable aqueous dispersion is required.134 SDS is the most frequently utilized surfactant in manufacturing applications, such as transparent conducting films, batteries, chirality separation, organic photovoltaics, drug delivery, and healthcare. However, residual SDS acts as spacers that block the van der Waals interaction and π–π stacking, and can require additional post-processing purification for potential applications.

SDS, an anion surfactant that was utilized to disperse the nanotube and manage the interaction with gel beads throughout the sorting process, was employed to cover the sorted species. Therefore, the removal of SDS is critical toward the applications of SWCNTs.135 Several approaches have been considered for SDS removal for the reasons of simplicity, cost-effectiveness, and efficiency. There is dialysis, alcohol washing, chloroform/methanol/water (C/M/W) extraction, acetone extraction, H2SO4-control method, and HCl-control method.

5.1 Dialysis

This method is a very simple technique to separate unwanted molecules in a solution. This common laboratory technique operates on the same basic principle as medical dialysis. By this method, unwanted molecules such as salts and reducing agents were removed. This technique also helps to understand buffer exchange and drug binding.136 However, dialysis is incapable of removing the SDS surfactant effectively or thoroughly. Thus, researchers got curious and interested in searching for an alternative washing approaches.

5.2 Chloroform/methanol/water (C/M/W)

This washing methodology was reported by Wessel and Flugge.134,137 First, 2 ml of solvent containing sorted SWCNTs were mixed into 8 ml of methanol. Next, 2 ml of chloroform and 6 ml of water were added for phase separation. Then, the solution was vortexed until homogenous and centrifuged for 10 min at 5000 rpm. The sorted SWCNTs were localized at the interphase. The upper supernatant was removed carefully. Then, 6 ml of methanol was added to the lower chloroform phase. Again, the mixture was centrifuged for 10 minutes at 10000 rpm. The upper supernatant was carefully removed. The C/M/W ratio is 1:4:3 (v/v).138 After applying the C/M/W method to the sorted species, the surfactant is removed efficiently, as shown in Fig. 12.

5.3 HCl extraction

Firstly, the SWCNTs were dissolved into a 6 M HCl solution in the ultrasound bath to convert sodium salt into deoxycholic acid. This treatment caused precipitation of the material. Then, the obtained mixture was filtered through a vacuum filter, and flushed with water and acetone. In the next step, the filtrated nanotubes were redispersed in ethanol and heated at 80 °C to dissolve the deoxycholic acid covering the nanotube surface. 6 Formation of SWCNTs for various structural shapes

This section discusses the string shapes of the SWCNT constraints and their immediate application to molecular wires, fibrous composites, and field emission sources. However, the shape of the SWCNTs that are consistently grown in a controlled manner as long strings into various geometry topologies, particularly the tori shape, could open new possibilities. It is interesting that their electronic behavior depends on geometric structures. Furthermore, gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) were deposited on SWCNT rings for theragnostic applications.

Since the discovery of CNTs, nanomaterials and nanotechnology have made significant progress. CNTs have a lot of exciting physical, optical, electronic, and electrical properties, as well as high tensile strength, flexural rigidity, and aspect ratio.140,141 CNTs have always had a geometry of straight cylinders since they were first reported by Iijima. Subsequently, a plethora of studies have reported on the various geometric shapes of SWCNTs.142,143 The geometric formation of nanomaterials can be formed by using a variety of procedures, including laser growth phase, organic method, and chemical deposition.52,144–147 Through theoretical studies, a number of geometric shapes (coil, branched, twisted, bent, kinked, coalescent, and toroidal) have been investigated.45 These geometric shapes are based on the insertion of non-hexagonal defects into seamless hexagonal grids. In particular, the regular TCNs are based on a unique arrangement of pentagons and heptagons in a complete hexagonal lattice. Notably, among these geometric shapes, the tori/coil geometric shapes are more stable and functional.148–151

The formation of SWCNTs, which have been regulated as long strings in a ring\tori topology, creates new opportunities for potential applications.58–60 Recently, a lot of research has focused on the significance of tori geometric structures and their electrical properties. The formation of a tori in a very controlled manner with good stability is impressive. It should be noted that their behavior in electronics is actively dependent on their geometric structures.62,64,65 The geometric shape of the tori started with the work of Liu et al., who introduced a low yield TCN synthesis (0.01–1%) and observed the tori at 300–500 nm in diameter and 5–15 nm in thickness.152 Subsequently, Avouris and co-workers fabricated SWCNT tori (TSN) through sidewall–sidewall van der Waals interactions. The arrangement of the coil/tori by the bubble–liquid interface was also investigated. Later, Sano et al. synthesized the rings by using end-functionalized tubes.146 Nagy and co-workers shaped a few MWCNT rings with a diameter of 500 nm. Colomer et al. observed rings by reducing the solution of Mg0.9Co0.1O in the H2–CH4 atmosphere.153 Martel et al. observed that straight CNTs under ultrasonic radiation induced tori/ring shapes. Most recently, Tsukruk et al. developed DWCNT rings, which were similar to TSNs, with a diameter of almost 500 nm. By using the CVD method, Xie's team made TSNs with the minimum possible diameter of 120 nm.64,153

Several approaches have been suggested to understand the formation of TSNs. In this review, simple and large-scale approaches are revealed for the formation of coil/tori shapes that can be used in a wide range of applications. Furthermore, the emulsion technique is a simple method for forming TSN. This emulsion method is rapid, straightforward, and cost/time effective.154 The hydrophobic (oil-in-water) and hydrophilic (water-in-oil) emulsion is employed for the formation of TSN, m-TSN, and sc-TSN, as shown in Fig. 13.61 First, the SWCNTs are dispersed in dimethylformamide (DMF), and then toluene is applied to the above suspension.155 After that, water is applied in a single shot, when DMF is diffused into water, and the tori shape is revealed. After being turned into TSN, the hydrophobic SWCNTs become hydrophilic.

7 Biosensors for COVID-19 detection

In this section, we cover the detection of COVID-19 using electrochemical, FET-based, and optical nano-biosensors. Biosensors provide continuous and real-time detection via nanomodification, making them a new diagnostic tool for COVID-19. This technology is rapid, cheap, accessible, and efficient. The properties and electron transfer ability of m-SWCNTs and sc-SWCNTs for detecting target analytes are further determined.

In the last few decades, human coronaviruses have emerged and grown into the most severe threat to worldwide social health.169–171 Three major viruses with high fatality rates have been identified to date: the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus (SARS-CoV) in 2002, the middle east respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERS-CoV) in 2012, and the existing severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2).172–177 The World Health Organization (WHO) recognized the present coronavirus as COVID-19 in January 2020, and the International Committee on the Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV) named COVID-19 as SARS-CoV-2 in February 2020.178 Thus, in March 2020, the WHO declared a global pandemic for COVID-19 due to the rapid increase in cases and released interim management guidelines.179 The coronavirus can infect both animals and humans, causing a wide range of respiratory illnesses, but ∼35% of COVID-19 instances are asymptomatic. Thus, it is critical to distinguish healthy people from those who are infected and asymptomatic.180 The most prevalent symptoms are cough, fever, breathing discomfort, and headache. Patients with weak immune systems suffer from severe respiratory pathologies.181 Therefore, the primary objective is to develop tools, methodologies, and strategies for combating this pandemic based on its infection rate and impact on human health.182–184 Many monitoring and screening procedures still need to be developed to detect COVID-19 and its modified variants for early detection.

Since 1965, viruses that can infect both people and animals have been extensively studied and usually spread from animal to human or human to animal.185,186 Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), Ebola virus, and some coronaviruses are all examples of potentially deadly animal viruses.185,187,188 These viruses can be more fatal in the early stages of an epidemic because there is no treatment or vaccine available. Coronaviruses are single-stranded enclosed RNA viruses that are further classified into four groups: alpha (α), beta (β), gamma (γ), and delta (δ), as shown in Fig. 15.189,190 These four groups of viruses impact the upper and lower airways, nose, and lungs. 229E and NL63 coronaviruses belong to the alpha group. OC43 and HKU1 belong to the beta group (SARS and SARA-CoV-2), and are transmitted by mammals. Gamma coronaviruses are primarily associated with birds, and both mammals and birds could be infected by delta coronaviruses.191 Compared to MERS and SARS, COVID-19 is more harmful in terms of pathogenicity. Therefore, it is critical to distinguish between healthy and symptomatic individuals. Symptomatic patients are further classified as asymptomatic and pre-symptomatic. Patients who test positive for COVID-19 (but are pre-symptomatic) may not show symptoms for up to 14 days following their diagnosis.180,192 Thus, rapid and reliable testing is required for both COVID-19 positive and symptomatic patients for the safety of healthy people.193–195

The prerequisites for developing a sensing device are rapid detection, low cost, and ease of use. Biosensors are an excellent alternative for detecting and diagnosing these viruses since they can detect nanomodifications in real-time. To put it another way, a biosensor is an analytical device or a bioreceptor capable of detecting and transducing physical signals resulting from the biological change. Nano-biosensors are made of antibodies or DNA, and can transmit signals optically, electrochemically, or via FETs.200,201 Therefore, utilizing unique nanomaterials for nano-biosensors can overcome some obstacles and limitations. To detect viral contamination, researchers have focused their efforts on developing biosensors based on electrochemical, optical, FET-based, and microfluid-based technologies, among other approaches.202–204 The selection of nanomaterials is further influenced by parameters, such as the size, shape, and electrical properties (single-chirality). After selecting the material for biosensor, the biosensor is evaluated on the basis of sensitivity, the limit of detection, linear range, repeatability, selection, and other responses. Biosensors are essential for the rapid detection of body analytes because they allow for real-time analysis.205 In this section, we review the numerous potential biosensors for the early detection of viruses that are low cost, allow for rapid detection, and are simple.

Generally, biosensors comprise biological parts (organic), transducers (inorganic), and signal analysis components that are capable of detecting a target analyte and providing data. The bioreceptor of a biosensor interacts with the analyte, whereas electrochemical interference offers a surface for reaction and converts the biochemical signals into electrical signals for accurate and rapid detection. To increase the sensitivity and specificity, nanomaterial-based biosensors can be identified with antibodies or other particular molecules with high affinity for the targeted markers/components. Biosensors can be categorized into electrochemical, optical, mass-sensitive, and other types depending on their signal sensing methods and principles of transduction.

7.2 FET biosensor for COVID-19 based on sc-SWCNTs

In the past, FETs were built with semiconductors, such as doped silicon and indium gallium arsenide, as channel materials. The FETs are building blocks for electronic devices. Unfortunately, the present semiconductors are subject to scaling (shrinking in dimension), power consumption, and processing limitations. The extended π-band conjugation (from rigid sp2, C–C bonds) in SWCNT leads to excellent transport characteristics, such as high-effect mobility (79000 cm2 V−1 s−1), high carrier mobility (>100000 cm2 V−1 s−1) at room temperature, high hole mobility (20000 cm2 V−1 s−1), high thermal conductivity (>2000 W m−1 K−1), long free path (exceeding ∼0.7 μm) and high on/off ratios (107–8).220–223 These intrinsic properties make sc-SWCNTs favorable to replace the traditional semiconductor in high-performance electronics, biosensing, sensors, and low-cost FETs.13 Moreover, the flexible and transparent sc-SWCNTs will be likely incorporated into plastic electronics.224,225

SWCNTs are metallic or semiconducting, depending on their chirality. When biomolecules are attached to SWCNTs, the electrical properties of the SWCNT-based FET can be altered. An individual SWCNT-based biosensor was developed by Besteman et al. on a molecule-level basis.218 So et al. reported on SWCNTs that were grown on a Si substrate. Photolithography was used to define the source, drain, and electrodes, and lift-off techniques were used to define the SWCNT-based FET source, drain, and electrodes.226 Martnez et al. used a CNT transistor array to detect electronics DNA hybridization.227 It was designed by Oh et al. to detect amyloid b (Ab) in human serum using a SWCNTs film-based biosensor.228 Yanagi et al. and Topinka et al. showed that increasing the sc-SWCNT content resulted in the transition of the conduction mechanism from a weak to strong localization regime.229–231 The strong localization arose from the disordered nanotubes, which may occur due to Schottky barriers between the m-SWCNTs and bandgap differences between sc-SWCNTs. Notably, from the simulation, a high and uniform current was observed in pure m-SWCNTs or sc-SWCNTs, whereas the mixture or combination of metallic and semiconducting tubes blocked the current.229

Today, numerous sensing methods for biosensing and detection have been proposed and studied to alleviate environmental and public health concerns.232–235 Locating the source of viral-related illness can be difficult, but it is essential. Thus, the ability to rapidly and accurately identify viral infections and detect disease is necessary for medical diagnostics.205 To design the biosensor, two essential factors must be considered, the number of layers (self-assembled) and the device dimensions. The layered patterns imply that SWCNTs are mostly adsorbed parallel to the substrate. A significant role is played in biosensor design by the selection of materials and the single-chirality of the sensor. The conductance of a biosensor decreases as the length of the sensor increases, and electron transfer increases as the distance between the two electrodes are reduced. Due to their high surface-to-volume ratio, high electron mobility, and strong electrical conductivity, SWCNTs are commonly used in various applications. The SWCNTs’ large surface area increases the biomolecule loading capacity. At the same time, the nanotube's small bandgap and good conductivity allow them to transport electrons between the electrode surface and the biomolecules via adsorption and covalent crosslinking. Thus, the electrical resistance of the composite is determined by the single-chirality of the m- and sc-SWCNTs resistance, as well as the contact resistance of the nanotube.

Zhang et al. designed an immunosensor capable of rapid detection and capturing the COVID-19 protein (spike) with a detection limit of 0.2 pM in ∼2 minutes.236 Seo et al. developed a graphene-based FET biosensing device for the detection of the COVID virus.204 For viral disease detection (COVID-19), the graphene based-FET sensor showed significant potential as compared to other detection techniques (NAAT, RT-PCR) within a short period of time. Brohmann et al. stated that the junction only partially determined the overall charge carrier mobilities in pure and mixed SWCNT networks.237 Interestingly, another group recently examined the functionalized single-chirality sc-SWCNTs (high purity) with a specific antibody to detect COVID-19 proteins (S and N antigens) in terms of the time/cost reduction, as well as rapid detection and label-free FET-based biosensors.

The high purity of sc-SWCNTs (single-chirality) results in a higher on/off ratio for FET-based applications than other 1D nanomaterials (raw SWCNT), which leads to higher analytical sensitivity. The molecular biosensor based on sc-SWCNTs displayed high sensitivity at an order of magnitude lower than the other sensors, and were able to diagnose the sample (distinguish between positive and negative COVID-19 result) efficiently, as shown in Fig. 18.238 The antibody functionalization was revealed with sc-SWCNT by Raman spectroscopy, and two significant peaks were found in the RBM region. After functionalization, the peak between 125 and 225 cm−1 decreased in intensity, suggesting a preference of antibody functionalization on SWCNTs with larger diameters. The shift of the threshold voltage toward more negative gate voltages and the decrease in the device conductance in the p-type region demonstrated the successful antibody functionalization on the sc-SWCNTs, as shown in Fig. 18. With their large surface area, semiconducting behavior, band gap fluorescence, and high Raman spectral signal, the single-chirality SWCNTs (along with functionalized SWCNT sidewall) are suitable for sensing biomolecular interactions and detection.239,240 Single-chirality SWCNTs with enriched (10,5) were used to fabricate FETs, which had a higher on-state current than the small-diameter ones by Zhang and colleagues.43 This was because the large-diameter SWCNTs were capable of delivering a high current, and it was also relatively simple to construct low-resistance connections for them. Additionally, the sc-SWCNT FET detection technique enabled the multiplex detection of not only viral antigens, but also antibodies specific to these antigens. Thus, the single-chirality, selective-diameter, and purity of the SWCNTs play an important role in the current detection biosensing for COVID-19 and other potential applications. 8 Chirality based SWCNTs for phototherapy and NIR imaging

This section discusses how CNTs are good heat delivery vehicles for the laser treatment of tumors (PTT) due to their high optical absorption in the NIR region and effective heat generation. The well-functionalized SWCNTs can evade the endosomal compartment and translocate directly into the cytoplasm of different types of cells. At the same time, the associated enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect affords a high tumor uptake even without the targeting agent. Furthermore, without the off-resonance nanotube (non-contributing to heat generation), the sc-SWCNTs (single-chirality) have the highest light-to-heat conversion efficiency under laser irradiation with a low dose and low toxicity.

There has been a tremendous increase in the use of nanomaterials for biological applications in recent years. A wide range of nanomaterials, such as liposomes, polymeric, magnetic nanoparticles, quantum dots, carbon-based nanomaterials, gold nanoparticles, as well as cyclodextrins, have been created with promising results in vitro and in vivo for drug delivery, imaging, and therapy.262 One-dimensional nanomaterials like CNTs play a significant role in efficient biological research and development.263 They exhibit strong optical absorption in the infrared range (NIR I & II), PL, Raman scattering, and photoacoustic properties, which make them ideal candidates for monitoring in biological environments. Covalent or non-covalent functionalization of SWCNTs with drugs and targeting agents through π–π stacking allows for controlled drug release, enhanced bioactivity, and reduced toxicity.264

The inability of nanoparticles to target drug resistance and other factors is a key limitation of their use in medicine.265 These challenges can be overcome by combining therapies that use numerous drugs to overcome drug resistance, reduce drug doses, and affect various therapeutic targets.266 On the other hand, the insoluble nature of CNTs in an aqueous solution is a significant impediment to their employment as drug carriers, due to the more hydrophobic nature of the graphene side wall and the strong π–π affinity between individual tubes. Thus, Foldvari et al. recommended four fundamental approaches for the desirable dispersion of nanomaterials in a solvent: (1) side wall functionalization, (2) biomolecular dispersion, (3) solvent-dispersion, and (4) surfactant-assisted dispersion.267,268 Additionally, toxicity is influenced by numerous parameters in the biological environment, including purity, functionalization, size, and length.269 For reliable results, the uniformity and stability of the CNT dispersion in an aqueous solution are critical because they are essential for gastrointestinal absorption, blood transit, and biocompatibility.108,270–272

Functionalization has proven to be the most effective approach among those. In addition, length, purity (single-chirality), and size (diameter) are the key factors.273 This approach can reduce cytotoxicity, improve biocompatibility, and allow for drug, protein, or gene appendage molecules to be used as delivery systems.274

SWCNTs have been extensively explored in recent years for their potential use in the detection/diagnosis, monitoring, and treatment of cancer cells.275–278 The great optical absorption in the NIR region and the effective heat generation of SWCNTs make them ideal heat delivery vehicles for laser treatment of malignancies (PTT).277,279,280 Simultaneously, the increased EPR effects confer significant tumor uptake even in the absence of a targeting agent.278

SWCNT-based PTT has been considered safe, non-invasive, and effective since 2005, when NIR laser irradiation was introduced. Dai and co-workers synthesized DNA-coated SWCNTs for cancer cells (in vitro) through PTT.160 Significant cell death was seen when HeLa cells were subjected to a laser power density of 1.4 W cm−2 for two minutes in the presence of DNA-wrapped SWCNTs. Moon et al. showed that PEGylated SWCNTs injected into a tumor can exhibit a robust photothermal response when irradiated with an 808 nm laser.277 Welsher et al. investigated the biodistribution of SWCNTs (optical properties) in vivo for deep tissue and arterial imaging.67 A cisplatin was loaded into SWCNTs for efficient drug delivery, as shown by Borowiak-Palen et al.281 Zhang et al. conducted an in vivo cancer therapy using positively charged SWCNTs to transfer therapeutic siRNA into cancer cells.282 In addition, a SWCNTs-siRNA complex was directly injected into the tumor, rather than systemic injection of the siRNA. In another study, Liu et al. employed polyethyleneimine (PEI)-grafted MWCNTs for DNA binding and delivery approaches with less cytotoxicity.283 It is also necessary to investigate the ability of single-chirality SWCNTs to be used in multidrug treatments that target the intended area of interest. Thus, Cherukuri et al. studied NIR PL by using sc-SWCNTs in rabbits to track the nanotubes.284 Apart from the photothermal impact, the bandgap structure of sc-SWCNTs confer the ability to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) and PL, which are essential for photodynamic therapy (PDT) and bioimaging, respectively. Furthermore, the emission (S11) and excitation (S22) of SWCNTs inside the biological transparency window (700–1400 nm) have drawn interest in biological imaging methods using NIR fluorescence. So, the use of single-chirality is desired in order to enhance the efficiency of potential applications.97 A study was conducted to determine the significance of single-chirality and metallicity of the SWCNTs. For this, single-chirality (9,4), semiconducting, and ordinary SWCNTs were prepared under the same conditions (environment) and in the same manner (mass per unit area). Concerning biocompatibility, PEG was wrapped around the sample surface through functionalization. When all samples were placed under a light-emitting diode (LED) at a wavelength of 735 nm, single chiral (9,4) SWCNT exhibited 4-fold high excitation and a ∼15-fold higher absorption and PL intensity. It is also worth noting that the dose concentration of single-chiral (9,4)-based SWCNT is ∼4 times lower (in terms of toxicity). In addition, (9,4) and other SWCNTs were examined in mice to further explore the bioimaging of the whole body (including the inner organs, tissues, and vessels). The (9,4) gave clear vascular and deep-organ (heart, lung, and liver) imaging with a high signal-to-noise ratio and high temporal resolution with a lower dose, as shown in Fig. 20.97,285 Antaris et al. demonstrated that (6,5) SWCNTs that were resonantly photothermally heated at 980 nm had reached the requisite photothermal tumor ablation temperatures with a ∼10 fold lower injected dose than the as-synthesized SWCNT mixtures, as shown in Fig. 21.112 They claimed that on a per mass basis, single-chirality SWCNTs absorbed 10–20× more light than the optimized gold nanorods or nanoparticles when under strong resonance with a laser. They achieved the lowest dose of 0.254 mg kg−1 for intravenously injected in vivo photothermal therapy. The separated sc-SWCNTs with subpopulations that resonate with the excitation NIR had superior NIR adsorption owing to VHs.

Achieving a low dose of SWCNTs in biological systems would be possible with selective-diameter and single-chirality. It is now possible to isolate chirality-specific SWCNTs that resonate inside the biological window by using a single excitation wavelength due to the recent development in SWCNTs separation, such as (6,5), (9,4), (12,1), and (11,3).112 Separated SWCNTs without the off-resonance nanotubes would not contribute to heat generation (thermal treatment) or photoluminescence (imaging), and would significantly reduce the therapeutic doses, alleviating toxicity concerns.112 Separated SWCNTs can penetrate deeper within tissue because the excitation wavelength falls within the biological window (700–1100 nm), avoiding the absorption of biological tissue and water. For instance, the Dai group had shown that chirality-sorted SWCNTs, (12,1), and (11,3)-enriched sc-SWCNTs had ∼5 folds higher PL intensity under resonant excitation than unsorted SWNTs on the per-mass basis.289 Usually, only a small fraction of nanotubes (<9%) can be resonantly excited under a single excitation wavelength, which limits the use of nanotubes in biological systems. The chirality enrichment allows the strong emission from resonance excitation, which results in much more effective NIR-II fluorophores and in vivo NIR-II imaging. It is foreseen that by removing off-resonance nanotubes, the separated SWCNTs would have enhanced heat generation and PL. Separated SWCNTs (single-chirality) without the off-resonance nanotubes that do not contribute to heat generation or PL would significantly alleviate toxicity concerns. 9 Toxicity effects of SWCNTs

Safety (low toxicity) is the most fundamental requirement in the biological environment. Numerous studies have been conducted in recent years to determine whether or not nanomaterials are harmful. The solubility and biocompatibility of nanomaterials (SWCNTs) depend on various factors, such as material selection, surface chemistry, functionalization, length, and electronic structure. SWCNTs have the potential to cause asbestos-like pathologies, such as pulmonary and inflammatory responses, due to their string-like structure.308,309 So, the toxicity of nanomaterials (SWCNTs) on cells and tissues has been observed in both in vivo and in vitro studies. Cytotoxicity, genotoxicity, epigenetic effects, splenic toxicity, immunotoxicity, eye irritation, and skin hypersensitivity are all possible impacts of nanomaterials.310–313 Among these types of toxicity, genotoxicity is the most significant risk factor for cancer caused by SWCNTs (unmodified and functionalized).314 SWCNT toxicity is determined by those factors, such as the selection of the nanomaterial, the electrical structure, and the degree of functionalization. On the other hand, untreated nanomaterials (SWCNTs) are more harmful in a biological environment. ROS can be linked to SWCNTs in several ways, such as direct contact with biological components or indirect damage that leads to ROS. That is why it is so well-known that ROS has both positive and negative impacts on the biological environment.

Cui et al. and Ding et al. reported that CNTs induced cell cycle arrest and enhanced apoptosis/necrosis in human skin fibroblasts.315,316 According to Sayes et al., the toxicity of SWCNTs was also affected by the density of functionalization.317 Jin et al. observed that SWCNTs taken up by cells through endocytosis exited cells through exocytosis without affecting cell viability.318 Shvedova et al. demonstrated that SWCNTs were potent inducers of TGF-β, which draws macrophages to the lungs and induces inflammation.319 However, morphological alterations of SWCNTs and flow cytometry exhibited aspects of cytotoxicity, which was reported by Mittal et al.314 Thus, the toxicity impacts of SWCNTs (1D) were studied based on their size, functionalization, electronics structure, dose, administration routes, and morphologies, as shown in Fig. 22.

One of the most essential physiochemical features of nanomaterials is their particle size. Numerous studies have been conducted on various nanomaterials to determine their size-dependent biological effects and biodistribution. Brown and their colleagues found that the cytotoxicity of SWCNT was proportional to the length of the nanotubes.320 Particles with a diameter of less than 1 μm can easily penetrate cells, whereas particles with a diameter of more than 1 μm cannot cross the outermost layer of skin in most cases.321 The study further revealed that small nanoparticles were harmful and generated ROS, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and apoptosis due to their high surface-to-volume ratio, which decreases with increasing nanoparticle size.322 According to a study by Rotoli et al., only shorter SWCNTs substantially impacted the survival of human lung cancer cells.323 The shorter length SWCNTs exhibited higher toxicity, which might be due to the shorter tubes being more mobile and less likely to aggregate, making penetration easier compared to the longer tubes. Thus, nanoparticles with different sizes are used for various applications. Nanoparticles (Au) with a size of 2–15 nm have been used in tissue engineering, while 20–60 nm particles are used for drug delivery and DNA testing, and 80–250 nm particles are used for medical electrics and X-ray optics.324,325

The electronic structures (metallic and semiconducting) significantly impact the toxicity and stress response of SWCNTs. The toxicity of m-SWCNTs is much higher than that of sc-SWCNTs.326–331 Numerous studies revealed that sc-SWCNTs with comparable diameters were significantly less reactive than m-SWCNTs. The enhanced electron density and conductivity around the Fermi level of SWCNTs could be attributed to the high response of m-SWCNTs. The micrometer length and conductive properties of m-SWCNTs allow them to operate as conductive bridges over lipid bilayers, allowing cells to potentially short-circuit and release energy into the external environment. Vecitis et al. suggested that m-SWCNTs increased cellular oxidation by forming a conductive bridge over the lipid bilayer, and then helping the oxidation of specific polypeptides, like glutathione, which was important for keeping the cell's redox environment stable and leading to cell death.332,333 Furthermore, the shape of nanoparticles had an impact on the catalytic characteristics of the particles, which in turn impacted the bio-interface interaction and facilitated cellular uptake. The surface area of materials varied based on their shape, which was another consideration. A study revealed that spherical nanoparticles had a better uptake than nanorods, which had a lower resistance.334,335

The Gambhir group conducted the first in vivo toxicity research of functionalized SWCNTs.336 However, DNA damage and genotoxicity were the most common toxicity categories for both carboxylated and hydroxylated SWCNTs (functionalized), regardless of whether an excess surfactant was present or not. Prato et al. revealed that SWCNTs covalently functionalized by 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition, including primary immune cells, were safe for testing cell lines.337,338 In addition, Bertozzi et al. created CNTs that were coated with a biocompatible coating (PEG), which was determined to be safer for cell growth than other CNT coatings.339,340 It was found that carboxylated SWCNTs caused more genotoxicity, chemical stress, protein damage, and overall toxic effects than hydroxylated SWCNTs. However, the oxidative stress level was the same in both functionalized SWCNTs. Yang and their colleagues also demonstrated that SWCNTs dispersed in Tween-80, a coating that was probably not appropriate, had a low toxicity test in a biological environment (in vivo) at a high dose, and they also followed this toxicity study for three months.341 The oxidative stress induced by accumulated SWCNTs in a biological system may also contribute to toxicity. The carboxylated SWCNTs had a high permanently negative charge, and were substantially more stable in water than the uncarboxylated SWCNTs. Due to their greater water solubility and dispersion, functionalized SWCNTs had a higher chance of binding to the protein, peptide, or oligonucleotide by Yang and coworkers.342 Another group demonstrated that COOH modified short SWCNTs (1–2 μm) had higher genotoxicity than unmodified SWCNTs. Poland and colleagues observed that intraperitoneal injections of long and non-coated MWCNTs (10–50 μm) into the abdominal cavity of mice caused inflammation and lesions known as granulomas.343 Thus, the toxicity of SWCNTs would be determined by the nanotubes surface, chemical composition, and size.

10 Conclusions

This review highlighted how nanomaterials (SWCNTs) with intrinsic properties are widely used in various potential applications, including diagnostics, drug delivery, and bioimaging. Several covalent and non-covalent functionalization techniques for SWCNTs have been devised to enable the use of nanomaterials for this purpose. Functionalized SWCNTs have been used for the ultra-sensitive detection of biological species because of their electrical, optical, and electronic properties. They are also being studied for various applications, such as PTT, PDT, biosensors, and early detection of viruses. Functionalized nanomaterials can selectively aggregate and effectively ablate cancer cells by utilizing NIR light and the EPR effect in early detection. However, when nanomaterials cause hyperthermia in response to NIR irradiation, DNA damage and protein destabilization can occur, which leads to tumor death. Furthermore, by utilizing SWCNTs that have been irradiated with NIR light, oxygen in the surrounding environment can be transformed into ROS. Moreover, functionalized SWCNTs with biocompatible coatings (PEG) are durable in biological spots and exhibit nontoxic properties when exposed to NIR light or used for drug delivery. SWCNTs used for tumor targeting are capable of both passive targeting via the EPR effect and active targeting via designed ligands, which have the potential to assist in cancer treatment.

In this infectious and pathogenic environment, viral diagnostic tools are essential. Therefore, biosensor-based techniques have the potential to significantly reduce the spread of respiratory viral infections such as COVID-19 because of their high throughput, low cost, and rapid detection. Herein, we review the recent advancements in the fabrication/detection of electrochemical, optical, and FET biosensing for COVID-19 detection and how these sensors can meet the time requirement. Additionally, the multiplex detection of viral antigens and antibodies is possible with sc-SWCNT biosensor detection approaches.

Apart from their inherent properties, SWCNTs can be loaded with anticancer drugs such as DOX and metformin for tumor therapy. Surface functionalization chemistry is a significant and vital factor in binding proteins and drugs with nanomaterials. A coating such as long and branching PEG on SWCNTs provides inertness in the biological environment. Furthermore, it reduces toxicity and non-specific binding, thereby making them more biocompatible. Thus, the reduction of non-specific binding is significant in SWCNT-based biosensors and bioimaging because it increases the chance of detection sensitivity. Non-specific endocytosis of SWCNTs is reduced by the absence of non-specific binding, which favors the selective administration of drugs to specific cell types. Moreover, the SWCNTs are strongly influenced by their surface coating in the biological environment (in vivo). SWCNTs with a hydrophilic coating can extend blood circulation and lower the RES uptake. The use of single-chiralities and size of SWCNTs in biological sensing and imaging results in further improvement, which is attributed to better tumor targeting.

The intrinsic properties of the SWCNTs have been reviewed based on their specific subpopulations. Recently, how to sort the single-chirality, selective-diameter, and particular shapes of SWCNTs to use their unique electrical and electronic properties has been explored. Gel chromatography is among the most significant separation methods because of its simplicity and ease of repetition to achieve the specific subspecies (with distinct parameters). However, the separation mechanism remains a subject of debate, where the understanding of the self-assembly structure of the surfactant on SWCNT would improve the separation process. Surfactant adsorption on SWCNTs may alter the structure and morphology of the surfactant, as well as its structural composition. Inspired by the redox effect, the temperature effect could suppress the oxidation, hence overcome the deficiency of gel chromatography in separation of single-chirality and selective-diameter. To resolve and elute the separated fraction with a single co-surfactant system, researchers have found that the temperature governs the interaction between nanotubes and surfactants, which simplified the iterative separation largely. As a result, the high purity sc- and m- achieved.

Furthermore, the light penetration depth is a significant concern in phototherapy for cancer treatment. Due to lower light absorption and scattering in the NIR window, NIR absorbing photothermal agents are definitely favored over those with visible absorption. The NIR light penetrates tissues at a depth of less than 1 cm. The photothermal impact of SWCNTs can significantly reduce tumor growth in superficial tumors such as melanoma. Still, for deeper tumors (bone cancer), the efficacy of PTT is limited by the depth to which the laser penetrates. Thus, single-chiralities play a vital role due to their electronic structure and different wavelength absorption. By using single-chirality, the dose concentration will be reduced, absorbing more light energy at relevant frequencies and emitting more heat at the tumor site to kill cancer, as compared to other nanomaterials and mixed chiralities of SWCNTs. In bioimaging, the single-chiralities also help to see the inner organs of the body up to ∼10-fold higher than the others. In recent work, we observed that researchers preferred semiconducting single-chirality over semiconducting materials as a biosensor for COVID-19 detection, which gave better results due to its bandgap and subspecies. The size of the nanomaterials significantly affects their retention and penetration at the tumor site. A diameter range of 50–100 nm exhibits prolonged blood circulation and a high rate of tumor accumulation due to the EPR effects. The geometric formation also impacts the application's use due to its shape and length. Among them, the coiled geometric shape ensured stability under the strong sonication pressure and is further utilized in photothermal applications.

In addition, the functionalization alters the level of toxicity, whereas the carboxylated one causes more damage to DNA and proteins than the hydroxylated one. Carboxylated SWCNTs have a stronger permanent negative charge and significant water solubility. The m- and sc-SWCNTs were also found to have significantly different toxicity levels and stress responses, which were shown to have higher toxicity for m-SWCNTs than sc-SWCNTs. So, low dose (single-chirality) and biocompatible coatings are essential due to toxicity factors. Thus, the selective-diameter and single-chirality of nanotubes have the potential to revolutionize semiconductor and biological applications, which is one of the ultimate aims of nanotube research. The single-chirality of SWCNTs can be used as a distinct color in NIR PL-imaging and Raman scattering because all nanotubes can be in resonance with a single excitation wavelength. Based on these aspects (single-chirality, selective-diameter, and specific geometric shape), SWCNTs are useful and ideal candidates for healthcare, early detection, therapy, and other potential applications.
