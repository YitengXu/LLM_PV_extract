Abstract

Piezoelectric and thermoelectric materials that can directly convert mechanical and thermal energies into electricity have attracted great interest because of their practical applications in overcoming the challenges of the energy crisis. In this research, a new family of two-dimensional (2D) group-VI Janus ternary compounds with Î± and Î³ phases are predicted. After the stability testing, only the Î±-TeSSe monolayer has dynamic and thermal stability. The band structure and the optic, piezoelectric, and thermoelectric performances of the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer are calculated via first-principles calculations. Janus Î±-TeSSe is a narrow indirect bandgap semiconductor with a value of 0.953 eV at the HSE06 functional considering the spinâorbit coupling (SOC), which is beneficial to its thermoelectric performance, and its excellent absorption coefficients indicate that it may be a promising optoelectronic material. The piezoelectric calculations show that Janus Î±-TeSSe exhibits not only appreciable in-plane piezoelectricity (d11 = 17.17 pm Vâ1) but also superior vertical piezoelectricity (d31 = 0.22 pm Vâ1). Furthermore, a new TransOpt code is used to calculate the electrical transport coefficients with a constant electronâphonon coupling approximation, which is more accurate than the constant relaxation time approximation. The origin of ultralow lattice thermal conductivity is also discussed in detail. Finally, ultrahigh ZT values of 0.77 and 1.95 occur in n-type and p-type doping at 600 K, respectively, indicating that it is a promising thermoelectric material. Our work demonstrates that Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayers have potential applications in optoelectronic, piezoelectric, and thermoelectric devices, which will greatly stimulate research-related experiments.

1. Introduction

Thermoelectric materials1,2 that can directly convert waste heat into electrical energy and piezoelectric materials3â6 that can directly convert mechanical energy into electrical energy have attracted great interest, due to the possibility of overcoming the challenges of global warming and the energy crisis. Thus, it is of great significance to design and find excellent thermoelectric and piezoelectric materials. The Janus structure, which has a particular structure with inversion asymmetry and is different from traditional 2D materials, has attracted great attention because of its novel properties and potential application in electronics, optoelectronics, piezoelectrics, and thermoelectrics.3,5,7â10 In recent years, typical Janus transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs)5,8,11â16 have been widely studied after the Janus MoSSe monolayer was first successfully synthesized from MoS2 through chemical vapor deposition (CVD) by Li et al. in 2017.10 Compared to MoS2, Janus TMD monolayers lack reflection symmetry for the central metal atoms, resulting in out-of-plane electric polarization. Thus, modifying the conventional 2D materials with intrinsic centrosymmetry to form Janus structures is an effective way to generate piezoelectric properties.17

It is well-known that the properties of materials strongly depend on their crystal structure. Typical 2H (Î³) and 1T (Î±) phases are usually present in 2D graphene-like hexagonal structures.9,15,18,19 In general, the 2H-phase is more stable than the 1T-phase in group-VIB (Cr, Mo, W, Fe) TMDs/TMOs.18,19 However, the 1T-phase hexagonal structure is more stable than the 2H-phase hexagonal structure in group-VI 2D selenium and tellurium.20â22 A new family of 2D materials, group-VI elemental 2D materials, has emerged in recent years. These materials have superior properties such as high carrier mobility, high photoconductivity, and thermoelectric responses.23 Zhu et al.22 and Chen et al.24 predicted and successfully synthesized tellurene on highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) substrates by using molecular beam epitaxy in 2017. Subsequently, based on liquid-phase exfoliation, Xie et al.25 also synthesized 2D tellurene nanosheets, which had superior electronic transport properties26 and could be used in high-performance field-effect transistors.27 Moreover, previous reports20,28 indicated that tellurene and selenene had extraordinary thermoelectric performances due to their ultralow lattice thermal conductivity kl.20,28,29 Recent studies have suggested that the compounds composed of Te and Se have excellent thermoelectric and electronic transport properties.30,31 However, no theoretical and experimental investigations focused on the piezoelectric and thermoelectric properties of 2D group-VI Janus ternary compounds have been reported to date.

Unlike traditional Janus TMDs, the central atom of the group-VI Janus ternary compounds is not a transition metal. Furthermore, tellurene20,28,29 and selenene20,28 have the lowest recorded kl among the family of 2D materials to date. Therefore, we assume that the group-VI Janus ternary compounds consisting of tellurium and selenium may possess excellent piezoelectric and thermoelectric performances. Motivated by unique Janus structures consisting of group-VI elements and potential applications in piezoelectric and thermoelectric performance devices, we systematically studied the family of group-VI Janus ternary compounds. The results of this study show that the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer has excellent optic, piezoelectric and thermoelectric properties, which will have promising applications in nanoelectronics, piezoelectrics, and thermoelectrics. The rest of this work is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the computational methods and details. Section 3 presents the results and discussions, including the stability, electronic properties, piezoelectricity, electron transport properties, phonon transport properties, and thermoelectricity. Conclusions are drawn in Section 4.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Stability and electronic properties

Typical 2D graphene-like hexagonal structures mainly contain two phases, 2H (Î³) and 1T (Î±).9,15,18,19 In this paper, we focus on the group-VI ternary Janus structure with those two phases belonging to the P3m1 (No. 156) space group, in which the centrosymmetric structure is broken because the two sides are atoms of different elements. Six Janus monolayers can be obtained according to the type of atoms at the central location, as shown in Fig. S1 (ESIâ ) and Fig. 1. We first evaluate the stability of all these Janus structures with Î³-phase and Î±-phase using phonon dispersion, as shown in Fig. S2 and S3(a) (ESIâ ). The calculated results imply that only Janus Î±-TeSSe is dynamically stable due to the absence of an imaginary frequency.52 Thus, only Janus Î±-TeSSe is taken into account in the rest of this work.

As shown in Fig. 1, Janus Î±-TeSSe possesses a 1T-MoS2-like structure and belongs to C3v point group symmetry. For the Î±-TeSSe monolayer, a central Te atom is located at the Mo site, while an S and a Se atom in the lower or upper layer are located at the S sites. The optimized lattice parameters are a = b = 3.905 Ã and the out-of-plane thickness is 3.195 Ã. Considering the van der Waals (vdW) radius of elements the effective thickness is 6.895 Ã.29

Fig. 2(a) represents the band structure of the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer for different functionals, such as PBE, PBE + SOC, HSE06, and HSE06 + SOC. It is revealed that the PBE calculations underestimate the bandgap by about 0.5 eV compared with HSE06, which is common in other reports.8,55,56 As a common rule, the spinâorbit coupling (SOC) that splits the energy level plays a vital role in the electronic band structure when heavy atoms are present in the materials. Thus, to obtain an accurate calculation for the electronic band structures, the effect of the SOC is taken into account in our calculations based on PBE and HSE06 functionals. It is conspicuous that the inclusion of the SOC removes the spin degeneracy, and the conduction band maximum (CBM) experiences obvious band splitting. The bandgaps of Janus TeSSe are 0.476 eV and 0.953 eV based on the PBE + SOC and HSE06 + SOC levels, respectively, and this feature of the narrow bandgap of semiconductors benefits the thermoelectric performance.57,58 Since the calculated result based on the scheme of the HSE06 + SOC functional is closer to the experimental value, the following calculations of the related properties adopt this method. From Fig. 2(b), it is notable that the total density of states (DOS) near the valence band maximum (VBM) is mainly contributed by S and Se atoms, while the DOS near the CBM is mainly contributed by Te and Se atoms, suggesting that relatively light atoms dominate the DOS near the VBM while relatively heavy atoms dominate the DOS near the CBM. This phenomenon has also occurred in previous calculations.59

The optical properties are the basis of many important applications of semiconductors, such as optoelectronics and nanoelectronics. 3.3 Piezoelectricity

It is essential to calculate the elastic stiffness coefficients before evaluating the piezoelectricity. The relaxed-ion elastic stiffness coefficients are calculated by the finite-difference method,6,35,36 as summarized in Table 1. The elastic stiffness coefficients fully satisfy the mechanic stability criteria,70C11 > 0 and C11 > |C12|, confirming that the Î±-TeSSe monolayer is mechanically stable.

A useful measure of anharmonicity is given by the GrÃ¼neisen parameter Î³,91 where a0 is the equilibrium lattice constant, i is the phonon branch index, and q is the wave vector. We calculate Î³ for all acoustic and optical modes of the TeSSe monolayer, as shown in Fig. 8(b). Since a large Î³ implies a large anharmonicity that can severely limit the phonon transport and lead to a relatively low kl,29,92,93 we uncover a giant ZA mode anharmonicity at the long-wavelength limit, as also reported for single-layer TMDs94 and SnX2 (X = S, Se).88

To gain further insight regarding the atomic anharmonic interactions, the total phonon scattering rates as shown in Fig. 8(c) are also calculated. Compared with other Janus monolayers, such as M2XY (M = Ga; X, Y = S, Se, Te),12 one can easily see that TeSSe possesses a larger total phonon scattering rate determined by three phonon transition probabilities. The phonon scattering rate is inversely proportional to the phonon lifetimes, which have a significant impact on the anharmonicity and attenuation of the lattice thermal conductivity.29 Combined with the above discussion, we deduce that the ultralow kl originates from the fact that the Janus TeSSe monolayer has the characteristics of a small phonon group velocity, a giant GrÃ¼neisen parameter, and a large phonon scattering rate.

Then, by employing the calculated PF, ke, and kl, we determine the dimensionless figure of merit as depicted in Fig. 9 using the formula: ZT = S2ÏT/(ke + kl). Normally, a larger figure of merit implies a better thermoelectric performance of the materials and vice versa. Fig. 9(a and b) shows the figure of merit of the n-type and p-type doping Janus Î±-TeSSe at different doping levels as a function of the temperature, which ranges from 300 K to 600 K. It is worth noting that the value of ZT for n-type doping exhibits the opposite behavior with temperature compared with that of p-type doping. For the p-type case, ZT increases with the temperatures, as is common in most other reports,8,55,95 mainly due to both ke and kl decreasing with the increase in the temperature. However, the value of ZT decreases slightly for n-type doping as also reported in the SiTe2 monolayer.85 This can be attributed to the fact that when the temperatures vary from 300 K to 600 K, the PF decreases significantly, mainly due to the decrease of the electronic conductivity Ï (see Fig. S6, ESIâ ). Additionally, kl decreases significantly with increasing temperature, which compensates for the decrease in the PF, and thus leads to the maximum value of ZT being almost unchanged. Furthermore, the p-type doping thermoelectric performance is superior to that of n-type doping, which is related to a larger DOS near the VBM.30,79 This shows that the Janus TeSSe exhibits excellent ZT, such as 0.79, 0.78, and 0.77 for n-type doping, as well as 0.89, 1.15, and 1.95 for p-type doping at 300 K, 400 K, and 600 K, respectively, which makes it a promising candidate for thermoelectric applications.

4. Conclusions

In this paper, based on first-principles calculations, the band structure, and the optic, piezoelectric, and thermoelectric performances of the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer are analyzed. The phonon dispersion and AIMD simulations confirm that the Î±-TeSSe monolayer is dynamically and thermally stable. Janus Î±-TeSSe is a narrow bandgap semiconductor, which is beneficial for thermoelectric performance. The ELF is also systematically studied. With such outstanding absorption coefficients, the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer may be a promising optoelectronic device. Furthermore, the DFPT calculations confirm that Janus Î±-TeSSe has an excellent piezoelectric response with the coexistence of in-plane and out-of-plane piezoelectric coefficients d11 (17.17 pm Vâ1) and d31 (0.22 pm Vâ1), indicating its potential application in piezoelectric devices.

Finally, the electronic transport and phonon transport properties are evaluated. The hole (electron) mobility follows Tâ1.23 (Tâ2.03) behavior due to the lattice scattering mechanism being dominant. The ultralow lattice thermal conductivity occurs due to the small phonon group velocity, large phonon scattering rate, and large GrÃ¼neisen parameter. The EFF at room temperature is also calculated, and it describes the band complexity concerning thermoelectric behavior. Moreover, combined with ultralow thermal conductivity and outstanding electrical properties, ultrahigh ZT values are found for p-type doping of the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer, indicating that it is a good p-type thermoelectric material. In conclusion, our research shows that the Janus Î±-TeSSe monolayer has broad application prospects in the fields of photovoltaics, piezoelectrics, and thermoelectrics, which will greatly stimulate related experiments.
