High temperature sodium batteries: status, challenges and future trends†

Abstract

The progress in the research and development of high temperature sodium batteries suggests that all-solid-state batteries with inorganic or polymer solid electrolytes are promising power sources for a wide range of applications due to their high thermal stability, reliability, long-cycle life and versatile geometries. The electrolytes play a fundamental role in terms of current (power) density, the time stability, and the safety of batteries and, as a result, their continuous improvement and innovation are indeed critical to success. In fact, inorganic solid electrolytes pave the way for improving the cost-effective development of rechargeable sodium batteries. This review describes a state-of-the-art overview of most of the Na+ conductors for use as electrolytes in sodium/sulphur and ZEBRA batteries. The emphasis of this article is on inorganic solid electrolytes, especially, ceramic and glass-ceramic electrolytes as promising alternatives applicable to all solid-state batteries. As part of a continuous effort to find new materials that operate at room temperature and moderate temperatures, NASICON electrolytes will also be considered. Polymer electrolytes based on poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) are also very suitable for all solid-state batteries. Hence, the review focuses on ion transport based on the observed conductivity, electrolyte preparation, safety and environmental impact.

Broader context

On a broader scale, energy innovation plays an important role in the reduction of carbon emission in the atmosphere and global warming. In consequence, advances are needed in energy storage technology for the integration of intermittent renewable energy resources on electricity markets and grids. The low-cost and high abundance of sodium versus lithium make sodium batteries a promising technology in the long term. Na battery technology is regarded as promising in the future of stationary energy storage and battery electric vehicles (BEVs). For this purpose, the development of novel electrode and electrolyte materials is needed. Although inorganic or polymer solid electrolytes play a fundamental role in the successful progress of all solid-state rechargeable sodium batteries, they have received less attention until very recently. For this reason, this article focuses on solid electrolytes for next generation batteries.

1 Introduction

Energy storage technology will be of vital importance in the near future as a result of energy demand and the gradual increase in the price of fossil fuels and their environmental impact. Energy storage systems are required not only for load leveling because of the intermittent nature of energy sources (solar, wind and tide), but also for energy usage (peak vs. off-peak hours). Such cells would smooth fluctuations in the electrical grid, thereby optimizing energy collection, storage, and usage. Batteries represent an excellent energy storage technology for the integration of renewable resources. Due to the fact that many global lithium reserves are located in remote or in politically sensitive areas and the high cost of lithium-ion batteries, Na and Na-ion batteries have re-emerged as candidates for medium and large-scale stationary energy storage. The use of a naturally abundant element, low cost and very suitable redox potential (E°(Na+/Na) = −2.71 V versus standard hydrogen electrode; only 0.3 V positive of lithium), means that rechargeable electrochemical cells based on sodium also hold much promise for energy storage applications.2,3 All-solid-state batteries with inorganic solid electrolytes are promising power sources for a wide range of applications because of their safety, long-cycle life and versatile geometries.4 However, not only cathodic and anodic materials must be optimized, but also new electrolytes should be synthesized as they are the key points for the success of Na batteries.5

Among the possible solid electrolytes, ceramic or polymer electrolytes each provide specific advantages such as easy handling, high thermal stability, low self-discharge rates, operable over a wide range of environmental conditions, durability, reliability, no flammable components and without the need for containment as for liquid electrolytes. The high elastic moduli of ceramics make them more suitable for rigid battery designs whereas the low elastic moduli of polymers are useful for flexible battery designs. The main advantages of the solid ceramic electrolytes are their simple manipulation, versatility, high stability, long life, high performance, safe operation and no leakage.

Ionic conduction in ceramic compounds occurs by the movement of ionic point defects, the creation and/or movement of which requires energy, so the conductivity follows the Arrhenius law with activation energy in the range of 0.25 to 1 eV. Therefore, ceramic solid electrolytes are suitable for high-temperature applications.6 Sodium–sulphur (Na–S) and ZEBRA batteries have been the high temperature systems commercially available up to this point of time.

1.1 Sodium–sulphur batteries, Na–S

Li–S cells are receiving intense interest for applications such as transportation due to a high theoretical specific energy (2567 W h kg−1).11,12 In the same way, sodium–sulphur (Na–S) batteries (760 W h kg−1, with a low rate of self-discharge) can meet the future requirements economically, efficiently and also in terms of reliability, but in this case for advanced large-scale energy storage systems. These batteries exhibit reasonable power and energy densities, temperature stability, and, moreover, low cost because of its abundant raw materials and suitability for high volume mass production. Other advantages of Na–S technology are cycling flexibility and low maintenance requirements.13 In addition, Na–S batteries are environmentally benign, since these batteries are sealed and allow no emissions during operation. More than 99% of the overall weight of the battery materials can be recycled (the steel, copper and aluminium). Only sodium must be handled as a hazardous material.14 However, processes for recycling sodium and sulphur from these batteries are apparently still not developed.15

Since the development of Na–S batteries in the 1970s, sodium and sulphur have been utilized as the anode and cathode active materials, and sodium-conducting β-alumina ceramic as the electrolyte separating both electrodes. The sulphur impregnates a graphite felt for electron exchange. The use of the light elements, Na and S, endows the Na–S system with large capacity and, high energy density (not only in mass, but also in volume 2584 W h l−1), making them suitable storage batteries.16 Actually, elemental sulphur for positive active materials has a very high theoretical specific capacity of 1675 mA h g−1 based on two electron change reaction. In addition, elemental sulphur is a low-cost, safe and environmental friendly material. The electrode and overall Na–S cell reactions can be described as in eqn (1)–(3).

 
(Anode) 2Na ⇔ 2Na+ + 2e−(1)
 
(Cathode) xS + 2e− ⇔ Sx2−(2)
 
(Overall cell) 2Na + xS ⇔ Na2Sx, E = 2.08–1.78 V(3)

The reaction products, sodium polysulphides (Na2Sx), coexist in the liquid state, yielding high electrode reactivity. Initially, at 2.075 V (see Fig. 4) a two-phase region of sulphur and sodium polysulphide (Na2S5) is present, as these two liquids are immiscible at the operating temperature.


Fig. 4 Na–S cell voltage profile with the different phases that exist at each stage-of-charge. (Derived from ref. 17.)

As the cell is discharged, all S combines with Na to form a single phase region (Na2S4). At 1.74 V reduction to Na2S3 occurs and at deeper discharge solid Na2S2 is formed. At this point, increased resistance at the positive electrode prohibits further discharge.17 The round-trip ac-to-ac efficiency of the Na–S system is approximately 80%. The estimated life of a Na–S battery is practically 15 years after 4500 cycles at 90% depth of discharge. However, despite the high specific capacity of sulphur cathodes, low cost and availability, wide operation temperature range and the possibility of long cycling, Na–S represents only the third ranking in installed capacity (≈3600 MW h) worldwide (see ref. 1).

It is known that the beta-alumina solid electrolyte (BASE) avoids self-discharge, because it is a good conductor of sodium ions but an insulator for electrons. When sodium gives off an electron, the Na+ migrates to the sulphur container. The electron drives an electric current through the molten sodium to the contact, through the electrical load and back to the sulphur container. Here, another electron reacts with sulphur to form S42−, sodium polysulphide. The discharge process can be represented in eqn (3) and graphically in Fig. 5, whereas a schematic diagram of a Na–S battery is shown in Fig. 6.

Although the theoretical gravimetric capacity of the sulphur electrode is 1675 mA h g−1 based on full reduction to Na2S, most reports fail to achieve one-third of this capacity (see ref. 2). Therefore, the highest disadvantage of this cell is the limited gravimetric capacity of the positive electrode (836 mA h g−1) caused by the polysulphide formed as by-products during the process of discharge (see Fig. 4). Another drawback of Na–S batteries is the necessity to be operated at temperatures ≈300 °C to use liquid-state sulphur and sulphide positive electrodes and sodium negative electrodes, and to enhance the conductivity of the β′′-alumina solid electrolyte (see ref. 4). The high operating temperature forces a fraction of energy to be used to maintain this operating temperature, resulting in a lower overall efficiency (87%).18 Besides, at the operating temperatures (270–350 °C), molten sulphur, sodium and the polysulphide compounds are highly corrosive, therefore resistant containers such as high chromium steel and molybdenum-lined steels have to be used in these cells (see ref. 2).

Another concern is the possibility of a violent uncontained reaction between molten sodium and molten sulphur in case of a rupture of the thin and fragile β′′-alumina ceramic membrane. In fact, a recent fire incident related to Na–S batteries made by NGK Insulators temporarily stopped their use as a precautionary measure. For safety reasons, Na–S batteries are considered unfavourable for transportation and they are being exploited predominantly for stationary storage.19 The Na–S batteries are available for grid applications, with 200 installations worldwide, accounting for 315 MW of discharge power capacity.20

NGK Insulators, Ltd. has implemented countermeasures since the incident occurred on September 21, 2011 involving the Na–S batteries for electricity storage. The main causes of the fire were: one cell out of 384 battery cells which form the modular battery was faulty and leaked hot molten material, making a short circuit between battery cells in an adjoining block. There was no fuse installed between the battery cells so the short circuit current flowed continuously and emitted heat, which destroyed other battery cells and they caught fire.21 The safety enhancement measures to prevent the spread of fire in modular batteries were installing fuses between battery cells in modular batteries, insulation boards between blocks and anti-fire boards between battery modules.

Other critical components of these batteries are seals, which must not only be hermetic in the 300 to 350 °C range but also withstand the Na and S vapours and/or contact with the highly reactive molten electrode materials. Recent studies show that the glass-ceramic sealants can be highly resistant to sodium and that the thermal expansion coefficient (TEC) of these materials can be modulated to match with those of α-alumina and β-alumina. Song et al.22 studied the borosilicate glass with TiO2 as the nucleating agent. Moreover, in their work, Y2O3 was doped to modulate the TECs, and CaF2 was added to decrease the viscosity of the borosilicate glass. The TEC of the sealants was 6–7 × 10−6 °C after heat treatment. In addition, the thermal shock resistance of the seal was high, and no microcracking appeared after 100 thermal cycles.

Also Na/Ni3S2 cells with liquid electrolytes have successfully been developed.23 However, today's tendency is the development of solid-state batteries because cells with liquid electrolytes may form dendritic growths that may cause short circuits, similar to lithium batteries cycled at room temperature.24 Therefore, a safer alternative is to use a solid electrolyte that acts as a barrier to prevent dendritic growths and replace flammable solvents (see ref. 18).

From a safety viewpoint, developing low-cost Na–S batteries with high-capacity energy storage systems that operate at lower temperature is strongly desirable in order to overcome the hurdles described above. All-solid-state batteries are the promising power sources for a wide range of applications because of their long-cycle life, versatile geometries, low cost and safety. Moreover, these batteries do not suffer from leakage, volatilization, or flammability.

Although ambient temperature sodium batteries are in their early stage of development, they are highly promising for large-scale grid storage applications due to the abundance and very low cost of sodium-containing precursors used to make the components.25 In order to meet the challenge of developing solid-state batteries that operate at ambient and moderate temperatures, solid electrolytes that have high Na+ ion conductivities at room temperature and close contact with electrode active materials are indispensable (see ref. 4). Therefore, the low temperature Na–S cell entails new approaches to devise protective layers for both the positive and negative electrode, and the development of improved electrolytes holds promise for the future of these batteries (see ref. 2). One strategy to develop a design that works at low temperature is to use NASICON membranes to allow operation at 90 °C with all components remaining solid. On the other hand, since the design of these batteries is based on many fragile parts involving ceramic electrolyte membrane, glass (ceramic) sealants, and ceramic insulators, more contributions to improve all the ceramic materials should be made.

1.2 ZEBRA batteries

The ZEBRA batteries also have gained high attention due to the number of useful characteristics such as high energy density (120 W h kg−1), high voltage, maintenance free operation, no gassing, and zero self-discharge.26 The safety features of this storage technology make ZEBRA batteries particularly suitable for electric and hybrid vehicles. They can also be used for stationary applications in support to renewable energy for load-leveling.

These cells contain a liquid sodium negative electrode, a metal chloride positive electrode, usually NiCl2, and β′′-Al2O3 as an electrolyte and a separator. Addition of iron to the cell increases the power response. The charge–discharge reactions and potentials versus sodium at 300 °C are shown:

 
(Anode) 2Na ⇔ 2Na+ + 2e−(4)
 
(Cathode) NiCl2 + 2Na+ + 2e− ⇔ Ni + 2NaCl(5)
 
(Overall cell) NiCl2 + 2Na ⇔ Ni + 2NaCl, E = 2.58 V(6)

In the case of iron chloride the homologous overall cell reaction is as follows:

 
FeCl2 + 2Na ⇔ Fe + 2NaCl, E = 2.35 V(7)

The cathode has a porous structure of nickel and salt NaCl which is impregnated with NaAlCl4, a 50/50 mixture of NaCl and AlCl3. The sodium ion transport during the discharge process is depicted in Fig. 7. Sodium ions resulting from the oxidation of sodium at the negative electrode are transported through the β′′-Al2O3 electrolyte to NiCl2 by the secondary electrolyte (NaCl and AlCl3). The ZEBRA battery typically operates at 300 °C or above due to the high ohmic resistance of a thick beta-alumina solid electrolyte (BASE) and the necessity of the molten secondary electrolyte NaAlCl4 (melting point = 157 °C) in the cathode so as to ensure facile sodium ion transport between the BASE and solid cathode materials. The nucleation of Ni° and NaCl is also facilitated.

Another function of the NaAlCl4 salt is to overcome the problems of overcharge reactions. The charge capacity of the ZEBRA cell is determined by the quantity of salt (NaCl) available in the cathode. When the charge voltage is continuously applied in a fully charged cell, the liquid salt NaAlCl4 supplies a sodium reservoir following the reversible reaction:27

2NaAlCl4 + Ni ↔ 2Na° + Ni(AlCl4)2

This overcharge reaction requires a higher voltage than the normal charge as illustrated in Fig. 9.


Fig. 9 Na/NiCl2 (ZEBRA) cell reactions at 300 °C. Theoretical specific energy: 790 W h kg−1, under normal operation. (Derived from ref. 27.)

The overcharge reaction has three favorable consequences: (a) any further charge current is stopped automatically as soon as the increased open circuit voltage equalises the charger voltage. (b) If cells fail in parallel strings in a battery, the remaining cells in the string with the failed cells can be overcharged in order to balance the voltage of the failed cells. (c) For a vehicle with a fully charged battery which goes downhill there is an overcharge capacity of up to 5% for regenerative breaking so that the braking behaviour of the vehicle is fundamentally unchanged.

Moreover, as from the very first charge the cell has a surplus of sodium in the anode compartment so that for an over-discharge tolerance sodium is available to maintain the current flow at a lower voltage. The ZEBRA battery exhibits a number of advantages over the Na–S battery, which include higher voltage, better tolerance against overcharging and easiness of assembly in the discharged state with NaCl, AlCl3, nickel and iron powder. ZEBRA cells are produced in the discharged state so that no metallic sodium is to be handled and all the required sodium is inserted as salt. In addition, the positive electrode is comprised mostly of solid materials which reduces corrosiveness and makes the cell intrinsically safer than the Na–S battery. On the other hand, ZEBRA battery performance has been optimized to solve several technical issues such as the formation of low-conducting nickel chloride, the solubility of nickel chloride in NaAlCl4 melt and cell capacity fade with time. These problems have been mitigated by using additives such as sodium bromide, sodium iodide, sulphur, aluminium, iron, and sulphide.29

The addition of NaF and other sodium halides (NaI and NaBr) is beneficial in suppressing the FeCl2 dissolution and provides a degree of overcharge protection. However, the formation of AlF3 on the surface of β′′-Al2O3 is the cause of the resistance increase and subsequent failure of the cells cycled at 260 °C. Therefore, as NaI and NBr provide the same benefits associated with NaF, then the formation of AlF3 whose melting point is 1291 °C can be avoided. Nevertheless, capacity loss in the cells cycled at 350 °C can be attributed to the growth of Ni particle size. A combination of high temperature, increased NiCl2 dissolution in the molten electrolyte and the formation of Ni3S2 phase are responsible for Ni grain growth in high temperature cells as reported by Petric and Hosseinifar.30

ZEBRA cells can be connected in parallel and in series. Different battery types have been made with one to five parallel strings, up to 220 cells in series and 100–500 cells in one battery pack. Between every second cell there is a cooling plate through which ambient air is circulated. For thermal insulation and mechanical support the cells are surrounded by a double walled vacuum insulation typically 25 mm thick. ZEBRA cells may be of practical use in electric vehicles especially buses if the synthesis and processing of the β′′-alumina components are optimized (see ref. 2). Another interesting strategy is lowering the temperature (≤200 °C) to improve cycle life. These may decrease the degradation mechanisms due to particle ripening and side reactions that occur in the cathode at high operating temperatures. The reduced temperature technology may also enable the use of low cost materials of sealing and construction providing an easier path towards broad market penetration for grid storage. A new low-temperature design has been proposed by Lemmon et al.31 They cycled a ZEBRA battery with a planar composite BASE (α-Al2O3/yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) discs sintered in loose β′′-Al2O3 powder) under the C/3 rate at 175 °C. This study concludes that the operating temperature gave significant influences on the stability of cell performance during cycling. Overall, the higher operating temperature resulted in the more rapid increase in cell polarization with cycling, 55%. Increase in end-of-charge polarization was observed at 280 °C after 60 cycles, while little changes were found in the cell tested at 175 °C (see Fig. 11). Moreover, the performance degradation at higher temperatures was due to the particle growth of both nickel and sodium chloride (∼50 μm in size) in the cathode. Therefore, these results indicate the feasibility of cell operation below 200 °C resulting in a better stability.

2 Electrolyte materials

The electrolytes play a fundamental role in terms of current (power) density, the time stability, and the safety of the battery. Besides the ionic conductivity, a good electrochemical stability and compatibility with the electrodes are also desirable in electrolyte optimization. The electrochemical stability is evaluated by considering the anode/electrolyte interfacial resistance, the electrochemical window and the electrolyte behaviour during the battery cycling test. The solid electrolyte is the critical part of the cell configuration; therefore, to deliver the expected performance the following important requirements should be met:32 (i) high ionic conductivity to reduce the resistance polarization effects in a solid state battery. (ii) Need to be thermodynamically stable and to perform well in a wide temperature range. (iii) Should be physically and chemically compatible with the anode and cathode.

In order to give a global view of the sodium electrolytes for the described batteries, different types of inorganic solid electrolytes such as beta-alumina, glass, glass-ceramic and NASICON-type are described along this section, among others. In some cases, lithium electrolytes will be included to compare their ionic conductivity with the sodium ones.

2.1 Beta-alumina

β-Alumina solid electrolyte (BASE) is historically the first fast ion conductor material used in both Na–S and Na-metal chloride batteries so as to take advantage of the increased conductivity of this solid electrolyte at elevated temperatures. There are two distinct crystal structures: β-Al2O3 (hexagonal: P63/mmc; a0 = 0.559, c0 = 2.261 mm) and β′′-Al2O3 (rhombohedral: R3m; a0 = 0.560, c0 = 3.395 mm). The β-Al2O3 has a stoichiometry of Na2O·(8–11)Al2O3, while the β′′-Al2O3, which exhibits higher ionic conductivity, has a lower alumina content with an ideal formula of Na2O·(5–7)Al2O3. Therefore, they differ in chemical stoichiometry and the stacking sequence of oxygen ion across the conduction layer. The basic unit consists of spinel blocks and conduction planes. The spinel block is a four layer ABCA stack of oxygen ions with aluminium ions and the conduction planes or slabs contain fewer O atoms and more room for sodium cations. One full unit cell requires three sets of this pairing. Fig. 12 shows the ABCA oxygen layer of the spinel blocks and conduction planes of the structure.33
Fig. 12 Crystal structure of β′′-Al2O3 showing the spinel blocks identified with D and conduction slabs by H. Large circles are O2−. Medium-sized shaded circles are Na+ and the small solid ones are Al sites while checkerboard circles are Al2 sites where stabilizer ions substitute (Mg2+, Li+, etc.). (Derived from ref. 33.)

There are a variety of methods to synthesise β′′-Al2O3 powders such as via a conventional solid-state reaction,34–37 the sol–gel process,38–46 the co-precipitation technique,47,48 the spray-freeze/freeze-drying method (see ref. 44 and 45), flame spray pyrolysis,49 microwave heating50 and mechanochemical methods.51 The β′′-Al2O3 phase was found to give a better ionic conductivity than the β-Al2O3 phase. Nevertheless, in the solid-state reaction route and the solution-based chemical methods it is difficult to obtain a uniform product because the synthesized β′′-Al2O3 is often epitaxially mixed with β-Al2O3 and with remnant NaAlO2 distributed along grain boundaries in the first route. Boehmite and bayerite have been used to prepare pure β′′-Al2O3 (see ref. 31). Besides its composition, the ionic conductivity of polycrystalline β′′-Al2O3 depends on the ratio of β′′/β and the microstructure (grain size, porosity, impurities, etc.).

It is also important to take into account the reactivity of the conventional β/β′′-Al2O3 with water. The conventional solid-state reaction process leaves remnant NaAlO2 along the β/β′′-Al2O3 grain boundaries, making the product susceptible to H2O and CO2 attack from the atmosphere. A vapour-assisted method has been proposed by Virkar et al.52 to obtain Na-β′′-Al2O3 with superior chemical stability and mechanical strength compared to Na-β′′-Al2O3 synthesized by the conventional solid-state reaction route. Fig. 13 illustrates a schematic description of the vapour-assisted conversion process in the case of Na-β′′-Al2O3 formation. In this process, the starting materials of α-Al2O3 and YSZ (yttria-stabilized zirconia) are sintered at 1500–1600 °C and then buried in a loose powder of sodium source (e.g. Na2CO3 or NaAlO2). The alkali compounds decompose and allow a vapour-assisted reaction of alkali oxides with α-Al2O3.

The effect of Li+ and Mg2+ stabilizers on the β′′-Al2O3 phase content has been investigated recently by Zhu and Xue.55 Stabilization of the β′′-Al2O3 phase is basically the substitution of Al3+ ions by cations of stabilizers to compensate extra Na+ ions. The compensating Li+ ions generally substituted the octahedral Al3+ ions, and Mg2+ ions substituted tetrahedral Al3+ ions in the spinel blocks. They studied the relationship between the β′′-Al2O3 phase content and the amount ratio of octahedra and tetrahedra (AAl(VI)/AAl(IV)) in Li+ and Mg2+ stabilized systems, respectively. The β′′-Al2O3 phase content increased by the addition of the stabilizers while the amount ratio of octahedra and tetrahedra (AAl(VI)/AAl(IV)) of β-Al2O3 decreased with the increasing β′′-Al2O3 phase content.

Fracture of the β′′-alumina ceramic is a vital concern because it leads to cell failure, whereas poor control of the ceramic microstructure results in interfacial reactions with the reactants. Although large-scale production of this ceramic has been achieved, production yields and cost are still major concerns.

2.2 NASICON

One of the optimum candidate structures for ion migration was Na Superionic Conductor (NASICON) and was developed by Goodenough et al. in 1976, in order to achieve a high ionic conduction.56 The original NASICONs were solid solutions derived from NaZr2P3O12 by partial replacement of P by Si with Na excess to balance the negatively charged framework to yield the general formula Na1+xZr2SixP3−xO12 (0 ≤ x ≤ 3).57 The NASICON compound is produced by Ceramatec, Inc of Salt Lake City, Utah and has been proposed for using as a solid electrolyte in solid-state electrochemical devices such as Na–S batteries,58 gas sensor devices,59–61 and ion sensors.62

NASICON as the β-aluminas is a superionic solid with a covalent framework. They have high Debye temperature and, therefore, high lattice thermal conductivity. Na3Zr2Si2PO12 shows a weak-second order-like transition that involves structural changes of smaller magnitude. These superionic solids with covalent framework are also of great technological importance because they have high thermal and chemical stabilities. Moreover, it is important that the system does not undergo considerable structural changes near the operating temperature.63

The 3-D network of NASICON compounds is a phosphosilicate characterized by structural building units (SBUs); thus, Na3Zr2Si2PO12 has corner sharing tetrahedra of phosphate and silicate, (Si,P)O4, with zirconia, ZrO6, centred octahedral sites, where for every two octahedral sites there are three tetrahedral sites comprising two silicate and one phosphate centre. This way the SBUs are connected by (Si,P)O4 tetrahedra to form infinite ribbons parallel to the c-axis. The Na+ interstitial sites, located in the inter-SBU positions, are connected by a wide channel where Na+ ions can diffuse through bottlenecks that exceed 4.8 Å.64 The conduction pathways of sodium ions (Na(1) and Na(2) sites) are of a zigzag shape in three dimensions (see Fig. 16). NASICON-type compounds exhibit monoclinic to rhombohedral reversible structural transition, at the transition temperature depending on the composition (x), in the interval 1.8 < x < 2.2 a small distortion of monoclinic symmetry takes place.


Fig. 16 NASICON structure in rhombohedral symmetry, showing ZrO6 and (Si,P)O4 polyhedra. Dark-shaded circles represent Na(1) positions, light-shaded circles represent Na(2) positions, and curved arrow represents the conduction path of Na+ ions. (Derived from ref. 60, 64 and 71.)

This NASICON framework allows a wide range of chemical substitutions65 and its flexibility is responsible for its low thermal expansion, and makes it one of the most attractive families of materials for solid electrolytes.66 Most of the studies on this family of materials have been concentrated in the composition range 1.8 < x < 2.4, presenting the highest ionic conductivity for x = 2, higher than that of β-alumina which has two-dimensional mobility. In addition to its high ionic conductivity, it has claimed stability in humid air and aqueous solutions. However, NASICON-type ceramics have a low stability when in contact with molten sodium or molten sodium salts (see ref. 64).

In the synthesis of NASICON, ZrO2 impurities appear to be (see Fig. 17) decreasing the ionic conductivity. To avoid remaining ZrO2 in the final NASICON phase not only the synthesis method but also controlling the sintering temperature is the key factor.67 Porkodi et al.68 have proposed a new synthesis method to yield phase-pure NASICON based on a molecular precursor. Pellets with a pure Na3Zr2Si2PO12 phase prepared by this route and annealed at 900 °C for 4 h exhibited ionic conductivities at room temperature of about 5.3 × 10−3 S cm−1 and 2.5 × 10−3 S cm−1, for the sample sintered at 1220 °C for a period of 40 h.

The relationship between the microstructure and electrical properties of the Na1+xZr2SixP3−xO12 materials has been studied by Fuentes et al.69 This work proved that the grain size of NASICON yttria-doped tetragonal polycrystalline zirconia (ZrO2)0.97(Y2O3)0.03 ceramics depends on sintering conditions, temperature and time. They showed the existence of an increase of the grain size when ceramics are sintered at higher temperatures or for longer periods of time. A maximum conductivity value of about 2.7 × 10−3 S cm−1 at room temperature was obtained with the yttria doped tetragonal phase.

Mixtures of Na4Zr2Si3O12 and TiO2 have been patented by Takeuchi et al. as possible solid electrolytes for sodium–sulphur batteries70 due to their conductivity value (4.9 × 10−4 S cm−1 at room temperature). However, other NASICON materials such as Na1.8Ti1.2Al0.8(PO4)3 samples exhibit an ionic conductivity close to 10−5 S cm−1 at 200 °C.71 NASICON may be fabricated in thin films of no more than a few microns in thickness to provide strong membranes and a mechanical barrier to sodium dendrites. NASICON ceramic membranes have been developed to improve the performance of sodium–sulphur batteries to operate at temperatures below 200 °C. Various dopants may be added to the NASICON to improve strength, conductivity, and/or sintering.72

Rapid capacity fade on cycling and high self-discharge are the two main reasons for shortening of sodium–sulphur battery life. However, NASICON-type membranes have minimal capacity fade and a relatively stable shelf life, as they selectively transport sodium ions but not the electrode solutions. Therefore, NASICON-type materials are promising candidates as electrolytes if the conductivity at room temperature is enhanced.

2.3 Glass and glass-ceramic electrolytes

Another choice for using a solid electrolyte is a Na+ ion-conducting glass. Different glass-ceramic electrolytes have been synthesized toward realizing all-solid-state Na–S batteries. Conventionally, glasses are made by heating raw materials above the melting point and quickly cooling to below the glass transition temperature (Tg). In a different route to the amorphous (glassy) state below the liquidus or melting point for a given phase, crystalline materials that are excessively mechanically deformed may react to create amorphous products that are chemically distinct from the starting materials (see ref. 18).

Alkali sulphide glasses, such as xLi2S–(1 − x)P2S5 glassy electrolytes, have attracted significant interest because of their high ionic conductivity (3.2 × 10−3 S cm−1) at room temperature and negligible electronic conductivity. Moreover, these glasses have a wide electrochemical window and also excellent cycling and rate performances.73–76 The crystalline material Li3.25Ge0.25P0.75S4 exhibits the highest ambient temperature conductivity of 2.2 × 10−3 S cm−1 and the lowest conduction activation energy of 20 kJ mol−1 in the series of thio-LISICON (see ref. 74), together with negligible electronic conductivity, high electrochemical stability, no reaction with lithium metal, and no phase transition up to 500 °C.77,78 Also the 3-D framework solid electrolyte Li10GeP2S12 or superionic conductor presents an extremely high ionic conductivity (about 1.2 × 10−2 S cm−1 at room temperature). Moreover, the electrochemical stability from the cyclic voltammogram of a Li/Li10GeP2S12/Au cell with a lithium reference electrode has been tested. No significant currents due to electrolyte decomposition were detected in the scanned voltage range of −0.5 to 5 V. Other advantages of this superionic conductor are its stability, safety and a wide potential window.79

As far as we are aware, there is no equivalent sodium phase such as Na10GeP2S12. However, xNa2S + (1 − x)P2S5 amorphous materials (see ref. 18) and Na2S–GeS2 glasses80 have been recently prepared by melt-quenching and mechanical milling, and by mechanical alloying, respectively. In a thorough study, Martin et al. characterized these materials with Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) to examine the extent of chemical reaction between Na2S and P2S5 (see Fig. 21).

2.4 Other sodium ion conductors

In solid electrolytes, the more stable structure and higher sodium ion-conductivity have been much expected. In order to fulfill these essential requirements Onishi et al. have predicted that the NaAlO(CN)2 perovskite can be utilized as a sodium ion-conductor. This phase has two sodium ion-conduction paths along x- and z-axes. However, the activation energy for sodium ion-conduction along the x-axis (5.55 eV) is too high, while the activation energy along the z-axis (0.06 eV) is very small for sodium ion-conduction at room temperature.81

2.5 Polymer electrolytes

Polyethers like poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) have been mostly used with lithium metal anodes and commercial batteries now use LiFePO4 cathodes. Polymer electrolytes are less conductive than superionic inorganic materials, and need to work at 60–80 °C to reach 10−4 S cm−1 but they are easily made into large surface films, leading to batteries with a total thickness of 100–200 μm. While sodium metal is extremely reactive with classical liquid electrolytes, PEO is much more resistant. All solid-state Na°/PEO-NaClO4/V2O5 batteries have been studied by West et al.82,83 Several tens of cycles could be obtained with limited capacity fading. Keeping a battery at this operating temperature consumes 10% of its own energy over one week, to be compared with 14% per day for high temperature systems.

Ahn et al.84 have reported the electrochemical properties of a Na–S battery using a PEO polymer electrolyte that is operated at 90 °C. The first discharge capacity is about 505 mA h g−1 sulphur, which is similar to that expected for the formation of Na2Sn (n ≤ 3), especially Na2S3. The upper plateau region may be related to the reduction of sulphur to Na2S4 or Na2S5, and the lower plateau to the reduction of sulphur to Na2Sn (n ≤ 3) (see Fig. 25).

3 Conclusions

Sodium batteries are considered promising candidates as a substitute for lithium batteries due to the lower price and abundance of sodium, and to its similar intercalation chemistry to lithium. As the U.S. Department of Energy (DEO) Pacific Northwest National Laboratory compiles on the website,86 the sodium batteries based on Na–S and Na–NiCl2 (ZEBRA) are two of the most promising candidates for stationary energy storage and battery electric vehicle (BEV) applications, respectively. Although these batteries need high temperature (250–300 °C), the tendency, nowadays, is shifting towards designs that work at intermediate temperatures (between 100 °C and 170 °C) and ambient-temperature in order to avoid significant thermal management problems and thermal sealing issues.87 A new work about an advanced intermediate-temperature (∼150 °C) Na–S battery has been recently reported by the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL).88 Lu et al. tested Na–S cells with the mixture of S and Na2S4 as the cathode and tegraglyme plus 1 M NaI as the catholyte at 150 °C. This battery showed a dramatic improvement in the rate capacity and cycling stability over room-temperature Na–S batteries.

Furthermore, an optimization of the ceramic electrolyte/electrode interfaces is needed. ZEBRA battery performance projections will focus on achieving specific energy and peak power values of more than 200 W h kg−1 and 400 W kg−1 and enhancement of the power rate to improve the power/energy ratio. All-solid-state Na–S batteries, which operate at >100 °C, will be developed in near future using glass-ceramic electrolytes. Moreover, NASICON-type electrolytes such as Na3Zr2Si2PO12 or Na1+xZr2SixP3−xO12 (1.6 < x < 2.4), that are substantially impermeable to water, are proposed to avoid the drawbacks of using the hygroscopic, fragile and requiring high temperature β′′-alumina solid electrolytes (BASE). The Na3PS4 glass-ceramic electrolyte can also be used to develop all-solid-state rechargeable sodium cells that are safe and possibly inexpensive. Polymer electrolytes offer another route that is inexpensive with high productivity technology. All these next-generation batteries with new electrolytes operating at room temperature may contribute to promote sustainable energy.
