Light harvesting photovoltaics

Photophysical studies of biomimetic systems have provided key insights into the mechanisms of energy and electron transfer in natural photosynthesis and have driven the development of supramolecular systems for the production of artificial photosynthesis. Organisation of dye assemblies by non-covalent interactions governs the fast migration of excitation energy by which energy transfer can occur.4â€“9

The natural chlorophyll has tuneable electronic and redox properties that allow it to absorb light across a broad range of wavelengths with the ability to act as both an electron donor and acceptor. Semisynthetic chlorophyll-based systems have been shown to undergo efficient electron transfer once photoinduced.10,11 Wasielewski and co-workers have previously reported use of zinc methyl 3-ethylpyrochlorophyllide (Ch1) functionalised at the 20-position to generate a series of donorâ€“acceptor dyads with naphthalene-1,8:4,5-bis(dicarboximide) (â€˜napthalenediimideâ€™, NDI) or perylene-3,4:9,10-bis(dicarboximide) (â€˜perylenediimideâ€™, PDI) as the electron acceptors.12 These systems showed ultrafast electron transfer upon photoexcitation in both polar (Ï„CS = 13â€“19 ps) and non-polar solvents (Ï„CR = 12â€“25 ps). However the charge recombination was too rapid in polar (Ï„CR = 25â€“43 ps) and non-polar (Ï„CR = 1.2â€“3.3 ns) solvents for applications in artificial photosynthesis, where a slower diffusional time scale is required.

To slow down the charge recombination, work has been undertaken towards the formation of self-assembling supramolecular structures that better replicate bacteriochlorophyll.13â€“15 Ch1 exhibits metalâ€“ligand coordination, hydrogen bonding, Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions and hydrophobic effects within the protein environment that enables it to form supramolecular assemblies.16 So, appending a pyridyl functionalised pyromellitimide-naphthalenediimide (PI-NDI) dyad at the 20-position of Ch1 allows the creation of discrete supramolecular Ch1 systems. The incorporation of a coordinating pyridine ligand into the triad facilitates supramolecular assembly through metalâ€“ligand coordination. This has led to the formation of cyclic tetramers (molecular boxes) when dissolved in dry toluene. Wasielewski's groups has synthesised a range of Ch1-donorâ€“acceptor triads (Fig. 1) in order to determine how self-assembly governs the photoinduced charge separation of the systems.

Donorâ€“acceptor co-polymers

The highlights of Dâ€“A supramolecular polymers in 2012 include self-healable materials, organic photovoltaics and OFETs that take advantage of the properties of donorâ€“acceptor chemistry, such as charge transport and reversible formation.

Recently â€˜smartâ€™ or self-healable materials have received considerable interest due to their obvious advantages over conventional materials, and supramolecular strategies have often been implemented in their production. While these approaches achieve high healing efficiencies, they often yield mechanically weak materials due to the weakness of the non-covalent interactions that form the polymer itself. Fox's group has reported a system that builds on their earlier work on self-healable polymers and that overcomes the weakness issue of their previous system.28 The group combined polynaphthalenediimide and pyrenyl capped polymers to form a supramolecular material that could be healed by mild heating with the addition of a reinforcing filler of cellulose nanocrystals (CNC) to form a high strength, healable, supramolecular polymer nanocomposite.29

The polymer blend shown in Fig. 10 was made in films from 6:1 CHCl3:hexafluoro-propan-2-ol followed by re-dissolution in DMF and mixing with different weight percentages (1.25â€“10%) of CNC dispersed in DMF. The addition of increasing amounts of CNC increased the tensile strength of the material in comparison with that of the non-CNC polymer; up to 40 fold for the 10 wt% CNC (8 MPa for 0% vs. 341 MPa for 10% CNC). The nanocomposites were all thermally responsive and able to regain their pristine tensile moduli. However the rate of healing decreased with increasing CNC: 1.25â€“2.5% within 1 minute, 5% within 5 minutes while 7.5% took 80 minutes (all healings at 65 Â°C). Samples with more than 7.5% CNC samples showed phase separation which impacted on the performance of the films.29

Polymer solar cells (PSCs) have been developed as an alternative to inorganic solar cells; they use bulk hetero junctions containing conjugated polymers as donors and fullerenes as acceptors. The most promising PSCs are those consisting of both p-type (donor) and n-type (acceptor) materials to maximise the internal donorâ€“acceptor interfacial area. Further developments in this area include use of benzotrithiophene (BTT) based copolymers, and all-carbon copolymers. A common theme in all these polymers is the use of long alkyl chains that are directly appended to the aromatic groups, which not only allows the solubilisation of the polymer, but also increases the order within the bulk polymer due to van der Waals forces-driven arrangement of the side chains. The Polydispersity Index (PDI) in nearly all of the PSCs reported is of 2 or above.

Chen and co-workers synthesised such supramolecular polymers using the hydrogen bonding interaction between polythiophene, modified with imidazole side chains (P3HTM, n = 6) and carboxylic acid-appended fullerene (PCBA) (Fig. 11). The supramolecular organisation of the complex P3HTM:PCBA was performed using a 1:0.8 (w/w%) mixture in chlorobenzene that leads to a single species as observed in the UV-vis absorption spectrum with MeOH (2% v/v) as a processing additive. A PSC was fabricated from this material using spin casting followed by annealing at 130 Â°C, into a photovoltaic array. The optimised device was shown to have PCE values of 3.2% with a VOC of 0.67 V.30

Mechanically interlocked molecules (MIMs)

Cyclobis(paraquat-p-phenylene) (CBPQT4+) and its variants shown in Fig. 17 have been shown to be versatile acceptors in the field of mechanically interlocked molecules(MIMs), as well as in that of pseudocatenanes and scavengers. The Stoddart group has been particularly active in this field.

Stoddart and co-workers reported the incorporation of CBPQT4+ into a switchable bistable [3]-catenane based on a polyethyleneglycol containing tetrathiafulvalene (TTF) and 1,5-dialkyoxynapthalenes (DN) moieties that can be used as a molecular motor as shown in Fig. 18.36 The switchability was achieved by utilising the redox activity of the TTF which has a high binding affinity for CBQFT4+ when neutral, but which decreases upon oxidation. While the original aim to synthesise a Borromean ring (three rings all interlocked with each other) was unsuccessful, three ring-in-ring complexes were synthesised as shown in Fig. 19.37 The large CBPQBT4+ allowed two hexaethyleneglycol (HEG) linked DN or TTF moieties into the cavity; it was then hoped to bind a dialkyl ammonium in the crown ether hoops above and below the DN/TTF CBPQBT4+ complex.
