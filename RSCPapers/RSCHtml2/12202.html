Abstract

A series of donorâ€“acceptor type of conjugated polymers were designed and synthesized via palladium catalyzed Stille copolymerization. These copolymers consisted of the electron-donating benzodithiophene or indacenodithiophene units and the electron-deficient cyclic imide substituted quinoxaline derivatives. It was noted that all copolymers exhibited typical dual-absorption characteristics in both solution and as thin films with an optical band gap in the range of 1.61â€“1.72 eV. In comparison with the copolymers based on the electron-deficient unit of 5,9-di(thien-2-yl)-2,3-diphenyl-7-octyl-6H-pyrrolo[3,4-g]quinoxaline-6,8(7H)-dione (TPQD) as the electron-accepting unit, the resultant copolymers based on the enlarged coplanar N-octyl-10,13-di(thien-2-yl)-2,7-dioctyldibenzo[a,c]phenazine-11,12-dicarboxylic imide (TBPDI) unit exhibited an obviously enhanced absorption coefficiency. All polymers showed relatively deep highest occupied molecular orbital energy levels ranging from âˆ’5.40 to âˆ’5.52 eV. It was also realized that the hole mobility of copolymers based on TBPDI is much higher than those based on the TPQD unit as measured by the space charge limited current method. Polymer solar cells with a device architecture of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer:PC71BM/Ca/Al demonstrated that TBPDI-based copolymers exhibited higher performances than TPQD-based copolymers. The best device performance with a power conversion efficiency of 4.60% was achieved by using the indacenodithiophene-alt-TBPDI copolymer as the photoactive layer. Further optimization of devices by introducing a cathode interfacial layer and solvent vapor annealing in chloroform led to an impressive power conversion efficiency of 5.58%. These observations indicated that the developed TBPDI unit that has enlarged coplanarity can be a promising building block for the construction of highly efficient conjugated polymers for solar cell applications.

Introduction

Over the past two decades, bulk-heterojunction polymer solar cells (BHJ-PSCs) have attracted considerable attention due to their specific advantages such as light-weight, flexibility and potential mass production on the basis of the low-cost roll-to-roll process.1 The BHJ photoactive layer typically consists of blends of a conjugated polymer as the electron-donor and a fullerene derivative as the electron-acceptor. With the long-term exploration and unremitting efforts of researchers, an encouraging power conversion efficiency (PCE) over 10% has recently been achieved by single-junction devices.2 It is well-established that the design and synthesis of novel donor materials has played a crucial role in the development of PSCs, where the ideal electron-donating conjugated polymers are supposed to have a low optical band-gap to attain excellent sunlight harvesting, a deep highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy level to maximize the open-circuit voltage, an appropriate lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy level to effectively split the excitons, as well as a high hole mobility to allow for efficient charge transport.1c,3 To address these requirements, electron-donating polymeric materials are typically designed by using an alternating donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) architecture in the backbone, wherein the hybridization of molecular orbitals offers facilely tunable band-gaps and energy levels.1g,4 In this respect, large quantities of Dâ€“A type of conjugated polymers have been developed and have exhibited excellent photovoltaic performances by carefully choosing an electron-rich unit, such as indacenodithiophene (IDT), dithienosilole (DTS), benzodithiophene (BDT), etc. and an electron-deficient unit such as benzothiadiazole (BT), benzotriazole (BTA), quinoxaline (Qx), etc.1d,5

As a widely used electron-deficient species for constructing Dâ€“A conjugated polymers, much attention has been attracted by the quinoxaline moiety owing to the strong electronegativity of the two nitrogen atoms.6 For example, Hou and coworkers designed and synthesized a [bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophen-2-yl)benzo(1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²)dithiophene]-alt-[bis(2-thienyl)-2,3-diphenylquinoxaline] copolymer that exhibited a PCE of 5.0%.7 Wang et al. reported a Dâ€“A type of low band-gap polymer based on thiophene as the electron-donating unit and quinoxaline as the electron-accepting unit, named poly[2,3-bis-(3-octyloxyphenyl)quinoxaline-5,8-diyl-alt-thiophene-2,5-diyl], which exhibited an impressive PCE of 6.0% with an open-circuit voltage (Voc) of 0.89 V, a short-circuit current (Jsc) of 10.5 mA cmâˆ’2, and a fill factor (FF) of 64%.8 Chen and coworkers reported a fluorinated quinoxaline-based conjugated polymer by using benzodithiophene derivatives as the electron-donor, and a comparatively high PCE of 8.0% has been attained by adding 1,8-diiodooctane as the solvent additive.9

Recently, the cyclic-imide fused benzodiathiazole unit has emerged as an electron-deficient unit for the construction of narrow band-gap conjugated polymers. The introduction of the electron-withdrawing cyclic imide can simultaneously decrease both the LUMO and HOMO energy levels of the conjugated polymers,10 and the N atom on the imide can offer a straightforward opportunity toward the alkylation of the imide unit that can enable good solubility of the resultant polymers. Very recently, Wang et al. reported an electron-deficient cyclic imide fused quinoxaline unit, 5,9-di(thiophen-2-yl)-6H-pyrrolo[3,4-g]quinoxaline-6,8(7H)-dione (PQD), which was copolymerized with the electron-donating BDT unit to give the Dâ€“A type of conjugated polymers with a relatively high PCE of 4.9%.11

With the motivation of developing new semiconducting polymers based on the cyclic imide fused quinoxaline unit for PSC applications, here we designed and synthesized a series of Dâ€“A copolymers. These copolymers consisted of the electron-rich units of benzodithiophene (BDT) and indacenodiathiophene (IDT) as the electron-donating species,12 and the electron-deficient units of the cyclic imide fused quinoxaline (TPQD) or, with its two phenyl rings covalently connected, dibenzo[f,h]quinoxaline (TBPDI) as the electron-accepting species. It was supposed that the enlarged coplanarity of TBPDI can be favorable for its molecular packing due to the inhibition of the rotation of the two pendent phenyl units. The PSC devices using the TBPDI-alt-IDT-based alternating copolymer (P4) exhibit a maximum PCE of 5.58% with an open circuit voltage (Voc) of 0.92 V, a short circuit current (Jsc) of 10.73 mA cmâˆ’2 and a fill factor (FF) of 56.48%.

Electrochemical properties

Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were performed to evaluate the frontier molecular orbitals of the resulting conjugated copolymers, with the corresponding electrochemical characteristics of copolymers shown in Fig. 3. Theoretical calculations

In order to further understand the molecular orbital geometry and electrical properties of the polymers, we carried out the density functional theory (DFT) calculations on model compounds of the donorâ€“acceptor repeating units of the resulting copolymers at the B3LYP/6-31G (d) level by using the Gaussian 09 package.21 The repeating units of copolymers P1, P2, P3 and P4 are denoted as M1, M2, M3 and M4, respectively. Note that the alkyl side chains in these repeating units are replaced by a methyl group to simplify the calculation.5h,i The calculated frontier molecular orbitals are illustrated in Fig. 4. The LUMOs are predominantly located at the electron-deficient TPQD and TBPDI units, while the HOMOs are mainly located at the electron-donating BDT and IDT units. The comparison of the calculated molecular orbitals of M1 and M3 reveals that the covalent connection of the ortho positions of two substituted pendent phenyl rings in quinoxaline units did not significantly disturb the HOMO levels, while the LUMO levels obviously decreased from âˆ’2.75 eV to âˆ’2.94 eV, indicating the improved electron affinity of the TBPDI based copolymer P3 compared to P1. The combinations of the nearly unchanged HOMO levels along with the decreased LUMO are supposed to result in reduced HOMO â†’ LUMO band gaps from 2.95 eV for M1 to 2.05 eV for M3. However, it is not consistent with the observations of UV-vis absorption and CV measurements. This may be due to the fact that the DFT calculation assumed ideal conditions, while the significantly enlarged coplanarity of TBPDI may potentially have steric hindrance with the adjacent thiophene substituents in the BDT units.

Morphology

Insight into the surface topography of the bulk heterojunction copolymer:PC71BM blend films was provided by tapping mode atomic force microscopy (AFM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM), where the blend films of copolymer:PC71BM with a weight ratio of 1:3 were spin-coated on the top of the prefabricated PEDOT:PSS layer under exactly the same conditions as those for the solar cell devices. From Fig. 7 one can observe that the blend films based on copolymers P1 (Fig. 7a) and P2 (Fig. 7b) comprising the electron-deficient TPQD moiety exhibited comparatively smooth topography with root mean square (RMS) values of 0.71 and 0.57 nm, respectively. In contrast, the blend films based on P3 and P4 consisting of TBPDI exhibited a slightly rougher surface topography. It can be clearly seen that the blend film based on P3 exhibited nanoscale fibrous textures across the entire film (Fig. 7c) while the blend film based on P4 exhibited spherical features with a size of 60 Â± 15 nm (Fig. 7d). After treating the blend film of P4:PC71BM by chloroform vapor annealing for 60 s, one can observe that the surface morphology can indeed be modified with the emergence of slightly enlarged granular characteristics (Fig. 7e).

Further morphology characterization of polymer:PC71BM blend films was carried out by transmission electron microscopy (TEM), which is illustrated in Fig. 7(fâ€“j). It was noted that blend films of P1:PC71BM (Fig. 7f), P2:PC71BM (Fig. 7g) and P3:PC71BM (Fig. 7h) did not show distinctive features. This unfavorable phase separation cannot provide effective pathways for charge carrier transport, which may lead to the accumulation of charge carriers and ultimately increased non-geminate recombination that is detrimental to fill factors.6a,23 In contrast, the blend films based on copolymers P4:PC71BM exhibited distinct interpenetrating features (Fig. 7i), which was more pronounced after solvent annealing with chloroform for 60 s (Fig. 7j). Note that this interpenetrating fibrous feature is favorable for charge transport in the photoactive layer, which can lead to an increased short circuit current and a decreased probability of non-geminating recombination.26 These observations are in excellent agreement with the significantly increased short circuit current along with the enhanced fill factors.

Conclusion

In summary, we have designed and synthesized a series of donorâ€“acceptor type of conjugated copolymers based on the electron-donating benzodithiophene or indacenodithiophene units and the electron-deficient cyclic imide substituted quinoxaline derivatives via Stille polymerization. It was noted that the copolymers comprising the enlarged electron-deficient TBPDI unit exhibited an obviously enhanced absorption coefficiency along with an increased hole mobility with respect to their TPQD-based polymer counterparts, leading to the obviously improved photovoltaic performances of the former. The champion power conversion efficiency of 4.60% was obtained by the solar cell device on the basis of the copolymer indacenodithiophene-alt-TBPDI (P4) as the photoactive layer. Further optimization of the solar cell device based on P4 by using a water/alcohol interlayer and solvent vapor annealing resulted in a much higher power conversion efficiency of 5.58%. These observations demonstrated the great potential of using the TBPDI unit as the building block for the construction of conjugated polymers for highly efficient photovoltaic devices.
