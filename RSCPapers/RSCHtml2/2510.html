Near-infrared phosphorescence: materials and applications

Abstract

Room-temperature phosphorescent materials that emit light in the visible (red, green, and blue; from 400 to 700 nm) have been a major focus of research and development during the past decades, due to their applications in organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), light-emitting electrochemical cells, photovoltaic cells, chemical sensors, and bio-imaging. In recent years, near-infrared (NIR) phosphorescence beyond the visible region (7002500 nm) has emerged as a new, promising, and challenging research field with potential applications toward NIR OLEDs, telecommunications, night vision-readable displays. Moreover, NIR phosphorescence holds promise for in vivo imaging, because cells and tissues exhibit little absorption and auto-fluorescence in this spectral region. This review describes the overall progress made in the past ten years on NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes including Cu(I), Cu(II), Cr(III), Re(I), Re(III), Ru(II), Os(II), Ir(III), Pt(II), Pd(II), Au(I), and Au(III) complexes, with a primary focus on material design complemented with a selection of optical, electronic, sensory, and biologic applications. A critical comparison of various NIR phosphorescent materials reported in the literature and a blueprint for future development in this field are also provided.

1 Introduction

Infrared (IR) light is electromagnetic radiation with longer wavelengths than those of visible light (400700 nm), extending from the nominal red edge of the visible spectrum at 700 nm to 1 mm. This range of wavelengths corresponding to a frequency range of approximately 430 THz to 300 GHz can be divided into three parts: near-infrared (NIR), mid-infrared, and far-infrared regions. The NIR region typically falls in the spectrum from 700 to 2500 nm (120 to 430 THz). Nearly 50% of the energy from the Sun reaching the Earth is in the form of NIR radiation, as shown in Fig. 1a. At present, one of the key limiting factors for organic solar cells or photovoltaic cells (PVCs) is the mismatch between the absorption spectrum of organic dyes and the solar emission spectrum, thus it is important to extend the absorption of organic dyes from the ultra violet (UV)/visible into the NIR region. Using NIR light for sensing and detection offers additional benefits for being invisible and having less interference. Moreover, biological tissues have minimal absorption and auto-fluorescence in the NIR region (Fig. 1b), allowing for deeper penetration of light in comparison to visible light. This imaging window is a clear advantage for the use of NIR materials as tags or stains for real-time in vivo biological imaging. NIR light is also commonly used in fiber optic telecommunication and night vision-readable displays. Therefore, considerable and growing attention has been spent during the past decade on the development of NIR light-emitting materials or dyes for a variety of applications in the chemical and life sciences.115 A search in Web of Knowledge, Thomson Reuters, for documents containing the terms NIR/IR and emission or its synonym in the title and published from 2000 to September, 2012 resulted in about 8400 hits (Fig. 2), in which 30% are about NIR light-emitting materials.

In general, visible1631 and NIR115 light-emitting materials consist of inorganic materials (metal oxides and semiconductor nanocrystals) and organic materials (metal complexes and pure organic compounds), which are fluorescent and widely studied due to their rich chemical structures, ease of chemical modification and high fluorescence quantum yield (F). In contrast to the short emission decay lifetime () associated with standard fluorescence, lanthanide complexes3236 typically exhibit long-lived emissions, in the order of microseconds to milliseconds, in the visible and NIR regions. Accordingly, appropriate delay time filters allow for the avoidance of interference from short-lived background fluorescence and scattering light with these complexes. However, the extremely sharp metal-centered (MC) emission of most lanthanide complexes is not phosphorescence in the conventional sense because it does not originate from a transition between two states of different multiplicity and is not sensitive to oxygen.32,37,38 Recently, room-temperature (RT) visible (red, green, and blue) phosphorescent d6, d8, and d10 transition-metal complexes,3759 such as Ru(II),6064 Pt(II),6569 Ir(III),70,71 Au(I), Au(III),7275 and Cu(I),76 have been a major focus of research, due to their wide and useful applications in phosphorescent organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs),37,4244,47,49,54,56,57,62,64,68,69,71 light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs),40,42,46,55,63 triplettriplet annihilation based upconversion (TTAU),51,59 PVCs,41,47,53,61 chemosensors,3839,52,57,58 therapy,45,74 and bio-imaging.48,58 Compared with traditional fluorescent materials, phosphorescent transition-metal complexes offer several important advantages. Phosphorescence originates in the transition from the triplet excited state (T1) to the singlet ground state (S0), and thus enables the harvest of both singlet and triplet excitons to achieve a maximum internal quantum efficiency of 100% in OLEDs.77,78 Phosphorescence provides a longer decay lifetime up to milliseconds. Phosphorescence exhibits larger Stokes' shifts compared to fluorescence due to the energy loss in intersystem crossing between S1 and T1, thus decreasing spectral interference between the excitation light and the phosphorescent emission. The possible excited states of phosphorescent transition-metal complexes52 include triplet metal-to-ligand charge transfer (3MLCT), intraligand charge transfer (3ILCT), ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (3LLCT), ligand-to-metal charge transfer (3LMCT), metalmetal-to-ligand charge transfer (3MMLCT), ligand-to-metalmetal charge transfer (3LMMCT), and metal-to-ligandligand charge transfer (3MLLCT), which depend on the metal centers, chemical structures and triplet state energy levels of the ligands, local environment, and intermolecular interaction, and thus their absorption (abs) and emission wavelength (em), , and phosphorescence quantum efficiency (P) can be fine-tuned through the modification of ligand structures and metal centres.

Up to now, there have been some published reviews summarizing the recent developments and applications of NIR fluorescence115 and visible phosphorescence.3776 Combining their advantages, NIR phosphorescence has emerged as a new, promising, and challenging research field. This critical review, which focuses mainly on material design, describes the overall progress made in the last ten years in NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes and their applications. Additionally, a blueprint for future development in this field is suggested. In this review, NIR emitters are defined as materials where their em is equal to or longer than 700 nm at RT, but sometimes some of their derivatives, whose em is shorter than 700 nm but longer than 665 nm are also involved for comparative purposes.

2. General principles of NIR phosphorescence

2.1 Nature of the excited state in phosphorescence

Photoluminescence (PL) that includes both fluorescence (F) and phosphorescence (P) is spontaneous emission following absorption of electromagnetic radiation of an appropriate frequency in the UV/visible region. Phosphorescence, a spin-forbidden transition, occurs during relaxation of an electron from the triplet excited state to the ground state (T1 S0), while fluorescence is a radiative transition between electronic states of the same multiplicity (S1 S0). Since phosphorescence involves the intersystem crossing between S1 and T1, (106103 s) and Stokes' shift of phosphorescence are typically longer and larger than that of fluorescence (: 109107 s), as shown in Fig. 3. Since the ground state of oxygen is a triplet state, it can quench phosphorescence by through-space energy transfer with the long-lived triplet of the luminophore. Accordingly, another important feature of phosphorescent materials is that they can serve as oxygen sensors.38,7983

It is rare to observe RT phosphorescence from pure organic compounds due to the predominance of accessible thermal decay pathways. Therefore, incorporation into typically organized media (in the host of cyclodextrin,84 polymer,85,86 or crystal8789) or the addition of heavy atoms such as halogens,8891 Tl(I),92 Pb(II),92 P(III),93 Sb(III),93 or Bi(III)9294 [McClure heavy-atom spin-orbit coupling (SOC) effect]95 are required for RT phosphorescence. Still, most of these materials exhibit weak phosphorescence in solution at RT. A more efficient way to achieve RT phosphorescence is adopting cyclometalated transition-metal complexes. These complexes exhibit higher stability and stronger RT phosphorescence, in both solution and solid state, than other organic dyes because of the following three reasons. (1) Cyclometalated transition-metal complexes tend to form a strong bonding interaction with transition-metal elements. (2) The stronger metalligand bonding increases the dd energy gap and affords less radiation-less quenching due to the suppressed population of the higher lying, repulsive dd excited state; this is particularly true for the third-row transition-metal elements. (3) The occurrence of close-lying, ligand-centered 3* electronic transitions allows for facile tuning of the emission wavelength.49,96

For transition-metal complexes, the construction of Jablonski diagrams via electronic configurations from the molecular orbitals (MOs) description follows the same general lines described for organic molecules.97 Since most NIR transition-metal complexes are Pt(II) complexes, a schematic MO diagram for Pt(II) complexes is shown in Fig. 4 as an example. Unlike other well-known octahedral d6 Ru(II), Ir(III), Re(I), or Os(II) complexes, d8 Pt(II) complexes adopt a square-planar geometry and possess a vacant coordination site at the Pt(II) center, which allows axial substrate-binding interactions and might lead to effective emission quenching (Fig. 5). Therefore, Pt(II) complexes that have diverse excited states including not only (*) 3ILCT and (d*) 3MLCT common to normal transition-metal complexes, but also 3MMLCT can emit strong phosphorescence from the visible to the NIR region resulting from avoiding the quenching of free axial coordinating site and strong PtPt interactions.

2.1.1 MC dd excited state. Typical metal complexes with a partially filled d shell at the metal center are characterized by low-energy MC excitation states which arise from electron hopping between the nonbonding (d) and antibonding (d*) orbitals. These dd transitions are Laporte-forbidden, showing exceedingly low transition probability for the occurrence of both MC-based absorption and emission signals. The emissions derived from the dd excited states of Pt(II) complexes are broad, structure-less and relatively matrix-independent but temperature dependent. The quantum yield is generally low, but can be increased by removing high-frequency vibrations from the coordination sphere; a typical example is [Pt(bpy)(Cl)2]98101 (bpy = 2,2-bipyridine) (Fig. 6). The emitting state of [Pt(bpy)(Cl)2] was assigned to a 3(dd) transition as early as in 1989, but a more probable 3MLCT assignment cannot be excluded.98 The 3(dd) emission from this complex in diluted solution is vanishingly weak but at 77 K, a broad unstructured emission with em at ca. 620 nm is observed in diluted glassy solution. Another milestone example that exhibits green emission (em = 514 nm and = 9 s) in aqueous solution is [Pt2(-P2O5H2)4]4.102 This pyrophosphito-bridged diplatinum(II) complex with strong PtPt interaction exhibits unique photophysical properties from a 3[nd* (n + 1)p] excited state (Fig. 7).

2.1.2 ILCT. Intraligand (IL) or ligand-centered (LC) excited states originate from electronic transitions between orbitals that are mainly localized on the ligand chromophore. As such, if the metal perturbation upon coordination is minimized, the luminescent complex may exhibit a transition that has relatively solvent-independent spectral properties closely resembling the free ligand states. The 3LC emission energy is much less affected by heavy metal ions. A typical example is [Pt(TPP)] (TPP = meso-tetraphenylporphyrin) (Fig. 6). The 3LC emission from this complex in CH2Cl2 solution at RT occurs at em = 660 nm, similar with the free ligand of TPP (em = 650 nm) (Fig. 8). Another typical example is [Pt(bpy)(en)]2+ (en = ethanediamine).98 The 3LC emission from this complex in diluted glassy solution at 77 K occurs at em = 485 nm with overlapping vibronic progressions at ca. 1400 and 1000 cm1, which is in excellent agreement with the phosphorescence spectrum of [Zn(bpy)2]2+.

2.1.3 MLCT. The photochemistry of transition-metal complexes is mainly dominated by studies of the 3MLCT excited state of luminescent Ru(II) polypyridine complexes,103 such as [Ru(bpy)3]2+ (Fig. 5 and 6). 3MLCT states involve electronic transitions from a metal-based d orbital to a ligand-based delocalized * antibonding orbital. In a conventional sense, the corresponding excitation results in metal oxidation and concomitant ligand reduction. These transitions are commonly observed in the middle- and late-transition-metal complexes possessing relatively low oxidation potentials.44 As a result, the emission energy of 3MLCT, which depends on the reduction potential of the ligand and the oxidation potential of the metal ion, can be tuned by the auxiliary ligands and affected by the polarity of the solvent/medium. A typical example of 3MLCT Pt(II) complexes is [Pt(phbpy)(PPh3)]+ (phbpy = 6-phenyl-2,2-bipyridine and PPh3 = triphenylphosphine)104 (Fig. 6), which exhibits an intense greenish yellow emission in degassed MeCN solution at 298 K (em = 535 nm and = 0.9 s). Other typical examples include [Pt(3,3(MeO2C)2bpy)Cl2]100 [3,3(MeO2C)2bpy = 3,3-dicarbethoxy-2,2-bipyridine] and [Pt(5,5Me2bpy)(CCPh)2]105 (5,5Me2bpy = 5,5-dimethyl-2,2-bipyridine and CCPh = phenylacetylene) (Fig. 6). The lowest-lying excited state of [Pt(3,3(MeO2C)2bpy)(Cl)2] is now the 3MLCT state, but the dd state lies only a little higher in energy, offering the 3MLCT a non-radiative decay pathway and also rendering this complex non-emissive at RT like [Pt(bpy)(Cl)2]. On the other hand, [Pt(5,5Me2bpy)(CCPh)2] is much more emissive at RT than [Pt(bpy)(Cl)2] both in fluid solution (max = 545 nm, =1.6 s, and P = 0.64 in CH2Cl2) and in the solid state, due to the stronger ligand-field associated with the acetylide compared to the chloride co-ligands. Notably, many highly emissive transition-metal complexes, especially for high-performance phosphorescent OLEDs applications, are Ir(III) or Pt(II) complex, such as [Ir(ppy)3]106 (ppy = 2-phenylpyridyl), [Ir(ppy)2(acac)] (acac = acetyl acetone),107 and [Pt(ppy)(acac)],108 with emission characteristic of phosphorescence from 3MLCT and/or 3ILCT.
2.1.4 LMCT and LLCT. Transition-metal complexes with metal atoms in high oxidation states can show luminescence from 3LMCT.44 These complexes typically constitute early transition-metal complexes, such as Ta(V) complexes,109,110 and exhibit RT phosphorescence at the lower energy region of the visible spectra with lower quantum efficiencies. Other candidates that exhibit 3LMCT emission are polynuclear d10 complexes involving Au(I) and Cu(I) complexes.393LMCT excited states are seldom the lowest in energy for Pt(II) complexes. In heteroleptic complexes where LLCT excited states have been proposed, it is likely that the LLCT excited states should also exhibit MLCT character. This is because Pt(5d) orbitals should strongly couple with the orbitals of L. Typical examples are [Pt(-diimine)(dithiolate)] complexes,111,112 such as [Pt(dbbpy)(tdt)] (dbbpy = 4,4-di-tert-butyl-2,2-bipyridine and tdt = toluene-3,4-dithiolate) complex (Fig. 6), where L is dithiolate and L is the aromatic -diimine ligand. Most of these complexes, however, have lower P than previously mentioned for 3MLCT and/or 3ILCT emitting materials.

Miskowski and co-workers proposed oligomeric 3MMLCT (Fig. 7) based on a series of discrete d8d8 binuclear Pt(II) complexes, namely, [(Pt(tpy))2(-L)]n+ (tpy = 2,2:6,2-terpyridine, L = bidentate ligand),113,114 which exhibit red-to-NIR emission (em = 630730 nm), depending on the PtPt distances (3.003.43 ), in the solid state at RT. For shorter PtPt contacts (3.00 ) in [(Pt(tpy))2(-can)]3+ (can = arginine) (Fig. 6), the lowest excited state is 3[d*(dz2(Pt)) *(diimine)] (3MMLCT) (em = 730 nm). How to identify these excited states? Many methods, such as excited-state lifetime measurements,115,116 time-resolved luminescence spectroscopy,100,115,117,118 and computational investigations,44,119121 have been used to understand the nature of the excited state in transition-metal complexes. The most important and useful transitions of phosphorescent transition-metal complexes are 3ILCT and 3MLCT. Yersin et al. utilized the amount of zero-field splitting (ZFS) to classify the degree of MLCT perturbation and SOC efficiency in the emitting triplet state for phosphorescent transition-metal complexes,56,122126 thus providing a useful scale not only for an assessment of the MLCT perturbation in the T1 state but also for phosphorescence (Fig. 9). SOC provides a softening of the spin-forbidden character of the transitions between T1 and S0 resulting in a desired reduction of . Therefore, phosphorescent transition-metal complexes with larger ZFS are expected to have shorter values. In addition to P and em, is another important parameter of phosphorescence in regards to applications. For example, phosphorescent transition-metal complexes with short values ( < 10 s, ZFS > 10 cm1, 3MLCT or 3MLCT/3ILCT) have less triplettriplet annihilation and result in less roll-off in the quantum efficiency in phosphorescent OLEDs.127 On the other hand, due to the lack of direct incorporation with metal-center electron density, transition-metal complexes with 3ILCT are subject to much longer . This is useful for avoiding the spectral interference from short-lived background fluorescence and scattering light and it has clear advantages in such applications as chemical sensors and bio-imaging. It should be noted that mixing of 3ILCT and 3MLCT states may play an important role for decent P, because this intersystem crossing can be enhanced for the systems involving notable MLCT participation.44

2.2 Tuning the lowest excited states

It is important to tune the lowest excited states, the type or energy (emission color or wavelength), of transition-metal complexes because they decide the photophysical properties. The most effective methods for tuning the lowest excited state involves modifications in the participating (cyclometalated and/or ancillary) ligands, such as changing the degree of -conjugation, altering substituents, and adding heterocycles.44,128

As mentioned previously, three Pt(II) bipyridine100 complexes (Fig. 10) with similar chemical structures exhibit different lowest excited states. The energy of 3ILCT [*(bpy)] in [Pt(bpy)(en)]2+ is lower than that of 3MLCT [d*(bpy)] and 3LF (dd) and thus the lowest excited state of [Pt(bpy)(en)]2+ is 3ILCT (Fig. 10). The large stabilization of 3MLCT and 3LF for [Pt(bpy)(Cl)2] can be attributed to the strongly -donating character of the chloride ligands, resulting in 3LF becoming the lowest excited state. Furthermore, the strongly electron-withdrawing substituents of CO2Me on the bipyridine ligand of [Pt(3,3-(MeO2C)2bpy)(Cl)2] have stabilized a bpy * level, yielding a 3MLCT [d*(bpy)] state as the lowest energy excited state. In addition to cyclometalated ligands, ancillary ligands [(CCR)] in [Pt(phbpy)(CCR)] heteroleptic complexes129 also exhibit a strong effect on the nature of the lowest excited state, as shown in Fig. 11.

2.3 Tuning emission into NIR region

The optical and electronic properties of organic molecules are determined by their energy gap, which is the energy separation between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO), also called the HOMOLUMO gap. The red shift in the absorption and emission spectra resulting from a narrowing of this energy gap is a key in the design of NIR emitters. Generally, for NIR fluorescent materials, several design factors, such as the -conjugation length, bond length alternation, and donoracceptor (DA) system,3,5,11 should be considered. These factors are also useful for the ligand design in NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes. Moreover, the great success in the material design for visible phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, especially for phosphorescent OLEDs, provides useful experiences for extending visible emission into NIR emission.
2.3.1 Effect of substituents in ligand. The effect of substituents on the emission characteristics is quite complex and generalizations should be made with caution. Both the nature and position of a substituent can alter these characteristics. As early as 1991, the first extended series of fac-tris-ppy Ir(III) complexes with different electron-withdrawing (e.g., F, CF3) and electron-donating (e.g., t-Bu, OMe) groups was reported by Watts and coworkers.130 Electron-withdrawing substituents tend to stabilize the HOMO by removing electron density from the metal and result in a blue shift of emission, whereas donating groups have an inverse effect. Moreover, the cyclometalated and ancillary ligands can be separately substituted with electron-withdrawing and -donating groups in heteroleptic complexes, which enables deliberate control of the excited state from blue to red emission,106108,128,129 as shown in Fig. 12. Tuning phosphorescence from blue-green-red emission into the NIR region merely by the substituent effect, however, is rare, presumably since the contribution of ligand substituents to the emission characteristics is much smaller than that of the intrinsic -conjugated system of the parent ligand. Some other effects, such as vibrational coupling of the excited and the ground state and energy gap law (see Section 2.4), must be considered as well.

2.3.2 Effect of -conjugation length in the ligand. For fluorescent materials, the extension of the -conjugated system can lead to a decrease in the energy gap to a certain extent.131133 For example, by adjusting the number of fused naphthalene units in the rylenediimide dyes,131 the energy gap can be tuned over a quite large range, e.g., from roughly 1.29 eV (960 nm) to 2.15 eV (577 nm) (Fig. 13). However, the energy gap of a -conjugated system can only be lowered to a certain limit by increasing the chain length. There is strong experimental support, in the case of polythiophene and phenylvinyl oligomers, that oligomers with more than 5 to 8 repeating units bear all of the essential electronic properties (e.g., absorption and emission of light) of infinite polymer chains.134137 Another challenge is how to synthesize and improve the solubility of these rigid extended -conjugated materials.

For phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, one of most effective ways to reduce the emission energy into NIR region involves expanding the length of -conjugation in the ligand. There are many successful instances43,106108,128,129 of red shifting in phosphorescent emission spectra by extending -conjugation length in the ligand. For example, compared with [Ir(ppy)2(acac)], other [Ir(L)2(acac)] complexes with the larger -conjugated system of either an isoquinoline or a naphthyl group, show a red shift in emission spectra (Fig. 14).43 The complex involved the rigid azaperylene ligand demonstrated a particularly marked red shift, emitting at 932 nm. This emission energy is very close to the phosphorescent emission from perylene (800 nm), suggesting that the excited state in this complexes is predominantly 3ILCT. Furthermore, if the ancillary ligand has a lower triplet energy than the cyclometalated moiety, this ligand becomes directly involved in the emission process. For example, the ancillary ligand of 1-ethynylpyrene in the [Pt(phbpy)(CCpyrenyl-1)] heteroleptic complex (Fig. 11) affords a low-energy and narrow-bandwidth emission at 664 nm with major vibronic progression, and P of 1230 cm1, 20.8 s and 0.01, respectively, in CH2Cl2 solution at 298 K. This deep-red emission is also assigned to 3ILCT in the ancillary ligand of 1-ethynylpyrene.129

Nonetheless, is it right that the more (the -conjugated length in the ligand), the better (the decrease of energy gap)? On the one hand, the energy gap of a -conjugated system for fluorescent materials or ligands can only be lowered to a certain value by increasing the number of -bonds involved, as discussed previously. On the other hand, for phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, as the length of -conjugation in the ligand increases, the extent of participation of metal d orbitals in the highest-occupied molecular orbitals decreases while the S1 T1 energy gap increases, resulting in the attenuated ISC effect. As a result, the fluorescence becomes observable while the intensity of the phosphorescence decreases due to the combined effects of a lower yield of triplet formation, a decreased T1 S0 radiative decay rate, and increased non-radiative decay as the excited state energy decreases.38 Thus, dual fluorescence/phosphorescence emission is obtained by reducing phosphorescence and increasing fluorescence. There are several examples138142 of dual fluorescent/phosphorescent Pt(II) and Os(II) complexes, because these complexes usually have lower efficiency of spin-orbit coupling than Ir(III) complexes.140,142 For example, whereas [Pt(thpy)(acac)] [thpy = 2-(2-thienyl)pyridine] solely displays intense phosphorescence (P = 0.36) from a triplet state of mixed 3*/MLCT character, the phosphorescence of [Pt(th2py)(acac)] [th2py = 5-(2-pyridyl)-2,2-bithiophene] is weak (F+P = 5.8 103), strongly red shifted, and accompanied by higher-energy fluorescence (Fig. 15).140 Therefore, extending -conjugated length in the ligand is a double-edged sword, in that it might have positive and negative effects for the purpose of NIR phosphorescence.

Complexes containing softer and more polarizable heteroatoms such as sulfur and oxygen incorporated in the ring system of the ligand are much easier to oxidize and have significantly lower transition energies. 2.3.4 Effect of the donoracceptor (DA) system in the ligand. Introduction of a strong intramolecular DA system in strictly organic small molecules and polymers is a common strategy of lowering the energy gap.3,5,11 If the HOMO levels of the donor and the LUMO levels of the acceptor moiety are close in energy, the resulting band structure will show a low energy gap as depicted in Fig. 17. Recently, a significant amount of work43,143150 has demonstrated that DA system is a new strategy for phosphorescent transition-metal complexes to achieve the purpose of lowering the energy gap. 3. Classification according to the ligand

As discussed above, phosphorescence originating from either 3MLCT or 3ILCT is related to the ligands, thus the aromatic N-donor and/or cyclometalated ligands play an important role in phosphorescence. For easy metalation, the cyclometalated ligand usually has an ancillary donor, such as an N, P atom or a carbene, that can form an initial dative bonding with the transition-metal element, holding the adjacent CH bond close to the electron rich metal center. Then, intramolecular cleavage of the CH bond takes place, forming both the metal-containing cyclic framework along with a metalhydride fragment; the latter then undergoes hydride abstraction leaving only the more stable complex, as depicted in Fig. 21.49

In the literature, there are several reviews about the mechanism of such cyclometalation reactions174177 and the synthesis of cyclometalated ligands;178 these reviews should be consulted for selecting suitable cyclometalated ligands and transition-metal reagents. According to its coordination number, the aromatic N-donor and/or cyclometalated ligands can be classified into the following categories.

3.1 Bidentate ligands

3.1.1 NN ligand. The first and classic type of bidentate ligand consists of the bipyridyl framework, such as bpy, 1,10-phenanthroline (phen), 4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline (4,7Ph2phen) (Fig. 22), denoted as NN chelate. These ligands are capable of possessing -donating nitrogen atoms and -accepting molecular orbitals to implement the required metalchelate bonding interaction. These NN ligands can be metalated with many transition-metal atoms, such as Ru(II), Cu(I), Ir(III), and Os(II), to form the corresponding ionic complexes (e.g., [Ru(bpy)3]2+). Such complexes are useful for the application in LECs,40,42,46,55,63 because the emissive layer requires an excess of mobile ions. Alternatively, due to the strong electrostatic attractions of the oppositely charged particles in the condensed phase, these ionic complexes are significantly less volatile and become immiscible in a non-polar matrix, making them poorly suited toward the application in vacuum-deposited OLEDs. Recently, highly emissive neutral Ru(II) and Os(II) complexes for the application in OLEDs were achieved by using the cyclometalated ligand of 2-pyridyl-C-linked azoles, such as pyrazole (pypz), triazole (pytriz) and tetrazole (pytetraz) (Fig. 22).43,49,64 The high acidity of the azole NH bond makes C-linked 2-pyridyl azoles more reactive and able to extend the scope of the NN cyclometalation reaction.

Metal d6 tris-NN complexes have some disadvantages, however, for instance, [Ru(bpy)3]2+ complexing with asymmetric chelating ligands can have either a facial (fac) or a meridional (mer) configuration. For these cationic Ru(II) diimine complexes, it has been difficult to obtain a specific isomer. Moreover, it is difficult to tune the photophysical properties for this class of ligands, either ppy or pypz, due to their similar electronic and coordinating characteristics.106 Therefore, the combination of the -accepting pyridyls with oxidizable metals leads to low-energy 3MLCT, as in the infamous red-emitting [Ru(bpy)3]2+ complex.

3.1.2 CN cyclometalated ligand. The second and well-known type of bidentate cyclometalated ligand, denoted as CN chelate, consists of 2-phenylpyridyl, such as ppy, 46dFppy, 1-phenylpyrazolyl (ppz), 1-(4,6-difluorophenyl)pyrazolyl (46dFppz), and thpy (Fig. 22).43,49,106,107 These ligands are famous for the metalation of highly emissive neutral Ir(III) complexes for the application in OLEDs. Since this metalation involves removal of a proton from the aromatic heterocycle, the cyclometalated ligands are anionic, in contrary to neutral bpy ligand. Therefore, combining with a -accepting heterocycle (e.g., pyridine), the strongly -donating CN ligands offer very strong MC covalent interactions as well as highly stabilized ligand-field strength toward the metal ion. Consequently, the energy of MC dd excited states in these complexes, which generally serves as a major nonradiative deactivating channel, is raised substantially relative to those of neutral diimine ligands such as bpy; the latter tends to yield relatively less luminous metal complexes at RT. As a result, CN cyclometalation offers a good synthetic protocol, yielding efficient luminescent metal complexes. Moreover, the * energy gap of the CN ligand plays an important role in fine-tuning the emission wavelength of the complexes.43,49
3.1.3 CC and CP cyclometalated ligand. Recently, N-heterocyclic carbenes (NHC)179 and benzyldiphenylphosphines (bz-PPh2),180,181 denoted as CC and CP chelates, respectively, have been introduced as a new variation for cyclometalated chelates (Fig. 22). These -conjugated or non-conjugated NHC and CP ligands with large enough ligand-centered * energy gaps are good candidates for blue and near ultraviolet-blue phosphorescent transition-metal complexes,180186 which are out of the focus of this review.

3.2 Terdentate ligand

Developed from bpy, the terdentate ligands, such as tpy (denoted as NNN), phbpy (denoted as CNN), 1,3-di(2-pyridyl)benzene (dpyb) (denoted as NCN), and 2,6-diphenylpyridine (dppy) (denoted as CNC) (Fig. 23) are also well studied.16,43,187190 Complexes involving d6 metals and the extended -conjugated terdentate cyclometalated ligands typically have high stability due to the dissociation of the metal ion and the favourable formation of bis-terdentate complexes, resulting in red-shifted absorption and emission spectra. Bis-terdentate d6 complexes offer structural advantages over tris-bipyridyl analogues, being achiral and more amenable to linear extension. Nevertheless, bis-terdentate d6 complexes exhibit distorted octahedral geometry with the poorer bite angles and weaker ligand-field strengths and thus the P is sometimes lower than tris-bidentate d6 complexes. Unlike d6 complexes, d8 complexes, such as Pt(II) complexes, adopt a square-planar geometry, which is favourable for the formation of [Pt(terdentate)(L)]n+ complexes with high P.187190 Moreover, as discussed in Section 2.3.5, this kind of multinuclear Pt(II) complexes might exhibit strong intramolecular metalmetal interactions, which represent another effective strategy for the design of NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes.

3.3 Tetradentate ligand

Tetradentate ligands (Fig. 24), acting as chelating reagents, can react with many metal ions and result in stable metal complexes. 4.2 Cu(I) and Cu(II) complexes

4.2.1 Mononuclear Cu(I) and Cu(II) complexes. Copper complexes, in which the copper ion has two common oxidation states: Cu+ (d10) and Cu2+ (d9), have attracted significant attention considering their high relative abundance, low cost, and nontoxic properties compared to noble metal complexes. Clearly, the complete filling of d orbitals prevents MC dd electronic transitions in Cu(I) complexes. Such transitions are available to d9 Cu(II) complexes, however, and they are deactivated via ultrafast non-radiative processes. Accordingly, the most luminescent Cu complexes are not Cu(II) complexes but Cu(I) complexes.37,76 The coordination behavior of Cu(I) complexes is strictly related to its electronic configuration. The complete filling of d orbitals (d10 configuration) leads to a symmetric localization of the electronic charge, which favors a tetrahedral disposition of the ligands around the metal center in order to locate the coordinative sites far from one another and minimize electrostatic repulsions (Fig. 27). However, these four-coordinated Cu(I) complexes readily suffer a JahnTeller based distortion (flattening) in the excited state, and consequent formation of a five-coordinated exciplex, which promotes nonradiative decay (Fig. 27).222 Therefore, luminescent Cu(I) complexes, [Cu(NN)2]+223228 or [Cu(NN)(PP)]+228232 complexes, usually have sterically bulky substituents on the ligands to avoid this distortion. As a result, some [Cu(NN)2]+ (Cu1Cu11)223225,227 and [Cu(NN)(PP)]+ (Cu12)228 complexes emit weak NIR phosphorescence (3MLCT, P < 102) at RT (Fig. 28 and Table 2). Recently, changing the geometry of Cu(I) complexes from four-coordinated into three-coordinated configuration was demonstrated to be an alternative strategy to solve the problem of distortion.233235

4.3.1 Re(I) complexes. Among d6 Re(I) complexes, tricarbonylRe(I) diimine complexes268 of the type [Re(NN)(CO)3(L)]n+ (NN = diimine ligand, L = monodentate ligand and n = 0 or 1) are the best studied and are known to exhibit RT phosphorescence, which is highly sensitive to the nature of the environment and can be used as photoswitchable materials, chemical probes and photocleavage agents.269271 Most of their emission spectra are often found to occur near 600 nm and are structure-less and assignable to a 3MLCT state, because carbonyl ligands undergo significant back-bonding with the metal-centre. Consequently, the energy gap between the metal d orbitals and the * ligand orbitals involved in the 3MLCT transition is small enough for acting as low-energy emission. Their quantum yields are typically small (103) compared with complexes of the later transition metals. Nonetheless, some [Re(NN)(CO)3(CNR)]+ (NN = bpy or phen, R = t-Bu or n-alkyl) exhibit exceptionally high P (> 0.7) and long (>100 s) in fluid solutions at RT.272 This series of complexes is particularly interesting as their emission wavelengths are centred in the 450500 nm region with strong vibronic character, consistent with the diimine ligand emission. Since the emission envelope of the coordinated diimine ligand is red-shifted from that of the free ligand, a combination of 3ILCT (3* diimine) and 3MLCT state is often employed to account for the emission behavior. After that, much attention has been focused on improving their RT phosphorescence and thermal and photochemical stability for the applications in OLEDs, TTAU, and charge transfer.273278

4.4.2 Ru(II) complexes with terdentate or tetradentate ligands. As discussed in Section 3.2, the tridentate tpy-type ligands offer several synthetic and structural advantages compared to the bidentate bpy-type ligands, but [Ru(tpy)2]2+ (non-emissive, = 250 ps measured by transient absorption spectroscopy) exhibits less favourable photophysical properties (in particular, lack of luminescence and very short excited state lifetime at RT) than [Ru(bpy)3]2+.317 Many efforts have been devoted to the design and synthesis of terdentate polypyridine ligands to improve the photophysical properties of resultant Ru(II) complexes. The typical approach is to take advantage of the substituent effects for tuning the energy gap of 3MLCT and 3MC states, which involves the insertion of judiciously selected heterocyclic substituents onto the tpy chelate. Increasing the ligand bite angle also can be attempted to make the complex adopt a less distorted octahedral arrangement and hence increase the ligand field strength and reduce the nonradiative decay. As an example of substituent effects, the presence of electron-withdrawing or -donating substituents on tpy leads to a red shift in emission into the NIR region (Ru41Ru48)318 (Fig. 43 and Table 4); this results from a destabilization of the metal-based HOMO to a greater extent than the ligand-based LUMO. Moreover, electron-withdrawing substituents increase the luminescence quantum yield and the excited state lifetime, whereas electron-donating substituents have an opposite effect because the electron-donating substituents do not destabilise the 3MC state to a great extent and nonradiative decay back to the ground state is facilitated. Acetylene substituents also can be used to tune photophysical properties of [Ru(tpy)2]2+-type complexes (Ru49Ru53).317 The acetylene groups grafted directly onto the terpyridine core can enhance electron delocalisation and stabilise the ligand-based LUMO, resulting in an increase in the energy gap between 3MLCT and 3MC and a subsequent reduction in the mixing of the 3MLCT state with the 3MC state. Moreover, an increase in the number of acetylene units was found to decrease the emission energy and increase the excited state lifetime (Ru50: em = 690 nm, = 55 ns; Ru51: em = 710 nm, = 170 ns). An alternative approach to prolong the lifetime of [Ru(tpy)2]2+-type complexes was achieved by the introduction of electron-withdrawing pyrimidine groups (Ru54Ru66)319321 onto tpy ligands. Unlike phenyl groups, pyrimidine groups can act as coplanar substituents through intramolecular hydrogen bonding to enhance electron delocalization in the ligand and enlarge the energy gap between 3MLCT and 3MC, resulting in relatively long for Ru63320 ( = 1.81 s).

4.5 Os(II) complexes

Polypyridine d6 Os(II) complexes possess similar photophysical behavior with d6 Ru(II) complexes and they have been thoroughly investigated since the 1980s.331334 The relationship between the photophysics of Ru(II) diimine and Os(II) diimine complexes illustrates that the emissive excited state is Os to diimine 3MLCT in character. The luminescence of Os(II) complexes is generally of lower energy than that of the related Ru(II) complexes, in part due to the significantly more negative one-electron oxidation potential of the Os(II) complexes. Moreover, the excited state lifetimes of Os(II) diimines are generally shorter than those of the corresponding Ru(II) complexes, which can be explained by the SOC effect and energy gap law.335 For example, [Os(bpy)3]2+ (Os1) has a longer em and shorter of 740 nm and 49 ns, respectively, compared with [Ru(bpy)3]2+ (em = 606 nm, = 870 ns). However, it was demonstrated that the incorporation of strong - accepting ligands, such as phosphine, arsine,336339 or carbonyl,340,341 in combination with polypyridyl ligands can improve the and P up to tens of microseconds and 0.76, respectively, in the visible region at RT. In these complexes, the 3MLCT and/or 3* ILCT states are responsible for extended and improved P. High-performance OLEDs based on these complexes have also been successfully demonstrated.62,64,339

4.6.4 Polynuclear Ir(III) complexes. In addition to Ir(III) centers, polynuclear complexes containing either Ru(II), Os(II), Re(II) or Cu(I) polyimine units linked by different bridging ligands display a rich variety of photoinduced energy transfer processes.70 As shown in Fig. 56, a dinuclear complex [(tpy)Ru(py4pyz)Ir(Cl)3]2+ (Ir40)377 [py4pyz = 2,3,5,6-tetrakis(2-pyridyl)pyrazine] incorporating Ru(II) and Ir(III) centers is linked via the bridging ligand of py4pyz. The lowest energy absorption in the electronic spectrum of the bimetallic complex, is an iridium-based 3MLCT transition, while, irrespective of the excited moiety, the emissive state (em = 810 nm,) appears to be a ruthenium-based 3metal-to-bridging ligand charge-transfer (MBLCT) state. This is consistent with Ir Ru energy transfer because [Ir(tpy)(Cl)3] and [Ru(tpy)2]2+ both emit from 3MLCT excited states with a em at 632 nm ( = 1.31 s) and 670 nm ( = 30 ns), respectively. On the contrary, [(bpy)2Ru(HAT)Ir(ppy)2]3+ (Ir41)378 (HAT = 1,4,5,8,9,12-hexaazatriphenylene) linked via the bridging HAT ligand exhibits Ir-based emission (em = 760 nm, < 10 ns), consistent with Ru Ir energy transfer ([Ir(ppy)2(HAT)]+: em = 716 nm, < 10 ns; [Ru(bpy)2(HAT)]2+: em = 676 nm, = 470 ns). Since the electrochemical data indicate that the closely lying IrC -bonding and Ir d orbitals are located above the Ru-centered d level, the emission of Ir41 is attributed to a strong admixture of Ir HAT 3-bond to ligand charge transfer (SBLCT) and 3MLCT behavior. Further examples of tetranuclear heterometallic complexes (Ir42 and Ir43)379 reported by S. Serroni et al. demonstrate that the process of energy transfer in these polynuclear complexes is not only affected by the ligands but also by metal centers. For [M((23dpp)Ir(ppy)2)3]5+ [23dpp = 2,3-bis(2-pyridyl)-pyrazine] with M = Ru(II) or Os(II), the direction of the energy flow is reversed depending on the nature of the central metals. For [Ru((dpp)Ir(ppy)2)3]5+ (Ir42) (em = 812 nm, P = 1.5 104, = 2.2 ns), the central position is occupied by a [Ru(23dpp)3]2+ unit and the photon absorption of this chromophore results in efficient energy transfer to any one of the peripheral [Ir(dpp)(ppy)2]+ fragments, which emits. Conversely, for [Os((23dpp)Ir(ppy)2)3]5+ (Ir43) (em = 825 nm, P = 4.5 104, = 5.2 ns), the absorption by one of the Ir(III) chromophores is followed by a periphery-to-center excitation energy, and the emission is [Os(23dpp)3]2+-based. These results agree with the known ordering of the emissive triplet state energy levels: [Ru(23dpp)3]2+ > [Ir(dpp)(ppy)2]+ > [Os(23dpp)3]2+. Even though the study of photoinduced processes in Ir(III)-based arrays has attracted much attention, the resulting complexes usually exhibit low quantum yields (104),70,188 which limits their applications as NIR luminophores.

Although fewer d8 Pt(II) complexes are known to be emissive in fluid solution at RT compared to the d6 metal ions complexes already discussed, Pt(II) complexes with the 4-coordinate square-planar geometry and strong PtPt interactions have uniquely different photophysical properties from the other metal ions, such as Cr(III), Ru(II), Os(II), and Ir(III) with 6-coordinated octahedral geometry as well as Cu(I) with 4-coordinate tetrahedral geometry. The square-planar conformation is responsible for many of the key features that characterize the absorption, luminescence and other excited state properties of Pt(II) complexes. 4.7.3 Pt(II) complexes with tetradentate ligands. As discussed in Section 3.3, d8 Pt(II) complexes with the 4-coordinate square-planar geometry have inherent advantages in being chelated by tetradentate ligands and result in highly stable and emissive complexes. Unlike polynuclear Ru(II) or Ir(III) complexes, some polynuclear Pt(II) complexes (Pt125Pt142)154,155,160,161,432,454458 (Fig. 80) are highly emissive in the NIR region and have received great attention. 4.9.1 Au(I) complexes. The photophysics of luminescent mono-, di- and polynuclear d10 Au(I) complexes has been extensively studied.39,7274 The most common coordination geometry observed for Au(I) is two-coordinated (linear) or three-coordinated (trigonal planar) resulting in the uniqueness of the weak intermolecular attractive interaction present in between gold centers. 4.9.2 Au(III) complexes. Compared with their isoelectronic Pt(II) complexes, cyclometalated d8 Au(III) complexes that are highly emissive in fluid solutions are still sparse. The high electrophilicity of Au(III) ion renders the unoccupied d*(dx2y2) orbital low-lying and thus the low-energy LMCT excited states close to the emissive intraligand excited states, resulting in effective emission quenching. A representative example is [Au(bpy)(Cl)2]+ which emits in the solid state, but not in solution. To increase the luminescence efficiency of Au(III) complexes, strong-field ligands are desired to push the d* orbital to higher-lying energy.73,75 The incorporation of a strong -donating auxiliary ligand L, such as mesityl, acetylide or NHC ligands, is found to be one successful way to obtain high P up to 11.4% in solution at RT.477 High-performance yellow OLEDs (EQE = 11.5%) based on [Au(CNC)(CCAr)] complexes are also known.478 Many square-planar d8 complexes are unstable with respect to molecular distortions that lead to a symmetry decrease from D4h to D2d, and this is known to facilitate non-radiative excited state decay, as discussed in Section 4.7.2. A common strategy to avoid this problem is the use of terdentate477,478 or tetradentate chelating ligands rather than bidentate chelating ligands. Unfortunately, contrary to other metalloporphyrins, ordinary Au(III) porphyrin complexes are essentially non-luminescent in solution at RT.75 A recent study reveals that Au(III) N-confused tetraphenylporphyrin complex (Au11)479 (Fig. 84) is emissive in solution at RT. The electron donation from the confused pyrrole to the Au(III) atom through the Csp2Au(III) bond might contribute to the relatively electron rich Au(III) center, which results in a raising of the energy of the dd transitions high enough to inhibit emission quenching.

The photophysics of luminescent Au(I) complexes has been extensively studied, while, for Au(III) complexes, research until now has focused almost exclusively on CNC and CNN ligands, and there has been essentially no experimental work on other terdentate or tetradentate ligands. Therefore, for either visible or NIR phosphorescence, the photophysics of cyclometalated Au(III) complexes is virtually unexplored.

5. Prospects and conclusions

Recent advances in research on NIR phosphorescent materials and applications were described with emphasis on the emerging class of NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes and a number of technologically important applications. With respect to material design, various types of transition-metal complexes reported in the literature, such as Cu(I), Au(II), Cr(III), Re(I), Re(III), Ru(II), Os(II), Ir(III), Pt(II), Pd(II), Au(I), and Au(III) complexes, were systemically reviewed, discussed, and compared. Among these complexes, Pt(II) complexes with the 4-coordinate square-planar geometry and strong PtPt interactions have uniquely different and useful photophysical properties in comparison to the other metal ions for the purposes of NIR emission. In general, there are three efficient ways to achieve NIR phosphorescence. The first one is to extend the -conjugated system in the ligands. The representative and successful examples are Pt(II) -extended porphyrin complexes, which display many advantages including high thermal and chemical stability, good solubility, multiple options for chemical modification, and high luminescence for a wide variety of applications including NIR OLEDs, PVCs, sensors, TTAUs, and biomedical imaging. The second strategy is to protect Cu(I), Re(III), and Au(I) cluster complexes with ancillary chalcogenide or phosphine ligands. The last tactic involves using intermolecular and/or intramolecular metalmetal interactions to lower emission energy, as discussed in Section 4.7.

In respect of the development of NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, the following aspects should be considered. (1) Macromolecules containing NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, especially, dendrimers with the different properties of core and antenna, are expected, due to their many advantages including protection of the long-lived phosphorescent core by functional or biocompatible antenna, good solubility in solvent, convenient fabrication into thin films or nanoparticles, and wide applications. (2) Supermolecules building on self-assembly of transition-metal complexes through intermolecular and/or intramolecular metalmetal interactions, interactions, and/or hydrogen bands might be another hot spot for NIR phosphorescent materials. (3) New chelating ligands, especially porphyrin ligands, with extended -systems and stronger rigidity are in demand. Previous efforts mainly focused on fusing aromatic units at -positions of porphyrin, while a recent review480 revealed that porphyrins fused with other aromatic units at both the meso- and -positions through intramolecular oxidative coupling show very strong bathochromic shifts of absorption (abs reaching 1.52 m in some cases). This would be helpful for the design of NIR phosphorescent porphyrin complexes. (4) Polynuclear (n > 20) Cu(I), Re(III), and Au(I) cluster complexes protected by ancillary chalcogenide or phosphine ligands are needed because they might have some unique different photophysical properties from small molecules and nano materials. For instance, crown-like Au(I) cluster complexes containing up to 36 Au(I) atoms have been reported.481 In terms of size, these polynuclear Au(I) cluster complexes are comparable to small metallic Au clusters or nanoparticles, which also are found to show weak NIR emission. This might help in the design of luminescent Au nanoparticles with a precise number of metal atoms by using polynuclear Au(I) cluster complexes as the precursors. (5) It has been demonstrated that incorporating DA systems into ligands is an efficient tool for obtaining NIR emission for fluorescent materials,11 while, for NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes, there are rare successful examples. Therefore, more attention should be spent on the development of appropriate DA-based transition-metal complexes. (6) To date, the study on NIR phosphorescent transition-metal complexes mainly focus on em ranging 700 to 1000 nm. Phosphorescent transition-metal complexes with em longer than 1000 nm are scarce. The longer em with high P is eagerly expected. Of course, it is not always true that the longer em is better. Moreover, at any time, it should be kept in mind that the longer em would lead to the lower P and shorter , tentatively rationalized by the low energy gap of which the radiation-less deactivation may be governed by the large amplitude vibration.

When this review was prepared, a new study482 demonstrated another strategy for obtaining long-lived triplet excited state by excited states intramolecular proton transfer (ESIPT) in a series of naphthalimides conjugated with 2-(2-hydroxyphenyl) benzothiazole (Fig. 87).
