Abstract

New n-type conjugated polymer semiconductors bearing an electron-deficient naphthobisthiazole diimide (NBTDI) moiety have been synthesized and their electronic energy levels, solid-state morphology, field-effect charge transport and photovoltaic properties were investigated. Stille coupling polymerization of the new monomer 5,11-bis(4-bromophenyl)-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H, 8H)-tetrone with distannyl derivatives of the arylene moiety (1,4-phenylene/2,5-thienylene/vinylene) yielded poly(naphthobisthiazole diimide)s (PNBTDIs) with number-average molecular weights of 73â€“89 kDa and polydispersity indexes of 2.46â€“3.08. Thin films of the PNBTDIs have strong absorption bands in the visible region, resulting in an absorption edge optical band gap of 1.73â€“2.02 eV. X-ray diffraction analysis of neat films of PNBTDIs revealed lamellar crystalline materials with a rather short intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance of 0.34â€“0.35 nm. In thin film transistors, the PNBTDIs showed unipolar n-channel transport with electron mobility as high as 1.5 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. All-polymer solar cells incorporating the PNBTDIs as an electron acceptor and thiazolothiazole copolymer (PSEHTT) as a donor had power conversion efficiencies of 1.1â€“1.5%. The results demonstrate that poly(naphthobisthiazole diimide)s are promising n-type materials for field-effect transistors and all-polymer solar cells.

1. Introduction

Ï€-Conjugated polymer semiconductors are of growing interest for the diverse applications in electronics and optoelectronics, including organic field-effect transistors (OFETs)1 and organic photovoltaics (OPVs).2 In the past decade, the majority of synthetic efforts and structureâ€“property studies in the field have been devoted to hole-conducting (p-type) polymer semiconductors, which have enabled major advances in device performance and the understanding of charge transport.2â€“4 In contrast, electron-conducting (n-type) polymer semiconductors remain relatively scarce and only limited structureâ€“property studies have been reported.5â€“9 Hence, there remains a need to develop new n-type polymer semiconductors to enable studies of fundamental structureâ€“property relationships and to facilitate advances in n-channel OFETs1,7 and all-polymer solar cells.10,12

Currently, the majority of the n-type conjugated polymers are typically made up of electron-deficient aromatic diimides.7â€“12 Among the aromatic diimides investigated as the building block in n-type conjugated polymers for n-channel OFETs and all-polymer solar cells, naphthalene diimide (NDI)9,10 and perylene diimide (PDI)11,12 are the most common. NDI-based conjugated polymers in particular are attractive because of their facile synthesis, good solution processability, and high crystallinity, which have enabled improved charge transport and device performance relative to PDI-based conjugated polymers.11 NDIâ€“arylene copolymers are known to exhibit high electron mobilities in n-channel OFETs9 and more recently they have shown good electron transport properties in highly efficient all-polymer solar cells.10 On the other hand, electron-deficient benzobisthiazole (BZT, Chart 1) copolymers are well studied in organic electronics13a,e in the past decade and are known to exhibit a high degree of molecular order with robust thermal and oxidative stability as well as excellent mechanical properties.13b,c Although BZT copolymers have been successfully used as electron transport materials in organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs),13c,f their typical electron affinity (âˆ¼3.0 to 3.3 eV) and electron mobilities are too low for either high performance n-channel OFET or OPV applications. Recently, our group14 and others15 have explored naphthobisthiazole diimide (NBTDI), a hexacyclic ring in which NDI is laterally extended by thiazole moieties, as a new building block (Chart 1) for air-stable, solution-processable high performance n-type organic semiconductors. As expected, the electron affinity (â‰¥4 eV) and ionization potential (âˆ¼6 eV) of NBTDI-containing molecules are significantly enhanced compared to BZT-bearing materials; furthermore, the increased polycyclic ring size and more symmetric connection to adjacent moieties compared to NDI suggest that NBTDI has potential for developing n-type small molecules and polymer-based organic semiconductors for n-channel OFETs and OPV devices.

In this paper, we report the synthesis, field-effect charge transport and photovoltaic properties of three new n-type conjugated polymers with the donorâ€“acceptor structure comprising of electron-accepting naphthobisthiazole diimide (NBTDI) flanked by two 1,4-phenylene groups and various electron-donating moieties (1,4-phenylene, 2,5-thienylene and vinylene). The new polymers, poly(5,11-diphenyl-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone)-alt-4,4â€²benzene [PNBTDIP], poly(5,11-diphenyl-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone)-alt-2,2â€²thiophene [PNBTDIT], and poly(5,11-diphenyl-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone)vinylene [PNBTDIV], whose molecular structures are shown in Scheme 1, were synthesized from the novel monomer 3 (5,11-bis(4-bromophenyl)-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone). The new PNBTDIs were designed and synthesized with the aim of achieving (i) highly planar Ï€-conjugated hexacyclic ring, enhanced molecular rigidity and co-planarity, and enhanced intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking compared to the corresponding NDI or BZT rings; (ii) low lying LUMO energies (âˆ¼âˆ’4.0 eV) essential to realize efficient electron injection under ambient conditions; (iii) good thermal oxidative stability and ambient air durability; and (iv) tunable HOMO/LUMO energy levels, charge transport, and optical properties through the donorâ€“acceptor architecture.16 X-ray diffraction studies show that films of the PNBTDIs are lamellar crystalline with a very short intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance of 0.34â€“0.35 nm. We used OFET devices to investigate the charge transport properties of the PNBTDIs and found that they showed unipolar n-channel transport with electron mobilities of up to 1.5 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. When used as electron acceptors in all-polymer solar cells by blending each PNBTDI with the donor polymer PSEHTT, the bulk heterojunction solar cells had a power conversion efficiency of 1.1â€“1.5%.

3.1. Synthesis

Three new copolymers PNBTDIP, PNBTDIT and PNBTDIV were synthesized in good yields (67â€“94%) by the Stille coupling polymerization of 5,11-bis(4-bromophenyl)-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone (3) and 1,4-bis(tributylstannyl)benzene, 2,5-bis(trimethylstannyl)thiophene, and trans-1,2-bis(tributylstannyl)ethene, respectively, in the presence of Pd2(dba)3 and P(o-tol)3 in anhydrous chlorobenzene (Scheme 1). 3.2. Optical properties

Fig. 1 shows the normalized optical absorption spectra of PNBTDIs in dilute chloroform (âˆ¼10âˆ’6 M) and as thin films. Both solution and thin film spectra showed two characteristic bands: (i) an absorption in the wavelength range of 310â€“420 nm, which corresponds to nâ€“Ï€* or Ï€â€“Ï€* transitions; and (ii) a relatively broad and intense absorption in the range of 460â€“662 nm, which corresponds to the intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) between the strong electron accepting naphthobisthiazole diimide and the electron donating arylene subunits.16 In the solution spectra, the UV absorption maximum and the visible ICT peak are red shifted from 344 to 384 nm and 531 to 600 nm, respectively, the strength of the electron donating arylene moiety increases from benzene in PNBTDIP to thiophene in PNBTDIT (Fig. 1a). In solution, the visible region absorption maximum of PNBTDIP, PNBTDIT and PNBTDIV centered at 531, 585 and 600 nm, respectively. Similar to the solution spectra, the thin film absorption spectra of PNBTDIs were identical in shape but the vibronic structures were slightly enhanced. The thin film absorption spectra of PNBTDIP, PNBTDIT and PNBTDIV had peaks at 522, 622 and 599 nm, respectively. We note that whereas the absorption maximum (Î»max) of PNBTDIV in the solid state is unchanged from its solution value, the Î»max of the PNBTDIT thin film is red shifted compared to the solution, which is likely a result of the enhanced Ï€-stacking interactions. In the case of the PNBTDIP thin film, the Î»max appears to be blue shifted compared to the solution, however the appearance of a shoulder peak at 558 nm suggests that electronic delocalization is indeed enhanced in the solid state. The absorption edge optical band gap (Eoptg) of these n-type polymers varied from 2.02 eV in PNBTDIP to 1.73 eV in PNBTDIT (Table 1). The broad absorption bands in the UV-visible region and tunable optical band gaps (Eoptg) of the new PNBTDIs make them promising energy harvesting materials in all-polymer solar cells.

3.3. Redox properties and HOMO/LUMO energy levels

The electronic structures of the new PNBTDIs, highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO)/lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels, were estimated by cyclic voltammetry (Fig. 2). Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of two copolymers (PNBTDIP and PNBTDIT) showed irreversible oxidation waves with onset oxidation potentials (Eonsetox) of 1.45 and 1.20 V (vs. SCE), respectively, from which we estimated (EHOMO = eEonsetox + 4.64)18 HOMO energy levels of âˆ’6.09 and âˆ’5.84 eV. The observed 0.25 eV difference in the HOMO energy levels of these two copolymers is mainly due to the enhanced intramolecular charge transfer by increasing the strength of the electron donating moiety from benzene in PNBTDIP to thiophene in PNBTDIT. However, in the case of vinylene-linked PNBTDIV, the oxidation peak was not observed in the CV; therefore, the HOMO energy level was calculated from the optical band gap (EHOMO = Eoptg + ELUMO) to be âˆ’5.73 eV. The CVs of all three PNBTDIs showed irreversible reduction waves with onset reduction potentials of âˆ’0.70 to âˆ’0.78 V (Table 1). The corresponding LUMO energy levels of PNBTDIs were calculated to be âˆ’3.94 to âˆ’3.86 eV (ELUMO = eEonsetred + 4.64 eV). The relatively small difference (âˆ’0.08 eV) in the LUMO energy levels among the three polymers is due to the common electron-deficient naphthobisthiazole diimide moiety in the PNBTDIs. An electrochemical band gap (Eelg) of 2.17 eV in PNBTDIP and 1.90 eV in PNBTDIT is obtained from CV data. 4. Organic field-effect transistors (OFETs)

Top contact bottom gate organic field effect transistors were fabricated on heavily n-doped silicon with thermally grown oxide (200 nm) substrate to investigate the charge transport properties of PNBTDIs (Fig. 4 and S8, ESIâ€ ). Output and transfer characteristics of all PNBTDI OFETs showed clear current modulation and saturation with unipolar n-channel field-effect charge transport characteristics (Fig. 4a). PNBTDIs had moderate current on/off ratios of 103 to 105 and the threshold voltages range from 22 to 42 V. A factor of 10 higher electron mobility of vinylene-linked PNBTDIV compared to those of the other two copolymers could likely come from its higher molecular weight (Mn = 88.8 kDa) and shorter Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance (0.34 nm). Overall, the fairly high electron mobility of PNBTDIs is mainly attributed to high molecular weight, lamellar crystallinity with strong interdigitation of polymer side chains, and the short Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance of âˆ¼0.35 nm (Tables 1 and 2). However, we note that the electron mobility of the PNBTDIs is 1â€“2 orders of magnitude lower compared to previously reported poly(naphthalene diimide)s (PNDIs),9,10a,f which is surprising since it could not be explained on the basis of molecular weight, lamellar crystalline ordering and LUMO energy level of the PNBTDIs.

6. Conclusions

We have synthesized and characterized a novel monomer (5,11-bis(4-bromophenyl)-2,8-bis(2-decyltetradecyl)-benzothiazolo[4,5,6,7-lmn]thiazolo[5,4-f][3,8]phenanthroline-1,3,7,9(2H,8H)-tetrone), and polymerized it by Stille cross-coupling reaction to obtain a new class of n-type conjugated polymers, the poly(naphthobisthiazole diimide)s (PNBTDIs). These new n-type polymer semiconductors combined quite high molecular weights (Mn = 73â€“89 kDa, PDI = 2.46â€“3.08) with excellent solubility in organic solvents. X-ray diffraction analysis of neat films of the PNBTDIs revealed good lamellar crystalline ordering with a rather shorter intermolecular Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking distance of 0.35 nm. The PNBTDIs have strong absorption bands in the visible region with optical band gaps of 1.7â€“2.0 eV. The three polymers all showed unipolar charge transport in thin film transistors with electron mobility that varied from 1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 in PNBTDIP and PNBTDIT to 1.5 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 in PNBTDIV. As acceptors in all-polymer BHJ solar cells, the PNBTDIs gave a power conversion efficiency of up to 1.5%. These results show that naphthobisthiazole diimide is a promising building block for developing n-type polymer semiconductors in organic electronic applications.
