Abstract

To assess the feasibility of the organicâ€“inorganic perovskite iodides ABI3 (A = CH3NH3, NH2CHNH2; B = Sn, Pb; X = I) for thermoelectric applications, we estimated their figures of merit (ZTs) as well as that of Bi2Te3, which is optimized for temperatures around 300 K, as a function of chemical potential on the basis of density functional theory calculations. Our analysis employed the tetragonal structures (P4mm) of (CH3NH3)PbI3 and (CH3NH3)SnI3, the trigonal (P3m1) structure of (NH2CHNH2)PbI3, and the orthorhombic (Amm2) structure of (NH2CHNH2)SnI3 to examine their thermoelectric properties around room temperature. Our work reveals that the ZTs of electron-doped ABI3 perovskites can be as large as that of hole-doped Bi2Te3 whereas those of hole-doped ABI3 are rather smaller so that, in thermoelectric performance, electron-doped perovskites ABI3 can be as good as hole-doped Bi2Te3.

1. Introduction

In organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite iodides ABI3 (A = CH3NH3, NH2CHNH2; B = Sn, Pb), BI6 octahedra containing divalent cations B2+ form a three-dimensional (3D) perovskite lattice of BI3. The monovalent cations A+ such as methyl ammonium (MA), CH3NH3+, or formamidinium (FA), NH2CHNH2+, occupy the center of each B8 cube made up of eight corner-sharing BI6 octahedra (Fig. 1). These perovskites have been examined as light harvesting materials,1,2 solar-cell absorbers,3â€“6 and the tunability of their ferroelectric properties.7 They were also considered as new n-type thermoelectric materials8â€“10 and as solar thermoelectric generators, an alternative to thermoelectric devices.11â€“13 The efficiency of thermoelectric energy conversion is measured by the figure of merit, ZT = (S2Ïƒ/Îº)T, where S, Ïƒ, Îº, and T refer to the Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and temperature, respectively.14 Efficient thermoelectric materials need to possess a relatively large value of the power factor, S2Ïƒ, and a low thermal conductivity Îº = Îºe + ÎºL, where Îºe is the electron thermal conductivity arising from charge carriers, and ÎºL is the lattice thermal conductivity. In general, Îºe increases with increasing Ïƒ, so that ÎºL is expected to be more important in controlling the ZT. Electronic structure calculations for ABI3 perovskites show that, as expected, they all have a band gap.8,15â€“17 However, as-grown samples of (MA)SnI3 were reported to exhibit metallic properties due to a trace amount of spontaneous hole-doping in the crystallization process.15 A systematic investigation revealed that samples of ABI3 perovskites behave as p- or n-type semiconductors depending on the preparation method, and n-type samples have a low carrier concentration.9 The lattice thermal conductivity of (MA)PbI3 was found to be very low.18

Very recently, He and Galli10b evaluated the thermoelectric properties of (MA)BI3 (B = Pb, Sn) using the Kane single band model19 on the basis of DFT calculations. Their results show that the maximum ZT values of these compounds can be obtained from electron doping rather than from hole doping. In their evaluation of the thermal transport coefficients, they calculated the electrical mobility Î¼ by considering only the acoustic phonon scattering (electronâ€“phonon interaction). In the present work we assess the feasibility of the perovskite iodides the ABI3 for thermoelectric applications by evaluating the power factor (PF), S2Ïƒ, and the figure of merit, ZT = (S2Ïƒ/Îº)T, as a function of the chemical potential (equivalently, as a function of charge carrier density) on the basis of density functional theory (DFT) calculations. For this purpose, it is necessary to calculate the thermoelectric properties of these perovskites using their crystal structures around room temperature, and compare them with those of the well-known thermoelectric material Bi2Te3 that is optimized for temperatures around 300 K.20,21 The structures of the perovskites ABI3 depend on temperature, because the organic cations A+ rattle around within their B8 cages interacting with the BI3 framework.15,22â€“24 Thus, we examine the tetragonal structures (P4mm) of (MA)PbI3 and (MA)SnI3, the trigonal (P3m1) structure of (FA)PbI3, and the orthorhombic (Amm2) structure of (FA)SnI3 (Table 1).8 Experimentally, the precise atomic positions of these perovskite iodides are poorly determined (particularly, those of the organic cations) so that we first optimize the atomic positions of these crystal structures by DFT calculations and then use the optimized structures to calculate their thermoelectric properties. Our work suggests that, in their thermoelectric efficiency, the iodide perovskites ABI3 can be nearly as good as that of Bi2Te3 if enough electrons can be doped. Under hole doping, they are not expected to be as efficient as Bi2Te3.

2. Theoretical aspects and computational details

For a semiconductor, the band gap Eg is given by Eg = ECBM âˆ’ EVBM, where ECBM and EVBM refer to the energies of the conduction band minimum (CBM) and valence band maximum (VBM), respectively. The chemical potential Î¼ for holes (Î¼ < 0) is defined as Î¼ = E âˆ’ EVBM where E < EVBM, and that for electrons (Î¼ > 0) by Î¼ = E âˆ’ ECBM where E > ECBM. The charge carriers available for a hole-doped or an electron-doped semiconductor at a given chemical potential Î¼ can be approximated by integrating the electronic density of states (DOS) N(Î¼).

Our DFT electronic structure calculations employed the frozen-core projector augmented wave (PAW) method25 encoded in the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP)26 using the generalized-gradient approximation (GGA)27 of Perdew, Burke and Ernzerhof for the exchange-correlation functional with the plane-wave-cut-off energy of 550 eV, the Monkhorstâ€“Pack k-points mesh of 10 Ã— 10 Ã— 10, and the threshold of self-consistent-field energy convergence of 10âˆ’6 eV. In general, it is not easy to measure exact positions of small atoms such as hydrogen atoms by XRD measurements. For this reason, the hydrogen atom positions of organic molecules are not specified in most cases. Thus, we add H atoms to the FA and MA molecules and then optimize the atom positions of ABI3 while keeping constant the cell parameters ABI3 on the basis of density functional calculations. The atomic positions of (MA)PbI3, (MA)SnI3, (FA)PbI3, and (FA)SnI3 were fully optimized until all the residual forces are smaller than 0.005 eV Ã…âˆ’1 with their cell parameters fixed (Table 1). The atom positions obtained from these optimizations are summarized in Tables S1â€“S4 of the ESI.â€  Heavy elements such as Pb, Sn and I possess a strong spin orbit coupling (SOC) effect. We examine the SOC effect on our calculations by comparing the band gaps of the perovskite iodides obtained from GGA and GGA + SOC calculations. As listed in Table 2, the band gaps determined from the GGA calculations agree better with the experimental band gaps than do those from the GGA + SOC calculations, as has already been reported for (MA)PbI3.16 In estimating thermoelectric properties, it is important to describe a band gap correctly so that our discussion of the perovskite iodides will be based on GGA calculations.

3. Electronic structures

The band dispersion relations calculated for (MA)PbI3, (MA)SnI3, (FA)PbI3, and (FA)SnI3 are summarized in Fig. 2. (FA)PbI3 and (FA)SnI3 have a direct band gap at (0.5, 0, 0), while (MA)PbI3 and (MA)SnI3 have a direct band gap at R = (0.5, 0.5, 0.5). In cubic ABI3 perovskites, a direct band gap occurs at R = (0.5, 0.5, 0.5).17 The low-temperature distorted phases of ABI3 do not differ much from their high temperature cubic phases so that the electronic structures of the low-temperature distorted ABI3 are quite similar to those of their high-temperature cubic structures. Thus, the direct band gap opens at R for (MA)BI3 (B = Pb, Sn). The occurrence of a direct band gap at (0.5, 0, 0) for the low-temperature (FA)BI3 arises from the increase in the unit cell size caused by the structural distortion and the concomitant folding of the Brillouin zone. The calculated band gaps of (MA)PbI3 and (FA)PbI3 are greater than those of their Sn analogues. The calculated band gaps obtained from our GGA (GGA + SOC) calculations are 1.69 (1.12), 1.62 (0.91), 0.86 (0.78), and 0.74 (0.75) eV for (MA)PbI3, (FA)PbI3, (MA)SnI3, and (FA)SnI3, respectively. The experimentally observed band gaps are 1.55, 1.48, 1.35, and 1.41 for (MA)PbI3,35 (FA)PbI3,36 (MA)SnI3,8 and (FA)SnI3,37 respectively.

The total DOS and projected DOS plots calculated for (MA)PbI3, (MA)SnI3, (FA)PbI3, and (FA)SnI3 are presented in Fig. 3. The VBM is composed of the Pb 6s and I 5p states for (MA)PbI3 and (FA)PbI3, and the Sn 5s and I 5p states for (MA)SnI3 and (FA)SnI3. The CBM is made up of the Pb 6p states for (MA)PbI3 and (FA)PbI3, and the Sn 5p states for (MA)SnI3 and (FA)SnI3. At the VBM, the Pb 6sâ€“I 5p antibonding is weaker than the Sn 5sâ€“I 5p antibonding, most likely because the Pbâ€“O bonds are longer than the Snâ€“O bonds and because Pb 6s orbital is more diffuse than the Sn 5s orbital. The organic cations A+ of the perovskites ABI3 have their filled states well below the VBM, their empty states well above the CBM, and their contribution to the Fermi level is very weak. Thus, the transport properties of ABI3 are governed by the electrons or holes generated in the BI3 framework. The important role of the A+ cations is that, by rattling within the B8 cages, the A+ cations would disturb the acoustic phonon dispersions of the BI3 framework and hence lower the lattice thermal conductivity ÎºL, as found for the rattling cations of skutterudites in their cages.38

4. Thermoelectric properties

We now compare the calculated thermoelectric properties of ABI3 compounds with those of a p-type semiconductor Bi2Te3. The Seebeck coefficients S of ABI3 calculated as a function of the chemical potential Î¼ are shown in Fig. 4a, which exhibits two peaks at Î¼ â‰ˆ +0.1 and âˆ’0.1 eV. At 400 K the maximum Seebeck coefficients are âˆ¼1600 Î¼V Kâˆ’1 for (MA)PbI3 and (FA)PbI3, and âˆ¼800 Î¼V Kâˆ’1 for (MA)SnI3 and (FA)SnI3. The Seebeck coefficients are considerably larger for the Pb systems because they have narrower band dispersions around the Fermi level and a larger band gap than do the Sn systems. The Seebeck coefficients of the ABI3 perovskites are quite large compared with that of Bi2Te3 because the perovskites have a much larger band gap than does Bi2Te3 (Eg = 0.21 eV for Bi2Te3). In general, the electron momentum relaxation time Ï„ of conventional semiconductors is of the order of 10âˆ’14 s.20,21,39,40 For example, Ï„ â‰ˆ 2 Ã— 10âˆ’14 and 3 Ã— 10âˆ’14 s have been reported to reproduce the experimental resistivities of Bi2Te3 and PbTe, respectively.20,21,40 Thus, in calculating the electron conductivity Ïƒ and hence the power factor S2Ïƒ for all the ABI3 perovskites and Bi2Te3, we employed Ï„ = 2 Ã— 10âˆ’14 s for direct comparison of their transport properties. Indeed, the calculated electronic conductivities at low carrier concentrations show a good agreement with the experimentally observed values for (MA)SnI3 (ref. 18) and (MA)PbI3.10a The dependence of the power factor S2Ïƒ on the chemical potential Î¼ is presented in Fig. 4b, which exhibits maximum peaks around Î¼ â‰ˆ 0.8 eV with values slightly larger for the ASnI3 than for the APbI3 perovskites. The maximum S2Ïƒ values of ABI3 are âˆ¼0.007 W Kâˆ’2 mâˆ’1 at 400 K, which can be achieved by electron doping. On the other hand, the maximum S2Ïƒ value attainable by hole doping is an order of magnitude smaller than that attainable by electron doping as well as that of Bi2Te3. In terms of S2Ïƒ alone, the perovskites ABI3 would be expected to be a poor thermoelectric material compared to Bi2Te3 (Fig. 4b). However, what matters for thermoelectric properties is the figure of merit ZT = (S2Ïƒ/Îº)T, which can be enhanced by reducing the value of the thermal conductivity Îº = ÎºL + Îºe.

5. Concluding remarks

The thermoelectric properties of the perovskite iodides ABI3 were compared with that of Bi2Te3 on the basis of DFT calculations. The ZTs of the electron-doped ABI3 can be as large as that of hole-doped Bi2Te3 while those of hole-doped ABI3 are rather smaller. Thus, electron-doped perovskites ABI3 are expected to exhibit as good a thermoelectric performance as does hole-doped Bi2Te3. Experimentally, it is known that the carrier density is rather low in electron-doped ABI3 perovskites. It would be an interesting challenge to see if ABI3 perovskites can be doped with a large number of electrons.
