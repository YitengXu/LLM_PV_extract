Abstract

In this study, we have designed and synthesized a novel ZnO@CuO coreâ€“shell heterojunction photoanode modified with cocatalyst ZnFe-layered double hydroxides (ZnFe-LDH). As expected, the deposition of CuO enhances light harvesting and shortens the diffusion distance for charge transfer. The ZnO@CuO heterojunction also enhances charge separation and suppresses recombination. Furthermore, modification with cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH facilitates photogenerated hole transport and accelerates the surface oxidation reaction kinetics. On account of the synergistic effect of the coreâ€“shell heterojunction in ZnO@CuO with cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH, this photoanode exhibits an optimal photocurrent density of 2.08 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE, which is about 5.3 times that of the pristine ZnO photoanode. Therefore, the construction of ZnO@CuO coreâ€“shell nanorod arrays coupled with cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH provides an effective and novel route for designing low-cost and high-efficiency photoelectrodes.

1. Introduction

In the past few decades, excessive consumption of fossil fuels has exacerbated environmental problems and the energy crisis.1â€“3 Thus, it is urgent to explore clean and renewable alternative energy sources.4 It is well known that hydrogen energy is considered as one of the most promising clean energy sources due to its high combustion value, no carbon emission and sustainable nature.5,6 Among the various technologies for hydrogen production, photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting has received growing attention as a technology with the potential to realize large-scale solar hydrogen production.7 Since the pioneering work by Honda and co-workers in 1972, various semiconductor materials have been employed as photoelectrodes for PEC water splitting, including TiO2, CaBi6O10, BiVO4, g-C3N4, WO3 and ZnO.8â€“14 Among them, zinc oxide (ZnO) has attracted widespread attention owing to its facile synthesis, low cost and natural abundance.15 Despite these properties, the practical application of ZnO photoanodes is severely restrained in PEC water splitting because of their limited light response range, the serious recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs and the slow kinetics at the interface.16â€“18

It is well established that nanostructure design is an effective approach to enhance the PEC performance of photoelectrodes.19 So far, various nanostructures of ZnO have been developed as photoanodes for PEC water splitting. In particular, one-dimensional (1D) semiconductors including nanowires, nanotubes and nanorods (NRs) possess a lower defect density, larger specific surface area, and shorter diffusion length compared to the bulk and particle materials, which could boost the charge transfer, thus exhibiting excellent PEC water splitting performance.20,21 Hence the development of vertically well-arranged ZnO nanorod arrays with a large surface area is of great importance for effectively improving the PEC performance.

Furthermore, constructing heterojunctions by sensitizing ZnO with a narrow bandgap semiconductor is an effective approach to broaden the visible light absorption range and promote charge separation. Particularly, 1D coreâ€“shell heterostructures have aroused widespread attention in PEC water splitting owing to their numerous advantages.22 Among various semiconductors with a narrow bandgap, CuO is a suitable candidate to sensitize ZnO owing to its relatively narrow bandgap (ranging from 1.3 to 1.7 eV), easy synthesis procedures, non-toxicity and matching band edge positions with ZnO.23 In our previous work, we have synthesized a CuO modifier through a simple electrochemical deposition method and found that the size of the CuO modifier can be controlled by adjusting the electrochemical deposition time.24 Hence, it is believed that the deposition of the CuO modifier on the ZnO NR array could lead to a superior water splitting performance due to the synergistic effect of the ZnO@CuO heterojunction.

It is well known that decorating a water oxidation cocatalyst (such as cobaltâ€“phosphate (Coâ€“Pi), FeOOH, NiOOH, etc.) on photoanodes has been proved to be an effective approach to lower the water oxidation overpotential and facilitate the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pair separation at the cocatalyst/semiconductor interface. For instance, Liu et al. have prepared 1D SnO2/BiVO4 nanostructures decorated with a Coâ€“Pi cocatalyst and achieved an improved photocurrent density of 2.63 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE.25 Wang et al. have applied NiCoFeP as the water oxidation cocatalyst in GaN:ZnO photoanodes, demonstrating significantly accelerated carrier separation and injection at the photoanodeâ€“electrolyte interface.26 Recently, layered double hydroxides (LDHs) have attracted increasing interest since the presence of LDHs can facilitate the carrier transport to accelerate the water oxidation process.27,28 Zhang et al. have reported that the direct preparation of metal hydroxide electrocatalysts for the OER is more desirable since others (such as pure phase metal sulfides, nitrides, and phosphides) are time consuming and technically challenging.

Based on the above-mentioned issues, we have designed and synthesized a novel ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH ternary composite photoelectrode for PEC water splitting in this study. The CuO modifier is uniformly deposited on the surface of ZnO NR arrays by a simple electrochemical deposition method. The presence of the CuO modifier not only broadens the visible light absorption range of ZnO NRs, but also forms a heterojunction with ZnO to accelerate the carrier separation. Furthermore, ZnFe-LDH as the cocatalyst further accelerates the surface water oxidation reaction kinetics. Therefore, the obtained ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH ternary photoanode exhibits significantly enhanced PEC performance in water splitting. It is expected that this design of combining the coreâ€“shell heterojunction structure with an LDH cocatalyst could provide a new prospect for the preparation of photoelectrodes with high quality in PEC water splitting.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Preparation of ZnO NRs

ZnO NRs were prepared through the dip-coating method and aqueous bath deposition.15 Before the deposition of the ZnO seed layer, fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) substrates were ultrasonically cleaned with acetone, isopropanol and absolute ethanol for 15 min, respectively. Firstly, the ZnO seed layers were deposited on FTO substrates by a dip-coating method and the precursor solution was composed of 0.3 M zinc acetate dehydrate (Zn(CH3COO)2Â·2H2O), 0.3 M ethylene glycol monomethylether (CH3OCH2CH2OH) and a certain amount of ethanolamine (NH2CH2CH2OH). Afterwards, the FTO substrates coated with the precursor were annealed at 200 Â°C for 30 min and at 400 Â°C for 60 min to obtain ZnO seed layers. Finally, the seed layers were placed in an aqueous solution containing 0.05 M zinc nitrate hexahydrate (Zn(NO3)2Â·6H2O) and 0.05 M hexamethylenetetramine (C6H12N4) and heated in a water bath at 90 Â°C for 4 h. ZnO NRs were obtained by washing the samples with deionized water and then drying in air.

2.2. Deposition of a CuO thin layer

The CuO thin layer was deposited on ZnO NRs by the electrodeposition method in a three-electrode system.24 Typically, 0.48 M copper sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO4Â·5H2O) and 3 M lactic acid (C3H6O3) were dissolved in deionized water. Then, the NaOH aqueous solution was slowly added dropwise to the mixed solution to adjust the pH until 10. The Cu2O thin layer was electrodeposited at a potential of âˆ’0.5 V vs. Ag/AgCl over 40 s and the solution was maintained at 35 Â°C. After drying at room temperature, the as-prepared Cu2O was transformed into CuO by annealing at 500 Â°C for 1 h. The chemical reaction equations in the CuO deposition process are as follows:24

2.3 Deposition of a ZnFe-LDH layer

The ZnFe-LDH co-catalyst was prepared by the photoassisted electrodeposition method as shown in a previous report.29 In a typical procedure, 0.11 M zinc nitrate hexahydrate (Zn(NO3)2Â·6H2O) and 0.15 M ferrous sulfate heptahydrate (FeSO4Â·7H2O) were dissolved in 50 ml of deionized water under magnetic stirring to obtain the electrolyte. The electrodeposition was carried out in a three-electrode system. Subsequently, the as-obtained samples were electrodeposited under a cathodic bias of âˆ’1.0 V vs. Ag/AgCl for 90 s. After deposition, the samples were subjected to spontaneous oxidation in air that leads to the conversion of Fe2+ to Fe3+, thereby forming the ZnFe-LDH layer.

3. Results and discussion

The preparation process of the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode is demonstrated in Fig. 1. Firstly, a uniform ZnO seed layer was coated on the FTO transparent conductive glass substrate by the solâ€“gel method, which could provide nucleation sites for the growth of ZnO NRs.32 Thereupon, ZnO NRs were grown directly on the seed layer via aqueous bath deposition. After that, a CuO thin layer was electrodeposited on ZnO NRs from an alkaline solution of copper sulfate stabilized by lactic acid. According to previous reports, the loading amount and average size of the CuO thin layer would increase with the electrodeposition time.24 Whereas, if the electrodeposition time is too long, the gaps between ZnO NRs would be filled with the CuO modifier, which is not conducive to the carrier transport. Moreover, excessive deposition time would cause the crystal structure of ZnO NRs to be damaged due to its intolerance to the alkaline environment.33 Finally, the ZnFe-LDH co-catalyst was deposited onto the top surface of ZnO@CuO NRs by the photoassisted electrodeposition method. After the entire electrodeposition process was completed, the as-prepared sample was placed in air to promote the self-oxidation of ZnFe2+-LDH to ZnFe3+-LDH.

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was carried out to determine the surface electronic state characteristics of the Zn, Cu, Fe and O elements in the as-prepared ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode. Before investigating the PEC performance, UV-vis diffuse reflectance spectroscopy was used to study the light absorption features of ZnO NRs, ZnO@CuO NRs, and ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanodes. As shown in Fig. 7, the absorption edge of bare ZnO NRs is about 385 nm, which indicates that only photons in the ultraviolet range can be absorbed by ZnO NRs. As we all know, the narrow absorption region relative to the overall solar spectrum severely limits the photoelectric conversion efficiency of ZnO NRs in PEC water splitting. Therefore, it is important to broaden the visible light absorption range of ZnO photoelectrodes. As expected, after depositing another narrow band-gap light absorbing CuO modifier, the ZnO NRs exhibit a significant increase in visible light absorption. The ZnO@CuO NRs show a red shift of the absorption edge by 130 nm compared with the pristine ZnO NRs. Furthermore, the absorption edge of the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode is red shifted to nearly 535 nm.


Fig. 7 UV-vis absorption spectra of ZnO NRs, ZnO@CuO NRs and the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite.

Subsequently, the photoelectrocatalytic activities of ZnO NRs, ZnO@CuO NRs and ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanodes were estimated by a series of PEC measurements. Fig. 8a shows the linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) of the as-obtained samples, and it can be noted that all the samples present positive photocurrent, indicating the intrinsic n-type characteristic of the ZnO NRs. At an applied potential of 1.23 V (vs. RHE), the pristine ZnO NRs generate a low photocurrent density of 0.39 mA cmâˆ’2. After decorating with the CuO modifier, the ZnO@CuO NRs reached an improved photocurrent density of 1.31 mA cmâˆ’2 at 1.23 V vs. RHE. Evidently, the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode yields a higher photocurrent density of approximately 2.08 mA cmâˆ’2, which is 5.3 times that of the bare ZnO NRs at 1.23 V vs. RHE. The higher photocurrent density of ZnO@CuO NRs is attributed to the significant enhancement of light absorption in the visible light region and the promotion of the photogenerated carrier separation and transport. Moreover, the onset potential of ZnO@CuO and ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH photoanodes shows excellent negative shifts of about 36 mV and 85 mV, which were mainly due to the accelerated oxygen evolution reaction (OER) kinetics at the interface between the semiconductor and electrolyte.

Photocurrent densityâ€“time (Jâ€“t) curves were recorded to investigate the photocurrent response of the as-prepared photoanodes. As shown in Fig. 8c, the Jâ€“t curves were measured at a constant bias of 1.23 V vs. RHE under intermittent illumination. When the light is turned on and off, the photocurrent density of all photoanodes rises and falls rapidly, which indicates that all photoanodes exhibit a rapid response to illumination. Under illumination, the photocurrent densities of ZnO@CuO NRs and ZnO@CuO/LDH photoanodes are significantly improved compared to that of bare ZnO, which is consistent with the LSV results. Normally, the photoanodes will generate positive photocurrent transient spikes when the light is turned on, which indicates the accumulation of holes at the semiconductor/electrolyte interface.42 Notably, ZnO@CuO NRs and ZnO@CuO/LDH photoanodes present more rectangular photocurrent responses, demonstrating a significant suppression of the carrier recombination.43 To investigate the stability of the as-prepared photoanodes, the photocurrent density of the samples was measured at potentiostatic 1.23 V vs. RHE under continuous illumination for 2 h. In Fig. 8d, the photocurrent density of ZnO NRs decays rapidly in the initial 1200 s, and the photocurrent density drops to 0.16 mA cmâˆ’2 (about 41% of the initial photocurrent density) after testing for 2 h. In the same duration, the ZnO@CuO NRs and ZnO@CuO/LDH photoanodes retain 90% and 95% of their initial photocurrent density at 1.23 V vs. RHE, respectively, providing solid evidence of the improvement in photostability with the modification of CuO and ZnFe-LDH. Furthermore, the cycling stability curve of ZnO@CuO/LDH photoanodes is shown in Fig. S3.â€  It can be seen that ZnO@CuO/LDH photoanodes exhibit excellent cycling stability; specifically, this photoanode maintained 97% of the initial current density value after 1000 cycles. These advantages mainly originate from the electrostatic field at the heterojunction of ZnO@CuO NRs and restrain the recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs and the presence of ZnFe-LDH accelerates the extraction of photoinduced carriers.

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed to evaluate the charge transfer resistance kinetics of photoanodes. It is well known that the semicircle radius (correspond to the large semicircle in Fig. 9) in the low frequency region reflects the charge transfer resistance at the interface of the photoanode and electrolyte, and the semicircle radius (corresponding to the small semicircle in Fig. 9) in the high frequency region reflects the charge transfer resistance at the interface of the counter electrode and electrolyte.44â€“47 In other words, the smaller semicircle radius in the Nyquist plots indicates the lower charge transfer resistance. As presented in Fig. 9, the semicircle radii of all photoanodes are listed in the following order: ZnO > ZnO@CuO > ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH. The semicircle radius of ZnO@CuO NRs is much smaller than that of bare ZnO NRs, which indicates effective charge separation of the ZnO@CuO NRs. This is mainly because the favorable band alignment at the interfaces between ZnO and CuO is conducive to the extraction of photoinduced carriers. The ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode exhibits the smallest semicircle radius, which corresponds to the lowest charge transfer impedance at the electrode/electrolyte interface. This means that the modification with both CuO and ZnFe-LDH will lead to a better co-catalytic effect than the pristine one, further proving the synergistic promotion effect of CuO and ZnFe-LDH in PEC water splitting.

To sum up, the carrier separation and transport performance was enhanced significantly in the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode than that of the pristine ZnO photoanode. The improvement of photon utilization efficiency and carrier separation/transport was demonstrated in all of the LSV, ABPE and EIS curves in this study. Herein, on the basis of the above experimental results and their analysis, we have drawn the schematic diagram of internal photoinduced electronâ€“hole pair separation and transport process as in Fig. 10. It was obvious that the pâ€“n heterojunction was constructed when the p-type CuO shell was coated on the n-type ZnO core. Meanwhile, both the conduction band (CB) and valence band (VB) levels of CuO are higher than those of ZnO.48,49 Therefore, the gradient energy band alignment between ZnO and CuO is favorable for fast carrier separation and transport in the ZnO@CuO based photoanode. Furthermore, the cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH serves as a hole transport layer, which effectively drives the photogenerated holes to the surface for participating in the water oxidation reaction. As a result, the introduction of the ZnO@CuO heterojunction and ZnFe-LDH cocatalyst effectively accelerates the separation/transport of photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs and promotes the water oxidation kinetics.

4. Conclusion

In summary, we fabricated the ZnO@CuO NR array coreâ€“shell photoanode by depositing a CuO thin layer on ZnO NR arrays. The introduction of CuO can not only broaden the light absorption range of the ZnO photoanode, but also form a coreâ€“shell heterojunction with ZnO NR arrays, which could significantly improve the light harvesting efficiency, shorten the diffusion distance for the charge transfer and inhibit the recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs, thus resulting in markedly enhanced PEC performance. Subsequently, the OER cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH was deposited on the ZnO@CuO heterojunction surface to further promote the hole transfer and accelerate the water oxidation reaction kinetics. As a result of the synergistic effect of the coreâ€“shell heterojunction in ZnO@CuO NRs and cocatalyst ZnFe-LDH, the ZnO@CuO/ZnFe-LDH composite photoanode yields an optimal photocurrent density of 2.08 mA cmâˆ’2 and a photostability of 95% over 2 h at 1.23 V vs. RHE, which is much higher than that of pristine ZnO NRs.
