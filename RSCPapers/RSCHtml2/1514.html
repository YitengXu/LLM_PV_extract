Abstract

Absolute quantum efficiency (QE) of up-conversion (UC) materials is still limited to â¼3%, which strongly limits their applications in many areas. Herein, we report highly efficient UC Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-cylinders prepared via a newly developed Li+/Na+ ion exchange method (IEM) with absolute QE as high as â¼4.0%. A small amount of Li+ is interstitially incorporated in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+, which not only maintains well the micro-cylindrical morphology but also enhances the emission intensity by â¼40%, doubles absolute UCQE (â¼3.0% under a fixed excitation power density of 15 W cmâ2), and prolongs the lifetime. The UC excitation profile is composed of a sharp peak at 976 nm and a weak shoulder at 935 nm. The absolute UCQE of Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ is systemically studied over a wide excitation power density of 0.4â100 W cmâ2, and we obtained impressively high UCQE of 4.0% under 60 W cmâ2 power density, which is among the highest values in the literature. The application of two green UC emissions of Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ in ratiometric thermometers is successfully demonstrated. The high relative temperature sensitivity (SR) of 1113/T2 and excellent repeatability as indicated by three heatingâcooling cycles (293â753 K) suggest their promising application as a temperature sensor.

Introduction

Up-conversion (UC) emission of lanthanide ions, which is realized through the conversion of multiple low energy photons into one high energy photon, has been widely investigated for applications in bio-medicine, optical labels, solar cell converters, lasers, waveguides, and anti-counterfeiting fields owing to its high signal-to-noise ratio, high sensitivity, and large anti-Stokes shift.1â10 Yb3+/Er3+-doped fluoride micro/nano-crystals, such as Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+, have been recognized as one of the most efficient UC materials due to their low phonon energy.11â17 To evaluate the emission efficiency of UC materials, absolute UC quantum efficiency (UCQE), which is defined as the ratio of emitted UC photons to the absorbed excitation photons, is rarely reported.18,19 For example, the theoretical maximum absolute UCQE of the two-photon emission of Yb3+/Er3+ is 50%, in which every two photons absorbed at 980 nm are used for emitting one visible photon (â¼540/655 nm). However, owing to the nonlinear nature of UC emission, UCQE greatly depends on the excitation power density, and the reported absolute UCQE values, which are measured in different excitation power densities, are difficult to be compared with each other. Systematic studies on the excitation power density-dependent absolute UCQE are urgently required. Moreover, the reported highest absolute UCQE in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-crystals is only 2â3% with excitation power density in the range of â¼10â1000 W cmâ2;20â22 such low UCQE greatly limits their applications in many areas, and the development of novel methods to increase UCQE is one of the urgent requirements in the field of UC materials.

For UC nanocrystals, many methods, such as the one using designed coreâshell architecture and plasmon effect, are widely used to enhance UCQE.23â26 However, to enhance the UCQE of micro-crystals, the applicability of these methods is limited.19 Recently, the UC efficiency of lanthanide-doped Î²-NaYF4 micro-crystals has been greatly improved via our newly developed hydrothermal ion-exchange modification (IEM) method.7,8,27,28 The IEM method is a very promising method to synthesis UC fluoride materials with high UCQE and controlled morphology. For example, our recently reported IEM method has already realized Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+, which has comparable efficiency with that of the materials prepared by the traditional high-temperature solid-state method, and the absolute UCQE reached â¼3% for IEM Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Tm3+ under 10 W cmâ2 power density and 980 nm laser excitation.7,8 Furthermore, in previously reported studies, co-doping of a small amount of inert ions has been frequently used to modify the local structure of lanthanides and to improve the UCQE of lanthanide-doped micro/nano-crystals.29,30 For instance, via co-doping of a small amount of Li+ in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+, the UC emission intensities of green and red emissions can be enhanced by 47 and 23 times, respectively.30 Co-doping of a small amount of Li+ in Y2O3:Yb3+,Er3+ also realized 25 times enhancement in the UC emission intensity.31 Consequently, higher UCQE is expected for Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ by the combination of two methods via co-doping of small amount of Li+ during the IEM process.

In this study, a small amount of Li+ is co-doped into Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-cylinders synthesized via a newly developed Li+/Na+ ion exchange method (IEM) using Î³-YF3 as a precursor. Li+ is evidenced to be interstitially doped in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ with well-maintained micro-cylindrical morphology. The absolute UCQE of Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-crystals is systematically studied under various excitation power densities from 0.4 to 100 W cmâ2. Impressively, the highest UCQE of as high as 4.0% with only 60 W cmâ2 power density is obtained, which is among the highest values in literature. The application of two green UC emissions as temperature sensors is demonstrated. The excellent repeatability in three heatingâcooling cycles and high relative temperature sensitivity (SR) of 1113/T2 indicate their applications in temperature sensing.

Interestingly, all diffraction peaks of Î²-NaYF4 gradually shift to a lower angle with an increase in the LiF/NaF ratio. As an example, the peak of the (100) reflection gradually shifts to lower angle with an increase in the LiF/NaF ratio from 17.18Â° (LiF/NaF = 0/100) to 17.14Â° (LiF/NaF = 82/18), as shown in Fig. 1b. A similar phenomenon can also be observed for other diffraction peaks, as exemplarily shown in Fig. 1c. According to the well-known Bragg's law, this result indicates expansion of the unit cell of the Î²-NaYF4 crystal. There are two possibilities by which Li+ can enter the Î²-NaYF4 crystal lattice: displacing (i) Na+ lattice sites or (ii) interstitial sites. The ionic radius of Li+ (76 pm, in an octahedral environment) is â¼25% smaller than that of Na+ (102 pm, in an octahedral environment), resulting in very low solubility of Li+ in the Na+ sites.35 Thus, the displacement of Na+ lattice sites by Li+ ions leads to the shrinkage of the unit cell of the Î²-NaYF4 crystal and the shift of the diffraction peaks to higher angles, which is not supported by XRD. On the contrary, when Li+ interstitially enters Î²-NaYF4 lattice, it results in the expansion of the unit cell of Î²-NaYF4 and the movement of the diffraction peaks to lower angles, which is fully supported by XRD. Consequently, we believe that Li+ ions interstitially enter the Î²-NaYF4 lattice, which leads to the expansion of the unit cell of Î²-NaYF4 and benefits the UC emission (which will be discussed later).

The appearance of a small amount of Li+ in the Î²-NaYF4 phase is verified by ICP-OES analyses of Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+ (LiF/NaF = 40/60), as summarized in Table 1. The atomic ratio of Y:Yb:Er is determined to be 78.1:19.3:2.6, which is similar to the nominal composition of 78:20:2. The atomic ratio of Li:Na is 98.6:1.4, which confirms the appearance of small amount of Li+ in the Î²-NaYF4 crystal. The Li+ concentration is far below the concentration in the designed LiF/NaF ratio, hinting that most of LiF does not react with YF3, and only a small amount of Li+ enters the Î²-NaYF4 lattice in the interstitial sites.

However, the excitation spectrum is vital for choosing the optimum pumping source, e.g., wavelength and width. Thus, we have developed a home-built optical system to measure UC excitation spectrum by scanning the laser wavelength from 900 to 1000 nm with a step of 2 nm and a fixed excitation power density of 3 W cmâ2. The incorporation of a small amount of Li+ in Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+ in the interstitial sites distorts the local symmetric environment of Yb3+/Er3+, as indicated by the XRD results in Fig. 1aâc. The 4fâ4f transitions of trivalent lanthanides (Ln3+) are parity-forbidden. The transition strength of Ln3+ is directly linked to the crystal structure symmetry around Ln3+. The distortion of the local symmetric environment of Yb3+/Er3+ due to Li+ incorporation into the interstitial sites leads to enhanced absorption strength and radiative emission rates, resulting in an overall enhancement of UC emission intensity and UCQE, as is proven by other reports that have used similar co-doping method.29â31,36,37 Thus, strongly enhanced UC emission intensity and prolonged lifetime can be observed via Li+ co-doping in IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+.

UC is a nonlinear process, which means that the absolute UCQE strongly depends on the excitation power density, and high excitation power density (below the saturation value) results in high UCQE value. However, the absolute UCQE value has been rarely reported even at a fixed power density due to technical difficulty. We developed a home-built optical system to measure UCQE, which is dependent on the excitation power density. Only UC emissions in green and red regions were considered for the UCQE calculations. We first measured the absolute UCQE values of Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+ with LiF/NaF = 0/100 and 40/60 at a fixed power density of 15 W cmâ2. The UCQE value of the LiF/NaF = 40/60 sample was determined to be 3.0%, which was around two times higher than that of the LiF-free sample. This result further confirmed the improved UC performance of Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ prepared by Li+/Na+ ion exchange. UC emission mechanism

Fig. 4 schematically illustrates the UC emission mechanism for Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+. Upon excitation with 980 nm LD, electrons of Yb3+ ions are excited from the ground state of 2F7/2 to the excited state of 2F5/2. Two sequential energy transfers from Yb3+ to Er3+ sequentially populate Er3+: 4I11/2 and Er3+: 4F7/2 levels.30 The following non-radiative relaxations populate the green UC emitting levels of 2H11/2 and 4S3/2. There are four routes to populate the red UC emitting level of 4F9/2: (i) back energy transfer (BET) from Er3+ to Yb3+via Er3+: 4S3/2 + Yb3+: 2F7/2 â Er3+: 4I13/2 + Yb3+: 2F5/2, followed by energy transfer from Yb3+ to Er3+via Er3+: 4I13/2 + Yb3+: 2F5/2 â Er3+: 4F9/2 + Yb3+: 2F7/2; (ii) the radiative emission process via Er3+: 4S3/2 â 4I13/2, followed by energy transfer from Yb3+ to Er3+via Er3+: 4I13/2 + Yb3+: 2F5/2 â Er3+: 4F9/2 + Yb3+: 2F7/2; (iii) the non-radiative relaxation from 4I11/2 to 4I13/2, followed by energy transfer from Yb3+ to Er3+, and (iv) the non-radiative relaxation from 4S3/2 to 4F9/2. The large energy gaps of 4I11/2/4I13/2 (3600 cmâ1) and 4S3/2/4F9/2 (3600 cmâ1) make the routes (iii and iv) less possible. Thus, we think that the BET process from Er3+ to Yb3+ is the main route to obtain red UC for Li+/Na+ IEM Î²-NaYF4:20%Yb3+,2%Er3+. The BET process from Yb3+ to Er3+ is widely reported in Yb3+/Er3+ co-doping system to achieve red UC emission of Er3+;38,39 it is caused due to the very close distance between Yb3+ and Er3+ with high doping concentrations of Yb3+ and Er3+, but the radiative emission via Er3+: 4S3/2 â 4I13/2 may also happen. In consideration of the high Yb3+ doping concentration (20 mol%) used in our study, the non-radiative BET process is more dominative.

Repeatability of UC ratiometric thermometry

Conclusions

In conclusion, we report highly efficient up-conversion emission of Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ micro-cylinders prepared via a newly developed Li+/Na+ ion exchange method by using Î³-YF3:Yb3+,Er3+ nanocrystals as a precursor. Li+ enters a Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ crystal via an interstitial pathway. Co-doping of a small amount Li+ in Î²-NaYF4:Yb3+,Er3+ not only maintains well the micro-cylindrical morphology but also results in enhanced emission intensity (â¼40%) and prolonged lifetime. UCQE of the Li+/Na+ IEM sample (3.0%) is around two times bigger than that of the Li+-free sample under excitation of a fixed excitation power density of 15 W cmâ2. Power-dependent UCQE of the Li+/Na+ IEM sample is investigated over a wide power density range of 0.4â100 W cmâ2. Impressively, the highest UCQE value is as high as 4.0% with 60 W cmâ2 power density, and it is among the highest values in literature. The application of two green UC emissions as temperature sensors is demonstrated. Also, excellent repeatability in three heatingâcooling cycles and high relative temperature sensitivity (SR) of 1113/T2 hint their applications in temperature sensing.
