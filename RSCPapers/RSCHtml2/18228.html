A solar and thermal multi-sensing microfiber supercapacitor with intelligent self-conditioned capacitance and body temperature monitoring†

Abstract

A solar and thermal multi-sensing all-solid-state microfiber supercapacitor (ASSMFSC) with prominent electrochemical and mechanical performance, reliable environmental responsivity, and intelligent self-conditioned capacitance was constructed using a cellulose nanofibril-graphene-conjugated polymer, which was assembled as both a core-sheath microfiber electrode (CSMFE) and a sensing unit. The multicomponent hierarchical synergistic design fully exploited the unique attributes and synergistic interaction of each component to endow the well-designed CSMFE with high capacitance and mechanical robustness as well as excellent thermal sensitivity and photothermal conversion, enabling the valid modulation of redox reactions, interface performance, or electronic/ionic transfer in the microfiber supercapacitors toward complex external stimulations and building interrelated working responses. The fabricated ASSMFSC presented a total volumetric capacitance of 85.8 mF cm−3 (vs. the whole device), excellent cycle stability, and a volumetric energy density of 11.9 mW h cm−3. Moreover, ASSMFSC showed excellent solar-thermal conversion capacitance enhancement. At one solar power density (1 kW m−2), the capacitance was two times that of the device in the dark and the device exhibited self-regulation capacitance ability with the change in the solar intensity. As a thermosensitive device, ASSMFSC with a current sensitivity of 0.47437 ± 0.00549 °C displayed a fast response and excellent reproducibility to sense subtle temperature changes. This work demonstrates that the CSMFE-driven microfiber supercapacitor can be used as a photothermal conversion energy storage system and for body temperature monitoring, which provides an essential reference and optimistic incentives for new generation energy-related devices.

Introduction

With the rapid development of various intelligent electronics, it is of crucial practical significance to design innovative wearable energy storage systems with integrated multiple functions that are especially capable of sensing and responding to changes in the external environment, meeting different usage needs and expanding the interactive experiences of users. Multifunctional energy storage devices with shape memory, electrochromic and humidity sensing capabilities can be accomplished by adding nano sensing materials with stimulus-response capacity or connecting them in series with other sensing devices.1–4 Unfortunately, most of the currently reported devices are based on single stimulus-response systems lacking in diversity. In addition, the integrated devices constructed by conventional on-chip electrodes in series with other functional devices have the flaw of complex manufacturing processes, increased overall size, and reduced flexibility, which may limit their practical applications in wearable electronics.5,6 In this context, the microfiber supercapacitor (MFSC) is a promising candidate due to its apparent advantages, including lightweight, small dimension, integrated compatibility, and mechanical flexibility.7 The exploitation of innovative and intelligent MFSCs is a feasible strategy from theory to practice. Nevertheless, due to the unique 1D structure, it is difficult to effectively regulate the synthesis of active materials and the overall structure of the microfiber. Problems such as the unsatisfactory energy density, weak mechanical strength, and slow stimulus-response ability caused by the limited reaction surface area, weak interface bonding, and low utilization of active materials are also the key issues to be solved.8,9 Due to the strict requirements of the electrode materials, response units, preparation methods and compatibility, the design of a multi-sensing MFSC is indeed a formidable challenge.

Nowadays, to build a better homeland, space and polar explorations have become one of the most important research spotlights in the world. However, in a low-temperature environment or as the temperature goes down, the capacitance performance of energy storage devices, especially supercapacitors, can be severely degraded or even fail.10 In this regard, there is significant research value in effectively expanding the charge storage capacity of supercapacitors and solving the problem of capacitance hysteresis at low temperatures through practical methods. As we all know, solar energy is an excellent renewable resource through the conversion and utilization of solar energy and some processes like seawater desalination, photocatalysis and artificial photosynthesis.11,12 Notwithstanding, few studies have focused on their applications in solar response fiber-shaped supercapacitors to date.10 Hence, finding suitable electrode materials to prepare an MFSC with a solar response capacitance must change to meet the work requirements under different environmental conditions, which is crucial for the development of supercapacitors.

Among various electrode materials, graphene (GE) and polyaniline (PANI) are endowed with desirable photothermal conversion effects, making the integration of energy storage and solar response in a wearable device possible.13–16 As a nano additive used to enhance the mechanical and hydrophilic properties of graphene, cellulose nanofibrils (CNFs) have been widely studied for their inexpensive, biodegradable and eco-friendly features. Currently, a series of studies on the graphene/cellulose nanofibril (GE/CNFs) composites have been published. These research studies mainly focus on the mechanical and capacitive properties.17–19 To the best of our knowledge, there is no report about constructing a multifunctional core-sheath GE/CNFs/PANI microfiber supercapacitors with the solar and thermal response. Based on the above considerations, a practical and effective design strategy was used to construct novel microfiber electrodes, so that microfiber capacitors could achieve excellent energy storage capacity and mechanical performance, while having the multifunctional response and scalable, practical technical achievement. This is not only a valuable innovation, but also particularly crucial and urgent.

As a conceptual exhibition, a solar and thermal response multi-sensing ASSMFSC was realized by using a core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber as a hybrid electrode and sensing device. This device was inspired by the 3D network structure between the collagen fibres and elastin fibres in the reticular layer of animal skin.20 We suspect that firm and robust electrode materials (graphene and polyaniline) can combine with soft and flexible nano-reinforcers (CNFs) successfully to build these high-performance hybrid microfibers through effective strategies. Therefore, we utilize the macromolecular interaction and intercalation effect of graphene and cellulose nanofibrils to prepare GE/CNFs core hybrid microfibers to adopt a simple and controllable wet-spinning method. Ultimately, core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers are formed by in situ depositing highly oriented polyaniline nanorod arrays on the surface of the GE/CNFs core microfibers. By a multicomponent hierarchical synergistic design strategy to entirely probe the synergy effect of the components, the GE/CNFs@PANI core-sheath microfiber electrode (CSMFE) integrates excellent electrochemical activity, mechanical robustness, photothermal conversion effects, and thermal sensitivity into one single CSMFE. Thus, such well-designed CSMFE can act contemporaneously as a current collector, hybrid electrode, and stimuli-responsive device to endow ASSMFSC with prominent electrochemical performance and versatile applications, including a photothermal conversion energy storage system and temperature sensors. In addition, the mechanism of the interface bonding of various components, electronic/ionic transfer and redox reaction in CSMFE, and the effect of the external environmental stimulations on ASSMFSC in practical applications are also elucidated to understand its working principle.

Experimental section

Materials

All reagents were of analytical grade and used as received. Pine powders were bought from (Yangzhou, China). Graphite was obtained from the Shanghai Yifan Graphite Co., Ltd. KMnO4, NaNO3, NaClO2, KOH, aniline, 5-sulfosalicylic acid (SSA), ammonium persulfate (APS), and hydroiodic acid (45%) were bought from the Aladdin Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. H2SO4 and CH3COOH were purchased from the Nanjing Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) was acquired from Sigma-Aldrich Co., Ltd.
Preparation of the GE/CNF core microfibers. First, the well-dispersed graphene oxide aqueous dispersion (GO, 10 mg mL−1) and CNF aqueous dispersion (10 mg mL−1) were mixed with different weight ratios of 1:0, 1:0.25, 1:0.5, 1:0.75 and 1:1. Then, the uniform dispersions were formed by sonication for 30 minutes. After that, the GO/CNF dispersions were injected into a rotating acetic acid coagulation bath by a continuous automatic wet spinning micro-device. After steeping for one minute in the coagulation bath, the microfibers were solidified and dried by an infrared heating lamp. Then, we collected the microfibers by a winding device and put the microfibers into a vacuum oven, and they were reduced by diluted hydroiodic acid at 85 °C for 10 h. The microfibers were then removed and washed several times with ethanol and water, respectively. Finally, the GE and GE/CNF microfibers were obtained by drying overnight in a vacuum oven at 65 °C.
Preparation of the core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers. The GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers were fabricated by chemical in situ polymerization. 1.82 g of 5-sulfosalicylic acid (SSA) was dissolved in 65 mL Milli-Q water by ultrasonic dispersion for 15 minutes to achieve a homogeneous solution. Then, the aniline monomer (0.150 mL) was added to the solution for ultrasonic dispersion for 30 minutes. Next, the GE/CNFs microfibers were placed in the solution at 2 °C. Two hours later, the ammonium persulfate (APS) solution (the molar ratio to aniline is 1:1) was added and reacted at 2 °C for 4, 6, 8, 10, 12 and 24 h. Finally, the GE/CNFs@PANI core-sheath microfibers were obtained by washing the solution 6 times with ethanol and Milli-Q water, and then placed into a vacuum oven at 65 °C for 12 h.
Assembly of the two-electrode aqueous microfiber supercapacitor (AQMFSC). The schematic diagram of the two-electrode AQMFSC is shown in Fig. S1d.† 1 M H2SO4 electrolyte was prepared by mixing 50 g H2SO4 in 500 mL Milli-Q water (1:10, in weight) and stirred until the solution was mixed uniformly. Two samples of the same length were taken from the GE, GE/CNFs, GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers, and assembled into positive and negative electrodes (effective length: 0.4 cm) employing platinum plate electrode clips. Then, the positive and negative microfiber electrodes were immersed into a C001 electrolytic cell filled with the sulfuric acid electrolyte.
Assembly of the all-solid-sate microfiber supercapacitors (ASSMFSCs). The schematic diagram of the ASSMFSCs is shown in Fig. 6d inset. The PVA/H2SO4 gel electrolyte was prepared by mixing 6 g PVA with 6 g H2SO4 in 60 mL Milli-Q water (1:1:10, in weight), and stirred at 95 °C until the solution became clear. The two GE/CNFs@PANI core-sheath microfibers were coated with this gel electrolyte several times. Then, the two PVA/H2SO4 coated composite microfibers were slightly twisted, and finally covered with PVA/H2SO4 along the twisted part to obtain ASSMFSC. The effective working length (wrapped area) of all ASSMFSCs is 0.4 cm.

Results and discussion

As shown in Fig. 1a, we first prepared the GO/CNFs composite dispersions by mixing the GO flakes and CNFs at different mass ratios. The CNFs made by chemical mechanical methods had excellent aspect ratios.21 The average thickness of the GO flakes was about 1 nm, and the lateral dimension was about 800 nm to 1 μm (Fig. 1b–e). The zeta potential was used to further determine the stability of the GO/CNFs dispersions (Fig. 1f). The CNFs and GO had excellent stability in Milli-Q water. When the pH value was 7, they were both negatively charged, and the zeta potential was −63.3 mV and −43.8 mV for the CNFs and GO, respectively. Compared with GO, the CNFs had a high zeta potential value. The reason for this phenomenon may be attributed to the sufficient microfibrillation of the cellulose fibers during the repeated ultrafine grinding process, so that more hydroxyl groups on the surface of CNFs can be exposed. In addition, although potassium hydroxide can remove most of the hemicellulose, some hemicellulose remains in the CNFs. These few residual hemicelluloses have been proven to be more conducive to the uniform dispersion of CNFs, which makes it have a high zeta potential value.22 The mixed dispersions also have excellent stability. After 20 days, the zeta potential remains at a higher potential window, and no precipitation occurs (Fig. 1a, inset). The reason for maintaining excellent dispersion stability is that the electrostatic repulsion between the charged hydroxyl groups on the CNFs and GO sheets and the steric hindrance formed by CNFs prevent the scattered GO flakes from reassembling.23

Shear viscosity measurements were used to determine the rheological behaviours and spinnability of the GO/CNF mixed dispersions (Fig. 1g). The GO/CNF composite dispersions showed that the viscosity became thinner with increasing shear rate, and this trend did not change with the increase of CNF content. On the other hand, with the addition of CNFs, the viscosity of the composite dispersion tended to increase. The exception was the intertwining of CNFs, which indirectly proved the interaction between CNFs and graphene oxide flakes. In the GO/CNFs mixed spinning solution, the abundant hydroxyl groups of the CNFs can form hydrogen bonds with the neighboring graphene oxide flakes due to the availability of –O– to –O– bonding (Fig. 1h).24

The interactions between CNFs and GO flakes provide an excellent opportunity for binding the firm GO flakes with the flexible CNFs rebar. We fabricated a core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber through a multicomponent hierarchical synergistic design strategy. First, GO/CNF dispersions were injected into an acetic acid coagulation bath using a continuous automatic wet-spinning device to form GO/CNF microfibers (Fig. 2). Acetic acid has high acidity and hydrophilicity, which is an effective coagulant for the GO/CNF solution. Acetic acid can not only absorb moisture from the GO/CNF gel microfibers, but also diffuse into water and ionize to release protons. This inhibits the dissociation of the acidic groups on the GO sheets. Hence, the GO flakes and CNFs can be effectively coagulated by strong hydrogen bonding interactions.25 Finally, robust GE/CNF core microfibers were obtained by a subsequent hydroiodic acid reduction and drying treatment. The effect of the CNF addition on the performance of the GE/CNFs composite microfiber was analyzed from the aspects of hydrophilicity, mechanical properties, and electrochemical properties (Fig. S1–S4, ESI†). After a comprehensive analysis, the GE/CNF (1:0.75) composite microfibers was selected as the core microfiber electrode with an area capacitance of 185 mF cm−2 (at 10 mV s−1), a conductivity of 5950 S m−1, and a tensile strength of 423 MPa. More details of the results and discussion are written in the ESI† Analysis section. Finally, a core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber was obtained by in situ depositing highly oriented polyaniline nanorod arrays onto the surface of the GE/CNF core microfibers (Fig. 2). By controlling the depositing time (4–24 h), different morphologies of the polyaniline nanorod arrays can grow on the surface of the GE/CNF microfibers (Fig. S5, ESI†). Furthermore, we illustrated the principle of the in situ polymerization growth of polyaniline nanorods array (ESI† Analysis section). GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers are highly flexible and robust, so they could be woven into the letters NFU (an abbreviation of Nanjing Forestry University) and pentagonal shapes in the cotton textiles (Fig. 2b and c inset). Ultimately, an ASSMFSC was constructed by twisting two GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFEs covered with a PVA/H2SO4 gel electrolyte.

The GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFEs with the unique multicomponent hierarchical structure described above possess the following features: (i) CNFs as an ideal nano-functional agent not only can effectively inhibit the re-stacking of the graphene flakes and improve the electrolyte wettability of the hybrid microfibers, but the 3D network interpenetrating structure formed by CNFs can also cause the light scattering inside the microfiber as a “secondary light source” for the GE sheets to reabsorb and use, thereby reducing the light transmission of the CSMFEs. (ii) The highly oriented polyaniline nanorod array can provide a larger reaction surface area, which is more conducive for the electrolyte and solar energy to enter the active region, thereby improving the capacitive performance and photothermal conversion efficiency for the CSMFEs. (iii) By optimizing the reliable synthesis strategy and multicomponent hierarchical structure design, the CSMFE can act contemporaneously as a current collector, hybrid electrode, and stimuli-responsive devices to endow ASSMFSC to achieve the efficient integration of energy storage and multifunctional stimulus-response without sacrificing other properties.

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was used to dissect the morphology and structure of the as-prepared microfibers. The GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber demonstrated a flexible mechanical performance that can be knotted without any fractures (Fig. 3g). Compared with the GE@PANI microfiber (Fig. 3a), the diameter of the GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber (Fig. 3d) increased from 40 μm to 45 μm. This is because the presence of CNFs can expand the space among GE nanosheets, which is consistent with the results of the XRD test analysis (Fig. S2a, ESI†). Although a large number of the PANI nanorod arrays were grown on the surfaces of the GE@PANI microfiber (Fig. 3b) and GE/CNFs@PNAI microfiber (Fig. 3e), the surfaces of the microfibers still showed a uniform distribution without severe agglomeration. Fig. 3c and f further demonstrates that the polyaniline nanorods have a width of about 20 nm and a height of 100 nm, which are perpendicular to each other and crosslinked on the microfiber surface. Moreover, the energy dispersive X-ray element mapping analysis (EDS) shows uniform distribution of carbon and nitrogen on the GE/CNFs@PANI microfiber, indicating that the PANI nanorods were successfully coated on the GE/CNF microfiber (Fig. 3h and i).

To further investigate the interaction among GE, CNFs and PANI, X-ray diffraction (XRD), X-ray photoelectron spectra (XPS), Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) and Raman spectroscopies have been employed. Fig. 4a compares the XRD patterns of the CNFs, GE, PANI, GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers, where the sharp peaks of the CNFs located at 21.58° correspond to the (2 0 0) planes of cellulose I.26 The diffraction peaks at 24.18° show a typical graphite structure (0 0 2) of GE, and the peaks at 15.68°, 20.58°, and 25.6° correspond to the (0 1 1), (0 2 0), and (2 0 0) planes of PANI in the emeraldine salt form, respectively.27 Compared with the principal (0 0 2) peak positions of GE, the three microfibers gradually shift to the left as follows: GE (24.18°), GE@PANI (23.86°) and GE/CNFs@PANI (23.3°). According to the Bragg formula, the (0 0 2) interlayer spacings of GE, GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI are 0.367 nm, 0.372 nm and 0.381 nm, respectively.28 The XRD of the GE/CNF core microfibers with different CNF content also shows the same trend (Fig. S2a, ESI†). This result shows that the CNFs can effectively increase the spacing of graphene sheets inside the microfibers, which are consistent with the SEM conclusion.

This confirms that a large number of cations were generated between PANI and GE, resulting in high conductivity.30 As shown in Fig. 4c, the C 1s spectrum of GE/CNFs@PANI can be categorized into four main peaks of 284.6 eV (C–C sp2), 285.3 eV (C–C sp3), 286.4 eV (C–N/C–O), and 288.2 eV (CO). The relatively small recorded peaks at 286.4 eV (C–O) and 288.2 eV (CO) suggest that GO was been well reduced.31,32

The Raman spectra of the GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers both reveal typical PANI peaks, indicating that the PANI nanorods were well assembled with the GE and GE/CNFs core during the process of in situ polymerization (Fig. 4d). Notably, the Raman spectrum of GE/CNFs@PANI shows an enhanced CN stretching of the quinoid ring at 1477 cm−1, and a new peak appears at 1590 cm−1 designated as the CC stretching vibrational mode of the quinoid ring (labeled as a red circle), compared with that of pure PANI. These features probably result from the strong intermolecular π–π stacking, which causes the overlapping of the pz orbitals between the basal planes of GE and the quinoid rings of the PANI backbone.33,34 Thus, PANI can be well adsorbed on the surface of GE. Additionally, the characteristic broad peaks located at 1632, 1479, 1381, and 1095 cm−1 are ascribed to the CC stretching vibration of the quinonoid ring, the CC stretching vibration of the benzenoid ring, C–N vibration of PANI, and C–O stretching vibration mode (νC–O).35,36

The electrochemical performances of the as-prepared microfiber electrodes were first evaluated via constructing a symmetrical aqueous microfiber supercapacitor (AQMFSC) in 1 M H2SO4 liquid electrolyte. To further explore the effect of the polymerization time of the PANI nanorods on the electrochemical performance, the GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI composite microfibers with different polymerization times of PANI (4, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 24 h) were prepared by the same procedure. Fig. 5a shows that the increased current density of the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMSCs with increasing PANI polymerization time below 10 h should be ascribed to the pseudocapacitance provided by PANI, which was uniformly deposited on the GE/CNFs core microfiber. However, with a polymerization time exceeding 10 h, such as 12 and 24 h, the excessive PANI deposited on the surface of the GE/CNFs core microfiber led to the outermost polyaniline being unable to effectively contact the surface of the microfiber. This resulted in the charge passing through the microfiber being unable to effectively diffuse into the electrolyte, thereby decreasing the electrochemical properties of AQMSCs.37 The detailed CV curves (from 10 mV s−1 to 100 mV s−1) of the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSCs with different polymerization times are shown in Fig. S7.† At the polymerization time of 10 h, GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFE has the highest capacitance at 303 mF cm−2 with a scan rate of 10 mV s−1 (Fig. 5b). By comparing the CV curves of the GE@PANI AQMFSCs with different polymerization times at the same scan rate (Fig. S6 and S8a, ESI†), it can be similarly found that it has the most significant area capacitance with 10 h of polymerization time. Based on the above results, 10 h is supposed to be the best optimized PANI polymerization time, so the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h CSMFE was selected for the follow-up studies. As shown in Fig. 5c, the CV curves of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC present rectangular shapes in the scan rate range from 10 to 100 mV s−1, indicating the high-grade electrochemical behaviours and excellent charge transmission. The galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) curves of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC show nearly triangular shapes at various current densities ranging from 0.1 to 1 mA cm−2 (Fig. 5d). The corresponding specific capacitances (Fig. 5e) of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h CSMFE were 615 mF cm−2 (547 F cm−3) at a current density of 0.1 mA cm−2. Compared to the current density of 0.3 mA cm−2, the capacitance was greatly improved. It may be due to the degradation products of PANI, hydroxyl or amino-terminal oligoaniline at lower current densities, which produced an extremely high area capacitance.38 Although the capacitance of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h CSMFE showed a gradual drop at high current densities, it was still maintained at 325 mF cm−2 (289 F cm−3) at 1 mA cm−2. Furthermore, the charge transfer motive mechanism of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC was analyzed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) (Fig. 5f). The inherent ohmic resistance (Rs) of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC is 47.25 Ω. The vertical lines at low frequency and the short expected length in the Warburg regions of the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC indicate the functional ion diffusion capabilities and excellent capacitive performance. The electrochemical stability was evaluated by a long-term GCD test with a current density of 2 mA cm−2. In Fig. 5g, the GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC maintained an initial capacitance of 92.6% after 5000 charge/discharge cycles without significant attenuation, showing excellent cycle stability.

Based on the CV curve at 30 mV s−1, the curve changed from a leaf shape to the rectangular shape after the combination of GE with CNFs and PANI, which shows that the capacitance performance improved (Fig. 5h). The corresponding area capacitance of the four CSMFEs were calculated to be 17 mF cm−2 (GE), 150 mF cm−2 (GE/CNFs), 180 mF cm−2 (GE@PANI) and 255 mF cm−2 (GE/CNFs@PANI), respectively. The area capacitance of GE/CNFs@PANI-10 h AQMFSC was 15 times better than that of GE AQMFSC. According to Fig. 5i, the Nyquist plots show the inherent ohmic resistance (Rs) of GE, GE/CNFs, GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSCs is 19.25 Ω, 22.26 Ω, 35.62 Ω and 47.25 Ω, respectively. The relatively close Rs values indicate that our efficient synergistic design alleviates the conductivity decline of the CNF addition to CSMFE. The interfacial charge transfer resistance Rct of the GE, GE/CNFs, GE@PANI and GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSCs is 15917 Ω, 2642 Ω, 867 Ω and 458.6 Ω, respectively. Compared with the as-prepared GE and binary AQMFSCs, the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSC has the smallest interfacial charge transfer resistance (Rct). This suggests the improvement of the charge transfer process of electrons and ions. In addition, the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSC exhibits quasi-vertical lines at the low-frequency region, which indicates the excellent charge storage performance.39 The reason for the performance conversion can be summarized into two aspects: owing to the severe self-aggregation of the GE flake structure inside the GE and GE@PANI microfiber, the area near the roots of the PANI nanorod and inside the microfiber may not be easily accessible to the ions in the electrolyte approaching from the outside. However, when the CNF template was used, the effective ion channels were formed by the 3D network structure of the CNFs inside microfiber. A large number of hydroxyl groups on the molecular chain greatly facilitated the accessibility of the ions to the GE flakes and PANI nanorod arrays, increasing the interfacial bonding between the microfiber and the electrolyte ions, which resulted in a more extensive specific capacitance. On the other hand, the multicomponent hierarchical structure avoids the super-high density deposition of the active material to make PANI have excellent redox activity. Highly oriented polyaniline nanorod arrays with a regular arrangement help increase the diffusion rate of the electrolyte ions to the CSMFE surface, thereby reducing the internal resistance of the material. This can provide a more distinguished capacitance for the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSC. Benefiting from the synergistic effect that promotes the interfacial charge transfer among GE, CNFs and PANI, the GE/CNFs@PANI AQMFSC demonstrates a commendable capacitance. GE and PANI with the excellent photothermal effect contribute properties that help GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFE become a solar response device.10,13–15 Hence, we used GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFE as the hybrid electrodes and sensing units to fabricate the solar response ASSMFSCs (Fig. 8g). Interestingly, the newly designed GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC shows excellent solar-thermal conversion performance. The reason can be attributed to the fact that GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFE with its multicomponent hierarchical structure plays a crucial role in the process of solar utilization. First of all, the directed PANI nanorod arrays located on the surface of the GE sheets not only provide the GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFE with a larger reaction surface area, which is more conducive to sunlight entering the GE surface, but also prolong the interaction length between the solar energy and matter, and reduces the sunlight reflection to improve the photothermal conversion rate.10,47 Second, the 3D interpenetrating network structure formed by CNFs can cause the light scattering inside the CSMFE as a “secondary light source” for GE to reabsorb and use, thereby reducing light transmission of the CSMFE (Fig. 8c).48 As expected, the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC has more significant temperature changes than GE ASSMFSC under the same solar power density (Fig. 8a and S9†). At the intensity of one solar (1 kW m−2), the infrared image clearly shows the temperature changes of GE/CNFs@PANI and GE ASSMFSCs after sunlight exposure (Fig. 8b). After 200 s, the temperature has increased by 41.6 °C and 30.8 °C, respectively, which verifies the existence of the photothermal effects in the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC.

To further explore the causes of the capacitance enhancement, the Nyquist plots and EIS were investigated (Fig. 8f). Under different solar power densities, the inherent ohmic resistance Rs (the first intercept along the real axis) of the device was reduced from 3.004 kΩ to 1.945 kΩ, indicating that the conductivity of the electrode material was improved (Fig. 8h inset). The reason for this improvement is the increased temperature of PANI and GE under simulated sunlight illumination. This made the electrons become thermal activated to move from the valence band to the conduction band, generating electron and hole carriers, which resulted in a reduction in resistance.49 The interface charge transfer resistance (Rct) of the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC under different solar power densities are 5.43 kΩ (in dark), 4.562 kΩ (0.5 kW m−2), 4.117 kΩ (0.75 kW m−2) and 3.789 kΩ (1 kW m−2), suggesting a decreased barrier to ionic transport. The rising slope of the curves in the low-frequency region implies an improvement in the capacitor performance. Considering the negligible contribution of the PVA/H2SO4 electrolyte and PET substrate to the photothermal conversion, the change in the capacitance of ASSMFSC under simulated sunlight illumination can be attributed to the increase in conductivity due to the photothermal conversion of the GE/CNFs@PANI CSMFEs and the improvement in the ion transport efficiency.

In order to confirm that the photothermal effects mainly trigger the enhanced capacitance under solar illumination, the electrochemical properties of the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC were investigated (Fig. S11, ESI†) by heating and irradiating to the same surface temperature (35 °C and 60 °C) respectively. The CV curves, GCD curves and Nyquist plots of heating to different temperatures were found to be similar to the corresponding photothermal conversion under solar illumination (Fig. S11, ESI†), indicating the thermal-driven predominant capacitance enhancement mechanism. Notably, the CV integral area and GCD discharge time of the solar radiation are slightly more significant than that of only heating at the same temperature, which shows that it had a more superior capacitance performance. To understand the basic principles of the capacitance change behaviour discussed above, we conducted an AC impedance analysis of the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC. The inherent resistance (Rs) and interface transfer resistance (Rct) at a solar radiation photothermal temperature are all slightly smaller than those at the corresponding heating temperature, which means that the ASSMFSC has better conductivity and charge transfer efficiency under solar radiation. Considering that the infrared camera records the surface temperature, and the ASSMFSC has a temperature transition zone under solar radiation, the overall temperature of the ASSMFSC under solar irradiation was slightly lower than the temperature during direct heating.10 Therefore, the reason for this phenomenon may be that the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC has a specific photoresponse during the solar radiation process.50 The periodic and rapid light/dark conversion experiments on the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC were conducted to further confirm that ASSMFSC has the ability of photoresponse. Interestingly, under the periodic light/dark conversion experiment of simulating sunlight (0.5 kW m−2), it can be found that the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC has a fast and reproducible current change (Fig. S12a, ESI†). This shows that during the solar radiation, the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSCs have the photoresponse behaviour.9

In the face of the real-time simulated body temperature changes (from 37 °C to 42 °C), ASSMFSC reveals the corresponding current curve and has a sensitive ability to sense subtle temperature changes. Therefore, we can accept different response signals, which accurately correspond to the average body temperature, low fever and high fever (Fig. 10c).52

To further confirm the feasibility, we installed ASSMFSC on the forehead, arms and forefinger of a volunteer for the body temperature monitoring and recorded its monitoring signal through the electrochemical workstation. Amperometry i–t curves are shown in Fig. 10d. Precisely, the ASSMFSC was first placed at room temperature to maintain a stable current signal and then installed on the volunteer's forehead, arm and index finger. Each monitoring time was controlled for about 15 s, and then it was released. After cooling at room temperature for 30 s, we put it to the area again for monitoring. Fig. 10e shows that ASSMFSC can capture the current fluctuation signals on different body temperature surfaces. Consequently, ASSMFSC has the potential to assess the body's thermoregulatory capabilities and assist patients in self-diagnosis. It should be noted that in research studies on the temperature monitoring performance of the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC, we pay more attention to the design concept of achieving a multifunctional response-intelligent energy storage system. In actual use, ASSMFSC can reasonably plan the overall structure to divide the thermal response unit and energy storage area, or be connected in series with another ASSMFSC for a power supply.

Conclusion

In summary, we show a new kind of solar and thermally sensing all-solid-state microfiber supercapacitor (ASSMFSC) with outstanding charge storage performance, intelligent energy administration and proper environmental responsiveness. We utilize the macromolecular interaction and intercalation effect of graphene and cellulose nanofibrils to construct GE/CNFs core microfibers. Afterwards, core-sheath GE/CNFs@PANI microfibers were formed by the in situ depositing of highly oriented polyaniline nanorod arrays on the surface of the GE/CNFs core microfibers. The multicomponent hierarchical synergistic design fully exploits the unique attributes and synergistic interaction of each component to endow the well-designed CSMFE with not only highly capacitive and mechanically robust properties, but also excellent thermal sensitivity and photothermal conversion function. This enabled the valid modulation of redox reactions, interface performance, or electronic/ionic transfer in ASSMFSC toward complex external stimulations and building interrelated working responses. Hence, based on these principles, the GE/CNFs@PANI ASSMFSC can be used as a solar-responsive energy storage system with self-regulating capacitance, and a temperature sensor with fast response capability and accuracy for human body temperature monitoring. In addition, the fabricated ASSMFSC presents a total volumetric (vs. the whole device) capacitance of 85.8 mF cm−3, excellent cycle stability, and volumetric energy density of 11.9 mW h cm−3. Our recent research study provides a valuable design solution for the multi-functional energy storage systems, which provides a positive impetus for the development of a new generation of energy-related systems.
