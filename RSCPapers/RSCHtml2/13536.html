Abstract

Variation in the composition of the A, B, and B-sites to develop novel ABBX6 halide double perovskites is an important topic in areas as diverse as materials- and nano-science, photovoltaics, photocatalysis, device technologies and bandgap engineering. In this study, we examined the geometrical, dynamical, electronic, and optical properties of the rhodium-based halide series A2AgRhBr6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na, Li) with double perovskite stoichiometry using density functional theory with the meta-GGA functional SCAN-rVV10. Geometry-based stability analysis suggested the formation of the first two members of the series as perovskites and the remaining three as distorted perovskites, but all are direct bandgap materials displaying electronic transitions at the high-symmetry X-point. Phonon dispersion calculations suggested that Cs2AgRhBr6 is dynamically stable, whereas all other systems of the series are dynamically unstable, as is often the case for single halide perovskites at the high symmetry points of the first Brilliouin zone. The real and positive nature of the three independent elastic constants, used to examine the Born criterion, and the six eigenvalues of the elastic stiffness matrix, indicated that all the A2AgRhBr6 compounds are mechanically stable. While the SCAN-rVV10 bandgaps of these systems were underestimated compared to those evaluated using the HSE06, PBE0, G0W0 and GW0 methods, the density functional perturbation theory based dielectric function spectra, as well as the refractive index and absorption coefficient spectra, feature broad peaks covering the infrared and visible regions with the onset of optical (direct) absorption occurring near SCAN-rVV10 bandgap energies between 0.74 and 0.77 eV. A high frequency dielectric constant of 7.38 to 8.11, a refractive index of 2.70 to 2.85, and an absorption coefficient in the range of 5.7 1056.3 105 were calculated for A2AgRhBr6 in the region 0.05.0 eV, suggesting possible application of these systems in optoelectronics.

1. Introduction

Over the last decade, halide double perovskites have been rediscovered as photovoltaic,1 photocatalytic,2 X-ray detector,3,4 and photo-luminescent semiconducting materials.57 They are represented by the chemical formula A2BBX6 (X = Cl, Br, I). The cation at the A-site is a spacer or guest, responsible for the electrical neutrality of the perovskite structure, and is either organic (such as MA = methylammonium) or inorganic (alkali ions). The B- and B-cations are usually main group or transition metal cations involved in building the 2D or 3D inorganic framework of the crystal lattice. The former is usually found in the +1 oxidation state (e.g., Cu+, Na+, Cs+ and Ag+) and the latter in the +3 oxidation state (In3+, Cr3+, and Bi3+).19 Examples of halide double perovskites whose 3D structures have been reported include A2AgInX6 (A = Cs, Rb, Na; X = Cl, Br, I),10,11 A2AgBiX6 (A = Cs, Rb, Na; X = Cl, Br, I),1114 and A2NaInX6 (A = Cs, Rb; X = Cl, Br).1520

Although only a handful of halogenated double perovskites have been characterized as photovoltaic, photocatalytic, and luminescent materials,120 many of them are inefficient converters of light in the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum for viable application in solar cells,21 and the quality of the synthesized samples are not as good as that of single Pb-based perovskites.9 For example, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of Cs2AgBiCl6 and Cs2AgBiBr6 was reported to be <3%,1 which is very small compared to that certified for the best light absorber halide perovskite, MAPbI3 (PCE 25.2%).22,23 The high PCE of MAPbI3 was achieved only in 2019,23 and was a significant improvement over the value of 3.8% reported ten years ago.24 Clearly, continuous experimental developments and refinements played a role in achieving such a milestone.25

One of the principal reasons why lead-based perovskites cannot be widely used as a sustainable source of converting light into electric energy is that they are not environmentally friendly, toxic and because they deteriorate, especially when exposed to heat, visible and UV light, applied electric fields, water vapor pressure, mechanical pressure, and even ambient air.2628 This may not always apply to main group and transition metal based A2BBX6 halide double perovskites given that many of them have been shown to be non-toxic, and to be environmentally and thermodynamically stable.29,30 They are therefore deserving of exploration, both theoretical and experimental, in anticipation of discovering efficient materials for application in optoelectronics.31,32

Interest in all-inorganic halide single and double perovskites as luminescent and spintronic materials has grown significantly because of their inherently high absorption coefficients, leading to broader applications.110,33,34 As such, this density functional theory study investigates the lattice properties, electronic, elastic, phonon and optical properties of the A2BBBr6 series with double perovskite composition, where A = Cs+, Rb+, K+, Na+, and Li+; B = Ag+; and B = Rh3+. The first aim of this study was to geometrically isolate stable perovskites from non-perovskites in the series using traditional3537 and newly38 proposed tolerance factors. Next, we examined phonon dispersion and density of states and elastic properties to determine whether the A2BBBr6 compounds identified as perovskites and non-perovskites are dynamically and mechanically stable.39 We also examined the dielectric and optical constants40 to determine the extent to which the A-site cation substitution by lighter alkali ions affects these properties. We investigated the effect of the A-site cations responsible in setting orbital states associated with the upper and lower valence and conduction bands, and demonstrate the importance of their involvement in determining the band structure and optical absorption edges of A2AgRhBr6. Finally, we examined the nature of the mobility of charge carriers associated with the valence and conduction band edges41,42 and provide our views on whether the systems investigated could find application in optoelectronics.4144

2. Computational methods

The meta-GGA SCAN (Strongly Constrained and Appropriately Normed45,46) functional in combination with the rVV10 van der Waals functional was used for the energy-minimization of the unit-cell lattice constants, volume and ionic positions of each A2AgRhBr6. The combination of SCAN with rVV10 has been shown to model well the properties of metallic, insulating and semiconducting systems, as it accounts for the non-local correlation part necessary for the description of long- and short-range dispersion effects.45,46 SCAN has been used to study the electronic structure and properties of several systems,4550 including halide perovskites,51 and halide double perovskites,5 and in particular is suitable for the study of the atomic structure of hybrid perovskite materials.5,51

Together with SCAN-rVV10, the -centered k-point mesh 8 8 8 for sampling the first Brillouin zone, the Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) method52 and a plane-wave basis set at an energy cut-off of 520 eV were used. The tolerance in the total energy for relaxation of the electronic degrees of freedom was set to 108 eV per cell, and the residual HellmannFeynman forces per ion was less than 0.006eV 1. Unless otherwise stated, spin polarization was considered given that an Rh3+ ion in an octahedral environment in the perovskite lattice is assumed to have a low spin 4d6 (t62ge0g) electron configuration.53 All calculations were performed with the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP).54,55

The linear tetrahedron method with Blchl corrections56 was used for the calculation of the density of states (DOS) of A2AgRhBr6. The electronic band dispersion was calculated using a standard self-consistent (SC) procedure, followed by a non-SC calculation.57 The same dense k-point mesh as above was used for sampling the Brillouin zone, and the band levels were those recommended for the Fmm space group.58 Post processing Linux-based software Pyband57 and Sumo58 were used for analysis and plotting the DOS and band structures.

VASP5.4 lacks implementation of the meta-GGA (SCAN-rVV10) functional to model the linear response properties.59 To this end, the Density Functional Theory Perturbation Theory (DFPT) method,60 in conjunction with the (PBE) PAW potentials, often used for this type of calculation,43,6164 was adopted. The energy and force cut-offs and the size of the k-point mesh were maintained (see above). The relaxed SCAN-rVV10 geometries of A2AgRhBr6 served as inputs. The number of empty conduction band states found in the single point SCF calculations to generate the wavefuntions, were doubled. The number of frequency grid points was set to 2000, as recommended.65 Similar calculations were performed with the PBEsol functional, in conjunction with DFPT, to see what effect this combination has on the nature of the dielectric function.

The dynamical stability of the A2AgRhBr6 lattice was examined using the computed phonon band structures and phonon density of states spectra.39,6670 These calculations were performed using the Phonopy code.67 The DFPT method was used to calculate force constants in reciprocal space, all within the harmonic approximation, adopting a linear response calculation to obtain the force constant matrix. We used a -center scheme for k-point sampling integrations.43 An energy-cutoff of 520 eV for plane wave basis set and a total energy cut-off of 108 eV were chosen. In addition, we have adopted the finite difference method (FDM),70,71 together with SCAN-rVV10, to compute the same properties, and to verify whether the DFPT results are always reliable. In the latter case, the dynamical matrix was calculated by forcing a finite displacement on each atom and evaluating the forces.

The mechanical stability,39 elastic anisotropy,72 and soft character73 of A2AgRhBr6 were examined using the results of the elastic constants and the six eigenvalues of the 6 6 stiffness elastic matrix Cij of A2AgRhBr6, as well as of the various elastic moduli derived using them. These were calculated within the harmonic approximation. The finite difference approach was used to determine the second derivatives (Hessian matrix and phonon frequencies). A 2 2 2 k-point mesh was used, together with the tight convergence criteria mentioned above. The SCAN-VV10 relaxed geometries of A2AgRhBr6 were used.

3.2 Perovskite stability

The geometrical stability of ABX3 (X = Cl, Br, I) perovskites7577 and ABO3 perovskites37,38 has been extensively investigated using a combination of the octahedral factor , and the Goldschmidt tolerance factor t. These factors are determined using the ionic radii r of A, B and X, and empirical relationships for and t are sought, where = rB/rX and , respectively. This approach has also been applied to halide double perovskites78 and vacancy-ordered double perovskites.79 For instance, the crystal structures of 170 known A2BX6 (X = I, Br, Cl, F) double halide perovskites were recently analyzed to determine the applicability of and t.79 It was found that t is indeed applicable for double perovskites in a manner analogous to its applicability to ABX3 perovskites.44 It is therefore an important tool in designing new hybrid organicinorganic double perovskites.

The relation governing t is applicable to A2BBX6 halide double perovskites, but with rB (rB + rB)/2. The ionic radii of A, B and X may be evaluated theoretically using first-principles methods; however, following convention,7579 we used Shannon's radii for ions that have been empirically derived from experimentally reported crystal structures.80 Specifically, we used the ionic radii of octahedral Ag+, Rh3+ and Br ions, and dodecahedral A+ ions (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na) (Table 2). Since the ionic radius of dodecahedral Li+ is unknown, we used its octahedral radius. 3.3 Phonon band structure and density of states

Phonon band structure and phonon density of states are important features of any solid state material since they are associated with the nature of phonon vibrational modes, providing insight into a material's lattice and phonon stability, ionic transport, and the recombination and scattering of charge carriers.9094 Soft phonon modes are generally associated with imaginary frequencies.95 These modes often appear in the low frequency regime of the vibrational phonon spectra, caused by lattice or phonon instability.96

To provide further insight into the stability of the A2AgRhBr6 lattice, the phonon dispersion was evaluated using the dynamic matrix that was constructed by means of the Hessian matrix as the second derivatives of the total energy with respect to the atomic displacements.90,95 The Hessian matrix was calculated in reciprocal space using the DFPT method with Phonopy,67 as well as by using the FDM71 together with the SCAN-rVV10 functional. DFPT cannot be coupled to SCAN-rVV10 for such a calculation since the combination of meta-GGA with linear response routines is not supported by VASP. Although large supercells are often recommended for such calculations,97 we selected the primitive cell (10 atoms) and its 2 2 2 extension (80 atoms), as well as the 2 2 2 extension of the conventional unit-cell (320 atoms). The k-meshes of 4 4 4, 2 2 2 and 1 1 1 were used for the corresponding cells, respectively.

Fig. 2ac compares the phonon band structure, together with the phonon (total) density of states, of Cs2AgRhBr6 obtained with DFPT and FDM. It is clear that the three acoustic modes (two transverse and one longitudinal) are stable at the center of the zone boundary (-point) and are somewhat stiffened across the zone boundary, regardless of the nature of the size of the cell and computational method used. One of the optical modes is relatively shallow at the center of the zone boundary, which is prominent in the DFPT phonon dispersion shown in Fig. 2c.

The result above is not fully in accord with that obtained when the conventional cell and its 2 2 2 supercells were used (see Fig. S2 of the ESI), suggesting that the unstable phonon at the -point is an artefact of the computational procedure, as well as of the nature of the cell used. In fact, DFPT suggested a few optical modes, which are long wave phonons, associated with negative imaginary frequencies, thus explaining why several curves appear below the 0.0 THz frequency line (Fig. S2b and c, ESI).

As the number of atoms N in the cell increases, the phonon spectra became denser since there is an increase in the number of the phonon modes following the 3N rule (Fig. 2vs. Fig. S2, ESI). (The imaginary modes occurred at frequencies of 7.6 and 15.0 cm1 when the conventional cell was used and at frequencies of 7.3, 10.7 and 14.9 cm1 when 2 2 2 extension of the conventional cell was used.) The presence of these modes shows up only at the center of the Brilliouin zone. We have also symmetrized the force constants to see whether the imaginary modes in the GGA dispersion are an artifact, but this did not provide any new physical insight. Such negative (soft) modes have been seen in ferroelectric crystals,91 suggesting that Cs2AgRhBr6 will experience -point instability. Similar results were found for the other A2AgRhBr6 (A = Rb, K, Na, Li) structures, regardless of the nature of computational approaches adopted (DFPT or FDM); this means that all these latter members of the series are likely to be dynamically unstable. Fig. S3 of the ESI provides a similar phonon instability feature, suggesting the phonons are unstable across the paths L X and L X W for Rb2AgRhBr6 and K2AgRhBr6, respectively.

Since the effect of anharmonic contributions, together with external temperature and pressure, on the soft modes were not considered in this study, it is difficult to speculate what role these have on the stability of the observed phonon modes. The presence of soft modes at the R- and -point is known for many halide perovskites, including, for example, CsPbI3,67,69 CsPbCl3,98 MAPbI3,99 MAPbBr3,100 and FAPbI3 (FA+ = formamidinium).101

For CsPbI3, the imaginary mode was associated with one of the triply degenerate acoustic modes at the -point, with a phonon frequency as large as 15 cm1. Further instabilities occurred at the high symmetry R- and M-points (around 20 cm1), and were due to the optical modes that indicate the presence of strong anharmonic effects and a very flat energy profile around equilibrium.69 Brivio and coworkers,70 and others,102 have shown that such instabilities are a common feature of the perovskite structure and represent antiferroelectric distortions linked to rotations and tilting of the octahedra in neighboring unit cells. Nevertheless, plots of the thermal properties (Helmholtz free energy F, entropy S, and heat capacity at constant volume CV) as a function of temperature for Cs2AgRhBr6 (Fig. S4, ESI) show that F decreases monotonically as temperature increases, unlike S and CV, as anticipated from classical thermodynamics.103106

Although the A-site species does not play a direct role in determining the VBM and CBM, as observed for MAPbI3,107 the replacement of Cs+ in Cs2AgRhBr6 by the lighter members of the alkali series shrinks the A2AgRhBr6 lattice. This does have an impact on the nature of the orbital character of the VBM and CBM. In particular, the presence of the smaller sized A-site cations in A2AgRhBr6 causes an increase in the 4d-type (Rh) contribution to the VBM from 71.6% in Cs2AgRhBr6 to 71.0% in Li2AgRhBr6. The 4p-type (Br) contribution is virtually unaffected (see Table 3). A similar decreasing feature is noticeable with the Rh(4d), as well as with the Ag(5s), states responsible for the CBM, but the nature of the Br(4p)-orbital states contributing to the band is virtually affected. An exception is notable for Na2AgRhBr6 and Li2AgRhBr6, and is due to plane-wave potential.

The results above suggest that both the CBM and VBM of A2AgRhBr6 are an admixture of p- and d-type orbital energy states, but the dominance of dd type electronic transitions between the VBM and CBM is expected to play a role. This is in contrast to what has been reported for MAPbX3 halide perovskites, in which CBM and VBM located at the R-point were found to be substantially of Pb(p) and Pb(s) + X(p) character, respectively.108 For the GaAs solar cell absorber, these had Ga(s) + As(s) and As(p) character, respectively; and for Si, the orbital origins of the corresponding bands were Si(s + p) and Si(p), respectively.108

3.5 Nature of charge carrier mobility

It is evident from Fig. 4b and d that the top of the valence band is broader and flatter than the bottom of the conduction band. It is therefore expected that the mobility of electrons associated with the conduction band edge should be faster than those of the holes at the valence band edge. To prove this hypothesis, we have calculated the effective masses of the electrons and holes by parabolic fitting of the band edges. The basic relationship between the effective mass (m*) of charge carriers, and the curvature of the band edges (2E(k)/k2) was used.42,109 This is given by mh* = 2(2E(k)/k2)1 and me* = 2(2E(k)/k2)1, where E(k) is the energy of the conduction/valence band edge associated with electrons/holes, k is the wave vector, is Plank's constant and mh*/me* is the effective mass of electrons/holes.

The effective masses of the charge carriers are summarized in Table 4; they are evaluated along two principal directions at the X point. The VBM is two-fold degenerate. One of them has a massive hole than the other, especially that along the X W path (because of the significantly fatter nature of the band along this path). The data in Table 4 refer to the band with lighter mh* along both the paths, comprising of spin-up holes. Each is associated with two principal directions. The mh* is lighter along the X direction compared to that along the X W direction. For example, in Cs2AgRhBr6, one of these bands has mh* close to 2.11 m0 and 1.44 m0 along the X and X W directions, respectively, where m0 is the electron rest mass. For the other, mh* = 2.09 m0 and 17.33 m0 along the same directions, respectively. This feature persists regardless of the nature of the A-site cation in A2AgRhBr6, but the difference in the mh* values along the two directions reduces as one passes from Cs2AgRhBr6 down the series, except for Li2AgRhBr6.

4. Conclusion

In this study we used various theoretical approaches to examine the perovskite stability, geometries, electronic and optical properties of the series A2AgRhBr6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na, Li) and showed that whereas the first two members of the series have a stable perovskite structure, the latter three are best regarded as distorted perovskites. This is emerged from the newly proposed tolerance factor, even though the joint analysis of octahedral and Goldschmidt tolerance factors suggested that A2AgRhBr6 (A = Cs, Rb, K, Na) are stable perovskites. The global instability index, on the other hand, recognized all the systems as perovskites, which means that it may not always be suitable for the analysis of the formability of perovskites.

We have shown that all the five A2AgRhBr6 systems display very similar light-absorption properties in the infrared-to-visible region. Specifically, we showed that the optical constants, viz. the refractive index, absorption coefficient, and optical conductivity would increase marginally when the cation Cs in Cs2AgRhBr6 is replaced by lighter alkali atoms; thus the A-site substitution by the lighter alkali atom does not play a significant role in modifying the geometrical, electronic and optical properties of these systems. Using the phonon band dispersion and DOS, we showed that A2AgRhBr6 (A = Rb, K, Na, Li) were dynamically unstable, mechanically stable and ductile. Such phonon instabilities are by far the most common features of single halide perovskites in the cubic phase, as previously observed for cubic MAPbX3 (X = Cl, Br, I) light absorbers. Taking into account the properties such as the geometrical stability, the direct nature of the bandgap, the small effective masses of charge carriers, and the high absorption coefficient, we conclude that A2AgRhBr6 (A = Cs, Rb) are two possible candidate species for synthesis and optoelectronic applications. Although Cs2AgRhBr6 was found to be dynamically stable, the phonon instability observed for especially for Rb2AgRhBr6 and K2AgRhBr6 shouldnt prevent their synthesis, given that this was possible for dynamically unstable single perovskites MAPbX3 (X = Cl, Br, I).
