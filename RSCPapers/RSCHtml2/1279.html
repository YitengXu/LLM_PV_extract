Abstract

Development of high-performance and highly selective NIR photodetectors (PDs) using wide band gap semiconductors is a significant field of research in the present scenario. Herein, cost effective and easy fabrication of NIR PDs is demonstrated by employing the thin films of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS (x = 0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04 and 0.05) colloidal quantum dots (CQDs). The influence of doping on the transport behaviour of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD films was investigated by device performance under laser light illumination at various wavelengths ranging from 405 nm to 782 nm. An improvement in the photoresponsivity of the material (CdS) beyond its intrinsic absorption spectral range due to the incorporation of the dopant is noticed. In spite of the cost effective and easy fabrication process without the requirement of any rigorous synthesis procedure, the devices presented here demonstrate competitive figures of merit to those that are designed with complex structures and tedious procedures. The performance parameters viz response time, responsivity, photosensitivity, quantum efficiency, and specific detectivity were improved as a consequence of Mg doping and attained a maximum value of 110 ms, âˆ¼26.9 A Wâˆ’1, âˆ¼4.7 Ã— 104%, âˆ¼41.23% and âˆ¼3.45 Ã— 1013 Jones, respectively. Besides, the sensing range of the PDs can be tuned from visible (650 nm) to NIR (782 nm) with a huge improvement in selectivity by incorporation of Mg.

Introduction

Presently, nanostructured materials are widely studied due to their distinctive properties at the nanoscale.1â€“17 Among the functional materials, semiconductor nanostructures (SNSs) are regarded as potential candidates for applications in various fields including electronic and optoelectronic devices.1,2 The extra attention attracted by them is due to their large surface to volume ratio and quantum confinement effect, which not only just modify the bandgap, density of states and adsorption effect but also prolong the photo-carrier life time over the corresponding bulk counterpart. Besides, colloidal quantum dots (CQDs) are compatible and printable into flexible substrates with the advantages of low costs and light weight and have prospective applications in touch screens and displays, biomedical sensors, artificial skins, solar cells, flexible displays, etc.3 One of the most studied metal chalcogenide SNSs is cadmium sulfide (CdS) that exhibits a direct bulk bandgap (âˆ¼2.42 eV at room temperature (RT)) with a wide range of applications including solar cells, light emitting diodes, lasers, photodetectors (PDs), gas sensors, temperature sensors, and bioimaging.8â€“17 CdS belongs to the category of wide band gap SNS materials and usually shows photoresponse for visible and ultra violet (UV) radiations,18â€“25 whereas for the sensing of near infrared (NIR) or infrared (IR) radiations relatively narrow band gap SNS materials like HgTe, HgSe, PbS and PbSe are preferred and most widely studied.26â€“30 Though the narrow band gap SNS materials possess high photosensing performance parameters, they usually suffer from lower environmental stability as compared to large band gap QDs (e.g., CdS and CdSe) that restricts their utility for long duration. On the other hand, the large band gap SNSs as an ideal case in defect free form are inappropriate for NIR PD applications due to their inability to absorb NIR photons of lower energy. However, the photoresponse of CdS can be possibly tuned from the visible to NIR region using a well-known and widely used approach of defect engineering and elemental doping. In semiconductor nanotechnology, elemental doping is widely used to intentionally introduce impurities in the host material to modulate the electrical, optical and other properties. For example, photosensing studies were performed on Cl doped CdS nanoribbons (NRs) by Wu et al. They observed an enhancement in the conductivity with an increase in dopant concentration and were able to tune the conductivity of the CdS NRs by about five orders of magnitude from 10âˆ’9 Î© cmâˆ’1 (undoped CdS) to 10âˆ’4 Î© cmâˆ’1 for the highest Cl doped sensor under 470 nm illumination.31 P. Maity and group had studied the photosensing properties of CdS:Co CQDs for NIR. They noticed an increase in dark conductivities of the CdS:Co thin films with increasing doping concentration. In addition to the dark current, the photosensitivities of these CdS:Co CQD thin films increase gradually with doping and a significant enhancement was also observed in the ZnO/CdS:Co CQD heterojunction films. They have observed the highest detectivity for the 8% doped CdS:Co heterojunction PD with a detectivity of 3.1 Ã— 1011 Jones under the illumination of light of 820 nm wavelength and an external bias voltage of 5.0 V.1 Ma et al. illustrated a drastic drop in the resistivity of In-doped CdS nanobelts (âˆ¼3.27 Î© cm) as compared to undoped CdS nanobelts (104 Î© cm).32 NIR sensing was also carried out by Amiri et al. in polypyrrole (PPy) and PPy/CdS QD PDs at 805 nm under the illumination of different light intensities.33 The various photoconductive parameters such as responsivity, external quantum efficiency, response time and stability of Sn doped CdS individual nanowires (NWs) were analyzed by Zhou et al. to evaluate the advantage of doped NWs and the feasibility for PD application. They had observed a broader range of spectral sensing by Sn doped CdS in comparison to CdS NWs, which is beneficial for extending the application range of PDs.34 N. I. Najm et al. investigated the photosensing properties of Ag doped CdS thin films and observed the highest responsivity under visible light for 5% Ag doped CdS thin films.35 The influence of Mg content on ZnMgS based Schottky barrier PDs was studied by Lu and co-workers. They had shown a significant increase in the concentration of non-radiative deep levels upon increasing the Mg content, thus leading to a small decrease in the responsivity and quantum efficiency of the detector.36

The literature suggests that an appropriate doping in CdS nanostructures is much desired to tailor the opto-electrical properties and enhance their response up to the NIR region1 with good sensitivity and selectivity which is the stimulus for our research. Magnesium is one of the appropriate alkaline earth metals to dope CdS due to the fact that the radius of the Mg2+ ion (0.72 nm) is close to that of Cd2+ (0.97 nm). Previously, we have successfully tuned the band gap of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS QDs from 2.58 eV to 2.66 eV by varying the x values from 0 to 5 and observed a nanotwin microstructure in the QDs.37 These QDs are highly emissive and in the present work, the same QDs are used for deposition of CQD thin films by a low cost and efficient spin coating method and the effects of Mg doping on the structural, optical and electrical properties of CdS are investigated.

In the present work PDs are developed using a metalâ€“semiconductorâ€“metal (MSM) configuration. Further, the samples were studied as PDs by irradiation with light of different wavelengths from visible to NIR. The characteristics and device merits have been evaluated for different sensors.

Experimental

Synthesis of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS (x = 0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04 and 0.05) QDs

The synthesis of Mg doped CdS QDs with systematically varying the concentration of Mg (0% to 5%) has been carried out at RT by the chemical co-precipitation method and discussed elsewhere in detail.37 Briefly, the two-step synthesis process involves the preparation of fresh, homogeneous and clear solutions of 0.1 M Cd(NO3)2, 0.5 M Na2S, and MgNO3 (0.001 M for 1% to 0.005 M for 5% Mg doping) in deionized water by stirring magnetically for 1 h as the first step. Secondly, the freshly prepared solution of MgNO3 (0.0 M to 0.005 M) was mixed into Cd(NO3)2 solution to achieve a desired concentration dopant viz. 0% to 5% of Mg during continuous stirring for further 1 h followed by the dropwise injection of Na2S solution and appropriate amount of ethylenediamine into each aqueous solution of mixture under constant stirring. The colloidal solutions were left stirring for further 1 h. Finally, yellow precipitates with slightly different shades were obtained, indicating the formation of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS QDs. These precipitates were washed 3â€“4 times and dried at 150 Â°C. The dried samples were crushed to obtain a fine powder of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS QDs.

Fabrication and characterization of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD thin film PDs

The Cd1âˆ’xMgxS (x = 0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04 and 0.05) CQD thin films were then deposited on a p-type Si substrate using DMF as the solvent by spin coating at a rotating speed of 2500 rpm for 30 s. Prior to the deposition, the substrate was cleaned ultrasonically by keeping it for 10 min in acetone followed by ethanol and then in DI water. After each coating, the as deposited films were dried at 200 Â°C on a hot plate.

The morphology of 3% Mg doped CdS QDs was studied to confirm the shape, size and structural phase using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) operating at a voltage of 300 kV using a TITAN G2 60-300 equipped with a high coherence, high brightness, field emission electron gun (X-FEG), a Cs-image corrector and an electron gun monochromator. The crystal structure and structural properties of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD films were studied with a Rigaku Miniflex X-ray diffractometer with Cu-KÎ± line (Î» = 1.54 Ã…). Absorption spectra of the films deposited on glass substrates were recorded using UVâ€“Vis absorption spectroscopy (UVâ€“Vis Shimadzu 2600). A Keithley 2480-semiconductor characterization system was used to study the conductivity of the as prepared thin films. Further, to test the photosensing properties of Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD films, photosensing devices (S0, S1, S2, S3, S4 and S5 corresponding to x = 0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04 and 0.05 respectively) were fabricated by making a metalâ€“semiconductorâ€“metal (Mâ€“Sâ€“M) structure in symmetric geometry using high purity silver paste as designed earlier.18,19 The photosensing characteristics are tested under illumination with different lasers of wavelengths 405 nm, 532 nm, 635 nm, 650 nm and 782 nm and a power of 1 mW at different bias voltages.

Results and discussion

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

TEM is performed to investigate the shape, size, size distribution, defects and defect density in Mg doped CdS QDs. The sample for the TEM measurement was prepared by drop-casting the colloidal solution on carbon-coated TEM grids.

The TEM micrograph in Fig. 1(a) depicts isolated, homogeneous, and mono-dispersed QDs of 3% Mg doped CdS. The size distribution of the said sample is also shown in the inset of the figure. The estimated particle size for 3% Mg doped CdS QDs is 3.9 nm with particle distribution in the range of 2.3 nmâ€“9.1 nm. The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern is taken to confirm the crystalline structure of QDs. The inset of Fig. 1 shows the SAED pattern of the Mg doped CdS QDs. The SAED pattern consists of concentric broad rings corresponding to (1 1 1), (2 2 0) and (3 1 1) diffraction planes and thus identified and confirmed the cubic structure of CdS. HRTEM was used to examine the microstructure of the QDs and is shown in Fig. 1(b). The different spots located in Fig. 1(b) correspond to the (111), (220) and (200) planes of CdS with inter-planar spacings of 0.3336 nm, 0.224 nm and 0.371 nm respectively. These planes represent the cubic phase of the CdS as the symmetry favours the cubic structure for the spherical nucleus during the nucleation process. Further, the CdS QDs possess single twin boundary highlighted in Fig. 1(b) and illustrated by white colour. The existence of the twin boundary affects the electrical properties of the materials due to the application of voltage across the grain boundary as they form both reverse and forward biased junctions. Besides, defects like twin boundaries also modify the optical properties of the materials.

UV-Vis absorption spectroscopy

Absorbance of the Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD thin films is recorded in the range 350 nm to 1000 nm. It is obvious from absorption spectra that Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD thin films show good absorbance in the visible and NIR region of the electromagnetic spectrum of light. The development of the absorption peak in the NIR region with an increase in concentration of Mg ions can be clearly seen in contrast to just a hump in the case of the CdS thin film. Moreover, absorbance in the NIR region with Mg ion concentration is not linear. It increases up to 2% of Mg concentration and then decreases up to 4% of Mg concentration. Further, an increase in Mg concentration (5%) results in an increase in absorbance of the films. The generation of the NIR peak in Mg doped CdS may be due to the construction of a doping band (DB) in between the conduction and valence band of CdS as in the case of Co doped CdS which is possibly located in the vicinity of the valence band.1

As far as the case of the CdS CQD thin film is concerned, there are several reports in the literature that suggest the existence of NIR absorption in solution processed films due to the defects present. For instance, Sharma et al. reported the prominence of the peak at 760 nm by growing Au nanoislands over CdS due to the stimulated emission of charge carriers which get trapped in trap states present near the conduction band of the CdS film.38 In a nutshell, it can be inferred that even being a wide band gap semiconductor, CdS can exhibit NIR absorption which can be further enhanced and tuned by an appropriate doping element and its concentration in solution processed CQD thin films.

Generally, the wide band gap semiconductors are limited for NIR PDs due to their intrinsic absorption in the UV or visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum. However, in recent years, great efforts have been made by various researchers to achieve NIR sensing using wide band gap photosensitive materials. For example, Chandra et al. have used a heterojunction of p-type vertically aligned silicon nanowires and n-type CdS nanoparticles for NIR detection. Their PD was capable of achieving a maximum Iph/Id ratio, R, and D values of âˆ¼6.7, 821 mA Wâˆ’1 and 1.21 Ã— 1012 Jones respectively for an NIR source of 900 nm wavelength and âˆ¼8 mW cmâˆ’2 power.39 Mahapatra et al. have exploited a simple Al/SnO2/Al photoconductive device for broad band spectral detection from UV to NIR which showed an R value of 0.19 mA Wâˆ’1 under 800 nm light.40 Zheng et al. have developed a PD using ZnO/PbS QD heterostructures for detection of multiple spectral regions (UV-Vis.-NIR) and recorded an R value, D value, and response time of 0.011 A Wâˆ’1, 4.2 Ã— 107 Jones and 500 ms respectively under the exposure to 900 nm radiation of power âˆ¼2.55 mW cmâˆ’2.41 The six times enhancement in the NIR (850 nm) photoresponsivity of polypyrrole (PPy) by incorporation of CdS QDs (22 ppm) was noticed by Amiri and Alizadeh.33 Their PPy/CdS QD (22 ppm) PD illustrated a high overall performance with a R value, on/off ratio, EQE and D value of 3.8 mA Wâˆ’1, 120, 560%, and 2.1 Ã— 1016 Jones respectively under the illumination at 850 nm wavelength with a power of 15 mW at a bias voltage of 10 V. Modeling on account of three mechanisms (diffusion, generationâ€“recombination, and trap assisted tunnelling in dark current contribution) was proposed by Zeiri et al. to explore the electrical and optical characteristics of CdS/ZnSe based NIR PDs. Besides, the simple architecture and cost effective approach are the additional gems for the present device.

Conclusions

Highly selective and high performance NIR PDs are devised using Cd1âˆ’xMgxS CQD thin films. The PDs show multispectral sensing features in bare CdS and at low concentration of Mg. However, an improvement in selectivity for NIR is noticed with increasing content of Mg up to 4%. The present study illustrates the fabrication of PDs with high FOMs using a simple and cost-effective technique. The tremendous photocurrent reproducibility, selectivity and stability revealed that the fabricated PDs are remarkable candidates for application in NIR light detection.
