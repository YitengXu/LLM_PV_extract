Abstract

Periodic nanostructures, a group of nanomaterials consisting of single or multiple nano units/components periodically arranged into ordered patterns (e.g., vertical and lateral superlattices), have attracted tremendous attention in recent years due to their extraordinary physical and chemical properties that offer a huge potential for a multitude of applications in energy conversion, electronic and optoelectronic applications. Recent advances in the preparation strategies of periodic nanostructures, including self-assembly, epitaxy, and exfoliation, have paved the way to rationally modulate their ferroelectricity, superconductivity, band gap and many other physical and chemical properties. For example, the recent discovery of superconductivity observed in magic-angle graphene superlattices has sparked intensive studies in new ways, creating superlattices in twisted 2D materials. Recent development in the various state-of-the-art preparations of periodic nanostructures has created many new ideas and findings, warranting a timely review. In this review, we discuss the current advances of periodic nanostructures, including their preparation strategies, property modulations and various applications.

1. Introduction

The progress of human civilization is in line with the development of materials. From the Stone Age to Bronze and from Bronze to Iron, the application of these materials brought a revolution to the farming tools, accelerating the efficiency of agricultural production. The application of rubber to sealing steam and metals for electricity are two key inventions during the first and second industrial revolutions, respectively. Decades ago, the development of semiconducting materials and their advances in electronic and optoelectronic device technologies significantly influenced modern life. Nowadays, it is the era of nanotechnology, and various nanomaterials have been developed and applied to different areas in catalysis,14 biotechnology,57 energy conversion,810 energy storage,8,9,11,12etc.,13,14 regardless of their composition and morphology.1518 Among these nanomaterials, periodic nanostructures (PNSs), including notable superlattices, have attracted wide attention from the scientific community due to their periodic ordering that offers unique properties for various applications.

Periodic structures in general consist of several or more regularly repeating identical arrays of nano, micro, or macroscale entities with a geometric shape in an orderly patterned manner. Often, these entities organize along one or several independent directions, and generate short- or long-range ordered assemblies with different configurations and dimensionalities on all scales. In recent years, there has been a growing research interest in the rational design and fabrication of periodic nanomaterials, owing to their potential in a wide range of properties, and further applications by the virtue of periodic orderings at the nanoscale.1921 In contrast to micro- or macro-scale periodicity, nano-scale periodicity strongly influences the electronic, optical and other properties of the material,2226 and offers distinct collective characteristics that may be very different from those of their bulk counterparts.2729 Especially, the discovery of the superconducting graphene bilayer moir superlattice has brought PNSs to a new level.30 Benefitting from the advantages of these properties, the applications of PNSs in energy conversion,20 and in photoelectronic and electronic devices19,31,32 have been intensively studied.

The concept of PNSs covers a wide range of materials, such as traditional IIIV group superlattices,33,34 metal oxide superlattices,24,35 periodic layered materials,28,29 and nanoparticle based periodic arrays.36,37 A number of review papers have discussed some of these PNSs according to their dimensions and compositions, such as reviews on oxide superlattices,38 chemical synthesized 2D superlattices,19 van der Waals 2D material-based superlattices,21 and 2D layered PNSs.20 However, there is still a lack of an overview of PNSs with integrating their preparation strategies, properties, and applications.

Therefore, in this review, we will summarize the advances in periodic nanostructured materials, including their preparation methods, properties, and applications. Herein, we will begin the review by discussing different preparation strategies, including vapor phase preparation, solution phase preparation and other complex preparation methods, which provide the platform for further in-depth studies. Then, the properties of the periodic nanostructured materials, ranging from ferroelectricity, ferromagnetism, piezoelectricity and superconductivity to the electronic, optoelectronic, and optical properties, will be discussed. Finally, we will summarize their applications in energy conversion and (opto)electronic devices, and then present the challenges and opportunities of PNSs for future study.

2.1. Vapor phase preparation

Vapor phase preparation is widely used to modify the substrate surface, and is also one of the most important methods used to synthesize PNSs. The vapor phase preparation can be divided into chemical vapor deposition and physical vapor deposition. The two separate deposition methods will be summarized and discussed in the following subtitles.
2.1.1. Chemical vapor deposition. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) is a general method used to deposit one or more chemical gases or vapors onto the substrate surface to synthesize thin films or nanomaterials.39 In recent years, much progress has been made in fabricating lateral or vertical hetero-nanostructures from CVD epitaxial deposition based on using the lattice mismatch between the multiple crystalline films. These hetero-nanostructures have the potential to be used in electronics and optoelectronics by modulating the electronic properties of ultrathin 2D nanosheets.40

In fabricating two different kinds of transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) monolayers, mono- and multilayer nanosheets of TMDs initially deposit on the SiO2 surface via CVD method.4143 Subsequently, the second layer epitaxially grows on the edge or surface of the existing TMD layer.4450 It is important to have a fresh, unpassivated active edge to grow the second layer. Recently, different gas-phase deposition methods have been developed to fabricate lateral and vertical epitaxial hetero-nanostructures of TMD nanosheets. Duan and co-workers reported a CVD-based epitaxial growth technique in preparing lateral hetero-nanostructures composed of WSe2WS2 and MoSe2MoS2 by in situ switching the vapor-phase reactants without exposing the active edges to ambient conditions.44 Some other studies demonstrated that tuning the growth temperature45 and the growth time46 are effective approaches to control the growth of MoS2WS2 heterostructures. By using the step-by-step CVD process, 2D ternary heterostructures (WS2WSe2MoS2 and WS2MoSe2WSe2), instead of 2D binary heterostructures, were synthesized (Fig. 1).51 In addition, the repeated operation of the same method yielded an ABAB type PNS, i.e., WS2WSe2WS2WSe2 superlattice.

2.1.2. Physical vapor deposition. Physical vapor deposition (PVD) shares the similar process with CVD. However, there is no chemical reaction during the preparation process, and the chemical vapor directly deposits on the target substrate. Various heterostructures were prepared based on the inorganic,5356 organic,5759 and inorganicorganic hybrids60,61via PVD technique. Many organic related materials prepared by PVD showed PNSs due to the self-assembly via supramolecular interactions.62

The preparation of PNSs via PVD method involves different supramolecular interactions, including van der Waals interaction, hydrogen bonding, halogen bonding, intermolecular interaction, and electrostatic interaction. These interactions influence the construction of PNSs, such as the direct formation of backbones and accommodation of additional components.62 For instance, the vapor phase sequential deposition of cobalt(II) hexadecafluoro-29H,31H-phthalocyanine (F16CoPc) and nickel(II) tetraphenyl-21H,23H-porphine (NiTPP) formed densely packed, well-ordered 2D crystalline regions with 1:1 composition on Au(111) through fluorine-phenyl interactions.63 The ordered 2D bimolecular self-assembled 2D regions extended about 100 nm, covering 25% of the surface. The CoPc and NiTPP prepared by coadoption in an ultra-high vacuum (UHV) showed a densely packed, well-defined bimolecular structure, but with a disordered composition.64 The H-bonded honeycomb-like porous network of melamine-PTCDI on a silver-terminated silicon surface in which the pores encapsulated C60 is an example for a well-defined three-component system.65 The hexagonal pores of the binary melamine-PTCDI network consisting of melamine in the vertices and PTCDI in the straight lines accommodated heptametric C60 by inducing the co-location of the several C60 molecules. This new surface phase served as a template for the formation of an additional C60 layer on the hexagonal network of the melamine-PTCDI framework. In another study, chloro[subphthalocyaninato]boron(III) (SubPc) and C60 were self-assembled to well-ordered 2D binary monolayers on Ag(111) in UHV.66 Two phases of the 2D PNSs were constructed: (1) a 2D periodic stripe phase consisting of alternate 1D quasilinear chains of C60 and zigzag rows of SubPc, and (2) a 2D hexagonal star phase of the C60 trimer surrounded by three SubPc units (Fig. 3).

Pulsed laser deposition (PLD) is another kind of PVD method. The high-power laser beam is applied to assist the evaporation and ionization processes of target materials that will be used to deposit on the substrate to form thin films.67 The PLD process can occur not only in UHV, but also in the presence of background gas, such as O2, which is suitable for the deposition of oxide films. Hence, PLD is widely used to synthesize metal oxide-based vertical PNSs via alternate deposition.35,7174 For instance, LaNiO3/La0.7Sr0.3MnO3,72 BiFeO3/Ce-doped-LaMnO3 (Fig. 5),73 LaMnO3/SrIrO3,71 and BiFeO3/BiMnO335 can be prepared on the SrTiO3(001) substrate, and MgTi2O4/SrTiO3 can be deposited on the (001)-oriented cubic spinel MgAl2O4 substrate,74 respectively, via PLD method.

2.2. Solution phase preparation

The solution phase preparation method is widely applied for the large-scale preparation of PNSs. In addition, it is an important supplementary preparation method to vapor phase preparation. In this section, solution phase preparation strategies (including intercalation and exfoliation methods to prepare 2D materials), which act as precursors for 2D PNSs and intercalated PNSs will be discussed, and followed with the self-assembly methods for 1D, 2D, and 3D periodic nanomaterials.
2.2.1. Intercalation and exfoliation. 2D materials are essential precursors for the preparation of 2D layered/based PNSs, but the low yield efficiency of vapor based preparation methods limits the synthesis of 2D layered PNSs based on 2D monolayers. Liquid exfoliation of 2D materials from their bulk counterparts is the general method used to produce 2D materials in a large scale. The critical challenge in exfoliating 2D nanosheets from their bulk materials is overcoming the van der Waals forces between the adjacent layers. The liquid exfoliation has two general routes, and the first route is direct exfoliation in various solvents with the assistance of ultrasonication. This route has been used for the production of graphene, h-BN, and TMDs, and others.7577 The solvent can minimize the energy of exfoliation. However, it still faces the difficulties to produce 2D monolayer materials in high yield.

Intercalation of ions into the interlayers of the bulk-layered material is another general process applied prior to exfoliation in preparation of 2D materials in the large scale. After the interaction step, the van der Waals forces decrease due to the enlarged spacing between the adjacent layers. Although the intercalation of ions may introduce additional impurities and induce a complex purification process, exfoliation after the interaction of the ions is still one of the mainstream methods, and is widely used to prepare various 2D nanosheets, such as notable TMD nanosheets and LDH nanosheets.78 As shown in Fig. 6, Li+ and related reagents are the first choice to be intercalated into the interlayers of bulk materials. The intercalation of Li+ can not only expand the interlayer spaces to weaken the van der Waals interactions, the products of Li(OH)2 and H2 gas that are produced from the reaction of metallic Li and water will further push apart the layers to accelerate the exfoliation process.78


Fig. 6 Electrochemical lithiation process for the fabrication of 2D nanosheets from the layered bulk material. Reprinted with permission from ref. 78. Copyright 2011, John Wiley and Sons.

As discussed, the exfoliation process is an essential step prior to further assembling the 2D layered PNSs. However, as a derivative process, the ion-intercalated bulk layered materials can also be transferred to ion/nanoparticle-confined PNSs by exchanging ions with other nanoparticles.7981 This method is convenient for modulating the spacing between the sheets to further study the effect of the interlayer distance on the activity of the layered materials,79 and also to introduce external active materials to further enhance their performances.80,81

2.2.2. Self-assembly. Self-assembly is a powerful and versatile bottom-up synthetic approach that generates structurally-highly organized periodic aggregates from molecular to macroscopic sized components. Various non-covalent interactions, such as hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interactions, -stacking, van der Waals interaction, metalmetal interactions, hydrophobic interactions, and others, are usually involved in the assembly process.20,62,82,83 Coordination-driven self-assembly, such as metal coordination bonding between the electron-rich end groups of organic linkers and transition-metal atoms, is also an appropriate bonding for self-assembly.83,84 Self-assembly relies on the chemical complementarity and structural compatibility of the starting materials. Therefore, by rationally designing and fabricating the starting materials with complementary shapes and chemical/physical properties, the structure of the final assembly can be encoded in the starting materials. Thus, preconceived self-assembled structures can be fabricated under appropriate reaction conditions.20,82,84 Integrating different concepts and synthetic strategies, enormous complexity and diversity can be introduced to the self-assembled nanostructures.
2.2.2.1. One-dimensional PNSs. One-dimensional (1D) materials usually refer to nanowires, which have been intensively studied for various applications. Nanowire can be applied in different areas, such as electronic and optical devices.32,85 In particular, periodic nanostructured nanowires may demonstrate enhanced performances due to their multiple periodic components.86 Therefore, different preparation strategies have been developed to prepare periodic nanowires via solution-based self-assembly process.

A variety of supramolecular interactions, such as electrostatic interaction, stacking and others, can contribute to the construction of 1D PNSs in solution phase via self-assembly. Stacking is widely used for the construction of organic 1D nanowires based on planar -conjugated molecules.87 Stacking is more suitable for preparing homogeneous nanostructures, rather than PNSs, due to the nature of the -interactions that have no disparity towards various molecules. Stacking may also construct heterostructures, but it is hard to control their periodicity. Electrostatic interactions are another important type of interactions used to build up supramolecular 1D nanostructures.88 Mirkin et al. reported on a 1D colloidal superlattice based on triangular Au nanoprisms and cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) via the balance between the attractive depletion forces and repulsive electrostatic forces.89 This 1D superlattice demonstrated a large spacing distance, which is four times the thickness of a single nanoprism. Zheng et al. proposed a method to produce a Pd nanosheets based 1D Pd superlattice nanowire.90 Different from previous work that used depletion force to balance the electrostatic repulsion, the authors introduced counter-cations (e.g., Fe3+, Al3+) to decrease the repulsive electrostatic force between the negatively charged Pd nanosheets.90 This method offered a controllable length of Pd superlattice nanowire (ranging from 25 nm to 283 nm). By controlling the concentration of the counter-cations in solution, the length of the Pd superlattice nanowire can be increased as a result of the increasing ratio of counter-cations (Fig. 7ac). It is easy to construct 1D nanowires on planar molecules and 2D nanosheets since the interaction forces align with the growth direction of the nanowires. However, it is challenging to assemble nanowires on nanocrystals (NCs) due to their isotropic nature, which induces nonobvious directional interactions.91 Hence, external assistance is necessary to support the construction of 1D nanowires based on NCs. Dong et al. raised a general method for the preparation of a 1D NC superlattice based on NCs with the assistance of molecular clusters.92 The preparation method yields single-NC-wide polymer-like flexible linear chains containing up to 1000 close-packed NCs (with the length exceeding 18 m), as shown in Fig. 7dg. This method is suitable for a wide range of hydrophobic NCs, regardless of their composition, size, and shape. It is believed that the balance of solvophobicity and solvophilicity dictates the growth of the 1D NCs superlattice.

2.2.2.2. Two-dimensional PNSs. Two-dimensional (2D) periodic nanomaterials include lateral single-layered periodic nanomaterials and vertical multi-layered periodic nanomaterials. The single-layered 2D periodic nanomaterials can be prepared by direct assembly from small molecules, while the vertical PNSs are prepared by assembly from single-layered materials yielded by exfoliation process.

A wide range of molecules have been self-assembled to 2D periodic supramolecular assemblies by employing directional intermolecular hydrogen bonding (H-bonding) as the driven force for the molecular recognition process.9396 The position and the number of functionalities that can form H-bonding strongly influence the structural and functional diversity of the ultimate H-bonded assembly. Trimesic acid (TMA) is an interesting H-bond linker molecule that forms H-bonded supramolecular motifs between the carboxylic groups either with cyclic dimer- or trimer-configuration due to its planer aromatic core with 3-fold carboxylic acids.

Self-assembly of TMA forms a 2D-periodic grid architecture with two different phases on a solid interface; a six-fold chicken-wire structure and three-fold flower-like structures, which are different in terms of the H-bonding pattern.93,94 The periodic cavities in the H-bonded 2D template of TMA form various hostguest multicomponent nanostructures via hostguest complexation. For example, Fig. 8 demonstrates that the TMA-coronene supramolecular hybrid was fabricated by introducing guest coronene (COR) molecules (diameter of 1 nm) into the hexagonal cavities of the chicken-wire TMA hexagonal cell monolayer at 1-octanoic acid/single-layer graphene supported by SiO2.93

The self-assembly of C60 and oligothiophene macrocycles in H-bonded TMA templated 2D network at the liquidsolid interface of heptanoic acid solution and highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) was also studied with scanning tunnelling microscopy and spectroscopy.94 Here, the Saturn-like complexes of C60 with oligothiophene macrocycles in mono- and bilayers have also been epitaxially assembled on the H-bonded 2D-host template of TMA at the interface of heptanoic acid solution and HOPG via double hosting the C60 molecules by non-covalent interactions.94 The hexagonal cavities of the chicken-wire structure of the 2D TMA monolayer on the liquid/graphite interface with the pore diameter around 12 provides the host sites for the epitaxial ordering of the -expanded macrocyclic oligothiophenes hosting C60 molecules. Here, the self-assembly of the Saturn-like complex was driven by the site recognition of C60 molecules in the templated monolayer of TMA. This study demonstrates that the H-bonding and charge transfer interactions between the donoracceptor systems can be used to manipulate the electronic properties of the final nano-assembly.

The diverse range of 1,3,5-tris(10-carboxydecyloxy) benzene (TCDB)-based multicomponent architectures are also constructed by encapsulating various guest species into the tetragonal cavities of the 2D, hydrogen-bonded TCBD host network on HOPG by coadsorption approach with scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) under ambient conditions.95 For example, the inclusion of copper phthalocyanine (CuPc) and coronene as the guest species to the cavities of the host network of TCBD on HOPG formed well-ordered CuPc/TCDB and coronene/TCDB hostguest architectures in large areas.95 Another study demonstrated that cavities of the 2D network of TCBD on HOPG could accommodate vanadyl phthalocyanine (VOPc) as a guest molecule to construct 2D VOPc/TCBD hostguest architectures.96 Interestingly, the VOPc/TCBD showed thermal annealing induced phase transformation from the VOPC dimer entrapped VOPc/TCDB (II) phase to the monomer-entrapped VOPc/TCDB (I) phase.96 As illustrated in Fig. 9, it was also found that different unsubstituted metallophthalocyanines (MPc), such as H2Pc, CuPc, VOPc, and ClGaPc, and halogen-substituted MPcs (such as F16CuPc) could be encapsulated in the porous H-bonded network of formed MPc/TCDB architectures.97

The hydrogen bonded porous network of TCBD can also be used to entrap different helical and saddle oligothiophenes on HOPG. Four different nonplaner-ligothiophene molecules, i.e., helical (TMS)2-[7]helicene (T1), naphthalene-cored double (TMS)2-[7]helicene (T2), saddle-shaped cyclooctatetrathiophene (T3) and its derivative ,,,-tetraphenyl-cyclooctatetrathiophene (T4), were encapsulated into the pre-formed porous TCBD-host template to construct TCBD_T1, TCBD_T2, TCBD_T3 and TCBD_T4 on HOPG by using STM under ambient conditions.98 The structure of the TCBD-T1 to T4 strongly depends on the balancing of the H-bonding of the TCBD network, and the other non-covalent interactions between the host-TCBD and guest molecules.

So far, we discussed two component hostguest supramolecular porous networks comprising a single type of host molecule, in which the pores can accommodate guest molecules with specific sizes. However, there are very few experimental studies on multicomponent, periodic hostguest supramolecular networks composed of two or more kinds of host molecular components at the liquidsolid interface. These multicomponent assemblies possess cavities with different sizes/shapes that could entrap appropriate guest species via size- and shape-selective guest inclusion. Therefore, optimized recognition and selection processes are important in the hierarchical self-assembly of a monolayer with preferential binding sites from multiple components, and in controlling the hostguest complexation within the monolayer to accommodate various guest species. The first four-component 2D crystalline architecture was successfully self-assembled from a Kagom-type heterocomplex-stabilized nanoporous network of bisDBA-C12, which hosted a COR1-ISA6 cluster and TRI molecules in the hexagonal and triangular pores, respectively, at the 1-octanoic acidHOPG interface with STM at room temperature.99 Here, the control manipulation of the size and shape of the guest molecules/clusters with the nanopores of the Kagom patterned host complex was successfully applied as the design strategy. Another four-component 2D crystalline architecture was reported from the host molecules of TCDB, host 1,2,17,18,33,34-hexaaza-[2,6](4,40)cyclophane-1,17,33-triene (3NN-Macrocycle), and guest molecules of coronene and heptanoic acid at a liquidsolid interface on HOPG.100 TCBD and 3NN-macrocycles formed the binary hybrid host network with two different cavities, hexagonal (A) and triangular (B), with sizes of about 1.3 and 0.8 nm, respectively. 2.2.2.3. Three-dimensional PNSs. As the most common morphology of nanomaterials, three-dimensional (3D) nanomaterials have been widely studied for various applications. Therefore, different preparation methods were developed to satisfy the wide range of applications. The 3D periodic nanomaterials are usually constructed from ordered, close-packed nanoparticles featuring a high surface area, abundant active sites, and collective properties that differ from the disordered/isolated nanoparticles. Since self-assembly is a general strategy to prepare the 3D periodic nanomaterials, herein the preparation methods of 3D periodic nanomaterials via self-assembly are summarized for further studies.

The spontaneous self-assembly of colloidal particles in suspensions or at the airliquid interface provide a simple and versatile approach to form highly ordered nanoparticle arrays or structural assemblies known as colloidal superlattices with precise periodicity across large dimensions from several nanometres to micrometres.82 In comparison to the randomly configured nanoparticle mixtures, colloidal superlattices exhibit precise uniformity in particle sizes, as well as the interparticle distances, generating well-defined surface topographical patterns. The assembly of colloidal particles with nano-dimensions, such as quantum dots, nanoparticles, and nanocrystals, are interesting in the design of size-tunable colloidal superlattices.110112 The assembly of the colloidal superlattice is, in general, driven by the colloidal forces between the individual nanoparticle that lead towards equilibrium. Therefore, various superlattice architectures can be designed by modulating the interparticle interactions and thereby tuning the internal ordering, interparticle distance, precise stoichiometry, and other parameters of the nanoparticles in the superlattice (Fig. 15). 2.3. Other preparation methods

Previous preparation methods have already provided popular strategies for the construction of PNSs. However, there are still some strategies that rely on multiple techniques that have not been demonstrated yet. As a supplement, this section will offer some useful methods for the preparation of PNSs.
2.3.1. Lithographic templating. The combination of bottom-up assembly with top-down microfabrication techniques has been applied in fabricating periodically structured materials at the nanoscale. This integrated approach combines the advantageous features of the top-down-lithography and bottom-up-self-assembly to create complex periodic architectures with well-defined size, shape, and functionality from either single or multiple components. Some studies have shown that the spontaneous self-assembly of the block copolymer (BCP) into ordered periodic structures can be integrated with advanced lithographic techniques to fabricate highly ordered densely packed nanometre-scale structures, such as nanodots, nanowires, quantum dots, nanopores, and others, on solid substrates.124127 Here, the self-assembled nanometre-sized domains of ordered BCP thin films serve as a lithographic template to fabricate periodic nanopatterns irrespective of the chemical nature of the underlying solid substrate.128130 The subsequent selective removal of the organic components creates the patterned structures of the metal particles, which are compatible with the periodic BCP domain ordering. Even though this approach can be successfully applied in fabricating one-component nanopatterns, creating multicomponent nanopatterns is challenging, as the geometry of the self-assembled nanodomains of BCP is generally limited to single shapes, such as spheres, cylinders, or lamellae.

Kim et al. demonstrated a multilevel block copolymer (BCP)-based self-assembly approach in fabricating laterally ordered and multicomponent periodic nanopatterns, consisting of metal nanowires and nanodots of a diverse range of metallic elements (Au, Pt, Fe, Pd, and Co) at the sub-nanometre scale (Fig. 18).131 Here, thin films of polystyrene-block-poly(4-vinylpyridine)(PS-b-P4VP) BCP were initially deposited by directed self-assembly by using a topographically prepatterned substrate of Si(100) substrate via graphoepitaxy effect. The as-deposited well-ordered surface parallel nanocylinder arrays were used as a template for the subsequent metallization. The plasma etching selectively removes the organic components, forming arrays of nanodots or nanowires of parallel cylinders. In the second cycle, PS-b-P4VP thin films were re-deposited on the metal nanowire or nanodot array, followed by the second cycle of metallization to create graphoepitaxially controlled multicomponent nanopatterns of Pd nanodot arrays, alternated with Pt nanowires or a Pt-Au binary nanodot array. This multilevel approach allows for the precise control of the lateral ordering of the metal particles, and enables the fabrication of extended-long range structures with a diverse range of pattern shapes, dimensions, and chemical compositions. Furthermore, the size of the nanocomponents was independently modified without affecting the pattern periodicity. However, a significant amount of research still needs to be conducted in order to unravel the potential of the directional self-assembly of BCP-based multicomponent nanopatterning to create different shapes and sizes.

2.3.2. Preparation of twisted PNSs. Compared with the common PNSs in which periodicity comes from the array of materials, the periodicity of twisted PNSs is yielded from the twisted angle between different layers of materials. Among these, the moir superlattice is one of the most well-known twisted PNSs. The emerging novel properties of moir superlattices have attracted intensive studies for the preparation of twisted PNSs. The preparation of the graphene-hBN moir superlattice is relatively easier. After the construction of the graphene-hBN bilayer heterostructure, the lattice mismatch of = 1.8%, and the rotational misalignment between graphene and the hBN layers, the graphene-hBN bilayer heterostructure can transform to moir superlattices naturally.132 In contrast, the preparation of graphene-graphene bilayer moir superlattices needs to tear the graphene flake by using the van der Waals force between hBN and graphene, and manually rotate the separated graphene pieces by a twisted angle before restacking to form graphene bilayer moir superlattices.30 Yuan raised a water bath-assisted process to fabricate MoS2 bilayer moir superlattices (Fig. 19).133 A MoS2 bilayer was first deposited on the SiO2 substrate, then a MoS2 bilayer accompanied with the SiO2 substrate was dropped in 2 M KOH solution at 80 C. The hot KOH solution etched the SiO2 layer to release MoS2 film. The tensile strain promoted the released part of the MoS2 film to curve out of the plane and roll up to a nanoscroll, yielding the SiO2 bilayer moir superlattices.

3.1. Ferroelectricity, ferromagnetism and piezoelectricity

3.1.1. Ferroelectricity, ferromagnetism and multiferroics in superlattices. Materials that exhibit primary ferroic orders, which can be manipulated by external fields, are central to many technological applications, ranging from data storage and sensors to catalysis. Ferroelectricity is a characteristic of certain materials that have a spontaneous electric polarization, which can be reversed by the application of an external electric field, while ferromagnetism is the spontaneous orientation of magnetic moments that gives rise to ferromagnets. Ferroelectric and ferromagnetic PNS are currently the subjects of intensive research due to the interest they raise for not only technological applications, but also the fundamental scientific importance. Moreover, the coexistence of ferroelectricity and ferromagnetism in a single phase of materials yields the novel designed multiferroics,38 allowing for the manipulation of magnetic properties by an electric field in a more energy-efficient way.

Constructing artificial materials in the form of oxide superlattices has been regarded as a promising way to enhance or induce ferroelectricity, which can be precisely controlled by altering the supercells and periodicity. For example, the bismuth ferrite, BiFeO3, shows great potential in several fields, such as spintronics and ferroelectric memories, due to its versatile physics properties. However, the large leakage current greatly hampers its properties by causing spontaneous depolarization.137,138 Song et al. overcame the leakage current limits of BiFeO3 by artificially fabricating a BiFeO3/Ce-doped LaMnO3 vertical superlattices through PLD, enhancing the ferroelectricity compared with BiFeO3.73 The orthorhombic Ce-doped LaMnO3 has excellent lattice matching with the rohombohedral BiFeO3. More importantly, Ce substitution in LaMnO3 provides an efficient way to precisely control Ce doping for BiFeO3 at the atomic-scale level. This plays the key role for reducing the leakage current and enhancing the ferroelectricity of the BiFeO3/Ce-doped LaMnO3 PNS, because Ce prevents the volatility of Bi cations, as well as suppresses the reduction of Fe3+ to Fe2+.137,139 The strong interface coupling in complex oxide superlattices also helps to stabilize ferroelectricity at room-temperature in ultrathin oxide layers. For example, tunable ferroelectricity in (Sr1TiO3)n/BaTiO3 superlattices was reported recently by Wang et al., in which enhanced ferroelectricity was found when 5% and n = 6 compared with their parent bulk materials.140 In addition to enhancing the ferroelectricity, Wang and his co-workers predicted the emergent ferroelectric property in SrTiO3/SrRuO3 superlattices by finely modulating the oxygen vacancy at the interface based on density functional theory (DFT) calculations, as well as kinetic Monte Carlo (KMC) simulations.141 After achieving the theoretical predication, intriguing ferroelectricity has been observed in the SrTiO3/SrRuO3 superlattices, which opens a way to induce the external physical properties, such as ferroelectricity, by defect-engineered symmetry breaking in the PNS systems.141 In addition to the magnitude of the electric polarization being modulated in the oxide superlattices, the spatial arrangement of the electric dipoles can be engineered into various types of emergent polar topologies towards the realization of magnetic skyrmion-like topological structures in ferroelectric materials (i.e., polar skyrmions), including the demonstration of chiral vortices,142,143 chiral,144 and ferroelectric bubble domains.145 More recently, Das et al. observed room-temperature nanometre-scale polar-skyrmion bubbles in [(PbTiO3)n/(SrTiO3)n]m superlattice systems, which can contribute to the advancement of ferroelectrics towards practical functionalities, such as tunable negative capacitance devices.146

Controlling the magnetic ordering in ferromagnetic materials is central to the functionalities and performance of spintronic devices. The construction of artificial oxide superlattices has also become an important method to tailor the magnetic orders via spin-correlated interfacial coupling. In 2020, Huang et al. successfully modulated the magnetic order in [(LaMn3+O3)m/(SrIrO3)m]n superlattices.71 Four superlattices (m = 8, 4, 2, 1, while n = 1, 2, 4, 8) have been fabricated by PLD, as shown in Fig. 21a. The reduction of magnetization was observed when the repeating period is shorter (Fig. 21b). Simultaneously, the magnetic hysteresis loops were noticed in all four [(LaMn3+O3)m/(SrIrO3)m]n superlattices, while the loop vanished in the reference sample (Fig. 21b). Therefore, they proved that both magnetization and coercivity can be tuned by modulating the period of the [(LaMn3+O3)m/(SrIrO3)m]n superlattices (Fig. 21c and d). They argue that the insight of this phenomena is due to the 3d/5d interface electronic correlation and the Rashba-style spinorbit interaction. Consequently, a new route has been demonstrated for engineering the magnetic properties, which can make contributions to future developments in high-capacity storage and advanced computing applications.

Multiferroics materials with the co-existence of ferroelectric and ferromagnetic ordering have greatly attracted attention because of the coupling between ferromagnetism and ferroelectricity, which enables the control of one ferroic order with the conjugated field of the other with particular excitement of the direct electric-field control of ferromagnetism for unprecedented applications in multifunctional devices, such as energy-efficient logic and memory devices. However, it is challenging to simultaneously integrate both ferroelectricity and ferromagnetism, and induce their coupling in a singe phase of materials. Oxide superlattices provide an effective pathway to achieve the interfacial coupling between ferroelectricity and ferromagnetism through the epitaxial integration of oxide layers with distinct properties. Such designed multiferroics has recently been demonstrated by Mundy et al. in the vertical superlattices of (LuFeO3)m/(LuFe2O4)1 (Fig. 22a). This is achieved by inserting a single layer of geometrically frustrated ferrimagnetic LuFe2O4 into the matrix of geometric ferroelectric LuFeO3. The severe lattice rumpling native to LuFeO3 brings a lattice distortion to the neighboring LuFe2O4. Such an interface-induced geometric distortion enhances the magnetism of LuFe2O4 by reducing its spin frustration, while at the same time, ferroelectricity is induced in it by breaking the mirror symmetry of the LuFe2O4 layer. The magnetoelectric coupling between the ferroelectric and ferrimagnetic order in LuFe2O4 was demonstrated by the direct electric-field control of ferrimagnetism at 200 K (Fig. 22b).147 A similar strategy has been utilized recently by Jin et al. in the vertical multiferroic superlattices of (BiFeO3)m/(BiMnO3)m, as shown in Fig. 22c. The strong polarization field of the ferroelectric BiFeO3 layers exerts an induction effect on adjacent BiMnO3 layers, and causes a displacement of the Mn ions that gives rise to ferroelectric polarization in the otherwise centrosymmetric crystal structure. In contrast to the enhanced magnetism in the (LuFeO3)m/(LuFe2O4)1 case, the originally strong ferromagnetism in BiMnO3 is reduced through interfacial antiferromagnetic superexchange coupling (via FeOMn). Nevertheless, the remaining ferromagnetism co-exists with the induced ferroelectricity in a single phase of BiMnO3, which can be modulated by precisely controlling the supercell size (Fig. 22d), contributing to the practical applications of artificial multiferroic systems.35

3.1.2. Piezoelectricity in superlattices. Piezoelectricity represents the electric charge accumulation in certain solid materials in response to the external applied mechanical stress, or the generation of mechanical stress due to the applied voltage. Similar to ferroelectricity, the occurrence of piezoelectric properties in materials requires non-centrosymmetric crystal structures, and they often co-exist with ferroelectricity. For example, the PbTiO3/SrTiO3 heterostructure has been regarded as an ideal platform for the investigation of the electrostatic effect, and the manipulation of the superlattice periodicity and the volume fraction of PbTiO3 becomes an efficient way to modulate the physical properties of PbTiO3/SrTiO3-based superlattices.148,149 Kim et al. fabricated [(PbTiO3)6/(SrTiO3)6]5 superlattices on the SrTiO3 substrate, as shown in Fig. 23a. They argue that the increase of the piezoelectric response is attributed to the local disordered polarization introduced by the interfacial coupling of the superlattice.150

3.2. Superconductivity

Superconductivity is one of the most fascinating phenomena in condensed matter physics both due to its huge technological implications, as well as its fundamental importance. Artificial PNS offer an effective route to induce or modulate the superconductivity via interface engineering with atomic precision. The most recent breakthrough in the realization of unconventional superconductivity in twisted graphene superlattices30 at the so-called magic angle allows us to tap into superconductivity via band topology engineering, and gives rise to an emerging new field of twistronics. In this section, recent advances in the realization of superconductivity in PNS are discussed.
3.2.1. Superconductivity in complex oxide superlattices. Similar to the controlling of ferroic orders in complex oxide superlattices, superconductivity can also be induced or enhanced at strongly coupled complex oxide interfaces prevalent in superlattices. In 2017, the LaNiO3-based superlattice was reported as a potential platform to achieve the high-temperature superconducting state.72 Using PLD technique, a high-quality vertical superlattice with the unit of [LaNiO3 (2 u.c.)/La0.7Sr0.3MnO3 (3 u.c.)] was formed, as shown in Fig. 24a. The temperature-dependent resistance performance shows a semiconductor-type property from room temperature down to a few K, and an abrupt superconducting transition was observed at around 3.7 K (the inset of Fig. 24b) with a current of 5 A. By applying a larger current at 0.1 mA, the superconductivity was destroyed, indicating that the superconductivity arises from the oxide superlattice. The magnetic susceptibility characterization yields the magnetic onset of superconductivity at around 3.5 K (Fig. 24c), which is consistent with the zero-resistance temperature. Furthermore, the magnetic hysteresis loop at 2 K suggests a type-II superconducting nature of this complex oxide superlattice, which is associated with a cuprate-like 3d electron occupancy through the artificial engineering of nicklate heterostructures. More recently, Hu et al. reported the intriguing observation of emergent superconductivity in the spinel insulator, MgTi2O4, by engineering a superlattice with SrTiO3. [MgTi2O4/SrTiO3] superlattices were fabricated on the MgAl2O4 substrate, and the schematic view of this superlattice is illustrated in Fig. 24d. As shown in Fig. 24e, the [MgTi2O4/SrTiO3] superlattice turns to a superconducting state between 3 K to 3.5 K, and eventually reaches the zero resistance at 3 K at zero-magnetic-field condition. The occurrence of superconductivity in MgTi2O4 is associated with the elongation of the out-of-plane lattice constant induced by the epitaxial strain. By modulating this lattice spacing (through controlling the growth temperature of the superlattice), a remarkable tuning of the superconducting transition temperature can be achieved. Intriguingly, the observed superconductivity, albeit induced by interfacial coupling, does not seem to be anisotropically confined to interfaces as typically expected at complex oxide interfaces.74 An isotropic upper critical magnetic field was observed by magnetotransport measurements with both in-plane and out-of-plane magnetic fields (Fig. 24e). They suggest that the emergence of superconductivity can be related to the suppression of orbital ordering by electron-doping effect of the non-stoichiometric Ti/Mg ratio. This work shows the great potential in further exploring the superconductivity in spinel families. However, key questions remain to be answered, such as the isotropic superconductivity behavior, which is in stark contrast to the interface conductivity observed in other oxide heterostructures.

3.2.2. Superconductivity in intercalated van der Waals PNS. A hybrid vertical PNS by intercalating organic molecules into 2D van der Waals materials (inorganic materials) provides a new avenue to induce or enhance the superconductivity. Due to the intercalation of the organic species, the interactions between the inorganic layers can be significantly altered. The introduced organic intercalation layer can screen the Coulomb interaction and reduce the interlayer coupling of cooper pairs, leading to the superconductivity, or act as molecular dopants to dope electrons to the inorganic layer to enhance the superconductivity (e.g., increased Tc). For example, Shi et al. reported that by intercalating the FeSe single crystal with organic cations of tetrabutyl ammonium (TBA+) via electrochemical intercalation method, a novel hybrid vertical PNS can be formed.151 As shown in Fig. 25a, the distance between adjacent FeSe layers is increased from 0.55 nm to 1.49 nm, indicating the successful intercalation with TBA+ ions. Both transport measurements and magnetic susceptibility measurements revealed a consistent superconducting transition temperature of 50 K (Fig. 25b), which is the highest for bulk FeSe superconductors (compared with 8.9 K for pristine crystals). Furthermore, the magnetic susceptibility characterization demonstrated no flux pinning effect, indicating a very small amount of impurities or defects introduced during the intercalation process. This hybrid PNS approach provides a new pathway to induce the high-Tc superconductivity in iron-based bulk superconductors.152 A similar strategy of organic cation intercalation can be widely used in other 2D materials to form hybrid vertical PNS in which the superconducting properties, along with other exotic correlated properties, can be fashioned with atomic-scale accuracy. For example, coupling superconductivity with topological properties could open up exciting opportunities for topological quantum computation.153,154 MoTe2 and WTe2 are Weyl semimetals with many fascinating quantum properties in their monolayer limit, such as edge conduction155 and quantum spin Hall effect.156 Although the superconducting state has been reported in both gated monolayer WTe2 (Tc = 0.82 K)157 and MoTe2 (Tc = 8 K),158 it is very challenging to obtain monolayers of WTe2 or MoTe2 through mechanical exfoliation, greatly hampering the practical applications in the future. Zhang et al. recently reported a strategy to form WTe2 and MoTe2-based vertical inorganicorganic hybrid superlattices through organic cation intercalation.154Fig. 25c shows the schematic illustration of the new class of hybrid TMD intercalated with organic cations. The interlayer coupling strength was reduced after organic cation intercalation, resulting in the transition from a 3D type-II Weyl semimetal to a 3D weak topological insulator.159 The hybrid PNS sample shows larger resistance than the pristine sample at high temperature, as shown in Fig. 25d, but without the sudden change in resistance between 240 K to 260 K of the pristine sample (inset of Fig. 25d) characteristic of phase transitions, suggesting the weaker interlayer coupling effect in intercalated samples. Fig. 25e shows that the onset superconducting transition temperature of intercalated MoTe2 is 7 K, which is significantly higher than that of the corresponding pristine crystals (Tc of 0.25 K). The greatly weakened interlayer coupling by organic intercalation is also expected to lead to 2D superconductivity, which is indeed confirmed by linear RT behavior with a characteristic 2D BerezinskiiKosterlitzThouless (BKT) transition temperature of 4.7 K. Similarly, intercalated WTe2 displays an enhanced Tc of 2.3 K compared to that of 0.82 K for the monolayer gated WTe2. Consequently, hybrid vertical PNS provides an important pathway for manipulating the superconducting properties of topological 2D materials.

In addition to the layered 2D materials being intercalated with organic molecules to form vertical PNS that give rise to emergent properties as discussed above, two different kinds of 2D materials can be intercalated to form vertically stacked superlattices. Unlike oxide superlattices with strongly bonded heterointerfaces, which place a stringent requirement of lattice mismatch for the epitaxial growth, the weak van der Waals type interlayer coupling entails that 2D intercalated superlattices can be constructed arbitrarily by tolerating a much larger lattice mis-match or even incommensurate lattices. The so-called misfit layered compounds is such kind of 2D vertical superlattices with a general formula of (MX)1+(TX2)m, in which the TMD layer with a m number of layers (represented by TX2) is incommensurately intercalated with a monochalcogenide layer (MX). represents the mismatch index with the value of 0.080.28, which is related to the ratio of the mismatched lattice parameters.160 Among the numerous forms of misfit-layered compounds, those involving TaS2 have received particular attention because of a multitude of correlated states displayed by TaS2, including CDW, chiral charge order and superconducting transition.161,162 Early studies in 1990s demonstrated that the numerous types of (MS)1+TaS2 misfit-layered compounds show the superconducting state with Tc above 70 mK.163 More recently, Sankar et al. reported a method to grow large-sized single crystal misfit compound of (SnS)1.15TaS2 through chemical vapor transport method.164Fig. 26a and b depict the HRTEM image and schematic view of the misfit (SnS)1.15TaS2, which confirms the quality of the intercalation. It is higher than the one of the pristine of TaS2 (Tc = 1.90 K)165 and other similar types of misfit compounds,165,166 indicating the enhancement of superconductivity. Additionally, the Tc shows a linear increase with the pressure, as shown in Fig. 26d, reaching 3.85 K at 1.95 GPa. This is likely due to the change of Fermi surfaces upon compression.167 In another example, large-size single crystals of (SnSe)1.16(NbSe2) misfit-layered compound have been reported as a multi-band superconductor with a Tc of 3.4 K.168 Both of these two examples suggest that the misfit PNS is an important system for studying the superconductivity in layered van der Waals materials.

Superlattices involving the highly anisotropic 2D materials also provide an avenue to explore superconductivity in its clean 2D limit, which can shed light on understanding the unconventional superconductivity or facilitate the construction of unconventional superconducting phases.169,170 Although it is possible to preserve the 2D superconductivity in a monolayer of 2D materials, it often requires encapsulation layers such as BN to protect the fragile 2D superconductivity from the external environment, which complicate the device fabrication and could introduce additional defects and/or disorders during the manual stacking of layers. Very recently, Kaxiras et al. developed a method to synthesize a novel 2D vertical superlattice consisting of alternating layers of a 2D superconductor H-NbS2 and a spacer layer Ba3NbS5, as shown in the STEM result (Fig. 27a).171 The individual layers of Nb H-NbS2 in the resulting Ba6Nb11S28 bulk single crystal are well decoupled and protected by the blocking layer with an electron mobility a few orders of magnitude higher than that in bulk NbS2 or unprotected monolayers. The superlattice crystal turns into a superconducting state with an onset Tc of 1.6 K (Fig. 27b). Magnetotransport measurements show that the upper critical magnetic field, which destroys the superconductivity, increases rapidly as the magnetic field is tilted away from the direction normal to the 2D layers (Fig. 27d). This further corroborates the occurrence of a clean 2D superconducting state in the superlattice.

The beauty of the bulk van der Waals superlattices described above lies not only in providing bulk crystalline materials with emergent or enhanced superconductivity, it also opens up a route to construct artificial quantum materials by incorporating 2D materials with other exotic quantum phases, such as topological insulators, which can be modulated or protected by the intercalated spacer layer. 3.2.3. Superconductivity in moir superlattices of twisted bilayer graphene. 2D materials not only can be constructed into artificial vertical heterostructures and superlattices by introducing an arbitrary angle of rotation between two adjacent layers, such as in a bilayer graphene, a moir superlattice with in-plane periodicity featuring a much larger supercell can be formed. The new periodic potential associated with the moir pattern modifies the original band structure and electron transport behaviors, leading to many fascinating new properties, including unconventional superconductivity. It also gives rise to a burgeoning field called twistronics, which has quickly become the holy grail in condensed matter physics. Bilayer graphene, which normally adopts two graphene layers stacked in the Bernal (AB) stacking geometry, is the archetypical example of twisted moir superlattices. By twisting the bilayer graphene system with a certain angle, the Dirac bands of graphene are modified into moir Bloch bands that depend sensitively on the twisting angle.172 At a large twisting angle (e.g. > 10), only the higher energy bands of the widely separated Dirac cones are modified by the interlayer coupling to form saddling points with corresponding Van Hove singularities (VHSs) in the DOS. The low energy bands close to the Dirac points remain barely changed from isolated graphene. As the twisting angle is reduced, interlay coupling starts to affect the low energy Dirac cone states with the VHSs brought closer to the Dirac point. Near the so-called magic angle ( 1.1), the VHSs merge into very narrow and ultra-flat moir minibands (510 meV) with a nearly vanishing Fermi velocity, and the corresponding electron density is strongly localized at the AA stacking region of the moir superlattice (Fig. 28a and b). The very low kinetic energy of electrons that fill the flat bands implies that the Coulomb interactions can dominate the electronic behavior, and drive the system into strongly correlated phases. Early experimental attempts at twisting angles close to the magic angle indeed revealed signs of correlated insulating states.173,174 A breakthrough was made in 2018 by Cao et al., who reported two ground-breaking discoveries in magic-angle bilayer graphene superlattices that exhibited Mott-like insulating phase at half flat-band filling (i.e., two electrons/holes per moir unit cell)175 and most surprisingly, unconventional superconductivity when the system is electrostatically doped slightly away from the half-filling state.30 The group achieved the magic twisting angle in bilayer graphene by adopting a modified tear and stack method starting from a single monolayer graphene,173,175,176 allowing for the precise control of the twisting angle with a resolution of 0.10.2. The twisted bilayer graphene (TBG) was sandwiched between h-BN layers for encapsulation with a gate underneath the bottom h-BN that tunes the electron density in graphene (see Fig. 28c for device schematics). When the Fermi energy is moved below the charge neutrality point near the half filling of the lower flat band through electrostatic gating, the magic-angle TBG exhibits a superconducting transition with a Tc around 1 K (Fig. 28d) with highly anisotropic critical magnetic fields that destroy superconductivity. The temperature-dependent VI characteristics confirm the 2D superconductivity with a BKT transition temperature of 1.0 K (Fig. 28e). Fig. 28f shows the heat-map for the four-probe resistance of one of the TBG devices (M1) as a function of density and temperature. Superconducting domes were noticed on each side of the half-filling Mott insulating state when the system is doped with additional holes or electrons (relative to half-filling). This phase diagram closely resembles that of high-temperature superconductors, such as cuprates, but is fully electrically tunable. This offers a great advantage to explore unconventional superconductivity and other strongly correlated phenomena over the entire competing phases without the need of introducing impurities or changing compositions, as in cuprates. Following Cao's work, Yankowitz et al. reproduced the superconductivity in TBG with a twist angle of 1.1 both in the hole-doped and electron-doped regions, and TBG with a twist angle larger than the magic angle can be brought back to the superconducting states by enhancing the interlayer coupling through pressure.177 Codecido et al. demonstrated that the TBG showed the superconducting phenomena at an even smaller twist angle of 0.93, with a critical temperature of 0.30.5 K,178 and superconductivity with an even smaller twist angle of 0.79 was reported very recently;179 however, without adjacent Mott-like insulating state. These studies suggest that the range of the magic angle is more robust than what was previously believed, which, while it could benefit device applications, also places constraints on the theoretical explanations on the origin of the superconductivity. Nevertheless, moir superlattices based on bilayer graphene provide a 2D carbon-based material platform to investigate the electron correlation with precise tunability, which in conjunction with band topology can lead to other exotic phases, such as ferromagnetism,180 quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) effect,181,182 and ferroelectricity.183 Following this success, twisting other 2D materials to realize moir superlattices, such as topological materials, has become a fertile land to achieve the emergent superconducting state. For example, for WSe2, it has been reported that the zero resistance was observed at the cryogenic regime in twisted homo-bilayer WSe2, suggesting a possible transition to the full superconducting state.184

3.4. Optical properties

The formation of PNSs provides an approach to modify the optical properties of materials, such as the photonic band gap, light scattering, and plasmonic resonance. It enhances the intrinsic optical properties compared to their bulk counterparts, which pave a way for the next-generation photodetectors.

Photonic crystals present periodic dielectric structures, through which the light propagation has been well studied. 1D, 2D and 3D photonic crystals with the periodicity of dielectric materials along one or more directions exhibit interesting light phenomena. The device applications of the photonic crystal can be designed by combining simple optical components of mirrors, waveguide and resonant cavities based on theoretical predictions of optical properties.195 Here, examples of integrating the periodic nanostructures to modulate light propagations and optical properties in specific devices are discussed.

In 2011, Li et al. demonstrated the tunable photonic bandgap in one-dimensional superconducting-dielectric photonic crystal superlattices by a transfer-matrix method.196 With the swelling of the thicknesses of the superconductor layer and the dielectric layer, an enlarged width of the forbidden gaps and increased number of photonic bandgaps can be achieved. Some PNSs showed temperature-sensitive refractive indexes, which provided an easy-to-manipulate method to tune the optical properties of such materials. Ye et al. reported a systematic analysis to address this temperature effect on the optical properties of lithium niobate-based and liquid crystal-based 1D superlattices.197 From theoretical calculations, they found that the band structures and the density of states (DOSs), as well as the propagation wavelength, the refractive index and layer width were tunable by varying temperature. Especially, the concurrence of the null photonic gap and zero refractive index occurred in the visible optical range via temperature modulation. For example, the null photonic gap and zero refractive index appeared simultaneously in the 450664 nm range when increasing the temperature from 68 K to 29893 K. This work shows the potential of superlattice materials for adaptive photonic devices with temperature-adjustable optical properties.

Not only can introducing periodic molecular structures into the material tune the material properties, the rational design of a material system integrated with period structures can improve the overall properties as well. Yan and coworkers developed a light-trapping structure of 1D nanograting bonded porous 2D photonic crystal (PC) perovskite (G-PC-PVK) films, as illustrated in Fig. 30a.198 The regular periodic structure consisted of highly ordered nanogratings on a compact MAPbI3 perovskite layer with period 2D porous PCs. Benefiting from the porous scaffold and the nanoimprinting process, the as-synthesized G-PC-PVK films showed improved crystallinity, larger grain size and prolonged carrier lifetime, compared with conventional flat perovskite (F-PVK) films. In addition, owing to the coupling effect of grating diffraction and 2D PC architecture, the light reflectance and transmission were reduced, thus exhibiting enhanced absorbance (Fig. 30b and c), which led to improving the performance of their G-PC-PVK-based photodetectors. The 1D nanograting arrays also equipped the devices with polarization-dependent light detection with a peak-to-valley ratio of 1.6, indicating the high polarization sensitivity.

Similarly, Meng et al. applied the periodic plasmonic grating structure with the InAs/GaSb type-II superlattice active layer to mid-infrared photodetector devices.199 Silver gratings were embedded on the active layer to improve the light absorption. The integration of InAs/GaSb type-II superlattices and silver gratings showed a combination of the FabryProt mode and the surface plasmon polariton (SPP) waveguide mode. The gold electrode worked as a transmission-block layer to drive the back-and-forth light traveling, leading to the FabryProt cavity mode at a certain wavelength where the resonance was strong with the reflectivity of nearly zero. By controlling the thickness of the InAs/GaSb active layer, the reflectivity in the resonant absorption spectra could be tuned. At the same time, the period of the silver gratings influenced the SPP resonant wavelength. This hybrid mode greatly improved the light absorption at selective wavelengths without sacrificing the infrared absorption of their reference device without silver gratings. Their device with silver grating also exhibited high polarized sensitivity.

A fundamental understanding of light-matter interactions is critical to the rational design of periodic nanostructures for optical property modifications. The light interactions with periodic particle and hole arrays were comprehensively reviewed and summarized by FJG De Abajo in 2007.200 This work gives a quantitative model, which describes the light scattering in the planar periodic systems. One important topic in the research field of light-matter interactions is the nanophotonics in 2D and van der Waals materials. Polaritons in van der Waals materials, especially the highly confined plasmon-polaritons observed in graphene, have broadened the applications for sensing,201 nanolasering202 and light modulations,203 manipulating light under the nanoscale. Tailoring the material structure enables the tunable polaritonic characteristics of van der Waals materials towards desired optical properties. Polaritons in 2D and van der Waals materials have been systematically discussed in a number of reviews.204,205 Advanced technologies control photonic quasiparticles, such as plasmons, excitons and magnons, in the same way as photons. A representative review demonstrates a unified view of describing different lightmatter interaction phenomena with photonic quasiparticles by macroscopic quantum electrodynamics, providing experimental directions.206

4.1. Energy conversion

Energy is one of the key factors that promote the development of human society. However, the application of traditional fossil fuels caused a series of environmental problems that affect our lives. Therefore, the modern energy conversion has become a main strategy to produce clean energy for the sustainable development of society with the protection of nature. Different types of materials were developed for energy conversion,4,9,10,12 among which PNSs have demonstrated outstanding merits for the enhancement of energy conversion performance due to their unique structural periodicity. The periodicity of PNSs has introduced various benefits to PNSs for energy conversion, such as synergistic effects that can be enhanced to improve catalytic activity,207 catalysts-reactants nanospace interfaces that can be engineered to smoothen product transference,136,208 and bandgaps that can be modulated to broaden light absorption,209 to name a few. The detailed applications of PNSs in various energy conversion processes, including water splitting, CO2 reduction, fuel cells, thermoelectricity, and solar cells, will be discussed in the following sections.
4.1.1. Water splitting (HER, OER). Facing challenges of a global warming crisis, carbon neutral is one of the most important solutions that the scientific community has devoted to explore. However, reducing the emission of carbonaceous species is the first challenge. As one of the most potential clean energy candidates, hydrogen has attracted great attention as an ideal replacement for the fossil fuels since it has zero carbonaceous species emission. However, the efficient generation of hydrogen is one of the key problems impeding their practical applications. Water, one of the main sources of hydrogen, is cheap, easy to get, and abundant around the world. The most common hydrogen generation process by water is known as water splitting, which consists of two half reactions: (i) hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and (ii) oxygen evolution reaction (OER).210213 Due to the adverse issues during the water splitting process, extra energy is required to initiate and maintain the catalysis process. Therefore, various strategies including electrocatalytic,214,215 photocatalytic,216,217 and photoelectrocatalytic218,219 water splitting processes have been studied so far. Although catalysts based on precious metals, such as Pt, Ir, and their oxides, are good candidates for these strategies, their expensive prices are not ideal for large-scale production. Hence, catalysts that contain less or no precious metals are required to be designed for efficient water splitting. In the next sections, the catalytic activity of PNSs in water splitting will discussed.
4.1.1.1. Electrocatalytic water splitting. As an important branch of water splitting, electrocatalytic water splitting has been intensively studied. Based on the unique electronic properties, many electrocatalysts with PNSs were developed to optimize the cathodic HER and/or anodic OER, as outlined in Table 3.214,215
4.1.1.1.1. Electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction. The electrocatalytic HER process involves a simple two-electron migration mechanism, whereas the reaction pathways are different, owing to the type of catalysts and pH conditions.215 According to the mechanism of electrocatalytic HER (Fig. 31), many catalysts were developed, aiming to reach or even outperform the catalytic activity of Pt. Catalysts with PNSs showed good performance in electrocatalytic HER,20,220 making them potential candidates for practical applications.

2D materials, such as 2D TMDs,221 graphene and its derivatives,222,223 are well studied for water splitting. For instance, metallic 1T-MoS2 nanosheets (NSs) showed good electrocatalytic HER activities with Tafel slopes of 40 mV dec1,224,225 where graphene acts as either electroactive component or functionalized support.223,226 Constructing layer-by-layer PNSs using different 2D materials may significantly enhance the catalytic activity via synergistic effect.207 A van der Waals heterostructure consisting of g-C3N4 and graphene is one such example for electrocatalytic HER.227 Qiao et al. used a 2D porous g-C3N4 nanolayer that was exfoliated from porous C3N4 and N-doped graphene to construct PNSs (denoted as PCN/NG). The out-of-plane pores between the layers of PCN/NG can benefit mass transport during the catalysis process, where the in-plane pores can provide sufficient active sites for catalysis. Therefore, PCN/NG exhibited good electrocatalytic HER activity with a lower Tafel slope of 49.1 mV dec1 after initial 750 cyclic voltammetry (CV) compared with the corresponding value of PCN/NG without CV cycles (Tafel slope = 88.6 mV dec1).27 Another example is the unilamellar metallic nanosheets, which exhibit better electronic property in energy conversion application in comparison to the corresponding few-layered bulk material. Sasaki et al. restacked unilamellar metallic 1T phase MoS2 nanosheets and cationic poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA)-modified reduced graphene oxide (PDDA-rGO) to construct 1T-MoS2/PDDA-rGO superlattice for electrocatalytic HER study. Benefitting from the loosely restacked structure of the 1T-MoS2/PDDA-rGO superlattice, a large interlayer spacing of 2.5 nm was expected to provide more accessible active sites to enhance the electrocatalytic HER performance, offering a low overpotential of 137 mV (current density = 10 mA cm2) and Tafel slope of 48.6 mV dec1.28 Although the 1T phase MoS2 has good catalytic activity, the 2H phase MoS2 is a thermodynamically stable structure. Ma and co-authors prepared a periodic nanostructure based on 2H-MoS2 and unilaminar graphitic nanocarbon (2H-MoS2/GNC). Different from the direct assembly of two materials, the synthesis of 2H-MoS2/GNC experienced a calcination process after threonic acid intercalation into MoS2 (T-MoS2). The interlayer spacing of 2H MoS2 increased from 0.63 nm to 0.99 nm in T-MoS2, decreasing both the overpotential and Tafel slope from over 500 to 421 mV and 103 to 53 mV dec1, respectively. However, a slight decrease in the interlayer spacing from 0.99 to 0.98 nm was observed after the formation of 2H-MoS2/GNC, whereas its overpotential and Tafel slope significantly reduced to from 421 to 195 mV and from 53 to 47 mV dec1, respectively. The enhanced electrocatalytic HER activity of 2H-MoS2/GNC may be ascribed to the conducting unilaminar graphitic nanocarbon structure that promoted an efficient electron transfer for the catalysis and shortened diffusion distance.228

Confined catalysis has attracted increasing attention due to the nanoconfinement effect, which can be used to enhance the catalytic activities in some reactions, such as CO oxidation, H2 oxidation, and electrolysis of water.229 MoS2 has demonstrated good HER activity in acidic condition, owing to the proper adsorption energy of hydrogen at its edge.230 Hence, 2D MoS2 confined nanoparticle electrocatalysts may exhibit high catalytic activity. Various precious metal nanoparticles, including Pt, Ru, Pd, and Au nanoparticles, were intercalated into the layers of MoS2. All of them showed good catalytic activities towards HER, especially the MoS2-confined Pt nanoparticles (MoS2/Pt-NPs) exhibited superior catalytic activity and long-term stability, which outperformed the commercial Pt/C catalysts. The flaw of the conventional way of modifying metal catalysts on the surface of 2D materials is that the metal catalysts would corrode in acidic condition and leach away, which can be avoided by the MoS2/Pt-NP system due to the synergistic host-intercalant effect.80 In comparison to the high HER activity of MoS2 in acidic condition, MoS2 suffered from poor HER activity in alkaline condition due to the sluggish water dissociation process.231 However, it is still important to study alkaline HER since the cost of alkaline electrolytes can be decreased, and the alkaline condition allows the use of earth-abundant transition metals to further reduce the cost compared to that of the precious metals-based catalysts.215 Co species, such as Co(OH)2, is widely considered as good water dissociation centres since it can reduce the energy barrier to break the H-O bond by absorbing oxygen, which can benefit the water dissociation in alkaline conditions. Therefore, Liu and co-authors introduced Co(OH)2 nanoparticles to multilayered MoS2 nanosheets to construct a 2D MoS2 confined Co(OH)2 nanoparticle (MoS2/Co-NP) electrocatalyst. The new MoS2/Co-NP catalyst enhanced the HER performance via accelerating water dissociation in alkaline condition with a Tafel slope of 53 mV dec1.81

The moir superlattice has experienced a rapid development in recent years due to the unconventional superconductivity at the magic-angle in twisted bilayer graphene.30,175 The conductivity of the moir superlattice can be easily tuned by adjusting its twisted angle. Conventional methods to enhance the catalytic activity of MoS2 mainly focused on optimizing popular factors, such as active sites and vacancies. However, the interlayer potential barrier is seldom studied. Yuan and co-authors have fabricated a MoS2 moir superlattice with a twisted angle of 7.3. Benefitting from the twisted structure of the bilayer MoS2, a reduced interlayer potential barrier was confirmed by first-principles calculations and ultralow frequency Raman spectra. The low interlayer potential barrier promotes the electron transfer from the conductive substrate to the active sites in the moir superlattice of MoS2, leading to an enhanced electrocatalytic HER performance with a Tafel slope of 73 mV dec1.133

Wang et al. utilized a template-assisted printing-based assembly process to construct striped-pattern (SP) superlattices. The SP superlattices could catalyze various gas evolution reactions by using various components. The small gaps between the strips of the SP superlattices can provide a smaller bubble stretch force and higher transference, which significantly enhanced the catalytic activity (Fig. 32). For instance, Pt nanoparticles-based SP superlattices showed a significant enhanced electrochemical HER performance with small gap widths between the strips of 5 m, yielding a small Tafel slope of 33 mV dec1 and an overpotential of 15 mV.136

Due to the good electric conductivity, layered structures, such as layered metal oxides, including metal hydroxides (M(OH)2, where M refers to transition metals) and metal oxyhydroxides (MOOH) showed high electrocatalytic OER activity.232 In particular, early 3d transition metal-based hydr(oxy)oxides, benefitting from their low overpotential in alkaline conditions, have attracted great attention for constructing cost-effective OER electrocatalysts.233 Furthermore, their layered double hydroxides (LDHs) showed comparable electrocatalytic efficiency to noble metals (such as IrO2).234

The PNSs constructed by layered metal oxides and other 2D layered materials can further enhance the electrocatalytic OER activity via synergistic effect.235 Graphene and its derivatives are good candidates to be the counterparts to construct multilayered PNSs with LDHs, since their good electrical conductivity,223 high mechanical strength,236 and large surface area237 provide good support to LDHs in catalysis.238 Ma et al. utilized NiMn239 and CoNi240 LDHs to construct layered PNSs with graphene oxide (GO) or reduced graphene oxide (rGO) for electrocatalytic OER. The NiMn/GO and NiMn/rGO PNSs were the first studies. Compared to the thickness of GO (1.0 nm), rGO nanosheets have a thinner thickness of 0.5 nm due to the oxygen groups having been partially removed. The difference in the thickness between GO and rGO induced the various interlayer spacings of NiMn/GO (1.1 nm) and NiMn/rGO (0.83 nm). Since the close distance between the layers can shorten the path and accelerate the charge transfer, NiMn/rGO showed better electrocatalytic OER activity with a smaller overpotential of 0.26 V and Tafel slope of 46 mV dec1, where the corresponding values of NiMn/GO are 0.28 V and 50 mV dec1 in alkaline condition (1 M KOH aqueous solution), respectively.239 Co(OH)2 has been widely used for catalysis. However, its inferior electrical conductivity is not satisfactory for being an electrocatalyst. Hence, different morphologies, atomic substitutions, and counterparts with high conductivity may significantly enhance the electrocatalytic activity of Co(OH)2. Layered CoNi hydroxides, with various ratios of Co and Ni (Co1xNix, where x = 0, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1), and GO-based PNSs (Co1xNix/GO) were studied for electrocatalytic OER. The Co2/3Ni1/3/GO showed excellent electrocatalytic OER activity with the overpotential of 259 mV and a Tafel slope of 35.7 mV dec1, which indicated that the rate-determining step is the transformation of M-O to M-OOH. The outstanding catalytic activity may be ascribed to the composition tunability, which optimizes the electronic structure and binding intermediates and catalysts.240 However, the catalytic activity of the CoNi-based catalyst may be improved by using rGO as a counterpart rather than GO, according to the previous study.239 To further develop the catalysts, there are various strategies, such as changing components of PNSs109 and exposing more active sites of catalysts.241 Despite the graphene and related materials having good properties for electrocatalysis, their corrosion in alkaline condition cannot be ignored.242 Therefore, the more robust and carbon-free RuO2.1, with high catalytic activity and low redox potential was used to replace the role of GO and rGO. Recently, the trimetallic CoNiFe LDHs and RuO2.1 nanosheets were used to assemble CoNiFe/RuO2.1 PNSs. Compared to the mono or bimetallic LDHs, trimetallic catalysts offered better OER catalytic activity via synergistic effect.243 Hence, the CoNiFe/RuO2.1 yielded an overpotential of 281 mV and a Tafel slope of 48.9 mV dec1.109 An increase of the exposure of active sites is a general strategy to improve the catalytic activity. Since the edges of LDHs are important sources of active sites for catalysis, the array of small LDH nanosheets can offer more active sites than the large LDH nanosheets with the same mass. Zhao et al. prepared periodic tiled/standing nanostructures (NiAl/GO) based on NiAl LDH and GO. The electrocatalytic activities were studied based on the different ratios of NiAl:GO (1:15:1 for NiAl/GO-1, 5.6:1 for NiAl/GO-2, 6:1 for NiAl/GO-3, 7:1 for NiAl/GO-4, and 8:110:1 for NiAl/GO-5, respectively), where NiAl/GO-5 showed the best electrocatalytic OER activity in 0.1 KOH aqueous solution with a Tafel slope of 118 mV dec1, and low overpotential of 200 mV (current density of 100 mA cm2) and 180 mV (current density of 50 mA cm2), respectively (Fig. 34). The good electrocatalytic activity of NiAl/GO-5 may be ascribed to: (1) the high ratio of NiAl:GO that provides more active sites; (2) the standing form of NiAl nanosheets that exposes more active sites, as compared with the tiling form.241 However, replacing the NiAl LDHs with other more active materials may yield enhanced catalytic activity.

The layer-by-layer method is a more controllable process to prepare PNSs with an accurate layer number, as required. Iron porphyrin (Fe-PP) is a good candidate for enhancing the charge transfer between LDHs and the electrode due to its extensive 2D 18 p-electrons. The thickness of the (CoNi/Fe-PP)n UTF increased by 2 nm per bilayer number, and its OER catalytic activity was continuously enhanced until the bilayer number n increased to 30, with the lowest overpotential of 264 mV and Tafel slope of 37.6 mV dec1 in 1.0 M KOH. Then, the corresponding activity decreased as a result of the excess bilayer number.104 The results indicate that the proper thickness of the periodic catalyst is necessary for efficient OER catalysis, and an excess thickness may hinder the charge transfer and mass diffusion, thereby influencing the catalysis activity.


4.1.1.1.3. Electrocatalytic overall water splitting. Electrocatalytic overall water splitting (OWS) is the integration of electrocatalytic HER and OER.244 Therefore, the catalysts for electrocatalytic OWS need to be rationally designed for both HER and OER. There are two possible strategies that can apply to develop PNSs to catalyze OWS: (1) assemble dispersed catalysts for OWS in good order to enhance the catalysis activity; (2) integrate various catalysts for HER and OER, respectively, into hetero PNSs to realize electrocatalytic OWS.

Featured with high surface area and abundant active sites, the well-ordered and close-packed nanoparticle-based 3D superlattices have attracted much attention for the first strategy to efficiently catalyze OWS. Nitrogen-doped carbon (CN)-coated CoMnO nanoparticles were studied as units for constructing CoMnO@CN nanoparticle superlattices. Herein, CoMnO cores offered an optimized electronic structure for electrocatalytic OER, where the CN shells showed good electronic structure for HER and enhanced electrical conductivity between neighboring nanoparticles. Therefore, the collective properties of the CoMnO@CN superlattices showed good electrocatalytic OWS with low overpotentials of 263 and 71 mV at 20 mA cm2 for OER and HER, respectively.37

A general strategy to fulfill the requirements for OWS is to utilize the collective properties of PNSs constructed with various materials with either electrocatalytic HER or OER activity. Ma et al. combined NiFe LDHs and GO/rGO for constructing Ni2/3Fe1/3/GO and Ni2/3Fe1/3/rGO, respectively, for water splitting. With the assistance of GO, Ni2/3Fe1/3/GO achieved good electrocatalytic OER activity with an overpotential of 0.23 V and a Tafel slope of 42 mV dec1. However, the Ni2/3Fe1/3/rGO demonstrated a slightly improved OER activity with a lower overpotential of 0.21 V and a smaller Tafel slope of 40 mV dec1, which can be ascribed to the better conductivity of rGO. In addition, the observed HER activity of Ni2/3Fe1/3/rGO indicated its ability for FWS catalysis.245 However, rGO is not an ideal candidate for HER catalysis, whereas TMDs, especially MoS2, showed excellent HER activity. In addition, DFT calculations predicted an enhanced charge transfer between the interlayer of LDH and TMD nanosheets, which may further improve the electrocatalytic activity. Hence, various PNSs based on NiAl, NiFe, and MoS2 were synthesized as area-balanced (NiAl/MoS2-1 and NiFe/MoS2-1), charge-balanced (NiAl/MoS2-2 and NiFe/MoS2-2), and LDH-excess (NiAl/MoS2-3 and NiFe/MoS2-3) compositions, respectively. The charge-balanced PNSs, NiAl/MoS2-2 (overpotential of 0.22 V and Tafel slope of 67 mV dec1 for HER, overpotential of 0.31 V and Tafel slope of 56 mV dec1 for OER) and NiFe/MoS2-2 (overpotential of 0.18 V and Tafel slope of 82 mV dec1 for HER, overpotential of 0.25 V and Tafel slope of 45 mV dec1 for OER), showed the best electrocatalytic activities for both HER and OER. According to X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), increased binding energies for Ni 2p, Fe 2p, and Al 2p, and a decreased binding energy for Mo 3d were observed. The oxidation of Ni, Fe, and Al ions were accompanied with a reduction of the Mo ions, demonstrating the charge transfer from LDHs to MoS2, which verified the prediction by DFT calculations.29 A recent study further revealed the importance of materials selection for building periodic nanostructure-based catalysts. Three different restacking PNSs, MoS2/NiFe, NiFe/Graphene, and MoS2/Graphene, were prepared. The NiFe/Graphene showed improved OER activity (Fig. 35a), and MoS2/Graphene showed enhanced HER activity (Fig. 35b) compared to sole NiFe and MoS2, respectively, due to the graphene-accelerated charge transfer for both NiFe/Graphene and MoS2/Graphene to further increase their catalytic activity. However, due to the significantly enhanced synergistic effect, MoS2/NiFe showed the best catalytic activity for both HER and OER with an overpotential of 0.11 V and Tafel slope of 77 mV dec1 for HER, and an overpotential of 0.21 V and Tafel slope of 46 mV dec1, indicating its high catalytic activity towards OWS (Fig. 35c).107

4.1.1.2.1. Photocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction. Photocatalytic HER yields from water splitting have three key steps: (1) photon absorption; (2) exciton separation and migration; (3) surface reaction and gas desorption. Various catalysts were designed to optimize these steps to further enhance the photocatalytic HER activity.248

Compared to the dense materials, porous semiconductors showed advantages for photocatalysis since the porous structure reduces the charge recombination rate of hole-electron pairs. This is due to the shortened distance between the reactive surface and the place where the pairs are generated. In addition, the cavities of the porous structure can accommodate other components to further improve their photocatalytic activity via synergistic effect. Therefore, Feng et al. synthesized a series of crystalline porous chalcogenide semiconductors based on In3+, S2, and one of the transition metal cations (such as Cd2+, Zn2+, Mn2+, and Cu+). ICF-5InCuS-Na (Na14In17Cu3S35xH2O), with a 3D diamond-type superlattice structure with a low band gap of 2.0 eV, is suitable for photocatalysis in the visible light region. ICF-5InCuS-Na showed good HER activity with 18 mol h1 g1 of H2 gas production in Na2S aqueous solution under visible light irradiation, which can be maintained for over 96 h with the accumulation of more than 890 mol of H2.249 Subsequently, Hu et al. synthesized a series of soluble supertetrahedral chalcogenido T4 clusters (T4-1, T4-2, and T4-3) with twisted diamond-type superlattice structures. T4-1 ((BMMim)9(Cd3In17S31Cl4), where BMMim refers to 1-butyl-2,3-dimethylimidazolium) and T4-3 (BMMim)9(Cd3In17Se31Cl4)(4,4-bpy), showed a twisted hexagonal diamond-type superlattice structure, whereas T4-2 ((BMMim)9(Cd3In17Se18Cl4)) showed a twisted cubic diamond-type superlattice structure. Compared to the band gaps of T4-1 (3.25 eV) and T4-2 (2.76 eV), the lowest band gap of 2.60 eV for T4-3 offered T4-3 the best photocatalytic activity with the H2 evolution rate of 15.9 mol h1 g1 in the solid state and 91.5 mol h1 g1 in DMSO, whereas corresponding values of T4-1 and T4-2 are 3.5 and 5.6 mol h1 g1 in the solid state, and 6.1 and 27.5 mol h1 g1 in DMSO (Fig. 37), respectively. Replacement of S with Se offered weak bond energy for the H-bonds and adjusted band structure, which enhanced the photocatalytic activity of the T4 clusters. However, besides the different components, the poor solubility of T4-1 limited further enhancement of the photocatalytic activity in DMSO, which also exhibited the importance to introduce Se to replace S for enhanced solubility.250

Different from photocatalytic HER and OER that have already been studied by experiments, studies on the photocatalytic OWS mainly stay at the theoretical calculations. However, these calculation results have demonstrated the great potential of the periodic nanostructure for photocatalytic OWS.

Various PNSs for photocatalytic OWS were studied via DFT and related calculation methods, regardless of the dimensions of materials. 4.1.2. CO2 reduction. The excess emission of carbon dioxide (CO2) since the industrial revolution via combustion of fossil fuels has become a significant environmental problem because the high concentration of CO2 induces a critical greenhouse effect. Besides the elimination of fossil fuels, the reduction CO2 to CO is a strategy for both renewable energy and carbon neutrality. However, the high dissociation energy of 750 kJ mol1 for the CO bond is a key barrier before the CO2 reduction reaction (CRR). Hence, various catalysts were designed with different solutions to overcome the barriers of CRR.256,257

AuCu bimetallic systems showed attractive electrocatalytic CRR activity, which is controlled by the ratio of each composition.258 Besides the ratio of each composition in the bimetallic system, the order of the structure also showed its unique contribution towards electrocatalytic activity by modulation of active site exposure and mass transfer.208 The electrocatalytic activity between the ordered and disordered materials can be significantly different. For instance, the disordered alloy AuCu bimetallic nanoparticles mainly produce H2 gas, whereas after the transformation from a disordered structure to ordered structure with alternate layers of Au and Cu, the ordered AuCu bimetallic nanoparticles (Fig. 39) showed significantly enhanced CRR activity with a high faradaic efficiency of 80%, a lower overpotential of 200 mV and an enhanced catalytic turnover by 3.2-fold.259

4.1.3. Fuel cells. A fuel cell is a chemical device that directly transfers chemical potential energy stored in fuels to electrical energy with high efficiency, and consists of a cathodic oxygen reduction reaction261,262 and anodic fuel oxidation reaction.263,264 During the reaction, fuel cells need a continuous supply of fuel and oxygen to maintain the chemical reaction. For better catalytic performances, many different materials were studied for electrodes for fuel cells, with good electronic properties. Periodic nanostructured materials are among the choices.
4.1.3.1. Oxygen reduction reaction (ORR). Oxygen reduction reaction is one of the most important parts for fuel cells. The most desirable pathway for the oxygen reduction reaction is the reduction of oxygen to water, which involves a direct 4-electron pathway. However, the sluggish cathodic ORR kinetics limit the performance of fuel cells. Therefore, various catalysts for accelerating the ORR process were designed and synthesized, aiming to further enhance the efficiency of fuel cells.

Pt-Based catalysts are widely studied for electrocatalytic ORR due to their extraordinary catalytic activity and stability, whereas their high cost and scarcity limit their practical applications. Hence, many efforts were devoted to developing new Pt-based catalysts for enhancing activity and stability, and minimizing the portion of Pt to reduce cost.261 It was reported that the presence of Pd could significantly promote the catalytic activity of Pt towards ORR by synergistic effect. This is ascribed to the modification of the Pt d-band centre by Pd through diminishing the electronic interaction between the exposed Pt and oxygenated species, and thereby improving ORR activity. The Pt and Pd nanocrystals-based 3D binary superlattices (BNSLs) showed superior electrocatalytic ORR activity compared to the Pt octahedra and PtPd random mixture.262 However, the use of alloy nanoparticles to construct superlattice arrays (SLAs) is the more general strategy. Wang et al. produced PtAu SLAs by using bimetallic PtAu nanoparticles. In comparison to the commercial Pt/C (50%, wt.%) and Pt black catalysts, the new PtAu SLAs exhibited enhanced electrocatalytic ORR activity with higher catalytic current density and more positive onset potential (0.903 V).264 However, the alloys based on two precious metals still suffer the limitation of high production cost, which may be solved by the introduction of earth-abundant metals to alloys. Hence, trimetallic PtCoFe alloy-based monolayer superlattices were prepared. This 2D PtCoFe superlattice displayed a high electrochemically active surface area (ESCA) of 62.9 m2 g1, which is comparable with 70.2 m2 g1, the ESCA of commercial Pt/C. The PtCoFe monolayer superlattice showed excellent electrocatalytic ORR activity with high specific activity and mass activity, which are 17.4- and 15.2-fold higher than the corresponding values of Pt/C (Fig. 40).265 Although these alloy-based superlattices have already demonstrated high electrocatalytic ORR activity, their catalytic activities can still be further enhanced via ordering the composition of alloy nanoparticles.208 For instance, structural ordered bimetallic PtFe266 and trimetallic PtNiPb267 alloy nanoparticles showed enhanced electrocatalytic ORR activities.

The development of a Pt-free catalyst for electrocatalytic ORR is the ultimate strategy to decrease the cost for their practical applications. Manganese oxides are potential precious metal free electrocatalysts for ORR. Especially Mn3O4, benefiting from its mixed valence of Mn, exhibits rich electrochemical properties. Therefore, carbon-coated tubular monolayer superlattices (TMSLs) were constructed by using hollow Mn3O4 nanocrystals (denote as h-Mn3O4-TMSLs). With all these benefits, h-Mn3O4-TMSLs showed excellent electrocatalytic ORR activity with a positive onset potential of 0.91 V and diffusion-limiting current density of 5.7 mA cm2, which are comparable to the corresponding values of Pt/C (0.90 V and 5.8 mA cm2).268 Featured with large surface areas and highly exposed active sites, 2D materials have always been good candidates for various catalysts, especially since their catalytic activity can be further enhanced via building 2D/2D PNSs due to the synergistic effect. 4.1.3.2. Ethanol oxidation reaction (EOR). Ethanol is a common liquid with low toxicity and low production cost. In comparison to compressed hydrogen, ethanol is more convenient for storage. With all these benefits, the ethanol-based fuel cell has attracted much attention from the scientific community. However, the process of the ethanol oxidation reaction is kinetically more difficult than that of the hydrogen oxidation reaction. Hence, various catalysts were developed to enhance the efficiency of the ethanol oxidation reaction.263,270273

Although the precious metal-based catalysts for EOR have many limitations for large-scale applications, several precious metal-based catalysts were developed due to their extraordinary catalytic activity.270273 Constructing alloys by using precious and non-precious metals is a general method that can be applied to decrease the production cost and enhance the catalytic activity. The aforementioned trimetallic PtCoFe alloy monolayer superlattices showed bifunctional catalytic activity for both ORR and EOR. This catalyst exhibited a peak current density of 4.75 mA cm2 and high mass activity, which are 17.1 and 3.8 times higher than commercial Pt/C benchmark, respectively.265 Like their ORR activity, the EOR activity of the PtCoFe alloy monolayer superlattices may also be enhanced via ordering of the PtCoFe nanoparticles.208


4.1.3.3. Methanol oxidation reaction (MOR). Methanol is another candidate for fuel cells. Methanol offers many advantages in fuel cells in comparison to ethanol, such as low CO2 emissions, and easier to catalyze since there is no CC bond in methanol to break. In addition, methanol can be easily synthesized from natural gas and from other renewable biomasses. Hence, intensive studies focused on the development of new catalysts have emerged for optimizing the MOR process.

Besides the composition, the structure of the catalyst also influences the ultimate catalytic activities. An ordered array of active metal alloy nanoparticles can demonstrate enhanced catalytic activity.208 For instance, the PtAu superlattice array showed good electrocatalytic MOR activity with a peak current density of 41.77 mA cm2, which is significantly higher than that of commercial Pt/C (13.91 mA cm2),264 whereas the study of the PtPd nanocrystal superlattices further confirmed the importance of the formation of PNSs for catalytic activity. Compared to the monodispersed PtPd nanocrystal nanoparticles, PtPd nanocrystal superlattices showed extraordinary MOR activity with ECSA of 46 m2 g1 and specific kinetic current density of 2.08 mA cm2.274 Crack-tips enriched PtCu superlattice nanoflakes (NFs) were employed as highly efficient anode electrocatalysts for MOR in methanol fuel cells. The PtCu NFs showed 4 times higher mass activity, 6 times higher specific activity and 6 times higher stability than those of the commercial Pt/C catalyst due to the synergistic effects of crack-tips and superlattice crystals.275

4.1.4. Thermoelectricity. With the increasing concern on the environmental protection situation, the invention and development of clean energy has become a key area of global scientific research, among which the thermoelectric conversion technology has been considered an efficient renewable energy technology due to its small size, high reliability, no emission of pollutants, and wide operating temperature range. Thermoelectric materials have the capacity to reversibly convert thermal energy to electric. The thermoelectric materials that combine n- and p- type materials use a temperature difference to generate electricity, and utilize electricity to accurately control temperature. The efficiency of a thermoelectric material is determined by the dimensionless figure of merit ZT = S2T/, where is the electrical conductivity, S is the Seebeck coefficient, T is the absolute temperature, and is the thermal conductivity and can be expressed with p+ e (for the phonon and the electronic contribution, respectively). To compete with other energy technologies, ZT is expected to be above 3, but this has not been realized so far. It seems that the simple strategy of increasing the power factor S2 and decreasing can improve the ZT value. However, those values often counteract each other. For example, the increasing of the electrical conductivity normally causes a decline of S and rise of e. Therefore, it is essential to optimize those parameters to achieve high ZT.

Nanoengineering has achieved many fruits to improve ZT by means of boundary scattering at the interface, where the thermal conductivity decreases without affecting the power factor too much. Recently, periodical nanostructures show much power to control those parameters through filtering low-energy carriers and magnifying the electron density of the states to increase the Seebeck coefficient, adjusting the carrier concentration and mobility to improve the electrical conductivity, and coherent phonon effects to further reduce the thermal conductivity at the sites of boundary scattering. In the following, we will summarize recent achievements on those periodical nanostructures of thermoelectrical materials.

In 2002, a substrate-free, bulk-like PbSeTe/PbTe quantum dot superlattice was fabricated by molecular beam epitaxy and a cooling device based on one-leg of this material was set up. A recorded ZT value of 2.0 was obtained even without optimized structure parameters, and this device cooled down to 43.7 K below room temperature (300 K).68 Lately, the ZT values higher than 4 and 6 are predicted for 5 nm PbSe/PbS and PbTe/PbSe superlattice nanowires at 77 K, respectively, by optimizing parameters such as the segment length, diameter, materials, and doping level. This work stated that the quantum confinement in the superlattice nanowires induced the potential barrierwell inversion as the electron mass differences between different materials.276 A ballistic thermoelectric transport was proposed in a superlattice nanowire. Because of the low temperature, dephasing from electronelectron and electronphonon scattering can be ignored, resulting in higher output power than a 3D superlattice material.277 However, for practical applications, we may need high superlattice nanowire density, where interwire transport needs to be considered, such as electron hopping strengths, energy levels, and electron tunneling rates.278 Those discoveries inspired the investigation of superlattices with other materials.

As an eco-friendly light-element-based thermoelectric material, C/Si/Ge superlattices are studied both theoretically and experimentally. The thermal conductivity was predicted to be decreased by 92% in Ge/Si superlattice nanowires compared to pure Si nanowires due to the interface phonon scattering and coherent effect.279 By optimizing the wire diameter and superlattice period, ZT values above 1 was proposed for 1.7 nm Ge/Si superlattice nanowires because of the enhanced Seebeck coefficient, unique electronic properties, and ultralow lattice thermal conductivity.280 Further doped Ge/SixGe1x superlattices exhibited enhanced ZT and power factor compared with both SiGe alloy and Si/Ge superlattice materials at room temperature because of the doping that increased the electrical conductivity.281 The optimized doping concentration in the SixGe1x barrier was calculated within the range of 1020%.282 When combined with graphene, the atomically thin C3Si3/C (C3Ge3/C) superlattice could achieve a ZT value of 1.95 (2.72) by tuning the carrier concentration.283 If a conducting polymer was alternately assembled with the layered SiGe alloy, the thermal conductivity was reduced much more than the electrical conductivity, resulting in a doubled ZT value.284 The decrease of the thermal conductivity on n-Ge/SixGe1x superlattices was experimentally observed.69,285,286 The doping concentration and strain in Si1xGex help to increase the Seebeck coefficients by tuning the energy band structure and neutralizing the negative interface effect on the electrical conductivity.69,286

For high temperature applications, the Al2O3 interlayer in the Al2O3ZnO (AO/ZnO) superlattice thin films was fabricated by atomic layer deposition.287 An enhancement of the Seebeck coefficient was observed due to the quantum confinement, whereas the in-plane thermal conductivity was reduced by 261 times compared to the bulk materials, despite the decrease of electrical conductivity. The overall ZT value was doubled compared with the AO/ZnO film.287 In terms of the cross-plane, the low energy carrier filtering and quantum confinement clearly enhance the power factor, producing ZT above 0.45 at 500 K after being combined with low thermal conductivity. This value is of 26 times improvement compared with the ZnO film.288 During the studies, new methods to measure all cross-plane thermal electric properties in superlattice films were developed, where a micro-Peltier heater and Cu foil/plates were used.289,290 They discovered that the Seebeck coefficients are not only related to the temperature, but also varied with the applied temperature difference.289 Furthermore, 6-cycled AO/ZnO superlattice films were tested with the highest ZT value of 0.45 at 500 K.290 After that, they applied this test method to measure the Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity of the p-type Bi2Te3/Bi0.5Sb1.5Te3 superlattice film, where a recorded ZT value of 1.44 was obtained.291 The Hf-doped ZnO superlattice films were also prepared with atomic layer deposition to obtain a net improvement in the power factor. Further introduction of 18O into the Hf:ZnO films suppressed the thermal conductivity without scarifying the electrical properties.292

Other oxides are also fabricated into superlattices. In [(NbOx)a/(Nb-doped SrTiO3)b]20 superlattices, a decrease in the layer thickness ratio (a/b ratio) would improve both Seebeck coefficient and electrical conductivity.293 For YBa2Cu3O72La2/3Ca1/3MnO3 superlattices, an interesting phenomenon was found, where its Seebeck coefficient is positive, while the two individual materials have negative Seebeck coefficients due to a long-range electronic reconstruction at the interfaces.294 The thermoelectric properties of a series of ABO3 compound superlattices were calculated with density functional theory and Boltzmann transport theory. The interface polarity at the LaNiO3/SrTiO3(001) superlattices leads to a band offset and the formation of an electric field, and then induced multiband splitting.295 A large positive Seebeck coefficient (135 V K1) and a high figure of merit (0.35) for room-temperature was predicted.295 In (LaNiO3)n/(LaAlO3)n(001) superlattices, high Seebeck coefficients of 600 V K1 were predicted under tensile strain.296 The epitaxial strain could tune the metal-to-insulator transition and the band velocities near the Fermi energy. They found that the thinner layer with n = 1 strongly enhanced the thermoelectric properties compared with n = 3 because the confinement induced Ni-site disproportionation.296 A similar phenomenon was also found in (SrCrO3)1/(SrTiO3)1(001) and (SrMnO3)1/(SrTiO3)1(001) superlattices with Seebeck coefficients of 500600 V K1 near the band edges.297

Another family of periodical thermoelectrical materials is the organicinorganic superlattices, which has been reviewed by Karppinen et al. in 2015.298 Herein, we will state the advances of organicinorganic superlattices after that. The main merit of this material is its flexibility and suitability for portable device fabrication. Usually, they are prepared by periodically combining the conductive polymer/organic molecules with other inorganic thermoelectrical materials, such as Bi2Se3/Bi2Te3, layered double hydroxides, TiS2 and others. There are different types of organicinorganic superlattices. For example, Bi2Te3 nanoparticles are monodispersed in the continuous conductive polymer matrix. The periodic nanofillers significantly scatter phonons, while the continuously conducting polymer phase provides high electronic transport, and this resulted in an excellent ZT value at room temperature.299 Different ladder-type heteroacene molecules in 2D gold nanoparticlemolecule superlattices modified the energy levels to determine the electrical conductivity and affected the Seebeck coefficient by adjusting the charge carrier in the arrays, and thus adjusting the PF.300 The periodic assembly of the layered double hydroxide layer structure with poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)-poly(styrene sulfonate) produced an organicinorganic alternately superlattice, in which the carrier mobility was improved to enhance the electrical conductivity. Furthermore, the low-energy carriers were filtered by sufficient interfaces between organic and inorganic phases, and thus promote the Seebeck coefficient, resulting in improved power factor.301

A general method was developed to synthesize flexible thermoelectric materials through a facile electrochemical intercalation.302 The organic cations intercalated into the TiS2 layers to form a hybrid superlattice of alternating inorganic TiS2 monolayers and hexylammonium ions (Fig. 41). The lattice thermal conductivity was reduced by two orders of magnitude due to stronger phonon scattering, and the electrical conductivity was increased due to the increased carrier density in the hybrid material. The electron mobility is not affected because the Coulomb force can be effectively screened by the polar water molecules and the positive hexylammonium ions. As a result, a high ZT of 0.28 at 373 K was obtained. Furthermore, the mechanical flexibility is even lower than that of plastic materials, such as Teflon. This method can be possibly used to fabricate other organicinorganic superlattices using many other layered material candidates, such as Bi2Te3 or the transition metal dichalcogenides and various organic molecules with different functional groups.302 Inspired with the above work, the Bi2Se3/organic superlattice with enhanced ZT value was prepared with multiple steps, including lithium intercalation, ionic exchange, and organic exchange.303

For thermoelectrical applications with periodic nanostructures, many parameters need to be optimized, such as the period length, height, and shape of the periodic potential barriers.304 Other effects, such as the surface roughness304 and applied fields (e.g., magnetic field),305 may also play important roles in determining the thermoelectric properties. 4.1.5. Solar cells. As one of the most important applications in energy conversion, solar cells have been developed for decades, and they are widely applied to various areas. Along with the development, different types of solar cells are also studied, such as IIIV GaAs solar cells, quantum dots solar cells, dye-sensitized solar cells and perovskite solar cells, aiming at high solar power conversion efficiency (PCE) and stability for industrial manufacturing and real-life application. During the development of solar cells, PNSs contribute to their unique properties, leading to the improvement of solar cells, which will be summarized in this section.
4.1.5.1. GaAs based solar cells. The GaAs-based solar cell is one of the most important branches of solar cell studies. According to the report from National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), GaAs solar cell has the top conversion rate,308 with strong plasticity, good temperature resistance, and good sensitivity to light. The PNSs offer better performance to this type of solar cell.

GaAs PNSs are intensively studied for the enhanced optoelectrical properties in comparison to their bulk counterparts.309 Lattice-matched InGaAsP/InGaP superlattices were grown with alternate layers of In0.32Ga0.68As60P40 well, and the In0.49Ga0.51P barriers were fabricated into solar cells with good performance (Fig. 42a).33 The EQE at wavelengths beyond the band-edge of In0.49Ga0.51P was greatly improved with increased periods, as shown in Fig. 42b. In this report, the carrier escape mechanism of the superlattice structure was studied. It was found that the carrier tunneling and thermionic emission coexisted in the system, while the carrier tunneling played the dominant role with a thin barrier, as indicated in Fig. 42a. However, for the strain-balanced InGaAsP/InGaP structure, the tunneling probability was zero due to a relatively thicker barrier.310 Under the coexistence of carrier tunneling and thermionic emission, the EQE was improved because carriers are more likely to escape from the quantum well (QW) region and promote the photocurrent.33 Their solar cell device showed further enhanced EQE at resonance peaks beyond the band-edge, with the FabryProt resonance induced by the gold back surface reflector. Resulting from the high EQE in the sub-bandgap, the JSC was improved by 26% compared with the standard InGaP solar cells (Fig. 42c). The VOC in one sun condition was 0.08 V higher than that of the bulk InGaAsP solar cells. The resultant JSC, VOC, FF, and PCE of the device with back surface reflector and antireflection coating were 20.5 mA cm2, 1.136 V, 75.3%, and 17.6%, respectively. It also confirmed the positive effect of the back surface reflector and the antireflection coating on the device performance. However, the optimization on the lattice-matched superlattices are desired to increase the relatively low JSCJSC and FF. Recently, a single-junction p-i-n GaAs solar cell fabricated with low-cost flexible metal foils was reported.311 By optimizing the thickness of the intrinsic layer, the best device achieved a PCE of 13.2%, showing great opportunity for enhancing the device performance by modifying the GaAs layer.

Fundamental studies on the carrier dynamics of periodic structure based-solar cells also revealed the great potential for performance enhancement. Aeberhard et al. performed a quantum-kinetic simulation to investigate the carrier transport in the GaAsSb/GaAsN type-II QW superlattice solar cells.34 It was reported that the electron and hole states were localized with increased period thickness, reflecting different carrier transportations with various period thicknesses. At the periodic thickness (dper) of 3 nm, photocarriers proceeded quasi-ballistically, which converted to sequential tunneling with a thermal component when the dper was increased to 12 nm. The transition of the carrier extraction from quasi-ballistic to sequential tunneling resulted in a significant drop in the carrier extraction efficiency (Fig. 42d and e). This work provided a theoretical background in optimizing the design of the GaAsSb/GaAsN superlattice towards efficient carrier extraction in solar cells.


4.1.5.2. Quantum dots solar cells. Quantum dots (QDs) are the sub-nanometre sized semiconducting nanoparticles, which show tunable electronic and optical properties through controlling the size of individual QDs or periodic structure of the QDs array due to the quantum mechanisms favored by solar cells. The parameters, such as the radius, dot-to-dot spacing and number of layers have a profound effect on the device performance.312 Computational simulations revealed that the PCEs of QD solar cells (QDSCs) are highly dependent on the structure of QDs. For Ge/Si QDSCs, QDs with a large volume have a stronger quantum confinement effect and lower ground-state energy, while a large thickness of QDs has a negative effect on the PCE. An optimum PCE of 27.22% was predicted for bilayer conical QD superlattices (QDSLs) with optimized size.

As mentioned, introducing PNSs could induce a miniband in the energy band, which significantly influences the carrier transport. The minibands could also form in closely-stacked QD through coupling the electronic states along the stacked directions.313 Sho and coworkers demonstrated that the miniband in InAs/GaAs QDSCs had the effect of spatially separating carriers in the built-in field, which subsequently increased the carrier lifetime and inter-subband absorption strength.314 In particular, the formation of a miniband significantly enhanced the two-step photocarrier generation. The temperature-dependent time-resolved-PL decay results revealed that a miniband was formed above 40 K. The photocurrent caused by the inter-subband excitation significantly increased with the formation of a miniband at temperatures above 40 K, which evidenced the effective carrier separation in the miniband. Lee et al. first reported a layer-by-layer method to fabricate distance-controlled CdTe QDSLs with modulated dimensions of the quantum resonance.315 Simply changing the concentration of the QD solution, the distances between the quantum dots were controlled, thus achieving the dimension modulation of 1D, 2D-monolayer and 3D-multilayer QD superlattices. Quantum resonance was achieved in 3D with high in-plane QD density, which means that the quantum resonance not only occurred in the stacking direction. Fig. 43ad shows the excitation energy-dependent PL spectra of the CdTe solution and QD superlattices. Obviously, no PL peak shift was observed for the QD superlattices, while the peak positions shifted to lower energies with decreased excitation energy for the QD solution with no quantum resonance. This evidenced the formation of minibands in the CdTe QDSL with different dimensions. Introducing highly mismatched GaSb QD superlattices into bulk GaAs solar cells were illustrated to have the effect of preserving the VOC in the solar cells.316 By using the intermediate band material QD with a partially filled intermediate band, the sub-bandgap photon absorption was increased, which consequently enhanced the overall performance by 11.43% compared with the bulk single-junction GaAs solar cells.

Tanibuchi and coworkers applied time-of-flight spectroscopy to investigate the carrier transport dynamics in InAs/GaAs QDSL.317 They observed a rapid carrier flow across the cell under the excitation with high power density, resulting from the state filling effect in the QDSCs. This work demonstrated a direct way to investigate the ultra-fast carrier dynamics of the QDSL with a probe structure in the quantum well structure, revealing fundamental knowledge. The studies on the carrier dynamics of the QDSL SCs address the importance of band engineering for efficient SCs, for which the band tuning effect of the periodic nanostructure is one of the strategies.

Hot carrier solar cells can utilize the excess energy of hot carriers to generate power, providing a great potential to increase the PCE. The low-dimensional periodic-nanostructure system has been demonstrated to have slowed carrier thermalization.318 The performance of hot-carrier QDSCs shows the dependence on the period of the periodic structures as well.319Fig. 43d illustrates the device structure of InAs/GaAs QDSL SCs with the superlattice period of 5 and 1. The light absorption of the device cell increased with a higher period, which subsequently improved the hot carrier extraction-induced VOC and JSC (Fig. 43e). The tunnel-assisted carrier escape is also suppressed in high-period QDSL as the adjacent superlattice re-traps the hot electrons, extracted to the conduction band edge.


4.1.5.3. Dye-sensitized solar cells. The dye-sensitized solar cell (DSSC) has been developed through mimicking photosynthesis in nature. Its easy-fabrication, low cost and high performance have attracted great research interests.320,321 The typical DSSCs consist of two primary reactions, which are photoexcitation of the dye by an oxidation reaction on photoanodes and the regeneration of the dye by a reduction reaction on counter electrodes, respectively. The counter electrode transfers electrons to the electrolyte for further process. Pt is the most commonly used material for counter electrodes in DSSCs. However, it is easy to be corroded and the cost is quite high.322 Periodic-nanostructured materials with unique properties are under the investigation for electrode materials. In 2014, Gratzel's group achieved a PCE of 13% with porphyrin dye-based dye-sensitized solar cell.323 Graphene nanoplatelets on FTO glass were employed as the counter electrode material. Wang et al. synthesized a superlattice counter electrode material with highly ordered P2Mo18/MoS2 superlattice nanosheets on C nanoparticles (P2Mo18/MoS2@C).324 The superlattice structure is depicted in Fig. 44a. A 1D interface was formed between the 2D nanosheet and the 3D nanoparticles, resulting in a large specific surface area of 174.2 m2g1, providing more catalytic active sites. In this hierarchical structure, the electron transport kinetics improved. Cyclic voltammetry measurement and electrochemical Impedance spectroscopy revealed that the superlattice material showed prominent properties of high reduction peak current density, a low peak-to-peak separation and high catalytic capability and high diffusion rate of I3, compared with Pt. A PCE of 8.85% was achieved by the DSSC with this material, while their Pt reference device demonstrated a PCE of 7.37% under AM 1.5G illumination (Fig. 44b).

4.1.5.4. Perovskite solar cells. Perovskite has attracted much attention over the last decade due to its superior optoelectrical properties. A record PCE of 25.5% has been achieved for single-junction perovskite solar cells, which is comparable to that of the silicon-based solar cells.308 A 2D perovskite with periodic quantum-well structure, which consists of alternately arranged metal halide layers and organic layers, exhibits better stability over the 3D perovskite materials, offering a great potential for solving the stability issue of the 3D counterparts.325 The RuddlesdenPopper (RP) phase 2D perovskites have the general formula of A2An1MnX3n+1, where the A site is occupied by a large alkylammonium cation, A represents the monovalent organic cation, M is occupied by a divalent metal cation, X is a halide, and n is the number of metal halide monolayer sheets between two neighboring organic layers. For the Dion-Jacobson (DJ) phase 2D perovskite, diammonium cations occupy the A site, forming the formula of AAn1MnX3n+1. By varying n, the thickness of each perovskite layer can be tuned.

In 2016, Hsinhan and coworkers adopted the hot-casting technique to fabricate perovskite solar cells based on the RP phase 2D mixed-cation (BA)2(MA)3Pb4I13 perovskite (n = 4) (BA: butylamine, MA: methylamine).326 The solar cell device with the structure of FTO/PEDOT:PSS/(BA)2(MA)3Pb4I13/PCBM/Al demonstrated a peak PCE of 12.52%, a VOC of 1.01 V, a JSC of 16.76 mA cm2 and a FF of 74.13%. The 2D-based devices were far more stable than 3D solar cells. The unencapsulated 2D device experienced 20% of degradation in PCE after 200 hours of light illumination, while the 3D device only retained 40% of its original PCE after 24 hours. Their (BA)2(MA)2Pb3I10 perovskite (n = 3) device with the same device structure had a PCE of 11.44%, a VOC of 1.056 V, a JSC of 14.37 mA cm2 and a FF of 75.39%, which was inferior to the (BA)2(MA)3Pb4I13 (n = 4) device because the insulating organic layer inhibits the carrier transport in the out-of-plane direction. Constantinos et al. successfully fabricated a (BA)2(MA)4Pb5I16 perovskite (n = 5)-based solar cell with the architecture of FTO/PEDOT:PSS/(BA)2(MA)4Pb5I16/PCBM/Al.22 The corresponding PCE achieved 8.71% with a VOC of 1.0 V, a JSC of 11.44 mA cm2 and a FF of 75.59%. However, the mixed-cation perovskite solar cells were not comparable to pure 3D phase solar cells in PCE, mostly due to the large bandgap.

Generally, the optoelectrical properties of the RP-phase perovskite would be improved when increasing the n value from 1 to 4, but deterioration occurs with higher n values. The mixed phases with different n values would lead to the structure distortion and, consequently, poor film quality. The coexistence of 2D and quasi-3D phase were reported in NpMA (1-naphthalenemethylammonium) and AnMA (9-anthracenemethylammonium)-based A2(MA)2Pb4I13 perovskite films (n = 4).327 Diffraction rings and Bragg spots were both observed in the GIWAXS patterns (Fig. 45a), implying the disordered orientation. The existence of several phases was clearer in the HRTEM image, in which various lattice spacings were found in different regions (Fig. 45b).

In the RP phase 2D perovskite, the two adjacent organic layers interact with each other by van der Waals force, while in in the DJ phase, the relatively weak force is substituted by the hydrogen bonding between two amino groups, having the potential to reinforce the structure stability. Li et al. reported on an ultra-stable DJ phase (PDA)(MA)3Pb4I13 (PDA+: 1,3-propanediamine) 2D perovskite solar cell.31 The molecular structure is illustrated in Fig. 46a. The amino groups at both ends of the PDA2+ bound with the inorganic frame by hydrogen bonding. The optoelectrical properties of (PDA)(MA)n1PbnI3n+1 with n = 1, 2, 3, 4 were characterized. With increased n values, a broader light absorption was observed (Fig. 46b), as the bandgaps were reduced from 2.46 eV for n = 1 to 1.63 eV for n = 4, suggesting a tunable bandgap. Through TRPL and EIS measurements, it was found that the carrier extraction of the DJ-based device at the FTO/TiO2 side was more efficient, and the charge recombination resistance is over two times larger than the RP-based device. The (PDA)(MA)3Pb4I13-based solar cell showed ultrahigh stability, which retained 95% of the original efficiency after the exposure to ambient air for 4000 hours (Fig. 46d) and to light illumination for 3000 hours. Shi et al. introduced asymmetric diammonium DMAPA (DMAPA: 3-(dimethylammonium)-1-propylammonium) into the perovskite structure and synthesized (DMAPA)MA3Pb4I13 with n = 4.329 The solar cell device with the structure of FTO/TiO2/(DMAPA)MA3Pb4I13/Spiro-OMeTAD/Au exhibited the performance of a PCE of 15.16%, a VOC of 1.06 V, a JSC of 22.07 mA cm2 and a FF of 65% (Fig. 46e). 80% of its initial PCE was maintained after the 300 hours under AM 1.5G illumination in air. Bat-El reported on the DJ-phase perovskite with a high n value of 10, (BzDA)(Cs0.05MA0.15FA0.8)9Pb10(I0.93Br0.07)31 (BzDA:1,4-benzenedimethanamonium).330 BzDA consists of diammonium groups and an aromatic ring, which could stabilize the molecular structure into an arranged orientation, and benefit the carrier transport with free -electrons. Intensity-modulated photocurrent and photovoltage spectroscopies (IMVS/IMPS) and transient photovoltage and photocurrent (TPV/TPC) measurement revealed that this 2D perovskite exceeded the 3D perovskite ((Cs0.05MA0.15FA0.8)Pb(I 0.93Br0.07)3) in its recombination lifetime, charge collection efficiency, carrier diffusion length and carrier extraction under bias, mostly contributed from the molecular structure of BzDA. The best performance of solar cell devices in a mesoporous configuration, which is illustrated in Fig. 46f, displayed a PCE of 15.6%, a VOC of 1.02 V, a JSC of 21.5 mA cm2 and a FF of 71% (Fig. 46g). The 2D-based device exhibited better stability than the 3D-based device, which retained 80% of the initial PCE after 84 hours under the dark in an ambient environment.

The 2D perovskite has demonstrated great potential for ultra-stable solar cells, but the enhancement in PCE is highly desired. Generally, 2D perovskites with n = 1 are not suitable for solar cell devices despite the high stability. This is because of the inefficient carrier transport limited by the strong quantum-well effect,331 while the high n values decrease the stability due to increased 3D compositions. So, the rational design of a perovskite structure with optimized n value should be considered. It is worth noting that during the synthesis, the phase purity is critical. A mixed structure with multiple n values would lead to low film crystallinity with low uniformity and pinholes, which is detrimental to device performance.

4.2. Optoelectronic and electronic devices

Forming PNSs is a novel approach to manipulate the optical and electronic properties, compared with the conventional doping strategy. By exploiting the talent of the PNSs, photodetectors, light-emitting diodes (LEDs), memory devices have been realized by a lot of groups, which will be discussed under this subtitle.
4.2.1. Photodetector. Photodetectors can convert light signal into electrical signal, which are widely used for imaging, security, and sensing purposes. Since the periodic nanostructured semiconductors demonstrate improved optoelectronic properties compared to their bulk competitors, they are able to serve as excellent candidates for photodetectors.

Type-II superlattices are suitable for infrared detection due to the tunable band structure towards a small band gap, reduced Auger recombination,332 enhanced light absorption due to the overlap of the electronhole wavefunction and excellent material quality.333 Razeghi and coworkers reported on the InAs/InAs1xSbx type-II superlattice nBn photodetector for its long wavelength infrared detection, comprising two n-doped superlattice active regions and a conduction-band-barrier.334 A thin layer of AlAs was introduced into the middle of the InAs layers, which separated the InAs quantum well and induced the conduction-band-barrier due to the large bandgap of AlAs. The barrier had a continuous valence band alignment with the n-doped active regions so that the diffusion of the holes was allowed, while the transportation of electrons was blocked. The device based on the InAs/InAs0.45Sb0.55 type-II superlattices exhibited a 50% cut-off wavelength of 10 m at 77 K, which achieved the best responsivity (R) of 3.47 A W1 under front side illumination without anti-reflection coating. The dark current density was characterized to be 4.4 104 A cm2 at 90 mV bias at 77 K. The detectivity (D*) of the device stayed almost constant in the wavelength range from 5 m to 9.5 m at low biases between 100 mV and 50 mV, suggesting the potential in infrared imaging applications. InAs/InAsSb type-II superlattices are also applied to mid-infrared photodetectors. Evangelia et al. reported an innovative nBn-structure photodetector in which superlattices were grown in GaSb/AlSb/Si buffer by heteroepitaxial growth.335 AlSb/GaSb dislocation filters were used to introduce additional strain to promote the growth of GaSb on Si. Fig. 47a is the schematic diagram of the nBn-structure device, demonstrating a mid-infrared response (Fig. 47b). In the spectral range from 3.7 to 4.9 m, the device presented a R of 0.88 A W1, an EQE of 25.6%, a D* of 1.5 1010 Jones and a 50% cut-off wavelength at 5.4 m under 100 mV bias at 200 K (Fig. 47c).

Photodetector devices were fabricated on p-type Si semiconductors with a SiO2 layer (Fig. 48c), which exhibited an on/off ratio of 105, a R of 2.5 103 A W1, an EQE of 8.6 105 and a response time of 10 ms at 5 V bias under 365 nm excitation with the light intensity of 3.75 mW cm2. Single GaN nanowires with 20 periods of the AlN/GaN:Ge heterostructure in the AlN shell were developed for visible-band photodetectors (Fig. 48g).32 Due to the coalescence of nanowires with a displacement of the GaN:Ge nanodisks, higher dark current and lower photosensitivity factors were observed as the effective superlattice length was reduced by a smaller number of the active superlattice periods (Fig. 48g). This work indicates that the coalescence of nanowires would reduce the homogeneity, influencing the device performance and leading to low reproducibility. Maria and coworkers reported the single-nanowire photodetector based on GaN with 30 periods of GaN/AlN superlattices with good homogeneity.336 A highly-n-doped GaN capping segment was introduced into the structure, leading to a bias-dependent light response. As illustrated in Fig. 48h, there were two built-in electric fields existing in the system that were induced by the polarization differences between the GaN stem and the superlattice, and between the superlattice and the GaN cap. Under positive biases, a photocurrent was favorably generated in the GaN/AlN superlattice (Fig. 48j-i), while under negative biases, the collection of carriers in the GaN stem was favored (Fig. 48j-ii). As a result, the device showed enhanced responses in the 280330 nm range at positive biases, and in the 330360 nm range at negative biases (Fig. 48i).

Ho et al. fabricated a InGaZnO superlattice nanowire-based UV photodetector,337 where the superlattice structure consisted of In, Ga/ZnO layers and InO layers. They tuned the material properties by varying the Ga concentration in the superlattices, which subsequently changed the oxygen states with the reduced oxygen vacancies. The best photodetector performance was obtained with In1.8Ga1.8Zn2.4O7 superlattices, delivering an on/off ratio of 1.9 105, a R of 1.95 105 A W1, an EQE of 9.28 107% and a rise time of 0.93 s at 1 V bias under 261 nm excitation with the light intensity of 2.3 mW cm2. Interestingly, a transparent photodetector device was developed, which showed a transparency of over 80% in the visible light range. Wei et al. demonstrated highly-sensitive superlattice structure InSe-based photodetectors.192 At a bias of 1 V, their device showed a high R of 1.7 104 A W1, a D* of 1.4 1013 Jones, an EQE of 1.7 107% and a response time of 20 ms, which is much better than the performance of pristine InSe-based photodetector devices. The superior device performance was largely contributed from the lower contact resistance, which was 2.4 k, 210 times lower than that of pristine InSe.

Periodic-nanostructure integrated perovskite materials also present great potential in photodetector applications. In Li's work, a 1D nanograting bonded porous 2D photonic crystal perovskite photodetector demonstrated a R of 12.67 A W1 and a D* of 3.22 1013 Jones at 10 V bias, under white light illumination of 32 W cm2, which was 67 times higher than the typical perovskite film-based device with a R of 1.82 A W1 and a D* of 5.11 1012 Jones.198 This enhancement in performance resulted from the improved light absorbance by introducing highly ordered nanogratings and 2D porous structure. However, due to the limitation of the measurement set up, the response time was as long as 21 ms for the rise time and 67 ms for the decay time.

4.2.2. Light-emitting diodes. A light-emitting diode (LED) is a semiconductor light source, which emits when a certain current flows through it. It obtains several advantages over the traditional technology in terms of lower power consumption, longer lifetime, faster switching and other properties. The PNSs show great potential in the LED applications for its band-modulating capability.

Fig. 49a illustrates the device structure of a deep ultraviolet (DUV) LED, of which the n-type confinement layer consisted of stepped and 2 period superlattice structure of n-type Al0.6Ga0.4N/Al0.57Ga0.43N.338 The superlattices showed the band-modulating effect on the device. As shown in Fig. 49b, the introduction of a superlattice lowered the effective barrier height of the carriers, which subsequently increased the carrier concentration with efficient carrier injection, guaranteeing the high radiative recombination rate. The electrostatic fields in the QWs were also decreased, enhancing the confinement and reducing carrier leakage in the active region. The DUV LED had an emission peak at 289 nm with the maximum IQE of 53.9% and a light output power of 39.52 mW, which were improved by two times of those of the simple device (26.8% and 19.46 mW, respectively). Efforts were also made to modify the intermediate p-region in the DUV LEDs. Ramit et al. specially designed superlattice quaternary nitride alloy materials for the p-region.339 The material compositions are illustrated in Fig. 49c. All devices had an emission peak at 272.96 nm. The DUV LED device of structure D with p-Al0.62In0.03Ga0.35N/p-Al0.5In0.03Ga0.47N superlattice material with 5 periods demonstrated the best device performance. Its output power was 28.3 times that of the conventional DUV LED with structure A at the current density of 200 A cm2, and the maximum internal quantum efficiency (IQE) achieved was 93.92%, as shown in Fig. 49d and e. Especially, the DUV LED in the structure had an efficiency drop of 0.84%, lowered by 99.08% in comparison to the conventional structured device, contributed from the lower energy barrier for the holes. A rational design of the composition and the period structure of the periodic nanostructure material provide more chances of performance enhancement.

At the same time, the band modulation also improved the hole transport, resulting in enhanced light output power (248 mW at 500 mA) and reduced dynamic resistance (0.94 ). 4.2.3. Memory devices. Memory is one of the key components of electronic devices for information technology. Especially, random resistive access memory (RRAM) has been acknowledged as a promising candidate for the next-generation non-volatile memories. The RRAM is operated through resistive switching behaviors occurring between the high resistance state (HRS) and low resistance state (LRS), originating by the formation or deformation of a conductive filament across the dielectric materials or the generation of oxygen vacancies due to oxygen atom diffusion from the oxide-based dielectric materials.341343 In designing RRAM devices, PNSs have attracted major interests due to its improved properties compared to their mixed counterparts, such as significant power efficiency and switching properties. The bipolar resistive-switching characteristics were observed in LA0.7Sr0.3MnO3/SrRuO3 superlattices.344 A planar-structure memory device with 30 layers of LA0.7Sr0.3MnO3/SrRuO3 presented a memory window, which was described by an on/off ratio of 14, and an endurance of 3040 cycles. As an excellent semiconductor material, halide perovskites have also been employed in RRAM devices. Wang et al. first reported the memory application of the mixed-halide perovskite MAPbI3xClx.345 The device with the structure of Au/MAPbI3xClx/FTO showed a bipolar resistive switching (RS) characteristic at low bias (<1 V), keeping an endurance of over 100 cycles and a retention time of 104 s. However, the on/off ratio was relatively small, making it difficult to distinguish the storage information. A multi-level memory device was developed on layered 2D perovskite (BzA)2CuBr4 (BzA: benzylamine), which achieved an on/off ratio as high as 108.346Fig. 50a is the IV-curve of the voltage sweep of the device Ag/PMMA/(BzA)2CuBr4/Pt, of which the memory window is large. Furthermore, the device showed reliable HRS and LRS characteristics at different compliance currents, confirming its capacity for multi-level storage (Fig. 50b). The good stability of the 2D perovskites also equipped the device with high endurance of 2000 cycles and a retention time of over 1000 s.

Interfacial phase change memory (iPCM) is another type of memory device, which is driven by the changes of the optoelectrical properties of the material with different phases. It was reported that the arrangement of GeTeSb into a superlattice structure can reduce the switching power consumption, as well as improve the electrical properties.347 Mitrofanov et al. demonstrated a high-speed iPCM device based on the GeTe/SbTe superlattices.348 The device structure is shown in Fig. 50c. In this case, there were two polarity-sensitive structural states in the GeTe/SbTe superlattices that could be tuned by the applied bias, leading to the bipolar switching. The transition between LRS and HRS originated from the switching of GeTeGeTe/TeGeTeGe stacking and TeGeGeTe stacking (Fig. 50d).349 The superlattice device had an endurance of 104 cycles and a retention time of over 200 hours. Interestingly, the switching speed of the superlattice device was 1000 times faster than that of the conventional GeSbTe alloy-based iPCM (Fig. 50e).

5. Challenges and opportunities

In this review, we have discussed the advances in periodic nanostructured materials, focusing on their preparation methods, properties and applications in energy conversion, optoelectronics and electronics. PNSs provide a versatile platform for property modulation and various applications due to their influence on the properties at the atomic and molecular level, collective properties of the corresponding compositions, and synergistic effects. The examples discussed in this review have already showcased their great potential for advanced applications. However, there are still challenges that need to be overcome by providing great opportunities for further studies, and even towards commercialization as summarized below.

(1) The preparation strategies cover a wide range of methods, including vapour phase, solution phase and other complex methods. However, most of these methods suffered from flaws of low production efficiency, uncontrollable morphology and stability issues. For instance, the CVD method can precisely control the morphology, but it is hard to produce lateral PNSs in a large scale; the complex preparation methods used to synthesis DNA-mediated PNSs and most of the moir superlattices result in low-scale production; the substrate-free self-assembly method cannot precisely control the repeating layers of 2D layered PNSs; and basically every supramolecular assembled materials will face the mechanical strength issue since the nature of non-covalent interactions cannot provide a permanent structure with high durability. Hence, in preparing PNSs, there are opportunities for future studies to either integrate the current preparation methods or develop new methods to balance these shortcomings. For instance, adding coatings to the self-assembled PNSs can permanently maintain their periodic structures. Introducing a microfluidics technique to prepare the supramolecular assembled PNSs can more precisely control the concentration and ratio of each component in a relatively small space, thereby controlling the composition of PNSs, as well as preserving the large-scale production.

(2) A variety of emergent properties have been investigated in various types of PNS, often properties that are absent in their parent compounds. However, the constituents of PNS are mostly restricted to one type of material, e.g., oxide superlattices, with the exception of hybrid organic-2D PNS. It will be interesting to further explore the formation of PNS by combining different classes of materials, e.g., 2D van der Waals materials and 2D metal oxides in terms of inducing new emergent properties by harnessing the synergistic effect. In the organicinorganic hybrid PNS, the real atomic structure of the organic intercalation layer remains to be clarified. This is in stark contrast to, for example, oxide superlattices in which TEM provides an accurate description of the interfacial atomic arrangements. In addition, the intrinsic properties of the organic species are less explored in the hybrid PNS. It will be interesting to introduce the molecular species with emergent properties, such as magnetism, to combine with the 2D van der Waals layers to exploit the interfacial coupling for new functionalities. In terms of forming novel PNSs, besides the adjustment of compositions or dimensions, the idea of twisting the bilayer graphene with a certain angle opens up a new avenue to induce the emergent superconducting state or strongly correlated phases. This intriguing architecture greatly inspires researchers to try more 2D materials, such as TMDs. However, the fabrication of such structures remains laborious with very low device yields, placing significant challenges for the practical applications of twistronics. Innovations in the fabrication technology for the precise assembly of 2D van der Waals materials with arbitrary sequence and rotation are desired, for example, high throughput automation enabled through robotic arms and machine-learning approaches.

(3) The extraordinary properties of PNSs offer great potential for various applications, and current studies have already demonstrated their unique contribution. For instance, overall water splitting needs to integrate both HER and OER catalytic activities into one catalyst, which is the strength of PNSs since they can collect properties from each composition and enhance the performance via synergistic effects. Introducing PNSs with non-precious compositions to catalysts for fuel cells can reduce the ratio of precious metals in catalysts to further decrease their costs, and enhance their catalytic activity via synergistic effects. Importantly, the tunable optoelectrical properties from the easy-to-modulate periodic structure provide more opportunities to fabricate high-performance applications. By introducing PNSs, enhancements of the carrier transport and light absorption could be achieved for improving the performance of solar cells, LEDs, photodetectors and other optoelectronic devices. The modulation of bandgaps of PNSs can be well suited to the demand of photochemical catalysts, which is also favored in the fabrication of LEDs with desired emission peaks and photodetectors with specific detection range. However, an in-depth understanding of the effect of PNSs for various applications is still limited. For example, studies of the carrier dynamics in 2D perovskite materials are highly required. Although 2D perovskites exhibit superior stability over their 3D counterparts, the quantum confinement still hinders the PCE of solar cells. Performance breakthrough relies on the precise modulation of the optoelectrical properties for PNSs with solid knowledge of the structure.

In summary, faced with all of the benefits, challenges and opportunities of PNSs, the up-to-date studies have resulted in many breakthroughs. However, these breakthroughs are inadequate, and there still exists the gap between laboratory research and industrial applications. After comprehensive review and constructive presentation in this review, we hope to shed some light on the further advancement of next-generation PNSs to be built-up on novel advanced materials for the scientific communities of this specific research area. With further synergistic combination of the lateral development in data-driven materials discoveries, in situ microscopies and spectroscopies, and high throughput manufacturing, the development of PNS science and technology should be further accelerated in the near future.
