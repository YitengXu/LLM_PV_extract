Abstract

There is currently a large effort to improve the performance of low cost renewable energy devices. Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are emerging as one of the most promising low cost photovoltaic technologies, addressing secure, clean and efficient solar energy conversion. Vegetable dyes, extracted from algae, flowers, fruit and leaves, can be used as sensitizers in DSSCs. Thus far, anthocyanin and betalain extracts together with selected chlorophyll derivatives are the most successful vegetable sensitizers. This review analyses recent progress in the exploitation of vegetable dyes for solar energy conversion and compares them to the properties of synthetic dyes. We provide an in-depth discussion on the main limitation of cell performance e.g. dye degradation, effective electron injection from the dye into the conduction band of semiconducting nanoparticles, such as titanium dioxide and zinc oxide, outlining future developments for the use of vegetable sensitizers in DSSCs. We also discuss the cost of vegetable dyes and how their versatility can boost the advancement of new power management solutions, especially for their integration in living environments, making the practical application of such systems economically viable. Finally, we present our view on future prospects in the development of synthetic analogues of vegetable dyes as sensitizers in DSSCs.

1. Introduction

Increasing concerns about the energy crisis, climate change, decreasing availability of fossil fuels and environmental issues are motivating the research on sustainable and renewable energy resources.14 The key for the success of this goal lies in the development of efficient energy conversion and storage devices. Energy management, used to decouple the timing of generation and consumption of electric energy, is also fundamental for cost reduction and/or increasing income from electricity and heat generation.

Renewable energy has been estimated to be half of newly installed electric capacity worldwide in 2010 and is becoming progressively more important with a total of 20.8% of the current global electricity production,5 with a prediction to reach 25% by 2018.5

The sun can provide the ultimate solution to the challenge of a sustainable energy supply. In one hour, the earth receives 13.6 TW from the sun, more than what we currently consume in a whole year (13 TW). Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight into electricity.6,7 They allow power to be produced near the end user, avoiding transmission losses and costs, and are gaining interest for applications in many sectors of daily life, from mobile devices8 to building integrated PV.912

Current PV technology is dominated by silicon (Si) solar cells,13 with energy conversion efficiency (), see Section 3, up to 24.7%.14 Despite significant development over the past decades,15 high cost limits their widespread use. The purification to metallurgical-grade Si has high energy demand and creates significant amounts of hazardous by-products.16 Thin film solar cells such as amorphous silicon (a-Si),17 cadmium telluride (CdTe),18 copper indium gallium diselenide (CIGS)19 and thin film crystalline Si20 are known as second generation PVs. The development of thin film solar cells has been driven by the potential of manufacturing costs reduction. However, for example, the less expensive Si alternative, a-Si modules suffer the StaeblerWronski (SW) degradation.21 In a typical a-Si solar cell the is reduced by up to 30% in the first 6 months as a result of the SW degradation.22 The effect is temperature dependent so that performance of PV cells tends to recover some in the summer months and drop again during winter time.22 This light induced degradation is the major disadvantage of a-Si as a PV material.23

The third generation PV cells aim to achieve a similar or higher than Si solar cells by using novel light absorbing materials.24,25 A recent development is the meso-super-structured solar cell,26 based on an organic halide perovskite (e.g. CH3NH3PbX3 where X is bromine, iodine, chlorine, or their combination) as photosensitizer,2629 and an organic hole-transport material.27Ref. 27 reported = 15.6% in a meso-super-structured (perovskite) solar cell for an un-optimized device, while = 20.1% has been recently reported on the NREL Best Research-Cell Efficiency chart and developed at KRICT (Korean Research Institute of Chemical Technology).30 Nevertheless, perovskites may not satisfy sustainability requirements, due to their lead content. Organic PV cells can be manufactured economically compared with Si cells, for example by a roll-to-roll process31 or other coating techniques.32 Another class of PV cells, known as dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs),33 relies on the dye-sensitization of the semiconductor band gap,34 and allows reaching 13% exploiting porphyrin sensitizers and graphene nanoplatelets (GNPs) at the counter electrode (CE).35 Because of their simplicity and low cost, DSSCs have great potential for large-scale applications, especially when transparency and high under cloudy and artificial light conditions are needed.

Metalorganic dyes (mostly ruthenium34,36 and osmium37 polypyridil complexes) have been used as efficient sensitizers. The preparation routes for metal complexes are often based on multi step reactions involving long, tedious and expensive chromatographic purification procedures.

In contrast, natural dyes and their organic derivatives are ideal candidates for environmentally friendly solar cells, since they are non-toxic, low in cost, renewable and abundant. Thus, the possibility to achieve solar energy conversion exploiting natural dyes has been largely investigated,3841 suggesting a cheap and simple approach based on the chemical and physical processing of these dyes, avoiding any hazardous waste by-products. In particular, vegetable dyes can be easily extracted from fruit, leaves, flowers and algae, see Fig. 1, and used in DSSCs.3849 This approach has been proposed since 1993, when ref. 38 clarified the photo-electrochemical properties of chlorophylls (from the Greek chloros = green and phyllon = leaf) in DSSCs achieving = 2.6%. The same group47 has also exploited anthocyanins (from the Greek anthos = flower and kianos = blue), a class of water-soluble, vegetable dyes extracted from flowers, e.g. malvaceae, anthurium, and leaves, e.g. Norway maple, as DSSC sensitizers. Ref. 47 extracted anthocyanins from California blackberries, achieving = 0.56%. Apart from the mere value achieved, more importantly, ref. 42 initiated an intense research activity for the exploitation of natural dyes in DSSCs. Betalain (from the Latin Beta vulgaris, the beet from which betalains were first extracted) dyes represent another class of vegetable dyes that have been exploited in DSSCs39,41 achieving up to 2.7%.50 These dyes are present in different parts of plants, in particular in caryophyllales,51 including flower petals, fruits, leaves, stems and roots.

2. Device structure, key component parameters and the working principle

A DSSC is a new type of photo-electrochemical solar cell, the concept of which traces back to Becquerel's work on the PV effect almost two centuries ago.7 A typical DSSC (see Fig. 2) is assembled using a sandwich-type approach, placing in succession a nanocrystalline mesoporous film sensitized with a dye molecule, an electrolyte solution containing a redox system, and a catalyst coated onto a conductive substrate, usually made with a transparent conductive oxide (TCO) on glass.33 The most widely investigated TCOs for DSSCs are the semiconductor-based ternary compound fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO)52 and indium tin oxide (ITO).53,54 The latter is the widest used transparent conductor (TC) in many other optoelectronic applications such as touch screens and displays.55 Unfortunately, ITO suffers severe limitations: an ever-increasing and fluctuating cost56 due to indium scarcity, although this issue is still debated,57 processing requirements,58 difficulties in patterning58,59 and sensitivity to both acidic and basic environments.55,65 Although many other TC materials have also been proposed as window electrodes for DSSCs such as doped oxide-based thin films (ZnO:Al (AZO),60 and ZnO:Ga (GZO)61), metal-oxide (ZnS/Ag/ZnS,62 ZnO/Ag/ZnO,63 and ZnO/Cu/ZnO64), and many others,59 FTO is the reference TCO in DSSCs because it has the highest work function (i.e., 4.9 eV vs. 4.8 eV of ITO),65 best thermal stability, mechanical and chemical durability, associated with the least toxicity and most of all the lowest cost with respect to all the aforementioned materials.65 Carbon-based nanomaterials,66e.g. carbon nanotubes (CNTs)67 and graphene,68,69 have also been proposed as TC electrodes. However, the performances, i.e. sheet resistance, Rs, and transmittance, Tr, of the current carbon-based TC are below the requirement for PV application, i.e. Rs = 10 1 and Tr = 90%.70 In addition, DSSCs exploiting graphene-based TCs have achieved so far71 unsatisfactory values, thus more work is needed to improve Rs and Tr of these materials before they can be fully exploited as TCs in DSSCs.

Commonly, the photoanode consists of high porosity nanocrystalline nanoparticles (10 to 20 nm in diameter) e.g., wide band gap semiconductors (usually the n-type semiconductor titanium dioxide TiO2 with a bandgap of 3.2 eV).4 Other nanoparticles such as zinc oxide (ZnO)72 or hybrid materials e.g. TiO2/ZnO73 have been proposed as scaffolds for the photoanode, but with lower performances, i.e. , with respect to the ones achieved with TiO2 nanoparticles.33 The mesoporous photoanode is sensitized with a self-assembled layer of dye molecules (photosensitizer) anchored to the semiconductor surface.33,74 This represents the main difference with respect to the conventional Si-solar cells, where both light absorption and the generated charge carrier transport are carried out by the same material, i.e. Si.13 In contrast, in DSSCs the functional element responsible for light absorption (dye molecules) is separated from the charge carrier transport material itself.75 The photosensitizer acts as an electron pump, i.e., absorbs the visible light, pumps an electron into the semiconductor, and accepts an electron from the charge mediator and then repeats the cycle again and again.33 To fulfil such requirements the dye must necessarily possess the following properties:

(i) its absorption spectrum should cover a broad range of wavelengths ranging from the visible to the near-IR region of the solar spectrum,

(ii) its molar extinction coefficient (, M1 cm1) must be as high as possible to enable efficient light harvesting.

Furthermore, in order to ensure both efficient electron injection into the photoanode and an efficient regeneration process it is indispensable that:

(iii) the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)76 level of the dye should stay energetically above the CB edge of the semiconductor while,

(iv) the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO)76 of the dye must be below the energy level of the redox mediator to allow efficient regeneration of the oxidized dye77,78 (see Fig. 2).

(v) The electron injection pathway is optimized when the dye is strongly anchored onto the semiconductor surface.

Polypyridine complexes of transition metals such as ruthenium,34,36,7986 osmium,37,79,87 cobalt,88 iron,89 copper,90 rhenium,91 as well as a large variety of metal-free synthetic organic-dyes, such as coumarin,92,93 triarylamine,94,95 tetrahydroquinoline,96,97 indoline,98100 squaraine,101,102 and others such as phthalocyanins103107 and porphyrins108111 together with vegetable3849 and artificial bio-inspired dyes112 have been used as sensitizers in DSSCs.

The as-prepared photoanode is then faced to the CE with the liquid electrolyte solution containing a redox couple (e.g. iodide/triiodide, I/I3) placed in between.33 In the first nanostructured DSSC, a LiI/I2 redox couple dissolved in acetonitrile (AcN) was used achieving = 7.1%.33 The use of AcN113 is intrinsically related to its chemical stability114 and low viscosity (0.361 mPa s).115 Moreover, AcN does not absorb light in ultraviolet and visible regions of the electromagnetic spectrum, a characteristic necessary for photochemical and photoelectrochemical experiments.116 Furthermore, AcN is a well-known polar solvent (dielectric constant is 36 at room temperature RT).117 Solvent polarity strongly affects both electron and energy transfer (see Section 6), key processes for photochemistry and photoelectrochemistry.118

Thus, thanks to the aforementioned properties,118 AcN became a common solvent for DSSC applications, mostly at the laboratory scale.222 Nevertheless, AcN has also some drawbacks such as toxicity119 and low boiling point (78 C).120 The latter is a practical disadvantage for long term DSSC endurance due to the leakage of the solvent due to volatility.121

With the aim of overcoming some of the aforementioned critical factors (e.g. toxicity, volatility, leakage), alternative nitrile liquids such as methoxy acetonitrile (MAcN),118 3-methoxypropionitrile (MePN),118 glutarinonitrile (GN),122 ethyl123 and butyl124 cianine, as well as cyclic ester (e.g., ethylene125 and propylene carbonate126 or lactone solvents such as -butyrolactone (GBL)127 have been widely investigated. Ionic liquids128 characterized by electrochemical stability together with high viscosity129 (e.g., 30 mPa s for [Bmin]-[Tf2N])32 and low vapour pressure (e.g., 100 pPa at RT for [C4mim][PF6]130 compared with 3 kPa for H2O131) have emerged as further promising alternatives to liquid solvents.

However, the high viscosity and low ionic mobility of ionic liquids negatively affect the of the ionic liquid-based DSSCs because the aforementioned properties limit the diffusion rate of the iodide and tri-iodide. To solve this limitation, mixing ionic liquids with high boiling solvents such as sulfolane has been recently proposed.132 DSSCs based on these new electrolyte formulations have shown excellent stability (exceeding 2320 h under full sunlight at 60 C).132

In the last decade, fervent research activity has been devoted to replace the liquid electrolytes by solid-state133136 or quasi-solid-state electrolytes.137,138 Commonly, a quasi-solid-state electrolyte is composed by an organic polymeric matrix incorporating a liquid electrolyte to form a stable gel network structure via physical or chemical interactions.139142 Owing this solgel character, quasi-solid-state electrolytes possess both the cohesive properties of solids143 (the work of cohesion, i.e. the energy per unit area required to separate two flat surfaces from contact)144 and the diffusive transport (i.e., high ionic conductivity) characteristics of liquids, overcoming the disadvantage of volatility of liquid electrolytes. This favours the wetting of the mesoporous dye-coated nanocrystalline TiO2 film by the electrolyte and also the regeneration of the oxidized dye molecules.145 of 6.1% was achieved for a DSSC using a ruthenium dye (Z907, see Section 4.1) and a quasi-solid-state gel electrolyte system based on 1,3:2,4-di-O-dimethylbenzylidene-D-sorbitol(DMDBS)/3-methoxypropionitrile.140

Concerning solid-state electrolytes, currently, there are two main classes investigated in DSSC applications. One is based on a solid compound (e.g., polyethylene oxide) containing the I/I3 redox couple,146148 while the other makes use of a hole transport material (HTM), such as the spiro-OMeTAD (2,2,7,7-tetrakis(N,N-di-p-methoxyphenylamine)-9,9-spirobifluorene).123,149,150 Wang et al.147 reported = 6.7% using N-methyl-N-butylpyrrolidinium iodide (P1,4I)-doped succinonitrile electrolyte containing the I/I3 redox couple. A solid-state electrolyte containing I/I3 by introducing TiO2 nanoparticles into poly(ethylene oxide) was used to fabricate a DSSC as reported by Stergiopoulos et al.,146 obtaining = 4.2%. More recently, ref. 151 achieved = 9.48% exploiting N719 dye and a structurally interconnected block copolymer, prepared by the oligomerization of poly(oxyethylene)-segmented diamine and 4,40-oxydiphthalic anhydride, followed by curing to generate amide-imide cross-linked gels.

Although the first attempt to use spiro-OMeTAD, reported by Bach et al. in 1998,149 resulted in low (0.74%), the development in the subsequent years of this solid electrolyte permitted to achieve up to 3.2%.150 More recently,152 chemical doping of a spiro-OMeTAD-based DSSC by tris(2-(1H-pyrazol-1-yl)pyridine) Co(III), (FK102) has allowed the achievement of = 7.2%.

Several other materials such as CuSCN,153 poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT),154 polyaniline155 and poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) (PEDOT),135 have been investigated as HTMs for DSSCs with = 4.5% for P3HT and 6,6-phenyl C61-butyric acid methylester (PCBM).156 Recently, Kanatzidis and co-workers157 reported an all-solid state inorganic solar cell formed by a p-type direct band-gap semiconductor CsSnI3 (doped with SnF2) as HTM, nanoporous TiO2 and the dye N719 achieving = 10.2%. This result was obtained with a solvent free DSSC, thus overcoming the issue of electrolyte leakage, solvation of the dye by the electrolyte solvent and, at the same time, preventing the corrosion of the catalyst (see below) by the electrolyte.157

The third main component of DSSCs is the CE. The role of the CE in a DSSC is twofold, first it back transfers electrons arriving from the external circuit to the redox system,33 second it catalyzes the reduction of the oxidized charge mediator.33 The key requirements for CEs are low charge-transfer resistance Rct (i.e., the electrodeelectrolyte interface resistance), high exchange current density (i.e., the rate of electron transfer between the electrolyte and the electrode), and a high specific surface area (SSA). Commonly, CEs are made of a few nanometre thick platinum (Pt) layer, acting as a catalyst,33 deposited onto TCO or, in the case where transparency is not an issue, on a highly conductive and reflective substrate such as stainless steel.158 Apart from the limiting factors of ITO and FTO discussed above, Pt is costly other than having limited reserves on earth.159 Moreover, Pt tends to degrade over time when in contact with the I3/I liquid electrolyte, reducing the of DSSCs.160 Thus, the replacement of Pt with low-cost and/or more effective (leading to high devices) materials is at the centre of on-going research effort. In this context, several groups have used carbon-based nanomaterials as catalysts in the CE, such as amorphous carbon,161 CNTs,158,162 hard carbon spherules,163 graphite,163 GNPs,164 nitrogen-doped GNPs,165 graphene oxide (GO)166168 and hybrid structures of GNPsRGOCNTs169,170 with results, i.e., , close to or exceeding the ones based on Pt. Indeed, a new high record efficiency of DSSCs ( = 13%) has been achieved exploiting GNPs at the CE.164

4. Inorganic and synthetic organic dyes

The dye plays a key role in DSSCs. Based on the requirements outlined in Section 2, different dyes have been studied and significant progress in designing sensitizers for DSSC applications has been made. Here, we briefly summarize the main properties of inorganic and synthetic organic-based dyes.

4.1 Metal complexes

Metal complexes consist of a central metal ion with ancillary ligands (L), typically bipyridines (bpy) or terpyridines, bearing at least one anchoring group198 able to link the dye to the semiconductor surface in a dyebridgeanchor array. This circumstance causes a large electronic interaction (coupling) between the *-orbital of the ligand, which is the lowest LUMO of the complex, and the d-orbital of semiconductor oxide, resulting in electron injection from the metal complex into the semiconductor CB. Typical anchoring groups for metal oxide semiconductors are sulphonic acid (SO3H),199201 phosphonic acid (PO)(OH)2,185,202204 carboxylic acid (COOH)205207 and its derivatives (acid chlorides,208 amides,209 esters209 or carboxylate salts209), with carboxyl groups being largely used.172 The carboxyl group can form bidentate-bridging-linkage (Fig. 4a), as well as chelating- (Fig. 4b) or ester-linkages (Fig. 4c) with the TiO2 surface,198 depending on the structure of the dye, the binding groups, the pH, and the metal oxide synthesis.210

Light absorption in the visible and near-IR regions of the solar spectrum is attributed to a metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT)211 transition. In this regard, polypyridine complexes of d6 metal, such as Ru(II),79,81,84 Os(II)79,84 or Ru(I),91 are typical examples of compounds characterized by intense bands in the visible region due to MLCT transition.211 The MLCT energies can be tuned by proper selection of the substituents (alkyl, aryl, heterocycle, etc.) on the ancillary ligand, as well as by changing the ligand itself.212 Usually, electron-withdrawing substituents, such as the carboxylic group,213 decrease the *-orbital energy of the polypyridine ligand.213,214 In contrast, electron-donating substituents, such as thiocyanate (SCN)- or the amine group or alkyl chains, show the opposite effect.213,214

4.2 Synthetic organic dyes

In order to replace the noble and expensive Ru(II)-complexes in DSSCs,265 other sensitizers have been proposed as alternative such as the synthetic organic dyes.25,94,99,100,266282 These dyes exhibit many advantages with respect to the aforementioned metal-based ones, such as: (i) efficient light-harvesting capabilities (e.g., in the near-IR region), (ii) higher , which depends on the fact that for this dyes the transition involved in the absorption of the light is *, which generates a Ligand Centered (LC) excited state that is not distorted as MLCT state populated in the case of Ru(II) based ones,213 (iii) easiness in tailoring the molecular structure and photo-physical characteristics.

The nature of the frontier orbitals (i.e., HOMO and LUMO) in porphyrin macrocycles has been an active area of research for computational and spectroscopic investigations. The absorption spectra of porphyrin derivatives show two characteristic * absorption regions, the weak Q band (transition to the first excited state (LUMO)) in the 550700 nm range and the intense Soret band (transition to the second excited state (LUMO + 1)) in the 400450 nm range.292Ab initio methods (restricted Hartree Fock, local DFT)293 have shown that metal-free (e.g., thiophene substitute294) and metal (Mg(II), Cu(II), Zn(II), Ni(II)) containing porphyrins generally possess two a1u and a2u orbitals as the HOMO and HOMO 1,294 see Fig. 7. The electron density in the a1u type orbital is essentially localized on the C and C atoms of the pyrrole rings, while in the a2u orbital it is mainly confined on the pyrrole nitrogens and on the meso-carbons,294 see Fig. 6. As for the case of the HOMO, also the LUMO levels are similar in metal free (e.g., thiophene substitute) and metal containing porphyrins: there are two nearly degenerate e2g(*) orbitals essentially centred on the porphyrin macrocycle,294 see Fig. 7. The presence of a1u() and a2u() orbitals in many metal porphyrins can account for the formation of a radical cation that plays an important role in the biochemical function of porphyrins. For example, the radical cation formation is key in the photooxidation of chlorophylls, which are essentially Mg containing porphyrins, see Section 5.3.

5. Vegetable dyes

Driven by the need for new environmentally friendly materials, vegetable dyes336 such as betalains,41,49,337 carotenoids,338 anthocyanins39,339343 and chlorophylls38,344347 have been proposed as sensitizers in DSSCs. Vegetable dyes are available in large quantity, easily and safely extracted from fruits, flowers, leaves, low in cost and non-toxic. All these factors make the use of these natural products an intriguing challenge in the development of cheap and commercially available DSSCs.

Unlike artificial sensitizers, whose desired energy levels and absorption properties can be tailored by changing the ligands and/or the central metal atom, for vegetable dyes this is a more difficult task simply because their molecular structure is designed by nature. As a consequence, the research activity is mainly devoted towards the selection of the right vegetable source to isolate the sensitizer and finding the best operating conditions (concentration, pH and extraction techniques of the dyes, size of TiO2 nanoparticles, thickness of photoanode film, soaking time, electrolyte composition, etc.).340,348 For DSSC applications, it is possible to follow a bio-inspired strategy that through simple and clean synthesis, produces on demand structural modifications for a quasi-natural biomimetic sensitizer showing properties similar to those of the natural dyes. In this framework, optimizations in the design of suitable sensitizers have been obtained in the case of flavylium compounds.349

5.1 Anthocyanins

Anthocyanins belong to flavonoid group of phytochemicals (from the Greek phyto = plant), a family of water-soluble chemical compounds that occur naturally in plants.350352 Anthocyanins are responsible for the different colors of plants, including roots, stems, leaves, flowers and fruits.353 Red- and blueberry, black and red raspberry, black currant, cherry, Sicilian blood orange, eggplant and red cabbage are just few examples of the multitude of vegetables that are rich in anthocyanins.354358 Amongst the large variety of vegetable compounds exhibiting the basic flavylium ion structure, the applications of anthocyanins have increased enormously in the last 20 years, not only due to their colouring and anti-oxidant properties359363 but also as pollution attractants and phytoprotective agents, protecting plant's DNA from damage caused by sunlight irradiation.353 Currently, more attention has been devoted to anthocyanins due to their potential health benefits.364 Moreover, in the last few years growing attention has been paid towards the exploitation of anthocyanins as multistatemultifunctional systems for information processing at the molecular level,359361 and for application in optoelectronics, e.g., solar cells.349
5.1.1 Structure, photochemistry and photophysics of anthocyanins. Anthocyanins are derived from anthocyanidins or aglycones (see Fig. 8) which have, at least one pendant sugar moiety (glycoside form).365 The core of both anthocyanidins and anthocyanins (or anthocyans) is a 2-phenyl-benzopyrylium chromophore (i.e., flavylium cation), which consists of an aromatic ring (A) bonded to a heterocyclic ring (C) containing an oxygen atom which, in turn is linked by a CC bond to a third aromatic ring (B) so, it may also be described as a C6C3C6 skeleton.365 The extended conjugation, as well as the presence of positive charge and free OH groups on the flavylium cation, allows the anthocyanins to absorb light in the visible region leading to a large variety of dye colours.365,366 The difference between anthocyanins of different types is mainly due to: (i) the number of hydroxyl and/or methoxy groups in the molecule, (ii) the nature and number of sugar moiety attached to the phenolic units, (iii) the nature and number of aromatic or aliphatic acids attached to the sugar moiety.364 The most common sugar is glucose, but galactose, arabinose, rhamnose, xylose, fructose and other may also be present in the anthocyanins structure.367

Furthermore, the sugar moiety may be acylated by aromatic acids, generally hydrocinnamic acids (coumaric, caffeic, gallic, sinapic and ferulic acids) and sometimes by aliphatic acids (acetic, oxalic, malonic, malic, and succinic acids).369,370 Aromatic and/or aliphatic acylation may occur in the same molecule, forming a polyacylated structure.351,352,371,372 Generally, the acylic moieties are bonded to the C6 carbon atom in the sugar molecule. Anthocyanins occurring in nature typically have hydroxyl substituents at the position 3 (always glycosylated, i.e., a hydroxyl or other functional group is attached to a carbohydrate of another molecule) and at the position 5 (occasionally glycosylated) of the flavylium cation, while the B-ring (or 2-phenyl) has one or more hydroxyl or methoxy substituents373,374 (see Fig. 8).

Natural anthocyanins, in which the 7-hydroxyl group is glycosylated or replaced by a methoxyl group, can also occur but they are quite rare. Anthocyanins mono- and di-glycosylated at positions 3, 5 and even 4 are rare as well with respect to the anthocyanins typically having hydroxyl substituents at the position 3 and 5.365,375 Due to this chemical complexity, there is a large variety of anthocyanins in nature350 and more than 20 currently known anthocyanidins or aglycones. Nevertheless, only six anthocyanidins, i.e., cyanidin (Cy), delphinidin (Dp), malvidin (Mv), pelargonidin (Pg), peonidin (Pn) and petunidin (Ptn) are common in higher plants.370,376 Amongst these anthocyanidins, the three glycoside derivate non-methylated i.e., Cy, Dp and Pg, are the most widespread in nature, being present in 80% of leaves, 70% of fruits and 50% of flowers.377

The number of hydroxyl and methoxyl groups determines the intensity (i.e., ), type (i.e., the absorption wavelength) and stability of anthocyanins' colour.370 Colour stability refers to the capacity of the molecule (dye) to maintain its colour properties.370 Generally, predominance of hydroxyl groups on the aromatic skeleton of anthocyanins gives rise to an intense blue colour while, a red colour is observed when methoxyl groups prevail instead.366,378 Glycosil substituents also influence the colour stability, probably due to a loss of co-planarity of ring B with respect to the rest of the molecule, which causes a decrease in conjugation and stability.379 The colour stability of monoglycosides and diglycosides anthocyanidines remains higher than that of the corresponding aglycones.379 Therefore, both hydroxylation and glycosylation influence the colour properties of the dyes.

Colour stability of cyanidin (i.e., cyanin without sugar moiety) is higher than that of malvidin, but lower than that of malvidin 3-glucoside.366 Additionally, the colour stability of mono and diglycosyl anthocyanidins is dependent on the glycosyl substituent nature and its anchorage on the anthocyanin skeleton. Broennum-Hansen and Flink380 reported that the anthocyanins containing the disaccharide sambubiose (a component of some glycosides),380 are more stable than glucose containing monosaccharidic anthocyanins. A possible explanation could be ascribed to the ability of the sugar moieties to avoid the degradation towards instable intermediates such as phenolic acid and/or aldehyde compounds.381 For the use of anthocyanins as dyes in DSSCs, the aforementioned structural variations are critical in the choice of the natural sensitizer characterized by the most favourable structural and optical properties (e.g., maximum absorption-max, , colour stability). Thus, the choice is not a simple task, because anthocyanin extracts are strongly affected by pH,382 processing and storage temperature,383 concentration,384 aggregation of dyes,385 solvents,386 light intensity and frequency,387 presence of oxygen,388,389 metallic ions,390,391 enzymes and other substances.392,393

Due to their ionic nature, anthocyanins are involved in a series of pH-dependent equilibria giving rise to different chemical species (red flavylium cation AH+, bluish quinonoidal base Q, colorless hemiketale pseudobase, H, and colourless chalcone Ch), which are unstable in neutral pH media. A change in pH modulates the anthocyanin stability.394 Indeed, anthocyanins are more stable in acid solution (low pH) than in alkaline media (high pH)365 (see Fig. 9). Under high acidity (pH 2) conditions, the reddish-coloured AH+ species become predominant.395

With increasing pH value, the flavylium cation is involved in two parallel reactions: (i) de-protonation to form the quinonoidal base Q, predominant at pH values between 2 and 4, and (ii) hydration by nucleophilic attack of water,396,397 at the charge defective C2 and/or C4 positions, followed by proton loss.396,397 The latter produces a reduction of the AH+ concentration and the appearance of the hemiketal pseudobase, H.349 Due to the loss of the conjugated double bond between the A- and B-rings, the hemiketal form does not absorb visible light and appears colorless or light yellow.396,397 With increasing pH values (pH = 46), the hemiketal forms, due to the opening of the C-ring, the colorless cis-chalcone Chcis, which is then followed by a slow isomerization to the trans-chalcone Chtrans, see Fig. 9. Therefore, from the primary flavylium cation structure, secondary quinonoidal or hemiketal structures are derived. Moreover, hemiketal structures can further tautomerise (i.e., migration of a proton, accompanied by a switch of a single bond and adjacent double bond)396,397 to chalcone species. Tertiary structures, e.g., isopropane that has centred carbon (defined tertiary carbon) attached to three other carbon atoms, can result from molecular associations of primary or secondary forms, either between themselves (self-association)398 or with other molecules (inter-molecular co-pigmentation).399402

At equilibrium, the system reported in Fig. 9 behaves as a single acidbase equilibrium between the flavylium cation and a conjugate base, ConBas (defined as the sum of the concentrations of the other species: [ConBas] = [Q] + [H] + [Chcis] + [Chtrans]).403 As a consequence, at diverse pH values, which are typically different for fresh and processed vegetables, each anthocyanin will be, most probably, a mixture of the aforementioned forms.

Molecular co-pigmentation between anthocyanin and other compounds (co-pigments) is an important stabilizing mechanism of chromophores.400403,415 Generally, co-pigments are rich -electrons systems able to associate with the electron-poor flavylium ion. Polyphenols such as rutin, quercetin, chlorogenic acid, tannic acid, flavan-3-ol, lignans, stilbenes and many other unknown flavones can act as co-pigments.415 Other molecules such as organic acids,400402,415 alkaloid,399 acyl groups,416 metallic ions417 and anthocyanins themselves (self-association),398,399 have also been identified as co-pigments. In the self-association398,399 an aggregation between two anthocyanins, one is the chromophore while the other is the co-pigment, takes place.

Co-pigmentation reaction can occurs through inter- and/or the intra-molecular interactions. In inter-molecular co-pigmentation, the most prominent in almost all the anthocyanin compounds,350,351,401 a interaction takes place through a vertical stacking between the planar structure of anthocyanin chromophores and the co-pigment molecules having delocalized -electrons.350,351,401 The result is an overlapping arrangement of molecular orbitals (see Fig. 11), which provides protection to nucleophilic attack of water at position 2 of the pyrylium nucleus, leading to colorless hemiketal and chalcone forms (Fig. 11).350,351,401 Intra-molecular co-pigmentation acts in a similar way to inter-molecular one, with the advantage that co-pigment and anthocyanin are covalently linked by linkers or spacers, in a more effective way.350,351,401

5.2 Betalains

Betalains represent another class of water soluble vegetable-dyes of great interest for their potentiality as light absorbers in DSSC technology.41,447 The colour of flowering plants belonging to Caryophyllales and some Basidiomycota plants are determined by the content of betalain dyes.448,449 Generally, these dyes have orange and red colour, mostly depending on prevailing dye content: betacyanin and betaxanthin. For example, prickly pear contains betanin and indicaxanthin and is orange, while the Bonel variety of red beet contains betanin and vulgaxanthin exhibiting an intense red coloration.

For long time betalains were erroneously categorized as flavocyanins (betaxanthins) and nitrogen anthocyanins (betacyanins). It was only in 1968 that Mabry and Dreiding450,451 attributed to both dyes the name betalains, i.e., the beet from which betalains were first extracted.450,451

Structurally, betalains are immonium derivatives of the main chromophore betalamic acid (I). In fact, all betalain molecules have a betalamic acid (I) moiety bearing substituent R-groups extending from the nitrogen atom (II). The nature of these substituent R-groups determines whether or not the molecule belongs to bethacyanins (i.e., betanidin and betanin) or the betaxanthin family.394,452 The glycosidation of one of the substituent pattern at the C1- or C2-position in the benzene ring, differentiate the individual betacyanins (III). Betaxanthins comprise various amino acid or amino-conjugated moieties of betalamic acid. A typical example is the indicaxanthin, a proline-containing betaxanthin (IV), one of the first chemically identified betalains found in the beet root.453 Glucose, glucuronic acid and apiose are the most common sugar monomers for the betalain molecules, while malonic and 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaric acids as well as caffeic-, p-coumaric, and ferulic acids represent typical acid substituents.449 Therefore, betacyanins are considered the condensation product between betalamic acid and cyclo-3-(3,4 dihydroxyphenylalanine) (cyclo-DOPA) while, the condensation between betalamic acid and amines or amino acids gives rise to betaxanthins.454 The most common betaxanthins are vulgaxanthin I (glutamine-betaxanthin) and indicaxanthin (proline-betaxanthin), found in red beet and in yellow cactus pears, respectively.454

Betalains, contrary to other dyes such as anthocyanins or chlorophylls, have received much less attention for research activity, probably because the red beet has been considered the only edible betalain's source for long time. However, in the last fifteen years the research activity of betalains (e.g., leaf and grain amaranth,455 prickly pear,456 beet root,49 and Bougainvillea457) have seen a rising interest both for their nutritional aspect (antioxidant456 and radical scavenging activity458) and in natural food colorants.459,460 In particular, thanks to their strong absorption in the visible part of the electromagnetic spectrum and their ability to act as electron donor, the betalains have been considered good candidates in the processes of photon induced electron transfer. Differently from the large number of anthocyanins (up to 550),351 betalains have a lower number (55), including the red-purple betacyanins (max 535 nm), and the yellow-orange betaxanthins (max 460480 nm).461 The bathochromic shift of the absorption spectrum of betacyanins with respect to betaxanthins is attributed to the presence of the cyclo-Dopa aromatic structure in the betacyanins.462 Furthermore, in the betacyanins, the aglycone betanidin shows a slightly red-shifted absorption spectrum with respect to betanin.

Betalains have high (e.g. = 6.5 104 M1 cm1 for betalamic acid and betanin)49 and pH dependent redox properties.49 Moreover, betalains exhibit a tinctorial strength (or colour strength, i.e. the ability of a dye to maintain its colour upon mixing with another dye) up to three times higher than anthocyanins.461 In addition, betalains possess the carboxyl functional groups (COOH) that is a key requisite to bind to the TiO2 nanostructure via the formation of ester-type linkage.452,463 In particular, the betalamic acid possess two COOH groups, while its derivatives (betanidin, betanin, indicaxanthin) have a further carboxyl group attached to the heterocycle through a sp3 atom.464 Upon adsorption of the dye onto the TiO2 surface, the proton of the carboxyl group is transferred to the oxide (e.g., TiO2).464 Contrary, for anthocyanins the adsorption onto TiO2 requires the presence of two ortho-hydroxyl-groups that, if from one hand leads to strong electronic coupling and a rapid electron transfer from the dye to the TiO2, on the other hand assists also the back electron transfer (i.e., recombination process). Nevertheless, a characterization and clarification of the electron injection mechanism from betalains to TiO2, contrary to the case of anthocyanins and chlorophylls,283,465,466 is missing and the current understanding is mainly correlated to the analysis of the IPCE experiments.39,41,49

Betalains, in general, show pH stability in the 37 pH range467,468 which, makes them particularly suitable for uses in low-acidity ambient as well as in pH neutral conditions.467 Moreover, betalains are less susceptible to hydrolytic cleavage (the cleavage of chemical bonds by water addition), see Fig. 13, if compared with anthocyanins.452 In this context, betalains can be an alternative to the less water soluble anthocyanins which under the same pH conditions lose their photochemical properties due to changes of their colours.469 Despite their pH stability, at least in the 37 pH range,467 the betalains are sensitive to temperature changes. In fact, in the combination of high temperature (5075 C) and acidic condition (pH < 6) deglycosylation, as well as hydrolytic cleavage of the aldmide bond of betalanins and decarboxylation occur,470 see Fig. 13. A strategy to protect the aldmine bond from cleavage consists in the acylation of betalains with aromatic or aliphatic acid.471

It was found that betanidin can be oxidized more easily than betanin. In fact, the redox potential (at pH = 6) of the betanidin is slightly lower (0.32 V) than that of the betanin (0.67 V),49 identifying in the enediol group (alkenes with hydroxyl group on both sides of the double bond) the oxidation site for the aglycone.472 In 2002, cyclic voltammetry measurements by Butera et al.456 reported for betanin three anodic waves (oxidation) at 0.62, 0.84 and 1.22 V (vs. NHE). Further, by the similarity of the redox potentials of betanin and indicaxanthin (0.83 and 1.12 V, respectively), the authors assumed that the first oxidation of betanin occurs at the aromatic ring.456

5.3 Chlorophylls

Chlorophyll is a green coloured dye of leaves and green stems of plants, responsible for the absorption of energy from light during the photosynthesis process476 where, the light absorbed by chlorophyll transforms carbon dioxide and water into carbohydrates and oxygen (CO2 + H2O (CH2O) + O2).477
5.3.1 Structure, photochemistry and photophysics. Structurally, chlorophyll refers to a class of cyclic tetrapyrrole,283,284 similarly to porphyrin and phtalocyanines discussed in Section 4.2. Caventou and Pelletier478 in 1817, identified chlorophyll as a chlorin ring having a magnesium ion at the centre. Unlike porphyrins (see Section 4.2), chlorophylls have a reduced pyrrole ring and a phytol group, a highly hydrophobic 20-carbon atoms alcohol esterified to an acid side chain. Bacterio(chlorophylls) are similar to chlorophylls, except for the reduction of an additional pyrrole ring that determines a shift (with respect to chlorophylls) of their absorption maxima to wavelengths around 1000 nm (NIR).479

The basic structures of the aforementioned compounds are depicted in Fig. 16. Although, after Caventou and Pelletier,478 many new chlorophyll structures were identified and isolated, such as Chl-d,480 and Chl-f,481,482 essentially Chl-a (C55H72O5N4Mg, cyan), Chl-b (C55H70O6N4Mg, chartreuse) and Chl-c (C35H28O5N4Mg, blue-green), are actually recognized as the most common chlorophylls. In particular, Chl-a and Chl-b are the two most diffused chlorophylls in plants. Their concentration depends on the insolation, i.e., the total amount of solar energy received per unit surface area (MJ m2), of the plants. The main difference in the structure of Chl-a and Chl-b relies in the composition of a single side chain of the cyclic tetrapyrrole (see Fig. 16), which is a CH3 in Chl-a (blue-green) and a CHO in Chl-b (yellow-green). Chl-a and Chl-b have a highly stable polycyclic network of alternating single and double bonds (polyenes) conjugated structure which, allows the orbitals delocalization, making chlorophylls ideal photosensitizers.483

Chemically, chlorophylls are instable in both acid and basic ambient and have a strong tendency towards aggregation and/or interaction with their molecular environment. Differently from the other tetra-pyrrole derivates such as porphyrins110 and phtalocyanines,287 the production of which needs complicated and expensive synthesis and purification pathways, chlorophylls and their derivates are cheap and environmentally friendly compounds. Coloured and nontoxic derivatives of chlorophylls are utilized as dyes in the cosmetic, textile, and food industries,484,485 as well as components of luminescent materials.486

Beyond the natural abundance and low toxicity, as well as the capability to absorb solar energy in different region of the electromagnetic spectrum, the cyclic tetrapyrrole Chl-type dyes possess some fundamental advantages with respect to several inorganic and organic sensitizers. In fact, the excited states lifetime of chlorophylls can be tuned by changing the central metals (Zn, Mg, Ni, Cu, Pd, Fe, etc.) [for the case of Ni see as an example ref. 487] and consequently, the LUMO orbital of the sensitizers can also be adjusted to match the energy level of the CB edges of semiconductors.

The absorption spectra of chlorophylls consist of two main bands, the Soret-band located in the blu region, deriving from the electronic transition with the strongest oscillator strength (2.88 eV) and the Q-band lying in the red region, consisting of Qx and Qy transitions (see Fig. 7) at 1.87 and 2.14 eV, respectively.488 The positions and intensities of Q-bands can be appropriately adjusted by chemical modification of the macro-cycle.488 Furthermore, chlorophylls display up to 105 M1 cm1, amongst the highest observed for organic compounds. As an example, Li et al.489 reported for Chl-a and Chl-b as high as 7 104 M1 cm1 with strong absorption bands in the blue and red regions of the visible spectrum (max 430 and 665 nm in Chl-a, and 425 and 655 nm in Chl-b).

5.3.2 Chlorophylls as photosensitizers. In the first studies on ZnO wide-bandgap semiconductor photosensitized by chlorophylls, Tributsch and Calvin490,491 reported an extremely low EQE (up to 12.5%). In the following years, chlorophylls as sensitizers for organic PV cells were the subject of a fervent research activity,492496 and EQE up to 30% were achieved by depositing Chl-a monolayers on SnO2 TC electrodes.339

The first investigations on the photosensitization of TiO2 using chlorophyll derivatives was carried out by Kamat and co-workers.497,498 In 1993, Kay and Grtzel38 published an exhaustive study on colloidal Ti02 electrodes photosensitized with chlorophylls and related derivatives, achieving EQE up to 30%. These low EQE values, may be attributed to the chlorophylls structure, where the presence of alkyl groups rather than carboxyl or hydroxyl groups makes chlorophylls unable to efficiently bind with the nanoporous TiO2 surface.434 Kay and Grtzel38 demonstrated that Chl-a exhibits a poorer adsorption and sensitization on TiO2 when compared with chlorophyll derivatives where phytyl group is replaced by carboxylic one. Further, bio-inspired strategy mimics the light harvesting antenna (LHA) effect developed by different natural photosynthetic systems,564,565 such as the green sulfur bacterial chlorosome,566 the Pcb (prochlorococcus Chl-a2/b binding) protein567 or the light-harvesting complex of photosystem II (LHC-II),568 the purple bacterial light-harvesting complexes 1, 2 and 3 (LH1LH3)569 and many others. Despite the wide structural differences of LHA, influenced by the different habitats in which the diverse LHA systems exist, their light harvesting mechanism exploits similar principles.570 During this process, light absorption followed by charge separation and efficient energy transfer to a reaction center (RC) are performed by molecular dyes. Natural LHAs are composed by a central molecular core (i.e., RC) associated with an array of peripherical dyes, (antenna, A).565 The role of the antenna is twofold: it collects the solar radiation and, through energy transfer, transfers the excitation energy570 to the RC, where charge separation takes place.570

The energy transfer process can take place via Frster571 or Dexter572 mechanisms which, mainly differ in length scale (i.e., the donoracceptor distance, RDA). In the Frster model, the energy released from an excited light-sensitive molecule (donor) excites a second chromophor (acceptor) through nonradiative dipoledipole coupling (coulombic interaction) and efficiencies (i.e., the ratio of acceptor emission intensity to that of the donor)573 sensitive to RDA. Therefore, Frster energy is a short and intermediate range process (10100 ) (ref. 571 and 574577) mainly restricted to a singletsinglet energy transfer.571 Differently, Dexter mechanism572 is a double electron exchange between the donor and the acceptor, depending strongly on the spatial overlap of their molecular orbitals. Thus, it is a very short range process (<10 ), decaying exponentially with RDA.572 Furthermore, Dexter mechanism can promote both singletsinglet and singlettriplet excited-state energy transfers.572 A useful feature for energy transfer from fluorescent organic antenna to transition metal complex sensitizer.578

The knowledge gained in the field of natural photosynthesis579,580 constitutes a valuable help to overcome the limits (i.e., low , weak absorptivity, etc.) of LHE efficiency in the sensitization of wide-bandgap semiconductor for solar cell applications. In fact, in a DSSC the sensitizer is usually the sole light collector, and the cell JSC strongly depends on the fraction of the light absorbed relatively to the whole incident solar flux on the photoanode. Thus, any improvement in the LHE constitutes a viable way in increasing the cell performances (i.e., JSC, VOC, FF, IPCE and ).

The antenna effect concept introduced by Balzani and Lehn581 was first used in DSSCs by Scandola and co-workers in 1990 (ref. 582) which, proposed the use of a antenna/sensitizer structure based on trinuclear ruthenium polypyridine complex in order to increase the LHE of sensitized TiO2-semiconductor. Since then, the inspiration of photosyntesis has prompted a considerable interest in the use of organic dyes for solar-energy applications and significant amount of artificial molecular and supramolecular architectures have been constructed and investigated.583590 In Ru(II)-complexes based DSSCs, the low (104 M1 cm1) (ref. 36, 83, 220, 221 and 225) value and the weak absorptivity in the near-IR region, see Section 4.1, remain the main weakness for LHE. Differently, in spite of their narrow absorption wavelength bandwidth, several vegetable dyes show intense * transitions and wide tunability over a large electromagnetic spectral region, making them suitable candidates in the multichromophores sensitizer molecular devices (panchromatic effect).591

A critical factor in the design of a photosynthetic LHA system is the choice of the individual components (sensitizer and antenna) as well as their geometrical arrangement.592 Amongst synthetic organic multi-dye arrays, porphyrinic assemblies have proved their potentiality for DSSCs purpose.304,584,593596 In these systems, the light absorption by an antenna dye is followed by singletsinglet excited-state energy transfer process towards the porphyrin nucleo which, in turn injects the excited electrons into anchored TiO2 semiconductor.597 In addition, other dyes such as carotenoids,583 coumarins,598 cyanine,599 xanthenes600 and perylene dyes601,602 that, absorb in spectral regions where porphyrins have low values, can be covalently bound to the main porphyrin macrocycle to enhance its LHE. As an example, synthetic covalent perylene-imide/porphyrins dyes have shown high LHE in the spectral blu-green region (i.e., between the porphyrin Soret and Q-bands (450500 nm)).603605 A similar idea was exploited by Hupp and Lee606 using in DSSC a bichromophore (dyad) zinc-porphyrin/boron-dipyrrin (4,4-difluoro-4-bora-3a,4a-diaza-s-indacene, (Bodipy)) with Bodipy acting as LHA molecule to fill out the blue-green region of the spectrum. As a result of the larger solar spectral coverage, the Zn-porphyrin/Bodipy based cell has shown = 1.55%, nearly twice compared to that of the Zn-porphyrin based reference cell ( = 0.84%).606

In 2011 Odobel and co-workers591 realized a trichromopheric sensitizer (triad) consisting of covalently linked Bodipy, ZnPr and squaraine units.591 The first two chromophoric subunits (Bodipy and ZnPr) act as LHA while the squaraine behaves as sensitizer for electron injection into TiO2. In such a way, the assembled supramolecular architecture covered a wide window of the solar spectrum, combining the absorption properties of the individual components (see Fig. 26). 7. Scaling-up and costs

The final goal of any PV technology development is the scaling-up of lab cells to the specific size required by target applications. Nowadays PV systems are utilized in different contests: conventional PV plant, space solar power,608,609 building integrated PV (BIPV),610612 energy harvester for sensors and other information and communication technology devices,613,614etc.

Concerning DSSCs, the good response to low illumination conditions615 and diffuse light,616 the reduced angular dependence of the efficiency617 and the capacity to easily tune the colour and transparency of the cells,618 make them well suited for BIPV619,620 and indoor applications.615

The scaling-up from the lab size cells (less than 1 cm2) to large area cells or modules, is usually associated with a reduction of performances (i.e., ).621 This lowering in is due primarily to the increase of the internal voltage drop caused by sizeable increases in the series resistances. The Rs of TCO (>7 Ohm 1)65 is not negligible and induces considerable reduction of the FF already for cells larger than 1 cm2. Such issue is common to all PV technologies and several module architecture have been developed to mitigate this scaling problem.622625 Being electrochemical devices, however, specific problems arises for scaling up DSSCs, such as the management of the liquid electrolyte. For DSSC mainly four module architecture have been proposed, one for parallel connection and three for series connection of the cells (W-type, Z-type and monolithic),622 see Fig. 28.


Fig. 28 Connection schemes of DSSCs in a module.

In parallel connection, similarly to crystalline (c-Si) PV technology, metal fingers are used to collect the generated current. However, metal finger corrosion can be induced by the electrolyte.626 For this reason, special encapsulation strategies for metal grids have been developed for DSSC.627631 In Z-type series connected DSSC module the anode of the cell is connected to the cathode of the adjacent cell through a vertical metal contact.623625 Also in this case, vertical metal contact should be properly sealed to prevent the corrosive action of the electrolyte. This corrosive effect of the electrolyte is mitigated in the metal-free W and monolithic architectures. W connection exploits the bi-facial property of DSSC that is the ability to operate with front or rear illumination. In this architecture one cell (front illuminated cell) is connected to the adjacent cell, which is oriented in the opposite direction (back illuminated cell), thus eliminating the metal vertical connection of the Z-scheme. Such kind of architecture suffers of cell unbalance being the front illuminated cell typically more efficient than the back illuminated one.632,633 For this reason, a careful design of the module should be consider.634637 Finally, in the monolithic architecture the printed back contact is also used for interconnecting adjacent cells.638,639 Usually, carbon materials such as carbon black,640,641 graphite,642 graphite/carbon black643,644 and graphene sheet/carbon645 are used for the back contact even though other opaque or transparent materials can be used, also adopting vacuum-based deposition techniques (thermal evaporation, sputtering etc.).639

The scaling up concepts presented above have been widely applied in DSSC sensitized with Ru-based dyes and industrialization activities are well advanced. G24 Power is commercialising flexible DSSC modules while companies like Toyota-Aisin, TDK, Fujikura, Sony, DongJing-Semichem, Dyepower and others are in the pre-industrialization phase.622 Moreover, quite recently there have been several announcements about IEC61646 endurance tests cleared by some Ru- and synthetic organic-based DSSC cells and modules.646648 This demonstrate the maturity of the DSSC technology also with respect to the severe stress test presented in the IEC norm originally developed for conventional thin-film PV technology.

While Ru- and synthetic organic-dyes DSSC modules are well developed and first commercial applications have been launched on the market, limited example of DSSC modules sensitized with vegetable dyes have appeared. 8. Outlook

The thought of imitating nature has been a dream for scientists over the centuries. In particular, the study of natural dyes remains an attractive and challenging goal contributing to a solution for future alternative environmentally sustainable energy conversion as predicted by G. Ciamician in his New York speech of 1912, The photochemistry of the future where he stated forests of glass tubes will extend over the plains and glass buildings will rise everywhere; inside of these will take place the photochemical processes that hitherto have been the guarded secret of the plants, but that will have been mastered by human industry which will know how to make them bear even more abundant fruit than nature, for nature is not in a hurry and mankind is.655

Within the energy-harvesting field, DSSCs represent the most remarkable example of biomimetic devices, directly inspired by the photosynthesis. Many different types of synthetic and natural dyes have been investigated and a large set of data on the relationship between their chemical structure and PV performance of DSSCs have been collected. Despite the literature on the subject and notable progress made in power-conversion efficiency, there is still a long way to go before vegetable-based DSSCs can be considered an alternative to other PV technologies.656 While it would be beneficial to use vegetable dyes because they are more abundant, cheaper and eco-friendly with respect to synthetic metal-complexes and/or organic dyes, the efficiency of DSSCs sensitized by vegetable dyes is still lower than that achieved by the aforementioned synthetic dyes. The highest efficiency of DSSCs based on vegetable dye extracts, as reported in Fig. 31, is still lower than 5% for chlorophyll-based DSSC. Nevertheless, as discussed in Section 7, chlorophylls as sensitizers are less expensive than Ru-dyes (18%) or synthetic organic dyes (45%), see Fig. 30. Thus, in terms of performance/cost ratio, some vegetable-based DSSC, such as those sensitized with chlorophyll, have a potential advantage over both Ru-based and synthetic organic DSSCs. Even though vegetable dyes based DSSC have a lower efficiency, see Fig. 31, they could be used in some niche applications, e.g., indoor applications657 (diffuse light) for powering small systems and home appliances, battery chargers for portable devices or educational demonstrators, where they can in principle compete with silicon-based solar cells. Nevertheless, to make natural dyes a solution of choice for DSSC technology, additional efforts are needed as briefly outlined below.

Efficient natural sensitizers, as well as artificial ones, need to fulfill stringent properties such as harvesting light in wide range of the solar spectrum, bind strongly to the semiconductor nanoparticles and, upon excitation, efficiently inject electrons into the semiconductor conduction band. Moreover they require a rapid regeneration by the electrolyte and should exhibit a high VOC and high stability. Usually, the low VOC value, affecting the wide majority of vegetable dye-based DSSCs, is ascribed to a shift of the TiO2 Fermi level, which follow the adsorption of vegetal dye onto semiconductor layer, and to the dark current;41 and future research should address these two issues. Moreover, the dye structure is another fundamental parameter that affects the DSSCs performance. Steric hindrance of the dye molecules limits their ability to uniformly bind to the semiconductor surface, affecting the electron transfer process at the photoanode. Dye aggregation is another critical issue that deserves further investigation. Indeed, dye aggregation on the semiconductor layer produces high absorbance which does not directly translate into efficient electrons injection. Several kinds of additives such as chenodeoxycholic acid,289,303 or other compounds with either long alkyl chains (e.g., hexadecylmalonic acid)658 or bulky groups (e.g., dineohexyl bis(3,3-dimethylbutyl)) phosphinic acid659 can be exploited as co-adsorbant avoiding the dye aggregation on the semiconductor layer.

Another key point to be addressed in the development of vegetable dye-based DSSCs is the electrolyte. Indeed, unlike conventional DSSCs (based on synthetic organic/inorganic dyes), where enormous effort has been made to develop efficient electrolytes, vegetal dye-based DSSC still require optimization. A number of electrolyte additives have been developed for DSSC based on synthetic dyes to enhance the efficiency of the cell and to stabilize its PV performances. Carboxylic (e.g., hexadecylmalonic acid) and phosphonic acid derivatives (e.g., decylphosphonic acid) have been used to reduce carrier recombination, preserving dye integrity. 4-tert-Butylpyridine and N-methylbenzimidazole are commonly used as electrolyte additives, suppressing the dark current thus improving the efficiency of the cell.235,660 However, vegetable dyes such as anthocyanins, are pH sensitive, making additives such as 4-tert-butylpyridine or similar compounds less desirable. In fact, the basic nature of 4-tert-butylpyridine influences the dye molar extinction coefficient thus affecting the optical absorption properties (i.e., a red-shift in the absorption spectrum). Moreover, the 4-tert-butylpyridine addition shifts the conduction band edge toward negative potentials, minimizing the recombination losses due to dark currents. The addition of various cations on the electrolyte has been investigated and found to increase the PV performance of DSSCs. For example, Li in the form of Li+ intercalates in the TiO2 nanoparticles due to its small size or strongly adsorbs at their surface. The presence of Li can cause a positive shift in the flatband of TiO2 with a consequent reduction of VOC, and can also accelerate the rate of oxidized dye reduction by I.

A common problem, not only intrinsic to vegetable dyes, in DSSC technology is the back electron injection from the TiO2 to the oxidized dye. The creation of interfaces between the dye and the semiconductor could be a viable solution to overcome such issues. For example, a Schottky barrier, which is a potential energy barrier for electrons at the metalsemiconductor junction, produced by inserting a metal layer between the semiconductor nanoparticles and the dye could be a possible strategy to reduce the back electron transfer.661

Metal layers, e.g., gold nanoparticles can also enhance charge separation in DSSCs,662 due to their ability to accept and conduct the current from the excited state of natural dyes to TiO2, thus preventing charge recombination. Unfortunately, the main problem relies on the anchoring of the vegetable dye to metal nanoparticles layer, affecting the electron transfer efficiency from the dye to the nanoparticles themselves. A solution could be the use of molecular layers as bridge structure, able to anchor with the nanoparticles and at the same time linking the vegetable dye.

The successful use of vegetable dyes in DSSCs not only depends on the identification of the right dyes, but also on the ability to modify on-demand their photo-electrochemical properties. Although optimization of vegetable dyes is in progress, exploiting functional groups to improve the charge transfer between the dye and electrolyte mediator and from the dye to the semiconductor layer surface,663 more effort is still required. The exploitation of carbon-based nanomaterials (e.g., graphene and carbon nanotubes) to modify the architecture of the semiconductor film could improve the electron lifetime thus reducing the dark current at the photoanode. Preliminary results,664i.e., the incorporation of graphene in the anthocyanin dye mixture and the subsequent co-adsorption onto the titanium nanoparticles to suppress electron recombination, suggest that this could be a route worthy of further study. Another area where carbon-based nanomaterials can strongly contribute in the improvement of DSSCs is the replacement of expensive Pt-based counter electrodes.

Panchromatic systems, sensitive to all the light in the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum, are often used successfully in DSSCs because of their capability to harvest a broader portion of the solar spectrum in comparison to individual dyes. Vegetable dyes are ideal systems for such a task due to their diverse and complementary absorption properties. Unfortunately, although vegetable dyes show high molar extinction coefficient over a wide absorption range, they tend to have low efficiencies when used as dyes in DSSCs. An advanced panchromatic system could be developed preparing a double layer (bicolor photoanode),665 exploiting a similar approach to the one used in photography for the preparation of color films.

The photo-stability of vegetable dyes-based DSSCs is another crucial point that requires further investigation.666 Indeed, the dye must be capable of many redox cycles (turnover higher than 106, i.e., number of cycles that a dye molecule can support within a cell calculated from the ratio of dye regeneration rate by electrolyte and oxidative dye degradation rate) without undergoing decomposition. Nature supplies a large variety of vegetable dyes, designed to protect fruits and flowers in different ways, e.g., antioxidant activity, and scientists working in this field are called to exploit them alone or in mixture (co-pigmentation) to enhance the stability and efficiency of vegetable dye-based DSSCs. The use of UV and anti-reflective filters also improve dye reliability light reflection. Another route toward the increase of dye photo-stability could be the use of co-pigmentation and/or the use of additives linking the dye with the semiconductor layer without affecting the photo-electrochemical properties, while still preserving the stability against heating or undesired redox reactions.667

Life-cycle analyses now available for conventional DSSC PV systems668,669 show a limited environmental impact of such PV technology and vegetable dyes are expected to decrease such impact even further.
