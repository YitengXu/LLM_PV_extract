An investigation of crystal structure, surface area and surface chemistry of strontium niobate and their influence on photocatalytic performance

Paraskevi Efstathiou a, Xiaoxiang Xu a, Hervé Ménard b and John T. S. Irvine *a
aSchool of Chemistry, EaSTChem, University of St Andrews, St Andrews, Fife KY16 9ST, UK. E-mail: jtsi@st-andrews.ac.uk; Fax: +44 (0)1334 463808; Tel: +44 (0)1334 463817
bSasol Technology (UK) Ltd., St Andrews, KY16 9ST, Scotland, UK

First published on 18th April 2013

Abstract

Sr1−xNbO3 is an unusual material that displays both metallic type conduction and photocatalytic activity, despite being an NbIV oxide, and it sustains photo-oxidation without degradation. The influence of crystal structure, surface area and surface chemistry on the photocatalytic activity of strontium niobate has been investigated. The crystal structure of strontium niobate depends on the Sr content of the A site, with cubic symmetry for Sr ≤ 0.92 and orthorhombic symmetry for 0.92 < Sr ≤ 0.97. The change of crystal structure from cubic to orthorhombic symmetry seems to have a negative effect on the photocatalytic activity, as the NbO6 octahedra become distorted and unfavourable for d-orbital overlapping. The photocatalytic activity increased significantly by enlarging the surface area through ball milling, nevertheless, a clear trend for the surface area effect on activity is not obtained among samples with different Sr content. An enrichment of Sr on the surface of strontium niobate was observed by XPS, which seems to be a governing factor for improving stability.

Introduction

Green energy is our civilization's hope in order to escape from the increasing and disastrous demand for fossil fuels. Photocatalysis is a method that converts light energy into chemical energy in an appropriate material (usually a semiconductor) and through this process new fuels can be produced. Specifically in the case of water splitting under visible light, precious H2 and O2 are produced just by exploitation of the solar light. Unfortunately, very few materials have been found to be able to carry out the whole water splitting procedure and even less can do that under visible, instead of UV, light.2,3 Successful water splitting under visible light is often achieved by the production of a Z-scheme,4–8 which is a system consisting of two photocatalysts, connected by a redox couple (e.g. Fe3+/Fe2+), one photocatalyst produces H2 and the other produces O2. However, much study has been focused on materials’ ability to complete the water splitting half reactions (either H2 or O2 production) using a sacrificial reagent. The fact that a material succeeds in photocatalytically producing either H2 or O2 with the help of a sacrificial reagent is a good indication of the material's potential to photocatalytically fully split water. The most efficient and most widely studied material, although absorbing in the UV, with many applications is TiO29–17 and the form which is found to be the most photocatalytically active is that of anatase as opposed to that of rutile.9 However, given the importance of exploiting as large an amount of solar energy as possible the value of materials that can exhibit photocatalytic activity under visible light is profound.

Research to improve the photocatalytic process focuses both on the enhancement of the efficiency of the photocatalytic reactions, as well as on the design of the photocatalyst in order to be able to absorb and operate under solar or visible light. Some of the steps taken are loading the photocatalyst with a metal or an oxide (e.g. small amounts of Pt or NiO) that work as co-catalysts, or introducing sacrificial reagents (e.g. oxalic acid) in order to introduce active sites for H2 evolution, or facilitate hydrogen production by providing a more favourable oxidation path than water oxidation. These actions have proven to enhance the reactivity of the material.15,18,19 Studies have also been carried out on the size of the semiconductor particles. It was found that the smaller the particle size of the TiO2 catalyst, the more effective the photocatalytic reactions.10,20 This is expected as a result of the increase of surface area, hence the increase of active sites on the surface of the photocatalyst. An effective way of achieving particle size reduction is ball milling.20–22 Alongside surface area increase, more phenomena take place during the ball milling of the samples, some of which are potentially harmful to the photocatalytic performance (e.g. decrease of crystallinity and creation of structural defects).20 Therefore, particular care is required during the process.

Many metal oxides have already been reported to be photocatalytically active under visible light22–25 using different materials as sacrificial reagents. The majority of materials used for light harvesting applications are semiconductors, due to their ability when irradiated to produce electrons and holes, which separated by the band gap, constitute reactive reduction and oxidation sites respectively. However, strongly coloured metals could substitute the use of semiconductors since the electron emissions between bands after irradiation could still achieve the necessary electron and hole production, the separation of which could be favoured by increased mobility. In this report we address such a strongly coloured metal oxide with metallic behaviour, strontium niobate, as a possible photocatalyst that under visible light can produce H2 from the water splitting half reaction. In addition, we study the material's behaviour before and after a ball milling process intending to increase the material's surface area and correlate the different parameters that determine the photocatalytic performance it exhibits.

In our previous work,1 it has been shown that strontium niobate successfully achieved the water splitting half-reaction of H2 production under visible light irradiation with oxalic acid as a sacrificial reagent, as well as the photooxidative degradation of methylene blue and oxygen evolution from water with Ag+ as a sacrificial reagent. In addition, its conductivity was measured equal to 3 × 103 S cm−1 for x = 0.2 and it was found to decrease slightly when the temperature increased, in agreement with what has already been reported in the literature.26–28 The temperature coefficient of resistance was calculated to be αt = 2.05 × 10−4 K−1. Thus, the material is a red metallic oxide.

As a metal the excitation of electrons cannot be between the occupied valence (VB) and the almost empty conduction band (CB), as in the case of semiconductors, as the electronic structure is quite different and the conduction band has a significant fraction of its states occupied. Instead excitation occurs between the highest fully occupied band (B1) and the CB or the CB and the lowest unoccupied band (B−1). In the case of strontium niobate the absorption energy gap, as taken by the UV-VIS absorbance spectra, is ∼1.9 eV (Fig. 1) as calculated by Kubelka–Munk transformations.29

Strontium niobate is an oxide with perovskite structure. That means that it has the formula ABO3, where A and B are cations, and it has an ideal cubic structure with NbO6 octahedra sharing corners in all 3 dimensions. It can only be synthesised as A-site deficient (Sr1−xNbO3) for 0.3 ≥ x ≥ 0.03. Its colour changes from blue to red as the Sr content increases.26,30 Above and below those limits the material gives complex XRD patterns revealing more than one phase. It has also been reported that its ideal cubic structure is distorted to a lower symmetry structure, the orthorhombic one, for x < 0.1 and particularly for x = 0.03.26 Furthermore, the material is slightly oxygen deficient (small δ) and at temperatures higher than 300 °C it starts to get oxidized in air, as shown by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Fig. 2). When fully oxidized, the material turns into a white, amorphous powder. This A-site deficiency, and particularly the Sr content of the synthesized samples, seems to determine the material's characteristics correlating to the photocatalytic activity it exhibits. Specifically, the fact that while the Sr content increases the Nb gets more reduced leads to changes not only in the crystal structure26,30,31–33 (cubic → orthorhombic) but also to its electronic configuration, hence the absorption energy gap and nature of the material's surface. Different phenomena take place during the ball milling of the samples too, with a beneficial effect on the photocatalytic performance (increase of surface area and possible oxidation of the surface).


Fig. 2 TGA analysis of Sr0.9NbO3−δ, heating up to 900 °C at 5° min−1 under air, holding for 5 minutes and cooling back to room temperature. The material starts to be oxidized at ∼300 °C. In this case δ is found to be 0.12.

In this report, we have focused on the synthesis of the strontium niobate compound (Sr1−xNbO3) with different strontium content, varying between 0.2 ≥ x ≥ 0.03, and using it as a photocatalyst for the production of H2 from water splitting, using 0.025 M oxalic acid as a sacrificial reagent. The compound's photocatalytic performance under visible light has been screened, both before and after ball milling, in order to determine the role that the three important parameters play in the performance of the oxide: structure, surface area, surface morphology.

Experimental

Synthetic procedure

Earlier syntheses of strontium niobates Sr1−xNbO3 have encountered many difficulties, with pure samples of good homogeneity being rather hard to obtain.30,34 The challenges are the reaction conditions as well as the choice of precursors. Previous methods using SrO as a precursor under vacuous quartz conditions have many drawbacks, not only because SrO is sensitive towards CO2 and H2O, but also because it may react with quartz to form stable silicates.27,34 Later, replacing SrO with strontium niobate precursors such as Sr5Nb4O15 or Sr4Nb2O9 was very helpful but a long reaction time was still needed.27 This was due to the low reaction temperature that is limited by the softening temperature of quartz (1200–1250 °C).30 Recent work has used a niobium metal container (ampoules or tubes) under vacuum or under flowing Ar conditions.27,28,31 Here we use Sr4Nb2O9 as a precursor and the reaction is performed under flowing Ar conditions.

The precursor powder Sr4Nb2O9 was mixed in stoichiometric amounts with Nb metal (Alfa Aesar, 99.8%, −325 mesh) in order to produce samples with Sr contents (x = 0.2–0.03) that cover most of the range for which the material can be synthesized.30 After the two reagents are mixed, they are ground for at least 40 minutes with a mortar and pestle until the mixture is as homogeneous as possible. Then pellets of 13 mm are pressed under a pressure of 50 MPa and they are calcined, wrapped in molybdenum foil, at 1400 °C under a reducing atmosphere (Ar) for 20 h. Pellets of bright red colour are obtained. The precursor powder Sr4Nb2O9 is synthesised by mixing SrCO3 (Aldrich, ≥99.9%) and Nb2O5 (Aldrich, 99.9%, −325 mesh) in a stoichiometric ratio and grinding until a homogeneous mixture is attained. Calcination at 1200 °C for 24 h follows, then the mixture is ground again and the calcination process is repeated, before the precursor powder is successfully synthesized.

Electrical conductivity was measured on sintered pellets using a standard 4-probe DC method at room temperature (20 °C). The measurement was carried out on a home-made jig with 4 terminals.35 Each terminal is connected to a Pt strip with 1 mm thickness. All Pt strips are parallel to each other with a separation of 3 mm. The inner two Pt strips were used as potential probes and the outer two as current probes. A current of 100 mA (model: Keithley 220, Keithley Instruments Inc, USA) was applied subsequently in both directions and the resistance was measured as a gradient of potential vs. current. The electrical conductivity was then calculated using the geometrical factor of the pellets.

Characterization

X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis took place on a PANalytical Empyrean Series 2 diffractometer system to ensure the purity and crystallinity of the samples, with a step of 0.0167° and data collection time 85090 s at every step. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) to calculate the actual oxygen content of the material was done by heating up the samples from room temperature (25 °C) to 900 °C under air and then cooling back down to room temperature. The ultraviolet–visible absorbance (UV-VIS) spectra were collected on a JASCO-V550 spectrophotometer and the band gap energies (Eg) were calculated by the Kubelka–Munk transformation. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed on a Kratos AXIS Ultra DLD, with a monochromatic Al Kα (1486.6 eV) X-ray source and for peak calibration adventitious C was used at 285.0 eV. Then the binding energies of the peaks acquired were compared and found to be in accordance with the ones reported in the literature.34 The microstructure of samples was inspected by a scanning electron microscope (SEM) on a JEOL 5600 SEM. Ball milling was used to decrease the particle size of the material and increase the surface area in a Fritsch Planetary Micro Mill Pulverisette 7 using zirconia balls as milling media and isopropanol as a dispersant. The attained surface area was measured in a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 V3.00 H surface area analyzer, with nitrogen as an adsorptive gas, and the BET equation (Brunauer, Emmett and Teller, 1938) was used for the calculation. The particle size distribution (PSD) was carried out in a Matersizer 2000 Hydro 2000 s Malvern Instruments analyzer and isopropanol with 2% triton was used as a dispersant.

Photocatalytic testing

The photocatalytic activity of the samples was evaluated by their hydrogen production under visible light irradiation after a period of 18–26 h. The lamp used is a 250 W iron-doped metal halide ultraviolet–visible lamp with a UV cut-off filter (λ ≥ 420 nm; Borosilicate Coated Glass HM07, UQG (optic), Cambridge, UK). An amount of 0.2 g of the photocatalyst was used for the non-ball milled samples and 0.1 g for the ball milled ones and it was dispersed in a quantity of 200 mL of aqueous solution, which contained 0.025 M oxalic acid as a sacrificial reagent. The reactor used is a home-made metallic box with PTFE lining on the inside and a quartz glass lid that seals tightly on top, where the irradiation comes through to reach the photocatalyst. A water jacket, with constant flow, on the outside of the box keeps the temperature constant and close to room temperature. The H2 produced was measured by an online gas chromatograph (Agilent 3000 Micro Gas Chromatograph).

Results and discussion

Phase compositions and conductivity

Perovskite strontium niobate can exhibit different crystal structures depending on the Sr content in the A sites. Cubic symmetry (space group Pmm) has been reported when the occupancy of Sr lies between (1 − x) = 0.75 and 0.9,27,28 while distorted structures with a higher Sr occupancy have been found at room temperature with decreased symmetries.26,31

More specifically, the material exhibits a cubic phase until around x = 0.08 and from that point on, the NbO6 octahedra reorientate and the structure adopts the less symmetric form of the orthorhombic phase.26,30,31–33 In our synthesis, a series of samples (Sr1−xNbO3−δ) were prepared with Sr content among 0.8 and 0.97. XRD patterns of prepared samples can be indexed with cubic or orthorhombic symmetry (Fig. 3), consistent with previous results. Some impurity peaks were occasionally present in the patterns but they were quite small. The refined unit cell parameters are listed in Table 1. A gradual increase in the unit cell parameters from Sr0.8NbO3 to Sr0.97NbO3 was observed and can be attributed to the decrease of A site vacancies as well as the reduction of Nb5+ (ionic radius 0.64 Å at 6 coordination) to Nb4+ (ionic radius 0.69 Å at 6 coordination)36 within the structure.41

The measured DC electrical conductivity at room temperature for the samples was presented in our previous work.1 Although the electrical conductivity is still lower than that of typical metals such as silver (6.3 × 105 S cm−1) or copper (5.96 × 105 S cm−1), it is in the same order of magnitude as graphite or some metal alloys like nichrome (∼103 S cm−1).37–39 Our previous measurement and other reports confirmed that the conductivity has metallic behaviour, i.e. decreasing as the temperature increases. Therefore, they are essentially metallic conductors. However, the highest conductivity was observed for sample Sr0.8NbO3 where Nb was least reduced (average valence +4.4) among all the measured samples. Prior reports show similar magnitudes,27,28 although values do vary probably due to differing microstructures of the sintered pellets.

From SEM images (Fig. 4), it can be seen that the freshly sintered pellets all present a porous surface with particle sizes as large as several microns. The measured density for all pellets only reaches around 56% of the theoretical density.


Fig. 4 SEM images from the surface of the sintered pellets, (a) Sr0.9NbO3 and (b) Sr0.8NbO3 showing a particle size of around 10 μm.

UV-VIS diffuse reflectance spectra

The optical absorption spectra of the sample powders are displayed in Fig. 1. The spectra were converted from diffuse reflectance spectra and Kubelka–Munk transformation was performed to help with the determination of the band gap. The red or purple colour of Sr1−xNbO3 is justified by the absorption tail extending to nearly 700 nm. The corresponding energy gap calculated by extrapolating the onset of Kubelka–Munk curves to the energy axis is around 1.9 eV. Theoretical calculations based on the wavelength-intensity distribution of sunlight suggest an optimal band gap of 1.5 ± 0.5 eV for the maximum conversion efficiency;42 therefore Sr1−xNbO3 can be a promising candidate for solar energy utilisation.

Photocatalytic performance

The photocatalytic activity of Sr1−xNbO3 (0.03 ≤ x ≤ 0.2) samples under visible light irradiation was studied by measuring the H2 produced from water splitting with oxalic acid (0.025 M) as a sacrificial reagent. As can be seen from the H2 production rate of the different samples (Fig. 5), the photocatalytic activity changes with Sr content. In particular it seems to increase for x = 0.2 → x = 0.1, it reaches a maximum for x = 0.06–0.08 and it drops again for x = 0.03. On average the production rate is 5.95 μmol h−1 g−1 and the maximum rate for x = 0.06 is 8.21 μmol h−1 g−1. Time courses of H2 evolution for the samples x = 0.2 and x = 0.1 are shown in Fig. 7. In the same diagram the stable nature of the material can be observed. The steady performance, test after test, gives evidence of no changes taking place in the morphology and surface of the sample (Fig. 6). The material stability was proved by XRD and XPS analysis before and after testing. The same behaviour in photocatalytic activity as seen by their H2 production rate is exhibited by the ball milled samples too (Fig. 5).
Fig. 5 Comparison of the photocatalytic H2 production rate (μmol h−1 g−1) from water splitting under visible light irradiation in aqueous oxalic acid solution (0.025 M) between Sr1−xNbO3 samples before and after ball milling. Columns represent the initial sample and boxes represent the same sample after ball milling.

Again the activity increases until x = 0.06–0.08 where a maximum is observed and then drops for x = 0.03. Additionally, a significant increase in the samples’ performance can be seen after ball milling as opposed to the performance of the same samples before ball milling. Specifically, the performance increased from a minimum of 3 times (for x = 0.14) to a maximum of 14 times (for x = 0.03). The average production rate for the ball milled samples is 23.60 μmol h−1 g−1 (5 times higher than the average rate for non-ball milled samples) and the maximum is 46.14 μmol h−1 g−1 given by x = 0.08. It is clear enough that both the Sr content of the sample, which influences the structure of the material, as well as the surface area of the sample play an important role in determining the photocatalytic performance that it exhibits. Moreover, as shown below, the Sr content at the material's surface may also be important, since perovskites tend to create an Sr rich layer on their surface.40,41 All these parameters influence greatly the photocatalytic activity and it is important to study each of them separately and to determine the relative importance of structure, surface area and surface nature.

The effect of Sr content on the photocatalytic performance

Increasing Sr content in the structure not only increases unit cell parameters, which leads to less orbital overlapping, but also triggers structure changes from cubic to orthorhombic when exceeding (1 − x) = 0.92. In the distorted, orthorhombic phase of strontium niobate, d orbital overlap is reduced in comparison with the cubic symmetric phase. As a result, the charge mobility decreases with increasing Sr content. Such a fact is a downside for the photocatalytic activity of a material that has strong metallic behaviour, since the prevention of electrons and holes recombination is thought to be related to the high charge mobility. On the other hand, increasing the Sr content of the material leads to more reduced Nb cations, so that the bands are enriched with electrons occupying higher energy states. In addition, shifting of the UV-VIS absorbance peaks of the material towards higher frequency (blue shift) was observed when the Sr content in the samples increased, suggesting increased energy gaps (Eg) (Fig. 7). This implies that electrons in the higher Sr content samples are likely to be excited to higher energy levels with higher reducing power. Such an effect is an upside for the photocatalytic activity, particularly for photoreduction. According to experimental data, in the range of Sr content (1 − x) = 0.8 − 0.92 where cubic structure is still maintained, increasing Sr content has a positive effect on the photocatalytic activity. However further increase of the Sr content seems to have a negative effect on the photocatalytic activity as the crystal structure changes from cubic to orthorhombic and becomes more distorted.
Fig. 7 Band gap energy as calculated by Kubelka–Munk transformations from the UV-VIS spectra of the samples plotted against the stoichiometric Sr content of every Sr1−xNbO3 sample.

The effect of surface area on photocatalytic activity

The surface area increases greatly after the ball milling of the samples. The XRD patterns of the samples remain the same revealing that the material suffers no chemical transformation; however the peaks are broadened due to the particle size decrease. The unchanged quality of the sample, as well as the achieved particle size decrease, is observed in the SEM pictures too (Fig. 8). The particle size distribution before and after ball milling, after two minutes of sonication in order for the agglomeration to break down and particle size distribution to be more obvious, can be seen in Fig. 9. Before ball milling most of the particles have a particle size of ∼6 μm and after the ball milling process the size drops to a few hundred nm for the majority of the particles. From the broadening of the XRD peaks after ball milling and by using the Debye−Scherrer equation the particle size for (1 − x) = 0.92 was found to be 72 nm and for (1 − x) = 0.94 was found to be 92 nm. The performance of the samples greatly increases after ball milling. However, the plot of the H2 production rate of the samples against their surface area in m2 g−1 (Fig. 10), as measured by BET analysis, reveals that there is no simple dependence of the performance upon the surface area and other factors, such as the Sr content, are also important.

The influence of chemistry on the photocatalytic activity

The relationships between hydrogen production rate, oxygen content and surface chemistry are examined in Fig. 11. XPS measurements clearly show Sr surface enrichment for all the samples. Such Sr enrichment has previously been reported in a number of different perovskite systems.40,41 The Sr content of the surface is found to vary according to the bulk stoichiometric Sr content of the sample up to the point (1 − x) = 0.94. There is a clear correlation with hydrogen production rate; however, changes in both Sr enrichment and hydrogen production rate are thought to depend upon unit cell symmetry suggesting that the correlation is indirect. The actual bulk oxygen content of the samples (Sr1−xNbO3−δ) was calculated from the weight gained during TGA analysis, after heating up to 900 °C under air and then cooling back to room temperature. Oxygen deficiency decreases with decreasing A-site deficiency but seems not related to photocatalytic activity.
Fig. 11 (a) The photocatalytic H2 production rate of the samples, (b) oxygen stoichiometry as determined from TGA in air, similar to Fig. 2. (c) The Sr:Nb ratio content found on the surface of the samples by XPS, all plotted against Sr content (1 − x = 0.86, 0.9, 0.92, 0.94, 0.97).

Conclusions

Strontium niobate is a metallic A-site deficient perovskite oxide that strongly absorbs light of 1.9 eV energy and above. It is photocatalytically active for hydrogen evolution without a precious metal co-catalyst, although a sacrificial element is generally required. Activity depends on several factors and increases with particle surface area and average Nb oxidation state, which increases as Sr deficiency decreases. These tendencies are disrupted when the structure distorts from cubic to orthorhombic at high Sr contents where activity decreases. The material is stable against photocatalytic degradation and reaction with water at ambient temperatures, which might not be expected for such a low Nb oxidation state material. Absorption of light by interband transitions in a strongly coloured metallic oxide is not surprising; however, that this light can yield excitations able to drive photocatalytic reactions is surprising as recombination would be expected to dominate. In a semiconductor a depletion layer between n- and p-type regions near the surface allows photocarrier separation, but this would not be possible in a metallic oxide. The observation of Sr enrichment at the surface via XPS does suggest an answer to these problems as the Sr-rich layer would serve to chemically protect the underlying reduced Nb oxide and would provide a surface potential barrier to facilitate charge separation.
