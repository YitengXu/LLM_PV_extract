Perspectives on ab initio molecular simulation of excited-state properties of organic dye molecules in dye-sensitised solar cells

Abstract

In this mini-review, we summarise and critique the emerging field of quantum-based molecular simulation of dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSCs), with particular focus on the deployment of organic-based dyes therein. We assess the underlying methodologies, including developments, pitfalls and challenges, whilst gauging predictive performance vis-à-vis experimental performance. The predictive capabilities of simulation methods with respect to elucidation of underlying methods is considered in the light of progress towards the ultimate goal of predictive in silico design of DSSCs, to complement hand-in-hand experimental approaches in the development of state-of-the-art DSSC devices.

Introduction

The search for low-cost, efficient solar cells has inspired a great deal of interest in dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSCs), based on sensitisers (dyes) adsorbed to the surfaces of inexpensive transition metal oxides, such as TiO2.1 The more established family of dyes are coordination complexes of ruthenium; however, given the expense of such dyes and their less than environmentally friendly nature, the focus in emerging DSSC technologies has shifted more recently towards the use of more inexpensive and versatile organic dyes as possible replacements for Ru-dyes.

A unique distinguishing feature of DSSCs from conventional inorganic photovoltaic (PV) systems, such as Si solar cells, is that light absorption and charge carrier generation are effected by the sensitiser, and are effectively independent from the nature of the semiconductor, whose primary function is charge separation and electron transport. Therefore, the electron- and hole-transporting functions are spatially separated, leading to significantly suppressed charge recombination and hence more efficient device charge collection.1 DSSCs can be designed by the appropriate choice of sensitiser to absorb selected spectral regions of solar radiation whilst still maintaining a high level of transparency in the remaining wavelength range.2,3

A typical DSSC is composed of a mesoporous semiconductor metal oxide, an adsorbed layer of dye on the semiconductor surface, an electrolyte, and a counter electrode.1–3 The surface-adsorbed sensitisers are excited by the incident light of suitable energy. The excited dye then injects an electron into the conduction band of the semiconductor, leaving the oxidised sensitiser molecule to gather an electron from the surrounding electrolyte. The electron in the conduction band is taken through the external circuit where it performs external electric work before reaching the platinised counter electrode. The sensitiser returns to its neutral ground state by accepting an electron from the I−/I3− redox couple present in the electrolyte.4 In turn, the redox couple receives an electron from the counter electrode and completes the electrical circuit. Although this mechanism seems straightforward, it involves a high level of complexities and disparate timescales. A recent review by Hagfeldt et al.5 provides a brief description of the intricacies involved in the process.

The overall cell efficiency depends on several factors. For instance, the electron from the conduction band can recombine with the photo-oxidised dye molecule or the redox mediator in the electrolyte solution, thereby reducing cell performance.5,6 Also, the efficiency of electron transfer from sensitiser to the semiconductor's conduction band depends upon the excited-state redox potential, and the nature of electron flow from the donor to the acceptor moieties of the sensitiser. The complexity of the system also provides several ways to improve the performance of the cell. For example, the introduction of π-conjugated ring(s) or a push–pull type electron flow improves the stability as well as the performance of the sensitiser.7 In addition, improved electronic coupling between the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital of the sensitiser and conduction band of the semiconductor may improve charge transfer between the dye and titania.8,9

In this review, we will concentrate on the molecular mechanisms of photo-excitation in the DSSC core, namely the dye-sensitised mesoporous semiconductor oxide film, and explore the key charge injection processes taking place at the dye–metal oxide interface, with a particular focus on progress and challenges in quantum molecular simulation10 to model these processes.

Dyes

In DSSCs, a monolayer of sensitiser is adsorbed on the semiconductor surface. Among various proposed dyes, state-of-the-art ruthenium-based sensitisers have high-energy conversion efficiencies exceeding 11%.11–13 However, ruthenium-based dyes are expensive and generally possess a much lower molar absorption coefficient (ε) compared to organic dyes. Dyes with a higher absorption coefficient allows for the fabrication of DSSCs with thinner light-absorbing semiconductor-dye layers. To reduce the manufacturing cost of DSSCs, various metal-free, organic sensitisers are now being proposed and investigated; these organic dyes are advantageous due to their adjustable light harvesting and electrochemical properties.14,15 A typical organic sensitiser consists of a donor moiety (an electron-donating group), an acceptor moiety (an electron-accepting group), together with a linker moiety, as shown in Fig. 1.

To design new efficient organic dyes for DSSCs some general criteria must be observed,5,14 as listed below:

(a) A dye must absorb light completely in the visible region and also a good part of the near-IR part of the terrestrial solar spectrum.

(b) The molar extinction coefficient (ε) of the dye should be sufficiently high to absorb essentially all of the light within a narrow path length.

(c) The dye should have its highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) localised at the donor moiety and its lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) localised at the anchor/acceptor part, which is important for efficient charge separation, leading to ‘a push–pull effect’.5,14

(d) The HOMO of the dye cation should be situated at a lower energy compared to the redox mediator, such that rapid regeneration of the dye is easily achievable.

(e) The adsorbed dye may be desorbed from TiO2 due to the presence of trace amounts of water present in the electrolyte. To prevent such desorption, and concomitant accompanying cell instability, the dye must have peripheral hydrophobic groups.

(f) Electron back-transfer from the semiconductor conduction band to the dye cation can occur, leading to lower incident photon-to-current conversion efficiencies (IPCE or external quantum efficiency, EQE). The probability for this electron back-transfer could be reduced by locating a net positive charge at the donor moiety of the dye cation. As the donor moiety is placed away from the titania–dye interface and much closer to the redox mediator in the electrolyte, this dye architecture also facilitates rapid reduction of the cation, and, hence, dye regeneration.

(g) The dye should adsorb only as a monolayer on the semiconductor surface and must not aggregate.

In accordance with these guidelines, an appreciable number of new organic dyes have been designed, synthesised and studied as DSSC sensitisers, with a number of different donor and acceptor groups and linkers. In particular, commonly used donor groups include N,N-dimethylaniline,16,17 coumarins,7,18–23 tetrahydroquinolines,24,25 pyrrolidine,26 carbazoles,27 diphenylamines,28 triphenylamines.29–32 A linker moiety is a spacer added between the donor and acceptor moieties. Different π-conjugated linker groups have been investigated in many new dye structures, such as (–CC–) chains,7,16–19,22,30 thiophene units (and derivatives).7,20,22–28,32,33 Thiophene linkers are favored over methine units (CC double bonds) due to the increased possibility of the latter forming isomers, which might lead to instability of the dye in the long term. In particular, two types of acceptor/anchor groups are used widely in organic dye synthesis, namely cyanoacrylic acid7,16–32 and carboxylic acid.34–36 Apart from carboxylic acid anchors, rhodamine-3-acetic acid37 and sulphonate (−SO3−)38,39 have also been studied. Here, we will discuss some of the organic dyes comprising these moieties.

Very recently, Yella et al. have reported a record DSSC efficiency of around 12% in mesocopic porphyrin-sensistised solar cells with a cobalt II/III-based electrolyte, which makes use of the iodide/triiodide redox couple mechanism.40 The use of porphyrin dyes offers a more cost-effective solution than Ru dyes, as do organic dyes, and this is an important future avenue of research, along with the optimisation of electrolyte and redox mechanism for long-term performance, durability and DSSC operational lifetime.

Semiconductors

By far, the most commonly used semiconductor in DSSCs is TiO2, but ZnO,41 SnO242–44 and Nb2O545–47 have also been studied to a limited extent. Some ternary oxides, namely SrTiO348 and Zn2SnO4,49 have also been reported. Apart from these, core–shell structures like ZnO-coated SnO2 are also under investigation.5 All of these metal oxides have wide band gaps and absorb light only in the ultra violet (UV) region of the solar spectrum. Although research is continuously underway to find alternative metal oxides for deployment in DSSCs, TiO2 has given the best solar cell efficiencies to date.

TiO2 semiconductor

O'Regan and Grätzel1 used mesoporous TiO2 for dye sensitisation for the first time in 1991, and since then it has been widely seen as the best semicondcutor oxide for DSSCs. TiO2 has found widespread use in various products like sunscreen UV absorber creams, paints, toothpastes and food (E171).5 TiO2 is found naturally in mainly three crystalline forms namely, rutile (tetragonal, a = b = 4.587 Å, c = 2.953 Å),50 anatase (tetragonal, a = b = 3.782 Å, c = 9.502 Å) and brookite (rhombohedral, a = 5.436 Å, b = 9.166 Å, c = 5.135 Å).51 Apart from these three, one other form called cotunnite52 is also formed and is reported as the hardest polycrystalline material available. Among these forms, only rutile (cf.Fig. 2) and anatase (cf.Fig. 3) are the most adopted forms for solar energy conversion applications. In fact, brookite transforms to the rutile form even at low temperature. In rutile, as well as in anatase structures, one titanium atom binds with six surrounding oxygen atoms.

Dye adsorption on TiO2

Kalyanasundaram and Grätzel57 have summarised six modes of adsorption of dyes on metal oxide surfaces. These modes are: (1) direct attachment of dye on the surface via covalent bonding/attachment; (2) electrostatic interactions occurring due to ion exchange, ion-pairing or donor–acceptor interactions; (3) hydrogen bonding; (4) self-assembly of hydrophobic groups of long-chain fatty acid derivatives; (5) physisorption of dye on the surface via van der Waals interactions; and (6) physical entrapment. The adsorption mode of carboxylic group on TiO2 is important to insure efficient charge transfer from the donor group.

In this section, we will focus mainly on studies relevant to the dye adsorption through the carboxylic acid anchor groups on the anatase (101) surface. Dye adsorption through carboxylic acid anchors can take place mainly through chemisorption.5,58,59 On TiO2 surfaces, chemisorption occurs through acidic dissociation. Here, a hydrogen atom dissociates from the carboxylic acid group and bond formation takes place between the carboxylate oxygen atoms and the surface titanium atoms of the metal oxide. Binding through chemisorption can be of a monodentate ester type, bidentate chelating or bidentate bridging types.5,59

Experimental studies of dye adsorption on TiO2

Finnie et al.60 studied the adsorption of Ru(II) dyes on anatase nanocrystallites with crystallite sizes ranging from 15 to 25 nm through two carboxylic acid groups of bi-isonicotinic acid moieties, using IR and Raman spectroscopic techniques. They found that the carboxylic acid anchor binds through bidentate bridging (type d) or chelating (type c) coordination modes on anatase. In addition, Nazeeruddin et al.59 reported FTIR results showing that ruthenium dyes (N3, N719 and N712) adsorb on anatase in the bidentate bridging mode (type d).

Another FTIR study by Falaras et al.61 on ruthenium–polypyridyl complexes containing two carboxylic acid anchor groups suggests chemisorption via the formation of an ester-like (type b (cf.Fig. 5)) linkage with TiO2. A further study by Duffy et al.62 on ruthenium complexes suggests bidentate bridging (type d) as the mode for carboxylate group adsorption on TiO2.


Fig. 5 Different modes reported for carboxylic acid and carboxyl groups.

A recent FTIR study by Schiffmann et al.63 on various ruthenium-based dyes suggests bidentate bridging (type d) in the absence of (surface) protons and a mixed bidentate bridging or chelating (one of the two binding carboxylic acids bind in each form) in the presence of (surface) protons. The study suggests that both monodentate and bidentate forms are favourable and can interconvert from one to the other. This study also reports that the bidentate bridging form is energetically more stable than the monodentate form, but in presence of protons, the single monodentate (one of the two binding carboxylic acids) form can be stabilised firmly.

Findings of a study by Ma et al.64 supported both the bidentate chelating (type c) and bidentate bridging (type d) modes for two porphyrin derivatives they studied. Further, Weng et al.65 used interface-sensitive transient triplet-state molecular probe methods for adsorption studies of all-trans-retinoic acid (ATRA) adsorption on TiO2 nanoparticles. This study suggests adsorption occurs through anions (type a) and hydrogen bonding (type e, f) by about 3%, through the ester form (type b) by about 63% and through chelating (type c) and bridging (type d) by about 34%.

In contrast, an FTIR study by Hara et al.22 showed that coumarin-based NKX-2311 dye was adsorbed on TiO2 in a bidentate carboxylate mode. Another study by the same group also shows that a N,N-dimethylaniline donor based NKX-2553 dye adsorbs on TiO2 in the bidentate carboxylate mode.16 An FTIR study by Srinivas et al.58 on cyanoacrylic acid adsorption on TiO2 rules out the possibility of physisorption, and authors suggest that cyanoacrylic acid adsorption occurs through bidentate bridging mode.

In view of these reports, it is appropriate to consider that the mode of adsorption is significantly influenced by the type of dye as well as on the presence of various other additives in the system. However, the commonly observed bridging mode in most efficient DSSC systems so far is the bidentate mode and it may be considered therefore as the optimal case for DSSC performance.

Theoretical studies on dye adsorption on TiO2

Apart from experimental studies, a few theoretical studies58,66–69 for adsorption of carboxylic acid derivatives on TiO2 surface have also been reported. Vittadini et al.67 studied formic acid (HCOOH) and sodium formate (HCOONa) adsorption on an anatase-(101) surface using a Car–Parrinello (CP) approach with generalised gradient approximation (GGA) functionals and ultrasoft (US) pseudopotentials. Their results suggest monodentate-binding for formic acid and bidentate-bridging binding for sodium formate with a two-fold coordinated oxygen atom on dry anatase surfaces. However, on hydrated anatase surfaces, formate ions favour binding in the bidentate-bridging mode and acid favors the monodentate-binding mode. The authors also reported that the bidentate-chelating mode is unstable.

Along with the experimental work (discussed in the previous section), Srinivas et al.58 also investigated the theoretical aspects of adsorption using the Perdew–Wang 1991 (PW91) exchange-correlation functional and US pseudopotentials, and concluded that bidentate-bridging modes bind best on anatase-(101), which coincides with their experimental findings. Persson et al. used periodic Intermediate Neglect of Differential Overlap (INDO) semi-empirical quantum methods for binding studies of bi-isonicotinic acid on anatase (101) surfaces.70 Their results suggest the bidentate bridging (type d) has the least binding energy (most stable) compared to bidentate bridging and monodentate ester-type adsorption modes.

Howie et al.68 reported a DFT study with 6-31G* on three indoline dyes, namely D149,71 D102 and D139.72 Their results suggest that all three dyes adsorbed in a bidentate-bridging mode on the TiO2 surface. Furthermore, Pastore et al.69 also reported a study on D102 and D149 dyes. They performed optimisation of the dye–titania complex using the CP approach, with the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation functional, plane-wave basis set and US pseudopotentials followed by single-point MP2 and DFT calculations with the 6-31G* basis set and the Conductor-like Polarisable Continuum (CPCM) solvation model. This study also suggests a bidentate binding mode for carboxylic acid anchor based indoline dyes adsorbed on anatase (101) nanoparticles. The findings of these theoretical investigations point to the predominance of bidentate-bridging types, as concluded above for experimental observations.

Over the years, very many functionals have been developed, and corresponding variations and improvements have taken place. These benchmarks for these exchange-correlation functionals has been described by Perdew and Schimdt as a ‘Jacob's ladder’.79 There are significant drawbacks and limitations associated with the DFT framework, however, and many of these factors are particularly present in the description of DSSCs. One major difficulty lies in the representation of Coulombic interactions between electrons – arising due to the approximations and conditions used to construct the exchange-correlation functional, and called the self-interaction error (SIE). This SIE is not present in the HF method, as it is cancelled by the exchange term. The shortcoming of this cancellation in DFT manifests itself in incorrect prediction of KS eigenvalues; therefore, ionisation energies are incorrect, activation barriers tend to be less than reliable, especially for SN2 type of reactions, along with proton transfer reactions, band gap (HOMO–LUMO gap) prediction and dissociation energies. To address this shortcoming, Perdew and Zunger have introduced a self-interaction correction (SIC) to the KS orbitals.81 The optimised effective potential and the average density SIC (ADSIC) are other approaches. However, none of these methods tackle the problem of self-energy prediction sufficiently. This requires Green's Function (GW) methods (vide infra) to address satisfactorily.

The overview thus far of the methodologies in use (or of obvious potential for imminent use) in the ab initio simulation of DSSCs requires some further remarks as to the strengths and drawbacks of each method. Although ground-state DFT-like methods, particularly of the KS variety, are very useful for establishing the optimised geometry of (chemically) adsorbed binding motifs/modes, as well as approximate distribution of charge, they are of little use in establishing (photo-) absorption spectra, excitation energies or electron transfer kinetics. TD-DFT is the most widespread method for such predictions (apart from the latter). However, care must be taken vis-à-vis the appropriate selection of functionals in DFT and TD-DFT. There has been widespread use of the B3LYP functional applied to dyes and DSSCs, and less critical examination of alternative functionals – this is a key shortcoming and more accurate functionals are needed, e.g., perhaps of the Coulomb long-range corrected/attenuated type.73 Dreuw et al. have investigated long-range charge-transfer excited states in TD-DFT and concluded that non-local exchange is sine qua non, given that electrostatic attraction between separated charges in long-range exictations is non-local, ipso facto.93 However, as remarked previously, the (TD-)DFT community has focused to a very large extent on B3LYP for such a hybrid-functional approach. Peach et al. have performed a comparative and diagnostic investigation of excitation energies with B3LYP and CAM-B3LYP for a set of 59 excitations of local, Rydberg, and intramolecular charge-transfer character in 18 theoretically challenging main-group molecules.94 It was found that CAM-B3LYP performed best overall, displaying no systematic error vis-à-vis corresponding experimental results. Very recently, Dev et al. have performed a very focused study on a suite of different functionals (PBE, M06L, B3LYP, M06, CAM-B3LYP, and wB97), representing the various classes of approximations that are currently being used to study material properties, and applied these towards the estimation of TD-DFT excitation energies for a variety of different organic dyes, such as 4-(N,N-dimethylamino)benzonitrile, alizarin, squaraine and polyene-linker dyes.95 It was found that CAM-B3LYP performs best in general, and a quantitative measure of orbital overlap was developed to aid in the a priori selection of the most appropriate functional to use in TD-DFT applications.

In addition to considerations of an adroit choice of functional for (TD-)DFT, computational expense and expediency is another key consideration. Although NAMD can offer much insight into real-time electron injection dynamics, along with associated electron–phonon coupling and energy transfer relaxation processes, full-scale MD simulation can be computationally expensive. Even so, in view of the rich information that NAMD offers, this is clearly a highly attractive methodology on the kinetic and dynamic aspects of understanding and manipulating charge transfer mechanisms. GW methods have the drawback of being extremely computationally expensive, but can offer substantially improved absorption spectra and excitation energies, etc. the recent development of the GWL methodology is very promising in this respect.

Charge transfer mechanism

Charge injection from the dye's donor region to the semiconductor's conduction band is one of the key processes determining DSSCs' performance. Therefore, several studies have documented the electron transfer (ET) or charge transfer (CT) rates. Here, we will discuss some of these key studies.96–106 Hannappel et al.96 reported sub-25 fs ET timescales from ruthenium-based dyes [ruthenium(II) cis-di(isothiocyanato)-bis(2,2′-bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate)] to a colloidal anatase TiO2 film. Ellingson97 reported ∼50 fs ET times for [Ru(4,4′-dicarboxy-2,2′-bipyridine)2(NCS)2] (N3) dyes adsorbed on TiO2 films using pump-probe spectroscopy. Lian and coworkers reported the CT time for some Ru-based dyes to TiO2 in sub-100 fs time windows.98,99 Rehm et al.100 reported sub-200 fs ET times from coumarin dye (C343) to TiO2 nanoparticles using ultrafast fluorescence dynamics. Ghosh et al.101 reported a 125 ± 25 fs ET time for the same system using transient IR absorption spectroscopy. Huber et al.102 reported a very fast CT on a 6 fs timescale from alizarin to TiO2 using transient absorption spectroscopy. Further, Schnadt et al.103 reported even faster CT times for bi-isonicotinic acid adsorbed on to TiO2, using resonant photoemission spectroscopy. Slower CT rates have also been observed more recently by O'Regan and co-workers.104–109

The CT rates from the dye to surface rely on electronic coupling between the dye and the metal oxide surface orbitals. Among these, the dye acts as the electron donor and the metal oxide acts as the electron acceptor. To investigate the effect of electronic coupling on charge transfer, Asbury et al.98 introduced CH2 spacer groups between the bipyridine donor and carboxylate anchor groups of the “Re(L1)(CO)3 Cl(ReC1A)” dye. This introduction of the CH2 spacer groups leads to the reduction of electronic coupling between the dye and semiconductor, which serves ultimately to increase the electron injection time upon the increase in the number of spacers.

The CT rate is also determined by the density of states (DOS) of metal oxide coupled with the dye. Asbury et al.99 reported that the CT rate depends on the type of semiconductor orbital involved in the conduction band formation. The conduction band of semiconductors formed from d-orbitals has a higher bulk-DOS compared to the conduction band formed from s-orbitals; therefore, the former leads to stronger coupling with dye and faster ET.98,103 Furthermore, the electronic nature of the conduction band also influences the coupling between the dye and semiconductor. Asbury et al.98 reported femtosecond-time scale CT for a ruthenium-based dye when complexed with TiO2, while the CT time increases when coupled with ZnO or SnO semiconductors on the sub-picosecond scale. Asbury et al., suggested that this could be due to the better overlap between the π* orbital of bipyridine group of a dye with π-symmetry d-orbital of TiO2 conduction band compared to the 4s orbital of ZnO and SnO.99 This led to the conclusion that, if the dye states mix with the conduction band formed form the d-orbitals of semiconductors such as TiO2, WO3 and Nb2O3, faster ET is likely to occur.

Direct and indirect CT mechanisms

In DSSCs, CT can occur through two most likely routes classified as “direct” and “indirect” CT mechanisms. In the direct mechanism, CT takes place in one step from ground state of the dye to the conduction band of the semiconductor. However, in the indirect mechanism, CT takes place in two steps: firstly, from the ground state of the dye to an excited state localised on the dye itself and then to the conduction band of the semiconductor. In direct CT, a new band may appear in the absorption spectrum of the dye–surface complex, which is neither present in the absorption spectrum of the free state of the dye nor that of the semiconductor surface. The origin of the new absorption peak also suggests a strong electronic interaction between the dye and the semiconductor.110,111 Contrary to the direct mechanism, in indirect CT, a new excitation peak does not appear in the absorption spectrum. According to the nature of CT mechanisms, DSSCs can be distinguished into two types. In type-I DSSCs, CT takes place indirectly, whereas in type-II DSSCs, CT occurs via the direct mechanism.

Various theoretical studies have discussed CT mechanisms for N719 Ru(II), [Fe(CN)64−, catechol, alizarin, perylene and coumarin dyes adsorbed on TiO2.112–121 De Angelis et al.115 found for N719 dye, localisation of the HOMO on the dye and the localisation of LUMO, mostly on the titania nanoparticle, led to a small contribution from the dye for the highest excitation in the absorption spectrum. In the absence of a new or shifted absorption band, and on the basis of considering MOs, these authors deduced a direct injection mechanism for N719 dye on TiO2 nanoparticles. De Angelis et al.116 reported a direct CT for the [Fe(CN)6]4−/TiO2 complex due to the localisation of LUMO on TiO2 nanoparticles. The absorption spectrum for this complex also does not show a new peak. Another study by Angelis et al.,117 on perylene dyes adsorbed on TiO2 nanoparticles, suggests non-adiabatic CT for one dye (Dye 1) and direct CT for another dye (Dye 2). Here, the results suggest the localisation of unoccupied MO involved in the photo-excitation on the dye 1 itself. For dye 2, the LUMO is localised on the dye as well as on the surface, suggesting a strong coupling between the dye and the nanoparticle. For dye 1, the absorption spectra shows a λmax at approximately the same energy for both the complexed and the free state of the dye with the absorption spectrum of the complex showing only a slight red shift.

TD-DFT estimation of absorption spectra

The application of TD-DFT towards the calculation of absorption spectra, typically via the linear-response approach outlined above in eqn (10)–(12), has become widespread in the past decade or so. However, it is important to note that linear-response is often inappropriate in intense electric fields, for multi-photon processes, or frequency mixing, where other approaches are necessary.82 Many of the articles cited above, for instance, have also applied TD-DFT to estimate the absorption spectra (typically via the linear-response approach), and comparison with experimental results is always necessary to gauge the quality of the computational spectrum. The key challenges in calculation of absorption spectra lie in the correct prediction of frequency modes (excitation energies) and oscillator strengths; the difficulties of ensuring numerical accuracy in the correct calculation of the eigenvalues of Casida's equation (eqn (12)) have been remarked upon above, along with convergence problems in the more computationally expedient O(N2) Sternheimer approach around resonances or excitations. Naturally, the fundamental challenge of using a reasonable functional has already been discussed (vide supra); as remarked, many in the community use the B3LYP functional essentially exclusively, although the validation of functionals (either a posteriori or, better still, a priori) is a matter of rather existential importance underling the utility and interpretation of TD-DFT spectra. Another important matter affecting the direct comparison with respect to experiment (quite apart from the challenges of experiment per se, e.g., synthesis and securing high purity, etc.) is the correct identification and interpretation of modes from UV/vis spectra, i.e., the correct deconvolution procedure (e.g., Lorentzian/Gaussian) to determine separate excitation modes and their heights and widths – to some extent, this may be subjective, and close communication between the simulator and the experimental data analyst is often necessary.

Bearing these points in mind, it is perhaps appropriate to discuss some examples of work where TD-DFT estimation of spectra has been an important component, although this is by no means intended to be a wholly representative survey of this fast-growing field; indeed, it is not. Catechol and alizarin are very well studied model dye systems and have been profoundly used for the functionalisation of TiO2via ab initio methods.110,112,118–121 Free catechol shows absorbance below 300 nm, while upon adsorption on TiO2 a new absorption band appears at ca. 400 nm. This new absorption band appears due to a strong electronic coupling between catechol and titania as well as due to the direct CT mechanism. In the case of alizarin dye, a red shift has been reported when it is adsorbed on TiO2 (ca. 500 nm) compared to the free state of the dye (ca. 440 nm). On the basis of absorption spectrum and MOs, an indirect CT is envisaged for alizarin to TiO2.

Agrawal et al. have studied via TD-DFT the mechanism of coumarin122 and polyene123 dye CT to titania surfaces. The absorption spectra and deprotonation of the NKX-2311 and NKX-2593 coumarin dyes at titania surfaces were investigated.122 The effect of deprotonation of the sensitisers' carboxylic groups during adsorption on the titania surface was investigated and the excited state electronic properties of the resulting species. The NKX-2553, NKX-2554 and NKX-2569 polyene dyes were also studied123 in terms of performance, in isolation as well as upon their absorption on TiO2 nanoparticles. The performance of the NKX-2569–titania complex was optimal because of the longer linker group. These three dyes123 showed a non-intuitive trend in their respective efficiencies; although NKX-2554 has an additional donor group, it is less efficient compared to the simpler NKX-2553 dye, that contains only one donor group. When the NKX-2554 structure is slightly modified by lengthening the linker group, one obtains the most efficient dye among this set, namely, NKX-2569. It was shown that changes in the donor moiety had very little or no effect on the efficiency of these dyes, but the improved performance of the NKX-2569–titania complex could be understood to be as a result of the longer linker group.123 In both of these studies,122,123 the role of continuum solvation approaches within the modelling was found to lead to markedly better agreement with corresponding experimental spectra.

Conclusions

It has been seen that ab initio simulation of DSSCs has progressed in the past decade or so underpinned by increased computer power and developments in more accurate DFT functionals and theoretical methodologies. In particular, the proper incorporation of dispersive and Coulombic interactions is a key challenge for DFT and TD-DFT. It is hoped that more widespread application of the latest developments in long-range-corrected and hybrid functionals will help advance the more accurate ab initio modelling of DSSCs. Despite the increase in computing power, there remains the inherent problem of DFT to handle larger systems, which is a key drawback to the more realistic modelling of DSSCs, especially for larger dyes where a corresponding larger surface area is needed. Also, given the problems in inherent accuracy of DFT and system sizes that may be simulated, the correct identification of ground-state (chemical) adsorption binding modes and finite-temperature (entropic) effects regarding stability of adsorption modes is also a key challenge. The agreement of absorption spectra (e.g., from TD-DFT) with experiment (e.g., UV-vis spectra) is often challenging to achieve quantitatively, but developments in GW methods (particularly the more efficient GWL technique) offer some encouraging signs for more accurate agreement with experimental data. The role of (continuum) solvation approaches within ab initio modelling should also be emphasised in order to afford a more realistic comparison with experimental conditions vis-à-vis in vacuo calculations; the use of explicit solvation remains a rather elusive computational challenge. Likewise, NAMD techniques are of promising use for characterising and investigating CT dynamics in DSSCs. Therefore, in order for ab initio simulation to mature to the point of being a genuinely predictive in silico molecular design tool, further progress is needed in computational scaling with system size for DFT, TD-DFT and GW methods, together with more accurate functionals. The progress to date in the past decade is certainly encouraging, but a strong reliance on careful calibration with experimental measurement is still essential before this ambitious goal could ever become a more routine reality.
