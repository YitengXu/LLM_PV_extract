Abstract

The introduction of a double-gallium (Ga) gradient plays a key role in pushing the performance of Cu(In,Ga)Se2 (CIGSe) solar cells to a more competitive level. In particular, the location of the front-gradient starting position strongly affects the barrier of the electron transport path, and this is not based on either significantly reducing the electron collection or decreasing the fill factor. Here we present and implement a double-Ga gradient design for CIGSe absorber layers using a diamineâ€“dithiol solution approach. By regulating the front-gradient starting location, three CIGSe absorber layers with different Ga gradients were successfully constructed; these materials maximize solar spectrum absorption and improve the charge collection efficiency. Moreover, this facile solution-process approach does not require expensive vacuum equipment and can be applied to roll-to-roll printing technology. By combining optimal front-gradients with the back gradient, the short-circuit current has been increased by 17% and a maximum improvement in CIGSe efficiency to 13.12% achieved through adjusting the front-gradient to an optimum location.

1 Introduction

Chalcopyrite photovoltaics based on Cu(In,Ga)Se2 (CIGSe) and related materials have proven to be one of the most competitive solar technologies, with steadily increasing record cell efficiencies up to 22.6%.1â€“3 Unfortunately, almost all of the high performance CIGSe solar cells have been fabricated via vacuum-based techniques, including co-evaporation and sputtering; these techniques require expensive vacuum equipment and have high energy consumption.4â€“9 It is believed that a solution-process approach has the potential to significantly lower the cost and increase throughput compared to conventional vacuum-based methods. However, the highest certified efficiency obtained using hydrazine-based solution processed CIGSe solar cells is 17.3%, which is still 30.6% lower than that of vacuum-based devices (22.6%).10 Theoretical and experimental studies have identified that constructing double-Ga gradients is an effective approach for improving the collection of minority carriers and for decreasing recombination that occurs near the molybdenum (Mo) back electrode.11â€“13 One of the benefits of a Ga gradient in CIGSe absorber layers is the formation of an additional electric field, which assists the drift of free electrons towards the front junction. This contributes to an effective collection of charge carriers, especially for near-infrared photon energies.11â€“13 Another advantage is that it results in improving the absorption of low-energy photons because of the presence of a notch region close to the CdS/CIGSe interface. In addition, a small conduction band offset (<0.3 eV) can effectively improve the interface quality.12,14,15 As for the back-gradient, the Ga/(Ga + In) ratio declines gradually with increasing distance from the Mo electrode and comes to an end near the space charge region (SCR). Î¾A can effectively increase the diffusion length of electrons, and we can therefore achieve a higher short circuit current (Jsc) by building a stronger back-gradient. However, the narrower bandgap width at the pâ€“n junction will inevitably lead to a smaller open circuit voltage (Voc). Hence, a front-gradient is introduced to improve Voc, but the front-gradient will cause a transport barrier for photon-generated electrons. If the front-gradient starts from the edge of the SCR, it will not form a barrier for electronic transport at zero bias; however, under high bias, it still forms a barrier to electrons, which affects the transport of electrons. If the front-gradient starts from the quasi-neutral region (QNR), and forms an electron transport barrier at any bias potential, it will significantly decrease the electron collection and reduce both the short circuit current and the fill factor (FF), thus reducing the photoelectric conversion efficiency (PCE).2,17 The selection of the front-gradient should not be based on either significantly reducing electron collection or on FF. Therefore, it is still of great importance to determine the optimal locations of the front-gradients.

Given the discussion above, in this report, we adjusted the starting location of the front-gradient to improve the performance of CIGSe solar cells via a low-toxicity solution-process approach. Four CIGSe thin films with different Ga gradients were successfully prepared and clearly detected. This result firmly demonstrates that the Ga gradients were constructed not only via co-evaporation techniques but also via the solution-process approach. Thus, our approach involves narrowing the efficiency gap between hydrazine and non-hydrazine solution-based CIGSe solar cells, and it is believed that this approach is an effective and key method for pursuing the record efficiency of vacuum-based CIGSe solar cells.10,18 In this work, Cu2S, In2Se3, and Ga were used as the starting materials and dissolved in a mixture of 1,2-ethanedithiol (edtH2) and 1,2-ethylenediamine (en). A particular benefit of this approach is that impurity elements can be absolutely excluded, and the solvents used in the fabrication process are safer and more cost-effective compared to those used in the hydrazine-based solution process.19â€“22

2 Results and discussion

We designed four precursor films with different gradient types based on a thiolâ€“amine solution process to explore systematically the influence of the starting location of front-gradients on the performance of CIGSe solar cells. Schematic illustrations of the fabrication procedure of the CIGSe absorber layers with different Ga gradients are shown in Fig. 1. In this work, we chose Cu2S, In2Se3, Ga and Se powders as starting materials, which are highly soluble in a solvent mixture of 1,2-ethanedithiol/1,2-ethylenediamine (edtH2/en).23,24 Four CIGSe precursor solutions with different Ga ratios (10%, 30%, 35%, and 50%) were prepared by adjusting the [Ga]/([Ga] + [In]) ratios, as shown in Fig. S1.â€  The dissolution of the starting materials is initiated by the transfer of protons from the thiol (edtH2) to the amine (en).25 Because of the presence of the R-NH2 groups, the edtH2 is deprotonated. A possible hypothesis for the dissolution of these metal selenide, metal sulfide, and elemental metal appears to be the formation of thiolatoselenometallate/thiolatometallate anions counterbalanced by +H3N-R-NH3+ cations.26 Three different Ga gradients were designed according to the fabrication procedures illustrated in Fig. 1bâ€“d. We controlled the thickness of each of the four precursor films within the range of 1730â€“1760 nm to better compare the homogeneous films and graded films, and the detailed thicknesses are given in Table S1â€  and Fig. 2. Both the shape of the front-gradient and the notch region with a lower band gap close to the front interface play key roles in enhancing the absorption of low-energy photons.12,27 Therefore, the design of the front-gradient is critical for the performance of the CIGSe devices. In this study, we adjusted the location of the notch region by controlling the thicknesses of stages 2 and 4 and by keeping the thicknesses of stages 1 and 3 at the same level. Using this adjustment, we expected to determine an optimal starting location for the front-gradient to achieve the best performance of the CIGSe solar cells.

Cross-sectional SEM images of the designed precursor and selenized CIGSe films with different Ga gradients (type Iâ€“IV) are shown in Fig. 2. The detailed thicknesses of the precursor films are marked in the images. Compared to the homogenous selenized films (type I), the crystallinity of the graded films from type II to IV gradually gets worse. It is also observed that the fine-grained layer becomes thicker when the Ga ratio increases from 30 to 50% in the vicinity of the Mo electrode. Thus, excessive Ga doping can obviously inhibit grain growth, and this result is consistent with many previous reports regarding the influence of the [Ga]/([Ga] + [In]) ratios in absorbers on crystal growth.28â€“30 As for the explanation, Ga atoms tended to accumulate near the bottom Mo/CIGSe interface resulting from the intentional designs of different Ga gradients and different diffusivities of Ga and indium (In) atoms during the process of selenization.31,32 Therefore, an accumulation of Ga atoms near the Mo electrode decreases the grain size, which is why a thicker separated layer with small grain size appears close to the Mo/CIGSe interface as the [Ga]/[Ga] + [In] ratio rises.

The chemical composition of the selenized CIGSe thin film plays an important role in determining the performance of CIGSe solar cells. To investigate the composition of the CIGSe absorber layers with different Ga gradients, the selenized films were dissolved in 5% nitric acid solution for total film analysis, and inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectrometry (ICP-AES) measurements were then carried out to determine the detailed elemental ratios in the samples; the results are shown in Table S2.â€  The obtained results were in the ranges of [Cu]/([Ga] + [In]) = 0.89â€“0.91 and [Ga]/([Ga] + [In]) = 0.26â€“0.29, which are the ranges required for high efficiency CIGSe solar cells.33

To determine the structural quality of the as-prepared and selenized CIGSe thin films with different Ga gradients, X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurements were performed. The results demonstrated the well-crystallized and single-phase nature of the CIGSe film on Mo glass (Fig. 3a and b). Apart from the peaks of Mo and MoSe2, all diffraction peaks could be indexed to a tetragonal chalcopyrite-type phase, which demonstrates that introducing excess Ga did not change the crystalline phases. To further reveal the distribution of elemental Ga throughout the selenized films with Ga gradients, a chemical etching method was carried out on type III of sample C. The chemical etching method used solutions of HBr/Br2/H2O to gradually etch the CIGSe film from the surface (the etch rate was about 100 nm per 100 s).31,34,35 In this study, five identical selenized films with type III gradients were prepared and then etched at a certain position (marked [a] to [e] in the inset of Fig. 3d). The enlarged (220/204) peaks of the XRD patterns of the corresponding films are shown in Fig. 3e. Interestingly, the enlarged peaks of the (220/204) plane exhibited an asymmetric shape, first shifting to a smaller angle (from 44.63Â° to 44.60Â°), and then shifting toward a higher angle (44.80Â°). Also, the full width at half maximum (FWHM) became wider. The (112), (312)/(116), (400), and (332)/(316) planes also showed very similar trends. It is clear that the diffraction peaks systematically shift to higher angles because of the decreased unit cell volume with an increasing amount of In being replaced with Ga.31,34 In addition, it is also observed that the peaks become weaker with longer etching time. This result suggests that the different Ga compositions of the graded films result in inhomogeneous layers, and implies that the upper and bottom layers may have a higher concentration of Ga than the middle layer. As expected, this result corresponds well with the designed distribution of Ga throughout the graded films.

To detect the Ga composition profiles, the CIGSe absorber layers with different Ga gradients were investigated by secondary ion mass spectrometry (SIMS) and the resulting Ga profiles of the four samples (Aâ€“D) are shown in Fig. 4. The heights of the front-gradients and back-gradients are indicated as Î”Xf and Î”Xb, respectively, and the distances from the notch to the front surface and to the back contact are indicated as Î”Lf and Î”Lb, respectively. The influence of a front gradient has previously been investigated via computer simulations and experiments.2,36,37 Generally, it is believed that electrons produced within the SCR are efficiently collected because of the assistance of a strong electric field (Î¾A).2,16 However, an overly pronounced front gradient tends to result in a barrier to electrons, leading to increased recombination in the QNR.12 A 4-stage spin-coating process was used in the fabrication of the CIGSe absorber layer to obtain suitable front and back gradients. In this work, we were able to adjust the thicknesses of stages 4 and 2 and keep the thicknesses of stages 1 and 3 the same by controlling the spin-coating/sintering cycles. By controlling the thicknesses of stages 4 and 2, we can adjust the position of the notch on the surface. Meanwhile, Î”Lf was increased from 80 nm for sample B to 440 nm for sample D. Furthermore, a noticeable increase of Î”Xf from 0.051 for sample B to 0.075 for sample C was observed. With a short length of stage 4 for sample B, a weaker front-gradient (Î”Xf = 0.051) and a lower Ga content near the pâ€“n junction were obtained. It is reported that a slight front-gradient usually minimizes recombination in the SCR, and a strong back-gradient effectively drives the photo-generated charge carriers separated from the Mo back electrode.10 In the case of sample D, a relatively flat and wide grading profile (Î”Xf = 0.039 and Î”Xb = 0.069) was obtained by further increasing the spin-coating cycles for stage 4 and decreasing the spin-coating cycles for stage 2. In comparison, sample C clearly has a suitable Ga grading profile (Î”Xf = 0.075 and Î”Xb = 0.125). More direct evidence of the existence of inhomogeneous selenized films with different Ga compositions was obtained using EDX line measurements (Fig. S2â€ ). For the type I films, no apparent variation in the Ga concentration was observed throughout the films, except for a distinct fluctuation close to the interface of Mo/CIGSe. In contrast, the concentration distributions of Ga in the designed graded films (type IIâ€“IV) varied significantly with the film thickness. As expected, these various trends in the EDX line measurements are very similar to the SIMS results. This further confirmed the presence of different Ga gradients with which we intentionally designed the CIGSe precursor films. 3 Conclusions

In conclusion, we have presented and implemented several new Ga gradient designs for CIGSe solar cells that demonstrably improve Jsc by âˆ¼17% and achieve a maximum improvement in CIGSe efficiency (to 13.12%). Our approach involves narrowing the efficiency gap between hydrazine and non-hydrazine solution-based CIGSe solar cells, and it is believed that this approach is an effective method for pursuing the record efficiency of vacuum-based CIGSe solar cells. In this study, the location and depth of the notch in the Ga gradients are found to play key roles in device performances. When Î”Lf was limited to values of about 200 nm, we obtained relatively high Voc and Jsc, and the recombination in the SCR and QNR was minimized. In addition to the relative proportions of the four stages, sintering temperature, and deposition rates, it was observed that the selenization temperature and time during CIGSe growth are crucial for determining the grading profile. Thus, we believe that further improvement for achieving even higher efficiencies requires extensive systematic investigations to optimize the Ga grading profiles, processing conditions, and all of the layers involved in the devices.
