Abstract

Two polyyne oligomers using the thiophene(diketopyrrolopyrrole)thiophene unit, TDPPT, and the organometallic synthon trans-Pt(PBu3)2(CC)2, [Pt], ([L1][Pt])m (P1), here used for comparison purposes, and ([L4][Pt])m (P4), where L1 and L4 are TDPPT and (TDPPT)2, respectively, were synthesized by CuI-catalyzed dehydrohalogenation and fully characterized. The optical and electrochemical properties were investigated by UV-visible absorption and cyclic voltammetry, and P4 was found to exhibit a very low band gap. DFT (density functional theory) and TD-DFT (time-dependent DFT) computations were undertaken to address the polymers geometry, their electronic structures and calculated HOMO and LUMO energies. The S1 level is best described as a * excited state. P1 and P4 exhibit fluorescence lifetime in ps timescale and the fs-TAS data indicate an ultrafast electron transfer to PC61BM ((1Px* + PC61BM Px+ + (PC61BM); time scale < 113128 fs) evidenced by an increased formation of the triplet state upon back electron transfer (Px+ + (PC61BM)) 1,3Px* + PC61BM; x = 1, 4). The timescale of recombination for P4 (P4+ + (PC61BM) 3P4* + PCBM) is found to be 3 ps. The photovoltaic performance of Px was investigated by fabricating the photovoltaic solar cells (PSCs) with conventional device structure ITO/PEDOT:PSS/(P1 or P4):PC71BM/PFN/Al. After solvent vapour annealing with THF, the power conversion efficiencies, PCEs, are 7.36% (P1; Jsc = 12.94 mA cm2, Voc = 0.92 V, FF = 0.62) and 9.54% (P4; Jsc = 16.24 mA cm2, Voc = 0.89 V, FF = 0.66). The higher PCE of P4 may be related to the faster extraction and longer charge carrier lifetime for P4 based PSCs as compared to P1.

Introduction

Conjugated polymers containing the diketopyrrolopyrrole synthon 3,6-di-2-thienyl-2,5-dihydro-2,5-diethylhexylpyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione (DPP) and its derivatives are known for application in transistors1 and photovoltaics.2 Applications in photocatalysis,3 photodynamic therapy,4 2-photon absorption,5 and electro-chromism6 have also been reported.6 In order to improve the material properties, motif modifications have also been performed. For instance, conjugated homo- and copolymers containing covalently bonded thiopheneDPPthiophene units (TDPPT) were prepared using the trans-CCPtL2CC-residue (L = P(alkyl)3), [Pt], and DPP-containing polymers were recently reported, notably with thiopheneDPPthiophene7 (P1) and (thiophene)2DPP(thiophene)2 (P2) motifs (Chart 1).8 Their charge mobility,7 cyclic voltammograms,8 and photophysical properties9,10 were evaluated but their use in photovoltaic devices showed modest photocurrent efficiencies.9,10
Chart 1 Structures of other related polymers reported in the literature and discussed in the text.710

In a quest for new DPP-containing materials with improved electronic and optical properties, we now report the synthesis and characterization of a new narrow band gap oligomer, ([Pt][(thiopheneDPPthiophene)2])n, P4 (Chart 2), and provide a detailed comparison with that of the known related oligomers P1, with the slight exception that the solubilizing alkyl chains are different. In this study, their cyclic voltammograms are analyzed and their photophysical properties are examined with the aid of DFT (density functional theory) and time-dependent DFT (TDDFT) computations. We also report steady state and ultrafast time-resolved absorption and emission spectroscopy in solution (77 and 298 K) and in thin films. Moreover, the photo-induced electron transfer between P1 and P4 as donors and [6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester, PC61BM, as the acceptor is investigated in thin films. From fs-transient absorption spectroscopy (fs-TAS), the time scale of transfer is ultrafast (128 fs) for P1 and P4, and the time scale of recombination (P+ + PCBM 3P* + PCBM) is 3.0 ps for P4 (rise time). Finally, the optimized PCEs are 7.36% (P1; Jsc = 12.94 mA cm2, Voc = 0.92 V, FF = 0.62) and 9.54% (P4; Jsc = 16.24 mA cm2, Voc = 0.89 V, FF = 0.66).

Results and discussion

Synthetic strategy and characterization

General procedure for synthesis of L1 and L4. L1 was synthesized in four steps from 2-thiophenecarbonitrile using a previously reported procedure (see Scheme S1 in ESI). Indeed, similar molecules were previously prepared for OPSC applications.8,10 However the soluble chain on DPP was changed from n-C6H13(L1)10 and C8H17(L2)8 to linear and longer n-nonyl chains to enhance solubility and self-organization in films, potentially enhancing their optoelectronic properties when aggregated.1113L4 was synthesized according to Scheme 1. The DPP dimer 5 was prepared using a modified procedure from the literature14 using a Pd-catalyzed Stille coupling reaction of 4 with 1.5 eq. of hexabutyldistannane. 5 was isolated as a dark purple solid in 56% yield. Then, the bromination of 5 afforded a mixture of mono- and dibrominated compounds 6 and 6 as detected by 1H NMR and MALDI-TOF/MS. Their separation proved to be tedious, so the mixture was directly engaged in the final step. L4 was obtained by a Sonogashira coupling reaction between the dibrominated DPP 6 and trimethylsilylacetylene (TMSA).15 It was isolated as a dark green solid in 43% yield from 5 with good purity after purification by flash chromatography (see Scheme S2 in ESI).

Synthesis of P1 and P4. The syntheses of P1 and P4 were performed using a CuI-catalyzed dehydrohalogenation reaction between the protected bis-trimethylsilyl-ethynyl ligands L1 and L4 and freshly prepared trans-dichlorobis(tri-n-butylphosphine)platinum(II), trans-[PtCl2(PBu3)2]1618 (Scheme 2). The bulky PBu3 groups are chosen for solubility reasons.

Initially, the reaction with the deprotected ligands was successfully performed using Wong's procedures10 for the synthesis of P1 from the deprotected L1 but failed for P4. In this case, the deprotection of L4 led to immediate degradation as confirmed by 1H NMR. To overcome this limited solubility and stability problem, in situ deprotection of the trimethylsilyl groups (TMS) was performed with tetrabutylammonium fluoride (TBAF) using reported procedures.8,19 The formation of P1 was confirmed by 1H and 31P NMR (Fig. S9, ESI). The difference in 1H chemical shift between L1 and P1 reveals that upon complexation the resonance of the thiophene-terminated protons H3 and H4 in the vicinity of the triple bond is shifted by 0.13 and 0.38 ppm, respectively (Fig. S11, ESI). The upfield shift for H4 is consistent with the donor effect of Pt(II) in the trans-[Pt(PBu3)2(L1)2]n moiety. Concurrently, the 1H decoupled 31P NMR spectrum of P1 consists of one signal at 3.96 ppm. This signal is flanked by 195Pt satellites with a coupling constant J(31P195Pt) of 2289 Hz, which is assigned to P-atoms trans to L120,21 (Fig. S9, ESI). The GPC (gel permeation chromatography) traces exhibit a broad molar mass distribution, whose number-average molar mass (Mn) is 18200 g mol1 for P1 with a dispersity value of 2.1. These characteristics compare with those reported for similar polymers (Fig. S15, ESI).8,10 Using in situ deprotection conditions8,19 of L4 during the synthesis of P4, a mixture of platinum(II) complexes was observed. The 31P NMR spectra display two signals, one at 7.5 ppm corresponding to the intermediate trans-[Cl[Pt]Ln[Pt]Cl]22 and another at 3.9 ppm attributed to the trans-P4 metallooligomers. The presence of these intermediates indicates a lower reactivity, which could be explained by the instability of L4 during the in situ-deprotection, hence decreasing the number of ligands during the polymerization. After adding four portions of 0.25 eq. of ligands and then of 0.5 eq. of TBAF the clean formation of P4 was confirmed by 1H and 31P NMR analyses (Fig. S10, ESI). First, the difference in 1H chemical shift between L4 and its corresponding metallooligomer P4 reveals that upon complexation the resonance of the thiophene-terminated protons H3 and H4 in the vicinity of the ethynyl bond is shifted by 0.21 and 0.32 ppm, respectively (Fig. S12, ESI). The 31P{1H} NMR signal at 3.88 ppm flanked by 195Pt satellites with a coupling constant J(31P195Pt) of 2290 Hz for the metallooligomer P4 is consistent with the trans-geometry of the platinum(II)polyyne oligomers, i.e. trans-[Pt(L4)(PBu3)2]n. The GPC analysis indicates a relatively low average molar mass and a higher dispersity value for P4 (Mn = 6600 g mol1, = 11.1) (Fig. S15, ESI). The average number of repeating units per chain is lower for P4 (34 units) than that for P1 (1415 units). This lower mass for P4 is explained by the unexpected decomposition of the metallooligomer in THF with time as evidenced by the solution discoloration. However, the high dispersity value cannot be associated with the presence of free ligands L1 and L4, nor with by-products, as they are not detected by NMR (Fig. S9 and S10, ESI). These new ligands and metallooligomers were characterized by IR ATR spectroscopy in the solid state. The IR spectra displayed one sharp (CO) band in the 16571662 cm1 range and one strong (CC) signal in the 21352145 cm1 range for L1 and L4 (Fig. S3 and S6, ESI), and 20632081 cm1 for P1 and P4 (Fig. S13 and S14, ESI). These [Pt]-containing oligomers exhibit (CC) bands at lower wavenumbers than their uncoordinated ligands, which is consistent with the back-bonding effect.23 Concurrently, the characterization of metallooligomers by MALDI-TOF/MS is generally rarely performed nor reported as they often form by-products under ionization conditions due to their poor UV stability. However, their detection can be improved using a suitable matrix.24 Indeed, DCTB (2-[(2E)-3-(4-tert-butylphenyl)-2-methylprop-2-enylidene]malononitrile) was used for similar metallopolymers (i.e. trans-[Pt(p-BTD-EDOT)-(PBu3)2]n)25 (BTD = 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (acceptor unit), EDOT = (ethylenedioxy)thiophene (donor unit)). In this work, the MALDI-TOF analyses demonstrated that P1 and P4 were indeed oligomers as the mass spectra exhibited several ion series from m/z 1000 to 9000 with mass gaps of 11961198 and 1748 Da, respectively, between series of molecular masses equal to the repeating units. Five repeating units for P1 and slightly less for P4 were observed, which is consistent with the GPC analyses (Fig. S16 and S17, ESI). Each trans-[Pt(Ln)(PBu3)2]n ion series is composed of several peaks, which are difficult to assign as different ionization states (with Na+, K+ or other ions) and by-products under UV irradiation are generated during measurements. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) indicated that P4 exhibits higher thermal stability than P1 (Td = 221 C) with a decomposition temperature (measured at 5 wt% loss) of 261 C (Table 1 and Fig. S18, ESI).

Electrochemical characterization. The electrochemical properties of P1 and P4 as thin films were investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV). P1 and P4 exhibit well-defined but generally irreversible oxidation and reduction peaks (Fig. 1). The HOMO and LUMO levels are respectively estimated from the onset oxidation and reduction potentials using EDHOMO = (Eonsetox + 4.73) eV and EDLUMO = (Eonsetred + 4.73) eV.26 The difference between EDHOMO and EDLUMO provides the electrochemical band gaps (Eelec.g) (Table 1).

The EDHOMO (5.38 eV) and EDLUMO (3.76 eV) for P1 gave an Eelec.g of 1.62 eV. This value is consistent with the results reported by Wong's group10 for P1. After optimisation, L1 and L4 are expectedly planar, whereas the models for P1 and P4 are found to be quasi-planar (169 < x < 175). In these cases, the bond distances are found to be normal. Overall, this outcome is consistent with the X-ray structure of DPP-containing molecules27 and agrees with other DFT computations.2830 Moreover, due to the presence of single bonds in L1 and L4, various conformations were also examined. Electronic, optical, fluorescence properties and MO analysis

Prior to describing these properties, relevant comments are made. P1 and P4 belong to a category of polymers defined as pushpull or often called donoracceptor, where [Pt] acts as the electron rich moiety3136 and DPP37,38 is the electron withdrawing group. Thus both are prone to generate low-energy intramolecular charge transfer (ICT)3941 excited states, which are known to lead to near-IR absorption bands and narrow bandgaps ([Pt],19,4244 DPP4552). The literature indicates that in conjugated thiophene/DPP-containing polymers, the thiophene residue is considered as the donor.53 One should also consider that in the presence of a strong withdrawing group, thiophene may adopt the role of a donor unit. Moreover, P1 is essentially identical to that reported by Wong and collaborators (P1),10 which differs only in the nature of the soluble side chains. This difference is not suspected to affect the electronic properties of the polymer. Consequently, P1 is investigated for comparison purposes and to confirm some reported photophysical data (below).

Based on the experimental evidence and DFT/TDDFT computations below, one of the key features is that there is no strong low-energy ICT excited state in P1 and P4, but rather just *. This is somewhat surprising and literature assignment for this low-energy band in the so-called pushpull thiophene-containing conjugated polymers may warrant a revisit or reconsideration.

The general observations are as follows: (1) the frontier MOs are -systems spreading all over the ligands and polymer chains, (2) no clear ICT process for HOMO LUMO (and other low-energy transitions), so the excited state is mostly *, (3) the calculated HOMOLUMO gap decreases qualitatively with the extension of the -system (Fig. 7), (4) the calculated positions of the low-energy bands are consistent with this trend and match reasonably with the experimental data of Table 2: for L1 and L4, calculated (nm): 535 and 669; exp. onset (nm): 620 and 750, respectively; for models P1 and P4, calculated (nm): 675 and 779; exp. onset (nm): 680 and 790, respectively, (5) the simulated spectra also compare favorably with the experimental one (Fig. 8), and (6) the lowest-energy transitions are well-isolated from any other transitions (i.e. other excited states), meaning that excited state mixing is minimal (i.e. HOMO LUMO is the major component). All in all, the computations indicate that these materials do not exhibit strong ICT interactions and that the low-energy absorption band arises rather mainly from a * transition. This conclusion is consistent with other computational investigations on other species containing only thiophene and DPP: (thiophene)DPP(thiophene),65,66 (thiophene)2DPP(thiophene)2,65 [Me(thiophene)2]2(DPP),67 [thiopheneDPPthiophene-(thiopheneCCSiMe3)]2(CC),68 (thiopheneDPPthiophene)4.69 However it has been reported that in the polymer {[(thiophene)DPP(thiophene)](thienothiophene)}n, the thienothiophene acts as the donor, leading to ICT excited states.70 This theoretical conclusion was also reported for other related polymers.7175

Fluorescence properties. Fluorescent DPP-containing organic conjugated polymers are common.7681 However, when a heavy atom is incorporated into the polymer chain, the heavy atom effect may exert a negative effect on the amplitude of the fluorescence quantum yield (F) and lifetime (F) in comparison with their corresponding metal-free monomeric model compounds.82 For instance, F is generally in the range of 2575% for substituted monomeric derivatives of both (thiophene)DPP(thiophene) and (thiophene)2DPP(thiophene)2,82,83 but falls, F < 0.01, when a Pt-atom is introduced in the monomer and polymer chain.82 Two effects need to be considered. Generally, F ranges from 5 to 7 ns for monomeric (thiophene)DPP(thiophene) species,84 but when incorporated within a conjugated polymer, either in solution or as thin film (for example [(thiophene)DPP(thiophene)(naphthyl)]n), the fluorescence decay may become shorter (ps) and polyexponential.85 Moreover, recent examples of bis-DPP chromophores, such as Ph[(thiophene)DPP(thiophene)]2Ph86 and (2-pyridine[(thiophene)DPP(thiophene)]22-pyridine),87 were also studied and their F values proved somewhat smaller (F = 3.1 ns;86F = 2.1 ns, F < 0.0187) than for the mono-DPP compounds. Finally, for the [(thiophene)DPP(thiophene)]2(CC) chromophore, only one study was reported, which stated the presence of a strong emission,88 but no photophysical parameters were provided. Concurrently, no photophysical investigation exists on the latter two bis-DPP species placed inside conjugated polymer chains. The main conclusion of this literature survey suggests that going from monomer to polymer, from mono-DPP to bis-DPP, from solution to films and adding a Pt-atom into the chain, all contribute to decrease both F and F values. An examination of the photophysical data of P1 indicates that F = 10.1 ns10 (i.e. greater than that of all DPP species, monoexponential decay; in CH2Cl2; 293 K), and that the F value decreases modestly from 4.56 to 3.45% despite the presence of a long conjugated polymer (Mn = 22400 g mol1) and a heavy atom (Pt). These data are curious and justify a revisit through the use of P1. Concurrently, P4 is the material that exhibits the most red-shifted absorption band and represents the first conjugated polymer containing a bis-DPP moiety to be investigated for its photophysical properties. The fluorescence bands exhibit some vibronic features and are a mirror-image of the absorption bands, which is typical of the fluorescence behaviour of DPP. Moreover, the gap between the 00 peaks of the absorption and emission (i.e. Stokes shift) bands is small (50100 cm1) and these species exhibit emission decay in the ns-time scale at 77 K (note that the larger F values at 77 K compared to the singlet lifetimes extracted from the fs-TAS at 298 K (below) are due to the combined action of an increase in medium rigidity and lower temperature). Combining these observations permits one to assign this emission to fluorescence. The fluorescence decay is bi-exponential (Fig. 10), which is most likely due to the effect of a polymer chain85 or the presence of many terminal groups (which have a different environment from the internal units). Indeed, the emission spectra exhibit weak shoulders on the blue side of the fluorescence band. This latter outcome is a consequence of the small oligomer sizes of P1 and P4, a phenomenon well-known for trans-Pt(PR3)2(CC)2-containing oligomers.89 The F values at 298 K are not accessible on our instrument (the FWHM of the nano-LED pulse is only 90 ps), so fs-transient absorption spectroscopy (fs-TAS) is used instead thus permitting one to measure these rather short singlet excited state lifetimes. This observation is consistent with the conclusion drawn from the literature described above (i.e. (S1) is <90 ps). As stated, the DPP-containing species are known to aggregate, and despite many examples of films exhibiting fluorescence,7681 aggregation-induced quenching of their emission90 has been observed and thus the measurement of their luminescence in the solid state is often reported as unlikely. This is also the case here except for P1, which shows a weak fluorescence (Fig. 11). However, no emission was detected for P4. This weaker intensity compared to that for P1 is consistent with the somewhat shorter singlet lifetimes (2 to 4 fold) extracted from the fs-TAS measurements below. This significant decrease is explained by the energy gap law (which states that as the S0S1 energy gap decreases, the S1 lifetime decreases since the non-radiative internal conversion process becomes more probable) as recently demonstrated for conjugated organic polymers, including DPP-containing polymers.91

fs-Transient absorption spectroscopy (fs-TAS). In order to access the excited state lifetimes of P1 and P4 in solution and as thin films, fs-TAS was used. This technique has also been successfully used to measure the rate of formation of the charge separated state and recombination for (thiophene)DPP(thiophene)-containing organic conjugated polymers and various electron acceptors,91 and throughout these previously reported investigations, the S1 lifetime of the pristine polymer films has also been evaluated.9299 The singlet exciton lifetimes in these films turn out to be highly variable (1.6,94 2.5 and 41,93 8 and 20,94 14,95 15,96 41,97 49,98 and 175 ps).97 For comparison purposes, the (thiophene)DPP(thiophene) monomer as a thin film exhibits a singlet exciton lifetime of 150 10 ps.99 The time-evolution of the fs-TAS of P1 and P4 in CH2Cl2 solution (Fig. 12) and as pristine films (Fig. 13) is characterized by strong bleaching bands at 660 (narrow) and 700 nm (broad), along with weaker transient signals evolving in the 500760 nm window all being formed within the laser pulse. The decay-associated spectra exhibit four species that are easily assigned. The two relatively intense species decaying with ps time constants are the singlet excitons (see details in Table 5). In addition, an intense signal with a similar spectral shape for P1 as a neat film and decaying with a lifetime of 1.5 ps is also assigned to the singlet state. The presence of two or three singlet species is not unseen. Indeed for (thiophene)DPP(thiophene)(naphthyl)n, three ps-components (6.5, 65, and 185 ps) were depicted in the fluorescence decay.85 All these lifetimes associated with the most intense signals, which range from 1.5 to 145 ps, are perfectly consistent with the literature data for DPP-containing organic conjugated polymers mentioned above. The species of weaker intensity and decaying in the fs time scale (470940 fs) are the S2 species. The excitation wavelength (635 nm) is long enough to excite both the S2 and S1 excited states (see the positions of the calculated transitions in Table 4 for example). Finally, the long-lived species (decaying with a lifetime 8 ns (i.e. 7.8 ns or longer) and exhibiting the weakest intensity are mostly triplet excitons). DPP-containing organic polymers are not prone to efficient triplet exciton formation, but these species are known to be occasionally efficiently formed upon charge recombination: (polymer+ + (electron acceptor) (polymer)* + (electron acceptor), where (polymer)* represents singlet (25%) and triplet (75%) excitons). Indeed, triplet species of (thiophene)DPP(thiophene)-containing organic polymers decaying with lifetimes of 8 ns95 and 1.2 to 5.5 ns93 are known. This time scale contrasts with the triplet state lifetime of the monomolecular (thiophene)DPP(thiophene) species (14 and 20 ms).100 For the Pt DPP-containing polymer, Schanze and collaborators reported a triplet lifetime of 86 ns.82 Interestingly, P1 and P4 bear striking similarities in emission lifetimes with a recently reported [Pt]-containing polymer, ([Pt]-(AQI(BMPA)2))n (AQI = anthraquinone diimine; BMPA = bis(para-methoxyphenyl)amine), an extremely rare example of a [Pt]-containing polymer that is both fluorescent and phosphorescent.101 For this polymer the F and P values are as follow: F = 10 ps at 298 K; F = 0.18 (5), 0.80 (5), 3.7 (36), 10.6 ns (54%) at 77 K; P = 1.1 (22), 4.4 (36), 17.0 ns (42%). Photoinduced electron transfers between P1, P4 and PCBM

Platinum polyynes are suitable compounds for photovoltaics.104,105 Specifically, DPP-containing conjugated polymers are known to be involved in oxidative photo-induced electron transfers with PCBMs (PC61BM (phenyl C61 butyric acid methyl ester) and PC71BM ([6,6]-phenyl C71 butyric acid methyl ester)) and fullerene derivatives (polymer* + PCBM polymer+ + PCBM) leading to their fluorescence quenching.106112P1 and PCBM undergo an obvious fluorescence quenching in their 1:1 blend (Fig. 14), which is consistent with the fact that both products, polymer+ + PCBM, are non-emissive. Evidence for electron transfer is readily demonstrated by observing the common and ultrafast triplet state formation upon charge recombination (i.e. DPP-polymer+ + (PCBM) (DPP-polymer)* + (PCBM), where (DPP-polymer)* represents singlet (25%) and triplet (75%) excitons).93,95 Indeed investigations of DPP-containing organic polymers in the presence of various electron acceptors, including PCBMs, are common,92,97,113117 and the charge separation usually occurs within the laser pulse.118,119 Most of the time, this time scale turns out to be <250 fs,9395,98100 but in one case, a rise time of 500 fs for the formation of the polaron has been observed.96 The fs-TAS spectra of the P1/PCBM and P4/PCBM blends (1:1 in weight) in the 500750 nm range are presented in Fig. 15. The key feature is that the transient signals decaying with a time constant >8 ns (i.e. triplet state) exhibit a significantly higher relative intensity than that measured for triplet species depicted in the pristine polymers (Fig. 13). Moreover, the relative intensity of triplet exciton signals (vs. the other transients) in the blends is also larger compared to that for the pure polymers. Altogether, there is a clear enhancement of the triplet species in the blends indicating the presence of charge separation followed by recombination. This is clearly seen from the decay profiles of the transient signals at various wavelengths where the baseline does not return to zero in the ns time scale (Fig. 16). The other transient species exhibit different responses in comparison with that for the pristine polymers and significantly longer decay (i.e. charge recombination; details in Fig. 15). The associated time scales of recombination, rec, vary from 4 to 310 ps for P1 and from 1.6 to 220 ps for P4. The slower recombination processes are on a time scale that is consistent with that of other organic DPP/PCBM blends (100 < rec < 900 ps).93,95,100 The large variability of rec may be associated with the morphology of the polymer blend.93

Photovoltaic performance

The photovoltaic performance of P1 and P4 as polymer donors was investigated by fabricating the photovoltaic solar cells (PSCs) with the device structure ITO/PEDOT:PSS/P1 or P4:PC71BM/PFN/Al. The P1 or P4:PC71BM blends in CHCl3 solution (14 mg mL1) were spin coated onto the top of PEDOT:PSS to form the active layers. Since the weight ratio of donor to acceptor plays an important role in determining PSC performances, the blend ratio was tuned from 1:05 to 1:2 to obtain the optimal weight ratio of 1:1.5. Without solvent vapour annihilation (SVA) treatment, the PSC with a weight ratio of 1:1.5 showed PCEs of respectively 4.54 and 5.76% for P1 and P4 based active layers. The value of 4.54% for P1 is interesting since it is significantly superior to the PCEs reported for P1, P1 and P3 (Chart 1)10 which ranged from 0.28 to 1.40%. With the exception of the side chains, the main differences are that the literature devices were not optimized for the donor and acceptor ratio, the acceptor was PC61BM and there was no SVA treatment.

Subsequently, the optimized active layers were subjected to SVA in THF for 40 s to further optimize the PSC performances. The currentvoltage characteristics of the PCEs under illumination with SVA treated P1:PCBM and P4:PCBM active layers and the photovoltaic parameters were acquired (Fig. 20a and Table 6). The best PSC based on P1 exhibited a PCE of 7.12% (Voc = 0.92 V, Jsc = 12.96 mA cm2) which is lower than that for P4 (PCE = 9.54%, Jsc = 16.24 mA cm2, Voc = 0.89 V, FF = 0.667). Noteworthily, the value of 9.54% is the highest ever observed for any devices containing the [Pt] unit whether it is contained within a conjugated polymer or a small molecule.121 Compared to the P1 based PSC, the P4 based counterpart showed slightly lower Voc, which may be due to the deeper HOMO energy level of P1, since the Voc of the PSCs based on BHJ active layers is directly proportional to the energy difference between the HOMO of donor and the LUMO of acceptor. The higher value of Jsc for P4 based PSCs compared to the P1 counterpart may stem from the broader absorption profile and low bandgap of P4 compared to P1, since the Jsc of the PSC is directly related to the light harvesting ability of the active layer.

To obtain information on the exciton dissociation and charge carrier collection efficiencies of the PSCs, the variation of photocurrent density (Jph) with effective voltage (Veff)122 was measured (Fig. 21). The Jph is described as Jph = JL JD, where JD and JL are the current densities of the devices in the dark and under illumination, respectively. The Veff is given by Veff = Vo Va, where Va is the applied voltage and Vo is the voltage when Jph is zero. At low voltages, the Jph increases linearly and tends to saturate around 1.45 and 0.81 V for P1 and P4 based devices, respectively, and completely saturates at high values of Veff for both devices. Therefore, the photogenerated excitons are dissociated into free charge carriers which are then collected at the corresponding electrodes. The exciton dissociation probability (Pdiss) and charge collection probability (Pcoll) are defined by Jph/Jsat under short circuit conditions and maximum power point, respectively. The Pdiss/Pcoll values are 0.942/0.73 and 0.972/0.81 for P1 and P4 based devices, respectively. These values are consistent with the measured values of Jsc and FF of the corresponding PSCs. Moreover, the maximum exciton generation rate (Gmax) in the PSCs is estimated by Gmax = Jphsat/qL (where q is the elementary charge and L is the thickness of the active layer (90 nm)) and is 0.97 1028 and 1.17 1028 m3 s1 for P1 and P4, respectively. The higher value of Gmax for the P4 based PSC is consistent with the wider absorption band compared to that for P1.

Conclusions

Two [Pt]/DPP-containing polymers were prepared and investigated in detail. One of them was known in the literature (P1) and was used for comparison purposes, whereas the other was new (P4). Very low band gaps were measured for P4. Based on DFT and TDDFT computations, the S1 level is better described as a * excited state rather than an ICT. The photophysical properties of P1 and P4 were thoroughly studied as representative examples and they were found to exhibit fluorescence lifetimes in the ps timescale (1P1* and 1P4*), long enough for efficient photo-induced electron transfer to PCBM. Indeed, the fs-TAS data indicate that the electron transfer is ultrafast (<113128 fs) and quickly evolved towards the formation of the triplet state (1P4* + PCBM P4+ + (PCBM) 3P4* + PCBM). In one of the two polymers investigated (P4), the timescale for recombination was established from the observation of its rise time (3 ps). Overall, these new organometallic materials exhibit many photophysical features associated with the presence of the DPP unit in the chain as for the numerous organic versions reported in the literature, and the presence of the [Pt] residue is shown to be clearly not detrimental. The photovoltaic performances of P1 and P4 were also investigated by fabricating the photovoltaic solar cells (PSCs) with the device structure ITO/PEDOT:PSS/(P1 or P4):PC71BM/PFN/Al with SVA treatment. Under illumination, the best PCE obtained for P1 is 7.12% (Voc = 0.92 V, Jsc = 12.96 mA cm2) which is lower than that for P4 (PCE = 9.54%, Jsc = 16.24 mA cm2, Voc = 0.89 V, FF = 0.667). The higher value of PCE for P4 based PSCs is associated with the improved values of Jsc and FF and is related to the larger value of exciton generation rate, more balanced charge transport, charge collection, faster extraction and longer charge carrier lifetime. The important outcome of this work is the PCE value of 9.54% for DPP dimer-containing metallooligomer P4, which is the highest ever obtained for [Pt]-containing polymers or small molecules.

Experimental

General information

All the reactions were carried out under a nitrogen atmosphere. All the chemicals were purchased from commercial suppliers (Fischer Scientific, Sigma-Aldrich, Strem Chemicals, VWR chemicals) and used as received. The DFT geometry optimizations and Time Dependent DFT (TD-DFT) calculations were carried out using the pbe0 method. A 6-31g* basis set was used for C, H, and N atoms. It is important to note that DFT calculations for all the molecules were made using a dichloromethane solvent field.

Device fabrication and characterization. The polymer solar cells with the structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/active layer/PFN/Al were fabricated to evaluate the photovoltaic performance of P1 and P4 as donors and PC71BM as the acceptor. PSCs were fabricated using ITO coated glass substrates (10 sq1) which were cleaned with detergent water, deionized water, acceptor and isopropyl alcohol in an ultrasonic bath sequentially for 20 min, and dried in a vacuum oven at 40 C. A thin layer (35 nm) of PEDOT:PSS was first spin coated on the pre-cleaned ITO coated glass substrates at 3500 rpm and baked at 110 C for 15 min under ambient conditions. The PFN as an electron transport layer was spin coated on the top of the active layer at 3000 rpm from a methanol solution. Finally, the Al electrode was thermally evaporated onto the top of the PFN layer at a pressure less than 105 Pa. A shadow mask was used to define the PSC active area (16 mm2). A 300 W xenon solar simulator with an AM1.5 filter operated at 100 mW cm2 was used to simulate the AM1.5G solar irradiation. The charge carrier mobility (hole and electron mobility) of the active layer was determined by fitting the dark currentvoltage characteristics of the hole-only device (ITO/PEDOT:PSS/active layer/Au) and the electron device (ITO/Al/active layer/Al) to the space charge limited current (SCLC) model. Transient photocurrent (TPC) measurements were made under the short circuit condition to explore the time dependent extraction of photo-generated charge carriers. The 10 ns light pulse was chosen as the light source. The devices were kept in the dark between pulses in order to avoid any influence of pulse frequency on the current responses. Transient photovoltage measurements were made under open circuit conditions to explore the photovoltage decay. The photovoltage decay kinetics of devices follow a non-exponential decay: (v) = Aexp(t/), where t is the time and is the charge carrier life-time, and give information about the degree of charge recombination.
