Abstract

The technology to convert sunlight into electric current by employing organic photovoltaic systems has been an issue of an intensive research over the last two decades and nowadays receives an increasing industrial interest. Dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSCs) provide a technically and economically credible alternative to the present well-known p–n junction or thin film photovoltaic devices. In this Perspective, we review the basic concepts of DSSCs and we specifically discuss the recent theoretical research aimed at optimising DSSC's properties, as well as several of the controversial and emerging issues in this field.

1. Introduction

The present energetic and environmental crisis has stimulated interest in the design of renewable energy sources. Indeed, with the foreseen 1013 watts of new power needed for the coming 50 years, the carbon dioxide concentration resulting from the fossil fuel-based energy generation will exceed the current level (∼400 ppm) that should be maintained if not decreased. The development of carbon-free sources of energy that are also scalable in order to meet increasing societal demands is probably the most important scientific challenge of this century. In this framework, solar photovoltaic devices are likely to be leading technologies in a promising “low-carbon level” future.1–8

The photovoltaic effect, i.e. the conversion of light into electricity, was first reported in the work of Chaplin and coworkers9 in which a silicon-based single p–n junction device showed a Solar Power Conversion Efficiency (SPCE)10,11 of about 6%. This innovative study led to the first-generation photovoltaics that currently dominate the solar technologies market as wafer-size single-junction cells based on crystalline silicon assemblies. This generation is expensive but is very efficient in terms of conversion (with a maximal SPCE of 33%). Though in 2007, first-generation solar cells accounted for 89.6% of the commercial production, the is market share is now declining. The manufacturing processes that are used to produce these cells are inherently expensive, meaning that they may take years to pay for their purchasing and energetic costs. Nevertheless, major efforts have been performed in order to significantly reduce the cost of power production by : (i) increasing the SPCE; (ii) modifying the semiconductor (SC) nature in order to reduce the amount of light-absorbing compounds and; (iii) lowering the assembly cost of the solar cells modules.4,12–15

Second-generation solar cells (also referred to as thin film technology) which were under intense development in the 1990s and early 2000s, are significantly cheaper to produce than first-generation cells, but present lower efficiencies. These are most frequently associated with thin film solar cells, that use minimal materials and cheap manufacturing processes. The most popular materials used for this second-generation designs are copper, indium, gallium, selenide and cadmium telluride (e.g.II–VI semiconductors), combined with amorphous silicon and micromorphous silicon. Whilst the controlled production of second-generation cells remains a challenge, their commercial use is still growing, due to one of the great advantages of thin films: their flexibility. Also, this thin film technology has spurred lightweight, aesthetically pleasing solar innovations such as solar shingles or solar panels that can be rolled out onto a roof or on other surfaces.16,17

Concurrently, over the past 20 years, the science and design of organic semiconducting materials has progressed very rapidly, leading to the production of a large panel of organic-based solid-state devices with multiple applications like light-emitting diodes,18 photodiodes and transistors,19,20 as well as photovoltaic cells.21 The ability to significantly modify the chemical and physical properties of organic compounds by a fine tuning of their structures constitutes the principal reason for expanding research and increased industrial interest in organic photovoltaics. The organic-based approach, and all those which are not directly related to a single p–n junction are referred to as third-generation devices and they include: (i) multijunction cells derived from group IV and III–V semiconductors;22 (ii) hybrid approaches that are related to the combination of nanostructured inorganic semiconductor such as titanium dioxide (TiO2) with organic materials;23,24 (iii) more elaborate hybrids, in which inorganic quantum dots are doped into a semiconducting polymer matrix;25–27 (iv) organic solid-state approaches with organic molecular and polymeric semiconductor forming films, thanks to a large variety of non-covalent interactions;28–30 and (v) the dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSCs), that have been pioneered by Grätzel in the last ten years.31,32 In this article, we will mainly focus on this latter case. The dye sensitised solar cells are composed of a wide band gap semiconductor deposited on a translucid conducting substrate, an anchored molecular sensitiser, and a redox electrolyte (usually the I−/I3− couple is found in most of the “conventional” DSSCs). In such systems, titanium dioxide is the semiconductor of choice because of its many advantages: low cost, wide availability, non-toxic, biocompatiblity, and wide use in domestic applications such as paint pigmentation.33

The first reliable and consistent experimental tests dealing with the operating mechanism of electron injection from photoexcited dye molecules into the conduction band of n-type semiconductor substrates were obtained in the 1960s.34 The standard dye at that time was tris(2,2′–bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate) ruthenium(II) (also known as panchromatic sensitiser), the carboxylate ensuring chemisorption (see Section 2 for further details). Advances were rather slow, until the early 90s report of a DSSC showing a 7.1% SPCE.35 Currently in the DSSC field, the Ru complexes photosensitisers such as the N3 compound (see Scheme 1) [cis-di(thiocyanato)-bis(2,2′-bipyridyl-4,4′-dicarboxylate) ruthenium(II)] remain the most popular category of dyes as they have a solar energy-to-electricity conversion efficiency close to 10%.36–41 Meanwhile, an increasing interest in purely organic DSSCs as substitutes for Ru complexes has occurred in recent years due to the crucial advantages they bring: high molar extinction coefficient, simple and relatively inexpensive preparation processes, as well as compliance with environmental and health issues.42 Moreover, the solid-state DSSCs based on organic dyes show good performances, suggesting promising commercial applications.43 Therefore, metal-free dyes like triphenylamine,44 coumarin,45,46 merocyanine,47 indoline,48 xanthene,49 hemi-cyanine,50 perylene and fluorene51–53 are good potential candidates for building DSSCs.

While the physics of p–n junction and thin film cells is reasonably well-understood, the understanding and optimisation of the processes governing organic DSSCs are far less converged and remain an intense topic of research, and are the focus of the present article.54 We certainly do not intend to provide a detailed description of the wide panel of organic photovoltaic materials that have been developed in the past 20 years; this can be found in notable recent contributions.29,55–58 Nowadays, molecular modelling techniques and especially quantum chemistry (QM), offer competitive alternatives for the interpretation of the experimental data arising from industrial interests and developments. The improvements in CPU resources over the past 10 years allow the study, at various levels of approximation, of the structural and electronic properties of large panel molecular species. In particular, one can now consider the study of the dyes used in DSSC applications at the ab initio level, allowing a better comprehension of the processes and the role played by the different partners (dye, semiconductor, electrolyte, see Section 2 for more details) involved when running a dye-sensitised solar cell.

Our objective in this Perspective is to introduce the reader to the recent progress and theoretical research in the DSSC field. To this end, the present contribution is organized as follows: In Section 2 we discuss the engineering aspects related to a conceptual dye-sensitised solar cell with an emphasis on the physical process and the resulting energetic balance involved in the actual device. In Section 3, we present recent DSSC theoretical research and developments in an appropriate historical context, and we consider the controversial and emerging issues in the field. Section 4 offers new insights into the DSSC field with the presentation of new unpublished data from our own recent results. Future prospects and challenges in the DSSC domain conclude this work.

2. Description and operation principle of a DSSC

The detailed operating scheme of DSSCs is still extensively studied to identify the processes that actually limit their performance.32,33,57,59–61 A schematic representation of the principles ruling a DSSC is depicted in Fig. 1. The “core” of the system is constituted by a mesoporous oxide layer, generally TiO2 (anatase) although alternative oxides (with a wide band gap) such as ZnO or NB2O5 can be used as well. A monolayer of the sensitiser dye is grafted on this nanocrystalline material, and the photoexcitation of the dye (that yields dye*) results in the injection of an electron from the donor orbital of the excited dye to the conduction band (CB) of the oxide semiconductor film (Injection). Unfortunately, the injected electron can recombine with the oxidized sensitiser dyes (Recombination). This impeding reaction is in competition with the regeneration of the oxidized dyes by the redox mediator which is usually an organic solvent containing a redox system, such as the iodide/triiodide couple (Regeneration). The remaining electrons can be transported (Diffusion) in the semiconductor film as the conducting electrons (CEs). Unfortunately, before reaching the counter electrode, these CEs can also react with the redox mediator molecules or with solvated compounds during the diffusion process (Leak reaction) and these reactions that occur in the vicinity of the TiO2 surface are induced by dispersion and electrostatic forces as well as by complex formations. Finally, the remaining CEs flow into the external circuit, and the iodide can be regenerated in turn by the reduction of triiodide at the counter electrode (of platinium, in our case). The maximum voltage under illumination (also referred to as the open-circuit photovoltage Voc) therefore corresponds to the difference between the quasi-Fermi level of the electron in the TiO2 CB and the redox potential of the electrolyte. Of course, the balance of these processes is essential to achieve high performance and it is obvious that both the rate and the efficiency of the full procedure tremendously depend on the nature of the sensitiser dye and semiconductor film.

The photoexcitation of the sensitizer (S) is followed by electron injection into the semiconductor (TiO2) conduction band (CB). The dye molecule is regenerated by the iodine/triiodide couple, which is regenerated at the counter platinum (Pt) electrode by the current electrons passing through the load. Potentials in electron volt (eV) in the scheme are with reference to the Normal Hydrogen Electrode (NHE).

A second direct solution to optimise the efficiency of DSSCs is to increase the Φinject. and consequently make the injection reaction more exoenergetic. To this end, we must raise the excitation energy of the dye without substantially altering EdyeOX.

A third option for improving the DSSC operation is to increase Voc. At open circuit, the rate of the electron injection and recombination/interception are equal, and their values determine both the steady-state electron concentration in the SC, and the quasi-Fermi level of the latter (also referred to as the electron chemical potential by the chemists).68 The quasi-Fermi level, and thus Voc, can be increased by: (i) increasing kinject. and/or decreasing kextinct.; (ii) using alternative redox couples with a more positive potential;32 (iii) shifting the CB to higher level but this solution is not recommended because it would reduce Jsc. Indeed, an increase of the SC quasi-Fermi level corresponds to a decrease of the overlap between the acceptor state density and the donor orbital(s) of the excited dye.63,64

2.3 Organic sensitisers

The basic structure of most of organic sensitisers is composed of a donor (D), a bridge (B, typically a π-spacer), and an acceptor (A), which are usually combined following a D–π–A rod-like configuration (Fig. 7) in order to improve the efficiency of the UV–VIS (Ultra Violet VISible) photoinduced ICT. We have nevertheless to point out the specific case of the panchromatic sensitisers, for which no typical B and A groups can be pinpointed. For this kind of dye, the D chromophoric unit (i.e., the structural part of the molecule which is the most affected by the electronic excitation) is directly linked to the semiconductor through the carboxylates, so that the molecule is adsorbed onto the oxide substrate.

To further design and develop more efficient metal-free dyes for DSSCs, appropriate DBA systems with sharply tunable properties need to be designed by applying the adequate structural modifications. For instance, the D moiety is expected to greatly confine the cationic charge from the semiconductor surface and to efficiently hamper the recombination. It should also offer steric obstruction, so to impede the unfavourable dye aggregation on the semiconductor surface.69 The bridging groups fix the light-absorbing regions of the DSSCs, and subsequently the scale of the electron injection from the dye excited state to the semiconductor surface. Note that the A group is commonly functionalized by one or several carboxylate group(s).

Generally, the factors having a critical impact on the sensitisation are : (i) the excited state redox potential has to match the energy of the semiconductor CB edge; (ii) the HOMO of the dye (Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital) must fit the iodine/triiodide redox potential, and the corresponding LUMO (Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital) has to be higher in energy than the semiconductor conduction band edge; (iii) a large light-harvesting ability of the dye is crucial to get a substantial photocurrent response; (iv) an efficient conjugation is needed across the donor and anchoring groups to ensure the charge transfer (CT) character of the electronic transition; (v) the electronic coupling strength between dye's LUMO and the semiconductor CB is of course a key property for an efficient electron injection from the dye onto the semiconductor surface. On top of that, the formation of dye aggregates tends to lead to low conversion efficiency for many organic dyes in DSSCs. The same holds for charge recombination between the CB electrons and the cationic dye or the electrolyte.70–72

3. Modern DSSC theoretical research

In this section, we intend to review the principal theoretical studies of DSSCs that have appeared over the last decade. Most works have aimed to provide a better understanding of the role of (i) the sensitiser; (ii) the solvent; (iii) the electrolyte; and (iv) the semiconductor film, all taking part in the operational principles of a DSSC. Of course, literature provides such a large number of investigations that we certainly do not intend to cover them all in this Section. Let us first trace herein the history of the different investigations performed on very popular classes of panchromatic dyes (also known as Grätzel′s dyes) as well as recent studies of very promising metal-free organic dyes (e.g. coumarins, perylenes, triphenylamines) in DSSC applications. Both families of dyes have been widely studied with the help of a large panel of theoretical tools and procedures, including QM theories, molecular mechanics (MM), or molecular dynamics (MD) approaches.

In most DSSC devices studied so far, light is absorbed by a ruthenium-based molecular dye, such as N3 (see Scheme 1). The first theoretical investigations on this type of dye sensitiser date from the late 1990s and early 2000s, and were mainly performed by means of semi-empirical,73–76 Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations,77 and more recently by Time-Dependent DFT (TDDFT).66,78 Indeed, numerous investigations of ruthenium-based sensitisers have appeared in the literature since 2002.79–87 These works essentially include a combined theoretical/experimental effort aimed at providing a better understanding of the role of the sensitiser, and specifically, the impact of ground and excited state electronic structure and properties are the efficiency of the DSSC devices.

In particular, Fantacci and coworkers,81,83,84 as well as Guillemoles et al.80 have shown that the inclusion of solvation effects, within the Polarisable Continuum Model (PCM) framework,88 is crucial when describing the electronic structures and absorption spectra of Ru(II)-polypyridil complexes. As an illustration, large differences are found in the electronic structures and spectra of the N3 derivative simulated in vacuo79 or in solution.82 Note that solvation effects have also been found to be crucial to reach a correct description of dye-sensitised TiO2 nanoparticles.85 In a recent work, Nazeerudin et al.89 carried out (TD)DFT calculations taking into account solvation effects, of the geometries, electronic structures and optical absorption spectra of derivatives of the N3 and the N621 dyes. They compared the theoretical results with the outcome of electrochemical and spectroscopic studies. Moreover, they extensively investigated the effects on their electronic structures and optical properties of the terminal carboxylic group protonation as well as the presence of counterions on the electronic structure and optical properties of the two kinds of dyes. They have also analysed the line-up of the dyes' energy levels within the semiconductor band edges of a model TiO2 nanoparticle. From this joint theoretical and experimental effort, significant insights into the nature of the excited states involved in the electron injection process, and the role of dye protonation on the device performances have been unravelled. In particular, some general trends can be inferred though the results have been obtained considering the non-interacting dye and semiconductor systems: (i) the hypsochromic shift of the excitation energies occurs with increasing deprotonation; (ii) for both N3 (4 protons) and N621 (2 protons) derivatives, the fully protonated or fully deprotonated (N3−4) and N621−2) species show the smallest and largest first excitation energies, respectively; (iii) the monoprotonated and diprotonated forms of the N3 derived dyes constitute an optimal choice with respect to the fully protonated and deprotonated species due to the good compromise between their light-harvesting capability and the alignment of their excited states with the TiO2 conduction edge.

Several theoretical studies based upon more elaborate interacting dye-SC models can be found in the literature.90–95 Specifically, De Angelis and coworkers90 performed a comparative study of the electronic structure and excited states of the N3-TiO2 and N719-TiO2 supramolecular edifices within a combined PCM-TDDFT and MD Car–Parrinello96 framework. More precisely, the authors investigated the alignment of the sensitiser excited states with the TiO2 conduction band. This quantity determines the rate of electron injection together with the electronic coupling between the dye and the semiconductor, and has therefore a central role in DSSCs. In their work, the authors were the first to determine the excited state oxidation potential for the isolated dyes and TiO2 nanoparticles in solution, by expressing the Edye*OX as the difference between the EdyeOX and Edye0–0 (see eqn (11)), and using different approximations for the two latter quantities. The resulting Edye*OX values are found to agree very well with the available experimental data. Next they compared the alignment for the non-interacting dye and SC to the results of calculations performed for the explicitly coupled system. Interestingly, the latter results appear to be very close to the non-interacting case (within ∼0.1 eV). The excellent agreement of their results with experimental data indicates that both the uncoupled and coupled approaches of their work could be selected to help designing dye-sensitised solar cells.

In spite of their important SPCE, the interest in Ru-derived sensitisers tends to fall off and this decrease of popularity can mainly be interpreted as a consequence of the very expensive production costs of such dyes, as ruthenium is not abundant in nature.97 Consequently, metal-free sensitisers such as organic dyes and natural dyes have been investigated as alternative molecules for DSSC applications.97

Present-day literature frequently claims achievements of ∼8% efficiency with metal-free organic dyes.42–53,98,99 Currently, DFT, TDDFT as well as LRC-TDDFT (Long-Range-Corrected TDDFT)78,100–103 theoretical studies can provide valuable and reliable informations on the first elementary steps of the DSSC mechanism (for instance the absorption and fluorescence spectra, the interactions with the redox mediator, and the influence of the solvent bath on the efficiency of a DSSC). Several papers have recently been devoted to simulations of organic dyes at the atomistic level.44,102,104–108 However, only a few papers have treated the dye-SC system, most of them being limited to the study of edifices made of N3 and TiO2.109–112 In spite of the inherent limitations of TDDFT for the modelisation of supramolecular edifices (clusters), such theoretical studies have provided valuable insights and play a central role in improving solar cells performances.

In agreement with these previous investigations, Labat et al.113 recently used DFT techniques within a periodic approach to provide a detailed investigation of the eosin-Y dianion (EYD) assembled with a ZnO cluster (wurtzite).114–116 This system has been fully characterised at the geometric, energetic and electronic levels. In order to check the feasibility of an electron transfer from the absorbate to the dye, the authors considered a reduced form of the EYD/ZnO assembly for which the electronic structure analysis includes densities of states (DOS) calculations and spin densities analysis. The main conclusions of this work can be summarised as follows: (i) EYD tightly adsorbs to ZnO, and the UV–VIS spectral signature of the isolated dye is conserved once adsorbed on the semiconductor surface; (ii) both the HOMO and the LUMO of the dye lie within the ZnO band gap and are exclusively localized on the dye. Thus, direct HOMO → LUMO excitation does not lead to electron injection into the semiconductor. Only when an electron is injected in the LUMO + 1 level, the first unoccupied orbital with significant contributions from the ZnO, can a direct injection take place. This conclusion is fully consistent with experimental measurements (that found a maximal SPCE of only ∼2%);115 (iii) a two-electron mechanism is proposed in order to rationalize the low efficiency of the EY/ZnO solar cells. In addition, Labat's team suggests two strategies to improve the cell operation: (i) the addition of a remote electron-donating group and; (ii) the appropriate chemical modifications of the dye to tune the LUMO energetic level and bring it above the bottom of the CB of ZnO, which should result in a direct injection process. For a rational design of the dye-SC interactions, the authors underline that the choice of the anchoring group can be of major importance, although this group remains optically inactive.

Along the same lines, we have to evoke the recent contribution of Srinivas, et al.117 who report the synthesis and theoretical investigations of a series of four newly-designed anthracene derivatives. The theoretical investigation of the electronic excitations was performed at the PCM-TDDFT and DFT levels of theory, whereas the modelisation of the adsorption modes of the dyes on the TiO2 anatase surface has been carried out using the periodic DFT scheme implemented in the PWscf code.118 The experimental data show that all four investigated anthracenes have reasonable conversion efficiencies (with a maximal IPCE of ∼6%). On the other hand, the theoretical investigations have shown that the binding of the cyanoacrylic acid group to the SC surface is up to 6 kcal mol−1 more favorable than the malonic acid group complexation. This larger binding energy seems to be the main reason for the higher efficiency of the sensitisers functionalized with a cyanoacrylic acid acceptor group.

Aside from the “traditional” QM approaches like TDDFT, numerous explicit time domain simulations can be found in the literature. These approaches most directly mimic the time-resolved experimental data and provide a detailed description of actually-occuring mechanisms. Indeed, the ultra-fast time of the electronic and vibrational processes at the dye–SC interface make it difficult to invoke the standard theories summarized above. In this context, we would like to highlight the works published by Prezhdo's crew,119–128 and in particular, one of their most recent contributions127 in which the authors expand on their earlier investigations and study the relaxation of the injected electron inside the TiO2 CB, the back electron transfer reaction from the semiconductor to the alizarin, as well as the regeneration of the neutral chromophore. They consider the geometric and electronic structures of the interface optimised at zero Kelvin and study the thermal fluctuations that may substantially affect the interface electronic structure and activate atomic vibrations, to eventually focus on each relaxation process.128 Besides the QM approaches, time-resolved calculations are of particular interest for elucidating the ultra-fast electron transfer processes that show a variety of individual events with well-defined dynamical signatures.129–131 Moreover, such a description of the interface induces intuitive representations appreciated by most chemists and creates a clear understanding of the effects of atomic structure, binding, chemical functions, and electronic interactions, occurring through bonds and through space. It is important to underline that such simulations are still uncommon and require a huge computational effort. Nevertheless, the field is rapidly progressing and state-of-the-art techniques for the dynamics simulations are being energetically expanded. As the theoretical research advances, larger systems and longer time scales will become accessible, allowing to probe more examples and to obtain finer details of the chemical dynamics.

The last topic we retain in this Section concerns the simulation at an extended scale of DSSC performances using the MM and MD schemes performed by Chen et al.132 They investigated, at the atomic scale, the transport phenomena of the iodide/triiodide redox couple in the ionic liquid electrolyte of a DSSC model. More precisely, the authors successfully reproduced the molecular diffusion mechanism of the redox couple, which partly limits the solar cell performances, and have proposed an optimal composition for the ionic liquid. Their results were turned into a prototype of a solar cell and it has been concluded that the maximum power and photocurrent efficiency both coincide with the experimental data collected by Grätzel and Nazeeruddin.133,134

4. The route to new improvements

A DSSC comprises four major components: (i) the dye, (ii) the redox shuttle, (iii) the SC photoanode and (iv) the cathode. In order to induce significant improvements in device efficiency, however, it is obviously necessary to alter at least one of these four major components and we will now present a specific way to upgrade the dye sensitiser properties.

4.1 Triphenylamines: optimal structures

Recently, it has been found that triphenylamine (TPA)-like moieties and the cyanoacetic acid group (see Fig. 7) are units of choice as electron donor and electron acceptor/anchoring groups, respectively.69,70 We have reported on the design of new molecules44 derived from several organic dyes recently synthesised by Tian et al.135 The investigated dyes consist of TPAs connected to the acceptor (cyanoacetic acid or rhodanine-3-acetic acid) moiety by a phenylene. 5. Conclusions and prospects

This Perspective proposed to introduce the reader to the recent progress and theoretical research in the DSSC field. The first objective was to discuss the engineering science related to a conceptual dye-sensitised solar cell with an emphasis on the physical process, as well as on the resulting energetic balance involved in the actual device. Afterwards, we presented selected recent DSSC theoretical research and developments in order to outline the controversial and emerging issues in the field. Most of the work presented herein aimed to provide a better understanding of the function of (i) the sensitiser; (ii) the solvent; (iii) the electrolyte; and (iv) the semiconductor film that are all involved in the operating principles of a DSSC. In Section 3, we traced the history of the modern investigations performed on the very popular classes of panchromatic dyes and on the recent studies of the very promising metal-free organic dyes. Both families of dyes have been widely studied with the help of a large panel of various theoretical tools and procedures, deriving from QM theories, molecular mechanics (MM) or molecular dynamics (MD) approaches. In Section 4, we came up with new insights into the DSSC area through the presentation of new unpublished data form our recent research. In order to achieve a 10% energy conversion efficiency, we have shown that the configuration of current generation DSSCs has been efficiently and amazingly well optimised. As suggested by T. W. Hammann et al. in a recent publication,32 in order to realise the full promise of DSSCs as high-efficiency energy conversion devices, it is necessary to simultaneously modify at least two of the four major components (the dye, the redox shuttle, the SC photoanode and the cathode). In this context, we have presented in this Perspective article a fundamental-level understanding of the electronic excitation and electron injection processes to allow a smart optimisation strategy. More precisely, we have herein presented a procedure that offers new insights into the geometrical and electronic structures of the triphenylamine (TPA) derivatives and brings out the efficient structural modifications needed to improve both the free energy of the electron injection onto the TiO2 substrate and the light harvesting abilities of the dyes. It is our hope that such study on the ICT and about the electron injection processes may act as a guide for the design and synthesis of efficient organic dyes. For instance, TPA-5 shows the best key parameters values (ΔGinject., LHE, Voc and SLF) and is therefore expected to be a very promising molecule in the DSSC field.

Similarly, we want to underline the recent work by Hagfeldt and Tian et al., who report the synthesis of a novel dye containing two TPA units connected by a vinyl group, linked to a rhodanine-3-acetic acid moiety as electron acceptor. This dye has been synthesised as a reference to study the intramolecular energy transfer (IET) and charge transfer (ICT) processes and the results suggest that both processes show a positive effect on the performance of DSSCs.140

Undoubtedly, the close future of organic solar cells relies on their economic potential that depends upon several critical factors like the efficiency, the manufacturing costs, as well as sustainability, weight, scalability or lifetime. At this point, a couple of conditioning techniques can be foreseen: solvent and solvent-free processing. The first process offers a remarkable efficiency and are low-cost. However, these cells use volatile solvents in their electrolytes, which is problematic for outdoor solar panels applications, in view of the need for a robust encapsulation. Therefore, solvent-free ionic liquids have been looked for to obtain attractive solutions. Device efficiencies of over 8% were recently achieved by using some low-viscosity formulations containing a ternary melt of imidazolium iodure.141 This result is of great importance in the perspective of large-scale real-life applications of DSSCs. Nevertheless, whilst such a strategy has been widely applied for Ru(II)-based organic cells, this process has not yet been transferred to metal-free organic solar cells like triphenylamine. In such a context, simulations of the interactions between the dye and the ionic solvent would be warmly welcomed to allow a clear optimisation strategy.141–143
