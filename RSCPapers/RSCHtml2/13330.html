Connecting charge transfer kinetics to device parameters of a narrow-bandgap polymer-based solar cell†

Abstract

To achieve intrinsically light-weight flexible photovoltaic devices, a bulk-heterojunction-type active layer with a narrow-bandgap polymer is still considered as one of the most important candidates. Therefore, detailed information about the charge transfer efficiency from a photo-excited species on an electron-donating polymer to an electron acceptor is an important factor, given that it is among the most fundamental quantitative measures to understand the solar power conversion efficiency, in particular at the initial stage followed by primary exciton formation. To obtain accurate information in this regard, wide-range acceptor concentration-dependent transient absorption spectroscopy with femtosecond laser pulse excitation was performed using a representative narrow-bandgap polymer, commonly known as PTB7. The investigated acceptor concentration range covered was from 0.01 wt% up to 10 wt%, in addition to a 0 wt% pristine polymer sample and a sample with a conventional acceptor concentration of 60 wt%, which is important for high efficiency. From the kinetic data, an almost two orders of magnitude faster acceptor-induced charge transfer rate constant in addition to the native primary exciton lifetime of about 100 picoseconds could be extracted. These data were used to verify the suggested kinetic model and compare with device properties that show no meaningful loss during the extraction of photo-generated charge carriers.

Introduction

Narrow-bandgap (NBG) polymers are still believed to be important candidates for use as an active layer component of soluble polymeric bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) solar cells as significantly higher power conversion efficiency (PCE) has been reported for these polymers.1–3 Although the PCE of an NBG polymer close to 10% is not yet comparable with market-dominating silicon-based solar cells, the polymer has shown an impressive possibility not only for light-weight flexible photovoltaic (PV) devices but also for promising printable electronics due to its intrinsic compatibility with the high throughput solution process.4

To prepare an efficient PV device with a NBG polymer, a suitable counterpart, typically a fullerene derivative, is used, which is known as an effective electron acceptor which forms an optimum BHJ active layer where incident photons are absorbed and subsequently transformed into free charge carriers (CCs) rather than recombining mono-molecularly. In fact, acceptor-induced efficient charge transfer (CT) can be easily confirmed by measuring efficient photoluminescence (PL) reduction upon adding a fullerene derivative to, for instance, a poly-3-hexylthiophene (P3HT) solid. Furthermore, the acceptor-induced CT rate constant can be estimated even by means of acceptor concentration dependent steady-state PL spectroscopy, as shown with a comparable π-conjugated polymer blend system previously.5

Problems arose when a polymer system has a very low photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY) as for the case of some NBG polymers such as PCPDTBT.6 In this case, the so-called transient absorption spectroscopy (TAS) must be performed to depict the nonradiative exciton dynamics, including the CT. TAS enables the detection of the population of both radiative and nonradiative excited species, including primary singlet excitons, triplet excitons, or bound electron and hole pairs. Numerous TAS studies have been conducted related to organic solar cells (OSCs), including a comprehensive review of TAS that deals with many specific aspects related to P3HT-based OSCs.7

However, the reason to use TAS is not only the difficulty in PL signal detection but also the amplitude mismatch between PLQY and the device's external and internal quantum efficiencies (EQE and IQE, respectively). As mentioned, PCPDTBT has a significantly low intrinsic PLQY of approximately 6%6 as the pristine film, while the IQE of the PCPDTBT's optimized device is more than 80%.8 The important point here is that both the PLQY and the IQE are actually ratios calculated similarly based on the same total number of absorbed photons in an active layer, i.e., the former is the ratio of the emitted photon number and the latter is that of the photogenerated electron number normalized to the same basis. This means that the high IQE cannot be explained when the CC photogeneration is described only as a competitive process against the PL emission process. This is also valid for a well-studied P3HT system although its PLQY is significantly larger than that of PCPDTBT or similar NBG polymers. Pristine samples have a PLQY of approximately 33%,9 which is clearly smaller than its EQE, often denoted as the incident photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE), and is smaller again than the reported IQEs of most optimized devices.7

Consequently, CT from a donor to an acceptor has to be considered at least as a competitive process against all mono-molecular instantaneous relaxation processes including nonradiative as well as radiative processes. Hence, TAS or similar methods employed to depict nonradiative latent processes are important to evaluate active materials for PV applications. Therefore, many NBG polymers including PCPDTBT, the predecessor of PTB7 having a higher PCE, and PTB7 itself with a number of other NBG derivatives were often studied by means of various kinetic studies including TAS. Clarke et al. have shown the influence of a dithiol additive for PCPDTBT by TAS up to the millisecond time range.10 Jarzab et al. have reported 0.04% PLQY with voltage bias and temperature-dependent PL decay kinetics of PCPDTBT blend samples.6 In addition, Mikhnenko et al. have reported about PCPDTBT's exciton diffusion length of about 10.5 nm using PL decay kinetics.11 Various NBGs' exciton dynamics to clarify the effects of chemical structures on PV properties using TAS with fs laser pulses were also shown by Tautz et al.12 Furthermore, Granzini et al. have shown additional TAS with extremely short laser pulses.13 Recently, similar kinetic studies for various NBG derivatives were also reported.14–16 Intensive studies about PCPDTBT have been performed due to the fact that it was the first NBG polymer to successfully overcome the PCE limit of P3HT. Helpful reviews about the synthesis of conjugated polymers for organic PV applications17 and strategies to improve the PCE of BHJ solar cells18 are mentioned here for a better overview.

Shortly after the successful debut of PCPDTBT, further improved PTB7 was introduced followed by numerous time-resolved spectroscopic studies, for instance, by Carsten et al. with a conventional TAS method.19 Soon et al. have shown a TAS study with PTB7, especially for its correlation with the triplet population and character.20 Herdley et al. have shown acceptor concentration-dependent PL decay mainly to discuss about the optimum morphology of that system.21 Later, a comprehensive TAS study on the PTB7 neat and blend systems was presented by Szarko et al.22 Recently, Gerhard et al. have reported a study about CT states by temperature- and energy-dependent near infrared emission.23

In spite of the above-mentioned intensive efforts and valuable contribution in order to understand the complicated exciton dynamics of NBG polymers, connecting the exciton dynamics of those solid systems to their corresponding device properties is difficult; in other words, connecting the kinetic parameters to their short-circuit current (JSC) values or IQEs in a strictly quantitative way is difficult. The quantitative connection between kinetic parameters and device properties is important not only for understanding the underlying mechanisms of BHJ organic PV devices, but also for the verification of the meaning and value of those time-consuming time-resolved kinetic studies on active materials. Simultaneously, such parametric correlations should also be essential for removing any ambiguities caused by the complexity of kinetic behaviours, in particular within such disordered random solids. These are undoubtedly essential not only for the expansion of scientific knowledge but also for the improvement of practical performances of those materials for PV applications.

Therefore, we have decided to investigate a representative NBG polymer, PTB7, by means of an acceptor concentration-dependent TAS. The concentration range covered was from 0.01–60 wt% including a neat sample in order to extract a reliable acceptor-induced CT rate constant, primary exciton decay lifetime, and subsequent recombination kinetics that occur afterwards. At the same time, typical BHJ devices were prepared and characterized for the same concentration under the same active-layer deposition conditions to maintain consistency. Later, those kinetic parameters were used to calculate the possible number of photogenerated electron and hole pairs for a given acceptor concentration in order to compare those values with their corresponding device parameters. Finally, the validity of the suggested scheme for various excited species and their dynamics were discussed using a strictly limited reaction kinetic framework rather than by technical fitting with multi-exponential parameters. It should be noticed that the concentration range used in this study is mainly focused on the range between 0.01% and 10% which is lower than that of usual device preparation conditions because that range covering three orders of magnitude is more important to depict precise exciton dynamics for the target system.

Experimental

Preparation

All chemicals including poly[4,8-bis[(2-ethylhexyl)oxy]benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b′]dithiophene-2,6-diyl][3-fluoro-2-[(2-ethylhexyl)carbonyl]thieno[3,4-b]-thiophenediyl] (PTB7, 1-Material Inc.) and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM, 1-Material Inc.)—an electron donor and an electron acceptor—were used as purchased without any further purification.

All devices were prepared as described in the earlier study22 except the effort made to keep all active layer thicknesses as identical as possible given that one of the main purposes of this study is to depict the relationship between acceptor concentration dependency of their spectroscopic kinetics and that of their device parameters, fill factors (FF), open-circuit voltages (VOC), short-circuit current densities (JSC), and PCEs. The structure of the prepared devices is typically as follows: “glass (1.1 mm)/ITO (180 nm)/PEDOT:PSS (50 nm)/PTB7:PC71BM (100 nm)/LiF (0.3 nm)/Al (120 nm).”

In addition, single-layer samples on glass substrates for diverse optical characterization were prepared in a similar manner to those on ITO-covered glass substrates for device preparation as described previously24 to maintain the film quality as much as possible. In this study, the concentration of solutes, namely the total weight concentration of PTB7 and PC71BM together, was 2.16 wt% in chlorobenzene (CB) solvent. The PC71BM concentration in the total blend solutes was 0.01, 0.03, 0.1, 0.3, 1.0, 3.0, and 10, 60 wt%, respectively, to obtain a wide-range acceptor concentration dependency for accurate spectroscopic kinetics. Thin films were deposited on substrates by conventional spin-coating at revolutions-per-minute (RPM) values ranging from 6500 for 0.01 wt% to 5000 for 10 wt%. The RPM values were varied to keep thicknesses the close as possible for all samples used. After the deposition of those active layers on substrates, either on bare glasses or ITO-covered glasses, they were encapsulated with engraved cover glasses to prevent any possible degradation problems during measurement.

Characterization

UV-visible absorption (UV-Vis) spectra as well as transmittance spectra of solution samples and films spin-coated on glass substrates were recorded using a spectrophotometer (Neosys-2000, Scinco). Additional reflectance spectra were measured using a spectrophotometer (AvaSpec-ULS2048, Avantes) especially for device samples with an Al top electrode due to their opacity. Steady-state fluorescence spectra both for a solution and films were measured using a spectrophotometer (FS-2, Scinco) equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp as an excitation light source. Absolute PLQYs were obtained using a commercial system (Quantaurus-QY, C11347, Hamamatsu Photonics K.K.).

Ultrafast transient absorption spectroscopy (TAS) was performed by utilizing a Ti:sapphire regenerative amplifier laser system (Libra, Coherent) with a pulse width of approximately 100 fs at a repetition rate of 1 kHz in conjunction with a pump–probe spectrometer (Helios, Ultrafast Systems LLC). To select a specific excitation wavelength, an optical parametric amplifier system (TOPAS, Coherent) was used. For white light continuum to probe the excited species, a sapphire crystal was used. The TAS kinetics was measured up to 3 nanoseconds (ns) with picosecond (ps) time-resolution, and the probing wavelength ranges covered from 450 to 900 nm for a visible (Vis) wavelength range detector and from 900 nm to 1400 nm for an IR wavelength range detector. The above-mentioned detection ranges were given for optimal signal to noise ratio (S/N) cases. Often there was a gap between approximately 800 nm and 900 nm where there was not sufficient S/N available, especially in a low excitation intensity situation. In most cases, three different intensities (200 μJ cm−2, 80 μJ cm−2, and 30 μJ cm−2) of the pump beam per pulse were used to perform TAS. The average size of pump beams was approximately 1 mm in diameter. This value is relatively larger than the other conventional TAS cases because we were trying to reduce too strong intensity-dependent bimolecular behaviours by decreasing the photon flux density because such a strong bimolecular influence can affect the patterns of native kinetic behaviours. TAS spectra were recorded as the change in absorbance (ΔA) using a personal computer and a program provided by the manufacturer of the pump–probe spectrometer.

PCE values with device parameters FF, VOC, and JSC were estimated carefully according to conventional standard conditions as described in detail previously.24 Thicknesses of the active layers were measured primarily by using a surface profiler (XP-200, AMBiOS) and later the values were verified with the results obtained using atomic force microscope (XE-70, Park systems), typically under a noncontact mode. The data were also cross-checked with the reflectance data of devices to prevent any significant deviation in film thickness, especially between semi-transparent single-layer samples and opaque device samples.

Results and discussion

Steady-state UV-Vis and PL spectra of a diluted solution in CB and a thin pristine film are shown in Fig. 1. PL spectra were recorded at an excitation wavelength of 660 nm. A significant change achieved by solidification was that the maximum PL peak at 767 nm of a pristine film was red-shifted by about 70 meV compared to that of the solution at 743 nm as is usual in π-conjugated polymers due to enhanced π–π interaction25 and/or the extension of the effective π-conjugation length26 under densely packed conditions. However, the UV-Vis peak maximum of a pristine film was marginally blue-shifted to 672 nm from 678 nm of a solution sample, and this is unusual compared to the red-shift that occurs upon solidification. Furthermore, both secondary shoulder peaks of the solution and film samples were similarly centred at approximately 626 nm. The unchanged or marginally blue-shifted feature in the UV-Vis spectrum can be explained by using the term forbidden state formation for an energetically low-lying state having an extended π-conjugation. If only a transition to the next higher energy level is allowed, then a blue-shifted band can be observed.27 The fine structures with more than two peaks in the UV-Vis spectra of both the solution and the film had approximately 140 meV differences and this is a reasonable value to assign to a vibrational structure caused by a CC double bond stretching mode. The values of the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of approximately 250 meV were virtually the same for each PL band of a solution and a film sample. The FWHM of approximately 250 meV is also similar for the first UV-Vis band of a solution sample while the first UV-Vis band of a film sample seems approximately 40 meV broadened.
Fig. 1 UV-Vis and PL spectra of a PTB7 solution in CB and the PTB7 film.

The difference in the UV-Vis band caused by solidification can be described as an inhomogeneous broadening of the native FWHM value of a solution sample. In fact, the observed inhomogeneous broadening of the UV-Vis band upon solidification is also a sign of the significant interaction between polymer chains and the subsequent increase of energetic disorder of the solid system.28 It is noteworthy that the shifts in PL and UV-Vis spectra of the P3HT:PC61BM system were approximately 0.5 eV.29 This value being an order of magnitude larger than that of the PTB7:PC71BM system reflects the difference between pronounced molecular ordering facilities of the regioregular P3HT.30

The obtained PLQYs, upon excitation at 660 nm, of the solution and pristine film samples were about 5.2 and 2.2%, respectively. The value of a pristine film is comparable with the value of 0.02 reported by Hedley et al.21 This implies that the intrinsic PLQY of PTB7 is much lower than the usual π-conjugated polymer materials even in a dilute solution; in other words, PTB7 has inherently very efficient nonradiative decay channels. The reduction of the PLQY from a solution to a film is a typical feature of π-conjugated polymeric solids due to enhanced PL quenching probability caused by the three-dimensional (3D) dense packing of the polymer chains.6 In spite of the low PLQY, a systematic reduction of PLQY as a function of acceptor concentration was observed, as shown in Fig. 2. The PLQY was reduced by almost an order of magnitude, from 2.2% down to 0.3%, upon the addition of 10 wt% acceptor to the narrow-bandgap polymer matrix.

It should be noted that eqn (1) was slightly modified and plotted to compare with the non-normalized experimental PLQYs, as shown in Fig. 2. The obtained value of kQ(PL) was about 1.8 × 1013 s−1 when using a τ0 of 100 ps extracted from the TAS kinetics discussed later in this study. This value is almost an order of magnitude larger than the value of 3.2 × 1012 s−1 for the PPV:TNF system.5

However, the experimental PLQY values follow the calculated theoretical curve only up to approximately 1 wt%, as shown in Fig. 2. Interestingly, kQ(PL) for an acceptor concentration higher than 1.0 wt% seems less effective than that for a lower acceptor concentration range. This is attributed to the pronounced phase separation-induced change in morphology, which is typical for analogous BHJ systems, especially with a higher acceptor concentration as reported earlier.21 Such systems can exhibit severe deviation from well-dispersed mixture systems such as the solution for which the original Stern–Volmer formalism was derived.31 It should also be noted that technical limits during absolute PLQY measurement can be reached when the PLQY value is close to the order of 10−3.

In Fig. 3, current density–voltage (J–V) plots of devices with various acceptor concentrations are shown. From the plots, PV device parameters are extracted and plotted in Fig. 4 as a function of acceptor concentration and are summarized in Table 1. The acceptor concentration-dependent PCE trend has virtually the same functional dependence as JSC. The FF also has a similar trend to PCE but decreases slightly up to approximately 0.1 wt% and then stays constant up to approximately 10 wt% with a value of approximately 25%. Subsequently, the FF increases about two-fold from 10 wt% to 60 wt% where the PTB7:PC71BM device reaches the highest PCE as reported in many previous reports.1,32 However, a dramatically different dependency was observed with VOC. VOC was virtually constant with a value of approximately 1.1 V from 0.01 to 0.1 wt% and then decreased monotonically with increasing acceptor concentration. JSC from 3 wt% to 60 wt% increased by about two orders of magnitude whereas JSC from 0 wt% to 3 wt% increased only by about two-fold. As a result, the PCE increased from 3 wt% very rapidly as PCE is a product of JSC, VOC, and FF. It might be helpful to notice that both PCE and JSC are presented in the logarithmic scale, while FF and VOC are presented in the linear scale.

The gradual decrease of VOC with increasing acceptor concentration should reflect that the VOC depends on the accumulation of photogenerated CCs inside the BHJ active layer because of insufficient CC transport facility below approximately a 10 wt% range. This can also be supported by the fact that the exponentially increasing acceptor concentration dependency of JSC in the 3–10 wt% range, despite the spectroscopic results strongly supporting the fact that the CC, or at least the bound electron and hole pairs that can be referred to as precursors of free CCs, and generation at a moderate acceptor concentration had already reached a significant level, as discussed in the later part of this study. At the same time, the two-fold increase of the FF above 10 wt% is also strong evidence that the bipolar CC transport inside the active layer is more improved due to optimal morphology formation above 10 wt%. Analogous CC generation and transport facility dependence on the acceptor concentration was also reported with a polyphenylenevinylene copolymer solid and a hexa-peri-hexabenzocoronene solid, each blended with perylenediimide.33 Expectedly, device behaviors of P3HT:PC61BM and P3HT:ICBA were also comparable, especially in the low concentration range.29

A similar interpretation of the enhanced CC mobility above a suitable acceptor concentration was already suggested in our previous study dealing with the thickness-dependent IQE of the same BHJ system.24 For the same compositions of PTB7 and PC71BM for the active layers with approximately 90 nm thickness, the obtained PCEs were higher than 6% and the corresponding IQEs were also higher than 70% virtually over the entire spectral range of photon absorption. Even for the less efficient device with 320 nm thickness, its PCE was 2.2% and IQE was approximately 20%. This means that approximately only 2% of the PE population that decays radiatively cannot be a major source of photogenerated CCs at all.

To discuss the TAS results, it might be helpful to define crucial states to describe the exciton dynamics within the active layers, as shown in Scheme 1. For that, simple two-state models were used for both PTB7 and PC71BM to obtain a clear kinetic overview. After the absorption of incident photons, primary excitons (PEs) are generated. These PEs can return to their ground states (GSs) through either radiative (R) or nonradiative (NR) decay, assuming that extrinsic acceptors do not exist and CT via intrinsic defects can be ignored. As soon as an electron accepting moiety is incorporated, the PEs can have at least one more competitive decay pathway via CT that can form at least intermediately electrostatically bound electron and hole pairs. The electron and hole pairs can be either bimolecularly recombined to form GSs again or separately transported to each counter electrode in a device. However, in single-layer samples being general for TAS, the CT population has to return to GS bimolecularly due to the lack of an externally connected circuitry. That is the reason why JSC, a device property, cannot be easily compared with any spectroscopic data without a device structure, although JSC is often referred to as a measure of CC photogeneration probability due to its short-circuit conditions. In other words, even for measuring JSC, the photogenerated electrons have to travel through the active layer and survive to be detected by a suitable external electronic device.

The TAS of the Vis range is shown only up to the pump beam position for convenience. The sign of IR range bands is positive while that of the Vis range bands is negative at the prompt moment of pump beam irradiation. All bands converge toward zero with time as usual. Therefore, the generated CCs cannot effectively travel through the active layer and are extracted by any electrodes as in PV devices; rather, they have to lose their excess energy within the active layer and return to their GSs through various pathways.

3D contour type maps of TAS spectra shown in Fig. 5 are raw data without any de-chirping correction. De-chirping treatment is performed for all kinetic data for better accuracy, especially at an almost prompt time range of 1–3 ps.

There are strong acceptor concentration-dependent TAS features. First, the broad IR band centered at about 1350 nm and pronounced at low acceptor concentrations was transferred to a band center at about 1100 nm upon increasing the acceptor concentration. Second, the width of the IR band became narrower after transfer. Third, the IR band at higher acceptor concentrations showed clear spectral relaxation features as reported previously.22 This is also clearly observable for the Vis range band in Fig. 5C. Fourth, the recovery time of the GSB is extended with increasing acceptor concentration as the second IR band's lifetime is extended.

From the first and second observations, the IR band centered at about 1350 nm could be assigned as a PE band while the IR band centered at about 1100 nm could be assigned as an acceptor-induced band, often referred to as the CT band.

Then the Vis band centered at about 680 nm could be assigned as a GS band, also due to the opposite sign of ΔA compared to the PE and GT bands. This BHJ solid system has an unusual benefit for TAS due to very low PLQYs because strong emission bands can overlap partly with GS and/or with IR bands. This overlap often makes kinetic traces more complex. The third and fourth features reflect that the PE band and the CT band are the major kinetic components through which the native GS population is recovered and is also shown with kinetic traces in Fig. 6. Hence, it is strongly suggested that there is no significant need to consider any other additional processes except for the PE, CT, and GS band population and de-population processes, as depicted in Scheme 1.

As mentioned in Fig. 5, the acceptor concentration-dependent CT is clearly seen again in Fig. 6. However, the entire PE band cannot be recorded using our current set-up, but the PE band seems to have a fairly broad FWHM of approximately 0.5 eV compared to the CT band's FWHM of approximately 0.2 eV, if the bands can be assumed as symmetric Gaussian shapes. The broad FWHM of the PE band should be the reason for the lower intensity of the PE band compared to the GS band. Actually, the spectra of (A), (B), and (C) were scaled to have the same initial GS band intensity as the initial PE population criteria presented with dashed lines in the Vis area of each plot. Due to this normalization with the initial de-population amount of GS, the systematic reduction of PE upon increasing the acceptor concentration can be clearly recognized. It is also remarkable that the CT population, i.e. the CT band intensity, at 3 wt% is already very similar to that at 60 wt%. This feature will be discussed in more detail with kinetic traces presented in the next part.

In Fig. 6, normalized TAS kinetics at various detection wavelengths for the acceptor concentrations of (A) 0.01 wt%, (B) 3.0 wt%, and (C) 60 wt% are shown. The TA kinetic decays for the data in Fig. 6 were recorded with the same medium excitation intensity described in the Experimental part. The first point to notice is that the deviation between PE and CT kinetics increases with increasing acceptor concentration. Indeed, the deviation between PE and CT kinetics was not intensely observed at 0.01 wt% as expected. In addition, GSB recovering kinetics at 0.01 wt% was almost the same as both PE and CT kinetics, except below 0.2 in the normalized ΔA scale.

In principle, the CT band may not appear at the prompt moment because the CT band is a subsequent population after forming the PE population. However, PE and CT kinetics seem very similar, especially at low concentrations because the PE band's FWHM is extremely broad as this could cover even the whole CT band area as shown in Fig. 5 and 7. Hence, CT and PE band kinetics cannot be easily traced independently. Nevertheless, the deviation of TAS kinetics between PE and CT at 3 wt% seems to be notably enhanced.

At 60 wt%, the deviation seems more enhanced; the shortening of the lifetime in the initial stage should be a result of acceptor-induced PE depopulation, and the slightly delayed rise of kinetic features should be explained with the subsequent population of CT states. To extract statistically stable kinetic parameters, in particular for an acceptor-induced CT rate constant, a wide range of acceptor-concentration dependent TAS spectra were also recorded and are presented in Fig. 8. From Fig. 6, the following qualitative conclusions can be drawn; first, the basic PE decay lifetime should be about 100 ps; second, subsequent acceptor concentration-dependent PE quenching that correlated with CT population should be much faster than the PE lifetime; third, the final process, namely CT decay lifetime, possibly via bimolecular recombination, should have a much slower lifetime than the PE lifetime as shown in Fig. 6C.


Fig. 8 Normalized kinetics detected at (A) 684 nm, (B) 993 nm, and (C) 1304 nm of the film samples with various acceptor concentrations, from 0.01 wt% to 60 wt%.

However, the PE kinetic curves significantly deviate from a typical exponential function, mainly due to a pronounced bimolecular decay term especially at high excitation intensity depicted with the intensity dependence results in Fig. 9.


Fig. 9 Normalized intensity-dependent kinetics detected at 993 nm of the film samples with various acceptor concentrations, from 0.01 wt% to 60 wt%. (A) Low intensity@30 μJ (pulse cm2)−1, (B) medium intensity@80 μJ (pulse cm2)−1, and (C) high intensity@200 μJ (pulse cm2)−1.

It is crucial to discuss the excitation intensity-dependent influence on TAS kinetics, in particular to avoid mixing an additional bimolecular exciton decay term, e.g., singlet–singlet annihilation.35 Avoiding too intense excitation is also important to prevent any damage to the investigating films due to the relatively long and severe TAS measurement conditions mainly to improve the small S/N. Although a great amount of information about wide-range excitation intensity-dependent exciton decay behaviors is crucial to expand the knowledge about novel functional materials, a detailed discussion about the incident photon flux-dependent bimolecular behavior is strictly limited to checking the validity of the obtained TAS kinetics, which is a requisite for the explanation of the essential PV phenomenon.

As shown in Fig. 9, the most impressive influence upon the reduction of excitation intensity to the kinetics traced at 993 nm is as follows: first, the extension of lifetimes regardless of the acceptor concentration and the excited species, i.e., PE and CT, and second, all kinetic traces of low acceptor concentration samples recover their exponential decay functionality from power-law functionality with a slope of about −0.5 at high excitation intensity. It is evident that the exciton dynamics within PTB7:PC71BM thin films are highly sensitive to excitation intensity; thus, the PE population and therefore bimolecular processes are dominant at higher intensities compared to other decay processes.

It might be helpful to compare the excitation intensity of the laser pulse with the conventional 1 Sun power of 1 kW m−2, which is a standard for PV operation as well as for steady-state characterization.36 However, serious differences can be noticed on comparing the steady-state continuous-wave (cw) condition directly with the pulsed TAS experimental condition. Assuming that the excitation intensity was 30 μJ (pulse cm2)−1 with the beam diameter of 1 mm at 1 kHz repetition rate, the total incident energy per second on a square meter was about 0.3 kJ (s m2)−1. Although the value of 30 μJ (pulse cm2)−1 was the lowest available intensity to obtain a reasonable S/N with the equipment used here, the converted incident energy is already comparable to that of the standard 1 Sun conditions. In addition, pulsed TAS has a different spectral distribution due to the use of a highly monochromated laser beam source compared to the conventional white light sources for PV application. Furthermore, if the difference between the 100 fs pulse operation of TAS and the continuous 1 Sun intensity of steady-state operation is compared, then the difference in photon flux density at the impact moment of the pump beam is enormous. Hence, applying the kinetic data from conventional pulsed TAS to understand the normal operating situation needs significant attention as expected. One additional significant difference might be the fact that the pulsed kinetic situation is governed by the nonequilibrium conditions probed by the spectral relaxation as shown in Fig. 5, while the steady-state situation should be dominated by the equilibrium conditions. The spectral relaxation within conjugated solid systems was intensively discussed using the Kohlrausch–Williams–Watt-type stretched exponential.28

In order to extract the reliable PE's native lifetime, kinetic curves detected at 1304 nm with low excitation intensity for 0 wt% and 0.01 wt% samples were used and the value of the extracted TAS PE lifetime was about 85 ps as shown in Table 2. Thus, the lifetime values in Table 2 taken at the 1/e position of the normalized ΔA value were corrected to 100 ps according to the analysis of intensity dependence of the kinetics as plotted in Fig. 10A.

Interestingly, the acceptor concentration-dependent quenching rate constant of 1.5 × 1013 s−1 extracted from the PLQY, especially at low concentrations shown in Fig. 2 is about 6-fold faster than that of 3.3 × 1012 s−1 extracted from the systematic shortening of PE lifetimes over a wide range of acceptor concentrations shown in Fig. 8. However, the rate constant of 3.2 × 1012 s−1 for the PPV:TNF system and that extracted with the PLQY for a higher concentration range in this study seem reasonably comparable. All of those trends have a deviation from the linear dependency relation at higher concentrations, i.e., the lowering of kQ. This lowering of the PLQY with the PTB7 case seems to be analogous to a phenomenon of an increase of PL due to the bigger domain size after annealing of the P3HT:PC61BM system37 in addition to its extremely low PLQY values discussed before with Fig. 2.

Considering the difference in molecular weights (MWs) of TNF (315.19 g mol−1) and PC71BM (1030.99 g mol−1), a TNF molecule has only about 1/3 of the weight compared to a PC71BM molecule. Consequently, about 30% shortening of the average inter-molecular distance for TNF at the same wt% concentration is expected by using a simple cubic lattice arrangement assumption. Thus, it can be concluded that PC71BM has a more efficient quenching capability in a homogeneously dispersed situation at lower concentrations than the smaller TNF and PDI acceptors. This may be one of the reasons for the higher PCE of organic BHJ solar cells with a fullerene derivative in addition to their morphology and electronic benefits.

As shown in Fig. 11, the calculated kinetic curves have comparable functional dependence on the experimental kinetics as shown in Fig. 9A. These curves are combinations of PE and CT kinetic curves for each acceptor concentration in order to simulate the experimental kinetic curves traced at 993 nm at a low excitation intensity due to a broad PE band. Actually, the model calculation for Fig. 11 had to include a bimolecular decay term in order to include the power-law decay character at a high excitation intensity as shown in Fig. 9C. In addition, a spectral relaxation term has to be applied in an appropriate way, for instance, by adapting a stretched exponential term to correct the nonequilibrium behavior of PE in the solid system. However, additional terms for bimolecular decay and spectral relaxation were not explicitly included here in order to keep the model as simple as possible. In this study, only the experimental PE lifetime was slightly extended to include the fact that the kinetics without serious excitation intensity conditions should have more pronounced exponential functionality. For that, the extracted native 1/e lifetimes with various excitation intensities as listed in Table 2 were compared and they increased about 18%, from 85 ps to 100 ps, also for simplicity.

To compare the calculated and measured kinetic curves at a glance, the kn of 100 ps for the kinetic calculation seems shorter than it should be, especially in the initial time range. However, the tail parts of the experimental kinetic curves, where ΔA is reduced to approximately 20% compared to their initial value, were significantly extended. This feature should be correlated with stretched exponential behavior being pronounced in a broad density-of-states (DOS) being typical for disordered solid systems. Such disorder is often referred to as a needed condition for the energetic down-hill migration of excited species, and can be observed as spectral relaxation shown in Fig. 5C. Therefore, only a moderately extended value of 100 ps was chosen as a reliable compromise of kn to simulate the model kinetics.

In Fig. 10A, PE decay lifetimes at various excitation intensities listed in Table 2 are shown. Interestingly, all the lifetimes in the high acceptor concentration range were linearly shortened and converged to a common value. Therefore, theoretical calculations could be done by using the same kQ independent of excitation intensity. The characteristic plateau regions that lowered with increasing excitation intensity were also observed. The lowering of the plateau means the shortening of PE lifetimes due to intense bimolecular decay up to a certain acceptor concentration at a given intensity. This means that the PEs readily undergo bimolecular decay very effectively rather than acceptor-induced decay when the PE population is high. In addition, the wider average distance between acceptors at a lower acceptor concentration provides results in a greater probability of the photogenerated PEs to interact with each other before they can reach any acceptors at the initial decay phase. To simulate low excitation intensity in order to explore the usual photon flux situation for ordinary PV operation, a theoretical trend curve for low excitation intensity was calculated with a PE lifetime of 500 ps and is presented in Fig. 10A. By using the data for 1 wt% in Table 2, a linearly decreasing relation can be observed for lifetimes as a function of excitation intensity, while the functional dependencies are super-linear with acceptor concentrations lower than 1 wt% and sublinear with acceptor concentrations higher than 1 wt%. Indeed, the bimolecular decay effect is taken into account by subtracting a constant value from tn for each excitation intensity. This is a practical method as shown but should have limitation as a rough approximation because of the complicated exciton dynamics in disordered BHJ solid systems.

In Fig. 10B, the total number of CT population, assigned as n(CT), obtained by integrating all generated CTs with various native PE lifetimes is plotted as a function of [Q]. The calculations were done with various values of tn in order to correct the bimolecular effects eventually caused by high excitation intensity. In order to compare n(CT) with JSC, n(CT) was multiplied with the value of conductivity converted by the experimentally obtained RS and this should be proportional to the extracting probability of CCs from an active layer to an external circuitry. Finally, n(CT) and the total number of detected CCs, assigned as n(CC), can be compared to disentangle the quantitative relation between CT and CC; in other words, spectroscopic kinetics and electronic device properties. In Fig. 10B, JSC was used to compare with the spectroscopic results because JSC is the photocurrent of a PV device at an external electric field of 0 V. Therefore, JSC can be regarded as the number of photogenerated and detectable CCs being more similar to the character of CT detected by TAS than the number of CCs collected under maximum power point conditions.

However, it seems that the experimental JSC and the calculated n(CT) have dramatic differences in their functional dependencies as shown in Fig. 10B. For lower excitation intensities observed only for hypothetical calculations with 500 ps tn, the calculated IQEs of CT are 82% for 10 wt% [Q] and 83% for 60 wt% [Q]. A plausible explanation for the discrepancy can be based on the fact that the CCs cannot fully escape from the inside of an active layer to the electrodes, especially at low acceptor concentrations as many groups have reported that the CC mobility as well as the PCE is much improved via suitable phase separation at high acceptor concentrations, mostly at about 60 wt%. Actually, our previous study about the thickness-dependent IQE using the same PTB7 BHJ system20 has shown decreasing IQE with increasing active layer thickness. This reflects the fact that the number of safely extracted CCs decreases with increasing thickness although the PE generation efficiency should not be affected by the increasing thickness.

The values of n(CT) are approximately 76% for 10 wt% [Q] and 82% for 60 wt% [Q] with the lowest experimental excitation intensity used in this study. Under these conditions, the used tn was 100 ps. The IQE obtained with a 90 nm-thick device was about 70–80% over the entire absorption spectral range with a 60 wt% device.20 As the same thickness of 100 nm was used for all the samples in this study, it can be concluded that the generated CTs mostly survive by escaping from the active layer to electrodes, except approximately 20–30% of the whole PE population. It is clear that the 20–30% loss is nothing but the portion of PEs dissipated to GS via native unimolecular direct decays. The conclusion can be simplified and expressed as eqn (4) and (5).

 
n(PE) − n(DD) = n(CT)(4)
 
n(CT) − n(BR)(d) = extracted n(CC)(5)
where DD stands for direct monomolecular decay from PE. In this study, DD is a combination of R and NR terms as depicted in Scheme 1. However, the additional loss increases with increasing active layer thickness as shown previously,24 probed by the decrease of IQE with increasing active layer thickness. Therefore, the number of BR is written as a function of active layer thickness as n(BR)(d) in eqn (5).

From that conclusion, we have to correct our previous conclusion that the CC collection probability of the approximately 100 nm-thick active layer is almost 100% rather than the approximately 70% given in the previous study24 because the 30% loss is not caused by CC or CT but by PE through direct native decay. Consequently, it should be concluded that it is possible that the already-formed acceptor-induced CTs can form CCs without any significant loss and subsequently the CCs can also be transported without any significant loss, especially under relatively thin active layer conditions except for the DD loss of PE. Differently expressed, the CC-collection probability at <100 nm thickness is about 100% without any meaningful bimolecular recombination losses.

Conclusions

In this study, PTB7 thin films blended with an acceptor, PC71BM, including a wide range of low acceptor concentrations, were investigated by means of TAS. From the obtained acceptor concentration-dependent kinetic data, PTB7's native spectroscopic lifetime of approximately 100 ps in conjunction with the acceptor-induced CT rate constant of approximately 3.3 × 1012 s−1 was extracted. Furthermore, a CT decay lifetime of about 10 ns was extracted from the TAS kinetic data. These results were examined by performing a numerical simulation based on the suggested kinetic model. The conceptual model seems enough to reconstruct all important dependent functions as a function of acceptor concentration.

Finally, the spectroscopic data were compared with device properties, in particular JSC and the previously estimated IQEs of the PTB7 BHJ systems. By adapting experimentally estimated conductivity as a converting as well as an escaping probability of n(CT) to detectable n(CC), it seems that most CT populations were converted to CC without any significant loss. Therefore, it can be concluded that most CT can be extracted as a CC bimolecular recombination loss freely in an optimized device with a high blending ratio of about 60 wt% and a thin active layer thickness of about 100 nm.

It is noteworthy that this study is needed to be extended to the acceptor concentration range close to the conventional preparation range, from 10% to 80%, in order to confirm the suggested model shown in Fig. 10 more firmly as well as the concentration range where the acceptor portion is significantly dominated to enhance the current understanding of the semiconducting polymer blend systems.
