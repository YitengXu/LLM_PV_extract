A review on g-C3N4/graphene nanocomposites: multifunctional roles of graphene in the nanohybrid photocatalyst toward photocatalytic applications

Abstract

2-Dimensional conductive surface hybrid semiconducting materials have been drawing attention among researchers owing to their photocatalytic performance toward environmental remediation and energy conversion. Surface hybridization between 2D hybrid nanomaterials is considered well-organized owing to their unique physical and chemical properties, inhibition toward photocorrosion, and expansion in spectral response. New approaches toward structural design at the nanoscale level introduce tailored electronic properties and extend their applications as visible light-induced photocatalysts. In particular, the large specific surface area, an increased number of active sites, and high charge mobility endow these 2D/2D hybrid materials with remarkably high performance. In view of recent advances, unlike other reviews in this field and our research work, in this review, we have summarized the designing methods, properties, photocatalytic applications, and the mechanisms of carbon-based 2D/2D hybrid material with special reference to g-C3N4/graphene nanostructured hybrids. The architectural assembly of well-connected g-C3N4/graphene nanostructured hybrid materials exhibit superior activities due to the extended light absorption and improved transportation of charge across the interface. We have categorically discussed the multifunctional role of graphene as a functional material, as a conductor, as a solid-state mediator, and as a co-catalyst in g-C3N4/graphene nanostructured hybrids. We hope that this review will offer new insights into lucid design strategies for efficient photocatalysts to address energy and environmental problems in a sustainable manner.

1. Introduction

Heterogeneous semiconductor photocatalysis has received significant awareness due to its potential of solving energy crisis and environmental pollution using solar energy.1 In response to the worldwide energy demand due to fast growth and increase in population, it has become most important to generate an environmentally clean, sustainable, and cost-effective source of energy. Generally, coal, oil, and natural gases (fossil and fuels) remain the major sources of energy as they are easily accessible and effective.2 Moreover, the high energy of combustion of hydrogen (122 kJ mol1), compared to that of gasoline or any other fossil fuel, and environment-friendly nature with no secondary byproducts makes it a suitable alternative source of energy in the near future.13 Traditional methods of hydrogen production involve the formation of a large amount of CO2, resulting in environmental pollution. Since the last few decades, designing a suitable, highly efficient semiconductor photocatalyst for water reduction has been a great challenge. In this context, the utilization of solar energy to produce hydrogen energy photocatalytically by avoiding the formation of any greenhouse gases is emerging as one of the promising techniques. Henceforth, a good number of photoactive semiconducting materials have been explored and their hydrogen production potential as well as environmental remediation activity have also been evaluated.4,5 Heterogeneous semiconductor-based photocatalytic water redox reactions have been found to be a facile and proficient method and are promising alternatives for the generation of energy and the environment.

Among all, 2D semiconducting materials have fascinated researchers since the last few decades. 2-Dimensional nanomaterials correspond to a class of promising photocatalysts that have layered structures and a width of single or many atoms.6 In the search for highly proficient semiconductor-based photocatalysts, metal-free semiconductor photocatalysts such as g-C3N4 (graphitic carbon nitride) have drawn much more interest in the research community due to the simplistic designing method, unique electronic structure, high thermal stability, and abundant nature.79 G-C3N4 is an emerging artificial semiconductor photocatalyst and is easily prepared using various easily obtainable nitrogenous starting materials such as melamine, urea, thiourea, cyanamide, and di-cyanamide. The 2D-layered structure of g-C3N4 composed of a linear polymer, which is interconnected with a tri-s-triazine ring via secondary nitrogen, was first discovered by Berzelius and its name was assigned by Liebig.10,11 Wang et al. first discovered metal-free polymeric g-C3N4 toward H2 evolution reaction in 2009.10 In 2004, the discovery of another 2D material consisting of single-sheet graphene also has fascinated the research community due to its excellent thermal, electrical, and mechanical properties. New approaches toward the structural design of g-C3N4 and graphene at the nanoscale level tend to introduce tailored electronic properties and extend their applications as visible light-induced photocatalysts. In particular, elevated specific surface area with more number of reactive sites and high charge mobility endow these 2D/2D hybrid materials with remarkably high performance.1013 In addition, the design of 2D/2D nanocomposites is favorable for the catalytic stability and the separation of photogenerated charge carriers. Currently, 2D/2D heterostructures are more beneficial because of their unique band alignments, high charge transfer behavior, and superior photocatalytic activity. In this direction, our group has also worked extensively on various 2D materials such as LDH, graphene, carbon nitride, boron nitrides, and metal dichalcogenides for application in energy and environment.1428

In the past few years, some excellent reviews have also been published on 2D nanoheterostructures, which have focused on the design strategies, modifications, tunable band positions, the role of an interface, computational screening, and photocatalytic applications in various fields.2933 However, a very few number of reviews has been published on g-C3N4 and graphene nanohybrid involving the multifunctional roles of graphene in photocatalysis. Therefore, we have tried to systematically review the design strategies of g-C3N4 and graphene-based nanohybrids in the field of photocatalysts, highlighting the latest advancement in the field of energy and environment. The well-built in-plane chemical bonds along with weak van der Waals interactions make these materials beneficial for surface-related applications. The state-of-the-art for the design and modification of g-C3N4/graphene-based hybrid materials are discussed in detail along with the influence of electronic, electrical, structural, and surface manipulation strategies, giving special emphasis on the diverse roles of graphene. 2. Importance of 2-dimensional carbonaceous materials in photocatalysis

Among various 2-dimensional nanomaterials, carbon-based nanomaterials such as graphitic C3N4, graphene, CNT (carbon nanotube), and modified 2D carbon frameworks have come out as prospective photocatalysts due to their unique characteristics such as quantum confinement effect, efficient visible light absorption, tunable energy levels, superior charge transport, high surface area, easy processability, and high quantum yield.3437 Carbonaceous semiconducting materials are considered as gifted nanomaterials to convert solar energy into chemical energy. Moreover, carbonaceous materials are plentifully available and involve easily accessible methods for modification at the nanoscale level. The design of various carbon-based functionalized hybrid nanomaterials has also been reported by incorporating carbon-based species into/onto g-C3N4 2D nanosheets with improved electronic properties, electrical conductivity, and synergistic functionalities to realize excellent photocatalytic performance. The induced surface functionality readily improves the interaction through covalent linkage, stability, processability, biocompatibility, and physical adsorption onto the surface of the hybrid nanomaterial. Strong in-plane covalent bond and van der Waals interaction within the planes results in the formation of heterojunctions for the easy channelization of electrons.3841 In recent research studies, new design strategies have been introduced to engineer the band structure by calculating the number of layers and their interfaces in multi-layered g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrid heterostructures to increase charge transfer between the adjacent layers.42,43 The electronic structure and density of electrons at the Fermi level of these materials play a very important job in the process of adsorption of the reactant molecules on the surface.

3. g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids in photocatalysis

Graphene, among all carbonaceous nanomaterials, has appeared to be a miraculous material in the field of material science. Graphene is known to be made up of a mono-atomic layer of graphite having conjugated sp2 hybridized carbon networks arranged in a hexagonal manner. It has been used in various fields such as solar cells, batteries, fuel cells, photocatalysts, and supercapacitors. It has a wide range of applicability due to its surprisingly high surface area (2630 m2 g1), high mechanical strength (1060 GPa), excellent electron mobility on its surface (200000 cm2 V1 s1), high thermal conductivity (3000 W m1 K1), and favorable chemical properties. A defect-free 2D single layer is the most appealing due to speedy charge transport and a more conductive surface to facilitate photocatalytic reactions.44,45 The insertion of graphene as a conductive carbon support favors the charge transferability and tends to enhance the number of active reaction sites to carry out photocatalytic reactions. However, as graphene lacks reactive edges and light absorption, it may be functionalized or modified to make it suitable for reactant diffusion and dispersion of other semiconducting materials on its surface.4648 In 1859, at Oxford University, Benjamin C. Brodie, for the first time, synthesized GO, and in 1898, L. Staudenmaier attempted it again. Later on, Hummers et al. reported a modified technique for the preparation of GO from graphite, potassium chlorate, and nitric acid. Most commonly GO is formed by the oxidation of natural graphite with strong oxidizing agents such as (KMnO4 and NaNO3) in an acidic medium to insert a good number of oxygenated functional groups. The extent of oxidation also introduces several surface defects on GO, which act as centers of recombination for photogenerated charges and would decrease the photocatalytic activity. Moreover, the presence of a large number of functional groups destructs the conjugation in the graphitic structure and decreases its conductivity, and makes it an insulator. To optimize its photocatalytic properties various research groups reported mild oxidation methods for the formation of GO by introducing a fewer number of functional groups, maintaining its conjugated structure and conductivity. The method of conversion and structure of graphene to reduced graphene oxide is schematically represented in Fig. 2(a). As another emerging carbonaceous material, highly stable crystalline g-C3N4 possesses a lamellar structure, known to have numerous excellent electronic, optical, and physiochemical properties. Recently it has emerged as a potential candidate for numerous photocatalytic applications. With a suitable bandgap of 2.7 eV and appropriate band edge potential, it could undergo photoredox reactions, making use of more energy from solar radiation. However, bulk g-C3N4 due to the low surface area, inadequate light absorption, and quick recombination tendency shows low quantum yield. Graphene, owing to its extraordinary surface area and porosity, is able to overcome the disadvantages of g-C3N4 to some extent, and provides many active sites for photocatalytic reactions. Taking into consideration the most serious limitations of g-C3N4, a number of g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids has been reported by different research groups. The introduction of rGO into the g-C3N4 network has the tendency to (1) widen the light response of g-C3N4 and improve the absorption intensity; (2) favor charge transferability due to excellent conductivity and by the formation of hydrogen bond, and (3) make available more number of active sites for photocatalytic reactions. Owing to the well-organized geometrical interaction between two-layered structures, graphene, and g-C3N4, the fabrication of g-C3N4/graphene hybrids can be easily realized through electrostatic or stacking intermolecular interaction. The 2D/2D hybrid nanostructure between graphene and g-C3N4 has been most commonly created by electrostatic self-assembly of positively charged surfaces of protonated g-C3N4 and electronegative RGO. Theoretical hybrid functional calculations further established the structural and electronic properties of g-C3N4/graphene based nanohybrids successfully.45,48 The geometry and electronic structure of g-C3N4 and graphene and of the g-C3N4/graphene hybrid confirms the presence of type interaction at the interface.49Fig. 2(b) and (c) represent the optimized geometry of monolayer g-C3N4 and graphene, and Fig. 2(d) and (e) represent the corresponding band structure calculated by computational method. The concentration of oxygen in rGO is found to be a vital parameter that affects the electrical property of rGO. It helps in the dispersion in polar solvents and allows the formation of nanocomposites with g-C3N4. The incorporation of rGO into the g-C3N4 network plays an important role in the photocatalytic performance due to certain voltage loss during the electron transfer process resulting from the energy difference between the CB of g-C3N4 and rGO. Moreover, the presence of oxygen rich sites in rGO has the capacity to form crosslinked COC bonds. The presence of these COC bonds formed due to strong stacking interaction results in positive shifting VB edge potential of the g-C3N4/rGO nanohybrid.36 Moreover, the shorter spatial distance of the COC bonds further lowered the local energy barrier for intermolecular charge carrier transfer through the cohesive interface. With tunable oxygen concentration, a staggered band alignment was achieved in the nanohybrid.

Several studies have showed that it is conceivable to enhance the amount of charge carriers on the g-C3N4 photocatalysts by inducing comparable sp2 -conjugated carbon structures for modifying the electronic characteristics from the customized band structure. Coupling between two -conjugated carbon structures favor interfacial charge transport, resulting in band bending in the rGO/g-C3N4 nanohybrids. Fermi levels have the ability to alter the space charge areas at the interfacial contact surface, as shown in Fig. 2(c). During the band bending process, free photocarrier aggregation might occur along the interface between rGO and g-C3N4. The intrinsic architecture of g-C3N4 is altered by these triggered internal electric fields, resulting in the development of direct Z-scheme (Fig. 2(k)) and type II (Fig. 2(l)) heterojunctions.

The design process mostly involves the modification of the g-C3N4 structure for the development of reverse charge on its surface so that it can readily assemble with the RGO sheets. For the synthesis of g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids, various synthetic methods such as thermal annealing, solvothermal method, photoassisted electrostatic assembly, and chemical impregnation are used. However, most common design processes involve chemical impregnation, followed by calcination, involving nitrogen-rich precursors of g-C3N4 (cyanamide, dicyandiamide, melamine, urea, ethylenediamine) and graphene oxide.49,50 The mixture of melamine and GO was reduced by hydrazine monohydrate with constant stirring and then calcined at 823 K. Depending on the graphene content, the surface area and light absorption capacity of the hybrid varies.51 Depending on the uniqueness of graphene, it may act as a support material, may introduce a functionality, may act as a conductor to increase charge separation, or as a solid-state electron mediator in a ternary heterostructure through Z-scheme mechanism, or in the formation of 0-D quantum dots, it may act as a co-catalyst in case of various g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids (Fig. 3).

3.1 Graphene as a support material in g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids

The unique structural features of GO can notably enhance the photocatalytic activity of g-C3N4 by extending the -conjugation, which helps in the mobility of photogenerated electrons, promotes the charge separation on the surface of g-C3N4, and increases the light absorption in the visible region. Owing to the presence of surface functional groups, single or even a few layers of GO sheets exceptionally load other nanomaterials onto the high surface area and open up new opportunities in photo/electrocatalysis. Furthermore, the reduction of GO (sp3 carbon bond) to RGO (sp2 carbon bond) makes a more efficient charge transfer and offers a conductive 2D carbon network. The reduction of GO mainly us focused on the loss of oxygen functional groups to form RGO, which acts as an exceptional conductive support, which helps in efficient exciton separation and charge transport.

Li et al. reported the designing of a g-C3N4/RGO nanohybrid through a cross-linked approach, as shown in Fig. 4(a).52 The electronic band structure of the nanohybrid can be controlled by varying the cyanamide to the graphene oxide weight ratio during synthesis. Compared to other carbonaceous materials graphene possesses an exceptionally high surface area that improves interfacial contact between other incorporated materials. Moreover, the defective sites present on the surface of graphene-based nanohybrids work as nucleation centers to incorporate other semiconducting nanoparticles. Specifically, reduced graphene oxide (RGO), having a negatively charged surface, attracts other materials onto the surface to get adsorbed. Due to this attractive force, the incorporated nanoparticles get distributed and prevent agglomeration. Also, the surface can be modified with various surfactants to improve the adsorption efficiency and photocatalytic activity.

3.2 Graphene as a functional material in g-C3N4/graphene-based heterojunctions

Although graphene is known to have zero bandgaps, the functionalization of graphene sheets by oxidation results in the formation of graphene oxide (GO). The formation of graphene oxide leads to a change in the state of hybridization from sp2 to sp3 and an increase in the electronegativity of the oxygen atoms. Due to this, GO sheets behave as p-type semiconducting materials and develop a bandgap. The bandgap of GO mainly depends upon the oxidation level and can be easily tuned by controlling the extent of oxidation.54 Also, owing to the presence of oxygen-containing functional groups on the GO surface, other semiconducting materials or their precursors get deposited on GO sheets more readily than that on graphene. This would enable the enhancement in the stability as well as the dispersion of the incorporated material. Most commonly, GO is formed by the oxidation of natural graphite in the presence of strong oxidizing agents (KMnO4 and NaNO3) in an acidic medium. Due to the introduction of a good number of oxygenated functional groups on the edge and basal planes of GO, nanosheets of about 1 nm thickness are formed with good dispersibility. The excess surface defects upon oxidation also act as centers of recombination for photogenerated charges and would decrease the photocatalytic performance. Thus, upon high-temperature treatment with NH3, these defects on the surface of the GO sheets can be repaired by removing some of the carboxyl and epoxy groups and by the insertion of various nitrogen functionalities, i.e., pyridinic, pyrrolic, and quaternary groups. Furthermore, the bandgap of GO can be decreased due to the delocalization of electrons through the overlapping of -orbitals of N and C atoms; also, it results in a slight upward shift in the position of HOMO.32 Thus, designing processes that mostly involve the modification of the g-C3N4 structure for the development of reverse charge on its surface, so that it can readily assemble with the RGO sheets is important. Though GO shows superb adaptability for coupling with g-C3N4, the presence of an optimum number of functional groups through strong interactions and the residual defects will significantly reduce the electrical conductivity by lowering the charge carrier separation and finally degrade the photocatalytic performance.32 Hence, it is highly essential to optimize GO sheets through reduction to have reduced graphene sheets (RGO), which can couple with 2D g-C3N4 easily to extend the photocatalytic performance. Generally, a variety of reducing agents such as NaBH4, hydrazine monohydrate, hydroiodic acid, vitamin C, and hydroquinone have been used to synthesize RGO through the chemical reduction strategy. However, due to hydrophobic property and strong staking, RGO sheets get easily aggregated. To overcome this drawback and to improve the dispersibility, methods such as pH control or chemical modification may be adopted.

Recently, a good number of attractive and intriguing nanohybrids of g-C3N4 with GO and RGO were established as the most exceptional metal-free visible light-induced photocatalysts.55 It has been established that by calculating the RGO weight ratio, the oxidation power of g-C3N4/RGO nanohybrid was found to increase by a negative shift in the VB potential. Moreover, it has also been reported that the formation of a heterojunction between the n-type g-C3N4 and p-type RGO can easily modulate the band structure by varying the doping amount of RGO. Yang et al. investigated a g-C3N4/RGO nanohybrid, which involves a combination of g-C3N4 with doped graphene as the conductive component that synergistically assists in proton adsorption and HER activity.56 Yang explained the creation of a larger number of surface-active sites for HER after the introduction of g-C3N4 on doped graphene due to good chemical coupling and electronic effect. Furthermore, the method of introduction of g-C3N4 through the sonication of the mixture of dicyandiamide and B-doped graphene (BG), upon evaporation, lyophilization, and polymerization, was found to strengthen the coupling effect and successfully adjust the surface chemical and electronic properties of the nanohybrid. The porous layered structure of g-C3N4/B-doped graphene with available defects works as dynamic catalytic centers for hydrogen adsorption and enhanced HER activity.56 As reported by Zheng et al., the incorporation of phosphorus and nitrogen atoms as dopants was found to stimulate the carbon atoms in graphene, facilitate the adsorption of a proton, and reduce to show excellent HER reactivity.57 However, excellent catalytic activities were reported using boron atom as a p-type dopant, in comparison to nitrogen atom as an n-type dopant, on the crystal lattice of graphene.56 This improved activity was mainly accredited to the lower electronegativity value and positively polarized B-atom compared to that of C-atom, due to which boron attracts negatively polarized oxygen atoms more readily, leading to the activation of OO cleavage.

Xu et al. explained for the first time the importance and role of oxygen in g-C3N4/RGO-based nanohybrids.55 g-C3N4 and RGO interfacial contact was also systematically investigated using density functional theory studies of the nanohybrid. It was observed that in comparison to isolated mono layered g-C3N4, the bandgap energy of the nanohybrid was found to decrease at higher oxygen concentration. Fascinatingly, an optimum oxygen concentration even modifies the direct-band gap of the nanohybrid to the indirect-band gap. In the case of the g-C3N4/RGO-based nanohybrid, a type-II, staggered band alignment was obtained at higher oxygen concentration. O atoms on the surface of RGO, being negatively charged, became active sites for adsorption to carry out photoredox reactions. From the results of the hybrid functional calculations, it was observed that the interfacial interaction is largely dependent on the oxygen atom concentration. Furthermore, it was also observed that by controlling the concentration of oxygen, the band structure can also be readily adjusted. The optimum concentration of oxygen will significantly increase the ability of photocatalytic water redox reaction over the g-C3N4/RGO nanohybrid.

Mou et al. designed g-C3N4/NGQDs (nitrogen-doped graphene quantum dots) nanohybrid to investigate the visible light-induced H2 evolution activity.58 During nitrogen doping, a number of surface amino groups are introduced in NGQDs, which can provide reaction sites for binding with g-C3N4 by the deamination reaction. During the preparation process by the thermal condensation of urea and NGQDs, lone pair of electrons on NGQDs get attached covalently through the NC bonds of tri-s-triazine units, resulting in the p network, as shown in the TEM image (Fig. 6(ad)).

However, after coupling with the NGQDs (1.66 wt%), the absorption edge of the NGQDs/g-C3N4 nanohybrid is red-shifted and the bandgap decreases to 2.55 eV in the case of NGQDs/g-C3N4. The reduction in the bandgap is due to the extension in conjugation in the 2D plane (Fig. 6(e) and (f)). Mou et al. investigated visible light-induced H2 evolution rates over NGQDs(x)/g-C3N4 nanohybrid (0.5 mg mL1) for 3 hours of irradiation ( 420 nm) (Fig. 6(g)). Compared to neat g-C3N4, the optimized NGQDs ratio (1.66 wt%) shows an enhanced H2 evolution rate of 139.6 mol h1, which is about 7.7 times more than that of the neat g-C3N4 (18.2 mol h1).58 The higher H2 evolution rate in NGQDs(1.66)/g-C3N4 nanohybrid explains better charge separation due to the generation of new bands by NGQDs into the g-C3N4 network. Moreover, wavelength-dependent H2 production was also investigated and suggested a higher rate at 520 15 nm due to more new bands. The intensity of PL emission peaks of the as-synthesized samples and at different excitation wavelengths further confirm maximum quenching in the case of NGQDs (1.66)/g-C3N4 nanohybrid. The PL emission peaks are due to interband electronic transitions caused by the surface defects. This decreased PL intensity suggests efficient charge separation due to integration with the NGQDs in the g-C3N4 lattice, which is favorable due to the presence of the p network and the electron-withdrawing nature of the N atoms within the conjugated system.

This deamination coupling among NGQDs and the g-C3N4 matrix is capable of generating new energy levels within the bandgap through a unique p link, which decreases the bandgap and thus strengthens the absorption of light radiation. The photogenerated electrons of NGQDs/g-C3N4 not only gets transferred to the CB of g-C3N4 but also infuses directly into the newly created energy levels, as shown in Fig. 6(h). Moreover, the presence of unique p connections significantly decreases the charge transfer distance, considerably increasing the charge separation and the photocatalytic performance.

3.3 Graphene as a conductive material in the g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids

The design of g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids is of great significance as they improve the conductivity as well as the photocatalytic performance. By the introduction of graphene into the g-C3N4 network, the transfer of electron is facilitated as the graphene sheets serve the purpose of electron-conducting channels. The presence of -conjugation and oxygen-containing functional groups on GO simultaneously makes it sensitive for light absorption and favors the photocatalytic performance due to its strong hydrophilic nature. By incorporating oxygen-containing functional groups, the degree of oxidation varies and GO could change its behavior from a conductor to an insulator depending on the C/O ratio. Thus, optimum functionalization could facilitate surface modification and make it suitable to design hybrids with other semiconducting materials. The most commonly used thermal annealing method for the design of g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids usually results in hybrids with a low specific surface area due to the aggregation of the 2D-sheets of graphene and g-C3N4. To overcome this discrepancy, Xiang et al. reported a nanocasting method for the design of sandwich-like g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids.59 The synthesis procedure involves the preparation of GO-based silica nanosheets by the hydrolysis of tetraethylortho-silicate and the cationic surfactant. The TEM image (Fig. 7(a) and (b)) of the nanohybrid confirms a layered and more compact structure than that of graphite as g-C3N4 got sandwiched between the graphene sheets during the calcination process. All the g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids are found to have higher specific surface areas, which increase with an increase in the graphene amount. Moreover, optical absorption is also found to increase in the case of the g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids as the color gradually changes from yellow to gray with the addition of graphene, as in Fig. 7(c). The series of g-C3N4/graphene nanohybrids designed by Xiang et al. were investigated for photocatalytic H2-evolution activity in the presence of Pt as the cocatalyst (Fig. 7(d)).59 After the formation of the nanohybrid, g-C3N4 gets anchored onto the graphene surface and results in a layered photocatalytic material. Graphene sheets behave as conductive channels to improve charge carrier separation and thus enhance H2 production activity with good stability (Fig. 7(e)). Upon irradiation with visible light, electrons (e) get excited from the VB (N 2p orbital) to the CB (C 2p orbital) of g-C3N4, leaving behind holes in the VB. A fraction of electrons that moved onto the surface of Pt nanoparticles get accumulated and effectively carry out water reduction reaction to produce H2. However, in the presence of graphene sheets, these photogenerated electrons get transferred to graphene sheets from the CB of g-C3N4 due to excellent electronic conductivity, leading to effective charge separation. A novel hybrid g-C3N4/RGO (CN-G) photocatalyst was prepared by Ai et al. through in situ thermal condensations of a mixture of melamine with GO.61 Graphene was found to behave as a promising promoter as well as conductor for the photooxidation of phenol and MB degradation over g-C3N4. After hybridization, GO and g-C3N4 formed closer interfacial interactions, resulting in a compactly-packed porous structure. The removal rate was about 95% on CN-G-0.1 and CN-G-0.2 and 100% on the CN-G-0.4 and CN-G-0.6 samples. It was observed that both the adsorption of MB and the absorption of light were found to increase with an increase in the graphene content, and thus, the photocatalytic activity was also enhanced.61 CN-G-0.4 was further used to study the photodegradation of phenol. It has been observed that about 60% of phenol was removed in 210 min in the presence of 0.2 g L1 of catalyst dosage. With an increase in the catalyst loading to 0.5 and 1.5 g L1, phenol degradation was about 75% and 87%, respectively. The apparent reaction rate constants with different amounts of catalyst loading (0.2, 0.5, and 1.5 g L1) were found to be 4.59 103, 6.35 103, and 8.71 103 min1, respectively. With an increase in the catalyst loading, more photogenerated active sites were obtained and the introduction of graphene could appreciably improve the optical absorption, thus increasing the photooxidation activity. The CN/graphene nanohybrids facilitated the transfer of photoelectrons from the VB of g-C3N4 to the CB and is then channelized to the graphene sheet, which results in a decrease in the rate of charge recombination. Moreover, the extra electron, which was transferred into the sp2 hybridized carbon network, maybe inhibited at the Lewis basic sites of rGO but get transferred to the water and oxygen molecules from the basal plane and the defective sites of graphene to form active radicals responsible for MB degradation and oxidation of phenol.

Hafeez et al. investigated visible light-induced H2 evolution over the RGO-supported g-C3N4/TiO2 ternary nanohybrid.62 The photocatalyst was prepared by the ultrasonication-assisted simple wet impregnation method by varying the g-C3N4 to TiO2 weight ratio. The incorporation of RGO and TiO2 results in a red-shift in the absorption edge from 455 nm to 505 nm. Thus, the results increased the photoabsorption and improved the photocatalytic performance. It was found that a mass ratio of 70:30 in the case of g-C3N4 and TiO2 in the ternary nanohybrid exhibits maximum H2 evolution rate (23143 mol g1 h1) through water reduction in the presence of glycerolwater solution as the sacrificial agent,62 which was about 78 times and 2.5 times more than that of neat g-C3N4 (296 mol g1 h1) and TiO2 (11954 mol g1 h1), respectively. Effective H2 evolution was further supported by reduced PL intensity, confirming the formation of a pn heterojunction and faster transfer of photoelectrons through the interface. Further quenching of the PL intensity by loading RGO reveals faster electron mobility due to low resistance offered by RGO and the effective separation of charge carriers. Hafeez and group also carried out H2 evolution activity over the ternary nanohybrid by taking different sacrificial agents (methanol, TEA), which shows 13978 mol g1 h1 and 8510 mol g1 h1, respectively. To comprehend the formation mechanism, Hafeez carried out the preparation using two different methods. In the first method, g-C3N4 was added to a mixture of RGO and TiO2 as g-C3N4TiO2/rGO (CTR). In another way, he prepared the nanohybrid by adding TiO2 to a mixture of g-C3N4 and RGO after ultrasonication as TiO2g-C3N4/rGO (TCR). From the rate of H2 evolution, it was found that the CTR sample shows better hydrogen evolution compared to TCR, which may be due to the ease of transfer of electrons through the interface formed between TiO2 and RGO through direct contact. However, the role of MoS2 in favoring the charge transfer activity of g-C3N4/reduced graphene nanohybrid was investigated by Yuan et al.63 Designing a well-defined 2-dimensional ternary nanoheterojunction offers an intimate contact interface, provides a short diffusion path, and superior charge transportation toward enhanced H2 evolution. The g-C3N4/RGO/MoS2 photocatalyst was designed by the pyrolysis method to couple g-C3N4 and RGO in an argon atmosphere. GO gets reduced on the g-C3N4 surface prepared formed from urea and gets attached to the surface. Subsequently, through hydrothermal treatment, a solution of g-C3N4/RGO nanohybrid and (NH4)2MoS4 in dimethylformamide results in the formation of the g-C3N4/RGO/MoS2 ternary nanoheterojunction through the crystallization of MoS2. As reported by Yuan et al., the efficient H2 evolution activity of the g-C3N4/RGO/MoS2 ternary nanohybrid was chiefly accredited to the combined effect of RGO and MoS2, where MoS2 acts as a charge transfer path and favors charge separation. However, an optimum amount of MoS2 is helpful for efficient photocatalytic activity. Due to the presence of black-colored RGO or MoS2, light absorption is shielded and the photocatalytic activity decreases. H2 evolution by g-C3N4/RGO with 0.5 wt% RGO was found to be 48 mol g1 h1 because of accelerated charge transfer rate.63 Although the presence of a small content of MoS2 (0.4%) noticeably increased the rate of H2 to 138 mol g1 h1, the optimum amount of MoS2 was found to be 1.2%, which shows the maximum rate of H2 evolution (317 mol g1 h1) with an apparent quantum yield of 3.4%. Moreover, the catalyst was found to be photostable and shows a steady rate of H2 evolution during the next four successive runs with H2 evolution found to be 317, 314, and 313 mol g1 h1, respectively.63 Xu et al. in another study reported the effect of doping Co-atom in the MoS2 lattice. Due to the incorporation of unsaturated atoms in the MoS2/g-C3N4 nanohybrid, the number of active sites increases.64 MoS2 lacks a sufficient number of active sites due to the presence of coordinatively unsaturated atoms in MoS2 that restrict its performance. However, in the CoMoS2/rGO/g-C3N4 nanohybrid, the synergistic effect of RGO and Co-doped MoS2 nanosheets is supposed to increase the conductivity and charge separation. MoS2 usually behaves like a good electrocatalyst due to the less number of available active sites and its photocatalytic performance is quite low. However, by designing the heterojunction, its interface conductivity can be increased and the photocatalytic activity can also be enhanced. Xu et al. designed a ternary heterojunction by introducing RGO in CoMoS2/g-C3N4 by the solvothermal method, in which the high conductivity of RGO plays a vital role in the electron transfer process and Co-doping in MoS2 increases the number of active sites in the MoS2 nanosheets. The preparation process involves Co(NO3)2 and (NH4)2MoS4 solution as the starting materials to design Co-doped MoS2 due to the formation of well-built 3d interactions connecting Co2+ and MoS42. During hydrothermal treatment, RGO gets attached to of the g-C3N4 surface through strong interaction. As reported by Xu et al., XPS analysis shows the atomic ratio of Mo6+/Mo4+ in the case of the MoS2/RGO/g-C3N4 nanohybrid, which is found to increase from 0.74 to 0.98 by Co-doping in the case of CoMoS2/RGO/g-C3N4. An increase in the Mo6+/Mo4+ atomic ratio confirms the creation of more defect levels through Co-doping. The photocatalytic H2 evolution rate under visible light over neat g-C3N4 nanosheets showed 11 mol g1 h1, which was almost negligible. Upon loading 0.5 wt% RGO, the rate of H2 evolution was found to increase to 18 mol g1 h1. However, by incorporating 2 wt% MoS2 as the cocatalyst, the rate increased to 279 mol g1 h1 and in the case of the CoS/rGO/g-C3N4 ternary heterostructure, it increases to 400 mol g1 h1. By incorporating Co-doped MoS2, the H2 evolution rate was significantly increased to 684 mol g1 h1 along with excellent stability, which was solely dependent on the atomic ratio of Co/Mo.64 The enhanced photocatalytic performance (about 30%) due to RGO loading is mainly accredited to the function of RGO as an electron mediator, which greatly influences the transfer of charge carriers. The insertion of transition metal (Co) in MoS2 offers an effective technique to modulate the geometry and increase the active sites through bonding with coordinatively unsaturated atoms. Thus, Co doping by introducing more defects in the crystal structure further enhances the H2 evolution activity through catalytic activity. Therefore, the enhanced photocatalytic activity was due to the synergistic effect of efficient light absorption, superior interfacial charge transfer, and good adsorption over the defect sites.65,66

3.4 Graphene as an electron mediator in the g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrid

In the race of designing artificial Z-scheme systems to provide an efficient mechanism for charge separation, a combination of two semiconductor photocatalysts with strong redox ability is highly essential to carry out several photocatalytic reactions through the Z-scheme charge transfer process, which involves the combination of electrons from the CB of one semiconductor system with the holes present in the VB of another semiconductor. Thus, this helps in decreasing the recombination tendency of the photogenerated charge carriers and increasing the efficiency. The whole process requires an electron mediator to offer sites for charge recombination and channelization. Therefore, the selection and development of a suitable mediator is a big challenge before the researchers. Moreover, the low diffusion rate of the ions in the liquid state restricts the transfer of charge carriers. To overcome such drawbacks, insoluble solid-state electron mediators are reported to solve the purpose best by acting as a shuttle between the two photosystems. Taking this into account, the major requirement of a solid-state electron mediator is high conductivity, less energy barrier, minimum interfacial resistance, shielding effect, and high stability. GO acts as an efficient solid-state electron mediator. Among a good number of studies involving GO as an electron mediator in g-C3N4 based systems, some of the recent studies are discussed below.

Manchala and group designed a smart g-C3N4/graphene junction-based ternary photocatalyst by inserting ZnIn2S4. ZnIn2S4 (ZIS) is a chalcogenide (AB2X4) photocatalytic material that has drawn significant interest due to its high chemical stability, high surface area, and good absorption with suitably narrow bandgap energy.67 In addition, by developing a ternary hybrid photocatalytic system, the recombination of photogenerated charge carriers can be successfully suppressed at the interface. Manchala et al. synthesized a graphene-based ZnIn2S4/g-C3N4 (ZIS-CN-G) smart architecture through a one-step simple hydrothermal route that showed excellent H2 evolution under visible light, i.e., 477 mol h1 g1. They also have synthesized various binary nanocomposites using neat ZnIn2S4, graphene, and g-C3N4 (CN) materials, i.e., g-C3N4/graphene (CN-G), ZnIn2S4/g-C3N4 (ZIS/CN), and ZnIn2S4/graphene (ZIS/G) to investigate their hydrogen evolution performance. The FE-SEM image of neat ZIS shows marigold flower-like microspheres and in the CN-G nanohybrid, tiny 2D nanoflakes of CN were found to be evenly dispersed on the graphene surface. In the case of the ZIS-CN-G nanohybrid also (Fig. 9(af)), the ZIS flower and CN nanoflakes were well distributed on the surface of the 2D graphene sheets. The results of nitrogen adsorptiondesorption isotherms display higher surface area of the ZIS-CN-G nanohybrid (189 m2 g1) than that of ZIS-CN (79 m2 g1) and ZIS-G (127 m2 g1), which can make more surface active sites available for adsorption. The rate of H2 evolution over the synthesized photocatalysts was also dependent on the irradiation time during 4 h of solar light irradiation along with good stability and reusability. The rate of H2 evolution in the case of the ZIS-CN-G nanohybrid is 477 mol h1 g1, which is much more than that of the corresponding neat counterparts such as neat CN (6.1 mol h1 g1), neat ZIS (139 mol h1 g1), CN-G (192 mol h1 g1), ZIS-CN (184 mol h1 g1), and ZIS-G (400 mol h1 g1) (Fig. 9(g)). The ZIS-CN-G nanohybrid illustrated 78.2, 3.4, 2.5, 2.6, and 1.2 times increased rate in comparison to the corresponding binary nanohybrids.67 This enhanced photocatalytic performance of the ZIS-CN-G nanohybrid is ascribed to the trapping and transport of photoelectrons immediately due to graphene, which is confirmed from the photocurrent data. During the photocatalytic reaction, photoexcitation results in the transfer of electrons to the CB, leaving behind holes in the VB of ZIS and CN in the presence of sacrificial agents (Na2S and Na2SO4). Because of the difference in the band potentials and proper band alignment, the electrons get transferred from the CB of CN to the CB of ZIS through a heterojunction mechanism and reduce H+ to H2. The graphene sheets not only trap the electrons but also act as a transport medium in the ZIS-CN-G smart nanojunction architecture, thus promoting charge separation, as shown in Fig. 9(h).

A ternary CdS/RGO/g-C3N4 nanohybrid was fabricated by Jo et al. through the hydrothermal method. CdS-Integrated RGO and exfoliated nanosheets of g-C3N4 were found to show efficient H2 evolution and atrazine (an herbicide) degradation under visible light irradiation.69 The enhanced photocatalytic performance was mainly attributed to improved charge separation because of RGO as an electron mediator, which accelerates the charge transfer process at the CdS/g-C3N4 interface, following a Z-scheme charge transfer pathway. Moreover, the greater surface area of the exfoliated nanosheets of g-C3N4 and reduced distance for charge transfer offer superior redox ability. Thangavel et al. reported the g-C3N4/RGO/Fe2O3 nanohybrid by distributing the RGO/Fe2O3 coreshell structures on the g-C3N4 surface via the ultrasonic aided hydrothermal method and simple wet impregnation.70 From the FESEM and TEM images, a laminar sheet-like morphology of g-C3N4 and Fe2O3 uniformly coated with RGO were confirmed. In our group, Nayak et al. synthesized the NiFe LDH/N-RGO (nitrogen-doped GO)/g-C3N4 heterostructure, which shows superior photocatalytic performance toward the mineralization of RhB and phenol, along with good H2 (2508 mol g1 2 h1) and O2 evolution (1280 mol g1 2 h1) under visible light.71 In addition, the photocatalytic mechanism involved a mediator-based Z-scheme charge transfer. NiFe LDH enriched with defects through oxygen vacancy and the role of N-RGO as an electron mediator follows a Z-scheme charge transfer mechanism, which was further supported by electrochemical studies. 3.5 Graphene as a co-catalyst in g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids

In recent times, carbon quantum dots (CDs) and graphene quantum dots (GQDs) have fascinated researchers much owing to their exceptional nature and a broad range of applications in the field of photo/electrocatalysis and bioimaging.72,73,79 Several studies have been reported for the successful design of high conductive N and/or S doped GQDs showing good electrocatalytic activity and have found applications in lithium-ion batteries.74 Graphene quantum dots (GQDs), having a lateral diameter less than 100 nm, usually consist of single or few-layers graphene, which possesses a size-dependent quantum confinement effect as well as special optical and electronic properties. Thus, GQDs in a photocatalytic system behaves as an excellent light absorber and good transporter of electrons to improve the photocatalytic hydrogen evolution. Taking advantage of these properties, many research groups have studied their applications in the field of photocatalysis and considered g-C3N4/GQDs nanohybrids as an effective photocatalyst for hydrogen evolution. However, the inherent hydrophobicity of GQDs does not result in strong electronic coupling with g-C3N4. Due to this, doped GQDs (GQDs) were preferred over normal GQDs by many scientists because of their efficient light absorption and strong coupling to favor electron transfer. Zou et al. reported a nitrogen-doped GQDs (NGQDs) induced excitation independent upconversion PL property and significantly improved light absorption of even longer wavelength than that of GQDs.75 Nitrogen doped-graphene quantum dots/g-C3N4 nanohybrid was fabricated using g-C3N4 obtained from different precursors and GQDs prepared hydrothermally to study visible light-induced hydrogen evolution. Graphene quantum dots have drawn much interest in photocatalysis and bio-imaging owing to their unique properties. In particular, nitrogen-doped graphene quantum dots possess exceptional PL properties and show extended absorption in the visible region. Because of these properties, GQDs behave as good co-catalysts in photocatalytic hydrogen evolution. The increased photocatalytic activity was attributed to a significant red shift in the absorption edge, quantum confinement effect of GQDs, and good electronic coupling between N-doped GQDs and g-C3N4.

4. Conclusion

In this review, various g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrid photocatalysts are overviewed thoroughly with particular weightage on in-plane or inter-plane interactions, synergistic properties, and enhanced photocatalytic activity. Remarkable intrinsic properties of two polymeric, commercially available, and environment friendly carbon-based nanomaterials are found essential to design green, sustainable, and efficient photosystems for energy and environment. The unique structural and electronic properties of some representative g-C3N4/graphene-based nanohybrids were systematically discussed considering the various roles of rGO. Most importantly, it was concluded that strong electronic coupling between g-C3N4 and graphene due to functionality or flexibility in the structures, effective light absorption, compatible band edge potential along with shorter diffusion pathways excitingly increases the interfacial charge transfer, which were found to be the most important to extend their potential applications. Although a good number of studies with encouraging results are available, still, due to limited compatibility and recyclability, many more challenges exist in using g-C3N4/rGO-based nanohybrids in photocatalysis and require more attention to extend their commercial applications in many more challenges such as

1) Although various design strategies involving interface coupling,59 cross linking,52etc., for fabricating g-C3N4/rGO-based nanohybrids have been established due to the local in-plane -conjugation,60 the major limitation is the strong dependency on their composition and controlled oxidation process to reduce the defective sites. During the formation of a composite (functionalized graphene and g-C3N4), the stacking issue of two 2D materials is also a concern. Thus, major advancement in optimized synthesis techniques must be required to design high-performance g-C3N4/rGO-based nanohybrids. For large-scale preparation, the optimization of numerous reaction conditions such as pH, temperature, and proportion of starting materials is very essential. Moreover, the defect sites introduced during functionalization are crucial to obtain an efficient photoactive material.

2) Even if the first principles study and DFT calculations to some extent revealed the presence of strong electronic coupling at the interface,45 it is also highly essential to carry out an in-depth study of the conjugated domains and functional groups to establish interaction mechanism between g-C3N4, rGO, and other photoactive materials and the actual photocatalytic mechanism.

3) The thermal and chemical stability of the g-C3N4/rGO-based nanohybrids is also important to extend their practical application. As it has been observed that the percentage of rGO changes the semiconducting character of g-C3N4,52 it is essential to tune the semiconducting character as required for a particular photocatalytic reaction. The possible synergistic effects in the nanohybrids, the electrical conductivity of the hybrid materials, could also be further improved by adopting optimal reaction conditions.

4) As the intrinsic reactivity of these materials relies on synergistic electron transfer process, the role of unpaired electrons in the photocatalytic mechanism should be given more attention both theoretically and experimentally with the help of localized probing technique. The intrinsic reactivity of the nanohybrids can be maximized by tuning the edge defects suitably.

5) As a low-cost metal free sustainable photoactive material, g-C3N4/rGO-based nanohybrids are very important for practical benefits and can be used to substitute hazardous metal-based materials. Despite great advances, the practical applications are still a long-term challenge to design a realistic device for energy and environmental applications.
