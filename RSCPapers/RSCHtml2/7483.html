End-capped group manipulation of non-fullerene acceptors for efficient organic photovoltaic solar cells: a DFT study†

Shahnaz Ahmed and Dhruba Jyoti Kalita *
Department of Chemistry, Gauhati University, Guwahati-781014, India. E-mail: dhrubajyoti.kalita@gauhati.ac.in

Received 17th July 2020 , Accepted 24th September 2020

First published on 25th September 2020

Abstract

A series of acceptors, S1–S5, has been designed based on the acceptor–π–donor–π–acceptor (A–π–D–π–A) architecture by incorporating a phenothiazine unit as the central donor unit. Density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) methods have been employed to study the effect of various end-capped groups on the geometric, electronic, optical and charge transport properties of the designed acceptor molecules. The results reveal that on increasing the electron-withdrawing nature of the end-capped groups, the performance of the acceptor molecules increases. It is also observed that on increasing the flexibility of the end-capped groups, the planarity of the molecules gets destroyed and, as a result, the performance of the acceptor molecules decreases. The investigated molecules exhibit high electron affinity (EA) and low reorganization energy for electrons (λ−), indicating the electron acceptor nature of the designed molecules. The absorption properties of the molecules manifest that compounds S2–S4 possess high values of the maximum wavelength (λmax) of absorption. We have also studied the properties of a D/A active layer by considering PffBT4T-2OD as the electron donor and arranging PffBT4T-2OD/S1–S5 molecules in a face to face manner. Properties of the D/A blend indicate that molecules S2–S4 have capacity to promote charge carrier separation at the D/A active layer. Our results provide guidelines for further designing of acceptors to enhance the performance of organic solar cells (OSCs).

1 Introduction

The bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) organic solar cells (OSCs) have attracted great attention as an environment-friendly technology for transforming solar energy into electrical energy. Compared to the inorganic materials, organic materials used in BHJ type organic solar cells have advantages of mechanical flexibility, easy synthesizability, light weight and low cost of production.1–7 BHJ devices consist of electron rich donor and electron deficient acceptor molecules. Earlier, fullerene derivatives such as PC71BM ([6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester), PC61BM ([6,6]-phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester), ICBA (indene-C60 bisadduct) etc. were most commonly used due to their high electron affinity and great electron transporting capacity.8–10

Over the past few decades, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of fullerene-based OSCs has undergone an increase of up to 11.7%.8,11 Besides their high PCE, fullerene derivatives still possess some disadvantages such as their wide band gap, which results in weak absorption in the visible and near infrared (NIR) regions of the solar spectrum, thermal and photochemical instability, high synthesizing cost, difficulty in the purification process, limited utility in high voltage sub-cells of tandem devices etc.12,13 To overcome these issues, non-fullerene small molecule acceptors have been rapidly developed in the last ten years. Non-fullerene small molecule acceptors have tunable structures, appropriate energy levels, a broad range of absorption spectra, and low cost and easy methods of synthesis and purification.2,14–17

To obtain efficient OSCs, it is important to design high performance small molecule acceptors and to explore them with suitable donor materials. It was already reported that the electrical, structural and optical properties of both the donor and acceptor materials play a crucial role in determining the photovoltaic performance of devices.11,18 The PCE of single-junction OSCs has increased above 12% to date.19 To improve the PCE of OSCs, the design and synthesis of small molecule acceptors is beneficial. Presently, an A–π–D–π–A type of small molecule acceptor in which an electron-rich donor unit (D) is linked to two electron-deficient acceptor (A) groups via two π-bridges is one of the most hopeful molecule design strategies.20–22

The conversion of light energy to electrical energy in OSCs involves a series of steps. In the first step, a bound electron–hole pair (exciton) is generated via absorption of a photon by the donor material. The second step is the exciton diffusion step where the exciton formed diffuses to the donor–acceptor interface. In the third step, the exciton gets dissociated and the bound electron is transferred to the acceptor at the interface. In the final step, the free charges (i.e., well-separated electron and hole) are transported and collected at the respective electrodes.10,23

The structural properties, electronic energy levels, absorption properties, carrier mobility, solubility parameters etc. can be easily tuned by modifying the three parts, D, π and the A of the acceptor materials.21,22 The popular donor units used include fluorene, carbazole, indacenodithiophene etc. have a rigid coplanar structure.20,24,25 At the same time, malononitrile, 3-ethylrhodanine, dicyano-n-hexylrhodanine etc. and their derivatives have been used as the end-capped groups in the A–π–D–π–A type small acceptors.26–28 Suitable modification of the end-capped groups can lead to successful and efficient tuning of the absorption and energy levels, which in turn helps in the achievement of high performance of the OSCs.29

Herein, we have designed five A–π–D–π–A type small acceptor molecules, S1–S5, by choosing phenothiazine as the central donor (D) unit. Benzo-triazole, thiophene and ethylene groups have been used to extend the π-conjugation.30,31 3-methylrhodanine, 3-methyl thiazolidine-2,4-dithione, 1,1-dicyano-methylene-3-indanone, 1,1-dicyano-methylene-5,6-difluoro-3-indanone and 1-cyano-4-methyl-thiophene have been used as the end group acceptors. The sketches of the designed molecules (S1–S5) are provided in Fig. 1. In this work, our goal is twofold. Firstly, we try to tune the structure of small molecule acceptors so that the solar energy absorption by the OSCs is increased. Secondly, we further investigate the role of small molecule acceptors in the charge transfer process at the donor/acceptor (D/A) interface. To fulfill this purpose, the geometric structures, electrical properties, optical properties and charge transfer processes have been studied using quantum chemical methods. Moreover, taking PffBT4T-2OD as the donor and S1–S5 as acceptors, the electron transport processes have been investigated.28

2 Computational details

All the calculations have been carried out using the Gaussian 09 program package.32 The ground-state geometries of all the compounds S1–S5 have been optimized by employing the density functional theory (DFT) method. The coordinates of the optimized structures are provided in Table S1 (in the ESI†). The optimized structures have been confirmed to be at minimum-energy points by vibrational analysis. To validate the functionals used in our calculations, we have performed test calculations with the compound BAF-4CN reported in the literature as it has structural similarity with our designed compounds.20 The ground state calculations have been performed using the DFT method with the functionals B3LYP, PBEPBE, HSEH1PBE, MPW1PW91 and CAM-B3LYP and the 6-311G(d,p) basis set.20,33–35 The excited state calculations have been performed with the time dependent-DFT (TD-DFT) method using the functionals CAM-B3LYP, ωB97XD and M06-2X-D3 and the 6-311G(d,p) basis set.20,36 The respective results are reported in Table S2 (in the ESI†). From Table S2 (ESI†), it was observed that for the ground state calculations, the results obtained using the HSEH1PBE functional are in agreement with the experimentally reported values. On the other hand, for the excited state calculations, the results obtained using the CAM-B3LYP and M06-2X-D3 functionals are in better agreement with the experimentally reported values. However, to keep the computational cost under control, we have performed the excited state calculations at the CAM-B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level of theory. Solvent phase calculations have also been carried out using the conductor-like polarizable continuum model (CPCM) using chloroform as the solvent.37 Cationic state optimization, and calculation of neutral and cationic energy, ionization potential (IP), electron affinity (EA), and reorganization energy (λ) values have been performed at the same level of theory. Electronic coupling matrix element (V) values have been calculated at the M06-2X-D3/6-31G(d,p) level as it includes the dispersion correction and can be used to study the charge transfer properties.20 However, the calculation of exciton binding energy (Eb) and Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) has been performed at the HSEH1PBE/6-31G(d,p) level of theory.

3 Theoretical methodology

The performance of a photovoltaic device is dependent on the stability of holes and electrons and the energy barrier of the charge injection process. Many studies have reported the effect of the energies of the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO), IP and EA of the molecules on the charge injection and transportation properties. Herein, we have calculated both the IP and EA in two different ways: (i) vertical (denoted as v) and (ii) adiabatic (denoted as a). The vertical and adiabatic IP and EA can be calculated using the following equations:33,34,38
 
IP(v) = E+(M0) − E0(M0),(1)
 
IP(a) = E+(M+) − E0(M0),(2)
 
EA(v) = E0(M0) − E−(M0),(3)
 
EA(a) = E0(M0) − E−(M−),(4)
where E+, E− and E0 are the energies of the molecules in the cationic, anionic and neutral states, respectively, and M+, M− and M0 represent the cationic, anionic and neutral geometries of the molecules, respectively.

The reorganization energy (λ) usually has two contributions, the inner sphere and the outer sphere. The outer sphere reorganization energy arises from the relaxation of nuclei/electrons or polarization of the surrounding medium. The inner sphere reorganization energy arises from the geometry relaxation process when a charge is released or accepted by the molecule.33,34 In this work, we have taken into account only the inner sphere contribution.

The reorganization energy values for holes (λ+) and electrons (λ−) are calculated using eqn (5) and (6):33,39–44

4.1.4 Bond length alteration (BLA) parameter (Δr). To establish a relationship between geometric structure and electronic properties, we have calculated the bond length alteration (BLA) parameter (Δr) values of the compounds. The Δr value is related to the transport properties, linear–nonlinear polarizabilities, and photon absorption characteristics of organic molecules. BLA can be defined as the difference between the average C–C double and C–C single bonds of conjugated systems.6,34 Lower values of Δr indicate the presence of a greater extent of conjugation in the compounds. The calculated values of Δr are provided in Fig. 3. From this figure, it was observed that compounds S3 and S1 possess the lowest and highest values of Δr, respectively. This indicates that the extent of conjugation present is maximum and minimum in compounds S3 and S1, respectively.

4.2 Electronic properties

4.2.1 Frontier molecular orbitals and density of states. The energies of the HOMO and LUMO and their difference (ΔH–L values) are important parameters that markedly affect the electronic properties, carrier transport properties and optical absorption. The energies of the HOMO and LUMO and ΔH–L of the studied compounds are reported in Table 4. From this table, it was observed that ΔH–L values of the studied compounds are found to be in the range from 1.50 eV to 2.10 eV, which lies within the reported values (1–4 eV) of ΔH–L required for OSCs.34 This indicates that our compounds are suitable for OSCs. The observed ΔH–L values follow the order S5 > S1 > S2 > S3 > S4. This trend can be explained with the observed trend in the LUMO energy levels of the compounds. The LUMO energy levels of the compounds follow the order S5 > S1 > S2 > S3 > S4. The LUMO energy of a molecule depends on the strength of the attached electron-withdrawing units in the molecule. Among the acceptor units, the electron-withdrawing capacity is maximum for compounds S3 and S4 due to the attached carbonyl and dicyano groups, respectively. Owing to this, these two molecules possess lower LUMO energy values. Furthermore, the acceptor unit of the S4 molecule has been substituted with fluorine, which further increases the electron-withdrawing capacity of the end-capped unit. Therefore, it is obvious that the ΔH–L value decreases with the decrease in the LUMO energy level. The ΔH–L values in the solvent phase follow the same trend as in the gas phase. The density of states (DOS) can be defined as the number of different states at a particular energy level available for electrons. The partial density of states (PDOS) signifies the relative contribution of a particular orbital or atom to the total DOS.52–55 For a comparative study of electronic properties with the frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs), we have calculated the partial density of states (PDOS) of the studied compounds and the same is plotted in Fig. 6. The plots of frontier molecular orbitals of the studied compounds along with the contribution of the end-capped groups to the HOMO/LUMO are presented in Fig. 5. From this figure, it was observed that replacement of the oxygen atom of the end group of compound S1 with the less electron-withdrawing sulphur atom in S2 increases the contribution of the end group to both the HOMO and LUMO. Similarly, the presence of an electron-withdrawing F atom in the end group of compound S4 increases its contribution toward the LUMO (compared to S3) but decreases that toward the HOMO (compared to S3). On the other hand, attachment of 1-cyano-4-methyl-thiophene as the end group in compound S5 decreases its contribution towards both the HOMO and LUMO among the studied compounds. From the above discussion, it is clear that except compound S5, in all the studied compounds (S1, S2, S3 and S4), the end groups offer a significant contribution to their LUMO. Thus, we can conclude that except for compound S5, the studied compounds are suitable for use as acceptor molecules in OSCs.

5 Properties of the D/A active layers

5.1 Exciton binding energy

The working mechanism of the OSCs is such that the acceptor molecule absorbs sunlight to produce the exciton, which splits only after reaching the D/A interface. However, the splitting of the exciton into free charged particles at the D/A interface must overcome the exciton binding energy (Eb).20,58Eb can be described as Eb = ΔH–L − E1, where E1 is the optical band gap, i.e. the energy required for the S0 → S1 transition. We have provided the respective values of ΔH–L, E1 and Eb in Table 11.

Parameters (eV) S1 S2 S3 S4 S5

E 1 1.624 1.414 1.366 1.313 1.743

ΔG −1.176 −1.417 −3.594 −1.737 −1.006

It is already reported that the Eb values for organic semiconductors vary from 0.2 eV to 1 eV.59 However, it is also reported that a lower value of Eb facilitates dissociation of excitons into free charges.60 From Table 11, it was observed that for all compounds, the Eb values are in the reported range. Moreover, it was observed that compared to compounds S1 and S5, S2–S4 have lower Eb values. This implies that exciton dissociation is more favourable in compounds S2–S4.

Upon illumination of light of a suitable wavelength, the ground state electron of the donor gets excited. Transfer of this excited electron to the acceptor takes place spontaneously when the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) becomes negative. The electron–hole pair gets dissociated into free charges at the D/A interface and they spread into the acceptor. Simultaneously, the electron and hole should be collected by the respective electrodes. The change in Gibbs free energy (ΔG) can be calculated from the Rehm–Weller equation as follows:58

 
ΔG = IP(D) − EA(A) − E1 − Eb(10)
where IP(D) represents the ionization potential of the donor and EA(A) represents the electron affinity of the acceptor, which are considered to be the negative of the HOMO and LUMO energies of the donor and acceptor, respectively. E1 is the first excitation energy of the donor.58 The calculated ΔG values of the studied compounds are reported in Table 11. From Table 11, it was observed that for all the designed acceptors, the obtained ΔG values are negative. This implies that the excited electron of the donor can spontaneously transfer to the acceptor molecule. However, compared to the acceptors S1 and S5, S2–S4 have lower ΔG values, which indicates that exciton dissociation is more favourable in compounds S2–S4.

6 Conclusions

In this work, we have designed five new A–π–D–π–A type conjugated molecules, S1–S5, to explore more efficient acceptor materials for organic solar cells by incorporating phenothiazine as the central donor unit and changing the end-capped acceptor units. To illustrate the structural and electronic properties, we have calculated the dihedral angles, inter unit bond length, distortion energy, BLA parameters, the HOMO and LUMO energies, ΔH–L values, absorption properties etc. We have also estimated the properties of D/A blends after combining the designed acceptor molecules with the reference donor PffBT4T-2OD to monitor the electron injection process from the donor to the acceptor molecules.

To envisage the effect of the end-capped acceptor unit in an A–π–D–π–A type acceptor molecule, we have modified the acceptor unit of the molecule with various electron-withdrawing groups. We have also analyzed the effect of fluorination on the end-capped group. We have observed that with an increase in electron-withdrawing effect of the end-capped groups, dihedral angle, Δr and ΔH–L values get decreased. From the study of absorption properties, it was observed that compounds S2–S4 possess high λmax values. We have also studied the effect of polar and non-polar solvents on the absorption properties of the molecules and observed that λmax values get red shifted in the solvent phase. It was also observed that when increasing the flexibility of the end-capped groups, the performance of the molecules decreased. This was observed in the case of compound S5. Owing to the presence of higher flexibility of the end-capped group, this molecule exhibits the maximum ΔH–L and distortion energy and the lowest λmax value. This indicates that S5 is not a good option for using as an acceptor in OSCs.

From the properties of the PffBT4T-2OD/S1–S5 blends, it was observed that compounds S2–S4 have the ability to promote charge carrier separation at the D/A interface. Thus, it can be concluded that compounds S2–S4 can be used as promising acceptors to improve the efficiency of OSCs. Moreover, it can be concluded that by modifiying the end-capped groups with strong electron-withdrawing units, the optoelectronic properties of the acceptor molecules can be better tuned.
