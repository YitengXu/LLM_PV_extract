The photo-, electro- and photoelectro-catalytic properties and application prospects of porous coordinate polymers

Abstract

Since the discovery of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), covalent–organic frameworks (COFs) and zeolite–imidazole frameworks (ZIFs), many of their outstanding properties have been explored such as their large specific surface area, significant gas adsorption, and high catalytic activity. Some kinds of porous coordination polymers (PCPs) and their derivatives have shown high photocatalytic or electrocatalytic activity; thus, they may be able to catalyze the degeneration of organic molecules, the production of hydrogen and oxygen, carbon dioxide reduction, and so on. This review mainly focuses on the electrocatalytic, photocatalytic and photoelectrocatalytic properties of PCPs including MOFs, ZIFs and COFs. Several experiments have been reported in this article to compare their catalyst properties. Moreover, the reasons why they show these differences have been analyzed. These PCPs can be applied for tackling environmental pollution in water, carbon dioxide reduction and the production of hydrogen, which can help solve the energy crisis, the greenhouse effect and facilitate the utilization of solar energy.

1. Introduction

Nowadays, industrialization as well as population growth have led to a large demand for fossil fuels, which brings with it many problems such as energy shortage and the greenhouse effect.1 In fact, the energy crisis and global warming are among the most important problems.2 Ever since these problems arose, many efforts have been made to try to solve them. Photocatalysts as well as electrocatalysts are two kinds of materials that can help solve these problems because they can catalyse many reactions such as hydrogen evolution, carbon dioxide reduction and the degradation of organic contaminants.3

For electrocatalysis, many studies have proposed some ideas to achieve efficient energy conversion. However, most of these designs are based on two-electrode systems, in which the anode part involves some chemical fuel oxidation or oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and the cathode relates to the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) or hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). However, practical application of these systems has not been achieved due to the usually slow rate of oxygen evolution. In the past, electrocatalytic OER, HER and ORR have been extensively studied. Many kinds of catalysts have been designed for these reactions. However, most catalysts have the disadvantage of being unstable or expensive; this makes them not viable commercially.4 Moreover, for photocatalysis, PCPs offer a unique opportunity to integrate various molecular components to achieve light capture and promote photocatalytic CO2 reduction and hydrogen evolution. Sunlight can activate the photocatalytic reaction to drive reactions that cannot be performed under normal conditions. Photocatalysis is considered a very important synthesis method for the construction of C–C bonds, but its use is affected by the fact that most organic substrates lack photon absorption in the solar spectrum.5 In particular, there is a lack of research concerning photoelectrocatalysis. Therefore, it is necessary to continue such research. Thus, how to find efficient photocatalysts and electrocatalysts remains a great challenge. TiO2 is regarded as a kind of photocatalyst and Ir/Pt is a kind of electrocatalyst. Actually, TiO2 is used as a commercial photocatalyst, denoted as P-25. Attempts have been made to optimize the structure of TiO2,6 but their prohibitive cost and low efficiency currently restrict their development and application.1,7 Hence, it is crucial to find new types of catalysts.

Recently, work has focused on metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), covalent–organic frameworks (COFs) and zeolite–imidazole frameworks (ZIFs) due to their large specific area, high pore volume and design structures.1 Because of these properties, they are considered in various applications, such as gas absorbers, catalysts and chemical sensors. The metal clusters and organic linkers of these frameworks can be controlled to change their properties.8 MOFs usually show considerable catalytic activity. For instance, MOF-48 as well as MIL-47 can catalyze the oxidation reaction of methane.9 They can be used as photocatalysts and electrocatalysts.10 Sometimes they may be combined with other catalysts, which enlarge the catalytic activity of these complexes.8 Herein, we review the photocatalysis, electrocatalysis and photoelectrocatalysis of MOFs, ZIFs and COFs. The history of these frameworks in catalysis is introduced and some experiments and reports are mentioned. Finally, we analyze the outlooks and future perspectives in this area.

2. Methods of determining the catalytic properties

2.1 Determination of the photocatalytic properties

In general, the determination of the photocatalytic properties of MOFs includes the use of MB or RhB as a substrate and a gas, such as CO2, as a substrate. The test methods corresponding to these tests are as follows, respectively.
2.1.1 The degradation of organic pollutants. In general, RhB and MB can simulate organic contaminants in water to determine the catalytic effect of MOFs on the decomposition of organic contaminants in wastewater. MB or RhB can be dissolved in an aqueous solution and used as a reaction solution. Since the reaction is an oxidation reaction, the solution should be agitated in the dark for a period of time to dissolve a certain amount of oxygen in the solution and to balance the dissolved/released oxygen in the solution. The solution is then irradiated (commonly using a Xe lamp). Then, the catalyst to be tested is added. Since most MOFs are insoluble in water, the system should be kept in suspension during the reaction by continuous stirring. The remaining concentration of RhB or MB after a certain period of time reflects the catalytic efficiency of the catalyst. The concentration of RhB or MB is reflected by the intensity of absorption at the wavelength of maximum absorption in the UV-visible spectrum. Typically, RhB absorbs at 554 nm and MB at 664 nm.11,12
2.1.2 Heavy metal ion pollution treatment. Unlike MB and RhB, Cr(VI) simulates heavy metal ion contaminants in water. Heavy metal ions in water can also cause environmental pollution. Reducing the valence of Cr(VI) by a reduction reaction converts it to an hydroxide to form a precipitate, thereby removing heavy metal ions from the water. The method utilized in this type of experiment is roughly the same as that previously described with RhB or MB, except the experiment was conducted in a nitrogen atmosphere to prevent oxidation. A hole scavenger (ammonium oxalate) may also be added before stirring. The concentration of Cr(VI) is reflected by the intensity of absorption at the wavelength of maximum absorption in the UV-visible spectrum, which is usually taken as 540 nm.13
2.1.3 HER, OER and ORR. In recent years, research in HER, OER and ORR has become a hot spot for researchers. Some MOFs have photocatalytic activity for these three reactions. Since these three reactions involve the generation, collection and measurement of gases, the test apparatus for these reactions is different from the above. In addition, the amount of generated gas is measured by gas chromatography.14

2.2 Determination of the electrocatalytic properties

In the case of the reaction of a precipitating gas, such as HER and OER, generally, the corresponding MOF and its derivatives can be attached to the electrode substrate. The electrocatalytic performance can be tested by linear sweep voltammetry (LSV). Generally, the lower the initial potential, the greater the current, and the better the electrocatalytic properties.15 For some substances, redox reactions, such as hydrogen peroxide oxidation, can be tested by cyclic voltammetry (CV) using an electrochemical workstation. Here, if a change in the concentration of the substance causes a significant change in the current peak at the electrode where the MOF is present, then the MOF or its derivatives are prove to have good electrocatalytic properties.16

3. Catalytic properties of MOFs

3.1 History of MOFs as catalysts

According to a previous report, good photocatalysts should have certain aspects: (1) suitable topological structures, with a large enough porous volume and channels connecting the pores, (2) redox-active metallic nodes to allow charge separation, and (3) photoresponsive structures that can capture light.17 In fact, many surveys and research studies have been completed concerning the catalytic properties of MOFs, and lots of discoveries have been reported.18 Two key advantages allow MOFs to be used as photocatalysts: first, the high pore volume enables them to enhance the reaction speed, second, their optical absorption characteristics can be adjusted via designing their coordination.19 Modification of their organic linkers and metal ions leads to a variety of electronic structures, which makes it possible to control the chemical and physical properties of MOFs.20 For example, it is possible to increase the band gap of UiO-66, UiO-67 and UiO-68 (UiO = University of Oslo)21via adding more benzene repeating units, which makes their band gap between 2.9 and 4.1 eV.22 The rigidity of the utilized organic linkers enabled carboxylate clusters to form 3D structures.8 In 2007, Alvaro and coworkers studied the degradation of phenol under light in the presence of MOF-5.23 One year later, Cavka et al. managed to prepare UiO-66, with the chemical formula [Zr6O4(OH)4(CO2)12],24 which was later transformed into a photocatalyst. From then on, many kinds of frameworks were studied by researchers. MIL-101(Fe), MIL-53(Fe) and MIL-88B(Fe) (MIL = Materials Institute Lavoisier)21 were studied, and their photocatalytic activity demonstrated.25 Song et al. pointed out that only if MOFs have stability and can catalyze reactions under natural environment can they be used as catalysts.26 Besides, many kinds of MOFs have valuable optical properties, and they can be used as luminescent materials, visible light photocatalysts and optoelectronic elements.27

3.2 Photocatalytic properties of MOFs

Photocatalysis research is beneficial to take advantage of solar energy and to control the density of chemical reaction sites and chemical selectivity.28 MOFs were first defined by Yaghi et al.29 The large specific surface area as well as orderly structure of MOFs enable them to be used as a new type of photocatalyst. MOFs, through modulation of their organic linkers and metal clusters, in combination with other metal complex catalysts, can adsorb a reactant and accelerate the reactive activity and charge separation efficiency, which enables them to have excellent photocatalytic properties. Generally speaking, there are three key steps related to photocatalysis: (1) light capture, (2) charge separation and transfer, and (3) reaction, which is triggered by the charge.30 Thus, it is important to promote light capture and charge separation. Besides, as for organic semiconductors, once they are activated by light, electrons will jump from the highest occupied molecular orbitals to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals. MOFs also follow this rule. However, the inefficiency of separation between the poles and electrons in MOFs limits their application. Hence it is important to improve their photocatalytic activity. Combining MOFs with other catalysts is one solution to help solve this problem. MOFs work as the absorber and absorb gases or organic molecular, while the other catalysts catalyze the reaction.8 From these angles, many kinds of photocatalysts have been prepared.

Wastewater treatment has been related to environmental problems. The organic compounds in sewage can cause eutrophication, which leads to water pollution. However, many MOF-based photocatalysts can catalyze the degradation of organic compounds. Generally, methylene blue (MB), Rhodamine B (RhB) and many other dyes are used as simulated pollutants, because they are difficult to be decomposed under the irradiation of ultraviolet light and can thus be used as representatives of the pollutants in wastewater.31 They can thus be used to test the catalytic activity of those photocatalysts.32,33 Besides, X3B is also difficult to be decomposed under ultraviolet light, which means it can also be used as a simulated pollutant.34 A lot of experiments have been done in this field.

Wang et al. prepared a kind of MOF via heating a mixture of Bi(NO3)3, KOH, 2-mercaptonicotinic acid (H2mna) and DMF, which was donated as Bi-mna. It could be activated by visible light. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) showed that photogenerated electrons could easily jump to the surface of Bi-mna, which enabled a considerable efficiency for the separation between poles and electrons (Fig. 1a). The photocatalytic activity of Bi-mna for the degradation of RhB was also tested. By contrast, a-Bi2O3 was also tested. The results are shown in Fig. 1b. Clearly, Bi-mna is superior to a-Bi2O3 as a photocatalyst, and it also showed good photocatalytic properties.32 Meng et al. synthesized a MOF, CuI3(L1)2(L2)[PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2]·2H2O, in which L1 = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene and L2 = 1,2,4,5-tetra(4-pyridyl)-benzene. Its photocatalytic activity was examined in a solution of RhB under the irradiation of ultraviolet light. The results showed that 50% of the RhB was decomposed in the presence of the photocatalyst in 6 h, though this catalytic activity may be a little lower than other catalysts.35 Kozlova et al. studied the photocatalytic activity of nine MOFs, namely UiO-66, UiO-66-NH2, UiO-66-NO2, MIL-100(Al), MIL-100(V), MIL-100(Fe), MIL-100(Cr), MIL-125(Ti) and MIL-47(V). Their ability to catalyze the decomposition of MB under ultraviolet light was studied. All of them exhibited photocatalytic activity for this reaction except MIL-100(Al) and MIL-47(V). UiO-66-NH2 showed the highest catalytic activity among these compounds. In the presence of UiO-66-NH2, the reaction rate reached 1.05 × 10−4 min−1. MIL-100(V) and MIL-100(Fe) also exhibit considerable activity, with reaction rates being 0.86 × 10−4 min−1 and 0.67 × 10−4 min−1.36

Not only should the organic pollutants in wastewater be treated, but also the heavy metal ions too. Cr(VI) is harmful to the ecological environment due to its acute toxicity, mutagenicity and carcinogenesis.42,43 Hence it is crucial to find an efficient catalyst that can catalyze the reduction of Cr(VI). Wang et al. used TiO2 as a photocatalyst for the reduction of Cr(VI) under ultraviolet light.42 Liang et al. reported MIL-53(Fe) as a photocatalyst for the reduction of Cr(VI). It was non-toxic, inexpensive and stable. Its photocatalytic activity was studied with respect to N-doped TiO2 under visible light (Fig. 4a). Clearly, the photocatalytic activity of MIL-53(Fe) was higher than N-doped TiO2, and indeed almost all the Cr(VI) was reduced in 40 min. In addition, ammonium oxalate and N2 were both beneficial to this reaction, and they showed a synergistic effect (Fig. 4b). Besides, the mixture of RhB could also be treated by MIL-53(Fe) (Fig. 4c and d). MIL-53(Fe) showed excellent stability in the four sets of cycling experiments. About 60% of the Cr(VI) was reduced and 80% of the RhB was decomposed in the mixed system. So MIL-53(Fe) was demonstrated to be a good candidate for wastewater treatment.13 In addition, In-benzenedicarboxylate (MIL-68(In)) and In-2-NH2-benzenedicarboxylate (MIL-68(In)-NH2) were also reported to have photocatalytic activities for the reduction of Cr(VI), and so these were tested. MIL-68(In) had excellent thermal stability and high photocatalytic activity.44 MIL-68(In) was synthesized via mixing 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid and In(NO3)3·xH2O under stirring and heating the mixture,45 while MIL-68(In)-NH2 was synthesized via heating a mixture of 2-aminoterephthalic acid, In(NO3)3·xH2O and DMF.46 The results are shown in Fig. 5a. It can be seen that MIL-68(In) showed no photocatalytic activity for this reaction. However, over 90% of the Cr(VI) was reduced in the presence of MIL-68(In)-NH2. Besides, the optimal pH value was 2, which meant that this reaction should be carried out under acidic conditions. Finally, the stability of MIL-68(In)-NH2 was also studied by carrying out three sets of cycling experiments (Fig. 5b), and MIL-68(In)-NH2 was proven to be able to be recycled for at least 3 times.47

HER, OER and ORR, which are related to energy production and translation, have attracted the attention of many researchers. In order to make the best of solar energy, one of the methods is to store the energy in a covalent form in hydrogen.17 Some MOFs are able to selectively regulate HER.48 Many kinds of MOFs can catalyze these reactions so they may be able to be applied to hydrogen and oxygen production. Many MOFs that can catalyze these reactions have indeed been reported. Al is the most abundant metal in the earth's crust, and aluminium carboxylates show excellent chemical and thermal stability,49,50 which means that Al is adequate and useful for many applications. Two kinds of Al-based MOFs were synthesized by An et al., which are termed as Al–TA with the organic linker terephthalic acid and Al–ATA, with the linker 2-amineterephthalic acid. They have special photocatalysis and photoelectric effects, and can catalyze OER. A mixture of terephthalic acid, Al(NO3)3·9H2O and DMF was stirred and heated to gain Al–TA. The procedure to prepare Al–ATA was similar to that for Al–TA, but the terephthalic acid was replaced by 2-amineterephthalic acid. CoPi (cobalt phosphate) is also an abundant compound in the earth's crust and it exhibits catalytic activity for water oxidation.51 It can be combined with Al–ATA and Al–TA to obtain CoPi–Al–ATA and CoPi–Al–TA. Their photocatalytic activity was tested (Fig. 6). It was clear that the production of oxygen was improved a lot via the modification with CoPi. That is, CoPi could improve the catalytic activity of Al–ATA and Al–TA.52 MIL-101 can catalyze OER. Chi et al. studied its photocatalytic activity for OER under visible light with respect to MIL-101-NH2. The results are shown in Fig. 7. About 36.50 μmol O2 was generated in the presence of MIL-101 in 10 min. The optimal pH value was 10.53

MOFs can work directly as photocatalysts. Wu and coworkers reported a kind of copper-based MOF, {[CuII2(DCTP)2]NO3·1.5DMF}n, denoted as 1. It could also catalyze the degeneration of MB. As a result, 70% of MB was degraded in the presence of 1 but without visible light. When both of them co-exist, more than 80% of MB was decomposed.54 Kan et al. studied four kinds of MOFs, namely [Ag7(4,4′-tmbpt)(HL)2(L)(H2O)] (denoted as 2), [NaCd3(4,4′-tmbpt)(L)2(OH)]·H2O (denoted as 3), [Cd3(3,3′-tmbpt)2(L)2-(H2O)2]·3.5H2O (denoted as 4) and [Cd3(3,4′-tmbpt)2(L)2(H2O)]·1.5H2O (denoted as 5). They could also accelerate the degradation of MB under ultraviolet light. According to the report, 3 had the lowest photocatalytic activity among these MOFs, due to its coordination of ions, amount of water molecules and volume of Cd clusters. In fact, there are three-core units in 5, which easily allow electron–hole pairs into the surface of the material, which is beneficial for photocatalysis, while 4 has two-core units. Then a comparative experiment was done to compare the catalytic activity of 2, 3 and 5 (Fig. 8). According to the data, with the existence of 2, 3 and 5, the decomposition rates of MB reached 54%, 65% and 73%, respectively in 1.5 h, while the rate was 24% without catalyst. Hence, the photocatalytic activities of these MOFs were proved.55 Two kinds of Anderson-type polyoxometalate (POM)-based MOFs, namely H{Cu2(μ2-OH)2L3[CrMo6(OH)6O18]}n·4H2O, and {Cu2L4[CrMoVI5MoV(OH)6O18](H2O)4}·4H2O (L3 = N,N′-bis(3-pyridinecarboxamide)-piperazine, where L4 = N,N′-bis(4-pyridinecarboxamide)-piperazine), were reported, which could catalyze the degeneration of MB under the irradiation of ultraviolet light, visible light and sunlight. According to the experiment results, the decomposition ratios of MB in the presence of the two MOFs under the irradiation of ultraviolet light were 94.3% and 82.1%, respectively. For visible light and sunlight, the decomposition ratios were 88.4%, 82.2% and 87.4%, 83.4%, respectively.56 Sun et al. reported several kinds of POM-MOFs, including three-dimensional (3D) [Cu(H2tda)(H2O)2]4[SiW12O40]·12H2O (6), one-dimensional (1D) [Cu2(H2tda)2(H2O)4][SiW12O40]·(TMA)2·3H2O (7) and zero-dimensional (0D) [Cu(H2tda)(H2O)3][SiW12O40]·(TMA)3·H2O (8). Their photocatalytic activity for the degradation of RhB under the irradiation of ultraviolet light was studied and their preparation strategy provided. Compound 6 was synthesized via heating a mixture of H3tda (1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid), CuCl2·2H2O, H4SiW12O40 and distilled water, while 7 was obtained by heating a mixture of H4SiW12O40, CuCl2·2H2O, H3tda, tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAH) and distilled water at 100 °C. The synthetic strategy of 8 was similar to that of 7, except for heating the mixture at 70 °C. Compound 6 had a 3D structure, and each structural unit possessed a SiW12 cluster, four H2tda organic linkers and four Cu ions. Different with 6, 7 and 8 had 1D and 0D structures, respectively. Their photocatalytic activity was examined. According to the results (Fig. 9), the conversion rate of RhB reached 90% in the presence of 6, while it was less than 50% in the presence of 8. This phenomenon showed that photocatalytic activity is related to the structure of the photocatalysts. In general, the higher the dimension the photocatalysts have, the higher the efficiency is.57 Two kinds of MOFs whose photocatalytic activity was reflected by the degradation of X3B under visible light were reported: [Ni2(bimb)3(H2O)6·(aobtc)·(DMF)2·(H2O)2]n (9) and [Cd(3,3′,4,4′-bptcH2)(H2O)·(bimb)]n (10) (bimb = 4,4′-bis(1-imidazolyl)biphenyl, 3,3′,4,4′-bptcH4 = 3,3′,4,4′-biphenyltetra-carboxylate acid-aobtcH4 = azoxybenzene-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic acid). According to the results, 10 exhibited a higher photocatalytic activity than 9. Their stability was also proved to be excellent.58

Many reports also provide mechanisms for the photocatalytic reactions catalyzed by MOFs. MOF-74 has been studied for years. In the presence of MOF-74(Mg)-Ti or MOF-74(Zn)-Ti, about 98% of MB was decomposed in 3 min. Besides, their photocatalytic activity was higher than TiO2, which indicated that the combination of MOFs and Ti leads to a higher photocatalytic activity. The mechanism of this reaction was also illustrated (Fig. 11b), whereby once the electrons are excited, they are transferred form the organic linker to Ti4+ and form Ti3+. Thus, the organic linker is oxidized and changes into benzoquinone, which is stable. However, MB can deoxidize the benzoquinone, so MB is decomposed. Then, Ti3+ is oxidized by the oxygen in the air. The chemical constituent of the photocatalysts isn't changed and so cyclic catalysis is achieved.63

Fe3O4@C/Cu had higher photocatalytic activity for catalyzing the degradation of MB than Fe3O4@CuO in the presence of H2O2. According to the report, when more H2O2 was added to the reaction system, a higher catalytic activity of Fe3O4@C/Cu was seen. The photocatalytic activities of Fe3O4@HKUST-1, Fe3O4@C/Cu, Fe3O4@CuO were studied under visible light and compared with graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4), C/Cu, Fe3O4 and TiO2. According to the report, Fe3O4@C/Cu exhibited the highest catalytic activity, among these materials.112 Also, g-C3N4 showed high catalytic activity, which agrees with previous reports.113,114 MIL-125(Ti) could be modified with g-C3N4 to form g-C3N4/MIL-125(Ti), which could catalyze the degradation of RhB and also exhibited high stability during the reaction.12

The reduction of carbon dioxide has been a research hotspot. As mentioned above, the global energy crisis and global warming are two important problems that we are faced with today. The reduction and fixation of carbon dioxide can not only reduce greenhouse gases, but can also provide energy for human activities.3,115–117 Li et al. reported three kinds of MOFs that could catalyze the reduction of CO2 and the degeneration of RhB.118 Besides, UiO-66-NH2 and MIL-125-NH2 can also catalyze the reduction of CO2 under the irradiation of visible light. Carbon dioxide is transferred to HCOO− in this reaction. Interestingly, only when UiO-66 and MIL-125-NH2 are aminated do they exhibit photocatalytic activity for the reduction of CO2.119,120

Some organic synthesis reactions were reported to be catalyzed by MOFs. MIL-68(Fe) and MIL-100(Fe) were used as photocatalysts for the hydroxylation of benzene to phenol under visible light, and the photocatalytic activity of MIL-100(Fe) for this reaction was tested. According to the results, the conversion ratio of benzene in the presence of MIL-100(Fe) was 30.6% after 24 h. Also, the stability of MIL-100(Fe) during the reaction was also studied via three sets of cycling experiments, and it was proved to be stable. Though the conversion ratio of benzene wasn't very high, this study highlighted that photocatalytic reactions catalyzed by MOFs may also be applied for some organic syntheses.121 Also, MIL-101 can be used as a catalyst in the synthesis of some spices and drugs, which can help solve the lack of the natural sources of these products.48

Porphyrin can be applied for electrocatalysis and photocatalysis due to its specifical catalytic activity, and it can be used as a chemical sensor.122 A Cu porphyrin (5,10,15,20-tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)porphyrin)-based MOF, denoted as SCu, was reported. It could catalyze the reduction of carbon dioxide. Here, CO2 was reduced to methanol, which could be used as a fuel. The productive rate of methanol was 262.6 ppm g−1 h−1, which represents a considerable rate.3

In addition, many kinds of MOFs can be applied for atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP). An indium-based MOF ((Me2NH2)[InL]·3(H2O)·0.5DMF, donated as NNU-32, L: [5,5′-(anthracene-9,10-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic]), could catalyze ATRP. NNU-32 could be prepared via heating a mixture of H4L[5,5′-(anthracene-9,10-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic acid], In(NO3)3·4H2O, hydrochloric acid, DMF and H2O. UV was used to assess the adsorption of light by MMU-32, and it exhibited an adsorption band between 400 nm and 650 nm. According to the report, NNU-32 could catalyze the in situ synthesis of a cuprous activator, then the cuprous activator could initiate ATRP. The efficiency of this catalytic experiment was examined under visible light. The conversion of raw material is shown in Fig. 20a. Clearly, this polymerization could easily be controlled by light, and the conversion reported was considerable. The mechanism of this polymerization was also provided in the report (Fig. 20b); whereby In-MOF offers an electron to Cu(II), and Cu(II) is transformed into Cu(I). In exchange, the copper compound provides In-MOF with an anion. Then, the In-MOF cation can be deoxidised by an amine. Up to here, NNU-32 has finished the catalytic reaction of the cuprous activator production. Hence, MOFs can be applied for the catalysis of ATRP.10 However, the prohibitive cost of indium may restrict the development of NNU-32.

The factors that influence the photocatalytic activity have also been studied. The size of the molecules can influence the efficiency of the reaction. MOF-5 is a commercially available material. It has the ability to perform heterogeneous catalysis and can be used as an adsorber.123 It has been used to catalyze the degradation of 2,6-di-tert-butylphenol (DTBP) and phenol. However, under similar circumstances, the decomposition ratio of DTBP is higher than that of phenol. Half of the phenol was decomposed in 3 h while almost all the DTBP was decomposed during the same time. According to the report, it is the volume of DTBP and phenol that causes the difference. Phenol is much smaller than DTBP, which enables it to get into the pore of MOF-5. However, it is difficult to induce the photocatalytic reaction except on the surface of MOF-5. Hence it is more difficult for phenol to be decomposed than DTBP.124 Photocatalysis is also related to chirality. Wu et al. reported two kinds of MOFs as enantiomers.125 Chiral MOFs are suitable for heterogeneous asymmetric catalysis because they can control the shape of materials through their pores and embedded chiral functional groups.126–128 A triphenylamine group and L- or D-pyrrolidin-2-ylimidazole were set in a framework to obtain Zn-PYI1 and Zn-PYI2. These were enantiomers. The triphenylamine group induced the motion of photogenerated electrons, while the L- or D-pyrrolidin-2-ylimidazole promoted the asymmetrical catalytic reaction. They showed a synergistic effect according to the research of Wu et al. It has been proved that the asymmetric selectivity of combining a photocatalyst with an asymmetric catalyst in a MOF is superior to a simple combination of photocatalysis and asymmetric catalysis.125

To sum up, MOFs are promising for use in photocatalysis, due to their distinctive structures and photoexcitation activity. Lots of measures have been adopted to promote light capture and charge separation in order to improve the photocatalytic activity, such as combining MOFs with dyes, functionalization of ligands and uniting MOFs and photosensitizers or photocatalysts. Among the materials mentioned above, MIL-88A, MIL-53(Fe), Pd@MIL-100(Fe), CNNSs-MIL, Fe3O4@MIL-100(Fe) and some other compounds exhibit outstanding photocatalytic activities. According to those previous reports, photocatalysis can be applied for wastewater treatment, hydrogen production, carbon dioxide reduction and organic synthesis. The efficiency of these photocatalysts should be improved and the costs should be controlled. So further research on them ought to be carried out.

3.3 Electrocatalytic properties of MOFs

Nowadays, seeking a renewable energy source has become one of the most important issues facing modern society. Electrocatalysis is the most used catalytic method for HER, OER and ORR, which can provide renewable energy. Jahan et al. combined GO with Cu-MOF, which showed electrocatalytic activity for OER, HER and ORR, but the power density of this composite was only 76% of the traditional commercial Pt catalyst.129 MOFs can used as electrocatalysts. Some MOFs have photovoltaic characteristics.130 In general, the many shortcomings of MOFs make it difficult to apply MOFs to electrocatalysis. For instance, most MOFs have poor electron-conductive properties, which make them unable to work as electrocatalysts.131,132 Hence, many strategies have been discovered to solve this problem.131,133–135 For example, MOF films can be functionalized with ferrocene carboxylate.136 The catalysts are usually processed into membranes or spheres in order to simplify the recycling technology in a heterogeneous catalytic reaction. However, many MOFs have proven to be brittle crystals, so their electrode needs to be made with polymer binders or other additives. These additives can downgrade the conductivity of electrodes made of MOFs and may clog up the pores, which may affect the electrocatalytic performance. Thus, the electrodes of MOFs without polymer binders are sought.137 Actually, a lot of studies have been done to find the most suitable electrocatalysts for various applications. In general, the electrocatalytic activities of various catalysts are tested using cyclic voltammetry (CV).

MOFs that work as electrocatalysts can catalyze redox reactions. Cu3(BTB)2(H2O)3·(DMF)9(H2O)2, which is commonly called MOF-14, is reported to be able to catalyze the reduction of H2O2 and the oxidation of glucose. In order to prepare MOF-14, a mixture of H3BTB, dimethyl sulfoxide, Cu(OH)2, HNO3, DMF and deionized water was heated. A peak of the oxidation current appears at 0.7 V, which shows that MOF-14 exhibits good electrocatalytic activity for the oxidation of glucose (Fig. 21a). Similarly, CVs of the reduction of H2O2 also show the considerable electrocatalytic activity of MOF-14 for the reduction of H2O2 (Fig. 21b). Thus, MOF-14 may be a good candidate for electrochemical sensors.14 OER, HER and ORR can occur in the presence of MOFs or their derivatives working as electrocatalysts. PdCo alloy can be combined with N-doped carbon to form PdCo@CN, which shows electrocatalytic activity for HER. The composites are named after the molar ratio of Pd:Co. For instance, when Pd:Co = 1:6, the material is named S-6. The overpotentials of S-10, S-8 and S-6 are 100 mV, 85 mV and 80 mV when the current density is 10 mA cm−2, which are lower than Co@CN. The stability of S-6 was also tested. The hydrogen reduction in the presence of S-6 did not change after 10000 cycles, which indicated that S-6 had high electrocatalytic activity.138

4. Catalytic properties of ZIFs

Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs) are new kinds of porous material with zeolite-like topologies. The organic linker here is imidazole.170 Similar to MOFs, they can also be applied to photocatalysis and electrocatalysis. Their derivatives can also be used as catalysts. They may be carbonized, modified to form catalysts or they can encapsulate other molecules.171 For example, lots of photocatalysts and electrocatalysts can be prepared from ZIF-8 and ZIF-67.172–175 Generally speaking, it is nanoclusters or nanoparticles that exhibit catalytic activity,176 so the catalytic activity can be improved via doping the compounds with nanoparticles. In addition, the separation of poles and electrons and the transformation of electrons are important for photocatalysis.177–181 There are many reports about the catalytic activities of ZIFs.

4.1 Photocatalytic properties of ZIFs

The cobalt-containing benzimidazolate ZIF (Co-ZIF-9) was prepared and used as a cocatalyst. The synthetic strategy was provided in a previous report.182 It could work with Ru-based photosensitizers to catalyze the reduction of CO2 to CO. The relationship between the mass of Co-ZIF-9 and the efficiency of photocatalysis was studied (Fig. 28a). The production of CO reached a maximum for 1 mg Co-ZIF-9 added to the reaction system. It was pointed out that the reaction efficiency was mainly influenced by the mass of Co-ZIF-9 in region I, while it was influenced by the kinetics in region II.182 The production of H2 was also related to the mass of Co-ZIF-9, indicating the influence of Co-ZIF-9 on the redox reaction. This research may give indicators to the studies on artificial photosynthesis.183 Besides, the long-lived photoexcited state of ZIF-67 was discovered, indicating the potential of ZIF-67 for photocatalysis.184

ZIFs can be used as a precursor to prepare various photocatalysts, because they have various topological structures, which makes it easier to control the structure of the photocatalysts.185,186 Cao et al. synthesized ZIF-8 on 3D graphene networks (3DGNs), where the ZIF-8 worked as a precursor to prepare ZnO/3DGN.187 It was suitable to grow ZnO on 3DGNs because it is inexpensive and efficient as a photocatalyst.188 The photocatalytic activity of ZnO/3DGN was tested (Fig. 28b). 4.2 Electrocatalytic properties of ZIFs

Many noble metals are used as electrocatalysts, such as Ru, Pt and Ir composites.194 Ag, Au, Pt, Pd, Ir, Ru are usually used for the reduction of CO2.104,138,195–199 But noble metal composites have a major disadvantage which is that the sources of noble metals are pretty rare and thus tend to be expensive, and this limits their development. In addition, the stability of noble composites during the reactions is low, which means that the catalysts need to be replaced frequently.133,200

Nitrogen–boron codoped graphitic carbon cages, which can encapsulate Co nanoparticles (denoted as Co@BCN), were prepared via heating a mixture of boric acid solution and ZIF-67 and calcining the products. It exhibited electrocatalytic activity for HER. The electrocatalytic activity of Co@BCN was studied. The overpotential of Co@BCN was measured to be 0.096 V when the current density was 10 mA cm−2, which is pretty low. The electrocatalytic activity was proved to be excellent.201

The derivatives of ZIFs may also be able to be applied to electrocatalysis. According to Wei et al., SO3H-functionalized graphene oxide and ZIF-8 could be combined to form ZIF-8@SO3H–GO. It could be removed from the reaction system via filtering, and it could be recycled for about 10 times. It is also suitable to be used as an electrocatalyst.202

Several kinds of Zn- and Co-based bimetallic ZIFs (BMZIFs) were synthesized, and they were denoted as BMZIF-n, in which n refers to the molar ratio of Zn:Co, where BMZIF-100 refers to the BMZIF without Co and BMZIF-0 refers to the BMZIF without Zn. Furthermore, it was stable in a solution of methanol and KOH.190 ZIF-8 can also grow on Te nanowires and be carbonized to form a composite that has an electrocatalytic activity for ORR.204

Briefly, a eutectic mixture of LiCl–KCl and ZIF-67 were mixed together by ball-milling, and the mixture was carbonized at 800 °C to prepare N, Co-codoped carbon nanosheets (N, Co-CNSs-800), which showed electrocatalytic activity for ORR. Also, NCNTF showed electrocatalytic activity for ORR as well as OER,207 and then when the coordination was released, it exhibits photocatalytic activity for the degradation of MB.192

5. Catalytic properties of COFs

COFs represent a series of frameworks that are formed by light elements, such as C, B, N, O and H.208 The catalytic properties of COFs have also been studied. A series of CdS–COFs were synthesized and they were proved to be able to catalyze hydrogen production under visible light. The synthetic strategy of CdS–COFs was provided in the report. Briefly, mesitylene, acetic acid and dioxane were mixed and heated to prepare the COF. Then the COF was mixed with dimethyl sulphoxide and Cd(OAc)2 and heated. Then, the CdS–COF hybrid was obtained. 6. characterizations

In order to explore the relationship between the characterization and catalytic performance of the catalysts of the above type, some of the more typical catalyst characterizations described above and the relationship between the catalytic performance and characterization are summarized here.

In general, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) can be used to determine the presence of an element by measuring the binding energy, whereas X-ray diffraction (XRD) can determine the presence of each component in PCPs. The combination of the two measuring methods can explore the existence of each component in PCPs derivatives and their relationship, and then initially determine the photocatalytic activity.77 SEM can present the most intuitive crystal morphology of PCPs and their derivatives, which can help determine the most basic crystal morphology and degree of crystallinity, and compare whether their crystalline structures are affected following the combination of PCPs and other compounds.2,53,65 In particular, as for electrocatalysts, the Tafel slope as well as the overpotential can reflect the electrocatalytic properties.4

XRD, SEM and UV-vis spectra can be used to help determine the catalytic properties of materials. For instance, both BWO/UiO-66-1 and AgCO/UiO-66-0.5 are typical examples of combining MOF and inorganic compounds. The SEM and XRD results showed that UiO-66 still retained its original structure after combining with inorganic compounds (Fig. 31). This shows the stability of UiO-66, which is exactly what is required of it as a catalyst.2,65 The ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) of a photocatalyst causes it to show some characteristic peaks in UV-visible absorption spectra. For example, the absorption edge of MIL-88A is about 650 nm with many discrete absorption peaks.39

As mentioned earlier, Zou et al. used metal ions to replace titanium ions in the original MOF metal ions, thus obtaining a new MOF with an improved catalytic performance. The metal ions in MOF-74(Zn), MOF-74(Mg), PCN-333(Sc) and MIL-100(Sc) were replaced with Ti(III). The Ti content of the four products was analyzed by energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) and inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). Among these, the conversion of MOF-74(Zn)-Ti was 100%, while the conversion of MOF-74(Mg)-Ti was only 35.1%. Although they have the same framework structure, the conversion rates of metal ions were quite different. According to the author's analysis, this was due to the strength of the coordination bond of the metal ion and the ionic radius. The Zn ion radius is close to that of the Ti ion, resulting in a lower lattice tension; moreover, the bond strength of Zn ions in the crystal lattice is lower than that of Mg and was therefore easier to replace. On the other hand, the conversion rate of PCN-333(Sc)-Ti was 85.9%, which was significantly higher than that of MIL-100(Sc)-Ti (52.0%), although both contained the same metal ions. This difference was mainly caused by the difference between the two connector lengths. The length of the PCN-333(Sc) connector was greater than that of MIL-100(Sc), resulting in larger voids in the crystal and greater flexibility. Therefore, the metal ions in PCN-333(Sc) could be more easily displaced.63 This provides a guideline for the preparation of PCPs by ion exchange, whereby the conversion rate can be increased by increasing the length of the linker and choosing ions with similar radius and coordinative bond strength.

As for the photocatalysts, the UV-vis spectra (Fig. 32) in Liang et al.'s study of MIL-68(In) and MIL-68(In)-NH2 shows that the light absorption range of MOFs can be adjusted by introducing amino groups into the MOFs. The introduction of amino groups can move the absorption edge to the visible light range. It can be seen that the introduction of auxochromes such as amino groups into the linker can increase the utilization of visible light for PCPs.47

7. Conclusions

The search for renewable energy and clean energy to replace the current fossil energy will be an increasingly important issue for human society in the future. The use of alternatives to fossil fuels can not only reduce environmental pollution but also ease the energy crisis. In terms of electrocatalysis, when PCPs act as an electrocatalyst, they can catalyze the HER, OER, ORR and the oxidation of some compounds and the reduction of heavy metal ions. Qualified electrocatalysts should have the following characteristics: (1) stable at higher potentials, (2) high conductivity to transfer charge,4 and (3) reasonably priced. In the case of PCPs, the majority of PCPs are electrically conductive and therefore can increase the conductivity of PCPs by combining the PCPs with other compounds, such as some ionic compounds, or by carbonizing MOFs, while retaining the catalytic ability of PCPs. For photocatalysis, the catalyst should not only have high catalytic activity and stability, but also a certain light trapping ability and charge-transfer ability. Except for some PCPs that can be directly used as photocatalysts, many of the remaining PCPs must be carbonized or combined with other compounds. In a heterogeneous photocatalytic reaction, the photocatalyst can be recovered, which allows the photocatalyst to be reused, and avoids the pollution of the product and the environment by substances in the photocatalyst.5 As a photocatalyst, PCPs are often used to treat organic pollutants, heavy metal pollutants or to react in some reactions, such as HER, OER and ORR, and are of great significance to attempts to solve the energy crisis and environmental pollution. Many studies may open new doors for photocatalysis, electrocatalysis and photoelectrocatalysis. For instance, Chaudhari et al. reported some examples of porous 2D nanoplatelets made of 3D MOF structures and developed the supramolecular self-assembly method in order to synthesize MOF nanosheets under ambient conditions using a host–guest strategy for their in situ functionalization. The MOF nanosheets obtained by this method have considerable photosensitivity, and may be used as chemical sensors. The nanosheets can be combined with catalysts, and the nanosheets could be excited by light to release electrons in the composite. Then the composite can exhibit good photocatalytic activity.213

However, there are also many challenges for MOFs, ZIFs and COFs in electrocatalysis, photocatalysis and photoelectrocatalysis. For instance, the mechanisms of many catalytic reactions haven't been clarified yet, such as water splitting catalysed by MOFs.1 and the conductivity and photosensitivity of PCPs should be improved in order to improve the photocatalytic and electrocatalytic activities. Photocatalysis may be applied for organic synthesis, but the efficiency needs to be improved.121 As mentioned before, combining MOFs with other photosensitizers, dyes or catalysts may be a good idea to improve the efficiency. Besides, ligand functionalization and quantum doping can also help. More research should be done. Besides, as for COFs, according to a previous report, it is now well equipped to prepare small COF crystals that are suitable for single crystal electron diffraction, but it is difficult to prepare large COF crystals, which would be very useful for exploring the properties of COF, so more research is needed.214 However, despite so many challenges, PCPs are good candidates for catalysis and the outlook is bright in this field. All in all, more research should be done to optimize the catalytic performances of PCPs.
