Abstract

In recent times, optoelectronic materials have become emerging technology and several designs have been established to overcome the past failures and desire for new efficient devices. Numerous analyses employing imidazole, carbazole, triphenylamine, phosphine oxide, squaraine, BODIPY, dithienosilole, dithienogermole, and fullerene/non-fullerene derivatives have been developed in different donor–acceptor combinations with the inclusion of pi/spacer conjugations. Most of these designs are constructed by organic, organometallic, and solid-state (or hybrids) to improve the external quantum yield, lifetime, and color saturation. Recently, few bio-molecular materials (peptide-conjugated) have attracted attention due to the screening of their electronic and luminous properties. Peptide-based conjugate molecules are a promising class of soft materials, which are currently gaining importance among the material community due to a wide range of applications (protein therapeutics, drug delivery, optoelectronics, and catalysis). However, peptide-based materials are currently a focused area of study since inadequate results are available and it is required to increase interest in the field with peptides as the main focus in optoelectronics. The current review explains optoelectronic materials based on peptides moieties, which have unexplored applications due to fewer reports on them. In optoelectronics, photovoltaics (solar cells), organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), thin-film transistors, and electrochromism have been discussed. Photoactive materials design based on the donor and acceptor concept has been presented and discussed. This evaluation contributes to the materialistic properties of the peptides for electro-optic applications and their design strategy along with the current status. The discussion on the future perspectives of optoelectronics via peptide-based materials will certainly give an insight into the new upcoming trends.

1. Introduction

Luminescence is a bright endless source in Nature and has been utilized for several human needs. Among the reported systems, lighting applications have become a high priority and field.1,2 Among luminescent tools, a few categories of materials are attractive, which are smart stimuli-responsive materials, mechano-responsive luminescence, templates for casting metal nanowires, biosensors-targeted molecular imaging, delivery agents, energy storage materials, light harvesting-display devices, and nanopiezoelectrics.3,4 Several molecular materials such as pyrene, carbazole, triphenylamine, fluorescein, phosphine oxide, squaraine, BODIPY, dithienosilole, dithienogermole, fullerene/non-fullerene, and rhodamine-based designs have been proven to have potential applications in the field of lighting applications, especially in the field of optoelectronics.5,6 To develop the photophysical properties of optoelectronics such as high photochemical/thermal stability, long lifetime, high quantum efficiencies, several approaches are developed and they are heavy metal ion usage, hydrogen bond, matrix-assisted isolation, crystallization, and aggregation-induced emission.

Most of the optoelectronic designs are developed with organic, organometallic, solid-state compositions, and, less frequently, bio-based materials. In bio-based materials, peptide-related architecture is favorable in light harvesting and electron transfer processes among other bio-functionalities. Peptide-based/related materials are an attractive research trend and currently, few research groups are focusing on the same.7–9 However, there is a lack of designs for optoelectronics based on peptides and it is time to understand the importance of peptide materials to fulfill the requirement of future generations. Since day-to-day energy consumption rate is increasing, there is a great need to search for fulfilling sources that also save the natural sources (fossil fuels). Among renewable energies, optoelectronics is a steadily growing field, which plays a major role in the sustainability of the human society.

Still, the scarcity of peptide-based materials for optoelectronics has motivated this review. In common, both photon as well as electron responses are essential in optoelectronics and choosing fluorophores required minimum standards such as brightness, excitation, emission wavelength, photostability, photobleaching, chemical reactions, and photophysical reactions.10–12 In the case of peptides, these materials are used for antimicrobial peptides, glycopeptides, cyclopeptides, tissue engineering,13 cell culturing, drug delivery, bio-sensing,14 and self-healing processes.

The self-assembly property of peptides, proteins, and their biodegradable/biocompatible properties makes them suitable to explore their applications further.15,16 From the last few decades, the interest in peptide-based systems has increased due to easy molecular engineering to fine tune the redox properties of peptide molecules. However, peptide-based materials employed in optoelectronic applications is a new idea because peptide derivatives are active light-sensitive, electroactive, and thermally active. Peptide and protein-based materials are also adaptive to living systems. The functional materials of these adaptive systems can yield molecular complexes (peptide groups in to materials) in the near future and investigation is underway on the understanding of peptide materials. In this context, M. Venanzi and S. Kimura expressed their view in 2013 on peptides and how these are going to be active in upcoming research.17 Based on the light response, several bio-based concepts are reported.18 Proteins and enzymes in biological systems have been reported with a chiral configuration between the two enantiomers and they grow through supramolecular interactions, which can be realized in supramolecular constructions by structural mismatch, self-sorting, and pH variation.7,19 The introduction of proteins into materials science in the near future may lead toward new bioinspired resources for optoelectronics and nanoelectronics. More recently, bio-compatible nanomaterials have useful electron, hole, and proton transport properties in biomaterials, even they are engaged in solar cells, field-effect transistors, and display devices.20,21

In addition, nanoscale roughness gives direction to superhydrophobicity and it depends on the surface free energy. Few molecules such as pentafluoroaniline in peptide nanowires have been used to decrease the surface free energy and hydrophobic coatings helpful for solar cells as well as smart windows.22 The Gatto and Venanzi group reported multi-component peptide-functionalized with photoactive and electro-response groups.23 Thermal responsive materials are added constructively to sensing applications; these responses lead to the excitation of the molecule for light generation while heating. The presence of electron transfer or electron-rich species in the peptide group leads to intramolecular attraction, which reveals the sensing property. In addition, color change with heating is an advantage to the thermal response. Similarly, mechanoluminescence also has several reports, where color changing with grinding results in two colors and less common multicolor.24

Electroactive peptide-based materials are valuable in the arena of optoelectronics. Especially, in optoelectronics, most of these materials are used as photovoltaics (solar cells), organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), thin-film transistors, and electrochromism. The main goal of light-emitting materials is utilizing the absorbed energy to yield an efficient outcome. However, theoretical prediction gives 100% result but experimental or real application do not give satisfactory results. Suppose in the case of a silicon wafer, a thick silicon wafer absorbs 60% of the available power and it is well known that all absorbed light is not converted into electrical power.25 Focusing on the same, in this review, we discussed four areas of optoelectronics, which are (i) photovoltaics, (ii) OLEDs, (iii) thin-film transistors, and (iv) electrochromism. In addition, peptide-based mechanoluminescence is discussed.

Light-responsive materials in the above fields are discussed based on the peptide or related materials. Fluorescence tuning with the reversible behavior of solid-state materials is an attractive research area and has potential application in fluorescent probes, photoelectronic devices, displays, and sensors.26 The construction of donor and acceptor moieties is an effective strategy for the optoelectronics study. Several reports are available for understanding the donor–acceptor concept; however, this review considered all the donor and acceptors with the pi-conjugation/spacer in one place. In addition, fewer reports on peptide-based electronics are emphasized. This review constructs the knowledge gap between the lighting application and the peptide functional groups (Fig. 1).

1.2 Sensitization strategies

Electrons in the molecules excite higher energy states by absorbing light energy in the form of a photon. Each molecule has its respective absorption wavelength and all the molecules cannot be excited directly because of the absence of electrons, from a bonding/non-bonding orbital into the empty anti-bonding orbital (may be due to the absence of conjugated system of pi-electrons). Therefore, a sensitizer is very much essential, which can populate the excited state.31,32 Photosensitization can be explained with two major processes, which are shown in Fig. 2. In optoelectronics, especially in dye-sensitized solar cells, light is absorbed by the dye molecule and electron injection initiated. Here, majorly energy-desired parts depend upon the absorption capability of the molecules and the driving force of an electron excitation. A solar cell discussion cannot be made without the conduction (CB) and valence bands (VB). The energy difference between the CB and VB reveals the efficiency of the device.
Fig. 2 Light absorption process by dye sensitization (left) and organic molecule with metal incorporation (right).

Another process is that in which light is absorbed by organic materials and transfers to higher energy levels and/or for light generation (absorption → emission). Here, fluorescence (FL, singlet to singlet) and phosphorescence (PL, triplet to singlet) are initial processes. The energy process in Fig. 2 (right) is shown with a metal supplement. Pure organic molecules are limited to singlet radiative emission as per spin selection and Laporte selection rules.33 Based on the triplet and metal excited state energy levels, there is a possibility of back transfer (BT). The incorporation of the metal into organic molecules increases the emission capability by overcoming the singlet to triplet energy forbidden transfer, resulting in luminescence (LM) from metal ions. However, this procedure is not ideal as it is difficult to find a sufficiently stable phosphorescent emitter structure. Therefore, it is necessary to look at the differences in the energy levels, in which absorbed light can be transferred in the form of emitted light.

2. Peptide-based optoelectronics

Peptide and protein-based materials show optoelectronic applications and they have interesting photoluminescent properties in π–π aromatic conjugated interactions, as well as hydrogen bonding properties, which lead to photoluminescent assets. Peptides such as diphenylalanine-based peptides show temperature-dependent emission at 225 nm excitation and strong photoluminescent behavior during blue excitation.60 Based on emission spectral analysis, Tao et al. reported aromatic cyclo-dipeptides, which are (i) cyclo-phenylalanine-tryptophan (cyclo-FW) and (ii) cyclo-WW (W = tryptophan). Cyclo-FW emitted fluorescence emission at 460 nm at an excitation of 370 nm and cyclo-WW showed emission at 425 and 520 nm with the same excitation source. The absorption peak at 515 nm is attributed to the peptide (ligand) metal charge transfer. Pansieri et al.61 used the blue luminescence of Alzheimer's amyloid fibers and it has a phenylalanine peptide source. Here, first, we discuss about peptides for monochromic luminescence and continue to the same peptides for optoelectronics. This part discusses photovoltaics, OLEDs, thin-film transistors, and finally, electrochromism.

2.1 Peptide fluorophores

The combination of amine and acid groups into a molecule to form a peptide bond with the inclusion of an aromatic moiety leads to luminous emission properties. In general, fluorophore emission causes the absorbed excitation light to interact with an electron of the fluorescent molecule. Fluorescent molecules have already shown a wide range of applications and further eminent applications are still being investigated. In this scenario, peptide-based molecular materials are an attractive research area. Further, in the area of peptide fluorophores, recently, S. Loic62 defined peptide fine-tuning by amino acid modification. These fluorescent peptides lead to new achievements in the field of optoelectronic device materials.

In the case of mechanochromic luminescence, the luminous (color changes) property is induced by mechanical forces. Mechanochromic luminescence refers to mechanical-stimuli-response and reversible color change of the compounds. Nowadays, peptide materials for mechanochromic luminescence is an eye-catching area and this concept has become exciting by the incorporation of the aggregation-induced emission process.63,64 The design of these peptide-based materials contains small and/or dendron-type molecules.

3. Photovoltaics

The conversion of light into energy using semiconducting materials that show the photovoltaic effect, a phenomenon investigated in physics, photochemistry, and electrochemistry, is known as photovoltaics (PV).74,75 Photovoltaic cells are made up of two or more layers of semiconductors lined up next to each other, one with a positive charge and the other with a negative charge. Sunlight, which is constituted of small energy packets known as photons, strikes the cell and is either reflected, transmitted, or absorbed. When the photons are absorbed by the negative layer of the photovoltaic cell, the energy of the photon gets moved to an electron in an atom of the cell. The electron departs the atom's outer shell as its energy increases. The released electron naturally migrates to the positive layer, resulting in a potential difference between the two layers. The electron flows through the circuit when the two layers are coupled to an external circuit, creating a current. In barely over a decade, the photovoltaics of organic–inorganic lead halide perovskite materials have surpassed the peak efficiency of semiconductor compounds such as CdTe and CIGS (copper indium gallium selenide) used in solar cells.76,77

Solar cells are used to generate direct current and can be used as a power equipment. To convert the DC current produced by PVs to AC, an inverter with an efficiency of about 95% is required. The solar cells must be protected from harm; thus, they are usually packed tightly under a sheet of glass. The solar panel is then employed as a component in a larger photovoltaic system.

Integrated PV modules are being utilized to replace traditional building materials in some areas of a structure, such as the roof and windows. They are available in several forms, such as flat roofs, pitched roofs, facades, and glazings. Peptide-based materials are preferred in the device in less quantity (for active layer formation) since the peptide-based layer decreases the energy transfer efficiency with the thickness. In addition, dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSC) have been growing vigorously and few materials have already proven to be potential candidates. However, to further improve the efficiency, the findings are in progress. Earlier reports on DSSC showed the covalent linkage with organic dyes on the metal oxide surface, which resists the aggregation of molecules.78–81 Further, photocurrent generation analysis is made by peptide-based materials, which are thermally stable and conformationally arranged. These are feasible to self-assemble on a gold surface and lead to light-induced electron–hole separation. This initiative work motivated work toward peptide molecules as a solar cell material.

3.1 Advantages of photovoltaic cell

Photovoltaic cells produce clean and green energy because no hazardous gases such as COx, NOx, and other pollutants are emitted. They also do not pollute the environment, making them perfect for use in residential areas.82 PVs are economically viable—the cost of operating and maintenance is very low. The cost of a solar panel is merely the only cost, which includes purchase and installation. Solar panels are easy to install and can be made available in remote areas or sparsely populated areas at a lower cost than traditional transmission lines.

4. Organic light-emitting diodes

OLEDs are developed with a giant number of molecules to improve the efficiency of the device. Presently, we are focused on only peptide-based materials for organic light-emitting diodes as the already designed lighting materials are mainly organic or metal-based complexes and rarely corban/graphene associated constructions. OLEDs are a sandwich type of model that consist of glass substrate/anode//hole transporting layer/emissive layer/electron transporting layer/cathode. The device, when it is a forward bias, electrons and holes move to the emissive layer and recombine to generate the emissive light, which is called electroluminescence (Fig. 19). The pioneer work from Tang and Van Slyke in 1987 on organic light = emitting diodes is a milestone in the optoelectronics field and from this point onwards, there have been numerous reports.88
Fig. 19 Ligand excited energy level diagrams and the general construction of the OLED device.

Generally, fluorescence and phosphorescence are emissive processes in photoluminescence; the excitation source is the photon, and in the case of electroluminescence, electron is the source of light generation. In the electroluminescence process, singlet energy is limited to one-fourth and the triplet energy is limited to three-fourth of the total energy as it is known that singlet to triplet transition is a forbidden transition and is limited to only 25%. To utilize the remaining unutilized 75% of the energy, few methods are available, which are tremendously moving toward the complete utilization of the excitation energy toward emission. Such methods are heavy metal ions (transition and rare earth metals preferable), thermally-activated delayed fluorescence (TADF), hybridized local and charge transfer (HLCT), and triplet–triplet annihilation (TTA).89 Heavy metal ions are utilized for phosphorescence emission and reaming is mostly fluorescent emission.

TTA is a process that has long-lived excitons and arises due to the bimolecular process of two triplet state excitons. The combination of two triplet states’ energy gives rice a high singlet population and it is equal to almost two-folds of the original triplet state energy. The process is carried out as a triplet and triplet to the excited triplet (Tn). From here, energy is transferred to the first singlet state and finally reached the ground state via light emission. In the case of TADF, triplet energy (T1) is transferred to singlet (S1) energy level via reverse intersystem crossing (RISC). The design strategy includes the high priority of the energy gap between the singlet and triplet levels. Metal–organic complexes as well as pure organic compounds are more preferable for TADF emitters since they have flexible construction of molecular arrangement. Along with TTA and TADF, another method that has also been familiar in the recent years for utilizing the triplet state energy is the HLCT method. In this process, S1 and T1 are located lower than the host triplet excitons (Fig. 20). In the HLCT transfer of energy from these, hot S1 and T1 states can appear via RISC/due to the twisting angle. It leads to the arrangement of triplet and singlet states to be quite difficult to design to achieve the HLCT process. At present, tremendous progress has been going on to receive the high singlet population either via the direct (fluorescence) or the indirect (TTA, TADF, HLCT) process and to improve the high efficiency devices (high EQY, lifetime).

4.1 Phosphorescent OLEDs

Phosphorescent OLEDs are attractive in two ways to exploit the triplet state such as the metal-free organic compound and the metal-based complex. In the above-discussed TADF method, few of the molecules exhibit phosphorescence instead of fluorescence. In addition, a metal-free organic molecule for phosphorescence is cost-effective, easier to synthesize, and fabrication feasibility. The predicted possible values are for blue fluorescence to harvest the 25% singlet excitons and phosphorescent red and green emitters to harvest the 75% triplet excitons. It means theoretically allowing 100% exciton use.90 However, efficient triplet harvesting with standard blue emitter systems has been not satisfactory. Fluorescent blue emitters have been proposed for white OLED to harvest the electrically generated singlets and to transfer the triplets to the phosphorescent green and red emitters. Usually, RGB colors are required to obtain white emission; based on this concept, the highly efficient white OLEDs are designed by combining the primary color emitting phosphorescent materials.

4.2 Fluorescent OLEDs

The maximum efficiency of the device as the external quantum efficiency (EQE) is limited to approximately 5% due to the negligible spin–orbit coupling (forbidden) of the triplet in pure organic molecules. Several fluorescent devices are established to overcome 5% of EQE by utilizing the triplet excitons. Not only pure organic molecules but metal-based coordination complexes are introduced based on iridium (Ir) and platinum (Pt) as the active layer.91 However, organic emitters have several benefits compared to metal-based fluorophores such as lower cost (since no metal) and synthetic flexibility with organic compounds. In pure organic compounds, the triplet energy is harvested; the TADF mechanism was introduced by Uoyama et al. in 2012.92 Based on this process, diverse electron donor and acceptor combinations (chemical bond/intermolecular interaction) have been reported.93,94

Later on, hot-excitation mechanism was introduced to improve the efficiency and the mechanism includes the conversion of triplet excitons (upper-lying/hot) to singlet excitons via reverse inter-system crossing. From the introduction of hot-excitation by Li et al. and Yao et al., various researchers are working to improve the singlet population manifold by excitation using sensitizers (pure/hybrid form).95,96 Recently, in 2015, Peng et al. introduced a neutral radical with the donor–acceptor composition as the emitting layer.97

5. Thin-film transistors (TFT)

TFTs are essentially MOSFET transistors, and the first TFT was produced in 1962, about two years after the first MOSFET. The first TFT-LCD was established in 1973, establishing the main direction of the TFT technology's research and development. TFTs and MOSFETs do not have the same fabrication issues. This distinction explains why TFT-L took over two decades to develop.100,101

TFT is a component of an LCD, which is used to increase the display's quality and control. It is basically a tiny transistor connected to each and every pixel on the screen. TFT technology has the highest resolution of all flat-panel systems in today's market.102 TFT glass contains the same number of TFTs as the number of pixels displayed; however, a color filter glass features a color filter that provides the color. The liquid crystals move depending on the voltage difference between the color filter glass and the TFT glass. Back light is the amount of light determined by the amount of liquid crystal movement required to generate the color.103

TFTs are most commonly used in LCD screens. TFTs, on the other hand, are commonly utilized as sensors in X-ray detectors. It is also used in a variety of sensing applications. TFT technology has been used for physical, chemical, and biological sensing as well as LCDs. TFT displays are also known as active-matrix LCDs. The liquid crystal of TFT LCD (Thin Film Transistor Liquid Crystal Display) screens is sandwiched between two glass plates. TFTs in active-matrix LCDs operate as basic ON/OFF switches that move at different speeds depending on the refresh rate of the LCD, for example, 60 Hz. A simple structure of TFT consists of three terminals: the gate, the source, and the drain. A gate insulating film separates the gate from the semiconductor film, while the drain and source are in direct contact with the semiconductor layer.104,105

5.1 A simple hin-film-transistor (TFT) structure

To turn on a simple TFT, such as an N-channel TFT, a positive voltage is applied to the gate; the insulation layer acts as the dielectric layer in a capacitor, causing negative charges to be induced on the semiconductor channel, which is the region between the source and the drain; these negative charges cause electrons to flow from the source to the drain. When a negative voltage is applied to the gate, electrons in the channel are depleted, and there is almost no current. The ON current is affected by a number of factors, including the channel width, channel length, gate voltage, and TFT threshold voltage. When the TFT is turned on, a data voltage is applied to the source, and the drain with the LC load capacitance charges up to the same voltage, delivering the data voltage from the data line to the pixel electrode. There is no current in the channel when it is turned off, and the data voltage cannot be transferred. A TFT substrate is made up of a matrix of pixels and an ITO (transparent electrically conductive film) region, each of which contains a TFT device. The image on the screen is made up of thousands or millions of these pixels.106,107

TFTs have the advantage of being quick enough for video, having a vast and smooth color palette, and being pixel addressable via an electrical two-dimensional control matrix. Most low-cost displays use plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition to produce an amorphous silicon crystal layer onto the glass. Modern displays are the result of a variety of TFT technologies. Poor viewing angles, contrast, and backlighting were among the first issues to be solved. Many displays have become more vivid as a result of improved light sources, diffusers, and polarizers, with some even claiming to be daylight readable. In-plane switching, a modern technique that makes the crystals move parallelly to the display plane rather than vertically, improving the viewing angles. Modern displays have improved speeds and contrasts, resulting in high performance at a reasonable cost.108,109

5.2 Function of TFT

The passage of light is controlled by a liquid crystal in a TFT. A TFT-LCD panel's basic structure can be conceived of as two pieces of glass sandwiched together by a layer of liquid crystal. A color filter is installed on the front glass, while transistors are installed on the back glass. When a transistor is powered up, the liquid crystal bends, enabling light to flow through and produce a pixel. The backlight is made up of a light source, which is often an LED that is situated at the back of the screen. A color filter is installed on the front glass, giving each pixel its own color. The image on the panel is created by combining these pixels in various hues.110

7. Future opportunities and conclusion

The intrinsic conductivities of the peptides enable their electrical applications. Peptides are not only related to biological compounds but can also act as bioelectronics. As discussed in this review, several bioelectronic and peptide materials have been applied in optoelectronics. The current review discusses the fundamental concept of the peptides, the status of peptide-based materials, and their design strategy. The suitable design of D–A combination leads to high-efficiency devices in optoelectronics. Most of the D–A pairs are designed with spacer or pi-conjugation but replacing these with a peptide is a new approach. The rare availability of reports indicates the less use of peptides in optoelectronics. In addition, the assumption of “peptide is a biological/living species” is now changing to “peptide is an electronic bridge” concept.

The current review covered the essentials of peptides and their design strategy, involving the development of cost-effective and eco-friendly synthetic strategies to achieve peptide-based conjugates. Optoelectronic applications such as photovoltaics, organic light-emitting diodes, thin-film transistors, and electrochromism are discussed based on peptides. Further discussion has been made on peptide-based color change mechanoluminescence and their optical performance. Peptide-based materials will certainly give future directions for optical applications, which may overcome the existing issues such as less lifetime, moderate quantum yields, and unsatisfactory device efficiency.
