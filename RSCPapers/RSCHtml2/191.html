Recent progress on lanthanide-based long persistent phosphors: an overview

Sivakumar Vaidyanathan *

Abstract

Persistent phosphors are highly explored materials due to their diverse applications (including anti-counterfeiting, information and data storage, photocatalysis, sensing, and bioimaging). Lanthanide activation offers effortless tuning of colour in several host lattices based on previously established theories such as the Dorenbos model. In this review, we present a compilation of some long persistent phosphors including various crystal structures exhibiting a variety of colours with varying host lattices and activating lanthanide ions. The understanding of various mechanisms of persistent luminescence (PersL) is refined, detailed and divided into various models, mainly the electron trapping–detrapping and hole trapping–detrapping models. These mechanisms involve various processes such as tunnelling between energy states to achieve PersL. Several phosphors have been commercially used and are integrated in everyday life, primarily in display applications. Continuous research has improved the properties and widened the future scope for these long persistent phosphors. This review deals with only a few examples in this well-established field, focusing on the past decade, separated by the crystal structure of phosphor materials and denoting the colour and time of their persistent afterglow. This comprehensive review presents guidance for the design and synthesis of new persistent phosphors and their versatile applications together with their structure–composition–property–relationship.

Introduction

Long persistent luminescence is an astonishing phenomenon in which the emission from a phosphor is maintained for a while after removing the excitation source, often referred to as afterglow. The time of uninterrupted emission may vary from a few milliseconds to several hours and sometimes even days, depending on different phosphors. The primary reason for this is the accumulation of excitation energy in phosphors by absorbing ultraviolet or visible light, which is gradually released in the form of light (visible or infrared) over a long period depending on the thermal disturbance in the phosphor system.1–3 The first known afterglow phenomenon dates over thousands of years, but perhaps not in the same way as known today. The first scientific report with an explanation goes back to the sixteenth century.4 Long persistent phosphors (LPPs) are applied in different fields, including chemistry, physics and materials science being. However, the current research interest in LPPs is extending their application in many other fields including bio-imaging and medicine.5 The interest in LPPs was boosted last two decades ago after the discovery of the important LPP SrAl2O4 in 1997, although the active research on LPPs dates back more than half a century ago.6,7

The extensive research in the field of persistent phosphors has led to the development of hundreds of persistent phosphors; however, only a few have been commercialised to date. Many LPPs in the form of polymer and organic-based LPPs are also known and studied.8,9 Inorganic LPPs possess inorganic crystals as their matrix and their luminescence results from the introduced dopants, mainly main group or/and transition metal ions. However, in recent studies, the attention has shifted to the utility of lanthanides for the same purpose due to their characteristics and comparatively broad transition range, versatility and high efficiency. Long persistent phosphors have widely found applications in signalling, including optical displays, sensing, military applications, emergency signs, and anti-counterfeiting.5,10–13 However, their application has been extended to the fields of optical storage14 and bio-imaging due to their peculiar energy ranges, which are in the biologically transparent window, i.e., the infrared region.1 Previously, sulphur-based hosts were widely used, but recently their interest has depleted due to their shortcomings, mainly chemical instability. The consideration is shifting towards silicate-based host matrixes together with exploring phosphate and transition metal-based lattices over aluminate and other hosts.15 Also, the conventionally utilised transition metal doping for the induction of afterglow has recently decreased due to the more promising effects of lanthanide ions for the stimulation of long persistent phosphorescence. Lanthanides are especially useful in cases of high-lying 5 d orbitals. Lanthanide ions offer a wide range of trap energy levels when incorporated as a trap, while the innumerable excited energy levels of lanthanide ions provide a broad range of transitions compared to main group elements and transition metals.16 Some lattices offer better results in the simultaneous activation of activators (lanthanides) and sensitizers (lanthanide ions or transition metal ions). Sensitizers mediate and enhance the intensity or duration of PersL.

Although countless LPPs are used globally, this review focuses on host lattice (different mineral based such as garnets, silicates, aluminates, borates, and perovskites)-based LPPs with an Ln activator/co-activator, considering mainly the last decade of research and development in the lanthanide activated long persistent phosphors, which are sorted based on colour for convenience. Mechanism

The emitted radiation from LPPs falls in the range of UV to near IR including the visible region. All these regions have been extensively utilised for wide-ranging applications. Phosphorescence is no longer limited to inorganic solids but has recently been extended to even organic molecules including polyurethanes, phosphors, and metal coordination polymers.8,17,18 However, although there have been extensive discussions and debates over decades regarding their mechanism, few models have been formulated to simplify their explanation, discussion, and understanding for further study. In this case, although many aspects are still unknown or not followed for some special cases, these models generalise the phenomenon of LPPs.19 The most important characteristics of these phosphors are the colour of their emission and the time in which their glow persists, which are determined by the energy of excitation and trap depth, respectively.

Requisites of long persistent phosphorescence

The requirement of long persistent phosphorescence materials are as follows:20 (1) inorganic matrix (host), (2) activated doping ions (activator/emission centre), (3) traps (trap centres), and (4) excitation source.

The activators include lanthanide ions (e.g., Ce3+, Eu2+, Nd3+, and Er3+)[5d → 4f or 4f → 4f transitions]; transition metal ions (e.g., Cr3+, Mn2+, Mn4+, and Ni2+)[d → d transition]; and main group elements (e.g., Pb2+ and Bi3+) [p → s transition]. The traps centres include lattice/intrinsic defects such as oxygen vacancy, F-centres, anti-site defects; impurities such as Cu2+, Co2+, and Ti3+ in different host matrices; and intentionally introduced co-dopants, e.g., Dy3+, in SrAl2O4:Eu2+ and Nd3+ in CaAl2O4:Eu2+.

Models

Several models can be employed to explain the PersL phenomenon in long persistent phosphors developed by various researchers over time,4,21 which include the electron trapping–detrapping model (and conduction band engineering), hole trapping–detrapping model (and valence band engineering), self-electron/hole trapping–detrapping model (and band gap engineering), quantum tunnelling model, and oxygen vacancy model.

Mechanism overview

Although the mechanism and attributes of the emission in phosphors, including the trap nature, type, depth, energy levels of bands and traps can vary to a certain extent, they may be considered to follow the same mechanism according to conventional definitions, where the overall mechanism can be generalised in the following steps:4,19,20

1. Excitation of an electron to the conduction band

2. Trapping of electrons/holes in traps

3. De-trapping of electrons/holes from traps

4. Recombination of electrons and holes.

Nevertheless, it is crucial to note that new specific mechanisms have been formulated, and many former mechanisms have been modified and/or discarded simultaneously based on the recent advancements in research for specific crystal structures.

The energy provided for excitation determines the transition energy levels. Given that the difference in dissimilar energy states is different, the emission energy also is variable, changing the colour. The energy levels of the traps between the energy levels may also change the emission wavelength and can also be utilised in tuning the emission. The variation in trap depth determines the time of LPPs, where very shallow traps result in a very short emission time, and subsequently an increase in the trap depth increases the persistence of afterglow. The delay in emission is determined by the depth of the trap. The depth of traps is related to the energy levels of the dopants. If the traps are accessible at high temperatures, the emission will be attained only at subsequent high temperatures and if the traps are very shallow, then the afterglow is lost instantly even at room temperature. By carefully monitoring the reaction condition and reactants, we can tune the phosphorescence according to the requirement. The afterglow can be introduced easily via doping or introduction of impurities. The exact mechanism involves simple electron inducing charge carriers to complex ionic entities, trapping or relaying the charge carriers. Slow de-trapping is maintained by the high trapping capacity, which usually requires a higher temperature than room temperature. The energy level of traps should be comparable to that of the conduction band or valence band for better PersL.

(1) Electron trapping–detrapping model. This model focuses on the trapping of electrons. The electrons are excited from the valence band of the host/matrix to its conduction band and the electron traps are located lower than the conduction band. These traps take up the electron and retain it for some time, varying according to the depth. The electrons are detrapped only on acquiring the appropriate amount of thermal energy. The subsequent delayed release of electrons results in delayed phosphorescence.22,23 The electron trapping–detrapping model is illustrated in Fig. 2, where the electrons are represented by a small blue circle and their movement can be traced by the arrows, starting from the excitation from the valence band, leading to the appropriate trap. Their subsequent release by a tunnelling process leads to delayed emission, and hence PersL.

(2) Hole trapping–detrapping model. The excitation of an electron to the conduction band creates a hole in the valence band behind it, which is free to be relayed within the valence band. This model focuses on the trapping of the hole created by the excited electron. The hole traps are located above the valence band. The hole traps capture the holes by providing an electron to the valence band and creating a hole in the dopant itself. This process leads to the temporary unavailability of any electron acceptor. The electrons in the valence band are taken up by the traps to enable the hole to obtain an electron, generally by thermal agitation. The recombination of the de-trapped hole and excited electron results in an emission after some delay.24,25 This hole trapping–detrapping model is summarized diagrammatically in Fig. 3, where the hole is represented by a small white circle, created and trapped upon the excitation of an electron by a hole trap. The delayed release of this hole leads to delayed and persistent emission.

The PersL duration and efficiency are determined by the trap depth and the trap density distribution for both the electron traps and hole traps. The traps are selected based on the band gap of the host, which is the difference between the conduction band and the valence band. Various theoretical studies on these parameters helped to determine the correct host nature and the activating lanthanide ions to be doped.26

Thermoluminescence (TL) measurement is usually considered a powerful tool for calculating the trap level positions. The trap depth and nature are studied by complementing the TL properties with the following theoretical formula:

I(T) = Aexp(−ET/kT)
where I(T) is TL intensity, K denotes the Boltzmann constant, T is the temperature (K) of the peaks observed and ET is the trap depth. In TL studies, the plot of ln(IT) vs. 1/T gives the trap depth (ET) as the slope of the fitted straight line.26,27a The calculated data from the TL peak corresponds to the trap level positions.

(3) Quantum tunnelling model. Quantum tunnelling refers to the quantum mechanical phenomenon where a particle tunnels through a barrier that it classically cannot and this process may occur between the traps and near activator excited state levels. In contrast to the CB–VB model, this model does not require physical proximity to the trap level and the CB/VB. This model has been used to explain the afterglow mechanism related to deep traps. In the early days (1958), the existence of quantum tunnelling in sulfide phosphors was proposed by Hoogeustraten et al.27b Later, a similar model related to SrAl2O4:Eu2+,Dy3+ LPPs was also proposed by Tanabe et al.27c by measuring the photocurrent excitation spectra, and it was found that the divalent europium 5d level at the divalent Sr site with a weak crystal field were located in the conduction band. They concluded that the effective path for trapping and de-trapping is accomplished by the quantum tunnelling path not through the conduction or valence band.
(4) Oxygen vacancy model. The oxygen vacancy model was proposed to account for the trapping and de-trapping progress by evaluating the EPR spectra. The oxygen vacancy model requires oxygen vacancies as electron traps to capture electrons, which is appropriate for analyzing the afterglow mechanism of oxide-based LPPs. This model was built based on the identification and confirmation of trap types, besides the CB/VB and quantum tunnelling models.

Different types of synthesis methods used for LPP synthesis

Conventionally, solid-state materials are synthesized via the high-temperature method. However, although they are still prevalent, several alternative synthesis methods have been developed to enhance and diversify the synthesis and the properties of the materials. Some of the contemporary methods are described below.

High-temperature solid-state reactions

High-temperature reactions are performed in silica and quartz crucibles. Annealing in the form of compressed pellets is also prevalent for better contact -with the starting materials during the synthesis process. Generally, high temperatures ranging from 200–1600 °C are required depending on the nature of the desired materials. For example, 900 °C for Ca2BO3Cl: Eu2+,Dy3+;28 and 1100–1250 °C for Ba5Si8O21:Eu2+,Dy3+.29 These high temperatures are attained in muffle furnaces and tube furnaces. Some of these furnaces, especially tube furnaces, coupled with appropriate control systems and gas (hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen) connections are equipped to control the reaction conditions when an inert, reducing or oxidising atmosphere is needed.

A reducing atmosphere is needed when the dopants or any reactants are readily oxidizable and the synthesis is targeted to attain ions with lower valences, such as Eu2+ in (Sr1−δBaδ)2MgSi2O7:Eu2+,Dy3+;30 and Ca2BO3Cl:Eu2+,Dy3+.28 The oxidising atmosphere is also coupled with a reducing atmosphere depending on the desired materials, such as Na3ScSi3O9:Ce3+,Dy3+.31 Inert conditions are needed when the ions may be unstable during the synthesis reactions such as CaAlSiN3:Eu2+.32

Hydrothermal/solvothermal

As its name suggests, this method involves the synthesis route via solvents, one of the most common being water. Solvothermal synthesis involves the dispersion of materials in the reaction solvent reacting at high temperature in closed reactors/autoclaves, which generates some pressure for the reaction. The solvothermal method is best suited for reactions involving pH control by acids such as acetic acid or bases such as sodium hydroxide and sodium hydrate to form the desired final product at comparatively lower temperatures.33–36 β-NaYF4:Tb3+,37 Y2O2S:Eu3+,M2+ (M = Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba), Ti4+ are a few phosphors that can be synthesized by the hydrothermal method.38

Combustion method

This is an exothermic synthesis process, which involves the use of an oxidiser such as metal aluminates and fuels, most commonly, urea. In combustion synthesis, stoichiometric ratios of the starting materials are employed and oxidiser and fuel are added to them. Since the latter components are hygroscopic, a paste is formed. This paste is taken in a China dish or any other heat- and fire-resistant material and heated to flame temperature or ignition temperature, at which the prepared paste catches fire, which ceases in a few seconds. This method removes most of the impurities, and generally produces smaller particles compared to other synthesis routes.39–41

Sr3SiO5:Eu2+,Nb;26 Ca4LaO(BO3)3:Eu3+ and Ca4EuO(BO3)342 are some of the lanthanide-activated long persistent phosphors discussed in this review, which are synthesized by the combustion method.

Sol–gel synthesis

Sol–gel synthesis results in the formation of solid-formed xerogels. These xerogels are formed using a stoichiometric ratio of the starting materials, including the dopants, in acid, which are later neutralised and the reaction pH is adjusted to around 7 using basic solutions. Different acids such as HNO3, HCl, and citric acid are utilised depending on the reaction requirements, where generally ammonia solution is used to neutralise the reaction pH. The initial acidic reactant solution is heated at a temperature of around 60–80 °C to initially form wet gels, which convert to xerogels at a comparatively higher temperature of around 120 °C. The formed xerogels can later be sintered at higher temperatures of 500–1200 °C, depending on the desired material to form the phosphors.43–46 CaMgSi2O6:Eu2+,Mn2+,Pr3+,47 and Sr2ZnSi2O7:Eu2+,Dy3+48 are some of the lanthanide-activated long persistent phosphors that are synthesized via sol–gel synthesis.49

Co-precipitation process

As hinted by its name, this method involves the precipitation of the desired phosphor after interaction. This synthesis route primarily involves the preparation of a separate aqueous metal salt solution (most commonly metal nitrates), where HNO3 is used for formation of nitrates of metal oxides. These separate solutions are mixed, forming a solid precipitate. Generally, this process is facilitated by some precipitating reagents or high temperatures in some cases. Additionally, this process can be combined with other methods such as hydrothermal, sintering or even microwave heating to obtain the desired phosphor.50–52 LaAlO3:Cr3+,Sm3+,53 Y3Al2Ga3O12:Ce3+,Cr3+,Nd3+ are a few afterglow phosphors synthesized using the sol–gel method.54

Czochralski method (growing single crystals)

This method was named after Polish scientist Jan Czochralski, who discovered it in 1915. The Czochralski method is applied to form single crystals of different materials. This method involves heating finely ground starting materials in a platinum crucible at a temperature slightly higher than their melting point, forming a melt of the desired product. The single crystal are formed in the desired orientation by the introduction of the seed particle of the same target material in the melt at high temperature. The crystallisation is uniform under controlled and consistent rate of cooling of the reaction melt. Subsequently, the obtained large crystal can be cut into the desired shape based on the requirements.55,56 This method has been successfully utilised for the preparation of phosphors such as LiNbO3:Mg2+,Pr3+.57

Applications of long persistent phosphors

Long persistent phosphors (LPP) have been used for different applications, such as lighting, signalling, optical data storage, military applications, bio-imaging, anti-counterfeiting, and lasers (Fig. 4).

Lightning

Generally, solid-state phosphors are widely used in commercial lighting applications. The afterglow property of certain phosphors offers an enhancement in lightning applications. Subsequently, the afterglow property can be tuned to conserve energy, given that these materials can show a long afterglow compared to the initial charging irradiation periods. In display screens the wide variety of colours accessible in afterglow phosphors offers a range for selection and leads to display screens with not only different colours but also different afterglow times, offering visual delight together with the target purpose. Y3Al2Ga3O12:Ce3+,Cr3+,58 SrSi2AlO2N3:Eu2+,Ln3+ (Ln = Nd, Dy, Ho, Er, and Tm),59 and Y3Sc2Ga3−xAlxO12:Ce3+ can be potentially used for lightning and display applications.60 The afterglow spectacle is applied in the production of afterglow toys, which seem to catch the attention of children.

Signalling

Long persistent phosphors, especially those that can be charged by sunlight irradiation or have a short irradiation charging time are utilised for signalling in the dark. They are commonly observed as background screens on watches, as road markings, making an effort towards safe travelling, etc. The signalling application is modified for additional use in emergency signalling, which is most commonly used for signalling any danger. Afterglow signalling is successfully used for safety signalling given that it can be utilised without any additional power consumption. Long persistent phosphors such as BaZrSi3O9:Eu2+,Pr3+,61 and CaAl2O4:Eu2+,Nd3+ + Y3Al5O12:Ce3+ can be potentially used for signalling applications.62

Optical data storage/military applications

Afterglow phosphors have been successfully utilised for optical data storage due to their characteristic property. This application is greatly used for secret coded message transfer in the military. Phosphors can also be utilised in ballistic and thermal imaging such as SrAl2O4:Eu2+,Dy3+,63,64 Ba2SiO4:Eu2+,Ho3+,65,66 and Sr3SiO5:Eu2+,Nb.26

Bio-imaging applications

Many near infra-red (NIR) after glowing phosphors have attracted attention for their utility in bio-imaging. Given that the NIR glowing regions can be uninterrupted by any visible tissues surrounding them, this range offers an advancement in bio-medical applications. Phosphors such as CaMgSi2O6:Eu2+,Mn2+,Pr3+,47,49 and LaAlO3:Cr3+,Sm3+ have been utilised for the same purpose.53

Anti-counterfeiting

The use of phosphor materials for the successful detection of counterfeiting currency is one of the recent applications of lanthanide based phosphor materials. Given that the emission of lanthanide is very sharp, coupling it with tuned excitation requirements, it can be applied as for counterfeiting detection. The phosphors used for these applications do not emit by visible range energy. Often, the use of some laser excitation reveals different patterns on the currency. Some of the phosphors that can be utilised are BaTiO3:Er3+,Er3+–Ho3+,Yb3+–Tm3+;12 and SrBi4Ti4O15:Er3+ (SBTO:Er3+).67a Among the available techniques, the luminescent printing practice has gained much attention due to its distinctions of easy and scalable production, simple operation, and most importantly tuneable and customizable optical performance. Indeed, luminescent inks excitable by ultraviolet (UV) light have been widely used as anti-counterfeiting marks on banknotes and medicine packaging.

Encouraged by the multi-color long afterglow feature, the NaCa2GeO4F:Tb3+ (NCGOF:Tb3+) phosphor67b can be used for anti-counterfeiting applications. Furthermore, the letters “LZU” and a smiley face were also printed by mixing three different phosphor concentrations. The two images showed dissimilar colors, indicating that the currently studied phosphor (NCGOF:Tb3+) has good potential for anti-counterfeiting (Fig. 5d).

Lasers

Laser devices have found many applications in various fields such as displays, projection, data storage, communications, and biomedical field. The phosphors applied in lasers need to be stable, have different emission wavelengths, high intensity, longer emission time, etc. Some of the phosphor materials that can be employed as lasers include BaMgAl10O17:Eu2+;68 and Y3Al5O12:Ce3+.69

Lanthanide-activated inorganic LPPs

Lanthanides are a series of elements generally represented as [Xe] 4f0–14, having remarkable optical properties.70 The filling of electrons in the 4f orbitals of lanthanides can be represented as 4f0→14: from 4f0 (La) to 4f14 (Lu). Due to their compact energy levels, resembling a hierarchy, lanthanides can generate a continuous emission in the UV-visible to NIR region.5,58,71 The luminescence effects of the lanthanides are specifically caused by the electron redistribution in their 4f orbitals, given that the influences of the ligand field are essentially minimized by the shielding effects of the 6s and 5p orbitals.

The Laporte selection rule forbids electronic transitions in different levels of 4f orbitals, and thus the radiant states of lanthanides are occupied in the presence of energy transfer from a sensitizing host.72,73 Thus, the lanthanide emission centres and host materials are both highly significant and directly affect and regulate the emission of doped nanocrystals. Intersystem crossing and energy transfer processes between the host lattice and lanthanides are the infrequently observed phenomenon of long-lived luminescence in lanthanide-doped compounds. August 1996 marked a breakthrough for LPPs, shifting the focus to a previously overlooked area, as a result of the published works of the research and development (R&D) of the Nemoto group. The discovery of the after-glowing properties of SrAl2O4 attracts attention from many researchers in this field, even after two decades since its first registration.74 The alkaline earth metals in the host lattice show considerate afterglow properties. Sr2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ was first reported back in 1996, and since then it has been studied and improved extensively.75–77 Some of the other silicate-, phosphate- and aluminate-based blue LPPs are Lu2SiO5:Ce;78 Sr3Al10SiO20:Eu2+;79 Ca9Bi(PO4)7:Ce3+,Tb3+,Mn2+;80 and CaAl2O4:Eu2+,Nd3+.81 Red long persistent phosphors are rare compared to blue and green and are attracting research focus due to their unparalleled application fields of red display and biomedical applications in the near IR region by tuning some transitions. Initially, Eu2+/3+ was highly explored for red phosphors, but recently other lanthanides, especially Sm3+ have also been gaining focus for red emissions. Some of the prominent red phosphors are CaS:Eu2+,Dy3+;82 Ca2SnO4:Sm3+;83 SrCaSi5N8:Eu2+,Tm3+84 and Y2O2S:Eu3+,Mg2+,Ti4+.85 Warm colour emitting phosphors are uncommon and rarely activated by sunlight, which is currently limiting but emphasizing enhancement in this range of emission. Warm colours are more important in emergency signalling due to their higher emission wavelength and lower scattering. Phosphates are exceptionally compatible with persistent afterglow. Some other yellow LPPs are Ca2ZnS2O7:Eu2+,Dy3+86 and Gd3Al2Ga3O12:Ce3+.87

Although theoretically all colours can be available by controlling the emission and mixing of blue, green, yellow and red emissions of different phosphors, it is generally not observed in applications and only some primary colours are commercialised to date. This has increased the interest of researchers in controlling the emissions of other colours such as white, orange, and cyan, which can be applied in various other fields. White light is seldom investigated due to the involvement of simultaneous multiple emissions required for mixing and its generation, due to chances of non-uniformity and requirement of significant levels of lattice defects. Some of the reported white afterglow phosphors are CdSiO3:Dy3+88 and CaxMgSi2O5+x:Dy3+ (x = 1, 2, 3).89

The emissions of some phosphors can be controlled at a larger wavelength difference by varying the concentration and nature of the activating ions, which may also affect the duration significantly. LPP has been utilised in many fields, where among them bio-imaging is a crucial and emerging field. Many phosphors have been tested, and subsequently improved over the years for bio-imaging as nano-probes (more suitable due to small size).47,49

Examples of long persistent phosphors

Garnet-based long persistent phosphors. Garnets, belonging to the nesosilicate group of materials, generally occur as minerals in metamorphic rocks. Most often due to its myriad of colours, this mineral is used as a gemstone. Furthermore, given that its crystal structure, X3Y2Z3O12, is highly stable, it has found applications in lasers and phosphors.90 Several garnets with Ce-activation show green emissions.60,91 This section deals with some garnet structures, comprised mainly of gallium and germanium in the host matrix.

A garnet type green-emitting phosphor, Ce3+-activated Y3Sc2Ga3−xAlxO12 garnet, was prepared via the traditional solid-state synthesis method involving high temperature and monitored atmosphere.60 It can be potentially used in LED devices. This garnet phosphor crystallised in a cubic structure with cell parameters of a = b = c = 12.4801 Å and belong to Iad. It was established from the crystallographic analysis that Ga exists in two dissimilar sites with 4 and 6 coordination sites and speculated to create defects that are responsible for promoting afterglow, as shown in Fig. 8(a). It can be inferred from the PLE and PE spectra of varying concentrations that for Y3−xSc2Ga3O12:xCe3+, x = 0.04 has the maximum intensity, after which concentration quenching occurs. The excitation occurs from the ground energy level of Ce3+ to the two d excited levels, resulting in two excitation peaks, obtained by the deconvolution of the observed emission spectra. The emission is theoretically estimated to be from the 2F7/2 and 2F5/2 levels of Ce3+. The PLE and PL spectra of the phosphor changed with varying concentrations of Ce3+ as well as the ratio of Ga and Al. Further, substitution of Ga with Al in the crystal lattice led to a slight red shift in the spectra, which is ascribed to the change in the crystal environment energy variation of the 5d–4f transition of Ce3+. The smaller radius of Al changes the Ce3+ surrounding on complete substitution of the Ga sites in the structure. The afterglow spectra correspond to the PL spectra at 495 nm (Fig. 8(b)), but the atmosphere affects the spectra to a great extent in this phosphor, given that it affects the generation of defects. The comparison of the emission spectra in different environments led to further studies in an O2 environment due to the notable results (Fig. 8(c)). Temperature-dependent emission studies revealed the presence of various shallow and deep traps together. The decay curves at variable temperatures and Al concentration denoted that the matrix affected the spectra, increasing the high-temperature band for high Al. In the lattice, different defects may act as electron and hole traps. Defects such as antisite defects act as hole traps and the oxygen vacancy defects act as electron trap centres. Here, Ce3+ is both the activator and trap source. The host acts as a carrier and transfers energy to Ce3+ from the traps after the excitation source has ceased to obtain afterglow. The increased amount of Al in the lattice increased the crystal field strength and the bandgap and high concentrations of Al can eventually reduce the generation of traps and afterglow.60

Another green-emitting garnet phosphor, Ce3+- and Cr3+-doped Y3Al2Ga3O12, was identified as a transparent phosphor-in-glass to be twice as efficient as a solid powder for this garnet proportion.91 Similar garnets have also been studied in the form of transparent ceramics by S. Tanabe et al.93–95 The phosphor-in-glass showed improved afterglow characteristics due to reduced scattering. The single-phased phosphor was synthesized via conventional solid-state reactions. The compound crystallized in a cubic crystal structure,91 with cell parameters of a = b = c = 12.1829 Å. (The phosphor-in-glass phosphors were synthesized via the low-temperature co-sintering technique.) The cubic lattice consisted of a 4-fold, 6-fold coordination around Al3+, Ga3+ and 8-fold coordination around Y3+ ions, as illustrated in Fig. 8(d). The Cr3+ dopant occupied the octahedral sites of Al3+ or Ga3+, while Ce3+ preferred the sites occupied by Y3+ due to their similarity in size. The PLE spectra showed the presence of two excitation bands, estimated to arise from the 4f–5d transition of Ce3+ (activator). Concentration quenching was observed at concentrations above 0.5% mol of Ce3+. The emissions in the co-doped samples became more independent due to the separation in energy levels due to the presence of Cr3+. The introduction of Cr3+ in the lattice also opens an avenue of persistence due to its ability to trap charge carriers. Successful green afterglow was achieved upon irradiation with blue light. The emission was observed for up to 50 min from some of the prepared compositions. Persistent luminescence was found to be significantly dependent on the excitation wavelength, attaining a peak at nearly 435 nm. The traps were located uniformly at a depth in the range of 0.71 to 1.02 eV, which was verified due to the shift in the TL spectra with time. Some deep traps were also present, which released electrons at high temperature. According to the mechanism, UV and blue radiation excited the 4f electrons in Ce3+ to the conduction band via the 5d levels, from where electrons were captured by a series of traps. The presence of Cr3+ increased the number of traps and also provided a tunnel between the shallow and deep traps. The recombination of free electrons and conduction bands led to green emissions. The release of electrons from the deep traps could also be stimulated by NIR radiation. Due to its excitation lying in the blue emission range, it can potentially be used in display applications.

In 2019, Huimin Li et al. reported the synthesis of a Pr3+-activated Ba2Ga2GeO7 long persistent white phosphor.92 The synthesized phosphor was crystallised in the tetragonal lattice with cell parameters of a = b (Å) = 8.21766, c (Å) = 5.65548, α = β = γ (°) = 90. Three types of coordination polyhedra existed in the system including dodecahedral BaO8 and tetrahedral GaO4 and GeO4 (Fig. 8(d)). Pr3+ has multiple low-lying (compared to other frequently used activators) 4f energy levels, which can contribute to multiple excitations theoretically and verified experimentally for varying intense multiple emissions in a specific lattice environment. The 4f–4f transitions of Pr3+ are the reason for the long persisting phosphorescence. The PL and PLE spectra of the Pr3+-activated Ba2Ga2GeO7 phosphors, as illustrated in Fig. 8(f), indicated that the absorption intensity increased with an increase in Pr3+ concentration. The bands at 491 nm (blue) and 650 nm (red) are generated from the 3P0 → 3H4 and 3P0 → 3F2 transitions, respectively. Pr3+ exhibited its characteristic f–f transitions given that its d-levels are higher and cannot take electrons from low-lying traps. The persistent luminescence, observed by the naked eyes, by irradiation for 5 min under a 254 nm UV lamp, lasted for 3 h.

A novel Sm3+-doped Na2CaSn2Ge3O12, having a garnet-like structure was established for its reddish-orange emission (due to 4G7/2 → 6H5/2 and 4G5/2 → 6H7/2 emissions).96 Stannate-based lattices were selected as the host mainly due to their chemical stability and versatility compared to sulphides, oxides, etc.97,98 The garnet crystallised in a cubic lattice (space group Iad) with cell parameters of a = b = c = 1.537 Å. The tetrahedral GeO4 and octahedral SnO6 surround each other in all the phases given that all the corners are shared, forming the skeleton (Fig. 9(a)). The PLE spectra of Na2CaSn2Ge3O12:Sm3+ denote the broad excitation of the host in the range of 230 to 300 nm, confirming the energy transfer between the host and Sm3+. The excitation spectra also have narrow peaks between 340 to 500 nm, arising from the 4f–4f transition of Sm3+. In the PL spectra, four sets of transitions were recorded, occurring from de-excitation to lower energy levels in the range of 550–720 nm, as depicted in Fig. 9(b), owing to the reddish-orange emissions, which had the CIE colour coordinates of (0.55, 0.40). Na2CaSn2Ge3O12 showed different colours on activation by different lanthanides. The Dy3+-activated white phosphor Na2CaSn2Ge3O12 was studied for its afterglow properties, which can - potentially be applied in optical storage applications.99 Na+ and Ca2+ form octahedral coordination, which are possibly replaced by the Dy3+ dopants, causing vacancy defects to neutralise the overall lattice. The PL excitation spectra of Na2CaSn2Ge3O12:Dy3+ show a broad charge transfer band for stannate (230–290 nm) and sharp weak 4f–4f peaks (290–400 nm).

The PL emission spectra showed sharp characteristic peaks in the range of 400–700 nm, which can be group into 450–500 nm, 575–600 nm and 650–700 nm (Fig. 9(c)), indicating crystal field splitting, simultaneous emission leading to white colour. Dy3+ did not affect the shape but enhanced the intensity of the emission peaks. The persistent emission for this Dy3+-doped garnet was observed for more than 7.8 h after the initial UV irradiation. The TL studies revealed the presence of four traps. The traps at 0.28 and 0.40 eV were assigned to internal defects (Na+, Ca2+ vacancy) and acted as hole capture centres. The other two traps at 0.98 and 0.65 eV were assigned to foreign defects (Dy3+ replacing Na+, Ca2+) and acted as electron trapping centres. The mechanism for afterglow in this white phosphor involved the above-mentioned traps after UV excitation for trapping the charge carriers and observing delayed emission after deferred recombination of released electrons and holes.

Pr3+-activated pink gallogermanate, La3GaGe5O16, crystallises in a triclinic structure with the parameters of a = 4.827 Å, b = 8.090 Å, c = 15.707 Å, α = 90.76°, β = 94.01°, and γ = 90.01°.102,105 The structure of the phosphor is composed of a [GeO6] octahedra, [GeO4] tetrahedra and [GaO4] tetrahedra. Each [GeO6] octahedron is attached to six tetrahedra via common corners and each tetrahedron bridges two octahedral (Fig. 9(d)). Self-trapping vacancies were formed during the synthesis process involving GeO2. The PLE spectra showed an intense band for the 4f–5d transition compared to the weak 4f–4f transitions. The PL spectra were structured by two sets of transitions in the range of 470–510 nm and 580–620 nm, as demonstrated in Fig. 9(e). With a variation in the concentration of dopant, the usual trend was observed with concentration quenching after 0.02. The persistent afterglow in La3GaGe5O16:Pr3+ was observed for 3 min. The theoretical studies suggest the fitting of two Gaussian curves in the TL spectra, representing the trap depths at 0.71 and 0.78 eV.

There could be intrinsic defects related to O and Ge vacancies. The Pr in the mixed-valent in the lattice, present as Pr3+ and Pr4+, generates additional defects such as oxygen vacancies, which can act as hole traps in the host lattice. The excitation leads to the separation of electrons and holes in the conduction and valence band. The electrons can now directly radiate or be trapped in either shallow or deep traps, recombining after some delay. The characteristic emission is from 3P0 to 3F2 and 3H. The deep-lying traps are responsible for the slow afterglow.

Mn2+, Yb3+-doped NaCa2GeO4F, crystallizing in a garnet structure, was synthesized by the traditional high-temperature solid-state methods, in which successful colour tuning was achieved by adjusting the sites of Mn2+ emitters and speculated to have potential for optical storage applications.104 NaCa2GeO4F:Mn2+,Yb3+ conformed in a single-phase orthorhombic arrangement with cell parameters of a = 5.3540(1) Å, b = 7.2861(0) Å, c = 12.63511(3) Å, α = β = γ = 90°. Two separate octahedral sites for Na+ and Ca2+ were observed with a tetrahedral site for Ge4+, as illustrated in Fig. 9(f). Based on the chemical behaviour of Mn2+ and Yb3+, they tend to occupy the octahedral sites over the tetrahedral sites. The lowest band gap between the conduction and valence band was observed to be 4.95 eV. The vacancies and co-dopants act as a trap in the lattice, possibly enhancing the luminescence and PersL.

The PL spectra of NaCa2GeO4F:Mn2+ showed a clear emission in the red region. Upon the incorporation of Yb3+ in the phosphor composition and evaluation of different concentrations, it was observed that at low concentration, the spectral intensity varied with a fixed position, while at higher concentration, an evident blue shift of up to 50 nm was observed from red to green–yellow region, as depicted in Fig. 9(g). The green colour was estimated to arise from Mn2+ occupying the tetrahedral sites of Ge4+, which occurred at a higher concentration of Yb3+ to maintain the charge neutrality and subsequent generation of vacancies in the lattice. The afterglow decay spectra denoted the initial rapid phase, followed by slow decay, with the process lasting for a maximum of 13 h when charged by a UV excitation source and 5 h when charged by natural sunlight for the specific proportion of NaCa2GeO4F:0.2%Mn2+,0.4%Yb3+. The colour-tuned green–yellow was a weak persistent phosphor. The TL study showed that the traps greatly depended on the temperature. The shift in the spectra was ascribed to the continuous and broad trap distribution and localisation of electrons in deeper traps with an increase in temperature (Fig. 9(h)). The decrease in intensity is a result of shallow traps, easily releasing the charge carriers at high temperature. The estimate of trap depth was in the range of 0.52 to 1.15 eV. However, the actual trap description was unexplored here.

Silicate-based long persistent phosphors. Silicate-based materials are generally comprised of a tetrahedral coordination of silicon and oxygen. The stable structure can provide a stable host matrix for feasible applications.106 A remarkable green phosphor, Eu2+, Ho3+-activated orthosilicate, Ba2SiO4, was developed by Chuanlong Wang et al., which may have possible application in the field of optical information storage.65 The designed phosphor could continuously radiate green afterglow for more than 24 h. The Eu2+, Ho3+-doped barium ortho-silicate was synthesized via the traditional solid-state technique and a film was formed with silica gel. The phosphor crystallised in an orthorhombic structure and the crystal structure is illustrated in Fig. 10(a). The experimental bandgap for the host lattice was determined to be 5.481 eV. Theoretically, the bandgap was optimum for Eu2+ to act as an activator and for Ho3+ to act as a trap. Eu2+ most likely substitutes Ba2+ at the lattice site due to their similar size. The broad PLE spectra (peaking at 350 nm) were attained due to the 4f–5d transition of Eu2+. The PL spectra illustrated a peak in the range of 430–650 nm. The nature of the PLE and PL spectra remained unaffected with the addition of Ho3+, denoting its role as a trap centre, which also decreased the initial emission intensity due to unequal substitution in the lattice. Fig. 10(b) depicts the PerL decay curves at varying concentrations and optical images of Ba2SiO4:0.005Eu2+,0.01Ho3+ emission up to 24 h. The TL spectra of the Ho3+-doped and undoped samples were very different, signifying its role in trap generation. The positive traps generated by Ho3+ were speculated by charge non-equivalence with Ba2+. In the generated multi-layered traps, the deep traps were filled before the shallow traps. The trap depth was continuous in the range of 0.71 to 0.86 eV. The stimulation after a few hours after the first excitation led to the redistribution of the trapped carriers from deep to shallow traps, enhancing the emission intensity. The stimulated release from deep traps of by Ba2SiO4:Eu2+,Ho3+ could be utilised for optical information storage, which in turn has many applications.

Alkaline earth metals such as magnesium and barium in the host lattice show considerable afterglow properties.109 Sr2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ was first reported in 1996, and since then studied and improved extensively over time.75–77 Erin Finley et al. optimised the long-time luminescence of the Sr2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ blue phosphor for use in point-of-care diagnostics.30 It was synthesized via the convention solid-state method, attaining a phosphor with an unidentified impurity and tetragonal system with the cell parameters of a = b = 8.0071 Å, c = 5.1674 Å and Rietveld refinement parameters of Rp = 0.0829, Rwp = 0.1179 and χ2 = 4.453. The unit cell is formed of alternating tetrahedral of SiO4 and MgO4, forming polyhedral sheets incorporating Sr2+ between them, as shown in Fig. 10(c). Sr only has one distinct position in the crystal lattice, making the emission of phosphor narrow. This is a disilicate producing blue–green emission at 450–475 nm. (Sr1−δBaδ)2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ and SrAl2O4:Eu2+,Dy3+ were explored for the enhancement of LFA (lateral flow assay) for time-gated imaging using smartphones. The substitution of Eu2+ is at the site of Sr/Ba in the lattice as the emission centre for the luminescent properties. Replacement of the 1S0 valent Sr by Ba in the lattice resulted in a blue shift due the increase in the resultant radius of the unit cell, which affected the luminescence properties due to the decrease in the crystal field splitting and greater separation of the energy levels. Blue emission was observed in (Sr1−δBaδ)2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ samples, which showed long persistence for up to 9 min after the excitation ceased. The afterglow characteristic was further boosted by the introduction of Dy3+ co-dopant in the lattice. Fig. 10(d) illustrates the time-gated luminescence imaging and intensity profile scan of (Sr1−δBaδ)2MgSi2O7:Eu2+ and (Sr1−δBaδ)2MgSi2O7:Eu2+,Dy3+. Dy3+ prolonged the emission lifetime by ∼3 min compared to the samples doped with only Eu2+. The TL spectra confirmed the presence of traps states in all the phosphor phases in the range of 240 K to 371 K. The trap depths generated here were 0.40 and 0.89 eV, which are in the optimum range for LPP to occur at RT. Some other traps were also present, resulting in a slight variation in the intensity and range of the emission. Blue and green emissions were observed on excitation by a smartphone flashlight. It is also worth noting that the green constituent was dominated by blue emission.

Bluish–green Eu2+, Nd3+-doped BaLu2Si3O10 silicate phosphors were synthesized via high-temperature solid-state reaction and studied for their optical properties by Jie Liu et al. The synthesized phosphor crystallised in a monoclinic crystal.108 The structural analysis revealed that the lattice consists of Ba2+ in 8 coordination and Lu3+ in 6 coordination and Si in the tetrahedral (SiO4) site in the crystal structure, as shown in Fig. 10(e). The bandgap was theoretically estimated to be 4.843 eV. Further, according to the Brillouin zone study, the bandgap of the crystal was in the appropriate range to use Eu2+ as the emission centre and Nd3+ as the trap centre. Broad excitation (λex = 426 nm) was observed from 250 to 400 nm. This range contained the excitation peaks of Eu2+, which on introduction in the lattice occupies the Ba2+ and Lu3+ sites. The emission peaks centred at 430 and 507 nm, obtained from emission from two different types of sites (430 nm from Ba centre and 507 nm from Lu centre) resulted in the blue colour of the phosphor, as shown in Fig. 10(f). The BaLu2Si3O10:Eu2+,Nd3+ phosphor acted as a single doped crystal, given that Nd3+ had little or no interference with the emission wavelength and increased only the intensity of the peaks. The phosphorescence colour of BaLu2Si3O10:Eu2+,Nd3+ was bluish-green, owing to the 5d–4f transitions of Eu2+. Eu2+ in the Lu sites had a greater impact on the long persistence, making the phosphorescence appear green. The introduction of Nd3+ (occupying Ba centres) introduced many defects in the lattice due to the asymmetry, which can act as traps. These traps increase the persistence of phosphorescence from 60 s for BaLu2Si3O10:Eu2+ to over 6 h for BaLu2Si3O10:Eu2+,Nd3+, which can be used for several applications. According to the thermo-luminescence study, it was speculated that the emission was enhanced by the Nd3+ centres, which is likely to be due to the defects introduced. In the case of BaLu2Si3O10:Eu2+,Nd3+, its colour changed from blue to bluish–green during phosphorescence. In its structure, Eu2+ occupies the Ba and Lu centres, whereas Nd3+ occupies the Ba sites and it acts as an effective trapping centre. On providing excitation energy, Eu2+ is excited from 4f7 to 4f6d1. A part of the Eu2+ present in the Ba sites emits blue-coloured luminescence. The excited electrons in the conduction band are mainly formed by the Lu ion and are captured by some shallow or deep Nd3+ traps. Upon the thermal release of electrons from the traps, the electrons revert to the conduction band by quantum tunnelling, and ultimately show emission from the Eu2+ in the Lu3+ sites.

Zhizhen Wang et al. explored the effects of enhanced afterglow properties by incorporating transition metals in the Eu2+-activated silicate–phosphor Sr3SiO5 to obtain exceptional yellow afterglow.26 Sr3SiO5 crystallised as a tetragonal lattice belonging to the P4/ncc space group, containing two different 6-coordinated Sr crystallographic sites and one 4-coordinating Si site, as shown in Fig. 11(c). The introduction of various transition metals Sc3+, Hf4+, Zr4+, Ti4+, and Nb5+ replaced Si4+ in the lattice due to the larger deviation in ionic radius with Sr2+. Among the metals, Nb5+ showed the best results. The transition metals improved the afterglow of the phosphors from 0.5 h to more than 14 h in the Nb5+ co-activated phosphors due to the highest electron trap concentrations. Sr3SiO5:Eu2+,Nb5+ could be activated by natural sunlight, which resulted in afterglow for more than 7 h, increasing the range of its applications. The excitation and emission spectra remained unchanged on the incorporation of different transition metals, showing their negligible effect in emission and signifying their role in the trapping mechanism. The excitation band incorporated the 5d–4f transitions and the emission band in the range 500–700 nm peaked at 580 nm, exhibiting yellow emissions. Co-doping with transition metal ions introduced the oxygen valency to compensate for the charge imbalance created in the phosphor. These impurities created a sequence of electron traps, which helped in the persistence of emission, especially for Nb5+. The trap depth in Sr3SiO5:Eu2+,Nb5+ was in the range of 0.67–1.19 eV, which is in the appropriate range of persistent afterglow. These conclusions were inferred by various theoretical and experimental studies such as DFT, EPR, and TLE. Zhizhen Wang et al. investigated the cation replacement approach of Ba/Sr to obtain Eu2+, Nb5+-activated (Sr1−xBax)3SiO5 having warm orange–yellow afterglow observed for over 20 h.110 The introduction of Ba in Sr3SiO5 led to no impure phase generation and crystallised similar to Sr3SiO5 phases, which was verified by XRD studies. Ba substitution in the lattice initially resulted in a red shift in the emission peaks until the concentration x = 0.18, above which a blue shift was observed. The inclusion of Ba in the lattice also resulted in an increase in electron trap depth near the conduction band, which enhanced the duration of the afterglow.111 Thus, Ba plays a dual role in shifting the emission range by increasing the observed time of afterglow. The mechanism is the same as that of Sr3SiO5, which was further verified by some theoretical studies.

Haijie Guo et al. reported the synthesis of a cyan colour-emitting silicate phosphor, BaZrSi3O9:Eu2+,Pr3+, attaining persistent luminescence for over 15 h.61 The silicate host lattice provided stability over the aluminate host. Eu2+ is an established activator for hosts in the whole region. The persistence was speculated to be enhanced by the introduction of trivalent lanthanide ions (RE3+), according to the observations of Clabau.112 Samples with variable concentrations of Eu2+ and Pr3+ were prepared via the solid-state method and they crystallised in a hexagonal structure. The BaO6 and ZrO6 distorted octahedra share an edge, forming an alternating arrangement, while SiO4 forms tetrahedra, with shared corners with BaO6 and ZrO6, as illustrated in Fig. 11(e).113 Due to their similar radius, both Eu2+ and Pr3+ are speculated to substitute Ba2+ over Zr4+ (although the C. N. for both Ba2+ and Zr4+ is 6). The band gap between the conduction band, comprised of 5d levels of Ba and 4d levels of Zr and valence band, primarily formed by 2p state of O was found to be 5.057 eV. The excitation profile coincides with the radiation of the sun, implying its probable activation by solar radiation. The PL spectrum is comparatively sharp, peaking at 478 nm, and producing a distinctive cyan colour, as shown in Fig. 11(f). The emission corresponds to the 5d–4f transitions and the lack of distinguishing peaks of Pr3+ shows its inability to act as an emission centre. The afterglow process can be separated into two phases, an initial fast decay process, followed by delayed slow decay.

The combination of both processes led to delayed emissions, which were observed for up to 15 h. Pr3+ successfully forms sharp-shallow and continuous deep traps. Eu2+, acting as an activator, is excited to the 5d level from the ground 4f orbital. The excited energy levels lie in proximity with the conduction band for easy exchange of electrons. The traps can capture carriers easily from the conduction band. The deep and shallow traps are linked and exchange by the tunnelling effect. After a delayed time on thermal stimulation, the traps get empty and release carriers, which recombine with emission centres to produce the characteristic cyan emission. White-light-emitting long-lasting phosphorescence in SrSiO3:Dy3+ has also been reported.114

Jumpei Ueda et al. devised and synthesized a novel orange silicate phosphor, Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+,Sm3+/Tm3+ based on the vacuum referred binding energy (VRBE) diagram.115 Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+ was discovered by Toda et al., but the problem lies with its persistence, given that there were no effective traps present in the crystal.116 The host Ca3Si2O7 (rankinite mineral) with a monoclinic crystal structure (Fig. 12(a)) was synthesized via the conventional solid-state method. On doping with Eu2+, it replaced Ca2+ in its site due to their similar size. The PLE spectrum of Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+ showed 4f–5d transitions, while for Eu3+, 4f–4f transitions appeared, and these transitions showed a clear dependence on the electronic configuration. A broad charge transfer band was also observed in the range of 200–300 nm for the Eu3+-doped sample. The host lattice band gap was predicted to be 7.32 eV. The Dorenbos model predicted that effective co-dopants are Tm3+ and Sm3+ based on VRBE given that the bandgap between the conduction band and co-dopants was 0.49 and 0.96 eV, respectively. The Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+ sample showed no persistent afterglow, but the same sample co-doped with Tm3+ and Sm3+ show the same emission pattern of afterglow to the luminescence of Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+. The identical emission for PL and PersL confirmed that the mechanism of both are the same, and the role of co-dopants was trapping. The detectable afterglow was observed for up to 50 min for Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+,Tm3+, as illustrated in Fig. 12(b). The TL spectra were analysed for the trap estimation. The TL spectra of Ca3Si2O7:Eu2+ co-doped with Tm3+ and Sm3+ were different in position and nature. The two peaks for the co-doped samples were due to the traps and their depth was found to be 0.60 and 0.81 eV for Tm3+, while that for Sm3+ was 1.08 and 1.34 eV, respectively. Ca2+ has three non-equivalent sites in the lattice. The two TL peaks were speculated to be a result of substitution at the different sites by Tm3+ and Sm3+. Sm3+ formed a deeper trap than Tm3+ and de-trapped very slowly. The excitation of electrons occurs from the 4f orbital of Eu2+ to the conduction band. Then, the electrons from the band are trapped by Tm3+ or Sm3+, which delays the emission due to electron capture, and after subsequent thermal stimulation, the electrons are finally released to the excited levels of Eu3+, from which they return to the ground state, attaining afterglow. Given that the energy gap here corresponded to the same emission range of orange colour, orange afterglow was attained.

Nitride phosphors are thermally stable, and accordingly explored in light-emitting diodes.118 A sequence of Eu2+-doped CaAlSiN3 phosphors was synthesized by Jin Wang et al. to study the effect of UV and NIR simultaneous excitation.32 CaAlSiN3:Eu2+ offered exceptional stability and proficiency as a host matrix. The orbitals of Eu2+ split due to the unsymmetrical environment. However, the emission peak could be fitted by two Gaussian peaks due to the similar concentration of Al and Si. An increase in the concentration of Eu2+ as a dopant shifted the emission band towards a higher wavelength (red shift), which was inferred to be from the greater interaction between Eu2+. The oxygen contamination was unavoidable due to the starting materials, but O2− replaced N3− in the lattice, forming efficient hole traps and inducing persistent afterglow to a great extent. In the study of various phosphors with variable concentrations of Eu2+, the afterglow was acquired for up to 45 min.

Phosphate-based long persistent phosphors

Phosphate systems form flexible crystal structures, which may provide space for slight distortion and lead to the allowance of some forbidden transitions, and hence these systems are explored for emission spectroscopy studies.122 Guifang Ju et al. synthesised and reported Eu2+-doped persistent luminescence after removal of the excitation source for a few seconds in the Ca3Mg3(PO4)4 phosphate phosphor.123 Ca3Mg3(PO4)4 was previously reported to crystallise in the monoclinic orientation. A series of single-phase Ca3−xMg3(PO4)4:xEu2+ was prepared by solid-state reactions, where Eu2+ replaced Ca2+ in the lattice due to their compatible radii. Here, 6, 7 and 8 coordination sites were present, where 7 and 8 (3 types) were solely occupied by Ca2+, while Mg2+ and Ca2+ both occupied the octahedral sites, as shown in Fig. 15(a). The bandgap between the conduction and valence band was determined to be 3.6 eV. The PL spectrum had the optimum proportion, where Ca2.99Mg3(PO4)4:0.01Eu2+ showed a broad peak centring at around 433 nm and the broadness signified the emission from multiple types of sites. The emission occurred due to the 5d–4f transitions. The PLE spectra showed peaks in the region of 250–400 nm, exciting the 4f electrons to 5d orbitals, as illustrated in Fig. 15(b). The afterglow spectrum was consistent with the photoemission spectra, where concentrations above 0.01% increased the emission spectra, but decreased the persistence duration, causing concentration quenching. The 4f7 configuration of Eu2+ was excited to 4f65d1 on UV irradiation. The latter levels lie close to the conduction band, creating an electron escape route. The trapping of charge carriers from the conduction bands occurs together with the usual emission. Once the traps are saturated and the excitation ceased, the traps can release the carriers on thermal stimulation or agitation, causing emission after a delayed time and producing afterglow mediated by the trapping of electrons. The traps were studied using the TL curve (Fig. 15(c)). There is a probability for ionisation of Eu2+ to form Eu3+.

Borate-based long persistent phosphors

A large proportion of borate systems are transparent in the visible region, and therefore significant in optoelectronic applications. Doped borates have been highly explored for applications such as lasers, displays, and dosimeters.139–141 Zhen Chen et al. reported the synthesis of a colour-tunable, borate-based host phosphor,142a Ca4LaO(BO3)3:Eu3+, where its colour could be tuned on increasing the level of the Eu3+ dopant and was independent of concentration quenching (forming Ca4EuO(BO3)3).142b The phosphor was synthesized via the combustion method, producing a pure single-phase monoclinic lattice belonging to the C1m1 space group. The crystal structure of Ca4LaO(BO3)3 is depicted in Fig. 18(a). The host lattice exhibits a broad absorption peak from 200 nm to 400 nm and the bandgap of the matrix was assessed to be 5.63 eV. On doping with Eu2+, a charge transfer absorption band appeared at 270 nm due to the Eu3+ and O2− interaction. Also, sharp peaks between 380–470 nm arising from the 4f–4f transitions of Eu3+ were observed. Fluoride-based long persistent phosphors

Recently, Mn-activated fluoride phosphors have been widely explored for their excellent red luminescence, establishing the prominent role of fluoride-based hosts, which are now explored for various phenomena such as afterglow, in accordance with lanthanide doping.154 Yue Hu et al. recently reported an X-ray-excited green afterglow in Tb3+-activated hexagonal fluoride phosphor, β-NaYF4.37 Superlong afterglow phosphors can be potentially used for in vivo imaging, making β-NaYF4:Tb3+ a suitable candidate for further exploration due to its persistent emission for around 120 h. The Tb3+-doped phosphor was synthesized via the conventional hydrothermal method to prepare pure hexagonal phase β-NaYF4. The formed hexagonal crystal structure is elucidated in Fig. 20(a).155,156 After excitation via X-rays, the luminescence emission had a similar profile to that from normal excitation at 543 nm with some additional peaks in the range of 350–500 nm and 570–630 nm. These emission peaks were speculated to result from the 5D3 and 5D4 to the lower 7FJ levels. The afterglow spectra resembled the emission spectra, showing an excellent duration of green afterglow, as observed in Fig. 20(b). The thermal and photo-stimulated luminescence study revealed the presence of three shallow traps, corresponding to 353, 446 and 557 K, and several deep traps, questioning the persistent emission at room temperature. Further studies confirmed the occurrence of a tunnelling process. In this mechanism, the deep traps are F-centres, with the F vacancy trapping one electron and the shallow traps are F’ centres, trapping two electrons. The excited electrons from the valence band go to the conduction band, from where it falls into different traps below the band, which are created as F-vacancies due to X-ray excitation. Tb3+ loses an electron to stabilise its configuration and form a hole (Tb3+ + h). When the energy of trapped electrons is exhausted, they return to the conduction band and are trapped by F-vacancies to form F-centres. The F-centres and holes (from Tb3+ + h) recombine through the Auger effect and energy if translated to excited Tb3+, which leads to emission from the 4f → 4f transition. The electrons trapped in the F centre to F vacancy via tunnelling form new F centres, mediating between the deep and shallow traps, which can explain the long persistent afterglow. Fig. 20(c) illustrates the PersL images of the X-ray-excited sample disks at varying excitation times and recovery of the original intensity by heating even after 24 h. This work offers great room for development for use in photodynamic therapy to make its use safe and efficient.

Some other oxide and oxysulphide-based LPPs

Recently, Xinquan Zhou et al. developed a novel red Sm3+-activated phosphor, BaGa2O4, which showed enhanced properties when Sr2+ was introduced partially in place Ba2+ to observe afterglow for up to 2 h.27 The phosphor crystallised in a hexagonal unit cell belonging to the P63 point group. The formula Ba1−xSrxGa2O4 represents a double oxide composition with Ba or Sr as divalent forming two types of coordination spheres, one consisting of nine bonded oxygen atoms and the other bonded with seven or eight oxygen atoms and trivalent Ga, forming GaO4 tetrahedra, as elucidated in Fig. 22(a). Based on their similar ionic radii, Sm3+ on doping replaces Ba2+ in the host matrix, which causes charge imbalance, leading to the formation of oxygen vacancies to neutralise the overall crystal system. Sr2+ and Sm3+ doping caused no impurity as verified by slight changes in the XRD peaks. The PL excitation spectra of Sm3+-doped BaGa2O4 showed a band in the UV region with the characteristic peak of Sm3+ at 404 nm. The emission spectra revealed the three emission peaks of Sm3+ at 565, 602 and 656 nm. The introduction of Sr2+ enhanced the emission and peaked at 6% substitution. The presence of defects in the host is the main reason for the persistent afterglow and the source of traps. The thermoluminescence spectra revealed a trap depth in the range of 0.77 eV, which is the prevalent range for the afterglow. Further theoretical studies revealed the energy variation in the range of 0.45 to 0.84 eV during the two hours in which the afterglow was observed, indicating the involvement of continuous trap distribution in the host. The possible mechanism is that Sm3+ substitutes at two different sites of Ba2+. The traps in the host capture the charge carriers, i.e., the hole and electrons, which recombine to produce the delayed emission. After UV excitation, the electrons move to the valence band, creating holes in the conduction band. The electrons and holes are captured by traps in their proximity. After the excitation is ceased, the traps eventually release the charge carriers via tunnelling induced by thermal or photo-stimulation. The persistence afterglow is the resultant emission from only one of the Sm3+ crystallographic sites, which can be verified by the difference in the PL emission and persistent emission spectra (Fig. 22(b)). The other Sm3+ remains non-emissive due to the large distance, leading to ineffective tunnelling. The most prominent composition, Ba0.94Sr0.06Ga2O4:0.01Sm3+, offered bright red afterglow for more than 2 h, which can be referenced for further enhancement and applications in various fields.

Aluminate-based LPPs

Aluminates such as SrAl2O4 and CaAl2O4 are some of the initially studied phosphor materials and have been sequentially modulated to other aluminates activated by lanthanides such as Y3Al5O12:Gd3+,Ce3+,Eu3+ and/or Tb3+.6,74,173 Arun Kumar, et al. investigated the application aspects of SrAl2O4:Eu2+,Dy3+, a well-known and highly explored phosphor.64 The synthesis was done via a high-temperature solid-state synthesis procedure. The crystal is packed in the monoclinic P21 space group (Fig. 23(a)). Eu2+, Dy3+ integrates into the crystal, replacing Sr2+ in the lattice. Here, the Dy3+ dopant acts as traps in this lattice. There are some disagreement in the trap states, originating from the involvement of oxygen vacancies. The synthesized phosphor was yellowish under daylight. The excitation of the phosphor lies in the visible region available by sunlight, as observed from the PLE spectrum depicted in Fig. 23(b). The emission is described as 4f6 5d1 → 4f7 and lies in the green emission range, as illustrated from the PL spectrum in Fig. 23(c). The SrAl2O4:Eu2+,Dy3+ phosphor also shows thermoluminescence and a rare known phenomenon of mechano-luminescence. The long phosphorescence is made possible due to the presence of Dy3+. Matsuzawa et al. described the trap depth to be 0.65 eV. The Eu, Dy-doped strontium aluminate showed afterglow for hours. This phosphor can be potentially used as a commercial phosphor for optical displays and military applications predominantly due to its sunlight activation and is currently being explored by many researchers. The Eu2+ creates shallow traps, while Dy3+ traps are deeper and the presence of Dy3+ is responsible for the prolonged delay in emission for more than 5 h. The photoconductivity from the valence band suggests the conversion of Eu2+ to Eu+ to generate a hole and Dy3+ is converted to Dy4+ due to hole capture. Then hole is released on thermal agitation after excitation ceases and goes back to the ground state Eu2+ and leads to delayed emission. Hybrid/composite material-based LPPs

Occasionally, multiple systems are simultaneously employed for upgraded emissions. These systems can be achieved by mechanical forces or core–shell type hybrid structures such as NaGdF4:Nd3+,Yb3+@CaF2:Eu3+.177 CaAl2O4:Eu2+,Nd3+ is one of the phosphors explored very early, receiving continuous attention ever since.6,62 CaAl2O4 formed a monoclinic structure.178 The persistent afterglow was speculated to arise due to the effective trapping of holes and the persistent luminescence generated amid Eu3+ transitions. Nd3+ improved the afterglow due to enhanced energy transfer and the introduction of an appropriate trap depth. Wenbo Chen et al. reported the colour tuning of CaAl2O4:Eu2+,Nd3+ (CA) on the addition of a yellow phosphor, Y3Al5O12:Ce3+ (YAG).62 The different samples were synthesized by varying the concentrations of the two components, CA and YAG. The excitation spectra of YAG and persistence luminescence spectra of CA are in the same region, and hence energy transfer was speculated between them for enhanced results. The persistent luminescence spectra of the CA/YAG ratio showed increased emission in the yellow region, as observed in Fig. 24(a and b), and hence the overall colour of the phosphor was light blue compared to the intense blue of CA. The persistence emission of CA was impressive but more intensified with the addition of YAG. The intensity and the duration of the afterglow increased to above 2-folds on complementing CA with YAG. Based on the established results that YAG does not show afterglow, here it could be inferred that the YAG centres only emit, while CA accounted for both the trap and emission centres. The TL spectra also verified the presence of some deep traps due to CA, which lowered on external stimulation. The addition of YAG decreased the intensity of the CA spectrum, further validating its role in the emission. The trap depths lie in the range of 0.63 to 0.79 eV. The LPP mechanism was described for the CA doped with Eu2+ (Eu2+ is the emission centres), as the electrons of Eu2+ get excited on UV-irradiation to 5d, and finally to the conduction bands. The electrons here were prone to photoluminescence and trapping. The electrons were widely trapped by the oxygen vacancy sites and defects created by Nd3+. The emission of delayed luminescence overlapped with the excitation profile of YAG, which gets excited during the delayed emission, producing a yellow emission band together with blue emission and shifting the intensity to light blue colour, produced by blue–yellow mixing. The persistent luminescence is mediated by the host lattice and can effectively be enhanced in nanoparticles with a controlled particle size and morphology. The CaF2:Dy @NaYF4 core–shell hybrid nanoparticle, with tunable morphology presented a model example of an afterglow mechanism dependent on nanoparticles. The inner core–shell CaF2:Dy was prepared via colloidal synthesis and later incubated for the growth of the outer shell, NaYF4. Both materials have a similar crystal structure. Different shell growth morphologies were achieved by changing the shells to LiYF4, NaYF4:Ca,NaGdF4 and NaYbF4, as schematically depicted in Fig. 25. The formed CaF2:Dy@NaYF4 could by excited by UV and X-ray radiation. This morphology-controlled material has potential application in the bio-medical and catalysis field.179

Conclusion and future perspectives

In this review, we summarized lanthanide-based long persistent phosphors and their detailed mechanisms in different host lattices based on their colour emission over the last decade and presented insights for - further research and development of new long persistent phosphors and discovery of their out-of-box applications. It can be clearly observed that the wide range of host lattices and various lanthanides involved in the emissions span the entire visible range and NIR spectral range. The studies also suggest fine tuning based on the involvement of various emissive sites as co-dopants as well as physical mixing. Thus, it can be concluded that long persistent phosphors have been indisputably researched and have been successfully applied in several fields and still offer potential to be explored. According to the -thorough literature study, it is worth noting that there is ample space for the further research and development of LPPs, as follows: (i) it is a big challenge to identify new activators, co-activators, and host lattices to explore new LPPs with desirable performances, (ii) an in-depth understanding of the generalized mechanism is required, (iii) spectral window tuning (absorption and emission) of wavelengths based on applications is also a challenging task, (iv) search for long afterglow (phosphorescent) material (host structural design and activator combination) using a combination of high-throughput evaluation and machine learning needs to be encouraged180 and (v) more attention to synthetic methods (for example nano-sized LPPs need to be explored).

Although this field gained significant attention and substantial growth, there is still room to improve the efficiency of LPPs and explore numerous host lattices for finding new LPPs. Specifically, a brilliant prospect and vast products can be anticipated in the near future with advanced engineering on the new discoveries of long-persistent phosphors. Therefore, it is evident that introducing new technology or advancing the current state of the art will have an enormous impact on these sectors.
