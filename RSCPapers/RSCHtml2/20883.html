Abstract

With applications in high performance electronics, photovoltaics, and catalysis, two-dimensional (2D) transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) attract extensive attention due to their extraordinary physical properties. People have focused on TMDC-based materials for years, while the low mobility greatly hinders their further application. TMDC-based heterostructures with tunable band alignment have been experimentally confirmed to be feasible for photoelectronic devices or photocatalysts. Based on the density functional theory (DFT), there are four discoveries in this work: (1) we propose two new heterostructures based on BSe and MoS2/WS2 that have quite low mismatches and intrinsic type-II alignments. (2) Even though the VBM of BSeâ€“MoS2 are completely contributed by BSe, the heterostructure is still endowed with a lower effective mass and a better transport characteristic in comparison with pristine structures. (3) A promoted absorption ability and a better transport characteristic oppose each other and the two characteristics cannot be obtained at the same time. (4) Tension strained structures can induce promoted light absorption in the solar spectrum and the predicted efficiency of the BSeâ€“MoS2 bilayer can be as high as âˆ¼19.3%, when the external electric field is applied. This theoretical survey proves that BSeâ€“MoS2/WS2 with high flexibility and tunability are potential candidates for novel electronic devices and photocatalysts.

Introduction

The study of 2D structured materials has long been the most promising fields in nanoscience and nanotechnology since the successful exfoliation of graphene for their rich and various physicochemical characteristics.1â€“3 In the scope of TMDC compounds, WS2 and MoS2 are two most important components. Their inlayer atoms are connected by strong covalent bonds, which leads to a remarkable structural stability. In experimental characterizations and theoretical calculations, the transition of the bandgap from indirect to direct exists in most TMDCs such as WS2/MoSe2 when exfoliated from the bulk to the monolayer.4,5 With these electronic and mechanical features, TMDCs demonstrate their considerable potential application in photoelectron,6,7 energy harvesting,8 and catalysis.9,10 Also, the intrinsic spinâ€“orbit coupling (SOC) effect in TMDC monolayers make them favorable for the purpose of spintronic and valleytronic utilizations.11,12 Monolayer MoS2/WS2 all have a direct bandgap and decent absorption ability in the visible range, high on/off ratio, and a pretty well structural stability.13â€“15 However, their relative low mobility, susceptible mechanical features, and rapid carrier recombination affect the further exploration to some extent.16 Improvement through some approaches is necessary for the better use of materials and a possible route is through heterostructure techniques to achieve type-II alignment.17 Different parts of materials in the composite structure contribute different bands; thus, the electron/hole carriers are naturally located in different sections and an internal electric field can form spontaneously across the entire heterostructure, which contributes a better separation characteristic. The above electronâ€“hole separation process does not require additional doping treatment and can avoid the problems of impurities.18 However, the design of an ideal heterojunction is not an easy task since the selected materials have to meet two requirements. First, the valence band maximum (VBM) and the conduction band minimum (CBM) should originate from different parts and the optimal band alignment is type-II, which will achieve an efficient separation of the carriers after irradiation. Taking TMDCs as an example, the band alignment of the opposite part should be elaborately selected to ensure the type-II offset. Also, the bandgap of the opposite part should be sufficiently large to guarantee the good preservation of band edges. Second, carriersâ€™ mobility should be at least higher than that of pristine TMDCs to maintain a practicable internal quantum efficiency of the fabricated heterostructures.19,20

The 2D vdW heterostructure shows many interesting properties far beyond that of individual components and is regarded as a field full of life.21â€“23 The mismatches of most 2D heterostructures can be controlled to a low degree and will provide broad possibilities for the manufacture of new nanoelectronic devices.24 The physical and chemical properties of materials largely depend on the band alignment around the interface, and appropriate adjustments to the band structure will provide new ideas and methods for the design and application of nanocomposites. Theoretical and experimental works of 2D heterostructures have been well studied. Hong et al.25 experimentally fabricated nano-thin MoS2â€“WS2 composite structures, and for the first time observed the ultra-fast charge transfer phenomenon through femtosecond pump probe spectra and photoluminescence spectra. The results show that the MoS2â€“WS2 composite structure coupled by the vdW forces exhibits a fast carrier transfer rate within 50 fs after excitation. Tang et al.26 calculated the electronic and optoelectronic properties of the black phosphorus (BP)-MoS2 heterostructure; the results revealed that a high photodetection responsivity exists in the corresponding composite and is in good accordance with the reported results. Both linear dichroism and the high carrier mobility of the freestanding BP can be preserved in this heterostructure. Their work illustrates the outstanding electronic/optoelectronic properties that can be achieved through composite BP with MoS2. Opoku et al.27 explored the roles of m-BiVO4â€“MoS2/WS2 heterostructures for use in hydrogen evolution and catalytic degradation. The electronic properties and photocatalytic activity of the related structures are discussed in detail. The results prove that the type-II alignment, narrowed bandgap, and built-in potential of the composite are beneficial for the separation and transfer of carriers and finally enhance the photocatalytic activity. These outstanding works have greatly stimulated people's research interest in 2D composite materials.

More recently, a certain degree of research has been performed on monolayer BSe. Its thermal stability has been discussed based on the theoretical calculations.28 BSe is a semiconductor with indirect bandgap. It shares the same hexagonal crystal structure (Pm2) with MoS2/WS2 and reveals small lattice mismatch after the composite with MoS2/WS2 in subsequent calculations.29 There are reasons to predict that the heterostructure of BSe and MoS2/WS2 would be generated in experiments under certain conditions. It is thus meaningful to learn how BSe and monolayer MoS2/WS2 can form stable 2D heterostructures and whether they have potential value in practical applications.

Previously, S. Demirci et al.30 investigated the structural, mechanical, and electronic properties of group IIIâ€“VI binary monolayers with the 2D hexagonal structure, MX (M = B, Al, Ga, In and X = O, S, Se, Te), based on the DFT. The structural optimization calculations and phonon spectrum analysis indicate that all the binary compounds are thermally stable. The obtained electronic band structures show that the BSe monolayer is a semiconductor with a bandgap of 2.61 eV. They considered that the unique characteristics of group IIIâ€“VI binary monolayers were suitable for high-performance device applications in nanoelectronics and optics. Taking these into consideration, we perform a further study of MX-based heterostructures. After a series of careful calculations, we found that BSe and MoS2/WS2 can form perfect heterostructures with small lattice mismatch and well-defined type-II alignments. For all we know, the structural properties and physical/chemical attributes of BSeâ€“MoS2/WS2 are still unknown. The aim of this study is to obtain an understanding as comprehensive as possible of the basic characteristics and their response to external effects. We focus on the band edges variation and unique electronic properties endowed by their type-II alignments. It turns out that the contact type of BSeâ€“MoS2/WS2 can be effectively regulated and have broad prospects in photocatalysis and photovoltaic utilization. The next section introduces the construction method and the modeling process of two heterostructures, and the related calculation approaches are described in detail. After that, we demonstrate electronic structure and band realignment of BSeâ€“MoS2/WS2 through the applied external electric field and uniaxial/biaxial strain. The final section gives a brief summary.

Generally, reducing the bandgap can improve the utilization of light energy to a certain extent, which is also the aim of the doping technique. However, the doping approach will inevitably introduce foreign atoms and turn them into recombination centers to reduce light-generated carriers. Therefore, the heterogeneous structure seems to be a better choice. When a semiconductor is irradiated by light, electrons will be excited to CB, leaving holes in the VB. Part of the carriers will recombine spontaneously and no longer participate in the subsequent reactions. The other will quickly migrate to the outer space to react with surrounding ions and participate in subsequent physical/chemical reactions. The ideal situation is the second part of the carriers involved in the relevant reactions as much as possible and inhibit the recombination of the carriers in the first part.

Both BSe and WS2 can absorb photons after irradiation, which further excite electrons in the VB to the CB and leave the holes in VB. Conclusion

From recent theoretical and experimental studies, TMDC-based heterostructures have shown a broad prospect. After combining two specific materials, it will be expected to meet people's requirements for structure and performance. In this work, we proposed a new BSe-XS2 heterostructure that possesses intrinsic type-II alignment and systematically explored the electronic structure, band alignment, charge spatial distribution, light absorption, and strain response of two heterostructures through first-principles calculations. In freestanding states, the band structures of the two composites are flexible and adjustable and very beneficial for the application of photocatalytic materials or optoelectronic devices. A proper interfacial charge spatial distribution can further facilitate the separation of carriers around the interfacial region and inhibits the recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs to achieve better light energy utilization. We studied the regulation of the structural and electronic properties of the two heterostructures by the external effects, which provides a valuable reference for experimental research. It shows that the band edge alignment and absorption characteristics of the two heterostructures can be effectively manipulated by the external electric field and strain-stress. The absorption spectrum shows that they are more sensitive to photons of red wavelength in the solar spectrum when they are stretched. The predicted PCE of the BSeâ€“MoS2 bilayer reaches as high as âˆ¼19.3% after the external electric field is applied and is expected to be used in solar cells. In short, we believe the modeling process and the calculation procedures in terms of the efficient use of solar energy involved in this work can provide valuable guidance for other 2D vdW heterostructures.
