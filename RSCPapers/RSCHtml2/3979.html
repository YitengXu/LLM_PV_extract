Abstract

In order to study the relationship between the molecular structures of compact electron donor/acceptor dyads and the spinâ€“orbit charge transfer intersystem crossing (SOCT-ISC) efficiency, we prepared a perylene (Pery)â€“naphthalimide (NI) dyad, in which the Pery unit is the electron donor and the NI unit is the electron acceptor, where the two units adopt an almost orthogonal geometry. The photophysical properties of the dyad were studied with steady state and femtosecond/nanosecond transient absorption (fs TA and ns TA) spectroscopies, time resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (TREPR) spectroscopy and DFT computations. A very weak charge transfer (CT) absorption band was observed, but the fluorescence of the Pery unit is strongly quenched. Upon selective excitation into the NI unit, the fast intramolecular CS process (10 ps) occurs, followed by ISC and only the 3Pery* state is observed; whereas upon selective photoexcitation into the perylene unit, an ultrafast charge separation (0.66 ps) is observed, followed by SOCT-ISC (8 ns) to populate the 3Pery* state. Moreover, the perylene radical cation is also observed on the ns timescale, presumably formed by intermolecular charge transfer. The lifetime of the 3Pery triplet state was determined to be Ï„T = 214 Î¼s with ns TA spectroscopy. The singlet oxygen quantum yield was measured as Î¦Î” = 80%, although the potential energy curve of the torsion between the donor and acceptor is shallow. The SOCT-ISC mechanism was confirmed by TREPR spectroscopy. The dyad was used as a triplet photosensitizer for the generation of delayed fluorescence (luminescence lifetime Ï„DF = 57.3 Î¼s).

Introduction

The formation of long-lived triplet excited states is of fundamental importance for photosynthesis,1,2 photocatalysis,3â€“6 photovoltaics,7â€“9 photodynamic therapy10â€“13 and tripletâ€“triplet annihilation (TTA) upconversion.14â€“22 An efficient triplet photosensitizer (PS) should possess several photophysical properties such as strong light absorption, efficient intersystem crossing (ISC) and long triplet lifetime.23 The most straightforward approach to enhance ISC in organic compounds is the heavy atom effect (based on I, Br, Pt, Ir, Os atoms, etc.).23â€“27 However, this method suffers from some drawbacks, such as complicated synthesis routes, costly preparation and toxicity. Therefore, it is highly desired to develop novel heavy atom-free molecules with efficient ISC. Other than the heavy atom effect for enhancing ISC, a few other approaches have been reported. For instance, exciton coupling,28 radical pair ISC (RP-ISC) or charge recombination (CR)-induced ISC,29,30 electron spin conversion31 and radical enhanced ISC (REISC).32 Furthermore, molecules with the nâ€“Ï€* â†” Ï€â€“Ï€* transition are also known to exhibit efficient ISC production (the EL-Sayed rule).33 The main challenge of all these approaches is the difficult synthesis and the difficulty in predicting the ISC ability.

RP-ISC is known for weakly coupled electron donorâ€“acceptor systems. In this case, photoexcitation of either the electron donor or the acceptor induces an electron transfer from the donor to acceptor moiety with the formation of charge separated states (CSS).29,34,35 The triplet formation involves the ISC among CS state 1CS â†’ 3CS promoted by hyperfine coupling interactions (HFI), followed by the rapid decay of the 3CS to the 3LE state. However, the ISC efficiency of these systems is usually low, which is a major disadvantage of this approach. Moreover, the synthesis of these dyads is difficult, thus it is highly desired to develop a simple molecular structure profile for electron donor/acceptor dyads to attain efficient ISC.

Recently, the production of triplet states through the so-called spin orbit charge transfer ISC (SOCT-ISC) mechanism in compact electron donor/acceptor systems has attained much consideration.36â€“41 In these systems, the linker between the electron donor and acceptor is short, and the two moieties are preferably arranged in an orthogonal geometry with each other (different from the conventional donor/acceptor dyads with long and rigid linkers showing RP-ISC). For these compact dyads, the RP-ISC mechanism is inhibited due to large electron exchange (J values). In SOCT-ISC, electron transfer between perpendicularly oriented donor/acceptor units is accompanied by a change in the molecular orbital angular momentum, which generates a magnetic torque able to flip the electron spin. As a result, the change in molecular orbital angular momentum compensates the change in the electron spin angular momentum, facilitating the ISC process.36â€“39,41â€“43

Although the occurrence of SOCT-ISC has been demonstrated in several compact electron donor/acceptor dyads, still much room is left to fully understand this mechanism. Up to now, only a limited number of visible-light-harvesting chromophores have been used for SOCT-ISC, such as anthracene,37 Bodipy40a and perylenebisimide.44 Different from Bodipy chromophores,45 we show that perylene is unique concerning SOCT-ISC dyads, because it can be used as either the electron donor or acceptor to achieve SOCT-ISC.44,46 Different electron donors may offer different driving forces for CS, and the geometric restriction may increase the ISC efficiency. It was previously proposed that in the case of Bodipy, SOCT-ISC occurs for dyads with Bodipy as the electron acceptor, but not for dyads with Bodipy as the electron donor.45 However, it is still in doubt whether or not this rule is applicable to other chromophores. Recently, we have investigated the SOCT-ISC in orthogonal peryleneâ€“phenothiazine dyads with the perylene unit as the electron acceptor, which showed excellent triplet formation.46 In peryleneâ€“Bodipy dyads, the perylene unit is the electron donor, and efficient SOCT-ISC was observed.44 In a NI-PTZ dyad, we observed a long-lived 3CT state.40b More evidence is required to evaluate the limitation on the role that a chromophore can play in the electron donor/acceptor dyads.

In order to address the above challenges, we prepared a compact electron donor/acceptor dyad, with perylene as the electron donor/visible light-harvesting chromophore and naphthalimide (NI) as the electron acceptor (Scheme 1). In this system, the SOCT-ISC can be efficient, given that the geometry of the electron donor/acceptor is nearly orthogonal. We studied the photophysics of the dyad using steady state and femtosecond, subnanosecond and nanosecond time-resolved transient spectroscopies. Efficient formation of the 3LE state of the perylene moiety is confirmed. We found that the photophysics of the dyad is excitation wavelength-dependent. Upon selective photoexcitation into the NI unit, only the 3Pery* state is populated. On the other hand, photoexcitation into the perylene unit promotes the formation of a perylene radical cation via intermolecular charge separation, as well as the 3Pery* state. Time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (TREPR) spectroscopy was used to study the electron spin polarization (ESP) of the triplet states. We confirmed the SOCT-ISC mechanism showing that the observed ESP and population ratio of sublevels of the triplet state (TX, TY, TZ) are different from those of the SO-ISC mechanism. Additionally, delayed fluorescence was observed with the dyad as the triplet PS and perylenebisimide (PBI) and Solvent Green 5 (SG-5) as triplet acceptors/emitters.

Results and discussion

Design rationales of the Pery-NI compact electron donor/acceptor dyad

NI is a typical electron acceptor, and has been used widely in the conventional electron donor/acceptor dyads where the donor and acceptor had large separation.47â€“49 On the other hand, arenes can be used as electron donors.50â€“52 We prepared a Pery-NI compact electron donor/acceptor dyad (Scheme 1). The mutual orientation of the two chromophores is expected to be orthogonal, due to the conformation restrictions. The dyad was prepared by direct connection of the perylene unit and the NI moiety with the Suzukiâ€“Miyaura cross coupling reaction. The yield was satisfactory, and the molecular structure of the product was fully verified with 1H NMR, 13C NMR and HRMS. Pery-Ph and NI-Ph were prepared as reference compounds for the photophysical studies.

To further investigate the photophysics of our systems, we analyzed the solvent polarity dependence of the fluorescence (Fig. 2). For Pery-NI, the fluorescence emission intensity upon NI excitation is highly dependent on the solvent polarity (Fig. 2a). Femtosecond transient absorption spectroscopy

The dynamic evolution of the excited states in Pery-NI has been studied by femtosecond transient absorption spectroscopy with 400 nm excitation, where absorption is mostly due to the perylene moiety. The transient spectra were acquired in DCM with a time interval spanning up to 1.5 ns and analysed using singular value decomposition (SVD) and global fitting to obtain the Evolution Associated Difference Spectra (EADS). To have a reference, we also measured the ultrafast transient absorption spectra of Pery-Ph (Fig. S18, ESIâ€ ). For this compound, an excited state absorption (ESA) band centred at 708 nm (S1 â†’ Sn transitions) and ground state bleaching (GSB) band centred at 450 nm were observed. Moreover, a stimulated emission (SE) band was observed in the range from 480 nm to 535 nm. All these bands are typical signatures of the singlet excited state of perylene and no obvious ISC was observed for this compound.

For Pery-NI, a GSB band at 445 nm and an ESA band extending up to 700 nm, previously assigned to an S1 â†’ Sn absorption of the perylene,46 were initially observed (Fig. 4a). A band centred at 553 nm rapidly appeared, similar to that observed in the ns-TA spectra, where it has been assigned to the perylene radical cation.50 Previously, it was reported that the NI radical anion shows absorption at 420 nm (Îµ = 23500 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1) and 491 nm (Îµ = 3000 Mâˆ’1 cmâˆ’1).58 In this case NI anion bands are not clearly visible since they may overlap with negative GSB bands. Global analysis allows extraction of the timescale of the spectral evolution. The first EADS (Fig. 4b, black line) shows the occurrence of partial ultrafast CS, with the appearance of the GSB band and a positive band at 570 nm, which can be assigned to the perylene cation, besides a broad tail extending up to 750 nm, due to the residual absorption of the neutral perylene excited state.

In the following evolution, occurring in 0.66 ps, the positive band due to the perylene cation increases in intensity and blue shifts to 553 nm, while the ESA band of the perylene singlet excited state at 700 nm decays completely. The perylene cation band slightly blue shifts on a timescale of 13.5 ps (red to blue evolution in Fig. 4b), due to vibrational cooling. The transient spectra show limited evolution at the following time interval (1.5 ns), indicating that the charge separated state has a lifetime >1.5 ns so that triplet production is not observed during this short delay time. This result confirms that the 3Pery* state is populated after the CS process.

In order to analyse the solvent dependence of CS and triplet production, toluene and acetonitrile were also used as solvents. In toluene, a stimulated emission band at 530 nm appears, whose position matches that of the dyad fluorescence observed in this solvent, and is due to the stimulated emission of perylene, which is directly excited at 400 nm. The SE band is superimposed to the positive signal of the perylene cation which starts to rise in about 6 ps (Fig. S19, ESIâ€ ). This result shows that charge separation partially occurs also in this non polar solvent, although with slow kinetics and lower yield, as demonstrated by the persistence of the ESA band assigned to the neutral perylene singlet excited state up to the nanosecond timescale. In ACN, the appearance of an intense positive band at 550 nm, due to the formation of the perylene cation occurs in less than 1 ps, indicating fast CS (Fig. S20, ESIâ€ ), while the triplet state production was not observed. In this case, the transient signal recovers within about 650 ps, due to CR (Fig. S20, ESIâ€ ). The occurrence of fast CR in acetonitrile agrees with the lack of triplet formation observed on the nanosecondâ€“microsecond timescale in this solvent. Selected kinetic traces are reported in Fig. 4c for three solvents.

Finally, we also studied the dynamics of NI-Ph (Fig. S21, ESIâ€ ). In this case, a broad ESA band with a dip at about 550 nm, due to superimposed stimulated emission, was observed. The transient signal showed no significant evolution up to the nanosecond timescale, and the limited decay of the kinetic traces indicated a long lived excited state.

Subnanosecond transient absorption spectroscopy

In order to reveal more details of the photophysical properties of the compounds, subnanosecond transient absorption spectroscopy upon selective photoexcitation was performed. Firstly, compound 2 (3-bromoperylene) was investigated. A sharp ESA band centered at about 702 nm (S1 â†’ Sn transitions) and stimulated emission at about 478 nm were observed. The ESA band decayed within 10 ns and a new band at about 500 nm appeared, which is the ESA band of the triplet state of perylene (T1 â†’ Tn transitions) populated by a heavy atom effect. The triplet state lifetime is ca. 500 ns (in deaerated solution, Fig. S22, ESIâ€ ).

Pery-NI was excited at 430 nm (perylene absorption band), showing an ultrafast CS process (i.e. < 10 ps), as also confirmed in fs-TA spectra. The formation of a perylene radical cation is confirmed, indicated by the absorption band centered at 550 nm (Fig. 5a).50 Moreover, a band at about 500 nm was observed to rise, which is attributed to the ESA of the perylene-localized triplet state,59 in good agreement with our ns-TA observation (Fig. 6). As mentioned in the fs-TA section, the NI radical anion bands should appear at 420 and 490 nm.58 In sub-nanosecond spectra they may overlap with ESA of 3Pery* or be covered by laser scattering at 433 nm (Fig. 5a). The decay kinetics of the bands assigned to 3Pery* and the perylene radical cation are presented in Fig. 5b. The ISC rate constant is extracted from the growth of the triplet state signal at 500 nm, and determined as 8 ns. The triplet state at 500 nm decays in ca. 300 ns in the presence of O2.

Exciting Pery-NI at 340 nm would preferentially excite the NI part (Fig. 5c). In this case, fast FRET towards the perylene is expected to occur. Within the time resolution of our sub-nanosecond measurement we nevertheless observe the band at 550 nm due to Pery+Ë™, and, within 20 ns, the rise of the ESA band at 500 nm assigned to the localized triplet state of perylene. The observation of the Pery+Ë™ band agrees with the fluorescence measurements reported in the previous section, showing the quenching of perylene fluorescence in slightly polar and polar solvents, and suggests that FRET can occur on a fast time scale, rapidly followed by charge separation. In ns-TA, with this excitation wavelength, we only observed the triplet state of perylene, but the radical cation band may be very weak (Fig. 9a), rising within our instrument response function (IRF is ca. 10 ns for the nanosecond transient absorption spectrometer, LP980).

In sub-nanosecond measurements with NI excitation, the band at 550 nm is due to a CS state because the perylene moiety cannot be excited at this excitation wavelength. The lifetime of this short-lived CS species is ca. 10 ns (in oxygen), which supports the excitation dependent photophysics of Pery-NI observed in nanosecond transient absorption spectra (Fig. 6 and 9). Indeed, CS occurs within 10 ps (time resolution of sub-nanosecond measurements), and it should be in competition with FRET to the perylene moiety.

Previously we demonstrated that the peryleneâ€“phenothiazine dyads, in which the perylene moiety is an electron acceptor, shows efficient ISC.46 Thus, we conclude that the perylene chromophore shows efficient SOCT-ISC in dyads with the perylene unit as either the electron donor or acceptor. It is known that the ISC of the NI moiety is due to S1 â†’ T4, a Ï€â€“Ï€* â†’ nâ€“Ï€* transition.64 Since the nâ€“Ï€* state energy is more sensitive to the solvent polarity than the Ï€â€“Ï€* state, the energy matching of the nâ€“Ï€* and Ï€â€“Ï€* states of NI-Ph is solvent polarity-dependent, and the ISC efficiency of NI-Ph can be solvent polarity-dependent. For Pery-NI, however, the ISC is not due to the NI moiety, because we were selectively exciting the perylene. In order to unambiguously confirm the formation of the triplet excited state upon photoexcitation, nanosecond transient absorption spectra of Pery-NI were recorded (Fig. 6a).

Time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (TREPR) spectroscopy

To gain further insight into the ISC mechanism of Pery-NI, time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance (TREPR) spectra were recorded (Fig. 10). TRPER spectroscopy is an important tool to study the triplet excited states, as well as the ISC mechanisms.29,37,46 Moreover, on the basis of zero field splitting parameters (ZFS), i.e. the D and E values, it is possible to differentiate the 3LE and 3CS states. To confirm the ISC mechanism of the dyad, the TREPR measurements were carried out in frozen solutions or a liquid crystal matrix at different temperatures varying from 80 K to 280 K (Fig. 10).

The ground state geometries of the compounds were optimized with DFT computations (Fig. 11). For Pery-NI, the dihedral angle between the NI and the perylene moieties is 72.5Â°. Although it is not a perfect orthogonal geometry, still we observed a very high singlet oxygen quantum yield in DCM (80%, Table 3).

Although the orthogonal geometry will give the smallest steric hindrance, Ï€-conjugation will decrease the electronic energy. This trend is significant with the reference compounds NI-Ph and Pery-Ph, for which the dihedral angles between the phenyl and the aryl moieties were found as ca. 54Â°.

Previously we studied the compact electron donor/acceptor dyads with perylene as the electron acceptor and phenothiazine as the electron donor.46 The dihedral angle between the electron donor and acceptor for that dyad is 90Â°, however, the singlet oxygen quantum yield is 60%. For the present dyad Pery-NI, although the dihedral angle is deviated from orthogonality, the singlet oxygen quantum yield is higher (80%). These results indicate that factors other than the orthogonal geometry also contribute to the SOCT-ISC efficiency.

The optimized ground state geometry of the dyad is not sufficient for acquiring the panoramic view of the energy landscape for the torsion between the electron donor and acceptor. Therefore, the potential energy curve of the torsion of the Câ€“C linker between the NI and the perylene units was constructed (Fig. S31, ESIâ€ ) to evaluate the geometries accessible at room temperature. The PES in the dihedral angle range of 60Â°â€“120Â° is rather flat, indicating that the geometries accessible at room temperature are those within the range of ca. 54Â°â€“123Â°. This range is larger than that recently reported for peryleneâ€“phenothiazine dyads showing efficient SOCT-ISC (79Â°â€“103Â°).46 This finding further shows that different factors may affect the SOCT-ISC significantly.

Upon nanosecond pulsed laser excitation, we observed the emission of PBI centered at 580 nm, which is long-lived (57.3 Î¼s, Fig. 13a). In order to monitor the kinetics of the TTA delayed fluorescence, the transient fluorescence signal at 580 nm was analyzed (Fig. 13c). Conclusions

In summary, a compact electron donor/acceptor dyad is prepared, in which perylene (Pery, electron donor) and naphthalimide (NI, electron acceptor) are connected with a single Câ€“C bond. The geometry of the dyad is close to orthogonal (dihedral angle between the Pery and NI moiety is 72.5Â°), and efficient spinâ€“orbit charge transfer intersystem crossing (SOCT-ISC) was observed (singlet oxygen quantum yield Î¦Î” = 80%). The photophysics of the dyad is excitation wavelength-dependent. Upon excitation into the perylene unit, ultrafast charge separation (CS) was observed (0.66 ps), followed by SOCT-ISC (8 ns), giving rise to the 3Pery state (Ï„T = 214 Î¼s). In this case a long-lived perylene radical cation was also observed (752 Î¼s). On the other hand, upon selective photoexcitation into the NI unit, only the 3Pery state was observed with nanosecond transient absorption spectroscopy. Studies in viscous solvents, such as triethylene glycol, suggested that the formation of the persistent perylene radical cation is an intermolecular process. Pulsed laser excited time-resolved electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy (TREPR) confirmed the SOCT-ISC mechanism, since the observed ESP pattern is different from that typical for SO-ISC. DFT computations showed that the potential energy curve for the torsion of the linker between the donor and acceptor is shallow, yet efficient SOCT-ISC occurs. With the dyad as a triplet photosensitizer, delayed fluorescence of different acceptors was observed (luminescence lifetime up to 57.3 Î¼s). These results are useful for future design of electron donor/acceptor dyads showing an efficient triplet state, as well as for the design of heavy atom-free triplet photosensitizers.
