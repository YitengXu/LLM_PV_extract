Abstract

The technological evolution has been progressing for centuries and will possibly increase at a higher rate in the 21st century. Currently, in this age of nanotechnology, the discovery of more economical and sustainable novel materials has considerably increased. The abundance of two-dimensional (2D) materials has endowed them with a broad material platform in technical studies and in the expansion of nano- and atomic-level applications. The innovation of graphene has motivated considerable attention to the study of other novel 2D materials, known as modern day alchemy, by which scientists are trying to convert most possible periodic table elements into 2D material structures and forms. 2D material devices with high quality and good optical encoder performance have a multitude of industrial applications. However, their stability and large size restrict their applications, but these problems can be overcome by functionalization and substrate-based formation of 2D materials. Therefore, via this review, first, basic attributes of 2D materials are described, and the mechanisms to further enhance their properties are also summarized. Second, the applications of 2D materials are discussed, along with their advantages and disadvantages. Finally, some effective device-fabrication approaches, such as heterostructure approaches, are applied to further enhance the properties of 2D materials; their novel device applications and opportunities are also presented. This updated review may provide new avenues for 2D material synthesis and development of more efficient devices compared to conventional devices in different fields.

1. Introduction

Graphene (2004) is a solitary atom layer derived from graphite, where carbons are bonded with each other in a hexagonal honeycomb lattice; it is a very prominent and well-matured 2D-layered material that has opened a new world of 2D materials owing to its exceptional properties.16 Graphene is the first and best-known 2D material;7,8 it has a zero band gap semimetal nature, where electrons are highly mobile and exhibit exceptional conductivity.9,10 Graphene was the first 2D material to represent a major advancement in science and technology due to its novel intrinsic physical properties, such as mechanical, electronic and thermal transport. Enormous numbers of applications, achievements, and fundamental science studies based on graphene have encouraged researchers to develop a plethora of 2D materials. Based on its 2D structure, the potential properties of graphene have inspired researchers to search for new members of the 2D family. Interestingly, the library of 2D-layered materials grows every year; currently, it consists of more than 150 interesting families. Research regarding 2D materials is in a growing phase, and many new materials are introduced and added to their list every year. In general, 2D materials are categorized based on their structures; examples include graphene,16 transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) (e.g. MoS2, WS2, MoSe2, and WSe2),11 layered double hydroxides (LDHs), LAPONITE clay, hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN),20 black phosphorous (BP), a family of mono-elemental compounds (Xenes), metal oxides, graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4),21 metal nitrides/carbides (MXenes),1215 transition metal halides (TMHs) (e.g., PbI2 and MgBr2),17 transition metal oxides (e.g., MnO2 and MoO3),18 perovskite-type oxides (e.g., K2Ln2Ti3O10 and RbLnTa2O7 (Ln: lanthanide ion)),19 and 2D polymers.22 Since the discovery of graphene, 2D-layered materials have undergone enormous research development.2426 The emergence of novel 2D materials other than graphene has illustrated their extraordinary potential applications for diverse technologies, in addition to concentrating on new discoveries in elementary science, such as sensors, LEDs, FETs, physics, catalysis, biomedicine, and environmental science.2729 The undeniable requirements of superior performance and lower power use in electronic systems are the major motivators of the electronics industry's search for device architectures based on novel 2D materials.

2D materials have importance due to their nano- and atomic-level device uses. Free-standing 2D material growth is a very active research topic. Device applications as well as the synthesis of free-standing sheets can be addressed by growing thin sheets of 2D materials on different substrates. Since the exploration of graphene, researchers have discovered a series of 2D layered crystals. Potential 2D materials such as hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN), metal chalcogenides, metal oxides, metal halides, metal carbides/nitrides (MXenes), and organic semiconductors (OSCs) may address the application limitations of 2D materials in different devices. Almost all the atoms on the surface of these materials are exposed after exfoliation (ultrathin form); thus, the surface areas of 2D materials are noticeably increased, which significantly increases their physical and chemical reactivity and influences the 2D wave function through quantum confinement effects. Consequently, these ultrathin 2D structures have unique electronic, photonic, magnetic and catalytic properties that differ from those of their bulk counterparts, endowing them with remarkable advantages in the aforementioned applications; however, this cannot be universally applied in advanced device fabrication.3034 Generally, materials can be divided into a range of groups, from zero dimensions (0D) to three dimensions (3D); however, here, we are interested in 2D materials, so these materials and their corresponding applications are shown in Fig. 1. Among all these applications, in this review, we will only explore in detail the optoelectronic-device applications of 2D materials.3034

Most 2D materials can undergo changes in their physical and chemical properties; however, these changes are not attainable with the most popular pure 2D materials. For example, in graphene doping, it is impossible to substitute half the carbon atoms with extrinsic atoms. To construct diverse 2D materials, stacking with other 2D materials generates novel material structures, along with novel properties. These diverse 2D materials with multiple stacked layers are recognized as heterostructures. To further enhance the atomic thin film properties, a promising group of materials, namely 2D heterostructures, has been synthesized. Heterostructure/hybrid 2D materials are considered to be a novel class of material which are capable of substituting some building blocks in the introduced material family structure and have potential device applications. These heterostructures are very efficient for optoelectronic devices due to their superfast electrical currents, which can be used in nanocircuits, and they are stronger than traditional circuit materials. Suitable band gap (BG) 2D semiconducting materials can be used in electronics, optoelectronics, and sensing applications. Therefore, due to their mixed properties, utilizing heterostructure approaches in 2D materials can broaden the BG range. 2D heterostructure ionic semiconductors (e.g. hybrid perovskites, chalcogenides) have been added to the family of covalent 2D materials (e.g., 2D Xenes). As an ionic material, these heterostructures have special properties that graphene and other 2D materials do not possess. They are potential successors to the popular Si in electronic devices, including solar panels, because Si has limitations. Researchers are devising alternative materials to meet the technology demands of energy to encode Si with efficient materials. Unlike other 2D materials, heterostructure 2D sheets undergo an unusual atomic scale structural relaxation. Advantageously, this subtle structural change in 2D materials led to a noticeable shift in the electronic BG. This shift in the BG does not occur in the bulk (3D) crystals of the counter materials, which is very advantageous to their optoelectronic properties. They generate efficient photoluminescence, and the emitted light can be tuned by changing the sheet thickness and obtaining heteroatom vertical arrangements with desired compositions. The arguments in this review are proposed to provide an overview of the electro-optical properties of the 2D material family and illustrate where electronics investigation is moving ahead in this novel and inspiring direction.

2. Families of two-dimensional materials

2.1. 2D transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs)

The isolation of monolayer MoS2 was first achieved using adhesive tape-assisted mechanical exfoliation in 1966 and by chemical exfoliation 20 years later.35,36 Later, Hall measurements were calculated, which resulted in theories for electronphonon scattering in TMDCs such as WS2 and MoS2.37,38 In 2010, the research interest in TMDCs was ignited by monolayer MoS2 with a direct BG35 and with the account of the exciting electrical properties of multi-layers (MLs) of MoS2 in 2011. However, the accuracy of the mobility derived from MoS2 transistors is doubtful. In between the most well-known TMDCs are the W- and Mo-based dichalcogenides, i.e., WS2, WSe2, WTe2 MoS2, MoSe2, and MoTe2. For simplicity, TMDCs can be represented by shorthand notation, e.g., MX2, where M is a TM (e.g., Mo, W, Re, or Ta) and X is a chalcogen family element (e.g., O, S, Se, or Te). More than 30 stable TMDCs have been reported using different synthesis methods.39,40 The band structures in 2D-TMDC materials can be tailored either through changing the radioactive decay lifetime of excitons or by stacking the heterolayers in bilayers or multilayers; however, in vertical stacking, the band alignment is limited to bilayer or trilayer. 2D-TMDs at room temperature (RT) have electron carrier mobilities of 200 cm2 V1 s1 with BGs of 1 to 2 eV in the visible range. In electronic devices, these materials are used in logic gate operations with high on/off ratios and low power. For the first time, Radisavljevc et al. used MoS2 in nano-devices as a transistor41 and in HfO2 insulator logic gates with onoff current ratios of 108. The carrier mobility in this process was recorded to be up to 200 cm2 V1 s1, which is comparative to Si technology. This group also used ML Si substrate-deposited MoS2 in logic gates for signal amplification.42 A general rule to obtain high carrier mobilities is through high -gate dielectrics. High -gate dielectrics are obtainable when the Coulomb and electron phonon scattering are minimal. At low temperature, i.e., up to 200 K, the Coulomb scattering is dominant; above this temperature, electronphonon scattering occurs. Coulomb scattering is the highest in monolayers because the distance is short between the carriers and impurities. Increasing the number of layers increases the dielectric constant; hence, carrier scattering decreases at the interface, and the surface carriers suffer from less Coulomb scattering. Less scattering at the surface can lead to higher electron mobility, up to 10 to 15 layers, e.g., 1000 (34000) cm2 V1 s1 for 1 L (6 L) MoS2 at 5 K, measured via Hall mobility measurements. In the case of temperature-dependent electronphonon scattering, the upper bond mobility limit is described through the large effective mass and out-of-plane polar phonons (i.e. 410 cm2 V1 s1 for 1 L MoS2).45,46 However, the highest mobilities obtained to date at RT are 190 cm2 V1 s1 for ML (10 nm) MoS247,48 and 250 cm2 V1 s1 for ML WSe2. On the other hand, atomically flat TMDCs display insignificant surface scattering, in contrast to the extreme surface scattering illustrated by 5 nm rough ultrathin Si.46 Around the same time, group V TMDCs, such as TaS2 and NbSe2, were also explored to evaluate their strongly connected electron phenomena, such as SC and charge density waves.39,49 Typical crystal structures of ML-TMDCs include trigonal prismatic 1H (for multilayer 2H), 1T, and 1T (i.e., distorted-1T) phases (Fig. 2(a)(c)).50,51

The electronic structure of a TMDC depends sensitively on the crystal phase and affords a range of electronic characteristics, including metallic, semimetallic, semiconducting, and superconducting (SC), for different TMDCs.52,53 The TMDCs based on group VB and VIB metals tend to be the most heavily studied due to their diversity of stable compounds and electronic behavior.39,54,55 Indeed, stable compounds exist with all stoichiometric ratios in group VB and VIB metals (i.e., V, Nb, Ta, Cr, W and Mo) and chalcogens (i.e., S, Te and Se), except CrS2/Se2/Te2 and VS2.50,51 Group IVB (i.e., Ti, Hf, and Zr) layered sulfides also possess interesting semiconducting performance; however, they suffer from a high tendency toward non-stoichiometric ratios due to the low energy barriers for intercalation of metal atoms.50,56 Group VB and VIIB metal TMDCs, such as 1T TaS2, have been studied for gate tunable charge density waves, and 1T ReS2 has been studied due to the linear anisotropy in its electrical and optical properties.39,50,51,5759 Group VIII (A, B, C) TM (e.g., pyrite and marcasite) halides and chalcogenides show both layered and non-layered structures with equally diverse electronic structures (e.g., CdI2 structure), including semiconductors such as FeS2.39,50,51 In the 2D area, semiconducting TMDCs, specifically MoS2, WS2, and WSe2, have emerged as electronic device materials due to their comparatively high charge carrier motilities and appreciable BGs, which enable large switching ratios in FETs.39,54,55,60 Furthermore, their successful growth at the wafer scale and ambient stability increase their prospects for practical applications.61 A direct BG at the ML limit provides large oscillator strengths, large spin orbit splitting, and access to the valley degree of freedom at RT. TMDCs also represent an attractive platform for elementary studies of light-matter interactions, optoelectronics, and nanophotonics.6264 In addition, because bulk TMDCs65 were historically analyzed in the catalysis community for desulfurization reactions, the recently renewed interest in 2D-TMDCs has rejuvenated the study of this material class for catalysis in biological and chemical sensor devices.40,60,66

Layered post-TM chalcogenides (PTMCs), such as GaS, SnS, SnS2, and InSe, are also being explored due to their high motilities, large photo-responses, and in-plane anisotropy.6770 Some layered TMDCs (e.g., Bi2Se3) have long been studied as thermoelectric materials, and more recent work has focused on their potential as topological insulators (i.e., topological materials conducting at a surface, while the core remains insulating).70 In spite of the increasing attention being paid to 2D-TMDC materials, there are a number of blockages that prevent their development in addition to that of TMDCs with contemporary semiconductor technology.71,72 Specifically, as prearranged through the international technology roadmap for semiconductors (ITRS), the major move in the direction of extremely well-organized electronic devices is toward metal electrode/TMDCs, which have Rc values as low as 100 mm.47,73 For most 2D-TMDC-based devices, this is not the case to date due to their damaging interface states.73 Furthermore, due to the types of metal/TMDCs, the contact geometry and chemical bonding play major roles in formatting Rc. The 2D-TMDC free dangling bond surface should not form any chemical bonds with metals to avoid increasing the Rc risk. On the other side, the electron/hole transport competency, mainly hindered by electron/hole entraps and phonon scattering centers, may be affected by impurities in the channels due to the high surface-to-volume ratio of 2D-TMDCs, thus providing a suitable foundation for high mobility devices.74,75 The novel properties and low dimensionality of 2D-TMDCs hinder electron/hole injection due to high Rc and deviate from ideal phonon-limited mobility carrier transport because of scatter centers and deficiencies in practical devices with dimensional confinement.

Although the thickness scaling effect leads to increases in BG by decreasing the number of layers due to quantum confinement, the holding position with MLs and few-layer MoS2 remains approximately constant, which is helpful for strong EF pinning (FLP) or SB for carrier injection; consequently, the SB height augments with decreasing thickness of the layers, and the ML limit of Rc greatly improves.41,76 Alternatively, no other 2D-TMDs display the FLP effect but show fair bipolar electron/hole movement due to their enormous vacancy configuration energies close to the mid-gap.77 In n-type MoS2, computational work was performed to design optimum scattering between the electrons and phonons at RT with 410 cm2 V1 s1 charge carrier mobility. The most probable reason for MoS2 p-type and n-type conduction at RT is reported to be impurities trapped at the interface by means of the gate dielectric. 2D materials with single layers are much thinner than the Debye screening lengths; they are prone to demonstrate these effects due to their atomically thin layers. Due to this effect, band engineering highly favors tuning the BGs in these materials to achieve high mobility. As a result, this has great significance not only to intensely inspect the major restrictions of conductance but also to identify approaches to surmount it. These restrictions facilitate precise management of the electronic properties of 2D-TMDs in the direction of high efficiency electronic devices beyond physical limits.

Historically, in the 1960s, Fivaz and Mooser were the first to study the temperature reliance of the Hall mobility in bulk TMDCs; they reported the value as = 0(T/300 K) with = 2 to 3, subjugated by electronphonon scattering at RT ( = 2.6, 2.5, 2.4, 2.1 for MoS2, MoSe2, WS2, and GaSe, respectively).38,78 Similarly, Keyes observed a T1.5 trend for mobility in bulk black phosphorous (BP) in 1953 and justified the same by electronphonon scattering.79 More recently, Kassbjerg et al. studied electronphonon scattering in MLs-MoS2 from first principles and calculated a phonon-limited RT mobility of 400 cm2 V1 s1.45 Most studies on MoS2 transistors report significantly lower values, which indicates that other scattering mechanisms need to be considered. Therefore, free carrier screening is weakened by high- environments, and CI screening is enhanced by high- environments. High- materials (e.g., HfO2 and ZrO2) also have low-energy SO phonons that can be excited remotely by electrons in the semiconductor. Although high- dielectrics have enhanced mobility in graphene, the results are mixed in the case of TMDCs. A systematic study on different combinations of top-gate and bottom-gate dielectrics (e = 1 to 25) concluded that most high- dielectrics are likely to degrade mobility in MoS2 (Fig. 3(d) and (e)).81 Therefore, the best strategy to maximize mobility is to decrease CI scattering using a suspended geometry or h-BN dielectrics. As expected, the mobility of 2D materials increases with the number of layers for the first few layers due to improved screening of CI and then begins to decrease in thicker samples due to increased interlayer resistance.47 Extending this concept, the mobility can be improved by introducing a spacer such as PMMA between the 2D semiconductor channel and the oxide substrate.88 The dependence of the mobility on the carrier density n2d can also be non-trivial. For example, at low n2d, the mobility increases due to increased screening of CI by free carriers; however, at high n2d, the characteristic energy levels of the carriers move closer to the polar LO phonon that is scattered by the Frhlich interaction. Thus, the transfer characteristics of MoS2 FETs are typically nonlinear; the drain current Id varies as (Vg)M, where M = 2 for no screening and M = 1 for complete screening, and the mobility in thicker samples saturates at a smaller Vg.89 The ultrathin bodies of 2D materials also endow them with high sensitivity to surface roughness (L).

Surface roughness is also a common problem in conventional group IIIV semiconductor heterojunctions, where the mobility degrades as the sixth-power law with surface roughness (L6) due to perturbation of quantized energy levels in the 2D electron gas.87 This effect is even more significant in high-mobility 2D materials, such as graphene, which implies that intrinsic mobility limits are achieved on the atomically flat surface of h-BN. Structural disorder, such as point defects and grain boundaries, can also have more deleterious effects in 2D materials compared to bulk semiconductors due to their large scattering cross-sections. Specifically, point defects typically cause short-range scattering, whereas charged defects and grain boundaries induce long-range fields for Coulomb scattering. For example, few-layer MoS2 sandwiched between h-BN films and contacted by graphene possesses low-temperature mobility exceeding90 104 cm2 V1 s1 (limited by CIs) and shows Shubnikov-de Haas oscillations, while its RT mobility (100 cm2 V1 s1) is limited by optical phonons. Sandwiching schemes based on h-BN were also applied to investigate the intrinsic performance of other 2D semiconductors such as h-BN-sandwiched InSe, yielding low temperature mobilities of 104 cm2 V1 s1 (and Shubnikov-de Haas oscillations) and RT mobilities of 1000 cm2 V1 s1, which are the highest for any n-type 2D semiconductor. Among p-type materials, few-layer BP has shown the highest mobility values (1000 cm2 V1 s1 at room temperature), although the BG of few-layered BP is essentially that of bulk BP (0.3 eV), which leads to a modest switching ratio (<500). Finally, variable range hopping has been reported in some low-mobility MoS2 transistors ( < 10 cm2 V1 s1).79,89 The conflicting reports on hopping versus diffusive transport for MoS2 suggest that processing residues and imperfect electrical contacts play important roles in many cases.79,89,93 It is instructive to compare the mobility and BG of the most promising 2D materials with competing technologies, such as thin-film transistors (TFTs), Si-on-insulator (SOI) devices, and 16 ultra-thin body (UTB) devices.54,55 Due to their high mobilities, large switching ratios (>108), and decreased short-channel effects, the progress in MoS2 transistors has been monitored by ITRS since 2012.

In particular, mechanically exfoliated MoS2 devices show comparable mobility to commercial UTB devices based on strained Si, SiGe alloys,90 and InGaAs-on-insulator materials.95 However, the performance of large-area MoS2 (saturated mobility (s) = 2.8 106 cm s1) devices is inferior to date; this implies that they are better suited as alternatives to TFTs, which are currently dominated by polycrystalline Si (s = 107 cm s1), OSCs, graphene (s = 5.5 107 cm s1) and metal oxides, such as InGaZnO.61,90,9698 Similarly, the reported BP-based electronic performance is also far from the theoretical value, which greatly limits its application. The most probable reason for this problem is inadequate device structure design and incomplete processing. In order to obtain high-performance electronic devices beyond the stability-enhancing approach, many other strategies, such as contact engineering and, most importantly, dielectric engineering, have been employed. Multilayer BP and InSe flakes show higher mobility than UTB technology; however, the issues of large-area growth and environmental stability must be resolved for practical viability while remaining sufficiently high to compete with alternative UTB technologies. Furthermore, although multilayer devices show higher mobility and are more forgiving for non-uniform large area growth, they compromise on short channel effects, which are considered to be among the most significant advantages of 2D electronic devices.

2.2. 2D layered transition metal oxide (LTMO) semiconductors

In 1990s, layered titanates were exfoliated for the first time by Sasaki et al.; this opened a new area in material science and technology known as LTMOs,99101 involving the precise design of nanostructures via layer by layer assemblage to afford significant improvements in properties such as conductivity, high SSAs, and charge separation.102105 LTMOs, e.g., layered niobates and titanates, show intercalation reactions such as cation exchange and grafting that are similar to those of layered silicates. The semiconductor properties of LTMOs and their versatility of composition for photocatalysis106,107 originally motivated their study. Currently, many types of LTMOs, such as manganese-,99 ruthenium-,108 and tantalum-oxides,109 have been attained from bulk ruthenium. Due to the easily polarized nature of O2, TMOs possess non-linear, non-uniform, and large-scale charge distributions within their lattices, which leads to electrostatic screening in the 1 to 100 nm range and produces excellent surface and interfacial properties. As a consequence, on the planar TMO surface, specific states of energy appear; this is different from bulk TMOs and also suggests the possibility of coulombic interactions with neighboring ions.110 Owing to the specific properties of TMOs due to the existence of oxygen and other characteristics, such as tuning of BGs, they have novel uses; the BGs of TMO honeycomb structures fluctuate in a wide range (1.64 to 7.98 eV at the G0W0 level of theory). Therefore, TMOs with large BGs, such as 1THfO2 (7.98 eV) and 1TGeO2 (7.07 eV), may show potential as high-k materials, whereas those with small BGs, such as 2HMoO2 (2.20 eV) and 1TNiO2 (2.15 eV), may be potential photocatalysts.

Interface potentials arise when two TMO materials are in contact, and the EF of the surface planes changes. The TMO surfaces are electronically activated because of hybridization of the ionic orbitals. When the TMO d-orbitals are aligned normal to their planes of spin, orbital ordering or frustration occurs,111 causing unconventional semiconducting properties. In oxygen, the presence of holes in the 2p-orbital results in magnetism.111 Magnetic frustration arises when cations are in the planar phase, resulting in complex anti-ferromagnetic structures and causing the Kondo effect.111 These effects can be minimized by a heterostructure approach by stacking different TMO sheets in a less regular order. In addition to oxygen, the fundamental properties of TMOs are also directed by cations (metals) and their flexibility to change oxidation states. TMO structures can be stabilized easily by simply selecting cations with different vacancies and binding relationships.112 Different metal vacancies provide different electronic properties; thus, materials ranging from metallic to insulating can be obtained for the same TMO with different stoichiometries. These different electronic properties present diversity in BGs, offering the prospect of electric and optical engineering at almost any desired wavelength.113,114 As a function of temperature and pressure (e.g. Mott and Verwey transitions), localized d-electronic states also govern the metalinsulator transformation. TMO redox properties are reversible115 and show excellent thermal and chemical stabilities.116,267 Tunability of TMOs can be achieved by using well-established methods of changing the oxygen electronic states.117 When there is a decrease in energy formation in TMOs, the cation reduction state increases, providing drastically different surface properties than their bulk counterparts. MoO3,118 WO3,119 Ga2O3,120 and V2O5121 exist naturally in anhydrate or hydrate phases as LTMOs (Fig. 4(a)). This indicates that these 2D oxides can be easily exfoliated to nanosheets and are stable in both water and air.

Another class of oxides, called perovskites, can also form TMOs with the general formula ABO3. The cubic unit cell of perovskite has an arrangement in which the B sites of the TM ions are in the center and the A sites are located at the corners (Fig. 4(f)). 2D perovskites can be synthesized directly by stacking through layer-by-layer deposition. There are three layered perovskites, i.e., DionJacobson (DJ), RuddlesdenPopper (RP) and Aurivillius (AU) phases (Fig. 4(f)). [Bi2O2]-[A(n1)BnO3n+1] is the general formula of AU phase for TMO synthesis. The rock-salt Bi2O2 functions as a separator for the AU phases. For example, SrBi2Ta2O9 and Bi2WO6 have been exfoliated successfully and are stable.112,122 The DJ phase general formula is MA(n1)BnO(3n+1), and their separators are lanthanide ions or alkali metal. CaLaNb2TiO10, La2Ti2NbO10, and CaLaNb2TiO10 have been exfoliated as DJ phase perovskites.102,112,123 The RP phase is similar to pristine perovskite, in which the separators are metal oxide layers, e.g., Ca2Ta2TiO10 and SrLaTi2TaO10.124,268 The perovskite parent structure in the 2D layered form is highly polar and has applications in ferroelectric and dielectric materials.102 Oxides lying between metals and insulators, e.g., plane cuprates, MnO2, and their composites, are usually taken as quasi-TMOs,125 where electrons can move freely in the weakly coupled oxide layers with SC-nature. For quasi-TMO stabilization and doping, ion layers such as strontium, barium, and lanthanum are used. Otherwise, these quasi-TMOs without doping can behave as Mott insulators. The growth of TMOs on substrates may influence the electronic, structural, and chemical properties of TMOs because of interactions between the TMOs. On one side, the interfacial interactions may cause intriguing phenomena, such as SC126 and quantum Hall effects.127 Conversely, TMO hybridization with 2D materials or substrates may adapt TMOs or compensate for the shortcomings of both parent materials in practical applications.125,128 For example, the interfacial interaction of TMOs with channel materials incorporating high k-dielectrics into microelectronic devices is very difficult but highly desirable because high k-dielectrics can considerably increase transistor gate capacity and efficiently decrease electron scattering through interfacial charged impurities.129

TMOs, due to their large BGs, are promising materials with high k-dielectrics to substitute conventional dielectric gates, e.g., SiO2. There are wide-range reports of planar TMOs utilized as semiconductor and insulating gate materials for creating FETs and memory devices. In FETs, the TMO is shaped into high carrier mobility doped regions near the interfaces for competent transport, like SCs. In traditional electronics, nanometer-thick dielectrics with minimal current leakage, low loss (tan) and high permittivity (high ) are desirable.130 Under the gate electrode, a typical modern transistor is made from SiO2, where the oxide has a gate length of 60 nm or less, and the gate oxide thickness decreases 1.5 nm below the critical limit, leading to quantum mechanical tunneling via the oxide layer, with current leakage beyond tolerable limits.131 According to their polarization nature, high- dielectrics can be broadly categorized:

(a) Non-polar atomic arrangements in metal oxides. These materials include Al2O3,132 TiO2,133 HfO2, Ta2O5,134 and similar oxide135,136 compounds that possess low tan; unfortunately, their permittivity is not as high as required (typically <100).

(b) Generally, very high- materials (typically >1000) are ferroelectrics that have residual dipoles; perovskites show a noteworthy range in this class.

Ferroelectric oxides such as Pb(Zr,Ti)O3 and (Ba1xSrx)TiO3 show wide static dipole moments. To obtain advantageous compositions with lower tan and high , mixed-phase or composite oxide materials are investigated.137,138 A high is valuable for screening disorder because of point charges in the environment or in the material itself; therefore, the electronic mobility increases. To increase the effective dielectric constant, high- oxides may be important as substrates or coating materials for other atomically thin materials.139,140 In the same way, a high dielectric constant may show the way toward high electron mobility in the oxide itself; for example, a mobility surpassing that of Si is experienced in 2D MoO3x because of this effect.141 Near the 2D-TMO surfaces, free charges may be confined in their electronic states, with elevated conductance. The electronic structure that can potentially host a 2D electron gas (2D-EG) relies on a heterostructure interface between two wide-BG metal oxides. Typical examples include LaAlO3/SrTiO3142 and ZnMgO/ZnO143 interfaces. Close to the TMO interface, the origin of 2D-EG is frequently connected to fields generated by O2 vacancies at interfaces that function as dopants. For developing resistive switching components, TMOs also demonstrate great potential for memristors. Numerous 2D-TMOs display modified conductance with applied voltage when sandwiched between two electrodes. These structural characteristics may demonstrate strong hysteresis, which is ascribed to diverse phenomena, including metallic or O2 component migration toward the 2D oxide or electrode that crosses the 2D layer via discharge filaments. Discharge of a filament can take place on either electrode or may be restricted to only one side in TMOs. Joule heating along a discharge filament can also transform the material.144 In TMOs, resistance switching is observed; typically, this switching is anion-based in perovskites, e.g., MnO2125 and ZnO,145 and simple oxides. The ferroelectric properties of TMOs can be particularly advantageous to establish memristors. In metal electrodes, carrier wave-functions can expand into the oxide because of strong ferroelectric polarization, permitting tunneling interactions into diagonally thin TMO insulators. For typical ferroelectric tunnel junctions, ferroresistive switches can be recognized as derived from ultrathin materials such as BaTiO3.146 For these structures, electric field changes are induced in the ferroelectric material by reversing the polarization by the interface transmission; therefore, the resistance of the device changes. The most interesting properties of switching memory performance include large on/off ratio resistance, low operating voltage, high staying power and long retention time. In 2D-hydrated WO3, the best characteristics are observed upon hydration, and the subsequent O2 deficiency improves the anion migration. In TMO semiconductors, free charge carriers are used for spintronic information.147 The EF imbalance spins result in spin polarization; hence, magnetism arises. In 2D-spintronic devices, there is a magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ). When the magnetic moments are opposite in direction, the tunnel is blocked. In this case, spin up electrons can simply channel into empty states with very high resistance. The resistance is low when the two magnetizations are parallel. The resistance-stable device depends on the field history and functions as a memory device. Magnetoresistive (MR) device function is based on MTJ. In 1995, the first RT-MR in MTJs was observed in Co/Al2O3/CoFe and Fe/Al2O3/Fe.148,149 The main MTJ applications are in non-volatile magnetic memory and in hard disc magnetic read heads.147 To obtain the strongest MR effects, the most desirable materials are half-metallic ferromagnetic. At the EF, the DOSs of half-metallic materials are finite for spin to 1D and 0D for the other. In spite of theoretical predictions based on calculations, very few TMOs have yielded good tunnel MR (TMR) values to date. 2D half-metal TMOs, including Fe3O4150 and CrO2,150 indicate TMRs of approximately 50%. In comparison, mixed-valence manganites151 and double-perovskites,152 such as La2/3Sr1/3MnO3 (LSMO) and La2/3Ca1/3MnO3 (LCMO), show TMR values of 100%.153 One more notable approach involves attempting to stimulate spin functionality in non-magnetic oxides during dilute doping of magnetic impurities into non-magnetic metal oxides such as Ti and ZnO.154 Conversely, to date, the most significant issue is to attain homogeneity for host dopants and secondary ferromagnetic phase possibilities. In addition, there are still many unknown defect aspects that must be addressed.154

In a lattice, off-center displacements of atoms are responsible for ferroelectricity. Generally, these displacements occur because of short-range covalent repulsion and long-range dipoledipole Coulomb interactions.155 Numerous mechanisms are suggested to be involved in 2D ferroelectric TMO conduction,155 including PooleFrenkel or FowlerNordheim tunneling, Schottky injection, the presence of grain boundaries, space charge limited currents, and more prominently, direct tunneling effects. For SrRuO3/2D-PZT/metal-based junctions, phonon-assisted tunneling or direct tunneling has been demonstrated to be a principal phenomenon, and the SNHs are smaller than theoretically proposed.156 The well-known ferroelectric material PZT is the most extensively observed material; it is mostly utilized as an active element in memory. This is because the switchability of instant polarization in ferroelectric 2D-TMOs and ferroelectric materials is executed satisfactorily in magnetic memory. In different 2D-TMOs, advantageous switching can be attained at comparatively low voltages. By utilizing a 2D ferroelectric material as an FET gate memory device, gate polarization can be sensed utilizing sourcedrain current adjustment. These devices demonstrate very low exhaustion under normal write/read cycles. 2D ferromagnetic insulators are still little known due to their relative scarcity.112 Most ferromagnetic insulators possess low Curie temperatures.157 As an alternative to insulating materials with more than one type of magnetic site, ferrimagnetic insulators shown ordering after antiferromagnetic super-exchange interactions. The major concern in 2D ferromagnetic insulators is utilization of tunnel barriers to entrap electrons selectively according to their spins for spintronic applications. TMO ferromagnetic insulators include very few known materials, e.g., SeCuO3158 and BiMnO3.158

2.2.1. Shortcomings of LTMOs. In their 2D forms, weak interlayer interactions mostly result in dangling bonds; this leads to strong surface polarization and induces 2D-TMO surface instability. Prominent lattice relaxation, structural rebuilding and substrate effects are recognized as main mechanisms in balancing these strong surface polarizations in 2D-LTMOs, as already observed in a Pd5O4 overlayer on Pd(111),159 a strained PdO(101) layer on Pd(100),159 a Ag1.83O trilayer on Ag(111),160 a RhO2 trilayer on Rh(111),161 multiple phases of 2D-Mn-oxides on Pd(100),141 and TiO2 on rutile TiO2(011).162 These noteworthy changes cause difficulty when fabricating 2D-LTMOs, in addition to posing a challenge for theoretical structure prediction results.

2.3. 2D layered halide perovskites

Due to the novel electronic characteristics of 2D semiconductors, they are promising successors to Si in future devices; a recent addition to the growing list of 2D semiconductors is the hybrid organicinorganic perovskites called 2D-layered halide perovskites (LHPs). These 2D perovskites have been established as novel solution-based 2D materials with interesting optical properties.163,164 On the other hand, unlike other 2D-semiconductors, in which covalent bonding is present, these 2D-hybrid perovskites undergo ionic bonding, which endows them with unique properties. The LHPs with the general notation ABX3, derived from metal-halide perovskites, have emerged as energy-efficient, profitable materials,165 where A denotes an inorganic or organic monovalent cation and B and X are substituted with a bivalent cation (Ge2+ Sn2+, or Pb2+) and a halogen, respectively. Layered dihalides and trihalides of TMs (e.g., FeCl2 and CrI3) and post-TMs (e.g., PbI2 and BiI3) also show unusual optical and magnetic behavior, such as ferromagnetism, in an insulating state.166,167 In 3D-perovskites, facile solid state ion inter-diffusion is an elementary issue in maintaining a stable heterojunction.168 The LHP family can be further subcategorized as RuddlesdenPopper perovskites (RPP), which are derived from 3D perovskites and shaped by slicing a 3D structure into 2D blocks, as shown in Fig. 5. Generally, the layered RPP chemical formula is (RNH3)2(A)n1MX3n+1, in which R is a long alkyl chain/aromatic group; A and M are a small monovalent metal cation (i.e. methylammonium) and divalent metal cation, respectively, X is a halide functional group, and n is an integer. In each layer, a 2D network of [MX6]4 octahedra resides at the corners and mediates the RNH3+ bilayers in these built-in 2D-crystal structures. Both sides of the long chains are capped to balance the charges, and intact unit-layer crystals are stacked together with vdW forces. The presence of RNH3+ (insulating dielectric) in the bilayers can be considered as a class of natural quantum wells (QWs) by means of their degree of quantum confinement. By changing the inorganic layer thickness, the relevant optical properties are customized. Compared to 3D-perovskites, which function as free charge carrier semiconductors, robustly retained 2D-perovskites (n 5) display strongly bound excitons with BE >100 meV.169 At RT, this produces deep PL with a single sharp color peak and strong exciton photon coupling.170,171 Lead halide RPPs also have enhanced chemical stability compared to 3D-hybrid perovskites and represent alternatives to 3D-hybrid perovskites in optoelectronics.171,172 Particularly, the chemical diversity of 2D-perovskites and their quantum confinement permit powerful PL emissions ranging from the UV to the NIR region173,174 which were not obtainable from previously reported 2D materials. The 2D sheets can be fabricated by solution-based methods or physical exfoliation.164,175 Suitable RPPs with confined dimensions were obtained as building blocks for photonic devices, e.g., LEDs.176178 However, most of these materials are not compatible with semiconductor processing, which implies that their applications in solid-state devices are limited. To obtain 2D-RP perovskites, mechanical exfoliation is a suitable method because the vdWs in the layered crystal structure are similar to conventional 2D materials.179 Using micromechanical exfoliation, a single layer to several layers of (C6H9C2H4NH3)2PbI4 flakes were obtained, as shown in Fig. 5(a).175 This method is not used in practical material manufacturing due to the lack of thickness and size control.163,180

2.3.1. Shortcomings of 2D perovskite materials and their solutions. 2D perovskite materials have made great achievements in various fields; however, there are still many obstacles for real application of 2D-perovskites. Despite the fact that 2D-perovskites are more stable than 3D-perovskites, they still face durability problems in device fabrication. Further efforts are required to first comprehend their deterioration mechanisms and determine straightforward methods to improve their structure stability. Single crystals along large lateral dimensions are extremely desirable for photodetectors. To date, single crystal-controllable large-scale synthesis of 2D-perovskite has not yet been achieved. Mixed n-2D-RPP based LEDs always demonstrate high performance, such as high EQE and luminescence. The LED performance is also affected by capping the long chains. The effect of long chain capping on LED performance is still not known. Long-term stability of metal-halide perovskite in moist environments is a supreme obstacle to the commercialization of perovskite optical devices; meanwhile, incorporating graphene or other 2D-TMDCs in perovskite films may offer a feasible solution to this obstacle and recover their properties.193

2.4. 2D carbide and nitride semiconductors (MXenes)

Layered 2D-carbide MXenes such as Mo2C23 as well as nitrides or carbonitrides177,194 are also being explored for optoelectronics. They share a general formula Mn+1Xn (n = 1 to 3), where M is an early TM and X = N or C. Carbide synthesis started in 2011 with Ti3C2.194 Generally, these materials consist of a layered structure of M with (n + 1) layers linked by n layers of X in a [MX]nM manner. The ML thickness of the MXene stack is generally less than 1 nm, allowing for a relatively small number of atomic layers. The ML thicknesses and lattice constants of carbide-based MXenes are greater than those of 12 nitride-based MXenes, which is related to the difference in atomic radii between N and C. The obtained stability trends can be seen from their Eform negative values as Ti2C < Ti3C2 < Ti4C3 and Ti2N < Ti3N2 < Ti4N3, which also correlates with their cohesive energy (Ecoh) calculations. It is also worth noting that Eform of Tin+1Nn is smaller than that of Tin+1Cn; therefore, synthesis of free-standing Tin+1Nn is more challenging, which has been proved by experimental observations. The Tin+1Nn adsorption energy entails a stronger predilection to hold fast to the terminal groups, which provides further active surfaces for nitride-based MXenes. In these MXenes, the overall calculated electrical conductance is higher than that of carbide-based MXenes. Mostly, the MXenes contain numerous surface functional groups, e.g., fluorine, hydroxyl, and O2. MXenes exclusively unite TM carbide/nitride conductivity with their OH/O2/F-terminated hydrophilic surfaces. This endows MXenes with beneficial electronic, magnetic, and optical properties.

Principally, metal ion selection decides the magnetism and electronic conductance of an MXene; they are also considerably affected by electron donating groups. Alternatively, MXene luminescence is largely attributed to size; defect-induced luminescence effects and quantum confinement also contribute. Conversely, before biomedical application of hydrophobic nanoparticles (e.g., upconversion nanoparticles, quantum dots (QDs)), complex surface modification is required. A variety of MXene compositions are found to be non-toxic; hence, they are biocompatible for living organisms.195 This is because C and N are major elements which are essential to maintain biological organism structures. A few early TMs, e.g., Nb, Ta, and Ti, are comparatively non-reactive to biological systems. Experimental studies on mice established that these MXenes can be degraded in mice.195 MXenes have strong absorption in the near-IR region, which is sufficient for both in vivo photothermal therapy (PTT) and photoacoustic imaging (PAI) in the first or second biological window. Additionally, carbide MXenes can be flexibly functionalized with surface modifications using different functional groups, which paves the way to new biomedical applications. These functionalized carbides and nitrides are 2 to 6-fold stronger than bulk MoS2 or graphite.196 Single stack MXene production by simple mechanical exfoliation is not sufficient. To imbalance the electronic charges, defects will be introduced.197,198 The surface terminations also affect the conductivity of carbide and nitride MXenes.198 These surface terminations of MXenes are applied for sensing of numerous gases and other reducing/oxide molecules. The MXene conductivity changes when these attached molecules on the MXene surface are exposed.198,199 The change in electrical conductivity produces electronic signals that are promising in the ultrasensitive sensing of definite biomarkers. MXene semiconductor properties are fundamentally helpful for specific applications, e.g., theoretical studies of biological catalysis also proposed the key factors that influence the optical properties of MXenes. In a typical example, M2C (M = Ti, Zr, Hf) has BGs in the range of 0.92 to 1.75 eV depending on the M composition; these BGs fall in the visible spectrum range.200

The BGs of MXenes can also be engineered to absorb UV radiation by changing their surface terminations, as shown in Table 2. Under termination-free conditions, several MXenes studied by theory were predicted to have magnetic properties (e.g., ferromagnetic and anti-ferromagnetic).203,204 However, due to the complexity of the synthesis of MXenes with termination-free surfaces, these magnetic properties are much less supported by experiments. In the etching synthesis method, the carbide MXene (MAX) phases are terminated; MXene surfaces require several hydrophilic terminal groups to be used as potential catalyst materials. Upon surface termination, the MXene formula becomes Mn+1XnTx, where M is an early TM, X = N or C, and Tx = the surface termination functional group (such as OH, O, or F). Mostly, MXenes are synthesized from selective etching of A (group IIIAVIA) elements from MAX phases; however, other synthesis methods have also been introduced in which the precursors are being further investigated.208 MXenes show a distinctive mixture of metallic conductance due to the TM carbide or nitride main structure and surface termination-free electrons. Due to their high electronic conductance and versatile chemistry, they have been proven to be promising for a range of applications, including transparent conductors,209,210 energy storage,208 electromagnetic interference shielding,211 pressure and gas sensors,212215 water purification,216218 photo and electrocatalysis,219 thermoelectrics,220222 and plasmonics.222,223 Due to the availability of a large range of TMs and surfaces decorated with functional groups, the properties of MXenes can be engineered through specifying combinations of TMs and X elements and by guiding their surface chemistry. Approximately 30 experimentally obtained MXenes are reported, and many more have been predicted theoretically.224235 Approximately 100 MAX phases were synthesized by controlling the number of double TM structures, providing continuous and rapidly increasing opportunities for forming MXenes with advantageous properties.236

2D titanium carbide/nitride-based MXenes have been synthesized; however, insufficient methodical studies have established their structure, electronic and elastic properties and therefore discovered their promising applications. The surface chemistry and structural stability of 2D material carbide/nitride-based MXenes are controlled through a few basic structural characteristics, such as equilibrium lattice constants, ML thickness, and atomic composition. The limitations in the experimental synthesis methods of ML MXenes and the scarcity of investigations of their atomic level structures are the main reasons for their limited electronic and optoelectronic device applications. Two experimental studies to date have been carried out to study the structural properties of Ti3C2Tx ML flakes by STEM. The surface kinetics and structure237 in addition to intrinsic point defects237,238 of Ti3C2Tx nanosheets were studied at the atomic level. Density functional theory (DFT) is another method to study the structure stability and surface chemistry of MXenes through measuring the Ecoh and surface adsorption energy. Ecoh, which is the energy required to detach the condensed material into isolated free atoms, is one of the most significant physical quantities in quantifying the thermal stability of a material. However, most previously studied DFT calculations have concentrated on investigating the layer thicknesses and lattice parameters of Ti-based MXenes239,240 and were not able to predict the structure stability and surface properties of the MXenes. There are only two DFT calculation studies241,242 that calculate the MXene Ecoh; however, they did not study distinct MXenes, and the consequence of terminal groups on Ecoh was also not considered. The surface properties of 2D materials are also very important for catalysis applications.243,244

The functionalization effects of MXenes with O terminated groups result in lower Ecoh than that of OH and F-terminated MXenes; thus, it can be concluded that the structural stability of functionalized MXenes decreases in this order: Tin+1CnO2 > Tin+1CnF2 > Tin+1Cn(OH)2 and Tin+1NnO2 > Tin+1NnF2 > Tin+1Nn(OH)2. This finding is consistent with nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy findings.245 Generally, Ecoh considerably decreases upon surface functionalization mainly with F and O groups, which shows that completely functionalized MXenes are more stable compared to their counterpart pure phases. This also offers theoretical enlightenment of why functionalized MXenes with terminal groups are generally experimentally synthesized.234 In addition, it is observed that the Ecoh values of Tin+1Cn and Tin+1CnT2 are lower compared to those of Tin+1Nn and Tin+1NnT2 with the same ML thickness, which shows that carbide-based MXenes are more stable in comparison to nitride-based MXenes. The experimental observations show good agreement with this conclusion.246,247

The first and most studied Ti3C2Tx MXene possesses the highest conductance (10000 S cm1) in thin film. The electronic conductance of MXenes is highly dependent on their etching and delamination methods along with their synthesis treatments. Due to the strong response of carbide MXenes to external stimulus, they have potential for use in chemical, light, and pressure sensors. The 2D nature of MXenes enables them to interact with other 2D materials through vdW heterostructures and results in some interesting electronic properties. Based on their plasmonic and optical properties, they have applications in ultrafast lasers, optical communication, surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) substrates, metamaterial broadband absorbers, and light-to-heat conversion. Mostly, MXenes are arousing increasing interest in PTT.

2.4.1. Shortcomings and solutions of 2D carbide and nitride semiconductors (MXenes). Despite the extensive research on carbide/nitride MXenes, the understanding of these exciting materials is still not fully developed. Many of their primary properties remain unexplored, e.g., their surface termination is still a question. Experimental synthesis of carbide/nitride MXenes with uniform surface termination remains one of the most essential and difficult tasks. MXenes without surface termination are synthesized by CVD in vacuum or by other methods. In addition, native carbide/nitride MXene semiconductors have still not been experimentally reported, despite DFT calculations. DFT has predicted several MXenes that retain full magnetization at RT, which is attractive in 2D spintronics. The optical properties of MXenes are also very interesting. However, the mechanisms of electromagnetic wave interaction with MXenes are not fully comprehended; these have significant importance in EMI shielding, plasmonic and photonics applications. It has been found that theoretical calculations can envisage MXene BGs with high accuracy. In future, theoretical calculations will play a major role in predicting MXene properties. It would be fruitful to take aid from experimental studies of other well-matured 2D materials (e.g. graphene or TMDC) to study the fundamental, optical, electrical, and magnetic properties of carbide/nitride MXenes.

2.5. 2D layered double hydroxide (LDHs)

Layered double hydroxides (LDHs) are in the class of 2D-clay materials; hence, they are also known as anionic clays. LDHs have the general formula M1x2+Mx3+(OH)2 [Ax/pp]x+mH2O, where M2+ and M3+ are metal cations with divalent and trivalent valences, respectively. Divalent (Ca2+, Fe2+, Zn2+, etc.) metals in the metallic layer LDH structure are sometimes substituted with trivalent metal (Fe3+, Al3+) cations, ensuing in a cationic brucite-like structure. In the interlayer space, these positive charges are balanced by anions and water molecules. The cavities and large specific surface areas (SSA) of LDHs expand their reach as multifunctional materials. Generally, LDHs are widely used as ion-exchangers, catalysts, fillers in polymers, and adsorbents. 2D clay materials have large SSAs, biocompatibility and pH sensitivity; thus, they are fascinating materials for drug and gene transport. Indeed, LDHs are potential inorganic carriers for genes, DNA, and other anionic bio-molecules, with an extra benefit of solubility in acidic medium. LTMHOs, e.g., Mn(OH)2 and double hydroxides, are frequently synthesized by wet chemical methods and are finding applications in CO2 reduction, energy storage, and hydrogen evolution. Layered materials based on actinides (USe2) and rare earth metals (GdCl3) have also been synthesized and characterized; however, their applications have not been explored extensively. LDHs contain numerous OH groups covalently bonded in 2D host films; they are anticipated to function as inorganic anion conductors. Generally, for bulk or lamellar LDH-OH, the ion conductivities are up to 103 S cm1, which is considered to be insufficient for real applications. ML-LDH nanosheets displayed remarkably high in-plane conductance, attaining 101 S cm1, which is the highest reported among anion conductors and similar to those of proton conductors in commercially available proton exchange membranes (e.g., Nafion). The in-plane direction conductivities are four to five times higher in magnitude than the cross-plane magnitude of the restacked LDH nanosheets. This impressive 2D ionic transport feature possesses enormous potential in different applications.

Hydrogen bonding networks among water molecules in aqueous phases are responsible for the propagation of protons and OH ions, according to the Grotthuss mechanism,248 with enormously increased ion mobility. In comparison, other hydrated ions are generally proliferated via physical interactions with low ion mobility that is one order of magnitude lower than those of OH and proton ions.249,250 Therefore, broad extended hydrogen bonding networks supplied by adsorbed water and the covalently bound OH host layer in LDH nanosheets may assist the ultrafast conduction of OH ions based on fast hydrogen bond breakage/reorganization. Consequently, the conduction of physically transferred interlayer hydrated anions is expected to be much slower than that of OH ions and thus can be overlooked. The in-plane OH ion conductivities of ML LDH nanosheets (10 to 100 layers) on comb electrodes were investigated. Both CoAl and MgAl-LDH nanosheets demonstrated amplified conductivities with increasing temperature and RH. For a general 2D-LDH material at 80% RH and 60 C, the measured values are on the order of 102 S cm1, which indicates that high in-plane OH conductance should maintained. Particularly, the lateral dimension reduction from several 100 nm to one nm leads to enhanced in-plane ion conductivities.

However, it is remarkable that multilayer in-plane conductivities are lower than that of ML-LDH; this indicates that in-plane conduction may be slightly compromised by increased steric incarceration from the narrowed interlayer spacing in addition to electrostatic repulsion from the sandwich and double interlayer anions. For multilayer MgAl-LDH nanosheets, in a representative sample deposited on comb electrodes, the in-plane ionic conductivity was measured with temperature dependence for over half a year (a large time interval). The results demonstrated that the difference in ionic conductivity was negligible after this long time, proving that the as-exfoliated LDH nanosheets possess outstanding stability in the form of OH ion conduction. It is notable that the measured conductivities of the LDH nanosheets appeared to be more sensitive to changes in humidity compared to those of LDH platelets, concluding that the LDH nanosheet surfaces may be more available to water compared to the interlayer LDH platelet spaces. This can be simplified as a significant advantage of exfoliating lamellar LDHs to ML sheets to reveal considerably more available SSA and adsorb additional water content under these conditions. Composite materials are synthesized by combining two or more constituent materials to obtain enhanced or tailored properties.7,8,13,255258 Sometimes, the properties of pristine materials are not sufficient to meet device application criteria, such as the 2D material pristine graphene. The pristine 2D MXenes as well as composite materials such as layered graphene/semiconductors may have large impacts in myriad applications. Composite materials that act as single-photon emitters may be valuable in quantum information technology, where the ability to produce a single photon on demand enables new applications. 4.1. 2D-TMDC semiconductor applications

Flexible technology comprises a large group of increased surface area devices, including thin film transistors (TFTs), transducers, sensors, and displays. Doping methods are carried out on 2D semiconductors to modify their BGs for device applications. The MoS2 in transistors is naturally n-type doped, and the doping is relatively facile. One type of doping, called electrostatic doping, is used to generate depletion mode and enhancement mode of MoS2; MoS2-based transistors generate pseudo-complementary inverters. p-Type or n-type doping in WSe2 channels is achieved via employing either contact metal WFs or an amalgamation of chemical and electrostatic doping of the contact metal. For ambipolar phosphorene, a parallel charge neutrality point splitting method was utilized to electrostatically dope n- and p-type transistors to generate corresponding pairs. Single 2D materials face challenges in complementary inverters because the conventional chemical doping method is not promising due to the atomically thin nature of the material channels. Combining inverters with transistors to acquire the benefits of doping with two diverse materials has been proved to generate CMOS inverters, which are not dependent on electrostatic doping.

Regarding electronic properties such as on/off (Ion/Ioff) ratio versus mobility spectrum, commonly known TMDCs, such as MoS2, have relatively small mobilities but exceptionally high Ion/Ioff ratios. Due to their wide BGs, numerous TMDCs have very small Ioff values; thus, they are very suitable for small power applications. In graphene, due to the poor Ion/Ioff current ratio and absence of a BG, graphene transistors are more useful in high power consumption devices than other commonly used FET technologies. Phosphorene can bypass the gap between MoS2 and graphene with an Ion/Ioff ratio around 104 by means of its mobility of 100s of cm2 V1 s1. A major side effect is contact resistance, which much be decreased for suitable device performance. Graphene devices possess the lowest contact resistances. In comparison to graphene, the contact engineering of phosphorene and TMDCs is less developed because of the relatively juvenile nature of 2D materials, especially the latter. W and Mo-based TMDC MLs possess direct BGs in the range of 1.5 to 1.9 eV. For MoS2 and WSe2-based transistors, the FE mobility ranges from 1 to 200 cm2 V1 s1, with switch Ion/Ioff ratios typically approaching 105 to 107. Compared to Si, these well-known 2D materials do not have good electrical properties; however, they appear to be suitable for use in some functional applications. With their ability to withstand 30% strain, which limits irreparable damage to the crystal lattice, TMDCs have revealed potential applications in foldable electronics; they compare satisfactorily to Si lattices, which are irreversibly damaged upon application of more than 10% strain. When their flexibility is compared to that of generally used semiconductors, such as amorphous Si (a-Si) and OSCs, TMDCs possess a minimum of an order of magnitude higher FE mobility, enabling advanced switching when transformed. Due to the lack of BG in graphene and its poor Ion/Ioff ratio, graphene transistors are inclined to expend more power than other FET technologies.

Composite material synthesis approaches have been found to be very helpful to improve Rc for MoS2 devices, e.g., TMDC devices fabricated using graphene. The use of graphene as a contact has unlocked electrostatic tuning possibilities to decrease the distinction between the electron affinity of the semiconductive channel and the effective WF of the contact, which ultimately decreases the device Rc. Unlike graphene or MoS2, phosphorene has a unique property of anisotropy. The anisotropic nature of phosphorene suggests its use in new devices, e.g., (i) plasmonic devices and (ii) highly efficient thermoelectrics, by orthogonal-direction electron transport and heat transfer. Unfortunately, phosphorene exfoliation degrades in the atmospheric environment. A capping agent, ALD, effectively protects the phosphorene sheets and facilitates their air stability for months of surveillance. Both Si and OSCs usually have electronic conduction less than 1 cm2 V1 s1, which is very small. The other 2D materials discussed in this review are less mature in photonic, electronic, and sensor applications due to their lower stability at ambient conditions.

4.2. Applications of 2D layered transition metal oxides (TMOs)

4.2.1. Gas sensor application of TMOs. TMOs such as TiO2, MoO3, WO3 and SnO2 are widely applied in gas sensing.269,270 At high temperature, they are used for chemisorption, and below 150 C, they are used for physisorption due to their high SSAs. In the chemisorption procedure, 2D V2O5, SnO2, and TiO2 cooperate in a reductive way while O2 is removed, whereas 2D WO3, Nb2O5 and MoO3 are interconnected and become ultra-doped with reducing gases such as H2. This interaction finally leads to water production. In addition to their conductance, Nb2O5, WO3 and MoO3 are gasochromic oxides; their color changes when they are exposed to reducing gases.271273 2D MoO3 has also been studied in plasmonic gas sensing and possesses a BG in the visible to IR range for plasmon absorption.274
4.2.2. Biological, optical and chemical sensor applications of TMOs. 2D-TMOs have shown diversity in biological sensors. For example, TiO2, FeOx, ZnO, MnO2, WO3 and MoO3 have been demonstrated for biosensors, bio-imaging and drug delivery to date. They offer abilities to increase and facilitate contrast in imaging, electro and photo-luminescence, facile surface decoration with functional groups, opportunity for producing polymeric composites, high surface areas, liquid media stability and anisotropic charge transfer benefits. TMOs are popular due to their low toxicity; only V2O5 is slightly genotoxic, and recently there has also been some concern regarding MoO3 toxicity.275 At present, 2D TMO exploration for biological applications is still limited. This is due to the basic nature and toxicity of these materials, which arises from the exposed crystals at their edge facets, chemical compositions and defect concentrations, with similar trends to 2D TMDs.276 The in vitro cell viability of 2D TMOs, including MnO2 and MoO3, has been reported to evaluate their toxicity. It was found that 2D MoO3 layers increase toxicity in the Michigan Cancer Foundation-7 (MCF-7) breast cancer cell line with the aid of caspase activation, but not in HaCAT cells; this suggests promising applications in breast cancer treatment.277 The platelets of 2D MoO3 have also been observed to be toxic to bacteria. On the other hand, 2D MnO2 was reported to considerably decrease cell feasibility in the breast cancer cell line MCF-7, suggesting that 2D TMO use should be restricted to avoid cell deterioration.278 The in vivo toxicity of 2D TiO2 was tested in a mouse model; it demonstrated low toxicity subsequent to intra-peritoneal inoculation, although accumulation of particles in the liver was noted, leading to slight abnormalities after long-term exposure.279

Balendhran et al. synthesized an FET-based biosensor platform using 2D MoO3 nanoflakes as a conductive channel.280 These biosensors showed activity within 10 s for bovine serum albumin, and the detection limit was as low as 250 g m L1; the outstanding response characteristics were attributed to the high permittivity of the 2D MoO3. It is remarkable that the MoO3 detection limit for bovine serum albumin changed to 1 pg m L1; the high level of ndoping in MoO3 enabled its application in a plasmonic biosensing platform.281 In a 2D MnO2, Yuan et al. utilized MnO2 nanosheets to fabricate independent ochratoxin A and cathepsin D biosensors with correspondingly favorable activities; this indicates that MnO2 nanosheets can be used as nano-platforms in homogeneous biosensing.282 Alternatively, Zhai et al. studied the mechanism of interaction of ML MnO2 nanosheets with 7hydroxycoumarin283 and established a new procedure for in vivo sensing of ascorbic acid with enhanced efficiency using MnO2 nanosheets. Due to their strong dd transitions and ultimately rigorous light absorption, 2D-TMOs are appropriate for use in constructing optical biosensing platforms. Consequently, they show decreased fluorophore luminescence after conjugation, mostly due to Frster resonance energy transfer (FRET).284 After analyte exposure, 2D-TMO causes alteration either in the binding affinity or structure and luminescence of the target material, and that of 2D-TMO is decreased. These two mechanisms result in re-establishment of the luminescence. The most studied biosensor material in the 2D-TMO domain is 2D MnO2 (Fig. 13(a)).

4.3. Applications of 2D layered halide perovskites

4.3.1. 2D halide perovskite optoelectronic applications. For 2D halide perovskites, the most interesting optoelectronic property is the quantum confinement effect, which emerges due to the large exciton BE as the dielectric screening is decreased. In 2D-RPPs, the excitons mostly show ML (n = 1) and Wannier-type BEs. The 2D-RPPs are generally in a 170 to 480 meV range, depending on the encapsulate organic layer. Typically, this large exciton BE is higher than the thermal energy at RT (kBT = 25.7 meV), i.e., the eh pairs originating from photogeneration are bound collectively by Coulomb attraction, so they are stable at RT and difficult to break down through thermal fluctuation. Through this approach, a sharp and strong peak underlying the BG onset can be observed at RT because of bound excitons (Fig. 15(a)). Yaffe et al. observed that the BE of BA2PbI4 (a 2D material with single unit cell thickness) is as high as 490 meV.163 This value is already much higher compared to its bulk material configuration and compared with MLs of subsequent 2D-RPPs (360 meV); this was attributed to the different environment. The exciton BE is also extremely dependent on the number of layers (i.e. the QW width), in which larger 2D-RPPs may have smaller exciton BEs due to decreased quantum confinement.297,298 Furthermore, the exciton BE can be approximately estimated from the difference between the onset of absorption and the first exciton absorption peak in the corresponding absorption spectrum at low temperature.299,300 Still, it is difficult to obtain a precise exciton BE value from the RT absorption spectrum due to the thermal broadening range.

Significantly, due to the large exciton BE of 2D-RPP, they are suitable choices for LED devices due to their enhanced radiative recombination and sharp exciton emission peaks. In reality, 2D-RPPs also have high PL QYs. Yuan et al. observed that colloidal 2D-RPP microdisks demonstrate up to 53% PL quantum yield by means of a fine dark blue emission.302 The large exciton BE of 2D RP perovskite demonstrates especially quick rapid radiative recombination of photo-generated holes. Through this method, the lifetime of the charge carriers is shorter than that in 3D perovskites, ranging from several to 10 ns.303 The large n values (>2) for 2D-RPPs indicated another attractive photophysics occurrence, which is exciton disintegration into free carriers at lower energy levels. In other words, once the carriers are entrapped at the edges, they will remain confined, and their energy will not decrease due to side-to-side non-radiative processes. Therefore, this activity can be developed to improve optoelectronic device performance. The 2D-RPPs with increasing n values show decreased BGs because of decreased quantum confinement.297 The combined effects of the BG and exciton BE on 2D-RPPs with a number of layers simultaneously influence the PL and absorption spectra; thus, it is more complicated to set the optical parameters. In any case, the photoluminescence peak shift and excitonic absorption peak to the low energy edge were observed with increasing layer number n (Fig. 15(b)).304

Blancon et al. systematically studied the exciton property dependence of 2D perovskite BA2MAn1PbnI3n+1 on the n value using optical spectroscopy and calculated the magnetoabsorption at 60 T. The findings showed that both the exciton BE and BG decreased with increasing n together with decreasing exciton-reduced mass.305 Furthermore, the BG of 2D-RPP is also affected by its weak physical thickness. Fig. 15(c) shows that the emission peaks of BA2PbBr4 sheets move toward lower wavelengths as their thickness increases.164 The PL peak blue shift may be due to structural relaxation of the in-plane crystal lattice; this shows that the vdW interactions between layers can also affect the 2D-RPP electrical properties even though the interactions are very weak. For PEA2PbI4, similar results were observed.306 Similar to other 2D inorganic semiconductors, the BG of atomically thin MAPbI3 was observed to decrease with increasing number of layers.307 For ML MAPbI3, a PL emission peak centered at 720 nm was observed, and it increased stepwise to 765 nm for the bulk (Fig. 15(d)). The BGs and PL peaks of MAPbI3 nanoplates were observed to change with thickness, as shown in Fig. 15(e). The enlarged BG of the ultra-thin MAPbI3 nanoplates can be attributed to the quantum confinement effect. However, the BG of ML MAPbI3 was still much smaller than that of 2D RPP MLs. The only difference between the MAPbI3 ML and 2D-RPP MLs is the existence of an organic encapsulating layer on the 2D-RPPs, which may lead to inorganic layer lattice distortion. Consequently, the different BGs can be ascribed to the different dielectric environments and lattice distortions. The 2D-RPP absorption spectrum, like those of 3D perovskites, can be readily engineered through partial substitution of halide cations, which are appropriate for tunable wavelength LEDs and arrangement of solar cells. Lanty et al. observed that the excitonic absorption of PEA2PbX4 (X = I, Br) can be readily modified through the partial substitution of Cl with Br, as illustrated in Fig. 15(f).308 The tunable absorption was attributed to BG tuning through the partial substitution of cations. Weidman et al. observed RPP nanoplatelets and concluded that the absorption spectrum and emission energy of materials with the chemical formula L2An1BnX3n+1 could be changed by changing the n values, B halide cation and X anion, while variation of the A cation only led to subtle changes.173 In addition, mixed halide nanoplatelets showed continuous spectral tunability from 2.2 to 3.7 eV, covering a spectral range of 1.5 eV.

4.3.2. Light emitting diode (LED) applications of 2D halide perovskites. 2D perovskites have large exciton BEs (>26 meV) and high PL QYs; therefore, they are also appropriate for use in LED devices. Initial investigations on LEDs based on 2D-RPPs can be traced back to the mid 1990s.309,310 For example, Era et al. synthesized the first 2D-RPP (PEA2PbI4)-based LEDs and illustrated a sharp emission at 520 nm with a full width half maximum (FWHM) of 10 nm at liquid-N temperature with high luminescence (10000 cd m2 at 24 V).28 The electroluminescence activity of RT was very small, possibly due to exciton thermal ionization. At any rate, its low working temperature considerably limited its advancement. Due to the growing interest of perovskites in LEDs, 2D-RPPs have regained consideration. At RT, 2D-RPP material-based LED devices with a single constituent can function efficiently; however, their external quantum efficiency (EQE) is still low.176,311 For example, Liang et al. observed (PEA)2PbBr4 nanoplates-based LEDs with deep blue (410 nm) emission in an ITO/PEDOT:PSS (30 nm)/PEA2PbBr4/TPBi (35 nm)/Ca (25 nm)/Al (100 nm) device structure (Fig. 16(a)).312 These devices showed strong light discharge by means of an ultra-narrow (14 nm) FWHM (Fig. 16(b)), and the EQE was found to be 0.04%, which is remarkably high for deep blue LEDs. The improved EQE was due to the conversion of the polycrystalline PEA2PbBr4 films into single-crystal nanoplates (Fig. 16(c)). Li et al. also reported 526 nm LED multilayer (C6H5CH2NH3)2PbI4 devices, which emitted light with a narrow FWHM of 15 nm.31 These devices provided 9 cd m2 luminescence at 5 V with a low (0.005%) EQE. In addition, these devices could only work for 1 min and were not stable. This low EQE has not been well recognized to date; it may be attributable to strong excitonphonon coupling in ML 2D-RPPs.312 For n 2, it is generally complicated to obtain pure phase 2D-RPP materials. However, several researchers found that LEDs consisting of 2D-RPP mixed phases could yield excellent activity compared to those prepared with single constituent counterparts.313315

4.3.3. Shortcomings of 2D halide perovskites. The long term stability of metal-halide perovskites in humid environments is a major limitation to the industrialization of perovskite optical applications; incorporating graphene or other 2D-TMDCs in perovskite films may represent a plausible solution to this shortcoming and enhance the properties of the films. Recent developments have confirmed that perovskite stability can be greatly enhanced through chemical surface-ligand termination and functionalization/passivation; however, more attention should be paid to improving the long-term stability of perovskite materials and the reproducibility of these devices. Although 2D perovskites, particularly 2D-RPPs, are more stable than 3D perovskites, the established devices still face durability problems. Understanding their deterioration mechanisms is the first and uppermost requirement; later, simple techniques should be established to recover their stability.

4.4. Applications of 2D carbide/nitride Mxenes

A conductive TM carbide/nitride core layer facilitates high electronic conductivity; thus, in applications of thin films of Ti3C2Tx, the first and most studied MXene, conductance up to 10000 S cm1 can be achieved. The electronic conductance of MXenes is related to their delamination and etching methods in addition to their post-synthesis treatments. Due to the strong response of carbide MXene conductivity to external stimulus, they are potential candidates for light, pressure, and chemical molecule sensors. The 2D nature of MXenes enables them to form vdW heterostructures with other 2D materials with appealing electronic properties. By exploiting their plasmonic and optical properties, applications such as optical communication, ultrafast lasers, metamaterial broadband absorbers and light-to-heat conversion are realized. Predominantly, using MXenes in PTT is a growing interest. Despite several years of widespread investigation of MXenes, research into this stimulating material family is still in an early phase. Eventually, MXenes without surface functionalization should be fabricated by CVD in vacuum or by other methods. Furthermore, pristine MXene semiconductors have not been experimentally synthesized to date, in spite of DFT calculations. The experimental dimensions of their electronic band structures and surface termination effects in addition to their TM oxidation states have not been reported. A number of stimulating properties, e.g., SC, topological insulation, and half-metallicity, are predicted by DFT and are awaiting experimental evidence. A number of MXenes are proposed to display strong magnetization at RT, which is attractive in 2D spintronics. The optical properties of MXenes have also been observed. However, the mechanisms of the interaction of MXenes with electromagnetic waves have not been clearly explained; this is of vital importance in photonics, EMI shielding, and plasmonic applications. MXenes produced from non-fluorinated etchants are efficient to produce in large amounts and are particularly used in biomedical devices. Chemical modifications or heating procedures should be developed to obtain homogeneous surface terminations. The experience built up from fundamental studies of TMDCs, graphene, and other 2D materials, such as their optical, electronic, and magnetic properties, should be applied in carbide/nitride MXenes.
4.4.1. Applications of 2D carbide/nitride MXenes in electromagnetic interference shielding. Due to the high electrical conductance of MXenes, they are promising in EMI shielding effect (SE) applications, where high conductance is a prerequisite. EMI-SE is directly related to the thickness and electrical conductance of the material, as depicted by Simon formalism.325Fig. 17(a)201 shows the electrical conductivities of the Ti3C2Tx, Mo2TiC2Tx, and Mo2Ti2C3Tx films, and Fig. 17(b) shows their analogous EMI-SEs with 2.5 m thick films. EMI-SEs for other MXene material films with the same thickness are given in Fig. 17(c).201 A 45 m thick Ti3C2Tx film showed 92 dB EMI-SE, which converts to 99.99% EMI blockage in the microwave range. To enhance their environmental and mechanical stabilities and shrink their masses, MXenes are inserted into polymer matrices. Due to their high conductance and large characteristic ratios, MXenes may cause network strain at moderately small filler concentrations. For example, Ti3C2Tx/sodium alginate (SA) composites showed 0.5 and 3000 S cm1 at 10 and 90 wt% MXene weight, respectively. The 9 m thick composite film demonstrated 57 dB EMI-SE. Further new work on Ti3C2Tx/polystyrene (PS) composites showed 10.8 S cm1 at 1.9 vol% with 54 dB EMI-SE.326 The latter study indicated that comparatively high conductance was achieved with low MXene concentrations, probably due to the electrostatic assemblage used to synthesize the composites, as shown in Fig. 17(d). Tong et al. synthesized Ti3C2Tx/polypyrrole (PPy) composites utilizing an in situ chemical oxidative method; they indicated that a 25 wt% composite 3 mm film showed 49.2 dB loss of reflection (99.99% absorption).328 The EMI-SE of Ti3C2Tx/PPy composites must be improved to decrease MXene oxidation for the duration of polymerization; this can be attained using oxidant-free polymerization techniques. 4.4.2. Application of 2D carbide/nitride MXenes as sensors.
4.4.2.1. Applications of 2D carbide/nitride MXenes as pressure and strain sensors. MXenes are already utilized as strain and pressure sensors based on their electronic responses to strain originating from an external force. MXene pressure sensors have piezoresistance, where the outside pressure is transformed into a resistance change. Logically, the conductance of MXenes increases (decreases) while the interlayer space shrinks (expands). Ma et al.213 utilized ML-Ti3C2Tx in a pressure sensor as a pressure-sensitive layer (Fig. 18(a)). The MXene conductance directly improved monotonically with increasing pressure, with a high gauge factor (GF) of 180 and a rapid detection time of less than 30 ms;213 0.19% to 2.1% strain was observed with high accuracy. In situ TEM images of the interlayer space response of MXenes to applied pressure established that the process is dynamic (Fig. 18(b)), representing the working mechanism in piezoresistive sensors. In addition, it was recognized that this sensor can be utilized to perceive movement of the human body, such as breathing and joint bending, as illustrated in Fig. 18(c). The same group also showed that a MXene/rGO aerogel has 22.56 kPa1 sensitivity to external strain after 10000 cycles. The low detection limit (10 Pa) of the aerogel is suitable for human pulse detection.340 Development of sensing activity was attained with a d-Ti3C2Tx/CNT composite sensor, as illustrated in Fig. 18(d).214 The sensing layer was fabricated by alternating spray-coating of d-Ti3C2Tx and single-wall CNTs in aqueous solution on a latex substrate. The resulting composites showed mechanical flexibility and could be expanded to 200% strain without breakdown (Fig. 18(e)). Fig. 18(f) shows the piezoresistive response of the composite to strain fluctuation from 0% to 80%. In a low strain range of 0% to 30%, the composite showed 64.6 GF, while the GF was enhanced up to 772 at high strain (40% to 70%). Furthermore, the composite exhibited stable activity for more than 5000 cycles. In contrast to preceding MXene sensor studies, where the conductance changes originated from changes in the ML Ti3C2Tx interlayer spacing, the d-Ti3C2Tx/CNT composite response results from d-MXene flakes and CNT sliding, which cut off the conductance. Consequently, the d-Ti3C2Tx/CNT sensor conductance decreases with external pressure. d-Ti3C2Tx/PVA hydrogel composites with more than 3400% mechanical flexibility were reported (Fig. 18(g)). The hydrogel exhibited 80 and 25 GF under compressive and tensile strain, correspondingly. More fascinatingly, the hydrogel demonstrated anisotropic electrical detection of strain direction, where its conductance decreased (increased) under tensile (compressive) strain, as shown in Fig. 18(h). This occurrence was attributed to the MXene nanosheet arrangement in the 3D network structure inside the hydrogel matrix, where compressive strain decreases the gaps between MXene sheets, resulting in enhancement of the composite conductance (Fig. 18(i)).215 A Ti3C2Tx/poly(diallydimethylammonium chloride) layer-by-layer arrangement on a flexible polymeric substrate demonstrated decreased conductance upon stretching and bending because of microcrack formation on the surface of the MXene. Fascinatingly, the gaps and cracks were repaired upon discharge of external pressure, improving the conductance and permitting the device to operate for 1000 cycles.341 Pressure sensors based on MXenes have received much consideration because of their outstanding activity and simple device manufacturing. To further enhance the sensing performance of MXene-based pressure sensors, suitable material selection and design of the composite/foam structure are necessary. Although the strain engineering effects on the band structure alignment in MXenes in MXene-based devices have still not been experimentally studied, highly sensitive pressure sensors may be achieved based on strain-induced metal-to-insulator transitions.

4.4.2.2. Molecule-based sensor applications of 2D carbide/nitride MXenes. The conductance of MXenes is responsible for their high sensitivity to adsorbed species; due to the numerous adsorption sites on their functionalized surfaces, they have applications in molecule sensors. Theoretically, it has been shown that semiconducting M2CO2 (M = Zr, Sc, Ti, and Hf) is highly sensitive to NH3 adsorption (Fig. 19(a)). Theoretical predictions showed that NH3 adsorption leads to charge transfer from NH3 molecules to TM atoms on the MXene surface, causing an increase in the electronic conductance of the MXene.342 Furthermore, it was found that NH3 molecules can be desorbed from the MXene surface after electrons transfer into the MXene layer; thus, the sensors are reusable. This occurs because the electronic states of MXenes have special properties, in which the DOS near EF mainly originates from the metal atoms. Therefore, the infused electrons spread mainly on the TMs due to the increase in the NH3 adsorption energy and metal bond length; thus, it is less actively favorable for NH3 to adsorb on the MXene surface.342 Sc2CO2 is sensitive to SO2 gas. Transfer of charge from SO2 to Sc2CO2 at the EF results in great enhancement in the DOS and several orders of increment in the electronic conductance of MXenes. Theoretical investigation has shown that O-terminated M2C (M = V, Ti, Mo and Nb) can be utilized in gas detection with Nb2CO2 and Ti2CO2, which are more sensitive to NH3, while Mo2CO2 and V2CO2 are more sensitive to NO. It is remarkable that transfer of charge from adsorbates to the bare MXene surface may cause dissociation of the adsorbate; thus, MXenes are potential catalysts for activation of small molecules.343 Despite intensive theoretical studies on MXene gas sensors, only a few experimental results have been reported.

Consequently, considering the effects and mechanisms of stimuli on the abovementioned MXene properties is a significant consideration for developing these sensors.


4.4.2.3. Optoelectronic property-based applications of 2D-carbide/nitride MXenes. Lightmatter interaction in a system is called nonlinear optics, where materials nonlinearly react to an electromagnetic field. This is necessary in applications such as photonic devices, laser optics, and optical communication. The nonlinear optical properties of MXenes have been demonstrated.352355 The Ti3C2Tx Z-scan measurement is the nonlinear light absorption (saturate absorption, SA) via the nonlinear increment in transmission with increasing luminance intensity. In general, the effective nonlinear absorption coefficient decreases with increasing luminance intensity in anticipation of the threshold intensity (Ith). The nonlinear absorption coefficient of Ti3C2Tx was found to be 10 to 21 m2 V2, which suggests its potential utilization in optical switching.354 This performance is believed to originate from plasmon-induced ground state absorption when the Ti3C2Tx MXene is illuminated with a laser equal to its plasmon resonance frequency.352 This is evident from the imaginary component of the peak dielectric constant (2) and the real component (1) at wavelengths of 800 and 1130 nm, respectively, where the previous 800 nm corresponds to surface plasmon resonance and the aforementioned 1130 nm corresponds to free-carrier oscillation. Similar plasmonic performance was observed in an electron energy loss spectroscopic study.356 Fascinatingly, SA occurred at dissimilar wavelengths in the 800 to 1800 nm range, which signifies that there may be a number of mechanisms participating in the non-linear SA performance of Ti3C2Tx.354 For Ti3C2Tx, it was anticipated that one-photon process SA takes place at lower intensity, while multiple-photon SA takes place at higher intensity, and the other nonlinear SA processes were found to be the dominant mechanism above the Ith laser intensity.354 Due to the SA ability of MXenes, they can be utilized in ultrafast laser applications. For this purpose, MXene acts as a spectral filter that stimulates mode-lock laser pulses. Metallic Ti3C2Tx is used in fs mode-locked lasers.353,354 Ti3C2Tx produces stable mode-lock laser pulses through a 65 ns period and 660 fs sequential width.353 Analogous performance was obtained for Ti3C2Tx.354 The broadband wavelength produced by Ti3C2Tx in the 1550 to 1620 nm range covers the necessary telecommunication band (C-band), representing promise for communication applications. The nonlinear optical activity of MXenes is analogous to that of other 2D materials, such as TMDCs, BP and graphene.354 Moreover, it was shown that Ti3C2Tx with a 5 nm thickness is more elastic at high laser intensity than other 2D materials.352 When Ti3C2Tx was coupled with C60, a reverse SA material was obtained that behaved as an optical diode.357 The device can be utilized to filter optical signals with a high refinement ratio. Ti3C2Tx was also shown to produce an arbitrary laser effect; when sonicated in rhodamine 101 solution, it showed metamaterial behavior.201 The study of the nonlinear optical properties of MXenes is still young; however, to date, it has shown potential in numerous applications. The interaction of MXenes with light requires additional efficient research. The optoelectronic, photonic, and plasmonic properties of different MXenes are awaiting further investigation prior to their practical application.

Non-precious metals intercalated with LDH nanostructures with different morphologies, e.g., MnO2MgAl LDHs359 and CuOMnAl LDHs,360 have been synthesized through facile hydrothermal and co-precipitation methods and show applications in non-enzymatic H2O2 biosensing, with 5 M and 0.126 M detection limits in addition to wide linear ranges of 0.05 to 78 mM and 6 M to 22 mM, respectively. 5. 2D heterostructure material applications

5.1. 2D-TMDCs-based heterostructure approach

In 2D materials, the vdW interactions between layers and dangling free bonds permit their incorporation with other 2D materials, e.g., graphene, to fabricate a diversity of vertical and planar functional heterostructures with diverse properties. For a sample geometry, 2D layer semiconductors such as InSe,368 WS2,369 MoTe2,370 and graphene were utilized as light absorbing materials and both-side electrodes, respectively, to fabricate planar metalsemiconductormetal-type phototransistors. In comparison to metal electrodes, graphene electrode has the benefit of modifying the SB between the 2D semiconductors and graphene via calculating the graphene EF. Ideal ohmic contact between the 2D semiconductor layer and graphene can be attained through SB tuning. High R values (4 103 W1) and fast rise/fall times (1/10 ms) were observed in planar graphene/n-InSe/graphene vdW heterostructures.368 For a broad range equilibrium of applied gate voltages, electrons travel from graphene to n-InSe because of the higher WF of graphene, resulting in an accumulation layer at the graphene/n-InSe interface. This innovative device architecture provides high broadband spectral response and fast electrical speed. In another study, irregular reliance of R on different incident light intensities was observed in a graphene/WS2/graphene heterostructure device.369 In this device, in the off state, R increases directly with optical power because of an efficient photo-induced charge transfer process where electrons dwell in the graphene and holes in WS2. Conversely, in the on state (Vg = 30 V), as the optical power increases, R decreases. In the phototransistor in the on state, the photo-gating effect can decrease the contact resistance and increase the EF. As a consequence, the effect of graphene illumination is decreased; this causes diverse power dependence on R.

In device fabrication, photo-gating effects can be diminished by using a 2D semiconductor as a medium for light production, while graphene provides a conduction channel for carrier transfer and exchange. Heterostructure phototransistor graphene-MoS2 fabricated by mechanical exfoliation displayed high R values of 5 108 and 1 1010 A W1 at 130 K and RT, respectively.371 Here, under illumination in ambient conditions, the photogenerated holes in MoS2 are trapped and the electrons are relocated to graphene through the gate electric field, resulting in high R. Conversely, the entrapped holes behave as local gates, conferring a considerable photo-gating effect on graphene via capacitive coupling. Ultimately, due to the long life of the trapped holes, the response time is slowed. An analogous phototransistor was fabricated with CVD-induced MoS2 and graphene.372 This hybrid heterostructure device demonstrated a comparatively low response of 1.2 107 A W1 due to the low carrier mobility of CVD-graphene and its slow response time (several 100 s); however, it is suitable for practical applications due to its facile synthesis on a large scale. Graphene/TMDC heterostructures also displayed important photodiode-type functionalities. Modulation of the production, separation, and transport processes of EF carriers was demonstrated by adjusting the heights of graphene and SB in vertically arranged graphene/TMD/graphene heterostructures. For example, graphene/WS2/graphene stack heterostructures demonstrated competent light-matter interactions, leading to improved photon absorption and eh production.373 Furthermore, an increment in R was observed from 102 to 1010 A W1 upon assimilating plasmonic metal nanostructures and heterostructures, which can enhance the absorption of light. These results can be utilized to construct dual-gate-type graphene/WS2/graphene heterostructures to regulate the polarity and amplitude of the PC by regulating the direction and intensity of the intrinsic electric potential.374 A typical heterostructure with trilayer WSe2 (2.2 nm thick) in graphene/WSe2/graphene, illustrated in Fig. 21(a),375 shows a fast response of 5.5 ps and high internal QE of 70%, as shown in Fig. 21(b) and (c). The 7.3% EQE could be further improved via using optical cavities or plasmon nanostructures in TMD films to enhance light-matter interactions.

5.4. 2D-MXene-based heterostructure approach

Baihai Li et al. studied a flexible 2D molecular bilayer heterostructure of asymmetric MXene/ML transition metal dichalcogenide (aMXene/mTMDC) with a high interfacial built-in electric field. In the bottom or top plane of the aMXene, the electronegative atoms of MXene were removed. The specific structural design of a MXene aims to achieve a high dipole moment perpendicular to the 2D molecular plane. However, the instability in the aMXene generated by un-passivated metal atoms can be overcome by electropositive coupling to the electronegative chalcogenide atoms in the aMXene/mTMDC bilayer. The precise composition of aMXene/mTMDC engineered through the dipole field is leveraged to provide unusual band alignment, structures, and charge injection/redistribution in the bilayer. Several aMXene/mTMDC bilayer designs with possible applications in microelectronics, optoelectronics, spintronics, and catalysis/photocatalysis have been shown.393

Yangfan Shao et al. studied the experimental synthesis of 2D MoS2-on-MXene heterostructures. Here, the mechanical, electronic, and electrochemical properties as well as the structural morphologies of MoS2@Ti2C and MoS2@Ti2CO2 heterostructures as anode materials for LIBs were systematically examined by taking advantage of vdW-corrected spin-polarized DFT to offer an atomistic approach. The results showed that for the MoS2@Ti2CO2 heterostructure, the MoS2 polymorph significantly influenced the electronic structure and Li diffusion at the interface. At the dT-MoS2@Ti2CO2 interface, the Li diffusion barrier along the zigzag direction (0.15 eV) is much smaller than that of H-MoS2@Ti2CO2 (0.67 eV). However, for the MoS2@Ti2C heterostructures, the Li diffusion performance and electronic structure are comparatively unaffected by the MoS2 morphology. Particularly, the MoS2@Ti2C heterostructures demonstrate an ultralow diffusion barrier, very low open-circuit voltage (0.62 to 0.25 V), high charge/discharge rates, and high mechanical flexibility. 5.5. LDH-based heterostructure approach

Graphene has been incorporated into the LDH crystal lattice to overcome the semiconductor nature of LDH materials; in response, their conductance increases. For example, nickel iron LDH/graphene nanocomposites395 and NiCo LDH nanosheets/graphene nanoribbons396 were prepared by electrochemical deposition and co-precipitation, respectively, to increase their electro-oxidation ability. The consequentially improved electrocatalytic performance of the non-enzymatic glucose sensor is due to the parallel effects of the carbon support and interrelated 3D structures with plentiful electrochemical active surfaces and fast charge transfer at the kinetic and nano-scale edges.399

6. 2D-material-based device challenges

Since the discovery of graphene (2004), the 2D material world has entered its teenage years, particularly in the fast-growing research areas of chemistry, condensed matter physics, material science, and nanotechnology. The development of 2D layered materials is ambitious; however, their applications are promising. 2D-material-based manufactured devices include high quality, high performance optical encoders for a multitude of industries and applications. Through BG engineering, zero BG 2D materials are potential candidates for electronic devices, and their stability issues have mostly been addressed by functionalization; however, some issues remain to be solved. Therefore, heterostructure approaches in 2D materials are also utilized because of their mixed properties; hence, the BGs are broadened in different required ranges. Heterostructure approaches have been found to be very helpful to improve the efficiency of electronic devices. Graphene as a contact has unlocked electrostatic tuning possibilities to decrease the distinction between the electron affinity of the semiconductive channels and the effective WF of the contacts, which ultimately decreases the device Rc. Both Si and OSCs usually have electronic conduction of less than 1 cm2 V1 s1, which is very small. The stability problem can be overcome by functionalization of 2D materials, and stable 2D-semiconducting materials with suitable BGs can be applied in transistor technology, which can be used in electronics, optoelectronics and sensing applications.

Although 2D materials should be available for consumer-feasible products, consistent, more cost-efficient methods need to be developed. It should be noted that the major challenges to resolve for 2D materials to attain market growth include (1) large production, especially roll-to-roll manufacturing, (2) repeatable and reliable fabrication methods, (3) low Rc, and (4) 2D-material-based precise characterization techniques. It is evident that one key to a favorable outcome is the capability to prepare 2D materials at the wafer level. In this way, it will be possible to create large amounts of 2D devices for fabrication and decrease the product cost. To date, wafer-scale deposition techniques have been developed for graphene, WS2 and MoS2. The second obstacle is progress of repeatable and reliable fabrication procedures. Fabrication processes involve transfer, etching, doping and deposition of dielectrics. Due to the novelty of 2D materials, novel methods are required to be developed to decrease impurities due to transfer, selectively exfoliate a variety of 2D materials, avoid defect configuration, and avoid oxidation, stability issues, and alternative doping of materials. 2D materials may have thicknesses of one or more atom, and Rc has been established to be a major problem. Development processes are needed to decrease the effects of the contacts by means of EF; doping and pinning of 2D materials may be applicable.

Although 2D-material-based devices have been created through exfoliation from their bulk counterparts utilizing diverse techniques, manufacturing procedures with on-demand tuning of electronic and structural properties have not yet been completely achieved. Development of large area, high-quality, and single-crystal 2D materials are needed for real application of 2D-material-based technologies and for high-volume manufacturing; this may be one of the most demanding tasks. At the atomic level, the control of layered material synthesis related to surface science and defect control should be comprehended at a level far above those explored in other multi-component material classifications. Another way to overcome the limitations of 2D material device applications is the fabrication of devices based on heterostructures. Atomically thin 2D materials offer a broad range of basic building blocks with exceptional, optical, electrical, and thermal properties that do not exist in their bulk counterparts. Interlayer vdW interactions provide the opportunity to exfoliate and reconstruct diverse 2D materials into randomly and vertically stacked heterostructures. Currently, vapor phase methods for 2D materials are being developed, providing another direction for directly manufacturing lateral and vertical heterojunctions. The variety of 2D-material-based heterostructures will open a novel research field, and BG engineering through heterostructure configuration offers the opportunity to develop novel devices with required properties and potential energy applications. It is predictable that advancement in 2D material growth, heterostructure configuration and device fabrication shall show the way for future real applications. To date, researchers have only scratched the surface of 2D material development, and time will enlighten where the field will advance.

For great utilization of the novel properties of 2D materials, it is essential to expand new characterization techniques to synthesize and investigate 2D materials. For the thickness of 2D materials, regular techniques such as SEM and TEM are used to illustrate and adjust the 2D materials. There are also detection problems for other techniques because of the minimum material quantity available for characterization. It is expected that with patience and time, these problems will be solved. At the same time, researchers will continue to push forward with the examination of novel 2D materials. Through research findings, we should produce a master chart of 2D materials detailing their properties and possible applications and decide how they can be utilized in combination with one another to shape functional devices. In addition, the layer stacking and heterostructure construction master chart will be enlarged to explain the stacking effects on each layer and include novel composite devices by means of engineered BG properties. At present, the elementary comprehension of 2D materials is entering the limelight, based on a quick search of their use in electronic devices. It is undeniable that rapid advancement in these applications is valuable; however, it may not be totally carried out on the basis of basic scientific understanding, but in the future will lead to much smarter and more efficient methods for harnessing 2D materials. Currently, it is necessary to establish stable key properties of how unique, tunable electronic structures based on 2D materials can be utilized in mature industrial-based electronic devices. Looking forward, 2D material synthesis methods are needed to control the transition from fundamental outcomes to large scale technologies. At the same time, the high-quality crystals synthesized through ME will continue to be crucial in elementary research; LPE and CVD possess immense promise for applications. It is clear that surmounting the shortcomings that hinder future inventions could bring about a novel era of 2D material tunable electrochemical devices and their heterostructures with enormously increased surface-to-mass ratios and apparently infinite functionalities.

Even after obtaining required 2D layer materials by suitably inexpensive and facile synthesis techniques and studying them with emerging highly precise modern characterization techniques, still there is another problem related to the conversion of these layer materials to devices, i.e., the contact between the 2D materials and metals. Hence, this critical point for electronic device manufacturing through contact between metals and 2D-Ms requires ohmic contact with low resistance to comply with the performance requirements of the ITRS (total parasitic series sourcedrain resistance of 130 m) because a high parasitic resistance restricts the general device performance. Much effort has been devoted to the development of contact resistance between metals and 2D materials, with surface contamination being one of the major causes of high contact resistance. Numerous attempts have been carried out to decrease graphene surface contamination, such as through utilizing elevated temperature vacuum annealing or surface treatments with ozone or O2 plasma, in which the contact in the presence of O2 plasma is made on a clean surface while the edge contact suffers from defects. A substitute route for 2D material-metal contacts that avoids surface contamination is the utilization of a pristine edge contact with graphene (i.e., 1D electrical contacts with 2D materials). For 2D materials that have BGs, the contact should be a metal with an appropriate work function in order to prevent the development of a Schottky barrier. Hence, if we are able to overcome all these limitations, we can enable omnipresent electronics in windows, walls or fabrics, and 2D materials will generate a totally new marketplace for electronic commerce rather than contending with or demanding to replace present well-established technologies to offer higher performance. Scientists around the globe at the academic and industrial levels are working on this task and agree that this is most likely an easier path towards the first utilization of this truly incredible material family.

7. Conclusion

More than a decade after the discovery of graphene, the world of 2D materials is reaching its teenage years, especially in fast-developing condensed matter physics, material science, and nanotechnology research. The emerging field of 2D materials provides the optical community with many exciting new opportunities for exploration of science and technologies across a very wide electromagnetic spectral range; however, these materials have some limitations. In this review, we also review the current progress in the applications of 2D heterostructures. The various heterostructures based on 2D materials open a new research field, and band structure engineering by heterostructure formation provides a possibility to develop novel devices with desired properties and potential energy applications. It is anticipated that further progress in 2D material growth, heterostructure formation and device fabrication shall lead to further practical applications on an economical level in the future. Therefore, it is time to introduce more facile large-scale synthesis methods and further advance our understanding of exploring new properties of 2D materials and their heterostructures to further identify their most promising applications in industrial electronic devices. We hope that this review will serve as a guide to those exploring the electronic and optical properties of 2D materials.
