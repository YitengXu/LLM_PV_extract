Abstract

The emerging field of lead-based hybrid organicâ€“inorganic perovskite (HOIP) photovoltaic devices has attracted a great deal of attention due to their very high conversion efficiencies and straightforward fabrication methods. Unfortunately, a major obstacle to commercialization remains the high toxicity of lead. Whilst to date the focus has been on understanding and improving device performance, there has been no reported effort to develop methods to recover and recycle the lead from these materials. In this work we demonstrate a simple, low-cost and environmentally friendly method of recycling lead from HOIP photovoltaics by dissolution and selective electrodeposition using a deep eutectic solvent. We demonstrate that up to 99.8% of the lead is removed from the solvent. The results presented here provide a viable solution to lead-based HOIP photovoltaic recycling, and also open the possibility for providing an alternative method to conventional smelting in the recovery and recycling of different lead-based energy materials.

1. Introduction

Lead and lead compounds have been utilised for thousands of years, finding wide-spread use by the ancient Romans for a number of applications including plumbing and cookware.1 Nowadays the use of lead is primarily in the area of energy applications (Fig. 1(a) and (b)), and predominantly due to the lead acid battery,2 but also as tetra-ethyl lead in â€œleadedâ€ petrol,3 and lead telluride used in thermoelectric generators.4,5 Recently lead has found further use as a key energy material in next-generation hybrid organicâ€“inorganic perovskite (HOIP) photovoltaics (PVs), and has subsequently generated a huge amount of scientific interest.6â€“8 The principal HOIP material, methylammonium lead triiodide (MAPbI3), has shown great promise as a low-cost, high-efficiency alternative to silicon-based PV technology.9â€“12 The alternative formamidinium lead triiodide (FAPbI3), whilst being the subject of fewer studies than MAPbI3, has demonstrated very promising properties for HOIP PV applications due to it having a narrower band gap than MAPbI3.13â€“15 The mixed halide perovskites (MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) have also been shown to possess good stability and are proving to be a popular choice of material for HOIP PV applications.6,12,16â€“18
Fig. 1 (a) The applications of Pb in industry in the 20th century (adapted from ref. 50), (b) lead production rates in the past 5000 years (adapted from ref. 51), (c) the correlation between the removal of Pb from petrol and the decrease in blood lead content (adapted from ref. 3), and (d) the correlation between the use of lead in paint, and the use of lead petrol, with murder rate (adapted from ref. 52).

A major challenge facing HOIP PV is the toxicity and environmental impact of lead.7,19 The toxic effects of lead have been well documented, dating back to the second century BC,20 and more recently an increased blood lead content in children has been linked to intellectual impairment,21 and an increase in juvenile delinquent behaviour.22,23 Previous studies highlighted in Fig. 1 show links between total lead usage and average blood lead levels (c) and somewhat controversially, rates of murder (d). Globally the primary source of lead waste is from the lead acid battery, which is recycled (like most lead-products) by high temperature smelting. An average concentration of lead in the blood of children who live in the surrounding areas of battery smelting plants in developing countries has been measured at 19 Î¼g dlâˆ’1, a value about 13 times higher than the average in children in the U.S.24 It is clear that a non-smelting route to recycling lead from HOIP PV must be developed.

In this work we present the application of a deep eutectic solvent (DES) to the recycling of lead-based HOIP PV materials by a clean, low-cost, electrochemical method. DESs are an emerging class of ionic solvent which have seen increasing popularity over the past decade,25,26 and have shown excellent capability for the dissolution of metal oxides and salts27,28 and electrodeposition of various metals,29 demonstrating a degree of re-usability,30â€“32 as well as being considered environmentally friendly.33 The DES used in this study is comprised of choline chloride (ChCl) and ethylene glycol (EG), which is shown to easily dissolve MAPbI3, FAPbI3 and MAPbI3âˆ’xClx before subsequent electrodeposition of the speciated lead from the solvent. The advantage of a DES over aqueous solvents is the reduction in the risk of waste solution being introduced to the water supply, as well as the need not to use volatile organic solvents and concentrated acids.34 Furthermore, using an electrochemical process significantly reduces the environmental footprint of high-temperature smelting. 2. Materials and methods

2.1 Substrate preparation for HOIP films

TiO2 thin films were formed on fluorine-doped SnO2 (FTO) substrates using a solâ€“gel technique, as developed elsewhere.44,45 The FTO substrates were cleaned by sonication in isopropanol for 5 minutes, and dried with N2 gas. A piece of tape covered approximately 1 cm2 of the end of the substrates prior to application of a TiO2 thin film to ensure a controlled coating area was used in each experiment and that the entire film could subsequently be dipped into the DES. A solution with 1 M Ti(IV) isopropoxide (Sigma-Aldrich) in 4 ml acetic acid (Sigma-Aldrich) and 2.5 ml methoxymethanol (Sigma-Aldrich) was prepared. With a spin-coater set to 500 rpm for 15 seconds and 3000 rpm for 45 seconds, ethanol was first spin-coated onto the FTO substrates. At the same setting, the Ti(IV) isopropoxide solution was spin-coated onto the FTO substrates. The piece of tape was then removed and the substrates annealed at 600 Â°C for 60 minutes in a muffle furnace (ramp-rate 2.5 Â°C minâˆ’1.). The 1 cm2 tape coating was replaced on the substrate prior to the addition of HOIP thin films.

2.2 DES preparation

DES mixtures were prepared by mixing ChCl (â‰¥98%, Sigma-Aldrich) and EG (â‰¥99.5%, Sigma-Aldrich) in a 1:2 molar ratio and heating and stirring at 60 Â°C using a hotplate until a clear and colourless liquid was formed. The specific quantities of the constituents for each experiment are detailed in the text.

2.3 Thin film HOIP synthesis and dissolution

Methylammonium iodide (CH3NH3+Iâˆ’) was prepared by adapting the methods of Eperon et al.18 Hydroiodic acid (HI, 57 wt% in water, Sigma-Aldrich) was added dropwise to methylamine (33 wt% in ethanol, Sigma-Aldrich) whilst stirring at room temperature. The solution was dried in a rotovaporator under vacuum at 60 Â°C. The product was washed with diethyl ether (Sigma-Aldrich) and dried overnight in a vacuum oven to form a white powder. This compound was then stored in a desiccator. Formamidium iodide (HC(NH2)2+Iâˆ’) was prepared by adapting the method contained within the ESI of the recent work of Eperon et al.13 A mixture of formamidinium acetate (Sigma-Aldrich) powder in 2Ã— molar excess of hydroiodic acid was stirred for 10 minutes at 50 Â°C, and then dried at 60 Â°C using a rotovaporator. It was then washed with diethyl ether and recrystallised once with ethanol. The product was dried overnight in a vacuum oven and stored in a desiccator until required.

To form the HOIP thin films, three precursor solutions were mixed in ambient conditions with anhydrous dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) as the solvent.18,46 As recycling of the HOIP, not performance, is the crucial aspect of this work it was deemed sufficient to prepare these HOIPs in ambient conditions.47 The non-stoichiometric Cl-MAPBI3 was formed as per Eperon et al.18 with methylammonium iodide dissolved with lead(II) chloride in a 3:1 ratio, and having concentrations of 2.64 M and 0.88 M respectively. The (CH3NH3+)PbI3 precursor was formed by mixing methylammonium iodide with Pb(II) iodide in a 1:1 ratio to form a 0.88 M precursor solution.13 The (HC(NH2)2+)PbI3 precursor was made by mixing formamidinium iodide with lead(II) iodide in a 1:1 ratio to form a 0.88 M solution.13 Each precursor was stirred at 70 Â°C for 30 minutes prior to spin-coating. In all three cases, 60 Î¼l of precursor was spread over the TiO2-FTO substrate spin-coated at 500 rpm for 15 seconds and 3000 rpm for 45 seconds. After spin-coating the tape was removed. The Cl-MAPBI3 and MAPBI3 were then annealed in air at 100 Â°C for 1 hour, whilst the FAPbI3 was annealed in air at 150 Â°C for 1 hour. The as-prepared HOIP thin films were placed immediately into the DES solutions or studied using X-ray diffraction (XRD) within 10 minutes of preparation. The samples prepared for scanning electron microscopy (SEM) were stored overnight in a desiccator prior to study. To study the stripping of the HOIP films (MAPbI3, FAPbI3, MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) from the TiO2 coated FTO substrate, the HOIP films were held in the 1:2 molar ratio ChCl:EG DES, whilst maintained at 60 Â°C, for 30 minutes to dissolve the HOIP layer. Following the stripping and dissolution of the HOIP material, the samples were studied with XRD and SEM to demonstrate the successful removal of the HOIP layer.

2.4 Powdered HOIP synthesis and DES electrochemistry

In order to synthesise HOIP powder, to provide the quantities required for higher concentrations of dissolved HOIP in the DES, the precursor solutions were dried overnight in a low humidity environment. This was done by freshly preparing precursor solutions, as previously, and placing the heated precursor solutions into a three-neck round bottom flask, which was then stirred using a magnetic stirrer bar. A rubber septum was placed over the middle neck, with greased valves placed over the outside necks. One valve was connected to an N2 gas source and the other to a gas bubbler filled with silicon oil. The solution was heated to the respective annealing temperature of the HOIPs which had two effects: first, the DMSO evaporated out of solution; second, all the HOIPs crystallised into solid black crystals that were capable of arresting the movement of the magnetic stir bar. The gas was allowed to flow for the duration of the evaporating and annealing process to minimise the humidity and prevent powder degradation. This powder was placed immediately into the DES at the desired concentration after breaking the nitrogen-rich environment.

DESs containing powdered HOIP were prepared by adding the powder to the ChCl-EG DES, whilst being maintained at 60 Â°C with EG:ChCl:HOIP mole ratios of 2:1:0.00057. The DESs were heated and stirred until the HOIP material was fully dissolved. Three electrode cells were prepared using an IrO2 coated Ti mesh counter electrode and Ag wire quasi-reference electrode and a working electrode of tin-doped indium oxide (ITO) (8â€“12 Î© per square, Sigma-Aldrich). The ITO working electrodes were ultrasonically cleaned in acetone, isopropanol and deionised water and dried with an N2 gas flow prior to placing into the DES solutions. For each electrochemical measurement fresh working electrodes were used. Cyclic voltammogram's (CVs) and potentiostatic electrochemical experiments were controlled using an Ivium CompactStat potentiostat with the DES maintained at 60 Â°C.

2.5 Complete Pb extraction from solution

The complete extraction of Pb from DES solutions containing dissolved HOIP powder was demonstrated by preparing DES solutions of each HOIP powder (MAPbI3, FAPbI3, MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) dissolved in a ChCl-EG DES, as described previously. The solutions were prepared containing the EG, ChCl and HOIP in mole ratios of 2:1:0.00057 with a total volume of 40 ml. From this 10 ml was extracted prior to the electrodeposition for inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) measurement of the pre-extraction Pb concentration. The remaining 30 ml of each HOIP-DES were maintained at 60 Â°C using a hotplate and incorporated into a three electrode cell using an IrO2 coated Ti counter electrode, Ag wire quasi-reference electrode and Pb foil working electrode. In each case the Pb foil (99.998% purity, Alfa Aesar) was prepared with a working area of 7.5 cm2, polished with 1000 grit SiC paper, soaked and rinsed in isopropanol and dried with N2 gas prior to insertion into the DES solutions. A fresh Pb foil was used for each of the three Pb extractions. The electrochemical cell was controlled using an Ivium CompactStat potentiostat, with a potential of âˆ’0.9 V vs. Ag applied for 120 hours to drive the deposition of the dissolved Pb onto the Pb foil working electrode. Following the complete extraction of Pb from each HOIP-DES solution, the concentration of Pb in each solution was measured using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) by the Chemical Analysis Facility at the University of Reading.

2.6 XRD and SEM characterisation

XRD patterns were measured using Panalytical X'Pert Pro and Bruker D2 Phaser diffractometers. SEM images were recorded with a JEOL JSM 5610 LV electron microscope and HR-SEM images were recorded using a LEO Gemini 1525 electron microscope.

2.7 Extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS)

EXAFS measurements of the HOIP-DESs were undertaken at beamline B18 at the Diamond Light Source, Harwell, UK. The samples were held in polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) cells with Kapton foil windows. The EXAFS spectra were recorded using the Pb L3 edge. The samples were placed in the beam path with a fluorescence detector at 90Â° to the beam. A hot air gun, set to 70 Â°C, was positioned around 20 cm from the samples, gently warming them above ambient conditions to prevent any formation of solid crystals in the liquids. Between 27 and 36 spectra were collected from each sample. These were summed, calibrated and background subtracted using the Athena program.48 The resulting spectra were fitted using the Artemis program to yield information on the speciation of the dissolved HOIP in the DES.48 The passive electron reduction factor S0 was set at 0.83.49 A k-range of 3-12 Ã…âˆ’1 and a R-range of 1.8â€“3.4 Ã… was used. 3. Results and discussion

3.1 Solvation of hybrid organic inorganic perovskite films

Films of each of the HOIPs (MAPbI3, FAPbI3 and MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) on TiO2 coated FTO were placed in 0.18 mol EG + 0.09 mol ChCl DESs for 30 minutes at 60 Â°C. This result provides an initial indication that the HOIP layers had, in each case, been dissolved. This result clearly shows that the HOIP films have dissolved into the DES, whereas the FTO and TiO2 remain on the electrode. The SEM images in Fig. 3 show the three HOIP films before and after stripping in the DES, and additionally an image of the TiO2 coated FTO before the deposition of a HOIP layer is presented for comparison (b). Regardless, it can be seen that the HOIP layer has, in each case, clearly been removed following immersion into the DES. The combination of the XRD and SEM results in Fig. 3 demonstrate the suitability of the DES system to stripping HOIP films from the TiO2 coated FTO layer of a HOIP solar cell, for example, these results show that the remaining TiO2 coated FTO substrate could possibly be reused in the fabrication of a new HOIP PV device, which will be the focus of further study.

3.2 Selective removal of lead from solvent

For the electrochemical measurements, powdered HOIP materials have been dissolved into the DES to better simulate the process of metal recovery from an industrial system, where much larger quantities of HOIP would be present in the DES solution than that from a 2 cm2 thin film. Each of the three HOIP powders (MAPbI3, FAPbI3 and MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) were dissolved in the DES at 60 Â°C such that EG:ChCl:HOIP molar ratios of 2:1:0.00057 were achieved. In each case the powder completely dissolved giving a translucent yellow coloured liquid. Cyclic voltammogram (CV) and potentiostatic measurements have been undertaken using tin-doped indium oxide (ITO) substrates as the working electrode to study the electrochemistry of Pb deposition and stripping from each HOIP dissolved in the DES. ITO has been used as the working electrode as it does not undergo any redox reactions in the DES and deposited Pb can be easily identified from the substrate during subsequent characterization. Through CVs and potentiostatic measurements the deposition and stripping processes of Pb from the DESs can be understood. 3.3 Complete lead extraction

Electrochemical deposition of Pb from each of the dissolved HOIP powders was done using a Pb working electrode in a three-electrode electrochemical cell. Using a Pb working electrode is highly beneficial as the process electrodeposits dissolved Pb onto a Pb electrode and reduces any further steps needed to transfer the deposited metal, meaning it is ready for new use in industrial applications in a highly pure form. The HOIPs (MAPbI3, FAPbI3 and MAPbI3âˆ’xClx) were added to each DES solution such that an EG:ChCl:HOIP mole ratio of 2:1:0.00057 was achieved. Potentiostatic deposition of Pb from each of the HOIP-DESs was undertaken for 120 hours at âˆ’0.9 V vs. Ag. ICP-MS measurements of the Pb concentration in each of the DES solutions, before and after 120 hours of Pb extraction, are presented in Table 1. It can be seen from the results that for each dissolved HOIP, â‰¥98.7% of the Pb has been extracted from the solution, with a maximum of 99.8% Pb extraction achieved for the Cl-MAPBI3 DES. Dissolving 2 cm2 MAPbI3, FAPbI3, MAPbI3âˆ’xClx thin films in approximately 21 ml of the EG + ChCl DES yielded Pb concentrations of 13.3, 3.5 and 5.3 ppm respectively. If this is scaled up based upon the pre-extraction Pb concentrations in Table 1, then using 1 litre of 2:1 EG + ChCl would enable the dissolution and extraction of HOIP films in the order of 1 m2. Further to this, the Pb concentrations in Table 1 do not represent the maximum dissolution of HOIP in the DES, so it is very likely that a higher amount of HOIP, and therefore high surface areas of solar cells, could be recycled in a single litre of DES. It is also important to note that by designing and engineering high-throughput electrochemical cells, the time taken to fully extract the lead from the DES would be significantly reduced.

3.4 The speciation of lead

EXAFS measures X-ray absorption as a function of energy and can be used to determine the arrangement of atoms surrounding the central absorbing atom. It is therefore element specific and is well-suited to the study of metal coordination sites in ionic liquids and molten salts, as illustrated in the review by Hardacre.41 The EXAFS signal results from interference between the out-going photoelectron waves and those back-scattered from surrounding atoms and manifests itself as an oscillation in the absorption coefficient at energies greater than that of the absorption edge. By extracting the experimental EXAFS signal and fitting with a theoretically calculated curve based on the EXAFS equation,42 the local arrangement of atoms (type, number, and distance up to âˆ¼6 Ã… (ref. 41)) around the absorbing atom can be elucidated.

EXAFS measurements have been carried out to understand the speciation of the dissolved HOIP in the DES. Plots of the normalised absorption data, fitted k-space EXAFS and fitted pseudo-radial distribution functions from the MAPbI3, FAPbI3, MAPbI3âˆ’xClx DES samples are all shown Fig. 5. Importantly it can clearly be seen from these plots that there is no significant differences between the data from the three samples. This conclusion is supported by the results of the fitting of the EXAFS (Table 2), which suggests that in all three samples the Pb cation is surrounded by three chlorine atoms at a distance of 2.65 Ã…. Based on these results, the most likely Pb species in the DES is [PbCl3]âˆ’. This species has been observed previously in lead(II) chloride ionic liquids.43 The speciation of Pb for each of the three dissolved HOIPs is consistent with the gradients of the Randlesâ€“Sevcik plots in Fig. 4(b), which were also the same, within experimental error.

4. Conclusions

Three lead-based HOIP materials, MAPbI3, FAPbI3 and MAPbI3âˆ’xClx have been solvated by a DES and the lead successfully recovered electrochemically. Potentiostatic lead deposition from the HOIP-DES solutions has been shown through XRD and HR-SEM on an ITO working electrode, and thin films of each lead HOIP have been successfully stripped from TiO2 coated FTO substrates. Higher concentrations of powdered forms of each HOIP have been dissolved in the EG-ChCl DES with the Pb subsequently extracted at rates of up to 99.8% using a Pb working electrode. EXAFS measurements have identified the atoms solvating the dissolved Pb to be Cl, forming a [PbCl3]âˆ’ species. For all three dissolved HOIPs the same speciation was observed in the DES. The combination of these results demonstrates a proof of principle of lead recycling for HOIP based solar cells. The need to develop environmentally friendly and low cost recycling of these materials is of critical importance if they are to ever find application in the end-user marketplace for solar power generation. We believe that DES based recycling is a hugely effective method to recover the lead from HOIP based solar cells, both for future market end-of-life disposal as well as a convenient method for the disposal of the increasing number of test cells currently being generated in research laboratories globally. Finally, we believe that this approach could be more widely adopted for all lead-based energy materials.
