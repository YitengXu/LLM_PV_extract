Abstract

Metal sulfide nanostructures, which have been widely studied in recent years, are one of the most promising candidates in energy devices. Herein, coralloid hierarchical structures composed of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle trunks and CuS nanosheet branches are, for the first time, well-constructed on fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) substrates via a multistep route. A seeding assistant strategy is applied to grow uniform and firm metal sulfide films on the FTO substrates. It is revealed that different seeds (i.e., TiO2, CuS and ZnO) all have a positive effect to facilitate the film growth because of their surface roughening behavior on the FTO substrates. Significantly, such three dimensional (3D) Co9S8â€“CuS electrodes show superior electrocatalytic performance in the reduction reaction of polysulfide electrolyte, and yield an enhanced power conversion efficiency of 4.50% for CdS/CdSe quantum-dot sensitized solar cells as counter electrodes when compared with the one dimensional (1D) Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle (3.25%) and two dimensional (2D) CuS nanosheet (3.83%) electrodes. More impressively, the coralloid structure exhibits excellent stability in the electrochemical cycling tests, keeping 80% current after 500 cycles. Above all, the synthesis strategy here is also feasible to fabricate hybrid Co9S8â€“CuS films on various flexible substrates, such as carbon cloth, Ti mesh, and Ni foam for potential applications in wearable energy devices.

1. Introduction

Transition metal sulfides have attracted intensive attention due to their superior optical, electrical and chemical properties, as well as their potential applications in photocatalysis, sensors, supercapacitors, and photovoltaic devices.1â€“4 In the past few years, much effort has been devoted to synthesizing many kinds of metal sulfides with various morphologies, such as one-dimensional (1D) nanotubes or nanowires,5,6 two-dimensional (2D) nanosheets or nanoplates,7,8 and three-dimensional (3D) hollow spheres or coreâ€“shell hierarchical structures.9,10 Among numerous micro/nano-structures, hybrid materials with hierarchical structures exhibit excellent electrochemical activity owing to the combination of advantages from each single structure.11,12 Many works related to the integration of metal sulfides with metal oxides or carbon materials have been reported in the past few years.10,13â€“16 In theory, metal sulfides usually possess superior electrical conductivity, mechanical properties, and stability as compared to their corresponding metal oxide counterparts.17 Therefore, the rational combination of different metal sulfides will be feasible to obtain high-performance electrodes for various applications, and several examples have been reported recently, such as Co3S4@MoS2,18 FeCo2S4â€“NiCo2S4,12 Ni3S2/CoNi2S4,19 and NiS2/CoS2.6 It is indicated that the hybrid metal sulfides with special heterostructures exhibit superiority in the fields of water splitting, energy conversion and storage devices.12,18,19

Among various metal sulfides, inexpensive metal sulfides, such as copper sulfides and cobalt sulfides, which have low resistance and high electrocatalytic activity, have gained great interest for quantum dot-sensitized solar cells (QDSCs), water splitting and energy storage devices.20â€“26 Cobalt sulfides have several stoichiometries, including CoS, Co2S, Co3S4, and Co9S8 with various morphologies, such as nanospheres,23 nanowires,27 and nanosheets.28 Meanwhile, as a well-known p-type semiconductor, copper sulfides also have a series of stoichiometries (e.g., CuS, Cu1.8S and Cu2S) and many nanostructures (e.g., nanorods, nanosheets and nanocrystals) with attractive electronic, optical, and chemical merits.29â€“31 In our previous work, different CuxS nanosheet and Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle electrodes were prepared which showed considerable electrocatalytic ability in the reduction of the polysulfide electrolytes, consequently yielding high-performance as counter electrodes (CEs) for QDSCs.20â€“22,32 Accordingly, it is expected that the incorporation of cobalt and copper sulfides will be beneficial for their electrocatalytic activity as well as stability in the electrochemical cycling tests. This is probably due to the enhanced conductivity and increased active reaction sites derived from the heterojunctions and heterostructures of the hybrid metal sulfide materials.

In this regard, we design coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures consisting of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle trunks decorated with numerous CuS nanosheet branches, which are in situ vertically grown on fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) substrates through a multi-step route combined with chemical and physical processes. The growth process of the hierarchical structures is carefully investigated and discussed. Subsequently, the resultant coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchically structured films are used as CEs in QDSCs, showing improved power conversion efficiency (PCE) for CdS/CdSe QDSCs as compared to the single Co9S8 nanoneedle and CuS nanosheet films. Importantly, such hierarchically structured electrodes possess great stability in electrochemical cycling tests. In particular, the designed fabrication strategy is also applicable for the growth of the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures on other substrates, especially flexible and fiber-shaped substrates.

2. Experimental details

2.1 Deposition of different blocking layers on FTO substrates

2.1.1 Deposition of TiO2 blocking layers. Fluorinated tin oxide (FTO) substrates (2 cm Ã— 1.5 cm) with a sheet resistivity of 15 Î© cmâˆ’2 are cleaned by ultrasonic agitation in acetone, deionized water and ethanol sequentially for 15 min. The cleaned FTO substrates are first immersed in a 0.18 M TiCl4 aqueous solution at 70 Â°C for 30 min, and then rinsed with deionized water. After being dried in air, the above treated FTO substrates are subjected to an annealing treatment at 550 Â°C for 60 min to form a thin TiO2 blocking layer.22
2.1.2 Deposition of CuS blocking layers. The radio frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering method is applied to deposit CuS blocking layers on the cleaned FTO substrates. Typically, the cleaned FTO substrates are placed in a vacuum chamber, which is then evacuated to a base pressure of 1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’3 Pa. The working pressure is adjusted to 3.0 Pa by filling the chamber with argon gas. The gas flow rate of pure Ar is fixed at 80 SCCM (standard cubic centimeter per minute). After 10 minutes, pre-sputtered CuS blocking layers on FTO substrates are obtained at 120 W for 30 min.32
2.1.3 Deposition of ZnO blocking layers. The ZnO blocking layer is deposited by the same method as the CuS blocking layers.

2.2 Fabrication of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films on FTO substrates

The Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films are fabricated on FTO substrates using a template assisted method followed by our previous work.22 The soft templates of Co(CO3)0.35Cl0.20(OH)1.10Â·1.74H2O nanoneedle films directly grow on the pre-treated FTO substrates by placing them in an aqueous solution containing 6.25% urea and 0.15 M cobalt chloride hexahydrate at 90 Â°C for 3 h. After the chemical bath deposition (CBD), the FTO substrates with the pink soft templates are transferred into a 100 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave containing a 0.01 M Na2S aqueous solution for a facile hydrothermal reaction at 180 Â°C for 10 h. After cooling to room temperature, black Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films are successfully formed on the FTO substrates.

2.3 Fabrication of Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures

The obtained Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films are further deposited using CuS seeds through the same RF sputtering process as for the deposition of CuS blocking layers to fabricate the 3D Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structure, and a hydrothermal post-treatment is applied. The CuS seeded Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films are placed in a 100 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave containing 60 mL deionized water with 0.01 M Cu(NO3)2 and 0.05 M thiourea. And the autoclave is maintained at 150 Â°C for 1â€“10 h. After cooling down to room temperature, the black films grown on FTO substrates are taken out, rinsed with deionized water and dried under vacuum. Finally, the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structure is obtained.

2.4 Assembly of CdS/CdSe/ZnS quantum dot-sensitised solar cells

The CdS/CdSe/ZnS quantum dot-sensitised solar cells (QDSCs) are assembled following our previous work.20,33 TiO2 nanoparticles (P25) are used as the photoanode material. The TiO2 paste is prepared by adding 0.5 g P25 powder and 0.5 g polyethylene glycol (PEG, mole weight = 20000) to 1 mL DI water and a 2 mL ethanol mixed solution, and magnetic stirring is performed for 3 h. The TiO2-coated photoanode with a thickness of about 20 Î¼m is prepared using the doctor blade method, and then sintered at 450 Â°C for 30 min in air to remove the organic substances. After cooling to room temperature, the TiO2-coated photoanode is immersed in a 0.2 M TiCl4 aqueous solution at 70 Â°C for 30 min followed by another calcination at 450 Â°C for 30 min.

The CdS and CdSe QDs are deposited on the TiO2-coated photoanode by a facile chemical bath deposition (CBD).34 First, the TiO2 films are immersed into a solution containing 20 mM CdCl2, 140 mM thiourea, 66 mM NH4Cl, and 230 mM ammonia for 80 min. After being rinsed with DI water and dried in air, the TiO2/CdS films are obtained. Secondly, for the deposition of CdSe QDs, the Cd(NO3)2 solution is used as a Cd source and the Na2SeSO3 aqueous solution is used as a Se source, which is prepared by dissolving 0.1 M Se powder into a 0.18 M Na2SO3 aqueous solution at 70 Â°C for about 7 h to ensure that the black Se powder completely reacted. After cooling to room temperature, the obtained Na2SeSO3 aqueous solution is filtered to remove the unreacted Se powder. It is worth noting that trisodium nitrilotriacetate monohydrate (Na3NTA) which acts as a chelating agent was essential to the CBD progress. The as-prepared TiO2/CdS films are then soaked into a mixture of a Na2SeSO3 aqueous solution and a 0.08 M Cd(NO3)2 (containing 0.16 M Na3NTA) solution with a volume ratio of 1:1 for 30 h. The whole CBD process is performed at 10 Â°C in a refrigerator in the dark. Finally, the successive ion layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) method is used to deposit the ZnS passive layer. The TiO2/CdS/CdSe films are alternately dipped into 0.1 M Zn(NO3)2 and 0.1 M Na2S solution twice for 2 min for each dipping cycle.

CdS/CdSe/ZnS coated TiO2 films with an active area of about 0.14 cm2 are assembled together with the above-prepared counter electrodes by applying a 60 Î¼m thick hot-malt sealed film as the spacer (SX 1170-25; Solaromix Co.). The polysulfide electrolyte used in this work consists of DI water and methanol with a volume ratio of 7:3 containing 0.5 M Na2S, 0.125 M S and 0.2 M KCl. The electrolyte is injected between two electrodes and driven by the capillary force through the hole on the hot-melt sealed film. In contrast, the traditional Pt, 1D Co9S8 nanoneedle and 2D CuS nanosheet CEs are also prepared on FTO substrates. To obtain Pt CEs, a 5 mM H2PtCl6 aqueous solution is drop cast onto the bare FTO substrates and annealed at 400 Â°C for 20 min. The CuS CEs are fabricated according to our previous work.32 Breifly, FTO substrates coated with CuS seed layers, which are deposited using the RF sputtering method at 120 W for 30 min, are placed in a 100 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave containing 60 mL deionized water with 0.01 M Cu(NO3)2 and 0.05 M thiourea for a hydrothermal reaction at 150 Â°C for 18 h.

2.5 Characterization

The morphologies and lattice structures of all samples are observed via field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM OXFORD ZEISS SIGMA) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEOL JEM-2100) with an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. The surface roughness of the bare and diverse blocking layer coated FTO substrates is tested via atomic force microscopy (AFM Bruker MultiMode 8 scanning probe microscopy). The phase identification is performed via X-ray diffraction (XRD, Panalytical X'pert PRO) as well as X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (PHI Quantum-2000 XPS, monochromatized Al KÎ± radiation (1486.6 eV)). The Jâ€“V curves of the QDSCs are measured under AM 1.5G simulated standard solar light (Oriel 300 W Xe lamp and Newport AM-1.5G filter). CV measurements are carried out by a CHI600E electrochemical workstation in a three electrode system with the saturated calomel (SCE) electrode as the reference electrode, Pt foil as the counter electrode, and the as-prepared samples as the working electrodes at a scan rate of 50 mV sâˆ’1. For QDSCs, the polysulfide electrolyte containing DI water (70 mL) and methanol (30 mL) with Na2S (0.5 M), S (0.125 M) and KCl (0.2 M) is used in CV analysis. The EIS and Tafel tests are both performed on the symmetric cells by the CHI600E electrochemical workstation using the same polysulfide electrolyte as in the CV test. The frequency of the EIS test ranges from 10âˆ’1 to 105 Hz. The scan rate of Tafel analysis was 5 mV sâˆ’1.

3. Results and discussion

As illustrated in Fig. 1, the complete growth of the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchically structured films on FTO substrates experiences multiple processes. Typically, the bare FTO substrates are first deposited with TiO2 blocking layers, followed by the direct growth of Co(CO3)0.35Cl0.20(OH)1.10Â·1.74H2O (CoT) nanoneedle films via chemical bath deposition (CBD), which serve as soft templates for the conversion of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle arrays.22 Subsequently, CuS seed layers are sputtered onto the surface of the Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films to assist the final growth of CuS nanosheet branches, consequently achieving the successful construction of the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures on FTO substrates. Among these growth steps, the first deposition of TiO2 blocking layers is especially important for the next in situ growth of the CoT films on FTO substrates. Thus, the selection and the effect of the blocking layers need to be further studied.

Fig. 2 presents scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of different blocking layers deposited on FTO substrates, the subsequently grown CoT soft templates and the final products of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films. As shown in Fig. 2aâ€“c, almost no film remains on the FTO substrates without any blocking layers, mainly due to the quite weak binding force between FTO and the required films. However, with a TiCl4 pre-treatment to form a thin TiO2 blocking layer on the FTO substrates (Fig. 2d), pink CoT nanoneedle films could grow tightly on the FTO substrates (Fig. 2e), which are further converted into black Co9S8 films (Fig. 2f). To investigate the effect of different blocking layers, CuS and ZnO thin films are also deposited on FTO substrates to replace the TiO2 blocking layer using a radio frequency magnetron sputtering method. As shown in Fig. 2gâ€“i, similar pink CoT nanoneedle templates and black Co9S8 films are also observed on the FTO substrates deposited with the CuS blocking layer. The same result is found for the ZnO blocking layer (Fig. 2jâ€“l). It is indicated that the surface of the treated FTO substrates (Fig. 2d, g and j) becomes rougher rather than that of the bare FTO substrates (Fig. 2a). Therefore, it is proposed that the blocking layers may provide sufficient sites for crystal growth and then serve as bridges to strengthen the binding force between the target films and FTO substrates.

To further verify the above conjecture, atomic force microscopy (AFM) is carried out. Fig. 3 shows the surface AFM morphologies of the four types of FTO substrates. It is obvious that the particle size on the surface of bare FTO is only about 1 nm, which is much smaller as compared to TiO2 (120 nm), CuS (70 nm), and ZnO (70 nm), suggesting the coarser surface of the treated FTO substrates. Moreover, the surface root-mean-square roughness (Ra) of each substrate is also calculated using the NanoScope Analysis software. The Ra value of bare FTO is 0.157 nm, which is extremely small in contrast with the TiO2 (22.4 nm), CuS (14.6 nm), and ZnO (13.1 nm) deposited FTO substrates, in good agreement with the above conjecture again. Besides, it is considered that the surface hydrophilic property may be another factor for the crystal growth, and then contact angle analysis is performed as well. The corresponding results (Fig. S1, ESIâ€ ) indicate that the TiO2 coating largely increases the substrate hydrophilicity while the CuS or ZnO deposition leads to the decreased hydrophilicity of the resultant FTO substrates. Then, it is considered that the surface hydrophilic property of the substrates likely has little influence on the firm growth of CoT nanoneedle films on FTO substrates. Moreover, the type of blocking layer rarely affects the morphologies of CoT and Co9S8 films (Fig. 2e, f, h, i, k and l). Thus, the Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle films with a length of approximately 3 Î¼m and an average diameter of approximately 100 nm based on the TiO2 blocking layer are chosen for the subsequent synthesis of the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures.

Furthermore, time-dependent evolution study and control experiments are carried out as well (Fig. S3, ESIâ€ ). When Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles pre-deposited with CuS seeds are hydrothermally treated at 150 Â°C for 1 h (Fig. S3a, ESIâ€ ), a few CuS nanosheets emerge on the walls of the Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles. Further prolonging the reaction times to 4 h (Fig. S3c, ESIâ€ ), 6 h (Fig. S3d, ESIâ€ ), 8 h (Fig. S3e, ESIâ€ ), and 10 h (Fig. S3f, ESIâ€ ), respectively, CuS nanosheets become thicker and thicker, and finally stack together, but they still retain the coralloid 3D hierarchical features. According to the above experimental results, the growth process of the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structure can be generally elucidated as follows. First, the pre-deposition of blocking layers promotes the effective in situ growth of CoT soft templates on FTO substrates. Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles are obtained through the reaction between CoT and Na2S using a sacrificial template method. This process can be explained by the Kirkendall effect, in which S2âˆ’ ions are dispersed from Na2S to react with cobalt ions in CoT to form hollow Co9S8 nanoneedles, which keep the same nanoneedle structure as templates but become hollow mainly owing to the faster outward diffusion of cobalt ions as compared to the inward diffusion of S2âˆ’ ions.22 As to the hierarchical structure, it is proposed that CuS nanosheets grow on the walls of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles through the heterogeneous nucleation process. Typically, the pre-sputtered CuS seeds on the walls of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles provide nucleation sites for the latter hydrothermal reaction. Aqueous Cu(NO3)2 serves as the Cu source to offer Cu2+ ions, and S2âˆ’ ions are released slowly by thiourea to combine with Cu2+ ions to form CuS nanosheets.

Subsequently, to investigate the electrochemical performance of the as-prepared samples, the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS electrodes prepared with different hydrothermal reaction times are employed as CEs for CdS/CdSe QDSCs. It is revealed that the Co9S8â€“CuS electrode prepared for 4 h exhibits the highest short-current density (JSC) and fill factor (FF), contributing to the optimal PCE value of 4.50%. Therefore, the optimized sample is chosen to compare with the conventional Pt film, 1D Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles (Fig. 2f), and 2D CuS nanosheets (Fig. S5, ESIâ€ ) prepared on FTO substrates.

4. Conclusion

In summary, coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS hierarchical structures composed of Co9S8 hollow nanoneedles decorated with CuS nanosheets are rationally designed and fabricated via a multi-step route. The growth process and mechanism are carefully studied. The heterostructured Co9S8â€“CuS films are further applied as CEs for QDSCs with an optimum PCE of 4.50%, showing enhancements of 38% and 17% as compared to the 1D Co9S8 hollow nanoneedle and 2D CuS nanosheet CEs, respectively. It is suggested that the incorporation of Co9S8 and CuS is beneficial for their electrocatalytic activity as well as stability in the electrochemical cycling tests. This is primarily due to the enhanced charge transport and increased active reaction sites from the heterojunctions and heterostructures of the hybrid metal sulfides. Importantly, this fabrication strategy can be applied to grow the coralloid Co9S8â€“CuS films on flexible substrates, such as carbon cloth, Ti mesh, and Ni foam. It may be available to realize various combinations of different metal sulfides, which will inherit individual merits from each single component and broaden their promising applications in many fields.
