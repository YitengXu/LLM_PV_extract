Abstract

A systematic investigation was conducted to determine the excitation energy dependent emission characteristics of CdSe quantum dotâÎ²-carotene and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell quantum dotâÎ²-carotene hybrid samples. The emission intensity was recorded at different excitation energies by continuously varying the excitation energy from the band edge value of the QDs to a maximum value. In both the hybrids, the emission intensity increased with an increase of the excitation energy and then a sudden quenching occurred, which is in contrast with the results from bare samples, where the emission intensity showed a decreasing trend at all excitation energies. The quantum yields corresponding to the maximum emission are 47.12% and 69%, for CdSe QDâÎ²C and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDâÎ²C hybrids, respectively. At higher excitation energy, electrons were transferred to the molecule's LUMO level, leaving behind holes in the valance band of the QD, and thus the produced charge separated state became responsible for the PL quenching in the hybrid sample. We have confirmed this charge separated state by lifetime measurements. It is because of this PL quenching behaviour of the hybrids that the nature of the interface band structure was deduced as type I. Furthermore, because of the involvement of higher energy photons in the quenching process, the transferred electrons in the LUMO level of the molecule were known as hot electrons. The present paper discusses the excited state electron dynamics across the interface between the two hybrid materials in detail.

1. Introduction

Size tuneable optical and electronic properties of semiconductor quantum dots (QDs) have made them interesting materials for various applications. Solar cell fabrication is one of the applications,1â5 in which QDs and their hybrids such as polymerâQD hybrids,6,7 CNTâQD hybrids8 and polymerâcoreâshell QD hybrids9â11 have been used. Tandem photo-electrochemical solar cells12 and depleted-heterojunction solar cells13 are some other new device designs recently tried for solar cell fabrication. Though QDs have several advantages over dyes, the QD sensitized cells are less efficient, because of various possible carrier loss mechanisms. Trap state assisted recombination within the QDs, recombination at the interface between the QD layer and electrolyte, recombination between the QD layer and oxide electrode and recombination between the adjacent QD layers are the major carrier loss mechanisms in the device design. By solving these problems, one can improve the performance of a QD based solar cell. Recently, there have been attempts to extract the hot carriers (electrons and holes) from QDs. If it is possible to utilize these hot carriers (for example, hot electrons) then the solar energy to power conversion efficiency of the solar cell can be increased further, because of the increase in the photocurrent or open circuit voltage of the device. The photocurrent is increased by a process known as carrier multiplication or multiple exciton generation, and the threshold energy required for this process to occur is two to three times greater than the band gap energy of the material.14â18 If the incident photon energy is below the threshold limit of producing multiexcitons, but produces hot electrons by exciting a few kT above the band edge state, then it is possible to increase the open circuit voltage of the device by directly extracting them towards the metal oxide electrode. If the extracted hot carriers, (for example, hot electrons) are injected into the metal oxide electrode, without energy and recombination losses, then the definition for the open-circuit voltage, which is usually defined as âthe difference between the redox potential of the electrolyte and the conduction band (CB) edge state of the QDâ could be redefined as âthe difference between the redox potential of the electrolyte and the higher energy state CB level of the QDâ. This fundamental difference in the carrier extraction process, then, improves the open circuit voltage of the device. However, the extraction process (of the hot carrier) has to take place before the ultra fast cooling towards the band edge states. In a bulk material, hot electron extraction is difficult, because cooling down is through continuum states on an ultrafast timescale (Fig. 1). In the absence of a phonon bottleneck effect in QDs,19 âAuger likeâ20 processes and âtrap statesâ21 are the pathways of cooling and are, in fact, dominant at higher excitation energies, as reported elsewhere.22 Therefore hot electron extraction from QDs is also a challenging process.23,24
Fig. 1 Schematic representation of a hot electron cooling process in the bulk material. Here, the CB states are continuous in nature and therefore the photo-generated hot electrons relax down towards the CB edge state, on an ultrafast timescale, and thereby lose their excess energy as heat.

Recently, Anshu Pandey and Philippe Guyot-Sionnest25 have extracted hot electrons from CdSe/ZnSe coreâshell QDs. They were irradiated with a resonant 4 Î¼m intraband pulse, after an interband excitation pulse, so that the valence band edge electrons were promoted to a higher energy state CB level (1pe) of the CdSe QD, and from there they were tunnelled out through the ZnSe outer layer as hot electrons. A similar hot electron extraction process was also realised in PbSe QDs by Aydil and Zhu.19 They prepared PbSe QDs with a size distribution of 3.3â6.7 nm and deposited a layer of them onto a TiO2 pre coated surface. For a particular size, hot electron extraction was made possible from PbSe QDs, because the interface is such that the higher energy state CB electrons of this particular size QD were able to transfer to the CB level of TiO2. Apart from these attempts, silicon based thin solar cell devices have also been constructed proving the hot electron effect in device performance.26

Here, we report on the successful extraction of hot electrons from CdSe and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDs by using the Î²-carotene (Î²C) molecule as an electron acceptor. All trans Î²C27 is a well known linear conjugated molecule and its structure has a backbone of eleven carbon double bonds. It is well known that this molecule can establish charge transfer type interactions28 with iodine and AsF5 and therefore its natural absorption is reduced or completely disappears. However, the interaction between Î²C and nanostructures like QDs, nanowires, nanotubes etc., is not well studied. Recently this molecule was successfully introduced into carbon nanotubes and was investigated for its structural stability and conformation.29 Here we have used this molecule to prepare a hybrid with CdSe and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDs and investigated their photophysical properties in detail.

2. Experimental details

2.1. Preparation of CdSe and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDs

CdSe QDs were prepared by a well-known method, as reported elsewhere.30 First we prepared 0.2 mmol of selenium stock solution by dissolving selenium powder in 5 ml octadecene (ODE) and kept the solution at 180 Â°C for an hour. Then we prepared cadmium solution by taking CdO powder as a precursor in a mixture solution of 1 ml oleic acid (OA) and 9 ml of octadecene, which was then heated to 180 Â°C for an hour until a clear solution was formed. While increasing the temperature of the CdO containing solution to 220 Â°C, 5 ml selenium stock solution was added and the reaction was allowed to run for 15 minutes to obtain large CdSe QDs. The as prepared colloidal QD solution, after repeated washing with ethanol, was subjected to structural and optical characterization. The as obtained final product was a semisolid which was suspended in 10 ml ODE for coreâshell preparation. The as prepared Zn and S precursors were simultaneously added into the already prepared CdSe QD solution (suspended in ODE), which was also kept at 180 Â°C. Now aliquots were taken out of this reaction every five minutes to obtain coreâshell QDs. The removed aliquots were washed several times with ethanol and re-dispersed in 10 ml of hexane. To prepare the hybrid sample, different volume of Î²C solution (1 mlâ5 ml) prepared at 0.01 mmol, was mixed with the re-dispersed QD and QD and coreâshell QD solutions.

2.2. Characterization

The crystal structure and size details of the as prepared samples were obtained using X-ray diffraction (XRD) and high resolution transmission microscopy (HR-TEM). XRD patterns were recorded by using an XPERT PRO model PANalytical X-ray diffractometer. HR-TEM images were recorded with a JEOL model STEM instrument. Optical absorption and steady state photoluminescence (PL) experiments were done using a SHIMADZU 3600 UV-Vis-NIR model spectrophotometer and a Horiba Jobin Yvon spectrofluromax 4, respectively. Life time measurements were conducted using time correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) and laser flash photolysis methods (details of the experiments are given in the ESIâ ).

Steady state PL characteristics of CdSe QDs and the hybrid samples were obtained at an excitation energy of 2.06 eV, which is equivalent to the band edge absorption value of the QDs. The quantum yield corresponding to the bare CdSe QD sample, calculated with reference to rhodamine B, is about 17.62%. In the case of the hybrid samples, the quantum yield increased with an increase in the Î²C concentration. When the added Î²C concentration was about 5 ml, then a saturated emission was noticed as shown in Fig. 2b. We also noticed a gradual shift in the emission maximum towards the higher energy side, as the Î²C concentration was increased from 1 ml to 5 ml (Fig. 2b). The quantum yield corresponding to the emission maximum is about 47.12%, and it is about 2.67 times higher than the yield value calculated for the bare CdSe QD sample. The improvement in the quantum yield and blue shift are due to the surface passivation effect on the QDs by Î²C. Because of this passivation effect, non-radiative relaxation pathways are arrested and therefore the quantum yield is increased. The reason for the observed blue shift in the emission maximum could be due to the induced Stark effect. The attachment of bio-molecules occurs via electrostatic or covalent forces; therefore, a distribution of these molecules on the QD surface imposes a net electric field across the QD core, as reported elsewhere.33 It could be because of this electric field assisted Stark effect that the energy levels of the core QDs are separated, thus resulting in a blue shift in the emission maximum.

3.2. Excitation energy dependent PL study up to 2.48 eV

Excitation energy dependent PL experiments were conducted on bare and hybrid samples. The quantum yield of the bare CdSe QDs was decreased gradually (curve âiâ in Fig. 3), from a value of 17.62 to 5.81%, as the excitation energy was increased from the band edge value of the QD to higher energy values. This gradual decrease in the quantum yield is usual behavior and has been observed by others. Unpassivated surface states, associated with the surface atoms of colloidal QDs, provide a nonradiative relaxation pathway or channel to the highly excited charge carriers, and therefore, the higher energy excited charge carriers are either inhibited or deviated from reaching the band edge states, from where radiative recombination results.34 In the case of the hybrids, the quantum yield increased with the increase of the excitation energy up to 2.28 eV, and this is unusual behavior. Beyond this, the quantum yield decreased suddenly (curve âiiâ in Fig. 3), reaching a minimum yield value of 0.25%, at 2.48 eV excitation energy and above. The initial increase in the quantum yield is due to the confinement of the photoexcited electrons in the CB offset between the QD and Î²C molecule. However, when the electrons were excited beyond the confinement level then there was a sudden drop in the quantum yield and that must be due to the transfer of electrons to the LUMO level of the molecule.

3.4. Transient absorption analysis

Finally, to confirm the charge separated state, with electrons in the LUMO level of Î²C and holes in the valence band edge state of CdSe QD, nanosecond transient absorption (TA) measurements were made. The wavelength of the laser source used for this experiment was 355 nm (3.50 eV). Fig. 6 shows the TA spectra of the hybrid sample with two negative bleach minima at 2.75 and 2.0 eV. As these bleach positions match with the absorption maximum value of Î²C and the absorption edge value of the CdSe QDs at 2.75 and 2.0 eV, their origin is expected to come from these two respective systems. This could not be due to the direct excitation of either Î²C or CdSe QDs because their respective individual bleach profiles recover at a much faster rate. For example, in the case of pure Î²C the excited state electrons in the S2 state undergo a fast internal conversion down to the S1 state within 200 fs. Again, from there the electrons undergo a downward transition to the S0 state in a few tens of picoseconds.39â41 In the case of the CdSe QDs, the band edge bleach profile recovers within a few nanoseconds, as reported elsewhere.42 Therefore the slowly recovering negative bleach at 2.75 eV must be due to the transferred electrons from the CdSe QD to the LUMO level of the Î²C. As the excitation energy used in this TA experiment is well above the second absorption maximum of the QDs, the VB electrons of the photoexcited CdSe QDs are transferred to the LUMO level of the Î²C molecule. The transferred electrons remain there as hot electrons for a few tens of nanoseconds and therefore any further transition to this molecular level (followed by the excitation of the molecule (HOMO to LUMO)) is blocked, and as a consequence the negative bleach is obtained at 2.75 eV. To make sure that this 2.75 eV negative bleach is from Î²C, we measured the difference between the two peak positions at A1 and A2 which is 0.27 eV, agreeing with the vibrational level separation of Î²C.
Fig. 6 The transient absorption (bleach) spectra recorded from the CdSe QDâÎ²C hybrid sample. The negative bleach positions â1â and â2â correspond to the presence of holes in the VB edge state of the QDs and to the presence of transferred electrons in the LUMO level of Î²C, respectively. The other slow recovering negative bleach at 2.0 eV is due to presence of âholesâ in the VB edge state of the CdSe QD. This 2.0 eV bleach appears until the holes in the VB edge state of CdSe QD are depleted by the back transferred electrons from the LUMO level of the Î²C. Hot electrons in the molecular LUMO level back transfer to the CB edge state of the CdSe QDs and from there they recombine with the holes in the VB level of the CdSe QDs, and by this process (cooling process) both the molecule and the QD are brought back to their respective neutral states. Fig. 7a and b show the time dependent absorption profiles measured at the 2.0 and 2.75 eV peak positions of the bleach spectra. To determine the average lifetime values associated with this back transferring process and the cooling of the charge carriers, we fitted the absorption curves measured at 2.75 and 2.0 eV with Gaussians. A value of â¼24.44 ns was obtained from the absorption curves at 2.75 eV and this could correspond to the average lifetime value for the back transferring of hot electrons into the CB edge state of the CdSe QDs. A similar average lifetime value measured from the absorption curves at 2.0 eV is â¼30.05 ns, and this corresponds to the time taken by the back transferred electrons to recombine with the holes in the VB of CdSe QDs (cooling process). Since the average lifetime value for the cooling process is higher in magnitude than that required for the back transferring process, this indicates that the recombination process is possible only after the arrival back of transferred electrons into the CB edge state of the CdSe QDs. These differences in the <Ï> lifetime values therefore suggest that the back transferring mechanism or pathway of electrons is via the CB edge state of the CdSe QDs. The slowly recovering negative bleaches at 2.75 and 2.0 eV are, therefore, direct evidence for the existence of hot electrons.

Type I coreâshell structures, such as CdSe/ZnS, are well known for their enhanced emission yield.43 However, when their outer surfaces are attached with electron accepting molecules like benzoquinone or surrounded by metal oxide nanocrystals (TiO2), then the photoexcited charge carriers are separated out of the CdSe QDs and the quantum yield from the core QD is quenched.44 In this surface functionalized type I CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QD, the photoexcited electrons are thermalized to the CB edge state of the CdSe QD, and then they tunnel out to the electron accepting system (molecule or metal oxide). It is because of this thermalization process that excess energy possessed by the electrons is lost in the core QD itself (when it is excited with a higher excitation energy). Interestingly, our hybrid system, which is also type I in nature, has shown different characteristics. It exhibits both fluorescence enhancement and quenching, and one can tune the sample between these two states just by varying the excitation energy. At a lower excitation energy, because of the confinement effect, the quantum yield was increased similar to a CdSe/ZnS coreâshell structure. But once the excitation energy was raised above a threshold value of 2.25 eV then the electrons were transferred to the LUMO level of the molecule, leading to the PL quenching. We believe that the electron wave function overlap with the molecular level is high for the higher energy state CB electrons of the CdSe QDs, and therefore they are transferred to the Î²C.

In this hybrid nanostructure, emission enhancement and quenching are possible only when Î²C establishes physical contact with the QD surface. This situation is equivalent to introducing a âholeâ on the top of a half-filled vessel such that a water outlet is possible only when the water level rises up to the âholeâ. In a similar manner electron extraction out of the QD is possible (in this hybrid) only when electrons are raised above the CB edge state of the QD. In the oleated CdSe QD, Î²C intercalates inbetween the oleic acid molecules as shown in Fig. 8a and can develop either a weak physical or strong chemical binding with the QD surface. However, because Î²C does not have any functional groups to bind, chemical binding with the QD surface is not possible. On the other hand, a weak physical interaction is possible. A similar weak interaction between the interacting molecules and the QDs is well reported.5 To confirm the intercalation, FTIR spectrum was recorded. Fig. 9 (curve b) shows the FTIR spectrum of the CdSeâÎ²C hybrid sample. Interestingly, we noticed a suppressed vibrational mode (of Î²C) at 974 cmâ1, which usually originates from the âCHCHâ out of plane vibration of the Î²C. (For comparison purposes, the FTIR spectrum was also recorded from bare QD and pure Î²C as shown in Fig. 9(a and c).) It can be said that this out of plane vibrational motion of the âCHCHâ is constrained in this hybrid sample, and we believe that this constraint is due to the intercalation of the Î²C between the oleic acid molecules (Fig. 8b).

3.5. Excitation energy dependent PL beyond 2.48 eV

In the excitation energy dependent PL study Î²C was not excited up to 2.48 eV. If Î²C was also excited then it would influence the emission process of the hybrid sample. To determine the nature of the influence, the excitation energy dependent PL study was extended beyond 2.48 eV. When the excitation energy was around 2.75 eV, then a weak emission band started to appear at 2.0 eV as shown in Fig. 10. As this emission maximum matched the band edge value of the CdSe QDs, it could originate from the CdSe QD, and either charge or energy transfer induced interaction between Î²C and the QD may be responsible for it.
Fig. 10 The excitation energy dependent emission behaviour in the range from 2.26 to 2.95 eV.

As the LUMO level of the molecule is at a higher energy position, the excited molecules can transfer their electrons to the CB edge state of the CdSe QD, leaving behind the holes in its HOMO level. These holes will be then preferentially occupied by the electrons from the VB edge state of the QD, and as a consequence holes are introduced in the VB edge state of the QD. When these introduced holes recombine with the transferred electrons in the CB of the QD, then band edge emission is possible, or otherwise if the holes are introduced from the QD's deep VB states into the HOMO level of the molecule, followed by the excitation of the QD, then they could be depleted immediately followed by the recombination process with the excited electrons in the CB edge state of QD. By this process Î²C and QD are neutralised. Now once again electronâhole pairs may be introduced into the QDs, followed by the excitation of the molecule, as discussed before, and this could lead to a weak band edge emission. However, the above charge transfer induced emission must occur at all higher excitation energies. However, the experimental results have shown (Fig. 10) that this weak emission appears only when the excitation energy matches the absorption maximum of Î²C at 2.75 eV. This means that the charge transfer induced process is not responsible for this weak emission. The other possibility is through the energy transfer assisted process. The emission spectrum of Î²C is in the range from 2.48 to 1.91 eV (Fig. S2(b))â  (the emission spectrum of Î²C given is similar to that reported elsewhere45) and as it is overlapping with the absorption band position of the acceptor CdSe QD at 2.0 eV, energy transfer assisted band edge emission from the CdSe QDs is possible.46 Some of the Î²C molecules which were not in contact with the QD surface, but still in close proximity can have an energy transfer type interaction with the QDs, and make them emit at the band edge value.

The spectrum corresponding to the coreâshell QDâÎ²C hybrid sample shows bands of the coreâshell QD and Î²C as in Fig. 11a (curve âiiâ). One more interesting observation is that the absorption maximum of the CdSe/ZnS QDs is also red shifted with respect to the absorption maximum of the CdSe QDs (Fig. 11a). A slight blue shift was seen in the emission maximum (Fig. 11b), and the reason for this could be the surface passivation effect on the ZnS shell layer by Î²C. When the delocalization of excitons into the ZnS shell layer is responsible for the red shift in the emission maximum of the coreâshell QDs, the observed blue shift in the emission maximum must be due to the decrease in the âexciton delocalization effectâ into the shell layer. The observed results therefore support the surface passivation of the ZnS shell layer and the consequent enhancement in the emission yield.

4.2. Excitation energy dependent PL behaviour of the CdSe/ZnSâÎ²C hybrid system

We observed the emission behaviour of the coreâshellâÎ²C hybrid sample by exciting it at higher energies, well above the second absorption maximum of the core CdSe/ZnS QD at 2.21 eV. Fig. 13 shows the excitation energy dependent quenching behaviour of the CdSe/ZnS and CdSe/ZnSâÎ²C samples. Here also the emission quenching started suddenly, once the excitation energy was increased beyond the absorption maximum (curve âiiâ in Fig. 13) at 2.21 eV. However, up to 2.21 eV, the emission intensity was increased and this means that the excited electrons up to this energy level are confined in the core QD. The sudden quenching was due to the transfer of higher energy (hot) electrons from the CdSe QD to the LUMO level of the Î²C. Though a similar higher energy CB electron transfer process becomes responsible for the quenching in the CdSeâÎ²C and CdTeâÎ²C51 hybrid systems, here the electron transfer process occurs via tunneling through the ZnS shell layer. It has to be pointed out that the emission quenching is not complete as was noticed in the case of the CdSeâÎ²C and CdTeâÎ²C hybrid systems. We believe that the mismatching of energy levels across the barrier (ZnS layer), limits the tunneling probability, because of the violation of the tunnelling conditions (Fig. 14).

4.4. Transient absorption analysis of the CdSe/ZnS coreâshell and its hybrids

We also recorded bleach spectra from the coreâshellâÎ²C hybrid sample and confirmed the presence of transferred hot electrons in the LUMO level of the molecule. Fig. 16a shows the transient absorption spectra or bleach spectra of the coreâshellâÎ²C hybrid sample which shows two negative bleach minima at 2.69 and 1.97 eV, respectively, due to the presence of transferred electrons in the LUMO level of Î²C and due to the presence of holes in the VB edge state of the core CdSe QDs. Both the negative bleaches recovered slowly, because of the back transfer process. Once the negative bleach at 2.69 eV recovered completely then the positive bleach started to appear at 2.69 eV due to the band edge absorption of the molecule. This positive bleach position of the molecule is red shifted because of the conformation change as discussed before. We also noticed one more positive bleach at 2.25 eV, as observed by several others in the coreâshell structure, and this is due to the Stark effect.31 The Stark field is developed inside the QD when its surface is passivated by the ZnS shell layer, and consequently the energy levels are split. When the absorption takes place, with the involvement of these split energy levels then the positive bleach starts to appear at 2.25 eV. We have also noticed a similar positive absorption bleach at 2.25 eV from the bare CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QD sample as shown in Fig. S4.â

This value is high in magnitude when compared to a similar value obtained at 2.75 eV from the ZnS passivation free CdSe QDâÎ²C hybrid sample. The obtained higher lifetime value suggests that the transferred electrons spend more time in the Î²C molecule, in the case of the coreâshell QDâÎ²C hybrid sample, than in the case of the CdSe QDâÎ²C hybrid, and that must obviously be due to the presence of the ZnS shell layer, which blocks the easy back transfer of electrons from Î²C to the core QD in the coreâshell structure. This delayed back transfer pathway is again via the CB edge state of the QD. The electrons tunnel through the ZnS layer and step down to the CB edge state of the CdSe QD and from there they recombining with the holes in the VB edge state of the CdSe QD. It is for this reason that the negative bleach at 1.97 eV recovers after the recovery of the negative bleach at 2.69 eV. The <Ï> value obtained from the bleach profile at 1.97 eV is â¼41 ns, which is high in magnitude when compared to the lifetime value calculated at 2.69 eV and this difference in lifetime value confirms the back transfer pathway. 5. Conclusion

In summary, we found that the photoluminescence behaviour of the CdSe QDs, CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDs and the hybrid with Î²C samples are excitation energy dependent and therefore both the emission enhancement and quenching were tuneable in these systems. Importantly, we demonstrated that it is possible to extract hot electrons from the photoexcited CdSe and CdSe/ZnS coreâshell QDs by using Î²C as an electron acceptor. The relative energy level positions of the QD and Î²C favour hot electron transfer from the higher energy state CB level of the QD into the LUMO level of the Î²C. In the case of the CdSe/ZnS coreâshell system, the ZnS shell layer passivates the CdSe QD surface and therefore the quantum yield was increased to 53.7%. At the same time, in the case of the CdSe/ZnSâÎ²C hybrid system, the ZnS shell layer is passivated by Î²C and hence, the quantum yield is improved further to 69%. In the CdSeâÎ²C hybrid system, the charge separated state, with electrons in the LUMO level of Î²C and holes in the VB of the QD, existed for a much longer time (a few tens of ns) and therefore the slowing down of the cooling process was possible in our hybrid sample. This cooling process was still slowed down in the CdSe/ZnSâÎ²C hybrid system, because the ZnS shell layer blocked the easy flow of electrons back to the core QD. If these hot electrons, present in the LUMO level of the Î²C can be further extracted to the electrodes of a solar cell device, without any energy loss, then the energy conversion efficiency of the cell could be increased further, and our future research will be on this problem.
