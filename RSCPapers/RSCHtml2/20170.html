Abstract

The IIIâ€“V semiconductor GaN is a promising material for photoelectrochemical (PEC) cells, however the large bandgap of 3.45 eV is a considerable hindrance for the absorption of visible light. Therefore, the substitution of small amounts of N anions by isovalent Sb is a promising route to lower the bandgap and thus increase the PEC activity under visible light. Herein we report a new chemical vapor deposition (CVD) process utilizing the precursors bis(N,Nâ€²-diisopropyl-2-methyl-amidinato)-methyl gallium (III) and triphenyl antimony (TPSb) for the growth of GaSbxN1âˆ’x alloys. X-ray diffraction (XRD) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) measurements show crystalline and homogeneous thin films at deposition temperatures in the range of 500â€“800 Â°C. Rutherford backscattering spectrometry (RBS) combined with nuclear reaction analysis (NRA) shows an incorporation of 0.2â€“0.7 at% antimony into the alloy, which results in a slight bandgap decrease (up to 0.2 eV) accompanied by enhanced sub-bandgap optical response. While the resulting photoanodes are active under visible light, the external quantum efficiencies remained low. Intriguingly, the best performing films exhibits the lowest charge carrier mobility according to time resolved THz spectroscopy (TRTS) and microwave conductivity (TRMC) measurements, which showed mobilities of up to 1.75 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and 1.2 Ã— 10â€“2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, for each timescale, respectively.

Introduction

In pursuit of alternative ways to generate renewable fuel sources, solar driven water splitting has gained great scientific interest in recent years. Photoanodes in photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting applications require a semiconducting material with a bandgap in the range of 1.7 to 2.2 eV. Additionally, the band edges need to straddle the H2/O2 redox potentials.1 A highly promising material in this regard is GaN, which shows high stability during photoelectrolysis and exhibits matching band edges. However, it features a wide (direct) bandgap of 3.45 eV and therefore only absorbs light in the UV region.2 In order to overcome this issue, the bandgap can be engineered toward more desired, lower values by alloying GaN with other materials. This way ternary and quaternary compounds can be formed, which exhibit more favourable electronic properties, while retaining encouraging properties, like PEC stability from their parent compound. To this end, several reports have been published during the last decade. For example GaON3 and GaN:ZnO4 have been investigated as potential photoabsorbers in the visible light range. Interestingly, the ternary material InxGa1âˆ’xN is the most explored among the GaN derivatives.5â€“7 While it was possible to narrow the bandgap to a desired value of approximately 2 eV, the required amount of In was found to be over 50 at%. This resulted in a considerable increase in material cost and a tendency toward phase segregation of the alloy.8,9 Instead of substituting the cations of GaN, isovalent anions, like As, Sb and Bi, can be used as dopants with bandgap narrowing effects.10â€“13 Due to the considerable size difference compared to N, the resulting materials are referred to as highly mismatched alloys (HMAs).10â€“13 This particular kind of anion substitution can influence the electronic band structure of the material to a larger degree than conventional alloying, due to the band anticrossing (BAC).14,15 Hence, HMAs are reported as promising materials for PEC water splitting, as their relatively narrow bandgaps compared to GaN, enable the absorption of a greater fraction of the sunlight, while their band edges remain at energies sufficient for water splitting.10 According to the BAC model, smaller amounts of Sb than As are necessary in order to narrow the bandgap to the desired value, indicating that a larger mismatch in terms of anion size in the photoactive material is beneficial.16,17

Theoretical reports on GaNxSb1âˆ’x in the dilute nitrogen region show electronic bandgap narrowing to negative values for âˆ¼8 at% N.1,18â€“21 On the other hand, for the dilute antimony regime, reported theoretical work is scarce.22 Experimentally, Sunkara et al. fabricated GaSbxN1âˆ’x in the dilute antimony regime via metalorganic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) utilizing the highly volatile but hazardous precursors trimethyl gallium (TMG) and trimethyl antimonide (TMSb). They show that even low Sb contents in the range 1â€“8 at% reduce the observed bandgap values to approximately 2 eV, while simultaneously retaining the desired band positions straddling the potentials of the water splitting reaction.23,24 The fact that the observed bandgap reduction is presumed to originate primarily from the shift of the valence band edge to higher energies, shows the great potential for optimization of the bandgap energy in this manner.24 A considerable challenge in the fabrication of this material system is the comparably low degree of crystallinity, which leads to high rates of recombination.25 Increased deposition temperatures may counteract this issue, however the induction of phase segregation and maintaining of a sufficient Sb incorporation, have been shown to be problematic at higher temperatures, using the molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) technique.26

MOCVD is a promising technology to fabricate IIIâ€“V semiconductors, as this technology enables superior interface quality to the underlying substrate, is readily scalable and results in high quality thin films. GaN based thin films have been mainly deposited using the alkyl class of precursors, like TMG and triethyl gallium (TEG), which are pyrophoric and therefore difficult to handle.27,28 In our previous work, we have identified bis(N,Nâ€²-diisopropyl-2-methyl-amidinato) methyl gallium (III) [Ga(Me)(dpamd)2] [1] as a suitable precursor for the deposition of Ga2O3via MOCVD, which is non-pyrophoric.29 Referring to a matching antimony precursor, there are complexes which are already known and have been applied in CVD based techniques. Like other group III elements, the most common Sb compounds are the alkyls. Trimethyl antimony (TMSb) for instance is well known and has already been applied in the synthesis of GaSb and GaNxSb1âˆ’x thin films.23,30,31 However, due to its pyrophoric and toxic nature32 and high volatility, TMSb is not suitable for this study, especially since only small amounts of Sb are to be incorporated in the film. Therefore, triphenyl antimony (TPSb, [2]), which exhibits a much lower vapor pressure was the precursor of choice in this study.

Herein, we present the synthesis of alloyed GaSbxN1âˆ’x thin films via a CVD approach in the dilute antimony regime, using the precursor compounds TPSb, and [Ga(Me)(dpamd)2]. The precursors were analysed via thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). GaSbxN1âˆ’x films were analysed regarding their structure, composition and morphology via X-ray diffraction (XRD), Rutherford backscattering spectrometry combined with nuclear reaction analysis (RBS/NRA) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) respectively. Films deposited on fluorine doped tin oxide (FTO) and quartz were investigated regarding the optical and PEC properties via UV-Vis spectroscopy and incident photon-to-current efficiency (IPCE) measurements. Furthermore, the charge carrier mobility was investigated employing time resolved THz spectroscopy (TRTS) and time resolved microwave conductivity (TRMC) measurements.

To investigate the elemental composition of the alloyed thin films, RBS/NRA was employed. Table 2 gives an overview of the material composition and the respective amount of alloyed antimony as a fraction of the anionic species. Almost all films are gallium rich exhibiting a Ga/(N + Sb) ratio of 1 to 1.5, increasing with decreasing temperature. This can be explained by the simultaneously rising oxygen incorporation, as the ammonia decomposition kinetics proceed considerably slower at lower temperatures, thus resulting in a high oxide contribution, probably due to minor reactor leaks. These oxide contributions may possibly result in additional GaO or GaON phases inside the deposits. The antimony contents of deposited GaSbxN1âˆ’x thin films were estimated to be in the range of 0.2 to 0.7 at% with respect to the deposited N species. Even though the Sb incorporation is considerably smaller than in the reports by Sunkara et al., the band narrowing effect should still be observable on this scale of doping.23 The fact that the Sb content increases with higher ammonia flowrates, indicates that the GaN and GaSb deposition reactions are not competing. Instead, ammonia appears to enhance the reactivity of the TPSb precursor. Apart from films grown at 400 Â°C and 800 Â°C with 30 sccm NH3, a low carbon contamination of below 5 at% was detected. The deposited films were analysed using XRD to identify their structure. The XRD patterns of the GaSbxN1âˆ’x thin films are depicted in Fig. 2b and c, showing four dominant reflections of Wurtzite type GaN. Particularly, the (100), (002), (101) and (110) at 32.5Â°, 34.6Â°, 36.6Â° and 58.1Â° reflections respectively. There are no reflections present which could be assigned to cubic GaSb, attributed to the small concentration of antimony inside the GaN thin films. This is in line with other studies considering the dilute antimony level.16 Interestingly, a shift of the (002) reflection, as described by Sunkara et al., could not be detected, and the reflection shifts would likely be small for such dilute incorporation.23 At low growth temperatures (400 Â°Câ€“500 Â°C) no reflections could be observed. This could either be an effect of low film thickness or a low degree of crystallinity or even a combination of both. At 600 Â°C and 700 Â°C, the (100) reflection is the most prominent, with the (110) and (101) reflections showing less intensity. Interestingly, at 800 Â°C the growth mode appears to change, and the previously observed reflections vanish, while the (002) reflection arises simultaneously. When the NH3 flow rate is reduced at a deposition temperature of 600 Â°C, the reflection intensity reduces simultaneously along with the reduction in film thickness. For the NH3 flow reduction at 800 Â°C, another change in growth mode can be observed as the (110) reflection becomes more prominent again. This indicates a higher structural similarity to the films deposited at lower temperatures.

To investigate the microstructure of the alloyed thin films, SEM images depicted in Fig. 3 were recorded. From the top view images, polycrystalline layers were formed which is in accordance with the findings in XRD. No clear crystallite shapes corresponding to the wurtzite structure could be observed. Interestingly, a decreased NH3 flow rate (Fig. 3b and c) during the deposition results in more flake like features.

Optical properties of the thin films deposited on FTO-glass at 600 Â°C were analysed by UV-Vis spectroscopy and assuming the direct character of the optical transition.34 The fundamental bandgap energy estimated from the Tauc plot was 3.4 eV for the electrode deposited at 30 sccm NH3 and therefore containing the lowest amount of Sb (0.4 at%, I) as well as the highest oxide contribution. Films deposited at higher NH3 flow rates and therefore higher Sb content of 0.6 and 0.7 at% (II & III) showed a red shift of the fundamental bandgap energy by about 0.2 eV (see Fig. 4 and Table 2). Notably, all layers reveal a significant absorption shoulder with a Tauc gap at ca. 2.8 eV. This shoulder might be due to a GaON contribution originating from oxygen impurities.35 Accordingly, films deposited at higher NH3 flow rates (II, III) appear yellow to the naked eye, while the one deposited at 30 sccm NH3 (I) is white. Given the comparable thicknesses of the films (Fig. 2a), this effect could be explained by the weak light absorption properties for the latter film as confirmed by relatively low absorption coefficient compared to the thin yellow films (Fig. S2â€ ). Interestingly, the IPCE and corresponding photocurrents recorded in the presence of sodium sulphite as a sacrificial electron donor are the highest for the white-appearing photoanode I for both the electrolyte (front side, FS) and the substrate (back side, BS) sides of irradiation over the UV and Vis range (Fig. 5 and Fig. S3â€ ). FS-IPCE for all three electrodes are higher than BS-IPCE values suggesting that the hole transport limits the performance of the compact films. Notably, at the whitish photoanode I (30 sccm NH3) the difference between the IPCE values recorded under the BS and FS is not that large compared to the two yellowish films deposited at higher NH3 flow rates (II, III). This suggests that the improved hole transport properties in film I are responsible for its overall superior PEC performance. Since the photocurrent onset potential corresponding roughly to the conduction band edge is comparable for all three films (approx. 0.7 V vs. RHE, Fig. S4â€ ), we conclude that the photocurrent response is negatively influenced by modification of the valence band and the hole transport properties due to the substitution of N by Sb.

TRMC transients measured in a longer time window from ns to ms are shown in Fig. 7a for the same three films on quartz as described above. Again, assuming no recombination losses in the available THz time window (see Fig. 6a), the mobility has declined to about 10% of its initial maximum value at a delay time of 1.5 ns. Due to lower time resolution, the peak mobility of 1.2 Ã— 10â€“2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 in the microwave experiment is measured roughly 10 ns later with respect to the THz conductivity peak. This means that almost an order of magnitude of photoconductivity within the non-addressable time range between the two techniques is lost. Together with the higher DC mobility of âˆ¼0.5 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1, extrapolated from the THz mobility spectra (Fig. 6b) at early times when recombination is expected lowest, suggests that the mobility retrieved by TRMC is even much lower according to formula (1). The TRTS and TRMC transients thus suggest multiple trapping/recombination processes occurring on all different time scales with a decrease of mobility and carrier recombination loss in parallel. However, at the current stage it is not possible to discern between the two decay mechanisms. As mentioned above, the film with the lowest doping level of Sb exhibits the highest IPCE values but not the highest mobility measured by TRTS and TRMC. The discrepancy may be possibly attributed to differences in bulk (i.e., grain boundaries) and reduced surface carrier lifetime. This suggests that IPCE could potentially be improved with future effective surface modification to reduce recombination combined with enhanced absorption properties and mobility of GaSbxN1âˆ’x films with higher Sb content.

Conclusion

A new MOCVD approach for the deposition of GaSbxN1âˆ’x thin films in the dilute antimony regime is reported. The deposited layers are crystalline and exhibit a rough surface morphology as revealed by XRD and SEM respectively. Under optimized process conditions, the antimony content was found to be in a range of 0.2 to 0.7 at% for thin films with a thickness of âˆ¼200 nm after a deposition time of 30 min, exhibiting reasonable carbon impurities as determined via RBS/NRA. Rather poor quantum efficiencies even in the presence of the sacrificial electron donor and low charge carrier mobilities are attributed to the suppressed hole transport through the films, which therefore could in future be addressed by nanostructuring of the films or by further reducing the defect density e.g., by epitaxial growth. In order to enable efficient water oxidation to dioxygen, further optimization steps should also include the deposition of proper cocatalysts.41â€“44 The film with the lowest Sb incorporation resulted in the highest IPCE, while charge carrier mobility measurements indicate the opposite trend. Whether these poor functional properties are an effect of the Sb doping or originate from oxygen impurities will be the subject of future studies.

Experimental

Precursor synthesis and characterization

[Ga(Me)(dpamd)2] (1) is a known compound and was firstly used as precursor for the deposition of gallium oxide via MOCVD.29 It was synthesized following the literature synthesis steps. TPSb (2) is commercially available and was used as received from Sigma Aldrich.

TG analysis was conducted on a Netzsch STA409 PC/PG at ambient pressure situated in an Ar filled glovebox (sample size 15 mg). A heating ramp of 5 Â°C minâˆ’1 and nitrogen (AirLiquide, 99.998%) flow rate of 90 sccm was employed. Simultaneous DSC allowed the determination of the melting points. The 1 Torr vapor pressure temperatures and molar evaporation rates were deduced from stepped isothermal measurements. Approximately 50 mg of the precursor was heated over a ramp of 10 K minâˆ’1 to the individual step with a step size of 10 K. Each step was held for 15 min. Calculation of the aforementioned variables was conducted according to literature.33

Thin film deposition

Film depositions via CVD were performed in a custom-built horizontal cold-wall reactor with a base pressure of the order of 4 Ã— 10â€“5 mbar.45 Prior to film depositions, the 1 cm2 Si(100) substrates (Microchemicals GmbH) or FTO coated 1.5 Ã— 2.5 cm2 Pilkington TEC Glass (Xop glass, sheet resistance 12â€“14 Î© â–¡âˆ’1) were cleaned using a sequence of sonication in 2â€“propanol (HPLC grade) and water (HPLC grade) to remove surface contaminations. The evaporation temperatures were set to 120 and 90 Â°C for the Sb and Ga precursor reservoirs respectively. Nitrogen (99.999%) was used as carrier gas, and all depositions were performed in the presence of dry ammonia as co-reactant (50 sccm). GaN and alloyed GaSbxN1âˆ’x layers were grown in a temperature range from 500 to 800 Â°C with a carrier gas flow rate of 100 sccm, transporting the precursors simultaneously (for GaN thin films, no Sb precursor was employed) into the reaction chamber.
