Abstract

Low production cost and ever-increasing efficiencies make perovskite solar cells a flourishing area of research. The high efficiency originates from not only the unique optoelectronic properties of perovskites, but also various device optimization strategies, such as interfacial engineering, defect engineering, and componential engineering. Behind these strategies stand the fundamental intermolecular interactions and bondings, such as hydrogen bonding, halide bonding, ionic bonding, Lewis acidâ€“base interactions, and van der Waals interactions. In this review, we focus on different types of interactions and summarize the progresses made in perovskite solar cells. Additionally, perspectives on further efforts in improving device performance and stability are presented.

1. Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites (OIHPs) have received tremendous attention owing to their multitudinous applications, including those in field-effect transistors,1 light-emitting diodes,2 solar cells,3 photodetectors,4 and lasers.5 In particular, OIHP-based solar cells, which convert light energy into electrical energy, can achieve power conversion efficiency (PCE) from the initially reported 3.8% to the latest certified 25.2% in their expansive development over the past decade,6,7 which makes them comparable to conventional silicon solar cells.8 This formidable accomplishment largely depends on the excellent intrinsic properties of OIHPs, including large absorption coefficient,9 high carrier mobility,10 long carrier diffusion length,11 and direct bandgap.12

Perovskite materials generally assume the chemical formula ABX3, where the monovalent A cation coordinates with the nearest 12 Xâˆ’ anions and the divalent B cation coordinates with the nearest 6 Xâˆ’ anions, taking shapes of corner-connected BX6 octahedra with interstitial A cations (Fig. 1a and b).13 The formability and stability of perovskite structures are usually estimated by the Goldschmidt's tolerance factor t, where RA, RB, and RX denote the corresponding ionic radii of the A, B, and X sites.14 The ideal perovskite cubic structure requires t to be in the range from 0.813 to 1.107 and ideally close to 1.19 Therefore, predicting the radii of multiple ions is critical to determine the proper tolerance factor. Note that t is not the only prediction criteria. Octahedral factor Î¼ (Î¼ = RB/RX) is another parameter for predicting perovskite formability,15 which should be between 0.442 and 0.895.14 Until now, in widely studied perovskites, the A site is often occupied by CH3NH3+ (MA+)/HC(NH2)2+ (FA+)/Cs+, the B site is usually occupied by Pb2+/Sn2+, and the X site is commonly occupied by Iâˆ’/Brâˆ’/Clâˆ’.

The property of a perovskite depends not only on its constituent atoms, but also on how these atoms are bound to each other. Taking a typical perovskite MAPbI3 as the example, outstanding photovoltaic properties result from the portfolio of direct bandgap pâ€“p transitions afforded by the s orbitals of Pb lone-pair electrons and perovskite symmetry, powerful antibonding coupling between Pb lone-pair s and I p orbitals, high ionicity, and so on.16 It is well known that MAPbI3 is essentially an ionic material.17 Wang et al. employed the optB86b + vdWDF XC functional calculation, which revealed that the main interactions between organic groups and inorganic framework are the ionic bonds (IBs) between the MA+ cations and Iâˆ’ anions for orthorhombic perovskite MAPbI3.18 Besides, there is a combined covalent bond (CB) and IB characteristic between Pb and I atoms. Agiorgousis et al. reported that Pb2+ cations and Iâˆ’ anions in MAPbI3 exhibit strong covalency due to the formation of Pb dimers and I trimers through CB in intrinsic defects, including I vacancy and Pb-on-MA antisite, Pb interstitial, and I-on-MA antisite.19 Owing to the existence of dipole moments for both PbI3âˆ’ (2.89 Debye (D)) and MA+ (2.29 D), dipoleâ€“dipole electrostatic interactions have also been demonstrated to remarkably contribute toward the binding energy.20 Dispersion forces are widely accepted to be the universal glue among organic matters. Egger et al. took advantage of calculation to indicate that dispersive interactions strongly affect the structure of MAPbI3 (pseudo-cubic phase), exhibiting significant volume contraction (âˆ¼18 Ã…3).21 In addition, the pertinent dispersive interaction contribution originates from the direct van der Waals (vdW) interactions between the halides, as shown in Fig. 1c.21 The energy of each atomic pair is around 0.1 eV, which is within the limits of typical vdW energies. Fig. 1c also shows that the hydrogen bond (HB) between H and I, which is âˆ¼0.25 eV (dispersive energy) per unit cell, plays an important role in the stability of the MAPbI3 structure.21 Motta and co-workers exploited the vdW-corrected density functional theory (DFT) calculations to find that the HB interactions between N in MA+ and Iâˆ’ in inorganic [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedra can contribute toward their MAPbI3 (pseudo-cubic phase) structural cohesion.22 The orientation of MA+ in MAPbI3, driven by the dispersive forces between MA+ and inorganic [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedra, has a robust impact on the bandgap, as shown in Fig. 1d. MAPbI3 films with different orientations of MA+ exhibit different absorption spectra. Additionally, Varadwaj et al. indicated the existence of Nâ€“Hâ‹¯I and Câ€“Hâ‹¯I HB interactions in MAPbI3.20 Based on the abovementioned data, although views may vary, the overall property of MAPbI3 structure should be attributed to the synergistic effect among various interactions.

In order to achieve high light utilization and effective carrier transport, high-quality perovskite films are required. However, the unavoidable defects generated in the bulk, at the surface, and at the grain boundaries (GBs) due to the polycrystalline nature and solution processability can induce nonradiative electronâ€“hole recombinations, leading to energy loss as phonons instead of photons and reduced photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY).23 Besides, the interfacial contact between the adjacent layers has a direct influence on carrier extraction and recombination, and therefore, it affects the device performance.24 It is worthwhile that huge progress has been made to ameliorate the device performance by different engineering strategies, such as componential,25 interfacial,26 and passivation engineering.27 Nevertheless, the implementation of these engineering was achieved mainly through interactions between the perovskites/charge transport layers and different molecules/ions, which primarily include (but not limited to) HB, CB, coordination bonding, halogen bonding (XB), Ï€ effects, IB, and vdW forces.

Intermolecular interactions have been extensively employed to ameliorate device performances. Varieties of interplays can facilitate the study of the fundamental physics of perovskites and provide powerful tools to examine the structureâ€“property relationship in the device. Additionally, in turn, it can further guide the design and synthesis of target-directed molecules to participate in improving device performances. Therefore, in terms of scientific significance as well as technical importance, a review of this field has become imperative at this stage. To the best of our knowledge, there are no reviews that have discussed the promising achievements made through the viewpoint of chemical interactions. In this review, we will cover the introduction, including crystal structures and intrinsic interactions of perovskites, as well as focus on the various types of interactions in perovskite solar cells (PSCs). Finally, perspectives on further efforts in improving the device performance and stability will be presented. It is expected that based on this comprehensive summary and analysis, explicit information about numerous engineering interactions can guide increased focus toward the understanding of the fundamental chemistry and physics of perovskites and devices.

2.1.1 HB interactions in perovskites.
2.1.1.1 Phase transition/ion migration in MAPbI3. It is well known that MAPbI3 undergoes consecutive phase transitions, from cubic to tetragonal and then to the orthorhombic phase, with a decrease in temperature.37 The cubic Pmm phase of MAPbI3 appears above 327 K, below which it turns into tetragonal I4/mcm and then into orthorhombic Pnma at 165 K.38 HB interactions participate in phase transitions by changing the lengths and bond angles. For instance, the phase transition of MAPbI3 from Pnma to I4/mcm strongly involves HB between the MA+ cation and the surrounding [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedral cages. Ion migration in perovskites mainly includes the migrations of hydrogens, halides, and organic cations. Organic cation and halide migrations in perovskites have low activation energies (0.2â€“0.8 eV; 4.6â€“18.4 kcal molâˆ’1), which allow the ions to easily move through the defects and GBs.40,41 Organic cation migration, which is related to the dipole interactions and Ï€ interactions have not been discussed here. Hydrogen migration can be attributed to the verisimilar motor defects of H+, H0, and Hâˆ’.42 Therein, H+ has been deemed to migrate by tracing a transient HB concatenating two equatorial iodides. This process affords a low barrier, but it accompanies an evident rearrangement of the iodide, Pb, and MA+ ions close to H+. A proton enables the iodides to move closer to each other, leading to the bridging of the two iodide sites in MAPbI3. H0 and Hâˆ’ have little association with HBs. As the formation of I2 results in the decomposition of the perovskite materials, incorporating organic cations with strong HB capabilities is very beneficial for suppressing ion migration and therefore further improving the stability of perovskite materials.

2.2 CB

A CB, involving the sharing of electron pairs between the atoms, is always affected by the electronegativity of the corresponding atoms, and it refers to the polarity of the bond. A bond formed by mutual attractions holds the resultant atoms together. The stronger the mutual attraction, the larger is the bond energy. In general, the bond energy of a CB is larger than that of a HB. Therefore, the CB may play a more significant role in PSCs.

Proppe et al. introduced 4-vinylbenzylammonium bromide (VBABr) to construct 2D/3D heterojunction with (MAPbBr3)0.15(FAPbI3)0.85.89 VBA, on top of the perovskite layer, can get photoexcited by UV light to crosslink via the vinyl groups, resulting in a new CB comprising the Câ€“C and/or Câ€“N bonds. A schematic of this formation process is shown in Fig. 7a. Various characterization techniques (UV-vis, XRD, ATR-FTIR, and 1H NMR) have been exploited to reveal the existence of photochemical crosslinking among the VBA molecules. Together with the additional 2D layer, these new CBs can further stabilize the perovskite system, affording better stability while retaining 90% of their initial efficiency after 2300 h of dark aging as compared to the retention of 20% performance in the case of 3D reference films. In addition, the introduction of VBABr passivated the trap states of the upper surface of the perovskite layer, which facilitated the improvement in device performance.

2.3 Lewis acidâ€“base reactions

A Lewis acid is a substance containing an unoccupied orbital that has the capacity of accepting a lone pair of electrons from another substance, forming a stable Lewis acidâ€“base adduct. The substance that donates the lone pair of electrons to the Lewis acid to form a dative bond is called a Lewis base.92 With regard to OIHPs, taking MAPbI3 as an example, Pb2+ has been considered to be a Lewis acid as it contains 6 electrons in the outermost valence shell (4 from Pb2+ itself and 2 from the surrounding Iâˆ’ anions). To satisfy the octet rule, an incomplete octet tends to acquire a lone pair of electrons from a Lewis base to fill the empty orbital. Typical Lewis bases that contain functional groups bearing lone pairs of electrons, e.g., nitrogen (N) donor, oxygen (O) donor, and sulfur (S) donor, are capable of forming intermediate Lewis acidâ€“base adducts with Pb2+. DMF, a widely used solvent in dissolving perovskite precursors, contains an O-donor functional group, where the lone pair of electrons fills the empty orbital of Pb2+ and forms a coordinate bond. Solventâ€“solute interactions driven by the Lewis acidâ€“base reaction facilitate the dissolution of perovskite reactants. More importantly, the perovskite crystallization process can be further controlled by carefully coupling Pb2+ with Lewis bases that have different degrees of basicity.

Except for solventâ€“solute interactions, the Lewis acidâ€“base reaction has also been applied in defect passivation. Perovskites are defective due to their â€œsoftâ€ and ionic characteristics.93 The defects are typically located at the surfaces and GBs due to the disorganized orientations of the neighboring crystals at the surface/GBs, including intrinsic defects such as vacancies (VMA, VPb, and VI), interstitial (MAi, Pbi, and Ii), cation substitutions (MAPb and PbMA), antisite substitutions (MAI, PbI, IMA, and IPb), and extrinsic defects (such as Pb and I impurities) (Fig. 8a and b).94,95 Although the dominant defects (i.e., vacancies) in OIHPs are usually shallow-level defects and perovskites are tolerant to defect-induced losses, the existence of defects can still induce ion migration, which accelerates the degradation of PSCs.96â€“104 On the other hand, deep-level traps, such as antisite defects PbI and IPb and interstitial Pbi, play an important role in limiting the PCE of PSCs reaching the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit. These deep trap states can create undesirable energy states that are deeply localized in the bandgap101 and capture photon-excited electrons and holes in transition as they pass through the bands. The energy required for the electrons and holes to escape from these trap states is larger than the thermal energy kBT (where kB is the Boltzmann constant and T is the temperature), which converts the electron excitation energy into vibrational energy (heat) comprising nonradiative recombinations, surface recombinations, and Auger recombinations. This further reduces the efficiency of photon-to-electron conversion. Due to the Lewis acidâ€“base characterization of these defects, the corresponding Lewis acidâ€“base passivation agents can be adopted to react and reduce the defect density, improving the performance of PSCs. In this section, we discuss the implementation of the Lewis acidâ€“base concept in both modulating the perovskite crystallization process and passivating the intrinsic defects in perovskite thin films.

2.3.1 Lewis acidâ€“base adducts for fabricating high-quality PSC films. The performance of a PSC highly depends on the quality of the perovskite's light-absorbing layer. Except for the successes of vacuum deposition and chemical vapor deposition (CVD) methods,105â€“111 a solution process remains a more widely used and viable strategy for fabricating high-efficiency PSCs. 2.3.2 Passivation. Due to the ionic nature and solution processability, perovskite thin films unavoidably possess noteworthy levels of impurities and imperfections.93 When compared with the bulk, the GBs and surfaces are more defective and have severer recombinations.144 Although perovskites have superior defect tolerance benefiting from their inherently defect-tolerant electronic structure,145 nonradiative recombinations induced by the inevitably deep traps still limit the carrier lifetime and cause large open-circuit voltage (VOC) deficits, which act as barriers for PSCs to reach the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit. Moreover, the more pronounced ion migration at the GBs accelerated by defects speeds up the degradation process and reduces the stability of PSCs.146,147 Undercoordinated Pb2+/1+/0 and I1âˆ’/0 are two typical types of defects in perovskites, including mobile Iâˆ’ anions, I2 interstitials, undercoordinated MA+/FA+ and Pb2+/1+ cations, and Pb0 clusters. The undercoordinated ions induce a local excess of positive or negative charges, i.e., the loss of Iâˆ’ induces a positive charge on the Pb atom, while uncoordinated Iâˆ’ anions cause excess negative charges. These charged sites are the locations in which photoexcited electrons and holes are trapped, which lower the photon-to-electron conversion efficiency. The number of electroneutrally removed electrons/holes from these sites to the conduction band/valence band (VB) is usually high.95 Yang et al. reported an iodide management strategy by adding a small amount of I2 to avoid deficient Iâˆ’ defects.148 However, it is known that excess I2 molecules can cause irreversible degradation as I2 triggers the decomposition of the chemical chain by reacting with Iâˆ’ anions and MA+/FA+ organic cations, which regenerate more I2 under illumination.149,150 Passivation of defects by forming excess PbI2 on GBs has also been reported as a useful strategy.151 However, recent studies have shown that the unreacted PbI2 on perovskite surfaces/GBs can undergo photodecomposition under continuous light illumination, generating metallic Pb and I2 that are deleterious to device stability.152,153 As the undercoordinated Pb2+/1+/0 and I1âˆ’/0 defects are Lewis acids and bases, respectively, passivation by corresponding adverse Lewis bases and acids becomes an effective pathway to reduce defects and improve the performance of PSCs.
2.3.2.1 Lewis base passivation. Undercoordinated Pb2+ having incomplete octets in the outer orbitals are Lewis acids as they tend to accept electrons to satisfy the octet rule. These undercoordinated Pb2+ defects, therefore, can be passivated by Lewis bases that donate lone pairs of electrons. Noel et al. proposed a strategy of using the Lewis bases of thiophene (S donor) and pyridine (N donor) to neutralize the net positive charges residing on Pb atoms caused by the loss of Iâˆ’ (Fig. 9a).154 The passivation of these Coulombic trap states significantly reduced the nonradiative electronâ€“hole recombinations, resulting in an impressively long carrier lifetime of 2016.5 Â± 5.3 ns. Later, Liu et al. investigated the passivation mechanism of thiophene and pyridine by using nonadiabatic molecular dynamics combined with time-domain DFT.155 They found that pristine perovskites without any passivation afforded high electron densities at the surface, which could serve as charge recombination sites. Thiophene passivated these defect sites by delocalizing the charges away from the surface. On the other hand, pyridine localized the electrons on itself and the Pb atom by the stronger Nâ€“Pb bonding and the more electronegative nature of the nitrogen atom, leaving the passivated site more energetically near the conduction band edge (Fig. 9b). The passivated site was found more like a shallow state located closely to conduction band maximum, which allowed the passage of electrons. Poly(4-vinylpyridine) containing a pyridine side chain was reported by Chaudhary et al. to coordinate with the undercoordinated Pb2+ in perovskites, which reduced the trap states and nonradiative recombinations and significantly increased the steady-state PL intensity.156 Other amino-group-containing Lewis bases have also been used to passivate undercoordinated Pb2+ defects. Urea-modified nitrogen-doped reduced graphene oxide could passivate the surface defects and reduce nonradiative recombinations by coordination between the Lewis-base nitrogen groups of urea and FA+ cations in the perovskite precursor, which consequently enlarged the film grain size and contributed to improved VOC.157 Melaminium iodide has also been used by Kim et al. to passivate Pb2+ defects via its three primary amines.158 Such passivation reduced the Urbach tail energy and lowered the trap density from 1.02 Ã— 1016 to 0.645 Ã— 1016 cmâˆ’3, which indicated reduced nonradiative recombinations. More importantly, undercoordinated Sn2+ sites were more readily oxidized.130,162,163 Ethane-1,2-diamine (edamine) has been used to passivate undercoordinated Sn2+ ions by its amino groups when fabricating Sn-based perovskites.164 PSCs after passivation showed increased VOC values by up to 0.1 V, which was in agreement with the reduced defect density. Undercoordinated Iâˆ’ defects, e.g., Iâˆ’ interstitials and Pb vacancies, are negative trap states that trap photoexcited holes and may cause nonradiative recombinations.95 Further, PbI3âˆ’ antisites are deleterious defects that induce the formation of deep traps and higher density of recombination centers.181 These defects are Lewis bases that tend to donate lone pairs of electrons and therefore can be passivated by Lewis acids. 2,3,5,6-Tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ) possessing strong electron-accepting ability has been used as a Lewis acid to passivate undercoordinated Iâˆ’via coordination.182 The passivation at the interface between the HTL and perovskite can lead to reduced trap density and consequently improve the VOC value and reduce Jâ€“V hysteresis. XB plays an important role in the crystallization process of perovskite films. Bi et al. were the first to report the effects of XB on the crystallographic kinetics of perovskite grains.207 They introduced 1,4-diiodooctafluorobutane (C4F8I2) and nonfluorinated 1,4-diodobutane (C4H8I2) into the perovskite precursor solution to modulate perovskite crystallization (Fig. 10c). It should be noted that the C4F8I2-treated film showed more significant improvement, which resulted from the stronger XB strength. In addition, the trap density and carrier mobility were improved after the introduction of XB donors, which increased the charge transport property. Due to the stronger XB bond strength, the C4F8I2-treated perovskite film showed better performance and stability, which possibly originated from the compact and pinhole-free morphology (Fig. 10e).

Ruiz-Preciado et al. introduced the bifunctional modulator, 1,2,4,5-tetrafluoro-3,6-diiodobenzene (TFDIB), to induce (FA0.9MA0.1)0.95Cs0.05Pb1.03(I0.93Br0.1)3 perovskite film crystallization.208 TFDIB was expected to interact with hybrid perovskites via XB, passivating the perovskite film surface and crosslinking the adjacent grains. 2D solid-state 19F NMR spectroscopy together with DFT calculations showed atomic-level bifunctional molecular crosslinking in perovskites via XB. Fig. 10f shows the interaction modes of TFDIB with the perovskite. Interfaces within the PSCs affect charge extraction and transfer, playing vital roles in device performance. In this respect, interfacial engineering, such as introducing a modifier layer at the interface, has been extensively studied.16 The introduction of a modifier is expected to improve the charge transfer, reduce charge recombinations, ameliorate the crystal and electrical structures, and modify the contact of the adjacent layers, leading to excellent device performance and stability. Zhang et al. computationally investigated the effect of an organic modifier (bromoacetate, BrCH2COOâˆ’),209 which was experimentally verified to be an effective modifier to improve the device performance, with a perovskite structure of (MA4PbI6)/bromoacetate/TiO2. Along with the â€“COOH group in the bromoacetate molecule, the XB occurring between the Br atom in bromoacetate and I atom in halide perovskite contributed toward the decent binding and coordination between the perovskite and TiO2, effective electron transfer, and minimizing the heterojunction lattice mismatch.

Until now, there are only a few reports about the application of XB in PSCs. However, it plays a vital role in the improvement of device performance. XB interactions can improve the crystallization of grains, enhance charge transfer, reduce charge recombinations, and therefore, ameliorate the device performance. Numerous molecules have been intentionally designed and utilized via XB interactions in PSCs.

2.6 IB

The energy of an IB is almost the same as that of a CB, but it is higher than that of a HB. The strong interactions between the various molecules with perovskites via IB are expected to improve the device performance and stability. According to the sources of as-exploited molecules or ions, we discussed the two directions in this section, including inorganic molecules or ions and organic molecules.
2.6.1 Inorganic molecules or ions. Inorganic molecules/ions have been proven to be effective factors to interact with ions and/or charged defects in perovskites via IB. Na+ ions have been the first reported metal ions that exhibited passivation effects on perovskite grains and surfaces. Bi et al. showed that Na+ within the conducting ITO substrate could diffuse into the perovskite grains over time234 and then interact with negatively charged halide ions in the perovskite to form an IB or be absorbed at the MA+ vacancies. This interaction can lead to reduced trap density and prolonged lifetime of perovskite films. Abdi-Jalebi et al. were the first to introduce K+ (KI) into a (Cs,MA,FA)Pb(I0.85Br0.15)3 system to reduce nonradiative losses and photoinduced ion migration in perovskite films.235 After the introduction of KI, the external photoluminescence quantum efficiency (PLQE) increased from 8% to 66%, and the corresponding internal PLQE exceeded 95%. This improvement resulted from the passivation of K+ to Iâˆ’ and Brâˆ’ vacancies in the perovskite through ionic interactions, as shown in Fig. 14a. This ionic interaction mode has also been supported by another report, which showed that KPbI3 and KX (X = I, Br, or Cl) could exist after KI doping in perovskites due to ionic interactions, as verified by 39K solid-state NMR analyses.236 Cao et al. introduced a series of alkali cations, such as Cs+, Rb+, K+, Na+, and Li+, into perovskites to occupy the interstitial vacancies.237 This interstitial occupancy depended on the size of ions revealed by DFT calculations, and K+ doping afforded the best results. They claimed that alkali cation doping could suppress ion migration, affording reduced hysteresis, weakened poling effects, and improved photostability of perovskite films. Son et al. pointed out that the notorious hysteresis of PSCs originated from the iodide Frenkel defects,238 which occur near the cathode because of electron accumulation. KI doping in perovskites can reduce such electron accumulation and/or prevent Iâˆ’ ion migration. Besides, K+ ions are energetically preferred to occupy the interstitial sites. KI doping, therefore, can reduce the trap density of perovskite films and hysteresis of devices. Alkali cations (such as K+) doping can surely lead to an improvement in the trap density of perovskite films. However, the reported metal ion position in perovskite films is not unified. Therefore, more efforts are needed to focus on researching the mechanism involving the effect of metal ions on the perovskite. Recently, Wu et al. introduced CdI2 to modify the perovskite film. 2.7 vdW interactions

The vdW interactions, originating from a transient shift in the electron density, is a distance-dependent force between the molecules or atoms. It plays a fundamental role in various fields, such as supramolecular chemistry, surface science, polymer science, nanotechnology, and so on. Although the vdW force is only 0.4 to 4.0 kJ molâˆ’1, the interaction among the different molecules becomes very sturdy, e.g., vdW heterojunctions. The vdW interactions are widespread in perovskites so that they are always considered in various calculation modes. Actually, vdW interactions have been extensively explored to improve PSC performances.

It has been reported that 2D/3D (PEA2PbI4/MAPbI3) perovskites fabricated from phenylethyl ammonium iodide (PEAI) could improve device stability.249 Due to the strong vdW interactions between the organic molecules (PEAI), higher energy (0.36 eV) is needed for removing PEAI than MAI from the perovskite. According to DFT simulations, after the introduction of PEAI into a perovskite system, the decomposition rate of the perovskite film reduced by around 1000 fold. Aharon et al. introduced different lengths of alkylammoniums to modify perovskite nanoparticles (NPs).250 Both computational and experimental results revealed that vdW interactions within different alkylammoniums were the main reason for the differences in the physical and optical properties of perovskite NPs. Besides, 2D perovskites were fabricated from different alkylammoniums and MAPbX3 with the formula of (CmH2m+1NH3)2(MA)nâˆ’1PbnX3n+1 (m = 8, 12, or 18; n = 1, 2, or 3; X: = Br or I). They claimed that the crystalline structures and optical properties of these 2D perovskites considerably depended on the vdW interactions among the different alkylammoniums. 3. Summary and outlook

OIHPs have shown promising potential for next-generation solar cells due to their low-cost materials, facile fabrication techniques, and constantly increasing PCEs. However, the existence of deep state defects considerably limits the photon-to-electron conversion efficiency and device stability. To overcome this issue, increasingly precise fabrication processes for absorber layers, which lower the defect density and use of passivation agents that can passivate inevitable defects, are needed.

In this paper, we have provided a general review of the bonding and intermolecular interactions between the intrinsic and extrinsic molecules and ions in perovskites. The IBs and HBs between the CH3+ cations and halide anions, CBs between Pb and I, and vdW interactions between halides play important roles in the arrangement and formation of ABX3 perovskite structures. Additionally, when preparing perovskite films, solvents and molecules having interactions with the perovskite precursors can help in regulating the perovskite crystallization process, resulting in high-quality films with low defect densities. In addition, the bonding and interactions enable the passivation of defects in perovskite films by passivation agents, which contribute toward reduced nonradiative recombinations and improved device performance. Moreover, as the library of effective molecules for both crystallization regulation and passivation is still limited, this review provides inspiration on how to purposefully seek for molecules with desired functionalities. However, although impressive device performance has already been achieved, stability issues caused by ion migration and defect sites, which relate to bonding and intermolecular interactions, still need to be investigated further. By giving this comprehensive review of bonding and interactions in perovskites, we hope to arouse more in-depth discussions on the degradation mechanism from an essential intermolecular interaction aspect.

The following perspectives on future research directions will be of importance for further ameliorating device performance and stability.

(1) It is said that an ounce of prevention is a pound of cure. A perovskite film with low defect density is always desirable for solar cell applications. A list of solvents and additives used to make high-quality and large perovskite grains and their working mechanisms of intermolecular interactions have been reviewed in this article. However, a more comprehensive study of the potential additives is still necessitated to further optimize the quality of perovskite films, particularly when making thick films for effective light absorption.

(2) Although theoretical calculations have shown the formation energies of different defects in PSCs, the underlying mechanism of how deep defect states generate and what types of interactions they have with their surroundings remain to be fully understood. A better understanding can help the selection of more suitable strategies for perovskite passivation.

(3) Additionally, benefiting from various interactions in the perovskites, molecules utilized to improve perovskites and device stability can be specifically designed, such as specific groups through HBs to bond with volatile methylamine ions and XBs to fix halogens.

(4) Ion migration usually causes phase segregation, interfacial interaction, and accelerated decomposition. To suppress ion migration in perovskites is extremely important for device stability. A more comprehensive study of how to avoid and suppress ion migration is highly needed in the future.

(5) Tandem solar cells show better prospects toward higher PCEs due to the ability of integrating wider solar spectrum into multiple bands that can be more efficiently absorbed by separate devices. Integrating PSCs and c-Si/CIGS solar cells into a tandem device is regarded as an effective tactic to overcome the Shockleyâ€“Queisser limit. In this respect, except for each subcell, more attention should be paid toward the interlayers that may cause connection problems. A more detailed study on the bonding and interactions at each interlayer can contribute toward achieving enhanced carrier transport and reduced recombinations.
