Abstract

A rechargeable lithium/iodine battery using commercial organic electrolyte, composed of iodineâ€“conductive carbon black composite as cathode and metallic lithium as anode, is first proposed in this work. The fabricated lithium/iodine battery presents superior high-rate capability and good reversibility based on the contributions from both the capacitive characteristics of conductive carbon black, and the redox capacity of active iodine in the composite.


Broader context

Traditional electrochemical batteries are facing the huge challenges for high power density to match the requirement of the electric tools, and hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs). Compared to traditional secondary batteries, the ultra battery, which combines the supercapacitor and battery in a single unit cell, has the advantage of adjustable power density and energy density. Meanwhile, active materials in supercapacitors and batteries are usually made of insoluble solid compounds, elements or alloys in electrolytes. It is difficult to use soluble active materials in electrolytes for rechargeable energy conversion and storage. In this work, the soluble active iodine and LiI are fixed on porous carbon materials based on strong adsorption of micropores or mesopores. Correspondingly, the hybrid battery system is fabricated by the combination of both contributions of the capacitive characteristic of porous carbon substrate and redox capacity of active iodine. In the fabricated lithium/iodine secondary battery, high power density and energy density can be realized with good reversibility. This strategy show possible approaches for future batteries with soluble active materials, not limited by heavy metals, metallic compounds, or oxides.

As a key technology for hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs), high power rechargeable batteries have attracted increasing attention because of the modern mobile life and growing worldwide crisis in energy.1 Traditional rechargeable batteries are based on a redox couple of a chemical element and compounds in aqueous and nonaqueous electrolytes.2 To achieve high power performance, these batteries can be usually updated with nanostructured electrode materials, as well as new engineering constructions based on fast kinetic reaction and good electrical conductivity.3â€“5 The ultra battery, which combines an asymmetric supercapacitor and a leadâ€“acid battery in single unit cells, has been recently developed to address the high power requirement for HEV application.6,7 Actually, this concept of the ultra battery can be expanded to many rechargeable battery systems.

The primary lithium/iodine battery, with high reliability and low self-discharge rate, is an important power source for implantable cardiac pacemaker applications.8,9 The low rate capability of the lithium/iodine battery is related to the large internal impedance due to the gradual formation of a lithium iodide (LiI) layer with a poor lithium ion conductivity during discharging. Recently, an all solid-state rechargeable lithium/iodine battery is presented, which shows a realizable rechargeability at a low current density (about 20 Î¼A cmâˆ’2).10 It is well known that the iodine/triiodide redox pair has a rapid electrochemical reaction with good reversibility as a charge carrier in dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) and supercapacitors.11â€“13 Therefore, a rechargeable lithium/iodine battery of reliable rate capability may be fabricated in liquid electrolyte with a fast ion diffusion. Moreover, active iodine can be readily loaded into the pores of porous carbon, similar to sulfur-porous carbon composites.2,14â€“17 Correspondingly, the electrochemical reaction is constrained inside the pores of porous carbon due to strong adsorption, ensuring good cycle stability, reversibility, and utilization by decreasing the solubility of active iodine and lithium iodide in the organic electrolyte. In particular, the capacitive characteristic of porous carbon and redox capacity of active iodine would be integrated to provide high energy and power performance for the rechargeable lithium/iodine battery.

In summary, we fabricate the iodine-conductive carbon black (CCB) composite by integrating the capacitive characteristics of the CCB substrate and the redox capacity contribution of active iodine. The fabricated prototypical battery exhibits a high discharge voltage plateau, excellent cycle performance, and high rate capability. The electrochemical reaction process of active iodine or LiI can be constrained inside the pores of CCB substrate by strong absorption, leading to good cycle stability. The superior high rate capability is attributed to the combination on both contributions of the capacitive characteristic of CCB substrate and redox capacity of active iodine in the composite. The average specific energy density and power density of the composite reach to 91.4 Wh kgâˆ’1 and 11.9 kW kgâˆ’1 with an average discharge voltage of 2.9 V, based on the cathode mass. Compared to supercapacitors, the lithium/iodine battery has an approximate power density, but a larger energy density.2,23â€“25 The fixation of active iodine on CCB substrate is the key to the success of the rechargeable lithium/iodine battery using the traditional organic electrolyte. Further exploration, focusing on the composite cathode with high loading of active iodine in microporous carbon, is necessary to meet the practical application of HEVs in the future.
