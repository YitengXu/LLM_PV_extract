Abstract

FAPbI3 perovskite is an ideal candidate for efficient and stable perovskite solar cells (PSCs). Unfortunately, attaining photoactive Î±-phase FAPbI3 under humid conditions is rather difficult due to the structural and moisture instability of Î±-phase FAPbI3. To obtain high quality Î±-phase FAPbI3 perovskite in ambient air, a strategy of incorporating methylamine thiocyanate (MASCN) as a stabilizer is introduced in this study. Due to its close effective ionic radius to Iâ, SCNâ is successfully incorporated into the lattice. The strong bond between SCNâ and Pb2+ and the strong hydrogen bonds between the N atom in SCNâ and FA+ lead to a high quality and stable Î±-phase FA-based perovskite at ambient 50 Â± 5% RH even at room temperature. With this high quality film, air-processed PSCs with a maximum power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 19.06% can be realized in a pâiân device. Moreover, it is also demonstrated that the stronger chemical bonding interaction between SCNâ and Pb2+ as well as the stronger hydrogen bonds between SCNâ and organic groups also improve the stability of PSCs. More than 90% of the initial efficiency is retained after 2000 hours of operation with maximum power point tracking under full sunlight illumination without an ultraviolet filter.

Introduction

Metallic halide perovskites with a general formula ABX3, where A = CH3NH3+ (MA+), HC(NH2)2+ (FA+), and Cs, B = Pb2+, and Sn2+, and X = Iâ, Brâ, and Clâ, have attracted a considerable amount of attention due to their high photovoltaic performance and the tremendous advance in stability. Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) with high efficiency are usually fabricated in a glovebox under inert conditions or a low-humidity atmosphere (relative humidity < 30%) to avoid the adverse impact of humidity in the film formation process.1,2 Such an environment can only be achieved by using a sophisticated air/humidity-controlling system and inevitably increases the capital costs of setting up the fabrication facilities, which is not desired for mass production. In order to promote the commercialization of PSCs, air-processed perovskites that allow perovskite fabrication under ambient conditions without the strict control of environmental humidity have attracted considerable attention because of its compatibility with state-of-the-art large-scale fabrication such as slot-die coating,3 doctor blading,4 spray coating,5etc. However, the device performance of PSCs prepared in air is still inferior to that prepared in a glove box,1 which is due to the adverse effects of moisture on the crystallization process of the perovskite films. For example, water molecules can bind with PbI2 to form lead complexes and lead to uncontrolled growth of lager 1D intermediates,6 which leads to incomplete coverage films. The hygroscopic perovskite precursor solution can adsorb moisture in a humid environment, which prolonged the evaporation of solvents and delayed the supersaturation of the solution. The reduced supersaturation induced low nucleation density and a large critical nucleus radius, resulting in an isolated perovskite crystal.7 In our previous work,8 we adopted an anti-solvent bathing method to solve the problem of incomplete coverage films in air. At the same time, it was found that the moisture in air affected not only the morphology of the film, but also the composition. The Cl ion preferred to remain in the perovskite film when fabricated in ambient air compared to that in a glovebox.

At present, a MA-based perovskite is widely used for the air-processed perovskite under high humidity (RH > 40%),6,8â12 where MA+ is dominant in the A site. Nevertheless, the MA-based perovskite is reported to be unstable against light and heat due to its low formation energy.13,14 Replacing the MA+ by FA+ to form the FAPbI3 perovskite achieved better photo-thermal stability15 and a lower bandgap that is closer to the optimum bandgap for a single junction solar cell derived from the ShockleyâQueisser limit.16,17 Thus, FAPbI3 perovskite could be an ideal candidate for efficient and stable PSCs. Unfortunately, attaining photoactive Î±-phase FAPbI3 under humid conditions is rather difficult due to the structural and moisture instability of Î±-phase FAPbI3.18,19 The preparation of Î±-phase FAPbI3 perovskite in air with high RH is rarely reported. Recently, Wang et al.20 succeeded in fabricating PSCs by using Î±-phase FAPbI3 as the light absorber under an elevated RH level of 40% and achieved a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 17.29%. They used N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) as an additive into the FAPbI3 precursor and they found that NMP assists in reducing the crystallization rate by forming an FAIâPbI2âNMP intermediate. In the annealing stage, NMP is rapidly detached from the intermediate phase, which leads to the conversion from an intermediate to Î±-phase FAPbI3 while avoiding the conversion route from an indirect Î´-phase to the Î±-phase, which could leave Î´-phase residues in the film. Alternatively, to fabricate Î±-phase FAPbI3 perovskite at a high humidity level with a simpler procedure, the photoactive Î±-phase FAPbI3 perovskite at room temperature must be stabilized. This objective is realizable by partially substituting FA+ with MA+ or Cs+ (ref. 21 and 22) to create a double- or triple-cation perovskite and alternatively substituting Iâ with Brâ or Clâ to create a double anion perovskite.23 A preliminary study by Miyasaka et al.23 confirmed that PSCs utilizing a double-cation/double-anion perovskite {(FAPbI3)0.85(MAPbBr3)0.15} as the light absorber can still provide a reasonably high PCE that reaches 15.7% when fabricated at 40% RH. Cs+ incorporation can also stabilize the Î±-phase FAPbI3 at high humidity, which was pioneered by Xu et al.24 The PSCs fabricated at 70% RH showed a PCE of 15.56% with improved thermal stability and moisture resistance. This performance was achieved by Cs0.15FA0.85PbI3 perovskite obtained through two-step spin-coating in which hot FAI solution was added dropwise onto a preheated PbI2âCsI0.15âFAI0.3 complex substrate. Considering the hygroscopicity of FAI, preheating the solution and substrate can remove moisture ingress from the growing perovskite layer during the spin-coating process.

Hydrogen bonding plays an important role in stabilizing Î±-phase FA-based perovskites.18 Enhancing hydrogen bonding between organic ammonium cations and [PbI6]4â octahedra has been shown to be an effective strategy to stabilize Î±-phase FA-based perovskite.21 For example, partially replacing the FA+ with the stronger dipole moment MA+ can enhance the hydrogen bonding between the organic ammonium cation and [PbI6]4â octahedron, thus stabilizing the 3D arrangement of the [PbI6]4â octahedron with a pseudo-cubic symmetry.21 Water in air is prone to bond with the Pb atom,6,25 which weakens the hydrogen bonding between the organic ammonium cation and [PbI6]4â octahedron, resulting in the formation of an Î´-phase in a humid environment.24,26,27 Therefore, reducing the bonding of water molecules with lead and enhancing the hydrogen bonding between organic ammonium cations and octahedrons are the key to obtaining a high quality Î±-phase FA-based perovskite in air.

In this work, methylamine thiocyanate (MASCN) is introduced to stabilize Î±-phase FA-based perovskite in ambient air. SCNâ has an effective ionic radii of about 217 pm, which is very close to that of the iodine's effective ionic radii of 220 pm. Therefore, SCNâ is expected to substitute a fraction of iodine atoms in the perovskite lattice. Comparing the spherical shape of Iâ with the linear shape of SCNâ from their Lewis structures, the lone pairs of electrons from S and N atoms in SCNâ can interact strongly with Pb2+ and N atoms can form strong hydrogen bonds with FA+, which will stabilize the frame structure of the perovskite even under a moist environment.28â31 Under optimized conditions, the PSCs with a planar pâiân structure obtain a maximum PCE of 19.06% and show superior stability. Under full sunlight illumination without an ultraviolet filter, the corresponding devices maintain over 90% of their initial efficiency after 2000 hours of operation.

Results and discussion

We prepared the perovskite films using a modified two-step method similar to our previous work8 in ambient air at 50 Â± 5% RH. Firstly, PbI2 dissolved in DMF and DMSO mixed solvent was spin on a substrate for 4 s to get a transparent intermediate phase film. This film was dipped into IPA anti-solvent to product a smooth and fully covered PbI2 film. Secondly, FAI with different stabilizers dissolved in IPA solvent was spin coated on the prepared PbI2 film to form a FA-based perovskite film. Here, we used MA+ as the cation of stabilizers because of its larger dipole moment and stronger interaction with [PbI6]4â octahedrons, which can stabilize the 3D arrangement of Î±-phase FAPbI3.21 We adjusted the type and content of anions to further enhance the interaction between the organic ammonium cation and octahedrons so that Î±-phase FAPbI3 can be stabilized under ambient conditions. The mole ratio of MA+ and FA+ is maintained at 24:76 and Iâ will be partially substituted by SCNâ. For the control sample, MA+ is originated from MAI (regarded as 0 MASCN). For the target sample, 50 mol% MAI is replaced by MASCN (regarded as 50 MASCN). It has been demonstrated that the edges of the band-gap are mainly occupied by inorganic matrices rather than organic cations, where the organic cations act to maintain charge neutrality32 and the band gap closely depends on the angle of inorganic metal-halide bonds.33 However, the size of organic cations plays an important role in the expansion or contraction of the whole lattice arising from the substitution doping of large or small organic cations. Replacing the large size FA+ cation with the small size MA+ cation will increase the band gap (1.48 eV for FAPbI3 and 1.57 eV for MAPbI3).34 Then, the bandgap of the alloyed MA+ and FA+ in the FAxMA1âxPbI3 perovskite should be between 1.48 eV and 1.57 eV. Based on Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations, Chang et al.35 found that the more FA+ doping into the lattice is, the narrower the bandgap is. Thus, the steady-state photoluminescence (PL) emission of the film with more FA+ will red shift, otherwise it will blue shift.35,36 However, there is one case that does not conform to this rule, that is, x = 0.5 and the direction of FA+ doping is along with the crystal plane [110]. In this case, the perovskite bandgap is even larger than that of pure MAPbI3 perovskite.35 Based on the above analysis, we recorded the PL emission spectra of an unannealed perovskite film to evaluate the degree of FA+ incorporation into the lattice. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the PL emission peak of the 0 MASCN sample is located at 788 nm, which is very close to the emission of pure MAPbI3 (775â780 nm),37,38 indicating that the organic cations that participated in the formation of the perovskite are mainly MA+. FA+ is not well incorporated into the lattice. According to the 2theta of 11.2Â° from X-ray diffraction (XRD) results in Fig. 1(b) that corresponds to Î´-phase FAPbI3, FA+ is involved in the formation of the Î´-phase rather than the Î±-phase. To further prove that the formation of the Î´-phase is mainly ascribed to the influence of water molecules in ambient air, we eliminated this influence by completing the whole preparation process in a glovebox with a water amount less than 0.1 ppm. The corresponding film shows PL emission at 796 nm, as shown in Fig. S1.â  Meanwhile, the XRD pattern in Fig. S2â  shows the disappearance of the Î´-phase, indicating that more FA+ is incorporated into the lattice and water molecules are the main cause of the Î´-phase in the mixed MA+ and FA+ perovskite. Surprisingly, when 50 mol% MAI is replaced by MASCN, the perovskite film prepared in the same air environment (50 Â± 5% RH) shows the emission peak of 819 nm, as shown in Fig. 1(a), indicating that a large amount of FA+ is involved in the formation of the 3D perovskite. The XRD in Fig. 1(b) also shows the disappearance of the Î´-phase, and the significant increase of (110), which corresponds to a 2theta value of about 13.9Â°, further demonstrating that MASCN has successfully stabilized the Î±-phase FA-based perovskite under ambient conditions at room temperature.2,39 The entire process of perovskite formation is summarized in Fig. 1(c). The precursor which contains MAI and FAI would separate into MA-based perovskite and Î´-phase FAPbI3 when fabricated in ambient air because of the low formation energy of MAPbI3 (ref. 11 and 12) and the strong coordination ability of water to lead atoms,24 which interferes with the hydrogen bond between FA+ and Iâ and produces the more energy stable Î´-phase. When the perovskite is fabricated in a glovebox with a N2 atmosphere to avoid the influence of water molecules, the Î´-phase disappears and more FA+ is incorporate into the perovskite lattice. Surprisingly, the introduction of MASCN can greatly enable FA+ to enter the perovskite lattice even in a moist environment. Such an impact can be ascribed to the stronger ionic interaction between SCNâ and Pb2+ than that between Iâ and Pb2+,40 which reduces the coordination ability of water to lead compounds,41 thus reducing the effect of water on hydrogen bonding between the inorganic octahedron and organic ammonium cation. What's more, strong hydrogen bonds are also formed between SCNâ and FA+, further enhancing the 3D framework of the perovskite. Fig. 1(d) shows the XRD patterns of the films after the thermal annealing treatment. There is still a small amount of Î´-phase perovskite in the 0 MASCN sample, but not in the 50 MASCN sample, indicating that the transition from the Î´-phase to the Î±-phase is not complete in the thermal annealing process, so it is necessary to stabilize the Î±-phase at room temperature. Furthermore, the 50 MASCN sample shows the strengthening of the (110) peak from 2382 cps to 3436 cps and the narrowing of the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the (110) peak from 0.28Â° to 0.23Â° compared to the 0 MASCN sample, indicating the improved crystallinity of the perovskite film.

Experimental

Materials

Lead iodide (PbI2, 99.99%), methylammonium iodide (MAI, 99.8%), formamidinium iodide (FAI, 99.8%) and PTAA (Mn = 3200 and Mw = 4900) were purchased from Xi'an Polymer Light Technology Corp (China). Methylamine thiocyanate (MASCN, >97%) was purchased from Tokyo Chemical Industry (TCI). Bathocuproine (BCP, 99.9%) and buckminsterfullerene (C60, 99%) were purchased from OLED Material Tech Co., Ltd (China). N,N-Dimethylformamide (DMF, anhydrous, 99.8%), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, anhydrous, 99.8%), isopropanol (IPA, 99.5%), ethyl alcohol (99.5%) and chlorobenzene (anhydrous, 99.8%) were purchased from Acros Ltd.

Precursor solution preparation

PTAA was dissolved in chlorobenzene (1.5 mg mLâ1) and then stirred for 30 min at room temperature. PbI2 (1.36 mmol) was dissolved in a mixed solvent of DMF/DMSO (9:1, v/v, 1 mL) and stirred at 75 Â°C for a whole night to get a clean solution. FAI (0.234 mmol), MAI (0.074âx mmol), and MASCN (x mmol) (x = 0, 0.0185, 0.037, 0.0555, and 0.074 corresponding to 0, 25, 50, 75, and 100 mol% MASCN respectively) were dissolved in ethyl alcohol (1 mL), and extra acetonitrile (8 Î¼L) was added into the solution.

Fabrication of perovskite devices

Pre-patterned ITO substrates were cleaned via sequential sonication with diluted detergent, deionized water, acetone, and IPA for 20 min each and then baked at 120 Â°C in an oven for 30 min. The as-cleaned ITO substrates were treated with UVâozone for 15 min. The preparation of the hole transport layer and perovskite layer was accomplished in ambient air with 50 Â± 5% RH. PTAA solution was spin coated onto the prepared ITO glass at 6000 rpm (with a ramp rate of 2000 rpm sâ1) for 30 s, and then heated at 120 Â°C for 10 min and cooled down to room temperature. To obtain a uniform and fully covered PbI2 thin film under ambient conditions, PbI2 solution was spun on the PTAA substrate for only 4 s with a ramp rate of 1000 rpm sâ1 and the spinning was stopped as soon as 4000 rpm was reached. The formed transparent liquid films were quickly dipped into IPA solution and bathed for about 12 s to ensure complete crystallization. After bathing, the films were blown-dry by using an air gun. After mixing the solution (280 Î¼L) with FAI, MAI and MASCN at different ratios were added dropwise at the centre of the substrates and spun at 4000 rpm with a ramp rate of 1000 rpm sâ1 for 3 min. After complete reaction, the films were transferred to a hot plate at 150 Â°C for 40 s, and then transferred to a 120 Â°C hot plate for another 12 min. Finally, the prepared films were transferred into a vacuum chamber. C60 (30 nm)/BCP (7 nm) were successively thermally evaporated in the vacuum chamber with a base pressure of <1 Ã 10â4 Pa. Subsequently, a metal electrode of 100 nm Cu was thermally evaporated through a metal shadow mask with an aperture area of 8 mm2.
