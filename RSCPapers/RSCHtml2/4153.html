Abstract

Gel polymer electrolytes (GPEs) are emerging materials for Znâ€“air batteries (ZABs), since the GPE can act as a combined electrolyte and separator in the battery. GPE-KOH was fabricated through the polymerization of poly(acrylic) acid in the presence of KOH and ZnO. GPE-KOH-KI was fabricated following a similar procedure, followed by immersion in a solution containing KOH, KI, and ZnO. The addition of KI to the electrolyte changed the conventional charging reaction to a reaction with a lower thermodynamic barrier, which improved the battery efficiency with a maximum increase of 36% relative to ZABs without KI. This work focused on extending the working temperature range of the ZAB to low temperatures (as low as âˆ’41 Â°C) as well as exploring the reaction mechanism for ZABs using KI as an additive. It is proposed that the discharging reaction at the air electrode is a combination of the oxygen reduction reaction and iodate reduction reaction.

Introduction

The increase in global temperatures in recent years has raised awareness of climate change caused by the use of fossil fuels.1,2 The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energies, such as solar energy and wind energy, is considered as a potential solution to mediate this problem.3,4 However, these energy sources are subject to weather conditions which can cause the supply to be unstable.5,6 Electricity, as one of the forms of these energy sources, can be stored in batteries. Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have proven their success in commercial use, but the high cost of using LIBs in grid storage makes them less competitive compared with other batteries, such as metalâ€“air batteries.7 One of the candidates that is receiving considerable attention is zincâ€“air batteries (ZABs).8 Zinc is a much safer material compared with Li and is more abundant in the earth's crust, which makes ZABs potentially more affordable.9â€“11

Gel polymer electrolytes (GPEs) are electrolytes stored in a polymer host.12 They can also act as a separator in ZABs and, therefore, prevent short circuiting caused by dendrite formation at the Zn electrode.10,13,14 In addition, GPEs have good mechanical properties which allow them to be used in flexible batteries.12,15â€“19 When applying ZABs in electronic devices, the batteries are expected to be able to function in reasonably cold environments.20 However, when the temperature is decreased to sub-zero values, GPEs may freeze, causing the electrochemical reactions to slow considerably, resulting in significantly reduced battery efficiency or even failure.21,22

The aggregation of water molecules linked by hydrogen bonded networks results in ice formation.23 Therefore, disruption of the formation of hydrogen bonds between water molecules can be used as a strategy to extend the operating temperature of GPEs to low temperatures. Selecting a suitable polymer host should be the first step as the polymer provides the backbone of the GPE. Pei et al. compared PVA and PAA as a potential host polymer for their GPEs and used density functional theory to calculate the interaction energy between the polymer and water molecules. A larger absolute value of the interaction energy indicates stronger bonds between the polymer and water molecules as well as better tolerance to low temperatures. PAA was selected as the host polymer because the carboxyl group in PAA with adjacent water molecules has a larger interaction energy (âˆ’54.06 kJ molâˆ’1) than that of PVA (âˆ’25.56 kJ molâˆ’1).24

In addition to the selection of a proper host polymer, additives can be utilized to reduce the crystallization temperature of GPEs. One approach is to add organic solvents (e.g., ethylene glycol and glycerol) to GPEs.25 This method reduces the crystallization temperature of GPEs, but the ionic conductivity of GPEs is reduced at the same time.24 To successfully add ethylene glycol to GPEs also requires costly and complex procedures that may be harmful to the environment.26 Another approach is to add a suitable salt (e.g., CaCl2, NaCl, ZnCl2, and their mixtures) to the GPE during the synthesis step.25 This method utilizes one of the colligative properties of ionic compounds. In the study conducted by Pei et al., alkalifying the carboxyl groups in PAA into the salt form improved the interaction energy between water molecules and PAA from âˆ’54.06 kJ molâˆ’1 to âˆ’70.96 kJ molâˆ’1.24 The crystallization of water was reduced due to ion hydration in this method.20 In the case of ZABs, 6â€“7 M KOH is generally selected as the electrolyte due to the maximum ionic conductivity and near-maximum Zn/Zn2+ exchange current density achieved.9 This feature gives ZABs an advantage in low-temperature applications as the crystallization temperature of the electrolyte without any additives may already be sufficiently low for most battery applications.

Efforts have been made through the use of catalysts for ZABs to achieve better battery efficiencies at room and low temperatures.27â€“34 From the perspective of electrolytes, KI has been reported as a reaction modifier in ZABs when utilized as an additive to the electrolyte. This is achieved by altering the path of the reaction when charging the battery. Instead of following the traditional oxygen evolution reaction (OER) path during charging, an alternative pathway which has a lower thermodynamic barrier is followed.35,36 Also, since KI is a salt, its colligative properties can be used to further reduce the crystallization temperature of the electrolyte.24 Therefore, the battery efficiency of ZABs at both room and sub-zero temperatures can be greatly improved. However, the discharging reaction(s) of the battery are not clear, which raises concerns regarding the reversibility of ZABs utilizing KI as an additive in the electrolyte.

In this study, GPEs using PAA as the host polymer with KI (GPE-KOH-KI) and without KI (GPE-KOH) are investigated for application in rechargeable ZABs at room and low temperatures. The addition of KI to the electrolyte altered the conventional charging reaction, which significantly improved battery efficiency. Both GPEs, with and without KI, demonstrated excellent low-temperature tolerance, to temperatures as low as âˆ’41 Â°C. The synthesis procedures for the GPE without KI and the air electrode electrocatalysts are based on previous work done within the authors' research group.37â€“41 The objectives of this study are to develop GPEs for ZABs that can deliver competitive battery performance at room and low temperatures and to explore the viability of utilizing KI as an additive to the electrolyte.

Experimental details

Synthesis of GPEs

GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI were synthesized for this study. All chemicals for the electrolytes were purchased from Fisher Scientific and Sigma-Aldrich and used in the as-received condition.

The target precursor solution volume for both GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI was 20 mL. For GPE-KOH, 7.29 g KOH, 0.3256 g ZnO, 0.685 mL acrylic acid, 0.0925 g N,Nâ€²-methylenebisacrylamide (MBAA), and 0.008 g KPS were added to a conical tube and mixed with deionized water (DIW) using a vortex mixer. The solution was mixed vigorously for about 2 min before transferring it to round-bottom tubes. The tubes were then kept in an oven at 65 Â°C for 1 h to complete polymerization. The prepared GPEs were air cooled to room temperature overnight before use. GPE-KOH-KI was synthesized using the same synthesis procedure but without ZnO added during fabrication. Instead, after air cooling overnight, samples without ZnO were immersed in a solution containing 6 M KOH, 2 M KI, and 0.4 M ZnO to obtain GPE-KOH-KI.

To estimate the crystallization temperature of GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI, the phase diagram for the KOHâ€“H2O system (shown in Fig. 1b) was used. The amount of K corresponding to a 6 M KOH aqueous solution is about 3.5 at%. As illustrated in the diagram, the corresponding crystallization temperature for KOH solution at this concentration is about âˆ’50 Â°C, which should be sufficient for most battery applications. When stored in a freezer at âˆ’41 Â°C overnight, neither of the GPEs froze. Therefore, GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI both have good low temperature tolerance and were later used in low temperature electrochemical tests.

Electrolyte retention capacity testing for GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI was performed in a closed container in both dry (58% relative humidity) and humid (85% relative humidity) environments by exposing the GPEs directly to air at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 1c, when tested in a dry environment, GPE-KOH-KI has better electrolyte retention than GPE-KOH, with an electrolyte retention of 98.7% at 12 h and 90.7% at 100 h compared with GPE-KOH with an electrolyte retention of 96.9% at 12 h and 84% at 100 h. The host polymer plays a vital role in the water retention capability of the GPE. PAA is highly hydrophilic and is known as a superabsorbent which can absorb water in amounts corresponding to several hundred times its weight, which may hinder water evaporation from the GPE.42,43 The slower dehydration rate of GPE-KOH-KI may be attributed to the hydration of K+ and Iâˆ’ that improved the attraction between ions and water molecules.36 When tested in an environment with an 85% relative humidity (RH), Fig. 1d shows that the weights of both GPEs increase at nearly the same rate, to 110% of their initial weights at 12 h and 155% at 100 h. This result reveals the hygroscopic properties of the GPEs.44 In daily use, RH fluctuation in ambient air is common and the intrinsic semi-open structure of ZABs allows water in the electrolyte to evaporate. Therefore, the GPE can absorb water when the RH is higher to offset water loss when the RH is lower. The volume occupied by the polymer in a 4 mL GPE was 0.28 mL, which means the polymer backbone in the GPE only occupies 7% of the total volume.

Fig. 1e and f show ionic conductivities, determined from the EIS plots, for GPE-KOH and GPE-KOH-KI at 21 Â°C, âˆ’3 Â°C, âˆ’28 Â°C, and âˆ’41 Â°C. Ionic conductivities for both samples are almost the same at low temperatures with the ionic conductivities of GPE-KOH being slightly higher than those of GPE-KOH-KI. At 21 Â°C, GPE-KOH has an ionic conductivity of 216 mS cmâˆ’1 whereas GPE-KOH-KI has a conductivity of 184 mS cmâˆ’1. An increase in the amount of ions may improve the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte. However, when the temperature is âˆ’3 Â°C or lower, the viscosity of the electrolyte increases and the ion transport kinetics are expected to decrease, which affects the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte. Depressing the freezing point of the electrolyte by adding more salt is considered an effective strategy for enhancing the ion transport kinetics of electrolytes at low temperatures.45 The addition of KI to the GPE lowers the crystallization temperature of GPE-KOH-KI relative to GPE-KOH, which helps narrow the ionic conductivity gap between the two electrolytes at low temperatures. Zhang et al. added KI to PAM and compared its crystallization temperature with that of the sample without KI. PAM with KI added exhibited a crystallization temperature of âˆ’63.3 Â°C, whereas the crystallization temperature of the sample without KI was âˆ’45.0 Â°C.36

It is important to confirm whether IO3âˆ’ converts back to Iâˆ’ during discharge to determine whether the battery is fully rechargeable. However, Liu et al. conducted linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) tests with electrolytes containing KOH, KI, and KIO3, in both Ar- and O2-saturated environments, and confirmed the coexistence of the ORR and the reduction reaction of iodate during battery discharge.54 In an Ar-saturated environment, the ORR cannot occur and the non-zero current displayed during LSV is solely due to iodate reduction. However, with the introduction of O2, the current during LSV is increased substantially and is much higher than that in the Ar-saturated environment, demonstrating that an oxygen-dependant reaction (i.e., ORR) occurs during the discharge process and accounts for a majority of the observed current. For electrochemical testing at 2 mA cmâˆ’2 (Fig. S7â€ ), the discharge voltage during cycling is almost the same for electrolytes with and without KI, which further supports that the ORR, and not the iodate reduction reaction, dominates the discharge process. In other cyclability tests at higher current densities (Fig. S8 and S9â€ ), there is more variation in discharge voltages, but they are similar for both types of electrolytes. Therefore, even though the reduction reaction of iodate happens during battery discharge, it is likely that the ORR dominates the discharge process.

Conclusions

In summary, both gel polymer electrolytes without KI (GPE-KOH) and with KI (GPE-KOH-KI) demonstrated competitive battery performance at various current densities and temperatures. The addition of KI to the electrolyte changed the path of the charging reaction from the traditional oxygen evolution reaction to the oxidation reaction of Iâˆ’ to IO3âˆ’, which has a lower thermodynamic barrier. As such, the charging voltage was significantly reduced and this led to greatly improved battery efficiency. However, the cyclability of the battery using GPE-KOH-KI at a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 was not as good as that of the ZAB using GPE-KOH when tested at 21 Â°C. The ZAB using GPE-KOH was able to cycle for 260 h at 21 Â°C and 10 mA cmâˆ’2, whereas the ZAB using GPE-KOH-KI showed accelerated performance degradation after 90 h under the same test conditions. It is likely that the addition of KI to the electrolyte transformed the traditional ZAB into a battery where the discharging reaction at the air electrode is a combination of the oxygen reduction reaction and iodate reduction reaction. The addition of KI to the electrolyte for ZABs provides a new strategy in terms of designing ZABs.
