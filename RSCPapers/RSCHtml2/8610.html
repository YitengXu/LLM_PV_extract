Abstract

Steam methane reforming (SMR) has been adopted for the mass production of hydrogen that has been actively used in various industrial processes for several decades. Currently, the demand for hydrogen for small-scale domestic and vehicular applications is growing rapidly. Although essential technical elements are similar at the industrial and retail levels, large-scale centralized SMR techniques do not apply to small-scale distributed SMR systems owing to the difference in their production capacity and purposes. In this review, we summarize the state-of-the-art centralized and distributed SMR processes for large- and small-scale hydrogen production. No review has been reported on the differences and difficulties in SRM technology according to productivity. By categorizing conventional and new technologies and presenting practical issues according to production capacity, this review will contribute to the rapid adoption of decentralized SMR based hydrogen production systems.

1. Introduction

In recent years, considerable efforts have been made in industry and academia to achieve a hydrogen economy, which involves the adoption of hydrogen-based energy systems over conventional fossil fuel-based systems. The need for hydrogen energy has garnered consensus around the world for a variety of reasons, such as the serious environmental problems of traditional fossil fuel use, the biased distribution of hydrogen-based energy sources, the ever-increasing need for sustainable energy, and its expected economic benefits.1

Hydrogen has been actively used in large-scale industrial processes for several decades, including oil refining processes for purifying heavy hydrocarbons into lighter hydrocarbons, hydrotreatments to remove impurities such as acidic components, ammonia production using the Haber–Bosch process, methanol production, and steel and glass treatment processes. Approximately 70 million tons of pure hydrogen are produced annually for industrial use, and continued growth is forecasted.2Fig. 1 shows the hydrogen consumption in a typical plant for large-scale essential applications, such as oil refining, ammonia synthesis, and methanol synthesis.3


Fig. 1 Hydrogen consumption in major industrial areas; others include steel production, flat glass production, and the electronics industry.

In addition to large-scale centralized hydrogen production for industrial purposes, the growth of the hydrogen market supports the expansion of small-scale hydrogen production and distribution routes for commercial and domestic purposes, such as vehicles and buildings. Underpinning this growth is the installation of infrastructure, such as hydrogen refueling stations. As of November 2019, there are 300 hydrogen refueling stations in operation, most of which are in Japan, Germany, and the United States, as shown in Fig. 2. In addition, 296 stations are planned for construction in Germany, the United States, and Korea, and 100 charging stations will be built in many European countries. Worldwide, 215 refueling stations have been installed that are no longer in operation, mainly in the United States, Germany, and Japan.4


Fig. 2 Number of hydrogen refueling stations in various countries currently in operation, planned for construction, and defunct as of November 2019.

Although the expansion of hydrogen production technology holds great promise for promoting the hydrogen economy, the large gap in the required capacity of hydrogen production for industrial and retail use is a major bottleneck for the widespread adoption of hydrogen. The capacity of industrial hydrogen production typically exceeds 100000 kg per day, whereas the production capacity for retail distribution, such as refueling stations and fuel cell systems, generally does not exceed 500 kg per day. Fig. 3 shows a histogram of the number of on-site refueling stations according to the hydrogen production capacity.5 Most hydrogen stations (72%) produce less than 150 kg of hydrogen per day.

Although they share technical fundamentals, their production purposes and applications at the industrial and retail levels are significantly different. Therefore, it is imperative to review hydrogen production approaches from the perspective of capacity, mainly to promote growth in domestic use of hydrogen because small-scale SMR techniques are still immature. They mainly focused on improving catalytic performance,7,8,11 SMR with carbon capture,12,13 and techno-economic evaluation of SMR.10

In this review, we explore recent advances in steam methane reforming (SMR) using natural gas or biogas, focusing on the practical issues of centralized and decentralized hydrogen production systems. SMR is the most widely used method for industrial hydrogen production and is also applied in systems with small capacities. Problems in large-scale hydrogen production include non-uniform temperature distribution in the reactor, selection of the optimal catalyst type to improve reaction performance, CO2 emissions, and the use of hydrocarbon fuels. In small-scale hydrogen production, issues are encompassing low economic feasibility, difficulty in miniaturizing the size of the SMR system, CO2 emissions similar to those in large-scale systems, and the production of green hydrogen via biogas. This review elucidates the research conducted to solve these challenges.

2. Steam methane reforming for hydrogen production

Numerous technologies exist for hydrogen production, including reforming, electrolysis, thermal cracking, biological processes, and gasification of various organic or fossil-based carbonaceous resources, as shown in Fig. 4. The most widely adopted method is the reforming of raw hydrocarbon-based feedstocks, such as liquefied petroleum gas, natural gas, and naphtha.10
Fig. 4 Hydrogen production technology using various energy sources.

Steam reforming or SMR is a process that produces CO, CO2, and hydrogen from hydrocarbons. Despite advances in various hydrogen production technologies, SMR is still the most widely used because the amount of hydrogen produced by SMR per mole of feedstock is higher than that of other reforming methods. Owing to its technological maturity and economic feasibility, SMR currently accounts for the largest share of hydrogen production worldwide.

As shown in Fig. 5, an SMR process typically consists of a reforming process, a water–gas shift reaction, and a hydrogen purification process. Desulfurization or adsorption of impurities is occasionally included in the process as a pre- or post-treatment step.15


Fig. 5 Schematic of the steam methane reforming process.

It is necessary to lower the concentration of sulfur to 0.2–0.5 ppm to prevent catalyst poisoning during reforming. Trace amounts of organic sulfur compounds contained in the feed can be removed in the form of hydrogen sulfide through hydrogenation.

SMR is advantageous under conditions of high temperature and low pressure because of the strong overall endothermic reactions, as shown in reactions (1) and (2).14

Therefore, the SMR process requires a significant external energy supply. Of the entire process, the steam reforming unit accounts for the largest portion of the energy requirement.16,17 Thus, an efficient heat supply device and an energy recovery scheme are required to improve energy efficiency.

In the water–gas shift reaction, the CO produced from the reformer reacts with water vapor and is converted into CO2 and additional hydrogen, as shown in reaction (3).

 
CO + H2O ⇌ CO2 + H2, exothermic(3)

A high-temperature shift or low-temperature shift reactor, which reacts at 400 and 200 °C, respectively, can be utilized independently or simultaneously depending on the process conditions and the desired CO concentration.

For fuel cell applications, the concentration of CO should be less than 10 ppm because CO deteriorates the performance of the electrode.18 A preferential oxidation of CO (CO-PROX) process, as shown in reaction (4), can be applied to further remove the CO that is not completely removed in the water–gas shift reaction.

 
2CO + O2 → 2CO2, exothermic(4)

Pressure swing adsorption (PSA) is typically applied as a gas purification system to obtain high-purity hydrogen using a pressure difference. Other purification methods include vacuum swing adsorption, temperature swing adsorption, and membranes.

3. Types of SMR according to production capacity

In order to analyze the types of different steam reformers according to hydrogen production, we investigated the articles published in the past 25 years that clearly specified the hydrogen production scale. We divided the hydrogen production systems into two categories: centralized processes with production capacities of more than 1000 kg per day and distributed processes with production capacities of less than 500 kg per day.

3.1 Large-scale SMR

Fig. 6 summarizes the various SMR reactor types and their specific hydrogen production capabilities in the range of 1317 to 11322000 kg per day. A unique feature of large-scale centralized systems is that the thermal energy required for the reforming reaction is supplied by nuclear energy that is hardly used for small-scale hydrogen production. Also, CO2 capture and combustion systems, such as chemical looping combustion (CLC) and oxygen-enriched combustion (OEC), are more commonly applied to large-volume production processes, although they are being studied over a wide range of capacities. In the two systems with the highest capacity,19,20 nuclear energy is used as the heat source for steam reforming.

4. Challenges for large-scale centralized SMR

In this section, critical issues raised in SMR for large-scale centralized hydrogen production are addressed, namely, homogeneous heat distribution, catalytic morphology, carbon sequestration, and the use of a variety of heat sources.

4.1 Homogeneous heat distribution

A centralized production system involves the installation of hundreds of reaction tubes filled with catalysts. As the operating temperature of the SMR increases, the forward reaction of SMR is favored, and a larger amount of hydrogen is generated. However, this causes damage to the tubes owing to thermal stress. When the operating temperature is 20 °C higher than the design temperature, the life of the reaction tubes is reduced by half.77 Retubing costs 10% of the total installation cost of the plant, which is 5–8 million USD.27,78 High-thermal-resistance materials can be used, but this also increases the capital cost. Hence, it is important to achieve a uniform temperature distribution to avoid mechanical replacement or damage. As shown in Fig. 8, the heat distribution should be homogenized to maintain the design temperature uniformly while avoiding superheating problems in specific areas, such as hot spots or hot tubes.
Fig. 8 Balancing furnace temperature, adapted from Kumar et al.79

Many researchers have addressed the thermal uniformity of centralized SMR systems, and the notable studies are summarized below.

As shown in Fig. 9(a), a uniformly balanced temperature distribution was achieved in a top-fired reformer model consisting of 96 burners and 336 tubes by regulating the fuel flow in a single tube.80 A reduced-order model was used to reduce the calculation time, and the affected zone was discretized into squares. For real-time optimization of the temperature distribution of a full-scale reformer, cloud-based profile data were utilized with a commercial computational fluid dynamics (CFD) program.


Fig. 9 Study on temperature uniformity; (a) single tube,80 (b) egg-crate steam methane reforming model,79 (c) grouped full-scale furnace,83 (d) two-dimensional axisymmetric reforming tube,85 and (e) furnace balancing in the 0th (left) and 6th (right) iterations,86 reproduced with permission from American Chemical Society and Elsevier.

Furnace temperature predictions have been conducted to optimize the temperature distribution in real-time.79,81 An egg-crate SMR (EC-SMR) temperature model, which combines the direct and induced effects of burner fuel on the tube wall temperature, was developed for this purpose.82 This model was based on a furnace-wide temperature model calibrated using measurement data. Mixed-integer programming was applied to a steady-state full-scale furnace, as shown in Fig. 9(b). It was found that the furnace operating temperature and tube wall temperature strongly influence the thermal efficiency of the SMR process.

A full-scale model is still needed to lower the computational cost because extensive sets of measurement data are difficult to obtain. Considering that the wall temperatures of adjacent tubes have similar values and dynamics, the tubes and burners were grouped, and a physics-based furnace model was developed using differential-algebraic equations.83,84Fig. 9(c) shows the simulation results of the developed grouped full-scale model. A narrow temperature distribution improves not only the efficiency of the reactor but also the efficiency of the entire system including a shift reactor, a steam separator, and a heat exchanger.

Lao et al.85 presented a fourth-order polynomial semi-variogram of the relationship between the outer tube wall temperature and the tube inlet location. As shown in Fig. 9(d), a model was developed based on a two-dimensional axisymmetric reforming tube using CFD. This study shows that the process operates stably in the presence of disturbances in the feed to the furnace side through a closed-loop proportional-integral controller.

The multi-objective optimization of the tube temperature uniformity and compressor duty was solved in a few iterations using simplified assumptions regarding the heating zone and flow rate of the outer and inner burner lanes, as shown in Fig. 9(e).86 The results suggested that an increased total fuel flow rate would achieve the maximum allowable outer tube wall temperature.

Various approaches have been developed to achieve a homogeneous temperature distribution, from predicting and normalizing the temperature of a single tube to optimizing the entire reactor furnace. However, there is still room for improvement to balance the temperature distribution of the reactor in the practical field. There are two approaches to improving the thermal uniformity of SMR reactors: control and design aspects. The thermal uniformity of reactors already in operation can be improved through real-time control and optimization; the productivity of the reactor must also be optimized in real-time while the overall temperature distribution is improved. The SMR reactors planned to be installed, on the other hand, can be configured from the initial design stage to ensure the thermal uniformity of the reactor.

4.2 Catalyst morphology

The efficiency of the catalyst is closely related to its morphology and particle size. Smaller particles that have higher activity increase the pressure, and it can be severe in tubes that are approximately 10 m, which is the typical length of reaction tubes in industrial-scale facilities. Therefore, a catalyst with a suitable size and shape is required, and the effect of physical parameters, such as size and shape, on catalytic performance has been examined to improve flow interactions at the particle scale. There are differences in the performance of catalysts of the same shape but different sizes, as well as differences due to the change in the surface area corresponding to different shapes.

In recent years, researchers have gone beyond the analysis of a single particle or a small number of regularly aligned and packed catalysts and have been studying many randomly packed catalyst particles to provide more accurate results that are closer to real-world conditions. The interface resistance between the fluid and the spheric catalyst was investigated using a two-dimensional steady-state non-isothermal model.87 The effect of wall resistance on pressure drop was also demonstrated via the Ergun equation, Eisfeld–Schnitzlein correlation, and Di Felice–Gibilaro correlation.

Karthik and Buwa88 compared the pressure drop, heat transfer, and reaction performance of six different catalyst shapes (cylinder, trilobe, daisy, hollow cylinder, cylcut, and seven-hole cylinder) using CFD, as shown in Fig. 10(a). They had the same aspect ratio and were randomly packed in the θ and π directions. The trilobal and daisy-shaped particles with external shapes showed a lower pressure drop, while particles with internal shapes, such as hollow cylinders, cylcut, and seven-hole cylinders, provided a larger surface area and reduced diffusion limitations. The heat transfer and reaction performance per pressure drop were the highest for the trilobal particles.


Fig. 10 Various catalyst types developed to enhance reaction performance; (a) six different catalyst particle shapes,88 (b) dependence of the methane conversion and methane conversion per pressure drop on the surface area of the catalyst shape,89 and (c) experimental images of four different catalyst shapes,91 reproduced with permission from John Wiley and Sons, Royal Society of Chemistry, and Elsevier.

As shown in Fig. 10(b), the methane conversion efficiency in SMR was compared for five catalyst shapes (trilobe, daisy, hollow cylinder, cylcut, and seven-hole cylinder), randomly arranged in several rows, using CFD.89 Owing to the effect of the large surface area, the methane conversion was higher for particles with an internal shape than for those with an external shape. The methane conversion per pressure drop was the highest for the cylcut particles.

Three different catalyst structures, namely, seven-hole pellets, monoliths, and foams, have been proposed to enhance the surface area within a compact space, and their effect on SMR performance has been explored with CFD.90 The monolithic structure exhibited the lowest pressure drop and the highest methane conversion per pressure drop because the monolith has a lower velocity owing to the streamlined flow guided by the channel walls.

Pashchenko91 conducted experiments to investigate the effects of four different catalyst types on methane conversion and pressure drop: a cylinder, a ring, a convex cylinder with seven holes, and a sphere with seven holes, as shown in Fig. 10(c). The seven-hole sphere showed a 23% higher methane conversion than the cylinder because of its larger surface area.

Catalysts of various sizes and shapes have been proposed because their morphology directly affects the reaction performance. In industrial areas, however, it gives rise to maintenance issues. Therefore, extensive research on the physical properties of catalysts of various shapes and their performance improvement is required, e.g., the effect of reactor operating conditions on the strength of the catalyst. In this respect, operating cost is also a critical factor to consider in a large-scale steam reforming reactor.

4.3 Carbon sequestration

Hydrogen has gained considerable attention in the transition from fossil-fuel-based energy systems to environmentally friendly low- or zero-carbon systems. However, conventional SMR generates CO2 during the reaction, and this is a fatal drawback because CO2 has been identified as a major contributor to global warming. There has been active research on the development of an SMR process combined with carbon capture technology to reduce CO2 generation. Among the various CO2 capture methods (e.g., adsorption, absorption, and membrane separation), absorption has been the most addressed, and studies on SMR combined with CO2 absorption have been mainly conducted from a process design point of view.12,28 Two well-developed SMR methods for carbon capture are presented below.

The sorption-enhanced reforming (SER) process, shown in Fig. 11, separates CO2 using adsorbents such as CaO, K2CO3, and MgO and promotes the forward reaction of SMR:

 
CaO(s) + CO2(g) → CaCO3(s), exothermic(5)


Fig. 11 Schematic of a sorption-enhanced steam methane reforming process, adapted from Johnsen et al.93

The temperature of the reforming process can be lowered to 600–700 °C, and the hydrogen concentration can be increased to approximately 95% or more, thus simplifying the purification facility. However, there are challenges associated with energy consumption and reproducibility when integrating continuous and repetitive regeneration reactions.92

Abbas et al.94 evaluated the methane conversion, hydrogen yield, and capture performance of SER using an adiabatic packed bed. The hydrogen yield and methane conversion increase as the space velocity decreases because of the longer residence time. The SER performance improves as the steam-to-carbon (S/C) ratio increases and the pressure decreases. The methane conversion rate increases with increasing temperature, but the CO2 capture efficiency decreases, revealing a trade-off between methane conversion and hydrogen purity.

Chemical looping combustion (CLC) occurs by a metal-oxide reduction in the fuel reactor and oxidation in the air reactor, as shown in reaction (6) and (7), respectively, rather than by supplying heat through direct combustion. This is advantageous in terms of CO2 capture compared to conventional SMR. The CO2 generated by the CLC process is not diluted with N2, as shown in Fig. 12, facilitating its separation and reducing energy consumption.35

There has been considerable research in recent years on the development of oxygen carriers, such as Ni-, Ce-, Fe-, Cu-, Mn-, Pr-, or Ca-based materials and perovskite-type materials, and their surface treatment to enhance their performance.95 Recently, solar-driven CLC using various types of oxygen carriers, including Cr-based,96 Ce-based,97 and perovskite-based98 materials, has been introduced. Furthermore, it has been suggested that integrating CLC with SER or water-splitting technology reduces energy requirements, particularly when combined with heat integration.23,99

SMR technology combined with carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) is not yet cost-competitive. For the realization of SMR with CCUS, it is crucial to increase its economic viability. Recently, the techno-economic feasibility of SMR combined with carbon capture was investigated,100 and a conceptual design of SMR with carbon storage or utilization was proposed.101,102

Even in large-volume SMR, the technology for reducing the amount of carbon emitted is still in its infancy. In order to use hydrogen as a truly clean energy carrier, intensive research on advanced steam reforming systems, including SER and CLC, is urged.

4.4 Application of various heat sources

Although industrial-scale SMR systems ensure high economic viability mainly due to the recovery of a significant portion of the waste heat, there is further room for reducing energy requirements by considering heat sources other than conventional hydrocarbon fuels. This is also beneficial in terms of reducing CO2 emissions. Therefore, various heat sources have been investigated, including the waste heat recovered from flue gases,23 heated helium,103 and solar energy especially for SMR-ATR hybrid reforming,104 and a gliding arc discharge plasma.105 Also, solar-driven SMR with CLC has been proposed and compared with other renewable hydrogen production methods in terms of energy efficiency.106 This process ensures consistent performance over a long period of four seasons.

5. Challenges for small-scale distributed SMR

In this section, critical issues in SMR for distributed hydrogen production are addressed, namely, feasibility, compactness, use of membranes, and carbon sequestration. Small-scale hydrogen production technologies enable the operator of a distributed hydrogen infrastructure to supply various consumers including hydrogen-powered vehicles, fuel cells, and hydrogen grids.

5.1 Enhancement of feasibility

Distributed SMR, encompassing hydrogen fueling stations and fuel cell systems, is a suitable route for small-scale hydrogen production because distributed SMR is considered the optimal solution to address the inadequacies in hydrogen supply.107 To this end, facilities for distributed SMR are essential to expanding consumer access, which will drive the hydrogen economy. However, while centralized SMR guarantees both economic feasibility and thermal efficiency, this is not the case for distributed SMR systems.108 Increasing economic viability is the most critical issue for accelerating the adoption of distributed and on-site hydrogen production. Several notable studies that have attempted to increase feasibility are introduced in this section.

Small-scale SMR plants have practical difficulties in recovering waste heat to increase the total heat efficiency of the system. Song et al.109 proposed an energy-saving process integrated with PSA technology. In a small-scale SMR system, it is difficult to increase the pressure difference between the inlet and outlet of the PSA process, which is the driving force of purification; therefore, the recovery rate of hydrogen is low, and the amount of hydrogen flowing into the tail gas of the PSA is large, resulting in high energy consumption. They proposed heat integration using pinch technology to reduce the requirements, and the total energy consumption and operating costs were reduced by 60.5% and 34.3%, respectively.

Researchers have also examined how to optimize operating conditions, such as the S/C ratio, reformer inlet temperature, and heat source flow direction, to enhance reforming efficiency and productivity at low temperatures for integration into fuel cell systems with stationary power engines.110,111 The changes in the thermal efficiency and conversion rate with the S/C ratio and air–fuel ratio of a 5 kW proton-exchange membrane fuel cell (PEMFC) showed that the conversion rate increased linearly as the S/C ratio increased, but the thermal efficiency varied in a concave shape.58

Despite these efforts, the economic feasibility of small-scale distributed SMR is still questionable. In some cases, it is more economical to transport hydrogen produced from a large-scale centralized facility to an on-site hydrogen station. In order to secure the viability of small-scale SMR and facilitate its adoption in areas close to consumers, it is necessary to lower the hydrogen production price by simultaneously increasing hydrogen productivity and thermal efficiency.

5.2 Compact integration

Efficient distribution of hydrogen in residential areas is required for vehicles and households, but the size of SMR units is limited by the installation area, which hinders their distribution. For this reason, vendors of commercial on-site hydrogen production systems, including HYSERVE of Osaka Gas, Hy.GEN of HyGear, and HYDROFORM-C of Mahler AGS, advertise the compactness of their systems to increase their acceptance by residents. In addition, as the volume of the reactor increases, the likelihood of dead volume in the furnace increases, considerably reducing the thermal uniformity and heat transfer in the furnace. Therefore, numerous efforts have been made to enhance the compactness of distributed SMR processes.

Ngo et al.60 and Nguyen et al.112 developed a small-scale on-site SMR system as shown in Fig. 13(a). They proposed a compact annulus-sleeve-type reformer with 16 nozzles via a three-dimensional half-scale CFD model and experiment. When producing 2.5 Nm3 h−1 of hydrogen, a thermal efficiency of 60% could be achieved. An optimized sleeve gap with the highest hydrogen production rate and thermal efficiency among the six geometries was proposed using a three-dimensional Eulerian CFD model.


Fig. 13 Reactor and various catalyst packing systems for compact steam methane reforming; (a) sleeve-type compact reformer,112 (b) macropatterned catalytic layer,113 (c) honeycomb-type catalyst,114 and (d) foam catalyst,115 reproduced with permission from Elsevier.

Conventional catalyst packing systems have limitations in designing narrow tubes and furnaces because table-type catalyst particles are packed into reaction tubes. In this respect, studies have been conducted to refine catalyst packing systems. A compact heat-exchanger-type reformer incorporating a metallic foam catalyst was proposed to produce 1 Nm3 h−1 of hydrogen, and its activity and performance were examined as a function of temperature, time, and gas hourly space velocity (GHSV).64 The metallic foam catalyst reached a high GHSV without a pressure drop, and a higher methane conversion rate and hydrogen production rate were obtained at higher operating temperatures and GHSVs, respectively.

It was found that methane conversion and hydrogen production could be improved by macropatterning of the catalyst layer, as shown in Fig. 13(b).113 The macropatterned catalyst also exhibited a uniform temperature profile and enhanced methane conversion.

Monolithic, honeycomb-type, and foam-shaped catalysts (shown in Fig. 13(c) and (d), respectively) have been proposed to improve heat transfer.114,115 In addition to the compactness of these novel packing strategies, other advantages, such as effective heat flux and large surface area, of the proposed catalyst types have been claimed. The honeycomb-type catalyst enhances the reformer performance owing to its large surface area.

Additionally, a reactor consisting of a Pt-based catalyst-coated combustion channel and a Rh-based catalyst-coated reforming channel has been proposed, with hundreds to thousands of channels stacked in parallel.116 Rectangular protrusions were designed to improve the effectiveness and performance of the reactor. To maximize the hydrogen production yield, fluid velocity, the flow direction of the two channels, and wall thermal conductivity were examined using numerical simulations.

As can be seen from the above results, a variety of catalyst packing systems have been proposed for microstructured reformers. An efficient catalyst packing system increases drastically the efficiency of the reactor, but the ancillary process units involved in the overall steam reforming process still occupy a place. Another approach to reducing space is to use a membrane reformer that will be investigated in the next section.

5.3 Membrane reformer

Membrane reforming technology has been increasingly advanced to replace large PSA units for small-scale distributed SMR systems. As shown in Fig. 14, the separation of hydrogen through the membrane promotes the forward SMR reaction, increasing the efficiency of the overall reaction even at low operating temperatures. Membrane reformers enable high-purity hydrogen separation and more importantly, they facilitate the compactness of the SMR system.
Fig. 14 Membrane reformer for compact hydrogen production, adapted from Shirasaki et al.47

There are two most critical challenges of membrane reactors: low cost-competitiveness due to the use of expensive Pd and membrane instability due to long-term operation.

Pd is a major component of membrane materials used to separate hydrogen. Pd-based membranes, typically used as hydrogen-permeable materials, are expensive because of the high cost of the metal. Various types of membranes, such as dense metals, polymers, or microporous ceramics, have been investigated to overcome this. A microporous silica membrane is one of the promising candidates for hydrogen separation, but it is hydrothermally unstable at high temperatures. Akbari and Omidkahah117 proposed a silica-zirconia membrane on a mesoporous modified alumina support using a chemical vapor deposition technique to enhance hydrothermal stability. The membrane support also plays an important role in production cost. Di Marcoberardino et al.118 analyzed the optimal area and production cost of a support layer made of porous stainless steel and alumina ceramic over a wide range of operating pressures and sweep gas flows through techno-economic analysis. Ceramic-based supports are less expensive than stainless steel-based supports but require 1.4 times more surface area, leading to higher hydrogen production costs.

As the reaction temperature increases, the methane conversion efficiency increases because it promotes the equilibrium reaction. However, the stability decreases above 550–650 °C owing to pore collapse.119 Numerous studies have been conducted to explore their reliability. Kume et al.48 demonstrated reliable and durable long-term operation while ensuring cost competitiveness. Anzelmo et al.120 evaluated a composite membrane with approximately a 13 μm Pd layer deposited on a porous stainless-steel support. Methane conversion in the membrane reactor was 72% in the first test and decreased to 30% in the last test.

Membrane reactors are promising alternatives to water–gas shift and purification, addressing safety issues as they operate under mild temperature and pressure conditions, but long-term stability must be ensured for the commercialization of membrane reactors because the performance of the membrane decreases over time.

5.4 Carbon sequestration

Similar to industrial SMR systems, small-scale SMR systems also have inherent problems with CO2 emissions. One solution is to improve the efficiency of the SMR process so that fewer carbon feedstocks and hydrocarbon-based fuels are required. However, there are still intrinsic limitations when using carbon-based feedstocks.

Sharma et al.121 proposed a simple novel idea of using waste heat to increase the reforming temperature to 100 °C while capturing 30–40% of the emitted CO2, resulting in a loss of exergy efficiency of only 0.53%. They developed a detailed process scheme that included a compact monolithic vacuum PSA to capture CO2 with only a 30–50% exergy penalty compared to that in the literature.122

Abbas et al.123 presented a one-dimensional heterogeneous numerical model of an adiabatic packed-bed SE-SMR reactor for application in small or mobile plants and evaluated the thermal efficiency under various operating conditions under low pressure. Furthermore, the cost compositions of the conventional CO2 capture, SE-SMR, and SE-SMR-solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) systems were analyzed.124 Each economic feasibility was compared against fluctuations in the spot price of electricity, carbon tax, and CO2 transport and storage prices.

Recently, a microreactor manufactured by direct metal laser sintering has demonstrated 500 sccm of methane conversion by absorbing solar radiation as a heat source instead of a carbon-based energy source.125

Research to reduce carbon emissions is much less active in distributed SMR processes than in centralized ones because carbon emissions in small systems are not as fatal as they are in large systems. However, more effort will need to be devoted to this area for the widespread adoption of small-scale SMR technologies in the long run.

5.5 Green hydrogen

There is growing interest in green hydrogen production through steam reforming of biogas and/or biomethane as a hydrogen source for future distributed hydrogen infrastructure elements. Biogas from biomass degradation has attracted attention for its use to supply heat to high-temperature fuel cells. This newly developed technology is rarely applied on a large scale due to the limited supply of biogas. In this section, we highlight the recent notable findings in biogas-assisted green hydrogen production.

A mathematical model of biogas reforming processes was developed and validated with experimental data showing a good correspondence between experimental and calculated gas compositions.126 Also, Di Marcoberardino et al.127 evaluated the potentiality of a biogas membrane reformer for decentralized green hydrogen production. From the results, they claim that membrane reactors are a promising technology for green hydrogen production using raw biogas. Reinders et al.128 investigated biogas as a source for producing hydrogen at wastewater treatment plants. The raw material for hydrogen production is digester gas, a renewable energy source. They evaluated the potential for integration of a hydrogen production system into a local supply chain in the vicinity of wastewater treatment plants. Park et al.129 carried out a system optimization for effective hydrogen production via anaerobic digestion and biogas steam reforming from food waste. Optimized conditions of anaerobic digestion and biogas reforming were a reaction temperature of 700 °C and an H2O/CH4 ratio of 1.0. More recently, Madeira et al.130 studied hydrogen production from swine manure biogas via SMR and water–gas shift and evaluated the potential of this waste to produce green hydrogen. The ecological efficiency, pollution indicator, and energy efficiency of the process were 93.7%, 19.2%, and 79.1%, respectively, showing viability from an ecological standpoint.

Steam reforming using biogas can be a promising alternative to fossil fuel-based reforming in the long term. Although this does not apply to large-scale plants due to the limited capacity of biogas, we believe that future research should be focused on producing green hydrogen using steam reforming of biomethane in small-scale systems.

6. Concluding remarks

Industrial SMR-based hydrogen production processes have become increasingly mature through the optimal design of SMR reactors. Although small-scale SMR technology for domestic applications, including fuel cells, is not as mature as large-scale reforming, many studies are being conducted for its rapid adoption. However, despite the technological difference in SMR according to the production scale, the analysis of SMR with different production capacities has rarely been performed.

In this review, we summarize the recent literature on SMR technologies for the centralized and distributed production of hydrogen. We conclude this review by suggesting future research directions for hydrogen production on different scales. First, to further advance SMR technology for centralized hydrogen production, real-time control and optimization of the reactor are of paramount importance. In industrial-scale plants, there is still a need to optimize the entire process to increase hydrogen productivity and maximize process efficiency at the same time. To this end, a multi-objective Bayesian optimization framework can be utilized. Second, for small-scale distributed hydrogen production, it is imperative to reduce the cost of hydrogen production by increasing energy efficiency or optimizing operating conditions. Furthermore, metal-based membrane reactors that are compact and do not degrade the system efficiency are also required. Regardless of the capacity, SMR has inherent limitations associated with CO2 emissions; thus, new advanced SMR techniques need to be developed to reduce CO2 emissions or capture emitted CO2. In small-scale systems, steam reforming technology using biogas is noteworthy as a promising solution.

This review will serve as a guide for the comprehensive analysis of SMR hydrogen production technology at scale and contribute to the rapid realization of the hydrogen economy.
