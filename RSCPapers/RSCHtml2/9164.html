Abstract

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites as new emerging functional materials stand out from numerous photovoltaic materials thanks to the unprecedentedly rapid improvement of their power conversion efficiency within a short period. To explore potentially more efficient photovoltaic candidates, the structural and electronic properties of FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 based on prototype MAPbI3 are investigated for superior performance. Specifically, structural relaxation is performed at the PBE+D2 level and the electronic and optical properties are investigated at the HSE + SOC level. Optical simulations show that significantly improved performance can be successfully achieved by means of the injection of FA cations. Moreover, the calculations of defect formation energies imply that MA-poor ambient conditions are energetically favorable to synthesize a variety of FA-doped pervoskite compounds FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 of different ratios. It is interesting to find that compared with the prototype MAPbI3, the optical performance of the perovskite series FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 is effectively improved with an increase in FA content; meanwhile the relative stability of the perovskite series is also efficiently enhanced. Our study not only sheds new light on further understanding perovskite absorbers but also provides the basic rationale for designing new functional materials used for photovoltaics.

1 Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskites (OIHPs) have been attracting increasing attention since methylammonium lead triiodide perovskite MAPbI3 (MA = methylammonium, CH3NH3+) was first used as a visible-light sensitizer for photovoltaic applications with a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 3.8% in 2009.1 However, at that time, the new finding received little attention due to low efficiency and fast degradation of the liquid electrolyte. Two years later, a quantum-dot-sensitized perovskite solar cell (PSC) was fabricated by Park's group with a PCE of 6.5%,2 the highest efficiency cell based on inorganic quantum dots reported at that moment, but the work was still not widely focused on because of similar dissolution issues. A key breakthrough in 2012 was the successful manufacture of a solid state PSC with a PCE of 9.7% and a stable performance over a timescale exceeding 500 hours under ambient conditions.3 From then on, the development of PSCs entered a whole new era. In the same year, Snaith et al. confirmed a non-sensitized PSC with a PCE of 10.9%, characteristic for mixed halide ions in the formula MAPbI3âˆ’xClx and coated Al2O3 thin films.4 In the following year, PSCs with PCEs of 12.3%, 14.1%, and 15.4% were then reported successively based on organic lead iodide perovskite MAPbI3.5â€“7 It is notable that the PCE of PSCs rapidly reached up to the recently verified 22.1% within eight years,1â€“13 and OIHPs can hence stand out among a variety of photovoltaic materials. In addition to their relatively high PCE, both facile fabrication technology and low cost are also quite important for that. Naturally, an enormous amount of research effort has been devoted to pursuing superior photovoltaic performance such as facile solution processability,14,15 high absorption coefficient,16â€“18 wide spectral range,19,20 large carrier diffusion length, long lifetime,21â€“26 high mobility27 and small effective masses,28 low trap-state density25 and benign grain boundaries.28 In this regard, the great potential of PSCs is provided by these superior performances, mainly including the replacement of inorganic quantum dots and conventional organic absorbers for photovoltaic applications. Therefore, further understanding the structural and optical properties of OIHPs is imperative to design high performance photovoltaic devices with desired characteristics.

To the best of our knowledge, the synthesis and characterization of prototype perovksite MAPbI3 was first carried out by Weber in 1978.29 Within the inorganic framework, metal ion Pb2+ with six neighbor halide ions Iâˆ’ constitutes so-called [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedrons, and apparently, iodide ions can be divided into two kinds, equatorial and apical. Moreover, [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedrons are corner-connected by iodide ions. Organic molecule MA is located at the center of four [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedrons, in other words, each organic cation is coordinated with twelve iodide anions.30â€“32 Additionally, the structure and symmetry of perovskites is closely related to ambient temperature, which has been confirmed by previous experiments.30,32 Generally, an ideal cubic perovskite (Pm3m space group) exists when the temperature is higher than 330.4 K. And then, a tetragonal phase is found under the conditions of 161.4 K < t < 330.4 K. At low temperatures of below 161.4 K, the perovskite would be distorted into the orthorhombic phase. In addition to the common three phases, the cubic phase may convert to a hexagonal phase named as the Î´-phase, which is not a typical perovskite structure, at temperatures of below 360 K, usually existing at a liquid interface.32 Accordingly, the enhancement of symmetry means a higher ambient temperature, which has been proposed to mostly depend on the octahedron distortion and the rotation of the MA cation within a Pbâ€“I inorganic framework. Among perovskite structures reported to date, the site of the MA cation in the orthorhombic phase is only experimentally fixed at a temperature of below 161.4 K.30 Meanwhile, it has been demonstrated that the lattice constants in the orthorhombic phase almost remain invariant at low temperature. Consequently, the orthorhombic perosvkite MAPbI3 is chosen as a prototype model for the present study.

The prototype perovskite MAPb3 has a resulting symmetry arising out of the lattice distortion and the rotation of organic cations within leadâ€“halide inorganic matrices.33 It has been demonstrated that the edges of the band gap are mainly occupied by inorganic matrices rather than organic cations, and organic cations act to maintain charge neutrality34 and the band gap closely depends on the length of inorganic metalâ€“halide bonds.5 However, the size of organic cations plays an important role in the expansion or contraction of the whole lattice arising from the substitution doping of larger or smaller organic cations. Then formamidinium (FA = formamidinium, NH2CHNH2+) lead halide perovskites FAPbX3 (X = halide) have been demonstrated with a tunable band gap range of 1.48â€“2.23 eV.35 We emphasize here that the band gap of 1.48 eV has been demonstrated to be the most suitable for single junction solar cells. Moreover, large electron and hole diffusion lengths are observed in the perovskite, which is favorable for the fabrication of planar heterojunction solar cells. In addition, to efficiently enhance light harvesting ability, different mixing proportions of MA and FA in the A site of perovskite APbI3 are used for extending the optical absorption onset into the red, which has been experimentally confirmed.36,37 So far, numerous theoretical studies on metal ion doping and halide ion doping based on OIHPs have been widely reported,5,16â€“19,38â€“40 however, with respect to the organic cation mixing based on prototype pervoskite MAPbI3, for the time being the systematic rationale is still uncertain, which is the main motivation for this work.

2 Computational details

All the theoretical calculations have been conducted without any symmetry constraint, applying the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP)41â€“44 code based on density functional theory (DFT). The valence electron configurations involved in the calculations are shown as H (1s1), C (2s22p2), N (2s22p3), Pb (5d106s26p2), and I (5s25p5), respectively, and the ionâ€“electron interaction is described by means of the projected augmented wave (PAW)45â€“47 method. Under the generalized gradient approximation (GGA), the exchange correlation functional developed by Perdew, Burke and Ernzerhof (PBE) was adopted for the structural and electronic properties calculations. For the present work, periodic boundary conditions are included in all three dimensions. The Brillouin zone was sampled with a Î“-centered k-point mesh of 4 Ã— 4 Ã— 4 in the Monkhorstâ€“Park scheme,48 and the plane-wave cutoff energy was set to 500 eV. In the meantime, all the structures in this work were fully relaxed with a conjugate gradient algorithm, and the convergence criteria were set so that the total energy variation per atom is less than 1 Ã— 10âˆ’5 eV and the Hellmannâ€“Feynman forces per atom are smaller than 20 meV Ã…âˆ’1. In addition, for OIHPs, van der Waals interaction has been demonstrated to play an important role in their stable phases,49â€“51i.e., the lattice parameters delivered by inclusion of van der Waals interactions agree well with the experimental values. In all calculations the PBE+D2 method was hence employed to account for dispersion forces by applying semi-empirical pairwise interaction corrections.52 Note that further tests on greater cutoff energy and denser k-points showed negligible difference in the resulting configuration, so the results in the paper are reliable. Besides, according to previous reports,53,54 the band gap determined by first-principles calculations without the effect of spinâ€“orbit coupling (SOC) was in good agreement with experimental results. And the accuracy is an accidental coincidence, moreover, it is attributed to the cancelation of correlation and relativistic (SOC) effects, both of which have opposite values.53â€“55

3.2 Electronic properties

To get insight into intrinsic characteristics, we here discuss the electronic properties of the perovskite series. As is known to us, the electron band gap is primarily determined by the sites of both the conduction band minimum (CBM) and valence band maximum (VBM). For the present work, in all cases the energy at the top of the valence band is set to zero. For prototype MAPbI3 labeled as FA-0.0 in Fig. 2, the bottom of the conduction band is mainly composed of a Pb-6p electron state with weak coupling with I ions, indicating the ionicity of MAPbI3. By contrast, the top of the valence band is chiefly composed of Pb-6s and I-5p electron states involving strong anti-bonding coupling. The electronic states in the edge of the band gap are in good agreement with previous investigations,66â€“68 and hardly involve organic cations as mentioned before. Specifically, the cases of FA-0.5a, FA-0.5b and FA-0.5c are referenced to the right panel in Fig. 2(e)â€“(g). The three cases indicate that two FA cations replace two MA cations in three different ways based on prototype MAPbI3. It is noteworthy that no impurity states exist in all cases, which means that the optimum electronic band gap delivers satisfactory photovoltaic ability. The band gaps of the cases of FA-0.25, 0.5a, 0.5b, 0.5c and 0.75 are 1.64 eV, 1.59 eV, 1.68 eV, 1.63 eV and 1.57 eV, respectively. Based on the analysis, the case of FA-0.75 corresponds to the optimum optical performance among all the FA-doped series with the exception of pure FAPbI3, which is attributed to the suitable band gap of 1.57 eV.

Therefore, it is undeniable that the photovoltaic capability of FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 based on prototype MAPbI3 is significantly improved with the benefits of the injection of FA cations. Although the toxicity of heavy metal lead is not directly alleviated, another perspective suggests that the use level of metal lead will certainly decline, due to mass production of other PSCs, such as those based on FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3.

4 Conclusions

First-principles calculations based on DFT have been performed for lattice relaxation and electronic properties of perovskite compounds FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 based on prototype MAPbI3 in an orthorhombic phase. Our studies have demonstrated that significant red shift and enhancement of the absorption coefficient can be successfully achieved with the benefits of mixing organic cations of MA and FA.

Based on the definition of tolerance factor, it is deduced that the stability of the perovksite series FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 is apparently enhanced thanks to the large-size FA cations instead of MA cations. Moreover, the absorption spectrum simulations indicate that the superior performance exactly corresponds to the case of FA-0.75 within the range of the visible light spectrum; by contrast, the perovskite series of the other doping proportions show improved ones to a relatively small extent. Additionally, it is energetically favorable to synthesize perovskite series FAxMA1âˆ’xPbI3 of different doping concentrations, which has been confirmed by the calculations of defect formation energy. The DOS analyses show that the bottom of the conduction band is mainly occupied by the metal lead of Pb-6p electron states while the top of the valence band is composed of metal lead of Pb-6s and iodide of I-5p orbits, which is similar to previous reports. It is noteworthy that the iodide ions are divided into two kinds: apical and equatorial. As for the prototype one, the top of the valence band is only occupied by the equatorial ions, while the situations vary with the injection of FA cations. Significantly, for the FA-doped series, the electronic states at the top of the valence band involve not only the equatorial iodide ions but also the apical ones, which is quite different from the prototype one. Therefore, it is implied that superior performance should be attributed to charge transfer arising from the injection of FA cations. With respect to the Bader charge analysis, lattice distortion of the perovsktie series is closely related to charge transfer of organic cations. In combination with the charge distributions of the VBM and CBM, it is not difficult to find that not only the lattice distortion but also superior optical capability is directly related to charge transfer for the cases of FA-0.25 and FA-0.75 in particular. Besides, the results are consistent with the experimentally reported cation-mixing PSCs with a PCE of 14.9%, so we expect that our simulations can provide a further insight for designing more efficient photovoltaic materials.
