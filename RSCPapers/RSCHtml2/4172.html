The role of proton coupled electron transfer in water oxidation

Christopher J. Gagliardi , Aaron K. Vannucci , Javier J. Concepcion , Zuofeng Chen and Thomas J. Meyer *
Department of Chemistry and Energy Research Frontier Center, University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, NC 27599-3290, USA

First published on 26th January 2012

Abstract

Water oxidation is a key half reaction in energy conversion schemes based on solar fuels and targets such as light driven water splitting or carbon dioxide reduction into CO, other oxygenates, or hydrocarbons. Carrying out these reactions at rates that exceed the rate of solar insolation for the extended periods of time required for useful applications presents a major challenge. Water oxidation is the key other half reaction in these schemes and it is dominated by PCET given its multi-electron, multi-proton character, 2H2O O2 + 4e + 4H+. Identification of PCET was an offshoot of experiments designed to investigate energy conversion by electron transfer quenching of molecular excited states. The concepts redox potential leveling and concerted electronproton transfer came from measurements on stepwise oxidation of cis-RuII(bpy)2(py)(OH2)2+ to RuIV(bpy)2(py)(O)2+. The Ru blue dimer, cis,cis-(bpy)2(H2O)RuORu(OH2)(bpy)24+, was the first designed catalyst for water oxidation. It undergoes oxidative activation by PCET to give the transient (bpy)2(O)RuVORuV(O)(bpy)24+, O-atom attack on water to give a peroxidic intermediate, and further oxidation and O2 release. More recently, a class of single site water oxidation catalysts has been identified, e.g., Ru(tpy)(bpm)(OH2)2+ (tpy is 2,2:6,2-terpyridine; bpm is 2,2-bipyrimidine). They undergo stepwise PCET oxidation to RuIV=O2+ or RuV(O)3+ followed by O-atom transfer with formation of peroxidic intermediates which undergo further oxidation and O2 release. PCET plays a key role in the three zones of water oxidation reactivity: oxidative activation, OO bond formation, oxidation and O2 release from peroxidic intermediates. Similar schemes have been identified for electrocatalytic water oxidation on oxide electrode surfaces based on phosphonated derivatives such as [Ru(Mebimpy)(4,4-(PO3H2CH2)2bpy)(OH2)]2+. A PCET barrier to RuIIIOH2+ RuIV=O2+ oxidation arises from the large difference in pKa values between RuIIIOH2+ and RuIV(OH)3+. On oxide surfaces this oxidation occurs by multiple pathways. Kinetic, mechanistic, and DFT results on single site catalysts reveal a new pathway for the OO bond forming step (Atom-Proton Transfer, APT), significant rate enhancements by added proton acceptor bases, and accelerated water oxidation in propylene carbonate as solvent with water added as a stoichiometric reagent. Lessons learned about water oxidation and the role of PCET and concerted pathways appear to have direct relevance for water oxidation in Photosystem II (PSII) with PSII a spectacular example of PCET in action. This includes a key role for Multiple Site-Electron Proton Transfer in oxidative activation of the Oxygen Evolving Complex (OEC) in the S0 S1 transition in the Kok cycle.

Broader context

Water oxidation is a key half reaction in energy conversion schemes based on solar fuels with light-driven water splitting or carbon dioxide reduction into CO, other oxygenates, or hydrocarbons notable targets. Water oxidation is the key other half reaction in these schemes. With its multi-electron, multi-proton character, 2H2O O2 + 4e + 4H+, it is dominated by proton coupled electron transfer (PCET). Single site Ru complex catalysts have been identified which undergo stepwise PCET oxidation from RuIIOH22+ to RuIV=O2+ or RuV(O)3+. The higher oxidation state oxo complexes undergo O-atom transfer with water giving peroxidic intermediates which undergo further oxidation and O2 release. PCET plays an important role in the three zones of water oxidation reactivity: oxidative activation, OO bond formation, and oxidation and O2 release. It has been shown that water oxidation reactivity is retained on oxide surfaces and that significant rate accelerations occur with added proton acceptor bases by Atom Proton Transfer pathways and in propylene carbonate with water added as a stoichiometric reagent. Lessons learned about water oxidation and the role of PCET have direct relevance for water oxidation in Photosystem II (PSII), with PSII a spectacular example of PCET in action.

1. Introduction

Although appealing as an ultimate energy source, solar energy is limited by its diffuse and intermittent character. Large collection areas and relatively high efficiencies are required for realistic applications. Even more challenging is the associated need for energy storage at massive levels for night time power generation.1,2 The only feasible solution to the energy storage challenge, at the required scale, is solar fuels and harnessing solar energy to drive energy storage reactions such as water splitting, eqn (1), or solar driven water reduction of CO2 to hydrocarbons, eqn (2), CO, or other oxygenates.
2H2O + 4h 2H2 + O2 (G = 4.92 eV, n = 4)(1)
2H2O + CO2 + 8h CH4 + 2O2 (G = 10.3 eV, n = 8)(2)

Integrating the required functions light absorption/excited state electron transfer or bandgap excitation; separation of oxidative and reductive equivalents by free energy gradients or internal electric fields; electron transfer activation of catalysis at physically separated half reactions poses a considerable challenge. Systematic approaches are available including a modular approach in which separate functional elements are developed independently, maximized for performance, and integrated in multifunctional arrays at the molecular level or nanoscale.312

Two applications of the modular approach are illustrated schematically in Fig. 1. One is a complex, multi-functional molecular assembly for water splitting. The other is a Dye Sensitized Photoelectrosynthesis Cell (DSPEC) for solar driven reduction of CO2. The latter is based on the Dye Sensitized Solar Cell (DSSC) design, but the goal is solar energy conversion to give high energy fuels rather than a photo-potential and photocurrent.3,4,1320

The genesis of the modular approach came in the early 1970's in an experiment designed to demonstrate, for the first time, that molecular excited states could undergo electron transfer. It was based on the, now famous, Metal-to-Ligand Charge Transfer (MLCT) excited state of Ru(bpy)32+, Ru(bpy)32+*. The ground state of this complex is an intense visible light absorber with max at 450 nm depending somewhat on the medium. Visible excitation over a wide wavelength range results in rapid relaxation and decay to the lowest energy MLCT excited state, Ru(bpy)32+*. The electronic configuration of this state is 3[(d)5(bpy*)1] with the excited electron in a * level on a bpy ligand. Ru(bpy)32+* is largely triplet in character and decays on the sec time scale in fluid solution.21,22

Based on quenching experiments with Co(III) ammine complexes, Adamson and coworkers suggested that electron transfer quenching could occur. However, their results were equivocal and could also be explained by energy transfer.2325 The first direct experimental demonstration came in experiments with added methyl viologen, MV2+. In solutions containing Ru(bpy)32+ and MV2+, at concentrations sufficient to quench the excited state, flash photolysis experiments demonstrated the appearance of MV+ radical cation at max 610 nm following the flash and the quenching scheme in eqn (3).2629

Ru(bpy)32+ (d6) + h RuIII(bpy)(bpy)22+* (d5)(*)1(3)

Ru(bpy)32+* + MV2+ Ru(bpy)33+ + MV+

This was an important result. It showed that excited state electron transfer quenching could occur, but, after inspecting the redox potentials for the couples involved, Table 1, something far more interesting emerged. Oxidative quenching had converted the initial, transiently stored excited state energy into redox products, Ru(bpy)33+ and MV+, which were thermodynamically capable of oxidizing and reducing water, respectively.29 This result, following on the observation by Honda and Fujishima that ultraviolet bandgap excitation of TiO2 led to water splitting,30 helped to create Artificial Photosynthesis and Chemical Approaches to Artificial Photosynthesis with the latter based on application of molecular events in molecular and nanoscale assemblies.3,12,18

Demonstrating electron transfer quenching and energy conversion was important but only a beginning. Following the excitation-quenching sequence, back electron transfer, Ru(bpy)33+ + MV+ Ru(bpy)32+ + MV2+, occurred on the 110 sec time scale releasing the transiently stored redox energy as heat. Energy conversion and solar fuels would require more, namely, multifunctional integrated assemblies that combine key elements for driving the physically separated half reactions leading to the designs in Fig. 1.

A series of integrated events is utilized in the molecular assembly in Fig. 1 for water splitting _ i) light absorption at an antenna, ii) energy transfer sensitization of a molecular excited state, iii) electron transfer quenching, iv) electron transfer separation of oxidative and reductive equivalents driven by free energy gradients, v) electron transfer activation of catalysts for water oxidation and reduction, vi) catalysis of the physically separated half reactions, and vii) proton equilibration _ all incorporated in a single assembly.3,12,18 In the photoelectrochemical DSPEC design for CO2 reduction, molecular level excitation and excited state injection occur at the interface of a high band gap semiconductor, typically TiO2, with injected electrons used to drive a catalyst for CO2 reduction (Catred). Oxidative equivalents are built up at a second catalyst for water oxidation (Catox) with proton equilibration by a proton exchange membrane (PEM) separating the half reactions. This design is appealing for its simplicity in applying the modular design approach.

Many designs are possible for solar fuel devices. At the inescapable heart of any is the need to carry out water oxidation at rapid rates with unlimited longevity in a fully functioning device.

2. The search for water oxidation catalysts. PCET appears

With the result on Ru(bpy)32+* in hand, the solar fuels challenge broadened to include catalysts for water oxidation and reduction of CO2. There was little to go on for water oxidation. Bruce Moyer, a graduate student in the Meyer group at the time, suggested that it would make sense to have a water molecule in the coordination sphere of a putative catalyst and prepared the aqua complex derivative of Ru(bpy)32+, cis-Ru(bpy)2(py)OH22+.

What he found, based on chemical oxidation and electrochemical studies, established a basis for PCET.3133 Oxidation to Ru(III) was pH dependent with a decrease in pKa from 10.6 for RuIIOH22+ to 0.85 in RuIIIOH23+, a decrease comparable to the decrease in pKa from FeII(OH2)62+ (pKa,1 = 9.5) to FeIII(OH2)63+ (pKa,1 = 2.2).34,35 Further oxidation resulted in a surprise when he found that both an electron and proton were lost from RuIIIOH2+ to give the oxo complex, RuIV(bpy)2(py)(O)2+.35 There was precedence in the literature for Ru(IV) but very limited evidence for well-defined oxo complexes.

This initial discovery led to important results quickly. The significant decrease in pKa upon oxidation led to pH dependent redox couples. The formal potential for the Ru(III/II) couple for cis-Ru(bpy)2(py)OH22+ (E) was found to vary predictably with pH as shown in eqn (4) which follows directly from the Nernst equation. The impact on E values of proton loss was of great importance for multi-electron redox couples and for multi-electron catalysis. As shown by E1/2 E values in Scheme 1, with E the formal potential, at pH = 7, sequential PCET oxidation from RuIIOH22+ to RuIIIOH2+ to RuIV=O2+ occurs with E = 0.11 V compared to 0.51.5 V typically found for sequential 1e couples.

The decrease in E was largely due to PCET. Loss of both protons and electrons in these PCET half reactions avoided charge build up.3537 This aspect of PCET is crucial in multi-electron, multi-proton catalysis because it provides a basis for accumulating multiple redox equivalents at single sites or clusters at closely spaced potentials. At the same time, PCET provides a basis for activation toward water oxidation. Loss of 2e/2H+ in the oxidation of RuIIOH22+ to RuIV=O2+ was already half way to the 4e/4H+ requirement for water oxidation. It also created a reactive RuIV=O2+ site and a mechanistic basis for OO bond formation based on O-atom transfer from RuIV=O2+, a well established pathway for RuIV=O2+ complexes.36

The mechanistic impact of PCET was also revealed in a kinetic study on the comproportionation reaction between RuIIOH22+ and RuIV=O2+ to give 2RuIIIOH2+, Scheme 2.3133 The study was conducted to establish how the proton was transferred between sites. Insight came from a H2O/D2O kinetic isotope effect (KIE) study which revealed an isotope effect of 16.1 and a mole fraction study which showed involvement by a single proton.31 These results led to the suggestion that the comproportionation reaction occurred by a concerted process in which both an electron and proton were transferred from the RuIIOH22+ donor to the RuIV=O2+ acceptor.


Scheme 2 Comproportionation reaction between RuIV=O2+ and RuIIOH22+ illustrating concerted electron-proton transfer (EPT).31,38

In the initial nomenclature, the concerted pathway was described as Proton Coupled Electron Transfer or PCET to distinguish it from H-atom transfer. The term PCET has come to be used more generally to describe reactions in which both electrons and protons are transferred regardless of mechanism. In the reaction in Scheme 2, electron transfer occurs between orbitals largely dRu(II) and dRu(IV) in character and proton transfer from (OH) to a lone pair on the oxo group of RuIV=O2+. These orbital characteristics distinguish this elementary reaction from H-atom transfer in which electrons and protons are transferred to or from the same bond. The term Electron-Proton Transfer or EPT has been proposed for this reaction and is consistent with ET for electron transfer and PT for proton transfer.35,39

The initial attempt to achieve water oxidation with cis-Ru(bpy)2(py)OH22+ failed. Oxidative activation to RuIV=O2+ was a step in the right direction but the thermodynamics of water oxidation were unfavorable by 0.1 V. Success was found with the structurally related blue dimer, cis,cis-(bpy)2(H2O)RuORu(OH2)(bpy)24+.4043 Related -oxo bridged complexes had been reported earlier by Dwyer and coworkers44 and the aqua dimer was synthesized at UNC. The structure was appealing in having two RuOH22+ sites available to match the overall 4e/4H+ requirement for water oxidation. Because of strong electronic coupling and RuO orbital mixing across the -oxo bridge, the required 2e change at each site was based on a Ru(III) Ru(V) oxidation state change rather than Ru(II) Ru(IV). The net effect was the same, 4e/4H+ oxidation of (H2O)RuIIIORuIII(OH2)4+ gave (O)RuVORuV(O)4+ which reacted rapidly and irreversibly with water, ultimately giving O2 as the product.4548

As shown in Scheme 3, water oxidation catalysis by the blue dimer is complicated by anion binding (anation) which inhibits catalysis. Anation is triggered by O2 release and is catalytically debilitating because it blocks further catalytic cycles that rely on coordination of two water molecules. Re-entry into the catalytic cycle cannot occur until water displaces the coordinated anion.4648

3. Single site water oxidation catalysis

With mechanistic insight in hand, including knowledge of the key OO bond forming step, we recently returned to water oxidation based on single site catalysts and initiated a kinetic and mechanistic investigation of water oxidation catalysis by the complexes Ru(tpy)(bpz)OH22+ and Ru(tpy)(bpm)OH22+ (tpy is 2,2,2-terpyridine; bpz is 2,2-bipyrazine; bpm is 2,2-bipyrimidine, Fig. 4).5254 Relative to bpy, both bpz and bpm have low lying * acceptor levels which back-bond and stabilize d6 Ru(II). This increases Ru(III/II) reduction potentials relative to cis-Ru(bpy)2(py)OH22+ and, with it, the oxidizing strength of the associated RuIV=O2+/RuIIOH22+ couples.55,56

Although the RuIV=O forms of these complexes do oxidize water, the rate of water oxidation is slow.52,57 CVs of the new complexes revealed something far more interesting. Extending the scans to more positive potentials resulted in electrocatalytic water oxidation and evidence for a new, pH independent wave for a RuV=(O)3+/RuIV=O2+ couple and RuV(O)3+ was clearly reactive toward water oxidation.

This cycle is related to the oxidative activation/O-atom transfer/peroxide sequence for the blue dimer in Scheme 3 but, importantly, it shows that a single site is sufficient for water oxidation catalysis. 6) When further oxidized to d3 RuV(OO)3+, the peroxidic intermediate rapidly loses O2 and re-enters the catalytic cycle as RuIIIOH2+.

7) From electrochemical measurements, the E value for RuIV=O2+ oxidation to RuV(O)3+ is higher than E for oxidation of either of the peroxidic intermediates.

Each of the intermediates from RuIV=O2+ to RuIV(OO)2+ has been observed spectroscopically and calculated results from DFT have provided molecular and electronic structural information.53 Depending on catalyst and conditions, the overall catalytic cycle can be rate limited by RuIIIOH2+ RuIV=O2+ or RuIV=O2+ RuV(O)3+ oxidation, OO bond formation, O2 release from RuIV(OO)2+, or oxidation of RuIV(OO)2+ to RuV(OO)3+.

Water oxidation catalysis by single site Ru catalysts has advanced rapidly since the initial observations were made.52,59,60 Variations in the surrounding ligands have been used to modify both redox potentials and reactivity with the ultimate goal of maximizing rates and catalytic turnovers.5256,6165 Conditions have been found for enhancing reactivity by introducing new reaction pathways.66 It has been even been shown that water oxidation can occur without water as the solvent by using propylene carbonate, with water added as a stoichiometric reagent.66

Reactivity toward water oxidation is retained on oxide surfaces_ ITO, FTO (fluorine doped SnO2), and a variety of oxide semiconductors including TiO2 by surface binding of phosphonate-derivatized complexes in interfacial configurations appropriate for electrochemical and photoelectroelectrochemical applications.54,56,58,66 As shown in Fig. 7 and 8, this includes the phosphonate derivatized catalyst [Ru(Mebimpy)(4,4-(PO3H2CH2)2bpy)(OH2)]2+ (1-PO3H2) (Mebimpy is 2,6-bis(1-methylbenzimidazol-2-yl)pyridine; 4,4-(PO3H2CH2)2bpy is 4,4-bis-methlylenephosphonato-2,2-bipyridine). 4. PCET in catalyst activation

As shown by the mechanisms in Fig. 6, and on an oxide surface in Fig. 8, water oxidation is a complex process involving multiple steps and multiple intermediates. As noted above, in this case, or any other involving water oxidation in solution or on a surface, complexity is an unavoidable consequence of the 4e/4H+ nature of the reaction. As illustrated in Fig. 8, there are three zones of reactivity for water oxidation: oxidative activation which poises the catalyst electronically and energetically to interact with water; OO bond formation; oxidation of an intermediate peroxide or peroxides with O2 release.

In the single site catalytic mechanisms in Fig. 6 and 8, initial oxidation occurs from RuIIOH22+ to RuIIIOH2+. Oxidation to RuIIIOH2+ is followed by the stepwise reactions in the catalytic cycle: RuIIIOH2+ RuIV=O2+RuV=O3+RuIII(OOH)2+RuIV(OO)2+RuV(OO)3+ RuIIIOH2+. By appropriate control of the ligand environment, E values for Ru(IV/II) couples can be increased sufficiently to enable relatively rapid water oxidation through cycles involving RuIV=O2+ rather than RuV=(O)3+. These cycles remain to be investigated in detail but appear to involve RuIV(OO)2+ as a key intermediate.

In principle, any of the steps in the overall water oxidation cycle can be rate limiting depending on the complex and reaction conditions. Kinetic complications due to a PCET kinetic inhibition are commonly observed in oxidative activation at the RuIIIOH2+ RuIV=O2+ stage. They arise from the significant difference in pKa properties between Ru(IV) and Ru(III). As shown in the E-pKa diagram for cis-Ru(bpy)2(py)(OH2)2+ at pH = 7, in Scheme 4, there is a pKa difference of more than 14 pKa units between RuIIIOH2+ and RuIV(OH)3+. The thermodynamic potential for oxidation of RuIIIOH2+ to RuIV=O2+ is only 0.78 V vs. NHE. However, at an inert electrode there is no low energy, direct pathway for simultaneous e/H+ loss. Stepwise mechanisms are available: 1) ET-PT in which oxidation of RuIIIOH2+ to RuIV(OH)3+ is followed by proton transfer to give RuIV=O2+. 2) PT-ET in which proton loss from RuIIIOH2+ is followed by oxidation of RuIIIO+. Both involve high energy intermediates with G > 0.4 eV for the first step in the ET-PT mechanism and G = 0.059(pKa-pH) at 25 C for the PT step in PT-ET.

5. PCET in OO bond formation

As noted above, the key to water oxidation catalysis is PCET oxidative activation of an aqua precursor with e/H+ loss giving an electron deficient intermediate thermodynamically capable of OO bond formation. In the catalytic cycle in Fig. 6 and 8, this occurs at the 3e/2H+ stage with RuIIOH22+ converted into RuV(O)3+. Oxidation creates a powerful oxidant with E 1.4 V for the 2e/2H+ RuV(O)3+/RuIIIOH23+ couple at pH = 3. The essential OO bond forming step is frequently the rate limiting step or a contributor to rate limiting behavior in water oxidation cycles. It is analogous to O-atom transfer reactions from RuIV=O2+ to more reducing substrates such as phosphines (RuIV=O2+ + PR3 RuIIOPR3), sulfides, or olefins.36,7175 In these reactions, net electron transfer occurs to a RuO acceptor orbital that is of strongly mixed dRu-pO character and antibonding with regard to the RuO interaction.

The initial RuIIIOOH2+ intermediate is stabilized electronically by the hydroperoxide-RuIII electronic interaction which is important in making the formation of the peroxide favorable in contrast to free hydrogen peroxide. This can be seen from the data in eqn (6). Oxidation by RuV(O)3+ to free HOOH is thermodynamically unfavorable by 0.4 eV at pH = 3, and yet G < 0 for the reaction between RuV(O)3+ and water since it proceeds to completion.

One forms the OO bond, while the other acts in concert as a proton acceptor. The concerted process is favored because it avoids the high energy intermediate RuIII(OOH2)2+. It has been termed Atom-Proton Transfer (APT) to emphasize the transfer of a proton in concert with O atom transfer in the key redox step.58

6. PCET in Photosystem II

In green plants the photosynthetic apparatus is found in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts. It consists of five membrane-bound, molecular assemblies and the Calvin cycle for CO2 reduction which is located in the stroma outside the membrane. Light driven water oxidation occurs at Photosystem II (PSII) and light driven reduction of NAD+ to NADPH at Photosystem I. In the overall photosynthetic process there is a net storage of 4.56 eV of chemical energy along with the production of 7 ATP molecules 31 kj/mol stored/ATP, eqn (8).

Photosystem II provides a spectacular example of PCET in action. In addition to driving the net reaction in eqn (8), 12 protons are transferred across the membrane from the stroma to the lumen for each O2 molecule produced with the resulting pH gradient used to drive ATP production.

2NADP+ + 2H2O + 4h 2NADPH + O2 + 2H+(8)

G = 4.56 eV + 7(ADP + Pi ATP)

Light driven water oxidation at PSII occurs by the four step Kok cycle shown in Fig. 15.76,77 As for any water oxidation cycle, PCET plays an important role as an unavoidable consequence of the 4e/4H+ nature of the reaction.35,78 Although enormously complex, many of the key structural and functional details of PSII have been elucidated giving unprecedented insights at the molecular level into how individual steps most likely occur.76,77,7987

Water oxidation is driven by the energy input from four sequentially absorbed solar photons. Each photon generates and stores an oxidative equivalent at the Oxygen Evolving Complex (OEC) where water oxidation occurs. In the net reaction, eqn (9), water reduces plastoquinone (PQ or QB) to plastoquinol (PQH2 or H2QB) giving oxygen. The reductive equivalents in PQH2 are ultimately used to reduce CO2. Each photon absorbed advances the Kok cycle sequentially through a series of states from S0 to S4 with oxygen evolution occurring at S4.7982

Proton management in and through the largely hydrophobic environment surrounding PSII in the thylakoid membrane is a major issue.78 Possible roles for PCET have been discussed in a recent article on integrating excited states and PCET6 and in a previous review where it was noted that it is possible to view the PSII Reaction Center as an intricate structure that is wired for protons.78 This account will survey only part of the story, the remarkable sequence of events triggered by absorption of the first photon in advancing the S0 S1 transition. The reader is referred to the earlier accounts for a more detail.78

The S0 S1 transition; oxidation of YZ

The sequence of events that occurs upon absorption of the first photon in the Kok cycle is shown in Fig. 15. It is based on a structure of PSII by Ferreira et al. at a resolution of 3.5 .82 A more recent structure at 1.9 has been published by Shen and coworkers with details illustrated below.79

Light absorption occurs at an antenna array, not shown in Fig. 15, followed by rapid, efficient energy transfer to the terminal chromophore, chlorophyll P680. The net reaction and how it occurs are remarkable. Absorption of a single photon drives a net electron transfer over a distance of 50 from the OEC to PQ (QB) by a series of 5 electron transfer steps. The reaction creates the first of four oxidative equivalents for water oxidation and a first reductive equivalent, as QB, for transmission to Photosystem I. The first photon also drives a proton from the stroma into the membrane and a second proton from the OEC through the membrane to the lumen. The proton exiting the OEC appears in the lumen on the 10s of sec timescale by transfer through a 30 proton exit channel beginning with Aspartate61, see below.35,78

Once formed by sensitized energy transfer, 1P680* is quenched by electron transfer through neighboring pheophytin pheoD1 to the quinone acceptor QA to give the redox-separated pair QAP680+. Tyrosine Tyrz intervenes between P680 and the OEC. Its oxidation, QAP680+,YZ QA P680,YZ, is essential because it increases the separation distance between the oxidative equivalent at P680+ and reductive equivalent at QA. This decreases the rate of back electron transfer, QAP680+ QAP680, which is essential for high efficiencies. As noted in the reaction scheme in Fig. 16, the rate constant for P680+ oxidation of YZ, eq. a, is relatively slow with k 107 s1, at least compared to the psec timescale for the initial electron transfer quenching of 1P680*. Even though slow, oxidation of YZ is considerably more rapid than back electron transfer and the first photo-produced oxidative equivalent is delivered to the OEC with high efficiency.

The presence of Histidine 190 (His190) is critical for YZ oxidation to occur and, even more profoundly, for photosynthesis to function.8587The removal of the His190 residue by mutagenesis shuts down photosynthesis!8890 Loss of this single base is sufficient to disrupt an enormously complex apparatus with multiply linked functional elements involving thousands and thousands of atoms.

As initially suggested by Babcock, but using a different terminology, and as illustrated in Fig. 16, the culprit is PCET and the importance of driving force on YZ oxidation. Oxidation of tyrosine by electron transfer without the loss of the acidic proton, eq. c in Fig. 16, occurs with E (TyrZOH+/0) 1.5 V. This makes oxidation of YZ by P680+ unfavorable by G +0.2 eV. The uphill nature of this reaction disrupts the electron transfer sequence and back electron transfer prevails.

With His190 in place, a Multiple Site-Electron Proton Transfer (MS-EPT) pathway becomes available. In this pathway, simultaneous transfer of an electron (to P680+) and proton (to His190) occurs avoiding TyrZOH+. Even though more complex than electron transfer, MS-EPT has a considerable thermodynamic advantage with G 0.2 eV for the reaction in eq. c in Fig. 16.

The S0 S1 transition; oxidation of the OEC

It has been suggested that PCET and concerted electron-proton transfer also play key mechanistic roles in the oxidative activation of the OEC.35,78 As illustrated in Fig. 16, the oxidative equivalent provided by the first photon is available to the OEC as the TyrZ-His190 EPT pair, TyrZ+H-His at E 1.11.2 V vs. NHE.35,78 The corresponding EPT couple, TyrZO+H-His/TyrZOHHis, is thermodynamically capable of water oxidation (E = 0.82 V at pH = 7 vs. NHE) and of driving water oxidation at the OEC. This is not true for the TyrZO/TyrZO couple which, in the absence of a coupled proton transfer, can only provide 0.72 V.

In the S0 S1 transition, the OEC loses one electron and one proton. Oxidation is thought to occur at the dangling Mn site in the OEC which is shown in Fig. 17 as Mn(4). Fig. 17 includes a blown up view of the CaMn4 cluster from the structure by Shen et al. which includes coordinated water molecules at Mn(4).79 Assuming the oxidation state distribution Mn(4)IIMnIIIMnIIIMnIV in S0, with the dangling site as MnII, oxidation to MnIII would be expected to greatly enhance the acidity of the bound water molecules. As noted above, Asp61 is also the entryway to the proton exit channel to the lumen. Given an anticipated large decrease in pKa between MnIIOH2 and MnIIIOH2 at Mn(4), concerted EPT oxidation, Mn(4)IIOH2 OOC-Asp61 Mn(4)IIIOH HOOC-Asp61, is favored over electron transfer, Mn(4)IIOH2 OOC-Asp61 Mn(4)IIIOH2+ OOC-Asp61, by 0.059(pKa(Asp61)pKa(MnIIIOH2)) by avoiding Mn(4)IIIOH2+.

Concluding remarks

Water oxidation, CO2 reduction and related small molecule reactions, including reduction of dinitrogen to ammonia, N2 + 6H+ 2NH3, are quintessential examples of PCET in action. All are fundamental in energy conversion in biology, in photosynthesis, and in artificial photosynthesis. The role of PCET in water oxidation catalysis is becoming clearer with common elements appearing and utilization of three zones of reactivity: oxidative activation, OO bond formation to give peroxidic intermediates, and their further oxidation with release of oxygen. With this information in hand, and the ability to use synthesis to both control reactivity and incorporate a water oxidation capability into assemblies and at reactive interfaces, we are, hopefully, one step closer in bringing to reality use of modular designs and artificial photosynthesis for the production of solar fuels.
