Abstract

Blood mimetic thixotropy solâ€“gel electrolytes were prepared and sandwiched between dye-coated porous TiO2 and a platinum counter-electrode to form a dye-sensitized solar cell. The highest overall conversion efficiency of 6.45%, and the fill factor of 0.74 under the white light irradiation AM 1.5 (100 mW cmâˆ’2) were obtained.

Dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) have been attracting much attention because of their low cost, ease of fabrication, high light-to-electricity conversion efficiencies, and environmentally friendly properties.1,2 A light-to-electricity conversion efficiency of more than 11% was achieved in a liquid electrolyte-based DSSC.3 However, considering the potential problems of the leakage and volatilization of the liquid, many efforts have been made in developing solid-state and quasi-solid-state electrolytes, such as polymer gel electrolytes,4â€“8 organic hole conductors,9â€“11 and inorganic p-type semiconductors (CuI, etc.).12â€“14 Due to low ionic conductivity, imperfect soakage of porous TiO2 film and poor contact with counter-electrode, the photoelectric conversion efficiency of DSSCs based on quasi-solid/solid-state electrolytes were less than 7.2%.15â€“17 If an electrolyte can avoid the leakage and volatilization of liquid electrolyte and keep a high ionic conductivity and good interfacial contact with both porous TiO2 film and counter-electrode, it is possible to accelerate the practical application of DSSCs.

For the fabrication of DSSCs, electrolytes are ideally liquid during the filling process, while they are better to become solid afterwards because of the sealing and saving conveniences. Blood thixotropy is the reversible transformation between solution and gel in which blood platelets act as the main framework with three-dimensional (3D) networks, which can be broken under stirring and rebuilt without disturbing. Inspired by this phenomenon, we prepared the blood-like thioxotropy electrolytes using a precursor by hydrolyzation of tetrabutyl titanate, whose behavior is similar to that of blood platelets by forming 3D networks. The nanometer titania particles are capable of self-organizing into newly dispersed aggregates to form 3D structures by supramolecular interactions through hydrogen bonding (as shown in Fig. 1a). Such network structures can be mechanically stirred and regenerated to form a gel by standing still for a while (Fig. 1a). Fig. 1b shows the photos of the as-prepared thixotropy titania precursor nanocomposite electrolytes (TPNEs) in the state of gel (left) and solution (right). The thixotropy property can enhance electrolyte fluidity and wettability by mechanical stirring, so that the electrolyte can be efficiently filled into the nanoporous TiO2 electrode. Then the electrolytes are self-assembled into a 3D network in the internal space between the nanoporous TiO2 photoanode and the counter-electrode of the DSSC. These processes generated highly efficient DSSCs with the quasi-solid state electrolyte.

The translucent titania precursor colloid (TPC) was prepared by slowly dripping 10 mL of tetrabutyl titanate (Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. The viscous ionic liquid electrolyte (VILE) was composed of 0.05 M iodine, 0.1 M LiI and 0.45 M N-methyl-benzimidazole (NMBI) in a mixture of 3-methoxypropionitrile and 1-propyl-3-methylimidazolium iodide (PMII; volume ratio 1:2). The conductive TPNEs were prepared by simply mixing different volume ratios of TPC with VILE under sonication for 2 h. Then the volatile composites were removed in a rotary evaporator at 60 Â°C under vacuum (2 h). Here, the TPNEs were prepared with the primitive volume ratios of TPC/ VILE as 5:1, 10:1 and 20:1, and labelled with TPNE1, TPNE2 and TPNE3, respectively. As shown in Fig. S1 and S2,â€  the TPNEs gels have very good thermal stability and photoelectrical properties. A thin layer of TPNEs was filled between the dye-sensitized TiO2 layer and a Pt/FTO counter-electrode. The brown TPNEs layer is in close contact with the surface of the dye-coated TiO2 photoanode and FTO counter-electrode to form the cell.

The photocurrent action spectra of DSSCs are shown in Fig. 3 (inset), which is close to the absorption spectra of the sensitizer (N3), indicating that the generated photocurrent results from electron injection of the dye. The maximum incident monochromatic photocurrent conversion efficiency is ca. 62% at 530 nm for DSSCs with TPNE1 and TPNE2, while the value is ca. 56% for TPNE3. In summary, the DSSC based on blood mimetic thixotropy solâ€“gel electrolytes fabricated in this paper gives a high conversion efficiency of more than 6% and a high fill factor over 70%. Another significant merit of our cell is its extreme ease in making and handling, making itself a low-cost alternative to liquid and quasi-solid electrolyte compositions. Its thixotropy performance can infiltrate into the pores of the TiO2 film and ensure good wettability and better contact with the TiO2 film. The electrolyte also displays good ionic conductivity. The ease of sealing is also worth noting, because the extremely viscous composite paste holds together both electrodes. Finally, the fabrication of dye-sensitized cells can be conducted to reduce the damage of dye molecules at low temperature, which can be applied to the fabrication of a plastic film-type flexible photocell by screen-printing the titania precursor nanocomposite electrolytes.
