Introduction

Nowadays researchers claim that graphene, an allotrope of carbon, containing a single sheet of atoms arranged in a two-dimensional honeycomb lattice, owing to its exceptional electronic and mechanical features, has an important role to play in redefining future technologies. There are many applications in our day-to-day life that can be reshaped and developed by utilizing those distinctive properties of graphene. Though graphene has made a major breakthrough in being considered for a wide area of applications, most of the interesting properties of graphene are still at the early stage of research. Graphene made attenuators and single and multi-frequency redesignable antennas are designed employing the tunability of graphene. As a result, the feasibility of application of one-layer graphene sheets in radio frequency applications and antenna designs is observed. Also, graphene flakes have been used as a base material to make graphene conductive ink and the developed screen-printed material has been made more conductive with the help of a compression process. It is an economical and efficient technique and the printed products are environment friendly, also having better flexibility. Printed graphene technology can also be used in development and use of ultra high frequency (UHF) radio frequency identification (RFID) antennas and near field communication (NFC) antennas. Researches prove that this technology significantly reduces the cost for RFID applications. Furthermore, RF enabled humidity sensors are designed applying basic experiments on graphene oxide (GO) dielectric properties expressed in the range of GHz. Wireless graphene security networks pave a path to the prospects of using printed graphene technology in internet of things (IoT) and wireless communication applications.

Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov were honoured with the prestigious Nobel Prize in the year of 2010 for discovering graphene in 2004 and bringing a renaissance in the field of two-dimensional materials. From the beginning of coming into the picture, graphene has drawn attention of researchers. A vast variety of applications could use this noble element to improve the current technology by a long way if successful. For a long time, most researchers were keeping their focus on the fundamentals of the associated physics and characteristics of graphene. However, the industrial scale application of this carbon allotrope has yet to be realized.

Graphene, being the first discovered two-dimensional material in nature, which has astonishing electrical and mechanical properties including high electrical and thermal conductivity, and higher electron mobility, has drawn attention from researchers, leading them to publish several studies and explore newer areas of application utilizing the extraordinary inherent properties. Due to its one atom structure, graphene has loss in the microwave region. This property can still be utilized and combined into current structures for designing reconfigurable antennas. The metal antenna acts as the main radiator for better efficiency. On the other hand, tunability is achieved in fabricating reconfigurable antennas by graphene. The size reduction of electronic devices is achieved very subtly by incorporating graphene into them. Graphene based devices attain a thickness of only 0.34 nm without changing the design dimensions of a circuit.

As the name indicates, the form of RF technology that uses electromagnetic fields for automatic identification purposes of tags attached to objects is known as radio frequency identification. For years, the use of pricy raw materials and intricate fabrication processes in metal coating and metal based conductive inks in printed electronics kept the involved cost very high. So, it is inevitable to replace the existing fabrication process with cheaper materials and simpler techniques. Researchers assert that printed graphene has the potential to be a competent alternative for RFID applications. Graphene flakes make the printed material much more conductive and in turn efficient, at the same time reducing the cost and increasing flexibility. The results of many research studies validate the use of graphene-based ink to fabricate small and compact size RFID antenna applications. Further, printed graphene ink technology can be combined with internet of things (IoT) and wireless communication technology to construct a graphene dependent wireless security network. The pioneering introductory work of screen-printed graphene antennas, wireless sensors and wireless graphene security networks will initiate upcoming developments and also the association with other two-dimensional sensing materials together to formulate drastic development of wireless sensing applications and cheaper communication systems.

In the present review, we have tried to summarize some important modern day approaches of graphene related material in RFID technology and the special features of the material to be applied in such a field.8,12,17,23

Graphene

Elements which are mechanically less robust are highly eligible to be used in electronics applications. Malleability and elasticity are two important characteristics of an element to be used in applications like sensors, biomedical devices etc. Of late, during electronic-device design, apart from mechanical flexibility and optimum electronics performance of the component material, a few more factors are highly considered – high conductivity and abundant electron mobility, keeping the dimension of the device almost intact – which graphene provides. Graphene can be easily integrated into an existing circuit by changing the thickness of the circuit to around 0.34 nm only.1,2

The use of inorganic-based electronics materials confines the applications of flexible electronics due to their mechanical distortion. Studies, driven by concern to address this issue, have shown that graphene is a leading alternative in various applications like energy harvesting devices,3 transistors,4 flexible electronics,5 photonic devices,6,7 touch panels8etc. For practical applications CVD grown graphene is taken into account instead of pristine graphene, which includes size limitation and fabrication troubles.9,10

Mechanical and thermal properties of graphene

The thickness of one-atom-thick graphene is found to have a value of 0.345 nm, making it the thinnest material ever produced.14,15 When traditional silicon transistors are on the nanometre scale, their performance and device dimensions, the channel length to be specific, do not hold a proportional relation.16 This leads to problems in power dissipation as well as self-heating. These extraordinary characteristics are utilized in scaling down transistor technology and alleviate issues like self-heating in many high power electrical and optical devices.

Surprisingly, the average size and the orientation of graphene flakes were found to be more determining parameters for the heat conduction, instead of the mass density of the graphene laminate. It has been experimentally tested that the scaling up of heat conductance is a linear function of the average size of graphene flakes for both compressed laminates as well as uncompressed laminates. Compressed laminates possess higher conductivity than uncompressed ones having the same flake size, because of the better flake arrangement.

Photodetectors

Photodetectors must have high responsivity and fast photodetection to perform the detection of broad spectral ranges of light. Optoelectronic applications like sensing, spectroscopy, imaging and communications require fast and accurate photodetection of a wide-ranging spectrum. Researchers suggested many conventional semiconductors for photodetection in the range between the mid-infrared region and far-infrared region. Semiconductor compounds with narrow bandgaps, such as lead selenide (PbSe), indium antimonide (InSb), and mercury cadmium telluride (HgCdTe), are widely used nowadays for the same.18–20 But these are costly and their complex fabrication process limits their use21 in practical applications, and opens a wider prospect for graphene to come into focus. The ability of graphene to absorb an ultrabroad optical spectrum and the better electron and hole mobility at high temperature make graphene advantageous compared to narrow bandgap semiconductors.

Light-emitting diodes

Solid-state light emitting diodes (LEDs) because of their incredible electronic properties have gained a worthy place to be used in the semiconductor industry. LEDs are almost seen in every electronic application like smart displays, opto-electronics, optical communications22–25etc. Some of the key variables to determine the functioning of LEDs are the current efficiency, power efficiency and external quantum efficiency (EQE).

Among these factors, the EQE is dependent upon the light extraction efficiency, and also upon the internal quantum efficiency.26

LEDs’ internal quantum efficiency has been so enhanced that the theoretical limit, i.e. greater than 90%,27 of it has already been achieved. But the light extraction efficiency is yet to achieve the theoretical limit. So, it is clear that to obtain higher EQE, the augmentation of the light extraction efficiency is necessary. Better light extraction efficiency is achieved by higher optical lucidity, high reliability and lower sheet resistance. Indium tin oxide (ITO), due to high optical transmittance (more than 80% at a wavelength ranging from 450 nm to 550 nm), besides low sheet resistance,28,29 has extensive usage for the same. But the mechanical fragility, higher cost30 and other disadvantages of ITO restricted its practical use. Further studies suggested that graphene can be a good alternative to ITO.31–35

Solar cells

Several inherent features like large power conversion efficiencies (PCEs), intense light absorption, longer carrier diffusion length and simple fabrication36–43 cause several cheaper perovskite solar cells to be analysed very carefully for practical applications. The perovskite solar cell (PSC) active device layers are placed in between electron and hole transport layers (ETL and HTL, respectively).44–46 The morphology of perovskite solar cells and various interfaces’ electronic structures affect the charge carrier transport properties.47,48 Graphene and related two-dimensional materials (GRMs)49–54 have vast utility to regulate the interfacial properties and architecture of PSCs.

GRMs are rapidly used in solar cells in order to improve efficient charge transfer. To attain this, GRMs are placed in between the ETL/HTL and perovskite layer as a separate layer and are integrated with the ETL/HTL.55,56

Thermal sensors

Single layer graphene shows excellent thermal characteristics, for which its use in heat management and temperature sensing applications is increasing. Ref. 57 shows fabrication and comparison of micro patterned SLGs on silicon dioxide (SiO2), a silicon nitride layer, and a Si wafer having etched rectangular shaped boxes. The graphene made sensor manufactured upon a silicon nitride membrane has a significantly faster response time, finer sensitivity due to the smaller value of the thermal mass, and greater mechanical stability than most other graphene-based sensors. This gives opportunities of using SLGs on silicon nitride for the applications of high sensitivity and high response speed.

Thermoelectric sensors

Besides thermal sensors, graphene can be used in many other critical sensor applications too. Detection at room temperature where the radiation is in the terahertz range is a very critical job58 but extensive use of terahertz radiation is present.59 Cai et al. in ref. 60 successfully published their work on a graphene thermoelectric terahertz photodetector with low noise-equivalent power and better sensing ability. They took the incident or absorbed power as the reference.

The electron–electron interactions are responsible for the quick heat gain61,62 of photo excited carriers, and subsequently the lattice energy decreases more slowly.61,63 A temperature gradient of electrons causes the diffusion of electrons. It generates a net current by local gating asymmetry64,65 or dissimilar contact metals.66 There are plenty of similarities between the operation of graphene-based sensors and modern room temperature terahertz detectors.67 However, graphene-based sensors have a capability of obtaining time-resolved measurements far quicker than the latter one.65,68 The identification, control and generation of plasmons69–71 are some of the foremost necessary things to be considered in nano-optical on-chip processing. In this context graphene shows controllable electrostatic field induced plasmons.72–75 Lundeberg et al.76 used the thermoelectric effect77,78 to generate a voltage by converting the natural decay product of the plasmon and developed a graphene-based plasmon detector. In this detector which operates at room temperature, graphene was encapsulated in hexagonal boron nitride. The most exciting feature of this detector was that only one leaf of graphene played the role of both the detector and plasmonic medium.

Biomolecule sensors

Application of sensors based on graphene for faster, accurate and trusted detection of diseases is a cost-effective and reliable approach. In ref. 79 Wu and others depicted how an electrochemical sensor array system made of graphene for cell sensing can be fabricated. The graphene-based chemical nose/tongue system has the ability to detect 100 cell samples that consist of (i) different cell types having almost 100% classification accuracy, (ii) artificial circulating tumour cells (CTCs) and (iii) cancerous, multidrug-resistant cancerous and metastatic human breast cells.

Gas molecule sensors

Many studies have been published that show the combination of graphene-incorporated mobile gas sensors with new age technologies viz. smart phones, cloud computing and the internet of things (IoT). Zanjani et al. fabricated a pioneering monolithically incorporated CMOS-single layer graphene gas sensor. This had a very minimum amount of after CMOS processing algorithms.80 Pour et al. conducted research and enhanced the electrical conductivity of graphene nanoribbons by lateral extension81. Lateral extension leads to a decrement of the band gap and electrical conductivity increases.

RFID tags

Nowadays, RFID tag fabrication is another branch of electronics in which scientists are massively adopting graphene for manufacturing processes due to its tiny size and low resistivity. The antenna of the tag must be of low resistivity; hence, silver and copper nanoparticles are the most suitable options for the manufacture of RFID tags by printing antennas on several substrate materials like plastic and paper with metallic nanoparticles as printing ink. But silver being an expensive choice for this process, a genuine need of replacement of silver and other metallic nano-inks became inevitable. This growing concern among researchers paved the path for graphene to be used in direct inkjet printing of low-cost RFID antennas. Presently, three types of RFID tags (based on nano carbon materials) are broadly used – a. graphene based, b. Single walled CNT based and c. multi walled CNT based.

RFID

The basic idea of RFID refers to Brard's idea of a type of communication established through transmission of radio waves between a transmitter, later known as an “RFID reader”, and a device, later known as an “RFID tag” (BRA 24). The transmitter acts as a distant power source to the tag. The constituents are a tuneable resonant circuit and a switch. The switch modifies the wave deflected 180 degrees by the tag and ensures a signal modulation so that better conveyance can be set up. The basic mechanism of RFID remains unchanged in the existing setup: an elementary tag is distantly powered by the reader, with two distinct states to set up communication.

In later times, during World War II, radar development was given high focus in order to utilize RFID technology for war purposes. Surprisingly, many technological advancements including RFID happened during this time of unrest. British researchers discovered the identification of friend and foes (IFF) system, a radio frequency transmitter system which used encoded signals to identify enemy planes.

Again, some major improvements and developments were achieved in the 1970s and the journey continues to date, making RFID technology an indispensable part of our everyday life. The widespread applications of RFID include advanced applications like logistics, inventory management, support systems for disabled people, household security, distributed sensor meshes,95 and healthcare mobility.96

It is anticipated by researchers that radio-frequency identification (RFID) systems will exist predominantly in future communications. Researchers are conducting experiments to introduce further advanced utility of RFID systems, viz. electronic toll collection, asset recognition, retail goods management, animal tracking, and vehicle safety.

According to an application perspective, in an open system the tag ID is universal and the correlation between the ID and the object can be found on a distant server. A very famous instance of an open system is the barcode, where articles are identified in a universal way. ID management in a closed system is processed internally and can’t be accessed externally. Thus, the framework of a closed-circuit network ceases at a local network accumulating information in a private database. On account of achieving a higher level of security, where IDs are to be kept confidential and not to be disclosed to the public, closed loop systems are necessarily used.

Logistics companies and distribution markets have opted out from conventional barcode systems and adapted RFID systems in order to improve efficiency and convenience due to the following pros of RFID systems over barcode-based systems – better, faster and more accurate identification capability, semi-permanent resilience, longer range data transmission, and pliability.

A simple and basic RFID system is made of a radio-scanner unit, namely a reader, and a remote transponder kit, namely tags. Two integrants of tags are an antenna and a microchip transmitter integrated with read/write memory.

Based on the power supply manner, RFID tags are classified into three types –

1. Passive tags – ones which use the electromagnetic field of the reader as a power source.

2. Semi-passive tags – they use the energy of the reader itself to respond to a signal from the reader. But, surprisingly, other ingredients of the chip viz. the microcontroller and the memory are supplied through a battery.

3. Active tags – in this case a battery is a stand-alone power source.

An active tag acknowledges the reader signal from its energy using a radio frequency front end. The action of writing and remotely fetching data from items with such tags attached is provided by an RFID (radio frequency identification) tag. RFID tags are made of a silicon microchip and an antenna. This antenna acts as a power supply to the tag and sets up communication with a reading device.97,98 For the production of economical RFID tags, the printing process generally includes direct inkjet printing on plastic and paper substrates using metal NP inks.

As the resistivity of the antenna is very low, because of the dependency of the read distance upon the antenna conductivity, silver and copper nano particulate inks are preferable. Many researchers report appropriate processes for antenna printing with the use of such inks.97–102 However, as has been already stated above, metallic nano inks are hardly used in this field, because of the huge price of silver, which is predominantly a commercially available material; instead, graphene-based RFID antennas are coming into the limelight.103

In today's era of technology, the application area of RFID is getting wider day-by-day due to its low sheet resistance, tunability and proper impedance matching between the load and source. RFID uses signals in the radio frequency range for automated detection of objects. RFID tags are applied widely in technologically advanced sectors of electronic toll collection, asset detection, manual fruit harvesting [rfid5] and many others applications. In an RFID system the reader acts as the interrogator and the tag basically acts as a transponder. A passive tag is constructed of an antenna and an application specific integrated circuit (ASIC) known as a chip. Passive ultra-high frequency (UHF) RFID systems are elucidated as long-range systems because their read range is longer than 1 meter. The different operating UHF centre frequencies are 866 MHz (Europe), 915 MHz (America), and 950 MHz (Asia and Australia).104

Methods for conjugate impedance matching

It has been described that the received power at the chip level is the foremost indispensable criterion to be considered while defining the reading range. Just a sample amount of energy transmission to the chip also necessarily needs to be achieved. Hence, the matching muddles between the chip and the antenna are to be addressed very carefully. As discussed earlier, complex conjugate matching is performed to optimize the energy shifting between the antenna within the tag and the chip, which in this case acts as the load. It is observed in complex conjugate matching that the chip causes the dissipation of half of the energy. The antenna resistance dissipates the rest. The antenna resistance is made of two components:

i. a radiation resistance and.

ii. a resistance representing the losses in the antenna (Joule loss and dielectric loss).

In case of cheap and economic devices, it is difficult to make use of extraneous matching networks incorporating lumped components. Accordingly, it is essential to embed the matching mechanisms within the tag's antenna formation. Most of the sundry feeding approaches that are espoused for the tuning of the antenna are amended versions of the well-known.

i. T-match.

ii. proximity coupling to a small loop and.

iii. comprehension of structured slots.

The configuration is considered to be useful if it is possible to tune the resistance and reactance completely independent while adjusting the geometrical parameters of the tag. Hence some matching methodologies have been analysed and juxtaposed in this review. For better coherence, the overall size is not given consideration. Instead, the maximum size of the antenna is allotted at half a wavelength. But this could be eventually not feasible for practical applications. We have reviewed miniaturization techniques afterwards. The matching capabilities of the feeding have been a bridged with the discussion of impedance charts, where the input resistance and reactance are a function of alteration of apposite geometrical parameters. Such a matching methodology is more limber, owing to the nature of the iso-line plots for resistance and reactance being mutually parallel.

Method of size reduction

There are applications where UHF RFID tags need to be fastened to small objects with an objective of inventory tracking. There will be remarkable deterioration of the radiation gain and efficiency because of the compensation between the antenna geometry and the performance. Hence, it has become a prime concern for RFID researchers to diminish and miniaturize the size of the tag antenna in a mode such that there is no degradation in performance.

Among many, meandering and inverted-F structures are two successfully and extensively utilized strategies in design of RFID tags. In this review we have illustrated the above two strategies.

The common feature of the two is the requirement of one or even more than one overlap of the radiating body, with the difference lying in the additional demand of a enough number of experimental evidence of the ground plane of the inverted-F structure. Although designing simple tag layouts can be gained utilizing matching charts and geometries having a large number of parameters, it is remarkable that the requirement of non-deterministic optimization tools which follow genetic algorithms is still prevailing.115

The meandering line's structure is a very popular method of miniaturization of the antennas. But two major drawbacks of this strategy are.

i. it can only accomplish low gain and.

ii. attain a narrow bandwidth.

On the contrary, the inverted-F structure possesses high gain, but the additional ground plane increases the manufacturing perplexities. As a result, it is not felicitous for production in large quantity.116

Meandering

As proposed by ref. 117, if the limbs of a dipole antenna can be folded along a meandered path as shown in Fig. 15, a wire configuration with capacitive reactance and inductive reactance as distributed parameters is produced. This particular type of configuration affects the antenna's input impedance. It is found that there is a phase reversal among the currents through the adjacent horizontal segments of meander-line antennas (MLA). This observation is present only up to the first antenna resonance. These transmission-line currents do not have any extraordinary offering to the radiated power. Instead, they eventually incur power losses. In the meandering process, much lower frequencies result in resonances, in comparison to straight dipoles of same height, at the cost of a narrow bandwidth and low gain.

There are cases, shown in Fig. 16, when the antenna height is reduced down to fractions of a wavelength. Hence, the maximum value of the gain of the tag is almost equal to a regular resonant dipole even if compatible size trimming prevails.

Inverted-F configuration

The inverted-L structure is produced by creasing a portion of the wire parallel to the ground. Such a structure has capacitive dominant reactance and a low resistance. Another motive of creating a similar structure is to deduct the size of the vertical wire monopole. Attachment of a shorting pin is done to serve two purposes – one is providing tuning freedom and the other is to provide an F-type (inverted F antenna, IFA) configuration, as described in Fig. 17. This can be also perceived as a monopole variety of the T-match, where folds are made in the radiating body with an aspiration of cutting down the space occupied. In such an inverted structure, the conductors that are orthogonal to the ground plane mainly are the radiating elements. The folded conductor along with its replica gives rise to a transmission-line current mode. Also, it produces power dissipation and an infinitesimally small amount of radiation. Hence, it is needless to state that this category of geometry does not evince higher efficiency. The antenna bandwidth can be bettered by the use of large strips containing wires, defined as the planar inverted-F antenna, PlFA. The residence of a ground plane is advantageous for being tagged over an object that has a high conductance value, and also for anchoring auxiliary electronics and sensors. By adding extra conductors and keeping orthogonal to the ground, multi-band tags can be permitted. Another form of structure in which the inverted-F and the ground plane are kept in one single layer only is called the co-planar geometry. Simplification of the fabrication process is the main objective of such an arrangement besides microchip connection. In alike cases, the finally evolving geometry is like an asymmetric dipole, in which the arms correspond to the inverted F structure and the ground plane. This sort of dipole has a size of about a quarter of a wavelength. With the variation of the geometrical parameters {a, b, d}, it is possible to obtain a spacious spectrum of composite input impedances. The segment of the antenna which is folded goes away from that ground plane. As a result, the ‘a’ value of the radiating conductor increases, and in turn the antenna becomes more inductive. Bandwidth issues

Though antenna miniaturization reduces the size of the tag to be fitted even in very small objects that need to be identified and tracked, a reduction in bandwidth is there, which is a matter of concern among RFID researchers and is being addressed cautiously. It is an easy task to detune the tag impedance by linking with the objects to be tagged, as well as by inter linkage with the characteristic structure. As a result of this, the reading range degrades significantly.

The realized gain becomes the most useful parameter in defining the RFID tag bandwidth, not the return loss as in the case of 50 Ω-matched antennas. In fact, the tag bandwidth is a function of the application specific required minimum reading range. Once firmed by the power limitations, this can therefore be connected to the system parameter τGtag. The stability of the realized gain instead of frequency indeed gives the range-width of the reader–tag system.

In order to generalize the bandwidth definition to be independent of the specific and distinctive application, a precisely matched isotropic antenna ([τGtag]0 = 1) is elected as a base. Thetag's band then corresponds to the frequency range, [fmin, fmax], where the following condition holds firmly:

 
[τGtag](f) = 0.5[τGtag]0(fc)(8)
where fc is defined as the middle-band frequency. With this condition taken into consideration, the activation range falls to not greater than 70% of the activation range of the base antenna. This definition allows the comparative analysis of the features of several tags differing in transmitter power and the microchip sensing capability.

Etching

Namely, the existing two types of etching process are as follows:

i. wet etching and.

ii. dry etching.

The names of the processes give an idea of the method adopted for the corresponding process. For example, in wet etching liquid chemicals technically termed as etchants are used to abolish metallic substrates. The pattern which is needed on the substrate is possible to shield. A photoresist mask does the job. The process of wet etching is tri-step:

1. the etchant is applied on to the surface;

2. a reaction happens between the etchant and the material to be taken out; and.

3. the by-products formed due to the reaction diffuse from the surface, which take part in the reaction.

Again, going by the name helps to have an idea of the basic methodology adopted for the process. In the dry etching process, no chemical liquid or etchants are used. Instead, it can be performed using any of the three following methods:

1. by the use of chemically reactive gases or plasma;

2. by physical removal of the material, mainly adopting momentum transfer; or.

3. by the fusion of the above two.123

The definition of the etch rate is portrayed as the rate of material removal. The unit of the etch rate is given as μm min−1. The etch selectivity is said to be the potential of the reactive chemical to etch away the required material only, keeping all other materials as is.124

Wet and dry etching have application specific characteristics. Wet etching is an isotropic, faster, simple, cheap and economic process. On the other hand, dry etching is an anisotropic process. The advantage of using wet etching is that it has a large etching rate and a good selectiveness property for most of the materials used. To discuss the disadvantages, it is insufficient for describing attributes which are less than 1 μm and may create chemical hazards. In the wet etching method, the required supplies are listed below:

• the solvent/development chemical,

• the acid/etchant,

• the substrate,

• a transparency sheet,

• the photoresist varnish,

• a heating chamber, and.

• an ultraviolet (UV) machine.

Dry etching has the ability to define traits smaller than 100 nm, but with a relatively high cost. Implementation of dry etching is comparatively tough. Moreover, it offers poor etching selectivity, and also can cause radiation damage.125 Hence, as stated above, metal etching is not congruous to use for production of tags in large quantities due to its wasteful and hazardous nature. In etching, a very small fraction of the raw materials is used and most of the other materials are either recycled or just get wasted, leading to a high production cost of the process. Additionally, the cleaning of the highly reactive materials should also be taken care of, to avoid any sort of damage. Hence, in short, etching is undoubtedly not an environment friendly procedure. Etching is useful on substrates that have tolerance towards chemical baths.

While etching is a subtractive process, printing is an additive process. In printing, it is possible to imprint designs onto certain areas according to the requirement. In the imprint process a stencil is cut out and a layer of conductive material is applied. Clothing and PCBs have well established application of printing. Because of good flexibility printing can be done to almost any type of material and surface. Researchers claim that printing has huge potential to be used in RFID antenna fabrication. Screen printing is better than the conventional etching method of fabrication from various aspects. Screen printing is advantageous in stability, flexibility, reliability and cost-effectiveness. But, above all results shows a very low impact on the environment than the earlier.126 In the process of printing, it is possible to fabricate complex and minute detailing, although conductivity is maintained throughout the design. In printing, the coating of conductive ink can be reduced, in turn reducing the amount of raw material required. Hence, it is clear that the production cost is very low and it offers the best cost per development ratio, making it affordable for almost everyone who needs it. Graphene based conductive ink. For the past few years, printed electronics has achieved quick development by virtue of its economical mass production ability. Hence, engaging a detailed, analytical and research-based study for enlarging the applications of printed electronics is necessary. Presently some of the applications include biomedical and biochemical sensors, PCBs, wearable electronics, flexible displays, smart labels etc.131 Printed electronics technology nowadays is predominantly applied in radio frequency identification (RFID) tag-antenna set-up. Achieving flexibility is of utmost importance for the commercialized application of these devices. Hence the use of flexible substrates for the production of such devices gives worth to use flexibility.132 Conductive-ink based screen-printing can imprint patterned structures in huge amounts. This is the reason screen printing is a chosen as the main alternative in printed electronic circuits and antennas.133 In this technique, conductive inks are transferred onto the designed patterns of the substrate through a mesh in order to form printed electronics. The available and popular conductive inks are generally divided into three types:

i. conductive polymers,

ii. noble metals, and

iii. carbon nanomaterials.

Silver nanoparticles have been thoroughly used as a conductive material for years due to two advantages:

i. the conductivity of silver is very high and

ii. its conductance does not decline significantly owing to oxidation. Rather, silver oxide is itself a good conducting material.

But the high cost of silver creates hindrances in the application of silver nanomaterials as conductive inks for mass production purposes.134 Therefore, while searching for other suitable conductive materials for ink and at the same time reducing cost, a proposal was given in favour of copper and aluminium. But copper and aluminium based conductive inks tend to get oxidized quickly. Moreover, unlike silver oxide, these oxides are non-conductive in nature. Hence the conductivity, performance and overall efficiency of the final product are reduced remarkably.135 Although it is possible to prevent oxidation by coating other materials on the surface to protect it from the air and humidity, this again incurs extra cost for both manufacturing and maintenance.136 So, the objective of cost reduction may not be achieved. Initially conductive polymers were assumed to be a good option for conductive ink materials. But later, studies found that owing to the lower conductivity and chemical and thermal instability they can be hardly used for RF applications.132 Carbon nanomaterials, i.e. carbon nanotubes, have a very low inherent resistivity of 10−8 Ω m and are intended to oxidise less even at high temperature.137 But the high sheet resistance of these having a value greater than 50 Ω sq−1 makes them a bad choice for RF applications. The high sheet resistance of printed antenna pattern makes the difficulty for radiation.138

To overcome all these difficulties and find a good alternative to silver, studies on graphene showed some promising outcomes. The high conductance of graphene drew attention and the idea of graphene based conductive ink has been developed. Graphene is an allotrope of carbon. Graphene atoms are single layered unlike junctions of carbon nanotubes and are arranged in a 2-D honeycomb lattice. Such an arrangement of atoms in graphene provides high resistance against oxidation and this along with high conductivity makes graphene a choice as a material of conductive ink. Experiments and research evaluate the conductivity of graphene as very high and almost independent of frequency in the microwave region.139–147 Graphene is regarded as a “wonder material” due to its unique characteristics. Graphene based conductive ink is one among the foremost commercial products which have been made from this wonder material. Here “graphene based” means that graphene flakes have been utilized as the base material to produce the ink without adding any binder material. The main purpose of adding binder materials in conductive inks is to improve the conductivity of the final product. But it has to be treated at a high temperature of 250 °C, called annealing. Due to this high temperature annealing process, substrate materials such as plastic or paper get destroyed. Therefore, the choice of substrate materials becomes limited. Hence, graphene based conductive inks which are binder-free are advantageous concerning low temperature processing but have the disadvantage of low conductivity, which is subject to improvement.

To achieve an enhancement in the conductivity of binder-free materials, the rolling compression method is adopted. This requires no high temperature annealing in the fabrication process. Moreover, it can be used in industrial-level screen printing. The conductance of the prototype can be increased more than 4 times compared to that of pure graphene. The conductivity is significantly improved and is enough for RF application. The rolling compression method alleviates the limitation of substrate material choice and the production cost significantly reduces. Thus binder-free graphene-based ink and screen printing are a perfect duo for high-throughput and mass production of low-cost RFID tag antennas.148 It is speculated that graphene screen printed RFID tags possess the potential of making a revolution in the RFID industry.

Conclusion

This review unfolds the possibility for low cost RFID sensors by the staggering material graphene. The basic and electromagnetic properties of monolayer graphene, printed graphene and graphene oxide (GO) are investigated and applied to fabricate 2D material frequency tunable antennas, RFID antennas, humidity sensing applications and wireless communication systems. RF attenuator transmission lines are designed by tunable conductivity of monolayer graphene under different voltages. The variable conductivity of graphene biased by an external voltage results in its tunability. The working frequency of the antenna can be altered by applying graphene switches which are responsible for varying the effective length of the antennas. A single frequency reconfigurable dipole antenna is developed and gradually a dual frequency reconfigurable antenna is introduced.

Different experiments and full electromagnetic wave simulations have been applied to analyse the potential for printed graphene applications and the possibility to merge with other 2D materials such as GO. It is evident that the electrical permittivity of GO in GHz enhances with increasing water uptake from humidity. An RFID humidity sensor is developed by tagging a printed graphene RFID tag and coated GO layer. The backscattered phase from the reflection of the RFID sensor can be measured and analysed by a commercial RFID reader to retrieve the surrounding humidity information.

The tagging of graphene and other 2D materials for health, environment sensing or tunable RF devices has ample scope to be further investigated. The RFID enabled sensing idea could be more exclusively used in monitoring applications. Printed graphene NFC coil antennas have a huge application. More research is needed to enhance the performance, along with integration of the design with sensors. NFC could play an important role in wearable electronics due to its simplicity. There are various IC sensors such as temperature and humidity sensors that could integrate with NFC tags. More bits need to be encoded to explore the possibility in commercial applications. Printed graphene antennas and systems for RFID applications are often complicated these days. Also in terms of the performance, the resonant peak is not too convincing yet. Intense investigation is still needed here.

The low cost wireless security network decorated with printed graphene is a successful example of a communication system built based on printed graphene UHF antennas. The next challenge is to bring NFC designs and compact designs into the system. More variations of RFID applications using printed graphene can be commercially adopted for IoT applications in various aspects like smart anticounterfeiting and alarms. An ecosystem built with low cost printed graphene could occur in the near future. Printed tags and antennas can be used in ticketing services and low cost wearable electronics and can be easily disposed of after use as they are non-hazardous. The RFID application is highly helpful to resolve the current environmental problems and will be green impact on the ecosystem as well.
