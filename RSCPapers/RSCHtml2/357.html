Abstract

Because of their ultimate thickness, layered structure and high flexibility, pn junctions based on layered two-dimensional semiconductors have been attracting increasing attention recently. In this study, for the first time, we fabricated lateral pn junctions (LPNJs) based on ultrathin MoTe2 by introducing two separated electrostatic back gates, and investigated their electronic and photovoltaic performance. Pn, np, nn, and pp junctions can be easily realized by modulating the conductive channel type using gate voltages with different polarities. Strong rectification effects were observed in the pn and np junctions and the rectification ratio reached âˆ¼5 Ã— 104. Importantly, we find a unique phenomenon that the parameters for MoTe2 LPNJs experience abrupt changes during the transition from p to n or n to p. Furthermore, a high performance photovoltaic device with a filling factor of above 51% and electrical conversion efficiency (Î·) of around 0.5% is achieved. Our findings are of importance to comprehensively understand the electronic and optoelectronic properties of MoTe2 and may further open up novel electronic and optoelectronic device applications.

Introduction

Semiconductor pâ€“n junctions (PNJs) are the basic building blocks for application in electronics and optoelectronics. Recently, PNJs constructed from atomically thin two-dimensional (2D) layered semiconducting materials have attracted considerable attention.1â€“3 In contrast to traditional PNJs, these kinds of PNJs are predicted to exhibit novel physics and high device performances in optoelectronics because of their ideal thickness, layer structure and high flexibility. To explore the native physics and open up new applications, wide varieties of PNJs have been initially studied, such as MoS2/WSe2, MoSe2/WSe2, WS2/WSe2, MoS2/GaTe, MoS2/BP (BP: black phosphorous) and so forth.4â€“12 Prototype devices like photodetectors, solar cells and light emission diodes have also been demonstrated. Based on the device configurations, PNJs can be categorized into vertical and lateral PNJs (VPNJs and LPNJs). The former are stacks of different ultrathin 2D layered materials held together by relatively weak van der Waals (vdWs) forces and the latter are formed by the seamless ultrathin 2D layered â€œpâ€ parts and â€œnâ€ counterparts being interconnected by strong in-plane covalent bonding. Since a VPNJ is held together by weak vdWs forces, there is a vdWs gap between its â€œpâ€ and â€œnâ€ parts. This structural feature may cause some problems for carrier transport. In comparison with VPNJs, there is a more tight connection between the â€œpâ€ and â€œnâ€ parts in LPNJs where these connections are chemical bonds.

Generally, three methods have been developed to fabricate LPNJs. The first one is in situ chemical vapor deposition, which involves epitaxial growth at the edge of another pre-grown 2D monolayer material. For example, Duan et al., Huang et al. and Gong et al. reported the growth of lateral heterostructures: MoS2/MoSe2, WS2/WSe2 and WS2/MoS2, and MoSe2/WSe2, respectively.8,11,13 This method usually requires high lattice matching and similar chemical compositions between the two materials. The second method is patterned chemical doping in one individual material. For instance, Choi et al. established a LPNJ on an ultrathin MoS2 nanosheet via chemical doping in a selected area.14 This process requires complex chemical reactions and may introduce possible contamination. Electrostatically tuning LPNJs via two separated local gates is another efficient approach, in which ambipolar materials are highly desired. With the discovery of ambipolar 2D layered materials, such as WSe2, BP and MoSe2, LPNJs based on them have been realized in succession.15â€“19

Recently, 2H-MoTe2 has been found to be another important ambipolar material among the family of 2D layered transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs).20â€“27 Its monolayer is composed of hexagonal Mo sheets that are sandwiched between two hexagonal Te sheets and the monolayer thickness is around 0.7 nm, as shown in Fig. 1a. MoTe2 has two typical phases, i.e. semiconducting 2H and metallic 1Tâ€² phases. MoTe2 can be switched from the 2H to the 1Tâ€² phase using laser illumination or by changing the temperature.28,29 Its phase change is much easier to perform than that of other TMDCs such as WS2, which needs dangerous lithium intercalation chemistry.30 Utilizing this property, ohmic homojunction contacts can be obtained for high performance devices.29 In addition, 2H-MoTe2 has an indirect band gap of around 1 eV in the bulk form and a direct band gap of about 1.1 eV in the monolayer form at room temperature, which is the smallest among all semiconducting TMDCs.31 Lezama et al. have even reported that bilayer and trilayer MoTe2 are direct band gap semiconductors.32 This means that MoTe2 can absorb more sunlight and longer wavelengths for high performance optoelectronic devices and we do not have to synthesize monolayer MoTe2 to achieve a direct band gap. Importantly, previous research indicates that the mobility of ultrathin 2H-MoTe2 can surpass 10 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 without any optimization process, which is comparable to figures previously reported for MoS2.25 However, so far the optoelectronic properties, like the photoresponse and photovoltaic (PV) effects, of 2H-MoTe2 have not been reported yet and PNJs based on MoTe2 have not been investigated.

Here, we constructed a LPNJ based on an ultrathin 2H-MoTe2 nanosheet by introducing two separated back gates and, for the first time, investigated its photovoltaic performance. By tuning the two back gate voltages, we can efficiently obtain pn, np, nn, and pp Iâ€“V characteristics. Clear rectification effects were observed in the pn and np junctions. The ideality factor of m â‰ˆ 2 for both pn and np configurations indicates that the current is mostly dominated by recombination rather than diffusion.19 Further, by carefully investigating the rectification Iâ€“V characteristics at various back gate voltages, we find that all of the parameters have an abrupt change, when the conductive type changes from n to p or p to n. This possibly arises from the joint effects of carrier densities, channel resistance, and pn junction barrier height when tuning the gate voltages. Under the illumination of a 473 nm laser, ultrathin 2H-MoTe2 LPNJs display unique PV properties, such as an open-circuit voltage, short-circuit current, high photogenerated power and high PV efficiency as well.

Results and discussion

Raman spectroscopy measurements were performed using a 532 nm laser, and are shown in Fig. 1b. The MoTe2 ultrathin flake shows several obvious Raman active modes between 100 cmâˆ’1 and 300 cmâˆ’1: the out-of-plane A1g at 171 cmâˆ’1, the in-plane E2g1 at 232 cmâˆ’1 and the out-of-plane B2g1 at 289 cmâˆ’1. These Raman features match those observed in 2H-MoTe2 very well, thus confirming that the MoTe2 crystal belongs to the 2H phase.25 The peak intensity ratio of B2g1/E2g1 is calculated to be around 0.15. According to the relationship between the Raman intensity ratio and the number of layers reported by Nakaharai et al.,22 the MoTe2 thickness shown in the inset of Fig. 1b is estimated to be âˆ¼8 layers.

To investigate the electrical properties of 2H-MoTe2, a simple back-gated two-terminal device has been fabricated. The inset in Fig. 1c shows an AFM image of the MoTe2 device. Fig. 4a presents the representative Iâ€“V curves based on the np (Vg1 = 5 V) junction at various Vg2 under 473 nm laser illumination. We next explore the PV effects under laser illumination with various power densities (P) from 284.0 to 15.8 mW cmâˆ’2. Fig. 4b presents the Iâ€“V curves of the np junction (Vg1 = 5 V and Vg1 = âˆ’5 V) under various light power densities. Fig. 4c shows the extracted electrical powers (Pel) as a function of Vds. As we can see, Isc follows a power-law dependence with P, namely, Isc = PÎ±.19,37,38 Here, Î± is an indicator of what kind of recombination mechanism dominates the carrier loss in photovoltaic devices. When Î± = 0.5 and 1, the carrier losses in the device are controlled by pure bimolecular recombination processes and pure monomolecular recombination mechanisms, respectively.37 Our results show that Î± = 1.23, which is close to one. So the carrier losses in MoTe2 photovoltaic devices are dominated by monomolecular recombination (i.e. SRH recombination). Meanwhile, the relatively large deviation from one indicates that there is more complex recombination in the device. Another device shows Î± = 1.15 (see Fig. S5 in ESIâ€ ). According to the planar model proposed by D. Cheyns et al.,39 the Voc follows a power-law dependence (dVoc/dlog(P) = 2.3nvoc(kBT/e)), where kB is the Boltzmann constant, T is the Kelvin temperature and e is the electron charge. nvoc is the factor determining the recombination processes for the electrons and holes at the interface. By fitting Voc with log(P) as shown in Fig. 4d, we obtain nvoc is 2.8. Generally, nvoc = 2 is considered as an indicator that Shockleyâ€“Readâ€“Hall recombination dominates the carrier loss during transport.10 So in electrostatically tuned MoTe2 pn junctions, there are possibly more complex recombination processes. Pmaxel approaches its maximum at around 7.6 pW. FF is nearly independent of the light power density and reaches its maximum at about 51%. This value is comparable to most of the others based on this kind of device structure.19 In addition, the gap distance between the two separated back gates may significantly affect the device performance. When we increase the gap distance up to 2 Î¼m, a better PV performance can be obtained (see Fig. S6 in ESIâ€ ).

Conclusions

In summary, for the first time we have constructed ultrathin MoTe2 LPNJs by fabricating two separated electrostatic back gates and we have systematically investigated their electrical and PV performances. The channel conductive type can be efficiently modulated by applying different polarity gate voltages, which is a benefit of the ambipolar properties of MoTe2. Strong rectification effects were observed in the pn and np junctions and the RF approaches âˆ¼5 Ã— 104. The ideality factor of the pn diode is calculated to be âˆ¼2, which indicates that the forward current is mostly dominated by recombination processes. Importantly, we find that the parameters for electrostatically tuned MoTe2 LPNJs experience abrupt changes during the transition from p to n or n to p. The ultrathin MoTe2 LPNJs display unique PV properties. FF approaches 51% and the electrical conversion efficiency reaches 0.5%, which are both comparable to most reported values based on this kind of device configuration. Our findings are of importance to comprehensively understand the electronic and optoelectronic properties of MoTe2 and further may open up a new pathway to novel electronic and optoelectronic device applications.

Experimental

To fabricate a back-gated FET device

MoTe2 ultrathin flakes were mechanically exfoliated from bulk MoTe2 (99.995%, HQ Graphene) using a standard Scotch tape method and transferred to a heavily doped p-type substrate covering a 300 nm thick thermal oxidation layer. The electrical contacts were defined using e-beam lithography (EBL) and then Cr/Au (8 nm/60 nm) was deposited using a thermal evaporation machine.

To fabricate the MoTe2 device with two separated local back gates

Fig. 2a illustrates the scheme of the device. Firstly, the EBL is used to prepattern a pair of local back gates separated by 200 nm and then a 3 nm/10 nm thick Cr/Au thin film is deposited. Secondly, a pair of 8 nm/80 nm thick Cr/Au electrodes respectively connected one of the prepatterned local back gates to the other as the Vg1 and Vg2 electrodes. Thirdly, the above Cr/Au patterns were covered by 20 nm HfO2 as the dielectric layer using an Atomic Layer Deposition machine (ALD, KMT-100O3, KE-Micro). Fourthly, the ultrathin MoTe2 flake is transferred onto the prepatterned separated local back gates using a wet transfer method.40 Finally, source and drain contacts (Cr/Au, 8 nm/80 nm) were finished using e-beam lithography and thermal metal evaporation.

Characterization and measurements

Confocal Raman spectroscopy (Renishaw Invia, 532 nm excitation laser) was used to collect the Raman signals of MoTe2. The morphology of MoTe2 was examined using atomic force microscopy (AFM, Veeco Multimode), and scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Hitachi S-4800). All of the electrical transport measurements were conducted on a probe station (Lakeshore, TTPX) equipped with a semiconductor characterization system (Keithley 4200) under vacuum (âˆ¼6.0 Ã— 10âˆ’5 torr) at room temperature, if not particularly indicated. The photovoltaic experiments were measured using a 473 nm laser (RGBLase).
