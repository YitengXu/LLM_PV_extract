Abstract

The synthesis, characterization and catalytic capability of the NiWâ€“nano-hydroxyapatite (NiWâ€“nHA) composite were investigated in this paper. The NiWâ€“nHA catalyst was prepared by a co-precipitation method. Then Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy, transmission electron microscopy (TEM), X-ray diffraction (XRD) and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDX) were used to analyze this material. In addition, the catalytic capacity of the NiWâ€“nHA composite was also examined by FT-IR and gas chromatography (GC). The results of FT-IR analysis indicated that Ni, W and nHA combined closely. TEM observation revealed that this catalyst was needle shaped and the crystal retained a nanometer size. XRD data also suggested that a new phase of CaWO4 appeared and the lattice parameters of nHA changed in this system. nHA was the carrier of metals. The rates of Ni/W-loading were 73.24% and 65.99% according to the EDX data, respectively. Furthermore, the conversion of 91.88% Jatropha oil was achieved at 360 Â°C and 3 MPa hâˆ’1 over NiWâ€“nHA catalyst. The straight chain alkanes ranging from C15 to C18 were the main components in the production. The yield of C15â€“C18 alkanes was up to 83.56 wt%. The reaction pathway involved hydrocracking of the CC bonds of these triglycerides from Jatropha oil. This paper developed a novel non-sulfided catalyst to obtain a â€œgreen biofuelâ€ from vegetable oil.

Introduction

With the development of renewable energy sources, wind power,1 solar cell,2 fuel cell3 and biomass4 have proved to be the most important. Biofuel5 could be used directly in compression ignition engines without modifications.6 It is attracting more and more attention. Using vegetable oil as liquid biofuel has several advantages over other alternative fuel options. The vegetable oils are renewable resources in nature.7â€“9 It is also cheap and environment-friendly for green fuel.10 In particular, Jatropha11,12 easily grows in almost any wasteland, even on sandy, saline soils, or stony and gravelly ground. This plant could yield much more biofuel per hectare than those from soybean, sunflower, and canola.13

Hydrotreatment has been developed to produce the straight chain alkanes from the fatty acid triglycerides of Jatropha oil.14 The catalyst of the reaction is critical. Metal sulfide catalysts (Niâ€“Mo,15 Niâ€“W,16 and Niâ€“La17) have been widely used for vegetable oil hydroprocessing, while commonly used carriers are SiO2,18 Al2O3,19 TiO220 or a combination thereof.21 However, these traditional carriers are limited due to their small poor activity.22 In addition, to improve cracking performance, the conventional catalysts are commonly sulfided, which would bring great harm to the environment, human health and instrument.10,23,24 Therefore, it is necessary to design a novel catalyst that has excellent hydrocracking activity without presulfurization.

Biofuels produced by nano-materials have significant advantages over the conventional methods.25,26 Nanosized materials27â€“29 offer different morphology and enhanced surface area that could yield better reaction rates. Nanosized hydroxyapatite (HA, Ca10(PO4)6(OH)2) is widely used in biomaterials due to its biocompatibility and bioactivity.30â€“33 It is the main inorganic component of the hard tissues and providing direct chemical bonds with Ca2+, OHâˆ’ and PO43âˆ’ groups.34 HA is the most basic calcium orthophosphate,35 which is insoluble in neutral and basic medium. Reported data36â€“38 indicate that nHA could adsorb divalent metals.39 The mechanisms of the cation retention include: ion exchange,40,41 absorption,42 precipitation,43 and surface complexation.44 In particular, the Caâ€“I site of nHA is preferred energetically for divalent metal substitution, such as Zn2+, Mg2+, Pb2+, Cu2+, Ba2+, Cd2+ and so on.45 nHA could load much more metals in order to increase active sites. Thus, it is an ideal carrier substituted traditional supporter. Therefore, our intention is to prepare a non-sulfide catalyst that combines the favorable characteristics of nHA and hydrocracking activity of Ni and W. In the new system, nHA carries Ni and W, forming an integration of supporter and metal activity. This metal-based catalyst has potential for hydrocracking â€œgreen fuelâ€ from Jatropha oil.

In this study, a NiWâ€“nHA composite was prepared by a co-precipitation method without presulfurization. The properties of this catalyst were characterized by Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), X-ray diffraction (XRD), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX). In addition, we investigated hydrotreatment of Jatropha oil over NiWâ€“nHA catalyst. Hydrocracking assays were also discussed.

Experimental

Preparation of materials

NiWâ€“nHA composite was synthesized by a co-precipitation method. All the chemical reagents used in this work were analytic reagent (AR). Briefly, calcium nitrate (Ca(NO3)2), nickel nitrate (Ni(NO3)2), ammonium metatungstate hydrate ((NH4)6W7O24) and trisodium phosphate (Na3PO4) were dissolved in water. Then the Na3PO4 solution was titrated into the [Caâ€“Niâ€“W] solution at a Ca/P molar ratio of 1.67. The titrated solution was vigorously stirred and the pH was adjusted to 10 by ammonia solution (NH4Â·OH). In addition, reaction temperature was kept at 25 Â°C. After titration, the stirring was continued for 5 hours and then the obtained slurry was aged for another 24 hours. Finally, the precipitate was filtered and washed with deionized water. Lastly, the calcination was carried out by sintering at 400 Â°C for 6 h.

For control, nHA was prepared by a wet method.46

Material characterization

Chemical analysis of the composite was carried out by a Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrophotometer (FTIR 650) in the range from 4000 cmâˆ’1 to 400 cmâˆ’1 at 1.5 cmâˆ’1 resolution averaging 32 scans.

The microstructure of the NiWâ€“nHA composite was observed with a transmission electron microscope (TEM, JME-100CX).

XRD was used to determine the structure of the catalyst. These measurements were performed with a Empyrean XRD analyzer (Cu KÎ±) and PIXcel3D detector from PANalytical Company. These samples were measured in the 2Î¸ range from 5Â° to 80Â° (scan speed of 0.02Â° per second).

Catalyst assay

Jatropha oil was purchased from Jiangsu Donghu Bioenergy Co. Ltd. The reaction was carried out in a fixed-bed reactor (FBR) at 360 Â°C and 400 Â°C, 3 MPa, LHSV (liquid hourly space velocity) of 1 hâˆ’1 and a ratio of H2 to oil in feed of 500 mL mLâˆ’1. The hydrocarbon yields and products were collected in a trap and were characterized by FT-IR and gas chromatography (GC). The gas-phase products were collected once for every reaction condition and analyzed offline using a standard three-column GC setup with flame ionization and thermal conductivity detectors enabling the detection of both permanent gases and hydrocarbons at the same time.

TEM observation

The TEM image (Fig. 2) illustrated that the NiWâ€“nHA composite has a uniform and ultrafine microstructure with an average grain size of 70â€“90 nm in length and 5â€“25 nm in diameter, with no glassy or amorphous interface along the grain boundaries. It was deduced that the three phases formed strong bonding in the catalyst. The EDX results of the NiWâ€“nHA composite catalyst revealed that the atomic percent of Ni and W elements were 3.75 and 1.96 (Fig. 3), respectively, while their maximum theoretical loading percentages were 5.12 and 2.97, respectively. In addition, these nano-catalysts were capable of quickly responding to the surrounding environment. Thus, the synthesized NiWâ€“nHA catalyst overcame the drawbacks, which would strongly affect the catalytic performance.

Sustainable production of biofuel is an important scientific challenge for the environmental problems resulting from the extensive use of fossil fuels.50â€“52 Hence, more and more scientists have attempted to develop a novel biofuel with (i) greater greenhouse gas (GHG) emission reduction in the complete life cycle in comparison with current biofuels53 and (ii) non-food biomass raw materials.54 A large number of cheap and environment-friendly vegetable oils could be developed for â€œgreen biofuelâ€.53 In particular, Jatropha oil, it is a candidate source. Refined Jatropha oil requires the removal of oxygen to obtain hydrocarbons by hydrotreatment.

The result of catalyst assay revealed that the complete conversion of triglycerides from Jatropha oil can be achieved over NiWâ€“nHA catalysts at 400 Â°C. During the hydrocracking of Jatropha oil, initially, the CC bonds of triglycerides were hydrogenated over the metal phases. This reaction at a low temperature (180â€“250 Â°C) formed solid waxes without liquid products by hydrogenation, which was the first step. Then, the deoxygenation of triglycerides occurred on the metal phases as the second step. nHA also played an important role in this stage. As we know, P was classified as a secondary promoter in NiMoW-based catalysts.55,56 The P affected on the BrÃ¸nsted acidity of the carrier without presulfurization. Impregnation of phosphorous prior to the metal phases led to the most active catalysts for hydrotreatment. Meanwhile, P maintained the high surface area of catalyst during the reaction.57 Thus optimum loadings of P improved the longevity of the catalyst. In the NiWâ€“nHA catalyst, P of nHA could be effective in enhancing the selectivity of the catalysts, which resulted in excellent hydrocracking. According to GC and FT-IR data, all of the oxygen atoms in triglycerides were removed to form H2O, CO, and CO2 by reduction, decarbonylation, and decarboxylation (seen in Scheme 1). The liquid products were n-C18H38, n-C17H36, n-C16H34 and n-C15H32. These products possessed high melting points, which was responsible for the high pour point of the product from the NiWâ€“nHA composite. In addition, there was no need for pretreatments and there were no side reactions. The pretreatments and no side reactions could decrease the efficiency of biofuel. When they were eliminated, the efficiency would increase sharply.

In the NiWâ€“nHA composite system, nHA provided not only a carrier, but also active sites. And the P of nHA could avoid aggregation of activities and improve the efficiency of hydrotreatment. Of course, excessive P content may deteriorate the performance of this multi-phase catalyst. Therefore, further research should focus on the balance of weight ratio between nHA and metal activities.

Conclusions

A co-precipitation method was used to prepare a nanometer-sized NiWâ€“nHA catalyst. Ni ions entered the n-HA crystal lattice and a new phase of CaWO4 was formed. The Ni/W-loadings were 73.24% and 65.99% in this system, respectively. In addition, the NiWâ€“nHA composite showed excellent efficiency of catalyst. A yield of 83.56 wt% and a conversion of 91.88 wt% were achieved at 360 Â°C over this catalyst. As temperature increased, it achieved a 100% conversion rate. The reaction pathway involved hydrogenation of the CC bonds of these triglycerides from Jatropha oil. In all, this approach as used in the current study produced a composite with excellent hydrocracking properties. Therefore, the resulting nHA-based multi-metal system held great potential for a catalyst.
