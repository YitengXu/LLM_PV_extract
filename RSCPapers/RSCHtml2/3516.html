Abstract

CZTSSe absorbers are synthesized by a one-step annealing process from Cu, SnS and ZnS stacks prepared by sputtering deposition. CZTSSe thin films are fabricated by a selenization process using an optimized precursor stack structure. The selenization temperature has a strong influence on the phase and reactions of the elements in the films. CZTSSe formation is initially triggered by the interaction of Cu with Sn(S, Se) to form CuSn(S, Se) compounds, which then react with ZnS. CZTSSe films obtained at 590 Â°C exhibit a kesterite structure. Sulfo-selenization is performed using an optimized selenization process. Based on the mass ratio of the chalcogen sources, selenium disulfide and selenium, the effect of the S/(S + Se) compositional ratio on the structural and morphological properties of the as-grown films and the optoelectronic parameters of solar cells fabricated using these absorber films are studied. Using this single-step sulfo-selenization method, CZTSSe devices with conversion efficiencies up to 9.47% are obtained.

1. Introduction

Thin-film photovoltaic materials need to be cost effective and earth abundant and offer a long functional lifetime without significant reduction in the solar cell efficiency. From an industrial production point of view, Cu(In, Ga)Se2 (CIGS) has shown the best conversion efficiencies among photovoltaic materials both on the lab scale and for full-sized modules and therefore has better potential in the long run for use in the most cost-effective solar cells.1 However, the amount of the expensive rare metal indium used in the mass production stage of CIGS thin-film solar cells is troublesome.2 Indium resources are becoming increasingly scarce with the expansion of the display field. For these reasons, the need to replace indium has led to the investigation of materials with similar properties. Among these materials, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS), Cu2ZnSnSe4 (CZTSe) and sulfo-selenide Cu2ZnSn(S, Se)4 (CZTSSe) thin films are the most promising photovoltaic absorbers to replace CIGS because they are composed of abundant and inexpensive elements and have a very high absorption coefficient of over 104 cmâˆ’1 and a direct bandgap energy of 1.5 eV.3 The theoretical solar conversion efficiency of CZTS-based thin film solar cells is as high as 32.2%.4 However, regarding the electrical characteristics of devices based on these materials, the maximum reported conversion efficiencies are still far from those achieved with more mature CIGS technologies. A key parameter impacting the increasing photovoltaic efficiencies in CZTSSe solar cells is bandgap control of the absorber through the control of the S/(S + Se) ratio in CZTSSe thin films. The well-renowned hydrazine process led to the world record efficiency for CZTSSe of 12.6% after proper optical design and control of the S/(S + Se) ratio.5 Various physical and chemical techniques, such as RF magnetron sputtering,6 thermal evaporation,7 pulsed laser deposition,8 nanocrystal coating,3 hybrid solution-particle coating,5 and sulfurization of electron beam-evaporated precursors,9 have been used for the synthesis of CZTSSe thin films for solar cell application. However, methods for precisely controlling the chalcogen composition in the absorber layer are rarely reported. In general, CZTS and the related selenide CZTSe and CZTSSe thin films have been prepared through different routes, including the reaction of metal or sulfide precursors in a sulfur or selenium atmosphere and single-stage physical vapour deposition. Nevertheless, freely adjusting the composition of the S/(S + Se) ratio in CZTSSe thin films using these methods is difficult.

In addition, the characteristics of CZTS, CZTSe and CZTSSe thin films and devices produced by a two-step process are greatly affected by the stacked structure of the precursor.10â€“15 In the case of our selenization annealing process, we chose a Cu/SnS/ZnS/Mo/glass stacked structure, which afforded CZTS and CZTSe with good crystallinity.10â€“12

We previously prepared CZTSSe thin films from sulfur compound precursors under an elemental selenium atmosphere in a sealed tube.12 The merits of this technique are as follows: (1) introducing Se and S into the precursor films leads to good uniformity of the films. (2) Deviation of the resulting compositions from those of the precursors can be suppressed through the thermal selenization process. (3) CZTSSe films without noticeable voids can be obtained. However, previous studies did not show free control of S/(S + Se) because the S originated not from a chalcogen vapour source but from a SnS and ZnS precursor stack. One method to prepare pentenary compounds from metal or sulfide precursors is to submit precursors to a multistep chalcogenization process, such as a two-step selenization/sulfurization growth process. Although the multistep reaction process can enable control of the S/(S + Se) ratio in the CZTSSe film, controlling the electrical properties and reaction growth of the absorber layers in our experiments is difficult. These reaction processes could require a large amount of process time and equipment. Thus, a single-step thermal treatment capable of introducing both sulfur and selenium is of interest. Some reports on single-step sulfo-selenization processes for Cuâ€“Znâ€“Sn precursors have been presented, but little is known about the effect of the process parameters on chalcogen incorporation.15â€“17

In this work, pentenary CZTSSe thin films are synthesized by a one-step sulfo-selenization process using a metal and sulfur compound hybrid precursor, Cu/SnS/ZnS/Mo/glass, deposited by RF and DC magnetron sputtering. We show the possibility of tuning the S/(S + Se) ratio to form CZTSSe films with increased sulfur content. We also demonstrate the ability to tune the S/(S + Se) ratio by varying the amount of each chalcogen placed in the thermal process chamber. The sulfo-selenization process can be performed in a pressurized chamber at a pressure of 1.2 atm. The S and Se vapour in the chamber of the heat treatment equipment is designed to be well trapped in the precursor sample holder, with the internal gas flow stagnating above atmospheric pressure, allowing the S/Se contents to be controlled by maintaining the reactivity. In our thermal process system, minimization of vapour source exhaustion and easy substitution with chalcogens in CZTSSe films are possible.

2. Experimental section

The precursor metal films were fabricated on molybdenum-coated SLG substrates. The metal and sulfide precursor layers were sequentially deposited by sputtering 99.99% pure Cu, ZnS, and SnS targets. The metal and sulfide precursors with Cu/SnS/ZnS sequences were formed on the Mo/SLG substrate by RF/DC sputtering at room temperature. The layers were deposited under sputtering powers of 150 W, 200 W, and 200 W for the Cu (DC power), SnS (RF power/Sn:S = 1:1) and ZnS (RF power/Zn:S = 1:1) targets, respectively, at a working pressure of 3 mTorr in an Ar atmosphere. The thickness of each layer of the precursor was adjusted to control the final thickness and composition ratio of the CZTSSe film. Each layer of the precursor was controlled to obtain a Cu:SnS:ZnS thickness ratio of 1:2.6:2.1. The metal-sulfide compound precursors were reacted and annealed in a nearly sealed halogen furnace with a heating block pushâ€“pull sample holder in the quartz chamber that enabled the precursor samples to be inserted or removed from the hot reaction zone. The selenium for the selenization process was placed under the precursor sample jig. The SeS2 addition method for the sulfo-selenization process was the same as that for the selenization process. To provide sufficient Se vapour and prevent severe decomposition of the CZTSSe films, the selenization processes were carried out in an Ar-filled furnace at 1 atm. A relief valve was attached to the end of the furnace to maintain the system pressure at slightly above 1.2 atm regardless of the furnace temperature. Before the annealing process, the halogen furnace was evacuated to a low 10âˆ’3 torr pressure to remove moisture and impurities. Then, Ar gas was introduced into the furnace, and the pressure was allowed to reach atmospheric pressure. Then, the heat treatment process was carried out, and the detailed heat treatment profile will be described in the results section. To fabricate solar cells, the obtained absorber layers were covered with a 50 nm-thick CdS buffer layer by chemical bath deposition (see S1â€ ). Then, a 50 nm intrinsic ZnO layer and a 300 nm Al-doped ZnO layer were deposited by RF sputtering. Finally, a 1 Î¼m Al collection grid was deposited on top of the device by thermal evaporation.

The samples were characterized by means of a solar simulator, external quantum efficiency (EQE) measurements, inductively coupled plasma (ICP) emission spectrometry, depth-resolved Raman spectroscopy, and capacitanceâ€“voltage (Câ€“V) measurements of the charge density (NCV). The Iâ€“V characteristics were measured under a simulated air mass 1.5 global (AM 1.5 G) spectrum and 100 mW cmâˆ’2 illumination (1 sun) using a solar simulator (Newport Co., model 94022A). The EQE values were obtained using an SR 830 DSP lock-in amplifier system (McScience Co.). ICP emission spectrometry (Shimadzu Co., model ICPS-8100) and STEM-EDS (Bruker Co., model QUANTAX-200) measurements were performed to analyse the compositions of the films. Raman spectroscopy measurements were performed using the 632.8 nm line of a Heâ€“Ne laser as the excitation source. The Câ€“V characteristics (Keysight Agilent, model E4980) were measured using 50 mV and 100 kHz AC excitation with a DC bias from âˆ’4.5 V to 0 V.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Growth of CZTSSe thin films by the selenization annealing process

In our previous work, we reported the growth of large-grained CZTSSe thin films using a fully sealed tube furnace with a SiC heating element.12 The fabricated CZTSSe thin films could be successfully produced in our previous work by vapour-phase selenization of stacked precursors prepared by sequential vacuum sputtering deposition of Cu, SnS and ZnS. In this paper, we introduced a halogen furnace with a square quartz system. For large-area chalcogenization, we changed our thermal treatment system from a cylinder-type tube furnace to a square and planar-type rapid thermal processing (RTP) system, and we developed a new base process for obtaining highly crystalline CZTSSe films without voids and a double layer in the absorber layer. Ensuring the heating profile in the selenization process, which causes volume expansion of the film from the sputtered precursor, is important. A K-type thermocouple was attached outside the chamber, and heat was applied by a PID controller. When the temperature was controlled through an external thermocouple, the actual internal temperature of the chamber was different, so the internal temperature was measured at the same time. The externally applied thermal profile in the selenization process and the actual internal temperature measured at this time are shown in Fig. 1. The surface of the K-type thermocouple could be easily selenized, so it was attached to the outside to improve the heat treatment reproducibility.

3.2 Fabrication of CZTSSe thin films by the sulfo-selenization annealing process

We used the optimized heating profile in our selenization process to control the composition of S/(S + Se) in the absorber layer. That is, the sulfo-selenization annealing process was performed under the optimum heating profile in Fig. 1 and atmospheric pressure, as determined from the pure selenization process. However, unlike the pure selenization process, the sulfo-selenization process involved the addition of SeS2 powder. To verify the predicted dependence of the chalcogen composition, we developed a single-step sulfo-selenization procedure to prepare CZTSSe thin films. 4. Conclusions

We carried out detailed studies on the crystal growth mechanism of CZTSSe films prepared from sputter-deposited metal and compound precursors and on the possibility of obtaining CZTSSe films with a wide range of S/(S + Se) compositions using a single-step sulfo-selenization annealing process. The selenization temperature has a strong influence on the phase and reactions of the elements in the films. The formation of CZTSSe is initially triggered by the interaction of Cu with Sn(S, Se) to form CuSn(S, Se) compounds, which then react with ZnS. The CZTSSe films obtained at 580 Â°C exhibit a kesterite structure. We also fabricated CZTSSe thin films and devices with varying S/(S + Se) compositions by adding SeS2 powder in an optimized selenization process. In the sulfo-selenization process, sulfurization easily occurs with a small amount of sulfur, so optimization of the chalcogen sources used in the process is essential. SeS2 has an advantage over S in bandgap control because it is less reactive than S and can further suppress the sulfurization of the film. We fabricated CZTSSe thin films and devices with controlled S/(S + Se) ratios by controlling the amount of SeS2. We obtained a device efficiency of 9.47% through the fabrication of a bandgap-tuned thin film and examined the changes in the characteristics with S/(S + Se). This study confirmed that the S/(S + Se) ratio in the film can be easily controlled by controlling the chalcogen sources. For higher efficiency, S and Se appear to be required to fabricate CZTSSe thin films with different depth distributions. If the S and Se sources are controlled according to the time conditions, then the depth distribution of the S/Se ratio of the absorber layer is expected to be adjustable.
