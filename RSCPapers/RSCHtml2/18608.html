Abstract

As a potential energy carrier, hydrogen has surged up the priority list as part of broader decarbonization efforts and strategies to build or acquire clean energy economies. Driven by renewable electricity, electrochemical water splitting (WS) promises an ideal long-term, low-carbon way to produce hydrogen, with the ability to tackle various critical energy challenges. To improve the efficiency of electrocatalytic water splitting, electrocatalysts with enhanced conductivity, more exposed active sites, and high intrinsic activity are crucial for decreasing the energy gap for the rate-determining step (RDS) and subsequently improving the conversion efficiency. The incorporation of multidimensional imperfections has been demonstrated to be efficient for modulating the electron distribution and speeding up the electrocatalysis kinetics during electrocatalytic processes and this is now attracting ever-increasing attention. Herein, in this review, we summarize recent progress relating to the regulation of electrical behavior and electron distributions for the optimization of electrocatalytic water-splitting performance via defect engineering. With an emphasis on the beneficial aspects of the hydrogen economy and an in-depth understanding of electron redistribution caused by defect effects, we offer a comprehensive summary of the progress made in the last three to five years. Finally, we also offer future perspectives on the challenges and opportunities relating to water-splitting electrocatalysts in this attractive field.

Broader context

Versatile hydrogen is deemed to be a crucial energy carrier that can facilitate the decarbonization of hard-to-change sectors and the transition of the global energy system, and it is now enjoying unprecedented momentum due to interest from policy makers and commercial companies. Electrocatalysts with cost-effectiveness, high activity, and long-term stability hold promise for further reductions in the cost of green hydrogen and the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis technology. The incorporation of imperfections with different dimensions into the perfect lattices of electrocatalysts induces significant perturbations of electronic structures and advantageous binding behavior with key intermediates, as well as lowered energy barriers for rate-determining steps. Here, in this review, we offer a comprehensive summary of recent advances achieved relating to defect-featuring electrocatalysts via defect engineering for the enhancement of the electrochemical conversion of water. Special attention is paid to advanced characterization methods for gaining an in-depth understanding of the electronic effects induced by the imperfections. Then, we offer perspectives and future challenges relating to this promising field from our point of view.

1. Introduction

Anthropogenic CO2 emission has caused catastrophic consequences, including but not limited to climate change, sea-level rise, and damage to human health and food security, which has triggered the worldwide transition towards carbon-free or carbon-negative technologies.1–8 Achieving the goals set by the Paris Agreement, which states a limited increase in global temperature to 1.5–2 °C in comparison with the pre-industrial mean by the end of this century, requires an efficient transformation of the global energy system from the traditional fossil-fuel-based one to a renewable-energy-based scenario.9–13 Powered by renewable electricity, hydrogen (H2) generated from at-scale commercial electrolyzers offers the feasibility of integrating variable renewable energies (VREs), including non-transportable solar power, wind power, tidal power, wave power, and run-of-the-river hydroelectricity, into the energy grid and creating a new downstream market for renewable power at the same time, leading to unprecedented political and business momentum for research into clean hydrogen.14–17 Given the present fact that today nearly all H2 is produced from fossil-fuel-based technologies (mainly natural gas and coal), the manufacture of H2 from low/neutral-carbon energy holds the key to the widespread deployment of H2 during the global energy revolution, which requires a further decline in the production cost.17 Broadly, the cost-competitiveness of hydrogen derived from renewable electricity can be secured by combining high electrolyzer utilization rates and low-price renewable electricity, which necessitates the exploitation of low-cost, eco-friendly electrocatalysts with desired intrinsic activity, long-term stability under harsh operating conditions, and fair price of the stock.16,18–20

It is well documented that the water cycle consists of a hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) on the cathode and an oxygen evolution reaction (OER) on the anode of the electrolyzer with a theoretical change in the Gibbs free energy (ΔG) of ∼237.2 kJ mol−1, which can be converted to a thermodynamical potential of 1.23 V.21 Principally, the HER involves two-electron transfer while the electron transfer number is four in the case of the OER process, which causes sluggish reaction kinetics and induces large energy barriers, thus slowing down the electrocatalytic overall water-splitting kinetics.22,23 Currently, platinum (Pt)-based nanomaterials represent state-of-art HER electrocatalysts, while iridium (Ir)-based electrocatalysts are used for the counterpart electrocatalytic OER process because of their high intrinsic activity and long-term durability.18,21 However, the scarcity and expense of noble metals severely hamper the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis technology, which necessitates the importance of alternatives.6,7,24–27 In the quest for the desired electrocatalysts, noble metals, carbides, oxides, hydroxides, sulfides, phosphides, and selenides have been proposed as potential alternatives for the electrocatalytic HER, OER, and overall water-splitting processes.8,18–20,22–24,28–31

Despite the high intrinsic activity of electrocatalysts, various strategies, including but not limited to the incorporation of imperfections, generation of strain, alloying with other species, and hybridization with foreign substances, have been adopted to modify the chemical (composition, valent state, etc.), physical (conductivity, surface area, etc.), electronic structure (band alignment, density of states, etc.), and surface properties (exposure of facets, unsaturated sites, etc.) of the electrocatalysts, aiming for further improvement in the electrocatalytic activity of the electrocatalysts.6,7,26,32–36 Among the multitudinous options, defect engineering, which features the intentional introduction of imperfections into the perfect crystal lattice, has demonstrated its significance in tuning the surface composition, electronic structure, charge distribution, and conductivity of the electrocatalysts, and the local environment around the active sites, which is now thriving as a promising protocol for the optimization of electrocatalysts.37 Implantation of imperfections with multiple dimensions exhibits significant influences on the electron distribution around the active sites, which includes: (I) optimizing the adsorption behavior of the intermediates; (II) forming an extra band; (III) facilitating favorable electron transfer; (IV) shifting the d-band center and (V) lowering the kinetic energy gap of the rate-determining step (RDS), thus optimizing the electrocatalytic activity of the electrocatalysts towards water splitting.8,22,23,37,38 The importance of imperfections with regard to the modulation of the intrinsic activity of electrocatalysts has been intensively investigated, which necessitates a comprehensive summary of recent progress in hopes of the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis technology.

Herein, we intend to provide an overall review and discussion of the progress that has been achieved regarding defect-featuring catalysts from point defects to bulk imperfections, which are applied in the water-splitting reaction, including HER, OER, and overall water electrolysis. We begin by introducing some discussion of the world's energy status and the important role hydrogen plays in the world energy transition to meet the climate goals proposed in the Paris Agreement. The basic mechanisms of hydrogen evolution and oxygen evolution are briefly introduced afterwards. An in-depth comprehension of the defect-induced electronic effect in optimizing the electrocatalytic performance of the electrocatalysts follows after. Next, recent developments regarding the optimization of electrocatalysts via the construction of defect-featuring motifs towards versatile electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution, oxygen evolution, and water splitting will be discussed and summarized. Lastly, insights and perspectives on the challenges and opportunities of water-splitting electrocatalysts will be provided.

2.2 Current status of water electrolysis technology

As mentioned above, today's hydrogen generation depends heavily on fossil fuels (natural gas and coal), which account for over 95% of hydrogen production, causing tremendous CO2 emissions. Direct splitting of water molecules into oxygen and hydrogen has been demonstrated as an advantageous approach for the production of hydrogen, which serves as one of the most established technological options to replace its energy-intensive counterparts. Different electrolyzer configurations, including alkaline (ALK) electrolyzers, polymer electrolyte membranes (PEMs), and solid oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs), have been developed to meet the harsh requirements under industrial operating conditions. The ALK technology is fully mature and is deployed on an industrial scale for the generation of hydrogen. Compared with the mature ALK technology, PEM electrolysis is rapidly emerging and entering commercial applications for its advantageous flexibility and reactivity.16 The improved flexibility of PEM electrolyzers offers beneficial overall economics for power-to-hydrogen, which provides alternative revenue streams from multiple electricity markets as compensation for the high cost of the devices.16 The SOEC technology is a potential game-changing strategy because of its enhanced conversion efficiency in comparison with the ALK and PEM technologies.42 Furthermore, the SOEC electrolyzer offers the possibility of the direct production of synthesized gas from CO2 and steam.43,44 However, its low technological readiness level hampers its large-scale applications, and SOECs can only operate on the lab-scale and small demonstration scales. Herein, in this section, we will take the thriving PEM technology as an example to rationalize the development of high-performance electrocatalysts by analyzing the overall hydrogen costs.

Driven by renewable electricity, the electrocatalytic production of hydrogen by splitting water is now thriving as an alternative to energy-intensive traditional approaches. However, the high cost of the present electrolysis technology has hindered its at-scale application due to the continuing high price of industrial electricity. 2.3 Definition and classification of multidimensional defects

Compared with other energy carriers, hydrogen produced through the low/neutral-carbon approach is a preferential option for decarbonizing these hard-to-abate sectors by storing electrical energy through bond breakage and re-formation during the electrolysis process.17,22 Electrocatalysts with high electrical conductivity, the maximum number of active sites, and optimized electronic structures that facilitate the reduction of the kinetic energy barrier of RDS are in high demand for large-scale deployment of electrolysis technology for hydrogen generation.27,29,36,46 Tremendous approaches, including defect engineering, strain optimization, hybridization, and alloying, have been employed to satisfy the rigorous requirements for the operation of electrolyzers under real industrial conditions by tuning the chemical (composition, valent states, etc.), physical (conductivity, electron/charge transport resistance, etc.), electronic structure (band alignment, the density of states, etc.), and surface properties (exposure of facets, unsaturated sites, etc.) of the electrocatalysts.20,24,27,30,33,46–50 Among these, defect engineering, which refers to the intentional incorporation of lattice irregularities into the perfect periodicity of the matrix, stands out as an efficient strategy for the realization of these ambitious requirements because of its promising potential for manipulation and its significant impact on the electrocatalytic performance of the catalysts via an optimized electron distribution status for enhanced intrinsic activity, tuned surface composition for the greater exposure of active sites, enhanced conductivity for facile electron/charge transport and favorable adsorption/desorption of key intermediates due to the preferential micro-environment around the active centers.37,38,51

Crystallographic defects, which are defined as lattice interruptions of long-range periodicity, can be categorized into several types based on their dimensionality or geometry, as illustrated in Fig. 4. Point defects, i.e., zero-dimensional defects, are localized imperfections that occur only at or around one single lattice point in the perfect atomic or ionic periodicity of the crystal structure and are usually generated as a result of thermodynamic equilibrium or intentional incorporation. Typical point defects include vacancies (absence of atoms at the lattice points), substitutional atoms (foreign atoms at the lattice points), interstitial atoms (atoms that occupy the position where no atoms usually exist), Frenkel defects (paired vacancies and interstitial atoms), and Schottky defects (unoccupied anion and cation pairs). Line defects, namely one-dimensional defects, mainly exist in the form of dislocations, around which the atoms in the crystal lattice are misaligned. Typical dislocations include edge dislocations (usually caused by the termination of atoms in the middle of the crystal lattice), screw dislocations (a topological defect of the crystal lattice in which a helical path is traced around the line defects), and mixed dislocations, as the results of the solidification process of the materials. Planar defects, namely two-dimensional defects, represent the discontinuity of the perfect crystal lattice across a plane, thus disrupting the long-range stacking sequence. Commonly investigated planar defects are grain boundaries (abrupt change of crystallographic directions), antiphase boundaries (opposite phases with the same crystallographic direction), stacking faults (local deviation of the stacking sequence), and twin boundaries (mirror-symmetrical arrangement of atoms). Bulk defects, i.e., three-dimensional defects, are pores (small volumes with no atoms present), foreign inclusions (other structures with different compositions or crystalline structure), and other phases. It is commonly argued that a significant influence of the imperfection on the fundamental properties of the materials on the nanoscale can be anticipated due to the more obvious perturbation of the electron/charge distribution than that on the macroscale, thus offering a facile advantage for modulating the electronic structure of the electrocatalyts.37

2.4 Fundamentals of electrochemical water splitting

It is well documented that the electrocatalytic water-splitting process is composed of HER and OER at the cathode and anode, respectively. The overall reaction of the electrolysis of water can be expressed as 2H2O = H2 + O2, which theoretically exhibits an energy difference of ΔG = 237.1 kJ mol−1 at a thermodynamic potential of 1.23 V.28,52 To serve as a potential alternative for the current state-of-the-art hydrogen production technology, such as steam reforming of coal and natural gas, the overall cost of electrolytic water splitting should be greatly reduced. Thus, the reaction mechanisms of both HER and OER in different solutions need to be comprehensively understood to improve the reaction kinetics, and the detailed mechanisms are introduced in the following sections.
2.3.1. The mechanism for the HER. The electrochemical HER process involves multiple elementary steps, which differ according to the pH value of the electrolyte (Fig. 5). Generally, the HER process consists of (I) the adsorption of H2O molecules or protons (H+) on the active site (i.e., the Volmer step) and (II) the following desorption of hydrogen molecules from the cathode via either a chemical (i.e., the Tafel step) or an electrochemical (i.e., the Heyrovsky step) route, as illustrated in Fig. 5, forming the Volmer–Tafel mechanism or the Volmer–Heyrovsky mechanism.33,53,54 In the Volmer step, a hydrated proton (in acid, eqn (1)) or H2O molecule (in alkali, eqn (2)) reacts with an electron at the surface electrocatalytic active center of the material (M), forming adsorbed hydrogen (MHads). Subsequently, the hydrogen can be desorbed from the active species via the Tafel reaction, or the Heyrovsky reaction, or via both approaches. In the Heyrovsky process, another hydrated proton (in acid, eqn (3)) or H2O molecule (in alkali, eqn (4)), accompanied by an extra electron, diffuses to MHads and forms a hydrogen molecule. 2.3.2 The mechanism for the OER. In comparison with the two-electron hydrogen evolution process, the oxygen evolution process represents a much more complex oxidation reaction process that involves multistep four-electron transfer and the generation of O2, which results in slow reaction kinetics.36,50 Similar to the HER process, the mechanism for the OER depends heavily on the surface structure of the electrocatalyst and on the pH value of the electrolyte.35,55 As illustrated in Fig. 6, the electrochemical OER process involves the formation of multiple intermediates (i.e., absorbed hydroxyl (M–OH), adsorbed oxo (M–O), and adsorbed peroxide species (M–OOH)), and oxygen can be generated via either the direct diffusion and reaction of two M–O intermediates or the decomposition of the adsorbed peroxide intermediates in both acid and alkali electrolytes. 2.3.3 Parameters for the evaluation of electrocatalytic performance. To comprehensively evaluate the electrocatalytic performance of electrocatalysts in the HER or OER process, some important parameters, including overpotentials (η), onset potentials (Eonset), exchange current density (j0), Tafel slope, electrochemically active surface area (ECSA), turnover frequency (TOF), long-term stability, and mass or specific activity (MA or SA), are often calculated and compared.6,7,23,32,34,36 A brief definition of and introduction to these parameters are discussed in the following section.

What needs our attention is the fact that η@10 mA cmgeo−2 does not necessarily reflect the intrinsic activity of the electrocatalysts due to the different mass loadings of the active materials.58,59 Previous work has validated the influence of mass loading on the values of η@10 mA cmgeo−2, which indicates that a higher mass loading on the electrode results in lower overpotential values.58 To exclude the influence of mass loading, turnover frequency and specific activity, which will be discussed in the following sections, are proposed to replace η@10 mA cmgeo−2 to offer a reasonable descriptor for the evaluation of the intrinsic activity of the electrocatalyst. However, the difficulty in identifying the real number of active sites that participate in the electrochemical process has made the evaluation of TOF ambiguous. The specific activities represent the current density per unit surface area, and the ECSA or Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area is typically used in the evaluation process. Another attempt involves taking the mass activity at a certain overpotential as a potential descriptor of the intrinsic activity.60 However, the choice of potential/overpotential has a significant influence on the mass activities and no universal standards have been established. Further investigation and development of descriptors for evaluation of the intrinsic activity of the electrocatalysts are required.

Further, the attained current density at η = 0 in the Tafel curve, j0, depicts the intrinsic electrocatalytic activity of an electrocatalyst in the case of reversible conditions. In essence, a high-performance electrocatalyst for HER or OER always exhibits a high j0 value and a smaller Tafel slope value at the same time.

The stability or durability of an electrocatalyst stands for a different crucial index to evaluate its practical capability by examining its long-term performance under reaction conditions. One commonly applied method for the evaluation of electrocatalysts is a comparison between the LSV curves at the initial cycle and those after thousands of cyclic voltammetry (CV) cycles. A smaller potential shift at the specific current density implies better stability. Another approach for the examination of stability is chronoamperometry or chronopotentiometry, which depicts the current density change under a certain potential or potential change by maintaining a specific current density. Negligible degradation in terms of the measured potential or current density during a chronic test can be observed for a robust electrocatalyst.

Apart from the parameters mentioned above, the faradaic efficiency (FE), which is calculated as the ratio between the quantity of produced gas obtained in the experiments to the theoretical quantity, ECSA, which represents the electrochemically active surface area, and MA/SA, which is calculated as the measured current normalized on the mass or the ECSA of the electrocatalyst, are also used to assess the catalytic properties.

2.5 In-depth discussion of the influences of imperfections during the electrocatalytic HER/OER process

It is well documented that the carrier concentration, mobility, band alignment, and distribution of electric charge are inner determinants of intrinsic electrical conductivity and reaction energy barriers in the electrocatalytic water–splitting process.22 From such a perspective, regulating parameters can help guide optimization of the limiting factors of electrolytic water performance, especially the intrinsic electrical conductivity and reaction energy barrier,19,31 which necessitate the illustration of different descriptors of electrocatalytic activity for the rational design of electrocatalysts. It is usually demonstrated that the rate of electrocatalytic reactions is strongly related to their outer electronic structure of active sites, which plays a determinant role in deciding their kinetic and thermodynamic factors via tuning the reaction barrier of the RDS. It determines the maximum energy requirement along the reaction path, the intermediate adsorption/desorption behavior, the binding energy of the key intermediates that would finally shape the reaction mechanism, and the activation energy that is related to system complexity.7,23,33,37 The introduction of imperfections with multiple dimensions, which induces the breakage of the long-range periodicity of the perfect lattice, represents one of the most efficient strategies for directly altering the electron distributions. In the following section, a summary of several patterns is provided according to which the defect causes the electron redistribution in the defected catalysts, in order to deliver a deep illustration of the defect-induced electronic structure in the electrocatalytic water-splitting reactions (Fig. 8).

Basically, a high-performance electrocatalyst features the exposure of more active sites, excellent intrinsic activity, and enhanced conductivity, which benefit the electron transfer bond-breakage processes, and can be facilely achieved via a defect engineering strategy.35,37,38,61 Incorporation of imperfections, such as vacancies, dopants, single atoms, amorphism, and interfaces, can preferentially induce perturbation of electron distributions and result in (I) the introduction of intermediate bands; (II) a change in d-band centers; and (III) a narrowing of bandgaps, which can simultaneously improve the density of active centers due to the defects’ multiple roles (directly serving as active sites or indirectly influencing the electronic structure of active sites) in the electrocatalytic process, improve the intrinsic performance of the reactive sites as a result of the optimized electron distribution, and enhance the conductivity by lowering the electron/charge transfer and transportation resistance (Fig. 8)19,28,52 (details are discussed in the following sections). With an eye on the theoretical aspects of the HER/OER/WS reactions, the results of modern computational techniques, mainly density functional theory (DFT), by solving the Kohn–Sham equations using various pseudopotentials and exchange–correlation functionals as approximation approaches, reveal that the defect-featuring electrocatalysts usually exhibit (I) optimized adsorption energy for the intermediates; (II) altered RDS for the electrocatalytic reactions, and (III) a reduced energy gap of the RDS, synergistically accelerating the electrocatalytic reaction rate and offering us highly efficient electrocatalysts (Fig. 8).8,20,29,30,35

3.1 Overview

Nowadays, to mitigate climate change, the splitting of water to generate hydrogen that can serve as an alternative energy carrier has been vigorously promoted (Fig. 9). Driven by the clean electricity generated via solar power, hydro-energy, wind energy, nuclear energy, geothermal energy, and so on, electrocatalytic water splitting is now an important approach for hydrogen production, accounting for about 4% of worldwide hydrogen supply.28 Water electrolysis can be performed over a large pH region with a relatively simple overall reaction: 2H2O = 2H2 (cathode) + O2 (anode), requiring a Gibbs free energy gap of 237.1 kJ mol−1. Based on the pH of the electrolyte, the 2e-transfer HER and 4e-transfer OER can proceed via distinct pathways, as depicted in Fig. 7. To further improve the reaction kinetics, introducing imperfections including zero-dimensional to three-dimensional defects into the crystal lattice of electrocatalysts has proved its potential for obtaining high-quality electrocatalysts with excellent conductivity, numerous active sites, high intrinsic activity, and high stability. 3.2 Multidimensional imperfections for the optimization of electrocatalysts towards the HER

Hydrogen evolution that generates hydrogen on the cathode is relatively simple, and proceeds via two-electron transfer by the depletion of H+ (2H+ + 2e− = H2) in an acidic electrolyte or generation of OH− (2H2O + 2e− = H2 + 2OH−) in an alkaline electrolyte. Inspired by the potential of the hydrogen economy, various electrocatalysts including precious metals, oxides, sulfides, phosphides, and carbides, have been investigated in detail in a combination of the incorporation of vacancies, dopants, single atoms, interfaces, and amorphization, by which highly efficient electrocatalysts towards HER have been proposed and prepared62–72 (Fig. 10).

Removing atoms from their lattice points to generate vacancies represents an effective method for modulation of the electron distribution inside electrocatalysts, which has been widely applied in the optimization of HER kinetics in both acidic and alkaline electrolytes (Fig. 10a).63,73,74,76–82 Taking transition metal disulfides as an example, Huang et al. proved that introducing sulfur vacancies into the MoS2 basal plane has a positive influence on the binding energy of the hydrogen intermediates, which approaches an optimum value of ΔG = 0, thus enhancing the electrocatalytic HER activity with an overpotential of 119 mV to achieve 10 mA cm−2 and a low Tafel slope of 50.7 mV dec−138 (Fig. 11a and b). As expected, the concentration of vacancies also matters and the appropriate concentration promotes HER to the maximum effect. Chhowalla et al. found that the activity of an individual vacant site increases with its concentration and peaks at a concentration of 5.7 × 1014 cm−2, where the intrinsic turnover frequency and Tafel slope exhibit the best values73 (Fig. 11c). As argued by the authors, the improved charge-transfer kinetics and thermodynamics of the vacancies and the induced strain account for the enhanced HER activity in an acidic electrolyte (Fig. 11d). As further demonstrated by Zhang et al., an S-vacancy of 12.5% gives the highest electronic state density near the Fermi level (EF), compared with those of lower or higher concentrations74 (Fig. 11e). And a suitable configuration of S-vacancies offers much more intensive electron delocalization, which yields a better ΔGH* and facilitates hydrogen adsorption onto the MoS2 surface, leading to enhanced HER performance (Fig. 11f). In an alkaline electrolyte, Pv-modified Ni12P5 has shown efficient HER activity according to its decreased overpotential at a current density of 10 mA cm−2, and a low Tafel slope via the generation of phosphorus vacancies75 (Fig. 11g). DFT-based results attribute the outstanding electrocatalytic performance to the facilitated intermediate desorption (potential-determining-step, PDS) behavior due to the enriched electron density of nickel and phosphorus atoms around Pv, which is achieved by weakening the hybridization of nickel 3d and phosphorus 2p orbitals (Fig. 11h).

Besides vacancies, another zero-dimensional defect, namely, dopants, serves as an additional approach for the adjustment of the electrocatalytic activity of the catalysts by regulation of electron distribution in both acidic and alkaline electrolytes (Fig. 10b).66–68,85,87–109 The adsorption energy of the intermediate hydrogen is often adopted as the key descriptor for characterization of HER performance, which promises the highest intrinsic activity under the condition where ΔG = 0, according to the volcano plot.26,32,33,110–112 Yu et al. report a phase transition from cubic to orthorhombic CoSe2 due to the doping of phosphorus, as a result of which the obtained electrocatalyst delivers an overpotential of 104 mV at a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in an alkaline electrolyte (Fig. 12a).83 The remarkable energetics stem from the favorable electronic structure, which exhibits an optimal binding of reaction intermediates (ΔG = 0.08 eV), and thus an enhanced water reduction ability (Fig. 12b). Non-metal element doping, such as carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus, has an obvious influence on tuning the electron distribution of the electrocatalyst and is now thriving as a beneficial approach.103,104,113 By doping carbon atoms into the NiO matrix, the obtained C–Ni1−xO shows a decreased bandgap from 1.5 eV to ∼0.6 eV, which can be ascribed to the downshift of the conduction band minimum (CBM) below the Fermi energy and the overlap of its density of states (DOS) with the valence band maximum (VBM), as reported by Li et al.84 (Fig. 12c). Further analysis indicates that the enhanced H adsorption activity, together with the appearance of newly active H adsorption sites accounts for the ultrahigh hydrogen evolution performance with an ultralow overpotential of 27 mV at 10 mA cm−2 and a Tafel slope value of 36 mV dec−1 (Fig. 12d). Zhao et al. report that a unique configuration of active sites can be readily generated by implanting copper atoms in the Ni electrodes (Ni(Cu)VOx) and the Ni–O–VOx sites can promote electron redistribution from Ni to VOx, which greatly weakens the H-adsorption and H2 releases free energy over Ni, leading to excellent HER activity comparable to that of benchmark Pt/C catalysts85 (Fig. 12e). Compared with single-element doping, synergistically doping more than one element into the nanomaterials offers new opportunities for the preparation of the desired electrocatalysts.86,104,106 By co-trapping selenium in the electrocatalyst surface and cobalt in the inner layer, Deng et al. prepared an MoS2 nanofoam catalyst, which exhibits an ultra-high large-current-density HER activity surpassing all previously reported heteroatom-doped MoS2.86 The inner layer-confined cobalt atoms stimulate neighboring sulfur atoms, while surface-confined selenium atoms stabilize the structure, which cooperatively enables a significant increase in abundance of active sites in the basal plane and edges of the electrocatalyst with optimized hydrogen adsorption activity (Fig. 12f).

Incorporating many advantageous features of homogeneous and heterogeneous electrocatalysts, single-atom catalysts (SACs) are now thriving as an innovative method for enhancing the electrocatalytic HER performance by dispersing transition metal atoms or noble metal atoms in the supporting matrix (Fig. 10c).64,118–136 Immobilized by the as-formed Mo vacancies, single-atomic platinum species can be readily implanted on MXene nanosheets (Mo2TiC2Tx), as reported by Wang et al. (Fig. 13a).114 The as-prepared Mo2TiC2Tx-PtSA exhibits superior electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution performance with an impressive overpotential as low as 30 mV when the current density reaches 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 13b). The implanted single-atomic platinum species can significantly enhance the DOS of the d-electron nearby the Fermi energy, resulting in an optimal value of ΔGH* and subsequently enhanced HER activity (Fig. 13c). By incorporating Co atoms into the framework of phosphorized carbon nitride (PCN), a Co1/PCN electrocatalyst shows outstanding electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution activity with a low overpotential of 138 mV under the condition of 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 13d and e). The unique configuration of a high-oxidization HO–Co1–N2 moiety upon hydroxyl adsorption, which significantly lowers the energy gap of the RDS (i.e., the H2O dissociation step in this case), is considered to be the reason for the superior electrocatalytic HER performance (Fig. 13f). Apart from an oxide or carbon-based matrix, metal can also serve as an alternative substrate for SAC preparation.120 Using a dealloying approach, Duan et al. created platinum nanowires decorated with single nickel atoms, which exhibit the optimum electrocatalytic performance in terms of specific activity and ECSA towards electrocatalytic HER116 (Fig. 13g and h). DFT-based computational results demonstrate that atomically dispersed nickel species that are liganded by two OH groups could electronically enhance the neighboring platinum atoms and improve their intrinsic activity for hydrogen evolution (Fig. 13i). The supporting substrate of the SACs also matters in designing highly efficient HER electrocatalysts.117,124 Using onion-like nanospheres of carbon (OLC) as the substrate, Song et al. successfully anchored Pt single atoms on it and prepared a Pt1/OLC electrocatalyst for HER117 (Fig. 13j). The Pt1/OLC catalyst delivers a low potential of −38 mV vs. RHE under the condition of 10 mA cm−2 and an elevated TOF of 40.78H2s−1 at an overpotential of 100mV, which is superior to those of previously reported SACs with similar Pt loadings on graphene substrates (Fig. 13k). Due to the cured morphology of the support, the tip-enhancement effect of the platinum species that can potentially draw localized electrons from the substrate is suggested to be responsible for the enhanced electrocatalytic performance, which optimizes the adsorption energy of the hydrogen intermediate to the optimum value and thus enhances the electrocatalysis kinetics of the electrocatalytic HER process (Fig. 13i).

Apart from the above-mentioned zero-dimensional defects, the construction of two-dimensional defects, i.e., grain boundaries or interfaces, represents another profound strategy towards the rational design of HER catalysts that are equipped with the required performance indexes by tuning the charge distribution (Fig. 10d).69,139–171 Composed of two homo-composition parts but different orientations, the grain boundaries can act as the active species for the electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction.137,172 Impressive electrocatalytic HER activity, long-term durability, and versatile universality in both acid and alkali electrolytes are simultaneously achieved via the construction of 2H–2H and 2H–1T boundaries in the MoS2 planes, as reported by Zhang et al.137 (Fig. 14a). DFT-based calculations offer the conclusion that in-plane grain boundaries (2H–2H or 2H–1T phase) of molybdenum disulfide that exhibit different crystal structures on either side can act as effective species during the HER process, which delivers a reduced energy barrier and optimized adsorption energy for the hydrogen intermediates and thus accelerate the reaction kinetics of the electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution reaction (Fig. 14b). Interfaces composed of (noble) metal and oxide/carbon-based materials represent another type of interface that has been widely investigated in the HER process.158,161,163,173–178 Li et al. report a multi-interfacial nickel/tungsten carbide hybrid that anchors on nitrogen-doped carbon (Ni/WC@NC), which exhibits superior electrocatalytic HER activity over a wide pH region138 (Fig. 14c). The excellent electrocatalytic HER performance is ascribed to the existence of abundant interfaces between nickel and tungsten carbide fragments, which induces electron transfer from the nickel component to the tungsten carbide part, in which case the simultaneously optimized electron distribution on both sides of the interface allows the significant modulation of the adsorption energy of the hydrogen intermediate, resulting in outstanding HER performance (Fig. 14d). Fan et al. obtained a heterostructured Nb4N5−xOx-MoS2 (0 < x < 1) decorated on nitrogen-doped graphene (defined as Nb4N5−xOx-MoS2/NG), which delivers superior HER stability and activity in an acidic electrolyte with a remarkable overpotential of 39 mV to reach 10 mA cm−2 and a Pt-comparable Tafel slope of 30 mV dec−1139 (Fig. 14e). The covalently connected Nb–(N,S)–Mo interfaces exhibit excellent chemisorption ability for both H and water and enhanced conductivity (Fig. 14f). The interaction between the organic component and the substrate can also greatly influence the electron distribution, thus altering the HER performance.140,157,179 Guided by theoretical calculations, Qiao et al. designed a Pt-NC/Ni-MOF heterostructure, which features unique Ni–O–Pt interfacial bonds that cause an increase in the *OH adsorption energy, as well as a reduction in the *H binding energy, making it a potential structure for HER140 (Fig. 14g). As validated experimentally, the optimized adsorption energy for H* and OH* of the Pt-NC/Ni-MOF heterostructure provides a superior mass activity of 7.92 mA μg−1 Pt and long-term stability towards the hydrogen evolution reaction (Fig. 14h).

By breaking the long-range ordering of the lattice atoms, amorphization can boost the electrocatalytic HER activity by exposing more active sites and enhancing conductivity.71,72,180–187 Sun et al. report the synthesis of amorphous RuSx nanoparticles (NPs) on an S-doped graphene oxide (GO) substrate, which delivers low overpotentials of 31 mV in an acidic electrolyte, 46 mV in a neutral electrolyte, and 58 mV in an alkaline electrolyte, to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2.187 As revealed by the computational results, the isolated ruthenium species shows Pt-like adsorption behavior and delivers a similar adsorption energy (−0.21 eV), which is responsible for the excellent intrinsic activity of the amorphous electrocatalyst towards electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution. 3.3 Multidimensional imperfections for the optimization of electrocatalysts towards the OER

The OER process that occurs on the anodic side typically involves the transfer of four electrons, which contributes to the sluggish electrolysis kinetics, thus preventing the energy efficiency of the overall water electrolysis.7,36,55 For the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis technology, low-cost electrocatalysts consisting of earth-abundant components, including transition metal oxides, layered double hydroxides, borides, perovskites, and carbon-based materials, with multiple defects from zero-dimensional to three-dimensional have been widely investigated.210–221 In this section, recent progress regarding multidimensional imperfections for the optimization of electrocatalysts towards OER is summarized (Fig. 15).

Generation of uncoordinated sites via the removal of anions or cations from the crystal lattice and subsequent modulation of the vacant sites shows profound potential in designing highly efficient OER electrocatalysts (Fig. 15a).219,224–234 Due to the introduction of vacancies into the benchmark NiFeOOH electrocatalyst, the change in ΔG for the RDS (S–OH → S–O step in this case) can be significantly decreased from 1.78 eV to 1.41 eV222 (Fig. 16a). Benefitting from the decline in ΔG, the oxygen vacancy-rich catalyst yields a more than 2-fold increase in current density (from 100 to 240 mA cm−2) under an overpotential of 270 mV (Fig. 16b). Compared with anion vacancies, cation vacancies can also exert a profound impact on the electron distribution of the electrocatalysts, and thus on the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution performance.223,227,234 As illustrated by Song et al., the α-Ni(OH)2 catalysts show improved activity with increasing VNi concentration223 (Fig. 16c). DFT-based calculations reveal that the introduction of VNi could enhance the intrinsic conductivity of α-Ni(OH)2 catalysts, thereby boosting the formation of true active species to promote electrochemical oxidation processes (Fig. 16d). Further modulation of the coordination environment offers another chance for the optimization of the electron distribution.224,232 Sulfur has been used to modify the oxygen vacancies in iron-cobalt oxide (FeCoOx–Vo) nanosheets, which can substantially stabilize oxygen vacancies (Vo) in the crystal lattice and simultaneously lead to the formation of Co–S coordination with Co active sites, thus lowering the adsorption energy of H2O224 (Fig. 16e). Owing to its modulated electron distribution status, FeCoOx–Vo–S shows outstanding electrocatalytic performance, requiring a low overpotential of 240 mV to achieve 50 mA cm−2 (Fig. 16f). Further computational calculations reveal the obviously enhanced interaction between the cobalt active species and adsorbed water molecules, which is responsible for the decreased adsorption energy and the accelerated OER kinetics (Fig. 16g).

Similar to the situation in the electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution process, doping the matrix with one or more kinds of heteroatoms can significantly affect the adsorption/desorption behavior of the key intermediates on the active species and accelerate the reaction kinetics during the electrocatalytic OER process96,235,236,238–271 (Fig. 15b). By doping FeOOH with Se atoms into the crystal lattice, the energy barrier for the RDS (M–O to M–OOH) can be significantly reduced, as reported by Wang et al., as far as the energy barrier of the step M–OH to M–O, indicating its potential for boosting OER kinetics235 (Fig. 17a). As expected, an industrial-scale current density of 500 mA cm−2 is achieved at the relatively small overpotential of 348 mV during the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution process, which is comparable to previously reported values (Fig. 17b). Co-doping with more than one element represents another promising approach for the design of OER electrocatalysts.237,246,247,266 Wang et al. reported the simultaneous doping of sp-nitrogen and sulfur atoms into few-layered graphdiyne and investigated its OER performance236 (Fig. 17c). The synergy between the sp-N and S atoms can boost the current density for OER and bring about excellent catalytic activity and faster kinetics (Fig. 17d). Further theoretical investigations imply that Fe3+-doped β-NiOOH co-doped with Cu2+ or Ag3+ will have even more favorable O–O coupling and molecular O2 desorption activity at the Fe sites, which can be ascribed to the lowered ΔGox, enhancing O–O bond formation (Fig. 17e and f).

Atomically dispersing metal atoms onto carbon-based materials, oxides, hydroxides, and selenides could not only activate the substrates but also act as active centers by forming unique single-atom configurations.220,221,272–277 Wu et al. report the atomic anchoring of Fe–Nx sites onto a nitrogen and sulfur co-doped carbon substrate, and the obtained single-atom catalyst exhibits excellent electrocatalytic OER activity in terms of overpotential at a current density of 10 mA cm−2 and Tafel slope. Further investigations indicate that the unique Fe–Nx species serve as active species during the electrochemical process and are responsible for the enhanced electrocatalytic performance.275 Yao et al. constructed an atomic dispersion of Pt–Co–Se species by using defected CoSe2 (CoSe2−x) as the substrate. The unique coordination configuration of the Pt–Co–Se moiety induces a profound asymmetry in the electronic structure, which in turn has a significant influence on the adsorption behavior between the cobalt species and the intermediate species (i.e., *OH, *O, and *OOH) during the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution process, as a result of which a 9-fold enhancement in the specific activity is obtained.274 Gu et al. report the synthesis of densely loaded iridium single atoms that anchor on an NiO support with a favorable ∼4+ oxidation state, which could act as active sites during the electrocatalytic process and simultaneously activate the inert NiO matrix surface to provide extra active species.269 As a result, the electrocatalyst delivers a low overpotential of 215 mV to achieve 10 mA cm−2 and an impressive current density in an alkaline electrolyte during the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution reaction process, which exhibits 57-fold and 46-fold enhancements in comparison with the NiO reference and a commercial IrO2 benchmark at an applied potential of 1.49 V vs. RHE, respectively.

Intentionally introduced interfaces can facilitate the water dissociation process by varying the electronic structure, e.g., alteration of the d-band center, reducing the energy required for the RDS, confining the active species at the interface, and the coupling effect between the components (Fig. 15c).278–295 Hu et al. report the successful preparation of NiSe2/CoSe2 heterostructures with different interfacial densities, which exhibit superior electrocatalytic properties in an alkaline electrolyte with a small overpotential of 286 mV to achieve 10 mA cm−2278 (Fig. 18a). Due to the synergy between the inner NiSe2/CoSe2 heterostructure and the electrochemically induced oxides/hydroxides, the energy barrier could be significantly reduced, which in turn accelerates the reaction kinetics of OER (Fig. 18b). Owing to the in situ generation of the hydroxide interfacial layer between layered double hydroxide (LDH) and FeNi foil, the electrocatalyst shows more favorable adsorption of the OH intermediate during OER than the pure LDH, leading to efficient reaction kinetics that deliver an η10 of 130 mV279 (Fig. 18c). As revealed by the computational results, the interfacial interactions between the two components shift the d-band center upwards, which in turn causes an increased number of empty antibonding states and thus an enhanced bond strength between the adsorbate and surface, eventually promoting the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution process (Fig. 18d). Furthermore, the interface between the organic molecules and the matrix was also investigated.280 As reported by Hu et al., the organic small molecule can activate commercial transition metal foam by directly evolving metal nanomeshes embedded in graphene-like films (M-NM@G) through a facile solid-phase migration process280 (Fig. 18e). The as-obtained M-NM@G shows a small overpotential of 284 mV to achieve 100 mA cm−2, which is quite competitive with previously reported state-of-the-art OER electrocatalysts in terms of the overpotentials required at a current density of 100 mA cm−2. (Fig. 18f).

The generation of amorphous electrocatalysts with multiple uncoordinated sites and dangling bonds facilitates water oxidation by constructing favorable electronic structures and reducing the energy barrier210,211,296–308 (Fig. 15d). Further investigations attribute the excellent electrocatalytic OER performance to the unique structural characteristics that involve distorted Fe octahedra and an elevated nickel valent state, under which conditions the nickel exhibits a +3 state. (Fig. 19b). To tackle the instability of the amorphous phase during the electrocatalytic process, Huang et al. confined the amorphous area into the crystalline surface, which showed an order–disorder–order transition of atoms51 (Fig. 19c). Thanks to the unique structure of the electrocatalyst, a superior electrocatalytic OER performance that requires a small overpotential of 190 mV to reach 10 mA cm−2, as well as a low Tafel slope value of 31 mV dec−1 can be achieved by the space-confined amorphous electrocatalyst. (Fig. 19d). Kang et al. report that a combination of the amorphous cobalt phyllosilicate (ACP) into the layered crystalline motif can substantially reduce the overpotential of the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution reaction process.297 (Fig. 19e). In addition, the amorphous material could also serve as the substrate, which would introduce strong coupling between the active sites/species and the substrate due to numerous unsaturated coordination sites and dislocation of the electrons. Yeo et al. present an OER catalytic system that consists of cobalt oxide (CoOx) with adsorbed Fe3+ ions, which significantly lowers the average overpotential to achieve the benchmark 10 mA cm−2 and Tafel slope values.298 The coordination number of Fe is substantially reduced due to adsorption on the amorphous support, which generates Fe sites with oxygen vacancies, leading to improved OER activity.

3.4 Multidimensional imperfections for the optimization of electrocatalysts towards overall water splitting

Combining the HER on the cathode and the OER on the anode, the overall water electrolysis follows the reaction: 2H2O = 2H2 + O2. By incorporation of multidimensional defects into the matrix, defect engineering is deemed to be an efficient method for the enhancement of water electrolysis electrocatalysts and has been widely investigated (Fig. 20).8,18–20,23,24,28–30

As revealed by various investigations, the vacancies in the electrocatalysts could effectively tune the electron distribution to facilitate the adsorption/desorption behavior of the key intermediates during the electrocatalytic HER and OER process, at the same time216,217,319–324 (Fig. 20a). By engineering the oxygen vacancies of cobalt tungstate nanoparticles, Cai et al. report the superior water electrolysis ability of CoWO4−x@C, which gives a small cell potential of 1.57 V to achieve the benchmark of 10 mA cm−2.216 DFT results imply that the presence of oxygen vacancies could result in a significant reduction in the energy barrier for the rate-determining step (the oxygen desorption step of the OER process in this case) during the electrocatalytic water-splitting process, thus facilitating the overall electrocatalytic reaction (Fig. 21a and b). Besides the often investigated anion vacancies, another type of vacancy, namely cation vacancies, also exhibits promising potential in the rational design of highly efficient electrocatalysts with desired electrocatalytic performance towards overall water splitting, similar to the case of hydrogen evolution or oxygen evolution.217,319 Zhang et al. propose the introduction of Fe vacancies into ultrathin feroxyhyte (δ-FeOOH) nanosheets, which delivers a small cell potential of 1.62 V to reach 10 mA cm−2, together with excellent long-term stability (>60 h)217 (Fig. 21c). Both experimental and computational evidence indicates that the constitution of abundant second neighboring Fe to Fe vacancies in δ-FeOOH nanosheets serve as the electrocatalytically active species for both the electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution and the oxygen evolution process, which exhibit optimal adsorption energy for the hydrogen intermediates in the HER and reduce the energy gap for the RDS (formation of *OOH intermediate in this case) (Fig. 21d). Furthermore, Cheng et al. synthesized defective RhCu nanotubes with multiple uncoordinated Cu sites, which exhibit excellent HER and OER performance.319 Theoretical calculations indicate that the RDS changes from the Volmer step to the Heyrovsky step for the defect-rich electrocatalyst in the HER process, and the energy barrier reduces from 1.39 eV to 0.91 eV, implying enhanced electrocatalytic activity (Fig. 21e). As for the OER process, the reduction in the calculated overpotential from 0.69 eV to 0.45 eV due to the existence of defects accounts for the improved reaction kinetics (Fig. 21f). The experimental water-splitting test validates the fact that the defect-rich sample shows superior water-splitting ability at all pH values, which requires a low cell potential of 1.59 V to achieve 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 21g).

The dopant can not only serve as the active species/sites but also stabilize the active species during the electrocatalytic reactions by modulation of the electron distribution, enabling the bifunctionality of the electrocatalyst96,214,215,257,325–350 (Fig. 20b). Incorporation of Rh atoms into NiFe-LDH could significantly lower the energy gap required for the RDS (decomposition of the *OOH step in this case) in the electrocatalytic OER process, as reported by S. Francisco et al.325 (Fig. 22a). The electrocatalyst needs only a low cell voltage of 1.46 V to drive the electrocatalysis setup to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2, which rivals the advanced electrocatalysts that have been previously reported (Fig. 22b). Du et al. propose three main effects that the unique Ir–O–V shows due to the doping of Ir atoms: (I) serving as catalytic sites for H2O dissociation, (II) decreasing the charge density on the bridging oxygen, thus facilitating HER there, and (III) increasing the charge density on V ions, which in turn boosts OER, which can be observed based on the differential-charge densities326 (Fig. 22c). Due to the synergy effect, the NiVIr LDH requires an ultralow cell voltage of 1.49 V to achieve 10 mA cm−2 in the electrolyzer, surpassing most NiFe-based electrocatalysts (Fig. 2d). By doping dual-cations into NiSe2 nanosheets, Li et al. prepared an Fe0.09Co0.13–NiSe2 porous nanosheet, which exposes more active sites and exhibits the optimum adsorption energy for the key intermediates for the electrocatalytic process, as a result of which the defected electrocatalyst shows impressive electrocatalytic performance for both the hydrogen evolution and the oxygen evolution processes327 (Fig. 22e). When assembled in a two-electrode water-splitting electrolyzer, the setup requires only a low cell potential of 1.52 V to deliver 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 22f).

Atomically dispersed nonmetal or metal sites on the support could potentially function as the active sites and enable the bifunctionality of the electrocatalysts by modulation of the adsorption energies of the key intermediates and lower the reaction barrier.351–359 Huang et al. prepared various single-atom catalysts via a cation exchange strategy, among which Rh SAC-CuO NAs/CF can achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2 under a cell voltage of only 1.51 V.357 As argued by the authors, the decreased adsorption energies of the reactive intermediates are responsible for the superior electrocatalytic activity, and the improved reaction kinetics of the Rh SAC–CuO electrocatalyst. Sun et al. propose a synergy between molybdenum single atoms and cobalt-containing supports, which can effectively change the adsorption energies of the key intermediate species. As a result, the overpotential of water splitting can be substantially decreased, and a low cell voltage of 1.56 V is required to achieve the benchmark of 10 mA cm−2 in an alkaline electrolyte.351

The construction of interfaces in electrocatalysts offers the advantage of facile electron transfer, synergy between the hetero-components, and enhanced exposure of active centers, thus enhancing the reaction kinetics in both HER and OER processes212,213,360–382 (Fig. 20c). Mu et al. report the preparation of a multisite Mo-Ni3S2/NixPy/NF electrode with abundant heterogeneous interfaces, which requires an ultralow cell potential of 1.46 V at 10 mA cm−2, where the excellent long-term durability of the electrolysis performance can be well guaranteed212 (Fig. 23a). DFT-based calculations attribute the excellent electrocatalytic performance to the synergistically optimized adsorption energies of key intermediates (*OH, *O, and *OOH) during HER and OER processes, which significantly accelerate the reaction kinetics, thus enhancing the water electrolysis activity (Fig. 23b). The significant electronic coupling at the hetero-interfaces could preferentially optimize the adsorption energies of both HER and OER intermediates, which reduces the energy barrier for the rate-determining step, as illustrated by Wang et al. via the construction of an MoS2/NiFe-LDH superlattice (Fig. 23c). As a result, the MoS2/LDH superlattice requires low cell potentials of ∼1.57, 1.63, and 1.71 V to deliver current densities of 10, 20, and 50 mA cm−2, respectively (Fig. 23d). G. Kanatzidis et al. designed an MoS2/Co9S8/Ni3S2/Ni electrocatalyst, which delivered a current density of 10 mA cm−2 under a cell potential of 1.45 V (Fig. 23e). Further computational results attribute the excellent electrocatalytic OER activity to electron transfer at the hetero-interface that extracts electrons from Co9S8 to MoS2, which significantly alters the adsorption energies of the intermediate species and reduces the energy barrier for the rate-determining step from 1.45 eV to 0.52 eV (Fig. 23f).

Amorphization of the electrocatalysts provides a unique advantage in boosting the water electrolysis activity by exposing more active sites, a unique three-phase interface, and a tailored energy barrier for the RDS, which has been intensively investigated186,384,386–388 (Fig. 20d). Under a U = 1.23 V potential, the overpotentials are 0.23 V (in acid) and 0.29 V (in alkali) (Fig. 24b). As a result, the amorphous ruthenium-tellurium nanorods exhibit extraordinary water electrolysis performance in a two-electrode setup, with a cell voltage of 1.52 V to achieve 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 24c). The amorphous material could also form in situ during the electrocatalytic reaction and act as the active species.384,389,390 Cao et al. argue that amorphous Ni–Fe–Mo (oxy)hydroxide is formed in situ during the electrolysis process and acts as the active species, which exhibits superior electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution and oxygen evolution performance because of the optimized hydrogen binding energy and lowered overpotential384 (Fig. 24d). Combined with the conductive substrate, which provides rapid electron transfer to the active sites, the electrocatalyst demonstrates a small cell potential of 1.56 V to deliver 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 24e). As a result, when assembled into a two-electrode electrolyzer, the device requires a cell voltage of only 1.89 V to achieve the industrial-level 500 mA cm2385 (Fig. 24f and g).

As discussed above, imperfections with different dimensions have a significant influence on the electrocatalytic activity of electrocatalysts. By incorporation of irregularities into the perfect lattice, an advantageous electronic structure can be realized, which could potentially lower the energy barrier for the rate-determining step, shift the binding energies of key intermediates towards optimal values and change the RDS, thus boosting the electrocatalytic performance of the electrocatalysts for HER, OER, and WS. The stability of the electrocatalysts represents another metric for the evaluation of the electrocatalytic performance of electrocatalysts. Although most defect-featuring electrocatalysts are demonstrated to be stable in a durability test, the dynamic evolution behavior of the imperfection itself is still under debate. The electrocatalysts would undergo structural and compositional changes under applied potentials, which are demonstrated to exhibit positive influences on the electrocatalytic performance of the electrocatalysts, as discussed in the following section. For transition-metal-based (especially Ni, Fe, and Co) electrocatalysts, the reconstruction behavior has been validated by multiple studies, through which the key intermediates and real active sites are generated, thus accelerating the reaction kinetics.223,299,386,401 Generally, the stability of the electrocatalysts is proved by chronoamperometry or chronopotentiometry, which measures the change in the current density or applied potential for a certain time. Further evidence involves ex situ characterization of the electrocatalysts after a duration test via microscopic and spectroscopic techniques, in which case negligible changes in the electronic states and morphological structures are commonly used to demonstrate the excellent stability of the electrocatalysts. For a better understanding of the role that imperfection plays during electrocatalytic reactions, advanced ex situ, in situ, and operando characterization methods are highly desirable, and will be discussed in the following sections.

4 Advanced characterization techniques for decoding structure–property relationships

4.1 Overview

Tremendous efforts have been devoted to optimization of the electrocatalytic performance of electrocatalysts, and significant advances have been achieved, owing to a combination of the optimized electronic structure of the electrocatalysts, which offers experimental evidence for the efficacy of the defect engineering strategy, and advanced characterization techniques, which rationalize the enhanced electrocatalytic performance and bridge the gap between the structures and the properties of the electrocatalysts. To further reveal the role imperfections play during enhancement in electrocatalytic performance, multiple characterization methods have been developed to reveal the structural or electronic factors that are intrinsically linked to the boosted electrocatalytic activity of the electrocatalysts via ex situ, in situ, or operando investigations.402–404 Thus, it is necessary to discuss recent progress achieved toward an in-depth understanding of the water electrolysis process using cutting-edge characterization techniques, including microscopic (high-angle annular dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy, HAADF-STEM) and spectroscopic (X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, XPS; X-ray absorption spectroscopy, XAS; Raman spectroscopy and Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy, FTIR) approaches (Fig. 25). In this section, we offer a brief discussion on the advanced ex situ, in situ, or operando characterization methods that can potentially decode the reaction mechanism during the electrocatalytic process and offer insights into the structure–property relation of the defect-featuring electrocatalysts.

4.2 Advanced microscopic techniques

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is nowadays essential for a description of the morphology of electrocatalysts at the nanoscale, and offers sufficient information on structure, size, shape, crystallinity, and even composition with the assistance of elemental mapping. Compared with conventional TEM, scanning TEM (STEM) addresses each pixel in series as the probe is scanned across the specimen, offering much higher resolution. HAADF-STEM produces annular dark-field images formed by very high angles, and the intensity of the signal is sensitive to the atomic number of the elements, forming Z-contrast images (Fig. 25). Typically, the intensity of the signal collected by the detector is proportional to Zα, where α is often quoted to be around 1.7 for most devices.7 With the help of advanced HAADF-STEM technology, a clearer description of the imperfections can be readily obtained and sufficient information can be indexed.

HAADF-STEM technology has been frequently applied in the identification of atomic species, including vacancies and single atoms in the electrocatalyst to validate their existence by offering Z-contrast images with extremely high resolution. As illustrated in Fig. 26a, the absence of atoms in the graphene substrate can be observed.405 After trapping the nickel species into the vacancies, the atomically dispersed Ni atom indicates a much higher intensity due to its higher Z number (Fig. 26b). As a result of this, the bright spot in the graphene substrate validates the existence of single Ni atoms. Atomically dispersed transition metal atoms and noble metal atoms are the most frequently investigated in the electrocatalysis area, which maximizes the utilization rate of the corresponding atoms and is now thriving as a promising strategy in various fields.407–409 Another example indicates that MoS2 with atomically anchored Co species exhibits superior activity and selectivity in the hydrodeoxygenation reaction.406 Similarly, the bright spot in the Z-contrast image indicates the existence of single atomic Co species, which is consistent with the simulated results (Fig. 26c and d).

4.3 Advanced spectroscopic techniques

4.3.1 X-ray absorption spectroscopy. X-ray absorption spectroscopy serves as a useful tool for investigating the local structure around selected elements that are contained within a material at the atomic and molecular scale. Typically, XAS measures the change in the X-ray absorption coefficient μ(E) with the energy of the X-rays. Generally, μ(E) decreases smoothly as the energy increases (approximately as 1/E3), and a sudden increase in μ(E) can be observed at certain energies that are characteristic of the atoms in the material.37 The energy points at which μ(E) increases significantly, namely, the X-ray absorption edges, appear when the energy of the X-ray is sufficient to liberate electrons from the low-energy bound states in the atoms, promoting a photoelectron to the continuum state (Fig. 25). The outgoing and scattered waves interfere in a manner that depends on the geometry of the absorber environment and the photoelectron wavelength, thus offering unique information on the local environment around specific atoms. Three regions of the XAS spectrum, the pre-edge region, the X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES), and the extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS), are the most frequently studied.416,417 Due to its unique advantages, XAS has been applied not only to crystals, but also to materials that possess little or no long-range translational order, such as amorphous systems, glasses, quasicrystals, disordered films, membranes, solutions, liquids, metalloproteins, and even molecular gases.417

Due to the sensitivity of XAS spectra to the local environment around the absorbers, in situ/operando XAS technology has been intensively applied for identification of the real active sites during electrocatalytic processes.417,419–423 Wei et al. have identified the formation of the high-valence HO–Co1–N2 structure in the alkaline HER process, which serves as the active site in Co1/PCN (atomically dispersed Co that anchors on the phosphorized carbon nitride) electrocatalyst.115Operando XAS spectra indicate a positive shift of the absorption edge by approximately 0.6 eV under potential bias, which implies an increase in the oxidation state of the Co species during the electrocatalytic HER process (Fig. 27a). Further analysis of the operando EXAFS spectra of Co1/PCN also validates the rearrangement of the local atomic structure around the Co atoms (Fig. 27b). By combining theoretical simulation and operando XAS results, the unique high-valence HO–Co1–N2 structure is deemed to be the origin of the high electrocatalytic activity. Recently, Tang et al. proposed that in situ formed Ni0.5Co0.5OOH0.75 species with high-valence metals and multiple oxygen vacancies in an Ni0.5Co0.5-MOF-74 electrocatalyst serve as the real active centers during the oxygen evolution reaction.418Operando XAS characterization reveals an increase in oxidation state as the applied potentials are elevated (Fig. 27c). Further EXAFS results indicate the formation of the collinear Ni–M–M structure, resulting in a two-step reconstruction process of the Ni0.5Co0.5-MOF-74 electrocatalyst, which involves the formation of the hydroxide analogue at the beginning and subsequent formation of the oxyhydroxide analogue (Fig. 27d). Furthermore, the elevated oxidation states of the metal species and the distorted atomic arrangement of the atoms in the electrocatalysts are found to be responsible for the enhanced electrocatalytic OER activity (Fig. 27e and f). Combined with the ab initio XANES simulations, the unique -Ni0.5Co0.5OOH0.75 with high-valence metal atoms and abundant oxygen vacancies are proposed as the real active sites during the OER process.

4.3.2 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) represents one well-established surface-sensitive technique for the analysis of solids, which offers atomic and molecular information for the outer 1–10 nm of the specimen. The physics that lies behind XPS technology is the photoelectric effect, which refers to electron emission when subjected to electromagnetic radiation (Fig. 25). The detector in the XPS device measures the kinetic energy of the photoelectrons, which carries information on the element species, chemical state, overall electronic structure, surface composition, and density of the electronic states. The samples in an XPS test can take the form of a solid or liquid, which makes it a relatively facile method for the analysis process. Furthermore, the use of synchrotron radiation X-rays could provide the possibility of an investigation of the surface (1–2 monolayers) of the electrocatalyst by tuning the photon energy. The unique advantages of modern XPS technology have meant it has prospered in the electrocatalysis field and they make it an efficient toolkit for an in-depth understanding of the electrocatalytic mechanisms during the electrochemical process.37

The in situ/operando XPS technique offers explicit information on the surface composition, electronic structure, and electron distribution status of electrocatalysts during the electrocatalytic process and has been widely applied in the investigation of the dynamics of the electrolysis reactions.424–430 Using operando ambient pressure X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, Sharp et al. validated the chemical and structural transformations of a Co3O4/Co(OH)2 biphasic electrocatalyst under an applied potential bias.424Operando XPS results indicate a shift in the binding energy for O 1s after applying the potential bias, implying variation in the electronic structure of the electrocatalyst (Fig. 28a). Further analysis of the Co 3/2 spectra demonstrates the existence of Co(OH)2 (Co2+) over the Co3O4 mixed (Co2+, Co3+) oxide phase (Fig. 28b). Detailed results from the valence band photoemission spectra indicate the existence of Co2+ and Co3+ under pristine and hydrated conditions and the effective water adsorption behavior under OER potentials, which can be inferred from the broad band at ∼10.1 eV below the Fermi level (Fig. 28c). Furthermore, the surface composition changes with the applied potential, and the surface undergoes a significant chemical transformation, resulting in the formation of the CoO(OH) phase during the OER process, which is responsible for the superior OER activity of the electrocatalyst (Fig. 28d).

4.3.3 Raman spectroscopy. Raman spectroscopy is based on the Raman effect, which states that when incident light excites molecules, the molecules will reflect light in a different wavelength. The magnitude of the Raman effect is related to the polarizability of the electrons in the molecules, which involves an inelastic scattering process (Fig. 25). The Raman spectrum features multiple peaks, showing the intensity and wavelength position of the Raman scattered light, which offers detailed information about the chemical structure, phase and polymorphy, crystallinity, and molecular interactions in the specimen. Various techniques, such as resonance Raman spectroscopy, tip-enhanced Raman scattering (TERS), and surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS), have been proposed to enhance the sensitivity of Raman spectroscopy by several orders of magnitude. It is common sense that the electrocatalytic process happens on the very surface of the electrocatalyst, which offers a suitable platform to reveal the potential of Raman spectroscopy.

As one of the most commonly used analytical methods, Raman spectroscopy has shown a significant effect in identifying active species, the surface reconstruction process, the validation of different reaction pathways, the content of the active phases, and key intermediates during the electrocatalytic process.404,431–433,435–441 With the assistance of operando surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy, Zhang et al.431 have identified the sulfur atoms in an Ag@MoS2 electrocatalyst as the real active sites during the electrocatalytic HER process. As illustrated in Fig. 29a, the Raman bands at 2532 cm−1 that correspond to the stretching of the S–H bond gradually appear as the applied potential decreases, which indicates the direct absorption of H onto the S atoms in the Ag@MoS2 electrocatalyst. And ν(D–H) shifts to lower positions in the isotopic substitution experiment. Zhao et al.432 reported a highly efficient NiFeCr electrocatalyst by doping Cr into the NiFe oxyhydroxide substrate, and the operando Raman spectrum is used for qualification of the active species. The operando Raman spectrum reveals that more active β-NiOOH content exists in the NiFeCr electrocatalysts, which can be deduced by the intensity ratio between the peak areas at ∼475 cm−1 and ∼555 cm−1 with a stronger peak at 555 cm−1 implying more β-NiOOH (Fig. 29b). By surveying the Raman spectra under different potentials, the surface reconstruction process can be deduced, from which the key reversible intermediates can be identified, as reported by Xiong et al.433 Taking NiNPS as a model, the irreversible phase transformation from NiNPS to α-Ni(OH)2 can be observed at relatively low potentials (Fig. 29c). And the following reversible phase transition between α-Ni(OH)2 and γ-NiOOH can also be deduced from the operando Raman spectra, which indicates that γ-NiOOH acts as the active phase for the electrocatalytic oxygen evolution process, resulting in a 48-fold enhancement in activity at 300 mV. The reaction pathways can also be decided by carrying out operando Raman investigations, as argued by Hu et al.,434 who investigated the different reaction mechanisms of NiOOH and NiFe layered double hydroxide (LDH) electrocatalysts. As revealed by operando Raman spectra, the NiIII-O bands observed at 455 and 535 cm−1 for both FeOOH–NiOOH and NiFe LDH were redshifted by about 22 cm−1, indicating O isotope exchange at 1.55 V (Fig. 29d). Further evidence indicates a 3 cm−1 isotopic shift of FeOOH–NiOOH under OER potential, implying a partial O exchange of 16% and no oxygen exchange for the NiFe-LDH electrocatalyst (Fig. 29e). In combination with the electrokinetic analysis, the authors conclude that the OER process can proceed in FeOOH–NiOOH via a bifunctional mechanism, and that the RDS is the formation of the O–O bond, which involves attack by OH− on an FeO with a hydrogen atom transfer to an NiIII–O site. Furthermore, Hu et al.435 have identified the cobalt superoxide species as the key intermediate and O2 release as the RDS during the electrocatalytic OER process using amorphous cobalt oxyhydroxide as the electrocatalyst. Operando Raman spectra under higher potentials have validated the existence of disordered CoO2, which exhibits obvious peaks at 581 and 474 cm−1 at 1.50 V (Fig. 29f). Isotope labeling experiments have proved the formation of the key intermediate, namely, the superoxide species (CoOO−), under OER potential, which can be deduced from the redshift of the Raman peaks corresponding to the A1g and so-called active oxygen species (Fig. 29g). In situ Raman spectra recorded at >1.45 V have shown a blue shift of the Raman peaks for both A1g and so-called active oxygen species, indicating the availability of both lattice O and OO− for exchange with the electrolyte during the OER process (Fig. 29h). D-labeling results indicate that the superoxide had hydrogen-bonding interactions with nearby OH groups, which can be deduced from the unchanged Raman position for OO− and the shift of 38 cm−1 in the FT-IR spectrum (Fig. 29i). In combination with the electrokinetic results, the release of O2 molecules from the superoxide intermediate is determined as the RDS during the electrocatalytic OER process.

4.3.4 Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy. Infrared spectroscopy is a technique based on the vibrations of the atoms of a molecule. The infrared spectrum offers information about the fraction of the incident radiation that is absorbed at a particular energy by measuring the infrared radiation passing through the specimen (Fig. 25). Generally, the energy at which any peak in the FTIR spectrum appears corresponds to the frequency of vibration of a part of a sample molecule. The FTIR spectra can be used to determine the functional groups of molecules relevant to both organic and inorganic chemistry. Due to constant improvement in modern technology, FTIR could potentially make further great strides by being incorporated in various in situ or operando devices to monitor the changes in the substance in real time. To further enhance the signal intensity, attenuated total reflection surface-enhanced infrared absorption spectroscopy (ATR-SEIRAS) and synchrotron radiation Fourier transform infrared (SR-FTIR) are frequently used in operando investigations.

By detecting the signal that stems from changes in the dipole moment, the FTIR spectrum has been widely used in detecting surface adsorbates and key intermediates during electrocatalytic reactions.60,442–445 Liu et al.60 have designed lattice-strained noble-metal-free metal–organic frameworks for bifunctional oxygen electrocatalysis, and operando SR-FTIR is applied in the investigation to identify the key intermediates during electrocatalytic processes. As revealed by the SR-FTIR results, a peak around 1048 cm−1 is observed for both the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and the oxygen evolution reaction, indicating that similar intermediate species appear for both reactions (Fig. 30a). Further isotope-labeling experiments consolidate the appearance of OOH intermediates for both ORR and OER processes (Fig. 30b). In combination with theoretical simulations, the authors conclude that Ni4+ species are the active sites that generate the key OOH intermediates, which is responsible for the superior mass activity of lattice-strained NiFe MOFs. In another example, Yao et al.442 probed the electrocatalytic intermediates for an Ru–N–C electrocatalyst during the OER process by investigating operando SR-FTIR spectra during the electrochemical reaction. As indicated in Fig. 30c and d, a reversible FTIR peak at ∼764 cm−1 can be observed during the electrocatalytic process, which implies the reversible adsorption and desorption of the key intermediate. Further computational results validate the origin of the new peak, which stems from the adsorption of single atoms on the electrocatalyst. Thus, the authors attribute the high activity and stability of the Ru–N–C electrocatalyst towards OER to the formation of O–Ru1–N4 active species.

5. Conclusions and outlook

In conclusion, driven by renewable electricity, the hydrogen produced from the electrolysis of water exhibits unprecedented potential in shifting the global energy system from a traditional energy-based one to a sustainable renewable-energy-based one, which necessitates the exploitation of high-performance electrocatalysts with desired properties. The imperfections of the electrocatalysts play a vital role in modulating the electronic structure of the nanocatalysts and thus enhancing the electrocatalytic activity towards HER, OER, and overall water splitting. Owing to the rapid development of various advanced characterization strategies, significant progress has been made towards an in-depth understanding of the prominent role that imperfections from zero-dimensional to three-dimensional defects play during the electrocatalytic process. Further computational approaches based on quantum mechanics and classical Newtonian mechanics offer a theoretical description of the patterns through which the experimental and theoretical results are linked and matched, which in turn offer instructive guidance for the rational design of highly efficient electrocatalysts with desired intrinsic activity, long-term stability, and practical utility under harsh working conditions. As discussed, the implantation of imperfections into the perfect lattice of electrocatalysts induces the breakage of long-range periodicity and significant perturbation of the electron distribution, as a result of which the electronic structures undergo a beneficial alteration that finally enhances the reaction kinetics of the electrocatalytic processes. The imperfections offer advantageous enhancement of the conductivity that facilitates electron/charge transfer and transport processes and increased exposure of active species/sites that allows facile access to the reactive intermediates. More specifically, the incorporation of irregularity into the crystal lattices could induce extra band alignment, shift the d-band center, or reduce the bandgap of the electrocatalysts by optimizing the electron distribution, which accelerates electrocatalytic reaction kinetics through changing the rate-determining step, lowering the energy gap that is required for the RDS and altering the binding energy of the key intermediates.

Although many advances regarding enhancement of the electrocatalytic performance towards hydrogen evolution, oxygen evolution, and overall water splitting, as well as an in-depth understanding of the mechanism behind the reinforcement, have been achieved, challenges and obstacles for the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis using defected electrocatalysts remain.

5.1. The controlled generation of defects

Numerous approaches, including but not limited to chemical, physical, and biological methods, have been applied to generate various imperfections from zero-dimensional to three-dimensional defects in crystal lattices. However, current preparation methods realize the incorporation of imperfections via either an in situ method or a post-treatment method, which result in a random arrangement of defects and the presence of multi-type defects, hampering the systematic understanding of the vital role they play during the electrocatalytic reaction. This necessitates the controllable production of imperfections with desired concentration, local coordination environment, and a specific type, which would facilitate the isolation of complex factors that work synergistically during the electrochemical process, paving the way to a comprehensive and incisive understanding of the defect effect. As difficult as it is, several approaches have been proposed and developed for this purpose. Energetic particle etching at the atomic sites has been investigated as a potential method for the controlled preparation of defected electrocatalysts. Furthermore, by elaborate optimization of the operation parameters, atomic layer deposition (ALD) has also been deemed a promising approach. Despite the small steps that have been taken towards the ambitious goal of the controllable production of imperfections, there is still a long road ahead.

5.2. The development of cutting-edge in situ/operando characterization techniques for investigations under real operating conditions

As is well documented, the composition, morphological structure, local environment around the reactive species, and the valent state of the electrocatalysts are dynamically changing during the electrocatalytic conditions, which hinders the clear identification and explicit study of the electrocatalytic active species/sites during the complex reaction process, impeding the rational design of the electrocatalysts. To shed light on the intricate electrochemical process in real time, tremendous in situ/operando techniques have been developed and applied in monitoring the compositional change, valent variation, and the morphological transformation when the electrocatalytic reaction is happening, which reveals the in situ change in the local environment and enables a fundamental comprehension of the electrocatalytic reactions. Currently, the most commonly used in situ/operando characterization techniques are based on spectroscopic and microscopic principles (such as in situ IR, Raman, localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS)), which are quite costly and require complicated devices, so limited results have been reported. This necessitates the development and exploitation of low-cost and simple in situ/operando strategies that provide sufficient and veracious information about the dynamic process of the electrocatalytic reactions.

Another concern involves the fact that current research commonly focuses on a fundamental understanding of the defect effect with a preference for point-defects, in which case the electrocatalytic HER/OER/WS activity is often evaluated in the laboratory with small current densities (<200 mA cm−2) and a relatively short-term stability test (<1 week). However, aiming at the large-scale deployment of water electrolysis technology, the rigorous requirements under real operating conditions should eventually be satisfied. This necessitates the importance of an investigation of the electrocatalytic performance under a large current density (>500 mA cm−2) with an ultralong stability test (>1 month). It is well documented that the electrocatalytic surface would undergo severe reconstruction under real operating conditions, in which the reaction kinetics would be different from those at the lab scale,447 making it another intriguing research area.

5.3. The design of novel electrolyzer configurations for highly efficient water electrolysis processes

For the lab-scale investigation of the electrocatalytic performance of defect-featuring electrocatalysts, three-electrode electrolyzers are typically used, in which case crossover of the products is unavoidable. The crossover of the products could be mitigated by separating the anode and cathode with selective permeation membranes, resulting in an H-type electrolyzer configuration. And the mass transport in the traditional electrolyzers is relatively low, which may hamper the overall energy efficiency when operated under ultrahigh current densities. For the compensation of the mass transfer controlled phenomenon, flowing the electrolyte to and away from the electrode has been proposed as one solution, which is seldom reported in water electrolysis investigations due to the limited current densities that have been reported. A further concern involves the separation of the products after electrolysis, which contributes to the increased expense of high-purity hydrogen.

In the journey from lab-scale investigations to fab-scale applications, a limited mass transfer under high potential and a novel design strategy that maximizes the overall energy efficiency is required. Fortunately, investigations into electrolyzer configurations by optimizing the membranes, gas diffusion electrode (GDE), current collectors, cell bodies, etc. have been reported.448 However, investigations concerning water electrolyzers have rarely been reported, making the search and optimization of the electrolyzer configuration an attractive and promising area. To address product separation problems, the decoupling of water electrolysis represents a promising stategy449,450 and greater efforts should be devoted to the optimization of this approach and other solutions should be explored.

5.4. The at-scale production of cost-effective defect-featuring electrocatalysts with desired imperfections

The production of defect-featuring electrocatalysts with various imperfections in the laboratory remains small scale due to the difficulty in precisely controlling the experimental parameters for the repeatable generation of desired imperfections. Towards the final target of industrial deployment of water electrolysis technology using defect-featuring electrocatalysts, cost-effective methods for the at-scale production of electrocatalysts are highly desired. A facile supersaturated co-precipitation method has been reported as a potential approach for producing amorphous electrocatalysts of ∼515 g in one trial, which retain high activity and stability for electrochemical tests.401 However, the large-scale production of defect-featuring electrocatalysts with desired imperfections and high performance is seldom reported. Thus, searching for preparation strategies for the large-scale generation of electrocatalysts with specific imperfections is highly desired.

In summary, the presence of imperfections in the crystal lattice induces a profound perturbation of the electronic structures of electrocatalysts and provides an advantageous enhancement of the electrocatalytic performance of electrocatalysts towards hydrogen evolution, oxygen evolution, and overall water splitting, thus powering a future transformation in energy from a carbon-intensive energy system to a low-carbon/carbon-neutral, or even negative-carbon energy system. When addressing the challenges in this lively field, there are chances and opportunities to turn lab-scale devices into fab-scale facilities via overcoming experimental and theoretical difficulties.
