Abstract

The use of layered perovskites is an important strategy to stabilize a formamidinium (FA) based photoactive Î±-FAPbI3 phase. However, the growth mechanism of FA-based layered perovskites and its influence on their physical properties have not yet been reported. Herein, we demonstrate an inclusive study on the growth, structure and optoelectronic properties of FA-based Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) type perovskites M2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 (M = benzylamine (PMA) or FA) during two-step sequential deposition, revealing the transformation of dominant layered perovskites from PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 to novel FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 with increasing FA:PMA ratios in precursors, and increased n values from top to bottom for the former case while a uniform distribution for the latter case. Moreover, we investigated the charge transport kinetics between quantum wells and illustrate the application of these structures in perovskite solar cells with a power conversion efficiency that exceeds 19%, accompanied by enhanced film stability in contrast to 3D analogs.


Broader context

Layered perovskite templates by small cations are expected to be ideal materials for efficient and stable solar cells, which however remain underexplored in terms of growth, physical properties and photovoltaic applications. Herein, we demonstrate for the first time FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 (FA = formamidinium) based solar cells and explored in detail in terms of their growth during two-step sequential deposition, structure and optoelectronic properties. We found that a higher FAI:PMAI (PMA = benzylamine) ratio in organic cations leads to the transformation of predominant Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper perovskites from PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 to novel FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1. The size-dependent diffusivity of cations results in increased n values from top to bottom for the PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 films while a uniform thickness distribution of the FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 of quantum wells (QWs) with perpendicular orientation to the substrate. The FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs therefore exhibit more efficient charge transport. Finally, the FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 based solar cells exhibit an enhanced stability and efficiency as high as 19.33%. This work may serve as a significant step toward understanding the formation of FA-based layered perovskites in the absence of solventâ€“precursor interactions and controlling their distribution, composition, and orientation, which provides a theoretical basis and technical guidance for stable and efficient perovskite solar cells.

Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic metal halide perovskites have attracted considerable attention owing to their excellent physical properties, including high carrier mobility, an adjustable band gap, and low manufacturing cost.1â€“6 The highest power conversion efficiency (PCE) of single-junction perovskite solar cells recently released by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) has increased to 25.5%, showing great potential in photovoltaics.7 Generally, organicâ€“inorganic halide perovskites adopt the chemical formula of ABX3, where A is an organic cation (e.g., methylamine (MA+) or FA+), B is a bivalent metal cation (e.g., Pb2+, Sn2+ or Ge2+), and X is a halide anion (typically Clâˆ’, Brâˆ’, Iâˆ’ or their mixtures). The three-dimensional (3D) network of the perovskite structure is based on corner-sharing [BX6]4âˆ’ octahedra, which are balanced with an equal number of positively charged cations residing within the small cavity spaces left behind by the metal halide network. Substituting the small A-site cation, either partially or completely, with organic cations larger than the perovskite network cavities leads to the breakup of this metal halide network into a low-dimensional structure, spatially separated by sublattices of the large cations. The inorganic framework holds great promise for optoelectronic properties, whereas organic cations themselves play a role in a vast array of interesting structural and other physical possibilities. One apparent practical application of low-dimensional structures is the enhanced stability provided by the large constituents which have higher hydrophobicity and crystal formation energy than small amines.

The first embodiments of Ruddlesdenâ€“Popper (RP) type low-dimensional organicâ€“inorganic perovskite solar cells were demonstrated by introducing phenylethylammonium (PEA+) into 3D MAPbI3, which broke the 3D structure into 2D (PEA)2(MA)2Pb3I10.8 Solar cells with the targeted material show a PCE of only 4.7%, however, the films were reported to provide improved stability when exposed to ambient humidity (52% relative humidity) at room temperature. After the success of this fabrication, great progress has been achieved via molecular chemistry, solution-processing, and a fundamental understanding of the physical properties of quantum wells (QWs) in films fabricated from MA-based 2D perovskites. For example, in situ analysis based on time-resolved grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) during solution casting established a fundamental understanding of phase crystallization kinetics during solution processing and its impact on the physical properties of QWs in the film.9â€“17 The formation of intermediate phases is important for the crystalline quality of the final film as the intermediate phases of PbI2 and PbI2-solvent solvates led to greater corrugation of the crystalline surface,18 smaller grain size, and orientation randomness of crystals. Fast fading of the solvent inhibits the formation of PbI2 crystals and the solvated phase, which in turn results in the faster insertion of organic cations into the inorganic framework. The complex formation mechanism of MA-based perovskite QWs has been well understood; this helps in achieving the desired QW distribution in the film, efficient carrier transport between QWs, and the final photovoltaic outcome.19

In contrast to these well-understood MA-based layered 2D perovskites, FA-based analogs, which are believed to be an efficient strategy for customizing the energy characteristics toward stabilizing the 3D Î±-FAPbI3 phase under harsh environmental and device operating conditions,20 remain under-represented to date. This is likely due to the phase instability of FAPbI3-based systems and the difficulty in obtaining high-quality FA-based layered perovskite films in contrast to MA-based analogs. For example, the FAPbI3 phase with a hexagonal (face-sharing) one-dimensional (1D) structure is favored at room temperature owing to its higher thermodynamically stability in contrast to its photoactive Î±-FAPbI3 phase, which adopts a cubic 3D corner-sharing [MX6]4âˆ’ octahedral framework.21â€“23 Thus, nucleation and growth either with corner-sharing or face-sharing structures should be considered when fabricating FA-based 2D layered perovskites, which makes crystallization of FA-based 2D layered perovskites more complex in contrast to that of MA-based analogs. Meanwhile, the interaction between FA cations and [PbI6]4âˆ’ octahedra is stronger24 in contrast to MA cations. FA cations interact less with the dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) solvent in contrast to MA cations, leading to a lower tendency to form a precursorâ€“solvent intermediate phase during film formation.25 The ability to release protons to generate HI in FA-based films under light is also lower than that of MA-based films.26 Nevertheless, attempts have been made to fabricate FA-based 2D perovskites over the past few years using a number of organic cations, including butylammonium (BA+), 5-ammonium valeric acid(AVA+), benzylammonium (BN+), n-pentylammonium (PA+), 2-thiophenemethylammonium (TMA+), iso-butylammonium (IBA+), and 1-adamantylmethanammonium (ADAM+).27â€“29 To date, these attempts have been limited to templating layered structures using various ligands and analyzing their potential for photovoltaic applications. In contrast to the well-elucidated growth mechanism of MA-based layered perovskites from intermediate phases formed with the solvent, the growth of FA-based layered perovskites, particularly in the absence of solventâ€“precursor intermediate phases, has not yet been reported. Furthermore, a comprehensive understanding of the influence of the growth mechanism on the physical and chemical compositions of FA-based layered perovskites remains to be explored.

Herein, we sought to increase the understanding of the growth of FA-based layered perovskites in the absence of solventâ€“precursor interactions, their structural and optoelectronic characteristics, and their application in photovoltaics based on representative RP type M2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 (M = PMA or FA) systems fabricated via two-step sequential deposition. The combination of femtosecond pumpâ€“probe transient absorption (TA), X-ray diffraction (XRD), and UV-Vis and photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy indicated the critical role of the size and stoichiometry of the loaded precursors on the growth behaviour, composition, and thickness distribution of QWs in films. We found a transformation of dominant layered perovskites from PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 to FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 perovskites with increasing FAI:PMAI ratios in the loaded precursors. The PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 film exhibited gradient segregation of 3D-like phases at the bottom surface, whereas low-n QWs tended to segregate on the top surface. By contrast, the FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 film showed a uniform thickness distribution of the QWs. QW orientation was investigated using grazing-incidence wide-angle X-ray scattering (GIWAXS) analysis. Furthermore, the charge transport and optoelectronic properties were investigated in detail to understand the influence of the QW structure on the emergent properties. Finally, we demonstrate for the first time the application of FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 film for solar cells, achieving a PCE of above 19% with much improved phase stability in contrast to 3D FAPbI3 analogs.

Results and discussion

We first prepared FA-based layered perovskite films via two-step sequential deposition, as illustrated in Fig. 1a. First, a thin layer of PbI2 film was deposited on the TiO2 electron transport layer, followed by thermal annealing for 1 min. The PbI2 film was then coated with isopropyl alcohol (IPA) solutions containing PMAI:FAI mixtures (concentration of 0.5 mol Lâˆ’1) with molar ratios varying from 2:3 to 2:9 and 2:59. The three as-cast films were thermal annealed at 150 Â°C under ambient conditions (30â€“40% relative humidity) for 15 min to obtain FA-based layered perovskite films.

The morphological evolution of the FA-based layered perovskite films was evaluated using scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The film thickness did not change significantly with the PMAI:FAI molar ratio (Fig. S1a, ESIâ€ ). Different uniformities and grain sizes were observed for the three films (Fig. 1b). The 2:3 film exhibited densely packed grains with lengths up to ca. 1 Î¼m, which is in contrast to the observation of 2D slices for MA-based RP perovskites.30,31 Such morphological differences might be attributed to different hydrogen bonds between the inorganic framework and FA+ or MA+ organic cations, which yield different nucleation/growth and lattice mismatches.24,32 A higher FAI ratio leads to larger grains, with their size increasing to âˆ¼2â€“3 Î¼m, which is close to what has been observed in 3D FAPbI3 thin films.30 The cross-sectional SEM images show a concrete-like appearance without obvious grain boundaries along the perpendicular direction for the 2:3 film. However, a higher FAI ratio results in more pinholes throughout the films, particularly for the 2:59 film, indicating different growth behaviors of the perovskite.

We observed a decrease in the diffraction intensity of the n = 2 PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs with higher FAI ratios, and the diffraction peaks completely vanished for the 2:59 film. This indicates less coherent crystallographic regions with the PMA2FAPb2I7 phase when processed from a higher FAI ratio. Importantly, although the PMA still has 2 parts in the initial precursor solution, we did not find the crystalline features of low-n PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs for the 2:59 film, except for the (111) and (202) planes of the 3D-like phases. Instead, we observed the low-n phase of FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 in the analysis that follows, so we believe that dominant layered perovskites change in the film at a high FAI ratio. Note that the lack of diffraction peaks for ordered regions with low-n QWs does not preclude their existence; instead, it might indicate merely that sufficiently coherent crystallographic regions cannot be detected by XRD.9 To better understand the formation of low-n QWs with different PMAI:FAI ratios, we used optical analysis, including UV-Vis absorption, photoluminescence (PL) and ultrafast transient absorption (TA) spectroscopy.

The UV-Vis absorption spectra show a band edge at ca. 816 nm corresponding to the 3D-like phase for the three films (Fig. 2b). An additional excitonic peak of the n = 2 PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 perovskite (PMA2FAPb2I7) was also observed at ca. 571 nm for the 2:3 and 2:9 films, which is consistent with the XRD observations indicating the co-existence of n = 2 PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 and 3D-like phase. To better understand the growth of FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 during film fabrication, we fabricated FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 films using two-step sequential deposition by introducing neat FAI on the PbI2 film, or FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 powders using ball-milling from FAI:PbI2 powders without the solvent, or FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 films via one-step spin-coating from the DMSO solvent. The PL spectra exhibited dominant emission peaks at ca. 734 nm, 780 nm, and 810 nm for the 2:3, 2:9, and 2:59 films (Fig. 2c), respectively. The charge transfer kinetics from low-n PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 or low-n FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs to 3D-like phases were compared and are shown in Fig. 3c and d, respectively. The signals of low-n QWs are much faster than those of 3D-like phases in both PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 and FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 films. Interestingly, the TA rise of 3D-like phases is accompanied by the TA decay of the PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 and FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs. This finding is consistent with what has been observed for MA-based 3D/2D perovskite films and should be ascribed to carrier transport from low-n FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 or low-n PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs to 3D-like phases.30,43 We observed a faster decay of the TA signal initially generated at low times (âˆ¼0.5â€“1 ps) within the low-n PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs, followed by a plateau-like feature up to âˆ¼100 ps and a slower decay afterward. Based on the aforementioned analyses, it can be concluded that the n = 1 phase is inhibited either in the PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 or FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 cases during two-step sequential deposition. Previous studies have shown that the n = 1 phase tends to be aligned parallel to the substrate and is unfavorable for carrier transport in the device.48 This peculiarity may indicate efficient charge transport in these three films. A key merit of the PMAI:FAI ratio in precursors is that a higher FAI ratio leads to the transformation of dominant layered perovskites from PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 to FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1. Interestingly, the films exhibited increased n values from top to bottom for the PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 case, whereas a uniform distribution of n values was observed along the perpendicular direction for the FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 case. These observations are in contrast to what have been commonly observed from previous MA-based low-dimensional perovskite films obtained from one-step spin-coating.13,15 During one-step fabrication, precursors and solvents (e.g., DMSO or N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF)) tend to form crystalline intermediate phases,13,15 which then provide a framework that releases PbI2 during thermal annealing to nucleate and grow low-n perovskite QWs.9 Therefore, the solvent plays a key in controlling the QW formation and distribution. By contrast, the QW formation during two-step deposition does not follow this pathway because of the absence of DMF or DMSO solvents. Furthermore, the distinct thickness distribution of M2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 QWs with organic cations varying from PMA to FA suggests that the organic cation is another influencing factor for the QW formation.

We attempted to connect this specific phase distribution with growth behavior during the two-step sequential deposition (Fig. 4). During one-step spinning, all the components are together and evolve in time before the deposition on the substrate surface. During two-step sequential deposition, however, the amine salt solution and PbI2 are separated and do not react with each other until the second step when they are in contact together and then ionic diffusion begins. Note that intermediate phases will not be generated during two-step sequential deposition because of the absence of DMSO or DMF solvents. During the conversion of PbI2 to perovskites after FAI:PMAI loading, FA cations diffuse and incorporate faster to the PbI2 layer underneath than the PMA cations because of the smaller ionic radius. As such, smaller organic cations were consumed in the lower part of the film, leaving a higher concentration of larger cations for the rest of the film, which would explain the preferential segregation of 3D-like phases near the bottom, whereas 2D phases existed closed to the surface, as observed for the 2:3 and 2:9 cases. This ionic diffusion-determined QW growth is in contrast to what we have previously observed during one-step deposition, where the formation energy of different QWs dominates their growth from solvents, resulting in low-n QWs at the bottom and 3D-like phases near the surface of the films.49,50 For the 2:59 case, given insufficient PMA cations, the overloaded FA cations dominated the QW growth, leading to a uniform distribution of FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we successfully synthesized FA-based low-dimensional perovskite M2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 (M = PMA/FA) via two-step sequential deposition and explored the growth mechanism in the absence of solvated intermediates and their structureâ€“property relationship. A key merit of the PMAI:FAI ratio in precursors is that a higher FA ratio leads to the transformation of dominant layered perovskites from PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 to FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1. Interestingly, the films exhibited increased n values from top to bottom in the PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 film. Meanwhile a uniform distribution of n values along the vertical direction for the FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 case was observed. Such a difference of n distribution is driven by ionic-diffusivity-dominated QW growth in the absence of solvated intermediates. FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 exhibited more efficient charge transport between QWs and a longer carrier charge lifetime in the crystals, in contrast to PMA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1. In addition, we demonstrated a FA2FAnâˆ’1PbnI3n+1 perovskite solar cell for the first time with a champion PCE of 19.33%. Moreover, these layered perovskites exhibited enhanced environmental stability corresponding to neat FAPbI3, which was fabricated and stored under the same conditions. Overall, the novel 2D perovskite structure templated by small cations not only increases the â€œplaygroundâ€ for structure modulation, but also improves the absorption spectrum range and improves the thermal and light stability for optoelectronic devices. We believe that the results may serve as a significant step toward understanding the formation of FA-based layered perovskites in the absence of solventâ€“precursor interactions and controlling their distribution, composition, and orientation, which provides a theoretical basis and technical guidance for stable and efficient perovskite solar cells.
