Abstract

In order to improve the optoelectronic performance of Ï€-conjugated molecules with electronic donors and electronic acceptors, people often choose multi-branch molecule structures, which can enhance the charge transfer ability. In this work, the photo-physical characteristics of (E)-4-(2-(3-methylquinoxalin-2-yl)vinyl)-N,N-diphenylaniline (QTPA1) and 4,4â€²-((1E,1â€²E)-quinoxaline-2,3-diylbis(ethene-2,1-diyl))bis(N,N-diphenylaniline) (QTPA2) in monodisperse systems and aggregated states were investigated in detail, showing that the photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY) of oligomers is apparently enhanced in high-polarity solutions and decreased in low-polarity solvents instead, after the oligomer is linked with a conjugated branch. Based on the pumpâ€“probe and pumpâ€“dumpâ€“probe, this unusual phenomenon is assigned to the polarity-dependent intramolecular charge transfer (CT) mechanism of oligomers induced by the conjugated branch. In the PL of aggregated state and the two-photon fluorescence (TPF), increasing the conjugated branch has a positive effect on the PL characteristics that the PL performance of QTPA2 is higher than that of the aggregated QTPA1. In the aggregated state, QTPA2 has a larger degree of Ï€ electron conjugation, which suppresses intramolecular vibrations and reduces the energy loss caused by non-radiative processes. In addition, the two-photon absorption section of QTPA2 keeps higher in comparison with that of QTPA1 owing to the enhancement of the intramolecular dipole intensity, leading to the enhancement of TPF. This research could elucidate the physical mechanism underlying the optoelectronic performance and help us develop novel organic optoelectronic materials with high performance.

Introduction

Over the past two decades, organic optoelectronic materials have been considered as alternatives to their inorganic counterparts for optoelectronic devices such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs),1,2 field-effect transistors,3 and solar cells,4 owing to their low cost,5 solution-processed fabrication,6 and flexible devices.7 In order to accelerate the commercial application of organic optoelectronic devices, a large number of organic conjugated materials have been synthesized recently. In most cases, the conjugated materials are composed of electron-donating (D) groups, electron-accepting (A) groups and Ï€-conjugated groups, which display an apparently different photo-physical process from that of inorganic semiconductors.8 Generally speaking, increasing the number of Ï€-conjugated units can enhance the molecular dipole moment of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A oligomers via affecting the charge-transfer (CT) ability, which can apparently improve the photo-physical characteristics and performance of organic optoelectronic devices.9â€“11 For example, Choi et al. exhibited that addition of Ï€-conjugated branches can improve the carrier mobility in organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) based on new Ï€-conjugated multi-branched molecules.12 Li et al. exhibited that addition of linear alkylthio groups can significantly improve the power conversion efficiency of 2D-conjugated bithienyl-benzodithiophene-alt-fluorobenzotriazole copolymers in polymer solar cells.13 Goodson et al. demonstrated that the photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY) of organic molecules (PRL dye series) can be significantly improved with the increase in branches containing a donorâ€“Ï€â€“acceptor system.10 Rogers et al. found that the addition of moderately long alkyl pendants on the fluorene bridging group not only imparts additional solubility to materials, but also increases the effective two-photon cross section.11 Our previous work demonstrated that the addition of electron acceptors (such as functionalized Î²-diketones difluoroboron complexes) can significantly improve the photoluminescence (PL) properties such as high PL intensity and long PL lifetime of pushâ€“pull chromophores.14 Recently, we have proved that linking the tert-butyl unit to difluoroboron Î²-diketonate pullâ€“push chromophores can lead to the red-shift of absorption and fluorescence peaks and accelerate the fluorescence relaxation rate.15,16 In brief, increasing the conjugated group is one of the most effective methods to improve the optoelectronic performance of organic conjugated materials at present.

Recently, two types of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A-type aminostyrylquinoxaline derivatives, (E)-4-(2-(3-methylquinoxalin-2-yl)vinyl)-N,N-diphenylaniline (named QTPA1) and 4,4â€²-((1E,1â€²E)-quinoxaline-2,3-diylbis(ethene-2,1-diyl))bis(N,N-diphenylaniline) (named QTPA2), were synthesized via Knoevenagel condensation reactions,17 which exhibit wide-range emission color turn-ability in the presence of pressure or acids. In addition, increasing the conjugated branch was found to play an unusual role in the PL performance of oligomers in the low-polarity solution that the PLQY of QTPA2 with two conjugated branches is lower than that of QTPA1. However, this unusual phenomenon has not been further confirmed in other optical fields such as nonlinear optics or aggregation photoluminescence. This unusual phenomenon has been reported in previous research, in which increasing a conjugated branch also weakened the PL intensity of pyran derivatives (named DCM) in monodisperse systems.18 Apparently, the strategy of adding conjugated groups to enhance the luminescence properties of organic optoelectronic materials has some limitations. If the corresponding physical mechanism cannot be explained clearly, it will affect the design of future organic oligomers and the performance improvement of optoelectronic devices based on organic oligomers.

In this study, we employed many steady spectroscopies to probe the photo-physical characteristics of QTPA1 and QTPA2 in the monodisperse system and the aggregated system and analyze the role of conjugated branches in the linear and nonlinear optical characteristics. The results indicate that the PL intensity only decreases in the low-polarity solution after the addition of a conjugated branch in the oligomers, whose mechanism is analyzed based on the pumpâ€“probe and pumpâ€“dumpâ€“probe technique. In the two-photon fluorescence and the aggregation PL, increasing a conjugated branch is able to improve the PL characteristics, whose origin is also discussed in detail. These studies will help us further understand the optical characteristics of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A oligomers.

Experimental methods

Materials

The materials were offered by Ran Lu group in Jilin University and the corresponding concentration in our experiment was about 5 Ã— 10âˆ’4 mol Lâˆ’1. The solutions were loaded in a 1 mmâ€“thick quartz cuvette during the measurements. All measurements in monodisperse systems were performed at room temperature.17

Thin films

The glass substrates were cleaned with deionized water, toluene, acetone and ethanol in sequence. The QTPA1 and QTPA2 films were achieved by spin-coating the QTPA1 and QTPA2 stock solution at 1000 rpm for 2 min in a N2-filled glovebox for spectroscopy measurements.

Experimental section

The steady-state absorption was measured using a UV-vis absorption spectrometer (Purkinje, TU-1810PC). The PL measurements were conducted using a fiber optic spectrometer (Ocean Optics, USB4000) at an excitation wavelength of 405 nm. The PL dynamics were detected by the time-correlated single-photon counting (TCSPC)28 technique. The temperature-dependent PL spectra were recorded using a home-built system, which involves a fiber spectrometer (Ocean Optics, Maya, 2000pro) and a temperature controller (Oxford instruments, Optistat DN-V). The correlated color temperature (CCT) values were tested by employing the white LED test, using commercially available UV-emitting LED chips with the peak emission centered at 375 nm, which were used as the excitation source for the down-conversion white LED.

Pumpâ€“probe and pumpâ€“dumpâ€“probe spectral measurements

The femtosecond transient absorption spectroscopy was reported elsewhere19 and the corresponding excitation wavelength was 400 nm. The polarization direction of excitation and probe beams was horizontal.

The pumpâ€“dump/pushâ€“probe system was pumped by a re-generatively-amplified femtosecond Ti: sapphire laser centered at 800 nm at a repetition rate of 500 Hz. The amplified beam was split into three paths. In the first path, white light continuum, acting as the probe pulse, was generated by focusing an 800 nm (1.55 eV) pulse onto a 5 mm-thick quartz cuvette filled with water, and the wavelength range was from 400 nm (3.1 eV) to 780 nm (1.55 eV). In the second path, the 400 nm pump laser was generated by doubling the 800 nm (1.55 eV) pulse via a 1 mm-thick Î²-barium borate (BBO) crystal. The third path was used to pump an optical parametric amplifier (TOPAS-C, Light Conversion) to prepare the dump pulse centered at 490 nm and 590 nm, respectively. The excitation pulses were sent to a delay line and modulated using a synchronized optical chopper (Terahertz Technologies Inc., C-995) at a frequency of 250 Hz. The time-dependent TA spectra were recorded using a highly sensitive spectrometer (Avantes AvaSpec-2048 Ã— 16). The polarization direction of the excitation and the probe pulse was horizontal. The excitation spot was âˆ¼0.3 mm in diameter.

Nonlinear optical measurements

Two-photon absorption cross-section measurements were carried out by employing an open-aperture Z-scan technique with a focal length of lens of 100 mm, and a pulse energy of 250 nJ, and the laser beam was modulated using an optical chopper at a rate of 500 Hz, 800 nm. The samples were placed in a 1 mm-thick quartz cuvette for the optical measurements. The signals were measured using a photodiode and then traveled through a lock-in amplifier (SR830, DSP), which was able to obviously suppress noise outside a narrow frequency interval, before they reached the computer. Before changing the sample, the two-photon absorption transmission spectrum of pure solvent was scanned, and then the laser light intensity was turned down until there was no solvent signal. This was done to eliminate the interference of the non-linear effect of the solvent itself. Two-photon fluorescence measurements were carried out using a CCD detector (Ocean Optics, USB4000), in which the wavelength of femtosecond laser is about 800 nm.

Results and discussion

The solution-dependent normalized absorption and photoluminescence spectra of QTPA1 and QTPA2 are shown in Fig. 1(A) and (B). As the solvent changes from toluene (TOL) to dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), the polarity of solvent apparently enhances. The absorption peak of QTPA1 red shifts from 421 to 437 nm and that of QTPA2 varies from 429 to 438 nm. Apparently, the difference between the absorption peaks is gradually diminishing, reflecting that the effect of the increased conjugated branch on steady absorption apparently weakens in high-polarity solvents. Similar phenomena have also been previously reported in the literature, which were ascribed to a greater degree of exciton delocalization between the chromophores in dimers.20 In this study, the absorption edge only red-shifts a little with the solvent polarity, suggesting that the electronic cloud of QTPA1 and QTPA2 in ground states is insensitive to the solvent polarity.21â€“27 The PL peak of QTPA1 in TOL is located at 506 nm and that in dimethylformamide (DMF) shifts to 620 nm. In dipolar solvents, the dipole moment of the excited Dâ€“Ï€â€“A molecules is stronger than that of the surrounding solvent molecules, resulting in an energy transfer process from the excited QTPA molecules to the surrounding solvent molecules via dipoleâ€“dipole interactions.17,28 Therefore, the excited state of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A molecules has a stronger solvent dependence compared to the ground state. Therefore, it is not surprising to observe such a large PL red-shift in high-polarity solvents. Similar phenomena can be observed in the solution of QTPA2, in which the PL peak of QTPA2 in TOL is located at 507 nm and that in DMF shifts to 658 nm. The shift of PL peak in QTPA2 is larger than that in QTPA1, as the solvent changes from TOL to DMF. This indicates that QTPA2 is more sensitive to the solvent polarity, after the addition of a conjugated branch in the oligomer.29,30 In other words, the difference in PL peaks between QTPA1 and QTPA2 gradually enhances with the polarity of solvent, suggesting that the effect of the increase in the conjugated branch on steady PL becomes obvious in high-polarity solvents. Based on steady spectra data, the role of the increased conjugated branch gradually weakens in the ground state but enhances in the excited state, as the polarity of the solvent enhances.

In order to investigate the unusual photoluminescence phenomenon occurring in the low-polarity solution as mentioned above, the photo-excitation relaxation process of QTPA1 and QTPA2 in solvents with low (TOL) and high (DMF) polarities was probed by employing the pumpâ€“probe technique. When QTPA1 is resolved in TOL (in Fig. 2(A)), the pumpâ€“probe spectra involve a positive band from 400 to 450 nm corresponding to the ground-state bleaching (GSB) signal, and a negative spectral feature from 500 to 800 nm representing the excited-state absorption (ESA) signal. It should be noted that there is a positive signal between 450 nm and 500 nm, and it decays rapidly for the initial 2 ps and then becomes a negative signal, suggesting that the valley should be related to the stimulated emission (SE) signal that originated from the local excitation (LE),33 reflecting that a LE state appears in the photoexcitation relaxation of excited QTPA1. According to the spectral feature at about 1 ps, the LE state undergoes a rapid vibrational relaxation and forms the LEâ€² state. After that, the excited QTPA1 relaxes from the LE state to the hybridization between the LE state and the CT state (HLCT),25,34,35 owing to the variance in the GSB signal in the initial 6 ps. With a long delay (as shown in Fig. 2(B)), the TA features all monotonous decays, corresponding to the relaxation characteristics of HLCT.36,37 Herein, the position of ESA peak located at 650 nm is almost invariable with the delay time, implying that the solventâ€“solute interaction is too weak to be considered.38,39 Herein, the LE state occupies the dominant position of the HLCT, in which the HLCT state can be responsible for the PL of QTPA1.40â€“42 The relaxation mechanism of excited QTPA1 in toluene is given in Fig. 2(C). There is a three-state system, involving the ground state, LE state and CT state in the relaxation process of excited QTPA1. After photoexcitation, the excited QTPA1 begins to relax from the LE state to the LEâ€² state via rapid vibrational relaxation, followed by a decay to ground state via passing the HLCT that is composed of LE and CT states.

If another conjugated branch is linked into QTPA1, the relaxation process of QTPA2 may become simpler. The transient absorption data shows that the PL signal of QTPA1 in toluene is determined by the LE state of the HLCT state and that in DMF is dependent on the CT state of the HLCTâ€² state. Apparently, the polarity of the solvent causes the variance in the photoexcitation mechanism of excited QTPA1. If a new conjugated branch is linked into QTPA1, the new photoexcitation mechanism is different from that of QTPA1, in which the CTâ€² state determined the emission process of excited QTPA2, no matter the solvent owns low polarity or high polarity. Considering that the LE state can reduce the energy loss related to the non-radiative channels compared to the CT state, QTPA1 owns a more efficient fluorescence yield in comparison with that of QTPA2.44 In the high-polarity solution, the emission state of QTPA1 was determined by the HLCTâ€² state, which is controlled by the CT state, and that of QTPA2 is also CTâ€™ state. As mentioned above, the PLQY decreases with the conjugated branch in the low-polarity solution. In order to further analyze the role of conjugated branches in other optical performance, nonlinear optical tests were employed, in which the concentration of QTPA1 and QTPA2 in solution is fixed at 5 Ã— 10âˆ’4 M. QTPA1 in TOL and DMF can emit photons, as shown in Fig. 8(A) and (B), after they are excited by a femtosecond laser at 800 nm. The absorption spectra of QTPA1 in TOL and DMF suggests that the PL dose not originate from single-photon excitation. The integrated PL intensity (IPLI) vs the excitation intensity is summarized in Fig. 8(C). In double logarithmic coordinates, the IPLI almost linearly enhances with the pump intensity and the corresponding slop is much close to 2, suggesting that the two-photon fluorescence (TPF) can be responsible for this PL phenomenon.16,54 Moreover, Fig. 8(C) suggests that the PL intensity gradually decreases with the polarity of the solution. Similar phenomena can be observed in the TOL and DMF solutions of QTPA2, which also exhibit apparent two-photon fluorescence (TPF) characteristics and exhibit similar tendencies. In addition, the TPF spectra of QTPA1 and QTPA2 both were observed slightly narrower and shifted in comparison with one-photon excited ones, no matter the polarity of solution is low or high. This phenomenon should be assigned to the reabsorption effect.60,61 For the nonlinear optical performance, it was found that the TPF intensity of QTPA2 is stronger than that of QTPA1 in both low-polarity and high-polarity solutions, reflecting that adding a conjugated branch can play a positive role. In other words, the unusual phenomenon disappeared in the nonlinear TPF characteristics. In order to understand the physical origin behind this phenomenon, the two-photon absorption (TPA) cross-sections of QTPA1 and QTPA2 were probed by employing the femtosecond Z-scan technique.54,55,62 As shown in Fig. 8(D), the Z-scan data can be plotted as an intensity-dependent transmission curve.63 The transmittance decreases as the sample gets closer to the laser focus (z = 0), which is attributed to the nonlinear absorption process. Conclusions

We systemically analyzed the role of conjugated branches in monodisperse systems and aggregated states by PL behaviors. Adding a conjugated branch can decrease the PLQY of Dâ€“Ï€â€“A oligomers in low-polarity solutions and improve the PL characteristics in high-polarity solutions, which induce an unusual phenomenon. The pumpâ€“probe and pumpâ€“dumpâ€“probe data show that addition of conjugated branches changes the photoluminescence mechanism, in both low- and high-polarity solutions, which originated from the variance in the intramolecular charge transfer mechanism. The addition of the conjugated branch can improve the two-photon fluorescence and the photoluminescence of the aggregated state. The former benefit from the enhancement of two-photon absorption cross section and the latter are assigned to the reduction in the intersystem spanning process of molecules and non-radiative processes. These experiments demonstrated the value of QTPA molecules in practical applications. Moreover, the final results provide help for systemically understanding the strategy of increasing the conjugated branch and further understanding their applications in molecular engineering of optoelectronic conjugated oligomers.
