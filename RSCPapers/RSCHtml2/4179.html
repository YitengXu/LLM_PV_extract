Abstract

Smart biomaterials have been rapidly advancing ever since the concept of tissue engineering was proposed. Interacting with human cells, smart biomaterials can play a key role in novel tissue morphogenesis. Various aspects of biomaterials utilized in or being sought for the goal of encouraging bone regeneration, skin graft engineering, and nerve conduits are discussed in this review. Beginning with bone, this study summarizes all the available bioceramics and materials along with their properties used singly or in conjunction with each other to create scaffolds for bone tissue engineering. A quick overview of the skin-based nanocomposite biomaterials possessing antibacterial properties for wound healing is outlined along with skin regeneration therapies using infrared radiation, electrospinning, and piezoelectricity, which aid in wound healing. Furthermore, a brief overview of bioengineered artificial skin grafts made of various natural and synthetic polymers has been presented. Finally, by examining the interactions between natural and synthetic-based biomaterials and the biological environment, their strengths and drawbacks for constructing peripheral nerve conduits are highlighted. The description of the preclinical outcome of nerve regeneration in injury healed with various natural-based conduits receives special attention. The organic and synthetic worlds collide at the interface of nanomaterials and biological systems, producing a new scientific field including nanomaterial design for tissue engineering.

1. Introduction

The terms smart biomaterials and constructs are used to describe: (1) biomaterials and structures with instructive or triggering effects on cells and tissues, created by engineering the material's responsiveness to both internal and external stimuli or by possessing smartly customized properties and functions that can encourage tissue repair and regeneration, such as pH, temperature, ionic strength, and magnetism, to promote damaged tissue repair and regeneration and (2) materials possessing intelligently tailored individual properties and controlled functions to actively1,2 participate in tissue regeneration in a valuable way. Despite the fact that simple polymeric biomaterials offer the necessary mechanical and physical qualities for tissue development, their poor biomimetic features and lack of interactions with human progenitor cells continue to impede the promotion of functional tissue creation. Therefore, the development of smart functional biomaterials that react to stimuli might be the next option for generating smart 3D biomimetic scaffolds that actively engage with human stem cells and progenitors as well as maintain structural integrity to rapidly build functional tissue. This article reviews the recent developments in smart biomaterials and constructs for tissue repair and regeneration. These biomaterials are used to evaluate, treat, enhance, repair, or replace tissues or organs in the body. Smart biomaterial advances have had a major influence on all sectors of medicine over the past several decades, revolutionizing tissue engineering, drug delivery, immunological engineering, and medical device manufacturing. The growing interest in precision medicines tailored to the individual needs of patients, the emergence of gene and immune therapies, advances in three-dimensional (3D) printing technologies, and more recently, focused modalities that have the potential to expand the boundaries of modern medicine, are all driving up demand for biomaterials.13 Tissue engineering and regenerative medicine techniques that utilize biomaterials offer amazing promise to overcome the current limitations of traditional methods and materials. Old age, trauma, fracture nonunion, removal of bone tumors, and other causes of large bone defects or injuries are major orthopedic issues that have a negative impact on human health and well-being.4,5 The use of autologous bone grafts to correct bone abnormalities is still considered the gold standard. However, the drawbacks of autologous bone grafting include secondary damages, high donor site morbidity, restriction to a specific shape, and insufficient autogenous bone. Accordingly, it is not widely used in therapeutic settings. Alternatively, tissue engineering makes it possible to create artificial bone grafts that may be used to repair major bone defects. Similarly, burns, diabetes, and other factors may cause wounds that alter the look and physiological activities of the skin. A significant region of damage increases the body's metabolism, causes excessive water and protein loss, and may even be deadly.3 Thus, to prevent cell death caused by direct cell injection, tissue engineering not only prevents wounds but also creates an appropriate growth environment for seed cells. In addition to being biocompatible, the ideal scaffold should promote cell attachment and proliferation. It must remove secretions and limit the loss of bodily fluids to prevent bacterial infection and provide a moist environment favorable to healing. Finally, the repair of the human nervous system is a complex and interconnected process, resulting in substantial hurdles in its research and understanding. Considering the advancements in the domains of tissue engineering and regenerative medicine, the scientific community has observed an upsurge in biomaterial-based techniques for neural tissue regeneration therapies. However, although tremendous results have been observed in pre-clinical trials for each of these tissue defect fillers for bone, skin, and nerves, clinical trials with these implantable scaffolds are still required, which will be a major accomplishment, undoubtedly promoting the research on smart biomaterials and tissue engineering. The anticipated data will be important in establishing these bridges as strong instruments in building long-awaited successful therapeutical procedures. 2. Smart biomaterials for bone tissue engineering

Bone tissue engineering relies on three main building blocks, i.e., scaffolds, cells, and growth factors (GFPs).6 Ideally, a scaffold should be biodegradable and biocompatible, bioactive, osteoconductive, and osteoinductive. Biomaterials and additives, such as medicines, growth factors (GFs), and stem cells, have been proven to be effective in the development of artificial bone scaffolds for the healing of broken bones. Composite materials, which combine two or more elements with exceptional qualities, are commonly employed in bone tissue engineering because of their versatility. Polymers such as calcium phosphates and calcium carbonate have been utilized to create scaffolds. For example, hydrogel networks, conductive polymers, and signaling molecules may be added to bone tissue engineering scaffolds to enhance their biocompatibility. Smart scaffolds and stem cell constructions for bone tissue engineering are two examples of smart biomaterial-based techniques, in addition to smart medication delivery systems for enhancing bone regeneration. The use of smart polymers and smart materials may help shift biofilm species from a pathogenic composition toward a healthy one, hence reducing the risk of drug resistance. Besides delivering and guiding stem cells to enhance tissue regeneration, these smart biomaterials may also administer medicines and bioactive substances with spatially and temporally controlled release, modulate/suppress the formation of biofilms and fight infections in wound sites. Regenerative medicine and hard tissue engineering may be improved significantly with the use of smart biomaterials, which have the potential to revolutionize the field of medicine.

2.2. Electrical nanomaterials harnessing piezoelectricity and magnetic conductivity smart biomaterials

2.5. Composite smart biomaterials for bone tissue engineering

Composite biomaterials are made up of two or more components. The use of composite materials is primarily intended to increase the processability, printing performance, mechanical characteristics, and bioactivity of scaffolds. Due to the particular biological responses between scaffolds and live tissues, Ti6Al4V, HA, b-TCP, and BG are frequently used as bioactive biomaterials. Among the newest studied materials, MgZn, PLLA, and tungsten disulfide nanoparticles (WS2) have shown promising outcomes.256258 Natural polymers (such as collagen, gelatin, silk fibroin, and chitosan) and synthetic polymers (such as PLA, PGA, and PCL) are the most common bioresorbable biomaterials used in bone tissue engineering (such as HA, b-TCP, and BGs). Because of their outstanding bone regeneration potential, scaffolds incorporating additives (such as growth factors) have been used in clinical applications. However, the extensive development of scaffolds is hampered by their high production costs. Ti6Al4V is a fantastic example of a metal matrix composite.259,260 The coating of Ti6Al4V scaffolds with a tantalum layer can dramatically improve bone ingrowth, osteointegration, and osteogenesis261 together with simvastatin/hydrogel262 or polydopamine-assisted hydroxyapatite coating (HA/pDA).263 However, although metal matrix composites, such as Ti6Al4V, have numerous advantages, their inability to degrade limits their potential as optimal materials. Scaffolds have made significant progress with the use of polymer matrix composites and ceramic matrix composites in bone tissue engineering. Biodegradability and mechanical characteristics are just two of the many advantages of polymer composites.264273 The predominant inorganic elements of natural bone are ceramic materials, especially HA.274 Composite materials made up of ceramic and polymer components have attributes that make them ideal for making scaffolds for bone tissue engineering.264,266,269,271 For example, the vitro bioactivity and biocompatibility of scaffolds increased when tungsten disulfide nanoparticles (WS2) were combined with microporous bioactive glass in a specific concentration range, resulting in composites with enhanced compressive strength through the stress transfer mechanism,258 as summarized in Table 5.

3. Smart biomaterials for skin tissue engineering

The skin is an essential first line of defense in the human immune system.7 It is not uncommon for wound healing to be the same regardless of the type of tissue, including the skin. Several factors influence the ability of wounds to heal and regenerate new skin tissue, including the type of wound (epidermal, deep dermal, and full-thickness), the degree of tissue damage caused by burns or physical trauma (first, second, and third-degree), the amount of moisture around the wound, and the presence of inflammation or secondary infections.8 There are several growth factors involved in skin wound healing, including epidermal growth factors (EGF) and alpha and beta tissue growth factors (TGF- and TGF-, respectively). Natural substances with anti-inflammatory, anti-microbial, and antioxidant characteristics, such as turmeric (active component curcumin) and honey, are often used in traditional skin wound care methods to speed up the healing process. Warm or cold fomentation may minimize swelling and speed up the healing process (Fig. 3).8,9

The simple contraction and proliferation of cells in wounds are needed to cure small wounds. Alternatively, there are several hazards associated with big skin wounds given that they take much longer to heal, and thus more likely to develop into chronic wounds (e.g., inflammation, infection, and scarring).8 Skin grafts are the first choice for the treatment of skin wounds. Autogenic, allogeneic, and xenogeneic skin transplants are the three types of grafts. Autogenic and allogenic grafts both have the potential to produce immuno-rejection and disease transmission; however, their status is the gold standard in organ transplantation.10 In contrast, skin replacements are a much more efficient choice than skin transplants in terms of time and cost. Cellular (containing cells) and non-cell-containing skin replacements (SNS) are used to heal skin wounds.11 Biobrane, Integra, Alloderm, Transcyte, Dermagraft, and Apligraf are all commercially available skin replacements, which each have drawbacks that limit their effectiveness as wound healing treatments. In general, these products are expensive.9,1114 The core principle of skin tissue engineering is to either create new, biocompatible alternatives for skin tissue or rebuild existing skin tissue. The cutting-edge techniques for skin regeneration treatments include the use of cells or cell implants, the transfer of growth factors to tissue, and the use of diverse matrices, such as scaffolds, to create three-dimensional (3D) cellular structures.15 Biomaterial modalities, such as infrared radiation (IR), electrospinning, and piezoelectric stimulation, may be used in conjunction with these techniques to enhance the process of wound healing. Here, we provide a quick introduction to these three modalities. For photorejuvenation, a short burst of IR-induced reactive oxygen species (ROS) has been identified in several studies to be advantageous. There was an increase in the secretion of transforming growth factor-1 (TGF-1), an anti-inflammatory cytokine, which activates fibroblasts for enhanced wound healing, when 812 nm IR radiation was employed for full-thickness skin wound healing in rats.16 Studies have also been conducted using infra-red radiation ( = 950 nm) to promote fibroblast proliferation in vitro, with positive results.17 For the fabrication of polymer-based nanofibrous scaffolds, electrospinning is one of the techniques employed. Electrically spun fibers can be fabricated with diameters in the nano to micro range, depending on the polymer solution used in the electrospinning process.18,19 The addition of bioactive compounds, either directly to the scaffold material or by incorporation in the pre-formed scaffold, may be used to alleviate the problem of bacterial infections. A variety of bioactive compounds, including growth factors, may also be placed on electrospun nanofibers. An endogenous electric field (EF) is produced by changes in the transepithelial potential (TEP) of the injured epithelial layer, regardless if the skin lesion is acute or chronic. Subsequently, the EF activity modulates the skin-cell behavior and facilitates regeneration. Angiogenesis and keratinocyte migration may be influenced by endogenous EF.20,21 Electrotaxis, a process in which certain cells migrate toward the cathode (e.g., fibroblasts, keratinocytes, and epithelial cells),21 while others (e.g., endothelial cells) migrate toward the anode, may be guided by EFs produced in vitro or in vivo. Electrical stimulation of a wound site has also been utilized in an inpatient environment as a means of enhancing wound healing. Exogenous EF treatment for wound healing may be widely used if it can be administered at the site of a wound without the need for external electrical equipment.22,23

3.1. Antibacterial smart biomaterials for artificial skin grafts

The occurrence of persistent infections is a defining feature of chronic wounds. Most chronic wound infections, including Staphylococcus aureus and Pseudomonas aeruginosa, can form bacterial biofilms.

Furthermore, bacteria in mature biofilms have up to 500 times higher antibiotic resistance than planktonic cells (freely living). Systemic antibiotics should only be administered if sepsis, abscess production, cellulitis, osteomyelitis, or lymphangitis are present.282 Also considering that antibiotics come with certain side effects and often have compliance issues, there is a need for alternative approaches to achieve infection-free wound healing. The antimicrobial properties of silver metal ions (Ag) are well-documented, where silver nanoparticles (nAg) are the most extensively employed reinforcement in polymer matrixes for chronic wound healing. Chitosan gel reinforced with nAg may inhibit the development of bacterial biofilms and kill bacteria in existing biofilms, according to research.283285 Furthermore, compared to the silver sulfadiazine employed as the reference treatment in trials, the new chitosan/nAgn formulation showed lower cytotoxicity.286 Another form of nanofiller employed in the production of antibacterial skin grafts and wound dressings is nanosized titanium dioxide particles (nTiO2). It has been shown that incorporating nTiO2 in a polymer matrix increases its mechanical characteristics while simultaneously providing antibacterial activity against a variety of Gram-positive and Gram-negative microorganisms. However, nTiO2 particles have been found to cause inflammatory reactions, cytotoxicity, and the formation of reactive oxygen species in a range of cell types in vitro. It is possible to reduce the cytotoxicity of nTiO2 by gradually releasing titanium ions from composite biomaterials, which provides antibacterial protection and accelerates wound healing.287,288 In addition, a chitosan/pectin/nTiO2/nanocomposite was produced, which showed antibacterial activity (tests on E. coli, S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, and B. subtilis), was nontoxic to mouse fibroblasts, and accelerated wound healing in a rat model.287 Also, antibacterial chitosan nTiO2 nanocomposite was created, which promoted rapid wound healing and a consistent immune response in a rat model.289 Likewise, a bilayered nanocomposite made of chitosan nTiO2 as the upper layer and an ECM sheet obtained from human adipose tissue as the lower layer was developed for full-thickness wound healing. 3.3. Electrospinning smart biomaterials for skin tissue engineering

Electrospinning is a method of fabricating fibers with diameters ranging from the nano to micro scale using polymer solutions.321328 Electrospinning is a method employed for the creation of a fibrous membrane on a collector by applying a high voltage a droplet projecting from the tip of a needle, resulting in an electrically charged jet of polymeric solution.322324,326,328 A variety of characteristics may be employed to modify the morphological and mechanical properties of nanofibers. These factors may be linked to the molecular weight of the polymer, viscosity and conductivity of the polymer solution; ambient variables such as temperature and humidity; and processing circumstances such as feed rate, voltage, needle-to-collector distance, and collector type.321,323,324,326 Electrospinning has become a popular technology in biomedical research, particularly tissue engineering, due to its capacity to alter nanofiber properties.323 It is also a straightforward, reliable, cost-effective, and versatile process, which can be utilized with a variety of polymers,323,324,326,329 such as chitosan,330332 polylactic acid (PLA),333,334 and polycaprolactone (PCL).332,335 Additionally, the highly interconnected porosity and high surface-to-volume ratio of electrospun nanofibers enable the transport of moisture and nutrients to the wound site and promote angiogenesis, collagen production, and cell adhesion, migration, growth, and differentiation.323,324,326 During successive phases of the electrospinning process, the characteristics of the nanofibers may be tailored (Fig. 4)336 to improve the features of the scaffold.

4. Smart biomaterials for nerve tissue engineering

Nerve damage in the periphery can be repaired surgically; however, if there is substantial substance loss (greater than 5 mm), grafts from various sources, including nerve guidance conduits (NGC), are required for repair.24 NGCs reduce myofibroblast infiltration and scar tissue formation and provide physical support for regenerating nerves.25 Before conducting in vivo experiments on different animal models, it is standard procedure to conduct preliminary in vitro studies. Mice and rats are usually used for short gaps up to 15 mm, while for long gaps, animal models such as rabbits, pigs, lambs, cats, dogs, and primates are typically employed.26,27 The above-mentioned strategy for nerves, similar to that for bone and skin, necessitates three prerequisites, i.e., scaffolds, cells, and growth factors. Hydrogels, which are crosslinked polymer networks and highly water-absorbent, have been widely developed as injectable scaffolds for central nervous system (CNS) regeneration. Currently, the main strategies for treating CNS injury and disease are drug delivery and cell transplantation. Drugs/cells can be delivered to the target sites using injectable hydrogels. Furthermore, glial scar formation serves as the major impediment to axon regeneration following CNS injury or disease.28 Thus, to solve this problem, injectable hydrogels may fill irregular holes for structural support, while simultaneously creating a suitable microenvironment for axonal development. Axonal development is insignificant if axons wander randomly because axons require guidance for effective regeneration to occur.29 To substitute autologous nerve grafts for peripheral nerve system (PNS) regeneration, NGC, a tubular scaffold that connects two severed nerve stumps, was designed. NGCs are used to improve the healing of long-distance peripheral nerve lesions in which axons must stretch over a large nerve gap and attach to the distal nerve segment.30 Growth factors and an extracellular matrix may be produced by cells put into the damaged nerve. Neural and other stem cells are being studied extensively in both CNS and PNS regeneration, with Schwann cells the most often employed in PNS regeneration.31 Furthermore, the fundamental qualities of an ideal nerve conduit are outlined and discussed in the nerve section, together with the advantages and shortcomings of natural-based biomaterials employed in the fabrication of nerve conduits (Fig. 6).

4.1. Smart biomaterials used as nerve conduits

In both the central nervous system (CNS) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), nerve regeneration is a significant therapeutic problem. Degeneration of the central nervous system (CNS) caused by disease (such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease) or trauma (such as traumatic brain injury and spinal cord injury) frequently ends in lifelong paralysis and loss of sensation. Spontaneous neuronal regeneration in the CNS is usually ineffective, whereas the PNS has an inherent potential to regenerate. However, PNS regeneration only occurs across very small distances. In many situations, damage to the PNS causes motor and sensory dysfunction. For neuroprotection and nerve regeneration, biomaterials in the form of scaffolds or nanoparticles have attracted significant interest. To deliver medications, nanoparticles can be made from a variety of lipids, polymers, metals, and carbon compounds.378 Collagen, gelatin, chitosan, alginate, hyaluronan, silk fibroin, poly(L-lactic acid), poly(glycolic acid), polycaprolactone, polyphosphoester, and polyurethane are among the natural and synthetic polymers used in neural regeneration. 4.5. Future design of smart biomaterials for nerve conduits

Conventional data processing technologies in conjunction with surface modification procedures have been successful in the production of NGCs to date.510 To create channels, natural-based biopolymers are primarily treated by casting porous membranes, and then wrapping, electrospinning, and dip-molding them. However, these approaches do not allow the encapsulation of biological signals such as growth factors and nerve supporting cells into the conduit to aid the regeneration process. Non-conventional methods have recently gained popularity as methods for processing natural-based polymers into NGCs.511 Together with other benefits, 3D bioprinting allows a tailored geometry to be obtained via the clinical imaging of injured nerves, and biological cues in the manufacturing process, which improve the efficiency of biological stimuli following in vivo implantation,512 has recently altered the approach of using bio-inks as forming materials in the production of NGCs, emphasizing the advantages of natural-based biopolymers over synthetic ones. Moreover, recent findings revealed that 3D bioprinting can be used to create neural guidance channels, lumen loading cells, and growth factor gradients throughout the length of nerve guides.513 Alternatively, recent biomaterial design advances have focused on the utilization of modified natural polymers to provide superior mechanical qualities.471,514 The use of natural polymers such as methacrylate is now widely used in additive manufacturing to create complex geometries. For example, gelatin methacrylate (GelMA) has been used in several sectors of tissue engineering due to its biocompatibility and mechanical tunability, and NGC with varying channel topologies has been generated via 3D bioprinting of GelMA.515 GelMA hydrogels manufactured in 3D have been investigated as nerve conduits with encouraging results. Because of its high hydrophilicity, it can be combined with nanoparticles516,517 or platelets518 to boost peripheral nerve regeneration and functional recovery.

5. Prospects and future direction

To date, although the biomaterials used for bone regeneration have shown promise, not all bone abnormalities are the same. For example, the healing of significant bone defects caused by trauma necessitates the use of a mechanically capable scaffold.519 Alternatively, arthroplasty needs measures to extend the life of implants, where the longevity of traditional materials is still a severe drawback in this environment.520 The key to success in this application is to improve the osseointegration of current implants by surface modification.521,522 Orthopedic infection is another important obstacle to implant-based bone repair, and other material features such as antibacterial capabilities should be considered.

Furthermore, a variety of patient-related variables, both local and systemic, such as old age, gender, pre-existing chronic diseases, and the use of certain drugs, may affect the biological milieu for bone repair.523 Nanotechnological techniques used for bone regeneration may deliver more effective and longer-lasting implants, lower infection rates, and promote bony healing, all of which may lead to better patient outcomes. Nanotechnology, in particular, has enabled the development of materials that can overcome the challenges associated with enhancing bone regeneration from a variety of perspectives, such as simulating the nano-composition and structure of bone, while also acting as a delivery vehicle for bioactive molecules and/or cells.524 Nano-biomaterials that can mimic more than one of the features of bone discussed herein may outperform conventional materials in clinical setting. Nanotechnology, for example, provides oncology patients with unique ways to integrate drug delivery functionalities into osteoinductive biomaterials, which may then be employed for both bone regeneration and cancer treatment.525527

There are numerous issues that may emerge if wound healing is delayed, including a greater psychological load and expense. When a patient has diabetes or an immune system that is already impaired, complications such as systemic infection or even amputation are likely to occur from even a little incision. Furthermore, is very difficult to treat a big wound, whether it has skin abnormalities or not, in a healthy young adult. Accordingly, wound dressings and applications, including negative pressure devices, hyperbaric oxygen treatment, and electrical stimulation, have flourished during the last few decades.528 There are several studies demonstrating the healing benefits of piezoelectricity-based electrical stimulation on wounds.529 However, in actual practice, electrical stimulation is often used for pain management and rehabilitation rather than wound healing. For wound healing, the huge size of the extracorporeal power supply is a key impediment, in addition to its high cost, unpleasant wearability and inability to monitor the healing process in real time. Currently, nano generators are employed to convert all of the energy we use every day into electricity.530 There is strong clinical evidence that IR may promote cell death through apoptosis, necrosis, and anoikis selectively.531 As a physiological reaction, it also causes cell differentiation rather than proliferation.532 For this particular therapy, electrospun nanofibrous scaffolds have shown efficacy.336 Shortly, biosensing systems may be entirely included inside nanofibers, similar to how drug delivery systems are inserted into nanofibrous scaffolds. We predict that additional commercialized items and rigorous methodology will be published in the area of employing nanofibrous scaffolds for wound healing and skin regeneration in the future years.

Finally, natural-based biomaterial-derived conduits have similar features to synthetic conduits, making them equally appropriate for peripheral nerve regeneration. Natural-based biomaterials are also bioactive, which is a crucial factor to consider. The impact of bioactivity on nerve regeneration has received significant attention in the literature, with some researchers proposing that the release of neurotrophic factors or the existence of bioactive binding sites inside a conduit may help neuron regeneration. Synthetic materials have the advantage of being easily tweaked and controlled, but they lack locations for specific proteins to connect to and cells to interact with, resulting in a lack of integration with native tissue. Researchers have employed a variety of strategies to improve conduit function, including the addition of internal filler hydrogels or cells that release neurotrophic factors, as well as direct conduit alteration. To increase their bioactivity, certain conduits are directly changed to enable the covalent attachment of biochemical cues such as proteins, peptide sequences, and growth hormones. Natural materials offer the benefit of providing the necessary biological locations for proteins to attach and biological signals to influence cell activity. Although this bioactivity is less controlled than that of synthetic biomaterials, the release of molecules such as chitooligosaccharides enhances nerve regeneration without interfering with other biological processes.454,455 Most preclinical investigations in the literature focus on nerve regeneration inside the conduit and biomaterial local effects in the implantation site rather than systemic repercussions. Indeed, few researchers have considered the systemic effects of biomaterials, which may be a technique for addressing the challenges of non-controlled multi-bioactivity of distinct biopolymers. Alternatively, researchers prefer to view the existence of bioactive sites on natural biopolymers as a positive trait rather than a flaw. Furthermore, some experts feel that to boost the bioactivity of natural biomaterials such as silk, they must be modified using several methods.533

In the future, researchers should focus on better investigating the association of biomaterials with the body to develop better immunoprotective biocompatible materials, embracing bioorthogonal and biocompatible chemistries for the synthesis of biomaterials, including living polymerization and click chemistry, together with incorporation of natural or biocompatible building blocks (e.g., natural metabolites, Generally Recognized As Safe substances) in biomaterial design. Improving our knowledge of material-cell interactions can be accomplished by simplifying the interactions between the two components and using simple in vitro or in vivo models that accurately capture the intended interaction. For example, certain of the functional groups of the material can be shielded or changed, enabling identification of the important entities engaged in influencing cellular contact or behavior, which can be further assessed by reporter assays related to the activity of particular cellular pathways (e.g., inflammation and proliferation). Another way to improve our knowledge of these interactions is to use computer models of the surface chemical structure of materials.

6. Conclusions

In conclusion, nanostructured materials have demonstrated that they have the complexity required to meet the difficulties in this new field. Because the future of bone regeneration is oriented toward a more individualized approach, nanostructured immunomodulatory materials will be among the most disruptive bone regenerative technologies. All these skin graft engineering advancements based on recombinant technology may pave the way for the development of next-generation smart biomaterials shortly. Overall, we believe that the technical advances in fundamental science and engineering, together with materials science research, have enormous future potential. Preclinical and clinical research on new skin biomaterials may open the door for effective treatments and the commercialization of skin-based products. Other information regarding conduit manufacture and data about conduit features, such as wall thickness or porosity, are frequently omitted from preclinical investigations, which may be important in understanding how the qualities of conduits impact nerve regeneration, and thus must be investigated further. Consequently, despite the fact that conduits have been shown to effectively support nerve regeneration and are often utilized, nerve autografts remain the gold standard for mending lengthy nerve lesions, emphasizing the need for more effective alternatives. Smart biomaterials can have a significant impact on the translation of preclinical studies into the clinic, but a number of factors must be considered, including regulatory and manufacturing constraints (such as material complexity and ease of scalability), consistency in performance, and biocompatibility over time. There may be regulatory and manufacturing issues and difficulties in demonstrating high levels of consistency in patients for smart biomaterials that comprise a blend of biologics, medication, or tissue components, each with its own specific regulatory process. Using smart materials in tissue engineering, the 3D printing of organs may become more stable and simpler, while also better integrated with the patient's own tissue, which can help prevent rejection and inflammation in the patient. To enhance biomaterial design, computational and machine learning technologies may be used to better anticipate the interactions with physiological components such as serum proteins, cell membranes, and cellular receptors, which have a significant influence.
