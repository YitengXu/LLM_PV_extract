Abstract

Further development of new building blocks is crucial for realizing next-generation, high-performance organic semiconducting materials. In the paper, the design and synthesis of a novel Ï€-extended analogue of isoindigo, vinylidenedithiophenmethyleneoxindole (VDTOI), and two VDTOI-based copolymers is reported. The centrosymmetric VDTOI unit possesses a special acceptorâ€“donorâ€“acceptor structure and contains a highly planar conjugated backbone because of the presence of Sâ‹¯O conformational locks. Of special importance is the fact that the VDTOI unit has the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital and energy levels of âˆ’5.35 eV/âˆ’3.42 eV, which are similar properties to those of the thiophene-flanked diketopyrrolopyrrole building block. The previously mentioned structural features indicate that VDTOI might be a potential building block for constructing polymeric semiconductors. As a trial, two VDTOI-based copolymers were synthesized, namely P1 and P2. The two copolymers show good thermal stability and broad absorption spectra ranging from 330 to 740 nm. Their HOMO energy levels of around âˆ’5.40 eV match well with the work function (5.13 eV) of a gold (Au) electrode, which is indicative of effective hole injections from the Au electrodes to polymer semiconductor films. Both the P1- and P2-based field-effect transistors exhibited typical p-type transport characteristics. The highest mobility of 0.35 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 was achieved in P1-based devices.

Introduction

The third-generation polymers, donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) copolymers, have been widely investigated because of their intriguing and adjustable electronic and optoelectronic properties for potential application in printable opto-electronic devices.1â€“3 During the past few years, significant progress in polymeric light-emitting diodes,4â€“6 organic photovoltaics,7â€“9 and organic field-effect transistors (OFETs)10â€“14 has encouraged the continued designing and discovering of new building blocks for constructing new polymers with high thermal stability, broad and intense absorption, and prompt and efficient charge transport. Up to the present time, however, most of the record high mobilities are still limited to several versatile electron-donating or electron-withdrawing building blocks derived from Dâ€“A type copolymers. These building blocks include diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP),15â€“18 naphthalene diimide,19â€“21 isoindigo (II),22â€“24 benzothiadiazole,25 benzobisthiadiazole,26 thieno[3,2-b]thiophene,27 (E)-1,2-di(thiophen-2-yl)ethene (VT), and (E)-1,2-di(selenophen-2-yl)ethene (VS), and benzodithiophenes,28,29 and so on. Therefore, designing more novel building blocks is of equal importance as developing high-performance, polymeric semiconductors and determining the inherent relationship between molecular structures and opto-electronic properties for realizing next-generation, printable electronics.

Since J. G. Mei presented the method for the synthesis of II unit, various II-based polymers have been synthesized and investigated for opto-electronic applications.30 For example, the highest power conversion efficiency of 8.2% was obtained in II-based polymer/fullerene bulk heterojunction solar cells, whereas a high hole mobility of over 3.0 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 was given by II-based polymer FETs.22,24a Encouraged by these achievements, great efforts have been paid to developing II derived building blocks and related polymeric semiconductors.31â€“33 For example, to obtain high planarity, the outer phenyl rings of II were replaced by thiophene rings which gave a new building block, thienoisoindigo, which has an enhanced planar conjugated backbone because of the elimination of steric repulsion between the protons on the phenyl rings and the carbonyl oxygens of the oxindoles. Furthermore, because of the disappearance of Î±-H on the phenyl rings, thienoisoindigo endows the copolymers with thiopheneâ€“thiophene links with optimized planarity, thus maximizing conjugation and further enhancing attractive interchain interactions. In another example, a novel II derived building block, benzodifurandione-based oligo(p-phenylene vinylene) (BDOPV), in which oxindoles are bridged by a strong electron-deficient benzodifurandione moiety was also developed.34 The resulting BDOPV has the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy level of âˆ’4.2 eV, compared to âˆ’3.5 eV for II. As result, the corresponding polymers exhibited enhanced electron transport performance and device stability under ambient conditions.

In recent years, the concept of conformational locks has been put forward and applied in developing high-performance semiconducting polymers. Conformational locks refer to weak noncovalent interactions such as Sâ‹¯O, Oâ‹¯H, Fâ‹¯H, and so on, which are considered to be favorable for conjugated backbone planarity, and/or acquiring strong intra- and inter-molecular interactions between neighboring molecules. In previous work, novel oxindole-based oligomers had been synthesized.34 Solution-processed OFETs based on the oligomers were obtained which exhibited a hole mobility of 0.045â€“0.075 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1. The promising results made this attractive for developing further oxindole-based building blocks for Dâ€“A copolymers. In the research reported in this paper, a novel Ï€-extended analogue of II, vinylidenedithiophenmethyleneoxindole (VDTOI, Fig. 1), was developed. The new VDTOI unit has a linear and planar conjugated system with centrosymmetric acceptorâ€“donorâ€“acceptor (Aâ€“Dâ€“A) geometry. Because of the centrosymmetric structure, the VDTOI unit is easily synthesized and, more importantly, is beneficial for VDTOI-based derivatives forming ordered molecular packing in the solid state. With the introduction of a Ï€-extended VS unit, the steric repulsion of Oâ‹¯H occurring in the II unit has been eliminated. Meanwhile, the VDTOI unit possesses Sâ‹¯O noncovalent interactions, which could bring additional intra- and inter-molecular interactions in VDTOI-based copolymers. In addition, the solubility of the VDTOI-based derivatives can easily be tuned by changing the alkyl side chains. Furthermore, the VDTOI unit has similar strong electron-deficient properties to those of the thiophene-flanked DPP unit, rather than the II unit. These structural features indicate that VDTOI might be a potential building block for constructing high-performance polymeric semiconductors. As a trial, two VDTOI-based copolymers P1 and P2, in which the VT or VS building block acts as donor unit, were synthesized (Fig. 1). The copolymers display good solution processability and broad absorption profiles throughout the visible spectra with the optical bandgap of approximately 1.70 eV. Solution-processed OFETs based on P1 exhibited a maximum hole mobility of 0.35 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and an average hole mobility of 0.21 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 with a high on/off current ratio of 106â€“107.

Device fabrication and characterization

Polymer FETs were fabricated with a bottom-contact/bottom-gate configuration. Heavily doped silicon wafers covered with 300 nm thick silicon dioxide (SiO2) layers were used as the gate electrode and gate dielectric. Gold was used as the source and drain electrodes. The substrates were subjected to cleaning using ultrasonication in acetone, deionized water, and iso-propanol. After drying under vacuum at 80 Â°C, the substrates were treated with plasma for 15 min, then modified with phenyltrichlorosilane (PTS). Thin films of the polymer were deposited on the treated substrates by spin coating using a polymer solution (4 mg mLâˆ’1) in o-dichlorobenzene at 2000 rpm for 60 s. The annealing process was performed in an ambient atmosphere at 140 Â°C for 5 min. The FET devices had a channel length of 10 Î¼m and a channel width of 1400 Î¼m.

The evaluations of the polymer FET devices were accomplished using a Keithley 4200 SCS semiconductor parameter analyser on a probe stage. The charge carrier mobility, Î¼ was calculated from the data in the saturated regime according to the equation:

where ISD is the drain current in the saturated regime, W/L is the channel width/length, Ci is the gate dielectric layer capacitance per unit area, and VGS and Vth are the gate voltage and threshold voltage, respectively.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and thermal stability

The synthetic routes of the VDTOI-based monomer 4, and copolymers P1 and P2 are shown in Scheme 1. Briefly, (E)-1,2-di(thiophen-2-yl)ethene, 1 reacted with nBuLi, then quenched with DMF at low temperature giving carbaldehyde 2 as a yellow solid with a high yield of 81%.35 Knoevenagel reaction of 2 and 6-bromoindolin-2-one in the presence of piperidine gave the key intermediate 3 as a black solid, which is insoluble in common organic solvents. Without further purification, intermediate 3 was N-alkylated with 11-(bromomethyl)tricosane to give the black-purple coloured crude solid. After further purification using silica gel chromatography, and recrystallization from chloroform/methanol, the desired VDTOI-based monomer 4 with single Z,E,Z configuration was obtained as a metallic solid in a moderate yield of 32% yield. The characterization of monomer 4 was performed using 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR, and HRMS. Frontier molecular orbitals and optimized molecular structures of the monomer 4 were also computed with B3LYP 6-31+G(d,p) levels (alkyl side chains were simplified to methyl groups) (Fig. 2). Computational results reveal that monomer 4 assumes almost planar conjugated backbones. Stille polymerizations of (E)-1,2-bis(5-(trimethylstannyl)thiophen-2-yl)ethene or (E)-1,2-bis(5-(trimethylstannyl)selenophen-2-yl)ethene, and monomer 4 were carried out in chlorobenzene at 110 Â°C to give copolymers P1 and P2, respectively. After the residual catalytic metal and low molecular weight oligomers of the resultant polymer were removed through a standard procedure given in the literature,11 the two desired copolymers were obtained as a black-purple, film-like solid in excellent yields of 90â€“93%. The two polymers exhibited good solubility in common solvents such as chloroform, chlorobenzene and dichlorobenzene and so on. The polymers were characterized by 1H-NMR and high temperature GPC (Table 1). P1 and P2 display high Mn of 36.8 and 24.6 kDa, respectively, and a PDI of 1.82 and 1.90, respectively. TGA showed that the VDTOI-based monomer and copolymers all have good thermal stability. The high decomposition temperatures (Td) with 5% weight less were obtained at 360 Â°C for monomer 4, 390 Â°C for P1, and 402 Â°C for P2 (Fig. S1â€ ). Differential scanning calorimetry measurements were performed to determine the thermal transitions for each polymer below the highest annealing temperature see Fig. S2.â€  Unfortunately, no obvious endothermic transitions and glass transitions were observed for the two copolymers at temperatures ranging from 50 to 250 Â°C. These observations imply that a successive morphology change would take place in the thin films of the two copolymers.

Optical and electrochemical properties of the monomer and copolymers

The optical properties of VDTOI-based monomer and copolymers were studied using UV-vis absorption spectroscopy (Fig. 3, 4 and S3,â€  and Table 1). As shown in Fig. 3a, monomer 4 in chloroform shows a single band absorption spectrum ranging from 350 to 600 nm with the maximum absorption wavelength at 536 nm. The two VDTOI-based copolymers in chloroform have similar and broad absorption profiles band ranging from approximately 330 to 740 nm with the maximum absorption peaks at approximately 600 nm. These observations indicate that strong intramolecular charge transfer exists in three Ï€-conjugated systems because of strong Dâ€“A interactions.36a Monomer 4 thin film shows a broader and red-shifted absorption spectrum containing two bands with peaks at 582 and 630 nm. The high energy band is attributed to effective intramolecular charge transfer, while the lower energy band could be assigned to aggregation or orderly packing of the conjugated polymer main chains in the solid films.7b The lower energy bands are obviously stronger compared to other polymers reported.22,23 Further investigations are in progress to fully define the compounds. Nevertheless, it is speculated that the decrease of steric twisting on the conjugated backbone in the thin film results in the enhanced absorption behaviour in the lower energy band.36b,c Based on the absorption edge of 680 nm, the optical bandgap of monomer 4 was estimated to be 1.84 eV. It is interesting to note that the P2 thin film exhibits markedly red-shifted absorption behavior and there was clearly a shoulder peak when the spectra were compared to that of P1 thin film. The red-shifted absorption is ascribed to the fact that the selenium atoms induce stronger interchain interactions leading to denser chain packing, which was revealed in several papers in the literature.15b,16b,25b The selenium effect was thought to be originating from the more mobile lone electron pairs of the selenium atoms.25b On the basis of the absorption cut off values, the optical bandgaps of P1 and P2 were estimated to be 1.72 and 1.68 eV, respectively.

Properties of a field-effect transistor

The field-effect properties of VDTOI-based copolymers were measured in bottom contact/bottom gate FET devices, where the SiO2 surface was modified with a PTS layer. The semiconducting layers of P1 and P2 were fabricated by spin-coating a polymer solution in dichlorobenzene (15 mg mLâˆ’1). Both copolymers show typical p-type transport characteristics. The representative output and transfer plots are presented in Fig. 5a and b, and the corresponding data are shown in Table 2. The pristine film of P1 only gave a low hole mobility of 0.11 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1; however, the annealed films (140 Â°C for 5 min) exhibited a maximum mobility of up to 0.35 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and average values of 0.21 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 (based on 15 devices), with a high on/off current ratio of 106â€“107. The great improvements in charge mobility reveal that strong molecular self-assembly actions occurred in the annealing process. Unexpectedly, the annealed thin film of P2 only displayed a maximum mobility of 0.037 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 and an average mobility of 0.021 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 (based on 15 devices), which are an order of magnitude lower than those of P1 in the same conditions (Fig. S4 and S5â€ ). In addition, no large difference in mobility was observed between its as-fabricated and annealed P2 films. The lower mobility of P2 may be ascribed to the introduction of selenium atoms, with larger van der Waals radius than those of sulfur atoms which may induce less efficient packing of polymer chains and suppresses intermolecular charge hopping.37a,b

Thin film microstructural characterization

Tapping-mode AFM and GIXRD were used to investigate the film morphology and molecular packing of VDTOI-based copolymers. As shown in Fig. 5c and S6,â€  the as-fabricated P1 thin film displays obvious grain boundaries, which are unfavorable for charge carrier transport. However, the annealed film exhibits a highly smooth and uniform morphology and network structure, which is composed of small polymer fibers. The morphology difference is correlated well with the changes in mobility as previously mentioned. For P2, however, no such great improvement in thin film morphology was observed when the annealing temperature was elevated (Fig. S7â€ ). When annealed at 140 Â°C, P2 thin film has better crystallinity than that of the as-fabricated thin film; however, the grain boundaries also become greater.
