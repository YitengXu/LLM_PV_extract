Abstract

Two Dâ€“A copolymers P1 and P2 were developed by copolymerizing a new lactam acceptor unit, [7,7â€²-bidithieno[3,2-b:2â€²,3â€²-d]pyridine]-5,5â€²(4H,4â€²H)-dione (BDTP), with benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene (BDT) or selenophene. Though the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of a single polymer solar cell (PSC) has reached 10.8%,1 it still needs to be improved significantly for future commercialization. Dâ€“A copolymers have been widely used as electron donors in PSCs for achieving a high PCE because of their excellent optical absorption and high mobilities.2 Among them, lactam-containing polymers have shown promising photovoltaic performance. These reported lactam acceptor units include diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP),3 isoindigo (IID),4 benzodipyrrolidone (BDP),5 thieno[2â€²,3â€²:5,6]pyrido[3,4-g]thieno[3,2-c]isoquinoline-5,11(4H,10H)-dione (TPTI)6 and others.7 They possess structural advantages: first, the strong electron-withdrawing capability of lactam units enables effective tuning of material bandgaps;8 second, the carbonyl groups on lactam can improve the backbone planarity and polymer crystallinity;9 third, long alkyl chains at the N-position of the lactam ring render good solubility.10 Recently, we developed a new lactam acceptor unit, 4,4â€²-bis(2-octyldodecyl)-[7,7â€²-bidithieno[3,2-b:2â€²,3â€²-d]pyridine]-5,5â€²(4H,4â€²H)-dione (BDTP), which consists of two tricyclic lactam units. PThBDTP:PC71BM solar cells gave a decent PCE of 9.13%.11 Benzo[1,2-b:4,5-bâ€²]dithiophene (BDT) and selenophene were selected as the donor units due to their good electron-donating properties.12 Two Dâ€“A copolymers P1 and P2 based on BDTP and BDT or selenophene were synthesized (Scheme 1). P1:PC71BM and P2:PC71BM inverted solar cells gave PCEs of 5.05% and 6.76%, respectively.

P1 and P2 were prepared by copolymerization of BDTP-Br11 with BDT or selenophene in 92% and 91% yields, respectively. The monomers and low molecular weight fractions were removed by methanol and hexane using a Soxhlet extractor. Finally, the polymers were extracted with chloroform and precipitated in methanol. The two polymers are soluble in CHCl3, toluene, chlorobenzene (CB) and o-dichlorobenzene (ODCB). The structures of P1 and P2 were confirmed by 1H NMR (Fig. S1 and S2â€ ) and elemental analysis. Gel permeation chromatography (GPC) experiments indicate that the number-average molecular weight (Mn) of P1 and P2 is 49.0 kDa and 62.1 kDa, with the polydispersity index (PDI) of 3.2 and 2.9, respectively. The two polymers show good thermal stability with decomposition temperatures (Td) of 339 Â°C and 431 Â°C for P1 and P2, respectively (Fig. S3â€ ).

The absorption spectra of P1 and P2 are shown in Fig. 1 and the optical data are listed in Table 1. In solution or as films, the polymers show two absorption peaks in the visible region. For film absorption, the maxima are at 590 nm and 640 nm for P1 and P2, respectively. From solution to film, P2 showed a larger red-shift (26 nm) than that of P1 (12 nm), suggesting that P2 makes better Ï€â€“Ï€ stacking in the solid state. The film absorption onsets of P1 and P2 are at 625 nm and 689 nm, corresponding to optical bandgaps of 1.98 eV and 1.80 eV, respectively. The narrower bandgap of P2 originates from better Ï€-electron delocalization of selenophene.13 Cyclic voltammograms of P1 and P2 are shown in Fig. S4â€  and the corresponding data are listed in Table 1. All potentials were calibrated against the Fc/Fc+ redox couple. According to the empirical formulae: HOMO = âˆ’(Eonox + 4.8) eV, LUMO = âˆ’(Eonred + 4.8) eV,14 the HOMO energy levels of P1 and P2 are calculated to be âˆ’5.53 and âˆ’5.51 eV, while LUMO energy levels of P1 and P2 are âˆ’2.68 and âˆ’2.80 eV, respectively. Generally selenophene can lower the LUMO level of Dâ€“A copolymers, which correlates with the large volume of selenium atoms.15 The deep HOMO levels of P1 and P2 can lead to a high Voc for solar cells.

Solar cells with a conventional device structure, ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer:PC71BM/Ca/Al, were fabricated to evaluate the photovoltaic performance of P1 and P2. After systematic optimization, the best P1:PC71BM solar cell gave a PCE of 3.14%, with a Voc of 0.87 V, a Jsc of 8.80 mA cmâˆ’2, and a FF of 41.1% (Fig. 2, Table 2). It had a D/A ratio of 1:1 (w/w), an active layer thickness of 198 nm, and 1 vol% 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO) in the mixed solvent (CB:CHCl3 = 2:1), and did not undergo thermal annealing. While P2:PC71BM solar cells, with a D/A ratio of 1:1.2 (w/w), an active layer thickness of 146 nm, and 1 vol% DIO in ODCB, gave the best PCE of 5.08%, with a Voc of 0.89 V, a Jsc of 8.60 mA cmâˆ’2, and a FF of 66.4%. The processing solvents were optimized to obtain a better film morphology and better device performance. The DIO additive significantly improved the solar cell performance by reducing the nano-domain size and increasing the Dâ€“A interfaces in the active layer.16 The morphology of the blend films without and with DIO was studied by atomic force microscopy (AFM). After addition of DIO, large PC71BM domains decomposed, more fine nano-structures can be observed, and the phase change is remarkable for the P1:PC71BM blend film (Fig. 3a and b). More Dâ€“A interfaces caused by the additive help to enhance Jsc and FF (Table S3â€ ).

Conclusions

In summary, two Dâ€“A copolymers P1 and P2 based on a novel lactam acceptor unit BDTP and the BDT or selenophene donor unit have been developed for polymer solar cells. P2 containing the selenophene unit shows broader absorption, narrower bandgap, and higher hole mobility than P1 with the BDT unit. P1:PC71BM and P2:PC71BM inverted solar cells gave PCEs of 5.05% and 6.76%, respectively. Further application of the BDTP unit in developing highly efficient Dâ€“A copolymers is ongoing.
