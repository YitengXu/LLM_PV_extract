Abstract

Polycrystalline sulfides are promising thermoelectric materials because of their abundance, non-toxicity, and low cost. However, their low thermoelectric performance hampers their possible large-scale industrial application. In this work, Cu self-doping in Cu2+xZn1âˆ’xSnS4, prepared using a simple processing route, realizes the decoupling of thermal and electric transport as follows. The additional copper ions occupy Zn sites and provide more carriers, which leads to excellent hole conductivity. Moreover, when x is above 0.125, the content of Cn/Zn disordered sites is beyond the percolation threshold (âˆ¼0.3) of thermal diffusivity. A maximum power factor (450 Î¼W mâˆ’1 Kâˆ’2) is obtained for Cu2.125Zn0.875SnS4. Combined with ultra-low specific heat below the theory predicted minimum for structural disordering on the copper and zinc sites, the Îº of the material reduces to 0.22 W mâˆ’1 Kâˆ’1 at 773 K, which is minimum in thermoelectric materials up to now. Moreover, hot forging (deformation strain âˆ¼64% and maximum of displacement rate âˆ¼18 mm minâˆ’1), a dynamic plastic deformation process, was employed to produce defects on different length scales (point defects, dislocations, stacking faults and deformation twins), that optimize the power factor and lattice thermal conductivity. Based on the above synergistic effects, a high ZT value of 1.1 has been achieved in Cu2.125Zn0.875SnS4 ceramics, and there is 18-fold increase in ZT value compared with that of conventionally processed intrinsic Cu2ZnSnS4.

Introduction

The energy crisis and global climate protection are drawing enormous attention to the need for more efficient utilization of energy. About 54% of the world's total energy need is consumed in the industrial sector, in which waste heat, a byproduct of industrial processes (such as steel mills, paper plants, refineries, chemical plants, oil and gas pipelines, and general manufacturing) accounts for about 60% of this consumption. High temperature (>400 Â°C) waste heat is efficiently recovered using steam turbines, however, the utilization of low-quality waste heat (<400 Â°C) is still a huge challenge, and corresponds to 70% of the total waste energy generated. Progresses in energy harvesting in this area will make a big contribution to energy recovery from waste heat, leading to a reduction in fuel consumption and CO2 emissions. Screw expander and low pressure saturated steam generation are possible choices; however, their disadvantages are low efficiency and short operating life. Thermoelectric materials, which convert thermal and electrical energy using the Seebeck/Peltier effects, provide us an alternative method to recycle low quality waste heat. They have already received great attention for solid state cooling and energy generation applications due to their additional advantages of being compact, lightweight, quiet, and reliable.1

However, there are two main challenges before realizing the commercial application of thermoelectric materials on a large scale for waste heat recovery. One challenge is to achieve a higher conversion efficiency of heat to electricity, competitive with traditional turbine power generators (30%). In order to achieve this, it is necessary to have materials with a high thermoelectric figure of merit ZT value ((S2Ïƒ/Îº)T, where S, Ïƒ and Îº is Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity and thermal conductivity respectively). Several effective methods, such as low-dimensional crystal structures and band engineering, have been developed to obtain higher ZT values.2 While materials with ZT values of as high as 2.0 have been reported, it seems possible to further improve the ZT value with further optimization.3 The other challenge is that most of the thermoelectric compounds with high ZT values contain toxic, scarce and expensive elements (such as Pb, Te, Se and Sb), which is inhibiting their large scale commercial application for industrial waste heat recovery. It is more urgent to develop inexpensive and efficient alternative materials using earth abundant and non-toxic elements.1

Sulphur is one of the most common earth abundant, nontoxic elements, and its extraction involves an economical preparation process. In the last two decades, some chalcogenide based semiconductors (such as CuxS or Cu2ZnSnS4) have attracted considerable interest because of their beneficial use in thin film solar cells with high power conversion efficiency of over 12%, being promising alternatives to the currently used toxic commercialized solar cell absorbers.4â€“6 Their performance can be improved by partial cation substitution to mitigate the negative effects of different types of defects.7 Recently, these sulfide compounds were reported to have good thermoelectric properties, which make them a hot topic in thermoelectrics research.8â€“10 Atomic-scale percolation was observed in digenite CuxS related compounds with single phase, in which copper ions/lattice interstices were hypothetically considered as a minor, electrically conductive phase, and an obvious enhancement of ZT values was obtained near the percolation threshold.11,12 However, it is impossible to further increase the ZT value of CuxS because the change of Cu/S ratio leads to a phase transition and the disappearance of the related digenite phases.11

The Cu2â€“Zn(Fe)â€“Snâ€“S4 solid solution is related to the mineral kesterite (belongs to diamond-like semiconductors), and has a complex crystal structure. For single crystal, the crystal structure has two electrical functional units: Cu2S4 units are helpful for electrical conductivity and ZnSnS4 units act as insulating units, as shown in Fig. 1a. Due to its insulating layer units, intrinsic Cu2ZnSnS4 has a major drawback as a thermoelectric material in that its electrical conductivity is low and therefore its associated ZT value is below 0.1. Since this compound can be considered as an ideal atomic-scale layered structure composite with electrically insulating and conducting components, it is possible to improve the TE properties using the effect of atomic percolation as follows. Some Zn sites in the insulating layers can be replaced by Cu ions (Fig. 1b), and at high temperature the Cu and Zn ions in the unit are distributed randomly. Meanwhile, the CuZn anti-site has a lower formation energy and relatively deeper acceptor level compared to the Cu vacancy,13 which leads to Cu2ZnSnS4 being an intrinsic p-type semi-conductor. If Cu2ZnSnS4 is self doped with Cu on the Zn site, more CuZn point defects will introduce significantly more hole carriers and reduce the electrical resistivity. As a result, an obvious enhancement of thermoelectric properties would be observed near the percolation threshold because of electric conductivity's increase.

Currently, Cu2ZnSnS4 is synthesized by melt processing14 or chemical methods (such as hot injection15 and solution method16). It takes a long time (3â€“7 days) for traditional melting processes to produce single phase Cu2ZnSnS4 ingots in order to avoid the explosion of silica tubes because of the evaporation of sulphur. An additional issue is that ZnS appears as a secondary phase during the cooling process. Chemical synthesis consumes a large amount of energy, has a low yield with high cost, and consequently is not suitable for large scale industrial production. Compared with these methods, mechanical alloying by ball milling is a simple and economic way to produce pure Cu2ZnSnS4 powders. In this paper we use mechanical alloying combined with spark plasma sintering to fabricate single phase, dense Cu2ZnSnS4 ceramics.

Additionally, dynamic plastic deformation can produce ultrafine-grained structures with preferred crystallographic orientation and is an effective industrial process for modifying the mechanical properties of structural metal.17â€“19 Up to now, this process has only been used to fabricate highly anisotropic, bulk, polycrystalline thermoelectric materials with excellent in-plane electronic/thermal transport properties, and there are few quantitative reports on the effect of the high-densities of defects introduced during plastic deformation on thermoelectric properties. Herein, we first adopt hot forging, a dynamic plastic deformation technology, to a cubic crystal system to introduce defects with different dimensions (point defects, dislocations, stacking faults and deformation twins) to optimize the power factor and thermal conductivity. Based on the above synergistic effects, Cu self-doped Cu2+xZn1âˆ’xSnS4 samples were successfully fabricated, which after optimization have a ZT value of 1.1, which makes its large-scale industrial application potentially possible.

Experimental section

Commercial copper, zinc, tin and sulfur powders (high purity, above 99.9%, all from Aldrich, UK) were selected. The powders were weighed in stoichiometric ratio and placed into stainless steel milling jars with steel balls (the weight ratio of the steel balls to powders was 20:1) in a glove box to avoid reaction with moisture and oxygen in the air (Beta, Saffron, UK). The mixtures were ball-milled at 350 rpm for 40 hours in an atmosphere of high-purity argon in a planetary ball milling machine (QM-3SP2, Nanjing University, China). The crystal structure of the powders was characterized using X-ray diffraction (XRD).

The powders were put into a graphite die (an inner diameter âˆ¼15 mm) and then sintered at 600 Â°C under 60 MPa at a heating rate (50 K minâˆ’1) for a holding time of 8 min in a spark plasma sintering furnace (HPD25/1 FCT; Rauenstein, Germany) in a vacuum environment.

Dynamic plastic deformation technology can refine crystal grains, and is an effective industrial process for modifying the mechanical properties of structural metals. The bulk modulus of kesterite-type Cu2ZnSnS4 single crystal is about 67.8 GPa calculated using density-functional theory (DFT), and its shear modulus is about 29.7 GPa.40 According to Pugh's criterion,41 the fact that its B/G ratio (âˆ¼2.28) is much larger than 1.75 indicates that kesterite-type Cu2ZnSnS4 should be quite ductile, especially at high temperatures. So dynamic plastic deformation may not destroy the wholeness of the samples and be suitable to tailor physical properties. Based on this assumption, in the following work we introduced superplastic (dynamic plastic) deformation by hot forging to tailor the transport properties in order to further improve the ZT value of Cu2.125Zn0.875SnS4 to above 1. Conclusions

Polycrystalline sulfide compounds Cu2+xZn1âˆ’xSnS4 (x = 0â€“0.15) have been fabricated using mechanical alloying combined with dynamic plastic deformation. The additional copper ions occupy zinc sites, destroy the insulating properties of the ZnSnS4 units, and act as acceptors to produce more holes, which is very effective to modulate the carrier concentration and therefore enhance the electrical properties. Above the order-disorder phase transition temperature, a Cuâ€“Zn liquid-like matrix with a Snâ€“S crystal network inside leads to intrinsic ultra-low specific heat values. More CuZn point defects (x of 0.125 and 0.15) are introduced and the content of Cu/Zn disorder is more than the percolation value (âˆ¼0.3), corresponding to enhanced phonon scattering and decreased lattice thermal conductivity. Meanwhile, CuZn point defects in the distorted structure lead to an improvement of the power factor and the decrease of lattice thermal conductivity, leading to a higher ZT value. To further improve ZT, hot forging was adopted as a dynamic plastic deformation technology to introduce more defects with different dimensions (point defects, dislocations, stacking faults and deformation twins) to optimize the carrier concentration, power factor and thermal conductivity. Based on the above synergistic effects, a high ZT value of 1.1 has been achieved for Cu2.125Zn0.875SnS4, which is an 18-fold enhancement compared with that of the intrinsic Cu2ZnSnS4 (ZT âˆ¼ 0.06), which makes it realistic for use in large-scale industrial waste heat recovery. The dynamic plastic deformation approach can also be extended to other emerging thermoelectric materials.
