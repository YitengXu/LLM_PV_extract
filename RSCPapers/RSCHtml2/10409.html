Impact of the polar optical phonon and alloy scattering on the charge-carrier mobilities of FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskites†

Abstract

Lead mixed-halide perovskites are promising absorption materials that are suitable for applications in tandem solar cells using existing silicon technology. Charge-carrier mobility is an important factor that affects the performance of tandem solar cells. However, a detailed understanding of the fundamental mechanisms of lead mixed-halide perovskites remains elusive. Here, we used LO (longitudinal optical) phonons and alloy scattering to the elucidate charge-carrier mobilities in the FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskite system. It was found that these scattering mechanisms provided very good quantitative agreement with the experimental results, between 11–40 cm2 V−1 s−1. Our findings provide new insights into charge transport scattering in lead mixed-halide hybrid perovskites and pave the way toward design of novel semiconductor alloys for solar cell applications.

1. Introduction

Metal halide perovskites (MHPs) have attracted much attention as photoactive materials for next-generation solar energy harvesters.1,2 This increasing interest is due to a combination of their outstanding properties, including high tolerance of structural defects3,4 and low-cost processing techniques,5–9 as well as desirable optical and transport properties. They have high absorption coefficients, tunable optical bandgaps, low recombination rates and high charge-carrier mobilities.10–12 Triggered by the development of solid-state perovskite solar cells (PSC) by Miyasaka et al. in 2009,13 the first organic–metal halide perovskite, methylammonium lead iodide (CH3NH3PbI3 or MAPbI3), was developed with an optical bandgap of ∼1.57 eV. It had an initial power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 3.8%.13 To date, the PCE values of MAPbI3-based PSCs have been improved, showing more than a five-fold increase to ∼20.4%.14 However, this material is unstable when exposed to humidity, light and oxygen, presenting a major drawback that leads to a very short useful PCE of about 100 hours.15

One of the archetypal photoactive materials, formamidinium lead iodide (CH(NH2)2PbI3 or FAPbI3) has been suggested as an alternative to MAPbI3.16–18 The replacement of an organic MA+ cation by FA+ enhanced the interaction between the organic FA cation and PbI3 sublattice. This resulted in a smaller band gap, ∼1.48 eV, and improved thermal stability.18 A PCE of ∼20% has been demonstrated in FAPbI3-based PSCs.17,18 However, FAPbI3 exists in an unstable state form, the yellow δ-phase, which presents reduced stability and charge-carrier mobility.16–18 Later, mixed-halide perovskites, such as FAPb(I1−xBrx)3, were successfully synthesized.19 This alloy system can be tuned with an optical bandgap that is between 1.5 and 2.26 eV, matching other applications in a tandem solar cell technology.20 However, FAPb(I1−xBrx)3 perovskites showed an amorphous film structure and reversible separation into I-rich and Br-rich regions under photo-illumination, a so-called photoinduced halide segregation.21,22 Partial substitution of FA+ with a low fraction of Cs+ has been done to form a material with a more stable crystallinity in lead mixed-halide hybrid perovskites, FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3. This was done to resolve the instability problems in FAPb(I1−xBrx)3.23,24 A broad optical bandgap, between ∼1.57 eV and 2.3 eV, can be tuned by varying the Br content. Moreover, the stability of a PCE based on this material system was greatly enhanced to more than 1000 hours.25,26 Owing to its tunable bandgap and higher material stability, a hybrid perovskite, FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I0.6Br0.4)3, with a 1.74 eV bandgap was integrated into the top layer of a perovskite-silicon tandem solar cell.23 Based on this perovskite material, measured PCEs, between ∼25–29%, have recently been demonstrated.27–29 The PCE of the current perovskite technology, i.e. a single junction perovskite solar cell (20%)17,18,30 and a perovskite-silicon tandem solar cell (29%),29 approaches the Shockley–Queisser limit of a single junction solar cell (∼33%)31 and a tandem solar cell (two cells, ∼44%),32 respectively. Thus, outstanding performance using low cost solution-based processes can stimulate research in PSCs, not only at a laboratory scale, but also commercially.

Even though there has been impressive progress in material synthesis and development of the optoelectronic properties of lead mixed-halide FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskites, an accurate description of THz mobility23,24 remains unattained. An optical pump THz probe (OPTP) has been used to measured THz charge-carrier mobility. Higher charge-carrier mobilities and longer diffusion paths, e.g., 11–40 cm2 V−1 s−1 and 0.8–4.4 μm, were respectively achieved. Therefore, a clear understanding of the microscopic quantum processes that affect charge-carrier mobility is crucial in this material system for next-generation photovoltaic materials. In this work, we present the current understanding of charge-carrier mobilities in FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskite thin films. Of special interest is the impact of polar optical or longitudinal optical (LO) phonons and alloy scattering on charge transport dynamics across its full alloy composition range. A polaron effect based on LO phonon scattering33,34 and alloy scattering originating from the alloy compositional disorders35,36 were proposed to describe their charge-carrier mobility results. The transport mechanism of polarons was successfully applied to describe charge-carrier mobility in both perovskite MAPbI337 and SrTiO3,38,39 as well as metal oxide TiO2.40 The alloy scattering mechanism well described charge-carrier mobility in semiconductor alloys such as AlxGa1−xN41 and Mg3.125−xYbxSb1.5Bi0.5Te0.001.42 The total charge-carrier mobility determined from both scattering mechanisms was comparable to the experimental results of THz mobility.19,24 The theoretical result was in very good agreement with the experimental measurements across the Br composition range.

Moreover, the carrier effective masses between 0.2m0 and 0.4m045 yielded an accurate description in terms of mobility values and accounted for their great impact upon charge-carrier transport properties. To investigate the impact of alloy (Br) concentration on the charge-carrier mobilities in FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskites, we proceeded by fitting the experimental data24 to the individual mobilities for both LO phonon and alloy scatterings. In numerical calculations, we used LO phonon energies and alloy scattering potentials (U) as the fitting parameters. The effective masses of charge carriers,45 measured lattice constants54 and dielectric constants of FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 (x = 0.17, 0.33, 0.5, 0.67, 0.83) listed in Table 3, were employed. Fig. 1(a) shows a comparison of fitted mobilities and the experimental data as a function of Br content. To indicate mobilities at x = 0.1 and x = 0.9, the orange and blue solid triangles in this figure are extrapolated from the fits. The effective charge-carrier mobility (orange solid triangles), μeff = μtot,e +μtot,h with total charge-carrier mobilities of μtot,e = (μLO,e−1 + μAL,e−1)−1 and μtot,h = (μLO,h−1 + μAL,h−1)−1, provided very good quantitative agreement with the THz mobility data at Br contents between x = 0.3 and x = 0.9. The fit gives the extract fitting parameters for ELO = 13 meV and U = 3 eV (or 4.806 × 10−19 V). It is suggested that the THz mobility data over the range of x = 0.3–0.9 was limited by both LO phonon and alloy scattering mechanisms. The effective charge-carrier mobility underestimated the mobility data for the I-rich films at x = 0.1 and 0.2. The pure LO phonon scattering model with ELO = 13 meV, (the blue solid triangle at x = 0.1 and the blue solid line at x = 0.2 in Fig. 1(a)), yielded mobility values of 34 and 32 cm2 V−1 s−1, which are consistent with the experimental results of 37 and 34 cm2 V−1 s−1, respectively. This indicated that alloy scattering is weak and the polaronic effect dominates. In Fig. 1(b), we used the charge-carrier mobility values of 34 and 33 cm2 V−1 s−1 calculated from LO phonon scattering at x = 0.1 and 0.17 (blue solid triangles) and the effective charge-carrier mobility values calculated from both LO phonon and alloy scatterings at x = 0.33–0.9 (orange solid triangles) to fit the experimental results.24 The fitted mobility relating to dominant scattering mechanisms (the red solid line) was in very good agreement with the experimental data across the full Br tuning range. The mobility value decreased from 34 cm2 V−1 s−1 at x = 0.1 to 12 cm2 V−1 s−1 at x = 0.9.

Our investigations provide new insights into the fundamental scattering processes in lead mixed-halide hybrid perovskites and yield basic knowledge of charge-carrier transport properties. First, the dynamic carrier-phonon interactions are described by Fröhlich electron–phonon coupling theory,44 and the charge-carrier mobility is calculated using polaron transport. The physical descriptors for polaron transport are the carrier effective mass, polaron binding energy (EPB) and Fröhlich coupling constant.55 In the effective mass of charge carriers, a large effective mass supports low mobility. Asymmetry in the electron and hole effective masses leads to mobility differences (μe > μh). For the polaron binding energy, we use a simple expression, EPB = 0.106α2ħωLO,44,56 to estimate the binding energy in lead halide perovskites. The polaron binding energy corresponding to carrier localization depends on the Fröhlich coupling constant and LO phonon energy. Its strength is related to a larger effective mass and a strong difference between ε∞ and εs. The calculated values, 8.0–19.8 meV for electrons and 10.0–22.7 meV for holes, arise using the data listed in Table S3 (ESI†). These binding energy values are in the range of large polaron binding energies in lead halide perovskites, 12–45 meV,57–59 and are lower than the thermal energy (kBT ≈ 26 meV) at room temperature. Polaron states are delocalized at room temperature. In the Fröhlich coupling constant, the strength of carrier-LO phonon interaction represents interaction between charge carriers and LO phonons in a charge transport process, correlated with a large effective mass and a strong difference between ε∞ and εs. The increased Fröhlich coupling constant leads to increased LO phonon scattering and reduced charge-carrier mobility.

Second, the alloy scattering mechanism is due to a random distribution of various atoms in the same lattice site. Atomic level disorder produces potential energy fluctuation, or the so-called alloy scattering potential. The mobility of an alloying system is lower than that of a pure compound. In fact, a characteristic of alloy scattering is reflected in its concentration fraction factor, x(x − 1). For an identical effective mass, this factor yields a minimum mobility value at x = 0.5. This indicates that a relatively strong alloy interaction occurs at limiting Br compositions with I/Br ratios closer to 1:1. In this transport model, the effective mass of charge carriers was strongly correlated with charge-carrier mobility. The effective mass-dependent mobility is represented by the relationship, μAL ∝ m*−5/2, in comparison to the scattering of ionized impurities, μII ∝ m*−1/2, acoustic phonon scattering, μAC ∝ m*−5/2, and LO phonon scattering, μLO ∝ m*−3/2.35 Moreover, alloy scattering limited mobility provided a temperature-dependent mobility that follows a power law, μAL ∝ T−1/2, in comparison to μII ∝ T3/2, μAC ∝ T−3/2,35 and μLO ∝ T−1/2.60 Thus, alloy scattering significantly affects charge-carrier mobility and cannot be neglected in the analysis of charge transport properties in lead mixed-halide hybrid perovskites.

Finally, a basic knowledge of both transport scattering mechanisms can be used to describe the highest mobility value, 40 cm2 V−1 s−1 in a FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 film, which is 74% higher than the 27 cm2 V−1 s−1 mobility in FAPbI3. The highest charge-carrier mobility resulted from reduced LO phonon scattering with respect to FAPbI3, as well as Fröhlich coupling constants of αe = 2.25 and αh = 2.50 that were lower than αe = 2.57 and αh = 2.85 in FAPbI3. So, the presence of 17% Cs in the I-rich film not only provided high impact on crystalline quality,24 but also reduced the LO phonon scattering mechanism. The same result was observed in comparison with FAPbBr3 and FA0.83Cs0.17PbBr3 films in which polaron binding energies and Fröhlich coupling constants were lower than those of lead bromide perovskites, EPB = 19.8–22.7 meV and α = 3.37–3.78. Furthermore, comparison of the calculation results of FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 and FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 revealed a significant impact of alloy scattering on charge-carrier transport properties. Under an increased Br content, the strength of alloy scattering rapidly increased to a maximum value at x = 0.67, and then it continuously decreased. Alloy scattering was neglected in the neat FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 film. The strength of LO phonon scattering continuously increased, producing both polaron binding energies and Fröhlich coupling constants that were greater than that of FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 (details in Table S3, ESI†).

4. Conclusions

We examined charge-carrier mobility in the FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskite system. For THz charge-carrier mobility, the theoretical results provided very good agreement with the experimental data for neat FAPbI3, FAPbBr3, FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 and FA0.83Cs0.17PbBr3 films as well as lead mixed-halide FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 films. In their neat films, LO phonon scattering was dominant. The scattering from both LO phonons and alloy disorders were dominant mechanisms in FA0.83Cs0.17Pb(I1−xBrx)3 hybrid perovskite films. These scatterings are microscopic processes that well explain the experimental mobility results, between 11–40 cm2 V−1 s−1. Thus, a small addition of Cs ions in the FA0.83Cs0.17PbI3 film significantly improved the charge-carrier mobility, increasing crystallinity and reducing charge-carrier scattering. Knowledge of the charge-carrier transport dynamics is fundamental for revising strategies to develop high performance absorption materials for applications in both perovskite-perovskite and perovskite-semiconductor tandem solar cell technologies.
