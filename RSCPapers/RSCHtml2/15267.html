Abstract

With solar light, modified d-block metal photocatalysts are useful in areas where electricity is insufficient, with its chemical stability during the photocatalytic process, and its low-cost and nontoxicity. However, because of their large band gap for excitation, only biohazardous ultraviolet (UV) light irradiation can excite d-block metals, which limits its application in the living environment. Modified d-block metal photocatalyts such as TiO2, ZnO, has recently been the center of research in Nano studies due to the ability of these metals to convert pollutants into CO2 and H2O for environmental applications such as energy, environmental purification and wastewater treatment. This paper highlights the present improvements on the photocatalytic activity of modified d-block metals through their developing structure modification strategies, solar harvest, methods of synthesis and solar light synthesis catalytic mechanisms of these photocatalysts. In this report, the full knowledge of the use of the remarkable solar light harvest potentials vis-à-vis the excellent structure of these metals are revealed. Also, this study on modified d-block metal Nano-based photocatalysts is expected to enlighten scientists on precise management and evaluating the environment, which may merit prospect research into developing suitable mechanism for energy, wastewater treatment and environmental purification.

Z-Scheme photocatalysis

The Z-scheme heterojunction was developed to solve the steric hindrance exerted in type II heterojunction.226 Currently there are three generations of the Z-scheme photocatlytic system. These types are: 1st, 2nd, and 3rd generation Z-scheme heterojunctions.

The 1st generation Z-scheme heterojunctions is built by combining two different semiconductors with a shuttle redox mediator (viz. an electron acceptor/donor (A/D) pair). Also, the 2nd generation Z-scheme photocatalytic system is composed of two different semiconductors and a noble-metal nanoparticle (NP) as the electron mediator.223–234 The use of the d-block metals solves the backward reaction that was happening in the first generation (liquid phase Z-scheme). Effect of adsorption onto photocatalytic process

During semiconductor photocatalysis, when exposed to light with photonic energy greater than the band gap of a semiconductor, the valence electrons (VB) are excited to empty the conduction band (CB). This excitation leads to the formation of electron (eCB) and positive hole (h+VB) pair.214,215 Metal oxides have poor adsorption ability for aqueous phase pollutants.156 These drawbacks have motivated researchers to couple graphene and other photocatalyts with a d-block metals to gain an improved photocatalytic activity. The photocatalytic degradation of phenolic compounds was studied under two conditions (simultaneous adsorption (A + P) and photocatalysis and adsorption followed by photocatalysis (A − P)) by Shandilya and coworkers.213 During the A + P process, synergetic effect between photocatalysis and adsorption caused enhanced phenol degradation. A + P was the most efficient for photocatalytic process of removal of phenolic compounds. The mechanistic details of adsorption process, it is crucial to identify main oxidant species involved in degradation of the pollutant. Usually, hydroxyl radicals (OH˙), superoxide radicals (O2˙), photogenerated holes (h+VB) and electrons (e− CB) are major oxidant species218 in adsorption reactions. Shandilya and group prepared fluorine doped graphene (FG) as high dispersed adsorbent by sonochemical exfoliation method. The simultaneous adsorption and photocatalysis was most efficient process for degradation of selected phenolic compounds. The oxidative radical species OH˙ and O2˙− played vital role in photodegradation and adsorption process.217

Enhancement of the activity by modification

To compensate the cost, further enhancement of these photocatalysts are still needed. At present, the modifications that enhance the visible-light photocatalytic activity of modified d-block metals may be more attractive as they can utilize a large portion of sunlight. Immobilisation on surfaces

Modified d-block metals can be configured in either a suspended form or an immobilised form. There are obvious disadvantages to configuration in an immobilised form such as a decreased surface area, however there are notable advantages such as the prevention of light scattering, ease of removal, and easy reuse. Modified d-block metals have been immobilised on various stationary surfaces such as thin films, reticulated foam monoliths, fiber glass, glass/ceramic rings, sand, glass beads, glass wool, silica fibres, titanium alloys, quartz, stainless steel and Pyrex glass tubes containing scratched polymeric fibres (to enhance the availability of light). There are other immobilisation substrates which are possible; however, these have not been applied for the interest of water remediation and will therefore not be discussed. There are numerous methods for immobilisation of modified d-block metals on surfaces and they all depend on the application required.

Different immobilisation techniques include: dip coating, sol gel and electrophoretic coating, thermal, and aerosol powder coating among others.122

Doping with metals

Many studies have been performed in relation to metal or ion doping in order to enhance the photophysical properties of the semiconductor in heterogeneous photocatalysis. Doping with metals such as the Lanthanides, Fe, Pt, Ag and other noble metals may effectively enhance the photocatalytic efficiency.121 It has been postulated that doping the d-block metal surface with various metals and oxides can reduce recombination processes by effectively trapping electrons and additionally extending absorption into the visible region. It can also enhance the rate of the photocatalytic reaction, enhance the yield of a particular product or change the reaction products.19

One of the major challenges with d-block metals photocatalysis has been electron–hole recombination as generally this process competes with photodegradation: doping with metals provides a solution to this problem. It prevents this process with migration of the electron to the metal, by the alignment of the Fermi levels of both the metal and the semiconductor. This not only suppresses the process but also the decrease in electron density causes an increase in hydroxyl acidity which affects the photocatalytic process. The major drawback with adding a dopant is that it heightens the cost of the process particularly when metals such as Ag or Pt are applied.

Selective oxidation/reduction can also be achieved by doping. Depending on the compound, d-block metals mediated photocatalysis can either preferentially oxidise or reduce the contaminant.96 In a study undertaken by (Hoffman, 1995 (ref. 120)). They found that doping with vanadium (IV) accelerated the oxidation of chloroform, and doping with ruthenium accelerated the reduction of carbon tetrachloride. Doping with other species such as silica-tungstic acid can effectively inhibit one of these processes also resulting in a potentially ‘selective’ decomposition.

At this concentration Adan and group then124 reported that various recombination processes start to compete with degradation. ZnO, Cu2O, CuSCN, and NiO nanostructures with different orientation were fabricated by the cost-effective electrodeposition method at room temperature.158,165 NiO has the highest catalytic activity among these modified d-block metal nanostructures of ZnO, Cu2O, CuSCN, and NiO. The greatest enhancement of the photocatalytic activity is obtained using a ZnO/NiO heterostructure, this can be ascribed to the heterojunction structure and extremely higher specific surface area, which can degrade MO.

Kaniou et al.125 found, in the photocatalytic degradation of sulfamethazine, that concentrations above 200 mg L−1 resulted in a decrease in the reaction rate. This particular study examined both TiO2 and ZnO on the degradation of sulfamethazine. Addition of H2O2 to ZnO, interestingly had no effect positive or negative on the reaction rate. Kaniou and co-workers125 explained this in relation to the negligible adsorption of H2O2 on the surface of ZnO which was also noticed by.126,127 The adsorption of H2O2 on d-block metals is much more photocatalytically favorable due to an increase in the reduction by photogenerated electrons of H2O2 and therefore a reduced e−/h+ recombination rate. All in all, optimum concentrations must be realised for the most efficient degradation.

Photo-reduction of bacterial endospore activity

To battle severe environments, thus, to endure in deprived nutrition conditions, several types of bacteria produce tough and temporarily dormant structures called endospores. Endospores can survive desiccation, high temperatures, extreme freezing, and conditions without nutrients.125,134,135 They are also resistant to c-radiation, UV radiation, and chemical disinfectants.135,142 Antibacterial agents, which destroy vegetative cell walls, have minimal effects on endospores. Bacterial endospores have, therefore, long caused serious problems in public health, food industry, and medicine because they are so difficult to kill. Kim and Lee131 first used photocatalysis to treat bacterial endospores. They identified that B. cereus endospores were resistant to treatment (see Fig. 8) using a commercial modified d-block metals such as, ZnO, CdS, TiO2, AgO, Cd–NiP photocatalysts, Degussa P25, but treatment with a nanocomposite, composed of d-block metals and carbon nanotubes, and solar UV lamps inactivated 90% of these spores. Application of metal doping in d-block metals photocatalysis further improved the efficiency of the UV-activated photocatalytic sporicidal effects.136,137 For visible light-responsive photocatalytic inactivation of bacterial endospores,132,133,141 synthesized an Ag, C, and S co-doped TiO2 substrate, excitable under visible light, which showed strong antimicrobial properties against E. coli cells and B. subtilis spores. Kau and group132 reported that photocatalysis by visible light- responsive d-block metals substrates doped with N and C significantly reduced the cell numbers and spore activity of B. subtilis, B. thuringiensis, B. cereus, and B. anthracis. In this report, findings on the photocatalytic treatment of the endospores of the bacterial strains were of particular interest. Although the visible light induced photocatalysis did not kill the spores as effectively as the cells, injection of the photocatalysis-treated B. anthracis spores into mice greatly reduced their mortality.132,138–140 These results indicated that despite incomplete destruction of the endospores, the photocatalysis inactivated some of their pathogenic components without eliminating their germination ability, thus reducing the induced mortality.

Hydrogen production

Designing semiconductor photocatalysts for conversion of renewable and limitless solar energy to clean hydrogen energy has been a promising center for study since its discovery by Fujishima and Honda.91,109,128,129,168–174 The main brain behind this promising investigation is to design an ideal material that should be low-cost, efficient for hydrogen production under visible light illumination.121,178–180

Ao and team researched into Ni2P–CdS hybrid by the in situ growth of well dispersed ultrafine Ni2P nanoparticles on CdS nanorods by a simple solvothermal method and a further surface phosphatization treatment for the first time. They found out that Ni2P–CdS hybrid with optimal composition presented a standard hydrogen evolution rate of 34.9 mmol h−1 g−1 which was about 23 times as high as pure CdS. The improved photocatalytic activity may be due to the intimate interface between well dispersed ultrafine Ni2P coctalysts and the CdS nanorods which was favorable for the photogenerated electrons transfer (Fig. 12) and separation which promoted the visible light catalytic activity.

Applications of modified d-block metals photocatalysis

Modified d-block metal photocatalysis is generally employed because of its versatility in that it can be carried out in both gas and aqueous or organic liquid phases. Herman & J. M. 2005.128 Photoinduced redox reactions were sparked by Fujishima and Honda109,129 with the discovery of water splitting by a single crystal d-block metal electrode. Nowadays, photocatalysis involving d-block metals can be used in various organic reactions such as oxidations, reductions, oxidative cleavages, polymerisations. d-Block metals also have potential uses in environmental decontamination: with the destruction of organic pollutants and the treatment of wastewater, furthermore d-block metals also feature in current solar cell models and self-cleaning window applications among other commercial uses.

It is important to note that the density functional theory (DFT) approach to large transition-metal complexes is currently the only feasible one if their chemistry shall be routinely investigated. Even if special variants of ab initio methods are feasible for a certain molecule, the molecule's structure is likely to be optimized in a DFT calculation under the assumption that the molecular structure will be reliable even if the corresponding DFT energy is not. Therefore, the assessment of DFT methods in transition-metal chemistry is decisive. One systematic failure of DFT of particular importance in transition-metal chemistry is the spin-state energetics problem.211,212

Theoretical investigations have illustrated the structure properties, physicochemical characteristics, and electrochemical behaviors of modified d-block metal materials when loading the uniform terminated –O, –F, and –OH in various density functional theory (DFT) studies205–208 For example, DFT studies have shown that the termination groups of Ti3C2 MXene layers mainly have two distinct configurations: hollow sites located near three neighboring C atoms and hollow sites distributed on the two sides of Ti3C2 sheets.44,46,156,208,209

Challenges in modified d-block metal-photocatalysis

There are two main challenges that face the field of d-block metal photocatalysis and currently these issues can impede its function in wastewater treatment and related.

a. Immobilization of d-block metal such that the material can be easily removed and recycled. Many immobilisation substrates are possible, although achieving the same removal rates and efficiency as suspended d-block metal is the overall goal.

b. The development of visible light sensitised photocatalytic materials such that cheaper visible light sources or solar light can be harnessed. Currently, UV light is required which heightens the cost of the process significantly. Achieving visible light sensitised photocatalysts can be done in many ways such as doping with metals, or coating with dyes. Overcoming these drawbacks is the present focus of research in modified d-block metal photocatalysis.

Future prospects and conclusion

From the discussion, the future research of the modified d-block metal photocatalysts may consider designing and synthesizing of more effective nanostructures, for instance, hollow spheres with a high surface area or nanoplates with the highly active surface; and the degradation behaviour and mechanism of more types of pollutants, especially for non-dyed pollutants, better CO2 reduction and hydrogen production.

Also, future prospect should consider scheming of modified d-block metals that are responsive to morphology monitoring, evaluating the photocatalysis practicality and efficacy of traditional synthesis and preparative strategies of modified d-block metals and then discovering the functions of distinct modified d-block metals particles in considering viable fuel production, and its effective application in solar energy utilization.

Modified d-block metals are known to have mostly been used as co-catalysts to help improve the separation and transfer efficacy of electron–hole pairs to enhance the photocatalytic performances of the catalysts. A work led by Li and coworkers201,203,210 indicated that the modified d-block metals such as Cu3P etc.… coupled with Nano based particles for instance, n-type graphitic carbon nitride usually forms a p–n junction to promote charge separation and transfer for an improved photocatalytic activity.

Modified d-block metals are very important in the management of the environment especially those which are used in the photocatalytic processes. This was confirmed by ref. 204 in their research which they indicated that noble metal catalysts have significant application prospects in the chemical production processes and environmental protection fields.

To conclude, this comprehensive discussion on modified d-block metal photocatalysis highlights the contemporary enhancements on the photocatalytic activity of these metals through their structure modifications, synthesis, and catalytic potentials. This perspective presents a better understanding of the various mechanisms of solar harvest of modified d-block metals for better visible light responsive activities. In view of that, more studies are also required in order to making full use of the excellent properties of these modified d-block metals in solar synthesis.
