Exploring the interfaces between metal electrodes and aqueous electrolytes with electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

Aliaksandr S. Bandarenka *
Center for Electrochemical Sciences, Ruhr-Universitt Bochum, Universittstr. 150, D-44780, Bochum, Germany. E-mail: aliaksandr.bandarenka@rub.de

Received 20th April 2013 , Accepted 7th July 2013

First published on 8th July 2013

Abstract

Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is one of the oldest electroanalytical techniques. With respect to the investigation of the electrodeelectrolyte interfaces, it has gained wide popularity as a non-destructive, sensitive and highly informative method. A particularly attractive advantage is that it provides a unique opportunity to distinguish contributions from different processes which take place simultaneously at the electrode surface. During the past decade, considerable progress has been made in the field of impedance spectroscopy to advance data acquisition, modelling and spectra analysis. EIS has evolved from slow data acquisition procedures with semi-quantitative interpretation to innovative methodologies which allow simple operation and accurate analysis using hundreds or even thousands of spectra; these spectra can often be recorded as a result of a single experiment. Impedance spectroscopy is nowadays widely combined with other techniques, with successful application in areas ranging from analytical and physical chemistry to localized impedance microscopies. The focus of this review is on recent experimental and theoretical achievements in the characterisation of the interfaces between metal electrodes and aqueous electrolytes using EIS. Some key challenges to further increase the informative power of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy are also outlined.

1 Introduction

Progress in the field of analytical chemistry is strictly dependent on the development of advanced, but affordable techniques and methodologies.13 In electro-analytical chemistry and electrochemistry, the development of efficient characterisation methods to better understand the boundaries between electronically conducting electrodes and ionically conducting electrolytes plays a crucial role.48 The properties of this electrochemical interface determine the performance of many important energy conversion and storage devices,912 such as fuel cells,1315 batteries,16,17 and capacitors.1820 In a wider context, insight into the electrodeelectrolyte interface is vital for a variety of areas of modern science and technology, from the development of new generations of sensitive and selective sensors2124 to corrosion protection technologies.2527

Nowadays, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is one of the most informative techniques for the characterisation of the electrochemical interface.2835 This method is based on the analysis of the response of the system under investigation to alternating current (ac) probing signals. The term impedance was introduced by Heaviside at the end of the 19th century to extend the concept of electrical resistance to ac circuits.29 In this framework, the impedance is a measure of the opposition of a system to the alternating current.

In different historical periods, progress in the field of EIS was stimulated by the dominant tendencies in physical and analytical chemistry, solid state physics, biology and other disciplines. The first significant breakthrough in the theoretical description of electrochemical systems under ac-probing was made by E. Warburg who derived the analytical equation for impedance caused by diffusional transport of electroactive species to an electrode surface.36 From a historical standpoint, this was probably the first deep analysis of the phenomena induced by an alternating current in an electrochemical environment. The next milestone in the development of the technique is associated with the work of Dolin and Erschler in 1940 (ref. 37) and Randles in 1947.38 They established a paradigm in which the response of an electrode in contact with an electrolyte was considered as the sum of two formally independent constituents: the response of the double layer (or the interface itself) and the response of the electrochemical (Faradaic) reactions. This idea has been successfully used since that time to describe the behaviour of the electrodeelectrolyte interface.

The invention of the first potentiostat by Hickling in 1941 (ref. 39) and, further, the development of frequency response analysers in the 1970s started a new era in EIS data acquisition. Capitalizing on these inventions, modern equipment can now record hundreds of impedance spectra during a single electrochemical experiment. Amazing progress in data acquisition has also stimulated the rapid development of advanced theoretical frameworks32,34,35 and software to interpret and process impedance spectra more efficiently.4043

During the past decade, EIS methodology has gradually moved from the analysis of a few spectra to the processing of large EIS datasets recorded as a function of many parameters, such as electrode potential, time, or spatial coordinates.4455 Nowadays, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy is frequently combined with other analytical techniques to perform several parallel measurements in one experiment.5663 These hyphenated techniques were shown to allow physical models of the electrochemical interfaces to be established with much greater ease, which provides deeper insight into the complex processes at the electrode surfaces.

The focus of this review is to analyse and illustrate some recent experimental and theoretical achievements which have significantly improved the informative power of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, particularly in the characterisation of interfaces between metal electrodes and aqueous electrolytes. Section 2 analyses the opportunities provided by dynamic impedance measurements, where data are acquired and analysed as a function of electrode potential. Some recent illustrative examples of the combination of EIS with other essentially electrochemical techniques are discussed in Section 3. Advances in the field of localised impedance measurements are summarized in Section 4. Finally, some key challenges necessary to improve EIS efficiency are outlined.

2 Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy becomes dynamic

2.1 EIS: probing signals and responses

A common EIS experiment normally assumes the use of three electrodes: a working electrode (the object under investigation), a counter electrode (to apply the electric current) and a reference electrode (provides a known reference point which is constant in time). A potentiostat establishes the required potential of the working electrode, E0, versus the reference electrode and maintains it. Then, a probing signal of different frequencies is applied E(t) = E0 + Emsin(t), where t is the time, Em is the amplitude of the probing signal (typically Em 10 mV), and is the angular frequency. Due to specific properties of the electrochemical systems, the current response will depend on the applied frequency and will be shifted in time with respect to the probing signal: I(t) = I0 + Imsin(t + ), where I0 is the direct current (which can also be a function of time), Im is the amplitude of the ac response and is the phase shift between the probing signal and the response.

The EIS data acquisition systems estimate the absolute values of the impedance |Z| = Em/Im and corresponding phase shifts at different frequencies within a selected frequency range. The spectra are commonly represented using several types of plots. Fig. 1A and B show an impedance spectrum using the so-called Bode plots: i.e. the dependence of |Z| and on the logarithm of frequency. Alternatively, Fig. 1C represents the same data using a 3D plot, as the dependence of the real part of impedance, ReZ = |Z|cos, and imaginary part of impedance, ImZ = |Z|sin, on frequency. A more compact way of presenting the data shown in Fig. 1C is to use a Nyquist plot (Fig. 1D), where the spectrum is represented as the dependence of ImZ on ReZ. Other types of EIS data representations are also used, depending on the properties of the systems under investigation. In general, the choice of the graphical representation of the data is also dependent on particular research targets. In some cases, it is convenient to represent the data utilizing other basic formalisms, for instance using the complex admittances, Y, the complex permittivity, , or the complex modulus, M, (where M = jC0Z, with C0 being the vacuum capacitance of the cell).64

The ultimate goal of the analysis of impedance spectra is to elucidate a physical model of the system and estimate its parameters. Unfortunately, unambiguous elucidation of the model and further data interpretation are not trivial tasks, if just one impedance spectrum (e.g. at some given electrode potential) is available.65 In order to facilitate these procedures, it is often necessary to examine how the impedance values and the shape of the spectra change as a function of some other key parameters. Additional methodological and experimental complications arise when the properties of the electrochemical system can irreversibly change with time during the measurements. Both issues certainly require that the impedance spectra should be acquired as quickly as possible.66,67 In the former situation, this would reduce the time of the experiment; while in the latter case, the spectra would be recorded before the properties of the system change significantly. During the past decade, relatively fast acquisition of large experimental datasets in EIS (as a function of different parameters) has become widely possible leading to unique and, previously, inaccessible opportunities, particularly in the field of electroanalytical chemistry.

2.2 Dynamic EIS: data acquisition schemes

In electrochemistry, the electrode potential is the parameter which determines the status of the system.68 Therefore, a number of attempts were undertaken to enable acquisition of impedance spectra as a function of the electrode potential at potential scan rates comparable to those commonly used in the potentiodynamic voltammetry.6977 Importantly, the scan rate and the frequency range must satisfy the quasi-stationarity criteria, i.e. the mean values of the parameters which define the system properties (such as surface concentration, electrode surface area, adsorbate surface coverage, etc.) should change only slightly or, ideally, not change at all during the acquisition of a single spectrum.67

Apart from simplicity of the above-mentioned idea, the way to its realisation was not straightforward. Perhaps the first illustrative demonstration of this concept was performed by Smith et al. in 1977.78,79 It was further elaborated in 1997 by Hz et al.80 to convert this approach to an affordable technique; only once computers and their performance could largely facilitate and optimise acquisition and analysis of large experimental datasets. To decrease the measurement time, Hz et al. used complex probing signals composed of tens of periodic functions with different frequencies, phases and amplitudes. The probing signal was then periodically superimposed onto a staircase potential scan. The response of the system at each of the potential steps was analysed using fast Fourier transform (FFT) algorithms to obtain the ac-current amplitudes, phase shifts and, consequently, the impedance spectra. As a result, complex dependencies of the impedance spectra on the electrode potential were obtained. Fig. 2A shows one example of these dependencies, where the real part of impedance is plotted as a function of the logarithm of frequency and the electrode potential.

The first potentiodynamic impedance measurements were performed with a potential scan rate of 2 mV s1. The current FFT-approach allows reliable acquisition of impedance spectra with scan rates close to those frequently applied in potentiodynamic voltammetries (50 mV s1). An illustrative example of a potentiodynamic impedance dataset is shown in Fig. 2B characterising anodic oxidation of a Zn electrode surface in an alkaline solution. This is a typical example of an intrinsically dynamic system where the electrode surface can change its properties markedly fast during the experiment, and where classical slow impedance measurement schemes often experience serious complications, particularly in spectra interpretation.

Despite the fact that the FFT-EIS has a number of advantages, this approach is sensitive to noise effects: the main problem comes from the low intensity of the constituent responses and their non-uniformity.32,70,81 One possible way to overcome these limitations is to accurately optimize the probing signal to maximize the intensity of the constituents of the current response.32,70 Another approach has been proposed to use frequency-by-frequency probing (similar to the classical experiments), however, with optimisations at the computer-hardware level combined with fast algorithms of the response analysis in real time.49 These relatively simple optimisations have resulted in the possibility of accurately recording impedance spectra for a variety of systems at the scan rates in the range between 0.5 mV s1 and 10 mV s1, maintaining the potential steps of the staircase potential scan as low as 2 mV.8288

Unfortunately, there are some methodological limitations of the potentiodynamic approach in EIS. These limitations originate from the fact that the potential scan rate controls the lowest probing frequency and, hence, limit the frequency range. Practically, this means that some phenomena (e.g. associated with the mass transport), which mainly manifest themselves at low ac frequencies, can be overlooked. One of the ways to decrease the probability of mistakes in data interpretation is to record the impedance spectra over a wider frequency range (including lower frequencies) at a few key points in a staircase potential scan.54 At these points, the potential step could be asymmetric: with increased step heights and, correspondingly, wider time windows the ac-probing could be performed. An alternative and promising approach to overcome many methodological limitations of potentiodynamic EIS is to use so-called hyphenated techniques which involve (in a single experiment) impedance spectroscopy measurements and measurements performed using other techniques to collect the maximum amount of independent data about the interface status and its properties; this will be discussed in Section 3.

It should be briefly noted here that advances in fast spectra acquisition also facilitated the investigation of electrochemical systems that change their properties with time at a constant electrode potential.59,90,91 For that, impedance spectra are recorded periodically at short-time intervals. This approach has recently been used to monitor the interface status during metal electrodeposition59,90 and intercalation.61

Apart from state-of-the-art acquisition schemes and advanced hardware, the key to determine the performance of an analytical technique is the ability to extract the maximum amount of valuable information from the measured response. A drastic increase in the amount of spectra recorded in modern EIS experiments has resulted in the development of new approaches and algorithms to facilitate modelling, processing and data analysis.

2.3 Data analysis and interpretation

The overall scheme of the analysis and interpretation of EIS data is similar to that used in other analytical methods (Fig. 3A): the object under investigation represents a black box with unknown properties. Essentially, the analysis of EIS data is the solution of the inverse problem.92 Based on the measured response, it is necessary to elucidate the model of the system and estimate its parameters by fitting the spectra to the model using special software.4043,92,93

It should be noted that before the actual modelling starts, experimental impedance spectra should be carefully inspected to satisfy at least three common criteria: (i) causality (i.e. the measured ac response is only due to the applied perturbation); (ii) linearity (i.e. the ac response follows the principle of superposition); and stability (i.e. the system does not significantly change with time during acquisition of one spectrum). Modern KramersKronig transformation (KKT) algorithms provide a check on the validity of EIS datasets obtained over a wide range of probing frequencies. Practically, the KramersKronig check aims at proving the basic hypotheses using relatively simple rules like, e.g. for a valid EIS dataset it is possible to accurately calculate the real part of the impedance from the imaginary part and vice versa. Alternatively, it should be possible to calculate the phase shift from the magnitude of the impedance. Further details on KKT in EIS data analysis, recent achievements in the development of the KramersKronig check algorithms as well as some interesting KKT-related applications can be found in ref. 35,94102.

After the KramersKronig check confirms the validity of EIS datasets, further modelling and analysis are possible. Perhaps largely due to historical reasons, the modelling of electrochemical systems in EIS is nowadays commonly performed using so-called equivalent electric circuits (EEC).29 The equivalent circuits can be considered as a schematic (graphical) representation of complex equations which describe the electrochemical system.93 Undoubtedly, the description of systems using only mathematical equations is much more universal: in some cases it is not possible to represent equations by a common EEC. However, the widespread use of equivalent circuits appeared to be convenient in everyday laboratory practice.

Obviously, while the forward problem has (in deterministic physics) a unique solution, the inverse problem, unfortunately, does not.103 In general, there could be a variety of equations and, correspondingly, EECs, which can describe the measured response. Therefore, similar to other techniques, modelling and data analysis in EIS is often a demanding procedure.

Typically, the models can be classified as (i) physical (or physico-chemical, or structural) and (ii) formal.103 Physical EIS models link measured EIS data with physico-chemical parameters of the process (kinetic parameters, concentrations, diffusion coefficients, etc.). Formal models describe the experimental impedances in order to obtain a good fit between the calculated and experimental impedances. The parameters obtained as a result of this procedure do not necessarily have clear physico-chemical significance. In order to gain deeper insight into the processes taking place at the electrodeelectrolyte interface, elucidation of physical models is mandatory. It should be noted that the physical models can be in many cases adequately expressed either by EECs or mathematical equations, depending on the particular properties of systems or methodologies used.

According to the current understanding of the interfaces between metal electrodes and aqueous electrolytes, the measured electrochemical impedance spectra contain three main contributions: the response of the interface itself (with the impedance Zint), the response due to electrochemical reactions (with the impedance ZF) and the response due to the electrolyte (with the impedance Zel) (Fig. 3B).32 Therefore, a typical model of the working electrodeelectrolyte interface in the presence of an electrochemical reaction will consist of at least three parts, connected as shown schematically in Fig. 3B, according to the general assumptions by Randles, Dolin, and Erschler, as discussed in Section 1. The goal is then to elucidate particular equations for these three constituents Zint, ZF and Zel. For instance, if the electrolyte is liquid, its impedance can be expressed through a resistance Zel = Rel. The latter depends on the electrolyte conductivity and relative positions of the electrodes.

Recently, the advantages of the modern EIS to acquire and analyse large experimental datasets have been demonstrated for detailed investigation of the kinetics of different classes of electrochemical processes, for instance surface limited adsorption,45,65 electrocatalysis,54 metal deposition,59 degradation of coatings, etc.57 In most cases, these experiments would be extremely difficult to perform in the framework of the classical EIS methodology even 15 years ago. It can be envisioned that the combination of the well-developed theory of the Faradaic impedance with advanced experimental procedures will gradually improve our understanding of a variety of important electrochemical reactions.

While the theories, which describe the Faradaic impedance, ZF, are relatively well-developed, covering different reaction mechanisms,106 diffusion limitations, adsorption, etc., the interpretation of the impedance response of the interface itself is often not easy.107 The difficulties with interpretation are caused by so-called frequency dispersion, when the measured values of the double layer capacitance depend on the frequency of the probing ac-signal.32,108111 These difficulties essentially result from the fact that the representation of the double layer as a simple ideal capacitance is not adequate for numerous electrochemical systems. These discrepancies can, in general, be caused by different reasons, which are not easy to predict. Due to these complexities, the part of the response related to the interface itself is often omitted from detailed considerations and discussions in impedance spectroscopy.107 It should be, however, noted here that the frequency dispersion is not specific only to the double layer capacitance. This phenomenon is frequently observed in ac-investigations of considerably different electrochemical processes, see ref. 112 and 113.

When = 1, this corresponds to the pure ideal capacitive behaviour. Introduction of the semi-empirical CPE represents the fact that the double layer and the interface itself do dissipate the energy of the probing ac-signals even in the absence of Faradaic reactions. Several hypotheses attempted to explain the frequency dispersion, such as the influence of surface roughness,114116 an effect of the fractal nature of electrode surfaces,117 a non-uniform current distribution in electrochemical systems,118 the effect of specific adsorption of electrolyte components, etc.119121 However, most likely, there is more than one factor which contributes to the observed non-ideal behaviour of the electrochemical interface. During the last two decades, there were a number of attempts to address issues related to the frequency dispersion of the double layer and acquire more interpretable information from this part of the ac-response.32,34,109,121 Arbitrarily, one can distinguish at least two types of approaches implemented recently.121 The first approach takes into account a variety of cases due to artifacts produced by the non-uniform distribution of electric fields across the electrode surface or due to dispersion of time constants for solution resistances and the interfacial capacitances. Another approach is based on experiments with well-defined model systems, often using single crystal electrodes to address kinetic dispersion effects or effects due to specific adsorption of electrolyte components. Those experiments resulted in important findings that in many cases 2D and 3D structuring effects at the interface, particularly in the double layer, hinder its ability to respond to the ac-probing as an ideal capacitor. Ordering at the surface causes a relatively slow exchange of ions between the outer and inner Helmholtz planes of the double layer and slow reorientation of H2O molecules at the electrode surface, as the interface is close to a local energy minimum.109 The latter fact leads to dissipation of the energy of the probing signal and is responsible for the CPE behaviour.

The examples shown above and also reported recently54 in the literature show promise that parameters of the CPE obtained as a function of the electrode potential can be reasonably used to detect phase transitions at the electrodeelectrolyte interfaces.107 This would provide better insight into the interface and increase the impact of measurements made by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy. However, while the accurate analysis and reliable interpretation of the parameters describing the Faradaic and double layer responses become more and more affordable when the physical model of the system is known, elucidation of the physical models themselves still represents a significant challenge.65 One of the possible ways to increase the efficiency of this procedure is to use independent information obtained during the same experiment by other techniques.

3 Towards more informative approaches in EIS: hyphenated techniques

Commonly, well-established analytical techniques have their known advantages, drawbacks and conventional areas of application. In electrochemical research, a variety of methods are available; however, each of these normally deals with a certain class of objects, processes and responses. The everyday practice in electrochemical laboratories creates a necessity to use different techniques sequentially, one-by-one, in order to elucidate the model of the system and get more comprehensive information about it. If the latter can irreversibly change its properties during the experiment, the disintegration between methods is a serious impediment for a detailed characterisation of these complex objects. Two viable approaches are used in modern EIS to overcome these issues. One of them is to combine EIS and non-electrochemical techniques, for instance photocurrent spectroscopy, or various in situ optical spectroscopies (these combinations are outside the scope of this review). Another approach is to combine essentially electrochemical methods to form hyphenated electrochemical techniques (i.e. the techniques combining two or more electrochemical methods, which complement each other in one experiment).

While EIS is considered one of the most informative techniques, potentiodynamic cyclic voltammetry (CV)124 is perhaps the most popular electroanalytical method due to its relative experimental simplicity and ability to provide the first overview of the system properties. This method uses a cyclic linear change of the electrode potential as a probing signal and analyses the direct current (dc) flowing through the system. It should be noted here that this approach is not entirely identical to the potentiodynamic EIS discussed in Section 2.2, as the potentiodynamic EIS measurements are not necessarily accompanied by dc voltammetric measurements. The idea to combine the two methods, EIS and CV, and perform the measurements in cyclic electrode potential scans has attracted noticeable attention recently, as it would facilitate the modelling, and enable a more detailed characterisation, of electrodeelectrolyte interfaces using both ac and dc responses simultaneously.65,77,105,125,126

Practically, acquisition of a dc voltammogram can be performed during potentiodynamic EIS experiments with relative ease. With a few modifications of the measurement protocol and data analysis, it is normally possible to extract the dc response from the overall current measured during the potentiodynamic scan.48,49,105 As a result, EIS and CV datasets are available simultaneously, describing how the constituents of the electrochemical impedance and direct current depend on the electrode potential in a cyclic potential scan. The ability to perform the parallel measurements in cyclic potential scans is especially important for systems which do not demonstrate the same properties in the forward and backward scans and also in cycle-to-cycle sequences. Possible reasons for this kind of behaviour include alloying, dielectric film growth, electrode surface oxidation, intercalation, etc.

Fig. 7 shows one example of a CV-EIS experimental dataset obtained in a cyclic potential scan for a glassy carbon electrode in a 5.2 mM K3[Fe(CN)6]+1 M KCl electrolyte at the scan rate of 4 mV s1.105 In Fig. 7, a typical cyclic voltammogram for this system is shown on the front facet, while the impedance spectra obtained in the same cyclic scan are shown behind the CV in the Nyquist coordinate system. It is notable that, while the CV shows considerable differences in the cathodic and anodic scans, the impedance spectra corresponding to the same electrode potential are practically the same, irrespective of the scan direction (due to the reversibility of the redox transformations). Importantly, simultaneous analysis of EIS and CV datasets gives, in this case, a more objective electrochemical picture of the system. EIS helps in elucidation of the interface model, while monitoring the charge passed through the system (obtained from the CVs) compensates possible weaknesses of EIS to detect relatively fast but at the same time irreversible changes at the electrode surface during the experiments. However, even with these opportunities, CV-EIS can still overlook some processes which do not contribute to ac and dc responses. The latter fact can seriously complicate understanding of the properties of the interface and lead to incorrect models of the system. Therefore, optimal hyphenated techniques are normally necessary, where a few methods are combined to maximally compensate their known disadvantages and reveal all important properties of the system. Consequently, the task for the experimental (electro)analytical chemistry in this case is to combine several techniques to obtain the maximum amount of independent and self-consistent data from the minimum number of parallel measurements.65

One of the above-mentioned combinations includes simultaneous monitoring of the electrode mass change performed in parallel with CV and EIS measurements. Nowadays, in situ electrochemical gravimetry is performed using the so-called electrochemical quartz crystal microbalance (EQCM). The latter is a sensitive technique capable of measuring sub-monolayer changes in mass on an electrode surface.127 A common EQCM sensor is composed of a piezoelectric quartz crystal, coated with a metal on both sides and built into a high-frequency oscillator. One of the coated crystal faces is exposed to the electrolyte solution and acts as the working electrode. A change in the mass of the working electrode, m, leads to a linear change in the resonant frequency of the quartz crystal, f, with the relationship:

f = Cfm(4)
where Cf is the sensitivity factor, which is a fundamental property of the quartz crystal. The analysis of f can therefore be used to elucidate mass loading at the electrode surface during electrochemical processes. It should be noted that the high-frequency QCM resonant circuits (the frequencies are typically higher than 5 MHz) normally do not interfere with the measurements of the electrochemical impedance, which are typically performed at frequencies lower than 100 kHz using liquid electrolytes.

EQCM introduces a new direction to the characterisation of the electrodeelectrolyte interfaces with the ability to detect changes of electrode mass within the nanogram range. This method makes the electrochemical investigation more objective, as it is possible to detect mass changes which do not contribute to the current response.128131 The first realisation of a hyphenated technique combining impedance and gravimetric measurements in a cyclic potential scan was reported by Berkes et al.132 and further elaborated to investigate different classes of electrochemical objects and processes.5861,65 These first experiments have indeed confirmed that analysis of the ac and dc responses, strengthened with gravimetric data, allows a relatively easy elucidation of the physical models and increases the objectiveness of data interpretation.

Other recent strategies to construct hyphenated techniques with EIS have reported the use of UV/Vis and EIS to study the reactivity of Ag nanoparticles,140 and different combinations of scanning probe techniques with simultaneous impedance measurements, as reviewed in detail recently.141

In summary, the development of hyphenated electrochemical techniques based on EIS has shown great promise for a detailed characterisation of the interfaces between metal electrodes and aqueous electrolytes. Their particular power is in more efficient elucidation of the physical models of electrochemical systems. However, this approach is in its infancy. New methodologies for more efficient and objective data analysis and interpretation are still necessary. To date, hyphenated EIS techniques have been applied using a few model systems and only several classes of electrochemical processes. Nevertheless, one can foresee that wider application of this approach in the future to investigate other real-world complex systems will rapidly advance the associated methodologies and experimental protocols.

4 Electrochemical impedance microscopies

Recent experimental and theoretical advances discussed in Sections 2 and 3 deal with global electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, where the data give averaged information about the whole sample surface. While many surfaces of single crystal electrodes can be considered as uniform, the surfaces of real-world functional materials are frequently non-homogeneous. In order to obtain spatial distribution information of the impedance for these kinds of samples, localized impedance measurements should be performed. This information is particularly important in electrocatalysis,142 bio-(electro)chemistry6,143 and corrosion science.144

One can distinguish several ways to generate the localized impedance data, which have been reported in the literature. Among them are vibrating probe techniques,145147 microcapillary-based techniques,148 bi-electrode probe techniques,144,149 and techniques based on the application of scanning electrochemical microscopes (SECM).6 Clearly, these methods have their advantages and drawbacks and, therefore, the particular choice of the experimental scheme in localized impedance measurements depends on the properties of the sample and specific goals of the research.

Perhaps, the least demanding experimental protocol, which generates a wealth of information as a result of the relatively simple experiments, is the one based on ac-SECMs. The particular advantage of this method is that spatial distributions of the impedance at different ac-frequencies are available in high xy resolution as a result of a single 2D scan. Notably, while other electrochemical scanning probe techniques, such as atomic force microscopy or scanning tunnelling microscopy, provide information about the surface,150,151 few methods are capable of giving such information about the properties of the solidliquid interface as a function of spatial coordinates.142 The ac-SECMs enable visualization of microscopic domains with different interfacial properties at different ac-frequencies.6 Importantly, they are robust and can be used for imaging the samples of significantly different properties, in low conductivity solutions, either in the absence or presence of redox mediators. In ac-SECM experiments, a Pt-disk electrode (typically with diameter 1030 m) shielded by a cylindrical glass shaft is used as a probe. An important peculiarity of the ac-SECMs is that the sample is not connected to a potentiostat**, which makes it unique for specific applications, such as corrosion studies under real conditions (practically, in this case only the probe can govern the local properties of the sampleelectrolyte interface).6

Localized impedance measurements when compared with the global impedance of the sample can give a lot of valuable information where bi-electrode probe techniques are used. Particularly, the localized impedance measurements were shown to be helpful in distinguishing the origin of the global CPE-behaviour of the interface.152 If the localized impedance measurements reveal no CPE-behaviour, the origin of the global CPE could be attributed to the geometry-induced non-uniform potential and current distributions. In contrast, if the localized impedance measurements show the local CPE-behaviour of the interface, this can be used to extract additional valuable information about the samples, particularly, distributions of conductivity of coatings formed at the electrode surface.152

In general, electrochemical impedance microscopies are becoming more and more popular for numerous applications ranging from electroanalysis to bio-medicine.6,142144,153155 With the increased power of actuators and data processing methods, it is clear that these techniques will play an increasingly important role in the future.

5 Summary and outlook

Through the years, analytical techniques based on ac-probing signals have proved to give affordable and efficient means for characterisation of the electrodeelectrolyte interfaces. EIS, despite being one of the oldest of these methods, nowadays keeps leading positions in electroanalytical chemistry. However, with new challenges which currently face electroanalysis, electrochemistry and materials science, for instance in the development of efficient functional materials for sustainable energy conversion and provision, this technique increasingly needs development of new advanced experimental protocols and methodologies as well as data analysis procedures.

The current stage in the development of EIS is largely influenced by the fact that the systems under investigation become more complex, while the required level of understanding gradually increases. On the one hand, this leads to a necessity for novel, viable and efficient methodologies for elucidation of physical models of the electrochemical interfaces. On the other hand, as modern EIS experiments become more and more sophisticated, they often require an efficient and accurate analysis of exceedingly large experimental datasets. These two key elements will likely determine the progress in the field of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy in the nearest future.

One straightforward way to facilitate and advance the modelling of the electrodeelectrolyte interfaces is to combine EIS with other methods to construct new efficient hyphenated electrochemical techniques as well as methods combining electrochemical and non-electrochemical approaches. Additionally, the development of electrochemical impedance microscopies in order to obtain local information on non-homogeneous samples is necessary to make this modelling more objective and accurate.

The development of new universal or special fitting strategies capable of processing hundreds, or even thousands, of spectra automatically is of particular importance. It should be noted that while modern equipment allows the acquisition of large amounts of data fast enough to investigate markedly dynamic systems, their fitting still represents a challenge in many cases. Practically, this means that due to complexities in the analysis of large EIS datasets, the opportunities which are provided by modern equipment are not fully used.

Nevertheless, the history of the methods and the current success of the EIS in solving many important and complicated analytical tasks allow us to be optimistic about the future of the development of this method.
