Abstract

Since the development of metallopolymers, there has been tremendous interest in the applications of this type of materials. The interest in these materials stems from their potential use in industry as catalysts, biomedical agents in healthcare, energy storage and production as well as climate change mitigation. The past two decades have clearly shown exponential growth in the development of many new classes of metallopolymers that address these issues. Today, metallopolymers are considered to be at the forefront for discovering new and sustainable heterogeneous catalysts, therapeutics for drug-resistant diseases, energy storage and photovoltaics, molecular barometers and thermometers, as well as carbon dioxide sequesters. The focus of this review is to highlight the advances in design of metallopolymers with specific sustainable applications.

1. Introduction

Although contemporary human achievements have been unprecedented in all aspects of life, they have raised a number of sustainability issues. Climate change, drug resistance, and depleting fossil fuel reserves all highlight the unsustainable aftermath of these achievements. Materials scientists are designing metallopolymers as innovative solutions or connections to these issues.1 Combined with the rich and continuously growing array of ligands and synthesizing methods for varieties of transition metals, these compounds have outstanding potential for use in a broad range of advanced sustainable applications including materials science,118 catalysis and biocatalysis,1929 healthcare,3034 energy,3545 and environment.4653 As a remarkable example, polymetallocenylsilanes discovered by Manners's group showed key properties of metallopolymers and built a connection between new fundamental properties and sustainable applications.5480 The implications of these metallopolymers for addressing these sustainability issues are now established.

Previous review articles8199 on metallopolymers mainly examined the synthesis and functionalities, but the present review will connect these functionalities with sustainable applications, specifically outlining how the functionalities of the metal center help in addressing the global challenges of climate change, energy crisis, and the burden of diseases as well as advancing sustainable chemistry. Our literature search reveals that a review presenting metallopolymers in this context is timely given the current importance of sustainable development. The review will mainly discuss the following aspects for advanced sustainable applications: metallopolymer design and synthesis, sustainable catalysis and biocatalysis, sustainable biomedical materials, sustainable energy storage and production, and mitigation of climate change. We will discuss these developments as well as challenges and outlook.

2. Metallopolymer design and synthesis

Metallopolymers contain a variety of metal centers from main group metals to transition metals and lanthanides. In addition, the metals are located either in the polymer main chain or in the side group structure. As a further subdivision, metallopolymers are linear, highly branched, or dendritic. The linkages binding the metals are covalent, leading to essentially irreversible or static binding, or noncovalent, which could allow potentially reversible dynamic binding.

In the past decades, groups worldwide have overcome many of the obstacles to the preparation of high molecular weight and soluble metallopolymers. A multitude of protocols for the synthesis of well-defined metallopolymers have been established through the development of new synthetic approaches that are compatible with the presence of metal centers.100127 Thus, chemists rely on a rich tool box to obtain desired metallopolymers with examples including polycondensation, ring-opening polymerization, chain-growth condensation polymerization, electro-polymerization, and living ionic as well as controlled radical polymerization. Besides these methods, many examples are known of post-synthesis installation: polymers modified by attaching metal centers to an already existing backbone or where metal-containing residues have been added to a polymer chain. Simultaneously, new advanced or improved characterization techniques have become available making the structural analysis of metal-containing polymers much easier.128132 Access to usable quantities of metallopolymers with a variety of structures has allowed detailed studies of their properties and a range of advanced sustainable applications.

3. Sustainable catalysis and biocatalysis

Recent developments concerning the application of metallopolymers in catalysis are reviewed in this section, with a special focus on carboncarbon formation. Examples of reactions that are covered include not only the classical Suzuki, Sonogashira and Heck couplings, but also aryl amination, epoxide opening, rearrangements, metathesis and cyclopropanation etc. Applications of metallopolymers as metalloenzymes in the field of biocatalytic asymmetric synthesis and its DFT are also discussed. This section covers the literature concerning the application of metallopolymer reagents in catalysis from 2008 until August 2018. For earlier reports in this area we refer to comprehensive reviews.1922,133,134 A variety of metallopolymer- and metallodendrimer-stabilized metal nanoparticles have been designed and applied to various catalytic reactions.135147

3.1 Homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis

According to the different types of metals the metallopolymers contained, this section will be mainly discussed and divided into: (1) Pd-complex containing metallopolymers; (2) Pd nanoparticles incorporating metallopolymers; (3) Pt-containing metallopolymers (4) Au-containing metallopolymers; (5) Ru-containing metallopolymers; (6) Rh-containing metallopolymers; (7) Ag-containing metallopolymers; (8) Cu-containing metallopolymers; (9) Fe-containing metallopolymers; (10) Ni-containing metallopolymers; (11) Co-containing metallopolymers; (12) other metal-containing metallopolymers; (13) mixed-metal-containing metallopolymers.
3.1.1 Palladium-containing polymers. Palladium catalyzed CC couplings are among the most powerful reactions in the toolbox of modern organic synthesis. The reason for their importance is their wide generality, the mild reaction conditions employed and the compatibility of CC coupling with many different functional groups. Palladium-containing polymers are mainly divided into palladium complex-containing and palladium nanoparticle-containing polymers as follows.148160

Increasing numbers of efficient polymer-supported palladium complexes have been developed for catalytic purposes. For example, Karimi and coworkers described the preparation and characterization of a group of main-chain organometallic polymers (NHCPd MCOP) with different N-alkyl substituted groups such as benzyl, n-hexyl, and n-dodecyl (Fig. 1).161 The polymers bearing the more lipophilic group, n-dodecyl, were found to be more reactive and recoverable catalytic systems in the SuzukiMiyaura cross-coupling reaction of chloroarenes with different types of arylboronic acid under aqueous conditions. Several experiments on the reaction mixture such as bright-field microscopy, dynamic light scattering (DLS) and kinetic monitoring at an early stage confirmed not only that the described organometallic polymers are a source of production of trace amounts of Pd nanoparticles, but the capsular structures of these lipophilic polymers in water provide a means of entrapment of nanoclusters in a hydrophobic region, thus accelerating the reaction in pure water in the absence of any co-organic solvent.


Fig. 1 A study on applications of N-substituted main-chain NHCpalladium polymers as recyclable self-supported catalysts for the SuzukiMiyaura coupling of aryl halides in water. Adapted with permission from ref. 161. Copyright 2011, American Chemical Society.

Reusable, self-assembled catalysts of poly(imidazole-acrylamide) and palladium species have been developed by Yamada, Uozumi and coworkers, who applied their methodology to the preparation of polymeric metal catalysts (Fig. 2).162 This metalloenzyme-inspired polymeric imidazole Pd catalyst (MEPI-Pd) was readily prepared by the coordinative convolution of (NH4)2PdCl4 and poly[(N-vinylimidazole)-co-(N-isopropylacrylamide)5] in a methanol/water solution. MEPI-Pd (0.840 mol ppm Pd) promoted the allylic arylation/alkenylation of allylic acetates and carbonates with tetraarylborates, arylboronic acid, and alkenyl boron reagents in alcohol and/or water. Moreover, MEPI-Pd efficiently drove the SuzukiMiyaura reaction of a variety of inactivated aryl chlorides as well as aryl bromides and iodides with aryl boron reagents in water with a TON of up to 3570000. MEPI-Pd was reused for the allylic arylation and SuzukiMiyaura reaction of an aryl chloride without loss of catalytic activity.

(B) Applications of MEPI-Pd in recycling of MEPI-Pd for allylic arylation. (C) Applications of MEPI-Pd in the SuzukiMiyaura reaction of an electron-rich aryl chloride in water. Adapted with permission from ref. 162. Copyright 2012, American Chemical Society.

The synthesis of heterogeneous catalysts for selective oxidation of CH bonds by co-polymerization of new N-heterocyclic carbenepalladium(II) (NHCPdII) monomers with divinylbenzene has been developed by Wendt and coworkers (Fig. 3).163 The polymer-supported NHCPdII catalyzed undirected CH acetoxylation of simple and methylated arenes as well as polyarenes, with similar or superior efficiency compared to their homogeneous analogues. The authors proved that their new polymer-supported catalysts maintained the original oxidation state of PdII after repeated catalytic reactions and exhibited no significant leaching of palladium. In addition, the new catalysts were successfully recovered and reused without loss of activity over several cycles of reactions.


Fig. 3 Polymerized NHCPdII monomer can catalyze CH acetoxylation of undirected arene. Adapted with permission from ref. 163. Copyright 2017, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

Heterogeneous catalysts, as an emerging research hotspot, have shown excellent advantages of stability, recyclability, and separation from reactants compared with homogeneous catalysts in recent years.

Shi and coworkers constructed a pillar[5]-arene-based organometallic cross-linked polymer (P[5]-OCP) via the complexation between imidazolium derivative pillar[5]arene (P[5]) and Pd(OAc)2 (Fig. 4).164 The catalytic activity and process of P[5]-OCP were investigated in the SuzukiMiyaura coupling reaction. The P[5]-OCP showed outstanding catalytic activity under mild reaction conditions. Meanwhile, the catalyst P[5]-OCP possessed excellent stability and recyclability, and it was reused at least five times without significant loss of activity.


Fig. 4 P[5]-OCP derived from the complexation between P[5] and Pd(OAc)2 and cartoon representation of the cross-linked structure of the P[5]-OCP and its application in the SuzukiMiyaura coupling reaction. Adapted with permission from ref. 164. Copyright 2018, American Chemical Society.

A new type of heterogeneous transition-metal catalyst comprising a polymer-supported terpyridine palladium(II) complex was synthesized by Suzuka and coworkers, and it was found to promote the SuzukiMiyaura, MizorokiHeck, Sonogashira, and TsujiTrost reactions in water under aerobic conditions with a high to excellent yield.165 Li and coworkers employed Pd(II) organometallic catalysts immobilized on a triphenylphosphine-functionalized microporous knitting aryl polymer (KAPs(Ph-PPh3)-Pd) as a new heterogeneous catalyst in the SuzukiMiyaura coupling reaction of benzyl chloride and exhibited excellent catalytic activity under mild conditions with a turnover frequency (TOF) of up to 76 min1 (4569 h1).166 Additionally, a heterogeneous secondary amine Pd(II) complex anchored to macroporous polystyrene beads, P-[Pd2(seam)2(OAc)2] (seam = secondary amine), were prepared by Islam and coworkers and used in catalytic oxidation of organic substrates.167

3.1.2 Pd nanoparticles incorporating metallopolymers. Astruc's group designed and prepared new metallopolymers by click polycondensation between bis(ethynyl)biferrocene and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG400 and PEG1000) fragments (Fig. 5).168 These metallopolymers were used for multiple functions and uses including the improvement of water solubility and biocompatibility, the stabilization of Pd and Au nanoparticles, in catalysis, electrode derivatization, and sensing, and as a polyelectrochrome and polyelectrolyte. As the stability of the palladium complex was low, most of the catalytic activity was attributable to clusters and NPs present during most of the reaction time and not to the initial palladium complex.
Fig. 5 Multi-function redox polymers prepared by click polycondensation as an electrochrome, polyelectrolyte, sensor, electrode modifier, nanoparticle stabilizer and catalyst template. Adapted with permission from ref. 168. Copyright 2014, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

The fabrication of a Pd nanocubes@ZIF-8 composite material by encapsulation of Pd nanocubes in ZIF-8 has been reported by Jiang's group (Fig. 6).169 This composite was used for the catalytic hydrogenation of olefins at room temperature under 1 atm H2 and light irradiation, and benefits from plasmonic photothermal effects of the Pd nanocube cores while the ZIF-8 shell plays multiple roles; it accelerates the reaction by H2 enrichment, acts as a molecular sieve for olefins with specific sizes, and stabilizes the Pd cores. Remarkably, the catalytic efficiency of a reaction under 60 mW cm2 full-spectrum or 100 mW cm2 visible-light irradiation at room temperature turned out to be comparable to that of a process driven by heating at 50 C. Furthermore, the catalyst remained stable and was easily recycled.


Fig. 6 Self-assembly of Pd NCs@ZIF-8 and plasmon-driven selective catalysis of the hydrogenation of olefins. Adapted with permission from ref. 169. Copyright 2016, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

A new phosphorus-doped porous organic polymer (POP) was developed by Ding and coworkers. This polymer was used not only as a support but also as a ligand for palladium nanoparticles (Fig. 7).170 This dual-functional material-supported palladium nanocatalyst was successfully used for the efficient and chemoselective hydrogenation of varieties of nitroarenes and ,-unsaturated compounds, as well as for the synthesis of indoles from 2-nitrophenylacetonitrile under 1 atm hydrogen in green solvents at room temperature. No obvious aggregation and loss of catalytic activity of the new nanocatalyst were observed after 10 runs in the reaction.


Fig. 7 Palladium/phosphorus-doped porous organic polymer as a recyclable chemoselective and efficient hydrogenation catalyst under ambient conditions. Adapted with permission from ref. 170. Copyright 2017, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

To diversify the field of Pd nanoparticles involved in metallopolymer-catalyzed coupling reactions, Kirschning and coworkers described the example of polyionic polymers as heterogeneous media for metal nanoparticles as catalysts in the SuzukiMiyaura and HeckMizoroki reactions under flow conditions.171 Also, Karakhanov and coworkers investigated the use of nanostructured regular materials based on cross-linked polypropylene imine (PPI) dendrimers and silica-gel polyamine composites for the synthesis of Pd nanoparticles. These materials were tested as catalysts and displayed high activity and selectivity for the hydrogenation of conjugated double bonds.172 Holliday and coworkers prepared a conducting metallopolymer as a precursor to fabricate palladium nanoparticle/polymer hybrids for oxygen reduction.173 Mastrorilli and coworkers reported resin-immobilized palladium nanoparticle catalysts for organic reactions in aqueous media.174 Vaidhyanathan and coworkers reported a Pd loaded amphiphilic metallopolymer as a catalyst for multi-fold Heck reactions, CC couplings and CO oxidation.175

3.1.3 Pt-Containing metallopolymers. Pt-Containing metallopolymers are interesting materials exposing single Pt atoms for photo/electrocatalysis and thus represent a potential low-cost alternative for bulk or nanoparticulate platinum group metals (PGMs).

Nann and coworkers synthesized an electropolymerizable monomer bearing a pendant terpyridine Pt unit for the selective complexation of PGMs (Fig. 8).176 Electrocatalytic tests of the resulting metallopolymer, poly-[(tThTerpy)PtCl]Cl, revealed activity both in the oxygen reduction reaction and hydrogen evolution reaction. Their fabrication of Pt metallopolymers proved to be simple, nonhazardous and versatile.


Fig. 8 Platinum terpyridine metallopolymer electrode used as a cost-effective replacement for bulk platinum catalysts in the oxygen reduction reaction and hydrogen evolution reaction. Adapted with permission from ref. 176. Copyright 2017, American Chemical Society.
3.1.4 Au-Containing metallopolymers. Peris and coworkers synthesized two triphenylene-based tris(N-heterocyclic carbene)goldacetylide main-chain organometallic microporous polymers (MOMPs) (Fig. 9).177 The new metallopolymers were tested in the catalytic reduction of nitroarenes with NaBH4 and in the three-component Strecker reaction for the synthesis of -aminonitriles and showed high activity in both processes. Whereas the activity of the solids in the reduction of nitroarenes may be attributed to the formation of Au nanoparticles due to the use of NaBH4 as a reducing agent, the activity in the Strecker reaction may originate from the Lewis acidic activation of the ketone or imine on coordination to Au.
Fig. 9 Structures of MOMPs and their applications in the catalytic reduction of nitroarenes with NaBH4 and in the three-component Strecker reaction for the synthesis of -aminonitriles. Adapted with permission from ref. 177. Copyright 2014, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

Metallodendrimers containing redox-robust centers may have applications in molecular redox recognition, sensing, biosensing and catalysis. Understanding the relationship between the location of nanoparticles (NPs) in an organic matrix and their catalytic performances is essential for catalyst design.

Astruc and coworkers reported metallodendrimers with two electronically communicating iron centers that are stable and isolated in both the FeII and FeIII oxidation states, and in addition as class-II mixed-valent FeIIFeIII complexes (Fig. 10).178 These dendrons are branched to arene-centered dendrimer cores either by Sonogashira coupling or click reactions. The latter reaction involves the introduction of intradendritic 1,2,3-triazolyl ligands, which allows investigation of the selective role of these ligands in intradendritic AuIII coordination and Au0 nanoparticle stabilization. As a result, and using the various metallodendrimers with different oxidation states, small Au0 nanoparticles were intradendritically stabilized by the triazole ligands, whereas with the related non-click dendrimers large Au0 nanoparticles are formed outside the dendrimers and stabilized by a group of dendrimers. These dendrimer stabilized AuNPs with controlled sizes are potentially applicable in catalytic reactions.


Fig. 10 Schematic picture representing the AuNP stabilized by dendrimers. (A) Intradendritic AuNP stabilization by a click dendrimer 6B27+ involving AuNPtriazole interactions. (B) Interdendritic stabilization by many Sonogashira dendrimers 6A27+. It is likely that chloride anions surround the AuNPs in both cases. Adapted with permission from ref. 178. Copyright 2014, Right Managed by Nature Publishing Group.

Astruc's group showed that catalytic activities of Au, Ag and CuNPs stabilized by dendrimers using coordination to intradendritic triazoles, galvanic replacement or stabilization outside dendrimers strongly depend on their location (Fig. 11).179 AgNPs are found at the inner click dendrimer periphery, whereas CuNPs and AuNPs are encapsulated in click dendrimer nanosnakes. AuNPs and AgNPs formed by galvanic replacement are larger than precursors and only partly encapsulated. AuNPs are better 4-nitrophenol reduction catalysts as they are less sterically inhibited by the dendrimer interior, whereas CuNPs are better alkyne azide cycloaddition catalysts, as they are better protected from aerobic oxidation inside dendrimers. Their work highlights the role of the location in macromolecules in the catalytic efficiency of metal nanoparticles and rationalizes optimization in catalyst engineering.


Fig. 11 (A) Synthesis of AuNPs and CuNPs stabilized by dendrimers. Catalytic reactions conducted with the AuNPs and CuNPs as catalysts, like, (B) 4-nitrophenol reduction; (C) alkyne azide cycloaddition. Adapted with permission from ref. 179. Copyright 2016, Right Managed by Nature Publishing Group.

According to the efforts of Astruc's group, upon catalyst and substrate encapsulation, an amphiphilic dendrimer containing 27 triethylene glycol termini and 9 intradendritic triazole rings served as a catalytic nanoreactor by considerably accelerating the CuI-catalyzed alkyneazide cycloaddition (CuAAC) click reactions of various substrates in water using the catalyst Cu(hexabenzyltren)Br (tren = triaminoethylamine) (Fig. 12).180 Moreover this recyclable nanoreactor with intradendritic triazole rings also strongly activated the simple SharplessFokin catalyst CuSO4 + sodium ascorbate in water under ambient conditions leading to exceptional TONs of up to 510000. This fully recyclable catalytic nanoreactor allowed a considerable decrease in the amount of this cheap copper catalyst down to industrially tolerable residues.


Fig. 12 Recyclable catalytic dendrimer nanoreactor for part-per-million Cu catalysis of Click chemistry in water. Adapted with permission from ref. 180. Copyright 2014, American Chemical Society.

A highly active and selective Au@UIO-67 catalyst has been recently assembled by Xu's group.181 Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) were monodispersed on the UIO-67 surface of a porous metalorganic framework, with the micropores in UIO-67 as templates for adsorbing Au ions and enhancing the interaction between AuNPs and UIO-67, favoring the formation of isolated and well-dispersed AuNPs. The catalyst exhibited high catalytic activity and CO selectivity for the reverse watergas shift reaction in a fixed-bed flow reactor.

3.1.5 Ru-Containing metallopolymers. Direct use of low pressures of CO2 as a C1 source without concentration from gas mixtures is of great interest from an energy-saving viewpoint. Ru-Containing metallopolymers as porous heterogeneous catalysts containing both adsorption and catalytically active sites are excellent candidates for such applications.

Kitagawa and coworkers reported a porous coordination polymer (PCP)-based catalyst, a PCPRuII composite, bearing a RuIICO complex active for CO2 reduction (Fig. 13).182 The PCPRuII composite showed improved CO2 adsorption behavior at ambient temperature. In the photochemical reduction of CO2 the PCPRuII composite produced CO, HCOOH, and H2. Catalytic activity was comparable with that of the corresponding homogeneous RuII catalyst. Additionally, catalytic activity was maintained even under a 5% CO2/Ar gas mixture, revealing a synergistic effect between the adsorption and catalytically active sites within the PCPRuII composite.


Fig. 13 Photochemical reduction of low concentrations of CO2 in the porous coordination polymer with a ruthenium(II)CO Complex. Adapted with permission from ref. 182. Copyright 2016, Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, Weinheim.

Polymer-supported metal (Ru or Fe) complexes for epoxide ring opening reactions by anchoring the bis(2-picolyl)amine ligand onto the polymer poly(chloromethylstyrene-co-divinylbenzene) (PCD) were prepared by Lee et al. These catalysts showed heterogeneous catalytic activity and easy recyclability in the ring opening reactions of various epoxide substrates with methanol or H2O at room temperature under mild and neutral conditions.183

3.1.6 Rh-Containing metallopolymers in catalysis. The intramolecular cycloisomerization of 1,6-enynes (in 9599% ee) using an immobilized Rh catalyst-organic framework synthesized from alternating ring-opening metathesis polymerization (altROMP) assembly was reported by Bergens and coworkers (Fig. 14).184 The framework was reused up to seven times, and it was used in high turnover number (TON) batch reactions. The immobilized Rh catalyst-organic framework provided great TONs (up to 890) for the cycloisomerizations, with catalyst loadings ranging from 0.2 to 0.06 mol%.
Fig. 14 Highly reusable rhodium catalyst-organic framework for the intramolecular cycloisomerization of 1,6-enynes. Adapted with permission from ref. 184. Copyright 2011, American Chemical Society.

Oppelt and coworkers synthesized a 2,2-bipyridyl-containing poly(arylene-ethynylene)-alt-poly(arylene-vinylene) polymer, acting as a light harvesting ligand system and coupled it to an organometallic rhodium complex applied in photocatalytic NAD+/NADH reduction (Fig. 15).185 Photocatalytic reduction of nucleotide redox cofactors was performed under visible light irradiation (390650 nm). Their new metallopolymer was used to cover large surface areas (e.g. glass beads) and, due to this immobilization step, was easily separated from the reaction solution after photolysis. Because of its high stability, the polymer-based catalyst system was repeatedly used under different reaction conditions for (photo)chemical reduction of NAD+.


Fig. 15 Rhodium-coordinated poly(arylene-ethynylene)-alt-poly(arylenevinylene) copolymer acting as a photocatalyst for visible-light-powered NAD+/NADH reduction. Adapted with permission from ref. 185. Copyright 2014, American Chemical Society.

The design of a hydroformylation catalyst through the immobilization of air-stable Rh nanoparticles (NPs) on a magnetic support functionalized with a hyperbranched polymer that bears terminal phosphine groups was reported by Rossi and coworkers.186 The catalyst modification with the hyperbranched polymer improved the metalsupport interaction, the metal loading, and the catalytic activity. The catalyst was active for the hydroformylation of natural products, such as estragole, and was used in successive reactions with negligible metal leaching.

Hapiot and coworkers have implicated the cooperativity to explain the catalytic performances of cyclodextrin-substituted polymers in aqueous rhodium-catalyzed hydroformylation of 1-hexadecene.187 Additionally, the results of this structureactivity investigation have provided compelling evidence that positive cooperativity was operative in this catalytic system, thus encouraging the elaboration of even more effective polytopic receptors for aqueous organometallic catalysis.

3.1.7 Ag-Containing metallopolymers. The widely studied porous coordination polymers, possessing large pores to adsorb waste carbon dioxide gas and further transform it into valuable chemical products, have been attracting research interest, both industrially and academically. Their applications in CO2 sequestering and absorbing will be discussed in detail in the climate section in the review.

The active silver(I) ions impart the specific alkynophilicity to activate carboncarbon triple bonds of alkyne containing molecules via activation. Incorporating catalytic Ag metal sites into the porous frameworks represents a promising approach to construct heterogeneous catalysts that cyclize propargylic alcohols with CO2, which is highly desirable for the environmentally benign conversion of carbon dioxide to fine chemicals. Duan and coworkers reported the preparation of porous coordination polymers (PCPs) with active silver sites and efficient silversilver bond formation by carefully modifying the coordination geometries of the silver sites (Fig. 16).188 The decentralized silver(I) chains in the porous frameworks enable the efficient conversion of CO2 and derivatives of acetylene to -alkylidene cyclic carbonates in a heterogeneous manner. They also presented the successful example of tandem conversion of simple alkynes and carbon dioxide to -alkylidene cyclic carbonates.


Fig. 16 Representation of the porous silver coordination polymer for the carboxylic cyclization of a phenyl propargylic alcohol with CO2 to -alkylidene cyclic carbonates. Adapted with permission from ref. 188. Copyright 2017, American Chemical Society.

Hou, Mi and coworkers synthesized a series of solvent-induced Ag-based coordination polymers (CPs) by regulating the size, shape, and polarity of solvent molecules (EtOH, iPrOH, 1,4-dioxane) as structure-directing agents (Fig. 17).189 These materials were used as heterogeneous catalysts to explore how the metalmetal bonds served as cooperative catalysts for the tandem acylationNazarov cyclization reaction to prepare cyclopentenone[b]pyrrole frameworks. They found that the unique structure containing the AgAgAg bonds endows them with intact multinuclear reaction pathways and well-defined multinuclear platforms that executed the tandem acylationNazarov cyclization reaction through the cooperative effect of AgAgAg interactions during the catalytic process.


Fig. 17 Solvent-induced assembly of silver coordination polymers (CPs) as cooperative catalysts for synthesizing cyclopentenone[b]pyrrole frameworks. Adapted with permission from ref. 189. Copyright 2017, American Chemical Society.
3.1.8 Cu-Containing metallopolymers. Ring-opening metathesis polymerization has been used for the synthesis of the amphiphilic block-copolymer poly(M1-co-M3)-b-poly which was loaded with Cu and the resulting CuI-loaded polymer poly(M1-co-M3)-b-poly-Cu was used for a series of Cu-catalyzed reactions under micellar conditions, i.e. for the [3+2] cycloaddition of azides to alkynes and for carbonyl hydrosilylation reactions. Under such micellar conditions, the polymer-bound Cu-catalyst was found to be an efficient catalyst for all reactions investigated. Turn-over numbers (TONs) in cycloaddition reactions were in the range of 200375, and those in hydrosilylation reactions 2000 allowing for Cu-loadings of 0.05 mol% with respect to the substrate (Fig. 18).190
Fig. 18 Ring-opening metathesis polymerization-derived, poly(M1-co-M3)-b-poly-Cu for click-chemistry (A) and hydrosilylation reactions (B) under micellar conditions. Adapted with permission from ref. 190. Copyright 2009, Royal Society of Chemistry.

Wu and coworkers synthesized a new 1D macrocyclic copper(II) coordination polymer, [CuL2] (L = N-(4-pyridyl)-D,L-valine), based on a valine derived ligand.191 The catalytic experiments showed that compound [CuL2] was an efficient catalyst for the cross-coupling reaction of arylboronic acids with imidazole in the absence of additional additives. Jia, Xu and coworkers synthesized porous CuO-T (T = 400, 500, and 600 C) nanofibers by thermolysis of Cu-Asp nanofibers at different temperatures. The CuO-500 nanofibers showed an excellent photocatalytic property for the catalytic reduction of RhB in the presence of H2O2, with the degradation rate reaching 96% within 160 min under visible light irradiation.192 Additionally, the CuO-500 nanofiber photocatalyst displays good photochemical stability under visible light irradiation. These results of the active species trapping experiments indicated that the holes play a predominant role in the photodegradation process.

3.1.9 Fe-Containing metallopolymers. Xu and coworkers reported the synthesis and assessment of a new non-precious-metal catalyst from pyrolysis of an iron-coordinated complex, which manifested superior activity in both alkaline and acidic media and applied this activity in an oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) (Fig. 19).193 11,11-Bis(dipyrido[3,2-a:2,3-c]phenazinyl) (bidppz) was selected as a ligand for the formation of a nitrogen-rich iron-coordinated coordination polymer (Febidppz). The catalyst pyrolyzed at 800 C (Fe-N/C-800) showed the highest ORR activity with onset and half-wave potentials of 923 and 809 mV in 0.1 M KOH, respectively, which are comparable to those of Pt/C (half-wave potential 818 mV vs. RHE) at the same catalyst loading. The optimal Fe-N/C-800 catalyst displayed much greater durability and tolerance of methanol than Pt/C. Electrochemical measurements showed that the Fe-N/C-800 catalyst in KOH and HClO4 follows the effective four-electron-transfer pathway. Because Fe-N/C-800 possesses excellent ORR activity while its specific surface area is not high, the authors proposed that the Fe-N/C-800 catalyst has a considerably high density of surface active sites.

3.2 Metalloenzymes and biocatalysis

Numerous enzymes are useful catalysts in synthetic organic chemistry, but they did not catalyze the myriad transition-metal-mediated transformations customary in daily chemical work. For this reason, the concept of directed evolution of hybrid catalysts was proposed some time ago. A synthetic ligand/transition-metal moiety is anchored covalently or non-covalently to a host biomolecule, thereby generating a single artificial metalloenzyme, which was then optimized by molecular biological methods. Also, some metals are catalysts and cofactors in enzymes, thus creating low energy pathways to speed up many chemical and biochemical processes. Scientists, therefore, incorporate metals into polymers to design metallopolymer catalysts with hallmarks of recyclability, phase separation, reactivity and selectivity.2329

Supramolecular anchoring of transition metal complexes to a protein scaffold is an attractive approach to the construction of artificial metalloenzymes since this is conveniently achieved by self-assembly. Artificial metalloenzymes have emerged as an attractive new approach to enantioselective catalysis.

Roelfes and coworkers presented a new strategy towards the creation of an artificial metalloenzyme, which involves grafting a new active site onto the dimer interface of the protein LmrR by conjugation of a bidentate ligand capable of binding CuII ions (Fig. 32).229 The dimer interface provides the chiral microenvironment for the catalyzed reaction. The artificial metalloenzyme is capable of catalyzing a DielsAlder reaction with excellent enantioselectivities, namely up to 97% ee, and it was shown that the hydrophobic pocket of LmrR indeed functions as the active site where the reaction takes place. These results represented the first example of an artificial metalloenzyme in which the metal complex was linked covalently that gave rise to excellent enantioselectivities in catalysis.

3.3 DFT on metalloenzymes

The development of artificial enzymes aims at expanding the scope of biocatalysis. Over recent years, artificial metalloenzymes based on the insertion of homogeneous catalysts in biomolecules have received an increasing amount of attention. Rational or pseudorational design of these composites is a challenging task because of the complexity of the identification of efficient complementarities among the cofactor, the substrate, and the biological partner.

Molecular modeling represents an interesting alternative to help in this task. However, little attention has been paid to this field so far. Several groups reviewed their efforts in developing efficient strategies to computationally drive the design of artificial metalloenzymes. From proteinligand dockings to multiscale approaches, they demonstrated that modeling was useful at the different steps of the design.264267

4. Sustainable biomedical materials

Metallopolymers are promising nanomedicine platforms featuring biodegradability and versatile functionalities providing multiple methodologies and applications in healthcare science. However, multi-step post-synthesis surface modification is usually required to functionalize as-made metallopolymers before their biomedical applications. New nanomaterials and advanced nanotechnologies continue to promote the fast development of new approaches for efficient tumor therapy. Different aspects of biomedical applications are embedded in numerous excellent reviews on metal-containing polymers.3034

More promising metallopolymers showing healthcare applications have emerged since Tang's review31 on metal-containing and related polymers for biomedical applications. Here a survey of the most recent progress in the healthcare applications of metallopolymers is given. Due to the unique optical, electrochemical, and magnetic properties, at least 30 different metal elements, most of them transition metals, have been introduced into polymeric frameworks for interactions with biology-relevant substrates via various means. According to their different applications, this review summarizes the following aspects: (1) metallopolymers as anticancer drugs; (2) metallopolymers as antimicrobial and antiviral agents; (3) metallopolymers for bioimaging; (4) metallopolymers as biosensors; (5) metal-containing polymers as drug delivery vehicles; and (6) photodynamic metallopolymers.

4.1 Metallopolymers as anticancer drugs

Platinum-based anticancer drugs are the mainstay of clinical chemotherapy regimens (Fig. 45).273 Nevertheless, the efficacy of platinum drugs is badly affected by serious systemic toxicities and drug resistance, and the pharmacokinetics of most platinum drugs is largely unknown. The design of platinum-containing metallopolymers is an emerging strategy that has been explored to significantly enhance performance, specifically to address relevant questions of many conventional therapeutics and prophylactics.

These metallopolymers overcame many of the drawbacks of existing drug delivery systems by virtue of tunable compositions, sizes and shapes, high drug loadings, ease of surface modification and intrinsic biodegradability.

In all tumor xenograft models evaluated, including CT26 colon cancer, H460 lung cancer and AsPC-1 pancreatic cancer, PEGylated NCPs showed superior potency and efficacy compared with free drugs.

4.2 Metallopolymers as antimicrobial and antiviral agents

Antimicrobial resistance threatens the achievements of science and medicine, as it deactivates conventional antimicrobial therapeutics. Scientists respond to the threat by developing new antimicrobial platforms to prevent and treat infections from these resistant strains. Metal-based antimicrobial macromolecules are emerging as an alternative to conventional platforms because they combine multiple mechanisms of action into one platform due to the distinctive properties of metals. For example, metals interact with intracellular proteins and enzymes, and catalyze various intracellular processes. Recent advances demonstrate the potential of metal-based antimicrobial macromolecules as effective platforms that prevent and treat infections from resistant strains (Fig. 50).281286

Bacterial infections have become a serious threat to global healthcare due to the diminishing effectiveness of existing antibiotics. Inappropriate and frequent use of antibiotics has led to the development of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which cause infectious diseases that are difficult to treat. Wang, Mao and coworkers synthesized a series of quaternary ammonium (Qa) or imidazolium (Im) cation-based poly(ionic liquid) (PIL) membranes and their corresponding zinc ion-coordinated PIL membranes (Fig. 51).287 The Zn-containing PIL membranes showed higher antibacterial activities compared to pristine PIL membranes due to the synergistic attributes of both organic cations (Qa or Im) and zinc atoms. 4.3 Photoactive and magnetic metallopolymers for bioimaging

Metallopolymers have been extensively studied as new imaging probes in the field of biomedical imaging. For example, quantum dots have been used as optical imaging agents, whereas superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles have been used as contrast agents for magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). As hybrid materials, metallopolymers were tailored to function as contrast agents for many imaging modalities, including MRI, CT, and optical imaging (OI).301

Nanoscale metalorganic particles (NMOPs) are constructed from metal ions and organic bridging ligands via the self-assembly process. Yang et al. fabricated NMOPs composed of Mn2+ and a near-infrared (NIR) dye, IR825, obtaining Mn-IR825 NMOPs, which were then coated with a shell of polydopamine (PDA) and further functionalized with polyethylene glycol (PEG) (Fig. 54).302 Upon intravenous injection, Mn-IR825@PDAPEG showed efficient tumor homing together with rapid renal excretion behaviors, as revealed by MR imaging and confirmed by biodistribution measurement. Notably, when irradiated with an 808 nm laser, tumors on mice with Mn-IR825@PDAPEG injection are eliminated without recurrence within 60 days, demonstrating the high efficacy of photothermal therapy with this agent. Their study demonstrated the use of NMOPs as a potential photothermal agent, which features excellent tumor-targeted imaging and therapeutic functions, together with rapid clearance, the latter of which would be particularly important for future clinical translation of nanomedicine.

4.5 Metallopolymers for drug delivery

The redox-activity of metallopolymers has been increasingly harnessed to address needs in drug delivery in recent years.322325

Winnik, Reilly and coworkers reported the synthesis and characterization of a biotin (Bi) end-capped metallopolymer Bi-PAm(DET-DTPA)36 and compared its behavior in vivo and in vitro to a similar one with ethylenediamine (EDA) pendant groups (Bi-PAm(EDA-DTPA)40) (Fig. 64).326 These metallopolymers were complexed to a streptavidin-modified Fab fragment of tmAb, then labeled with 111In to specifically deliver multiple copies of 111In to HER2+ cancer cells. 4.6 Photodynamic metallopolymers

As an alternative choice to various traditional therapies, non-invasive photothermal therapy (PTT) has attracted significant attention, mainly due to its low cost, highly localizing, specific tumor treatment and minimal side effects on healthy tissues. PTT was induced by photo-absorbing agents that can absorb and convert the external NIR laser into heat to ablate tumors.

Maggioni and coworkers synthesized a polymer complex (1P) by binding bis(cyclometalated) Ir(ppy)2+ fragments (ppy = 2-phenylpyridyl) to phenanthroline (phen) pendants of a poly-(amidoamine) copolymer (Fig. 69).331 The corresponding molecular complex [Ir(ppy)2(bap)]+ (1M, bap = 4-(butyl-4-amino)-1,10-phenanthroline) was also prepared for comparison. These results indicated that cell damage induced by 1M was avoided by binding the iridium sensitizers to the poly(amidoamine).

5.2 Metallopolymers for energy storage

The presence of multiple redox-active metals in polymers endows them with nanobattery and nanowire functions, which are integral to nanoelectronics. A rational design of the polymer framework and selection of metal center allow scientists to tune the redox-activity and photophysics of metallopolymers, enabling electron or photon transfer processes to efficiently harness energy from chemical bonds, sunlight, or biochemical systems. Indeed, metal-enabled electron or photon transfer processes in polymers permit the design of energy-storage materials.357369

Multilevel organic memories have attracted considerable interest due to their high capacity of data storage. Astruc and coworkers synthesized giant redox dendrimers with ferrocenyl and pentamethylferrocenyl termini up to a theoretical number of 39 tethers (seventh generation) (Fig. 78).370 Lengthening of the tethers proved to be a reliable strategy to overcome the bulk constraint at the dendrimers periphery. Cyclic voltammetry revealed full chemical and electrochemical reversibility up to G7, showing that electron transfer is fast among the flexible peripheral redox sites. Indeed, the redox stability of these new electrochromic dendrimers, i.e., a battery behavior, was established by complete chemical oxido-reduction cycles, and the blue 17-electron ferricenium and deep-green mixed-valence Fe(III)/Fe(II) dendritic complexes were isolated and characterized.

5.4 NLO and optical limiters

The nonlinear optical properties of some metallopolymers are increasingly explored in nonlinear frequency conversion and amplification, and in signal processing. Within the scope of nonlinear optics, optical power limiting (OPL) materials are commonly regarded as an important class of compounds which protect the delicate optical sensors or human eyes from sudden exposure to damaging intense laser beams. Metallopolymers are frontline precursors in the design of optical power limiters thanks to the synergy between the electronic property of the metal center and an organic ligand in the polymer. These metalated materials offer a new avenue towards a new family of highly transparent homo- and heterometallic optical limiters with good solution processability which outperform those of current state-of-the-art visible-light-absorbing competitors.407411

According to the report of Schanze, a series of platinum(II) acetylide complexes containing p-phenylenevinylene and moieties end-capped with triphenylamine groups have been incorporated into poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) monoliths for optical power limiting applications (Fig. 90).412 The absolute two photon absorption cross-section values for the monolith samples were measured and were comparable to the values obtained in solution. In the PMMA monoliths, the complexes retained the important two-photon absorption and reverse saturable absorption properties necessary for optical power limiting via a dual mode mechanism, and their strong nonlinear absorption property was demonstrated by the open-aperture Z-scan method.

Liang and coworkers developed a facile and efficient approach for scalable synthesis of polymer flowers decorated with Prussian blue (PB) and graphene nanosheets (Fig. 102).445 This approach involved graphene-induced crystallization of polyethylene capped with a cyanoferrate complex (PE-Fe), followed by in situ coordination polymerization of the cyanoferrate complex with Fe3+ on the surface. Such graphene-induced flower-like nanoarchitectures offer a catalog of functional nanomaterials useful for biosensing and nanodevices.

6. Environment and climate change

The coordination chemistry of metals is versatile and has inspired the design of porous coordination polymers. The porosity and the ability to functionalize these pores allow the design of technologies that sequester CO2. Also, some of the functionalities of metallopolymers are responsive to many important molecules like NO2 and halogens, as well as to changes in humidity and temperature, enabling these polymers to function as sensors, molecular barometers, and molecular thermometers. We will discuss advances that push the frontiers of metallopolymers towards their use as sensors and sequesters.

6.1 Metallopolymers for sequestering CO2

The stringent targets to minimize greenhouse gases demand a wide spectrum of corresponding measures. Recently, carbon dioxide capture and utilization (CCU) technologies, whereby CO2 is captured from a specific source and utilized as a green C1 chemical feedstock to produce valuable chemicals and fuels, are seen as sustainable approaches to reduce this greenhouse gas and achieve a carbon-neutral energy cycle. So far, various MOF solid adsorbents and metallopolymers have been constructed for this purpose, and the related studies have been summarized in some outstanding reviews.4653

The incorporation of accessible nitrogen-donor sites into the pore walls of porous adsorbents dramatically affected the CO2 uptake capacity and selectivity on account of the dipolequadrupole interactions between the polarizable CO2 molecule and the accessible nitrogen site. With this strategy, Yaghi and coworkers reported that the use of two primary alkylamine functionalities covalently tethered to the linkers of IRMOF-74-III resulted in a material that took up CO2 at low pressures through a chemisorption mechanism (Fig. 104).450 In contrast to other primary amine-functionalized solid adsorbents that take up CO2 primarily as ammonium carbamates, they observed using solid state NMR that the major chemisorption product for this material was carbamic acid. The equilibrium of reaction products also shifts to ammonium carbamate when water vapor is present. Yaghi's method is a new finding that has an impact on the control of the chemistry of CO2 capture in MOF materials and one that highlights the importance of geometric constraints and the mediating role of water within the pores of MOFs.

Extreme toxicity, corrosiveness, and volatility pose serious challenges for the safe storage and transportation of elemental chlorine and bromine, which play critical roles in the chemical industry. Solid materials capable of forming stable nonvolatile compounds upon reaction with elemental halogens may partially mitigate these challenges by allowing safe halogen release on demand.

The reversible chemical storage and thermal release of elemental halogens occur with no significant losses of structural integrity, as the parent cobaltous MOF retains its crystallinity and porosity even after three oxidation/reduction cycles.

6.2 Metallopolymers as molecular barometers

Relative humidity is a crucial environmental factor in industry, food, and health care applications. In particular, humidity sensors have rapidly developed and generally used in many application fields including environmental control, industrial production, food storage, meteorology, aerospace science, etc.479,480

The simple concept and use of ultra-low-cost materials suggested the potential for applications of porous metal-film-based optical humidity sensors in many fields. The sensor developed was capable of detecting and localizing water leaks in a number of pipeline networks in metropolitan areas or in industrial facilities, very fast and precisely. These sensors are cheap, easy to fabricate, and are ideal to be used in high end environmental and health monitoring applications including food handling, storage, transport and environmental detection as an economically attractive alternative to electronic-circuit-based humidity sensors.

Stark and coworkers prepared a series of porous metal films for optical humidity sensing from copper nanoparticles protected by a 23 nm carbon coating, a silicon tenside, and a polymeric wetting agent (Fig. 118).481 Exposure to water or solvent vapor revealed an exceptional sensitivity with optical shifts in the visible light range of up to 50 nm for a change of 1% in relative humidity. At low humidity, the effect was assigned to SPR shifts while thin-film interference within a condensed water or solvent layer formed on top of the film accounted for the intense coloration at higher humidity. Fratoddi and coworkers have studied a nanostructured organometallic polymer and palladium/polymer hybrid to investigate the surface properties and sensitivity to relative humidity and hydrogen in surface acoustic wave sensors.482

6.3 Metallopolymers as molecular thermometers

To make use of the luminescent properties of metallopolymers, lanthanide coordination molecular compounds were realized for their temperature sensing back in 2004.488 In order to use this kind of lanthanide molecular compounds for temperature sensing, they need to be dispersed and stabilized in a polymeric matrix.489494 One method is to assemble metal ions with organic bridging linkers into coordination polymers (CPs).495505 The solid CP materials are very promising platforms to explore stable temperature sensors.

Because metal ions were rationally incorporated into the isostructural lanthanide MOFs/CPs, we also made use of dual luminescent intensities from two different metal ions (e.g., Tb3+ and Eu3+) to establish self-calibrated robust temperature sensors. The first few examples of mixed-lanthanide metal MOF thermometers have been realized since 2012.506511 Tuning the triplet excited state energy of the organic linker could lead to the achievement of a new mixed-lanthanide MOF thermometer with higher sensitivity.

A number of examples have been reported of ratiometric luminescence thermometers that are based on the temperature-dependent phosphorescence of either the ligand or embedded dyes.512514

Besides that, Yan and coworkers have prepared a series of photofunctional polymer hybrid thin films based on rare earth ion functionalized metal organic frameworks via a polymerization reaction of ethyl methacrylate (EMA) and 4-vinylpyridine (VPD) (Fig. 123).515 These hybrid polymer thin films are dense and transparent and display multiple colors, including blue, red and blue-green. Among them, the Y0.9Tb0.0999Eu0.0001 fabricated hybrid thin film displayed a white light output. More significantly and interestingly, the Tb0.999Eu0.001 fabricated hybrid thin film can be used as a luminescent ratiometric thermometer based on the energy transfer from Tb3+ to Eu3+, whose color will change from blue-green to pink from 100 K to 320 K.

7. Concluding remarks

The review discussed and concluded the strategies explored by materials scientists to position metallopolymers for sustainable applications in healthcare, therapeutics, photovoltaics, data storage, carbon dioxide capture, heterogeneous catalysis, etc. Connecting the merger between metals and polymers with sustainability will stimulate interest in the exploration of metals, generally considered to be toxic, as untapped resources in the pursuit of sustainability chemistry.

Metallopolymers showed wide applications as electronically unique and homogeneously or heterogeneously accessible catalysts for organic synthesis and catalysis via hiring a variety of metal types. Scopes of catalytic reactions that are covered include not only the classical Suzuki, Sonogashira and Heck couplings, but also aryl amination, epoxide opening, rearrangements, metathesis and cyclopropanation etc. In particular, the metal-containing polymers or dendrimer stabilized nanoparticles with controlled sizes are perfectly effective and recyclable in a variety of catalytic reactions. Applications of metallopolymers as metalloenzymes in the field of biocatalytic asymmetric synthesis and its DFT are also well recognized and prospected.

Metal-containing polymers also showed great potential in biomedical fields and cancer treatment due to their unique optical, electrochemical, and magnetic properties. Several groups have proved that metallopolymers were contributory in the codelivery of multiple chemotherapeutics for treating drug-resistant cancer. These functional drug carrier systems have the potential for increasing solubility and potency of drugs while reducing side effects. Recent advances in metallopolymers have also demonstrated their possibility as effective platforms that could prevent and treat infections from resistant strains. These metal-based macromolecules were proved to be antibacterially active, biologically safe, and may have application as an antimicrobial and antiviral wound dressing in a clinical setting. Metallopolymers as a photothermal agent for photothermal therapy (PTT) feature excellent tumor-targeted imaging and therapeutic functions, which would be particularly important for future clinical translation of nanomedicine. To promote their clinical therapeutic applications, development of safe and effective magnetic nanocarriers is needed. In vitro transfection efficiency of metallopolymers could be significantly enhanced under an external magnetic field and they possess great potential for cancer gene therapy and molecular imaging.

The presence of multiple redox-active metals in metallopolymers endows them with nanobattery and nanowire functions, which are integral to nanoelectronics. Indeed, metal-enabled electron or photon transfer processes in metallopolymers hold great application potential in sustainable energy storage as data storage medium. These methods offer excellent possibility of metallopolymers to be utilized in the simple and rapid fabrication of bit patterned media for magnetic data recording. Isolation of these luminescent and emissive nanomaterials draw great interest of researchers for imaging, display, electrochromic, photosensitizer, photovoltaic and sensing applications. Metallopolymers showed self-healing capability that is particularly desirable for multifunctional polymeric materials because it could make the materials more reliable, reduce repair costs, and extend their lifetime in many applications. Experimental results indicated that this class of metallopolymers could be used in the preparation of organic electro-optic devices. Metallopolymers are frontline precursors in the design of optical power limiters thanks to the synergy between the electronic property of the metal center and an organic ligand in the polymer. Optical power limiting (OPL) metal-containing nanomaterials are commonly regarded as an important class of compounds which could protect the delicate optical sensors or human eyes from sudden exposure to damaging intense laser beams.

Functionalities of metallopolymers are responsive to many important molecules like CO2, NO2 and halogens, as well as to changes in humidity and temperature, enabling these polymers to function as sensors, molecular barometers, and molecular thermometers. Incorporation of alkylamine functional groups into metallopolymers could greatly improve the CO2 adsorption characteristics. Employing this smart design, metallopolymers showed great potential for industrial CO2 capture and dehumidification with an excellent CO2 permeance and an ideal CO2 selectivity. Experimental results proved that metallopolymers have very low toxicology and very good stability under simulated physiological conditions, indicating that these materials could be potentially utilized as a luminescent thermometer for biological applications. Simply by tuning the triplet excited state energy of the organic linker could lead to the achievement of a new metallopolymer thermometer with high sensitivity. Metallopolymers are also a kind of smart material that could work as sensors or actuators in wet environments or liquids.520,521 The simple concept and use of ultra-low-cost metallopolymers suggested great potential for applications of porous metal-film-based optical humidity sensors and barometers in many fields.
