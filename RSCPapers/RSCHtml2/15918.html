Abstract

In the context of global carbon capping and carbon neutrality, electrochemical methods for converting CO2 to CO are among the most promising and valuable methods for harvesting greenhouse gas pollutants and producing renewable energy. On this basis, nickel-doped layered boehmite (Niâ€“AlO(OH)3) was grown in situ on reduced graphene oxide (RGO) as a carrier, forming composites with many active sites and good electrical conductivity. The crystal structure, morphology, specific surface area and composition of the samples were characterized. The electrocatalytic carbon dioxide reduction (ECR) performance of these catalysts was evaluated. The results show that the Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO catalyst has high product selectivity, with only carbon monoxide and hydrogen as the gas phase products and no liquid phase products, and the carbon monoxide faradaic efficiency (CO FE) can reach 92.20%, while the CO FE of AlO(OH)@RGO and Ni(OH)2@RGO can reach 44.9% and 88.2%, respectively. It was shown that composite catalysts formed by depositing Niâ€“AlO(OH)3 hydroxides on reduced graphene oxide had a higher CO2RR performance, which was related to the synergistic effect of bimetallic hydroxides and reduced graphene oxide.

1. Introduction

In recent years, large-scale thermal power generation has led to a rapid increase in global carbon dioxide emissions, and the increase in greenhouse gas emissions has accelerated the global warming trend.1,2 Therefore, it is urgent to convert and apply CO2 and other greenhouse gases to recycle waste into resources.3 Electrocatalytic carbon dioxide reduction (ECR) to produce CO has attracted a lot of attention from researchers.4,5 In response to the environmental and energy problems caused by CO2 emissions, several measures have been taken by various countries. For example, the development and utilization of carbon dioxide capture and transport technologies can mitigate the global warming problem to a certain extent, but this does not solve the problem at its root. Therefore, CO2 conversion and reduction technologies have attracted a lot of attention from the scientific and industrial communities. Compared with other CO2 conversion technologies, CO2 electrocatalytic reduction is driven by intermittent renewable energy sources such as wind and solar energy, and can be performed at ambient temperature and pressure. Compared to photocatalytic CO2 reduction, electrocatalytic CO2 reduction has higher energy conversion efficiency and the selectivity of the product can be changed by changing the external conditions. In addition to temperature and pressure, the type of catalyst, structure and reaction potential can be changed. Therefore, electrocatalytic reduction of CO2 is a potential pathway for future CO conversion.6â€“8

In the ECR process, different catalysts promote the formation of different products. For 2-electron products, mostly CO and HCOOH, the metal catalysts for reduction to CO are mainly Ag, Au, Fe, Co and Ni. These catalysts use defect engineering, morphological changes, single atom doping, alloying, etc.9â€“13 to improve the activity of catalysts. A large number of scholars have shown that one of the main reasons for high reaction energy barriers is the excessive adsorption of CO on the catalyst surface. It has been suggested that the combination of transition metal elements with main group elements such as C and N can reduce the adsorption of CO on the catalyst surface.14â€“16 The Mâ€“Nâ€“C groups formed by the transition metal M (Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni) and the nitrogen (N) co-doped carbonyl C are considered catalytically active sites.13,17,18 Changing the electronic environment of the transition metal-center M by creating defects and introducing heteroatoms can improve its intrinsic catalytic activity and selectivity for CO2 reduction.19,20 However, it requires harsh preparation conditions and complex characterization methods to control the synthesis of the target active site, which suggests that such catalysts are difficult to prepare and apply on a large scale. Therefore, the search for a non-precious metal ECR catalyst with high activity, high selectivity, low cost and a simple preparation process is the key to achieving CO2 reduction and CO resource utilization.21,22Table 1 summarizes the properties of graphene-based non-precious metal composite catalysts studied in recent years.

For electrocatalytic applications, the supports are mainly carbon nanomaterials (CNMs), including carbon nanotubes (CNTs), graphene, etc.33 CNMs have high specific surface area and electrical conductivity, enabling high active site loading and fast electron transport, and a series of rapidly developing graphene production technologies have been developed, allowing graphene to be fabricated in large quantities and commercial applications. Graphene has a two-dimensional structure, is stable, and exhibits significant electron mobility, making it an ideal substrate for bimetallic hydroxides (LDHs). In addition, the low activity of CNMs towards HERs facilitates the suppression of competing reactions.34â€“37 The results show that Ru, Ni, Co and other metals have good catalytic activity for the reduction of CO2 to CO. However, the catalytic activity for carbon monoxide conversion exhibited by pure transition metal catalysts often does not meet the requirements of practical applications. Therefore, in order to obtain catalysts with excellent performance, catalysts must be designed in a targeted manner and suitable carriers and preparation methods must be selected.

Compared to precious metals, transition metal nickel is second only to ruthenium in catalytic activity and has a low cost. Due to its abundant surface acidity, certain thermal stability and flexible control properties, LDH has been widely used directly as a solid catalyst. Boehmite is a compound consisting of a positively charged main laminate and an interlayer anion assembled by non-covalent interactions. Its structure is similar to that of hydrotalcite Mg(OH)2, formed by the common edges of MgO6 octahedra. The unit layer structure has the following outstanding features. The chemical composition of the main layer can be adjusted; the type and number of interlayer guest anions can be adjusted; the particle size and distribution of the interlayer components can be adjusted. Based on the LDH precursor, the dispersion of nickel is improved, the chemical environment of the surface elements is adjusted, and the preparation of an efficient catalyst for the reduction of carbon dioxide to carbon monoxideis achieved through the LDHâ€“support interaction.

In this paper, a composite catalytic material with multiple active sites was grown and assembled in situ on a monolayer of reduced graphene oxide using a one-step hydrothermal synthesis method. This composite catalytic material has good electrical conductivity and selectivity, and low preparation cost. It provides a new strategy for the conversion and application of by-product CO2 at the Ningxia Energy chemical base, and also provides theoretical and technological support for the large-scale commercialization of electrocatalytic CO2 reduction.

2.1 Materials preparation

2.1.1 Preparation of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO, Ni(OH)2@RGO, AlO(OH)@RGO and Niâ€“AlO(OH)3. Reduced graphene oxideâ€“nickel-doped hydrated alumina composites (Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO) were synthesized by a simple one-step hydrothermal method. First, 24 mL of CH3NO was measured and mixed in a beaker containing 56 mL of deionized water and stirred well on a magnetic stirrer. Next, 2.4 g of CH4N2O was weighed and dissolved in the above mixed solution, and 80 mg of GO was weighed and dispersed in the above mixed solution and ultrasonicated for 30 minutes; 0.870 g of Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O and 0.375 g of Al(NO3)3Â·9H2O were weighed and added into the mixed solution and stirred well. Third, the pH of the above mixed solution was adjusted to 10 by dropping in 1 M NaOH solution and stirring was continued for 30 min, and the above experimental procedure was carried out at room temperature. Then it was transferred to a stainless steel autoclave for 8 hours at 180 Â°C, cooled to room temperature, and the material was washed several times with deionized water and ethanol. Finally, the obtained solid was dried in an electric blast dryer at 60 Â°C to obtain Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO.

The preparation of Ni(OH)2@RGO was the same as the experimental procedure of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO described above, except that Al(NO3)3Â·9H2O was not added. No Ni(NO3)2Â·6H2O was added during the preparation of AlO(OH)@RGO. No GO was added during the preparation of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3.

2.1.2 Preparation of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO, Ni(OH)2@RGO, and AlO(OH)@RGO electrodes. 5 mg of the prepared catalyst and 2 mg of carbon black were weighed and added to a test tube. A mixture solution including ethanol and Nafion was prepared at a volume ratio of 100:1. 0.5 ml of the mixed solution of ethanol and Nafion was taken in the test tube and sonicated for 30 min to form black ink. A pipette was used to take 40 Î¼l of black ink and it was spread evenly on a 1 cm2 carbon electrode, and dried in an electric blast drying oven at 60 Â°C for 6 h before use.
2.1.3 Electrochemical performance test. In this experiment, a Shanghai Chenhua CHI660E electrochemical workstation was used to study the electrocatalytic performance of composite materials for the electrocatalytic reduction of carbon dioxide with a three-electrode system. The reference electrode was an Ag/AgCl electrode, the counter electrode was a platinum mesh electrode, and the catalyst was coated on carbon paper as the working electrode. The electrochemical properties of all catalysts were tested in an H-type double-chamber electrolytic cell. The electrocatalytic CO2 reduction reaction was carried out in a cathodic reaction cell with 20 ml of 0.1 M aqueous potassium bicarbonate (KHCO3) solution for both the anode and cathodic reaction cells, and the pH of the electrolyte solution was adjusted to 6.8. A gas mass flow meter controlled the CO2 flow rate to 20 ml minâˆ’1, and the gas outlet of the cathodic reaction cell was connected to a gas chromatograph. The whole reaction was carried out at room temperature and pressure.

3.2 Electrocatalytic activity and stability experiment

The current density reflects the conductivity of the catalyst and the rate of the ECR reaction. Firstly, the electrochemical properties of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO, Ni(OH)2@RGO and AlO(OH)@RGO were evaluated by linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) for CO2 reduction, and a preliminary relationship between the catalytic current of the samples and voltage can be obtained. Fig. 7(a) shows the total current density measured at different potentials, Fig. 7(b) shows the Faraday efficiency curve for carbon monoxide, and Fig. 7(c) shows the current density for the electrocatalytic reduction of CO2 (jCO). The total current density and Faraday efficiency measured at 30 min were used for the discussion. Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO maintains the maximum total current density over the entire test voltage range, and Ni(OH)2@RGO has a slightly smaller total current density than Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO. Both Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO and Ni(OH)2@RGO have significantly higher total current densities compared to AlO(OH)@RGO. The value of the Tafel slope can indicate the difficulty of the electron transfer step in different catalyst systems. On the other hand, the Tafel curve is also a basis for intuitively judging the activity of the electrocatalyst. For ECR, generally, the smaller the Tafel slope, and the closer the catalyst to the lower right corner, the better its activity, and the lower the overpotential at the same current. Fig. 7(d) shows the corresponding Tafel curve. The Tafel slopes of AlO(OH)@RGO, Ni(OH)2@RGO and Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO are 160.4 mV decâˆ’1, 160.0 mV decâˆ’1, and 156.3 mV decâˆ’1, respectively. In contrast, Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO has the smallest Tafel slope, indicating that Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO has a large current gain when the applied potential is low, which is beneficial for promoting the progress of the catalytic reaction,53,54 further confirming its superior catalytic activity. These Tafel slope values indicate that the initial adsorption process (* + CO2 + H+ + eâˆ’ â†’ *COOH) is the rate-determining step of the entire ECR process. Generally, a lower Tafel slope indicates higher kinetic activity, which would be due to the higher electron-donating capability of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO. All of these results reveal that Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO exhibits higher catalytic activity than Ni(OH)2@RGO and AlO(OH)@RGO, and such excellent activity outperforms most of the reported molecular catalysts.

To further explore the ECR catalytic mechanism of the catalyst materials and determine the reactive surface area, cyclic voltammetry curves were analyzed for AlO(OH)@RGO, Ni(OH)2@RGO and Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO and the results are shown in Fig. 7(e). The voltage window is set to âˆ’0.6 to âˆ’0.7 V vs. RHE, the scanning speed is set to 1â€“10 mV sâˆ’1, the current density is set to âˆ’0.65 V, and the electric double layer of the material is obtained by fitting the slope capacitance (Cdl), and then comparing the electrochemically active areas.10,55,56 The results show that the Cdl of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO has the maximum value, which means the largest electrochemically active surface area (ECSA). The larger ECSA combined with the porous structure can further explain the high ECR performance of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO.

Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO showed a certain degree of stability in a 3 hour test. In practical applications, higher demands are placed on its long-term stability. The stability of Niâ€“AiO(OH)3@RGO was tested for 3 h at the corresponding potential with the highest carbon monoxide Faraday efficiency. Fig. 7(f) shows the results of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO in the 3 h stability test, in which the CO Faraday efficiency decays from 92.2% to 65% for Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO in electrocatalysis, and the total current density also decreases with time. The decay of CO FE during the reaction is explained here. Since the catalyst is coated on the carbon paper electrode with a binder, on the one hand, there is no more reliable interface between the catalyst and the carbon paper; on the other hand, with the progress of the reaction, a large number of bubbles will be generated on the electrode surface, so that the catalyst coated on the carbon paper electrode is gradually carried away by the bubbles and displaced. The decrease of the effective catalyst led to the severe hydrogenation of electrolytic water, the increase of H2 FE and the decrease of CO FE.

4. Conclusion

A composite catalyst with RGO as the carrier and nickelâ€“aluminum double metal hydroxide as the support was synthesized by a one-step hydrothermal method, so that the two lamella structures are interspersed to form a certain pore channel, which is conducive to the exposure of active sites. This is conducive to the adsorption and desorption of reactants and products.

AlO(OH)@RGO and Ni(OH)2@RGO at the optimum applied voltage have CO FE values of 44.9% (âˆ’0.8 V vs. RHE) and 88.2% (âˆ’0.9 V vs. RHE) respectively, and the CO FE of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO is 92.2%. Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO has the highest CO FE and the best electrocatalytic activity, indicating that the combination of double metal hydroxide and nitrogen-doped RGO greatly improves the efficiency of electrocatalytic reduction of CO2 to CO.

The excellent performance of Niâ€“AlO(OH)3@RGO comes from the abundant edge sites and defects, which promote the chemical adsorption of reactants and key intermediates. A high dispersion with a large number of pore networks is conducive to the transmission and diffusion of CO2 and electrolytes and can increase the reaction speed of ECR.
