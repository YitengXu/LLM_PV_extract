Abstract

Counterfeiting of valuable documents, currency and branded products is a challenging problem that has serious economic, security and health ramifications for governments, businesses and consumers all over the world. It is estimated that counterfeiting represents a multi-billion dollar underground economy with counterfeit products being produced on a large scale every year. Counterfeiting is an increasingly high-tech crime and calls for high-tech solutions to prevent and deter the acts of counterfeiting. The present review briefly outlines and addresses the key challenges in this area, including the above mentioned concerns for anti-counterfeiting applications. This article describes a unique combination of all possible kinds of security ink formulations based on lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials, quantum dots (semiconductor and carbon based), metal organic frameworks as well as plasmonic nanomaterials for their possible use in anti-counterfeiting applications. Moreover, in this review, we have briefly discussed and described the historical background of luminescent nanomaterials, basic concepts and detailed synthesis methods along with their characterization. Furthermore, we have also discussed the methods adopted for the fabrication and design of luminescent security inks, various security printing techniques and their anti-counterfeiting applications.

1. Introduction

Luminescent materials have found a wide range of applications in all walks of daily life, including advanced optical displays, solid state lighting, X-ray intensification, scintillation and many more.1 Therefore, enormous efforts have been devoted to explore novel luminescent materials so far, which is the need of the hour. These materials are generally characterized by the emission of light in the visible range, but can exist in other spectral regions also (such as ultraviolet or infrared) with energy beyond the thermal equilibrium.13 The ever-growing demand for newer luminescent materials has motivated scientific and technological efforts to improve the existing features of luminescent materials and to provide encouragement for the development of new effective luminescent nanomaterials having the desired shape, size, morphology, optical properties, etc.4 These nanomaterials have a wide range of applications such as photovoltaic, bio-medical, anti-counterfeiting, solid state lighting, display technologies, etc. One of their extremely important uses is the development of security inks for anti-counterfeiting applications. These nanomaterials can easily be applied inexpensively to different surfaces and have been implemented widely as security inks to protect high-value merchandise, documents, pharmaceuticals and currency.5 Counterfeiting is an ever growing global problem that challenges companies, governments and customers.6 Anti-counterfeiting techniques that make genuine items harder to copy and easier to authenticate are therefore important for the protection of brands and valuable documents.7 Luminescent tags are the most popular security elements for protecting authentic articles.7 One way to incorporate luminescent nanomaterials as luminescent security elements is to print them with luminescent inks.8 Nowadays, these printed luminescent security elements usually comprise only solid or halftone single ink color patterns.9 Typical examples are bank notes that show luminescent parts under UV light.9 Procedures for producing full color images by printing luminescent inks exist.10 These methods use several luminescent inks with different luminescent colors to form a full color image by luminescence.7 However, they are either colorimetrically inaccurate or use complex halftoning algorithms.7 Many luminescent inks and markers used, for example as security inks, are made of lanthanide compounds.11 The distinctive character of the emission from lanthanide ions and particularly, their unique spectral fingerprints, enable the use of emissions and colors from lanthanide blends as security labels, markers or tags.7

Counterfeiting in the fields of medicine and edible items can severely endanger human health. During the last decade, millions of dollars have been spent worldwide by governments and industries to safeguard their products and currencies from being counterfeited.12 The efforts have yielded positive results but still there is further demand to develop improved anti-counterfeiting nanomaterials and to build a suitable strategy for the fabrication and design of security inks to deter counterfeiting.

Here, we present a review which is deeply focused on the synthesis of strategic luminescent nanomaterials, their morphology/microstructural characterization, fabrication of security inks and their application against counterfeiting. During the past few decades, a number of anti-counterfeiting technologies such as simple markers, plasmonic security labels, holograms, and security inks have been developed as a shield against counterfeiting.1318 Merely a few review articles have been reported where development of anti-counterfeiting technologies is discussed collectively using lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials, quantum dots (semiconductor and carbon based), luminescent metal organic frameworks as well as plasmonic nanomaterial based security inks on a single platform. The present review article systematically describes in detail the historical background of luminescent materials, basic concepts, luminescent mechanism, synthesis methods with their characterization, fabrication of luminescent inks, security printing techniques and anti-counterfeiting applications using various types of luminescent nanomaterials: lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials (downconversion/downshift, upconversion and dual mode), quantum dots (semiconductor and carbon dots), luminescent metal organic frameworks and plasmonic nanomaterials.

1.1 Historical background and basic concepts of luminescent materials

Materials that glow in the dark after exposure to light have been known as phosphors ever since the early 17th century. Phosphor is a Greek word which means light bearer. There are many ancient reports related to minerals that glow in the dark. The most distinguished among them was Bolognian phosphor (impure barium sulfate, BaSO4) discovered by a cobbler Vincenzo Cascariolo in 1602.19 Before the discovery of Bolognian stone, it is believed that the Japanese had reportedly prepared phosphorescent paint from seashells. This fact is documented in a 10th century Chinese document (Song dynasty) which credits the Japanese for preparing phosphors for the first time.20 Similarly, blue colour emission was also observed from infusion of a wood by Nicolas Monardes (a Spanish physician and botanist) in 1565 and was used to treat kidney and urinary diseases (Fig. 1a).19,2126 Later on this wood was called lignum nephriticum. The extracts from this wood were further investigated by Boyle, Newton and others but the phenomenon was not understood at that time.19,27
Fig. 1 (a) Absorption and fluorescence colours of infusions of lignum nephriticum in daylight; (b) twinned crystals of green fluorite (from Rogerley, Weardale, Durham County, England) illuminated with sunlight (left) and a UV lamp (right).19 Reproduced with permission from ref. 19, Copyright 2014, American Chemical Society.

Later, Edward D. Clarke, Professor of Mineralogy at the University of Cambridge reported an abnormal property of fluorite crystals in 1819. The crystals were highly transparent in nature and had dichroic behaviour. In these crystals, the reflected light was deep sapphire blue, whereas the transmitted light was bright green as shown in Fig. 1b. But Clarke was unable to explain that phenomenon. Later on a French mineralogist Rene Just Hauy also discussed the double colour of some fluorite crystals in 1822.

He explained the phenomenon as a type of opalescence (which is observed with opal, a naturally occurring hydrated silica glass, and results from light scattering): the two colors were complementary, violet being the dominant shade of the scattered light and green being the dominant shade of the transmitted (i.e., unscattered) light.

Although the explanation was incorrect19 and the correct one was still a long way off Hauy's view, two minerals fluorite and opal were going to play a key role in understanding and naming the fluorescence. After that, a famous paper titled, on refrangibility of light published by Sir G. G. Stokes, a physicist and professor of mathematics at Cambridge in 1852 proved to be a ground breaking event in the history of photoluminescence.28

Later on in 1888, a German physicist, Eilhard Wiedemann was the first to introduce the word Luminescence for both the fluorescence and phosphorescence phenomena. The term luminescence was taken from a Latin word lumen, which means light. Nowadays, luminescence is defined as the phenomenon of spontaneous emission of radiation from an electronically excited species (or from a vibrationally excited species) not in thermal equilibrium with its environment.19,29 Fluorescence and phosphorescence can be distinguished on the basis of the duration of emission after the end of excitation: fluorescence is considered as emission of light that disappears simultaneously after the end of excitation whereas in phosphorescence the emitted light persists after the end of excitation.19 But such a distinction between fluorescence and phosphorescence is not always appropriate because there is long-lived fluorescence (e.g., divalent europium salts) and short-lived phosphorescence (e.g., violet luminescence of zinc sulfide) whose durations are comparable (several hundreds of nanoseconds).19 In 1929 Francis Perrin defined phosphorescence as the phenomenon where the excited species passes through an intermediate state before emission.30 More precisely, fluorescence and phosphorescence can be defined in terms of the change in spin multiplicity. The spin multiplicity remains the same in the case of fluorescence whereas it changes from triplet to singlet or vice versa in the case of phosphorescence.19 The schematic diagram (Jablonski diagram) for fluorescence and phosphorescence is shown in Fig. 2.

1.2 Lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials

Recently, lanthanide based luminescent nanostructures have been recognized worldwide for their good chemical and optical properties originating from their unique electronic structures as well as due to their wide range of applications in photovoltaics, bio-medical, anti-counterfeiting, solid state lighting, display technologies, etc.3159 Lanthanide based luminescent nanomaterials are also known as nanophosphors. Generally, nanophosphors have one or more trivalent lanthanide ions doped in the host lattice. There are 15 rare-earth elements including scandium and yttrium which can be used as trivalent ions in lanthanide based luminescent nanomaterials. The lanthanide based luminescent materials show sharp emission due to their intra-4f or 4f5d electronic transitions. The lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials have many advantages over other luminescent materials (organic dyes, quantum dots, plasmonic materials, etc.) like sharp emission, broad excitation spectra, good thermal stability, low toxicity, a longer lifetime, better chemical stability, high photochemical stability, etc.34 The lanthanide doped luminescent materials can exhibit emission from the ultraviolet (UV) to the near infrared light region depending upon excitation. The lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials are further divided into two types depending upon their luminescence emission mechanism as described below:
1.2.1 Downconversion/downshift luminescence. In this luminescence process, a high energy photon is converted into a low energy photon. This energy difference between the emitted and absorbed photons is known as the Stokes shift. A schematic diagram of the downconversion luminescence process is shown in Fig. 3.
Fig. 3 Schematic diagram for the downconversion luminescence process.

Downconversion luminescent materials generally consist of a host lattice doped with trivalent lanthanide ions. There are several types of lanthanide based host lattices such as oxides, fluoride, vanadate, oxy-sulfide, phosphate etc.34 The lanthanide trivalent ions doped in a rare-earth host lattice act as an activator. The role of the host lattice is to absorb the energy (photons) and transfer it to the activator. Mostly in downconversion luminescent materials, the host lattice also acts as a sensitizer. For example in the Y2O3 host lattice O2 acts as the sensitizer while in the case of GdVO4, VO43 acts as the sensitizer. The emission efficiency in both the cases is high due to efficient energy transfer from the host to the activator. The doping concentration of lanthanide ions is optimized so that the crystal structure of the host lattice with or without doping remains the same. In our earlier report, we have established that the optimum doping concentration of Eu3+ in the Y2O3 host lattice is 5 mol%.15 Besides the dopant concentration, the PL intensity is also influenced by some other factors, such as the host lattice, particle size, calcination temperature, environment, and synthesis conditions.15,34,6069 The efficiency of downconversion can also be enhanced by doping of another lanthanide ion which acts as the sensitizer. The sensitizer sufficiently transfers energy from the host lattice to the activator, resulting in enhancement of the luminescence efficiency. However, there are limited examples available in the literature of co-doping in the case of downconversion. The Ce3+ ion is the most commonly used for co-doping in order to increase the luminescence efficiency of Ln3+ (Ln = Eu, Tb, Dy, Sm) ions. There are many reports on downconversion luminescent nanomaterials reported in the literature, where Ce3+ acts as the sensitizer such as NaGdF4:Ce,Ln, BaYF5:Ce,Tb, YPO4:Ce,Tb, GdF3:Ce,Ln and Sr3Y2(BO3)4:Ce,Dy.6974 In all these luminescent materials, it is interesting to note that the excitation spectra are dominated by 4f5d absorption of Ce3+ ions, whereas the emission spectra are characteristic of Ln3+ ions, indicating the energy transfer from Ce3+ to Ln3+ ions.34

1.2.2 Upconversion luminescence. In upconversion (UC) luminescence, a low energy photon is converted into a high energy photon by a non-linear process. Conceptually, in the upconversion process, two or more photons are absorbed and converted into higher energy photons (generally NIR, visible and UV). In general, upconversion utilizes sequential absorption of multiple photons through the use of a long lifetime and real ladder-like energy levels of trivalent lanthanide ions embedded in an appropriate inorganic host lattice to produce higher energy photons. This energy difference between the emitted and absorbed photons is known as the anti-Stokes shift.38 The upconverting phosphors generally consist of an inorganic host lattice doped with emitters Er3+, Tm3+, Ho3+ and Yb3+ (trivalent lanthanide ions) which produce emission when they are in excited states. The emission wavelength can be tuned by appropriate selection of trivalent lanthanide ions. There are three main mechanisms for the upconversion process: excited state absorption, energy transfer upconversion and photon avalanche.45 The schematic diagrams for the upconversion process are shown in Fig. 4ag. A schematic diagram of the excited state absorption upconversion mechanism for single lanthanide ion doped luminescent materials is shown in Fig. 4a. The excited state absorption mechanism is a successive absorption of two photons as shown in Fig. 4a. When the lanthanide ion is excited with suitable energy photons from the ground state (E1) to a higher energy level (E2), another photon promotes the excited ion to a higher energy state (E3). The upconversion occurs when photons drop from the excited state (E3) to the ground state (E1). The energy-transfer upconversion process is the most efficient process. The five different types of energy-transfer upconversion processes are shown in Fig. 4bf. In excited state absorption (Fig. 4b), the sensitizer ions absorbed the photons and transit from the ground state (E1) to the excited state (E2). The energy transfer occurs from the sensitizer ion to the activator ion, which is in the excited state (E2). The energy transfer promotes activator ions from the lower energy state (E2) to the higher energy state (E3). In successive energy transfer (Fig. 4c), only the sensitizer ion absorbs photons and promotes the activator ion to energy levels (E2) and (E3) by two successive energy transfers. The sensitizer and activator ions both are of similar nature in cross-relaxation upconversion (Fig. 4d). The activator and sensitizer ions absorb photons in their excited state (E2). The energy transfer process then promotes the activator ion to its excited state E3 while the sensitizer reverts to the ground state.45Fig. 4e and f show that the energy transfer upconversion involves the cooperative effect. In these cases, luminescence nanomaterials having more than one ion take part in the sensitization or luminescence process. In the case of cooperative sensitization (Fig. 4e), two sensitizer ions (ion 1 and ion 2) absorb photons and then together transfer this energy to the activator ion (ion 3) which promotes ion 3 to an excited state (E3). However, in the case of cooperative luminescence, two excited ions (ion 1 and ion 2) absorb photons and cooperate in producing emission (Fig. 4f). The photon avalanche is shown in Fig. 4g. This process requires a minimum excitation power called the threshold value. Above the threshold value, the luminescence intensity increases by an order of magnitude. Basically, the photon avalanche is a looping process that is associated with processes of excited state absorption (ESA) for excitation light and efficient cross relaxation (CR) that produce feedback as shown in Fig. 4g. Initially non-resonant weak ground state absorption populates the level E1 of ion 2 and the looping process starts with the ESA process to elevate ion 2 at the level E1 to the emitting level E2. An efficient CR process of E2 (ion 2) + G (ion 1) E1 (ion 2) + E1 (ion 1) between ion 1 and ion 2 is then accomplished. Finally, ion 1 transfers its energy to ion 2 to populate its level E1, creating a complete loop. The resulting effect of the looping process is that a single ion 2 in the metastable E1 state produces two ion 2s in this state. When the looping process follows, two ion 2s in the E1 state will produce four ions and a further four will produce eight ions, generating an avalanche effect for populating ion 2 in its E1 state, and thus the photo avalanche (PA) UC from the emitting level of E2. It is easy to recognize PA because it generally requires a pump threshold and a long time (in seconds) to build up. Furthermore, the dependence of the UC photoluminescence on the pump power becomes extremely strong around the threshold pumping power.45

1.2.3 Quantum cutter luminescence. Quantum cutting is a luminescence phenomenon where one absorbed photon is converted into two or more photons. This phenomenon is also known as quantum splitting or photon cascade emission. Moreover, as the energy of a vacuum ultraviolet (VUV) photon is more than twice that of a visible photon, it is theoretically possible to achieve two visible photon emissions for every incident photon.75 The quantum cutting materials have a quantum efficiency of more than 100% that provides new directions for the development of solid state lighting, advanced display systems, strong security applications, etc.76 The quantum cutting phenomenon was theoretically predicted by Dexter.77 There are three possible mechanisms for the quantum cutting process: (a) quantum cutting using host lattice states, (b) quantum cutting on single ions and (c) quantum cutting on ion pairs.

The process (a) involves the absorption of photons with energy at least twice the band gap of the host materials. Briefly, this excited electronhole pair generates two electronhole pairs, each with energy approximately equal to the band-gap energy, via the Auger interaction.78 Process (b) requires the well-separated energy levels of ions or atoms that can be used to generate more than one visible photon out of one UV photon. In this mechanism, two conditions that must be fulfilled are: (i) the energy gap between the adjacent levels must be large enough to prevent multi-phonon relaxation and (ii) the branching ratio of visible emission must be high.75 There are a number of reports available which described in detail the quantum cutting process using single ions. Sommerdijk et al. first observed the quantum cutting effect in Pr3+-doped YF3.79,80 The quantum cutting phenomenon was also observed in many oxide host lattices using the Pr3+ ion as the dopant.8183 The Pr3+ ion doped fluorides and several oxides have been widely explored for quantum cutting with single ion doping due to the energy position of the 4f5d states with respect to the 1S0 state and the suitably large 4f5d energy separation.75 The energy level scheme of Pr3+ favouring quantum cutting (QC) by cascade emission is shown in Fig. 5. However, the quantum cutting phenomenon in other RE ions such as Tm3+, Gd3+ and Er3+ ions is also reported in the literature.8488

1.3 Luminescent quantum dots

Quantum dots are defined as spherical nanoparticles having physical dimensions smaller than the exciton Bohr radius confining its excitons in all three spatial directions.9092 Quantum dots have gained tremendous attention in research due to a number of distinguishing properties such as (i) high quantum yield (QY) and a long fluorescence lifetime,9395 (ii) simultaneous excitation of multiple QDs by using a single light source,96 (iii) narrow, symmetrical97 and (iv) broad spectral windows spanning from the ultraviolet to infrared region.98 An electronhole pair is generated when the quantum dots are photo excited with a particular wavelength and their recombination causes the emission of light. Particularly, an exciton is an electrostatically bound electronhole pair and is generated in the semiconductor as a result of excitation of an electron from the valence band to conduction band upon the absorption of a photon with energy equal to or greater than the band gap. Conceptually, the exciton Bohr radius represents the average distance between the photogenerated electrons and holes. When the size of the particle approaches that of the exciton (for example, the exciton Bohr radius for CdSe is about 6 nm), the optical and electrical properties of the material become dependent on its physical dimensions, due to quantum confinement effects.99 Under these conditions, the band structure of the semiconductor changes into discrete energy levels as shown in Fig. 7 and the energy difference between the highest occupied level and the lowest unoccupied level widens as the particle size decreases. Furthermore, the quantum confinement is also described by a simple model particles in box approximation in which the electron motion is restricted in all three dimensions by impenetrable walls. Klimov suggested a model for semiconductor based spherical QD with radius R, which predicts that a size dependent contribution to the energy gap is simply proportional to 1/R2, implying that the gap increases as the QD size decreases. Moreover, because of the quantum confinement the continuous energy bands of a bulk material split into discrete, atom like energy levels that result in the discrete absorption spectrum of QDs. This discrete absorption spectrum of QDs is entirely different from the continuous absorption spectrum of a bulk semiconductor as shown in Fig. 8.100 In other words, the smaller the particle size the greater is the blue shift in their luminescence as shown in Fig. 9.

Recently, another kind of quantum dot derived from carbon which is known as carbon quantum dots (CQD) or carbon dots (CD), including the popular graphene quantum dots (GQDs), has been investigated immensely.92,102105 Unlike the semiconductor quantum dots with band gap absorptions, carbon quantum dots have broad optical absorption due to a -plasmon that covers a broad UV/vis spectral range, extending into the near-IR. The photoluminescence emissions of these quantum dots are associated with defects related to the structure and surface or edge sites.92,106 The carbon quantum dots were first reported by Xu et al. in 2004.107 However, Sun et al. coined the term carbon quantum dots for fluorescent carbon nanoparticles in 2006.108

The fluorescence emission mechanism of GQDs/CQDs can be explained in two ways: (i) fluorescence emission due to bandgap transitions caused by conjugated -domains and (ii) fluorescence emission origins associated with surface defects in GQDs. In the first mechanism, the bandgap transitions originate from conjugated -domains. These -domains are sequestered by generating sp2 hybridised islands rich in -electrons through the reduction of graphene oxides.110,111 GQDs are synthesized in a way that there are no -connections between the sp2 islands in the graphitic lattice, because any -connections between the sp2 islands would lead to interisland quenching of the desired fluorescence emissions.112,113

For such kinds of bandgap transitions, single-layer graphene sheets have to be used to prevent interlayer quenching between the two layers.114 The single-layer graphene sheets are used as precursors for electronically slicing them into isolated -conjugated domains, which is similar to large aromatic molecules with extended -conjugation of the specific electronic energy bandgap for optical absorption and fluorescence emissions in GQDs.115 Such electronic transitions show strong absorption in the ultraviolet (UV) region, but weak or no fluorescence emissions as shown in Fig. 11a. Hence, strong absorption is possible due to light absorption by a large amount of high density -electrons in the sp2 hybridized islands, which form excitonic states while the weak emissions are probably a result of quenching via radiationless relaxations to the ground state during exciton migration to energy traps.109 The second class of the fluorescence mechanism originates from surface-related defect sites. Generally, such surface sites have non-perfect sp2 domains that act as surface energy traps. Both sp2 and sp3 hybridized carbon atoms and other functionalised surface defects116,117 like carbonyl-related localised electronic states114118 present in CQDs/GQDs contribute to their multicolour emissions that are intense in the blue and green regions of the visible light spectrum. These surface defects behave like aromatic molecules that are individually incorporated into solid hosts that exhibit multicolour emissions due to the existence of multiple surface defects with different excitation and emission properties.106115 Robertson and O'Reilly suggested that the optical properties of carbon nanomaterials which contain both sp2 and sp3 bonds are determined by the -states of the sp2 sites.119 Therefore, the bright surface defect-derived fluorescence of CQDs/GQDs is due to the recombination of electronhole pairs in the strongly localized and * electronic levels of the sp2 sites. These sites lie between the bandgap of the and * states of the sp3 matrix,120,121 which leads to strong visible emissions. Such electronic transitions exhibit weak absorption in the near ultraviolet-visible (UV-vis) region and strong emissions in the visible region as shown in Fig. 11b. Moreover, upon surface passivation or functionalization, the surface defects become more stable to facilitate more effective radiative recombination of surface-confined electrons and holes, hence achieving brighter fluorescence emissions.115 Similar to semiconductor quantum dots, the emission spectra of CQDs/GQDs can also be tuned without any surface modification. But, due to the low stability of defects, the quantum yield of CQDs/GQDs is very low. Therefore, the surface modification of these quantum dots with polymers leads to stable defects and strong emission. 1.4 Luminescent nanoscale metalorganic frameworks

Metalorganic frameworks (MOFs) are a new class of inorganicorganic hybrid materials synthesized from metal ions or metal clusters and organic ligands.122126 Metalorganic-frameworks are also known as coordination polymers (CPs) or coordination networks. Because metalorganic frameworks have both inorganic and organic components, these materials are very promising as multifunctional materials. Based on the geometries of the organic ligands/linkers and coordination modes of the inorganic metal ions or clusters of metal ions, their structures can be designed according to the desired properties.124,127129 As a consequence, they show promise for an impressive number of applications in luminescent barcode security, gas storage, drug delivery, diagnostics, sensing, catalysis, ion exchange or separation, magnetism, optics, etc.130136 However, metalorganic materials in the form of traditional bulk crystalline materials do not always fulfil all the specific needs for these applications. Depending upon the desired application, these materials are required not only to be fabricated as bulk crystalline solids, but also to be miniaturized on the nanometre length scale and immobilized at specific locations on surfaces.136 For example, in order to fabricate luminescent security inks, nanoscale MOFs are more suitable because it is easier to disperse them uniformly with high stability into the ink medium compared to bulk MOFs. In the above applications, the additional advantage of nanoscale MOFs is that they have higher surface areas than their macroscopic counterparts thereby reducing the amount of luminescent MOFs required for ink fabrication. A similar situation occurs with some of their physical properties. It is well known that unique physical properties emerge prominently when at least one dimension of a material is reduced to the nanometre scale. Thus, nanoscale metalorganic materials are also expected to show highly desirable size-dependent optical, electrical and magnetic properties.136 The MOFs show tuneable luminescence behaviour covering a wide range of the spectrum from UV to NIR. In MOFs, both organic ligands and inorganic metal ions or clusters can provide a platform to produce luminescence. 1.4.2 Lanthanide based luminescence in MOFs. Lanthanide metal ion based luminescence is associated with lanthanide complexes and rare earth metals that are sensitized by a ligand-to-metal charge transfer-type process. All Ln3+ ions except for La3+ and Lu3+ may generate luminescent ff emissions from a broad range covering UV to NIR.139,140 Eu3+, Tb3+, Sm3+ and Tm3+ emit red, green, orange and blue colour, respectively, while Yb3+, Nd3+, and Er3+ demonstrate well established NIR emissions. The transition metals emit intense and sharp emission bands originating from ff transitions. However, lanthanide ions have weak absorption due to the forbidden ff transitions. This problem may be overcome by coupling them with the species that can absorb and transfer energy, known as sensitization or can create the antenna effect.139,140 The antenna effect occurs in three steps: (i) absorption of photon energy by organic ligands, present around the lanthanide ions, (ii) transfer of absorbed energy from the organic ligand to lanthanide ions and (iii) emission from the lanthanide ions as shown Fig. 14. The antenna effect is also observed in MOFs containing actinide metal ions.141
1.4.3 Charge transfer luminescence in MOFs. In this process, luminescence is generated by an allowed transition from the charge-transfer excited state to the ground state. There are two types of charge transfer in MOFs: (i) ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) and (ii) metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT). LMCT involves the electronic transition from an organic linker-localized orbital to a metal-centered orbital, while MLCT corresponds to the electronic transition from a metal-centered orbital to an organic linker-localized orbital. Basically, the charge transfer luminescence is observed in d10 metal based MOFs such as MOFCu3(C7H2NO5)233H2O (C7H2NO5 = 4-hydroxypyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate), which exhibits blue emission peaking at 398 and 478 nm upon excitation at 333 nm, whereas CuAg2(C7H2NO5)2 and the free organic linker 4-hydroxypyridine-2,6-dicarboxylic acid exhibit green emission at 515 and 526 nm upon excitation at 358 and 365 nm.142 However, in some cases LMCT or MLCT luminescence and ligand based luminescence may exist together. For example in the case of the zinc MOF Zn2(ATA)3(ATA)2/2, there is a weak blue emission at 485 nm with excitation at 320 nm while a strong luminescence at 392 nm is generated for ligand-based emission.143

1.5 Plasmonic nanomaterials

A plasmon is defined as the quantum of coherent oscillations of conduction electrons in metals in response to incident light. These oscillations can strongly be restricted to a metaldielectric interface (surface plasmons). Such collective oscillation of conduction electrons is also known as localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR). Conceptually, the localized surface plasmon is observed in metal particles whose size is smaller than the wavelength of incident light.145148 When the metal nanoparticle interacts with incident light, the electron cloud (free electron cloud) undergoes a displacement from its original position relative to nuclei. As a result restoring forces arise due to the Coulomb interaction between the electrons and nuclei that results in collective oscillations of the electron cloud relative to nuclei. The oscillation frequency is determined by four factors: the density of electrons, the effective electron mass, the shape and the size of the charge distribution. 2. Methodology for synthesis and characterization of luminescent nanomaterials

2.1 Lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials

The luminescent properties of lanthanide nanomaterials are highly dependent on their structure, size, shape, host lattice etc. as reported in the literature. Therefore, the method of synthesis of luminescent nanomaterials plays a seminal role in determining their desired optical properties. In the past few decades, numerous efforts have been devoted to develop different facile strategies for the synthesis of luminescent nanomaterials with tuneable size, shape and morphology. However, soft chemical routes widely used for the synthesis of luminescent nanomaterials have several advantages over conventional methods such as solid state reactions in terms of the following aspects: (i) soft chemical routes are easy and effective ways to introduce ligands on the surface of products for preventing them from agglomerating when they are dispersed in polar or non-polar solvents. (ii) The shape and size of nanoparticles can be altered by synthesis conditions such as raw materials, acidity, solvent, concentration, additive, temperature and reaction time. (iii) Soft chemical routes provide synthesis of luminescent nanoparticles on a large scale at low temperatures using simple apparatus at an economical cost. For these notable reasons soft chemical routes have been adapted as the most favourable and versatile method for shape controlled synthesis of luminescent nanomaterials. In this section, we explored the different chemical methods for synthesis of lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials and their characterization with different techniques.34 Various kinds of soft chemical methods are discussed below:
2.1.1 Hydrothermal/solvothermal method. The solvothermal method has been widely used for the synthesis of luminescent nanoparticles. In brief, solvothermal synthesis is based on high pressure and mild temperature reactions between solids in liquid precursors. Normally, it is carried out in a Teflon-lined autoclave, which consists of an inner Teflon liner, an outer stainless steel shell and a cap. When heated to a certain temperature, reactants and solvents sealed in the autoclave are subjected to a high pressure environment, which can increase the solubility and reactivity of almost all solid reactants.34 Consequently, the solvothermal method aids in increasing the rate of reactions which cannot occur or hardly occur under atmospheric pressure conditions. Furthermore, the reaction temperature is often above the critical point of the solvent, whereas the temperature of the usual solvent based routes is restricted by its boiling point. These favourable features make the solvothermal method highly suitable and efficient for designing and preparing high-quality nanoparticles. Hence, various types of precursors and solvents can be used for nanoparticle synthesis except for the ones that can decompose and release a vast amount of gas that may cause an explosion. Normally, the solvothermal system used for preparing rare-earth (RE) materials consists of a precursor, solvent, and organic additive. The RE precursors are usually simple nitrates or chlorides. HF, NH4F, NaF, and NaBF4 are the commonly used fluoride sources. Na3PO4/(NH4)2HPO4/(NH4)H2PO4, Na3VO4/(NH4)3VO4, Na2MoO4/(NH4)2MoO4, Na2WO4, and HBO3 are good reactants to provide acid radical ions for the formation of RE phosphate, vanadate, molybdate, tungstate, and borate, respectively. The solvent mainly includes water, ethanol, and ethylene glycol (EG). The physicochemical properties of the solvent can significantly influence the reactivity, solubility and diffusion behaviour of the reagents and different solvents can influence and control their morphology and size. For instance, ethanol, with low RE3+ and F solubility, and EG, with high viscosity and tuneable diffusion rate of ions, both have been proved to be effective solvents to slow down the nucleation and growth rate of nanoparticles.34 Water is a clean and green solvent for chemical synthesis and when it is used as a solvent in solvothermal synthesis, the popular hydrothermal method is achieved.

Hydrothermal synthesis provides a number of other desirable advantages such as low cost, nontoxic solvent and potential for high yield production. However, using water as the solvent is avoided due to its boiling point (100 C) limitations. Besides, a large number of reports have demonstrated that, in the solvothermal method, the most efficient and straightforward strategy for fine-tuning the shape and size of a targeted material is to introduce organic additives, including hydrophilic and hydrophobic ones. On one hand, the coordination effect between the hydrophilic ligands and metal ions will affect the actual concentration of free ions, thereby influencing the concentration of the monomer and growth kinetics, on the other hand, the selective adsorption of ligands on different surfaces of crystallites favours a controlled morphology. The organic surfactants/additives such as oleic acid (OA), polyethylenimine (PEI), EDTA, and cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) are used to control the size and shape as well as the surface modification of nanomaterials.31,160164 Additionally, the pH value of the final precursor solution is an important parameter in the hydrothermal process. The crystalline phase of the morphology of nanomaterials is highly dependent on the pH value. J. 2.1.3 Thermal decomposition method. Thermal decomposition is another method which is widely used for the synthesis of luminescent nanomaterials. The process involves the decomposition of organometallic compounds taken as precursors which decompose at an elevated temperature in high boiling point organic solvents. The organic precursors used in thermal decomposition are usually organic acid salts of rare earths such as trifluoroacetate, oleate, acetylacetonate, acetate etc. Commonly used organic solvents are oleic acid (OA), oleylamine (OM), tri-n-octylphosphine oxide (TOPO) and 1-octadecene (ODE). 2.2 Synthesis of quantum dots (QDs)

The synthesis of quantum dots has gained immense interest worldwide due to their wide range of applications. Several methods are reported in the literature that can be employed for the synthesis of quantum dots. However, the reproducibility with controlled size and band gap engineering of QDs is also an interesting and challenging topic of research. The various methods for the synthesis of QDs can be classified into two categories top-down and bottom-up.222224
2.2.1 Synthesis of semiconductor quantum dots (SQDs). The top-down technique involves the breaking of the bulk/large structure into small structures with dimensions in the nanometer range. In the case of semiconductor quantum dots, top-down techniques include molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), reactive ion etching (RIE), ion implantation, e-beam lithography and X-ray lithography techniques for the synthesis of QDs. Scherer and co-workers fabricated QDs using the reactive ion etching technique.225 In their study, the synthesis of QDs with a size 40 nm was reported. Tsutsui et al. also fabricated ZnTe quantum dots in a mixture of CH4 and H2 using the reactive ion etching technique.226 Bottom-up is another technique, where the fundamental building blocks such as molecules or atoms interact with each other and form nanostructures. There are several bottom-up techniques for the synthesis of QDs that can be divided into two types: wet chemical methods and vapour phase methods.222 The wet chemical methods include microemulsion, solgel, competitive reaction chemistry, hot-solution decomposition and electrochemistry, while the vapour phase methods include molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), sputtering, liquid metal ion sources and aggregation of gaseous monomers. However, most of the luminescent semiconductor QDs are synthesized by using wet chemical methods. In wet chemical methods, the size of quantum dots can be controlled by optimizing the growth parameters. Qu et al. synthesized CdSe QDs having sizes in the range of 69 nm using the wet chemical method.227 Murray and co-workers synthesized high-quality CdE (E = S, Se, Te) semiconductor QDs using a process based on the pyrolysis of organometallic reagents assisted by their injection into a hot coordinating solvent.228 In another interesting report, Qu and co-workers synthesized high quality CdSe nanocrystals in a very broad size range from 1.5 nm to above 25 nm using different wet chemical routes.229 Li et al. synthesized CdTe nanocrystals in the aqueous phase by microwave irradiation with controllable temperature.230 In general, MBE has been mainly used to self-assemble QDs from IIIV and IIVI semiconductors. Xin and co-workers synthesized CdSe QDs having diameters of 40 5 nm using the MBE technique.231 Furthermore, Liu and co-workers reported the synthesis of CdSe SQDs using commercial diesel as the reaction solvent and oleic acid as the ligand.232 The wet chemical methods have important advantages in that the size and shape of QDs can be controlled by simply varying the factors like temperature, electrostatic double layer thickness, stabilizers or micelle formation, concentrations of precursors, ratios of anionic to cationic species and solvent.
2.2.2 Synthesis of carbon quantum dots (CQDs). Top-down methods involve the breaking down of the larger carbon structures like nanodiamonds, graphite, carbon nanotubes, carbon soot, activated carbon, graphite oxide and reduced graphene oxide.233237 The most common methods like arc discharge, laser ablation and electrochemical oxidation are used to break down the large carbon lattice. Xu and co-workers reported the synthesis of fluorescent CQDs from carbon soot in 2004.107 Sun et al. synthesized CQDs by laser ablation of a carbon target in the presence of water vapors with argon as the carrier gas.108 The fluorescence was not detected in the as-synthesized sample. Consequently, to make the sample fluorescent, the as-synthesized sample was further treated with an acid for oxidation followed by surface passivation. The electrochemical synthesis of CQDs was reported by Zhou et al.234 They performed electrochemical synthesis in an electrochemical cell containing degassed acetonitrile solution with 0.1 M tetrabutylammonium perchlorate (TBAP) as the supporting electrolyte. The chemical vapor deposition (CVD) grown MWCNTs on a carbon paper were also inserted into the cell. The electrochemical process converts MWCNTs into highly efficient luminescent CQDs, which can easily be dispersed in various solvents. In another report, Ming et al. reported the electrochemical synthesis of CQDs using graphite as the carbon source which decreases the cost of precursor materials and increases the scale of production.238a Furthermore, J. M. Tour's group has reported a facile approach for large scale synthesis of GQDs at an economical cost from various types of coal.238b The bottom-up techniques involve the synthesis of CQDs from molecular precursors such as citrate, carbohydrates and polymersilica nanocomposites.109,239241 Usually, combustion/thermal treatments and supported synthetic and microwave synthetic methods are used for the synthesis of CQDs in the bottom-up approach. Liu and co-workers reported the synthesis of CQDs based on the use of modified silica spheres as carriers and resols as carbon precursors.242 Bourlinos et al. reported the synthesis of CQDs by the thermal decomposition method using ammonium citrate salts or 4-aminoantipyrine as precursors.239 In an interesting report, Zhu and co-workers synthesised CQDs from a solution of poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) and saccharide by heating in a 500 W microwave oven.243 However, cost is one of the major parameters for the large scale production of CQDs. In recent years, a large number of efforts have been devoted to develop green synthesis of CQDs. Furthermore, Li and co-workers reported the synthesis of fluorescent CQDs by using a simple and green route. They synthesized the fluorescent CQDs from ethanol by a one-step sodium hydroxide-assisted electrochemical process.244 The synthesized CQDs have high water solubility, better stability and good sensitivity to pH. Furthermore, there are many reports available on the synthesis of fluorescent CQDs using inexpensive and biocompatible precursors such as ethanol, citrate, glucosamine, ascorbic acid, saccharides, candle soot, orange juice, strawberry juice, sugar cane juice, chicken eggs, chitosan, organogel and gelatin.245261

Their miniaturization into the nano MOFs is expected to serve myriad applications.262 Consequently, enormous efforts have been devoted to synthesize MOFs by different methods. 2.4 Synthesis of plasmonic nanomaterials

The noble metal (Au and Ag) nanoparticles usually exhibit size and shape dependent plasmonic properties. Plasmonic nanoparticles with different shapes and sizes can be synthesized by using four different routes:266274
2.4.1 One-pot-one-step synthesis of plasmonic nanoparticles. This method is the easiest of all for the synthesis of metal nanoparticles. By using this method the morphology of nanoparticles (for example, stars, rods, plates, and prisms) can be controlled by changing the reaction conditions like temperature, solvent, additives, etc. The most commonly used reducing agent and capping agent for the nanoparticles is polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP). It can potentially modify the nucleation, growth, and shape of nanoparticles because of its proficiency in interacting with metal ions and surfaces as well as preferable attachment to distinct crystal facets of certain metals (e.g., silver).272,275280 Furthermore, the choice of solvent is also a very important factor for the synthesis of plasmonic nanoparticles. The nanoparticles can also be tuned to any desired shape by changing the solvent. Kedia and co-workers reported the effect of solvent on the synthesis of gold nanoparticles. They tuned the morphology of gold nanoparticles from spherical to star shaped by changing the solvent.281 This study reveals that apart from the capping agent, the interaction between the capping agentsolventmetal ion also affects the final structure of nanoparticles. In another report, Angelom et al. discussed the effect of temperature on the synthesis of plasmonic nanoparticles. In their study, they showed that the wavelength of LSPR in gold nanoparticles can be tuned from 600 nm to 1400 nm by simply varying the reaction temperature (between 30 and 10 C) and the reagent concentration.282 Zhang et al. demonstrated the synthesis of silver nanoprisms using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). The hydrogen peroxide facilitates the formation of planar-twinned defects which are a prerequisite for the formation of nanoprisms.283
2.4.2 Synthesis of plasmonic nanoparticles via seeded growth synthesis. This method includes two reaction steps. The first step reaction involves the formation of nuclei whereas, in the second reaction step, the seed formed in the first step is added to the growth solution. Furthermore, nanoparticles with different morphologies can be synthesized under different growth conditions. Langille et al. synthesized gold nanoparticles by the seeded growth method and in their report, they showed the effect of the halide ion on the growth of gold nanoparticles. Gold nanoparticles with different shapes can be synthesized by kinetic control, surface passivation, or by a combination of both, either in the absence or presence of silver ions as shown in Fig. 28.284 Ye et al. synthesized gold nanorods having different aspect ratios by introducing aromatic additives into the well-established seed mediated synthesis method with better control on the monodispersity of gold nanorods.285

3. Fabrication and design of luminescent security inks

The fabrication and designing strategies of luminescent security inks play a key role for their commercial use in anti-counterfeiting applications. The major challenge in this area is to fabricate a luminescent ink having high stability, with availability of ink medium at low cost, good dispersion of luminescent nanomaterials in ink medium, high luminescence intensity of ink, convenience of printing and sticky nature of ink medium on different surfaces (viscosity around 3000 P and good surface tension). Furthermore, it is also important to understand the chemistry of luminescent security ink formation (whether the ink is hydrophobic or hydrophilic in nature) prior to its potential use in printing of security codes/bars on different types of surfaces.

There are certain criteria to fabricate and design luminescent security inks with the desired properties as discussed below:

3.1 Choice of the luminescent ink medium

The appropriate choice of ink medium is an important aspect for the fabrication of luminescent inks in which the luminescent nanomaterials can be dispersed homogeneously and consequently form colloidal suspensions without forming any cluster or settling down at the bottom. Besides the above, the viscosity of the medium used for ink fabrication is also an important factor because it provides the essential sticky nature to the ink to make it suitable for printing on different types of surfaces. In addition to this, the boiling point of the medium used for ink fabrication is also a key factor. A volatile medium (such as toluene) exhibits the coffee-ring effect, which concentrates the medium along the rim of a drop as shown in Fig. 31a.294 Furthermore, the faster evaporation at the edges leads to flow of the ink medium towards the drop edge. This issue can be minimized by adding a solvent having a high boiling point. Blumenthal et al. have reported various solvents such as xylene, cyclohexanone and methyl benzoate for the minimization of the coffee-ring effect. They used methyl benzoate because of its ability to dissolve polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) and it is easy to mix with toluene. In their study, they observed that toluene and methyl benzoate mixed together in a volume ratio of 90:10 eliminated the coffee ring effect as shown in Fig. 31b.294 Apart from PMMA, many other media such as polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), aqueous solution of sodium hexametaphosphate (SHMP), and polyvinyl chloride (PVC) gold medium can be used for ink fabrication.12,15,294

3.2 Various types of luminescent ink formulation

This section briefly describes the formation of different types of luminescent security inks based on various types of luminescent nanomaterials such as lanthanide based luminescent nanomaterials (downconversion, upconversion and dual mode), luminescent quantum dots, luminescent MOFs and plasmonic nanomaterials as described below:
3.2.1 Lanthanide nanomaterial based luminescent security ink. In recent years the lanthanide based luminescent nanomaterials have gained huge attention due to their remarkable optical properties and wide range of applications in many potential areas such as luminescent security inks, optical displays, sensors, optoelectronics, laser cooling, bio-imaging, security applications etc. There are several potential advantages of using lanthanide based luminescent security inks for anti-counterfeiting applications compared to standard fluorescent inks, which normally produce visible emission when subjected to UV excitation. Furthermore, the lanthanide based luminescent inks exhibit both downconversion and upconversion processes in a single host lattice which makes them intrinsically more difficult to duplicate compared to the standard fluorescent dyes. Additionally, another advantage of lanthanide based inks over the commercially used fluorescent dyes is that the former have sharper transitions without photobleaching. Moreover, lanthanide based inks can be formulated to produce the correct color rendering index only under specific excitation power densities, making them even more difficult to duplicate the authentic read conditions.

This section of the review article will be mainly focused on the fabrication and design of lanthanide based luminescent security inks including downconversion/downshift, upconversion and dual-mode lanthanide based luminescent security inks.


3.2.1.1 Downconversion nanomaterial based luminescent security ink. In our earlier reports on luminescent security inks, we have demonstrated a strategy for the fabrication of downconversion security inks based on Y2O3:Eu3+ nanoparticles. Initially, sodium hexametaphosphate (SHMP) was dissolved in de-ionized (DI) water at a molar concentration of 5%. After that, 500 mg of Y2O3:Eu3+ nanoparticles were added to 30 ml of SHMP solution with continuous stirring for 15 min. Furthermore, 5 ml ethanol was added to the mixture and it was kept in an ultrasonic bath for about an hour. Finally, the capped nanoparticle particulates were collected using an ultracentrifuge. 0.2 mg cm3 of this was re-dispersed in fresh DI water at room temperature to make an aqueous-stable and highly transparent luminescent ink.15
3.2.1.2 Upconversion nanomaterial based luminescent security ink. Blumental and co-workers suggested an approach to fabricate the upconversion security ink using toluene as the ink medium.295 2 wt% of upconversion nanoparticles of NaYF4:Er,Yb were dispersed in toluene and 1 wt% poly methyl methacrylate (PMMA) was added to the dispersion along with enough methyl benzoate. Furthermore, direct-write and screen printing techniques were used to print with the fabricated ink. Meruga et al. also demonstrated a method for the fabrication of green and blue security inks based on upconversion nanoparticles of NaYF4:Er,Yb and NaYF4:Tm,Yb respectively for printing QR codes.11h For ink formation the upconversion nanophosphor and PMMA beads were added to a solution of 90:10 v:v toluene and methyl benzoate. Thereafter, the solution was vigorously stirred followed by sonication to form a uniform dispersion and complete dissolution of polymers and dispersion of nanoparticles. You and co-workers reported both hydrophobic and hydrophilic inks based on upconversion nanoparticles of -NaYF4:Er,Yb.295 For fabricating a hydrophobic ink having an optimum viscosity and surface tension, upconversion nanoparticles were added to a solution containing cyclohexane and glycerol trioleate in a volume ratio of 90:10 to 70:30.295 While for a hydrophilic ink, upconversion nanoparticles were added to a solution containing ethanol (10%) and glycerol in a volume ratio of 85:15 to 65:35. Furthermore, to control the surface tension, sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) was added to a hydrophilic ink at a concentration of 3 mg L1.
3.2.1.3 Dual-mode luminescent nanomaterial based security ink. In our earlier report, we presented a strategy to fabricate an invisible dual mode security ink using a commercially available PVC gold medium. In the typical ink fabrication process, 200 mg of dual mode nanoparticles were dispersed in 50 ml of PVC gold medium and ultra-sonicated at 45 kHz to form a good dispersion. Furthermore, the fabricated ink was used successfully for printing different security codes and images by the screen printing technique.12
3.2.2 Quantum dot based luminescent security ink. Lu et al. reported a multicolor rewritable fluorescent system using branched polyethyleneimine (BPEI)-CdTe quantum dots.296 In a typical synthesis, 0.01 mM red emitting QDs, 1% BPEI solution and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) masks were used to reversibly write and erase fluorescent patterns on a glass slide. Initially, the slide was immersed in the BPEI solution followed by washing and drying. Then the PDMS mask having a designed pattern was placed on the substrate and exposed to a QD solution for selectively anchoring CdTe nanocrystals via electrostatic force to form a pattern with red emission, which was imaged under 460 nm irradiation. The fluorescent pattern was easily erased with a drop of BPEI solution to decompose the surface-attached layer of QDs. After washing and drying, the film was ready for the next writing.296
3.2.3 MOF based luminescent security ink. Luz et al. also demonstrated a strategy to fabricate MOF based luminescent inks for inkjet printing. In their study, they discussed about the aqueous solution based red, green and NIR-MOF inks.297 Basically in this process an aqueous solution of MOFs was dispersed in a color cartridge to print the security codes for anti-counterfeiting applications. In summary, Luz et al. have demonstrated a facile, low-cost, and time efficient strategy for the positioning of photoluminescent Ln-MOFs on flexible substrates (paper and plastic foils). Literature reports suggest that a small volume of ink allows for the quick drying of the ink solvent; furthermore, rapid crystal nucleation permits the production of patterned photoluminescent Ln-MOFs without the multiple printing drying cycles.297
3.2.4 Plasmonic nanomaterial based luminescent security ink. Campos-Cuerva et al. demonstrated a strategy to print metallic nanoparticles as authentication tags on different types of paper using the screen printing technique.298 The metal nanoparticle based ink was fabricated using water-based or oil-based varnishes. 4.1 Screen printing techniques

This causes the ink to wet the substrate and be pulled out of the mesh apertures as the screen springs back after the blade has passed. 4.2 Aerosol jet printing techniques

The aerosol jet printing process employs aerodynamic focusing for the high-resolution deposition of colloidal suspensions and/or chemical precursor solutions. 4.3 Inkjet printing techniques

The inkjet printing process involves the ejection of a fixed amount of ink in a chamber, from a nozzle through a sudden, quasi-adiabatic reduction of the chamber volume via piezoelectric action.301 A chamber filled with liquid is contracted in response to application of an external voltage. This sudden reduction sets up a shockwave in the liquid, which causes a liquid drop to eject from the nozzle. This process has been analyzed to some extent and elaborated in recent review papers.302,303 The ejected drop falls under the action of gravity and air resistance until it impinges on the substrate, spreads under the momentum acquired in the motion, and surface tension aided flow along the surface.304 The drop then dries through solvent evaporation,305 as shown in Fig. 34. Recent studies306 show that drop spreading and the final printed shape strongly depend on the viscosity, which in turn is a function of the molar mass of the polymer. More interestingly, the aforementioned group also found the printing height dependence of the final dried-drop diameter, which was a function of the polymer concentration. The plasmonic nanomaterials have great prospects as next generation security labels/codes for anti-counterfeiting applications. 6. Conclusions: prospects, challenges and new directions

The widespread act of counterfeiting in all kinds of important documents including currency has become a real threat to society. This review article brings into focus the recent trends adopted for anti-counterfeiting applications which are urgently required for scientific communities, industries, government offices and consumers to protect their important documents. This review article begins with the historical background of luminescent nanomaterials, basic concepts of the luminescence mechanism, their classification and then covers in detail the entire process right from the development of the desired luminescent nanomaterials, ink designing and fabrication, various types of printing techniques for security codes and in the end explores their anti-counterfeiting applications. In this review, we have explored modern strategies adopted for the synthesis of lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials (downconversion, upconversion and dual mode), luminescent quantum dots (semiconductor and carbon dots), luminescent metal organic frameworks and plasmonic nanostructures, their surface morphologies and structural/microstructural characterization. Furthermore, we have described the choice of luminescent nanomaterials used for the formulation of different types of security inks and their properties. Moreover, we have discussed in detail the ink fabrication and design based on lanthanide doped luminescent nanomaterials, luminescent quantum dots, luminescent metal organic frameworks and plasmonic nanomaterials. After that, we described in detail the three popular printing techniques which are screen printing, aerosol jet printing and inkjet printing used for the printing of security codes and images. Using these fabricated security inks reported by us and other groups, we have clearly demonstrated their use in anti-counterfeiting applications by printing the security codes/bars, fingerprints and images on different surfaces and their clear detection. Additionally, we have also made some exploratory studies related to other important luminescent inks based on electroluminescence and chemiluminescence for anti-counterfeiting applications. The major outcome of this review provides a series of solutions related to the use and choice of various kinds of luminescent nanomaterials, and their use in ink fabrication for high end security printing for protection against the counterfeiting of documents, banknotes, medicine, branded items, etc.

Although huge progress has been made in the development of security inks based on different types of luminescent nanomaterials for anti-counterfeiting applications to date, numerous challenges and issues still remain to be resolved and there is still a long way to go to substantiate their practical application. In terms of the synthesis of different types of luminescent nanomaterials and design of ink formulations, novel synthetic methods still need to be explored to improve the shortcomings of the current methods. The limitations with these luminescent nanomaterials used presently are manifold. In general they are expensive, or have limited availability, a high photobleaching effect and a low yield of synthesis. The major challenge in the present scenario is the full exploitation of luminescent nanomaterials which are free of these constraints. Another important challenge in this area is to develop a medium for the fabrication of a luminescent ink having a long lifetime, the desired viscosity, and less cost with ease of dispersion of luminescent material into the medium with good colloidal stability. In a nutshell, the contents of the review are highly useful in the current scenario due to its wide coverage from the synthesis of different kinds of luminescent nanomaterial based inks to anti-counterfeiting applications.

The discussions in this review take up in detail the different kinds of luminescent nanomaterials, ink fabrication and various kinds of printing techniques for security codes for anti-counterfeiting applications and this will reignite the interest of researchers and serve as stepping stones to carry out further research in this field to develop a luminescent security ink with the desired properties for establishing a strong anti-counterfeiting technology.
