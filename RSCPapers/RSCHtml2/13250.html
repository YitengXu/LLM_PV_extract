Abstract

As a significant metal chalcogenide, copper sulfide (Cu2âˆ’xS, 0 < x < 1), with a unique semiconducting and nontoxic nature, has received significant attention over the past few decades. Extensive investigations have been employed to the various Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures owing to their excellent optoelectronic behavior, potential thermoelectric properties, and promising biomedical applications. As a result, micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS with well-controlled morphologies, sizes, crystalline phases, and compositions have been rationally synthesized and applied in the fields of photocatalysis, energy conversion, in vitro biosensing, and in vivo imaging and therapy. However, a comprehensive review on diversified Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures is still lacking; therefore, there is an imperative need to thoroughly highlight the new advances made in function-directed Cu2âˆ’xS-based nanocomposites. In this review, we have summarized the important progress made in the diversified Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures, including that in the synthetic strategies for the preparation of 0D, 1D, 2D, and 3D micro-/nanostructures (including polyhedral, hierarchical, hollow architectures, and superlattices) and in the development of modified Cu2âˆ’xS-based composites for enhanced performance, as well as their various applications. Furthermore, the present issues and promising research directions are briefly discussed.

1. Introduction

As a significant metal chalcogenide, copper sulfide (Cu2âˆ’xS, 0 < x < 1) with a unique p-type semiconducting and nontoxic nature has received significant attention over the past few decades.1,2 Extensive investigations have been developed to the diversified Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures because of their excellent optoelectronic behavior, potential thermoelectric properties, and promising biomedical applications.2,3 For example, a number of stoichiometric compositions of Cu2âˆ’xS from copper-rich Cu2S to sulfur-rich CuS exist with different crystal structures, leading to increased electrical conductivity and tunable direct band gap values in the range of 1.2â€“2.0 eV.4 Exciting surface plasmon resonance can be observed in the near-infrared region (NIR) in various Cu2âˆ’xS structures owing to the formation of free charge carriers.2,3 Moreover, Cu2âˆ’xS structures with different compositions have been extensively used for both in vitro and in vivo biomedical applications.3 Therefore, Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures are suitable for applications in the fields of photocatalysis, energy conversion, and biomedicine (including non-enzymatic biosensing and chemo-/photothermal therapy),1 which has resulted in tremendous efforts being put into the rapid development of micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS with well-controlled compositions, sizes, crystalline phases, and morphologies.

The synthetic methods previously reported for Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures mainly involve hydro-/solvothermal, electrodeposition, microwave irradiation, thermolysis, or template-assisted approaches.2,3 To date, micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS with a versatility in morphologies, such as zero-dimensional (0D) nanodots,5â€“22 one-dimensional (1D) nanostructures,23â€“55 two-dimensional (2D) nanostructures,56â€“82 three-dimensional (3D) polyhedrals, and hierarchical as well as hollow architectures,83â€“169 have been well-demonstrated. In particular, 0D nanodots can display a significant change in their physical and chemical properties due to the quantum confinement effect. Hollow nanostructure with a large specific surface area, low density, superior charge/mass transport, and high permeability can served as supports or active components for catalysts/drugs.172,173 An hierarchically anisotropic structure is more suitable in electronic devices owing to it allowing oriented electronic migration and structural integrity.40,174 However, investigation into the size-controlled and composition-tunable synthesis of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures is still in its infancy aside from so far highlighting the morphology-dependent properties. Consequently, the development of novel micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS with a controllable building block and composition as well as crystalline-phase features is still an interesting and challenging topic for both fundamental study and technological application.

Moreover, functional modifications (including hybridizing and doping) of the interfacial properties and electronic structures of Cu2âˆ’xS-based micro-/nanostructures can effectively bring about enhanced performance.175 Previously, it has been demonstrated that Cu2âˆ’xS-based hybrid micro-/nanostructures in combination with other materials can exhibit manifold enhanced activities in biomedical applications.3 Consequently, a systematic investigation on the synthetic strategies and formation mechanisms of different micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS can further optimize their intrinsic properties in different aspects (such as morphology, size, composition, crystalline phase, electronic structure, and heterogeneous interface), which could offer new insights to aid the development of high-performance Cu2âˆ’xS-based materials.

With consideration of the above-mentioned advancements, it is imperative to highlight the progress made in prevailing Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures. However, a comprehensive review on diverse Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures is still lacking so far. Therefore, in this review, we thoroughly summarize the important advances made in function-directed Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures (see Fig. 1). First, the crystalline structures and fundamental properties of Cu2âˆ’xS materials are covered in overview. Next, we mainly focus on the various synthetic strategies leading to the preparation of 0D, 1D, 2D, and 3D micro-/nanostructured Cu2âˆ’xS with well-defined morphologies, sizes, crystalline phases, and compositions. Then, the modified Cu2âˆ’xS-based composites for enhanced performances are discussed. Finally, we highlight the various applications (including photocatalysis, energy conversion, non-enzymatic biosensing, and chemo-/photothermal therapy) of Cu2âˆ’xS-based materials. Remarks on some present issues and perspectives are also briefly given.

2. Intrinsic properties

The crystal structure of Cu2âˆ’xS can be mainly divided into three types according to the packing of sulfur atoms in the lattice: cubic close packing (anilite and digenite), hexagonal close packing (djurleite and chalcocite), and an integration of hexagonal close packing and covalent bonding of sulfur atoms (covellite).176 Among these, the anilite and digenite forms represent the most stable group thermodynamically;177 while, the crystal structures of yarrowite, spionkopite, and geerite have still not been identified experimentally. As for the compositions, Cu2âˆ’xS with a small x value (0 < x < 1) exists in different crystalline phases ranging from copper-rich (Cu2S) to sulfur-rich (CuS). So far, the reported Cu2âˆ’xS compositions can be classified into eight different compositions: covellite (CuS), yarrowite (Cu1.12S), spionkopite (Cu1.40S), geerite (Cu1.60S), anilite (Cu1.75S, or Cu7S4), digenite (Cu1.80S, or Cu9S5), djurleite (Cu1.96S), and chalcocite (Cu2S). It is noted that all the above crystal structures of Cu2âˆ’xS possess Cu vacancies in the lattices, leading to a p-type semiconducting nature. Although the chemical formula of covelite (CuS) is simple, whereby the S ions have on average a âˆ’1 oxidation state, it has a peculiar and complex structure, which is formed by a repetition along the c axis and consisting of alternating layers of planar CuS3 triangles and CuS4 tetrahedrons with Sâ€“S bonds (see Fig. 2g).178 Effectively, it consists of a trilayer motif made of a layer of triangular CuS3 units between two layers of CuS4 tetrahedra, and the trilayers are stitched together by sulfurâ€“sulfur covalent bonds.179 These sulfurâ€“sulfur covalent bonds are quite important for the nanoscale transformations in covellite CuS nanocrystals in the presence of divalent metal cations (such as Hg2+ and Cd2+) at room temperature in the absence of phosphines. In this case, the combined presence of electron donors and foreign metal cations can trigger the entry of both electrons and metal cations into the covellite lattice, leading to reorganization of the anion framework because of the rupture of the sulfurâ€“sulfur bonds.179 Moreover, Cu2S and Cu1.96S/or Cu1.80S with short Cuâ€“Cu distances, such as in metallic Cuâ€“Cu bonding, or short Cuâ€“S distances can generate high electrical conductivity.180 Additionally, an interesting semimetallic character will occur as the critical temperature is 1.6 K, which makes Cu2âˆ’xS potentially act as a thermoelectric material.181 Notably, the digenite Cu1.8S porous architecture, with a second Cu1.96S phase prepared by sintering the Cu1.8S powders at 1173 K, can display a maximum dimensionless thermoelectric figure of merit (ZT) value of 0.5 at 673 K (Fig. 3a), because the Seebeck coefficient is enhanced while the thermal conductivity is reduced, which represents the highest value reported so far for p-type sulfide thermoelectric materials.182

Localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) is a collective oscillation of free carriers in resonance with incident light, in which the absorption cross-section, scattering cross-section, and local electric field intensity can be importantly improved at the LSPR frequency.65 The plasmonic aspect of self-doped Cu2âˆ’xS is a result of the collective oscillation of holesâ€”opposites to metals possessing free electronsâ€”and is induced through oxidation, resulting in the generation of Cu vacancies; thus Cu2âˆ’xS with different compositions often lead to unique LSPR in the near-infrared region.2 In a typical UV-vis-NIR spectrum of Cu2âˆ’xS, there is an obvious absorption band in the UV-Vis range and a broad band in the near-infrared region, and so these can be considered to be promising materials for a wide range of applications.2 Although generally two main peaks are seen in the UV-vis-NIR spectrum, previous reports have confirmed that Cu2âˆ’xS was a typical direct band gap p-type semiconductor owing to the Mossâ€“Burstein effect caused by Cu deficiency.183 The LSPR frequency is correlated with the charge-carrier density in the nanocrystals. Upon increasing the degree of Cu deficiency in Cu2âˆ’xS, the plasmonic resonance peak generally shifts to higher frequency (i.e., blue-shifts).184,185 Thus, LSPR cannot be observed in stoichiometric Cu2S nanocrystals because of the absence of free charge carriers.184 It should be noted that the classical Drude model offers a foundation for understanding the LSPR phenomenon and for relating the LSPR energy to the free carrier density. According to the Drude model, the concentration of free holes in Cu2âˆ’xS can be estimated from the peak LSPR absorbance wavelength and can be correlated with the copper deficiency (x).184 The Drude model described by theoretical formulas and Mie theory are detailed in previous references.184,185

A number of references have demonstrated that a composition-dependent, size-dependent, and shape-dependent LSPR shift (Fig. 3bâ€“f) can occur in different types of Cu2âˆ’xS.4,66,158,177,186â€“191 Significantly, a fast and reversible control of the plasmonic property of Cu2âˆ’xS can be achieved through an electrochemical redox reaction, which is applied in fast-switching NIR electrochromic devices.186 In order to better understand the plasmonic property of p-type Cu2âˆ’xS semiconductors, it is imperative to highlight the energy band structure, which is defined as the difference between the bottom of the conduction band (CB) and the highest occupied state in the valence band (VB).185 In this self-doped Cu2âˆ’xS system, the highest energy levels of the VB are occupied by the extra holes. The energy of the highest occupied electron states in the VB is therefore lower than that in undoped material (such as Cu2S), resulting in an increase in the optical bandgap. In general, the bandgap value (Eg) of Cu2âˆ’xS varies with stoichiometry and increases with copper deficiency â€˜xâ€™.4,141,177 For example, a blue-shift in the bandgap was observed with an increase in copper deficiency in previous reports (Eg = 1.2 eV for Cu2S, 1.5 eV for Cu1.8S, and 2.0 eV for CuS).141 As the x value increases, holes formed near the VB will result in a shifting of the lowest occupied energy level and changing of the bandgap value. Therefore, the different crystal structures, morphologies, and compositions have been demonstrated to generate unique optical, electrical, and thermoelectric properties,2 which could enable Cu2âˆ’xS to be used in various applications, and this will be further discussed in section 5.

3. Controllable synthesis of diverse Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures

3.1 Basic synthetic principles

3.1.1 Crystal growth theory. Thermodynamically, the equilibrium configuration of a crystal is determined by Gibbsâ€“Wulff's law,192 which is mainly attributed to minimization of the total surface energy. Generally, a crystallographic facet with a high surface energy would suffer disappearance or reduction from the final shape under an equilibrium environment, especially the unstable highly active facet. Kinetically, various non-equilibrium nanostructures could be prepared by tuning the precursor or reducer species, which would affect the nucleation and growth manner.193

Nevertheless, the final morphology with a specific exposed surface is a result of the interplay between the system thermodynamics and kinetics under a realistic reaction system. In particular, the introduction of capping reagent (such as surfactant agents, polymer molecules, and inorganic ions) in a liquid-phase reaction system is key for tuning the formation of a specific crystallographic facet based on the facet-selective adsorption mechanism, which can be viewed as an effective pathway to decrease the surface energy and form a non-equilibrium Wulff construction. Here, the capping facet will be retained, but the uncapping facets will disappear. To date, this viewpoint has been well-demonstrated in previous references,194â€“198 and this offers a guideline for preparing micro-/nanostructures with desired crystallographic facets.

As for Cu2âˆ’xS nanocrystals, the selectivity and capability of a capping agent on different facets is basically driven by the surface charge of the corresponding facet, so the selection of a capping agent with proper electronegativity is vital for controlling the preserved facet of the Cu2âˆ’xS crystal. Organic capping agents can play a crucial role in achieving the controllable synthesis of Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures because of their versatilities and availabilities. Furthermore, the supersaturation of growth species can also determine the surface energy of an exposed facet during a thermodynamical dissolutionâ€“recrystallination hydro-/solvothermal growth.199,200

3.1.2 General synthetic approach. Hydrothermal/solvothermal methods can effectively enable accelerating the solubility of a reactant in a solvent and speed up the chemical reaction process, ultimately attaining a rapid supersaturation under high temperature and pressure conditions. Thus, the synthesis of diversified Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures has been successfully achieved by this method.31â€“36,60,72,74,75,79,90,92,94â€“98,102,104,108,111,113â€“115,117â€“119,121,149,151,152,154,164,169 Different capping agents can be extensively employed to stabilize the narrow size distribution and dispersity in 0D nanodots, specific high-energy facets in 1D and 2D nanostructures, and 3D superstructures with controllable building blocks.

Thermolysis is an alternative approach and involves the formation of Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures by the thermal decomposition of Cuâ€“S complexes as single-source precursors (including copper alkylthiolate159,201 copper thiolate polymer,190 copper alkylthiophosphates,202 copper dithiooxamide,33 and copper alkyldithiocarbamate complexes203). The hot-injection method could also be considered as an effective solvent-mediated or straightforward surfactant-assisted nonaqueous thermolysis of Cuâ€“S complex.5,66 This strategy is useful to prepare uniform, monodisperse, and well-defined Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures from a mixture of organic solvents and specific capping agents in standard Schlenk equipment,5,14,26,66,204â€“207 which is similar to that used in Bawendi and coworkersâ€™ method for synthesizing monodisperse Cd chalcogenides.208 The general synthetic mechanism involves the sudden addition of cold reactants into a hot solvent/or surfactant, resulting in a sudden burst of nucleation and the subsequent growth of nuclei in the presence of the surfactant.209 Based on a thermodynamic viewpoint, the sum of the volume-energy (the energy generated when solution monomers are incorporated into growing nuclei) and surface energy (the energy that must be spent to create new solidâ€“solution interfaces) is significant for the growth of a nanocrystal.66 In this theory, the contribution of the volume-energy depends on the chemical potential of the solution monomers and time evolution, while the surface energy is dominated by the crystal geometry and the presence of surface-adhering ligands or surfactants, which would result in the formation of a size and shape dependence of polymorphs.66 Therefore, besides the hot-injection temperature, the choice of surfactant and solvent species is also crucial for the size- and shape-controlled synthesis of copper sulfides. For example, Xie and coworkers synthesized a series of monodisperse copper sulfide nanocrystals with anisotropic disk-like shapes and controlled sizes through a straightforward surfactant-assisted nonaqueous hot-injection route, which involved the fast addition of a room temperature S/oleyl amine solution to a suitably composed mixture of copper chloride/oleyl amine/oleic acid/1-octadecene preheated at 180 Â°C under an inert atmosphere, followed by a short annealing period.66 This method can further be expanded for the shape-/size-controlled synthesis of Cu2âˆ’xS-based heterogeneous nanocomposites.84,183,210â€“212 It is noted that the microwave-assisted thermolysis of Cuâ€“S complex precursors in the frequency range of 0.3 to 2.45 GHz is also a useful strategy to synthesize Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures, whereby the electromagnetic energy is converted into thermal energy, which then induces a chemical reaction toward the synthesis of nanomaterials.30,48,213â€“217

Electrodeposition or anodization is another facile and low-cost method to directly prepare 3D hierarchical Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures onto substrates, in which Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures can be grown on copper substrates or on copper foil acting as an anode and titanium metal as a cathode in the voltage range of 1.5â€“8 V in Na2S aqueous solution.57,112,218,219

The hard-template-assisted strategy is the most effective method to synthesize Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures with controllable sizes, morphologies, and hollow/porous features, which can copy the character of the template and inherit it into the appearance of the final product after removal of the template. This approach can be divided into three steps: (i) preparation of the template, (ii) direct synthesis of the target material using the above template, and (iii) removal of the template (if necessary).220 In the next section, we present a detailed discussion on the corresponding formation mechanisms by using different hard templates.

Notably, the synthesis of uniform and monodisperse Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures is of great significance to their practical applications. As a result, more modern techniques for both physical and chemical characterizations of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures are imperative. These techniques can provide important information on their elemental, structural (including size, crystalline phase, composition, and morphology), and electronic as well as optical properties. The characterization of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures could be performed by using a wide variety of techniques, such as X-ray diffraction (XRD), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) high resolution TEM (HRTEM), and UV-visible-NIR as well as photoluminescence (PL) spectroscopy, and so on.

3.2.1 Zero-dimensional (0D) nanodots. As a promising nanostructure, 0D Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots possess unique size-dependent optical properties, which opens up new opportunities for applications in the fields of light harvesting, nonlinear optics, and quantum information processing.5 Therefore, the development of new synthetic strategies for synthesizing 0D Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots is imperative. Currently, the capping-agent-assisted wet chemical method is the most common pathway to synthesize 0D Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots, such as in the low-temperature redox reaction,6,8â€“13 hot-injection method,5,7,14,15,17,21 microemulsion-assisted method,20 and microwave-assisted decomposition method.16 As shown in Fig. 4a,6 it can be clearly seen that water-soluble, monodispersed CuS nanodots (the average diameter is about 5 nm) were prepared at room temperature through the route of trapping the reaction intermediate (copper(II) chloride (CuCl2Â·2H2O)/sodium citrate dehydrate/sodium sulfide (Na2SÂ·9H2O)) using thiol-terminated, alkyl-containing short-chain poly(ethylene glycol)s (HS-(CH2)11-(OCH2CH2)6-OH, abbreviated as MUH). These MUH-coated CuS nanoclusters have superior stabilities in solution with varying the pH value and were stable in pure water for at least 10 months, and also displayed good potential anticancer and antibacterial performances. Monodisperse digenite Cu1.8S nanodots with an average diameter of 6.6 nm (see Fig. 4b) were synthesized at the gram scale following a typical hot-injection method by injecting a copper precursor into a sulfur precursor in the presence of oleylamine and oleic acid as ligands as well as 1-octadecene as a solvent.14 In this synthesis, the copper precursor was first prepared by mixing CuCl with a mixture of oleylamine and oleic acid at 130 Â°C under continuous mechanical stirring. Subsequently, the above copper precursor was naturally cooled to room temperature. The sulfur precursor was prepared in a three-necked bottle by dissolving sulfur powder into 1-octadecene at 200 Â°C under mechanical stirring. Then, the sulfur solution was then set to 180 Â°C, followed by a swift injection of copper precursor and kept at this temperature for 5 to 15 min, resulting in a black colloidal solution. Afterwards, the heated organic molecular solution was removed and cooled to room temperature. Finally, Cu1.8S nanodots with different sizes from âˆ¼6.6 to âˆ¼17.3 nm were obtained. It was noted that the as-synthesized colloidal nanocrystals underwent an in situ phase-transformation from rhombohedral Cu1.8S nanocrystals to hexagonal CuS clusters upon keeping the resulting colloidal solution for a few days. The Cu1.8S nanocrystals and CuS clusters exhibited composition-dependent LSPR in the near-infrared region. Djurleite Cu1.94S nanodots (see Fig. 4c) were synthesized by the thermolysis of the copper(II) acetylacetonate/dodecanethiol in oleylamine at 200 Â°C under the protection of a nitrogen atmosphere.18 Pure anilite Cu1.75S nanodots (see Fig. 4d) were synthesized, whereby a solution of copper(II) complex of 1,1,5,5-tetra-iso-propyl-2-thiobiuret precursor in octadecene was injected into hot oleylamine.19 Additionally, colloidal Cu2S nanodots (as shown in Fig. 4e) were prepared by following an injection reaction between copper(II) acetylacetonate and ammonium diethyldithiocarbamate in a mixed solvent of dodecanethiol and oleic acid.21 Moreover, Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots with various compositions and sizes were successfully synthesized by a modified hot-injection method (see Fig. 4f),15 which indicated that the LSPRs in these Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots originate from ligand-confined carriers.18,22 Similarly, Dai and coworkers demonstrated revertible shifts of the LSPR in CuS nanodisks, in which oleylamine acted as both a solvent and surface ligand covering on the CuS nanodisks during the thermolysis of a single-source copper ethylxanthate precursor.22 In this case, the revertible shifts of the LSPR by unloading and reloading the surface ligands on the CuS nanodisks was achieved, which could be attributed to the oxygen exposure and repassivation on the surfaces of the CuS nanocrystals. Therefore, the surface ligands-dependent shift of the LSPR can be attributed to the concentration of free holes in CuS nanodisks, which was controlled by the coverage and exchange of surface ligands as well as the oxygen exposure dose and time. Previous references demonstrated that the decomposition of ligands and oxidation is an effective strategy to tailor the composition and crystal phase of the above-mentioned surface ligands-dependent Cu2âˆ’xS, which results in a variation in the copper vacancies.15,18 For example, the post-oxidation of Cu2âˆ’xS nanodots (as shown in Fig. 4f) can lead to a decrease in the ratio between Cu2+ and Cu+, which in turn results in a broadening, decreased intensity, and red-shifting of the LSPRs.15 However, an obvious LSPR wavelength blue-shift that occurred in the djurleite nanocrystals was found after exposure to oxygen for different times as compared to that measured immediately on the fresh product, likely due to the increase in the hole (i.e., copper vacancies) density of djurleite nanocrystals in air. It should be noted that the hole density generated by the oxidation process will reach saturation gradually, ultimately leading to a decrease in the LSPR blue-shift after a long exposure time in air.18 A similar phenomenon was seen in Cu2âˆ’xS quantum dots due to exposure to oxygen.189 Briefly, the reason for the LSPR shift is complex, but could be attributed to the composition of Cu2âˆ’xS, and the effects of ligand coverage, oxygen exposure dose, and time. To date, the contradiction between an LSPR blue-shift and red-shift has not been well-explained, thus an in-depth investigation of this should be carried out.

Nanorods. Twinned CuS nanorods (see Fig. 5a) can be prepared by an in situ source-template-interface refluxing reaction at low temperature, in which CuCl2Â·2H2O and CS2 act as Cu- and S-precursors, respectively, and ethylenediamine as the attacking reagent to release S2âˆ’.23 CS2 is insoluble in water and remains as tiny oil-droplets, also acting as a microreactor. Moreover, the hydrothermal approach has also been used for the controllable synthesis of CuS nanorods. For example, Jin and coworkers reported that CuS nanorods could be synthesized in a mixture of CuCl2/Na2S/poly(vinylpyrrolidone) (PVP) under a hydrothermal condition (180 Â°C, 24 h).24 The gasâ€“solid reaction is one alternative approach to synthesize copper sulfide nanorods.25 As shown in Fig. 5b, Cu2S nanorod arrays can be obtained by passing a steady flow of H2S gas at 10 standard cubic centimeters per minute (sccm), O2 gas at 80 sccm, and N2 gas (saturated with H2O) at 160 sccm over a polished copper substrate.25 It was noted that O2 gas acted as a necessary reactant, but no copper oxide was observed in the product, suggesting that O2 played a different role from in a common oxidation reaction. The proposed growth mechanism was as follows. Initially, H2S and O2 react to produce H2O and S2âˆ’. Then, S2âˆ’ forms a close-packed sublattice, the polyhedral sites of which are occupied by Cu+. However, ionic vacancies (VCu) are generated in the absence of Cu+, which can subsequently diffuse in Cu2S to reach the copper substrate and become the driving force for this growth. Afterwards, a layer of Cu2S seeds are formed on the copper substrate, and the volumetric expansion as well as the annihilation of VCu by the defects in the Cu2S film finally results in cracks in the Cu2S film, generating nanorod arrays. In particular here, H2O acts as an indispensable reagent to facilitate charge transfer and ionic vacancy formation. The formation of nanorods is governed by the difference in Cu+ vacancy diffusion in various crystal directions. Moreover, the hot-injection method can be used to synthesize 1D Cu2âˆ’xS nanorods. As shown in Fig. 5c,26 a series of nanorods with different lengths could be easily formed between 10 and 100 nm by choice of the appropriate hot-injection temperature. In a typical synthesis, copper(I) acetate is first dissolved together with trioctylphosphine oxide (TOPO) in 1-octadecene (ODE) at a specific temperature before the sulfur source tert-dodecanethiol (t-DDT) is injected. After the injection, fine-tuning the temperature selection can enable one to accurately control the sizes and shapes of the Cu2âˆ’xS nanocrystals. In this reaction, tert-dodecanethiol as the sulfur source is the key for tailoring Cu2âˆ’xS nanocrystals, which can be easily decomposed at elevated temperature and can offer the reaction solution with reactive sulfur monomers. In addition, electrodeposition,27 the gas-assisted polymer template route,28 and the ultrasonication/microwave-assisted method29,30 can also be applied in the controllable preparation of 1D Cu2âˆ’xS nanorods.

Hollow architectures. The synthetic strategies for the synthesis of hollow Cu2âˆ’xS architectures are similar to the above-reported hierarchical architectures, and are mainly divided into three categories: (i) hard-template, (ii) soft-template, and (iii) template-free approaches. The involved growth mechanisms for the synthesis of hollow architectures generally include inside-out Ostwald ripening, oriented attachment, the Kirkendall effect, oxidization etching, and combined strategies.173,221â€“224
Hard-template strategy for synthesizing hollow architectures. The hard-template approach is the most effective strategy toward achieving a shape-controlled synthesis of hollow architectures, which can allow maximum inheritance of the configurations of the templates. Therefore, considerable efforts have been employed to develop novel hard-template strategies for synthesizing hollow nanostructures with well-controlled sizes, shapes, compositions, and arrangements of building blocks.122 So far, a number of hard templates have been used for synthesizing inorganic hollow architectures.122 In particular, morphological Cu2O templates have been widely synthesized and easily copied to form well-defined Cu2O@Cu2âˆ’xS coreâ€“shell architectures in a liquid-phase system containing Na2S, because of the very small solubility product constant Ksp of copper sulfide (Ksp = 10âˆ’48). Then, the advancements in other hard-template strategies for synthesizing hollow Cu2âˆ’xS architectures are also discussed.

When Cu2O meets Na2S in solution, it has been experimentally found that a thin Cu2âˆ’xS layer is formed immediately on the surface of Cu2O template, as evidenced by a fast color change from yellow or red to black. Finally, hollow Cu2âˆ’xS cages can be synthesized after removing Cu2O cores by ammonia or acid. 4. Modifications

To better understand the relationship between the modified interfacial/electronic structures and the enhanced performances is significant for developing novel Cu2âˆ’xS-based micro-/nanostructures. Generally, the modification of Cu2âˆ’xS can be classified into two strategies: (i) doping and (ii) hybridizing. Doping heteroatoms in Cu2âˆ’xS materials to modify their electronic structures is an effective strategy to improve the optical/or photoelectric properties; while hybridizing is an alternative approach for obtaining unexpected physicochemical properties, which is attributed to the synergistic effect between the active component and support. Therefore, Cu2âˆ’xS serving as the host component would provide a good opportunity to manipulate the interfacial properties of hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based architectures for expanding their technological applications. Currently, hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based composites with well-controlled compositions, morphologies, and sizes have been rationally designed and synthesized along with the rapid advancement in nanotechnology. 4.2.2 Hybrid semiconductorâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS. During the past decade, many investigations of hybrid semiconductorâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures have been carried out because multiple functions can be incorporated into a single system due to the fascinating properties induced by the heterointerfaces. Decorated semiconductorâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS. Copper sulfides are considered as an ideal absorber material because they are nontoxic, cheap, and abundant, and have good absorption characteristics,250 which can be widely used in the fields of solar cells and photocatalysts. Therefore, copper sulfide is suitable for enhancing the light absorption of a wide bandgap semiconductor to fabricate a number of decorated semiconductorâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS based devices. For example, CuS quantum dots (QDs) decorated on both the inside and outside of TiO2 nanotubes were successfully synthesized through a stepwise crystallization of CuS nanoparticles onto TiO2 nanotubes using L-cysteine linkers (see Fig. 26a).251 Herein, L-cysteine is an amino acid containing three reactive centers: a thiol group, an amine, and a carboxylic acid, which makes it favorable for binding CuS nanocrystals to the surface of TiO2. To prevent the formation of bulk CuS, TiO2 nanotubes were first treated with cysteine, which offered a binding site for copper ions. After introducing a Cu(II) acetate solution, the Cu(II) ions were expected to have a high affinity with the thiol group, and so they readily reacted with cysteine molecules, as evidenced by the deep blue-green color, indicating the formation of Cu(II) cysteine complexes. Finally, the CuS QDs were anchored onto TiO2 nanotubes after adding a Na2S solution. Briefly, coupling Cu2âˆ’xS with different support nanostructures (such as metals, semiconductors, carbon, and organic molecules) can remarkably promote an improvement of the physicochemical properties to achieve the desired performances compared to the bare Cu2âˆ’xS components. The rapid emergence of various multicomponent Cu2âˆ’xS-based hybrid composites is expected to provide new opportunities to expand our understanding of the fundamental importance of Cu2âˆ’xS crystals as well as to improve their potential applications. However, the intrinsic factors related to the controllable synthesis of morphological Cu2âˆ’xS decorated on different substrates needs to be investigated thoroughly, and the relationship between the interfacial structure of the hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based composites and their performance is still unclear. In addition, other layered 2D nanomaterials similar to graphene (such as carbon nitride, boron nitride, and molybdenum disulfide) decorated with Cu2âˆ’xS should be further investigated.

5.1.2 Photocatalytic hydrogen evolution. So far, photocatalytic hydrogen evolution by water splitting has been widely investigated, and the photocatalysts used must meet several criteria, including high sunlight harvesting, an effective separation of charge carriers, and good stability. Cu2S nanosheets have been proved to be an effective cocatalyst for photocatalytic hydrogen production. Li and coworkers demonstrated that Cu2S nanosheets composited with TiO2 nanofibers can show an about 5 times higher hydrogen evolution rate than that of single TiO2, which was attributed to the efficient light absorption from Cu2S and limitation of the recombination of electronâ€“hole pairs.187 Photogenerated electrons and holes are generated in both Cu2S and TiO2 under light irradiation, and subsequently the electrons migrated from the CB of TiO2 into that of Cu2S in the heterojunction (see Fig. 33b). Hence, a number of photogenerared electrons located in the CB of Cu2S result in a negative adjustment in the Fermi level, promoting electrons to transfer to H+ in water to produce hydrogen gas. Similarly, CuS/TiO2303,304 and CuS/ZnO,305 can also be used in photocatalytic hydrogen generation.

Notably, investigations of novel Cu2âˆ’xSâ€“metal sulfide photocatalystic systems for hydrogen production have been extensively developed.306â€“317 For example, porous CuS/ZnS nanosheet were prepared by a hydrothermal and cation-exchange reaction between preformed ZnS(ethylenediamine)0.5 nanosheets and Cu(NO3)2, in which the CuS nanoclusters were decorated onto the ZnS porous nanosheets.308 The as-prepared CuS/ZnS porous nanosheets (CuS loading content of 2 mol%) displayed a higher hydrogen production rate of 4147 Î¼mol hâˆ’1 gâˆ’1 from aqueous solutions containing Na2S and Na2SO3, even without Pt cocatalyst, and an apparent quantum efficiency of 20% at 420 nm. Cu2S-incorporated ZnS nanocomposites can also be used for photocatalytic hydrogen evolution, but the hydrogen production rate is lower than that of the above-mentioned CuS/ZnS porous nanosheets. 5.1.3 Photoreduction of CO2. Although fossil fuels play an important role in the energy supply and production, the emission of carbon dioxide (CO2) leads to pollution of the environment. Consequently, the conversion of CO2 is imperative. An effective pathway to mitigate the effect of the emissions is to close the carbon cycle by producing fuels, such as in the photocatalytic reduction of CO2. Up to date, it has been demonstrated that copper-based catalysts are some of the most promising candidates for achieving a high conversion efficiency of CO2,57,318 owing to the largely reducing conduction band and selectivity toward CO2 rather than the competing water reduction. Among the various copper sulfides, the p-type Cu2S semiconductor with a narrow indirect bandgap is attracting particular interest because it can serve as both a light absorber and cocatalyst and has been proven to reduce CO2.57 Moreover, Stolarczyk and coworkers demonstrated that Cu2S/Pt nanorods formed by a cation exchange between CdS and Cu2+ ions can be employed as a photocatalyst for CO2 reduction to CO and CH4 under broad visible-light illumination with a limitation of hydrogen production (see Fig. 33c).

Notably, the successful utilization of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanocrystals in photocatalysis greatly depends on the trapping and relaxation dynamics of the charge carriers. The photogenerated electrons and holes in nanocrystals could be readily trapped at the surface states under solar light irradiation because of the larger amount of surface states in nanocrystals compared to with the bulk materials, which typically leads to decreased photocatalytic performance. Consequently, a fundamental understanding of charge-carrier trapping and relaxation dynamics in Cu2âˆ’xS nanocrystals is important to rationally design high-activity photocatalytic systems. In the past few years, the LSPS property of Cu2âˆ’xS has been extensively investigated, but the spectral signatures and dynamics of photogenerated carriers in Cu2âˆ’xS semiconductors are less known and understood. The ultrafast dynamics can be explained in terms of hot-hole relaxation and hole trapping into the localized states. In 2015, Huang and coworkers reported that the hole dynamics of CuS nanodisks can be studied by using a combination of optical and X-ray transient absorption spectroscopy.319 In this report, the optical transient absorption results demonstrated the photogenerated holes signal following excitation of the plasmonic mode and bandgap excitation. It was seen that hole trapping occurred within subpicoseconds, and was followed by charge recombination with about a 100 ps time constant. X-ray transient absorption experiments confirmed that the trap states were possibly associated with sulfur, which had a copper deficiency, and/or the ligands on the surface of the semiconductors, indicating the possibility to tailor the hole dynamics by monitoring the surface structure or copper stoichiometry. Furthermore, the ultrafast hole-trapping process might compete with the recombination of photogenerated electrons/holes, thus a greater understanding of this will support the investigation into the following charge transfer to electron/hole acceptors and offer guidance on the potential application in the field of photocatalysis. However, investigation of the ultrafast dynamics of Cu2âˆ’xS is still its infancy, and more efforts should be employed in this field.

5.2.2 Lithium-ion batteries. Copper sulfide is considered as a suitable cathode material for lithium-ion batteries due to its high lithium activity, high theoretical capacity, and low cost, especially Cu2S, which is abundant and cheap in nature.40,320 The reactions of transition metal chalcogenide generally involve more electrons per 3d metal compared with the commercial one. Moreover, the potential of the transition metal sulfides versus lithium conversion depends on the ionocovalence of the bonding between the transition metal and the chalcogenide, leading to the continuous tuning from 0 to 3.5 V by adjusting the nature of the anion chalcogenide.40 Significantly, 1D nanowire arrays could exhibit a high contact area between the nanostructured electrode and electrolyte, short path distances for both electrons and lithium ions transport, and better flexibility for accommodating the strain arising from lithium-ion insertion/extraction. Hence, it is considered a promising structure for next generation electrode materials for lithium-ion batteries. Chen and coworkers demonstrated that large-scale, high-quality, and well-aligned Cu2S nanowire arrays on the metal current collector substrate possessed good cyclic stability, high reversible lithium storage capacity, long cycle life, and outstanding rate capability.40 As expected, the combination of conductive Cu components with Cu2âˆ’xS nanotubes exhibited good lithium storage properties with stable cyclability upon lithium ion insertion/extraction.53

However, investigation of the application of Cu2âˆ’xS in lithium-ion batteries is still rarely reported in the literature, and more effort should be applied in this field.

5.4 Biomedical applications

In this subsection, we briefly summarize the recent progress made in biomedical applications of various Cu2âˆ’xS-based nanoparticles. During the past few decades, Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles and their conjugates have been widely used in in vitro biosensing, especially in the detection of biomedical molecules, including glucose, DNA, and hydrogen peroxide, which could be attributed to their metal-like electrical conductivity and the ability to optimize electron transfer reactions with biomolecules. Significantly, the in vivo application of CuS nanoparticles, such as in chemo-/photothermal therapy and delivery and theranostic applications, has attracted more and more attention because of their highly versatile and ready control for various biomedical applications. Here, after a brief overview to Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles in in vitro biomedical applications, we further introduce the new advancements in in vivo chemo-/photothermal therapy by using conjugated Cu2âˆ’xS nanocomposites.
5.4.1 In vitro biosensing. In the past decade, extensive attention has been paid to electrochemical biosensor owing to its importance for facile and fast determination in clinical diagnostics and for applications in the food industry. The nonstoichiometric Cu component in Cu2âˆ’xS offers an intriguing activity in in vitro redox biosensing reactions. In particular, hierarchical and hollow Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures with good physicochemical properties are in favor for application in biosensing.

Precise detection of the glucose level in human body is imperative because the concentration of glucose is an informal indicator in diseases such as diabetics and other endocrine metabolic disorders. Currently, it has been proven that electrochemical non-enzymatic biosensors based on the direct oxidation of glucose can generate a higher stability than enzymatic biosensors. As for Cu2âˆ’xS biosensors, it has found that hollow CuS nanotubes could act as a modified electrode for the non-enzymatic detection of glucose in an alkaline condition, and displayed a large linear response range, high sensitivity, and a low detection limit.48 Furthermore, the detection of H2O2, human immunoglobulin G and DNA was also reported by using different CuS nanostructures.24,31,78,83 Additionally, CuS is a new kind of candidate for peroxidase mimics, which opens up a new kind of candidates over the previous Fe3O4 and carbon nanostructures. For example, CuS concave superstructures can present an intrinsic peroxidase-like activity, which quickly catalyzed the oxidation of typical horseradish peroxidase substrates, including 3,3â€²,5,5â€²-tetramethylbenzidine and o-phenylenediamine, in the presence of H2O2.113 Detailed biomedical application of CuS nanoparticles have been reported by a number of previous review articles.3,328 However, the inherent conductivity of Cu2âˆ’xS semiconductors is not very good, which leads to a decrease in the charge separation and transfer to some extent, and limits the electrochemical performance of the biosensors. Thus, Cu2âˆ’xSâ€“CNTs combined with conductive CNTs composites can effectively enhance the electrochemical performance toward the non-enzymatic detection of glucose.269 However, the exact biosensing mechanism in the Cuâ€“S system is still unknown, so the design and synthesis of new hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS is highly necessary.

5.4.2 In vivo imaging and therapy. Aside from the plasmonic gold nanoparticles with tunable optical absorption in the near-infrared region (NIR) for biomedical applications, low-cost Cu2âˆ’xS has been used as a new class of photothermal conducting agent for in vivo imaging and therapy because Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures also show strong absorption in the near-infrared region, exhibit highly efficient light-to-heat transformation under near-infrared laser irradiation, and generate selective thermal destruction to tumors. Therefore, Cu2âˆ’xS and their hybrids can be widely used in the fields of photoacoustic tomography (PAT) imaging, chemo-/photothermal therapy, drug delivery, and theranostics.3,328

PAT imaging is used for the detection of thermoelastic expansion and ultrasonic signals generated by the photothermal expansion of light-absorbing tissues or contrast agents under the irradiation of short laser pulses.328 Traditional PAT imaging generally depends on the generation of contrast through differences in the blood perfusion between normal and tumor tissues, but such an intrinsic contrast might be not precise enough for the early detection of cancer or the imaging of deeper tumor tissues. To date, it has been demonstrated that Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles could be employed in clinical applications, including imaging breast lesions up to 4 cm deep, as well as other superficial lesions in the skin, limbs, head and neck, and lymph nodes. Furthermore, enhanced specificity and sensitivity for the potential clinical application of Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles can be obtained by optimizing an efficient laser source. For instance, Li and coworkers found that CuS nanodots with strong absorption at wavelengths larger than 900 nm can acted as a novel type of contrast agent for the in vivo detection of PAT images,11 which was a higher wavelength than that possible with gold nanostructures and organic dyes, making it suitable to use Q-switched Nd:YAG laser light at 1064 nm for PAT imaging. Therefore, deep tissue PAT imaging can be obtained by using CuS nanoparticles, because of the low absorption and scattering coefficient in normal tissue at 1064 nm. Additionally, PEG-layered Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles were suitable for the in vivo imaging of tumor vasculature.287,329,330

Photothermal ablation is an active area of in vivo biomedical application where Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles are currently increasingly being investigated and can prospectively kill tumor cells by heating at 40â€“45 Â°C through a focused, skin penetrating NIR laser beam (typically in the range of 700â€“1065 nm). A number of heat sources have been used, such as microwave, magnetic field, radiofrequency, and laser stimulation. However, a severe bottleneck is posed by the non-specific absorption of heat by healthy tissues between the laser source and the tumor mass.3 Hence, it is necessary to develop novel photothermal agents with increased photothermal efficiency to decrease the energy dose of the used laser and hence the damage to surrounding tissues. The NIR absorption of Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles (the maximum absorption wavelength at âˆ¼900 nm) stem from the dâ€“d transitions of Cu2+ ions, which is different from the NIR absorption of Au nanostructures that rely on LSPR (maximum absorption wavelength <850 nm). So far, the use of Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures (such as nanodots,331,332 hollow,333 and superstructures94 and their hybrids334,335) as photothermal mediators has been widely investigated.3,328

The development of biocompatible targeted drug delivery platforms can importantly enhance metastatic cancer patient management. In the past decades, it has been proven that nanomaterials are effective carriers for the targeted delivery of anticancer drugs owing to their larger surface area/loading capacity, appropriate size, and versatile physicochemical property, which can lead to the formation of large specific surface volumes and numerous mesoporous pores for efficiently loading them with hydrophobic drug molecules. A number of research studies have demonstrated Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles with interesting properties as delivery vehicles, where hollow Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles132 and porous silica-Cu2âˆ’xS-based nanocomplexes,288â€“295 for example, have been utilized for controlling the drug delivery because both of them can exhibit an exceptionally high drug loading capacity for doxorubicin (DOX) or imaging agents and can release the anticancer drug in a pH-dependent fashion and with NIR light. In this case, Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles acted as both a gatekeeper, preventing the premature release of drugs, and as a photothermal conducting agent, for the ablation of cancer cells. It should be noted that hybrid organicâ€“inorganic nanosystems are of great interest as an emerging class of drug nanocarriers due to their broad compositional and structural diversity.269â€“276 Recently, a review article on copper-based nanostructure showed how it can serve as a versatile vehicle for drug delivery and image-guided therapy.328

Theranostics is a single platform for combining both therapy and diagnosis. The combination of different targeting ligands, imaging labels, therapeutic drugs, and various polymers to enhance their water solubility and biocompatibility, can allow for the effective and controlled delivery of therapeutic agents in patients, which can also be non-invasively monitored in real time.336 The detailed merits of Cu2âˆ’xS for theranostic applications are as follows:328 (i) they could act as nuclear tracers or contrast agents for a variety of imaging and diagnostic techniques; (ii) they can kill cancer cells through photothermal effects; (iii) they can release drugs controllably in response to an external stimulus. (iv) The above features can be easily integrated in a single system to fulfill both diagnostic and therapeutic requirements. Based on the above properties, Cu2âˆ’xS-based nanoparticles have also been investigated for cancer theranostics. With the incorporation of the radionuclide 64Cu, [64Cu]â€“CuS also acted as an effective imaging agent for positron emission tomography (PET) compared to free copper. 6. Conclusions and future perspectives

Diverse Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures have been extensively developed for application in the fields of photocatalysis, energy conversion, in vitro biosensing, and in vivo imaging and therapy, due to their unique optical and electronic properties, controllable morphologies, sizes, and compositions as well as crystalline phases. Herein, a comprehensive summary on the synthetic strategies and formation mechanisms, the morphological and compositional diversities, the functional modifications (including hybridizing and doping) of interfacial and electronic structures, and their current applications have been overviewed for different types of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures. The achievements made in the controllable synthesis of morphological and compositional Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures also offer a good chance for the rational design and fabrication of novel hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based composites with enhanced performances. Aside from the current studies and results researchers have investigated over the past decades, as outlined in this review, there is still a long way ahead before Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures can be comprehensively understood and well-applied. The present issues and promising research directions are proposed as follows.

In regard to the synthesis of a novel Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructure, a significant target is to obtain the optimum growth mechanism and thereby to tune the morphology, size, composition, crystalline-phase, monodispersity, surface/interfacial atomic arrangement, and electronic structure. As for 3D polyhedral Cu2âˆ’xS, the fabrication of more highly active facets, especially high-index facets, would provide a top priority for optimizing the chemical activity. However, currently the as-synthesized Cu2âˆ’xS polyhedra are all exposed with low-index facets, limiting the investigation of the facet-dependent property of Cu2âˆ’xS. Consequently, the synthesis of novel Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures exposed with controllable-index facets should be further employed to uncover the relationship between the surface atomic structures and performance. Moreover, new strategies should be developed for synthesizing highly active hollow and hierarchical nanostructures with much smaller sizes and tunable compositions. As for 2D Cu2âˆ’xS, the preparation of large-scale ultrathin nanosheets is still a challenge because the synthetic strategy still has limitations. As for 1D Cu2âˆ’xS, obtaining a hollow or hierarchical architecture with a nanoscale diameter (<100 nm) is still a bottleneck. As for 0D Cu2âˆ’xS, the current synthetic methods generally involve a surfactant and organic solvent, which would affect the purity of the product, thereby a surfactant-free synthetic strategy is worth exploring. Additionally, the development of morphological 1D, 2D, and 3D Cu2âˆ’xS superlattices can increase the number of active sites and enhance the mass transfer and migration, leading to an improvement of the unique performances. However, the controllable synthesis of Cu2âˆ’xS superlattices is still in its infancy. Therefore, it is still necessary to investigate the preparation of Cu2âˆ’xS superlattices with the desired crystallographic facets, morphologies, and compositions. Additionally, it is noted that the crystal packing of Cu2âˆ’xS with different x values can strongly affect the semimetallic behavior, thus the composition-controlled synthesis of Cu2âˆ’xS nanostructures is important in tuning their physicochemical properties.

To further optimize the performances of pristine Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures, modifications (including doping and hybridizing) are extremely imperative to be developed. For doped Cu2âˆ’xS, the current achievement is limited to only a few chemical elements (such as iodine and zinc), thus it is still a great challenge to expand the selective doping of more heteroatoms for enhanced performance, so an effective strategy for achieving the synthesis of new doped-Cu2âˆ’xS should be taken into consideration. For hybridized Cu2âˆ’xS, the selective-hybridizing of active components to specific facets of Cu2âˆ’xS micro-/nanostructures should be specially explored. Significantly, theoretical calculations of the doping electronic structures and hybridizing interfacial structures should be investigated thoroughly.

The application of low-cost Cu2âˆ’xS templates for the synthesis of ternary metal sulfides and multicomponent heterogeneous nanocomposites is a promising field. In particular, a sacrificial hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based template (such as metalâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS, semiconductorâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS and carbonâ€“Cu2âˆ’xS) could be applied to selectively design and prepare a number of novel multicomponent nanocomposites. Emphatically, the absorption band of Cu2âˆ’xS in the near-infrared region can result in the LSPR due to the presence of charge carriers, which brings about significant application potential in the fields of photocatalysis (including the photodegradation of organic dyes, photocatalytic hydrogen production, and the photoreduction of CO2) and biomedicine (including in vitro biosensing and in vivo cancer imaging and therapy). Although hybrid Cu2âˆ’xS-based nanocomposites (various combinations of hollow/porous/coreâ€“shell architectures, polymeric coatings, and functional moieties) are considered as promising candidates for biomedical applications, several challenges still need to be overcome in the future, such as the pharmacokinetics and potential toxicity of Cu2âˆ’xS nanoparticles and the lower photothermal conversion efficiency.

Overall, this in-depth review on the synthetic strategies and formation mechanisms, the modifications, and their perspective applications, will help to accelerate the development of function-directed Cu2âˆ’xS-based micro-/nanostructures. This field remains greatly untapped yet offers expansive opportunities.
