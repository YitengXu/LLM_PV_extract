22  Fullerenes

P. R. Birkett
Department of Chemistry and Materials, Manchester Metropolitan University, Manchester, UK M1 5GD

First published on 17th July 2003

There have been significant advances made in the development of nanotube chemistry and in the search for applications for both fullerene- and nanotube-based materials which is reflected in this review.

1 Introduction

This review complements previous reports1 and is a collection of the highlights revealed by Web of Science and Chemical Abstracts searches for the year 2002. The inquiry into some of the results published by Jan Hendrick Schön, formerly of Bell Laboratories has, however, been the major feature of fullerene and nanotube chemistry during the year.2 The papers concerned,3 which are now regarded to contain fraudulent data, have been reviewed previously within this series of articles1 and during 2002 by Panthöfer and Jansen and should now be disregarded.4

2 Synthesis, separation and properties of fullerenes

Gas phase chemistry of a cubane expanded by the insertion of twelve buta-1,3-diynediyl moieties leads to the observation of signals assigned to [56]- and [54]-fullerene anions after gas phase treatment.5 Nine different isomeric C30 fragments have been identified by retrosynthetic analysis of [60]fullerene.6 [60]Fullerene has been synthesised in isolable quantities in twelve steps; the final synthetic step requiring flash vacuum pyrolysis and is estimated to produce about 3% of [60]fullerene.7 The same aromatic non-fullerene precursor has been shown, independently, to result in the formation of fullerene ions under matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight spectroscopy (MALDI TOF-MS) conditions.8 The experimental Circular Dichroism (CD) spectrum of D2 [84]fullerene has been assigned by time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) calculations, which has enabled the identification of the absolute configuration of the [84]fullerene as being the (fA) enantiomer,9 using the nomenclature developed earlier.10 A single isomer of [92]fullerene having C2 molecular symmetry has been isolated and characterised using 13C NMR spectroscopy; further isomers of [92]fullerene could not be separated from each other.11 Five isomers of the [2 + 2] dimer of [70]fullerene, formed by the reaction of [70]fullerene with potassium carbonate, have been separated by HPLC.12 Femtosecond time-resolved spectroscopy has provided experimental evidence for the photoisomerisation of the higher fullerenes.13 Scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM) analysis of the [2 + 2] [60]fullerene trimers formed as a result of treating [60]fullerene with 4-aminopyridine shows that a variety of isomers are present within the reaction product.14 Fullerenes form as a result of pyrolysis of single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWNT) which have been subjected to an ultra-sonication and heat treatment methodology.15

The electronic states and transitions of [60]- and [70]-fullerene have been reviewed,16 as has the linear and non-linear optical properties of fullerenes in solid state materials.17 The distortive effect of π electrons in [60]fullerene has been modelled by varying Hückel bond parameters which confirms that the fullerene system lies below the threshold for spontaneous distortion to non-icosahedral symmetry.18

A review of the impact of fullerenes in analytical chemistry has been published.19 A [60]fullerene-containing column efficiently preconcentrates cadmium and cadmium metallothioneins extracted from fish liver.20 Similarly a fullerene derivative, prepared by the photoreaction of [60]fullerene with sodium diethyldithiocarbamate, has been used for speciation of lead in environmental waters.21 The physisorption of nitrogen on [60]fullerene has been simulated using the Grand Canonical Monte Carlo method22 and an aqueous suspension of [60]fullerene was found to exhibit a 2.4 times better loading of hydrogen gas than activated carbon.23

3 Endohedral fullerenes

14N is encapsulated within [60]fullerene efficiently, compared with standard methods for encapsulation, after vaporization of the fullerene in an rf-plasma.24 N2@C60 and N@C60 have been separated using HPLC.25 A sample of [60]fullerene containing about 9% Kr@C60 co-crystallises with the nickel(II) porphyrin, NiII(OEP) where OEP is the dianion of the octaethyl porphyrin.26 The 129Xe NMR spectrum of Xe@C60 has a signal 8.89 ppm upfield relative to the signal of xenon gas in the same solvent, benzene.27 Encapsulation of phosphorus in [60]fullerene has now been reported.28 EPR and ENDOR techniques have been used to investigate the properties of nitrogen atom-containing endohedral fullerene molecules.29

Endohedral metallofullerenes have been reviewed30 and the molecular polarizability of a number of these molecules calculated.31 Endohedral uranium fullerenes have been detected as atmospheric particulates emitted by coal-burning power plants.32 Reduction of endohedral metallofullerenes to their anions followed by extraction has been used as a new method for the separation of these materials from bulk soot.33 Three isomers of Tm@C82 were found by 13C NMR spectroscopy to have fullerene cages with Cs, C2 and C2v molecular symmetries.34 Two isomers of Er2@C82, one with the fullerene cage having Cs molecular symmetry35 and the other with a cage of C3v molecular symmetry,36 have been characterised after crystallization with the porphyrins, CoII(OEP) and NiII(OEP) respectively. A new trimetallic nitride endohedral fullerene, Lu3N@C80, has been characterised by crystallography after crystallization from o-xylene.37 A Diels–Alder adduct of Sc3N@C82 has been synthesised38 and characterised by crystallography.39 The complicated temperature dependence of the dipole dynamics of La@C82 has been revealed by variable temperature dielectric constant studies.40

4 Organic chemistry

Once again the past year has seen a large number of papers dedicated to fullerene organic chemistry. The reaction of [60]fullerene with 3-chloro-3-alkyldiazirine has been used to determine the decomposition pathway of this molecule under photolytic conditions.41 A Diels–Alder adduct was obtained, rather than the anticipated N-methylfulleropyrrolidine, from reaction of trans-2-stilbenecarboxaldehyde and N-methylglycine with [60]fullerene.42 The [4 + 2] cycloaddition of trans,trans-2,4-hexadiene and cis,trans-2,4-hexadiene with [60]fullerene results in the formation of the same product due to isomerisation of the less stable cis,trans-hexadiene unless elevated reaction temperatures are employed.43 Several σ-quinodimethane [60]fullerene derivatives bearing a hydroxyl group have been synthesised and the conformation of the addended cyclohexene ring investigated in detail using NMR spectroscopy.44 Tetrasubstituted Diels–Alder [60]fullerene cycloadducts undergo photorearrangement to a mixture of the bis-fulleroid and bis-methanofullerene by a new reaction intermediate.45 A similar approach has been employed to synthesise two diastereomeric tetrakis[60]fulleroids from a bis-Diels–Alder adduct of [60]fullerene.46

A quantitative study of the substituent effect of the dihydro[60]fullerenyl group has been published and the Hammett σ value found to be 0.06, which is consistent with [60]fullerene producing a small electron withdrawing effect.47 Nitroxide-containing [60]fulleropyrrolidine bisadducts have been synthesised and their EPR properties investigated.48 [60]Fulleropyrrolidine molecules containing either two or three [60]fullerene moieties form ordered cluster aggregates.49 In addition, [60]fulleropyrrolidines, with up to six [60]fullerene groups, have been synthesised by reaction of mono[60]fulleropyrrolidine derivatives containing an amino functional group with polycarboxylic acid derivatives using standard coupling techniques.50 The rate of transformation of aza-fulleroids substituted with aromatic groups to the corresponding aziridino[60]fullerene is dependent upon the type of aromatic group present.51 Cyclo-octylmalonates have been used in a tether-directed functionalisation strategy to produce bis- and tris-adducts of [60]fullerene which have large molecular channels in the solid state.52 Crown ether-like malonate derivatives of [60]fullerene have been synthesised using standard reaction conditions.53 Diels–Alder cycloaddition has been used as an alternative strategy for the synthesis of similar crown ether-containing [60]fullerene bis-derivatives which were found to have ion complexing behaviour dependent upon the size of the crown ether ring.54 A comprehensive study of mono- and bis-crown ether derivatives of [70]fullerene synthesised using the tether directed approach has been published.55 Enantiomerically pure 1,1′-binaphthyl methano[60]fullerene derivatives containing two fullerene moieties have also been prepared.56

5 Biologically active fullerenes

A review of a series of hybrid systems with biological activity, including several [60]fullerene derivatives, has been published.57 [60]Fullerene molecules containing a sequence specific DNA minor groove binding moiety comprising of an oligonucleotide and 5,6,7-trimethoxyindole conjugate have been synthesised and the formation of unstable triple helices observed.58 Water soluble, multi-charged fullero[60]pyrrolidine derivatives with three ethylene glycol and three ammonium groups have been synthesised and have been shown to be ineffective for neuroprotection and to have concentration dependent toxicity.59 Fullerene peptides prepared using solid state techniques have specific activity against the gram-positive bacterium S. aureus.60 A tris-methano[60]fullerene, known to have interesting pharmacological activity, has been included in a study into the biological activity of several other non-fullerene molecules; in general the activity of these molecules is attributed to their ability to form aggregates.61 [60]Fullerene derivatives bearing artificial lipids produce self-organised fullerene bilayer films.62 The tris-methanoadduct of [60]fullerene bearing perfluorinated alkyl chains has excellent solubility in perfluorinated solvents and is shown to be an efficient sensitiser for singlet oxygen formation in fluorous media.63 A highly water soluble covalent β-cyclodextrin-[60]methanofullerene conjugate bearing two β-cyclodextrin moieties has a partition coefficent, log P = 1.58, suitable for biological studies.64 The synthesis of a methanofullerene functionalised with diphosphonate groups suitable for investigations in which bone tissue is targeted has been achieved.65 The reversible binding of a poly(amidoamine) substituted anthracene moiety to [60]fullerene results in a water soluble molecule which may act as a biologically active agent which releases [60]fullerene intracellularly.66 Ring opening of methano[60]fullerenyl iminoesters gives the corresponding 1,2-dihydro[60]fullerenyl glycine derivatives via a novel reductive reaction mechanism.67

6 Materials chemistry

Self-assembly of [60]fullerene derivatives capable of forming either three68 or four69 intermolecular H-bonds has been achieved and the molecules shown to be suitable for the construction of supramolecular arrays. The best Langmuir–Blodgett films of methano[60]fullerene malonates were obtained when the largest polar head groups (ethylene glycol moieties) and dodecyl chains were present.70 It has been shown that [60]fullerene carboxylic acid conjugates readily form monolayers on semiconductor surfaces and modify the work function of the semiconductor.71 Similarly carboxylated [60]fullerene derivatives can be adsorbed onto nanocrystalline ZrO2 films and display reversible electrochemical behaviour, explained by a percolation/electron hopping mechanism, with currents being 200 times higher than those measured for monolayers of the fullerene derivatives alone.72 The properties and synthesis of porphyrin-containing fullerene architectures has been reviewed.73 Bipyridine-containing methano[60]fullerene derivatives have been used to form ReI and ReII complexes; the latter having a higher quantum yield of singlet oxygen sensitisation.74 A family of [60]fullerene-containing dyads consisting of meso–meso linked oligoporphyrin scaffolds with one [60]fullerene moiety attached to each of the two external porphyrin groups have been synthesised and distinct conformational effects observed as a result of the strong [60]fullerene and porphyrin interactions.75 A study of the electron transfer processes in a 2D π system versus that in a 3D π (fullerene) system shows that intermolecular electron transfer is likely to occur at larger distances in the latter system even if the [60]fullerene acceptor molecules are held at the same critical distance (van der Waals contact) as the acceptor in the 2D system.76 Fluorescence and transient absorption measurements show that large energy gaps for the charge separated state and a small reorganization energy assist in stabilizing the charge-separated states in strongly coupled phthalocyanine [60]fulleropyrrolidines.77 An enhancement of photocurrent generation (30× and 10× for the two systems investigated) is observed when self assembled monolayers (SAMs) of porphyrin[60]fullerene dyads are formed on indium tin oxide (ITO) electrodes compared to those of the single chromophore systems.78 Similarly an independent study has shown that the photocurrent generation quantum yield of a porphyrin[60]fullerene dyad in SnO2 nanocrystalline semiconductor film electrodes is twice that of the porphyrin moiety alone.79 SAMs of a porphyrin[60]fullerene conjugate on gold electrodes also have an improved photocurrent generation quantum yield which is about 4× that of the porphyrin system alone.80 ITO electrodes modified by a layer-by-layer nanostructured porphyrin-[60]fullerene dyad, which acts as the active layer, have significantly improved light harvesting properties.81 In porphyrin[60]fullerene dyad systems an exciplex precedes the complete charge-separated state in polar solvents and the singlet excited state of the [60]fullerene moiety in non-polar solvents.82 A series of porphyrinoligothiophene[60]fullerene triads have been synthesised in which the spacer length of the oligothiophene was varied and separated the porphyrin unit from the [60]fullerene moiety.83 Photonic switching of photoinduced electron transfer occurs in a dithienylpophyrin[60]fullerene triad and can be cycled many times.84 The possibility of forming reversible, non-covalent bonds between, for example, the nitrogen group of a pyridine ligand on a fullerene based dyad and zinc tetraphenylporphyrin has been exploited to form self-assembling supramolecular triads comprising of the [60]fullerene dyad and the porphyrin.85 Similarly, reversible ensembles of [60]fullerene derivatives and zinc phthalocyanine,86 zinc porphyrin87 and subphthalocyanine88 have been synthesised and their photophysical properties measured. Oxygen can play an unusual role in photoinduced electron transfer processes as it can impede charge recombination and therefore acts as an inhibitor of the back electron transfer reaction from a [60]fullerene radical anion to a porphyrin-like cation within photolytically generated radical ion pair states.89

Zwitterionic [60]fullerene derivatives containing a ferrocene donor moiety possess accelerated charge separation and decelerated charge recombination compared to that observed for analogous neutral molecules.90 The same derivatives have been used to construct layer-by-layer photovoltaic thin films in which the rigidity of the dyad system plays a part in the formation of a nanowire superstructure promoting charge transport in the films.91 A ferrocene group directly bonded to an azafullerene (C59N) moiety results in the formation of a neutral molecule in which cyclic voltammetry reveals that there is strong electronic coupling between the ferrocene and azafullerene groups in the ground state.92 Effective energy transfer occurs in pyrene[60]fullerene dyads.93 A two photon active diphenylaminofluorene[60]fullerene dyad has been synthesised.94 A series of [60]fulleropyrrolidine dyads, containing a variety of covalently linked donor molecules, have been synthesised and one, in which a dihexyloxybenzene-thiophene moiety was present, used to prepare a photodiode which showed external monochromatic efficiencies close to 10%.95 Several hexakisadducts of [60]fullerene bearing up to six electro- and photo-active groups function as energy- and electron-transducer systems.96 Solar cells with a power conversion efficiency of 0.37% have been constructed using an azaothiophene[60]fullerene dyad as the photoactive layer.97 Oligothienylenevinylene[60]fullerene containing up to three fullerene groups have been synthesised and their electronic properties investigated.98 Anthracene derivatives bearing π-extended tetrathiafulvalene systems have been used in a Diels–Alder reaction with [60]fullerene resulting in the formation of thermally reversible donor-acceptor materials which function as fluorescence switches.99 Efficient photoinduced charge separation was observed in tetrathiafulvalene[60]fullerene dyads.100 Four bis isomers, containing a tetrathiafulvalene moiety, formed by Diels–Alder reaction with [60]fullerene, have been separated and subjected to detailed EPR examination after oxidation and reduction.101 Large photocurrents are generated when gold electrodes are modified by SAMs of [60]fullerene-linked oligothiophenes bearing a substituted tetraphenylmethane having a three mercaptomethyl arm tripodal anchor.102 Fullerene-functionalised gold nanoparticles, which form the central core, have also been synthesised103a,b and used in photoactive cells.103a Photovoltaic devices have also been prepared which contain [60]fullereneoligophenyleneethynylene conjugates, the most active containing an aniline group.104

Poly(aryl ether) dendritic branches terminated with triethyleneglycol chains have been attached to [60]fullerene and the resultant materials shown to have efficient optical limiting properties.105 A similar synthetic strategy has been used to produce liquid crystalline [60]fullerene oligophenylenevinylene conjugates.106 Photophysical studies show that dendron groups present in [60]fullerene derivatives enable unstable species such as the fullerenyl anion to persist.107 The photophysical investigation of the highly symmetrical decakis-adduct of the [2 + 2] dimer of [60]fullerene show that there is a ground state interaction between the two [60]fullerene cages and enhanced fluorescence yield.108

The possible structures of the oxa- (C59O) and thia-fullerenes (C59S) have been investigated systematically using the AM1 method to preoptimise the structures followed by the B3LYP/6-31G* density functional level of theory to further refine the structures.109 A new triepoxide with C3v molecular symmetry has been separated from the reaction of [60]fullerene with m-chloroperbenzoic acid.110 Ozonation of [70]fullerene has enabled the characterisation of several new mono-adducts including two ozonides, C70O3, and four C70O oxidoannulene isomers, the latter undergo photoisomerisation to two isomeric [70]fullerene epoxides.111 Dimeric [70]fullerene oxides, C140O, have been synthesised by the hydrothermal treatment of mixture of [70]fullerene and its epoxide, C70O.112

Several fluoro[60]fullerenes have been isolated.113a–d The single crystal X-ray structure of tetrahedral C60F36 shows that the bonds within four planar six-membered rings become almost equal in length.113b A third isomer of C60F36 has been separated and shown by single crystal X-ray analysis to be a C1 isomer113c which undergoes a room temperature rearrangement into the C3 isomer.113d Reaction of diethylbromomalonate with C60F18 results in the formation of a [60]fullerene derivative which has an 18π electron annulenic system embedded within the surface of the fullerene cage.114 A variety of [60]fulleropyrrolidines have been isolated from the reaction of C60F18 with sarcosine and aldehydes.115 Electron transfer reduction of C60F18 to C60F17− occurs efficiently in the presence of a weak, one electron reductant such as p-chloranil.116 Sulfur has proved to be a useful matrix for laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry and has enabled the characterisation of polyfluoro- and polychloro-[60]fullerenes.117 Several methylated [60]fullerene species have been isolated by HPLC after reaction between C60Cl6 and MeLi.118 2D INADEQUATE NMR spectra have been measured for 13C enriched samples of C60H2 and C60H6 which has enabled the detailed assignment of the spectra.119

7 Polymers

The first example of a liquid crystalline [60]fullerene derivative which has the [60]fullerene moiety acting as a key functional component of its mesogen unit has been synthesised using an organocopper reagent based strategy to yield pentaaryl addended [60]fullerene.120 [60]Fullerene-based polyesters, synthesised by reaction of a preformed bidentate [60]fullerene adduct with either tetraethylene glycol or polyethyleneglycol, have been incorporated into photovoltaic cells.121 Similarly regio- and stereo-regular [60]fullerene polyamides result from reaction of a methano[60]fullerenobis acetic acid with aromatic diamines.122 [60]Fullerene molecules spaced along the backbone of an optically active helical polymer result from copolymerisation of an achiral [60]fullerene bound phenylacetylene and an optically active phenylacetylene.123 Star copolymers comprising [60]fullerene and conjugated polyphenylene units have also been prepared.124 Addition of [60]fullerene, typically up to 0.45 mol%, to polystyrene results in slow diffusion of gases through the fullerene-containing polymer films which improves the gas separating properties of the polymer.125 [60]Fullerenated poly(2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate)s containing 0.6–3.05 weight% of [60]fullerene have been prepared and the [60]fullerene-rich polymers have lower solubility in common organic solvents.126 Significant hydrophobic interactions are present in polymers containing [60]fullerene and assist in the formation of polymer blends with, for example, poly(1-vinylimidazole).127

Solar cells, made of a non-covalent blend of regioregular poly(3-alkyl)thiophene and a [60]fulleropyrrolidine, which have undergone a mild thermal treatment, heating to 55 °C for 30 min, have significantly enhanced cell performance with a three- to four-fold increase in the power conversion efficiency being observed.128 Doping of a new low bandgap polymer consisting of alternating electron rich N-dodecyl-2,5-bis(2′-thienyl)pyrrole and electron deficient 2,1,3-benzothiazole units with a methano[60]fullerene derivative produces solar cells with promising power conversion efficiencies.129

8 Inclusion and charge-transfer complexes

The structure of haloform intercalated [60]fullerene, which was reported to be superconducting by Schön et al,2,3 has been studied in detail by synchrotron X-ray powder diffraction and tight binding band calculations for the surface layer performed, and show that the density of states is smaller for the haloform intercalates than pure [60]fullerene and hence the reported superconductivity cannot be explained.130 Intercalation of molecules larger than CO2 into [60]fullerene crystal structures was thought to be impossible but has been achieved with C2H4 and C2H6 when supercritical fluid (SCF) antisolvents are used.131 A series of [60]fullerene complexes with aromatic hydrocarbons containing flexible phenyl substituents have been prepared and a wide variety of packing motifs observed in their solid state structures by single crystal X-ray diffraction.132 An absorption band due to charge transfer has been observed in the visible region of the spectrum for aniline complexes of [70]fullerene.133

p-Phenylcalix[5]arene shows a strong and selective binding for [60]fullerene, [70]fullerene is not complexed.134 A double bridged calix[5]arene having two bipyridine groups in the bridge only efficiently complexes with [70]fullerene when Cu+ is present and complexes with the bipyridine units (Fig. 1).135 An anomalous electrochemically generated blue-green emission of [60]fullerene results at the cathodically polarised aluminium/aluminium oxide interface of aqueous solutions of the [60]fullerene-γ-cyclodextrin complex.136 Poly(ethylene oxide) capped with [60]fullerene at one end only forms a stoichiometric complex with α-cyclodextrin in high yield.137 A helical nonapeptide receptor with two ferrocenoyloxybenzyl substituents binds [60]fullerene in the electron rich, hydrophobic and wide cavity present in the peptide structure.138 Substituted triazine molecules form stable complexes with [60]fullerene139a,b and in one example the crystal structure has the fullerene molecules situated in a one dimensional channel.139a

A new attractive fullerene-porphyrin interaction identified in ‘jaws-like’ porphyrin hosts represents a novel supramolecular recognition element and involves an affinity of a curved molecular surface with a planar surface at a closer interaction distance than is traditional for normal π–π interactions.140 A bis-porphyrin, which does not possess a cavity, becomes a good [60]fullerene host in the presence of a Pd(II) complex.141 A porphyrin tetramer with a p-terphenyl axis has been designed and is able to host two [60]fullerene molecules between two pairs of porphyrin molecules.142 A dendritic receptor for [60]fullerene containing six porphyrin units is able to bind three [60]fullerene molecules at any one time.143 A rigid dendritic porphyrin self-organises to form a supramolecular complex in the presence of [60]fullerene.144 Similarly a cyclic dimer of rhodium(III) porphyrin forms a complex, which behaves as a supramolecular oscillator, with the [2 + 2] dimer of [60]fullerene.145 A detailed study of the [60]fullerene:2ferrocene has been published.146 Laser flash photolysis of metal octaethylporphyrins and mixed [60]- and [70]-fullerene systems show that electron transfer takes place from the ground state of the porphyrin to the triplet excited states of the fullerenes.147

9 Organometallic chemistry

Density functional calculations have been performed on a model system, pyracylene, of fullerene interactions with transition metals.148 Substitution reactions of a [60]fullerene triosmium cluster complex have been investigated149a,b and lead to the identification of a new σ-type μ3-η1:η1:η2-[60]fullerene of bonding mode in one of the products, Os3(CO)7(CNCH2Ph)(μ3-CNCH2Ph)(PPh3)(μ3-η1:η1:η2-C60).149a SAMs of Os3(CO)8(CN–(CH2)3Si(OEt)3)(μ3-η2:η2:η2-C60) on ITO or gold surfaces results in the observation of four electron reduction of [60]fullerene.150 An η2-complex of [60]fullerene bearing a carborane moiety, [(η2-C60)Ir(o-HCB10H9CCH2Ph2–B,P)(tBuNC)2], has been synthesised.151 A [60]fullerene derivative, C60Me5H, which has a cycopentadienyl entity within its surface after removal of the hydrogen atom, has been used to form a stable, hybrid [60]fullerene ferrocene derivative.152 A strong electrochemical interaction is observed between a Rh metal cluster and two [60]fullerene centres in the complex, Rh6(CO)5(dppm)2(C60)2.153 Similar dumbbell shaped tungsten and molybdenum [60]fullerene complexes, M(η2-C60)2(CO)2(dbcbipy), where M = W or Mo and dbcbipy = 4,4′-di(butyl carboxyl)-2,2′-bipyridine, have now also been prepared.154 A new [60]fullerene dimer having the two fullerene moieties connected by a diphenylsilane bridge and a single C–C bond has been prepared using a mechanochemical synthetic strategy.155 Time-of-flight mass spectra of silicon and germanium clusters on the surface of [60]fullerene show that clusters of up to four atoms of either Si or Ge are present on the surface of each [60]fullerene molecule.156

10 Fullerene radical, cation and anion chemistry

A conclusive study of the EPR spectrum of a freshly prepared pure sample of [60]fullerene does not contain the ‘spike’ signal and therefore there is no EPR evidence for a triplet state of the C602− ion or a quartet state of the C603− ion.157 By contrast, an independent study claims that the ‘spike’ signal is due to the in situ formation of fullerene derivatives.158 EPR spectroscopy shows that a dendritic 19-electron FeI complex containing 64 FeI reactive centres reduces [60]fullerene to its mono-anion at each of these centres producing an insoluble precipitate of the salt.159 Thermal- and photochemical-reactions of salts such as Na[Mn(CO)5] with [60]fullerene results in electron transfer producing the C60− anion as a reaction intermediate which reacts further to produce the η2-[60]fullerene complex, Na[Mn(CO)4(η2-C60)].160 σ-Bonding between the Co atom and one of two C60(CN)2˙− species is present in the crystal structure of the complex formed between CoIITPP, where TPP = tetraphenylporphyrin, and C60(CN)2.161 At 100 K the fulleride ions, C60−, present in the complex, Cp*2Cr·C60 obtained from co-crystallisation of [60]fullerene and decamethylchromocene, form singly bonded dimers.162 Similarly [70]fulleride dimers are present in the complex formed by reaction of [70]fullerene with Cs in the presence of the bowl-like cycloveratrylene.163 The data from a refocussed INADEQUATE NMR experiment on the polymer phase of CsC60 supports the suggestion that a fully three dimensional electronic structure is present rather than a quasi-one-dimensional one.164 Close contacts between the D atoms of ND3 molecules and the electron rich 6:6 bonds of [60]fullerene units control the intermolecular packing in the superconducting ammoniated alkali metal fullerides, (ND3)xNaA2C60 (0.7≤ x ≤ 1, A = K, Rb).165 Fulleride salts with varying K ∶ [60]fullerene ratios have been prepared from samples of reduced mesoporous niobium oxide and excess K3C60 or [60]fullerene.166 Face centred cubic C703− phases have been synthesised and are not superconducting above 5 K due to the low density of states at the Fermi level.167 The molecular structure of the C604− anion in Cs4C60 is consistent with a Jahn–Teller distortion.168 The magnetic structure of a [60]fullerene based molecular ferromagnet, Eu6C60, reveals that orbital mixing can account for the observed magnetic properties of the material.169

A singly bonded dimer of [60]fullerene functionalised with diethoxyphosphorylmethyl groups results from the treatment of the C602− ion with diethyl iodomethylphosphonate followed by one electron oxidation with iodine.170 NMR spectroscopy shows that the added electrons of C606− and C706− are mostly located in the five membered rings, which are concomitantly diatropic.171 [60]- and [70]-fullerenes have been employed as tert-butylperoxy radical traps resulting in the formation of mixed peroxides C60(O)(OOtBu)4 and C70(OOtBu)10.172 Alkylated [60]fullerene carbocations have stabilities that are comparable to those of simple tertiary alkyl cations.173

11 Carbon nanotube production and purification

The Nanotube Site on the internet has been reviewed.174 Single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWNT) grow efficiently from methane on alumina-supported metal catalysts in a narrow temperature window of 680 °C to 850 °C.175 Four rate limiting processes have been identified for the growth of SWNT.176 A chemical vapour deposition (CVD) study of the growth mechanism of carbon nanotubes provided evidence for the ‘cap growth’ mechanism.177 Nanotube strands of up to several centimetres in length have been obtained as a result of catalytic pyrolysis of hexane with an enhanced vertical floating technique178 and the materials obtained characterised by microscopy, micro-Raman spectroscopy and small angle X-ray diffraction techniques.179 Long semi-conducting nanotubes also result when isolated Fe2O3 nanoparticles are used as the catalyst for growth.180 A continuous yarn of carbon nanotubes 30 cm long has been obtained during an attempt to pull out a bundle of nanotubes from the bulk material.181

Explosive decomposition of unstable organic molecules such as picric acid182 or polyalkyne systems183 results in the formation of carbon nanotubes under the correct conditions. ‘Nanohorn’ carbon materials result when a Ru/Au bimetallic cluster is used as a seed which forms pure Ru nanoparticles on decomposition and which subsequently act as catalysts for carbon ‘nanohorn’ formation.184 Compression of this type of nanohorn material increases the microporosity of the materials.185 Nickel formate also decomposes to form pure Ni nanoparticle catalysts which act as an efficient catalyst for the formation of SWNT.186 Identical molecular nanoclusters based on molybdenum oxide have been used to produce similar sized SWNT.187 Similar control over the diameter sizes of nanotubes has been achieved using monodisperse iron nanoclusters prepared by decomposition of Fe(CO)5.188

A review of two- and three-dimensional alignment and patterning of carbon nanotubes has been published.198 Organised assemblies of carbon nanotubes have been produced by a variety of methods including photolithography,199 a combination of a soft-lithography and surface wettability manipulation of an elastomeric polymer,200 substrate aluminium patterning,201 use of anodic aluminium templates,202 patterning and directional growth using a local electric field parallel to the substrate203 and use of mesoporous silica.204 Carbon nanotubes have also been shown to form honeycomb-like networks when the ratio of the catalyst to carbon feedstock is carefully controlled205a and by pyrolysis of iron phthalocyanine.205b

12 Nanotube chemistry

The chemistry of carbon nanotubes has been reviewed.213 An amphiphilic α-helical peptide has been used to disperse carbon nanotubes in aqueous solutions.214 Similarly reversible, aqueous dispersions of nanotubes have been achieved using starch in which the starch is activated toward complexation by wrapping itself helically around the nanotubes (Fig. 2).215 Gelatin can also be used to form aqueous solutions of nanotubes.216 A variety of metalloproteins and enzymes217 have been adsorbed on oxidised, purified nanotubes and the enzymes shown to retain their activity.217 A pyrene molecule functionalised with an ammonium ion forms water soluble non-covalent nanotube complexes.218 Tetraazaannulene macrocyclic complexes are strongly adsorbed by SWNT from ethanol solutions.219 Substituted poly(m-phenylenevinylene) have been used to selectively suspend MWNT220 and SWNT221 in organic solvents. Short rigid polymers poly(aryleneethynylene)s, which are unable to wrap around nanotubes, have also been used to form organic solvent soluble π-stacking complexes.222 Poly(dimethylsiloxane) has been used as a template, having small channels, for the room temperature assembly of carbon nanotube ‘strings’.223 Controlled self-assembled arrays of nanotubes have been realised using colloidal solutions of SWNT without the addition of any complexing agent but rely instead on the maximisation of the van der Waals interactions between tubes.224 Subjecting a MWNT solution in polyesters to polymerisation under a strong magnetic field results in the preparation of anisotropic MWNT-polymer composites.225 Carbon nanotubes can be visualised when they are coated with fluorescent polymers.226 MWNT-doped polyaniline exhibits an order of magnitude increase in electrical conductivity compared with the pure polymer.227 Similarly polypyrrole coated MWNT has high charge storage capacity, several times that of either MWNT or polypyrrole, and improved response times relative to polypyrrole.228

13 Nanotube properties and applications

Potential nanotube applications274 and electrochemical sensors based on carbon nanotubes275 have been reviewed. In addition, a review of room temperature ballistic conduction in carbon nanotubes is now available.276 Nanotubes have been used in electrode systems to study the electrocatalytic oxidation of norephephrine.277 Independently, carbon nanotube-based electrodes have been found to behave as three-dimensional electrodes as the redox reaction was found to occur at the outer surface of the carbon nanotube film and at the interior surface of the nanotube-based electrodes.278 A nanothermometer has been developed using a nanotube loaded internally with gallium.279 The reduced brightness of an electron beam from a MWNT is more than a factor of ten higher than that of a state-of-the-art electron sources in electron microscopes which suggests that nanotubes should lead to significant improvement in the performance of high resolution electron beam instruments.280 The effects of various gases on the field emission of carbon nanotubes has been investigated and the conclusion drawn that the presence of H2 does not affect the field emission current.281 Fluorescence has been observed directly across the band gap of semiconducting carbon nanotubes.282 Actuator strains of up to 300% in the thickness direction of carbon nanotube sheets is developed in pneumatic carbon nanotube actuators.283 Nanotubes have been shown to be useful materials for damping applications.284 In addition, polypropylene fibres are significantly strengthened by the incorporation of just 1% by weight of nanotubes.285 Direction-sensitive stress has been measured using SWNT embedded in a polymer by monitoring the position of the D* Raman band which is strongly dependent on the strain transferred from the polymer matrix to the nanotubes.286 Nanotubes have also been used as effective fire retardants in polymer materials.287 Transition metal coated nanotubes have been used as effective catalysts in direct methanol fuel cells288 and in proton exchange membrane fuel cells.289
