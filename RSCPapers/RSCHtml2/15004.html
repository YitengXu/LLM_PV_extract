Abstract

Ordered mesoporous carbonâ€“carbon nanotube (OMCâ€“CNT) nanocomposites were prepared and used as catalyst supports for polymer electrolyte fuel cells. The OMCâ€“CNT composites were synthesized via a nanocasting method that used ordered mesoporous silica as a template and Niâ€“phthalocyanine as a carbon source. For comparison, sucrose and phthalocyanine were used to generate two other OMCs, OMC(Suc) and OMC(Pc), respectively. All three carbons exhibited hexagonally ordered mesostructures and uniform mesopores. Among the three carbons the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites showed the highest electrical conductivity, which was due to the nature of their graphitic framework as well as their lower interfacial resistance. The three carbons were then used as fuel cell catalyst supports. It was found that highly dispersed Pt nanoparticles (ca. âˆ¼1.5 nm in size) could be dispersed on the OMCs via a simple impregnationâ€“reduction method. The activity and kinetics of the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), measured by the rotating ring-disk electrode technique revealed that the ORR over the Pt/OMC catalysts followed a four-electron pathway. Among the three Pt/OMC catalysts, the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst resulted in the highest ORR activity, and after an accelerated durability test the differences in the ORR activities of the three catalysts became more pronounced. In single cell tests, the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based cathode showed a current density markedly greater than those of the other two cathodes after a high-voltage degradation test. These results were supported by the fact that the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based cathode had the lowest resistance, which was probed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The results of the single cell tests as well as those of the EIS-based measurements indicate that the rigidly interconnected structure of the OMCâ€“CNT as well as their highly conductive frameworks are concomitantly responsible for the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites exhibiting higher current density and durability than the other two carbons.

1 Introduction

Polymer electrolyte fuel cells (PEFCs), including polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) and direct methanol fuel cells (DMFCs), are considered as promising power sources of alternative energy, due to their cleanliness and high efficiency, the reusability of exhaust heat, and the versatility for mobile, transportation, and stationary applications.1â€“4 However, the widespread use of PEFCs is critically impeded since they require expensive platinum-based catalysts. Furthermore, these catalysts exhibit sluggish kinetics for oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and low long-term stability. To circumvent this situation, multi-dimensional efforts have been made over the last decade to develop cathode electrocatalysts for PEFCs that are more active and stable than the currently most prevalent carbon black-supported platinum (Pt/C) catalysts.5â€“12 One prominent direction of research has been the development of Ptâ€“M (M = transition metals, such as Co, Ni, Cr, and Fe, to name a few) alloy and nanoparticles with a coreâ€“shell structure for use as catalysts.13â€“16 These catalysts generally show enhanced catalytic activity and stability while requiring less amounts of platinum.2â€“5,8,11,12 Another important approach has been to use novel nanostructured carbon supports,5â€“7,9â€“11 which have a synergistic effect on the activity and durability of Pt catalysts. These nanocarbons have included carbon nanofibers,17 carbon nanotubes (CNTs),9,18,19 ordered mesoporous carbons (OMCs),20,21 macroporous carbons,22 and graphene.23,24

Among the above-mentioned nanostructured carbons, OMCs that are synthesized by â€œhardâ€ nanocasting25,26 or by using micelles as templates27 have emerged as ubiquitous materials for energy conversion and storage devices, including fuel cells,20,21 solar cells,28,29 and batteries.30 For fuel cell applications, in particular, the OMCs have a range of advantageous characteristics: their large surface area enables high dispersion of supported metal nanoparticles while their highly interconnected, uniform mesopores allow for the efficient transport of fuels and by-products. Since Ryoo and co-workers first utilized these OMCs as fuel cell catalyst supports,21 a number of research groups including ours have made a great deal of progress in this area.23,24,31â€“50,51â€“71 Significant efforts have been made to maximize the catalytic activities of the OMC-based catalysts, and these efforts have included attempts to control the graphitic nature, pore sizes, and particle sizes of the OMCs as well as to modify functional groups present on their surfaces.

The use of OMCs as fuel cell catalysts, however, still poses a major challenge since these OMCs exhibit low durability.56 In fact, the durability of electrocatalysts, along with their low activity, is one of most critical factors preventing the practical application of fuel cell systems. The degradation of fuel cell catalysts occurs via several pathways. These include the dissolution of the Pt particles, the Ostwald ripening of the Pt particles, the corrosion of the carbon support, and the detachment and agglomeration of the Pt particles.72 The corrosion of the carbon support occurs because of the oxidation of carbon to carbon dioxide under the typical PEFC operation conditions. This carbon corrosion can potentially result in the weakening of the interaction between the carbon support and the Pt particles, thereby leading to the detachment and agglomeration of the Pt particles. Yet, in spite of the obvious importance of OMC-based catalysts, few studies have investigated the long-term durability of these catalysts, particularly in the single cell configuration.58,61

Recently, we reported a new type of porous carbon material, OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites, in which the primary particles of the OMCs are highly interconnected via CNTs.29 We demonstrated that these OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites could be used to make high-performance counter electrodes for dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) and that these counter electrodes could even rival the state-of-the-art Pt-thin-film electrodes.29 The structure of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites is such that it combines the advantages of both the carbon entities and should make them particularly attractive for use in high-performance fuel cell catalysts: the OMCs can provide a large surface area, mesoporosity and interconnected porous structure, whereas the CNTs can function as electrical connectors between adjacent OMC particles, thereby lowering the interfacial resistance.

In the present work, we report the use of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites as highly conductive and durable fuel cell catalyst supports for the first time. The OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites were synthesized via a nanocasting method that used ordered mesoporous silica (OMS) particles as a template and Niâ€“phthalocyanine as a carbon source. For comparison, two OMCs with varying degrees of conductivity, OMC(Suc) and OMC(Pc), were also prepared using sucrose and phthalocyanine, respectively. All three carbons exhibited high surface areas, high pore volumes, and uniform mesopores. The conductivity measurements revealed that the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites showed the highest conductivity, followed by the OMC(Pc) and the OMC(Suc). The three carbons were used as fuel cell catalyst supports, and they could readily support Pt nanoparticles with high dispersion (ca. âˆ¼1.5 nm in size) that were generated via a simple impregnationâ€“reduction method. The ORR activities and kinetics of the Pt/OMC catalysts were measured by the rotating ring-disk electrode (RRDE) technique. The Kouteckyâ€“Levich analysis as well as the H2O2 yield calculation of the catalysts indicated that the ORR over the Pt/OMC catalysts followed a four-electron pathway. Among the three Pt/OMC catalysts, the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst showed activity that was superior to those of the Pt/OMC(Suc) and Pt/OMC(Pc) catalysts. This trend was even more pronounced after accelerated durability tests (ADTs), which were performed to test the durability of the catalysts. In single cell tests that are more relevant with respect to the practical applications, the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst showed a current density that was higher than those of the other two catalysts after a high-voltage degradation test. The half cell and single cell tests using the Pt/OMC catalysts indicated that the rigidly interconnected structure as well as highly conductive frameworks of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites were concomitantly responsible for their enhanced durability and single cell performance. We believe that this work provides unprecedented insights into the durability of the OMC-based catalysts under realistic, single cell operation conditions. In turn, these insights may help in establishing the guidelines for the design of high-performance PEFC electrocatalysts based on OMC supports.

2.1 Preparation of OMC supports and Pt/OMC catalysts

Hexagonally ordered mesoporous silica SBA-15 was synthesized following the previously described method, except the modification of hydrothermal temperature.73 8 g of Pluronic P123 (Mw = 5800, Aldrich), 251.4 g of deionized (DI) water, and 48.6 g of 35% HCl (35 wt%, Samchun) were added to a 500 mL polypropylene bottle, and the mixture was stirred at 35 Â°C. 2.3 Electrochemical characterization of Pt/OMC catalysts

The electrochemical characterization of the catalysts was performed using an IviumStat electrochemical analyzer. The accelerated durability test (ADT) was performed on the catalysts by cycling the electrode potential between 0.6 and 1.2 V at 50 mV sâˆ’1 for 2000 cycles.

2.4 PEMFC single cell test

The electrodes were prepared by a decal process. In order to fabricate the cathode, a slurry of the catalyst was prepared by mixing the Pt/OMC catalyst, deionized water, and 5 wt% Nafion solution. The amount of Nafion used in the solution was 35 wt% of the electrode. This slurry was ultra-sonicated for 30 min and then subjected to magnetic stirring for 30 min. The squeezing speed for the decal process for fabricating the cathode was 3 mm sâˆ’1, the temperature was 30 Â°C, and the height of the blade was 300 Î¼m. After the decal process, the cathode was dried at room temperature overnight. The anode was prepared using commercial Pt/C (40 wt% Pt/C, HiSPEC 4000, Johnson Matthey), and the ionomer content was 27.5 wt%. The height of the blade was 100 Î¼m and other parameters were the same as those for the fabrication of the cathode. After being dried, both the anode and the cathode were hot pressed with a membrane (NRE-212, DuPont Inc.) at 130 Â°C and 5 MPa for 4 min in order to fabricate the membrane electrode assembly (MEA). A single cell (Fuel Cell Technologies Co.) was assembled using the prepared MEAs, a gas diffusion layer (SGL Carbon Inc., Sigracet 10 BB), and a silicon fabric gasket (Taconic Co., Ltd.) and was used to evaluate the performance of the catalysts. Pt loading in the case of both the anode and the cathode was 0.4 mg cmâˆ’2. The electronic load (3311D, Prodigit) and mass flow controllers (3660, KOFLOC) were controlled and their performances were recorded using LabVIEW and the data acquisition system (National Instruments Co.), respectively. Fuel and air with a 100% relative humidity were supplied to the anode and cathode with stoichiometry of 1.5 and 2.0, respectively. The resistance of the single cell was evaluated using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), which was measured over frequencies ranging from 10 kHz to 0.1 Hz at a current density of 200 mA cmâˆ’2 with the amplitude of current being 142 mA. To evaluate the durability of the Pt/OMC catalysts, a high voltage (1.4 V) was applied to the single cell for 3 h, and the polarization curve was determined and EIS was performed again.

3 Results and discussion

3.1 Synthesis and characterization of OMC supports

OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites and two hexagonally ordered CMK-3-type OMCs with varying degrees of conductivity were synthesized and used as supports for PEFC cathode catalysts, and their catalytic activities and durabilities in half cell and single cell configurations were evaluated. The OMC(Suc) carbon was synthesized via the liquid-phase impregnation of sucrose while the OMC(Pc) carbon and the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites were prepared by a solid-state method using Pc and NiPc, respectively.

The morphology and porous structure of the SBA-15 template as well as those of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites and the two OMCs were determined from their SEM and TEM images (Fig. 1 and 2). The SEM image of the SBA-15 template (Fig. 1(a)) shows sphere-like particles, with their diameter around 1 Î¼m. It is noted that the obtained SBA-15 particles were sphere-like when the reaction mixture used to prepare SBA-15 was aged without being stirred at a low temperature (35 Â°C), whereas continuous stirring at 35 Â°C yielded elongated, fiber-like SBA-15 particles that were a few tens of micrometers in length.75 A TEM image of the SBA-15 particles (Fig. 2(a)) taken along the pores shows uniform mesopores with a diameter of 11 nm in a hexagonal arrangement. The SEM images of the OMC(Suc) and OMC(Pc) (Fig. 1(b) and (c)) show that the original shapes of the SBA-15 templates, which were sphere-like, were preserved after the replication process. The TEM images of these two carbons (Fig. 2(b) and (c)) clearly show a hexagonal array of uniform carbon nanorods, indicating that the pores and silica walls of the SBA-15 template were faithfully replicated into the carbon frameworks and pores of the OMCs, respectively. In the case of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites, the use of NiPc as a carbon source led to the generation of the sphere-like OMC particles as well as the CNTs, as demonstrated previously (Fig. 1(d) and 2(dâ€“f)).29 The TEM image of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites (Fig. 2d) also reveals that the CNTs are connected to adjacent OMC particles. A closer observation of the interface between the OMC particles and the CNTs (Fig. 2(e) and (f)) showed that the terminal parts of the CNTs were embedded within the OMC frameworks. The formation of CNTs in the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites can be attributed to the in situ generation of Ni particles during the high-temperature pyrolysis step. The TEM image of the carbon/SBA-15 composite formed using a NiPc precursor (Fig. S1â€ ) shows the formation of Ni particles, which were subsequently etched away during the removal of the SBA-15 template by HF. Therefore, it is likely that the Ni particles served as catalysts for the formation of the multi-walled CNTs, as reported previously.76 The unique structure of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites would result in the lowering of the interfacial resistance, which, in turn, would lead to the efficient transport of electrons across the OMC particles.

Previously, alternative approaches towards composite structures composed of OMC and CNTs were reported.78,79 Pak and co-workers employed a physical mixture of OMS and CNT as a template to prepare OMCâ€“CNT composites.78 Zhao and co-workers used a multi-step, sequential process to prepare OMCâ€“CNT composites, which consists of (i) preparation of the carbon/OMS composite, (ii) deposition of a metal catalyst on the external surface of the carbon/OMS composite, (iii) growth of CNTs catalyzed by the metal catalyst, and (iv) the removal of the OMS template and catalyst to generate OMCâ€“CNT composites. Compared to these previous works, our method for the preparation of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites is simple in that the carbon/OMS and CNT structures could be generated simultaneously from a single precursor, NiPc. Furthermore, the CNTs in our OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites are rigidly embedded within the OMC frameworks and interconnect adjacent OMC particles, as confirmed by their SEM and TEM images. These structural features make the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites as rigidly interconnected and highly conductive scaffolds, which should be advantageous for energy conversion and storage applications.

3.2 Preparation and characterization of Pt/OMC catalysts

The three carbons, the OMC(Suc), the OMC(Pc), and the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites, were used as catalyst supports for Pt nanoparticles. The Pt/OMC catalysts were prepared via an impregnation of a Pt precursor, which was followed by hydrogen reduction. The nominal content of Pt was controlled to be 20 wt%. Fig. 6 shows TEM images of the Pt/OMC catalysts and the particle size distributions of the Pt nanoparticles in these catalysts. The TEM images clearly revealed that the Pt nanoparticles were homogeneously distributed on the carbon nanorods in all three Pt/OMC samples. In the case of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst, the Pt nanoparticles were also formed on the CNTs, but the density of particles was rather low owing to the inert nature of the surfaces of the CNTs with respect to the anchoring Pt nanoparticles. The average sizes of the Pt nanoparticles, calculated using approximately 200 particles, were 1.3, 1.5, and 1.2 nm for the OMC(Suc), the OMC(Pc), and the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites, respectively. The wide-angle XRD patterns of the Pt/OMC catalysts are displayed in Fig. 7. Each catalyst showed three broad peaks, around 40Â°, 66Â°, and 81Â°, respectively, which could be indexed to the (111), (200), and (222) planes of the face-centered-cubic structure of Pt. However, it was impossible to estimate the size of Pt nanoparticles because the peaks were too broad. It should be noted that Pt nanoparticles smaller than 2 nm in size could be generated via a simple incipient wetness-H2 reduction method. The extremely high dispersion achieved in the case of our catalysts could be ascribed to the large surface area of the OMCs as this provided numerous surface nucleation sites for the growth of the Pt nanoparticles. We had previously demonstrated that Pt nanoparticles of 3 nm in size could be supported on the OMC supports, even when the Pt loading was as high as 60 wt%.36,49 The structural integrities and porosities of the Pt/OMC catalysts were determined from their small-angle XRD patterns and nitrogenâ€“adsorption isotherms. 3.3 Electrochemical activities of Pt/OMC catalysts before and after the ADTs

The electrochemical properties of the Pt/OMC catalysts were investigated by CV and linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) for the ORR (Fig. 8 and 9). The CV and LSV measurements were made before and after the ADTs. The CV measurements of the Pt/OMC catalysts were made using N2-purged 0.1 M HClO4 solution over a scan range of 0.05 to 1.2 V (Fig. 8). The three Pt/OMC catalysts exhibited similar oxidation and reduction peaks, with the shapes of the peaks being slightly different. One distinctive difference was that the current in the Pt/OMC(Suc) catalyst in the double layer region was higher than those in the case of other two catalysts. This was due to its large BET surface area (see Table 1). Two hydrogen oxidation peaks, indicating the oxidative desorption of the adsorbed hydrogen, were observed between 0.05 and 0.4 V. 3.4 PEMFC single cell test and EIS characterization of catalysts

The activities and durabilities of Pt/OMC catalysts were investigated further in a single cell configuration, as this configuration can provide deeper insights into the practical applicability of the Pt/OMC catalysts. MEAs using the Pt/OMCs as cathode catalysts were fabricated for this purpose. Since the same commercial Pt/C catalyst was used as the anode in all the MEAs used, any differences in PEMFC performances could be attributed solely to the ORR activity at the cathode. The polarization curves for the PEMFCs in the single cell configuration were determined at 70 Â°C using H2 and air as the fuel and oxidant, respectively (Fig. 11 and Table 2). The open circuit voltages of the single cells were similar for all three Pt/OMC catalysts and were around 0.9 V. This was consistent with the results of the ORR activity measurements using a half cell configuration. The three MEAs showed similar current and power densities with only marginal differences: the Pt/OMC(Pc)-based MEA showed the highest values, followed by the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based MEA and the Pt/OMC(Suc)-based MEA. We believe that at an initial stage of single cell tests the difference in the structure of OMC-based supports had no significant effect in the PEMFC performances. The current density at 0.6 V and maximum power density in the case of the Pt/OMC(Pc) catalyst were 610 mA cmâˆ’2 and 400 mW cmâˆ’2, 560 mA cmâˆ’2 and 360 mW cmâˆ’2 for the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst, and 490 mA cmâˆ’2 and 340 mW cmâˆ’2 for the Pt/OMC(Suc) catalyst.

The durabilities of the Pt/OMC catalyst-based PEMFCs were investigated by determining the single cell polarization curves after the application of a high voltage (1.4 V) for 3 h in order to degrade the catalysts. A significant corrosion of carbons occurs at 1.4 V, and hence the fast comparison of the durability of the single cells is possible under this condition. After the high-voltage degradation tests, a clear difference in the polarization curves of the MEAs could be observed (dashed lines in Fig. 11). The Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based MEA exhibited a current density at 0.6 V that was greater than those of the Pt/OMC(Pc)- and Pt/OMC(Suc)-based MEA by 50 to 115%. The current densities at 0.6 V and maximum power densities of the MEAs after the high-voltage degradation tests were 300 mA cmâˆ’2 and 220 mW cmâˆ’2 in the case of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst, 200 mA cmâˆ’2 and 160 mW cmâˆ’2 for the Pt/OMC(Pc) catalyst, and 140 mA cmâˆ’2 and 100 mW cmâˆ’2 for the Pt/OMC(Suc) catalyst. It is likely that the markedly superior single cell current density of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based MEA was due to the unique structure of the OMCâ€“CNT supports. In the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites, the CNTs can function as rigid electrical connectors that could maintain the interconnection of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites under the harsh conditions of the high-voltage degradation test. Furthermore, the CNTs can lower the interfacial resistance between the OMC particles.

To further explore the origin of distinctively different single cell polarizations, electrochemical impedance spectra of the cells were obtained before and after the high-voltage degradation test. The Nyquist plots for the PEMFCs with the Pt/OMC cathodes are shown in Fig. 12, and their ohmic (Rs) and charge-transfer (Rct) resistance values are listed in Table 2. The ohmic and charge-transfer resistances of the three Pt/OMC cathodes before the durability tests were similar. This is indicated by the fact that the sizes of the half circles in the Nyquist plots are similar. After the high-voltage degradation tests, the Nyquist plots of the PEMFCs showed clear differences, with the plot of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based MEA having the smallest circle. The charge-transfer resistance of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT-based MEA was lower than those of the MEAs based on the Pt/OMC(Pc) and Pt/OMC(Suc) catalysts by a factor of 2. This difference can be directly correlated with the differences in the respective current densities of the catalysts as determined from their polarization curves. From these results, it is clearly shown that the conductivity of the carbon support system, including the conductivity of frameworks of carbon support as well as interfacial conductivity between the primary particles of carbon support, can significantly affect single cell performance of MEAs based on the carbon-supported catalysts.

4 Conclusions

In the present study, we systematically investigated the use of three OMC-based supports that exhibited varying degrees of conductivities (namely, OMC(Suc), OMC(Pc), and OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites) for applications in PEMFCs. In particular, we explored the use of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites as a fuel cell catalyst support for the first time. The three OMC-based supports were prepared via a nanocasting strategy and showed large surface areas and pore volumes as well as well-developed mesoporosities. Among the three carbons, the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites showed the highest conductivity, followed by OMC(Pc) and OMC(Suc). The three carbons could support highly dispersed Pt nanoparticles (1.5 nm). The ORR activities and kinetics of the Pt/OMC catalysts measured by the RRDE method revealed that the ORR activity of the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst was higher than those of the Pt/OMC(Suc) and Pt/OMC(Pc) catalysts. This trend was even more pronounced after the ADTs. In single cell tests, the Pt/OMCâ€“CNT catalyst also showed a markedly superior current density to other two catalysts after the high-voltage degradation tests. We suppose that the rigidly interconnected structure and the highly conductive frameworks of the OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites are concomitantly responsible for its enhanced conductivity, activity, and durability. We believe that the results of this study provide unprecedented insights into the durability of the OMC-based catalysts under realistic, single cell operation conditions. These insights may, in turn, be used to establish the guidelines for the design of high performance PEFC electrocatalysts based on OMC supports.

It is worth noting that the hybridization of two different carbon-based building blocks has emerged recently as a novel strategy for enhancing the activity and durability of fuel cell catalysts. For instance, Huang and co-workers recently reported that the durability of a graphene oxide-supported Pt catalyst can be significantly improved through its hybridization with carbon black particles.80 Our OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites synergistically combine the advantages of the OMCs and CNTs, thereby showing enhanced activity and durability over the OMC-only supports. We believe that when considering the results of our previous study,29 wherein we reported the use of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites for high performance counter electrodes for DSSCs, the results of this study demonstrate the versatility of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites as general platform materials for energy conversion and storage devices. Hence, the use of OMCâ€“CNT nanocomposites in a wider range of applications is envisaged.
