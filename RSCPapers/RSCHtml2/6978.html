Abstract

The electrochemical kinetics of the electrode material plays a crucial role in the development of various energy storage devices such as batteries, supercapacitors, and hybrid supercapacitors. Battery-type hybrid supercapacitors are envisaged as excellent candidates to bridge the performance gap between supercapacitors and batteries. Due to its open pore framework structure and more structural stability, porous cerium oxalate decahydrate (Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O) is found here to be a potential energy storage material partly because of the presence of planer oxalate anions (C2O42â). Superior specific capacitance equivalent to 78 mA h gâ1 (capacitance: 401 F gâ1) at 1 A gâ1 in the potential window of â0.3 to 0.5 V was observed in an aqueous 2 M KOH electrolyte. The predominant pseudocapacitance mechanism seems to operate because of the high charge storage capacity of the electrode as intercalative (diffusion control) and surface control charges stored by the porous anhydrous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O, which were close to 48% and 52%, respectively, at a 10 mV sâ1 scan rate. Further, in the full cell asymmetric supercapacitor (ASC) mode in which porous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O is the positive electrode and activated carbon (AC) is the negative electrode, at the operating potential window of 1.5 V, the highest specific energy of 96.5 W h kgâ1 and a specific power of â¼750 W kgâ1 at 1 A gâ1 current rate and a high power density of 1453 W kgâ1, the hybrid supercapacitor still attains an energy density of 10.58 W h kgâ1 at a 10 A gâ1 current rate, which was obtained with a high cyclic stability. The detailed electrochemical studies confirm a high cyclic stability and a superior electrochemical charge storage property of porous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O making it a potential pseudocapacitive electrode for use in large energy storage applications.

Introduction

Sustainable renewable energy such as solar energy, wind energy, and tidal energy is now crucial for solving the fossil fuel crisis and the greenhouse effect.1,2 A reliable electrochemical highly-efficient energy storage/conversion device plays an important role in the practical application of that renewable energy. Supercapacitors are the most promising electrochemical energy storage device when compared to other electrochemical devices (such as batteries) because of their fast chargeâdischarge cycle, high power density, and long cycle life.3â5 The electrical double-layer capacitors (EDLC) and the pseudocapacitors are major energy storage processes that are required for supercapacitor applications. Electrostatically, the charges are adsorbed at the electrodeâelectrolyte interface for the EDLC, and the energy is stored via the surface redox reaction (faradaic law because of the charge transformation reaction involved) and intercalation of ions on the electrode surface (electrosorption/desorption) for the pseudocapacitors.6,7 Now more interest has been given to constructing the battery-type supercapacitor hybrid device (BSH), because of its higher energy density capabilities than that of the EDLC, and higher power density than that of the rechargeable batteries.8 Ruthenium(IV) oxide (RuO2) is an excellent material, that was the first reported material used for pseudocapacitor applications.9 Manganese oxide (MnO2Â·xH2O) was used as a capacitor in a neutral electrolyte.10 Other transition metal oxides and non-oxide-based materials have also been used as pseudocapacitive charge storage materials.11â17

According to the charge storage mechanism, pseudocapacitive electrodes are able to access different oxidation states for the redox charge transfer/interactions of ions (K+, Li+, and Na+) to achieve a higher energy density compared to that of EDLC in a basic/neutral electrolyte.18â28 The asymmetrical cell gives a higher energy density performance because the capacitor component stores electrochemical energy using electrostatic force, and the battery component enhances the electron transfer/ion intercalation in the hybrid electrode system, and performs better charge transfer reactions at high rates.29

Metalâorganic frameworks (MOFs) have an interesting open framework structure where materials are constructed by joining metal-containing units with organic linkers, using strong bonds with permanent porosity.23 Highly porous metal oxalate frameworks show battery-type faradaic pseudocapacitive characteristics. A reversible surface redox/ion intercalation reaction mechanism was observed for charge storage on a material containing an active redox couple for charge transfer.30â38 In this work, we have successfully synthesised hydrated Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O. An electrochemical study was carried out using an aqueous electrolyte for pseudocapacitor applications. Porous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O was synthesised by single-step process, and after co-precipitation, the synthesised power was dried at 80 Â°C for 3 h. The specific capacity value was 78 mA h gâ1 (capacitance: 401 F gâ1) at 1 A gâ1 for a porous anhydrous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O electrode in an aqueous 2 M KOH electrolyte. Furthermore, the aqueous ASCs were fabricated, where porous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O was made as a positive electrode and AC was used as a negative electrode. The highest specific energy was 96.5 W h kgâ1 and the specific power was â¼750 W kgâ1 at 1 A gâ1, by using the combination of porous anhydrous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O and AC to give high cyclic stability. Synthesis, characterisations and the detailed electrochemistry of the developed porous Ce2(C2O4)3Â·10H2O electrode are presented in this paper.
