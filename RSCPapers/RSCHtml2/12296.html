Abstract

Since the first isolation of graphene, new classes of two-dimensional (2D) materials have offered fascinating platforms for fundamental science and technology explorations at the nanometer scale. In particular, 2D transition metal dichalcogenides (TMD) such as MoS2 and WSe2 have been intensely investigated due to their unique electronic and optical properties, including tunable optical bandgaps, directâ€“indirect bandgap crossover, strong spinâ€“orbit coupling, etc., for next-generation flexible nanoelectronics and nanophotonics applications. On the other hand, organics have always been excellent materials for flexible electronics. A plethora of organic molecules, including donors, acceptors, and photosensitive molecules, can be synthesized using low cost and scalable procedures. Marrying the fields of organics and 2D TMDs will bring benefits that are not present in either material alone, enabling even better, multifunctional flexible devices. Central to the realization of such devices is a fundamental understanding of the organicâ€“2D TMD interface. Here, we review the organicâ€“2D TMD interface from both chemical and physical perspectives. We discuss the current understanding of the interfacial interactions between the organic layers and the TMDs, as well as the energy level alignment at the interface, focusing in particular on surface charge transfer and electronic screening effects. Applications from the literature are discussed, especially in optoelectronics and pâ€“n hetero- and homo-junctions. We conclude with an outlook on future scientific and device developments based on organicâ€“2D TMD heterointerfaces.

1 Introduction

Two-dimensional (2D) materials have attracted tremendous interest over the last decade due to their novel and intriguing properties with a wide range of potential applications for next-generation electronics and photonics.1â€“4 Being atomically thin, they provide unprecedented access to the exploration of fundamental interface science at the nanoscale, which is essential for developments in device miniaturization. 2D materials are also of special interest in flexible electronics and optoelectronics applications. As the first member in the 2D family, graphene is chemically inert and exhibits fascinating electronic and optical properties.5,6 However, because of the absence of a natural bandgap, graphene has limited applications in electronic devices such as field-effect transistors (FETs) which require high on/off switching ratios. Although a small bandgap (few hundred meV) in graphene can be opened by chemical functionalization,7 stress,8 electric field9 and other methods,10 a better approach is to search for other 2D semiconductor materials with diverse electronic bandgaps. In contrast to graphene, transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) are chemically versatile and offer a broad range of semiconducting bandgaps depending on crystal symmetry, composition and phase.4,11 The recent emergence of different 2D TMD materials has led to a rapid burgeoning of the field.

One major challenge in the field of 2D TMDs is the reliable and scalable synthesis of defect free 2D TMD materials. This is in stark contrast to the well-established field of organics â€“ traditionally a key player in the field of flexible electronics. Molecules with targeted electronic and optical properties can be controllably synthesized, and several of these molecules can even exhibit photo-, electron-, electric-, chemical- and/or temperature-induced conformational changes12 (e.g., azobenzene and derivatives13). These molecules are used to make single-molecule tunnel junctions,14 switches,15 and diodes,16 as well as scalable organic thin film devices17,18 that are low-cost, durable and flexible. The greatest success story of organic devices is that of organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), which are already used in cell-phone displays and other applications. Organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) are also widely studied, but generally suffer from poor mobility.19,20

Since both 2D TMDs and organics are important for next generation flexible devices, it is natural to ask what would happen when we marry the two fields. Ideally, this would lead to a superior generation of flexible devices that would benefit from advantageous properties of both materials. At the very least, the sheer number of organics currently available would provide an interesting playground to investigate the properties of organicâ€“2D TMD heterostructures and greatly expand the possibilities of these flexible nanostructures. Fig. 1a lists the relative advantages and disadvantages of organics and 2D TMDs. A number of researchers have already successfully harnessed the advantages of combining organics with 2D TMDs. For example, a high performance photodetector was constructed by combining organic dye molecules with MoS2, taking advantage of the high optical absorption efficiency of organic dyes and the relatively high charge mobility in MoS2.21 In another study on the zinc phthalocyanine (ZnPc)â€“MoS2 interface,22 relatively fast (within 100 ps) singletâ€“triplet transitions were observed. This is a potentially useful result for improving solar cell performances using the singlet fission process,23 and was made possible by the large singleâ€“triplet splitting in organic materials together with the strong spinâ€“orbit coupling in TMDs. Organicâ€“TMD pâ€“n junctions also support ultrafast exciton dissociation across the organicâ€“TMD interface, resulting in long-lived charge transfer states.24 In general, organicâ€“2D TMD heterostructures have promising applications as photovoltaics,25â€“29 pâ€“n junctions,26,30â€“36 chemical sensors37,38 and catalysts37,39 (Fig. 1b). Stimulus-induced conformational changes in organic molecules and phase transformations in 2D TMDs suggest intriguing possibilities on the levels of device control that one might achieve with organicâ€“2D TMD heterostructures.

The field of organicâ€“2D TMD heterostructures is arguably a nascent one. While multifunctional organicâ€“2D TMD devices are an enticing prospect, an important first realization is that organic functionalization can dramatically improve the properties of 2D TMDs (Fig. 1c). Integration of 2D TMDs with organics is a promising and controllable approach to modify the properties of the TMDs and yet avoid structural damage, thereby retaining the desired properties of the 2D materials. Organics have been used to passivate defects in 2D TMDs,40â€“43 tune the carrier type44,45 and concentration in the TMDs,46,47 and change electronic screening properties.48 In this way, the 2D TMDs exhibit significantly enhanced charge mobility41 and near unity photoluminescence yields.49â€“52

Although some work has been done to investigate the electronic and optical properties of the organically functionalized TMDs and their performance in devices,53â€“56 there is a need for a unified and comprehensive understanding of organicâ€“2D TMD heterointerfaces at the atomic scale. The energy level alignment and carrier relaxation paths and decay times at the interface are critical for any device applications. Yet, the underlying physical mechanisms that determine these quantities are largely unexplored. Compared to conventional bulkâ€“bulk interfaces, the organicâ€“2D interfacial properties are further complicated by low dimensional physical phenomena such as many-body interactions, and effects of electronic screening in 0D and 2D.53,56 In this article, we will review the current understanding of the organic/2D-TMD interface. We begin in Section 2 with a brief introduction of the basic geometrical structures and electronic properties of 2D-TMDs, and highlight some of their limitations. In Section 3, we discuss the atomic structure of the organic/2D-TMD interface, focusing on non-covalent and covalent interactions. In Section 4, we present what is known about the fundamental electronic properties of the organic/2D-TMD interface, focusing on energy level alignment (ELA) â€“ a critical property for any device application. We discuss interfacial charge transfer as well as the effects of electronic screening on the ELA. In Section 5, we highlight a few examples where organic functionalization has resulted in significant enhancement of 2D TMD properties (in particular the mobility and photoluminescence yields), and in Section 6, we discuss devices based on organic/2D-TMD heterojunctions, particularly pâ€“n junctions. We conclude with a forward looking perspective on future scientific challenges and device developments in the field.

2 Structural and physical properties of pristine TMDs

TMDs in general have the chemical formula MX2, where M is a group IV, V or VI transition metal (typically Mo, W), and X is a chalcogen (S, Se or Te). They are layered materials with graphite-like lamellar structures enabling exfoliation into 2D layers with thickness of a single unit cell and high chemical stability. For the layered TMDs, there are two most common symmetries, hexagonal (1H/2H) and tetragonal (1T), where the sandwiched M atoms have trigonal and octahedral prismatic coordination with the basal X layers respectively (Fig. 2a and b). Here 1 or 2 indicates the number of layers per repeat unit along the direction vertical to the (0001) plane (z direction or c axis). The electronic properties of TMDs can be determined by the crystal symmetry, for instance, MoS2 is semiconducting in the 1H/2H phase, and becomes metallic in the 1T phase.11

Moreover, because of their reduced dimensionality, 2D TMDs exhibit exotic layer-dependent phenomena due to quantum confinement, such as bandgap increase and indirect-to-direct bandgap crossover with decreasing layer numbers (Fig. 2câ€“f),57,58 extremely large exciton binding energies,59 valley polarization60 and so on, which are absent in their bulk counterparts. These diverse properties of 2D TMDs enable a wide range of applications in flexible nanodevices, including field-effect transistors, photovoltaics, photodetectors, sensors, complementary metal-oxideâ€“semiconductor (CMOS) logic and so on.3,61,62

The properties of TMDs have been reviewed in detail elsewhere.2,11,63â€“67 Here, we wish to highlight that despite its many excellent properties, 2D TMDs suffer from significant drawbacks. In particular, it is difficult to obtain defect-free samples of 2D TMDs. From first principles calculations and transmission electron microscopy images, it has been established that the predominant defects in most 2D TMDs are chalcogen vacancies,68â€“72 edges,73 and grain boundaries,58,74 which introduce localized states in the band gap.68â€“70,74,75 These defects are important for some catalytic processes,76 but are detrimental to the performance of TMDs in electronic and photonic applications. In particular, the vacancies limit the charge mobility in TMDs â€“ an important quantity for any electronic, thermoelectric, or optoelectronic device. For example, the mobility of 2D MoS2 devices is typically âˆ¼40 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 at room temperature, more than an order of magnitude lower than the intrinsic phonon-limited mobility.41,72,77 In addition to Coulomb impurities and interfacial roughness, scattering due to sulfur vacancies is thought to play a major role in reducing the mobility in MoS2.41,72

The optical properties of 2D TMDs have been of great interest in recent years. Compared to the bulk, monolayer TMDs have a direct band gap and stronger photoluminescence (PL) intensities.78,79 The band gaps range from visible to near-infrared photon wavelength.2,80,81 Reduced screening in low dimensions results in the formation of stable excitons (bound electronâ€“hole pairs) in semiconducting 2D TMDs, even at room temperature.82,83 Thus, 2D TMDs are being explored for applications in excitonic light emitting diodes, lasers, photovoltaics and so on.81,84,85 However, the PL quantum yield in 2D TMDs is typically 0.01â€“0.6%,16,49,78,80 meaning that the number of photons radiated is much lower than the number of generated electronâ€“hole pairs. This low PL quantum yield has been attributed to defect-mediated nonradiative recombination80 and biexcitonic recombination.16 Another complication is the formation of a different many-body bound state, a charged exciton (trion), in 2D TMDs, due to the interaction between the exciton and charge carriers in the TMD. Depending on the application, the formation of trions may or may not be desirable. The conversion of excitons to negatively charged trions has been found to reduce the PL yield, because the recombination of excitons results in a larger PL intensity than the recombination of trions.52

In this review, we will show that combining organics with TMDs can overcome the above-mentioned difficulties, significantly improve the mobility and optical properties of 2D TMD materials.

3 Atomic structure of organic/2D TMD interfaces

Integration of ultrathin and uniform organic layers with 2D TMD compounds is the first essential step towards understanding chemical functionalization. Many strategies have been used to fabricate the organicâ€“2D TMD hybrid structures, including vacuum epitaxial growth,26,86â€“89 chemical solvent deposition41,48,90 spin-coating,91 stamping92 and so on.38 Among them, molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) is a common approach for preparing high quality 2D organic and inorganic crystals with controllable thicknesses down to one monolayer,93â€“95 while chemical solvent deposition and spin-coating is an effective and low-cost method and widely used to functionalize TMDs through covalent bonding.41,96 In contrast, the physical method of stacking ultrathin 2D TMD flakes with organic crystals (e.g., rubrene microplates) using a micromanipulator92 is less common.

In general, the fabricated organicâ€“2D TMD structures can be divided into two categories according to the interactions between the organic compounds and 2D materials: van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures and covalent hybrid heterostructures. The epitaxial self-assembly of organic layers on 2D materials can be driven by intermolecular electrostatic interactions and the relatively weak moleculeâ€“2D material interactions to form well-defined vdW organicâ€“2D TMD heterointerfaces. Examples include perylene-3,4,9,10-tetracarboxylic dianhydride (PTCDA) and fluorinated fullerene C60F48 on WSe2.88,89 On the other hand, defect sites73 and metallic phases (e.g., 1T MoS2, 1T WSe2, etc.) with high chemical reactivity can facilitate the formation of covalent bonds.97 Defects at organic/2D material interfaces cannot be neglected even for thicker organic layers, because they can significantly influence their electronic and photoelectric properties by facilitating or hindering carrier or energy transport crossover at the interfaces. For instance, it has been shown that defects at the pentacene/MoS2 interface can reduce the hole transfer yield in photovoltaic applications.25,34 Understanding the moleculeâ€“2D TMD interactions at interfaces is key to control the atomic configurations as well as electronic structures for the rational bottom-up design of devices.

3.1 Interfaces without covalent bonds

Due to the atomically smooth nature and absence of dangling bonds in the layered TMD materials, organics can form high-quality crystalline films on 2D TMDs, with atomically sharp interfaces, in many cases without covalent bonds. In such structures, the organics interact with the 2D TMDs predominantly via vdW interactions. The weak organicâ€“2D TMD interactions allow the organic molecules to self-assemble according to the intermolecular interactions, similar to self-assembly on graphite and boron nitride (BN).18,98â€“100 For example, PTCDA molecules assemble in a monolayer on 2D WSe2, with molecules laid out in a characteristic herringbone pattern.88 This herringbone pattern results from electrostatic interactions between the PTCDA molecules and is almost identical to that observed on Au(111)101 and graphite,102 indicating that the interactions between PTCDA and the WSe2 layer are weak.

Selective self-assembly of organics on 2D TMDs has also been achieved by engineering the phase of the 2D TMD, with organics preferentially binding to the domains of the metastable phase. In recent work, intrinsically patterned monolayer platinum diselenide (PtSe2) was obtained by controlling the annealing temperature and Se coverage on a Pt(111) substrate.86,103Fig. 3a illustrates the atomic structure of the highly ordered triangular tiling patterns constructed by 1H and 1T phases in monolayer PtSe2, and the corresponding STM image is shown in Fig. 3b. Unlike monolayer MoS2 which favours the 1H structure, the stable form of PtSe2 monolayers is the 1T phase, which is semiconducting.103 It is found that pentacene molecules adsorb selectively on the metastable 1H domains due to its higher electron affinity, forming an ordered self-assembled pentacene/1H PtSe2 heterostructure (Fig. 3c and d).86 Such preferential adsorption of selected molecular species to one specific domain could be used in dual functionalization for catalysis or other applications.

In addition to vdW interactions, charge transfer across the interface can take place resulting in electrostatic interactions between the 2D TMD and the organics, especially for strong donor and acceptor molecules. The resulting charge on the organic molecules also play an important role in dictating the self-assembly of molecules on the TMD. Using photoemission measurements and density functional theory calculations, Song et al. demonstrated that fluorinated fullerene C60F48, a strong acceptor molecule, acquired electrons when deposited on a WSe2 monolayer supported by graphite.89,104 STM showed that at dilute coverages (e.g. 0.05 ML in Fig. 3e), the C60F48 molecules formed a loosely packed liquid phase, whereas at higher coverages (e.g. 0.01 ML in Fig. 3f), the molecules condensed to an ordered solid phase.104 The formation of the liquid phase indicates that repulsive lateral interactions must be present, without which the molecules would aggregate into close-packed islands due to attractive vdW forces between the molecules. Since the C60F48 molecules are non-polar, the most likely source of these repulsive forces is the intermolecular dipoleâ€“dipole interactions induced by interfacial charge transfer. As the density increases in the close-packed solid phase, intermolecular vdW interactions increase due to reduced intermolecular distances, while the amount of charge obtained per molecule decreases, resulting in a reduction in repulsive intermolecular interactions. These changes in intermolecular interactions with decreasing intermolecular separation drive the observed liquidâ€“solid phase transition. Charge transfer at this interface will be further discussed in Section 4.

3.2 Interfaces with covalent bonds

In contrast to the interfaces discussed in Section 3.1, considerable effort has been made to synthesize organic/2D-TMD interfaces with covalent bonds, where electrons are shared between the organic and the 2D TMD. Typically, this synthesis procedure involves a chemical reaction where bonds are broken in the organic reactant. Covalent functionalization of 2D TMDs are important because covalent bonds are generally stronger than non-covalent ones, and potentially have larger impact on TMD properties. 2D TMDs are also of great interest as nanoscale, flexible chemical sensors105 and here, functionalization of the TMDs with specific molecules is required for selective detection of target molecules. For example, 2D MoS2 functionalized with glucose oxidase was used for the detection of glucose, with the MoS2 layer enhancing both the bioactivity and sensitivity of glucose oxidase as a glucose sensor.106

Pristine semiconducting 2D TMDs are generally inert and cannot easily form covalent bonds with organic molecules. Many of the studies on covalently functionalized TMDs involve the metallic TMDs,37,97,107â€“109 where the functionalized metallic TMDs are used as electrochemical sensors and catalysts. One covalent functionalization method is the reaction of organic halides with metallic T phase TMDs such as MoS2 (Fig. 4a) and WS2.108,109 As shown in Fig. 4a, single layer TMD nanosheets can be obtained from chemical exfoliation using butyllithium (BuLi) solution.97,110 The chemically exfoliated sheets contain a large fraction of negatively charged 1T-phase mixed with neutral 2H-phase. The formation of the 1T metallic phase from the 2H bulk is induced by the charge transfer from the butyl group of the BuLi to the TMDs during intercalation, and the charged 1T phase is more stable than 2H phase in common solvents. The mixed nanosheets are next functionalized with 2-iodoacetamide, iodomethane or other organic halides.37,97,108,111 Facilitated by electron transfer between the electron-rich 1T-phase and the organic halide reactants, the functional groups are covalently bonded to the 1T-TMD nanosheets leading to dramatic changes in the optoelectronic properties of the materials. As shown in Fig. 4b, two main peaks are observed for the functionalized 1T MoS2 (Fct-1T MoS2) (red curve) in contrast to the PL quenching in non-functionalized 1T phase (blue curve). The intensity of the PL peaks can be tuned by varying the proportion of functionalized nanosheets, which can be increased up to 30% (Fig. 4c). These PL spectra together with measured transport properties clearly indicate that in this case, covalent bonds with the organics have changed the metallic phase to a semiconducting one. Interestingly, by controlling the annealing temperature of the resultant functionalized T phase MoS2, a covalently functionalized H phase TMD can be obtained.108,109,111 Thus, phase engineering of the TMDs provides an elegant means to achieve basal plane covalent functionalization of otherwise inert H phase 2D TMDs. Many follow up studies have been performed, including both theoretical calculations112â€“114 and experimental measurements,111,115,116 which suggest that this methodology is versatile, and can be used to functionalize 2D-TMDs with desired novel properties (e.g., ferroelectric114) for future device applications.

4 Fundamental electronic properties of organic/2D-TMD interfaces

Herbert Kroemer, in his Nobel Lecture in 2000, coined the famous phrase that â€œthe interface is the deviceâ€.123 In organic/2D-TMD flexible nanodevices, the interface literally makes up most of the active layers, and therefore is even more so, the device, compared to bulk heterostructures. It is therefore of paramount importance to have a clear understanding of the fundamental electronic properties of organic/2D-TMD interfaces, as well as the chemical and physical processes that determine these properties.

Kroemer further noted that energy band diagrams are central to any semiconductor problem, and that interfaces in semiconductor heterostructures provide a new degree of freedom for device engineers to obtain effects not possible in homogeneous crystals.123 The importance of energy band diagrams, or the energy level alignment at interfaces, lies in the fact that it determines energy barriers for charge carriers to tunnel across the interface. These energy barriers are in turn critical determinants of the tunneling rates across the interface, and the interface resistance â€“ important figures of merit for any device application. In this section, we examine the factors determining the interface ELA and discuss the complexities in organic/2D-TMD interfaces.

4.1 Energy level alignment

Energy level alignment (ELA) refers to the relative energies of the quasiparticle levels at both sides of a heterointerface. As shown in Fig. 7, different types of alignments can be formed for a semiconductor heterointerface, depending on the relative energy position of the occupied and unoccupied states in the semiconducting layers. In the case of organic materials with discrete orbitals, the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) have to be considered instead of the valence band maximum (VBM) and conduction band minimum (CBM). The ELA determines electron/hole tunneling barriers at the interface and the direction of charge transport, and thus is critical for any electronic device application or in solar cells. We note that electrons relax to lower energy levels while holes relax to higher energy levels among the interlayers. Therefore, type I alignments (Fig. 7) are useful for energy transfer, e.g., in organic light emitting diodes (OLED),124,125 while type II alignments are important for electron/hole separation across the interface, e.g. in photodetectors,21 photovoltaics25 and photodiodes.126 The type II alignment can be further divided into two categories, type IIa and IIb, depending on how close the VBM (HOMO) of semiconductor 1 is to the CBM (LUMO) of semiconductor 2 (Fig. 7). The type III interface is less commonly observed, because interface charge transfer effects would typically change it to a type IIb ELA, as discussed in Fig. 8d below. In these schematics, we have omitted band bending effects that are commonly seen in band diagrams for bulk semiconductor heterointerfaces.125,127,128 This is because band bending takes place over long length scales of several nanometers, whereas the organic/2D-TMD interfaces considered here are atomically thin.

In general, the ELA at the interface is determined by an interplay between charge transfer, electronic screening, and in some cases, chemical bond formation (Fig. 8a).128 It is useful to distinguish between the concept of energy level lineup (ELL) before two materials come into contact (using the vacuum level as a reference), and the final ELA at the interface after the two materials come into contact. The initial ELL can be quite different from the final ELA at the interface. The initial ELL determines the direction and magnitude of charge transfer, because when two materials come into contact, charge will flow to equilibrate the Fermi levels. The initial ELL also determines to some extent the nature of the chemical bonds, if any, between the two materials, because in a simplistic picture, bonds are formed by the hybridization of electronic states at the same energy levels. The charge transfer and chemical bond formation in turn contribute to the final ELA at the interface after equilibration. In particular, charge transfer results in the formation of interface dipoles which shift the levels at the interface. For example, in Fig. 8d, two semiconductors initially have a type III ELL before contact, resulting in electron transfer from SC1 to SC2. SC1 thus acquires some positive charge and SC2 some negative charge. The positive charge drives the local electrostatic potential at SC1 down, thus resulting in a downward shift of the levels in SC1. Similarly, the negative charge in SC2 results in an upward shift of the levels in SC2. This results in a type II ELA at the interface. Many researchers have worked to tune the ELA through the interface dipole at bulk heterointerfaces, including organicâ€“organic, organicâ€“metal, and organicâ€“semiconductor (e.g., silicon) interfaces.123,127â€“130 In organic/2D-TMD heterointerfaces, the ELA can be tuned with the orientation, substitutional groups, and chain lengths of the organic molecule, analogous to the discussion for Fig. 6b. The use of organic materials thus provides a wide range of choices to tune the ELA at organic/2D-TMD interfaces, for particular application requirements.

Besides charge transfer and bond formation, electronic screening is also important for the ELA (Fig. 8a). This concept is best illustrated when considering the organicâ€“metal interface, which we will discuss next. While in Fig. 7, we have focused on ELA at semiconductor interfaces, organicâ€“metal interfaces are also ubiquitous in many devices, and are relevant to this review as well, because some 2D TMDs are metallic. In Fig. 8b, we illustrate how the energy levels of the molecule change as they approach and make contact with the metal surface.131 In addition to interfacial charge transfer and chemical bonding, there is a noticeable reduction in the HOMOâ€“LUMO gap due to electronic screening from the metal surface. To understand this electronic screening effect, it is helpful to recall what the HOMO and LUMO levels represent. The HOMO level represents the amount of energy required to remove an electron from the molecule, while the LUMO level represents the amount of energy needed to add an electron to the molecule. The removal of an electron from the molecule (e.g., by photoemission) results in a hole being left behind. When the molecule is on the metal surface, the metallic electrons rearrange to screen the effect of the hole, thus stabilizing the hole and making it easier to remove the electron. This raises the HOMO level. Similarly, the added electron in the molecule (e.g., by inverse-photoemission) is stabilized by screening from the metallic electrons, thus lowering the LUMO level. This reduction in HOMOâ€“LUMO gap can be as large as several eVs, e.g., for benzene on metallic surfaces.132â€“134

For single molecules physisorbed on metal surfaces, the substrate screening effect can be captured within a classical image charge model. This is shown in Fig. 8c where the substrate is generalized to a dielectric medium with dielectric constant Îµ. To screen the charge q on the molecule, the substrate electrons rearrange in a way that is equivalent to having an image charge qâ€² in the substrate. The position of the image charge qâ€² is determined by the image plane position z0 of the substrate, while its value depends on the dielectric constant Îµ of the substrate, as shown in Fig. 8c (being equal to âˆ’q for a metallic substrate). The charge q interacts with its image charge qâ€², giving the Coulombic interaction energy Î”W (Fig. 8c). The resulting image charge energy is equal to Î”W/2.135 This image charge energy is approximately the magnitude of the shift in HOMO and LUMO levels due to substrate screening effects, for single molecules physisorbed on substrates.132â€“134 In recent work, Chen et al. had shown that in the single molecule limit, the predominant effect of electronic screening on the ELA for molecules chemisorbed on metallic substrates can also be captured by this simple classical model.136 For chemisorbed molecular layers, however, additional substrate-induced intermolecular screening effects are present, which result in a larger reduction of the HOMOâ€“LUMO gap compared to the gas phase molecule.136 Besides the formation of chemical bonds, charge transfer across the interface can also give rise to additional (dynamical) screening effects, which bring the HOMO or LUMO levels closer to the Fermi level.137 Thus, the screening effect and the ELA are interdependent quantities (Fig. 8a).

While electronic screening is present in all dielectric materials, the impact of substrate screening on the ELA at organic/inorganic interfaces is especially significant because of the much smaller electronic polarizability (i.e. smaller degree of screening) within the organic molecule, compared to the environment (substrate). Semiconducting 2D TMD layers also have small electronic polarizabilities because of reduced screening in low dimensions. As a result, the quasiparticle band gap of 2D TMDs is also renormalized by electronic screening from the substrate (although less so than in small organic molecules). For example, state-of-the-art calculations showed that both the quasiparticle band gap and the exciton binding energy in suspended SL-MoSe2 were reduced by about 0.1 eV when MoSe2 is placed on bilayer graphene substrate (the optical band gap, given by the difference between the quasiparticle band gap and the exciton binding energy, remained the same).59 This phenomenon can be understood from the fact that electronic screening from the substrate stabilizes the quasiparticles while also reduces the electronâ€“hole interaction energy in the exciton.

Therefore, compared to traditional bulk semiconductor heterostructures, electronic screening (in addition to charge transfer and bond formation) is expected to have a large impact on the ELA at these low dimensional organic/2D-TMD heterostructures. It has been found that dielectric screening from the organic can significantly affect the electronic properties of the 2D material, and dielectric screening from the 2D material can likewise have an important effect on the electronic properties of the organic material.22,48,88 In real applications, the organic/2D-TMD heterostructures are usually integrated with other functional layers, e.g., dielectric substrates/gates and metal electrodes. This integration further complicates the ELA at the organic/2D-TMD interface. In the following, we discuss a few examples from the literature of fundamental studies of charge transfer and screening effects at the organic/2D-TMD interface. We also discuss experimental and theoretical approaches to determine these effects and their existing limitations.

4.2 Charge transfer at the interface

Surface adsorption of strong molecular electron acceptors or donors has been approved to be a simple and effective tool to tune the electronic properties of graphene.138,139 For example, the electron acceptor, tetrafluoro-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4-TCNQ) and tetracyanoethylene (TCNE) have been used to modify the carrier density of graphene by p-type doping.138 On the other hand, 2-(2-methoxyphenyl)-1,3-dimethyl-2,3-dihydro-1H-benzoimidazole (o-MeO-DMBI) was found to induce n-type doping for CVD-grown p-type graphene.33 Inspired by these studies, researchers have also begun to study the effect of strong electron accepting or donating molecular adsorbates on 2D TMDs.

Experimentally, photoelectron spectroscopy (photoemission and inverse photoemission), and scanning tunneling spectroscopy can be used to determine the energy positions of the frontier states and core levels of the organic and TMD layers, relative to a substrate. Subsequently, the shifts in core or frontier levels estimated from the energy diagram (e.g., Fig. 6b) are used as a probe of the changes of the local electrostatic potential, which in turn gives an idea of the direction and extent of charge transfer (Fig. 8d). Recently, the ELA in a metal/molecule/metal device geometry was determined using ballistic electron emission spectroscopy.140 For all these experimental techniques, the substrate or electrodes must be sufficiently conducting to prevent charging effects. Theoretically, first principles density functional theory calculations are very useful for providing atomic scale information on the electronic structure of the interface. Density functional theory (DFT) is based on the Hohenbergâ€“Kohn theorems, which conclude that the total energy of a system can be expressed as a functional of the charge density E[Ï], and minimizing this functional with respect to the density Ï(r) gives the ground state density and corresponding ground state total energy. Thus, the ground state charge density is a natural quantity that comes out of any DFT calculation.

4.3 Electronic screening at the interface

In Section 4.1, we have discussed the concept of electronic screening at the interface, and explained how electronic screening can affect the ELA (as well as exciton binding energies) (Fig. 8). We also noted that this effect is particularly important in low dimensional organic/2D-TMD interfaces. Due to electronic screening, the quasiparticle gap of the organic is smaller when placed next to the 2D-TMD, and likewise, that of the 2D-TMD is smaller when placed next to the organic. In Fig. 8c, we provided a schematic of the classical image charge model which can be used to compute the predominant effect of screening on the ELA for single molecules adsorbed on metallic surfaces and dielectrics (the correction to the levels being given by Â±Î”W/2).132â€“134,136 The image plane position can be obtained using classical methods,145 and in the case of metal surfaces, quantum mechanical exchange correlation effects can be included as well.133 How do we estimate the image charge correction at the organic/2D-TMD interface? The static dielectric constant Îµ required to estimate the image charge correction to the ELA is well-defined only in three dimensional systems.21,146 Screening due to 2D TMDs is fundamentally different. For example, the in-plane dielectric screening in 2D materials has been shown to be highly nonlocal, showing a logarithmic divergence for small distances and reaching the unscreened Coulomb interaction at large distances.147 While significant progress has been made in understanding the effects of 2D screening on exciton binding energies within the 2D material,147 little is known about the effect on excitations adjacent to it, such as the quasiparticle gap of an adsorbed organic molecule. Thus, the effect of electronic screening on the ELA at organic/2D-TMD interfaces is a relatively open question, and efforts are underway in our group to address this timely issue. In the following, we present a recent experimental and theoretical study of the ELA for perylene-3,4,9,10-tetracarboxylic dianhydride (PTCDA) physisorbed on SL-WSe2 supported on graphite.88 Despite the theoretical approximations made in this study, a consistent qualitative picture emerged, namely, that the monolayer WSe2 interlayer provided substantial dielectric screening at the interface.88

Theoretical prediction of the ELA is essential to clarify the structureâ€“ELA relationship, which cannot be easily obtained from experiment. However, quantitative prediction of the ELA from first principles is a non-trivial task. The workhorse of first principles calculations is DFT. While DFT generally gives reliable ground state charge densities and total energies, the energy levels predicted from DFT are generally not quantitatively accurate, resulting in band gaps that are too small. The HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps of molecules, especially, are usually underestimated by several eVs. This error can in part be rectified by the use of hybrid exchangeâ€“correlation functionals within the DFT calculation. However, unless a reliable estimate for screening is included in these functionals,133 the HOMOâ€“LUMO gaps computed by DFT generally does not include effects of screening from the environment. Other commonly used approaches to quantitatively predict quasiparticle band gaps are the many-electron GW method148 for semiconductors, and the delta-SCF149,150 method for molecules. The practical limitations of these approaches are that the GW method and DFT with hybrid functionals are typically computationally too expensive for large interface calculations, and that the delta-SCF approach150 works only for localized systems. Thus, one typical way to predict the ELA at bulk semiconductorâ€“semiconductor interfaces is as follows.151,152 First, one computes the DFT ELA at the interface, taking into account charge transfer and chemical bonding effects. We note that band bending effects are not accounted for due to the much larger length scales involved there. Next, one computes the quasiparticle levels accurately (using GW, delta-SCF methods, or DFT with suitable hybrid exchangeâ€“correlation functionals) for the separate bulk semiconductors. These calculations enable one to know the self-energy correction to the DFT levels in the separate semiconductors, Î£separate (given by the difference between the true quasiparticle levels and the DFT levels). Î£separate can then be added to the DFT ELA, thus giving a better estimate for the ELA at the bulk semiconductorâ€“semiconductor interface. For the organic/2D-TMD interface, a further step (step 2, Fig. 11a) needs to be included, to account for the non-trivial effects of electron screening on the ELA in low dimensions. This correction, Î£screening, reduces the band gaps, and can be estimated using the approaches discussed in preceding paragraphs. A similar approach for the semiconductor/metal interface is shown in Fig. 11b. Here, the metal Fermi level does not need to be corrected because the DFT calculation can give a reliable work function.

5 Advanced TMD properties arising from organic/2D-TMD interactions

In Section 2, we highlighted a few challenges associated with as-prepared 2D TMDs, especially for electronic and optoelectronic applications. Here, we discuss how organics have been used to improve the properties of 2D TMDs (Fig. 1c), particularly their carrier mobility and photoluminescence yield.

5.1 Improved carrier mobility

The transport properties in the as-prepared 2D TMD layers are significantly limited by charge traps, impurities and short-range defect scattering. Sulfur vacancies are the most common defects in as-prepared MoS2.72,153 In addition to covalent functionalization as discussed in Section 3.2, thiol molecules can also be used to repair the sulfur vacancies in MoS2.41,42,154 Yu et al. revealed that (3-mercaptopropyl) trimethoxysilane (MPS) molecules repair the sulfur vacancies by two steps,41 as shown in Fig. 13a. In the first step, analogous to the functionalization process, MPS absorbs onto an S vacancy and the Sâ€“H bond dissociates to form a thiolate surface intermediate. In addition, dielectric screening affects the carrier mobility because it reduces the electrostatic forces between the electrical carriers and charged impurities, and thus increases the time between Coulomb impurity scattering events. A remarkable improvement in the carrier mobility has been achieved in MoS2 by using a high-k dielectric material (e.g., HfO2) as the top-gate.155,156 It has also been found that polar organic molecules intercalated in layered TiS2 can be used to tune the electron mobility of the TiS2 layers up to a factor of three, using selected polar organics with increasing dielectric constants.48 However, the mobility in the organic/TMD hybrid structure is lower than that in pristine TiS2, due to Coulomb forces introduced between the negatively charged TiS2 layer and the positively charged organic layer. Thus, further work needs to be done to utilize organics to optimize the carrier mobility in 2D TMDs. On the other hand, the intercalation of polar molecules helped increase the carrier concentration and reduce the overall thermal conductivity, resulting in a significantly improved thermoelectric figure of merit ZT at room temperature.48 The reasons for the improved thermal properties in the organic/TMD hybrid systems are still unclear, and thus require further research efforts.

5.2 Enhanced photoluminescence

As described in Section 2, there has been much interest in the optical properties of 2D TMDs. In principle, the optical responses of 2D TMDs are dominated by the generation of electronâ€“hole pairs and the subsequent transition and recombination processes, where the carrier concentrations and exciton lifetimes play critical roles. The adsorption of organic materials on 2D TMDs is thus expected to be an effective method to optimize the optical properties of the TMDs by controlling the carrier/impurity density and passivating the defect states as discussed in previous sections.

We first focus on the modulations of the PL with organic dopants. PL results from radiative recombination of a photo-excited electronâ€“hole pair. A typical PL spectrum of pristine SL-MoS2 is shown in Fig. 14c (the blue curve) with two major peaks known as A (âˆ¼1.85 eV) and B (âˆ¼2.05 eV) excitons.52 Both A and B features arise from the direct optical transition at K (Kâ€²) point, and the energy difference between them corresponds to the VB splitting induced by the strong spinâ€“orbit interaction in 2D TMDs.60,157,158 Precise fitting of the PL spectrum in Fig. 14d further reveals that the feature A contains two basic components, namely Xâˆ’ (negatively charged trion) and X (neutral exciton). The formation of the trion, a many-body bound state composed of an electronâ€“hole pair and an electron or a hole (Xâˆ’ or X+), is a result of the interplay between the exciton and charge carrier.82,83 In general, Xâˆ’ and X are observable in the n-type TMDs, e.g., MoS2 and WS2;52,82,159 while all the excitonic species, Xâˆ’, X+ and X, can be found in ambipolar MoSe2160 and WSe2.161 The concentrations of the different excitonic species are tunable via the applied electric field82,160 as well as chemical doping52,159 to control the available carrier species and densities, and thereby to modulate the PL intensity. As demonstrated in Fig. 14c, the PL intensity (feature A) is dramatically enhanced by the adsorption of a p-type organic dopant, F4TCNQ, which withdraws electrons from MoS2.52 The formation of the trions, which is dominant in the as-prepared sample (Fig. 14d),82 is significantly suppressed due to the reduced electron density. In consequence, exciton emission is dominant in the F4TCNQ-doped sample and the exciton PL intensity drastically increases without forming trions.52 Similar enhancements are achievable in SL-MoS2 and SL-WS2 with other p-type dopants like TCNQ and 2,2-bis-1,3-dithiolylidene.159,162 In contrast, n-type dopants, e.g., nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NaDH), result in a PL decrease by donating electrons to the MoS2 to increase the likelihood of trion formation.52

2D TMDs exhibit extremely low quantum efficiency below 1% due to a large number of defect sites.29,108,160,163 That is, instead of the radiative recombination which is desired for many optoelectronic applications, the dominant mechanism is defect-assisted recombination in which electrons and holes are mainly captured by defects via Auger scattering.80 PL quantum yield (QY) here is defined as the ratio of the population of photons the material radiates over the population of the total generated electronâ€“hole pairs. By treatment with an organic superacid, bis(trifluoromethane) (TFSI), Amani et al. managed to eliminate the defect-mediated nonradiative recombination in MoS2.49 As shown in Fig. 14eâ€“g, the PL intensity of a TFSI-treated MoS2 monolayer has a 190-fold increase in magnitude without a visible change in the peak shape, indicating a significant improvement in the sample quality. A maximum QY >95% was extracted from the pump-power dependence of the calibrated luminescence intensity at a low pump intensity (10âˆ’2 W cmâˆ’2), and a much longer lifetime of âˆ¼10 ns is obtained (compared with several tenths ns in the pristine MoS2). The near-unity QY suggests that the nonradiative recombination in the MoS2 is greatly removed by the TFSI treatment due to defect passivation, healing, or both, which is not yet fully understood.49

We note that the enhanced photoluminescence properties described above also translate into enhanced electroluminescence properties in the 2D-TMDs, as both processes involve the radiative recombination of excitons.164 Thus, organics can enhance the efficiency of 2D TMDs as excitonic light-emitting diodes.

6 Organic/2D-TMD devices

In Section 5, we discussed how organics can be used to improve the properties of 2D TMDs (Fig. 1c). Here, we focus on device properties of organic/2D TMD hybrid structures. Several recent studies have revealed the excellent potential of various organicâ€“2D TMD junctions for future device applications.21,26,35,91,126,165,166 Many of these studies have been on organic/2D-TMD pâ€“n junctions with type II level alignment (see Fig. 7). pâ€“n junctions are one of the most essential components for many electronic and optoelectronic devices, such as diodes, bipolar transistors, photodiodes, and so on.167,168 pâ€“n junctions have been engineered from 2D TMD semiconductor heterostructures, with their tunable bandgaps and carrier types offering a wide range of choice.169 By stacking or stitching the selected n-type and p-type TMD layers together, it is possible to engineer vertical or lateral pâ€“n heterojunctions with desired properties for specific device applications.170â€“172 The integration of organic molecules with 2D TMDs enables one to go further, not only in terms of greater tunability, but also resulting in an improvement of device properties. The ultimate goal is to obtain desired optical and electrical properties by combining the advantages of both organics and 2D TMDs, e.g., by utilizing the relative high carrier transport mobility of 2D TMDs and the high absorption efficiency of organics (see Fig. 1a and b).

In this section, we review the recent developments in the field of organic/2D-TMD device applications, focusing on the photocurrent response and transfer characteristics of pâ€“n junctions. In Section 6.1, we focus on organic/2D TMD pâ€“n heterojunctions where both the organics and 2D TMD materials are active components (see Fig. 1b). In Section 6.2, we discuss TMD pâ€“n homogeneous junctions formed via organic doping.

6.1 Organic/2D-TMD pâ€“n heterojunctions

We first discuss the fundamental processes that take place when an organic/2D-TMD pâ€“n heterojunction is illuminated. Experimentally, time-resolved pumpâ€“probe measurements give valuable information on the different time scales involved in these processes, as shown schematically in Fig. 15a for pentacene/MoS2, a prototypical type-II pâ€“n heterojunction.25 For the 2D-TMD alone, e.g., SL-MoS2 in Fig. 15d, the relaxation pathways for the (photoinduced) excitons (considering A-exciton only) include radiative recombination (Ï„4: hundreds of ps), excitonâ€“phonon scattering (Ï„3: tens of ps), and defect-assisted electronâ€“hole recombination which consists of ultrafast (Ï„1 < 1 ps) and slow (Ï„4 > 100 ps) carrier trapping.25,49,80 We can see that radiative recombination, which is responsible for photoluminescence in MoS2, is associated with the longest time constant. This observation is consistent with the fact that PL is limited by defect-assisted electronâ€“hole recombination, which takes place much faster, thus explaining the PL enhancement from defect passivation, as discussed in Section 5. In the pentacene/MoS2 heterojunction, an additional decay pathway exists for the intralayer exciton within the TMD (Fig. 15d). Specifically, the type-II alignment at the interface favours ultrafast hole transfer from the photoexcited TMD layer (e.g., MoS2) to the organic layer (e.g., pentacene) or vice versa (Fig. 15f) (Ï„2 = 6.7 ps < Ï„1 â‰ª Ï„4 in ref. 25). This lifetime is much longer than any exciton relaxation time in the TMD monolayer (Ï„1, Ï„3 and Ï„4 in Fig. 15d). The dynamics of these processes is also reflected in the reduced PL intensities for the pentaceneâ€“MoS2 system (compared to MoS2) (Fig. 15b). Conversion of the intralayer exciton to the interlayer exciton takes place faster than the radiative recombination of the intralayer exciton, while the interlayer exciton has a long lifetime, with a low PL quantum yield, due to the spatial separation of the electron and hole.176 Thus, the PL intensity is greatly quenched. 6.2 TMD pâ€“n homogeneous junctions via organic doping

Besides using 2D TMD and organic molecules as p- and n-type materials or vice versa to form pâ€“n heterojunctions, an alternative is to fabricate homogeneous TMD pâ€“n junctions using organic layers as dopants to tune the carrier type in the 2D materials, as discussed in Section 4. Fig. 16d shows a vertical MoS2 pâ€“n homogeneous junction fabricated by Li et al.35 In the as-fabricated back-gate FET, a few-layer mechanical exfoliated MoS2 flake with thickness of 11 nm was used as the channel. The bottom surface was n-doped by a spin-coated benzyl viologen (BV) layer, and the top surface was p-doped by AuCl3. A Cr/Pd top electrode (drain) and a Cr/Pd/Cr bottom electrode (source) were contacted with the top and bottom surfaces of the MoS2 flake, respectively. The transfer curve in Fig. 16e shows a clear rectifying effect on the output current, with the current rectification ratio extracted to be âˆ¼100. Ambipolar carrier transport characteristic is observed in Fig. 16f with a hysteresis window of 60 V, due to the presence of electron and hole transport at the bottom n-doped MoS2 and the top p-doped MoS2 regions, respectively. A photocurrent is also observed at both forward and reverse bias, as indicated in Fig. 16f. Interestingly, a small current near zero gate bias is observable in Fig. 16e and f, which is attributed to pristine MoS2. This observation is consistent with the fact that the chemical doping depth is very limited. The authors further investigated the thickness-dependent electrical and optoelectronic performances of the vertical pâ€“n homojunction devices by varying MoS2 layer numbers, and revealed that the chemical doping depth is 1.5 nm only (two layers of MoS2). Correspondingly, the thinnest pâ€“n homogeneous junction obtained in this way was 3 nm; reducing the thickness below 3 nm results in one type of doping overwhelming the other. Moreover, current rectification behavior only occurred in the pâ€“n junctions with MoS2 films thicker than 8 nm, while a reversed rectification dominated by tunneling was found for films thinner than 8 nm. The understanding of the depth limit for surface charge transfer doping, and the thickness-dependent performance of these TMD pâ€“n junctions is of significant importance for the design of flexible, transparent electronic and optoelectronic devices.

7 Future perspectives

We present a current review of the literature on the organic/2D-TMD interface and its hybrid structures. The current understanding of the chemical and physical interactions between organic molecules and 2D-TMD layers is reviewed, including interfaces with and without covalent bonds. We discuss in some detail the fundamental electronic properties of the interface, specifically the energy level alignment (ELA) as well as charge transfer and electronic screening effects. We highlight unique properties of the low dimensional organic/2D-TMD interface that makes several of these physical effects particularly important and interesting. We also discuss reports demonstrating that organic molecules can be used to overcome existing limitations in 2D TMDs, giving rise to superior TMD properties such as enhanced carrier mobility and photoluminescence yields. Finally, we review the relatively recent work on devices based on organic/2D-TMD heterojunctions, which show promising device characteristics.

We believe that this field of organicâ€“2D hybrid interfaces and devices will progress rapidly in the coming years, just as the separate fields of organic and 2D materials have progressed and matured over the years. The technology developed for both organic and 2D materials can be utilized to synthesize and study organicâ€“2D hybrid interfaces more extensively. In addition to organicâ€“2D TMD interfaces, organic-black phosphorus (BP) interfaces have also been recently synthesized. These organic materials passivate, dope and even modify the electronic band structure of BP by non-covalent and covalent functionalization.163,198â€“202 As discussed in Section 1 and Fig. 1, organics and 2D TMDs (and more generally 2D materials including BP and X-enes203) have many complementary strengths, and marrying the two fields carries huge potential, much of which remains largely untapped. For example, just as dye-sensitized solar cells made an impact in the solar cell community by harnessing the light absorption efficiency of dyes and the carrier mobility in the semiconductors,204 significant breakthroughs may be possible with organicâ€“2D hybrid devices that combine various positive attributes of both organics and 2D materials. Furthermore, combining organics with 2D materials provides more parameters to tune the properties of the hybrid devices, with organics and 2D materials responding to different external stimuli (e.g., photo-induced conformational changes in organics versus doping-induced phase transformations in 2D TMDs). The fact that both organic and 2D materials are flexible materials implies that progress in this field could be spurred by potential applications in the fields of flexible electronics and wearable devices, where the superior properties of 2D materials could replace some components in organic electronic devices.

Another up and coming development that has not been covered in this review is the chemical enhancement of Raman intensities at interfaces between organics and 2D materials, including TMDs.205â€“207 This development holds promise for the use of 2D materials to detect organics with high level of specificity, without the need for covalent functionalization. While the chemical enhancement has been attributed in general to charge transfer effects and/or dipoleâ€“dipole interactions, more studies are required to understand at the atomic level the exact nature of this chemical enhancement mechanism.

Although interface physics and chemistry is a well-established field, the low dimensionality of organics and 2D materials brings new perspectives to the problem. As we have discussed in Section 4.3, a clear fundamental understanding of the electronic screening effects by 2D materials is lacking at present. Chemical bond formation and charge transfer effects at the interface further complicate the effects of electronic screening on the ELA.49,136 Furthermore, the theoretical studies on charge transfer at organic/2D-TMD interfaces have thus far focused on ordered sub-monolayer or monolayer coverages, different from the organic/2D-TMD interfaces present in actual device geometries. A more extensive study of the effects on atomic-scale morphology on interface properties, including the effects of higher coverage, disorder and defects, is required to strengthen the link between fundamental scientific studies and device applications.

In conclusion, we believe the field of organic/2D hybrid structures is very much in its infancy and has immense potential for further development, both on the scientific and technological fronts. Here, we have reviewed what is known about organic/2D-TMD interfaces and highlighted a few open questions worthy of further study.
