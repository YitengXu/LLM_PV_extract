Abstract

Electrochemical water splitting is a mature technology for hydrogen generation. Numerous studies have focused on the development of highly efficient electrocatalysts to produce hydrogen and oxygen from water electrolysis through the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER), respectively. Up to now, the most effective electrocatalysts for electrochemical water splitting are still materials based on Pt, Ru and Ir. Although these materials exhibit very good performance, their massive utilization is limited due to the scarce resources. Therefore, the search for suitable, efficient, inexpensive, stable and abundant materials for electrochemical water splitting is imperative for the large-scale, worldwide, commercial implementation of electrolytic hydrogen generation. Abundant first-row transition metal-based electrocatalysts for water splitting are considered as promising alternatives to those based on noble metals. It has been found that materials combining two metals exhibit enhanced electrocatalytic activity. It is the focus of this review to show that efficient first-row bimetallic materials can be obtained from the corresponding metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) as precursors. The present review describes recent advances in bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic MOFs containing first row transition metals, including bimetallic oxides, bimetallic chalcogenides, bimetallic nitrides and their composites. Challenges in this area and future research directions are highlighted.


Broader context

There is an ongoing shift from fossil fuels to alternative, renewable sources with zero CO2 footprints. In this context, green electricity from hydraulics, wind turbines, solar panels, tides, etc. appears to be a viable possibility that can be deployed at the massive scale needed to provide the enormous required energy in the range of TW. However, implementation of green electricity technology, in contrast to electricity from CO2 emitting power plants, requires vast energy storage systems to accumulate energy at moments of production surplus to be consumed during high-demand peaks. One appealing possibility is to store renewable electricity in chemical fuels (power-to-fuels), hydrogen obtained through water electrolysis being among the best options. To be efficient, water electrolysis requires special electrodes containing catalysts to decrease the overpotential for fast hydrogen evolution and increase electrocatalytic efficiency. The present review summarizes the state of the art on one of the most efficient electrocatalyst classes prepared using bimetallic MOFs as precursors. While electrolysis has been traditionally performed using noble metal electrodes, large scale water electrolysis is not possible based on precious metals and efficient alternative electrocatalysts are urgently needed. In this context, bimetallic materials derived from MOFs have considerable advantages in terms of availability, affordability, efficiency and stability.

1. Introduction

Global warming and climatic crisis have to be solved by the development of clean and sustainable energy alternatives to fossil fuels.1â€“6 Thanks to its huge energy density, abundance, and lack of negative environmental impact, hydrogen is considered as a clean fuel. Despite this promising perspective, a green, sustainable, and efficient method for hydrogen large-scale production is still highly requested. Currently, industrial methods of hydrogen production (e.g. steam reforming) are based on the use of hydrocarbons and emit greenhouse CO2 gas. As an alternative, electrochemical water splitting is a viable technology that can produce high purity hydrogen under mild conditions on a large scale. Hydrogen generation from water could be achieved using green electricity from renewable resources (like hydraulics, wind, tidal, and solar). In a certain way, hydrogen production can be considered as a system for renewable electrical energy storage in a chemical compound.

Electrochemical water splitting involves two half-reactions: the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER). From the theoretical point of view, the minimum decomposition voltage for water splitting is 1.23 V; however, an overpotential (Î·) is required to overcome the kinetic barrier to generate hydrogen at a sufficiently high rate in real electrolytic experiments. Overpotentials increase the energy consumption and, as a result, diminish the efficiency and economic competitiveness of electrochemical water splitting. By catalyzing electrochemical reactions, electrocatalysts are able to minimize the overpotential, increasing the current density at a given voltage. For this reason, an efficient electrocatalyst with high catalytic performance and stability is highly demanded, especially considering the real opportunity of immediate massive water electrolysis implementation. Today, noble metal-based electrocatalysts are used for this purpose (Pt-based catalysts for the HER and catalysts based on Ir or Ru for the OER).7â€“9 Obviously, the commercial use of these materials is restricted because of their high cost and scarcity. Thus, the design of inexpensive metal catalysts for water splitting is a topic of considerable importance, and in the last few years, there has been much progress in the development of such electrocatalysts from earth-abundant metals for water splitting.10â€“12

In general, electrocatalysts behave differently for the HER and OER and their efficiency strongly depends on the electrolyte. The integration of two types of electrocatalysts in a single medium for the HER and OER often results in lower performance due to the mismatch of the optimal pH domains at which these electrocatalysts maintain their stability and activity. Currently, existing electrocatalysts show a high OER, but a low HER performance in alkaline solution. The activity is reversed in an acidic environment that is more favorable to the HER due to the lower potential under these conditions. As a consequence, high-performance bifunctional electrocatalysts capable of simultaneous catalysis of both the HER and OER under the same conditions are of particular significance and it still remains an unsatisfactorily solved issue. These bifunctional electrocatalysts can greatly facilitate the production, while causing a significant decline in costs.13â€“15

Bimetallic materials play a crucial role in many areas related to electrical energy storage and conversion, such as in fuel cells, supercapacitors, metalâ€“air batteries and water splitting.16â€“20 In comparison to their monometallic counterparts, bimetallic materials usually perform better as a consequence of synergistic effects between the two metals, meaning that the electrochemical performance is better than any of the two components independently.21â€“23 Bimetallic catalysts offer much more possibilities for improving the catalytic performance than single metals, since there are many more combinations and the performance generally varies depending on the atomic ratio of the two metals. Among the essential factors, one of the most important parameters is the tuning of surface electron density in the bimetallic catalyst.24â€“26 Another key factor regarding the electrochemical activity is the control of the catalyst morphology, increasing the surface area exposed to the catalysts or inducing a preferential facet exposure.27,28 The latter is mainly attributable to the newly formed bimetallic structure of catalysts, which can lead to strain and surface tension effects that alter the electronic and catalytic properties of the material.29,30

2. Metalâ€“organic frameworks

As a novel class of porous substances, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are the self-assembled network of metal ions and organic ligands through coordination and coulombic bonds.31â€“34 MOFs have attracted the attention of researchers because the wide range of compositions and structures makes them appropriate choices for applications such as sensing, luminescence, catalysis, photocatalysis, energy storage, drug delivery and imaging, adsorption and separation, gas storage, and so on.35â€“41 These materials provide an ideal structural support for preparing advanced electrocatalysts with unique properties, such as large surface area, high porosity and flexible chemical structures.42â€“45 However, the poor electrical conductivity, large crystal size and low structural stability of MOFs have severely limited so far their direct application as electrocatalysts. Nonetheless, MOFs can be transformed into derived materials with high electrocatalytic activity, particularly in the case of bimetallic MOFs.

It has recently been proved that the partial substitution of metal ions in MOFs with a second metal with a similar ionic radius and properties not only preserves the original crystal structure, but also may enhance the performance of the resulting bimetallic material derived therefrom.46â€“48 Thus, materials derived from bimetallic MOFs with a desired structure and chemical composition could be a viable type of low-cost noble metal-free electrocatalyst with comparable or even superior performance respect to noble metal-containing benchmark electrocatalysts.49,50

Some tunable MOF properties of importance in the field of electrocatalysis that are inherited by the resulting derived materials include porosity and high surface area, and processability. In addition, these materials can be obtained in large quantities. Fig. 1 illustrates these properties. One of the most salient properties of MOFs is their high surface area and porous structure. It is confirmed that the high surface area is an important parameter to enhance the performance of electrocatalysts as macropores or mesopores enhance the density of open active centers on the electrocatalyst surface and favor their accessibility. After the thermal treatment process, the MOF-derived materials can inherit special properties, particularly unique particle morphology, higher surface area and porosity, combined with higher stability that altogether can be responsible for the better electrochemical performance of MOF derived materials. For example, after pyrolysis of a layered Co-naphthalene dicarboxylate MOF grown on Cu-foil, Co3O4 nanoparticles with a strong interaction with a carbon nanowire matrix are obtained. The large surface area, improved charge transfer, mass transport and structural stability, together with easy release of oxygen bubbles of this Co3O4/carbon nanowire electrocatalyst, lead to a remarkable OER and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) performance in alkaline electrolytes.51

The ratio and the type of metals in bimetallic-MOFs are crucial to tuning the electrochemical performance of the MOF derived materials to meet the requirements of the electrochemical process. Materials having two or more metals offer much more flexibility to adapt the potential of the active site to the optimal value for a process than analogous electrocatalysts having a single metal. By tuning the Zn/Co ratio in a ZnCo imidazolate MOF, the structural properties such as graphitization degree and the surface area of the resulting carbon can be rationally adjusted.52 In this ZnCo imidazolate, the role of volatile Zn is to create porosity, while metallic Co nanoparticles remain on the N-doped graphitic carbon formed in the pyrolysis from imidazolate. The graphitic carbon residue can inherit the polyhedral shape of the ZIF-8 precursor.53 The resulting Co/N-doped carbon exhibited great ORR behavior because of the high surface area, uniform doping of Co/N and high degree of graphitization.

The framework topology and pore size can be modulated by selecting the metal nodes having adequate coordination directions and linkers with appropriate geometry. MOFs that have the same structural topology are known as isoreticular metalâ€“organic frameworks (IRMOFs). MOFs in this class differ from each other in the length and substituents of the linker or/and the metal composition. Although the increase in the length of organic linkers increases the corresponding pore volume, if the pore size is too large it could result in the interpenetration of networks into each other, thus resulting in a low free internal volume.54,55 Control of the internal polarity and pore size in isometric MOFs can determine the structural and chemical factors of the derived material that are going to be crucial for the activity as an electrocatalyst. In hierarchical MOFs, mesopores and micropores coexist and favor intracrystalline molecular diffusion. A large surface area is desirable for performing catalytic reactions. The pore tunability and large surface area are very favorable properties for electrochemical applications.

A large variety of MOFs can be synthesized by selecting among a wide range of metal nodes and linkers. Also, post-synthetic modification can exchange or remove metal nodes and linkers, enlarging further the possible compositions and structures. Furthermore, the surface of MOFs can be controlled by the size and morphology of the crystallites and crystallographic phases.56 Such flexibility in design and synthesis are the strengths of MOFs that additionally can act as hosts incorporating guests, thus expanding the diversity of advanced composite materials that can be produced.57,58

Ion migration and electron transfer are two important elementary processes in electrochemical reactions. High internal electrical resistance generates high overpotential and diminishes considerably the efficiency of electrochemical reactions. Most MOFs are electrically insulating materials due to metalâ€“oxygen bonds. Many research efforts have been made to overcome this drawback by modulating the electronic structure, including â€œthrough-bondâ€ electron conduction strategies, and by improving the electronic conductivity, including â€œthrough-spaceâ€ approaches, via Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions.59â€“61 However, up to now, the inadequate electrical conductivity of MOFs accompanied by their large crystallite size (typically microns) has hampered their direct use as electrocatalysts. Efforts should be made at overcoming this limitation. In this sense, it could be that the special morphology of 2D MOFs characterized by a high aspect ratio and small thickness of only a few nanometers can aid in overcoming this limitation, opening the direct use of MOFs in electrocatalysis.

Regarding ionic conductivity, there are various strategies for improving the ion and proton conductivity of MOFs, one of which involves incorporating ionic guests to introduce sites that facilitate ion exchange and open an ion conduction mechanism.62â€“64 Proton conductivity has been studied in this class of materials because of the growing interest in membranes for hydrogen fuel cells. Due to the demand for solid electrolytes in energy storage devices, it is predicted that ion-conducting MOFs will attract increasing research attention. The tunability of composition and structural, electronic and mechanical properties of MOFs could make it possible to achieve the goals of electrical and ionic conductivity, considering the vast opportunities in the synthesis and post-synthetic modification offered by MOFs.

Another point to be considered regarding MOF synthesis is their processability and scalability. These aspects are important and necessary factors for moving from laboratory scale to commercial applications. In terms of scalability, some companies have produced these materials on a large scale.65â€“67 These companies employed electrochemistry, solvent-free mechanochemistry and flow chemistry methods to prepare different MOFs. In the mechanochemical method, only a physical mixture of the linker and metal salt is used, producing minimal chemical waste or without the requirement of a solvent or heating. Another method for scalability of MOF synthesis is microwave-irradiation, in which MOFs are rapidly synthesized from precursor solutions.68,69 Electrochemical syntheses provide uniform and rapid growth of MOFs on conductive substrates by in situ generation of metal ions that react as they are formed with the organic linker present in the solution.70 The use of different substrates and layer thickness control may allow electrochemical application. The facile synthesis of MOFs, their flexible composition and variable structure, the low cost and availability of organic linkers, the possibility of using abundant transition metals, accompanied by their large surface area and porosity, favorable reagent diffusion, the high percentage of metal sites, and excellent chemical and thermal stability have made MOFs among the preferred adsorbents and most versatile solid catalysts. One logical extension would be to consider MOFs also promising candidates as electrocatalysts, but as previously commented this has not still been possible in spite of the multiple redox centers that MOF lattice may contain.

Besides MOFs of a single metal, the preparation of mixed metal MOFs in a wide range of atomic ratios has become routine in many cases. Bimetallic MOFs offer the possibility of adjusting the redox pair potential and the structure of the active sites as well as defects to the requirements of an electrochemical process. This flexibility can be used to adapt the structure of the redox sites with improved catalytic properties that is crucial in electrocatalysis, since they could lead to a diminution of overpotentials, resulting in an improvement in HER and OER processes.49,71,72

It has been possible to prepare MOFs of virtually all transition metals. Particularly important are MOFs based on the first-row, earth-abundant transition metals. First row transition metals are less expensive and more abundant in comparison with metals such as Ru, Rh, Ir, and Pt. Therefore, some first-row metals are generally considered as advantageous elements from the point of view of resource availability. Besides higher natural abundance than heavier 4d and 5d metals, 3d metals have lesser toxicity in comparison with heavier metals, which make the use of these metals more practical and advisable in terms of environmental benignity and green chemistry principles.73

As it will be discussed below, MOFs based on metals like Ni, Fe, Co, and Cu have attracted special attention as precursors in various novel applications related to renewable electricity, including electrocatalysis. Moreover, a large number of different MOFs containing Cr, Ni, Co, Fe, Cu and Zn metals have been synthesized now, making use of the rich coordination chemistry of these transition metals with organic ligands.74,75 Among them, several Fe-based MOFs exhibit a considerable thermal and chemical structural stability and have been widely used in catalysis. Another earth-abundant 3d transition metal is Cu. Owing to its ability to coordinate with hydroxyl and its ability to break peroxy bonds, MOFs with this metal exhibit significant activity in oxidation reactions and MOF-derived materials are promising as stable electrocatalysts particularly in the OER process. These unique catalytic properties have made this metal widely utilized in electrochemical water splitting. Ni is among the most active transition metal with electrocatalytic activity for water splitting. Ni-containing MOFs in particular are of great interest when using water as the medium. Ni-MOFs exhibit significant redox activity and strong oxidizing potential. Ni-MOFs also show notable catalytic activity in alkaline media. Co is another transition element with versatile applications in electrocatalysis. Co has various oxidation states and the oxidation potential of the Co(III)/Co(II) pair is close to that of the required value for water oxidation (E = 1.23 V vs. RHE).76â€“79

3. Bimetallic MOF synthetic methods

Bimetallic MOFs are synthesized using many protocols. This section will introduce them shortly. Crystal phase characterization of bimetallic MOFs can be performed using appropriate techniques such as energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDAX) and X-ray diffraction. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) and atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) are employed to calculate the metal composition. Also, the coordination sphere around metals can be determined using X-ray absorption fine structure (XAFS). Deep characterization of bimetallic MOFs requires the combination of the previous techniques and high resolution electron microscopy.80,81

3.1 Direct synthesis

Bimetallic MOFs can be synthesized by mixing metal salts through a solvothermal process. According to the formation kinetics of bimetallic MOFs from different metal ions, the presence of two different metal salts is not enough to guarantee the formation of bimetallic MOFs. Hence, in order to prevent the formation of mixed phases and achieve a defined structure, it is necessary to control parameters such as solubility, molar ratios of metal ions and pH. The synthesis of pre-defined secondary building units (SBUs) as metal node precursors appears to be a promising strategy for the preparation of bimetallic MOFs with a defined composition. Thus, in order to obtain the mixed metal MIL-127, Serre et al. prepared FeIII2MIIO(H2O)2[O2C-CH3]6-nH2O (M: Co or Ni) as the building block and used it to synthesize bimetallic MOFs (Fig. 2a). 3.2 Post-synthetic modification

If bimetallic MOFs cannot be synthesized using a direct method, a post-synthetic metal ion exchange method can be utilized.83 Factors like metal ion valence, SBU environment, MOF lattice and solvent affect the exchange process efficiency. If metals in SBUs have large coordination numbers, then, an exchange process is observed. Because of a higher electronegativity and the formation of more stable covalent bonds, Cu2+ usually tends to be exchanged with transition metals such as Mn2+ and Zn2+. The exchange process is easier for SBUs of those MOFs that have a more flexible network. The exchange process of Fe2+, Li+, Zn2+ and Co2+ in Mn3[(Mn4Cl)3(BTT)8(CH3OH)10]2(1-Mn2+; BTT: 1,3,5 benzenetristetrazolate) in methanol suspension has been reported by Long's group. The degree of exchange was determined using inductively coupled plasma-atomic absorption (ICP-AA). The exchange process was predicted to mostly occur in MOF structures that do not have high stability, but there are reports observing this process in robust MOF structures. For example, Zn2+ exchange by Mn2+ in ZIF-71(Zn) to obtain a bimetallic ZIF-71(Zn, Mn) was reported by Cohen's group (Fig. 2b).84 The metal exchange process can also happen between two solid MOFs, as Cohen et al. reported a solidâ€“solid exchange process (Fig. 2c).85

3.3 Template synthesis

A template method can be used to properly control the composition of metal ions in bimetallic MOFs. This method is illustrated in Fig. 2d for the synthesis of bimetallic MOF-74 starting from an insoluble Zn(H2O)2(dcbcc)n solid as the template.86 The result is that the molar ratio of the two metals in the MOF is equal to 1:1 in the final MOF regardless of their initial value and they have uniform distributions. Additionally, hollow bimetallic MOFs can be synthesized via this synthesis method. These MOFs have advantages such as high active sites and easy mass transport. This synthesis method can be performed in two different ways including self-template and exterior-template methods. The self-template method is a facile method to synthesize hollow bimetallic MOFs since it is not required to remove the template and the method is as dissolution-regrowth. In the exterior-template method, a sacrificial template is employed which has to be removed after the synthesis.

3.4 Post-synthetic exchange

Post-synthetic metal ion exchange is particularly appropriate for the synthesis of core/shell MOFs. Typically ion exchange starts from the external surface going deeper into the solid as the exchange progresses. Hence, bimetallic MOFs with a core/shell morphology can be obtained by controlling the exchange process and selective transmetalation in this method. Lah's group reported Zn-HKUST-1 and Zn-PMOF-2 with a coreâ€“shell heterostructure obtained using this method (Fig. 2e).87

3.5 One-pot synthesis

A facile but challenging method to synthesize MOFs is the one-pot synthesis method in which single metal seeds appear first, and then the seeds grow slower into larger crystals incorporating the second metal. This is a simple synthesis method and needs no further steps for separation. In this synthesis method, the precursors required for the core and the shell are mixed together in a solution. The conditions must be controlled in such a way that the MOF shell merely grows on the core with dissolution. Temperature, time, modulators and solvents play a decisive role in the obtained material. In a report by Zou's group, core/shell CoZn-ZIFs were synthesized using a low Co/Zn ratio. First, the core containing Co2+ was formed quickly and afterwards, a layer containing Zn2+ was formed above it (Fig. 2f).88

4. MOF-derived materials

The high electrical resistance of MOFs and their micrometric large crystals preclude in most of the cases hampers their direct application of MOFs as electrocatalysts in water electrolysis. Thus, the many favorable MOF properties, such as the high percentage of isolated transition metal ions, large surface area and porosity, cannot be advantageously exploited in electrocatalysis. Fortunately this situation can be partially overcome by transforming MOFs into other materials. The types of MOF-derived electrocatalysts include oxides,89 sulfides,90 phosphides,91 nitrides,92 layered double hydroxides (LDHs),93 and monoatomic electrocatalysts.94 These MOF-derived materials inherit the metal composition, large porosity, surface area and even the particular crystal morphology, while exhibiting a much diminished electrical resistance. As a consequence, MOF-derived materials have been used as noble metal-free electrodes for the HER and OER in water electrolysis with comparable or even better electrocatalytic performance as well as greater electrochemical stability than the noble metal-containing electrocatalysts. Due to the immediate application in electrolyzers, recent studies have reported a high activity for many MOF-derived stable inorganic electrocatalysts. As a result of these studies, it has been found that bimetallic electrocatalysts can be even more effective electrocatalysts for the HER and OER than materials containing a single metal. Since the number of combinations of two metals, without counting the range of atomic ratio to tune their properties, is much larger than single transition metals, they offer a wider range to enhance the electrocatalytic performance.95 Thus, not surprisingly, first-row transition metal-containing bimetallic MOFs have been recently found as useful precursors for the preparation of various porous nanostructured derivatives and nanocomposites with good electrocatalytic activity for water splitting. MOFs as precursors of bimetallic electrocatalysts offer not only a wide range of atomic ratios, but also the resulting materials exhibit porosity, high surface area and even a defined particle morphology.

In spite of a large number of research articles in the area, there is no comprehensive review describing the use of MOFs as precursors of bimetallic electrocatalysts with advanced performance for water electrolysis. The present article intends to fill this gap, showing the advantage in terms of synthesis simplicity, versatility and properties that the use of bimetallic MOFs offer as precursors of materials for electrocatalysis.

Several reviews have been published summarizing the recent developments of MOF-based electrocatalysts for the OER or HER. In the last few years, the number of papers with the keywords â€œtransition metalsâ€, â€œMOFâ€ and â€œwater splittingâ€ has increased considerably, which indicates an explosive growth in this field. However, no reviews have specifically focused on the first-row transition metal-containing bimetallic electrocatalysts that are either bimetallic MOFs (very few so far) or can be derived from bimetallic-MOFs (the vast majority). These reports show the advantages of bimetallic MOFs in terms of superior performance and their potential for a simple preparation of first-row transition bimetallic advanced electrocatalysts.

In the present review we intend to summarize the current state of the art and the recent advances in bimetallic first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic MOFs for electrochemical water splitting. For each class of material, electrocatalytic activity is considered and discussed in comparison with benchmark electrodes. The origin of this superior electrocatalytic activity is the high density of metal sites exposed on the surface and the synergism due to the presence of two metals in the same phase or in intimate interfacial contact. The combination of the two metals in the active site adjusts the redox potential of the active site to the requirements of a particular electrochemical reaction. This is something difficult or not possible to achieve with a single first-row transition metal and represents an important advantage of bi-/multimetallic materials. 5. The advantages of bimetallic materials as electrocatalysts

Bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic MOFs show better electrocatalytic behavior and performance compared to monometallic derivatives. This superiority can be related to the factors such as adjusted redox potential and better catalytic performance derived from the synergistic effect between metal centers in the reaction mechanism. In fact, when the second metal is introduced into the MOF structure, properties such as electronic density distribution and stability of the material are improved. In bimetallic electrocatalysts, activation energy barriers can be reduced by optimizing the charge transfer rate and optimizing the electronic structure. The energy of the eg orbitals of transition metal sites of the bimetallic MOFs and derivatives, which also play a decisive role in the electrocatalytic behavior, is adjusted depending on the nature of the metals involved. For example, the NiCo-MOF was employed as an electrocatalyst towards the OER in a report by Thangasamy et al. The electronic configuration of the Co2+ valence shell is high-spin 3d7. The t2g orbitals of Co2+ contain unpaired electrons which can interact with O2, while the t2g orbitals in Ni2+ are fully occupied. This leads to repulsion between O2 and Ni2+. As a result, when Co2+ is hybridized with Ni2+ in the bimetallic MOF, the repulsion between Ni2+ and O2 leads to a partial charge transfer from Ni2+ to Co2+. Therefore, the presence of the two metals Co2+ and Ni2+ in bimetallic MOF causes a change in the eg orbital that improves the OER performance.96 In another report, Zhu's group described a synergistic effect between the two metals of a FeCo-MOF resulting in an excellent electrocatalyst performance towards the OER. According to the experimental and theoretical results, the optimal hybridization of the two metals in the reported structure has led to the excellent electrocatalytic behavior.97

In a report by Kramm's group, a bimetallic MOF-derived (Co, Mo)â€“Nâ€“C electrocatalyst was investigated towards the HER.98 In their study, this group found that the MeN4 site plays a significant role in electrocatalytic behavior and the synergic effect between Mo and Co has an important role in the differences between a bimetallic sample and a counterpart monometallic sample. A hydrogen binding energy value is obtained for Mâ€“Nâ€“C in a bimetallic sample as a semi-filled triangle which is different from that of the monometallic counterparts CoN4 and MoN4 due to electronic interaction with the second metal. The bimetallic electrocatalyst has also exhibited high stability in galvanostatic tests. Bimetallic MOFs containing Ni as electrocatalysts that accept hydride and proton on their surface are very suitable for the HER. The existence of Ni and Mo in a structure can give the material a significant HER performance owing to the synergistic effect. This claim can be confirmed based on a report from Su et al. They introduced NiMo2C@C as an electrocatalyst with ideal behaviour towards the HER in acidic and alkaline solutions.99 Graphitic carbon present in NiMo2C@C and characterized by its signature in Raman spectroscopy enhances the corrosion resistance, thereby improving its electrocatalytic stability.

6. Electrochemical water splitting fundamentals

Overpotential, Tafel slope, and stability are three important parameters in the evaluation of the performance of HER and OER electrocatalysts. As commented, in order to have a high gas evolution real electrolytic systems require the extra potential for the HER and OER compared to their thermodynamically predicted standard values (0 V vs. RHE for the HER and 1.23 V vs. RHE for the OER at pH = 0).104 This difference between the real applied voltage and the thermodynamic value is called overpotential (denoted by the symbol Î·). Another important parameter to determine the electrocatalyst performance is the stability of the activity in water splitting. Stability can generally be assessed in two ways: repeated cyclic voltammetry (CV) scans and potentiostatic/galvanostatic electrolysis.106 In the CV method, also known as the accelerated degradation test, the cycles are performed at onset potentials of the HER or OER regions. After performing several thousand cycles, the LSV curve is repeated and compared with the original curve. An electrocatalyst that has significant stability shows little change in overpotential after several thousand CV cycles. 7. First-row transition metal based bimetallic electrocatalysts

Transition metal based materials are ideal candidates as electrocatalysts for water splitting due to their various oxidation states with potentials that can be aligned with the values required for water electrolysis. The advantages of this type of electrocatalyst can be summarized as follows: (1) high activity and stability for the OER under alkaline conditions, (2) cost-effectiveness, (3) tunable electronic structure and electrical conductivity, and (4) possibility to prepare high surface area materials.

8. Bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic-MOFs

In recent years, MOFs have been vastly employed as precursors in the synthesis of derived materials with remarkable properties in terms of particle morphology, porosity and specific surface area. This includes the use of MOFs as precursors of electrocatalysts due to their inherent porous structure that is partially retained by the derived material and as a source of metal.107â€“109 MOF-derived electrocatalysts can achieve porous and hollow structures by maintaining the porosity of the parent MOF and by releasing gas during the high-temperature calcination process, providing a larger specific surface area and subsequently, more accessible active sites.110 Thanks to their metal ion content, MOFs can form multimetallic materials. Transformation of these multimetallic MOFs can result in metal phases with extremely good interfacial contact or even to single phase mixed metal materials thus, synergistically enhancing the electrocatalytic performance.111

8.1 Bimetallic nitrides

Transition metal containing materials are good candidates for electrochemical applications.112,113 To date, many different transition metal-based materials such as carbides, chalcogenides, oxides, nitrides, phosphides and hydroxides have been used as promising HER and OER catalysts.114,115 Transition metal nitrides because of their unique electronic structures, chemical stability and high electrical conductivity in a wide range of pHs have received special attention recently.116,117 In particular, bonding with negatively charged nitrogen atoms can modify the d-band density of transition metals, causing metal nitrides to have in a certain way an electronic structure similar to that of noble metals from group VIII.118 Also, experimental and theoretical studies indicate that bimetallic materials can be better tuned compared to their monometallic counterparts for enhancing the electrocatalytic performance by tuning the potential of the active site to those required for the HER o OER.119 The introduction of the second metal can effectively adjust the electron density and the electronic structure of the bimetallic material, diminishing the free energy of hydrogen adsorption (a simple descriptor of the electrocatalytic HER activity) and increasing the HER performance. However, the synthesis of homogeneous bimetallic nitrides has remained a challenge due to the uncontrolled formation of several independent phases and the strict requirement of stoichiometric ratios for valence balance.120 The last requirement limits the range of atomic ratios that can result in a single mixed metal nitride phase. Among the different approaches, direct MOF pyrolysis can be used to effectively prepare transition metal-based composites.121,122 The use as a precursor of multivariate MOFs with diverse metal centers significantly simplifies the synthesis of controllable and homogeneous bimetallic composites in a wide range of atomic ratio compositions, wider than other alternative synthetic strategies. Due to the uniform distribution of metallic elements in MOF nodes, the derived composites have unique intrinsic electronic characteristics and elemental structures.123

Besides composition and porosity, particle morphology is another parameter that can also be controlled when using MOFs as precursors. A common observation is that MOF-based derivatives maintain the structure of the parent MOF, hence, exhibiting large surface area, uniform size distribution and enhanced porosity compared to analogous materials obtained from alternative routes.161,162 For these reasons, MOF-derived solids exhibit superior electrochemical activities with respect to analogous materials. Additionally, organic ligands and metal ions have found extensive applications in self-organizing templates for hollow functional materials to be used in water splitting.163,164 Among other effects, metal doping facilitates the optimization of the adsorption energy of substrates adjacent to the catalytic sites, the modulation of the electronic density of the surrounding atoms and the decrease of the activation energy of the reaction intermediates. Based on this, Du et al. synthesized hierarchical Cu-doped Co9S8 nanoarray structures using CuCo-MOF containing less Co2+ species (Fig. 12a).165 The synthesized material increases the H2O chemisorption and diminishes the activation energy or reaction intermediates, while Cu doping increases material conductivity. The optimal Cu-Co9S8-6 h nanorods exhibit excellent water reduction and oxidation performance under alkaline conditions. It was found that the electrocatalytic activity depends on the sulfurization time using thioacetamide as a sulfur precursor. For the optimal performance (sulfurization time of 6 h), the morphology of the MOF derived Cu-Co9S8-6 h material constitutes nanorods composed entirely of aggregated nanoparticles making the surface rough and resulting in the formation of many voids. During the procedure of sulfurization and decomposition from thioacetamide, S2âˆ’ ions first react with Co ions. Then, during the sulfurization process, the reaction of S2âˆ’ ions is controlled by the diffusion of Co2+ and a one-dimensional structure is obtained. The nanorod morphology leads to a shortened electron transfer path. The results of DFT calculations showed that the superior electrocatalytic activity of Co9S8-6 h can be attributed to the increase in adsorption energy and conductivity of water due to Cu doping. These DFT calculations indicate that the density of states for Cu-Co9S8 close to the Fermi level is higher than that of Co9S8, justifying a fast charge transfer and better electrical conductivity of the bimetallic material. In other words, Cu doping significantly modulates the activation energy of the intermediates, resulting in a substantial decrease in the overall activation energy, which can explain why bimetallic Cu-Co9S8 exhibits better catalytic activity that increases the performance of water splitting reactions (Fig. 12bâ€“d). The optimal Cu-Co9S8-6 h electrocatalyst just needed 1.49 V under 1 M KOH at 10 mA cmâˆ’2 current density.

8.4 Bimetallic oxides

Transition metal oxides, especially Ni and Co-based oxides, have received great attention and have been widely studied. Although phosphides, sulfides, and other derivatives have shown better electrocatalytic behavior than their oxide analogs, oxides present the advantage of a more convenient synthesis under less harsh and more amenable conditions, better controllability, and lesser probable contamination. Among these advanced catalysts, transition metal oxides (especially Co/Ni oxides) have attracted special interest due to their high stability and unique 3d electronic configuration. The special attention for first-row transition metal based electrocatalysts derives from their attractive properties such as low cost, availability, and superior activity.166â€“168 Meanwhile, transition metal oxides show excellent OER activity in alkaline media; their excellent performance is due to the strong binding energies for the OER intermediates (*O, *OH, *OOH) on the catalyst surface, the corrosion resistance in alkaline solution during the OER process and the distinctive redox features.169â€“171 However, Co/Ni oxides show poorer than expected electrocatalytic performance in the OER due to inevitable self-aggregation and low intrinsic electrical conductivity. These limitations can be overcome if the metal oxide nanoparticles are wrapped in a carbon matrix that in addition increases the electrical conductivity. Therefore, poor electrical conductivity and adequate control of the charge distribution of metal oxides are important limitations that have to be overcome for improving their electrocatalytic performance. An effective strategy in this regard could be the introduction of a second metal to the electrocatalyst, which can modulate the charge distribution and tune the energy levels to the required potentials to promote the HER and OER, resulting in a synergistic effect between the two metals.172,173 Thus, bimetallic oxides show higher electrocatalytic performance than their monometallic counterparts.174

Zhang et al. reported carbon-metal (or dual-metal oxide) electrocatalysts using bimetallic-MOFs with pillar linkers for the OER.175 Among all the prepared bimetallic derivatives, CoNi1@C demonstrated the lowest OER overpotential. After pyrolysis, bimetallic MOFs with a high Co ratio showed the best electrocatalytic OER performance, owing to their high valence state and exceptional structure. The electrocatalytic activity improvement for the OER was justified considering: (1) synergistic cooperation of the two metals in bimetallic oxides. In particular, the high-valence nickel (serving as an active site) promotes the OER. Spinel trivalent ions play a decisive role in activating the electrostatically adsorbed oxygen to form MOOH and then inducing the OOH deprotonation to generate O2.176â€“179 (2) Extraordinary hierarchical porous honeycomb arrangement that can promote interfacial electron and ion transfer and exhibits structural stability during electrochemical tests.180â€“182 Also, the carbon shell matrix ensures the electrical continuity of bimetallic oxide particles and prevents the aggregation of nanoparticles in the electrochemical tests and during pyrolysis.

8.7 In situ techniques in the water splitting

In situ/Operando techniques provide important information regarding the nature of the active sites and the intermediates. These techniques have been amply used to gain insights into electrochemical processes,285 including examples in which the electrocatalysts are MOF-derived bimetallic materials. These techniques include transmission electron microscopy (TEM), X-ray diffraction spectroscopy, X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), Raman, Infrared spectroscopy (IR), and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). Not unfrequently, electrocatalysts undergo structural reconstruction during water splitting and in situ/operando monitoring can be employed to evaluate the evolution of the electrocatalyst sites under reaction conditions. The structural changes of the electrocatalyst can be complex and depend on numerous experimental factors, requiring the combination of the information of various techniques to gain a deeper understanding. The changes in the electrocatalyst can be in electronic configuration, oxidation state, particle morphology or crystal phase, limiting the performance of the electrocatalysts. In situ/operando techniques offer information on reaction intermediates which cannot be obtained by ex situ techniques.

Su et al. assessed the OER performance of NiFe-PBA (NF-PBA; PBA: Prussian Blue Analog) at the atomic level based on the operando techniques combined with the ex situ methods.286 Available data indicated the transformation of the material structure under OER conditions, leading to an excellent performance of the material. Thus, using operando techniques such as Ni K-edge XAS studies, it was revealed that in situ-produced amorphous Ni(OH)2 is the real electrocatalyst. The operando XAS technique also indicated that Ni ions are capable of being oxidized into high valence oxides in amorphous domains, thus they can exhibit high activity. DFT calculations of this system indicate the overlap of 3d orbitals of Ni4+ with the 2p orbitals of oxygen, resulting in an upward energy shift that favors the OER (Fig. 25).

9. Conclusions and outlook

One of the most feasible ways to produce hydrogen is electrochemical water splitting. To date, various types of electrocatalysts have been explored in the field of electrochemical water-splitting. Among them, electrocatalysts derived from MOFs have attracted great attention. This class of MOF-derived materials have unique structural properties compared to other types of electrocatalysts with no alternative preparation procedure. Their advantages are related to the chemical composition, porous structures and defined morphology. Starting from MOFs it is also possible to introduce a second metal in a wide range of proportions. A common criticism of why to prepare a crystalline material with an organic component that later has to be decomposed is answered by the superior performance of the MOF-derived material as a consequence of the homogeneous distribution of the two metals located at the nodal position and the controlled particle morphology, frequently unique. The advantage of MOF-derived materials is also evident when the organic linker is an affordable organic linker, like terephthalate, trimesic acid, imidazole and others. Thus, derivatives of these bimetallic-MOFs exhibit improved advantages and enhanced electrocatalytic performance derived from the synergism between the redox pairs of the two metals, excellent interfacial contact among the various possible phases and increased electrical conductivity.

The present review has summarized the latest developments in first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic-MOFs as advanced and efficient electrocatalysts for the HER/OER and various structural strategies to improve their electrocatalytic activity. Based on the current state of the art, the following conclusions can be drawn:

(1) The surface composition of bimetallic electrocatalysts is one of the major parameters determining their performance. The results show that the presence of two metals largely improves the performance and stability of this class of materials as electrocatalysts for the HER/OER.

(2) The unique structural properties of MOFs, including high surface area, porosity and composition, are inherited to a large extent when they are transformed into their derivatives. In the case of bimetallic MOFs a large number of active centers are formed as compared to monometallic-MOFs or even other precursors, resulting in a superior electrocatalytic performance.

(3) There is a paucity of theoretical studies and modeling to understand the present activity data and predict the performance of unexplored combinations. It is expected that DFT calculations will be more frequently used in the future to predict the electronic structure of bimetallic electrocatalysts, leading to the design of ideal surfaces for electrochemical reactions.

(4) New synthetic methodologies to transform first-row transition metal-containing bimetallic MOFs into electrocatalysts can result in even higher specific surface area, novel morphologies and appropriate structures. Besides pyrolysis, other chemical and electrochemical methods could be implemented.

(5) Surface functionalization and selection of the appropriate composition should further extend the application of bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from MOFs beyond water electrolysis in other electrocatalytic processes.

(6) Besides bimetallic, it can also be envisioned that tri- and multimetallic MOFs can render, upon transformation, derived multimetallic oxides, sulfides, phosphides and composites with even better performance as water splitting electrocatalysts.

(7) The direct use of MOFs as HER/OER electrocatalysts could be possible by appropriate control of the crystal morphology. Thin 2D MOF nanosheets appear to be specially suited for this purpose.

Despite advances in the design and synthesis of first-row transition metals-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic-MOFs, academic and industrial research in this field faces challenges. At the academic level, the number of reports on the efficient design and synthesis of first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic-MOF for electrochemical water splitting is considerable and is growing rapidly. Among them, the combination of cobalt and nickel is the one that has been most widely investigated in the development of cost-effective electrocatalysts for electrochemical water splitting. However, in most of these reports, strategies to improve the performance of electrocatalysts have been pursued by improving the electron and ion transfer and increasing the specific surface area. Few reports have focused on understanding the reaction mechanism and adapting surface and composition to each individual half-reaction. The combination of in situ/operando techniques with computational calculations on the model should provide insights into the origin of the improved performance and provide descriptors and hints to design new generations of advanced materials.

Electrocatalysts to be used for commercial hydrogen generation on a large scale via an electrochemical water splitting method are required to be inexpensive, earth abundant, and highly efficient for addressing the slow kinetics of the OER. As electrocatalysts that contain first row transition metals are cost effective, earth abundant, and can be utilized in basic media, they have made significant progress in this field. Detailed studies are needed to logically and accurately design these electrocatalysts with numerous active sites and ideal stability in industrial applications. Despite significant progress in this field, considerable efforts and research should be done to commercialize the first row transition metal based MOF derivative electrocatalyst. Discussions on active sites and details of the mechanism of these electrocatalysts require much more detailed research with an understanding of the reaction mechanisms and predictive tools. For commercial application of these electrocatalysts and their capability for practical applications, their stability needs to be determined at much higher current densities in the range of 100 mA cm2 and for extended periods. Therefore, in order to consider and apply the first row transition metal based electrocatalysts in industry and commerce, a series of challenges such as identification of active sites by suitable techniques, optimization of their performance and especially their stability under relevant conditions and investigation of their structure and composition during electrochemical processes should be addressed. Generally, a deep understanding of the behavior and performance of these electrocatalysts facilitates their design with improved performance. The growing efforts of the research community provide a valued aid for the screening and design of advanced electrocatalysts based on first row transition metals suitable for practical applications.

Industrially, the production-scale and cost are of the greatest importance for mass distribution of electrocatalysts. So far, although the number of reports on the synthesis of first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic MOFs is large, reports on their use in high power electrolyzers are scarce. In current research, few studies have examined the effect of production scale-up on the electrocatalyst performance. Since the current evidence shows the improved performance of bimetallic electrocatalysts and considering the importance of water electrolysis in the future decarbonized energy, it is important to assess the techno-economical viability of large scale production for these bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from MOFs.

Methodologies designed for first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic MOFs can be utilized to develop other electrocatalysts as well. In fact, heteroatom-doping, morphology engineering, hybridization with additional components, and defect engineering with the generation of oxygen vacancies have also been employed to increase the performance of other electrocatalysts. However, an optimal electrocatalyst with ready for commercialization in electrochemical devices still remains a major challenge. In summary, first-row transition metal-containing electrocatalysts derived from bimetallic-MOFs are promising candidates as electrocatalysts which in addition to the excellent performance bring compatibility and simplicity to electrocatalytic systems. With continued research in this field, it is expected that first-row transition metal-containing bimetallic electrocatalysts derived from MOFs could play in the near future a valuable and prevalent role in the production of green hydrogen.
