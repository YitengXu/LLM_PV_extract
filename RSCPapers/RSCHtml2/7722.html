Abstract

Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs), owing to their high energy density, light weight, and long cycle life, have shown considerable promise for storage devices. The successful utilization of LIBs depends strongly on the preparation of nanomaterials with outstanding lithium storage properties. Recent progress has demonstrated that hollow/porous nanostructured oxides are very attractive candidates for LIBs anodes due to their high storage capacities. Here, we aim to provide an overview of nanoscale metalâ€“organic frameworks (NMOFs)-templated synthesis of hollow/porous nanostructured oxides and their LIBs applications. By choosing some typical NMOFs as examples, we present a comprehensive summary of synthetic procedures for nanostructured oxides, such as binary, ternary and composite oxides. Hollow/porous structures are readily obtained due to volume loss and release of internally generated gas molecules during the calcination of NMOFs in air. Interestingly, the NMOFs-derived hollow/porous structures possess several special features: pores generated from gas molecules release will connect to each other, which are distinct from â€œdead poresâ€; pore size often appears to be <10 nm; in terms of surface chemistry, the pore surface is hydrophobic. These structural features are believed to be the most critical factors that determine LIBsâ€™ performance. Indeed, it has been shown that these NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides exhibit excellent electrochemical performance as anode materials for LIBs, including high storage capacity, good cycle stability, and so on. For example, a high charge capacity of 1465 mA h gâˆ’1 at a rate of 300 mA gâˆ’1 was observed after 50 cycles for NMOFs-derived Co3O4 porous nanocages, which corresponds to 94.09% of the initial capacity (1557 mA h gâˆ’1), indicating excellent stability. The capacity of NMOFs-derived Co3O4 is higher than that of other Co3O4 nanostructures obtained by a conventional two-step route, including nanosheets (1450 mA h gâˆ’1 at 50 mA gâˆ’1), nanobelts (1400 mA h gâˆ’1 at 40 mA gâˆ’1) and nanoflowers (694 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100 mA gâˆ’1). The capacity is also better than Co3O4 octahedra obtained by a one-step hydrothermal method (946 mA h gâˆ’1 at 100 mA gâˆ’1). In this review, we will summarize the recent research advances on NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides as LIBs anodes. The enhanced lithium storage properties have been discussed in relation to their special structural parameters. Moreover, remarks on the current challenges and perspectives for future NMOFs applications are proposed. Through this systematic review, we aim to stress the importance of NMOFs templates for the fabrication of hollow/porous functional materials that would result in improved physicochemical properties and provide insights to guide future research for LIBs applications.

1. Introduction

The energy issue is the most important topic in our society due to the development of the economy, which requires cost-effective, efficient and environmentally benign energy storage devices that can power energy-demanding areas.1,2 Nowadays, LIBs have been employed for large-scale applications in various fields, such as hybrid and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles, solar and wind electricity generation systems and so on. To meet the requirements of these applications, further improvements in terms of energy power, densities and cycle time are required. The research on anode materials with superior capacity has been a hot field in recent years.3â€“13 Among various anode materials, oxides have been proven to be possible candidates because of their high theoretical capacities, widespread availability, and environmental benignity. However, the rapid capacity fading which is mainly caused by structural reorganization and volumetric variation upon chargeâ€“discharge has impeded their further development. Currently, it is suggested that the rapid capacity fading of oxides could be possibly overcome by making proper nanostructures (hollow/porous structure, suitable pore size, surface feature, and so on) with the capability to sustain structural disintegration and promote lithium storage reactions. Many previous results prove that increasing the porosity could be an effective way to improve the lithium storage properties of the materials, as the void space provided by these pores can buffer the volume variation during the chargeâ€“discharge cycles, leading to enhanced capacity. Pore size is also an important factor to determine the lithium storage capacity. If the size is too large, it is difficult to maintain inter-particle contact, resulting in a decrease of electrical conductivity. If the pore size is too small, the liquid surface tension could prevent the electrolyte diffusing into the inner region of the materials. In addition to the porosity, the hollow structure often provides a much higher specific surface area and other particular features, which is beneficial for energy devices because the reaction/interaction between the devices and the interacting media can be significantly enhanced due to the sufficient space for lattice expansion and electrolyte diffusion. Inspired by their special properties, many efforts have been dedicated to the rational design of hollow/porous structures with well-defined morphologies, and versatile synthetic strategies have been developed recently to generate such structures, including conventional templating methods, the Kirkendall effect,14 ionic exchange,15 self-template-directed growth16 and so on. Although much progress has been made in the synthesis of hollow structures, the shells of the resulting hollow architectures from these approaches are usually primarily nonporous. The obtained hollow architectures with a porous shell might be more attractive than closed hollow structures in some aspects. For example, the porous shell is more convenient for Li+ ions diffusing into the inner region of anode materials due to the large amounts of pores in their walls, which could effectively increase materials utilization. Also, pores obtained by some strategies (such as wet chemistry methods) often exhibit hydrophilic properties due to the existence of many hydrophilic groups, which could hinder electrolyte (organic phase) diffusion when the materials are used as anodes. On the other hand, some reports have shown that the cycleability of oxides is strongly influenced by their morphology. Therefore, some research efforts have prepared novel nanostructures with particular shapes, which has been proved as an effective method to improve the electrochemical properties of oxide anodes.17â€“22 In this regard, anisotropic hollow/porous structures with nonspherical shapes and complex hierarchical architectures are particularly interesting for many applications. However, conventional templating techniques to create nonspherical hollow structures still suffer from many difficulties, ranging from the formation of uniform coatings around high-curvature surfaces to the paucity of nonspherical templates available.23,24 Based on the above analysis, new developments in the field of synthesis hollow/porous oxides with suitable structural parameters are believed to hold the key to further breakthroughs in LIBs applications.

MOFs contain metal ions linked by coordinated ligands into an infinite array, which is described as a new class of porous materials.25â€“30 Bulk phase MOFs have shown promise for many applications including gas storage, catalysis, nonlinear optics, separations, sensing, and light-harvesting.31â€“36 However, it is found that the shape and size are important factors to fine tune the properties of the material. Therefore, nanometre sized materials of MOFs often exhibit remarkable size- and shape dependent physical and chemical properties, which cannot be observed in their bulk analogues.37,38 Precise control over the nanocrystal structure, size, shape and chemical composition not only allows one to observe unique properties of the MOFs nanostructures but also allows the tuning of their chemical and physical properties as desired. In recent years, NMOFs have been widely synthesized by various strategies, such as nanoprecipitation, solvothermal, reverse microemulsion, and surfactant-templated solvothermal reactions.39â€“49 In particular, M3II[Co(CN)6]2Â·nH2O (M = Mn, Co, Cd, Zn, Fe) particles have been obtained at room temperature in waterâ€“ethanol systems.50â€“52 These NMOFs not only have been explored for gas storage, biomedical imaging and drug delivery,53â€“59 but also employed as templates/precursors for preparation of nanomaterials.60â€“63 Recently, a new burgeoning direction in the field of NMOFs is their use as â€˜precursorsâ€™ of new nanostructured materials, primarily via a thermolysis procedure. As compared to other templates/precursors, NMOFs offer distinct advantages because of their unique structure and porosity, which provides a tremendous opportunity to develop a series of highly tailorable functional nanomaterials for their possible applications. By choosing NMOFs with special morphologies and under suitable experimental conditions, obtaining oxides with desired morphologies will become possible. Very recently, our group and others have successfully fabricated the hollow structure with porous shells (nonspherical shapes, from cubes to polyhedron) using NMOFs as templates/precursors. This strategy has proved to be simple, inexpensive, tunable and scalable. In addition to hollow/porous features, the NMOFs-derived oxides also often demonstrate other special structural features, such as large surface area, inter-connected pores, hierarchical pore sizes and a hydrophobic surface. Most importantly, when evaluated as anode materials for LIBs, these NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides exhibit excellent electrochemical performance for LIBs due to the above-mentioned structural features. Despite the great progress achieved so far, the research on the synthesis of inorganic functional materials derived from NMOFs and their applications in energy conversion and storage is still at its very early stage. Moreover, up to now, no reviews have been published to focus on the high performance of NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides in LIBs applications. Therefore, a comprehensive review of the published literature is greatly needed to further promote LIBs development based on NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides. Our emphasis will be on oxides which have been receiving increasing interest. We first highlight the recent NMOFs-templated synthetic strategies towards various oxides with different nanostructures. In the following section, we will describe the high performance of these NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides when used as anodes for LIBs, and the underlying mechanism will be also discussed. Finally, we briefly presage the future opportunities that NMOFs face.

2. NMOFs-templated synthesis of hollow/porous oxides

Among different synthesis methods for the preparation of oxides, there is a two-step route to fabricate porous oxide nanostructures. The precursor compounds (such as hydroxide and carbonate) are synthesized in the first step, and then the precursor is calcinated to obtain oxides with the conserved morphology of the precursor. For example, cobalt hydroxide has been calcinated to prepare Co3O4 nanoplates.64 Yu et al. reported an approach to obtain porous Co3O4 particles by decomposition of CoCO3 crystals.65 Co(CO3)0.5(OH)0.11Â·H2O was also employed to obtain mesoporous and single-crystal Co3O4 nanobelt array.66 However, the synthesis methods of these precursors for oxide nanomaterials involve complex processes which need a high reaction temperature (hydrothermal/solvothermal methods). In particular, the composite oxides are difficult to fabricate by this strategy. NMOFs are alternative candidates for the synthesis of hollow/porous nanostructured oxides. By calcining the NMOFs in air at moderate temperature, the metal ions in the NMOFs can be transformed into metal oxides, and the C and other elements (such as N, H) are oxidized into gas molecules. Hollow/porous oxides with porous shell and inter-connected pores could be readily produced due to gas molecule release during the calcination process, as shown from Scheme 1. Several advantages should be noted as compared to the traditional two-step route. First, most NMOFs could be obtained at relatively low temperatures. In particular, some Prussian Blue Analogue (PBA) nanoparticles can be synthesized by a simple procedure at room temperature. Second, this NMOFs-templated strategy is more suitable for fabricating composite oxides at the nanoscale via using a NMOF precursor which contains two different metal ions. Third, by changing the metal ions in MOFs, various kinds of hollow/porous oxides will be obtained, indicating the versatile features. 3. Applications in LIBs

The structural reorganization and volumetric variation upon chargeâ€“discharge have been identified as the main causes for the cracking of active materials and rapid capacity fading. The rapid capacity fading of oxide materials could be possibly overcome by making proper nanostructures with capabilities to sustain structural disintegration and promote lithium storage reactions. In recent years, researchers have made an active effort to pursue novel nanostructures, which has been confirmed as a promising method to improve the electrochemical properties of metallic oxide anodes. Nanomaterials with distinct structural characteristics often exhibit improved capacities. The porous structure obtained from NMOFs possesses pores, generated from gas molecule release, which will connect with each other to form inter-connected channels (active pores), which are distinct from â€œdead poresâ€; the size of the pores often appears to be <10 nm with hierarchical porosity. Generally, metal oxides synthesized by wet chemical methods are hydrophilic because of their surface hydroxyl groups. However, they can be made hydrophobic by changing the surface morphology and chemical composition.102 Surface roughness is often added to amplify the hydrophobicity of the materials. The calcination strategy is often employed to obtain NMOFs-derived hollow/porous oxides, which leads to a clean and rough surface that exhibits hydrophobic features (without any hydrophilic group, such as hydroxyl). Both the hollow/porous structure and hydrophobic pore surface affect the transport of organic electrolytes, which is critical to the electrochemical performance of these materials. As shown in Table 2, all the NMOFs-derived oxides exhibited excellent lithium storage properties, including high specific capacity and excellent cycling performance, which is attributed to their special structural characteristics. The NMOFs-derived oxides with a hydrophobic surface may easily disperse in organic electrolytes (such as ethylene carbonate or diethylene carbonate) due to the hydrophobic surface when used as anodes (Scheme 2). The active inter-connected pores with a suitable pore size (2â€“10 nm) are convenient for Li+ diffusing into the inner region of anode materials with little resistance, leading to a remarkable improvement in the storage capacity. If the pore size is too large (>10 nm), it is difficult to maintain inter-particle contact, leading to the decrease of the electrical conductivity and poor calendar life. The liquid surface tension could prevent the electrolyte diffusing into the inner region of the materials if the pore size is too small (<2 nm). Hollow structures could also buffer efficiently the stress caused by volume variation during the chargeâ€“discharge process, resulting in long calendar life. Moreover, one of the potential disadvantages associated with the development of a nanostructured anode is the possibility of significant sub-reactions with the electrolyte due to the high external surface area, leading to safety concerns and poor calendar life. These problems may be solved with secondary nanomaterials in which the particles are typically of micrometer dimensions but consist internally of nanometre-sized regions or domains, which could not only reduce side reactions with the electrolyte, but also have the advantage of ensuring excellent electrical properties. In this regard, the NMOFs-drevied Fe2O3 microboxes with hierarchically structured shells are promising anode materials for high performance LIBs.

4. Summary and perspective

Hollow/porous materials represent a class of interesting and important micro/nanostructures due to their superior performance in most applications. This review focuses on the most recent progress related to NMOFs-templated synthesis of hollow/porous oxides and their potential applications as anode materials for LIBs. It is shown that NMOFs could be selected as suitable templates or precursors for the fabrication of nanoscale materials with hollow/porous structures. The process is a simple and efficient method of preparing hollow/porous structures. In particularly, shape, size, crystal structure and purity can be easily controlled by varying experimental conditions such as the shape and size of NMOFs or thermolysis temperature. The most significant feature of this method is gas molecules released during the annealing process, which is beneficial for the generation of hollow/porous structures with their surface properties different from their counterparts prepared via a solution process. When evaluated as anode materials for LIBs, the obtained products exhibit excellent electrochemical performance because of the special structural features, including porous shells, a hydrophobic particle surface and active interconnected pores with a suitable pore size (2â€“10 nm).

There has been a rapid growth in the development of oxides with hollow/porous structures for potential applications as LIBs, as evidenced by the dramatic increase in the number of papers published in this area, however, the oxide hollow/porous structures generated from NMOFs are still very limited. The versatile NMOF-templated synthesis procedure can be readily modified to prepare more oxides with desirable properties by varying the type of MOF (both the metal ions and coordinating moieties). Without a doubt the vast expanse of MOF chemistry has allowed us to explore new opportunities for the fabrication of hollow/porous structures of other functional materials, such as metal, alloys, porous carbon and so on (Fig. 35). Moreover, the NMOFs could be coated and modified by other materials to fabricate composites with more complex structures. For example, nitrogen-doped porous carbon/graphene, metals and alloys may be produced by decomposing M3II[Co(CN)6]2Â·nH2O nanoparticles in an argon atmosphere. Silicon (Si) is one of the most attractive anode materials for use in LIBs due to its higher specific capacity (4200 mA h gâˆ’1). However, up to 400% volume expansion with Li causes particle pulverization and fracture, which results in rapid capacity fading. The NMOFs-derived hollow SiO2 spheres can be employed as templates to prepare hollow Si spheres with a porous shell under a reducing atmosphere. The structure parameters of NMOFs-derived hollow Si spheres could alleviate particle pulverization and fracture during chargeâ€“discharge, thus improving its electrochemical performance. TiO2 is also a very attractive material for LIBs. However, the LIBs application of NMOFs-derived porous TiO2 has not been studied. It is valuable to investigate its Li+ storage capacity not only for further understanding the relationship between NMOFs-derived structures and their performances as anode materials for LIBs, but also for pursuing the practical applications. The annealing condition is an important factor to influence the structural parameters, including the average pore size, inter-connection between the pores, the thickness of shell, and so on. Through modifying the annealing conditions, such as atmosphere (air, nitrogen or argon), temperature or heating rate, we believe that NMOFs-derived nanostructures more suitable for LIBs applications will be achieved. The techniques for characterizing the inter-connection and surface feature of pores should be also established to further clarify the mechanism underlying the enhanced properties. The rate capability is an important parameter for many applications of batteries such as electric vehicles and portable power tools, which require fast discharge and/or charge rate. Therefore, the investigation of Li+ storage properties of NMOFs-derived oxides under high currents is necessary. Of course, the further development of a facile method for fabrication of NMOFs with various shapes and sizes is critical to obtain NMOFs-derived functional materials with desired properties. We should not only inherit the existing synthetic methods but also develop new and advanced synthetic strategies to generate NMOFs at room temperature by changing the solvents and surfactants. We need to make vigorous efforts to understand the relationship between the reaction parameters and the final structures and properties of the resulting materials. In addition to LIBs applications, many other potential applications of NMOF-derived hollow/porous oxides must be examined, including biomedical applications, supercapacitors, catalysis, and so on. Overall, the research concerning preparation of hollow/porous oxides from NMOFs has opened up an exciting avenue toward functional nanoporous materials for energy-related utilization.
