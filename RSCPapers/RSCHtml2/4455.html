Novel benzodithiophene-based polymer acceptors for efficient organic solar cells

Abstract

All polymer organic solar cells afford unique potentials due to the tunable chemical and electronic properties of both polymer donors and polymer acceptors. Compared with the rapid development of polymer donors, the development of polymer acceptors lags far behind. To seek high-performance polymer acceptors used in organic solar cells, based on the experimentally reported DA polymer acceptor (NDI2OD-T2)n (P1), a series of novel acceptors, designated as (BDTNDI2OD-T2)n(P2), (BDTNDTI)n(P3), (BDTNDI2OD-Tz2)n(P4), and (BDTNDTzI)n(P5), were designed by introduction of a benzodithiophene (BDT) unit and the nitrogen atom in the bridged thiophene ring. The density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) methods were applied to study the effect of the BDT unit and the nitrogen atom on the geometrical, optical, electronic, and charge transport properties. The obtained results show that incorporation of the electron-donating BDT unit into P1 and the replacement of a carbon atom by the nitrogen atom in the bridged thiophene ring are effective strategies to lower the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy and exciton binding energy, and enhance light-absorbing capacity and electron mobility. Moreover, among the investigated molecules, P2 and P5 exhibit stronger and broader light absorption, higher light absorption efficiency and exciton separation ability as well as electron mobility; therefore they are recommended as promising polymer acceptors for future high-efficiency organic solar cells.

1. Introduction

Organic solar cells (OSCs) have attracted widespread attention owing to their ease of processing, mechanical flexibility, and potential for cost-effective mass production.16 At present, the performance of bulk-heterojunction-type (BHJ) OSCs7 has been improved steadily and the power conversion efficiency (PCE) over 11% has been achieved for fullerene-based OSCs.810 The traditional electron acceptors, fullerene derivatives (e.g., PC61BM and PC71BM), show many advantages involving high electron mobility, and delocalization of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) over the whole molecular surface to accept and transport electrons in three dimensions.11 However, fullerene-based acceptors possess several significant disadvantages, including invariable energy levels, relatively weak absorption ability in the red and near-infrared regions, difficult purification, and lack of chemical/morphological stability.1215 Therefore, recent studies have been devoted to explore non-fullerene acceptors, which can be classified into two types: small molecules5,14,1618 and polymers.1922

All-polymer solar cells (all-PSCs)2,19,23,24 with both donor and acceptor polymers have attracted remarkable interest due to their significant advantages over fullerene-based solar cells. The PCE of the all-PSCs has been steadily increased from 2% to 8% recently.19,23,25 For example, the PCE of PPDT2FBTH:N2200 based all-PSCs has been increased to 5.1%.21 A PCE of 7.7% was achieved by using a high-mobility polymer acceptor (PNDIS-HD) and polymer donor (PBDTT-FTTE).26 It is a significant challenge to exploit new polymer acceptors with remarkable electron mobility, a broad absorption spectrum, and well-matched energy levels with polymer donors in the all-PSCs. Of the building-blocks used for designing polymer acceptors, perylene diimide (PDI)-based27 and naphthalene-based diimide (NDI)21,23,28 acceptors are widely used due to their small stacking distance, relatively high electron mobility, air stability, tunable solubility, and ambipolarity capabilities.29 For instance, P(NDI2OD-T2) shows unprecedented characteristics due to its high field-effect electron mobility (0.10.85 cm2 V1 s1), high electron affinity, and light absorption ability at near-IR wavelengths.12,21 P(NDI2OD-T2) and its derivatives have been widely used in all-PSCs,2,19,25 where a maximal PCE of 8.27% has been reached by using a polymer donor J51 with P(NDI2OD-T2) as the acceptor.19

In addition, due to the advantages of the modified benzodithiophene (BDT) unit such as a large planar conjugated structure and the ability to easily form co-facial stacking, BDT units as per alkylthiophene- or alkylthiothiophene-substituted BDTs form a promising class of high-performance -conjugated polymer donors with remarkable hole mobility and spectral coverage.30 For instance, Li et al. synthesized a BDT-based polymer donor, PBDTT-S-TT, which showed PCEs over 8.4%.31 Although 2D-conjugated BDT units were used as donor materials in the PSCs in the past few years, polymer acceptors based on BDT units have received much less attention. Chen et al. designed the first BDT and naphthalene diimide (NDI)-based copolymer in 2010,32 whereas the photovoltaic performances of PBDTNDI were not characterized at that time. In 2013, Xu and coworkers synthesized two BDT-NDI polymer acceptors together with PBDTNDI as the polymer donor achieving a very small PCE of 0.02% in PSC devices.33 Hou and coworkers reported a PSC based on polymer acceptor PBDTNDI-T showing a high PCE of 2.88%.30 Obviously, there is still much scope for further enhancement in the performance of BDT-based polymer acceptors.

Herein, on the basis of the reported donoracceptor (DA) polymer (NDI2OD-T2)n (P1),2,19,34 we designed four novel DA conjugated polymers (BDTNDI2OD-T2)n (P2), (BDTNDTI)n (P3), (BDTNDI2OD-Tz2)n (P4), and (BDTNDTzI)n (P5) (see Fig. 1 for the structures), where the modified BDT is the electron-donating unit, and NDI or NDTI acts as the electron-accepting unit. The geometric structures, the energy levels of the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the LUMO, the electron transport abilities, the light absorption properties, and the electron-harvesting abilities of P1P5 were investigated in detail. In addition, referring to the donor material J51,19 the free energy loss and the exciton binding energy were also investigated to provide in-depth insights into the charge transfer characteristics and the performance of the OSCs.

2. Computational details

All calculations on the oligomers studied in this work were fully optimized by the density functional theory (DFT) method35 using the Gaussian 09 software.36 The calculated vibrational frequencies of all molecules show that no imaginary vibrational frequencies are present, which means that all the optimized structures are stable structures. In the calculations, all the alkyl side chains of polymers were replaced by methyl groups, because the long chains have proved to only aid in increasing the solubility with little effect on the frontier molecular orbitals and optoelectronic properties.37,38 Solvent effects of chloroform were added using the conductor-like polarizable continuum model of solvation (C-PCM).39

It is well known that the energy levels of organic molecules strongly depend on the employed functionals in DFT calculations. In order to find the reliable method, we have tried a series of hybrid functionals (PBE0,40 B3LYP,41 and HSE0642) with the 6-311G(d,p) basis set to compute the HOMO and LUMO energies of (NDI2OD-T2)1 (M1), (NDI2OD-T2)2 (D1) and (NDI2OD-T2)3 (T1) for P1, and the obtained results together with available experimental values are summarized in Table 1. Note that the experimental HOMO and LUMO energies are evaluated from an empirical formula proposed by Brdas et al., which is based on the onset of the oxidation and reduction peaks measured by cyclic voltammetry in experiments.43 As shown in Table 1, the calculated HOMO energy on the trimer (T1) model of P1 at the HSE06/6-311G(d,p) level (5.54 eV) is very close to the experimental value of P1 (5.52 eV).12 Similarly, the calculated result of the LUMO energy at the HSE06/6-311G(d,p) level on T1 (3.93 eV) agrees well with the reported experimental value of P1 (4.02 eV).12 So the ground-state properties of all oligomers in this work were studied at the HSE06/6-311G(d,p) level, which have been proven to be successful in previous similar studies.44,45

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Optimized geometries and electronic properties

The ground-state geometries significantly impact the electronic properties, optical absorption, intermolecular stacking, and electron mobility of the polymers.48,49 Therefore, it is necessary to analyze the ground-state structures of the polymers. Here the ground-state structure parameters of P1P5 are deduced on the basis of those of the corresponding monomers (M1M5), dimers (D1D5), and trimers (T1T5) (see Fig. 1). The optimized structures of T1T5 are shown in Fig. 2.

The summary of selected bond lengths and dihedral angles of monomers, dimers, and trimers of P1P5 is shown in Fig. S1 (ESI).

As shown in Fig. 2 and Fig. S1 (ESI), the optimized bond lengths and dihedral angles do not generate prodigious variation with the oligomer size, which indicates that describing the basic structures of the polymers by their trimers is appropriate. The selected bond lengths of the carboncarbon bonds (CC) (1.431.47 ) of monomers, dimers, and trimers for P1P5 are shorter than that of the CC single bond (1.54 ), which exhibit some double-bond character due to -bonding interaction, thereby strengthening and shortening the bridge bond. The above results indicate that the -electrons are delocalized over the entire molecular configuration rather than being localized on the donor or acceptor unit. The calculated absolute values of dihedral angles for the oligomers of P1, P2, and P4 in the range of 1560 indicate that these non-planar arrangements can reduce self-aggregation in BHJ blend films and stabilize the molecules.50,51 When the NDTI replaces the NDI2OD-T2 unit, the dihedral angles of the monomer, dimer, and trimer of P3 between the electron-rich unit and the electron-deficient unit are almost negligible. The decrease of the dihedral angle is due to the new core NDTI with a large molecule plane, which is beneficial to strengthen the intramolecular conjugation and intermolecular stacking. The dihedral angle further reduces when the carbon atoms on the NDTI unit in P3 are changed by the nitrogen atoms on the NDTzI unit in P5. Among the five polymers, P5 has the best planarity, which implies that it may have stronger intrachain conjugation, a greater interchain electronic coupling effect, and a higher carrier mobility in comparison to other polymers.

3.2 Frontier molecular orbitals

The HOMO/LUMO energies and the HOMOLUMO energy gaps (Egs) are greatly associated with the electronic properties and optical absorption, which are key factors for the PCE.38,52 An ideal acceptor should possess suitable HOMO/LUMO levels in comparison with those of the donor to ensure fast charge separation and further electron transport. Hence, we analyze the HOMO/LUMO energy levels and Egs of the five polymers. The HOMO/LUMO energies, and energy gaps (Egs) of P1P5 were obtained by plotting the HOMO and LUMO energies, and Egs of the corresponding monomers, dimers, and trimers inverse their reciprocal chain length and extrapolating to infinite chain length (n = ) (representing the corresponding polymers), which has been successfully employed to investigate other polymers.52,53 The HOMO/LUMO energies, and the energy gaps (Egs) of monomers, dimers, and trimers of P1P5 are listed in Table 3 and Fig. S2 (ESI). The molecular orbital plots of T1T5 are given in Fig. 3. We can see that the HOMO of T1 is mainly delocalized on the bridge units, while for T2, T3, T4, and T5, the densities of the HOMO are mainly delocalized on the bridge units as well as on the BDT ring. Moreover, the LUMOs of the five trimers are mainly centralized on electron-deficient units.

As shown in Table 3, Fig. 3 and Fig. S2 (ESI), the HOMO and LUMO energies have the same trend that with the increasing conjugation lengths the HOMO energies increase, whereas the LUMO energies decrease. The calculated HOMO and LUMO energies and Egs exhibit excellent correlation with the repeat unit of the polymer, where the extrapolated HOMO energies of P1, P2, P3, P4 and P5 reduce to 5.24 eV, 5.17 eV, 5.51 eV, 5.49 eV, and 5.64 eV, respectively, while the corresponding LUMO energies increase to 4.00 eV, 4.12 eV, 4.21 eV, 4.05 eV, and 4.30 eV, meanwhile the Eg values decrease to 1.23 eV, 1.20 eV, 1.29 eV, 1.44 eV, and 1.34 eV, respectively, as the degree of polymerization tends to infinity. The calculated HOMO/LUMO energies of M1, D1, and T1 are 6.08 eV/3.79 eV, 5.62 eV/3.90 eV, and 5.54 eV/3.93 eV, respectively, showing that the calculated HOMO and LUMO energies of P1 gradually approach the experimental values (5.54 eV and 4.02 eV)12 with the increasing values of the repeat unit. Furthermore, it is appropriate to study the trimer (T1) for polymer P1, because the calculated HOMO/LUMO energy (5.54 eV/3.93 eV) for T1 is close to the experimental value of P1 (5.52 eV/4.02 eV).12

So we mainly analyze the HOMO and LUMO energies and Egs of trimers T1T5. The Eg is 1.60 eV for T1, 1.48 eV for T4, 1.44 eV for T5, 1.41 eV for T3, and 1.41 eV for T2. The Eg is indirectly proportional to the maximum absorption of the polymer. The lower Eg of T2, T3, T4, and T5 compared to that of T1 indicates that the newly designed polymers may enhance the short-circuit density (JSC) of the OSC compared to T1, because the lower energy gap will generate a broader absorption range which will increase the amount of charge carriers, thus leading to a higher PCE. The comparison of T2 with T1 reveals that the HOMO and the LUMO levels move upward from 5.54 eV to 5.31 eV and from 3.93 eV to 3.90 eV respectively, while the energy gap moves downward from 1.60 eV to 1.41 eV. This result is due to the introduction of the electron-donating BDT unit into T1. By replacing the carbon atoms in the conjugated bridges of T2 with nitrogen atoms in T4, the HOMO/LUMO energies decrease from 5.31 eV/3.90 eV to 5.50 eV/4.02 eV, respectively, leading to an increase in the energy gap from 1.41 eV to 1.48 eV. These results are due to the introduction of a stronger electron-withdrawing nitrogen atom in the conjugated bridge to replace a carbon atom in P2, which can significantly increase the electron-withdrawing ability of the conjugated bridges of the polymer. By the change of the NDI2OD unit in T2 to the NDTI unit in T3, the HOMO/LUMO levels decrease from 5.31 eV/3.90 eV to 5.53 eV/4.12 eV, while the energy gap is almost unchanged. These results are due to the enhancement of the conjugation degree of the copolymer backbone in NDTI than that in NDI2OD-T2. Besides that, changing the carbon atom on the NDTI unit in T3 to a stronger electron-withdrawing nitrogen atom in T5 can reduce the HOMO/LUMO energies from 5.53 eV/4.12 eV to 5.65 eV/4.22 eV, and increase the energy gap slightly from 1.41 eV to 1.44 eV. These results mean that control on the molecular planarity and the electron-withdrawing ability of the electron-deficient unit can tune both the HOMO/LUMO levels and energy gaps.

It has been experimentally found that the LUMO energy of an n-type air stable transistor should be below 4.0 eV.52 As can be seen that the extrapolated LUMO energies of the designed acceptors P2, P3, P4, and P5 are all below 4.0 eV, indicating that they might act as air-stable electron-transporting semiconductors in OSCs. Compared with PC60BM, the typical acceptor mostly used in OSCs with a LUMO energy of 4.3 eV,53 the designed polymer acceptors (P2, P3, P4 and P5) with higher LUMO energies are beneficial to improve the VOC of the OSC devices because the energy offset between the HOMO of the donor and the LUMO of the acceptor is directly related to the VOC.

As shown in Fig. 4 and Table 4, the maximum absorption of T5 is blue-shifted by only 4 nm in comparison with T1, but the light-absorbing efficiency and the absorption strength of T5 in the visible and near-infrared region increase significantly in comparison with T1. Consequently, taking , the maximum absorption wavelength, and the absorption region in the visible and near-infrared into account, the JSC can be improved with T2, T3, and T5 as acceptors.

3.4 Other factors affecting JSC

J SC is influenced by exciton dissociation, charge transfer, and the capability of charge photogeneration and so on. Next, we will analyze the electron density difference, the charge transfer factors, the exciton binding energy, and the Gibbs free energy loss in the charge dissociation process to reveal the performance of these factors.

3.4.2 Exciton binding energy and the Gibbs free energy loss. It is known that the acceptor of the organic active layer can harvest sunlight to generate excitons, followed by the transfer of holes from the HOMO of the acceptor to the HOMO of the donor, and subsequently the exciton diffuses until it arrives at the D/A interface.45,56 However, the holeelectron pair does not straightly separate into free charges which are bounded by a large exciton binding energy (Eb).45 Here, Eb is defined as the energy difference between the electronic and optical band gap energies. The electronic band gap is of the energy difference between the HOMO and the LUMO in the ground state, and the optical gap is taken as the first singlet excitation energy. For a charge transfer process of an acceptor exciton, the condition of exciton dissociation to be energetically favorable is EB,A smaller than IPA IPD. IPA and IPD are the ionization potentials of the acceptor and donor, which are about equal to the negative HOMO of the acceptor and the donor.57 The calculated HOMO value of donor J51 is 4.80 eV. This reveals that T1T5 can guarantee efficient exciton dissociation at the donor/acceptor interface. Compared with T1, T2 and T3 have smaller Eb data, indicating better performance of T2 and T3 for exciton dissociation.

On the other hand, an electron of the ground state in the donor would be excited upon illumination of light of a suitable wavelength. Then, an electron of excited state in J51 can spontaneously transfer to the acceptor when the Gibbs free energy loss (G) is a negative value.58 The key step is the dissociation of the electronhole pair into free charge carriers at the organic interface and the charge spread in the acceptor. Meanwhile, the hole and the electron can be collected by the electrodes. We can assess the ability of charge transfer through the Gibbs free energy loss (G), which is estimated from the RehmWeller equation as follows:49,54

G = IP(D) EA(A) ES1 Eb(3)
where IP(D) and EA(A) represent the ionization potentials of the polymer donor J51 and the electron affinity of the acceptor, respectively. ES1 and Eb are the energies of the S1 state of the free-base donor and exciton binding energy, respectively. The G data for the five trimers are summarized in Table 5.

As shown in Table 5, the G values of acceptors T1, T2, T3, T4, and T5 are 1.41 eV, 1.47 eV, 1.57 eV, 1.66 eV, and 1.76 eV, respectively. All the G values of the five acceptors are negative, implying that an electron of the donor in the excited state can spontaneously transfer to the acceptor. The G values of the designed trimers are lower than that of T1, which means that the excited electrons in the donor would easily transfer to the newly designed acceptor.

Combining the above two aspects (Eb and G), it can be seen that the designed acceptors T2, T3, and T5 can effectively promote the capability of charge carrier separation, potentially enhancing CT, and the photocurrent generation in OSCs.

3.5 Electron-harvesting ability of acceptors

In OSCs, high-electron-affinity of the acceptors has been certified to be an effective method for speedy exciton dissociation at the donor/acceptor interface, and the electron affinity of the acceptors must be large enough to permit effective injection of electrons into the empty LUMO of the semiconductor molecules.2,59 The electron affinity needs to be higher than 3.0 eV, but should not be much higher than 4.0 eV.60 The calculated vertical electron affinities (VEA) and adiabatic electron affinities (AEA) of T1T5 are given in Table 5.

As shown in Table 5, the VEA/AEA values of T1, T2, T3, T4, and T5 are 3.73 eV/3.80 eV, 3.67 eV/3.74 eV, 3.96 eV/4.00 eV, 3.79 eV/3.85 eV, and 4.05 eV/4.09 eV, respectively. This result shows that the electron affinities of the five trimers are all higher than 3.0 eV, and not much greater than 4.0 eV. So the electron affinities of the five trimers are appropriate to allow efficient injection of electrons into the empty LUMO of these acceptors, and the five trimers might be stable in air. Our results show that the five trimers can ensure efficient exciton dissociation at the donor/acceptor interface.

3.6 Performance of acceptors in intermolecular electron transport

Once the exciton splits into free charges in the charge transfer state at the D/A interface, the generated electrons and holes move along the interpenetrating network of the acceptors and donors toward their respective electrodes. In the OSC device, the active layer as a charge transport layer with high hole and electron mobility contributes to improving the charge transport (to increase JSC and FF). It is well known that the transfer integral is significantly influenced by the path of the intermolecular arrangement of the neighboring molecules. However, an accurate prediction of intermolecular -stacking paths for polymers is still a great challenge in theory.49,61 To date, many reports have shown that the face-to-face -stacking with a large orbital overlap has a major contribution to the intermolecular electron transport properties of organic semiconductors, which is beneficial to enhance the electronic coupling.44,49,61 Hence, we only consider the face-to-face parallel -stacking direction between the adjacent molecules for the intermolecular electron transport properties of these copolymers. The starting distances of the two piled molecules in the five investigated systems T1T5 are set as about 3.95 , which is the experimentally measured stacking distance in P1.12 The 3D models of the two adjacent fragments of T1T5 are given in Fig. S3 (ESI). 4. Conclusions

In this work, to explore more efficient acceptor materials for organic solar cells, we have designed four new DA conjugated polymers (P2, P3, P4, and P5) by incorporating the BDT unit in the experimentally synthesized polymer P1. To illustrate the optical and electronic properties, we studied their HOMO/LUMO energy levels, light-absorbing efficiencies, and electron affinities as well as electron mobility of the trimers (T1T5) by quantum-chemical calculation methods and Marcus theory. By comparison of T2 with T1, it is found that incorporation of the BDT unit in T2 not only yields a stronger and broader light absorption in the visible and near-infrared region, and enhances the light-absorbing efficiency and the exciton separation efficiency at the donor/acceptor interface, but also manifests high electron transport rate. Moreover, by comparing T5 with T3, we conclude that increasing the electron-withdrawing ability of the electron-deficient unit can further improve the exciton separation efficiency at the donor/acceptor interface and the electron mobility among the interchains. In our designed compounds, the polymers (P2 and P5) exhibit stronger and broader light absorption, high light absorption efficiencies and exciton separation abilities as well as higher electron mobility among all polymers, indicating that they are potential candidates for acceptor materials of OSCs. This study presents guidelines for designing efficient copolymer acceptors for organic solar cell applications.
