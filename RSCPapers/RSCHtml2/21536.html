Abstract

Advanced materials for electrocatalytic and photoelectrochemical water splitting are key for taking advantage of renewable energy. In this study, ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays with a nanoporous surface were fabricated via ion exchange and successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) processes. The ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample displays a high photocurrent density of 12.0 mA cmâˆ’2 under AM 1.5G illumination, achieves the highest IPCE value of 89.5% at 500 nm at a bias potential of 0.2 V versus Ag/AgCl, and exhibits greatly improved photostability. The functions of the ZnSe, CdSe, and CuxS layers in the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS heterostructure were clarified. ZnSe is used as a passivation layer to reduce the trapping and recombination of charge carriers at the interfaces of the semiconductors. CdSe functions as a highly efficient visible light absorber and builds heterojunctions with the other components to improve the separation and transportation of the photoinduced electrons and holes. CuxS serves as a passivation layer and an effective p-type hole mediator, which passivates the defects and surface states of the semiconductors and forms pâ€“n junctions with CdSe to promote the hole transportation at the semiconductorâ€“electrolyte interface. The nanoporous surface of the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays, together with the tunnel transportation of the charge carriers in the thin films of ZnSe and CdSe, also facilitates the kinetics of photoelectrochemical reactions and improves the optical absorption as well.

1. Introduction

Making full use of the enormous solar energy on the Earth is a promising solution to resolve the energy crisis and energy-related environmental problems.1â€“3 Nowadays, there are still a number of obstacles to achieve efficient capture, conversion, and storage of solar energy.4 Photoelectrochemical (PEC) water splitting offers an ideal route to realize the conversion of solar energy to renewable and clean hydrogen fuel.5â€“8 At present, the crux of this energy conversion process lies in finding highly efficient and stable photoelectrodes.9â€“13 Though a variety of semiconductor materials have been investigated as photoanodes or photocathodes, the intrinsic properties of a single semiconductor often restrict its efficiency of photoelectrochemical water splitting. For example, wide bandgap semiconductors such as TiO2 and ZnO usually demonstrate poor photocatalytic properties under sunlight irradiation, because they can only take advantage of UV light, which makes up less than 5% of solar energy,14â€“16 whereas narrow band gap semiconductors, such as CdSe and CdS, often suffer from severe recombination of charge carriers and photocorrosion though they can capture most of the sunlight.17 An effective way to cope with these issues is to build heterojunctions, which can take advantage of not only the excellent visible light absorption of narrow band gap semiconductors, but also the high electron mobility and stability of wide band gap semiconductors. Moreover, the modulation of crystalline structures and the modification of semiconductor surfaces by chemical and physical treatments provide additional routes to adjust their photocatalytic properties.18â€“20

ZnO nanowire arrays possess improved light scattering characteristics, high surface-to-volume ratio and effective electron transport pathways along the longitudinal direction and thus are considered to be excellent candidates for constructing photoelectrochemical systems with high performance.21â€“25 ZnO nanowire arrays sheathed with narrow band gap semiconductor shells with favorable band alignments, which achieve enhanced light absorption and improved photocatalytic properties, have emerged as novel photoelectrodes for photoelectrochemical systems or solar cells.26â€“30 CdSe is an n-type semiconductor with a narrow band gap of 1.74 eV. Owing to its large extinction coefficient and appropriate band gap, its light absorption is well matched with the solar spectrum. Chouhan and his colleagues fabricated CdSe QD-sensitized ZnO nanorod arrays for photoelectrochemical water splitting. A 6-fold increase in photocurrent density was observed for CdSe QD-sensitized nanorod arrays over pristine ZnO samples and the highest photocurrent density of 2.48 mA cmâˆ’2 was registered at an applied voltage of 0 V and a power density of 100 mW cmâˆ’2.31 Nevertheless, the defect and surface states of ZnO and CdSe will inevitably cause trapping and recombination of the charge carriers at the semiconductor interfaces, which limits their photoelectrochemical activity. To reduce the depletion and leakage of photoinduced electrons and holes, a buffer or passivation layer positioned between the selenide and the oxide is necessary. ZnSe is an environmentally friendly n-type semiconductor with a band gap of 2.67 eV. Due to its higher conduction band edge compared to ZnO, it can cooperate with ZnO to construct type-II heterojunctions32 and function as a buffer or passivation layer in ZnO-based photovoltaic applications to contribute to their superior photovoltaic performance.33 Furthermore, analogous to CdS, CdSe suffers from photocorrosion in aqueous solutions and exhibits poor hole transfer between the semiconductors and electrolytes.34 Polysulfide electrolytes are adopted to prolong the stability of cadmium chalcogenide sensitizers, but the results are not satisfactory to date.35 In solar cells, CuI, CuSCN and CuxS usually act as effective and cheap inorganic p-type hole-transporting materials to improve the interfacial charge transfer kinetics.36â€“38 When CuxS, which is a group of narrow band gap semiconductors with p-type behavior, is deposited onto CdSe, it serves as a surface passivation layer and an effective p-type hole mediator to reduce the photocorrosion of CdSe. In addition, pâ€“n junctions may form between CuxS and the selenides, which facilitate the separation and transportation of photo-induced electronâ€“hole pairs.

In addition, the surface structure also plays a critical role on the photoelectrochemical properties of an electrode. When compared with a flat surface, a nanoporous surface provides larger surface area and more reactive sites, which improve the kinetics of the photoelectrochemical processes. Moreover, light scattering within the nanoporous structure enhances the absorption of light. When the particle size is smaller than the hole diffusion length of the semiconductor, bulk carrier recombination can be effectively suppressed.20,39,43,44

Based on the aforementioned points, we have constructed ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays with nanoporous surfaces via a facile ion exchange and successive ionic layer adsorption and reaction (SILAR) methods. In situ ion exchange ensures the formation of close and uniform layers on ZnO nanowires. The proportion of the different components, which influences the optical absorption and photoelectrochemical properties of the heterostructure, was optimized by adjusting the preparation conditions. The ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell heterostructure revealed a high and stable photocurrent density under AM 1.5G illumination. The absence of CdSe, ZnSe or CuxS layer decreases the photoelectrochemical activity and stability, which proves the indispensable contribution of the shell layers to its high photoelectrochemical performance. Therefore, it can be concluded that constructing coreâ€“shell heterostructures with nanoporous surfaces and taking advantage of the specific functional layers holds great promise for building highly efficient and stable photocatalysts for solar cells and photoelectrochemical devices.

3.2 Photoelectrochemical properties

Electrochemical measurements such as the currentâ€“potential characteristic and the potentiostatic currentâ€“time transient response testing were adopted to evaluate the photoelectrochemical properties of the prepared samples. 3.3 Mechanism

Fig. 8 shows the electrochemical impedance spectra (Nyquist plots) of the samples scanned from 105 to 1 Hz both in the dark and under visible light. The Nyquist plots can help interpret the separation efficiency of the photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs, as well as the charge transfer resistance. A smaller circular radius corresponds to more efficient separation and transportation of the charge carriers.42 As depicted in Fig. 8, the circular radius of the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample is the smallest among the samples both in dark and under visible light. The fact helps interpret the improvement of photoelectrochemical properties via the deposition of CuxS, which benefits from the enhanced charge separation and transportation efficiency. In addition, the circular radius of the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe sample is smaller than that of ZnO, ZnO/ZnSe, and ZnO/CdSe. Thus, it can be inferred that the attachment of ZnSe and CdSe facilitates the separation and transportation of electrons and holes in the heterostructure, while the depletion of the ZnSe layer increases the charge transfer resistance at the interfaces.
Fig. 8 Electrochemical impedance spectra for the (A) ZnO, (B) ZnO/ZnSe, (C) ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe, (D) ZnO/CdSe, and (E) ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS samples under visible light (solid lines) and in the dark (dash lines).

To evaluate the photoelectrochemical efficiency of the samples as a function of illumination wavelength, the IPCE measurements were conducted at a bias potential of âˆ’0.2 V versus Ag/AgCl. Fig. 9 displays the IPCE curves for the ZnO, ZnO/ZnSe, ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe, ZnO/CdSe, and ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS samples. The photoresponse of the ZnO sample is limited in the UV region; the ZnO/ZnSe sample extends its photoresponse to the visible region. The photoresponse range of the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe sample is radically enlarged to 730 nm, which is in good agreement with the results obtained from the UV-vis spectrum. In addition, the IPCE under visible light is significantly amplified. This is related to the narrow band gap and multiple exciton generation effect of CdSe, which results in the enhancement of light absorption and an increase in the number of electronâ€“hole pairs generated per incident photon flux. The depletion of the ZnSe layer reduces the entire IPCE values of the ZnO/CdSe sample in the whole region. This is because the absence of ZnSe increases the trapping and recombination of electrons and holes at the semiconductor interfaces, which decreases the utilization efficiency of the photoinduced charge carriers. The ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample has a slightly extended wavelength range of photoresponse compared with the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe sample; in addition, the IPCE in both the UV and visible regions increases and a highest IPCE of 89.5% is achieved at 500 nm. It is worth-noting that the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample clearly shows infrared absorption according to the UV-vis DRS results, whereas the IPCE values in the near infrared region is negligible. This might result from the competitive infrared absorption of the electrolyte. The CuxS layer directly passivates the defect states on the CdSe surface and functions as an effective p-type hole mediator layer to promote the transportation of holes toward the semiconductorâ€“electrolyte interface. The pâ€“n junctions formed between CuxS and CdSe improves the efficiency of interfacial separation and the transportation of charge carriers, all of which favor the increase of the IPCE.

According to the results described above, ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays present ultra-high visible light photoelectrochemical activity and high stability. As both the starting material and the core of the samples, ZnO nanowire arrays endow them nanoforest-like characteristics and offer direct pathways for efficient electron transportation. The ZnSe layer can block the surface states of ZnO and decrease the recombination possibility of the photoinduced charge carriers between ZnO and CdSe. The CdSe layer is adopted as a highly efficient visible light captor to produce the photogenerated electronâ€“hole pairs. Moreover, the nanoporous CdSe layer is composed of nanocrystals, which suppress bulk carrier recombination and provide a high specific surface area and appreciable reactive sites to improve the kinetics of the photoelectrochemical reactions, as well as a rough surface to increase the harvest and utilization of incident light. The coreâ€“shell heterostructures also facilitate the separation and transportation of the photoinduced electrons and holes. Further deposition of CuxS passivates the defect and surface states in the semiconductors and promotes hole transportation at the semiconductorâ€“electrolyte interface, which decreases the photocorrosion of CdSe and improves the stability of the heterostructure. The pâ€“n junctions formed between CdSe and CuxS further enhance the photoelectrochemical performance of the nanowire arrays. Scheme 2 illustrates the behaviors of the photoinduced charge carriers in the photoelectrochemical water splitting process over the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample. The ion exchange on the walls of the nanowires may result in an uneven coating. In some domains of the coreâ€“shell nanowires, the intermediate ZnSe layer between ZnO and CdSe is so thin (1â€“0 nm) that the tunnel transportation of the charge carriers is realized. The tunneling could also happen in the CdSe layer. So upon visible light illumination, the photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs emerge in CuxS, CdSe, and ZnSe. The photoinduced electrons are excited to the conduction bands of the semiconductors and the holes are left at the corresponding valence bands. Due to the potential gradients originating from the relative positions of their energy levels, the excited electrons in CuxS flow to the conduction band of CdSe and then, together with the electrons from CdSe and ZnSe, cascade to the conduction band of ZnO. The electrons are collected, transferred through the ZnO nanowires to the FTO substrate, and then go through the external circuit to the counter electrode to produce hydrogen. In addition, the holes in the valence bands of ZnSe and CdSe flow to those of CuxS and then react with the hole scavengers in the polysulfide electrolyte. The nanoporous surface, coreâ€“shell heterostructure, appropriate bandgap alignment, and specific functions of the different layers in the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample contribute to its high photoelectrochemical performance.

4. Conclusions

ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays perpendicularly aligned on FTO glass substrates were constructed using sequential ion exchange and SILAR processes. A single nanowire in the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample is composed of a ZnO core, which is covered tightly by conformal ZnSe, CdSe and CuxS multiple layers as the shells. The ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe/CuxS sample demonstrates a high photocurrent density of 12.0 mA cmâˆ’2, very high incident photon to electron conversion efficiency, and greatly improved photostability. In such a coreâ€“shell heterostructure, the ZnO core serves as the pathway for efficient electron collection and transportation. ZnSe is used as a passivation layer to block the surface states of ZnO and reduce the trapping and recombination of the charge carriers at the semiconductor interfaces. CdSe functions as a highly efficient absorber to capture a great quantity of visible light and build coreâ€“shell heterostructures of favorable band gap alignment with other semiconductors to improve the separation and transportation of the photoinduced electronâ€“hole pairs. The ZnSe and CdSe layers are composed of nanoparticles with small size, which form a nanoporous surface to improve the optical absorption and facilitate the kinetics of photoelectrochemical processes. The CuxS serves as a passivation layer and a hole mediator, which passivates the defect and surface states of the semiconductors and reduces the photocorrosion of CdSe. Thus, the photostability of the sample is greatly improved. In addition, the pâ€“n junction formed between CuxS and CdSe promotes hole transportation at the semiconductorâ€“electrolyte interface. All these contribute to the enhancement of the photoelectrochemical performance of the ZnO/ZnSe/CdSe coreâ€“shell nanowire arrays. This study demonstrates that the construction of coreâ€“shell heterostructures and the utilization of specific functional layers hold great promise for massively fabricating highly efficient and stable photocatalysts for photoelectrochemical hydrogen production or other optoelectronic devices.
