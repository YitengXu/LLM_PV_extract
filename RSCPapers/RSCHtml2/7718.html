Abstract

Lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries are severely hindered by low sulfur utilization and short cycling lives, especially at high sulfur loading levels. An effective solution to address these problems is to improve the sulfiphilicity of lithium polysulfides (LiPS) and the lithiophilicity of the lithium anode. However, it is a great challenge to simultaneously optimize both aspects. To solve these problems, herein, a modification strategy involving a functional separator is used, coating a composite including MoP nanorods decorated with a N-doped thin carbon layer (MoP@NC) and porous carbon nanofibers (PCNFs) onto the surface of a Celgard separator. The obtained MoP@NC material, derived from a molybdenum-based MOF (Mo-MOF), possesses abundant porosity and multiple catalytic sites, with uniform and stable rod-shaped morphology. The PCNFs and N-doped thin carbon layer can effectively alleviate volume expansion, buffer the electrolyte, and trap LiPS. When applied as a modification layer coated on the separator, the MoP@NC/PCNFs-based cell with optimized lithiophilicity and sulfiphilicity enables desirable sulfur electrochemistry, including high reversibility of 902.9 mA h gâˆ’1 after 400 cycles at 1C and 654.5 mA h gâˆ’1 after 500 cycles at 2C. This work provides a novel strategy by which MoP derived from a MOF can be applied in the design of high-performance Liâ€“S batteries.

1. Introduction

With the development of human society and the worsening of environmental pollution, it has become urgent to establish a balance between meeting increasing energy demands and alleviating growing pressure on the environment.1,2 Electricity can be directly obtained from clean energy sources, such as wind power, hydroelectric power, solar energy, etc., but these clean energy sources are all intermittent. Therefore, it is particularly indispensable to explore advanced energy storage and conversion devices with excellent performance.3â€“5 Therefore, research into efficient and sustainable energy storage devices is becoming more and more important. In addition, as a representative energy storage device, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have already found great use in portable electronics.6,7 However, with the ever-increasing demand for next-generation energy storage systems, traditionally commercial LIBs cannot meet ever-increasing energy demands due to their limited energy density.8,9 Lithiumâ€“sulfur (Liâ€“S) batteries, as next-generation electrochemical storage device candidates, have attracted intense interest owing to their high theoretical specific capacity (1675 mA h gâˆ’1) and energy density (2600 W h kgâˆ’1), their environmentally benign nature, the abundance of the raw resources, and the low cost of elemental sulfur.10,11 Despite these multiple virtues, the practical implementation of Liâ€“S batteries is still impeded by several major shortcomings, including the limited conductivity of elemental sulfur and Li2S2/Li2S, severe LiPS shuttling, and large volumetric expansion during cycling.12,13 These disadvantages will inevitably cause low levels of sulfur utilization and sluggish reaction kinetics, thereby resulting in unsatisfactory electrochemical performance. In the past few decades, great effort has been applied to overcome these issues, with the following main approaches: (1) the design and preparation of cathode materials with special structures; (2) configuring novel types of functional electrolytes; and (3) developing functional separators.14,15 Among these, the rational design, preparation, and application of functional separators in batteries has been confirmed as an effective and convenient way to advance the performance and support the future practical application of Liâ€“S batteries.16,17

A separator is a crucial component of Liâ€“S batteries; while ordinary separators allow the transmission of lithium ions, LiPS can also easily diffuse through the separator to the lithium anode.18,19 During cycling, insoluble low-order LiPS (Li2S/Li2S2) can form an insulating layer on the anode surface, which is inconvenient for the conduction of lithium ions and results in serious polarization. In order to minimize the drawbacks, diverse pure carbonaceous materials (porous carbon,20 carbon nanotubes (CNTs),21 graphene oxide,22 carbon quantum dots,23etc.) have been investigated and applied as functional layers to coat separators. These functional separators with a pure carbon coating layer can reduce the shuttle effect of LiPS through physical adsorption. However, the poor interaction between nonpolar carbon-based materials and polar LiPS usually cannot effectively inhibit the â€œshuttle effectâ€ of LiPS, especially under high sulfur loading conditions.9,24 To enhance the trapping of LiPS, some carbon-based materials have been doped with heteroatoms (N,25 P,26 O,27 S,28 and B29), which leads to improved electrochemical performance. In contrast, polar materials (such as metal hydroxides,30 metal oxides,31 metal sulfides,32 metal nitrides,33 metal carbides,34etc.) have been studied with interest because of their strong chemical adsorption of LiPS and excellent electrocatalytic activity. These attempts not only curb the LiPS shuttle effect via combining physical shielding and chemical adsorption, but unreacted LiPS can also be recycled, guaranteeing to reduce the shuttle effect and improve sulfur utilization, effectively enhancing electrochemical performance.

In addition, recently, metal organic frameworks (MOFs) have been intensively investigated for their applications in energy storage and conversion. Owing to the fact that MOFs are composed of organic ligands and metal ions with high porosity and tunability, various MOF-derived nanostructures can be obtained through precise subsequent treatment, including transition metal oxides (TMOs),35,36 carbides (TMCs),37 phosphides (TMPs),38,39 and so on. Catalysts derived from MOFs possess several merits which support their application in electrocatalysis: (i) metal-based nanoparticles generated during carbonization may be effective for electrocatalysis due to their combination of strong catalytic performance and good conductivity;40,41 and (ii) MOF-derived products with a porous nature are beneficial for mass transportation, hence contributing to the improvement of catalytic performance during electrocatalysis.2,42 Among the available materials, TMPs derived from MOFs usually have high theoretical capacitance and excellent conductivity. These merits are attributed to the lower electronegativity of elemental P in phosphides compared with elemental O and C in the corresponding oxides and carbides, which can allow fast electronic transport and facilitate active redox reactions in the case of TMPs.43,44 The P atoms on the surface of TMPs also endow them with inherent charge (MÎ´+ and PÎ´âˆ’); therefore, they can coordinate with LiPS (MÎ´+) and form bonds with sulfur atoms (PÎ´âˆ’), thereby showing high activity during LiPS adsorption.45 Thus, TMPs (e.g., MoP,46 Ruâ€“Mo4P3,47etc.) can initially be applied in the cathodes of Liâ€“S batteries. Nonetheless, the unique advantages of TMPs cannot be fully realized in these assembled Liâ€“S batteries when they act as the sulfur host due to low sulfur loading and average rate performance (â‰¤2C), even at a relatively low areal sulfur loading. Therefore, TMPs have gradually been exploited as separator modifiers. Duan et al. described a multifunctional interlayer composed of MoP2 nanoparticles derived from molybdenum sheets and CNT film, where CNT film acted as a conductive skeleton to support the MoP2 nanoparticles; this was regarded as a current collector and provided abundant electron pathways. A Liâ€“S battery containing the multifunctional MoP2/CNT interlayer showed a reversible specific capacity of 905 mA h gâˆ’1 over 100 cycles at 0.2C, with capacity decay of 0.152% per cycle.48 As is well known, MOF-derived doped carbon and metal compounds possess large surface areas, low charge transfer resistance, and favorable LiPS adsorption abilities. Moreover, converting elemental metal atoms in MOF compounds to metal compounds (e.g., CoP), with stronger adsorption capacities and electrochemical activities, can further enhance the performance of Liâ€“S batteries because of the large surface area, high porosity, diverse composition, and easy construction. Zhang et al. synthesized CoP with a N-doped carbon nanocage derived from ZIF-67 as an efficient sulfur host for use in advanced Liâ€“S batteries, and Liâ€“S batteries assembled with the CoPâ€“HNC electrode showed significantly enhanced sulfur utilization (about 800 mA h gâˆ’1 at 5C), improved cycling stability, and an ultralow capacity decay rate of 0.02% per cycle after 1000 cycles.49

Previously, our group has made some explorations relating to porous carbon nanofibers (PCNFs) and PCNFs decorated with LaF3,50 CeF3,51 YF3,52etc. as sulfur hosts for lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries, and they showed excellent electrochemical stability. Herein, we first synthesized MoP nanorods decorated with a N-doped thin carbon layer using Mo-MOF as a precursor, utilizing MoO3 and imidazole as raw materials, and we then coated these with porous carbon nanofibers (PCNFs), prepared using PVP nanofibers as a precursor, with electrostatic blowing technology used to coat one side of a Celgard 2340 separator (labeled MoP@NC/PCNFs) for use in Liâ€“S batteries for the first time. The PCNFs can shorten the ion/electron transport paths, alleviate sulfur volume changes during charge/discharge processes, and provide abundant active adsorption sites for adsorbing LiPS. More importantly, the prepared MoP@NC nanorods were capable of capturing LiPS to form Liâ€“P, Pâ€“S, Nâ€“Li, and strengthened Moâ€“S bonds. The resulting battery with the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator exhibited mitigated self-discharge behavior and remarkable reversible capacities of 902.9 mA h gâˆ’1 after 400 cycles at 1C and 654.5 mA h gâˆ’1 after 500 cycles at 2C. Even under high sulfur loading conditions (3.8 mg cmâˆ’2), the areal capacity of the cell with the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator is still maintained at 612.1 mA h gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles at 0.5C.

To investigate the hydrophilicity of the separator before and after modification, dynamic electrolyte contact angle characterization was performed, as shown in Fig. 5. EIS is often employed to study the electrochemical behavior of cells. Fig. 6a shows the EIS curves of cells with MoP@NC/PCNFs, Mo-MOF/PCNFs, and Celgard separators before cycling. The Nyquist curves comprise a semicircle in the medium-to-high-frequency region (implying charge transfer impedance at the interface between the electrolyte and cathode) and a diagonal line in the low-frequency region (signifying diffusion impedance related to LiPS in the electrolyte).66 The fresh MoP@NC/PCNFs cell manifested a low Re value of 3.65 Î©, which was obviously smaller than those of the Mo-MOF/PCNFs (6.94 Î©) and Celgard separator counterparts (15.12 Î©), due to the increase in the hydrophilicity of MoP@NC/PCNFs and the reduction in the diffusion resistance of the liquid electrolyte. MoP@NC/PCNFs possessed a much smaller semicircle diameter, demonstrating faster charge transfer at the interface of MoP@NC/PCNFs and the electrode; this was attributed to the rapid conversion kinetics of soluble LiPS due to the high conductivity of PCNFs and N-doped thin carbon, electrocatalysis due to MoP nanorods, and an advanced ion diffusion rate.67,68 Additionally, the inclined line at low frequency reflects the speed of ion diffusion into the electrode material, and the finding that MoP@NC/PCNFs has the highest slope among these samples certified its superior ion diffusion properties for LiPS conversion.69 Consequently, it can be speculated that the prepared MoP@NC/PCNFs material had minimal polarization due to the synergistic combination of excellent conductivity, satisfactory physical and chemical adsorption of LiPS, and efficient electrocatalytic capacity. Furthermore, the charge transfer resistance clearly confirms that the MoP@NC/PCNFs-modified separator possesses a more conductive and stable structure than the pristine separator, resulting in excellent electrochemical performance in the Liâ€“S cell. Impedance data were also collected after cycling (Fig. S9â€ ) to evaluate any changes in charge transfer resistance. As anticipated, the charge transfer resistance of the cell with the MoP@NC/PCNFs-modified separator manifested a lower Rct value (4.21 Î©) than the cell with a pristine separator (28.6 Î©), which is mainly due to the high electrical conductivity and improved electrolyte wettability between the separator and sulfur cathode.

It can be found that the lithium anode disassembled in the cell with the Celgard separator, presenting a very uneven surface with many irregular particles and lots of holes (Fig. 10c and d); this resulted from serious side reactions between the shuttled LiPS and lithium metal during cycling. Simultaneously, in order to better understand the function of the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator in suppressing lithium dendrites and to reveal the advantages of the functional separator, the cycling performances of Liâ€–Li symmetric batteries are shown in Fig. S10.â€  The lithium symmetric battery assembled with the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator yielded a voltage of 0.015 mV in the 1st cycle and the voltage was almost maintained at this level for 500 h. These voltage fluctuations likely come from unstable SEI formation, which results in changes in the surface morphology of lithium during cycling. It is apparent that the voltage profile of the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator is more stable over time. All the results further confirm that the prepared MoP@NC/PCNFs separator can facilitate uniform lithium nucleation and growth due to the synergistic effects of effective chemical/physical restriction and the improved interfacial conductivity, subsequently allowing the lithium metal anode to achieve high cycling stability.87,88 Lithium dendrites originate from the disproportionate deposition of lithium during chargingâ€“discharging processes.89 MoP@NC/PCNFs should be credited with capturing LiPS via both chemical adsorption and physical suppression, and only a small amount of LiPS can diffuse to the anode, which can retard the corrosion of lithium. In addition, the uniform distribution of the MoP@NC/PCNFs coating layer was conductive to restraining the formation of dendrites via facilitating the homogeneous dissolution and deposition of lithium.90

4. Conclusions

In summary, we elaborately designed and prepared a MoP@NC/PCNFs composite comprising MoP@NC nanorods and PCNFs through annealing Mo-MOF and using electrostatic blowing technology, respectively, and applied it as a multifunctional sulfiphilic and lithiophilic mediator in Liâ€“S batteries. The obtained MoP material has almost the same morphology as Mo-MOF nanorods, exhibiting high porosity and a uniformly embedded N-doped thin carbon layer. The N-doped thin carbon layer can not only limit LiPS through physical action, but it also prevents MoP from being oxidized. In addition, the MoP nanorods with high porosity and abundant active sites can effectively trap LiPS, significantly catalyze the redox kinetics during polysulfide conversion, and provide fast paths for lithium ion diffusion. Also, the high conductivity of PCNFs with N doping accelerates redox reactions at the separator surface and is conducive to inhibiting the â€œshuttle effectâ€ of LiPS during cell cycling. The combination of PCNFs with N doping and a N-doped thin carbon layer has a synergistic effect on electron transfer and electrocatalytic activity. As a result, a Liâ€“S cell with the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator delivers high specific capacities of 1139.9 mA h gâˆ’1 at 1C and 1045.0 mA h gâˆ’1 at 2C and excellent cycling stability (902.9 mA h gâˆ’1 after 400 cycles at 1C with a low capacityâ€“decay rate of 0.05% per cycle and 654.5 mA h gâˆ’1 after 500 cycles at 2C with a low capacityâ€“decay rate of 0.08% per cycle). A Liâ€“S cell with sulfur loading of 3.8 mg cmâˆ’2 displays excellent cycling performance; of the initial discharge capacity of 952.7 mA h gâˆ’1, it retains 612.1 mA h gâˆ’1 after 200 cycles (fading rate of 0.18%). Also, a Liâ€“S cell with the MoP@NC/PCNFs separator delivers high specific capacities of 1193.4, 1112.4, and 1008.8 mA h gâˆ’1 at 0.2C, 0.5C, and 1C, respectively. When the current density returns to 1C, 0.5C, and 0.2C, MoP@NC/PCNFs shows high reversible capacities, retaining 80.1%, 83.5%, and 85.7%, respectively, of the initial discharge specific capacity (0.2C). This work will provide an effective strategy for the design and preparation of anchoring and catalytic separators for use in advanced lithiumâ€“sulfur batteries.
