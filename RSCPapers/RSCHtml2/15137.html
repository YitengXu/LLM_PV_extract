Abstract

The need for energy storage and conversion is growing as a result of the worsening consequences of climate change and the depletion of fossil fuels. Energy conversion and storage requirements are rising as a result of environmental problems including global warming and the depletion of fossil fuels. The key to resolving the energy crisis is anticipated to be the quick growth of sustainable energy sources including solar energy, wind energy, and hydrogen energy. In this review, we have focused on discussing various quantum dots (QDs) and polymers or nanocomposites used for SCs and have provided examples of each type's performance. Effective QD use has really led to increased performance efficiency in SCs. The use of quantum dots in energy storage devices, batteries, and various quantum dots synthesis have all been emphasized in a number of great literature articles. In this review, we have homed in on the electrode materials based on quantum dots and their composites for storage and quantum dot based flexible devices that have been published up to this point.

1. Introduction

Quantum dots (QDs) exhibit size dependent characteristics, strong photostability, and good charge carrier mobility, as well as band gap tunability with appropriate surface treatment, making them appealing alternatives for low-cost, solution processed optoelectronic devices. QDs have been explored as luminous materials for over ten years. In contrast to photolysis, the luminescence mechanism of QDs includes radiative combination of photo-generated carriers. Semiconductor QDs have been adsorbed on photocatalysts in recent years to increase visible-light photoactivity.2 QDs, a name for semiconductor nanoparticles, are distinguished by having diameters that are typically smaller than 100 nm (Fig. 1).

They have unique characteristics that differ from those of the bulk counterpart materials as a result of their small size. They also have a large band gap, which for ZnS at ambient temperature has a specific value of 3.66 eV.3 It is feasible to stimulate QDs in a large wavelength range, below the wavelengths of fluorescence because they have very intriguing fluorescence features.4 The emission wavelength strongly varies depending on the particle size as well as the structure of the corresponding phase. They perform more fluorescence-wise than traditional organic dyes. In addition, the particle size, which is directly related to their technique of synthesis, the type, and amount of the doping element, has a significant impact on the quantum yield fluorescence and emission spectrum.5 QDs were thought to be among the finest possibilities for a wide range of uses based on these specific properties, particularly those focused on electroluminescent research, photovoltaic panels, infrared windows, and dielectric filters. As applications have been extended to various fields in biology, chemistry and physics, many new exigencies in the native nanoparticles are required.6 Producing quantum dots on a large scale is vital for achieving the objective of incorporating quantum dot technology in different sectors like biomedicine, energy, and electronics. In a recent study conducted by Junfei Ma et al., they were able to successfully create multi-color CDs using heavy oil and its three SDA products: light deasphalted oil (LDAO), heavy deasphalted oil (HDAO), and asphalt. Among these precursors, the CDs obtained from asphalt had the highest yield of up to 64%, which is the highest recorded so far.7 On the other hand, Shouzhen Li et al. present a novel approach to building white light-emitting diodes (WLEDs) using machine learning guided V4C3 MXene quantum dots. The study used machine learning algorithms to predict the optimal conditions for synthesizing quantum dots, and the resulting particles exhibited excellent thermal and photostability. This study offers significant contributions to the field of quantum dot technology.8 Quan Xu et al. present an innovative approach to the synthesis of quantum dots using microwave-assisted techniques with narrow size distribution and machine learning algorithms. They also show the potential of these quantum dots for use in biomedical imaging, specifically for detecting residual H2O2 in human teeth.9

The quantum yield of carbon-based quantum dots is an important parameter that determines their efficiency in various applications. To increase the quantum yield of as-prepared carbon-based quantum dots, certain parameters like heteroatom doping and edge effects need to be optimized. In recent studies, heteroatom doping was used to introduce heteroatoms, such as nitrogen or sulfur, into carbon-based quantum dots, which can improve their electronic properties and increase their quantum yield. On the other hand, edge effects, which refer to the surface structure of the quantum dots, have been found to play a significant role in their quantum yield enhancement. Increasing the number of edge sites on the surface of carbon-based quantum dots can lead to higher quantum yields. Overall, optimizing these parameters, namely heteroatom doping and edge effects, can significantly enhance the quantum yield of carbon-based quantum dots, making them more effective in various applications.

Recently, a new category of carbon nanostructures known as carbon-based nanocrystals (C-QDs) has received a lot of attention, particularly in the field of energy applications. C-QDs have recently undergone a thorough examination for photocatalytic applications. Graphene quantum dots (GQDs) are mono- or very few graphene sheets having lateral dimensions smaller than 100 nm, which are a subclass of C-QDs.10 GQDs are widely used in many fields of science, such as biology, sensors, and energy conversion.11 They have distinctive qualities, such as their outstanding up-conversion and size dependent photoluminescence, durable chemical inertness, high water solubility, and low cytotoxicity. GQDs have also lately been investigated for use in photocatalytic processes. The GQDs and semiconductors have a strong chemical connection that makes their composites more stable with improved charge transfer and higher photocatalytic activity.12 The three categories into which the most commonly used QDs can be broadly divided are: (i) cadmium chalcogenide QDs, which include CdS, CdSe, and CdTe and their own alloyed nanocrystals;13 (ii) top spot chalcogenide QDs, which include PbS, PbSe, and PbTe and their alloyed nanocrystals;14 and (iii) a new type of QD. For solar applications, various co-sensitization systems, such as CdS/CdSe, have also been investigated to improve light harvesting and decrease charge recombination.15 The insufficient light harvesting caused by the narrow spectrum response constrained to the UV visible area is one of the significant problems restricting the enhanced performance for a variety of QDSCs, as is well known. As a result, if infrared light beyond 700 nm can be successfully utilized, which makes up about half of solar energy, photovoltaics should perform significantly better. Due to its near-infrared (NIR) sensitive light harvesting, lead chalcogenide QD-based photovoltaics, particularly PbS QDSCs,16 have been widely explored recently among all of these options. PbS has a high exciton Bohr radius (r = 18 nm) and a limited range gap (Eg = 0.41 eV), which makes it possible to fully utilize solar energy and has high potential to actualize the MEG effect.17

CDs and graphene are both used to create GQDs, which have a diameter under 20 nm.18 This 0-D nanomaterial is a great probe for better contrast image and sensing applications because of its steady luminosity, high solubility in a variety of solvents, strong biocompatibility, potential for edge functionalization, and acceptable conductivity. Additionally, other QDs (CdS, CdSe, ZnS, ZnSe, PbS, HgTe, Ag2Se, Ag2S, CuInSe2, CuInS2, InAs, and InP)19 that are not employed in biological systems because of the presence of toxic metals have been suggested as a less harmful substitute for GQDs.20 Furthermore, GQDs are easily functionalized with oxygen-containing groups like hydroxyl, carboxyl, and epoxy groups to improve their hydrophilicity and biocompatibility.21 The answer to global warming, the energy problem, and environmental pollution is decarbonized clean energy like solar, wind, and geothermal energy.22 New energy will take over as the primary source of electricity in the framework of decarbonization, and the storage of huge volumes of renewable power will be a significant difficulty. These energy sources must be maintained and released at any moment because they are naturally intermittent. The primary solution to this problem will be electrochemical energy storage, such as batteries and super-capacitors.23 Additionally, photocatalytic hydrogen evolution relies on promoting electrochemical hydride creation and photochemical bonding to create fuel, which is another “green” way to store energy.24 As a result of their own large specific area, extendable size, quick electron transfer path, non-toxicity, low price, adjustable photoluminescence, and simple surface functionalization, quantum dots have received a lot of attention recently as possible future electrochemical energy storage devices. The electrode materials employed have a significant influence on the overall efficiency of an electrical energy storage system.25 The surface of QDs is rich in heteroatomic functional groups, offering a variety of active locations. They demonstrate excellent conductivity, high rate, big capacities, and cycle stability,26 which greatly improves the efficiency of electrochemical storage devices.27 They can be employed as composites for conductive substrates and active electrodes for storage devices. Quan Xu et al. reported the review summarizes the applications of quantum dots (QDs) in energy storage and conversion systems such as lithium–sulfur batteries, supercapacitors, and photocatalytic hydrogen production. However, there are imitations of the synthetic QDs strategy and the scarcity of systematic research on QD composites is expected to encounter an explosive growth in the near future.28 Himadri Tanaya Das et al. reported the quantum dots (QDs) and polymer nanocomposites have been utilized in SCs to improve their performance, with carbon-based QDs being the most effective. Ongoing research is focused on utilizing waste materials for SC components, leading to reduced costs and more sustainable energy solutions.29 Hence, the objective of this review is to discuss the impact of advancements in energy conversion and storage technologies on the development of next-generation technology. The review also highlights the importance of batteries and supercapacitors in modern electronics and their potential to be used in future energy-oriented technologies. Additionally, the review provides an overview of the use of quantum dots and nanocomposites in supercapacitors and their contributions to other applications such as medication delivery, biosensors, and electrochemical devices. The objective is to provide insight into the current state of research on these materials and their potential for future applications.

2. Various quantum dots are used in supercapacitors

Quantum dots are nanometer-scale particles made of semiconductor materials that have unique electronic and optical properties due to their small size and quantum confinement effect. The quantum confinement effect is a phenomenon in which the electrons in a material are confined to a small space, leading to quantization of their energy levels. This results in discrete electronic transitions and a unique set of optical and electronic properties that can be tuned by adjusting the size, shape, and composition of the quantum dots.

The structure of quantum dots typically consists of a core material, such as cadmium selenide, surrounded by a shell of a different semiconductor material, such as zinc sulfide. The core–shell structure enhances the stability of the quantum dots and improves their optical properties. The electronic and optical properties of quantum dots make them important for a wide range of applications in fields such as electronics, optoelectronics, and medicine. In electronics, quantum dots are used to create transistors and other electronic devices with improved performance and energy efficiency. They are also used in solar cells to enhance their efficiency by increasing the absorption of light. In optoelectronics, quantum dots are used as light-emitting diodes (LEDs), lasers, and sensors. Quantum dot LEDs have several advantages over traditional LEDs, including a broader range of colors and higher brightness. Quantum dot lasers have potential applications in telecommunications and high-speed computing. Quantum dot sensors can be used for the detection of gases, biological molecules, and other substances. Quantum dots are also used in biomedical imaging and drug delivery. In imaging, quantum dots can be used as fluorescent probes to visualize biological structures and processes. They can also be used for in vivo imaging and diagnostics. In drug delivery, quantum dots can be used as carriers for targeted delivery of drugs to specific tissues or cells.

2.1. SnO2 QDs

Tin oxide quantum dots (SnO2 QDs) have gained significant attention as a promising material for energy storage applications due to their unique electronic and structural properties. An ecologically friendly semiconductor with excellent chemical and physical stability is tin oxide (SnO2).30 It has been incorporated into research projects across many different sectors. SnO2 QDs have recently been employed in the creation of solar cells, hybrid electro-chemical capacitors, lithium-ion batteries, and gas sensors. SnO2 QDs were prepared using a variety of techniques. The rationale for choosing SnO2 QDs for energy storage applications can be detailed by considering the following factors: (1) high surface area: which is favorable for increases the number of active sites available for electrochemical reactions in energy storage devices. (2) Abundant and low cost: benefits for large-scale energy storage applications. (3) High theoretical capacity: for lithium-ion storage (782 mA h g−1), which is higher than that of traditional graphite-based anodes. (4) Fast charge and discharge rates: to exhibit excellent rate performance for high-power applications, such as electric vehicles. (5) Stability and durability: reliable material for long-term energy storage applications.

Compatibility with other materials: SnO2 QDs can be easily integrated with other materials, such as carbon and metal oxides, to form hybrid nanocomposites with enhanced performance. 2.2. CdS QDs

Cadmium sulfide (CdS) quantum dots, with a huge surface area, a low band gap (2.42 eV), and quantum confinement, are the most important II–IV semiconductors.34 Their outstanding electrochemical, optical, and fluorescence properties enable their efficient application in the pharmaceutical analysis and element detection fields. It is well known that the efficiency of CdS nanoparticles is significantly influenced by their size and spatial structure. As a result, much work has gone into developing CdS nanoparticles with the proper forms and dimensions. Examples of traditional preparation methods include the sol–gel method, hydrothermal synthesis, organic solvent approach, and microwave process. These methods result in CdS QDs with different sizes and morphologies, which affect their electrochemical properties. For example, smaller CdS QDs with a diameter of less than 10 nm have been found to exhibit better capacitance and cyclic stability compared to larger QDs (Fig. 4).
Fig. 4 Organometallic halide perovskite bilayer electrode structures with cadmium sulfide quantum dots for supercapacitor applications.35

Recent studies have shown that CdS QDs can be effectively utilized as electrode materials in SCs due to their excellent electrochemical properties, such as high capacitance, good cycling stability, and fast charge–discharge rates. For instance, Li et al. reported a facile synthesis of CdS QDs and their subsequent use as electrode materials for SCs, demonstrating an impressive specific capacitance of 265.5 F g−1 at a current density of 0.5 A g−1 and good cycling stability over 5000 cycles.36

In this study, layers of self-produced cadmium sulphide QDs and organometallic perovskites materials served as active electrodes for thin-film electrochemical capacitors. Organometallic halide perovskites display remarkable electrical qualities as well as intriguing ionic responses. Electrochemical capacitors were made using these features, and they demonstrated outstanding cycling properties with steady capacitance output beyond 4000 cycles. Perovskites function not only as functional electrodes but also as solid electrolytes,37 increasing the devices’ capacitance and, consequently, the energy densities, according to tests made using impedance spectroscopy. High surface areas in the layers allow electrolytes to access the electrode materials, and it was also noted that the layers’ charge transfer resistance and relaxation time were both quite low. This research creates new possibilities for the advancement of thin-film supercapacitors.

Despite these promising results, there are still several challenges that need to be addressed to fully realize the potential of CdS QDs in SCs. One of the major challenges is the low electrical conductivity of CdS QDs, which can limit their performance as electrode materials. To overcome this challenge, various strategies have been proposed, such as hybridization with carbon-based materials and doping with conductive polymers.38,39

Another challenge is the toxicity of CdS QDs, which can raise environmental and health concerns. To address this issue, researchers have explored the use of non-toxic QDs, such as ZnS and CuS, as alternatives to CdS QDs in SCs.40,41 In summary, CdS QDs have shown great potential as electrode materials in SCs, but further research is needed to address the challenges and fully realize their potential. Future research directions could include exploring new synthesis methods, developing more effective hybridization and doping strategies, and exploring non-toxic alternatives to CdS QDs.

4. Quantum dots composites for storage

The rapid need for energy storage devices in the market has urged for mass production and budget friendly synthesis of carbon dots (CDs). There has been numerous research going on to develop novel materials for energy storage devices like-batteries and supercapacitors.42 The performance of these energy devices highly depends on the nature of electrode materials. Researchers have found that carbonaceous materials showed exceptionally good electrochemical properties mainly due to their suitable structure.93 Among the various carbonaceous materials, graphene was widely employed for energy storage applications mainly due to their sp2-hybridized carbon atoms which exist in conjugated fashion. Graphene also exhibits certain other interesting features which includes-thermal and electrical properties, high surface area, chemical stability along with interesting mechanical properties as well. However, graphene sheets when cut down to a few nanometers gave exciting physicochemical properties and this gave rise to graphene quantum dots (GQDs).93

Since then, various cost-effective synthesis methodologies have been done where the researchers were successful in synthesizing GQDs without use of initiators, surfactants and toxic reagents.94 The addition of heteroatoms into CDs proved to increase electrochemical activities like OER/HER and conductivity. By enhancing the dimensions, researchers were able to tune the material to a greater extent to obtain desired properties.94 Some of the major advantages in using quantum dots like CDs and GQDs in energy storage can be well explained by three main reasons: (1) quantum dots have attractive properties which provide electron–hole pairs for electrical conductivity, surface area for functionalization and wettability for enhancing electrochemical properties. (2) Quantum dots can serve as precursors and templates to produce materials with distinct structures. (3) Quantum dots can be incorporated to diverse systems by-either adding directly during synthesis or created using in situ methods like electrophoretic deposition, hydrothermal or microwave-assisted techniques.94 Further detailed study and in-depth knowledge of the quantum dots can help us in building materials with exceptional electrochemical performance which makes them ideal for energy storage. Quantum dots composites are found to be the hottest topic in research when it comes to electrode materials in energy storage devices.

Carbonaceous materials have received a lot of attention recently for their use as innovative anode materials and in energy storage applications. Quantum dot composites are one of the many promising carbonaceous materials available today. A hydrothermal approach was used by Zhao et al. to manufacture graphene quantum dot-coated carbon nanotubes (GQD–CNT).95 The conductive qualities of both GQD and CNT were incorporated in the hybrid structure. After 100 cycles, the composite demonstrated a 700 mA h g−1 specific capacitance at 100 mA g−1. The material demonstrated good stability even at high current densities of 1000 mA g−1 after 350 cycles, a specific capacity of 483 mA g−1 was attained.95 The hybrid material, hence proved to be a good anode material for lithium-ion batteries showing fast redox reactions and outstanding rate and cycle stability.

The use of organic polymer composites with quantum dots (QDs) in supercapacitor (SC) applications has gained considerable attention in recent years due to their potential for enhancing the electrochemical performance of SCs. However, there is a need to explore the influence of QD morphology on the performance of these composite materials.96

A study conducted by Zhang et al.97 investigated the impact of QD morphology on the electrochemical performance of a composite material made from polyaniline (PANI) and CdS QDs. The authors found that the shape of the QDs significantly influenced the electrochemical properties of the composite material. They observed that CdS QDs with a rod-like morphology showed improved electrochemical performance compared to those with a spherical morphology.97 The higher surface area and greater accessibility of the rod-like QDs led to enhanced charge storage capacity and improved cycling stability. Similarly, another study by Chen et al.98 explored the use of a composite material made from poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) and CdS QDs with different morphologies. The authors observed that the electrochemical performance of the composite material was strongly influenced by the shape of the QDs. They found that CdS QDs with a rod-like morphology showed higher specific capacitance and better cycling stability than those with a spherical morphology.

These studies highlight the importance of considering QD morphology in the design and synthesis of organic polymer composites for SC applications. Further research is needed to explore the influence of QD morphology on the performance of composite materials made from other organic polymers and QD materials.

5. Conclusion and future scope

The advancement in energy conversion and storage technologies over the past few decades has had a significant impact on the development of next-generation technology. Utilizing batteries and SCs is essential in cutting-edge electronics, and hopefully in the coming years, we will be relying more and more on them. SCs have been shown to be extremely dependable when used in a variety of real-time applications. Due to their advantageous qualities, such as excellent power density, longer cycle life, involvement of lower cost, etc., they have been shown to be an appropriate candidate for next-generation energy-oriented technologies, such as solar systems, faster trains, electric vehicles, and wind power equipment. To make its use more effective, scientists are still working to analyze and improve their qualities. In this review, we have focused on discussing various quantum dots (QDs) and nanocomposites used for SCs and have provided examples of each type's performance. Effective QD use has really led to increased performance efficiency in SCs. However, QDs have made excellent contributions in a variety of other applications as well, including those for battery electrode materials, medication delivery, biosensors, bioimaging, electrochemical devices, UV sensors, and others. In this review, we have honed in on the electrode materials based on quantum dots and their composites for storage and quantum dot based flexible devices that have been published up to this point.
