Abstract

The need to reduce the accumulation of CO2 into the atmosphere requires new technologies able to reduce the CO2 emission. The utilization of CO2 as a building block may represent an interesting approach to synthetic methodologies less intensive in carbon and energy. In this paper the general properties of carbon dioxide and its interaction with metal centres is first considered. The potential of carbon dioxide as a raw material in the synthesis of chemicals such as carboxylates, carbonates, carbamates is then discussed. The utilization of CO2 as source of carbon for the synthesis of fuels or other C1 molecules such as formic acid and methanol is also described and the conditions for its implementation are outlined. A comparison of chemical and biotechnological conversion routes of CO2 is made and the barriers to their exploitation are highlighted.

1. Introduction

1.1 The natural carbon cycle and anthropogenic emissions

The natural carbon-cycle of carbon dioxide uses processes occurring in terrestrial and aquatic environments in a dynamic exchange with the atmosphere. The estimate for the “atmospheric” content of CO2 is 750 Gt of carbon, while the “carbon-flow” between the atmosphere and water basins equals ca. 90 Gt of carbon per year and global fixation by terrestrial plants and microorganisms, plus underground inorganication, amounts to ca. 90 Gt per year. Such uptake is balanced by the emission of carbon in the form of CO2 during the respiration of plants and animals, and by natural emissions of volcanoes. Deforestation and “spontaneous fires” contribute ca. 1.5 Gt y−1 of carbon. The amount of CO2 emitted in human activities is estimated at 5.5 Gt carbon per year. However, the total anthropogenic carbon can be assumed to amount to ca. 7 Gt per year or 25.7 Gt CO2 per year (including the indirect effects of deforestation), that represents a ca. 3.9% excess with respect to the yearly CO2-flow in the natural “carbon cycle”. Such an amount is not balanced and, as a result, the concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere has kept steadily increasing in the last 200 years, rising to 380 ppm from ca. 270 ppm in the pre-industrial era.1 An issue that deserves attention is the fact that only 30–35% of the chemical energy content associated with the carbon (fossil, and provided from the biosphere) used in anthropic activities is converted into and used as various forms of energy (electric, mechanical, others), while the remaining 65–70% is lost as heat that directly affects the atmosphere. To this direct warming is added the effect due to the increase of the concentration of CO2 and other greenhouse gases (GHGs), among which is water vapour, that prevent the infrared radiation emitted by the surface of our planet during the night-time escape to the troposphere. The two combined effects are considered to cause the “atmospheric warming” that is associated with global climate change and planetary temperature increase.

1.2 Technologies for carbon dioxide reduction

However, much interest is concentrated these days on technologies that may reduce CO2 emission into the atmosphere. Besides efficiency technologies (in the production and use of electric energy) that may make a better use of fossil fuels and reduce the amount of heat and carbon released per unit of kWh, the recovery and disposal of CO2 from point-continuous sources, such as power plants and industries, is afforded much attention as carbon dioxide capture and storage (CCS) looks particularly suited for the elimination of large volumes of CO2.2

The capture of CO2 and its disposal is an energetically-costly procedure: per each ton of disposed CO2ca. 0.4–0.5 t of CO2 are generated, which causes a net increase in the amount of extracted fuel. It should also be considered that sites for disposal are not easily available in all countries. Therefore, the long-distance transportation of CO2 to available disposal sites represents a prominent part of the economic and energetic costs of CO2 disposal. A new concept of electric energy production and distribution, based on the conversion of fuels into energy at the fuel extraction site and electric energy distribution, has to be evaluated for the minimisation of the cost of disposal of CO2 that might be placed in the spent fields from which fossil fuels were extracted.

It is worth mentioning that the reduction of CO2 emission is also a key issue in the case of large scale hydrogen-production. In fact, unless water splitting using solar energy is implemented, hydrogen will be produced mainly from carbonaceous materials with co-generation of CO2: if the “hydrogen economy” is to be implemented, CO2 cannot be eliminated into the atmosphere or there will be no benefit from an environmental point of view.

1.2.1 Carbon dioxide capture and disposal. The capture of carbon dioxide from the flue gases of coal-, oil-, or gas-fired power plants and from industrial processes is a mature technology, commercially available. Either solid phases or liquids, such as monoethanolamine (MEA, by far the most used liquid), are used.3 More recently, membranes have been introduced for CO2 separation. The latter are less space-demanding, while being more expensive than MEA. The capture of CO2 is already implemented at the Mt level, mainly for the separation of liquefied natural gas (LNG) into CO2 and methane. Separated CO2 can be confined in natural sites such as: aquifers, deep geological cavities, spent oil- or gas-fields, coal-mines or the ocean.4 Each of the above technologies has limitations and uncertainties that will require a large research effort before complete exploitation. So far, only the disposal in spent LNG-fields and the use in enhanced oil recovery (EOR) are proven technologies that still need monitoring of the disposal sites to guarantee that there are no significant losses of confined CO2. This practice is already exploited in Norway where large volumes of separated CO2 are re-injected into spent gas-fields.5 This is a reference case for CO2 separation and disposal.

1.3 Industrial uses of CO2

Carbon dioxide has been used for the industrial synthesis of organics, such as urea6a and salicylic acid,6b,c and inorganic compounds (pigments) since the second half of the 19th century. New applications have been investigated and applied (carboxylation of epoxides) after the 1973 oil-crisis. The topic has been comprehensively reviewed by several authors.7

In recent years, carbon dioxide has also found a growing application as a fluid in dry-cleaning, refrigerators, air conditioners, fire-extinguishers, separation techniques, water treatment and the food- or agro-chemical industry (as packaging, an additive to beverages or a fumigant).8 Supercritical-CO2 finds an application as a solvent for reactions, nano-particle or -composite production, and polymer-modification. With the exception of the case of water treatment, in all other technological applications, CO2 is not converted into other chemicals, and can be recovered as such at the end of the application.

Both use as a building block in the synthesis of chemicals and use as a technological fluid, in contrast to disposal, reduce dependence on fossil fuels and generate profit. Such uses of CO2 are well-in-line with a sustainable chemical industry, as they are based on clean reactions that reduce carbon dioxide emission into the atmosphere. A question is: how much CO2 is avoided by implementing maximum utilisation of CO2, and if such technology should also be considered for “global warming control” and be coupled with CCS? As a matter of fact, the correct estimate of the amount of CO2 emission that can be avoided by chemical or technological utilisation requires the analysis of the whole reaction cycle of existing and new processes based on the use of CO2.9b To do this, life cycle assessment (LCA) is the only reliable methodology.10

1.3.1 Industrial uses. How much CO2 is used in the chemical industry or other applications? 110 Mt CO2 per year are either converted into chemicals9a such as urea (70 Mt CO2 per year), inorganic carbonates and pigments (ca. 30 Mt CO2 per year) or used as an additive to CO in the synthesis of methanol (6 Mt CO2 per year). Other chemicals, such as salicylic acid (20 kt CO2 per year) and propylene carbonate (a few kt per year), have a minor share of the market. In addition, 18 Mt of CO2 per year are used8b as a technological fluid.

Among these industrial uses, the syntheses of urea and salicylic acid are purely thermal processes. In the reaction of Na/K-phenate with CO2, the Na-cation favours the formation of the salicylate, while the K-cation promotes the formation of the para isomer, 4-OH-benzoic acid. Conversely, both the carboxylation of epoxides and (to a much greater degree) the synthesis of methanol require the intervention of catalysts, mainly metal systems. Several other options for CO2 conversion exist that need the development of new catalytic systems.

2. The carbon dioxide molecule

2.1 Molecular geometry and spectroscopic properties

Carbon dioxide, a 16e− molecule, belongs to the D∞h point group and is linear in its ground state. Consequently, although it contains two polar C–O bonds, the molecule itself is apolar. CO2 presents two different reaction sites: the carbon atom is an electrophile, while the oxygen atoms are nucleophiles: that makes it a bi-functional catalyst,11 maybe best suited for its activation or conversion. The Walsh diagram12 shows that the population of the LUMO-orbitals (as may occur in electronically excited CO2, the radical anion CO2− or the adduct of CO2 with an electron rich species) causes the distortion of the molecule from linearity (when the carbon atom of CO2 is bonded to a third atom, the OCO angle is always close to 133°), the variation of the molecular energy and the increase of the C–O bond length.

Infrared (IR)13 and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)14 techniques are much used as diagnostic tools of the state of the CO2 molecule or for its quantitative determination. UV-Visible spectroscopy15 has not found extensive use. Table 1 shows the IR data for CO2 in different states. The CO symmetric stretching of free CO2 is not IR active, while in the Raman spectrum such a vibration is found at 1285–1388 cm−1. IR spectroscopy can be used for the determination of CO2 in the atmosphere (by non dispersive infra-red, NDIR) and as a tool for indicating the mode of bonding of CO2 to metal systems. The utilisation of labelled O or C, e.g. by using 13CO2 and C18O2, helps in the determination of the mode of bonding in metallorganic species containing CO2 as ligand.

The reactions above, and those in which CO2 reacts with hydroxyl ions, OH−, amines or olefins are good examples of processes in which CO2 is converted without any external energetic input. Therefore, by using opportune catalysts, CO2 could be conveniently used for the direct synthesis of useful organic products such as carboxylates and lactones (RCOOR′), carbamates (RR′NCOOR″), ureas (RR′NCONRR′), isocyanates (RNCO), carbonates [(ROC(O)OR′], supplanting more polluting starting materials and processes currently in use.

On the contrary, the reactions that generate reduced forms of CO2 (such as HCOO− (formates), [C(O)O]22− (oxalates), H2CO (formaldehyde), CO, CH3OH and CH4) require energy that may be provided as heat (thermal processes) or electrons (electrochemical processes), or else radiation.

Anyway, as a general rule, the convenience of developing a process based on CO2 does not depend on the fact that it is endo- or exoergonic. Today, several strongly-endothermic processes are run at the industrial level that consume a lot of energy and, additionally, produce large amounts of waste. Consequently, a new process based on CO2 will be more sustainable than an existing one if it minimizes the material and energy consumption and CO2 emission.

FTIR spectroscopy,41 more recently coupled to density functional theory (DFT)42 has been used for the investigation of matrix interaction of CO2 with metal atoms: the co-ordination of CO2 is influenced by the metal and the gas matrix. Late-transition metals [Fe, Co, Ni, Ag and Cu] form one-to-one M(CO2) complexes, while left-hand elements [Ti, V, and Cr] easily insert into one of the CO bonds yielding oxo-carbonyl species.42 Additionally, in net CO2 the cumulene is easily side-on coordinated to nickel (binding energy = 75 kJ mol−1) to afford a 1 : 1 complex, while if argon is used to dilute the matrix, no reaction occurs, even after annealing. 3.2 The insertion of CO2 into M–E bonds

CO2 inserts into metal-element bonds (M–E), where E is H, N, C, O, P. This chemistry is of great interest as M–OC(O)–E or M–C(O)O–E forms may be useful intermediates for synthetic applications. The mode of insertion of CO2 depends on the charge distribution on the M–E couple of atoms and on the energy of the M–C, M–O, E–C, E–O bonds. Often, the driver of the insertion is the difference in energy between the M–C and M–O bonds. Such energies also drive the potential use of such intermediates in synthetic chemistry, as will be described in section 4.1.1.

4. Carbon dioxide as a building block for organic products

The incorporation of carbon dioxide into an organic substrate with formation of compounds containing the entire COO moiety (carboxylates), is a good example of a “sustainable process”. In fact, compared to processes on stream, the direct carboxylation of organic substrates causes the reduction of waste at source, uses less and diversified starting materials, and recycles carbon. One can also expect that, under the appropriate reaction conditions, being the carboxylation an exo-ergonic reaction, such direct carboxylations may be associated to a reduction of energy consumption with respect to processes on stream.

4.1 Synthesis of monomeric carboxylated products

Although the incorporation of CO2 into an organic substrate to afford C–COOH, C–COOC, E–COO–C (EN,O), or C–OC(O)O–C moieties is a process of great importance from the industrial point of view, to date there are still severe barriers to its exploitation. The formation of a terminal “carboxylic moiety” C–COO, is industrially achieved through multi-step syntheses (such as oxidation of aliphatic or aromatic moieties, hydration of cyanides, Grignard reagents), that are not selective nor quantitative,51 but are thermodynamically and kinetically possible. Cyclic compounds containing a “COO” moiety require even more complex synthetic procedures. The synthesis of the moieties reported above can be considered as the insertion of CO2 into the C–H, C–C, E–C, C–O–C bonds, respectively. Such reactions are expected to have a favourable thermodynamics eventually associated with adverse kinetics. The use of an appropriate catalyst is essential in order to overcome the kinetic barrier, but even this is not enough as it is often necessary to develop ad hoc synthetic strategies.

Despite the fact that direct carboxylation of organic substrates represents a great synthetic opportunity that would greatly improve the environmental quality of existing processes,55–57 only very few attempts have been made in the past in such a direction. Olefins, which are rich in energy, easily react with CO2 in the presence of transition metal systems in a low oxidation state, such as Ni(0),65 Ti,66 Fe(0),67 Mo(0),68 Rh(I).69 The reaction of ethene and CO2 with the above metals results in an “oxidative addition” to afford either a metallacycle or a metal hydride (Scheme 4). In both cases, the elimination of the product (a four-membered lactone or acrylic acid) does not occur spontaneously, despite the fact that the formation of either product from ethene and CO2 is permitted thermodynamically. An organic carboxylate (propanoic acid or acrylic acid) can be released upon reaction of the metallacarboxylate with a strong acid, such as HCl, which leaves the metal in an oxidation state two units higher with respect to the starting one: such a metal-species is not able to act as a catalyst. Therefore, for practical applications, such reactions should be carried out using metal compounds that favour the release of the lactone or acid, regenerating the original system: tailored ligands may promote such elimination, as shown by DFT calculations.70 Transition metal catalyzed electrochemical carboxylations of olefin or halogenated hydrocarbons with CO2 have been successfully used for the synthesis of several carboxylated organic substrates.71

4.1.2.1 Mimicking the carboxyphosphate enzyme. The carboxy phosphate enzyme [CP, (−O)2P(O)OC(O)O−] is used for the elimination from a living organism of ammonia and amines in the form of urea or its derivatives. The active form of CP is produced by reacting HCO3− with ATP, which is converted into ADP, a reaction that does not occur in vitro. CP is able to transfer CO2 to amines with formation of the carbamate (RR′N-COO−) moiety that is the precursor of ureas. Inorganic phosphate HxPO4(3−x)−, cannot be directly reacted with CO2 nor with HCO3− to afford CP. Conversely, phosphonic acids such as R2P(O)OH can be easily reacted with dimethylcarbonate (DMC) to afford a mimic system of CP, namely R2P(O)OC(O)OCH3, that can be advantageously used as substitute for CP in the catalytic conversion of amines of industrial interest (toluene diamine or methylene dianiline) into their carbamates,85h which are the precursors of isocyanates. Interestingly, the chemical system, although performing the same activity of CP, requires less energy with the same selectivity.

4.2 Other options of using CO2 as co-monomer in the synthesis of polymers

The use of CO2 as co-monomer may find application in other areas in addition to the production of poly(carbonate)s. This particular use of CO2 is of great interest, as the materials obtained may have a long life, on the order of several decades. This property differentiates polymers from monomeric compounds, which are quickly converted back to CO2 after use. Therefore, the development of routes to new polymeric materials based on CO2 is relevant to the storage of CO2. For example, poly(urethan)s can be used as insulating materials (from heat, noise, and others) in buildings. They would store CO2 for decades on a scale of several Mt per year. Poly(urethan)s can be synthesised from CO2 and cyclic amines such as aziridines and azetidines or N-analogues of epoxides, avoiding the synthesis of isocyanates.

Another reaction that it would be of interest to develop is the co-polymerisation of ethene and CO2 to afford poly(ester)s. Such a reaction recalls the co-polymerisation of ethene and CO, but presents substantial differences. In fact, in the case of CO2, M–O bonds are formed and this will introduce kinetic and thermodynamic differences with respect to the use of CO as co-monomer.

Research is needed in these areas for the development of new catalysts and understanding of the reaction mechanism.

5. Reduction of CO2 to afford energy-rich products

Table 2 shows that the conversion of CO2 into other C1 (or Cn) molecules, characterized by a lower O : C ratio, that means a higher H/C ratio, requires energy that can be provided in various forms: heat, electrons, radiation, or carried by chemicals. Despite this, the utilisation of CO2 as carbon source for the synthesis of products such as fuels may result a convenient alternative to the production of CO or syngas (molar ratio H2 : CO = 2 used for making methanol, gasoline and diesel fuel) from conventional routes based on the use of more reduced carbon (coal, LNG, HC) that are quite low in efficiency and strongly endothermic. Additionally, CO2 is a convenient starting product for the synthesis of other C1 molecules such as formic acid and methanol.

5.1 Formic acid and its derivatives

The transition-metal catalysed hydrogenation of CO2 to formic acid or its derivatives (such as formamides) has so far been extensively investigated. The reaction is carried out in organic solvents, water or ionic liquids, or else directly in supercritical CO2.118 Methods for the production of methyl formate have not been developed yet. Interestingly, carbonate or hydrogen carbonate can be hydrogenated to formate salts at very high rates, with TOFs as high as 9600 at 80 °C and 1364 at room temperature. The hydrogenation of aqueous bicarbonate is sensitive to CO2 pressure that most likely influences the HCO3− concentration or may be converted into formate. Ru and Rh systems have been found to be active catalysts in both aqueous media and organic solvents. A recent publication has coupled IR studies with DFT to elucidate the reaction mechanism and the role of Ru–(H2) complexes in the reduction of CO2.118i

In general, the production of formic acid is promoted by an amine, present in the reaction medium, which converts the acid into ammonium formate. The formation of the latter improves the thermodynamics and shifts the equilibrium to the right as a consequence of the formation of a weaker acid (such as the ammonium cation, which has a Ka four to five orders of magnitude lower than formic acid). This reduces the probability of a back-addition to the metal centre. Ligands bearing amine functional groups as pendant arms were found to improve the catalytic activity of metal complexes but at a lower level than an external amine.

Ru(II)–trimethylphosphine complexes118 have also been extensively investigated from the kinetic point of view: it has been shown that the rate of formation of formic acid is first order in both H2 and CO2. Sc-CO2 as a solvent has a significant positive influence118 on the reaction rate.

Most probably, a cationic mechanism is operating and the active cationic species is generated with the assistance of the added base or alcohol that promotes the loss of an halide ion. This point needs further investigation. Among the Rh(I) catalysts, Rh(H)(diphosphine) is the most active.118 NiCl2(dcpe) is the most active of other metal complexes, but still it has yields and rates well below those of Ru and Rh.

The best catalyst precursors for formic acid production are RhCl(TPPTS)3, Rh(hfacac)(dcpb) and RuCl(OAc)(PMe3)4, all very close in activity,118 although ranking of catalysts is not easy as they have all been used in quite different conditions and normalisation is difficult. Several aspects of the reaction still need a better explanation, such as the high activity of the aqueous-phase catalysts, not easily explained taking into account the mass transfer rates.118 Recently, combined experimental and theoretical studies118i have shed light on the reaction mechanism.

5.2 Production of CO and syngas

Syngas (CO/H2 mixture) can be used for the synthesis of methanol or long chain hydrocarbons, such as gasoline, and oxygenates. Therefore, the production of CO and syngas has been investigated in detail.

The adsorption and reaction of CO2 on clean Rh, Pd, Pt, Ni, Fe, Cu, Re, Al, Mg and Ag metal surfaces128 have been studied by several authors using spectroscopic methods. The formation of CO2− is a key step, as it may either dissociate to CO and O− or convert into adsorbed CO32− and CO, depending on the metal. Adsorbed CO2 dissociates on Fe, Ni, Re, Al, and Mg and remains unchanged over Pt and Cu surfaces. If oxygen is pre-adsorbed on the metal surface, the formation of stable carbonate structures is favoured.

Syngas is formed from methane following three different ways: the partial oxidation [eqn (16)], steam reforming [eqn (17)] and CO2 reforming [eqn (18)], also called dry-reforming.

 
CH4 + 1/2 O2 → CO + 2 H2; ΔH0298 = −36 kJ mol−1(16)
 
CH4 + H2O → CO + 3 H2; ΔH0298 = 206 kJ mol−1(17)
 
CO2 + CH4 → 2 CO + 2 H2; ΔH0298 = 247 kJ mol−1(18)

Only the partial oxidation of methane [eqn (16)] is exothermic and can also (if only partially) compensate the endothermic reactions (17)–(18).

The combination of the three reactions (16)–(18) is known as the “tri-reforming”129 that produces the proper CO : H2 molar ratio (1.7) for methanol or higher hydrocarbon synthesis.130 Although CO2 reforming of methane is more endothermic than steam reforming, it produces syngas with a lower residual CH4 with respect to steam reforming, which leaves up to 2% of unreacted methane. The great existing interest in dry reforming is justified by the fact that it can be used in remote natural gas fields to convert on-site CO2-rich LNG into liquid fuels (gas to liquid, GTL), which are more easily transportable (at a lower cost) than gas. Tri-reforming, a good option for CO2 and methane co-utilisation,131 can be integrated advantageously into the direct re-use of flue gas from C-based power plants. The new catalysts, Ni–La2O3, which prevent coke-formation may push forward the exploitation of this process. An innovative approach is the utilisation of catalysts under cold-plasma conditions for methane dry-reforming,132 that can also be used for the direct synthesis of oxy-fuels.133 In 2002, a pilot plant for the conversion of natural gas to liquid fuel (GTL) under plasma conditions started operation in Alberta.134

Efficient catalysts for ethanol formation from H2/CO2 have been developed135 and higher alcohols can be produced in the same way.136 Nevertheless, the yield is lower than using H2/CO and the technology requires further development for practical application.

C5+ olefins can be obtained from CO2 using iron carbide (Fe5C2) as catalyst. C2–C4 olefins are produced using a Fe–K/alumina catalyst with a selectivity of about 44% at a CO2 conversion of 68% under 2 MPa at 400 °C.137 Alternatively, C2–C4 olefins are made with > 90% overall selectivity by first making methanol using a Cu–ZnO catalyst, and secondly converting the alcohol with a solid acid catalyst.138

CO2 can be used as selective oxidant towards hydrocarbons. For example, it is conveniently used as dehydrogenating agent in the conversion of C6H5CH2CH3 into styrene, C6H5CHCH2 [eqn (19)].139

 
C6H5CH2CH3 + CO2 → C6H5CHCH2 + CO + H2O(19)

5.3 Production of methanol

CO2 can be converted into methanol, with 100% selectivity and high TOF. The excellent performance, most likely due to a different reaction mechanism,136 compensates in terms of efficiency the extra amount of dihydrogen needed for CO2 reduction [eqn (21)] with respect to CO [eqn (22)].
 
CO2 + 3 H2 → CH3OH + H2O(21)
 
CO + 2 H2 → CH3OH(22)

CO2 is currently added (up to 30% of total C) to syngas in the synthesis of methanol over Cu–ZnO at 250–300 °C and 5–10 MPa. The addition of CO2 to the H2/CO feed significantly improves the methanol yield and the energy balance, via a direct conversion of CO2 to methanol without a preliminary reduction to CO.140,141 The Cu–ZnO catalysts can be further improved if Pd is added.142 The first pilot plant (50 kg per day) for the production of methanol from CO2 and H2 has been built in Japan143 using a SiO2 modified Cu–ZnO catalyst. Recycling the feed produces a space–time yield of methanol around 600 g L−1 h−1, with 99.9% selectivity over 8000 h operation at 250 °C and 5 MPa. The methanol production from H2/CO2 is technically competitive with the industrial production from syngas, also if economically less convenient.

5.3.1 Methanol from CO2 and water using a cascade of metal enzymes. The synthesis of methanol from CO2 and water can be carried out using a cascade of enzymes that allow the conversion of CO2 into HCOO (formate dehydrogenase), CH2O (formaldehyde dehydrogenase), and CH3OH (methanol dehydrogenases).121,122 All enzymes are easily available and can be used either separately or co-encapsulated into a unique system providing a one-pot–three-step conversion of CO2 into methanol.122b The limiting factor is the electron source: the NADPH+/NADP couple has at this point been used up. Cheap reducing agents or solar energy should be used11 to generate the electrons necessary for the reduction of CO2 into methanol. This process would be of great importance, as the production of methanol under such conditions would represent the solution to recycling CO2 as well using it as source of carbon in the energy and chemical industry.

5.5 The key issues: the energy source and light efficiency

While progress has been made after the pioneering work of Lehn and Ziessel and Mackor and Halnrann,146 and several metal complexes or semiconductors have been used, effective photocatalysts for CO2 reduction have not yet been found. In general, low light efficiency is often coupled with scarce selectivity. Encouraging results have been obtained with Co and Ru complexes. CoHMD2+144b has been used successfully as a catalyst for photochemical reduction of CO2 because of the small Co(II)HMD2+/Co(I)HMD+ reorganization energy, the fast CO2 binding to Co(I)HMD+ (1.7 × 108 M−1 s−1) and the large KCO2. XANES studies suggest that the Co(I) species can promote the two-electron transfer to the bound CO2 with formation of CO22− and a consequent easy reduction to CO.144b–d

Semiconductors145 have been used in an attempt to reduce CO2 in water under sunlight irradiation. HCOOH, HCHO, and CH3OH are produced under solar irradiation of a CO2-saturated aqueous suspension of semiconductors such as TiO2 and SrTiO3.146 The quantum yield is very low,141c being one order of magnitude below the efficiency of natural processes. It may be improved by using sacrificial hole traps or electron donors, such as n-propanol, tertiary amines, or EDTA; a solution not economically-convenient as the organic materials may be more valuable than the CO2 reduction products. A Ti-modified mesoporous silica catalyst reduces CO2 in H2O147 with a selectivity of 30% for ethanol, compared to 1.4% over bulk TiO2.

The photochemical reduction of CO2 is of great interest as it allows cheap carbon-recycling.148 Further investigation is needed for a practical application.

6. Conclusions

Carbon dioxide is, thus, a suitable source of carbon in a variety of synthetic applications and can be used as a technological fluid supplanting other gases with higher GHG potential. The use in carboxylation reactions appears the most promising synthetic application in the short-term. Addition to CO for making methanol and use as mild oxidant are other interesting applications. Such uses recycle CO2 and reduce CO2 emission.

Currently, some 130 Mt CO2 per year are used in several applications. This does not mean that the release of 130 Mt CO2 per year is avoided: the amount used does not represent the amount of CO2 release avoided, which can be correctly estimated only by applying the LCA methodology.

A fair estimate of the amount of CO2 release that can be avoided in the short–medium term ranges around 300 Mt per year; should all options of CO2 utilization be implemented, including use in air conditioners.148

The photochemical reduction of CO2 in water under solar light irradiation is an option that, if implemented, may greatly increase carbon recycling.

The cost of CO2 and the energy needed for the separation/purification are key issues. The recovery of pure CO2 from sources such as fermentation processes should be better exploited, as the cost of recycling is much lower than when power sources are considered.

However, at the moment there is no single technology that can alone reduce the worldwide emission of CO2 into the atmosphere at the levels required by the Kyoto protocol. Therefore the most-correct approach to reducing the CO2 emission into the atmosphere is to select of a number of technologies, each able to reduce emission by a given fraction of Gt of CO2 per year, and add up their effects to reach this goal. In this frame, the utilization of CO2 could well be a technology contributing to the reduction of the atmospheric loading, by recycling carbon and reducing emission at the source, implementing sustainable production technologies that reduce waste and make better use of energy and carbon. The net effect can be the reduction of CO2 emission by an amount larger than the amount of fixed CO2. The utilization of CO2 has a different philosophical approach to disposal. In the utilization option, CO2 replaces fossil-carbon: what is important is not the permanence time of CO2 in the products, but the rate and the energy of formation of the product from CO2 with respect to the rate of formation of CO2 from the product.

Efforts must be directed towards the discovery of fast and low-energy pathways for the utilization of CO2. Concerted policies are necessary, which will make research less random and focus science and technology upon fields that may better contribute to reducing the CO2 emission. As a matter of fact, the utilization of CO2 is the only technology that may produce profit out of recovered CO2, while contributing to reducing its global emission.
