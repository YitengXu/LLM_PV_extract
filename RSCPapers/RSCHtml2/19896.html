Synthesis of diketopyrrolopyrrole and anthraquinone-based polymers of D–A1–D–A2 architecture by direct arylation polycondensation and designing inorganic/organic nano-heterostructured photoanodes for visible light water splitting†

Abstract

This article reports the synthesis of two polymers of donor–acceptor1–donor–acceptor2 (D–A1–D–A2) architecture employing the direct heteroarylation poly-condensation reaction. Both the first polymer (PAQTDPP) and the second polymer (PAQBTDPP) comprise anthraquinone (AQ) and diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP) as the acceptor1 and acceptor2 components. In contrast, where PAQTDPP incorporates thiophene as the donor, PAQBTDPP incorporates the bithiophene moiety as the donor. Later, two n–n inorganic/organic nano-heterostructures (NHs)—N-TiO2/PAQTDPP and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP—were fabricated using nitrogen-doped TiO2 nanorods (N-TiO2 NRs) and PAQTDPP and PAQBTDPP polymers on the fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) coated glass substrate. The N-TiO2/PAQTDPP and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH materials were utilized as the photoanodes for visible light photoelectrochemical water splitting. At 1.23 VRHE, the N-TiO2 NR, N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH, and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH photoanodes exhibited a photocurrent density of 0.157 mA cm−2, 0.504 mA cm−2, and 0.397 mA cm−2 (@10 mV s−1), respectively, in a 0.5 M aq. Na2SO4 electrolyte. The inorganic/organic nano-hybrid photoanodes also exhibited cathodically shifted water oxidation onset potential compared to the N-TiO2 NR electrode. The augmented photoelectrochemical performances of the inorganic/organic nano-hybrid materials are attributable to the absorption capability of a broader spectrum of visible light by the polymers, suitable band alignment between the inorganic and organic components of the heterojunction, and better and faster-photogenerated charge carrier separation and transportation.

1 Introduction

Donor–acceptor–donor (D–A–D) and donor–acceptor1–donor–acceptor2 (D–A1–D–A2) type polymers as organic electronic materials have greatly shaped the realm of organic electronics. Polymers of these architectures have been widely applied in organic field-effect transistors (OFETs), organic photovoltaic devices, organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), and memristors.1–3 Generally, polymers are synthesized through either Ni(II) or Pd(0) catalysis reactions.4–6 Among these reactions, the Stille polycondensation6 is the most widely used because of its high functional group tolerance, simplicity, and controlled nature. Nevertheless, the Stille polycondensation involves expensive, sensitive, and toxic organometallic reagents as precursors. Moreover, the polycondensation reaction involves more intermediate steps and produces hazardous by-products, limiting the scale-up process and raising serious environmental and sustainability issues. The presence of metallic contaminants in the polymers can act as carrier traps, affecting the device performance.7–9 However, recently, catalytic dehydrogenative cross-coupling of heteroarenes with aryl halides is emerging as an improved and relatively green alternative to the conventional cross-coupling reactions,10–12 producing polymers with fewer amounts of metal impurities.13 In comparison, employing the direct arylation polycondensation (DArP) technique, different polymers with a higher molecular weight, better regioregularity, and high yield have been synthesized by different research groups.12–15

In this context, we have synthesized two anthraquinone (AQ) and diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP)-based D–A1–D–A2 type copolymers through the direct heteroarylation poly-condensation reaction. The structure of the polymers is shown in Scheme 1. In polymer 1 (PAQTDPP), thiophene serves as the donor moiety (D), while anthraquinone and DPP serve as the first acceptor (A1) and the second acceptor (A2) moieties, respectively. In contrast, in polymer 2 (PAQBTDPP), the bithiophene moiety acts as the donor, and like polymer 1, anthraquinone and DPP serve as the acceptor 1 and acceptor 2, respectively. It is worth mentioning that DPP has been a successful molecule in organic electronics because of its easy accessibility, high thermal stability, robustness, absorption and emission, and high excited-state lifetime.16,17 Anthraquinone-based systems also hold enormous promise as organic semiconducting materials due to their easy accessibility, high electronegativity, and chemical and thermal stability.2

Hydrogen is highly regarded as one of the next-generation fuels to cope with the ever-increasing energy demand and propel economic growth in a sustainable and eco-friendly way.18,19 Photoelectrochemical water splitting is considered one of the best and environmentally benign processes to produce hydrogen using solar light.20–22 Lately, inorganic/organic hybrid materials have been emerging as potential candidates for photoanodes and photocathodes in photoelectrochemical (PEC) cells.23,24 Several researchers have reported functional oxide-based inorganic/organic nano-hybrid electrode materials for PEC water splitting such as Ti3+/TiO2/P1,2 Ti3+/TiO2/P2,2 ZnO/P3HT,25 TiO2/polyheptazine,26 TiO2/branched polyethyleneimine/MoS2,27 ZnO/PC71BM,28 boron-doped TiO2/NDIEHTh@Au-Pd,29 TiO2/Au/polythiophene,30 WO3/polypyrrole:Ru4POM,31etc. Organic semiconductors owing to their customizable optoelectronic properties, large-scale synthesis, and solution processability, have caught much attention.23 A variety of organic semiconducting materials could easily be synthesized by different molecular engineering techniques. In inorganic/organic nano-heterostructures (NHs), the organic semiconductors could provide high carrier concentration and mobility and absorb a wider bandwidth of visible light.32

In this backdrop, we have coupled D–A1–D–A2 type polymer 1 (PAQTDPP) and polymer 2 (PAQBTDPP)—synthesized by the direct arylation poly-condensation reaction—with nitrogen-doped TiO2 nanorods (N-TiO2 NRs) by a facile spin-coating technique to fabricate N-TiO2/PAQTDPP and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs, respectively. TiO2 nanorods have been prepared by a solvothermal route on a FTO-coated glass substrate, and nitrogen doping has been achieved in a facile wet-chemical route. Nitrogen doping in TiO2 nanorods is done to improve light absorption and enhance the carrier density in TiO2.33,34 The rod-like structure of N-TiO2, apart from providing a strong mechanical strength to the NHs, could also facilitate faster transport of the photogenerated electrons towards the rear contact from the hetero-interface. Finally, we have explored nano-hybrid materials as the photoanode for visible light water oxidation and found the results quite satisfactory compared to other recently reported inorganic/organic hybrid photoanodes.

2.1 Synthesis of monomers and polymers

The synthesis of compound 2 was previously reported.2 However, the synthesis process involved an excess of the costly reagents, which produced more by-products while the yield was only ∼60%. As an alternative to it, the reaction has been carried out differently. The di-alkoxy (2) compound was stirred in acetic acid at room temperature (RT) in the presence of 2 equiv. of NIS and 2 equiv. of strong protic acid (Table 1). Unlike the previous method, it takes place without stoichiometric iodic acid and iodine and produces fewer by-products. It is observed that trifluoromethane sulfonic acid (TfOH) and methanesulfonic acid (MSA) give the highest yields of 98% and 96%, while other acids do not give a reasonably good yield. Only p-toluenesulfonic acid (PTSA) gives ∼60% yield of compound 2. It is observed that strong acid (pKa < 0) catalyzes the reaction efficiently in acetic acid. The acidic medium stabilizes the transition state involving iodonium ions which can attack the anthraquinone ring. It is also observed that a stoichiometric amount of acid is necessary to generate a stoichiometric amount of iodonium ions. If TfOH is added in a catalytic amount, then the reaction does not proceed well. Moreover, the reaction does not take place in non-acidic organic solvents like CHCl3 because it cannot stabilize strongly ionic intermediates. This finding indicates that a more polar and ionizing medium like acetic acid is essential to stabilize the reaction intermediate.

2.2 Thermal properties

Thermal stability is essential for the practical application of different organic electronic materials. The thermal stability of the polymers was studied using the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere (Fig. S2(a)†). The decomposition temperature (Td) is defined as the temperature at which a polymer loses 5% of its initial mass. Both the polymers—PAQTDPP and PAQBTDPP—showed good thermal stability, substantiated by their high Td. The PAQTDPP polymer exhibited a Td of 241 °C, while the PAQBTDPP polymer showed a Td of 329 °C. These results indicate that the latter polymer has better stability towards heat than the former, which can be ascribed to the structural rigidity of the latter polymer. The differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) thermogram (Fig. S2(b)†) does not show any obvious transition, which indicates that the polymers are stable in a wide range of temperatures.

2.3 Structural order

Single crystal X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was carried out to confirm the position of iodine in compound 2 (whether it is ortho or para)36 and its packing pattern in the solid state. For this purpose, thin needle-like crystals of compound 2 were obtained by the slow evaporation method from an ethanol/DCM solvent mixture (6:4), and a suitable crystal was chosen for X-ray diffraction. The ORTEP (Oak Ridge Thermal–Ellipsoid Plot) diagram of compound 2 is shown in Fig. 1(a). Crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters are listed in Table S1 in the ESI.† The molecule crystallizes in orthorhombic space group Fdd2. Substitution of iodine and the n-BuO group at C1 and C4 positions of the anthraquinone ring induces 1,3-interaction between the oxygen atom and iodine and between the oxygen atom and n-BuO group of the anthraquinone moiety. To minimize this interaction, the carbonyl carbon atoms (C7, C7) of the middle quinone ring deviate from the middle C5-C6-C6-C5 plane by 26.4° (Fig. 1(b)). Similarly, the fused benzene rings at the terminal positions also deviate from the middle C5-C6-C6-C5 plane by 11.3° (Fig. 1(c)) to minimize these interactions via this stable geometry in the solid-state.

2.6 XPS analyses

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is one of the best techniques to analyze the elements constituting a material. Here, all the XPS data have been calibrated against the adventitious carbon 1s peak at 284.8 eV. The high-resolution curve-fitted XPS spectrum of Ti 2p (Fig. 4(a)) obtained from the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs reveals six peaks. The peaks positioned at 458.90 eV and 464.60 eV represent the Ti 2p3/2 and Ti 2p1/2 states of the Ti 2p doublet related to the +4-oxidation state of titanium in TiO2.37,38 The presence of the Ti 2p small doublet with the Ti 2p3/2 and Ti 2p1/2 peaks centered at 457.0 eV and 462.51 eV, respectively, endorses the presence of the +3-oxidation state of titanium in nitrogen-doped TiO2.37 The +3-oxidation state of titanium is achieved by nitrogen doping.33 It is known that the Ti3+ states in TiO2 are regarded as the oxygen vacancy associates.31 The peaks located at 454.90 eV and 460.68 eV could be correlated with the Ti 2p3/2 and Ti 2p1/2 states of nonstoichiometric titanium nitride (TiNy).37 Moreover, the 460.68 eV peak could also be associated with defective titanium oxide (TiO2−x).37 As a consequence of nitrogen doping, some oxygens in the TiO2 matrix get substituted by nitrogen, resulting in oxygen vacancies.33 The area under the curve calculation reveals that the oxygen vacancy-related Ti3+ 2p doublet contributes nearly 12.31% of the total area under the six curves. In comparison, the total area under the TiNx related peaks contributes around 10.30%.
Fig. 4 High-resolution deconvoluted XPS spectra of (a) Ti 2p, (b) O 1s, (c) C 1s, (d) N 1s, and (e) S 2p states of the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH sample. (f) The corresponding XPS survey spectrum.

On deconvolution, the high-resolution O 1s XPS spectrum produces three peaks centered at 530.11 eV, 532.28 eV, and 533.18 eV, respectively (Fig. 4(b)). The 530.11 eV peak could be associated with the lattice oxygen (OL) in the N-doped TiO2 matrix. The most intense peak at 532.28 eV originates from oxygen in the carbonyl groups (CO, oxygen attached to carbon by sp2 hybridization) present in the polymer.2 Moreover, the oxygen vacancies (Vo), generated by nitrogen doping, also contribute to this 532.40 eV peak.2 The low-intensity 533.18 eV peak could be attributed to the alkoxy (C–O–R) groups present in the polymer.2

The deconvoluted high-resolution XPS spectrum of C 1s (Fig. 4(c)) produces five peaks at 283.97 eV, 284.8 eV, 285.68 eV, 286.46 eV, and 287.90 eV. The peak at 283.97 eV could be attributed to sp2 hybridized carbon (CC).39 The 284.8 eV peak is regarded as the signature of adventitious carbon. However, this peak also signifies sp3 hybridized benzenic carbon (C–C) in the polymer.40 The peak centered at 285.68 eV is attributable to thiophene carbon involved in the C–S bond.2 The 286.46 eV binding energy peak could be correlated with carbon involved in the C–O–C bond41 and bond with pyrrolic nitrogen (specifically, the C–N+ bond of pyrrole polarons).42 The remaining 287.90 eV peak could be associated with the CO bonds.41

The high-resolution deconvoluted N 1s XPS spectrum (Fig. 4(d)) consists of three prominent peaks, positioned at 396.01 eV, 400.03 eV, and 401.14 eV. The 396.01 eV peak typically corresponds to the substitutional nitrogen dopants in the Ti–N bonds, while the 400.03 eV peak could be ascribed to the nitrogen molecule incorporated into the TiO2 matrix by interstitial doping.43,44 The high-intensity 400.03 eV peak also bears the signature of pyrrolic nitrogen present in DPP.45 The 401.14 eV peak could be attributed to X-ray oxidized charged nitrogen.2

The deconvoluted high-resolution XPS spectrum of the S 2p state (Fig. 4(e)) depicts one spin-orbit doublet with the S 2p3/2 and S 2p1/2 related peaks positioned at 164.1 eV and 165.4 eV, respectively, representing neutral thiophene sulfur. Additionally, a low-intensity peak at 169.2 eV can also be observed from the deconvoluted S 2p XPS spectrum. This peak generally indicates a low level of oxidized sulfur, which probably occurred due to the exposure of the sample to the laboratory atmosphere.2

2.7 Photoelectrochemical performances

Fig. 5(a) depicts the current density vs. potential (J–V) plots of the three photoelectrodes under dark, and visible light illumination conditions, obtained by performing linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) at 10 mV s−1 scan rate in 0.5 M aq. Na2SO4 electrolyte. It can easily be observed that the photocurrent densities (Jph) considerably increase after the inorganic/organic nano-heterojunction formation. At 1.23 VRHE, the recorded Jph values are 0.157 mA cm−2, 0.504 mA cm−2, and 0.397 mA cm−2 for the N-TiO2 NR, N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH, and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH photoelectrodes, respectively. These results imply nearly 221% and 153% increments in the Jph values for the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs, respectively, compared to the N-TiO2 NRs at 1.23 VRHE. Fig. 5(c) exhibits the J–V plot under chopped visible light illumination, which substantiates the fast and reproducible photosensitivity of the photoelectrodes. It could be observed from Fig. 5(a) and (c) that both of the NH photoanodes produce a distinct reduction hump. The reduction peak appearing only under visible light illumination could be attributed to the consumption of the photogenerated electrons by one of the constituent moieties (probably AQ) in the polymers.

The water oxidation onset potential (Vonset) could be defined as the potential at which the extrapolation of the maximum-slope part of Jph intersects the dark current density (Jdark).46 The water oxidation onset potential might be visualized as that minimum voltage, which is additionally needed despite the energy of the photogenerated electron–hole pairs to initiate the water oxidation reaction. From Fig. S15,† it could be found that the Vonset potentials of N-TiO2 NRs, N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs, and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs are 0.68 VRHE, 0.45 VRHE, and 0.47 VRHE, respectively. Clear cathodic shifts in the water oxidation onset potential could be observed for the inorganic/organic NH electrodes compared to the N-TiO2 NR electrode. The N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs exhibit a slightly lower Vonset value than the N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH electrode. This could be related to the difference in the chemical composition of PAQTDPP and PAQBTDPP, which ultimately results in a bit better surface charge transport behavior in N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs.

Linear sweep voltammetry is often performed in the presence of an aqueous electrolyte containing a hole scavenger such as Na2SO3 to investigate the surface injection efficiency (ηinjection) of the photoanodes. Owing to the lower activation energy and faster oxidation kinetics of SO32− ions than water, ηinjection is assumed to be 100% for SO32− species.48 To probe the surface injection efficiency of the three photoanodes, here, we have performed the LSV measurement in an aqueous electrolyte containing 0.5 M Na2SO4 and 0.5 M Na2SO3 (pH 9.85). The open-circuit potential (Voc) is a potential at which the net exchange current across the semiconductor-electrolyte junction (SCLJ) becomes zero. Under the open-circuit conditions, the Fermi level of the semiconductor aligns with the redox potential of the electrolyte, making the net current flow across the SCLJ zero. The higher Voc value (less negative) in the dark might be correlated with the higher electron density of the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs than that of N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs.2 On illuminating a photoanode, the quasi-Fermi level of holes gets positioned near the valence band of the photoanode material at the SCLJ. The open-circuit potential becomes more negative compared to that under the dark conditions to restrain the photogenerated hole flow from the photoanode towards the electrolyte—resulting in a zero net current flow.2 ΔVoc is the difference between the Voc values under the dark and illumination conditions, and it reports on the photovoltage.52 The average ΔVoc values of the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs are 0.156 V and 0.164 V, respectively. Therefore, it is seen that the photovoltage is slightly higher in the N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs compared to that of its counterpart.

3 Conclusions

In conclusion, we have synthesized two anthraquinone and diketopyrrolopyrrole-based π-conjugated copolymers—PAQTDPP and PAQBTDPP—of D–A1–D–A2 architecture by the direct arylation polycondensation technique. In the PAQTDPP polymer, thiophene serves as the donor moiety. In contrast, bithiophene serves as the donor component in the PAQBTDPP polymer. The polymers are thermally stable, absorb the visible light spectrum, and are soluble in different organic solvents. We have fabricated two n–n inorganic/organic nano-heterostructures—N-TiO2/PAQTDPP and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP—by spin-coating. We have explored the visible light photoelectrochemical properties of the inorganic/organic hybrid materials as the photoanode in the PEC cell. We have found that the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH photoelectrode and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH photoelectrode exhibit photocurrent densities of 0.504 mA cm−2 and 0.397 mA cm−2 at 1.23 VRHE, which are 221% and 153% higher than that shown by the N-TiO2 NR photoelectrode, respectively. Each inorganic/organic NH photoanode also registers a significant cathodic shift in the corresponding water oxidation onset potential compared to the N-TiO2 NRs. Moreover, after 1 h of visible light illumination, the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH photoanodes produced 3.21 μmol cm−2 and 2.23 μmol cm−2 oxygen gas, respectively, at 0.5 VAg/AgCl. Under the same conditions, the quantities of collected hydrogen gas at the Pt wire for the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NHs and N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NHs were 6.25 μmol cm−2 and 4.46 μmol cm−2, respectively. The amelioration in the overall photoelectrochemical performances showcased by the NH electrodes is attributable to the better visible light absorbance, expedited delocalization of the photogenerated charge carriers at the heterointerface, and faster charge transportation from the organic polymers via the N-TiO2 NRs. However, the overall PEC performances of the N-TiO2/PAQTDPP NH photoelectrode is slightly better than that of its counterpart—the N-TiO2/PAQBTDPP NH photoelectrode—which could be ascribed to the higher order of dominance of the surface defects in the later mentioned NHs photoelectrode.
