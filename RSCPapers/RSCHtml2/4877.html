Fabrication of high performance printed flexible conductors by doping of polyaniline nanomaterials into silver paste†

Abstract

Large-scale industrialization of flexible printed electronics will thrive on advanced functional conductive pastes with excellent properties such as high electrical conductivity and mechanical stability. Herein, we develop an effective method to simultaneously decrease the electrical resistivity and improve the mechanical stability of silver-resin-based conductors by adding a small amount of nanostructured polyaniline (PANI). PANI nanomaterials for improving the properties of conductive composites were synthesized by a facile and repeatable chemical oxidative polymerization method and characterized. Significant improvements in electrical performance were observed. For example, for the 60 wt% silver-filled flexible conductors, the addition of 0.5 wt% PANIs reduces their electrical resistivity to one-thirtieth (from 1253.1 × 10−5 Ω cm to 37.1 × 10−5 Ω cm). After adding 0.5 wt% PANIs, the bending stability (ΔR/R0) was also greatly improved from 92% to 2.3% (about 1/40th). Meanwhile, elastic conductors showed a much better performance in resistance stability and fatigue life (5 times longer than before) during stretching cycles after the addition of PANIs. A bendable printed circuit and a printed tensile resistive sensor were fabricated using the above PANIs to enhance conductive composites, which proved their potential applications in flexible printed circuits, stretchable pressure sensors, and e-skin or other fields.

1. Introduction

Recently, printed electronics technologies as well as flexible and stretchable electronics have experienced enormous breakthroughs and drawn scientists’ tremendous interest. Flexible and stretchable technologies, especially those focusing on the manufacturing of devices, tracks or electrodes on flexible and stretchable substrates, can meet the demands of bending, twisting and stretching. Compared with conventional photolithography, vacuum deposition, and electroless plating processes, fabricating flexible or stretchable electronics by printing technology is a faster, low capital cost and eco-friendly method.1,2 Among the conductive materials, one of the most important types of printing inks is the electrical conductive composite (ECC) consisting of conductive fillers and elastic resin matrices.

Nowadays, advanced printed electronics technologies have been increasingly utilized to fabricate flexible and stretchable electronic devices with high precision, and mass-produced and energy-efficient functional devices, including organic thin film transistors (OTFTs),3,4 organic field effect transistors (OFETs),5 organic light emitting diodes (OLEDs),6 flexible perovskites or organic photovoltaic solar cells,7,8 radio frequency identification (FRID),9 sensors10 and so on.

Nevertheless, many fundamental research studies are still necessary to fabricate flexible and stretchable circuits with a lower electrical resistance and better mechanical durability (refer to electrical resistance durability during bending, twisting or stretching process), which are very critical for functional devices working with low power consumption and low noise immunity.

Therefore, numerous significant efforts have been focused on how to improve the conductivity or mechanical durability of flexible and stretchable electronic devices based on the perspective of materials science, namely conductive fillers and organic matrices. A few approaches have been utilized along this line. Firstly, surface treatment technology, with I2,11 acids,12 potassium bromide (KBr),13 polyethylene glycol (PEG)14 and so on, is a direct and effective method that can replace or remove the organic layer on the conductive fillers, which is beneficial for conductivity. Some surfactants, such as silane coupling agents15 or fluorine surfactants,16 can improve the conductivity of elastomers by enhancing the compatibility of resins and conductive fillers, which is also beneficial for the mechanical properties of ECCs. Secondly, some nanomaterials,17–19 such as silver nanoparticles, or nano-gels20 were controllably synthesized and added into the ECCs, which can fill the gap between conductive fillers and increase conductive paths. Other low dimensional nanomaterials, such as metallic nanowires,21 CNTs22,23 and graphene,22,24 were also added to achieve better electrical or mechanical properties. Sometimes, low temperature sintering between nanomaterials and conductive fillers will also occur at temperatures lower than 200 °C, which could drastically enhance the electrical conductivity of ECCs. Besides, some micro interconnection methods, including electroplating,25 photonic sintering26 or laser welding,27 can join the conductive materials together at a faster rate and with high efficiency. However, these micro-interconnect technologies faced various challenges such as high cost of equipment or extreme accuracy operation requirements.

On the other hand, during the printing process, the remaining polymer matrices are considered to provide the essential mechanical properties of the ECCs. Generally, epoxy-based composites and polyurethane-based (PU-based) resins are often used where strong bond strengths are required.14,28 PDMS-based ECCs show a much better tensile strengths of more than 800%.9,29 PDMS and PI show good thermal stability when printed devices are needed to work at high temperature.30

Therefore, because of the abundance of materials and various requirements for applications, there are still great challenges for developing innovative ECCs with advanced functionality by using new approaches with low cost and easy operation.

PANIs, used as dopants in this work, are a kind of intrinsically conducting conjugated polymer, and they have been used in the fields of gas sensors or biosensors,31–33 tissue engineering in biomaterial components,34 removal of heavy metal ions,35 and thermoelectric materials.36 However, due to their low electrical conductivity compared to metals, PANIs have been used less in the field of conduction, such as printed circuits or electrical conductive adhesive.

Herein, we have proposed a simple and effective method for improving the electrical conductivity and mechanical durability of flexible or stretchable circuits by adding a small amount of PANIs into traditional silver-based pastes, namely PANI enhanced conductive composites (PECCs). Although some conjugated polymers (polypyrrole37 or PEDOT:PSS20) have been found to improve the conductivity of non-flexible conductors, there are no similar reports on polyaniline or conjugated polymer applications in flexible electronics. We not only prove the effect of PANIs in improving the conductivity of flexible or stretchable conductors, but also for the first time, we prove the application of such conjugated polymers in improving the electrical stability during the deformation process. The improvement of electrical conductivity and stability during bending and stretching is enabled by more coherent conductive paths and robust resin matrices resulting from interactions of silver flakes and PANIs. Systematic research will be undertaken based on the synthesis, preparation, characterization and application to understand the mechanism of these improvements enhanced by PANIs. The feasibility of the flexible conductors is further proved by a screen printing process.

2. Experimental section

2.1 Materials

Aniline (ANI), polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), ammonium persulfate (APS) and ethanol were purchased from Aladdin, Shanghai, China. Hydrochloric acid (HCl) was purchased from Tianjin Fengchuan Chemical Reagent Technologies Co., Ltd, Tianjin, China. N,N-Dimethylformamide (DMF) was purchased from Damao Chemical Reagent Factory, Tianjin, China. Ag flakes with average diameters of 8–10 μm were donated by Bolong Silver Industry, Changsha, China. Polyvinylbutyral (PVB) as a bendable resin matrix was purchased from Meryer Chemical Technology Co., Ltd, Shanghai, China. Thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) as a bendable resin matrix was purchased from Evermore Chemical Industry CO., Ltd, Taiwan, China. Ecoflex 00-30 as a stretchable resin matrix was purchased from Smooth-on Inc, Pennsylvania, US.

2.2 Synthesis of PANI nanomaterials

The synthesis of PANIs by using an oxidative polymerization approach38,39 is presented as follows: 6 mmol of PVP and 40 mmol of APS were dissolved in 40 ml of DI water at room temperature in air. Simultaneously, 6 ml of 1 M HCl was added as a doping agent. After APS and PVP were completely dissolved and the solution became clear and transparent, 1 ml of ANI was rapidly injected into the above-mentioned mixture. The reaction mixture was kept stirring at 400 rpm for 4 hours. The color of the solution changed from transparent to green, indicating the successful polymerization of PANIs. Finally, the product was centrifuged and washed with ethanol five times until the supernatant became transparent. Yield: 72%.

2.3 Preparation of PANI enhanced silver-based ECCs

For the preparation of the ECCs, three types of resin, namely PVB, TPU, and Ecoflex, were chosen to achieve various ECCs with different flexibility and stretchability. PVB (20 g per 100 ml ethanol) and TPU (10 g per 100 ml DMF) were chosen as resin matrices respectively to prepare PANI enhanced bendable conductors (PEBCs), and Ecoflex 00-10 (a kind of two-component material with a mixing weight ratio of 1:1, cured at room temperature for 2 hours) was chosen as the resin matrix to prepare PANI-enhanced stretchable conductors (PESCs).

Ag flakes were added into the resin matrix with a mass ratio of 60–75 wt% to obtain ideal electrical conductivity and rheological properties. Then, a tiny amount of PANIs, typically 0.5 wt%, was mixed with the Ag-based pastes to enhance the electrical properties and mechanical durability. These materials were mixed together using a mixing deaerator (SIE-MIX80, Sienox Industrial Products Ltd China), which could mix the samples uniformly by rotation and revolution simultaneously. It usually takes 3 minutes at a speed of 2500 rpm to disperse PANIs and conductive fillers well in the pastes using the mixing deaerator. Electrical tracks for testing and applications were formed by a flat-bed screen printing technique.

For the resistivity test, films of electrical composites on the surfaces of glass slides were prepared by knife coating (76 mm × 19 mm). For the preparation of PANI-enhanced conductive conductors, fine-pitch and bendable or stretchable conductive tracks were prepared by a similar stencil printing process on flexible PET substrates and soft Ecoflex substrates to power the capacitance, resistance, transistor, and LED devices.

2.4 Characterization methods

The morphologies of the PANIs and the distribution of PANIs in the ECCs were characterized using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM, TESCAN MIRA3, Czech) with an accelerating voltage of 5 kV. Optical microscopy (OM, OLYMPUS BX51, Japan) was used to observe the phase separation phenomenon. The measurements of the X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) were performed by using a VG-Scientific ESCALAB 250Xi spectrometer with a monochromatic Al Kα X-ray source at 1486.6 eV.

Bulk electrical resistivity was obtained by conversion from sheet resistivity measured using a four-point probe system (RTS-9, 4 PROBES TECH, Guangzhou, China). Each sample was tested 9 times to calculate the average parameters and errors. The electrical resistivity (ρ) of the ECCs was calculated using the following equation:

ρ = Rst
where Rs is the sheet resistivity obtained from the four-point probe system and t is the thickness of the film.

Bending tests were performed using a homemade bending machine, and the bending speed was set as 2 seconds per cycle (0.5 Hz). The samples were bended at 180° with a diameter of 1 cm. Stretching tests were performed using the same machine as in the bending test at a rate of 1 mm s−1 and stretchable conductors were fabricated with a 20% pre-stretch.

3.2 Morphologies of PANI nanomaterials and their distributions among silver flakes

In order to improve the dispersibility of PANIs in ECCs, nano structured PANIs were desirable considering that smaller size PANIs could fill the gaps between the silver flakes in the matrices. PVPs were used as surfactants to tune the sizes of the PANIs. With the help of PVPs (for example, 6 mmol per 40 ml of solution), the morphologies of PANIs changed from large blocks bigger than 10 μm without PVPs to nano-dendritic fibers, as shown in Fig. 2a and b, respectively. These dendrite-structure nanomaterials with an average diameter of 60.6 nm ± 8.7 nm showed better performance in the PECCs in stencil printing and uniformly filming.
Fig. 2 SEM images of PANIs synthesized (a) without or (b) with PVP as a surfactant; (c) SEM image of large blocks of PANIs (shown in figure a) mixed with the silver flakes; (d) SEM image of nano-structured PANIs (shown in figure b) mixed with the silver flakes.

Fig. 2c and d show the SEM images of the above-mentioned two kinds of PANIs mixed into silver flakes. Some large PANIs are inlaid among the silver flakes, as shown in Fig. 2c. This uneven separation might be the reason for why adding large PANIs will worsen the printing properties, as shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†). Further, the large size semi-conducting PANI powders, which have less conductivity than the silver flakes, may block the conductive pathways. However, the PANIs with nano-dendritic-fibers dispersed homogeneously between the silver flakes, as shown by the red arrow in Fig. 2d. This improved dispersion of PANIs among the Ag flakes might be favorable for the conductivity of PECCs. It was also observed that after adding PANIs into the pastes, the color of the conductive pastes changed from silver to light chartreuse.

3.3 Application of PANIs in improving electrical properties

Fig. 3a and d show the digital photographs of PVB-based PECC pastes and PU-based PECC pastes. After adding a small amount of PANIs (here it is 0.5 wt%), the electrically conductive pastes still maintain excellent dispersibility and suspension stability in terms of appearance. Table S1 in the ESI† shows the viscosity changes of PU-based PECCs after adding different amounts of PANIs (measured at 25 °C). The addition of PANIs lowers the viscosity slightly, which is beneficial for printing properties.
Fig. 3 Images of as-prepared PVB-based (a) and PU-based (d) conductive pastes with 0.5 wt% PANIs; four-point probe test results of bulk resistivity of PVB-based (b) and PU-based (e) PEBCs with or without 0.5 wt% PANIs; four-point probes test results of bulk resistivity of 65 wt% silver-filled PVB-based (c) and PU-based (f) PEBCs with different amounts of PANIs.

As shown in Fig. 3b (blue histograms), the conductivity increases with simply adding more silver filler, which is in accordance with the percolation theory.40 It was observed that bulk resistivity decreased from 1253.1 × 10−5 Ω cm for 60 wt% silver-filled ECCs to 27.1 × 10−5 Ω cm for 75 wt% PVB-based silver-filled ECCs. A significant conductivity change was observed between the ECCs filled with 60 wt%-silver flakes and 65 wt%-silver-flakes, indicating that the percolation threshold of these ECC materials was about 65 wt% silver. According to the percolation theory, adding more conducting fillers was not advisable for further improvement of conductivity when the dosage of the fillers is over the percolation threshold, which is also not conducive to reducing the cost and maintaining the rheological properties.

When comparing the conductivities of ECCs with or without 0.5 wt% PANIs, the significant improvement of conductivities for the ECCs with PANIs revealed the decisive role of PANIs in enhancing the electrical properties of ECCs (Fig. 3b). It is worth mentioning that after a small amount of PANIs (0.5 wt%) were added into 60 wt% silver-filled ECCs, the bulk resistivity of the ECCs with PANIs (37.1 × 10−5 Ω cm) decreased to 1/30th of that of the conventional ECCs without PANIs (1253.1 × 10−5 Ω cm). This significant increase of conductivity is most likely attributed to the synergistic effect of the nano-structured PANIs in filling the gaps between silver flakes and inducing phase separation slightly between silver flakes and resin matrices, which will be further discussed in a later section (3.7).

Fig. 3c shows the changes in resistivity of the PVB-based-ECCs (with 65 wt% of silver flakes) after adding different amounts of PANIs. It was observed that by only adding a small amount of PANIs, 0.5 wt%, the resistivity of the composites decreased from 171.3 × 10−5 Ω cm for the one without PANIs to 16.6 × 10−5 Ω cm. Using less silver filler for a remarkable electrical performance enhancement is very beneficial for saving costs and reducing the impact on rheology. However, the conductivity of the PECCs stopped rising with the further increase of PANIs beyond 0.5 wt%. This was possibly because the phase separation induced by PANIs is a main factor in enhancing the conductivity of ECCs. However, phase separation will not increase the conductivity infinitely, which will also be further discussed in a later section (3.7). Besides, other kinds of PANIs with different micro-structures or sizes were also added into the ECCs for improving the electrical properties. As shown in Fig. S2 and S3 (ESI†), actually, the improvement in conductivity is hardly affected by the PANI morphology when using nano-sized PANIs.

Similar results of the influences of silver content and/or PANI weight percentages on the conductivity for PU-based ECCs were also observed (Fig. 3e and f). In particular, for 65 wt% silver-filled PU-based-ECCs, the bulk resistivity significantly decreased from 125.6 × 10−5 Ω cm to 14.5 × 10−5 Ω cm after adding 0.5 wt% PANIs (Fig. 3f). Nevertheless, for the 60 wt% silver-filled PU-based ECCs, the value of resistivity is much higher than others and is extremely unstable for flexible electronics, which suggested a higher percolation threshold for PU-based ECCs than that of PVB-based ECCs, showing the influence of the matrix on the electrical properties.

It is worth pointing out that the electrical conductivity of the 60 wt% silver-filled PU-based ECCs with 0.5 wt% PANIs (18.7 × 10−5 Ω cm) was even better than that of the 75 wt% silver-filled ECCs without PANIs. This comparison indicated that the use of a small amount of PANIs can improve the conductivity significantly and can be used for reducing the cost by lowering the dosage of silver flakes.

On the other hand, as shown in Fig. 3f, upon further increasing the content of the PANIs, different from the PVB-based ECCs, the bulk resistivity of the PU-based ECCs was not lowered, which might be due to the fact that too many semiconducting PANIs are harmful for the electrical conductivities of the PU-based ECCs. Therefore, a moderate amount of PANIs is necessary for improving the electrical properties of the PU-based silver-filled ECCs.

It was found that our flexible printable conductors reach an excellent conductivity of 1.31 × 104 S cm−1 for the 75 wt% silver-filled PU-based PECCs with 0.5 wt% PANIs in their release state. 3.5 Bending properties of the PANI-enhanced conductive composites

For real applications in flexible devices, currently significant endeavors must also focus on how to enhance the mechanical properties of ECCs.

Besides the conductivity improvement by using PANIs for the ECCs, it was also found that the PANI nano-materials can simultaneously improve the mechanical durability, namely electrical durability, during the bending and stretching of the conductors. As shown in Fig. 5a–c, resistance changes of the PEBCs (0.5 wt% PANIs/65 wt% silver flakes/TPU-based) at 180° bending over 2500 cycles were investigated by using a conductive film with dimensions of 1 cm × 5 cm. The printed track I with a thin electrical layer (6 μm) and without PANIs showed an increase in resistance during the bending process and finally failed at a low number of cycles (about 1500 times). This bad performance was improved by simply increasing the thickness of the electrical layer (shown as printed tracks II with 13 μm); however, the relative resistance still increased by 92% (from 4.03 Ω to 7.76 Ω) after bending over 2500 cycles. Nevertheless, during the same bending test, printed track III (PANI enhanced conductive tracks) showed an excellent mechanical durability in terms of resistance. Fig. 5d shows more details about the resistance changes of printed track III (PEBCs) during the bending process from the 900th to the 950th cycles. It is interesting to note that the resistance of the PEBCs showed excellent stability with a resistance variation ratio of 2.3% (namely ΔR/R0 = (R − R0)/R0, where R represents the final resistance in the state of bending and R0 represents the initial resistance). The 2.3% variation for printed track III is much smaller than those of printed tracks I (more than 500%) and II (92%). Thus, the above results showed the innovative discovery of using a small amount of PANIs to improve the bending properties of the conductive pastes.


Fig. 5 (a) Relative resistance change of three types of PEBCs during the 180° bending process; (b) image of the conductive films with dimensions of 1 cm × 5 cm; (c) conductors were bent along a rod with a diameter of 10 mm; (d) relative resistance change of printed track III during 950–1000 bend–release cycles.

3.6 Stretching properties of PANI-enhanced conductive composites

Besides the bending stability, the stretchable stability of conductors is another very important property to maintain as reliable when working in the area of e-skin or wearable electronics. The comparison of the tensile resistive behavior of the PANI-enhanced ECCs under cyclic stretching with progressively increasing strain amplitude was explored and is plotted in Fig. 6.
Fig. 6 (a) Digital photographs of the original and stretched PESCs with a stretchability of 100% and 200%. Cyclic stretch–release measurement of the resistance change of electrical conductors (b) without doping or (c) with 0.5 wt% PANIs at strain amplitudes of 10%, 20%, 30%, 40% and 50%.

Fig. 6a shows the connection method and the stretching process of the PESCs (0.5 wt% PANIs/80 wt% silver flakes) with different elongations. There was no mechanical damage or micro-cracks when stretched to 200% of their original length.

Fig. 6b and c show the resistance response of the stretchable conductors without or with 0.5 wt% PANIs under different cyclic strain rates (10%, 20%, 30%, 40% and 50%) for 10 stretching cycles. For stretchable circuits, PANIs played a similar role in improving the electrical conductivity and mechanical stability. Taking the strain rate of 20% as an example, the resistance variation ratio (namely ΔR/R0 = (R − R0)/R0, where R represents the final resistance in the state of stretching and R0 represents the initial resistance) of the stretchable conductors decreased from 842% without PANIs to 24% after adding 0.5 wt% PANIs. Other details of the resistance variation ratios are shown in Table S2 (ESI†).

Fig. 7 shows the comparison of the resistance of the stretchable conductors with or without 0.5 wt% PANIs during 3500 cycles of the 30% strain rate test. The resistance of the conductors without PANIs, shown as red lines, increased exponentially over the first 700 cycles and then finally failed. In contrast, the conductivity of the PESCs, shown as blue lines in Fig. 7, basically remained invariable during 3500 cycles of strain.

3.7 Mechanisms of high conductivity and excellent mechanical properties

Some studies on the use of conjugated polymers (polypyrrole37 or PEDOT:PSS20) to enhance conductive composites revealed that the conjugated polymers could connect the gaps between silver microflakes. However, there are nearly no reports on conjugated polymers that could enhance mechanical durability and the mechanism.

To thoroughly understand the reason why such a small amount of PANIs can tremendously enhance the electrical conductivity, especially the bending stability and the stretching stability of the ECCs, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and optical microscopy (OM) were used to characterize the phase separation process and the self-assembly process in these elastic conductors, as shown in Fig. 8.


Fig. 8 Optical microscopy images of 65 wt% silver-filled PU-based ECCs without (a) and with 0.5 wt% (b), 1.0 wt% (c), 1.5 wt% (d) and 2 wt% PANIs (e). Cross-sectional SEM images of the corresponding ECCs films without (f) and with 0.5 wt% (g), 1.0 wt% (h), 1.5 wt% (i) and 2 wt% PANIs (j). (k–n) Schematic representation of the ECC micro-structural change and variation of the gap between the silver flakes caused by phase separation. Scale bars of (f)–(j) are 50 μm.

Adding PANIs altered the distribution of the silver flakes in the resins gradually, as observed visually in the OM images (Fig. 8a–e). Fig. 8f–j show the cross-sectional view of the SEM images of the corresponding ECC films. The original state of the silver flakes was uniformly tiled on the surface of the substrate along with the direction of the knife coating, as shown in Fig. 8f. As the amount of PANI dopants gradually increased, the dispersion of the silver flakes in the matrices turned into a state of interlaced distribution. This phenomenon of phase separation can be observed as long as 0.5 wt% PANIs was added, as shown in Fig. 8g. Fig. 8k–n show the schematic diagram of the micro-structure and the variation of silver flake distribution in the matrices from surface-to-surface contact to closer contact form. Once PANIs were added, phase separation occurs and the electrical and mechanical properties can be improved after ECC curing.

XPS tests were further used to characterize the interactions of silver and PANIs at the interface. The Ag 3d spectrum of the bare silver flakes and silver flake/PANI composites is shown in Fig. 9. The Ag 3d5/2 and Ag 3d3/2 peaks are located at the binding energies of 368.22 and 374.22 eV, respectively. Compared with those before adding PANIs, the values of the binding energy of Ag 3d5/2 and Ag 3d3/2 become slightly lower by 0.36 eV. It has been reported that chemical reactions between some conjugated polymers (such as polypyrroles51,52 or PANIs53) and silver can lower the binding energy of Ag. This is usually considered to be caused by silver oxidation after bonding with PANI chains.54 Therefore, we supposed that the interactions of the interfaces between silver and PANIs result in an unstable dispersion of silver flakes in the resin matrices, namely phase separation.


Fig. 9 XPS Ag 3d spectra of the silver flakes and silver flakes/PANI composites.

Due to such an integrated phenomenon of phase separation and self-assembly, conductive paths became more coherent and resin distribution became more robust at the same time. During the stretching process, the silver-rich region maintains stable electrical conduction and the resin-rich region ensures softness and stretchability. The phenomenon of electrical property and mechanical property improvement caused by phase separation and molecular self-assembly has also been reported by Matsuhisa et al.55,56

However, when an excessive amount of PANIs over 1 wt% was added, too severe phase separation will affect the filming performance of the ECCs during stencil printing. In addition, there is a minimum tunneling distance, so the tunneling resistance will not be enhanced infinitely when adding more PANIs (after adding 0.5 wt% PANIs). Therefore, here, we recommend the use of 0.5 wt% PANIs in the ECCs in order to obtain excellent devices.

3.8 Applications of the ECCs for bendable printed electronics and tensile resistive sensors

To demonstrate the potential applications of our PANI-enhanced conductors in the actual printed electronics industry, some flexible high-conductivity tracks were generated by stencil printing.

Fig. 10a shows a printed alternating flashing circuit, whose flicker frequency was controlled by voltage. Fig. 10b shows the excellent adhesion and flexibility of the printed circuits. Fig. 10c shows a circuit of a wireless radio. Fig. 10d and e illustrate the working state of the circuit shown above under bending. All the above flexible PANI-enhanced circuits were prepared by PU-based 65 wt% silver filled with 0.5 wt% PANIs on a PET film.


Fig. 10 Application of PEBCs in printed circuits. (a) Printed circuits with a line width of 1 mm. (b and c) Printed circuit in a bending state. (d and e) Alternating flashing LED devices working under the bending state with a driving voltage from 2.5 V to 4.5 V.

Furthermore, considering the excellent stability and tensile response of the PESCs during the stretching process, a tensile resistive sensor was fabricated on fingers, as shown in Fig. 11. It is necessary to explain that, here, the bending of the fingers led to the stretching of the film. Therefore, during the process of bending and releasing the fingers, the relative resistance of the film showed a quick increase and a rapid corresponding decrease. Therefore, the simple device on fingers proved the extensive applications of PESCs in e-skin besides printing stretchable circuits.

4. Conclusion

In summary, we develop an effective and facile method to simultaneously decrease the bulk electrical resistivity and improve the mechanical stability of flexible or stretchable printed conductors by adding a small amount of PANI nanomaterials into the composites of silver flakes with PVB, PU, and/or Ecoflex resins. The PANIs act as dopants causing phase-separation to certain degrees and self-assembly of silver flakes and resin matrices, which was considered as the main reason for these improvements. In particular, after adding 0.5 wt% PANIs into traditional conductive composites, a significant improvement in performance was observed. For example: (1) electrical conductivity was enhanced 30 fold for the 60 wt% silver-filled PVB-based ECCs; (2) resistance fluctuations dropped to one-fortieth during the bending experiment for the 65 wt% silver-filled PU-based PEBCs; and (3) the fatigue life was extended by more than 5 times during the stretching experiment for the 80 wt% silver-filled Ecoflex-based PESCs. Therefore, we demonstrated that, with a little effort, the functionality of the new pastes with a small amount of PANI as a dopant was improved significantly. This PANI-enhanced-performance method is expected to have great capability for applications in various printing systems, offering high electrically conductive, bending stable and/or stretching stable electronics.
