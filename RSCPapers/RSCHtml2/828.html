Plasma modified flexible bucky paper as an efficient counter electrode in dye sensitized solar cells

Soumyendu Roy a, Reeti Bajpai a, Ajay Kumar Jena b, Pragyensh Kumar b, Neha kulshrestha a and D. S. Misra *a
aDepartment of Physics, Indian Institute of Technology Bombay, Mumbai 400076, India. E-mail: drk1955@gmail.com.
bDepartment of Metallurgical Engineering and Materials Science, Indian Institute of Technology Bombay, Mumbai 400076, India

Received 21st December 2011 , Accepted 17th February 2012

First published on 20th February 2012

Abstract

Platinum (Pt) -free counter electrodes (CEs) for dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) were developed using freestanding flexible single wall carbon nanotube (SWNT) films called bucky papers (BPs). BP was irradiated with microwave plasma, created using a mixture of Ar (1%) and H2 (99%) gases, for 2 h. Raman scattering measurements revealed that no significant defects were created in the SWNTs as a result of the treatment. Plasma-treated BP (P-BP) developed vertically oriented, micron sized, pillar-like structures on its surface, while its base was still a dense random mesh of SWNTs. This unique flexible film had a larger accessible surface area and better catalytic properties. The plasma treatment improved the efficiency of BP-based DSSCs from 2.44% to 4.02%, which is comparable to Pt thin film (4.08%). The P-BP based solar cell operated with an open circuit voltage of 0.73 V and a fill factor of 0.70. It also had much higher efficiencies than films of randomly oriented plasma treated SWNTs. Using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy, the charge transfer resistances of P-BP and Pt were found to be 1.46 and 1.73 cm2, respectively.


Broader context

Efforts are being made on a worldwide scale to develop technologies that can meet the ever increasing demands for energy in a way that is environmentally sustainable. Photovoltaic devices are expected to play a major role. Dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are new generation solar cells that stand out because of their ease of fabrication. One disadvantage of DSSCs is the use of a precious and scarce metal, platinum (Pt). Carbon-based materials, especially carbon nanotubes (CNT) have been touted as a possible replacement for Pt. The electrocatalytic capabilities of CNTs have been well established thorough numerous experiments in the recent past. Herein, flexible substrate-free single wall CNT (SWNT) films called bucky papers (BPs) were used as counter electrodes in DSSCs. Using a microwave plasma treatment procedure, the BPs were converted into a unique material (P-BP) that had vertical SWNT microstructures on its surface. P-BP-based DSSCs had performance characteristics comparable to Pt, and superior to untreated BP and films of randomly oriented plasma-treated SWNTs. The charge transfer resistance of P-BP for the I/I3 redox couple measured by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was also found to be slightly lower than Pt. We expect further optimization and modifications to lead to further improvements in the properties of P-BP, and it could become a suitable substitute for Pt, especially in flexible solar cells.

Introduction

Dye sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) are emerging as one of the key technologies in the field of photovoltaics because of their low cost, simplicity of fabrication and significant performance efficiency. The main electron exchange processes1,2 occurring inside a DSSC are: (1) photo excitation of dye molecules. (2) Transfer of electrons into the conduction band of TiO2 (the most popular wide band gap semiconductor material used in DSSCs) from the excited dye molecules at the anode. (3) Transportation of electrons through the nanocrystalline TiO2 particles to the anode. (4) Reduction of the oxidized dye molecules by I, which regenerates the dye molecules enabling further absorption of photons. I3 formed as a result of the reaction diffuses to the cathode or counter electrode (CE). (5) Reduction of I3 to I at the CE by the reaction: I3 + 2e = 3I. This step completes the photon-induced charge separation in the DSSC as well as the redox cycle of the electrolyte. Each of these steps exerts a profound influence on the overall energy conversion efficiency of DSSC. Here we concern ourselves with the catalyst material used to facilitate the reduction of I3 at the CE. Pt has been established as the most suitable material to catalyze this reaction.1 However, for commercialization and mass usage it is imperative to reduce or eliminate the dependence on Pt because it is not only expensive but is also scarcely available on this planet.

Experiments have shown appreciable performance from various carbon-based cathode materials such as mesoporous carbon,3 activated carbon,4 carbon black,5 nanocarbon,6 graphene,7 conducting polymer8 and carbon nanotubes (CNTs).913 In addition to the cathode, CNTs have also been used in other components of DSSCs, for example as the transparent and conducting coating on electrodes.10 It has been used in composites with TiO2 to improve electron transportation at the anode,10,14 as a replacement for TiO215 and even directly as the photoactive material for the generation of charged carriers.16 The efficiency of DSSCs using CNT based cathodes has been found to be lower than Pt ones. To overcome this shortcoming, the general strategy has been to form composites with Pt,17 conjugated conducting polymers like PEDOT,18 TiN,19 CoS,20 polypropylene,21etc. One aspect that has been largely overlooked is that vertically aligned CNT films may be catalytically more active than randomly oriented ones and some work in this direction has started emerging only very recently.22,23

Freestanding or unsupported films of CNTs are called bucky papers (BPs). These are generally formed by self assembly techniques like vacuum filtration. Although macroscopic in dimension, these films retain most of the properties of the constituent 1D nanotubes. In this study we have used single wall carbon nanotubes (SWNTs) to make BPs. Such films have been used for several applications like Li+ ion batteries, fuel cells, actuators, heat conductors, field emission, supercapacitors, etc.2428 They are highly flexible, mechanically robust, have a large accessible surface area, high conductivity and are chemically inert. These properties make them an attractive choice for electrodes in electrochemical devices. There is also a demand for light-weight and flexible solar cells for applications like satellites and foldable electronics.29 BP is especially suitable for these purposes.

Low-temperature plasma is a versatile tool for the fabrication and manipulation of nano-materials and thin films.30 In this article, we propose the use of plasma treated BP (P-BP) as a CE in DSSCs. When exposed to microwave plasma, the densely packed horizontal SWNTs in BP stand up and form pillar-like vertical microstructures on the surface. The treatment significantly improves the BrunauerEmmettTeller (BET) surface area of BP without any additional defect creation in the SWNTs.27 We found that the BP-based CE yielded a lower efficiency than conventional sputter-deposited Pt thin film (Std Pt). However, P-BP and Std Pt had comparable performances. The efficiencies for both these materials were 4% and open circuit voltages were slightly higher than 0.7 V. Both showed good fill factors of around 0.7. P-BP was redispersed into SWNT bundles and drop-casted on conducting glass electrodes to form films of randomly aligned plasma-treated SWNT (P-SWNT). This had much lower efficiency than P-BP. The catalytic properties of the CEs fabricated using all these different materials were further studied using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The observed values of charge transfer resistances (Rct) for both P-BP and Std Pt were low; 1.46 and 1.73 cm2, respectively.

Experimental

Synthesis and plasma treatment of BP

SWNTs were grown by catalytic chemical vapour deposition (CVD) method. CH4 diluted with H2 gas was blown over a heated catalyst kept at a temperature of 960970 C and under atmospheric pressure. The catalyst consisted of a solid solution of Co and Mo in MgO (Co:Mo:MgO = 1:0.5:300 by weight) prepared by combustion synthesis.31 The mean diameter of the SWNTs was roughly 1.35 nm.27 The catalyst material was separated from the tubes by dissolution in 11 M HNO3 solution. Purified SWNTs were dispersed and filtered through a polyvinylidene fluoride membrane under an applied pressure gradient. The BPs used in these experiments were about 1.71.8 cm in diameter and 1012 m in thickness. The plasma treatment of BP was carried out inside a microwave plasma CVD chamber for a duration of 2 h. A mixture of H2 and Ar gases with 99% H2 by volume was exposed to microwave radiation (2.45 GHz) to create the plasma. The magnetron power used was 400 W. The temperature and pressure inside the chamber were kept around 800 C and 70 Torr, respectively.

Preparation of CEs

For the fabrication of P-SWNT CEs, 0.1 mg of P-BP was added to 50 ml of isopropanol and ultra-sonicated for about 15 h until the P-BP was sufficiently redispersed into individual bundles. Roughly 3.8 ml of this solution was drop-casted over an area of approximately 1.5 cm2 of fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) coated glass (TEC 7, Pilkington U.K., with Sheet resistance 8 ohm/ and thickness 2.2 mm) giving a CNT loading of roughly 5 g cm2. In other experiments, BP and P-BP were directly clamped to the anode using FTO glass for support and electrical contact. To assess the efficiency of these solar cells in relation to the industry standards, a CE was formed by sputter depositing a thin Pt film (2025 nm) onto FTO (Std Pt). This closely resembles conventionally used Pt electrodes. Sputtering was carried out in a Nordiko2000 RF sputtering system. The Pt target used had a purity of 99.999%.

Preparation of photo anodes

Titania (TiO2) slurry was made in ethanol with solid loading of 6% v/v. Poly-ethylene glycol (PEG) (MW approx. 600, Thomas baker 99.9%) was used as the dispersant as well as the binder. The composition of the slurry was 7.2 g TiO2 (P25 Degussa, mean particle size 25 nm, anatase:rutile 75:25), 7.2 ml PEG 600 and 21 ml ethanol. The mixture was roller milled for 24 h. The TiO2 paste was coated on FTO glass using the doctor blade technique. The TiO2 films had an area of about 0.16 cm2 and a thickness of 89 m. These films were dried in air and sintered at 450 C for 1 h. TiO2-coated FTO was dipped in a dye (Dyesol) made of 0.3 mM N3 (cis-bis(isothiocyanato) bis(2,2-bipyridyl-4,4-dicarboxylato)-ruthenium(II)) in EtOH for 24 h at room temperature. Almost a monolayer of dye molecules get adsorbed onto the surface of TiO2 nanoparticles. These serve as the photoanodes.

Assembling the DSSC

The DSSC was formed via the following three steps: (1) a surlyn sheet of 60 m thickness was placed across the periphery of theTiO2 film. This serves as a sealer as well a spacer between the photoanode and CE. (2) A few drops of electrolyte were smeared over the TiO2 film. The composition of the electrolyte was 0.6 M PMII (1-methyl-3-propylimidazolium iodide, 98% Aldrich) + 0.5 M LiI (anahydrous, 98%, Merck) + 0.05 M I2 (99.8%, Thomas Baker) + 0.5 M 4-tert-butyl pyridine (TBP) (96%, Aldrich) in acetonitrile (Merck). (3) The CE was placed over the TiO2 photoanode and the whole arrangement was clamped tightly.

Characterization

The surface microstructure and morphology of the BP was examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (JEOL, JSM-6400). The high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) and Raman spectrometer used for imaging and probing the SWNTs are JEOL, JEM 2100F and Horiba Jobin Yvon, HR 800, respectively. The photovoltaic response of the solar cells (current voltage characteristics) were measured on a Keithley 2420 source meter (least counts: 50 V and 100 pA) under illumination from a Newport class A solar simulator. Solar radiation of intensity 100 mW cm2 for air mass 1.5 G was provided by the device. EIS measurements were performed on the DSSCs using Autolab PGSTAT 302N (Metrohm). The cells were kept under the same illumination as above and a DC bias was applied so as to maintain an open circuit condition. The frequency of the applied AC signal was varied from 1 Hz to 105 Hz and the amplitude was kept at 0.05 V. Z View version 3.2c was used to fit the experimental EIS data with a circuit model.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1(a) presents HRTEM images of as-synthesized SWNTs and Fig. 1(b) shows an individual SWNT bundle under high magnification. SEM images of the BP surface before and after the plasma treatment are shown in Fig. 2 (a)(c). The BP can be folded and unfolded without causing any visible damage (see the inset of Fig. 2(a)). The surface morphology of BP undergoes dramatic changes after irradiation with H2 + Ar plasma. Layers of CNT material on the BP surface stand up and are then etched by the plasma to produce vertical pillar-like microstructures.27 The flexibility of BP remains intact after the treatment. Vertical arrays of CNTs can also be produced by alternative methods such as chemical vapour deposition (CVD) on lithographically patterned catalyst. But these suffer from disadvantages like the presence of metal catalysts and weak adherence of CNTs to the substrate. The presence of the substrate itself can also lead to undesirable effects in several applications. P-BP on the other hand is substrate-free and composed only of CNTs. The D band in the Raman spectrum of BP does not change much as a result of the treatment, as can be seen in Fig. 2(d). This indicates that although SWNTs are being sputtered away by the plasma, those that remain have their sp2 crystallinity intact. This is somewhat counter intuitive, but has always been observed in our experiments. Accordingly, no significant difference between SWNT and P-SWNT were seen inside HRTEM. The BP-based DSSC had an efficiency of 2.44%, which improved significantly after plasma treatment. We found that the performance of DSSCs based on P-BP and Std Pt films were similar. Both showed an efficiency of about 4%. Jsc of Std Pt was higher than P-BP; 8.38 and 7.83 mA cm2, respectively. However, the FF of P-BP (=0.7) was slightly better. P-BP was redispersed into isolated SWNT bundles and drop-casted on FTO to form a film of randomly oriented plasma-treated SWNT (P-SWNT). However, this rearrangement resulted in a loss of efficiency (see Table 1). of P-SWNT film was close to that of ordinary BP. In addition, the shape of its IV characteristic differed from the expected rectangle-like shape. This may be due to the unstable attachment of CNTs to FTO. The low efficiency of P-SWNT indicates that there is no significant change in the catalytic properties of individual SWNTs on plasma irradiation. The improvement in the efficiency of P-BP is solely due to the modification of geometry and structure of its surface. Plasma treatment increases the BET surface area of BP by almost three times.27 Additionally, the vertical alignment of SWNTs exposes more edges of the nanotubes. Edges are known to be more reactive than the side-walls. These factors lead to more efficient I3 reduction at the cathode. It is worthwhile to note that back-reflection of light from the cathode has been known to improve absorption at the anode and hence the efficiency of the DSSC.2 Pt film is reflective but BP is not. Thus P-BP is catalytic enough to overcome this drawback and work as efficiently as a Pt thin film. Although the perfectly crystalline basal plane of graphite is known to have very little catalytic effect,32 CNTs with their high conductivity and large specific surface area have been used in several electrocatalytic applications in addition to DSSCs.24,25,3335 Previously, there have been several attempts to develop Pt-free DSSCs. B. Fan, et al. developed CE using composites of conducting polymer poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) or PEDOT and multi-wall CNTs.18 These were about 76% as efficient as Pt electrodes. Recently Guo-Ran Li, et al. used TiN nanoparticles dispersed on multi-wall CNT surfaces as CE.19 The performance of this electrode was similar to that of Pt thin film electroplated on FTO. Jeng-Yu Lin, et al. made a CoS/CNT composite film by electrophoresis of CNTs onto a FTO substrate followed by CoS electrodeposition.20 The composite CE outperformed Pt. S. I. Cha, et al. produced unique micron sized balls from densely packed CNTs and used this to produce CEs.36 The best efficiency that they could obtain was 85% of that of a Pt electrode. In 2011, Tingli Ma, et al. published an extensive study in which they investigated nine different forms of carbon material as CEs.11 Among these, carbon dye, ordered mesoporous carbon and to some extent multi-wall CNTs showed performances comparable to Pt. Very recently, films of vertically aligned CNTs have also been used as CEs. In ref. 23, the authors showed that vertical CNTs grown directly on FTO electrodes by CVD have higher efficiency than both Pt and randomly oriented CNTs. However, the authors had taken no steps to remove the catalyst metal nanoparticles used to grow the CNTs, and these particles have been shown to contribute significantly towards the electrocatalytic activity of CNTs.37 In our case, the acid purification step after the synthesis of SWNTs dissolves all free metal and metal oxide impurities. K. S. Lee, et al. prepared nitrogen-doped vertically aligned CNT films that could be transferred to any substrate. These CEs performed much better than undoped films and comparably to Pt.22 In both ref. 22 and 23, the authors have used multi-wall CNTs. In comparison, SWNTs are thought to be better catalysts because of their larger aspect and surface-to-volume ratios.

To further investigate the catalytic activity of the electrode materials we performed electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements. Nyquist plots for the different materials are shown in Fig. 4(a). The circuit model used to fit the experimental EIS data is shown in the inset of Fig. 4(a). The charge transfer resistance (Rct) is a measure of the ease of electron exchange between the electrode and the electrolyte. It is directly proportional to the diameter of the semicircles that appear in the Nyquist plots. Constant phase element (CPE) is used to model the capacitance that develops at the electrode/electrolyte interface due to the accumulation of ions at the electrode surface. For an inhomogeneous and rough electrode like the ones used in these experiments, CPE is used to simulate the AC response instead of an ideal capacitor. The element Rs accounts for the resistance within the bulk of the electrolyte and the electrodes, the contact resistances at the various joints, etc. As can be seen from Fig. 4(a), the Nyquist plots consist of two dominant semicircles. The smaller one in the high-frequency region (close to the origin) is due to the CE and the larger one in the low-frequency domain is due to the TiO2 film at the anode.5,18,38 At even lower frequencies, a third semicircle has been reported which is due to the diffusion of ions from the bulk to the electrode.38 Since the prime concern of this study is to compare the catalytic activities of the CEs, we have avoided the diffusion region. Hence, the Warburg impedance element has also been omitted from the model. In the circuit used to mimic the EIS set up, the first block consisting of Rct1 and CPE1 represents the CE, while the second block represents the TiO2 anode. Similar circuits have been used to model DSSC systems earlier.5,38 The quantity of interest here is Rct (see Table 1). The values obtained for P-BP and Std Pt are 1.46 and 1.73 cm2, respectively. The best reported values in the literature are generally of the order of 1 cm2.1,3,5Fig. 4(b) shows the Bode plots. The phase variation of the impedance of the DSSC is plotted against the frequency of the input AC voltage. The high frequency peak corresponds to the reactions at the CE. It has been shown through simulations and experiments that the intensity of this peak drops with a decrease in Rct.39 In our case, both P-BP and Std Pt have peaks with much lower intensity than either P-SWNT or BP, with that of P-BP being the lowest. Low Rct for the I/I3 redox couple in EIS translates into higher efficiency of the DSSC. Thus the results of EIS corroborate well with the performance of DSSCs. Finally, there is scope for further optimization of the effectiveness of P-BP. For example, one can increase the defects in SWNTs by changing the plasma parameters or the composition of the gas mixture, or dope the SWNTs by introducing gases like nitrogen and diborane during the plasma treatment.

Conclusions

Substrate-less, freestanding, flexible SWNT films or BPs were used as the CE in a DSSC. It had an efficiency of 2.44%. The surface morphology of BP changed dramatically on exposure to microwave plasma formed using a mixture of Ar and H2 gases. The more-or-less smooth surface of BP is replaced by arrays of vertically aligned pillar-shaped microstructures. The catalytic property improved, resulting in a higher efficiency of 4.02%, which is comparable to that of conventional Pt thin film CEs. The Voc (0.73 V) and FF (0.70) of the P-BP-based DSSC were slightly better than that of Pt. However, the Jsc was lower at 7.83 mA cm2. EIS studies further confirmed the superior electrocatalytic properties of P-BP. Rct for the I/I3 redox couple obtained with P-BP, Pt and BP electrodes were 1.46, 1.73, and 2.11 cm2, respectively. When P-BP is redispersed and assembled into a film of randomly oriented P-SWNTs, the efficiency falls back to the level of BP, confirming the importance of the surface microstructure of P-BP and vertical alignment of the nanotubes. The properties of P-BP appear promising and there is further scope for modification and improvement. This material could be instrumental in developing Pt-free DSSCs, especially in cases where flexibility is important.
