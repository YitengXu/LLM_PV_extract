Abstract

Multiple absorbers that function in different absorption regions (near infra-red (NIR) and UV-Visible (UV-Vis)) have been widely used in solar cell applications to enhance the light-harvesting. Herein, two special co-sensitizing Models 1 and 2, which feature either saturated dye IQ21 or saturated co-sensitizer S2, have been added to a TiO2 surface to explore the effect of the altered sensitizing sequence, namely the co-sensitizing ratio of IQ21/S2 and S2/IQ21 on the electrostatic potential variation (ÎV), electron injection efficiency (Î·injâ²), and FÃ¶rster resonance energy transfer (FRET), using density functional theory and first-principle molecular dynamics simulations. The ÎV related to the open-circuit voltage (Voc) is insensitive in both Models 1 and 2. However, the absorption (Î»abs) and Î·injâ² associated with the short-circuit density (Jsc) display a significant deviation (the Î»abs for 1 is red-shifted compared to that of 2, and the Î·injâ² for 1 is improved by 56%). Meanwhile, Model 1 manifests a suppressed FRET and potentially favors co-sensitizer S2 functioning as the electron-injector and not the energy-donor. Another two possible Models 3 and 4 that feature a reduced adsorption of IQ21 and S2 relative to 1 and 2 were considered further, and the result mirrors the main trend in 1 and 2, except for the Î·injâ². Overall, it implies that sensitizing a larger absorber with NIR features to saturate it first, then introducing a smaller absorber with UV-Vis features, can potentially improve the electron injection and diminish electronâhole recombination considerably. Our results provide a comprehensive analysis of the active role of an optimized sensitizing sequence to improve the conversion efficiency.

Introduction

Since dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSCs) were proposed in 1991 by OâRegan and GrÃ¤tzel,1 this technology has attracted much interest among both scientists and engineers around the world. Dye as one of the most critical components should harvest as much light as possible, both in the spectral response range and the intensity of absorption.2 Metal organic complexes have favorable near infra-red (NIR) light absorption and slow electronâhole recombination properties,3,4 whereas their counterparts, metal-free organic dyes, have strong absorption around the UV-Visible (UV-Vis) region and a structural diversity that has given rise to increasing attention.5 However, a single absorber does not achieve panchromatic absorption across the solar spectrum unless the device absorbs all incident light.6 To improve the conversion efficiency Î· (Î· = Jsc Ã Voc Ã FF/Pin, where Jsc is the short-circuit current density, Voc is the open-circuit photovoltage, FF is the fill factor, and Pin is the power of the incident light), co-sensitizing multiple kinds of absorbers has been proposed for improving light-harvesting and diminishing electronâhole recombination.7â10 Using this strategy, the record Î· of 14% was achieved in 2015.11

Generally, devices with different co-sensitizing absorbers are achieved by step12 and synergetic13 methods. The step approach involves first sensitizing the photoanode by one dye to saturation and then introducing the co-sensitizer to fill the remaining attachment sites. By contrast, the synergetic approach is completed by sensitizing a photoanode with various kinds of dyes and co-sensitizers in an optimized ratio simultaneously.

It has been verified that the co-sensitizing strategy manifests the advantages of filling more vacancies on the photoanode surface, suppressing the aggregation of molecules, improving the light-harvesting capability, impeding the electronâhole recombination,14 and preventing the approach of oxidized species in the electrolyte to the photoanode surface.15 Furthermore, it has been found that FÃ¶rster resonance energy transfer (FRET) occurs during the light radiation in the co-sensitizing system,16â21 where dye and co-sensitizer produce an energy cascade, and the UV-Vis absorber can transfer the fluorescence energy to the NIR absorber via dipoleâdipole interactions.22,23

In previous theoretical calculations for the single dye sensitized system, the static electronic structure of the dye@substrate interface and the dynamics of electron transfer, which by time propagation of the wave function,24 survival probability,25 Tully's surface hopping and density matrix26 method, have been efficiently modeled to qualitatively/quantitatively resolve the interfacial coupling. For instance, Persson et al. investigated the static electron injection time by computing the mixing of the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) on the sensitizer with the manifold virtue orbitals on TiO2.27 Troisi et al. proposed a model which quickly screens out prominent dyes via evaluating the orbital symmetry and a quality factor Q (representing the propensity of insulating the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) on the dye from the LUMO on the semiconductor).28 Prezhdo et al. proposed the procedure of separating the nonadiabatic and adiabatic electron transfer to study the detailed picture of the photoinduced process.29 On the other hand, the energy transfer,30 charge transfer,31 absorption property,30 and interfacial energy level alignment32 in co-sensitizing systems have been well described to provide a deep comprehension from the atomic scale. De Angelis et al. proposed a computational model to evaluate the FRET rate between metalâorganic complexes,30,33 Tateyama et al. studied the adsorption energy of a dye and co-sensitizer located in varied binding positions on a TiO2 surface,34 and in our previous work, we explored the influence of an oligothiophene-functionalized co-sensitizer on the electron injection efficiency.31

In this work, we used density functional theory (DFT), time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT), and first-principle molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to characterize the effect of the co-sensitizing ratio on the electronic properties of dyes@TiO2 heterogeneous interfaces. The considered prototype co-sensitizing system consists of a DâAâÏâA architecture indoline dye IQ2135â37 (Fig. 1(a)) and DâÏâA motif tribenzylamine co-sensitizer S236â39 (Fig. 1(b)), where the former is the NIR absorber and the latter is the UV-Vis absorber; both of them are well explored and representative as the dye and co-sensitizer. The motivation or inspiration for this theoretical study is the following: the different Î· is achieved by the step method, namely, the Î· depends on the sensitizing sequence of IQ21 and S2: (a) for first sensitizing IQ21 and then sensitizing S2, the Î· approaches 10.4% (denoted as IQ21/S2, Jsc = 19.8 mA cmâ2, Voc = 731 mV, FF = 0.72); (b) as for first sensitizing S2 and then sensitizing IQ21, a decreased Î· of 8.3% is detected (denoted as S2/IQ21, Jsc = 16.0 mA cmâ2, Voc = 733 mV, FF = 0.71).40 The interesting aspect behind this phenomenon is that the sensitizing sequence can lead to different adsorption coverage between the dye and co-sensitizer, and subsequently, vary the interfacial electronic structures.

Models and computational details

Models

The co-sensitizing models were constructed by simulating the two aforementioned stepwise sensitizing sequences. Before constructing the models, the binding mode of each dye on the TiO2 surface should be determined because different modes can affect the binding energy,41 electronic coupling,31 and the tilt angle42 between the axes of the dipole moment and the surface normal of the TiO2 film. It has been observed that bidentate bridging (B) and monodentate/hydrogen-bonding (M) binding modes exist in parallel (Fig. 1(c)), and the former type is dominant.43 Meanwhile, in our previous study, mode B is more stable than mode M from the point of adsorption energy.43 Thus, we only consider mode B unless otherwise specified. Moreover, it is assumed that both IQ21 in situation (a) and S2 in situation (b) are saturated because the sensitizing time is long enough (12 hours). The details of the co-sensitizing models are as follows: for situation (a), it first accommodates the maximum quantity of IQ21 on the (TiO2)124 surface (total three molecules), and then introduces S2 on the remaining binding sites. Accordingly, the IQ21/S2-3/1 (simplified as Model 1) is constructed. Notably, there are multiple arrangements between IQ21 and S2 on the surface (see Fig. S1 in the ESIâ ), and we only consider the closest interacting one which reflects the strongest intermolecular interaction (Fig. 2(a)). As for situation (b), the S2/IQ21-3/1 (simplified as Model 2, Fig. 2(b)) has been constructed following the same method as for in Model 1.

Computational details

For calculations of heterogeneous dye + co-sensitizer@TiO2 interfaces, a (TiO2)124 cluster was obtained by appropriately âcuttingâ an anatase slab, with the majority (101) surface exposed (Fig. 1(d)). The geometry optimization and first-principle molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were carried out by the DFTB+ program package44,45 with the parameter sets mio-1-146/tiorg-0-147 for C, H, O, N, and S/Ti atoms, and the Lennard-Jones dispersion correction was taken into account for van der Waals interactions.48 For MD simulations, the 5.5 ps NVE trajectory was performed after initial 5 ps NVT equilibrium at the temperature of 298 K. For excited state calculations, the functionals CAM-B3LYP,49 MPW1K,50 and B3LYP51 as implemented in the Gaussian 09 package52 were tested with the aim of checking the effect of the chosen functional on the vertical excitation energies. In conjunction with a conductor-like polarizable continuum model C-PCM53 dichloromethane (CH2Cl2 solvent), it was found that the MPW1K/6-31+G(d) level can reproduce the experimental spectra best. Therefore, the excited states of all structures were calculated at the MPW1K/6-31+G(d) level for 100 lowest singletâsinglet excitations. For a molecule before adsorption on the TiO2 slab, the calculation was performed in the implicit dichloromethane solvent, and for a molecule after adsorption, it was performed in vacuum on the optimized structure of the adsorbed dye + co-sensitizer obtained by removing the (TiO2)124 cluster.54 To analyze the FRET process from the energy-donor to energy-acceptor, the emission spectra were calculated by assuming that the emission follows the Kasha rule.55,56 The electronic coupling between dye/co-sensitizer and (TiO2)124 substrate was analyzed by the NewnsâAnderson model27,57 using the B3LYP functional combined with 6-31G(d)/LANL2DZ basis sets for C, H, O, N, and S/Ti atoms (details can be seen in the theoretical background in the ESIâ ).

It has been observed that different co-sensitizing sequences yield a pronounced difference in Jsc (19.8 mA cmâ2 for IQ21/S2 and 16.0 mA cmâ2 for S2/IQ21). The Jsc is determined by the light-harvesting efficiency, electron injection efficiency, and electron collection efficiency. The former two items are closely associated with the properties of the absorber, whereas the latter is mainly connected with the properties of the photoanode (it is regarded as a constant in the same operation environment).

For isolated IQ21 and S2 in solution (Fig. 3(a)), the simulated maximum absorption peak Î»abs is around 553 and 522 nm, respectively. Conclusions

In summary, the effect of the sensitizing ratio at multiple absorbers@inorganic heterogeneous interfaces on the surface potential, electron injection efficiency, and FRET have been investigated to analyze the dependence of the conversion efficiency on varied sensitizing sequences. For the special Models 1 and 2, the results show that the stable adsorption of both IQ21 and S2 is observed in 1, while the apparent desorption of all absorbers is detected in 2. The electrostatic potential variation ÎV, which is close to the value of Voc, is insensitive both in 1 and 2. However, the two critical items of absorption characterization and electron injection efficiencies Î·injâ² associated to the Jsc in 1 and 2 display significant differences. In Model 1, the Î»abs is red-shifted to 590 nm compared to the isolated IQ21 and S2, while in Model 2, the Î»abs is blue-shifted pronouncedly to 530 nm, influencing the light-harvesting in the NIR region. As for Î·injâ², it is estimated to be 0.94 in Model 1 and 0.60 in Model 2, and this stems from the faster electronâhole recombination of S2 in 2. On the other hand, the MD simulated intermolecular distance rDA and orientation Îº imply that the FRET can occur in these systems, and the FRET rate kFâ² reflects that the FRET in 2 is faster than that in 1, which is evidenced by the average kFâ²(C3) in 1 (17.02 Ã 10â6 Ãâ6) that is smaller than the kFâ²(C1) in 2 (58.45 Ã 10â6 Ãâ6). The insensitive ÎV explains the subtle difference in Voc from experiment upon changing the sensitizing sequence for IQ21 and S2, and the apparent difference in Î»abs, Î·injâ², and kFâ² account for the distinguishable Jsc. Furthermore, the possible reduced ratio of IQ21 in Model 3 and S2 in Model 4 have also been explored. It mirrors the main change tendency in 1 and 2 except for the Î·injâ². Overall, the result implies that the sensitizing ratio, which reflects the sensitizing sequence for varied absorbers, plays a critical role in the conversion efficiency, and sensitizing a larger absorber with NIR features preferentially to saturation, and then introducing a smaller absorber with UV-Vis features can potentially improve Jsc and diminish the electronâhole recombination significantly.
