Abstract

Interfacial engineering represents a critical step towards passivating trap states and facilitating charge transfer across interfaces in perovskite photovoltaics, thereby resulting in substantially improved device performance. Herein, we report a robust strategy for tailoring interfacial carrier dynamics via judiciously synthesized uniform perovskite CsPbX3 (X = mixed Br/I ions) quantum dots (QDs) by capitalizing on an amphiphilic star-like diblock copolymer as a nanoreactor. The composition-tunable perovskite QDs possess impressive colloidal stability due to intimate ligation with a thin layer of polymer hairs (i.e., hairy QDs). By precisely tuning the composition of CsPbX3 QDs and deliberately positioning them between a perovskite film and hole transport layer (HTL), trap states (e.g., pinholes) on the perovskite surface and at perovskite grain boundaries can be effectively passivated, and concurrently cascade energy band alignment is achieved. As such, carrier separation and interfacial hole transport are greatly facilitated, thus leading to reduced carrier transition time, prolonged life time and improved charge collection efficiency. Consequently, the resulting perovskite solar cells deliver a progressively increased power conversion efficiency up to 19.21% (with interfacial passivation using CsPbBr0.025I2.975 QDs) over the 16.15% of the control device (i.e., absence of CsPbBr3 QD-incorporation). In principle, this perovskite QD-based interfacial engineering strategy may open up new possibilities to passivate traps at interfaces and grain boundaries via convenient compositional tuning of colloidally stable hairy perovskite QDs for a wide range of high-performance optoelectronic devices.

Introduction

Organicâ€“inorganic hybrid perovskite solar cells (PSCs)1â€“6 have garnered much attention as they offer an appealing combination of low-cost solution processing and high efficiency due largely to a set of outstanding optoelectronic properties such as flexibly tuned energy bandgap, high absorption coefficient (>3 Ã— 104 cmâˆ’1), long electronâ€“hole diffusion length (>1 Î¼m), and excellent carrier mobility.7â€“10 The certified maximum power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 25.2% for PSCs has been reached.11 Among various approaches to improve the PCE of PSCs, including compositional tailoring and morphological control of perovskite layers,10,12â€“14 and design and implementation of effective electron transporting layers (ETLs)15â€“17 and hole transporting layers (HTLs),18,19 interfacial engineering represents a compelling route to improving PCE.

The ability to create favorable energy band alignment at both ETL/perovskite and perovskite/HTL interfaces and to passivate trap states that act as recombination centers would greatly facilitate charge carrier transport.20 This remains an active area of research. For example, organicâ€“inorganic hybrid MAPbBr3âˆ’xIx quantum dots (QDs) are deposited at a perovskite/HTL interface, leading to increased hole transport.21 Moreover, the insertion of all-inorganic CsPbX3 (i.e., Î±-CsPbI3) perovskite nanoparticles at a perovskite/HTL is found to improve hole injection and electron blocking characteristics due to the optimized energy band alignment of the perovskite/Î±-CsPbI3/HTL.22 However, MAPbX3 QDs suffer from poor stability due to the volatile organic methylammonium cations.21 On the other hand, Î±-CsPbI3 is also unstable at room temperature and transforms into the orthorhombic phase due to a lower Goldschmidt tolerance factor.23,24 Clearly, despite great potential in improving the photovoltaic performance of PSCs, there is a vital need for the synthesis of perovskite QDs with a suite of long-term stabilities (e.g., water, colloidal and photo stabilities) and tunable bandgap for improving light harvesting and charge transfer.13,25

Herein, we report a viable strategy to craft uniform hairy perovskite CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with markedly improved colloidal stability and readily tunable compositions that result in concurrent cascade energy band alignment and facilitate charge transfer at the perovskite film/HTL interface, thereby leading to improved device performance. The key to hairy CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs of varied Br/I ratios lies in the use of amphiphilic star-like poly(acrylic acid)-block-polystyrene (PAA-b-PS) diblock copolymers with a well-defined molecular weight of each block as nanoreactors. The resulting PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs are positioned at the perovskite/HTL interface (i.e., MAPbI3/Spiro-OMeTAD). The incorporation of these QDs not only suppresses the trap states on the MAPbI3 film surface and grain boundaries as corroborated via a set of studies including photoluminescence (PL), time resolved photoluminescence (TRPL), and intensity-modulated photocurrent/photovoltage spectroscopy (IMPS/IMVS), but also facilitates cascade energy band alignment at the MAPbI3/Spiro-OMeTAD interface. As a result, the photogenerated holes in the MAPbI3 layer can be effectively extracted and injected into the Spiro-OMeTAD layer with the assistance of CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs. This is revealed by the enhanced charge collection efficiency (98%), improved recombination resistance (350 ohm) and reduced trap states manifested by the longer lifetime (125.93 ns), as compared to âˆ¼92%, 300 ohm and 71.74 ns, respectively, without the incorporation of the CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QD interfacial layer. Consequently, a notable increase of PCE from 16.15% for the CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QD-free device to 19.21% for the CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QD-decorated device is achieved, representing a 19% increase. This work demonstrates a viable approach for interfacial engineering of PSCs by capitalizing on stable polymer-ligated perovskite QDs to impart efficient carrier transport and extraction and thus enhanced device performance.

The synthesis is detailed in the ESI.â€  Briefly, a unimolecular star-like poly(tert-butyl acrylate)-block-polystyrene (PtBA-b-PS) diblock copolymer (upper fourth panel) is first synthesized by sequential atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP; a living polymerization technique)26 of tert-butyl acrylate (tBA; upper third panel) and styrene (St; upper fourth panel) using a 21Br-Î²-CD initiator (upper second panel) obtained from the esterification of Î²-cyclodextrin (Î²-CD; upper first panel) with 2-isobutylbromide. The subsequent hydrolysis of the PtBA block in star-like PtBA-b-PS yields amphiphilic star-like PAA-b-PS (lower fourth panel), which is employed as a nanoreactor to template the nucleation and growth of uniform CsPbBr3 QDs. The strong coordination interaction between the metal moieties of CsPbBr3 precursors (i.e., CsBr and PbBr2) and the carboxyl groups (â€“COOH) of PAA facilitates the preferential loading of precursors in the compartment occupied by inner hydrophilic PAA blocks of star-like PAA-b-PS in dimethylformamide (DMF). Upon dropping the precursor-loaded star-like PAA-b-PS DMF solution into toluene (a nonsolvent for CsPbBr3 yet a good solvent for PS hairs), PS-ligated CsPbBr3 QDs are instantaneously formed (i.e., hairy QDs; lower second panel). The partial anion exchange of Brâˆ’ with ZnI2 leads to PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs. By altering the amount of ZnI2 introduced, a series of CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with different Br/I ratios can be conveniently achieved (lower first panel). It is notable that the diameter of QDs and the length of the outer hydrophobic PS blocks intimately ligated on the QD surface can be precisely tailored by controlling the molecular weights of inner PAA blocks and outer PS blocks during the ATRP of tBA and St, respectively.

The implementation of PAA-b-PS as a template for CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x synthesis is advantageous compared with conventional ligand-assisted methods. Star-like PAA-b-PS block copolymers are capable of forming unimolecular, stable, spherical micelles in solution. The inner PAA blocks enable the coordination with the metal moieties of precursors via their carboxylic acid groups to yield CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with a uniform diameter dictated by the length of PAA blocks. In addition, the outer PS blocks possess high hydrophobicity and resistance against many environmental factors. The covalent bond between PS and PAA would lead to the formation of CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with permanently ligated PS chains on the surface, thereby avoiding the ligand dissociation issue as in the case of commonly used organic ligands. The PS chains provide a physical barrier to inhibit the diffusion of moisture and other polar solvents, thus resulting in enhanced stabilities of PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs.

To scrutinize the effects of PS-ligated perovskite QDs on the device performance of PSCs, the concentration of both CsPbBr3 and CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs was systematically altered. For the devices assembled with CsPbBr3 QDs of varied concentrations (Fig. S12 and Table S4â€ ), it is not surprising that the PCEs are reduced due to the lowered JSC and FF compared to those of the control device, which can be attributed to unfavorable energy band alignment as noted above, thus hindering hole transport. On the other hand, for CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs, the PCEs are progressively increased at low QD concentration (c â‰¤ 0.3 mol Lâˆ’1; Fig. S13 and Table S5â€ ). It is notable that at the optimal concentration of 0.3 mol Lâˆ’1, a highest PCE of 18.00% was obtained. It is notable that when the QD concentration is low (0.1 mol Lâˆ’1), they cannot completely passivate the trap states on the MAPbI3 surface. At high QD concentration (0.6 mol Lâˆ’1), they tend to aggregate, leading to decreased device performance. Similar situations were seen in CsPbBr3 QD-incorporated devices (Fig. S12â€ ). Nonetheless, the improved performance can be ascribed to the enhanced charge transition and collection efficiencies, resulting from the effective passivation of surface trap states by CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs in conjunction with their cascade energy band alignment with MAPbI3 and the HTL (Fig. 2b), as will be further discussed below. It is important to note that the insulating PS on the surface of CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs can function as an effective passivating molecule for improved performance of MAPbI3-based PSCs.36 Moreover, PS with an electron-rich benzyl ring on its repeat unit is capable of forming electrostatic interaction with cationic MA+ to immobilize the MA+ and reducing ion migration, thereby highly improving the stability of the MAPbI3 layer and device durability.37 Although the ligated PS chains are insulating, the CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QD-incorporated PSCs can still display excellent performance, which may be due to a small amount of PS38 (i.e., 21 PS chains with a molecular weight of 7.7 kg molâˆ’1 per chain) situated on the surface of a 14 nm QD, as well as reduced ion migration and trap state passivation, as noted above. All PSCs were studied at the same scan rate and there were no significant differences between the reverse-scan and forward-scan device parameters (Fig. S14â€ ), indicating less hysteresis and higher reliability of the Jâ€“V curve measurements.

To investigate the effects of PS-ligated QDs on the optical properties of the MAPbI3 film, steady state PL measurements were conducted. The pristine MAPbI3 film exhibits a PL peak at 776 nm (Fig. 3a), which can be ascribed to the bulk exciton radiative recombination emission.39 With the passivation of PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs (x = 3 for CsPbBr3 and x = 0.5 for CsPbBr0.5I2.5), both PL intensities are largely increased. Owing to the different PL peak positions of these QDs compared with those of the MAPbI3 film (Fig. 1c), the increased PL intensity cannot originate from the emission of QDs. Thus, the significant PL enhancement may be attributed to the high-quality MAPbI3 film with suppressed surface trap states and compact crystal grains as a result of the deposition of CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs. We can see from the normalized PL of the perovskite film with and without PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs that there is a slight FWHM broadening of pristine MAPbI3 compared with its QD incorporated counterpart, which may be attributed to the composition variation, interface roughness, and surface trap states of the perovskite film (Fig. S15â€ ). With perovskite QD passivation, the crystallinity of MAPbI3 is optimized, showing a decreased FWHM of steady-state PL. On the other hand, CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with the optimized concentration interfaced between MAPbI3 and Spiro-OMeTAD can facilitate hole transport, as confirmed from the corresponding PL spectra. The stronger quenching of the PL of the MAPbI3 film suggests a faster hole extraction after the incorporation of CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs, due to the passivated surface states of the MAPbI3 film after CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QD incorporation. The photo-excited holes can transfer into the Spiro-OMeTAD layer, and space-separating electrons and holes can hardly recombine, leading to stronger PL quenching of MAPbI3 with the Spiro-OMeTAD HTL. For the MAPbI3/CsPbBr3/HTL structure, CsPbBr3 can hinder hole transport from MAPbI3 to the HTL due to the unfavorable energy band alignment, and the photo generated electrons and holes can recombine radiatively and PL intensity will be increased compared to that of the Spiro/MAPbI3 structure. However for the MAPbI3/CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs/HTL sample, CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs can further facilitate hole transfer from MAPbI3 into the HTL due to more favorable cascade energy band alignment, resulting in decreased PL intensity (Fig. S16â€ ). Moreover, the TRPL decay curves of MAPbI3 films with and without QD incorporation were measured, from which the PL decay times can be determined (Fig. 3b). The PL decay curves were fitted using a biexponential decay function according to previous reports.40 The MAPbI3 film deposited with CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs exhibits a much longer lifetime (125.93 ns) than the QD-free MAPbI3 film (71.74 ns) (Table S6â€ ). For the perovskite layer without a HTL on the surface, the PL decay is attributed to a tradeoff between radiative and nonradiative recombination rather than carrier extraction by the HTL layer.20,21 The longer lifetime with CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs is indicative of slower charge recombination, suggesting suppressed nonradiative recombination centers on the surface of the perovskite film. The photogenerated holes after excitation in the MAPbI3 film can transfer to the VB of CsPbBr0.5I2.5 QDs, and effective separation of electrons and holes ultimately retards the charge recombination, thus enhancing the PL lifetime.40

Conclusion

In summary, we developed a viable and effective strategy by capitalizing on judiciously crafted hairy all-inorganic perovskite CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs with mixed halides to tailor carrier dynamics at the organicâ€“inorganic perovskite/HTL interface. The hairy CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs are highly stable as their surface is intimately ligated with hydrophobic PS chains. The positioning of these PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs at the organicâ€“inorganic perovskite/HTL interface leads to largely increased efficiency of the resulting PSCs. This may be attributed to the synergy of efficient passivation of surface trap states by PS-ligated CsPbBrxI3âˆ’x QDs and advantageous energy band alignment for hole transfer from the organicâ€“inorganic perovskite to the HTL, thereby resulting in shortened carrier transition time, long life time and enhanced charge collection efficiency. The champion CsPbBr0.025I2.975 QD-incorporated PSC manifests a PCE of 19.21% over 16.15% of the control device. The hairy perovskite QD-enabled interfacial passivation strategy may also be implemented to yield other stable perovskite-based optoelectronic devices with markedly improved performance.
