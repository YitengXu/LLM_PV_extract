Abstract

Electronic doping is applied to tailor the electrical and optoelectronic properties of semiconductors, which have been widely adopted in information and clean energy technologies, like integrated circuit fabrication and PVs. Though this concept has prevailed in conventional PVs, it has achieved limited success in the new-generation PV materials, particularly in halide perovskites, owing to their soft lattice nature and self-compensation by intrinsic defects. In this review, we summarize the evolution of the theoretical understanding and strategies of electronic doping from Si-based photovoltaics to thin-film technologies, e.g., GaAs, CdTe and Cu(In,Ga)Se2, and also cover the emerging PVs including halide perovskites and organic solar cells. We focus on the chemical approaches to electronic doping, emphasizing various chemical interactions/bonding throughout materials synthesis/modification to device fabrication/operation. Furthermore, we propose new classifications and models of electronic doping based on the physical and chemical properties of dopants, in the context of solid-state chemistry, which inspires further development of optoelectronics based on perovskites and other hybrid materials. Finally, we outline the effects of electronic doping in semiconducting materials and highlight the challenges that need to be overcome for reliable and controllable doping.

1. Introduction

Energy depletion and environmental pollution are two primary bottlenecks for global development. Solar energy is one of the promising candidates for replacing fossil fuels to address the energy crisis. The Sun provides more than enough energy to meet the whole world's energy needs. Photovoltaics directly convert solar energy into electricity, which is an important sustainable energy source. However, approximately only 1.7% of the global electricity demand has been met by electricity generated by PVs up to now. The only limitation of solar energy utilization is its higher cost for electricity generation compared to other technologies.1

In PV technology, semiconductor materials are usually used as light absorbers. Electronic doping is a process that enables the local manipulation of charge carrier density and conductivity in a semiconductor, and it is essential for semiconductor-based electronic devices, in particular solar cells (Table 1). Through electronic doping, pn junctions can be formed, which are the elementary building blocks of solar cells. Based on the p-type and n-type wafers, c-Si solar cells were produced and used for the first time in a prominent application when they were equipped on the Vanguard satellite in 1958.2 However, the cost of generating electricity from solar cells was staggering due to their high manufacturing cost and low conversion efficiency at that time, which was still as high as $100 per watt in 1971. In c-Si solar cells, the Schottky barrier, a region with high resistance for carriers, can be formed at the interface between the metal and the semiconductor.3,4 Recombination of photo-generated electrons and holes in these contact regions severely limits the PCEs of these devices. Through heavy doping, BSF was established in the metalsemiconductor contact interface, promoting the efficiency of commercial Si solar cells to 20%, thus reducing the generation cost significantly.5

Even so, Si solar cells remain expensive and still have a high EPBT.30 New-generation PV materials feature weak chemical bonding, low cost, and solution processability, which are promising for achieving PV grid parity. However, it is difficult to achieve reliable and controllable doping, especially for halide perovskite materials, limiting their device performance for commercialization.3133 The fundamental understanding and modulation of the underlying properties of these materials are still limited, which hinders the future material/device design. In addition, typical physical approaches that are successfully adopted in conventional PVs, like ion implantation, may cause severe crystal damage to materials featuring soft lattice and ionic behavior.34,35 Thus, chemical approaches have been developed and intensively investigated.36,37 However, systematic investigation is still required to reveal their doping validity and impacts on materials properties and consequent carrier behaviors.

Doping in new-generation PV materials focuses on the introduction of certain kind of impurities into the lattice for controlling crystal growth and/or tuning the device performance (efficiency and stability).3840 In this review, the electronic doping techniques applied in PV technologies are summarized. We review the electronic doping mechanisms, strategies, and impacts from Si-based photovoltaics to thin-film technologies (e.g., CdTe, CIGS), to the latest generation of PSCs and OPV materials finally. To understand the evolution of electronic doping, we make two comparisons: (1) conventional semiconductors and halide perovskites, and (2) physical and chemical approaches, in which their applications in PVs are extensively discussed. In particular, we discuss chemical approaches to electronic doping, emphasizing various chemical interactions/bonding throughout materials synthesis/modification to device fabrication/operation from a chemistry perspective. New classifications (atomic doping, molecular doping and self-doping) and quantitative models of electronic doping are proposed based on the physical and chemical properties of dopants in the context of solid-state chemistry, which hopes to inspire further developments of optoelectronics for perovskites and other hybrid materials. We hope our contribution will shed light on the understanding of electronic doping and development of effective approaches to modulating the electronic properties of materials.

2. The working principles of electronic doping

Different from a single, isolated atom with discrete energy levels, a semiconductor crystal consists of a huge number of atoms (N ) whose atomic orbitals overlap and split into a near-continuous energy band. The dopants can be incorporated into the semiconductor lattice as substitutions or interstitials, or they may be segregated at lattice defects, grain boundaries and interfaces.4143 Usually, dopants are dispersed in a low concentration without an orbital overlap, which forms a series of isolated energy levels scattered in the energy bands. The electron configuration in the semiconductor under thermodynamic equilibrium follows the FermiDirac statistics. But the introduced localized electrons from dopants do not follow the original electronic distribution, and were in temporary violation of the FermiDirac statistics.44 The dopants must have the statistical tendency to move to a new thermodynamic equilibrium that obeys the FermiDirac statistics, resulting in the electron transport between dopants and the semiconductor.

The doping type is determined by the introduced localized energy level. If an isolated full energy level appears after doping and it is higher than the original full energy level, that is, VB of the semiconductor, the electrons in this energy level are more likely to enter an empty band, that is, the CB of the semiconductor and become free electrons. This doping is called n-doping. In contrast, if an isolated vacant energy level appears after doping, as long as it is lower than the CB of the semiconductor, it is more likely to accept electrons and leave a hole in the VB. This doping is called p-doping.

When the dopant is atomic, the positions of the introduced energy levels are usually related to the number of valence electrons, so we can simply use the atomic valence to analyze the doping type.45,46 When the dopant is molecular, the positions of empty and full bands are related to the valence electrons of the molecule, the doping type is determined by the HOMO and/or LUMO energy levels of the molecules as compared to the semiconductor energy levels.47,48 In addition, some semiconductors with low defect formation energy and high defect concentration exhibit unusually observed self-doping.49,50 So far, numerous studies have explored the introduction of free charge carriers into semiconductors under equilibrium conditions by atomic doping, molecular doping, or self-doping.

3. Atomic doping

The most prevalent strategy for semiconductor electronic doping involves the introduction of charged impurities compensated by excess charge carriers.5153 Unlike molecular doping, this strategy has been widely adopted in the industry to yield highly stable n- or p-type doped semiconductors.54 Conventional electronic doping is realized by ion implantation via the bombardment of semiconductors with energetic ions (the dopants) followed by thermal annealing, which has been successfully utilized in Si, GaAs, CdTe, and oxides.5558 However, atomic doping remains a challenge in some systems in which only a small amount of excess charge carriers can be introduced from the charged defects, especially for perovskite materials.59 In most cases, charged defects are compensated by localized counter charges at the grain boundaries or surfaces.54,59

3.2 Doping strategy

The ionization energy and electron affinity energy of an isolated atom are primarily determined by the number of electrons in its outermost shell (valence electron), which determine whether the dopants give or take electrons from the semiconductor. According to the valence of doped ions, the doping strategy can be divided into heterovalent doping and isovalent doping.
3.2.1 Heterovalent doping. In heterovalent doping, heterovalent atoms are incorporated into host semiconductors, and excess free charge carriers are introduced according to the difference between the valence of dopants and the semiconductor. Heterovalent doping is the most common strategy for semiconductor electronic doping, which has been successfully applied to Si, GaAs, CdTe, CIGS and other semiconductors.

Crystalline Si is the earliest and the most widely used photovoltaic material with a diamond crystal structure, which is composed of two interpenetrating face-centered cubic primitive lattices with each atom in a tetrahedral coordination environment (Fig. 3(a)). Crystalline Si has an indirect bandgap of 1.12 eV at 300 K, leading to a high theoretical efficiency limit according to the SQ limit. As the pn homojunction is the basic structure for Si solar cells, both p-type and n-type Si semiconductors are needed. The B atom, which has a high diffusion coefficient in the Si, has been widely used in p-type wafer production, with the advantage of obtaining a spatially homogeneous doping concentration.6 And for monocrystal Si solar cells, P and Al can also be used to make n- or p-type regions, respectively, by the solid-state diffusion method.61 After decades of research on Si doping techniques, the correlation between the resistivity and dopant concentration has been thoroughly investigated and documented (Fig. 3(b)).62

GaAs is another frontrunner in solar cell technologies. With a crystal structure similar to Si, GaAs has a direct bandgap of 1.44 eV at 300 K. With higher electron mobility and larger light absorption coefficient, the GaAs based solar cell has obtained the best PCE among single-junction solar cells of 29.1%. When Si atoms replace some of the trivalent Ga atoms in the lattice, shallow donor levels (EC 0.0058 eV) and additional valence electrons are introduced.63,64 However, Si atoms can also replace parts of As atoms at high Si concentration conditions and act as acceptors.65 Interestingly, preferred substitutions in the GaAs lattice that can be altered by doping techniques have been demonstrated, as shown in Fig. 3(c).66 By HVPE, the Si doping in GaAs was close to nominal due to the near-equilibrium character of this growth method, which could incorporate Si in the Ga sites and lead to n-type doping. However, the incorporating of Si in the Ga sites can be almost completely suppressed, and the As sites are predominantly replaced by Si in the strongly non-equilibrium MBE technique for constructing p-type conductivity. Moreover, the IVA group of elements, Ge and Sn, can also be used as a heterovalent cation to prepare n-type GaAs.67 In addition, Zn was used as a p-type dopant, which can replace Ga and introduce a shallow-acceptor level (EV + 0.031 eV).68

CdTe has a direct bandgap of 1.48 eV at 300 K and a Zinc blende crystal structure similar to that of GaAs. With a more optimal bandgap and lower carrier effective masses, it has a higher theoretical PCE than Si. The CdTe champion device has an architecture with a p-type CdTe/n-type CdS heterojunction, thus its p-doping is important.

Group IA and group VA elements have been used to substitute Cd and Te, respectively, for p-type doping, such as Cu, P and As.69,70 The first-principles calculation proposes that the Cu dopants in CdTe can occupy the Cd sites and act as acceptors, or occupy the interstitial positions as donors.71,72 Under Te-rich conditions, Cu dopants can be used to increase the hole concentration due to their larger preference of substitution over interstitial positions. The resistance of CdTe layers decreased rapidly at first and then increased slowly with the increase of doped Cu atom concentration, leading to the same evolution of efficiency and minority carrier lifetime of CdTe (Fig. 3(e)(g)).73 The formation of compensating donor-type defects may be responsible for the increase in resistivity. At moderate doping concentration, the efficiency and carrier lifetime of CdTe solar cells can be optimized. In addition, P and As dopants can form p-type doping when coordinated tetrahedrally.72,74,75 However, defects of PTe and AsTe could transform into deep donors, which limits the doping efficiency.

Chloride treatment, typically with CdCl2, has been widely used to form n-type CdTe.76 Cl ions may be concentrated at the grain boundaries and form ClTe defects, which can offer free electrons and become positively charged.77 After Cl doping, the quantum efficiency of CdTe quantum dots dramatically enhanced from 10% to 90% and the carrier lifetime increased from 1.2 ns to 2.3 ns. Moreover, MgCl2 could also be used to replace CdCl2 for low-cost and environment friendly processing.78

CuInSe2 has a bandgap of 1.01 eV at 300 K, which can be tuned by the substitution of In to Ga and/or Se to S to construct CIGS. Alkali metals, such as Li, Na, K, Rb, and Cs, are also commonly used as the post-deposition treatment dopants after CIGS growth.79 DFT calculations suggested that alkali atoms tend to occupy the In sites and these substitutional defects have a lower formation energy and act as acceptors (Fig. 3(d)). Besides, alkali metals could increase the intrinsic defect formation energy and passivate the CIGS layer, which decrease the deep-level defect density and increase the net carrier concentration.80,81 In brief, local pn junctions can be formed by electronic doping to facilitate charge separation.

Halide perovskites have a tunable bandgap and can be adjusted through chemical substitution. The structure of halide perovskites is a framework of corner-sharing lead iodide octahedra (PbI3) with a cation, such as Cs, MA and/or FA, at the center of each cuboctahedral cage (Fig. 5(a)). Theoretically, heterovalent heteroatoms can be inserted into the A, B, or X site of the perovskite lattice to tailor its carrier concentration. However, the orbitals of A-site cations tend to fall much deeper within the bands, which has limited effect on carrier concentration.82 B and X site ions majorly contribute to the CBM and VBM in perovskites, so it is easier to tune the carrier concentration and EF by the B and/or X site ion doping (Fig. 4).

Replacing Pb with monovalent or trivalent cations is the commonly used doping method to adjust carrier concentration in halide perovskite semiconductors. Generally, substituting Pb with monovalent cations could increase hole concentration and achieve p-type doping. The effect of monovalent cation halide additives such as CuBr, CuI, NaI, and AgI was explored in MAPbI3 perovskite films.83 These results showed that a better PCE was achieved in NaI- and CuBr-based films, but the JSC of the AgI-based films decreased. KPFM measurement identified that the work function of additive-based perovskites significantly shifted (about 0.1 V) toward the work function of Au, which was responsible for efficiency improvement. In contrast, it is also reported that Ag incorporation resulted in an enhancement of the PCE from 16.0% to 18.4% in MAPbI3 based devices, and from 11.2% to 15.4% in MAPbI3xClx based devices.28 The EF of the Ag-doped perovskite shifted to the VBM, indicative of increased hole concentration. Interestingly, Ag doping can realize not only p-type doping, but also n-type doping. Zhang et al. found that an n-type perovskite film was obtained by adding AgI to the perovskite precursor solution, which was attributed to the crystallization control with aligned orientation of MA cations.84 Therefore, electron mobility was improved from 7.9 103 cm2 V1 s1 (pristine MAPbI3) to 1.5 101 cm2 V1 s1 after AgI doping, which enhanced the PCE from 17.59% to 20.02%.

The copper(I) complexes can be incorporated into the perovskite film to modulate carrier concentration. Incorporating Cu(thiourea)I into perovskites was found to form a pn bulk heterojunction, which increased the depletion width from 126 to 265 nm, accelerating hole transportation.85 Besides, it was proved that Na could form substitutional defects in the Pb-site of the perovskite lattice. A 0.5% Na doping significantly improved the electronic conductivity of perovskite, from 107 1 cm1 (undoped MAPbI3) to 106 1 cm1 (Na-doped MAPbI3).86 In addition, DFT calculations indicated that In and Tl were promising candidates to form p-type doping in perovskites materials (Fig. 5(b)).27

In addition to monovalent cations, trivalent cations were also used for the heterovalent doping of perovskites. The first-principles calculations implied that the substituting of Pb with Sb and Bi could form an n-type doping (Fig. 5(c)).27 Due to a similar ionic radius to that of Pb (1.18 ), Bi (1.02 ) was the mostly-used heterovalent doping ion, which can be easily doped into lead halide perovskites. As shown in Fig. 5(d), the free carrier concentration measured by the Hall effect increased from 109 cm3 (undoped MAPbBr3 single crystals) to 10111012 cm3 after Bi doping. And the conductivity measured by the four point probe method also increased by 4 orders of magnitude, from 108 1 cm1 for the undoped crystal to 104 1 cm1 for the Bi-doped crystal.26 The conductivity of Bi-doped perovskite polycrystalline film increased from 3.02 106 to 9.76 106 1 cm1.87 Based on Sb doping, the electron density in the CB of MAPbI3 was increased and the quasi-Fermi energy level was elevated.88 Furthermore, Al has a higher valence and smaller ionic radius than Pb, which can substantially reduce the microstrain of the MAPbI3 film and enhance the device performance.37

Other methods like X-site doping were also applied to manipulate the carrier concentration. Oxygen can act as an acceptor dopant, in which the O atoms replaced the I atoms or accommodated interstitially.89,90 Experimental results showed that the concentration of holes and iodine vacancies increased with oxygen pressure under illumination. Surprisingly, the concentration of majority carriers increased at low oxygen pressure, but decreased when the pressure is high, which may be related to the increase of the defect state concentration.88

Besides the heterovalent ion substitution, the electronic doping can be also modulated by interstitial impurities. In Si solar cells, the monocrystalline silicon surfaces have abundant Si dangling bonds, which introduce a half-occupied deep energy level state in the bandgap.91,92 Hydrogen atoms can chemically passivate the Si dangling bonds, and act as acceptors in n-type Si or as donors in p-type Si.6,93 Surface H could further migrate into the bulk region as interstitial H, which can neutralize shallow dopants, so it can act as a compensating donor or acceptor in p-type Si or n-type Si, respectively.94

With a small ionic radius, Li+ cations may locate on interstitial lattice sites and act as n-dopants in halide perovskite materials. DFT calculations indicate that interstitial occupancy is energetically favorable for Li+ compared to A-site substitutions. After Li-doping, the measured EF in MAPbI3 shifted up by 440 meV, reducing the non-radiative process by trap filling.95 Besides, Li+ can do n-doping for CsPbBr3 electrochemically, which improves the electron injection efficiency.96

3.3 Doping efficiency

The effective carrier concentration produced by impurities is affected by many factors. Among them, three main factors may further determine/limit the doping efficiency:115 (1) the desired dopant may have a low solubility. (2) The desired dopant has good solubility, but the defect transition energy level may be too deep, thus, the defect cannot be ionized at normal operating temperatures. (3) The desired dopant has good solubility and proper ionizability, but as the EF shifts due to the increase of carrier density, which may also lead to the formation of oppositely charged native defects or defect complexes of the dopant, thus limiting the change of the EF.

Taking these three main factors into account, the effective carrier concentration n generated by doping can be expressed as follows:

n = ndopant IP (1 Self-compensation),(7)
where ndopant is the dopant concentration, which is limited by solubility, IP is the donor or acceptor ionization probabilities, and Self-compensation is the self-compensation probability.

4. Self-doping

Different from the conventional PVs materials with strong chemical bonding, halide perovskite materials have weak ionic and secondary bonding, which makes it easy to modulate the electronic doping.40,126,127 However, the soft nature and low defect formation energy of this new-generation PV materials may lead to the severe compensation effects from the intrinsic defects, leading to the challenge of efficiently and reliably tuning the carrier density.51,128 In addition, typical physical doping approaches, which have been widely adopted in conventional PVs, like ion implantation, may cause severe crystal damage in halide perovskites.129 Therefore, chemical doping strategies should be developed according to the material features. New deposition approaches, which can tune the materials properties by the easy chemical methods, are required, such as controlling the stoichiometric ratio in precursor solution for manipulating the intrinsic defect properties (self-doping, Section 4) and introducing dopants (in precursor solution and/or surface modification) to collect/donate electron from/to the semiconductor. The atmosphere also determines the atomic chemical potential, which plays an important role in the self-doping of perovskites (Table 6). However, the initial states cannot be fully recovered due to the formation of the hydrate phase on the perovskite surface, and prevent the oxygen diffusion into the film. Heating the sample, instead, could restore the EF completely, in which the waterfilm barrier on the surface was fully evaporated, resulting in the intercalation of oxygen into the bulk.156

5 Molecular doping

New-generation semiconductor materials have been developed with low-cost, and solution-processability, and the corresponding devices exhibit unique mechanical, optoelectronic, and chemical properties. Different from the classical electronic doping strategy (atomic doping), the solution process enables a new electronic doping strategy, the molecular doping. The molecular dopants can be designed and customized through heteroatom substitution (such as F4TCNQ and F6TCNQ) and structural modifications.24,207,208 Furthermore, the distribution, diffusion, and concentration of the dopants in the semiconductor precursor solution and films can be precisely controlled to modulate the electronic properties of the material.207,209

5.1 Doping mechanisms

The mechanism of molecular doping is illustrated in Fig. 18 by comparing the energy level differences between perovskites and dopants. The dopants possess unoccupied and/or occupied molecular orbitals for electrons. As shown in Fig. 18, if the LUMO level of the molecular dopant (e.g. F4TCNQ) is near the VB of the semiconductor (perovskite), electrons will transfer from the VB of semiconductors to the empty electron acceptor, leaving holes in the semiconductor. As a result, the doped molecules become negatively charged and build up a spacecharge layer with semiconductors. This process in turn raises the LUMO and HOMO (or electrochemical potential) of the molecule till thermodynamic equilibrium. If the HOMO of the molecular dopant (e.g. CoCp2) is higher than the CB of semiconductors, electrons will transfer from the dopant to semiconductors, resulting in n-type doping.

5.2 Doping strategies

5.2.1 Bulk molecular doping. Benefiting from the chemical processability and hybrid features of halide perovskite materials, molecular doping has been successfully applied. Molecular additives have various chemical interactions/bonding with the solvent/perovskite, so they can adjust the crystallinity, regulate the orientation and stress, and form doping gradients. More importantly, the properties of molecular dopants can be easily tuned through heteroatom substitution and molecular design, which provide endless approach to manipulating the electronic doping.

Nitrile groups, a stronger electron withdrawing group compared with fluorine, was used to further increase the EA. The F2HCNQ, in which two fluorines are substituted by nitrile groups, showed a higher EA, which was 0.4 eV higher than that of F4TCNQ.216,222 In addition, with six fluorine groups, the EA of F6TCNNQ, was further elevated to 5.60 eV.219,223,224

DDQ was also widely used as a p-dopant. Though its EA was lower than that of TCNQ,24 the DDQ had high solubility with good processability, leading to easy and efficient doping.225227 In contrast, HAT-CN had poor solution processability due to its larger and rigid planar structure.228 However, comparing with the above micromolecule dopants, HAT-CN was relatively stable without noticeable diffusion. The success of DDQ and HAT-CN utilization has encouraged the development of higher EA molecular dopants. With an EA of 5.87 eV, CN6-CP has become the strongest p-type dopant in the recent literature for OPV.227,229,230 Although this neutral molecule has been reported to be insoluble and highly reactive, they appeared to be stable and soluble when mixed with conductive polymers, such as the PDPP(6-DO)2TT, in CH2Cl2. And the conductivity of PDPP(6-DO)2TT was promoted from 106 1 cm1 to 3070 1 cm1 after doping by CN6-CP.230

The organo-metal dopant, Mo(tfd)3, with a high EA of 5.37 eV and air stability, has also been investigated.231233 However, due to the poor solubility in solutions, it can only be processed by using vacuum deposition.24 To improve its solubility that enabled the solution process and prevented the unwanted diffusion, structural modifications were adopted. Substituting the nitrile functional groups with either methyl or octyl esters was applied to increase the solubility. For, example, Mo(tfd-CO2Me)3 has an improved solubility, which is 20 times higher than that of Mo(tfd)3.234,235 Similarly, a series of F4TCNQ analogues, with nitrile functional groups substituted by either methyl or octyl esters, showed an increased solubility of more than 100 times higher and without change in EA.236 According to these molecular design principles, a high EA soluble molecular dopant, TMCN3-CP, was proposed and synthesized.208 And groups like CN3 and COCF3 were also applied to increase the solubility of dopants.208,209 The F4-R-TCNQ was synthesized to adjust its diffusion properties by replacing the nitrile with the tailorable ester group, which significantly increases the dopants solubility and reduces its diffusion rate in the OPV layer.24 We expect that tremendous efforts need to be devoted for customizing the molecular dopants such as Mo(tfd-R)3, F3-R-TCNQ, and F4-RTCNQ to enable a controllable solution processability.

To minimize the diffusion of the dopants in the composite film, three-dimensional (3D) dopants were proposed, such as the C60 molecules. Replacing the hydrogen atoms with the electron-adsorbing fluorine atoms in C60 molecules increases the EA, tuning the C60 molecules to a p-dopant and derivatives such as C60F36 and C60F48 were synthesized.237,238 With higher EA than that of F4TCNQ, they showed excellent stability against thermal diffusion in experiments. However, due to their poor solubility, they can only be processed under vacuum deposition. Meanwhile, 3D structure dopants occupy a large volume in OPV layers, limiting the doping energy levels. And their large size could prevent them from being inserted into the crystalline lattice, except for the amorphous materials with a loosen structure.239,240

In the early stage, the n-type dopants used for organic semiconductors were atomic dopants.241 However, the atomic dopants are easy to diffuse, limiting the stability and commercial applications of OPV devices. In principle, n-type dopants with a low HOMO level (2.83.6 eV) could achieve a high doping efficiency for OPV.207 The widely used n-type molecular dopants and their energy levels are shown in Fig. 20 and Table 10. Organometallic dopants, with strong electron-donating properties, such as mesitylene pentamethylcyclopentadienyl ruthenium dimer (RuCp*mes)2, dimers of rhodocene, (RhCp2)2, and pentamethylcyclopentadienyl cyclopentadienyl rhodium dimer, (RhCp*Cp)2, are widely used as n-type molecular dopants.209,242,243 Besides, metallic sandwich compounds, such as CoCp2 and CoCp*2, were also used as n-type dopants, but they are unstable when exposed to air or water.244246 The metal sandwich dimers, which cleaved upon doping, can resolve the air-instability issues. And the dimer can extended the storage lifetime of the dopant, providing a strategy for stabilizing the low IE dopants.247249 It needs to be reminded that with the increase of EA, strong P dopants are difficult to be stabilized.230

Bulk molecular doping was also investigated in perovskite solar cells. 5.2.2 Surface transfer doping. Surface transfer doping relies on the electron exchange between a semiconductor and dopants situated at its surface, which is a meritorious doping strategy for nondestructive doping of soft semiconductors.254 For the conventional doping process as ion implantation, the energetic ions may destroy the lattice of soft semiconductors.129 However, as a low cost method, the surface transfer doping avoids the utilization of energetic ions, which can effectively dope the semiconductors without lattice damage.

QDs have a large surface-to-volume ratio, which can be utilized to control the passivation and net doping by surface ligands.255257 Surface transfer doping in QDSC was first observed by Shim and Guyot-Sionnest in CdSe QDs.258 Through sodium biphenyl surface treatment, the CdSe QDs exhibited n-type doping due to the electron transfer from sodium biphenyl to the LUMO of colloidal semiconductor. It opened the door to a successful doping method for QDSC without atomic doping in the QD bulk. Moreover, other colloidal semiconductor nanocrystals, such as CdS and ZnO, can also be modulated to n-type by sodium biphenyl.

Organic PV materials are usually bonded via weak intermolecular interactions, such as hydrogen-bonding, van der Waals interaction force, or electrostatic interactions, making it easier to introduce defects through the traditional doping process.129 Surface transfer doping has received broad interest as a gentle method for organic semiconductor doping. SAMs can be used to modify the surface of an organic semiconductor. Organosilanes with a higher fluorine content have a stronger electron-withdrawing ability, which induce a higher conductivity and more effective p-type doping. Owing to solution processability, the surface transfer doping is also applied to tune the carrier concentration of PSCs via chemical approaches.

Transition metal oxides, such as MoO3, was a widely used p-dopant due to their deep-lying electronic energy states.267,268 With 64 of the n-type MoO3 (EA of about 6.7 eV) deposited upon MAPbI3, the energy levels of MoO3 shifted toward a lower binding energy by 0.30 eV, while the energy levels of MAPbI3 shifted toward a higher binding energy by 0.25 eV, indicating the electron transfer from MAPbI3 to MoO3.269 Mo4+ and Mo5+ oxidation states, with 3d5/2 binding energies at 230.1 eV and 232.0 eV respectively, appear in the XPS core level spectrum, revealing that a chemical reaction takes place at the perovskite/MoO3 interface.270,271 Furthermore, the DFT calculations showed that MoI covalent bonds and NHO hydrogen bonds were formed, leading to the increase of work function by 0.52 eV.272 Interestingly, the MAPbIBr2/MoO3 contact was revealed to form a holeelectron recombination region through in situ UPS and XPS measurements (Fig. 23(a)), which provided a new pathway for hole transfer.270 In addition to MoO3, other oxides, such as WO3 and V2O5, were expected to modulate the electronic characteristics of perovskites.

Small molecules such as the F16CuPc and HAT-CN are commonly used dopants in organic materials, while they can be deposited onto perovskites by physisorption or chemisorption.270 F16CuPc has a larger EA (4.7 eV) than MAPbIBr2, which can downshift the vacuum level of MAPbIBr2 by 0.7 eV (Fig. 23(b)). The p-type doping enhanced the built-in electric field and increased the energy barrier to electron diffusion, improving the PCE from 15.3% to 18.1%.276

HZ-TIPS and its homologue, OZ-TIPS, formed an interfacial dipole at the interface due to the van der Waals interaction between molecules and the first few layers of the CsPbBr3 surface. The HZ-TIPS/CsPbBr3 interfacial dipole made the energy band bend downward. Future research needs to establish the quantitative relationship between doping concentration and carrier concentration, which may require improving chemical imaging and energetic mapping techniques. Also, new models, such as delocalized electronic states, are needed to understand how the dopant's orientation and shape affect the magnitude and delocalization of the disorder in the semiconductor. In addition, controllable solution processing techniques need to be developed, which can modulate the distribution, diffusion, and concentration of dopants within the semiconductor.

In conclusion, when dopants are atoms, the valence, radius, and electronegativity of the atoms determine bond formation and carrier generation. Generally, a negatively charged dopant leads to p-type doping, and the opposite is true for positively charged dopants. When dopants are molecules, the HOMO and LUMO levels of dopants determine the direction of electron transfer between semiconductors and dopants. Usually, a high EA molecule can accept electrons from the VB of a semiconductor for p-type doping and a low IE molecule gives electrons to the CB of a semiconductor for n-type doping. In addition, some semiconductors have low defect formation energy and high intrinsic defect density, showing the unusual defect physics of self-doping. If the intrinsic defect is effectively and negatively charged (cation vacancy or anion interstitial), the hole concentration increases, which leads to p-type doping. If the intrinsic defect is positively charged (anion vacancy or cation interstitial), it may show an intrinsic n-type semiconductor. It is expected to reveal new chemistry and physics in halide perovskites with defect profiles that are distinguished with conventional PV materials.

6. Effects of electronic doping

The essence of electronic doping is the redistribution of electrons in the CB and VB, which leads to macroscopic differences in conductivity, charge transport, carrier recombination, device performance, etc. The desired electrical characteristics of a semiconductor can be obtained through electronic doping, which further determines the performance of photovoltaic devices and related optoelectronic devices.

6.1 Conductivity

Under an electric field, charge carriers (electron and/or hole) can drift and contribute to the current, and their concentration determines the internal resistance of conventional PVs. Owing to the high intrinsic defect density and ionic behavior, facile migration of intrinsic defects of new-generation PV materials could also contribute to the current, especially for the perovskite material. According to different conducting mechanisms, conductors can be divided into ionic conductors, electronic conductors, and mixed conductors. Electronic conductors, such as metals, graphite, and metal compounds are conductive initiated from the movement of free electrons and no chemical changes occur during the conduction process. Similarly, the electrons in the CB of semiconductors flow along a direction driven by the electric field, which is also the electronic conduction. Ionic conductors, such as electrolyte solutions or molten electrolytes, rely on the oriented movement of ions (ion migration) to conduct electricity, which involve chemical changes. In ionic solids, the ionic conduction occurs mainly through the migration of ions through lattice defects. Previous studies proved that new-generation PV materials are mixed conductors, which have both ion and electron transport properties.289,290

Normally, DFT calculation is a helpful tool to evaluate the activation energy and understand the anomalous ion transport; however, the experimentally measured activation energy is often lower than the value predicted by DFT calculation.90,280 These differences are not surprising because the theoretical method only calculates the ionic conductance in a bulk crystal (only considers bulk point defects), while the crystal surface and grain boundaries play as major channels for ion migration.328,329 In addition, there are many other possible ion migration paths, such as strain induced extended defects and soften lattice caused by light illumination.310,330 New theoretical models (common assumptions in DFT calculations) should be proposed to predict ionic conductivity by considering these possible paths as more as possible.

However, the doping concentration also need to be well controlled. As the doping concentration increased, the scattering and recombination centers gradually increased, thereby reducing the lifetime of the carriers and facilitating the nonradiative recombination, so an appropriate doping level is crucial. In silicon PVs, a moderate p-type doping concentration is about 1015 cm3 (in intrinsic crystalline silicon, there are approximately 5 1022 atoms per cm3) and the moderate n-type doping concentration is about 1017 cm3.61,374,375 Doping concentration above about 1018 cm3 is considered degenerate at room temperature.

FF is determined by many factors, of which the most important ones are the RS and RSH resistance.376,377 At a high current density, the FF is determined by the RS (Fig. 27(e)). The Rs is influenced by both ohmic elements, such as the perovskite layer, the conductive substrate, transport layers, and the metal back contact, as well as the interface resistance, such as Schottky barrier. The electronic conductivity of bulk and surface can be improved to reduce RS, thus to optimize the carrier transport and to get a high FF. Under high internal electric field conditions, RSH has a pronounced effect on the shape of JV curves (Fig. 27(f)). It arises from the current leakage through the device, such as the carrier recombination in bulk and interface. Electronic doping can minimize the recombination as well as reduce the spacecharge buildup to increase the RSH value. Moreover, carrier lifetime determines the charge extraction and the steepness of the JV curve under low-internal field conditions or near VOC. Appropriate doping concentration can increase the carrier lifetime and thus increase FF.

During the development of Si technology, increasing the current density contributes greatly to the efficiency improvement at the preliminary stage. Afterwards, the efficiency is improved through passivated emitter and rear cells, which accounts for enhanced FF and VOC.293,378 And the development of the efficiency of PSCs appeared in the same trend (Table 12). As JSC gradually approaches the SQ limit of JSC (JSQ), the improvement of VOC and FF is the key to further enhancing the PCE of PSCs. For GaAs, the ratios of the achieved performance parameters to the limit values of the SQ limit are 88% for PCE/PCESQ, 99% for VOC/VSQ, 91% for JSC/JSQ and 97% for FF/FFSQ.376,379 However, PSCs exhibit low FF and VOC ratios compared to GaAs. The FF of most reported PSCs is around 0.70.8 and even certified devices in Solar Cell Efficiency Tables with PCEs of 22.7% and 23.3% show low FF values less than 0.8 (FF/FFSQ < 90%).380,381 Therefore, the optimization of performance parameters VOC and FF will be the key factors for further PCE improvements.

Furthermore, through a robust pn homojunction, a simplified PSC in the absence of both ETL and HTL was constructed, yielding a PCE of over 8% without using any interfacial modification layers. 6.3.2 Device stability. The device stability of solar cells depends on two aspects: intrinsic material stability and extrinsic stability against environment. The encapsulation of solar cells can prevent moisture and oxygen invasion effectively and some external encapsulation materials have already been applied to solar cells for commericilization.395 However, the intrinsic material stability, which is affected by complex factors such as the phase and structure stability, chemical stability, and ion migration, is difficult to improve significantly, severely limiting the further enhancement of long-term stability and leading to the reduction of EPBT of solar cells, especially for PSCs.40,396

Defect density of PV materials has huge influence on the intrinsic device stability. Material degradation is generally initialized from defect sites, which are more reactive with water and oxygen.397399 In addition, defects are channels for water and oxygen capture and rapid diffusion, which further accelerates the degradation of bulk materials.395 For covalent-bond based PV materials like silicon, structural defects are usually electrically neutral. Therefore, eliminating structural defects mainly involves a chemical reaction with primary chemical bonding. For Si solar cells, structural defects are usually eliminated by forming chemically stable oxides and nitrides, i.e., SiO2, SiNx, or AlOx, through thermal oxidization and/or chemical vapor deposition.127 Benefiting from the ionic nature of new-generation PV materials, especially for perovskites, the structural defects are generally charged defects, which enable multifarious elimination methods including redox shuttle and chemical bond enhancement.280

DFT calculation showed that perovskites were chemical unstable, whose enthalpy of formation (with respect to MAI and PbI2) can be negligible.206 Intrinsic degradation would occur in perovskite materials upon ageing treatments such as heat, oxygen, light and moisture. On one hand, Pb2+ can be easily reduced to metallic Pb0 upon heating and/or illumination, which was probed in the halide perovskite film by XPS.400402 Besides, the nonstoichiometric composition and/or the asynchronous degradation of halide perovskites could leave undercoordinated Pb2+ ions on the film surface and grain boundaries, which had higher reduction or oxidation (redox) activity.280 On the other hand, I was prone to be oxidized to I0, which triggered the chain reactions and accelerated the degradation of perovskite films.403 Sacrificial agents such as HPA, EtOH and BQ were used to eliminate either Pb0 or I0 defects.402,404,405 However, these additives diminished soon after taking effects. To solve this problem, the redox shuttle concept was proposed to constantly eliminate Pb0 and I0 simultaneously in PSCs over their life span by using the ion pair Eu3+Eu2+ on the basis of the following chemical reactions (Fig. 29(a)):406

Although the reaction is thermodynamically favored with a Gibbs formation energy (G) of 173.6 kJ mol1, metallic Pb and I2 powder only led to limited formation of PbI2. Interestingly, this reaction can be easily triggered by applying ion pairs with appropriate redox potentials. Eu3+ could easily oxidize the Pb0 defects and Eu2+ could reduce the I0 defects, which was the best candidate of ion pairs. The resultant device (unencapsulated) retained 91% of the original PCE after 500 h of MPP tracking. In addition, the devices maintained 89% and 92% of the original PCE after heating at 85 C or under 1 sun continuous illumination for 1500 h, respectively.

Moreover, chemical bond enhancement, incorporating the dopants with strong interactions with the perovskite crystal lattice, can immobilize ions to reduce intrinsic defects and increase chemical stability. Fluoride ions with extremely high electronegativity were introduced into the perovskite film, which formed strong ionic bonds with Pb2+ and strong hydrogen bonds (NHF) with organic cations (MA/FA), leading to the reduction of both the organic cation and halide anion vacancies (Fig. 29(b)).407 The devices maintained 90% of the original PCE after 1000 h of MPP tracking under continuous one sun illumination. In addition, the devices retained 90% and 95% of the original PCE after heating at 85 C or under 1 sun continuous illumination for 1000 h, respectively. Recently, fluorine-containing molecules and organic salts, such as FEAI and amphiphilic TFMBAI, had demonstrated great improvement on the stability of PSCs.304,408,409

Under electric field, illumination, and thermal treatment, the charged defects migrate, which could lead to the JV hysteresis, phase segregation, band bending or even material degradation.410414 When an electric field was applied to the perovskites, the negatively defects, i.e., VMA and Ii, migrated along the opposite direction of the electric field. At the same time, the positively defects, i.e., VI and MAi, migrated in the direction of the electric field, which caused the charge accumulation in HTL/perovskite/CTL interface and unintentional doping effect.413,415 According to the electric neutrality principle, the negative charged defects migrated and accumulated in the perovskite surface which introduced equivalent numbers of holes to chemically p-dope the interface. Similarly, the migration of negative defects in the perovskite surface led to a chemically n-doped perovskite surface. The chemical doping at the interface led to the generation of an additional electric field in the perovskite absorber that compensated the built-in electric field. It hindered the photogenerated carrier transport, which was harmful for device efficiency and stability.318,319 In addition, the migrating ions have the potential to react with the transport layer or metal electrode. It was proved that the oxidized spiro-OMeTAD+ can chemically react with the mobile I to form an electroneutral spiro-MeOTADI complex under light illumination or thermal stress (85 C aging).416418 Besides, the chemical reaction between the mobile I and metal electrodes, such as silver, was observed in the experiment.414 After aging the MAPbI3-based device, silver electrodes would change from white to yellow due to the formation of AgI, which originated from the chemical reaction between the migrated MAI and Ag. The chemical reactions are also observed in the perovskite/TiO2 interface, which may form TiIPb bonds between TiO2 and MAPbI3.416 To solve these ion migration issues, methods have been developed including alkali metal ion doping, introducing functionalized additives, adjusting perovskite components, and forming a passivation layer on the perovskite surface, which is described in detail in Section 6.1.2.

The band mismatch and defect states at the perovskite/transport interfaces would cause carrier accumulation and undesired nonradiative recombination, which damaged the long-term stability of the device, especially for MPP tracking.419421 The Lewis acidbase theory is widely adopted for chemical passivation and doping in perovskite surface. Lewis base-based deep traps, such as the undercoordinated I and antisite PbI3 can be eliminated by the Lewis acid, such as C60 and its derivatives (PCBM, ICBA, etc.) with the capability to accept a lone pair of electrons.422424 Similarly, Lewis acid-based traps, such as the undercoordinated Pb2+ and Pb clusters, can be eliminated by the Lewis base, such as the molecules containing functional groups like nitrogen (N) or sulphur (S) with a pair of nonbonding electrons.425428

The charged nature of the defects in the perovskite surface enables the neutralization through ionic bonding, such as conversion of surface imperfections to wide bandgap materials. PbI2 is a wide bandgap material with a band gap of 0.8 eV greater than that of MAPbI3. After annealing at 150 C for 60 min, enriched PbI2 formed at the perovskite/TiO2 interface, leading to n-doping and suppressing the recombination at the surface.429 Besides, the formation of a layered 2D perovskite at the 3D perovskite surface can create type-I band alignment, which is another effective approach to improve the stability. Through in situ reaction between n-hexyl trimethyl ammonium bromide and the perovskite, a WBH perovskite was established on the NBH perovskite layer (Fig. 29(c)).380 The modified devices without any encapsulation kept nearly 80% of its original efficiency after 1008 h under 85% RH at room temperature. The operational stability of modified devices significantly increased, where the modified devices maintained over 95% of their initial PCE for 1370 h and the control devices quickly degraded within 100 h. Other chemicals, such as butylammonium (BA+), PEA+, or AVAI were reported to form different layered perovskites at the interface.430432

Due to the weak secondary bonding between the passivation molecules and the perovskite surface, inorganic anions, which can form strong chemical bonds with the perovskite, are investigated. Similar to the Si passivation strategies, the chemically stable lead oxysalt layer can be established on the perovskite surface, which can provide sufficient protection for extreme conditions such as thermal ageing and humidity aging (Fig. 29(d)).127 Considering the operation conditions of PVs, lead salts that are insoluble in water are ideal candidates. PbSO4 and Pb3(PO4)2, whose solubility is merely 0.0443 and 0.00014 g L1, respectively, at 20 C, were formed through the surface reaction by using (C8H17NH3)2SO4 and (C8H17NH3)3PO4 salts. Devices with the lead oxysalt layers (encapsulation) retained 96.8% of their original efficiency after 1200 MPP tracking under 1 sun continuous illumination at 65 C, whereas the control device showed severe degradation within 474 h. Recently, this strategy was applied to inverted PSCs, where the PbPyA2 and TMS were introduced to form strong leadsulfur bonds (PbS) that not only are chemically stable, with an equilibrium constant (Ksp) of 1.0 1028, but also upshifted the EF and offered a BSF for electron extraction.285 In addition to the above lead compounds, other lead compound with low solubility (Ksp), such as PbCO3 (1.46 1013), PbO (1.26 1015), Pb5(PO4)3Cl (3.98 1085), Pb5(PO4)3Br (7.94 1079) and Pb5(PO4)3F (2.51 1072), await to be developed and investigated.

7. Conclusions and outlook

Electronic doping is crucial to modulate the electrical properties of photovoltaic materials. As the material composition and/or molecular structure extend beyond conventional materials systems, like the new-generation PV materials, the classical understanding of electronic doping based on the crystalline silicon cannot be directly applied. In some cases, these arguments are even controversial which require further investigation. New theories and techniques need to be developed to investigate electronic doping in new PV materials.

In this review, we have first introduced conventional electronic doping (atomic doping using physical approaches mainly in crystalline silicon). We emphasize that physicochemical properties of atoms such as the valence electron, electronegativity, radius, and the redox properties determine the doping type. Subsequently, we turned to chemical approaches, which are developed to tackle with the new-generation of PV materials featuring weak chemical bonding and a soft lattice. New electronic doping strategies (molecular doping and self-doping) have been summarized with the focus on chemical approaches. We concluded that by adjusting the HOMO or LUMO levels of dopant molecules (molecular doping) and the intrinsic defects in polycrystalline semiconductor films (self-doping), controllable electronic doping can be achieved in new-generation PV materials.

In addition, the effects of electronic doping are discussed and summarized. Electronic doping can bring slight or steep shifts in electronic band structure across semiconductor bulks and interfaces. It thus modulates carrier concentration, conductivity, and thus carrier dynamics, which further improves device performance. To assess the effects of electronic doping quantitatively for new-generation solar cells, new models based on the physicochemical properties of individual dopants and the formation and transformation of intrinsic defects are proposed in this review, which can help to better control the carrier concentration of semiconductors and guide the design of devices.

Finally, we have proposed a few parameters of essence that play an important role in different doping strategies, including the chemical properties of materials, defect density, processing conditions, etc.

Reviewing the development of electronic doping, the technologies in conventional semiconductors may shed light on the design and optimization of new solar cells. Constructing pn homojunction and gradient heterojunction is effective to optimize carrier transport and improve the efficiency in silicon solar cells, and they are expected to be feasible for the emerging PVs. Indeed, pn homojunctions and gradient heterojunction constructed by the solution process have been reported in OPV and PSC.24,152,391,392,394 However, facile ion migration and defect interdiffusion in these PV materials remain a problem for the stability and effectiveness of the homojunction and heterojunction structures, which need further research.51

Electronic doping at the surface is an effective strategy which can be accomplished by facile in situ chemical/wet processing. It could modify the electrodesemiconductor interfaces for efficient carrier extraction, which is potentially feasible for perovskite solar cells without ETL or HTL.280,366 However, for new-generation PV materials, like halide perovskites, it is likely to react with metal electrodes, thus the investigation of inert conducting materials to retard their corrosion is highly demanded.127,285,414

Furthermore, self-compensation is a common phenomenon in new semiconductors with a soft lattice due to their low lattice formation energy, which limits the concentration of electronic carriers.25,51,150 Non-equilibrium growth, such as quenching and rapid solvent evaporation, which has been widely used in CdTe, may be applicable to overcome the limitations of self-compensation.12

New electronic models and assumptions for DFT calculations need to be developed to interpret experiment results, which are important to guide the processing for effective electronic doping in new-generation PVs. For atomic dopants, self-compensating effects and defect complexes are worth careful consideration. Also, the location of impurity atoms in the crystal lattice affects the doping significantly, and precise location control is desired. For molecular dopants, the dopant solubility, configuration, and size can affect charge delocalization and disorder. To further control electronic doping, the development of new electronic models and calculation methods are required to compute charge and non-localized systems more efficiently. At the same time, time-dependent defect model instead of static defect model must be developed to simulate influences caused by dynamic effects such as aggregation, diffusion, and drift.

The new-generation solar cells, especially for PSCs, have shown rapid development and have the potential to realize PV grid parity. However, the emerging solar cells face significant challenges. In particular, the fundamental understanding is still quite limited, which is far behind the development in device efficiency. On one hand, novel materials and doping methods need to be developed to facilitate the further commercialization of new-generation solar cells. On the other hand, theories applicable for new materials are urgently required to design and conduct effective electronic doping. It is important that the theoretical and experimental investigators, and academic and industrial researchers work closely together to have a comprehensive consideration of electronic doping, and develop more realistic and a reliable process for new-generation or next-generation solar cells.
