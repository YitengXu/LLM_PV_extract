Abstract

We review computational contributions to the understanding of the physical principles underlying interface phenomena related to dye adsorption at the surface of titanium oxide, within the scope of dye sensitized solar cell applications. We focus our attention on the theoretical studies aimed at computationally representing dye-sensitized solar cells under realistic conditions, e.g. by including the solvent and the electrolyte in interactions with dye-sensitized TiO2 through protocols accounting for thermal nuclear motion. The impact of dye clustering and self-aggregation into monolayers on the optical and transport properties of dye-sensitized TiO2 is addressed. Computational studies of surface protonation, charge- and energy-transfer, or the influence of the presence of additive agents or co-sensitizers are also reviewed in relation to the electronic, spectroscopic, kinetic and diffusion properties of self-assembled dye monolayers sensitizing TiO2.

I. Introduction

The progressive depletion of fossil fuels along with the dramatic increase in environmental pollution and noxious gas emissions has stimulated an urgent need for renewable energy technologies. Since solar radiation strikes the earth's surface at a continuous rate of 1.2 × 105 TW, largely overcoming our current worldwide power demand, converting solar energy into electricity1 or storing it in chemical fuels (producing oxygen and a reduced fuel from water)2 represents one of the most challenging scientific and technological tasks. In this perspective, nanostructured dye-sensitized solar cells (DSCs),3–7 which have been recently successfully transferred to actual industrial applications, represent promising alternatives, with a comparable cost and, in principle, a lower environmental impact, to traditional silicon-based photovoltaic devices.

Two electrodes and a redox mediator are the three main components of a DSC. Solar light absorption occurs at the photo-electrode interface, where a dye sensitizer is chemically bound to a wide band gap mesoporous semiconductor substrate (usually TiO2). The photo-excited electrons are then injected into the unoccupied semiconductor states (CB), and the charge hole which is created on the dye upon excited state charge injection into the semiconductor CB is transferred to the redox mediator, which is regenerated by a catalyst at the counter-electrode, closing the circuit (see Fig. 1).

(1) The dye (D) catches a photon and enters its electronic excited state (D*), from which (2) it can inject an electron into the semiconductor conduction band. This electron will flow (3) through the circuit and reduce (4) the electrolyte, which will react (5) with the oxidized dye D+ to regenerate it into D.

Up to now, the highest-performance DSC devices employing the liquid I−/I3− electrolyte8 are those sensitized with Ru(II)-based dyes, such as N3, N719 or C106,9,10 although a number of promising alternatives are being developed to tackle ruthenium scarcity, including for instance iron-based solar cells.11,12 Metal-free dyes13–15 have also been developed, with overall efficiencies approaching 10%.16,17 In conjunction with ferrocene or cobalt-based electrolytes, organic dyes have been shown to clearly outperform Ru(II) dyes.13,18–22 Moreover, the recent replacement of the dye sensitizer with hybrid organic–inorganic perovskite materials has represented a revolution in the DSC technology, with unprecedented certified power conversion efficiency exceeding 20%.23–25 From a different perspective, the use of a mixture of dyes having complementary absorption spectra represents an appealing strategy to obtain panchromatic TiO2 sensitization,26–41 and, as a matter of fact, the new DSC efficiency record, exceeding 14%, has been reported for co-photosensitized solar cells exploiting two metal-free sensitizers bearing an alkoxysilyl and a carboxylic anchoring group, respectively.42

General “golden rules” to appropriately design and combine isolated DSC components (i.e. dyes,43–47 semiconductors48,49 and electrolytes50,51) are derived from simple considerations on the material's structural, redox and optical properties.7,52 A potential highly-efficient dye should, in fact, possess broad and intense absorption, associated with a long-lived charge-separated excited state, strongly coupled to the oxide CB states, and ground and excited state oxidation potentials properly aligned to the redox potential of the mediator and the semiconductor CB respectively, favouring extremely fast electron injection and regeneration kinetics.53 The overall cell efficiency is, however, strongly affected by the rate/efficiency of the charge separation processes taking place at the multiple metal oxide/dye/electrolyte heterointerfaces. Thus, besides optimization of standalone components (i.e. dyes, semiconductors and redox shuttles), optimizing the energy level matching, the morphology and the optical properties of the dye/TiO2 interface is crucial to boost the device efficiency.54–62 A recent work also reports on the use of dye monolayers as a hole transporting material, by exploiting intermolecular hole migration.63,64 As a matter of fact, under working conditions, dye sensitizers are usually closely packed on the semiconductor surface.65–69 The molecular structure of the dye,70–75 surface protonation,61 as well as the general dyeing conditions (e.g. solvent, dipping time, use of co-adsorbents, etc.) can, however, significantly modify the surface coverage of the dye.

Prototypical organic dyes, bearing a cyanoacrylic anchoring group and characterized by an adsorption configuration with the dye almost perpendicular to the substrate surface,76–79 usually present high surface coverage (∼2 molecules per nm2).65,66 For D–π–A organic dyes anchored by means of a rhodanine-3-acetic acid anchoring group, on the other hand, lower surface densities have been reported (0.3–0.6 molecules per nm2).67 The lower surface coverage is basically related to the different anchoring configuration adopted by these dyes,77,80,81 lying bent with an angle of about 45° with respect to the TiO2 surface. This adsorption mode, which is typical of indoline dyes, is characterized by strong π-stacking interactions, which are manifested by fast hole transfer among surface adsorbed dyes.82 Ruthenium dyes usually show surface coverages in the range of 0.3–0.8 molecules per nm2,68,83 although a value of 1.8 molecules per nm2 has been reported upon formation of dye multilayers.83 Since the macroscopic DSC efficiency is directly influenced by the molecular and supramolecular characteristics of the dye layer, extensive experimental and computational efforts have been focused, in the last few years, on the conformational, topological, optical and charge transfer properties of dye-sensitized TiO2 surfaces.57,65,76,84–95 The commonly accepted picture, mainly grounded on a Langmuir-type adsorption behavior,96,97 is that dye molecules organize themselves on the TiO2 surface into highly ordered self-assembled monolayers (SAM),98–100 although the existence of dye multilayers83 and inhomogeneous films101 has been reported. The complexity of the dye/metal oxide interface morphology has been, however, recently reported by a number of joint experimental and computational investigations aimed at characterizing the self-assembly properties of both organic and inorganic sensitizers on TiO2 surfaces,85,88 the existence of multi-conformational binding modes84,86,89 and competitive adsorption.90 By using high-resolution scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM) and spectroscopy (STS), atomic force microscopy (AFM), electronic structure and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, these studies provided atomic-level insights into the heterogeneous adsorption modes, molecular arrangement, topography and interfacial energy level alignment of dye-sensitized TiO2. This fruitful interaction between theory and experiment as well as the large number of studies published in the last few years demonstrate that modern first principles computational methodologies, such as those based on density functional theory (DFT) and its time dependent extension (TDDFT), provide the theoretical/computational framework to describe most of the desired properties of the individual dye/semiconductor/electrolyte systems and of their relevant interfaces.

This review is organized as follows: Section II is devoted to the discussion of the different dye/electrolyte and electrolyte/dye-sensitized TiO2 interactions. It especially reviews two types of redox shuttles: iodine and iodine-free (cobalt complexes) mediators, combined with inorganic sensitizers containing ruthenium or with all-organic dyes carrying coumarin, carbazole or ethylenedioxythiophene moieties. Section III addresses the influence of dye self-aggregation (dimer clustering) on the whole optical properties of the dye-semiconductor interface. The computational treatment of dynamical effects and self-aggregation of indoline dyes at the surface of TiO2 nanoclusters has then been addressed, showing how indoline monolayers can be adsorbed onto anatase. The optical and charge-separation properties are subsequently reported in Section IV, where the aim is to account for numerous physical phenomena allowing one to computationally approach realistic cell conditions. In particular, photoabsorption and charge-injection properties were modelled, incorporating a co-adsorbing agent in contact with the dye, chemically anchored at the surface of anatase nanoclusters. The impact of surface protonation is then reported, in relation to the energy level shift and the electronic coupling of the dye–TiO2 cluster. The effect of incorporating solvent monolayers at the vicinity of the dye-nanocluster heterojunction is also reported, before energetic and electron/hole transport phenomena are finally studied within a dye monolayer in Section V, through FRET (Förster Resonance Energy Transfer), analyzed for co-sensitized interfaces and for dyadic donor–acceptor dyes.

II. Interaction between redox mediators and dye-sensitized TiO2

Amongst the various factors influencing the global efficiency of a solar cell, the appropriate energy level alignment of the diverse components (the dye, the electrolyte, and the semiconductor) is often considered as a truly determining factor, which must be duly taken into account when designing new devices. In this regard, the appropriate combination of an (in)organic dye with a given redox couple is of seminal importance for the preparation of viable devices, and the various restrictions inherent to the use of the most widespread mediator (I−/I3−, see below) in conjunction with organic dye sensitizers have already been extensively studied. Alternative organic redox couples were also introduced to overcome the limitations of the classical iodide/triiodide couple, but they suffer from the similarity to the I−/I3− couple as they also involve a two-electron redox process.102–105

In spite of these considerations, at present, the most exploited redox shuttle is the iodide/triiodide redox couple which, despite its corrosive character and some diffusion limitations, is still preferred since it leads to more stable and efficient cells due to extremely slow triiodide/TiO2 recombination kinetics and a rapid regeneration process of the oxidized dye. Unfortunately, while the combination of this mediator couple with inorganic ruthenium-based dyes is often regarded as very promising, the above mentioned regeneration agent has already been shown to inflate the occurrence of unwanted interactions with metal-free dyes, which are unfavourable to photocurrent generation, hence decreasing the overall cell efficiency and lifetime.

It is established that the recombination process occurring between oxidized dyes and injected electrons is not only due to the action of the mediator on the dye or the semiconductor themselves, and that this recombination phenomenon is not the only factor leading to a global decrease of efficiency. However, the highly undesired interactions between the redox couple and the dyes in their oxidized state are known to be responsible for the limited exploitation of certain sensitizers. Indeed, while it is commonly accepted that the adsorbed dye monolayer acts as an insulator by preventing direct interactions between the semiconductor and the recombination agent, it is also established that there exist certain atoms or dye fragments that favour stable interactions between the dye itself and the mediator, leading to a strong increase in the mediator concentration in the vicinity of the semiconductor surface, hence promoting the aforementioned unwanted recombination processes.

II.1 Interaction between metal-free sensitizers and iodide-based mediators

As far as the interaction between organic chromophores and iodine-based redox couples is concerned, multiple studies have shown that there might exist a stabilizing interaction between I2 and the ethylenedioxythiophene (EDOT) moiety of metal-free dyes, in which sulphur and/or oxygen atoms can act as a binding site for iodine. Indeed, it has been shown that using a bis-EDOT rather than a single EDOT fragment in the organic dye induces a substantial enhancement of the sensitizer's tendency to form charge-transfer complexes with I2.

This has firstly been assessed by means of quantum mechanical calculations106 on two sensitizers/I2 adducts, named MP124–I2 (single EDOT, the left part of Fig. 2) and MP-I-50–I2 (bis-EDOT, the right part of Fig. 2) complexes, for which the equilibrium structures have been computed by DFT/B3LYP/6-31G(d) in the gas phase. Interaction energy between the dyes and iodine has further been deduced from single-point calculations on the equilibrium geometries of the complexes at the second-order Möller–Plesset (MP2) level.


Fig. 2 Sketch of the two EDOT-containing compounds investigated in the electrolyte–dye interaction survey.106

To check the reliability of the employed computational protocol, preliminary calculations were carried out where the structures of EDOT–I2 and thiophene–I2 complexes were optimized using MP2 instead of DFT, prior to interaction energy evaluation. The DFT/MP2 strategy was then applied to MP-I-50 for which three binding sites were identified: the direct oxygen-I2 (OI) and two oxygen-I2-sulfur sites (OIS-1 and OIS-2). The difference between OIS-1 and OIS-2 can be assigned to the proximity of the –CN moiety close to OIS-2, and it was identified that the OI interaction mode is the one displaying the highest interaction energy amplitude (8.1 kcal mol−1) with a binding distance of 2.93 Å. Note that OIS-1 adduct formation has also been shown to be energetically feasible, though the complexes are only stabilized by weak I–O and I–S interactions. In light of these conclusions, speculations have been made, related to the relationship between EDOT–I2 complex formation and charge-recombination kinetics of MP-I-50 and two other EDOT-containing dyes experimentally exhibiting similar behaviour.

Following the ability of this strategy to appropriately retrieve crucial information regarding the dye-redox shuttle interactions, the DFT/MP2 protocol has been applied in a second contribution107 to two coumarin-containing dyes and a carbazole-containing sensitizer, interacting with I2 and I3− accompanied (or not) by the lithium cation. The sketch of the selected dyes, named NKX2587, NKX2697 and MK3, is reproduced in Fig. 3, where we see that the difference between the first two dyes is the number of thiophene subunits (one for NKX2587 and three for NKX2697) separating the coumarin and the cyanoacrylic acid moieties, while MK3, for which the coumarin-donor part has been replaced to include a carbazole, contains three thiophene units in its bridge.


Fig. 3 Sketch of coumarin-containing NKX2587 and NKX2697 dyes, and the carbazole-containing MK3 sensitizer.

By exploiting this selection of chromophores, it was possible to separately assess the influence of the presence of coumarin carbonylic oxygen/carbazole nitrogen binding sites for iodine, and the spatial extension of the bridge, more precisely the number and relative position of sulphur binding site(s). A larger 6-311G(d,p) basis set was employed for both geometry optimization and single-point calculations. Since some dispersion interaction might take place upon complexation, single-point calculations of the binding energies were also carried out using B3LYP and dispersion-corrected B3LYP-D3, M06 exchange–correlation functional, and MP2 levels of theory. To assess the influence of the solvent medium, calculations were done in the gas phase and in acetonitrile solvent (using the conductor-like polarizable continuum model – C-PCM) when the B3LYP/6-311G(d,p) and MP2/6-311G(d,p) levels of theory are considered.

Various complexation sites were identified. As far as NKX2587 was concerned, iodine was shown to interact selectively with the carbonylic oxygen of the coumarin fragment (O–I2), with the sulfur atom of the thiophene (S1–I2) and with the cyano group of the cyanoacrylic acid anchoring function (CN–I2). Additionally, I3− was shown to interact with the nitrogen of the donor's fused rings (dye–I3−). Regarding the directionality of these interactions, one sees107 that while the complexation of iodine with S1 and CN is collinear with a distance of ca. 3.41 Å and 2.93 Å, respectively, in the case of the dye–I3− interaction, the nitrogen–iodine axis is orthogonal to the triiodide's molecular axis, with a characteristic distance between the fused ring's nitrogen and the triiodide centre of mass being quite substantial (4.98 Å). The angle between iodine and the coumarin's carbonyl group in NKX2587 is 123° with a distance of approximately 2.84 Å.

When the number of thiophene units is increased to three (namely S1, S2 and S3, with S3 being the closest to the anchoring function and S1 being the farthest) in NKX2697, we see that when going closer to the CN group (from S1 to S3) the bonding distance between iodine and sulphur increases (from 3.38 Å for S1 to 3.48 Å and 4.23 Å for S2 and S3 respectively), denoting an increasing weakness of the sulfur–iodine bond due to the growing proximity of the cyano group. The same trend is observed for MK3.

A more general conclusion related to the iodine–dye interaction strength for the two coumarin-containing sensitizers can be deduced by observing the binding energies, which reveal that the preferred binding site for the two dyes is the carbonyl group of the coumarin itself. The cyano's nitrogen follows and finally comes the sulfur of the thiophene vicinal to the coumarin (S1). Such conclusion is in perfect agreement with the previous statement related to the EDOT–[O]–iodine and EDOT–[S]–iodine binding energies (−6.9 and −5.1 kcal mol−1 respectively) as well as the thiophene–[S]–iodine interaction energy (−4.5 kcal mol−1), computed at the DFT/MP2 level of theory.106 When comparing DFT and MP2 binding energies, we see in Table 1 of ref. 107 that while B3LYP slightly underestimates the iodine–dye interaction with respect to MP2, corrected-DFT (B3LYP-D3 and M06 xc-functionals) on the other hand overestimates these values. If one now considers the influence of solvation on the binding energies, the general trend reported in ref. 107 for NKX2587, NKX2697 and MK3 is that embedding the molecular systems into a polar medium such as acetonitrile decreases the iodine–dye interaction magnitude.

Besides, the interaction between the preferential binding sites of the sensitizers and the lithium cation was investigated, together with the dye–Li+–I3− adduct formation. Indeed, it was previously reported108 that the electron-rich moieties of a coumarin-based dye can attract the lithium cation and bind the triiodide. For simulating this phenomenon, two situations were considered, where (i) the triiodide interacts directly with the lithium bound to the coumarin's carbonyl group and (ii) the triiodide interacts with the nitrogen of the dye's donor fused rings. Results show107 that the lithium cation lies at about 1.90 Å from the carbonyl oxygen, and that it has no significant role to play in triiodide complexation with the dye, especially when I−3 is not interacting directly with the cation, which is the most stable solution. Indeed, the (ii) adduct has been shown to be more stable than the (i) adduct by 2.3 and 3.6 kcal mol−1 when the interaction energy was computed from single-point B3LYP and MP2 calculations, respectively, and both interaction distances and energies were shown not to be substantially affected by the presence of Li+ in the vicinity of pyran.

The anchoring dynamics of the representative NKX2587 adsorbed on the TiO2 cluster was investigated by Car–Parrinello Molecular Dynamics (CPMD) simulations over 3.2 ps. The CPMD results show that, due to the rigidity of the whole NKX2587 structure, the latter can be considered as mostly remaining orthogonal to the surface through the dynamics, without experiencing any striking geometrical distortion. Further, optimization of (I2)-NKX2587@(TiO2)38 (see Fig. 4) showed that only slight variations in the coumarin–[CO]–I2 bond angle (∼150°) and distance (2.84 Å) with respect to the isolated or solvated adducts occur (a bond angle of 123° and a bond distance of 2.85 Å). As the average carbonyl oxygen–titanium oxide surface distance during the NKX2587@(TiO2)82 dynamics and the optimized distance for NKX2587@(TiO2)38 were close (11.9 and 11.0 Å), it was therefore concluded that a reliable estimation of the iodine–semiconductor distance can be made from the optimized dye@(TiO2)38 structures by evaluating the distance between the binding sites and the surface (see Fig. 4).

This approach also allowed us to comment the relative importance of the TiO2–dye–iodine and TiO2–dye–lithium cation interactions, and more especially the influence of the electrolyte or ion complexation on the recombination phenomenon. Indeed, the binding site distance to the surface was related to the experimental cell lifetime with high iodine concentrations.109 According to the optimized structures of the dye@nanocluster systems, indeed, the coumarin's carbonyl binding site of NKX2587 is closer (11 Å) to the surface with respect to that of NKX2697 (17 Å), in agreement with its shorter lifetime.

II.2 Cobalt-based redox shuttle and Ru(II)-dye sensitized TiO2

If one is now interested in an inorganic redox shuttle, interacting with a transition metal complex as a sensitizer, there is a recent mixed computational/experimental survey aimed at assessing the efficiency of Ru-sensitizer/Co-redox couples as well as organic/iodine, organic/inorganic and inorganic/iodine couples. In terms of lifetime, it was shown experimentally that the inorganic sensitizers have an increased lifetime when they are in contact with the iodine electrolyte rather than with the cobalt redox shuttle, while the organic sensitizers investigated (D21L6 and D25L6) have no striking preference regarding the nature of the regenerating agent.

The ruthenium-based sensitizers investigated in ref. 110 are the well-known N719 and Z907 dyes (in N719 two carboxylic acid groups were replaced by alkyl chains), while the cobalt complex is the tris-bipyridine [Co(Bpy)3]2+/3+ with different coupling patterns based on its high-spin/low-spin states. The aim of the related investigation was to unveil the regeneration mechanism of oxidized Ru dyes N719 and Z907 by the d7Co(II) electrolyte in order to explain the loss of efficiency caused by replacing the iodine electrolyte by a cobalt complex. In its reduced state, this d7 complex can be in high-spin (quartet) or low-spin (doublet) states, though it was reported111 that at room temperature, the quartet form of [Co(bpy)3]2+ is predominant. However, the regeneration rate of the ruthenium complex from its oxidized to its reduced state by a covalently-bound cobalt complex was shown to be higher when the latter is in its low-spin state, due to decreased intramolecular reorganization with respect to its high-spin counterpart. When considering more realistic cell conditions, where the sensitizer is chemically grafted at the surface of titanium oxide, and the cobalt redox shuttle is not covalently bound to the Ru-complex but free to diffuse through the medium and to interact with the sensitizer's ligands, Car–Parrinello molecular dynamics simulations were used to investigate the nature of the dye-sensitized interface and redox mediator interactions. To this end, N719 (carrying one proton for the sake of global charge neutrality) adsorbed onto a (TiO2)128 nanocluster was analyzed in the presence of an oxidized tris-bipyridine cobalt(III) complex during 9 ps CPMD simulation. This simulation gave some novel insights into the dye regeneration process in general, and allowed the rationalization of the mechanism of Ru-sensitizer regeneration by the cobalt complex.

The starting point of the simulation is a N719(1H)3− dye anchored to the anatase slab through three carboxylic groups69,112–114 and the [Co(bpy)3]3+ complex at about 15 Å from the sensitizer. After local optimization and thermalization to 350 K for 2 ps, the CPMD simulation first showed a temporary decrease of the cobalt–ruthenium distance from 15 to 8 Å and of the cobalt–TiO2 surface distance from ca. 22.5 Å to 16 Å during the first 3 ps, attributed to the negative charge carried by the carboxylate group, attracting the positively-charged cobalt complex. Over time, we observe a net decrease of the distance between the cobalt complex and the TiO2 surface, this distance being lower than the Ru–TiO2 distance around the end of the simulation. Three snapshots were taken from the CPMD simulation, at 3.2, 7.2 and 8.6 ps, respectively, in order to evaluate the strength of the electrolyte–sensitizer interaction. For this purpose, the titanium oxide slab was removed from the structures before optimization was performed using the DFT B3LYP/LANL2DZ level of theory, with acetonitrile effects retrieved from the C-PCM model. Binding energies were found to be lying in the 0.46–0.71 eV range for the three optimized geometries, with the highest binding strength found for the positively-charged mediator interacting with three carboxylate groups. Regeneration kinetics were also studied using a Marcus charge-transfer model117 for the complexes of N719(1H)3−, N719(2H)2− and Z907(0H)−2 with [Co(bpy)3]2+. Reaction profiles were constructed to highlight that the low-spin state of the cobalt complex leads to much lower activation barriers than its high spin counterpart for the three adducts; this behaviour was assigned to lower reorganization energies.

Kinetics aspects were by other means demonstrated to play an important role in the design of viable devices. This perspective was more extensively reported118 again for iodine-free redox shuttles, with special attention devoted to the comparison of iodine-free metallorganic complexes and metal-free redox shuttles, considering what is known about the recombination/regeneration kinetics of nickel, cobalt, or iron-based electrolytes, which displayed remarkable charge-transfer kinetics. Recent findings actually paved the way for the design of new redox couples leading to higher photovoltage, combined with a decreased recombination rate which, unlike most of the sulphur redox couples, remains quite substantial for this type of metal-based electrolyte. Finally, a recent survey57 on a metal-free sensitizer coupled with a cobalt-based redox couple unveiled how an enhanced dye coverage at the electrode, controlled by the dye bath with tuneable concentration, can provoke a dramatic increase of the overall cell performance, which was explained by the increased electron lifetime induced by a high TiO2 passivation.

III. Dye aggregation: dynamical and optical properties

Intermolecular interactions among adsorbed dye sensitizers may directly affect the charge generation at the dye/TiO2 interface (a higher dye loading absorbs more photons) and, as previously discussed, may also have indirect beneficial effects on the photovoltage by preventing the recombination reactions between TiO2-injected electrons and oxidized species in the electrolyte. The formation of a homogeneous (partial) dye layer was recently shown to be critical for DSC performances.119 More often, however, strong dye/dye interactions on the semiconductor surface also have the drawback of introducing undesired effects which may lower the DSC efficiency. Among these we mention filtering effects by non-TiO2-adsorbed dye molecules, possibly found as a dye overlayer, which would absorb light but not produce photocurrent. Also, strong dye aggregation is known to induce intermolecular excited state quenching phenomena, reducing the DSC photocurrent.120–122 In some cases, however, controlled aggregation has been proven to enhance the photocurrent generation as a result of the larger light-absorption window of the aggregates.60,121,123–125 Appropriate molecular engineering and the use of anti-aggregation co-adsorbents (such as chenodeoxycholic acid, CDCA) can prevent to some extent the dye-aggregation on the nanocrystalline-TiO2 surface, improving the overall photovoltaic performance.124,126–130

To the best of our knowledge, the first computational study on the aggregation of organic dyes on realistic TiO2 nanoparticle models was put forward by some of us in 2009.80 The developed approach, based on a combination of MP2, DFT and TDDFT calculations, fully accounted for the experimental evidence of strong aggregation of the indoline D102 dye when adsorbed on the TiO2 surface and also identified, in the case of dimeric aggregate models, the most stable mutual arrangements. Excited state calculations carried out on the preferred dimeric structures were also able to almost quantitatively reproduce the measured red-shifts. In the framework of a static picture (T = 0 K), however, all the dyes adopt the most stable configuration (bidentate or monodentate)78 on an ideal semiconductor substrate. It should be kept in mind, however, that this is just a special case hardly realized from a statistical point of view and that, as we shall illustrate later, a realistic description of the dye/semiconductor interface should properly consider the effect of thermal fluctuations.81,86,131–135

III.1 Self-aggregation: dimer clustering

We just mentioned self-aggregation as a possible source of cell efficiency lowering. It might take place when the solar cell implies the chemical anchoring of organic π-conjugated systems at the surface of a semiconductor. The intermolecular interaction, mostly of dispersive nature, such as π-stacking, is favoured by the structural properties of the monomers. Indeed, the planarity of some π-conjugated cores enhances the possibility of stabilization of the dimer form of the system, due to the communication between the π-structure of the fragments forming the multi-chromophore cluster. This communication also favors the intermolecular charge-transfer, which constitutes a deactivation pathway, i.e. a possible factor for the decrease of the photocurrent generation probability due to the limitation of the possibility of charge-injection into the semiconductor conduction band. For tackling such problems, the synthesis of novel organic sensitizers often implies the grafting of alkyl groups, which are supposed to prevent the dimerization process.

A recent computational study on organic chromophore aggregation reports the influence of self-aggregation on the structural, energetic and electronic properties of four sensitizers.136 This investigation has been placed in the framework of the Kasha's molecular exciton model137 which characterizes the splitting of excited states occurring upon sensitizer dimerization. This simple model is exploited for characterizing the optical properties of the four above-mentioned dyes, with segregation being made between the two dyes bearing alkyl groups and the two remaining dyes. In particular, the absorption spectra and topological properties of the excited states of monomer/dimer dyes were compared.

III.1.3 Structural modifications of the π-conjugated moieties. While the role of dimerization in the alteration of the optical features of the dye is known, the influence of grafting an alkyl group to the π-structure is less understood. Indeed, this structural modification of the dye is supposed to prevent the interaction between the organic conjugated skeletons of multiple monomers, but theoretical calculations of dimer stabilization energies showed that the chemical grafting of hexyl groups to simple organic compounds leads to a forced overall planarity of the two molecular sub-systems with hexyl groups pointing outward from the cluster, favouring the dispersive intermolecular interactions (π-stacking) and intermolecular charge-transfer, which was quantitatively assessed through a natural population analysis. In contrast, two dyes with no hexyl group but bearing t-butyl fragments will exhibit a lower stabilization energy amplitude. One should thus be aware of the influence of dimerization on the photo-physics of a dye, and should perform a critical analysis of the possibility of efficiency reduction due to structural deformation induced by adding specific alkyl moieties to certain dyes. This important feature should therefore be duly taken into account when designing novel metal-free molecular systems, targeted for highly-efficient solar-based devices, as a favoured communication between the π-structures of molecular sub-systems may lead to an intermolecular charge-transfer, quenching the electron injection of the dye in the conduction band of the semiconductor.

IV. Optical properties and charge separation in realistic cell environments

The DSC is an intimate complex system, involving electron transfer processes at multiple interfaces, thus in real cases the interdependencies among the various components and the efficiency of the various desired electron transfer and charge transport processes against the recombination losses dictate the overall device performance.

While the redox potential of the mediator is basically constant under operation conditions due to the high concentration of redox species,145,146 the quasi-Fermi level (EF,n) of the semiconductor depends “statically” on the semiconductor CB energy and “dynamically” on the charge density accumulated in the semiconductor.147 Clearly, the open circuit voltage has only an indirect dependence on the nature of the sensitizer layer, which can either affect the rate of recombination reactions77,107 as discussed above or change the energetics of the semiconductor CB states.148 On the other hand, the photocurrent, Jsc, is directly proportional to the dye's injection efficiency, ϕINJ, defined in terms of the ratio between the injection rate kINJ and the possible radiative (kR) and nonradiative (kNR) deactivation processes for the excited states of the surface adsorbed dye. Various spectroscopic studies have reported ultrafast injection kinetics on sub-picosecond time scales,149 orders of magnitude faster than the typical time scales of the competing deactivation processes. Similar pH effects have been reported also for sensitized nanocrystalline TiO2 films,166–170 where surface protonation has been effectively employed to positively shift the conduction band edge thus improving Jsc.156,170–174 Analogously, electrolyte additives, such as lithium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide (Li-TFSI), 1-ethyl-3-methylmidazolium-TFSI (EMIM-TFSI), tetra-n-butylammonium (TBA+) or 4-t-butylpyridine (TBP), are also widely used to modulate photocurrent and photovoltage.175–183 It has been demonstrated that Li ions in proximity (even adsorbed) of the TiO2 surface induce a positive shift in the CB edge,174 reducing the open circuit voltage of the cell but, as discussed by Durrant and co-workers,156 possibly increasing the density of states available for electron injection from the dye's excited state. The effect of surface protonation, as well as that of other potential determining ions, on the measured Jsc is actually more complicated than a mere shift in the semiconductor CB, since the dye's excited states, in particular its nature, its energy and the coupling with the TiO2 CB states can also be significantly influenced.59,141,184,185 In the following we separately discuss the modelling of the effect of ionic additives (Section IV.1)59 and adsorbed protons (Sections IV.2 and IV.3)158 on the optical and charge generation properties of the dye-sensitized TiO2 interface; the effects of the solvent molecules at the interface and those interacting with the dye are analysed in Section IV.4.

IV.1 Modelling the dye/additive@TiO2 heterointerface: light absorption and charge injection

Abrusci et al. investigated the effect of lithium cations on the charge generation and device efficiency in P3HT/D10271-based solid state DSCs,184 achieving an order of magnitude increase in the photocurrent when the Li-TFSI salt (Fig. 13, top a) is used with the expected drop in the open circuit photovoltage. The sizeable increment in the photocurrent was essentially ascribed to the increased charge-transfer nature of the dye's excited state, which shifts at lower energies (from 2.61 to 2.42 eV), resulting in a higher coupling with the TiO2 CB states and in an accelerated electron injection. The use of the ionic liquid EMIM-TFSI (Fig. 13, top b) was also shown to provide a similar effect on the excited state, with comparable red-shift (0.25 eV) and increased photocurrent but with a negligible drop in the Voc. Similar spectral shifts were also reported by Zhu et al.,141 who observed a significant broadening of the principal absorption band going from solution to TiO2 film, suggestive of dye aggregation phenomena,71,80 associated with a 0.15 eV red-shift of the absorption onset; addition of Li-TFSI also contributes to the red-shift, further lowering the absorption onset by 0.12 eV but leaving unchanged the absorption maximum.

While for the conjugated JK2 the LUMO stabilization is ca. 0.33 eV, comparable to the ca. 0.40 eV shift of the CB edge, we observe a sizeably smaller stabilization of the D102 LUMO, amounting to about 0.1 eV. This is clearly the consequence of the different electronic structure of the interface: the energy levels of the conjugated dyes (JK2 in this case) are more strongly perturbed by surface protonation and the stabilization of the TiO2 CB edge translates into a similar stabilization of the dye's LUMO, while the LUMO of the uncoupled D102 dye is practically unaffected by what happens to the TiO2 DOS. As a consequence of the progressive charge accumulation on the surface, the dye's HOMO also turns out to be stabilized in the case of the conjugated dyes: in the JK2 dye, the HOMO stabilization is about 0.1 eV, while, for the same reasons discussed above, the D102 HOMO is practically unchanged upon TiO2 protonation. The different HOMO and LUMO energy shifts with surface protonation lead on average to the reduction of the dye's HOMO–LUMO gap (see Table 1) with increasing number of adsorbed protons. The entity of such HOMO–LUMO gap variation is strongly dependent on the peculiar electronic structure of the dye, and it is fully in line with the observed red-shift of the absorption spectrum of D5-sensitized TiO2 upon acidic treatment, which was recently reported by some of us.61 To better rationalize the relationship between the predicted change in the optical properties of the protonated dye-sensitized TiO2 film,61 the calculated enhanced injection rates and the measured photocurrents and device efficiencies, we report in the next section a spectroscopic and photovoltaic study of protonated and non-protonated sensitized TiO2 films employing a prototypical D–π–A organic dye (D5).

IV.4 Solvent effect on dye-sensitized TiO2

In order to treat the solar cells under the most realistic conditions, the effect of the chemical environment on the adsorption geometry and stability as well as the electronic structure of electrolyte/dye-sensitized TiO2 adducts was duly taken into account.164 Indeed, it is known that the molecular vicinity of the heterojunction influences the global efficiency of a given cell. Especially, water molecules in the surrounding of the interface are highly undesired,194 as they might cause partial dye desorption from the titanium oxide surface or just weakening of the interaction between the dye and TiO2.195,196 Titanium dioxide, sensitized with three Ru-based dyes, namely N719, Z907 (see Section II.2 above) and N3, exhibits variable behavior with respect to the presence of water, in relation to the chemical composition of the dye. Indeed, it was shown that substituting two carboxylic acid groups of N719 by hydrophilic chains to give Z907 did efficiently protect the TiO2 surface from water adsorption, while replacing two of the N719 carboxylic acid protons by tetra-n-butyl ammonium to give N3 did not significantly affect the dye amount in the vicinity of the titanium oxide surface.

It is therefore established that water in the proximity of the heterojunction affects the whole cell efficiency. So do other solvents, and studies have been carried out to show that when the cell efficiency is considered in relation to the liquid medium with which the dye-sensitized TiO2 and the electrolyte are in contact, acetonitrile is the solvent that provides the best results.

As the adsorption geometry and energy of the dye play an important role in photocurrent generation (adsorption mode of molecules has indeed been extensively investigated), a first-principles study evaluating the effect of water and acetonitrile solvents on the adsorption energy/geometry, together with the related impact on the electronic properties of adsorbed dyes, has been carried out on three prototypical sensitizers, benzoic acid (BA), 2-cyano-3-phenyl-acrylic acid (CA) and 4-(diphenylamino)phenylcyanoacrylic acid (L0).164 Joint DFT/CPMD calculations were exploited to model the adsorption of the dyes on a dye-sensitized three-layer 48-unit periodic anatase slab in contact with solvent molecules (CPMD) and (38-TiO2)-dye/solvent nanostructures (DFT). Three monodentate and one bidentate binding modes were identified and inspected. After the geometries were optimized in vacuo, water or acetonitrile solvent molecules were added in order to saturate the titanium sites at the surface of the anatase slabs.

The act of adding a monolayer of water molecules to the optimized structures was shown to impact the bidentate titanium-[O]-dyes distances (where the [O] points the binding-carboxylic acid oxygen of the sensitizers). Replacing water by acetonitrile monolayer results in an elongation of the dye-slab (titanium–oxygen) binding distances and decrease of the adsorption energy. A similar behaviour is observed when a water monolayer is added to one of the monodentate structures, while acetonitrile leads to modifications of the binding mode: the structure switches from a molecular to a dissociative monodentate. Solvent molecules were also shown to favour the deprotonation of the dye by modifying the acid/base properties of the dye-sensitized system.

The (101) TiO2 surface was also studied covered by solvent molecules saturating all the five-coordinated titanium atoms through water–[O]–Ti centers or acetonitrile–[N]–Ti centers. Here [O] and [N] stand for the oxygen (respectively nitrogen) atom of water (acetonitrile) solvent molecules. Different inter-water molecular interactions are also identified in the optimized structures. The results show that while water is able to compete with the dye for chemical anchoring at the surface of anatase, acetonitrile displays a lower ability to compete with the sensitizers.

The computed interaction between the slab and water or acetonitrile leads to an upward shift of 1.2 eV (water) and 2.4 eV (acetonitrile) for the conduction band, which was attributed to the increase of negative charge at the titanium oxide surface due to the donor character of the adsorbed water molecules. Those results were shown to be in good agreement with previous theoretical and experimental data.

DFT was then employed to investigate the electronic structure of sensitized TiO2 in the presence of solvent. The three previously mentioned dyes (BA, CA and L0) were studied, anchored to the TiO2 nanocluster, in the absence/presence of coadsorbed solvent. Computational results indicate that the presence of water up-shifts the anatase conduction band by 0.2 to 0.5 eV with respect to data obtained in vacuo. Acetonitrile solvent shifts this conduction band upward by 0.6 to 1.0 eV with respect to dye sensitized-TiO2 in the presence of water. This actually means that the presence of chemically-anchored dye reduces the impact of the chemical environment when the latter is restricted to solvent molecules in the vicinity of the titanium oxide slab surface.

V. Energy and electron/hole transfer within dye monolayers

The morphology of the dye monolayer, apart from directly influencing the optical and charge-generation properties of the dye-sensitized TiO2, clearly determines the rate and efficiency of energy, electron and hole transfer processes among the adsorbed species.197 In the following we shall discuss how long-ranged energy transfer processes81,198 and lateral hole diffusion86 in realistic dye/TiO2 interfaces can be effectively modelled to provide an experimentally observable result, also taking into account thermal fluctuations by means of ab initio molecular dynamic simulations. In the following the dipole moment (in the case of long range energy transfer) and electronic coupling terms (in the case of hole transfer) are calculated for isolated dyes or dimeric dye arrangements without considering the TiO2 surface, but keeping them in the conformation adopted when they are adsorbed on the semiconductor surface. This approximation is legitimate by the fact that usually the perturbation on the electronic structure and the optical properties of the dye grafted on the TiO2 surface is weak and simply translates into a systematic small charge delocalization toward the TiO2 substrate148 and a slight spectral shift.140

If inclusion of dynamical aspects is expected to have a relatively small impact when the FRET process occurs between co-adsorbed donor and acceptor units in close packing,198 it becomes, in principle, mandatory in dyadic systems. Indeed, in spite of its large popularity in deriving quantitative structural information on biological macromolecules, the validity of the fundamental assumptions underlying the modelling of FRET processes has recently been questioned.206–211 Basically, the most debated issue is the above mentioned “isotropic regime”, whereby both the donor and the acceptor are assumed to rapidly sample all possible orientations, resulting in an average orientational factor for the dipolar interaction κ2 of 2/3. A second assumption which is implied in the isotropic model is that the distance between the donor and the acceptor unit is not correlated to their relative orientations.210 As a consequence, in recent years molecular dynamics (MD) simulations have been largely employed to study the simultaneous motion of the donor/acceptor pair, thus supporting FRET experiments in obtaining structural parameters in various fields, including biology.208,212–216 To the best of our knowledge, the first study investigating the effect of thermal fluctuations on the FRET processes in antenna-acceptor dye sensitizers was recently reported by some of us.81 By combining Car–Parrinello Molecular Dynamics (CPMD) and Time Dependent DFT calculations we have characterized the dynamical evolution of FRET processes in two dyadic carbazole–phenothiazine (CZ–PTZ distance) dyes60,81 where the CZ unit, linked to PTZ via an alkyl C6H12 chain, acts as an antenna donor and the PTZ unit is a TiO2-anchored acceptor. The two dyes (see Fig. 21), hereafter termed CSORG1 and CSORG4 after ref. 60, differ in the anchoring group and hence in the relative mobility of the CZ and PTZ constituents. CSORG1 shows electron conjugation between PTZ and the cyanoacrylic acid anchoring group, enforcing the PTZ moiety to an almost perpendicular arrangement with respect to the TiO2 surface. In CSORG4, in contrast, the PTZ moiety is allowed to bend more freely about the rhodanine-3-acetic acid anchoring group.

V.2 Hole diffusion within dye monolayers

Upon electron injection in the semiconductor CB, lateral hole diffusion within the dye layer can occur,217 especially in solid-state DSCs where the hole transport materials cannot completely fill the mesoporous TiO2 electrode.64,218–221 This phenomenon of lateral hole hopping is also relevant in the context of the transport of charges to catalytic sites for water splitting, solar fuel production, molecular transistors, batteries, or any other molecular electronic device.222–224 Therefore, being able to predict the kinetics of charge diffusion within a molecular monolayer is of significant interest. In recent work, some of us have modelled the lateral hole transport between dyes on sensitized TiO2 nanocrystalline films investigating the relationship between molecular configuration and the bulk diffusion coefficient of holes across the monolayer.86 Experimentally, hole diffusion has been observed to depend on the nature of the dye and the surrounding medium. This is rationalized given that the chemical structure of the dyes and their physical arrangement in a given system will control their electronic interactions and hence the conductivity of the monolayer. Indeed, one can expect the lateral hole hopping rate to depend on the separation between dye molecules, which, in turn, depends on the density (or loading) of molecules on a given surface and the electronic coupling between the charge donor and acceptor (which is strongly dependent on the geometry of the pair of molecules) and on the dynamics of molecular rearrangement. Thus, to get a realistic evaluation of the lateral hole diffusion rates, taking into account the effect of both intermolecular interactions and thermal fluctuations, we have combined quantum chemical calculations of electronic structure and coupling with ab initio CPMD simulations or rigid molecular packing (RMP)225 and kinetic Monte Carlo.226 We note that, in a fully quantum mechanical picture, the Hamiltonian describing electronic coupling should be continually updated for the new conformations in a self-consistent manner, although this is most important for systems with a high degree of order, such as organic semiconductors.227 In our work, to reduce the computational cost for our more disordered system, we adopted an approximate approach decoupling molecular conformation and electronic coupling. We considered the two indoline D102 and D149 dyes (Fig. 23a and b, respectively), which have different degrees of conformational disorder on the TiO2 surface and a different surface coverage due to different packing motifs.80 This allowed us to examine the influence of this disorder on transport. We choose these dyes because they perform well in solid-state DSSCs228,229 and because their hole diffusion coefficient is among the highest reported.82,220

We address the variety of conformations in two ways. First, in order to sample the conformations explored in short times, less than the time for a typical charge transfer event (∼1 ns), we use Car–Parrinello molecular dynamics. However, in order to explore the conformations visited on time scales longer than charge hopping times, we adopted a less expensive method by approximating the set of conformations by those that are compatible with rotations of the dyes around their bidentate binding sites defined by the two oxygens of the anchoring group. The calculated electronic coupling distributions can be used with a continuous-time random walk algorithm (CTRW)226,231 to simulate hole diffusion across the dye molecule monolayer. twait is the adaptive time step, after which a hole changing its site depends on the rate of charge transfer and hence on the square of electronic coupling, Jij2, between nearest neighbours. Therefore, we have to consider the relative time scales of the various configurational changes with respect to the hole hopping to appropriately integrate the Jij distributions within the CTRW algorithm. To treat the problem appropriately we would need a statistical ensemble of conformations that spans the full range of dye dynamics. To limit the computational effort, we use the cheaper RMP generated conformations to include the effect of changing conformations on hopping. In this approach, the fastest dynamics are neglected. To examine this approximation, we use our CPMD generated ensemble to estimate the effect of fast dynamics on hopping rates. Three algorithms are implemented as described below and in more detail in ref. 86

(1) The fast limit, where the molecular rearrangement is much faster than the hole hopping time. This applies to the distributions calculated using CPMD, as the geometrical changes reported here cover up to 25 ps.

(2) The static limit, where the molecular rearrangement is much slower than the hole hopping time. In practice, this is equivalent to randomly assigning a value of electronic coupling (sampling the distributions generated using RMP) to every bond of the lattice at the beginning of the simulation and keeping it unchanged.

(3) The intermediate regime where the molecular rearrangement happens on a similar time scale to hole hopping. A new parameter is introduced: the renewal time, tren, after which the electronic coupling of each bond of the lattice is randomly reassigned.232 Here, we use the distributions of Jij for the pairs of dyes generated using RMP (also using Boltzmann-weighted CTRW). The fastest dynamics are neglected in this scenario (i.e., we do not obtain any Jij from the CPMD generated distributions) and hence the kinetic Monte Carlo simulation is expected to underestimate the hole diffusion coefficient relative to the case where both large amplitude conformation changes and fast dynamics are included, as we show below.

The fast limit (1), where we use the average of the squared electronic coupling from the CPMD distribution, shows the highest diffusion coefficients. Within this limit, the conformational disorder is limited to small fluctuations around an upright, reasonably well-coupled dye geometry; hence, the hole moves faster across the lattice. The static limit (2) is the case where the disorder is maximal because the lattice is frozen, and we logically obtain the lowest diffusion coefficients for both dyes in this limit. The difference between Dcalc calculated for both dyes is the greatest. The diffusion coefficient increases as the renewal time decreases. Indeed, after each time interval, tren, the bonds of the lattice are reassigned with a different Jij, drawn from the distribution. As a result, the charge is more likely to escape a weakly coupled site and moves faster across the lattice. For D102, we see that the diffusion coefficient with the smallest renewal time (10−7 s) is 95 times higher than the diffusion coefficient within the static limit. For D149, it is only 2.6 times higher. This can be rationalized by looking at the Jij distributions given in Fig. 24, parts b and d. The distributions are much wider for D102 than they are for D149, which implies higher disorder. Therefore, the reassignment of the electronic coupling has a stronger effect for D102, which explains the bigger increase in the diffusion coefficient. It thus seems that dynamical disorder helps charge conduction for our two-dimensional dye monolayer, in line with what was observed in three-dimensional organic semiconductors.233 It is interesting to see how our calculated diffusion coefficients compare with the measured ones. Because a straightforward comparison will still not be rigorous, we focus here on the relative kinetics of the two dyes. We observe that it is the intermediate regime at short renewal time that reproduces the experimental trend best. The ratio of the diffusion coefficient between the two dyes is consistent between calculated (DD149,calc ≈ 2.7DD102,calc) and measured (DD149,exp ≈ 2.6DD102,exp) values. This implies that the hole diffusion kinetics observed in real systems can be explained by a fast rearrangement of the molecules adsorbed on TiO2. This corroborates previous observations where the diffusion coefficient is found to decrease when the viscosity of the surrounding hole transporting medium increases.220,234,235 Indeed, we expect a slower rearrangement of the molecular lattice in a viscous environment and therefore, as demonstrated in ref. 86, a lower diffusion coefficient. In conclusion, it was proven that a static model cannot give a diffusion coefficient high enough to explain the experimental trend, as opposed to a model with a rearranging lattice. Thus, this suggests that it is likely to be the motion of the dyes on the surface that allows the fast conduction of holes through the monolayer. As a result, a greater range of configurational disorder will result in a slower hole transfer dynamics but can be balanced by a fast rearrangement of the molecules, which allows charges to escape structurally trapping configurations. Consequently, the viscosity of the medium surrounding the dyes is likely to play a role, as it may slow down the molecular dynamics. To design fast conducting molecular wires, one needs to account for the freedom of the molecules once attached on the substrate.

VI. Summary and conclusions

We reviewed the various aspects of interfacial phenomena taking place at the vicinity of the dye–TiO2 heterojunction, and as a consequence of the chemical anchoring of dye sensitizers onto the semiconductor surface. More especially, we addressed different points related to several physical phenomena characterizing this anchoring, under the most realistic conditions available computationally, i.e. by paying attention to the environment of the grafting zones and by accounting for the dynamical nature of the systems' structure. To this end, we first highlighted the importance of the interaction between the dye-sensitized TiO2 and the regenerating agent, when the latter is a classical iodine redox couple, or a cobalt complex. It was especially highlighted that depending on the composition of organic dye sensitizers, some atoms (especially oxygen and sulphur) can serve as binding sites for iodine, decreasing its average distance to the surface and favouring the recombination process.

We also reported recent studies on dye aggregation, by considering a simple model involving two monomers clustering into a dimer. Geometrical considerations were addressed, and the remarkable optical properties of the dimer, resulting from the interaction between the subsystems, were justified using the molecular exciton theory of excited state splitting. A more evolved approach accounting for the dynamical motion of dye monolayers highlighted the fact that upon clustering, the various constituents of the monolayer are chemically anchored at the surface of anatase through different binding modes. Spectral shifts were evaluated and attributed to self-aggregation. We further reported a computational study related to the optical and charge-separation properties of dye-sensitized semiconductor nanoclusters under realistic conditions where the heterointerface was modelled by including a dye additive. Depending on the structure of the co-adsorbing agent and the solvent used for computing the optical properties of the adducts, the calculations revealed that while the presence of additives can affect the optical properties of the dye in relation to its environment, this environment, modelled by a polarizable continuum method, was shown not to strongly impact the optical characteristics of the additiveless dye.

A theoretical investigation related to proton adsorption was then reported, where electronic structure calculations highlighted the fact that injection rate values are strongly affected by the number of protons that are adsorbed at the surface of TiO2. Spectroscopic photovoltaic studies of protonated and non-protonated sensitized TiO2 further showed that TiO2 absorption shifts to longer wavelengths upon proton adsorption. Moreover, it was reported that an acidic treatment of titanium oxide also leads to higher interfacial electronic coupling and dye coverage, as well as higher photocurrent values, confirming that this treatment leads to an overall better photovoltaic performance.

Including a solvent monolayer in the vicinity of the heterojunction in the computational treatment of the interface showed that while water is able to compete with the dye for occupying titanium oxide sites, acetonitrile on the other hand did not have the same characteristics. This was revealed by observing the modification of dye–Ti bond distances in the presence or absence of the solvent monolayer, and by comparing the sensitized TiO2 conduction band energy in vacuo and in the presence of solvent.

Energy and charge-transfer issues were then addressed through the report of a study highlighting that when modelling the co-sensitization of TiO2 it appears that some preferential dye orientations can be dictated by the surface topology and influence the resonance energy transfer occurring between the two co-sensitizers, with the FRET coefficient varying by two-to-three orders of magnitude. The inclusion of dynamical effects was also reported for dyadic systems by combining CPMD with TDDFT for the study of time-dependent FRET of anatase sensitized with two dyes differing in their anchoring function (cyanoacrylic acid and rhodanine-3-acetic acid respectively).

Finally, hole diffusion into dye monolayers was reported for two indoline-based compounds with different degrees of conformational disorder and different surface coverage on the titanium oxide surface. CPMD, rigid molecular packing and kinetic Monte Carlo calculations were performed to evaluate the lateral hole diffusion rates, electronic coupling distribution and charge mean square displacement. The time random walk algorithm was also exploited to calculate diffusion coefficients, and compare the theoretical results with experimental reality implying a fast molecular rearrangement of adsorbed molecules, which could not be retrieved from a static model.

Dye-sensitized solar cells are now a relatively mature field of application of photovoltaic technology with relevant industrial perspectives. Theoretical and computational modelling has definitely contributed to the deep understanding of the factors underlying solar cell efficiency and the interplay among various cell components in defining the final device properties. In this review we have offered an overview of the employed computational strategies and of relevant results, which we believe to represent the most up to date cutting edge of dye-sensitized modelling. The recent emergence of perovskite solar cells has definitely changed the landscape of hybrid/organic photovoltaics, casting new problems on the playground, which again call for the use of advanced computational modelling strategies. We believe that most of the work done on dye-sensitized solar cells will be transferred to perovskite solar cell modelling, whereby similar computational modelling strategies may be appropriately adapted to the new perovskite challenge.
