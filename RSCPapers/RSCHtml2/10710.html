Abstract

Two donorâ€“acceptor (Dâ€“A) copolymers (PBDTBT and PBDTBSe) based on bithienyl-benzodithiophene (BDT) as the donor (D) unit, fluorinated benzothiadiazole (BT) or benzoselenadiazole (BSe) as the acceptor (A) unit, were designed and synthesized. The optoelectronic properties of the two polymers, including the absorption spectra, electronic energy levels, hole mobility and photovoltaic properties, were fully studied and compared. The effects of the replacement of S by Se in the BT unit and F substitution on their optoelectronic properties were investigated in detail. The replacement of S by Se in the benzothiadiazole unit red-shifted the absorption spectra and increased the hole mobility of the Dâ€“A copolymers. Polymer solar cells (PSCs) based on the polymers as the donor and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC70BM) as the acceptor were fabricated, and the photovoltaic performance of the PSCs was optimized by optimizing the device fabrication conditions, including the solvent, donorâ€“acceptor blend ratio, thermal annealing and solvent additive. The highest power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the optimized PSCs based on the polymers reached 5.06% and 2.20% for PBDTBT and PBDTBSe, respectively. The lower photovoltaic performance of the PSCs based on PBDTBSe is due to the poorer morphology of its active layer.

Introduction

Conjugated polymers have attracted great attention in the past three decades for their novel optoelectronic properties and applications in polymer light-emitting diodes (PLEDs) and polymer solar cells (PSCs) etc. The most important properties of the conjugated polymers include absorption and photoluminescent spectra, the energy bandgap, electronic energy levels and charge carrier mobility. In particular, broad absorption with a lower bandgap, higher hole mobility and relatively lower HOMO (highest occupied molecular orbital) energy levels are pursued for the application of conjugated polymers as donors in PSCs,1 because broad absorption and a higher hole mobility of the polymer donor lead to a larger short circuit current (Jsc),1c and a lower HOMO energy level of the polymer donor will result in a larger open circuit voltage (Voc) of the PSCs.2

The Dâ€“A copolymerization of the donor (D) and acceptor (A) units is a popular and successful strategy in reducing the bandgap and decreasing the HOMO energy levels of the conjugated polymers.3,4 The Dâ€“A approach allows facile tuning of the frontier molecular orbital energies (HOMO and LUMO) and bandgaps by intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) from a donor unit to an acceptor moiety within the repeat unit of the copolymers. In the Dâ€“A copolymers, the incorporation of an electron-withdrawing fluorine atom onto the periphery of the acceptor unit is attractive, since its small size is expected to minimize any undesired steric interactions, and also its strong electron affinity is expected to decrease the HOMO and LUMO energy levels.5 Furthermore, fluorination can decrease the bandgap of the polymer films in some cases, which arises from the improvement in the planarization of the conjugated backbone and F atom-induced intramolecular and/or intermolecular interactions.6 Indeed, the direct incorporation of fluorine onto the periphery of electron-deficient benzothiadiazole (BT),6b,7 benzotriazole8 and quinoxaline9 units has recently been shown to increase the photovoltaic performance of some (but not all) donor polymers, mainly through an increase in the Voc.

The replacement of sulfur (S) with selenium (Se) within aromatic rings in conjugated polymers tends to induce a red shift in the absorption spectrum. This is because the Se atom is much larger in size and less electronegative compared with the S atom, and thus Se-containing polymers are expected to be more effective in extending the absorption spectrum toward the infrared region. In addition, the Se-containing unit is more polarizable than the S analogue; thus, the interchain Seâ‹¯Se interactions could improve the charge mobility of the polymers. Recently, it has been found that replacing S with Se in the thiophene Ï€-bridge in diketopyrrolopyrrole (DPP)-containing conjugated polymers (SeDPP-based polymers) can decrease the bandgap and enhance the charge transport properties in organic field effect transistor devices.10 However, the photovoltaic performance of the SeDPP-based low bandgap polymer was lower than its corresponding polymer with a thiophene Ï€-bridge, mostly due to its higher HOMO level and thus the lower Voc of the photovoltaic device.10d Similarly, recent efforts to replace the sulfur atom in thieno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (TPD) with selenium to form the electron-deficient seleno[3,4-c]pyrrole-4,6-dione (SePD), which narrowed the bandgap of the resulting polymer, but reduced the Voc significantly and featured a lower PCE.11 Another developed Se-containing electron-deficient building block is benzoselenadiazole (BSe), which is a benzothiadiazole (BT) analogue, where S is replaced by Se.12 BSe-based polymers have been used in PSCs and gave an unsatisfactory PCE of 2.5%.12d On the other hand, very recently Tajima et al. reported a BSe-based low bandgap polymer with a promising PCE up to 5.18%, which was slightly higher than that of the BT-based analog (5.01%).13 The above contradictory results show that there is a need to develop a novel Se-based electron-deficient unit to build Dâ€“A type copolymers, and compare the properties of the resulting S- and Se-based copolymers for a more clear understanding of the effects of Se substitution.

In this study, we chose benzodithiophene (BDT) as the electron-rich unit and fluorinated BT or BSe as the electron-deficient unit to synthesize two new Dâ€“A copolymers (PBDTBT and PBDTBSe, see Fig. 1). The aim of the design of these polymers is to investigate the effects of Se substitution on the optoelectronic properties by comparing BT and BSe-based Dâ€“A type copolymers. BDT was employed as the electron-rich donor unit because of its rigidity, coplanarity by fusing benzene with two flanking thiophene units, high hole mobility, extended Ï€-conjugated structure, and easy side chain modification for tuning the solubility.14 Some BDT-containing copolymers have shown great potential in PSCs with enhanced mobilities and high PCEs surpassing 7%.6b,15 Here, we attached alkylthienyl groups to the 4 and 8 positions of the BDT unit to afford a two-dimensional (2D)-conjugated BDT featuring more electron-rich donor groups and extended conjugation, which is expected to result in red-shifted absorption spectra, lower HOMO energy levels and a higher hole mobility.7a,15c On the other hand, the introduction of F atoms with high electron affinity to the BT and BSe moieties is designed to further lower both the HOMO and LUMO levels of the resulting polymers. The basic properties of the two polymers, such as their absorption spectra, energy levels, hole mobility in the films, and photovoltaic performance, were fully studied and compared. The effects of Se and F substitution on these properties was investigated in detail.

The PSC devices were fabricated with a structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/active layer/Ca/Al, where the active layer is composed of a conjugated polymer as the donor and [6,6]-phenyl-C71-butyric acid methyl ester (PC70BM) as the acceptor. As shown in Fig. 3, the two polymers show similar broad absorption spectral features across the entire UV-vis region, both in chloroform solution and as solid films. It should be noted that the polymers show one additional high-energy band in addition to the apparent characteristic Dâ€“A dual-band.16 One high-energy peak located at approximately 350 nm can be assigned to the conjugated thiophene side chains. The other high-energy band at around 430 nm and the low-energy band located at about 580 nm correspond to the characteristic dual-band of the Dâ€“A system, which are attributed to localized Ï€â€“Ï€* transitions in the polymeric backbone and the ICT between the BDT donor and the BT or BSe acceptor moieties, respectively. The absorptions of the polymer films are broader and only a little red-shifted compared with that in solution (Fig. 3), which reveals that strong aggregation exists in solution for the fluorinated polymers.6b,8b In addition, these types of fluorinated polymer exhibit an additional absorption shoulder in solution and as thin films, implying additional polymer-chain stacking both in solution and in the solid state.6b,15 Such broad absorption and ordered packing of the polymer chains could result in a larger Jsc and charge carrier mobility. Furthermore, compared to PBDTBT, the absorption peaks of the BSe-based copolymer PBDTBSe is red-shifted by about 10 nm in the high-energy band and 30 nm in the low-energy band (Table 2). The more obvious red-shift in the low-energy band relative to the high-energy band indicates that heavy atom (Se) substitution can lead to a remarkable red-shift in the low-energy transition, while the high-energy band remains relatively constant in energy.17 The red-shift in the low-energy transition will result in a decrease of the bandgap value. The optical bandgaps (Eoptg) of PBDTBT and PBDTBSe are 1.74 and 1.66 eV, respectively, calculated from the absorption edges (Î»edge) of the solid state films (Table 2). Obviously, the BSe-based polymer PBDTBSe features a lower bandgap than the BT-based polymer PBDTBT. These results clearly indicate that replacing a S atom with a Se atom is a simple and effective way of lowering the bandgaps and red-shifting the absorption spectra of BT-containing Dâ€“A type copolymers to the long-wavelength region.8b,13,17

The HOMO and LUMO energy levels (EHOMO/LUMO) of these polymers were measured by CV (Fig. 4). The CV curve was recorded by using a Ag/AgCl electrode as the reference, which was calibrated against the redox potential of the ferrocene/ferrocenium couple (Fc/Fc+ measured as 0.39 V vs. Ag/AgCl), whose ionization energy was assumed to be 4.80 eV below the vacuum level.18 Thus, EHOMO/LUMO = âˆ’e(Eox/red + 4.41) (eV), where Eox/red is the oxidation/reduction onset potential of the sample vs. Ag/AgCl. Based on the onset values of the oxidation potentials (0.93 and 0.89 V) of the two polymers, the EHOMO values of PBDTBT and PBDTBSe were calculated to be âˆ’5.34 and âˆ’5.30 eV, respectively. Additionally, the ELUMO values were calculated from the onset values of the reduction potentials (âˆ’1.18 and âˆ’0.94 V), which are âˆ’3.23 eV for PBDTBT and âˆ’3.47 eV for PBDTBSe. The HOMO levels are analogous to those of BDT-based low bandgap polymers (âˆ’5.30 to âˆ’5.50 eV),14b which can be expected to achieve a high Voc in PSCs, according to the dependence of Voc on the difference between the HOMO of the donor and the LUMO of the accepter.4 The conjugated thiophene side units attached to the BDT unit can slightly lower the HOMO levels of the polymers around the corresponding ideal energy level of âˆ’5.40 eV.19 It is interesting to note that the electron-deficient fluorine groups substantially lowered the HOMO energy levels. Compared with the BT- and BSe-based analogue polymers,13 lower HOMO energy levels down-shifted by ca. 0.08 eV and 0.12 eV were achieved for PBDTBT and PBDTBSe respectively, which indicates that fluorinated PBDTBT and PBDTBSe could be promising photovoltaic materials with a high Voc. These results agree well with the previous reports by You that the incorporation of fluorine is effective in decreasing the HOMO energy levels.6b,8a Moreover, the lower-lying HOMO of PBDTBT compared with PBDTBSe is expected to increase the Voc of PSCs. The electrochemical bandgaps were also calculated to be 2.11 and 1.83 eV for PBDTBT and PBDTBSe, respectively, which were greater than Eoptg (Table 2) but within the range of error (0.2â€“0.5 eV).20

First, we found that the different processing solvents (CB versus DCB) has some effect on the photovoltaic properties of the devices based on the two polymers. As shown in Table 3, the devices (D3, D4, D9, D10) with DCB as the processing solvent showed higher PCEs than those of the corresponding devices using CB as the solvent (D1, D2, D7, D8) under the same fabrication conditions, which is mainly attributed to the increase of the FF and Jsc to some extent. Then the blend ratio of the polymer donor and PC70BM acceptor was optimized (D3â€“6 and D9â€“10). As the mixing ratio of PBDTBT and PC70BM increased from 1:1 to 1:2, the PCE of the device increased from 3.55% for D3 with a Dâ€“A weight ratio of 1:1 to 3.93% for D4 with a weight ratio of 1:2 (Fig. 5a, Table 3). When the weight ratio was increased further, the PCEs dropped sharply (e.g. PCE of D6 with a weight ratio of 1:4 dropped to 1.50%). The main reason for this is the decrease of both the Voc and Jsc of the PSCs. Therefore, the optimal blend ratio for this polymer was determined to be around 1:2. However for the PBDTBSe-based PSCs, the optimal blend ratio appears to be 1:1, because the PSCs with a weight ratio of 1:1 (D7 and D9) exhibited higher PCEs than the devices with a weight ratio of 1:2 (D8 and D10). A noteworthy fact is that the devices based on these fluorinated polymers show a significant improvement (âˆ¼0.2 V) in the Voc relative to those of the non-fluorinated analogues.13 Most of the PSCs based on the two polymers gave Voc values higher than 0.8 V, and some of them even exceed 0.9 V (D1, D3, D4, D11, and D15). As discussed in the â€œElectrochemical propertiesâ€ section, the electron-withdrawing nature of F lowers the HOMO energy levels of the fluorinated polymers compared to those of the non-fluorinated analogues.13 Since the Voc is largely determined by the difference between the HOMO of the polymer and the LUMO of PCBM, the reduction of the polymer HOMO energy level largely accounts for the increase in Voc after the F substitution.

Thermal treatment has proved successful for controlling the nanomorphology of the active layers. Moreover, use of an appropriate temperature and time of thermal annealing plays a key role in optimizing the morphology and enhancing the performance of PSCs.22 Here we obtained optimal conditions of pre-thermal annealing at 110 Â°C for 10 min, and found some interesting results. From Table 4, one can see that the influence of pre-thermal annealing depends on the processing solvent and the blend ratio of the donor and acceptor. For PBDTBT-based devices, when the blend ratio was 1:1, device D11 with CB as the solvent gave an improved PCE of 3.81% compared to D1, whereas device D14 with DCB as the solvent showed a decreased PCE of 3.33% relative to D3. However, when the blend ratio was changed to 1:2, device D12 with CB as the solvent gave a decreased PCE of 2.97%, whereas device D15 with DCB as the solvent showed an increased PCE of 4.06%, which opposes the results observed in the blend ratio of 1:1. On the other hand, for the PBDTBSe-based devices, an improved PCE of 1.69% was achieved with a blend ratio of 1:1 and DCB as the solvent. The changing trend of the PCEs is mainly in accord with the changing tendencies of the Jsc and FF of the corresponding devices (Fig. 5b, Table 4).

Besides the thermal annealing treatment, the processing solvent additive, such as 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO), also can affect the morphology of the active layer and the corresponding photovoltaic properties of the PSCs. As shown in Fig. 5c and Table 5, the processing additive DIO has a great influence on the PCEs of the PSCs based on the two polymers. When a small amount (3%, v/v) of DIO was added as the processing additive, the Jsc and FF can be improved significantly, resulting in improved PCEs even though the Voc dropped to some extent. A maximum PCE of 5.06% was achieved in the PSC (D20) based on PBDTBT, with Voc = 0.88 V, Jsc = 10.73 mA cmâˆ’2 and FF = 0.536. As for the PBDTBSe-based PSCs, a PCE of 2.08% (D23) was achieved when the blend ratio of PBDTBSe/PC70BM was 1:1 with 3% DIO as the additive. As the blend ratio is changed to 1:2, the PCE is further improved to 2.20% (D25) with Voc = 0.81 V, Jsc = 5.36 mA cmâˆ’2 and FF = 0.506. The improved efficiencies may originate from the increased absorption of light, enhanced charge carrier mobility, or the improved efficiency of charge carrier collection from the optimal morphology with DIO as the processing additive.

From the above results, it can be seen that there is a great difference in the photovoltaic performance between the two similar polymers. Obviously, the main reason is the substantial improvement in Voc and Jsc for the PBDTBT-based cells compared to PBDTBSe. For the PBDTBSe-based PSCs, the decreased Voc can be readily understood from its higher-lying HOMO energy level compared with that of PBDTBT (see Table 2). With regard to the reduced Jsc, the absorption properties should not be principally responsible, because the red-shifted absorption spectrum and the lower bandgap of PBDTBSe relative to PBDTBT should lead to a higher Jsc.

Fig. 6 displays the external quantum efficiency (EQE) curves of the PSCs. For the PSCs based on the two polymers with different fabrication conditions, the shapes of the EQE curves are similar, but the EQE values of the devices with thermal annealing and the DIO additive are enhanced. The highest EQE values are 52% (at 495 nm) and 40% (at 515 nm) for the PSCs based on PBDTBT/PC70BM and PBDTBSe/PC70BM respectively. Although the EQE value is not very high, the response covers a reasonable broad spectral range from 300 to 750 nm and a relatively high EQE value above 40% at 350â€“650 nm, which explains the high Jsc values of the PBDTBT-based devices. For the PSCs based on PBDTBSe/PC70BM, the response extends to 750 nm, which is longer than that of the PBDTBT/PC70BM system by ca. 20 nm and consistent with its red-shifted absorption spectrum. The lower EQE values of the PBDTBSe-based devices agree with the lower Jsc values of the corresponding PSCs.

Morphology characterization

In order to gain more insight into the observed difference in device performance (especially the Jsc) of the two polymers and investigate the effect of the thermal annealing and DIO additive on the photovoltaic properties, tapping-mode atomic force microscopy (AFM) was employed to measure the morphological properties of the BHJ films. As shown in Fig. 8b and h, the polymer/PC70BM (1:2, w/w) blend films spin-coated from DCB solution show a root mean square (RMS) roughness of 5.82 and 6.55 nm in the topography for PBDTBT and PBDTBSe, respectively. It should be mentioned that the larger RMS value of the PBDTBSe-based blend film could be one reason for the lower photovoltaic performance of PBDTBSe, as it may lead to inefficient charge separation, decreasing the Jsc and the device efficiency. In addition, the hole mobilities of 4.47 Ã— 10âˆ’3 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 for PBDTBT could already be high enough to balance its hole transportation with the electron transportation of PCBM, and the higher hole mobility of 1.03 Ã— 10âˆ’2 cm2 Vâˆ’1 sâˆ’1 for PBDTBSe may lead to hole accumulation at the anode/active layer interface which degrades the photovoltaic performance of PBDTBSe.

It is known that enriched PC70BM at the interface between the active layer and the metal cathode lowers the barrier and thus facilitates charge collection.24 Therefore, after annealing or adding 3% DIO (v/v), the vertical composition/phase separation may be optimized, so a higher Jsc, improved FF and thus larger PCE can be realized. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized two new fluorinated benzothiadiazole- and benzoselenadiazole-based Dâ€“A copolymers (PBDTBT and PBDTBSe), and demonstrated a detailed comparison between the two copolymers in terms of spectroscopic, thermal, electrochemical, charge transporting and photovoltaic properties. Obvious red-shifted UV-vis absorption was observed for the fluorinated benzoselenadiazole-based copolymers. The reduction of the bandgap energy by the replacement of S with Se is more due to the lowering of the LUMO energy level than the raising of the HOMO energy level of the copolymers. Moreover, the results showed that the incorporation of electron-deficient fluorine groups substantially lowers the HOMO energy levels, which contributes to the high Voc in the PSCs. The BHJ PSCs were prepared with the two polymers as the donor and PC70BM as the acceptor. The fabricated conditions, including the solvent, blend ratio, thermal annealing and additive, were fully optimized. The optimized photovoltaic performance (Voc = 0.88 V, Jsc = 10.73 mA cmâˆ’2, FF = 0.536 and PCE = 5.06%) obtained for PBDTBT showed a significantly higher Voc and Jsc than the benzoselenadiazole-based copolymer counterparts (Voc = 0.81 V, Jsc = 5.36 mA cmâˆ’2, FF = 0.506 and PCE = 2.20%), mostly due to the deeper-lying HOMO and better morphological properties of the active layer, respectively. Although PBDTBSe exhibits a broader absorption spectrum and a higher hole mobility, the PSCs still gave inferior Jsc, FF and PCE values due to the serious aggregation and low compatibility between the polymer and PC70BM. As a new electron-deficient unit, we believe that fluorinated benzoselenadiazole will be an effective acceptor unit used to build high-performance Dâ€“A copolymers in the foreseeable future if one can tune its compatibility with the PC70BM acceptor by copolymerizing with a suitable donor unit.
