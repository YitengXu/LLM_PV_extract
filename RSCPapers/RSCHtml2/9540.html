Abstract

The rational design of highly efficient and stable electrocatalysts remains a challenge for large scale hydrogen production. Regulating the heterointerface between two catalyst components could effectively modulate the electronic structure and tune the electrocatalytic activity. Herein, a hybrid RuO2â€“NiO electrocatalyst on nickel foam was prepared via a simple and easy industrial scale-up approach, which affords a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 at low overpotentials of 18 mV for HER and 187 mV for OER, respectively. The electrolyzer assembled by the RuO2â€“NiO bifunctional electrode needs a cell voltage of only 1.43 V to reach 10 mA cmâˆ’2 for the overall water splitting. Moreover, the electrolyzer achieved an outstanding activity and stability at high current densities, which needed a cell voltage of only 1.66 V to reach 500 mA cmâˆ’2, and could perform continuous electrolysis for 2000 hours at 1000 mA cmâˆ’2, thereby demonstrating a great potential for practical applications. Theoretical calculations revealed that the coupling of RuO2 and NiO could greatly accelerate water dissociation and modulate the chemisorption of hydrogen and oxygen-containing intermediates on the catalyst, and consequently enhance the intrinsic activity towards both HER and OER.

Introduction

Hydrogen energy has been hailed as one of the most important sustainable energy, as well as the best carrier of new energy economy.1â€“3 Water electrolysis is an ideal strategy for hydrogen production using intermittent renewable sources, such as wind or solar power that is sometimes hard to integrate into the power grid.4â€“6 Therefore, there is urgent need to develop a water electrolysis system with high efficiency, practicality, and reliability.

Electrocatalysts are required to accelerate the sluggish reaction kinetics for oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER).7,8 However, the rational design of a highly efficient and stable catalyst for the industrial-scale electrolysis system is still a challenge.9,10 Platinum and iridium oxide are currently the most active catalysts towards HER and OER, respectively.11â€“13 The obvious disadvantages of such materials are their high cost that severely obstructs their industrial applications.14â€“16 Although non-noble metal catalysts, such as sulfides,17 selenides,18 carbides,19 and phosphides,20â€“22 have been extensively studied over the past years, the relatively inferior activity, poor durability, as well as the complicated and high-cost synthesis, even with poisonous and noxious reagents, is still the main barrier for such catalysts in realizing the commercialization of hydrogen production.23â€“25 A widely acceptable compromise formula is to greatly reduce the consumption of noble metals and improve their utilization efficiency to the best extent possible, which could accomplish the low-cost hydrogen production.26,27 Thanks to the catalytic activity and chemical stability of Ru-based catalysts,28,29 especially RuO2,30â€“32 which is similar to that of Ir and Pt, as well as the low price among noble metals ($8.037, $31.893, and $47.583 per g for Ru, Pt, and Ir in 2020, respectively)33 that it is considered to be the most promising water electrochemical splitting catalyst instead of Ir and Pt. However, according to the NÃ¸rskov volcano theory, RuO2 still suffers from a non-neglectable distance from the peak of the 2D volcano plot in the OER.34,35 Meanwhile, a large number of experimental results have also shown that the best among the current RuO2-based catalysts still need hundreds of millivolts of overpotentials for OER to reach a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2.24,30,32,36 Beyond this, the report of RuO2 for HER catalysis is seldom due to the sluggish kinetics.24 However, a highly efficient bifunctional electrocatalyst for both HER and OER with the same electrolyte has the advantages of simple operation and low cost.37â€“40 Hence it is quite necessary and urgent to greatly improve both OER and HER catalytic activities of RuO2.

Many strategies, including elemental doping and shape or/and size control, have been developed to enhance the catalytic activity.32,41 Interfacial engineering can effectively tune the electronic states of the catalysts; thus, constructing a heterointerface structure is an effective way to improve the catalytic activity of electrode materials.42,43 The heterointerface structure with a strong coupling between the two components could induce definite synergies, which not only remains their inherent properties but also generate some new effects.44,45 The electronic structure of the catalyst could be well-tuned to match a moderate binding strength between the intermediates and the catalyst surfaces, and thus accelerate the reaction kinetics in HER and/or OER.42,43,46â€“48 Moreover, the heterostructured catalyst with abundant interfaces provides plentiful catalytic active sites, thus favoring the catalytic performance. In particular, the MoSe2â€“NiSe2â€“CoSe2 multiple heterointerfaces lead to an increased Fermi level and optimized Î”GH*, and an enhanced HER performance was experimentally achieved.49 In some typical cases, with the help of the synergy effect, the heterostructure catalysts fabricated by loading a tiny amount of noble metal on the active supports, such as N/P doped graphene and transition metal or metal oxide/phosphide/sulfide/nitride, have even achieved a performance in water electrochemical splitting superior to that of the noble metal based catalysts such as Pt, Ir, Ru, IrO2, RuO2, IrP, and RuP.50â€“55 In addition, the active noble metal sites could be well anchored on the support through the strong interaction, which could achieve an excellent catalytic stability and the uniform dispersion of noble metals,50,56 thus increasing its utilization efficiency. Thereby, the most effective and viable option to obtain an excellent water splitting electrocatalyst is to construct a large number of heterointerfaces between the noble metal and the active support. Owing to the high activity, earth abundance, and anticorrosion property in alkaline solution, transition metal oxides are generally regarded as promising catalysts for OER.47,48 Meanwhile, it is believed that transition metal oxides could promote water dissociation, which facilitates the production of the H intermediates, and thus favors alkaline HER kinetics. For example, Wan et al. reported that NiO accelerated the Volmer step, while metallic Ni facilitated the Heyrovsky/Tafel step; thus, the alkaline HER activity of the Ni-based catalyst could be significantly enhanced by tuning the Ni/NiO ratio.57 Dai et al. reported a NiO/Ni heterostructure attached to a CNT network, which exhibited high HER catalytic activity close to that of commercial Pt/C catalysts,58 and Cr2O3 could impart high stability to the Ni@NiO/Cr2O3 catalyst.59 With this consideration, transition metal oxides could be ideal candidates to couple with RuO2 in order to achieve better water splitting performance. However, most transition metal oxides suffer from poor conductivity, which greatly impedes the improvement of their activity efficiency.60 This issue is expected to be resolved by engineering a heterointerface structure that can effectively accelerate electron transfer.42,46,61,62

In the current work, NiO was selected as a model component to construct the heterointerface structure with RuO2 since it is facile to in situ fabricate the RuO2â€“NiO catalyst on nickel foam, which could efficiently increase the electrocatalytic stability. The hybrid RuO2â€“NiO complex catalyst on nickel foam (RuO2â€“NiO/NF) was prepared using a simple and easy scale-up dip and heating method. The RuO2â€“NiO/NF catalyst with abundant hybrid interfaces is capable of catalyzing both OER and HER at very low overpotentials. The corresponding dual-electrode electrolyzer only needs a cell voltage of 1.43 V to achieve a current density of 10 mA cmâˆ’2 for overall water splitting, and 1.66 V to reach a commercially practical 500 mA cmâˆ’2, which exceeds most of the reported water-splitting catalysts. Furthermore, the RuO2â€“NiO/NF catalyst presents high stability, which could perform continuous electrolysis for 2000 hours at 1000 mA cmâˆ’2 with a slight decrease in the activity (â‰ˆ10%). The interface-induced synergy was systematically investigated by theoretical calculations, which illustrate that the RuO2â€“NiO interface can effectively tune the electronic structure, increase the electrical conductivity, optimize the intermediate bindings, and promote H2O dissociation, thus favoring the OER and HER processes.

Experimental

Preparation of RuO2â€“NiO/NF

RuO2â€“NiO/NF was prepared via a simple method. First, commercial nickel foam (NF, 2 Ã— 3 cm2) was ultrasonically cleaned with 3 m HCl, ethanol, and ultrapure water. Second, the NF was directly immersed in 25 mL aqueous solution of RuCl3 with different concentrations (10, 20, 40, 80, and 120 mM) for 5 min. The sample was then dried in an oven at 60 Â°C and labeled as pre-RuO2â€“NiO/NF. Finally, Ru pre-RuO2â€“NiO/NF was annealed in air at 300 Â°C for 30 min with a heating rate of 5 Â°C minâˆ’1. The final product was labeled as RuO2â€“NiO/NF.

Preparation of NiO/NF

NiO/NF was prepared in the same way as that for RuO2â€“NiO/NF except for using NiCl2 solution instead of the RuCl3 solution. The NF was directly immersed into 120 mL aqueous solution of NiCl2 for 5 min and dried in an oven. Finally, the sample was annealed in air to obtain NiO/NF.

Preparation of Oâ€“NF

The blank NF was directly annealed in air to obtain the oxidized NF (Oâ€“NF).

Preparation of RuO2 and Pt/C on NFs

For comparison, 5 mg of Pt/C or RuO2 was dispersed in 660 Î¼L ultrapure water, 300 Î¼L ethanol, and 40 Î¼L 5% Nafion, then the suspension was dip-coated on NF and dried under ambient conditions to prepare Pt/C/NF and RuO2/NF with a desired catalyst mass loading. The mass loading of the Pt/C for Pt/C/NF sample was 0.19 mg cmâˆ’2 and the mass loadings of RuO2 for the RuO2/NF samples were 0.83 and 0.19 mg cmâˆ’2, respectively.

Electrochemical measurements

All electrochemical investigations were performed at room temperature on a CHI 760E electrochemical workstation (CHI Instruments, Shanghai Chenhua Instrument Corporation, China) using a carbon rod and a Hg/HgO electrode as the counter electrode and the reference electrode, respectively. The prepared samples were used as the working electrodes and the electrolyte was a 1 M KOH aqueous solution. All the potentials reported in this work were referenced to reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) by the following equation: E (vs. RHE) = E (vs. Hg/HgO) + 0.098 + 0.059 Ã— pH. All the linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves were reported with iR correction and the scan rate was 1 mV sâˆ’1 to minimize the effect of the capacitive current. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed under a fixed bias of 100 mV in the frequency range from 100 kHz to 0.1 Hz. Doule-layer capacitance (Cdl) data were collected by measuring the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves at various scan rates (20, 40, 60, 80, and 100 mV sâˆ’1). For faradaic efficiency (FE) determination, the theoretical amount of H2 and O2 was calculated based on the Faraday law; the actual H2 and O2 amounts were tested via a Hoffman apparatus setup in a two-electrode system. During long-term electrolysis, fresh 1 M KOH electrolyte was charged into the electrolytic cell to replace all of the former electrolyte every 100 h to maintain continuous operation.

Results and discussion

The RuO2â€“NiO heterostructure with hybrid interfaces was prepared by a simple approach as described in Fig. 1a, which permitted the convenient synthesis of the electrocatalyst at a large scale. The blank NF was firstly immersed in an aqueous solution of RuCl3 and then dried to obtain the pre-sample. After annealing in air, the RuO2â€“NiO/NF sample was obtained (Fig. S1â€ ). Obviously, this simple and easy preparation process is very suitable for large scale production (Fig. S2â€ ). X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis confirmed that Ni(OH)2 (JCPDS no. 03-0177, Fig. S3â€ ) was generated in the pre-sample (labeled as the pre-RuO2â€“NiO/NF catalyst), and no characteristic peaks can be ascribed to the Ru species due to the low crystallinity. After annealing, the XRD pattern (Fig. 1b) verified the formation of NiO (JCPDS no. 04-0835) and RuO2 (JCPDS no. 21-1172). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed to characterize the morphology of the as-prepared catalyst. Fig. S4â€  displays that blank NF has a smooth surface, while a large number of nanocrystals can be observed in the pre-RuO2â€“NiO/NF catalyst (Fig. S5â€ ). For RuO2â€“NiO/NF, the as-formed hybrid nanocrystals are distributed uniformly on the surface of NF (Fig. 1c). Energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) mapping images show a uniform distribution of the Ni, O, and Ru elements on the NF (Fig. 1d). The nanocrystals were peeled off by ultrasonic treatment and then characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Fig. 1e shows that the catalyst is composed of a large number of nanoparticles (NPs). The corresponding high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image shows the lattice fringes with interplanar spacings of 0.208 and 0.241 nm (Fig. 1f), which is consistent with the (200) and (111) planes of fcc NiO, respectively. The interplanar spacing of 0.255 nm corresponds to the (101) plane of tetragonal RuO2. The (200) and (111) facets of NiO and the neighboring (101) facet of RuO2 constitute the interface of the heterostructured catalyst. EDX elemental mappings demonstrate the uniform distribution of Ni, O, and Ru elements within the NPs (Fig. 1g). The EDX spectrum of RuO2â€“NiO NPs reveals that the ratio of Ni to Ru is 20:1 (Fig. S6â€ ). All these results confirmed the formation of the heterostructured catalyst.

To gain more insight into the improvement of the catalytic activity, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) investigations of RuO2â€“NiO/NF were performed. ECSA was evaluated by the calculation of double-layer capacitance (Cdl) and the Cdl of RuO2â€“NiO/NF with different loading masses of RuO2, which is presented in Fig. S17.â€  The result shows that the Cdl enlarged with increasing loading amount of RuO2, indicating that the increase in RuO2 effectively enhanced the electrochemical active surface area. To elucidate the effect of the interfaces that exists in the RuO2â€“NiO heterostructure on its catalytic performance, an atomic model was constructed to simulate the hybrid catalyst and first-principle calculations were performed based on DFT (Fig. 4a).32,50 The HER process in alkaline electrolyte specifically involves two elementary steps, namely, the dissociation of H2O and the subsequent associative desorption of H2. Fig. 4b shows the calculated free energy diagram of H2O dissociation on RuO2, which is an endothermic process with a large energy barrier of 0.36 eV. In contrast, the dissociation barrier of H2O at the RuO2â€“NiO interface is drastically reduced to 0.09 eV, indicating the significant effect of NiO in promoting H2O dissociation and substantially accelerating the production of H*. Î”GH* is generally used as an important feature to evaluate the activity for HER. An ideal catalyst should possess a moderate H adsorption energy near zero, leading to optimal HER performance due to the equilibrium between the adsorption/desorption steps.70,71 The optimized structures with the adsorption of H on the catalysts and the computed HER free energy plots are presented in Fig. 4c, S18, and S19.â€  The RuO2â€“NiO hybrid shows a lower Î”GH* than NiO and RuO2, suggesting that the generated interface could significantly improve its HER activity. Typically, OER undergoes a four-electron step process.70,72 The optimized structures of NiO, RuO2, and the RuO2â€“NiO hybrid with the adsorption of OOH, OH, and O intermediates are presented in Fig. 4d, S20, and S21, respectively.â€ Fig. 4e presents the calculated free energy profiles of the above catalysts with different applied potentials. At the potential of 0 V, the rate-determining step (RDS) for NiO is the conversion of OH* to O* due to its largest reaction free energy (Î”G) of 2.26 eV, corresponding to a theoretical overpotential (Î·) of 1.03 V. While the RDS for RuO2 is the conversion of O* to OOH* (Î”G = 2.06 eV, Î· = 0.83 V), which agree well with the reported calculation results.32,36,73,74 Notably, the value of reaction free energy dropped to 1.89 eV for RuO2â€“NiO, thus reducing the theoretical overpotential to 0.66 V. RuO2â€“NiO needs a lower potential than that of RuO2 and NiO to make all the steps become downhill. The decrease in the required potential demonstrates that the RuO2â€“NiO heterostructure facilitates the oxygen evolution process. The differential charge density of the interfacial model is illustrated in Fig. 4f, which shows that there is a redistribution of the charge density near the RuO2â€“NiO interface. Electron depletion on the Ru sites while accumulation near the O atoms would weaken H adsorption in O and thus be beneficial to the HER process.75Fig. 4g presents the projected density of states (PDOS) of NiO, RuO2, and RuO2â€“NiO. Unlike NiO with a wide band-gap, RuO2â€“NiO is intrinsically metallic, indicating an enhanced electrical conductivity.74 Compared with RuO2, the d-band centers (Îµd) of the Ru d orbital in RuO2â€“NiO shifts to a low-energy level, suggesting weakened interaction between the adsorbed oxygen species and the Ru site, thus favoring the OER process.14,36,62,76,77 Overall, it could be expected that constructing the RuO2â€“NiO hybrid interface could modulate the electronic structure, thus optimizing the adsorption energy of the intermediates in the Volmer and Heyrovsky steps for HER and accelerating the whole kinetic process for OER, which is conducive for the water-splitting process.

According to the experiment and theoretical analysis, the outstanding electrocatalytic performance of RuO2â€“NiO/NF could be attributed to the following: (i) the coupling effect of RuO2â€“NiO significantly improved the intrinsic activity of the catalyst. As illustrated in Fig. 5g, the RuO2â€“NiO interface could effectively promote H2O dissociation and optimize the Î”GH*, which greatly improved the HER performance. The d-band center of the Ru d orbital in the RuO2â€“NiO heterostructure shifted to a low-energy level compared to that of RuO2, which weakened the interaction between the adsorbed oxygen species and the Ru site, thus favoring the OER process. (ii) Constructing the heterointerface of RuO2â€“NiO could effectively modulate the conductivity so as to achieve a lower electrochemical impedance, and could also provide abundant catalytic active sites to increase the catalytic area. (iii) The homogeneous distribution of tiny RuO2 on NiO increased its utilization efficiency and the in situ fabrication process of RuO2â€“NiO/NF using NiO extracted from the support led to its superior stability.

Conclusions

In summary, we developed a facile strategy that could scale-up the synthesis of bifunctional RuO2â€“NiO/NF catalysts with outstanding performance for both OER and HER in alkaline media. The corresponding electrolyzer only needs a cell voltage of 1.43 and 1.66 V to achieve a current density of 10 and 500 mA cmâˆ’2 for full water splitting, respectively. RuO2â€“NiO/NF also presents excellent durability for long-term electrolysis at high current densities. Moreover, DFT calculations revealed that the d-band centers of the Ru d orbital in the RuO2â€“NiO heterostructure shifted to a low-energy level compared to that of RuO2, which weakened the interaction between the adsorbed oxygen species and the Ru site, thus favoring the OER process.
