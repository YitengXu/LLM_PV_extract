Abstract

An efficient electrocatalyst for the reduction of polysulfide electrolytes is vital to the construction of quantum dot sensitized solar cells (QDSCs). Herein, Co,N-bidoped carbon nanomaterials, prepared simply via the pyrolysis of bimetallic (Zn and Co) zeolite-type-metal organic frameworks, were for the first time attempted as electrocatalysts to develop counter electrodes (CEs) for QDSCs. The CEs developed exhibit superior catalytic activity for polysulfide reduction in QDSCs, resulting in a low charge transfer resistance at the interface of the CE/electrolyte, an improved fill factor (FF) and a high short circuit current density (Jsc). The outstanding performances of the CEs can be ascribed to the inherent characteristics of Co,N-bidoped carbons with homogeneously dispersed active dopants of Co and N atoms, large hydrophilic surface area, and good conductivity. Moreover, density functional theory (DFT) indicated that the Coâ€“Nx sites would be active sites for polysulfide reduction. When Co,N-bidoped carbons deposited on F-doped tin oxide glass were used as CEs, an impressive power conversion efficiency of 9.12% (Voc = 0.635 V, Jsc = 26.15 mA cmâˆ’2, FF = 0.549) under one sun illumination with 100 mW cmâˆ’2 intensity was observed on QDSCs using Znâ€“Cuâ€“Inâ€“Se QDs as sensitizers. Consequently, there is a good chance to fabricate QDSCs of high efficiency with the CEs derived from MOFs.

1. Introduction

Due to low cost solution processability and high theoretical power conversion efficiency (PCE) up to 44%, quantum dot sensitized solar cells (QDSCs) have become one of the most promising candidates for new generation solar cells.1â€“6 Liquid-junction QDSCs typically consist of a QD sensitized mesoporous TiO2 photoanode, an electrolyte containing a polysulfide redox couple, and a counter electrode (CE) with a catalyst supported on conducting substrates.7â€“9 The CE is responsible for transferring electrons from the external circuit to the electrolyte through catalytic reduction of oxidized species in the electrolyte, affecting substantially the photovoltaic performance of a cell device.10â€“13

With superior electrocatalytic activity toward polysulfide redox couples, metal chalcogenides especially copper sulfides are the most commonly used catalysts in CEs.11,14â€“17 However, copper sulfides dissociated from CEs may cling to the photoanode surface, consequently poisoning the photoanode and deteriorating the photovoltaic performance.18 Moreover, the poor long-term stability and relatively low surface area also hinder their performance as CEs.15,19 Owing to the good electrochemical catalytic activity, sufficient electrical conductivity, high durability and low cost, carbon materials are now considered as competitive candidates for new electrode materials in QDSCs.8,20â€“24 A series of pure carbons, such as mesoporous carbon, graphene, carbon nanofiber and foam, have been studied and confirmed to be effective for polysulfide reduction.8,12,20â€“24 Remarkably, N doped mesoporous carbon has exhibited better catalytic activity, pushing the PCE of QDSCs to a new level recently in our lab,25 which preliminarily reveals the potential application of heteroatom doped carbon in QDSCs. It is noteworthy that pure or light heteroatom-doped carbon catalysts reported for QDSCs are generally hydrophobic,20â€“26 which prevents the carbon materials from making full use of their advantage on highly specific surfaces and coming in contact with polysulfide aqueous electrolyte in the same plane.27â€“29 To further improve the photovoltaic performance of QDSCs based on carbons, the development of catalysts with comprehensive properties of both superior catalytic activity and good hydrophilicity to polysulfide redox media is therefore highly desired.

Being a new class of crystalline porous materials, metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs)30,31 with diversified and tailorable microstructures recently have been proven to be versatile precursors/sacrificial templates for the preparation of various homogeneous hetero-doping carbons with predetermined functionalities via the thermolysis process.32â€“41 Different from other precursors, both non-metallic elements (such as N and S from organic ligands) and metal ions are regularly arranged in the three dimensional framework structure of MOFs (as shown in Scheme 1).42â€“46 After pyrolysis, these non-metallic and metallic elements could be uniformly dispersed in the carbon skeletons.40,43â€“45 In the presence of a large amount of active heteroatoms, the electrical conductivity and surface polarity of carbons can be significantly enhanced. Moreover, the originally hydrophobic carbons become hydrophilic, which is crucial to the heterogeneous catalytic reactions occurring in a water system.47 Notably, the partly preserved high porosity and long-range ordering structure of MOFs may lead to the formation of porous carbon nanomaterials with high specific surface areas.48â€“51 Until now, porous carbon materials derived from MOFs have been extensively studied and demonstrated to be excellent catalysts for many reactions, such as the oxygen reduction reaction and oxygen evolution reaction.52â€“60 Unfortunately, no relevant studies on MOF-derived carbons as the catalysts in QDSC CEs have been reported in the literature, although MOFs are potentially ideal precursors to prepare carbon materials with all desired characteristics for the catalysts in QDSCs.

Herein, for the first time, Co and N co-doped porous carbon nanomaterials were prepared for high performance catalysts in QDSCs. By utilizing a series of zeolite-type-MOFs as precursors for pyrolysis, the derived porous carbons exhibit not only large specific surface area and high conductivity, but also homogeneous multi-doping and excellent hydrophilicity that have not been achieved simultaneously using other precursors. Owing to these advantages, the obtained carbons exhibit superior catalytic performance toward polysulfide electrolytes. Impressively, among the carbonaceous catalysts deposited on a FTO glass substrate reported thus far, the highest PCE for QDSCs based on Co,N-bidoped carbons of 9.12% was observed in this work.

3. Results and discussion

Preparation and morphology characterization

A series of Co,Zn-bimetallic zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF) precursors (denoted as ZIF-n, n indicates the molar ratio of Co/Zn; n = 0 and 1 related to ZIF-8 and ZIF-67, respectively) were prepared by mixing Zn(NO3)2 and/or Co(NO3)2 with 2-methylimidazole in methanol according to the literature method.34 The ZIF nanoparticles obtained were further pyrolyzed at 900 Â°C, followed by sulfuric acid treatment to produce the resultant Co,N-bidoped carbon nanomaterials (denoted as CoNâ€“C-n, n means the same as that of ZIF-n; n = 0 and 1 relate to the carbons derived from ZIF-8 and ZIF-67, respectively). Notably, Zn ions in the framework of MOFs are converted to ZnO first and then reduced by carbons to give Zn metal during carbonization. Since Zn is evaporated under the experimental conditions, no Zn metal or Zn dopant is left in the resultant heteroatom-doped carbons.35,41,55 For comparison, MOF derived porous carbons without the heteroatom dopant (denoted as C-MOF-5) were also synthesized using MOF-5 as the precursor.47 In addition, both mesoporous carbon (denoted as MC) and N-doped carbon (denoted as N-MC), exhibiting the best photovoltaic performances in recent QDSC related studies,21,25,26 were also prepared by the conventional carbonization of organics.65

The ZIF-0.035 precursor and its corresponding carbon nanomaterial were chosen as representatives to investigate the morphology change before and after pyrolysis. From the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images shown in Fig. 1a, certain amount of Co metal is encapsulated inside the initial carbon materials derived from ZIF-0.035 (denoted as Co@CoNâ€“C-0.035). As can be seen from Fig. 1b, Co metal encapsulated however can be mostly removed by acid treatment, which can also be demonstrated by X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) spectra (Fig. S1 in the ESIâ€ ). The peak at 51Â° for the Co@CoNâ€“C-0.035 sample, corresponding to Co metal diffraction of (200), disappears after acid treatment. Despite the removal of Co metal by acid treatment, the resultant CoNâ€“C-0.035 still has a similar size distribution (ca. 60 nm) to that of Co@CoNâ€“C-0.035. In addition, from the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images illustrated in Fig. S2â€  the resultant CoNâ€“C-0.035 nanomaterials are well dispersed and exhibit similar polyhedral morphology to the corresponding ZIF-0.035 precursors, indicating that the carbons obtained preserve the morphology of the ZIF precursors and no aggregation occurs during the pyrolysis process and acid treatment.

The CoNâ€“C-n based CEs were developed via screen printing for the construction of QDSCs.61 Although carbon catalysts supported on Ti mesh as CEs have recently led to the highest PCE record of QDSCs,25 the CEs fabricated suffer from the severe leakage of the polysulfide electrolyte in a few minutes, resulting in the rapid performance deterioration of the QDSCs constructed. Taking this into consideration, the conductive F-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass was employed herein as the support to evaluate the electrocatalytic activity of the carbons obtained. With FTO supported CoNâ€“C-n and the reference carbons (C-MOF-5 derived from MOF-5, MC, N-MC and commercial activated carbon (AC)) as CEs, QDSC devices were fabricated using Znâ€“Cuâ€“Inâ€“Se QDs as the sensitizer and a polysulfide water solution as the electrolyte. The optimized thickness of carbon film layers in the CE was confirmed to be 3 layers (see Fig. S3 and Table S1â€ ), and in the following discussion, all the CEs investigated are based on the optimized thickness.

Efficient working mechanism of Co,N-bidoped carbon based CEs

From the high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image (the inset of Fig. 1b), the presence of abundant graphitic lattice fringes with a d-spacing of 3.4 Ã… corresponding to the (002) plane of graphitic carbon can be observed, indicating the high degree of graphitization of carbons derived from ZIFs. Such a result is further confirmed by the XRD spectra as shown in Fig. S5.â€  For all CoNâ€“C-n samples, two carbon diffractions of (002) and (101) located at 26Â° and 44Â° can be found. The degree of graphitization is effectively enhanced by the increase in the doping content of Co, which can be ascribed to the catalytic ability of Co atoms toward the graphitization of carbon. The positive influence of the presence of Co on graphitization is also reflected by a lower ID/IG value in Raman spectra (Fig. S6â€ ). The smaller ID/IG the value, the better the graphitization.37,69 The ratio of the intensities of the two peaks at 1350 (ID) and 1590 cmâˆ’1 (IG) decreases with the increase in Co content. Notably, the high degree of graphitization of the resultant Co,N-bidoped carbons guarantees the conductivity of the CE that is beneficial to the charge transport to the active catalyst sites from the conductive FTO substrate.

Elemental mapping, whose results are illustrated in Fig. S7â€ , was conducted for CoNâ€“C-0.035 samples, confirming that N and Co are dispersed uniformly in the carbon matrix prepared. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis was then applied to confirm the form in which Co and N-doping atoms are present. As shown in Fig. 5a, there are four peaks of C 1s, N 1s, O 1s, and Co 2p located at 284.6, 400.1, 531.4, and 780.5 eV, respectively. In the high-resolution Co 2p spectra shown in Fig. 5b, a peak at 782.5 eV corresponding to Coâ€“Nx can be found.34 It should be noted that there is no characteristic peak for the Co metal. The results demonstrate that Co species might mainly be anchored on the carbon network, existing in the form of the Coâ€“Nx moiety. As for the high-resolution N 1s spectra of CoNâ€“C-0.035 shown in Fig. 5c, peaks at 401.4, 400.5, 398.5, and 399.2 eV, corresponding to graphitic-N, pyrrolic-N, pyridinic-N and Coâ€“Nx, respectively,34,70,71 can be observed (Table S3â€ ). It has been verified that Coâ€“Nx, graphitic-N and pyridinic-N are all of high activity in many catalytic reactions.34,36,47,55,70,71

It is well-known that surface wettability of catalysts plays a crucial role in the interactions between heterogeneous catalysts and reactants, consequently directly affecting their activity.47 To examine the wettability of CoNâ€“C-n by water, CoNâ€“C-n and reference carbons were dispersed in a mixture of ethyl acetate and water solution (the upper part is the ethyl acetate phase and the bottom is the water phase), as shown in Fig. 6a. Obviously, CoNâ€“C-n can be well dispersed in the water phase, indicating the hydrophilic nature of the doped carbons derived from ZIFs. By contrast, the hydrophobic reference carbon materials, C-MOF-5, MC, N-MC and AC, prepared from the pyrolysis of organics, can only stay in the ethyl acetate phase or accumulate at the interface. The hydrophilicity of Co,N-bidoped carbons prepared by the carbonization of ZIFs has been ascribed to the existence of graphitic-N that induces the permanent polarization between graphitic-N and adjacent C and thus facilitates the interaction between N species and nucleophilic water molecules.47 Moreover, the porosity of the obtained CoNâ€“C-n was characterized by N2 adsorption at 77 K (Fig. 6b). With the increase in Co dopants, the Brunauerâ€“Emmettâ€“Teller (BET) surface areas of the corresponding carbon materials decrease obviously (Table S4â€ ), which is consistent with literature results.34 It is expected that the large hydrophilic surface area as well as hierarchical pore structure of the Co,N-bidoped carbons prepared (Fig. S8 and Table S4â€ ) guarantees rapid diffusion of the electrolyte into the pores of catalysts and the subsequent exposure of active sites to reactant species, thus facilitating the catalytic reaction at the CE surface.

Regarding the fact that the BET surface areas of the heteroatom-doped carbons from CoNâ€“C-0 to CoNâ€“C-0.035 decrease with the increase in Co dopant ratio, the enhanced performance of Co,N-bidoped carbon based QDSCs could possibly result from the increase in Coâ€“Nx active sites that exhibit excellent catalytic ability in polysulfide reduction. To verify Coâ€“Nx of Co,N-bidoped carbon as possible active sites for efficient CE materials, density functional theory (DFT) calculations were also performed to understand the polysulfide reduction reactions (SRRs) occurring on the CE.

Since the exact species involved in the electrode reaction and reaction mechanisms appear to be rather complicated and still remain elusive for SRRs, all the possible dissociative reactions starting from S42âˆ’ and S32âˆ’ which occupy the overwhelmingly dominant in concentration72 were computationally investigated. 4. Conclusions

In conclusion, homogeneously Co,N-bidoped porous carbons were for the first time attempted as electrocatalysts in QDSCs. A series of zeolite-like MOFs were employed as precursors to synthesize Co,N-bidoped carbon nanomaterials CoNâ€“C-n via carbonization and subsequent acid treatment. The CoNâ€“C-n obtained were deposited on FTO glass via screen printing to develop CEs for the construction of QDSCs. It was found that the Co,N-bidoped carbons have higher electrocatalytic activity toward polysulfide reduction than reference carbons, such as pure mesoporous carbons derived from MOF-5, N-doped carbons from ZIF-8, MC and N-doped MC derived from organics as well as the commercial active carbons. DFT methods further confirm that the Coâ€“Nx active sites are beneficial for the electrocatalytic performance of CEs. The superior performance of CoNâ€“C-n CEs could be attributed to the advantages as follows: abundant Co and N dopants providing plentiful active sites; a large hydrophilic surface area ensuring thorough exposure of active sites to polysulfide species; a high degree of graphitization guaranteeing the electrical conductivity of CEs and consequently promoting the charge carrier transport. With the CoNâ€“C-0.035 based CE, PCE up to 9.12% was observed on the Znâ€“Cuâ€“Inâ€“Se QDSCs, significantly higher than that of the reported QDSCs based on carbonaceous materials supported on FTO.
