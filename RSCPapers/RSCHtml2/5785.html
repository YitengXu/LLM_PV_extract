Abstract

All-inorganic perovskite solar cells (PSCs) are attracting considerable attention due to their excellent thermal stability. However, their unsatisfactory power-conversion efficiency and moisture instability lead to challenges for practical commercialization. Here, a facile but effective strategy is demonstrated to stabilize/passivate the cubic phase of CsPbI3 by inhibiting the moisture invasion using 18-crown-6 ether (Crown). The strong interaction between Crown and surface Cs+ ion not only effectively improved the moisture tolerance, but also simultaneously reduced surface defects and related migrations, thus accordingly improving the phase and operation stability of the CsPbI3 films and devices. Based on the obtained high-quality CsPbI3âCrown films, PSCs with up to 16.9% efficiency were fabricated. The devices readily maintained â¼91% of the initial PCE for more than 1000 h (85 Â°C, N2) and even â¼90% for up to 2000 h (relative humidity 20%) without encapsulation. More importantly, the developed strategy has been successfully used to fabricate large-area CsPbI3 modules, showing an efficiency of up to 11.8% with the 16 cm2 total area. A stability performance that can retain â¼95% of the original value for over 500 h (operating conditions, relative humidity 60%) with encapsulation has also been achieved in the first PSC module based on the CsPbI3 system.

Introduction

With the rapid development in successful fabrication of high-quality perovskite films,1,2 perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have become promising candidates towards next-generation low-cost and efficient photovoltaics. Recently, a record power conversion efficiency (PCE) of up to 25.2% has been achieved in lab-scale hybrid PSCs,3 making the practical applications and even commercialization of PSCs more and more feasible. However, the instability of organicâinorganic hybrid PSCs still remains unsolved due to the volatility of their organic components that often induce degradation of devices under moisture, light or thermal conditions.4â9 Owing to the superior thermal stabilities over organohalide perovskites,10â12 all-inorganic perovskites have been attracting increasing research attention.13 Among them, cesium lead iodide (denoted as CsPbI3), possessing a high charge mobility and suitable bandgap for solar spectrum absorption in its cubic phase (Î± phase), is considered to be an excellent candidate for fabricating efficient all-inorganic PSCs.14

Unfortunately, due to the intrinsic structural instability of Î±-CsPbI3 with a relatively low tolerance factor,15 phase transformation to âyellowâ morphology (Î´-phase) always undesirably occurs during the fabrication process and the subsequent device applications, which strongly limits the preparation of efficient and stable inorganic PSCs. Despite the fact that various strategies, such as growth control,16 composition engineering,17â19 and fabrication of quantum dots20â22 or 2D/3D hybrid structures,23,24 have been employed to grow high-quality Î± phase CsPbI3 films for high-performance devices, it should be pointed out that most of these methods were still limited to being performed in an inert atmosphere and with complicated procedures, making it still challenging to scale up the fabrication process. In addition, how to stabilize CsPbI3-based PSCs under ambient working conditions is another big challenge. For the as-prepared Î±-phase CsPbI3 films, the moisture assisted polymorphic transition (Î±-to-Î´ phase) has been well acknowledged to be the dominant route for material inactivation for photovoltaics.15,25 Moreover, defect migration, which was believed to be induced by ion migration on the surface and within grain boundaries of perovskite films,26â29 was considered to be another important factor for long-term instability, especially under operating conditions.30 Until now, several attempts, such as coating with hydrophobic polymers and bulk organic cations, have been made to restrain moisture from attacking perovskites to improve the stability and efficiency of corresponding devices.31â34 However, the defect migration related instability still has not been considered in inorganic perovskite systems for most of the studies, although varieties of methods have been used to alleviate ion migration to reduce defects and charge recombination in hybrid perovskite films.35â38 And the currently reported CsPbI3-based PSCs also still suffered from degradation and have not yet been stable enough under real air ambient conditions.39,40 Therefore, exploring a simple and effective method to produce and stabilize high-quality CsPbI3 perovskite films represents a fundamental urgency for high-performance inorganic PSC modules toward potential industrialization.

Here, we develop a novel strategy to use 1,4,7,10,13,16-hexaoxacyclooctadecane ether (i.e. 18-crown-6, denoted as Crown) for suppressing water invasion and passivating defects, and thus facilitate the stabilization of Î±-CsPbI3 thin films for PSCs. In the developed method, a unique compound, PbI2 pre-coordinated with N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone in solid form (denoted as PbI2Â·NMP), was used to assist the formation of regular, pinhole-free and high-quality Î±-CsPbI3 films. The effective inhibition of moisture invasion and phase transformation was well accomplished by the Crown terminated CsPbI3 films, in which the methylene (âCH2) outer structure of Crown prevented water attack, and the inner cavity strongly bonded with surface Cs+ ions to passivate surface defects. The simultaneously suppressed defect migration due to the strong anchoring of Crown to perovskites via bonding with Cs+ ions was also evidenced to be highly responsible for the significantly improved operation stability. Consequently, together with excellent long-term storage and working and thermal stabilities, the corresponding PSCs successfully achieved a maximum efficiency of 16.9%. Moreover, large-area (16 cm2) PSC modules based on Crown-engineered CsPbI3 films were readily constructed and had a champion efficiency over 11.8%. The encapsulated PSC module predictably demonstrated an attractive stability which can maintain 95.2% of its original value for over 500 h under operating conditions at RH â¼60%.

Crown passivation enabled stable Î±-CsPbI3 films and devices

In this work, crown ether was preferably introduced to passivate CsPbI3 perovskites and thus effectively inhibit the moisture attack to avoid phase transformation not only during ambient air storage but also under operation conditions (Fig. 2a). Crown is a well-known chelating agent, containing an inner cavity that can bond strongly and selectively with Cs+ ions (Fig. S6 and S7â ),42 while the Crown-terminated CsPbI3 exhibits a methylene (âCH2) outer structure (Fig. S8â ) on the surface, helping to enhance the moisture tolerance of CsPbI3 and thus inhibit the phase transformation. The surface defects of CsPbI3 perovskites would also be expected to be effectively passivated by the surface modification like other introduced organic species.34 Thus, by introducing Crown engineering, overall improvement in the stability and photovoltaic performance of Crown-passivated CsPbI3 PSCs should be expected.

The characterization results of XRD patterns, UV-vis, and PL spectra (Fig. S9 and S10â ) well evidenced obviously higher optical properties and crystal quality in the Crown treated CsPbI3 films (denoted as CsPbI3âCrown) as compared to the CsPbI3 films. The CsPbI3âCrown and CsPbI3 perovskite films both displayed similar morphologies, but their properties were strongly dependent on the Crown concentration. The optimized amount of Crown was demonstrated to be 1 mg mLâ1 when used for the treatment (Fig. S11âS13 and Table S2â ). Here, the Î±-phase structure of CsPbI3 was evidently identified by HRTEM (Fig. 2b) for the crystalline domain of the Crown terminated CsPbI3 film. A well-resolved inter-planar distance of 3.1 Ã, corresponding to the (200) plane of CsPbI3 consistent with the XRD results (Fig. S14aâ ), was clearly detected. Considering the moisture-sensitive nature of CsPbI3 films, the improved film quality should understandably originate from the well maintained intrinsic crystal structure after the surface passivation.

Moreover, the CsPbI3âCrown PSCs presented an average PCE of RS 15.8% with a narrower distribution in comparison with CsPbI3 PSCs (RS 11.7%) (Table S3â ), suggesting the excellent reproducibility of the Crown-passivated devices (Fig. 2e). As expected, not only a stable efficiency output but also a fast photo-response due to the improved film quality with highly efficient charge extraction and lower charge recombination losses in the CsPbI3âCrown device were achieved (Fig. S17â ). As illustrated in Fig. 2g, the CsPbI3âCrown PSCs showed obviously improved operating stability and retained 15.3% efficiency (91% of the original value) after 600 min. These results allowed us to conclude that Crown passivation did effectively lead to high performance CsPbI3 PSCs with synergistic improvements in efficiency, stability and reproducibility.

Engineering mechanism of Crown via the strong interaction

To gain insights into how the Crown passivation enhanced the performance of CsPbI3 PSCs, the interaction mechanism between Crown and CsPbI3 was investigated by 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. As shown in Fig. 3a, the proton resonance signal of Crown (peaked at 3.517 ppm) was shifted downfield to 3.540 ppm in CsPbI3âCrown, similar to the chemical shift of the CsIâCrown (3.537 ppm) as a reference (Fig. S18â ). The 13C NMR and infrared (IR) spectra (Fig. S19 and S20â ) also demonstrated the strong coordination-bonding interaction between CsPbI3 and Crown, which is consistent with the previous report.42 The 1H NMR-based quantitative analysis verified that the Crown is only â¼1 weight% of the CsPbI3âCrown film (Fig. S21â ). The bonding information was also quantitatively characterized by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Fig. 3b). The XPS doublet peaks of Cs 3d3/2 and Cs 3d5/2 were detected at the energy of 738.7 and 724.8 eV in the CsPbI3âCrown film. As compared to CsPbI3, both Cs3 d3/2 and Cs3 d5/2 peaks for CsPbI3âCrown were shifted to higher binding energies, similar to the shift from CsI to the CsIâCrown reference. The increased binding energy of Cs 3d caused by Crown bonding is ascribed to the interaction of Cs+ with O atoms on Crown.43 Meanwhile, the XPS doublet peaks of Pb 4f7/2 and Pb 4f5/2 were not shifted in CsPbI3âCrown films compared to those of CsPbI3 films, suggesting no obvious CrownâPb2+ interaction in this case (Fig. S22â ). On the basis of the above results, the anchoring of Crown chemically onto the CsPbI3 surface and the strong interaction with surface Cs+ ions were believed to be the main reasons for the stabilization of the CsPbI3 films and corresponding PSCs.

Stability improvement by the inhibition of defect migration

Besides the moisture inhibition and defect passivation discussed above, benefitting from the strong interaction of Cs+ ions with Crown, the suppression of electric-field induced defect migration was reasonably considered to be another highly responsible reason for the operation stability improvement of the inorganic PSCs in this work. To verify the proposed electric-field driving induced defect migration and the suppression effect by Crown engineering on CsPbI3, Kelvin probe force microscopy (KPFM) was employed to monitor local surface potentials (SP) in perovskite films with applying different bias voltages to the tip.29,45â47 It can be seen that at 0 V of bias voltage under dark conditions (Fig. 4a), the average SP value for the CsPbI3âCrown increased to â45 mV, higher than that of the bare CsPbI3 film (â197 mV), which strongly substantiated that Crown was uniformly anchored on the surface of the CsPbI3 film. With the applied bias increased, the voltage dependent average SP values (with error bar extracted from the SP images of a broad domain (16 Âµm2) in Fig. S26â ) on the CsPbI3âCrown film present no obvious fluctuation, showing a fairly stable surface state. In comparison, the CsPbI3 film displayed a general trend that average SP values increased with the decrease of negative bias voltage. This comparison suggested that the defect migration, possibly moved using the monovalent cations (Cs+) and halide ions as carriers,48 was effectively inhibited on the CsPbI3 surface by Crown passivation.

Mini-modules based on Crown engineered CsPbI3 films

According to the above results, Crown engineering has successfully achieved improvement in optoelectronic properties, macroscopic uniformity and moisture/operation stability for the inorganic CsPbI3 films. Thus, scalable fabrication of inorganic perovskite modules was expected considering their superior advantages over the mostly researched hybrid perovskite modules. With this concern, the protocol developed in this work was extended to prepare large area PSC modules with the structure of FTO/ZnOâZnS/m-TiO2-perovskite/Spiro-OMeTAD/Au. A widely used patterning strategy, laser-scribing technique, was applied to produce interconnection of four sub-cells with an overall size of 4 Ã 4 cm2 (16 cm2) (Fig. 5a and b). The individual sub-cell is separated by means of selective ablation of P1 to P3 lines. The optimized laser-patterned monolithic series interconnection of the sub-cells is completed by the contact of bottom (FTO) and top (Au) electrodes through the P2 line. As shown in Fig. 5c, the CsPbI3âCrown based PSC module with an active area of 8 cm2 exhibited a Jsc of 4.10 mA cmâ2, a Voc of 4.05 V, and a high FF of 71.45%, giving an overall PCE of RS 11.87%. In the FS scan, an efficiency of 11.25% with a Jsc of 4.01 mA cmâ2, a Voc of 4.00 V, and an FF of 70.32% was achieved (Table S6â ). Furthermore, the operation stability of encapsulated modules based on CsPbI3âCrown still retained 11.06% efficiency (95.2% of its origin PCE, 11.62%) over 500 h under maximum power point (MPP) tracking with continuous one-sun irradiation at RH 60% (Fig. 5d). The excellent reproducibility in the efficiencies of modules was verified according to the PCE value distribution (Fig. S33â ). We have also further demonstrated the modified blade coating method50 as a potential scaling up method to fabricate large-area CsPbI3 films, and then achieved RS 10.73% and FS 10.54% efficiencies based on a 16 cm2 module (Fig. S34â ). This represents the first demonstration of the feasibility of using all-inorganic perovskite films to realize PSC modules with decent performance, showing the great potential of this passivation strategy for the fabrication of stable and large-area PSCs. A detailed fair review on previously reported representative work of CsPbI3-based PSCs has also been listed in Table S7â  for comparison. Our work is currently among the best reported and presents an obvious improvement in module area and operation stability.

Conclusions

In summary, a coordination manipulation of a functionalized additive on CsPbI3 was proposed and successfully carried out through passivating and stabilizing the high-quality Î±-phase CsPbI3 derived from Crown and a PbI2Â·NMP compound. It was experimentally demonstrated that due to the strong interaction between Crown and surface Cs+ ions, the moisture invasion was effectively inhibited as well as the surface ion migration, thus improving the phase stability and reducing the defects in the cubic structured CsPbI3 films. Further results evidenced that the additionally suppressed defect migration via the Crown anchoring also strongly contributes to the obviously improved long-term stability of the films. Therefore, a champion efficiency of 16.9% was achieved in the correspondingly fabricated devices, and the first all-inorganic perovskite module with a large-area (16 cm2) was successfully fabricated with an efficiency of over 11.8% and excellent operation stability. This work will pave a straight-forward and convenient avenue to design high-performance inorganic or hybrid perovskite-based devices for practical applications, such as solar cells, light-emitting diodes, photodetectors, and scintillators.
