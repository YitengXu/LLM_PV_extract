Investigating the influence of charge transport on the performance of PTB7:PC71BM based organic solar cells

Abstract

A key challenge for researchers in the field of organic solar cells (OSCs) is to develop a physical model for a device that correctly describes the charge carrier transport phenomenon. In this article, an analytical study on the charge carrier transport phenomenon in an OSC is reported, which expresses a balance between free charge carrier generation and recombination in low mobility PTB7:PC71BM blend layers. First, the current densityvoltage (JV) data for the fabricated OSC were extracted from experiments by varying the incident power light intensity (IPL) and then analysis through theoretical simulation was used to quantify the dominant interface recombination parameters limiting the devices performance. It was found that although the generation of free charge carriers increased at higher IPL values, the performance of the device remained low due to poor electrical transport properties which resulted in a considerable accumulation of generated charge carriers in the active layer. Therefore, it has become important to work out the complex relation between charge carrier mobility, exciton-recombination dynamics and the overall electrical performance parameters in a single framework. This article explains the influence of incident power light intensity and charge carrier mobility on performance parameters, which limits the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of the OSC. The presented analysis could be helpful in optimizing the architecture of future devices to increase the PCE of OSCs.

1. Introduction

Organic solar cells (OSCs) grab headlines with their enormous advantages including being light-weight, flexible and easy-to-fabricate.14 Therefore, researchers have been putting significant R & D effort into the development of this technology in order to increase both the power conversion efficiency (PCE) and the stability of OSCs.58 The ultimate PCE of an OSC is estimated to be about 29% using the ShockleyQueisser (SQ) theory.9 Yet the assumptions of the SQ theory are not applicable to OSCs due to their complex structure.1012 In OSCs, organic polymeric electron donor and electron acceptor materials are used to develop a nanoscale network known as a bulk heterojunction (BHJ) with the goal of simultaneously optimizing photon absorption, exciton generation, exciton dissociation and charge carrier collection.1317 The morphology of the donor/acceptor blends critically affects the photovoltaic processes in BHJ OSCs.

A detailed physical understanding of organic semiconducting devices is required for the further development of efficient OSCs. Devices made from the newly developed donor and acceptor materials have achieved a PCE exceeding 14%.18 A PCE of up to 20% is possible if an appropriate electronic structure of materials in a suitable device design can be developed.1921 Accordingly, several theoretical frameworks have been put forward which mainly consider the losses caused by geminate recombination21,22 which limits the short-circuit current density (JSC) or reduces the fill factor (FF) and the open-circuit voltage (VOC) due to non-radiative recombination pathways.23,24 However, recent experimental and theoretical work2528 concludes that the performance of different polymer:fullerene blends is largely affected by inefficient charge extraction caused by low mobility, in particular for systems with effective charge generation and/or a large active layer thickness. In most of the better performing OSCs, the influence of geminate recombination is less dominant compared to bimolecular recombination, where the latter plays a vital role.29 The recombination and extraction of charge carriers are balanced by the optical generation rate for a given illumination intensity. The net photocurrent is the difference between the extraction and recombination of charge carriers through which the FF of the device can be estimated.3032 Several studies have been reported which combine charge transport and recombination in terms of a mobility-lifetime product.3337 In this direction of work, an analytical model has been developed which utilizes the effect of drift, diffusion and trapping of photo-induced charge carriers.38 The model explains the electrical performance of OSCs which fits well with experimental JV curves assuming a constant lifetime of electrons and holes in the photoactive layer. Also, the 1-dimensional (1D) drift-diffusion model developed by Set et al. includes the effect of second order recombination on the performance parameters.39 Understanding these models, Ibrahim et al. established a detailed mathematical relationship to describe the role of bimolecular recombination rates on the performance of OSCs.40 These expressions include three empirical parameters for optimizing the bimolecular recombination rate which provides the best fits for experimental JV curves. However, to gain a deep insight into achieving high FFs as well as PCE values, there is a need to develop a systematic theoretical model which explains the strong dependence of the generation and transportation of free charge carriers on the incident power light intensity (IPL) and the effective charge carrier mobility.

Therefore, this analysis mainly aims to evaluate the charge transport limited performance of OSCs by realizing the dependence of the generation and transportation of free charge carriers on the IPL, as well as the effective charge carrier mobility. The empirical relationship derived for JV curves includes the effects of charge carrier mobility, IPL, and exciton and recombination dynamics in a single framework to achieve a maximum photovoltaic performance. Firstly, the dependence of the electrical performance parameters (JSC, VOC and FF) on a broad range of IPL (10 to 220 mW cm2) for a fabricated device has been studied by incorporating the dimensionless parameter ,41 which allows quantification of the recombination and extraction rates of the charge carriers. Furthermore, the relationship between electrical performance parameters, effective charge carrier mobility and recombination parameters has been worked out. The impact of the charge carrier mobility and the recombination factor on the PCE of the OSC has been clearly demonstrated. This study can help the research community simulate device performance more accurately depending on the opto-electronic properties of organic semiconductors. The approach presented here establishes device physics, which not only applies to the obtained experimental results of the fabricated OSCs in our laboratory but also to other OSCs and novel thin film technologies like perovskite solar cells used elsewhere.

2. Experimental

To investigate the photovoltaic performance with a charge transport limited current, the conventional device structure (ITO/PEDOT:PSS/PTB7:PC71BM/Al) was fabricated using a blend of PTB7:PC71BM with a blend ratio of 1:1.5.

2.1 Materials

The materials for the device fabrication were purchased and used as received from the following companies; poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene)polystyrene sulfonate (PEDOT:PSS; Clevios PH), poly[[4,8-bis[(2-ethylhexyl)oxy]benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b]dithiophene-2,6-diyl][3-fluoro-2-[(2-ethylhexyl)carbonyl]thieno[3,4-b]thiophenediyl]] (PTB7; 1-materials, Canada), [6,6]-phenyl C71 butyric acid methyl ester (PC71BM; 1-materials, Canada), chlorobenzene (Sigma-Aldrich), and 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO; Sigma-Aldrich).

2.2 Device fabrication and characterization

As usual for device fabrication, the ITO glass substrates (sheet resistance = 15 1) were initially cleaned in soap solution, acetone, isopropanol, and exposed to ultraviolet-ozone irradiation for 20 min. PEDOT:PSS was spin-coated over the ITO glass at 3500 rpm for 60 s and annealed at 140 C for 10 min. Then, a 1:1.5 blend ratio of PTB7:PC71BM was spin-coated in a mixed solvent of chlorobenzene/DIO (97%:3% by volume) solution (with a total concentration of 25 mg ml1) at 1500 RPM for 40 s. Finally, an Al electrode was thermally deposited on the active layer under high vacuum (5 106 mbar). The active area of the devices was 4.5 mm2 in every case. The current densityvoltage (JV) characteristics of the fabricated devices were measured under a solar simulator (class AAA) with a Keithley2420 source meter unit to vary the IPL (9 to 220 mW cm2). To measure the current density under illumination, the device was illuminated from the transparent ITO electrode side.

Fig. 2(a) shows the experimental JV characteristics of the fabricated OSC under various IPL values (symbols) from which the VOC can be found (Table 2). The extracted VOC value is plotted with respect to IPL in Fig. S1 (ESI) and the slope results in n = 1.1 (see Fig. S1 in the ESI). This value of n indicates strong bimolecular recombination in the fabricated OSC. However, a plot of JV using eqn (1) with n = 1.1 for varying IPL by including the effect of RS results in a very poor fit with the experimental data (Fig. S2 in the ESI). In particular, it is difficult to explain the interception of the JV curves and the strong illumination dependence of the forward current density using the Shockley equation. Here, JG is only the current density under IPL, which includes all generation and recombination processes and depends on the electronic properties of the semiconductor materials. However, materials used for OSC application have a low mobility. Because of this low mobility the extraction of free charge carriers remains insufficient and space charge regions build-up at the contacts or in the bulk. As a result, the free charge concentration at the donor/acceptor interface increases and hence recombination increases, which leads to a lower FF. Therefore, there is a need to include the effect of mobility on the low-mobility semiconductor material-based OSC, while analyzing the JV curve under illumination. The analysis of the JV curve fitted using eqn (1) does not provide information about the true ideality factor (except near VOC). In order to explore the possibility of space charge limited transport, a graph was plotted between J and the compensation voltage (VOC V) on a double-logarithmic scale (symbols) as shown in Fig. 2(b). A linear dependence of J has been observed at a low VOC V value due to competition between the drift and diffusion of free charge carriers and it shows a clear smooth transition to the saturation regime at higher voltages, where it becomes limited by the field and IPL dependence of the generation rate (G). Furthermore, as shown in Fig. 2(c), the experimental JSC (V = 0 V) value taken from Fig. 2a as a function of IPL has been plotted in a double logarithmic scale to check the space charge limited transport. The slope S (= 0.99) calculated by a linear fitting (shown in lines) of the experimental data confirms the absence of space charge limited transport in our fabricated PTB7:PC71BM system. However, the population of free charge carriers within the active layer is related to the difference between the internal voltage (Vinternal) and the applied external voltage between the two electrodes under varying IPL.41 The Shockley diode eqn (1) is only valid when Vinternal is equal to the applied voltage. For this condition, the applied external bias defines the density of the free charge carriers and the recombination rate in the active layer. This condition is realized for inorganic semiconductors with a high mobility, where charge collection is mainly by diffusion. However, an effective charge collection in the active layer materials with low mobility is driven by the gradient of quasi-Fermi levels. Hence, in this condition Vinternal does not remain the same as the applied external voltage (Fig. 2(d)). The space-charge limited transport (as discussed earlier) and the dependence of the free charge carriers on the quasi-Fermi levels within the active layer are well differentiated from each other. All of these issues have to be considered in more detail in order to analyze the performance of OSCs. It is thus inappropriate to employ Shockley's model, as it does not account for the impact of charge transport (it assumes infinitely large conductivities for electrons and holes).

For the study presented in previous paragraphs, the rate of recombination is assumed to be independent of the mobility. However, several studies12,30,41 have shown that the probability of charge carrier recombination is related to the effective mobility (eff) of the device. In addition, the induced recombination factor is introduced to explain the effect of morphology on the recombination in a phase separated blend. The parameter varies between 1 and 103 in OSCs.30 By considering these two parameters (eff and ), this study is extended to analyze the ultimate feasible PCE of the fabricated PTB7:PC71BM OSC with a better understanding of the recombination process as explained below. In order to visualize how eff and affect the device performance, JSC and VOC are plotted for a wide range of mobility with respect to the induced recombination factor () (Fig. 5(a and b)). Here, the value of JG is taken as 15 mA cm2 from the experimental data (Fig. 2(a)). At high eff (=104 to 100 m2 V1 s1) close to those of inorganic semiconductors (Si) or strong absorber material such as perovskites, the value of JSC reaches its maximum as shown in Fig. 5(a). In other words, all photogenerated charge carriers reach the electrodes and contribute to the photocurrent. These results show that at the higher eff values transport current loss is negligible due to the very low values of (Fig. S4 in the ESI). For instance, although the JSC reaches its maximum value at the higher eff values and isnt influenced by the high values, the FF and PCE of the device are limited due to the recombination process (Fig. S5a and S5b in the ESI). The reduction of the FF and PCE can also be explained on the basis of the reduction in VOC (Fig. 5(b)). Under open circuit conditions, the photocurrent density is zero due to the equal charge carrier generation and recombination rates. Recombination can occur either in the active layer bulk or at the active layer electrode interface. In this condition, VOC is derived through the splitting of quasi-Fermi levels, which is well explained by kR as a function of for a variation of eff. The plotted kR data (Fig. S6 in the ESI) reflect the dependence of the JSC, VOC, FF and PCE on the eff and as explained above. An increase in kr with increasing effective mobility leads to a steady decrease in VOC (Fig. 5(b)). As per this analysis, transport losses start to play an important role for a wide range of charge carrier mobility as reported in the literature for OSCs (from 1010 to 106 m2 V1 s1). As shown in Fig. S5b (ESI), if is equal to 103, the PCE of the cell drops only to 8.5% from 9.4% with a variation of eff from 100 to 1010 m2 V1 s1. It can be concluded that the deviation in FF and PCE of the JV curve for a PTB7:PC71BM OSC from a high value primarily results from the direct competition between the generation of excitons and the charge carrier dissociation and recombination during transport.

As a final result in Fig. 7 the calculated FF and PCE values are shown as a function of eff for different values. The plotted FFs and PCEs originate from the behavior of the JSC, VOC, P and RY as a function of eff and . It can be observed that for a given value, there is an optimum eff responsible for achieving a peak PCE. At lower values, a higher value of JSC and VOC with an efficient dissociation of charge carriers leads to higher FFs and PCEs. For example, decreasing from 1 to 0.001 increases the peak PCE from around 3% to 9.5%. By looking at Fig. 5(a) and 6, it is clear that a lower causes JSC and P to increase more rapidly with eff and provides a maximum value of PCE when the eff is around 108 m2 V1 s1 (see Fig. 5(b) and FF (Fig. 7(a))). Furthermore, our calculation matches well with the experimental highest efficiency achieved for the PTB7:PC71BM solar cell system at an effective carrier mobility of around 108 m2 V1 s1.30 Also, the impact of a mobility imbalance (between electrons and holes) on the performance of PTB7:PC71BM solar cells has been calculated and plotted as shown in Fig. S7 (ESI). The derived results clearly explain that an increase in electron mobility (e) by two orders of magnitude (1010 to 108 m2 V1 s1) with a constant hole mobility (h = 1010 m2 V1 s1) leads to an enhancement in the PCE with a higher FF, JSC and moderate VOC. But a further increase in the e leads to a greater imbalance between the extracted hole and electrons which ultimately increases the recombination and leads to a low PCE. As a result, not much improvement in PCE is expected when the effective mobility of charge carriers is further enhanced.

The results shown herein were obtained from a single PTB7:PC71BM device fabricated with a conventional structure, and experimental JV curves were analyzed using a charge transport model at varying incident power light intensity. Moreover, we extended these studies to include the effect of mobility and recombination factor on the performance of the fabricated device. The systematic simulation presented here explains the role of mobility and the recombination factor. As per the above discussed results, even one order of magnitude higher mobility as reported for PTB7:PC71BM systems is not going to enhance the device performance significantly due to the morphology dependent recombination mechanism. Thus, it is important to develop, synthesize and optimize the process of an organic blend which can enhance the performance with reduced recombination.

4. Conclusions

In essence, we have demonstrated an analytical approach to analyze the performance of an organic solar cell. The developed model explains the impact of charge transport on the current densityvoltage (JV) characteristics as a function of incident power light intensity (IPL) and the effective charge carrier mobility. The developed relationship between the performance parameters (JSC, VOC and FF) with IPL and the effective charge carrier mobility provides new insights for enhancing the performance of OSCs. The results discussed in this work offer an approach for targeted improvements in the performance of OSCs by indicating the right donoracceptor combinations with a suitable mobility range and a lower possibility of recombination.
