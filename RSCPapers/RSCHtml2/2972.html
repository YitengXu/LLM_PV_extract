Recent progress of dual-site catalysts in emerging electrocatalysis: a review

Abstract

Developing sustainable and clean electrochemical energy conversion technologies is a promising route to address energy scarcity and environmental pollution challenges, where electrocatalysts with high efficiency are of significance. To date, it is a huge challenge to prepare catalysts that facilitate multi-step reactions due to the difficulty in balancing the adsorption strength of multiple intermediates on a single catalytic site. Recently, owing to their multiple active centers and structural diversity, dual-site catalysts (DSCs), including catalysts containing heterointerfaces and heteroatoms, have attracted extensive interest to encourage multi-step reactions. Remarkable progress has been achieved by DSCs in the fields of electrocatalysis, such as hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen evolution reaction (OER), oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), and CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR). In this review, we provide a systematic summary of the development of DSCs in the past years, as well as their applications in typical catalytic reactions. We categorized the DSCs into heterostructure catalysts, elemental doping catalysts, and diatomic-site catalysts, focusing on the catalytic mechanism of different types of DSCs catalysts. This review is aiming to shed light on the physical and chemical properties of the emergent DSCs and gain insights into the synergistic effect within the dual site in close proximity, thus offering guidelines to the catalyst design for multiple reactions in the future.

1. Introduction

The ever-increasing demand for energy has led to massive worldwide consumption of fossil fuels, accompanied by concerns about their limited reserves and severe environmental problems.1–3 Searching for sustainable and renewable environment-friendly energy sources, such as solar and wind energy, is becoming one of the top priorities for the coming decades. However, the utilization of this clean energy is limited by its uneven spatial and temporal distribution, requiring efficient energy storage and conversion technologies. In this context, electrochemical technologies such as fuel cells, supercapacitors, and H2O or CO2 electrolysis systems are considered to be the most practical options to satisfy this energy storage and conversion requirements.4–7

Electrocatalysts are critical components in electrochemical energy storage and conversion technology, which can accelerate the slow reaction kinetics and reaction rates in catalytic processes.8–10 Therefore, rational design of electrocatalysts with high efficiency is of great significance, where a deep understanding of the active sites is required. In 1925, Taylor first proposed the active center theory and pointed out that the atoms located at the edges and top corners with unsaturated coordination are the potential active centers. However, for some catalytic reactions, it is still ambiguous about the active sites. The establishment of the active site has always occupied a crucial position, not only to improve the catalytic theory but also to provide guidance for the design of high-efficiency catalysts.11,12 Catalytic reactions, such as oxygen evolution reaction (OER), oxygen reduction reaction (ORR), hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in alkaline solutions, and CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR), generally go through multiple reaction steps with multiple intermediates involved.13–15 Nevertheless, balancing the adsorption/desorption ability toward these intermediates on one site is extremely difficult due to the scaling relationship between the binding energy of these intermediates on a specific site.16–18 Therefore, to diversify catalytic reactions, the key is to control the various intermediates separately, one of the most intriguing strategies is to create dual-site catalysts (DSCs) that feature distinct functions individually. For example, constructing different kinds of heterostructures, designing more complex diatomic systems based on single-atom catalysts, introducing heteroatoms, or forming alloys in which designed synergies can be used to improve catalytic activity and alter selectivity.

To date, tremendous progress has been reported on DSCs. Specifically, the development of single-atom catalysts has enriched the scope and connotations of DSCs substantially.19–21 Up to now, some reviews have been published regarding the developments of DSCs.22,23 Nevertheless, most of the DSCs-related reviews pay dominative attention to the single atom catalysts, where the two single atoms are loaded nearby to form the catalytic sites. However, the progress in other DSCs, including the new active sites provided by hetero-interfaces in heterostructures and hetero-domains in alloying catalysts, has been neglected.24–26 Indeed, the latter two have been the common types of DSCs for a long time. Therefore, a review that contains the emergence and variety of the DSCs is desired but not yet available.

Accordingly, in this review, we summarized the emergent progress of the DSCs with different types, aiming to provide an overview of the DSCs applied in energy-related electrocatalysis. Considering that there are multiple roles involved in the catalytic mechanisms of the DSCs, such as the electronic effects, stress effects, and spacing effects. This review focuses on the DSCs that provide new active sites for some intermediates in the reactions. We categorize the DSCs into the following three types (Fig. 1): 1) heterostructures, in which the heterointerfaces, containing dual components, are indeed the active sites for different reaction steps; 2) doping and alloying heteroatoms, which can provide synergetic effect between the support and the introduced dopants; 3) diatomic systems, the paired atoms in the close proximity form a dual-site catalytic domain. Then, the practical applications of the DSCs in electrocatalysis, such as HER, OER, ORR, and CO2RR, are discussed. Much emphasis was placed on the electronic structural modulation and the synergistic effects of the DSCs in various reactions. This review also highlighted the opportunities and challenges of DSCs in future research.

2. Types of DSCs

2.1 Heterostructure catalysts

The concept of heterostructure was first raised in the field of semiconductor physics. Generally, a heterostructure is a composite structure consisting of interfaces formed by different materials, including conductors, semiconductors, and insulators.27–29 Accordingly, heterostructure catalysts consisted of several materials that are usually chemically bonded together via an interface.30–32 In recent years, heterostructure materials, especially those with interfaces at the nanoscale, have shown great potential in the fields of energy storage and conversion.33–35 Typically, the heterostructure can lead to energy band bending, thus allowing the modification in the electronic structure of the interface, such as type-II heterojunctions.36–38 With respect to the electrocatalytic reactions, heterostructure catalysts exhibit the following advantages, including 1) heterostructure could provide dual types of active sites within the interface to facilitate multiple reaction steps, and 2) each building block in heterostructure catalysts is tunable, allowing for a various combination to engineering, thus encouraging the precise electronic-structure manipulation for the specific catalytic sites.39–41 The electron delocalization usually modulates the electronic structure or the energy band structure of the components, thus providing more possibilities to tune the catalytic performance of the catalyst.42–44

The most prominent feature of the heterostructure catalysts is providing additional adsorption sites for multiple intermediates during the reaction.45–47 For example, Zhao et al. reported the modulation of the atomic-level interface of Pt/NiO heterostructure via an annealing treatment.48 Fig. 2a and b shows the high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of the Pt/NiO heterostructure. As shown in Fig. 2b, the d-spacing of 0.24 nm for the lattice stripes of the substrate corresponds well to the (111) plane of NiO. The Pt nanoparticle (Pt NP) shows a set of interplanar spacing of 0.24 nm and a crossover angle of 60°, corresponding to the 1/3(422) crystallographic group of Pt. Interestingly, the lattice match of (111)NiO and 1/3(422)Pt indicates that the Pt NPs are epitaxially grown on NiO, which led to the formation of the Pt–NiO interface (Fig. 2c). Astonishingly, the heterostructure delivered by far the highest specific exchange current density of 8.1 mA cmPt−2 for hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR). The excellent performance is attributed to the optimized binding behaviors of hydrogen and hydroxyl species at the engineered Pt–NiO interface, thus the synergy between Pt and NiO dual-sites that greatly enhances the catalytic activity.

In past studies, precious-metal-based materials, such as platinum, palladium, ruthenium, and iridium, were widely employed as advanced catalysts.49–51 However, the scarcity and high cost of these noble metal materials are significant barriers to large-scale commercialization. Therefore, great efforts are devoted to developing highly active and durable non-noble metal catalysts to achieve high catalytic activity. Recently, Guan et al. reported a direct growth of FeOOH on Ni3N nanotube arrays supported on conductive carbon cloth as a bifunctional catalyst.52 The HRTEM image of FeOOH/Ni3N hybrid catalysts (Fig. 2d) clearly demonstrate the heterointerface of FeOOH and Ni3N. Furthermore, the heterointerface is also identified from the bright diffused halo of the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns (Fig. 2e and f). Specifically, the positively charged FeOOH can easily adsorb the OH− or H2O, whilst the negatively charged Ni3N adsorbs the H+ from H2O or OH− in alkaline media (Fig. 2g). Thus, the electron-delocalized interface accelerates the dissociation of H2O and the following hydrogen production. The synergistic effect in a hybrid system with a high density of heterointerface could sustainably trigger the improvement of HER/OER catalytic performance.

2.2 Elemental doping and alloying

For electrocatalysts, the electronic structure is the critical factor that determining their intrinsic adsorption capacity to various intermediates.53–55 Accordingly, tuning the electronic structure of catalysts is an effective approach to regulating the catalytic performance.56–58 Doping has been proven to be a common strategy to alter the electronic structure of catalysts.59–61 The introduced heteroatoms may serve as the new active centers, especially integrating the atoms in close proximity as dual active sites to enhance the catalytic performance.62–64 Zhou et al. employed the nickel dopants within Ni–MoS2 nanosheets to improve the inferior HER kinetics of MoS2.65 The introduced Ni sites could effectively reduce the kinetic potential of the initial water dissociation step and promote the desorption of the formed OH−, leading to an excellent electrochemical HER activity in 1 M KOH aqueous solution. Moreover, the doping strategy could not only introduce the additional active sites but also manipulate the electronic structure of the adjacent atoms. For example, Dou et al. fully exploited the catalytic potential of atomically thin CoSe2 nanoribbons for OER by incorporating Fe doping and Co/Se vacancies.66 Fig. 3a shows the scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) image of the CoSe2–DFe–VCo nanobelts. The Se and Co vacancies could be easily identified by the high-angle annular dark field STEM (HAADF-STEM) images (Fig. 3b and c). The theoretical calculations further demonstrated that the synergistic effect of Fe doping and Co vacancies resulted in a significant decrease in the binding energy of OH* and achieved high catalytic activity.

Alloying is another strategy to fabricate DSCs, which is achieved by incorporating metals into clusters or nanoparticles to form a domain.67–70 Compared with the undecorated one, the foreign metal could dramatically modify the alloyed domain, in electronic, strain, and catalytic properties.71–73 For example, Zhou et al. reported a NiPt alloy with plenty of atomically dispersed Pt at the edges to facilitate hydrogen evolution in alkaline solutions.74 As shown in Fig. 3d and e, HAADF-STEM images revealed the nickel nanotwin structures along {111} axis within the V–NiPt catalyst, in which the brighter spots observed in the topmost layer corresponded to the Pt distribution in the material, indicating the formation of Pt-rich domain, especially semi-atomic-dispersion Pt–Ni atomic pair, on the NiPt alloy. The schematic diagram in Fig. 3e shows the atomic illustration of the material, in which the Pt–Ni atomic pair is located at the edge of the particle. The formed Ni–Pt atomic pairs could accelerate the delicate adsorption/desorption equilibrium between various intermediates (OH* and H*) during the hydrogen evolution reaction to achieve a record 6 mV ultralow overpotential at the current density of 10 mA cm−2.

2.3 Diatomic-site catalysts

In 2011, the research group led by Tao Zhang achieved pioneering success in the preparation of a Pt-based single-atom site catalyst (Pt1/FeOx).75 Since then, single-atom catalysts (SACs) have become a frontier area in catalysis.76–78 Although SACs are expected to achieve 100% atomic utilization, they encounter great challenges in catalytic reactions involving multiple reactants, intermediates, and products. It is attributed to the involved exclusive active site, which makes it difficult to break the linear proportionality between the adsorption energies of the reaction intermediates, resulting in a fundamental limitation of catalytic efficiency.79–81 Also, in order to avoid the formation of metal nanocrystals due to the large surface energy of individual metal atoms, most of the reported synthesized catalysts have relatively low loadings, usually below 1.5 wt%, resulting in low overall activity. Naturally, researchers come up with the idea of constructing more fascinating catalytic sites that contain multiple metal atoms as active centers (to integrate their functions and even exploit potential synergistic interactions). Thus, diatomic-site catalysts (DACs) have recently emerged as a further extension of the SACs family to further pursue high-efficiency catalysis.82–84

The research work related to DACs reported so far can be categorized into homonuclear DACs and heteronuclear DACs.85 The homonuclear DACs consist of two identical and adjacent metal atoms immobilized on a substrate, where the interaction between the two active sites facilitates the optimization of the catalytic kinetics.86–88 For example, Zhang et al. synthesized a planar-like Fe2N6 structure by thermally migrating coupled isolated FeN4 sites on a carbon substrate (Fig. 4a and b).89 The planar-like Fe2N6 structure, with the synergistic advantage of an optimized oxygen intermediate adsorption state and multiple accessible active sites, exhibits a strong driving force for O–O bond breaking, thereby enhancing ORR kinetics as well as inhibiting side reactions.

Integrating two different metal atoms into the diatomic sites can further enrich the catalyst library. The various interaction with the heteronuclear DACs, specifically electron accumulation or depletion between the diatomic active sites, are more interesting for electronic structural modulation and further catalytic performance.90–92 For example, Zhang et al. successfully prepared Pt–Ru dimers anchored on nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes by two-step ALD processes (Fig. 4c).93 As shown in Fig. 4d, the two atoms show different contrast in the dimer, which indicates the dimer-like structure is composed of Pt and Ru atoms. First-principles calculations revealed that Pt–Ru dimers produced a synergistic effect by modulating the electronic structure, thus enhancing the hydrogen evolution activity.

3. Catalytic application of DSCs

3.1 Hydrogen evolution reaction

Hydrogen, owing to its zero-carbon properties and highest weight energy density, is considered the ideal energy carrier to change the global energy structure in the future.94–96 So far, a promising hydrogen production pathway is to electrolyze water, which involves hydrogen evolution reactions (HER) on the cathode and oxygen evolution reactions (OER) on the anode in the electrolytic cell. The efficiency of hydrolysis depends on the catalytic activity of the electrocatalysts used on both electrodes.97–99 For HER, it is a multi-electron process, containing a multi-step reaction involving many reaction intermediates such as H*, and OH* (* represents an adsorption site).100–102 In alkaline media, HER proceeds via the Volmer–Tafel or Volmer–Heyrovsky pathways, as shown in the following equation:
 
Volmer step: H2O + e− → H* + OH− (1)
 
Heyrovsky step: H* + H2O + e− → H2 + OH− (2)
 
Tafel step: H* + H* → H2 (3)

Both pathways involve the adsorption of H2O molecules, i.e., the reduction of H2O molecules to H* and OH−, the adsorption/desorption of OH− and the formation of H* intermediates as shown in Fig. 5a.103,104 Specifically, during the Volmer step, H* is adsorbed on the active sites of the electrocatalyst. In the next step, the adsorbed H* either combines with H2O (Heyrovsky step) or another adsorbed H* (Tafel step) to produce H2.

Since HER is a multi-step reaction in alkaline environments, balancing the adsorption/desorption ability for H* and OH* at one active site is extremely difficult.105–107 The most common strategy is to create dual active sites, in which each performs its functions.108–110 Therefore, a desired DSC catalyst for alkaline HER should be coupled with good H-adsorption and water-dissociation ability. For example, Markovic et al. designed a composite material by depositing ultrathin Ni(OH)2 clusters on Pt electrodes, in which Ni(OH)2 kinetically dissociated water and Pt promoted the subsequent adsorption of atomic hydrogen and desorption of the formed H2.111 In addition, the introduction of Li+ further enhanced the production of hydrogen intermediates due to the destabilizing effect of Li+ on the HO–H bond.112,113 Similar to Pt, the electronic structure of the 2D graphitic carbon nitride (g-C3N4) can be easily tuned to an appropriate level for Had.114,115 Qiao's group constructed a heterostructure consisting of 2D MoN and 2D g-C3N4 (C3N4@MoN).35 The specific Mo–N–C interface could dramatically promote the reaction kinetics of the alkaline HER (Fig. 5b). Theoretical calculations show that the high activity originates from the synergy between the favorable OH adsorption on MoN and the optimized H adsorption on g-C3N4. Therefore, an overpotential of 110 mV is required to drive the catalytic current of 10 mA cm−2, which is much better than the pristine g-C3N4 or MoN (Fig. 5c). MoS2 also has favorable adsorption-free energy for H adsorption. However, its unfavorable water adsorption and dissociation properties limit the practical application to an extent. Therefore, Zang et al. successfully conferred excellent alkaline hydrogen evolution ability to MoS2 by carbon doping, especially the induced orbital modulation.116 Theoretical calculations showed that carbon doping allows HER catalysis to proceed on a much lower potential energy surface (Fig. 5d). From the water adsorption/dissociation conformation on C-MoS2 (Fig. 5d), H2O is adsorbed on the C site, while the nearby S sites assist in water dissociation by attracting H from water molecules. Accordingly, carbon doping can endow MoS2 with an exceptional Pt-like alkaline HER performance by adjusting the orbital orientation on the basal plane (Fig. 5e).

It is highly desirable to create DSCs in a single-atom system to maximize the synergistic effect. Chen et al. fabricated metastable nanostructured RuAu single-atom alloys (SAAs) with novel properties through a laser ablation technique in liquid (LAL).117 As shown in Fig. 6a, Au atoms are atomically dispersed in the Ru host, and the magnified HAADF-STEM image clearly reveals that Au atoms occupy the positions within the Ru lattice, which is further proved by the profile of integrated pixel intensity along with the white solid line in Fig. 6b. DFT shows that Ru has the best driving force for the Volmer reaction, while RuAu has the optimal driving force for the Heyrovsky reaction (Fig. 6c). Thus, RuAu SAAs accelerate HER in alkaline medium by tandem catalysis, namely, Ru atoms capturing and splitting water molecules, and Au atoms adsorbing protons and promoting hydrogen formation to achieve high HER activity. A single-atom criterion combined with dual-site interface engineering should be an effective way to create DSCs. Lu et al. reported a cation-deficient electrostatic anchorage route to fabricate an atomically dispersed Pt–O realm, particularly including the dual-site Pt and Opt components, in monolayer Ti1−xO2 nanosheets (Pt1O1/Ti1−xO2).118 As shown in Fig. 6d, the titanium vacancies (Tivac) are clearly distinguished. In addition, the atomically dispersed Tivac could be utilized for the electrostatic trapping of platinum ions. Furthermore, the obtained Pt1O1/Ti1−xO2 nanosheets have a thickness of about 1.1 nm (Fig. 6e). The bright spot at the titanium lattice row, emphasized by the profile of the integrated pixel intensity, further confirmed the exclusive anchorage of Pt through dangling oxygen bonds at the site-specific Tivac (Fig. 6f). Theoretical calculations show that the dual Pt–Opt sites synergistically optimized the H adsorption at both sites. Moreover, the paired Hads, with optimal ΔGH* and spatial distance could be ideally recombined via the Tafel pathway. Therefore, the synergy of dual Pt–Opt sites substantially triggers the high-efficiency HER catalysis.

3.2 Oxygen evolution reaction

The construction of efficient oxygen evolution electrocatalysts is essential for various energy-related processes, such as electrochemical hydrolysis and metal–air batteries.119–121 The OER process involves four electron–proton coupling reactions followed by the production of oxygen on the catalyst surface (Fig. 7a).122–124 In alkaline conditions the OER reaction equation is as follows:
 
4OH− → O2 + 2H2O + 4e− (4)
It contains several basic steps generally following the pathway below:
 
OH− → OH* + e− (5)
 
OH* + OH− → O* + H2O + e− (6)
 
2O* → O2 (7)
or
 
O* + OH− → OOH* + e− (8)
 
OOH* + OH− → O2 + H2O + e− (9)

It should be noted that the production of O2 through O* can also involve two approaches: one is combining two O* (eqn (7)) to produce O2, and the other pathway is to first form OOH* (eqn (8)) and followed with O2 production.125,126

Since OER undergoes O–H breaking and O–O bond formation, high potential energy is required to overcome the kinetically hindered process. In contrast, DSCs can effectively increase the number of active sites and superior reaction kinetics, thus reducing the energy barrier of the reaction.127–129 In general, different components have specific activity for different fundamental steps of the electrochemical reactions. Meanwhile, the adjacent components could employ synergistic effects to promote the overall reaction kinetics. For example, Zhao and coworkers designed an Ir/Ni(OH)2 heterostructure with ultrafine IrOx nanoparticles anchored on Ni(OH)2 nanosheets.129 Moreover, the abundant interface between IrOx and NiOOH during OER not only ensures the exposure of the active sites but also adequately plays the role of breaking the scaling limitation. The intermediate OH* and O* species involved in the OER reaction could be adsorbed on Ni(OH)2/NiOOH and IrOx, respectively, during which a transfer of the intermediates within the interface is preferred. Thus, the engineered heterointerfaces could further accelerate the formation of OOH*, then, substantially improving the kinetics of oxygen production (Fig. 7b).130,131 Transition metal oxides (TMO) are excellent candidates for OER catalysts due to their multivalent oxidation states (e.g., M2+/3+/4+).132–134 Moreover, the catalytic performance of the Mδ+ center could be further enhanced by introducing the additional redox-flexible dopants. Dionigi et al. elucidated the reaction centers and OER mechanism of NiFe and CoFe (MFe) layered double hydroxides (LDHs) by electrochemical measurements, operando X-ray scattering and absorption spectroscopy, and DFT calculations.58 The results revealed that OER proceeds through the Mars van Krevelen mechanism (Fig. 7c). The Fe sites and the newly formed O-bridged Fe–M (M = Ni or Co) centers, in light of the modified flexible electronic structure, stabilize the unfavorable OER intermediates. Therefore, the corresponding OER performance is dramatically enhanced by several orders of magnitude.

With respect to breaking the related scaling relationship, the precise control of the adsorption of OH* and OOH* at specific sites should be a fascinating strategy for enhancing the catalytic kinetics.135,136 For example, Huang and co-workers reported the synthesis of single-atom transition metals (Fe, Co, and Ni) embedded in N-doped graphene (M–NHGFs) for the OER.127 Although M–NHGFs have an identical M–N4C4 moiety, their catalytic performances are different due to the tuned ligand field effect. In MN4C4 moieties, both M and C centers could serve as the adsorption sites for the OER intermediates. Moreover, the adsorption energy of the C site for the OER intermediates depends strongly on the number of d electrons of the metal. In contrast to the single-site catalytic route in Fe– or Co–N4C4, the OER processes over the Ni–N4C4 moieties through a dual-site pathway (Fig. 8a–c). Specifically, the intermediates including O* and OH* prefer to adsorb on the C site, while OOH* is favorably adsorbed on the Ni site, leading to the circumvention of their scaling relationship. As a result, the Ni–N4C4 samples exhibited superior catalytic activity than Fe–N4C4 and Co–N4C4 counterparts. Notably, the support material is one of the key parameters for the stabilization of the diatomic metal, then the synergistic interaction and the further promoted catalysis. Li et al. employed the transition metal carbides as support materials to stabilize metal atoms (e.g., Fe, Ni) to yield highly efficient single-atom OER catalysts.137 As shown in Fig. 8d, the atomically dispersed Fe/Ni sites at the edge of the thin amorphous WCx surface could be observed. The variation in atomic column intensity also verifies the presence with a random distribution of Fe/Ni atoms on the WCx surface (Fig. 8e). Theoretical calculations show that the atomically dispersed Fe/Ni atoms exist in metal–metal interactions with the WCx surface. During the OER process, the O-bridged Fe–Ni moieties were formed, in which the synergistic effect of Fe and Ni resulted in the lowest overpotential of 160 mV, as shown in Fig. 8f.

3.3 Oxygen reduction reaction (ORR)

ORR holds an essential part in energy conversion and storage devices such as fuel cells, and metal–air cells.138 According to the typical model of multi-electron ORR proposed by Wroblowa et al. The generated H2O2 can be further reduced to H2O involving a 2-electron process at another site (eqn (12)), which is considered to be a sequential pathway (Fig. 9a).139–141

O2 + 2H+ + 2e− → H2O2 (11)

The ORR process consists of multiple steps of adsorption of oxygenated intermediates (OOH*, O*, OH*) on the active sites of the electrocatalyst. Accordingly, the ORR activity of the electrocatalyst depends largely on the strength of the adsorption energy between the active sites and the oxygen-related intermediates. The DSCs can effectively degrade the free energy of the reaction process through the synergistic effect of different active sites in close proximity, thus delivering higher catalytic performance.142–144 The heterostructured catalysts could promote localized charge transfer and trigger the synergetic effect, owing to the varied interfacial properties.145,146 Then, the enhanced electrocatalytic activity and stability for the heterostructured catalysts could be delivered. Chen et al. constructed SrMn3O6−x–SrMnO3 (SMOx–SMO), a perovskite-containing hybrid material with strong interfacial interactions, by the epitaxial growth method.147 As a result, SMOx–SMO exhibited excellent ORR performance in alkaline media, achieving a half-wave potential of 0.74 V and an ultimate current density of −5.46 mA cm−2. Theoretical calculations showed that Mn3+ with partially filled eg orbitals and Mn4+ with unfilled eg orbitals formed a dual-active-site catalytic realm, in which the Mn3+ site served as the dominant site and Mn4+ site worked as the supplementary one. The high-valent oxidation state of Mn optimizes the adsorption of reaction intermediates (Fig. 9b), improves their reaction kinetics, and enhances the ORR catalysis.

Generally, single-atom catalysts are difficult to break the linear proportionality between the adsorption energies of the reaction intermediates.142,148 In contrast, the construction of a diatomic system by introducing foreign components could effectively solve this problem. For instance, Liu's group reported on synergistic catalysis between Pt–Co alloy nanoparticles and atomically dispersed Co–Nx–Cy sites, which improved the ORR catalytic performance (Fig. 10a–d).149 The strained Pt–Co core–shell nanoparticles (NPs) and the Co–Nx–Cy sites directly underwent ORR catalysis. Meanwhile, the H2O2 generated from the nearby Co–Nx–Cy sites was further reduced to H2O by the Pt–Co nanoparticles. DFT calculations further elucidated that the crossover pathway during the oxygen reduction between the Co–Nx–Cy site and the strained PtCo NPs (as shown by the green arrow in Fig. 10e). Therefore, the synergistic effect of the engineered DSC system delivered the outstanding ORR activity and selectivity.

The rational design of the bimetallic center is capable of exhibiting extraordinary ORR electrocatalytic activities.150–152 Wang et al. constructed the Fe–Co dual sites embedded in N-doped carbon nanotubes ((Fe,Co)/CNT) by a double solvent method.23 The activation of the OO bond could be initiated through the Fe–Co dual sites, thereby benefiting the sequential oxygen reduction. Finally, (Fe,Co)/CNT exhibited an excellent ORR activity with admirable onset potential (Eonset, 1.15 V) and half-wave potentials (E1/2, 0.954 V), superior to commercial Pt/C (1.05 and 0.842 V, respectively). Liu's group highlighted the key role of spontaneously formed OH* in promoting the catalytic process of Fe–Co binuclear sites.153 During the whole catalytic process, the exothermic formation of OH* on FeCoN5 makes it a stable modifier ligand throughout the ORR process. Therefore, the activity of the synthesized FeCoN5–OH binuclear site exhibited an activity more than 20 times higher than that of the single-atom FeN4 (Fig. 10f). DFT verified the electrochemical stability of the OH ligand at the binuclear site over the entire ORR potential range, which could weaken the binding strength of Fe–O. As a result, the FeCoN5–OH catalyst exhibited extremely superior onset and half-wave potentials of 1.02 and 0.86 V, respectively, in acidic media.

3.4 CO2 reduction reaction

Electrocatalytic CO2 reduction is a complex process involving multi-proton coupling and electron transfer, leading to a diverse range of reduction products (Fig. 11a).154–156 However, CO2 reduction always occurs at a relatively negative potential. In terms of thermodynamics, CO2 is a stable molecule with a very strong CO bond (750 kJ mol−1), which is much larger than the C–H (411 kJ mol−1), C–O (327 kJ mol−1) and C–C bonds (336 kJ mol−1). Therefore, a large energy barrier must be overcome to induce the conversion of CO2. Electrocatalysts possess the potential to facilitate electron transfer for the catalytic conversion of CO2 under mild conditions.157–159 Nevertheless, the hydrogen protons in the aqueous solution are prone to form H2, which becomes the main side reaction of CO2RR and greatly limits the Faradic efficiency (FE).160 Consequently, designing catalysts with excellent performance to reduce the overpotential and improve the selectivity and stability has become the focus of CO2RR research. In this regard, DSCs can effectively enhance the binding energy of the involved intermediates (e.g., COOH*, CO*, OCHO*, etc.) in CO2RR due to the synergistic effect of the multiple active sites, thus improving their tandem reaction efficiency.161–163

Synergistic catalysis of homologous paired metal diatoms can effectively enhance CO2-related activation reactions.164 For example, Jiao et al. reported on a Cu10–Cu1x+ paired diatom anchored on one-dimensional Pd10Te3 alloy nanowires for CO2RR.165 Notably, the sample with an optimized Cu loading of 0.10 wt% showed a high- efficiency and selectivity of CO2 reduction to CO (FECO of 92%). Moreover, the FEH2 during the CO2RR reaction is limited to ∼3%, implying a completely suppressed activity for the competing HER (Fig. 11b). DFT calculations (Fig. 11c) revealed that the Cu10–Cu1x+ binuclear site has the most favorable kinetic and thermodynamic characteristics. In detail, Cu10 adsorbs a CO2 molecule and the neighboring Cu1x+ adsorbs an H2O molecule, realizing an ideal configuration for stabilizing chemisorbed CO2. Thus, the Cu10–Cu1x+ atomic pair work synergistically to promote the key bimolecular step of CO2RR. In brief, the synergetic activation of the adsorbed bimolecular on diatoms demonstrates the unique advantages of DSCs in complex catalytic reactions.

In addition, heteronuclear diatomic catalysts can provide various synergies based on different molecules and their interfaces. Ren et al. reported on an isolated diatomic Ni–Fe site immobilized on nitrogenous carbon (Ni/Fe–N–C) as an effective electrocatalyst for CO2RR.166 Ni/Fe–N–C exhibits an extremely high selectivity with CO Faraday efficiency of 98% at −0.7 V vs. RHE. Theoretical calculations show that the Ni–Fe centers undergo a structural change upon CO2 uptake and CO adsorption, which reduces the energy barrier for intermediate COOH* formation and CO desorption, leading to a superior CO2RR activity (Fig. 12a). Fu et al. reported a Cr2O3–Ag heterostructure catalyst (Cr2O3@Ag) for electrocatalytic CO2RR with a superb FECO of 99.6% at −0.8 V vs. RHE with a high JCO of 19.0 mA cm−2. Operando Raman spectroscopy studies revealed that the markedly enhanced performance is attributable to the Cr2O3–Ag heterostructure that induces the stabilization of CO2˙−/*COOH intermediates (Fig. 12b–d). DFT calculations unveiled that the metallic Ag-catalyzed CO2 reduction to CO requires a 1.45 eV energy input to proceed, which is 0.93 eV higher than that of the metal/metal oxides-structured Cr2O3@Ag (Fig. 12e).

4. Conclusions and perspectives

Over the past few years, new paradigms using DSCs have been applied to various electrochemical reactions and exhibited tremendous advantages. In this review, we categorized the reported DSCs into three types: the heterostructured catalysts, the doped and alloyed catalysts, and the diatomic catalysts. The emergent progress of the DSCs in the fields of HER, OER, ORR, and CO2RR was summarized, in which the catalytic mechanisms were emphasized by comparing them with the single-site catalysts. It is illustrated that, by using the DSCs, the catalytic efficiency of the multi-step reactions could be optimized remarkably. Meanwhile, the electronic structure of the active sites can be optimized due to the charge transfer between different sites, based on which the binding energy to the intermediates can be tuned. Moreover, the diversity and operability of the DSCs allow for enormous possibilities for catalysis. Up to now, the DSCs have been one of the most active research topics in energy-related electrocatalysts.

Although some progress has been made for DSCs, challenges still remain in this field. For example, the precise identification of the active sites is still a problem that needs further investigation. In addition, the insight into the structure–function relationship between the identified active sites and catalytic performance should be further clarified. The prospects of DSCs in electrochemical applications may lie in the following aspects: 1) developing reliable synthesizing methods for catalyst engineering, such as sub-nanometer heterojunctions and high-yield diatomic catalysts; 2) employing the refined characterization techniques, such as spherical differential electron microscopy and in situ spectroscopy, to clarifying the reaction mechanism and structure–function relationships of DSCs; 3) expending the application of the DSCs to other multiple-step reactions, such as nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR), two-electron water oxidation reaction to generate hydrogen peroxide (2e− WOR), and ethanol oxidation reaction (EOR), and facilitating the tandem catalysis by using the specific synergistic effect. With the diversity of active sites in DSCs, research on non-precious metal DSCs can make a significant contribution to energy conservation and environmental protection. DSCs, as a frontier in electrochemical catalysis, to open up an avenue for high-efficiency electrocatalysis.
